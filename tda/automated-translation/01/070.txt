**Body Text Translation:**
Fond memories of "Gwanghallu," Recollections of a trip to the Peninsula
						Terada Ei

"Right, today we're going to Gwanghallu of *Chunhyangjeon* fame!"
Somehow, that planted a dream in our hearts.

We had heard many times before that *Chunhyangjeon* (春香傳) is a Korean classic comparable to Japan's own *Chūshingura* (忠臣藏). The deeply emotional and intense love story of Yi Mongnyong (李夢龍) and Chunhyang (春香), while perhaps appearing decadent depending on one's perspective, could also, depending on how one thinks about it, be called a tale celebrating the victory of pure fidelity.

—We were going to see Gwanghallu Pavilion (廣寒樓), which could be called the setting for the very first scene of that *Chunhyangjeon*.

The early winter sun shone reddishly on the withered grass, and the dry dust of southern Korea (南鮮) flowed, enveloping the road surface like a hazy smokescreen.

Perhaps it was just my imagination, but even the station signpost reading "Namwon" (南原) seemed imbued with the color and scent of ancient times. And yet, after passing through mulberry fields and finally finding our way into the town, how modernized its appearance was! <40>
I must confess that my first reaction was one of slight disappointment.

Moreover, while the town of Namwon being modern was one thing, I had firmly believed that Gwanghallu Pavilion would stand, ancient and venerable, on the rural outskirts away from such a town. Instead, alongside the main street shops of the town, the ancient Korean building abruptly towered with its tiled roof. What a negation of fantasy that was!

However, after momentarily closing my eyes, pushing the sense of connection with the town into the realm of oblivion, and then looking anew, Gwanghallu Pavilion was, after all, a stage within a legend.

"It seems Yi Mongnyong stood around here and saw Chunhyang enjoying herself on a swing over there."
Being told this, I looked in the direction indicated. Beyond several islets floating in the not-so-wide pond, through an island thick with bamboo, I could see an old pavilion.

"Is *Chunhyangjeon* merely a legend? Or is it a story that actually happened?"

"It is considered to be about real people, a real story."

It was a foolish question from the listener. The local people could immerse themselves in a more compelling sense of pride by believing it to be a true story rather than considering it a hypothetical legend.

The dozens, hundreds of framed tablets hung in the spaces above the beams of the tall pavilion must be the traces left by the brushes of countless sensitive writers and artists who rested their staffs here and entrusted their sentiments to them. One senses the passage of time even in the floorboards one treads upon. And yet, today, hundreds of years later, the addition of color over the peeling original pigments, smelling vividly fresh to the senses, spoils the mood.

"Speaking of Korea's *Chunhyangjeon*, it is, in any case, a representative classic. Here in Namwon, which could be called its birthplace, it's necessary to preserve this Gwanghallu Pavilion at all costs and create <41> a museum."
Mr. Chŏng(?) remarked admiringly.

"That's right. Actually, this place called Namwon is famous because of *Chunhyangjeon*, so people like to attach Chunhyang's name to everything – Chunhyang Inn, Chunhyang Bathhouse, Chunhyang House, Chunhyang Pavilion, Chunhyang Rice Crackers, and so on."

This, too, was an overly vulgar explanation, dragging the five-hundred-year dream of the Yi Dynasty down into an all-too-blatant reality.

We also saw the Chunhyang Shrine. Inside, a portrait of Chunhyang painted by Idang Kim Eunho (以堂金殷鎬) was enshrined.

"This Chunhyang is surprisingly modern, isn't she?"
It was Mr. Sŏ(?) who offered this frank criticism.

"If this were spring, it would feel just right,"
Mr. Yi(?) said, looking up at the surrounding grove of trees.

I realized that, regardless of whether *Chunhyangjeon* is a legend or a true story, the entire environment was far too contrived.

Everyone holds dreams. Those dreams must rest upon an affection that one does not want shattered. However, when those dreams are polluted by excessive reality, one is instead driven by a stunned revulsion.

Even now, I probably won't discard my basic affection for *Chunhyangjeon*. However, I feel that if I had visited Namwon but *not* seen Gwanghallu Pavilion, I would have valued the fantasy and permanence of the work itself far more highly.

This is likely just my own warped fastidiousness. (The author is the Arts and Culture Editor of the Keijō Nippō) <42>

**Summary**

The author, Terada Ei, recounts a visit to Gwanghallu Pavilion in Namwon, Korea, the famed setting of the classic tale *Chunhyangjeon*. Initially filled with romantic anticipation, he finds himself disappointed by the modernized appearance of Namwon town and the Gwanghallu site itself, which feels staged and overly commercialized with numerous businesses using the "Chunhyang" name. He reflects that this encounter with the "reality" of the place, including a modern-looking portrait of Chunhyang, has diminished the story's legendary magic for him, suggesting he might have preferred keeping the idealized image intact by not visiting. For historians, the article offers a valuable glimpse into a Japanese intellectual's perspective during the colonial period, revealing a complex mix of admiration for traditional Korean culture and disillusionment with its contemporary presentation under modernization. It highlights the tensions between preservation, commercialization, and the subjective experience of cultural heritage sites within the colonial context.

						寺田瑛
『さ、けふは春香傳の廣寒樓へ行くぞ』
それが、何となく私たちの胸に夢を持たせてくれた。
『春香傳』は、內地の『忠臣藏』にも匹敵する朝鮮の古典だとは、今までも幾度か聞かされてゐたことであり、李夢龍と春香との纏금綿激越なる戀愛圖繪は、見方によつては頺廢の姿であるかも知れないが、また、考へ方によつては、淸例なる貞操の勝利を讃へた物語ともいひ得る。
-その『春香傳』の最初の場面ともいふべき廣寒樓を見に行くのだ。
早い冬の陽は、枯芝に赭く照り映へて、乾燥した南鮮の土埃は、煙幕のやうに朦朦と道の面を包んで流れた。
「南原」と書かれた驛の立て札にも、氣のせいか、古い昔の色と匂ひが感ぜられるそれにしても、.桑畑の間を拔けて、漸く迪り入つた街の趣きの何と近代化してゐることよ。＜40＞
先づ驚かされた輕い失望であつたといを白狀せねばならない。
しかも、南原の街はそれでよいとしても、廣寒樓は、さうした街をはづれな田園の裾に、古色蒼然と建つてゐるものとばかり思ひ込んでゐたのに、街の目拔きの店舖と並んで、古代朝鮮の建築物が、唐突と甍を聳えさせてゐたのである。それは何といふ夢幻の否定であつたらうか。
たゞ、一應眼を閉ぢて、街との接觸感を忘却の彼方へ追ひやつた後、あらためて見る廣寒樓は、やはり一つの傅說の中の舞臺ではあつた。
「李夢龍は、との邊に立つて、あちらの方で鞦韃を樂しんでゐた春香を見たらしいんですね」
さう說明されて、指される方に眼をやれば、廣からぬ池に浮く幾つかの島の彼方竹蔞の繁る島を透して、古びたる一宇の亭が見えた。
『春香傳といふのは、單なる傅說ですか。それとも、實際にあつた物語ですか』
『實在の人物、實在の物語として考へられてゐますね』
聞く者の迂闊さであつた。土地の人たちは、それを架定の傅說として考へるよりは、實在の物語として信する方が、身に迫る優越に浸り得る。
高樓の楣間に揭げられたる幾十、幾百の扁額は、杖をこゝに休めた多感なる幾多墨客文人が、その感懷を託したる筆の痕であらう。踏む床板にも時代の移り變りが感ぜられる。そして、幾百年を經た今日、剝落した丹靑の上に色を加へて、色感の生生しく匂ふのが興を醒ます。
『朝鮮の春香傅といへば、兎に角、代表的な古典です。その發祥の地ともいふべき南原では、この廣寒樓を飽くまでも保存して、一つの博物館を造る必要があり＜41＞ますね」
鄭君は、さういつて感歎した。
「さうです。實際、この南原といふところは、春香傅のために有名なので、春香旅舘、春香湯、春香軒、春香亭、春香煎餅などと、何にでも春香の名をつけたがりましてね」
これも、卑俗に過ぎたる說明で、五百年の李朝の夢が、あまりにもあからさまなる現實の中へ、引きすり降された形であつた。
春香廟も見た。そとには以堂金殷鎬描くところの春香の畵像が安置されてゐた。
『この春香、案外モダンだね』
率直な批評をしたのは徐君である。
『これが春だと、すつと感じが出るだらうけど』
李君は、あたりの木立を仰ぎ見た。
私は、『春香傅』が傅說であるか、實在の物語であるかは別に、あまりにも一切の環境が仕組まれ過ぎてゐるのに氣がついた。
人間誰しも夢を持つ。その夢は破られたくない愛着の上に置かれなければならない。しかも、その夢、あまりなる現實性に汚濁される時、寧ろ呆然たる反感に驅られる。
今でも、私は『春香傅』への一應の思慕は棄てないであらうが、然し、南原に遊んで廣寒樓を見なかつたとしたら、その方が、遙かに作品としての夢幻性、、悠久性を高く評價したであらうやうな氣がする。
これは、私だけのひねくれた潔癖であらう。(氏は京城日報學藝部長)＜42＞
