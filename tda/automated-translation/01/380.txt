戰歌抄
						金東煥

Military Uniform Sewing Ladies
In the early spring of Shōwa 17 (1942), in a wide room at the headquarters of the Wartime Service Patriotic Corps (臨戰報國團本部), ladies of Chang'an (Seoul) gathered. They brought worn-out military uniforms from the Yongsan Army Command (龍山軍司令部) and were mending them with devotion.

I
The noble ladies of Chang'an, with their white hands,
Mend the military uniforms, worn out by time and dirt, stitch by stitch.
Was it worn by a broad-chested young soldier, or a boy soldier?
Like mending the clothes of a beloved husband, their sewing hands move swiftly with devotion.

Is this from a soldier from Kagoshima (가꼬시마)? It smells of seaweed.
And perhaps another came from Hokkaidō (北海道)? Birch bark clings to it.
Among them, one seems to have been worn by a volunteer soldier (志願兵);
The body odor of kkakdugi and garlic faintly wafts up.

In which mountain valley did they fight several battles wearing these clothes?
How many times did they charge towards machine guns, shouting war cries?
Whenever a trace burnt by ammunition is seen,
The young woman puts down her scissors, bows her fine eyebrows (媚眉), and her heart aches. <172>

All these men, who must have had hometowns, wives, and daughters,
Were grazing cattle on green grass, sowing seeds in long-furrowed fields,
But when the call of the Emperor (임군님) came, they immediately rose,
Rushed out to the ten-thousand-li battlefield, fighting until their clothes wore out like this, oh how they fought.

How noble is the husband in military uniform?
How radiant the son in military uniform must look?
A man lives to wear the military uniform, and dies to be wrapped in the national flag.
Now, Korean women too, hold bouquets for the young men running on the battlefield, hold armfuls of flowers in their skirts to offer.

The noble ladies of Chang'an, with their white hands,
Today too, sit silently, mending the worn-out military uniforms.
Will those who receive these clothes know this devoted intention?
Even so, how welcome it must be, as the devotion of Chang'an's women spreads to South China (南支) and North China (北支).

We Seven
In the early winter of Shōwa 16 (1941), after speaking for about an hour to some 300 young men of a Seoul youth group, encouraging them to become volunteer soldiers, seven young men raised their hands on the spot and immediately volunteered.

I
"We are only seven,
Though few in number, we shall strike even rocks,"
Said the seven youths, rising with hands raised as if shyly.
How can one say seven is few?
It wasn't that you became today's seven by dwindling from 700;
Aren't you the seven who will now rapidly grow to 7,000, to 70,000?

II
"We will know the border from the Songhua River (松花江)
To the Nile River (나일江) and defend it,"
They said softly, yet their mouths spoke with fervor. <173>
To me, it sounded like thunder.
Impulsively, I stepped down from the platform and rushed to embrace their bodies.
Though thin, their bodies were tough as bells (鍾), seeming ready to ring out loudly, wang- wang-

III
"Sleeping in tents,
We will be buried by the roadside, only our spirits returning, to the fatherland,"
They solemnly cried out.
Going forth to the battlefield to fight for the Emperor,
Having already resolved to lay down their lives, their brows showed determination.
There, I saw fire, I saw a great sun.

Namwŏn Journey
On December 16, Shōwa 16 (1941), on the way to Chŏnju for the inaugural meeting of the Chŏlla Pukto Branch of the Korean Wartime Service Patriotic Corps (朝鮮 臨戰報國團 全北支部發會式), I headed towards Kwanghallu Pavilion (廣寒樓), which I always longed for. Taking a night train, I arrived late at night in Namwŏn (南原), stayed one night, and immediately set off on the return journey the next day. Unable to suppress the fragrant charm of these mountains and rivers seen for the first time, I jot down these few pieces.

Passing Im실 (Imsil) and Unbong (雲峯)
Truly, how could I forget this road?
The road I passed more than twelve times in dreams, sitting in a four-bearer palanquin.
On a waning spring evening, raising a cup to get drunk,
This road where I lightly let affections flow, how could I truly forget it?

When a first-met friend asked of my hometown,
How many times did I point towards that Namwŏn by the Great Samnam Road (三南大路)?
And when they asked my age, how many times did I reply
That I was the same age as that boy, the descendant of Yi Hallim (李翰林)?

Dressing her in a fine ramie jacket with a crimson ribbon tied,
And I, wearing a fine ramie coat and dragging traditional shoes (갓신),
To fry flower pancakes (花煎) in spring, to swing on swings in autumn,
How many times did we walk hand-in-hand along this road in dreams?

Ah, what kind of place is Namwŏn, that <174>
Swallows fly in all seasons, and flowers seem to bloom in the snow?
Within the fortress walls, young and pretty ladies and scholars seem to walk hand-in-hand,
Making everyone feel drunk, as if they left someone precious behind, searching and wandering.

Is it now, is it later? Leaning against the window of the Honam Line train,
Namwŏn seems yet distant; "Imsil (任實) and Unbong (雲峯)!" they call out.
Lest I call these welcome names in vain,
Repeating them in my mouth, southward this body goes.
- Aboard the night train -

Before Ch'unhyang Pavilion (春香閣)
A portrait of Ch'unhyang was painted by Kim Ŭn-ho (金殷鎬), the master painter of the generation, and it was hung inside a graceful pavilion called Ch'unhyang Pavilion (春香閣). Before the portrait, incense smoke rose quietly.

I
Like Seville's Triana (쎄비리아, 트리안나), with its beautiful voice,
Entrusting fleeting youth to the flowing water and falling flowers,
That song, that melody, hummed faintly,
As even the paper lantern light dimmed in the fog.
Standing alone guarding the desolate twilight path,
Weary of waiting, plucking willow leaves, that night, that evening.

Everything could exist again just as it was,
But you, Hyangnang (香娘), alone, went first.

II
Like a Greek (끼리샤) lady filling a beautiful jar with clear water,
Going to the spring at first dawn, pushing aside oak leaves,
Carrying a pottery jar full of clean water on her head, that charm, that figure.
Picking morning glory flowers wet with dew from the back scenery,
Sifting them through a sieve, steaming them in a steamer to make fragrant sweet rice drink (甘酒),
Delivering it to every house of the fortress town's finest ladies, that heart, those hands. <175>
Everything could exist again just as it was,
But you, Hyangnang (香娘), alone, went first.

III
In the evening street, the Sŏsan (西山) donkey passes,
The sound of the pipa (琵琶) drifts from the tavern.
When the chivalrous youths of Namwŏn (南原), saying the morning drum hasn't sounded yet,
Pass by swaying and singing merrily,
Unable to find a way to deliver the silver hairpin, climbing the stone wall,
Why did the traveler from Seoul arrive a hundred years too late?

Ah, everything is gone, like flowing water, like falling flowers.
How am I to pass this night?

Crossing Ojakkyo Bridge (烏鵲橋)
Right next to Kwanghallu Pavilion (廣寒樓) is a five-arch rainbow-shaped stone bridge made of marble. This is Ojakkyo Bridge (烏鵲橋). Directly in front are the bamboo groves of Pangjangdo (方丈島) and Yŏngjudo (영주島) islands, boasting evergreen beauty throughout the seasons.

On the mirror-like Ojakkyo (烏鵲橋)
Five-arch stone bridge,
The slightly drunk traveler leans against the setting sun.
The bell sound from Sŏnwŏnsa Temple (禪院寺) breaks off, then breaks off again,
Shaking the lonely traveler's heart into confusion.

Are the elegant gentlemen of Namwŏn Prefecture (南原府中)
Leaving to play golpae (골패) saying "Paeksa asamnyuk (백사아삼륙)"?
In the bamboo grove of Yŏngjudo Island (瀛州島),
Colorful silk lanterns flicker chaotically.
From somewhere, a note from a Hojŏk flute (胡笛) tugs at the heartstrings.

Since it's a moonlit night, should I play the 25-string zither?
Since I can leave, should I catch a wild goose?
Why does Ojakkyo Bridge (烏鵲橋) remain in a landscape without the beloved?
Unable to resolve the unbearable sorrow and resentment (不勝情怨),
This body goes west, goes back again. <176>

Climbing Kwanghallu Pavilion (廣寒樓)
Five hundred years ago, during the early Yi Dynasty, Hwang Hŭi (黃喜), a prime minister from Namwŏn (南原), built this two-story high pavilion. Its magnificent beauty (輪奐之美) earns it the title of the finest pavilion in the Honam (湖南) region. Indeed, Kwanghallu (廣寒樓), heard of in song, with its layout and structure, is truly splendid and graceful, intoxicating the visitor.

Where round pillars stand and blue tiles rise high,
That is Kwanghallu (廣寒樓), where the literary figures of Honam (湖南) frequented.
Though the poems are gone, the music seems still to welcome me.

Climbing the pavilion, gazing at the path of the clouds,
The 48 districts of Namwŏn (南原) seem to lie below.
When the willow trees green in April by the market, Koksŏng (谷城), Kurye (求禮), and Nŭngju (綾州) must have been visible.

Leaning on the railing, playing ch'ilp'ae p'alp'ae (칠패팔패), riding a post horse,
The young Royal Inspector seems to gallop from Hanyang (Seoul).
Instantly, the clear voice reciting "Golden goblet, fine wine (金樽美酒)" grazes my ear.

Is there a life that lasts a hundred years? Is there a fortress gate that lasts a thousand years?
Before the flowing passage of time, both pavilions and beauties are all gone,
But a subtle fragrance still remains, making one regret growing old.

Alas, this finest pavilion of Honam (湖南),
Am I seeing it amidst a frozen lake and leafless bamboo groves?
When the south wind rises and the barley yellows, I shall come again, I shall come again.
- From the lyrical poetry collection "Haedanghwa" (海棠花) - <177>

**Summary**

This article presents three poems by Kim Tong-hwan under the collective title "War Song Selections." The first poem, "Military Uniform Sewing Ladies," praises elite Seoul women diligently mending uniforms for Japanese soldiers, including Korean volunteers, imbuing the act with patriotic devotion. The second, "We Seven," celebrates seven Korean youths who volunteered for the Japanese Imperial Army after hearing a recruitment speech, portraying their decision as heroic and foundational for future mass enlistment. The third, "Namwŏn Journey," is a travelogue describing the author's visit to Namwŏn, focusing on the famous Kwanghallu Pavilion and evoking the legend of Ch'unhyang, blending romantic nostalgia with the landscape. From a historical perspective, the poems are significant examples of pro-Japanese propaganda literature produced by a Korean writer during the colonial period. They explicitly promote participation in the Japanese war effort (Pacific War), glorifying military service for the Emperor and depicting Korean support, both civilian and military, as noble and necessary, which historians would find valuable for understanding cultural production and collaboration under Japanese rule.
