Descending the Yalu River's Flow, Memories of a Journey on the Peninsula
						Noritake Mitsuo

It is said the Yalu River (鴨綠江) was named because its color resembles that of a duck's head.
Also, speaking of the Yalu River, the following poem by Jeong Mong-ju (鄭夢周) immediately comes to mind:

Ŭiju is the gateway to the nation,
Since ancient times, protected by heavy barriers.
For how many years has the long wall stood?
Winding, following the hills and ridges.
The vast waters of the nomadic tribes,
Coming from the west, define the border.
My journey is already a thousand *ri*,
Reaching here, I still linger, wandering.
Tomorrow morning, I cross the river and depart,
Cranes in the open fields, boundless and vast.

This poem is unparalleled as one depicting the Yalu River. Though times have passed and changed many times, even today, if you go to the riverbank as dusk falls and gaze intently at the flow, you feel nothing but this sentiment. Moreover, I spent over ten years on this riverbank; my youth flowed in the river, and at times, the river was even the object of my thoughts. During those ten years, I also stayed in the upper reaches of the Yalu River, where the opposite <53> bank is close enough to answer if called, and there were times I lay in a junk and descended the river all day long. Therefore, there is quite a lot to say about the Yalu River, a kind of cherished affection that cannot be fully described within a limited number of pages. Gide has his *Travels in the Congo*, but, for example, the forest regions of the upper reaches where I traveled for over a month were truly primitive and rugged, with intense colors, making me feel for the first time the deep emotion of all living things. When I talk about the *konparichierichieri* (コンパリチエリチエリ) I caught in those fields – a red-bellied grasshopper about five *sun* long – every friend criticizes my poor pronunciation, saying there is no such Korean word. But since they are folks who have never actually seen the upper river region, I figure it can’t be helped, and I sadly resign myself to it.

It is now April. So, after the ice melt, how blue the water must be. The river, which floated ice floes every morning, must be completely warm now, and green grass must already be sprouting on the riverbanks, where the *Ch'ŏene* (チエーネ) [likely Korean girls/women] are probably treading through the dew, digging for *tattŭre* (タツレ) [likely *tallae*, wild chives]. It has been three years since I came to Keijō (京城) [Seoul], but in spring, I often dream of the river. Why is it always a dream of a certain dew-heavy morning, seeing countless ducks resting on the water from the bridge, flowing along, and just when you think they've flowed away, they gather again under the bridge? I can almost feel them cutting through the stuffy, salty wind with flapping wings. I often rode on the rafts moored at the riverbank, soaked by the lapping waves, letting my thoughts run to unpleasant human interactions and my empty youth. Those days, those memories amidst the waves. When I finish writing this manuscript, I intend to take a few coins and return once more to stand on the bank of the Yalu River. Not near the railway bridge in Sinŭiju (新義州), but further downstream, at the spot where there is a plaza behind the Forestry Office (營林署), where the river embankment ends at a divergence. And I want to lie down in the grassy field and wash away the murky grime of my recent hectic life. (The author is a poet) <54>

**Summary**

The author, Noritake Mitsuo, nostalgically recalls his ten years living by the Yalu River (Ōryokkō/Amnokgang). He reflects on the river's beauty, citing a poem by Jeong Mong-ju, and describes his deep personal connection to it, associating it with his youth. He contrasts the primitive, vivid nature of the upper river regions, including mentioning a large grasshopper and observations of local life (Korean girls digging for wild chives), with his current, hectic life in Keijō (Seoul). He longs to return to a specific spot on the riverbank downstream from Sinŭiju to find peace. From a historical perspective, the article offers insight into the life and sentiments of a Japanese individual (a poet) residing in colonial Korea. It portrays a romanticized view of the Korean landscape, particularly the Yalu River border region, while also casually mentioning observations of Korean people ("Ch'ŏene," possibly derived from *ch'ŏnyŏ*) and local terms (*tattŭre*, likely *tallae*), reflecting the everyday interactions and asymmetrical power dynamics inherent in the colonial setting. Historians might find the author's personal reflections, his descriptions of the region, and his specific mention of interactions (or lack thereof, regarding the grasshopper name) useful for understanding Japanese civilian perspectives and experiences in Korea during this period.

鴨綠江流を下る, 半島の旅の思出
						則武三雄
鴨綠江の名は鴨の頭の色彩に似てゐるので名付けられたといふ。
また鴨綠江といふと、すぐ唇に浮ぶのは次の鄭夢周の詩である。
義州は國の門戶であり
いにしへよセ重關のまもり
長城 何年か起ち
屈曲にして山岡にしたがふ
浩浩靽鞨の水は
西に來つて封彊を限り
我は往くすでに千里
ここに至つて仍ら、彷徨
明朝は江を過ぎて去る
鶴は野天 茫茫
この詩は鴨綠江を詠じたものとしてたぐひないものであり、時代過は過去り幾度變遷してゐるが、今日でも暮れゅく河岸に行つて流れを凝視してゐると、この感懷以外のなにものもないのを感じる。しかも自分はこの河畔に十年餘を過し、河に私の靑春がながれ、或時は河が私の思惟の對象でさへあつた。十年の間には鴨錄江が對＜53＞岸と呼べばこたへる近い上流地方にも旗し、戎克て臥て一日中河を下つたこともあつた。だから鴨綠江について言はうとすれば若干でないものがあり、限られた枚數では頓には敘べ得ないやうな、さう言つた愛惜がある。ジイドにはコンゴ紀行があるが、たとへば私が月餘を旅した上流の森林地帶も實に原始的であらあらしく、色彩が強烈で、はじめて生きとし生ける感慨を感ぜしめた。その野で捉へたコンパリチエリチエリといふ、五寸程もある、腹の紅い飛蝗の話をすると、そんな朝鮮語はないと言つて、どの友人達も私の拙い發音をけなすのである。が、實際の上流地方を見たことがない連中だから、まあ仕方がないと思つて僕は僕でさびしくあきらめるのである。
今は四月である。だから解氷した後で水はどんなにか碧いであらう。毎朝氷塊を泛べてゐた河がすつかり溫み、江岸にはもう綠の草が萠え出て、チエーネ達が露を踏みしだきながらタツレを堀つてゐる頃であらう。京城へきて三年になるが、春はよく河の夢を兄る。それはきまつて或る露がふかい朝、橋上から鴨が水の上に幾羽とも知れす休みながら流れ、流れたと思ふとまた群立つて橋下に集まる夢なのはどうしたものだらう。それがムツとする程潮つぽい風を切り羽搏いてゐるのが身に感じられる位なのである。私はよく江岸に繫留してある筏の上に乘つて打寄せる波に涵り、不快な人と人との交涉や私の空しい靑春に思ひを奔らせた。それらの日日、それらの波間の思ひ出。この稿を書き終えると、私は數錢を手にして再び鴨綠江の岸に立歸つて見やうと想つてゐる。それは新義州の鐵橋のあたりではなく、すつと下流の、營林署の裏手から河岸を傅つて廣場があり、河提防がそこで終る岐れ目の個所である。そして草原に臥て最近の生活の忽忙の濁つた垢を拂ひ去りたいと思ふのだ。(氏は詩人)＜54＞
