南方으로 보내는 꿈
Dream Sent to the South

꿈은 南域으로
Dreaming of the Southern Region

崔貞熙
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi

I learned that there is no winter in the distant lands of the Southern Region (南域) ever since I learned the song of the bluebird.
A place without winter, how strange and luxurious that must be. Just hearing the words makes me want to stretch out my four limbs wide.
Truly,
Even if it weren't for a harsh cold like this year's winter, the season of winter for us was just tiresome. Have we ever had a time when we could stretch our limbs wide and walk the streets?
Isn't that why we longed for spring all winter, crying, "Spring, come quickly"? Isn't that why we waited? Longing, waiting – these might be the emotions we cherish most, perhaps our proudest emotions, but it seems these very cherished and proud emotions sometimes give us excessive pain.
Living while biting back the pain – this too might be<169> a feeling we have preciously held onto, but in cases of excessive pain, don't we forget even the hymns we always enjoy singing? You too, me too, everyone – living even for just one cold season forgetting hymns must surely be a tedious affair.

So, I say,
Let's go to the land of the Southern Region where we can live through all four seasons without ever forgetting our hymns.
There, in the shade of the forests, the fruits of the Ebisu grass (에비스草) ripen plumply like bunches of grapes, and the African Tulip (亞弗利加 튜립) and Red Frangipani (赤素馨) are always in bloom. They say that on days when a warm wind blows from the sky and spreads over the sea, the flowers each release their proud scents – red scents, white scents, yellow scents.
Let's go there and play hide-and-seek, ducking in and out among the flowers. Let's live as if the whole world is nothing but flowers, without frowning even once.
When the white day, brightened by birdsong, darkens again into night with starlight, they say that inside the rooms, candlelight on Turkish (土耳其) candlesticks tells tales of legends, and in the shade of palm trees, native men and women are overcome with excitement from dance, song, and banjo (빤죠). How about we go there too and sing the song of the sun that never perishes? Surely our songs will harmonize with theirs. What does it matter if dew falls and dampens the hem of our clothes?
And while we're at it, let's not bother trying to maintain the dignity of our race or things like that; let's learn from them how to assert the human rights (權利) hidden within them – rights given equally by God to everyone – and teach it to them. Let's make them realize the movement of life (生命), the pride of being human.

南方에의 招待
Invitation to the South

金鐘漢
Kim Chonghan

"...Moshi moshi, is this Kim Chonghan-ssi? I'm leaving for the South (南方) tomorrow. Wouldn't you like to go together?"
A squall (쓰코올) echoing from the receiver.
The wind rustles the cute leaves of the rubber tree.
"Yes, yes..."<170>
Even after answering, the work piled up on the desk needs doing.
"...Then please go ahead first. Of course, I'll follow later."
Soon, the ship's whistle blows, poo poo. Celebrating a friend's beautiful voyage, the seagulls wave white handkerchiefs.
To look up at the Southern Cross (南十字星), on which island should one land? The islands of the South Sea (南海), stained with the blood of our compatriots (同胞). In the sea, lapping with an azure (瑠리빛) hue, lies a coral reef (珊호林) redder than blood. Oh, the poet unable to go to the South sits back and writes a sad poem.
Tended lovingly within the manuscript paper (原稿紙), the South was richer than young poetic inspiration (詩想).

地圖를 펴노코
Spreading Out the Map

李庸岳
Yi Yongak

If I go to the South (南方), will I too become someone who gathers stones and bows to them?
Perhaps because it's a place with many snakes (배암), the people there have come to believe in various gods.

Although my homeland, new with each season, is incomparably good, these are islands I want to pass through once. These are islands I want to live on once.

Just as the names of people living customs different from ours, like Arisyani (아리샤니) or Ppillounik'ani (삘로우니카니), have long sounded beautiful to my ears, why is it that the names of the Southern islands like Celebes (세레베스) or Manila (마니라) just seem gentle, simple, and lovely?

Even the most wicked (惡하다는) person there might perhaps be much closer to virtue (德) and further from sorrow (시름) than I.
Those people have surely received more light than darkness.

But as for me (내사), I shall not go to the South. When peaceful times come, if perhaps friends visit and return from that dreamland, I'll ask them to bring back pretty (고흔) seashells, put my dreams in them, and wish to be a joyful citizen (백성) in my homeland for my whole life.<171>

**Summary**

This collection features three short literary pieces by prominent Korean writers Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi, Kim Chonghan, and Yi Yongak, all centered on the theme of the "South" (南方, Nambang), referring to Southeast Asia during the Pacific War. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi romantically envisions the South as a warm, carefree paradise, a stark contrast to the harsh Korean winter, even suggesting a mission to teach the natives about human rights. Kim Chonghan's piece expresses a longing to join a friend heading south but is held back by duties, hinting at the ongoing war with mentions of "blood-stained islands." Yi Yongak reflects on the allure of the South and its different cultures but ultimately expresses a desire to remain in his homeland, content with hearing about the South from others after peace returns.
From a historical perspective, these pieces are significant examples of cultural production during the late Japanese colonial period, reflecting the wartime mobilization rhetoric promoting Japan's expansion into Southeast Asia. While seemingly expressing personal longing or escapism, they engage with the concept of the "South" as presented by Japanese propaganda, albeit with varying degrees of enthusiasm and subtle hints of the underlying conflict (Kim's "blood-stained islands") or personal reservation (Yi's choice to stay). They offer insight into how Korean intellectuals navigated the pressures and themes of the era.

南方으로 보내는 꿈
꿈은 南域으로
崔貞熙
南域 머언 나라에 겨울이 없다는 것은 내가 파랑새의 노래를 배우면서부터 알았다.
겨울 없는 곳, 얼마나 히얀하고 호사스러운걸까. 말만 들어도 네 활개를 쫘악 벋고 싶어진다.
정말이지,
금년 겨울처럼 모진 치위가 아니드래도 우리에게 겨울이란 節期는 지겨울뿐이였다. 한번 네 활개를 쭉- 쭉-펴고 거리를 걸어본 쩍이 있었든가.
그러기에 겨울내로 「봄아 어서 오너라」고 渴望했든것이 아닌가. 기다렸든 것이 아닌가. 渴望한다는 것, 기다린다는 것, 이런 것이 우리가 가장 애끼는 감정일지 모르고, 우리의 가장 자랑스런 감정일지 모르나, 행용 이 가장 애껴야할 자랑스런 감정이 우리에게 지나친 아픔을 주는 일이 있는것 같다.
아품을 깨물어 가며 산다는 일, 이것 또한 우리들이＜169＞ 귀하게 지니여 오든 감정이겠으나, 그러나 지나친 아품인 경우엔 우리가 항상 즐겨부르는 찬미조차 잊어버리는 것이 아니드냐. 너도 그렇고, 나도 그렇고 다못 한절기라 차드라도 찬미를 잊고 산다는 일은 심심한 일이 아닐수 없겠다.

그러니 말이지.
우리 사시장철 어느때나 찬미를 잊어안버리고 살 수있는 南域의 나라로 가잔말이다.
거기는 숲 그늘에 에비스草의 열매가 포도송이처럼 푸둘어 익어가고, 亞弗利加 튜립과 赤素馨이 늘 피여있어서 다사로운 바람이 하늘에서 불어 바다에 펴지는 날이면 꽃들이 각각 저들의 자랑인 빨간 냄새, 하얀 냄새, 노랑 냄새를 배터버린다 한다.
우리 그리가서 꽃과 꽃사이로 들낙 날락하며, 숨박꼭질 하자쿠나. 이마쌀 한번 찌푸리는 일없이 왼통 세상이 꽃인양, 살아보자쿠나.
새노래에 밝아진 힌 낮이 다시 별빛에 검어가는 밤이면 房안엔 土耳其의 촉대에 초ㅅ불이 傳說을 이얘기하고, 야자나무 그늘에는 土人의 남녀들이 춤과 노래와 빤죠에 흥이 겨웁다한다. 우리도 그리가서 영원히 滅하지 않는 태양의 노래를 불러봄이 어떠냐. 분명 우리의 노래는 그들과 和唱이 될 것이리라. 이슬이 내려 옷자락이 촉촉 느러지면 대수로울게 어디있겠니.
그러면서 우리는 우리 종족의 체면이라든가 이런건 채리려 들지말고, 그들속에 숨은 - 神이 누구에게나 똑같이 주신 인간의 權利를 주장하는 법을 그들에게 배워주자쿠나. 生命의 움즉임을 인간으로서의 자랑을 깨달게하자쿠나.

李庸岳
南方으로 가면 나두 돌이랑 모아놓고 절 하는 사람이 되는 것일까.
아마 배암이 많은 곳이래서 거기ㅅ사람들은 여러가지의 神을 믿어왔겠다.

철철이 새로운 내 고장이 비길데 없이 좋긴하지만 한번은 지나고 싶은 섬들이다 한번은 살고 싶은 섬들이다.

아리샤니  삘로우니카니 하는 우리와는 딴 風俗을 사는 사람들의 이롬이 그져 내 귀에 오래 고왔드시 세레베스니  마니라니 하는 南方의 섬 이름들은 어째서 그럴까 그져 어질고 수수하고 미롭게만 생각된다.

그곳에서 가장 惡하다는 사람일지라도 혹은 내 보담은 훨신 德에 가깝고 시름에 먼 사람일런지도 몰으겠다.
그 사람들은 분명히 어둠보다는 빛을 더 많이 받았으리라.
