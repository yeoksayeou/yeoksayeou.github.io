Let's Protect Motherhood; The Mortality Rate of Young Women is High - The Cause of Illness is Overwork, Nutritional Supplementation is Crucial

In this current situation where population growth is strongly demanded, the health of mothers is most required, but regrettably, the mortality rate of young women in Our Country (我國) shows the highest level among the civilized nations of the world. Ordinarily, according to the progress of culture, the mortality rate of young men and women, regardless of the country, sees the rate for men climb much higher, but Our Country shows the exact opposite phenomenon.
That is, in Our Country, up to the age of thirty-six, the rate for women is about ten percent higher than that for men.

There are various theories about this cause, but the biggest reason is that young housewives who have families are mostly trapped in a state of chronic fatigue. That is, before marriage, they relatively had free time and benefited from opportunities for health promotion like appropriate rest or welfare activities (厚生運動), but upon entering married life, their lifestyle suddenly changes completely. They are stuck inside the home all day long, busy with this and that from morning till evening, and the mental fatigue due to the sense of responsibility as a housewife is also added, gradually leading them into a state of chronic fatigue.

In this way, chronic fatigue lowers resistance to illness, and from mild headaches, dizziness, or insomnia, constipation, or loss of appetite, it eventually induces even stubborn gastrointestinal diseases or tuberculosis-related illnesses.

Generally, feeling fatigue from being overworked is because the body's nutritional components are rapidly depleted. For example, the burning of sugars, the source of vitality, or proteins becomes vigorous, and nitrogen compounds like urea and other waste products, so-called fatigue toxins (疲勞素), accumulate in the body.

Therefore, to recover from fatigue, it is most crucial to first invigorate metabolism and quickly expel fatigue toxins, while simultaneously sufficiently consuming fats, proteins, and carbohydrates (starch or sugars), which are the source of energy (에네루기-) for labor, and also continuously supplementing Vitamin B, which plays an important role in metabolic action.

This seems like quite a difficult task, but if one regularly uses the famous "Wakamoto" (와까모도), this goal can be achieved very easily and reliably.

Furthermore, because that B is rapidly assimilated (同化作用) by the human body and has a high rate of action, its effect is extremely precise as it is also contained in a state combined with phosphate salts.

Also, it appropriately contains nutrients indispensable for pregnant women, such as the three major digestive enzymes (starch digesting enzyme, protein digesting enzyme, fat digesting enzyme), amino acids, glutathione, calcium, iron, phosphorus, and other inorganic substances, glycogen, etc., so as a health supplement (保身藥) for young women, it is in fact equipped with substantial content.

Moreover, it exhibits a unique cell protoplasm restorative action (賦治作用), which is the comprehensive power of the aforementioned components, and by invigorating weakened gastrointestinal cells, it adjusts functions such as digestion, absorption, and excretion. **Summary**

This 1942 article from the magazine *Daitōa* highlights the alarmingly high mortality rate among young Korean women compared to men and women in other "civilized nations." It attributes this primarily to chronic fatigue resulting from the demanding domestic labor and responsibilities of housewives, leading to weakened immunity and susceptibility to diseases like gastrointestinal disorders and tuberculosis. The article strongly advocates for nutritional supplementation, specifically promoting the product "Wakamoto," a yeast-based supplement fortified with vitamins, minerals, and digestive enzymes, as an easy and effective solution to combat fatigue, improve health, and enable women to fulfill their maternal duties, aligning with the wartime emphasis on population growth. For historians, the article is interesting as it reflects colonial public health concerns (or purported concerns), gender roles placing immense domestic burdens on women, the influence of Japanese products and marketing in colonial Korea, and the framing of women's health issues within the context of state interests (population increase for the empire) during wartime. The direct advertisement woven into a health advice article is also noteworthy.
