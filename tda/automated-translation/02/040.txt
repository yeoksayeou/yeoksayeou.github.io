**Body Text Translation:**

(Contribution by 瑞原鍾麟 / Sŏ Wŏnjongnin, also known as Yi Chongnin = Former Ch'ŏndogyo Leader)

Tears of Gratitude for the Vast Imperial Grace

Regarding the conscription system proclaimed this time, there are so many reasons to offer thanks and emotion. To mention the four greatest among them:

First, we weep with gratitude for the high and broad vast Imperial grace (鴻恩) of His Majesty the Emperor, who views all with impartiality (一視同仁) and has opened the path to rebirth for us, the compatriots of the Peninsula.

Second, we must be thankful for the political achievements and merits of the successive Governors-General who provided all sorts of guidance and enlightenment so that we could receive this grace and honor today.

Third, we cannot cease admiring (感心不己) that our descendants have been nurtured to the point where those above can feel secure even when they are entrusted with the country's most important and vital task of bearing arms.

Fourth, we are deeply moved that we have been enabled to display the disposition of loyal retainers and righteous men (忠臣義士), renowned generals and brave husbands (名將勇夫), and to die, obtaining the fitting ground for it.

It is impossible to express all these words of gratitude one by one. We, the newly attached compatriots (新附同胞), having grown within such vast Imperial grace (鴻恩) for 33 years, are now chosen as brave soldiers of the Imperial Army (皇軍勇士), whom His Majesty trusts as his own arms and legs (肱股). There is nothing more awe-inspiring (惶悚한).

Now, the streets of Seoul are filled with the voices of joy and gratitude wherever the Peninsula masses gather.

However, now is not the time to be merely shedding tears of gratitude (感泣), giving thanks, admiring (感心), and feeling moved (감격). On the other hand, what is greatly concerning is that we Korean people, for several hundred years, have had times when we thought of the character for "soldier" (兵) as equivalent to the character for "death" (死). Our ancestors lived without knowing a conscription system. Even in the old times like Silla, Koguryŏ, and Paekche, although there might have been a system of hired soldiers (雇備) by the state, I think there was never an instance where all young men were taken into <23> military service under the principle of universal conscription (國民皆兵).

Not only do our ancestors lack much history of stepping forward for the country's affairs and gladly sacrificing their lives, but they also made it a practice to refuse and avoid even spirited and righteous young sons attempting to become soldiers. We must know that we must devote all our efforts to fundamentally overthrowing and cleansing (洗滌) the minds of these elders (父老) who hold this tradition of even selling their bodies and betraying their ancestors (賣身賣祖) to evade military service (兵役), and instill a new ideology of loyalty to the monarch and love for the country (忠君愛國).

During this preparatory period with only two years remaining, if there are perhaps stubborn elders (父老) who do not well understand this glorious system, we must roll up our sleeves and devote all our energy to enlightening them of this error. Also, concerning the youth, those who are fathers and elder brothers, and the knowledgeable persons (識者) of society must rise up as one to strive to elevate their morale.

In this way, we must strive to enlighten the minds of those, even if only one in ten thousand, who might harbor such mistaken thoughts (思念), so that this system of brilliance (광휘의) may truly achieve good results.
(End) <24>

A New Awakening

(Contribution by 松村紘一 / Matsumura Kōichi, former name 朱耀翰 / Chu Yohan)

As the implementation of the conscription decree, long awaited by the 24 million people of the Peninsula, was announced, the voices of Korean youth raising their arms, shouting "Manse!" (Long live!), and jumping for joy echoed in every corner of the country.

However, I ask you, sons of the nation, what truly makes you rejoice, what makes you glad?

Do you rejoice and feel glad, proud of your own splendid uniformed figure, shouldering a rifle and marching forward in step with the thunderous parade music?

Do you feel satisfaction and pride because you stand shoulder-to-shoulder with the youth of the Mainland (內地), no longer falling behind, no longer lacking anything?

No, no. If that is so, if that is all, your joy is a false joy (헷기쁨), and your pride is a false pride (헷자랑).

You should rightly fear before you rejoice; you should be humble before you boast.

Before rejoicing and celebrating, we ought to be thankful for the boundless Imperial grace (皇恩). Before boasting, we should reflect and train (수련할) ourselves, asking, "Can I sufficiently fulfill the responsibilities befitting a brave soldier of the Imperial Army (皇軍勇士)?"

Again, I ask the fathers, mothers, wives, children, sisters, and brothers of Chosŏn: are there none among you who perhaps grieve and worry because your beloved sons, husbands, older brothers, and younger brothers will go off as soldiers?

If so, I ask: Father, Mother, what truly do you worry about, what do you grieve? Do you say you are sad to part (리별함) with your beloved son? Do you say it is painful (아프다) that your beloved son might die?

I firmly believe there is not a single person like that, but if such sorrow and such worry exist, it is a mistaken thought. <25>

Instead of grieving, you should jump for joy; instead of worrying, you should hold a celebratory feast.

Now the day has come when we can repay the boundless grace of the Imperial Nation (皇國). Now your descendants, whom you painstakingly raised, can offer their lives for the country, making their upbringing worthwhile (보람있게).

Youth of Chosŏn, are you rejoicing? Before you rejoice, first compose your spirit.

Elders (父老) of Chosŏn, are you grieving? Instead of grieving, sing of the glory.

Last winter, after the Great East Asia War (大東亞戰爭) broke out, I appeared as a speaker at a lecture meeting on the current situation held in the auditorium of an elementary school in Koyang County (高陽郡). During the lecture, a harmonica ensemble formed by the school's children came onto the stage and performed military songs (군가). They played songs familiar to our ears like the Patriotic March, Warship March, Pacific March, National Advance March, Hinomaru March, etc., evoking emotion. Among them, they also played the song "父ヨアナタハ强カツタ" (Father, you were strong). "父ヨアナタハツヨカツタ" - "Father, you were strong." This song is sung by sons and daughters who sent their fathers off to war, singing of their fathers' hardships and military valor (武勳) on the battlefield. The ten or so elementary school students performing the ensemble piece—their faces flushed red with excitement, keeping time with their feet—sang the song enjoyably.

The listeners all heard the song with interest, but I alone could not stop the tears from flowing before the song was even finished. Why? Because among those children singing the song, there was not a single child who had such a strong father. Not one child had the qualification to send a father off to war, to be moved by and sing of his military valor (武勳). There were none on that occasion (當席), nor would there be in the future. Children who did not have fathers who sacrificed their lives for the country, children who had no prospect of having such fathers—they were singing <26> the song "Father, you were strong." How unfortunate these children must be. Therefore, I could not help but secretly wipe away my tears.

I felt sorry for those children whose fathers could not fulfill their duties for the country. Moreover, until then, the children themselves were also disabled (병신) [in this context]. Although the opportunity to sacrifice one's life for the country was not entirely absent, it was scarce for those children—listening to the song they sang, I could not stop tears from blurring my vision.

At that time, I addressed the gathered audience, saying that although we may not have the opportunity to go to the front line and offer our bodies for the Imperial Nation (皇國), if we work on the home front (銃後), dedicating all our effort, money, talent, and sincerity, it becomes patriotism (報國) no less than that on the front line.

But today is already different. The conscription system has arrived. Even those young boys who could not have brave soldiers as fathers have now gained the opportunity themselves to establish loyalty for the country. The sons and daughters of those boys will truly be able to sing the song, "Father, you were strong."

Everyone, there is the phrase "duty of military service." Of course, it is the duty of a citizen. But military service (兵役) is simultaneously a duty and a privilege (특권). It is the privilege (특권) to be able to offer one's life for the country. It is the privilege (특권), the honor (영예), to be able to make a human sacrifice for one's compatriots, for countless future generations.

Bestowing such a privilege (특권), such an honor (영예) upon us is entirely due to the vast, deep, and high benevolence of His Majesty the Emperor. Towards this benevolence, on one hand, we should have tears of gratitude, and on the other, we must have the resolve and determination to repay it.

With privilege (특권) and honor (영예) comes heavy responsibility (책무). Since His Majesty has granted us the privilege (특권) and honor (영예) of becoming His soldiers, we are burdened with the heavy duty to become soldiers truly worthy of His Majesty, to fulfill our duties completely through utmost loyalty and service to the nation (盡忠報國).

Then I ask, everyone, what kind of resolve do you have to avoid tarnishing this honor (영예) and to repay this Imperial grace (皇恩)?

Everyone, when the lively sound of the marching band's trumpets echoes in the streets, I know your shoulders naturally want to dance. When the roar of propellers fills the air, does your seated bottom fidget? When watching newsreels of the Imperial Army (皇軍) charging and charging again amidst the orchestra of bombs, artillery, and machine guns, can you open your chest with exhilaration, thinking, "Ah, now I too will go out like that"?

But everyone, let us reflect calmly and coolly. Do we truly have the resolve, at the moment of piloting an airplane to crash into an enemy fleet, <27> to carry explosives and self-detonate in a human-bomb attack against an enemy warship?

When the charge trumpet sounds thunderously 100 meters from the enemy line, do we have the resolve to block the gunports of the enemy pillbox with our own chests to ensure the success of our army's charge?

Our Imperial Army (皇軍) is the Invincible Imperial Army (無敵皇軍). We, called to be a part of the Invincible Imperial Army (無敵皇軍), must have an iron-like resolve not to tarnish that name. What kind of Imperial Army (皇軍) is ours? Our Imperial Army (皇軍) is the strongest military in the world. The brilliant war results of the Great East Asia War (大東亞戰爭) have proven this. Our youth, becoming members of the world's strongest army, are honored. Do you or do you not have the resolve worthy of that honor?

A brave soldier of the Imperial Army (皇軍勇士) is the most representative Japanese (日本人) among Japanese people. The essence of the Japanese spirit lies in Bushido, that is, the military spirit. Stern sense of righteousness, sublime spirit of sacrifice, the mindset of a warrior full of humaneness—do you truly have the resolve to embody and display this essential Japanese spirit?

You who become members of the Imperial Army (皇軍) are representatives of the Peninsula's blood. You must not just become soldiers, but particularly outstanding soldiers. You must achieve results above the average score. There must be a firm determination absolutely not to fall behind our compatriots from the Mainland (內地).

Ah, indeed it is difficult. The strongest military in the world, the most outstanding Japanese among Japanese people, and among such members of the Imperial Army (皇軍), an outstanding warrior who does not fall behind—you must become this. You must become this. It is truly difficult. It is awe-inspiring. Do you or do you not have such resolve?

To not disgrace becoming a part of the Invincible Imperial Army (無敵皇軍), five resolves are necessary:

(1) Be thorough in understanding the National Polity (國體). Our Imperial Nation (皇國) possesses the beautiful National Polity (國體) of One Monarch, Myriad People (一君萬民). His Majesty the Emperor is a god, the Monarch, and also the head of the household. Our country is thus one house, and His Majesty is the elder of that house. Therefore, the Imperial Army (皇軍) is His Majesty's army, and when dying, one must shout "Long live His Majesty the Emperor!" and die. Loyalty to His Majesty is precisely filial piety to one's parents; it is a nation of Loyalty and Filial Piety United (忠孝一致). For His Majesty's sake, not looking back at one's family lineage, parents, or household is loyalty, and its purpose becomes filial piety. It is fundamentally different from the Chinese (支那) morality that takes filial piety as its root. Therefore, one called by the nation has no time to look back even at parents breathing their last.

(2) Awaken to the great ideal of Hakko Ichiu (八紘一宇). The Imperial Army (皇軍) is an army of justice. The war objective of the Imperial Nation (皇國) is to construct a new order in East Asia and to create Hakko Ichiu (八紘一宇), where each nation of the world attains its proper place (各得其所). <28> It is an army of morality, an army of ideals, a sacred fight. Therefore, the army of justice is victorious. One can gladly offer one's life as a sacrifice for the liberation of the East Asian peoples.

(3) Fulfill your Loyalty and Integrity (忠節). The Imperial Army (皇軍) has no other thoughts in mind (염두에). There is only the nation. There is only the thought of offering my body for His Majesty. The glory of the family, personal success (영달), wealth, honor, and fame (부귀공명) are certainly not in mind (염두에). Politics and diplomacy are not in mind (염두에); theory and excuses are absent. There is only obedience to orders and See the Enemy, Surely Kill (見敵必殺).

(4) Transcend Life and Death (死生を 超越せよ). The true Japanese spirit is to die in silence (黙ツテ死ヌコト). It is just to accept death sweetly. The spirit of Dainankō (大楠公) who died in battle at Minatogawa (湊川), the spirit of the Three Human Bombs (爆彈三勇士), the spirit of the Nine War Gods (九軍神) of the Pearl Harbor (真珠灣) attack in Hawaiʻi (ハワイ) — this is the spirit of the Imperial Army (皇軍).

(5) Endure Hardship (困苦). Dying is rather an easy thing, but enduring long suffering is difficult. The trial (단련) of enduring the hardship (困苦) of hands and feet freezing off in the minus 40-degree Heilongjiang (黑龍江), the hardship (困苦) of suffocating in the 140-degree [Fahrenheit?] intense heat of the South Seas (南洋), the training life where blood flows morning and evening, the fierce training (맹훈련) of Monday, Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, Friday—all these hardships (困苦) make a true person.

Without greatly awakening to the above five points, one cannot become a true member of the Imperial Army (皇軍). Such a spirit, in truth, is not formed overnight (일조일석). It is the Japanese spirit passed down from the Age of the Gods, joined by over 700 years of Bushido, with the transcendence of life and death from Buddhism and the righteousness from Confucianism adding luster, that has formed such a great military spirit today.

We must achieve this historical great task in our generation. The reason why a life-or-death determination (사생결단) is indispensable if one truly wants to become a member of the Imperial Army (皇軍) worthy of the name lies here.

Everyone, young gentlemen who will soon be called and go forth, and the fathers, mothers, wives, and siblings who must support them from behind—let us reflect before rejoicing. Let us firm up our determination before boasting. The opportunity has come. By the boundless and vast (鴻大無邊) grace of His Majesty, the opportunity for us too to act as proper human beings has arrived. The Korean people, who had been long sick, paralyzed, and withered thin by 300 years of weak civilian rule (文弱政治), have been called upon to recover new strength, display new vigor, and become leaders in the new world of Great East Asia.

Whether we correctly use this once-in-a-thousand-years opportunity (千載一遇) to become citizens worthy of the name and humans worthy of the name now depends on our hands. Whether we misuse this opportunity, fail to repay the grace bestowed upon us, and fail to shed the shame of a fallen people also depends solely on your hands and mine.

Therefore, I cry out: Youth of Chosŏn, today you shouted "Manse!" Shout with all your might, with all your spirit. But from tomorrow, let us clench our teeth, clench our fists, and devote our bodies, our sincerity, and our spirits entirely to fulfilling this heavy responsibility. <29>

**Summary**

These articles by prominent Korean intellectuals Yi Chongnin (former Ch'ŏndogyo leader) and Chu Yohan (writer, using Japanese name Matsumura Kōichi) were published in 1942 to promote the newly announced Japanese conscription system for Koreans. Both authors express profound gratitude towards the Japanese Emperor for granting Koreans the "privilege" and "honor" of military service, portraying it as the ultimate path to becoming true Imperial subjects and repaying imperial benevolence. They urge Koreans, especially the youth, to shed historical aversion to military life, embrace the warrior spirit (Bushido), and prepare for extreme self-sacrifice, viewing service not as a duty but as a glorious opportunity. They call on elders to overcome traditional reluctance and support the system, framing conscription as a chance for Koreans to prove their loyalty, achieve equality with mainland Japanese, and contribute to the "Great East Asia War." Historically, these pieces are significant as they exemplify the active collaboration of influential Koreans in propagating Japanese imperial ideology and policies during a critical period. They reveal the complex pressures and motivations driving collaboration, framing submission and sacrifice within the colonial structure as a path to recognition and participation in the Japanese Empire, even as it meant fighting and dying for the colonizer. Historians can study the rhetoric used to justify collaboration and persuade the colonized population to accept policies like conscription.

半島靑年에게 與함, 徵兵制와 半島靑年의 覺悟
先導者は叫ふ
我等二千四百萬の半島同胞が待ちに待つた徵兵制度が수に再來年の昭和19年から實地せらることになつだが、民間指導層諸氏はこの感激を演壇より數萬群家にぶちまけ大なる成果を收めた。依つてこの記念すべき指導者の聲をここに輯錄することにしたが、思へば我等は今や名實共に皇國臣民として日の丸の下に大なる巨步を步むことになり、皇民への鍊成を一日も忽かに出來ぬ重大なる歷史的段階に達した。

半島靑年に 望む

伊東致昊
 (일본문, 20-22쪽)

鴻恩感泣

瑞原鍾麟(李鍾麟=前天道敎首領)
이번 발포된 징병제도로 말하면 감사와 감격을 드릴 가지가지가 하도 많습니다. 그 중에 가장 큰 것 네 가지를 말슴하자면
제1, 我等 半島 동포에게 갱생의 길을 여러 주신 천황폐하께옵서의 一視同仁하시는 높고 널부신 鴻恩에 감읍하는 바이며
제2 我等으로 하여곰 이 은전과 영예을 오늘날 받을 수 있게 온갖 지도와 계발을 하여 주신 역대 여러 총독의 정치상 공로에 감사하지 안으면 안되며
제3, 我等의 자손이 나라에 가장 중하고 긴한 병기를 맡어 가지게 되어도 우에서 안심하실 수 있도록 육성시켜 주신데 대하야 感心不己하는 바이며
제4, 我等으로하여금 忠臣義士, 名將勇夫의 소질을 발휘하여 써 죽되 맛당이 그 땅을 얻도록 하게하여 주심함에 감격하는 바입니다.
이렇게 감사한 말슴을 一非 다 할 수 업습니다. 우리들 新附同胞가 33년 간에 이만치 鴻恩속에 자라나서 오늘날은 폐하께서 肱股이라 하시고 믿으시는 皇軍勇士로 뽑아주시니 이런 惶悚한 일이 없습니다.
지금 서울 거리거리에는 半島 민중의 모힌 자리마다 기뻐하고 감사하는 소리로 가득찼습니다.
그러나 지금은 한껏 感泣, 감사, 感心, 감격만 하고 있을 때가 아닙니다. 그 반면에 크게 우려되는 것은, 우리 朝鮮사람은 幾百年來로 「병사 兵」字를 「死」字와 가치 생각하여 오든 때가 있섰습니다. 우리 祖先은 징병제도란 것을 모르고 지내왔습니다. 옛날 新羅, 高句麗, 百濟같은 때에도 나라에서 雇備으로 하는 兵制는 있섰으나, 國民皆兵이라 하여 청년들을 모조리＜23＞ 兵彼에 취케한 일은 없섰든 줄 생각합니다.
我等 祖先들은 제 자신이 나라 일에 나아가 생명을 기뿌게 바친 역사도 만치 못하였거니와 오히려 혈기 있고 의분 있는 청년자제들이 병정이 되려 나서는 것까지도 마다하고 기피하여 오기에 일삼었으니, 賣身賣祖하면서까지 병역을 스려 하든 이 전통을 가진 父老들 머리를 근본적으로 타도하고 洗滌하고 새로운 忠君愛國思想을 집어넣기에 전력을 다하여야 할 줄 압니다.
겨우 2개년의 기간이 남은 이 준비기 동안에 우리들은 완고한 父老가 혹은 있어 이번 영광스러운 이 제도를 잘 이해하지 못하는 경우가 있다면 우리는 발을 벗고 나아가 이 잘못을 깨치기에 전력하여야 할 것이며 또 청년들에게 대하여는 父兄된 자와 사회의 識者가 한글가치 이러나 그 의기를 높여 주기에 노력하여야 할 것입니다.
이리하여 우리는 만에 1인이라도 내포하기 쉬운 이런 그릇된 思念을 가진 자의 마음을 깨치기에 힘써서 광휘의 이 제도로 하여금 참으로 좋은 결과를 마지하게 하여야 할 줄 압니다.

(1) 國體에 철저하여라. 우리 皇國은 一君萬民의 아름다운 國體를 가젔습니다. 천황폐하는 신이시요, 君이시요, 또 가장이십니다. 우리나라는 곧 한집이요 폐하는 그 집의 어룬이십니다. 그러므로 皇軍은 폐하의 군대요, 죽을 때에는 반드시 천황폐하만세를 부르고 죽습니다. 폐하께 충성함이 곧 부모에게 효도하는 것, 忠孝一致의 나라입니다. 폐하를 위해서는 가문도 부모도 가정도 다 돌아보지 안는 것이 곧 충이요 목적이 곧 효가 됩니다. 효를 근본을 삼는 支那道德과는 근본적을 다른 것입니다. 그러므로 나라의 부르심을 받은 자는 숨이 넘어가는 부모라도 이를 돌아볼 겨를이 없는 것입니다.
(2) 八紘一宇의 대이상을 깨다러라. 皇軍은 정의의 군사입니다. 皇國의 전쟁목적은 東亞의 신질서를 건설하고 세계 각국이 各得其所하야 八紘一宇를 만드는 데＜28＞ 있습니다. 도의의 군사요 이상의 군사요 거륵한 싸움입니다. 그러므로 정의의 군사는 승리하는 것입니다. 동양민족의 해방을 위해서 즐거이 내 목숨을 제물로 바칠 수 있는 것입니다.
(3) 忠節을 다하라. 皇軍은 염두에 딴 마음이 없습니다. 오직 나라가 있을 뿐 입니다. 오직 폐하를 위해서 내 몸을 바칠 생각이 있을 뿐입니다. 가정의 영광, 자신의 영달, 부귀공명은 물론 염두에 없습니다. 정치와 외교가 염두에 없고 이론과 변명이었습니다. 오직 명령에 대한 복종이 있고 見敵必殺이 있을 뿐입니다.
(4) 死生을 초월하라. 참말의 日本精神은 말없이 죽는것(黙ツテ死ヌコト)입니다. 그저 죽엄을 달게 죽는 것입니다. 湊川에서 전사한 大楠公의 정신, 爆彈三勇士의 정신, 하와이 진주만 공격의 九軍神의 정신, - 이것이 皇軍의 정신입니다.
(5) 困苦를 견디어라. 차라리 죽기는 쉬윈 일이나 오랜 고난을 참고 견디는 것은 어려운 일입니다. 영하40도의 흑룡강에서 손발이 얼어 빠지는 고난, 140도 고된 더위의 南洋에 숨이 마키는 고난을 능히 견디는 단련, 아츰저녁으로 피가 나는 훈련생활, 월월화수목금금의 맹훈련이 모든 困苦가 참사람을 만드는 것입니다.
이상 다섯 가지를 크게 깨닫지 아니하고서는 참된 皇軍이 될 수 없는 것입니다. 이러한 정신은 기실은 일조일석에 된 것이 아닙니다. 神代부터 전해 나려오는 日本精神에다가 700여년의 무사도가 가입되고 불교의 사생을 초월하는 해탈과 유교의 대의명분이 광채를 더하야 오늘에 이러한 위대한 군인정신을 이룬 것입니다.
우리는 이러한 역사적 대업을 우리 당대에 이루어야 하겠습니다. 참으로 皇軍다운 皇軍이 되려하면 사생결단하는 일대결심이 없어서는 안 될 이유가 여기 있습니다.
여러분, 장차 부르심을 바다 나아갈 청년제군, 그리고 그 뒤를 바뜰어야 할 아버지, 어머니, 안해, 동생들 기뻐하기 전에 반성하십시다. 자랑하기 전에 결심을 굳게 합시다. 기회는 왔습니다. 폐하의 鴻大無邊한 은덕으로서 우리들도 사람 구실 할 기회가 왓습니다. 300년 文弱政治로 긴 병 들리고 마비되고 빼빼 말랏든 조선 사람이 새로운 힘을 회복하고 새로운 기운을 떨처서 大東亞 새론 천지에 지도자가 되라고 우리를 부르섰습니다.
이 千載一遇의 기회를 바로 이용해서 국민다운 국민이 되고 사람다운 사람이 되는 것도 이제는 우리들의 손에 달렸습니다. 이 기회를 잘못 이용해서 모처럼 나리신 은덕을 보답치 못하고 떠러진 백성의 부끄러움을 벗지 못함도 오직 그대와 나의 손에 달렸습니다.
그러므로 나는 웨칩니다. 朝鮮의 청년아 오늘은 만세를 불러다. 마음껏 기운것 소리껏 불러라. 그러나 내일부터는 니를 악물고 주먹을 뿔꾼 쥐고 이 무거운 책임을 완수하기 위해서 내 육체와 내 정성과 내 혼령을 다바치자.＜29＞

大道開けり
