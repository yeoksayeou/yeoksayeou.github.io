Publication Date: 1927년 02월 01일
Article Title: 嶺南民謠 (Yeongnam Folk Songs)
Author: 흥섭
Article Type: 시 (Poetry/Song Lyrics)

In front of the *sarang* room, planting chrysanthemums,
Picking chrysanthemums, brewing liquor.
Let's pour the liquor, let's draw it,
Let's buy a sieve,
Let's strain the liquor, *chalchal*,
My love is coming.

Shoots arrows well, shoots arrows well,
The Seoul scholar shoots arrows well.
Rides horses well, rides horses well,
The Bokdo cavalry ride horses well.

Wind, oh wind, don't blow so softly,
My love's hat string flutters.
Just because my love's hat string flutters,
Does that mean a harsh wind won't blow?

In the long alley of Hak-tong, Kosŏng,
A pair of maidens passed by.
Seeing only the ends of the maidens' hair ribbons,
A pair of bachelors disappeared.

A Hansan ramie kerchief, three *ja* long,
Bordered with a pine-soot purple line.
During the day, worn around the neck,
When night comes, hung on a cord.<54>

**Summary**

This article presents five short folk songs (minyo) from the Yeongnam region of Korea, collected by someone named Heungseop. The songs depict scenes and emotions from everyday life: preparing drinks for an arriving loved one, admiring the skills of archers and horsemen from Seoul and elsewhere, the capricious nature of wind and love, a fleeting encounter between young men and women in an alley, and the description and use of a traditional ramie kerchief. For historians, these songs offer valuable linguistic data from 1927, showcasing older spellings and potentially dialectal forms (e.g., 숭켜, 비빗네, 갓끗, 모달는다, 댕긔, 업네). They also provide a snapshot of enduring cultural themes—hospitality, admiration of skill, romantic notions, local life—persisting within the Yeongnam region during the Japanese colonial period, reflecting grassroots cultural expression.
