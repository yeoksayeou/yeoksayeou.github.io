Water Mill
						Chŏn Chong-hwan

I, becoming a foolish water mill,
Without distinguishing the seasons of spring and autumn,
Stand behind a tall house in the countryside,
Like a spinning top turning in the wind,
Always spinning round and round.

My bare body, rotten in the elements (水土),
Stands exposed in the sun without cover,
Hanging bound to a sturdy mountain pillar,
Always spinning round and round.

Still, this foolish water mill,
Without seeking the path called freedom,
Yesterday that path, today that path,
Always turns along that path. <67>

**Summary**

The poem "Water Mill" by Chŏn Chong-hwan personifies a water mill endlessly turning behind a country house. It rotates constantly, regardless of season or its own worn state ("rotten bare body"), bound to its place and unable to choose its path. The mill explicitly does not seek freedom, continuing its monotonous cycle day after day. From a colonial history perspective, the poem is potentially significant as an allegory for the monotonous, constrained, and unfree existence of Koreans under Japanese rule. The water mill's unchanging path and lack of striving for freedom could reflect feelings of resignation, powerlessness, or the harsh realities that made resistance difficult, offering historians insight into the psychological landscape of the era.

물방아
						錢鍾煥
나는 못난 물방아 되어
춘추라 시절을 가리지도 안코
시골에 노픈 집 뒤에 서서
바람에 도는 도르램이 가티
언제나 빙빙 도을고 잇서.

수토(水土)에 썩은 알 몸탱이를
양지에 서서 가리도 한코
억척산 기동에 매어 달리여
언제나 빙빙 도을고 잇서.

그래도 못난 이 물방아는
자유란 길을 찻지 안코서
어제도 그 길 오늘도 그 길
언제나 그 길을 도을고 잇서.＜67＞
