The Four Types of Human Blood Viewed Serologically
						Paek Inje

- Isohemagglutination Reaction - Blood Transfusion - Blood Type Distribution and Anthropological Considerations - Bloodstain Identification - Blood Type Inheritance and Forensic Applications - My Experiments and Humble Opinions on the Physiological Application of Hemagglutination Reactions.

Regarding Blood Research - specifically the Isohemagglutination Reaction in Human Blood - having read books and conducted my own research from time to time over the years, I venture to use this valuable space, endeavoring to write something that will hopefully not draw criticism from readers as excessively tedious and useless. As this originally belongs to a highly specialized field, I know my brief article and shallow knowledge may not adequately meet expectations. However, if through this, some readers, including so-called scientists and medical scholars, can grasp what applied science generally entails, and understand the approaches, perspectives on natural phenomena, interpretation methods, and efforts towards application in their so-called research process, it would truly be my great fortune.

1. Isohemagglutination Reaction (The 4 Types of Human Blood)
This time, I will first briefly introduce what the isohemagglutination reaction is and how human blood is classified into 4 types based on it, and then proceed to describe the various topics listed in the heading in subsequent installments. Therefore, although it may be somewhat lengthy, I will explain using the following several points for clear understanding.

Agglutination Reaction-. Originally, various agglutinins exist in animal blood. Depending on the agglutinable substance that is agglutinated by these agglutinins, there are names like bacterial agglutinin and hemagglutinin. Also, depending on whether they are naturally present from birth or acquired later, either naturally or artificially, there is a distinction between physiological (congenital) agglutinins and immune (acquired, artificial) agglutinins. The phenomenon where agglutinable substances like bacteria or blood cells clump together due to the action of agglutinins in a serum is called the agglutination reaction. This reaction, similar to various chemical reactions described by chemists, is an extremely necessary and frequently used term in serology.

Interpretation of the Nature of Agglutination Reaction-. Although our current knowledge cannot fully demonstrate and explain how these various agglutination reactions occur, comprehensively observing the characteristics of various agglutination reactions <46> based on research facts accumulated over several decades reveals the discovery of a consistent, rigid rule common among them. Relying on this rule allows for the demonstration of various agglutination reaction phenomena in serology one by one, making it clear that this rule is indeed the only knowledge we currently possess for interpreting the nature of agglutination reactions. This is the famous side-chain theory by Mr. Ehrlich. To explain simply, the conditions for an agglutination reaction to occur necessitate the presence of so-called agglutinins possessing both a haptophore (結合簇) and an agglutinophore (凝集簇), and side-chains, i.e., receptors (受體), that have the property of binding with the haptophore of these agglutinins. That is, the receptors possessed by cells, whether bacteria or blood cells, must bind with the haptophore of the agglutinins in the serum applied to these cells for the agglutinophore of these agglutinins to agglutinate the cells. Therefore, even if cells possess various receptors, if the serum lacks agglutinins capable of binding with them, or conversely, even if the serum contains various agglutinins, if the cells lack receptors to bind with them, the agglutination reaction can never occur. Consequently, the various agglutination reactions mentioned above all possess specific characteristics unique to their agglutinins or receptors. That is,
Bacterial Agglutinin and Hemagglutinin
are distinct, and naturally, their corresponding receptors also have different properties. Furthermore, even among bacterial agglutinins, each is specific according to the nature of the bacteria. For example, just as the dysentery bacillus agglutinin and the cholera vibrio agglutinin are different, so too are hemagglutinins for different species' blood cells and same-species blood cells distinct. Simultaneously, among different species' blood cells, the agglutinins for their blood cells differ, especially as the species are more distantly related in evolutionary terms.

As briefly described above, the agglutination reaction is one of the extremely important phenomena in serology, and research on the agglutination reactions of various bacteria and blood cells
has undoubtedly made significant contributions
in biology, medicine, and especially in diagnostics and therapeutics. However, previous research, across various animals and humans, had asserted that there was no hemagglutination reaction among individuals of the same species. This was partly because theoretical reasoning suggested such serological reactions wouldn't occur within an individual, closely related individuals, or the same species, and also because the inconclusive, fragmentary experimental results of a few scholars showed negative results consistent with this assumption. However, in 1901, research by Mr. Landsteiner shattered this previous misconception. He demonstrated and discovered the phenomenon of hemagglutination reaction between individuals within the human species, which is the origin of the term isohemagglutination reaction. According to his research, this reaction allows for the broad classification of human blood into 3 types. However, subsequent follow-up experiments by 4 or 5 scholars confirmed the existence of a 4-type classification, which I will now introduce in detail.

Within human serum, there are two types of hemagglutinins, tentatively named α and β. Correspondingly, blood cells possess two types of receptors, called A and B, respectively. However, blood containing α in the serum lacks A in the blood cells, and blood containing β in the serum lacks B in the blood cells. (3) Method for Selecting Standard Sera
To identify the blood type of an unknown person, as described above, using
both Type A and Type B standard
sera is the simplest and most complete method. How are these standard sera selected? Approximately 20 individuals' blood is collected, a few [units, perhaps drops?] (數 8式?) each. A small portion of each is added to a sodium citrate solution to prevent coagulation and make a blood cell suspension. The remaining larger portion is allowed to coagulate to collect serum. Then, the serum and blood cells of these 20 individuals are reacted sequentially with the serum and blood cells of the others. The positive/negative relationship of the reactions can be immediately judged against the structure table mentioned above to determine which of the 4 types
each belongs to instantly.
From these, based on the relationship of A, B, α, β in the blood cells and serum, selecting both A Type and B Type individuals provides the standard sera. Whenever needed, blood can be drawn from these A and B type individuals to obtain the serum.

(4) Experiment Regarding Inductive Proof for Isohemagglutination Reaction
It is certain that the hemagglutination reaction of human blood, as described above, clearly applies to each individual and allows for the distinct division into 4 types. Explaining the nature of this reaction, as previously mentioned, through the serological structure of blood cells and serum—that is, the presence or absence <49> of the two types of receptors A and B and the corresponding two types of
agglutinins α and β—
is extremely appropriate based on the general laws of agglutination reactions, leaving no room for doubt. However, if the nature is indeed so, inductive proof that can explain it point by point is necessary. And it must certainly be inducible. The experiment we should conduct is undoubtedly the agglutinin absorption test on serum.
The absorption test involves taking serum assumed to contain agglutinin α, or β, or both α and β, and reacting it with blood cells assumed to possess the corresponding receptors A, or B, or both A and B, thereby absorbing or removing
some or all of the agglutinins,
experimentally demonstrating that the serum becomes entirely devoid of α and β, like AB type serum. To describe one example method: Take a certain amount of O type serum, assumed to contain both α and β agglutinins, place it in a precipitation glass tube, first add an excess amount of B type blood cells assumed to possess the A receptor (Note: this seems incorrect, should be A type cells for A receptor to absorb α), shake well and mix, place in an incubator for 30 minutes, then centrifuge to completely precipitate the agglutinated
blood cells at the bottom of the tube.
Take the supernatant and test for agglutination reaction; it demonstrates that the supernatant has lost all of the α agglutinin from the original α and β pair, possessing only β. That is, this serum is no longer O type serum but has become B type serum (Note: Following the logic, if A cells were used, it should become B type serum containing only β. If B cells were used, it would become A type serum containing only α). Next, transfer this supernatant to another glass tube, and this time, add an excess amount of B type blood cells (Note: Should be A type cells if the previous step yielded B type serum, or B type cells if the previous step yielded A type serum) that will bind with the β agglutinin (Note: Should be α agglutinin if previous step yielded A type serum), perform the same procedure as before, and then examine the supernatant. It has lost both α and β agglutinins entirely, and its properties are exactly like AB type serum, unable to agglutinate any other type of blood cells. However, if this final supernatant is applied
to rabbit or sheep blood cells,
it is still observed to agglutinate them. This is analogous to how AB type serum, despite lacking the human hemagglutinins α and β, possesses agglutinins for heterologous blood cells like rabbit and sheep, thus demonstrating that this absorption test is methodologically sound.
The results of this absorption test are successful not only in stepwise converting O type serum into A type, B type (Note: should be A type then AB, or B type then AB based on correction above), and AB type serum, but it is also experimentally proven that, by the same logic, A type or B type can be converted into AB type.
Conversely to this absorption test, it has also been experimentally shown that we can obtain counter-evidence by mixing and adjusting various types of serum or blood cells (the principles of this absorption test and counter-evidence were applied in other experiments I conducted, which will be described later; it is a very interesting phenomenon).
Above, I believe I have fully explained what the isohemagglutination reaction is and how human blood can be classified into 4 types based on it. Truly, this phenomenon is undoubtedly a splendid natural phenomenon, no different from the great natural phenomena of the four seasons and the cycle of day and night. Furthermore, although it is unknown whether its influence on us is quantitatively the same as the aforementioned great natural phenomena, it is not difficult to imagine that it has effects that cannot be overlooked. <50>

**Summary**

This 1927 article by Paek Inje explains the scientific basis of the four human blood types (A, B, AB, O) based on the isohemagglutination reaction discovered by Landsteiner. It details the underlying serological principles involving agglutinins (α, β) in serum and receptors (A, B) on red blood cells, explaining how their combinations define the four types and their interaction patterns. The text describes methods for identifying an individual's blood type using standard A and B sera, how to select these standard sera, and confirms the theory using agglutinin absorption tests, demonstrating how serum characteristics can be experimentally altered. Paek emphasizes this blood group system as a fundamental natural phenomenon with significant implications. From a colonial history perspective, this article is significant as it showcases a prominent Korean intellectual, Paek Inje, engaging with and disseminating advanced Western medical science (serology) in the Korean language during the Japanese colonial period. It reflects the efforts to modernize Korean scientific understanding and potentially laid groundwork for applications in medicine and forensics within Korea, despite the colonial constraints.
