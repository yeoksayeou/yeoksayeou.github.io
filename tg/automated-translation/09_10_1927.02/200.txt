The System of Root Vowel Change
						An Chasan

(1) Having seen the opinions expressed by everyone, I find many points that do not align with my humble views. As I now wish to state, for the first time, the system of root vowel change, the time for an absolute explanation is still premature, so I intend to open the discussion with relative observations.

(2) The influence of the Sanskrit language is not only found in its Near Eastern neighbors like Iranian and Armenian languages but should also be compared with the distant Korean language. I cannot know if this is merely my own far-fetched, narrow view, but considering the history of revering Buddhism and translating Buddhist scriptures from the Three Kingdoms period to the era of King Sejo of the Yi Dynasty, it cannot be casually overlooked.

(3) Setting aside for now the fact that many Korean consonants are remarkably similar to Sanskrit consonants, the grammatical role of vowel change needs to be compared against the rules of Sanskrit. Why? Because, as Ogede(?) said, if one does not know foreign languages, one cannot study the nature of one's own language. Therefore, explaining Korean seems likely to become clear only by comparing it with languages similar to it.

(4) The esteemed opinions of the 18 scholars seem to aim at establishing a new rule that destroys the system of root vowel change, which is the grammatical function in forms like `되여` [to become], `그려` [to draw], `막혀` [to be blocked], etc., and unifies them with the sound (어). However, I believe this is decidedly unacceptable, whether from a logical category or a grammatical category.

(5) The ending varies depending on the type of stem. This grammatical function is reminiscent of Sanskrit grammar. While this system has various distinctions, let me first show just one type. For stems ending in a vowel of a normal syllable, they change using forms like `아, 여, 어, 이`. Its grammatical function is organized by analogy into four types: `ㅏ, ㅕ, ㅓ, ㅣ`.

(5) Grammatical System of ㅗ Stems
┌╴ 오 (come)  와  X ╴┐

Among these, the ones where the root vowel changes are systems of monosyllabic stems, and those where the ㅇ sound enters involve polysyllabic, i.e., double vowel, organization. This grammatical system ultimately has the same value as inflection (曲折). If one destroys this grammar and writes `되어` [twee], `그리어` [kŭrie], this is not proper language; words like `되어`, `그리어`, `가아서` [kaasŏ] do not exist anywhere in Korea.

(6) The few rules above represent the grammatical function of the change system, forming a definite category. If one now abolishes this and unifies everything with `어`, it becomes a foreign language with no actual examples in Korean. Who among Koreans says `되어` or `스이어` [sŭiŏ]?

(7) Root vowel change occurs according to the stem in Sanskrit or English as well. Examples of the same nature include the change in Greek from *Leipo* to *Leloipa*, in German *Binden* to *Band*, in Sanskrit *ecwo* to *ecwa*, and also the 3rd person singular active form *bhereti* whose middle form becomes *bherete*.

(8) Looking at the system of base vowels in simple English, *Lie* becomes *Lying* in the present [participle], and *tie* becomes *tying* in the present [participle]. The change of *ie* to *y* is a fixed rule. For the past tense of *want*, *ed* is added, while for *live*, only *d* is added. Thus, because it changed to a Y sound<58>, *y* is written, and because it changed [in a way represented by] *ed*, *e* is additionally included [note: this English analysis seems slightly confused]. Similarly in Korean, what becomes `여` is written as `여`, and the change of `스` [sŭ] to `서` [sŏ] is written as `서`. Why is this said not to be a rule?

(9) It seems you gentlemen insist on this based on the illusory viewpoint and definition that `ㅕ` is a combination of `ㅣ + ㅓ` and `과` [kwa] is a combination of `ㅗ + ㅏ`. `ㅕ` and `ㅘ` are not diphthongs (複母音) but changed vowels (化母音), that is, palatalized sounds and labialized sounds. Explaining `ㅕ` as resulting from the combination of `ㅣ` and `ㅓ` is not an explanation based on the physiology of sound; it relates to the momentary transitional sound (涉音 geide?) that arises at the point of connection (續合處). And `ㅘ` does not necessarily result only from combining `ㅗ + ㅏ`; it can also result from combining `ㅜ + ㅏ`. For instance, `무안` (無顔 - shameless) can be pronounced `뫈` [mwan], and `주악餠` (chuak cake?) can be called `좍` [chwak]. If one defines sounds based on such effects of connection, a consistent explanation cannot be established. `하여` [hayŏ] is also pronounced `해` [hae], so couldn't `ㅐ` be a combination of `ㅏ + ㅕ`? In Japanese, `けう` [keu] is pronounced `きょ` [kyo], so couldn't `ㅛ` be a combination of `ㅔ + ㅜ`?

(10) In Korean grammar, it is the correct rule that the system of change for stems characterized by changed vowels (化母音性) also results in changed vowels. If, as you gentlemen say, both `ㅚ` and `ㅕ` are considered diphthongs, then it is the correct rule that for a diphthong stem like `되` [toe], a diphthong like `여`, i.e., a sound of the same nature, enters: `되여` [toeyŏ], `쥐여` [chwiyŏ], `메여` [meyŏ], `내여` [naeyŏ], `의여` [ŭiyŏ?], `그려` [kŭryŏ], `와쳐` [wach'yŏ?], `괴여` [koeyŏ]. Such rules of change are the most exquisite rules in world grammar. If this is called vowel harmony, it is even more advanced than the harmony rules of Turkish. Why would one want to destroy such a well-formed grammar?

(11) The second condition proposed by you gentlemen seems to be that since other types of stems all take the `어` sound, `되` should also be unified with `어`. However, since the change of the ending follows the type of stem and differs accordingly, unifying them uniformly is impossible. For example, when verbs with a final consonant stem (假合フ終聲?) potentially form a [passive/causative?] form, they all use `히` [hi], resulting in `먹히다` [mŏkhida - to be eaten], `썩히다` [ssŏkhida - to let rot], `녹히다` [nok'ida - to melt (trans.)]. But for `죽` (死 - die), it does not become `죽히다` [chuk'ida]. Adjectives like `좁다` [chopta - narrow] become verbs `좁히다` [chop'ida - to narrow (trans.)], and `둥글다` [tunggulda - round] becomes `둥글니다` [tunggülnida? - to make round?], but `희` (白 - white) and `적` (小 - small) do not become verbs this way. Particles (助詞) like `가/이` and `로/으로` also attach differently depending on the type of stem they follow: `집이` [chibi], `소가` [soga], `집으로` [chibŭro], `소로` [soro]. Would you gentlemen unify all these uniformly based on your assertion?

(12) Seeking to trim everything uniformly without employing such analogical reasoning (頻推?) is incompatible with my foolish opinion. I wish to hear your esteemed views again.

(13) Even if `ㅕ` were a combination of `ㅣ + ㅓ`, insisting on writing the source `이어` instead of `여` belongs to etymological explanation, not grammatical explanation. It is improper to write the two characters `2, 1` where the character `3` should be written, just because `2 + 1` equals `3`. In natural science, one only uses axioms as standards; asking why `2 + 1` becomes `3` does not foster logic. Endings organized with `여` should be recognized as axiomatic without needing to discuss their origin; it is sufficient to state that the change system for such-and-such stems involves the entry of the `여` sound. If one searches for and writes the origin, it violates natural science; by what means could one find that origin? To rephrase, even for the same meaning, different sounds enter according to the word form. One explains and organizes by stating that for words with a final consonant (有尾音語), the particles (吐) `이/과` enter, and for words without a final consonant (無尾音語), `와/가` enter. One cannot find the origin of `이/가` and `와/과` to reorganize them anew.

(14) Some gentlemen even call `어` a particle (助詞) or a *t'o* (吐). It would be rude to say they can't tell fish from Lü (魚魯不辯 - i.e., are ignorant), but I think their word classification is underdeveloped. A particle is a meaningful dependent word that agglutinatively (膠着的) helps form the case of another word. `어` is a meaningless sound belonging to the ending changes of verbs, adjectives, and auxiliary verbs. Furthermore, `어` changes in various ways to express different logical meanings (各理意味?):
┌╴ ㅅ-다

|  보다 [poda - see]

먹어 [mŏgŏ - eat and...] ╴┤  서다다 [sŏda...da?]

|  지다 [chida - become]

└╴ ㅅ-서ㅅ-다 [...sŏs...ta?]

The `으` [ŭ] sound is also similar. This is precisely a rule similar to the examples in Sanskrit where verbs change by using the augment (加張法) and attaching special affixes (特殊의 接辭). Even particles like `이/가` can change according to the form of the stem; how much more so for the grammatical function of the ending change system? (Refer to my Revised Korean Grammar [拙著의 修正朝鮮文法])

(15) As Kim Chongjik (金宗直) wrote in the preface to the *Pyŏltongjip* (別洞集序), "When principle and writing fuse within my chest, they emerge as language (理與文融會於吾之胸中則其發而爲言語)." Language is the organ through which we express our thoughts. In exchanging the new thoughts of a new era, organizing the notation method of grammar is the first step in academic work. I hope we will discuss this sufficiently and reach a decision. Sacrificing the private for the public good should be the spirit of discussion.<59>

**Summary**

An Chasan argues strongly against simplifying Korean verb conjugations, specifically defending the complex variations involving vowel changes like `ㅣ/ㅚ/ㅟ/ㅔ/ㅐ` stems combining with endings to produce forms like `되여`, `그려`, `메여` instead of regularizing them to `되어`, `그리어`, `메어` by simply adding `어`. He draws parallels between these Korean root vowel changes and inflectional systems in Sanskrit, Greek, German, and English, suggesting Korean possesses a sophisticated, rule-based system comparable to inflection, which he terms "化母音" (changed vowel) phenomena. He dismisses the idea that sounds like `ㅕ` are mere combinations (`ㅣ+ㅓ`) and criticizes the push for uniformity as illogical and contrary to actual Korean usage and grammatical principles observed even in particle attachment.

This article is interesting historically as it showcases early 20th-century linguistic debates in Korea during the colonial period. It reflects the influence of Western and Indo-European comparative linguistics (especially Sanskrit studies, possibly linked through Buddhist studies) on Korean intellectuals analyzing their own language. An's defense of complex, existing forms against simplification highlights a nationalist undertone—cherishing the perceived intricacy and "exquisiteness" of Korean grammar—while engaging with international linguistic theories to validate his claims. The focus on establishing systematic rules and proper notation underscores the broader project of modernizing and standardizing the Korean language at the time.
