Article Title: 壇君窟에서(妙香山) (From Tan'gun's Cave (Myohyangsan))

Article Type: 시 (Poem)

From Tan'gun's Cave
						Ch'oe Nam-sŏn

In some distant age, the Lord (님) descended here;
When I think I, too, bloomed from a branch extending forth,
How could I not seek this place? How could I call it far or high?

Two
The endlessly open space ahead reaches the sea over there.
Among it, though mountains and mounds are numerous, jumbled together,
They lie prostrate like bowing grain stalks; none dares lift its head.

Three
How many times have wind and rain passed below?
Whenever the Lord's (님) house is to be brightened
With a clear sky and brilliant sun, clouds, just clear away!<47>

**Summary**

This three-stanza sijo by Ch'oe Nam-sŏn, written from Tan'gun's Cave in Myohyangsan, reflects on the poet's connection to the mythical founder of Korea, Tan'gun. The first stanza establishes a deep historical and personal link to the location. The second stanza observes the landscape, perhaps metaphorically describing the subjugated state of the nation ("none dares lift its head"). The final stanza expresses a yearning for a bright future, metaphorically asking the "clouds" (obstacles, perhaps colonial rule) to clear so that the "Lord's house" (Korea) can be bright again. For historians, the poem is interesting as an expression of veiled nationalism during the Japanese colonial period. It uses potent national symbols (Tan'gun, Myohyangsan) and natural imagery to lament the present state and express hope for future liberation, carefully navigating censorship while invoking patriotic sentiment.

몃몃번 비 바람이 알옛녁에 지냇는고
언제고 님의 댁엔 맑은 하늘 밝은 해를
