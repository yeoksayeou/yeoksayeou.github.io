Magazine Title: 동광 제4호
Publication Date: 1926년 08월 01일
Article Title: 戱曲 馬嵬驛에서 (Play: At Mawei Station)
Author: 梁白華 譯 (Translated by Yang Paekhwa)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Play: At Mawei Station
Translated by Yang Paekhwa

Characters
Emperor Hyŏnjong. An old emperor, over 60.
Imperial Consort Yang (Yang Kwibi). 37 years old. Voluptuously beautiful, but signs of aging are noticeable.
Yang Kukch'ung. Prime Minister of the Right. Imperial Consort Yang's older brother.
Lady of the State of Qin (Chin'guk Puin).
Lady of the State of Han (Han'guk Puin).
Lady of the State of Guo (Kwak'kuk Puin). All sisters of Imperial Consort Yang, enfeoffed in large states.
Ko Yŏksa.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng.
Others, numerous unimportant figures.

Time and Place
June, the 15th year of the Tianbao era (天寶).
Mawei (馬嵬), a desolate small station about a hundred *ri* from Chang'an (長安).

Scene
Emperor Hyŏnjong's imperial palanquin (鳳輦), having fled Chang'an in haste, is stopped at Mawei Station. Outside are the carriages carrying the three Ladies and the carriage carrying Yang Kukch'ung. The horses that pulled the carriages have been led away to be watered and are gone. Attendants, court ladies, etc., are scattered around the three carriages in the center; court ladies on foot are particularly noticeable. The curtain of Hyŏnjong's carriage has just been opened. In the background is a tavern. In the distance, among the trees below, the banners and flags of the Three Armies, seemingly resting, can be seen.

Attendant A. (Approaching the imperial palanquin) Your Majesty. It seems a messenger is coming from Yi Sonyong, who is overseeing the rear guard. Look how fast he rides his horse.
Hyŏnjong. (Sticking his face out of the carriage) Is there no hot water?
Attendant A. It's a messenger from Yi Sonyong, Your Majesty. Yi Sonyong's...
Hyŏnjong. Did I ask about that? I told you to bring hot water. The Consort’s tooth aches, bring hot water to rinse her mouth, I say.
Attendant A. Yes, Your Majesty. (Withdraws and enters the tavern.)
Attendant B. (Running out) It seems a messenger is arriving urgently from Yi Sonyong.
Hyŏnjong. Oh, really. Yi Sonyong? He’s probably in charge of the rear guard.
Attendant B. Yes, Your Majesty. He is a general fighting with utmost loyalty to ease Your Majesty's worries (聖慮).
Hyŏnjong. (Ignoring that remark, looking inside the carriage) Does it hurt that much?<57>
Yang Kwibi. (Her body unseen, only the hem of her splendid armor moves) Yes.
Hyŏnjong. What is to be done? A toothache is the most difficult thing. Where has the court physician gone? Why isn't he seen?
Court Lady. (Who was by the carriage) Yes, I've been looking for him since earlier, but it seems he's not here. Everyone says he didn't accompany the procession (扈從) from the start.
(At this moment, upstage, a commotion arises as if the messenger has arrived, and people run that way.)
Attendant B. Ah, he's here. I wonder what news he brings. (Runs off.)
Hyŏnjong. (Paying little attention towards the messenger) How does it hurt? Is it the gum (니ㅅ몸)? Or inside the tooth (니ㅅ속)?
Yang Kwibi. Oww, it hurts, it hurts so much! If only my tooth didn't ache, I'd give away all the night-glowing pearls (夜光珠) I possess. Oww, it hurts!
Hyŏnjong. Oh dear, what can be done? It would have been better if you had just pulled it out earlier.
Yang Kwibi. Yes. Your servant felt it wobble, wobble, so I kept saying, "Pull it out, pull it out." But the court physician said, "Please bear it for a while, bear it for a while," and that's why I'm suffering this pain. If that court physician were here, I'd want to punish him severely.
(Attendant A reverently brings a teapot filled with hot water.)
Attendant A. The hot water is here.
Hyŏnjong. Ah, the hot water has arrived. Rinsing with this should make it better. (Hyŏnjong helps Yang Kwibi.)
Yang Kwibi. (Drinks it) Eww, they didn't put salt in it. How thoughtless.
(Attendant B enters hurriedly.)
Attendant B. Your Majesty, please rest assured. The messenger reports that as soon as An Nokshan's troops entered Chang'an, they all settled down and began looting. He says they are immersed in wine and show no sign of pursuing Your Majesty. It might be good to dismount and rest for a while first.
Hyŏnjong. Yes. I was thinking the same. The road was rough, and the carriage shook so much my back hurt unbearably. Consort! Your toothache is also because of that. When the body shakes too much, teeth tend to ache. Getting down and resting will make it better.
Yang Kwibi. Yes! Your servant was thinking the same. It's too hot inside the carriage, making me flushed, and my tooth aches. Where should we...?
(Hyŏnjong first descends from the carriage, and the Consort follows. Attendants and court ladies move away from the Emperor's vicinity. Yang Kukch'ung and the three Ladies also descend from their carriages. After bowing (目禮) to the Emperor, they go to the lower side and settle down.)
Hyŏnjong. Where has Ko Yŏksa gone?
Attendant C. He went to see Chin Hyŏllyŏng about some matter.
Hyŏnjong. Feed the messenger from Yi Sonyong well. (Looks back at Yang Kwibi, who is pressing her cheek, grimacing in pain) How is it? Is it a little better?
Yang Kwibi. Not at all. It hurts more than before.
Hyŏnjong. Tsk. What can be done? Why not just pull it out swiftly if it's loose?<58>
Yang Kwibi. I would if I could. But if I touch it, I feel like I'll die.
Hyŏnjong. Let me see.
Yang Kwibi. I am indebted to your kindness, Your Majesty.
Hyŏnjong. It's fine. Let me pull it out for you.
Yang Kwibi. Your hands will get dirty.
Hyŏnjong. What does that matter? Between you and me.
Yang Kwibi. The attendants and court ladies are watching.
Hyŏnjong. It's fine. What does it matter at a time like this? Come a little closer. Yes, lean against me like this, and open your mouth a bit wider.
(Hyŏnjong draws the Consort closer, puts his right hand into her mouth, and searches for the aching tooth. The attendants and court ladies turn their faces away.)
Yang Kwibi. Oww, it hurts!
Hyŏnjong. Bear it! This one is really loose, wobbling back and forth. There, just a little more and it will come out!
Yang Kwibi. Oww, it hurts! Ouch! Ah, stop, please. Ouch!
(The tooth comes out.)
Hyŏnjong. See! Didn't it come out?
(Attendant A brings hot water.)
Hyŏnjong. Rinse your mouth. And calm down. The pain will subside soon. I couldn't bear to see you suffer. It hurt me more than if I were aching myself.
(Yang Kwibi remains for a while, pressing her hand to her mouth, head bowed. She keeps spitting saliva from her mouth. Then she rinses.)
Hyŏnjong. How is it? Has the pain calmed down? (Yang Kwibi nods.) There now. It's alright. You're saved now.
Yang Kwibi. (After a moment of silence, wraps the extracted tooth in paper taken from her bosom) This is your servant's first tooth.
Hyŏnjong. First tooth, what do you mean by that?
Yang Kwibi. It's the first tooth to fall out from your servant's body. Your servant once heard the poet Yi Paek say something. Just as one knows autumn has come from a single fallen paulownia leaf (梧桐), the first tooth falling out is a sign that the autumn of the body is now coming. (Gloomily) Oh, the pain is gradually lessening! And yet, your servant somehow feels sad. Somehow feels regretful. It feels as if something has just dropped out from within your servant's heart, leaving it empty. Oh, Your Majesty, please place your hand on your servant's chest! Your servant feels unbearably sad.
Hyŏnjong. (Moves closer to Yang Kwibi, placing his hand on her chest) Like this? Is this what you want me to do?
Yang Kwibi. (Touching her cheek) Oh dear, why, it feels like even the texture of my face has changed! Why do the cheeks feel so hollow?
Hyŏnjong. What are you talking about? Your cheeks are plump like a 16 or 17-year-old girl's.
Yang Kwibi. Oh, Your Majesty, please, don't mention age to your servant. 16 or 17 years old! When your servant hears the words 16 or 17 years old, I feel so sad, as if my spirit is fading away.
Hyŏnjong. My mistake! Let it be.
Yang Kwibi. No, it's not Your Majesty's fault. It's a fact that cannot be endured even by force. It's a fact that cannot be hidden. Your servant is already 30 this year... <59>
Hyŏnjong. Hey, I don't want to hear such things either. As long as your cheeks are always plump like that, and the pupils of your eyes are always dark like that, that's enough.
Yang Kwibi. Such things, such things are impossible to wish for from your servant, who is merely human. When I think about it...
Hyŏnjong. Yes, please stop. You keep picking apart my words like that, haha, try to be a bit more cheerful. I can't stand seeing you so distressed. Yes, be a bit more cheerful.
Yang Kwibi. (As if suddenly remembering) I wish I could see a mirror! (To a court lady) Kŭmhwa, you, the mirror, you must have brought it, right?
Court Lady A. Eh....
Yang Kwibi. You're my dressing table attendant (鏡臺 次知), so you must have brought at least one mirror for me. Please bring it here.
Court Lady A. Oh, Your Highness the Consort! I forgot. I forgot it. I was in such a hurry that I forgot to bring it.
Yang Kwibi. Argh, you stupid wench! You know how important (要緊) a mirror is to me, and yet you did this. Oh, how upsetting! Someone take this wench over there and wring her neck.
Court Lady A. Oh, please spare me! (Cries in fright and prostrates herself.)
Hyŏnjong. Pitiful girl. Spare her. It's commendable enough that she followed you through this chaos (亂離). Spare her.
Yang Kwibi. But still...
(Just as she is about to say something, Court Lady B comes from the three Ladies. She holds a mirror.)
Court Lady B. Your Highness the Consort. The Lady of Han (韓國夫人) asks if this would be of any use, please use it.
Yang Kwibi. What choice is there. Give me that at least. (Takes the mirror.) Oh dear, why do I feel scared to look in the mirror! (After hesitating, she looks.) Oh no, why does my face look so unsightly? There are blotches like this on my forehead, and the powder is caked. Oh dear, my skin has no strength at all, no luster. My eyes lack clarity. Oh, it's unsightly! Unsightly! Why does my face look like this? Oh, Your Majesty, please don't look at your servant's face at all. Your servant is ashamed. Ashamed.
Hyŏnjong. What are you saying? Even in your disheveled hair, there is elegance, and even in your powder-streaked cheeks, there is an indescribably beautiful charm.
Yang Kwibi. (Ignoring Hyŏnjong's words) Ah, how cruel. Is this my face? The face loved by the Son of Heaven of Great Tang? More sorrowful than being chased out of Chang'an is that my face has become hideous. That's right! I've been hideous until now. I've just been deceiving myself with makeup (丹粧) every day. Today, having missed makeup for just one day, the hidden hideousness is suddenly, clearly revealed. Oh, how cruel. I'd rather die than become this hideous.
Hyŏnjong. Hey, try to be more composed. What kind of things are you saying? Who says you are ugly? Isn't this Tang empire filled with talk praising your beauty? Don't they even go so far as to say the country has fallen into chaos because of your beauty?
Yang Kwibi. Even Your Majesty's words, even the words of the Great Tang's Son of Heaven, cannot change the reality reflected in this mirror barely five inches wide. Please push your servant out of Your Majesty's carriage and let her fall.<60> Your servant hates having this face and being beside Your Majesty, being pointed at as 'that's Yang Kwibi' wherever the imperial procession (行幸) goes.
Hyŏnjong. Be patient. You are too agitated, this won't do. This is not the time to talk about beauty or ugliness. Now is a very critical time. You just need to stay quietly embraced in my arms.
Yang Kwibi. But, your servant... Oh dear, to think that from now on I will become uglier day by day...
(A commotion arises from the right.)
Attendants, Court Ladies, etc. Ah, another messenger is coming. Another messenger is coming.
(5 or 6 people run in that direction. Hyŏnjong and Yang Kwibi silently watch that direction. Attendant A returns in panic.)
Attendant A. A second messenger has arrived from Yi Sonyong.
Hyŏnjong. Yes, what happened?
Attendant A. He reports that about 2,000 cavalry of An Nokshan's forces are pursuing from behind. Although they were repelled once, he doesn't know when the main army might follow. He urges you to depart without delay.
Hyŏnjong. Alright. I understand. Then tell Chin Hyŏllyŏng to prepare for departure.
(Attendant A goes downwards.)
Hyŏnjong. How is it? Does your tooth still hurt?
Yang Kwibi. The tooth is somehow better now. But more than that...
Hyŏnjong. Yes, more than that, cheer up and get ready to board the carriage. You only need to endure for a short while. We will soon return to the capital. Then you can once again, to your heart's content, become beautiful and surprise me.
(Hyŏnjong urges Yang Kwibi and is about to rise from his seat. Suddenly, the area below, in the woods where the soldiers are resting, becomes tumultuous. The sound of soldiers banging their shields is heard loudly. Attendant A returns running in distress.)
Attendant A. Your Majesty!
Hyŏnjong. Why is that, why is that?
Attendant A. They have rebelled.
Hyŏnjong. (Stunned) What! What are you saying! Rebelled, such a thing!
Attendant A. Yes, Your Majesty, even when Chin Hyŏllyŏng gives the order (號令), they refuse to move.
Hyŏnjong. (His face paling, yet) There must be a reason. Call Ko Yŏksa! Call Chin Hyŏllyŏng!
(Ko Yŏksa emerges from below. The soldiers become even more tumultuous.)
Hyŏnjong. Ko Yŏksa? What is going on? What is happening?
Ko Yŏksa. Your Majesty. A grave situation has arisen.
Hyŏnjong. Why is that? Have they rebelled?
Ko Yŏksa. No, Your Majesty. It's not a rebellion. They are all loyal soldiers. They are just demanding punishment for the person responsible for this military upheaval. They say they won't move a single step until the responsible person is punished. Some even say that if the responsible person is not punished, they will turn their spears around and surrender to An Nokshan.
Hyŏnjong. Does even Chin Hyŏllyŏng say so?<61>
Ko Yŏksa. Chin Hyŏllyŏng is using all his strength to persuade (效諭) the soldiers, but in this situation, power (實力) is what prevails. Even Chin Hyŏllyŏng's lifelong authority holds no sway.
Hyŏnjong. But, the one responsible for the military upheaval is I, the Emperor.
Ko Yŏksa. No, Your Majesty. The soldiers do not say that. They say there can be no responsibility attributed to the Supreme Ruler of Heaven and Earth (一天萬乘之至尊). They say the powerful minister who clouded Your Majesty's wisdom is the one at fault.
Hyŏnjong. Hmm. Who do they mean?
(Yang Kukch'ung, who has been listening to the exchange a few steps away, is anxious.)
Ko Yŏksa. I hope Your Majesty perceives clearly (洞燭).
Hyŏnjong. In such an urgent situation, is there time to perceive clearly? Speak directly.
Ko Yŏksa. With deepest apologies, Your Majesty, it is Yang Kukch'ung.
Hyŏnjong. (Angrily) What! Have you forgotten that Kukch'ung is the Consort's brother?
Ko Yŏksa. (Calmly) Your servant believes he speaks precisely because he has not forgotten.
Hyŏnjong. Convey my words to the soldiers. Tell them I cannot lose the Consort's brother. Yes. Tell them I cannot lose my close maternal relative (外戚).
(The sound of soldiers fiercely banging their shields is heard. Hyŏnjong hesitates. Chin Hyŏllyŏng enters. An old general, contemporary to Hyŏnjong.)
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Your Majesty, a grave situation has arisen. It is either lose Your Majesty's army, or lose Yang Kukch'ung. It is one of the two.
Hyŏnjong. Convey my words.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. With deepest apologies, Your Majesty, I believe it will not work. The soldiers are rampaging wildly like mad lions. I fear even Your Majesty's words may not enter their ears.
(The sound of shields banging grows louder.)
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. It is like that. Just like that.
Hyŏnjong. A difficult situation. The fate of the Tang dynasty's state (社稷) is also in danger.
Ko Yŏksa. That is not the case, Your Majesty. If only the soldiers' wish is granted, I believe the soldiers will gladly fight for Your Majesty.
Yang Kukch'ung. (Pale and trembling, steps forward) What! What? What are you saying, Ko Yŏksa! Are you telling me to hand me over to the soldiers?
Ko Yŏksa. Yes! It cannot be helped.
Yang Kukch'ung. What, you scoundrel! Hand over me, the Prime Minister of the Right, to the soldiers? Surely His Majesty wouldn't, wouldn't!
Ko Yŏksa. Be silent! Excellency, do you still not understand your responsibility? Even in such times, do you still intend to cause trouble for His Majesty? Know shame! Know shame.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. It is best to make a decision. At this point, all that remains is to choose between you dying cleanly or the soldiers killing you recklessly.
Hyŏnjong. Uh. (Chokes up and covers his face.)
Yang Kukch'ung. (After agonizing greatly) It cannot be helped. Hand me over to the soldiers. Everything is over!
Yang Kwibi. Oh dear, brother. (Clings to him.)<62>
Yang Kukch'ung. Oh, sister. Farewell. Chin Hyŏllyŏng, take me to where the soldiers are.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. You have decided well. Now, with your dignified attitude, make your final path clean. (Yang Kukch'ung and Chin Hyŏllyŏng leave.)
Yang Kwibi. (Clinging to Hyŏnjong) Your Majesty, my pitiful brother, my brother!
Hyŏnjong. Hey. Now that it has come to this, even my power cannot help.
(Hyŏnjong and Yang Kwibi embrace and weep. Suddenly, from below, the sounds of soldiers shouting and clamoring are heard. The sound of shields banging mixes in. Hyŏnjong and Yang Kwibi cover their ears. Ko Yŏksa returns running.)
Ko Yŏksa. Your Majesty!
Hyŏnjong. Oh, has Kukch'ung been killed?
Ko Yŏksa. Yes, Your Majesty.
(Yang Kwibi sobs. The three Ladies on stage also collapse and weep.)
Hyŏnjong. Oh, quickly, prepare for departure. I do not wish to linger for even a moment in such a dreadful place.
Ko Yŏksa. Your Majesty!
Hyŏnjong. What is it?
Ko Yŏksa. The soldiers are still not satisfied.
Hyŏnjong. Hmm! What are they saying?
Ko Yŏksa. They say that the responsible parties are not yet all accounted for.
Hyŏnjong. What! Insolent. What do they take me for? I will go myself. I will go and punish those insolent fellows.
Ko Yŏksa. That might be a futile effort, Your Majesty. The soldiers are genuinely concerned for Your Majesty's well-being and the state (社稷) of the Tang dynasty. Because they are concerned, they are united in their effort to remove the disease afflicting the nation.
Hyŏnjong. Oh, I don't understand any of this! But, who do they say is the responsible party now? Surely not...
(Yang Kwibi gives a start.)
Ko Yŏksa. With deepest apologies, Your Majesty, it is the three Ladies, sisters of Her Highness the Consort.
Hyŏnjong. What.
Yang Kwibi. What!
Hyŏnjong. Why, why are these Ladies guilty? They are mere women. They cannot possibly have done wrong. If they did wrong, then I am at fault. Are the soldiers trying to punish me indirectly by punishing my close relatives?
Ko Yŏksa. Not at all, Your Majesty. They say this: Enfeoffing women in large states is the root cause of national disorder. Rather than asking who enfeoffed them, first the reality... yes. They say they will eliminate the Ladies enfeoffed in large states.
(A threatening, tumultuous roar is heard. Immediately following, the sound of shields banging, as if urging something, arises. Chin Hyŏllyŏng enters.)
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Your Majesty, having seen Yang Kukch'ung's blood, the soldiers have become ferocious like tigers that have tasted blood. Nearly half of them have drawn their swords.<63> If their demands are refused, they are in a state where they might even rush before Your Majesty. Please listen to that. That uproar.
(A tremendous uproar erupts continuously. Cries of "Kill the three Ladies!", "Kill the three Ladies!" rise like a storm.)
Ko Yŏksa. It has finally come to this. If the demand is not met, who knows what kind of bloody act they might commit before Your Majesty.
Hyŏnjong. Ah, I no longer know what would be best to do.
(The three Ladies are weeping uncontrollably.)
The Three Ladies. Oh dear, please save us!
Yang Kwibi. Save you, of course. Such a thing, I will not allow it. Your Majesty, stop them. Your Majesty. Your Majesty.
Hyŏnjong. (Covers his face, silent)...
Yang Kwibi. (Frantically) They must absolutely not be killed. Even if your servant has to trade her life, absolutely, absolutely, I will not let it happen.
Ko Yŏksa. Your Highness the Consort, in this situation, we cannot overcome force. If Your Highness refuses, the soldiers will harm your sisters before your very eyes.
Yang Kwibi. Ah, such. Such. Such cruelty...
Ko Yŏksa. Your Highness the Consort, it is best to persuade your sisters to make their decision. Please do not engage in futile efforts. The fate of the Great Tang Empire hangs in this instant. Nokshan's soldiers are pursuing from behind. If we lose the soldiers' hearts now, not only will the nation perish, but who knows what will become of Your Majesty's person or Your Highness the Consort's person.
Yang Kwibi. (Silently sobs.)
(The three Ladies are led away, almost forcibly, by attendants. Chin Hyŏllyŏng follows behind.)
Hyŏnjong. (Lifting his face) Ah, it would be better to die.
(The fierce shouts and roars of the soldiers... no one on stage dares to speak... Then, a commotion arises from the upper side.)
Attendant D. Messenger! Messenger!
(Runs towards the upper side. The people on stage remain motionless like fossils. Attendant D returns.)
Attendant. Your Majesty. A messenger has come from General Yi.
Hyŏnjong. (Turning his face away) Ugh, such things, I don't know anymore.
Attendant. But, Your Majesty, it's not that you don't know. Nokshan's troops, who had temporarily retreated, have joined with 30,000 reinforcements and are attacking. Our side is fighting hard, but we don't know when or at what time we might have to retreat, so he asks that the imperial palanquin (鳳輦) proceed as quickly as possible, even by a moment.
Ko Yŏksa. Prepare the imperial carriage (御駕)!
(The sound of soldiers shouting loudly is heard, deafeningly. The court ladies who followed the three Ladies return weeping. A little later, a white horse is brought out. The Consort and Hyŏnjong seem reluctant to mount it... The soldiers' shouts grow even louder.)
Ko Yŏksa. Oh, are those fellows still not satisfied?
(Hyŏnjong and Yang Kwibi are also seized with anxiety... Suddenly <64> shouts of "Eliminate Yang Kwibi!", "Kill Yang Kwibi!" rise like a storm.)
Ko Yŏksa. Huh!
Hyŏnjong. Hmm!
(The cries of "Eliminate Yang Kwibi!" come closer. Spears glint among the trees.)
Hyŏnjong. Ah, those fellows intend to commit an outrageous act.
Yang Kwibi. (Clinging to Hyŏnjong) Oh my goodness, Your Majesty!
Hyŏnjong. Do not worry. Even if the sun and moon fall backwards, I will not give you up. As long as the pupils of my eyes are black.
Soldiers' voices. Kill Yang Kwibi!
Another voice. Eliminate the true traitor to the state (國賊)!
Another voice. Kill the worm in the lion's body!
(The gleam of swords and halberds flashes among the trees. Chin Hyŏllyŏng emerges, his face pale.)
Ko Yŏksa. Are the soldiers demanding Her Highness the Consort?
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. (Ignoring the question, to Hyŏnjong) Yes, they are, Your Majesty.
Hyŏnjong. Hmm, kill me first. Kill me first, and then do whatever you want with her. Chin Hyŏllyŏng, take me to the soldiers.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Absolutely not. They are loyal soldiers to Your Majesty. Only the state... .
Yang Kwibi. (Rising resolutely) Ugh, say no more. Chin Hyŏllyŏng, take me to where they are.
Hyŏnjong. (Startled, embraces Yang Kwibi) Oh, what are you saying? Do you think I would let you go? Anyone who tries to take you from me must first crush the back of my neck and kill me.
Ko Yŏksa. Your Majesty, in today's circumstances...
Hyŏnjong. I don't want to hear anything. Do not speak.
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Your Majesty...
Hyŏnjong. Say nothing more. If we are to perish, let us perish. Let us perish embracing this child!
(The cries of "Kill Yang Kwibi!" intensify.)
Ko Yŏksa. Your Majesty!
Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Your Majesty!
Hyŏnjong. (Does not answer.)
Yang Kwibi. (Suddenly trying to break free from Hyŏnjong's grasp) Your Majesty, please, let your servant die!
Hyŏnjong. Hey!
Yang Kwibi. No, Your Majesty. Your servant wants to die. For ten years, Your Majesty has never refused a single wish of your servant; please grant this final wish of your servant. Your servant sincerely wants to die. Truly wants to die.
Hyŏnjong. Why. Why. Why!
Yang Kwibi. Your servant is not saying she wants to die because there is no way out. Since looking at my face in the mirror earlier, I have grown weary of the world. Thinking that from now on, as I age, day by day, year by year, my face will grow uglier, your servant finds it sadder than death. Thinking that I will become an old woman full of wrinkles, pointed at with fingers asking, "Is this the end (未路) of Yang Kwibi?" terrifies me to the point of shuddering.<65> Your Majesty, please let this servant die. Please let me die while I am still even a little beautiful. And please let a beautiful memory of me remain in Your Majesty's heart.
Hyŏnjong. What, what's that? That's just your clever talk. Do you think such words can console my heart?
Yang Kwibi. No. It's not like that. This servant's whole heart, this servant's whole body, desires it. Now, let me die. Your servant has been thinking of dying since looking in the mirror earlier. That opportunity has come so quickly! And such a spectacular death, to die as the consort of an emperor before the Three Armies. As the beauty who captivated the nation (傾國), who moved the Son of Heaven of Great Tang. Oh, could there be another woman who dies such a spectacular death? (The cries of "Kill Yang Kwibi!" are heard.) Ugh, that is the sound praising my death. Ugh, my death will be sung of for ages to come. This beauty of mine will be passed down to later generations. Ugh, Your Majesty, rejoice! Your Majesty's beloved becomes the foremost beauty of the Central Plains. Ugh, there must be no delay! Chin Hyŏllyŏng, take me away!
Hyŏnjong. Consort! Stay still.
Yang Kwibi. No, Your Majesty. Please let me go. This is your servant's final, final wish. Ugh, my revered and beloved Your Majesty. May Your Majesty live forever (聖壽無疆)!
Hyŏnjong. (Now speechless)...
Yang Kwibi. Ugh, Chin Hyŏllyŏng. Please ensure I die beautifully. I hate to die uglily. Ah, Yu Yŏng, I entrusted a silk cloth to you, didn't I?
Yu Yŏng. (Brings it) Here it is.
Yang Kwibi. Oh, I will wrap my body with this, then hang me by the neck over it.
(Yang Kwibi covers her head with it.)
Hyŏnjong. Oh, Yang Kwibi!
Yang Kwibi. Ugh, Your Majesty. Please do not grieve too much for your servant. Your servant dies gladly. And, Your Majesty, please do not forget your servant, ever. Heh heh..., And there is one more thing I wish to say. Even after you return to the capital (還駕), please do not keep overly beautiful people close to you.
Hyŏnjong. Oh, what are you saying! My heart is now completely lost.
Yang Kwibi. Your Majesty, farewell, may you live ten thousand years without end (萬壽無疆)! (To the court ladies) Farewell to you all. Chin Hyŏllyŏng! Show the soldiers how the consort of the Great Tang Empire dies a beautiful and courageous death.
(Yang Kwibi exits, following Chin Hyŏllyŏng and Ko Yŏksa.)
Hyŏnjong. (Watching her go) Oh, someone please support me, I'm going to collapse.

**Summary**

The play depicts the tragic events at Mawei Station during the An Lushan Rebellion. Emperor Xuanzong (Hyŏnjong) and his beloved consort Yang Guifei (Yang Kwibi) flee the capital Chang'an. Stopped at Mawei, the desperate imperial troops demand the execution of Chancellor Yang Guozhong (Yang Kukch'ung), blaming him for the disaster. After he is killed, the soldiers' anger turns to Yang Guifei's sisters and finally to Yang Guifei herself, whom they see as the root cause of the turmoil. Faced with the army's ultimatum and fearing the loss of her beauty with age more than death itself, Yang Guifei persuades the distraught Emperor to let her die, choosing a spectacular end to preserve her image as the peerless beauty who captivated an empire. She is led away to her death by the generals, and the soldiers cheer the Emperor, their demands met.

From a historical perspective, this translation by Yang Paekhwa (Yang Chu-dong) in 1926 is interesting as it presents a classic Chinese historical tragedy to a Korean audience during the colonial period. While ostensibly a historical drama, the themes of misplaced blame, the burden of leadership, mob rule, and the tragic fate of those associated with power could potentially resonate with contemporary concerns, albeit indirectly. The focus on Yang Guifei's conscious choice of death to preserve beauty adds a layer of aesthetic and perhaps existential commentary. For historians, it showcases the cultural interests and literary activities of Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule, specifically the engagement with Chinese classics and the use of contemporary vernacular Korean for translation.
