無名氏傳, A氏略歷
						春園 (Ch'unwŏn)
Nine

There is no reason to recount in detail the fact that Mr. A, possessing political aspirations, formed a political association called the OO Society (OO-hoe) (which, excluding the Independence Club (독립협회), was likely the first patriotic political association in Chosŏn). There may be another opportunity later to discuss the key figures of the OO Society and the general facts concerning that association. Regarding this political association, the reader can only wait until then.

Ten

Leaping forward, the story moves to the summer of the OO year of Kwangmu (광무 OO년).

At a place where comrades of the Hyoch'unghoe (効忠會 - Loyalty Society) were secretly gathered discussing current affairs, a letter arrived. It certainly did not come through the mail. A soldier brought it, gave it to Mr. A, and ran away.

Upon opening and reading the letter, Mr. A's face turned pale, his eyebrows shot up and down, and his breathing grew heavy. Seeing Mr. A's state, everyone present fell silent as if their breath had stopped and their bodies had stiffened all at once.

"It was unofficially decided at today's cabinet meeting to disband the army!"

Mr. A threw the letter down before the group.

The letter had come from within the palace. It seemed to be from someone who had overheard the cabinet meeting, written entirely in vernacular Korean in a court lady's script, and very skillfully recorded the important passages from the ministers' discussions during the meeting—Prime Minister R, Minister of Home Affairs S, Minister of Finance K, Minister of Agriculture, Commerce, and Industry C, Minister of War R, and others.

According to it, Prime Minister R, stating it was the intention of a certain power (모처), forcefully argued that the army absolutely had to be disbanded, even saying that if Korea did not voluntarily disband its army, it would lead to war with Japan.

Regarding this, opinions were divided between proponents and opponents. Ministers like S (Home Affairs), C (Agriculture, Commerce, and Industry), and K (Finance) argued for disbanding the army as demanded by the other side to preserve their positions and rescue the people from devastation and slaughter (도탄, 어육). Others, like Minister of War R, Minister of Education R, Minister of the Imperial Household P, and Elder Statesman K, opposed it, questioning where there was a country without an army and where there were fools who would disband their own army just because someone else told them to. However, eventually, intimidated by the Prime Minister's words and threats (that opponents would face danger not only to their positions but also to their very lives!), they shrunk back like turtles pulling in their necks, one by one, until only two or three stubbornly held out.

Thus, it was decided that a formal meeting before the Emperor (어전회의) would be held the next morning for each minister to sign the royal decree for the army's disbandment. Chief Secretary of the Cabinet H was tasked with drafting the disbandment decree, Minister of Home Affairs S with notifying officials and citizens nationwide, Prime Minister R and Minister C (Agriculture, Commerce, and Industry) with persuading His Majesty, and Minister of War R with informing the Japanese military commander, arranging for Japanese troops to guard key points in the city, disarming the Korean army, and handing over the barracks to the Japanese army—this was the implementation task assigned. This meant that Minister of War R was to summon the commanders of each unit, convey the disbandment order, and oversee the disbandment procedures.

Seeing this letter, all the Hyoch'unghoe members present—Generals B, C, D, E, F, and others—were stunned as if struck by a bolt from the blue.

One person (like Mr. C) declared, flexing his arms,
"Let's give it a fight!"

Another ground his teeth, saying,
"Those bastards—those thieves who are devouring this country!" <58>

Yet another wept hysterically.

Finally, the consensus reached was,
"Let's resist the army disbandment with all our might."

However, among the six or seven members of the Hyoch'unghoe, the only ones actually in positions to command troops were Mr. D, commander of the 2nd Battalion of the Palace Guards (시위 2대대장), and Mr. C, commander of a Provincial Garrison (진위대대장) battalion located not far from Seoul. Mr. A, aide-de-camp to the Minister of War; Mr. B, commandant of the Military Academy (무관학교교장); and Mr. E, commander of a non-existent baggage train (치중대장), were men without even a platoon under their command.

"Alright. Let's see how this goes!"
Mr. C immediately rose from his seat.
"I'm leaving. Who knows if we'll meet again in this life? Any bastard who survives this mess is a son of a bitch!"
He rushed out without even finishing his farewells. He intended to get to his garrison before the disbandment order was issued.

Mr. B decided to seek out the commanders of each unit, Mr. F resolved to visit Minister of Home Affairs S (who was effectively the Deputy Prime Minister) and Minister C (Agriculture, Commerce, and Industry) to argue against the disbandment, and Mr. A promised to see Prime Minister R and Minister of War R to try to prevent the disbandment. They then parted ways.

The despair and fury of these men, who had earnestly strived, saying, "Let's build an ideal army," were beyond description.

Eleven

Mr. A immediately went to Prime Minister R's house but was told he had gone to the palace, so he couldn't meet him. He then proceeded to Minister of War R's house. He was also told the minister had gone to the palace, but since Mr. A was the Minister of War's aide-de-camp, he went into the reception room (사랑) of Minister R's house to wait for his return.

Not long after, the portly Minister of War returned home in a rickshaw, half-drunk. Mr. A greeted R-ssi matter-of-factly, as befitting an aide-de-camp.

"Oh, you're here?"
The Minister of War took off his military cap, resplendent with the gold braid of his army full-dress uniform, and handed it to a nearby servant. Mr. A intercepted the cap, received it, and slammed it down onto the ground. *Thud*.

"My goodness, what is the meaning of this?"
R-ssi seemed to sober up instantly.

"What use is a military cap when the army is about to disappear?"
Mr. A wiped away tears with his fist.
"This cap's crime is sitting atop the head of the Minister of War who intends to disband the army!"
He stomped on the splendid cap with his boot heel and ground it underfoot. The cap crumpled, became covered in mud, and rolled wretchedly under the flowerbed like the army being disbanded.

The Minister of War lowered his head without a word.

"Where did you hear such talk?"
R-ssi asked Mr. A, sitting down on a Western-style reception room chair and wiping sweat from his forehead with his fist. His voice was as weak as that of a guilty person begging for forgiveness.

"Your Excellency!"
Mr. A, discarding the decorum due to a superior officer, gripped the Minister of War's arm tightly.
"Your Excellency! You are a soldier. Even if all the other cabinet ministers are rotten and spineless, Your Excellency cannot act like this. Your Excellency! Oppose it! Say you cannot do it!"

"Why wouldn't I have tried to oppose it?"
Minister of War R lifted his lowered head wearily.
"But everyone says it cannot be helped. What can I do alone?"

"Everyone? You mean Your Excellency opposes it, but the other ministers insist on disbandment?"
At Lieutenant Colonel A's pressing question, R-ssi merely nodded his head a couple of times.

"Is Your Excellency clearly opposed?"

"Yes, of course, I oppose it. Do you think I would possibly agree? But what can be done when the Prime Minister's (首相) mind is made up? The Prime Minister initiated the discussion in the first place. Of course, it might not be his own will. There must be pressure from behind, <59> but since the Prime Minister's intention is set, what can I do? Isn't that so?"
R-ssi continuously wiped sweat from his forehead. The combination of the drink he had at the palace after the meeting—perhaps in celebration?—and the awkwardness caused by Mr. A's confrontation made sweat pour from his forehead and down his spine as if all the water in his body was trying to escape.

"So now, because the Prime Minister's will is bent on disbandment, even though it's not Your Excellency's wish, tomorrow you have taken on the duty of leading the effort and disbanding the army with your own hands? Do you think your neck is so precious that you can't even risk it at a time like this? Do you expect it to last for a thousand or ten thousand years?"

Minister of War R had no reply.

"Even if Your Excellency cannot risk your neck and fiercely argue against it, don't you even have the spirit to kick away the position of Minister of War, which will disappear tomorrow anyway, and step down? How can you have the audacity (렴치) to disband your own army with your own hands and sell off your own position as Minister of War just to keep eating?"

"..."

"Your Excellency! It is not yet too late. Write a statement right here declaring your resolute opposition to the army disbandment!"

"Well, even if I alone stand firm, will it work? What can I do about what the Prime Minister is doing?"

"Then, if the Prime Minister changes his tune like Your Excellency, will Your Excellency oppose it to the very end?"

"Yes, of course," Minister of War R was forced to reply.

"Then, please write me a letter stating that the army disbandment is unacceptable. I will take it and try to change the Prime Minister's mind."

"That won't work."

"Leave the success or failure to me, Your Excellency. Just write one letter."

Overwhelmed by Mr. A's impassioned demeanor and righteous logic, Minister of War R could find no further excuses to evade the request:

"To His Excellency, Prime Minister R,
Disbanding the Empire's army is something I absolutely cannot bear to do. Therefore, I intend to oppose it with my life. I sincerely hope Your Excellency will reconsider. For details, please inquire with Aide-de-camp A.
Month O, Day O, Evening. R respectfully bows."

R wrote this letter partly because he was moved by A's fervor and partly because he was intimidated by A's imposing manner. R felt as though A was emanating a murderous aura, as if he might even kill him.

"I shall return shortly."
A left Minister of War R's house. He intended to go straight to Prime Minister R's residence but thought it necessary to change into formal attire suitable for visiting the Prime Minister and briefly stopped by his own home.

Twelve

After changing clothes at home, he was about to leave by rickshaw when he thought it would be convenient to call first and see if the Prime Minister was in. He called Prime Minister R's house.

The line was busy.

As he tried to call for the third time, Mr. A heard the voice of Minister of War R through the receiver he held to his ear. Startled, Mr. A listened carefully—the lines had crossed.

"A is heading to your residence now with my letter. Please say you are not in and do not meet him."

Such words were heard. It was clearly Minister of War R calling Prime Minister R.

"Then shall I call the Military Police (헌병대) and have this fellow A arrested and locked up?"
This was clearly Prime Minister R.

"There's no need for that. What can he do if I just let him be? It's enough if Your Excellency doesn't meet him."
This was the Minister of War.

A wanted to scream, "You dog-like bastards!" right then and there, but he clenched his teeth, listened to everything, and then hung up the receiver.

"Ah, it's all ruined!"
Mr. A stamped his foot on the floor.

A let out a long, sobbing sound, "Ooh-hoo-hoo-hoo," then suddenly stood up, took out a revolver (륙혈포) from the wall closet, loaded the 12-shot cylinder, checked the mechanism, put it in the back pocket of his military trousers, impeccably dressed himself in the formal uniform of an Army Lieutenant Colonel (륙군참령), and went out in a rickshaw. <60>

**Summary**

The story depicts Lieutenant Colonel A, a member of the patriotic Hyoch'unghoe society, learning through a secret palace letter about the cabinet's decision to disband the Korean army under Japanese pressure in the Kwangmu era (likely 1907). Despite passionate arguments from some ministers, key figures like Prime Minister R and Minister of War R agree to the disbandment. A confronts the Minister of War, extracts a reluctant letter opposing the decision, but then overhears the Minister betraying him in a phone call to the Prime Minister, advising him not to meet A. Realizing the depth of the betrayal and the hopelessness of peaceful persuasion, A arms himself, presumably for violent resistance or retribution.

This installment vividly portrays the internal conflict and despair among Korean officials and military officers facing the imminent dissolution of their nation's armed forces, a pivotal event in the loss of Korean sovereignty. Historians might find interest in the fictionalized depiction of the cabinet meeting dynamics, the differing reactions (resistance, resignation, betrayal) among the Korean elite and military personnel (represented by A, C, and the Ministers), and the atmosphere of helplessness mixed with desperate patriotism leading up to the army's forced disbandment in 1907. The story highlights the moral compromises and pressures faced by individuals under colonial coercion.
