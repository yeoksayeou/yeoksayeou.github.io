Sentiment of Reminiscence
						Kim T'ae-o

Comrade!
Was that time already the old days!
When the warm spring light flowed drowsily
And a gentle breeze rustled in the fields and mountains,
When new sprouts sprang up fluffily on the burnt golden grass,
We cut willows by the stream, made flutes, and played them,
Running up to the back hill—
Lying there with arms spread wide,
Looking up at the pale white sky,
Then rolling about freely again on the grassy field,
Ah! That hill where we ran and played in childhood.

Comrade!
Was that time already the old days!
While strolling through the acacia-thick Yangnim forest,
Smelling the flower fragrance and singing songs, <90>
When the cicadas joined in, weaving melodies together,
We turned back again,
Tore off our upper clothes, dashed down the hot sandy riverbank,
And plunged 'whadak-dak' into the water—
Swimming and making huge ripples,
Splashing water, having water fights, and then—
Sucking in a gulp of water and spitting it out forcefully,
When the seven-colored rainbow stood beautifully,
Didn't we clap our hands and jump?
Ah! That time when we played naked and free.

Comrade!
Was that time already the old days!
Gathering with several friends in the front field,
Playing blind man's bluff—
On a moonlit autumn night when lonely reeds cried and leaves fell rustling,
Didn't we play hide-and-seek under the ginkgo tree until late at night and then return?
Better yet, playing general on hobbyhorses (竹馬),
When I stood at the front and gave fierce commands (呼令) like a tiger,
The foot soldiers (卒兵) following behind matched the military signals (軍號)
And dashed toward the enemy camp like arrows (走),
Where could the exhilarating feeling of that time be compared?
Ah! That field where we played on hobbyhorses.

Comrade! Was that time already the old days!
On a winter day when cotton-like white snowflakes
Poured down incessantly—
Going to school and having snowball fights with many friends,
Returning home and building a snowman with a friend,
Boasting about it to the neighborhood kids,
Or when many friends, grabbing sticks and clubs,
Whatever they could find, vigorously
Ran up the mountain to catch deer and rabbits,
Ah! That Mudŭngsan (無等山) where we ran and played vigorously.

Comrade!
Ah—that time when we grew up like that was already—
A fleeting happiness in the mist—
But our parents, without foresight,
Even loaded up and sent away their paddies and fields,
What could be done? One house, then another, northward—southward—
Clutching chests as if pierced by a dagger (匕首),
When they set off on the endless road of wandering (流浪),
Ah! Comrade, since you too started to leave your cherished hometown back then,
During the long ten years that have passed,
I too, in this thorny field of a lost homeland,
Hardships, adversity, oppression, dissatisfaction, complaints—
All these things, has there been a single day or hour
When they disappeared from my mind...?
Still, compared to your suffering, comrade, battling blizzards
In the unfamiliar foreign land (異域) of North Manchuria (北滿)—Siberia (西伯利亞),
Shall I call my hardship more leisurely?
These days, I too, as one of the unemployed masses (失業群) who have lost their livelihood,
Cannot help but curse (咀呪) this relentless reality.
Since this is a fact you know well, comrade—
It should not be surprising,
But still, experiencing it myself,
Why? Why is my head so heavy and my heart so full?
Oh, but comrade! As a fellow comrade,
Please accept my will. And let us firmly promise the future. To my brother wandering in the North Country.
(1930, 10, 30) <91>

**Summary**

This prose poem by Kim T'ae-o contrasts vivid, idyllic memories of childhood play through the seasons with the harsh realities of the present under Japanese colonial rule. The author reminisces about carefree days spent with a friend ("comrade")—playing in spring fields, swimming in summer rivers, engaging in autumn games, and enjoying winter snow—before shifting dramatically to the pain of displacement, forced migration (due to parents selling land), and the suffering experienced by both the author and the friend, who is now enduring hardship in North Manchuria or Siberia. The author, currently unemployed, laments their shared plight but ends with a call for solidarity and a promise for the future. For historians, the poem offers a poignant personal perspective on the economic pressures leading to migration, the emotional trauma of losing one's home and land, the widespread unemployment, and the sense of shared suffering and nascent resistance among Koreans during the colonial period, particularly the experience of those forced to wander far from Korea.

懷古의 情
						金泰午
동무여!
그때가 발서 옛날이엇구려!
따스한 봄빛이 조으는 듯 흘으고
실바람이 들뫼에 살랑거릴 때
불탓든 금잔디에 새싹이 나풀나풀 돋아 올으면
내ㅅ가에 버들꺽거서 피리맨들어 불며
우리는 뒤ㅅ동산으로 뛰어 올라가-
네 활개 쩍-쩍 벌리고 누어서
히멀건한 하눌을 치어다 보다가
또다시 잔디밭에서 맘대로 딩굴며 놀던
앗다! 어릴적 뛰고 놀던 그 동산말이여요.

동무여!
그때가 발서 옛날이엇구려!
『아까시야』 욱어진 楊林숩속으로 거닐면서
꽃 香氣맡으며 노래부르노라면 ＜90＞
매암이는 덩달어서 가닥으로 어우러질 때
우리는 다시금 발을 돌리어
웃텅을 벗어붙인채 불모래 강변을 내달어
물속으로 와닥닥 뛰어들어가-
헤염치며 크나큰 波紋을 이르키고
물장구치고 물싸홈하고 그리고 또-
물을 한숨에 쑥 드리켯다가 확 내품어 버리면
七色무지개 아름답게 설 때에
우리는 손벽치며 뛰지 안엇는가요
앗다 벌거벗고 자유롭게 놀던 그때 말이여요

동무여!
그때가 발서 옛날이엇구려
앞들에 여러 동무 같이 모여서
소경잡기 내기도 하며-
외로운 갈닢이 울고 나무닢 우스스 떨어지는
어느 달밝은 가을밤에 銀杏나무 밑에서
숨박꼭질하다가 밤이 으슥하면 돌아오지 안엇는가요
그보다도 竹馬타고 大將노름 한다고
앞에 서서 猛虎같은 呼令을 내리면
뒤에 따리든 卒兵들이 軍號를 마쳐
적진을 향해 손살같이 다릴(走) 때
그때의 痛快한 맘이란 어디에 比하리까?
앗다! 竹馬타고 놀던 그때 그 뜰 말이여요

동무여! 그때가 발서 옛날이엇구려!
솜뭉치같은 하-얀 눈송이가
시름없이 퍼붓는 어느 겨울날-
학교에 가서 여러 동무들과 눈싸홈하고
집에 도라오아서 동무와 같이 눈사람 만들어 놓고
이웃집 아이들에게 자랑하던 그때라던지
여러 동무들이 방망이 몽둥이
잡히는 대로 메어들고 씩씩하게
산에 뛰올나 노루며 토끼잡든 그때
앗다! 기운차게 뛰며 놀던 그 無等山말이여요

동무여!
아-옛날의 그러케 자라나던 그때는 발서-
한 때의 안개속 행복이엇구려-
그러나 우리 父母 주견없이
논마지기 밭뙈기마저 떠 실어보내니
어이하랴 한 집 두 집 북으로-남으로-
匕首로 어이는 듯한 가슴을 쥐여짜며
끝없이 流浪의 길을 떠날때
아! 동무도 그적에 情든 故鄕을 둥지기 시작한 후로
十年이란 長久한 歲月이 轉轉하는 그 사이
저 亦是 집잃은 이 땅의 가시벌판에서
파란, 역경, 억압, 불만, 불평
이 모든 것이 한날 한시인들 내 머리에
사라질때 잇사오리까마는...
그래도 낯선 異域 北滿-西伯利亞벌에서
눈보라와 싸우시는 동무의 苦生에 比하면
그보다 餘裕잇는 苦생이라고 할가요?
요즘은 저 역시 삶을 잃은 失業群의 한 사람으로써
이 악착한 現實을 咀呪치 안을 수 없읍니다.
이것은 동무도 잘 아는 事實임으로-
그리 놀나울 것이 없을 터이오나
그래도 제 自身이 當해 놓고 보니
왜? 이리도 머리가 무겁고 가슴이 벅차답니까
오 그러나 동무여! 나를 동지로써의
뜻을 받으소서. 그리고 앞날의 일을 굳이 약속합시다. 北國流浪의 兄에게
(1930, 10, 30) ＜91＞
