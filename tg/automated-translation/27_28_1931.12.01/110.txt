Discussion on Problems Concerning Sex
						Various Contributors

In this great transitional period for social systems, the family is almost in a state of destruction, and sexual chaos has reached its extreme. None other than sexual disorder in male-female interaction, the prostitution of gisaeng, public prostitutes, and private prostitutes, and family tragedies such as divorce and separation are rampant. Although the solution to all these problems cannot be obtained without a fundamental resolution of the social system, we decided to seek the opinions of various individuals on the following two major issues, hoping that raising necessary interest might contribute to solving the problems.

1. Sexual Knowledge and Male-Female Interaction
1. Through what opportunity did you gain knowledge of sex?
2. Based on that experience, how do you think sex education should be conducted?
3. Concrete methods for encouraging male-female interaction.

2. The Ideal New Family
1. If I had the ability to overhaul all systems, how would I change the systems concerning family, marriage, divorce, etc.?
2. The possibility of completely abolishing the Gisaeng system and the reasons thereof. (In order of arrival)

Questions
1. Through what opportunity did you gain knowledge of sex?
2. Based on that experience, how do you think sex education should be conducted?
3. What are concrete methods for encouraging male-female interaction?

Answers <34>

A Distance of Over One *Kan* is Necessary Between Men and Women
Yi Il, Hwimun Higher Common School

1. I cannot recall what opportunity it was.
2. Sex education is unnecessary; warning about the dangers of sex is necessary.
3. Male-female interaction is unnecessary. If absolutely necessary, the concrete guideline should be to maintain a distance of over one *kan* (approx. 1.8 meters) and sit or stand facing each other.

Proper Sex Education
Kim Yun-gyŏng, Paehwa Girls' High School

1. I gradually learned from friends and from adults' conversations.
2. Therefore, parents or educators cannot forever suppress the manifestation of that instinct just by hiding it. Incomplete knowledge picked up piecemeal from others like that can easily lead to the misuse of sexual desire. Thus, it would be best for parents and educators to teach properly and clearly, inform about the dangerous aspects, and advise caution.
3. I think implementing coeducation in elementary schools and schools above the professional school level is good in many respects. Also, it would be good not to distinguish between men and women in formal gatherings. Organizing mixed-gender clubs (sports, travel, hiking, reading...) would be good (with people of noble character).

School is a Sexual Knowledge Exchange Place
Kim Tong-in, Creative Writer

1. 2. In Chosŏn (Korea), where lewd talk and vulgar language are abundant, I believe one relatively early acquires knowledge about sex education without specifically going through a "sex education" process. "School," being a meeting place for children from various family backgrounds, seems to fulfill the duty of a "sexual knowledge exchange place."
3. is too vague (or if not vague, too broad) a question to answer easily.

Good Novels, Good Movies
OOO, Sinsaengsa (New Life Company)

1. Mostly from novels and the "screen," I suppose.
2. Therefore, one must select and watch good novels and good movies.
3. I believe coeducation starting from elementary school would be good.

In a Brothel
Yun Paeng-nam, Novelist

1. I married at the age of 16. However, being of that age, I knew nothing about sexual matters and went to Tokyo that year to study. Then, in the winter when I was 18, I was dragged along by a bad friend (?) <35> and experienced a night in a brothel. This became my first experience of sex in the true sense.
2. Based on the above experience, I believe the influence of such experiences has endless negative effects on youth. For that reason, I think it is perhaps appropriate to provide sex education to a certain extent openly, yet with a solemn attitude, informing them in advance so that they may save themselves from the frightening consequences of bad sexual intercourse; in other words, so they may gain self-control.
3. This is a very difficult problem. It is hard to answer in the given space, but abolishing the separation of seats for men and women in public gatherings, so as not to foster the idea of viewing women as peculiar, implementing coeducation, abolishing the parents' own practice of segregating sexes within the home—perhaps this is a problem to be discussed only after implementing these fundamental issues.

Social Clubs
Kim Ch’ang-je, Ihwa Chŏnmun (Ewha College)

1. Knowing about sex and having sexual knowledge must be different things. Therefore, my sexual knowledge is not practical but conceptual, which I naturally obtained through books, especially from sections on reproductive organs in physiology, and other books discussing sex education.
2. Based on my experience, books seem necessary, but this applies to adults. For young men and women in the bloom of youth, it might be extremely difficult to find appropriate books. If not careful, they could instead become material for provoking sexual desire. I agree with the theory that the most suitable person for sex education is the parent, especially the mother.
3. Coeducation might be the foremost method, and as other social facilities, having social clubs would perhaps be good.

At a Private Village School (*Sasuk*)
Yang Kŏn-sik, Novelist

1. I happened to acquire it while learning writing at a private village school (*sasuk*). It lies in the establishment of institutions, but before coeducation is realized, I doubt particularly good results will appear.
2. Solemnly, without provoking curiosity, at a necessary time, hint at male-female relationships using examples from plants and animals in simple terms, making them realize that sex is not something to be trifled with.
3. Noble entertainment where men and women can gather and play together is still lacking.

Astonishment Beyond Astonishment
Kim An-sŏ, Poet

1. Having heard that people come out from having their bellies cut open, I was astonished once again when I heard the secret from a friend who was the class leader at the village school that it was not so. For a young person, it was truly astonishment beyond astonishment. By the way, sexual knowledge doesn't mean sexual technique, does it?
2. It is something one naturally cannot help but know, and if it will be known naturally through instinct, then truly, more than necessary sex education should not be done, but I believe it is natural to teach according to place and time. <36>
3. Ah, I do not view male-female interaction as something dangerous, but since this is ultimately a matter of culture and character, leaving their interaction as it is as much as possible is also not bad, I think. And if, severely, they might even develop immunity, that too is an unavoidable trace, I believe.

Just a Waste of Tram Fare
OOO, Chosŏn Ilbosa (Chosŏn Daily News Company)

1. I gained knowledge about sex quite late, after becoming an adult. Since there were absolutely no sisters in my family and I was an only child, there was no reason for female friends of sisters to exist, and I grew up simply without any interest in that area. When I was 19, after finishing middle school and while staying in Beijing, I came to know a certain woman and, over several months, learned the mysterious power (?) of sex. After that, I entered society, engaged in activities, met women from various fields, and discovered various types of personalities, but can that even be named knowledge?
2. Based on my experience, when first encountering women, I was drawn by excessive curiosity and temptation, but it is a fact that it gradually becomes commonplace and indifferent. Therefore, regarding the issue of sex education, it seems best to implement coeducation from elementary school onwards, allowing gradual understanding of each other as they grow, mitigating the sudden temptation arising from abrupt discovery, and removing the harm of ruining one's entire life due to temporary excitement.
3. Is there really a need to encourage male-female interaction? I don't know about doing it unavoidably for business reasons. Usually, even when men and women gather for business reasons, they invite suspicion from third parties, and they often engage in behavior deserving of such suspicion in business contexts too. There are people who voluntarily engage in male-female interaction, but eventually, through frequent contact, circumstances that cannot be guaranteed might arise. Unlike interaction between men, it's easy to incur expenses, and more time is wasted, so there's no particular benefit. If you accompany a woman, even if you are not that close, you often end up naturally paying for unnecessary expenses like transportation and food, so isn't it a loss? Haha... In short, there is no need to establish methods for encouragement.

A Wise Mother and Books
Chŏn Yŏng-t’aek, Pastor

1. I don't remember well.
2. I don't have any thoughts worth mentioning properly, but perhaps using books is the best method. And perhaps the education of a wise mother is necessary. Hearing from immature friends is dangerous.
3. Gather the grown children of close families and occasionally let them play together (for example, inviting friends of both sexes for a birthday celebration). And according to their interests, whether music or literature, form "clubs" for them to gather, creating opportunities for men and women to meet, but teachers and seniors should supervise and guide them well. However, the fundamental policy should be coeducation starting from common school (elementary school).

Breaking Down the Sense of Shame
Yang Chu-dong, Sungsil Chŏnmun (Soongsil College)

1. Needless to say about the natural knowledge arising from physiological facts, the time I began to acquire somewhat theoretical and concrete knowledge beyond that was when I read books concerning sex in Tokyo (around age 20). I read 5 or 6 miscellaneous sex books circulating in the market, serially read a magazine called "Abnormal Sexual Desire" (變態性慾), and skimmed part of Ellis's Complete Works on Sexology. Since popular miscellaneous works often accompanied sensational writing styles, I stopped and read several specialized medical books, which were very beneficial.
2. It seems good to actually include sex education as a subject for upper-level students in middle-school-level institutions, from a purely scientific and biological viewpoint. <37>
In families with children in puberty, the parents should provide appropriate guidance. However, to do so, it would first be necessary to break down the Korean people's conception of sex – the excessive sense of shame, the overly mystifying tendency, the taboo attitude, etc.
One thing to particularly emphasize in sex education is the harmful effects of masturbation. Germany can serve as our model in that regard.
3. Implementing coeducation is, above all, the prerequisite condition. Many people speak of premature timing, citing harm to public morals, but the reality will likely be less than imagined, and even if there are some problems, they are merely temporary sacrifices for long-term future development, so I think there is nothing society needs to fear.
What I mentioned in points 2 and 3 above are all merely idealistic arguments about "how things should be." In fact, the implementation and spread of sex education, freedom of male-female interaction, abolition of taboo concepts regarding sex, etc., are all closely related to a series of social and economic facts. Therefore, without investigating them based on the premise of the transition, development, and change of those fundamental facts, discussing them would be nothing more than fictitious discourse. However, since such fundamental theory falls outside the scope of your company's inquiry, I omit it and just add this one note.

Sex Education Books are Necessary
Yu Hyŏng-gi, Sinsaengsa (New Life Company)

1. When I was working at a certain foreign hospital, I occasionally looked through the doctor's "Sexology" books in my spare time and got some grasp of the facts about sex. Even now, I intermittently read books by experienced people on sex.
2. Sex education must be done scientifically. First of all (僞善 - likely intended as 우선), sex education books are urgently needed for us.
3. Male-female interaction does not need special encouragement. The issue now is what kind of male-female interaction. For the pure interaction of unmarried men and women, the church is the best place. I oppose interaction between unmarried and married people from a moral and spiritual standpoint.

Passive Education is Necessary
Yi Chŏng-sŏp, Critic

I will answer your company's "mental test" simply. I would consider it fortunate if I could perhaps get a passing mark.
1. When I was attending middle school, there was an instance where I met a girl of 16 or 17 with classic beauty at almost the same place every morning. Whenever our eyes met on the way to school, I realized a kind of energy was formed that sought to join and harmonize that girl and me. This was one opportunity when I began to acquire knowledge of sex.
2. Sex is seen as the manifestation of animal instinct, or more specifically, the sum total of the act of intercourse between opposite sexes or the desire for intercourse, and the friendship between opposite sexes centered on (or starting from) it. Therefore, for youth with little self-control and judgment, a generally passive educational policy should be adopted.
3. Concrete methods for encouraging male-female interaction, you ask? Even Westerners are rarely seen encouraging interaction between the sexes for their children. Using appropriate opportunities is often seen. However, I believe there might be one situation where encouraging male-female interaction is warranted. Generally, widowers have eccentric personalities and are very weak in their ability to harmonize with others. Consequently, widower politics, where exiles gather, forming three factions among three people, is one reason for this. For such people, it seems best to get even a temporary wife if possible. During the time when there was much factional strife among friends staying in Shanghai for a while, if only there had been more women, that fighting might have been somewhat mitigated. <38>

**Summary**

This 1931 survey in *Donggwang* magazine explores the opinions of various Korean intellectuals (writers, educators, journalists, clergy) on sex knowledge, sex education, and male-female interaction during a period described as sexually chaotic. Responses vary widely: some advocate for open, scientific sex education starting young (often linked with coeducation) to prevent harm from ignorance or misinformation gained illicitly; others deem formal education unnecessary or even dangerous for youth, preferring parental guidance or simple warnings. Views on encouraging male-female interaction range from outright opposition or caution (emphasizing distance or strict supervision) to advocating for coeducation and social clubs as ways to normalize interactions and reduce harmful curiosity or taboo. Several respondents trace their own sexual knowledge to informal sources like peers, books, or even negative experiences like visiting a brothel, highlighting the lack of formal channels. The discussions touch upon societal issues like prostitution and the need to change traditional views characterized by excessive shame and mystification surrounding sex.

From a historical perspective, this article is valuable as it reveals the complex and often contradictory attitudes towards sex and gender relations among educated Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. It showcases the tension between traditional Confucian norms (separation of sexes, parental authority) and modern ideas (scientific education, coeducation, freer social interaction) influenced by Western and Japanese thought. The call for coeducation by multiple respondents, despite concerns about "public morals," indicates a progressive desire for change in gender dynamics. The frank discussion of acquiring sexual knowledge, including negative experiences, and the mention of specific issues like masturbation and the *gisaeng* system, provide insight into the social anxieties and reformist aspirations of the time. Historians can find this useful for understanding the intellectual discourse on modernity, social problems, and changing gender roles in colonial Korea.
