Article Title: 性에 關한 問題의 討論(其二), 理想的 家庭制 妓生撤廢 (Discussion on Issues Related to Sex (Part 2), Ideal Family System, Abolition of Kisaeng)
Author: 諸氏 (Various Contributors)

Discussion on Issues Related to Sex
						Various Contributors

Questions
1. If I had the ability to reform all systems, how would I change the systems related to family, marriage, and divorce?
2. The pros and cons of abolishing the Kisaeng system and the reasons thereof.

Answers

Family-Centered Democracy
Hyŏpsŏng Silŏp, Kim Yŏ-sik

1. To constructively and ideally build our families, which are in a state of ruin due to the evils of traditional customs, the clash of old and new ideas, and the instability of daily life, it is too premature and superficial to discuss any reform before achieving gender equality, breaking down class barriers, and securing industrial stability. However, if, as this question suggests, I had the ability to reform all these systems:
For the Family, I would make the current complex and strict system simpler and more "democratic," so that men, weary from work, could truly find rest and solace, and it would become a place for the harmonious gathering of other family members. The family should not be solely the kingdom of the head of household and the housewife but should be centered around the entire family, living and enjoying life together. For Marriage, while respecting the opinions and wishes of the parties involved <39> the most, I would ensure that young men and women, still lacking experience and knowledge, consider the guidance of parents or seniors. I would provide ample convenience for choosing a suitable spouse based on physiological, spiritual, and rational compatibility, allowing for a natural and rational union unbound by vanity and ceremony.
For Divorce, regarding those already married, separating couples where the wife is considered old-fashioned and ignorant, leaving many with nowhere to go, is not just a moral issue but severely disrupts the current system and casts a dark shadow over the children's future, which is unbearable. Therefore, we should rather endure sacrificially, and since future children will have free marriages, both parties can assert equal rights, thus permitting divorce freely. Divorce should never be based on temporary emotions but should occur only when the possibility of reconciliation or compromise between both parties is completely nonexistent. However, it must not be forgotten that it should be carried out within limits that do not harm the upbringing of any children born from the union.
2. The Kisaeng system must be abolished. Some argue that Kisaeng are not unique to the East, resembling the "hetairai" of ancient Greece and similar to modern "dancing girls," and that in our difficult circumstances, we often find solace in Kisaeng. However, regardless of whether they are called *ye妓* (藝妓) or anything else, the term Kisaeng immediately brings to mind something base, and they are individuals who offer no benefit to our lives. In this era of cultural advancement and developing class consciousness, the existence of this feudal relic, exclusively for the male-centered era, cannot be tolerated.

Abolition of Kisaeng and the Artification of the Home
Sungsil Chŏnmun, Ch'ae P'il-gŭn

1. If I had the ability to reform all systems, I would strive to make family life harmonious, peaceful, and pure. Firstly, I would aim for a small family system as much as possible. Marriage should occur freely after both men and women reach a suitable age. Regarding divorce, I would entrust the decision to the mutual agreement of both parties based on a solid moral standpoint.
2. Regarding the Kisaeng system, I know some may argue that Kisaeng, being artists unlike prostitutes (娼婦), should not be abolished. However, I believe this is an excessively formalistic argument. In reality, when considering issues of national health and social discipline, they often pose a greater danger than prostitutes in our society's current state. In this sense, I believe abolition is appropriate. If one were to try to reform such a system to be harmless and effective for the populace, I think it would be more difficult than each person making their own home artistic and a place of entertainment.

Marriage is Freedom
Chosŏn Ilbosa, Yi Sŏn-gŭn

1.(A) Family: A family centered around the couple, for the children.
(B) Marriage: Freedom.
(C) Divorce: In unavoidable circumstances, it should be done cautiously, fully considering the other party's personality and livelihood issues.
2. Ideally, of course, it must be abolished. However, implementation is the issue under the current social system.

Kisaeng Must Absolutely Be Abolished
Lawyer Yi In

You ask about all sorts of things. If I could do as I please:
1. Regarding food and clothing, I wish these were not prepared within the home, and that children aged at least 25 or 26 lived separately.
Marriage and divorce should be left to free will, but instead, I wish the party initiating the divorce would provide a considerable portion of assets to the other party.
2. Kisaeng must absolutely be abolished. They are toxic to public morals and hygiene, <40> destroy individual families, and their practice of selling chastity is detestable and must be prevented.

Discarding the Concept of Slavery
Artist An Sŏk-chu

1. A family composed of people capable of production, a family that has abandoned the concept of viewing women as slaves, free marriage, and divorce based on mutual consent.
2. Under the current social system, I can only observe for now.

Kisaeng Abolition is Right
Iwha Chŏnmun, Miss Yun Sŏng-dŏk

1. The premise "If I had the ability to reform all systems" is impossible for me, and besides, I have never deeply considered this issue in concrete terms.
2. I believe abolition is right, for the sake of creating a bright society of equal personhood.

A Society Without a Marriage System
Novelist Hyŏn Chin-gŏn

1. Judging by the preface to your question, you seem to view the family as an institution of solace, but I consider the family to be a laboratory for the hardships of life. No matter how the system changes, this hardship seems likely to remain as hardship. If I had the ability, it would be a society without systems for family, marriage, and divorce.
2. This question seems quite commonplace, yet perhaps also ingenious? However, it seems like an offshoot of question 1. When viewing the family as an institution of solace for men, the Kisaeng system would similarly be necessary. In modern bourgeois society, the family where one woman serves one man for lifelong prostitution can be seen alongside this temporary prostitution. Whether it's right or wrong is beside the point; something similar, this temporary prostitution, will naturally disappear when the male-dominated kingdom collapses.

Economic Independence Between Spouses
P'yŏngyang, Madam Yŏ Sun-ok

1. Family: Abolition of the extended family system. Economic independence between spouses. Consequently, personal equality between the two. Child education and general affairs concerning food and clothing require social facilities.
Marriage: Free marriage. Consequently, free divorce.
2. The issue of Kisaeng abolition seems like an armchair debate (卓上公論) before reforming the social conditions and foundations of their existence. It is like the fantasy of abolishing the public prostitution system (公娼制度) based on vague issues of public morals and humanity, without examining it from a social policy perspective. Even if Kisaeng were abolished, it is inevitable that second and third versions of Kisaeng would appear under different names. In any case, the question is, "What made that woman that way?"
Of course, the social harms are many. <41>

Respect for Free Will
Taegu, Sŏ Sang-il

1. Regarding the question, shouldn't the fundamental issue be resolved based on the standard of ethics and morality? Eastern ethics and morality were based on filial piety (孝), while Western ethics and morality were based on love (愛), which seems to be why the systems of family, marriage, and divorce, the fundamental norms of human life, differ between East and West. If we explore these systems, each likely has its strengths and weaknesses, but as we are in the process of reforming the old and adopting the new (革舊取新), in my humble opinion:
(1) The family system based strictly on monogamy (一夫一妻制) is likely the most rational.
(2) Marriage should respect the free will of both parties, but be conditional upon parental judgment (鑑定). The reason is that among the three major events in life, birth and death are left to the dominion of nature, but marriage alone lies within human will. Therefore, for marriage, which is the greatest concern determining one's lifelong fortune or misfortune, free will must be respected. At the same time, however, it is extremely difficult to guarantee that the perceptive ability (認識力) of children who have just completed secondary education is so penetrating (透徹) that they can choose their spouse well and not err in their future path. Isn't this why parental judgment should be a necessary condition? But ultimately, this is likely a matter of educational level.
(3) Divorce should be decided according to the moral law (道德律) of that society. Just as marriage involves the freedom of both parties, divorce also seems conditioned on the freedom of both parties. Just as marriage cannot favor the free will of only one party, divorce cannot tolerate the coercion of one party's free will. The divorce lawsuits currently prevalent in Chosŏn seem to require much consideration. In conclusion, unless we rely on the moral standard and educational level of the society, what benefit is there in applying even the best system?
2. Regarding this question, when the fundamental issues of the first question are resolved in the highest, most ideal manner, this will disappear on its own without needing abolition (不廢而自廢). In the transitional period (過渡期) before that, it may be a topic for theoretical discussion, but as a practical matter, the road ahead seems long. This is because it is a by-product system (副産制度) of a society where the first question has not been ideally realized. Therefore, to abolish the undesirable Kisaeng system, the first step should be the swift establishment of good and noble systems for family, marriage, and divorce. If wives accompany husbands to garden parties (園遊會) and banquets, the problem inherently presupposes its resolution.

Even from the Kisaeng's Perspective, Abolition is Right
Kyemyŏng Kurakpu, Paek Nam-gyu

1. In my household, idleness (遊逸) is absolutely forbidden for anyone; everyone does their respective work. Those with occupations, naturally, but even children run errands and earn money for their school fees (學費). Studying students are judged by their academic performance (成績), instilling deeply the notion that they can eat and dress only after fulfilling their duty of studying. This might not be about the family system itself, but it's something I think is necessary for Korean families and something I practice.
Marriage, of course, should principally be based on the free will of the parties involved, but since young people lack experience, it seems necessary to consider the calm opinions of parents and close friends.
Divorce, personally, I am absolutely against. Some might find me stubborn (頑固), but I believe marriage is a sacred union, and its sanctity is inviolable (不可侵). If a wife is lacking, I believe it is right to guide and teach her, living together.
2. Abolishing Kisaeng is right. Looking at the Kisaeng themselves or the men <42> who follow them, both parties suffer personal ruin, and there are many aspects that corrupt society. Moreover, in the context of Chosŏn, such immoral and hedonistic phenomena should not exist.
The few points above are not particularly novel, but as they are things I am actually practicing, I mention them.

Marriage to Be Undertaken Cautiously
Kimch'ŏn, Madam Hwang Hyŏn-sun

1. Family - I consider the family based on the husband-wife system to be close to the ideal. (In the family [extended] system, it's common for those other than the one managing the household to lack a sense of responsibility for finances and other matters.) Marriage - I believe one should marry only after gaining mutual confidence through careful observation and cool judgment during a deep relationship that transcends mere affection (情), assuring they can help each other progress. Divorce - When, due to irresistible force (不可抗力), it becomes impossible to continue together no matter what, divorce can occur through mutual consultation, but if there are children, I believe sacrificing everything for them is a noble act.
2. If a nation of perfect health and beauty, imaginable only with closed eyes, were fully realized on earth, the Kisaeng system would neither exist nor be necessary. However, in the current emerging reality, it is not only extremely difficult but I believe far more numerous harms would arise [from sudden abolition].

Marriage by Mutual Consent
Hamhŭng, Yi Sun-gi

1. Family, Marriage, Divorce, etc., seem relatively unconstrained under the current system. If I had thorough ideals and financial means, I could form an ideal family or have a marriage of mutual consent. Regarding divorce, it requires utmost consideration (12分의 考慮).
2. I advocate for the abolition of the Kisaeng system. The reason is that Kisaeng are a type of plaything (玩弄物) for men. In today's era of gender equality, there is no reason for women to have their rights trampled (女權蹂躪) after being sold for a small amount of money. Furthermore, it is mutually acknowledged that countless bankruptcies and delinquents arise from this system.

Beyond Companionate Marriage
Iwha Chŏnmun, Pae Sang-ha

1. Family... The family as an extension of society, that is, a family constituted with society as its entire purpose.
Marriage... Something slightly more developed than companionate marriage (友愛結婚).
Divorce... Like that in the "Soviet."
2. When bourgeois society collapses, their institutions of pleasure (the Kisaeng system being one of them) will also disappear. Bourgeois society must collapse. Therefore, the Kisaeng system must be abolished. However, the Kisaeng system is a profession that supports the livelihood of Kisaeng. How will they live without a profession? This is a groundless worry (杞憂). The new society prepares countless new jobs. As long as bourgeois society continues to exist, the Kisaeng issue is not even an issue, just a minor problem.

As Fellow Humans
P'yŏngyang, Madam Cho Sin-sŏng

1. Marriage and divorce must inevitably be left to the will of the parties involved. In other words, they should be left to absolute freedom, but if moral conditions that humans must observe are violated, there should be strict restraint without leniency (假借없어).
2. Kisaeng, of course, must be eliminated. This is because, as fellow humans, they merely serve as entertainment tools (娛樂器具) for vagrants (浮浪輩). <43>

**Summary**

This 1931 survey in *Tongkwang* magazine gathers diverse opinions from Korean intellectuals, professionals, and students (including women) on reforming family, marriage, and divorce systems, and abolishing the *kisaeng* (female entertainer/courtesan) system. Views on family range from maintaining traditional structures with democratic adjustments to advocating smaller, couple-centric units with economic independence for spouses. Marriage opinions span from respecting free will conditional on parental guidance to absolute free marriage. Divorce is debated, with some advocating freedom based on mutual consent (especially for future generations) while others prioritize family stability, children's welfare, or oppose it altogether. There is near-unanimous agreement that the *kisaeng* system should be abolished, citing reasons like its feudal nature, moral corruption, social harm, violation of women's rights, and incompatibility with modern ideals of equality, though some note the practical difficulty of abolition without addressing underlying societal structures or providing alternative livelihoods.

For historians, this article offers a valuable snapshot of social thought and reform debates among the Korean elite during the Japanese colonial period. It reveals the tension between traditional Confucian values and modern Western ideas regarding gender roles, individual autonomy, and social structures. The inclusion of female respondents (Yun Sŏng-dŏk, Yŏ Sun-ok, Hwang Hyŏn-sun, Cho Sin-sŏng, Pae Sang-ha) provides insight into women's perspectives on these critical issues. The near-universal condemnation of the *kisaeng* system, albeit for varied reasons (morality, equality, social health, anti-feudalism), reflects a shared desire for social modernization, while the differing approaches to family reform highlight the complexities of navigating cultural change under colonial constraints. The references to "bourgeois society" and the "Soviet" model by some respondents also indicate the influence of global political and social ideologies.

問題
1.만일 내게 모든 制度를 뜨더 고칠 能力이 잇다 하면 家庭, 結婚, 離婚 等에 關한 制度를 어떠케 할까.

回答
家族本位의 데모크라씨
