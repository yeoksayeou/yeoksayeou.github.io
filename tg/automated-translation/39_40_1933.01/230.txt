Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Waiting
					Min T'aek

Because moonlit nights make it easy for people to see,
Hearing my lover’s words to come on a cloudy night,
I looked up at the sky where patchy clouds drifted,
Praying and wishing for the hateful moon to be hidden.

My lover said they’d come out when the crescent moon set,
But even when the dawn rooster crows, they don’t appear.
Last time, they said they couldn’t come because they dozed off;
Did they fall into that light sleep again this time?

Weaving hemp cloth during the day, sewing at night <56>
Hey you! When you go over there, break off a reed leaf for me.
Break it off and make a pipe to play!
Lying here in this warm grassy field,
I want to listen to the sound of your pipe. <57>

**Summary**

This poem, presented as a folk song or the words of a country farmhand, expresses the speaker's frustration and longing while waiting for a lover who fails to appear, despite arranging to meet on a cloudy night to avoid detection. The speaker recalls a previous excuse of drowsiness and wonders if it has happened again. The latter part shifts tone, addressing a companion and requesting they make and play a reed pipe, expressing a desire for simple pastoral comfort. From a historical perspective, the poem offers a glimpse into rural sentiments and language during the colonial period. Its portrayal of a common person's (a farmhand's) emotions—disappointment in love contrasted with a simple desire for music in nature—and its use of older vernacular Korean spellings provide valuable insights into the everyday lives and cultural expressions of ordinary Koreans under Japanese rule.

기다림
					閔澤
달밤은 사람들이 보기 쉽다고
흐린 날 밤 오라는 임의 말 듣고
조박 구름 감도는 하늘을 보며
미운달 가리기를 빌고 바랏소

초생달 지면은 나온다든 임
새벽닭이 울어도 아니 나오네
저즘에는 졸기에 못 왓다드니
이번에도 뇌약잠 또 들어섯나

낮에는 뵈짜고 밤에 바누질 ＜56＞
이 사람아! 저 갈 때 닢을 꺾어오게
꺾어다 피리를 만들어 불게!
나는 이 따뜻한 풀밭에 누어
자네의 피리 소리가 듣고 싶네. ＜57＞
