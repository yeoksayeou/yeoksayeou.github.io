Sorrowful Kangnam
					Kim Man-jo

On the night of Ch’usŏk when the moon rises bright,
My longing for Kangnam, like a picture, grows deeper still.
On this moonlit night, the lady of Kangnam
Must be singing a song, yearning for her beloved.

To travel to sorrowful Kangnam and back,
From here, it is countless tens of thousands of ri.
When will I ever see the lady I long for?
On this moonlit night, the longing is deeper still.

Don't just long for faraway Kangnam,
Pluck a willow leaf and make it a letter.
Then the lady in Kangnam will see it
And return to find you.

Plucking a willow leaf, I make it a letter,
And spend the sorrowful moonlit night alone.
Playing a pipe to welcome you,
I spend this moonlit night alone. <57>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Man-jo expresses a deep sense of longing for a distant place called Kangnam and a lady residing there. The yearning is particularly acute on the night of Ch'usŏk (Korean harvest moon festival). The speaker laments the vast distance separating them and contemplates sending a message on a willow leaf, hoping the lady will return. The poem ends on a melancholic note, with the speaker alone on the moonlit night, playing a pipe in anticipation. From a historical perspective, the poem reflects common themes of separation, longing, and displacement potentially resonant during the colonial period. The reference to Kangnam, traditionally associated with a region in China but also used symbolically, could represent a yearned-for ideal, a lost home, or simply a distant, unattainable place, adding layers to the interpretation within the context of 1930s Korea.
