**Body Text Translation:**

Can Life Be Seen? A Biologist's View of Life
Bachelor of Science Kim Ho-jik (金浩稙)

Is Activity Force Life?

Kap: "Hey, is this person doing well? I came by to visit."

Ŭl: "Well, what brings you here? It's been a long time. Please, sit down. The moon is bright and the wind is cool; even you, who likes to stay indoors, couldn't resist, it seems."

Kap: "My friend, don't even ask. I don't know if it's the times, but for some reason, many questions keep coming and going in my head. Among them, there's one problem that, no matter how much I think about it, I just can't solve: 'What is life?' This question keeps me awake at night these days. So, since you're a biologist, I trust you must have studied life deeply. Please tell me something to clear my mind."

Ŭl: "Well, let's talk while having a cup of bitter tea. My friend, that's truly a question both easy and difficult. 'What is life?' I certainly can't give you a complete answer, but if you're asking how a biologist views life, I can tell you some of my thoughts."

Kap: "Well, whether it's a biologist's view or a naturalist's view, isn't it enough if it can be explained? But, my friend, maybe I'm wrong, but when I think about it, soil and stones don't move, while animals and plants do. So, could it be that the moving thing, that is, the activity force, is life?"

Ŭl: "Your thinking seems plausible, but you still don't know one thing. In a strict sense, we cannot distinguish between living and non-living things based on whether they move or not. Why? Because if any substance is broken down into extremely fine particles (微粒), so-called 'Brownian motion' (부라운氏 운동) (molecular motion) occurs, where they collide and repel each other. The movement of these fine powders can easily be seen with a microscope using special equipment. If we delve deeper and consider the movement of electrons constituting matter, it's a fact revealed through various experiments and experiences that negative electrons orbit around positive electrons at a fixed speed along a fixed path (規道), and if that arrangement and path change, the substance transforms into something entirely different.<52> Therefore, we cannot define life based solely on the presence or absence of motive force (運動力 如何)."

Kap: "But, my friend. I don't know the details, but the activity of life feels somehow... different from the 'Brownian motion' or electron movement you just mentioned. Am I thinking incorrectly?"

Ŭl: "No, of course, it can be different depending on the viewpoint. One could say 'Brownian motion' lacks a fixed direction and purpose, and thus control, while life's movement has purpose and control. From a value perspective, there's a clear difference. However, when looking objectively at movement itself, a fundamental difference cannot be found. Isn't it more reasonable to view life's movement as merely a form of movement that is somewhat more developed and complex than 'Brownian motion', possessing control amidst complexity?"

Can Life Be Seen?

Kap: "Perhaps another foolish question, but living things grow, while non-living things look the same day after day. So maybe the 'growth force' (生長力) is a special power, and that is life?"

Ŭl: "My friend, you create too many forces. Activity force, growth force... trying to constantly create such special forces, as you think, is a mistake many philosophers make. Growth! Objectively viewed, this too is not a phenomenon unique to living things. Who would argue that a crystal growing larger in its mother liquor (結晶母液) is not growth? Of course, there might be many differences in degree and method, but therefore, growth force itself is also not particularly special."

Kap: "Now that I hear that, none of my thoughts seem correct. I feel like I understand life, yet I'm being drawn deeper into a maze. Thanks to you, my head is completely muddled. Now, I have no choice but to wait for your explanation."

Ŭl: "Why surrender so quickly? A scientist's words lack embellishment and are direct, so they might be a bit hard to hear, but it's my inherent nature, so I can't help it. Then, I'll try to speak as simply and clearly as possible. Let's start by talking about things that are as concrete as possible, things we can see with our eyes and touch with our hands."

Kap: "Wait a moment. What does that mean? I thought life was something that couldn't be seen with the eyes or touched with the hands. Are you saying that the life of you biologists is different from mine?"

Ŭl: "My friend, can the life of a living being exist apart from its body? In other words, does your life occasionally leave your body and wander around as it pleases?"

Kap: "Hey, you! There's a limit to teasing! Who would think like that? Just start the story."

Ŭl: "No, if you think your way, doesn't it logically follow? What I mean by the concrete object of life is the living organism itself. In other words, if we fully understand the living organism, which is the source of life, then life itself can naturally be understood (解得). That is my main point (主志), and I intend to speak from that perspective."

The Material Basis of Life

Kap: "So you mean you'll talk about the material basis of life."<53>

Ŭl: "Exactly, yes. If we take protoplasm (原形質) (cells), considered the source of life, as purely as possible and analyze it, it's nothing special. Carbon, oxygen, hydrogen, and nitrogen, commonly found in the inanimate world, are its main components. Besides these, several other elements like sulfur, potassium, calcium [石灰, lit. limestone, likely meant Calcium], sodium [曹達, soda], iron, etc., make up various proteins. Protoplasm is composed of these proteins, so this protoplasm can be called the vessel containing life."

Kap: "Wait. Let me ask another thing. What is protoplasm (原形質)?"

Ŭl: "Today, I don't have time to explain protoplasm in detail. However, to the extent that it won't greatly hinder understanding the main point, let's say, if you cut a very thin slice of tree bark or human flesh and look at it under a microscope, you can see countless small compartments (小區分). These small compartments are called cells (細胞), and they are the units of structure and function in living organisms. Generally, the substance that serves as the unit of composition and function, even without distinct boundaries (區劃), is called protoplasm (原形質)."

Kap: "I understand a bit better now. Please continue."

Ŭl: "Then, returning to the main topic, once a living organism is analyzed and broken down into its constituent molecules, life is already gone, and only the vessel that contained it remains. Consequently, one comes to think that life is ultimately a phenomenon and a state. Therefore, statements like 'protein is protoplasm,' 'it's a colloid,' 'it's made of various elements,' etc., are all merely attributes (屬性) of life, and we can guess they are by no means the direct cause of life. Thus, delving into the substance and studying the material existence or properties inherent to the phenomenon of life might be the path to understanding it."

What is Life?

Kap: "So you mean you'll talk about the concrete phenomena of life."

乙: "Exactly. When speaking of the phenomena of life, there are three inseparable functions. 1. Metabolism (新陳代謝), 2. Reproduction (生殖作用), 3. Contractility/Irritability (收縮性). Following the order, let's first talk about metabolism. For a living thing to live, meaning to have life, signifies that the living thing continues its activities according to its nature. As the living thing acts, parts of itself are consumed. To replenish this or to use for the formation (造成) of other parts, the living thing must inevitably take in nutrients from the outside. Simultaneously, useless substances generated within the body due to activity must be expelled (搬出) outside the body. This process of taking in new substances and expelling waste products is called metabolism. This can be seen as the most fundamental material phenomenon of life in living beings."

Kap: "Ha, ha. So, people heading to the dinner table according to the season are doing so to ingest new substances, I see. That makes sense. But the expulsion of waste materials... I can't quite grasp that. Please explain before moving on."

Ŭl: "Alright. Well, you urinate four or five times a day, right? And when you do hard work, you sweat, don't you? And you breathe without pause even for a moment. Don't you know the reason for all that? Of course, you might have your own kind (一流) of interpretation, but we expel waste products through these three methods, and this is called the excretion process (排泄作用)."<54>

Kap: "My friend, what are waste materials (老廢物質)? You have to tell me that first for me to understand."

Ŭl: "Ah, time is pressing, so I jumped ahead a bit too much. Waste materials are nothing special. For a living organism to be active, a part of its body substance (體物質) decomposes, simultaneously generating 'energy' (나-지 [energy]) (force/勢力) and body heat. The decomposition products expelled at that time include organic acids (like lactic acid/乳酸), water, salts, carbon dioxide gas (炭酸瓦斯), etc. These are collectively called waste materials."

Kap: "Haha, now I understand a bit better. Since sweat (汗) is salty, water and salt come out as sweat, carbon dioxide gas (炭酸瓦斯) comes out with the breath we exhale, and so lactic acid (乳酸) must inevitably come out as urine (尿), I suppose. But I don't quite understand the principle."

Ŭl: "Indeed, how could one comprehend all principles in a single day? It's something that might not have an end even if studied for a lifetime."

Kap: "The words might sound a bit dirty, but what about defecation (大小便)? Does feces (大便) have any relation to the excretion process?"

Ŭl: "This is a point easily misunderstood. Feces (大便), that is, dung (糞), consists of undigested food residues (殘滓) within the digestive tract, intermediate products of digestion, etc., which simply gather, pass through the intestines, and are mechanically expelled (搬出) from the body. Whether the organism is active or not, the undigested matter is expelled, so isn't its nature completely different from the waste materials mentioned above? In Japanese-style characters (日本식 文字), 'daishōben' (大小便) is written, and the character '便' happens to be the same by chance, but in reality, they have no connection (因緣). Therefore, calling it 'fun'nyō' (糞尿 - feces and urine) is perhaps more appropriate than 'daishōben'."

"But my friend, it's already well past nine o'clock at night. Time is short, so I can't explain everything tonight. However, as mentioned above, among the three most prominent characteristics of life phenomena, I have spoken extremely generally about the most crucial one, metabolism. My explanation might have lacked polish (操練) and been difficult to listen to, but if it has brought you even slightly closer to the purpose of your visit, I would be glad."

Kap: "Indeed, I've sat here too long and caused you an unexpected waste of much time; I apologize profoundly. I've understood a little. Please take another opportunity to tell me about the remaining reproduction (生殖作用) and contractility/irritability (收縮性)."

Ŭl: "This time, because I couldn't finish the story, the explanation became quite insufficient, but from next time, I will study more and strive (期하고저 하네) for completeness. Well then, let's see each other again sometime soon." (Unfinished/未完) <55>

**Summary**

The article presents a dialogue between two friends, Kap and Ŭl (a biologist, Kim Ho-jik), discussing the definition of life. Kap proposes intuitive ideas like movement and growth as the essence of life, but Ŭl refutes these by citing examples from the non-living world like Brownian motion and crystal growth. Ŭl argues that life cannot be separated from its material basis (protoplasm, composed of common elements) and is best understood as a complex phenomenon or state. He begins explaining the key phenomena of life, focusing on metabolism (新陳代謝) – the intake of nutrients and excretion of waste products (like CO2, sweat, urine) generated by bodily activity – differentiating excretion from defecation. The discussion is cut short due to time, promising to cover reproduction and contractility/irritability later.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects the introduction and discussion of Western scientific concepts (biology, chemistry, physics - Brownian motion, electrons) in colonial Korea during the 1930s. It shows an intellectual engaging with modern science, using specific terminology (some potentially borrowed or influenced by Japanese usage, e.g., 瓦斯, 曹達, 日本식 文字), and attempting to explain complex ideas like metabolism to a layperson, indicating a growing scientific literacy and discourse within Korean society despite the colonial context. The author's background as a 理學士 (Bachelor of Science) highlights the emergence of formally trained Korean scientists.

乙. 「아니 자네 식으로 생각하면 그러케 될 이치가 아닌가. 생명의 구체적 대상이란 생물체를 말함이요 말하자면 생명의 근원인 생물체를 전적으로 이해하면은 생명은 자연히 解得할 수가 잇겟다는 것이 나의 主志요 또한 그러한 생각으로써 말해보겟다는 말일세.」

生命의 物質的 基礎
甲. 「그럼 생명의 물질적 기초를 말한다는 말이지.」＜53＞
乙. 「옳지 그래. 생명의 근원이라고 생각하는 原形質(細胞)을 될 수 잇는대로 순수하게 切取하야 그를 분석하면 별다른게 아니라 무생물계에 흔이 잇는 炭素, 酸素, 水素, 窒素가 그 주요성분이 되어 그 외에 硫黃 加里, 石炭, 曹達, 鐵分 등 數種의 원소로 구성되어 잇으며 이상 10여 종의 원소가 모여 각종의 蛋白質을 구성하며 그 단백질로써 原形質이 구성되어 잇으니 이 原形質이야말로 생명이 담겨 잇는 그릇이라고 할 수 잇지.」
甲. 「가만잇게. 또 하나 묻세. 原形質이 무어야.」
乙. 「오늘은 원형질에 대하야 자세히 말할 시간을 가지지 못하엿네. 하나 본론을 이해하는데 큰 불편이 없을 정도에까지 말하자면 가령 나무껍질이나 사람의 살떵어리나 아주 얇게 끊어서 현미경 밑에 놓고 보면 아주 무수한 小區分을 볼 수 잇다네. 그 小區分을 세포라고 하며 이것이 생물체의 構造 及 작용의 단위인데 구타여 명료한 區劃이 없드라도 구성과 작용의 단위가 되어 잇는 물질을 일반적으로 原形質이라고 하네.」
甲. 「좀 알아들엇네. 계속하야 이야기 하게.」
乙. 「그럼 본론에 도라가 생물체는 일단 분석하야 구성분자에 노나놓고 보면 거기에는 벌서 생명이 없고 그가 담기어 잇던 그릇 만이 남기어진다. 따라서 생명이란 마침내 한 가지 현상이며 상태라는대 생각이 미치게 된다. 고로 단백질이 다 원형질이다 膠質이다 여러 가지 元素로 되어 잇다 등 하는 것이 都是生命의 속성에 불과한 것이요 결코 생명의 직접원인이 아님을 짐작하겟다. 그러므로 실질에 들어가 생명현상이 본래 가지고 잇는 물적 존재 또는 성질에 대하야 연구하는게 이를 이해하는대 경로일까 하네.」

무엇이 生命인가
甲. 「그럼 생명의 구체적 현상을 말하겟다는 말인가.」
乙. 「그러코 말고. 생명의 현상이라고 할찐대 떼어 놓을 수 없는 세 가지 작용이 잇다. 1일 新陳代謝 2일 生殖作用 3일 收縮性. 순서를 따라 제일 신진대사에 대하야 말하지. 생물이 살아간다는 것 즉 생명이 잇다는 것은 다시 말하자면 생물이 각기 성질에 따라 활동을 지속
하여 간다는 것을 의미하는 것이니 생물이 활동함을 따라서 자체 일부가 消耗를 당하며 이를 보충하고저 또는 他部의 조성에 充用하고저 생물은 不可不 외부에서 영양물을 섭취하여야 하며 이와 동시에 활동을 말미암아 체내에 발생한 無用物質은 체외로 搬出하여야 되겟다. 이와 같이 新物質을 섭취하며 노폐물을 배출하는 작용을 신진대사라고 일켯는다. 이것이 생물에 잇어서 아주 근본이 되는 물적 생명현상이라고 보겟다.」
甲. 「하,하. 그럼 사람들이 게절을 따라 밥상에 향하는 것이 말하자면 新物質 섭취를 위하야 그러하는 것이로구려. 그것참. 그럴 듯 해. 그런데 노폐물질 배출이라는 것은 언뜻 생각이 안도는 구려. 다른 말이 나오기 전에 설명을 해주게.」
乙. 「그래 그럼 하로에 4,5차 오줌을 누지, 또 된 일을 하면 땀을 흘리지, 그러고 한 시라도 쉬지 않고 호흡을 하지, 그럼 그게 무슨 까닭인지 모르겟는가. 물론 자네 一流의 解釋이야 잇겟지마는 우리 이 세가지 방법으로 노폐물을 배출하고 잇으며 이르되 排泄作用이라고 한다.」＜54＞
甲. 「이 사람 老廢物質이 무엇인가 그것부터 말해주어야 알지.」
乙. 「아차 시간이 촉박하야 좀 너무 뛰엇구려. 무엇 노폐물이라는 것은 별것 아니야. 생물체가 활동하려면 體物質 일부가 분해가 되어 동시에 「—나-지」(勢力)와 體熱이 발생되며 그때 분해 생성물로 배출되는 것은 有機酸(乳酸) 水分, 鹽分, 炭酸瓦斯, 등인데 이것들을 통틀어 노폐물이라고 한다네.」
甲. 「하하 그럼 좀 알아 듣겟네. 땀(汗)이 짠 것을 보니 수분과 염분이 땀으로 되어 나오고 炭酸瓦斯는 내보내는 코낌으로 나오고 그러고 보니 乳酸은 不可不 오줌(尿)으로 되어 나온다고 볼 수밖에 없을테지, 하나 그 이치는 잘 모르겟네.」
乙. 「과연 一朝에 모-든 이치를 다 해득할 수야 잇겟나. 일평생 두고 연구해도 끝이 없을텐데.」
甲. 「말이 좀 더러운 듯하나 어쩌면 大小便이라니 대변은 排泄作用과 무슨 관계가 없는가?」
乙. 「까딱하면 오해하기 쉬운 문제일세. 대변 즉 糞은 소화기 내에서 소화가 덜 된 식물의 殘滓. 소화 중간 산물 등이 그대로 모여서 장내를 통과하야 기계적으로 체외에 搬出되는 것이니 생물체가 활동하거나 말거나 소화덜된 것이 체외에 배출되는것이니까 우에 말한 바와같은 노폐물질과는 전연 성질이 다르지 않은가. 日本식 文字에 대소변이라고 쓰매 便字가 우연히 같을 따름이지 그 실은 아무 因緣이 없는 것이라고 하겟네. 따라서 대소변이라고 부르는 것보다도 糞尿라고 하는 것이 좀 더 적절하다고 하겟네.
한데 이 사람 발써 밤 아홉시를 훨씬 돌앗네 그려. 시간이 촉박하니 오늘 밤에 전부를 말할 수가 없고 다만 우에 말한 바와 같이 생명 현상에 가장 뚜렷한 특성 세 가지 가운데서 제일 요긴한 신진대사에 대하야 극히 개괄적으로 말한 셈인데 설명에 操練이 없어 듣기에 거북햇는지 모르나 다소이라도 자네가 찾아온 목적에 가까이 감이 잇엇다면 다행일세」
甲. 「참, 너무 오래 앉아 자네에게는 의외에 많은 시간 허비를 주게되어 미안천만일셰. 조금 알아들엇네. 또 다시 기회를 타서 남아지 生殖作用, 收縮性에 대하야 말해주게.」
乙. 「이번에는 이야기를 다 못해서 해설이 아주 불충분하게 되엇으나 다음부터는 좀더 연구하야 충실을 期하고저 하네. 그럼 日間 또다시 보세.」(未完) ＜55＞
