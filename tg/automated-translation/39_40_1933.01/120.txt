**Body Text Translation:**
Annual Observances, January Edition: Various Customs of the First Month
					By Ch'ŏndaesanin

For us who have left the lunar calendar behind, discussing the lunar calendar-era customs is, of course, detached from reality. However, precisely because we have abandoned the lunar calendar and no longer practice its observances, we feel a certain nostalgic interest, and even a kind of curiosity about the exotic. Therefore, each month, we will introduce and explain the lunar calendar observances corresponding to that month.

1st Day (Ch'o-haru)
This day is also called Sinwŏn (新元), Sinjŏng (新正), Sindan (新旦), or Sŏllal. On this day, after performing the ancestral rites (ch'arye), men and women, old and young, wear new clothes and dress up in various styles. This is called *sejang* (歲粧 - New Year's attire). Of course, the food and drink consumed on this day are *sech'an* (歲饌 - New Year's food) and *seju* (歲酒 - New Year's liquor). There used to be many kinds of *seju*, but the worldwide economic depression and the impoverished lives of the oppressed XX people (被XX民族) have had the greatest impact even on this festive mood, and now *seju* is rarely seen. Li Bai (李太白) would be dying of thirst.
In China, Cui Shi's (崔寔) *Yueling* (月令) records drinking *jiaobaijiu* (椒柏酒), and Zong Lin's (宗檀) *Jingchu Suishiji* (荊楚歲時記) records eating *tususu* (屠蘇酒 - Toso wine) and *jiaoyatang* (膠牙餳 - sticky malt candy), but *tususu* is now common in Japan.
Glutinous rice is pounded to make long rice cakes called *changgubyŏng* (長股餠), also known as *kwŏnmo* (拳模) or *paekpyŏng* (白餠). Boiling these in meat broth is called *ttŏkkuk* (餠湯 - rice cake soup). This is similar to the *donghundun* (冬餛飩 - winter wontons) and *nyŏnbakt'ak* (年餺飥 - year-end noodles) mentioned in the commentary on Lu Fangweng's (陸放翁) *Suishou Shushi* (歲首書事). Also, steamed rice cake called *siruttŏk* (甑餠) is used for ancestral rites during the New Year season. Currently, in Seoul customs, *kangjŏng* (羌飣 - oil-and-honey pastry) is often used for family rites, which seems to correspond to the *ch'ŏ* (酒酏) in the *Zhouli Yishishu* (周禮 酏食疏) and the *pinkyŏn* (粉繭 - flour cocoons) in Yang Chengzhai's (楊誠齋) *Shangyuan Shi* (上元詩). After filling their stomachs with such delicious food, people set out for New Year's visits (*sebae* 歲拜). In the past, high officials would offer New Year's greetings (*choha* 朝賀) in the palace courtyard, provincial governors (*pangbaek* 方伯) would submit congratulatory letters (*hajŏn* 賀箋), and commoners also exchanged congratulatory words (*hasa* 賀詞), but the number was small. In Chosŏn, things like palace greetings (宮闕 문안), Sŭngjŏngwŏn's (承政院) auspicious poems (*Yŏnsangsi* 延祥詩), and the three rounds of New Year cannons (三度의 歲砲) have disappeared, but
New Year's cards (연하장) are so numerous that New Year's Day is a sorrowful day (설은 날) for mail carriers. In the past, instead of New Year's cards, it was common to send a female servant (*munanpi* 問安婢) to convey greetings. Servants would deliver a calling card (*seham* 歲銜) to the homes of officials or teachers, where visitors would simply sign their names in a register placed by the gate. This custom is also recorded in Chinese texts like Wang Qi's (王錡) *Yupuzaji* (寓圃雜記). Also, the Royal Bureau of Painting (圖畵署 - Tohwasŏ) used to paint New Year pictures (*sehwa* 歲畵) with auspicious meanings. Pictures of the two golden armored generals (金甲二將軍), one holding a whip (節) and the other an axe (斧), were pasted on the palace gates and called *munbae* (門排), but these too are no longer seen. In the alleys, one sees <24> pictures of chickens and tigers pasted on walls for prayers. Perhaps this follows Dong Xun's (董勛) saying, "The first day is for the chicken (一日로 爲鷄)"? The *Jingchu Suishiji* states that on the first day of the first month, pictures of chickens were pasted on doors, likely an imitation of this. Drawing a tiger probably signifies the Month of the Tiger (寅月). According to the *Yŏji Sŭngnam* (輿地勝覽), Gwangju custom (廣州俗) is to worship the sun and moon gods (日月神) on this day. This is not the only superstition that has been eliminated. There was the *samjaebŏp* (三災法 - Three Disasters Law): those born in the years Ki, Yu, Chuk (己酉丑) faced disasters in Hae, Cha, Chuk (亥子丑) years; Sin, Cha, Chin (申子辰) born in In, Myo, Chin (寅卯辰) years; Hae, Myo, Mi (亥卯未) born in Sa, O, Mi (巳午未) years; and In, O, Sul (寅午戌) born in Sin, Yu, Sul (申酉戌) years, which were called Chin years (辰年?). Those afflicted by these three disasters would draw three hawk (鷹) heads on the door lintel (門楣) to prevent misfortune. There was also the *ch'ŏngch'ambŏp* (聽讖法 - listening divination method), where one went out onto the street at dawn and predicted the year's fortune based on the first words spoken by the first person encountered. Hair collected from combing throughout the year (*t'oebal* 退髮) was gathered and burned outside the gate at dusk on New Year's Day to prevent epidemics (瘟疫). This is similar to the custom in China mentioned by Sun Simiao (孫思邈), "Burning white hair on the寅 (Tiger) day of the first month brings good fortune." As this is the first day of the year, it's natural that people would want to know the year's fortune and welcome blessings. Even now, in the countryside, when seeing a young person, people offer well-wishes (*tŏktam* 덕담) like "May you become an official this year" (a stark remnant of the harmful custom of revering officials and despising commoners!), or "May you have a son" (no one says "May you have a daughter"), or "May you make money." Outside the Seoul city walls (城外), Buddhist monks (僧侶) would beat dharma drums (法鼓) while chanting. If people tossed them a coin or two, or else exchanged two of their rice cakes for one of the monk's, eating the monk's rice cake was believed to provide protection against smallpox (痘疫) for children. Also, senior monks (*sangjwa* 上佐) from each temple would go around the neighborhoods (沼門?), calling at each house, and people would give them some rice as a way of welcoming New Year's blessings (新年의 福맞이). According to the *Yŏji Sŭngnam*, in Cheju (濟州) customs, shrines (神祠) were built on mountains, rivers, hills, and marshes. From New Year's Day to the 15th, shamans (*mugyŏk* 巫覡) would carry divine banners (*sintuk* 神纛), perform exorcism plays (*nahŭi* 儺戱), and enter villages beating drums. People would throw money to appease the deities (神祗). Digressing slightly, there is a ghost called *Yagwangi* (夜光) (also *Yagwang* 藥王?), who goes house to house on New Year's Eve, tries on children's shoes, and takes them if they fit. Losing shoes to Yagwangi on this day is considered unlucky. So, everyone turns off the lights, hides their shoes, and hangs a sieve (篩) on the hall wall (廳壁) or between the stairs (階庭). The Yagwang spirit supposedly comes, counts the sieve holes, forgets about the shoes, and leaves. The Yagwang ghost is said to have a very unpleasant face. In the countryside, when scaring young children, people still mention "Yagwangi," "Salgwangi," "Ŏŏboe," "Pinjwi"... but it's unclear what kind of person or deity Yagwang is. At night, people divine the year's fortune (*sinun* 身運), check marital compatibility (*kunghap* 宮合), or perform Five Elements divination (*ohaengjŏm* 五行占). The *T'ojŏng Pigyŏl* (土亭秘訣), popularly attributed to Master Yi Chiham (李之菡), is one such secret text. For Five Elements divination, wooden pieces similar to Go stones (棊子) are marked differently with Metal, Wood, Water, Fire, and Earth (金木水火土). Each element has a corresponding fortune verse. Like in the game of *yut* (윳노리), these pieces are tossed, and the fortune is read based on which characters face up or down. The game of *yut* uses four sticks, hence called *sahŭi* (柶戱 - four-stick game, also *malssŭgi* 말쓰기 - moving horses). The *t'anhŭi* (攤戱) mentioned in the *Chibong Yusŏl* (芝峰類說) might refer to this. (*Yut* songs (윳頌) can be found in the Chosŏnŏ Munhak Hoebo (朝鮮語文學會報 - Journal of the Korean Language and Literature Society), please refer to it.) Instead of four wooden sticks, split red sandalwood (赤荊?) or peas (豌豆) are sometimes used for *sahŭi*. When all four are face down, it's called *mo* (모, also myomyo me); three face down is *kŏl* (걸, also k'ŏl); two face down is *kae* (개); only one face down is *to* (토, also tol, toyaji, ttol); none face down is *yut* (육, also yung, syung). Similar to backgammon (雙陸), <25> two players move pieces on a board with 29 circles (圈). *To* moves one space, *kae* two, *kŏl* three, *yut* four, and *mo* five. The winner is decided based on shortcuts (遠近法) around the circles and the speed (遲速) of the pieces. Another women's game (婦女들의 놀음) remembered from New Year's Day is
*nŏlttwigi* (널뛰기, 超板戱 - seesaw jumping). Beautiful skirts flutter in the spring breeze, and the fragrant scent of New Year's makeup (*sejang* 歲粧), along with the "k'ungdŏng, k'ungdŏng" sound of the board, seems to completely awaken the alleys (閭巷) that had been quiet all winter. Regarding *ch'op'anhŭi* (超板戱 - *nŏlttwigi*), Zhou Huang's (周煌) *Ryukyu Kokki* (琉球國記 - Record of the Ryukyu Kingdom) mentions "a board dance (*panmu* 板舞) where women dance on a board," so perhaps it's unexpectedly a custom imported from abroad.

7th Day
This day is called *Inil* (人日 - Human Day). "遙憐故人思故鄕" (Remotely pitying old friends thinking of home) is from Gao Shi's (高適) poem on *Inil*. Nowadays, there might be New Year banquets and formal greetings (*sabangbae* 四方拜), but in the old Chosŏn cabinet (內閣), the Ministry of Works (工曹 - Kongjo) presented "flower ornaments" (*hwasŭng* 花勝), and round Daruma figures made of copper called *tonginsŭng* (銅人勝) were presented, one to each palace hall. Also, special state examinations held on *Inil* were called *Inil Chesi* (人日製試), counted among the seasonal exams (*chŏlche* 節製) along with those on the 3rd day of the 3rd month, 7th day of the 7th month, and 9th day of the 9th month. These are all customs that have now disappeared.

Upper Hae Day (Sanghae-il)
Upper Cha Day (Sangja-il), Myo Day (Myo-il), Sa Day (Sa-il). The first Hae (亥 - Pig) day of the first month is called Sanghae (上亥, also *totalki* 돝달기 - day of the pig). The first Cha (子 - Rat) day is Sangja (上子, also *chwidalgi* 쥐달기 - day of the rat). According to *Kukcho Kosa* (國朝故事 - Tales of the Dynasty), several hundred minor court officials (宮中小官) would hold a torchlight procession, shouting "*Hunswe*" (燻豕 - smoke out the pig) and "*Hunsŏ*" (燻鼠 - smoke out the rat), parching grain seeds, putting them in pouches, and distributing them to various officials. The *Haenang* (亥囊 - Pig Pouch) and *Chanang* (子囊 - Rat Pouch) refer to this event. After King T'aejo (太祖) of the Yi Dynasty (李朝) ascended the throne, this old system was restored. The custom of roasting beans (콩) in alleys while shouting "*Chwijwa ch'o, Chwijwa ch'o*" (鼠嘴焦 鼠嘴焦 - Rat mouths scorched, Rat mouths scorched!) arose from this, and the custom of washing with ground beans (*chodo* 澡豆) on Sanghae Day likely originated here too. In the Hoso region (湖酉俗 - Ch'ungch'ŏng Province), the torchlight gatherings called "*hwetpulssam*" (횟불쌈 - torch fight) are likely fires to sear the 'rats'. Also, washing one's face with bean powder (*tusol* 豆屑) on Sanghae Day is said to whiten a dark complexion, because the pig (豕) is considered a dark animal. The first Myo (卯 - Rabbit) day is called Rabbit Day (*t'okkinal* 토끼날). Thread drawn from cocoons (*kŭri* 繰) is called rabbit thread (*t'okkissil* 兎絲), which people carry on their bodies for protection against misfortune (*aengmagi* 厄막이). On Sa (巳 - Snake) Day, people do not manage their hair, due to the superstition that snakes will enter the house.

15th Day
This is the so-called "Chŏngwŏl Taeborŭmnal" (正月 대보름날 - Great Full Moon Day of the First Month) or "Sangwŏn-il" (上元日 - Upper Prime Day). At dawn, one drinks a cup of ear-sharpening liquor (*kwibalkisul* 귀밝이술, also *Myŏngnyŏnju* 明年酒, *Yurongju* 牖聾酒) and chews three chestnuts (栗) (*kyoch'anggwa* 咬瘡果 - bite-boil fruit). Drawing a bowl of water at dawn is called "scooping the Dragon Child" (龍子를 撈한다), and wrapping white rice (*paekpan* 白飯) in white paper and throwing it into the water is called "*ŏbusi*" (魚鳧施 - offering to fish and ducks). According to Ye Tinggui's (葉廷珪) *Hailu Suishi* (海錄碎事), the ear-sharpening liquor in China was *chirongju* (治聾酒 - cure-deafness liquor) drunk on *She-ri* (社日 - Soil God Day). Chewing chestnuts or radishes (*nabok* 蘿蔔) is called *chyangnang* (嚼囊 - chewing pouch). While chewing, one prays, "Grant peace and tranquility for the entire twelve months of the year." This seems to have originated from an old custom in Kyŏngju (慶州), according to the *Kyŏngdo Chapchi* (京都雜志 - Miscellany of the Capital). Also in Kyŏngju, women would go from house to house at dawn, calling out "Buy my heat!" The house owner would try hard not to answer. Isn't this custom of selling heat (*maesŏ* 賣暑) perhaps rooted in something like the note in Lu Fangweng's (陸放翁) poem commentary: "Before dawn on Lichun (立春 - Beginning of Spring), people call to each other selling spring drowsiness (相呼賣春困)"? In the Ŭiju (義州) region, young men and women eat malt candy (*yŏt* 飴) at dawn, said to be for comparing teeth (齒). Also, eating cooked preserved vegetables (*ch'inchae* 陳菜), dried gourds, dried mushrooms, radishes, beans, and red beans that were prepared in advance is believed to prevent suffering from the heat. Wrapping rice in seaweed (*haeeui* 海衣) or water chestnut leaves (*majich'ae* 馬蹄菜) is called *pokssagae* (裏福 - wrapping fortune, or 縛苫 - binding thatch), carrying a meaning of praying for grain (祈穀). The *Jingchu Suishiji* mentions "gathering seven kinds of vegetables on Human Day (人日) to make soup (採七種菜하야 作羹)." Could this have shifted to Sangwŏn Day in Chosŏn today? Also, <26>
*ogokchapban* (五穀雜飯 - five-grain rice) is eaten throughout the day. There's also a custom of eating *yakpap* (藥飯 - medicinal rice), made by mixing glutinous rice (*no mi* 糯米) with jujubes (棗), chestnuts (栗), oil (油), and honey (蜜), then steaming it. According to the *Tonggyŏng Chapki* (東京雜記 - Miscellany of the Eastern Capital, i.e., Kyŏngju), this originated in the 10th year of King Soji (昭智王) of Silla (新羅). On the 15th day of the first month, when the king visited Ch'ŏnch'ŏnjŏng Pavilion (天泉亭), a crow flew down and warned the king. Therefore, Sangwŏn Day was designated as *Ogi-il* (烏忌日 - Crow Memorial Day), and making glutinous rice (*no pan* 糯飯) to offer to the crow became the seasonal food (*sishik* 時食) of today. In the countryside, on the day before Sangwŏn, straw is tied into the shape of a banner (*kituk* 旗纛), wrapped with ears of the five grains, and cotton (*som* 솜) is attached to the end. This is erected near the house to pray for a good harvest. In mountain regions (山方), a tree is erected behind the cattle shed (牛宮), and grain ears (穀穗) and cotton bolls (綿花) are hung on it. Children come out at dawn, sing songs, and offer blessings. Also, on the night before Sangwŏn, those whose astrological sign corresponds to the Rahu star (羅睺直星 - *Nahujiksŏng*) make a straw effigy (*ch'uryŏng* 芻靈), called "*choyongi-yo*" (조용이요) in dialect (方言), which means Ch'ŏyong (處容). Copper coins are inserted into the head of the straw effigy, and it's thrown onto the street at early dusk (*ch'ohon* 初昏) as a form of *aengmagi* (厄막이 - warding off evil). Many children scramble to pick up the money. This is similar to the custom of selling fortune ladles (*pokchoru* 福조루) on the morning of the full moon day.

Ch'ŏyong (處容) was the name of a son of the Dragon King of the East Sea (東海 龍子) during the Silla period. The *Ch'ŏyongga* (處容歌 - Song of Ch'ŏyong, "On a moonlit night in the Eastern Capital...") found in the *Samguk Yusa* (三國遺事) and the *Ch'ŏyongmu* (處容舞 - Dance of Ch'ŏyong) described in the *Akhak Kwebŏm* (樂學軌範) are still known today. This custom of representing Ch'ŏyong with a straw effigy (*ch'uryŏng*) seems quite old. Young boys and girls carry three kinds of wooden gourds (*mokhoru* 木葫蘆) – blue, red, and yellow – and then, also on the night before Sangwŏn, secretly throw them onto the street for *aengmagi*. Also, red bean porridge (*p'atchuk* 팥죽) is often boiled and eaten before the full moon day. This resembles the ancient custom in Jiangnan (江南) where willow branches were stuck into red bean porridge for offerings (荊楚歲時記). In Seoul, there was even a mean-spirited custom where people dug up soil from the Chongno Crossroads (鍾路 十字街) early in the morning and buried it under the four pillars of their house, believing it would make them rich.

On the full moon day, there's a custom where women give thread (絲), called *porŭmsil* (보름실 - full moon thread). It's said that hitting a dog on this day causes flies to thrive and the dog to get sick, so dogs are not fed all day. The proverb "hungry as a full moon day dog" (*porŭmgae* 보름개) refers to this. However, when a young child is sick or frail (*pyŏngso* 病痟), there's a superstition that begging for rice from 100 houses, pounding it in a mortar, sitting facing a dog, and feeding the dog one spoonful while eating one spoonful oneself promotes health. Perhaps dogs from such households get well-fed. Also, placing stones in the forks of fruit tree branches on this day is said to make them bear much fruit (*silgwa* 實果), a practice mentioned in Xu Guangqi's (徐光啓) *Nongzheng Quanshu* (農政全書 - Complete Treatise on Agricultural Administration). Kites (*yŏn* 紙鳶) are flown for *aengmagi*. Kite flying is said to have been introduced when General Ch'oe Yŏng (崔瑩) conquered T'amna (耽羅 - Cheju Island), but the Chinese *fengzheng* (風錚 - wind zither kite) is similar. There's also the *paerin'gaebi* (배린갭이), made by attaching five-colored paper to a bamboo frame, which spins in the wind; it's also called *hoehoea* (回回兒 - Huihui child). Another activity is flying a *kogomae* (고고매), meaning phoenix (鳳凰) in Mongolian (蒙古語), which involves tying goose feathers (*kŏwit'ŏl* 鵝毛) to silk thread from a single cocoon (*tokkyŏnsa* 獨繭絲) and letting it fly in the wind. There's also a game called *sa ch'igi* (사 치기 - hitting the pile), where coins are tossed from a distance (*kŏri* 距離). Hitting a stack (*sa* 사 - overlapping coins) means winning; missing or hitting incorrectly means losing. In some mountain gorges (山峽), people play *t'ujŏn* (투전 - card game), but now various games like *hwato* (화토 - flower cards), Mahjong (麻雀), Go (*paduk* 바둑), and Korean chess (*changgi* 장기) have become widespread. Outside the Three Gates (三門) or in Ahyŏn (阿峴), people used to divide into teams for stone fights (*sŏkchŏn* 石戰). Stone fighting was originally most prevalent in P'yŏngan Province (平安道), and even the *Tangshu Gaoli Zhuan* (唐書 高麗傳 - Book of Tang, Account of Goryeo) records that Goryeo people held stone fights annually on the P'aesu River (浿水). However, it is now banned by strict police orders (警察의 嚴令). Not only that, but Ch'unch'ŏn (春川) and Kap'yŏng's (加平) chariot battles (*ch'ajŏn* 車戰) and the Yŏngnam region's (嶺南) vine battles (*kalchŏn* 葛戰) used to take place on this day, though they are rarely done now. *Kalchŏn* is a tug-of-war using kudzu vine ropes (*kalsŭng* 葛繩) and is still performed relatively often. The torch fights (*hwetpulssaum* 횟불싸움) of the Kiho region (畿湖) are also a type of ancient river-crossing game (*hyŏlha hŭi* 絜河戱?) that has been passed down to <27> the present day. In the mountain gorges of Kangwŏn Province (江原道), children engage in chasing birds by calling out their names, a kind of ritual for praying for grain (祈穀).

On this day, at early dusk (初昏), people climb high mountains for moon viewing (*talmaji* 달맞이). The first person to see the moon is considered lucky. If the moonlight is reddish, it predicts drought; if whitish, floods (漲水). The year's fortune for the four directions is divined from the moon's shape, size, emergence (湧浮), height (高低), and the thickness or thinness of its halo in the four cardinal directions (輪廓 四方厚薄). On this day, even in the capital (都城), women all go out for moon viewing. Not only do they go out, but they also walk on bridges (*tari* 橋, homophonous with 脚 - leg), which is believed to prevent leg ailments (*tari pyŏng* 脚病). In the Andong region (安東地方), during

*tapkyo* (踏橋 - bridge treading), one woman asks, "What bridge is this?" and the others answer in unison, "Ch'ŏnggyesan Kkurisoedari (淸溪山 꾸리쇠다리 - Cheonggyesan Bronze Bridge)!" What remnant of ancient custom could this be? Some households recite the *An'aekkyŏng* (安宅經 - Sutra for Household Peace) from the day before the full moon until the moon sets on the 15th to pray for the family's fortune. There's also a custom of erecting a one-foot-long piece of wood in the yard and determining the harvest outlook based on its shadow when the moonlight corresponds to noon (午時). There's also the custom that walking across twelve bridges on Sangwŏn Day prevents illness for the twelve months of the year. One cannot help but be amazed at the multitude of superstitions and curious customs.

Miscellaneous (기타)
Though less common now, in the past, even markets (*sijŏn* 市廛) chose specific days to open, invariably selecting an "insect day" (*moch'ung-il* 毛蟲日), with the Tiger day (寅日) being considered best. The Upper Rat (上子), Upper Dragon (上辰), Upper Horse (上午), and Upper Pig (上亥) days in the first month were called *Sin-il* (愼日 - days of caution), where one was careful about all matters. This was an old custom in
Kyŏngju (慶州), perhaps stemming from King Soji (昭智王) escaping misfortune related to the zither case (*kŭmhap* 琴匣) due to unusual signs involving a crow, rat, dragon, horse, and pig (烏鼠龍馬猪)? What could be the rural custom (*hyangsok* 鄕俗) of avoiding wooden objects (木物) on the 16th? The first and last quarters of the moon (*sanghyŏn* 上弦, *hahyŏn* 下弦) are days when the tides decrease (*chosu* 潮水), so if there's something to be avoided (*kugi* 拘忌) each month, people wait until after these days to conduct business. Calling the 8th day of each month a *P'ae-il* (敗日 - defeat day) is likely due to the similar pronunciation of "pal" (八 - eight) and "p'ae" (敗 - defeat). The 5th, 14th, and 23rd of each month are called *Sampa-il* (三敗日 - three defeat days), and avoiding these days for all matters is a remnant custom (*yusok* 遺俗) from the Goryeo (高麗) period. Avoiding "pal" (八) because it sounds like "p'ae" (敗) is similar to how Japanese people nowadays dislike phone numbers containing four (四 shi, sounds like 死 death) or forty-four (四四 shishi). (End) <28>

**Summary**

This 1933 article by "Ch'ŏndaesanin" nostalgically details various Korean customs associated with the first lunar month (Chŏngwŏl), particularly New Year's Day (Sŏllal) and the Great Full Moon Day (Taeborŭm). It covers traditional foods (*ttŏkkuk*, *yakpap*), activities (*sebae*, *nŏlttwigi*, *yutnori*, kite flying, moon viewing, bridge treading), divination methods (*T'ojŏng Pigyŏl*, observing the moon), and folk beliefs (warding off evil spirits like Yagwangi, *aengmagi* rituals, lucky/unlucky days). The author frequently compares Korean customs to Chinese and occasionally Japanese ones, citing historical texts, and notes the decline of many traditions due to changing times, economic hardship under colonial rule, and sometimes official bans (like stone fighting). From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its documentation of folk practices in the 1930s, the author's explicit mention of hardship as a "被XX民族" (oppressed people, with censorship), reflecting the colonial reality, and the commentary on the blend of native traditions with influences from neighboring cultures. The casual references to specific texts and historical figures (Chinese and Korean) also provide insight into the educated perspective of the time.

널뛰기(超板戱)니 아름다운 스카트가 봄바람에 휘날리고 歲粧의 향기로운 냄새가 「쿵덩」 「쿵덩」하는 널소리와 함께 오래 동안 고요하든 閭巷의 겨울을 완전히 깨트리는 듯하다. 超板戱는 周煌의 琉球國記에도 「그 婦女들이 板上에 춤추는 板舞가 잇다고 한 것」이 잇으니 뜻밖에 舶來의 풍속인지도 알 수 없다.

7日
이날을 人日이라고 하나니 「遙憐故人思故鄕」은 高適의 人日이엇다. 지금 같으면 新年宴會四方拜의 儀式이 잇겟지만 예전 조선 內閣에서는 工曹가 「花勝」을 드리고 또 銅으로 圓毬達摩를 만들어 「銅人勝」이라고 하여 各殿마다 하나씩 드렷다. 또 人日에 특별히 과거를 보이는 것을 人日製試라고 하야 3월 3일 7월 7일 9월 9일과 함께 節製의 하나로 곱든 것이다. 지금은 모두 사라진 풍속이다.

上亥日
上子日 卯日 巳日 正月의 첫 亥日을 上亥(돝달기) 첫 子日을 上子(쥐달기)라고 한다. 國朝故事에 宮中小官數百이 횟불 행렬을 하면서 「燻豕」 「燻鼠」라고 하여 곡물의 종자를 타처 주머니에 담아 諸臣에게 나누어 주엇다 하엿다. 亥囊 子囊은 이때의 일이다. 李朝 太祖가 登極한 후 다시 이 古制를 恢復하여 閭巷에 콩을 복그면서 「鼠嘴焦 鼠嘴焦」하는 풍속이 생겻고 上亥日에 澡豆하는 풍속도 이에서 난 듯 하다. 湖酉俗(忠情道)에
「횟불쌈」이라고 하면서 횟불모임을 하는 것은 또한 「쥐」를 지지는 불일 것이다. 또 上亥日에 豆屑로 얼굴을 씻으면 깜은 얼굴이 희어진다는 것도 도야지(豕)가 감은 동물이기 때문이다. 첫 卯日은 토끼날이라고 하여 線絲를 뽑은 것(繰)을 토끼씰(兎絲)이라고 하여 몸에 차고 단니면서 厄막이를 한다. 巳日날은 머리깔은 다스리지 아니한다. 뱀이 집에 들어온다는 迷信 때문에.

15日
이날은 소위 「正月 대보름날」 「上元日」이다. 새벽에 귀밝이술(明年酒 牖聾酒)을 한 잔 딿아 마시고 밤(栗) 세 개를 씹는다. (咬瘡果) 새벽에 물 한 그릇 긷는 것을 「龍子를 撈한다」하고 白飯을 白紙에 싸서 물에 던지는 것을 「魚鳧施」라고 한다. 그런데 귀밝이술은 葉廷珪의 海錄碎事에 의하면 中國에서는 「社日에 治聾酒를 마시든 것이다」 「밤」이나 「蘿蔔」(무)를 씹는 것은 嚼囊이라고 하여 씹을 적에 「1年 열두달 無事大平하게 하여달라」고 비는 것이 慶州 古俗에서 난 것인 듯하다. (京都雜志) 또 慶州에서는 婦女들이 새벽에 집집에 돌아다니면서 내더위를 사가라고 하면 주인은 대답하지 않기로 애를 썻다고 한다. 이는 陸放翁 詩註에 「立春未明에 相呼賣春困이라」 하엿으니 賣暑의 俗도 이런대 근원한 것은 아닐까? 또 義州地方에서는 젊은 男女가 새벽에 엿(飴)을 먹는데 이(齒)를 비교하는 것이라 하엿다. 또 제죽하여 두엇든 陳菜 마른 호박 표담 무 콩 팟를 삶마 먹으면 더위를 앓지 않는다고 海衣 馬蹄菜 같은 것으로 밥을 싸서 먹으면 복싸개(裏福 혹은 縛苫)이라고 하여 祈穀의 의미가 잇다고 한다. 荊楚歲時記에 「人日에 採七種菜하야 作羹이라」한 것이 오늘 조선 上元에 옴긴 것은 아닐가 한다. 또＜26＞
五穀雜飯을 종일 먹으며 또는 藥飯이라고 하여 糯米에 棗栗 油蜜을 混和하여 蒸調하여 먹는 풍습이 잇으니 이는 東京雜記에 依컨대 新羅 昭智王 10年 正月 보름날 王이 天泉亭에 가섯을 때 가마귀가 날아와서 王께 경고하엿기 때문에 上元日로 烏忌日을 삼고 糯飯을 지어 가마귀를 祭하는 것이 오늘의 時食이 되엇다고 하엿다. 시골로 가면 上元 前日에 짚을 묶어 旗纛 모양으로 맨들어 오곡의 이삭을 싸서 그 끝에는 「솜」을 걸어서 집 곁에 세워 풍년되기를 빌고 山方에서는 牛宮 뒤에 나무를 세우고 거기 穀穗와 綿花를 걸고 아이들이 새벽에 나와 노래를 부르면서 축복하는 것이엇다. 또 上元 전날밤 羅睺直星에 相當한 이가 芻靈을 맨들어 方言에 「조용이요」(處容)라고 한다. 卽 芻靈의 頭顱에 銅錢을 집어넣고 初昏에 길에 던저서 厄막이를 하는 것인데 묻 아이들은 그 돈 집어가지노라고 야단이다. 보름날 아침에 福조루 파는 것과 같은 風俗이다.

處容은 新羅때에 東海 龍子의 이름으로 「東京달 밝은 밤...」 處容歌(三國遺事)와 處容舞(樂學軌範)가 지금도 전한다. 處容을 芻靈으로 表하는 이 풍속은 상당히 오랜 것을 알겟다. 어린 男女들이 靑, 紅, 黃, 3種의 木葫蘆를 차고 다니다가 또한 上元 전날밤에 남모르게 행길에 던전서 厄막이를 한다. 또 보름 전에 「팢죽」을 흔히 끓여 먹는데 이것도 江南 옛날에 버들가지를 팢죽에 꽂아 놓고 제사하든 풍속과 近似하다. (荊楚歲時記) 서울서는 새벽에 일즉 鍾路 十字街의 흙을 파다가 네 기둥밑에 묻어 두면 부자된다는 심술궂은 풍속까지 잇엇다.

보름날 婦女들이 실(絲)을 주는 풍속이 잇으니 이것은 보름실이라 하고 보름날 개를 치면 파리가 성하고 패랜다고 해고 종일 개를 먹이지 않는데 속담에 굶주린 것을 보름개라고 하는 것이 이것이다. 그러나 어린 아기 病痟할때에는 보름날 1백집 밥을 얻어다가 절구를 타고 「개」와 마주 앉아서 「개」게 한 숫가락를 주고 저 한 숫가락씩 먹으면 건강해진다는 미신이 잇으니 아마 이런 집「개」는 배부를 것이다. 또 이날 과일 나무 가지에 돌을 끼우면 實果가 많이 맷힌다고 하는 것은 徐光啓의 農政全書에서 나온 말이다. 또 厄막이로 연(紙鳶)을 띠운다. 연은 崔瑩이 耽羅를 정벌할 적에 들어온 풍속이라고도 하지만 中國의 風錚도 이와 近似하다. 또 五色 종이를 竹骨에 부처서 바람에 돌리는 「배린갭이」가 잇으니 一名 「回回兒」라고 한다. 또 「고고매=姑姑妹(蒙古語로 鳳凰이란 뜻)」라고 해소 獨繭絲에 거워털(鵝毛)를 맺어 바람에 날리기도 한다. 또 「사」를 친다고 하여 相當한 距離에 돈을 던저서 「사」(돈이 서로 겹쌔운 것)를 처서 맞히면 이기고 잘못 맞거나 맞히지 못하면 지는 내기」도 잇다. 혹 山峽에 가면 투전도 하지만 인제는 「화토」 「麻雀」 「바둑」 「장기」 여러 가지 놀음이 보급되엇다. 三門 밖이나 阿峴에서는 편을 갈라 石戰을 하엿지만 石戰은 원래 平安道에 가장 盛하여서 「唐書 高麗傳」에도 年年 浿水上에
高麗人이 石戰을 한다는 기록이 잇어 왓지만 인제는 경찰의 嚴令으로 금지되어 버렷다. 그뿐 아니라 이날은 春川 加平의 車戰 嶺南의 葛戰이 잇엇으나 지금은 몹시 하지 않는다. 葛戰은 葛繩의 줄다리기니 比較的 아직도 많이 한다. 畿湖의 횟불싸움도 예전 絜河戱의 일종으로 지금까지 전하여＜27＞ 온다. 또 江原道의 山峽에는 아이들이 여러새 이름을 불러 쫓는 짓을 하나니 일종 祈穀의 의식이다.

이날은 初昏. 높은 山에 올라 달맞이를 하는데 달은 제일 먼저 본 이가 吉하고 달빛이 붉으면 가물고 희면 漲水가 많이 나고 또 달의 形體, 大小, 湧浮, 高低와 輪廓 四方厚薄으로써 四方 年事를 占한다고 하는데 이날을 都城에서도 土女들이 모두 달구경을 나선다. 나설 뿐 아니라 다리(橋=脚과 同音)를 밟는데 그리하면 다리(脚)病이 없어진대나? 安東地方에서는

踏橋를 하는데 한 女子가 「이게 무슨 다린고」하면 여러 사람이 「淸溪山 꾸리쇠다리(銅橋)」라고 일제히 대답한다고 하니 이는 무슨 古俗의 殘物인지? 어떤 집에서는 보름 전날부터 보름날 달의 지기까지 安宅經을 읽어서 一家의 福을 비는 집도 잇다. 또 一尺 되는 나무를 뜰에 세우고 그 月色이 午에 當한 때 그 나무 그림자로 풍흉을 결정하는 풍속도 잇다. 上元날 열두 다리를 밟으면 일년 열두달 병이 없다는 풍속도 잇다. 미신과 奇俗이 얼마나 많은지 놀라지 않을 수 없다.
