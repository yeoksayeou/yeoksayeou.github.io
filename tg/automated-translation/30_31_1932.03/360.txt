Magazine Title: 동광 제31호
Publication Date: 1932년 03월 05일
Article Title: (戱曲) 監督의 안해 ((Play) The Supervisor's Wife)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Characters
Chŏn. Factory Supervisor
Wife. Female factory worker
Daughter. (Around 4 or 5 years old)
Mop'ul
Violent Group. 3 people
Detective.

Time: Winter 1931
Place: Kyŏngsŏng (京城)
Stage
The house of Chŏn's family. From the front right, the kitchen, inner room, main floor (maru), and opposite room are visible. Next to the kitchen, there is a small jangdokdae (area for pottery jars).
In front of the front veranda (toe'maru), there is a narrow courtyard, and beyond that, another narrow yard. To the left, a wall is visible, and where the wall meets the house at the corner of the opposite room, a side gate (ilgakmun) is closed. Outside the wall is a wide road. In plausible spots like the yard and corners of the maru, plausible household items are scattered.
Early morning, before the electric lights go out. When the curtain rises, Chŏn, holding a lunchbox (bentokkŭremi) under his arm, is preparing to leave and stands on the courtyard, glaring at the inner room. (4-5 seconds of silence)
Chŏn. (In a suddenly angry voice) Aren't you coming out?
There is no answer from the room. Chŏn stands huffing and puffing for a moment, then abruptly steps up onto the maru and flings open the door to the inner room. The daughter, frightened, peeks out timidly.
Chŏn. Aren't you coming out?
Wife. (Appearing, holding her daughter's head with one hand and gripping the doorframe with the other) Why are you making such a fuss, demanding I come out? Such a fuss.
Chŏn. Who's making a fuss?
Wife. (Pretends not to hear, just stares at a distant mountain.)
Chŏn. (Huffing and puffing, then forcing himself to be calm, speaking as if reasoning gently) Don't be like that, just go. Listen to me when I ask nicely.
Wife. (Without looking) I don't want to. I can't go.
Chŏn. Why can't you go?
Wife. (Silent)
Chŏn. Come out now. Even if you quit, just finish up this matter after it's settled. (Facing her) Please, consider my position... Look, <105> I'm nominally the supervisor, trusted by the higher-ups to handle things, but my own wife sides with those wretched men and women, talking about forming alliances and whatnot...
Wife. Why? Is participating in the strike wrong? I don't know how grateful and fearful you are of the company, but we have nothing to be grateful for, nothing to fear...
Chŏn. (Cutting her off) Look, that's why I'm saying, just quit after this matter is settled, didn't I say that?
Wife. Why would I quit after it's settled? I'll work even better then. Do you think we went on strike intending to quit the factory?
Chŏn. Look, whether you work later or not, just come out now. The company harasses me daily, asking how a nominal supervisor can manage numerous other workers when he can't even handle his own wife.
Wife. (Smiling lightly) Then stop enduring that unpleasant situation and join our side.
Chŏn. (Ignoring her) Come on, come out now. (To the daughter) You come out too, okay? Go play at auntie's house again today, okay? Come here.
Daughter. (Starts to come out.)
Wife. (Holding her back) Where are you going! Don't go. Stay home with mother.
Chŏn. (Starting to get angry) Ah, are you going to keep this up?
Wife. (Doesn't look at him.)
Chŏn. (Raising his voice) Are you going to keep this up?
Wife. What is there to keep up or not keep up!
Chŏn. What?
Wife. What about?
Chŏn. (Pressing) Are you going to refuse to go and keep this up?
Wife. I'm not going.
Chŏn. (Shouting) Why aren't you going?
Wife. Because I have reasons not to go.
Chŏn. What reasons are there not to go?
Wife. It's none of your business.
Chŏn. Still not coming out?
Wife. (Acts as if she doesn't see him.)
Chŏn. (Threateningly) Ah, this bitch...
Wife. Why are you calling me this bitch, that bitch, right from the morning! What kind of spectacle is this? I've seen it all.
Chŏn. What? (Glares.) Don't you know that living even this much is thanks to the company, you bitch? Why are you making such a fuss for no reason? Such a fuss?
Wife. Hmph, I'm supposed to be so grateful. (Aside) How can a person be so thoughtless!
Chŏn. (Ignoring her) Good-for-nothing men and women, for no reason (Pause) what's this ridiculous alliance nonsense. You damned bastards!
Wife. Look, just go yourself and work hard like an ox or a horse. Stop meddling in others' affairs.
Chŏn. You bitch, I'm saying this because I'm getting blamed because of you, you bitch.
Wife. (Aside) Even men without wives manage to work at the factory just fine!
Chŏn. (Glares, then) Are you still not going to come out quickly and just run your mouth like this?
Wife. (Silent)
Chŏn. (As if about to explode) That damned bitch! Where is she!
Wife. (Confronting him) Look, why are you cursing like this since morning? Am I your servant's child?
Chŏn. So what if you're not, you bitch.
Wife. I'm seeing all sorts of nasty behavior. Just because I listen quietly, it gets worse and worse.
Chŏn. (About to retort, but startled by a siren sounding from somewhere, pulls out his watch, looks at it, then hurriedly turns, walks towards the side gate, and says) We'll see about this, you bitch. <106>
Wife. (Closing the room door) Like I'd be so scared!
Chŏn. Just you wait, you bitch. (Stomps off, flings open the side gate, and leaves.)
The stage is empty for 5-6 seconds, then Mop'ul appears on the road outside the left wall, lingering.
Mop'ul. (Peeking timidly through the side gate) Hem.
Wife. (From the room) Who is it?
Daughter. (Opens the room door and looks out.)
Mop'ul. Little girl, is your mother in?
Wife. (Voice) Who is it? (Comes out.)
Mop'ul. It's me.
Wife. Oh my, I wondered who it was... Please come in.
Mop'ul. (Enters the yard.)
Wife. But how come you're here so early?
Mop'ul. Yes... (Smiling faintly) I came early— I was watching the fight...
Wife. (Laughs along, but annoyed) You heard everything. Look at that wretched man.
Mop'ul. Yes, I heard everything.
Wife. And how can he curse so foully! (Asking) So?
Mop'ul. Yes, about that matter.
Wife. Yes. (18 lines omitted - original text)
Wife. (Closes the door firmly and goes into the room.)
The stage gradually darkens, then becomes completely dark, before slowly starting to brighten again. The lighting after it fully brightens should indicate twilight. The wife is faintly visible, cooking rice in the kitchen. A moment of silence. Chŏn bursts through the side gate, which he had left closed, like a thunderbolt. His clothes are all torn, and he has injuries on his face. He is slightly drunk. The wife pokes her head out from the kitchen, looks out, then appears, wiping her hands on her apron.
Chŏn. (Collapses onto the veranda, furious, glaring at his wife.)
Wife. (Casually) You're back now?
Chŏn. (Huffing and puffing, just glares.)
Wife. (Ignoring him) Where did you get into another fight? What happened to your clothes!
Chŏn. That bitch will be the death of someone.
Wife. (Feigning surprise) Why, are you upset again because you couldn't kill someone? Hurry inside. I'll get you some clothes. (Starts to step onto the maru.)
Chŏn. (Shouting) Where are you going?
Wife. Why?
Chŏn. Why? Hey, you bitch, get out right now.
Wife. Why are you acting up like this again?
Chŏn. Huh, that bitch, she really could kill someone. (Stares blankly up at her, then) Get out right now before I kill you.
Wife. Are you crazy! Why are you telling me to get out for no reason?
Chŏn. (Threateningly) Still not going to leave?
Wife. (Confronting him) Why should I leave? Why, is my behavior bad? Why are you telling me to leave for no reason, just telling me to leave?
Chŏn. Ah, you bitch, do you think I don't know?
Wife. Know what? What? What is this about? What about, huh?
Chŏn. You bitch, what were you doing at the company today?
Wife. So what if I went? I went because I had business there.
Chŏn. (Stares blankly up at her) Look at that bitch. (Pause) You bitch, how dare those words come out of your mouth?
Wife. Why? Why?
Chŏn. (Stands up abruptly, takes a step closer, speaking deliberately, syllable by syllable) Ah, you bitch. Look, after making me hated by the company by joining those bastards' side, you then join those same bitches and bastards today to come and destroy the company?
Wife. What if I went to destroy the company? Is it your company? Worrying about everything.
Chŏn. (Ignoring her, stepping closer) And you bitch, you joined the side of those bastards who swarmed like a pack of dogs <107> and beat me so severely?
Wife. If you got beaten, did you get beaten by me? Why are you acting like a fool, getting beaten up for no reason, and then taking it out on your wife!
Chŏn. (Sits down heavily) This won't do. I don't even want to lift a finger against a bitch like you. Just get out. The company says if they don't kick you out, they'll kick me out too. (Pause) Get out when I'm telling you nicely. (Lowers his head, tormented.)
Wife. Hmph, you just keep saying one foolish thing after another.
Chŏn. Ah, this bitch.
Wife. What's wrong with that? (Turns to go into the kitchen.)
Chŏn. (Rushes at her, grabbing her hair with his left hand) What, you bitch?
Wife. (Almost falls backward, but grabs the kitchen door, barely rights herself, turns around, and screams.) Why are you hitting me, why, huh? Why hit me, why?
At this moment, the Violent Group gathers on the road outside the wall, peeking through the side gate, whispering among themselves and watching.
Chŏn. You bitch! (Kicks the wife's thigh.)
Wife. (Screams and collapses.)
Daughter. (Opens the room door, rubbing her sleepy eyes, sees the scene, and runs down towards her mother.)
Chŏn ignores this and continues to hit and kick his wife. The wife, while being beaten, gets enraged, clings to her husband's leg, biting and pinching, fighting back fiercely. In the midst of this, the daughter gets caught in the crossfire, gets hit by stray blows, and cries hysterically.)
Violent Group A. Now that's worth watching.
Violent Group B. That man is working hard for us right now.
Violent Group C. It's probably sweeter for the wife than falling into our hands.
Violent Group D. I didn't know that woman was Chŏn's wife.
Violent Group B. Someone's family is heading for ruin.
Violent Group D. Let's go.
Violent Group C. Wait, isn't this interesting to watch?
Violent Group D. Watching is fine, but let's hurry and go beat up a few guys ourselves.
Violent Group A. Let's go. (Unfolds a piece of paper in his hand) This time, there are two guys living in one house.
All. Two guys are nothing.
Violent Group Members. (Exit)
Chŏn and his wife are still fighting. Chŏn repeatedly shouts, "Get out!" After a while, the two somehow separate. The daughter clings to her mother, crying. The wife tidies her hair, pins it up, and hugs her daughter.
Chŏn. (Standing, huffing and puffing, glaring) Is that bitch still going to stand there and not leave?
Wife. (Puts down the daughter she was holding) I'll leave. I'll leave, but I won't just leave like this. (Runs into the room and starts smashing furniture.)
Daughter. (Follows as if tumbling after her, clings to her again, crying.)
Chŏn runs back into the room, and they fight again. In the dimly lit room, the sounds of crashing, shouting, cursing, and a child's crying mix together as the entangled figures flail wildly. After a while, the wife runs outside. Her clothes are completely torn to shreds. The daughter follows, crying, but seems exhausted and just stomps her feet on the maru, crying. The wife goes into the kitchen, cursing under her breath, and violently smashes kitchenware with a clatter. Chŏn remains seated in the room. The wife comes out of the kitchen and then smashes the pottery jars in the jangdokdae. After smashing everything thoroughly, she tidies her hair, pins it up, and adjusts her remaining clothes.
Chŏn. (Running out of the room) You bitch, now that you've smashed everything, why aren't you getting out, you bitch?
Wife. I'm leaving even if you told me to stay, like this. (Stomps towards the side gate.) <108>
Daughter. (Wails louder, runs down, and clings to her mother's skirt.)
Wife. (Without trying to push her away) Go. Go live well with your father.
Chŏn. Do you think we won't live well without you, you bitch? Don't worry and just get out.
Wife. (Shoves the clinging daughter away forcefully) Go! Go to your father!
Daughter. (Clings on and doesn't let go.)
Chŏn. (To the daughter) Come here, you girl!
Daughter. (Clings even tighter, calling for her mother and crying.)
Wife. (Looks down at her, tears welling up.)
Detective. (Entering through the side gate) Is Mr. Chŏn here?
Chŏn. (Coming out) Yes? Who are you?
Detective. I'm from the station (署). (Looks the wife over) Is this your wife?
Chŏn. (Hesitantly) Yes.
Wife. Why do you ask?
Detective. (Annoyed) Why do I ask? Come with me. (Grabs the wife's elbow and pulls) Let's go.
Wife. (Shakes him off forcefully) Let go! I'll go where you want me to go.
Detective. (Sternly) Insolent woman! Let's go quickly. (Urges her on.)
Wife. (Pushing the daughter away) Don't cry. I'll be back soon.
Daughter. (Clings to her, crying.)
Chŏn. (Comes and picks up the struggling, inseparable daughter.)
Wife. (Looking back) Don't cry, I'll be back soon. I'll buy candy and come back soon.
Detective and Wife exit through the side gate. Chŏn, unable to console the crying daughter, stands blankly, watching them leave. (Curtain falls slowly) <114>

**Summary**

This 1932 Korean play, "The Supervisor's Wife," depicts the intense conflict between a factory foreman, Chŏn, and his wife, who is participating in a labor strike against the company Chŏn works for. Chŏn, caught between company pressure and his wife's solidarity with the strikers, demands she stop striking and return to work. Her refusal leads to escalating verbal and brutal physical abuse, culminating in Chŏn throwing her out. Amidst the domestic violence, observed briefly by a "Violent Group" (likely strike-breakers or anti-labor thugs), a detective arrives and arrests the wife, presumably for her role in the strike or related disturbances. The play ends with the distraught husband holding their crying child, watching his wife being led away.

From a historical perspective, the play is significant for its raw portrayal of labor conflict during the Japanese colonial period in Korea. It highlights the harsh realities faced by workers, the precarious position of Korean supervisors mediating between Japanese capital/management and Korean labor, and the deep social and domestic fissures these conflicts could create. The involvement of both organized violence (the "Violent Group") and state power (the detective arresting the wife) against striking workers provides insight into the suppression of labor movements at the time. Historians might find the depiction of gender dynamics within class struggle, the specific vocabulary of labor disputes ("동맹파업"), and the intersection of domestic violence with political/economic strife particularly revealing about colonial Korean society.
