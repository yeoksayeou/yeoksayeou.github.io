Two Little Soldiers

Returning from the newspaper office, I find the children taking a nap. The older child (six years old) lies sleeping, still wearing his military swords, bow slung across his back, and staff officer's cord dangling. He looks like a soldier exhausted from many days of fighting in a war, collapsing anywhere to sleep during a brief lull in the battle. As if ready to jump up and rush to the front line the moment the bugle sounds.

One military sword is a can opener. The other is a white iron pump for spraying disinfectant called "Supurei" (Spray). The bow is made from the handle of a fruit basket made of wisteria, strung with a cord. The staff officer's cord is a rubber catheter. The younger soldier, four years old, having had his good sword and bow taken by his older brother, the officer, wears only a broken ruler as his single military sword and has no bow. Perhaps he plays in his dreams with the white iron pump and bow that his brother would never let him have, no matter how much he asked. When I watch these children playing soldiers, my heart feels uneasy. It's because, in their current state, they cannot truly become soldiers. Is this, too, the futile sentiment of a father?

The Hope of Chosŏn

K is a man who earns a monthly salary of 30 wŏn doing dictionary compilation work for a certain academic society (모학회). He graduated from Yŏnhŭi College (연희전문학교). For someone supporting a wife and children, it goes without saying that a 30 wŏn salary is insufficient for living expenses. Just then, a certain newspaper company (모신문사) called K, intending to hire him. This was because K had applied to that newspaper company three years prior.

The president of the newspaper company asked K to join the company. The salary offered was more than 50 wŏn.

However, the president was surprised by young K's unexpected refusal.

"Sir. I cannot leave my work at the academic society. The society trained me for a whole year to give me the skills needed for that job. If I were to leave now, they would have to bring in a new person and train them for another year. If that happens, the project will suffer setbacks for a year. Not only that, but it won't be easy to find someone willing to come for a 30 wŏn salary. With what my wife and I receive, we are not at the point of starvation, so I cannot bear to betray the trust bestowed (하락할 신의) upon me by that society and supervisor Mr. Mo (모씨)."

"Good! Good! You are right!"

The president of the newspaper company, who had been urging K to join, cried out, deeply moved.

Isn't a person like K truly the hope of Chosŏn? <25>

**Summary**

This article presents two short reflections by the author, Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu). The first describes his bittersweet feelings watching his two young sons play soldiers with makeshift toys, saddened by the thought that their circumstances (likely colonial Korea) prevent them from genuinely aspiring to be soldiers. The second anecdote praises a young college graduate, K, who refused a significantly higher salary at a newspaper to honor his commitment to a low-paying dictionary project at an academic society, citing loyalty and the project's continuity. The author holds up K's integrity as the "hope of Chosŏn." From a historical perspective, the article reveals the underlying national consciousness and anxieties of Koreans under Japanese rule. The father's lament about his sons' inability to be 'real' soldiers hints at the limitations imposed on Koreans, while the story of K emphasizes moral integrity and dedication to cultural/scholarly work (like dictionary compilation) as patriotic virtues and sources of hope when political or military avenues were constrained.

어린 두 병정
신문사에서 돌아오니 아이들은 낮잠을 잔다. 큰 아이(여슷 살)는 군도들을 찬 채로 활을 매인 채로 참모ㅅ줄을 느린 채로 누어잔다. 마치 전쟁에서 여러날 싸움에 지이친 병정이 잠시 전쟁이 쉬인 것을 긔회로 아모데나 쓰러져서 잠이 든 것 같다. 취군나발 소리만 들리면 곳 뛰어 일어나 전선에 나서랴는 듯이.
군도는 하나는 통조림 떼는 것이오. 또 하나는 스푸레이라는 소독약 뿌리는 백철 폼푸다. 활은 등으로 만든 과일ㅅ광주리의 손잡이에 시위를 메인 것이다. 참모ㅅ줄은 고무 카테텔이다. 네살되는 작은 병정은 좋은 군도와 활을 상관인 형에게 다 빼앗기고 쪼개어진 자막대기를 단 한 자루의 군도삼아 차고 활은 없다. 그는 아모리 형에게 달라고 해도 주지 않던 백철 폼푸와 활을 꿈에나 가지고 노는가. 나는 이 아이들이 병정놀이 하는 것을 보면 맘이 언짢다. 그들은 아직 상태로는 정말 병정은 되어 볼 수 없는 까닭이다. 그것도 부질없는 아비의 맘인가.

조선의 희망
K는 월급 30원을 받고 모학회에서 사전편찬하는 일을 보는 사람이다. 그는 연희전문학교를 졸업하엿다. 처자를 거느린 사람으로 30원 월급이 생활비에 부족할 것은 말할 것도 없다. 마츰 모신문사에서 K를 채용하랴고 불럿다. 그는 3년 전부터 모신문사에 지원하엿던 까닭이다.
모신문사의 사장은 K에게 입사하기를 청하엿다. 월급은 50원보다 많은 수엿다.
그러나 사장은 K청년의 의외의 거절에 놀래엇다.
「선생님. 저는 모학회 일을 내어 놓을 수가 없습니다. 모학회에서는 1년 동안이나 저를 가르쳐서 그 일을 할 만한 힘을 주엇습니다. 그런데 이제 제가 그 일을 내어 놓으면 또 새 사람을 다려다가 1년을 가르치지 아니하면 아니 될 것입니다. 그리되면 그 사업은 1년간 낭패될 것입니다. 그 뿐 아니라 30원 월급을 받고 올만한 사람도 쉽지 아니 할 것입니다. 제 내외가 받는 것으로 밥은 굶을 지경이 아니니 참아 그 회와 주관자 모씨에게 하락할 신의를 져바릴 수가 없습니다」하엿다.
「좋다! 좋다! 그대 말이 옳다!」
하고 K의 입사를 권하던 모신문사의 사장은 감격하야 소리를 질럿다.
K와 같은 사람이야말로 조선의 희망이 아니냐.＜25＞
