Song of Youth
						Yu To-sun

Behind, a picture-like mountain stands,
And before, a field like honey-milk stretches out.
Working upon this, holding hope,
The one who will greet the promised new morning –
That is youth, like the just-rising sunbeam.

To the right, fragrant flowers smile,
And to the left, beautiful birds sing.
Loving within this, bearing passion,
The one who will craft the promised dream of happiness –<69>
That is youth, like a seabird of the ocean.

Above, mysterious clouds fly,
And below, a river like a bolt of silk flows.
Thinking amidst this, embracing courage,
The one who will gain the victory of the promised battle –
That is youth, like a warrior of the battlefield.<70>
<69-70>

**Summary**

This poem, "Song of Youth," uses vivid natural imagery (mountains, fields, flowers, birds, clouds, rivers) and heroic metaphors (seabird, warrior) to celebrate the vitality, hope, passion, and courage embodied by youth. Each stanza presents a scene and equates the potential actions within that scene—working towards a new day, loving passionately, fighting courageously—with the essence of youth. From a colonial history perspective, the poem is interesting as an expression of hope and resilience during a period of Japanese rule. While not overtly political, its focus on a "promised new morning," "promised dream of happiness," and "promised battle's victory," associated with the strength and potential of Korean youth, could be interpreted as a subtle yearning for a brighter future or national renewal, offering a glimpse into the spirit of the times.
