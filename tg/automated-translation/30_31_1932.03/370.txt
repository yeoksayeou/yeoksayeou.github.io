中國할머니 (Chinese Grandmother)
						中國 秋楓(作), 崔昌圭(譯) (China Qiu Feng (Author), Ch'oe Ch'anggyu (Translator))

When the dark shadow of the crumbling ruins of Kongwangchae (公王寨) stretched into the eastern village like black paper, the old grandmother returned from outside, just like a mother hen. She panted, huffing and puffing like an ox, her body trembling, her head nodding.

She was a day laborer who took on piecework. In spring, she weeded wheat fields for others and harvested wheat; in summer, covering her thinning hair with a wide lotus leaf, she fertilized others' rice paddies, carried lunch to the fields, and fed the farmers. In autumn, she planted taro for others, carried manure, and built haystacks. In winter, she could manage to earn something by mending clothes for farmers or by looking after children at the landlord's house.

It was now early autumn, a time when the southern weather could reach 80-90 degrees [Fahrenheit]. Like a young person, she wore a thick hemp jacket, faded, patched, and mended all over. Through the holes here and there, one could see skin, blackened by the sun, peeling like pine bark.

She rushed home and sat down, looking extremely tired. Sitting on a small stool with her back bent as if broken, she looked like a horseshoe set upright. Her head drooped down to her knees, and in her hand, she held a small bundle wrapped in a water taro leaf.

Whether intoxicated by the cool evening breeze blowing through the holes in the collapsed earthen wall or simply exhausted, she quickly fell fast asleep, even letting out faint breaths like a cat.

She had no children. Her son had been caught and shot dead ten years ago during the revolution, when the revolution underwent a change. He died too cruelly. Before dying, the *yangban* scoundrels (량반놈) poured kerosene into his nostrils; he bled from all seven orifices, and every single blood vessel ended up filled with blue kerosene. Before pouring the kerosene, the *yangban* scoundrels beat him with leather whips and kicked his lower belly, thighs, and buttocks with leather shoes. Before the whipping, the *yangban* scoundrels also hung him upside down like a spider. Finally, when they shot him dead,<109> it was merely using an already dead body as a target! Seventy or eighty people died with him. Some were burned to death, and some were buried alive.

The old grandmother also had no daughter-in-law. Her daughter-in-law had bled to death seventeen years ago. It was due to excessive fatigue and lack of postpartum care. Before dying, her lower body bled like a red stream for a full day and night, so when she died, her whole body was as white as paper.

However, the grandmother had an eighteen-year-old grandson, Chang Senyŏng (張森榮). He was a sturdy man like his father. He was clever and could work with very ingenious plans. Even before his father (chairman of the farmers' association) was captured, he had been a propagandist for the farmers' association. Having received a revolutionary baptism while the revolution was still proceeding secretly, he had already become an influential underground activist. When the revolution erupted, he led the well-organized farmers secretly and participated in the revolution...

The old grandmother woke up, startled by some dream. She coughed harshly, *khak khak*. Tears glistened in the dim evening light. The skin of her lips, like dried tangerine peel in the wind, trembled. The deeply wrinkled skin of her face twitched.

She looked at the small bundle in her hand and sighed very weakly.

"What are those fellows doing? Revolution? Ai! Those possessed by mad goblins (독갑이). Ten years ago, they took the life of Pok'a (福兒 - Senyŏng's father, grandmother's son). I just became lonely! Now those fellows are doing some goblin-like revolution again, either making it impossible for this old me to sustain my life, or his own (Senyŏng's) life will be lost. Ai— I really can't stand how pitiful it is!..."

She cut herself off, stood up, went to a small shelf, took out a box of matches, lit a fire in the brazier, and cooked evening rice.

This kind of thought was natural for someone like the old grandmother. What had the revolution given to the poor? Looking back at the last revolution, there were certainly no gains. Only several thousand, several tens of thousands of poor people died most good-heartedly. The darkness of society and the suffering of the people were the same as before the revolution; rather, due to the losses from war, the people's suffering only increased further. Who knows how many people were more unfortunate than the grandmother.

However, this fiery demand of revolution still clung tightly to the path of the poor, like lacquer paint. Not only did they not try to abandon the revolution, but they rather embraced it more fervently and prepared to cry out this fiery demand with roaring voices.

What was the reason for this? The old grandmother would never understand this issue. In any case, she would never understand the reason why her grandson was still doing the revolution.

—Are they really mad? Is everyone in the world mad? Why do they say it's good even while throwing away their own lives?...

The old grandmother muttered this while gazing at the brazier.

On the brazier sat a cracked earthenware pot (뚝박이). The water inside the pot was boiling. A few grains of rice were tumbling inside the pot. Underneath the pot, dry bamboo and dry wood chips were burning. The flames licked the pot like the tip of an ox's tongue, occasionally extending into the pot.

She untied and opened the small bundle she held. This was originally leftover cold rice obtained from the master's house where she worked. Very carefully, she poured the cold rice into the earthenware pot, measured the water level once with her aged finger, and then covered the pot with a piece of wooden board. Bowing her head, she returned to the <110> stool where she had first sat.

She spoke again in an extremely weak voice.
—That rascal Senyŏng doesn't listen at all. Everyone is afraid of this kind of work, like walking with a noose around your neck, but only this foolish child, like a pig, doesn't know fear. Ai! It's just a punishment for sins against Heaven for a thousand, ten thousand years, unable to receive salvation! Other people's children work well and are always with their parents, but only this good-for-nothing in the world just throws away his home as he pleases, and once he leaves, he doesn't come back for two or three days. The more I think, the more dumbfounded I am! This old woman still can't rest for a moment and has to use up even the little strength she has just to eat!

She shed a few more tears, looked at the earthenware pot from which white foam was rising, sighed once, and spoke again.
—If only this pig-like rascal wasn't here, I wouldn't be ruined. I wouldn't be looked down upon.

She also thought about how the villagers disliked her after the revolution. Many villagers who resented her son, or people with power in society, all looked down on her, saying her pig-dog belly had raised a fellow who didn't know propriety or Heaven, breaking the power and authority passed down from ancient times.

What made the old grandmother most furious was that the few relatives and kin she had now all resented and hated her, viewing her as they would a snake. As if the old grandmother's whole body were covered in long, poisonous thorns, they wouldn't come near her at all and saw the old grandmother as an enemy!

—Ai!...

She became so angry thinking about it that words failed her; she just shook her head vigorously.

—Ai!...

She cried. She cried thinking about several past events. Curses bursting from her frustration mixed with her panting and sobbing, hanging in the dark air like black ink poured into clear water, not scattering for a while.

The brazier fire leaped up brightly, illuminating the old grandmother's face. A dark light flickered on the old grandmother's sun-blackened face.

She wiped the tears from her face with the hem of her clothes and stood up pitifully, went to the side of the brazier, pulled out a dry bamboo stick from the fire, and lit the sesame oil lamp under the corner of the brazier. The weak light lacked the strength to drive away the darkness of the room; dark remnants of the night lingered in the lattice window and the holes in the walls.

The grandmother placed the earthenware pot on the wooden dining table. Steam from the cooked rice rose from the pot. Besides this, a bowl of cold, salty kimchi lay on the table...

After eating, she took the lamp and went to the other room. This was, of course, her bedroom. There was an old, damaged wooden bed, and on the bed was a pile of old cotton. Beside it was another bed made of wooden planks—three floorboards laid across. On it, apart from a worn-out mat with several large holes, there was nothing else. The room was as damaged as the kitchen where she cooked; large holes in the ceiling let in sunlight, so when it rained, the old grandmother had nowhere to sleep and couldn't help but wake up from a sound sleep. The sun peeked through the holes every day, aiming at her eyes and watching her. The walls were randomly cracked and looked very dangerous.

At the head of the bed lay a broken basket (바굼이) containing old clothes.<111>

The old grandmother placed the lamp on a long stool, wandered around the room once lonelily, then went to the wooden bed and lay down.

Perhaps because she was hardened by sorrow, she now lay down as if nothing had happened, just closing her eyes tightly as if asleep and panting.

At this moment, a young farmer entered from outside the door. Who could it be? Needless to say, it was her grandson, Chang Senyŏng (張森榮).

Simultaneously, three other young farmers stood outside the door. One must have been the elementary school student Hwang Kŭmhŭi (黃金熙). However, in the dark night, they couldn't be seen clearly.

Senyŏng went to his grandmother's room, but the grandmother lay still without moving. He approached the bed and carefully observed his grandmother for a moment. If he hadn't seen the sesame oil lamp lit, he would surely have been greatly startled. He hadn't returned for three days already, so he wouldn't have known even if his grandmother had died.

However, seeing the sesame oil lamp, he felt relieved. He glanced briefly at the old grandmother's face and knew she wasn't asleep yet. So he spoke in a low voice.
—Grandmother, have you eaten?

The old grandmother crawled up from the bed. Showing a very angry expression, she glared sharply at Senyŏng once and then said displeasedly.
—How could I have eaten!

Senyŏng seemed very distressed by these words, lowered his head, and tears already flowed.
—Grandmother, why are you angry? Are you unwell?

The grandmother seemed even angrier and said.
—You ask me why I'm angry? You don't need to come ask if I know you again. If you even thought of me, wouldn't you let me starve to death right where I sit! You crazy goblin-possessed (독갑이 들린) fool, do you even know I'm starving because of you!

Senyŏng understood this story clearly. He took another step forward, sat on the edge of the bed, and looking at the tears flowing straight down from his grandmother's deeply sunken eyes, asked anxiously in a comforting tone.
—Grandmother, I don't understand clearly. What do you mean I've caused you to starve? But I haven't been able to support you well, Grandmother, nor provide comfort, and instead made you do hard labor. However, I truly believe that we must put a complete end to this miserable life we lead, so that future people will not suffer such hardships again, never be hungry and cold their entire lives!

He believed this firmly. He thought that for the sake of the future, or the society that would appear very soon, he couldn't afford to look back at the present suffering.

However, hearing these words, the old grandmother became even angrier.
—Are you still pretending not to know? I already told you goblin-possessed fools to stop your revolution or whatever it is. But you didn't listen to a single word, and killing the father wasn't enough, now you're trying to kill this old me who can't even die properly! Get a grip! Tomorrow, I'll starve. Ch'ilsso (七嫂 - wife of the landlord in the eastern village) won't give me work anymore, saying my pig-dog belly raised you and didn't teach you any lessons! Tch, even though I'm seventy or eighty, old and worn out, I still get scorned because of you!

The old grandmother started crying again. She lay back down on the bed. <112> Senyŏng also started crying. The air in the room became grim.

Senyŏng paid attention to some small point. That small point gradually grew larger. It grew larger in his eyes, in his mind, eventually covering the whole world. He lowered his head, thought silently for a moment, and then spoke again, as if persuading, as if comforting.
—Yes. I know well that those two-legged beast scoundrels who look down on people use such tactics to threaten us, threaten and oppress us. But Grandmother, please don't blame the revolution too much. The revolution is our way to live. At the same time, this kind of thing cannot be suppressed anyway. No matter how much they harm and break the revolution, Grandmother, I hope you will muster a little more strength and not be saddened at all by their threats! Do you think that because Father died, I should serve those scoundrels well? Or do you think they will kill me just as they killed Father? I'm not scared at all. What those scoundrels killed were just a few fathers. But the people who oppose them, the people who continue the fathers' work, are not just a few, but endlessly many!

Senyŏng spoke, looking at the old grandmother. The old grandmother was panting, *hŏk hŏk*, and weeping even more intensely. Senyŏng added another sentence consecutively.
—Revolution is beneficial. This is the way to truly liberate us poor people, a thorough means of liberation...

The grandmother suddenly crawled up, as if remembering something.
—What? Are you saying what you fellows are doing is a good thing? Beneficial to the poor? Ai! Only a goblin (독갑이) would believe that. I won't believe it until I close my eyes. Your father was shot dead, Fourth Uncle Ku (丘四叔) was burned to death, I Yuk (李六) was buried alive (산장)... This is the reward for you fellows rushing around day and night without rest back then!

It was true, Senyŏng didn't know what to do. He knew that the previous revolution had naturally caused the old grandmother to despair. The *yangban* scoundrels still committed evil deeds between villages and towns, and everything the old society possessed hadn't changed one bit, but rather had become even worse.

The old grandmother continued to cry and lay down again as if tired, panting.
—Grandmother, Grandmother, your words are not wrong at all. The past revolution certainly made people despair. Many useful people couldn't help but die. They couldn't help but lose their lives under the swords and bullets of those scoundrels (the reactionary forces). But that's because we were sold out, deceived, and also destroyed due to the insidious bourgeoisie! We failed... we couldn't thoroughly annihilate the evil forces.

The people standing outside called out two or three times in succession. Senyŏng stood up and looked at the old grandmother. He instructed the old grandmother in a soft voice.
"Grandmother, please calm down a bit! I'm going to Kongwangchae (公王寨) now."

Saying this, he went outside.

The grandmother lay down alone again.

A little later, without turning off the lamp, she got up and wandered around the room.
—Where else could such misfortune exist? That goblin-possessed (독갑이 들린) rascal, tomorrow, aigoo! What will I do tomorrow? There's no rice. And there has to be someone who asks me to work!

She walked around, muttering to herself. What was she to do? There wasn't even a place to beg for food. A few poor people treated her kindly, showing more sympathy than ever for this pitiful old grandmother, but their own stomachs<113> never saw a full day; every day, they were all just patting empty bellies.

—I really can't understand their intentions. Those goblin-possessed (독갑이 들린놈들) fools. How could that possibly be a truly good thing? No matter how much they persuade me, will I believe it?

She walked closer to the bed again.

Bang! Bang! ... Someone was pounding continuously on the door from outside.

The old grandmother, startled, crawled up from the bed and asked strangely.
—Who is it?

The voice outside was very rough, carrying a fearful premonition.
—Open! Open the door!

The old grandmother groped along the wall, went to the door, and opened it. Three men holding lights and carrying long guns burst in noisily. The old grandmother stood behind the door, trembling, unable to even look at them directly.

Among the three who entered, Ch'ilsso's (七嫂) husband—Chang T'iŏnbu (張天富)—opened his large mouth and yelled.
—Is Senyŏng home, the son of a bitch?

—He's not here. That child hasn't come back for four days already.

The three men searched, flipping over all the bed boards. The grandmother stood quietly, just panting, *hŏk hŏk*. In a short while, they left. (End)
(The original title was 『할머니』 (Grandmother), which has been changed to 中國할머니 (Chinese Grandmother).) <114>

**Summary**

This 1932 translated story depicts the suffering of an elderly Chinese grandmother whose son was killed in a past revolution and whose grandson, Senyŏng, is now actively involved in revolutionary activities. She lives in poverty, faces social ostracism due to her family's political involvement, and deeply fears for her grandson's life, seeing revolution only as a source of death and hardship based on her past experiences. Senyŏng tries to convince her of the revolution's necessity for future liberation, but she remains unconvinced. The story ends tensely with authorities, led by a local landlord, searching for Senyŏng. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this story, translated by a Korean (Ch'oe Ch'anggyu) and published in a Korean magazine (Donggwang), is interesting as it reflects a contemporary Korean engagement with narratives of revolution and social upheaval in neighboring China. Historians might find it valuable for understanding how themes of revolution, class struggle (poor peasants vs. landlords/gentry-'yangban'), state suppression, and generational conflict were presented and consumed in colonial Korea, potentially resonating with or informing local socio-political sentiments, albeit through the lens of a Chinese setting.

中國할머니
						中國 秋楓(作), 崔昌圭(譯)
公王寨의 문허진 터의 시컴한 그림자가 껌은 종이와도 같이 동편마을로 벋어 들어올 때 늙은 할머니는 마치 한마리 암닭과도 같이 밖으로부터 돌아왓다. 그는 소가 헐덕이듯이 헉헉 헐덕이며 몸을 부들부들 떨며 머리를 껏덕껏덕 움직이엇다.
그는 조금식 끊어 맛는 일의 품파리군이엇다. 봄에는 그는 밀밧 속에서 남의 집 김을 매고 밀을 비어주엇고, 여름에는 넓은 련닙으로 버서진 머리를 가리우고 남의 집 벼에 거름을 주고, 밭으로 졈심을 날너다 농군들을 먹엿다. 가을에는 남의 집 토란을 심어 주고, 똥거름을 날르고, 집낫가리를 세워주엇다. 겨울에는 그는 농군들의 옷을 꼬매주거나 혹은 지주의 집에 가서 어린애를 보아 주거나 하야 얼마만큼은 버리를 궁리해 낼 수 잇엇다.
지금은 초가을이라 남방의 날ㅅ새 같으면 더위가 80-90도나 되는 때엿다. 그는 절믄 사람과도 같이 빛이 낡고 얼넉덜넉 깁고 또 기운 굴근 배적삼을 입고 잇다. 여기 저기 뚤린 구녁으로는 햇빛에 식커머케 타서 소나무깝질과도 같이 버서진 깝질을 볼 수 잇엇다.
그는 집으로 달려와서 매우 피곤한 듯이 앉엇다. 부러진 듯이 등을 꼬부리고 적은 의자에 앉은 모양은 마치 말굽신을 세워 놓은 듯 하엿다. 머리는 무릅 우까지 숙으러지고 손에는 물토란닙으로 싼 적은 꾸럼이 한 개를 들고 잇다.
묺어진 바탐벽 구녁으로부터 불어 들어오는 저녁 서늘한 바람에 취하엿는지 또는 피곤하엿는지 그는 아주 얼른 잠이 폭 들엇다. 고양이와 같이 아주 가는 숨소리까지 내이며.
그는 자식이 없다. 자식은 잇해 전 그때 혁명 때에 혁명에 변화가 생겻을 그때 잡히어 가서 총에 맞어 죽엇다. 그는 넘어도 참혹히 죽엇다. 죽기 전에는 량반놈이 코구녁에 석유를 부어넣어 그는 일곱구녁으로 피를 흘리엇고 핏줄이란 핏줄은 모다 시퍼런 석유가 그득차고 말엇섯다. 석유를 들붓기 전에는 량반놈들이 가죽 챗죽으로 그를 따리고 가죽신으로 그의 아랫배와 넙적다리로부터 응덩이까지 걷어찻다. 챗죽으로 때리기 전에는 량반놈들이 또 그를 거미와 같이 깍꾸로 달아매엿다. 마지막에 총으로 쏘아 죽일 때는＜109＞ 다 죽은 시체를 가저다 관역으로 마친 것에 지나지 못하엿을 뿐이엇다! 그와 함께 죽은 사람이 칠팔십명이나 되엇다. 불에 타서 죽은 사람도 잇엇고 산대로 파묻히고 만 사람도 잇엇다.
늙은 할머니는 또 며누리도 없다. 그의 며누리는 열일곱해 전에 피를 흘리고 죽엇다. 그것은 피로가 과도하엿든 것과 산후에 조리못한 탓이엇섯다. 죽기 전에는 하체는 불근 시내와도 같이 꼭 일주야동안 피를 흘리어 죽을 때에는 전신이 백지장 같이 하얏엇섯다.

-그래요. 저이들이 그 사람을 엄수히 보는 두발 가진 김생놈들이 그 따위 수단을 써서 우리들을 위협하는 것이나 위협하고 또 우리들에게 억가는 거야 잘 알죠. 그러나 할머니 결코 혁명을 너무 책하지 마서요. 혁명은 우리들의 살 길입니다. 그와 동시에 이러한 일은 하여간에 내려누를 수가 없는 것이죠. 그놈들이 아모리 혁명을 해하고 깨트려도 할머니 저는 할머니가 좀더 기운을 내서서 그놈들의 위협에 조금도 슬퍼하시지 아니하시기를 바라는 겁니다! 할머니는 아버지가 돌아가섯다고 내가 그놈들의 종질을 잘 하여야 된다고 생각하시우? 그러지 않으면 그놈들이 아버지를 죽이듯이 나를 죽일 줄 아시우? 저는 조금도 무섭지 않어요. 그놈들이 죽인 것은 아죠 멫명의 아버지들 뿐이지오. 그러나 그놈들을 반대하는 사람, 아버지들의 사업을 게속하는 사람은 멫명밖에 안되는 것이 아니라 한없이 많읍니다!
森榮은 말하며 늙은 할머니를 바러다 보앗다. 늙은 할머니는 헉헉 헐덕이며 더욱 격렬이 울고 잇다. 森榮은 한마디 더 연달아 말하엿다.
-혁명은 유익한 것이죠. 이것이 우리들 없는 사람을 참으로 해방하는 방법이며 철저한 해방수단입니다...
할머니는 무엇을 생각하엿는지 갑작이 기어 일어낫다.
-무엇 네놈은 네놈들이 하는 일이 좋은 일이라고 하는 말이냐. 없는 사람에게 유익한 일이야? 아이! 독갑이나 고지 들을는지, 나는 눈감기 전까지는 안믿어. 네 애비가 총에 맞어 죽고 丘四叔이 태워 죽고 李六이 산장을 당하고... 이게 네놈들이 그때 밤낫 할 것 없시 쉬지 않고 서들어댄 보수야!
그것은 참으로 森榮은 어찌할 줄을 몰랏섯다. 그는 간번 혁명이 늙은 할머니로 하여금 당연히 실망케 한 줄을 알엇다. 량반놈들은 의연히 시골과 마을 사히에서 못된 짓을 하엿고 구사회가 가지고 잇든 모든 것은 털끝만큼도 변하지 아니하엿을 뿐만 아니라 도리어 더욱 심하여젓섯다.
늙은 할머니는 여전히 울며 또 피곤한 듯이 누어서 헉헉이엇다.
-할머니 할머니 말슴은 조금도 그른데는 없어요. 과거의 혁명은 확실히 사람으로 하여금 낙심케 하엿섯지오. 많은 쓸만한 사람은 죽지 아니 할 수가 없지오. 그놈들(반동세력)의 칼과 탄자 밑에서 목숨을 끊지 않을 수가 없엇지오. 그러나 그것은 우리들이 팔리운 것이며 속은 것이며 또 음험한 유산게급으로 말미아마 파괴를 당한 것이죠! 우리들은 못하엿죠... 악한 세력을 철저히 소멸하지 못하엿지오.
밖에 서고 잇는 사람이 연하여 두서너번 불럿다. 森榮은 이러서서 늙은 할머니를 바라보앗다. 그는 부드러운 목소리로 늙은 할머니에게 분부하엿다.
「할머니 좀 안정하서요! 저는 지금 公王寨로 갑니다.」
말하며 밖으로 나갓다.
할머니는 다시 외로히 두러눕고 잇다.
조금 후에 그는 등불도 끄지 아니하고 이러나서 방안에서 어정거렷다.
-이런 불행이 참으로 어디 또 잇나. 독갑이 들린 녀석 래일 아이그! 래일은 장차 어찌 한단 말이냐. 쌀이 잇어야지. 또 날더러 일해 달라는 사람이 잇어야지!
그는 거닐며 한편으로 중얼거렷다. 그는 어찌 할 것이냐? 또 밥을 얻으띀¬ 갈 곳 조차도 없다. 멫사람 없는 사람이 그를 좋게 대하긴 하야 어느 때보다도 이 가련한 늘근 할머니에게 일층 동정은 하나 그러나 그들 자신의 배조차 배불러 보는 날이 도모지＜113＞ 없어 매일 모다 뷘 배만 뚜드리고 잇는 판이엇다.
-나는 참으로 그놈들의 심사를 알 수가 없어. 독갑이 들린놈들. 어째 그게 참으로 좋은 일일탁이 잇나. 아모리 권하여 보지 내가 고지 들을가?
그는 다시 침상 가까히 거러갓다.
쾅! 쾅! ... 문밖에서 누가 연방문을 두다리엇다.
늙은 할머니는 놀라서 침상에서 기어 이러나서 이상한 듯이 물엇다.
-누구야?
문밖 음성은 매우 거칠어워 무서운 예고를 띄웟다.
-열어! 문 열어!
늙은 할머니는 바람벽을 더듬어 가며 문으로 가서 문을 열엇다. 불을 들고 긴 총을 끌어든 세 사람이 후당탕 뛰어 들어왓다. 늙은 할머니는 문 뒤에 서서 벌벌 떨며 감히 그들을 똑바로도 보지 못하엿다.
들어온 세 사람 중의 七嫂의 남편-張天富가 커단 입을 버리고 소리를 질럿다.
-森榮이 집에 잇는가 개자식?
-없에요. 그애가 발서 나흘재나 돌아오질 않엇세요.
세 사람은 침상판자를 모다 뒤집어 가며 검사하엿다. 할머니는 가만이 서서 헉헉 헐덕이고만 잇엇다. 조곰만에 그들은 가 버리고 말엇다.(끝)
