Magazine Title: 동광 제38호 (東光 No. 38)
Publication Date: 1932년 10월 01일 (October 1, 1932)
Article Title: 狂人의 經濟學 (Madman's Economics)
Author: 뉴욕타임스誌 하롤드·캘렌더氏 (Mr. Harold Callender, New York Times Magazine)
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Story)

Madman's Economics
						Mr. Harold Callender, New York Times Magazine
(In the famous fairy tale "Alice's Adventures in Wonderland," there is this passage:
'The Cat pointed with its right paw, saying, "The Hatter lives that way,"
and pointing with its left paw,
"The March Hare lives this way. Visit either you like: they're both mad."'
Alice asked,
'"But I don't want to go among mad people."'
The Cat replied,
'"Oh, you can't help that. We're all mad here. I'm mad. You're mad."'
The author borrows the form of this fairy tale to attempt an economic critique.)

"Well, that's how it is," said the Hatter, drinking his second cup of tea. "Farmers produce too much rice, factories manufacture too many goods, and machinists make too many machines. Because they all work so well like this, we've become destitute. Isn't the logic clear?"

Alice said,
"It's not clear to me. If lots of rice is produced and many other things are manufactured, shouldn't everyone be eating well and dressing well?"

The Hatter continued,
"Instead, the prices of goods have fallen tremendously. That's why it's a depression."

Alice said,
"Why is it a depression because of that? People used to complain that prices were too high, so now that prices have fallen, shouldn't it be good?"<25>

Hatter:
"No, no. As machines keep getting better at working, human labor becomes unnecessary. That's why workers can't earn money, so they can't buy things. Therefore, the goods produced in factories can't be sold."
(Alice thought to herself, "Then what's the need to keep making them?")

The Hatter just continued,
"We who live here all practice thrift and saving to accumulate capital, and with that capital, we expand factories again. The efficiency of factories increases day by day. As efficiency increases, more goods are made, and fewer people are employed. So, unsold goods pile up like mountains in the market, and the unemployed increase. So much capital has been poured into manufacturing goods that the produced items cannot possibly all be sold."

Alice—
"Oh my, then is there no one who knows how to handle this?"

"There are economists. Those people already knew this would happen and warned us in advance, but they are scholars who just write books and give lectures. Do businessmen need scholars? However, rice pests (稻蟲) are needed."

"What are those needed for?"

"Rice pests eat rice stalks. If they eat the rice stalks, the price of rice doesn't fall," explained the Hatter.
"If rice pests don't appear, there's a good harvest. Then the price of rice becomes nothing."

"Can't you bring the rice pest insects and have them eat all the machines?"

"No. Instead, we raise tariffs to protect the domestic market."

Alice exclaimed,
"Aha, so if there are tariffs, things sell well!"

The Hatter spoke in a stern tone,
"No, no. Tariffs block the market and hinder trade. Our market closes its doors to other countries, and other countries' markets close their doors to us. Every country hopes for an export surplus. That is, every country wants to buy less and sell more."

Alice said—
"If one country wants to sell, shouldn't there be a country that buys?" (Alice was confident about this logic.) "So how can everyone sell at the same time and not buy?"

Hatter—
"It can't be done, it can't. They just destroy each other's trade and suffer together. Every country tries to be self-sufficient. They try not to use goods from other countries. If war were to break out, the country with the least import of foreign goods would be superior."

"There's no danger of war, is there?" Alice asked.

"Many treaties have been signed to guarantee peace. There's the League of Nations Covenant, the Locarno Treaties, and the Kellogg-Briand Pact. Still, everyone worries about war breaking out, and everyone expands their armaments. Armaments are more substantial now than when the Great European War began."

Alice, trying not to offend the Hatter's feelings, said,
"Isn't that... foolish?"<26>

"No, no. Treaties can't be trusted. Everyone wants to abide by the treaties themselves, but they doubt whether others will keep them." Alice: "Then what's the point of making treaties?"

The Hatter didn't answer that question.

Reparations Problem
"Have another cup of tea," he said.
At this moment, the sleeping Dormouse woke up with a start, rubbing its eyes,
"Tell her about the reparations problem. That's what has caused the most trouble."

The Hatter began again.
"Weren't the Germans given the responsibility to rebuild what was destroyed in the war? Since they lost the war."

Alice interrupted,
"So the German workers and materials came and repaired everything, I suppose?"

The Hatter:
"Don't be foolish. We couldn't allow that method. Then what would the French contractors and workers eat?"

"Then, the Germans paid with money, I guess?"

"They have to have money to pay. Since they don't have money, meaning gold, they would have to pay with other goods, but the creditor nations didn't want German goods and built tariff walls."

Alice asked,
"Then how did you collect the reparations?"

"We lent money to Germany. Told them to pay the debt with that. Germany used that money to continuously expand and improve factories, producing more goods and using fewer people, just like us. We lent them too much money, and now they're bankrupt?"

"If reparations are so bad, why don't you just get rid of them quickly?" Alice asked.

Hatter:
"It can't be done, it can't. France objects first, and also, the Allied side needs to receive money from Germany to repay the debts owed to America. This debt also has to be repaid with money earned from selling goods, but America's tariffs are high, so the goods that can enter are limited."

"Then how is that debt repaid?"

"Who repays it? Does anyone even think of repaying it? But we have to pretend we can repay it. One difficulty is that the debt changes every year. The debtors have ended up having to repay much more money than they borrowed."

"Why is that?"

"That debt was supposed to be paid in gold, but when the debt was incurred, it was effectively in goods. At that time, the price of goods was high, but now the price of goods has plummeted, so it amounts to having to repay at least 50% more than the money borrowed."

Alice sighed. She doubted if worldly affairs would ever become rational again. So she asked,
"Is there really no one who can sort things out?" The Hatter replied, "No one has managed to sort it out well yet."

Alice earnestly asked,
"Shouldn't something be done to sort things out?"
"Yes, of course, it absolutely must be done. Everyone knows that," the Hatter answered.
Hearing this, Alice finally recalled what the Cat had told her.<27>

**Summary**

Using the framework of *Alice in Wonderland*, Harold Callender critiques the economic paradoxes of the Great Depression era through a dialogue between Alice and the Hatter. The Hatter explains absurdities like overproduction causing poverty, falling prices being bad, technological progress leading to unemployment, tariffs destroying trade while nations strive for self-sufficiency, disarmament treaties coexisting with arms races, and the illogical cycle of war reparations and international debt, particularly involving Germany, France, and the US, exacerbated by the gold standard and fluctuating commodity prices.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting because it was published in a Korean magazine (Donggwang) in 1932, translating a contemporary Western critique of global capitalism and international relations. It shows an awareness among Korean intellectuals (or at least publishers catering to them) of the international economic and political crises, framing them through a familiar Western literary device. The critique of protectionism, irrational economic policies, and the looming threat of war, while focused on Europe and America, would have resonated in colonized Korea, which was deeply integrated into and affected by the Japanese empire's economic sphere and the turbulent global situation. The indirect, allegorical style might also have been a safer way to discuss systemic failures compared to direct political commentary.
