Duck
						Kim Nue-gye (金淚溪)

Oh, duck! Today, by the pond
Do you not intend to go out and play?
White sand, washed by the rain, has covered the narrow path
And the morning sun, softened by the mist,
Gently spreads over the water's surface.

Oh, duck! Even the frog swallows its croak,
When only the reed leaf and the fine ripples
Exchange unknowable whispers,
Do you not wish to float alone, like a dream?

Oh, duck! Even the young fish
Are fast asleep deep in the water;
Though an unbearable loneliness
Surrounds and presses upon your body,
Do not make a sound to call your companions.
For this is the time when you possess the greatest joy. <70>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Nue-gye addresses a solitary duck on a quiet pond, encouraging it to embrace its solitude. The speaker describes the serene, post-rain atmosphere where even frogs are silent and only reeds and ripples whisper. The poem suggests that this state of being alone, despite its inherent loneliness, is actually a moment of profound, private joy that should not be disturbed by calling for companions. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in 1932 during the Japanese colonial period, exemplifies the lyrical focus on nature and introspection common in Korean literature of the time. It could be interpreted as finding inner peace and resilience in quiet contemplation amidst a potentially oppressive external reality, a subtle form of escapism or assertion of the private self.

오리
						金淚溪
오리야! 오늘은 못가에
놀러 나가지 않으려느냐?
비에 시친 힌모래가 좁은 길을 덮엇고
안개에 풀린 아츰 볓이
수면을 고요히 할른다.

오리야! 개고리도 울음을 삼키고
갈잎과 잔 물껼만 남 모를
속사김을 주고 받을 때
너는 홀로 꿈같이 떠 잇고 싶지 않느냐.

오리야! 어린 고기까지
물속 깊이 잠들어
견대기 어려운 외로음이
네 몸을 싸고 졸라도
소리를 내어 동무를 부르지 말아라.
기쁨을 가장 많이 가진 때이니. ＜70＞
