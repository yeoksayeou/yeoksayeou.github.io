Poems of Wandering on the North American Continent
						By Han Hŭgu in the United States

Homeland
Chosŏn, my homeland!
People of Chosŏn, my compatriots!
Your form, mountains high and waters clear.
And my longed-for brethren!

In the complex life of the age,
Who can guarantee its safety?
If you believe in evolution and creation,
Who can deny its changes!

Ah! My Chosŏn.
Ah! My compatriots!
Until the bell tolls in the morning harbor, at the port,
Look up at the constellations, go forth and go forth.

In the morning, the East Sea (東海) weeps,
In the evening, the Western Sky (西天) burns!
On a moonlit night over the mountain pass,
Where does the clear stream of four thousand years flow!

Is the land I gaze upon, hand shading my brow,
Laid before me today!
The cuckoo on the back hill,
Though I hear it again this evening. <81>

By the moonless rapids,
Who flows by, weeping so!
In the windless forest,
Who shouts so loudly!

In the spirit of eternity,
Pining mountains and waters!
Tonight, the young ones,
Where have they gone!

Oh! My Chosŏn!
Oh! My compatriots!
And the high mountains!
The streams flowing day and night!

I long for my homeland!
1932.7.15. "Homesickness Chapter" (思鄕 篇)

Unfamiliar Street
Alone in the evening
Walking out onto an unfamiliar street
Before a house with colored lights
I stopped my feet.

Though there were no customers,
A dancing girl dancing tuned to the radio.
Watching intently, lost in thought,
It felt like some street back home.

A man walking briskly,
Hat pulled down low,
Thinking it was H,
I stood with my head tilted.

This place is Chicago, he's someone I've never seen before.
(1931.6.15. "Homesickness Chapter" (思鄕 篇) In Chicago)

Coming to a Foreign Land
Coming to a foreign land,
Though the sun has not yet set,
Why do I want to hear the songs of home
So constantly!

The days of indignation
Though they have not yet ended,
Why do I want to see the comrades of home
So constantly! <82>

Even a person like me,
Whom comrades might as well forget,
Having come to a foreign land,
Keeps thinking of them!
1932.7.17. "Homesickness Chapter" (思鄕 篇)

Homesickness (思鄕)
The streetcar running straight
Along Main Street,
Stopping my steps, standing still,
I gaze at the crimson twilight.

Does the phantom of my hometown's evening sky
Rise again!
When I heavily close my eyes,
It seems to seep deep into my heart.

Gently opening my eyes,
Walking on the asphalt,
Women in red coats,
And, the colored lights of the theater!
1932.4.8. "Homesickness Chapter" (思鄕 篇)

Hudson Riverbank (헏손 江畔)
New York's great artery,
Over the Hudson River,
The sunset glow of a crimson sky stretches.
Now, the lights of the Washington Bridge too.

On the pitch-dark river with no moon,
What boats come and go?
So many people sitting out on the riverbank,
Who could those people going on a boat trip be?

Pairs, shoulder to shoulder,
Men and women strolling the riverbank.
I, sitting alone with legs outstretched,
Disliking the song, watch another boat depart.
1932.7.5. "In New York" (放浪 篇 - Wandering Chapter)

On Deck
With a restless mind in a restless body,
I boarded a ship heading east. <82>
The ship passing down the Delaware River,
Where does my mind drift!

On the deck, clothes fluttering,
Men and women of the 1st class passengers!
Dancing, drinking ladies! Gentlemen!
Merely pretty dolls!

The fat in your bellies,
Could the class distance be greater than here?
How could you know the state of the 3rd class passengers?
Your bellies, for which "dollars" are enough.

AB, on the central deck,
"1st Class Passenger only"
On the narrow deck at the ship's stern,
"3rd Class Passenger only"

What mockery, what deception is this!
What does democracy mean?
A ship is also a miniature of society,
Where are equality and freedom?
1932.7.20. "Wandering Chapter" (放浪 篇)

World of Earth
1.

Does everything in the world
Come from the earth,
And return to the earth?

Here the poet
Wanders in madness,
And the artists
Carve and erect idols.

The splendid temple
Rings its bell,
And sorrowful lives
Breathe there!

2.

The ancient temple
Of the city, millions of years old,
Riding the flames of the last day,
Blazes fiercely.

People scream as if mad, <83>
And burn the altar with fire,
Even the cross of the splendid temple
Is buried in the heap of fire!

3.

Does everything in the world
Come from the earth
And return to the earth?

People, as if mad,
Build walls around what is theirs,
And tear down what belongs to others!
Ah! Upon a palm-sized clod of earth.

A palm-sized world of earth!
The hoofbeats of madmen!
Inside the city of falsehood,
The cries of the naked!

4.

A palm-sized world of earth
But, but
The scent of earth rising from the Earth's core!
And one leaf, a green leaf!
1932.7.9.

Expulsion Order, Thinking of K, Who Was Expelled
From the land of Chosŏn, like our own insides,
You came thirsty for learning,
From the classroom to iron bars,
Chased relentlessly, you went to a faraway country!

Though this country is said to prize freedom,
Is there any use for us with black hair!
Studying while working was hard enough,
Did they have to order expulsion like that?

When the flood of the Depression (띠푸레슌)
Drove us out of the school gates,
Saying it was a common fate,
Everyone was gripped by fear!

The immigration officer rolled his eyes
And caught you, unable to study!
You, who carried books from unfinished exams,
Wandering to pawn them!

Within 30 days, leave the country, <84>
You who received the expulsion order!
Could tears even have come!
In the burning fire of your tormented eyes.

"There's nowhere to go," said
The face of the anguished comrade!
Yet, with "two poems,"
You obtained the travel fare to a distant land!

Farewell! Comrade!
Before your blue lips dried,
Igniting the burning fire in your eyes,
You went to the XX country of red light!
1932.7.7.

As I Please!
As I please
Shouting
Wanting to run wild
Is today!

Like Niagara
Falls...
Like artillery cannons...
Like a whirlwind sweeping the North Pole...

An explosion!
An explosion!
The Earth's core trembles, rumbling,
The explosion of life!
1932.7.15

Flower-Selling Girl
On the hotel district corner
A young flower-selling girl
Tying flowers with string
Stood crying!

Why! Is she crying?
Even though people are watching
She just stands there crying!
Why is she crying?

Is her mother ill?
Did she skip breakfast?
Did she lose her lover?
Ah, why is she crying?

**Summary**

This collection of poems by Han Hŭgu, writing from the United States in the early 1930s, vividly portrays the experiences of a Korean exile. The poems explore deep homesickness for Chosŏn, feelings of alienation and displacement while wandering through American cities like Chicago and New York, sharp critiques of social inequality (class divisions on a ship, the hypocrisy of American democracy), and the harsh realities faced by Korean students, including poverty and the threat of deportation during the Depression. The poet expresses both despair and a resilient, sometimes explosive, yearning for freedom and homeland. Historians might find the poems valuable for their raw depiction of Korean immigrant/exile life in the US during the colonial period, capturing the emotional toll of displacement, the impact of the Great Depression on vulnerable immigrant students, the critique of American society from a marginalized perspective, and the complex feelings towards the lost homeland. The mention of a comrade being deported and heading to an "XX country of red light" (likely hinting at the Soviet Union or a socialist destination) is particularly interesting regarding the political leanings or desperate options considered by some Koreans abroad during this era.
