Publication Date: 1932년 10월 01일
Article Title: 聖者와 英雄(全1幕) (Saint and Hero (A One-Act Play))
Author: 韓利職
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play Script/Scenario)

Saint and Hero
						Han I-jik
Time: Contemporary
Place: A theater
Characters: Helma
Nora
Manager
Youth
Crowd
Stage: Background—Towards the left, there is a wall with two windows barred with iron rods set high up. To the right, there is another four-sided backdrop, on which are wall inscriptions (부벽서) and pictures. On the left side facing the audience, there is a white wall with a closed two-panel door of dark purple. This door must be pulled inward to open. Downstage—towards the right, there is a stage set up. On this stage-within-a-stage, there is one table, two chairs beside it, and a lit lamp on the table. To access this stage-within-a-stage, one uses the space between the main stage's right side and the audience. Towards the left are the audience seats. Around 10 PM. When the curtain rises, the audience seats are filled with fans from all walks of life (gentlemen, ladies, Russians (로소 - likely abbreviation for 'Russian'), etc.), engrossed in the play; the house is nearly full. On the stage-within-a-stage, "A Doll's House" (by Ibsen) is being performed. Helma is standing, dressed in a tailcoat, while Nora, wearing a hat and overcoat and holding a travel bag, is about to exit.

Helma: You're leaving your home, husband, and children? Don't you even think about what people will say?
Nora: I don't think about such things. I only think that I myself must do this.
Helma: That's unacceptable. By acting like this, are you saying you'll abandon your sacred duties?
Nora: What are these so-called sacred duties you speak of?
Helma: Don't you know? Your duty to your husband and your duty to your children.
Nora: I believe I have other sacred duties.<109>
Helma: There can be no such thing. What on earth are these duties of yours?
Nora: My duty to myself.
Helma: Above all else, you are first a wife and a mother.
Nora: I don't believe so. Above all else, I am a human being, just like you. At least, I want to become one. I know that most people hold opinions like yours. Books also say such things. But from now on, I can absolutely no longer be satisfied with what people say or what is written in books. I intend to think for myself and understand things clearly for myself.

(Just as she is about to exit, smoke suddenly fills the stage, and shouts of "Fire!" are heard from outside. Everyone on the stage rushes towards the left exit door. The two actors also drop what they were holding and hurry towards it. From outside, shouts of "Fire! Fire!" are heard again. People crowd even more towards the exit door. The door is closed. The room is dark, occasionally lit up by flashes of light. The room is a chaotic mix of men and women. Those far from the exit door (those at the back) frantically search for gaps in the packed crowd.)

Crowd at the back, away from the door: Open the door! Open the door quickly!
Crowd near the door: Don't push! (Bracing their feet against the door, trying to push back the people behind them, but to no avail) Stop pushing from behind and step back. We can't open the door.
Crowd in the middle: (A man) Ouch, they're pushing so hard. It's going to break! (Accompanied by shouts of "Open the door!" from the back.)
Young woman with a crying baby on her back: (Turning to look at the person pushing from behind) Hey, why do you keep pushing? Can my baby endure this? (Accompanied by shouts of "Can't open it!" from the front.)
Person addressed: Am I pushing? They keep pushing from behind, so I have no choice but to push too.
(Crowd behind shouts "Open the door, quickly, quickly!" accompanied by a sudden surge forward.)
Woman with the baby: Aigo! I'm going to die!
Crowd near the door: Please step back a bit from behind! People are getting crushed! Good grief! (Accompanied by a young woman near the door crying "Aigo, Mother!". Sounds of other women crying.)

(Flashes of firelight occasionally illuminate the stage. Each time, people surge even more towards the door, causing further commotion. No one yields an inch. Those at the back try to push ahead of those in front but are pushed back. Women wail.)

Crowd at the back: Open the door! (In unison, loudly) Open the door!
Crowd at the front: Step back, we can't open the door! (Several people near the door push outwards together [it doesn't budge]) Back off from behind!
People in the middle: Aigo, I'm getting crushed! (Man) Aigo, Mother! (Woman)
(Amidst the chaos, someone [where a gap appears] slyly grabs a young woman's wrist.)
Young woman: Which bastard grabbed my wrist? Aigo!
Crowd at the back: Wrist or ankle, just open the door! We're going to die!
Crowd near the door: You have to move back, we can't open it!

(At that moment, a man from the crowd briskly climbs onto the performance stage. Outwardly, he is indistinguishable from the other members of the crowd, but his demeanor is very composed. He is the theater manager.)

Manager: Now, everyone! Instead of just pushing like this, listen to me<110>! In a situation like this, just rushing won't solve anything. Please turn this way and listen to me. (However, the crowd only continues shouting "Don't push," "Open the door," "I'm getting crushed," "Aargh!" from various places. The Manager loudly) Everyone! If you keep carrying on like this, none of us in here will survive! (Some in the crowd turn to look at him.) Now, look! Right now (Flash of firelight. The crowd cries out again.) Be calm and listen to me, or we will die soon. (Calmly) Until we open that door, none of us can get out, and we will all die here. But that door absolutely cannot be opened by pushing; it only opens when pulled inward. Therefore, for the sake of your own lives, you must all simultaneously step back just a few paces. (Passionately) If everyone steps back just three paces together, we can easily open that door, and you will be able to save your lives from this death trap!
Crowd near the door: Indeed, that's absolutely right. Now, quickly, quickly, everyone step back just three paces. Then we can open this door.
(However, no one yields; they merely maintain a slightly calmer composure than before.)
Manager: Now, courage is needed at times like this. Let's summon our courage and step back three paces. (Loudly) Now, quickly, come this way! (Three or four people nearby step back. The vacated spots are immediately filled by people next to them, leaving only the ones who stepped back displaced. The displaced people try to pull out those who took their spots, sometimes failing, sometimes succeeding, sometimes fighting. During this, the crowd starts pushing towards the door again. Simultaneously, cries of "Aigo!" "I'm getting crushed!" "Don't push!" "Aigo, Mother!" etc., rise in a mixed chorus of men and women. The stage remains in commotion.)
Manager: Don't make noise, listen to me! Do you think (loudly) making noise will create an escape route? (Calmly) Our only way to survive lies in all of you yielding a few steps. (He looks around at those who took the places of the ones who yielded, points to one of them) If you move one step closer to the door, do you think you can save yourself from this fire? You won't! You won't! (Lifting his head) Now, let's quickly step back three paces together. For your lives, quickly, quickly!
Person who yielded: Unless our minds unite, that will absolutely never happen. If one person steps back, another just pushes forward, so who would yield? Instead, we need to think of another way. (Draws the crowd's attention.)
Manager: We absolutely cannot survive unless we open that door!
Person who yielded: (Stepping back a bit further, addressing the crowd) Everyone, let's break down that door! Won't that create a way out for us?
Crowd: That's right! Come on, let's break down that door!
(Simultaneously with these words, those standing near the door start punching it. From the crowd come shouts of "Bring an axe!" "Break the door!" "Break it quickly!". However, the door doesn't break easily, and due to the strong pushing from behind, the person punching the door presses their clenched fists and body hard against it, crying out, "Aigo, my chest!". From behind, shouts continue: "Quickly break down the door!" "Tear down the door!". Accompanied by cries of "Aigo, Mother!" (Woman) "I'm dying!" (Man).)
Person pressed against the door: Aigo, I'm going to be crushed!
Person next to him: Stop pushing from behind! Someone's going to die! (Slightly calmer) <111>
Manager: No, no! Turn this way and listen to me! (Forcefully) We absolutely cannot escape this trap unless we yield together and take three steps this way! (Calmly) I have thought of a method now. Every single one of you, without exception, must obey my command. Then we can survive. Now—we must all step back three paces together. When I say "One," you all take one step back together. When I say "Two," two steps, and "Three," three steps back, and we will already be saved people! (Raises his hand high) Now—pay attention! Not a single person should be left out! (Lowering his hand, bending his body, loudly) One! (Most step back one pace, but some do not. Pointing at them) Step back quickly! (They step back. The door moves slightly.) Two! (As they step back in unison, the door opens enough for people to potentially exit. Seeing this, the crowd, overjoyed, rushes towards the half-open door, wanting to be the first out. The door slams shut again. The stage erupts again in commotion with shouts of "Let's get out quickly!" "Don't push!" "Aigo!" "I'm getting crushed!")
Manager: (Stamping his foot, looking frustrated) Don't cause a commotion! That door isn't open! (Commotion lessens)
Someone in the crowd: Who made that door close again?
Manager: No one closed it deliberately. It closed naturally because you all pushed! (Forcefully) Everyone! (Calmly) Until that door is fully open, even if it opens slightly, it will just close again. Now, to get that door fully open, don't think about anything else, just watch me. Let's try it one more time like before. If we just endure for three steps, that's all, so let's focus! (Raises his hand high) Now! (Lowering his hand) One! (Everyone moves their legs in unison.) Two! (They step back again. The door opens slightly as before.) Be careful, if we just endure one more step, we all live. Keep your eyes fixed on me! (As he raises his arm high, about to shout "Three!", a large burning beam from outside collapses with a loud noise, and the firelight flooding through the windows fills the stage. The crowd's minds are filled with visions of terror. They rush frantically towards the not-yet-fully-open door again. The door closes again, commotion resumes.)
Manager: Everyone! Be calm! Pull yourselves together! Then we can still find a way out! My body is tired now. Signs of fatigue show on your faces too. But let's summon our greatest courage for the last time. Now, face me and prepare! (They face him, calm down. He raises his hand high, then lowers it) One! (They lift their legs in unison.) Two! (They move their legs again. The door opens slightly again. He raises his hand high, just about to utter the word "Three!" when suddenly a fireball falls from the ceiling in front of the Manager. At this, the stage descends into chaos again, with everyone rushing towards the closed door.)
Someone in the crowd: This building is almost collapsing now! We have to break down that door quickly and get out! (From the back, in unison) Break the door! (Accompanied by shouts of "Aigo!" "I'm getting crushed!" "Don't push!")
Manager: Oh, pitiful mortals! (Weakly) Can't you just be calm for one moment? Ah! (The crowd continues its commotion, discussing breaking the door. The Manager suddenly musters his strength, loudly) If you want to live, you must step back three paces! (He collapses. The room continues in commotion, with only a few people looking back at him. The firelight flashes increasingly brighter and more frequently than before.)

(At that moment, a young man from the crowd appears on the platform and supports the Manager. He has a vibrant spirit.)

Youth: Revered sir! You shouted for us until the very end. <112> But this foolish crowd still hasn't found the way out. I intend to follow in your footsteps and open the way for this crowd. (The collapsed man doesn't answer. The youth leaves him and stamps his foot forcefully.) Everyone! Be calm and listen to me! (Slightly quieter) Now—here lies someone who exhausted all his energy for you and is now unconscious. This is the man who strove to save you. If you had listened to his words, we would have already seen the bright world outside. But everyone! Our time is not yet completely lost. Though he is dead (sic - likely means unconscious/defeated), let us realize his still-living, great lesson! Now—
Someone in the crowd: Such words have nothing to do with us! This building is collapsing right now! When do we have time to step back? (Loudly) Hurry up and break down that door! (From the back, in unison) Break the door!
Crowd at the front: Can't break it! (Commotion continues)
Youth: Foolish crowd! I do not wish to argue with you! (He gathers scattered papers from the floor, takes out a match (석냥), and lights them. Holding the burning papers) Now, whoever does not wish to receive a "baptism by fire" (불세레), step back! (He throws the burning papers over the crowd. The crowd scatters instantly. They then rush towards the youth who threw the fire. At that moment, the door swings wide open. The crowd rushes to the door! To the door!) -Curtain-<113>

**Summary**

The play "Saint and Hero" depicts a crisis erupting when a fire breaks out during a performance of Ibsen's "A Doll's House." The audience panics, trapped by an inward-opening exit door they futilely push against. The theater manager attempts rational leadership, instructing the crowd to step back three paces in unison to allow the door to open. Despite brief success, panic repeatedly undermines his efforts, and he ultimately collapses. A young man then takes charge, using a shocking tactic: throwing burning paper at the crowd to force them back. This desperate measure works, the crowd scatters, the door opens, and escape becomes possible.

From a historical perspective, the play, written in 1932 colonial Korea, is interesting for its exploration of crowd psychology and leadership in crisis. The manager represents reasoned authority that fails in the face of primal fear, while the youth embodies radical, perhaps ethically questionable, action that proves effective. Historians might find this contrast significant, potentially reflecting commentary on the perceived ineffectiveness of moderate approaches versus the necessity of drastic measures within the constraints of colonial society, or perhaps an allegory for different modes of resistance or social change. The setting within a theater, a space for cultural expression, and the initial reference to "A Doll's House" (a play about escaping societal constraints) adds layers of potential meaning regarding freedom and entrapment.
