Author: 春園
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

To Him Who Goes to the Countryside, Seeing Off Brother Kwŏn Ku-hyŏn as He Returns Home to Yŏngdong
						Ch'unwŏn

Do not say that going to the countryside (田園) is a thing of the past
The countryside has been humanity's (人生) nest for millions of years
Go first, my friend; I too shall follow.

Do you admire Kropotkin?
I admire Tolstoy
Neither you nor I are people of today.

When you go to the countryside, there will be much work to do
If you cast your fishing line, will not the fish bite?
Even if the fish do not bite, I wish to see the water's color.

Sinmi Year, 12th Month, 27th Day. Ch'unwŏn, from his sickbed (病席) in Sungsam-dong. Hasty brushstrokes (亂筆). <83>

**Summary**

This poem by Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu) bids farewell to his friend Kwŏn Ku-hyŏn, who is returning to his rural hometown of Yŏngdong. Ch'unwŏn expresses a shared longing for the pastoral life, seeing the countryside as humanity's eternal home and expressing his own desire to follow his friend there. He contrasts their shared ideals, referencing Kropotkin and Tolstoy, with the current era, suggesting they are out of step with "today." He concludes by imagining simple rural activities like fishing, finding value even in just observing nature. From a historical perspective, the poem is interesting as it reflects a sentiment among some Korean intellectuals during the colonial period turning towards rural life, perhaps seeking refuge, authenticity, or an alternative to colonial modernity. The explicit mention of Kropotkin and Tolstoy points to the specific anarchist and agrarian philosophical influences shaping the thoughts of prominent figures like Yi Kwangsu and his associates during this time.
