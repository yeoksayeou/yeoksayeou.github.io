Urgent Telegrams

As announced in the New Year's issue, the "Urgent Telegrams" (至急電報) section has expanded its nature to act as an exchange office, asking questions on your behalf, or a reception room (應接室), providing direct answers when any reader wishes to ask someone a question or wants to know something. Please ask anything concisely and pointedly regarding writers, politics, law, economics, academics, or other aspects of daily life. We will exert the main function of "Urgent Telegrams" to promptly publish both the question and answer together.

To Mr. Hyŏn Sangyun:
We hear that your chronic illness has recovered in recent years, so we hope you will return to the educational world and resume your activities. (Kim Sŏngŏp, P'yŏngyang Tongs-A Ilbo Branch Office)
(Return Telegram)
I am immensely grateful for your kind regards. However, considering the meaning of the old giving way to the new (新陳代謝), perhaps my current life is rather appropriate. (Hyŏn Sangyun, Chŏngju)

To Mr. Yi Sŭngman:
Please abandon the life of an illustrator (揷畵記者) and raise a new flag in the art world (畵壇). (Chŏn Pongje) <63>
(Return Telegram)
I am overwhelmed that you show such grateful sentiments toward me, who hasn't even been able to raise a humble flag. I haven't applied my paintbrush to illustration (揷畵); I merely thrust my uncontrollable self into it. I can only look toward the future. (Yi Sŭngman)

I run various night schools (夜學) for propertyless children in a mountain village of sixty to seventy households. I would be grateful if you could tell me the physical exercise commands in Korean. (Chŏng Pyŏngjun, Labor Night School, Nosŏng-ri, Anjŏn-myŏn, Chinan-gun, North Chŏlla Province)
(Answer) The words you asked about seem to mean the commands used in ordinary military drill (敎練). Therefore, I will write down only a few simple ones now and write in more detail at the next opportunity. I am writing just two or three things that might be helpful for reference, so please understand. Also, the order I write them in is not specialized but follows the sequence for drilling children generally.
One. Commands are most necessary for training children. Firstly, the command must be authoritative. A weak command halves the value of the training objective. The person giving the command must assume a correct posture and be strict, and the command must have the spirit to drive people even through fire and water. Commands are divided into preparatory commands (豫令) and commands of execution (動令). The preparatory command should be voiced clearly and long, and the command of execution <64> should be forceful and short. Also, there should be enough time between the preparatory command and the command of execution to take three or four steps.
One. To gather the children: "Chwi, rip-" (聚立, Fall in)
One. To have them assume the position of attention: "Ki ch'ak" (氣着, Attention)
One. To align the ranks (整頓): "U(Chwa)-ro narani" (Right (Left) dress) or the command of execution "Narŭt"
One. To have them return to the original posture after alignment: "Paru-t" (Ready front)
One. To check the number of personnel: "Pŏnho-" (番號, Count off)
One. To let them rest: "Hyusik" (休憩, Rest)
One. To have them face right or left, or half-right (left): "U(Chwa), Hyang-u(chwa)" (Right (Left), Face), "Pan U(Chwa) Hyang-(chwa)" (Half Right (Left), Face)
One. To have them face the rear: "Tora-u" or "Tora-ut" (About, Face)
One. To make them advance: "Ap'ŭro-kat" (Forward, March)
One. To halt them: It differs depending on the size of the unit, but usually Squad (分隊), Company (中隊), or Entire Unit (全隊) is used.
1. When the unit is small: "Pundæ-rip" (Squad, Halt)
2. When the unit is large: "Chŏndæ-rip" (Unit, Halt)
One. To have them face right (left) while marching: "U(Chwa)hyang ap'ŭro-kat" (By the Right (Left) Flank, March)
One. To have them face half-right (left) while marching backwards [Note: Likely meant 'while marching']: "Pitkyŏ U(Chwa)-ro-kat" (Incline to the Right (Left), March)
One. To have them face the rear while marching: <65> "Tora Upyŏn ap'ŭro-kat" (To the Rear, March [lit. 'About right side forward-go'])
One. To stop keeping step during quick time [lit. 'common step']: "Pojo kŭch'ŏt" (Route Step, March [lit. 'step stop'])
One. To resume keeping step: "Pojo ch'wihæt" (Quick Time, March [lit. 'step take'])
One. To start double time from a halt: "Kupo-ro-kat" (Double Time, March)
One. To halt during double time: "Pundæ (hokŭn Chŏndæ) rip" (Squad (or Unit), Halt)
One. To go from double time to quick time: "P'yŏngbo-ro-kat" (Quick Time, March)
One. To dismiss a formation: "Hech'ŏ-kat" or "P'urŏ-kat" (Dismissed)
(Cho Ch'ŏrho, Army First Lieutenant, Former Central Gymnastics Teacher)

What books have been published by Tonggwangsa (東光社)? Please tell me the author, book title, and fixed price. (Wŏnsan Devoted Reader)
(Answer) Yi Ŭnsang, "Chosŏn Sahwa-jip" (朝鮮史話集, Collection of Korean Historical Tales) (Volume 1) (Fixed Price 1 Wŏn); Yi Yunjæ, ed., "Munye Tokpon" (文藝讀本, Literary Reader) (Volume 1) (Fixed Price 65 Chŏn). For now, these are the only two. We are preparing to publish more in the future. (A Reporter)

These days, many foreign words that I don't understand appear in newspapers and magazines, making reading very difficult. Please quickly let me know what suitable dictionary (辭典) is available. (K, K)
(Answer) There are various ones, but each has its pros and cons. The New Year issue of the Japanese magazine "Kaizō" (改造) came with a dictionary of social terms (社會語辭典) as a supplement; that seems convenient. The fixed price is 1 Wŏn. I have not heard of one written in Korean. There is also this book:
"Sin Sidae ŭi Ch'ŏmdanŏ Sajŏn" (新時代の尖端語辭典, Dictionary of Cutting-Edge Words of the New Era) by Nagaoka Kiku (長岡規矩) (Fixed Price 1 Wŏn 50 Chŏn) (Bunbu Shoin (文武書院), 6-21 Iida-machi, Kōjimachi-ku, Tokyo City) (Reporter) <66>

How many Koreans hold Japanese doctorates (博士)? Please tell me the type of doctorate and their names. (Pusan Reader)
(Answer) Doctor of Medicine (...)
Doctor of Science: Mr. Yi T'ægyu (李泰圭)
This is how I remember it. There might be some missed. (A Reporter) <67>

**Summary**

This article from the January 1932 issue of "Tonggwang" magazine functions as a reader Q&A column called "Urgent Telegrams." It includes personal messages to figures like educator Hyŏn Sangyun and artist Yi Sŭngman, practical advice such as a list of Korean-language physical education commands for a rural night school (provided by an army lieutenant), information about books published by Tonggwangsa, recommendations for Japanese dictionaries to understand foreign loanwords due to the lack of Korean options, and a partial list of Koreans holding Japanese doctorates (mentioning only Yi T'ægyu in science). From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into colonial Korean society: the need for Korean-language instruction materials (like P.E. commands), the challenges posed by the influx of foreign terms (primarily via Japanese) and the underdeveloped state of Korean lexicography, the nascent publishing activities of Korean companies, and the tracking of academic achievements by Koreans within the Japanese system. The specific military-style commands adapted into Korean are particularly interesting.
