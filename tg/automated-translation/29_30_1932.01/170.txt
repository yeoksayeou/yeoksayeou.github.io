Record of Silent Contemplation
						Yi Kwangsu

Small Things for Which I Was Greatly Thankful

A person's whole life is entirely indebted to the world. Especially for someone like me, who lost parents early and grew up without support or dependence, looking back on 40 years, it's all just debts to others. My shame for only receiving and not being able to repay! Among these, I want to reflect on a few small things for which I was greatly thankful.

The Old Woman Who Combed My Hair

When I was eleven, I lost my parents and wandered around. The most difficult thing was the lice swarming in my hair. Lice swarming on clothes could be dealt with by taking them off and catching them in places like mountain valleys, but with the lice swarming in my hair, I didn't know what to do by myself. One day (it was a winter day), while crossing a certain mountain pass, I was warming myself in the sun and resting my legs on a sunny patch of grassy ground where the snow had melted. As the sun shone warmly, the lice in my hair seemed to launch an all-out attack, and the itching was beyond compare. I was scratching my head with my fingers, picking off the lice as they came into my hand and placing them on the snow, taking revenge on the enemies that tormented me so unbearably, when suddenly an old woman approached from behind.

"Ah, you haven't been able to comb your hair. Seeing your white ribbon, your parents must have passed away. Come with me, I'll comb your hair for you," she said. I followed her. Her house was a small one. She combed my hair, used a fine-toothed comb (참빗) to scrape out even the nits (서캐), and said,
"Later too, if lice swarm in your hair, come here, okay?"
She even fed me some cold leftover rice before sending me on my way.

The Western-style Wardrobe (洋服장)

My wife's trousseau chest had become completely worn out. The bottom had fallen out, the doors had come off, and the decorations had fallen off. My wife was ashamed to show this miserable chest to clumsy guests—especially female guests, and among them, friends who lived prosperously.

So, my wife was always saying, if only we could buy a wardrobe, if only we could buy one <44> and it had already been over 3 or 4 years that she'd longed to buy a wardrobe, mentioning how someone had acquired mother-of-pearl furniture, or how someone else had a Western-style wardrobe (양복장).

But there was no spare money to buy a wardrobe. Hopes of buying one if I received a year-end bonus were all futile dreams. Before the money came in, ways for it to go out had already opened up. At the end of the year, at Chin'gogae and Chongno, they hold prize sales, offering items like wardrobes and chests (단스) as first prizes. Each time, my wife bought things, hoping to win a wardrobe. But whatever she drew was always a towel (타울수건), fire tongs/chopsticks (화저까락), or the like; the desired wardrobe was never won.

"Didn't you win the wardrobe?"
"I have no luck in drawing prizes."
This year, she won a copper brazier and a necktie. But still, no wardrobe.

On the last day of the twelfth lunar month, my wife, probably thinking, "Another year ends without getting a wardrobe," went out somewhere and returned full of joy.

"We've got a Western-style wardrobe!"
This was the first thing she said, even before greeting a friend who happened to be visiting.

"A Western-style wardrobe?"
I, too, was happy. Happy for my wife's wish being fulfilled.
"So, what did you buy to win it?"
I assumed it had been won as a prize. My wife, overjoyed, half-laughing, said,
"It's not like that,"
And she explained the origin of the wardrobe. The story was this:
My wife had gone to visit her elder sister's house. It turned out that they had rented out a building to a lumber dealer (목물전), and when the dealer went out of business (페업), they gave the landlord three Western-style wardrobes instead of several months' overdue rent (집세).

"If I had the money, I'd buy one too,"
My wife had voiced her lifelong wish where her sister and brother-in-law could hear. Hearing this, her brother-in-law quietly slipped out of the room and after a while called my wife,
"Come out and see this."
When she went out, indeed, a wardrobe she liked had been brought and placed in the yard.
"Do you like this one?"
Indeed, she liked it.
"Take this!"
At her brother-in-law's words, my wife asked,
"When should I pay for it?"
Worrying that her desire conflicted with her empty wallet, the brother-in-law said,
"Let's just count it as the two months' rent I couldn't collect. Take it and set it up [at your home]."
Thus, my wife obtained the Western-style wardrobe she had wished for these past 3 or 4 years.
This wardrobe will arrive at our house tomorrow morning. <45>

Ten Self-Mocking Poems Upon Facing Fifty (望五自嘲十首)
Prologue
It's 15 minutes from midnight (子正). Neuralgia (神經痛) keeps me awake, and idle thoughts of retrospection and prospect muddle my head. In just 15 minutes, I will be 41 years old. Having passed the age of forty (不惑), I now face fifty. What have I gained in 40 years? A sick body (病軀), debt (負債), a wife and children difficult to support, some twenty-odd unremarkable books (著書), countless failures, and a history of blame and praise (毁譽). What lies ahead?

Forty full years since losing my parents and my home.
In Japan (日本), in China (支那), on the empty plains of Siberia
Half a lifetime spent wandering has been a harsh dream.

My elder sister and younger sister, the only two siblings sharing my blood (同氣)
One is a thousand *li* away, the other ten thousand *li* away
Scattered, unable to meet for over 20 years now.

Heard the gunshots of the Kapchin year (甲辰, 1904) in Chŏngju Castle (定州城),
A hand that trembled in Tokyo (東京) over the Five-Article Treaty (5條約, 1905) and Seven-Article Treaty (7條約, 1907),
Wept at Osan (五山) on August 29th of the Kyŏngsul year (庚戌年, 1910).

The outbreak of the World War (世界大戰) in '14 found me in an inn (逆旅) in Chita (치따),
Spent November 11th [Armistice Day] in Beijing (北京),
Witnessed March 1st of the Kiminyŏn year (己未年, 1919) in Shanghai (上海).

To Wilson (윌손), Lloyd George (로이쪼지), Clemenceau (크레망소), those gentlemen,
After hand-typing and sending a long 800-franc telegram, I returned
And envisioned a New Government (新政府) in a corner of the French Concession (法租界).

Of the people from that time, who has died?
Those who became white-haired (白髮) in exile, him and him...
Why have I prolonged this wretched (苟且한), ailing life?

Intending to repay with the brush, I've written some 20-odd books.
Intending to repay with words, I've spoken tens of millions of words.
Looking back now, it was all in vain (허사엿서라).

Making a vow (盟誓) at 20, making a vow at 30,
Making a vow at 40, yet there's nothing I could achieve (읾일이 바이 없네).
The new vow upon facing 50 also feels merely forced (억진 듯만 싶어라).

Shall I try doing this? Shall I try doing that?
Saying I'll do it, I'll do it, but there’s nothing I’ve accomplished (해 놓은 일 바 엾으니)
Is it that I try to do the impossible? How shameful before others (남 부끄러 어이라).

What to do about this grand ambition, this great purpose crushed beneath a rock?
Trying and trying to unfold it, yet unable to unfold this aging ambition (묵는 뜻을)
It's my inborn foolishness, so let those who laugh, laugh (웃을 이는 웃으소).

Is it my inadequacy, or is the world mistaken?
Though I try and try, there isn't a single thing accomplished (하여진 일 어나 없네).
Only rash words I shouldn't speak* (*는 망발) follow one after another.
New Year's Eve (除夜), 1931. 1932, First [*day?]*, At the mortgaged (抵當잡힌) house in the Hundred-Year Old Capital's North Castle/City (百年舊都北城). <46>

**Summary**

This article by Yi Kwangsu, written on New Year's Eve 1931, presents personal reflections. He recalls small acts of kindness he received, like an old woman delousing him in his orphaned childhood, and shares the recent joy of his wife finally obtaining a long-desired Western-style wardrobe through an unexpected gift from her brother-in-law, highlighting the economic hardships of the time. The latter half consists of ten self-mocking poems written as he turns 41, lamenting his perceived failures, poor health, debts, and scattered family, while weaving in major historical events he witnessed: the lead-up to and annexation of Korea (1904-1910), World War I, the March 1st Movement (1919), and the establishment of the Korean Provisional Government in Shanghai, expressing disillusionment with his life's work and the struggle for independence. For historians, the article offers a poignant personal perspective from a major intellectual figure on daily life struggles under colonialism, combined with a deeply felt, though pessimistic, reflection on key moments in modern Korean history and the independence movement, including specific references to international diplomacy attempts post-1919. Yi's sense of personal and national futility at this juncture is particularly revealing.

黙想記錄
						李光洙
크게 고맙던 조고마한 일들
인생의 일생이 모도 세상의 은혜다. 더욱이 나와 같이 조실 부모하고 무의무가하게 자라난 사람은 40년의 과거를 돌아보면 모도가 남의 신세뿐이다. 지기만 하고 갚지는 못하는 내 부끄러움! 나는 그 중에 크게 고맙던 조고마한 일들을 멫가지 생각해 보려한다.

머리 빗겨준 늙은 부인
내가 열한 살에 부모를 여이고 표랑해 댕길 때에 제일 곤난한 것이 머리에 이 끄리는 것이엇다. 옷에 끄리는 이는 산ㅅ골작이 같은 데서 벗어서 잡아 입을 수가 잇지마는 머리에 끄리는 이는 나 혼자의 손으로는 어찌할 줄을 몰랏다. 하로는 (어느 겨울날) 어느 고개를 넘어가다가 양지짝 눈 녹은 잔디 판에 볏을 쪼이고 다리를 쉬이고 잇는데 따뜻하게 볏이 쪼이니까 머리에ㅅ이가 총출동을 하야 가렵기가 비길 데가 없엇다. 나는 손가락으로 머리를 긁적 거리며 손에 잡히는 대로 이를 눈우에 놓아 나를 못견디게 굴던 원수를 갚고 잇노라니까 문득 뒤로서 어떤 늙은 부인 한 분이,
「너 머리를 못 빗엇고나. 흰 댕기를 들인 것을 보니 부모가 돌아가섯고나. 나하고 가자, 머리 빗겨주마」
하엿다. 나는 그를 따라갓다. 그의 집은 조고마한 집이엇다. 그는 내 머리를 빗겨 촘촘한 참빗으로 서캐까지 훑어주고,
「후제도 머리에 이가 끌커든 와 응」
하고 찬밥까지 먹여서 나를 보내엇다.

洋服장
안해의 시집 올 때ㅅ 장은 다 낡어 빠지고 말앗다. 밑이 빠지고 문이 떠러지고 장식이 떠러지고. 안해는 서투른 손님- 그 중에도 여자 손님, 그 중에도 풍성풍성하게 사는 동무가 오면 이 비참한 장을 보이기를 부끄러워 하엿다.
그래서 안해는 매양 장을 하나 삿으면, 삿으면 ＜44＞ 하엿고, 누구는 자개 세간을 들엿든데, 어디는 양복장이 잇던데, 하고 장을 사고 싶어 한 것이 벌서 3, 4年이 넘엇다.
그러나 장을 살 여유는 없엇다. 오는 연종 상여금이나 타면, 하는 것도 다 헛 소망이엇다. 돈이 들어오기 전에 나갈 길부터 먼저 열렷다. 세말이 되면 진고개와 종로에서 장, 단스같은 것을 일등상으로 걸어놓고 경품부 대매출을 한다. 그 때마다 안해는 장이 빠지기를 바라고 물건을 삿다. 그러나 뽑는 것마다 타울수건, 화저까락 따위요. 바라는 장은 아니 빠졌다.
「장 아니 빠졋소?」
「나는 뽑는 복이 없어.」
금년에는 구리 풍로와 넥타이가 하나 빠졋다. 그러나 장은 빠지지 아니 하엿다.
섯달 그믐날 안해는 「금년에도 장을 못사는구나」 하는 생각을 아마 가지고 어디를 나갓다가, 기쁨으로 들어왓다.
「양복장 하나 생겻서」
이것은 마츰 와 잇는 친구에게 인사도 하기 전에 한 말이엇다.
「양복장?」
하고 나도 기뻣다. 안해의 소원성취를 위해 기뻣다.
「그래 무엇을 사고 빠졋소?」
하고 나는 경품으로 빠진준만 알앗다. 안해는 넘어 기뻐서 웃음 절반으로
「그런게 아니라」
하고 안해는 양복장이 생긴 유래를 설명하엿다. 그 유래는 이러하다.
안해는 형의 집에를 놀러갓다. 그 집에서는 목물전에 집을 빌려주엇던 것이 그 목물전이 페업을 하면서 멫달밀린 집세 대신에 양복장 세 개를 집주인에게 주엇다고 한다.
「나도 돈이 잇으면 하나 살텐데」
하고 내 안해는 형과 형부가 듣는 곧에서 평생 소원을 말하엿던 것이엇다. 이 말을 듣고 안해의 형부되는 이가 슬몃이 방에서 나오더니 얼마 잇다가,
「이것 나와 보시오」
하고 내 안해를 불럿다.
나와 보니 과연 맘에 드는 양복장 하나를 갓다가 마당에 놓앗더란다.
