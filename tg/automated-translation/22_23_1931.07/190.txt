Honesty, the Essence of the Sporting Spirit
						By Ch'oe Nŭngjin

Athletes who lack an honest spirit and the habit of acting honestly are merely a haphazard Army of Sports; they can never be true sportsmen. A sportsman must be honest. Dishonesty is forbidden in the world of sports; it is not permitted. On the playing field, the athletes themselves do not permit it, and the teacher does not forgive it. On the competition ground, the opponent or the referee does not forgive this forbidden act. It is precisely this dishonesty that sends a sportsman to hell. If an athlete, trying to become a member of a team, behaves dishonestly in word or deed towards the other players, that person will eventually become an outcast. If they are dishonest towards their teacher, regardless of their skill, they will be expelled from the team. Furthermore, if they act dishonestly towards opposing team members or the referee on the competition ground, they will be penalized, bringing disgrace upon themselves and dishonor to their school. Thus, the sports field is a stage for the honest and a laboratory for the practitioner who demonstrates real ability. Some young men, lacking athletic skill yet pretending to be good and boasting, try to become athletes but are dropped during practice. The coach prefers hidden talents over famous athletes. Therefore, they look at all those active on the field with the same eye, selecting only the skilled ones and discarding the showy but insubstantial athletes who are like 'bean paste cakes that look good but taste bad.' In this way, the idea of honesty is instilled in athletes from the very beginning. Also, the first thing a coach asks of athletes is to be honest: honest with each other, honest with the teachers, and honest in society. As athletes, they are asked to keep promised times, maintain absolute secrecy regarding game plans, and during competition, be honest by focusing only on skill, abandoning habits like pretending to kick the ball but kicking another's leg, or pretending to receive the ball while pushing another over with their shoulder, and simply play the game honestly with skill. However, despite such education, some athletes make dishonesty their trade, deceiving school authorities and the coach, scheming just to get food or perhaps stealing sportswear, eventually getting expelled mid-studies or wandering from school to school, destroying their own character as dishonest individuals. But their number is very small. In countries like America, where the sports world has become educationalized, the number of such individuals is extremely small. In our country too, the number of such dishonest people will decrease in the future. To make it decrease, coaches and homeroom teachers must prioritize the character development of individual athletes over the team's victory and punish dishonest athletes. This is a very difficult task. The author, too, has personal feelings on this point, but acts as an educator. Here, I will give a real example regarding this point. I intend to relate one story from the experiences of Mr. Stagg, world-famous and the father of the American sports world, the head of physical education and coach at the University of Chicago. Mr. Stagg is an old man in his seventies, but he still coaches and <54> is a warrior who still participates in tennis tournaments. In the more than 40 years he has been coaching at the University of Chicago, the number of graduates who received his coaching exceeds 12,000. Mr. Stagg boasts that although these 10,000-plus athletes are engaged in various types of professions, they are all successful in their respective fields. Among them, there is one great success story, a very wealthy man who now runs a factory business in the city of Pittsburgh. Twenty years ago, this man was the captain of the University of Chicago baseball team. At that time, this wealthy man, Harry, was about to graduate and was also an important second baseman. A baseball game against Wisconsin University was held at the latter's ballpark. The teams were evenly matched, and the score was 0 to 0 until the 9th inning. In the top of the 9th inning, this Harry, the second baseman and captain, hit a home run. Because of his home run, the University of Chicago won the game. The players and supporters couldn't contain their joy and carried Harry on their shoulders around the ballpark, shouting "Hurrah for the University of Chicago!" Thus victorious, the University of Chicago people were returning to campus by train from Madison. During the journey, amidst the joy, a great tragedy occurred—a tragedy caused by dishonesty. The content of this real-life tragedy is as follows. Harry, thinking about how in just one more month he would graduate and also hold the honorable title of captain of the victorious University of Chicago baseball team, was overjoyed and came to sit next to Coach Stagg, starting a conversation. The coach, being someone who enjoyed joking with young men, before Harry could finish his polite greetings, said with a laugh, "Harry? It seems a god must have possessed your bat! That's how you hit that home run. When in your life have you ever hit a home run like that?" Harry also laughed and replied, "Coach, what do you mean a spirit in my bat? This home run wasn't hit by my bat, it was hit by my brain." So Coach Stagg, laughing again, asked, "What do you mean by that? If you hadn't hit that ball, what power would your brain's strategic plan have had?" To this question, Harry answered very honestly, "Coach! It really wasn't a home run. After I hit the ball and was running towards second base, I saw the center fielder already trying to pick up the ball. No matter how I thought about it, it seemed impossible to make it all the way home, and if I couldn't, I wouldn't score a run. So, taking advantage of the moment when the crowd, the opposing team, and the umpire were all watching the center fielder running to grab the ball, I didn't touch second base, nor did I touch third base; I took a shortcut (cutting across) and ran straight home. Fortunately, no one saw it, so we won this time. So isn't this a victory of my brain? How can it be that a god possessed my bat and I won by hitting the ball well?" Coach Stagg, upon hearing this, remained silent for a moment, then said in a soft voice, "Harry! You are a dishonest person. You are a sinner who has disregarded character development for the sake of victory alone and committed an act dishonorable to the school. You are a dishonest person who deceives others. Resign from your glorious captaincy immediately, return your uniform, and withdraw from the University of Chicago athletic club," he ordered. Hearing these words was like a thunderbolt from a clear sky for Harry; he couldn't say anything and just did as he was told. One month later, Harry received his graduation certificate and left Chicago without a word. For over 20 years, there was no news of him. By chance, Coach Stagg stopped in the city of Pittsburgh on his way to his alma mater, Springfield College (스푸링필드 체육 전문학교). At the station, an unfamiliar gentleman came out to meet him, took his bag, and asked, "Coach! Do you remember me?" While Stagg hesitated, the gentleman spoke again, "Coach, if you don't remember me, do you remember the person who hit a home run during the baseball game against Wisconsin University over 20 years ago and was kicked off the team? That person is me." This incident was very memorable for Stagg. While riding in a car to Harry's factory office, he asked about his life over the past 20 years. Harry replied, "Coach, the thing that got me kicked out of the baseball club back then was taking a shortcut (cutting across, meaning deceiving others). Because of that, from that time on, I made 'Don't take shortcuts' my motto (表語). For over 20 years, I have never deceived others and have always been honest. As a result, starting as a bank clerk (사묊�원), I have now become a millionaire businessman, and moreover, I have never heard anyone call me dishonest." Stagg heard this and urged us, who are to become physical education teachers, to promote physical education centered on honesty. Indeed, it is true. The experiences and habits gained on the sports field truly soak into one's marrow (骨髓). If one just kicks a ball on the sports field without cultivating character, that would truly be a failure of education. In this regard, I hope that schools will not leave sports competitions solely to the students themselves but will select 지도자 (leaders/instructors) of character to achieve the educational purpose of the sports field. <55>

**Summary**

This 1931 article by Ch'oe Nŭngjin argues forcefully that honesty is the absolute cornerstone of the sporting spirit and athletic character development. He contrasts true "sportsmen" with undisciplined athletes, emphasizing that dishonesty leads to expulsion and disgrace. The core of the piece is a lengthy anecdote about the famous American coach Amos Alonzo Stagg of the University of Chicago, who expelled his star baseball captain, Harry, for admitting he cut bases after hitting a game-winning "home run." Years later, Harry attributed his great success in business to the lesson learned, having adopted "Don't take shortcuts" as his life motto. The author concludes by urging Korean schools to prioritize character education through sports under the guidance of ethical instructors, rather than focusing solely on winning.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its strong emphasis on Western (specifically American) sporting ideals and figures like Stagg as models for Korean physical education during the colonial period. It highlights the perceived role of sports in building moral character, potentially as a subtle counter-narrative to the purely militaristic or nationalistic uses of physical training. The focus on individual integrity and the condemnation of a "win-at-all-costs" mentality, illustrated by the Stagg anecdote, might reflect contemporary anxieties or aspirations within Korean society under Japanese rule.
