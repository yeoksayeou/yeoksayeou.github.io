**July Musings, A Hundred Flowers Blooming**
Various Authors

**Cactus Flower (仙人掌 꽃)**
Creator Kim Tong-in (金東仁)

In my house, there is a cactus shaped like a chestnut bur. I don't know how many years old it is, but it seems quite aged. Its height is just about a foot, and its diameter is 4 or 5 *ch'i* (寸). Its "growth" (長成) stopped 4 or 5 years ago, and every year it only produces dozens of offspring.
It started showing flowers four years ago. The first year, one flower bloomed. The second year, two flowers bloomed. Last year, two flowers also bloomed. This year, not even a flower bud is visible yet. Will it bloom at all this year?
Like a boil from shingles, a small unusual object appears between the thorns. It grows little by little, and after about a month, it becomes about an inch long. After another month or so, it grows to about three inches. Then, a pale white flower, like a gourd flower, blooms there.
However, the period the flower stays open is extremely short. Perhaps three or four hours. The first year the flower bloomed, I saw it almost fully open in the evening, expecting it to be fully open the next day. I waited for it and went to sleep, but the next morning, the flower had already withered and drooped lifelessly.
The following year, I saw the flower for the first time. A maiden raised in a secluded chamber (深窓)! That was the only way I could describe its elegance. But after three or four hours, it had already withered.
Last year, two flowers emerged at the same time. They grew for over two months and finally bloomed one evening after dark.
"I won't get to see the flowers fully this year either."
But I strongly forbade the children who wanted to pick the flowers. Although the flower would bloom while we slept and wither while we slept, I stopped the children from picking it to play with. The next morning, I saw the flower had already wilted and drooped.
Why? Why did I stop the children who wanted to pick and play with the flower that blooms while we sleep <43> and withers while we sleep? It wasn't for any other reason. It was out of gratitude for the effort it put in for over two months for a mere 3 or 4 hours of blooming.

The effort to show one or two flowers a year, and that too, to boast for only a short time of three or four hours, is truly tear-jerking.
In that cactus, I saw the magnitude of the effort to bloom and the astonishing pride of its flower.
Showing flowers is indeed a proud feat. The hardship of the cactus, growing for over 10 years, enduring the sun and rain daily, just to show one or two flowers a year (sometimes skipping a year, they say)—and that too, for a brief moment that one might miss if looking elsewhere—is truly astonishing. And though the blooming time is short, the pride of the flower is immense.

When will it bloom this year?
The bud, like a willow catkin, is still nowhere to be seen. Perhaps it will skip this year.

**Cheju Halla Mountain (濟州漢拏山)**
Chŏnju Sin Tong-gi (申東起)

Having been born and raised in Chosŏn (朝鮮) for over 30 years now, it is my constant regret that I have not traveled widely across the land of Chosŏn.
Though I traveled abroad early on, even crossing the Pacific Ocean, within the peninsula, I always postponed it, saying today or tomorrow, and thus it came to this.
If you go to Cheju (濟州) and ask the locals if they have been to Halla Mountain (漢拏山), they reply, "Why, is there some special reason?" It seems the vast majority of people, though they may gaze upon it like their front yard mountain throughout their lives, will die without ever climbing it.
Leaving Mokp'o (木浦) around 8 PM on the Chogwi's (朝汽) *Haeju Maru* (海州丸), one arrives at Cheju Castle in 11 or 12 hours. Halla Mountain begins to appear from a distance of 4 or 5 hours' sailing. Stepping onto the deck in the dim, cool dawn breeze, when Halla Mountain started to appear far away, I suddenly recalled the song I learned long ago, "From Paektu Mountain (白頭山) to Halla Mountain," a *ch'angga* (唱歌). The height of Halla Mountain is about 6,700 *ch'ŏk* (尺). It is located in the center of the island, and all the houses seem to be scattered around its foothills (山麓).
My trip this time was too short, and since I couldn't see much, rather than talking about the plant life widely distributed on Halla Mountain or other collection-related stories, I intend to introduce just a few customs and habits.
Cheju Island is said to have three abundances (三多): abundant wind (風多), abundant stones (石多), and abundant women (女多). Upon disembarking, the first thing that catches the eye is the structure of the thatched roofs (草葺); unlike the mainland, seeing the roofs thatched with incredibly thick ropes, one can tell the wind is indeed abundant. Also, due to the strong winds, volcanic ash flies around, which is certainly not beneficial for the respiratory system.
Looking at the walls of houses, the boundaries of fields (田畓), and the state of the roads, one can easily see the abundance of stones. Seeing the walls built with round volcanic rocks (熔灰岩), it seems the technique of wall-building is quite developed. Not only field boundaries but also livestock pastures are all demarcated by stone walls. According to the locals, shoes wear out about three times faster than on the mainland, so the roughness of the roads goes without saying. Seeing the *haenyŏ* (海女 - female divers) diving in their persimmon-dyed (柿) trousers and jackets by the shore truly gives one the feeling of being in the South Seas (南洋). The women are so healthy; those women walking around vigorously with chests puffed out must have several times the strength of mainland men. The market is filled only with women, those selling firewood in the streets are also women, and remarkably, I even saw a stone mill, which would normally be turned by the power of oxen or horses, <44> being turned by the strength of 3 or 4 women. Mainland women would surely be astonished to see the activities of Cheju Island women.
Also, within Cheju Castle, not a single beggar can be seen. Not only do both men and women work, but the original nature of Cheju people has similarities to Kaesŏng (開城) people; no matter how difficult their lives, they do not rely on others, and it seems everyone has at least some savings. Apparently, just 30 or 40 *ri* (里) outside Cheju Castle, there is much farmland where a *pyeong* (坪 - approx. 3.3 sq meters) costs less than 5 *rin* (厘). In my opinion, wouldn't it be more advantageous for our Chosŏn people to emigrate to Cheju Island rather than going as immigrants to Northwest Kando (西北間島)?

***Sŏdŏngyo* and *Chŏngŭpsa* (薯童謠와 井邑詞)**
Historian Mun Il-p'yŏng (文一平)

When spring comes to Nak'wa Rock (落花岩), red flowers dot themselves with rouge (臙脂),
When night falls on Yŏngwŏl Terrace (迎月臺), the slender moon paints its eyebrows,
Blending with the gentle, soft curvilinear beauty of the mountains and waters, these enchanting flowers and moon still evoke the warm sentiment of a Southern beauty (南國佳人) even today,
But the prosperity of the Paekche (百濟) capital during the 120-odd years since King Sŏng (聖王) has faded away like the trace of a clear dream.
Renowned for the beauty of its architecture among the Three Kingdoms (三國), the artistic capital boasted magnificent Buddhist temples (佛字) and detached palaces (離宮) like Samhŭng Temple (三興寺) and Imnyu Pavilion (臨流閣), adorned with splendid gold and jade decorations (黃金碧玉). They displayed the artistic genius (神技) of the Paekche people to the fullest, possessing exquisite taste (妙趣) amidst grandeur (壯麗) and subtle charm (幽韻) within intricacy (精巧). Yet, when the Sabi Royal Castle (泗泚王城) fell into enemy hands, it was tragically reduced to ashes by cruel flames, a regret for a thousand autumns (千秋의 遺恨).
Today, when even the 9-story pagoda of Hwangnyong Temple (皇龍寺), said to have been built by Abi-ji (阿非知), is gone, the only glimpses of Paekche architectural skill, though not from within our land, are found in the Hōryū-ji (法隆寺) of Yamato (大和) and, as a shameful symbol, only the P'yŏngje Pagoda (平濟塔). Thus, it is a fact that Paekche's art, in terms of architecture, has already perished (殘滅).
However, is this limited only to architecture? Haven't the songs and music (樂歌) also been lost?
In the *Sok'akchi* (俗樂志 - Section on Folk Music) of the *Koryŏsa* (高麗史 - History of Koryŏ), only a few titles of Paekche songs and music are recorded. The actual core, the lyrics (歌謠) themselves, were omitted because they were considered vulgar (卑俗). This is one example of us destroying our own art with our own hands (戕賊). If the *Samguk Yusa* (三國遺事 - Memorabilia of the Three Kingdoms) had not transmitted the one piece *Sŏdŏngyo* (薯童謠), and if the *Ak'ak Kwebŏm* (樂學軌範 - Canon of Musicology) had not preserved the one piece *Chŏngŭpsa* (井邑詞), then today, Paekche songs would have completely disappeared without a trace.
That these one or two songs happened to survive is truly fortunate (奇幸).
Moreover, both concern romance (로맨쓰) between men and women—one from the royal family, the other from commoners—representing both upper and lower strata, which evokes much interest.
*Sŏdŏngyo* was composed by King Mu (武王) of Paekche in his childhood (兒時) upon hearing of the beauty (艶麗) of Princess Sŏnhwa (善花公主) of Silla (新羅) and falling in love with her.
*Chŏngŭpsa* was written by the wife of a merchant, guarding her lonely chamber (孤閨), expressing the deep, heartfelt emotions (情懷) knotted within her heart. Its graceful beauty (婉美) and pure elegance (醇雅), reflecting the ancient style, touch the heartstrings (心琴) of anyone who encounters it.
(An excerpt from Art and Romance)

**From Anguish to Encouragement -(Thinking of a Sick Friend)- (焦悶으로부터 激勵에 -(病友생각)-)**
Poet Kim Hae-gang (金海剛)

Hey there, P! (P君!)
How much you must be suffering. The letter you sent from your sickbed pierces my heart again. <45>
My heart burns with even greater anguish (焦悶) than yours, lying suffering in your sickbed.
Why are we so unhealthy? We, who should be 10 or 20 times healthier than others, possess not even half of what they enjoy. What kind of contradiction is this?
Life snatches away even the opportunity for us to pursue health! Our reality, where we must engage in excessive labor, even harming our health, just to secure our daily living (圖得)—thinking about how long this will continue makes the future seem bleak indeed.
Hey there, P!
Seeing this bleak reality laid out before our eyes, must we simply overlook it? Some weak-hearted friends get swept away by the bleak torrent (奔流), losing even their consciousness of life, falling into the pit of degradation. The appearance of those wandering aimlessly, shedding cold tears, or the mindset (心影) of those in the prime of youth who even yearn for cheap suicide (歇價의 자살), shows how much they are fleeing from reality.
So then, P!
Should we just lament (咏嘆)? Should we just beat our chests in despair and take the path of self-abandonment (自暴自棄)? Should we escape reality? Here, I say—let's kick aside this dead-end reality and move forward. Here lies our fervent spirit (意氣), pulsing with the desire to live, our entire effort (全努力) solemnly existing (儼然 自存). Never let your spirit be discouraged (沮傷).
And you, friend.
As much as we are placed in a special environment, and as much as we are governed by a special environment, there must be growth in a strong consciousness of life.
Here, our spur-like progress in life will exist, and a steel-like foundation for life will be established.
However, P!
Judging from the meaning expressed in your letter, isn't the cause of your recent illness the discouragement (沮喪) of spirit (意氣) that budded (萌芽) from the depression (沈鬱) of an unstable life, caught up in that chaotic situation (the labor market) where you are tossed about? Though precarious, you managed to patch up (彌縫) your daily life by selling your labor, even at a low price (歇價). But when the deadly whirlwind of recession (不景氣의 旋風) struck, you couldn't find any reserve strength (底力) to defend against it. Your weak constitution, burdened further by fatigue disproportionate to your health, finally led to the discouragement of your spirit, causing you to fall ill amidst the lump-like depression (銳塊같은 沈鬱). This is what I think.
Whether my deduction hits the mark or not is a secondary issue. I have prefaced the above few words while accepting my own deduction (自許自斷).
Of course, even if my deduction is a groundless assumption (妄斷) born from excessive worry (杞憂)—and indeed, I sincerely hope (自期不捨) it is just a groundless assumption—it is an expression of affection (哀情) thrown out of an inescapable sense of duty (責務感) that we must sometimes whip (鞭撻) and encourage each other to move forward.
Please do not lack the magnanimity (寬大) to accept this sentiment.
Well then, P! Finally, I add one more word to present to you (奉呈).
Do not lose your health. For us who are fighting to seize (戰取) life, "Health"! This is the greatest preparation and the best key (要訣) above all else. The greater the deprivation (遞奪) of health, the stronger the power to fight and gain (圖得코저 戰取) health must be.
Let's blow away the shadow of lament (咏嘆). We must cultivate the underlying strength (底力) to fight even harder.
P! Do not lose your health. I'll stop here.
—Attaching good news for the future, along with the growth of a strong consciousness.

**Time-Based Life (時間生活)**
Haeju Ch'oe Ch'ang-mun (崔昌文)

Currently, in pioneering the destiny of our society, there are likely many tasks in various fields. <46>
Namely, rural development (農村啓發), youth guidance (少年指導), women's liberation (婦女解放), industrial movement (産業運動), illiteracy eradication (文盲打破), etc.—truly too numerous to list (枚擧키 難하다). To carry out all these movements, no single individual can do it alone; it must be done collectively by gathering the strength of many people. To exert the power of a group, many people must assemble (集會). For this assembly to be successful and the group to be solid (鞏固), there must be many members with a strong sense of time.
In today's busy (多事多忙) society, the slow and weak development of all organizations is, of course, due to other reasons as well, but I dare say it is largely attributable to the lack of time consciousness among the individuals constituting the groups. There is no doubt that a group whose meetings convene punctually and smoothly will prosper. If the concept of keeping time like one does for a train schedule were deeply ingrained in everyone's mind, the extremely bad practice of starting meetings an hour late or having them cancelled (流會) would surely disappear.
As someone involved in many group activities, the most headache-inducing aspect is the lack of time consciousness among the general public. Lack of interest in group life or the stagnation (不振) of group momentum (會勢) is largely caused by this, I believe. Among these, it is truly lamentable (長歎) that the lack of time consciousness is particularly severe among the intellectual class (知識階級) or the leadership class (指導階級). It is utterly detestable (可憎스럽기 짝이 없다) when those who pretend to be punctual themselves arrive late and then criticize others. Although this concept of time may seem like a cliché (陳腐) or a trivial matter, if the general public unites (一致協力) and diligently practices (勵行) it, the face of our society will truly be renewed (更新). All gatherings and groups will gain vitality and develop, and in all fields and endeavors, results will be achieved with half the effort (事半功倍).
Oh, young actors (役者 - workers/activists) who intend to build a new society (新社會)! We each need to reflect on our past and examine whether we have lived a time-based life or not. At the same time, let us firmly pledge (盟誓) to reflect deeply (猛省) so that in the future, no single individual causes harm to the masses or incurs shame (恥辱) from outsiders, and let us sacrifice ourselves first to practice the precious "time" movement.

**Oh, Youth! (靑年아!)**
Sariwŏn Yi Tŏk-hwan (李德煥)

Youth! Our youth! Our youth with the most tasks! Ah! If you faithfully do others' work as your own, you will become the sovereign (主權者) in the future. If you treat even your own work as negligently as others', you will easily become a slave (奴隸)! Youth, our youth, currently undergoing cultivation (修養)! Don't just seek employment with a dependent nature (依賴性); cultivate creative power (創作力) and create businesses! Youth, our youth, facing only difficulties (難關)! If you only exercise caution (謹愼), there will be nothing to do; take risks (冒險) and aim for success. With this, I sincerely wish you well (誠祝).

**The Real Estate Broker Old Man (福德房 녕감)**
Novelist Yi T'ae-jun (李泰俊)

Today at lunchtime, in a cold noodle (冷麵) house.
Of the two old men, one *yŏnggam* (old man), wearing a crumpled ramie *turumagi* (두루매기 - traditional overcoat) stained with grime, had a haggard complexion but somehow retained the air of a well-bred Seoul person. The other *yŏnggam*, wearing a glossy silk gauze (*hangna*) *turumagi* and having a decent physique, seemed like someone who had just come up from the countryside due to his naive appearance. <47>
They entered, continuing the conversation they were having outside. Sitting among many people, they chatted on in the same tone, regardless of whether others listened.
"They say building a house is hard, but that's an old saying. Nowadays, even a 40 or 50-*kan* (間 - unit of space) house, if you ask for it within a month, they'll have it perfectly finished within a month without needing any extra work. Just listen to me. Buy that land. I'll get it for you at that price..."
"But still, it's too isolated there. Shouldn't Koreans live somewhere..."
"No, sir. You don't understand. I'm building my own house with my own money, living off my own things. What does it matter if it's a Japanese neighborhood or even a Westerner's neighborhood? And sir, since you don't know, you really shouldn't mix in a Korean neighborhood. There are many unsightly things. Even tax collectors act differently when they come to a Korean neighborhood compared to a Japanese neighborhood. And firstly, I have children too, but you really shouldn't raise children in a Korean neighborhood. Korean kids, without exception, are from poor families, and the way they play or talk can't compare to those [Japanese] kids. Don't even think about choosing a house in a Korean neighborhood."
"Well, that's true. If even Mencius's mother moved three times for her child's education (三遷之敎), how can one not think of the children when moving house..."
"Exactly. As the old saying goes, 'He who touches pitch shall be defiled' (近墨者黑). You shouldn't raise children among Korean kids."
The haggard *yŏnggam* saying this was unmistakably a real estate broker (*poktŏkbang yŏnggam*). He must have said it just for his client to hear so the deal would go through, but it seemed no one in the room was deaf. Everyone glanced sideways disapprovingly. Finally, criticism came from one person's mouth. It was a neat-looking middle-aged man who had been eating *pibimbap* and was getting up.
"Hey, you seem to be a broker (仲介人). No matter if you live off commissions (口錢)..."
"..."
The real estate broker *yŏnggam* put down the noodle bowl he was eating and initially stared blankly without answering.
"Even if you're trying to make a deal, you should speak truthfully... Are you not a Korean? Where did you come from?"
"..."
"Old folks like us deserve to be cursed by mouths like yours. But why should the children be cursed by your mouth? Why should they be brought up in relation to whether you make a commission or not?"
"...A thousand times, ten thousand times, you are right. Your words are right..."
"You dog-like fellow. You must have been born to a Korean father and a Korean mother. Your children aren't of a different stock, are they? You despicable man."
"An old man spoke thoughtlessly. A thousand times, ten thousand times, your words are right."
The neat middle-aged man lowered his voice and said this to the country *yŏnggam* in the silk gauze *turumagi* before leaving.
"Sir, I don't know what deal you entrusted to this person, but if you do business with a cunning fellow like this, you'll always get cheated (속습넨다). Be careful."
That darn *yŏnggam*, serves him right!
Everyone felt refreshed. So did I. I felt ashamed that I myself couldn't curse like that at the darn *yŏnggam*'s remarks, but it served him right.
However, ideology aside, the real estate broker *yŏnggam* didn't seem like such a bad person. When he let out a deep sigh, pushed away the cold noodle bowl he seemed to be tasting for the first time in a while as if choking up, took out a pitch-black handkerchief, pretended to wipe his mouth but wiped his eyes instead, I couldn't help but feel pity rather than hatred.
What had made that old *yŏnggam*, who had lived his entire 60 years, say such unintentionally offensive words and suffer humiliation?
It felt like watching a small tragedy of life.
—From June 1st diary— <48>

**Korea's Objective Reality and the People It Demands (朝鮮의 客觀的 現實과 그 要求하는 사람들)**
Unggi Hyŏn Ki-t'ae (玄基台)

(Omitted beginning - original text) It wouldn't be futile (徒勞) for us to examine what kind of people the objective reality of Chosŏn (朝鮮) demands. This is why I chose this topic. I believe it strongly demands people who possess the five conditions listed below. Accordingly, a united body (團結体) composed of such people as its organizing elements will be our lifeline.
1.  People who cherish a lifelong grand plan (大計) for society and constantly strive to advance that cherished plan even by an inch (寸步).
1.  People who, no matter when, where, or what they are doing, nor what difficulties and hardships they face, are never disarmed (武裝解除) of their cherished grand plan.
1.  People who constantly maintain a clear observation of Chosŏn's objective reality.
1.  People who make special efforts (特別用力) to promote their physical health.
1.  People who, at the very least, manage their own livelihood.

**A Female Teacher in a Foreign Land (異域의 一女敎員)**
Yongjŏng Village Mo Yun-suk (毛允淑)

May 27th.
It has been two months since I settled across the Tuman River (豆滿江). The region (地帶) is already unstable, so why is the weather also so unsettled? My head feels strangely heavy, as if struck by something. I feel no attachment to any aspect of life. Perhaps because it's not our land, everything I see and hear is just rough rumor.
Why on earth did precious Chosŏn people flock to such desolate fields? Economic hardship (生活難)? Could it be? Indeed, the price of rice here is much cheaper than in Chosŏn. I heard a *mal* (말 - unit of volume) of rice is 25 *chŏn* (錢), and student lodging (宿費) is 3 *wŏn* (圓) per month, which is something to consider. Even so, the Chosŏn people who flocked here in proportion are extremely poor. For breakfast, I ate potato rice. It has quite a sweet taste (甘味). In the evening, I strolled along the Tuman River bank.
The sunlight in North Manchukuo (北滿) looks terribly desolate. The endlessly open plains spread out in all directions, and it seems one could freely let out one's vast heart and shout without anyone complaining.
A reddish-black, piercing dusk that makes one want to leave aimlessly for somewhere.
The sunlight, perhaps filled with anger at something, looks like it could burst at any moment. Though the season is summer, here it's no different from winter. The sound of the Tuman River's water creeps into my ears disturbingly, like the tragic cry (悲聲) of a dying hero (鐵雄) announcing his final fate. Is it the combined lament (綜合痛哭) of the tangled, heart-wrenching sorrows of fleeing masses?
Still, thinking this place offers freedom, countless people cross over secretly each day (하로에도), turning their backs on their heartbroken homeland. But that too was the happiness of a bygone era. Now they face a fate where they are subjected to double and triple exploitation (蹂躪) and must cross this water again.
Warm evening smoke drifts (몰몰) up from the mountain pass. The sight of a woman in white clothes, carrying a bundle of gathered greens (나물 버금지), hurrying into the kitchen reminds me of Kim Tong-hwan's (金東煥) "Night at the Border" (國境의 밤), which I read sometime ago. Suddenly, a thumping sound (툭탁툭탁) comes from the field ridge over there. Startled, I look towards the sound. In the darkness, no person is visible, only a choked voice crying out, "You scoundrel, give back my daughter-in-law! Quickly! Quickly!" It seems to be a Chosŏn person suffering, captured by some Chinese person (中人). My companion says, "Don't be too surprised. Such things are commonplace here. Lately, Chosŏn people are having the fields they farmed taken back, and women are violated (蹂躪) by ignorant bandits (馬賊). It's not just once or twice that families are ruined like that," finishing his words in the most ordinary tone.
I returned to my lodging (宿舍) with a disturbed mind (心懷). The night in this foreign land (異域) deepened. The clock struck 12, but I couldn't sleep. The choked cries of a Chinese steamed bun vendor (만투장사) rise and fade, carried by the loud wind sound. <49> Though nothing particular comes to mind, why is sleep so slow to come? Thump, thump, large raindrops begin to fall. What happened to that old man from earlier? My thoughts fly endlessly, hovering over the pitiful old man's eyes.
June 5th.
Today is also quite a chilly day. With no familiar mountains and rivers (山川), who can announce the spring season? Since no swallows fly in, who knows if Kangnam (江南 - south of the Yangtze, symbolizing a warm place) is comfortable? The weather is so capricious, how can illness not arise? Due to a cold, my head has felt heavy since last night, and I can't gather my clear thoughts. I skipped school and lay down all day. I can't even read books, and the idle fancies and delusions (空想妄想) that used to arise, one after another, annoyingly when I had nothing else to do, seem to have fallen ill and stopped (休止). My head is an empty sack (空 자루). With such a weak body and this kind of disposition (버리), why did I come all the way here (예까지)?
In the evening, Messrs. B and K (B, K 兩氏) sent letters. Seoul is now a world of green shade (錄陰世界), they say. Here, where snow still flurries, it sounds like a lie. It hasn't been long since I left my studies (學窓), less than two months since I let go of my friends' hands, yet receiving letters intensifies the longing (剌戟 - stimulus/longing). Here, there are no close friends to hold onto and talk with. Everyone is just awkward.
Six o'clock in the evening! Thinking I'd get some air by the riverbank, I walked to the nearby river. The river's name is Hae-lan River (海蘭江). The name evokes gentle associations, but the river's face is not peaceful. Muddy water surges like dragons (龍). Flowing downstream, it gets pushed back by the wind, tumbling over and over, emitting terrifying sounds. Darkness descends all around. Here and there, the clanging sound of Chinese army swords (칼자루 소리) echoes. A Chosŏn woman, wearing a tattered jacket, even carrying a small baby (어린 애기) on her back (뒤 등), dips her bright red legs into the water, attempting to cross the river.
The female general (女將) of Chosŏn crossing the fierce waves, across the Tuman River.
Returning to the dormitory (宿舍), I found the room in unexpected chaos (修羅場). A thief (도적놈) had broken in. Boxes and books were torn open and scattered (희터놓고) around the room; only the wallet (돈지갑) and a few clothes were taken. The room, seemingly trembled by the thief's eyes, feels terrifyingly desolate. Someone must have reported it, as several policemen (巡査) arrived. After enduring a lengthy, pointless interrogation (取調), my head aches (아푸고) and my fever rises further.
The desolate wind still beats against the window. Perhaps it's snowing. How will I survive a day (하로) tomorrow (래일) in this fearsome region? As I struggled to find my way through this worry and that thought, I fell asleep.

**The Wound on the Left Arm (왼 팔의 傷處)**
P'ip'an Sa Song Pong-u (宋奉瑀)

June Xth. Clear (Mon)
My body feels as heavy as a thousand catties (千斤). Perhaps because I went to bed late last night and woke up early (일즉), I feel extremely tired.
As usual, I climbed the mountain. I wandered back and forth through the forest (樹林) and stopped my steps at an unknown temple. I was recalling old memories with S (S君) when my steps halted unexpectedly. I imagined his form (形姿), currently in prison (獄中), and turned my head towards the western sky (西天). Ah! Unknowable are human affairs, and unpredictable are the ways of the world.
Pretending to eat breakfast (朝飯), I dusted off my clothes and left the house. It was to see about the XX matter. I sought out this person and met that person to make requests. They all readily agreed (快諾), saying, "Let's do that," but until this is all done, I don't know how many times my legs will have to walk to the gates (門前) of this house and that.
Returning to X, today there was a pile of letters. I read them all. After finishing all the tasks according to the senders' requests, my head aches. I tried joking (弄談) with Brother C (C兄), and he responded well. After laughing heartily, jumping around, and making noise, my headache feels lighter.
Today is also gone. A day of suffering in this world (娑婆苦) is gone. After eating dinner (夕飯), I sat around with C and Y. The topic of our conversation was the issue that a person named H had done XX [likely something negative/immoral]. H is apparently someone who does XXXX. The topic was whether it is a sin <50> for someone who does that to engage in XX. In the end, we concluded that such an act is a sin. And C insisted that in the future, if there are people of this kind, they must be resolutely rejected (斷然히 배척). "He is not XXXX but XX, a scoundrel who went around selling XXXX," he concluded.
At night, I went to bed early (일즉어니).

June Xth Clear (Tue)
As usual (如常히), after waking up (起寢), climbing the mountain (登山), and finishing breakfast (朝飯), I continued working until noon. Then my eyes grew dim (캉캄하고), and my head throbbed (틩하니). I ran up the mountain. After exercising and deep breathing (深呼吸), I sat under a pine tree (松下). As I stood up to return home, I saw Brother C sitting on the K School (K校) sports field (運動場). I picked up a small stone (小石) and, aiming (게누워서) at his back, threw it. Brother C jumped in surprise and looked back. I quickly hid behind a tree shade. Peeking through the branches at what he was doing, his looking around (두리번두리번) was truly funny (웃으엇다). He seemed to be looking for the person who hit him. I thought to myself, "Nya nya, bet you can't find me" (용용 죽겟니). Brother C eventually seemed to think it wasn't a place to stay and started walking slowly (어슬렁어슬렁) towards home. I suddenly stood up straight (벌덕) and threw another stone. This one missed. He didn't notice either. I burst out laughing, "Hahahaha," all by myself (그애 만에). He looked back towards the sound of laughter, spotted me, and grinned (빙글). Then he picked up a stone, aimed at me, and threw it (더진다). I threw one too (더젓다). Like children, we laughed and played (작란) and returned home together.
In the evening street, I met C (C君). I brought him home (더리고). In his usual hesitant manner of speaking (例의 우물쭈물하는 어법), he talked about the hardships he suffered in various places after dedicating himself to the movement. It was quite interesting (퍽 흥미가 잇섯다). And inwardly, I paid respect (敬意) to this warrior (勇士). He rolled up his left sleeve and showed a large wound (傷處). He said it was the trace of sacrifice. I played all sorts of pranks (작란) and joked (弄談) with him. Before I knew it, it was one o'clock in the morning (새로 한 시). We should have been sleeping, but C's talk wouldn't stop.

**The Fighter on Ŭlmil Terrace (乙密臺上의 鬪士)**
Poet Kim Ch'ang-sul (金昌述)

The new tactics (戰術) of our female fighter (女鬪士) Kang Chu-ryong (강주룡 女史), who recently set a new record in the Chosŏn labor movement, provide us with new lessons.
The strike at the P'yŏngwŏn Rubber Factory (平原 고무공장)!
Our brave sisters, who fought risking their lives (生命을 도하야) to resist the unreasonable wage cuts (賃金引下), were unable to continue their factory sit-in (籠城) for even a few days. Drowned in resentment (悲憤), they had no choice but to be driven out by the interference of those scoundrels (놈들).
Reality dictates that all rights are granted to those scoundrels, so isn't it something that cannot be helped? Right! It's something that cannot be helped.
Suppressing the resentment of being driven out and the fire burning in their chests, the firm footsteps of our brave sisters demonstrated (데모하엿다) through a street in the Western Capital (西都 - P'yŏngyang).
Ms. Kang, the leader (리-더-) of the P'yŏngwŏn Rubber Factory strike, deployed a new tactic of agitprop (아지 프로) high atop Ŭlmil Terrace (乙密臺) to demonstrate her will to achieve the goal and to encourage the steadfast hearts of all comrades. She greatly boosted morale for the striking group (퍄업단), and the responding spirit (意氣) of the strikers soared to the heavens (衝天). (Omitted middle part - original text)
However, Ms. Kang, resolving to die, carried out a complete hunger strike (斷食). Truly, 78 hours of fasting! What a moving, living lesson (山敎訓) it gave us! Those scoundrels must have been frightened and astonished by this. Thus, they could not bear not releasing her.
Ms. Kang sufficiently won (익이엇다) with just this.
Woman Staying Aloft (滯空女)!
While treating the journalists' (쩌내리스트) sensational term (奇拔한 述語) and their handling of the labor dispute (勞動爭議) with a focus on sensationalism (興味中心) with displeasure (不快), I lament (弔喪 - used here perhaps as 'lament' or 'criticize') the reformism (改良主義化) of the journalists.
Female fighter of the western front! Our leader! Advance to the end with firm unity (굳센 團結), pillar-like will (못기둥 같은 意地), and brave struggle (勇敢한 鬪爭)!
Are not countless comrades guarding your backs and fighting towards our dawn (光明)? (Omitted end part - original text)

**Summary**

This article is a compilation of short essays and reflections by various Korean intellectuals and writers in July 1931. Topics range from appreciating the brief beauty of a cactus flower (Kim Tong-in), observations on Cheju Island's unique culture and landscape (Sin Tong-gi), reflections on lost Paekche songs and history (Mun Il-p'yŏng), encouragement to a sick friend amidst social hardship (Kim Hae-gang), the importance of punctuality for social progress (Ch'oe Ch'ang-mun), exhortations to youth (Yi Tŏk-hwan, Hyŏn Ki-t'ae), a poignant poem on life's transience (Yi Ŭn-sang), a vignette exposing social prejudice through a real estate broker (Yi T'ae-jun), the harsh realities faced by Koreans in Manchuria (Mo Yun-suk), diary entries reflecting daily life and thoughts on an imprisoned friend (Song Pong-u), and praise for the resolute female labor activist Kang Chu-ryong (Kim Ch'ang-sul). From a historical perspective, the collection offers valuable insights into the diverse concerns of Korean intellectuals during the colonial period: anxieties about cultural loss, economic hardship, social consciousness, the challenges of life for emigrants in Manchuria, nascent labor activism, and personal reflections on life, nature, and societal expectations under Japanese rule. The pieces by Mo Yun-suk on Manchuria and Kim Ch'ang-sul on Kang Chu-ryong are particularly revealing about the difficulties and resistance movements of the time.

7월의 隨想, 百花爛漫
						諸家
仙人掌 꽃
創作家 金東仁

우리 집에 밤송이 형의 선인장이 한 포기 잇다. 몇 해나 묵은 것인지 알 수 없지만 꽤 나히가 드른 듯 하다. 키는 한자가 넘을락 말락하고 직경이 4, 5寸이 되는 것으로서 벌서 4, 5년 전부터 그 「長成」은 중지되고 年年이 수십 송이의 새끼만 친다.
그것이 4년 전부터 꽃을 보이기 시작하엿다. 첫 해에는 한 송이 픠엇다. 둘잿 해에는 두 송이 픠엇다. 작년에도 두 송이가 픠엇다. 금년에는 아직 꽃의 싹도 보이지 안는다. 금년에는 픠기나 할는지.
마치 종처 부스럼이와 같이 가스 틈에 조그만 이상물이 보인다. 그것이 조금식 조금식 커 나서 한달쯤 지나면 한치쯤 큰다. 한달쯤 더 지나면 세치 쯤 큰다. 그러고 거기 허-연 마치 박꽃 같은 꽃이 픤다.
그런데 그 꽃의 픠어잇는 기간은 지극히 짧다. 서너 시간 픨까. 꽃이 픠는 첫 해에는 저녁 때 거의 滿開가 된 꽃을 보고 내일이면 滿開가 되려니 하고 그것을 기다리면서 자고 이튼날 아츰에 보니 꽃은 벌서 저서 힘없이 느려젓다.
이듬해에 나는 그 꽃을 처음으로 보앗다. 深窓에 자란 처녀! 이러케 밖에는 형용할 수가 없는 우아한 꽃이엇섯다. 그러나 서너 시간 뒤에는 벌서 저 버렷다.
작년에는 꽃이 둘이 한꺼번에 나왓다. 그러고 두달 남아를 커 가지고 어떤날 저녁 어두워서 마츰내 픠엇다.
「금년에도 꽃을 완전히 못 보겟다」
그러나 그 꽃을 꺾으려는 아이들을 나는 한사히 말렷다. 우리가 잠잘 동안에 픠엇다가 잠잘 동안에 저 버릴 그 꽃이언만 아이들이 꺽어서 장난하려는 것을 말렷다. 이튼날 아츰에 보니까 꽃은 벌서 시들어 느러젓다.
웨? 우리가 잘 동안에 픠엇다가 잘 ＜43＞ 동안에 저 버릴 꽃을 꺾어서 작란 하려는 아이들을 나는 웨 말렷슬가. 그것은 다른 때문이 아니다. 두달여를 단 3, 4시간의 開花를 위하여 노력한 그 정성이 고마워서.

1년에 한 개 혹은 두 개의 꽃을 보이기 위하여 그것도 서너 시간 뿐의 짧은 시간을 자랑키 위하여 노력하는 정성 그것은 과연 눈물겹다.
나는 그 선인장에서 꽃을 픠이려는 노력의 큼과 그 꽃의 놀랄만한 자랑을 보앗다.
꽃을 보인다 하는 것은 과연 자랑스러운 노릇이다. 10년 이상을 자란 뒤에야 겨우 1년에 한 두 개(혹은 건느는 해도 잇다 한다)의 꽃을―그것도 잠시 다른데 눈을 팔엇다가는 보지도 못할 짧은 시간을 자랑키 위하여 10數 個年을 만날 해볓에 시달리우고 비를 맞으면서 자란 선인장의 고초는 과연 놀랄만 한 것이다. 그러고 그만치 비록 픠는 시간은 짧다 하나 그 꽃의 자랑은 크다.

금년에는 언제나 픠려는지.
버들개지와 같은 그 꽃의 싹은 아직도 보이지 안는다. 혹은 금년에는 건느랴는지.

濟州漢拏山
全州 申東起

朝鮮에서 출생하고 朝鮮에서 자란지 이미 30여 세 되엇으나 朝鮮 땅에 넓리 여행하야 보지 못함이 나의 늘 유감으로 생각하는 바다.
일즉부터 외국으로 다니며 太平洋까지 건너 갓다 왓으나 半島 안에서는 늘 오날 내일 미루느라고 이와 같이 됨이다.
濟州에 가서 그곳 사람다려 漢拏山에 가 보앗느냐 무르면 무슨 특별한 일도 없는데 하고 대답하며 아마도 많은 사람은 일생을 두고 앞산처럼 바라보기는 할지라도 올라가지 못하고 죽는 사람들이 태반일 것이다.
木浦에서 오후 8시경에 朝汽의 海州丸을 타고 11, 2시간이면 濟州城에 도착되나니 漢拏山이 보이기는 멀리 4, 5시간의 항로를 두고 보이기 시작한다. 어둑어둑하고 선들선들한 새벽 바람에 갑판우에 올라서 보니 멀리 漢拏山이 보이기 시작할 때 나에게는 문득 녯날에 배우든 노래 「白頭山서부터 漢拏山까지」라는 唱歌가 회상이 되엇다. 漢拏山의 高는 약 6,700여 尺인바 島의 중앙에 位하고 모든 人家는 그 山麓를 도라가며 散在하야 잇는 모양이다.
나의 금번 여행은 너머나 短時日이엇고 또한 넓리 보지 못한 관계상 漢拏山에 넓리 분포되어잇는 植物誌라든지 기타 채집에 관한 이야기보다도 몇 가지 풍속 습관만 소개하랴고 한다.
濟州島에는 3多가 有하니 風多, 石多, 女多 등이다. 下陸하자마자 제일 먼저 눈에 띄우는 것은 草葺의 구조이니 육지와 달라 뭇척 굴근 새끼로 집웅 엮은 것을 볼 때에는 가히 風多함을 알 것이다. 또한 바람이 많은 관계상 火山灰가 날리어 호흡기에 유리치 못한 것도 사실이다.
人家의 墻壁 田畓의 경계 도로의 상태를 보면 石多함을 可知할지니 熔灰岩의 둥굴둥굴한 둘로 墻壁을 쌓은 것을 보면 담쌓는 기술도 무던이 발달된 모양이다. 田畓의 경계 뿐만 아니라 목축하는 데도 모다가 석축으로 경계를 삼엇다. 그 곳 사람 말을 드르면 육지에서 보다 신이 3배 가량이나 더 해진다고 하니 도로의 험악함은 말할 것도 없다. 해녀들이 감(柿)를 드린 바지저고리를 입고 잠수질하는 것을 해변에서 볼 때에는 꼭 南洋에 간 감이 잇다. 여자들은 어찌나 건강한지 가삼이 쑥쑥 나오고 혈기잇게 다니는 그 여자들은 육지의 남자보다 몇 배의 힘이 잇을 것이다. 시장에는 여자뿐이고 거리에 나무 팔러 오는 사람도 여자이고 심할 것은 牛馬의 力으로 회전시킬 돌 방아까지 ＜44＞ 여자 3, 4인의 힘으로 도는 것을 보앗다. 육지의 여자들이 濟州島 女子들의 하는 활동을 볼 때에는 참 놀랠 것이 많을 것이다.
또한 濟州城內에서는 걸인 하나 볼 수 없다. 남녀가 다 같이 노동할 뿐 아니라 원래 濟州 사람 성질이 開城 사람과 같은 점이 有하야 아모리 어렵게 사는 사람일지라도 남에게 의뢰치 아니하며 다소간일지라도 저축은 다 가지고 잇는 모양이다. 濟州城에서 3, 40리만 나갈지라도 땅 한 평에 5厘 式도 되지 못하는 농경지가 많이 잇는 모양인데 나의 생각에는 우리 朝鮮사람으로 西北間島에 이민으로 가느니 차라리 濟州島가 더 유리하지 아니할까 생각된다.


薯童謠와 井邑詞
歷史家 文一平

落花岩에 봄이 들면 붉은 꽃이 臙脂를 찍고,
迎月臺에 밤이 오면 가느른 달이 눈섭을 그리며,
연 하고 부드러운 산수의 곡선미와 어울리어 이 艶麗한 꽃과 달은 오늘날도 오히려 南國佳人의 따뜻한 정취를 자아내고 잇지마는,
聖王以來 120여년 동안 百濟 서울의 번영은 맑은 꿈 자최로 살어 지고 말엇다.
건축의 美로 三國에 뛰어 나든 예술의 都府라 三興寺 臨流閣 같은 黃金碧玉의 장식이 으리으리한 佛字와 離宮이 잇어서 일은 바 壯麗한 중에도 妙趣를 띄우고 정교한 속에도 幽韻을 붙이며 百濟 사람의 예술적 神技를 긔껏 발휘하엿으되 그만 泗泚王城이 남의 손에 문어질 때 모진 불꽃으로 말미암아 재가 되어버린 것은 千秋의 遺恨이다.
