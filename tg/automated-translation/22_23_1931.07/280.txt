O Dawn, Poem and Sijo
						Mok Il-sin

Dark and lonely night!
No moonlight
Not even a single star <82>
Oh! This pitch-dark night!
Even traces of people have vanished from the streets
On this night when all things are sound asleep
The lost XX alone
Wanders, not knowing the way—
Oh! Dawn!
Come quickly!
Upon this lonely street
For that endlessly wandering XX
To light the way for XX—
Oh! Dawn!
Bringing new light
Come quickly!
Come quickly!<83>
<82-83>

**Summary**

This poem by Mok Il-sin expresses a deep yearning for dawn amidst a dark, starless, and lonely night. An unidentified figure, marked as "XX", wanders lost on deserted streets. The speaker desperately calls for the dawn to arrive quickly, bringing "new light" to illuminate the path for the lost "XX". From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, likely uses the imagery of darkness and the awaited dawn as metaphors for the oppressive colonial reality and the fervent hope for liberation or a brighter future for the Korean people (represented perhaps by the wandering "XX"). The explicit use of "XX", potentially due to censorship or as a deliberate choice to represent an ambiguous 'everyman' or the collective nation, is particularly interesting for historians studying literature and expression under colonial rule.
