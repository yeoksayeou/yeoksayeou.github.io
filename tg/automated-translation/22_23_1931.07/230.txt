Fragments of German Life, A Discourse on Jews, and an Injury Diary
						By To Yu-ho

A Scene from Frankfurt am Main (佛郞) City

After the university went on vacation, I went to the home of a certain high school professor, Mr. Wilken, to receive private German lessons. One day, I happened to be learning Heine's "On the Brocken" (뿌록켄 山上에서), a work by the poet of sorrow. In that text, there was the following passage: ... "[Omitted]... The flock of little lambs picks up bread crumbs. Cute little calves, glistening in the sun, run around near where we sit, ringing their bells. And they greet us, smiling with their large, joyful eyes." ... Professor Wilken, upon reaching this point, couldn't help but scoff, calling it a blatant lie. He said this wasn't the only nonsense in "On the Brocken." Professor Wilken explained that while it says the flock of little lambs eats bread crumbs, sheep simply don't eat bread. For a flock of grass-eating sheep to come near them as they sat eating lunch and eat the bread crumbs they threw was, he said, a blatant lie. And describing the calves as glistening in the sunlight was also nonsense, he claimed, and the description of the calves running about ringing bells was undoubtedly false. Furthermore, he stated that calves' eyes are by no means smiling eyes; they are not joyful eyes but rather the opposite. Following this, he offered his critique of Heine. He grounded Heine's romanticism in places where it's hard to find. Heine wrote the most sentimental poetry. Heine's writings are in German. We had known Heine as a German poet. However, Mr. Wilken did not affirm calling
Heine a German Poet.
He argued that although his writings were in German, his poetic ideas, inasmuch as he did not inherit Germanic (께르만) lineage, were not German. This implies that Heine, being a Jew (猶太人), expressed Jewish sentiments through the German language. He indirectly told me that Germans are not sentimental. I couldn't help but be surprised hearing Mr. Wilken's words. I couldn't ask him then, <74> but would he also claim that Einstein's (아인쓰타인) theory of relativity, having come from the mind of a Jew, is not a product of the Jewish spirit of inquiry?... His stance, taken a step further, seems inclined to base nationality on an anthropological perspective.

Jews who have lived and grown up in Germany for a long time—their native language is German. And among them, there are few with purely Jewish bloodlines. While it is customary for them not to intermarry with Germans, it is said to be a fact that they possess some German lineage. Of course, due to long-standing endogamy within their group, they are not without physiological characteristics. The so-called Jewish nose is unusually pointed and high at the tip. Also, the distance between the tip of the nose and the upper lip is distinctively short. And they have their own unique religion. This Judaism is exclusive to Jews. Furthermore, Jews possess distinctive traits. They are extremely astute regarding financial gain and loss. These are characteristics peculiar to Jews across various countries. Combining these points, it is common in both academic and secular circles to consider Jews a distinct ethnic group.

Early on, the Austrian socialist (social democrat) Bauer (빠우에르) designated the Jews scattered across various countries collectively as a distinct nation. However, in response to Bauer's observation, Kautsky (카우츠키) treated Jews as a social caste (Kaste - 카쓰테). I, along with Kautsky, wonder if considering Jews as a single 'caste' isn't the most appropriate approach. Although we speak of 'Jews, Jews,' there are differences among Jews themselves, and these are determined by their country of residence (住在國). German Jews are different from British Jews. While they inherit certain characteristics from the 'group' known as Jews, they also inherit certain traits from the broader atmosphere of their country of residence. Therefore, British Jews are as different from German Jews as they are British. Of course, treating Jews as a single nation involves discarding differences within the group and focusing on commonalities, targeting the shared traits under this general concept. However, the greatest obstacle to this approach is the lack of a common language. Furthermore, nationally determined interests often conflict with each other.

The most overt
Ethnic Persecution
of Jews occurred in pre-war Russia (露西亞) and its territory, Poland (波蘭). However, the ethnic persecution they suffered was due to their specific behaviors as a social stratum. And when considering Jews collectively as a single nation, another difficulty arises from the difference between the Jews currently in Palestine (팔레스타인) and those dispersed throughout European and American countries (歐米各國). The answer to this, of course, involves citing the difference in cultural heritage transmission between these two groups. However, this ultimately necessitates treating the two groups as different peoples. Yet, European attitudes seem disinclined to distinguish between the two. They distinguish Americans of British descent from British people ethnically, and Americans of German descent are treated as foreigners even within Germany, but Jews alone are all considered the same Jews. In the same way, they do not distinguish between Hungarian Gypsies (집시) and Spanish (西班牙) Gypsies but lump them all together as 'Gypsies.'

Bauer, citing cultural heritage transmission, recognized the nation as a community bound by fate (Schicksalsgemeinschaft) and treated the Jews collectively as one nation. However, treating Jews as a single nation ultimately goes beyond the concept of cultural heritage transmission and resorts to the issue of bloodline. Addressing the national question through bloodline inevitably requires seeking solutions within the fields of ethnology or anthropology.

Speaking of Jews, the place one might call the heartland of Jews in Germany is right here in Frankfurt (佛郞府). <75> Even the British Rothschild (로쓰촤일드) family traces its ancestry to Frankfurt. In Frankfurt, the influence of the 'Rotschild' (로췰뜨 - German pronunciation of Rothschild) family is immense. If you go out onto the main street of Bockenheim (뽁켄하임), grand white mansions line both sides; most of them are owned by Jews. And the Palm Garden (棕櫚公園) on this main street is also under Jewish management. The large shops in the city center are mostly run by Jews. The beauty of the Palm Garden is indeed said to be one of Frankfurt's prides. However, except on Sundays or holidays, most visitors are said to be Jewish. The crowds of men, women, old, and young sitting around the outdoor bandstand or in the café garden within the park are said to be mostly Jewish. Who but wealthy Jews would have the leisure to frequent this place during normal times? And the Jews that Germans think of are, at the very least, wealthy Jews, Jews who live exceptionally well compared to others. Thus, while Jews appear to Germans as a specific segment of the bourgeois class, Germans typically view this through the colored lens of ethnic prejudice.

Though the topic shifts abruptly, while distinguishing Jews from the general German population, a mutual antagonism arises. Antagonism breeds conflict. This antagonism and conflict indeed constitute the substance of history. Setting aside the Jewish Question, I would like to touch briefly upon human history centered on conflict. Karl Marx (칼 맑스) once said that the history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles. I would like to go a step further and categorize human history into three forms of struggle. First is the struggle against natural forces (對自然力的 鬪爭) through the construction of human culture. Second is the inter-group struggle (與族鬪爭) between different collective entities. Third is the class struggle within the same society.

Regarding the cultural struggle against natural forces and
Class struggle within a society,
I will omit my thoughts here and touch somewhat upon inter-group struggle (與族鬪爭). Under the concept of inter-group struggle, I have included both national struggles and tribal struggles. Inter-group struggle began first as conflict between tribes and later developed into conflict between nations. Generally, it is said that living beings possess gregariousness. Especially looking at humanity, humans have possessed gregariousness from the very beginning. This first manifests in certain primitive collective bodies that necessarily arise for the ease of survival under the principle of the struggle for existence. Once a collective body is formed, its members develop an attachment to it. This attachment simultaneously entails exclusivity. This is primarily for the safety of this collective body. But at the same time, members raised within this collective body become extremely unfamiliar with other collective bodies. Unfamiliarity, in turn, breeds fear towards them. Thus, distance and opposition arise between different types of collective entities. From there, struggles begin for the safety and prosperity of one's own affiliated collective body. In the most primitive times, this struggle flourished between tribes. The scope of the collective body then expanded from tribe to nation. However, this did not always happen according to fixed rules or necessity. Often, a nation would fragment back into tribes. This was particularly true of the feudal system of the Middle Ages. Moreover, the national question gained its most significant meaning after the collapse of feudalism, and there are certainly reasons for this. Within these collective bodies, social strata would eventually emerge. Consequently, the benefits derived from inter-group struggle (與族鬪爭) would be most abundantly bestowed upon the leading stratum within that social hierarchy. This leading stratum, in turn, seeks victory in ethnic/group struggles for the benefit of its own stratum. It thus appeals to the attachment each member feels towards the collective body. While this happens, the various social strata within that collective body continue their struggles based on conflicting interests. <76>

The history of Rome (羅馬) was precisely a history of this external group struggle (對外族的 鬪爭) and the internal struggle between social strata within Rome (the class struggle between patricians and plebeians). And while a nation originates from an ethnic group (種族), an ethnic group differs in meaning from a nation. An ethnic group often differentiates into several distinct nations. A nation, in its developmental process, typically unifies or splits into two or three entities, forming the institution called the state. It is then understood under the concept of 'citizenry' or 'nationals' (國民). However, citizenry (國民) and nation/ethnic group (民族) are not identical. Often, after the formation of a state, one nation fights another, has its statehood destroyed, becomes subordinate to the state established by the other nation, and is treated as citizens of that state. Thus, a nation might diverge into several citizenries, or several nations might be included within one citizenry. The English word 'Nation,' therefore, is applied when referring to an ethnic group in relation to the political institution of the state. When referring to an ethnic group excluding the political institution, the word 'Nationality' is used. Although there are slight differences in meaning, it is common in German as well to distinguish between Nation and Nationalitaet. That both the English 'Nationality' and the German 'Nationalitaet' originate from the word 'Nation' shows how closely related the formation of nations and states are, and how frequently nation and citizenry coincide. Furthermore, when a nation emerges as a collective entity, it typically possesses a common language.

Karl Kautsky once attempted to define nations based on differences in language (national language). He explained cases where different nations were linked by the same language by stating that several nations could be included within the same national language. This explanation, however, is not a clear scientific answer. Nevertheless, the relationship between this national collective and language is extremely close.

A nation engages in specific linguistic and cultural activities within this collective. In doing so, they establish a common language within themselves. This nation, however, has by no means held the same meaning throughout history. Our understanding of the concept of nation holds its most practical and crucial significance here. The modern concept of nation is markedly different from the concept of nation in Roman times. As mentioned above, the benefits of victory in national struggles are always most abundantly bestowed upon the leading social stratum within that nation—in other words, the ruling class. Thus, this ruling class, when pursuing
the interests of its own class,
appeals to the national affection of the entire nation. The result often culminates in the sacrifice of some other nation.

However, the transition of this ruling class changes the very concept of the nation itself.
Thus, the concept of the nation is redetermined by the conditions that determined the emergence of this ruling class. Therefore, attempting to explain the modern nation using simply the notion of 'nation' can lead to considerable misjudgment. Consequently, trying to understand the modern nation—which emerged based on the imperialist aggression that marks the final stage of capitalism—through the lens of the Roman-era concept of nation or the concept of nation during the Imjin War is a significant error. Yet, in reality, many people do precisely this. Above, I have touched upon national antagonism and other related matters. Nations have been in opposition until now. However, this by no means predicts future opposition. We have seen in the past how tribal opposition disappeared under national opposition. This might simply be considered a hint towards the future disappearance of national opposition, without much exaggeration. General national consciousness is extremely difficult to avoid. We witnessed this most clearly at the outbreak of the European War. The masses are always in a state of emergent psychology. This psychological state often ends in illogical phenomena. <77> However, through popular education, this can be brought under the sway of a sound ideology.

Above, I have discussed nations without particular reason. And I even touched upon the disappearance of national antagonism. However, the collective entity of humanity called the nation is distinguished mutually by the lack of linguistic unity and differences in cultural levels. This distinction is the greatest obstacle to the disappearance of national antagonism. The nation was originally a unit of cultural activity. A specific nation has always constructed a specific culture. The degree and form of this cultural construction were determined by the innate physiological abilities of that nation, the geographical conditions of its inhabited zone (including climate), and its relationships with other external groups, among other factors. However, these specific parts have mutually interacted throughout the flow of history. This specificity has become most conspicuously interactive in modern times. Modern civilization, viewed one way, involves interconnecting the specific cultures of these specific nations to encompass all of humanity. Having rambled on nonsensically, I shall now change the subject.

It was the morning of August 2nd. As it happened, the landlady had taken the children to the countryside, Mr. Ku (具先生) had gone to Paris (巴里), and Mr. Romel (로멜氏) was also set to go to where his wife had gone that day. That day was a Saturday. Normally, I visited Mr. Wilken's house five times a week and took Saturdays and Sundays off, but due to Mr. Wilken's circumstances, I had to attend class at his house today despite it being Saturday. That day too, I went by bicycle as usual. Just before leaving, I received a letter from Kim Chin-dong (金鎭東君) from Tianjin (天津). The letter contained quite a bit of interesting news of various kinds. It was on my way back from Mr. Wilken's house that very day. While clumsily maneuvering my bicycle, I ended up
getting hit by a car.
In a dazed state, I looked down and saw my right arm was broken. The bicycle was mangled here and there. Soon, a Red Cross ambulance arrived and took me away. It was the first time since I came into the world that I had broken an arm, and also the first time I had been anesthetized. The place I was taken to on a stretcher was the orthopedic surgery ward. Initially, I was put in a large ward, then later moved to a smaller room. Most of the people lying in this orthopedic ward were like me, with broken arms or legs. Later, talking amongst ourselves, we found out that the cause of the fractures was mostly traffic accidents. These days, nervous exhaustion is commonly called a disease of civilization; looking around, these illnesses caused by traffic accidents are unquestionably diseases of civilization. Just as the degree of civilization is measured by soap consumption, or sugar consumption is directly proportional to the degree of civilization, the degree of civilization is also measured by the development of transportation. The development of transportation entails an increase in the means of transport and the density of the transport network. This is for the civilized enjoyment of humanity. But on the flip side, there are victims like us lying here, sacrificed to this development of transportation. And producing victims is, in fact, unavoidable. However, one absolutely cannot curse civilization focusing solely on these victims.

In human society, the phenomenon called 'revolution' (레블류치온) occurs. Most of these are unavoidable for the development of society. However, this 'revolution' entails many sacrifices among members of society. Cursing 'revolution' with these sacrifices in mind is no different from cursing civilization because of traffic accidents. Lying in my sickbed, I had the most brazen thoughts. I had become a victim of civilization for a while. I had tasted civilization most profoundly—thus I secretly consoled myself with these poor thoughts. More than roasted chestnuts eaten secretly under the covers away from the mother-in-law's eyes, this self-consolation of mine was silent yet savory. This savory self-consolation is most effective when facing distressing situations. If one chews and swallows this silently and well, <78> even an ordinary person (凡人) suddenly becomes a philosopher. There are many ways an ordinary person becomes a philosopher, but among the easiest ways, I would first mention this philosophy of self-consolation.

An ordinary person sometimes strangely and eccentrically becomes a philosopher. For instance, the philosophy of nihilism upon waking from a nap unwillingly places the self on the philosopher's chair. However, if one falls for the trick (야마시) of this philosophy, one might get slapped silly (鬼拍) before even splashing one's face with cold water.

One thing I was impressed by after coming to Germany was the kindness of the German people. Even after I entered the hospital room, they were remarkably kind. The next day was Sunday. In the afternoon, Mr. Rang (랑氏), a car driver living in a nearby apartment annex, and his wife, along with Mrs. Schimpf? (쉼프 부인), whom I had recently befriended, came bearing bananas, peaches, and other things I used like my toothbrush, soap, and face towel, piled high in their arms. When I asked how they knew I was here, they said a policeman came yesterday looking for Mr. Romel (로엘씨 - likely Romel), but since no one was home, they asked the reason, and my story came out. The person lying to my left was a
worker from a small knife factory.
This fellow, having picked it up somewhere, spewed out an endless stream of English swear words. When asked their meaning, he said he knew some but not others. To my right lay a young fellow hit by a motorcycle, his legs and arms shattered. Occasionally, his mother and his lover came to visit. His lover, a young lady, would sometimes steal a kiss when others weren't looking. When leaving, her eyes would well up with tears.

When the person on my right moved to the opposite row, an American youth came into that spot. This young man was Peter? (뻬드君), whom I now knew well from the Mathematics and Physics department. He was an American of German descent and said they used German at home too. However, everyone saw Peter simply as an American. Peter's ailment was a toe problem resulting from playing 'ballon?' (빨네뽈 - likely a ball game) mischief. Lying there, we exchanged funny stories. My German being insufficient, I mumbled in English. Then Peter would translate. But there were also many times when I couldn't understand a thing others were saying. They say that funny stories are often more amusing in dialect than standard language. These fellows would be chatting normally, then suddenly burst into raucous laughter, while I alone missed the point. Even Peter couldn't grasp the nuances of the dialects. One awkward situation I encountered here was when they vented their complaints about Japan to me. Even when I said I wasn't Japanese but Korean, these friends wouldn't listen. They asked why Japan fought against Germany during the European War. I tried my best, whether effectively or not, to explain things I'd picked up from books, struggling with my poor German. But it seemed my words didn't register.

Two or three days after being admitted, I sometimes followed others for walks in the hospital courtyard. Sometimes, we would reach the Main (마인) River flowing nearby. I think the pointed rooftops of the city look most beautiful when viewed from the riverbank. The most endearing sight for me was the spire of the church across the river. Also, during this hospital stay, I received the most upsetting news. I finally received a letter I had long awaited from my father. It contained news of the deaths of my young uncle Chin-baek (鎭百) and my stepmother. Chin-baek was a boy several times cleverer than myself. Chin-baek was also a boy who was quite good at immense mischief. He was a spirited boy. Chin-baek had already passed away. During my hospitalization, I also received a censored postcard from my elder brother. <79>

**Summary**

The author, To Yu-ho, recounts his experiences as a Korean student in Frankfurt, Germany in 1931. He details a discussion with his German tutor about the poet Heine, which leads to broader reflections on Jewish identity, German nationalism, and the nature of ethnicity versus cultural affiliation, referencing thinkers like Bauer and Kautsky. He observes the prominent role of Jews in Frankfurt's society and the underlying ethnic tensions. The essay then shifts to a personal account of being injured in a traffic accident, his subsequent hospitalization, interactions with fellow patients (including German workers and an American student), and reflections on civilization's costs, personal resilience, and receiving sad news from home about family deaths. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is significant as it shows a Korean intellectual directly engaging with contemporary European discourse on complex issues like nationalism, ethnicity, assimilation, and identity (specifically Jewish identity, which had parallels to colonial situations), while also navigating his own identity ("I am Korean, not Japanese") in a European context. His reflections on struggle (class, inter-group, against nature) and the constructed nature of national identity offer a window into the thoughts of a colonized subject observing European society and its own internal conflicts and prejudices.

猶太人 猶太人하나 獨逸에서 猶太人의 本所라고 할만한 곧은 바로 이 佛郞府입니다. ＜75＞ 얼즉이 英國의 『로쓰촤일드』家도 佛郞府에 그 선조를 둡니다. 佛郞府에서도 『로췰뜨』 (로쓰촤일드의 獨語發音)家의 세력은 대단합니다. 『뽁켄하임』큰 길에 나서면 白堊大舘들이 좌우에 쭉 늘어 잇으니 그들의 대개는 猶太人의 소유입니다. 그리고 이 큰 거리 한가에 잇는 棕櫚公園도 猶太人系의 경영입니다. 그리고 市街의 큰 상점들은 대개가 猶太人의 경영입니다. 棕櫚公園의 아름다움은 실로 佛郞府의 한 가지 자랑이라고 합니다. 그러나 일요일이나 명절 이외에 이리로 드나드는 손의 대개는 猶太人이라고 합니다. 공원 내 옥외 奏樂臺 앞에나 『카페』뜰악에 늘어앉은 남녀노소의 무리는 대개가 猶太人이라고 합니다. 돈 잇는 猶太人이 아니고야 무슨 팔자로 통상 때에 여기에 드나들 수가 잇겟습니까. 그리고 獨逸 사람들이 생각하는 猶太人은 적어도 돈 많은 猶太人, 남보다 별달리도 잘 사는 猶太人입니다. 그러케 猶太人은 뿌르조아 계급의 어느 특수부분으로 獨逸人에게 대하오나 獨逸人은 이것을 민족편견의 색안경으로 보는 것이 통상입니다.
이야기는 새삼스럽게도 변하여 가오나, 猶太人 猶太人하고 일반 獨逸人과 구별하는 반면에 여기에는 서로의 대립이 성립됩니다. 대립은 투쟁을 초래합니다. 이 대립과 투쟁은 실로 역사의 내용을 조성하는 바입니다. 猶太人問題는 따로 치고 투쟁을 중심으로 한 인류의 역사에 조곰 언급하여 볼까 합니다. 『칼 맑스』는 일즉이 有史來 모든 사회의 역사는 계급투쟁의 역사라고 하엿습니다. 나는 좀 나가서 인류의 역사를 3개 樣態의 투쟁으로 구별하여 볼까 합니다. 하나는 인류의 문화건설에 의한 對自然力的 鬪爭입니다. 둘재는 異樣集合体間의 與族鬪爭입니다. 셋재는 동일한 社會內의 계급투쟁입니다.
對自然力의 文化鬪爭과
一社會內의 계급투쟁에 대한 소견은 여기에 略하고 與族鬪爭에 다소 언급하여 보겟습니다. 나는 與族鬪爭이란 개념 아래에 민족투쟁과 부족투쟁을 포함시켯습니다. 與族鬪爭은 처음 부족간의 투쟁으로 시작하야 다시 민족간의 투쟁으로 전개케 된 것입니다. 大体 생물은 일반이 집합성(Gregariousness)을 가젓다고 합니다. 더구나 인류에 이르러 보오면 인류는 태조부터 집합성을 가젓든 것입니다. 그것은 먼저 생존경쟁의 원칙 하에 생존조선의 安易를 위하야 필연적으로 생기는 어느 원시적 집합체에 먼저 體現됩니다. 어느 집합체가 성립되면 따라 그 집합체의 부원은 여기에 애착심을 가지게 되는 것입니다. 이 애착심은 동시에 배타성을 동반하게 되는 것입니다. 이것은 첫재로 이 집합체의 안전을 위하야 그럿습니다. 그러나 동시에 이 집합체 내에서 자란 부원들은 다른 어느 집합체에 대하야는 가장 낯이 설게 됩니다. 낯이 설다는 것은 반면에 거기에 대한 공포심을 초래하게 되는 것입니다. 이러케 異種集合体 間에는 相距가 생기고 대립이 생기게 됩니다. 거기서 自屬集合体의 안전 내지는 隆盛을 위하야 서로이 투쟁은 시작됩니다. 이 투쟁은 가장 원시시대에 잇어서는 부족부족 간에 성행하엿든 것입니다. 집합체의 범위는 부족으로부터 다시 민족으로 전개되엇든 것입니다. 그러나 이것은 늘 일정한 규율 하에서 필연히 그러케 되는 것은 아니엿습니다. 어느 민족은 민족적 집합체로부터 다시 부족으로 분리될 때가 많습니다. 더구나 중세기의 봉건제는 그리하엿든 것입니다. 그리고 민족문제가 가장 그 의미를 가지게 된 것은 봉건제 붕괴 이후의 것으로 거기에는 물론 이유가 잇습니다. 그리고 이 집합체 내에는 이럭저럭하는 동않�에 사회 층이 생기게 될 것입니다. 그리하야 與族鬪爭에 의한 이득은 그 사회층 중의 首位에 잇는 층에 가장 풍부히 부여케 됩니다. 이 首位層은 다시 자기 층의 이익을 위하야 族的鬪爭의 승리를 도모케 됩니다. 그리하야 그것을 각부원의 그 집합체에 대한 애착심에 호소합니다. 이러케 되는 일방 그 집합체 내의 각사회층은 상호의 이익상반에 의한 투쟁을 계속합니다. ＜76＞
