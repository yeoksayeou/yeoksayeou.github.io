Field of Curses, Poem and Sijo
						Yi Ŭng-su

In days gone by, I knew going out to my field as boundless joy.
The love of wildflowers offering a gentle smile!
The invitation of the reeds, beckoning with waving hands!
The sweet embrace of the endless blue sky!
And also, the profound and awesome Benevolence (慈愛) of the earth!
The natural Beauty (美) and purification of life drawn (汲) from all these things
Gifted me with rapture, solace, and fulfillment, so
In days gone by, I knew going out to my field as boundless joy.

But oh—why is going out to that field today so agonizing?
In my eyes, standing blankly on the weed-overgrown path,
All the Splendor (榮華) I saw in former days has vanished entirely,
And seeing only the sound of sighs flowing from the farmer's mouth,
The weariness of the countryside and the desolate ruins of humanity,
I behold the pale face of a world finally breathing its last.
Today, going out to my field is boundless suffering.
Today, going out to my field is boundless suffering.<83>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Ŭng-su starkly contrasts the speaker's past perception of the countryside as a source of joy, beauty, and solace with the present reality of suffering and decay. Once filled with the gentle beauty of nature, the field now reflects only the farmer's sighs, weariness, and the "desolate ruins of humanity," symbolizing a dying world. From a historical perspective, the poem offers a poignant glimpse into the despair felt in rural Korea during the Japanese colonial period. The imagery of overgrown paths, sighs, fatigue, and ruins likely reflects the economic hardship, exploitation, and sense of loss experienced by Korean farmers under colonial rule, making it a valuable piece for understanding the emotional landscape of the time.
