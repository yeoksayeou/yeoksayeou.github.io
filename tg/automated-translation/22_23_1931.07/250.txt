기사제목	普信閣鐘, 詩와 時調 (Bosingak Bell, Poem and Sijo)
필자	趙宗玄
기사형태	시 (Poem)

1
Chang'an is quiet, the late spring night has deepened.
Now is the hour past midnight, it must be a traveler's dream.
Perhaps the first cock will soon crow; I strain my ears to listen.

2
In days of old, around this time, the bell of Posin'gak would ring,
Awakening the sleeping crowds in ten thousand homes, they say, but
Who will light the path for those who must depart tomorrow?

3
If only this bell would ring, ring out loudly, high into the heavens,
And resound throughout the Samch'ŏlli, how refreshed my heart would be!
How sorrowful that it has kept its mouth shut for so, so many years.<81>

**Summary**

This article presents three *sijo* (a traditional Korean poetic form) by Cho Chonghyŏn contemplating the Bosingak Bell in Seoul (Chang'an). The poems contrast the bell's historical role of announcing time and awakening the city with its current silence. The poet expresses deep sorrow and longing for the bell to ring out again, powerfully and across the entire Korean peninsula (Samch'ŏlli), symbolizing a wish for national reawakening and relief from the present suppressed state. Historians might find the poems interesting as they reflect the muted national consciousness and yearning for liberation during the Japanese colonial period, using the historical symbol of the Bosingak Bell to express frustration over the silenced state of the nation and the desire for a return to normalcy and national vitality.
