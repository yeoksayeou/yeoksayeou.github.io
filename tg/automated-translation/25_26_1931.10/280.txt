Play: Let's Go [Kabo Year]
						By Cho Yong-man

Time: Night of March 19th, Kabo Year (1894) – Early Donghak Rebellion period
Place: A farming village near Namwŏn county town, Chŏlla Province
Characters:
Sundol
Kim Ch’ŏmji – Sundol’s Father, old, sick
Suni – Sundol’s younger sister
Pongmyŏng – Sundol’s close friend, Suni’s fiancé
Sundol’s Wife, Young Child, Infant
Village Elder A, B, C
Several Village Children (chorus offstage only)
Others
Stage:
A dilapidated farming house. On the left, entering through a sorghum stalk door, are the kitchen and rooms lined up side by side, with a narrow wooden porch in front of the rooms. On the right are earthenware jars. Behind, a sorghum stalk fence encircles the area, and beyond it, a dark thicket. The sky is pitch black without a single star.
The door to the upper room opens, and a flickering light shines out. Inside the room, only tattered bedding is visible. The lower room has its torn door closed, and the kitchen is also dark.
At the edge of the porch by the room door, Village Elders A, B, and C converse.

Elder A: (Tapping his pipe against the porch) Why is the weather so wretched like this? It’s almost April, yet it’s still this cold and gloomy—
B: Something’s wrong. The way things are going, it looks like another famine year. That… oh (Sighs).
C: Dammit, famine or good harvest, who knows the difference anymore?
A: Still, if it’s a famine like last year, even if we starve, our stomachs are at peace. It’s better than a good harvest where we don’t get a single grain of rice ourselves and just have it taken away – at least our stomachs don’t resent it as much—
C: Hmph! Stomachs at peace! You think they’ll leave us alone just because there's a famine? Recently, saying he was preparing for the magistrate’s minor concubine’s birthday, they demanded chickens and pigs, and when Pongdong’s father from the lower village couldn’t provide, they dragged him off and flogged him, they say. Truly, they scrape us clean!
A: Eugh, doing such terrible things to others, forcing them, taking things—how can it go down their throats? (Clicks his tongue).
-Pause-
B: When you think about it, there are no fools like us. The young kids these days, they won’t stand for it—something’s bound to happen.
C: Right, like two years ago when Pak Sŏndal was caught by Ch’oe Chinsa, and also—ah, last year when Kim Ch’ŏmji here was taken, didn’t Sundol and the other young lads make quite a ruckus, saying they’d tear down that bastard’s house? <87>
B: True, back then we needlessly got scared and stopped them. Now, there’s no one who’d listen even if we tried to stop them, and no one who’d try to stop them anyway—
A: Are we any less angry than them? It’s just that we think too much.
C: (As if chiding) What thoughts? We’re all just useless, you and everyone else.
B: Now, only anger rises. Going on like this, it doesn’t seem like we’ll ever see a peaceful world—
C: We just need to bring back the Taewŏn'gun (大院王). It’s all because of those damned Min clan (閔氏) people. Dammit! (At this moment, the song of children is heard from afar)
Lower village bird,
Upper village bird,
Chŏnju Kobu (全州古阜) mung bean bird,
Don’t sit in the green bean patch,
The mung bean vines will all rot.
-Pause-
A: (Lowering his voice) Right, yesterday Kaettong’s father went to the county town and came back saying everyone in town is in an uproar, saying there’ll be trouble this year. Something about the Kyoch’ŏn River (膠川江) turning blood-red for three days and all the fish dying. They say such omens appear when trouble is about to break out. It was like that during the Imjin War, and the Pyŏngja Invasion too, people were saying, absolutely buzzing about it.
C: Exactly, as I was saying earlier, it feels like something is going to happen. Even today, why did a double rainbow appear when the sun was shining brightly? It’s all a sign of ruin.
B: (Heavily) Perhaps the trouble will start here. (Lowers his voice) These days, my eldest son goes out somewhere as soon as he finishes dinner and only comes back at dawn. So, wondering what this rascal was up to, yesterday I had the younger one follow him. He said that in the thicket on the mountain ahead, all the young men of the village were gathered, whispering with some modern chap (개화꾼) with cropped hair.
A: (Hastily) Hey, who’s the modern chap with cropped hair? We don’t have any blacksmiths (천작쟁이 - *possibly meaning 'lowly occupation' or specifically 'blacksmith', context implies 'outsider/rebel'*) in our village.
B: How should I know who it is? So this morning, I called the elder son and asked him, but he played completely dumb. Thinking we’d just try to stop them again. So, finally, I told him. I’ve mostly figured it out, but this time, whatever you kids do, we won’t interfere—
(At this moment, a groaning sound "Keung" like stretching comes from inside the room, and the bedding moves)
A: Kim Sŏndal (*Note: uses Sŏndal instead of Ch’ŏmji here*), are you awake?
(No sound from the room)
C: (Looking into the room) How are you feeling? (Still no sound) Fallen asleep again!
A: (Continuing the conversation, looking at B) So, what are they doing gathered together? Are they planning to attack the magistrate? Are they going to tear down Ch’oe Chinsa’s house?
B: Hmph, who knows—
(Sundol and Pongmyŏng enter. Sundol has a haggard but determined face. Pongmyŏng is a robust young man)
Sundol: (Bows) Uncles, you’ve come.
Pongmyŏng: (Same)
Elders: Aye. (Together) (And watch the two young men’s movements)
Sundol: (Comes to the front of the upper room)
A: (Looking at the upper room) - Father is sleeping.
Sundol: (Gazes blankly into the room)
B: (Clicking his tongue, turning to Sundol)
Truly, your father’s illness is serious. Him sleeping like that isn’t really sleeping. He’s just completely exhausted, unable to gather his senses—
Sundol: (Gloomily) Is that so. As you uncles know, we can’t even eat properly, how could we afford medicine? (Sighs) <88>
A: Oh dear, we can eat grass roots, but we can’t feed a sick person that.
B: It’s all the sin of being born commoners.
(Raising his voice) They say Ch’oe Chinsa’s household held a shaman ritual (굿) last night because his newborn baby is sick.
Sundol: (As if sneering) Humph!
C: That Ch’oe Chinsa is the one who deserves to die. Why did he have to make an innocent man (Nods head towards the upper room) end up in this state?
Sundol: (Trembles wordlessly)
Pongmyŏng: (With a determined look, facing the Elders) Now, the day will come when he receives his punishment.
C: (Meaningfully) Now, whatever you young men do, we won’t interfere—
(The elders watch the young men’s movements, anticipating what they might say. The two young men hesitate slightly, looking at each other. Pause. Just as Pongmyŏng is about to speak, Elder A suddenly stands up as if remembering something)
A: Oops! Ttolttol’s father will be waiting at home—I’ll be going first.
B, C: (Standing up) Then we’ll go too.
(Elders A, B, C exit. Seeing the elders off, the two young men sit down on the edge of the porch.)
Sundol: They’ve caught on.
Pongmyŏng: Seems like it.
Sundol: Even if they know, so what. Now, it’s not like we’ll stop just because the uncles tell us to, hmph.
Pongmyŏng: Why would they stop us? Didn’t they just say they’d leave us alone?
Sundol: They must be reaching their breaking point too.
Pongmyŏng: (To himself) Damn it, anyway, by this time tomorrow, whether we die or they die, it’ll be decided. Today is the feast at the magistrate’s house, so many of those bastards will be gathered. First, I need to crush our magistrate’s balls… ha ha ha.
-Pause-
Sundol: (Solemnly) By the way, those people who came earlier, they were from General Noktu (綠荳將軍 - Jeon Bong-jun), right?
Pongmyŏng: Yeah, right, you were out on an errand earlier and didn’t see them. They brought a letter—saying things are going well there, and telling us to quickly bring the head of the Namwŏn Magistrate (南原府使) and come to Kobu (古阜)—hmph.
Sundol: And what was all that stuff loaded on their shoulders? Looked quite heavy.
Pongmyŏng: That’s all stuff we’ll need at dawn. Guns and gunpowder—
Sundol: (Nodding) But I hope it doesn’t fail like when we practiced. Those are all rusty guns, only good for shooting small birds, they say.
Pongmyŏng: Well, I don’t know. Earlier, General Kaenam (介男將軍 - Kim Gae-nam) fired one, and the sound was quite loud.
(At this moment, another groan comes from the upper room)
Sundol: (Stands up and looks into the room again)
Sundol: Father, are you awake?
Ch’ŏmji: (No sound.)
Sundol: (Looking outside) Oh, what’s taking her so long? Darn it.
Pongmyŏng: (Looking around) Right, where did Auntie go?
Sundol: She went to the village ahead to do day labor. What’s taking so long? (Frowning face)
Pongmyŏng: Didn’t you give him dinner?
Sundol: Of course not. Just a little porridge this morning, and he’s been like that ever since. Now he can hardly even eat porridge—
(Sits down again, lowers his head, and clicks his tongue bitterly. -Pause- Heavy silence between the two men)
Pongmyŏng: (As if finding it difficult to speak) Hey, no matter how I think about it, how about you just quit?
Sundol: (Snapping his head up) Quit what?
Pongmyŏng: (Coolly) This whole thing. <89>
Sundol: (Surprised, eyes wide) Why? Man, what’s wrong with you?
Pongmyŏng: It’s not that I don’t know how you feel. But considering various things, it seems best for you to quit.
Sundol: (Face contorted, remains silent)
Pongmyŏng: The old man is sick like that, not knowing when he might pass away, and seems to be starving. If you go out, how will Auntie manage everything alone afterwards? As for me, I have no parents, wife, or children, so I have nothing holding me back.
Sundol: If I stay, will Father not pass away? And am I even able to feed us right now? No, more than that, who drove us into this situation? Why do we have to have our money taken, our land taken by those yangban bastards, and then have our lives threatened too? If things go on like this, we’ll all starve to death. If we’re going to die, we should at least avenge ourselves so those who come after us can live.
Pongmyŏng: Does the vengeance have to be taken by you specifically? We’ll avenge everything, so you should wait for the next opportunity.
Sundol: The next opportunity? You mean after we’ve all starved to death? That’s nonsense. Last year, when Father was caught by Ch’oe Chinsa and beaten, in that moment of anger, I wanted to just grab a knife, go in, and stab that bastard, but Mother was terribly ill with worry, and also because of the uncles’ concerns, I stopped— Thinking back to that time… (Sighs)
Sundol: And what happened? In the end, Mother passed away, and Father came out sick like that, didn’t he? (Becoming agitated) Who caused Mother to pass away, and who made Father like this? Truly, I’ve been gritting my teeth, holding it in until now—
Pongmyŏng: (Coolly) It’s not just you suffering this, nor are you the only one who should be angry.
Sundol: So you’re saying I shouldn’t be the only one putting in effort? You say that because you haven’t experienced the bursting indignation. Truly, when Mother was dying, she couldn’t close her eyes, asking me to avenge her, avenge her, shedding tears… when I think of that— (Chokes up)
Pongmyŏng: No, I’m not telling you to quit. It’s just that if you go out now, you’re going to die—Father might pass away even tomorrow, and if he does, what will you do about things afterward?
Sundol: Afterward! Who knows about afterward? The children will probably starve to death. Is there anything special I can do even if I stay? Right now, if it weren’t for Father, I’d have nothing holding me back.
Pongmyŏng: Hmph, crazy talk! Do you really think the world will turn upside down in just one go? Of course, we must believe we’ll succeed—but that’s not how the world works. Does anything ever happen in a single breath? It happens through repeated struggle, struggle, struggle, whether there’s failure or bloodshed. I’m not doing this believing we’ll definitely succeed this time—I’m just offering my small amount of blood for the sake of future success.
Pongmyŏng: (Seeing Sundol remain silent, continues) What I mean is—since your situation is like this now, think more deeply and do something bigger, slowly, later on. Isn't leaving behind a parent on the brink of death the first thing to consider before doing something like this?
Sundol: (Beating his chest, agitated) But what do I do about this indignation threatening to burst my chest? Of course, I’m human too. I worry about my parents, my wife and children too. But am I the only one in this world with parents, wife, and children? If Father knew, he would surely tell me to go. He talks in his sleep about wanting revenge on the yangban bastards. What’s the use of just clinging to a parent who can’t be saved? And doing it slowly in the future—what exactly is the future? That’s all nonsense.
(At this time, right outside the fence, children pass by singing again)
Let's go in Kabo year (甲午歲) <90>
Let's go
Dawdling in Ŭlmi year (乙未)
Dawdling
If it becomes Pyŏngsin year (丙申)
We can’t go.
Sundol: (To himself) Let’s go, damn it, let’s go quickly before we become crippled (병신 - pun on Pyŏngsin year and 'cripple')—
...Pause...
(Sundol’s Wife enters, carrying the infant on her back and leading the young child. All three look pale from malnutrition. Behind her, Suni follows, carrying an earthenware pot (뚝백이) on her head. Ripe cherry-like fullness, almost lacking a maidenly quality.)
Child: (Running towards Sundol) Father—
Suni: (Sees Pongmyŏng, hesitates, then greets him with her eyes and enters the kitchen carrying the pot. A light turns on in the kitchen.)
Sundol: (To his weary wife, in a complaining tone) What took you so long?
Wife: (The baby on her back is asleep, head lolling. Sees Pongmyŏng and greets him) Brother-in-law (*term used for husband's younger friend*), you’re here—.
(Then, turning to Sundol) Aigo, using Father's illness as an excuse to come home earlier than others. (Goes towards the upper room) Is he sleeping? (Peeks inside)
(Then enters the kitchen)
...Pause...
Sundol: (As if remembering) Oh right, what about you?
Pongmyŏng: (Confused, not understanding)
Sundol: (Nodding towards the kitchen with his chin) I mean her. Now that things have turned out like this, what’s the point of marriage talks? (Troubled face)
Pongmyŏng: (Smiling faintly) Oh, that’s already decided. (Looks at Sundol with a meaningful expression)
Sundol: Decided? How?
Pongmyŏng: Heh heh, ask the person involved.
Sundol: So she already knows?
Pongmyŏng: I met her the dawn before yesterday and talked. She kept insisting on going together, whether we live or die, but I barely managed to persuade her to stay behind. And (Hesitates slightly, looking embarrassed) it’s embarrassing to say, but she’s not alone anymore—
Sundol: (Startled) What? Not alone?
Pongmyŏng: (Lowering his head) Yes. It’s shameful. It’s all my fault.
Sundol: No. What’s shameful about it? Oh (Nods his head) So that’s how it is.
Pongmyŏng: I only found out recently too...
Sundol: Anyway, it turned out well. (Smiles bitterly)
Pongmyŏng: I feel very sorry towards her. And what would Father say if he knew?
(At this moment, someone calls Sundol from outside the fence.)
Sundol: Oh, is that Tŏksŏng? Come in.
(Something else is said from outside)
Sundol: You want me to come out right now? Okay, I’m going.
(To Pongmyŏng) Let’s go together.
(As the two exit, Sundol looks into the kitchen)
Sundol: What are you talking about? Hurry up and make the porridge. I’m just going to Tŏksŏng’s place for a moment—
(The two exit)
Wife: (Holding the infant, looking back at Suni who follows her out) So, how could you keep quiet about it all this time? Really, little sister-in-law...
Suni: (Head bowed, silent)
Wife: (Teasingly) How many months? Huh?
Suni: (Getting angry) Aigo, Sister-in-law, all you do is tease. More importantly, tonight, older brother and he (*her fiancé, Pongmyŏng*), and all the young men in this village are going out to fight.
Wife: (Startled) What? Fight? What kind of fight?
Suni: They say they’re going to kill the yangban bastards. Ch’oe Chinsa and the magistrate, they say they’ll kill them all—
Wife: (Jumping) Aigo, kill those people? Who said that? What <91> do we have to rely on to do such a thing!
Suni: Rely on what? Then should we just leave the enemy bastards alone? (Glares)
Wife: Ah, so is child’s father really going too? With Father like this, why do such a thing? If they kill those people, will they even come back alive...
Suni: (Snapping) If things go well, why would they die? Aren’t you angry too, sister-in-law? Who caused Mother to pass away? Who caused Father's illness too? And didn’t they take all our money and land? Augh, I’m so angry. I just want to chew on those bastards’ livers. All their liquor is our blood and tears.
Wife: (Coolly) But what can we do? It’s all our fate.
Suni: Fate! Why is it our fate to have even our lives taken by those bastards?
(Frustrated) Oh, sister-in-law, really, what is fate?
Wife: (Turns back to teasing) Aigo, you learned all this from Pongmyŏng. Didn’t you also say last year, when child's father was talking about burning down that bastard's house, that it was fate and he should stop! Let's stop talking about that and (Pointing to Suni's stomach) So, how many months?
(At this moment, a strange coughing sound, 'k’ŏk k’ŏk', comes from the upper room)
Suni: (About to retort angrily, then catches herself) That, that coughing sound is strange. (Peeks towards the upper room)
Suni: (Startled) Aigo, sister! Look at him! Something terrible has happened... Quickly, go call Oppa (*older brother*). Now! Quickly—
(Enters the room and pulls back Kim Ch’ŏmji’s blanket. Blood has pooled on the blanket, and a pale face, just skin and bones, is visible)
Wife: Why are you like this? Huh? (Looks into the room) Oh my god, that blood, what happened...
(Hurries towards the door and bumps into Sundol)
Wife: Oh my goodness, husband, come quickly! Father just vomited blood!
Sundol: (Startled) What? Vomited blood? Huh?
(Pushes past his wife and enters the upper room. At this moment, shrouded in mist and clouds, a dimly copper-red moon begins to rise over the mountain)
Sundol: (Feeling his chest here and there, in a tearful voice) Father, Father, please come to your senses, Father! (Under the flickering light, only the pale face is visible, Suni is collapsed, weeping) Honey, bring some water. Quickly, and call Pongmyŏng too...
(Sundol’s Wife, holding a water bowl, hesitates, unsure what to do)
Sundol: (Pulls the drooping head onto his lap and feels the chest again) Aigo, his chest is getting colder and colder. (Pours the water he brought into the slack mouth)
Ch’ŏmji: (Eyes flicker open, looks at Sundol, and tightly grasps his son's hand with a trembling hand) Avenge me. Go... those yangban bastards—oh, go destroy them—quickly, quickly go and destroy them. (His voice grows fainter.)
Sundol: Suni, Father, please come to your senses, yes, Father—
Ch’ŏmji: (Grinding his teeth fiercely, his voice grows louder) Go— Go quickly, go—Quickly avenge me—Ugh—the commoners’ enemy.
Sundol: (In a choked voice) Father, Father, don't worry. We'll kill them all tonight, Father, don't worry about anything—Yes.
Ch’ŏmji: (Lets go of the hand and waves his hand towards the outside of the room) Go quickly—quickly—the magistrate bastard—Ch’oe Chinsa—(Grinds his teeth again)
(Village Elders, villagers, Pongmyŏng, etc., enter hastily, led by Sundol’s Wife)
Elder A: Kim Ch’ŏmji, oh, what is happening?
Pongmyŏng: Uncle, ah—Father-in-law! (Bursts into tears)
Ch’ŏmji: (Opens his eyes again and sees Pongmyŏng) Oh, Pongmyŏng-ah—Oh, my son-in-law, I know—I heard everything you all were talking about at dawn— Yes, go quickly, go quickly—Go quickly with Tol-i (*Sundol*)— (Gasps painfully, struggling for breath. Then coughs 'k’ŏk k’ŏk' again, spitting out blood like a thick stream)
Sundol: (Lifts his head from weeping) Father, oh Father—
(From afar, the sound of a janggu drum is heard, along with the shouts of young men. And the song—) <92>
Let’s go, let’s go
Dawdling, dawdling
If it becomes Pyŏngsin year, we can’t go
Ch’ŏmji: (Eyes flicker open again at the drum sound)
Go quickly—don’t worry about me, go quickly—the janggu is sounding—they said it’s done when the janggu sounds—quickly—quickly (Weakly gestures with his hand)
Elder B: (Solemnly) Sundol, go quickly. Pongmyŏng, you too, go quickly. We’ll take care of everything afterwards. Go quickly where young men must go—
(Overcome with emotion)
Elder C: Yes, don’t worry about things here, just go quickly—quickly, avenge your father—avenge us commoners—Go—quickly—
Suni: (Weeping) Oppa, go quickly—don’t worry, just go quickly—(Turns to Pongmyŏng) Please go quickly—go with peace of mind.
Sundol: (Hugs Ch’ŏmji and cries out loud)
Father—Father—pitiful Father—We will avenge you—We’ll avenge you tonight—Father, close your eyes, please—
(Sundol stands up, weeping. Pongmyŏng also stands up. Suni clings to Pongmyŏng)
Sundol: (Wiping away tears) Then, uncles, we are going.
(The sound of drums and shouts grows louder)
Sundol: (Looking into the room again) Father—we are going to avenge you—Yes—Father—
Pongmyŏng: (Pushes Suni away as she clings to him) No, you can't—(Pulls Sundol’s *arm* and leads him) Let's go quickly—Come on, quickly—
(Sundol leans on Pongmyŏng’s shoulder, crying loudly, heading towards the door. Outside, voices calling Sundol are loud)
Elders: Don’t worry about things here, go quickly—quickly.
(Outside the fence, shouts and the song)
Let's go
Let's go
-The curtain falls quietly- <93>

**Summary**

This one-act play, "Let's Go [Kabo Year]," depicts the desperate circumstances of poor tenant farmers in Namwŏn on the eve of a local uprising during the 1894 Donghak Peasant Rebellion. Sundol, the protagonist, is torn between caring for his dying father, Kim Ch’ŏmji—a victim of landlord Ch’oe Chinsa's cruelty—and joining his friend Pongmyŏng and other villagers in a planned attack against the oppressive local magistrate and landlords. Encouraged by his dying father's final wish for vengeance and the urging of fellow villagers who promise to care for his family, Sundol ultimately chooses to join the rebellion, leaving behind his grieving family as the sounds of revolt draw near. The play highlights the extreme poverty, exploitation by local elites (magistrates, yangban landlords), and simmering resentment that fueled the Donghak Rebellion, portraying the uprising not just as a political movement but as a desperate act of survival and vengeance by oppressed commoners pushed to their limits. Historians might find the depiction of class antagonism, the specific grievances against local officials and landlords, the role of figures like "General Noktu," and the blend of personal tragedy with revolutionary fervor particularly interesting for understanding the grassroots motivations and atmosphere of the Donghak Rebellion period. The use of the children's song "Kabo se" as a recurring motif effectively underscores the urgency and historical moment of the uprising.
