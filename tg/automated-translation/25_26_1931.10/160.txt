기사제목 시인 「꾀테」의 舊家를 찾고, 獨逸留學日記 (Visiting the Old House of the Poet Goethe, Diary of Studying Abroad in Germany)
필자 都宥浩
기사형태 문예기타 (Literature/Other)

Visiting the Old House of the Poet Goethe, Diary of Studying Abroad in Germany
						To Yuho
Old Mrs. Baumann asked me several times to please go with her to Erfurt for the Catholic holiday. She said her son, who had moved out, lived in Erfurt. I wanted to go to Erfurt, but after returning to Frankfurt, I somehow couldn't find the opportunity again. While I was in the village of Bollenhausen, there were many other interesting things. Let me tell you one more story from that time: it was election night. That night, the Baumann house held a dance party with a gramophone playing. That night, I also tried dancing for the first time in a long while. The young lady who taught me to dance was Mina from next door. Mina, along with her younger sibling, takes full charge of their motherless household chores. During the day, she goes to the fields to dig potatoes, and when she returns home, she cleans the cowshed and feeds the pigs. She also washes her widower father's clothes. When I held Mina's hand to learn dancing, her hand was several times rougher than mine. It might be somewhat impolite to say, but Mina's hand was undoubtedly the hand of a worker.

After I returned to Frankfurt, Rudi's letter was full of talk about Erfurt. When I was leaving, old Mrs. Baumann gave me a jar of jam, saying she had made it with wild strawberries she picked herself. Later, the Baumanns sent another jar. Coincidentally, my new landlord didn't provide butter with the morning bread, so I ate it gratefully. Moreover, the last jar came with blood sausage (도야지순대). I shared the blood sausage with Kilgess, who lived at the same lodging.

By the time I returned to Frankfurt, Mr. Ku had already moved lodgings. Thus, my current lodging is on a side street of Wangga Street. After returning to Frankfurt, I started visiting Professor Wilken's house again for a while. During that time, my right arm also healed.

After that, I even re-enrolled in the university. It's a bit cheeky to say, but given my age, I'm considered among the older ones at the university. When I first entered university, understanding the lectures was the most difficult, but now that has improved greatly. Since returning to Frankfurt, I haven't traveled anywhere else special. I only spent one night in Heidelberg. That was, of course, to find out about the university there. Heidelberg is a small town, but a very beautiful place. Heidelberg University <51> is one of the oldest in Germany in terms of history. Professor Rickert, the philosopher we often hear about, is also there. And Weber, a sociologist famous in Germany, is there too. This Weber is, of course, different from Max Weber, who once gained worldwide fame as a sociologist of religion. That Max Weber has already passed away. Another thing I saw in Heidelberg worth mentioning is the Heidelberg Man (Heidelbergmaon), which provided significant material for anthropological research, and the castle destroyed by the French army. As an old university city and a scenic place, Heidelberg also has beautiful songs. "Old Heidelberg, you are lovely... remainder omitted..."

One day, on my way back from Mr. Wilken's, I stopped by Goethe's house. This is Goethe's birthplace and where he lived through his youth and into his early adulthood. It is said that "The Sorrows of Young Werther," which once drew tears from young men and women, was also written in this house. Day after day, visitors endlessly stream into this house. There is also a Goethe museum there. However, the place truly called Goethe's home is in Weimar. One thing I found particularly interesting at Goethe's house that day was Goethe's puppet theater set (인형극장물). It's said that whenever Goethe was bored, he would gather these puppets and make them do exercises or perform plays. Goethe is someone we know well, but Germans mention "Goethe" at the drop of a hat. And statues and busts of Goethe are everywhere you go. There is one at the university too. Goethe's poetry is said to be unusually profound, and this semester at Heidelberg University, Professor Rickert is reportedly lecturing on "Faust" as a philosophy course topic.

Near my lodging, there is a beautiful Republic Park. It once bore the name of Hohenzollern but is now called Republic Park. It's a bit difficult to call it a park. In German, it's called an "Anlage," and these "Anlagen" are small spaces located along streetsides. In German cities, these are found everywhere. I sometimes took walks in this Republic Park. During the summer season, the weeping willows drooping down by the pond were most beautiful. Morning and evening, crowds of men and women gather ceaselessly in this park. And what one often sees in this park
are young lovers kissing without inhibition. It is said that after the war, social constraints on relationships between men and women in Germany became very liberal. Before the war, kissing like this in front of others was certainly unacceptable, but now people do it quite casually. They kiss audibly while others are sitting right in front of them. Others pretend not to notice. However, compared to France, this is still said to be far behind. Recently, I saw a weekly pictorial magazine featuring a section on kissing in Paris (巴里), and looking at the pictures, kissing was happening freely on trams, main streets, and in tea rooms. Kissing on main streets is said to often cause traffic policemen to scratch their heads. Kissing in Germany, however, is still mostly confined to parks. But even this park kissing naturally disappears as the days get colder and the number of strollers decreases. As I took my evening walks in Republic Park, the leaves began to fall, and soon only the sparse branches remained. More than anything, I felt the melancholy of autumn from the remaining branches of the weeping willows drooping by the pond. And somehow, I felt it was a pity. Especially as autumn deepened, the sight of dry leaves falling one by one, finally, made the surrounding air feel most desolate. It made me want to sing the following song once again:
The evening sun slopes far away,
When smoke curls from the chimney under the mud wall,
From the sparse branches of the old pavilion tree,
Silently falls a single dry leaf.
I wrote this song 7 or 8 years ago! My heart then was most beautiful. That time has already passed. But I always want to have the heart of that time. I try to always live like I did then.
Winter in Frankfurt is very mild. While the summer climate is cool, the winter climate is not cold. Snow fell once or twice then, but it usually melted right after falling. <52> Once, thinking the ice had frozen just enough, I went out with my skates, which I barely knew how to use, but that too melted away after a day. Last year the weather was quite cold, but this year it has become mild like usual years, they say. However, winter is winter, so one still needs to light a fire in the room to endure it. As this winter arrives, the lives of the poor everywhere become even more difficult. Occasionally, beggars appear at the landlord's door. Their bodies look healthy, but to beg for food, they inevitably adopt the voice of a disabled person. Among them, the majority are said to be unemployed. After becoming unemployed, they managed for a while on insurance money, but that only lasts half a year, so after that, they inevitably resort to this.
The number of these unemployed people is gradually increasing. Throughout Germany, the current number of unemployed is said to be over 3.5 million. As unemployment increases, the number of "beggars" (거지) also increases. Since this topic came up, shall I follow up with a story about unemployed women? No, calling them unemployed might be misleading. The people I am about to talk about are proper working women. Day or night, if you go out to Kaiser Street or the Altstadt, countless women are standing around in various corners. They are, without exception, all prostitutes (매음부). After the war, licensed brothels (여곽) disappeared in Germany. But in their place, these women who practice prostitution freely emerged. Among them, some are said to have permits, while others do not. Most of them had no other way to make a living. The number of these prostitutes is also increasing daily. I first witnessed their existence one Saturday afternoon. Kaiser Street is the busiest street in Frankfurt. It's like Jongno or Honmachi in Seoul, or Ginza Street in Tokyo. A friend (親故) walking with me pointed and asked if I knew what those were over there. When I looked closely afterward, their numbers were astonishingly large. Later, I heard that among the couples who come to tea rooms or restaurants with female companions, quite a few include such prostitutes.
One afternoon while I was at home, a young violinist appeared in front of the landlord's door (또아). Hearing a strange, beautiful sound from somewhere, I listened for a while, thinking it was coming from Mr. Kilgess's room, and went to listen. But no one was there. Then I opened the door (또아) and looked out, and right there was this young fellow playing his instrument. This was, of course, a beggar. He
was, however, dressed very much like an artist. Besides this, music sometimes drifts in unexpectedly. If you open the window and look down, such beggar musicians (乞人樂士) are performing on the street. However, sometimes one mistakes them for the Salvation Army. Speaking of the Salvation Army, in Seoul, when the Salvation Army played trumpets and beat drums on Jongno or Seodaemun streets, men and women, young and old, would gather around, but here too, I see the Salvation Army often giving tedious speeches on empty streets. Sometimes, if there's really no audience, they hold a megaphone (마가폰) and turn left and right, shouting until their faces turn red. The story is becoming increasingly disordered. Since music came up, let me also tell you about the violin-playing skills of Mr. Kilgess, who lives in the same lodging. His calm demeanor extends even to this. This skill also directly provides him with financial assistance. I sometimes eavesdropped on his violin playing. Once, with his sister who came to visit him, he held a piano-violin recital for an audience of two or three in a corner of the lodging. As someone ignorant of music, I cannot comment one way or another, but anyway, I listened with rapt attention. And I, for one, admire his talent. It seems that most Germans possess some degree of musical knowledge.
One day near the classroom, I met a law student. He asked me about America and whether I thought about studying abroad there for a year on an exchange scholarship. He asked me if I knew about the recruitment for next year's (1931) exchange scholarship students currently being conducted by the Prussian (普魯) Ministry of Education in Berlin (伯林) (Continued on page 63) <53> and inquired about the benefits. Of course, I couldn't answer. The benefit refers more to time than money. Studying in America means passing the state examination will be delayed by at least a year, he said. Then he added, "Even now, if my father passes away, my studies are over. Who knows when my father will pass away?" He then repeated "Time is money" several times. Later, the student visited me at my lodging again. He chewed over "Time is money" once more. Then the topic of conversation changed again. He asked me if there were parties like the Social Democratic Party (SPD), Communist Party, or National Party (國粹黨) in Korea. Our conversation included the following:
"Which political party do you favor? The SPD?"
"The SPD is not the issue. The issue is only whether it's the Communist Party or the National Party (國粹黨). But the platform of one of them is a bit vague..."
And he didn't answer when I asked which party's platform he meant. He then asked me if Koreans also enjoy political discussions (政談)... Germans, no, German youths enjoy political discussions. I even heard an evaluation of this from a British student. He told me then that in England, political talk in conversation is considered impolite. And he showed a very British gentlemanly manner...
I don't know how it is in England, but at least post-war German youths engage in political discussions without hesitation. <63> <51-53, 63>

**Summary**

This article is a diary entry by To Yuho (都宥浩), a Korean student in Germany in 1931. He recounts his experiences after returning to Frankfurt from a village stay, including social interactions, re-enrolling in university, visiting Heidelberg and Goethe's house in Frankfurt. He observes German society, noting the roughness of a working girl's hands, the prevalence of public displays of affection (kissing), the visible signs of economic hardship like unemployment, beggars, street musicians, and prostitution, and the German fondness for Goethe and music. He also describes conversations about exchange programs and the intense political engagement of German youth, discussing parties like the SPD, Communists, and the "National Party" (國粹黨, likely referring to the rising Nazi party), contrasting it with perceived British norms.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable glimpse into Weimar Germany through the eyes of a Korean colonial subject. It highlights the social liberalism (relative freedom in relationships) alongside severe economic distress (mass unemployment, poverty) that characterized the era just before the Nazi takeover. The mention of the intense political discussions among youth, specifically framing the choice between Communism and the "National Party," is particularly revealing of the polarized political climate. For historians, it provides a non-Western, colonial perspective on European society and politics during a critical period, and insights into the experiences and observations of Korean intellectuals studying abroad.

시인 「꾀테」의 舊家를 찾고, 獨逸留學日記
						都宥浩
『빠우만』 노부인은 나다려 몇 번이나 『에어빠흐』의 天主敎 名節에는 부대 함께 가자고 부탁하엿습니다.
『에어빠흐』에는 자기의 분가한 아들이 산다고 하엿습니다. 나는 『에어빠흐』로 가기는 싶엇사오나 佛郞府에 돌아온 뒤로는 이럭저럭 다시 기회를 몯얻엇습니다. 내가 『볼펜하우젠』 동리에 잇는 동안에는 그밖에도 자미잇는 일이 많엇습니다. 그 중에서 하나 더 이야기 해 보건대, 그것은 바로 선거일 밤이엇습니다. 그날 밤 빠우만宅에서는 유성기를 틀어 놓고 무도회를 열엇습니다. 그날 밤에는 나도 오랫만에 한번 『딴쓰』를 해보앗습니다. 나에게 딴쓰 가르켜 준 阿只씨는 엽집의 『미나』엿습니다. 『미나』는 자기 동생과 함께 주부 없는 자기 집일을 도맡아 합니다. 낮이면 밭에 가서 감자도 파고 집에 돌아왓어는 오양깐도 치고 도야지 물도 줍니다. 그리고는 홀아비 아부지의 옷도 빨어드립니다. 내가 『미나』에게 『딴쓰』를 배우노라고 손을 잡엇을 때에 그의 손은 내 손보다 몇 배나 거치려웟습니다. 다소 실례의 말이오나 『미나』의 손은 틀림없는 일군의 손이엇습니다.
내가 佛郞府에 돌아온 뒤에 『루듸』군의 편지에는 『에어빠흐』 타령이 춤을 흘리게 씨여 잇엇습니다. 내가 올 때에 老빠우만 부인은 내가 딴 딸기로 만든 것이라고 하며 쨈 한 병을 주엇습니다. 그 후에 빠우만댁에서는 다시 한 병 더 보내주엇습니다. 마침 새로 옴긴 主人에는 아침 麵麭에 뻐터가 없길래 나는 그것을 緊하게 먹엇습니다. 더구나 마지막 병에는 도야지순대도 부터왓섯습니다. 도야지순대를 나는 한 主人에 잇는 낄게쓰군과 나누어 먹엇습니다.
내가 佛郞府에 돌아온 때에는 발서 俱선생이 主人을 옴겻섯습니다. 그리하야 지금잇는 주인은 王家街 한곁에 잇습니다. 나는 佛郞府에 돌아와서는 한동안 윌켄교수 댁으로 다시 드나들게 되엇습니다. 그러는 동안에 옳은 팔도 나앗습니다.
