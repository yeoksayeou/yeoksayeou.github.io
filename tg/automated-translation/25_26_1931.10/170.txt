Twisting Rope and Studying Too         By Paekch'on In (白村人) from Sŏnch'ŏn (宣川)

一
After eating dinner and going to the village schoolhouse (書堂 - sŏdang), as I step onto the porch, the bustling sound of *seureurak seureurak sak sak* fills my ears. Opening the door and looking inside, about thirty people in total, young and old alike, are sitting with their backs against the four drafty walls of the three-room space. With bundles of straw (짚목) beside them, they are busily twisting thin straw rope for grain sacks (가마니날) or thick rope, making loud *seureu seureurak seureurak seureurakrak* sounds. In one corner, they chat and laugh; in another corner, some place books beside their straw bundles and peek at them, while still twisting *seureurak seureurak sak sak* busily.
After twisting rope busily for a while, when the whistle blows "*Horeureureu*", the room that had been noisy until now instantly becomes quiet. Everyone puts down the rope they were twisting. Some from under their straw bundles, others from their satchels, each takes out pencils and books and turns towards the side where the blackboard hangs. The night school (夜學 - yahak) has begun.
When the two-hour night school ends, they start twisting rope again, laughing and talking just like before, *seureurak seureurak*.

二
In the village where I live, the only educated people are Chŏng Ki-hwa (정기화), a graduate of Sungsil University (숭실대학), and Ke Ch'ang-sun (게창순), who dropped out of higher common school. About three years ago, these two tried to establish a night school, like in other places, to eradicate illiteracy in the farming village, but it was utterly impossible to make the night school happen. The reason was that my village is entirely poor, so even during the winter, the agricultural off-season, they had to weave straw sacks (가마니) day and night. During the day they wove sacks, and at night, fathers, mothers, and children alike had to twist the straw rope needed for the next day's sacks. Therefore, nobody had the leisure time, and although they wanted to learn, there was no one available to attend night school. This was truly a pitiful situation.
Ch'ang-sun and Ki-hwa discussed many times how they could overcome this situation and establish a night school to get people to attend, but that winter, the prospects looked bleak. If they didn't twist rope at night, they couldn't weave sacks, and if they didn't weave sacks, they would run out of rice for their meals. If you can't eat, what good is night school? You have to eat to live! That's right, you have to eat to live. Only after eating can you do anything. The uneducated farmers wanted to learn, but for the sake of eating, night school was a secondary issue. Ch'ang-sun and<54> Ki-hwa cursed this harsh reality but resolved to establish the night school somehow.
Until then, Ch'ang-sun and Ki-hwa had only thought that if they held night school, people wouldn't be able to twist rope. However, after thinking about it for many days, Ch'ang-sun suggested, "Let's have them bring straw to the night school and twist rope while they learn." Ki-hwa agreed, saying, "That's a great idea. Let's do that," and they immediately decided to set up the night school. Those who wanted to learn were to bring straw to the night school, twist rope for an hour before studying, study for about two hours, and then twist rope for another hour afterward. Ch'ang-sun and Ki-hwa covered the costs for the blackboard, chalk, and other expenses, while the night school students were responsible for providing firewood. They decided to use the village schoolhouse as the location.
On the night of the tenth day of the eleventh lunar month, Ch'ang-sun and Ki-hwa went door to door, telling everyone who didn't know how to read—adults and children alike—to come up to the schoolhouse with straw. Children unable to attend regular school, as well as young and old adults, all gathered at the schoolhouse with bundles of straw tucked under their arms.
The farmers who couldn't study because of rope-twisting can now twist almost as much rope in the one hour before and one hour after studying as they did when they only twisted rope without studying. This is because they utilize their time well. Rope twisting is done competitively every night, so everyone tries to twist even one foot more. Sometimes, prizes are given to the fastest twisters. The night school students twist rope diligently and study diligently.

An Episode at the Work Site    By Mumyŏng Kŏsa (無名居士 - Nameless Lay Buddhist)

I have already been eating and working alongside laborers for almost a month. Today, as usual, I set out for the new road construction site, carrying a hod (질통) on my A-frame carrier (지게) with a bowl of foxtail millet mixed rice perched precariously on top. From this alley and that alley, this street and that street, many workers emerge in groups of ten or twenty, carrying A-frame carriers or shouldering shovels, crowding together like ants. On the roadside, where the construction is still incomplete, a wave of people has formed. I too mixed in among them. After walking about half a *ri*, we reached the spot where yesterday we did piecework (푼빵 - carrying loads for a fixed price per load) and earned fifteen *ryang* (1 won 50 chŏn).
"We'll probably work here today too, right?" As I took off the A-frame carrier I had been carrying, everyone else also took theirs off and sat down to rest. Some smoked tobacco... some sang songs... Among them were also young men, showing off their vigor, wrestling on the soft, yielding yellow earth.
Just then, on the main road ahead, men in dark clothes and a young man in white clothes wearing a cap appeared at the front. To their left, right, and behind, laborers in dirt-stained clothes clustered irregularly. Seeing them, the people who had been sitting and resting in front began to stir and stand up, one or two at a time.
Among the laborers, one said, "Looks like it's that Ojima guy and Yoshida again today!"
Another person chimed in, "Where? One is Yoshida, but isn't the other one Hayashi?" and they started chattering. Among them, someone said, "Is that Ojima fellow going to act nasty again today? Really gives me OO pains," and as soon as these words were out, another, a rather radical young man, declared, "That darned fellow, if he says anything today, let's teach him a lesson even if we can't work. If we let guys like that be, they'll get into the habit and always act that way."
The laborers who had been sitting picked up their A-frame carriers and shouldered their shovels, heading towards the approaching group, clamoring, "Please hire us today for sure... Old Sir... Mr. Ojima..."
"Yakamasii! (Noisy! Shut up!)" "Munŏnasiyo. (Everybody get back!)" came the shouts. When they said this, the crowd would retreat but then surge forward again. Seeing this, they had no choice but to raise their clubs. They even hurled insults that were hard on the ears. Still, it was chaos. Unable to watch any longer, I went down, spoke with the foreman (什長 - sipchang), and then addressed the laborers.
"For the past few days, too many people have been coming, causing a commotion,<55> so it's been decided to hire only one hundred fifty people per day. Therefore, those who weren't here yesterday won't be able to work. Please don't cause trouble, you'll only get cursed at and beaten, there's nothing else you can do..." Hearing this, realizing it was futile, they resignedly stepped back one by one and sat down, thinking, "Can we force them if they say no?"
The time came to start work. There were about eight *p'yŏng* (a unit of area/volume for earthmoving) left over from yesterday's piecework. Even working at yesterday's piecework pace, it would require at least fifteen people, but they were told only seven people should do it. Ojima said in broken Korean, "Anihagaseumu darumusaramusigigetta (If you don't do it, other people will get it)." A group of those who hadn't gotten work and were sitting dejectedly jumped up *ururu*, but they hesitated to actually confront them. I told them it wouldn't work. The stubborn Ojima said, "Anihagaseumu geumanudura (If you don't do it, then quit)" and went over there. There too, he gave the same instructions. The laborers there were also bewildered. Among them, some retreated, muttering, "Are all Koreans dead that we have to take this kind of work?" Some said, "Forget this darned thing! My great-grandfather (큰아바지 - k'ŭnabaji, dialect for grandfather/할아버지 - harabŏji) who never did this kind of work managed to live." Yet, some still pleaded, "Old Sir, Mr. Ojima," begging to add just two more people, or just three more people. Ojima retorted with a single phrase, "Anihagaseumu dagara (If you don't do it, that's why [you don't get more people])."
This was the moment. My nerves paradoxically became calm. Facing the nearly two hundred laborers, I delivered a speech. "Everyone! Compared to you, I am young, know little, and also have no experience in this kind of labor. However, I will say a word before you all. Everyone! Today, we must all quit together. The number of laborers has exceeded the planned amount by this much, and everyone who came has no intention of quitting, so those hiring will inevitably ask us to do impossible tasks like this. Will we die if we don't do this work for one day! Even if we were all to starve to death tomorrow, let's all quit today. No matter how desperate we are, selling our labor day by day, seemingly only knowing how to eat, do laborers not have hearts? If we do the work they are telling us to do today, even if we work until our bones ache, we won't even earn 50 chŏn each.
(4 lines omitted - original text)
Not only that, but as laborers, how can we not know the value of our dignity, our labor power? Let's all quit!" As I finished, voices calling "Let's go!" arose from various groups. Some turned around, saying "That's right! Come on!" while others, seeing everyone leaving, lingered hesitantly, saying, "If they all go, let's try working from today." The Japanese supervisor came up to me, eyes blazing as if he could devour me, demanding, "If you don't want to work, just quit by yourself! Why are you telling others not to work?"
"If even one person works after we leave, know that you'll die!" "Ah! We get a day off today!" "It's a holiday for us workers!" "A day off!" "We need days like this too!" "The young folks are doing it, should we old folks just sit tight? Let's go, everyone!"... With such talk, even the elderly stood up. Seeing this, the supervisors had no choice but to gather up the ropes they had laid out for marking the work area and return to their headquarters.
At the work site, the morning sun only shone on the scattered red earth, making it look even redder.

Who Bears the Burden of the Two Sŏm?
By Ch'oe Ton-yŏn (崔燉蓮)

"What? The land assessed at one *sŏm* (섬 - unit of grain yield/tax assessment) used to be worth over 100 won, and now you say 40 won is too much and want to knock off another 5 won? How can I possibly sell land like this? It seems only the buyer can make a living, not the seller, haha!"
"Heh- It's always a difficult situation. Isn't the market price different now? Just look at the sale of Mr. Kim's (김소사) paddy field. Is that one any better? Unaffected by flood or drought, such eternally fertile land (萬年不敗良田 - mannyŏnbulpaeyangjŏn) went for barely 38 won. Compared to that, is your paddy even a paddy?"
"Look here, even considering that, I bought my land at 90 won 50 chŏn per *sŏm* of assessed yield (매섬지기 - maesŏmjigi). Just two years ago, wasn't it worth over 100 won? It has to be at least half that price..."
"But the assessment (石定 - chijŏng) is different now compared to when you initially<56> bought that land, isn't it? What is the assessed yield of that land anyway?"
"As you know, it's assessed at a good 20 *sŏk* (석 - same unit as 섬)."
"That's right, but when you first bought it, wasn't it assessed at 18 *sŏm*? Only in recent years did it become assessed at 20 *sŏk*, though."
"Isn't it always the same thing? You're not farming it yourself either, you're letting others cultivate it, aren't you? Mr. Kim said that even at 38 won, his land comes out to 50 won per assessed *sŏm*. Didn't they increase his assessment by five *sŏm* this time?"
"Was that so? Hmm, indeed, I'm not farming mine myself either..."
"So you see, at 40 won, I'm practically giving my land away..."
"Five won per *sŏk*... for a 20-*sŏk* assessment, that's about 100 won. Isn't that right? Well then, since we've already started negotiating, how about this? You take 30 won less, and I'll pay 70 won more. But in the contract, let's write it as assessed at 22 *sŏk* at 35 won per *sŏk*. That way, you benefit too, and I don't seem to lose too much..."
"Well, if we do it like that..."
And so, the seller ended up selling at 38 won 50 chŏn per *sŏk* (based on the original 20 *sŏk* assessment), subtly receiving a better price than expected, while the buyer is purchasing at less than 35 won per *sŏk* (based on the inflated 22 *sŏk* assessment written in the contract). Who bears the burden of the missing money? It is our tenant farmers (作人 - chagin), whose burden increases by two *sŏm* every year.

A Memorable Farewell Party
By Kang Paek-nam (姜白南) from Yŏngju (榮州)

South of T'aebaek Mountain (태백산), on the upper reaches of the Nakdong River (낙동강), there is a curious village shaped like a 'White Egret Peeking at a Fish' (白鷺窺魚型 - Paengnokuyu-hyŏng). This village is Naemae Village (乃梅 동리), the location of Naemyŏng School (內明學敎). There, some ten-odd years ago (1919), a small farewell party (餞別會 - chŏnbyŏrhoe) was held, attended by no more than 200 people, men and women, young and old. When the farewell address in the program was interrupted mid-speech, the gathering dissolved into a flood of tears. Unable to avoid leaving that place, I couldn't deliver my response and the farewell party ended in tears. I wasn't being exiled for committing a crime, nor was I heading to the gallows after receiving a death sentence, of course, but during the long ten years of my youth spent working as the principal and teacher (訓導 - hundo) at Naemyŏng School, seeing the situation of the people in this village—where transportation was inconvenient, culture was undeveloped, and they wandered lost in illiteracy—I established a night school (1909) and began to pioneer their future. The masses, starved for letters and thirsty for knowledge, rushed to enroll, vying with one another. Apart from the students of Naemyŏng School, almost everyone else enrolled, making it impossible to teach them all in one session. So, from 5 PM to 6 PM, I first taught basic Hangul, elementary arithmetic, and simple Hanja to the women and young girls. Then, from 8 PM to 9 PM, I taught the same subjects to the young men. For the middle-aged and elderly, whenever there was spare time, I read newspapers and magazines aloud to them and held discussions on common knowledge topics. I continued this consistently until the time of my farewell, to the point where there wasn't a single young man or woman born and raised in that village who couldn't read a newspaper. Illiteracy was eradicated to that extent. I found my sole pleasure in watching them learn to read, thinking of it as a resurrection movement for my people, and worked without a penny of compensation, sharing their joys and sorrows. Consequently, I came to understand their circumstances and became a friend with whom they shared secrets. I knew their hardships: weeding fields while their skin burned under the fiery, blazing sun; carrying firewood in the flesh-piercing cold; staying up through summer nights, fighting off sleepiness to spin and weave; shedding beads of sweat while cooking barley rice with damp firewood. Becoming intimately familiar with their struggles, I received the boundless love of those innocent people. Around July and August, like an annual event, I received pumpkin rice cakes and corn from the female students' book bags, fish from students returning from weeding the paddies, and melons and peaches from students returning from weeding the dry fields. The material value aside, the sincere, red hearts of the male and female night school students who brought these things were truly incomparable treasures (寶貝 - pobae). Thus, the memory of that farewell party remains an unforgettable event in my life, vividly recalled in my mind even now.

**Summary**

This collection presents four short anecdotal accounts from colonial Korea in 1931. The first describes a successful night school in a poor village where students twist straw rope before and after class to balance learning with essential income generation. The second recounts a labor dispute at a construction site where Korean workers, encouraged by the narrator, stage a walkout against unfair demands by Japanese supervisors. The third illustrates a shrewd land deal where the buyer and seller manipulate the recorded land assessment (in *sŏm*) to benefit themselves, ultimately increasing the burden on the tenant farmer. The final piece is a nostalgic recollection of a teacher's decade-long effort running a night school in a remote village, culminating in an emotional farewell, highlighting the deep bonds formed through literacy education and shared hardship. These stories vividly portray the struggles and resilience of ordinary Koreans facing poverty, illiteracy, exploitative labor conditions under Japanese authority, and complex land issues during the colonial period. Historians might find valuable insights into grassroots literacy movements, Korean-Japanese interactions in labor contexts (including linguistic barriers and resistance), the socio-economic pressures driving land sales and tenancy issues, and the strong communal bonds and desire for education in rural Korea.

募集實話, 民衆과 親하야
새끼도 꼬고 공부도 하고         宣川 白村人

一
저녁밥을 먹고 서당으로 가서 토방에 올라설라면 스르락 스르락 삭삭하는 요란한 소리가 귀에 들린다. 문을 열고 들어가 보면 어린애 어른 할 것 없이 전부 삼십 명 가량이나 되는데 삼간 방 사방 바람뚝을 등에 지고 앉어서 짚목을 옆에 놓고 가마니날인 가는 새끼를 혹은 굵은 새끼를 꼬노라고 스르 스르락 스르락 스르락락 크게 분주하다. 한 모통이에서는 이야기도 하며 웃기도 하며 또 한 모통이에서는 책을 짚목 옆에 놓고 디려다 보기도 하며 스르락 스르락 삭삭 분주하다.
한참이나 새끼를 분주히 꼬다가 「호르르르」하고 「호각」이 울면 이재껏 떠들든 집안이 일시에 고요해지며 다 꼬든 새끼를 놓고 어떤 사람은 짚목 아래서 어떤 사람은 지갑에서 제각금 연필과 책을 끄내여 놓고 칠판 걸린 쪽으로 향한다. 야학이 시작된 것이다.
두 시간식의 야학이 끝나거든 또 전과 같이 웃으며 이야기하며 스르락 스르락 새끼를 꼬기 시작한다.

二
내가 사는 촌에 공부한 사람이라고는 숭실대학을 졸업한 정기화와 고등보통학교를 중도에 퇴학한 게창순 두 분이 잇을 뿐이다. 이 두 사람은 한 삼년 전에 농촌의 문맹을 타파하려고 다른 곧과 같이 야학을 세우려 하엿으나 도제히 야학은 되지 않엇다. 그 이유로는 나 사는 마을이 전부 가난하여서 농한기라는 겨울에도 밤낮 가마니를 치지 않으면 안 될 지경이엇다. 낮에는 가마니치고 밤에는 아버지 어머니 어린애 할 것 없이 내일 칠 가마니날 즉 새끼를 꼬지 않으면 안 되는 고로 누구나 겨를이 없어 배우고는 싶어도 야학에 갈 사람이 없엇다. 이것은 참 딱한 사정이엇섯다.
창순과 기화는 어떠케 하면 이 지경을 면해서 야학을 세워 가지고 야학에 오게 할까 하고 여러 번 의론햇으나 그 해 겨을 볼 길이 망연하엿다. 밤에 새끼를 꼬지 않으면 가마니를 칠 수 없고 가마니를 안 치면 끼니을 쌀이 없어진다. 먹지 못하구야 야학이 다 무어냐? 먹어야 산다! 그러타 먹어야 산다. 먹구야 무슨 일이든지 한다. 무식한 농민은 배우고 싶으나 먹기 위하야는 야학도 제2문제다. 창순과＜54＞ 기화는 이 악착한 현실을 욕하면서 아무케 해서라도 야학은 세워 놓기로 결심을 햇다.
이적까지 창순과 기화는 야학을 하면 새끼를 못 꼬게 될 줄만 생각햇섯지만 여러 날 두고 생각하든 끝에 「짚을 야학에 가지고 와서 배우면서 새끼를 꼬게 하자꾸나」하는 창순의 말에 기화도 「그게 참 좋다. 그렇게 하자」하고 눌러 야학을 세우기로 하엿뉀¤. 배울 사람은 짚을 야학에 가지고 와서 공부하기 전 한시간 새끼를 꼬고 두 시간쯤 공부 한 후 또 한시간 새끼를 꼬게 하엿다. 칠판 백묵 그 외 비용은 창순과 기화가 담당하고 불땔 나무는 야학생이 부담하기로 하고 장소는 서당을 이용하기로 하엿다.

기억에 남은 송별회
姜白南

태백산 남쪽 낙동강 상류 白鷺窺魚型의 기묘한 동리가 하나잇다. 이 동리는 內明學敎 소재지인 乃梅 동리다. 거기서 距今 십 수년 전(1919)에 조그만한 餞別會가 개최되엇는대 모인 사람은 남녀노소 모도가 200명에 불과하엿다. 餞別式 프로그람이 餞別詞에 당하여 말이 중단되자 會席은 울음의 홍수가 쏘다젓다. 그 곧을 아니 떠날 수 없게 된 나는 답사의 말을 몯하고 눈물노 餞別會는 끝을 맷게 되엇다. 나는 죄를 짓고 정배가는 것도 아니고 사형선고를 받고 교수대로 나가는 것도 물론 아니겟지마는 10년이라고 하는 젊고도 긴 세월에 內明學敎 교장과 訓導로 근무하든 동안에 교통이 불편하고 문화가 미개하야 문맹에 해매이는 이 동리 민중의 情形을 보고 야학을 개설하고(1909) 그들의 장래를 개척하기 시작하엿다. 문자에 굶주리고 지식에 목이 마른 대중은 너도나도 앞을 닷토아 입학함에 內明學敎 학생을 제하고는 거이다 입학하게 되어서 도저히 일부 교수는 할 수 없어서 하오 5시브터 6시까지 부인들과 소녀들에게 위선 한글 초보과 산술 초보와 쉬운 한자를 교수하고 동 8시브터 9시까지는 청년들에게 같은 과목으로 교수하고 장노년들에게는 무시로 틈나는 때에 신문 잡지 읽어들니기와 보통 상식담 같은 것으로 과목을 정하고 餞別 당시까지 일관하여서 그 동리에 居生하든 이로서는 신문 한 장 읽지 몯할 남녀 청소년은 한 사람도 없이되리만치 문맹퇴치가 되엇다. 나는 그들의 문자 알아 가는 것을 유일한 낙을 삼아 동족의 부활운동이라고 생각하야 1푼의 보수도 없이 그들과 동고동락을 하엿다. 그러함으로 그들의 사정을 알고 비밀이 없는 친구가 되엇다. 그들은 불꽃같은 뜨거운 폭양에 살을 데워가며서 김매는 것과 살을 찌르 는 듯한 치위에 나무지는 것이며 여름 젊은 밤에 밤을 새여가며서 졸음을 이기고 길삼방적하기와 비 맞은 푸나무로 보리쌀 끄리기에 비지땀을 흘리는 그들의 苦勞를 알고 친밀하게 되어 천진시러운 그들의 끝 업는 사랑을 받게 되엇다. 7, 8월쯤이면 연중행사처럼 호박떡과 옥수수는 여학생들의 책보 속에서 물고기는 논매고 오는 학생에게서 참외와 복수아는 받매고 오는 학생에게서 받앗다. 그 물질의 가치는 고사하고 그것을 가저다주는 남녀 야학생들의 지성시러운 붉은 마음이야 실로 비할대 없는 寶貝이엇다. 그리하야 그 송별회의 기억은 내 일생에 잊처지지 안는 사건을 지금까지 머리에 선하게 떠오른는 것이다.
