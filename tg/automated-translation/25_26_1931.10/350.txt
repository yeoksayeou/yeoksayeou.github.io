기사제목	負傷兵, 이러나라 이러나서 영접을 받으라 (Wounded Soldier, Rise Up, Rise and Receive Your Welcome)
필자	李揆元
기사형태	시 (Poem)

Wounded Soldier, Rise Up, Rise and Receive Your Welcome
						Yi Kyu-wŏn

Like a mind standing on the scaffold, with spotless absorbent cotton,
The boiling, boiling blood of sacrifice pushes forth,
And on the lifeless arm, like a broken gun,
A miserable strip of bandage is wound this way and that.
O wounded soldier, beloved brother,
The murderous roar of guns has ceased, and the soul-lost advance rests,
But how long will you lie upon the bed?

While trembling nervously on the passing battlefield,
Taking great breaths as limbs snapped,
Carrying the people's fragile lives on your back like a cliff's edge,
You dashed into the battlefield like pouring rain.
O soldier, beloved brother,
The flock of merciful doves flutters its wings and circles away,
But how long will you caress the medicine bottle?

The solemn victory song fills every alley,
And the flag of victory flutters in the gentle breeze.
To the sound of steam whistles tearing silk from port to port,
Countless people blow trumpets to welcome you.
O wounded soldier, beloved friend,
The ship carrying your bed has reached the pier,<97>
Why do you not wish to receive the glorious laurel crown?

The light dims in the eyes of the mother searching for her son,
The voice of the child calling for his father has turned to weeping.
A crazed wife frantically runs out onto the sea,
And friends call out your name at the top of their voices.
O wounded soldier, beloved brother,
Without looking even once before your gaze darkens,
Why do you only utter a desolate scream?
-1931. 8. 15-<98>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Kyu-wŏn addresses a severely wounded soldier lying unresponsive on his sickbed. While victory celebrations erupt around him, with cheering crowds, victory songs, and fluttering flags, the soldier remains immobile, trapped in his suffering. The poem contrasts the external glory and celebration with the soldier's internal pain and the grief of his family desperately trying to reach him. The speaker implores the soldier to rise and accept the honor he has earned through his sacrifice on the battlefield. From a historical perspective, the poem, written in 1931 during the Japanese colonial period, could implicitly reflect the sacrifices made by Koreans, potentially in independence struggles or conflicts they were forced into. The themes of uncelebrated sacrifice, the disconnect between individual suffering and public narratives of victory, and the lingering trauma of conflict would resonate deeply within the context of colonial oppression and resistance, making it a point of interest for historians studying the psychological and social impacts of the era.
