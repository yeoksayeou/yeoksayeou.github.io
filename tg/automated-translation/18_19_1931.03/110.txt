The Story of Mr. Anonymous
						Ch'unwŏn

One

Mr. Anonymous. It's not that he didn't have a name. It's just difficult to reveal his name due to various circumstances.
Since his name is not mentioned, there is no need to mention his hometown either. It is enough to know only that he was a Korean person.
Let's conveniently call him—the anonymous him—A.

There was a reason A ran away from his hometown at the age of seventeen. It was because he couldn't contain his anger upon learning that his father had been subjected to all kinds of severe torture by a provincial governor (감사) after being falsely accused of a crime, and that most of his property, which yielded thousands of *sŏk* (석) in harvest, had been confiscated.
At that time, the affairs of state were chaotic, and the M family clan (M氏 一族), which held political power at the time, occupied all the good government posts throughout the country, such as provincial governor and magistrate (목사), making it their business to arrest good people (량민) and seize their wealth. How many roof tiles (개와ㅅ장) on the grand houses in Seoul were not made from the blood of these plundered good people?

A fled to Japan and, after preparing for some time, entered the Army Officer Academy (陸軍士官學校) in Tokyo.
At that time, besides A, there were about a dozen other anonymous individuals studying abroad at the Army Officer Academy, such as B, C, D, E, and F. Their ages were mostly similar, and their motives for coming to Japan were largely the same. Although they now talked of the world and discussed the nation, their initial motivation for leaving home was mostly that they were children from families who had suffered injustices at the hands of powerful aristocratic families (권문세가), seeking to thoroughly avenge their grievances and make a splash. B had his ancestral burial grounds (선산) taken by a *yangban* (량반), C's father was humiliated by a *yangban*, D's father had his property confiscated by a *yangban*, and so on.
However, while attending the Army Officer Academy, they witnessed the spirit and patriotism of Japanese soldiers and abandoned their initial petty motives, coming to embrace grand ambitions concerning the world and the nation.

Two

The Russo-Japanese War broke out. It was just when A and the others had finished the Officer Academy and were serving as officer cadets (견습사관) in the Japanese army. One day, the regimental commander (련대장) of the regiment A was in summoned him and said,
“Mr. A. This morning, our regiment received deployment orders and is set to depart for Manchuria within twenty-four hours. As you are a foreigner, you have no obligation to deploy, so you are free to act as you wish.”
A did not hesitate <48>
“Regimental Commander, if possible, please take me to the front lines. I wish to see and learn how Japanese soldiers courageously sacrifice their lives for their country. Since I, your subordinate (소관), have joined the campaign, I intend to serve your country with the same loyalty as your soldiers. I wish to repay the kindness your country showed in educating us on this occasion.”
The regimental commander immediately praised his courage and permitted A's deployment.

Three

Besides A, there were four or five others who deployed to Manchuria with the Japanese army during the Russo-Japanese War. They served in the army with the exact same spirit as A. Individuals like A, B, and C even commanded units on the front lines. Thus, when the war ended and the Japanese army returned triumphantly (개선할 때에), A and the others returned with them and even received medals (훈장). Then, A, B, C, and a few others stayed in Seoul and served at the Japanese Army Command Stationed in Korea (韓國駐剳 日本軍司令部).
It goes without saying that the purpose of their studies was not to serve as officers in the Japanese army. However, at that time, Korea lacked an army capable of employing them.
It wasn't that there was no army, but the generals (장관), field officers (령관), and company-grade officers (귀관) in the army at that time were mostly young masters from *yangban* households—ornamental officers (화초 장교) who didn't even know basic commands like "Attention" (기착) or "Right Face" (우로 나라니).
The army existed not to maintain public order (치안) or repel foreign insults (외모), but merely as a spectacle (구경ㅅ거리) for His Majesty (상감님) and a place for idle children of *yangban* families to earn a living (밥벌이 판). While there might have been one or two soldierly individuals among them, such people were instead treated poorly and could not fulfill their potential.
Moreover, it was a time when students (생도) returning from Japan were all regarded as adherents of the revolutionary thought (혁명사상) of the Kim Okkyun (김옥균) and Pak Yŏnghyo (박영효) faction, viewed with hostility and suspicion by important high officials (요로 대관) and *yangban*. Because of this situation, even though A, B, C, and others, originally commoners from the countryside, had completed excellent military training, there was nowhere to employ them, so they remained serving as officer cadets in the Japanese army.

Four

When A returned to his home country, he found the affairs of state in utter chaos (억망). While external forces (밖앝 세력) pushed in like the tide, the *yangban* holding power in the government were too busy biting and tearing at each other in power struggles to spare any thought for anything else.
A, along with his comrades B, C, D, E, F, etc., often gathered at brothels or gisaeng houses (청루주사), drinking until dawn and lamenting loudly (통곡하야) to forget the discontent welling up in their chests.
It was at this time.
A, carrying a revolver (륙혈포) on his person, visited the house of × Boguk (×보국).
× Boguk came from a powerful family (세도집) and was also one of the wealthiest men in Korea (조선 일부); truly, wealth and honor were his in equal measure (부귀가 쌍전하엿다).
An unexpected visit from a young officer—× Boguk could not refuse this Japanese officer. Why? Because after the Russo-Japanese War, even ministers in Korea at the time trembled before Japanese soldiers.
A respectfully bowed to × Boguk, showing courtesy (례) due to an elder.
× Boguk helped the inexplicably visiting young officer rise. Anxiety filled ×'s aged face (낱). <49>
“Your Excellency, do you recognize me?”
The young officer A began to speak.
“How would I know Your Lordship (령감)?”
× Boguk looked intently at A.
After a moment, × Boguk's complexion turned ashen (흙빛). Why? Because in A's face, × Boguk saw the visage of A's father, whom he had subjected to every cruel torture, leaving him half-dead (반생반사).
Seeing × Boguk's face turn ashen, A said,
“Now, Your Excellency, do you know who I am? My father, who became an invalid (페인이 되엇던) after suffering all the tortures Your Excellency inflicted, passed away less than a year later. His final dying wish (유언한 말) was for me to avenge him. The reason I studied for these ten-odd years was to avenge my father. Now that I have met Your Excellency today, know that your fate ends today.”
He pulled out a gleaming revolver from his military uniform trousers pocket (포켙) and aimed it at × Boguk's chest.
Caught unexpectedly, × Boguk pressed his palms together like a monk chanting prayers (례불하는 중 모양으로), looked up at A's sharp eyes, and trembled like a man standing naked outdoors (한데) in the harsh winter wind at the end of the eleventh lunar month (오동지말 설한풍에 벌거벗고 한데에 선 사람 모양으로), pleading, “Your Lordship! Your Lordship! Please wait just a moment. I will promptly return the assets taken from your late father, with interest added, so please just spare this old man's life.”
“Do you truly repent (뉘우치시오) your past wrongdoings?”
A glared at ×.
“I have long repented (뉘우친지는 오래외다).”
“Then, as proof of your repentance, will you do as I say?”
“Of course! If you just spare my life, I will do anything.”
A put the revolver back.
“If I were to take money from Your Excellency now, it would be for my own selfish desires (사욕), which is not something a man of character (대장부) should do. Your Excellency's wealth all belongs to the people. Will you release it, firstly, to establish schools and promote education; secondly, to help poor patriots (지사들) so they can work for the country without worry; and thirdly, to select bright young people (총준자제) and send them to study abroad (류학) to cultivate (양성하도록) talent for the nation?”
“I will simply do as Your Lordship instructs. I will establish a school immediately, starting tomorrow. As for poor patriots, I do not know any, so if Your Lordship introduces them, I will look after their needs as much as possible. Also, regarding students studying abroad, if Your Lordship recommends them, I will send them.”
“Since there will be no witness later to what we two have discussed alone, please write down those words now on paper (조희에) in your own hand (친필), sign and seal (서명 날인하시고) it, and also have your son sign and seal it.”
× Boguk pulled over paper, brush, and ink (지필믁).
Kwangmu (光武) XX Year, X Month, X Day, Regarding Instructions Given to Mr. A
One. Matter of Establishing Schools
One. Matter of Assisting Patriots with Stipends
One. Matter of Dispatching Bright Youth Abroad for Study
XOO Seal
Son's Name Seal
A took this pledge (다짐) and put it away.
“If Your Excellency fulfills this pledge exactly, you will surely receive the reverence (숭앙을<50>) of the entire nation's people. However, if you break this pledge, know that A's sword and revolver will always be hanging over Your Excellency's head.”
And with that, he left × Boguk's house.
After that, A never visited × Boguk's house again, but × Boguk, true to his promise to A, first established a school. And although this happened several years later, when A left government service and engaged in political party activities (정당운동), many impoverished patriots lived off A's support, and word has it that some of that money came from × Boguk. Also, certain students studying abroad were dispatched to Japan, America, and Europe under × Boguk's name. This × Boguk incident is the first anecdote (삽화) in the eventful ten years of A's subsequent life. (Continued in the next issue) <51>

**Summary**

This first installment introduces "A," a young Korean man whose father was ruined by a corrupt official ("× Boguk") under the politically chaotic late Chosŏn/Korean Empire period. Seeking revenge and empowerment, A and other similarly wronged youths go to Japan to attend the Army Officer Academy. They gain military experience, witness Japanese patriotism, and fight in the Russo-Japanese War, returning to Korea disillusioned with the corrupt and ineffective Korean government and military elite. A confronts × Boguk, forcing him at gunpoint not to return the stolen wealth personally, but to pledge in writing to use it for public good: establishing schools, supporting patriots, and funding overseas study.

The article is interesting for its portrayal of early 20th-century Korean nationalist sentiment arising from disillusionment with the traditional elite and admiration for Japanese military discipline/patriotism, even while seeking redress for grievances caused by Korean officials. Historians might find valuable the depiction of Koreans training in the Japanese military, participating in the Russo-Japanese war on the Japanese side, the motivations (personal revenge evolving into national concern), the critique of the Korean ruling class's corruption and incompetence, and the early ideas about national strengthening through education and supporting activists, framed within a fictional narrative possibly reflecting real sentiments or experiences of the time. The specific demands A makes of the corrupt official (schools, stipends for activists, study abroad) reflect common reformist goals.

無名氏傳
						春園
一
무명씨. 그에게도 명씨가 없을 리는 없다. 여러 가지 사정으로 그의 이름을 내놓기가 어려운 것 뿐이다.
이미 이름을 말하지 아니하니 그의 고향을 말할 필요도 없을 것이다. 다만 그가 조선ㅅ사람이엇던 것만 알면 고만이다.
그-무명씨인 그를 편의상 A라고 부르자.
A가 열일곱 살 되던 해에 그의 고향을 뛰어난 것은 까닭이 잇다.-아버지가 애매한 죄에 몰려서 감사 모에게 가진 악형을 당하고 수천석 타작하던 재산의 대부분을 빼앗긴 것을 알게 되매 분을 참지 못한 것이엇다.
그 때에는 나라 정사가 어지러워서 당시 정권을 잡앗던 M씨 일족이 감사요 목사요 하고 전국에 좋은 벼슬을 다 차지해 가지고 량민을 잡아 들여서는 재물을 빼앗기를 업을 삼을 때다. 서울에 큼직 큼직한 집의 개와ㅅ장이 이러케 빼앗아 올린 량민의 피 아닌 것이 얼마나 되나.
A는 일본으로 뛰어가서 얼마ㅅ동안 준비를 해 가지고 동경의 륙군사관학교에 입학하엿다.
그때 륙군 사관학교에는 A밖에 B, C, D, E, F의 무명씨들이 십여인이나 류학을 하고 잇엇다. 그들은 대개 나이가 비등하고 또 일본에 온 동긔도 대동소이하엿다. 지금은 비록 천하를 말하고 국가를 론하지 마는 애초에 집을 떠난 동긔는 대개는 권문세가에 원통한 일을 당한 집 자제로서 한번 톡톡히 원수를 갚고 설치를 하자는 것이엇다. B는 량반에게 선산을 빼앗겻고 C는 그 아버지가 량반에게 수모를 당하엿고 D는 그 아버지가 량반에게 재산을 빼앗겻고 등등.
그러나 그들의 륙군사관학교에 다니는 동안에 일본 군인의 의긔와 애국심을 보고는 처음 오던 조고마한 동긔를 버리고 천하, 국가를 경륜하고 큰 뜻을 품게 되엇다.

二
일로전쟁이 터지엇다. 때는 마츰 A씨등이 사관학교를 마추고 견습사관으로 일본 군대에 잇을 때다. 하로는 A가 잇는 련대의 련대장이 A를 불러
『A군. 오늘 아츰 우리 련대는 출정명령을 받아서 이십사시간 내로 만주를 향하고 떠나게 되엇소. 그대는 외국 사람이니 출정할 의무도 잇지 아니 한즉 행동을 자유로 할 것이오』
하엿다.
A는 서슴지 아니하고＜48＞
『련대장, 될 수만 잇거든 나를 전지로 다리고 가 주시오. 일본군인이 어떠케 충용하게 나라를 위해서 목슴을 바리는 양을 보고 배우려 합니다. 소관도 종군한 이상에는 귀국 군인과 꼭같은 충성으로 귀국을 도으려 합니다. 이번 긔회에 귀국에서 우리를 교육해 주신 은혜를 갚으려 합니다」
하엿다.
련대장은 곳 그 용긔를 칭찬하고 A의 출정을 허락하엿다.

三
