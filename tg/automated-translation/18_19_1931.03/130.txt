**Body Text Translation**
Turncoat! Begone, A Letter of Severance from a Young Fighter Wife to Her Turncoat Husband
						Kim Haegang

1
What did you tell us?
-Until the day victory comes to us
-Let us fight persistently. Let us not retreat.

2
After that, what else did you say?
-Even if the eighteen hundred comrades fall as one mass
-Let us not break our will. Let us not turn coat.

3
And that night, what did you whisper to me?
-You are not just my wife, but our steadfast comrade.
-Be brave. With utmost bravery, let us not turn coat. Let us not distort our work.

4
Thus, you buried warm breath in our chests,
Thus, you made thick pulses beat in my frail arms,
You were my magnificent husband, our brilliant leader.

5
However. Darling! Your great head,
Why is it the very first to bow?
Falling for the coaxing of a few bastards, breaking your will and retreating—what face is this?

6
Now, the eighteen hundred comrades
Have not eaten for five days,
Like beasts raging in the yard, they endure and roar – do you not see?

7
It's dirty! For the sake of filling their own few bellies,
Selling out comrades, abandoning righteousness,
You, leaving the hearts of eighteen hundred, are a hateful turncoat. A despicable coward.

8
Go. Go well.
Just because a few turncoats emerged, there is nothing to be ashamed of in our work.
Our shoulders, becoming ever firmer—only the insignificant are being cast off.

9
-Let us fight persistently. Let us not retreat.(?)
-Let us not break our will. Let us not turn coat.(?)
-Be brave. With utmost bravery, let us not turn coat. Let us not distort our work.(?)<52>

10
Hmph! Was the sharp-edged spirit of yesterday but a few fragments of a pale skull?
Tsk! You coward kneeling and stretching your neck! Turncoat!
Like the setting moon tonight, I hang the two words "husband" on your shoulders; carry them well and go.
-1931.1- <53>

**Summary**

This poem, presented as a "Letter of Severance," is a fierce denunciation by a wife against her husband who has betrayed a collective cause, likely a labor strike or independence movement involving 1,800 people enduring hardship ("five days without food"). The wife, identifying herself as a "fighter" and "comrade," contrasts the husband's past inspirational leadership and vows with his current cowardice and betrayal for personal gain. She declares their relationship over, dismissing him as a "turncoat" and "coward," while affirming the unwavering resolve of the remaining comrades. From a historical perspective, the poem is significant for its portrayal of female agency and political consciousness within resistance movements during the colonial period. It highlights the internal pressures, sacrifices, and potential for betrayal faced by activists, offering a raw emotional glimpse into the personal cost of political commitment and the ideological conflicts within such movements.

變節者여! 가라, 變節者인 남편에게 주는 투사인 젊은 안해의 絶綠狀
						金海剛
1
당신은 우리에게 무어라 일러 주엇읍니까?
-우리에게 승리가 오는 날까지
-긔차게 싸워가자. 물러나지 말자.

2
당신은 그 뒤 또 무어라 말슴햇읍니까?
-일천팔백의 무리가 한 덩이로 넘어질지언정
-뜻을 꺾지 말자. 변절을 말자.

3
그리고 그날밤 나에게 무어라 속삭엿읍니까?
-그대는 나의 안해라기 보다 든든한 우리의 동지다.
-용감하라. 끝장 용감함으로 변절을 말자. 일을 비뚤임이 없게 하자.

4
이러틋 우리의 가슴에 더운 호흡을 묻어주고
이러틋 연약한 나의 팔에 굵은 맥박을 뛰게 하든
당신은 훌륭한 나의 남편이요, 총명한 우리의 리-더-엿읍니다.

5
하거늘. 여보! 당신의 커단 머리가,
맨 먼첨 숙으러진다는 것은 무엇이오?
멫놈의 꼬임에 들어 뜻을 꺾고 물러서다니 무슨 낯짝이란 말이오?

6
지금 일천팔백의 무리는
다섯날째 먹지를 못하고
사장에 날뛰는 맹수처럼 버티고 포효하는 것을 - 보지 못하오?

7
더럽소! 제 멫놈의 뱃장을 채우기 위하야
동지를 팔고, 의리를 버리고,
일천팔백의 가슴에서 떠나는 당신은 증오할 변절자외다. 타기할 비겁자외다.

8
가오. 좋게 가오.
멫 놈의 변절자를 내엇다구 우리 일에 부끄릴 것은 없소.
더욱 더- 아구 찰 우리의 어깨는 OO는 OO가 떨구잇을 뿐이오.

9
-긔차게 싸워나가자. 물러나지 말자.(?)
-뜻을 꺾지 말자. 변절을 말자.(?)
-용감하라. 끝장 용감함으로 변절을 말자. 일에 비뚤임임이 없게 하자.(?)＜52＞

10
흥! 어제날 서슬이 파라튼 긔염은 부서진 멫쪽의 파리한 해골이엇드냐?
췟 무릅꿇고, 목을 느리는 비겁한 자여! 변절자여!
이 밤에 지는 달과 같이, 남편이란 두 글자를 당신의 어깨에 걸처 주노니 잘 지니고 갑소.
-1931.1- ＜53＞
