Magazine Title: 동광 제2호
Publication Date: 1926-06-01
Article Title: 스켓취(渡美 途中에서) (Sketch (On the Way to America))
Author: 任英彬
Article Type: 회고·수기 (Memoir/Personal Account)

Sketch
						Im Yŏngbin
I am not writing this sketch as a guide or a report. This is intended to record all the feelings that shocked my Feeling—long and short, good and unpleasant, interesting and dull—from the time I left for America until I return, whenever that may be, some years later.
It is difficult to guarantee whether my writing ability can maintain such health to continue steadily, and even if I could guarantee my writing ability, it is questionable whether the magazine publishing this piece can maintain such health. The magazine itself may have the strength to maintain its health, but being born on barren soil, there is concern that its health might not avoid harm. The reason we often announce the end just as we begin is partly due to inherent forces (原力), but isn't a greater reason the barrenness of the soil from which we draw nourishment?
However, although I don't know when or how things will turn out, I will do my best with sincerity until then. If it doesn't work out despite my sincerity, then my regret will be lessened. Sincerity it is—.

Departure
Did the fact of departing arise because the word 'depart' exists? Or did the word 'depart' arise because the fact of departing exists? Probably, the fact existed first, and the word arose to represent that fact. Whatever the word, the fact itself remains always solemn and cannot be deceived.
If, somehow, the word 'meeting,' which we currently regard most favorably, were used to represent the fact of <52> departing, and the word 'departing,' which we currently dislike the most, were used to represent the fact of meeting, wouldn't we have rejoiced upon hearing the disliked word 'departing' and felt displeasure upon hearing the favored word 'meeting'? Therefore, things become what they are not because of words, but because of facts. Even the most cunning, deceitful, and vain person cannot help but bow their head before facts, becoming diligent, honest, and truthful without being able to resist. Fact! It is always solemn and cannot be deceived.
I tried to deceive the fact of departing. I tried to avoid it. But that fact reached me without the slightest mercy. It became utterly unavoidable.
Why did this fact arise? Why did this fact of departing, which can neither be deceived nor avoided, come about? Why can't people always gather together and live harmoniously, but must always be threatened by this fact of departing? People who are departing always say they must do business, must study, must be active; why can't they do business, study, or be active without departing?
Is this flaw only present in today's society? Is it the terrifying fate that befell the descendants of man after the Tower of Babel? In a world constantly intertwined with departures and meetings, 'meeting implies parting' (合則離) has become known as an immutable truth. Is it really so?
I have encountered this fact four times. These four times are not the number I've encountered in my life so far, but the number I encountered on my journey to America. The first was departing from relatives and old friends in Seoul (京城), the second was departing from the rivers and mountains of my homeland in Pusan (부산), the third was departing from the landscapes of Japan in Kobe (神戶), and the fourth, though still Japanese soil, was departing from the Orient (東洋) in Yokohama (橫濱).
It is the morning of March 8th. At Kyŏngsŏng Station, there were about ten people facing this fact of departing. They were experiencing a strange feeling while looking into the harsh face of this fact. A strange feeling. I call it a strange feeling because it was a mixture of sadness, regret, and something indefinable.
As the electric bell faintly <53> trailed off its sound, and the engine began to vigorously puff smoke and roll, this fact of departing was not merely confronting them, but it shot an arrow into their hearts. Even those whose affection was not so deep must have felt a sharp pang at the moment that arrow struck. They couldn't have helped but feel it.
Departed! I sat down in the train compartment. The image of the white handkerchiefs they last waved is vivid in my eyes, and then all their faces appear clearly. Their hats, the clothes they wore, the shoes they had on appear visible. Their gestures, their voices also appear. And then, the times I spent with them appear, one by one, as if drawn. Even people I wasn't very close to become deeply missed. I even feel regret towards anyone I might have treated coldly. I feel sorry for anyone I might have wronged. Only after being struck by the arrow of this fact of departing does the affection for people emerge. Only after being struck by the arrow of this fact of departing do I understand the true taste of fellowship and the saying 'to be on good terms' (誼조타). Only after being struck by the arrow of this fact of departing do I realize that disliked people and hateful people disappear. Ah, departure is regrettable, yet through departure, I first learn the meaning of regret. If one felt the preciousness as keenly as at the time of departure, one would never have harmed feelings with disputes while together. Departure! You are something else! As much as you give sorrow to life, you also give life its profound taste! Ah, departure!
The train runs. People get on and off. But there is not a single one of my dear family elders or friends I used to play with. They exist only within the imaginary world of this small heart. Only in that world are they and I intertwined. The train rushes towards new land. But this heart is split in half. One half in Seoul, one half in the new land ahead.
Dusk approaches. Upon the mountains and rivers of Chosŏn, upon the villages of Chosŏn. Bare mountains, houses in miserable condition, people lacking vitality—yet they are all just missed. And why do those things, fading into the dimming light, look so sad and sorrowful?
When I think of departing from all these things of Chosŏn, even a collapsing hovel looks dear. In ordinary times, though Korean myself, <54> I often used contemptuous words about Koreans and lamented their lack of hope. But when I suddenly leave behind the mountains and rivers fading powerlessly into the dimming light, there is nothing that isn't missed, nothing that doesn't make me feel gentle. And this land like a virgin, this land like a bird that has never flown—if hope were attached, how much hope would there be?
Ah, the land where my ancestors are buried, the land where I spent my twenty-five years; it fades into the dimming light and I must leave it behind. The desolate mountains and fields of winter dormancy impart an even heavier feeling to this heart.
I boarded the ferry in Pusan. This opens the curtain on not being able to tread the soil of my homeland for who knows how many years. And leaving this land that held my body so well, whether bitter or sweet, no matter how much others say the country I'm going to is like paradise, how hard it will be to support this body in a strange and harsh place, and how profound the loneliness will be?
But alas, that dear land is buried in darkness and cannot be seen well! It looks like a canvas recklessly smeared thick with black paint by a brush, with only the electric lights shining like dots. I want to see it. Ah, in that blackness, my dear compatriots dressed in white will be eating dinner, working, and talking. I want to see them clearly one last time! And I want to ask them to be open enough to fully console the heartache I feel upon departing now, when I return.
There were many of our laborers on the ferry. It was good to see them, but their appearance, being driven away, looks pitiful and sad no matter how you look at it. They are pushed off to a foreign land, unable even to sign their names (記姓名). They helplessly leave their beloved hometown mountains and rivers to fill the places that others also left because they couldn't live there. They too must have been threatened by the fact of departing. And they must have firmly resolved, when facing that threat, to come back after earning a thousand won or so. Ah, pitiful multitude!
I saw the landscape of Japan for the second time. Four years ago, I saw it for the first time harboring great ambitions, and this time too, I see it for the second time, harboring what I consider great ambitions. Bamboo groves and citrus trees (蜜柑樹) speak of the warm country's charm. <55> My eyes, weary from yesterday's full day of winter dormancy's desolation, feel refreshed by the grassy green plains tinged with spring colors. Yesterday's displeasure from seeing irregular fields, streets, and villages has turned into the pleasure of seeing neat and orderly (綺麗하고 井井한) fields, streets, and villages. However, yesterday's weariness arose from virgin land, and yesterday's displeasure arose from immaturity. That too, if it opens up, grows, and matures in the future, will not be shameful before anyone. Ah, open up! Grow! Mature! Steadily and strongly.
After completing various troublesome procedures, I threw myself onto the large ship, the Taiyō Maru (大洋丸) of the Toyo Kisen Kaisha (東洋汽船주식회사), making its final voyage to America, around 10 AM on March 17, 1926. On the chaotic and noisy pier, two or three brothers residing in Kobe who came to see me off stood amidst the fluttering red, blue, white, and yellow ribbons, also waving the ribbon we had exchanged. As the departure time drew near, the inside of the ship quieted down, while only the deck and the pier became boisterous. The number of ribbons gradually increased. The sentiment of trying to keep the connection, even if only by a string, from breaking, out of reluctance to part, was touching. The wind, seemingly ignorant of such feelings, blew recklessly, snapping those poignant strings one after another.
The ship blew its whistle, the engine started turning, and as the water churned violently, the hull began to move, slowly leaving the pier. As the ship moved away, all those poignant ribbons snapped, and the two broken ends of the strings—blue, yellow, white, and red—fluttered pitifully in the wind. At that point, everyone discarded them. Then, blue, yellow, white, and red strings floated bobbing on the sea, pushed here and there by the waves. People waved hats and handkerchiefs, expressing ever more reluctance to part. Even when the pier was quite distant and it became impossible to distinguish who was standing there, they continued to wave handkerchiefs and hats at each other.
I was the only passenger [of my group] on this ship, receiving a warm send-off from two or three affectionate brothers. I had tried to make a companion by urging Mr. Kim (金군) to take this ship, and he came as far as Kobe, only for me to end up going alone—a heartless outcome. Mr. Kim failed the physical examination and was pushed back to the next ship.
If those two or three brothers from Kobe hadn't <56> come to see me off, I would have stood huddled alone in a corner, bidding farewell to those mountains and rivers without sorrow, while others loudly lamented their painful parting griefs (離恨). How lonely would I, walking alone with Korean words and Korean gait amidst those speaking clipped words and walking with abrupt steps, have been without the warm send-off from those two or three brothers?
When the ribbon I held jointly with those brothers snapped and fluttered away, I felt a sharper pang in my heart than in Seoul or Pusan. And my eyes stung, and trying to hold back the welling tears, I looked away from my brothers' faces. The ship moved far out, and the mountains and rivers became barely visible, fading into the distance.
—Ah, I'm finally on the boat!
I thought, and dejectedly (悄然히) went down to the third-class cabin. From that moment, I couldn't speak a single word of Korean. They were all Japanese. They were people going to make money, saying America is better for earning money than Japan. On the ferry, I had traveled with people coming [to Japan] saying Japan was better because they couldn't live in Chosŏn; this time, I traveled with people going [to America] saying America was better because they couldn't make money in Japan. So, which group is destined to live better? And people from America also come flocking back, saying Japan or Chosŏn is better for making money—how should this confusing situation (襄 판세) be understood correctly? Although Mencius (孟子) scoffed at King Xiang of Liang's (梁襄王) question, "Under whom will the realm be settled?" (天下는 烏乎定고), isn't that question always worth asking? Ah, this world turned upside down by profit (利)! This situation where people go anywhere as long as there's profit! Fights and calamities all stem solely from profit. Ah, under whom will the realm be settled?
We docked at Yokohama (橫濱). I went to Tokyo (동경) hoping to meet someone I knew, but it was completely different from four years ago, and things were hazy (依稀한 것), making it hard to find my way. So, after acting like a fool for a while, I gave up finding them and returned to Yokohama to sleep on the ship. The next day (잇흔날), I just looked around Yokohama. The remaining scars (瘡痍) from the terrifying earthquake and fire lingered everywhere, showing glimpses of past dreams.
The ship departed Yokohama at noon on the 20th, amid falling snow. This was the fourth time I faced the fact of departing, this time leaving the Orient (東洋).
With this departure, I came to actually tread upon and see the Pacific Ocean (태평양), which I had only seen on maps. And I definitively became someone going to play the role of a guest in a country whose customs and habits conflict with <57> all those of the Orient. Though East Asians differ in language and clothing, they possess a commonality that allows for mutual exchange. They envy and fight each other, but as East Asians, their sentiments are the same. I am leaving. Leaving this Orient!
Now that I've left, I miss it; why did I needlessly argue this way and that when I was there? How long can meetings last, how long can partings last? Saying that time is short, taking this side or that side (욋족 뱃족하니)—perhaps shallow things are contemptible (웃수업나) to the human heart!
Carrying endless thoughts (회포) in my chest, crossing the pitch-black (깜아케) deep Pacific, my mind broadens and my body grows larger! What lies ahead? Look at those waves!
Thus aboard the ship, the scenes of the past three years spent striving to reach this point naturally appear vividly before my eyes.
If I had devoted three years of single-minded effort solely to preparing for America, my current path might not be considered so strange. If I had spent three years attached to a Westerner as a personal secretary, erasing my name and traces, solely with the mindset of 'As long as you don't kill me, use me as you will, just send me to America,' wouldn't anyone consider it more than natural?
I see my past self pitifully yet admirably, wandering the streets of Seoul, striving to save even one more penny while receiving a meager salary (박봉) for three years preparing travel expenses. As a result, I am going to America, but it has made me into this kind of miserly person.
I see my former self again, striving—'Let's save even one more penny!'—while wandering around Seoul.
I boarded the ship. Unless it sinks (破船), I am inevitably going to America. Ah, seeing it this way, the three years of hardship have become a success. Am I happy (깃브냐)? Am I sad (슯흐냐)?
For three years, I was afraid it wouldn't succeed; now, I am anxious about how things will turn out (엇지나 될가?). The fear during those three years was easy to dispel, but when will this current anxiety be resolved? If human life is a terrifying adventure, then I am embarking on the most severe adventure among terrifying adventures. How will it turn out?
The ship pushes forward, parting the waters of the vast Pacific Ocean. The sound of the engine is distinctly audible. And I stand alone on the third-class deck, getting wet in the rain. Listening, as if in a dream, to the sound of large and small waves striking the ship's side.
Ah, is this a dream? I feel as if I am dreaming (꿈뀌는 듯). March 22, 1926 <58>

**Summary**

This memoir by Im Yŏngbin recounts the emotional turmoil and philosophical reflections experienced while departing Korea for America in 1926. He describes the pain of four distinct departures – from Seoul, Pusan, Kobe, and finally Yokohama, leaving the Orient. He contemplates the nature of parting versus meeting, the power of facts over words, and the deep sense of loss and longing for his homeland and connections, which intensify upon leaving. He observes fellow travelers, poor Korean laborers heading to Japan and Japanese heading to America, reflecting on the global pursuit of economic betterment (利) and questioning where true stability lies. The journey itself, the culmination of three years of difficult saving, brings both a sense of accomplishment and profound anxiety about the unknown future in America. This article offers valuable insight into the personal experiences and complex emotions of a Korean intellectual emigrating during the colonial period, highlighting the sorrow of leaving Chosŏn, the comparison with Japan, the observation of labor migration, and the existential uncertainty faced when venturing into the West. Historians might find the candid reflections on national identity, colonial realities (contrasting Korea and Japan), motivations for emigration (personal ambition vs. economic desperation), and the psychological impact of severing ties particularly interesting.

스켓취
						任英彬
나는 이 스켓취를 案內記나 보고서로 적는 것이 안이다. 이것은 내가 미국에 가려고 떠난 때부터 멧 해 뒤던지 돌아올 때까지 내 Feeling을 Shock한 길고 짧고, 조코 언잔코, 재미잇고 심심한 모든 늣김을 적으랴고 하는 것이다.
나의 필력이 그러케 능히 건강을 보전하야 꾸준히 내댈런지 담보하기가 어려우며, 내 필력을 담보한다션 치더라도 이 글을 실을 잡지가 그러케 능히 건강을 보전하야줄런지 의문이다. 잡지 자체는 능히 건강을 보전할 힘이 잇슬런지 몰으지만은 척박한 토지에 생긴 것이라 그 건강이 해를 밧지 안이할지 염려가 되지 안는 것이 안이다. 우리는 시작과 마찬가지로 종말을 고하는 것을 하게 되는 것은 原力도 까닭은 까닭이지만은 양분을 취할 토지가 척박한 것이 더 큰 까닭이 안인가 한다.
그러치만은 언제 어찌될 지 몰으지만은 그때까지는 정성을 다하야 해 볼 것이다. 정성으로 하다가 안되면 그 때에는 그리 마음에 미타함은 적을 것이다. 정성이다-.

떠남
떠난다는 말이 잇서서 떠난다는 사실이 생겻느냐? 떠난다는 사실이 잇서서 떠난다는 말이 생겻느냐? 아미도 사실이 먼저 잇섯슬 것이고, 그 사실을 대표하노라고 말이 생겻슬 것이다. 말이야 엇더튼지 사실은 늘 엄연하야 쇡이지 못할 것이다.
만일 엇지되여서 지금 우리가 제일 조케 녁이는 맛는다는 말로 이 떠난다는 사실을＜52＞ 대표하게 하고, 지금 우리가 제일 실케 여기는 떠난다는 말로 저 만난다는 사실을 대표하게 하엿더면 도리혀 실케 녁이던 떠난다는 말을 듯고 조하하엿슬 것이요 조케 녁이던 만난다는 말을 듯고 실혀하엿슬 것이다. 그러니 말 때문에 무엇이 되는 것이 안이고, 사실 때문에 무엇이 되는 것이다. 맘 압헤서는 극히 교활, 간사, 허망한 사람이라도 사실 압헤서는 제가 꼼작할 수 업시 머리를 숙이고 誠勤, 정직, 진실하지 안을 수가 업슬 것이다. 사실! 그것은 늘 엄연하야 속일 수 업는 것이다.
나는 떠난다는 사실을 속이려고 하야보앗다. 회피하려고하야 보앗다. 그러나 그 사실은 조곰도 가차가 업시 내게 당도하고 말엇다. 고만 회피 不得하게 되고 말엇다.
웨 이 사실이 생겻느냐? 워 이러케 속일 수도 업고 회피할 수도 업는 떠난다는 사실이 생겻느냐? 웨 사람들은 늘 한데 모혀서 단란히 살 수가 업고 늘 이 떠난다는 사실에 위협을 바다야 하겟느냐? 떠나는 사람들은 늘 말이 사업을 하여야겟네, 공부를 하여야겟네, 활동을 하여야겟네 하니 웨 떠나지 안코는 사업을 못하고, 공부를 못하고, 활동을 못하느냐?
이것이 결함만은 지금 사회에서니까 그러냐! 바벨탑 뒤에 사람의 자식들에게 온 무서운 운명이냐? 늘 떠나고 맛나는 일로 교착한 세상에서는 合則離를 불가변의 진리로 알게 되엿다. 정말 그럴 것이냐?

3년이라는 세월을 일심정력을 들이여 미국갈 공부만 하엿다면 지금 내 길이 그리 이상한 길이라고 못할 것이다. 서양인의 개인 서기로 일홈을 뭇고 자취를 끈코 오직 그 서양인에게 당신이 나를 죽이지만 안코 부릴대로 부리고 미국만 보내주구려 하는 맘으로 3년을 지내어 이 길에 올랏다면 누구나 당연 이상에 당연으로 알 것이 아니냐?
나는 親友를 몰으고 집안을 몰으고 나까지 몰으고 3년 동안 박봉을 바드면서 여비 준비하든 꼴이 가엽게도 장하게도 보인다. 그 결과로 미국을 간다만은 이와 갓흔 인색한 사람을 만들어 노코만 것이다.
나는 서울 바닥을 헤매면서 한 푼이라도 더 모흐자-하고 애쓰는 예전 내 꼴이 다시금 보인다.
나는 배를 탓다. 破船만 아니하면 나는 별 수 업시 미국을 가게 되엇다. 아아 이러고 보니 3년의 고행은 성공이 되엿다. 깃브냐? 슯흐냐?
3년은 성공이 못될가봐 겁이 나더니 지금은 엇지나 될가?하야 두려웁다. 3년 동안 겁나든 것은 풀기 쉬운 겁이엿지만은 지금의 두려움은 언제나 풀 것이냐? 사람의 일생은 무서운 모험이라 하면 나는 그 무서운 모험 중에도 제일 심한 모험을 하러 떠나는 것이다. 엇지 될고?
배는 작고 태평양물을 헷치고 나아간다. 엔진 소리는 유심히 들린다. 그리고 나홈자 비를 마즈면서 3등 갑판에 서 잇다. 큰 물결 적은 물결 배ㅅ전을 따리는 소리를 꿈이런 듯 들으면서.
아아 이게 꿈이냐? 나는 꿈뀌는 듯 만하다. 1926. 3. 22 ＜58＞
