Article Title: 사공이여 우리 사공이여 (휘트맨의 링컨 추도 노래에서) (O Captain! My Captain! (From Whitman's Song Mourning Lincoln))
Author: Walt Whitman (Original), Translator Unidentified
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done,
The ship has weather’d every rack, the prize we sought is won,
The port is near, the bells I hear, the people all exulting,
While follow eyes the steady keel, the vessel grim and daring;
But O heart! heart! heart!
O the bleeding drops of red,
Where on the deck my Captain lies,
Fallen cold and dead.

O Captain! my Captain! rise up and hear the bells;<30>
Rise up—for you the flag is flung—for you the bugle trills,
For you bouquets and ribbon’d wreaths—for you the shores a-crowding,
For you they call, the swaying mass, their eager faces turning;
Here Captain! dear father!
This arm beneath your head!
It is some dream that on the deck,
You’ve fallen cold and dead.

My Captain does not answer, his lips are pale and still,
My father does not feel my arm, he has no pulse nor will,
The ship is anchor’d safe and sound, its voyage closed and done,
From fearful trip the victor ship comes in with object won;
Exult O shores, and ring O bells!
But I with mournful tread,
Walk the deck my Captain lies,
Fallen cold and dead. (頌譯) <31>
<30-31>

**Summary**

This article is a Korean translation of Walt Whitman's famous poem "O Captain! My Captain!", an elegy mourning the death of U.S. President Abraham Lincoln. The poem expresses deep sorrow and shock at the death of the "Captain" (leader) just as his "ship" (the nation or cause) has successfully completed its perilous journey and reached the harbor, amidst public celebration unaware of the tragedy. The speaker, presumably a crew member, grieves personally on the deck where the Captain lies dead. For historians studying colonial Korea, the publication of this specific poem in 1926 is noteworthy. The allegory of a fallen leader after a great struggle could have resonated with Koreans experiencing loss of sovereignty and longing for leadership, even though the poem's explicit subject is American. It might represent a subtle expression of grief, loss, or perhaps a veiled commentary on the contemporary situation, using the acceptable form of translating a Western classic.
