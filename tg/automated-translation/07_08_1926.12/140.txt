**Body Text Translation**
Proverbs of the Malay People

To hang without a rope = To abandon one's wife without a letter of divorce (休書).

An ant dies in a pond = The end of a hedonist.

An owl laments looking at the moon = A person full of delusions and fantasies.

A bird returning to its hometown = Came empty-handed, goes empty-handed.

To light a lamp in broad daylight = An honest gentleman.

Is there anyone who would expose salt to the rain? = No one reveals their own faults.

The sun cannot be hidden = Great sins must eventually be revealed.

It is better to be the enemy of a wise person than the friend of a foolish person.

If you love your son, you will often make him cry.

If you want to love your wife, you must part frequently.

Though the wound heals, the scar (fault) remains.

If you try to block a river with a writing brush, the crocodile laughs.<50>

**Summary**

This short article presents a list of Malay proverbs, each followed by a brief Korean interpretation of its meaning. The proverbs cover topics such as divorce, hedonism, delusion, the cycle of life, honesty, self-preservation, the inevitability of truth, relationships, parenting, marriage, and the futility of weak efforts against powerful forces. For historians, this piece offers a small window into Korean interest in other Asian cultures during the colonial period (1926). It reflects an effort to understand and interpret foreign wisdom literature, potentially as a way to draw parallels or contrasts with Korean values, or simply out of intellectual curiosity prevalent in magazines like *Donggwang* (동광). The interpretations themselves reveal contemporary Korean understanding and framing of these concepts.

아들을 사랑하면 늘 울게 된다.
안해를 사랑하려면 자주 리별하여야 된다.
상처는 나하도 허물은 진다.
