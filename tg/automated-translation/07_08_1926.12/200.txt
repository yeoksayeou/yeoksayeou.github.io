Article Title	冷麪 (Cold Noodles)
Author	金浪雲 (Kim Nang'un)
Article Type	소설 (Fiction)

冷麪
						金浪雲

At the S Newspaper Company, it had been five days since payday, yet they still hadn't received their salaries, putting it off day after day.
So today too, though it had already been an hour since work ended, most of the employees hadn't left, waiting for news. Even K, famous for quickly dashing off the moment work finished, hadn't left and was sitting at his desk, doodling with his brush on manuscript paper.

『Damn it—are they not paying us today either?』
Sunho, who had been wandering around the editorial office, muttered this as if angry and plopped down on the chair next to K.

『Well... it's the last day of the month today, so maybe there'll be some news.』
K replied thus, tossing down his brush and turning his body sideways towards Sunho. Leaning back diagonally, he pulled out a pack of Haet'ae brand cigarettes from his lower suit jacket's side pocket.

Sunho stared at it blankly and said,
『Huh, you're still the same as ever. Give me one.』

『Sure, have one!』
K nodded and pushed out the cigarettes he had taken out. 『If we go broke, so be it!』
Then he took out another one for himself.

『Treating everyone?』 At that moment, another person reached out a hand from behind him.
『Let me join in too.』

『Go ahead! It's a losing game anyway.』
K, half-joking, magnanimously shouted and this time pushed out the whole cigarette pack. <61> Only one cigarette remained inside.

They lit their cigarettes. The smoke they exhaled lingered, swirling above their heads before dissipating, then dissipating again. Everything seemed to speak of boredom (倦怠) and weariness.

Hats, suit jackets, *turumagi* coats, canes—such things were messily hung here and there on the coat rack and chairs. On the wide, large desk, manuscript paper, newspapers, pens, paper scraps, ink bottles—these things were scattered haphazardly.

Some sat facing the messy desk, flipping through newspapers, while others, as if bored, scribbled something on manuscript paper. Some wandered about, laughing and chatting about this and that useless gossip. Some sat in a corner eating *naengmyŏn* (cold noodles), while others sat quietly fanning themselves, as if everything was bothersome.

But in everyone's head, the same thought resided: 『Are they not paying us today either?』

While smoking, Sunho scanned K, sitting opposite him, from head to toe. The silk 'white shirt' like dragonfly wings that he wore, his serge trousers with a sharp crease, the necktie pin with a red stone—whether it was a jewel or something else—stuck in his necktie, the fact that he always smoked only Haet'ae brand, the way his face always looked cheerful as if he had no worries, and how he still frequented restaurants—thinking about all these things, strange thoughts wandered through Sunho's mind as he scanned him blankly.

『Isn't his salary the same as mine? Aren't we both living off the same meager salary?』
Sunho compared himself to K. But no matter how he thought about it, he couldn't understand how, with the same salary, he himself couldn't even afford a decent linen suit and was always struggling, while K's life was always so luxurious. K had <62> fewer family members. But it didn't seem like that could cause such a big difference. Indeed, K had many debts and was often hounded by creditors. But having debts and being hounded by creditors was something Sunho himself also experienced.

『Won't ruin (破滅) come to him?... 』
Sunho worried about him for a moment. But since he himself also had debts and creditors, ruin would come just the same. It seemed K had some special talent. But he couldn't figure out what that special talent was. After pondering various disordered thoughts for a while, he
『In short, either he's a hero or I'm a fool... 』
reached this conclusion.

The large clock on the wall struck four.

At that moment, an office boy from the Business Department (營業局) came and shouted,
『They're asking you to bring your seals!』

K jumped up and was the first to rush out. Sunho followed a few people behind to the Accounting Department (經理部).

The person in charge of accounting looked up briefly at Sunho from the receipt book laid open before him and said,
『Mr. Kim Sunho? Please stamp your seal here.』
He pointed to the name.

Sunho took out his seal, pressed it firmly below his name where indicated, received an envelope, and returned to the editorial office.

The editorial office was bustling with murmurs, like boiling water. But K had already vanished, gone who knows where.

Sunho opened the envelope. Inside was fifty-five won, after deducting the twenty won salary advance (假拂).

Sunho felt numb, neither happy nor pleased. He had known this would happen all along, and although he had already budgeted <63> the fifty-five won, his head still felt heavy and his mouth tasted bitter. Because the budget fell ridiculously short, the worry of 『How am I going to make ends meet with this?』 pressed down on his mind anew.

『That's right. This time, I have no choice but to stiff all the creditors.』
Thinking this, he took his yellowish straw hat, used since last year, from the coat rack and was about to step outside when he hesitated, stopped, and thought,
『No, I have to pay for lunch.』

Turning back, he called the office boy,
『Insun-ah! Insun-ah!』

『Buy me a pack of Pigeon cigarettes and get change for this.』
He started to give the rushing office boy a five-won bill but then handed over a ten-won bill. After a while, the office boy returned with the change.

『I don't know how many places I went around to.』
『Yeah, thanks for the effort. Here, hold onto this and give it to them when they come to collect. It's my lunch payment.』
Sunho counted out one won and fifty *chŏn* and entrusted it to the office boy. Then he left.

However, as soon as he stepped outside the door, a Chinese man in a Western suit approached him, smiling broadly. He had come to collect the remaining payment for the suit—the one Sunho was currently wearing—that he had made last month.

Sunho immediately snapped,
『I don't have it! Didn't even get my full salary... Come next month. Okay? Next month.』

『Why does a gentleman lie? You just got paid.』
『Got paid what?』 Sunho glared up, his eyes wide.

『We know everything.』
The Chinese man said, smiling slyly.
『I just collected from Mr. Ri too.』

Sunho stared at the Chinese man for a moment with eyes full of contempt, then, as if he had no choice, pulled out a five-won <64> bill and said,
『Here. This is all I have, so come for the rest next month.』

『Why does a gentleman act like this...』
Before the Chinese man could finish his sentence, Sunho cut in,
『Look, gentleman or commoner, what can I do if I don't have it! I really can't give any more, so come next month. Okay?』

The Chinese man seemed to think for a moment, then lifted his head sharply.
『Alright, let's do it that way.』
He took the five-won bill from Sunho's hand.
『So now there's five won left. Right?』

『Yes. That's right. Thank you.』
The Chinese man nodded and went into the editorial office.

Sunho walked away with heavy steps, feeling as if he had shaken off malaria. But he couldn't suppress his inexplicable displeasure and rising anger. His plan to stiff the creditors had already been shattered. Something heavy pressed up within his chest, tormenting him.

He headed towards the park entrance bus stop.

The summer sunlight beat down as if burning. All the trees visible through the park gate held their breath, their limbs drooping lifelessly.

He boarded the streetcar bound for Ŭiju Road. Although there were many empty seats, people crowded near the doors seeking a breeze. Sunho slumped into an empty seat on one side. As he leaned back and sat down, he felt completely drained and acutely hungry. He felt the urge to eat something. As he thought about what to eat, his hunger intensified, and his energy waned further. He felt his pocket to check if the money envelope was still there, thinking he would get off near Chongno and eat a bowl of *naengmyŏn*. Instantly, a heaping bowl of *naengmyŏn*, topped with pork slices, pear slivers, and yellow mustard, appeared appetizingly <65> before his eyes. Consequently, his appetite sharpened, and his mind stretched towards that bowl of *naengmyŏn* like a taut bowstring.

The streetcar reached Chongno. Sunho abruptly stood up to get off, but then decided to go towards the courthouse instead and didn't alight.

The streetcar departed again, heading towards the front of the courthouse. Before the streetcar had fully arrived, Sunho moved towards the driver's platform. The two-story *naengmyŏn* house next to the courthouse came into view. As the streetcar began to stop, Sunho grabbed the handrail and stepped down onto the footboard. The conductor stopped the car and turned, saying,
『Ticket, please.』

Sunho deftly jumped off and blurted out,
『*Naengmyŏn*!』

『Excuse me!』
The conductor called out politely. Then, as if wondering why someone would say *naengmyŏn* when asked for a ticket, he looked down blankly at Sunho.

『Ah!』
Sunho himself was startled by the word 『*Naengmyŏn*』 he had uttered. In that instant, he felt his face flush hotly as if splashed with sparks. Out of habit, he meant to say 『Pass!』 but instead, the word 『*Naengmyŏn*!』 which had preoccupied his thoughts, had popped out.

『Pass!』 he quickly shouted again, intending to flee.

But the mischievous conductor didn't let him go so easily.
『Excuse me! Please show it to me.』

He pretended to take out and show his pass, then quickened his pace, circled behind the streetcar, and headed towards the front of the *naengmyŏn* house. However, it seemed as though the people getting on and off the streetcar, those inside the *naengmyŏn* house, and the pedestrians passing by were all mocking and insulting him. Even the glaring sunlight seemed to be laughing at him. He lowered his head deeply and walked straight past the *naengmyŏn* house, turning into the alley with the scrivener's offices. Thoughts of hunger and *naengmyŏn* <66> fled miles away, replaced only by an indescribable feeling of indignation and sorrow that seemed to choke his chest. He felt as if he had suffered a grave insult and unbearable shame. He felt such hatred for the conductor who had grabbed him and caused the fuss that he could have beaten him to death and still not felt satisfied. His heart pounded inexplicably.

He emerged again near Pikak through the back alleys behind Saejŏn, without realizing where he was going. The weather was extremely hot, but even more sweat flowed from his forehead. He took out his handkerchief and wiped the sweat.

He boarded the streetcar bound for Yŏngch'u Gate again. But still, it felt as though everyone was staring intently at him, laughing at him. He couldn't look at anything straight, couldn't think clearly about anything. His mind, as if frozen by cold water, burned by fire, or stung by a bee, was just dazed and listless. Yet that incident—calling out "Naengmyŏn" and suffering unbearable humiliation, the mere thought of which made his face burn—oppressively weighed down his entire mind.

The streetcar stopped at the Chŏksŏn-dong intersection. He held his pass ready beforehand and this time showed it without a word as he got off. He felt his suit pocket again to check if the money envelope was still safely inside, then started walking.

After crossing the Kŭmch'un Bridge and entering the Ch'ebu-dong alley, he walked for a while, then suddenly stopped short, thinking, "Oh no!" It was because he abruptly remembered his wife had been pleading, almost singing, since last month for him to buy her a bolt of ramie cloth as she had nothing to wear. He hesitated for a moment, wondering whether to go back out and buy it, then decided, 『Whatever, I'll do it later,』 and continued walking.

As he neared his house, his young children came to mind again. When he entered, the little ones would surely run up asking if he had brought snacks. So, he stopped at a small tobacco shop nearby and bought two five-*chŏn* bags of biscuits (삐쓰켓), tucking them into his suit pockets.

As he stepped inside his house gate, the three-year-old younger one, who was playing on the *maru* (wooden floor porch), beamed <67> and cried out,
『Daddy! Daddy!』
running towards the edge of the *maru*.

『Yes, yes.』
Sunho went towards him and began untying his shoelaces.

The six-year-old elder one stood behind his younger brother, just watching cautiously.

From one room, his youngest brother, who attended fourth grade in elementary school, and his mother looked out. From another room, his wife, heavily pregnant, also looked out. His older younger brother seemed not to have returned from school yet.

He took off his shoes and placed them on one side of the *maru*, then holding the younger child's hand, entered the room. The older child followed. He tossed his hat onto the floor, sat down near the upper part of the room by the door, and sighed deeply enough to make the room seem to sink. Then he took out his handkerchief and wiped his face.

The little one persisted,
『Daddy, kwa-ja, kwa-ja.』
pestering him. "Kwa-ja" meant "gwaja" (snacks/cookies).

『Okay, I'll give you some. I'll give you some.』
He took out the bags of biscuits and gave one to each son. They—innocent and adorable, without a care in the world—took them out to eat, holding the bags, happily running out to the *maru* and back into the room, muttering something.

『It's hot, why don't you take off some clothes?』
his wife said. Without answering this, he gazed blankly at his pregnant wife sitting to one side with pitying eyes and asked,
『Did you eat lunch?』
His wife often skipped lunch. Not because there was no rice—though sometimes that was the case—but because life was so harsh, she naturally often skipped lunch. Sunho always worried about this.

『Yes, I ate.』
『What?』 <68>
『I ate rice.』

Sunho sighed again. Then he took out a cigarette and lit it.

The thought of hunger, which had fled miles away, crept up on him again. His limbs felt achy, and his whole body felt utterly drained. Hunger knows no shame or decorum, and the fact that his stomach was empty was undeniable; despite everything, he couldn't help but feel hungry again.

『Why, are you feeling unwell somewhere?』
His wife asked worriedly.

『No—』

『Then why do you look so down?』

After a pause, Sunho replied.
『I'm starving right now. Tell someone to buy something.』

『Then why didn't you say so earlier?』
his wife said.
『What should I have them buy... Should I order a bowl of *naengmyŏn*?』

『Anything... 』
Actually, for some reason, he had been craving *naengmyŏn* since earlier. But remembering the embarrassing incident, the words "buy *naengmyŏn*" wouldn't come out. Rather than not coming out, he hated saying the word 『*Naengmyŏn*』. However, he was secretly relieved that his wife suggested buying *naengmyŏn* first.

『Ilnam-ah.』
The wife called the older son to run an errand.
『Call your youngest uncle.』

『Kkomaengi Samch'un (Little Uncle)?』
『Yes! Hurry and call him.』

Ilnam shouted.
『Kkomaengi Samch'un! Kkomaengi Samch'un!』
Because he was the smallest uncle, Ilnam always called him "Kkomaengi Samch'un."

Kkomaengi Samch'un came. <69>
『Quickly go order one bowl of *naengmyŏn*, okay?』

『Don't just order it to be delivered, take a bowl and bring it back yourself.』
Saying this, Sunho took out twenty-five *chŏn* and tossed it. The younger brother picked up the money and went out. The wife went outside, washed a large bowl, and brought it in.

After a while, Sunho asked,
『By the way, how much do we owe in total?』

『Just earlier, the rice bill collector came again.』
『Damn rascal!』
The husband said after a short pause,
『How much is the rice bill?』

『Altogether nine *mal* of rice, two *mal* of millet, two *mal* of barley, beans – so it comes to twenty-two won and ten *chŏn*, they say.』
『Are you sure?』
『I'm sure.』
The wife continued,
『Your brother-in-law knows exactly.』
『And what else?』
『Next is seven won twenty *chŏn* at the side dish shop, exactly four won for firewood, rent, electricity bill, water bill – that's about it.』

Sunho roughly calculated as his wife spoke, then took out the money envelope, tossed a ten-won bill towards his wife, and said,
『Here. I forgot and came back with it, so you take charge of it and at least get half a bolt of cloth cut to make something urgently. If we dawdle, we might lose both this and that.』
Then he moved the remaining thirty-five won into his wallet.

Holding the wallet, he sat blinking his eyes, calculating inwardly again.
『Rice bill twenty-two won – rent seventeen won plus five won overdue from last month – that's thirty-nine won – side dish bill seven won – makes forty-six won – plus firewood four won makes fifty won – electricity and water bills make fifty-three won... Children's school fees... 』 <70>
No matter how he figured, he was short by at least twenty won.

『Huh! Budgeting works out well.』 He muttered as if dumbfounded.

For a long while, he just puffed furiously on his cigarette, thinking,
『How am I going to cover this?...』
But there was no way. 『Can I postpone the side dish bill and pay ten won less on both the rice and rent?』 he considered. But if he shorted the rice bill by ten won, that ruthless vendor would surely stop giving credit. As for the rent, they were already demanding payment in advance; far from paying in advance, he was overdue from last month, and shorting it so much again this time seemed utterly impossible. 『Then maybe short them by five won each?』 he thought. But even then, he would still be ten won short.

『Agh!』
He scratched his head vigorously. Truly, his insides were burning with frustration.

At this moment, his mother, who had a quirky appearance and short stature, came over holding her tobacco pipe and asked,
『Did you get paid today?』

『Yes, I did.』
Sunho replied, his head still bowed.

『I need to use five won.』
『What for?』
He looked up sharply and asked.

『Goodness, your sister has written several times asking me to send her enough fabric for a French skirt (불란사치마).』
『What... I'm barely keeping my own head above water, what's all this about skirt fabric!』
He raised his voice in exasperation.
『That wretched girl! What's she doing needing a French skirt in the countryside, and if she wants one, shouldn't she ask her husband (서방) for it? Why like this.』

『That's why it's pitiful, isn't it.』
『Pitiful? Buddha statues are pitiful. She talks like that because her belly is full. <71> Let her try being hungry!』

『You married off your only girl, what did you ever give her? What did I ever give her?』
His mother also raised her voice slightly.

『Look, even if I couldn't give her anything, what can I do if I don't have it!』
Sunho shouted. Feeling resentful that his mother could be so oblivious to the fact that he was rotting inside trying to make a living, he found her exasperating.

『Don't be too harsh.』
『What do you mean, don't be too harsh.』
He stared blankly at his angry mother as if utterly dumbfounded, then suddenly slammed the wallet he was holding onto the floor.
『I don't know! Do whatever you want!』
he yelled. Grabbing his hat, he rushed out onto the *maru* and shouted,
『Devour me! Roast me thoroughly until my bones are crisp!』

Just then, "Kkomaengi Samch'un" arrived with the *naengmyŏn*, panting, and placed it on the *maru*. The three-year-old younger son followed him out, asking,
『Where are you going? Daddy, where are you going?』

After putting on his shoes, Sunho kicked the bowl of *naengmyŏn* sitting on the edge of the *maru* as if kicking a football.
『Today, even this damned *naengmyŏn* grates on my nerves!』
he roared. The innocent *naengmyŏn* tumbled unceremoniously into the yard. The bowl split neatly in two.

The six-year-old, Ilnam, stood bewildered on one side of the *maru*, not understanding what was happening.

Sunho strode heavily outside, as if he would never return.

Watching his retreating figure from the doorway, tears welled up in his wife's eyes. (End) -(November 1926)- <72>

**Summary**

Kim Sunho, a newspaper employee, struggles with delayed salary payments and mounting debts in 1926 Seoul. Despite receiving part of his salary, it's insufficient to cover basic necessities like food, rent, and his wife's request for fabric. He contrasts his precarious financial state with a colleague's seemingly affluent lifestyle. Humiliation from a streetcar incident involving a craving for *naengmyŏn* (cold noodles) and pressure from his mother for money for his sister exacerbate his stress. Overwhelmed, Sunho explodes in anger at home, ultimately kicking over the bowl of *naengmyŏn* his family had procured for him before storming out. The story vividly portrays the economic hardship and psychological toll faced by educated urban Koreans during the colonial period. Historians might find the detailed depiction of daily financial struggles, interactions with creditors (including a Chinese tailor), the cost of living (rice, rent, etc.), consumer desires (Western clothing like 'French skirts'), and the raw expression of frustration against systemic pressures particularly insightful for understanding social conditions and individual experiences under Japanese rule.

冷麪
						金浪雲
S신문사에서는 월급날이 지난지 닷새나 되도록 오늘 내일하며 월급을 주지 못하엿다.
그래서 오늘도 사원들은 일이 끗난지 벌서 한시간이나 되엇건마는 대개는 다 나아가지 안코 하회를 기다리고 잇섯다. 일이 끗나기가 무섭게 빨리 달아나기로 유명한 K 역시도 나아가지 안코 책상 아페 안자서 원고지에 붓작란을 하고 잇다.
『제-기 오늘도 안주나?』
