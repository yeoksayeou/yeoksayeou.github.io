필자	權九玄
기사형태	시 (Poem)

Mind of a Storm
						Kwŏn Kuhyŏn

This frustrated heart of mine, when I strike the ground with my fist,
The ground says nothing, only my fist breaks.
If I were to throw even my body down, what sound would be heard?

Whether there's a sound or not, I shall throw my body down.
Whether the ground collapses or this body bursts,
Since this heart is ablaze, how could I possibly endure?

Even placing just one foot on the ground resounds with a "thump,"
When I strike with this body, how great that sound must be!
If ten bodies were to join together, an even greater sound would arise. <32>

**Summary**

This poem by Kwŏn Kuhyŏn vividly expresses intense personal frustration and a desire for explosive release against an unyielding, silent force (symbolized by the ground). The speaker feels powerless individually but contemplates self-sacrifice and recognizes the potential for greater impact through collective action ("ten bodies"). From the perspective of Korean colonial history (published in 1927), the poem can be interpreted as a powerful metaphor for the suppressed anger and nationalistic aspirations of Koreans under Japanese rule. Historians might find the poem's themes of frustration, the futility of individual protest against an unmovable entity, and the concluding hint at the power of collective unity particularly interesting as an example of veiled resistance literature expressing the political climate of the time.

暴風雨의 마음
						權九玄
답답한 이내 가슴 주먹으로 땅을 치니
땅은 말이 없고 주먹만이 깨어지네
몸마자 던질적시면 무삼소리 들리리.

소리 있고 없고 몸마자 던지리라.
땅이 꺼지던 이 몸이 터어지던
불붙는 이 마음이니 참을 줄이 있으랴.

한발만 땅에 놔도 「쿵」하고 울리거니
이 몸을 팡가칠제 그 소리야 오작하리
열몸이 합할 적이면 더 큰소리 날러라.＜32＞
