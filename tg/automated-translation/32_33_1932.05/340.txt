A Day of Activity, Seeing the T'ongyŏng Citizens' Grand Athletic Meet
						Nŭlsaem

On this day, when the glory of the sun racing across the May azure sky shines down upon the earth, finely adorned with the soft light of new green,
Wrapped entirely in the gaze and cheers of the countless surrounding spectators,
The day of magnificent activity for the many brave young athletes begins.

Oh! How grand! This spectacular sight of this day!
Each time the grand program, divided into 50 events, unfolds,
The healthiness of shoulder to shoulder, rippling thickly under the May sun,
The swiftness of sturdy legs! Sturdy legs running forth and back without hindrance.

Resounding sounds shake the empty air,
Valiant figures act, pounding the heart of the earth!
Above the heads where the Red and White teams face each other, boosting their spirit for certain victory,
Red balls! White balls! Rolling back and forth in two streams.

Oh! Like thunderbolts pouring from the blue sky, like sparks colliding!
Wah! Wah! S! Enthusiastic cheers erupt from all four sides!
This grand spectacle of the day that moves the May earth!
The greatness of the united power of hearts boiling with passion!

As the May sun disappears beyond the horizon,
The tape finish line where the final victory of the day awaits!
Oh! The blood cells of the whole body leap at high speed,
The enthusiastic cheers of the countless spectators swirl around just one body.

Oh! That's right. Though they were a group of laborers (勞役群) who lost the light during the day,
Fighting hunger with sweat-soaked clothes and oil-stained faces,
Then collapsing at night on the cold floor in a gloomy corner room,
Repeating the bitter memories etched into their bones,

But when they once gather their breath and muster their strength,
There is nothing in the world to hinder them, the earth just trembles beneath their feet,
Their soaring spirit pierces the heavens,
Is only the final victory to be won not remaining?
1931. 5. 17
- At the T'ongyŏng Haguhoe Grounds - <11>

**Summary**

This poem vividly describes the excitement and energy of a large athletic meet held for the citizens of T'ongyŏng in May 1931. The poet contrasts the participants' usual lives as weary laborers facing hardship with their momentary transformation into powerful, spirited athletes striving for victory. The poem celebrates their strength, unity, and potential, finding a moment of exhilarating freedom and power in the collective activity of the sports day. For historians, the poem offers insight into social activities and expressions of collective energy among Koreans during the colonial period. The contrast between the daily toil under oppressive conditions ("lost the light during the day," "fighting hunger," "gloomy corner room") and the temporary release and empowerment found in a community event like the athletic meet is particularly revealing of the lived experience and resilience of ordinary people. The mention of the "T'ongyŏng Haguhoe" (T'ongyŏng Student Friends Association) grounds provides context for youth/community organization activities.
