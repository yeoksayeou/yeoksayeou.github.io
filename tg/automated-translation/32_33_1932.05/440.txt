잡지명	동광 제33호
발행일	1932년 05월 01일
기사제목	불이 황황 붙엇소, 두장의 편지 (Fire Blazed Up Brightly, Two Letters)
필자	김동섭
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Short Story)

불이 황황 붙엇소, 두장의 편지
						김동섭
(1)
“It’s been talked about for a long time, so surely this time…”
In reality, the other side wasn't even dreaming of it, but Pongu believed it so.
As he lay in his room, waiting only for the short and long hands of the clock to align vertically (precisely three o'clock), Pongu’s body felt a strange fatigue, as if floating in the air, then plummeting into the earth, sinking deep and being tossed about.
If, in such a situation, even just feigning care for a neglected body could trigger all latent illnesses to erupt at once—Pongu thought perhaps his body, which should have already broken down, was barely holding on due to some control mechanism.
The sound of the bell hanging on the main gate strangely excited Pongu’s nerves.
It seemed the owner’s daughter, S, was coming in.
S, who acted boldly for a girl, almost to the point of seeming immature—that S who used to fuss over Pongu—had become noticeably cold lately, he thought.
“Woo-ha, hahahahat…”
As S went inside, the sound of a girl’s cleverly modulated laughter flowed into Pongu’s ears from within.
This laughter sounded to Pongu like mocking ridicule (哄笑) isolating him from the world, so he covered his ears with both hands and shook his head vigorously.
“Ack!”
S, who must have come out unnoticed, flung open Pongu’s sliding door as if striking it with lightning.
“Oh my, you startled me! Don’t do that to someone who’s lost in thought.”
Somewhat pleased that S had suddenly sought him out, Pongu spoke as if admonishing her.
“What do you mean, lost in thought? But why are you covering your ears and acting so weirdly?”
“Such language. S’s laughter was unpleasant to hear!”
“Really?”
“Really!”
“Stop it. I knew you hated me that much.”
“No, no. How could that be? How could I possibly… S-yang…”<30>
Pongu sketched a round circle with both hands as if embracing her.
“So, what happened with that?”
Pongu tried to make another joke, but intimidated by S’s serious expression, he said,
“I’ll find out when I go now!”
Pongu hoped S would stay and chat until three o'clock, but she went back inside without entering.
Pongu gazed, as if covetously, at S’s smooth legs and the currently fashionable sock-overs, at the sharp curve where her shoe heels turned.

(2)
Suddenly, the whole world felt even bleaker to Pongu. Even the rustling of clothes seemed to stir a colder wind in the room.
Pongu thought of his younger sister, Hyesŏn, not just anew, but devastatingly.
Whenever he thought of S, thoughts of his sister followed chronologically.
“Ah…” a sigh escaped him unknowingly. It was just too heartbreaking.
He regretted not having cleared out his sister’s worn-out bedding, old wicker trunk, and basket tucked away on the shelf in the corner of the room sooner.
Hyesŏn had died at the age of nineteen, during the summer before her final two terms of girls' high school graduation.
Indeed, Hyesŏn had been set to marry at fifteen but had run off to Seoul, relying solely on her older brother, Pongu.
“Oh, your frozen hands too!” It was true. Pongu and Hyesŏn had always supported each other, thawing their hardships together while finding courage.
Pongu had thought that once he graduated from professional school, surely Hyesŏn’s tuition wouldn't be a problem, but during the full year he remained unemployed after graduation, Hyesŏn, for her part, endured all sorts of hardships.
In her fourth year of girls' high school, Hyesŏn became a private tutor for a businessman's family.
Just when they rejoiced, thinking the tuition problem was finally solved, his sister quit the tutoring job less than ten days later.
“What happened?”
Pongu asked his sister, whose face was strangely pale.
“It just wasn’t right. And a good friend suggested we commute to school together!”
This was Hyesŏn’s simple answer. Pongu, drawing from his own experience, interpreted this at the time as meaning it was difficult to live a “false life,” a rather fanciful interpretation. Also, given his own frustrating circumstances, he lacked the courage to press her further.
A week after she started living alone and commuting by train, during the lunch break exercise period, when her name was called, Hyesŏn couldn't answer and fainted from cerebral anemia.
When Pongu rushed to the girls' high school, the female gym teacher who knew the situation and several students even shed tears.
But Hyesŏn eventually died in Pongu’s rented room.
Nothing could have been more devastating for Pongu. As Hyesŏn lay dying, just as a candle flickers brightly before going out, she seemed to regain full consciousness.
“Oppa!” she called out clearly, staring intently at Pongu.
“Yes. Yes.”
Pongu’s hope surged, but the clarity of her voice and expression frightened him more as he answered.
“Oppa. I’m resentful. So resentful. Will I be able to live and take revenge?”
“Of course. Of course, you will.”
Those words struck Pongu with a strange pressure, as if his own fate was making a declaration.<31>
Hyesŏn looked at Pongu’s face again.
He had never seen Hyesŏn’s face look so terrifying.
“Yes. I’m unreliable, aren't I? I couldn't even slightly avenge your resentment (噴滿)!”
As Pongu spoke, choked with emotion, several streams of tears flowed down Hyesŏn’s thin cheeks.
Hyesŏn was extremely thin, but she calmly extended her pale hand.
“Oppa. Rub the back of my hand!”
Pongu rubbed her hand – rubbed it earnestly, thinking that rubbing her hand would improve blood circulation.
While he was earnestly rubbing, his sister passed away unnoticed.
Indeed, it felt like it happened in an instant.

(3)
The clock struck three.
Pongu presented his business card to the guard with a bristling mustache sitting in front of the main gate of H Bank.
A girl of about fifteen took Pongu’s card and led him to the reception room.
After waiting nervously for about 20 minutes, he was guided again towards the executive office, where he bumped into someone coming out from the other direction.
The two shook hands without words. Pongu broke the silence.
“What brings you here?”
“Me?”
“Yes.”
“Looking for a job. You?”
“Looks like I am too!”
The two laughed for a moment.
“Well, I heard there were two or three candidates selected for one vacancy, but I guess you weren't one of them…” Pongu laughed.
“Seems so, but what chance does someone like me have!”
The other person laughed too.
“Well, go on in!”
The other person let go of Pongu’s hand first and briskly turned the corner of the hallway. Then, suddenly turning back to Pongu, who was still standing there blankly, he said,
“Hey, Kim. It’s your turn to yield this time, haha…” The complex expression on Pongu's face, the other's hearty laugh, or something else unsettled his mind.
In such situations, jokes could be serious, and serious words could be jokes, Pongu thought.
He was Yun, a man with whom Pongu was very close.
Coincidentally, they had done everything together since entering middle school, even dropping out midway and transferring to another middle school, so there were many reasons for their closeness.
Pongu was not unaware of Yun’s difficult circumstances, but Yun’s final remark, which seemed to prioritize only his own situation, bothered him strangely.
Pongu began to gather his scattered emotions.
As was his habit, he brushed the dandruff off his suit collar and reached for the door of the executive office.

(4)
Pongu bowed respectfully before a young gentleman with two large safes arrayed before him, a deeply receding hairline on his pale forehead, and large glasses.
The late afternoon sun seemed to filter quietly through the curtains (카덴), making the bustling bank seem unusually tranquil.
A female typist, nicknamed “Office Wife” (오피쓰. 와이푸), sat right behind the young executive, typing lightly as if playing piano keys.<32>
She too seemed quite unhurried.
“Please sit there!”
Pongu absentmindedly sat in the chair indicated by the executive. The chair’s cushion (쿳숀) yielded with embarrassing elasticity, enveloping Pongu deeply before bouncing him back up.
The executive smiled at Pongu’s flustered expression and took a bundle of documents from an elderly secretary diligently writing a letter beside him.
It was a stack of hundreds of resumes.
“Who might you be? Ah, Mr. Kim (긴상). You graduated in law!”
Pongu, who had assumed he was already somewhat known, couldn't help but feel disappointed by this remark.
The executive briefly glanced at Pongu, then shifted his gaze back to the resumes.
“Well, here’s the situation. As you can see, this entire pile is resumes.”
Pongu’s heart began to flutter needlessly. It wasn't just the pile of resumes that made him flutter, but also a faint, old impression that he had met this executive somewhere, sometime, in a position not too far removed from Pongu's own status back then.
Pongu scolded himself for the reckless emotion that defied the daunting pressure of reality.
“However,”
The executive tapped the desk with a pencil.
“Our bank, more than anything else, is at the forefront (센딴) of navigating this recession. At a time like this—a time requiring utmost caution (경게), so to speak—hiring someone is no small matter. Frankly, our bank is already saturated with people.
Even if there’s a vacancy or two, the queue of candidates waiting right behind is overflowing (汎濫)!”
Although caught in a strange emotion, Pongu pretended to listen intently to the executive's words.
“Indeed, from this multitude of resumes, we try to conduct a fair selection (전형) if possible. Uh huh. To do that, one method is, first, that the candidate must be from a reputable school, and of course, must have a recommender credible to the bank. Only those who meet these conditions have been initially marked in red. Among those, the ones selected again are marked twice. You, sir (노형), are among them.
Indeed, they say that in Japan, graduates from the prestigious Akamon (赤門)—who used to be snapped up upon graduation like prized daughters—are now hired for 40 yen a month just to address envelopes. It’s that difficult!”
The executive sighed. Pongu wondered what connection this executive's lamentation (長講談) about the times had with his own job prospects, but whenever the executive looked at him while speaking, he couldn't help but adopt a respectful (공축한) expression and bow his head slightly in agreement, signifying, “Yes, that’s right!”

(5)
A clerk brought in payment vouchers (전표) for approval, placed them on the desk weighted down by an inkwell (잉크쓰보), and left.
The executive stopped talking to Pongu and began stamping each voucher attached with supporting documents, then suddenly shouted.
He pressed a white button, like a nipple, on a small box on his desk.
“Zzrring!”
Before the sound died down, the elderly employee (노행원) entered, bowing subserviently.<33>
Several veins seemed to stand out more prominently on the executive's taut facial muscles.
“Look here, I cannot approve this. Who authorized this decision under whose command?”
The executive slammed his approval stamp, which he had been using, onto the desk.
“It’s not like that, sir!”
The old employee, seemingly pleading (빌붓는 듯이), placed both hands on the executive’s desk and tried to explain gently.
“What do you mean it’s not like that? Look, if you make decisions so arbitrarily…”
“No, this is something I mentioned the other day. Since you were silent then, I thought you had approved and gave my consent,” the old employee managed to explain, continuously observing the executive’s facial expression.
“Oh ho—did you see that? Look, just because I was silent doesn’t mean I approved, does it? In any case, I cannot approve this voucher (덴뾰 / 傳票). We cannot let our bank suffer a loss just for the sake of one individual’s face—”
The old employee gathered the scattered vouchers and supporting documents, bowed even lower than when he entered, and left.
Having navigated the ways of the world for many years, the old employee seemed to understand that the phrase “in any case” meant there was no point for a mere staff member (평사원) like him to insist further.
The executive glanced at Pongu’s face.
Pongu felt suddenly startled. He noticed the executive’s face was still contorted (울퉁불퉁한).
The executive fiddled with the resumes again.
“Actually, the person our bank needs to hire is just one person for the vacancy—uh oh—Mr. Kim (긴상) and that Mr. Yun Ha-jung earlier are alumni, aren’t you?”
“Yes!”
“Uh oh. Then what should we do in this case?”
“In this case, you mean?”
“Let’s say… Mr. Noh’s (노형) friendship with Mr. Yun, or should I say, your alumni relationship.”
“…”
“I believe there is no virtue more beautiful in the East than yielding (사양). What if, for example, the friendship between Mr. Kim and Mr. Yun were destroyed simply because of self-interest?”
“Then Mr. Yun…”
Before Pongu could finish his sentence, the executive interrupted.
“No, I’m not saying anything about Mr. Yun, just hypothetically speaking, you know,” he denied.
Pongu, who hadn't been able to grasp the drift of the executive's grand pronouncements and ambiguities until now, felt a premonition that it was "already too late," which actively dredged up the sharp-edged emotions he had been painstakingly suppressing.
“So, does my friendship or alumni relationship contradict the fact that I am seeking employment?”
The executive seemed quite bewildered by Pongu’s trembling, abrupt question.
“Haha. Please don’t misunderstand. It’s not necessarily related to the hiring issue, but suppose, in your view, Mr. Yun’s situation is difficult, and in Yun’s view, your situation appears difficult. And only one person is needed. In such a case, could you truly push Mr. Yun aside, and could Mr. Yun truly push you aside? Let’s consider such a delicate (떼리케트한) situation.”
While offering this explanation, the executive repeatedly bounced on the armchair.
‘I never imagined this guy would engage in job campaigning while badmouthing me behind my back!’ <34> Pongu felt an urge to immediately find Yun, who betrayed his friend and ran off, and tear him apart.
“So you’re saying… one of the two must yield?”
“So to speak!”
“Then let me tell you. It may sound abrupt, but what if I say I cannot yield?”
As his own voice echoed clearly back into his ears, Pongu abandoned the thought of getting the job.
“Hahaha. I understand. In any case, we will let you know after the executive meeting (역원회).”
Receiving the executive’s cold expression, Pongu opened the door to the executive office and walked out.

(6)
As Pongu, buried in indignation (울분) and fantasy (공상), headed up towards An-dong to his lodging (사관), someone lightly tapped him on the back. It was Yun.
“I was waiting for you to come out.”
Yun tried to pull Pongu into a nearby teahouse (차집), but Pongu, finding it extremely unpleasant to face Yun, firmly refused.
“Actually, I was quite rude earlier.”
“Still, your situation is better than mine. I’m in a position where I have to get a job even if I have to bite down on a stone.”
Yun’s grimacing, almost grotesque (흉물) expression further soured Pongu’s mood.
“But, hey—”
Yun, oblivious to Pongu’s state of mind, continued.
“It’s hopeless. Hopeless. Apparently, those bastards already made an internal decision (내정)!”
“What did you say?”
Pongu, who had been walking silently, just grunting without any reply, finally exploded with displeasure.
‘So close, yet he puts on such a pretense (내숭) over a trivial job like this!’ Pongu felt a profound pity for people.
“So, who did they decide on?”
Pongu asked sarcastically, wanting to see what brazen answer Yun would give.
“ま ま 話したくもないよ. 諦めやうちや! (Ma ma hanashitaku mo nai yo. Akirameya uchi ya! / Well, well, I don't even want to talk about it. Let's just give up!)”
Yun, too, sounded annoyed.
“Word is, it’s the kid of someone related to H Bank management!”
“So, where did you hear that story?”
“There’s an employee I know in the General Affairs Section (서무과).”
“Look, let’s just drop it. I know anyway, whether you tell me or not!”
Pongu silenced Yun, who was about to say more. However, just as Pongu and Yun were about to part ways:
“Hey. Do you know who the Senior Managing Director (전무) is? That guy (칠자) was in the 4th year when we were in the 2nd year of X Middle School.”
Only then did Pongu resolve the suspicion that had arisen when he met the executive.

(7)
A day passed quickly. Ten days passed. There was no news for him. Thinking that even his supposedly close friend Yun might have pushed him aside and gotten the job, now strutting around proudly (웃쭐대고) in a new suit, made Pongu hate Yun immensely. Yun’s attitude the other day, slick as if spitting watermelon seeds out of his backside (똥구멍으로 수박씨를 까는 듯하든), was even more hateful.
“Friend-betraying bastard!” he spat. His disappointment in Yun was as great as his trust had been.
The room felt even more hollow, dark, and desolate.
Lately, even the shadow of S, who had seemed hopeful about his job prospects, <35> was nowhere to be seen. Loneliness, with no place to lean on wherever he looked, seized Pongu’s weakened nerves.
Suddenly, he felt terrified even to be in the room. But when he tried to shout and run out of the terrifying room, his legs and entire body felt an immense pressure, as if weighing hundreds or thousands of pounds.
He even experienced convulsions, as if strange, unknown black hands were gripping him, pulling him in all directions, threatening to tear him apart limb from limb.
“What’s wrong with me?” Pongu shook his head. His body felt increasingly shivery, and though it was still morning, he saw visions of dead people’s faces.
“Ah, Hyesŏn!”
Pongu stretched his hands into the air, calling out as if crazed. He jumped up abruptly and pulled out Hyesŏn’s photo tucked into the picture frame (가구부지).
“Forgive this useless older brother for doing only this much!”
He cried out, pressing the photo to his chest. Pongu happened to discover Hyesŏn’s handwriting on the back of the turned-over photo.
“What should I do? What should I do with this impure body? How can I face my brother? Why couldn’t I have bitten and torn at that bastard more? Why did I…
My body is dirty, dirty, and dirty again, but my heart is resolved on revenge. But, but, what am I to do with this pitiful body…”
It might have been Hyesŏn’s final writing, which Pongu had never discovered before. It was a heartbreaking record of regret and burning desire for revenge, lamenting her maidenhood, symbolized by her flowing braided hair, which seemed to boast of her younger days.
Pongu instantly thought of the businessman’s house where Hyesŏn had tutored.
“Then my sister lost her virginity!”
This new, enormous fact tore Pongu’s heart with extreme anger and revulsion. He ground his teeth.
“Revenge, revenge!”
He shouted. He felt he must take revenge, if only to offer a rite before his dead sister’s spirit. Like a man possessed by fever, Pongu scrambled to put on his clothes. As he was about to burst through the gate, the postman (체전부) arrived, handing Pongu two letters: a postcard and an envelope.
The postcard was unexpectedly from Yun. The enveloped letter contained the handwriting (글빨) of his nephew from the countryside.
Pongu’s mind, which had been unable to sort things out, suddenly cleared upon receiving the letters.
He even felt that perhaps instructions on how to exact revenge might be written within them.
So, Pongu tore open the envelope first.
The letter’s content was as follows:
“…A terrible thing happened at our house. Elder Aunt (큰 아지먼이) (Pongu’s eldest sister) took her children and ran away secretly in the middle of the night…”
“And then, suddenly, a fire broke out fiercely (황황 붙엇소) at our house. The baby got burned too. Grandmother just cries, it’s truly pitiful (딱하오)…”
It had been three years since Pongu last visited his hometown. A major reason was his discomfort with the family’s baseless reliance on him.
Thus, he didn't even know the exact number of his nieces and nephews, born year after year (년년생). Although there were several able-bodied men milling about (들석어리지만) in the house, led by his mute elder brother, none could properly read or write even a single letter of the Korean script (언문), so letters to Pongu were invariably written by others (대필).
Since his eldest nephew entered the fifth grade (오학년) of primary school, letters would occasionally arrive, composed like essays, mixing Japanese and Korean.<36>
Because of this, correspondence was naturally infrequent. Any letters that did arrive conveyed news of “thorough desperation (철저한 절박),” as if scraping the very bottom.
The news that his eldest sister had run away with her children or that the house had burned down seemed like predictable events, so it didn’t bring Pongu great despair.
“Humph, humph!” He tore it into pieces without even finishing reading. He read the postcard from Yun while tearing it up too.
“…I had been waiting with wide eyes for the job offer until now. But only yesterday did I hear that someone is already working at H Bank. Turns out it’s the kid who was internally decided on before. I thought about going to confront them but decided against it. Now that it’s turned out like this, my mind feels clearer (앗사리하여지는 것 같네). etc.”
Pongu’s feelings towards Yun completely dissolved.
Pongu’s mind also began to clear strangely. Just as Yun said, Pongu’s mood felt unprecedentedly clear (앗사리하여젓다).
He felt a lighthearted (가든한) thought, as if he had found a new solution arising from the thorough destitution (궁핍) pressing upon him.
—He discovered that only by hitting rock bottom did new strength and a new perspective emerge.
(February 1, 1932)<37>

**Summary**

Pongu, an unemployed graduate, anxiously awaits news about a bank job, only to find his close friend Yun competing for the same single position. He faces condescension from the bank executive who emphasizes connections and the virtue of yielding. Meanwhile, Pongu is haunted by the memory of his younger sister Hyesŏn, who died young after suffering hardships and hinting at a deep grievance. Later, both Pongu and Yun learn the job went to an insider. Pongu discovers a note revealing Hyesŏn was likely sexually assaulted, fueling his desire for revenge. Simultaneously, he receives news of disaster at his family home. Instead of despair, reaching this lowest point brings Pongu and Yun a strange sense of clarity and relief, suggesting a potential shift away from conventional aspirations.

For historians, this story offers insights into the intense job scarcity and competition faced by educated Koreans under Japanese rule, the prevalence of nepotism (내정) in colonial institutions, and the socio-economic pressures contributing to personal and familial crises. The psychological toll of unemployment, the complex dynamics of friendship under duress, the vulnerability of women, and the blend of Korean and Japanese language in daily life are vividly portrayed, reflecting the lived reality of colonial modernity. The ending hints at a possible turn towards alternative forms of resistance or survival beyond engagement with the colonial system.

불이 황황 붙엇소, 두장의 편지
						김동섭
(1)
『오래동안 이애기 되어잇든 곳이니까 이번에야 설마...』
실상 저쪽에선 꿈도 안 꾸고 잇건마는 봉우는 어러케 믿엇다.
시게의 단침 장침이 일직각(새로 세시) 되기만 기다리고 방안에 누엇으려니 봉우의 몸은 허공으로 뜨는 듯. 땅속으로 곤두박질해서 푹 가라앉아 까불어지는 듯한 괴이한 피곤을 느꼇다.
만일 이러한 경우에 내버려두든 몸이라도 아른체만 한다면 잠복하고 잇든 온갓 병이 일시에 일어나리라- 벌서 파탄이 되엇서야 할 몸이 어떤 통제기관에 의하야 간신히 부지해잇는 것인지도 모르리라고 봉우는 생각하엿다.
큰 대문에 매달어 논 종소리가 봉우의 신경을 이상히 흥분시켯다.
주인의 따님 S가 들어오는 모양이다.
철이 없다 하리만치 게집애로서 대담하게 짓거리를 하든 S가 - 봉우라면 허겁지겁을 하든 그 S가 요지음으로 몹시 냉냉하다고 생각하엿다.
『우하, 하하하핫...』
S가 들어가자 안악에서는 게집애의 마디를 꺾어서 재치잇게 웃는 웃음 소리가 봉우의 귀에 흘러들어왓다.
봉우는 이 웃음 소리가 저를 이 세상에서 따돌리는 홍소(哄笑)같아서 두 손으로 귀를 틀어막고 머리를 절레절레 흔들엇다.
『악!』
어느새에 나왓섯는지 S가 봉우의 미닫이 문을 벼락칠 듯이 열어제쳣다.
『아이고 깜작! 무심하게 잇는 사람에게 그러지 말어』
S가 돌연히 자기를 찾아 준 것이 얼마큼 반가워서 봉우는 타이르는 것처럼 말햇다.
『무에 무심이야요, 한데 귀는 왜 막고 흉불을 떨고 잇소?』
『말버릇 좋다. S의 웃음소리가 듣기 싫어서!』
『참말이야?』
『참말!』
『그만 둬. 그러케 미워하는 줄은 나도 알앗습니다』
『아니 아냐. 그럴이가 잇나. 내가 어떠케 S양을...』＜30＞
봉우는 두 손으로 포옹하는 것처럼 똥그란원을 그려내엇다.
『그런데 그건 어떠케 되엇수?』
봉우는 한 번 더 농을 건느려 하엿으나 S의 마지메한 표정에 질리어서
『지금 가봐야 알게 될테죠!』
봉우는 S가 새로 세시될 임시까지 놀아주길 바랏으나 들어오지 않은채 안으로 들어가 버렷다.
S의 미끈한 다리와 요새로 유행하는 양말오바가 구두 뒤꿈치의 급각도로 돌아간 반원을 탐스러운 듯이 봉우는 들여다 보앗다.

(2)
봉우는 금방으로 왼 세상이 더 쓸쓸하여졋다. 의복만 풀썩해도 방안에선 더 한층 찬바람이 돌앗다.
봉우는 새삼스레라니보다 기막히게 누이 헤선의 생각이 낫다.
S를 생각할 적마둥 연대적으로 누이의 생각은 낫다.
『아하...』하고 저도 모르게 한숨이 흘럿다. 너무도 안타까웟든 것이다.
방구석 선반 우에 틀어박힌 누이의 헌 이부자리와 낡은 고리짝, 빠스켙을 왜 진작이 치어버리지 못햇든가 하고 후회하엿다.
헤선이는 여자고보의 졸업을 두 학기 앞 둔 여름에 열 아홉을 일생으로 죽고 말앗다.
실로 헤선이는 열 다섯 살에 시집가게 된 것을 오라비인 봉우만 믿고 서울로 뛰어 올라왓다.
『얼고 언 손도!』 그러하엿다. 봉우와 헤선이는 언제나 서로 붙잡고 용기를 일으켜 주는 가운대에서 녹여내며 지내왓엇다.
봉우가 전문학교를 나오면 설마 헤선이 학비쯤이야 하엿든 것이 졸업하고도 일년을 말숙하게 노는 동안에 헤선이는 헤선이 대로 가즌 고생을 다 하얏다.
헤선이는 여고보의 4년급이 되어선 어느 실업가 집의 가정교사가 되엇다.
학비문제가 인제 해결됏는가보다고 그러케 기꺼워하든 누이애가 열흘이 넘지 않아서 가정교사 생활을 뛰어나오고 말앗다.
『왼 일이냐?』
하고 봉우가 이상하게 얼굴이 해쓱한 누이에게 물엇을 때에
『다 마땅하지 않아서요. 그리고 좋은 동무가 같이 통학을 하재요!』
이것이 간단한 헤선의 대답이엇섯다. 봉우는 자기의 경험으로 밀우어 그때에는 「허위생활」을 하기가 어렵다는 말로 공상적인 해석을 하엿다. 또 답답하기만한 제 처지로서 더 채처 물을 용기도 없엇다.
자취를 하며 기차통학을 한지 일주일 되든 점심 체조시간에 호명할 때에 헤선이는 대답도 못하고 뇌빈혈로 졸도를 하얏다.
봉우가 녀고보에 달려갓을 때에 사정을 아는 여자 체조선생과 멫 학생은 눈물까지 흘렷다.
그러나 헤선이는 봉우의 하숙에서 그여코 죽고 말앗다.
봉우에게 이러케 허탄한 일은 없엇다. 헤선이가 임종할 마당에 초불이 꺼지려 하면 툭하고 반짝하듯이 헤선이도 아주 정신이나 차린 듯이
『오빠!』 하고 또렷하게 부르고서는 봉우를 뚫어지게 보앗다.
『오냐. 오냐』
봉우는 희망도 컷으나 너무 또렷한 음성과 표정이 차라리 무서워하면서 대답하엿다.
『오빠. 분해. 너무도 분해. 내가 살아서원수를 갚을 수 잇을가?』
『암. 잇다마다』
봉우는 자기의 운명이 어떠한 선언을 하는 것같이 그 말이 이상한 압력을 가지고 자기에게 육박해왓다.＜31＞
헤선이는 또 다시 봉우의 얼굴을 바라보앗다.
봉우에게 그러케 무서운 헤선의 얼굴은 일즉이 없엇다.
『그래. 내가 신신치 못하지? 네 분만(噴滿)을 내가 조금치라도 사 주지 못하고 말앗다!』
봉우의 목메인 말에 헤선이의 야윈 뺨에서 멫 줄기 눈물이 흘럿다.
헤선이는 몹시 야위엇으나 하얀 손을
『오빠. 내 손등 좀 문대어 주우!』 하고 고요히 내밀엇다.
봉우는 손을 문질럿다 - 손을 문지르면 혈액의 순환이 잘 되리라는 생각으로 열심히 문질럿다.
열심히 문지르는 동안 누이는 어느새에 운명하고 말앗다.
실로 그것은 일순간의 일인 것 같앗다.

(3)
시게는 새로 세 시를 가르치러 들어갓다.
봉우는 H은행의 정문 앞에 수염을 뻐치고 앉은 수위에게 명함을 내엇다.
열 댓살 되는 소녀가 봉우의 명함을 들고 나와 응접실로 안내한다.
한 20분 동안이나 긴장돼서 기다리다가 다시 중역실로 안내되는 길에 저쪽에서 나오는 이와 마주첫다.
둘은 말이 없이 악수를 하엿다. 봉우는 맞어
『왼 일인가?』
『나?』
『그레』
『취즉하러. 자넨?』
『나둔가 보네!』
둘은 한참을 웃엇다.
『아니 결원 한 사람에 선택된 후보자가 이삼인 잇다드니 자네 엇섯네 그려...』 하고 봉우는 웃엇다.
『그런가 보네 마는 나 같은 놈이 될이야 잇겟나!』
저쪽에서도 웃엇다.
『자 들어가 보게!』
저쪽은 먼저 봉우의 손을 탁 놓고 휘적휘적 낭하의 굽이를 돌아가다가 아직도 멍히 서 잇는 봉우에게 벼란 간에
『여보게 김군. 자네가 이번엔 양보할 차빌세 허허...』 착잡한 표정이 봉우의 저쪽의 허허거리며 웃는 웃음이라든가 정신을 낮브게 하엿다.
이러한 경우의 말은 농담도 진담일 수 잇고 진담도 농담될 수 잇다고 봉우는 생각하엿다.
그는 윤이란 사람으로 봉우와는 너무도 절친한 새엿다.
공교로운 일은 중학교에 입학해서부터 그 학교를 중도 퇴학하고 딴 중학교로 옮아 가는 것까지 같이 하엿섯든 만큼 그와 봉우는 친히 지낼 게재가 많앗다.
봉우가 윤의 딱한 사정을 모르는 바도 아니지마는 윤의 제 형편만 내세운 듯한 마그막 말 한마디가 묘하게 마음에 걸렷다.
봉우는 흩으러진 제 감정을 줏어 모으기 시작하엿다.
버릇과 같이 양복에리의 비듬을 떨고 중역실 또어에 손을 대엇다.

(4)
봉우는 수제금고를 두 개나 벌여 놓고 대머리가 깊숙히진 허연 이마에 커다마한 안경을 쓴 젊은 신사의 앞에 정중히 허리를 굽혓다.
저녁 때의 넘어가려는 해가 고요히 카덴을 통하야 들어오는 듯이 분잡한 은행이라 할 수 없을 많지 조용하엿다.
소위 「오피쓰. 와이푸」라는 별명을 듣는 녀자 타이푸라이터는 이 젊는 중역의 바로 뒤에서 피아노의 건반을 치는 듯이 가벼히 활자를＜32＞ 쳣다.
그것도 몹시 한가하여 보엿다.
『게 앉으시지요!』
봉우는 중역의 명하는 대로 무심히 의자에 앉앗다. 의자의 쿳숀은 무안하리 만치 탄력을 내어 봉우를 푹 휩쌋다가 다시 튀어 일엇다.
중역은 봉우의 당황해하는 표정에 미소를 보내고 자기 옆뎅이에서 열심히 편지를 쓰고 잇는 늙수구러한 비서에게서 서류뭉치를 받아 들엇다.
그것은 수 백매를 헤일 수 잇는 이력서 뭉치엇다.
『누구시인죠? 아아 긴상. 법전을 졸업하섯군요!』
상당히 봉우 자기가 선전되어 잇엇을 줄은 알고 잇든 봉우는 이 말 한 마디에 미상불 실망 아니 되는 것도 아니엇다.
중역은 봉우에게 시선을 슬적 돌렷다가 다시 이력서 우으로 옮기면서
『자 이러하외다 그려. 보시다싶이 이것이 전부가 이력서 뭉치외다』
봉우의 가슴은 괜히 설레기 시작하엿다. 이력서 뭉치에 설레쥀€ 않는 것도 아니엇지마는 그 보담도 어디서 언젠지 이 중역영감을 봉우의 그때 지위와 그닥 멀지 않은 듯 했든 자리에서 만나 본 듯한 낡은 인상이 떠올라 서엇다.
봉우는 스스로 현실이라는 무서운 압력을 무시하고 덤비는 철모르는 감정을 꾸짖엇다.
『한데』

큰누이가 자식들을 끌고 유리를 햇다거나 집안이 불에 탓다거나 의레 잇을 일이 잇는 것 같아서 봉우에게 그리 큰 실망을 가져오지는 않앗다.
『흥 흥!』 하고 채 읽지도 않은채 갈기갈기 찢어 버렷다. 윤군에게서 온 엽서도 찢어가면서 읽엇다.
『... 나는 이제까지 눈을 똑바루 뜨고 취직되기를 기다렷섯네. 그랫들러니 H 은행엔 벌서 사람이 들어와서 일을 하고 잇다는 말을 어제서야 듣고 알앗네. 칠자는 전에 내정되엇다든 자식이라데. 담판을 갈가 하다가 그만 두엇네. 이러케 되고 보니까 마음이 더 앗사리하여지는 것 같네. 운운』
봉우도 윤에게 가지든 감정이 홱 풀리고 말앗다.
봉우의 머리도 이상히 맑아오기 시작하엿다. 윤군 말마따나 봉우의 기분도 전에 없이 앗사리하여젓다.
그는 자기에게 육박해 오는 철저한 궁핍에서 새로운 해결을 얻은 것 같은 가든한 생각이 들엇다.
-그는 맨 밑바닥에 들어서야 비로소 새 힘과 새로운 전망이 생기는 것을 발견하엿다.
(1932년 2월 1일)＜37＞
