**Foreign Land with Barbarian Winds - Thinking of Our Compatriots Suffering Disaster**
						Cho Chong-hyŏn

Even here, where they say it's warm, I tremble.
If my feet hesitate, freezing before stepping outside the window,
What need is there to speak of that plain in Manchuria?

The old man, indeed an old man; the child, how they shiver.
How can the parents bear to watch?
Are they clinging to each other, weeping, as the snowstorm rages?

Even if buried in this land, it would be such a grievous, resentful thing.
To become a handful of earth in that foreign land with barbarian winds?
That righteous fight (싸움) they cry out for – who knows who is right?

That gruel of millet, will they even get it on time?
Even a single layer of hemp clothes would be a blessing.
The heart that remains in the homeland finds no moment of peace.
- Revised 1931.12.14 -

**O Brethren of This Land**

You who grasp the handle of the hoe, out in the fields,
Please don't sing that song, "The mountains and waters are beautiful,"
But cry out, "Let's build strength and do new work!" please do.

You who go to the factory, carrying a packed lunch of coarse grain,
Please don't sigh and surely don't shed tears,
But believe that you will become the workers of the future, please do.

You who sit in church, hearing the bell ring,
Don't pray to go to paradise or heaven after death,
But resolve, "Let's do the work of this land while we are young!"

O youth of this land, scattered in various ways,
Let us gather our hearts as one, let us set our wills together.
Know that even a single drop of blood and sweat is precious-
- 1932.1.26 - <12>

***

**Summary**

These two poems by Cho Chong-hyŏn express deep sympathy for the suffering of Korean compatriots in Manchuria following the turmoil of 1931 (likely the aftermath of the Manchurian Incident) and issue a call for unity, hard work, and practical action among Koreans remaining in the homeland. The first poem contrasts the relative safety of Korea with the harsh conditions and violence faced by Koreans in Manchuria, lamenting their plight. The second poem urges Koreans in various walks of life—farmers, factory workers, religious adherents—to abandon passive sentiments or solely otherworldly concerns and instead focus their energy on building a better future for Korea through collective effort and valuing their labor. For historians, the poems vividly reflect the anxieties and nationalist sentiments among Koreans during the colonial period, particularly the concern for diaspora communities facing hardship and the emphasis on self-strengthening and practical contribution within Korea itself as a response to the colonial situation and external crises. The shift from lamentation (Poem 1, Dec 1931) to exhortation (Poem 2, Jan 1932) might also be of interest.

胡風異域, 遭亂同胞를 생각하며
						趙宗玄
胡風異域 - 遭難同胞를 생각하며
다숩단 말을 듣는 이곳서도 떨리워라.
창밖엘 발이 얼른 안 내듸쳐 멈추거든
하물며 만주 저 벌을 말해 무엇 하리요

로인도 로인일사 어린애 떠는 양을
부모 되는 이 참아 어이 보시는고
눈보라 모라 치는데 서로 잡고 우는가

이 땅에 부친대도 그런 즉엄원한일걸.
호풍 이역에 한줌 흙이 되단말가.
저 옳다 웨치는 정의(싸움) 뉘 옳은고 몰라라

조죽 그것인들 제때나 에올것가
베옷 한 벌만 가리워도 행이시리
고국에 잇는 마음이 노일적이 없어라
- 1931.12.14.改稿 -

이 땅의 형제여
호미 자루잡고 出園에 게신 이어
『산수 길러 좋에』 그 노랠랑 불지 말고
힘길어 새일 하자구 웨쳐주소 그리여

조밥 벤또 끼고 工場으로 가신 이어
한숨 쉬우시며 부대 눈물 짓지 말고
앞날의 일꾼 되실껄 믿어주소 그리여

종소리 들으시며 敎堂에 앉은 이어
죽어 극락 가리 천당 가리 願치진 말고
젊어서 이 땅의 일을 하여 보자 하소서

각가지 헤어진 이 이 땅의 젊은이어
마음을 한 대 모고 뜻을 같이 세웁시다
한 방울 피땀일망정 귀여운 줄 아소서-
