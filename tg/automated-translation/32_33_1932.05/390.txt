**Body Text Translation:**
Comrade
						Kim T'ae-o

Comrade, if you truly love Chosŏn,
Do not just shout with your mouth, become a person of substance.
Saying "I'll do it, I'll do it" ten million times is not as good as acting once.
Throw away the ink-smearing brush and shoulder the earth-piercing pickaxe.

Comrade, if you are truly for Chosŏn,
Never engage in fighting amongst our own people.
Do you claim to be for Chosŏn with such a dirty heart?
Moreover, we have so much work to do, and indeed, things to fight for! <13>

Comrade, if you truly love Chosŏn,
Become a powerful fighter who fights on with persistence.
Cast aside propriety; what is needed is dignified unity.
That powerful person, that firmly forged unity, will naturally achieve great things. <14>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim T'ae-o is a fervent appeal to fellow Koreans ("Comrades") to demonstrate their patriotism through tangible action, unity, and persistent struggle rather than mere rhetoric or internal division. The author contrasts the ineffective "ink-smearing brush" with the productive "earth-piercing pickaxe," urging practical contribution. He strongly condemns infighting among Koreans and calls for a determined, unified front ("powerful fighter," "dignified unity") capable of achieving significant goals. From a historical perspective, the poem reflects the nationalist sentiment during the Japanese colonial period, emphasizing the need for pragmatic action and solidarity in the face of oppression. The critique of intellectual inaction, the call for potentially manual labor, the explicit condemnation of internal conflict, and the likely censored words ("싸울 일이 실로" - things to fight for, indeed) offer historians insight into the frustrations, strategies, and constraints faced by Korean activists and intellectuals under Japanese rule in the early 1930s.
