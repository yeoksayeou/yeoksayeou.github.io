Magazine Title: 동광 제9호
Publication Date: 1927년 01월 01일
Article Title: 餞迓辭 (A Farewell and Welcoming Address)
Author: 崔曙海
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

餞迓辭
						崔曙海 (Ch'oe Sŏhae)

1
Brother,
It was humbling enough that you came all this long way to see me, and now you send such a warm letter; I am so moved I don't know what to say.
You seem disappointed, thinking I might be letting your words go in one ear and out the other, but truly, I haven't ignored them. Even now, the image of you sitting serenely, radiating a calm light, flickers before my eyes, and your gentle admonishments and earnest reproaches still echo clearly in my ears.

"Brother, why did you come up here?"
Last summer, when you came to Seoul, I took you to the pine grove in front of Sŏnggyungwan and asked you this.
"Why ask something so obvious? I came to take you back. To take you..."
Your voice trembled.
"What would you do with me if you took me back?"
I remember vividly your expression clouding over as I replied thus.
"What do you mean, what would I do? Hey, have you lost your mind? So, was it wrong for me to come?"
You beat your chest as if overcome with frustration.
"Brother, why are you so upset? Please leave me be. Let me do what I'm doing."
Not knowing how best to begin, this is how I answered.
"Well, what is this work you're doing? Huh... What right do I have to interfere, but seeing you doing this, how can I just stand by? Think about why you left home! Why you left home... Why won't you listen to me? Is it because I'm not your real brother?"
"Oh, brother!"
Before you could finish, I collapsed before you and wept.
"Because I'm not your real brother..."
Hearing these words, I couldn't fathom how you could be so cruel, and overcome with an unknown sorrow, I sobbed aloud.
"Don't be like this, let's go! Let's go home, eat gruel if we must, wait for better times, and then you can come back up!"
"If I am thin, you are plump; if you are absent, I am present... I'm an ignorant fellow, so what does it matter..."<29>
"If it weren't for that wife and kids of mine, I'd follow you around, even if my shoulders (*meaning carrying loads) broke, I'd pay your school fees."
During the five days you stayed in Seoul, you sighed like this, consoling, scolding, and persuading me, but in the end, you went back down without me.
"Go on down now. There's nothing more to say."
As you were leaving, when I went to the train to see you off, you said this and sighed. I, too, stood there wordlessly, then said, "Brother, farewell..." as tears welled up, and I turned away. Since then, not a day has passed that I haven't thought of you. And now, you send a letter like this, even enclosing travel money; I truly don't know what to say.

"My dear brother. As the weather turns cold, I think of you even more earnestly! How chapped is your face from the cold winds of Samgaksan? Your sister-in-law always talks about you, and little Yongson (your son) asks every day when his uncle is coming."
"I am dictating this letter, and Yongson is writing it. He's in the fourth grade this year and writes Korean quite well."
"Come quickly. I'm enclosing twenty won for travel expenses, so come right away. If you have debts for food, pay them after you come down. How nice it would have been to send it all at once, but living hand-to-mouth day by day, how could I! This is also from Yongson's savings that I withdrew. When I told him I was taking his savings, he cried and cried, but when I said I was sending it to you, he happily went and got it himself!"
"Thinking of Yongson's affection, you must come. Uncle - Yongson, waiting for his Seoul uncle, can't sleep. Every day he makes a fuss about going to the wharf to meet you."

Brother,
I wept.
"Shoes repaired!"
"Shoes shined!"
After wandering through alleys all day long calling this out, I came back, received your letter and the twenty won, and wept. How could I not shed tears, especially for the warm humanity emanating from Yongson's innocent heart?
However, stubborn as I am, I cannot accept that warm affection. More than your anger, more than your wife's disappointment, the thought of Yongson's letdown makes my heart not just ache, but tear apart. But I, who must walk my own path, must once again go against your wishes.
Until now, I haven't told you the reasons that led me to this path, but I will tell you now. I intended to tell you when you came to Seoul, but thinking it wouldn't make much difference even if I did, I remained silent. This time too, after much hesitation, I have finally decided to tell you the motive for living this life.

2
Brother,
It's already been five years since I came to Seoul. As you often say, the memory of leaving home is still fresh in my mind. When I quit the disgusting job as a township office clerk and set out, <30> my heart was troubled beyond measure. At that time too, you were wandering here and there chasing work, just like now, so you didn't see it directly and wouldn't know, but leaving my elderly mother behind was no small pain for me.
How did mother raise me? After my father passed away, she couldn't remarry because of me, letting her prime years wither away meaninglessly, pouring all her energy and devotion into me alone. When I was kicked out of the village school for not being able to pay the tuition, mother sold the hairpiece from her head to pay for it. When the holidays came and I had no change of clothes, fretting anxiously, she would unravel the silk clothes she had made in her youth to cover my body; those memories still surface. At that time, you too were farming in our hometown, so you saw all these things, and I also remember you buying me a scarf and gloves in winter, and in summer, bringing the hemp cloth your wife wove to make clothes for me.
"He is my mother's only son,"
you would say, always calling my mother 'mother'. My mother was your father's sister, so she was your aunt, but you never called her "Aunt," always "Mother."
"That In'gap (your name) is my brother's son, but I raised him like my own. You must treat him exactly like a real older brother. After my brother (your father) and sister-in-law (your mother) died, In'gap is the only one to carry on my brother's line, and you are the only one to keep your father's ancestral rites from ceasing, so you two must live like real brothers, and even after I die, do not forget this."
Mother would often call both of us and give this counsel. Growing up under such maternal influence, not only could I not forget you, but I never even thought about whether you were my real brother or not. And your influence was also great. Perhaps after my mother, you were the one who loved me most. Then, when I was seventeen, the year I started working as the township clerk, you lost your small field to a flood, and you, your wife, and Yongson, the three of you, left our hometown. Having lost the stability of your livelihood, you started wandering train stations, harbor fronts, and construction sites, while I stayed in our hometown with mother, barely getting by on my meager monthly salary of just over ten won and mother's earnings from sewing. Living like this, my suffering and anguish grew. And as I gradually came of age, the path ahead became increasingly clear.
Mother's aging, her dimming eyes and trembling hands, finally made it impossible for her to earn money from sewing. After mother lost all income, our lives depended entirely on my monthly salary, which was about eighteen won. From this time, we began to experience the sorrow of hunger.
"I'll die without even seeing you married!"
This was mother's greatest worry at the time, but for me, the great pains were not being able to provide even simple barley rice to fill my aging mother's stomach, and not being able to go to Seoul for studies like others. From that time, I enjoyed literature, set my heart on that path, and studied it. I believe this was the influence of my mother, who told me many old tales. <31>
Whenever I heard news that friends who attended elementary school and the village school with me had graduated from some school in Seoul or entered some university in Tokyo, the blood in my veins would not calm down. More painful than that was seeing friends, who were once equals in the same village school, return to our hometown appointed as teachers or county officials, coming to the township office on business trips, forgetting old friendships and sticking their noses up in the air; it was truly unbearable. Still, swallowing it down for the sake of my livelihood felt like shortening my life by ten years. Receiving such stimuli from outside and being pressed by poverty from within, how could my young heart be devoid of emotion? If I have the so-called civilized illness of nervous exhaustion, its origins must lie in this period.
I am still blamed by friends for not shouting "Manse!" during the Kimi Movement, and you too once asked,
"Why did you just stand still at such a time?"
But even then, I did so thinking of my mother. In my young heart at that time, mother was greater than the nation. Thinking back now, perhaps if I had run off to Seoul then, things might be better now? Just thinking of my tender feelings for mother and her restraining affection, I lived day by day, an unchanging, helpless life. But a person's heart is not fixed. It flows, always changing with the environment. My heart, which had only thought of mother under her command, gradually began to awaken. It fully awakened three years after the Kimi Movement, when I turned twenty-one. That year, by the sexagenary cycle, was Shinyu (辛酉), and the twenty-second day of the sixth lunar month was mother's sixtieth birthday (hwan'gap), as you also know. I still haven't forgotten that twenty-second day. Perhaps, like the day mother died and the day I left home, I will not forget it until dirt fills my eyes. If my spirit exists after death, that memory will follow even my spirit.
As the hwan'gap approached, my heart felt heavy, as if carrying a burdensome load on my shoulders. Mother, already sensing this, tried to reassure me -
"Son, don't worry about my hwan'gap. If we can't celebrate it this year, we'll do it next year... What's there to worry about? Things are tight right now."
Though she tried to soothe me, her words sounded to me like a lament, looking back on the glories of her youth and sighing over her old age.
"Your mother's sixtieth birthday is near, so your worries must be great. Just manage as best you can with what you have, and even if you can't manage it properly, don't be disheartened.
Though I can't provide fine delicacies, I wanted to gather under mother's wing and serve her a warm meal, but I have no travel money, and besides, I injured my leg the other day and cannot go."
At that time, you also sent three won with such a letter, but while I thought I would offer words of comfort if I had nothing, the desire to prepare plenty of food, invite many of mother's friends, and let her spend a joyful day burned like fire within me. <32>
"So-and-so's old lady couldn't even celebrate her hwan'gap! What is her son doing?"
After hearing people mock like this, my shoulders slumped even further. I went around to this friend and that, trying to gather even a small amount, but failed, and then reluctantly turned my steps towards Kim Ch'osi's house. Kim Ch'osi had been alone and without support until my father helped him become wealthy, so I thought he might return some kindness.
"Ah, that's too bad, but I'm so hard-pressed myself these days, I can't spare a single penny! That's really too bad! We have chickens at home, take one of those and boil it for her."
This was Kim Ch'osi's reply. I felt greatly insulted, agitated. I wanted to kick Kim Ch'osi, who sat stiffly, burping and rubbing his full belly, but I forcibly suppressed the urge. When I stepped outside, the world seemed yellow, and I couldn't collect my thoughts. Fearing mother would worry if she knew, I returned home with a composed expression, spent a sleepless night, and the next morning, the twenty-second day, I used the three won sent by the magistrate (Wŏnnim) to buy meat and rice, cooked rice and soup, and invited five or six elderly women from the neighborhood. Mother, having no daughter-in-law, prepared the meal with her own hands, and as she ate with her old friends, she wiped away tears. Sitting at the head of the table with mother, seeing her tears, I lost control of myself. It is said that during the reign of King Sukchong, a certain filial son cut off and sold his wife's hair to prepare a feast for his mother's sixtieth birthday. To console his weeping mother, he had his wife dance while he sang. King Sukchong, travelling incognito, inquired about the circumstances and subsequently posed the riddle,
"喪歌僧舞老人哭" (Sang ka sŭng mu noin kok - The mourner sings, the monk dances, the old person weeps), thereby recruiting the filial son. But perhaps lacking such devotion, I couldn't hold back my sorrow. I tried my best to ease mother's heart, not to show sadness, but the pouring tears and sobbing sounds were uncontrollable. Mother too, trying hard to suppress her sorrow, said,
"Don't cry. Why are you crying?"
and wiped her tears.
From then on, I thought more deeply about my existence and my relationship with society. Only then did I keenly feel the truth of 'survival of the fittest' (適者生存) and 'natural selection' (自然淘汰說). When I first read about it in some magazine or heard it from a friend, I accepted it theoretically, but seeing friends who studied steadily advancing while I regressed, I felt it acutely. And another thought arose: the unfairness of society.
"Let me too become one of the 'fit' (適者). Let me not be naturally selected out. Let me become a man of the times."
But then, lacking the conditions to become so – lacking the leisure to study to become fit, despite having the potential – reflecting on my circumstances, I couldn't just passively accept this unfair system.
Brother, <33>
If there is any socialist thought within me, its faint sprouts began then. But at that time, I didn't know if it was socialist thought or something else; it was merely the thinking taught to me by my environment. These various thoughts arising within me did not find any systematic channel to become scientific, but merely gave me this conclusion:
"It's useless. This damn township clerk job is just leading to further decline. As better-educated people emerge, eventually even the position of a township servant won't be available. Then my life will be worse than now. Should I run away? Heck, run off to Seoul and study while working? But what about mother? If only brother were still in our hometown... But staying tied to mother will only worsen things... Heck, let's leave. In three or four years, I'll figure something out. Mother probably won't pass away in that time, so I should build the foundation now to serve her well in her old age. What if she starves to death in the meantime? Still, nothing can be done. It's not due to my lack of devotion, but because society forced me into this situation."
Thinking like this, resolving contradictions, reconsidering, trying as much as possible to justify leaving home, I finally decided to leave. Even after deciding, the thought of mother held me back, preventing immediate action.
"Mother is my great benefactor and, at the same time, my great enemy."
Sometimes, I even had such thoughts.
Then came an autumn night under the moon in that year, Shinyu. I left home. Having decided to leave on the midnight connecting ferry, my mind was in turmoil, and I couldn't even eat dinner properly.
"Why are you eating like that?"
Mother, knowing nothing, worried about my small appetite. After eating, I went into the room dimly lit by the twilight and gathered the books worth taking. As I bundled the books, my heart grew even more restless. Like a soldier heading to a battlefield from which he might not return, everything felt precious and nostalgic. I opened desk drawers unnecessarily, rustled useless papers, and eventually even went out to the backyard. While doing this, what appeared ceaselessly before my eyes was mother. Even usually, when I thought of mother, I wouldn't see her familiar face but a miserable vision; on this day, it was even more so, appearing as an indescribably pitiful and tragic illusion. Eventually, she even appeared in the form of the old beggar woman you and I once talked about.
"Please, just a spoonful of rice. I'm looking for my runaway son."
Seeing the old woman, clad in tattered rags, begging from house to house, I felt contempt for the son who abandoned his elderly mother.
"Ah, am I following that son's example?"
At that moment, I cried out unknowingly. When mother's shadow overlapped with the old woman's, I closed my eyes, shuddered, and cried,
"Ah, Mother!" <34>
and headed towards the kitchen where mother was. Feeling fearful, as if I had violated a great principle of human relations, my heart wrenched as though my insides were being torn. However, upon reaching the kitchen door, I stopped. Somehow, the surging emotion cooled, as if someone pulled me back.
I went into my room, picked up the book bundle, and came out, saying,
"I'll be coming in a bit late tonight."
Looking at mother, I stepped down into the yard. My heart pounded even more than before, and all sorts of visions floated before me. As I looked around the dim yard, I sighed softly. Who could have dreamt that this would be my final farewell to my home? I didn't head straight for the wharf but went past the public cemetery to the sandy beach by the sea. The early tenth-month moon had risen high, but a blue mist thickly covered the sky, making it like a melancholic spring moonlit night, further stirring my sorrowful heart. I sat and stood on the sandy shore, gazing at the waves quietly calling under the dreary moonlight. Feeling the cold wind and bathed in the moonlight, watching those waves, all senses faded, and my whole body felt drawn into the sea. Like this, as the night deepened, I walked along the seashore towards the wharf. It was eleven o'clock. As I paid ten won for the ticket, it felt like someone behind me shouted,
"You rascal!"
It felt like secretly spending stolen money. That money was my entire monthly salary received from the township office that day – eighteen won in total. It wouldn't even cover a night's drinking for a wealthy person, but it was a large sum for our household then, and mother counted the days until payday. Thinking of deceiving such a mother and using it for my travel, how could my heart be at ease?
"Oh, my son! Why are you doing this? Hmm... Sob... If you leave me, what will I do? Just throw me into this sea and go!"
As I boarded the ship, it felt as though mother was following me, wailing like this. Amidst such torment, the thought that I would finally see Seoul made me feel like jumping for joy. Why was this Seoul so longed for? Why did I want to come to Seoul so badly? Thinking about it today, perhaps I too was lured by the so-called urban-centric thought of civilization. It seems everyone, myself included, is drawn to the cities like this. Why wasn't I born in the countryside, learned nothing but farming? Such thoughts arise, but thinking of you, brother, that too feels like a foolish notion.
Brother,
Did you start wandering the cities because you didn't know how to farm? Or do you seek out urban places because you long for the city? Few love the countryside and farm as well as you do, brother, but having no land, you follow labor, and labor is in the cities, so inevitably, people flock to the cities. If only the cities welcomed them well, but the difficulties of finding work and making a living drive them back out. But where else can they go? It is here that various phenomena, unbearable from a humane perspective, explode. <35> However, it cannot be denied that this explosion is a phenomenon of humans seeking a truly human life.

3
Brother,
On the night I left, I wrote a letter to mother from the ship, and the next day, after landing in Wŏnsan, I came to Seoul by train. Seeing new landscapes from the ship and train, I felt both happy and sad, unable to control my turbulent heart. Moreover, the sound of mother's weeping seemed to echo in my ears intermittently, making my heart ache secretly. Then, getting off at Namdaemun Station and boarding a streetcar, everything was bewildering. The friend travelling with me pointed out,
"That's Namdaemun, that's Namsan, that way is Ponjŏng - Chin'gogae, here is the Bank of Chosŏn."
While I looked around with curiosity, I couldn't help but be conscious of the eyes of people nearby.
"Hey mister, you haven't seen Seoul until now?"
It felt like they were scolding me, and I felt a kind of humiliation. But blaming my friend who was kindly explaining things was entirely unreasonable, so I just swallowed my pride and kept quiet.
The day I arrived in Seoul, I found lodging in a student boarding house at the end of Kyedong. The smell of fermented soybean paste from that boarding house still lingers on the tip of my tongue.
As one day passed, then two, and I gradually saw the inner workings of Seoul, I discovered it was completely different from my imagination. The most jarring things were the "halmŏm" (old women servants) and the "kŏji" (beggars).
Brother,
Where are there "beggars" in our Hamgyŏng Province? And there are no "halmŏm" either. But in Seoul, every alley is full of beggars, so while poorly dressed people like me are left alone, gentlemen wearing even a scrap of Western clothing cannot walk the streets due to the beggars' pestering. And "halmŏm" refers to female servants; the old ones are called "halmŏm" and the young ones "ŏmŏm," always treated condescendingly. Even those who supposedly advocate freedom and equality keep servants like this and treat them familiarly using "hey" and "you." When I saw that, thoughts of my mother suddenly arose. If my mother were forced by circumstances, she could end up like that. Thinking about this and that, how much I thought of mother, and how pitiful those halmŏm seemed. Whenever the halmŏm came to my room to light the fire, I would light it for her instead, and I never used informal language with her. After doing this for a few days, the boarding house owner pointed at me and said,
"That one's a Hamgyŏngdo commoner's son! What can you do? Can a leopard change its spots?"
A fellow student told me he was subtly insulting me. And the halmŏm too started speaking informally to me, saying,
"Sir, could you light the kitchen fire too?"
I got so angry that day, I called the owner and the halmŏm and gave them a piece of my mind. Now, I regret that. If I truly had sympathy for them in my heart back then, I should have quietly endured all the insults. After making a scene, the owner not only took back the blanket he had lent me but also became so harsh in demanding the boarding fees that I eventually lost my temper and raised hell myself. <36>
At that time, as I also wrote to you, brother, I barely survived the winter of the year I came up. Wearing the unlined clothes I came in during autumn, I spent the winter without a blanket, sometimes unable to sleep at night, going out into the yard to run around. When I was freezing cold, my body would stiffen, but after running around, I would feel warm. The ignorant friends in the boarding house at the time laughed at me, saying I was practicing hygiene.
Brother,
Amidst such hardship, receiving mother's letter saying,
"Since you've already left home, stay healthy,"
and your letter saying,
"I am looking after mother, so don't worry and do as you please,"
all my troubles would melt away, and my courage would rise. But there was no way to get money for food, I was hungry, and my back was cold – in such circumstances, how could I find the time to study? At this time, a greater anguish than when I was at home arose in my heart. Piling agony upon agony, when I went outside, the sky and earth looked yellow, as if muddy water had been stirred up.
In ancient times, some studied by the light of fireflies, and others forgot their hunger, engrossed in study, but when I was cold and hungry, I couldn't hold a book. Whenever that happened, thinking,
"This is due to my lack of devotion,"
I would pick up the book and pen again, but the wind blowing through the cracks in the window pierced my bones, my stomach felt empty as if my organs were removed, and I had no energy.
After spending the winter like that, the following spring, I started working at a magazine company, gathering manuscripts and proofreading. My life became a little easier then, but I was so busy with work that I had no time to study. When I left home, I had intended to enter a school and study properly, but I felt that was something for those in the leisured class, not for people like us. After thinking this, I read whatever books came to hand whenever I had free time, without discrimination, but even that was not freely possible.
During this time, I got acquainted with writers and started writing fiction that was published in magazines. The first time I met writers, the next time I wrote a story, and the next time it was published in a magazine, I was truly happy. Now, I find it laughable and have lost attachment to that kind of life, but back then, I was so happy, I felt like I had become a great master. Not only that, as I gradually wrote more and frequented literary circles more often, I received invitations from various newspaper and magazine companies, along with other writers, to places like Yŏngdosa, Myŏngwŏlgwan, or Siktowŏn, where I encountered food I had never eaten in my life and received smiles from kisaeng I wouldn't have dared to look up at before. My shoulders seemed to rise high with pride. However, whenever I faced fine food, the thought of my mother choked me up.
Brother,
I believe this is how people become vain. From then on, I subtly began to care about my appearance. I wanted to get haircuts often, examine my hands, and tried to wear clean clothes. But where would the essential element, money, come from to satisfy all these desires? This also became a source of torment, <37> but mother's face, appearing before my eyes from time to time, helped me reject all those temptations.
"Hmm, am I falling into vanity? This is not the time for me to seek comfort. I must only look towards my goal and disregard everything else."
When I thought like this, all fantasies seemed to fade away, yet when I went out on the street, my attention first went to my clothes, and when I met someone, the thought 'I am a writer' would arise. All temptations did not retreat easily. Thus, the mind rejecting temptation and the temptation trying to consume that mind fought painfully within my heart. It's embarrassing to tell you this, brother, but it was during this time that I first tasted love. She was a woman who came from the hometown of my close friend, Kim-gun, and was nineteen at the time. Not exactly beautiful, but her round facial features and intense gaze appealed to me.
"This is Mr. Pyŏn Kiun (my name), who writes novels."
"This is Ms. Chŏng Insuk, who works at XX Kindergarten."
After meeting Insuk through Kim-gun's introduction, I felt subtly drawn to her. Later, I met Kim-gun and asked,
"Hey, is that Ms. Insuk still in Seoul?"
"Why, are you interested? Let me act as a matchmaker so you two can form a sweet home,"
Kim-gun laughed. Whether fortunate or unfortunate, this became reality, and a so-called romance developed between Insuk and me. Love truly is mysterious, I believe. No matter how I think about it, how we met, the scenes of our meetings seem like a dream, indescribable. You probably don't know much about it, brother, but nowadays, almost all young men and women have probably tasted love. Yet, if you ask them, they all say it felt like a mysterious dream, indescribable. And thinking back now, that love, which feels so bitter, seemed so sweet then; I was intoxicated by its sweetness and didn't know what to do. Inexperienced in love, I fell into it and didn't know how to swim, which led to considerable trouble.
"What does love have to do with me, who left my old mother behind? I have much work to do..."
Though I thought this dozens of times a day and tried to break it off, I was drawn to Insuk's smile. From then on, I wanted to improve my appearance even more. Standing next to some gentleman in a "sebiro" (suit) while wearing my dirt-stained turumagi (Korean overcoat), if Insuk happened to see us, my eyes would dart between the gentleman's "sebiro" and my clothes, my shoulders would slump, and my whole body felt like sinking into the ground. At the same time, it felt as if the gentleman was insulting me, saying,
"Ah, how could a beautiful person like you love a beggar like this..."
making me feel even more wretched. Whenever such thoughts occurred, I felt incredibly ashamed and embarrassed in front of Insuk. So, sometimes I confessed such feelings to Insuk.
"What does money matter? We live by affection,"
Insuk would say whenever I confessed my feelings. Hearing such replies, I felt happy and simultaneously more apologetic. However, Insuk buying a piano and wrapping herself in silk, indulging in extreme luxury, did not sit well with my heart. <38> It seemed we could never truly reconcile. At that time, the magazine company had collapsed, and my circumstances were even more desperate, so such thoughts became stronger. Truly, my premonition was not wrong. Two months after I left the magazine company – it was the spring of Kyehae year (1923). One day, I went looking for Insuk.
"She moved out the day before yesterday, I don't know where,"
the landlord said. Suspicious, I returned home, unable to calm my uneasy mind. At that time, I was desperately pressed for rent money and struggling to sell manuscripts, so I let three days pass distractedly. On the fourth day, I heard from a friend that Insuk and Kim-gun, the one who introduced us, had somehow gotten together, and she was already three or four months pregnant. On the spot, I wanted to find those two bastards and stab them with a knife, but thinking,
"It's a foolish thought. She and I were never going to be truly compatible anyway, and what's the use of chasing someone who leaves? Where is the right to kill?"
I blamed my poor circumstances and gave up. At the same time, I finally saw the psychology of women. And I also learned the true nature of so-called close friends.
"I have a great purpose. This is no time to be heartbroken over love."
Still having lingering feelings, I tried to console myself with such forced reassurances. To speak without any deception, the reason I let it go then was probably because I was too hungry. Unable to see straight due to the struggle for rent money, I apologize to the proponents of 'love is supreme', but I couldn't devote all my energy to it. From then on, I couldn't lift my head from writing manuscripts. Although the manuscript fees were small, they were indispensable for my livelihood at the time, and besides, right or wrong, that was the only skill I had learned, so what else could I do?
I wrote manuscripts. Wrote them and sent them to magazine and newspaper companies. How could I possibly recount all the humiliating experiences after sending them? When they first requested them, they used all sorts of flattery to take them, then, patting their bellies, they would drag out paying the manuscript fees, which were worth less than dung, and even then, not pay properly. I couldn't fight over it, so I burned with frustration and anger alone. There were places that paid somewhat better, but it felt awkward to send manuscripts there every time. If it were just me, it might be different, but most writers were hoping for those fees, so how could the magazine companies accommodate everyone? Back then, like now, the difficulty of managing magazines was extreme. Writing purely for profit like this, without any regard for artistic impulse, I thought of manuscripts whenever I thought of money. So, I forced out feelings that weren't there, squeezing them out, writing on and on. This pain was considerable. When something clearly seemed bad to my eyes, I would keep writing it, but then, unable to bear it conscience-strickenly, I'd think,
"Eck, stop it!"
and throw down the pen, tearing up the manuscript – this happened more than once or twice. But then, thinking of "next month's rent," I would pick up the pen again, like eating mustard with tears. I had to write, but nothing came out, frustration built up, and sometimes I even pulled my own hair in vain anger.
Also, at that time, payment wasn't based on the quality of the writing but on the number of pages, <39> so even things I wanted to shorten because they were rambling, I sent them as they were, fearing the fee would decrease. Thus, I gradually declined, and writing without any study, what brilliant words could possibly emerge? Yet, living like that, the desire to study was mountain-high, but I couldn't.
Eventually, even my so-called principles changed. My vow never to write for the organs of any group or individual whose principles differed from mine changed to:
"I'll write. Anywhere. As long as they pay."
Doing this, I naturally incurred the criticism of friends, and the torment of my remaining conscience grew daily. Even if I hated the stance of a certain magazine or newspaper, I swallowed my pride to sell the manuscript. The pain of enduring that was truly immense. At this time, I not only wrote things I didn't believe in but also forced smiles I didn't feel. I had long felt my work turning into a commodity, but gradually reflecting on my attitude, I couldn't help but think of the prostitutes (賣春婦) of Shinmachi (新町). Who would wish to become a prostitute (賣春婦)? But the threat to livelihood forces them into such pits. Similarly, I came to think that I too – my art too – was becoming a prostitute. If my thoughts had stopped there, it wouldn't have been a problem, but they went a step further:
"But they – the prostitutes – become that way without being conscious of this and that, so there's room for forgiveness. But you (I), being conscious of all this and that, can you bear to do such work?"
This thought struck my head, causing even more agony. After pondering deeply like this, I came to strongly sympathize with the actions of XX-ism (XX主義). It was at this time that XX-ist thought clearly took hold in my mind, and it was also at this time that my footsteps began to frequent XX-ist groups. At first, I went around spouting fiery rhetoric as if we could build an ideal society in two or three days, but realizing it wasn't something achievable in a day or two, my fervor gradually subsided. Subsided not in despair, but because the thought struck me that first, methods needed to be established, and during the time of establishing methods, one must eat.
Brother,
Thus, I again sought the employment I had been seeking before. Buttering people up here, currying favor there, getting introductions, speaking directly, I tried to get a position as a newspaper reporter. But lacking the signboard of a diploma and a strong background, I couldn't avoid failure. Then, as now, there were quite a few candidates for newspaper reporter positions, and I saw some offering bribes like mountains to the presidents and editorial directors of certain newspaper and magazine companies. In such a situation, how could I, without bribes, get a foothold? Moreover, then as now, if I had the means to offer bribes, I would have used it to feed myself. Seeing this situation – the hidden ugliness within a newspaper company, supposedly the public instrument and representative of the masses – I grew weary of this world, hated it, and wanted to smash it. Eventually, in solitary anger, I cursed the ugliness of newspaper and magazine companies, but then thought,
"It's all my fault. If I truly had outstanding knowledge, <40> they would seek me out before I even tried. Let me improve myself first!"
And I attributed everything to my lack of knowledge, consequently trying to cultivate knowledge. But again, the problem was cultivating knowledge. With what resources could I cultivate knowledge? Living uncertainly like this, I eventually considered abandoning everything and returning to the countryside. But thinking of my situation – not having even a square foot of land in the countryside and not knowing how to farm – that too seemed like a fantasy.
"Eck, I'll carry dung buckets anywhere!"
Even thinking this, the vanity and concern for appearances remaining in a corner of my mind did not readily permit it, and with a lingering hope, I decided to try again for a reporter position at some newspaper. While dithering like this for about a year, I received the sad news about mother.

4
Brother,
I still haven't forgotten that time. It was the morning of the fourth day of the second lunar month last year. At that time too, I was heading out to look for work when I received your telegram stating:
"Mother critically ill."
As days passed like this, the sorrow grew, but at that moment, whether sad or resentful, I was just dumbfounded, not knowing what to do. I stood blankly, as if dreaming, and left the boarding house with an indifferent attitude. Thinking back now, I believe I was so shocked that my entire nervous system was paralyzed. Leaving the boarding house like that, I walked towards Chongno, then gradually regained my senses, and sorrow surged up, so I returned to the boarding house and wept. The day after receiving the telegram, I received your handwritten letter and was even more distraught.
"How much are you crying after receiving the telegram?
Mother is gone. Mother is gone forever. As she was passing, she called your name dozens of times. I didn't know mother would go so easily. For three days, she complained of a headache and nausea, didn't eat, and lay down. On the morning of the fourth day, she suddenly vomited blood, beat her chest, kept calling for you, and then passed away.
Because she went so suddenly, I couldn't even write to you. If I had known, I would have notified you earlier so you could have been here at her deathbed. Your foolish brother has inflicted an eternal, indelible regret upon mother's and your hearts."
After reading this letter from you, brother, the world turned black. All the blood in my body seemed to clot in my heart, feeling like it would burst, my throat constricted, and I felt so terribly guilty, I wanted to run to mother's grave and cry,
"Mother, mother, please kill this unfilial son!"
But I couldn't even do that.
Mother passed away because of me. If this unfilial son hadn't been so desperately missed, would she have called for me until her last breath? I truly cannot bring myself to speak, to convey this sorrow and that. I trust you deeply understand, brother. Since then, my resentment towards the world has grown even stronger. How could I not feel resentment? Life and death are natural, who can prevent them! But still, if I hadn't been born into such a distorted society, why would mother have passed away like that, and why would I have done such unforgivable things? <41> Truly afraid to face the heavens, I went to the Han River several times intending to die, drew a knife, but wanting to see how this world turns out, I gave up the thought. What use is it if I die? Even if I die, this world will remain as it is, and the facts of my past life will remain as facts. Besides, who would even sniffle just because my single self disappeared?
"In this world, there's no one to trust but myself."
From this time, I felt this keenly. Everyone is in the same boat, when would they think of my affairs? The world knows how to sneer but doesn't know how to rescue or help. They might sneer if someone who enjoyed glory yesterday carries dung buckets today, but they don't know how to help. Also, carrying dung buckets doesn't inherently damage one's character. It's because people can't shed their masks that they are pulled this way and that.
At this point, I finally made a firm decision and took up this shoe-repair kit. I became a cobbler (갓바치). Despite having made the decision, when I first went out onto the street with the kit on my back, even the dirt on the roadside seemed to mock me. If I saw friends' faces in the distance, I would stealthily avoid them. Habits truly are that difficult to break.
"Hmph, if they mock me, will they feed me? And does carrying this (shoe-repair kit) damage my character?"
Steeling myself like this, I have come this far. Comparing my past life with my present life, I regret not having done this work sooner. It's truly comfortable. I keenly feel that things like being a gentleman, western suits, shoes, glasses, and reputation truly kill a person.
Brother,
However, please do not hear my song -
"Shoes repaired! Shoes shined!"
- this cobbler's song, as merely the sound of someone content with a comfortable life. At the same time, please do not think I have fallen just because I live this life.
"When will you too become a county magistrate or a teacher like others?"
This might seem quite unsatisfactory to your heart, brother, which wished this for me, but I do not want to live such a life of falsehood and intoxication. The world may call that comfortable, but to me, it is not comfort, and while they may regard it as the highest ideal, I believe that is talk born from not yet knowing the sorrow of hunger. Also, I have no right to seek comfort. Mother passed away like that, how could I seek comfort? Even living for a day and seeing the sky feels overwhelming, but I intend to live even one day longer. Becoming a cobbler was also for that reason; it's because I want to extend this life even by a day. If this life lasts even one day longer, these two eyes will see more clearly how this world is changing, and these arms and legs will fight for one day longer.
Brother,
Now, the only way to console mother's departed spirit and resolve my own resentment is this. Therefore, I cannot accept your warm heart, your wife's deep affection, and Yongson's innocent intention. My heart tears even more for not being able to accept them. You are a brother who truly cares for me, and you are the only brother I have, so how could I let your words go in one ear and out the other? I believe that upon seeing this farewell address (餞迓辭) of mine, welcoming a new life after annihilating the old, you will clear away all doubts. November 25, 1926 <42>

**Summary**

This fictional piece, written as a letter from a man named Pyŏn Kiun to his elder brother-figure (Hyŏngnim), details his painful journey from a hopeful young man in the provinces to a disillusioned intellectual attempting to survive in colonial Seoul, ultimately finding a form of harsh peace as a street cobbler. He recounts his poverty, the sacrifices of his mother, his frustrated attempts at education and literary success, failed relationships, encounters with social hypocrisy, and his eventual embrace of manual labor as the only honest path left, rejecting his brother's plea to return home. The story powerfully illustrates the crushing economic hardship, social stratification, and limited opportunities faced by aspiring intellectuals in 1920s colonial Korea. Historians might find the depiction of urban migration, the disillusionment with both traditional hierarchies and modern aspirations (like literature or journalism), the veiled references to socialist ideas (XX주의), and the narrator's eventual choice of low-status labor as a form of resistance or authenticity particularly revealing about the socio-economic and psychological pressures of the period.

하고 모든 것을 나의 학식 업는 탓으로 돌리엇고 딸아서 학식을 닥그려고 하엿슴니다. 그러나 또 문제는 학식 닥는 것임니다. 무슨 여유로 학식을 닥슴니까? 이러케 민민히 지내던 끄테 나는 모든 것을 버리고 농촌으로 돌아가려고 하엿슴니다. 그러나 농촌에 간대야 땅 한평도 업고 농사질 줄도 모르는 내 힘을 생각하면 그것도 공상이엇슴니다.
『엑 아무데서나 똥통이라도 메지!』
이러케까지 생각하면서도 그저 맘 한구통이에 남은 허영과 체면은 얼른 그것을 허락지 안코 행여나 하는 희망으로 다시 어느 신문사 긔자로 운동하리라 하엿슴니다. 이러케 어물어물하고 일년이나 지내던 판에 어머니의 흉음을 바닷슴니다.

4
형님,
지금도 그때가 이치지 안슴니다. 그것이 작년 이월 초사흔날 아침이엇슴니다. 그때에도 직업운동을 나가던 판인데
『모주 작고』
라는 형님의 전보를 바닷슴니다. 날이 가고 가서 이러케 되면서는 설음이 점점 커지는데 그때에는 슬픈지 원통하지 그저 어리벙벙해서 어쩔 줄을 몰랏슴니다. 멀거니 꿈꾸듯 섯다가 무심한 태도로 하숙을 나섯슴니다. 지금 생각하면 그때 넘우도 놀라서 온 신경이 마비가 되엇던 것이라고 생각함니다. 나는 그러케 하숙을 나서서 종로로 나가다가 차츰 정신이 들고 설음이 복바치어 하숙에 돌아가 울엇슴니다. 전보 밧든 이튼날 형님의 친필을 밧고서는 더욱 어쩔 줄을 몰랏슴니다.
『전보를 밧고 얼마나 우늬?
어머니는 가섯다. 어머니느 영영 가섯다. 어머니는 가시는 때에 너를 수십번 부르섯다. 어머니는 그러케 쉽게 가실 줄 몰랏다. 사흘 동안이나 머리가 아프시고 가슴이 울렁거리신다고 하시면서 음식을 잡숫지 안코 누어 게시다가 나흘 되던 날 아침에 갑작이 피를 토하시고 가슴을 치시면서 너를 작구 부르시다가 돌아가섯다.
이러케 급히 가시게 되어서 네게 편지도 못하엿다. 그럴줄 알앗더면 네게 밀이 통지나 하여 임종에 뵙게 할 것을 미련한 형은 천고의 슬어지지 못할 한을 어머니와 네 가슴에 박앗구나』
나는 이러한 형님의 편지를 읽고나서 천지가 앗질하엿슴니다. 온 몸의 피가 모도 심장에 엉키어 들어서 심장이 터지고 목구녕이 메는 듯하고 어떠케 죄송한지 어머니의 무덤에라도 달아가서
『어머니 어머니 이 불효 자식을 죽여줍시오』
하고 시펏스나 그것도 못하엿슴니다.
어머니께서는 나 때문에 돌아가섯슴니다. 이 불효자식이 여복 보고시프섯스면 림종까지 부르섯겟슴니까? 나는 참아 입이 떨어지지 안하서 이런 말슴 저런 설음을 여쭐 수 업슴니다. 형님은 기피 통촉하실 줄 밋슴니다. 그 뒤로부터 세상에 대한 나의 원망은 더 커지엇슴니다. 내게 어찌 원망이 업겟슴니까? 죽고 사는 것은 자연이라 누가 막으리오! 마는 그래도 이러한 변태적 사회에 나지 안햇드면 웨 어머니가 그러케 돌아가섯스며 내가 이러케 못할 짓을 하엇겟슴니까? ＜41＞ 나는 참아 하늘이 보기 무서워서 몃변이나 죽으려고 한강까지 갓다오고 칼을 빼어 들엇다가도 이 세상이 어찌 되는 것을 보려고 그만 단념하엿슴니다. 내가 죽으면 소용잇슴니까? 내가 죽어도 이 세상은 세상대로 잇슬 것이요 나의 지내온 사실은 사실대로 남아 잇슬 것임니다. 또 내 한 몸이 업서젓다고 누가 코나 찡그리겟슴니까.
『세상에는 나 밧게 미들 놈이 업다』
이때부터 나는 이러한 느낌을 절실이 바닷슴니다. 모도 그러한 꼴인데 언제 나의 일을 생각하겟슴니까. 세상은 비웃을 줄은 알아도 건저주고 도와줄 줄은 모름니다. 어제는 영화를 누리다가 오늘날 똥통을 멘다고 비웃기는 하지만 도을줄은 모름니다. 또한 똥통을 멘다고 그 인격에 손상이 생길리도 업는 것임니다. 모도 탈을 못 벗은 까닭에 이리저리 끌리는 것임니다.
나는 이에 비릇오 꽉 결심하고 이 구두ㅅ짐을 젓슴니다. 갓바치 노릇을 하엿슴니다. 그러케 결심하엿것마는 처음 구두ㅅ짐을 지고 거리에 나서니 길ㅅ가의 흙까지 비웃는 듯하엿슴니다. 친구들의 나티 먼데 보이면 슬근이 피하여 젓슴니다. 참 습관이란 그처럼 벗기가 어려운 것이엇슴니다.
『흥 그네가 나를 비웃으면 나를 먹이어 줄테냐? 또 내가 이것(구두ㅅ짐)을 젓다고 내 인격에 흠이 생기나?』
이러케 스스로 가다듬으면서 오늘까지 내리어왓슴니다. 예전날 생활과 오늘날 생활을 비교하는 때마다 나는 벌서 웨 이런 일을 못하엿던구 하는 후회가 남니다. 참 편함니다. 신사니, 양복이니, 구두니, 안경이니, 명예니 하는 것이 참으로 사람을 죽인다는 것을 절실히 낌니다.

그러나 나의 노래를 -
『구두 곤칩시요! 구두약 칠합시오』
하는 이 갓바치의 노래를 참으로 편한 신세를 읇조리는 소리로는 듯지 마시기를 바람니다. 동시에 내가 이러한 생활을 한다고 타락이라고도 생각지 마소서.
『언제나 너도 남과 가티 군수나 교사나』
하시던 형님의 맘에는 퍽 못맛당하게 생각되시겟지만 나는 그런 허위의 생활과 취한 생활은 하고 십지 안슴니다. 세상은 그것을 편하다하지만 내게는 그것이 편한 것이 아니요 그네들도 그것을 최대이상으로 녀기지만 그것은 아직도 배고픈 설음을 몰라서 하는 수작이라고 밋슴니다. 또 나는 안일을 구할만한 권리도 업슴니다. 어머니는 그러케 돌아가섯는데 내가 어찌 안일을 구하겟슴니까. 하로라도 살아서 하늘 보는 것까지 황송함니다마는 나는 하로라도 살기는 더 살려고 함니다. 내가 갓바치 된 것도 그 때문이니 하로라도 이 목숨을 더 늘이려고 하는 까닭임니다. 이 목숨이 하로라도 더 부터 잇스면 그만침 이 두 눈은 이 세상이 되어가는 꼴을 똑똑히 볼 것이요 이 팔과 다리는 하로라도 더 싸워 줄 것임니다.
형님,
이제 어머니의 원혼을 위로하고 내 원한을 풀 길은 이 밧게 업슴니다. 이럼으로 형님의 따뜻한 맘과 아즈머니의 두터운 정과 용손의 순진하 뜻을 못 밧는 것임니다. 그것을 못 밧는 내 가슴은 더욱 찟김니다. 형님은 진정으로 나를 위하시는 형님이요 내게는 오직 형님 한분이시라 어찌 형님의 말슴을 귀 밧그로 듯겟슴니까. 형님께서는 - 이제 이 옛날의 생활을 전멸하고 새생활을 맛는 나의 전아사(餞迓辭)를 보시고 모든 의심을 푸실 줄 밋슴니다. 1926, 11월 25일 ＜42＞
