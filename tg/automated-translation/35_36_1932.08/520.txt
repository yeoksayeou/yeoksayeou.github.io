The Tent of the Arabs
						By Lord Dunsany (Author), Yi Ha-yun (Translator)
(Characters)
King
Belnabŭ, Aubŭ -- Camel Drivers
Minister
Chabŭra (Notable)
Eznaza (Desert Gypsy Woman)

(Setting)
Outside the city gates of T'allanna

(Time)
Unspecified

Act 1
Outside the city gates of T'allanna

Belnabŭ: By evening, we'll be walking the desert again.
Aubŭ: Right.
Belnabŭ: Then for many weeks, we won't be able to see a city.
Aubŭ: Ah... ah...
Belnabŭ: Looking back on the path the camels trod, we'll see twinkling lights. That will be the last city we see.
Aubŭ: By then, we'll already be in the desert.
Belnabŭ: Inside the wrathful old desert.
Aubŭ: Why does that blasted desert hide its springs so cunningly? Perhaps the desert holds some grudge against us humans. The desert doesn't welcome people like the city does.
Belnabŭ: Of course, it holds a grudge. I hate the desert.
Aubŭ: Perhaps there's nothing in the world as beautiful as a city.
Belnabŭ: Truly, the city is a beautiful thing.
Aubŭ: I think the city is most beautiful sometime after dawn, when night vanishes from the houses.<107> The city slowly distances itself from the night, as if taking off a coat. It sheds the night. And stands naked, reflecting its beautiful shadow in some wide river. Then the stars rise and kiss its brow. I think the city is most beautiful then. The voices of men and women begin to rise in the streets. Barely audible at first, they rise one by one, and finally, all voices merge into a slow, large sound. At such times, I often feel as if the city is speaking to me. The city might speak to me in its own voice like this: "Aubŭ, Aubŭ. You will die someday. I am not of this world's common things. I have always existed since long ago. I never die."
Belnabŭ: Unlike you, I don't think the city is most beautiful at dawn. We can always see the dawn in the desert. I think the city is most beautiful just as the sun sets and darkness gently descends upon every narrow street. A time that is not yet night, yet cannot be called day either; that mysterious early evening when we can see the shapes of people in coats but cannot quite make out who they are. And just as it's about to get dark, when we're out in the desert and can see nothing but the pitch-black horizon and the pitch-black sky above it, right then, aren't lanterns swayed by the wind lit, lamps appear one by one in windows, and don't the colors of clothes look completely different from the daytime? Then a woman might secretly slip out of some small door and flee into the night streets. Or a man, hiding a knife, might secretly creep in to settle an old grudge (遺恨). And 'Sŭk'ami' will keep his shop lit up as bright as day and sell alcohol all night long. Men will sit on chairs placed outside his shop, gambling under a small, dim blue lantern, smoking strong tobacco from large pipes billowing smoke. Hmm, seeing such things is truly good. And as I sit smoking my pipe and watching these things, perhaps far away in the desert, large red clouds are spreading like wings. Looking at those clouds, any Arab knows that tomorrow a hot wind (열풍) will blow, the cursed breath of 'Eblis', the father of the devil 'Satan' – thinking such thoughts is also good.
Aubŭ: Indeed, thinking about the hot wind is pleasant when one is safely in the city, but I don't want to think about it now. Before this day is over, we'll be heading towards 'Mek'a'. Besides, hasn't it been said that no one has ever predicted or found out what lies in the desert? Going into the desert is like tossing bones one by one to a dog. Some bones might end up in the dog's mouth, while others might be dropped. The desert might put our bones in its mouth. Or perhaps we might pass through the desert and reach the splendid 'Mek'a'. Ah, I wish I could become a merchant, sit all day in a small shop on a bustling street, and earn some money.
Belnabŭ: Yes, indeed. Tricking some nobleman who comes to the city to buy silk or trinkets is easier than <108> cheating death in the desert. Oh! Desert, desert. I love the beautiful city. And I hate the desert.
Aubŭ: (Pointing far to the left) Who's that over there?
Belnabŭ: What? You mean the person visible near the desert edge where the camels are?
Aubŭ: Yes, who could that be?
Belnabŭ: That person is looking at the path where the desert camels walk. They say the king often comes out to the edge of the desert and looks towards the desert. In the evening, he stands there for a long time looking towards 'Mek'a'.
Aubŭ: The king is looking towards 'Mek'a'? What good does that do? He can't go to 'Mek'a', can he? He can't even go into the desert for a single day. Messengers would immediately follow, call his name, and take him to the council chamber or the court. If those messengers couldn't find the king, that fellow's head would be chopped off and placed high on some windswept roof. And then a judge would point to the head and say, 'It seems one can see better from here,' wouldn't he?
Belnabŭ: Right. The king cannot leave for the desert. If God made me king, I would go out to the edge of the desert once, shake the sand from my turban and beard, and then resolve never to see the desert again. Let the ill-tempered, greedy old man with thousands of devils as children cover the springs with sand and unleash hot winds; whether it takes years or hundreds of years, I wouldn't care a bit. If God made me king.
Aubŭ: Everyone says you resemble the king.
Belnabŭ: Yes, I resemble the king. Since the king's father used to dress like a camel driver and visit the villages where we live, I often think to myself: 'God is fair. So if I disguised myself as the king and drove the king out as a camel driver, perhaps God, being fair, would even be pleased.'
Aubŭ: If you did such a thing, God might say, 'Look at Belnabŭ. I made him be born a camel driver, but he has forgotten it,' and He might forget you, Belnabŭ.
Belnabŭ: Who knows what God might say.
Aubŭ: Who could know? What He does is strange.
Belnabŭ: I wouldn't do such a thing, Aubŭ. I wouldn't. It's just something I think about alone when I'm smoking my pipe or when I'm in the desert at night. 'Belnabŭ is the King of T'allanna,' I think to myself. And I try saying, 'Minister! Summon 'Sŭk'ami' and bring wine, lanterns, and the gambling table here. And gather all the townspeople, let them drink wine in front of the palace and praise my name.'
Pilgrims: (Calling from afar) Belnabŭ, Belnabŭ, you son of a dog! Come quickly and lead the camels! Let's hurry and depart for the holy land of Mek'a!
Belnabŭ: Cursed desert! I wish it would be struck by lightning!
Aubŭ: The camels are getting up. The caravan is leaving for Mek'a. Farewell, beautiful city. Goodbye then.
(Pilgrims' voices calling "Belnabŭ, Belnabŭ" from afar.)
Belnabŭ: Coming, coming, you hasty sons of dogs!
(Belnabŭ and Aubŭ exit)
(The King, wearing his crown, comes out from the gate and sits down on the stone bench.)<109>
King: A crown is not something for a king's head. A king's jade scepter (玉笏) is not something to be carried in a king's hand. It is right that the crown be woven with gold chains and the scepter be driven into the ground like a stake, and the king's ankles be tied there. If only that were done, the king could perhaps resign himself to the fact that he cannot wander the beautiful desert or see the palm trees by the spring. Oh T'allanna, T'allanna, I hate this city. How I hate this city with its narrow streets and the wicked 'Sŭk'ami's' place where drunken fellows engage in dirty gambling every evening. Oh, I wish to marry the daughter of a lowly family whose ancestors for generations have never seen a city. Then we would ride camels together from here down the long desert road, just the two of us, even as far as an Arab's tent. And the crown—let's give it to some foolish, greedy person as a seed of suffering (괴롬) and sorrow (섫음). But all this is impossible. A king, no matter what, is undoubtedly still a king.
(The Minister enters through the gate.)
Minister: Your Majesty (페하).
King: Ah, Minister. Is it you? Is there more business for me?
Minister: Yes, there is much work to be done.
King: I was just hoping for a little free time tonight. The camels are turning their heads towards 'Mek'a'. So I wanted to at least bid farewell to the caravan departing into the desert I cannot go to.
Minister: There is too much for Your Majesty to do. The nation of Ikt'ŭra has rebelled.
King: Where is this Ikt'ŭra?
Minister: It is a small country that pays tribute to Your Majesty. It is a nation in the 'Chebŭtallŭn' mountains.
King: If only that hadn't happened, I might have asked you this favor: to let me join the camel drivers and head towards the golden holy land of 'Mek'a'. It's already been five years that I've played the role of king and listened to my subjects, and all this time the desert has always been calling me. 'Come to the tents of my children. Come to the tents of my children,' it says, yet all this time I've lived only within these castle walls.
Minister: If Your Majesty were to leave this city now—.
King: I won't leave. We must raise soldiers and severely punish the people of Ikt'ŭra.
Minister: Please designate the names of the generals, Your Majesty. Your Majesty's troops must be summoned from 'Agŭraba', from the city 'K'olduno' in the thicket, and also from 'Mŏsŭk'ŭ'. For this, an edict (칙령) personally stamped with Your Majesty's royal seal (옥쇄) is required. Several people who have matters to report to Your Majesty are also waiting in the council chamber.
King: The sun has almost set, why hasn't the caravan departed yet?
Minister: I do not know. Then, Your Majesty—
King: (Placing his hand on the Minister's arm) Look there. Look at that! It's the shadow of the camels departing for 'Mek'a'. See how quietly that beautiful shadow glides over the ground. Soon they will emerge into the desert and flatten onto the golden sand—. Then the sun will set, and they will be enveloped in darkness, becoming one with the night.
Minister: If Your Majesty is interested in such things, there are plenty of camels...<110>
King: No, no. I don't want to see camels. Because camels can never take me to the beautiful desert. Forever leaving the city, freely... I must stay here and attend to the king's duties. Only my dreams can go. The shadow of the camels carries my dreams— to seek peace beside the tents of the Arabs.
Minister: Will Your Majesty proceed to the council chamber now?
King: Yes, alright. I'll go now.
(Sound from afar: 『Hoyo! Hoei! Hoyo! Hoei!』)
King: The entire caravan is leaving now! Listen to the sound of the camel drivers loading their cargo. They will run alongside the camels for the first ten miles, then tomorrow they will mount the camels' backs. By then, even looking back at 'T'allanna', nothing will be visible. Around them, only the desert will lie spread out, receiving the sunlight shining down on the smiling golden sand. Then a new light will dawn on their faces. I firmly believe that at night the desert will whisper to them, 'Peace be with you, my children. Peace be with you, my children.'
(While he says this, the Minister holds the door open for the King, bowing respectfully. The King places his hand firmly on the open door.)
Minister: Your Majesty, will you proceed to the council chamber?
King: Yes, I'll go right away. If only this Ikt'ŭra incident hadn't occurred, I might have set out on the road, lived for a year in the golden desert, and seen the holy land of 'Mek'a'.
Minister: Perhaps Your Majesty might have departed if the Ikt'ŭra incident hadn't happened. I curse Ikt'ŭra—.
(The King moves to enter the door.)
(Just as the King and Minister reach the door, Chabŭra enters from the right.)
Chabŭra: Your Majesty.
King: Ah, ah, what else has befallen this unfortunate king?
Chabŭra: Ikt'ŭra is pacified.
King: Pacified?
Chabŭra: It happened suddenly. The people of Ikt'ŭra engaged with Your Majesty's few soldiers, and by chance, an arrow struck the enemy commander. So the rebel army, though numerous, all fled. They were reportedly shouting 'Long live His Majesty the King' for three hours.
King: Will I really get to see the land of 'Mek'a' and the tents of the Arabs I've dreamt of? From now on, departing for the golden desert, I shall—
Minister: Your Majesty—
King: After a few years, I shall return to you all.
Minister: Your Majesty, that is impossible. We cannot possibly govern the people for more than a year. They will say, 'The King has passed away. The King!'
King: Then I shall return in one year, exactly within one year.
Minister: Your Majesty, even a year is a long time.
King: I shall return at noon on the day one year from today.
Minister: But Your Majesty, we also need to go and escort the princess arriving from the nation of 'T'aba'.
King: I thought she was coming from 'K'aswiswi'.
Minister: Everyone thought it would be more advantageous for Your Majesty to form ties with 'T'aba'.<111> The difficult mountain pass over the border belongs to the King of 'T'aba'. Moreover, that king has significant trade with 'Swaran' and several other islands.
King: Do as you please.
Minister: But Your Majesty, the ambassadors depart within two weeks. The princess will arrive here within three months.
King: Make it noon on the day after one year from today.
Minister: Your Majesty!
King: Well then, farewell. I am busy. I must prepare to go out into the desert.
(Sound of speech after entering the door.)
King: ...Mother of happy people, only that age... into the golden desert...
Minister: (To Chabŭra) A person given wisdom by God would not have delivered such a report to our young king who is prone to madness.
Chabŭra: But wouldn't he find out even if not asked? If not reported immediately, who knows what various other matters might arise.
Minister: I've known about that matter since this morning. Now His Majesty is leaving for the desert.
Chabŭra: Truly, an unexpected thing has happened. But we must try to persuade him to return somehow.
Minister: He probably won't return easily anytime soon.
Chabŭra: His Majesty's favor is like gold.
Minister: Like much gold. What kind of Arabs are these who receive the grace the King will bestow? The walls of their houses are scraps of old cloth. Even the snails crawling there carry finer walls than their houses.
Chabŭra: Ah, this is indeed a great problem. I wish I had said such things. We will become poor people.
Minister: Now, for a long time, there will be no one to give us money.
Chabŭra: But while the King is away, you will govern T'allanna, won't you? You can freely collect more taxes from merchants or farmers.
Minister: They pay taxes only to the King. When he is in 'T'allanna', he bestows grace upon the loyal and honest, but while he is absent, even the goods that should fill the King's treasury will end up in the hands of the unrighteous or those with dirty beards who do not fear God.
Chabŭra: Truly, we will become poor beggars.
Minister: Some gold might come in from those who commit unjust acts. Or perhaps some money might be generated from settling the trial of some legitimate rich person. But beyond this, there is nothing more to hope for until the King, watched over by God, returns.
Chabŭra: May God protect him. But how will you try to dissuade him?
Minister: No, if His Majesty comes with an entourage and guards, I will walk beside his horse and tell him that if His Majesty passes through the desert, the Arabs will be impressed by his authority and devote their whole hearts to him. And I will secretly whisper to the officer following behind the guards, so that this officer later tells the commander-in-chief something like this: within a few days, lose the camel tracks, let His Majesty<112> and his retinue all wander in the desert, and arrange it so they somehow accidentally find their way back to 'T'allanna'. Of course, I think it will work out well. Let's wait here until His Majesty's party arrives.
Chabŭra: Will the commander-in-chief definitely do such a thing?
Minister: Yes, the man named 'T'akŭba' is poor and upright.
Chabŭra: But what if it's not 'T'akŭba' but some other greedy person who demands more gold than what would be given to 'T'akŭba'?
Minister: Well, in that case, we have no choice but to give him what he wants. God will punish the sin of greed.
Chabŭra: His Majesty will pass by where we are, won't he?
Minister: Of course, he'll pass this way. He'll summon the cavalry from 'Salloyia'.
Chabŭra: Then the cavalry will arrive after dark?
Minister: No, he's in a great hurry, so he'll pass before sunset. He'll order the horses brought out immediately.
Chabŭra: (Looking far to the right) Nothing seems to have happened towards 'Salloyia'.
Minister: (Also looking there) No, no, I don't see anything either. But His Majesty will surely cause a commotion.
(As they are watching, a man wearing a worn brown coat pulled down over his forehead comes out of the gate and quietly exits to the left.)
Minister: Who could that person be? He walked towards where the camels are.
Chabŭra: It looks like he's giving money to some camel driver.
Minister: Look, he mounted a camel.
Chabŭra: Could it possibly be the King?
(Sound of "Hoyo! Hoei!" comes from far left.)
Minister: What, it must be some camel driver departing for the desert. How full of joy his voice sounds.
Chabŭra: The hot wind might swallow that man up.
Minister: Oh dear, what if that was the King!
Chabŭra: Well, if that was the King, we just have to endure hardship for a year.
—Curtain—

Act 2
Setting: Same as before
Two years later
(The King, wrapped in a camel driver's cloak, sits with the desert gypsy woman Eznaza.)

King: Now I know the desert, and I have lived in the tent of the Arabs.
Eznaza: There is no place better than the desert. And there are no people better than the Arabs.
King: Everything has already passed. I return to the castle of my ancestors.
Eznaza: The god of Time cannot help that. I will return to the desert that raised me.
King: Did you ever think, during those days spent on the sand or the mornings greeted in the tent, that the day would come when my year was up, and driven by the power of my own promise, I would be dragged back like a prisoner to the royal castle?
Eznaza: I knew the god of Time would make it so. Our people well understand the power of the god of Time.<113>
King: Then did the god of Time laugh at our useless prayers? Is he who laughs at our prayers greater than God?
Eznaza: He cannot be called greater than God. But we prayed that our precious year would not pass. Even God could not save that year.
King: Right, yes, we offered such prayers. Others would all laugh if they heard.
Eznaza: Those prayers are not laughable prayers. Only the master who governs the sun was heartless. If someone begged a ruthless, wicked 'Sultan' to save his life, the 'Sultan's' slaves might laugh, but I don't think begging for one's life is laughable.
King: True. We are slaves of the god of Time. Tomorrow is the day the queen arrives from the nation of 'T'aba'. Let us bow our heads and submit to fate.
Eznaza: Our people say the god of Time lives in the desert. That he is there in the sun.
King: No, no, he doesn't live in the desert. Doesn't nothing ever change there?
Eznaza: Our people say the desert is his homeland. That's why he doesn't destroy the homeland where he was born, but our people say he conquers all lands outside the desert, anywhere in the world.
King: Truly so. The desert is always the same. Down to the smallest pebble.
Eznaza: Also, they say the god of Time loves the Sphinx, and does not harm it. That he wouldn't dare think of harming the Sphinx. The Sphinx bore the god of Time many gods worshipped by those pagans.
King: The father of those gods is more fearsome than all the gods of falsehood.
Eznaza: Oh, if only the god of Time had spared our little year.
King: He spares nothing; he destroys everything completely.
Eznaza: There is a little son of a man stronger than the god of Time. That son saves this world from the god of Time.
King: What is this little son, stronger than the god of Time? Is it the god of Love? Is that which is stronger than time—
Eznaza: No, not the god of Love.
King: If it can conquer even the god of Love, there would be nothing stronger in the world.
Eznaza: The god of Time can chase away even the god of Love with frailty, white hair, and wrinkles. Poor little Love, pitiful god of Love, the god of Time drives him away.
King: What is this son of man who overcomes even the god of Time and is braver than the god of Love?
Eznaza: It is called Memory.
King: Indeed. When the wind blows from the desert and locusts strike our heartless city walls, I shall call upon Memory. When I cannot see the desert nor hear the sound of the desert wind, still I shall call upon Memory.
Eznaza: Memory will bring back our year that the god of Time cannot erase. If Memory just says 'No,' the god of Time cannot kill that year. Even though that year has already <114> been condemned, the execution of the sentence is suspended (猶豫). From a place a little far off, we can see that year as many times as we want. All the hours and days of that year come to us one by one, dance and go, then come and dance again.
King: Yes, that's true. They return to us. I thought that even one who performs miracles in heaven or on earth could not do this one thing. I thought the past days, once entered into the hands of the god of Time, could never be reclaimed.
Eznaza: That is the magic Memory can perform. Memory quietly creeps in wherever people are, whether in the city or the desert, even for just a few days. And like a strange and bizarre magician who sings to snakes, it performs its magic before people. It does it again and again, over and over.
King: Let us sometimes ask Memory to bring back the old days that have passed. Even after you return to your people, and I pitifully marry the princess who comes from 'T'aba'.
Eznaza: Memory will come with sand on its feet from the beautiful golden desert. Each memory will come bearing on its head the sunset of some day already long past. Its lips will smile with the same sound as some past evening.
King: It's almost noon. It's already noon. Noon has come.
Eznaza: Well then, let us part ways.
King: Oh! Come to the city and become queen there. I will send the princess back to 'T'aba'. Please, you become the queen of T'allanna.
Eznaza: I will go to where my people live. You will marry the princess of 'T'aba' tomorrow. You said so. I knew it would be so.
King: Oh, if only I hadn't promised to return!
Eznaza: A king's word is the same as his crown, his jade scepter (玉笏), and his throne. Though, like the city, it's truly useless.
King: I cannot break my promise. But you could become the queen of 'T'allanna', couldn't you?
Eznaza: T'allanna probably wouldn't accept a gypsy as queen.
King: I will make T'allanna accept a gypsy as queen.
Eznaza: I believe you couldn't make a gypsy live inside the city for even a year.
King: I know one gypsy who came to live in the city.
Eznaza: I am not that kind of gypsy... Let's hurry back to the tent of the Arabs.
King: I cannot go. I have given my word.
Eznaza: There have been kings who broke their promises.
King: I am not such a king.
Eznaza: Then only the little son of man called Memory belongs to us.
King: Yes, that's right. Memory will return to us one of the vanished days before we parted.
Eznaza: It seems best to make it the very first day. The day we met by the spring when the camels arrived at 'Elollidŭ'.
King: Our year is short a few days. My year began from here. By then, it was already several days after the camels had set out into the desert.<115>
Eznaza: You came riding a camel over the sunset-bathed land, a little apart from the caravan. Your camel was swaying and striding along. Yet you looked very tired.
King: You had come to draw water at the spring. At first, I could see your eyes. Then the stars rose, the surroundings darkened, and only your form was visible. And only above your head remained a little bright light. Whether it was because the starlight shone, or for some other reason, I don't know, but I know well that your head was shining.
Eznaza: And then you said something to me about the camel.
King: Then I heard your voice. You didn't speak then as you speak now.
Eznaza: Of course, I didn't say such things.
King: You didn't even speak in the same manner as now.
Eznaza: That time comes back dancing.
King: No, no. Only the shadow of that time returns. And we went together to the holy land of 'Mek'a'. Just the two of us, we pitched our tent in the golden desert and lived there. We heard the wild, free days sing the song of freedom. We also heard the beautiful sound of the night wind. Nothing remains of that year but an empty shadow. Memory whips the shadow. But the shadow does not dance.
(Eznaza does not answer.)
King: We parted in the desert. Let's not let the city hear that sound.
(Eznaza covers her face. The King quietly rises and ascends the stone steps. Just then, the Minister and Chabŭra enter from the left. Unaware of the others, they face each other.)
Minister: He will surely come. He will surely return.
Chabŭra: But it's already past noon. We who were fat have become thin like this. Our enemies see us and laugh. If the King doesn't return, they'll say God has forgotten us. Won't our friends in the world pity us!
Minister: If he is alive, he will surely return.
(Belnabŭ and Aubŭ enter.)
Chabŭra: Oh, it seems past noon already.
Minister: Then has he passed away? Was he captured by bandits?
(The Minister and Chabŭra put dust (몬지) on their heads.)
Belnabŭ: (To Aubŭ) God is fair. (To the Minister and Chabŭra) I am the King.
(At this moment, the King's hand had reached the door, but when Belnabŭ says this, the King descends the steps again and sits beside the gypsy. The gypsy woman lifts her head, which she had been holding in her hands, and stares blankly at the King. The King, covering half his face in the Arab style, watches Belnabŭ, the Minister, and Chabŭra.)
Minister: Are you truly the King?
Belnabŭ: Yes, I am the King.
Minister: You have changed so much in a year.
Belnabŭ: When a person stays in the desert, of course they change. They change quite a bit.
Aubŭ: Your Excellency, this man is truly the King. When His Majesty disguised himself and secretly went to the desert, I served as his camel attendant. This man is truly the King.<116>
Chabŭra: It is indeed His Majesty (페하). I recognize His Majesty when I see him.
Minister: You haven't seen His Majesty very often.
Chabŭra: I have seen him often.
Belnabŭ: Yes, we met often. Quite often.
Minister: If someone besides this man who came with you could accurately identify you as His Majesty, we too would believe with assurance.
Belnabŭ: There's no need for that. Because I am the King.
(At this point, the King stands up, extends one hand, and turns the palm downward.)
King: In the sacred land of 'Mek'a', in the holy land of 'Mek'a' with its many gates and blue roofs, we knew this man to be the King.
Belnabŭ: Yes, that's absolutely right. I too saw this man in 'Mek'a'.
Minister: (Bowing his head low) Please forgive me, Your Majesty (페하). The desert has indeed changed you.
Chabŭra: I knew well that it was Your Majesty.
Aubŭ: So did I.
Belnabŭ: (Pointing at the King) Give this man a suitable reward and grant him some position of honor in the palace.
Minister: Yes, Your Majesty (페하). I shall do so.
King: I am a camel driver, so I will now go to where my camels are.
Minister: As you wish.
(Belnabŭ, Aubŭ, Minister, Chabŭra exit through the gate.)
Eznaza: You have done a truly wise thing. The reward for wisdom is happiness.
King: They now have a king. Let us return once more to the tent of the Arabs.
Eznaza: They are truly foolish people.
King: That's why they found a foolish king.
Eznaza: To gladly wish to live within the city walls is the utmost foolishness.
King: Some are born kings, but that man becomes king because he wants to.
Eznaza: Come, let's hurry. Let's leave such people behind and depart.
King: Yes, let's go back there again.
Eznaza: Let's return to the tents of my people.
King: Let's live, pitching our beautiful brown tent in a place a little apart from others.
Eznaza: We'll be able to hear again the sand whispering softly in a thin voice to the dawn wind.
King: At dawn, we'll be able to hear the nomads chattering from far away inside their tents.
Eznaza: We will hear the footsteps of the leopard walking past us, returning to the mountain where it lives.
King: When evening comes and the sun sets, we will have no need to weep and lament for the day already gone.

**Summary**

The play "The Tent of the Arabs" depicts a king weary of city life and royal duties in T'allanna, yearning for the freedom of the desert associated with the Arabs and Mecca. He contrasts the confinement and corruption of the city with the perceived purity and timelessness of the desert. Despite his obligations, including an impending arranged marriage, he secretly escapes for a year, living amongst the Arabs with a desert gypsy woman, Eznaza. Bound by his promise to return after one year, he comes back at the appointed time, only to find a camel driver lookalike, Belnabŭ, claiming the throne. Seizing the opportunity, the true king validates the imposter's claim and chooses to return permanently to the desert with Eznaza, embracing a life of freedom over kingship.

From a historical perspective, the publication of a translated foreign play like this in colonial Korea (1932) is interesting in itself, showing cultural exchange. The play's strong theme of escaping the confines of urban life/structured society (represented by the city and kingship) for the freedom and perceived authenticity of the 'primitive' desert might have resonated with Koreans seeking escapism or dreaming of liberation from the strictures of colonial rule, albeit allegorically. The contrast between the 'civilized' but corrupt city and the 'natural' desert offers a romanticized view of non-urban life that could be interpreted in various ways within the colonial context.

왕  왕관이란 왕의 머리에 쓸 것이 아니다. 왕의 玉笏은 왕의 손에 들고 다닐 것이 아니다.  왕관은 금사슬로 엮고 玉笏은 말뚝 같이 땅에다 박어놓고서 왕은 거기 발목을 매여놓는 것이 옳은 일이다. 그러케라두 해놓면 왕은 아름다운 사막을 떠돌아 다닐 수도 새암 까에 잇는 종려나무를 볼 수도 없는 것이라고 단념할 수가 잇을 것이다. 오 탈란나여, 탈란나여 나는 이 도시를 미워한다. 좁디좁은 길과 술취한 놈들이 더러운 도박을 하는 저 악한 『스카미』영감 집에서 저녁마다 노름만 하고 잇는 이 도시를 얼마나 미워하는지 모른다. 오 나는 그 선조때부터 대대로 도회를 본 일이 없는 천한 집 딸에게 장가를 들고싶다. 그래서 우리들은 여기서 사막의 길다란 길을 주욱 약대를 타고 가서 다만 둘이서 아라비아인의 천막까지라도 가보련다. 그리구 왕관은―어느 미욱한 욕심 많은 사람에게 괴롬과 섫음의 씨로 주어버리자. 그러나 이런 일은 모두 될 수 없는 일이다. 왕은 아무래두 역시 왕에 틀님 없으니까.

왕  응, 대신. 자낸가. 무슨 내 볼 일이 또 생겻나.

대신  페하께서 하실 일은 너무나 많습니다. 익트라국이 반란을 일으켯습니다.
왕  익트라란 어디 잇는 나란가.
대신  그건 페하께 조공을 들이는 조그만 나라올습니다. 『제브달른』 산속에 잇는 나라입    니다.

대신  만일 페하께서 지금 이 도시를 떠나가신다면―.
왕  떠나가지 않네. 우리는 병정을 일으켜 익트라의 백성을 중벌해야지.

왕  해도 거이 저물엇는데 웨 대상의 무리는 아직 떠나지를 않엇을까.
대신  저는 모르겟습니다. 그러면 페하께서는―.
왕  (손을 대신의 팔에다 얹으며) 저기 보게. 저걸 보아! 『멕카』로 떠나가는 약대의 그림잘  세. 저 아름다운 그림자가 얼마나 고요하게 땅 우를 미끄러저 가고 잇느냐 말이야. 얼마     안해 그들은 사막 안에 나서서 금모래 우에 평평해질 것이지―. 그때 해는 지고 그들은     어둠에 쌓여 밤과 하나가 되고 말 것이다.
대신  만일 페하께서 이런 일에 관심을 하신다면 약대는 얼마든지 잇습니다만...＜110＞
왕  응 아니야. 나는 약대를 보고싶지는 않어. 어느 때라두 약대가 나를 아름다운 사막으로  다리고 갈 수는 없으니까. 영원히 도시를 떠나, 자유롭게 나는 여기 머물러서 왕의 일을     보지 않으면 안되네. 다만 내 꿈만은 갈 수 잇지. 약대의 그림자가 내 꿈을 실고― 아라비   아인의 천막 곁에 평화를 찾으러 가 줄 수가 잇네.
대신  지금 페하께서는 회의실에 나가시렵니까.
왕  응, 그래. 지금 갈테야.
(멀리서 들리는 소리 『호요! 호에이! 호요! 호에이!』)
전대상이 지금 떠나가는군! 짐을 실은 약대꾼의 소릴 좀 들어보게. 그들은 처음 십마일은  약대들을 따라 다름질을 해가다가 내일이 되면 약대등 우에 올라 탈 것이다. 그때는 『탈    란나』를 돌아본댓자 조금도 안 뵐 것이구. 그들 주위에는 사막이 웃음을 먹음은 금모래     우에 나려쬐는 햇빛을 받아 질펀하게 놓엿을 따름이겟지. 그때 그들의 얼굴에는 새로운     빛이 떠오를게다. 나는 확실히 밤이 되면 사막이 그들에게 『평화로워라, 내 자식들아. 평    화로워라, 내 자식들아』라구 속삭일 것을 믿는다.
(이 말을 하는 동안 대신은 왕을 위하야 문을 열고 거기에 공손이 몸을 굽혀 대하고 잇다. 열린 문에다 손을 꽉 대이고서.)
대신  페하, 회의실에 나가시렵니까.
왕  응, 곳 가겟네. 이 익트라의 사건만 일어나지 않엇드라면 나도 아마 길을 떠나 황금의   사막에 1년이나 살 수가 잇엇고 『멕카』의 성지를 볼 수도 잇엇으련만.

왕  아, 아 불행한 왕에게 또 무슨 일이 생겻느냐.

왕  오늘부터 일년이 지나간 그 잇흔날 오두록 해.

벨나브  응, 과연 옳은 말이야. 나두 이 사람을 『멕카』에서 본 일이 잇어.
대신  (머리를 숙혀 절하며) 용서해주십시오. 페하, 사막이 당신을 달라지게 햇습니다 그려.
자브라  나는 페하시라는 것을 잘 알고 잇엇습니다.
아우브  저도 역시 그랫습니다.
벨나브  (왕을 가르치며) 이 사람에게 상당한 상급을 주어 궁중의 무슨 성위를 하나주어.
대신  네, 페하. 그리하겟습니다.
왕  나는 약대꾼이니까 이제 곳 내 약대 잇는 곳으로 가겟습니다.
대신  자네 맘대로.
(벨나브, 아우브, 대신, 다브라, 문을 지나 퇴장)
에즈나자  당신을 참 현명하신 일을 하셧습내다. 지헤에 대한 보수는 행복입니다.
왕  그들에게는 인제 왕이 생겻다. 우리들은 또다시 아라비아인의 천막으로 돌아가자.
에즈나자  그들은 참 미욱한 사람들이군요.
왕  그러니까 미욱한 왕을 찾어냇지.
에즈나자  즐겨 城속에서 살기를 원하는 것은 미욱하기 짝이 없는 사람이지요.
왕  왕으로 태난 사람도 잇지만 저 사람은 제가 좋아서 왕이 되는구면.
에즈나자  자, 오서요. 저런 사람을 바리고 떠나가십시다.
왕  그래, 다시 그곳으로 돌아가자.
에즈나자  우리 족속들의 천막으로 돌아가십시다요.
왕  다른 사람들과 좀 떠러진 곳에서 어여뿐 우리들의 갈색 천막을 치고 살어나가자.
에즈나자  모래가 새벽바람을 보구 얒은 소리로 가만히 속삭이는 것을 다시 들을 수도 잇겟  지요.
왕  새벽이 되면 멀리서 유목민들이 천막 속에서 떠들어대는 것을 들을 수도 잇겟지.
에즈나자  표범이 우리들 곁을 거러서 저 사는 산으로 돌아가는 발자최 소리도 들릴 것입니  다.
왕  저녁이 되어 해가 넘어 갈 때도 우리들은 이미 가버린 그날 때문에 울고 탄식할 것은   없다.
에즈나자  밤이 되어 머리를 들어 하늘을 처다보면 아직 사거나 팔니거나 한 일이 없는 날고 날근 별들이 내 머리카락 속에서 반짝일 것입니다. 우리들은 왕관을 장식한 세계의 여왕을 도모지 부러워 할 것은 없습니다.
