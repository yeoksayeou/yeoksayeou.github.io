Rain During a Journey in Hong Kong
						Chŏng In'gwa

Master Ch'unwŏn,
How have you been lately? Has San'ong arrived in the capital? I thought I had escaped the dust of the world while at sea, but I found myself traveling together with a crowd of 800 Japanese immigrants heading to Brazil. Indeed, it seems they too are going in search of a somewhat better life than they had in Japan. It resembles the lives of Korean farmers. They say that the opportunity to go to South America is not permitted for Chinese or for us Koreans, only for Japanese.

Today, I arrived in Hong Kong. Due to epidemics and internal unrest, landing was forbidden, but I earnestly requested permission and managed to land. Overcome with indescribable feelings, I soon returned to the ship. Fourteen years ago, I visited the Ojut'ae Inn (五州太여관), where I stayed with San'ong, and I climbed up to the Sŏnsi Upper Floor Anlagwŏn (先施上層安樂園), where we often went to escape the heat and relax. Like the drizzling fine rain, tears fell, and after meditating for several hours, I reluctantly left that place. I went to the Mainland Telegraph Office (大陸電報局), where we used to go whenever there was business, but I neither received nor sent any particular news. After wandering the city streets, I returned. The master (주인옹) who used to plan this and that in various fields is gone, and I only saw the places where he used to frequent. I cannot stop by Shanghai and am proceeding directly.
(June 13th, Lonely Traveler Chŏng In'gwa) <92>

**Summary**

This article is a personal letter written by Chŏng In'gwa to "Master Ch'unwŏn" (likely Yi Kwang-su) during a sea journey in 1932. While traveling, he observes Japanese emigration to Brazil, drawing parallels to the struggles of Korean farmers and noting the discriminatory restrictions preventing Koreans and Chinese from similar opportunities. Upon briefly landing in Hong Kong despite restrictions, he revisits places he frequented 14 years prior with "San'ong" (possibly An Ch'ang-ho), feeling deep nostalgia and sorrow for the absent companion and changed circumstances. The letter conveys a sense of melancholy, displacement, and constrained mobility under colonialism. For historians, the text offers insights into Korean intellectual perspectives on Japanese emigration, the awareness of discriminatory colonial policies impacting movement and opportunity, and personal reflections on past connections (potentially related to nationalist activities hinted at by "planning this and that") within the broader context of East Asia in the 1930s.

香港의 旅雨
						鄭仁果
春園선생
저간 안녕하시며 山翁이 입경되엇습니까. 海上에서 世塵을 脫한 줄 알엇드니 日本 이민 800명이 「부라질」로 가는 군중과 동행이 되엇습니다. 과연 그들도 日本 국내에서 보다 좀 나은 생을 가지려고 하야 가는 모양입니다. 朝鮮 농민의 생활과 相似합니다. 南米에 가는 기회도 日本人 외에는 中人과 吾人에게도 불허한답니다.

금일 香港에 도착하야 전염병이니 내란이니 하야 상륙을 허하지 않는 것을 切請하야 상륙하고 말할 수 없는 느낌을 얻고 그만 승선하엿습니다. 14년 전에 山翁과 같이 香港 와서 留하든 五州太여관을 訪向하고 종종 피서 겸 놀러가든 先施上層安樂園을 登하야 부슬부슬 뿌리는 細雨와 같이 落淚가 되어 수 시간을 묵상하다가 부득이 그곳을 떠나 大陸電報局에 무슨 일이 잇을 때마다 가든 그곳에 가서 별 소식을 받지도 傳치도 못하고 시가로 방황하다가 돌아왓습니다. 이 방면 저 방면, 이런 일 저런 일을 획책하시든 주인옹은 가버리고 그의 놀든 장소만 보앗습니다. 上海는 들리지 못하고 직행합니다.
(6월 13일 孤客 鄭人果)＜92＞
