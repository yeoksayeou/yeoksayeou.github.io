May of Sorrowful Parting
						Chŏng Hŭng-p'il

Mother—
Where did that May, which had come to me, stealthily run away?
That May, which I intended to keep always, always.
Why? Where did it go!<85>

Mother—
I dislike this hazy heart.
Now that May is gone, fog begins to settle in this chest again.
Inside this terribly oppressive gloom.

Mother—
Please take me to the place where May went.

Passing through the dense forest, following along the tide,
There should be a shepherd boy tending cattle, so ask the way carefully.
Let’s cross far over the mountain ridge buried in white snow,
And traverse the beautiful garden where flowers bloom and butterflies dance.
Then, from somewhere, the sly laughter
Of that May which abandoned me will faintly be heard.

Mother—
Please take me to the place where May went.<86>

**Summary**

This poem is a lament addressed to "Mother," expressing deep sorrow over the departure of "May," a symbol of a cherished past or happiness. The speaker feels lost in a "hazy," fog-filled present gloom and longs to be taken back to where May resides. The journey imagined involves traversing forests, shores, snow-covered mountains, and finally reaching a beautiful garden where the "sly laughter" of the lost May might be heard. From a historical perspective, the poem's pervasive melancholy and yearning for a lost idyllic time ("May") could be interpreted as reflecting the broader sense of loss, displacement, or longing for happier times felt by Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. The intense personal expression of sorrow might resonate with collective feelings of oppression and the desire for restoration or escape.
