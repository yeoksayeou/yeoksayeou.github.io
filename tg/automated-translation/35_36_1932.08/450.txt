Student Literary Circle (學生文壇)
The Carriage Going Over That Hill (저 언덕 넘어가는 마차)
Chang Sŭngdu (張承斗), Ch'ŏngju Higher Common School (淸州高普)

Please stop
That carriage over there
Flickering in the spring rain
In the dim twilight
Playing a sad flute
Going over that hill
That carriage over there.

Please stop
Please, just stop
With red reins (곱삐)
Pulled by two black horses
That blue carriage. <93>

Submerged in tears
The spring dream of youth
Sadly loaded, disappearing
Never to return again
That carriage over there. <94>

Life and Death (生과 死)
Kim Hoewŏn (金回源), Chungdong School (中東)

I saw the farmers cultivating,
I saw the farmers harvesting,
Oh! Life (삶음) and Death (죽엄)
What do you satirize?
Life, it was cultivation
Death, it was the Harvest (收獲).

Let's Plant Potatoes (감자를 심으자)
Kim Songbong (金松峰), Kyŏngnong School (鏡農)

Farmer,
The time has come, the frozen earth has melted.
In the melted earth <94>
All things (萬象) that were dead breathe the breath of revival (蘇生)
So the desolate universe is full of the spirit of new life.
Farmer!
Roll up your sleeves, bare your feet, grasp the hoe handle firmly (광이자루에 춤받아 쥐고)
Run vigorously out to the fields
With blood spreading through your body from your pounding heart (염통) and muscles in your forearms bunched like red copper (적동같치)
Dig the earth
And pick out plump potatoes and plant them.
Then the new strength blooming abundantly in the earth will become the strength of the naked and starving (헐벗고 배골는) multitude (무리).
Farmer!
Dig the earth forcefully
And plant plump potatoes.

Baby (아가야)
Kim Ch'angsik (金昌軾), Fushun Middle School (撫順中學)

The baby threw back the front cloth (앞바라지). Just then, avoiding the raindrops, a sparrow fluttering (포독거리는) as it tried to land (푸트려고) on the low wall where the eaves of the thatched roof (초가넝) sagged deeply caught his eye.
Raindrops patter, patter!
A baby sparrow
Clung to the top of the wall (담뚝). <95>
Ah(!) It fell.
It clung again.
The baby cried out thus with a sharp cry of excitement.
The baby walked towards the back threshold.
Just then, the sound of the kitchen door was heard. The baby seemed to sense intuitively that mother was entering the kitchen.
At that moment, because the wind gusted (홀딱) through the open window, the baby's simple doll (숫당인형), which was placed in front, rolled (불러왓다) over next to the baby. The baby went out to meet mother.
Mommy, well (글새애)
The wind just blew right in (흘 들어오너껀)
The baby doll (애개야) (a doll with a paper skirt on a cotton body)
Came and sat next to me
Sat right down
Look at this (옷 뽀라우) (Meaning 요것 보라는)
The mother, unable to contain her joy, hugged him and bounced him.
"That is awfully (끔찍인) cute."
The baby, being bounced, nestled in his mother's arms, tilted his head (개울거리며) and looked up into his mother's eyes.
Mommy
What baby (웬 애개야) is in mommy's eyes?
Ah(人) laughed.
Because I laughed, it follows
(Then, because you laughed, it laughs.)
Ah, here too (요켄 눈에두). <96>
Mommy
『Yes』
Your eyes are very (흐게) pretty
Shall we trade with my eyes?
(Smooch. Mother kissed him (입을 마초앗다))
『Your eyes are even cuter than mine.』
Thus, the affection between mother and baby appears in the baby's tilting 『neck (고개쎄)』 and the mother's blinking 『eyes (눈쎄)』.

Twilight on the Namdaech'ŏn (南大川의 황혼)
Kim Ye-hwan (金禮煥), Sinsŏng School, Sŏnch'ŏn (宣川信聖)

As the golden sunset withdraws its light from the Namdaech'ŏn water
And the young women who were washing clothes gather the laundry (빨내) they had spread on the sand
A purple curtain that came around the distant mountainside
Suddenly covers the field before my eyes.

Even the gentle wind that rolled beads on the clear water
Like a child tired from play falling asleep
Tries to sleep in nature's embrace
The sound of a grass flute, thin and beautifully heard
I want to find who is playing it.

Already on the sandbank by the stream, there is no laundry
Even the bunting (촉새) that sat on a rock preening (짖다듬든) has gone somewhere <97>
The sound of the grass flute I listened to, captivated, has now ceased
Only the sound of the Namdaech'ŏn water
Eternally drives a nail into my ear.

Small Scene on a Boat (船中小景)
Kim Sŏngdo (金聖道)

Mountains in the distant white clouds
Mountains faint like in a dream
Only their reddish heads are clearly visible (뵈이누나).

Beyond the pure white sand dune
Two masts (돗대) are visible.
Those masts, whose boat hulls are unseen
Look as if planted in the sandy field.

A boat hoisting its sail, running (달르는) below the mountain
Glides coolly over the water.

The song of the boatman (뱃사공) sitting at the boat's end
Is leisurely. Is beautiful.

Two water birds, black-headed and white-bellied (腹)
Are flying, singing tweet-tweet.
As if welcoming us who came from afar
As if proud of flying over the water. <98>

Turning my head, I look back at the mountain we crossed
The clouds are hiding the sun.
But, but, that face at least
Is visible (뵈이는고나) beneath the skirt hem

Ah, tranquil river
Heart-pleasing river!

(At Ttuksŏm (뚝섬))

Untitled Short Verses (無題短章)
Ko Munsu (高文洙), Chaeryŏng Agricultural School (載寧農校)

I swung my arms and shouted
The red blood in my chest surged in my youth
Let it be, there's nothing that strength cannot achieve

On a rainy morning, I resent being the older brother
Seeing my younger brother going to school wrapped in a worn-out sack (후대)
If I earn money, if I earn money, I only sang the money song. <99>

**Summary**

This collection presents seven poems and vignettes by Korean students from various schools (including one in Manchuria) published in 1932. The pieces cover themes of youthful melancholy (Chang Sŭngdu), reflections on life and death through agricultural imagery (Kim Hoewŏn), a call to agricultural labor possibly hinting at national self-strengthening (Kim Songbong), a tender scene of mother-child interaction (Kim Ch'angsik), lyrical descriptions of nature and twilight (Kim Ye-hwan, Kim Sŏngdo), and poignant observations on youth, ambition, and poverty (Ko Munsu). The works showcase a range of student emotions and styles, from lyrical and observational to reflective and socially aware. From a historical perspective, the poems offer insights into the emotional landscape of educated youth during the Japanese colonial period, revealing their engagement with nature, daily life struggles (poverty in Ko Munsu's poem), philosophical thoughts, and perhaps subtly nationalistic sentiments expressed through themes like agricultural self-sufficiency (Kim Songbong). The inclusion of students from various regions and types of schools (common, agricultural, middle school in Manchuria) is also noteworthy.
