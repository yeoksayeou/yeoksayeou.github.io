First Part: Introduction
1. Methods of Expressing Intention
It can be seen that humans generally have three methods for expressing their intentions. These are speech (language), writing (script), and gestures (actions). Each of these three methods of expressing intention has its own distinct strengths and weaknesses.

First, speech:
(a) Expresses intention through sound.
(b) Is understood through hearing (auditory sense).
(c) Can only be used with those present at the same time and place.
(d) Is imperfect for conveying messages to those at different times or places, as it relies solely on memory.
(e) Can evoke emotions like joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure through intonation (音調).
(f) Can be used even in dark places.
(g) Spoken sounds (語音) are prone to changing easily according to time and place.

Second, writing:
(a) Expresses intention through certain fixed symbols.
(b) Is understood through sight (visual sense).
(c) Can be used without limitations of time (시간) or distance (공간).
(d) Not only has no limitations in conveying messages to different times and places but can also convey them most accurately.
(e) However, lacking the equivalent intonation (音調) of speech, it is easily misunderstood and lacks emotional impact.
(f) Cannot be used in dark places.
(g) Being more fixed and conservative than speech (언어), it changes more slowly than speech, leading to differences like classical vs. modern writing, literary vs. colloquial styles, or historical orthography (記音法/綴法) vs. phonetic orthography.

Third, gestures (꼴):
(a) Merely serve to assist speech.
(b) Cannot fully express intentions on their own (although one might say the mute express intentions solely through gestures (몸ㅅ짓), it is incomplete).

**Summary**

This article serves as the introduction (序篇) to a planned series by Kim Yunkyŏng on the history of Korean writing (朝鮮文字). Kim outlines the scope, acknowledging the breadth of related fields like linguistics and phonetics, and presents the planned structure, covering pre-Hunminjŏngŭm writing systems, the creation and development of Hunminjŏngŭm, and related historical events and figures up to the contemporary Han'gŭl movement. He humbly presents his work as a compilation seeking feedback from senior scholars. The introduction itself discusses the three primary methods of human communication – speech, writing, and gestures – detailing their characteristics, advantages, and limitations. From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting as it represents a Korean intellectual's effort in 1931 to systematically study and present the history of the national script, Hunminjŏngŭm (referred to as Han'gŭl in the preface but discussed under its original name), thereby asserting a distinct Korean linguistic and cultural identity during Japanese rule. The explicit mention of the Government-General's spelling regulations and the "recent Han'gŭl movement" situates the study within the contemporary context of language policy and national consciousness under colonialism.
