Kim Okkyun's Tomb and 1 Other Piece
						P'ain

Kim Okkyun's Tomb (金玉均墓)

One day in early winter, when white snow had chaotically covered the old battlefield of Asan, I was on my way to Hŏsŏ and sought out the tomb of Master Kim Okkyun.
The tomb lies in a mountain valley on the east-west side of the town, densely surrounded by chestnut and pine trees that seem to embrace the Master's body, shivering from the cold. After meeting his end in Shanghai, his lock of hair was buried in Tokyo's Aoyama Cemetery by the hands of Toyama Mitsuru (頭山滿) and Inukai Tsuyoshi (犬養毅). Years ago, his adopted son, Mr. Kim Yŏngjin (金英鎭), moved it and buried it here, erecting two stone horse statues with bowed heads before the grave, seemingly symbolizing lamentation. Watching a tomb keeper sweep the snowy path by the grave, blowing on the back of his hands – At Asan

Gush, gush, red blood, spurted out like a whale spout,
When you tried to moisten this dry land,
You were magnificent, like a sheet of cloud covering the vast sky,
But the star of the East (東方) vanished forever, leaving no trace.<54>

Even pine seeds scattered by the wind in the mountain valley
Become ridgepoles in ten years, but alas, only the Master –

Weep, weep, tombstone by the grave, weep loudly like an ox,
Fill this empty mountain valley at least with sound.
If even sound cannot come out, then stamp your hooves.
Before heaven and earth collide, I wish to hear your voice once more.

From east, west, south, north, the wind brings all kinds of news,
But even waiting ten years, and then another ten, the Master's voice is heard no more, alas.

Shed, shed, tears of blood, flowing by the *mal* (斗),
Oh, people of this Eastern Peninsula, shed great tears of repentance,
Let us make flowers bloom by the grave of the departed one.
When flowers bloom, flags will fly too, ships will head to the harbor.

The wind is cold, the day grows dark,
Even crows fly over the mountain; with whom does the Master spend this night?

Cotton-Picking Girl (木花따는 少女)

In the land of Paekche, there are cotton fields everywhere, and in those cotton fields are village girls picking white cotton like snow. Flowers in winter, how delightful they are.

When cotton flowers scatter,
A song comes along too.
Owner of the song beyond the fence,
You only pick flowers, only move your lips,
Why do you steal another's heart?

I like cotton flowers;
Even if snow falls and wind blows,
They live if left, live if picked.
Whether you go or stay,
Who could snatch away this heart?<55>
<54-55>

**Summary**

This article presents two poems by "P'ain". The first, "Kim Okkyun's Tomb," is a somber reflection at the grave of the reformist Kim Okkyun in Asan. The poet laments Kim's unfulfilled potential ("star of the East"), his violent death, and the long silence following his demise, contrasting his fate with the growth of nature. It calls for repentance and remembrance, hoping for a future renewal symbolized by blooming flowers, flags, and ships. The second poem, "Cotton-Picking Girl," offers a lighter, pastoral image of girls picking cotton in the former Paekche region, comparing the cotton to snow and reflecting on the steadfastness of the heart amidst the changing seasons and personal relationships. From a colonial history perspective, the first poem is particularly revealing. It expresses deep national sorrow and frustrated aspirations through the figure of Kim Okkyun, a controversial historical figure seen by some as a failed modernizer. The mention of Japanese figures (Toyama, Inukai) involved with his remains adds a layer of complexity to the Japan-Korea relationship. The call for repentance and future blooming hints at a subdued desire for national revival under colonial rule. The second poem, while seemingly simpler, depicts a Korean rural scene, possibly romanticizing traditional life while subtly asserting an enduring spirit ("Who could snatch away this heart?").

金玉均墓 외 1편
						파인
金玉均墓
첫 겨울 어느날 힌 눈의 牙山 古戰場을 어지러이 덮엇는대 나는 湖西로 가든 길에 金玉均 先生의 무덤을 찾다.
무덤은 읍의 東西便 山谷 속에 잇는데 밤나무와 잣나무가 추어서 떠는 선생의 몸을 안아드리는 듯 빽빽히 둘러섯다. 上海에서 최후를 마치신 뒤 頭山滿 犬養毅들 손으로 東京 靑山묘지에 그 遺髮이 묻히어 잇든 것을 年前에 그 嗣子 金英鎭씨가 이곳에 옴겨다 묻고서 墓前에는 통곡을 상징함인 듯 머리숙인 石馬像 두 基까지 하여 세웟다. 墓직이 한 분이 손등을 혹혹 불며 무덤가 눈길을 쓰는 것을 보면서 - 牙山에서

뿜어 뿜어 붉은 피 고랫살 같이 홱 뿜어
마른 이 강산 축이려 드시올 제
구만리 저 하늘 덮는 구름짱이런 듯 장하옵더니
東方의 별, 그만 영영 자취를 감추고 말앗네.＜54＞

바람에 날리는 산골 솔씨조차
십년이면 도리기둥 된다 하는데, 아하 선생만 -

울어 울어 무덤까 저 비석이 황소같이 크게 울어
빈 이 산골 소리로나 채어 놓소.
소리조차 안 나옵거든 네 발굽이나 굴르소.
하늘 땅 맞붙기전 임의 소리 한번 듣고저.

東西南北에 바람은 별소리 다 가지고 오건만
十年에 또 十年 기대려도 선생의 소리 다시 없네 아하

흘려 흘려 피 눈물 말(斗)로 흘러
東半島의 이 백성아, 懺悔의 눈물 크게 흘려
가신 님 무덤 가에 꽃이나 피어드리세
꽃 필 때 旗도 날리리, 배는 항구로 가리.

바람은 차고 날은 저문데
가마귀조차 山 넘으니 선생은 누구 더불어 이 밤 지내나.

木花따는 少女
百濟땅의 곳곳이는 木花밭이 잇고 그 木花밭에는 눈같은 힌 솜을 따는 시골 소녀들이 잇다. 겨울에 잇는 꽃이라 더욱 반갑구나.

木花꽃 날리울 제
노래조차 함께 오네
장태 너멍 노래 임자
꽃만 따고 입만 따지
남의 마음 웨 따가나.

나는 좋데 木花꽃은
눈 오고 바람 불어도
두어도 살고 따도 살고
그대야 가거나 말거나
이 마음 누가 앗으리.＜55＞
＜54-55＞
