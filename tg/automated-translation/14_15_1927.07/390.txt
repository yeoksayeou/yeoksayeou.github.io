Summary up to the Previous Issue
Ch'ae Yong (蔡邕) of Han, courtesy name Paekkye (伯喈), was born in a sagely era and possessed the talent to manage the world, but because his parents were old, he abandoned his ambitions for high office. He newly married Lady Cho Onyang (趙氏五娘), and they enjoyed the harmony of the household, with the couple getting along well and the parents healthy and peaceful. At this time, an edict for recruiting talented men (招賢) arrived in the district, and the Prefect (太守) reported Ch'ae Yong's name. Yong wished to fulfill his filial duty to his aged parents and firmly refused, but due to the urging of his neighbor Elder Chang (張太公) and the strict command of his father, he entrusted all matters to Elder Chang, parted with Cho Onyang after only two months of marriage, made the thousand-li journey to the capital (京師), passed the examination (應試), and earned the glorious crown of the top graduate (狀元).
At that time, Prime Minister Grand Tutor Niu (丞相牛太師) was renowned throughout the generation for his learning and virtue. Having lost his wife earlier, he had one daughter who was gentle, virtuous, and deeply cherished by the Grand Tutor. Receiving an imperial command (聖旨), he sought to take the top graduate Ch'ae Yong as his son-in-law. However, Yong, being a filial son and also loyal to his past (舊에 厚함), submitted a memorial (表) to resign from office and return to his country home.

**Summary**

This excerpt from Act 16 of the adapted play *Pipa Ji* depicts the scholar Ch'ae Yong's desperate plea before the Han emperor to be allowed to resign his newly awarded position and return home to care for his elderly parents, especially given recent news of famine. His request is formally submitted via a memorial detailing his filial obligations and anxieties. However, the Hwangmun'gwan (eunuch official) delivers an imperial edict firmly rejecting his plea, commanding him instead to obey, serve the state, and proceed with the arranged marriage to the Prime Minister's daughter, emphasizing that duty to the ruler supersedes filial piety when state matters are pressing. The act concludes with Ch'ae Yong's despair and the Hwangmun'gwan's admonition to accept fate and prioritize loyalty and official success.

From a colonial history perspective, this 1927 Korean adaptation of a classic Chinese tale about filial piety versus state duty is noteworthy. It reflects the continued cultural influence of Chinese classics in Korea during the Japanese colonial period. Furthermore, the central conflict—an individual's desire to fulfill personal/familial obligations clashing with the demands of a higher authority (the Emperor/State)—could potentially resonate with or subtly comment upon the pressures faced by Koreans under colonial rule, where personal aspirations often conflicted with the demands and expectations imposed by the colonial state. The emphasis on loyalty (忠) and filial piety (孝), core Confucian values, and the tension between them, provides fertile ground for exploring ethical dilemmas relevant even in a modernizing, colonized society.
