Prayer Offered by the Sea
						Chin U-ch'on

The setting sun’s light shines on the far side of the sea
And a single white bird drifts above my head
At this sea, the Holy Temple (聖殿) You built with Your own hands,
I bow my head and think of You.
Countless waves rush towards me
But my heart does not yield
And thinks of You more sincerely.
Even the rushing waves kneel before my feet.

I am a lonely body with not even a blade of grass to rely on.
My heart weeps along with the cries of the water birds,
Yet when You are in my heart like now,
How sacred! Everything kneels before me.
1927. 5.

Reflection -On Tano Day-
With the wounds and sullied pride <9>
Of my many bygone days,
Now, with the burning flames of reflection and indignation,
I newly vow for the brightening day.

Reality, filled with falsehood and baseness (野卑),
Has given me disappointment and despair (自暴),
And the secret resentment (怨恨) of disappointment and despair
Tried to forget the moment with potent liquor.

Ah, but my pride—
This weapon of much resentment that rebelled against reality—
The cup of liquor I sought to forget, even for a moment, rather
Inflicts wounds (傷處) and stains upon myself.

Ah, now, like the ancient people
Who washed their hearts in the clear river water,
I too, with the burning flames of reflection and indignation,
Newly vow for the brightening day.
1927. 6. <10>

**Summary**

The article presents two poems by Chin U-ch'on. The first, "Prayer Offered by the Sea," expresses a deep sense of faith and finding inner strength through communion with a divine presence ('You') amidst the vastness and power of nature (the sea). Despite loneliness, this connection brings solace and a feeling of mastery over the surrounding world. The second poem, "Reflection - On Tano Day," is a more personal and anguished piece reflecting on past suffering, disillusionment with a reality perceived as false and base, and the failure of escapism (alcohol). It concludes with a strong resolve, inspired by traditional purification rituals associated with Tano Day, to embrace reflection and indignation and make a vow for a better future ("the brightening day"). From a historical perspective, the poems offer insight into individual coping mechanisms and sentiments during the colonial period. The first poem shows a turn towards spirituality and nature for strength, while the second explicitly critiques "reality" (potentially alluding to the colonial situation) and expresses a fervent hope and vow for a new beginning, which could be interpreted as having nationalistic undertones common in literature of that era. The use of Tano Day adds a layer of cultural context to this pledge for renewal.

바다가에서 올닌 祈禱 외 1편
						秦雨村
바다가에서 올닌 祈禱
지는 해빛이 바다 저편에 빛나고
한 마리 흰 새가 내 머리 우에 떠도는
그대가 손소 지으신 聖殿 이 바다가에서
나는 머리를 숙여 그대를 생각함니다
수 없는 물결이 내 앞으로 몰려드도다
그러나 나의 마음은 屈하지 않고
더 精誠되이 그대를 생각하나니.
몰려드는 물결도 내 발 앞에 무릅 굴는다.

풀 한닢 의지 없는 외롭운 몸이오.
물새 우름에도 따라우는 마음이어늘
지금같이 나의 마음에 그대가 게실 때는
거륵하다 모든 것은 내 앞에 무릅 굴는다.
1927. 5.

反省 -端午날에-
나는 지나간 많은 날의＜9＞
상처와 때묻힌 나의 自尊心으로
이제 反省과 鬱奮의 타는 불길로
밝는 날을 새로히 盟誓하노라

거짓과 野卑로 차지된 現實은
나에게 失望과 自暴를 주어 왔으며
失望과 自暴의 남모를 怨恨은
毒한 술로써 한때를 잊으랴 하였다

아아 그러나 나의 自尊心을
現實을 叛逆한 한 많은 武器를-
한때나마 잊으랴던 술잔은 돌이어
내 스스로 傷處와 때 묻히나니

아아 이제 맑은 江물에
그 맘을 씻는 옛 사람을 본받아
나도 反省과 鬱奮의 타는 불길로
밝는 날을 새로히 盟誓하노라.
