**Body Text Translation**
Critique of Old and New Korean Athletes
Kim T'aeho

Yi Yŏngmin, Kim Chongse, Ma Ch'unsik

Foreword
An unexpected command—from Tonggwangsa (東光社), asking me to write a "Critique of Old and New Korean Athletes."
While Tonggwangsa, which gave the command, is free to do so, for the one commanded, the writer, it is truly a difficult task—because hitting is a greater responsibility than receiving the 'sign' from the 'bench coach'. In 'team play', the most difficult yet unavoidable thing is self-sacrifice. I know that if one does not move bravely and boldly, yet carefully, according to the 'coach's' command, even knowing that some disadvantage may come to oneself, that 'team' loses its life and consequently reveals a disastrous result to the world. Having received the 'coach's' command, it probably wouldn't be such a foolish statement to say that by simply doing one's best, one has fulfilled more than half of the responsibility. Thus, the writer has decided to comply with Tonggwangsa's command and intends to wield a pen that will likely contain many errors, so I ask in advance for the understanding of the current old and new athletes mentioned herein, as well as all readers.

Ma Ch'unsik
Ma Ch'unsik! When speaking of athletic competitions among Koreans, there is probably no one who does not know him.
He grew up at Paejae Haktang (培材學堂), carried Paejae Haktang on his back, and reigned over the Korean athletic scene for nearly 20 years as a great athlete. While at Paejae Haktang, he was active as a famous pitcher in baseball and a famous 'center full-back' in soccer, not only bringing glory to his alma mater but also remaining as a staff member of his alma mater after graduation. He coached baseball, soccer, and track and field, while<45> further honing his own skills. In baseball, he led the entire Paejae team, and in soccer, he led the Buddhist Youth Association (佛敎청년회) team and later the Chosŏn Soccer Team (朝鮮축구단), possessing the literal strength of an ever-victorious general, finding no rivals wherever he went. Not only that, but in the 400 meters, until 6 or 7 years ago, he was the holder of the top record in all of Korea (暀 鮮) with 57 seconds.
As discussed above, he was an all-around athlete. But he wasn't just an all-around athlete; he was also a famous coach. Rather than a detailed explanation of why he was a famous coach, the fact that prominent contemporary athletes like Yi Yŏngmin, Paek Kiju, Sŏng Yonghwa, and others all grew under Ma's tutelage is sufficient eloquent proof that he was a famous coach.
His ability to overwhelm opponents in sports was not solely due to his outstanding technique. He possessed a spirit and passion unattainable by ordinary people.
Just as 'Babe Ruth' is the home run king, he also has a fiery temper, sometimes getting into fights on the field (even during games) with spectators, opposing players, or even his own teammates, reportedly often being penalized. Ma also sometimes came close to crossing the line due to his innately quick temper. For someone with exceptional spirit and passion like him, this was a strength that served as the driving force for his extraordinary performance in various sports, but at the same time, when disputes arose, it became a weakness, causing him to sometimes go too far and be misunderstood by many people. In any case, his contributions to his alma mater, Paejae School, over 20 years were great, and his contributions to the Korean sports world were second to none. Yet, his shadow suddenly disappeared from this land for three years. Hearing rumors that he even attempted suicide at one point, the writer lamented.
I wondered if we lacked the strength to accommodate him here, a meritorious figure and great senior in the sports world, and felt anew that the society we inhabit is too incompetent and heartless. No, I still believe so. I hear he might be in Shanghai (上海) or perhaps Japan (日本). However, it seems no one knows his definite whereabouts, but I know there are many of his 'fans' who want to know how he is doing. If you happen to read this through Tonggwang, please send even a letter.
Sir! Where are you and what are you doing now?<46>

Kim Chŏngsik

Kim Ŭnbae
Kim Chongse
The name 'Pockmarked Pitcher' is so widely known that there's likely no one in the world who hasn't heard it; further elaboration is unnecessary. Just as people might not know the real name 'George Herman Ruth' of the world-famous home run king but everyone knows the nickname 'Babe Ruth', perhaps people don't know the real name Kim Chongse, but there's likely no one who doesn't know the nickname 'Pockmarked Pitcher'. Fourteen years ago, he entered the then Chungang School (中央學校) and immediately became widely known as an iron-armed pitcher. However, due to certain circumstances, a year later he transferred his enrollment to Hwimun Ŭisuk (徽文義塾). As his natural talent gradually began to flourish, he met the opportunity to fully display his innate abilities under the coaching of Mr. Pak Sŏgyun. This was eight years ago. In the Korean preliminary round of the All-Japan Middle School Baseball Tournament hosted by the Osaka Asahi (大朝), facing Kyŏngsŏng Middle School (京城중학) in a decisive game, they crushed them ten to zero at the Hullyŏnwŏn (訓練院) field. He then set a record by participating in the main tournament held at Naruo (鳴尾) Field in Ōsaka (大阪) as the Korean representative, the first and last (to date) time for a Korean team. Although they defeated the Manchurian (滿洲) representative, Tairen (大連) Commerce, and suffered a regrettable loss to Ritsumeikan Middle School (立命舘 中等部), the cause lay in the injury to catcher Kim Chŏngsik, which damaged the entire team's morale; it wasn't a defeat due to lack of skill, as can be inferred from the contemporary commentary by the Osaka Asahi (大朝). The fact that the Hwimun Kobo (徽文高普) baseball team traveled all the way to Japan and became recognized by themselves and others as among the top-tier teams in this tournament, composed solely of regional representatives, was certainly due in part to stars like catcher Kim Chŏngsik and shortstop Chŏng In'gyu, but the main reason was the iron-armed pitcher Kim, who bore 70-80% of the team's responsibility in defense. He was not only a famous pitcher but also, along with Kim Chŏngsik, a powerful hitter, forming the core of the offense.
After Hwimun Kobo returned from the Japan expedition, the publicity was such that Koreans and foreigners alike talked of Hwimun Kobo, Hwimun Kobo, making Hwimun known to almost everyone. If this achievement belongs to the school's baseball team, one can understand how great the contribution of Kim, the central figure of that team, was. Even after leaving Hwimun School, Kim sometimes came up to Kyŏngsŏng (京城) with the Chŏnju (全州) team or the Pusan (釜山) team, and although his former prowess may have faded, he still showed his characteristic presence. As such, he is not only a celebrity known to Koreans and foreigners by the nickname 'Pockmarked Pitcher' but also an unparalleled famous pitcher who decorated a page in Korean baseball history. Perhaps misfortune befell him because his fame was too great, but the Kim of today wanders, east yesterday and west today. Though he reportedly still hasn't abandoned baseball life, it is likely baseball for consolation as an endless wanderer; as an athlete, he probably belongs to the past.

Yi Yŏngmin
While at Paejae Kobo (培材高普), he was active in baseball, soccer, and track and field. After graduating and while attending Yŏnhŭi Chŏnmun School (延禧專門學校), he finally reached his full potential. In soccer, baseball, and track and field, he ranks among the representative Korean athletes, displaying activity several times that of ordinary people. In this respect, he can be called the all-around athlete following his great senior, Ma Ch'unsik. While he was at Yŏnhŭi Chŏnmun, during a rival baseball game against Kyŏngsŏng Medical College (京城醫專), he hit a home run over the center field fence, becoming the first Korean to set the record for a fence-clearing home run at Kyŏngsŏng Stadium (京城運動場). After graduating, he joined the Bank of Chōsen (殖銀). Three years ago in the summer, during a match against the Keio University (慶大) expedition team, he played as a Bank of Chōsen athlete and hit another massive home run far over the center fielder's head against the strong pitching of Miyamoto Saburō (宮武三郞)—Keio's ace pitcher and, at the time, a great pitcher nationwide in Japan, who, after graduation, astonished the world by being offered an initial salary of 300 yen by various competing employers. Since then<47>, Yi's fame has grown even greater. The fact that he maintains a distinct presence—sometimes as pitcher, sometimes catcher, sometimes outfielder—in the Bank of Chōsen team, which includes more than half of the players from the Tokyo Big6 Baseball League, proves his skill thusly.

Kim Chŏngsik
While attending Hwimun Kobo (徽文高普), along with Kim Chongse, he was a famous catcher, famous runner, and powerful hitter, a pillar during the golden age of the Hwimun baseball club. He was also a master in soccer and short-distance track and field. In the current Korean sports world, he certainly forms a pair of jewels alongside Yi Yŏngmin and is famous as the 'Perennial Athlete' or 'Ageless Athlete'. Considering he is 32 years old, it's natural for him to earn such nicknames among Koreans who tend to age early. He has stated he is confident he can play until at least 40. If his words hold true, we will joyfully be able to see his brave figure on the sports field for another 8 years. If any readers wish to see his face, go to Kyŏngsŏng Stadium (京城運動場) and look for the Kyŏngsŏng City Celebration (京城府慶) baseball team. Among them, there will be an athlete who is the most dark-skinned—at first glance appearing as if made of bronze. If you assume that athlete is Kim, you won't be mistaken.

Kim Ŭnbae
When and by what means could a Korean ever advance onto the world stage? To step onto the grand world stage, move alongside world figures, and also shout out loud? This has been a great question and consequently a central point of interest. To this, Kim Ŭnbae of Yangjŏng Kobo (養正高普), a middle school student of slight build and short stature, decisively broke the world record of 2 hours, 32 minutes, 39.8 seconds set by the Finnish giant Kolehmainen in 1920, during the marathon race at the Chōsen Shrine Games (朝鮮神宮競技大會) held on October 18th last year. He established an astonishing new world record of 2 hours, 26 minutes, 12 seconds, solving our great question. He delivered a great iron hammer blow to the pessimists who claimed Koreans could not advance globally, not only achieving the absolute merit of opening up a dark path ahead but also, as the 10th International Olympics were imminent, this news was widely transmitted throughout the world, causing a huge 'sensation' in the field. He is a record holder who ranks among world figures as a Korean.
Also last autumn, on November 3rd, representing Korea (朝鮮), he participated in the marathon at the Meiji Shrine Games (明治神宮競技大會) in Tōkyō (東京). Although he couldn't win due to a miscalculation of the distance on the return route, the record he set then still placed him 4th in the world marathon rankings for last year, firmly establishing his abilities, which had been doubted by some. Consequently, Kim's movements are being watched closely by people worldwide. Since the first Korean preliminary round for the International Olympics also concluded on May 8th, if he is selected in the second preliminary round on May 25th, we anticipate he will participate in the 10th International Olympics, where over 2,000 representative athletes from more than 50 countries gather to fight for the honor of their home countries. Anticipating that he will unleash tremendous spirit there for the fellow countrymen residing abroad, we once again wish for his strong performance.

Closing Remarks
While writing a critique of athletes, I know I should primarily focus on the strengths and weaknesses of their techniques. However, considering that it's difficult even for specialists in this field to find interest unless they specifically study it, I have created something closer to a 'biography' than a 'critique'. Due to lack of time, there were things I should have investigated further, but I didn't have the leisure. I also know it would have been better to select and introduce more old and new athletes, but due to space limitations, I inevitably omitted athletes who definitely deserved introduction, potentially inviting criticism like "If you introduced Mr. So-and-so, why didn't you introduce Mr. So-and-so?". This was due to unavoidable circumstances.
Finally, for convenience, I used the designation '君' (kun - Mr./Sir). I hope the great seniors will not be too disappointed, considering it an attitude lacking deference.<48>

**Summary**

This 1932 article by Kim T'aeho critiques prominent Korean athletes of the era, focusing on Ma Ch'unsik (baseball, soccer, track), Kim Chongse (baseball pitcher), Yi Yŏngmin (baseball, soccer, track), Kim Chŏngsik (baseball catcher, track), and Kim Ŭnbae (marathon). It praises their skills and contributions to Korean sports, often highlighting their "all-around" abilities and leadership, while also touching upon their personalities (Ma's temper, Kim Chongse's wandering life) and the challenges they faced. Kim Ŭnbae's recent world record in the marathon is presented as a major achievement bringing international recognition to Korea. For historians, the article offers insights into the burgeoning sports culture in colonial Korea, the role of sports figures as national heroes despite colonial constraints, the specific achievements and public personas of key athletes, the social difficulties faced by some (Ma Ch'unsik's disappearance, Kim Chongse's instability), and the immense pride associated with international competition and record-breaking, particularly Kim Ŭnbae's marathon achievement ahead of the Olympics. The language also reflects the linguistic state of Korean in the 1930s.

朝鮮新舊運動選手評
						金泰浩

李榮敏君 金鍾世君 馬春植君
머리말
뜻하지 아니한 명령-東光社로부터 『朝鮮新舊運動選手評』을 좀 써보라는 것이다.

馬春植君
