A Rainy Evening
						Kim Nu'gye (金淚溪)

Rain is falling! Rain is falling! Mist shed by the foolish sky, trying to see new sprouts from the withered grass and trees, is falling.

Water pools on the bright red earth. A boy splashes about! Wearing a visor cap (目出帽) and drawers (잠뱅이), and 20-jeon soldier's boots! Splashing about as if he were an Army General. In his hand, he holds a rubber gun, and from his lips flows a sorrowful tune.
"Should have just slept where I worked,
Isn't the 10-ri mountain pass fearsome?"

A colt turning in the leaky mill shed (연자깐)! Twisting its hind legs round and round. On its skinny rump, the merciless whip cries. Kicked out of the mill shed, where is there to go?

A cart (牛車) loaded with liquor barrels passes by, staggering on the muddy road. A bitter yet sweet smell wafts through the village.
Old and young open their nostrils wide and breathe it in. Breathe it in! The poison gas (독까쓰), the smell of a ruined nation.

On the well curb, two handfuls (쥐에기) of dried radish leaves (시라리)! A woman heavy with child, having lost her shoes, wanders about. The sticky mud clings to her rubber shoe-pair and won't let go. Balancing with the child on her back and the child in her belly, she wanders, unable to go forward or back. The water jar on her head impatiently urges her on. A single crow flies across the sullen sky.
It flies off, cursing.
"This darned field is far too flat!
We living things need to eat too, don't we?"

The child carried on the back cries! Throwing off the blanket (포단), it wails! Thrashing its arms, it wails! Trying to see the sunlight, seeking the Light (光明).

1931, 11, 26 <83>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Nu'gye paints a bleak and somber picture of a rainy evening, likely in rural colonial Korea. It juxtaposes the innocence of a boy playing soldier with the harsh realities faced by adults: a struggling horse, the pervasive smell of alcohol likened to poison gas and national ruin, and a heavily pregnant woman stuck in the mud. The poem culminates with a crying baby seeking light amidst the gloom, symbolizing a desperate yearning for hope in a desolate environment. For historians, the poem offers a poignant glimpse into the hardships and atmosphere of despair experienced by ordinary Koreans during the Japanese colonial period. The references to military imagery (boy as general, soldier's boots), economic hardship (worn-out horse, struggling woman), the comparison of alcohol fumes to "poison gas" and the "smell of a ruined nation," and the final cry for "Light" (光明) are particularly revealing of the socio-political climate and the psychological impact of colonial rule.

비오는 저녁
						金淚溪
비가 온다! 비가 온다! 말라 죽은 草木에서 새 싹을 보려고 미련한 하눌이 뿌리는 안개가 온다.
새빩안 땅엔 물이 고인다. 소년이 절버덕거린다! 目出帽에 잠뱅이 입고 20전짜리 군병구두! 제법 陸軍大將이나 된 듯이 절버덕거린다. 손에는 고무 총을 잡고 입에서는 애닯은 노래가락이 흐른다.
일하든 오굼에 잠이나 자지
고개 길 10里가 무섭지 안나

비 새는 연자깐에 돌아가는 망아지! 뒷다리를 뱅뱅 꼰다. 말라빠진 엉뎅이엔 사정없는 채찍이 운다. 연자깐에서 쫓겨난 거라지어 갈 곳이 어드메뇨?
술통 실은 달구지(牛車)가 흙탕 길을 비뚝거리며 지나간다. 쓰고도 달콤한 냄새가 동리를 휘돈다.
늙은이 젊은이 코구멍을 벌리고 들어마신다. 들어마신다! 독까쓰를, 망신 국 냄새를.

우물 돌우에 시라리(말린무잎) 쥐에기가 두덩이! 막 달 찬 여인이 신을 빠치고 헤매인다. 늘찐한 진흙은 고무신 짝을 물고 놓지 안는다. 등에 업은 아이와 배속에 안은 아이로 중심을 잡으며 오도 가도 못하고 헤맨다. 머리우에 물동이는 어서 가자 똥똥 거린다. 신멀툭한 하눌에 가마귀 한 마리 날아간다.
욕하며 날아간다.
이 놈의 벌판은 너무도 빤빤하구나
우리도 산 목숨 먹어야 하지안나
등에 업힌 아이가 운다! 포단을 헤치며 울어 댄다! 두 팔을 내저으며 울어 댄다! 해빛을 보랴고 光明을 찾으려.
1931, 11, 26  ＜83＞
