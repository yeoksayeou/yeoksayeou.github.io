잡지명 동광 제34호
발행일 1932년 06월 02일
기사제목 호텔 콘(HOTEL CONE) (Hotel Cone)
필자 在米國 韓黑鷗 (Han Hŭkku, In the United States)
기사형태 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Hotel Cone
By Han Hŭkku, In the United States

1
"Hotel Cone! Hotel... Cone...! ... Hotel Cone!"
The person shouting in a thin, listless voice, standing in front of Madison Station Square (廣場), was an East Asian young man who still looked not yet past thirty, with black hair. He stood stock-still in front of a car painted with "Hotel Cone" and shouted once more.
"Hotel—"
"Say! Go into the platform and call out!" Just as he was about to shout again, the person sitting and ordering him about was the car's driver.
"Alright!" Without a moment's thought, he tossed back the reply and headed straight into the crowd of passengers pouring out of the station exit. Then he entered the platform.
His uniform and hat, of course, bore the name "Hotel Cone," and there was no one at this small Madison station who didn't know him.
He looked around blankly, scanning the gazes of the passengers lining up and coming out onto the platform. Hoping someone might call for him. But no one was looking for him.
He only saw men and women calling out "Aigo Honey" or "Daddy," hugging each other and kissing.
He turned his head this way and that, walking distractedly along the platform like a man out of his wits.
"Damn bastards! Kissing like their damn lips are going to fall off..." He turned his head the other way and muttered to himself. To him, everything seemed like bullshit.
"Can't even get one damn passenger!" As he stood muttering this,
"T'omasŭ Ri! (Thomas Lee)" someone called from behind. He turned his head quickly toward the spot with a startled gesture. It was because he instantly recognized the voice as Korean. The person running toward him, calling his name, was his hometown friend, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ Kim (Chester Kim), whom he hadn't expected at all.
"What brings you here, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ?"<55> He stepped forward and grasped his hand tightly.
"You're having a tough time, I see!" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ glanced over T'omasŭ's uniform and hat.
"Not really! How did you end up here?"
"I was just passing through and heard T'omasŭ was here, so I came looking. Luckily, I saw T'omasŭ passing by from the train, quickly got off, and managed to find you!"
"Thank you!"
The two stood there, overjoyed but unsure what to do. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ once again carefully observed T'omasŭ's uniform. Just then, a young woman, standing with three handbags set down, called out languidly,
"Boy!"
"Yes, Ma'am!" T'omasŭ replied, turning toward the young woman, then turned his head back.
"Wait for me outside the station for a bit, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ!" he said quickly and walked away.
Ch'oesŭt'ŏ stood blankly, watching T'omasŭ's retreating figure as he tucked one bag under his arm, carried one in each hand, and walked briskly away with his head tilted to one side. Even after T'omasŭ had exited the station, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ stood there멍하니, imagining T'omasŭ's appearance.
"Hotel Boy!" He uttered the words softly.
The stationmaster came out, pulling a watch from his pocket and putting a whistle to his lips. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ picked up the handbag he had placed in front of him and looked up at the clock on the wall indicating nine in the morning as he walked out to the square outside.
"Ch'oesŭt'ŏ!" T'omasŭ called from one side of the square. The two met up.
"Have you had breakfast?"
"Yeah! Ate on the train—"
"Then... where should we meet tonight? Let's... let's meet at that Chinese restaurant," T'omasŭ said, glancing at the car parked on one side of the square.
"Do you have time?" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ also turned his head that way.
"Sure. I sent that girl to the hotel in another car... That fellow seems to be waiting for me..."
"Then hurry up and go!"
"Ah, it doesn't matter! I can get off work (옵푸) at six in the evening... We can meet then. Let's meet at Kwangdongnu (廣東樓) on Wasingt'ŏn Street (街)!"
"That sounds good. Then I'll head over there around six!"
"Alright! Let's do that!"
"Okay, hurry up now!"
"Okay, I will—but by the way, have you found lodging (려관)?" T'omasŭ asked, turning back.
"Oh, I'll find somewhere. Let's just meet tonight!"
"Alright! Goodbye!" "Goodbye!" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ stood watching until the car carrying T'omasŭ disappeared from view.

2
Ch'oesŭt'ŏ found lodging (려관) and went out sightseeing. But for him, who had spent a long time in Shik'ago (Chicago), there wasn't much that caught his eye. Although Wisŭk'ansin-ju (洲 - Wisconsin State) wasn't particularly rich in resources (富源), he hadn't known that Maedisŭn (Madison), the state capital (州廳)<56>, was such a small city.
Until evening, he toured around public institutions like the library, museum, park, and state university.
It was just past six o'clock when Ch'oesŭt'ŏ asked for Wasingt'ŏn Street (Washington Street) and found his way to Kwangdongnu (廣東樓). Stepping inside Kwangdongnu, the room was filled with an Eastern smell. The smoke-filled dining hall was mostly occupied by black-haired East Asians; only a few white men and women were visible.
He chose a table on one side and looked up at the clock on the wall. Ten past six. Chinese waiters glanced at him furtively, muttering something. "Yapponttoi (Japanese person)! (日本사람)" one of them muttered while standing there. After a short while, one brought a teapot and cup.
"You Yapponttoi?" he asked. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ understood but just smiled.
"No! I don't know Chinese."
"No speak Cheinee (Chinese)?"
"No."
"You Jepaenee (Japanese)?" he asked again, this time in broken English.
"No," Ch'oesŭt'ŏ replied again, smiling.
"What you?" He seemed determined to find out.
"What am I?" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ smiled again. Just then,
"Nei gei hou mou? (How are you?)" T'omasŭ called out in Cantonese as he entered.
"Ho T'omasŭ. Kolai (Korean person)! Ng! (Ah!)" the waiter said, smiling as if he finally understood.
"Ya, Kolai Sinsang! (先生 - Mr. Korean!)" and all three laughed.
"What would you like, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ?"
"Anything's fine."
"Sei! Yingkoi dou fu fong ke chap chae gong... uh... Pakiya kong ke..." (Waitress! Give me also sweet and sour pork chop suey... uh... white rice...)
"Dou fu fong Pakiya kong ke..." (Also sweet and sour pork white rice...)
"Hoikomdo!" (Okay!)
"Ya! Ya!" the waiter replied, bowing, poured them each a cup of tea, and left.
"When did you learn Cantonese, T'omasŭ?"
"Just picked up a phrase or two. Learned a few words from a Cantonese guy I work with at the hotel—" The two lifted the cups of tea placed before them and drank.
"So, what's the plan, anyway?"
"What plan? I'm heading to the University of Minesot'a (Minnesota) now, but I don't know if there'll be a chance (쵄스) to work my way through school there!"
"Minesot'a? Shouldn't be bad! Didn't Mr. Pak graduate from there before?"
"Pak? Back then, it was easy to study anywhere. Now, it seems hard even to get food, let alone work your way through school anywhere."
"Really? How is it working your way through school in Shik'ago?"
"It's tough. This year is a complete disaster! Didn't the gangster king (暴漢者의 王) Mayor T'amsŭn (Thompson) just get kicked out by Cermak of the Democratic party? Well, it served him right, but in this Depression (띠푸래순 - 恐慌), it seems life has gotten even harder for those without money—"
"That guy seemed quite smart, talking about rationalizing taxes and whatnot, right?"
"Hmph, who's going to pay them! T'amsŭn supposedly taxed the rich heavily, but until now, he just let small business owners be, without even investigating their business licenses (營業場), let alone imposing business taxes—"<57>
"That wasn't bad."
"Exactly. Nowadays, the city hall is about to close down due to lack of funds, dozens of banks close daily, parks are full of people sleeping on newspapers, public school teachers haven't received salaries for five months. Plus, every park is full of campaigns by communists and socialists, it's quite a mess."
"Hmph! Nothing can be done! Nothing!"
"Lately, socialists seem to be gathering more at the Shik'ago headquarters, looks like some big mob movement might break out."
"That's why the government says they'll deport all foreigners who spread so-called dangerous ideas, isn't it?"
"According to the newspapers, dozens are deported every month already."
"No matter how many they deport, it won't solve anything. Just a short while ago, they just let them be, didn't they? And now, by deporting and arresting them, society becomes more aware of the issue—" Just then, the waiter brought a tray of Chinese dishes. It had been a long time since they had eaten East Asian food.
"Care for a drink, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ?" "No, I don't know. Do they have alcohol?"
"Alcohol? Hmph! Asking if there's money is the question, but alcohol? That's not an issue! The term 'prohibition country' (禁酒國) means 'gold-liquor-country' (金-酒-國)!"
"Hmph!" The two laughed and started eating.

"Anyway, enough other talk, how did you end up working at the hotel?" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ asked, smiling, thinking the question was a bit late.
"Oh, it just happened. Did I have any specific plan (게획) when I came here from Shik'ago? I just set out thinking maybe if there was a job, I could work for about a year, save up a thousand dollars, and then try going to P'ullansŭ (France)."
"So, are you still studying painting?" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ had always been very sympathetic towards T'omasŭ's art studies.
"Painting? It's pathetic. Since leaving the Shik'ago Art Institute, I haven't even seen a canvas."
"What do you plan to do?"
"What plan is there? Just if I get about a thousand dollars, I'll cross over to P'ullansŭ, look around, and if I run out of food again, maybe cross over to Rŏsia (Russia), and then there's nothing left but to go home."
"Why? Aren't there quite a few good places in America too, like Yeil (Yale) or Habŏdŭ (Harvard)?"
"Do I have money? Money is an issue, but what's the point of studying that stuff? If Chosŏn were a rich country like this one, maybe I could make a living selling paintings, but I've moved beyond all that."
"Still, aren't there many modern artists here?"
"Artists? It's money-fueled nonsense. One painting by P'ik'aso (Picasso) makes all the art in this country pale. For a while, they raved about Sejannŭ (Cézanne) or Koho (Gogh), but lately, it's all 'Long live P'ik'aso'."
"But it seems the era of imitation has passed."
"Well, you could see it that way. But while American philosophy or literature might have emerged against a new American backdrop, in art, they haven't yet escaped the foundation of 'artistic pleasure'. The towering buildings (삘딩 - building), expressions of pragmatist philosophy, jazz, like some crazy dance born from the perversion of accelerated civilization's people—that's the American style! Anyway, in art, apart from the practical products of advertising techniques (廣告術), there's nothing special, still just chasing after classical art..." T'omasŭ declared this with a serious attitude.
"Hey, shouldn't we order more rice?"
"No."
They put down their chopsticks. T'omasŭ lit a cigarette and continued the conversation.<58>
"I've worked at this 'Hotel' for two months now, and although I haven't earned much money, if I stay until next summer—it's September now—I could earn a thousand dollars. Then, as I planned, I intend to travel the world once and then return home."
"..."
"And my sole desire is to find out what kind of art is most demanded by the present age, by our Chosŏn society. As for me, I always think about life first and its eternal extension, and believe that creating what is necessary for that is the eternal art of humanity...
Also, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ, rather than going to Minesot'a to study sociology, if sociology is your goal, it might be better to study here at the University of Wisŭk'ansin."
"Well! Of course, the University of Shik'ago is famous for sociology, but I'm coming to a rural school because of tuition fees... If I can work my way through school anywhere at a state university, that would be good..."
"Not only that, but Professor X, a socialist scholar, is the head of the sociology department here, and quite a few students gather around him. I don't know about Western students, but there are currently over 200 Chinese students. Mostly they came because the agriculture department (農科) is famous, but quite a few seem to be studying under Professor X."
"Really! I've heard a lot about Professor X..."
"Of course, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ and I have much understanding, but the point is, let's study more thoroughly! Let's ditch the idea of a Ph.D., which we swore vehemently upon on the T'aep'yanyang (Pacific Ocean). We need to delve more practically into what we require! Actually, nowadays in Chosŏn too, PhDs and Bachelors are becoming common, and their existence and activities are faint, so it seems feudal ideas like honor and whatnot have diminished..."
"Indeed. Here too, lately, many people are interested in practical society."
"Of course! Rather than being buried inside the school, flattering people for graduation or degrees, practical research in society is more necessary... Besides, what feudal existence is there in our country anymore! We were ruined by it. And graduation is really just for show, isn't it!"
"Aren't Kim, Ri, and Pak doing good work out there without graduation certificates? They used to live buried in the library day and night..."
"That's right! Exactly! Ch'oesŭt'ŏ, you should try learning from Professor X here too. And since the semester starts in about two weeks, rather than going somewhere first and not knowing how things will turn out, maybe you could enroll here right away, study, and perhaps get a few hours of work where you work? You could probably graduate in a year here, right? You've completed 3 years in Shik'ago, haven't you?"
"Yes."
"And also see what it tastes like to stand there shouting 'Hotel Cone'... Ha... ha." The two laughed.
"But Ch'oesŭt'ŏ will work indoors, and starting next week, when the black guy (껌둥이) returns, I'll also work indoors. I've been going out shouting constantly in his place, but I can't bring in customers as well as he does—"
"Then, maybe T'omasŭ can help me find a way to work my way through school here!"
"Well, it's not like I have to help, but think about studying here."
"Please try your best."
"So, where should we go hang out!" T'omasŭ said, glancing around the dining hall. Here and there, young Western men and women<59> sat around. They got up from their seats, paid the bill, and went outside.
The night street was chaotic amidst the noise. At the theater across the street, large letters spelling out Ch'ap'ullin's (Chaplin's) "City Lights" flashed on and off like a panorama.

3
It was a Sunday night, one week before the Christmas break began.
T'omasŭ, before finishing his work, threw down what he was doing and returned to his room.
"These damn bastards..." Leaning alone against the wall, he stared down at the floor with an angry face. His face was pale blue. His eyes were narrowed slits. His tightly pressed lips looked ready to let out a thunderous shout at any moment.
"Aargh—" With a powerful shout, the statue (彫像) of Venus that had been on the table shattered on the floor. Following that, Rodin's statue also shattered. Then his eyes darted around the room like lightning.
Just then, someone knocked on the door.
"Nobody! Get outta here! (아모도 없다! 여기서 잃어저라!)" He shot out, eyes fixed on the door. But the knocking continued.
"It's me (나야!)! It's me!" It was, of course, the Chinese guy. He also knew T'omasŭ was angry, so although he usually just walked in, this time he knocked. T'omasŭ jerked the door open irritably.
"What you want?" he snapped, still holding the doorknob. But the Chinese guy stepped into the room, saying "No, no," as he entered.
T'omasŭ simply collapsed onto the chair. The Chinese guy waited for T'omasŭ's anger to subside, closed the door, and stood quietly.
T'omasŭ's bowed head seemed unlikely to lift anytime soon. The Chinese guy, looking around at the fragments of the shattered statues on the floor,
"T'omasŭ!" he called out. T'omasŭ looked up at him with a flushed face, without saying anything.
"Let's go out and beat that bastard up! That..." he started, moving the hand in his pocket.
But T'omasŭ's head drooped again, and there was no reply. The Chinese guy also stood silently, gazing blankly at T'omasŭ's form.
For a long while, only the motionless shadows of the two men faced each other in the room. Only the sound of the wind blowing outside the courtyard occasionally echoed.
Finally, the Chinese guy grasped the doorknob.
Startled by the sound of the door opening,
"Where are you going?" T'omasŭ stood up.
"..."
"Come back in!" The Chinese guy came back in.
"Where are you going?"
"Nowhere specific!"
"No, don't do that! Go to your room and sleep. Let's meet tomorrow morning. We have things to do then! Understand?" he said softly, placing a hand on his shoulder. The Chinese guy, hearing T'omasŭ's softened tone, seemed relieved.
"Alright, that's good!" he said and returned to his room.<60>
After the Chinese guy left T'omasŭ's room, for some time, the sound of T'omasŭ sobbing could be heard. Besides the portraits of Marksŭ (Marx), Koho (Gogh), and Pairon (Byron) hanging on the wall, there was, of course, no one else to witness his form, collapsed on the bed, weeping.
It was when the sound of the wind passing outside the window had ceased. He slowly sat up on the bed and looked around the room with red eyes.
After a while, he sat facing the table and opened his diary (日記冊). Then, after gazing at the window for a long time, he picked up his pen.
"XX Month XX Day. After leaving home, I lived having forgotten tears. No matter how painful things threatened me, I just laughed them off.
The life of Marksŭ in the slums of Rŏndon (London), Renin's (Lenin's) prison life, and the wandering life of the artist Kogan (Gauguin) always gave me great strength and lessons. Besides them, all the warriors of will were things that could make me forget all this suffering.
And all the facts that I exist in the world and that I also move are pursuits of their teachings and also for the extension of that.
However, a person without XX, whether they go to Manchuria, Saiberia (Siberia), or America, wherever they go in this world, will have no place to stand, not even enough ground to stick an awl into.
This iron rule (鐵則) will not disappear until people forget hunger. Am I not unable to avoid such burdens, such stings?
'Today, I also...'" Writing up to this point, he looked at the hot teardrop falling onto the paper. Then he buried his face, trying hard to calm his pounding chest.
After a while, whatever he thought of, he put away the diary and took out a sheet of paper.
"Dear Ch'oesŭt'ŏ-gun—
Everything did not go as planned. Of course, my plan to go to P'ullansŭ (France) was given up long ago. But now, if I leave this place, your affairs might also fail. However, since there's a week left of vacation, it seems there might not be a major failure, but starting tomorrow, it seems to be our fate for you too to bid farewell forever to 'Hotel Cone.'
Then, I hope you will strive to work elsewhere next semester. I am leaving $20 here, hoping you will use it for the week.
There is no need to know where I am going now. A drifting body wants to drift as much as it pleases with a drifting heart. But there is no need to be disappointed in me either. I think I will probably head towards the place I always talked about.
And there is no need to know why I am leaving here either. Just, as I said, I hope you do not work at 'Hotel Cone' any longer.
I will write again later, ending here for now.
-4 AM on the last night at Hotel Cone - T'omasŭ"
Finishing the letter, he took out his suitcase and gathered his books and everything else. Then he quietly closed the door and went downstairs.
Of course, there was no one in the guest rooms. Only the female clerk, who had been dozing in the side room since evening, was there.
He quietly went and called the clerk. The clerk looked at him, startled.
"Where are you going?" she asked, seeing the suitcase.
"No!" T'omasŭ said, taking two letters out of his pocket.
"Give one to the manager, and give the other one to Ch'oesŭt'ŏ when he comes tomorrow afternoon!"<61> Placing the two letters in front of the clerk, he slipped quietly outside. The black doorman also asked, "Where are you going?" but he just said "No" and stepped outside.
The wind was calm, but the winter night in the north country was bitingly cold.
He stood blankly in front of the door for a while, looking up at the reddish windows of the hotel.
After some time, a taxi took him to the fateful station front. There were still thirty minutes left before the train to Nyuyok (New York).
He looked at the black people and laborers sleeping on the benches in the waiting room.
The silent winter night was chillingly cold. He stared blankly out the window at the square covered in snow, glistening under the moonlight.
After a while, startled awake by the bell like the laborers, he lined up towards the ticket counter.

4
The next afternoon, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ finished his exams at school and came to Hotel Cone as usual. The clerk, as if waiting, called him to stop as he was going upstairs.
"Receive this letter!" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ took the letter without a word and went upstairs. Just then, the Chinese guy came down from the stairs (층대).
"Hey Ch'oesŭt'ŏ! Do you know where T'omasŭ went?"
"Where?" he asked back, then pulled the letter from his pocket and carefully looked at the handwriting again. Then he quickly tore open the envelope.
After reading it, his face turned pale blue. The two faced each other on the stairs (층대) for a long moment.
"Come to my room!" the Chinese guy said, moving his foot from the stairs. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ, with a puzzled thought about what might have happened, followed the Chinese guy into his room.
The Chinese guy sat facing him and began to tell everything that had happened the previous night.
"Last night, a big fight broke out between T'omasŭ and P'ol (Paul - the head of the lower staff)."
"How?" Ch'oesŭt'ŏ asked with startled eyes.
"That damn bastard..." The Chinese guy looked around the room with a dumbfounded expression, gesturing as if ready to fight again right now.
"Okay, tell me why they fought—"
"Ch'oesŭt'ŏ doesn't know, but!" he began slowly.
'For the past two years at this hotel, there's been a black girl (껌둥이 계집애) who comes every morning to tidy the beds in the guest rooms, right? Maybe Ch'oesŭt'ŏ doesn't know her. Anyway, that girl used to glance flirtatiously at T'omasŭ, and the manager, the clerk, and that troublemaker P'ol always teased T'omasŭ, laughingly asking, "Do you love that girl?" right?
Then T'omasŭ would just smile without answering, or sometimes say, "Don't be silly! (어리석게 그러지 말어!)"
One day, I don't know what P'ol said to that girl, but P'ol told T'omasŭ that the girl was dying for him, asking why he didn't love her, teasing him again, right?
Until then, T'omasŭ thought everyone was joking (롱), but as they started saying such things with increasingly strange attitudes, T'omasŭ apparently told P'ol, "If you say that again, I won't stand for it, so be warned and cut it out!"
And one day, he also told me, "Even if Westerners look down on (없수이 본대도)<62> East Asians, I didn't know it was this bad. What does it matter if I happen to love that girl! It's absolutely not because I think she's lowly or black. I'm someone who wouldn't think that way even about a blonde white girl.
But it's not just a joking attitude; they seem to think that people like us, East Asians, whether it's a black person or anyone, will just fool around with love or whatever if we get the chance!
Damn bastards!
How many white girls live with black guys! Damn them!
Ask Tevisŭ (Davis - the black servant)! What does he say? Doesn't he claim all the white girls, whether noble ladies or gentlewomen, are his?
Not long ago, the newspapers made a huge fuss about some incident involving screwing around with a dog, life or death stuff, what are they talking about, what they themselves have done? Damn bastards!
But this isn't about us getting involved with black people; it's because they look down on us East Asians as being only of that level, that's why I'm angry!'
We laughed about it like that and let it go, and since then until now, things have been fine without any mention of it, right?" he said, smacking his lips.
'But lately, that black girl seems pregnant, her belly is all swollen, and she's walking around like that!
So, for some time now, P'ol and everyone at the hotel have been gossiping, "Whose baby is it?" and P'ol keeps saying, "Well, it must be T'omasŭ's!" laughing and making a fuss, you know!
So yesterday evening, while we were working in the kitchen, P'ol came in and said to T'omasŭ,
"When is your baby due?" and then laughed quietly to himself, can you believe it! T'omasŭ got furious, picked up a water bowl nearby, and threw it in his laughing face! Then P'ol lunged at him, right?
Just as a fistfight was about to break out, the manager came in and stopped them, but T'omasŭ kept trying to charge, shouting,
"Damn bastard!" but everyone held him back tightly and wouldn't let go!
"So whose child is it, tell me?"
"You damn bastard! Only bastards like you, you lechers, would do that! Do you think any East Asian person is like that?"
"So you're saying it's not your child!" P'ol shot back even louder, and the manager went "Ha! Ha!" and laughed again, so T'omasŭ broke free, shouted,
"Goddamn!" and stormed out, right? So I also got angry and went out with my pistol!' he said, turning his head towards the drawer. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ sat with his head tilted, let out a long sigh, and slowly stood up.
"What should we do?" The Chinese guy also stood up and asked. After a moment, he said in a low voice,
"Just endure it... T'omasŭ has left for somewhere, and I'm quitting my job here too, so that's that!"
"Then I'll leave too," the Chinese guy said sadly.
"But there's no need for you to leave, so just stay put until something else happens! It'll be the same wherever else you go," Ch'oesŭt'ŏ said, came out to his room, and exited through the back door without another word.
Back home, Ch'oesŭt'ŏ sat like a man out of his mind until late at night, unable to study. He thought about the time three years ago when he crossed the Pacific with T'omasŭ, about T'omasŭ's<63> past, who had talent in composition, singing, and drawing since elementary school, and how T'omasŭ had encouraged him to continue his studies, paying half his tuition, finding him work, and being a great support. Thinking of this, he felt a sense of resentment towards the world.
Midnight passed, and the night was very quiet. Outside, only the footsteps of the dormitory supervisor patrolling on the snow could be heard.
He changed into his pajamas and sat down facing his desk. He opened his diary.
'XX Month XX Day. I do not intend to criticize you. I always admire how the hot blood in your body always surges for the love of Chosŏn and for justice.
Although young people are said to live by emotion, I always admire your great emotion—your hatred for the dirty world, your cursing of cold-hearted people, and your sympathy for the pitiful.
Let us transcend small emotions for the sake of great emotion! Isn't this what you have always emphatically argued?
I have no doubts regarding the circumstances you faced because of the black girl. Of course, I had heard many times since our Shik'ago days that you, more than anyone, understood, sympathized with, and pitied the lives of black people and their unfortunate situation.
Of course, you are stronger in class consciousness than in race consciousness. You, who love the masses, would have surely sympathized even more with the modern slaves, the black people, and also the masses whom others deem lowly.
Even if you had loved that girl, you would not have been afraid or ashamed to admit it.
Your kinky, masculine hair, rough like firewood, yet your hands, surprisingly gentle—whenever I see you, I don't know how much I resent the world.
But, my friend! Where are you going!
Where are you heading, embracing a boiling heart! In this land where there is no place to rest our hearts, how long must we sing the Bohemian's song! But all this will surely make us create a great tomorrow.
Thank you! I will use the $20 you left for next semester's tuition! Your affection only ever makes me weep.
I hope you do not forget that I always anticipate your great future.
The day is biting cold. Outside, it is a deep winter night with no sound. I feel like I've lost my mind all day. I think again of my forgotten hometown.
I plan to write my semester thesis (론문) on "A Sociological Study of National Psychology."
I couldn't prepare for the exams, but as the night is deep, I lie down (눗는다) in bed as I am.

**Summary**

This short story depicts the experiences of Korean students, T'omasŭ Ri and Ch'oesŭt'ŏ Kim, in Madison, Wisconsin, USA, during the Great Depression (circa 1931). T'omasŭ works a menial job as a "hotel boy" to save money, hoping to study art in France, while Ch'oesŭt'ŏ plans to study sociology. The story highlights their struggles with poverty, disillusionment with American society, and encounters with racism. A central conflict arises when T'omasŭ is falsely accused by white colleagues of impregnating a Black hotel maid, leading to a violent confrontation and T'omasŭ's abrupt departure. Ch'oesŭt'ŏ reflects on T'omasŭ's idealism, suffering, and the harsh realities faced by Koreans abroad, contemplating themes of identity, justice, and the search for meaning in a hostile environment.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a valuable glimpse into the lives of Korean students in America during the colonial period and the Great Depression. It starkly portrays the economic hardships that forced educated individuals into low-wage labor, the pervasive racism they faced (both subtle and overt, directed at them as East Asians and complicated by interactions involving Black Americans), and their critical perspectives on American society versus their enduring connection to Korea. The discussion about socialist ideas (Professor X, Marx, Lenin) and disillusionment with traditional academic paths reflects contemporary intellectual currents among some Korean students seeking practical solutions for their homeland. The complex racial dynamics depicted—white prejudice towards Koreans, the specific accusation involving a Black woman, and T'omasŭ's own expressed sympathy for Black suffering coupled with anger at being stereotyped—are particularly revealing.

『(전략-원문) 어제밤에 그 껌둥이 게집애는 객실 358호에 류하든 배뚱뚱한 백인이 다리고 아라스카로 떠낫소. 들으니 그 백인은 장사하는 사람이라고 하는데 그 게집애에게 千불자리 퍼콧(털외투)을 사입히고 다리고 갓다고 합니다. 그러고 그 게집애는 얘가 낳게 되엇다고 얼마동안 기다리라 하엿으나, 그대로 다리고 갓다고 합니다. 물론 오래전부터 친햇든 모양입니다. (후략-원문)』
