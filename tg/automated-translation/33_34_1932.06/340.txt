Magazine Title: 동광 제34호
Publication Date: 1932년 06월 02일
Article Title: 花山과 赤城江 (Hwasan and Chŏksŏnggang River)
Author: 楊相殷
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Hwasan is piled only with rocks,
Between them, red decorations
Are azaleas, beautiful azalea flowers.

Below, the Chŏksŏnggang water winds around,
Floating on the deep blue surface
Are herons, flocks of pure white herons.

In olden times, when fairies came to play,
They gathered armfuls of azaleas
And scattered them upon the deep blue water.

Azaleas on the mountain, azaleas on the river,
Above and below, mountain and water are red hues,
That's why it became Hwasan and Chŏksŏnggang.

Even now, azalea flowers float on the water,
But the fairies from Heaven are nowhere to be seen,
Only idle herons come and go. <83>

**Summary**

This poem by Yang Sang-ŭn lyrically describes the scenery of Hwasan (Flower Mountain) and the Chŏksŏnggang River. It explains the origins of their names through a local legend: fairies once scattered red azaleas from the rocky mountain onto the river below, turning both mountain and water red, hence "Flower Mountain" and "Red Fortress River." The poem contrasts this mythical past with the present, where the azaleas still bloom and float, but the fairies are gone, leaving only herons. From a historical perspective, the poem reflects an interest in local Korean landscapes and folklore during the colonial period. While not overtly political, its focus on the natural beauty and legendary origins of a specific Korean place could be seen as a subtle affirmation of Korean identity and connection to the land amidst external rule.

花山과 赤城江
						楊相殷
花山은 바위로만 쌓이엇는데
그사이 사이사이 붉은장식은
진달래 아리따운 진달꽃이라.

그아래 赤城江물 감돌아든데
새파란 水面에서 떠도는 것은
해오리 새 하얀 해오리떼라.

예전에 仙女들이 놀러오면은
진달꽃 한아름을 보듬고와서
새파란 물우에다 뿌리고간걸.

山에도 진달래꽃 江도 진달꽃
우아래 山도물도 붉은빛이라
그래서 花山되고 赤城江인걸.

지금도 진달래꽃 물에떠돈데
天上의 仙女들은 간데가없고
한가한 해오리만 오고가는걸. ＜83＞
