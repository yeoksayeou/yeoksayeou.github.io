**Prayer for Paradise**
稻原 鄭日秀

Prayer for Paradise

Moon, you look down upon the whole world at once,
Where is the paradise I long for?
Is it the night of that distant desert where there is no sign of people, only star shadows?
-It is only round, then waning, and bright.

Wind, you even tell fortunes to the tree leaves,
Whisper secretly into my ear of my paradise,
Is it the spring garden where a feast of pleasure opens with light and scent and song?
-It only blows and flutters.

Sea, you embrace this world in your bosom,
The paradise I seek is also there,
Is it beyond that horizon where water and sky affectionately touch?
-It is only endlessly wide, they say.

Mountain, they say you know all things of past and present,
I pray, give me a subtle hint of my paradise,
Is it the heart of the person most suited to my will? Is it when I call them "Beloved"?
-It is only long-standing and high, they say.

**I Want to Go to Venice**

I want to go to Venice (Penissŭ)! I want to see Harbin (Halbin) too!
They say one cannot sleep for the sound of water and song,
Somehow, I want to go see Venice by night.
Though there is nothing waiting for me, my young heart is drawn.

Perhaps in a different place like Harbin,
I might even meet a lover who is also a traveler on the road.
Meeting by chance in a far-off foreign land and parting sadly,
Such a wayfarer’s love must surely be a noble thing.

**The Weaver Girl and I**

The one who lulls me gently to sleep each night
Is the big girl in the house across, weaving sashes (腰帶).
Pale like a four-o'clock flower grown in the shade,
But a fully grown maiden.

After reading and writing for a long time, alone, truly alone,
As I curl up in bed, it's mischievous youth,
My heart naturally stirs, doesn't it? Gets excited, doesn't it?

Even after the night deepens and the whole village sleeps,
As if she knows my inner thoughts, as if comforting me,
She makes the rhythmic (리즈미칼) sound of the shuttle, *talgak talgak*.

Oh, beautiful worker! My heart, even now,
Unknowingly drawn to you, follows the shuttle's sound,
Keeping time, one, two, and thus becomes a dream.

They say she weaves and weaves the thread of hope for the future,
What point is there in talking about whether others sleep or not?
Though you may not know, your shuttle's sound even wakes my dawn dreams.

Thus, from dawn till dusk, while not asleep,
As hope blows in your weaving heart, thoughts increase in my listening heart,
Shall I call this love? Or sympathy? Or perhaps my own pointless anguish?

Paying no heed to the daylight, paying no heed to the clear wind,
Paying no heed to the moon and stars,
In the dim corner of the room, as if crying, as if laughing, your life is laborious,
Oh, is this an ordeal for happiness? Or the caution of love?

-It can all be called a dream of a fleeting moment. <85>

**Summary**

This collection of three poems by Chŏng Il-su (under the pen name To Wŏn) expresses a multifaceted yearning. "Prayer for Paradise" voices a universal, almost spiritual longing for an ideal place, questioning nature's elements (moon, wind, sea, mountain) for its location, only to receive evasive answers, finally suggesting paradise might be found in human connection ("Beloved"). "I Want to Go to Venice" shifts to a more concrete desire for travel and exotic experiences (Venice, Harbin), tinged with youthful romanticism about chance encounters abroad. "The Weaver Girl and I" presents an intimate, empathetic observation of a neighbor girl's diligent labor, contrasting her tangible work producing "threads of hope" with the speaker's introspective "anguish" and dreams, blurring the lines between admiration, sympathy, and perhaps nascent affection.

From a historical perspective, the poems subtly reflect the era. The longing for escape, whether to an abstract "paradise" or specific foreign cities like Venice and Harbin (a major hub for Koreans in Manchuria at the time), could suggest a desire to transcend the limitations or hardships of life in colonial Korea. The depiction of the "Weaver Girl," engaged in laborious, perhaps low-paid work ("laborious life," "dim corner," "as if crying, as if laughing"), offers a glimpse into the economic realities and quiet perseverance of ordinary people, particularly women, during that period. The speaker's complex feelings towards her – a mix of sympathy, admiration, and potentially romanticized projection – hint at the social dynamics and intellectual reflections of the time.
