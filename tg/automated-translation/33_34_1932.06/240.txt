The Waves of May, Father's Corpse Lying on the Seashore
						By Yi Kyu-wŏn

As the May sun, like a beast struck by an arrow, covered its face and fled into the clouds,
Red blood falling high from the sky stained the sea慘凄 (ch'amch'e, miserably).
Beneath the solemn atmosphere, the sound of the great ocean's waves, shattered into pieces,
Roamed about as fiercely as the lion's roar (獅子帆) of a revolutionary army.
The stalwart men of the sea, standing on the golden sands,
Lift their arms and close their eyes like the people of Israel offering burnt sacrifices.

Beneath the May sky where the bells of resurrection ring loudly,
What is this sorrowful funeral dirge (輓歌) of death?
On the sacred May seashore where flower petals float,
Why the furious waves (怒濤) biting the rocks, why the shadow darkened with blood?
The mast fluttering in the gentle breeze (微風) is like a flag returned from the battlefield,
And the sailors (水夫), leaning weakly, are like wounded soldiers struck by bullets.

Thinking the brave sailors who set off last night would return singing lusty songs,
We waved hands to greet the ship flowing around the distant island touching the sky, but oh, the shock!
The sailing ship (帆船) was harshly (모지락스럽게) wounded by unexpected waves,
And the trembling sailors laid the corpse of the old stalwart (丈夫) down on the boat's edge.
For them, there is no spring, May was also inside a desolate grave.
Oh Heaven, why do you not know how to stop this miserable tragedy?
While the limbs of many, tormented by waiting, have weakened from struggle,
Father of the village! Why do you lie wordless beneath the straw mat (멍석)?

Oh lips that bit the tongue, oh space below the nose where cold blood flows,
The two arms that fought the sea have hideously shriveled,
And the legs that fiercely leaped about have collapsed and cannot rise.
Following the sad melody of the water birds taking flight with fluttering wings,
The gong booms dully through the clouds,
And the sounds of weeping sink deep, deep into the sea.

The May sunlight tumbles upon the black corpse,
Then dashes off into the infinitely vast silence.
Though the prelude (푸레류드) that tore the hearts of the poor people has not ended,
The boat once again pushes through the wind and waves (風浪), leaving only the old corpse on the shore.
Ah, the sound of those terrifying waves will surely never, ever stop,
But May will pass silently, leaving only the old corpse on the shore.
-1932. 5- <54>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Kyu-wŏn vividly depicts the grim discovery of a fisherman's ("Father of the village") corpse washed ashore in May. It contrasts the potential beauty and rebirth associated with spring ("May," "resurrection bells") with the harsh reality of death, struggle, and sorrow faced by the seaside community. The imagery is stark, portraying the sea as both a source of livelihood and a deadly force, and the sailors as returning wounded soldiers bearing a fallen comrade. The poem culminates in a sense of unending tragedy and the cyclical nature of hardship ("the prelude that tore the hearts of the poor people has not ended"), as the boat must sail again despite the loss, leaving the corpse behind under an indifferent sky.

For historians, this poem offers insight into the lived experiences and emotional landscape of Koreans, likely representing the impoverished fishing communities, during the colonial period. The themes of struggle against overwhelming forces (the sea, fate, poverty symbolized by the "prelude"), premature death, communal grief, and a sense of inescapable hardship resonate with the broader socio-economic difficulties and political powerlessness experienced under Japanese rule. The "prelude" might metaphorically refer to the ongoing suffering under colonialism. The raw depiction of loss and the bleak outlook reflect a common sentiment found in Korean literature of the era.
