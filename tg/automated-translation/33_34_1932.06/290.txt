Publication Date: 1932년 06월 02일
Article Title: 내 흙덩이를 들여다 보네 (I Peer into My Clod of Earth)
Author: 鄭且運祚
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

I Peer into My Clod of Earth
						Chŏng Ch'a-unjo

Beneath the blazing sun,
Roasted and groaning,
A clod of earth—
Wherever I may wander,
I stop and peer into it.

Among billions of clods of earth,
That it should be this mountain's clod—
My heart is drawn ever closer,
Gazing upon it with devotion (精城).

Even if moved to an experimental machine
And analyzed,
Even if mixed with water and drunk,
Away from this winding, flowing mountain range,
It cannot be replicated—
This precious clod of earth.

Ah,
Beneath the blazing sun,
Wherever I may wander,
Roasted, like a watery lump,
This clod of earth—
I peer into it.
1931, 8, 10 <82>

**Summary**

The poem describes the speaker's intense focus on a single clod of earth found on a mountain under the hot sun. Despite the existence of countless other clods, the speaker feels drawn to this specific one, seeing it as uniquely precious and inseparable from its origin on the mountain ridge, something that scientific analysis or displacement cannot capture. For historians, the poem's intense focus on a piece of native soil, imbuing it with unique value tied to its specific location ("this mountain"), can be interpreted as a subtle expression of attachment to the homeland and perhaps a form of quiet resistance or assertion of identity during the Japanese colonial period, when overt nationalism was suppressed. The "precious clod" could symbolize Korea itself.
