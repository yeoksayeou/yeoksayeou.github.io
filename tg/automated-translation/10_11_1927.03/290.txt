P'ip'agi
Yuan Dynasty Gao Dongjia (Original) Chosŏn Yang Paekhwa (Translation/Supplementation)

Act 7: The Talented Scholar Sets Out
Characters

Ch'ae Yong
Yi Kunok (Friend of Ch'ae Yong)
Sang Paekchang (Same as above)
Nak Tŭkhŭi (Same as above)

[Ch'ae Yong enters, singing]
Willow catkins fall onto my clothes in the wind, and fallen petals fly past my speeding horse. The weather is neither cold nor hot, quite pleasant, but facing the scenery of rivers and mountains, the sorrow of parting feels new. Turning my head, I look back the way I came; the place where my parents are only grows farther away. How could I so easily leave their loving care? While I cannot bear the longing feeling, from somewhere the cuckoo's cry moistens the sleeves of this traveler.

[Yi Kunok enters, singing]
The fragrant grass grows lush, but my old home is distant. The traveler's road is winding, and his appearance is weary; with whom shall I share conversation at the inn?

[Sang Paekchang and Nak Tŭkhŭi enter, singing]
They say Loyang grows ever closer, but how many cities still block the way?

[All sing together]
To relieve our pent-up feelings, let's seek out Apricot Blossom Village, loosen the saddle straps, and drink until we are drunk.

Ch'ae Yong: "Orioles chirp for a thousand li amidst green reflections on red;"
Sang Paekchang: "At the waterside village and mountain hamlet, wine banners flap in the wind."<84>
Nak Tŭkhŭi: "Travelers seem as if within a painted scene,"
Yi Kunok: "The weather is neither warm nor cold, just right; travelers coming and going meet."
All: "At this time, who does not sigh for east or west?"
(They look at each other)

Nak Tŭkhŭi: May I ask your surname?
Ch'ae Yong: I am called Ch'ae.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: And your style name?
Ch'ae Yong: It is Paekke.
Sang Paekchang: Elder brother, what is your surname?
Yi Kunok: I am called Yi.
Sang Paekchang: And your style name?
Yi Kunok: It is Kunok.
Ch'ae Yong: Elder brother, what is your surname?
Nak Tŭkhŭi: My surname is Nak.
Ch'ae Yong: And your style name?
Nak Tŭkhŭi: It is Tŭkhŭi.
Yi Kunok: Elder brother, what is your surname?
Sang Paekchang: My surname is Sang.
Yi Kunok: And your style name?
Sang Paekchang: It is Paekchang.

Nak Tŭkhŭi: I have long heard of your esteemed reputations, and I am fortunate to meet you today. Are you all on your way to the capital to take the civil service examination? (Smiling) I hope you elder brothers will not forsake this younger brother. And how about resting here for a moment and speaking of our learning and aspirations?
All: That sounds good.
Sang Paekchang: Brother Ch'ae, please speak of your scholarship.
Ch'ae Yong: As for me, "Sitting, I read; walking, I recite verse; year after year, I diligently search with great effort. My prose startles the world, unequalled; it is all because I cherished every moment in those days."
Sang Paekchang: Indeed, that must be so.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Brother Yi, speak of your scholarship.
Yi Kunok: As for me, "I do not entrust poverty or success to fate, but always bind diligence to Heaven. When human effort is exhausted, Heaven's principle appears; how could high talent be confined to forests and springs?"
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Those words are correct.
Ch'ae Yong: Brother Nak, please speak of your scholarship.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: As for me, "I strive hard in reading, always studying by the light of the firefly window. Constantly thinking that youth does not come twice, I realized anew how precious the daylight is. Thus, I have thoroughly read the Classic of Filial Piety and the Book of Rites, broadly studied the Book of Songs, the Book of Documents, and the Book of Changes, and investigated the main points of every chapter of the Spring and Autumn Annals and the Hundred Schools of Thought." However, the other day when I went to school, the teacher said he was worried.
Yi Kunok: Why did the teacher say he was worried?
Nak Tŭkhŭi: He said, "Heh heh, this scholar doesn't recognize a single character by sight."
Yi Kunok: Elder brother, you must have been dreaming!
Ch'ae Yong: Brother Sang, what is your scholarship like?
Sang Paekchang: As for me, "I do not speak carelessly, and there is great method in my calligraphy. First, I thoroughly grind fine ink until it's very dark, then dip a pure goat-hair brush into it. Opening the clean desk and bright window, I spread out brocade paper and embroidered stationery. Whether it's standard, cursive, seal, or clerical script, whatever I write becomes a model copybook. Large characters are as thick as pillars, small characters as fine as hairs. Even Wang Xizhi would bow to me and take me as his master, and Ouyang Xun would be astonished to see me." However, just now, trying to write the character for 'person' (人), I completely forgot the downstroke and the left-falling stroke.<85>
Yi Kunok: Well, there's the saying, "One stroke makes dragons and snakes run."
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Enough idle talk; the sun is setting, let's go a little further.

[Ch'ae Yong sings]
My heart is dry, my body withers. Day after day on this 1,000-li road, I think of my parents. The green plums have already grown large as beans, but news from Chang'an is hard to send. While white hair increases on my parents' heads back home, the traveler's path is shadowed by a solitary cloud.

[All sing together]
The taste of the road, the traveler's body—how can it compare to the smoky gate of a flowing stream? Don't turn your head back even once; the spirit is already lost, and the few cries of birds are unbearable to hear.

[Yi Kunok sings]
The scenery is truly that of late spring. Though the traveler's hardship is severe, how can I worry? Falling flowers under green shade scatter fragrant dust, showering the traveler's clothes. Aiming for nobility at the Cloud Ladder and Moon Palace, do not despise poverty while lodging by water and dining in the wind.

[All sing together]
The carp leaping in warm waters passes the Dragon Gate with one clap of thunder. On the day this body returns in glory, the green sash will shine brightly; let not wife and sister-in-law laugh at Su Qin.

[Nak Tŭkhŭi sings]
Whose house is that by the water, with willows by the bridge? A red gate is faintly visible between them, and a beautiful woman flashes past on a swing outside the wall. What should I do? Oh, save this man's life! The heartless laughing voice gradually fades, tormenting the lovesick man outside the wall.

[All sing together]
Worrying about home and travel expenses—is it like paying respects at the gates of the powerful ten times? As we proceed on our journey and achieve success, we shall enter the great palace and listen to imperial decrees near the Phoenix Pool and Tortoise Enclosure.

[Sang Paekchang sings]
Where the distant haze shimmers, the Loyang palace must surely be near. Weary from the long road, my body is truly tired. A cup of good wine would be welcome, but do not stop the thin horse under the drooping willows; the crows are gradually settling in the old trees at dusk.

[All sing together]
Twilight deepens, dusk falls, and a single lingering horn blast sounds from the woodcutter's gate. Let's find lodging quickly; the lights of the village ahead signal dusk.

[All sing again together]
Let's quickly find lodging, unsaddle the horses, buy wine, and discuss literature together. Truly, tonight, "Rain pelts the pear blossoms behind deeply closed doors."

[Exit Poem]
(Ch'ae Yong) "The scenery of rivers and mountains naturally evokes sorrow,"
(Nak Tŭkhŭi) "North, south, east, west—all for profit and fame."
(Sang Paekchang) "On the road, there are flowers and also wine,"
(Yi Kunok) "Making one stage of the journey into two."

Act 8: Selecting Scholars at the Literary Examination
Characters

Clerk (of the Ministry of Rites)
Examiner
Ch'ae Yong
Nak Tŭkhŭi<86>

Clerk: (Enters) "The new examination list from the Ministry of Rites stirs Chang'an; on all nine avenues, people rush on horseback to see. In one day, fame spreads throughout the land; the whole city's peaches and plums belong to the Spring Official." I am none other than a clerk of the Ministry of Rites (Yejo). This year is an examination year. The court has appointed an Examiner who is already at the examination hall, and candidates with eligibility from every province have gathered. The Examiner will be coming out now, so I am waiting here. This is truly a case of "As soon as one edict seeking worthies is issued, no talent in the four seas remains abandoned." As I speak, the Examiner is already arriving.
(Examiner enters)

[Examiner sings]
Receiving grace, I am appointed Examiner; my reputation carries the weight of hills and mountains.
Clerk, regarding those candidates,
Just ask if they have literary talent; why restrict by place of origin?

Clerk: What will you do with those who have literary talent?
[Examiner sings]
Select them for the highest ranks, to become pure and important officials.

Clerk: What will you do with those who lack literary talent?
[Examiner sings]
Even if they have the influence of fathers and elder brothers, they will be sent back empty-handed.

Clerk: Truly fair indeed, Your Honor.
Examiner: This is an examination year. I intend to recommend worthy talents for the country. Call in all the scholars who have come from every province, prefecture, and county to take the exam.
Clerk: Yes, sir.
(Ch'ae Yong and Nak Tŭkhŭi enter)

[Ch'ae Yong sings]
The Sophora flowers are perfectly yellow; scholars going to the examination hall are busy indeed. Friends from the National Academy have set out with their traveling packs.

[Ch'ae Yong and Nak Tŭkhŭi sing together]
Five hundred heroes are all here; who knows who will become the top scorer (Changwŏn)?

[Nak Tŭkhŭi sings]
I only vaguely know a few lines of the Thousand Character Classic; talent and learning are utterly absent, but I'll blindly push ahead and try my luck.

[All sing together]
Same as above.
(The Clerk shouts for the gate to be opened.)

Ch'ae Yong: The examination hall gate has opened! Everyone, let's enter in order.
Examiner: Clerks, give each scholar who has come to take the exam a roll of hemp paper and a candle. Have them separate into the east and west wings and wait for the examination topic to be announced.
Clerk: Yes, sir.
(They look at each other)

Examiner: Listen, all you scholars. The timing of the court's examination is fixed, but the topic is set freely by the Examiner. I am an Examiner of refined tastes, different from previous ones. In previous years, the exam topics were, first, essay; second, discourse; and third, policy proposal. But this year, the first round will be couplets, the second round riddles, and the third round songs. If there is someone who matches the couplets well, solves the riddles well, and sings the songs well, that person will be awarded the rank of Changwŏn, wear the Imperially bestowed flower, drink the Imperial wine, and parade through the streets. If there is anyone who matches the couplets poorly, fails to solve the riddles, and sings poorly, his face will be painted black with ink, and he will be beaten with clubs and thrown out.
Ch'ae Yong, Nak Tŭkhŭi: Yes, sir.<87>
Examiner: Ch'ae Yong from the East Corridor, come and receive the topic.
Ch'ae Yong: Yes.
Examiner: I will give a line about astronomy; you complete the couplet.
Ch'ae Yong: Yes, please state it.
Examiner: Stars fly, heaven releases pellets;
Ch'ae Yong: The sun rises, the sea throws a ball.
Examiner: Ah, well done! Well done! Now, go stand over there. Scholar Nak Tŭkhŭi from the West Corridor, come and receive the topic.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Please state it quickly.
Examiner: The Book of Songs has three hundred poems;
Nak Tŭkhŭi: There are also eleven sections.
Examiner: Not right! Not right! Go stand over there for a moment. Ch'ae Yong, come forth. I will now state a riddle based on the names of the eight provinces of the realm. Solve it.
Ch'ae Yong: Please speak.
Examiner: "A single clap of thunder shakes the heavens; the two shoulders cannot be idle. Go buy paper to make backing; debts owed to others have not yet been repaid."
Ch'ae Yong: The first line is Kyŏngdong, Kyŏngsŏ (When it thunders, the West [西] is startled [驚] and the East [東] is startled. Startled [驚] sounds like Kyŏng [京]). The second line is Kangdong, Kangsŏ (Meaning carried on two shoulders, i.e., the character 扛 [to carry on shoulders]. 扛 sounds like Kang [江]). The third line is Hodong, Hosŏ (Meaning paper is pasted; the character for paste is 糊. 糊 sounds like Ho [湖]). The fourth line is Chŏldong, Chŏlsŏ (When debts are not paid, one must sell property at auction [折賣] to pay them. 折 [Chŏl - break/sell] and 浙 [Chŏl] sound similar).
Examiner: Correct! Correct! Go wait over there for a moment. Nak Tŭkhŭi, come here. I will now state a riddle about four kinds of trees found in the mountains. Solve it.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Please state it quickly.
Examiner: "Adorned in the rain, yellow when seen from afar (Sophora); standing alone deep in the mountains, exceptionally tall (Willow). Timber fit for palace halls should be selected (Cypress); every household weaves beautiful silk garments (Maple)."
Nak Tŭkhŭi: The first line is cypress (柏), the second line is Sophora (槐), the third line is maple (楓), the fourth line is willow (柳).
Examiner: Wrong! Wrong! Go stand over there for a moment. Ch'ae Yong, come here. I will sing one verse of a song; you continue with the next verse.
Ch'ae Yong: Yes, please sing.
[Examiner sings]
Wealth and honor in Chang'an are truly rare. Delicacies include mountain game, bear paws, camel humps; the aroma spreads pleasingly in all four directions.
Examiner: Now, continue below this.
[Ch'ae Yong sings]
I shall offer them to the Examiner to be served as side dishes.
Examiner: (Laughing) Ah, talented! All three rounds were well done. You are truly a scholar. Go wait over there for a moment. Nak Tŭkhŭi, come here. I will sing another verse; you continue below it.
Nak Tŭkhŭi: Please sing quickly.
[Examiner sings]
What is in your belly? All that's inside is just dregs. If I let it out, everyone will avoid it.
Examiner: Continue quickly!
[Nak Tŭkhŭi sings]
I shall offer them to the Examiner to be served as side dishes.
Examiner: No! No! Paint this fellow's face with ink and beat him<88> with clubs and throw him out!
Nak Tŭkhŭi: No need to hit me! This is truly "Ill-fated Liu Sheng ultimately fails the exam; thick-skinned Jizi just returns home."
Examiner: Scholar Ch'ae, many participated in this examination, but none possess talent and learning as outstanding or prose as mature as yours. I shall immediately report to His Majesty and grant you the first place, Changwŏn. Put on the official robes and cap, parade through the streets, and attend the Imperial Banquet. Clerk, bring the robes and cap.
Clerk: (Bringing the robes and cap) "The robe is bestowed upon the presented scholar; the battle-axe is granted to the general."
Examiner: Changwŏn Ch'ae, put on the robes and cap, follow me to the court, and express your gratitude for this grace.
(Changes into the robes and cap.)

[Ch'ae Yong sings]
Joyfully seeing the time of supreme royal favor, today reveals a man's ambition. Taking off the hemp robe, I exchange it for silk; around my waist, a golden belt is tied horizontally. A fine horse with embroidered saddle is truly a fine sight.

[Examiner sings]
Your reading of ten thousand scrolls was truly no easy task. Joyfully passing the exam and selected for the top rank, fame and achievement are destined; how could the timing be wrong? Unrivaled in the three thousand rites and music, all five hundred heroes yield to him.

[Clerk sings]
A person's life should bring glory to their family gate, conferring titles on sons and wives, honoring oneself. Announcing the Changwŏn's return before his horse, he inscribes his name with a brush on the Wild Goose Pagoda. In one go, his name resonates throughout the land.

[Exit Poem]
(Examiner) "In one leap, he alone occupies the head of the giant turtle,"
(Ch'ae Yong) "Who knew it would be like a clap of thunder on level ground?"
(Clerk) "Tomorrow morning, riding his horse in the spring breeze,"
(All) "All return triumphant from the Yellow Capital."

Act 9: Lamenting While Applying Makeup
Characters

Cho Onyang

[Cho Onyang enters, singing]
The green silk curtains have faded in color, and the incense fire flickers low in the golden censer. Clouds rest over the pavilions of Chu, the moon is cold over the towers of Qin, evoking the sorrow of the departed one. Gazing toward the horizon, clouds and mountains are far, far away. His parents back home have snow upon their heads, yet how is it there is no news at all?

[Old Style Poem] "A bright mirror in its box, reflecting the lovely morning makeup. Recalling the time I served my husband, I rose at cockcrow from his bed, faced the mirror to arrange my pins and hair, and followed him to inquire after his parents. But once separated afar, the mirror box hides its blue light. Flowing dust darkens the patterned lattice, green moss grows in the inner chamber. Fallen are the gold hairpins and ornaments, faded are the silk clothes. Pitiful is this haggard form, no longer fragrant with orchid and iris. My heart is pierced with sorrow, the road is blocked and long. How can I, your wife, lament this? My worry is for his parents. Thinking of those distant apes and monkeys, cherishing the light of this mulberry and elm [metaphor for twilight/old age]. I wish to fulfill my wifely duties, but cannot forget the wandering son. Do not pluck the green silk zither, the broken string makes one grieve; do not listen to the 'White Haired Lament,' its sad tune breaks one's heart. Human affairs are full of misfortune, shaming those paired mandarin ducks." I married Ch'ae Paekke, and barely two months passed. At first, we planned to serve his parents together throughout their lives, but due to his father's strict command, my husband left to take the civil service examination, and there has been no news at all. Now, I serve his parents alone with all my heart. If I may say so, the first reason is to help my husband establish himself, and the second is to uphold the duties of a daughter-in-law.<89> "Though the ends of the earth and corners of the sea may have limits, only this feeling has no end."

[Sings]
Just after our marriage, he was called to the examination. As the Yangguan farewell song ended, I saw him off at Nanpu; how has such a long time passed in separation? Tears stain my silk lapel. Tears indeed stain it. The treasured zither lies uselessly covered in dust; the silk brocade quilt is painful to unfold. The jade window is desolate, the vermilion door bleak; time flows by meaninglessly. Quietly thinking, your affection and my heart were like morning dew in a single day. You are on a ten-thousand-li journey; I am in sorrow. If you should think of me, even though it is far, please turn back and look this way.

[Sings again]
My rosy, beautiful face is not as before; my cloud-like fine hair is truly sad to comb. The one who painted my eyebrows is far away; has the powder-applying groom gone? The phoenix in the mirror is ashamed to dance. I await his return, I await his return, but fish and geese [messengers] are distant, and the phoenix feels ever more lonely. Has the fragrant grass grown again, green on the riverbank? Uselessly, only thoughts of bygone days arise. Alas! You draw nearer to the capital day by day, but I can only meaninglessly gaze towards Chang'an under the fragrant grass and setting sun. You will not be a prodigal son, nor I a wanton woman. To whom can I speak of the ten thousand sorrows in my heart?

[Sings again]
Moving my lotus steps, I go to my parents-in-law. If their food is insufficient, I will bring more. If their clothes are torn, I will mend them. If they wish to walk, I will support them. Thus, day by day, I inquire and serve them, but the sun sets behind the western mountains; what is to be done? The sun sets behind the western mountains; what is to be done? Pitiful, pitiful, my situation is pitiful! Whom can I ask to convey these circumstances to my husband? By the time you rise among the blue clouds [achieve high office], perhaps your parents will be in the Yellow Springs [underworld]. Did I not give you countless instructions when we parted? Did I not give such instructions again and again?
Would you, perchance, covet another's wealth in the ten-li red mansions [pleasure quarters]? Even if you should forget me, please, please think of your parents. Alas, to whom can I lament this sorrow? Why are you so heartless towards me?

[Sings again]
Scholars taking the examination are all brilliant talents. They give no thought to the luan or phoenix [talented individuals] falling, each striving to ascend the Dragon and Tiger List [examination ranking]. Even if you abandon me, I will not resent it. Even if you abandon me, I will not resent it.
Having accepted the trust of offering humble sacrifices, how could I refuse? Rather, I will become a filial daughter-in-law and virtuous wife, a filial daughter-in-law and virtuous wife, so that my name may be passed down eternally in the blue histories [annals]. Then, even this suffering today will not have been in vain. During this time, I will endure alone, not sullying my husband's name, only acting for my husband's sake. Engrave this in your heart, engrave this in your heart: if perchance you come to wear a golden belt and purple robes, do not forget me in my hemp skirt and wooden hairpin. Ah, ah, ah, this piled-up sorrow—even the writings of Song Yu would find it hard to depict.<90>

[Exit Poem]
"Turning my head, the sun has already set over the high hall,
Why does the traveler linger at the edge of the sky?
Rosy-cheeked beauties often have ill fates,
Do not worry, spring wind, but sigh for oneself."

Act 10: Spring Banquet in the Apricot Garden
Characters
Chief Official
Clerk (令史 - Yŏngsa)
Post Station Master (驛丞 - Yŏkch'ŭng)
Ch'ae Yong
Candidate A
Candidate B
Banqueting Official
Attendant (丞直 - Sŭngjik)

Chief Official: (Enters with subordinates) "In the morning, a village lad; in the evening, ascending the Son of Heaven's hall. Generals and ministers are not born to their station; a man must strengthen himself." I am none other than a Chief Official of the Hanam-bu (Henan Department). Previously, when the Changwŏn attended the Imperial Banquet and paraded through the streets, the preparations for the horse he would ride and the arrangement of the banquet seats were all handled by the Prefectural Governor-Adjutant. But this year, Ch'ae Paekke has become the Changwŏn, and following precedent, he will attend the banquet. The Prefectural Governor entrusted this matter to me. Yesterday, I already gave instructions to the Clerk (Yŏngsa) in charge of horses at the T'aeboksi (Court of the Imperial Stud) and the Post Station Master (Yŏkch'ŭng) in charge of arrangements in Loyang County. If I beat the drum three times here, they should all gather. (Beats the drum) The one in charge of horses, come forth!

Clerk (Yŏngsa): (Enters) "If asked, I answer; if not asked, I don't reply." Why did you call?
Chief Official: Are the horses all prepared?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. Let me report. There are ten thousand fine horses here.
Chief Official: How do you know they are fine horses?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. Their ears are like overlapped pairs of bamboo slips, their manes are multicolored. With the chest of a phoenix, the mane of a dragon, the head of a leopard, and the forehead of a tiger held high, their thudding hooves seem carved from jade. The dripping red sweat flows like pearls. Their clear blue, angular eyes seem like mirrors set in place. Their tall, sturdy bodies are as strong as iron. When they leap once, their tails brush the Milky Way, traversing Xuanpu and Kongtong horizontally. In less than an instant, they circle the Divine Land (China), swift as shooting stars or flashing lightning. Even if Jiufang Gao saw them and named a price of 1,000 gold pieces, it would not be excessive.
Chief Official: What colors are available?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): They are all no less fine than the famous horses of old: white, red, black, blue, piebald, dapple-gray, Bujeoldamal, silver-dapple, Byeolbaegi, Rokimal, Hangburu, Damgaramal – horses of every color are available. Truly, "Five-colored patterns scatter like clouds covering their bodies; only after ten thousand li does one see the bloody sweat flow."
Chief Official: What are the names of the horses?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. Feilong (Flying Dragon), Xianglin (Soaring Qilin), Chitu (Red Hare), Ziyan (Purple Swallow), Zhuifeng (Wind Chaser), Juebo (Wave Breaker), Fuyun (Floating Cloud), Fadian (Emitting Lightning), Wuzhui, Yucong, Ninglu (Congealing Dew), Tengshuang (Soaring Frost), Linju (Qilin Foal), Longzi (Dragon Son), Zhaoying (Shadow Reflector), Xuanguang (Hanging Light), and also Hongchipō, Zichipō, Feixiaopiao, Yuxiaoyao, Quanmaogua, Shizihua, etc. Hualiu and Luer cannot all be mentioned; Susu and Qiji cannot all be counted. Truly, "They come from Qinghai and Yuezhi; they were brought from Dayuan and Yuedan."
Chief Official: Are there good stables?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. Feilong (Flying Dragon), Xianglin (Auspicious Qilin), Jiliang (Good and Fine), Longmei (Dragon Steed), Taotu, Tiju, Yuanluan (Phoenix Pair), Chuqun (Outstanding Group), Tianhua<91> (Heavenly Flower), Fengyuan (Phoenix Garden), Benxing (Rushing Star), Neiju (Inner Foal), Zuofei (Left Flying), Youfei (Right Flying), Zuofang (Left Stable), Youfang (Right Stable), Dongnannei (Southeast Inner), Xinannei (Southwest Inner) – such fine plaques hang on each stable. Indeed, "All bear the imprint of the three flowers and flying phoenix character; within are stored ten thousand fine dragon steeds."
Chief Official: How is the horse tack?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. The silk saddle flaps brilliantly shimmer like clouds, the silver stirrups glitter brightly in the sun, fragrant silk covers the golden saddle. Red reins hold the jade bit, the bridle is adorned with agate, and the whip handle is made of coral. Truly, "Red tassels, purple reins, a coral whip; jade saddle, golden cage, a golden headstall."
Chief Official: Among those, how many have you selected here?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, reporting sir, but currently there are none.
Chief Official: Currently none?
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. Initially, there were ten thousand horses, but among them, 1,300 have hoof ailments, 2,700 have facial sores, 3,800 have developed lumps, and another 2,700 have eye diseases. Furthermore, the saddle frames are broken, the saddle pads are askew, the reins are nothing but straw ropes, and the whip handles are just punishment rods. Not a single piece of horse tack is intact; they are all unusable.
Chief Official: What kind of talk is that? If you haven't prepared everything, I will report to the Prefectural Governor and won't let you off easy.
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, please spare me! How can this humble person possibly make things right?
Chief Official: Once the horses are prepared, wait outside the Meridian Gate and mount the Changwŏn when he emerges from the palace after expressing his gratitude.
Clerk (Yŏngsa): Yes, sir. "Swift is the horse's hoof in the triumphant spring breeze; in one day, let him see all the flowers of Chang'an."
(Clerk (Yŏngsa) exits)

Chief Official: Where is the person in charge of arrangements?
Post Station Master (Yŏkch'ŭng): (Enters) "One call from the hall above; a hundred responses from below the steps." Did you call?
Chief Official: Are the banquet preparations complete?
Post Station Master (Yŏkch'ŭng): Yes, reporting sir. It has been arranged most sumptuously, awaiting your inspection.
Chief Official: Most sumptuously?
Post Station Master (Yŏkch'ŭng): Yes, sir. Let me describe: The pearl curtains are hung high, the embroidered screens are drawn low, coral mats are laid out appropriately, tortoiseshell mats are arranged skillfully. Agate burns fragrantly in golden censers, rare flowers are beautifully arranged in jade vases. Painted screens line all four sides, the left side is fully covered with silk cushions. **Summary**

This text presents Acts 7 through 10 of a Korean adaptation of the classic Chinese play "Pipa Ji" (琵琶記), titled "P'ip'agi". Act 7 introduces scholar Ch'ae Yong and his friends journeying to the capital for the civil service exams. Act 8 depicts the humorous examination process where Ch'ae Yong excels and becomes the top scorer (Changwŏn), while his friend Nak Tŭkhŭi fails comically. Act 9 shifts to Ch'ae Yong's wife, Cho Onyang, lamenting his long absence and expressing devotion to his parents. Act 10 shows the celebratory procession and banquet for the successful candidates, including another humorous incident with a candidate falling off his horse, concluding with Ch'ae Yong feeling melancholic about his distant family despite his success.

From a colonial history perspective, this adaptation published in 1927 is interesting. It showcases the continued cultural relevance of Chinese classics in Korea but translated into contemporary (1920s) Korean, including older orthography. The themes of ambition through state examinations versus filial piety and family obligations might have resonated differently under Japanese rule, where pathways to success were altered and often involved navigating the colonial administration. The humorous portrayal of less competent scholars could also subtly reflect contemporary social commentary or anxieties about merit and success.
