**幷書不可論, 한글토론** (Argument Against Byeongseo, Hangeul Debate)
						**安自山** (An Chasan)

(1) Gentlemen, your proposal to write the tense sounds (   ) using doubled identical letters (幷書), namely (ㄲ ㄸ ㅃ ㅉ), is based on two arguments: 1. Restoring the ancient form (古形), and 2. Adhering to phonetic principles (音理). However, I present three points demonstrating why this is unacceptable.

(2) Firstly, the use of Byeongseo (doubled consonants) at the time of the creation of Eonmun (諺文) was fundamentally flawed. Although there are three reasons for the origin of 'ㅅ', now is not the time to explain them all. Whatever the case, the old method is the old method, and the current method is the current method, is it not? There is no need to obstinately preserve the old ways with Confucian thinking (儒敎思想). This is the first point to consider. Secondly, at the time of creation, it was an agreement among a small number of people, but once it spread and became public property (公有物), it was publicly established (公定) by the majority of the public (公衆) using 'ㅅ'. Therefore, Byeongseo is primitive, while using 'ㅅ' is evolutionary. Thirdly, even if something is publicly recognized and established (公認公定), if it is irregular, there is a need to organize (整理) it. However, since a consistent system (範疇) using 'ㅅ' has already been achieved, what need is there to revise it again or revert to the old ways (復古)?

(3) Speaking in terms of phonetic principles (音理), it is not necessarily true that only the Byeongseo form (ㄲ) is acceptable. Why? Because when the 'ㅅ' sound is attached to any consonant, it becomes a tense sound: *satgat*... ㅅㄱ, *motttuk*... ㅅㄷ, *pitppal*... ㅅㅂ, *utjip*... ㅅㅈ.

(5) If you think about it, just because a drum (鼓) is made of leather (革製), it doesn't mean it can only be intensely struck <42> with leather to produce a sharp sound (激發). Whether it's iron (銕), wood (木), or even stone (石), striking it intensely with anything will produce a sharp sound. This is a phonetic principle (音理上 原則). Likewise, for the five sounds ㄱ ㄷ ㅂ ㅅ ㅈ, attaching any sound strongly transforms them into tense sounds: *sonkarak*... combination of ㄴㄱ, *samdae*... combination of ㅁㄷ, *mukppat*... combination of ㄱㅂ, *chipsin*... combination of ㅂㅅ, *yŏljip*... combination of ㄹㅈ.

(6) Just because anything can become tense when strongly attached doesn't mean we should write them combined in any way. Therefore, the public has already organized this and unified it with 'ㅅ'. So why this insistence that only Byeongseo aligns with phonetic principles?

(7) Except for reed organs (風琴) or certain musical instruments (樂器), it is impossible to simultaneously produce two identical sounds. This is a fundamental phonetic principle. Therefore, Byeongseo is phonetically highly unacceptable.

(9) I believe you gentlemen (諸氏) hold this misunderstanding because you think of the combination of ㅅ and ㄱ like 'th' or 'ph' in English. Originally, the tense nature (激發性) of the five sounds is distinctly different from the 3-character roots of Semitic languages or the 2-character roots of Arabic. Do you know this or not?

(9) The five tense sounds (5激音) are sounds without value in terms of complete structural composition (構成). That is, they are not formed by an independent physiological process of pronunciation but are a way of adjusting (¸®調 - *kinjo*, firmly adjusting) a sound without aspiration (非氣帶). Therefore, when slightly emphasizing the meaning of a word, they are merely sounds that cause a forced tone (强迫調) to produce tenseness. *koch'angi*... *kkot'ch'angi*, *palka*... *ppalka*, *torang*... *ttorang*, *chulgi*... *tchulgi*.

(10) Since it is already a sound made by adjustment (調), representing it with some kind of symbol (符號) is sufficient. Therefore, 'ㅅ' is a marker (表) for tense sounds, not a phonetic transcription (寫音的 表記). Thus, using 'ㅅ' is unifying, while Byeongseo can only be called scattered (散亂的).

(11) Writing <43> is not just about transcribing sound (寫音); one must also consider beauty (美) to prevent the power of familiarity (親線의 勢力) from becoming tiresome (倦怠). Therefore, when writing identical characters consecutively, there are cases where they are represented by a colon-like mark (:) for ease of writing and beauty.

(12) Writing with Byeongseo looks unpleasant and crude (엉성하고), is inconvenient to write, and evokes no aesthetic sense (美的 觀念). However, writing as    ㅆ  evokes a sense of attention to rules (注意法則) and balance (均齊), and visually conveys power (勢力), making its artistic value (藝術的 價値) vastly superior to Byeongseo.

(13) Let's interpret it differently. The Byeongseo of ancient times referred to the voiced sounds (濁音) of Chinese characters. The Korean language originally has no voiced sounds but has tense sounds (激音). It is a huge mistake (大誤) to foolishly apply the voiced sound method (濁音法) to tense sounds. Therefore, even from the *Yongbiŏch'ŏn'ga* onwards, Byeongseo was rarely used. Later generations realized this and did not use the voiced Byeongseo; instead, they consistently stipulated the use of 'ㅅ' for tense sounds.

**文典의 整理** (Organization of Grammar Books)

(1) Currently, discussions regarding the study of the Korean language (朝鮮語 硏究問題) are tumultuous (擾擾), but organizing the grammatical case system (語格法) in grammar (文法) is needed first. Only when this is organized can dictionaries (辭典) be compiled, orthography (表記法) be unified, and teaching (敎授) be corrected, right?

(2) There have been over 10 types of grammar books (文典) published so far. Listing them in order of publication gives the following (their contents are each scattered and disorganized):
Yu Kil-chun (兪吉濬) - Tae Chosŏn Munjŏn (大朝鮮文典), Taehan Munjŏn (大韓文典)
Ch'oe Kwang-ok (崔光玉) - Taehan Munjŏn <44>
Kim Hŭi-sang (金熙詳) - Ch'odŭng Kugŏ Ŏjŏn (初等國語語典), Chosŏn Ŏjŏn (朝鮮語典)
Chu Si-gyŏng (周時經) - Kugŏ Munjŏn Ŭmhalk (國語文典音學), Kugŏ Munpŏp (國語文法), Mal-ŭi Sori (말의 소리)
Kim Tu-bong (金枓奉) - Chosŏn Malbon (조선말본)
Yi Kyu-yŏng (李奎榮) - Hyŏn'gŭm Chosŏn Munjŏn (現今朝鮮文典)
Kwŏn Tŏk-kyu (權悳奎) - Chosŏn Ŏmun Kyŏngwi (朝鮮語文經緯)
Yi Kyu-bang (李奎昉) - Chosŏn Ŏpŏp (朝鮮語法)
Yi P'il-su (李弼秀) - Chosŏn Chŏngŭm Munjŏn (朝鮮正音文典)
Besides these, there seem to be 4 or 5 mimeographed versions.

(3) Regarding teaching children, first Mr. Ch'oe Kwang-ok toured various regions giving instruction, and much later Mr. Chu Si-gyŏng gave some kind of lecture (講說). Mr. Yu Kil-chun's grammar book was published in Tokyo 40 years ago from now, so the primary contributors (首功) to Korean grammar study are Mr. Yu and Mr. Ch'oe. Mr. Chu is their successor (後進). Why Mr. Chu is being promoted (推戴), I don't know if this is also the illusion created by schemers (手段家), but I hope the public is not deceived.

(4) All those various grammar books are each different and lack unity (統一). For starters, even word classification (單語 分類) is chaotically inconsistent, sometimes 7, sometimes 8, sometimes 9, sometimes 6 categories. Teaching in various schools is also like that. Grammar books are not something to be authored carelessly. First, studying Paul's principles of grammar (바울씨의 文法原理) would be advisable.

(5) In grammar, the standard language (標準語) should be decided first. However, even within the standard language, there must be a standard. Using vulgar (卑言) or colloquial (俗言) words like (다귀 - *ppyŏdagwi*) or (늑다리 - *nŭktari*), as exemplified in (*Chosŏn Malbon*), is unacceptable.

(6) Mr. Kwŏn Tŏk-kyu's statement in *Ŏmun Kyŏngwi* that (구실 - *kusil*) should be called (구슬 - *kusŭl*) to become standard is also something to think about. <45> If everyone says (구실 - *kusil*), then (구실 - *kusil*) becomes the standard. It is unacceptable to strictly rely on ancient words (古語) or one's own ideals.

(7) Originally, the logic of grammar lies in the axioms (公理) of abstract facts of language usage (語法). For example, look at the rule for causative verbs (使動詞). *an-gi-da*, *kal-gŏ-da* (likely *karida* - replace? or a typo? Translating contextually as related to causatives/passives), *chŏp-hi-da*, *mŏg-i-da*, *nŏm-gi-da*, *tŭl-li-da* (들이다/들리다), *chap-hi-da*, *chug-i-da*, *ut-ki-da*, *nal-li-da* (날이다/날리다), *nup-hi-da*, *ssŏg-i-da*. The rule is that for words ending in ㄴ, ㅁ, ㅅ stems (바팀語), (기) is added; for ㄹ stems, (리) (original says 니) is added; for ㅂ stems, (히) is added; for ㄱ stems, (이) is added. It is unacceptable to try to unify all rules with only (이) or (기) without knowing the axiom (公理). The rule is to follow analogy (類推) based on how the stem (語幹) changes. Things like trying to unify (되여 - *toeyŏ*) into (어 - *ŏ*) are not permitted by the axiom.

(8) Look at the structure (조직) of proximate vowel change (近母音變化) in pronouns (大名詞). 이 (*i*) - 여긔 (*yŏgŭi*)... the sound 이 (*i*) changes to (여 - *yŏ*). 그 (*kŭ*) - 거기 (*kŏgi*)... the sound 으 (*ŭ*) changes to (어 - *ŏ*). It's not that (이어 - *iŏ*) becomes (여 - *yŏ*), nor that (그어 - *kŭŏ*) becomes (거 - *kŏ*). The proximate vowel changes directly. Therefore, the (여 - *yŏ*) in (먹혀 - *mŏkhyŏ*) is also not a combination of (이어 - *iŏ*). (이 - *i*) directly changes to (여 - *yŏ*). Thus, verb ending changes (語尾變化) have a significant relationship with pronouns. Do you know this or not? The scope of logic must be standardized based on such abstract phenomena.

(9) Waking up early in the dim dawn (컴컴한 새벽), you washed your face (洗面) with five-colored water (五色水). But upon seeing each other in daylight (日光), everyone looked like a painted face (廣大 - *kwangdae*, clown/mask). You must wash your face again while looking in the mirror (面鏡).

(10) The grammar books currently available are all different, crude (艾 - *ssuk*), un-unified, and without a single redeemable one (無一可取). Therefore, we must compare them anew against science (科學) and carry out a complete reorganization (一新整頓). They each establish their own school (各立門戶) and claim only theirs is correct, but when viewed against scholarship (學術), they are all varied (色色). I ask you, gentlemen, or towards me (향하야), please let me hear the logic with which you reject others. Don't just reject blindly or with insults (辱說).

**表記法整理의 整理** (Organizing the Organization of Orthography)

(1) Recently, facing the modern era, there's a common trend of having the blind view (盲目的 主見) that because old customs (舊習) must be improved (改良), everything must be reformed (改新). There are even cases where, after seeing some unofficial history (野史), the original title (本號) of our national founder (民祖) is privately changed (私改) from 檀 (Tan) to 壇 (Tan) and taught to students. Such reckless actions (妄發) ultimately cast doubt on the sacred name (聖號) and simultaneously raise suspicion about the truth (眞否) of the national founder's facts, leading to the destruction of the national spirit (民族的 精神) towards Tangun (檀君).

(2) In Korean language research (朝鮮語硏究), too, there are efforts seeking to create a desolate scene (殺風景). Regarding the organization of orthography (表記法整理), various opinions are raised, conversely confusing the actual grammar (實際文法). Ultimately, the fundamental spirit (根本精神) is lost, leading to rumors (風說) like writing horizontally (橫書), abolishing Eonmun (諺文廢止) and writing in Roman letters (羅馬字), or that unifying with Japanese (日本語) would be easier.

(3) There seem to be many teachers studying Korean now, but their mindset (精神) is strange. They clamor without considering <47> the potential impact (影響), the relationship with science (科學關係), the connection to the fundamental spirit (根本精神關係), or practical implementation (實行).

(4) Let's take the single issue of revising (改定) the name Eonmun (諺文) to 'Hangeul' (한글), as proposed by you gentlemen (諸氏). Your excuse (口實) is that Chinese characters (漢文) were honorifically called Chinseo (眞書, true script) while our script (本文) was disparagingly called Eonmun (諺文, vulgar script). However, this is a misunderstanding (誤解).

(5) Originally, Chinseo (眞書) was a term used by Zhao Mingcheng (趙明誠) of the Song dynasty to refer to Regular Script (楷書). It was a type of calligraphy style (書體). During the reign of King Sukjong (肅宗) of Koryŏ (高麗), there was even an examination subject called Chinseo-gwa (眞書科) in calligraphy assessment (書品). So, Chinseo is not an honorific term but a type of calligraphy (書法).

(6) Eonmun (諺文) is synonymous with Hunminjeongeum (訓民正音), meaning it is not the script of a special class (特殊階級) but has the meaning of 'common' (平常的) or 'phonetic script' (音字). Therefore, even the explanation of the character 諺 (Eon) in Sŏngho Saesŏl (星湖僿說) states it refers to "common talk of the villages, close to human feelings, passed down mouth to mouth" (閭巷之常談人情之切近而口口相傳). Thus, the term Eonmun did not originate as a derogatory term (卑稱) but meant 'common script' (平常文) or 'phonetic script' (音字), making Eonmun the special noun (特別名詞) for the Korean script (朝鮮文).

(7) Originally, there was a superstitious naming practice (迷信的 作名法) in Korean custom (朝鮮習慣). We should not pursue only form (形式) with such empty thoughts (浮虛한 思想). Why must we change a term (名詞) that everyone in society already knows? Even if there were instances of looking down upon it (賤視), that is an issue of treatment (待遇問題), not related to the name (名稱關係). We should now abandon vanity (虛榮心) and simply adopt practical methods (着實한 方法).

(8) We should prioritize universality (普遍的). Let's cease peculiar (好奇的) or biased (一偏的), awkward (쑥스런) actions.

(9) It is preferable to use the already known names for letters (字의 名稱), like (기역 - *kiyŏk*) and (디긋 - *tigŭt*). <48> There is no need to needlessly revise them and make awkward sounds like (기윽 - *kiŭk*) or (시읏 - *siŭt*). The name of a letter and its phonetic notation (記音) can be separate without any problem (拘碍). In the West too, (H) is called 'aitch', W 'double-u', and Y 'ipsilon' (original uses Greek name).

(10) Even if we try to organize simply now and plan for unified implementation (一致的 實行), success seems difficult. If we revise everything uselessly and burden the spirit (精神) of the social masses (社會民衆), implementation might easily be abandoned (暴棄) out of renewed annoyance (귀찬한 생각).

(11) Some argue that all initial consonant letters (初聲字) should be usable as final consonants (尾音 즉 바팀 - *batch'im*), and that grammar explanations (文法說明) must also be done this way. This is crude/wrong (쑥). I absolutely say that using all initial consonants as finals is unacceptable. If we do that, the Korean language (朝鮮語) will perish (滅亡). Regarding this, as it requires a somewhat lengthy explanation (張遑), I will not speak of it now. Also, if your proposals are not implemented, my protest (抗議) is also ineffective (無効). First, publicly disclose (公開) the reasons why your proposals are correct. Only after that will I present my view that "Using all initial consonant letters as finals will cause the Korean language to perish." Let public consensus (民衆의 公認) be sought from this point.

(13) In any case, because you gentlemen view things through the colored glasses (色眼鏡) of 'reform' (改新), you deem the entire current grammar (現行 文法) a misunderstanding (誤解). Your perspective, therefore, leans more towards emotion (情的) than intellect (智的). That is why some have newly coined pure Korean terms (純朝鮮語) like (임 - *im*) for nouns (名詞) and (움 - *um*) for verbs (動詞) for explanations, and also harbor the thought of expelling (逐却) all words derived from Chinese characters (漢文). Their sincerity (誠意) <49> might be called praiseworthy (갸륵하다), but implementation is utterly impossible (到底 不可).

(14) Because you see through emotional colored glasses, you drive your own fantasy (幻想) as the sole truth (惟一의 眞理). If you examine general grammar books or the explanations from Lesson 10 to Lesson 12 of Mr. Kwŏn Tŏk-kyu's *Ŏmun Kyŏngwi*, anything that contradicts your own views (自己主見) is dismissed as 'habitual sound' (習慣音). Pfft! That is the thinking of Confucianism (儒敎의 思想).

(15) You gentlemen separate phonetic principles (音理) and variant sounds (變化音), calling the latter so-called 'habitual sounds' (習慣소리), but it is not like that. The principles (原則 原理) of speech sounds (語音) include original sounds (元音) and variant sounds (變音). These variant sounds also occur according to the principles of phonetics (音聲學의 原理). Changes outside these principles are phenomena of dialect generation (方言 發生의 現像). Not knowing this, you establish certain sounds as fixed like the North Star (北辰) and dismiss everything else as 'habitual sounds'. This is either crude/wrong (艾), a misunderstanding (誤解), or else ignorance (孤陋濛昧), or perhaps emotional (情的).

(16) The rules (法則) by which sounds change (轉變) involve physiological causes (生理的 原因), physical causes (物理的 原因), correlational causes (相關的 原因), etc., with phonetic laws (音聲學上 法則) such as progressive assimilation (順行同化) and regressive assimilation (逆行同化). This is a phenomenon common throughout the world (世界).

(17) If you don't know the rules of sound change exist and dismiss everything as 'habitual sound,' you will not be able to find the principles (法理). Because you gentlemen do not refer to these rules, you consider it a misunderstanding and propose to revise everything. Therefore, you gentlemen should first study the (Vocal-pyramid). Also, if you do not study ancient East Asian phonology (東洋 古代 音韻學), it is impossible (不能). (The explanation that '' (*arae-a*) is a combination of ㅣ and ㅡ is truly inappropriate.)

(18) To conclude (結論), before we speak of organizing orthography (表記法 整理), let us first organize the muddy thoughts (흐린 생각) in our brains (腦膸). I hope you will pay deep attention (注意) and discuss this.) <50>

**Summary**

An Chasan argues against proposed Hangeul reforms in 1927. He defends the existing use of 'ㅅ' (siot) preceding consonants to indicate tensity (된소리) over the revival of doubled consonants (幷書, Byeongseo), citing evolutionary practice, phonetic reasoning, and aesthetics. He criticizes the proliferation of inconsistent grammar books, advocating for a unified standard based on linguistic principles rather than individual preferences or flawed interpretations. He also defends the traditional name 諺文 (Eonmun) against the proposed 'Hangeul', arguing 'Eonmun' means 'vernacular/phonetic script' not 'vulgar script', and opposes changing letter names or expanding the set of final consonants (받침). He urges reformers to adopt a more scientific, less emotionally driven approach grounded in phonetics and historical linguistics before attempting standardization.

From a historical perspective, this article vividly illustrates the complex and often contentious debates surrounding Korean language standardization during the Japanese colonial period. It reveals tensions between traditionalists and reformers, differing interpretations of linguistic history and phonetics, and the underlying nationalist desire to define and codify the Korean language, even while debating the means. An Chasan's conservative stance, emphasis on practicality, and criticism of both inconsistency and radicalism provide insight into the diverse intellectual currents shaping modern Korean linguistics.
