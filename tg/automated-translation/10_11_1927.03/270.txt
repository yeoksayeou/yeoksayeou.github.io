Masterpiece "Lydia"
						Kim Tong-in

Already 360 years ago. The stage is T City, which could be called the center of Southern European art at that time.

A month has passed since the death of the great painter Ventron, whose name shines brilliantly even after three centuries.
The elderly widow, who had enjoyed 50 years together with her husband before suddenly losing him, was incomparably lonely.
The sun shines brightly. The wind also blows gently. People come and go on the streets, full of hustle and bustle. However, all this seemed merely annoying and noisy to the widow. What are you all so happy about? Don't you know that a great life, one that you could boast about to the end of time as having been born here, suddenly disappeared a month ago? What are you so happy about?
Although she had endured it for three months, the widow decided to leave this noisy city where "the longer she stayed, the more desperately she missed her deceased husband," and live quietly in some tranquil countryside.
Thus, as part of her preparations to leave the city, she intended to sell off all of her late husband's remaining works, numbering some 20 or 30 pieces.
A few days later, all the art critics and art dealers (畵商) in T City received notice from Ventron's widow that she would be selling her late husband's cherished paintings and remaining works, and to come for a viewing on XX Day.
And all of the late Ventron's works, both finished and unfinished, which had decorated the various rooms of the house, were gathered into the deceased's studio.

On XX Day, the day set for the viewing, various nobles, "plutocrats who understand art," and art dealers came and went from the widow's house like a marketplace, starting from the morning.
The mistress, sitting in her room upstairs, let out a lonely sigh each time a servant announced a visitor, and merely said,
"Show them in."
She did not go down to see them herself.
However, a little after lunch, when visited by the Grand Duke R and the foremost art critic of the time, Mr. Y, the widow could not help but go down herself out of respect for these two famous men. <70> The mistress personally guided the two famous men and took them to the studio, which she herself had not yet looked into (due to strange fear, anxiety, and memories). However, the old mistress, well aware of her authority as the widow of a great painter of the time, led the two men with the most dignified and haughty demeanor.
But the studio was embarrassingly messy, to the point where using the word "chaotic" felt inadequate. None of the paintings were hung; they were stacked haphazardly and leaned against the wall.
"Oh my,"
The mistress let out a dignified exclamation.
The Duke and the critic walked quietly over to where the paintings were and began looking at them one by one, moving them aside. But after viewing a few, they placed one painting against the wall and stepped back three or four paces.
The mistress was startled to see it. How did such a painting get mixed in there? It was "Lydia," a painting by the late Ventron's most foolish disciple, a man named Miran, a portrait depicting a woman's strange smile.
"That is—"
Startled by the unexpected event, the mistress forgot her dignified manner and rushed forward to explain, but the critic Mr. Y waved his hand.
"Madam. I understand. You mean this is the painting the late Mr. Ventron cherished most secretly (秘藏), don't you? Duke! Look here. I have seen and criticized thousands of paintings until now, but I have never seen a painting like this. Duke, what do you make of the smile (微笑) of the woman in this painting? That enigmatic smile. Ah, truly Ventron was a painter without precedent or successor."
"Hmm."
The Duke also let out a meaningful exclamation.
The two men, who had stood silently before the painting for about half an hour, turned away, glancing back reluctantly as they left.
After seeing the two guests off, the mistress returned to her lonely room, but she could not calm her mind, troubled by that foolish work (愚作) "Lydia."
—How had her husband acted when his most foolish disciple, Miran, brought that painting to show him? —At that time, her husband had glared fiercely and reprimanded Miran—
"What on earth did you intend to paint with this?"
"I tried to capture a woman's alluring smile."
"Alluring? Fool! Where is such allure? After studying under me for 20 years, you can't even paint one alluring smile? Does that look like an alluring smile to you? You fool. That rather looks like a face crying from hunger. A disciple like you is useless, so from today, go find another master."
Miran was even excommunicated (破門) because of that painting and went away crying. Afterwards, Ventron, still simmering with anger, spoke to his wife like this—
"Truly, there's nothing harder to handle than a fool (愚人)! Other boys, after 4 or 5 years, even if not perfect, can produce at least one decent painting, but where is there such a fool who, after eating my food for over 20 years, paints a crying face <71> while intending to paint a smiling face?"
—How did that "Lydia," so scorned, get mixed in among her late husband's remaining works? Not only that, but how could there be such an embarrassing situation where the foolish work (愚作) of that fool (愚人) was perceived as her husband's work by the foremost critic of the time, Mr. Y? The mistress thought that if only she were a woman without refinement, she would even now run out, kill Mr. Y and Duke R who had seen the painting, and burn the painting itself.

However, the next day, something unexpected happened. A messenger from Grand Duke R came, gave the mistress five thousand gold coins, and took away that foolish work "Lydia"—a strange event indeed. The mistress did not understand what was happening.

For three centuries since then, that foolish work "Lydia," along with Ventron's name, became ever more famous and valuable, decorating the guest rooms of various plutocrats, nobles, or royal palaces. Then, 50 or 60 years ago, it was exchanged for the terrifying sum of 50,000 pounds and now hangs most preciously in the front of the Ventron room at the G Museum. And during that time, all the first-rate or minor (群小) artists and critics who stood before that painting all utter the same exclamations and praises towards Miran's foolish work "Lydia," and as they turn away, they all think this—
"It's a masterpiece. Truly a masterpiece. What on earth does that smile mean? Mockery (嘲笑)? Joy? Laughter? An alluring smile (妖笑)? It's truly an enigma. A smile that no one but Ventron could possibly have painted. Ah, why wasn't I born with Ventron's talent?" (The End) <72>

**Summary**

The story recounts how a painting titled "Lydia," created by Ventron, a famous painter's untalented disciple named Miran, is mistaken for a masterpiece by the master himself after his death. Ventron had actually detested the painting and expelled Miran for it, deeming the intended "alluring smile" a failure resembling a crying face. However, when Ventron's widow sells his collection, a renowned critic and a Duke misinterpret "Lydia" as Ventron's secretly cherished, enigmatic work. This mistaken attribution leads to the painting's immense fame and value over centuries, with subsequent viewers marveling at its "profound," "enigmatic" quality, forever attributing Miran's ineptitude to Ventron's genius. The story satirizes art criticism, the power of reputation, and the subjective, often misguided, nature of appreciating art.

From a historical perspective, this 1927 story by a prominent Korean author reflects the engagement with Western art and culture during the colonial period. It implicitly critiques blind reverence for authority and established reputation, themes potentially resonant in a society under colonial rule where power structures heavily influenced perception. Historians might find it interesting for its commentary on artistic valuation, the construction of fame, and the subtle exploration of perception versus reality, penned by a major figure in early modern Korean literature.

그리하여, 그는 이 도회를 떠날 준비의 하나로서 한 2,30점이 되는 제 그 지아비의 유작(遺作)을 죄 팔아 버리려 하엿슴니다.

『그것은-』

『명화다. 사실 명화다. 대체 그 웃음은 무엇을 뜻함일까. 조소(嘲笑)? 기쁨? 웃어움? 요소(妖笑)? 사실 수수꺽기야. 「벤트론」이 아니면 도저히 그리지 못할 웃음이다. 아아 나는 웨 「벤트론」만한 재질을 못 타고 낫나?』 (끗)＜72＞
