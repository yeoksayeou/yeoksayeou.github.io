**Song of Turning Back on Heaven and 3 Others**
						Pa'in

**Song of Turning Back on Heaven (背天歌)**

Ttubŏk, ttubŏk, ttubŏk, treading the Earth's strata,
Tens of thousands of footsteps march like thunder. Unable to wait for several centuries, they cover millennia in a single stride and go yonder. Our comrades.

From Year 1 to Year 1900. From one noon to 1.6 billion people,
Humanity has grown well, like maggots. The world has also grown old, rotten, and thin. Living here, in this rotten humanity, this old world, isn't it just too regrettable?

Let's quickly release Chosŏn from this regrettable land.
Let's pull humanity out from the soil where it grew. <62>
Isn't the sky vast? Go on and on, yet the same soil, the same sun and moon, in this mad place.

So, comrades treading the Earth's core, ttubŏk ttubŏk,
Shall we be called Apostates (背天者) for turning our backs on heaven? Turning back today, obeying heaven tomorrow (順天), so let's quickly sing the Song of Turning Back on Heaven loudly and go find the Heart of the World, comrade.

**Comrade (同志) (4 Songs)**

If even a stone would weep, wouldn't you weep? If even you would weep, wouldn't I weep? It's mutual, this weeping passed down for a thousand years. If we came out resolved not to weep, I too would follow you and laugh; I came into the world.

If I devoured ten bags of flames, would it be this hot, friend? You go west, I go east, let's circle this world once. Where our hearts are ablaze, isn't it flames? Where flames <63> rise, wouldn't even green trees burn?

Did you ride a horse? Did you ride a cow? Riding a cow, you are my friend; riding a horse, you are my friend. That cloud crossing the pass—the one ahead becomes wind, the one behind becomes rain. Friends heading to the new country, whether ahead or behind, will all become wind and rain.

Though we were enemies in a past life, let's be friends in this life. It's a big tree; can two or three break its roots? Let's assume ten or four hang on a single leaf, friend, let's rush in like the tide. If we gather, aren't we friends?

**Farmer's Song (農夫歌) (Ballad - 打令)**

Is one called a farmer for digging the earth? Is one called a farmhand for growing the five grains? Digging and digging until entering the grave, yet not a single mal of rice appears. Where did the thousands of sacks of rice made by my hands go? My mouth, my entire life, has only drawn cobwebs (starved). <64>

If I plant thistles in the field, will my heart feel refreshed? At harvest time, if I gather pebbles, will the resentment lessen? If it's not ours anyway, why shed blood and sweat? Yet, it's a fate where we must still shed them. Ah, never become a farmhand again.

Why shouldn't one become a farmhand? If made by these hands, it's our grain. Would a sparrow eat without moving its hands? Would an eagle just prey on others' backs? What is the land itself? It's a field of grass, a field of stones. Without us, there's nothing to gather but bird droppings.

Let's dig the earth, farmers! Aren't all the fist-sized grains ours? Yesterday, the hoeing chest couldn't overcome the whip, but today, the hoeing chest naturally dances with shoulder movements. Look ahead! Beneath the sky, the long-ridged field, and above it, a Bountiful Year (豊年) crawls near. The sound of a bountiful year didn't even cross five ri before, but from this year, it goes 100 ri, it crosses 1,000 ri. E-he-ya, let's dig the earth, farmer! <65>

**Summary**

These four poems by Pa'in express a potent mix of despair, defiance, and revolutionary hope. "Song of Turning Back on Heaven" rejects the decaying state of the world and humanity, calling for a radical break from the past ("turning back on heaven") to forge a new future. "Comrade" emphasizes solidarity and shared struggle, urging unity despite differences or past conflicts. "Farmer's Song" depicts the bitter reality and exploitation faced by farmers, contrasting their immense labor with their poverty, but ultimately ends with a powerful call to action and a vision of future prosperity achieved through collective effort. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, these poems are highly significant. Written in 1927, they reflect the burgeoning socialist and nationalist ideologies challenging the oppressive colonial system and perceived global decay. The themes of rejecting the old order ("Back to Heaven"), class consciousness (farmer's plight and empowerment), and comradeship ("Comrade") resonate strongly with the anti-colonial and social revolutionary movements of the era, offering historians insight into the intellectual and emotional landscape of resistance.

背天歌
뚜벅, 뚜벅, 뚜벅 地層을 밟으며
수십만 발자국이 우뢰가티 간다. 여러 世紀를 기다리다 못해 한 걸음에 千百年씩 묵거 노흐며 저리로 간다. 우리 무리들이.

1年부터 1900年까지. 한낫부터 16億까지
인류는 구데기가티 잘도 자라낫다. 세상도 낡고, 썩고, 여위고 그래 썩은 인류, 낡은 세상 여긔에 살기까지 넘우 안타깝쟌으냐.

조선을 어서 안타까운 이 땅에서 노하 보내자.
인류도 제 자란 흙 속에서 빼어 내노차.＜62＞
하늘은 넓지 안흐냐, 가고 또 가도 가튼 흙 가튼 日月 미틴데

그래, 地心을 밟고 뚜벅뚜벅 가는 무리여
하늘을 등지니 背天者랄까, 오늘 背天, 래일 順天이니 어서 노피 背天歌 부르며 世界의 心臟 차저 가자 동무야.

同志 (歌曲 4首)
돌이라도 울려든 자네 안 울까, 자네라도 울려든 내가 안 울까, 천년 두고 운 울음 피차라, 안 울자고 나왓다기 나도 자네 딸아 웃으려 세상 나왓네.

불꼿 열섬 집어 먹으면 이리 뜨거울까 벗아, 너는 서으로 나는 동으로 이 세상 한 박휘 휘 도자 우리 심장이 다 이는 곳 불꼿 아니랴 불꼿＜63＞ 이는 곳 생나문들 안 타랴.

말 탓느냐, 소 탓느냐, 소 타도 내 벗 말 타도 내 벗. 고개 넘는 저 구름 압선 것은 바람 되고 뒤선 것 비 된다. 새나라 가는 벗 압서거나 뒤서거나 다 비바람 될 것을.

저 생엔 원수 돼도 이 생선 벗 되자. 하 큰 나무이기 두셋 힘으로 뿌리 꺽그랴, 나무닙 한 알에 열낫 매달릴 셈 치고 벗아, 潮水가티 달려 들자. 모이면 우리가 벗 아닌가.

農夫歌(打令)
흙을 뚜지니 농부라든가, 오곡을 지으니 농군이랄까, 무덤에 들 때까지 파고 또 파도 쌀 한 말 안생기네. 내 손으로 된 몃 천섬 쌀섬 다 어되 갓나. 내 입엔 한 일평생 거머줄만 치네.＜64＞

바테 철안을 심으면 속 시원할까 추수 때 짜작돌 거더 들이면 덜이나 원통할까, 우리 것 아닐 바에 웨 피땀 흘리나, 그래도 흘려야 하는 신세, 아서라 다실랑 농군 되지 말라.

농군이 웨 되지 말랴, 이 손으로 지엇스면 우리 곡식이지 참새라고 손 안 놀리고 먹을라, 수리개라고 남 등만 처 먹을라, 땅이야 제 무엇이랴 풀바티요 돌바티지. 우리가 업스면 새똥 박게 거더들일 것 업네.

흙을 뚜지세 농부들아, 주먹가튼 곡식알 다 우리 것 아닌가. 어젯날 매는 기슴은 채찍에 못 이겻더니 오늘날 매는 기슴 억개춤이 저절로 되네. 압다 저 봐라 하늘 아레 사래 긴 밧 그 우로 豊年이 기어오네. 풍년 소리 오리도 안 넘더니 금년부턴 100里두 가네, 1,000里두 넘네 에헤야 흙을 뚜지세 농부야.＜65＞
