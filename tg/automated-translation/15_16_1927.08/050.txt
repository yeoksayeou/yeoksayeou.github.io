As the Brush Goes, Summer Heat Relief Special, A Full Measure of Cool Taste
						Yi Ŭn-sang

Because life is fundamentally disordered and lacks harmony, it's a situation where one cannot fully savor the taste of each season as it comes.
When spring arrives, perhaps one might taste the flavor of spring only by plowing a medicinal herb field or transplanting flowering trees. Now that summer has come, in circumstances where I can't even drive out a single bedbug, wouldn't dreaming of a mountain villa be presumptuous?
At most, if I venture out onto the street and enter an ice stall (氷幕), that might be called the greatest glory. Perhaps finding the forgotten date again after drinking a glass of strawberry water is rather a costly pleasure.
If it gets too hot, one might forget not just the date but even one's own name. What I mean is, in summer, everything that meets the eye is troublesome, and everything that enters the ear is entirely irritating.
Moreover, since numbers (數字) demand much mental effort, seeing and remembering them in hot weather is truly sweat-inducing work.
For those who create train timetables or product price lists, it's their destined work (팔짜), so it can't be helped, but the headache must be indescribable. And while mathematics teachers or math youths might be doing their favorite dance, so no one would stop them, it still looks pitiful to others.
A few days ago, while reading Song Pang-hyang's (宋方向) "Pakch'aekp'yŏn (泊宅編)," I came across the passage "Han T'oe-ji (韓退之) wrote 360 poems of much sorrow, 30 of which involve weeping; Paek Nak-ch'ŏn (白樂天) wrote 2,800 poems of much joy, 900 of which involve drinking wine..." Far from finding the meticulous counting amusing, or rather, far from being impressed by it, my first reaction was that I broke out in a hot sweat and didn't want to read it. Even now, copying it here is, in truth, a moment of sweating.

While being hot in the hot summer isn't necessarily a crime, it feels somewhat awkward before characters like "enjoying the cool" (納凉) or "dispelling the heat" (滌暑).
Of course, enjoying the cool doesn't necessarily mean going to Haeundae (海雲臺) or Sŏgwangsa Temple (釋王寺), nor must one go to Myŏngsasimni or Haekŭmgang (海金剛).
Even if one's fortune (福祿) is meager, forcing one to spend the hot season sitting in a room with a low ceiling, one can still possess the taste for enjoying the cool if one chooses to.
Placing two or three goldfish (붕어) in a clean bowl beside the husband's desk or the wife's bedside and looking at them also qualifies as enjoying the cool.
Recently, it's said that because of "goldfish" (금붕어) at the theaters, hundreds gather nightly instead, and the smell of people stings the nose, suggesting that "goldfish" might be risky for enjoying the cool. However, "silverfish" (은붕어, likely meaning common carp or similar non-fancy fish) should be safe enough, so perhaps catching even loaches and placing them nearby to escape this punishment by heat (炎刑) would be good.
- On the road, having come to Seoul in July 1927 - <16>

**Summary**

The author, Yi Ŭn-sang, laments the inability to fully enjoy the seasons due to life's inherent disorder. Focusing on summer, he describes the oppressive heat that makes even simple tasks like remembering the date or dealing with numbers feel burdensome and irritating. He contrasts the unattainable luxury of mountain villas or famous cool spots with simpler, accessible ways to find relief, such as keeping goldfish or other small fish in a bowl at home. He humorously notes the potential "danger" of popular goldfish-themed entertainment drawing crowds, suggesting common fish are a safer bet for personal cooling. The piece reflects a desire for simple pleasures and mental escape amidst the discomforts of summer and the complexities of life. For historians, the article offers a glimpse into everyday life concerns (dealing with summer heat) in colonial Seoul, mentions of specific leisure spots (Haeundae, Sŏgwangsa), references to classical Chinese literature (Han Yu, Bai Juyi) indicating the cultural background of intellectuals, and commentary on simple domestic pleasures as a form of accessible respite during a potentially difficult era. The mention of crowded theaters suggests emerging popular culture.

붓가는 대로, 銷夏特輯 凉味萬斛
						李殷相
원악 정돈없고 조화없는 생활임으로 시절딸아 그 시절의 맛을 톡톡히 볼 수는 없는 처지다.
봄이 되어 藥 밭이나 갈고 꽃 남기나 옴긴다면 재서나 봄 맛을 볼는지 여름이 되었으나 빈대 하나 구축 못하는 터에 꿈엔들 산장이 황송하지 않으랴.
힘껏해야 거리에 나섰다가 氷幕에나 들어간다면 그것이 최대의 영광이라 할는지. 딸기물 한 잔을 마시고는 잊어버리었던 날ㅅ자를 다시 기억해 찾아내는 것이 오히려 값비싼 유쾌라고나 할찌.
넘우 더우면 날ㅅ자는 구만두고 제 이름까지도 잊어버리지나 아니할는지. 하니 말이지 여름에는 시각에 걸리는 것이 모도가 괴롭고 청각에 들어오는 것이 죄다 골이 난다.
더구나 數字란 머리의 노력을 많이 요구하는 것이기 때문에 더운 때에는 정말 보고 기억하기가 땀나는 일이다.
기차 시간표를 만드는 사람이나 상품 정가표를 만드는 사람은 그것이 제 팔짜에 타고난 일이매 어찌할 수 없는 일이나 두통은 말할 수 없는 두통일 것이요 수학 교사나 數學 청년은 제 좋아하는 춤이니 말릴 뉘도 없지만 그래도 남 보기에 딱한 일이다.
몇 날 전에 宋方向의 「泊宅編」을 읽다가 「韓退之多悲詩三百六十首, 哭泣者三十首, 白樂天多樂詩二千八百首, 飮酒者九百首... 」란 구문에 가서는 그 精査해 놓은 것이 웃읍기는 커녕 아니 거기에 감복하기는 커녕 첫째, 더운 땀이 나서 읽기가 싫었다. 그것을 여긔다 또 벗기어 올리는 지금도 실상은 땀 흘리는 순간이다.
더운 여름에 덥다는 것이 그리 죄될 것은 아니지만 納凉이니 滌暑니 하는 문자 앞에선 좀 미안스럽기도 한 노릇이다.
母論 납량이란 것이 반듯이 海雲臺나 釋王寺로 가야하고 명사십리나 海金剛으로 가야만 하는 것은 아닐 것이다.
福祿이 적어 천정 낮은 방 안에 앉아서 더운 한 절을 지나게 된다 하더라도 납량하는 취미를 가지려면 가질 수 있는 일이다.
남편의 案頭 곁에 안해의 침상 곁에 붕어 두어 마리를 깨끗한 그릇에 담아다 놓고 보아도 납량ㅅ격은 되는 것이다.
近日 극장에서는 「금붕어」 때문에 돌이어 밤마다 수 백명씩 모이어 들어 사람 냄새에 코가 저린다 하니 「금붕어」는 납량에 위험성 있는 것이라 하더라도 「은붕어」쯤은 안심할만한 것이니 미꾸라지라도 잡아다 놓고 이 炎刑을 면하는 것이 좋을 법도 하다.
- 1927년 7월 서울 왔던 길에 - ＜16＞
