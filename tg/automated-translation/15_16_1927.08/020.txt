잡지명	동광 第十六號
발행일	1927년 08월 05일
기사제목	天下絶景 萬物草, 銷夏特輯 凉味萬斛 (Unrivaled Scenery Manmulch'o, Summer Heat Relief Special Feature: A Full Measure of Cool Refreshment)
필자	崔南善
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

Unrivaled Scenery Manmulch'o, Summer Heat Relief Special Feature: A Full Measure of Cool Refreshment
						By Ch'oe Nam-sŏn

Hanhagye Stream

The 30-ri (approx. 12 km) long valley stretching west from Onjŏng-ni to Onjŏng Pass, carved between the twin peaks of Kwanŭm and Obong, has recently been called Hanhagye (寒霞溪). The Kwanŭm peak to the left embodies extreme majestic transcendence, while the Obong peaks to the right display delicate beauty and wondrous moistness. In the gently sloping basin between them, the clear stream of Onjŏngch'ŏn flows, brightly expansive, deep, and serene, allowing visitors to fully savor an aspect of Kŭmgangsan's grandeur and spacious tranquility.

Where the village ends, crossing the lower reaches of Onjŏngch'ŏn via a long bridge named Hagyo (霞橋), the gently ascending path begins below Sujŏngbong (水晶峯, Crystal Peak). This is the main road connecting Kosŏng on this side to Hoeyang on that side; the road is wide and comfortable, making the journey effortless. Moreover, seeing those bringing in seafood, those emerging weary of the mountain gorges, merchants, travelers, ox-drawn carts (牛馱), and porters (車卜) passing in an endless stream (絡繹不絶) makes one feel as if, after a long time in a fairyland, they have returned to the human world (人寰). When hearing a cart driver (車馬夫), his leggings rolled up to his knees and a whip over his shoulder, looking towards Onjŏng Pass wafting the scent of magnolia wood, singing nasally "The water flowing down from Chŏkpyŏkgang <2>" and "On the bright moon of Samilp'o," it feels like unexpectedly encountering the voice and cough (聲咳) of a beloved whom one thought never to hear again, and the longed-for human touch almost intoxicates a person.

However, lifting one's eyes to observe, the surrounding scenery—front, back, left, and right—remains extraordinary, uncommon, remarkably strange, and exquisitely wondrous: a hidden garden of creation. It is still a fairyland, even amidst the human world. The strange peaks (奇峰) like carved white jade and the clear waves (淸波) like dissolved azure glass (碧琉璃) might seem similar to other places, but here, the former are layered while the latter are strangely turbulent, yet run straight and level (平直), long and drawn-out (悠長), imbuing the mountain air (山氣) itself with a rustic charm (野趣)—a new aspect unseen until now. Possessing the essence of verticality while simultaneously offering the novel taste of horizontality is the unique characteristic of the Hanhagye valley.

In a word, Hanhagye is where the deep-mountain Kŭmgangsan is presented as if in an open field. It is a place where one can view Kŭmgangsan sitting down, or even lying down, unlike the usual experience of viewing it standing or holding onto things for support. Creation, having decided not to omit anything whatsoever, must have intended to finally complete Kŭmgangsan with this new arrangement. In Hanhagye, where Kŭmgangsan, previously rolled up like a scroll or folded like a comb box, is spread out like floor paper, one discovers a new Kŭmgangsan of relaxed breadth (寬紓) and peace.

Having panted only amid concentrated, pressing beauty until now, one begins to catch one's breath in this new expansive, caressing flavor. About 10 ri (4 km) in, around the back of Kwanŭmbong, seeing a quite magnificent waterfall to the south, and reaching "Samgŏ-ri" (三巨里) at 10 ri and "Ch'ikdŏp'i" (칡덥히, Arrowroot Field, 葛田) at 20 ri, one feels utterly refreshed inside, as if able to well appreciate the state of mind described as "bright wind and clear moon" (光風霽月) or "vast sky and broad sea" (天空海闊). As you walk, pausing to sit on a streamside rock (溪邊石頭), your chest feels suddenly clear, realizing that the accumulated indigestion of half a lifetime is being newly relieved. Across from the inn at "Ch'ikdŏp'i," nestled among two or three peach trees, lies a flat stone slab (一張平石). Upon closer inspection, one can discern the three characters "Yuk'waam" (六花岩, Six Flower Rock) carved into it. Recent travelers mostly don't know it or pass it by casually (尋常히) even if they do recognize it, but in the old days, this was the marker stone for the entrance to Ch'ŏnbuldong (千佛洞, Thousand Buddha Grotto), considered the very heart of Kŭmgangsan.

Behind the inn, a secluded valley (一洞) penetrates deeply, and a clear stream (淸溪一道) shoots out through a pine forest that looks embroidered, conveying the hidden news of Ch'ŏnbuldong. In the past, it was a scenic spot that travelers invariably appreciated (遊子必賞), but nowadays, favoring ease and speed, people gradually neglect such slightly cumbersome places even during Kŭmgangsan sightseeing. Ignorant of its reality, it circulates in popular lore through absurd legends claiming that Ch'ŏnbuldong houses mysterious carved images of Buddhas and Bodhisattvas from previous eons, which are not permitted for ordinary mortals (凡夫) to view freely (縱覽). In reality, Ch'ŏnbuldong, like Paektaptong (白塔洞, White Pagoda Grotto) or Manmulch'o, is merely a naturally formed unique rock forest (奇石林). Yang Pongnae (楊蓬萊), who could be called the representative mountain ascetic (山岳行者) of modern times, had a deep affection for Ch'ŏnbuldong; it is said that the carving "Yuk'waam" here was also his work. I will now transcribe an excerpt from Pongnae's Record of Ch'ŏnbuldong.

"Climbing Paekchŏngbong (百鼎峰, Hundred Cauldron Peak), looking north towards Taejŏkpyŏk (大赤壁, Great Red Cliff), the terrain is high. Rainbows rise, clouds surge from the mountain's back. Not resting for twenty ri, arriving above the cliff, one finds Kyŏgonggyo (遽空橋, Sudden Void Bridge). It is not a bridge; there are no ladders, troughs, or sawtooth steps (梯槽雁齒) to climb (可緣). Beside the cliff wall is a hole; putting one's eye to it (湊目), heavenly light shines through from outside. The mouth of the hole (穴嘴) has a slight stone ledge. Crawling (匍匐) for over a hundred hand-lengths, it suddenly opens (呀然) into a gate. Paths inside and out are cut off. Looking out dozens of ri, white snow shames the brightness. Jade is carved into screens (削玉爲屛), precious stones laid out to open the scene (舖瓊開局). Dense groves (叢蔚) shine brilliantly (輝粲), standing tall and separate (離抱亭矗). Dragging the belly, stretching the head (曳腹舒頭), one reaches the stone threshold (石閾) by kneeling (乃膝). There are slight stone folds (石褶).
Half-bodied against the wall, entering the central scene, first there are jade pavilions and precious terraces (玉樓瑤臺). Some figures appear like giant personages in official caps and robes, bowing courteously as if in ritual (或冠帶鉅人揖遜如禮). Some resemble commanders in armor (介冑之帥), brandishing weapons and assuming postures (掉闔張勢). There are Buddhas with spiraled topknots and mist-like robes (螺鬟霞帔), immortals appearing and flying. There are strange sights and peculiar forms (奇觀詭相), demons raging and actors smiling (鬼怒優笑). Myriad things that have just shed their embryonic forms (萬物之己脫胚胎者) are indescribable (不可名狀). The surrounding cliffs (環壁) extend twenty ri, perfectly round, not elliptical (圓正不楕).<3> The ground is flat and expansive (地平衍), capable of holding several thousand kyŏng (頃) of fields. Right in the center is a white jade round platform (白玉圓臺), over ten chang (丈, approx. 30 meters) high. A single turn allows one to take in the entire view. Stone springs everywhere are sweet and cool (甘洌); drinking from them makes one forget hunger (忘飢). At dusk, the grotto sky (洞天) is bright as dawn (晃朗如曙). Deep in the night, pairs of crane calls (兩兩鶴聲) descend, circling (徘徊). Moonlight shines brilliantly (月光璀璨). Cranes gather on the round platform. Trying to follow them as they soar (翔翔), they let out a clear cry (淸唳) but are not startled. Soon, the morning light reveals colors (晨光辨色), and the cranes fly away."

Paekchŏngbong (百鼎峰) is so named because countless naturally formed cauldron-shaped rocks are arrayed there. There are two peaks called Paekchŏng on the eastern side of Kŭmgangsan; the Paekchŏngbong mentioned in Pongnae's record is the southern one, considered a direct part of the Kŭmgangsan family (眷屬). The other is located another fifty or sixty ri further north, behind T'ongch'ŏn and Ongch'ŏn (Dokpyŏru).

Manmulch'o (Myriad Things Meadow)

Ascending from Kaltŏn (葛田), the path steepens slightly. After about another 10 ri, Obongsan (五峰山, Five Peaks Mountain) nearly touches one's forehead, and Onjŏng Pass comes clearly into upward view. Here, a massive stone gate (石門) stands piercing the void, revealing within it a grotto-world (洞府) that, even at a glance, is clearly extraordinary. It feels as if the earth's wrath against humanity exploded simultaneously from every corner, filling the space with an uncontrollable, measureless, countless array of wondrous, strange, fantastical, and solidified forms. A gasp of "Eokumeoni!" (에쿠머니, Oh my gosh!) involuntarily escapes the throat. The intuition arises: where else could be like this but Manmulch'o?

Beside the entrance, leaning against a stone cliff (石嶂), a small pavilion has been constructed, selling tea and fruit. It's called Mansangjŏng (萬相亭, Myriad Forms Pavilion), a place for Manmulch'o explorers to catch their breath, have lunch, and retie their shoe straps. The wind blowing out from the unfathomably deep, secluded valley (洞壑) is so cool it feels like it could freeze one's very lungs (廢腑). Thinking this must be the pure breeze from above (上方), rustling the jade pendants (玉佩) of immortal officials and caressing the snow-like skin (雪肌) of divine maidens, its chill carries a refreshing crispness (홋홋한 맛), making one wish to embrace it if it had form.

From Mansangjŏng, turning towards the right shoulder, between massive stone peaks thrusting sharply towards the sky like the sharp horns (利角) of a giant dragon (鉅龍) or the sharp awl (利釗) of a giant spirit (鉅靈), there is a saddle-like dip (駝鞍같이 잘룩해진 곳). This is called "Sajanmok" (獅子목, Lion's Neck) and is a viewpoint for Manmulch'o. A steep path (바드러운 길) climbs through a fissure (裂罅) in the rock. Holding onto a slanted iron railing, twisting and turning (꺽김꺽김) upwards (아가면), one soon reaches the saddle (鞍部). From here, looking out at the continuous range (連亘) of Obongsan and Sejibong (勢至峰) feels much like gazing at the Inner Kŭmgang peaks from Hyŏltŭngnu (歇得樓) or looking across towards Chunghyangsŏng (衆香城) from Paegundae (白雲臺). This is what is called Manmulch'o (the Old Manmulch'o, or Outer Manmulch'o).

"Hee hee!" The sound comes out first. This is the utmost exclamation of astonishment at the utmost strangeness, implying that Creation is simultaneously clever, skillful, playful, and perhaps even a bit vulgar (상스럽다). How deeply intricate (深深密密)! How winding and twisting (曲曲折折)! How multiply layered (重重疊疊)! How tiered and sectioned (層層區區)! How strange and marvelous (奇奇妙妙)! How illusory and empty (幻幻虛虛)! Hee hee! What need was there to go *that* far? The thought arises that Creation's curiosity (好奇) is also excessive. Grasping and grasping, Manmulch'o could be called a single, complete carving (一張全彫) allowing one to survey all 12,000 wonders (萬二千奇) of Kŭmgangsan at a glance (一覽全得). Indeed, Manmulch'o is, first and foremost, a catalog of Kŭmgangsan.

The right flank (右翼) of Sajanmok is a sharp three-pointed peak (三尖峰) that looks like it would cut if touched, resembling a demon-quelling sword (降魔劍) guarding the purity of a secret palace (秘宮); its blade-like edge (서슬) is truly blue-green. Modern people call this Samsŏnam (三仙岩, Three Immortals Rock), but the name is inappropriate. In contrast, the blunt peak (鈍峰) on the left flank (左翼) has a head remarkably resembling a human figure (人形), and furthermore <4> looks fierce; one cannot help but marvel that the name Kwimyŏnam (鬼面岩, Demon Face Rock) becomes ever more fitting (善形容) the more one looks. Viewed from afar or near, it is unmistakably (영낙없는) a demon face; perhaps this is a natural guardian pillar (天然長柱) tasked with protecting the divine precinct (神域鎭護).

The layered cluster of peaks (重重한 叢峯) spread out behind Kwimyŏnam is Manmulch'o. Gazing intently, not a single stone, not a single corner, lacks the form of something (物形), and no two are the same. It could be said that there is no form of thing that cannot be found within it. Is that all? Even a single form, which looked one way moments ago, changes presently; viewing it from here it looks like this, but from there it appears different (아지어서), without even a single instant (一瞬) of fixed appearance. Its divine strangeness and fantastical illusion (神詭怪幻) are beyond description. Of course, what is seen in the morning will not be the same in the evening, and what is viewed in clear light will not be the same under overcast skies. The grand transformation resulting from multiplying these conditions by themselves (自乘又乘) is almost beyond human imagination.

Even someone who prefers simplicity (단촐) cannot help but become verbose when talking about Manmulch'o. Trying to list what this resembles and what that is similar to naturally leads to dredging up all manner of things in existence (萬有). Once one starts talking, even Vimalakīrti would have no choice but to play the role of a chatterbox (잔말장이). Well-known objects from the past have already been rehashed (울여 먹었거니와) multiple times by people of old. Even speaking of modern things (시체 새 것), for instance, the pointy peaks could be likened to broadcasting antennas (放送 「엔테나」), and their collective composition forming a living picture (活畫) is utterly like the projection screen (영사막) of a moving picture (活動寫眞). What is ceaselessly broadcast from there is the sublime melody (「멜로듸」) of the universe's vibrant spirit (宇宙靈活), and what is projected there is the dizzying light and color (앗질앗질한 光色) of heaven and earth's true beauty.

It is natural that the more items one knows, the more chaotic the scope of metaphors (形喩) becomes when describing Manmulch'o. This is because there seems to be nothing it cannot be compared to. One could witness a scene from Greek mythology there, or a tableau from the Jewish apocalypse (묵시록); the heavens of India (印度의 諸天地) and the Records of Immortals of China (支那의 列仙傳) could be said to constitute just small parts within it. All the scenes from heaven and hell witnessed by Dante (「딴테」), all the beautiful women and knight-errants from *Water Margin* (水滸志), all the scenes from *Ch'unhyangjŏn* (春香傳), *Hong Kildongjŏn* (洪吉童傳), *Chŏn Uch'i-jŏn* (田禹致傳), and *Samsŏlgi* (三說記)—not a single one seems to be missing. To put it concisely, essentially all forms of things (物形) in the universe, and all the beautiful sights that can be constructed from them, are contained entirely within Manmulch'o.

Is it any wonder that even the ancients, who knew no exaggeration, and Koreans, said to be strangers to symbolic magnification, faced with such overwhelming scenery, ultimately resorted to calling it Manmulch'o (萬物草, Myriad Things Draft/Meadow)? It is said that when the Creator distributed all things in heaven and earth, Manmulch'o was the design plan and prototype (雛形) He drafted (草 잡으신). In today's terms, it would mean the entirety, the comprehensive view (總覽) of object-form beauty. Indeed, Manmulch'o is truly a catalog of Kŭmgangsan, simultaneously a comprehensive catalog of mountain beauty, and also a complete catalog of physical transformations.

Recently, Japanese people, perhaps due to phonetic similarity (音相似), call it Manmulsang (萬物相, Myriad Forms/Appearances). However, this, like the occasional ancient usage of Manmulsyo (萬物肖, Myriad Resemblances), damages the original meaning (古義). While 'sang' (相) might seem like an elegant Buddhist term (雅名), one should consider that it lacks the direct and pertinent flavor (剴切한 맛) of calling it the 'draft' (草) of all things. <5>

Sin Manmulch'o (New Manmulch'o)

If one were to state the characteristic feature of Kŭmgangsan most succinctly, it would be a cluster (叢集) of sculptural beauty (彫刻的 美). If asked where this characteristic of Kŭmgangsan is most succinctly expressed, it is precisely Manmulch'o. And if asked which part displays this characteristic of Manmulch'o to the utmost degree, it is Sin Manmulch'o (新萬物草).

The Manmulch'o viewed from Sajanmok is actually the exterior, the entrance hall, the wrapping (包裏), and the label, so to speak, of Manmulch'o. To know the true appearance and real value (眞相實價) of Manmulch'o, one must properly see its inner courtyard (內庭), its main hall and inner rooms (堂奧), and examine its contents and feel its substance. Old Manmulch'o, that is, Outer Manmulch'o, is certainly fine, but one shouldn't just linger in the outer area (內깐) and stop there. Not until after stepping further into the inner courtyard (안 뜰) and exploring the secrets (秘奧) of Sin Manmulch'o can one speak of the true taste (眞味) of Manmulch'o. Who can know the savory true taste (참 맛) by just eating the outer leaves of cabbage (잦을 송이)?

Visitors often see only what is visible from Sajanmok and consider their Manmulch'o sightseeing complete. Some even remark, based on this alone, that Manmulch'o is nothing special. However, the exposed Outer Manmulch'o (舊萬物草) is, essentially, not the True Manmulch'o (眞萬物草). Speaking most generously, Old Manmulch'o is merely a relatively insignificant small part of the Great Manmulch'o (大萬物草). The path from Old Manmulch'o to Sin Manmulch'o is admittedly not uncomplicated (까달롭다), but anyone visiting Manmulch'o absolutely must see Sin Manmulch'o, just as it is indispensably necessary to see Manmulch'o when visiting Kŭmgangsan. Those astonished by Old Manmulch'o, to be truly astonished once more, and those not captivated by Old Manmulch'o, to finally grasp the taste of Manmulch'o—everyone should definitely not spare the effort to reach Sin Manmulch'o.

Crossing Sajanmok, passing below Kwimyŏnam, treading the stony stream bed where a small brook trickles, one rushes towards the projection screen of Manmulch'o seen across the way, tucks it under one's arm (넙구리에 줌처 끼고), and enters a narrow rock gorge (岩峽) opening to the left, heading into a gloomy (우중충한), pitch-dark (까마아득한) unknown world beyond. The layered stone floor forming steps (격지격지 층단) and the sharply angled rock edges (반뜻 모소리진 바위 빈 양) are, whether as fragments or whole scenes (왼 幅), all skillful masterpieces of sculpture (大彫刻). The only thing that gives a sense of lack as one proceeds is the relative scarcity of moisture (水分 水素). I heard that to fully taste fish stew, one shouldn't add beef garnish (쇠고기 꿈이); perhaps Creation intended Manmulch'o to be a place to intensely savor only the taste and beauty of rock (石味石美), thus using water as sparingly (졸략히) as possible.

Winding inwards and outwards, bumping up against things and being bumped into (안돌고 지돌고 치받고 들이받아), the path certainly cannot be called smooth (平順). However, everything one brushes against (탁 거치는) is utterly extraordinary—like Xi Shi (西施) or Yang Guifei (楊貴妃) among humans, or peonies and rugosa roses (목단 해당) among flowers—so the hardship itself becomes entirely pleasure (樂). Clusters upon clusters (무덕이 무덕이) of beautiful forms (美의 群像), strung together like linked chains (連環的) or long ropes (長繩的), present new marvels at every turn (轉瞬), more than enough to make one forget fatigue. If these were truly such beautiful women instead of natural stone beauty, would we even have dared to touch the hems of their skirts (치마ㅅ자락)? The thought brings a renewed sense of regret (有感) as we scrape our knees (무릅을 시친다) and embrace the torsos (허리통을 껴 안는다) of these forms.

After some distance, the path splits. Taking the left narrow path (狹路), leaving the right path leading to the so-called O Manmulch'o (奧萬物草, Inner Manmulch'o), one continues piercing through and following (뚫고 아가기), feeling as if still walking through a valley of ten thousand flowers (萬花谷). After covering a total of six or seven ri (approx. 2.4-2.8 km), <6> one crosses another pass called Sajanmok. Although Sajanmok is a common name in Kŭmgangsan for saddles (鞍部) on prominent, abruptly rising stone peaks, here one sees a natural fierce lion (猛獅) roaring towards the sky (仰天大吼), pressing down on the neck of the pass, perfectly matching name and reality (名實이 뷘틈없이). Since Manmulch'o already has two Sajanmoks, calling this one An-Sajanmok (안ㅅ사자목, Inner Lion's Neck) would be suitable. Climbing this Sajanmok, the slopes become gentler here and there, and the valley opens up somewhat, giving a sense of generosity, like finding an amiable side to a rigid person.

Crossing Sajanmok, the path becomes somewhat flatter, allowing hands and knees to cease their role as auxiliary aids (補助 노릇) to the feet (발), enabling one to stand straight and walk. A short distance further, the second fork (岐路) leading to O Manmulch'o appears, where a signpost (標木) indicating 5 ri (approx. 2 km) stands. About 2 *kungji* (弓地, bow-shot distance, perhaps 200-300 meters) in from Sajanmok, a stone wall suddenly blocks the way like a heavenly fortress (天城). Looking up at the dark precipice, a hole just large enough for one person offers a glimpse (엿보이어 주다) of sky and world beyond. Thinking how desperate (긔가 막히다) one would have been without it, one might feel that hole is like one's own nostril. Considering that we have been guided thus far by grace (恩寵), but now seem barred from seeing further inside, it feels undoubtedly certain that something immense must lie within, sparking intense curiosity. But not being a bird, one cannot help but hesitate (망단하다) for a while. Wondering how to get beyond, one scans around, and a subtle path (살뜻한 一路) suddenly catches the eye.

The Grand View from Ongnyobong Peak

Looking closely, rock fissures (巖罅) run vertically, and footholds (뿌다치) protrude here and there. As if by the compassion (悲愍) of some savior, an iron chain (一條鐵索) hangs down (좇아 들이우어 있음니다). "Aha!" one thinks, leaping up eagerly (踴躍一番). Grasping with hands and fumbling with feet, half-suspended in air, one eventually climbs up to what is literally a heavenly gate (天門). So glad just to be ascending, the sweat, effort, and steepness (바드러운 것) are completely forgotten. Only when the celestial wind (天風) blowing from the heavenly gate wipes the sweat from the forehead does one look down (아래를 나려다 보고) and realize what a truly perilous place has been scaled (더위 잡아 올랐구나). "Right, now I too am joining the ranks of immortals (仙班에 들 叅列하는구나)," thinking this, one steps through the gate. The boundless scenery rushing in (홱 끼어처오는) instantly sparks the thought, like electricity, "This is another heaven and earth, not the human world" (別有天地非人間). On the north rock face just outside the gate, the characters "Kŭmgang Che-ilmun" (金剛 第 一門, Kŭmgang First Gate) are carved. The thought arises that calling it "Heavenly First Gate" (天上 第 一門) might be more appropriate.

Outside the gate is sheer void. One can only look down into the vast empty space stretching from Pirobong (毘盧峰) down towards Obongsan. Only a branching continuous ridge (一枝連嶂) bending to the left beckons us forward. Turning one's eyes, pure white stone peaks, without a grain of sand, already present their wonders (奇) and display their skills (巧), point by point (頭頭尖尖), making it difficult to fix one's gaze (怳惚히 眼睛을 정하기 어려우며). Furthermore, the branching, outstretched ridge resembles the antlers of an idle great deer (大鹿). Traversing this gap (틈바구니), crossing horizontally and vertically, is, in a sense, like attending court in the Jade Capital (玉京), riding on the back of a white deer (白鹿). Along the way, one jumps over countless fissures (千耶萬耶한 裂罅), and in places where the rock wall curves and the bird-like path (鳥逕) is interrupted (좇아 끊기었는데), several logs have been laid across to allow passage—the path is indeed thrillingly precarious (아슬아슬하기 짝이 없읍니다). Wondering what sights await to justify receiving such <7> an advance payment (先金 代價), one feels regret at every turn, yet upon lifting one's eyes, the breathtakingly marvelous views (神奇妙觀) that make the jaw drop instantly cause repentance (뉘우처집니다) for the hardship.

Putting everything else aside, just being thankful for not being blown away by the wind, one climbs as high as possible and proceeds as far as possible. Reaching the absolute peak of climbing (樊緣이 絶頂) and the focal point of viewing (眺望이 초점), a stone carved with "Ch'ŏnsŏndae" (天仙臺, Heavenly Immortal Terrace) catches the eye. Indeed! Only heavenly immortals (天仙) could come and play in such a place. Even if a mortal who eats cooked food (火食하는 人間) comes to such a fine place, they can be considered an immortal, at least for a moment. Considering the effort (工夫) required to climb this far, it wouldn't be entirely baseless to say one has undergone apotheosis (羽化) from the flesh. Anyone, no matter who, standing here could possibly be called a Heavenly Immortal.

Though having entered through narrow ways, the field of vision (眼界) has finally opened up splendidly. Though having passed through perilous places, one gets to see magnificent sights (奇壯한 구경). Truly sublime! Yes, sublime! It cannot be described as this or that or anything specific; resorting to a possibly vague but most encompassing expression, one can only say "sublime" (거룩하다). Ch'ŏnsŏndae is at an elevation of 3,500 *ch'ŏk* (feet, approx. 1060m), so it's not extremely high. However, the state of spiritual exaltation (昻奮하여지는 精神)—a psychological state blending mystery, ecstasy (恍惚), grandeur (壯快), and fear (恐怖)—experienced upon setting foot here is certainly a boundary crossed for the first time in Kŭmgangsan, and a sensation unobtainable anywhere else. In terms of business ventures (事業經營), it's like achieving ultimate success after seven falls and eight rises (七顚八倒). In terms of battlefields (戰場 境界), it's like gaining final victory after desperate struggles (惡戰苦鬪). Thus, having finally climbed Ongnyobong Peak's (玉女峯頭) Ch'ŏnsŏndae is undoubtedly a great joyous event (一大快事). However, faced with the indescribable spectacle (不可名狀할 壯觀) of the extraordinarily unique and surpassing great world of wonders (超特越逸한 大怪奇界) unfolding before one's eyes, a sense of fear arises at encountering the mysterious power of creation for the first time. Furthermore, with sheer cliffs (낭떠러지) on either side, feeling as if one's body stands isolated (兀立) in the void, shivers run down one's spine (이턱 저턱 소름이 쭉쭉 끼치기도 합니다).

Ongnyobong (玉女峯, Jade Maiden Peak) is truly a monarch. The vast waves (浩波) of the East Sea (東海) guard it from afar, and Pirobong (毘盧峰), like a fully armored general (具甲힌 大將), protects its nape (덜미). A distinct ridge line (一脈) runs northeastward, while to the southwest lies Sangdŭngbong (上登峯), to the north Obongsan (五峯山), and to the east the twin peaks Seji (勢至) and Munsju (文殊). Within the surrounding screen of peaks (藩屛), the Jade Capital (玉京) called Manmulch'o is laid out, and the Jade Emperor (玉皇) named Ongnyobong is enthroned (奠坐). Billions of immortal officials in caps, sashes, robes, and tablets (帽帶袍笏), tens of thousands of heavenly maidens in crowns, gowns, rings, and pendants (冠裳環珮), the Nine Streams and Hundred Schools (九流百家), the Four Barbarians and Eight Manchu Tribes (四夷八蠻), the myriad beings of the six realms of existence and four types of birth (䶀‰道 六趣 四生 兆品)—all have their own place and role, attending court and surrounding the throne (朝觀環擁). Of course, no earthly emperor ever possessed such glory. Neither Caesar's (「케사르」) palace nor Emperor Taizong of Tang's (唐太宗) court could have had such grand ranks in assembly. Only the scene of the Hwanung Assembly (桓雄王會), where gods and humans gathered together (率歸同集), perhaps approached this magnificence.

One section clearly depicts the Heavenly Emperor's Son, Hwanung (天帝子 桓雄), feeling pity for the suffering of the human world under the three calamities (三災塗炭), sincerely petitioning (啓達) his Father Emperor (父皇) with a vow to save the world. Another section clearly shows the Heavenly Emperor and his Son, having established the grand design for the earthly paradise, looking down upon the human world (人世), deciding where to place the marker of this glory, and specially selecting Great Paeksan (大伯山) in Great Chosŏn (大朝鮮). Another section, with the script written and stage set, clearly depicts the Great Divine Actor (大神優), Hwanung Ch'ŏnwang (桓雄 天王), revered by both heaven and humanity (人天齊仰), enacting the great drama of "Benefiting Humanity Broadly" (弘益人間). He descends majestically (威儀있게 降臨하사) beneath the Sacred Sandalwood Tree (神檀樹下) on T'aebaeksan (太伯山) with the Three Heavenly Seals (天苻三印) and 3,000 divine followers including the Wind Earl (風伯) and Cloud Master (雲師), sequentially announcing the Heavenly Government of 360 divine affairs (360 神國天政). <8> Another section clearly shows the divine transformation (神化) of the Heavenly Emperor's Son shining brilliantly like the sun (赫臨) and spreading pervasively like great light (周遍), causing the thousand demons and myriad evils (千妖萬邪) that ran rampant across the land (長闊에 跋扈하던) to scatter in defeat (狼狽顛倒) seeking holes, while the myriad beings and countless creatures (萬彙群生) groaning in deep suffering (深苦) revive their dying breaths under the dripping sweet dew (甘露滴滴), waving their hands and shouting "Hurrah!" (歡呼嵩呼). Another section clearly depicts Tan'gun Ch'ŏnwang (檀君天王), the King of Kings for the eternal human world (人界久遠), being born (誕育) in the "Pulgunnae" Garden (「불그내」園) amidst myriad blooming flowers (萬花 繚亂), through the utmost devotion (精進) of the Bear Mother (態母). This scene here, that scene there—the entire unfolding history of 10,000 years of "Chosŏn" since the age of mythology is vividly visible within it.

Of course, within it, the scene of the Royal Assembly (王會) of Great Chosŏn during its golden age is also clearly revealed. Eskimo people (「에스키모」人) wearing fish skins (魚皮), Chukchi people (「죽지」人) with deer antlers (鹿角), Ainu people (「아이누」人) receiving bear paws (態掌), Koryak people (「코리악」人) with whale-hair braids (鯨髮), the Sushen (肅愼) and Malgal (靺鞨, Mohe) with stone arrowheads and hu-wood arrows (石砮楛矢), the Shiwei (室韋) and Khitan (契丹) who used horses in summer and pigs in winter (夏馬冬猪), the Northern and Southern Xiongnu (匈奴) of the Great Hunt in the Yinshan Mountains (陰山大獵), the Donghu (東胡) and Xianbei (鮮卑) who sent off spirits at Chishan (赤山送神), the successive dynasties of the Western Lands (西土歷朝) with embroidered ceremonial robes (黼黻文章), the various peoples of the Southern Islands (南島諸夷) with grass clothing and blackened teeth (卉服漆齒)—all gather, bringing tribute packed in bundles (짐짐이), carried on heads (임임이), in baskets (바리바리), and on carts (수레수레). The scene appears vividly like sculpted cinema (彫刻體의 「기네마」). The fierce divine hosts of India (印度의 諸神衆), the beautiful immortal troupes of Greece (希臘의 諸仙群), red-haired, blue-eyed people from the distant West (紅毛碧眼의 遠西人), black-skinned, naked people from the southern seas (黑膚裸形의 海南人)—no race, no class, no appearance is absent. What is even more praiseworthy (갸륵갸륵) is that all these figures from near and far (遐邇) are equally clothed in the divine transformation of heaven, clad from behind in pure white spiritual light (白淨靈光).

To start saying "There is this" or "Someone is doing that there" would be endless. Moreover, merely listing myriad things (歷數) becomes idle chatter (잖말) without fully conveying their likeness (形似を盡喩). Not only that, but using too many such small, petty (잘고 좀스러운) comparisons damages the original flavor (本味) of mysterious, simple wholeness (渾撲), becoming what's called "carving flesh to make a sore" (剜肉生瘡). Therefore, I will simply conclude with the phrase: an indescribable, ineffable, unnamable Great Compendium of Myriad Forms (萬形大全), Great Assembly of Myriad Wonders (萬奇集成). It is said this was the draft (草本) from which creation made all things, but in my thought, it seems not only things already made, but also many unstarted drafts (未着手 草本) for which only the sketches exist and the actual objects have yet to emerge.

While Kŭmgangsan as a whole is formed by the weathering (風化所成) of a single rock mass (一塊石), Manmulch'o in particular is a single small corner of Kŭmgangsan's diamond-like rock (金剛金石) showing the extreme of weathering power (風蝕力), almost to the point of cruel carving (慘刻). It is a grand, uniquely shaped (畸形的) product. You may have seen pictures of the tremendous spectacle of Saguaro cactus clusters (覇王樹簇生) in the mountains of Arizona (「아리쏘나」) state in the United States (合衆國). Similarly, the overwhelming sight of Sin Manmulch'o, where myriad stone peaks of bizarre and fantastical shapes (奇怪幻譎) are strewn about in clumps (무덕이로), scattered like bursts (발로 떨기로), attempts to rip open the viewer's hundred-kalpa heavy obstacles (百劫重障) of aesthetic vision (美眼) in an almost threatening, high-pressure (威赫的 高壓的) manner. Those pretending to be enlightened (達觀하는 체 하는 이) might perhaps say Sin Manmul is just Old Manmul again, what's so different? Those pretending to be clever (똑똑한 체 하는 이) might perhaps say Sin Manmulch'o is just seeing the Old Manmulch'o, previously viewed from the surface, by going around to the back (裏面). However, although both are Manmulch'o, compared to Sin Manmulch'o, Old Manmulch'o hardly has a face to show; Sin Manmulch'o is that utterly different (딴판) and distinct (동띈 것). One might say Old Manmulch'o is the False Manmulch'o (假萬物), and Sin Manmulch'o is the True Manmulch'o (眞萬物草). Those who stop after seeing only the entrance of Old Manmulch'o from Sajanmok are out of the question. Those who proceed, intending to see the inside fully, but turn back midway (中路) or upon reaching Kŭmgang First Gate, saying "Aigu, I can't go further," have essentially not seen Manmulch'o truly. And those who haven't properly seen Sin Manmulch'o cannot claim to have seen Kŭmgangsan well. I want to urge people born into this world to come to Sin Manmulch'o and peel the scales from their eyes (눈ㅅ겁풀을 벗기라).<9>

Poems and songs (題咏) about Manmulch'o have not been few since ancient times, but none, of course, have resonated more than a mosquito's buzz (蚊聲) against Mount Sumeru (須彌山). Lines like those by Yi Hwarang (李峿堂, Yi Sang-su 李象秀):
"Peaks startled,拔物(pulling things up), vie in glaring at each other; / Stones enraged, 排空(pushing the sky), all seem about to fly." (峰驚拔物爭相睨 石怒排空盡欲飛)
are famous verses recited by everyone (人口에 膾炙), but they represent, at best, a tiny prick (약간 따아짝 하였다고나 할 정도) with a needle tip on the vast, complete mass (渾圓한 大塊). While Kŭmgangsan as a whole seems designed to prevent anyone—poet, artist, orator—from laying hands or mouth upon it, Manmulch'o, especially Sin Manmulch'o viewed from Ongnyobong Peak, is a place where one cannot even leave a fingernail-sized mark (손톱 반만한 자국). An artist capable of truly capturing this (神融해 낼 美術家) will likely never appear in all future ages (盡未來際).

The composition and form of Sin Manmulch'o are truly majestic (威稜), exhilarating (痛快), and masculine (男性的). Yet, I observe two feminine elements (女性的 分子) mixed within. One is the 7 parts pure white, 3 parts tender yellow (七分淨白 三分嫩黄) lichens and mosses (地衣蘇苔) that chase after the rugged stone surfaces (石面), delicately adorning and clothing them (곱닿게 곱닿게). The other is the fragrant and alluring trace (香艶한 자취) called Ongnyŏ Sehŭdubun (玉女洗頭盆, Jade Maiden Hair-Washing Basins), three of which remain just below the summit (上峰). Embracing the summit rock (絶頂의 바위) and looking down westward (西向으로 俯瞰), on a flat stone surface about 100 *ch'ŏk* (feet, approx. 30m) directly below (直下), three round stone basins (石壺) carved deep are placed side by side. The largest is about 3 *ch'ŏk* (approx. 90cm) in diameter, the smallest about 1 *ch'ŏk* (approx. 30cm). Water that never dries up even in severe drought (大旱不渴) collects here. Legend says that when heavenly maidens descend (下峰) to Ongnyobong, they wash their hair there before leaving, hence the name Ongnyŏ Sehŭdubun. Originally, the name Ongnyŏ Sehŭdubun has origins in China (支那); similar features exist at famous mountains throughout Chosŏn (朝鮮), and also at several other locations in Kŭmgangsan, such as the Upper Eight Ponds (上八潭) in Manp'oktong (萬瀑洞). It is difficult now to state its definite ancient meaning (古義), but my thought is that they are likely relics of ancient ritual sites (古代祭典). To go see them (가아 봄에는), one must descend to Kŭmgangmun (金剛門) and circle around the rock.

From the valley entrance (洞口) to Ongnyobong Peak (玉女峯頭) is considered 10 ri (approx. 4km), but the actual distance is about half that. Due to the difficult path, ascending takes about 2 hours, but descending takes only 30 minutes. Descending slightly from Kŭmgangmun and turning left (左折), crossing the saddle (鞍部) of Uibong (羽衣峯, Feather Robe Peak) leads to the so-called O Manmulch'o (奧萬物草, Deep Manmulch'o). Exploration in this area has also become increasingly popular recently. O Manmulch'o is the mountain ridge (山背) connecting Obongsan and Sejibong, seen from both Old and New Manmulch'o. The peaks and cliffs (峰嶂) on both sides are sharply cut (截斷的), chiseled, carved, and elegant (劖刻削秀), presenting strange sights and magnificent views (奇觀壯聸) almost overwhelming to behold (應接不可). Its summit is, of course, higher than Sin Manmulch'o, and the waters of the East Sea feel close enough to lap at one's feet (발 끝에 채일만치). Although the path is somewhat rough, only by traversing the entire Manmulch'o valley this way and exiting at Changjŏn harbor (長葥港) can one claim to have fully seen Manmulch'o (비릇오 萬物草 구경을 온전히 하였다 할 것입니다).

To be appended (附記) is the alternate route (別路) from Outer Kŭmgang (外金剛) into Inner Kŭmgang (內金剛) that passes in front of Manmulch'o. The main road (大路) ascending directly from the front of Manmulch'o crosses Onjŏng Pass (溫井嶺, 2,600 feet), the saddle of Obongsan, passes through Sinp'ung-ni (新豐里) and Sedong (細洞), follows the Kŭmgangch'ŏn stream (金剛川), goes through Marhwi-ri (末輝里, Pukch'ang 北倉) and T'apkŏ-ri (塔巨里), and connects all the way to Chang'ansa Temple (長安寺). Crossing Onjŏng Pass, amidst dense forests and hidden paths (密林幽徑), strange flowers and exotic scents (奇花異香), while glimpsing the East Sea peeking (갸웃갸웃하는) through the trees, proceeding in an infinitely friendly atmosphere (親和한 기분), is said to be one of the most delightful routes (通路) in Kŭmgangsan. Also, past Sedong, at a place called Ssukpat (쑥밭, Mugwort Field), turning south and entering the valley descending between Yŏngnangbong (永郞峯) and Nŭnghŏbong (凌虛峯), lies Kŭmjŏndong (金傳洞), containing what is said to be the tomb of King Kŭmjŏn of Silla (新羅金傳大王陵). Further up is Kusŏngdong (九成洞), <10> known since ancient times as a place famed for springs and waterfalls (泉瀑の勝) like Mansŏngp'ok (萬聲瀑). Due to transportation issues, this area is still neglected (閑却) by ordinary sightseers, but after the Kŭmgangsan electric railway is opened (開鑿) this way, its fame will surely rise and become widely known (著聞). <11>

**Summary**

This 1927 travelogue by Ch'oe Nam-sŏn describes a journey through parts of Kŭmgangsan (Mount Kŭmgang), focusing on the Hanhagye valley and the Manmulch'o ("Myriad Things Meadow," often called Manmulsang) rock formations. Ch'oe contrasts the relatively accessible Hanhagye with the increasingly complex and stunning beauty of Outer (Old) Manmulch'o and especially Inner (New) Manmulch'o, reached via challenging climbs including Sajanmok ("Lion's Neck") and the "Heavenly Gate." He culminates the description with the awe-inspiring, almost overwhelming view from Ongnyobong ("Jade Maiden Peak"), emphasizing its unique sculptural beauty and suggesting it represents a catalog of all possible forms and even Chosŏn's mytho-history. He notes the inadequacy of language or art to capture its essence and concludes by mentioning alternative routes and future development prospects (like the electric railway). From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for Ch'oe Nam-sŏn's attempt to frame Kŭmgangsan's beauty through Korean mythology (Hwanung, Tan'gun) and history (invoking images of ancient Chosŏn's vast influence), implicitly asserting a distinct Korean cultural identity and deep historical roots even within the context of Japanese rule. His mention of Japanese renaming ('Manmulsang') and the anticipated development via Japanese infrastructure (electric railway) subtly highlights the colonial presence and its impact on experiencing and interpreting the Korean landscape.

기사제목	天下絶景 萬物草, 銷夏特輯 凉味萬斛

기사형태	기행문

天下絶景 萬物草, 銷夏特輯 凉味萬斛
						崔南善
寒溪霞
溫井里에서 西으로 溫井嶺까지 觀音, 五峯의 양 連峯간에 뚫어진 30리 長谷을 근래 寒霞溪란 이름으로 부르니 좌측의 관음은 雄博超絶을 極하고 우방의 五峯은 嫩麗奇潤을 발뵈인 중에 비스듬하게 낮아가는 바닥으로 溫井川의 淸溪가 昭曠深淡하게 흘러 나리어서 노는 이로 하여곰 金剛山의 웅대하고 間曠한 일면을 흠씻 맛보게 하는 곳입니다.
마을이 끝나는 곳에서 霞橋라고 이름한 一長橋로써 溫井川의 하류를 건느면 水晶峯 밑으로 하여 민틋하게 올라가는 길이 비롯됩니다. 高城 이 짝으로붙어 淮陽 저 짝을 통하는 대로라 길이 넓고 편하여 행역의 수고가 도무지 없고 또 해물 싫고 들어가는 자, 峽山 싫고 나오는 자, 상인, 여객, 牛馱 車卜이 絡繹 不絶하여 오래간만에 선경으로붙어 人寰에 돌아온 듯한 생각을 가지게 합니다. 갈 감게를 무릅까지 하고 채 쪽을 어깨에 메인 車馬夫가 木蓮香을 몰려오는 溫井嶺을 내다보면서 「적벽강＜2＞ 나린 물이」와 「三日浦 밝은 달에」를 코ㅅ소리 범으려서 부르는 것을 들을 때에는 못 들을 줄 녀기었던 애인의 聲咳를 뜻밖에 접한 듯도 하여 그립던 인간미가 거의 사람을 취하게 한다.
그러나 눈을 들어 살피어 보면 전후 좌우의 광경이 의연히 超凡 出常하고 卓奇 절묘한 조화의 비원입니다. 의연히 人寰 그대로 선경입니다. 백옥을 쪼은 듯한 奇峰과 碧琉璃를 푼 듯한 淸波는 다른 대와 같다면 같으되 전자는 중첩한 채 후자는 奇激한 채 平直 ㅎ 고 悠長하여 山氣 그대로 野趣를 띄우었음이 이때까지 보지 못하던 새 국면입니다. 乘直的의 본질을 지닌 채 수평선의 新味를 겸한 것이 寒霞溪 골작이의 특색입니다.
일언으로 蔽하면 두메의 金剛山을 들로 구경하게 한 대가 寒霞溪입니다. 서어서 구경하고 덩기 잡으면서 구경하던 金剛山을 앉아서 구경하고 들어 누어서라도 구경하게 한 곳입니다. 무엇이던지 하나도 빼이지 아니리라고 작정하신 조화는 이러한 새 배포로써 긔어히 金剛山을 완성하시려 하신 것입니다. 두루마리 같이 돌돌 말고 빗접같이 착착 접었던 金剛山을 장판지 같이 피어서 보는 寒霞溪에서 寬紓 평화한 새 金剛山 하나를 발견하게 됩니다.
이때까지 圍集的, 威迫的의 美 만에 헐떡이다가 이 발산적 撫摩的의 새 맛에 겨우 숨 돌우기를 비롯하여 觀音峯 뒤 쯤 되는 곳에 꽤 웅장한 폭포를 남으로 보면서 10리에 「三巨里」와 20리의 「츩덥히」(葛田)를 다달으면 속이 아주 시원해서 光風霽月이니 天空海闊이니 하는 심경을 잘 짐작할 뜻 하여집니다. 가다가 溪邊石頭에 궁둥이를 부치어 보는 쪽쪽 가슴이 훌연하여 반생의 체기가 새로 새로 살아짐을 깨닷게 합니다. 「츩덥히」 주막 건너에 桃花 兩 三樹에 쌓이어 一張平石이 있고 자세히 보면 「六花岩」 3자의 사기었음을 알아 내리니 요새 행인은 대개 모르기도 하고 알아도 尋常히 간과하되 옛날에는 이것이 金剛山 중의 金剛山이라고 하던 千佛洞口의 표석이던 것입니다.
주막 뒤로 은윽한 一洞이 깊다랗게 뚫이고 淸溪 一道가 수 놓은 듯한 송림 중으로 쏘처 나옴이 千佛洞의 숨은 소식을 전하는 것이니 옛날에는 遊子 必賞의 승지였으나 簡易 速成 좋아하는 근래는 金剛山 구경에서도 좀 페스러운 이런 곳은 차차 돌아보지 않게 되고 그 실지를 모르매 천불이란 이름만으로써 상상하여 千佛洞에는 諸佛 여래의 前劫적 刻像이 있는데 신비하여 凡夫의 縱覽을 허락지 아니한다는 황당한 전설로써 사람의 입에 오르나립니다. 실상은 千佛洞도 白塔洞이나 만물초나 한 가지로 천연한 一奇 석림에 불과한 것입니다. 근대에서 대표적 산악 行者라 할 楊蓬萊는 千佛洞에도 깊은 애착을 가지었는데 위선 여긔 六花岩이라고 새긴 것도 그의 짓이라 합니다. 이제 蓬萊의 千佛洞記를 抄해 두겠음니다.
登百鼎峰, 北望大赤壁, 地勢高, 霓起雲漲, 從山背, 不舍二十里, 到壁上, 得遽空橋, 非橋也, 無梯槽雁齒之可緣, 壁畔有穴, 湊目外通天光, 穴嘴微有石帶, 匍匐行百餘手, 呀然作門, 內外路絶, 望數十里, 白雪羞明, 削玉爲屛, 舖瓊開局, 叢蔚輝粲, 離抱亭矗, 曳腹舒頭, 乃膝石閾, 微有石褶,
半身附壁入中局, 先有玉樓瑤臺, 或冠帶鉅人揖遜如禮, 或介冑之帥掉闔張勢, 有螺鬟霞帔佛現仙飛, 有奇觀詭相鬼怒優笑, 萬物之己脫胚胎者, 不可名狀, 環壁二十里, 圓正不楕,＜3＞ 地平衍, 可置土田數千頃, 正中有白玉圓臺, 高十餘丈, 一旋面可收全景, 石泉在在甘洌, 飮之忘飢, 日暮洞天晃朗如曙, 夜深兩兩鶴聲徘徊而下, 月光璀璨, 鶴集圓臺之上, 試隨而翔翔, 一作淸唳, 亦不驚, 己而晨光辨色, 鶴則飛去.
百鼎峰이란 것은 천연한 정형석이 무수히 列在하였다 하여 부르는 이름이니 金剛山의 동변에 百鼎으로 일컷는 봉이 둘인데 蓬萊記의 이른바 百鼎峰은 그 南인 자로 金剛山의 직접 眷屬에 드는 것이요 또 하나는 여긔서 다시 북으로 五六十리나 올라가서 通川, 甕遷(독벼루) 뒤에 있는 것입니다.

萬物草
葛田에서붙어 오르는 맛이 좀 늘어서 한 10리를 더 나가면 五峰山이 거의 이마에 닿고 溫井嶺이 빤하게 치어다 보이는 곳에 우람한 石門 一構가 허공을 헤치고 선 속으로 얼른 보기에도 심상ㅎ지 아니한 一洞府가 열리었읍니다. 인간에 대한 대지의 노염이 일종에 一角식 일시에 폭발한 것처럼 것잡을 수 없는 무량 무수한 奇秀 幻凝이 그 속에 그득히 들어 장여 있음을 보고는 에쿠머니의 소리가 문득 목구멍에서 튀어 나읍니다. 萬物草 밖에는 이럴대가 어대랴 하는 直覺이 납니다.
동구 옆으로 石嶂을 의지하여 一小亭을 얽고 茶果를 파는 것은 萬相亭이라 하여 萬物草 探勝客의 숨도 도르고 점심도 하고 또 신들메를 고처 하는 곳입니다. 얼마나 깊은지 모르는 은윽은윽한 洞壑 속으로붙어 나오는 바람이 시원하다 못하여 조곰하면 사람의 폐부를 얼리려 니다. 이것이 仙官의 옥패를 울리고 신녀의 雪肌를 스처나오는 上方의 청풍이거니 하면 쌀쌀한 채로 홋홋한 맛이 있어 형체가 있으면 껴안고라도 싶습니다.
萬物亭으로붙어 右肩으로 돌아가면 鉅龍의 利角처럼 鉅靈의 利釗처름 일대 석봉이 촉기있게 한울을 찌르는 사이에 駝鞍같이 잘룩해진 곳이 있으니 이것이 「獅子목」이라 하여 萬物草의 관상 지점입니다. 바위의 裂罅를 타아서 바드러운 길이 났는데 바스듬한 鐵欄을 붓들고 꺽김꺽김 아가면 어언간 鞍部가 되는데 여긔서 五峰山, 勢至峰의 連亘을 데미다 봄이 마치 歇得樓에서 內金剛 列嶂을 바라다 보는 것도 같고 백운대에서 衆香城 건너다 보는 것도 비슷합니다. 이것이 萬物草(舊 萬物草, 곳 外 萬物草) 란 것입니다.
히히! 하는 소리가 먼저 납니다. 이것은 造化가 다 십스럽기도 하고 공교스럽기도 하고 또 작난스럽기도 하고 좀 상스럽기도 하다는 뜻을 먹음어서의 최대 奇異에 대한 최대 驚嘆聲입니다. 深深密密도 하거냐 曲曲折折도 하고 重重疊疊도 하거냐 層層區區도 하고 奇奇妙妙도 하거냐 幻幻虛虛도 하신지고. 히히! 저렇게까지 하실 것이 무엇이리. 조화의 好奇가 또한 과하시다는 생각이 납니다. 줄 잡고 줄 잡아도 金剛山의 1만 2천奇를 一覽 全得하도록 一張 全彫해 놓은 것이 萬物草라 할 것입니다. 글세 萬物草는 첫째 金剛山의 목록이라고 하겠읍니다.
사자목이의 右翼은 닿으면 버여질 듯한 날카로운 三尖峰인데 秘宮의 淸淨을 捍衛하는 降魔劍과 같아서 서슬이 과연 푸릅니다. 이것을 요새ㅅ 사람이 三仙岩이라 하지마는 적절하지 못한 이름이며 여긔 반하여 左翼인 一鈍峰은 頭部가 흡사한 인형이요 게다가＜4＞ 험상스러우며 鬼面岩이란 이름이 볼쓰록 善形容인 줄을 감탄할 밖에 없읍니다. 멀리 보나 가까히 보나 영낙없는 鬼面이니 이것은 필시 神域 鎭護의 소임을 맡은 천연 長柱일까 합니다.
鬼面岩 뒤로 벌리어 있는 重重한 叢峯이 萬物草란 것이니 가만히 떼미다보면 어느 돌 하나와 어느 모 하나고 物形 아닌 것이 없고 그것이 둘도 같은 것이 없으며 그 속에는 없는 物形이 없다고 할 만도 합니다. 그나 그 뿐인가 하나를 가지고도 아까 불 적에는 이렇던 것이 있다가는 저러하고 이리 보면은 이러하다가 저리 보기에는 아지어서 일쯕 一瞬만한 定觀이 있지 아니하매 그 神詭 怪幻함이란 이로 이를 길이 없읍니다. 무론 아침에 보던 것이 저 녁의 그것이 아닐 것이며 晴光으로 보는 것이 吟詠의 그것이 아닐 것이니 이 모든 조건을 自乘 又乘한 대변화는 거의 사람의 상상 밖이라 할 것입니다.
아무리 단촐 좋아하는 이라도 萬物草 이야기를 하자면 말이 저절로 번잡스러워질 밖에 없읍니다. 이것은 무엇 같고 저것은 무엇 비슷한 것을 엮으려 하면 저절로 萬有 물품을 죄다 들추게 되매 한 번 입을 떼기만 하면 維摩詰이라도 잔말장이 노릇을 할밖에 없는 것입니다. 옛날 물건의 이름 있는 것은 옛 사람들이 이미 몇 번식 울여 먹었거니와 시체 새 것을 가지고 말할찌라도 위선 뾰족뾰족하기는 봉마다 방송 「엔테나」 같다고도 할 것이요 그것들이 모이어서 一幅의 活畫를 일우기는 도무지가 활동 사진의 영사막과 같다고도 할 것입니다. 그런데 쉬일 새 없이 그리로서 방송되는 것은 우주 靈活의 거륵한 「멜로듸」며 거긔 映現되는 것은 천지 진미의 앗질앗질한 光色입니다.
萬物草는 그 아는 品物의 종목이 많을쓰록 形喩되는 범위의 점점 어수선해짐이 자연한 일입니다. 어대 무엇에고 끌어다 대어지지 아니할 것이 없을만 하기 때문입니다. 희랍의 신화 한  편도 거긔 구경할 것이며 유태의 묵시록 한 판도 거긔 구경할 것이며 인도의 제 천지와 支那의 列仙傳 같은 것은 그 속에서도 작은 한 부분을 일우었다고도 할 것입니다. 「딴테」의 구경한 천상 지하의 모든 광경과 水滸志에 나오는 미인 협객 모든 인물과 春香傳 洪吉童傳 田禹致傳 三說記의 모든 장면이 어느 것 하나도 빼어질 것이 없음니다. 一言으로 잘라 말하면 무릇 천지간의 物形이라는 모든 物形과 또 그것으로써 구성할 수 있는 모든 미관이라는 것은 萬物草의 속에 모주리 들어 있다고 할 것입니다.
오죽해야 과장할 줄 모르는 古人과 상징적 擴大視 허고는 담을 쌓았다 할 조선인도 원체 엄청난 경계를 당하였는지라 긔껏 형언하여 萬物草라는 소리를 하게 되었음니다. 造物主가 천지 만물을 배포하실 적에 설계서와 雛形으로 草 잡으신 것이 이 萬物草니라 합니다. 시방말로 하면 物形美의 全量이요 總覽이라는 뜻일 것입니다. 대저 萬物草는 진실로 金剛山의 목록인 동시에 山岳美의 총목록이며 아울러 물체적 변화의 전 목록입니다.
萬物草는 근래 일본인들이 音相似란 계로 萬物相이라 하지마는 이는 고인의 중에 혹 萬物肖라고 쓴 이가 있은 것과 마찬가지로 古義를 傷하는 일입니다. 相이 불교적으로 雅名이 될 법도 하되 萬物의 草라고 하는 단적 剴절한 맛이 없음을 생각할 것입니다.＜5＞

新萬物草
金剛山의 특색을 가장 단적하게 말하자면 조각적 미의 叢集이라고 할 것인데 金剛山의 이러한 특색을 가장 단적하게 표현한 곳이 어대냐 하면 곳 萬物草의 이러한 특색을 아직 극도로 발휘한 곳이 어느 쪽이냐 하면 그것은 신 萬物草입니다.
사자목이에서 데미다 보는 萬物草란 것은 실상 萬物草의 외부이요 현관이요 또 包裏요 상표같은 것이니 萬物草의 진상 實價를 알려 하면 모롬직이 그 內庭을 보아야 할 것이며 그 堂奧을 보아야 할 것이며 또 그 내용을 살피고 실질을 더듬어야 할 것입니다. 舊 萬物草 곳 외 萬物草가 좋기도 하거니와 內깐에서만 어정거리다가 그만 둘 것 아니라 안 뜰로 웃적 들어서어서 新萬物草의 秘奧를 더듬어 본 뒤가 아니면 萬物草의 진미를 말할 수 없을 것입니다. 잦을 송이로 할 꼬야 고소한 참 맛을 누가 알겠읍니까.
구경하는 이가 흔히 사자목이에서 건너다 보이는 것만을 보고 萬物草 구경을 다 하였다 하며 혹 이것만으로써 萬物草도 별 것 아니라는 말을 하는 이도 있지마는 거죽에 들어난 구萬物草는 요하건대 眞萬物草가 아니며 아무리 너그럽게 말하여도 舊萬物草란 大萬物草 중의 비교적 변변ㅎ지 아니한 작은 일부분일 따름입니다. 舊萬物草로붙어 新萬物草까지 길은 좀 까달롭지 않은 것 아니지마는 萬物草 구경을 왔다가는 新萬物草를 반듯이 보아야 할 것이 金剛山 구경을 왔다가는 萬物草 구경을 아니할 수 없는 것 이상으로 필요 당연한 일입니다. 구 만물에 놀란 이는 참으로 한 번 놀라보기 위하여 또 舊萬物에 자미 들이지 못한 이는 긔어히 萬物草 맛을 알기 위하여 누구나 긔어히 新萬物草까지의 걸음을 아끼지 말 것입니다.
사자목을 넘어서 鬼面岩 밑으로 하여 실개천 졸졸 흐르는 돌 시내 바닥을 밟고서 건너다 보는 萬物草의 영사막을 달겨들어 넙구리에 줌처 끼고 좌편으로 뚫린 좁다란 岩峽으로 하여 우중충한 밖으로 까마아득한 미지의 세계를 찾아 들어갑니다. 격지격지 층단을 일운 돌 바닥과 반뜻 모소리진 바위 빈 양이 한 조각으로나 왼 幅으로나 다 솜씨 있는 대조각인데 다만 한 가지 가고 가면서 부족한 생각을 주는 것은 수분 水素의 비교적 빈약함입니다. 생선탕 맛을 온전히 맛보자면 쇠고기 꿈이를 넣지 말고 긇이어야 한다는 말을 들었더니 아마도 萬物草는 조화가 石味石美만을 오지게 맛보이시려 하는 곳일새 물은 아무쪼록 졸략히 쓰신 양 합니다.
안돌고 지돌고 치받고 들이받아 길은 과연 평순하달 수 없읍니다. 그러나 탁 거치는 것은 도무지 범상한 물건이 아니라 사람으로 치면 西施 楊貴妃요 꽃으로 말하면 목단 해당 같으매 괴로운 그것이 도무지 다 樂입니다. 무덕이 무덕이 미의 군상이요 또 이것이 連環的 長繩的으로 줄다 하였으매 轉瞬마다의 新奇觀이 가쁨을 잊기에 모자랄 것 없음니다. 돌이어 자연미이기에 망정이지 이것이 참으로 저만한 미인 그것이었더면 우리네가 치마ㅅ자락인들 근더려 본다 하였을까 하면 이렇게 무릅을 시친다 허리통을 껴 안는다 함에 다시 한 번 유감한 생각이 납니다.
얼마 가다가 길이 양분하여 이른바 奧萬物草로 가는 길을 右로 비키고 左로 狹路를 취하여 의연히 萬花谷이나 것는 듯한 생각으로 뚫고 들어가고 뚫고 아가기를 합하여 六七리나 ＜6＞ 하면 또한 사자목이란 고개를 넘는데 사자목이란 金剛山에서 우뚝 주춤한 석봉의 鞍部에 흔히 쓰는 이름이지마는 여긔야말로 仰天大吼하는 천연한 猛獅가 고개ㅅ목을 눌어 서서 명실이 뷘틈없이 들어 맞임을 봅니다. 萬物草에도 사자목이 이미 둘이니까 여긔를 안ㅅ사자목이라고 부르는 것도 좋슴니다. 이 사자목이를 올라서 보면 비탈도 여긔 저긔 지고 골작이도 좀 환하게 터지어서 마치 빡빡한 사람의 싹싹한 구석만한 너그러운 맛이 듬니다.
사자목을 넘어 서면 길이 좀 평탄해 지어서 손과 무릅이 비롯오발의 보조 노릇을 면하고 바로 서어서 걸을 수 있게 됩니다. 조곰 나가다가 奧萬物로 가는 제2 岐路가 나아서는데 여긔서 5리라는 標木이 서어 있음을 봅니다. 사자목이로붙어 2 弓地 쯤 들어가면 별안간 석벽이 天城처럼 앞을 가리고 깜앟게 올리어다 보이는 곳에 사람 하나나 갈 만한 구멍이 있어 그 밖에도 한울이 있고 세계가 있음을 엿보이어 줍니다. 이것이 없었던들 긔가 막히었을 일을 생각하면 그 구멍이 내 코ㅅ구멍이란 생각을 할 것입니다. 이때까지 은총으로써 더듬어 들어오게 하신 우리언마는 새삼스레히 안까지는 보일 수 없다 하시는 듯이 여긔 간을 막으신 것을 생각하면 미상불 엄청난 무엇이 그 속에 들어있을 것이 분명 확실하여 궁금ㅅ증이 더럭 나지마는 나는 새 아니고 한참 망단하지 아니할 수 없읍니다. 어떻게 하면 저 밖을 나갈까 하여 휘휘 둘러 볼 때에 살뜻한 一路가 문득 눈에 들어옵니다.

玉女峰頭의 大觀

新萬物草의 구성과 형태는 진실로 威稜하고 통쾌한 남성적의 것입니다. 그런데 그 중에 두 가지 여성적 분자의 섞이었음을 봅니다. 하나는 7分 淨白 3分 嫩황, 地衣 蘇苔가 험상스러을 석면을 쫓아다니면서 곱닿게 곱닿게 단장시키고 옷 입히었음이요 또 하나는 상봉 바로 밑에 옥녀 洗頭盆이라는 香艶한 자취를 셋 식이나 머물러 가지었음니다. 절정의 바위를 안ㅅ고 서향으로 俯瞰하면 100척쯤 直下의 편편한 석면에 똥글앟게 깊이 판 石壺 셋이 나란히 놓이었으되 큰 것은 직경 3척, 작은 것은 1척 여 되는 것이 있으니 大旱 不渴의 물이 고이는데 전하기를 이 玉女峯에 천녀들이 하봉하면 거긔 머리를 감고 간다하여 옥녀 洗頭盆이란 이름이 있다 합니다. 원래 옥녀 洗頭盆이라 하는 것은 支那에 출처를 가지는 것이요 조선에도 명산 處處에 이것이 있으며 또 金剛山에 萬瀑洞 上八潭 기타 여러 곳에 동명 동형의 것이 있으니 이제 그 확실한 古義를 말하기는 어려우나 우리 생각으로는 대개 고대 제전이 무슨 유적일가 함니다. 그것을 가아 봄에는 金剛門까지 나려와서 바위를 끼고 돌아가야 함니다.
洞口에서 玉女峯頭까지 10리를 잡으나 실상 그 반쯤 되는 里程이요 길이 험난함으로 올라가기에는 2시간이나 걸리되 나려 오기에는 30분이면 족합니다. 金剛문에서 조곰 나려오다가 左折하여 羽衣峯의 鞍部를 넘어서 가면 이른바 奧萬物草로 하게 되는 떼 근래 이 방면의 탐승도 차차 성행합니다. 奧萬物草는 新舊 양 萬物草에서 보던 五峯山과 勢至峯을 연결하는 山背이니 좌우의 峰嶂이 截斷的으로 劖刻削秀하여 奇觀 壯聸 거의 應接 불가할 지경이며 그 정상은 무론 新萬物보담도 높고 동해의 물이 거의 발 끝에 채일만치 임박해 있음니다. 길은 좀 험하지마는 이리로 하여 萬物草 全洞을 遍踏하고 長葥港으로 빠아지어 나가야 비릇오 萬物草 구경을 온전히 하였다 할 것입니다.
附記할 것은 萬物草 앞으로 하여 지나가는 外金剛으로서 內金剛 들어가는 別路입니다. 萬物草 앞에서 바로 올라가는 大路는 五峯山의 鞍部인 溫井嶺(2,600척)을 넘어서 新豐里, 細洞으로 하여 金剛川을 끼고서 末輝里 (北倉),  塔巨里를 거치어서 長安寺까지 통한 것이니 밀림 幽徑과 奇花 異香에 겸하여 나무틈으로 동해가 갸웃갸웃하는 것을 보면서 무한히 친화한 기분 중으로 행진하는 溫井嶺 넘이는 金剛山 중에서도 가장 취미 있는 통로의 하나라 하는 것입니다. 또 細洞 지나 쑥밭이란 데서 남으로 꺽이어 들어가면 永郞, 凌虛 양 峯間으로 떨어지어 나려오는 골짝에 金傳洞이 있어 신라 金傳大王 陵이란 것이 있고 또 그 우에는 九成洞이라 하여＜10＞ 예로붙어 萬聲瀑같은 泉瀑의 勝으로 들린 곳이 있으니 교통 관계로 이 방면은 심상한 探勝客에게 아직 閑却되나 金剛山 전차가 이쪽으로 개†µ한 뒤에는 응당 명성이 웃적 著聞할 것입니다.＜11＞
