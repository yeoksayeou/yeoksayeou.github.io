Article Title	肉親 (Blood Relation)
Author	李暎燮
Article Type	소설 (Novel)

Blood Relation
						Yi Yŏng-sŏp

Even after getting off at N Station, his wife's house was still nearly 20 *ri* away. After following the railway embankment for a while and crossing a bumpy path through the middle of the rice paddies, one emerged onto a plain. Beyond a mountain and ridges that seemed set adrift in the middle of the plain, nestled in a concave mountainside, decaying thatched houses clung like a multitude of warts, spread out like oyster shells on the uneven slope. In front of the village, there were neither proper rice paddies nor dry fields, just three or four sparse plots where one might see grains of rice only if there was a drought. Beyond this, it became almost wasteland. Every year, floods would sweep in and cover everything with a layer of silty mud (멩게흙). Only the reeds growing profusely in this silty mud seemed to have a bountiful year. The villagers nearby, however, couldn't touch a single blade of grass from this farming that grew by itself without exploiting their own backs; they could only earn a *doe* of millet by working as day laborers cutting reeds. This was the hearsay Namsu had picked up when passing through here years ago.

Now, it was winter, the time when the river across freezes over, and the weather was harsh and forbidding. On this indescribably rough plain, it was impossible to tell if the haze on the distant horizon ahead was wind-blown snow or sand blowing from the riverside. Pedestrians had ceased. Nothing stirred on the ground. The only living thing was the wind. Like a pack of savage beasts, it roared through the air—*whoosh, whoom*—sweeping the silty mud from the ground and plastering it onto Namsu's face, then rushed towards the mountain bend over there, whirling and soaring back into the sky. Namsu, walking forward, turned his back for a moment to avoid this violence. A magpie that had built its nest and was hunkered down in the zelkova and acorn trees at the village entrance tried to fly towards the entrance but was caught in the gust. It flapped its wings for a long while trying to escape before finally retreating into its nest. Then again, a biting, flesh-tearing wind brushed past his ear like a whistle, then suddenly, like a startled colt, buffeted Namsu’s chest. Namsu resisted stubbornly, staggering sideways as if about to take a step back. He bent his upper body in half, like an ox trudging along, pushing through the headwind, and put one foot forward. Nature unleashed its terrifying violence as if to swallow the one living creature that had appeared on this plain, but this creature named Namsu seemed to say, "No matter what you do, I will live."

"This damned wind is acting crazy!" he muttered under his breath. Regret surfaced—why did he have to leave on such a day? But the feeling of his body growing colder and colder, wrapped tightly, <61> and the sensation underfoot of the dry reed tips breaking with a winter sound, gradually transformed into a sentimental emotion. Finally, the image of his wife, sitting with their sick child lying before her, weeping helplessly, floated before his eyes, becoming unbearably painful.

—If you were any good, couldn't you feed and raise your own wife and child? Instead, you've dumped them in someone else's house to get sick and die!— A good-for-nothing, disloyal man shirking his responsibilities! —You possess nothing needed to sustain your own life.—
When such scorn arose from a corner of his mind, he squared his shoulders resolutely and strode forward recklessly. Along with this, self-recrimination, derision, and heartache for his wife erupted all at once, igniting his emotions. It had been over a year since he had properly visited. He had dredged up all his wife's faults, used them as excuses, made all sorts of harsh accusations, and finally abandoned her in her parents' home. To feel such emotions now, facing this situation today—more than remorse for wronging his wife, more than a concern for face based on some worn-out sense of responsibility—thinking of how he had been ruthlessly trampled by the harsh reality and bankrupted without any capital made his teeth grind with rising fury. Even so, he should have maintained control over his own mind and body, yet the sight of himself, unable to resist worthless temptations, floundering and being dragged along, was so repulsive he wanted to plunge a knife into his own chest. Had he only just realized this? No. One moment he would clench his fists as if ready to smash this damn world, but the next, when the threat of needing to plant his feet on the ground and sustain his life stared him down and grabbed the back of his neck, Namsu would collapse without even struggling once. Namsu didn't know what to do about this. One thing he wanted was either to bang his head against the ground until he died or, if there were people before him, to bite them indiscriminately like a mad dog; perhaps then his heart would feel relieved.

"Whatever happens, I have to save him first. What if he dies?" Thinking this again, it seemed laughable. Human blood ties are unfathomable. Hearing his child was dying, he had spent several sleepless nights weeping, then done all sorts of embarrassing things trying to borrow money. Now, trudging along, trying to understand his own heart, he found it incomprehensible. Lost in these formless, surging thoughts, Namsu forgot both the cold and the wind. He was startled back to reality by dogs barking fiercely and running out at him; he had already crossed the ten-*ri* long plain and reached the house of Japanese immigrants. Built like a dugout into the slope, exposed to the winds from all directions, without even a fence, the house had wildly grown fuzzy weeds (보푸라) standing bare and straggly like worn-out bush-clover brooms, their branches whipping about, adding to the clamor. Despite the cold, a couple, likely the owners of the house, covered in dark rags, were threshing rice stalks. They glanced briefly at Namsu, then sharply called back their dog, which was barking as if seeking its masters’ backing, and returned to their threshing, heads bowed. The man had a sharp, dark face, his eyebrows drooping down to his eyelids, making him look like a monkey, only his eyes glittering. The way he erratically hit the <62> rice stalks with the stick in his right hand was typical of these people, but given the weather, it seemed he was threshing the cold rather than the rice. The woman, however, wore a black towel with white letters on her head. As she gathered the stalks the man had threshed, bundled them, and carried them behind the wall to stack, her hands trembled uncontrollably like a fly's legs (곰배파리) from the cold, a pitiful sight. Though her whole face wasn't visible under the towel, her rounded chin and firmly closed lips looked quite pretty. When the man mumbled something, she giggled and started tying the stalks piled behind him, but her hands were too numb to work properly, so she called the man and chattered something. The man turned his head sharply, "Oh, a guest (손닝이)... You can't do it with one hand (히데 데기루 몬쟈네야)... Need to put more effort (못도 넹이레데야라나끼야 다메야)..." He snatched the bundle from the woman's hands, tightened it firmly, "There... See (호라... 이이쟈로...)" and tossed it aside. The woman, seemingly embarrassed, gave a slight, graceful bow (깔씬하더니) and started gathering more stalks. Namsu kept his eyes on the couple as he passed the house. Even after rounding the mountain bend, he glanced back once more. Jealousy tugged at Namsu's heart. What about his own situation? What about himself, tearing his hair out every evening from hunger and loneliness? But the thought that in a few hours, warmth might flow into the frozen depths of his own heart made him suddenly long to see his wife. Unbearably long. His wife, who, when he visited some time ago, had buried her face in his shoulder and sobbed—his wife, who, when he said he was leaving after less than three days, had pleaded with him to stay just one more day—she had become terribly gaunt from hardship. Her face, which used to be quite pretty when tidied up just a year ago, was now worn down by hunger and worry; three or four wrinkles creased the corners of her mouth when she smiled, and a scar (숭) below her chin, once hidden and barely visible, was now clearly apparent, paining Namsu's heart. His arrival had been so unexpected for her. Flustered and agitated, she had spilled the water she brought onto Namsu's lap, her face flushed red as she darted away. Or in the kitchen, coaxing the child, "Look, Father is here. Go in and bow. Come on, be good," and when the child resisted, "No, scary. He scowls," she ended up spanking his back until he cried—that wife's heart at that time—

When the two sat facing each other for the first time, the wife gazed at Namsu's face for a long, long time. A lonely smile, full of affection and delight despite her haggard face, flickered across her lips. Then, perhaps overcome by her troubled heart, she sighed,
"I don't resent you. It's my own fault for being worthless... So, no matter how much I suffer, as long as your heart doesn't change, and you come down occasionally like this to show your face, that's enough for me. —Ah, everything in the world is tiresome..."
And the day before he left, the wife had told Namsu, "Listen, when you go back up, please send just enough material for the child's trousers, <63> and since your clothes have no cotton padding, buy one *won*'s worth, and also buy that toy gun. Please send it tomorrow. Please don't forget." The wife, well aware of Namsu's poor household, couldn't bring herself to ask for anything for herself, only worrying about the child and Namsu's clothes. She had mentioned it several times in letters, but Namsu had simply forgotten. Or rather, he hadn't wanted to trouble his mind with such things. Especially the toy gun. During his last visit, finding one day tedious and stifling, he had borrowed an air gun from the village and set out magnanimously to hunt birds. As Namsu loitered around the fence, he glanced back and discovered his six-year-old child, shivering pale blue with cold like a little beggar, obstinately following him. Whether he followed to watch the bird hunting or simply out of affection for his father, Namsu couldn't tell, but at that moment, the thought wasn't unpleasant.
"Go back inside. I'll catch a bird and bring it to you."
Namsu said this, pitying the shivering child. But the child, usually shooed away like a caterpillar, never allowed near, was suddenly addressed so affectionately. Overjoyed, the child rushed towards Namsu.
"No, I don't want to. I want to watch Daddy catch birds." He shook his head stubbornly, then planted himself firmly in front, frowning and staring intently. The child, unsure what to do, pleaded in a whining, affectionate tone, "Daddy, if you catch a bird, you'll give it to me, right? Huh? Give it to me?"
Trying to soften Namsu's fierce temper, the child looked up at Namsu's stern face. Namsu was suddenly moved to pity and turned his head to hide his tears. "Of course, I'll give it to you. I'll catch many and roast them all for you," he said. And surging regret followed.
"Why has my temper become like this? What crime has the child committed... Why torment your innocent wife and child after being trampled by the world?"
Namsu cried out inwardly and looked down again at the child's appearance. His frog-like trousers, patched all over with scraps of black rags, shone slickly with dried mucus and grime. On his feet, one shoe was an adult's rubber shoe, the other so worn that his toes peeked out. With his hands thrust under the collar of his jacket, the child stood waiting for Namsu's command. The sight cut Namsu's heart like a knife.
"Alright, want to come with me? Won't you be cold?"
Namsu stroked the child's head and took his hand.
"No! If I watch you catch birds, I won't be cold. —I'll go!"
Holding Namsu's hand, the child struggled to keep his balance, nearly falling forward as one shoe dragged, yet he chattered continuously,
"There are birds in that fence! Shoot the gun."
Or,
"Catch magpies too. Magpies always come to the hill behind our house."
But Namsu's replies didn't seem to capture the child's interest much, and eventually, the child fell silent. <64> They wandered around the village without catching a single bird and returned. From then on, however, the child became extremely fond of the gun. Back home, afraid to ask Namsu directly, he pestered his mother, begging her to let him just touch it, until she relayed the message to Namsu.
"Hey, could you let him see that gun for a bit? He's pestering me to death."
"What next? Why the gun?"
"He went out with you earlier to catch birds or whatever, and since coming back, he's been whining to play with it."
"No. It belongs to someone else."
"Still, just let him touch it for a moment. He's just throwing a tantrum. Look at him over there."
"What do you mean by always giving in to things like that? What will become of him if his spoiled behavior grows?"
Rebuffed by Namsu and embarrassed, the wife went out, tried reasoning with the child, then scolded him. But when he still wouldn't listen and kept whining and crying, she dragged the child back in.
"Look at this. What should I do? He just keeps crying like this."
She presented the child, one arm covering his face, the other pulled by his mother, sobbing as he stood. Looking sideways at him, Namsu said,
"Why are you doing this? Stop it. Stop crying now."
When the wife pushed aside the hand covering his face, his smudged face, streaked clean by tears, was unbearable to look at. Namsu's temper suddenly flared.
"Why are you crying, you brat?"
He was about to shout, but abruptly remembered his earlier resolution never to unleash his fierce temper. He took the child from his wife's grasp, wiped his face with a towel, and coaxed him,
"Don't cry. Crying isn't manly. Stop it. I'll let you shoot the gun."
The wife stood watching the two, smiling faintly.
"That rascal is getting spoiled," she said.
Namsu thought inwardly:
—Even if he's young, liking a gun isn't necessarily a bad trait. He shouldn't end up weak, foolish, and shy like me. A gun is a weapon. Instilling the need for and concept of weapons, even in a child, builds courage. Strength is what clears one's path.—
Thinking this, he led the child outside. He drew a circle with ink on paper and stuck it low on the wall by the sarangchae gate. Namsu stood three or four strides back and demonstrated hitting the target. Then he led the child to the target, pointed out the hole, explained that hitting the very center was best, and then handed him the gun. He taught him the posture and aiming method, then stood him alone and told him to try shooting. But the child, though mimicking the posture as instructed, staggered forward under the gun's weight and couldn't seem to fire.
"Keep your body still and pull the trigger," Namsu urged repeatedly from a distance. He had no expectation the child could hit the target; just firing the gun himself would be a complete success.
"As if he could shoot that thing," the wife said, doubled over laughing.
"Come on, pull the trigger. Hurry up!" Namsu prompted.
Somehow, the child managed to fire once (startled by the recoil, he dropped the gun and stood wide-eyed, unsure what to do), hitting the sarangchae door, completely off target. Namsu burst out laughing. The wife laughed until tears came, eventually clutching her sides, her mouth just opening and closing. The child, satisfied nonetheless, started boasting,
"I'm gonna ask Father to buy me a gun like this. When I know how to shoot well, I'll catch birds."
But that was the end of it then. Later that evening, when Namsu returned from the village meeting hall, chaos had erupted inside the house. The child was huddled in a corner of the room, unable to get up, just crying. The wife was furious, threatening the child as if ready to kill him.
"What's wrong? Huh? What's the commotion?"
Namsu, baffled, looked back and forth between his wife and the child.
"What do you mean, what's wrong? You teach the child such useless things, and he nearly put out another kid's eye!" the wife retorted sharply like a wasp.
"Ah, so he was playing with the gun again."
"What else? Is this brat going to ask for a gun again? A snot-nosed kid acting high and mighty asking for a gun—looking like a little beggar, he has the nerve to ask for a gun."
She gripped the switch again and lunged towards the child. The child let out a dying cry and scrambled towards Namsu. Namsu shielded the child behind him, dodging the incoming switch.
"Let's hear the story first. What happened?"
But the wife, ignoring Namsu's words, unable to contain her fury, panted heavily and desperately tried to snatch the child from behind Namsu.
"What are you doing? Just tell me! What happened?"
"What happened? Today I'm going to beat this child to death!"
Unable to wrest the child from Namsu, she ended up striking wildly with the switch wherever the child's body peeked out from behind Namsu. Namsu, obstinately pushing his wife away and blocking the switch, took a hard blow on the back of his hand. The wife, shocked by her careless mistake, turned pale.
"Oh! What have I done?"
Embarrassed and ashamed, her vehemence subsided, and she didn't know what to do.
"Why are you losing your mind like this? Tell me what happened before you hit him!"
Namsu's voice suddenly rose. His explosive temper took on the ferocious posture of a tyrant before his wife. But he held back. In his eyes, his wife looked too weak. She seemed too fragile to endure violence. He merely frowned and glanced down briefly at the back of his hand.
"Are you badly hurt?"
The wife's fierceness towards the child melted into remorse towards Namsu. With a face expressing genuine apology, she placed her hand on the back of his.
"It's alright. Just tell me the story you were about to tell."
Namsu, still suppressing his displeasure, gently pulled his hand away from hers and threw out a curt reply. The wife remained silent, her lips just twitching. A moment of silence passed.
"Whose child was it, anyway?"
"Would you know if I told you? It's the boy named Chŏmswae."
"Wasn't he badly hurt?"
"Hardly! The iron pellet grazed his face and got stuck in his outer ear."
Namsu glared fiercely down at the child. The wife seized the opportunity to report:
After Namsu left, the child, seizing the chance (얼시구나), took the gun from the wall closet where it hung and gathered his friends (including the injured Chŏmswae, apparently). Bragging as if he had accomplished something great, he made a fuss about target practice. The wife, in the next room absorbed in rushing to finish Namsu's clothes, had no idea he was playing with the gun.
"Then, realizing it was late for dinner, I put away my sewing and came out to find that commotion. I was so afraid he'd broken someone else's property that I quickly took it back and returned it, then went to draw water."
"As I was coming back with the water, Chŏmswae's mother was in the yard, shouting at the top of her lungs. Not knowing what was happening, I went in to find Chŏmswae crying with blood on his face, and his mother yelling all sorts of vicious things at our child, shaking her fist. Then she saw me and lunged, screaming at me to bring her child back to life. What was I supposed to do?"
Hearing this from his wife, Namsu turned his head and looked down at the child again. The child, as if dead, barely breathing, leaned his head against the door, spitting on his finger and twisting it in a hole in the papered window, huddled down.
"So then, where was he at that time?"
The pain from the blow on his hand still lingered, and his unpleasant mood was fueled by his wife's report. His voice trembled with renewed anger.
"That brat, was he still holding the gun? Was he just standing there trembling, unable even to cry because he was scared stiff? —The little monster... Anyway, when I asked what happened, the children who were watching said that Chŏmswae had taunted him, saying things like 'Your father doesn't even know how to catch birds' and 'He just borrows other people's things to play like that.' That apparently started the fight. —At first, our child just listened quietly, but when Chŏmswae finally tore up the target stuck on the wall, he just lunged at him, pointed the gun at his face, and fired. What do you think happened then? As Chŏmswae fell down screaming, our child apparently kept hitting him with the gun he was still holding."
The wife paused, glared at the child again, and <67> unaware of Namsu's mood, snapped,
"That brat is going to kill someone someday. How can a young child <67> have such a temper?"
"Just finish the story. Stop the useless talk," Namsu shouted. The wife, finally seeming to realize, looked up at Namsu's face, which looked like thunder was about to strike, as if regretting that her words had provoked his temper.
"So Chŏmswae's mother came running, and that's what happened," she finished, closing her mouth. Namsu forgot everything else and leaped up. His hand grabbed the child's nape, and he flung him towards the upper part of the room like an object. The child didn't even have time to scream before his head hit the wall cabinet with a thud! He tumbled down, unable to make a sound, just kicking his legs. The wife, witnessing this terrifying scene, sat with her mouth agape, stunned. Then, fire gradually kindled in her eyes, and fury towards Namsu for his senseless violence—the terrifying power of shared blood—made her tremble like an aspen tree. Eventually, her lips turned indigo-blue, and her face became deathly pale like white paper. But she said nothing. Namsu, for his part, leaned against the wall and closed his eyes.
"Die! You deserve to die!"
This was the wife's cry after some time. Only then did the child let out a wail. The two adults remained silent. The evening sunlight, which had been shining brightly through the back window, suddenly vanished, plunging the room into darkness. Still, the two said nothing. Only the child wept pitifully, unable to get up, just rolling on the floor. The room became completely dark. The child stopped rolling and lay still. Still enough to suspect he was dead...
But Namsu's chest began to stir. An ache gnawed at his insides. Finally, a torment arose as if tearing him apart. He banged his head once against the back wall—*thump*—and opened his eyes. The wife, still composed, lit the oil lamp. When their eyes met, their inner feelings aligned. Suddenly, the wife collapsed forward. She let out sobs she tried hard to stifle. Even the child, thought dead until now, cried out, "Mommy!" An unknown force seemed to possess Namsu as he rushed to the upper part of the room and embraced the child. An even more unbearable agony, like his heart was being squeezed, struck him. This time, he pulled his wife into his embrace too. The wife sobbed harder. Hearing his mother's weeping, the child cried louder. Namsu bit his lip, then, unable to bear it, rubbed his face against the child's face and the wife's face, weeping with them.

The next day, Namsu told the child,
"When Father goes to Seoul, I'll buy you a gun. Okay? Good?"
Hearing the word "gun," the child exclaimed,
"Yeah! Gun! When?" He ran to his mother to boast,
"Mommy, I'm getting a gun..." to which she clicked her tongue, "You brat, still talking about guns?"
Thus, the day before Namsu left, the wife pleaded, "Forget everything else, but please don't forget the gun."
However, after returning [to Seoul], a month passed, then Lunar New Year came and went, and Namsu forgot not only the gun but even the child's trouser material. Occasionally, the thought occurred, but where would he get that kind of money—Namsu, who couldn't even feed himself... <68>
The wife sent several more letters mentioning "trouser material, gun, and cotton padding." But Namsu, receiving such letters, had nothing to say and didn't reply. Exasperated, his wife stopped mentioning it. Another month passed. Then, a week ago, the wife suddenly sent word that pierced Namsu's heart: the child was gravely ill, asking him to come visit. Still, Namsu assumed he would get better soon, and besides, he had no money to go down, so he remained indifferent. But a few days ago, a telegram stating the child's life was in danger landed in Namsu's hands, and simultaneously, his heart suddenly sank as if jolted awake. What happened after that? He barely slept, thinking only of money. The suspicion even arose that his senseless violence might have caused internal injury and be killing the child. If so, the thought that the child might die without ever getting the gun he loved so much tormented him. The next day, skipping breakfast, he rushed around everywhere and finally managed to scrape together the money. Now, at last, he was on his way down, trudging along.

"I bought the gun you like, but please, just don't die," he muttered inwardly, weeping. By the time he reached the riverside, it was already dusk. On the wide, vast river, boat shadows had vanished. The wind had calmed somewhat, but the remaining waves (놀 波) surged, forming sharp, long angles, creating patches of white foam here and there. The water, having absorbed the deep cold, undulated like heavy, leaden masses, resembling muddy floodwaters. The night wind rolling over these waves pierced the flesh. *Swash— Swash—* From the middle of the river came the sound of waves, writhing like living creatures, crashing and breaking against the sandbanks. This sound, extremely monotonous yet vast and deep, also echoed from the river's center. *Swash— Swash—*
The night seemed to deepen with this sound; the mountains on the opposite bank were invisible, only whitish shadows visible on the water's surface. The silence of the waves after the storm was an extremely solemn, stark desolation. Namsu grew increasingly desperate, his hands and feet numb, losing sensation from the cold.
"Boatman, take me across!"
In this deserted place, Namsu knew shouting was futile, that no one across could hear, yet unconsciously, he stretched his neck and yelled. No response. He called out a couple more times. Silence and desolation swallowed the sounds without a trace. A different thought suddenly struck Namsu, and he began searching up and down the riverbank. If there was even one boat, he intended to take it himself. He wasn't entirely unskilled with water, and if misfortune struck and he drowned, it wouldn't feel like such a great loss.
"But you—just don't die."
His heart was incomprehensible. The thought of the child dying kept recurring, making him frantic like a cow whose first calf has been taken away. He rushed up and down the riverbank, darting back and forth. But there wasn't even a shadow of a boat. He went further than before, but there was no finding one. Namsu's frantic steps traced diagonal lines across the sandy shore. Where the last flood had gouged out deep hollows, he leaped across recklessly. Finally, his eyes deceived by the darkness, he mistook flat ground for a hollow and jumped. At such times, he laughed inwardly, *hmph*. Unknowingly, Namsu's body plunged into a pit (*thump*). Water came up to his waist. As his feet sank deeper into the sticky mud (개 흙) at the bottom, he thrashed his arms, splashing water (물탕). Trying to get out... But the bundle (뭉텡이) containing the gun and trouser material clutched in one hand, he held onto it even tighter, his voice crying out,
"Just don't you die!"
The night continued to deepen with the sound *Swash — Swash*. Across the river, a lamp flickered brightly, then blinked out. - End - <69>

**Summary**

The story "Blood Relation" follows Namsu, a man consumed by poverty and guilt, as he journeys through a harsh winter landscape to visit his critically ill young son, whom he had previously neglected and abandoned at his wife's parental home. Haunted by memories of his past cruelty, particularly a violent outburst towards the child, and fueled by a desperate, rekindled paternal love symbolized by the toy gun he finally bought as promised, Namsu faces the unforgiving elements and his own internal turmoil. The narrative climaxes with his desperate attempt to cross a dark, forbidding river, highlighting his powerlessness against both nature and his circumstances, yet driven by the singular plea for his son's survival. For colonial history, the story offers a stark portrayal of rural Korean poverty, the difficult living conditions possibly exacerbated by colonial policies, the protagonist's profound sense of impotence and despair reflecting broader societal frustrations, and a brief glimpse into the presence and lives of Japanese settlers in the Korean countryside.

肉親
						李暎燮
N역에 내려서도 안해의 집은 거의 20 리나 되었다. 철도뚝을 한참 끼고 나려가다가 논ㅅ 가운대로 울묵 줄묵 혹이 돋힌 길을 건너서면 벌판 한가운대 떠다 놓은 듯한 산과 등셍이 넘에로 오목한 산ㅅ기슭을 끼고 쓸어져가는 초가ㅅ집들이 뭇 사마귀처럼 다닥다닥 붙어 엇비슷한 산비탈에 굴딱지처럼 쪽 깔렸다. 마을 앞으로는 논도 아니요 밭도 아닌 가물이나 들어야 벼 알갱이를 구경한다는 전장들이 뚜염뚜염 서너 군대 백이고 이것만 지내 놓으면 그 다음은 황무지가 되다 싶이 년년히 닥치는 홍수에 한 뚜게씩 멩게흙을 쓸어다 덮고 이 멩게 흙 속에서 길길이 자라는 갈때만이 풍년이 뜬다는 이 벌판에 간혹 요행수나 바라는 마음으로 갈ㅅ대밭을 뻬집고 슬몃이 메물ㅅ되나 뿌려 두면 수가 좋아야 장국ㅅ그릇이나 훌젹꺼려 보지 그렇지 않으면 떡잎도 채 피기 전에 송두리채 큰 물이 집어 삼키고 마는 것이 이 벌판의 년중행사다. 그러나 갈ㅅ대만은 년년히 풍년이 들어 여긔서 나는 소출이 적지 않지만 앞 마을 사람들은 자긔네 등ㅅ골을 빼지 않는 제절로 되는 이 농사에는 풀 한 포기 건드리지 못하고 다만 갈 빌 때 날품팔이나 해서 좁쌀ㅅ 되나 얻어먹는다는 것이 이 곳을 지나는 남수의 년전에 왔을 때의 들은 풍월이다.
지금은 겨을 중에도 강물 건너 집히는 때 날새 좇아 험살궂다. 말할 수 없이 거칠은 이 벌판에 앞으로 긴 지평선 우에 자욱한 것은 바람꽃인지 강ㅅ가의 모래가 나림인지 분간할 수가 없다. 행인도 그치었다. 땅 우에 꿈틀대는 것은 하나도 없다. 오직 살아있는 것은 바람이다. 이것이 무슨 사나운 짐숭떼처럼 공중을 윙 - 웅 하고 울고 지나가면서 땅 우에 멩게 흙을 쓸어다가 남수의 얼굴에 답새고는 저 편 산 모롱이로 치달려서는 회호리쳐 다시 공중으로 올아 솟는다. 남수는 앞으로 걸어 나가다가 뒤로 돌아서서 이 폭력을 잠깐 피한다. 마을 앞 느틔나무 상수리에다 집을 짓고 들어 엎듸었던 까치 한 마리가 동구를 바라고 날다가 이 삽품에 쌓여 한참동안 벗어나려고 날개를 치다가 집 속으로 들어가고 만다. 이러다가도 살을 물어뜯는 매운 바람이 귀ㅅ가에 휘파람처럼 슷치어 지나가다가 금시루 놀랜 망아지같이 남수의 복장을 안군다. 남수는 완강히 저항하면서 자칫하면 뒤ㅅ걸음을 칠 것 같이 옆으로 비실비실한다. 그는 뜸베질하는 소 모양으로 반 허리를 굽흐리어 앞으로 들어받는 바람을 뚫고 발ㅅ길을 내어 놓는다. 자연은 이 벌판에 나타난 생물 하나를 집어 삼킬 듯이 무서운 폭녁을 내이지만 남수라는 이 생물은 그래도 네가 암만 이래도 나는 살아보고야 말겠다」 하는 것처럼.
「이 빌어먹을 바람이 지랄하지 안나!」 하는 욕설을 입 속으로 중얼거린다. 하필 이런 날에 떠났던고 하는 후회도 난다. 그러나 자긔 몸을 휩싸고 점점 얼어 올아오는 몸뚱이와＜61＞ 발 밑에 갈ㅅ대 끄트럭이가 겨울 소리를 내고 부러지는 촉감은 점점 쎈치멘탈한 감정으로 변하여 나종에는 자긔가 지금 찾아가는 안해가 병 앓는 어린 것을 앞에 누이고 어떻게 할 줄 몰아 울고만 앉았는 꼴이 눈 앞에 떠올아 견딀 수가 없이 괴로워진다.
- 오작 잘 생기었으면 제 계집과 자식을 먹여 기르지를 못하고 남의 집 구석에다 처박어두고 병들어 죽게 하느냐 - 못생긴 놈 책임을 저버리는 의리 없는 놈 - 너는 네 목숨을 부지할만한 아무 것도 갖후지 못하였나니라. -
이렇게 마음 저 편 구석에서 조소가 일 때는 분연히 어깨를 축켜 올이고 앞으로 발ㅅ길을 함부로 내어 놓는다. 이럼을 딸아 저를 원망하는 비웃는 또한 안해에게 대한 마음 아픈 생각이 한꺼번에 터저나와 그의 감정을 불붙여 놓았다. 일년이나 넘어서야 변변히 가 보지도 않고 말하자면 안해의 흠쩜을 있는대로 끄집어 내어 이것을 핑계 삼아가지고 별별 악착한 소리를 다 한 끝에 종래는 친정 구석에다 처박어 둔 오늘날 지금 당하여 이러한 감정을 느낀다는 것은 안해에게 잘못한 것을 뉘우친다는 회한보다도 또한 체면을 생각하는 다 낡은 책임관보다도 넘우나 험살궂은 현실에서 무참하게 짓밟힘을 당하고 밑천도 없이 파산을 당한 것을 생각하면 이가 갈리도록 분ㅅ길이 치밀었다. 이렇다 할찌라도 제 정신과 몸뚱이는 그래도 제 것을 가지고 있어야 할텐데 오히려 것지도 않은 유혹에 휘어날 줄을 모르고 허위적대면서 질질 끌려가는 꼴이란 칼로 제 가슴을 푹 찔어 웁이고 싶도록 보기가 싫다. 그러면 이것을 이제야 깨달았느냐 하면 그렇지도 않다. 이놈의 세상을 하고 금방 때려 부실 듯이 주먹을 부루쥐었다가도 땅 우에 발을 듿고 서서 목숨을 부지한다는 위협이 눈을 부릅뜨고 뒤통수를 웅켜잡을 때 한 번 버르적대 보지도 못하고 깜으러지고 마는 남수다. 이것을 어떻게 하면 좋으냐 하는 것을 남수는 모른다. 한 가지 하고 싶은 것은 머리를 쾅쾅 땅에다 부듸처 죽던지 그렇지 아니하면 눈 앞에 사람이 있다면 미친 개처럼 함부로 물어 뜯던지 해야 마음이 시원할 것 같다.
「아무턴 살려놓고 볼 일이다. 그게 죽어서야」 하고 다시 생각한 즉 웃읍기도 하다. 사람의 피ㅅ줄이란 알 수 없는 것이다. 어린 것이 죽게 되었다니까 몇 날 잠을 못 자고 밤을 새워가며 운 끝에 돈을 변통하려 별별 얼굴 아픈 짓을 다 하여 지금 터덜거리고 내려가는 자긔 마음을 스스로 헤아려 봐도 알 수 없는 것이다. 남수는 부정형된 이러한 치받히는 생각에 추위도 바람도 다 잊어버리고 것다가 개가 함부로 짖고 내닷는 바람에 눈을 들었을 때는 벌서 십 리나 되는 긴 벌판을 지내 놓고 일본 사람의 이민의 집 앞에 다달았다. 이 팔풍 바지에 울타리도 없이 토굴 모양으로 쌓아 올려 지은 집으로 길차게 자란 보푸라가 모진 바람에 다 닳아빠진 싸리 비ㅅ자루 모양으로 벍어 벗고 늘어 서서 가지를 휘두루는 것이 더욱 소란스럽다. 이 추운대도 이 집 주인인 듯한 두 내외가 싷검한 누데기를 뒤어쓰고 벼ㅅ대를 털다가 남수를 한 번 힐끗 바라보고 녁성해 주기나 바라는 것처럼 제 주인을 돌아보며 짖어대는 개를 소리를 뻑 질러 불러 들이고는 여전히 고개를 숙이고 벼ㅅ대를 턴다. 사내는 뾰족한 얼굴이 샣감한데다가 눈섭이 눈시울까지 내리덮어 마치 원숭이 모양으로 눈만 반짝반짝하는 것이라던지 올은 손에 쥐인 막대로＜62＞ 벼ㅅ집을 방정맞게 따리는 것은 이 사람들의 흔한 본보기지만 날새가 이러하니 벼ㅅ집을 터는 것이 아니라 추위를 터는 듯도 싶다. 그러나 게집은 머리에 검은 글ㅅ자 백인 수건을 쓰고 사내가 털어 논 벼ㅅ집을 묶어 담벽 ㅅ 뒤로 옮겨다 쌓고 돌아올 적에는 추위에 못 견듸어 두 손을 앞으로 곰배파리 모양으로 발발 떠는 것이 애삭하여 볼 수가 없다. 수건 밑에 가리어 얼굴 전체는 잘 보이지 않으나 동그스름한 턱 우에 꼭 담은 입술이 꽤 입부장스럽게 생기었다. 사내가 무엇이라고 웅윌댄 즉 깨득깨득 웃으며 사내의 등 뒤에 쌓아 논 벼ㅅ집을 묵다가 손이 곱아 제대로 안되는지 고만 사내를 불어 무엇이라고 쫑쫑댄다. 그런즉 사내는 고개를 홱 돌려 「오메 손닝이 히데 데기루 몬쟈네야... 못도 넹이레데야라나끼야 다메야...」
하고 게집의 손에서 벼ㅅ단을 앗어서 꽉꽉 조르더니 「호라... 이이쟈로...」하고 옆으로 집어 내던진다. 게집은 무안한 듯이 허리를 깔씬하더니 다시 벼ㅅ집을 긁어 모은다. 남수는 집 앞을 지나올 동안 이 두 부부에게서 눈을 떠나 보내지 않았다. 산 모퉁이를 돌아서서도 또 한 번 힐끗 돌아보았다. 질투가 일 만치 남수의 마음을 집어다렸다. 자긔를 돌아볼 때는 어떤가? 저녁마다 주림과 외로움에 머리를 쥐어뜯던 자긔를 돌아볼 때는 어떤가. 그러나 몇 시간 후면 얼어붙은 자긔 마음 밑 바닥에도 따뜻함이 흐르려니 생각할 때는 갑작이 안해가 보고 싶다. 참을 수 없이 보고 싶다. 얼마 전에 내려갔을 때 얼굴을 남수의 어깨에다 비비며 느껴 울던 안해가 - 사흘도 채 못 되어 떠난다니까 제발 하로만 더 묵어 가라고 봇채던 안해가 - 그는 말할 수 없이 고생살이에 여웠었다. 일년 전까지도 얼굴을 가다듬고 나면 꽤 이뿌던 얼굴이 주림과 근심에 바서저 입 가장자리에 서너 줄 주름이 웃을 때마다 접히고 턱 아레 숨어 잘 보이지도 않던 숭이 완연히 나타난 것은 남수의 마음을 아프게 하였다. 남수가 자긔를 보려 내려오기는 넘우도 뜻밖인지라. 마음이 달떠 허둥허둥치는 바람에 물을 떠다가 남수의 무릅 우에 엎지르고 얼굴이 빩해서 튀어 나가던 것이나 부억에 나가서 어린 것을 가지고 「얘 아버지 오셨다. 들어가 절해라. 어서 착하지」 하고 달래고
「싫어 무서워. 골을 내는 걸」 하며 말을 안 듯는 것을 나종에는 등ㅅ줄기를 패어 울리던 안해의 그 때 그 마음을 -
두 사람이 처음으로 마주 대하여 앉았을 때 안해는 오래오래 두고 남수의 얼굴에서 눈을 떠나 보내지 않았다. 그의 신산한 얼굴에 애정과 환흐에 넘친 쓸쓸한 웃음을 한 번 빙긋 웃어 보였다. 그러다가 넘어나 심란한 탓인지
「내가 당신을 원망ㅎ지 않아요. 저 못생긴 탓에... 그러니까 내가 암만 고생을 한 대도 마음 하나만 변ㅎ지 말아주면 그리고 가끔 이렇게 내려와서 얼굴이나 보여주고 가면 고만이야요. - 아 세상이 다 귀찮고...」
안해는 이렇게 탄식하였다. 그리고 떠나던 전날 안해는 남수에게 「여보시우 올아 가거던 어린 것 바지ㅅ 거죽 하나만 하고＜63＞ 당신 옷에 솜이 없으니 그것 일원 어치만 하고 그리고 그 작난깜 총 하나만 사서 낼이 부치세요. 부디 잊어 버리지 말고 부처 주세요.」 하고 신신 당부를 하였다. 남수가 가난한 가ƒ을 잘 아는 안해는 참아 가긔까지는 말도 못 부치고 얼인 것과 남수의 옷을 근심하여 몇 번인가 편지로 그 말을 하였으나 남수는 그대로 잊어 버리고 말았다. 잊어버렸다니 보다 그런 것까지 마음을 괴롭히기가 싫었다. 특히 작난깜 총이란 것은 그 때 내려가 있을 동안 단 하로가 지리하고 갑갑한 고로 그 동리에서 공긔총을 하나 빌려 가지고 배포 유하게 새를 잡으러 나섰다. 남수는 어슬렁어슬렁 울타리 밑을 빙빙 돌다가 뒤를 흴끗 돌아다 보니까 여섯 살 어린 것이 시펄하게 얼어 가지고 거지 새끼 모양으로 으르르 떨면서 남수의 뒤를 한사하고 딸아다님을 발견하였다. 새 잡는 것을 구경하려고 딸아 단녔는지 혹은 그대로 아비라고 정다워서 딸아 단녔는지 모르지만 하여간 그 때 남수의 생각에는 그리 불쾌한 것은 아니었었다.
「들어가 있거라. 내 새 잡어다 주께」
남수는 추위에 떠는 것이 불상하여 이렇게 일었다. 그러나 어린 것은 평생 송충이처럼 곁에도 와 앉지 못하게 훌뿌리던 남수가 별안간 이렇게 정답게 구니까 어린 것은 고만 더할라위 없이 흡족해서 남수에게로 달려 들며
「난 싫어. 아부지 새 잡는 것 볼테야.」 하고 고개를 흔들어 고집을 세워 보다가 양미간을 찡기고 앞에 딱 막고 서서 뚫어지게 노려 보니까 어린 것은 어찌 할 줄 몰아 애원하는 듯이 어리광 치는 말로 「아부지 새 잡거던 나 주지? 응 나 주지」
하고 제딴에도 남수의 사나운 성깔을 눅으러트려 볼 량으로 남수의 응문 얼굴을 치어다 보는 모양이 남수는 금방 불상하여저서 고개를 돌려 눈물을 참았다. 「암 너 주지. 많이 잡어 너 다 구어 주지」하였다. 그리고 치받이는 후회가.
「왜 성미가 이 모양이 되었단 말이냐? 어린 것이 무슨 죄가 있기에... 세상에게 짓밟힘을 당하고 애매한 네 게집과 자식을 들들 복는 까닭은 무슨 까닭이냐?」
