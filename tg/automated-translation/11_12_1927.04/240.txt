Article Title: 學窓을 떠나면서, 京城 各學校 運動選手 (Leaving the Halls of Learning, Athletes from Various Schools in Kyŏngsŏng)
Author: 秦蕃 (Chin Pŏn) [Introduction]

Leaving the Halls of Learning, Athletes from Various Schools in Kyŏngsŏng

The snowy, cold winds that blew over Pugak Mountain have now retreated, and the warm season that lifts people's spirits, spring, is upon us, opening the door to happiness. Whenever this time comes, a sorrowful sound flows from the mouths of students at every school, as they come to sing, "Alma mater? Farewell. I am leaving."

The soaring spirit of the athletes who fight with "sweat" and "blood" for the athletic competitions that determine the rise and fall of their school! From them, only a vigorous and firm will brimming with courage and a lively spirit can be seen.

Now, introducing the hardships and the feelings of reluctance to leave their alma mater of the athletes departing the school gates this time, I only hope it will provide some help and reward to the world of sports. - (Chin Pŏn)

Kyŏng Chae-ch'ang, Captain of the Hwimun Baseball Club

How regrettable, and yet how joyful, must it be for someone leaving the school gates, leaving behind the affectionate and loving school where they studied for five years, and the innocent and affectionate friends with whom they ran and sang together? However, actually leaving the school gates, the hoped-for joy turns into anguish and pain, and the feeling of regret and the longing for school once again becomes earnest. But since we entered school with purpose and hope, leaving school is a matter of course, yet the earnest feeling of wanting to attend school again is truly inexplicable. What is even more regrettable is leaving the friends with whom I exercised together, helping each other without regard for joy or pain, sweating profusely under the scorching summer sun, and leaving that athletic club. Having developed an interest in sports, I played baseball for several years, not necessarily hoping to become an athlete, but naturally, the school gave me the title of athlete, and additionally, the baseball club appointed me captain. For me, lacking understanding and common sense about sports, it was an even more distressing and irresponsible task. However, under the supervision and direction of the teachers, the help of senior brothers, and also with the coaching of Mr. Son Hyo-jun last year, we continued ever more vigorous practice. Thus, in last year's middle school baseball league (리그) tournament, our entire team became warriors who felt compelled to participate, but alas, we became defeated soldiers, returning swallowing tears, causing pain and anger in the hearts of over seven hundred fellow students, which was excessively shameful and heart-wrenching. The cause of that defeat, more than technical issues, could be said to be a lack of team spirit. It goes without saying that team spirit is necessary for team sports, but it was because we also regarded sports as a kind of lobbying (유세). If learning subjects in the classroom is considered 'literary' (文), then exercising on the athletic field<72> can be called 'martial' (武). Thus, by training one's physical strength through the martial, one can fully display and utilize the intellectual power gained. Therefore, I wanted to engage in sports properly and fight once more this year with the teammates who lost last year, but the fate of graduation has already made me leave the school gates, which is truly regrettable. Leaving the school gates, I miss all my friends, and I also miss middle school life once again.

Paek Kŭn-wŏn, Captain of the Kyŏngsin Basketball Club and Soccer Club

It has only been a few decades since the sound and light of sports began to shine on the mountains and rivers of the Peninsula (半島江山). Consequently, in other words, it has that short a history. As it has a short history, athletic talent has also not been abundant. What can I say, being one element within this current situation of ours, when asked for my impressions as an athlete leaving the school gates? However, I intend to write down my brief thoughts on sports.

Five long years have already passed since I came to Hanyang Sŏng (漢陽城). I have spent five springs in the garden of Kyŏngsin School (儆新學校), a three-story brick school building standing tall on a rock east of Hanyang Sŏng. Kyŏngsin was the first among school names in Chosŏn, and Kyŏngsin was also the first school to practice baseball when it was first imported into Chosŏn from abroad. At that time, its fame was so high that it would not be an exaggeration to call it the king of baseball. Just as the school has a long history, the athletic world also has a long history. In the past and present, the spirit of sports at our alma mater generally lies in making the youth's bodies healthy. In other words, if making physical education sound is the main purpose, then winning championships is secondary to that.

Originally, my body was not very strong, but I enjoyed sports since elementary school. Because I enjoyed it, I ran and ran again, playing energetically with such bold courage and cheerful spirit. As my clothes wore out and my shoes fell apart, my athletic skills developed, and my body grew healthier and healthier. Consequently, my spirit also became refreshed daily. However, leaving the schoolyard where I ran, laughed, and shouted every day, to where can I compare this feeling of regret, and to whom can I complain? Even if I leave my middle school days and advance to enter a professional school, I intend to proceed with the same level of athletic enthusiasm as in my middle school days. If one says sports are done for the sake of sports, there's nothing more to say, but since I do sports for my body and for the spirit of sports, I have resolved to do it for my entire life. Furthermore, because our environment and surrounding circumstances are not like others', while we must, of course, devote all our energy to research upon research and effort upon effort in science and various other fields, I believe that all endeavors can be accomplished based on a healthy body, which is incomparably precious in the world.
Considering all these points, a sound spirit lies in manifesting a sound body, and cultivating a sound body lies solely in not neglecting exercise; this is what I firmly believe.<73>

Kim Nak-hyŏn, Captain of the Paejae Baseball Club

Anyone would rejoice in having a healthy and robust body. Since my childhood, my body was very weak, and I often struggled in suffering. My only thought was how to make my body strong. What stirred my heart was the desire to become an athlete filled with the vigorous will brimming with courage that other youths possess, and whose red blood boils. Thinking back, it was 7 years ago. Thanks to the guidance of Kim Chŏng-sik and Yi Kyŏng-gu, giants in the Chosŏn baseball world, I developed an interest in sports, and thinking that I have now become the owner of a completely healthy body, I cannot help but express my gratitude to both gentlemen. Afterwards, I entered Paejae (培材), my alma mater from which I graduate this year, and the guidance of Mr. Ma Ch'un-sik and the help of Mr. Ch'oe Tŭng-man were also of great assistance to me. What I have gained from my 7 years of athletic life in the past is solely having built a body that cannot be exchanged even for gold; where else could such happiness be found?

Ah! Thinking back, in the old days, I spent time in tears due to my weak body, but now I spend my time cheerfully in a garden of laughter, welcoming the flower-like season of youth. I have realized it. I keenly feel that athletic life possesses the driving force to open up the path of life. Of course, everyone probably tastes such feelings, but it seems I have tasted them even more deeply. Even today, leaving the school gates, I first look back on bygone days (昔日) and resolve not to spare efforts to further brighten the alma mater's reputation through sports, and at the same time, to be more active for the Chosŏn sports world.

Ch'oe Kyŏng-nak, Captain of the Yangjŏng Track and Field Club

I have come to leave the alma mater I grew attached to over 5 years. When I left the school gates after finishing the graduation exams, there was undeniably a mix of sorrow and joy welling up in my heart. Will I never be able to see my various friends and the track again? Even if I leave the school, I cannot forget the classroom and the ground. Since the school I intend to enter is a normal school, I will be able to do even more sports and have the opportunity to receive much coaching, so I have been thinking ahead about setting the highest record in the Chosŏn long-distance running world. In any case, leaving the school gates, I have firmly resolved to strive hard, drawing out all my existing energy, so that much bright light may shine on the Chosŏn athletic world.

Chang Pyŏng-sŏn, Captain of the Paejae Soccer Club

I remember hearing these words at some stadium: "People of the world fight on the earth, but only spirited youths fight well with soccer." This is a meaningful saying by Mr. Yi Sang-jae. Indeed, it is so. We should rightly live through play ('purei') that promotes friendship and unity through soccer. The philosopher Rousseau once said, "Sports are the flower of youth and also life. The improvement of youth, staunch <74> struggle—all these are nurtured solely from innocent hot blood (血潮) and achieved right there on the ground." Sports possess some kind of power that makes youth newer. We must seek the joy of life in the great natural world through athletic competition. I believe that the current social system allows only those who succeed through struggle and effort to continue their existence. Therefore, while diligently pursuing our studies, we must also make our individual bodies somewhat stronger. Finally, leaving the school gates, shedding tears of farewell, I intend not to spare myself in helping my alma mater with body and mind.

Ch'oe Sam-jun, Captain of the Chungang Baseball Club

Before long, the ice thaws, the snow melts, clear water flows in the mountain valleys, the southern hill is screened by rocks, a flower mat is made of azaleas, and the beautifully first-emerged pasqueflower courteously bows its head as if greeting. At this time, what is most joyful is that the long-dreamed-of sports season is approaching. However, facing this time of leaving the school gates, the life of most cheerfully running, playing, and dancing during the past five years (五個 星霜) refreshes my memory once again. Recounting the golden age of sports at our school, the contribution of Mr. Sŏ Sang-guk, famous not only as a power hitter from Waseda (早稻田) but also as the leader of the Japanese study abroad baseball expedition team, was truly immense. Mr. Sŏ came to the school in June 1924, recruited baseball aspirants, and started communal lodging. Thus, waking up early every day, breathing the clear air coming down from Samgak Mountain (三角山), practicing catching the ball for an hour or two, and in the afternoon after school, changing into uniforms like lightning with the teacher, we came out and practiced with all our heart and strength, just like with our studies in the classroom. Wearing the sports uniform and stepping onto the field, the sight of running like flying amidst the billowing red dust (紅塵), the valiant posture (勇姿) holding the bat waiting for the ball, the image (情像) looking up at the ball floating in the air, raising both hands waiting for the ball to descend—though we ran around all day shedding sweat like red bean porridge, finding pleasure only in the step-by-step improvement of skills, even as the sun set and the day grew dark, we followed the ball's path, rubbing our eyes, hating the passage of time. Thus, in just a few months, we organized a team that could be called unified (一雜), and disregarding the summer vacation, rather using that opportunity purely for practice through matches, we first went to Inch'ŏn (仁川) for a trial and achieved a great victory. In late August, we went on an expedition to Wŏnsan (元山), won both matches, and the following year in October, we went on an expedition to P'yŏngyang (平壤), achieving what could be called a good result of one win and one loss against All P'yŏngyang. We were able to do it, and in the first middle school baseball league (리그) tournament, after extremely exciting the general populace, we finally achieved victory. Also last year, we won both the baseball league tournament and the All-Chosŏn Tournament, becoming capable of competing not only against middle schools but also against any other strong opponent. Thus, the path to Mr. Sŏ's success is gradually opening, and we graduates also leave the headquarters with a connection that makes us unable to forget our teacher, and with an exceedingly satisfied heart, unable to stop rejoicing. Consequently, what can be called the pride of our school is planting the seed (씨(種)) in Chungang and offering thanks for the grace given through the efforts of eight hundred people.<75>

No Chwa-gŭn, Captain of the Paejae Track and Field Club

Although track and field in Chosŏn has helplessly shown a state of stagnation until now, in the future, star athletes dispatched from Chosŏn, appearing like comets, will surely emerge even for the international Olympic games. The reason is that judging from the degree of sports promotion and the setting of highest records, the past and present clearly show progress. Therefore, I believe that in the future, it will surely sweep the world's athletic scene and introduce Chosŏn through sports. Track and field, unlike other sports, has the special feature (特點) that a large number of athletes play ('purei') together in one place for friendship, hence there is no country in the world where this sport has not spread. In Chosŏn, this sport was first imported only in 1915, and the fact that today the general public has reached a level where they can recognize the nature and effect of this sport is something to rejoice about together with the authoritative athletes of this field (斯界). My ideal is, while striving hard on the athletic field to make the school name shine is also a primary goal, that we sportsmen should play games ('kkeim') that prioritize friendship over victory or defeat. Then, ultimately, victory or defeat becomes a secondary issue. Simultaneously with leaving the school gates, I wish to embroider the athletic world, but whether surrounding circumstances will permit is a question within a question.

Ham Si-yŏng, Captain of the Kyŏngsin Baseball Club

It has not been that long in the distant past since I began to learn sports ('sŭp'osŭ'). However, in the meantime, I have thought a little about what sports are and have come to realize something. In former days (前日), we understood sports merely as the play of courageous technicians, and it was not understood by the general public that sports are courage combined with skill, and also fair justice. Eloquent speakers or politicians try to capture the audience's attention by spewing witty remarks, and writers or thinkers arrange strange phrases (文句) to seek popularity from readers. Likewise, our sportsmen also compete for victory or defeat fairly and squarely on the wide green ground under the blue sky to satisfy their self-esteem. Consequently, when unfortunately defeated in that match, that self-esteem finally receives a fatal blow. When trying to revive self-esteem again, only base courage arises, and both skill and justice disappear. At that time, a fight like wild boars (산도야지) breaks out. Although everyone is not unaware of such facts, putting them into practice seems difficult. Also, as students, it seemed we focused too much only on sports and were not diligent in our main duty, studying. However, I have thought that student sports should originally be done for health during the leisure time from studying. I am now busy preparing for the entrance exam of a certain school and will lay down my pen, but I declare in advance that I will play the role of an athlete again at some higher school.<76>

**Summary**

This 1927 article compiles farewell reflections from graduating student-athlete captains of various Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) middle schools (Hwimun, Kyŏngsin, Paejae, Yangjŏng, Chungang). The students, involved in baseball, basketball, soccer, and track & field, express mixed emotions of sadness at leaving their schools and teammates, pride in their athletic achievements (and sometimes disappointment in losses), and hope for the future. They emphasize the importance of sports for physical health, character building (courage, spirit, teamwork), and school pride. Several students mention specific coaches and past victories, highlighting the growing importance and organization of school sports. They express a desire to continue their athletic pursuits in higher education and contribute to the development of sports in Chosŏn, often linking physical strength to personal and potentially national well-being.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it provides a snapshot of elite colonial Korean youth culture, specifically the burgeoning world of organized school sports in the 1920s. It reveals the adoption of Western sports, the emerging importance placed on physical education alongside academics (framed partly as building strength for the nation), the development of inter-school rivalries and leagues, and the personal aspirations of students within the constraints and context of colonial Chosŏn. The mention of specific schools, coaches, and tournaments offers valuable details about the early history of Korean sports.

學窓을 떠나면서, 京城 各學校 運動選手
北岳을 넘어 불어오던 雪寒風도 이제는 뒤ㅅ걸음을 치어 달아나고 따뜻하고 사람의 흥을 돋는 봄청이 행복의 문을 열어주는 이때가 닥치어오았다. 매양 이때가 오면 각 학교 학생 입속에서 흘어나오는 애닯은 소리가 있으니 「母校야? 잘있거라. 나는 간다.」를 부르게 되는 것이다.
자기 학교의 성쇠를 좌우하는 운동경기을 위하여 「땀」과 「피」로써 싸우는 운동선수의 양양한 기상! 그네들에게서는 다만 용기에 넘치어 흐르는 식식하고도 굳센 의지와 활발한 기상만 뜨어 돌아보일 뿐이다.
