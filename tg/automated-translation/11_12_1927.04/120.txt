**Body Text Translation:**
Should Korean Script Not Use Double Vowels? Hangul Discussion
1. This column allows Korean language researchers to present unique opinions.
2. This column also includes assertions, refutations, or questions and answers regarding Korean language research.
3. Even if the orthography or punctuation differs from this company's preferred style, manuscripts in this column only will be printed as originally written.

Double vowels (複母) mean using vowels complexly. Looking at the convention of how our script has been written, it has been mistakenly established as a rule that vowels cannot be used complexly, which is truly regrettable. Even for those who conduct research upon research with many misconceptions, perfection is elusive; how much more so for our profound and incomparable script? Since modern times began, the Correct Sounds (正音, *Jeongeum*, i.e., Hangul) have been called the Vulgar Script (諺文, *Eonmun*), relegated merely for the use of women and children, while great scholars dismissed it as a trivial thing unworthy of attention, not placing particular importance on it. This is truly lamentable. Furthermore, for some time, it was hidden away among secluded monks (靑山白衲) becoming their exclusive property, unable to emerge beyond the mountains. Consequently, the methods of using vowels and consonants were not employed according to proper rules; instead, individuals asserted their private views, failing to respectfully uphold the original intent of the sagacious King Sejong (聖王) who possessed profound depth. Therefore, sound waves became complex and inconsistent, lacking clarity in pitch (high/low) and length (long/short). Only the customs handed down from Chinese characters (漢字) were adopted, <48> while the original meaning hidden within the Correct Sounds was not revealed. This cannot but be considered the fault of Korean scholars. Now, if we look at the method of representing Chinese characters, there are the four tones (4聲) of Level (平), Rising (上), Departing (去), and Entering (入), each having a mark: the Level tone mark is O, the Rising tone mark is ●, the Departing tone mark is *, and the Entering tone mark is *. By recording these like this, to find the Level tone also in Rising, Rising in Departing, and Departing in Entering, marks according to the four tones were placed beside the character. Also, because the Level tone can become Rising, Rising become Departing, and Departing become Entering, following the sound produced, adding one dot (一點) to the left of the character makes it Departing tone, adding two dots (二點) to the left makes it Rising tone, the Level tone has no dot, and the Entering tone has the same dot usage [as Departing/Rising, implying one dot] but the sound becomes abrupt (急促). It was stated that in the Correct Sounds too, this Chinese character example is used: adding one dot to the left makes it Departing tone, no dot to the left makes it Level tone, and the Entering tone has a dot and an abrupt sound. For example, for *nun* (眼, eye) there is no dot, but for *nun* (雪, snow) there is a dot, written as 눈 with a dot; for *pal* (足, foot) there is no dot, but for *pal* (簾, screen/blind) there is a dot, written as 발 with a dot; for *kam* (甘, sweet) there is no dot, but for *kam* (柿, persimmon) there is a dot, written as 감 with a dot. Considering this, our customs have been quite meticulous yet unable to break free from the foundation of Chinese characters, discarding the inherent freedom (自在) of our native script and instead adopting cumbersome and unclear methods—this can truly be called slavishness (奴性). Now, concerning consonants, they are used complexly according to sound, both same (同性) and different (異性) types being used complexly. In the initial position (初聲), same consonants like ㄲ <49> ㄸ ㅃ ㅆ ㅉ are doubled (疊用). In the final position (終聲) too, different consonants like ㄹㄱ ㄹㅂ ㄹㅁ ㄹㅍ are used in clusters (疊用). Because of this, our language is comprehensive and complete, lacking no sound that cannot be recorded, to the extent that even the sounds of wind, cries of cranes (風聲鶴唳), crowing of roosters, and barking of dogs (鷄鳴犬吠) can all be recorded. This is because the usage of vowels and consonants is exceedingly thorough (周備). Why then should it be definitively asserted that only vowels cannot be used complexly? Since the time of Mr. Ch'oe Sejin (崔世軫氏), the method of writing consonants has also been severely restricted, mistakenly decreeing (謬斷) that only these 8 letters—ㄱ ㄴ ㄷ ㄹ ㅁ ㅂ ㅅ ㅇ (옛이응)—can be used in the final position (終聲), and these 6 letters—ㅈ ㅊ ㅋ ㅌ ㅍ ㅎ—cannot be used in the final position. Arbitrarily suppressing the infinite undulations and flexibility (屈曲伸縮) of sound waves for the sake of consonants is quite lamentable, and for vowels to also suffer this collateral damage (池魚之殃) is an even more regrettable matter for vowels. Now, let me present the method of using double vowels: when intending to produce a high and long (高長) sound, if one repeats the vowel once more while reading, one can naturally produce a high and long syllable. For instance, to make the ㅏ sound high and long, write ㅏㅏ; to make the ㅓ sound high and long, ㅓㅓ; for the ㅗ sound, ㅗㅗ; for the ㅜ sound, ㅜㅜ; for the ㅡ sound, ㅡㅡ; and for the ㅣ sound, ㅣㅣ. Even if written this way, the method is simple, easy, and systematic, such that anyone in the country who sees it cannot but approve. Why then is the inherent freedom within the grammar of the Correct Sounds not applied? Instead, it causes foreigners learning our language <50> to observe the lack of high/low and long/short distinctions in the script and frame it as an underdeveloped script. Furthermore, by adopting foreign writing, namely the Chinese character method, it became indescribably cumbersome. Recently, even the esteemed Mr. Yi Nŭnghwa (李能和氏), perhaps also rejecting this method of adopting Chinese characters, used a long vowel mark when the sound was high and long in his writings. This long vowel mark was the character '-'. For *saram* (person), he wrote *sa-ram*; for *Yi Sŏbang* (Mr. Yi), *Yi-sŏbang*; for *kama-kyo* (palanquin bridge?), *ka-ma*. While this can be said to be more convenient than the Chinese dotting method, it also does not escape being cumbersome. In my opinion, it is best to discard all such irregular methods and reintroduce the unique and inherent freedom (獨特自在) of our script to the world. Often in linguistic circles, I have seen the use of double vowels. For example, when translating the Chinese language (支那語), it became unavoidable: 朋友 (friend) was written as *p'ingi*ㅜ, 高麗 (Koryŏ) as *kka*ㅗ*ri*, 九點 (nine o'clock) as *chi*ㅜ*den*, and 城裡頭 (inside the city) as *ch'yŏngnit'ŏ*ㅜ. Thus, the two letters ㅗㅜ were frequently used complexly. Writing like this allowed for accurate transcription of the speech. Why then, when recording our own language, are double vowels not used complexly, and if someone mentions it, they react with great alarm (大驚小怪) and pay no attention? I hope that all esteemed members of the academic world (學界 諸位) will reconsider this matter carefully (三復致思). It is not that they are entirely unused in our script. The two letters ㅘ and ㅝ are formed with double vowels, and there are many precedents of their common use in our language. But why must it stop at only the two letters ㅘ and ㅝ? Why can other vowels not be used complexly? Now, the method of using double vowels is recorded below: <51>

Seeing it for the first time, it might seem strange (이ㅣ상한듯하나), but once it becomes customary (습과ㅏㄴ이), it will be very beautiful (매오 아람다울지니), and no one will be able to deny it. Also, recently (그ㅡㄴ일), literary figures (文學諸公) are advocating for writing our script horizontally (횡서하자). If we are to write horizontally, it will be difficult to write high and long sounds (高長音) without adopting this method. I earnestly hope and pray (深望切祝하니이다) that everyone will not dismiss these humble words (蒭言) as strange (怪오에) but will research and try using it. <52>

**Summary**

This 1927 editorial argues passionately for the adoption of double vowels (e.g., ㅏㅏ, ㅓㅓ) in Korean writing to represent long or high-pitched sounds, replacing the cumbersome and "slavish" reliance on methods derived from Chinese characters, such as tone marks (side dots). The author critiques the historical neglect of Hangul (dismissed as *Eonmun*) and the arbitrary restriction of final consonants attributed to Ch'oe Sejin. Citing the existing use of complex vowels like ㅘ and ㅝ, and examples from transcribing Chinese, the author proposes extending the principle systematically. The piece concludes by linking this orthographic innovation to the contemporary movement towards horizontal writing, suggesting double vowels are essential for representing vowel length/pitch in that format.

From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it reflects the intense linguistic nationalism and desire for orthographic reform in colonial Korea. It shows a conscious effort to purify the Korean script from perceived foreign (Chinese) influence and establish a uniquely Korean system based on the script's own internal logic. The critique of past scholarly neglect and the reference to Yi Nŭnghwa's alternative (the '-' mark) highlight ongoing debates about standardization. Furthermore, the connection made to the nascent discussion of horizontal writing reveals early considerations of how script directionality might impact orthographic conventions.
