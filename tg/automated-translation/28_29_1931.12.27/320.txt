Ms. Grigsby is a female poet raised in Scotland who traveled through places like Chosŏn, China, Japan, and India.
Previously, she published poems obtained from Chosŏn and Japan in a poetry collection titled "LANTERNS BY THE LAKE." She now spends the remainder of her life in Western Canada.
What is translated here was published in "ASIA" magazine, issue 11, page 72. Two woodblock prints depicting Chosŏn's rural autumn scenery and mountain scenery were also inserted.
Regardless of the content of this poem, it is translated because it is rare for foreign poets to publish poems about Chosŏn in periodicals. Let us simply hear a foreign poet's impression of Chosŏn! (Translator)

Korean Autumn
						BY JOAN S. GRIGSBY, Translated and excerpted by Han Segwang (韓世光) in the USA

How dark the fields are tonight!
The sorrowful cry of geese coming from afar,
I listen alone.
And, their wings against the evening glow,
I watch alone.
The day grows dim,
The pale sky lowers.

One by one, the white-clothed people (白衣人=Translator)
Disappear along the roadside.
Quietly, quietly—
And, the women
Carry the day's gathered foodstuff on their heads,
And the old men pull carts.

The sun gently sinks
Beside the earth-built shacks.
And, also the old men,
Wearing black, tall hats (갓) on their heads,
Light their two-foot-long tobacco pipes (二尺煙竹).

Inside the house's pantry,
Yellow rats scurry about.
The naked children,
Throwing the stones and sand they played with,
Run towards their mothers standing by the road.
And the hungry children
Gather at the edge of the yard. <117>
Over the fields beyond where the grain stacks are piled,
The North Wind blows.

The lonely goose beats its wings
And flies towards the mountain.
Now, the autumn leaves on the mountain turn red.
But, ah! Before long,
Like the sorrowful cry of the geese,
White snow will fall.
-1931, 10-<118>
<117-118>

**Summary**

This article presents a Korean translation of a poem titled "Korean Autumn" by Scottish poet Joan S. Grigsby, originally published in "ASIA" magazine. The poem depicts a somber, quiet scene of rural Korean life at dusk in autumn: geese flying, white-clothed people returning home, women carrying food, old men smoking long pipes near earthen shacks, children playing, and rats scurrying. It evokes a sense of melancholy and the impending arrival of winter snow. The translator's introduction notes the rarity of foreign poets writing about Korea and presents the translation primarily to share this foreign perspective, regardless of the poem's specific content.

For historians, the article is interesting as it shows a Western perspective on rural Korea during the colonial period, highlighting iconic imagery like white clothes, *kat* hats, and earthen homes. The translator's explicit motivation—to share a rare foreign view of Korea published abroad—also reflects the cultural context and perhaps a desire for international recognition or understanding of Korea during a time of limited national sovereignty. The specific imagery chosen by Grigsby and the slightly melancholic tone offer insights into how Korea might have been perceived by foreign visitors.

조선의 가을
						BY JOAN S. GRIGSBY, 在米國 韓世光 譯抄
GRIGSBY女史는 스코틀랜드에서 生長한 여류시인으로 조선, 중국, 일본, 印度 等地로 遊覽하엿다.
일즉이,「LANTERNS BY THE LAKE」라는 詩集으로 조선과 일본에서 얻은 詩들을 출판하엿다. 지금 그는 西部 캐나다에서 여생을 보낸다.
여기에 번역한 것은 「ASIA」잡지 11호 제 72頁에 발표된 것이다. 조선농촌의 秋景과 山景 2幅의 木版畵도 揷入하엿다.
이 詩의 內容如何는 問題外로 하고 외국시인이 定期刊行誌에 조선에 대한 詩歌를 발표함이 稀少함으로 이 詩를 번역한 것이니 다못 외국시인의 朝鮮感을 듣자!       (譯者)

오늘밤의 뜰판은 캄캄도 하다!
멀리서 오는 구슲은 기러기소래
나는 혼자 듣노라
그러고, 저녁노을에 비낀 그의 날개
나는 혼자 바라 보노라.
날은 저믈어
히머-ㄹ건 하날은 낮어지도다.

하나식, 하나식, 힌자최(白衣人=譯者)는
길가로 살어지도다.
가만이, 가만이-
그러고, 女人들은
한날에 얻은 량식을 머리우에 이고
그러고 늙은이들은 구루마를 끌고.

해는 흙으로 지은 SHACKS가으로
실며시 잠기도다.
그러고, 또한 늙은이들은
검고, 높은 갓을 머리에 쓰고
두자기리 담뱃대 (二尺煙竹)에 불을 부치도다.

집안 청간속에는
누런 쥐들이 싸단긴다.
빨가숭이 애들은,
가지고 놀던 돌과 모래를 던지고,
길가에 섯는 어머니께로 달려 들도다.
그러고 배고픈 애들은

그러나, 아! 멀지않어

하얀 눈이 나리리로다.
