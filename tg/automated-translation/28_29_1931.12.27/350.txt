기사제목	발까락이 닮엇다 (The Toes Resemble)
필자	金東仁
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Short Story)

발까락이 닮엇다
						金東仁

The old bachelor M made an engagement.
When we heard this news, we unintentionally looked at each other's faces.

M was thirty-two years old. Although times had suddenly changed, and many people remained bachelors late due to economic problems, difficulty finding a suitable spouse, or simply out of defiance against the concept of early marriage (早婚), a thirty-two-year-old bachelor, no matter how one thought about it, felt a bit too old. Thus, whenever his friends had the chance, they would urge him, almost naggingly, about marriage. However, whenever M received such advice (though it was clear he was very interested inwardly), outwardly he would always refuse his friends' words with a bitter smile (苦笑). And yet, this M, unbeknownst to us, had somehow gotten engaged.

M was poor. He was a salaried employee at a very unstable company. It seems this fragile economic state might have kept him a bachelor for so long. And because of this, his friends felt sorry for M's bachelor life and urged him to marry.

However, I alone had a different interpretation of why M didn't marry. A physical defect in M, discovered through my profession as a doctor—I believed this was why M remained a bachelor past thirty.

M had lived a life of great debauchery since his student days. Lacking financial means, his debauchery was of the lowest kind. If he got even 50 *chŏn* or 1 *wŏn*, he would immediately run off to an *udong* house or the red-light district. With a constitutionally strong sex drive, he never missed an opportunity that presented itself to quench that burning desire. When meeting friends, he was the type to ask them to treat him to the red-light district rather than a meal.

"In terms of quality (質), I don't know, but in terms of quantity (量), I probably wouldn't lose to anyone in the world."

He didn't hesitate to boast like this about the number of women he had been involved with; he didn't go through the process of selection (選擇), he just "picked them up." By his early twenties, he announced he had already surpassed 200. By the time he was thirty, he must have looked down upon the monstrous monk (怪僧) Sin Ton far below him. As such, there wasn't a single venereal disease (性病) he hadn't <120> experienced. Moreover, being excessively fond of alcohol and having an unusually strong sex drive, he never abstained even while afflicted with STDs. There wasn't a single day in the year when he was free from venereal disease. Pus constantly flowed, and about every other month, he would come to me for injections, walking awkwardly due to orchitis (睾丸炎), barely able to walk properly. Even during such times, if he got 50 *chŏn* or 1 *wŏn*, he would engage in sexual activity again. Because of this, he was, of course, already a man who had lost his reproductive ability.

Knowing this well, I connected it to the reason M didn't marry and even sympathized with his sense of chivalry (?). He, who would spend his life in poverty and live solitarily in old age without children (슬하) to care for him—indeed, he who would have to swim through this sea of suffering (고해) with his own hands until his hair turned white because he had no descendants to support him—was truly a pitiful existence.

This M, who had been like this, somehow, unbeknownst to us, hesitantly got engaged.

Actually, something like this happened a few days ago. After dinner that day, while I was alone reading a new treatment report, M came to visit. With a relatively dark expression, he seemed reluctant to answer my questions, forcing words from his lips, and then threw this question at me:

"If a man has had syphilis, can he not reproduce?"
"It should be fine."
"What about gonorrhea?"
"Well... if the testicles weren't damaged (오까사레루하지 않으면 - likely from Japanese 侵される *okasareru*, 'to be violated/damaged'), it's okay."
"The testicles—a friend of mine had orchitis and is quite pessimistic, saying he can't reproduce now. Is it absolutely impossible if the testicles are damaged? He says he had it in both..."
"If he had a mild case, it probably wouldn't have an effect."
"Suppose it was mild—would you say my case was mild? Or severe?"

I unintentionally looked at his face. After suffering such a severe case, asking now whether it was mild or severe sounded like nothing but a joke. M's face remained heavy and dark. Like someone awaiting a grave sentence, he lowered his eyes deeply and waited for my answer. After looking at his face for a moment, I found it so absurd that I blurted out,
"It was a very mild case."
"A mild case?"
"Yes."

And so we parted. Thinking back now, it seems that conversation that evening might have led to his engagement today.

It was a friend named K who brought the strange news (奇報) that M was engaged. At that time, four or five friends (all of whom knew M) happened to be gathered.

"An antique (骨董)—a national treasure (國寶) is lost."
Someone made this comment. I asked K:
"So, was the engagement a result of romance?"
"Romance? What romance? Where would a guy who knows no women other than prostitutes (갈보) and bar hostesses (나까이 - likely from Japanese 仲居 *nakai*) find someone for romance?"
"Then, did she come with a dowry (持參金)?"
"Whose name is Dowry?"

Here, I saw the most unpleasant aspect of this engagement. If he, having remained a bachelor past 30, had bent his principles due to the peculiar affair called love, that would be cause for celebration, <121> not criticism. Even if he got engaged hoping for a dowry, for us living in the world today, especially knowing his poverty up to now, it wasn't something we could condemn. However, this engagement, being neither for love nor money, allowed for only one, most unpleasant conclusion.

"Then—"
I said this in the most unpleasant tone.
"He's getting a wife simply to save the expense of visiting the red-light district."

K looked at me as if displeased by this harsh criticism (酷評).
"There's no need to speak so harshly. M is already thirty—two, was it? Or three? In any case, at that age, he probably wants to hold a child on his knee, and yet he has no way or method to choose a suitable wife—"
"A child? For a guy who suffered such severe orchitis? A child? A child is—"

Feeling unpleasant, I rashly let slip a professional secret, but stopped myself mid-sentence. However, I couldn't take back the words already spoken.
"What? What did you say?"
Questions about M's reproductive ability came from all sides. Unable to avoid responsibility for what I had said, I struggled for a while to twist my words.—It couldn't be stated definitively, but perhaps M might lack reproductive ability. However, since no examination had been done, perhaps he might also have it. Feeling unpleasant about M making such a nonsensical engagement, I had spoken harshly, but I retracted those words. I embellished my explanation with this meaning. Then, R, a young man of about twenty present in the gathering, offered this opinion:
"Isn't it more comfortable to go through life without children?"
To this, I changed the subject to the issue of "the love for one's blood relations (血屬), which young people absolutely cannot understand," and a critique of the current trend of disregarding that issue too much.

M even held a wedding ceremony secretly. None of his friends knew the date of M's wedding in advance. Not only that, but instead of having the so-called modern wedding ceremony that everyone does these days, he had an old-style one at his home. The bride, a graduate of a certain girls' high school, reportedly disliked the old-style wedding, but M insisted on it.

And so, the red-light district lost a diligent customer.

"Monopoly is truly a pleasant thing."
After getting married, M reportedly said this to a friend. Although it wasn't a marriage founded on love, it didn't seem like a failed marriage either. This old bachelor, who used to throw away 50 *chŏn* or 1 *wŏn* to buy momentary sexual satisfaction, had monopolized a "virgin" he couldn't have dreamed of, so his pride must have been considerable. It wasn't a love match, but it seemed love had developed after the marriage. His face, which always carried a gloomy mood, seemed somewhat brighter, perhaps because things were looking up.

"May you be blessed."
We—especially I—sincerely congratulated their marriage. Initially, I prayed that this would be a happy marriage for his young wife, a great sacrificial victim who had been joined with M merely as an instrument for his sexual activity. Whatever the motive, let it bear beautiful fruit in the result. Don't let your young wife become a mere "sacrificial victim." Let your wife, who has no chance to taste the joy of motherhood, instead taste double the joy of being a wife compared to others. Whenever I thought of M, I sincerely prayed like this.

A few days after the honeymoon, rumors began to surface slightly that M was abusing his young wife. <122> There was even talk of him using physical force. However, I didn't think much of this issue. Whenever such rumors reached my ears, I would replay the story of the genie (魔神) from the Arabian Nights in my head.

A fisherman was casting his net (그믈). Then, one time when he pulled up the net, there was no fish, but instead, a bottle (甁) was caught in it. The bottle was stoppered, and on top of that, it was sternly sealed (봉감). After hesitating for a moment, the fisherman broke the seal on the bottle and pulled out the stopper. Immediately, a stream of black smoke rose from the bottle towards the sky (하눌). And the smoke that rose to the sky gradually coalesced (뭉처서), and there appeared a huge genie.

"The one who imprisoned me in this bottle is the prophet Solomon. While trapped (가쳐잇는) in this bottle, I swore (맹서햇다) to myself. If someone frees me from this bottle within a hundred years, I will give that person immense wealth (富) as a reward. I waited a hundred years, but no one (아모도) rescued me. So I swore again. If someone rescues me within the next hundred years, I will give that person all the treasures in this world. After waiting another hundred years in vain, I extended it by another hundred years, swearing that if someone rescued me within that hundred years, I would give that person the greatest power and glory in this world. But even after those hundred years passed, still no one rescued me. So, finally, I swore again. Now (인제), whoever rescues me, I will kill that person (놈) immediately to vent my anger (분푸리) for being trapped all that time (그새)."

This was the story of the genie released from the bottle. When I heard the rumors that M was abusing his young wife, I couldn't help but think of this story. He was venting the pain and loneliness of having remained a bachelor past 30 onto his wife. Go ahead and abuse her all you want. Abuse her all you want. I prayed even harder.

One evening, almost a year after M's marriage, he and I were quietly having dinner together somewhere.

His face was particularly dark and heavy that day. He barely touched his food and only drank alcohol (슬). Usually not very talkative, he was even more tight-lipped that day.

When he was so drunk he couldn't drink anymore, he finally opened his mouth voluntarily. His bloodshot eyes gleamed frighteningly.
"Hey. Hey. Don't deceive (쇡이지말구) me, tell me truthfully. Do I have reproductive ability?"
"Well. We'd have to do a test (검사해 보야지)."
I tried to brush it off with that.
"Then please test me sometime."
"Why all of a sudden—"

He was about to answer immediately. But he swallowed the words that were about to come out. Then, after drinking another glass of alcohol, he lowered his eyes deeply and said:
"No. It's nothing else, but if I don't have reproductive ability, isn't it pitiful for that person (his wife)? So, if it's determined that I don't have it, shouldn't we separate (헤저서) while she's still young and give her 'time' to forge her own destiny again? That's why I'm asking."
"We'd have to test."
"Then when should we test?"

A few days later, I was startled to hear a rumor that M's wife was pregnant (임신). There was no need for a test. M likely lacked the ability. Yet, M's wife was pregnant.

And I guessed M's intention from a few days ago when he said he wanted to be tested. It wasn't, as he claimed that day, "for his wife's future," <123> but rather because of suspicion (의혹) towards his wife. He himself wasn't entirely unaware (모르는 배는 아니로서); it was highly likely (십중팔구) that he was incapable of reproduction (생식긔불능자), yet his wife was pregnant. Here, he wanted to confirm the one or two chances out of ten (십중일이) of hope that remained.

Thinking about it, it was an interesting play. M, lacking reproductive ability, got married without showing any sign of it. Thus, the new wife who married M became pregnant. The wife, unaware that her husband is incapable of reproduction, would proudly display the seed of her sin to M. M, who had never revealed that he might be incapable of reproduction, now lacked the right to blame his wife even in this pit (구렁텅이) of suspicion. If he requested a test and it was confirmed he was infertile, it was impossible to guess what measures he would take. To blame his wife's adultery, he would have no choice but to expose his own deceptive act. To hide it, he would only increase his own torment.

One day, he came asking for the test. At that moment, as several patients were waiting, I asked him to wait a bit in my private room (私室) and went down after finishing with the patients. But he had left without waiting for me.

The next day, he came again. But somehow, he had left again.

Truthfully, I too hadn't firmly decided what to do. To say the sperm (정충) were alive after the test, when they would surely be dead, was something my scientific conscience wouldn't permit. But to say the sperm were dead was tantamount to a terrifying sentence ruining a person's life. M's wife, who sought illicit pleasure despite having a legitimate husband like M, was clearly a woman deserving blame. However, observing this incident from another angle, if I just closed my eyes once and gave an incorrect examination result (검안), then M (who otherwise absolutely couldn't) could have a lovely child (?) by his side, find solace (위한) in his old age, and everything could be resolved smoothly.

Standing at a crossroads with two paths I could freely choose, I hesitated in judging which path to take.

This problem resolved itself four or five days later. That day too, M came to visit with a gloomy face. Out of obligation, I asked,
"Shall we do the test today?"
He answered simply:
"Already did it."
"Huh? Where?"
"At K Hospital."
"And the result?"
"They said they're alive."
"?"

I unintentionally looked at his face. It wasn't so much because I heard an unexpected answer, but rather because his voice saying "they said they're alive" was so grief-stricken...
"Then you must be relieved."
While replying thus, I felt relief at having escaped the burdensome task I almost had to bear, and at the same time, I couldn't help but widen my eyes at the unexpected result from K Hospital.

M's eyes, meeting mine, darted around here and there as if flustered. And from those eyes, I knew that what he had just said was a lie.

Then why did he lie? To protect his wife's honor? To have children by his side, deceiving the world and even his own heart? I couldn't understand his mind. <124>

He opened his mouth. His voice was heavy and gloomy.
"Hey. Do you understand this feeling (キモチ - kimochi)?"
"What kind?"

He paused for a moment before starting to speak:
"A salaryman received his salary. As soon as he got it, he went out, ate, spent, bought things, used the money freely as he pleased. Now he's on his way home. It's clear there isn't much money left in his wallet (지갑속에). But he can't open the wallet. Before opening it, he can cling to the lucky thought that maybe there's still quite a bit left, but once he opens it, the fact that only a few coins remain will be revealed, won't it? Fearing that, he thinks 'it's still there, it's still there,' deceiving himself. He needs to buy rice, buy firewood. If he opens it, the fact that there isn't enough money to buy them will be revealed. So, keeping his hand as far from the wallet as possible, he returns home. Do you understand that feeling (キモチ - kimochi)?"

I nodded my head.
"I understand."

He sealed his lips again. But in that moment, I understood. M hadn't even had his semen tested. He is afraid. He avoids the test. His wife is pregnant. Judging by common sense, it must undoubtedly be the husband's child. There should be no one who harbors doubts about it. There is no need to harbor doubts. Why? Because it is natural for a wife to become pregnant when she takes a husband.

If an unpleasant result emerged from doubting this unquestionable matter, it would be self-inflicted misfortune with nowhere to lay blame. Stirring up a hornet's nest is foolish. M decided to avoid the "test," which would yield an unpleasant result nine times out of ten (십중팔구). Not wanting to buy despair for himself—and wanting to cling (부처두려) to a glimmer of hope even amidst torment—M completely decided not to touch the dangerous hornet's nest called the "test." And he resolved to forcibly cultivate affection for the child (in his wife's womb), which common sense dictated was his. If the test showed living sperm, it would be fortunate, but if they were dead, setting aside the tragedy, anger, and despair that would arise between him and his wife, he would inevitably fall into a state of despair, knowing he would spend his life without blood relatives by his side and without even the possibility of having someone to rely on in old age. This was a terrifying prospect. There was no need to undertake such a risky act, even rejecting what common sense dictated.

Thus, although he gave up on the test, it seemed he couldn't completely extinguish the suspicion in his heart. Some day after that, while talking about this and that, he said this:
"It would be nice if children definitely resembled their fathers..."

To that, I gave various examples of resemblance.

He sighed.
"It must be worrying when a woman gets pregnant. If the child resembles the father or the real father, there's no problem, but wouldn't it be worrying if it resembles the mother's side, or doesn't resemble anyone? It's best if it just resembles the father."

I replied:
"Exactly. It's not my specialty, so I can't remember the name, but isn't there a German novel like this?" "Is it the play called 'The Father'? There's a story about agonizing because you don't know if the child born is yours or not, right? That too would have no problem if it just resembled the father."
"Ah-ah. It's all so troublesome."

M's wife gave birth to a son.
The child grew to be about six months old.
One day, M came to me, personally holding the child, to show me its illness. <125> Its bronchial tubes were slightly affected.

Even after receiving the medicine, M sat there for a while and then, unprompted, said this:
"They say this fellow looks exactly like his great-grandfather."
"Really?"
Feeling considerable interest in his words, I responded like this. From my perspective, there was no connection whatsoever between the baby and M—so it was strange that the child resembled M's great-grandfather... M's relatives, unable to find anyone similar among the child's paternal and maternal close relatives (近親), seemed to have dredged up a long-dead ancestor out of necessity. And M, harboring great suspicion and even greater hope (wishing the suspicion was a misunderstanding) regarding the child, seemed to have readily believed those words. At least, he seemed to have decided to try to believe them.

Seeing that I was interested in his words, M hesitated for a moment, then finally brought out the second point he had prepared.
"Besides, there's a part that resembles me."
"Where?"
"Look here."

M carefully shifted the baby to his left arm while supporting it, and with his right hand, he took off (버섯습니다) his own sock.
"Look at my toes. My toes are different from other people's; the middle toe is the longest. It's not a common toe. But—"

M lifted the swaddling clothes (강보) and gently exposed the baby's foot.
"Look at this fellow's toes. Aren't they exactly like my toes? They resemble..."

M looked intently at my face, as if seeking confirmation. How desperately must he have searched for a resemblance to find one in the toes? I was moved to tears by M's state of mind and effort. M's struggle to somehow suppress the great suspicion amidst it all was life's most poignant tragedy. Without looking at the baby's toes that M had exposed for me to see, I instead stared at his face for a long time, and finally said this:
"Not just the toes, there's a resemblance in the face too."

And avoiding his eyes (mixed with suspicion and hope) that flew towards my face, I turned away. <126>

**Summary**

The story follows M, a man in his early thirties who marries late despite being infertile due to past venereal diseases contracted through extreme debauchery. His friend, a doctor (the narrator), is aware of his condition. When M's young wife becomes pregnant, M is plunged into suspicion but avoids a medical test to confirm his infertility, clinging to denial and the faint possibility the child might be his. He fabricates a story about a positive test result from another hospital. Ultimately, desperate for a connection, M points out the supposed resemblance between his own distinctively long middle toe and his infant son's, a flimsy proof he desperately wants to believe. The narrator, pitying M's tragic self-deception, lies and tells him there's a facial resemblance too.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into social anxieties in 1930s colonial Korea. It touches upon changing marriage norms (late marriage), economic hardship affecting personal lives, the devastating impact of untreated STDs before widespread antibiotic use, and the immense cultural pressure to produce male heirs for lineage continuation. Furthermore, it depicts the collision between traditional beliefs/desires (lineage, resemblance) and modern science (medical diagnosis of infertility), showcasing an individual's psychological struggle and retreat into self-deception when faced with an unbearable scientific truth.

						金東仁
노총각 M이 혼약을 하엿다.
우리들은 이 소식을 드를 때에 뜻하지 않고 서로 얼골을 마조 보앗습니다.
