Public Recommendations for the World's Top 10 Figures
						By Various People

Whether poets or thinkers, world-renowned great figures hold much significance for us. Indeed, whom do the reporters, writers, authors, lawyers, and other figures from various circles in Korea admire? We believe this provides us with much interest and benefit. Thus, we posed the following question, and these are the answers received.

Across East and West, past and present, who are the figures you most revere or perhaps most admire?

If you ask me to write down the figures I like from various fields, I suppose I would answer roughly like this. <60>
1. Among Politicians: Someone like Abraham Lincoln (아부라함 링컨), who strove for the emancipation of Black slaves.
2. Among Military Leaders: Someone like Yi Sun-sin, who rendered distinguished service in saving the nation during the Imjin War.
3. Among Thinkers: Someone like Shakyamuni (석사모니), who sought to deliver the boundless multitude of beings from suffering.
4. Among Poets: Someone like Tagore, with his rich mystical colors.
5. Among Novelists: Someone like the reverent humanitarian author Tolstoy.
6. Among Musicians: Someone like the 'man of feeling' Beethoven, who has the beautiful story of playing the famous Moonlight Sonata for a poor, blind woman.

**Summary**

This 1931 article from the magazine *Tongkwang* presents the results of a survey asking various Korean intellectuals (writers, journalists, lawyers, artists, etc.) to name the figures they most admire across ten categories: politician, military leader, thinker, poet, novelist, musician, actor, painter, scientist, and other. The responses show a wide range of admiration for figures from both East and West, ancient and modern, including historical Korean figures like Yi Sun-sin and Chŏng Mong-ju alongside international names like Lincoln, Marx, Beethoven, Tolstoy, Edison, and Chaplin. The introduction highlights the value of understanding who intellectuals admire, and a concluding note acknowledges the apparent ideological contradictions in the choices, attributing them to the complexity of human nature.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects the intellectual currents among Korean elites during the Japanese colonial period. The frequent mention of Yi Sun-sin highlights enduring national pride and resistance symbolism. The admiration for figures associated with revolution (Lenin, Marx), national liberation (de Valera, Gandhi), humanitarianism (Lincoln, Tolstoy), and scientific progress (Edison, Einstein) suggests the diverse aspirations and influences shaping Korean thought under colonial rule, encompassing nationalism, socialism, liberalism, and modernization. The inclusion of Japanese figures (47 Ronin, Osanai Kaoru, Fujiwara Yoshie) alongside Western and Korean ones also provides a glimpse into the complex cultural landscape.
