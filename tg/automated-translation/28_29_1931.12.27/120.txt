**Header Section:**
Magazine Title: 동광 제29호
Publication Date: 1931-12-27
Article Title: 東西古今人物座談會 (A Roundtable Discussion on Figures East and West, Ancient and Modern)
Participants: Kim Pyŏngno (金炳魯), Pak Ch'anhŭi (朴讚熙), Ch'a Sangch'an (車相讚), Mun Ilp'yŏng (文一平), Yu Kwangnyŏl (柳光烈), Yi Kwangsu (李光洙), Yi Yongsŏl (李容卨), Chu Yohan (朱耀翰), Pak Hyŏnsu (朴玄衰), Yi Chongsu (李鍾洙)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Roundtable Discussion)

**Body Text Translation:**
Chu: The topic for this evening's discussion is figures from East and West, ancient and modern. First, please share your thoughts on the appropriateness of venerating great figures and heroes.

I. Hero Worship, Past and Present
Kim: Regarding that, I think we first need to define what a hero is. The point of discussion will differ depending on the definition and perspective. If a "hero" is defined as someone who builds great enterprises and achieves significant accomplishments for the masses in contemporary society, who would oppose that?
Chu: But isn't it said these days that such great enterprises are carried out by the populace, not by heroes?
Kim: That probably means it's not done solely by the hero's power, but by the masses. However, without a leader representing and commanding the masses, a great enterprise cannot possibly be achieved.
Mun: Absolutely. A populace without a leader is like scattered sand. In the East, from ancient times, those called heroes were often skilled killers who liked to rule despotically, so the populace disliked them. They disliked despotic rulers.
Ch'a: When speaking of heroes, I believe the difference lies in whether past heroes acted out of personal ambition, while present heroes act <33> based on the general populace; other than that, there seems to be little difference. Even Lenin worshippers behave no differently than those who worshipped heroes in the past. Whether for their own nation or globally...
Yi Kwang[su]: Ancient heroes like Qin Shi Huang did things in their own name, but now heroes unify by leading.
Kim: Ancient heroes acted independently and despotically, which is why the populace disliked them. Take Lenin, for example; isn't he worshipped by certain societies and populaces? Speaking of dictatorship, in Soviet Russia, although formally based on the working class, where is dictatorship more severe than in the "Soviet"? The issue, I believe, lies in whether they act based on the masses or not.
Chu: Then, is a hero necessary for a popular great enterprise? Please speak on that.
Kim: In previous eras, people were naturally developing and existed as their natural character dictated. Therefore, the general level of culture and education was low, leading to the emergence of despotic heroes. Now, however, knowledge levels and activity capabilities have become more equal, resulting in many fundamental differences between the past and present. A hero can be defined as someone who achieved a great enterprise based on the populace of that era. If a hero is someone who accomplishes a great enterprise under the reality of their time, then anyone would want to become such a hero, and if such a hero existed, everyone would worship them.
Ch'a: "Yŏng" (英) means flower, and "Ung" (雄) means 'male', so ultimately it means something superior to others. As Mr. Kim Pyŏngno said, in the past, human intelligence was dim, so if someone had a sharp mind, they could do as they pleased. Now, one cannot simply do as they please. However, even now, if someone has outstanding natural talent and extraordinary qualities, they will naturally become a central figure worshipped by all.
Mun: Indeed, it becomes a question of the necessity of a central figure.
Kim: I believe the current aversion to hero worship stems from hatred towards those who merely "pretend to be heroes."

II. Korea and the Leadership Problem
Chu: What are your thoughts on the issue that there are no capable people in Korea now, or that a leader is necessary?

(1) Selection of and Obedience to Leaders
Yi Kwang[su]: There's a particular issue in Korea; I see a tendency among young people to deny anyone superior to themselves. Moreover, while they might worship a foreigner, they tend to look down upon fellow Koreans.
Kim: Anyone, regardless, would be worshipped if they accomplished a great deed, and one should also have the aspiration to accomplish great deeds oneself. Lacking such aspiration, or worse, disliking and envying what others do, is the root of self-destruction.
Mun: That's right. It's said the British make great figures, but the Korean people's level (民度) hasn't reached that point yet.
Kim: Envying others for doing well is a worthless thought. If we view a group as the hero, not an individual, and then elevate the central figure of that group, then as the group grows, <34> no matter who opposes it, it won't matter.
Yi Kwang[su]: Where there is no group, there is no leader.
Kim: It's not that Koreans alone have a weak inclination to worship others. However, due to being under special circumstances, it's difficult to concentrate the total strength of the populace. Also, since there hasn't been anyone who has accomplished a truly great deed, they are not worshipped.
Yu: That's right. The fact that there's no worship means there are no heroes.
Yi Yong[sŏl]: Isn't there a general tendency among Koreans to dislike worshipping others?
Pak: Heroes don't just spring from the ground, nor is the idea of "making" them meaningful. Ultimately, don't heroes emerge through struggle? If there were someone in Korea who did great things for the populace, they would naturally be worshipped. I don't see a psychology of deliberate (우진) rejection just because someone is Korean. Regardless of the title "hero," if a person's deeds and the scale of their work are great, they are naturally worshipped. Past heroes had sharp minds and forced the populace to conform to their will, but present heroes represent the interests of the masses—in other words, they can be seen as guiding and monitoring the masses with critical ability. Therefore, although the content differs between past and present heroes, if someone possesses a sharp mind and critical ability and stands on the side of public opinion, I believe they will receive general support.
Mun: You mean standing on public opinion, not on arbitrary judgment?
Pak: That's right. So, someone like Lenin...
Mun: It depends on how you put it (Laughter).
Yi Kwang[su]: Some say that if there were someone worthy of being a hero, they would naturally be worshipped, or that there is no one in Korea worthy of worship. However, I believe that today is an era where the general populace elects and produces leaders. I read a book called "Social Psychology" by Ross, which mentioned two principles of national education: first, the wisdom to choose a leader, and second, obedience to the chosen leader. A nation's rise or fall is determined by whether it can do these two things. Looking at our Korean compatriots, don't they seem to think they can live without leaders? While Japan or Europe produce leaders, we neither think of choosing leaders nor do we obey them. It seems we don't even feel the need for leaders. However, leaders require a group; without a group, there are no leaders. If undertaking educational work within Korea, I think we must teach the selection of leaders and obedience to those selected leaders.
Yi Yong[sŏl]: Rather than saying we don't worship because there's no one to worship, isn't there a psychology among the general youth to reject anyone better than themselves <35>? Another reason might be the dislike of one person monopolizing what the populace has created.

(2) Social Life and Leaders
Chu: Mr. Yu, please share your thoughts.
Yu: Calling them heroes (英雄) might have negative connotations, so perhaps "great leaders" (大指導者) is better. Koreans, having never experienced much social life, lack both the wisdom to select leaders and the mindset to obey, I believe. These things are learned through social life. Whether one is a communist or an anarchist, the ideology in their head doesn't disappear overnight but changes gradually. Similarly, the ideology of worshipping great figures among Koreans will not be acquired suddenly.
Korea, having held feudal ideas, experienced the growth of capitalism after the Gabo Reforms (甲午以後), and shortly thereafter, finds itself looking towards a communist society. Koreans still retain many feudal ideologies. Standing on feudal ideology narrows one's perspective, making one see only oneself as significant and naturally becoming self-centered. Consequently, they dislike things outside their own group. Firstly, the lack of opportunity for social life; secondly, the persistence of feudal ideology; and thirdly, the extremely difficult conditions for undertaking enterprises in Korea—these three factors, as cause and effect, spin around, making it even harder to achieve notable accomplishments. As a result, individuals who could be designated as leaders fail to emerge, and consequently, the general populace finds itself in a situation where it doesn't know whom to follow as a leader. This, I believe, is the element contributing to the inability to select leaders and obey them. Regarding the claim that Koreans fundamentally lack the psychology to worship heroes, I disagree. On the contrary, Koreans worship quite readily. For instance, Lenin or Mussolini. Isn't Mussolini praised as a great man, even though he's a reactionary politician? It's said that it's not the same among fellow Koreans, but regarding, say, Dr. X [referring to Yi Yongsŏl, the doctor present], among surgeons, regardless of Japanese or whoever, don't they say, "Dr. X is the best"? As Koreans engage more in social life, I believe they will become better at selecting leaders and obeying them. Currently, isn't there a pitfall where Koreans tend to obey great figures unconditionally?
Ch'a: Indeed, they are quick to judge unconditionally, this way or that. We often experience this ourselves; for example, if someone says, "Hey, something you said is written in such-and-such magazine," they typically respond, "Those damn reporters!" without even looking at the content.
Kim: (To Ch'a Sangch'an) You're a magazine guy, so it's different (Laughter). As Mr. Yi Kwangsu said, the wisdom to select leaders and obedience are necessary. However, this is not an absolute issue, but a relative one. Don't we see this in reality in Korea? (Gesturing with hands) For example, there's a school; when a certain person is principal, it runs well, and when another person is principal, <36> it doesn't. We always see that regardless of the group, it fares well when guided by one person and poorly when guided by another. A hero can be seen as the great leader among leaders. So today, people within a group should be able to select a great leader.
Ch'a: That's right.
Kim: When we talk about leaders in Korea, we speak relatively within the Korean reality, not comparing with the Soviets or the United States. We speak relatively within the current reality of Korea. Therefore, it's not that we cannot choose because there are no people. After selection, obedience is necessary, but this too is not absolute. In principle, one should obey, but if circumstances change and the once-chosen person can no longer lead, obedience is not possible. However, unconditional resistance is not worth discussing here. Whether heroes, great figures, or great entrepreneurs, they differ by century, so we must view them based on "the populace of that era." Thus, our worship of heroes is also directed at the great figures of that era. If one worships Marx, one must understand his economic theory. Determining whether his economic theory is right or wrong requires historical perspective, considering feudal economy, capitalist economy—like Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations or economic theories. Or, looking politically, judging whether "Soviet politics" are necessary for our populace requires a historical view of feudalist and capitalist politics. Without this, if one only considers Soviet politics and doesn't think of looking back at the past, it wouldn't even be a matter of critique. Therefore, I want the youth to attain comprehensive knowledge. And based on that, judge what is appropriate for the current reality.
Yu: Before we talk about hero worship, couldn't we say this: Our hero is society itself. Meaning can only be realized through society, not on an individual basis. For example, a ship's captain is necessary for the entire ship; for a school, the entire school; for society, the common interests of the entire society must be considered. Therefore, above all, society must be the standard. Only through social life can leaders emerge; without social life, there are neither leaders nor anything else. So, even by forming groups for walks, reading clubs, or just chat groups, the issues of leader selection and obedience will arise.
Ch'a: And by attending roundtable discussions... (Laughter)

III. World Great Figures
Chu: Who are the people currently attracting the most public interest?
Mun: Do you mean categorized?
Chu: In the past, great figures were politicians or military men. Is that correct? <37>
All: That's right.
Chu: Then who would be the foremost politician?
Mun: Alexander, Caesar, Napoleon are usually named. In the East, Qin Shi Huang...
Ch'a: Qin Shi Huang doesn't really attract popularity, does he?
Pak: In modern times, Lenin...
Ch'a: Zhuge Kongming certainly draws popularity.
Yu: As a politician?
Chu: Yes.
Mun: In modern times, Lenin would be number one.
Yu: It would be Lenin.
Chu: Who comes after Lenin?
Yi Yong[sŏl]: In America, Lincoln would surely be chosen. Some worship Lincoln like Jesus.
Yu: After Lenin comes Stalin.
Mun: After that, Wilson, considering his work transcending nations.
All: It seems you're contrasting socialists and nationalists.
Chu: Among living people today, who is number one?
Mun: As a politician detached from profession, Gandhi might be better.
Ch'a: Gandhi went all the way to England carrying his spinning wheel; his profession... (Laughter)
Chu: What about Mussolini?
Kim: Regardless of ideology, Mussolini is a politician from a small country like Italy, yet there's hardly anyone who doesn't know the name Mussolini. Gandhi cannot be called a practical politician...
Yi Yong[sŏl]: He hasn't had the opportunity...
Ch'a: Once, some newspaper reporter said Mr. Kim Pyŏngno looks like Gandhi (Laughter).
Chu: In modern East Asia, would it be Sun Yat-sen (孫逸仙)?
All: Yes... That's right.
Chu: Who would it be in Japan?
Kim: Itō Hirobumi or Ōkuma Shigenobu.
Chu: Saigō seems popular too. In China, among living people, who is number one...
Yi Yong[sŏl]: Chiang Kai-shek?
Kim: Chiang Kai-shek is a military man, not quite a politician...
Mun: Still, he's better than others.
Chu: Enough about politicians. Traditionally, heroes meant military leaders. Who ranks first, spanning East and West?
Yu: Hindenburg might stand out.
Chu: Hindenburg was a defeated general.
Pak: But in terms of tactics, who could match him...
Kim: For skillful warfare, it's Napoleon. But for courage and strategy, one must look to the East. <38>
Mun: Temüjin (Genghis Khan) is number one. Globally too, it's rare to find someone better than him.
Kim: A true military strategist shouldn't just use what others have established but should be someone who creates things themselves.
Ch'a: Yi Ch'ungmugong (Yi Sunsin) might come up then.
Kim: Huangdi Xuanyuan (黃帝軒轅氏) was outstanding. Discovering the south-pointing needle (針南石 - likely compass) back then, when everything was foggy and indistinguishable...
Yi Yong[sŏl]: In the East, Yi Sunsin should be nominated.
Mun: Temüjin built upon an existing foundation, but Yi Ch'ungmugong achieved victory without a foundation, which is extraordinary.
Chu: As for thinkers, who would be number one?
Kim: Marx.
Mun: Marx is greater than Darwin's theory of evolution.
Chu: Who are the thinkers in the East?
Pak: Are there any in the East?
Ch'a: Excluding religious figures, there are no thinkers.
Chu: Then including religious figures.
Yu: Laozi, Confucius cannot be treated as thinkers...
Chu: Among religious figures, who is considered the best?
Kim: We have no choice but to pick the most illusory one...
Ch'a: Then it's Poch'ŏn'gyo (普天敎).
Kim: No. Buddhism is the most illusory. I once read about the theory of emptiness (眞空論). What is it? Being is non-being, non-being is being, both being and non-being are emptiness. Now (laughing), how can anyone understand this?
Ch'a: Poch'ŏn'gyo is like that too. The infinite (無極) is the supreme ultimate (太極), the supreme ultimate is the infinite... (Laughter)
Chu: There were many in Silla, but only their three-character names remain!
Yu: I once read a book written by a German, stating that a painting by Ajwa (아좌) from Paekche, who went to Japan, is a world-renowned masterpiece.
Ch'a: Who knows about that? Just because he says he likes it doesn't mean anyone else has to recognize it. It's probably less credible than our roundtable discussion. It's like showing a Westerner these straw shoes (짚신) and saying, "Our ancestors wore these 1,000 years ago." The Westerner thinks for a moment and replies, "It couldn't be 1,000 years, but maybe 100 years." (Laughter)
Chu: Would Shakespeare be number one?
Ch'a: One has to read it directly for it to register...
Chu: In classical Chinese writing, who is number one?
Ch'a: Do you mean poetry and prose? There are so many genres. <39>
Chu: All included.
Kim: There is one great orator, though.
Ch'a: Mencius.
Kim: Yes, no matter who you name, Mencius is number one.
Mun: Who would be the literary figure?
Ch'a: Since his surname is Mun (文), Mr. Mun Ilp'yŏng must be number one (Laughter, joking back and forth, Mun - "My appearance is decent..." Laughter).
Yu: For literary figures in the East, we'd have to pick from China.
Mun: Then Zhuangzi, Mencius, Han Feizi, Xunzi would be chosen. Zhuangzi's writing is unrestrained, Mencius's writing...
Yu: Are they artists? The author of 'Dream of the Red Chamber' (紅樓夢) was brilliant.

IV. Figures in Korean History
Chu: Alright, let's stop there and talk about Korea. What about Ŭlp'aso (乙巴素)?
Ch'a: Is there anything concrete about him?
Chu: Who would you pick as a politician in Korea?
Mun: Kaesomun (蓋蘇文) would be chosen.
Kim: In Korea, excluding kings, one couldn't become a politician.
Chu: In the turbulent times of late Joseon (韓末風雲), who was the most outstanding person?
Pak: The deceased Kim Okkyun (金玉均).
Ch'a: Hong Yŏngsik (洪英植) was the backbone and did the work, but the one who appeared on the surface was Kim Okkyun.
Mun: In modern times, the Taewŏn'gun (大院君), but he was just uneducated and ignorant (不學無識) - this might be a bit offensive -
Kim: What's offensive about it...
Yu: For that time, he wasn't uneducated or ignorant.
Mun: Still, he was a hero. Ch'ŏng'o (靑吾 - Ch'a Sangch'an's pen name), if told to go out and fight, you'd go, wouldn't you? You couldn't refuse. Achieving that much makes him a hero. How feared he was by the Japanese at the time...
Kim: Initially, after the Meiji Restoration, Japan had intentions of cooperating with Korea, but after actually coming and seeing the situation, their mind changed.
Mun: That's right, as they gradually came and saw, they began to look down on us.
Chu: Around the time of the Five-Article Treaty and Seven-Article Treaty (五條約及七條約 前後), who were the significant figures?
Kim: There's Min Ch'ungjŏnggong (閔忠正公 - Min Yŏnghwan).
Ch'a: What about him? Just because he died doesn't make him great.
Yu: Who was there at the time of annexation (合倂當時)? (Here, there was discussion about Yi Wanyong (李完用) involved in high-interest loans, Song Pyŏngjun (宋秉畯), the talent of Pak Yŏnghyo (朴泳孝氏), Mr. Kim Pyŏngno's strong self-confidence, anecdotes about Kim Okkyun (金玉均氏), and the assassin Hong Chongu (洪鐘宇), a student returned from France who published the Ch'unhyangjŏn, said not to have acted out of personal greed and regretted killing Kim Okkyun at the time of his death. However, at Mr. Mun Ilp'yŏng's suggestion not to write about figures from the annexation period, this part is omitted from the record. Following this, there were mentions of Catholic figures, Ch'oe Cheu (崔濟愚), Son Pyŏnghŭi (孫秉熙), Chŏn Pongjun (全琫準) etc., their characters, especially the connection between Chŏn Pongjun's revolution and the Taewŏn'gun, and doubts about Pak Yŏnghyo visiting Chŏn Pongjun in prison, but due to space constraints, all <40> this is regrettably omitted.)

V. Contemporary Korea and Figures
Chu: Please talk about contemporary figures.
Ch'a: That's difficult.
Mun: Difficult indeed.
Kim: That's for the masses to decide.
Chu: Let's exclude those overseas and talk only about people currently here.
Ch'a: Even if published, these words will draw criticism.
Mun: It's a matter of comparison, how can we do it?
Chu: For example, can't we at least say who might be suitable to replace Mr. Yi Sangjae (李商在氏)?
Pak: Given the current various circumstances and capabilities, wouldn't it be Ch'oe Rin (崔麟氏)?
Kim: Let's drop this question. Rather, if we were to form some organization, what kind of system would be implemented in that organization? We don't know that, but the person who would oversee it would likely be someone we haven't even thought of.
Mun: Even if there are figures now, we wouldn't know. The environment has to allow people to emerge.
Ch'a: Chiang Kai-shek's emergence also happened because he possessed the potential beforehand, didn't it?
Chu: How about we pick 5 to 10 people from those currently present?
Ch'a: Rather than doing that, it might be better to divide into categories like religious figures—further dividing into Christianity, Buddhism, Ch'ŏndogyo, etc.—nationalists, and socialists, pick one person from each category, bring them together, and elect a chairman, like choosing a moderator.

Ch'a: That's right.

VI. Regional Figures

Im [? Likely participant, possibly Yi Yong-sŏl or Pak Hyŏn-su]: Ch'ungch'ŏng Province (忠淸道) also has none.
Chu: In Kyŏngsang Province (慶尙道).
Mun: In Kyŏngnam (慶南 - South Kyŏngsang), Mr. An Hŭije (安熙濟氏)...

**Summary**

This 1931 roundtable discussion among prominent Korean intellectuals, journalists, and professionals delves into the nature of heroism and leadership, both globally and within colonial Korea. Participants debate the definition of a hero, contrasting past autocratic figures with modern leaders supposedly representing the masses (like Lenin). A major theme is the perceived lack of leadership within Korea, attributed to factors like colonial constraints, lingering feudal mentalities, a tendency among Koreans (especially youth) to criticize rather than support potential leaders, and the absence of robust social organizations. They discuss historical figures from Korea (e.g., Kaesomun, Kim Okkyun, the Taewŏn'gun, Yi Sunsin) and the world (e.g., Napoleon, Lenin, Gandhi, Marx), as well as contemporary figures, cautiously naming individuals in various fields like religion (Ch'oe Rin, Han Yongun), socialism (Yŏ Unhyŏng, Kim Yŏksu), business (Kim Yŏnsu), and regional influence (Cho Man-sik), while acknowledging the difficulty and sensitivity of such evaluations under Japanese rule. The conversation highlights the anxieties and frustrations of the Korean intelligentsia regarding national leadership and unity during a period of suppressed political activity. For historians, the article provides valuable insight into the intellectual climate of colonial Korea, the specific individuals considered prominent or controversial at the time (including socialists, nationalists, religious leaders, and collaborators), the criteria used to evaluate leadership, the awareness of international figures and ideologies, and the profound sense of crisis regarding Korea's political and social future under Japanese occupation. The frank, albeit sometimes cautious, discussion on figures like Yi Wanyong, the Taewŏn'gun, and various socialist leaders is particularly revealing.

朱.  오늘저녁 말슴들려 주실 問題는 東西古今人物에 關한 것입니다.  먼저 偉人崇拜, 英雄崇拜의 可否에 對하야 말슴해 주십시오.

一. 英雄崇拜의 古今
金.  거기 對해서는 먼저 무엇이 英雄인가를 作定하야 될 줄 압니다.  그 定義와 見地에 따라서 논점이 달라질 것입니다.  「英雄」을 現實社會에서 大衆을 爲하야 큰 事業을 建設하고 偉功을 세운 사람이라고 하면 그것을 저헤할 사람이 어디 잇슴니까?
朱.  그러나 요새는 그런 大事業은 民衆이 하는 것이 英雄이 하는 것이 아니라고 하지 안습니까?
金.  그것은 英雄의 獨力으로 한다는 것이 아니라 大衆이 한다는 말이겟지오.  그러나 大衆을 代表하고 統率하는 指導者가 없으면 到底히 大事業을 이룰 수 없습니다.
文.  그럼은요.  指導者없는 民衆은 흐터진 모래와 같지오.  이전부터 東洋에서 英雄이라고 하면 사람을 잘 죽이고 專制하기를 좋아해서 民衆側에서는 싫어햇지오.  專制하는 支配者를 싫어한 것임니다.
車.  英雄이라고 하면 過去의 英雄은 個人의 野心으로 무슨 일을 햇고 現在의 英雄은 一般民衆을 本位로 무슨 일을 한 것이 ＜33＞ 다르지 그밖에는 다른 것이 없을 줄로 압니다. 레닌 崇拜者도 과거에 英雄을 崇拜하는 사람의 태도나 다른 것이 없습니다. 自己民族이나 世界的으로...
李光.  秦始皇같이 옛날 英雄은 自己이름으로 무슨 일을 햇지마는 지금 영웅을 끌어가는 것으로 통일하는 것이지오.
金.  옛날 英雄은 獨力的으로 專制的으로 한 까닭에 民衆이 싫어햇지 假令 레닌은 어떤 社會, 어떤 民衆이고 그를 崇拜하지 안습니까.  獨裁로 말하면 쏘베트로서아에서는 形式上으로는 勞働階級을 本位로 하지 마는 「쏘베트」와 같이 獨裁가 甚한 데가 어디 잇습니까.  문제는 大衆을 本位로 하는 것인가 아닌가 하는데 잇는 줄 압니다.
朱.  그러면 民衆的 대사업에 영웅이 必要합니까.  거기 對해서 말슴하여 주십시오.
金.  以前時代에는 사람이 自然生長的이엇고 自然品性自體 그대로엿기 때문에 一般文化와 敎養程度가 低級하여지고 거기에 專制的 英雄이 생겻으나 지금은 知識程度와 活動力이 均等하게 되어서 根本的으로 예와 지금에 다른 點이 많습니다.  그 時代 그 時代에 그 大衆을 土豪로 大事業을 成就한 것이 英雄이라고 할 수 잇습니다.  그 時代에 그 現實 아레서 大事業을 이루는 것이 英雄이라고 하면 누구나 영웅되고 싶을 것이요, 그런 영웅이 잇으면 누구나 崇拜할 것입니다.
車.  英이라면 꽃이라는 뜻이고 雄은 「숳」이란 뜻이니 결국 다른 것보다 우수한 것이라는 뜻으로 압니다.  金炳魯氏 말슴과 같이 過去에는 人智가 暗澹하야 어떤 사람의 頭腦가 투철하면 自己마음대로 할 수 잇엇으나 지금은 제 마음대로는 못하지오.  그러나 지금도 天質이 뛰어나고 非常한 點이 잇으면 自然 中心人物로 모든 사람이 崇拜하게 되겟지오.
文.  그럼은요, 中心人物의 必要 問題가 되겟지오.
金.  지금 英雄崇拜를 싫어하는 것은 「英雄인 체」하는 그것을 憎惡하는 것인 줄 압니다.

二. 朝鮮과 指導者 問題
朱.  지금 朝鮮에는 사람이 없다.  或은 指導者가 必要하다 하는 問題에 對해서는 어떠케들 생각하십니까.

(1) 指導者의 選擇과 服從
