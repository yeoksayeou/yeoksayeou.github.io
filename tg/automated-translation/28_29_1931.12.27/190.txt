Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary/Miscellaneous)

New Year's New Signals from the New Woman
The Woman Laborer Looking Toward the Future
Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng

New signals from the New Woman in the new year. Truly, it seems the name 'New Woman' has become quite well-known. Why is there no 'New Man,' only a 'New Woman'? In any case, thank you. For attaching the splendid character 'New' (新) only to women.
However, the New Woman and the new signals I see are somewhat different from the New Woman you (those who speak of the 'New Woman') see. Lately, at Chongno Crossroads, one sees New Women competing over trendy makeup and fashionable clothes. Among them are those who studied abroad in faraway America thanks to Jesus, those who finished excellent schools and say even being a concubine is fine if one is rich, and the various New Women representative expressions of late-stage capitalism, like those in cafes. The new year's new signals of these New Women are likely being studied while facing the mirror, perhaps reminiscing about Chŏngchaok Samwŏl (a famous kisaeng?).
However, the true New Woman I see, who will ring in the new year's signals and sound the powerful signal for the sacred new life of the future, can only be found within the black coal smokestacks of tobacco, silk reeling, and textile factories. They move their tired legs along with the five o'clock morning whistle that hastens their lives! Here, their steps are indeed the drill for the march that will pioneer this future new life, and their tears and the blood and sweat of their toil signal the promise of the future. Besides them, our Korean women must walk the path trodden by many great revolutionary women like Rosa [Luxemburg], Kollontai, Clara [Zetkin], Naidu, etc.; one misstep, and we can see destruction coming. We must raise the signal with this as our goal. <70>

The Unity of the Masses
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk

Having returned to the mountains and rivers of my homeland after nearly 10 years, the emotions and hopes surging in my heart are truly many. Among them, there is one thing I earnestly wish will happen in the new year and expect effort towards from the advanced gentlemen of society and the aware brothers and sisters. That is the unity of the masses. During my travels through various European countries over the past year or so, and especially during my time in India, I had the opportunity to meet many people of ambition and introduce Korea, albeit slightly. Whenever I did, those with some interest in the Korean question would first ask this above all else: Surely Korea must have a Nationalist Party (國民黨), something like India's Congress must exist, so who is its leader (領首)? Every time I heard this, I experienced a feeling like my face was flushing red, and at the same time, I lamented that we still lack any such organization and wished for such a group to exist in our Korea as soon as possible. Of course, even if our surroundings and environment prevent us from having exactly such a thing, I hope that a group capable of representing this populace, at least within the limits allowed by our surroundings and environment, or within the limits of our visible potential (可能性), will be born this year.

Breaking the Binding Ropes   Reporter Kim Wŏnju

If someone who has the most work to do is bound hand and foot, told they cannot do that work, how frustrating a fact is that even for others to hear? However, for the person who has been bound, rather than feeling frustrated and anxious about the fact of being bound unable to move, it would be more beneficial for oneself to cultivate the physical strength to break those ropes, contemplate how to untie them, and then, having regained leeway, think about the work laid out ahead. We are people bound very unfreely (이미롭지 못하게). Moreover, women are people bound by several more layers.
Must we feel this fact only at the New Year? At this juncture, crossing a short knot in the eternally flowing time, this bound body cautiously opens its mouth to signal to fellow companions who are similarly bound: let us straighten our pale faces (얼골) and hunched (웅그러트린) chests, cultivate physical strength, and simultaneously think and plan to break these tangled (얼크러진) ropes. <71>

Publication of an Organ for the Newly Arising Women   Samch'ŏlli Company, Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi

When I cry out, "Let's liquidate the errors (誤謬) of the past and do better starting this year...," the hardening of my small fist sends a message through the nerve lines scattered throughout my whole body, even into the skull (骸骨) of my head. I would say my main mission is to try to fight for us women, who only cry out (웨치는) for freedom and equality in our hearts within a male-centric society. As for the means and methods, since I am a reporter, I will utilize the media organizations I am directly involved with to increase my true comrades, and as far as my power allows, I bravely(?) declare that I will settle accounts (淸算) with organizations where I have influence, to a certain extent, left and right.
My proposal is that in Korea, unlike in other countries, even if only a few women writers with a sense of purpose exist, they should form a Women Writers' Club (女人文藝家크럽). Even though one might say pioneers like Kim Myŏngsun and Kim Ilyŏp existed in the past, examining them objectively (客觀的으로 檢討) today, I regret that they produced achievements and results that cannot be approved.
Starting this year, I hope women writers will gradually (漸次的) advance and, under one organizational structure (組織體), publish an organ (機關紙) genuinely for women.
This year, I expect that the 'leaders' (리더) who guide us will further eradicate (抹殺) personal connections and, from a strictly (嚴正) objective standpoint, point out and criticize (指適批判) errors stemming from insufficient awareness or conscious errors due to unavoidable circumstances.

Studying Newly Arising Disciplines
An Chŏngok

Before marriage, there were many things I wanted to do, and things I was determined to succeed at, but somehow I ended up becoming a clumsy housewife, so now I'm in a situation where I can't just jump back out and engage in work that suits my taste. Moreover, the work I had always wanted to do and fervently hoped for is film art, which is extremely difficult to succeed at given the current reality (實現) in Korea. However, now, due to circumstances, I have given up (斷念) more than half of it. But I vaguely (漠然히) believe a good opportunity will come and pass the time...

However, the anguish (苦憫) is considerable when I think about what I will become in the end after spending day after day just cooking rice, doing laundry, running errands for my husband (男便). Though I lack talent (才操), a person with individuality should have some special skill (長技) or characteristic to have value, but I dislike growing old as just one ordinary homemaking wife. I regret abandoning my studies too early, but now it's difficult to go out again carrying a book bundle (冊褓). However, starting from the New Year, I intend to decide on one course (課程) and read books systematically (體系). Social science seems most urgent (緊切) and good, but it's utterly difficult, so I can't read it. That said, I don't envy wives who just sit around hitting (뚜드리고) the piano or playing records. <72>

Revolution Begins in the Kitchen
Yi Kyŏngwŏn

It's barely been a month since I got married, but it's amusing to myself and, on the other hand, sad that I've already become a plausible housewife in my daily actions and thoughts. Thinking that I might end up being just a faithful 'hen' (암닭) for my whole life makes me sad and, at the same time, fearful. Spending nearly half the day in the kitchen wearing an apron, what work can I possibly take up in that reduced (縮少) spare time?

The world today faces a great crisis in various senses. The economic and political crises of capitalist nations, and on the other hand, the sharp (尖銳) and intensifying (熾烈化) upsurge (勃興) of the rising labor class movement. Truly, eventful and troubled (多端多事) times are approaching, becoming increasingly difficult and busy. In such times, though slight (微微), there must be help in the right sense; even if one cannot stand on the front line holding a rifle muzzle, there must be sincere effort as the rear guard (後衛), both overtly and covertly. To do so, we must first have a genuine grasp of the times, observation of trends, and preparation of capabilities. That is, we must study theory and tactics more.

If one becomes just a kitchen mom and remains solely a faithful 'hen' (암닭), what leisure (何暇) would there be to do much studying? From this viewpoint (見地), the female class, more than anything else (following 2 lines omitted).
"Revolution begins in the kitchen" — (following 3 lines omitted).
We must strive for the simplification (質朴化) of the kitchen (a poor kitchen doesn't mean simplification is impossible) and the simplification (單純化) of life (simplification is necessary precisely because life is poor) to devote much time and energy mainly to studying, thus busying (汲汲) ourselves with preparing our capabilities.
In this time that grows sharper moment by moment (刻刻으로 尖銳化하야가는), even if this isn't the sharpest and newest signal, I hope it is at least the right signal.

Abolition of Evil Systems   Kaegwan Company, Song Kewŏl

Working harder than men, the wages (賃金) received at most are half those of male laborers. On top of that, oppression (壓制) and exploitation (搾取)! We must absolutely (絶對) oppose this. Thus, fighting to reform the unreasonable social system, permeated by such unpleasant (곱지 못한) things, into a rational (合理) one is the burden placed squarely on the shoulders of us young women. <73> To achieve this, constant (不斷) effort is necessary. This effort arising ceaselessly (끈임없이), interlocking (맛물어), is one condition demonstrating the fervor for women's liberation.

Guiding (引導) the young women struggling (버둥) in the dark streets (暗黑街), unable to find their way, and those tightening their belts in factory corners, towards the path of future light (光明) should be one of the missions (使命) to be achieved as part of the social movement. However, I do not wish to paint a future utopia. Merely from a practical (實際的) standpoint, I first believe the realization (實現) of the following various policies (諸政策) is necessary and also propose we struggle (鬪爭) towards their realization.
1.  Establish complete protection laws for working women (勞動婦人) or professional women (職業婦人) (The important points are: equal wages (賃金) for men and women, establishment of an 8-hour workday system, special holidays (休日) before and after childbirth (産前産後)).
2.  Establishment of women's suffrage (婦人參政權) and women's right to associate (婦人結社).
3.  Equality (均等) of educational institutions (敎育機關) for women (To raise the education level of the female masses, the doors of education for women must be opened (文戶開放)).
4.  Prohibition of the prostitution system (娼婦制) based on human trafficking (人身賣買).
    (Whether licensed (公娼) or unlicensed (私娼), the existence (存存) of a prostitution system originating from (基因) human trafficking is a shame (恥辱) and suffering for women, therefore we must fight to the end).
5.  Abolition (廢止) of various feudalistic (封建的) laws that insult (侮辱) women.
    (The feudalistic laws currently practiced, such as inheritance law (相續法), marriage law (婚姻法), and divorce law (離婚法), show contempt (侮蔑) for women's personhood, so we must fight for the swift abolition of these feudalistic laws).

Until the day the above conditions are realized for us, our spirit (氣焰) will not cease (끄치지 않을).

Women's Liberation Begins with Economics   Hwasin Department Store Female Clerk, Ch'oe Kiyŏng
Miss Ch'oe Kiyŏng

Having left my school days (學窓) and taken my first step into the real world (實社會), entering the business world (商界) for the first time (初陣), I don't have any special impressions, but I think that for us women to escape our unfair situation (處地), economic knowledge is necessary first of all (爲先).
Born into our unusually tragic (悲慘) society, we white-clad people (白衣人 – Koreans), especially women who suffer double pain (二重苦痛) conventionally in society, are unable to grasp equal (同等) rights and status as fellow human beings and face disadvantageous circumstances (境遇) in slave-like (奴隷的) lives because, firstly, we lack economic knowledge and have relied (依賴) on others. To escape the groaning (呻吟) of double pain, it is necessary to cultivate (養成) an economic spirit and possess independent (獨立的) economic thought.
As I greet the new year, I feel desire (慾望) and courage (勇氣) surging up (북바처) in my heart to create a free world, escaping the suffering our general women endure. At the same time, I want to cry out to general women, "The path to our women's liberation lies solely in economic independence. First, eliminate the tendency to depend parasitically (빌붓는) on men." <74>

My Hope is Our Hope   Gasoline Station Attendant in front of the Prefectural Office, Pak Ŏjin

Just because it's the New Year, my hopes aren't anything special. My hope is the hope of all us women laborers, and isn't it unnecessary to speak at length here about the hope of all us women laborers? As we greet the new year, our hearts are merely renewing our resolve (각오) to vigorously do what must be done for the benefit (리익) of our laboring class, rather than having hope or new spirit (신기염).
Specifically, as a gasoline girl (가소링 껄로서), I don't have any particular new spirit. Oh, there is one thing. If wishing not to see unpleasant things is a hope, then there is one. It's nothing other than, as you can see over there, doesn't it say 'No Smoking Here'? Even an elementary school child would know that a cigarette flame is dangerous near gasoline, but gentlemenly (점지않고) types, acting like 'sinsa' (신사), get tipsy (얼근이) drunk and pass by, making a fuss wanting to buy cigarettes. When told this isn't a place that sells cigarettes, they glare with unpleasant eyes asking for matches then. It's exasperating (기가 맥혀서)! Then they take out matches they were carrying, light (부치고) a cigarette, tease (히야카시), and leave. I pray (축수) that such people disappear starting from the new year. I especially wish such gentlemen (냥반) would learn that we are professional women who earn our living through our work. (Interview) <75>

**Summary**

This collection of short articles from the December 1931 issue of *Tongkwang* presents varied perspectives from Korean women identified as "New Women" on their hopes and resolutions for the coming year. The pieces range from critiques of superficial modernity (fashion, cafes) by Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng, who champions factory workers as the true progressive force, to calls for mass political organization by Ch'oe Yŏngsuk, inspired by movements in India and China. Kim Wŏnju uses the metaphor of being bound to urge women towards self-strengthening and strategic planning. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi advocates for a women writers' organization and critical media engagement. Others like An Chŏngok lament the constraints of domestic life while seeking intellectual growth, Yi Kyŏngwŏn calls for simplifying domestic duties ("Revolution begins in the kitchen") to allow time for studying revolutionary theory, Song Kewŏl demands specific legal and social reforms (equal pay, suffrage, abolition of feudal laws and prostitution), Ch'oe Kiyŏng emphasizes economic independence as key to liberation, and Pak Ŏjin, a gasoline station attendant, expresses solidarity with fellow women laborers and frustration with disrespectful male customers.

These texts are significant for historians as they provide a nuanced snapshot of female consciousness and activism in colonial Korea. They reveal internal debates within the "New Woman" discourse, contrasting the experiences and priorities of educated elites, writers, housewives, and working-class women. The articles highlight key concerns of the era: the critique of Westernized consumerism, the search for effective political strategies under colonial rule, the tension between domestic roles and public aspirations, the push for concrete legal rights (labor, suffrage, marriage/inheritance reform), the demand for economic self-sufficiency, and the awareness of international feminist and revolutionary movements (Luxemburg, Kollontai, Zetkin, Naidu, Indian Congress). Pak Ŏjin's piece adds a valuable working-class perspective on daily struggles and dignity.

특별이 가소링 껄로서 별다른 신기염이 잇지 안습니다. 오라, 한 가지 잇습니다. 꼴사나운 것을 보기 싫다는 희망이 희망이라면 한 가지 잇습니다. 그것은 다른 것이 아니라 저기 보시는 바와 같이 여기서는 담배 피우지 말라고 써 부치지 않앗습니까. 가소링 잇는 부근에서 담배불이 위험하다는 것쯤은 보통학교 아이도 잘 알 것인데 점지않고 신사랍시구 하는 분네들이 술이 얼근이 취하면 지나가다가 담배를 사자고 야단입니다. 여기는 담배파는 데가 아닙니다라고 하면 그러면 성냥 좀 달라고 아니꼬운 눈을 흘김니다. 사람이 기가 맥혀서! 그러다가는 자기가 가지고 잇든 성냥을 꺼내가지고 담배 불을 부치고 「히야카시」를 하고 나하니 새해부터는 이런 사람이 없어지라고 축수합니다. 그런 냥반은 특히 우리는 우리가 일을 해서 먹구 사는 직업부인이라는 것을 배왓으면 합니다.
