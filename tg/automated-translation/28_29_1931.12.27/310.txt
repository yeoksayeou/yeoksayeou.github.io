Puppets, To Tongin
						Ansŏ

We are puppets, bodies entangled in strings.
Pull the strings, oh Lord, we shall leap and dance.
This body that will collapse while dancing.
What shall we call this life? A whirling bubble.

We are puppets, the Lord's playthings.
Blow the whistle, oh Lord, we shall play.
Do not call this life wandering,
Song and dance never cease for a moment.

We are puppets, bodies tied to strings.
Pull the strings, oh Lord, leap and play.
This short and long life is a ceaseless fight.
Will there be even one day of peace? This wailing heart.

We are puppets, the Lord's playthings.
Blow the whistle, oh Lord, we shall play.
Even going mad, leaping and playing, is all the Lord's power,
Born for the Lord, we play for the Lord.

We are puppets, bodies entangled in strings. <116>
Pull the strings, oh Lord, we shall leap and dance.
Looking all around, there is no path to take.
In the sea, only water; in the sky, only wind.

We are puppets, the Lord's playthings.
Blow the whistle, oh Lord, we shall play.
We dance the same dance, sing the same song every day,
On the day we rest, we will surely lose this life.

We are puppets, bodies tied to strings.
Pull the strings, oh Lord, we shall dance and play.
How precarious! This exhausting dance every day.
If we fall, we are simply trampled upon.
-La 23an, oktobro, 1931- <117>
<116-117>

**Summary**

This poem by Ansŏ, addressed to "Tongin," uses the metaphor of puppets (꼭두각시) manipulated by a "Lord" or "Master" (님) to depict a life devoid of agency. The speaker describes being forced to dance, play, and fight ceaselessly at the whim of the controller, finding no escape ("In the sea, only water; in the sky, only wind") and facing destruction ("If we fall, we are simply trampled upon") if they falter. The poem expresses a deep sense of fatalism, exhaustion, and helplessness. From a historical perspective, written in 1931 during the Japanese colonial period, the poem's depiction of powerlessness and predetermined suffering under an unseen "Lord" could be interpreted as an allegory for the Korean condition under Japanese rule, reflecting the lack of self-determination and the feeling of being mere playthings in the hands of a controlling power. Historians might find the poem's stark fatalism and the ambiguous identity of the "Lord" useful for understanding the psychological impact of colonialism on Korean intellectuals and artists.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 줄에 얽힌 몸.
줄을 채라, 님이어, 뛰며 춤추리.
춤추다가 그대로 쓸어질 이몸.
이 生을 무어랄까, 휘도는 거픔.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 님의 작난감.
호각불라 님이어, 작난을 하리.
이 生을 放浪이라 이르지 말게,
노래와 춤 잠시도 끊일날 없네.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 줄에 매인 몸.
줄을 채라, 님이어, 뛰며 놀으라.
짧고 길은 이 生은 끊임없는 쌈.
하로나 平安하리, 울부짖는 맘.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 님의 작난감.
호각불라, 님이어, 작난을 하리.
미처서 뛰고 놂도 모다 님의 힘,
님위해 태어나서 님위해 노네.

우리는 꼭뚝각시, 줄에 얽힌 몸. ＜116＞
줄을 채라, 님이어, 뛰며 춤추리.
두로두로 보아도 갈길은 없네.
바다엔 물뿐이요, 하늘엔 바람.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 님의 작난감.
호각불라, 님이어, 작난을 하리.
같은 노래 같은 춤 날마다 추네,
쉬는 날엔 이 生을 잃고 말거니.

우리는 꼭둑각시, 줄에 매인 몸.
줄을 채라, 님이어, 춤추며 놀리.
위태코나 날마다 고단한 이 춤.
넘어지면 그대로 밟히고 마네.
-La 23an, oktobro, 1931- ＜117＞
