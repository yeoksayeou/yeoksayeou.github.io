Author: 丁西林 (Original Author), 丁來東 (Translator)

China Modern Special

Translator's Foreword
This play is the most popularly acclaimed work since the emergence of New Theater in China. Although the original author is certainly not prolific, he is likely given the highest praise for skillfully expressing difficult emotions through lighthearted language. Moreover, among his various one-act plays, this "One Wasp" (一隻馬蜂) is particularly famous, and it is the only one included among the Chinese New Theater works in the "World Drama Complete Collection Publication Society" (世界戲曲全集刊行會) published by Japan Modern Company (日本近代社). However, that Japanese translation was truly an unspeakably bad and erroneous translation. When I was translating this work, I encountered some difficult passages and consulted the Japanese translation, but it was mostly full of errors. Japan is originally famous for the accuracy of its translations, but seeing such mistranslations makes one realize that reputation cannot always be trusted.
As I compared it with the Japanese translation, I marked the mistranslated parts. In this play of barely 20 pages, there were about 50 instances of mistranslation, and among them, several places had entire dialogues of 4-5 lines or one person's speech written completely differently from the original text. Seeing this, I consulted with other fellow students about the more dubious parts and revised it two or three times. Generally, the meaning of the original text has been preserved.

Dramatis Personae
Mrs. Kil: About 50 years old. Thin but healthy constitution, her simple attire is quite neat.
Mr. Kil: Mrs. Kil's son. About 26-27 years old. Strong, lively, extremely ordinary and natural attire.
Miss Yŏ: About 25-26 years old. Beautiful posture, expressive face, neat attire.
Servant

Setting
A small rectangular room. In the middle of the back wall is a double door. To the left of the door is a chair, and against the wall is a small desk with fresh flowers on it. Against the right wall is a bookshelf filled with Chinese and Western books bound in cases. In the inner part of the left wall is a single door; in front of the door is a large window, and beside the window is a writing desk with stationery on it. In the back half of the right wall, there is another door; in the front half of the wall is a bookshelf (bookstand) with decorative items on it. Calligraphy and paintings hang on the walls. In the center of the room, slightly forward and to the right, is a small round table with a tea set on it. To the right of the table is a sofa, and to the left is a long couch suitable for two people. Between these two seats is a small chair with a cushion on it.

(At the opening of the curtain, Mrs. Kil is sleeping on the sofa. A high footstool is under her legs, and a newspaper has fallen from her hand onto the floor.) <105>

Kil: (Slowly opens the left door, sees his mother sleeping on the chair, tiptoes to the chair, takes a thin overcoat, comes to the front of the chair, and gently covers his mother. Mrs. Kil wakes up. Kil smiles brightly.) Were you sleeping?
Mrs. Kil: I was just trying to close my eyes and rest a bit, but I fell asleep before I knew it (sits up).
Kil: An old person's eyes are like a child's eyes. You shouldn't close them. Once they're closed, you can't control them (picks up the newspaper and sits on the small chair).
Mrs. Kil: What time is it now?
Kil: (Takes out his watch from his pocket and looks) It's three fifteen.
Mrs. Kil: Have you been there all this time?
Kil: I wrote two letters in the study (書室).
Mrs. Kil: Ah, yes, yes. You were going to write that letter for me, weren't you?
Kil: Right, I'll write it now. (Goes to the writing desk, sits down, takes out letter paper and an envelope from the drawer, pours water from a bottle, grinds ink, picks up a brush, and prepares to write.) What should I write?
Mrs. Kil: Just write a few words. Tell them the date we are leaving. Tell them to hire a boat and meet us at the port.
Kil: I'll write as you speak. Are we definitely leaving next Tuesday?
Mrs. Kil: Yes, it's already so late, shouldn't we go already!
Kil: (Writing, reading aloud, and speaking simultaneously)...Leaving on the nineteenth to return south. (Stops the brush, counts the days on his fingers) Nineteenth, twentieth, twenty-first (21) (Writes.) Arriving at the port on the twenty-first. Tell Chang Hung (張宏, a name) and Kang's mother to hire a boat and meet us at the port. (Asks.) Is that right?
Mrs. Kil: Yes, it's best to call for the boat from the Li family's fourth son (李老四家) - it's clean. If the Li family's boat is out, calling for one from Teng Hsiang-fa's (鄧祥發) place is also acceptable.
Kil: (Writes.) It's best to call for the boat from the Li family's fourth son. (Writes while reading softly under his breath)... calling for one from Teng Hsiang-fa's place is also acceptable. (Asks.) Is there anything else?
Mrs. Kil: (Thinking to herself) The weather has been quite severe these past few days, so it would be good to tell them to take out the fur clothes from the south room (南房) and air them out.
Kil: Alright. Is there anything else?
Mrs. Kil: Nothing else. (To herself) Wang's mother (王어멈) said she would come back after going home for the holiday, I wonder if she's back by now?
Kil: (Continues writing the letter.)
Mrs. Kil: We should send some gift to Miss Yŏ (余孃).
Kil: (Finishes writing the letter first, then answers, and writes the envelope.) Didn't you say you would send her a set of clothing material? (Finishes writing the envelope.) Right, it's all written.
Mrs. Kil: (Startled from deep thought because of Kil) Are you finished writing?
Kil: (Goes to the front of the chair and holds out the letter) Won't you take a look?
Mrs. Kil: You read it once.
Kil: (Reads the letter.)
To my second older sister...
Mother says, "It's already so late, shouldn't we go already!"
Mrs. Kil: What did you write this for?
Kil: This is the opening of the letter. (Continues reading the letter sentence by sentence.) <106>
"Mother is leaving on the nineteenth and will arrive at the port on the twenty-first. Have Chang Hung and Kang's mother hire a boat and meet her at the port. It is best to call for the boat from the Li family's fourth son. It is also clean. If the Li family's fourth son's boat is out, Teng Hsiang-fa's is also acceptable."
"The weather these past few days has been quite severe, so it would be good to take out the fur clothes from the south room and air them out. Wang's mother said she would come back after going home for the holiday, I wonder if she's back by now?"
I didn't write anything wrong, did I?
Mrs. Kil: (Laughs.) Yes, is this how you all write letters nowadays?
Kil: This is the most fashionable direct-style (直寫式) vernacular writing. If there's something to say, you just say it. Is there anything else you wanted to say?
Mrs. Kil: No.
Kil: Below this is my own business. (Continues reading.)
"Mother has been well during her stay in Seoul this time. However, there are two things that are greatly dissatisfying to her..."
Mrs. Kil: What do you mean I'm dissatisfied about something?
Kil: (Doesn't answer and continues reading.)
"First, the purpose of Mother's visit to Seoul this time was to persuade her son to marry quickly and have grandchildren soon. A grandson with a square head, big ears, chubby and fair body. Ah! Two months in Seoul, and the results are quite meager. There's no sign of a daughter-in-law, so news of a grandson is even more distant. Second, Mother's whole intention was to arrange a marriage between cousins, making her niece her daughter-in-law and her nephew her son-in-law. But unexpectedly, her unfortunate daughter is stubborn, does things her own way, and does not follow Mother's wishes. Because of this, she hasn't said a word for the past few days and is full of worry. That cousin (表姪) is currently asking friends widely and searching in all directions. If sincerity can move heaven, and diligence can move mountains, how difficult could it be to find a suitable wife in the capital, teeming with beautiful women and talented men (佳人才子)! Therefore, within a couple of months, there will surely be a good match. In the future, a celebratory drink might offer some comfort to the old folks. I sincerely hope that all lovers and non-lovers in the world become family (眷屬)."
Did I say it right? You mustn't get angry.
Mrs. Kil: (Slightly displeased) What time do I have to get angry with you youngsters! I've already told you about your affairs from the start. Do as you please, I won't interfere at all. Say whatever you want to say.
Kil: (Seals the letter, affixes a stamp, goes to the edge of the chair, rests one hand on the chair, and strokes his mother's hair with the other.) Mother, you are truly a special woman. Everything about you is extraordinary. You are an unusually good wife and an unusually wise mother. But there is only one thing where you cannot escape the example of other mothers.
Mrs. Kil: Hang up this big coat. (Kil hangs the coat back in its original place. Mrs. Kil reminisces about her past life.) "Wise wife, good mother" (賢妻良母), am I worthy of these four words! (Kil sits back in his previous spot.) When your father passed away, you were only eight, and Yun'er was five. At that time, I didn't trust the teaching methods of the private tutor (私塾先生) - and partly because I disliked you children receiving that barbaric restraint - didn't I resolve to teach you myself? I taught you continuously until you were sixteen. At that time, all our property was just the 50 *mu* of poor land we received when it was divided. Now, do you worry about what to wear <107> or what to eat? It's not bragging, but if you hadn't spent over a thousand won each year on tuition, perhaps our wealth would be ten times greater by now.
Kil: That's why I said it. You are a special woman.
Mrs. Kil: Well, what's so amazing about being a wise wife and good mother? Don't the current generation of new women (新女性) look down on it from morning till night?
Kil: They have points that can be forgiven too. Having been unable to speak for thousands of years, now that they hold pens and write, they just talk and talk, not even knowing themselves what they are saying.
Mrs. Kil: What's there to admire about these current new women? They can't fulfill their roles as human beings, nor manage a household. I don't know what's good about them?
Kil: They are all like vernacular poems (白話詩). No rules, no charm, strange for others to look at, but their merit lies precisely there.
Mrs. Kil: I don't understand what you mean. You don't like this kind of person, and you don't like that kind of person; the old style you call the eight-legged essay (八股文), and the new style you call vernacular poetry...
Kil: That's right. They are all equally tasteless and uninteresting.
Mrs. Kil: Then what kind of person do you want?
Kil: (Shrugs his shoulders) I don't know what's bad myself. Getting a wife is like finding an unknown variable in mathematics; if you just set up the algebraic equation, it's quite easy.
Mrs. Kil: Why are you and your male cousin so different! One pesters here and pleads there, acting as if he can't wait even until tomorrow, while you don't even consider the matter seriously.
Kil: Don't consider it seriously! It's precisely because I view the matter too seriously that I haven't married to this day. If I treated it merely like getting eyeglasses fitted, your grandson would already be in middle school.
Mrs. Kil: (As if resigned) Pour me a cup of tea. (Kil pours a cup of tea, gives it to his mother, pours one for himself, and drinks slowly. Mrs. Kil thinks deeply for a moment.) Do you know, or don't you know? That your cousin (表兄) has asked me several times to act as a matchmaker for him?
Kil: Of course, I know.
Mrs. Kil: Do you know who he asked me to speak for?
Kil: Isn't it Miss Yŏ (余孃)? Did you ask her?
Mrs. Kil: (Replies very slowly.) I haven't asked.
Kil: Why haven't you asked?
Mrs. Kil: Why haven't I asked? (Pauses slightly) I intend to ask today. Would that be alright? (Looks at Kil while speaking.)
Kil: Of course, it's fine. A nurse going to a doctor fits the principle of mutual aid and the spirit of cooperation. It would make the best speech topic at the wedding.
Mrs. Kil: (Sighs softly.)
Servant: (Opens the left door) Madam, Miss Yŏ is here.
Mrs. Kil: Ask her to come in. (The servant exits. Kil puts down his teacup, quickly goes to the desk to arrange the writing implements, and folds the newspaper on the small table.)
Servant: (From outside, opens the left door, lets Miss Yŏ enter, follows her in, and removes the tea set from the small table.)
Yŏ: (Wearing a hat and gloves, holding a bag in one hand, enters, bows to <108> the hosts while taking off her gloves, places the bag on the small table near the door, and removes her hat.) Elder Madam! Mr. Kil!
Mrs. Kil, Kil: Miss Yŏ! (Kil takes Yŏ's hat and hangs it on the chair.)
Yŏ: Elder Madam, I apologize for keeping you waiting.
Mrs. Kil: Not at all. Please sit down. (Offers the sofa to Yŏ.)
Yŏ: Oh, Elder Madam, please sit. Elder Madam, please don't be so formal. I'll sit here. (Helps Mrs. Kil sit on the sofa, sits herself on the small chair, and Kil sits on the long couch.) I intended to come at half-past two, but suddenly a patient arrived, and I was busy tidying up a room until now. I was about to call and say I couldn't come, but then I remembered Elder Madam is returning to the South (南方), and no matter how busy I am, I should spend some time with you.
Mrs. Kil: Thank you. We know things are busy at the hospital, so we haven't asked you to come out until now. Today, since we are leaving for the South soon, we wanted to see you once and express our gratitude. You've truly worked hard this time. First, Mr. Kil stayed for two weeks, all thanks to your efforts, and then myself... We don't know how to thank you enough.
Yŏ: Elder Madam, you are too polite. It's our duty. Elder Madam, are you eating a little better these days?
Mrs. Kil: My stomach is weak. I'm always like this. When I came to Peking (北京) last time, I suffered a little hardship on the road, which made me feel quite unwell, but there was no particular illness. Our Mr. Kil insisted I be hospitalized, saying how comfortable and clean the hospital is. I really didn't want to go. Later, he said again that if my spirits were low, I definitely wouldn't sleep well, so I should go somewhere quiet and rest (靜養) for a few days. I was finally persuaded and then entered the hospital. When I was leaving, he again suggested I stay a few more days.
Kil: Our mother doesn't trust hospitals, nor does she trust nurses.
Mrs. Kil: I never said I don't trust nurses. I just often heard that care in hospitals isn't thorough.
Kil: It's alright. Now you not only trust them, but you also like them.
Yŏ: We know too. That many people outside speak ill of us. I'm not trying to defend myself now, but sometimes it's truly not the nurse's negligence, but rather that some sick madams and young ladies are troublesome. I often joke with other colleagues like this: These kinds of people not only can't do anything but also don't know how to be sick...
Kil: Wanting to be sick and actually falling ill easily isn't originally an easy task either.
Yŏ: Firstly, they don't want to listen to the doctor. They want to do this or that, pressing the call bell dozens of times for no reason. If you tell them not to eat something, a little later they'll want to go out and buy fruit, or a little later ask their family to bring chicken soup. Think about it. If they expect us to attend to them like maids attending to the mistress or young lady in an ordinary household, where would we find the time? On average, one of us attends to ten people. Ah, it's useless to talk about it. How can they understand reason?
Kil: Being a nurse is inherently a very difficult profession. The most unreasonable person in the world is a drunkard, and after that, one can count the sick. <109>
Yŏ: There are many amusing incidents. If you encounter a certain type of peculiar person, when they are just about to recover, they insist on talking incessantly. (Glances at Kil.)
Kil: That must indeed be a predictably annoying thing. If it's a man, it might still be manageable, but if it's a woman, there's probably no way to handle it.
Mrs. Kil: I don't believe that people other than you are like you. Even if there are others as capable as you, they probably wouldn't be as kind and understanding.
Servant: (Enters through the left door. Holds a tea tray containing a teapot, teacups, plates of snacks, etc.)
Yŏ: (As Mrs. Kil is about to pour tea) Elder Madam, please sit. I'll pour. (Pours a cup of tea and gives it to Mrs. Kil.)
Mrs. Kil: Oh, thank you. (Kil pours tea and gives it to Yŏ.)
Yŏ: (Accepting Kil's tea) Thank you. (Is about to pour tea for Kil.)
Kil: Please don't bother. I won't have any tea.
Yŏ: (Drinking tea) Elder Madam, why don't you stay in Peking for a few more days? Since Miss Kil is at home, you shouldn't have any worries?
Mrs. Kil: That child does everything well. But this time I've been away from home for too long. I originally came because Mr. Kil was sick.
Yŏ: I think Miss Kil must be truly capable.
Mrs. Kil: There's nothing to call capability. It's just that I make sure she knows the things a woman should naturally know.
Yŏ: To be as capable as Elder Madam would truly not be easy.
Kil: Being the child of capable parents is quite arduous. During summer vacation, in such hot weather, when you return home, it's only for two weeks. After two weeks, one goes to the countryside to farm, and the other goes to the kitchen to cook.
Mrs. Kil: (Laughs.) I am a very stubborn person. - Now that I'm so old, I'm not afraid of others laughing. - I don't think knowing one more thing necessarily has a downside. I don't believe that a woman who doesn't cook necessarily can't write.
Kil: That's true. But the difficulty is that the woman who knows how to cook doesn't know how to write, and the woman who knows how to write doesn't know how to cook.
Yŏ: Is Miss Kil coming to Peking? I really want to know her. I imagine she must be as peaceful and lovable as Elder Madam.
Mrs. Kil: She has no other merits, just cheerful. I dislike her being flighty (새풍기).
Yŏ: (Pleased) I believe we will surely become good friends. When she comes, please tell her to write to me, Elder Madam.
Mrs. Kil: (To Kil) Don't you have a picture of her?
Kil: I had one, but I don't know where it went.
Yŏ: (As if remembering) Ah, Mr. Kil's letter mentioned Elder Madam wanted me to bring a photograph. I brought it today. (Walks towards the small table.)
Mrs. Kil: (As if not understanding) I never mentioned a photograph. (To Kil) When did I...?
Kil: Why do you say you didn't? You self-assured old folks forget things that happened just a few days ago when talking about the past.
Yŏ: (Pretending not to hear, takes out a small photograph from her bag.) This picture isn't very good. It doesn't quite look like me. When I have a better one later, I'll give you another. <110> (Gives the photograph to Mrs. Kil.)
Mrs. Kil: (Looking at the photo) You've already grown up so prettily. This picture is better.
Kil: (Takes the photo from Mrs. Kil and jokingly says to her) Mother, you usually speak well, why are you speaking incorrectly today? You should have said something like this: "Although this picture is good, it doesn't compare to the person in the picture." (Compares it with Yŏ.)
Mrs. Kil: I only speak the truth.
Kil: Why don't you folks stay a bit longer? (To Mrs. Kil) I'll bring you some nice photos I have. (Takes the photo and exits through the left door. The two women are silent for a moment. Mrs. Kil watches Yŏ, who doesn't know what to say and picks up a piece of candy to eat.)
Mrs. Kil: Miss Yŏ. I've wanted to say something for a long time. (Moves her chair closer. Yŏ quickly swallows the candy in her mouth, straightens her skirt, sits upright, and listens attentively.) You probably think I'm someone who likes comfort very much. But when I was young, I suffered greatly. Since our Mr. Kil lost his father at a young age, I managed all the big and small affairs of the household, even teaching the children myself. I struggled for almost twenty years and barely managed to raise them like this. Now, I don't have to worry about anything regarding their affairs. However, there is still one thing I cannot rest assured about. That is, the children are not yet married. (Yŏ's body trembles slightly. I have spoken to Mr. Kil about this matter several times, but he just doesn't seem to take it seriously.) - I don't know either. What his intentions are. Nowadays, marriage for children is originally not something parents should interfere in, so I just leave them to do as they please. (Sighs and pauses slightly) I have a nephew from my side of the family. (Yŏ shifts and resumes natural breathing.) You probably know him too. He came to the hospital to see me. Although he hasn't seen you many times, he has heard me often speak of how good you are, so he respects you greatly. He has asked me several times to act as a matchmaker, but I never brought it up. Why? Because if I don't even interfere in my own child's affairs, when would I have the time to meddle in others' matters? He says, firstly, he doesn't know your intentions, so he can't speak to you, and secondly, although he wants to talk to you, he hasn't had a good opportunity. He is a very good person. He studied medicine and now runs his own clinic. His character is excellent, beyond reproach, and he has no other flaws. - Ah, I am a very corrupt old woman, I know. I know you young people greatly dislike things like matchmaking. If so, please don't be angry.
Yŏ: (As if dreaming) I am very grateful for Elder Madam's kind intentions. How could I possibly be angry?
Mrs. Kil: He wants to hear an answer before I leave. I don't think this is something that needs to be rushed. If you want to think about it carefully, you can let me know by letter later, I think that would be fine. (Pauses slightly.) What are your thoughts? If you have anything to say, please say it all. As you know, I see you as my own daughter.
Yŏ: (After thinking for a moment, as if resolved) I think that we young people have no experience. We should seek guidance from elders in all matters. What is Elder Madam's opinion?
Mrs. Kil: Hmm-. This is your own affair, so you must decide for yourself. <111>
Yŏ: If it pleases Elder Madam, then it must surely be right.
Mrs. Kil: Then it seems you are quite willing, is that so?
Yŏ: That's true, but I must write a letter to find out my parents' wishes.
Mrs. Kil: Of course, you certainly must do that. Then, you write home, and when there's a reply from home, we'll talk again.
Yŏ: It doesn't seem quite appropriate for me to write the letter alone.
Mrs. Kil: Why wouldn't it be alright?
Yŏ: How about Mr. Kil writes a detailed letter explaining Elder Madam's intentions to my family, and I write a separate letter to enclose with it?
Mrs. Kil: Yes, yes. That's exactly how it should be done. I will tell Mr. Kil right away to write a letter. Then I'll send it to you when it's written. How about that?
Yŏ: Elder Madam's idea is very good.
Mrs. Kil: Shall we sit a little longer? Or shall we go to the park now?
Yŏ: What are Elder Madam's thoughts?
Mrs. Kil: How about we go right now? Let me have the servant call Mr. Kil right away. (Stands up and presses the call bell.)
Yŏ: I'd like to use the telephone for a moment.
Mrs. Kil: It's in the courtyard over there. You know where it is. (Yŏ exits through the right door, and the servant enters through the left door.) Go and ask Mr. Kil to come. Tell him we are going to the park now. (The servant exits through the left door, and Mrs. Kil sits back in her previous spot, seemingly thinking about something.)
Kil: (Enters through the left door. He holds a photograph mounted in a frame. Enters, places the photo on the bookshelf, looks at it once, then moves it.) Where did Miss Yŏ go?
Mrs. Kil: (Deep in thought) She went to make a phone call.
Kil: (Sits on the small chair, picks up a caramel, slowly unwraps it, and asks casually.) How did your matchmaking go? Did you ask her?
Mrs. Kil: I asked.
Kil: What did she say? (Puts the candy in his mouth.)
Mrs. Kil: She seemed quite satisfied.
Kil: (Takes the candy out of his mouth) Satisfied? She said she was satisfied? What did she say!
Mrs. Kil: She didn't say much specifically.
Kil: If she didn't say much, how do you know she was very satisfied?
Mrs. Kil: Is there even a need to say it?
Kil: Ah, that's right. Matters like this don't need to be stated so clearly. Isn't that so? You just need to observe the mood, like the weather, to know. (Mrs. Kil gives Kil a stern look.) Then is it settled already?
Mrs. Kil: She said she would write a letter to ask her parents. So we have to wait...
Kil: Ask her parents! (As if understanding) Ah! (Throws a piece of candy into his mouth.)
Mrs. Kil: What are you laughing at? You're laughing because she respects her parents. Isn't that right? When I heard that, I was very pleased.
Kil: How could that be! Hearing it makes me very pleased too! (Puts another piece of candy in his mouth.) When does she say she'll write the letter?
Mrs. Kil: Actually, she asked me to ask you to write the letter for her. <112>
Kil: Ask me to write it for her! That's quite funny. I'm not her brother, nor her close uncle, why would she ask me to write the letter? This is truly strange and bizarre?
Mrs. Kil: Does it seem bizarre to you? To me, it doesn't seem bizarre at all.
Kil: Why isn't it bizarre?
Mrs. Kil: It's because you don't know her well. You don't know her well. She is a lady from a respectable family, so she knows what words are appropriate to say and what words are inappropriate to say. She knows shame.
Kil: Ooh! Woman! Shame! (Puts another piece of candy in his mouth.)
Mrs. Kil: You usually don't eat candy, why are you eating so much today?
Kil: Today's candy tastes particularly good! (Jumps up happily.) Are you folks going to the park now?
Mrs. Kil: After Miss Yŏ finishes her phone call.
Kil: (Thinks for a moment) Mother, aren't you going to change your clothes?
Mrs. Kil: We're just going to sit in the park for a bit, who changes clothes for that?
Kil: But the weather is quite cool, if you're not changing, perhaps put on another layer! Where is it? Shall I bring it for you?
Mrs. Kil: I'll get it, you wouldn't know where it is. (Kil opens the right door, lets Mrs. Kil exit, then closes the door, goes to the bookshelf, picks up the photo, and examines it closely. Puts the photo down and paces the room twice. Yŏ enters through the right door.)
Kil: Did you make the call?
Yŏ: I did. (Notices Mrs. Kil is not in the room and they look at each other.)
Kil: (Pushes the sofa forward slightly) Mother went back to change her clothes. She asked you to wait here for a moment. Please sit down.
Yŏ: (With a woman's intuition, sensing an interesting discussion is about to happen, prepares to resist, first touches her hair once, straightens her teeth, and sits on the side of the long couch farther from the small chair. Kil sits on the small chair.) Elder Madam is truly an admirable person. Even at her advanced age, she pays more attention to her clothes than us young girls.
Kil: It's not necessary for people to excel in other things, but one must certainly dress reasonably well.
Yŏ: Why is that?
Kil: Because humans are social animals. All the material happiness and mental pleasure a person has while living in the world are given by society. Therefore, people should repay society to the best of their ability.
Yŏ: What does that have to do with dressing?
Kil: The connection is quite large! Repaying society can be done in many different ways. People with professions do it through their professions, those with skills through their skills. Soldiers kill people for us, lawyers handle lawsuits for us, doctors cure illnesses for us. But there is another type of person - people like us - who have no profession and no skills, so at the very least, we should wear a few decent clothes so that when we appear before others, we are not unpleasant to look at.
Yŏ: (Laughs.) Haha, I understand. You mean the more useless a person is, the better clothes they should wear. Is that right?
Kil: Yes. But that doesn't mean useful people should wear bad clothes. Regardless of the profession in society, we cannot approve the absolute right to disregard one's appearance, nor can we approve the privilege that at any <113> stage from birth to death, one does not need to adorn oneself. For example, if a woman, just because she is already married, disregards the height of her hair, or because she has given birth to a son, disregards the length of her sleeves, or if a man, because he has composed a few poems, doesn't know how to keep his face clean, or because he can draw landscapes or insects a little, doesn't know how to shave the beard under his chin or wear his socks straight, then that is a sin against society.
Yŏ: If you put it that way, perhaps we are all sinners against society.
Kil: You mean you? Ah! (Starts to speak but stops.)
Yŏ: What about me?
Kil: You? Didn't I tell you everything two months ago when I falsely claimed to have a fever?
Yŏ: When did I falsely claim you had a fever?
Kil: Then it wasn't a lie? Saying the temperature was 39 degrees and the pulse was over a hundred, weren't those all rumors you fabricated? That's right. Completely rumors. But I am very grateful. If it weren't for your rumors, how could I have stayed in the hospital for two weeks? Ah! Those two weeks! The most cheerful two weeks of my life! (Sighs) Ah, no matter what, they will never come again.
Yŏ: (Recalling that time) Yes. I don't know how much talking we did! I had never seen such a talkative patient before.
Kil: That's right. It was all extremely sincere, extremely ordinary, extremely proper talk. Why can't we talk like that normally? Why must a man pretend to be sick just to be able to speak? Why must a woman listen and then falsely claim he has a fever? If right now I were to tell you how captivatingly beautiful your eyes are, and how lovely your mouth is, you would pretend not to hear, touch my forehead, adjust my pillow, and say, "Rest a little now. You're talking too much," right? Society is truly an unnatural thing! What's wrong with saying such things? Why can't we say them now?
Yŏ: Because you don't have a fever now!
Kil: How do you know I don't have a fever? I haven't had a day without a fever in a whole year. If you don't believe me, test it now. (Extends his hand onto the long couch. Yŏ moves from one end of the long couch to the other, first straightens her skirt, takes his pulse with her right hand, and looks at her left wristwatch. Silence for about a few seconds.) I talked a lot when I was sick, didn't I? (Yŏ nods.) What did I talk so much about?
Yŏ: (Withdraws her hand) You said that China is a pitiful society. And that men are even more pitiful. You said that unless they gamble, they can't meet other people's daughters or wives, and unless they get sick, they can't gain a woman's sympathy. So you play mahjong once a week and feign illness once a month.
Kil: That's right! Is this what a sick person says? Do I have a fever? Or not?
Yŏ: (Hesitantly) 77 beats.
Kil: Your lie has been exposed.
Yŏ: Why?
Kil: Because you didn't even count!
Yŏ: Ah, can a person just lie whenever they want?
Kil: Naturally, one can't just do as one pleases. But in this unnatural society of ours, <114> people ask questions that shouldn't be asked, and words that are harmless cannot be spoken, so we just use the method of lying to cover up many unpleasant things.
Yŏ: We learn from childhood that lying is an immoral act.
Kil: Morality has no standard; it changes with the times and individuals. What is commonly called "so-called" morality is either the superstition of the majority towards the minority or the prejudice of one group of people towards another.
Yŏ: If you put it that way, does it mean there's no standard of good and evil, like and dislike in the world?
Kil: In the world, only dirty habits are bad habits, and dirty actions are bad actions.
Yŏ: So, does it mean that any lie is acceptable as long as it sounds good? That theft or gambling is fine as long as it's done gracefully?
Kil: Exactly. However, people with developed aesthetic nerves (美神經) are very few in the world. When stealing or gambling, most actions are not very pleasant to look at. There are many people who lie well, but the only one I admire the most is you.
Yŏ: I originally don't lie. What proof do you have that I lie?
Kil: That's right! The reason I admire you is precisely because no proof can be found. However, if a person lies too much, they can't always get away with it, and there comes a time when their true colors are revealed.
Yŏ: I originally dislike lying.
Kil: I suppose so. Since just talking is useless, let me ask you one thing.
Yŏ: What is it?
Kil: Mother acted as a matchmaker for you, didn't she?
Yŏ: (Hastily) You shouldn't ask about this.
Kil: Why not?
Yŏ: This kind of talk isn't even asked by my own parents or siblings, how can a friend ask about it?
Kil: Ah, new culture! New culture! But do you know, or don't you know? In the past, marriage was dictated by parents, but now parents don't need to manage it, so parents don't need to ask. (Mr. Kil's opinion is that unless marriage requires no assistance from others, if assistance is needed, parents should be the most important figures. The current feeling that they don't need to ask is partly because they [the children] dictate the matter, and partly because they [the parents] cannot provide assistance. As others have not yet grasped this, it is stated supplementarily.) However, current marriage is dictated by friends, and if one wants to marry, one cannot do without the help of friends, yet you say friends shouldn't ask. This is completely wrong.
Yŏ: I'm going to see Elder Madam. (Stands up as if to leave.)
Kil: (Stands up and blocks her) You can't go, you can't go. I haven't told you the important thing yet. Please sit down. (Both sit down together.) When I wasn't here, Mother talked a lot with you, didn't she? Huh?
Yŏ: Yes, she did.
Kil: Did Mother also mention that I don't intend to get married? <115>
Yŏ: She mentioned it quite a lot.
Kil: You know, don't you? That I don't intend to marry.
Yŏ: Why don't you want to get married?
Kil: Because the most precious thing for any person is their aesthetic sense (美神經), and once a person marries, their aesthetic sense becomes dull.
Yŏ: If you put it that way, then perhaps it's better not to marry after all.
Kil: That's right. Will you join me?
Yŏ: Join you in what?
Kil: Join me in not getting married. (Walks up to Yŏ and spreads his arms.) Let's not get married together!
Yŏ: (Moved by the sincerity and affection in his eyes) Alright. (Gives her hand.)
Kil: Give me proof of that.
Yŏ: What proof do you require?
Kil: An embrace! (Lets go of her hand and makes an embracing gesture.)
Yŏ: (Avoids him.) When you get sick again.
Kil: But my mother told me that you already agreed to become her nephew's wife, what about that?
Yŏ: (Triumphantly) It doesn't matter. My parents don't want to marry me off to a doctor!
Kil: So, I understand. We are a pair born to lie! (Takes advantage of her unguarded moment and embraces her with both arms.)
Yŏ: (Startled, cries out.) Aha! (Mrs. Kil enters startled from the right door, the servant from the left door simultaneously. Kil has already let go.)
Mrs. Kil: What's wrong, what's wrong? (Yŏ covers her face with one hand, her face red, unable to speak.)
Kil: (Goes in front of Yŏ, pulls her hand away, and looks at her face) Where? Were you stung?
Mrs. Kil: What happened? What on earth happened?
Yŏ: (Takes a deep breath) Ah, it was a wasp! (Gives Kil a look of gratitude** **.)

**Summary**

This text is a Korean translation of the Chinese one-act play "One Wasp" (一隻馬蜂) by Ding Xilin, published in the magazine *Tonggwang* in 1932. The translator, Ding Laidong, prefaces it by praising the original's subtlety and criticizing a previous Japanese translation for numerous errors. The play features the elderly Mrs. Kil, her modern-thinking son Mr. Kil, and the capable nurse Miss Yŏ. Mrs. Kil attempts to arrange a marriage between Miss Yŏ and her nephew. However, Mr. Kil and Miss Yŏ engage in witty, flirtatious banter, revealing their mutual attraction and shared modern views that subtly challenge traditional expectations, particularly regarding marriage. The play culminates in a physically close moment where Mr. Kil embraces Miss Yŏ, which is quickly covered up by Miss Yŏ claiming she was startled by a wasp, an excuse Mr. Kil seems complicit in.

From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it showcases the translation and reception of modern Chinese literature in colonial Korea, indicating cultural flow despite Japanese rule. The translator's sharp critique of the Japanese translation can be seen as an assertion of Korean intellectual capability and perhaps subtle resistance. The play's themes—parental matchmaking versus individual choice, the role of "new women," the critique of social conventions, and the use of indirect communication—reflect the social and cultural transformations occurring in both China and Korea during that period, making it a valuable snapshot of East Asian modernity in the 1930s.
