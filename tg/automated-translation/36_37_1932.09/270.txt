**Roundtable on Rural Relief**
Sponsored by the Overseas Alumni Association (海外學友協會)
Location: Ponghwanggak (Phoenix Pavilion) (P'yŏngyang Prefecture, Kirim-ri)

**State of Rural Impoverishment**

No: Our Chosŏn countryside declines daily, and today it has reached a point where it can no longer sustain itself. Our association feels this keenly, which is why we have invited you, teachers, despite the intense heat. First, please tell us about the current impoverished state of our rural areas.

Yi Kyŏng-sŏn: The current state of impoverishment in our countryside can be expressed in one phrase: there is nothing to eat and nothing to use. In other words, the debt (usurious loans) in our rural areas is about 500 million [yen], said to correspond to 60% of the value of cultivated land. Newspapers sometimes report the number of starving households at about 1.45 million. According to recent statistics, it is a fact that not only are owner-farmers and owner-tenant farmers decreasing markedly, but even tenant farmers have now reached a stage of utter collapse.
Besides this, debt is excessive compared to assets, creating a so-called "minus" livelihood situation. Beyond the economic aspect, moral and spiritual collapse is imminent. On top of that, business stagnation and criminal cases increase daily, making the state of impoverishment unbearable to witness.

No: Next, please tell us what causes this phenomenon of impoverishment.

**Causes of Rural Impoverishment**

Kim Tong-wŏn: There are many causes for our rural impoverishment, but my honest thought, my gut feeling, is that a major cause of impoverishment is living beyond one's means, where expenditures exceed income. For example, straw shoes have become rubber shoes, and hemp cloth has been replaced by artificial silk. Most lamentable is that whereas people used to grow and consume their own tobacco, now high-priced monopoly tobacco from the Monopoly Bureau is spreading widely. Next, when our people drink alcohol, many do not balance income and expenditure, drinking not just for socializing but taking out loans at 3 or 4 percent monthly interest, disregarding the balance of income and expenditure.<57>

Kim Hang-bok: The use of chemical fertilizer (金肥) was encouraged too much, disregarding agricultural economics.

Yu Kwan-hŭi: Rural interest rates are too high; there's too much high-interest lending.

Yi Chŏng-sun: Although Chosŏn is fundamentally an agricultural nation, the facts show that rice production policies centered on agriculture and subsidiary business promotion centered on agriculture have failed to adapt to global trends.

Kim Hang-bok: First, Chosŏn society's individual lives lacked training for modern civilized life, that is, cultural life based on economics. Second, the inability to economize consumption; in this regard, measures like strict alcohol prohibition could be one approach. Third, it is regrettable that irrigation cooperatives (水利組合) have currently failed to benefit the vast majority in the countryside based on profit.

Han Kŭn-jo: Shall we call it a lack of self-control? Or perhaps that the use of all consumer goods inevitably becomes necessary? In other words, shall we say it has become mercantilist (重商主義) instead of prioritizing agriculture (重農)? Ultimately, our livelihoods, from ancient times, involved catching fish from rivers and seas without ownership, bringing rice from fields without ownership, and gathering firewood from mountains without ownership. With the advent of modern times, due to forest and land surveys, ownership arose. Wouldn't Mr. Political Affairs Superintendent Kimaida's style (今井田政務總監式) of "Returning to the Old Days" (「還古」), meaning going back to the past, be better than the current mercantilist era with established ownership? Personally, I strongly favor returning to the old ways. I would be grateful if the authorities regarded farmers more highly.

Kim Sŏng-hyŏn: Excessive spending on ceremonial occasions (冠婚喪祭) is the primary reason that annually increases the debt of farmers living in the countryside, leading them to abandon their homes and farming. The second reason is that failing to pay rent to the landlord in the year it's due, and then having to pay it the following year when grain prices have fallen, often turns it into debt.

Yi Kyŏng-sŏn: The excessiveness of public levies (公課金) is a source of suffering every year. Because it's mercantilist, farmers in Japan pay about 8 times more in public levies than merchants and industrialists. Some scholars say this is undue profit for landlords and capitalists due to population increase. Also, tenant rents are mostly somewhat excessive. In rural Japan, the landlord takes 40% and the tenant 60%, but in Chosŏn, there are often cases where the landlord takes up to 80%.

No: As it's very hot today, let's stop discussing the causes of rural impoverishment and trouble you a bit more regarding relief measures. Mr. Yun, please share your opinion on rural finance.

**Relief Measures**

Yun To-sŏng: Regarding rural financial relief measures, a point to consider is that the rural population currently seems divided into two classes: the propertied class and the small farmers & tenants. The sole financial institution in the countryside is the Financial Cooperative (金融組合), but presently, it appears the former, the propertied class, uses it more. Originally, the spirit behind establishing Financial Cooperatives was based on serving small farmers and tenants. However, because money borrowed from the Financial Cooperative was often used for other purposes like ceremonial occasions instead of farming – meaning it was used for consumption rather than production – it eventually led to loans based on collateral (物信) rather than personal credit (人信), deviating from the cooperative's founding spirit. Going forward, Financial Cooperatives should hire technicians to directly guide small farmers and tenants in promoting subsidiary businesses to increase income. They should plan finances based on the original spirit of serving small farmers and tenants, and if possible, obtain special interest-free loans from the government to further lower interest rates.

Kim Sŏng-hyŏn: Regarding ceremonial occasions, administrators should study more concrete plans and, if necessary, cooperate with the private sector, perhaps even using semi-compulsory restrictive methods, to improve efficiency.

Kim Tong-wŏn: Financial Cooperatives identifying intentions to engage in commerce and ensuring, as an absolute fact, that funds do not go out as commercial capital is one major measure against rural impoverishment. In reality, it seems this still happens somewhat, so authorities should pay special attention and exercise strict vigilance.
Beyond the current Financial Cooperatives, it would be good if there were a separate<58> institution acting directly for the countryside, operated by the government. Existing Financial Cooperatives provide neither low-interest funds nor benefits to the rural areas.

Kim Hang-bok: Regarding rural finance, it would be good to lower the interest rates and establish something like an anti-usury association (暴利取締會) with authority and control to enforce this thoroughly.

Han Kŭn-jo: For that, there's also a need to further lower the rates in the existing Interest Limitation Ordinance (利息制限令). I also agree with the proposals recently put forward by the provincial councilors (道參與官) as Chosŏn rural relief measures.

Yi Kyŏng-sŏn: Progressive taxation needs to be thoroughly implemented as social policy.

Hong Sŏng-sam: As a plan for settling current rural debt, a moratorium (모라토리움) is urgently needed more than anything else.

Yu Kwan-hŭi: For reducing rural debt, a moratorium is of course necessary, but repayment periods should be extended, and interest rates lowered.

Kim Tong-wŏn: Rural relief measures are the government's task, and the private sector can only entrust it entirely to the authorities for handling. However, the best approach would be for us to establish a nationwide organization for rural relief research, gather extensive, solid, and detailed data as a basis, draft concrete plans, provide this research material to politicians, and thereby press the authorities into action.

No: What about rice policy?

Han Kŭn-jo: Rice price control itself should generally be fair. Planning to increase Chosŏn rice production and then discriminating against it compared to rice produced in mainland Japan is unjust.

Yi Chi-hyŏn: Rice Production Cooperatives (産米組合) and joint sales systems for silk cocoons (蠶絹共同販賣制度) are causing significant harm in the countryside; these need reform.

Yi Kyŏng-sŏn: Halting all plans for currently unprofitable irrigation cooperatives would be beneficial for the countryside.

Sŏ T'aek-wŏn: Currently, irrigation cooperatives are entirely forced to entrust work to civil engineering contractors. From now on, not entrusting work to contractors but allowing cooperative members themselves to do what they can within their capacity would be in the members' best interest.

Hong Sŏng-sam: Local county (郡廳) and township offices (面所) should supervise local civil engineering projects, ensure local residents are contracted, and avoid commissioning contractors as much as possible to eliminate corruption and undue profits.

Kim Hang-bok: Price increases and currency inflation policies are necessary for rural promotion.

Yi Chŏng-sun: All state-owned land and all privately-owned land should be provided to establish a semi-governmental, semi-private land company (半官半民的 土地會社). This land company would take responsibility for collecting rent and giving it to landowners. The company would distribute tenants collectively, create model villages, ensure no one is idle or unemployed in each village by fully utilizing labor, establish cooperatives for joint cultivation, consumption, credit based on rural needs, and production. Government, landowners, and tenants should cooperate to create entirely ideal rural villages.

No: Currently, the issue of rural self-reliant rehabilitation (自力更生) is frequently arising. Please say a bit more about this.

Yi Kyŏng-sŏn: Promoting farmers' self-awareness and revitalizing their spirit is necessary. Specifically: (1) There is a need to urgently open training centers, lecture meetings, night schools, correspondence courses, etc., to teach knowledge necessary for rural enlightenment (開敎).
(2) Farming methods must be changed.
Shift from single-crop staple grain farming to diversified farming (多角農), apply more science, promote subsidiary businesses, and enrich the countryside.
(3) Thorough implementation of lifestyle improvement, reform of bad customs (廢風改良), resolute abstinence from tobacco and alcohol, self-sufficiency and self-reliance (自作自活) is necessary.
(4) Organization is necessary.
The most urgent issue is farmer organization. There is great power in groups. Without organizing production, consumption, sales, credit, and other necessary associations to pursue individual farmers' benefits and collective welfare, sustaining life is utterly impossible.

No: Time has passed, so let's conclude for today.<59>

**Summary**

This 1932 roundtable discussion, hosted by the Overseas Alumni Association in P'yŏngyang, brought together local elites (businessmen, bankers, educators, officials) to address the severe impoverishment of the Korean countryside. Participants identified multiple causes: farmer debt (especially high-interest loans), declining farm ownership, excessive spending (ceremonies, imported goods, alcohol, tobacco), exploitative tenancy rates, high taxes, the negative impacts of chemical fertilizers and irrigation projects, and government policies perceived as favoring commerce over agriculture and discriminating against Korean rice. Proposed solutions were diverse, ranging from financial reforms (lower interest rates, better cooperative management, debt moratorium/restructuring), government intervention (price controls, land reform via a semi-public company, public works management), changes in farmer behavior (thrift, temperance, diversified farming, scientific methods), and crucially, farmer education and organization (cooperatives for production, consumption, sales, credit) to foster self-reliance (自力更生).

From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable window into Korean elite discourse on the rural crisis during the colonial period. It reveals contemporary understandings of the complex interplay between individual behavior, colonial economic structures (debt, taxes, market integration, Japanese policies), and traditional practices. The frank discussion of issues like discriminatory rice policies, the failures of colonial development projects (irrigation cooperatives), and the call for farmer organization and self-awareness highlights Korean agency and critique within the constraints of colonial rule. Historians might find the mix of proposed solutions—from advocating personal austerity to demanding systemic changes like land reform and fairer policies—particularly interesting, reflecting the varied ideological currents present among educated Koreans at the time. The mention of Political Affairs Superintendent Kimaida suggests awareness of specific Japanese administrative figures and policies.

기외에도 부채가 자산에 비하면 과다케 되어 소위 마이나스 살림상태는 경제적으로         보는 외에도 또 정신적, 도덕적 몰락이 임박하여 그 우에 영업불량, 범죄건이 逐日          증가가 되어 피폐상태는 목불견이외다.
盧     그 다음에는 이 피폐현상이 무엇에 원인되는지를 말슴하여 주십시오.

柳寬熙  농촌금리가 너무 고이자가 많습니다.
