Lullaby
						Kim Sich'ang (金時昌)

Hush hush, sleep well, hush
Our baby, sleep well, hush
Closing your willow-like slit eyes, you go to dreamland
Seeking dreamland, you become a large butterfly
Dancing in a garden where flowers bloom
If you open your eyes for no reason, you're a bad child
If you whine, I won't take you to the back hill either.

Hush hush, sleep well, hush
Our baby, sleep well, hush
Snoring prettily, you go to dreamland
Seeking dreamland, you become a water strider
Circling in the ditch of a deep valley
If you wake from your dream for no reason, you're a bad child
If you whine, I won't take you to the front stream either.<97>

**Summary**

This poem is a simple, traditional Korean lullaby by Kim Sich'ang. The speaker coaxes a baby to sleep, describing pleasant dreams of becoming a butterfly in a flower garden or a water strider in a valley stream. Each stanza ends with a mild threat: if the baby wakes up or whines unnecessarily, they won't be taken on outings to the back hill or the front stream. From a historical perspective, the poem offers a glimpse into child-rearing practices and common imagery used in early 20th-century Korea. The language, including older spellings and dialectal forms ("잘두자장," "이뿌장한," "소곰쟁이," "돌앙," "압시내," "꾸리길며"), reflects the Korean vernacular of the 1930s colonial period and provides valuable linguistic data for historians studying language evolution and regional variations during that time. The gentle connection to nature (butterflies, flowers, hills, streams) paints a picture of rural life.
