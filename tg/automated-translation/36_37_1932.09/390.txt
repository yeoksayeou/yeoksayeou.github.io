Student Life March

It is delightful that the Student Life March, composed of the two parts "Hurrah for Our School" and "Critique of Girls' Schools," has won great acclamation from male and female middle and professional school students across all of Chosŏn. However, this is certainly not meant to be a great help to student life. As stated in the July issue of this magazine, this is a column for "ten minutes' rest in student life." "And how could one not say that such a ten-minute rest column is precious throughout one's entire life?"
Indeed! The energetic sound of "Hurrah for Our School," sung loudly with hopes full for the future, has resonated throughout the three thousand *ri* of our land. We will now introduce a portion of it, but please understand in advance that not a few contributions had to be omitted due to space limitations. Whereas last time we focused on Kyŏngsŏng, this time we have prioritized the provinces.
Compared to the "Critique of Girls' Schools," the "Critique of Boys' Schools" contained not a few instances where the uniquely sharp observation and piercing blows characteristic of women could sufficiently send a chill down the spines of male students. However, even a ten-minute rest column has its limits, and there are space constraints; moreover, it is precisely the autumn for reading! Wondering if laying things out too lengthily would be appropriate, we have decided to omit the "Critique of Boys' Schools" entirely and promise it for a later date. We ask for the understanding of all those who sent in their valuable manuscripts. (1 Reporter) <87>

**Summary**

This article from the September 1932 issue of *Tonggwang* magazine presents survey results from male students across colonial Korea, critiquing various girls' schools ("Critique of Girls' Schools") and boasting about their own schools ("Hurrah for Our School"). Students evaluate girls' schools based on perceived atmosphere, student demeanor (lively, ladylike, extravagant, simple), appearance, and academic focus, often employing stereotypes. The second part allows male students to highlight their schools' pride points (academic achievements, sports dominance, unique traditions, famous teachers/students) and identify friendly and rival institutions, revealing regional networks, school spirits, and intense inter-school competition, particularly in sports like soccer and basketball. The article, framed as a "ten-minute rest," provides candid, sometimes humorous or critical, glimpses into student life, perceptions, and priorities in 1930s Korea.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its raw portrayal of student attitudes during the colonial period, reflecting social norms, gender expectations (e.g., critiques of girls' "extravagance" or "masculine" traits), and the strong emphasis on school identity and rivalry, often tied to sports prowess or academic reputation. It showcases regional differences and networks (Kyŏngsŏng vs. P'yŏngyang vs. provinces), the influence of mission schools, the emergence of specific school cultures, and the students' awareness of their place within the colonial education system and broader society, including mentions of figures like Chaplin and Gandhi, and the use of loanwords ('modern', 'OK', 'sports', 'team').

學生生活行進曲
「우리 학교 만만세」 「여학교 평판기」의 2부곡으로 된 학생생활 행진곡이 全조선 男女中等專門學生의 대갈채를 博한 것은 유쾌한 일이다. 그러나 이것이 학생생활에 큰 도음이 되겟다는 것은 물론 아니다. 본지 7월호에 일언한 바와 같이 이것은 「학생생활 십분 휴식」의 장란이다. 「그러고 그 십분휴식의 장란이 인생생활 일생을 통하야 어찌 귀하다 하지 않으랴」 하는 것이다.
과연! 장래에 가득한 희망을 가지고 소리 높이 부르는 「우리 학교만만세」의 기운찬 소리는 삼천리 강산을 올리어왓다. 이제 그 일단을 소개할 것이나 지면관계상 할애한 분이 불소한 것을 미리 양해해 주기 바란다. 전번에는 경성을 중심으로 한데 반하야 이번에는 지방을 위주로 하엿다.
「여학교 평판기」에 비하야 「남학교 평판기」는 여자의 특유한 예민한 관찰과 통쾌한 일격이 능히 남학생의 심흉을 서늘하게 할만한 것이 불소하다. 그러나 십분 휴식의 장란에도 정도가 잇고 지면의 관계도 잇거니와 때는 정히 독서의 秋! 너무 길게 널어놓는 것이 어떨까 하야 「남학생 평판기」는 일체 할애하고 후일을 약하기로 한다. 貴稿를 주신 여러분의 양해를 비는 바다.(1기자)＜87＞
