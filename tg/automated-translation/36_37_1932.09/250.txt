**Body Text Translation:**
What is in the Hearts of Senior Figures After 10 Years of Enduring Hardship and Acting Prudently?

Life is in Death, Death is in Life - Ch'ŏndogyo's Ch'oe Rin

Reporter: Through the pages of "Tonggwang" magazine, we'd like you to convey your thoughts to the Chosŏn populace. It has been over 10 years since you endured hardship and refrained from overt activities. During that time, the situation in Chosŏn has also changed significantly. Looking at the present Chosŏn, what are your thoughts about the future?

Ch'oe Rin: (Remains silent for a long while) I do not like being published in newspapers or magazines.

Reporter: Of course, due to our circumstances, there may be things you cannot say, but surely you can speak to the extent possible? Currently, what kind of measures do you envision regarding the impoverished rural populace?

Ch'oe: I believe one can speak according to one's "status" (신분). For example, as a member of a family, one has the "status" of a family person; as a member of society, the status of a societal person. Now, as a member of society, I ought to have opinions on social phenomena, but I consider myself like a "widow" (寡수) guarding a temple, or perhaps an old maid. How can an old maid comment this way or that about worldly affairs?

Reporter: However, even if a "widow," being a member of society, wouldn't she have thoughts about what should be done regarding social phenomena, especially in Chosŏn where she lives? Please speak, even setting aside political issues.

Ch'oe: Our lives can be divided into various aspects like political issues, economic issues, or cultural issues, but I believe that all problems, especially economic issues like the currently most pressing matter of livelihood stability, cannot be resolved without resolving political issues. There is much talk in the papers about rural relief, but isn't that just like a musical score without an instrument? Do we have such power? I know it cannot be done.

Reporter: But even if we lack power, can we just remain idle? A way to live emerges only when we proactively do something. Isn't that so?

Ch'oe: Since the Chosŏn people are in a passive state, what power do they have? However, I believe the general public must have a great awakening. My words are vague, but I believe there must be a great realization. Unless there is the resolve to seek "Life in Death, and Death in Life" (生在死中이요 死在生中), I believe it won't work. Among those called "leaders," I wonder if even few possess such resolve. Will such an awakening occur? If there is no resolve for "life in death," it is futile. Otherwise, measures like rural relief will amount to nothing more than gently stroking a boil on a patient who requires major surgery.

Reporter: That is the point I wanted to ask you, sir. Such resolve <52> is, so to speak, a fundamental attitude or a prescription. To instill such resolve, or to take a step into concrete action with such resolve, shouldn't there be a "doctor" (의사)? How do you think of yourself, sir?

Ch'oe: I am a widow guarding a temple. If it proves useful, I am prepared to offer my body as a sacrifice. I am prepared to offer my body (몸떵어리) and everything my strength allows.

Reporter: The meaning of "widow" or "old maid"?

Ch'oe: I do not intend to offer an explanation (釋明).

Reporter: Will the Chosŏn people be able to possess such an "awakening"? Or will they be unable to muster such strength and just end up XX [text obscured/omitted]?

Ch'oe: I believe even Heaven cannot do anything about the strength of people determined to live. (Following 3 lines omitted)

Reporter: The world believes it is waiting for you to come out and work. Do you have any intention of emerging as a "doctor"?

Ch'oe: Would the world think so? No. Shall I say I am a "widow" guarding a "temple," or an old maid? So, the world is just observing idly, wondering why that old maid doesn't get married to some husband. They are not trying to arrange a marriage for her. If they were to arrange a marriage, I would go. Of course, there is the head of the household (가장). If the head of the household, by resolution of a family council (가족회의), tells me to marry, I will gladly offer my body. (Responsibility for the text lies with the reporter)

What Can Be Done with Individual Strength? - Yun Ch'i-ho

Reporter: "Sir, you have spent over 10 years neither chirping nor flying (不鳴不飛). Please tell the 200,000 populace through Tonggwang about your aspirations for any future endeavors."

Yun: "Please write that I have no aspirations whatsoever."

Reporter: "Surely that cannot be. I hear that you have much interest in the rural movement and the spread of primary schools. Do you have any plans in that direction?"

Yun: "Things like that... And besides, what can one possibly do with individual strength? These days too, they clamor about rural relief, but what means do we truly have?"

Reporter: "If individual strength is insufficient, shouldn't influential elders exert themselves to move the power of the state?"

Yun: "Will it work? No, it won't."

Reporter: "Then, setting aside political issues, surely you have expectations in other areas?"

Yun: "..." (No answer).

Besides these, there were responses from several others, but they are unavoidably omitted. <53>

**Summary**

This article presents interviews with two prominent Korean figures, Ch'oe Rin and Yun Ch'i-ho, in 1932, after a decade of relative public silence. Both express deep pessimism about the possibility of effective action to improve Korea's situation, particularly regarding rural poverty, under the constraints of Japanese colonial rule. Ch'oe Rin uses metaphors like being a "widow guarding a temple" or an "old maid" to describe his perceived powerlessness and reluctance to engage publicly unless called upon under specific conditions, emphasizing the need for a fundamental "awakening" and resolve ("life in death") among Koreans, while doubting its feasibility. Yun Ch'i-ho is even more blunt, stating he has "no aspirations" and dismissing the effectiveness of individual efforts or influencing the state for rural relief. Historically, these interviews are revealing of the subdued political atmosphere and the sense of impotence felt by even established Korean intellectuals and former activists during this period. Their coded language and explicit reluctance to discuss politics or concrete plans underscore the repressive environment and the strategic calculations individuals made when speaking publicly under colonial surveillance.
