Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Dallaejang (Wild Chive Soup/Paste)
						O Akkyo
Putting fresh wild chives in soy paste, boiling soup in an earthenware pot (구수동이),
Though it's the taste I tried back in the early spring that passed,
Eating alone without my beloved, I do not know its sweetness.

Halmikkot (Pasqueflower)
The pasqueflowers on the back hill are endlessly pitiful.
Blooming first on a frosty morning to announce the news of spring,
Child, lest you happen to break them, I give advance warning.

Jeuksa (Impromptu)
The sound of a pipe from the front stream, the sound of many birds on the back hill;
On a tender spring morning, every sound is full of joy, yet
The sound of driving cattle in the long field, I hear most gladly. <97>

**Summary**

These three short poems (sijo) by O Akkyo capture moments of everyday life and nature in rural Korea. "Dallaejang" expresses loneliness and nostalgia through the taste of wild chive soup eaten alone. "Halmikkot" reflects affectionately on the early-blooming, vulnerable pasqueflower. "Jeuksa" contrasts various pleasant spring sounds, finding the sound of farm work—driving cattle—the most welcome. From a historical perspective, these poems offer a glimpse into the quiet, personal sentiments and connection to nature experienced by Koreans during the colonial period, focusing on pastoral themes rather than overt political commentary. They reveal the persistence of traditional poetic forms (sijo) and highlight the details of rural life and seasonal changes that remained central to many people's experience.

달래장
						吳樂敎
햇달래 장에 넣고 구수동이 국끄리니
지나간 이른봄에 먹오보던 맛이연만
임없이 홀로 먹자니 단맛 몰라 하노라.

할미꼿
뒤동산 할미꽃은 개륵하기 한이 없네
봄소식 전하려고 서리아침 먼저 핀 게
아이야 행여 꺾을가 미리 당부하노라

卽事
앞시내 피리소리 뒤동산에 뭇새소리
다정한 봄아침에 소리마다 흥이연만
사래밧 소모는 소리 더욱반게 듣노라. ＜97＞
