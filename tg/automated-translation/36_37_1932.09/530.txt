Sorrowful Scenes of the Farming Village and 1 Other Poem
						Song Sun-il

1
When sparks fly from the hoe blade and sweat flows down the back,
Onto burning lips, only fiery breath is splashed,
In a starving mouth, how could even dancing be enough?
Even the cicada crying in the distant forest sounds like a thorn in the ear.

When the cicada's song fades
All the crops ripen, but,
Where do the ripening crops go?

2
As the monsoon rain clears, as the great wind passes,
Children with shaggy hair gather in the orchard,
Children drooling over fallen unripe fruit,
Even that, they can't enjoy freely under the owner's glare.

When the summer monsoon passes
All the fruits will ripen, but,
Where do the ripening fruits go? <100>

3
Working as if starving, wheat porridge uncommon,
Still, at lunchtime, returning to the empty house,
The son, back from school, sniffles in the yard, saying
You said you’d sell the wheat, why don't you give me the "tuition fee"?
My teacher says you only deceive...

They say, no matter how hard it gets, there's food for rats and daughters,
That's all foolish talk from the old days.
The daughter visiting in the seventh month, shouldn't she be welcome?
Instead, her coming is resented.
Can't afford even a slice of meat—
Can't afford even one piece of clothing—
Whenever there's a chance, nagging the eldest son,
The tearful complaints of the old mother!

4
Even if the whole family clings together
To raise the spring silkworms,
They can't even buy one *ja* of hemp cloth,
It's not even worth the price of silkworms.
Yet the village clerk fusses,
Telling them to raise autumn silkworms again. <101>
Even if only the black dog barks,
The debt collectors stir restlessly,
Is the bailiff (집달리) coming?
Is someone from the "Cooperative" (조합) coming?
(1932.7.2)

What am I crying out for?
I have a mouth but cannot speak.
I am not mute, yet I cannot speak.
Though I lack the eloquence of Su Qin (소진),
My mouth is unfortunate, unable to freely speak
The words boiling up, choking my throat,
Ah, the blood in my heart cries out loud.
What am I crying out for? My heart is breaking, my heart is breaking.

My eyes do see.
But not one satisfying or refreshing thing is seen,
Only shapes as desolate as
A worn-out pair of shoes discarded by the roadside, <102>
Ah, the blood in my heart cries out loud.
What am I crying out for? My heart is breaking, my heart is breaking.

My ears do hear sounds.
Though I try to hear the gentle breath of "Eden"
Where lions and deer frolic,
Only the pitiful sound of a sheep being shorn,
Like a chick snatched by a kite,
Only desperate screams are heard,
Ah, the blood in my heart cries out loud.
What am I crying out for? My heart is breaking, my heart is breaking.

Still, let's stop crying, let's wipe away the tears.
A collapsing house
Cannot be propped up and rebuilt with weeping.
Though this body is useless like a worm-eaten stick,
If it cannot be a pillar, would it refuse to be even a rafter?
If not even that, then at least a single strand of straw rope.
1932. 7 <103>

**Summary**

The work consists of two poems depicting the harsh realities of rural Korean life in 1932. The first poem, "Sorrowful Scenes of the Farming Village," portrays the cycle of intense labor yielding no reward, the hunger and poverty faced by families, the inability to afford education, the burden of debt, and the constant fear of collectors and officials. The second, untitled poem expresses deep anguish and frustration at being unable to speak out against injustice or see any hope, hearing only sounds of suffering. It concludes, however, with a resolve to stop weeping and contribute in any small way possible, like a rafter or even a strand of rope, to rebuilding what is broken. For historians, these poems offer a poignant glimpse into the economic exploitation and social despair experienced by Korean farmers under Japanese colonial rule. The specific mentions of tuition fees, low prices for agricultural products (silk), debt collection practices involving officials and cooperatives ("조합"), and the overall sense of powerlessness vividly illustrate the socio-economic conditions of the time.

農村哀相 外 1篇
						宋順鎰
1
허미날에 불꽃일고 등허리에 땀흐를 때,
타오르는 입술에는 끼얹나니 불낌인데,
헐먹은 입속에는 춤인들 혼하리오.
먼숲에 우는 맴이도 그귀엔 가시같애.

맴이소리 사라질 때
모든곡식 익것만,
익는곡식 어데로.

2
장마ㅅ비 개이자, 큰바람 지나자,
과수밭에 모여드는 더벙머리 아이들,
떠러진 선실과에 춤흘리는 아이들
주인의 눈총맞아 그것도 맘못놓아.

녀름장마 다지나면
모든실과 익것만,
익는실과 어데로.＜100＞

3
밀죽도 흖지안어 굶다싶이 일하고,
그래도 점심때라 뷘집에 돌아가니,
학교갓든 아들아이 마당에서 쿨적이며
밀팔아준다드니 「수업료」를 웨안주워
속이기만 한다고 우리선생 가라는데...

아모리 어려워도 쥐와 딸은 먹일것 잇다고
다 어리석은 옛날소리러라.
칠월나드리온 딸애기 반갑지야 않으리만
도리혀 온것이 원수-ㄹ네라.
고기한칼 못하온다-
옷한가지 못해준다-
틈만나면 맛아들을 졸라대는
늙은어머니의 눈물겨운 하소연이어!

4
온식구가 매달려서
봄누에를 첫다해야
뵈한자도 못사는걸
누에값도 안되는걸
가을누에 또치라고
면서기는 야단이지.＜101＞
검정개만 짖어도,
도리꾼들 수서수선,
「집달리」가 오는가
「조합」사람 오는가.
(1932.7.2)

무엇을 바라고 우는지
나는 입이 잇어도 말을 못합니다.
벙어리는 아니여도 말을 못합니다.
소진같은 말재조는 없어도,
목메이게 끓는말을 마음대로 못하는
불행한 입이어니,
아아, 나의 가슴의 피는 소리처 웁니다.
무엇을 바라고 우는지 가슴이 메여 가슴이 메여.

나의 눈은 보기는 합니다.
그러나 맘에 맞고 시원한 일 하나도 안보이고
길가에 버림받은 해여진 신짝같이도,＜102＞
이따의 쓸쓸한 모양만 보이거니,
아아, 나의 가슴의 피는 소리처웁니다.
무엇을 바라고 우는지 가슴이메여 가슴이메여.

나의 귀는 소리를 듣기는 합니다.
사자와 사슴이 재롱부리는 『에덴』의
부드러운 숨소리를 들으려 해도
털깎이는 양의 애처러운소리,
솔개에게 채인 비닭이같이도,
안타까운 비명만 들리거니,
아아, 나의 가슴의 피는 소리처웁니다.
무엇을 바라고우는지 가슴이 메여 가슴이 메여.

그래도 울기는 그만두자, 눈물을 싲어버리자.
묺어지는 집채를
울음으로 고이고 세우지는 못하리니,
좀먹은 작대같이 쓸모없는 몸이라도,
기둥감이 못되면 석가래들 마다하랴,
그것도 못되거든 한오리 새끼라도.
1932. 7 ＜103＞
＜100-103＞
