Magazine Title: 동광 제25호
Publication Date: 1931년 09월 04일
Article Title: 사라지는 그림자(序曲外 1幕), 戱曲 (The Fading Shadow (Prologue and Act 1), Play Script)
Author: 蔡萬植 (Ch'ae Manshik)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

The Fading Shadow
						Ch'ae Manshik
Characters
Kim Sŏndal: Around 60 years old
Wife: Around 60 years old
Sangwŏn: Around 40 years old
Daughter: Around 27 years old
Inwŏn: Around 23 years old
Sŏnggŭn (Maternal Grandson): Around 10 years old
Chongshik (In the prologue, Kim Sŏndal’s messenger): Around 40 years old
Others: Kim Sŏndal’s friends, village men and women, numerous beggars

Time
Early Summer 1931 (Prologue is 15 years earlier)

Place
Rural village

Prologue
In front of Kim Sŏndal's house gate. A large main gate is wide open facing the stage center. To the right is a row of outer buildings (줄행랑), to the left, beyond a low wall (개와담), the eaves (첨아) of the men's quarters (사랑채) are visible. Inside the main gate, a privacy screen wall (채면담) blocks the view. To the left is the middle gate leading to the men's quarters, and to the right is the middle gate leading to the inner quarters (안채). When the curtain rises, shabbily dressed village men and women, male and female servants carrying tables on their heads, and elegantly dressed guests bustle about or slowly enter and exit through the main gate. Various beggars gather beside the main gate, shouting. From the men's quarters, the loud sounds of a feast mingle with the leisurely strains of *Samhyŏn* (三絃) music. This continues for about 2 minutes.
Chongshik: (Emerging from the middle gate of the men's quarters inside the main gate, seeing the beggars) Who are all these people! Huh.
Beggars: (Simultaneously) Please give us something to eat from this generous house.
We came to get food from the feast house.
We've been waiting like this since morning.
Please give us a bite to eat quickly.
Let us have a cup of wine and send us off generously.
Chongshik: (Waving his hand outward) Not now. Come back later in the evening. Go over there and wait, then come in the evening.
Beggars: (Simultaneously) We've been waiting since morning.
Please just give us something to eat, anything.
Let us quickly get a bite and go elsewhere.
Chongshik: (Eyes glaring) Won't you leave!
Kim Sŏndal: (Coming out from the middle gate of the men's quarters, sees them, and steps outside the main gate) What's all this commotion?
Chongshik: (Bowing slightly and stepping aside) It's the beggars...
Kim Sŏndal: Shouldn't you feed the beggars and send them off quickly! Hurry, set out wine, rice cakes, and plenty of meat so they can all eat well. Hurry! (Looking at the beggars) They'll bring out the food right away, so eat before you go. Huh? If it's not enough, ask for more and eat until you're full.
Beggars: (Simultaneously) We are deeply grateful.
We await your kind disposal.
We've been waiting since morning...
Kim Sŏndal, Chongshik (Exit as the curtain falls.)

Stage <74>

Kim Sŏndal's current house. A humble hut with worn-out lumber and roofing. To the right, past a skeletal fence and a tiny plot of land, stands the house; a shed and the men's room (사랑방) are visible, with a narrow wooden porch (뒷마루) in front. To the left, a screen made of straw matting (거적) serves as a nominal boundary with the inner room (내실). The front of the stage is a narrow yard. At the right fence, a similarly nominal wicker gate lies askew. The yard, fence, and plot are overgrown with weeds and shrubs typical of the season. Around 2 PM. When the curtain rises, Kim Sŏndal, his Wife, and Daughter are sitting around a straw mat spread in the yard, cutting the ears off harvested barley stalks.
Wife: If only we had given this some ammonia (아모니), it wouldn't have failed to grow like this!
All: (Silence for a while)
Kim Sŏndal: Hoo—I'm hot and thirsty.
Wife: (Anxiously) You must be thinking of rice wine again!
Kim: My incorrigible gut still remembers the amount I used to drink...
Wife: They say you become a child when you get old. (Looking at the Daughter) Go fetch some cool water for him. (Sighs)
Daughter: (Starts to get up)
Kim: Never mind.
Daughter: (Sits back down) Mother.
Wife: Yes?
Daughter: Let's sell just one *mal* of this barley at the market tomorrow.
Wife: What for?
Daughter: (Glances at her father, then gives her mother a meaningful look)
Kim: (Realizing) You're talking nonsense too. The old man wants to buy alcohol for a snack when we're about to starve any minute, and you talk of selling barley!
Daughter: (Almost crying) How much could you possibly buy? While you suffer like this, wanting it so badly, you can't even have a single cup of rice wine...
Kim: Don't even utter such thoughtless words. One *mal* of barley can feed our family for a day, can't it?
Wife: Whether it feeds us for a day or a month, leave that aside, the price of barley is just too low.
Daughter: They said it was 23 *chŏn* at the last market day.
Kim: Hoo. Grain is so cheap in the world, yet we live starving like this!
Wife: Back when we lived well, even the rice that flowed down the drain hole (수채 구먹) would be enough for us to eat now.
Daughter: If only we could collect all the debts owed to us...
Kim: Even if the entire three-thousand-*ri* land of Korea were washed away, I wouldn't feel regret, but losing the Ulmulbaemi *hansŏmjigi* plot—I won't be able to close my eyes even when I die.
Wife: (Speaking to the Daughter) That paddy field, your father inherited it from your grandfather, and using that as capital, he built up a fortune worth 3,000 *sŏk*.
Daughter: I heard second older brother pawned it (잽혓다면서요) when he left for Qing China (청국)?
All: (Brief silence)
Wife: (Almost crying) It's already been over six years, and we don't know if he's dead or alive. No news since he left...
Kim: (Verbally only) That damn unfilial son! Abandoning his mother and father to go to a foreign land thousands of *ri* away! Humph!
Wife: Still, don't say such things. He thought he was doing something important when he left. (Pause) Besides, how much must he be suffering in a foreign land thousands of *ri* away?
Kim: What 'important work'! Even the ancient sages said, "Cultivate oneself, regulate the family, govern the state, bring peace to the world (修身齊家治國平天下)." A man who can't manage himself or his own household, who ran off leaving his old parents to suffer like this—what kind of important work could that be! Wife: Still, there's no finger you can bite that doesn't hurt. If only I could see him once before I die, it wouldn't be such an eternal regret. <75>
Sŏnggŭn: (Enters listlessly through the wicker gate, carrying his school books wrapped in a cloth)
Wife: (Sees him first) Sŏnggŭn is here.
Kim, Daughter: (Watch him anxiously)
Sŏnggŭn: (Goes to Kim Sŏndal) Grandpa.
Kim: Yes.
Sŏnggŭn: (Silent)
Daughter: Why are you bothering Grandpa?
Wife: (Quietly) He's hungry. The poor child went ten *ri* and back without even eating breakfast, wouldn't he be?
Kim: Sŏnggŭn-ah.
Sŏnggŭn: Yeah. (Sits beside him)
Kim: Are you hungry?
Sŏnggŭn: Yeah.
Kim: Wait there. When your eldest uncle comes with rice, I'll tell your mother to cook for you, okay? Don't cry.
Sŏnggŭn: No.
Kim: What? You're not hungry?
Sŏnggŭn: N-no.
Kim: Well then, your eldest uncle will bring rice soon.
Sŏnggŭn: No.
Kim: Then what?
Sŏnggŭn: Teacher said because I didn't bring the monthly tuition fee (월사금)...
Kim: Ah, the tuition fee.
Sŏnggŭn: He said not to come starting tomorrow.
Kim: (Strokes his head, clucking his tongue.) Your father is a wretch who deserves to die. A worthless man.
Daughter: (Bows her head, tears falling)
Wife: (Almost crying) Don't cry. What good does crying do now! It only hurts your heart.
Kim: (Sighs deeply) A wretch who deserves to die. Abandoning his faithful first wife (조강지처) to live with a schoolgirl mistress, not even looking after his own child! That man will face divine punishment now.
Daughter: (Unable to bear it, covers her face with her skirt hem and goes inside)
Sŏnggŭn: (Calls for his mother and follows her inside)
Wife: (Crying) Don't be too harsh. Isn't he your son-in-law's child, still a child?
Kim: What 'child'! Even my own children born from my flesh are gone once they leave the nest, let alone that man who has now even transferred the family register (민적)!
Wife: That happened because you stamped the seal in the first place, didn't it?
Kim: Don't talk such frustrating nonsense! That man was acting like a madman, insisting on a divorce and shaming me like that, what was I supposed to do?
Wife: Be that as it may, perhaps when he gets a bit older, there will be a time when he repents. It's not like he bears a grudge against his wife and child; he's just infatuated with another woman for the time being.
Sangwŏn: (Enters through the wicker gate carrying a small sack with a few *toe* of rice in one hand and a few dried croakers tied with straw rope (색기) in the other)
Wife: Welcome back. You must be hungry.
Sangwŏn: (Putting down what he carried beside the mat) You should have the children do things like this. (Pause) I managed to get some rice, tell them to cook it quickly.
Wife: Alright. (Gets up, takes the sack and croakers, and goes inside.)
Kim: (Unhappily) Where did the rice come from?
Sangwŏn: I went to the south village and spoke to old man Pak Chang'ŭi (朴掌儀), and he gave me one *wŏn*...
Kim: (Sighs. Brief silence) Is there any news about when Inwŏn might be coming?
Sangwŏn: I stopped by the town just now to check, and one of his friends (등무애) came, saying he should arrive either today or tomorrow. <76>
Kim: What is that boy doing in Seoul (서울)?
Sangwŏn: I don't know either, but it seems he's managing to survive without starving, so don't worry too much.
Kim: (Sighs) Still, he's the last one I'm counting on. (Pause) It seems all hope is lost now. (Sighs)
Sangwŏn: (Sits silently for a moment, then goes inside, takes off his jacket and hat, comes out again, and exits through the wicker gate)
Wife: (Comes out from inside and resumes cutting barley ears)
Kim: (Watching Sangwŏn's departing figure, Aside [傍白]) Your situation is pitiful too. Selling off all the good fields and paddies worth thousands of *sŏk*, squandering it all wastefully (허평대평), and now you're a man past 40, heading into your 50s.
Wife: That's also fate's doing (팔짜 소간); blaming now won't help.
Kim: (Annoyed) What do you mean, fate (팔짜)! (Pause) They might suffer for the sin of selling off what their parents passed down, but what sin did these two old folks commit! Did I ever kill a person in my life? Did I ever say harsh words (모진 소리) or do cruel things to others! And yet, in my final years (말래), I'm clutching a starving stomach, about to die of hunger. When I think about it, it's infuriating and sad.
Wife: (Sighs)
Kim: If only I could just die quickly, I wouldn't have to see this miserable state, but this stubborn life just won't end. (Sighs)
Wife: It's all because we committed many sins in our past lives, and we're atoning for them (죄다짐).
Chongshik: (Enters the wicker gate and calls out) Sangwŏn.
Kim: (Without looking back) Sangwŏn isn't here.
Chongshik: (Enters)
Wife: Welcome. I wondered who it was.
Chongshik: How have you been? (Bows slightly, touching the back of his head with his hand.)
Kim: (Looks at him with aged eyes) Who is it?
Chongshik: It's me, sir. Chongshik.
Kim: (Coldly) Ah, Chongshik... Welcome—have you been well?
Chongshik: Yes... How have you been getting along lately?
Kim: What's the use of talking...
Wife: Is your business going well?
Chongshik: Not at all. I'm at my wit's end too. I've lent out a lot of money, but I can't collect it. (Sits on a corner of the mat)
Kim: So, is your respected father well?
Chongshik: Yes, just the usual...
Wife: I heard you gained a daughter-in-law?
Chongshik: Yes, I managed to get the only child I have married.
Kim: You have something to show for your life. (Pause) As you know, I have several children, but they've all ended up in this state, making their old parents suffer like this in their final years...
Chongshik: (Sits silently for a moment) Where did Sangwŏn go, far away?
Wife: He was just here, maybe he went to the village...
Kim: Why, has something else happened?
Chongshik: Yes, well, it's not something else, but as you probably already know, it's about your family's ancestral mountain (先山). (Looks uncomfortable)
Kim: Ah, you mean the one Sangwŏn pawned (잽혓다는) to you?
Chongshik: Yes, but the deadline (긔한) has passed several times, Sangwŏn keeps avoiding meeting me, and even when I send letters asking him to at least pay the interest, he ignores them...
Kim: (Sighs) Is that so?
Chongshik: So, although it's something I really shouldn't do, I'm also suffering considerable losses because of this, so I inevitably have to proceed with the formalities...
Kim: Formalities? You mean sell it? (Becomes somewhat displeased)
Chongshik: Yes.
Kim: (Startled) What? Sell my ancestral mountain? Look, everyone else might not know, but even though my family became destitute and pawned it to you, <77> should words like 'selling it' really come from your mouth?
Chongshik: But what am I supposed to do?
Kim: Ugh, you ungrateful wretch!
Chongshik: (Angrily, stiffening) What are you saying?
Kim: What do I mean? (Stops working and sits facing Chongshik) Look here, you rascal! When you were rolling around wrapped in your father's coat tails (두루맥이), wasn't it I who took you in and raised you because you were a friend's son? Didn't you grow up (잔뼈가 굵엇지) in my house? And didn't you build up your current prosperity (성세) with the capital I provided? Such a person, even if you can't look after me now that I'm on the verge of death, you'd sell my ancestral mountain! Is that human decency!
Chongshik: What, if I grew up in your house, did I just play and eat for free? I also did back-breaking work (뼈앞은 일) beyond my station for you. I shed many tears while chewing on cold rice lumps (식은 밥뎅). If you provided the capital, did I just waste it? What are you talking about?
Kim: (Trembling with anger) Ugh, you wretch. You wretch!
Chongshik: Why are you calling me 'wretch'!
Kim: (Tries to lunge at him)
Wife: (Grabs and restrains him) Husband, husband, calm down. (Cries) Husband, calm down. Didn't you know human hearts are like this? (Looking at Chongshik) Go now, go. The world is heartless, but I've never seen someone like you.
Chongshik: I'm going. I'll go even if you don't tell me to...
Kim: (Struggling while being held) You wretch, you wretch!
Sŏnggŭn: (Runs out, grabs Kim Sŏndal, and cries.)
Daughter: (Cannot come out, stands beside the screen wall and cries.)
Kim: You wretch, just wait until our Inwŏn comes down from Seoul, then we'll see, we'll see, you wretch!
Chongshik: (About to leave, looks back) Does Inwŏn have some special talent (산빼는 재조)? Not a chance.
Kim: You wretch, just wait and see, wait and see!
Chongshik: I'm not scared.
Kim: Just try selling my ancestral mountain! I'll set your house on fire! (Grinds his teeth)
Chongshik: (Turns and leaves) Do as you please, Hmph.
Inwŏn: (Wearing work clothes, holding biscuits in his hand, enters through the wicker gate and stops, startled)
Chongshik: (Glares at him and leaves)
Wife: (Sees him first, lets go of Kim Sŏndal, runs to Inwŏn, hugs his neck, and cries) Inwŏn-ah, Inwŏn-ah.
Kim: Inwŏn-ah, you're here? Look, look at that wretch. That wretch says he's going to sell our ancestral mountain. That wretch who grew up eating my food...
Inwŏn: Why is that person selling our ancestral mountain?
Kim: He pawned it. Pawned it. Pawned it, and the deadline passed...
Wife: Don't even talk about it. That person owes us his gratitude, he became successful because of us, but the abusive remarks (폭백) he hurled at your father just now! Being poor is sad enough, but now even someone who ate our food looks down on us (업수역이니) like this, truly, it's too sad to live. Inwŏn-ah. (Cries harder)
Inwŏn: Don't cry. Don't cry. Scum like him, how long can his arrogance (눈물을 흘리며) last? That kind of world won't last long now. Don't cry.
Kim: Inwŏn-ah, Inwŏn-ah. You must recover the ancestral mountain. The ancestral mountain. The ancestral mountain where your ancestors' bones (선형백골) are buried. And where will these old parents, who might die today or tomorrow, be buried! Inwŏn-ah, I was counting only on you.
Inwŏn: (Hangs his head, unable to speak)
Wife: (Lets go of him) Let's go. Go sit up there on the porch.
Sangwŏn: (Enters hurriedly through the wicker gate) What happened? When did Inwŏn arrive? <79>
(Seeing his attire, shows a look of disappointment [락망])
Inwŏn: I just got here.
Sangwŏn: Then why was there such a loud commotion?
Wife: Chongshik came saying he would sell our ancestral mountain, and when your father rebuked (나물햇더니) him a little, he spewed unbearable abusive remarks and left. That's why your father is so angry right now.
Sangwŏn: That bastard deserves to be killed! (Looking at Inwŏn) And you just let that wretch go without breaking his leg bones!
Inwŏn: Brother, don't say such things. What's so satisfying about beating up one scoundrel moneylender (월수쟁이 돈노이(貸金業)) like that?
Sangwŏn: Even so, you just let him go?
Wife: He ran off when this boy arrived.
Inwŏn: No. It's not whether he ran away or not. Rather, you shouldn't have pawned it in the first place, or if you pawned it and couldn't recover it, then that's that. The whole world is like this now; grabbing hold of one scoundrel like that, arguing, and hitting him—that's truly like drawing a sword to catch a mosquito.
Kim: (Collapses onto the mat) It's all ruined. All ruined. I was still counting on you! Now everything is ruined. (Curtain) <79>

**Summary**

This play excerpt, "The Fading Shadow," depicts the tragic decline of Kim Sŏndal, a formerly wealthy landowner, now living in poverty in rural 1931 Korea. The prologue contrasts his past affluence during a feast with his present destitution. The main act shows the family struggling with hunger, the shame of poverty (inability to pay grandson's tuition), and internal conflicts stemming from past mistakes (Sangwŏn pawning ancestral land, another son abandoning the family). The climax arrives when Chongshik, a man Kim Sŏndal once helped, now a prosperous moneylender, comes to announce the seizure of the family's ancestral burial mountain due to unpaid debts. The return of the youngest son, Inwŏn, initially brings hope, but his pragmatic, almost resigned attitude towards the situation ("The whole world is like this now") crushes his father's final hope, highlighting generational differences and deep despair.

From a historical perspective, the play vividly portrays the harsh realities of rural Korean life under Japanese colonial rule: widespread poverty, indebtedness to predatory moneylenders, the breakdown of traditional social structures and obligations (like Chongshik's ingratitude), and the loss of land, particularly ancestral land which held deep cultural significance. Inwŏn's weary resignation and comment about "this kind of world" might reflect a sense of hopelessness or perhaps a nascent, fatalistic understanding of broader socio-economic forces crushing individuals, making it a valuable snapshot of social conditions and mentalities during that period.
