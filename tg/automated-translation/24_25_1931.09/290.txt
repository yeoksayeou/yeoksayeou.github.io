잡지명	동광 제25호
발행일	1931년 09월 04일
기사제목	獨逸留學日記 (Diary of Studying Abroad in Germany)
필자	獨逸에서 都宥浩
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

獨逸留學日記
						From Germany, To Yu-ho

In early September, I had the opportunity to visit the village of Wolfenhausen (볼펜하우젠), where the sister of Mrs. Lang from next door had married into a family. Wolfenhausen is a small farming village nestled in the famous Taunus (타우누쓰) forest. The Baumann (빠우만) family, into which Mrs. Lang's sister had married, ran a small inn (客主집). When I arrived at the Oberbrechen (오버뿌레허) station, Mr. Rudi (루듸), the younger brother of Mrs. Lang's sister's husband, was there to meet me. On the bus ride that day, I saw for the first time the image of a shepherd tending his flock, something I had only seen in pictures. The Taunus forest under the cloudy sky looked most picturesque. After arriving at the inn that day, I went for a walk in the woods with Rudi. During our walk, our conversation, common among old bachelors, started with talk of pretty young women and eventually turned to hunting.

Along the way, we met a gentleman from this village who runs a small leather factory and hunts for leisure (消日 사냥꾼). The hunter's eyes were exceptionally sharp; even in the dim evening light, he pointed to the opposite mountainside and showed us the shapes of animals moving about. I think it was probably the next day. Rudi came into my room and began to vent his frustrations about the current political situation in Germany. Rudi is a National Socialist (國粹社會主義者). Raising his voice, he passionately argued for the necessity of a 'dictator' (듹타토) government. He repeatedly complained that the salaries of government officials were too high. His main grievance was that these bureaucratic salaries were excessive compared to the income of farmers and laborers. He attributed this, moreover, to the fault of the current republican government.

The Baumann household currently has three sons. The eldest son died in the last Great War,<49> the next son moved out to start his own family, and another living son is also elsewhere, leaving only Rudi and his older brother, Mr. Wilhelm (윌헴), who is Mrs. Lang's sister's husband, at home. There is also an adopted son in this house, whose name is also Wilhelm (윌헴). The family calls the biological son Willi (윌리). The adopted son Wilhelm's father apparently died in the last war. Soon after, his mother also passed away, leaving four or five children (兒孩) at home. Consequently, several families in the village took in the children as adopted sons and daughters (양자녀). Also living in this house is Mr. Seppel (젭펠) from Bavaria (빠이언), who is staying here for room and board after becoming unemployed. They all drive wagons to the fields or the mountains. Only the youngest son, Rudi, despite being 30, still retains a habit of dependence (엉석하는 버릇) and remains stuck by the kitchen hearth (정주간). The elderly Mrs. Baumann sits by the oven (오븐) knitting socks (보선) or peeling potatoes, while the younger Mrs. Baumann, along with the maid, milks the cows, feeds the pigs, makes butter (뻐터), and handles other food preparations. Because Rudi is plump, his brother, sister-in-law, and mother (자당되시는 분) often tease him by calling him 'fatty' (뚱뚱보). Mr. Willi apparently went to war.

He was shot in one leg.
After returning, he remained until the armistice was signed. Fatty Rudi, after wearing a military uniform for only two or three days, reportedly returned to his hometown (鄕里) crowned with the laurel wreath of a veteran dispatched to the front. One day, I went along with Mr. Willi and Seppel in the wagon to haul timber. Willi began recounting his experiences from the front. Among them were stories of roasting potatoes in a French (佛蘭西) farmhouse, crawling through dense forests like this one, and bullets whistling (씨르릉하고) over his head. Once, as he was passing by, he saw numerous French soldiers; some were sitting, some lying down, some leaning against telephone poles (電柱). They had all been exposed to poison gas (독까쓰) because they lacked protective gear (방비구). On another occasion, he went three weeks without a chance to send a letter home, and everyone at home assumed he was dead. One day, the elderly Mrs. Baumann called me into her room and showed me a photograph of her eldest son in his military uniform. Then she let out a long sigh. She said she still often sighs, thinking of her deceased son. During the war, 15 or 16 Frenchmen were apparently held captive here in Wolfenhausen. They were all French residents in Germany at the outbreak of the war who were arrested as prisoners and put to work here. Two or three of them stayed at the Baumann house. The person who supervised their labor (사역) was reportedly Captain Petsch (펫츠), an old sea captain who still lives in the village. This old Mr. Petsch once

served for a long time on the East Asian sea routes.
He came here to his wife's hometown (本鄕). If you go to his house, you'll see a photograph of a sailing ship he once sailed on hanging in front of his desk. He said he had passed the Korean (朝鮮) coast several times and had seen many Koreans in Vladivostok (海參威). He has many photographs of the houses, customs, and other aspects of the Koreans in Vladivostok. When asked, he said they were all from over 30 years ago. He was the first person I met in this village who uses standard language (표준어). Old Mr. Petsch mentioned that he still has trouble understanding the local dialect (사투리). What kind of country is Germany anyway? The dialects (方語) are astonishingly numerous. Apparently, during the feudal era, one couldn't even travel freely from one village to the next. Thus, the difference between the speech of two villages barely five *ri* (里) apart seems to be as great as the difference between Hamgyŏng Province dialect and Chŏlla Province dialect. German children receive eight years of compulsory education. Only standard language is used in schools. However, as soon as they return home, they switch back to dialect. So, what can be said about their dialect after graduation? Moreover, even when those who have remained within a single village speak standard language, their accent (악센트) invariably remains the dialect accent.

Having played the role of a refined gentleman (큰 생원님<50>) living in the city for a while, since coming to this village, I've had to get used to the smell of cow dung and horse manure. This house, being an inn, has a room on the second floor resembling a large hall. This room has a stage at one end, and facing the stage, there's even a gallery (깰레리) like in a theater. On the stage, there's a canvas (캔버쓰) backdrop for plays, which they said was used last Christmas. When Christmas comes, they apparently hold dances here too. On the door (또아) entering this room, there's a police notice (告示) prohibiting entry to boys and girls under 17. To get to the toilet (변소), I have to pass through the main living room (안방) and enter a small room (間) at the corner of the stage. Inside the toilet, I can hear the chirping of chicks and the grunting of pigs from below. This toilet is specifically for guests, but it can be somewhat awkward for first-time visitors. Moving abruptly from toilet talk to food talk might seem out of order, but at mealtimes, I go down to the kitchen (정주) with the family. What I eat most here is freshly made, golden-yellow butter (뻐터). And next is pork blood sausage (도야지 순대), which is rather hard to come by in Frankfurt (佛郞府). Originally, Frankfurt is a sausage-producing region (産地), and

Frankfurt sausage (佛郞府 순대) is famous,
but for gentlemen who frequent restaurants, opportunities to freely slice it thick like this and eat it spread on bread (麵麭) are rare. Usually, you get just one thin slice of sausage served with potato salad (감자 쌀낫). And here too, like others, I eat five meals a day. It might sound like I'm complaining about being full, but five meals a day give me constant indigestion (목트림이 거치러워서), so I inevitably skip one. Thus, I've been granted an exemption from the snack between breakfast and lunch. In this village, like others, the church bell rings at noon (午正), and several times a day, a police officer reports the news, standing in the street, ringing a bell, and shouting something like a radish vendor. Also here, once a week on Saturday evening, they clean the streets, tidy up the cowsheds (오양깐) and outhouses (뒤깐), and pick off the clumps of dung stuck to the horses' hindquarters. Meanwhile, fruit cake pans constantly go in and out of the baker's (떡장수 집) doorway. It's customary to eat fruit cake on Sundays, but since ordinary homes don't have baking ovens (가마), it's common to make the batter and take it to the baker's to be baked. They say that after working all week, on Sunday, they first fortify themselves with this fruit cake and coffee (카피), and then spend the rest of the day relaxing and having fun. Young ladies and gentlemen, in particular, make it their business to go dancing or stroll in the woods. Sometimes, acquaintances who looked like beaten owls during the week appear on Sunday dressed more splendidly than a county official (郡主事어른) from over the pass, and womenfolk (안악네) who looked like red bean porridge vendors (팟죽장수) sitting by the big ferry landing just yesterday are suddenly transformed into Seoul brides (서울 색시) visiting the main house.

That was the first Sunday I experienced after arriving in this village. That afternoon, I followed the others to a dance hall on Langhecke (랑헥케) street. As luck would have it, there were two dance halls with bands (악대) there today, and people from neighboring villages had gathered in droves. I joined the others at the first place, smoking cigarettes and drinking alcohol. Everyone else fortified themselves with beer (맥주), but having had a bad experience twice before mixing beer and apples (林檎), I stuck to wine (포도주). Wine was 40 Pfennig (페늭, about 20 *chŏn* (전)) per glass, while

beer was only 20 Pfennig per glass.
Thus, I, the wine drinker, was naturally elevated to the status of a new nobleman (새 양반) seated in a place of honor (堂上). When the band struck up a tune (뚱따라 뚱따라), pairs of young men and women would embrace and whirl around and around. Meanwhile, I sat there awkwardly watching the dancing (춤을 게게 흘리고 앉아서). With my right arm, set in plaster (석고), slung in a strap, trying to hold a wine glass with my left hand made my rear end wiggle (꽁무니가 때때로 옴씰거려서) uncomfortably. We went back and forth between the two dance halls several times. Sometimes, while others danced, a group of gentlemen (생원님네) would stand by the bar (술통가), pouring beer into large mugs (대병) or boot-shaped glasses (장화 모냥으로 생긴 잔), raising toasts (건배), and singing songs. During one such moment, our group from Wolfenhausen village gathered together to raise a toast.<51> A young lady from the neighboring village (건딘골 색시) perched on Seppel's knee. Seppel, full of excitement, sang a song. I gradually began to feel tipsy (정신이 떨떨해 왓습니다). Following the others' lead, I sang a simple song (單목 노래) I had learned since coming here.

The song wasn't anything special; in our Korea, it would be similar to "Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo" (아리랑 아리랑 아라리요) or "There are many stars in that sky, and much gossip in my life with my in-laws" (저 하날에는 별도 많고 이내 싀집살이엔 말도 많다). "Ich hab dich tanzen gesehen und deine Schönheit mich toll gemacht." (이히 합디히탄첸 게젠 운드 따이네 쇤하잇미히 톨게마흐트 - I saw you dancing and your beauty drove me crazy.)

Another thing I found interesting (자미잇게) since coming to this village was their proficiency in singing (歌詠). Every Saturday night, the village people gather in the Baumanns' recreation room (遊戱室 - the large room mentioned earlier) for singing practice (歌詠연습). The attendees are all men, and a teacher always comes for these sessions. He usually stays overnight, and their singing continues until late at night (정밤). After the singing ends, beer glasses are brought out. Their singing voices sometimes resonate most solemnly. I heard that these singing groups (歌詠團) exist in every village, and competitions (경쟁회) are held several times a year. Wolfenhausen is among the frequent winners, and the trophies (전리품) won in competitions are proudly displayed (트근이 놓여 잇습니다). The songs they sing sometimes ring out powerfully, sometimes sound incredibly poignant (자릿자릿하게), and sometimes make my eavesdropping heart feel utterly lonely. The following Saturday, the singing practice didn't finish, so they resumed it on Sunday morning. I went to listen again then. Sitting in the gallery (갤레리 - attic), I endlessly watched their facial expressions (얼골 씰우는 법) and how they moved their mouths (입 놀리는 법).

The teacher occasionally bangs the table (테이불)
and corrects the parts that aren't right (안된 대). Their voices start from the highest point, gradually descend, and finally enter the most profound depths. Old Mr. Baumann, mouth agape (하 벌리고), looks up at the ceiling, his expression filled with sorrow (애조). Just when I thought the sound had ended, it subtly begins again, gradually rising higher. Finally, the most resonant sound emerges. Mouth closed, I silently followed them, singing the song without sound. That was this very day.

This day is September 14th, the election day of 1930. A few days before this day, right in this large room, there was a small election speech rally for the German National People's Party (독일국민당). The audience listening to the speech numbered only a few dozen. Among them were some who occasionally attempted to challenge the speaker. The speaker denounced all other political parties. The challenger would occasionally tap the table with his beer glass and scoff. A few days before coming here, I had attended a sound film (발성사진, 'talkie') election speech by the SPD (사회민주당) at a cinema (활동사진관) in Frankfurt. Using 'talkies' (토키) for election speeches was truly a 1930s phenomenon. At that time, with my limited German, especially hearing it from a 'talkie', I found it difficult to understand well. However, the words that came at the end were, as usual, attacks on other parties. The speaker in the 'talkie' then finally raised his voice dramatically, saying, "Don't fall for the words of the Communists (공산주의자)! Don't be deceived by the nonsense of the National Socialists (國粹社會主義者)! Socialism! Socialism! What we advocate is the true path!" Election speeches were incessant everywhere in the lead-up to this election day. And starting from September, the police had also been strictly controlling weapons (흉기). Amidst all this, election day arrived. In Wolfenhausen village,

the voting took place in the elementary school auditorium.
Men and women aged 20 and over went there. As you readers well know, German elections are not cast for individual names but for party names (黨名). The party names are then represented by numbers. Voting ended by 5 p.m. (?). That evening, I went into the shop (海店) across the street and had a drink with Seppel and several other acquaintances gathered around. At that moment, someone sitting next to me pointed to an acquaintance who had quietly entered and sat down in a corner, telling me, "That person is a Communist." Later, I learned the results of today's election from that acquaintance.<52> It's hard to recall exactly, but I believe the results were Social Democratic Party (SPD) 147 seats, National Socialist Party (國粹社會黨) 37, and Communist Party (공산당) 36. The order of party scores in the 1930 German election was precisely SPD, National Socialist, Communist. In Germany, the so-called radical parties (과격정당) are the National Socialist Party and the Communist Party. In defeated Germany, there are very many young people worried about the decline of the nation's status (國體의 零落). They look back fondly on the nation's prosperity during the Imperial era (帝政). They believe the current national crisis must first be overcome through national strength (國强). For them, National Socialism holds the strongest appeal (압필 - appeal). The National Socialist Party (國粹黨) gained the affection of many sentimentalists (感情家). On the other hand, Germany's industrial bourgeoisie (『뿌르조아지』) puts great effort into utilizing this. They faithfully provide their weapon, money, to this National Socialist Party. And the greatest enemy of this National Socialist Party is the Communist Party. They frequently clash with each other.

During my stay in Wolfenhausen, my most persistent pleasure was strolling through the endless rows of fir trees (전나무). At those times, I always felt drawn by some charm. The charm of nature is truly great. All the beautiful thoughts hidden in the corners of the human heart receive the sweetest breath when captivated by nature's charm. When my gaze bends towards a cluster of nameless flower blossoms bowing their heads amidst the dimly shaded trees, when profound silence occasionally whispers laughter into my ears, an unknown sprout of life pushes up verdantly (폴음폴음히) in my heart.

On the other hand, nature also whispers the most affectionate solitude (孤寂) to me. Solitude sometimes makes me feel a gentle sorrow (비애). Sorrow most carefully nurtures the sprout of life pushing up in my heart. The poet Wordsworth (워즈워드) once recited (을퍼) a song:

Thoughts of great joy have brought
Sad thoughts to the mind.
To her fair works did Nature link
The human soul that through me ran;
And much it grieved my heart to think
What man has made of man.
(Note: This seems a misremembered/paraphrased mix, closer in theme to lines from "Lines Written in Early Spring" than a direct quote)

Wandering alone here and there in the dense Taunus forest, I was intoxicated many times (몃 번이나) by the intertwined fantastic music of joy and sorrow. There, I also contemplated the eternity of the universe. I felt the mystery of life (生). Sometimes my mind soared above worldly thoughts. Then my mind settled back down to the lowest point. In the Taunus forest, I thought of them.

When I walked in this forest, I sometimes went strawberry hunting (딸기 사냥). One day, too, I spent the entire day hunting strawberries. During that time, I occasionally sat down to rest and sometimes wandered endlessly. Sometimes I softly recited songs. It was when I was hiding in the Diamond Mountains (금강산) due to illness (身病). One day, wandering through fir trees lined up just like here, I saw a single red rose standing alone by the roadside. I recited the inadequate poem I wrote afterwards many times in this Taunus forest. And, indulging myself, I always declared myself a most plausible poet. People say everyone lives according to their own fancy, but at least I live according to mine. However, it's unfortunate that others don't recognize it, but whether they do or not, I still live my own way. I recited:

In the deep valley below Chungsang Castle (衆香城)
Amidst the flow of evening bells,
Leisurely I come there.
There, beneath the lined fir trees,
A single rose, standing silent!

There, where gentle dew falls at night,
In that deep place where bells toll,
My heart calls softly,
But bowing without answer,
A single rose beneath the shade!
(A single rose beneath the shade)

There, in the deep Taunus forest, I softly called out to a nameless flower blossom. While I was occupied with such idle thoughts, sometimes deer (노루) or herds of roe deer (사시미떼) would do mischievous things and run off. That day too, I still carried my strawberry basket (딸기통) and entered the forest. Without even preparing to pick strawberries, my mind was still captivated by idle thoughts. Wandering aimlessly, I again thought of eternity, thought of impermanence (無常), thought of the past, and thought of the future. There, I thought of life's departures and arrivals (離沓) and thought of rest (안식). The Poet Sage Goethe (詩聖 꾀테) on the Kickelhahn (끽켈한<53>) mountain, reportedly felt deeply moved upon re-reading a verse he had written on the wall by hand when he was still young.

Over all the hilltops
Is quiet now,
In all the treetops
You perceive
Hardly a breath;
The little birds are silent in the woods.
Just wait, soon
You too shall rest.
(Über allen Gipfeln ist Ruh...)

He reportedly recited the last line, "Ja, warte nur, balde ruhest du auch" (야 와르테누아 빨데루에스트뚜아우흐), one more time before descending the mountain. The rest he yearned for in his youth, he yearned for again most dearly now, with white hair frosted upon him. But isn't rest ultimately to be found only in death (죽엄)? When all things enter the land of sleep quietly after a day's painful struggle, the Poet Sage Goethe also sang of his heart finding refuge. But the ultimate refuge lies only in death. Then, was Goethe singing of death at that time? No matter how much I ask here, I certainly have no way of knowing Goethe's feelings. However, if Goethe was human, at least this much must have been certain: he felt weary from life's struggle (生의 고투). He longed for rest. But on the other hand, he held an attachment (애착심) to this struggle. Troublesome and endlessly bothersome, yet too precious to discard—that is precisely life (生이외다).

At least
for Goethe, that was life (生이
엇습니다). Yes, indeed. We seek rest. But it feels a bit wasteful to discard this struggle entirely. However, as long as we do not discard this struggle, we must not simply muddle through (어펑펑히). Since we have already grasped it, let us fight with all our might in this battle. Thinking this, I followed Goethe and recited that last line once. "Warte nur, balde ruhest du auch." As I shouted this, suddenly two deer leaped out from this side and ran past me. I was wishing for rest (안식을 追願), but these deer, oblivious, ran off as if startled out of their wits. Others often say life is 'irony' (아이로니); are these deer too experiencing life's 'irony' now? ...Looking at the fleeing deer, I thought once more. Goethe's song from over a hundred years ago, at this moment, in a corner of the Taunus, caused me to startle these deer. It made them taste life's 'irony'. If everything in the universe is governed by causality (因果律), then today's cause and effect (因果) are indeed strangely intertwined. Thinking quietly, this net of causality is most intricately tangled. The force of this causality constantly flows here and there. But when these forces collide, change (變) occurs. Tragedy, whether in the natural world or the human world, arises when the flow of this causality is not smooth.

That day too, my strawberry hunt was somewhat unsuccessful. As time passed, the day grew dark, and I felt a bit hungry, so I headed back home aimlessly. As I entered the door (또아) and was about to head towards the kitchen, the elderly Mr. and Mrs. Baumann called me from the doorway of the bar (酒房). They said many young ladies (색시) had arrived, so I should come in. Since my strawberry basket was empty, I felt a bit embarrassed to go before these two for a moment. But then, at the word 'ladies', my heart felt somewhat lighter (후리후리해서), and I headed that way. There, several dozen young ladies sat around, among them

were reincarnations of Yang Guifei (양귀비의 후신) and daughters of Helen (헬덴의 따님),
and almost-but-not-quite Venuses (비너쓰). For some reason, I suddenly felt shy (수접은 생각) and couldn't properly lift my head. Mrs. Baumann introduced me to the group. "This gentleman is from Japan (일본)," she began. (Omission - original text) Soon, their gaze shifted from me back to the strawberry basket hanging from my left hand. When they said they couldn't see anything at the bottom no matter how hard they looked, it was attributed to the weak electric light. The group affirmed my explanation with laughter... Among these young ladies, men were sparsely seated, and among them, Seppel sat with a lady on each arm, beaming with delight (희색이 만만하야) and chattering away. At first, I thought these ladies were already close friends with Seppel.<54> But they said they had all met for the first time today. They had left Erbach (에어빠흐) today, hiked here and there (遠足), and were now on their way back to Erbach. Some of them held guitars (끼타), others had mandolins (맨돌린) placed before them. They were set to rest there until 8:30 p.m. During that time, I ate dinner and went up and down to my room aimlessly. Meanwhile, they sang songs and played their instruments. Soon, they said they were leaving. The men remaining there offered to escort them part of the way. At Rudi's suggestion, I joined them. (Of course, it wasn't that I didn't want to go.) A crescent moon gradually rose in the sky. The moonlight was very hazy (몽롱하엿습니다). The men following behind began to drop off one by one, two by two. Eventually, only Seppel, the Baumanns' adopted son Wilhelm, another acquaintance, and I remained. Finally, we had to part ways. It happened to be beside a main road, with the fir forest of the Taunus forming a fence on one side. Sitting in the grassy clearing there, we engaged in a final farewell gathering. One by one, people in the group sang songs, strummed the mandolin, and played the guitar. Occasionally, one of the young ladies (阿只氏) would stand up and dance. Soon, Seppel began to sing Bavarian folk songs. Seppel strummed the guitar this way and that and sang.

"My wife is pretty, but she's plump, three *cha* and three *ch'i* wide..." (우리집 마누라는 이쁘기는 하지마는 몸집이 뚱뚱해서 넓이가 석 자 세 치......) ...At this, the young ladies doubled over laughing, slapping the ground. Seppel continued, picking out another tune. Since the songs were folk songs,

there were many parts difficult to understand due to dialect (방언).
Moreover, Bavarian (빠이언, Bavaria) dialect is vastly different from standard German; the difference is said to be even greater than that between Dutch (和蘭語), which is also a Germanic language but now established as a completely independent national language, and standard German (표준어). So, after singing each part, Seppel would translate it into standard German (?). After the translation, laughter would erupt. Then the song would receive an 'encore' (앙코). Soon, the turn to sing passed to Wilhelm. Wilhelm knelt before the seated young ladies, plucked the guitar strings, and sang a most melancholy song. After the song ended, one young lady exclaimed in a most beautiful voice, "What song is that? It's so pitiful," offering words of admiration (감탄의 辭). Everyone agreed. While this was happening, the night grew deeper. The younger ladies urged departure (催促). The slightly older ladies countered with "Just a little longer" (조곰만 더). Among them, a short young lady sighed, "The moonlight is so hazy," expressing great reluctance to leave. Finally, we parted ways. The ladies all invited us, saying the Monday after next was their village's Catholic holiday, so we must come visit. One of them called out to Seppel, telling him to bring the Chinese guest (중국 손님) when he came. This time, I was playing the part of a Chinese person.

After parting with these ladies, we turned back towards Wolfenhausen village. Seppel, still dressed in Bavarian style—a jacket (저고리) with pointed ends, worn leather trousers (가죽바지), and long socks (長襪) covering from below the knee to the ankle—walked with great energy, his nail-studded boots (釘 박은 구두) thudding loudly on the ground. Sparks occasionally flew (벗적벗적 이러납니다) from his boot soles. Wilhelm, carrying an oddly shaped guitar slung in front of him, repeatedly sang, "That black-haired girl is quite pretty." We took a shortcut (질음길, 近途), passing through the woods, and came out onto the main road again. Seppel then proposed picking apples.
