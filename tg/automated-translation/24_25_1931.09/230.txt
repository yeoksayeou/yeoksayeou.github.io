Flare

I know this sort of thing must happen all the time, because I've experienced it twice now.

It was just a few days after I moved from Sup'yojŏng to the top of the mountain in Hongp'a-dong. I came home in the afternoon and was tidying up the still-unpacked household items when I heard a trembling voice from outside, "Is the master of the house in?" The owner of the voice was a man around thirty, wearing a white *turumagi* stained dark with grime and a crumpled cap. Autumn leaves had begun to fall, and his white cotton *turumagi* looked desolate. In his hand, he dangled a string of seaweed he must have gotten somewhere.

"It's nothing much..." He seemed to have a Ch'ungch'ŏng-do accent. "My wife gave birth yesterday, you see. I came out like this because we don't have any rice to boil... I received this string of seaweed from the house down below, but we need rice..."

I thought to myself, "Ah, this beggar is the type who won't leave with just a copper coin," and was reaching for my pocket when my wife's voice came from behind me.

"Oh, dear, does your wife give birth twice a month? You came at the beginning of the month saying she gave birth, and she gave birth again yesterday?" A cruel exposure.

"This is the first time I've come to this house."

Before his excuse was even fully out, she retorted,
"Ah, didn't you carry a string of seaweed when you came to Sup'yojŏng too? If you're going to deceive people, try changing your methods a bit."

Finally realizing it was hopeless, the fellow looked intently at my wife's face, and whether he remembered her or not, he gave up.
"What can a poor fellow do? I'll try somewhere else."
He tossed out the words as if vexed and shuffled away.

We showered his retreating back with cheerful (?) laughter. At that moment, his profession of earning a living with a string of seaweed and the tip of his tongue just seemed comical.

This incident reminded me of an old woman who, several years ago in P'yŏngyang, also shortly after we had moved, came begging with the exact same story and was disgraced. That time, the old woman's story was that her daughter-in-law had given birth and was lying down.

Looking at both cases, it seems that saying a new mother has nothing to eat, evoking sympathy for two lives, is more effective than simply saying someone is ill.

A young man claiming to be from an employment agency came looking to place a housemaid. I turned him away, saying we didn't need one. A few days later, he came back and asked me to find him a position somewhere. When I asked why, since he was at an employment agency, he said he didn't get a salary, only introduction fees, and couldn't make enough to eat.

After that, he visited frequently. One day, he came and pleaded that he hadn't eaten for two days, so I gave him fifty *chŏn*. He left beaming. My wife mocked my foolishness, but I felt the satisfaction of having done a good deed.

About a year later, he came again. When I asked where he had been, he said he had gone to the countryside to farm but came back because the work was too hard. Looking at his hands, they weren't the hands of someone who had worked. I began to harbor suspicions. Just then, a friend named H came to visit and, seeing the young man, asked,
"What are you up to these days?"
Seeing him hesitate, my suspicion grew. Mr. H seemed to know his face from his previous connection with the employment agency.

As the young man was leaving that day, he whispered in my ear,
"Please don't tell Mr. H that I borrowed money from you."
After that, he never came to our house again. He must have known, of course, that Mr. H would have revealed his whole story.

"My dear nephew, your esteemed father and I were bosom buddies."
An old man with greying whiskers came to see me.
"You know Mr. Pyŏn XX, the head of the XX Organization, right? The one who died in Andonghyŏn (安東縣) last year. His son is studying here at XX School, and now during the break, he wants to go to his uncle in Andonghyŏn, but he owes fifty *wŏn* for his boarding fees. We need to help him go."

Then, as now, I couldn't afford to donate fifty *wŏn*. Also, around that time, I had just finished a round of begging here and there for a friend who had been imprisoned, so I had nowhere else to turn for help.

The next day, the old man called again. Responding to my refusal, he pressed me for about thirty minutes, arguing about duty, propriety, and face, asking how I could refuse. It was the first time in my life I had spoken on the phone for so long. My hand was sweating. Later, I met my father and asked about the old man. He just shook his head vigorously.
"He's unbearable, unbearable. That old man lives exactly like that."

A fellow who claimed to have lived in Changjiakou (張家口), China, was arrested in Fengtian (봉천), served time in Sŏdaemun Prison, and got out. He said he needed travel expenses just to get to Kaesŏng to return to Changjiakou. It's been a year already, and he's still going around asking for travel money to Kaesŏng. There have often been occasions where people who gave him money for Kaesŏng gathered and roared with laughter.

"Hey, that guy is telling a complete lie. Don't give him even a penny!"
This is a comical scene that occurred in the teachers' room of a certain middle school.
"Sir, it's enough that you don't give him money. What's your motive for telling others not to?" an elderly teacher retorted.
"So, are you saying it's right to give money to a fellow who goes around telling such lies?"
The old teacher replied...
"Sir, how hungry must one be to resort to such shameless acts just to eat?"<39>

**Summary**

The author recounts several personal anecdotes illustrating various scams and deceptions prevalent at the time, where individuals use fabricated sob stories (childbirth without food, needing job placement, helping a fictional student, needing travel money after prison) to elicit money. The author initially feels sympathy but grows cynical through repeated experiences and revelations from others. The article concludes with a debate highlighting the moral ambiguity of giving aid to known liars, questioning whether desperation justifies deception. From a historical perspective, the article vividly portrays the economic hardship and social conditions in colonial Korea during the early 1930s. The desperation leading to these scams, the mention of specific locations like Sŏdaemun Prison, and the social dynamics reflected in the interactions (e.g., reliance on connections, the employment agency struggles) offer historians insights into the daily lives and survival strategies of ordinary people under colonial rule.

照明彈
이따위 일을 두 번 당해보앗으니까 아마 노상 잇는 일인 줄 안다.
수표정에 살다가 홍파동 산꼭댁이로 이사온지 메칠 안돼서의 일이다. 오후에 집에 돌아와서 아직 정돈안된 세간을 치우노라니까 밖에서
『주인나리 게시오니까』
하는 떨리는 목소리가 들린다. 목소리의 주인공은 때가 깜아케 묻은 힌 두루마기에 구겨진 캡을 쓴 삼십 근방의 사나이다. 가을잎이 떨기 시작햇는데 힌 광목 두루마기가 쓸쓸해 보엿다. 손에는 어디서 낫는지 미역을 한 줄거리 대룽대룽 들엇다.
『다름이 아니라와요...』 충청도 말씨같다. 『제 안해가 어저께 해산을 햇솨와온대요, 밥 끄릴 쌀이 없어 이러케 나섯솨와요. 저 아렛댁에서 미역을 한 줄거리 주서서 받아가지고 왓습니다마는 쌀이 잇어야지오...』
나는 속으로 『아하 이 거지는 예의 동전 한 푼으로는 가지 않을 친구로구나』하고 혼자 생각하면서 호주머니로 손을 가저가고 잇는데 내 등 뒤에서 내 안해 목소리가 난다.
『아, 여보, 당신 마누라는 한 달에 아이를 두 번씩 낳는단 말이오? 초승에도 와서 아이 낳노라드니 어적게 또 낳서요?』잔혹한 폭로다.
『이 댁에 처음이올시다.』
하는 변명이 채 떠러지기도 전에
『아, 당신이 수표정 왓을 때도 미역을 한 줄거리 들고 다니지 않엇소. 남을 소기랴거든 좀 수단을 갈아보아요.』
그제야 할 수 없는 것을 깨다른 이 친구는 내 안해 얼굴을 유심히 보드니 기억이 낫든지 아니 낫든지간에 단념하고
『그저 어려운 놈이 어쩔 수 잇습니까. 다른 데로 가 봅지오.』
민정대는 듯이 한 마디 던지고 어슬렁어슬렁 돌아갓다.
우리는 그 등 뒤에 유쾌한(?) 우슴을 퍼부엇다. 그 당장에는 미역 한 줄거리와 혀끝으로 벌어 먹고 사는 그의 직업이 그저 우섭기만 하엿다.
이 사건은 멧 해 전에 평양서 역시 이사간 지 멫 일 못되어 꼭 같은 소설을 가지고 구걸 왓다가 망신한 노파의 일을 생각나게 하엿다. 그 때 그 노파의 이아기는 메느리가 아이를 낳고 누엇다는 것이엇다.
두 사건 다 보드라도 단순히 누가 알른다는 것보다 산모가 먹을 것이 없다는 것이 두 생명에게 동정을 가게 하는 것이므로 효과가 특월한 모양이다.

직업 소개소에 잇노라는 청년이 어멈 하나 두라고 찾아 왓다. 소용이 없다고 돌려 보냇더니 멫일 뒤에 다시 와서는 자기를 어디 좀 넣어 달라고 한다. 직업 소객소에 잇다면서 웨 그러느냐 하니까 월급이 없고 소개료나 받는데 밥 먹을 것이 못된다고 한다.
그 뒤에 자주 단니드니 하로는 와서 밥을 이틀째 굶엇다고 애원을 하는 고로 오십전을 주엇드니 히색이 만면해서 갓다. 안해는 나의 어리석음을 비웃엇으나 나는 좋은 일한 만족을 가졋섯다.
한 일 년 지나서 또 찾아 왓다. 어디 잇엇느냐고 물으니까 시굴 가서 농사하다가 일이 고돼서 못하고 왓노라고 한다. 손을 보니까 일한 손이 아니다. 그래서 의심을 품기 시작햇는데 마침 H라는 친구가 놀러오다가 보고
『요새 멀 하오』
하고 뭇는 바람에 어물어물하는 것을 보고 더 의심이 낫다. H군은 이전에 직업 소개소에 봉직햇든 관게로 얼굴을 아는 모양이다.
그 날 돌갈 때에 귀에다 대고
『저 H선생님에게 제가 돈 얻어갓다고 말 말아주세요』
하고 간 뒤에 다시는 우리 집에 아니왓다. 물론 H선생이 자기의 밑둥을 다 이아기햇을 것을 아는 까닭이다.

『여보, 조카님, 내가 춘부장하고 막역지간이외다』
어떤 수염이 히뜩히뜩한 노인이 나를 찾아 왓다.
『저 XX단장 편XX씨 잇지 안쏘. 저 안동현서 거년에 죽은 사람 말이야. 그 아들이 여기 XX학교에서 공부를 하는대 지금 방학에 안동현 자기 삼춘한테로 가려하는데 밥값 밀린 것이 오십원이야. 좀 가도록 해 주어야겟구려.』
나는 그때나 지금이나 오십원씩 적선할 여유도 없엇고 또 마츰 그 적에 옥에 드러간 어떤 친구의 일로 여기저기 구걸을 한바탕 하고난 뒤라 다시 어디 말해볼 데도 없엇다.
이튼날 그 노인은 또 전화를 걸엇다. 그리고 내가 거절하는 말에 대하야 의리와 법과 낯을 보아서 그럴 수가 잇냐고 약 삼십 분이나 졸라댄다. 평생에 전화로 그러케 말 오래해 보기는 처음이다. 손에서 땀이 낫다. 그 뒤에 부친을 만나 그 노인의 일을 무러보니까 고개만 홰홰 내젓는다.
『질색이야, 질색. 꼭 그러케 살아가는 영감이야』

중국 장가구(張家口)에 살다가 봉천서 잡혀서 서대문 감옥서 징역하고 나왓다는 친구. 장가구로 돌아갈텐데 개성까지 갈 여비만 달라고 하는 지가 어언간 일년인데 아직도 개성여비를 얻으러 단닌다. 그 친구에게 개성 여비 준 사람이 한 자리에 모여서 하하 대소한 일도 가끔 잇다.
『여보, 그놈이 웬통 거짓말입디다. 아예 한 푼이라도 주지 맙시오』
이것은 어떤 중등학교 교원실에서 생긴 희화다.
『여보, 노형이나 아니 주엇으면 그만이지 남까지 말라고 하는 것은 무슨 심사요?』하고 어떤 노인 선생이 대들엇다.
『그럼 그런 거짓말하고 단니는 놈에게 돈을 주는게 옳단 말이요?』
노선생 대답...
