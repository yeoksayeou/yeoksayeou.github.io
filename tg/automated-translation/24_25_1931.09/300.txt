Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Novel)

Yi Saeng and Ch'oe Nang, A Strange Tale from Kŭmo Sinhwa
Translated by Han'gyŏl Saeng

Near Nagt'a Bridge (駱駝橋) in Songdo (松都) lived a man named Yi Saeng (李生). He always attended the National Academy (國學) and recited poetry on the streets. At the age of 18, he was known for his good looks and talent.

Also, in Sŏnjuk-ri (善竹里) at that time lived Ch'oe Nang (崔娘), born into a noble family (宮門 貴骨), approaching the age of sixteen (二八). Her appearance was also beautiful, and she was renowned for her skill in embroidery and her ability in poetry (詩賦). Thus, people said of them:

"The refined son of the Yi family (風流李氏子),
The graceful daughter of the Ch'oe family (窈窕崔家娘).
If talent and beauty could be eaten (才色若可餐),
They could cure a hungry stomach (可以療飢腸)."

Whenever Yi Saeng went to school carrying his books, he passed by the wall behind Ch'oe Nang's house. There stood several dozen weeping willows whose branches drooped so low they nearly touched the ground, and Yi Saeng usually rested beneath them. One day, Yi Saeng peeked over the wall and saw famous flowers in full bloom, with bees and birds singing among them. Beside them was a small pavilion (樓) hidden among the flower clusters. The jeweled curtain (珠簾) was half-raised, and the silk screen (羅幃) hung low. Inside, a beautiful woman was embroidering. She paused her needle, rested her chin on her hand, and recited a poem:

"Leaning alone by the silk window, embroidery slows (獨倚紗窓刺繡遲),
Amidst the hundred flowers, orioles chatter (百花叢裏囀黃鸝).
For no reason, resentment secretly ties to the east wind (無端暗結東風怨),
Wordless, stopping the needle, lost in thought (不語停針有所思).
Whose fair-faced young man is on the road (路上誰家白面朗)?
Blue collar, large belt, reflecting by the weeping willow (靑衿大帶映垂楊).
How can I become a swallow in the hall (何方可化堂中燕),
To snatch away the jeweled curtain and cross the wall askew (佤掠珠簾斜度墻)?"

Hearing this, Yi Saeng found it hard to suppress the tickling in his heart, but the main gate was high and imposing, and the inner gate was deep and layered, so he could do nothing but leave sadly. On his way back, Yi Saeng wrote three poems on a piece of white paper, tied a pebble to it, and threw it over the wall:

"Mount Wu's (巫山) twelve peaks shrouded in returning mist (巫山六六霧重回),
Half-revealed sharp peaks piled in purple and green (半露尖峯紫翠堆).
Troubling King Xiang's (襄王) lonely pillow dream (惱却襄王孤枕夢),
Willing to descend from Yang Terrace (陽臺) as clouds and rain (肯爲雲雨下陽臺)?
Sima Xiangru (相如) wished to woo Zhuo Wenjun (卓文君) (相如欲挑卓文君),
How much emotion was already overflowing (多少情懷已十分)!
Peach and plum blossoms艳丽on the red-powdered wall (紅粉墻頭桃李艶),
Where does the scattered pollen fall with the wind (隨風何處落繽粉)?
Is it a good fate or a bad fate (好因緣邪惡因緣)?
Vainly holding a sorrowful gut, day feels like a year (空把愁膓日抵年).
The twenty-eight-character matchmaker is already made (二十八字媒已就),
On what day will I meet the immortal at Blue Bridge (藍橋何日遇神仙)?"

Ch'oe Nang had her maidservant Hyang'a (香兒) retrieve it and saw it was Yi Saeng's poetry. Reading it over two or three times, she couldn't contain her joy and immediately wrote eight characters on a small piece of paper and threw it back. The words were:

"Have no doubts about me, let dusk be the appointed time (將子無疑, 昏以爲期)."

As instructed, Yi Saeng went under the cover of darkness and saw a branch of peach blossom resting on the wall, its shadow swaying visibly. Approaching closer, he saw it was a swing rope with a bamboo seat (竹兜) attached and lowered. Yi Saeng immediately used it to climb over the wall. Just then, the moon rose over the eastern hills, flower shadows embroidered the ground, and a clear fragrance tickled his nose. Yi Saeng thought, "Have I entered an immortal realm?" His heart overflowed with secret joy, yet the clandestine nature of the affair made his hair stand on end. Looking around, he saw the woman already in the flower bushes with Hyang'a, picking flowers to put in her hair, and spreading a felt mat (氈褥) in a secluded spot. Seeing Yi Saeng enter, she smiled brightly and recited first:

"Amidst peach and plum branches, richness and nobility (桃李枝間富貴)
On the mandarin duck pillow, the moon is beautiful (鴛鴦枕上月嬋娟)."

Yi Saeng responded in kind:

"If this spring news leaks out later (他時漏洩春消息),<56>
The merciless wind and rain would also be pitiful (風雨無情亦可憐)."

Ch'oe Nang's expression changed as she said:
"I originally intended to serve you with dustpan and broom (箕箒) for life and share joy together, but why do you speak such words now? Though I am a woman, my heart is steadfast. As a man of spirit (丈夫之意氣), how can you say such things? If, in the future, the affairs of the inner chamber are revealed to my family, place the blame on me. I will bear it."
She then sent Hyang'a to the room to bring wine and fruit. Hyang'a went as commanded. All around was quiet, with no sound of people. Yi Saeng asked:
"What place is this exactly?"
Ch'oe Nang replied:
"This is beneath the small pavilion (小樓) in our rear garden (後園). My parents, loving me dearly as their only daughter, built this pavilion separately by the Lotus Pond (芙蓉池) so that when the myriad flowers bloom beautifully in spring, I could enjoy myself here with my maids. My parents' quarters (閨閣) are far and deep, so even if we talk and laugh loudly here, they won't hear."
She poured a full cup of green ant wine (綠蟻酒) and offered it to Yi Saeng, reciting:

"Curved railing presses down on the Lotus Pond (曲闌下壓芙蓉池),
On the pond, amid flower clusters, people converse (池上花叢人共語).
Fragrant mist drifts, spring is warm (香霧霏霏春融融),
Composing new verses, singing the 'White Ramie' song (製出新詞歌白紵).
Moon moves past flower shadows onto the felt mat (月轉花陰入氍毹),
Together pulling long branches, red rain falls (共挽長條落紅雨).
Wind stirs the clear fragrance, scent pervades clothes (風攪淸香香襲衣),
Jia's daughter first steps into the spring sun dance (賈女初踏春陽舞).
Gauze robe lightly brushes crabapple branches (羅衫輕拂海棠枝),
Startling the sleeping parrot among the flowers (驚起花間宿鸚鵡)."

Receiving the cup, Yi Saeng responded:

"Mistakenly entered Peach Blossom Spring, flowers splendid (誤入桃源花爛熳),
So much emotion, unable to speak (多少情懷不能語).
Green tresses doubly coiled, golden hairpin low (翠鬟雙綰金釵低),
Graceful spring shirt cut from green ramie (楚楚春衫裁綠紵).
East wind first breaks the paired-stem flower (東風初折並蔕花),
Don't let dense branches battle wind and rain (莫使繁枝戰風雨).
Fluttering immortal sleeves, shadows dance (飄飄仙袂影婆娑),
In the shade of cassia groves, the moon goddess dances (叢桂陰中素娥舞).
Before joyful matters end, sorrow surely follows (勝事未了愁必隨),
Don't compose new verses to teach the parrot (莫製新詞敎鸚鵡)."

After drinking, Ch'oe Nang said to Yi Saeng:
"Today's event is surely no small fate. Please follow me briefly to unfold our affections (情疑)."
Finishing her words, she entered through the north window. Yi Saeng followed. In the middle of the room were stairs leading up to the pavilion, so he went up there, reaching the pavilion room. Inside, writing implements and a desk (文房几案) were arranged extremely neatly and cleanly. On one wall hung paintings of "Misty River and Layered Peaks" (煙江壘嶂圖) and "Secluded Bamboo and Ancient Trees" (幽篁古木圖), both famous works (名畵). The artists were unknown, but verses were inscribed above each. The first read:

"Whose brush tip has such surplus power (何人筆端有餘力),
To paint this thousand-layered mountain mid-river (寫此江心千疊山)?
Magnificent, Fanghu, thirty thousand feet high (壯哉方壺三萬丈),
Half emerging from ethereal mist and clouds (半出縹緲烟雲間).
Distant vista hazy for hundreds of miles (遠勢微茫幾百里),
Nearby, steep peaks like green螺coiffures (近見崒嵂靑螺鬟).
Vast blue waves float in the distant sky (滄波淼淼浮遠空),
At dusk, gazing afar, sorrow for homeland arises (日暮遙望愁鄕關).
Facing this makes one feel desolate (對此令人意蕭索),
Suspecting one drifts on the Xiang River in wind and rain (疑泛湘江風雨灣)."

The second read:

"Secluded bamboo rustles as if with sound (幽篁蕭颯如有聲),
Ancient trees stoop and bend as if with feeling (古木偃蹇如有情).
Wild roots twist, attracting moss (狂根盤屈惹苺苔),
Old trunks gnarled, repelling wind and thunder (老幹矢矯棑風雷).
Within the mind lies the cave of creation (腦中自有造化窟),
How can the marvel be explained to bystanders (妙處豈與傍人說)?
Wei Yan (韋偃) and Ke (可) are already ghosts (韋偃與可已爲鬼),
How many know the leaked secrets of heaven (漏洩天機知有幾)?
In the bright window, calmly facing each other (晴窓嗒然淡相對),
Love to watch the phantom ink's divine samadhi (愛看幻墨神三昧)."

On another wall hung four poems depicting the Four Seasons. It was unclear whose poems they were, but the calligraphy imitated the style of Zhao Mengfu (松雪眞字) and was extremely refined (精硏).

Poem 1 [Spring]:
"Hibiscus curtain warm, fragrance like threads (芙蓉帳暖香如縷),
Outside the window, red apricot rain falls softly (窓外霏霏紅杏雨).
Lingering dream at the tower, fifth watch bell (樓頭殘夢五更鍾),
Thrush sings in the magnolia grove (百舌啼在辛夷塢).
Swallows linger, days long, chamber deep (燕子日長閨閤深),
Lazily, wordlessly stopping the golden needle (懶來無語停金針).
Beneath flowers, butterflies fly in pairs (花底雙雙蛺蝶飛),
Chasing falling blossoms in the courtyard shade (爭趂落花庭院陰).
Tender chill lightly penetrates the green gauze robe (嫩寒輕透綠羅裳),
Vainly facing the spring wind, heart secretly breaks (空對春風暗斷腸).
Who can fathom this silent feeling (脉脉此情誰料得)?
Amidst a hundred flowers, mandarin ducks dance (百花叢裡舞鴛鴦).
Spring colors hidden deep at Huang Si's home (春色深藏黃四家),
Deep red, light green reflect on the window gauze (深紅淺綠映窓紗).
A courtyard of fragrant grass, spring heart suffers (一庭芳草春心苦),<57>
Lightly lifting the pearl curtain to watch falling flowers (輕揭珠簾看落花)."

Poem 2 [Summer]:
"Wheat grains forming, swallow chicks hatch (小麥初胎乳燕科),
South garden opens, pomegranate flowers bloom (南園開編石榴花).
Green window, skillful woman, sharp Bingzhou knife (綠窓工女幷刀饗),
Intends to try cutting purple mist for a red skirt (擬試紅裙剪紫霞).
Plum rain season, drizzle fine (黃梅時節雨廉纖),
Parrot turns in locust shade, swallow enters curtain (鸚轉槐陰燕入廉).
Another year's scenery ages (又是一年風景老),
Paulownia flowers fall, zither bridge sharpens sound (棟花零落箏生尖).
Hand picks green apricot to hit the parrot (手拈靑杏打鸚兒),
Wind passes south veranda, sun's shadow lingers (風過南軒日影遲).
Lotus leaves already fragrant, pond water full (荷葉已香池水滿),
Deep in blue waves, kingfishers bathe (碧波深處浴虧鶿).
Rattan bed, bamboo mat, wave patterns (籐床筠簟浪波紋),
Screen painting, Xiao and Xiang rivers, a smear of cloud (屛畵癘湘一抹雲).
Too lazy to wake from midday dream (懶慢不堪醒午夢),
Half window slanted sun about to set in the west (半窓斜日欲西曛)."

Poem 3 [Autumn]:
"Autumn wind rustles, autumn dew condenses (秋風策策秋露凝),
Autumn moon bright, autumn water green (秋月娟娟秋水碧).
One sound, two sounds, wild geese return (一聲二聲鴻雁歸),
Listen further to paulownia leaves by the golden well (更聽金井梧桐葉).
Beneath the bed, hundred insects chirp (床下百虫鳴喞喞),
On the bed, beautiful woman sheds pearl tears (床上佳人珠淚滴).
Husband far away on campaign (良人萬里事征戰),
Tonight at Jade Gate Pass, the moon is white (今夜玉門關月白).
Wants to make new clothes, scissors cold (新衣欲製剪刀冷),
Lowers head, sniffs sleeve, calls for flatiron (低嗅丫兒呼熨斗).
Iron's fire dies, completely unaware (熨斗火銷全未省),
Finely plucks Qin zither, scratches head again (細撥秦箏又搔首).
Small pond lotus withered, banana leaves yellow (小池荷盡芭蕉黃),
On mandarin duck tiles, new frost sticks (鴛鴦瓦上粘新霜).
Old sorrows, new regrets cannot be contained (舊愁新恨不能禁),
Moreover, hearing crickets chirp in the bridal chamber (況聞蟀蟋鳴洞房)."

Poem 4 [Winter - Original uses 제5폭 (Poem 5) but context implies Winter]:
"A branch of plum blossom shadow leans towards the window (一枝梅影向窓橫),
Wind tightens, west corridor moon bright (風緊西廊月色明).
Stove fire not yet out, golden tongs stir (爐火未銷金筋撥),
Quickly calls maid to change the tea kettle (旋呼丫䯻換茶鐺).
Forest leaves often startled by midnight frost (林葉頻驚半夜霜),
Returning wind blows snow into the long corridor (回風飄雪入長廊).
A night of lovesick dreams for no reason (無端一夜相思夢),
All takes place on the icy river, ancient battlefield (都在氷河古戰場).
Window full of red sun, warm like spring (滿窓紅日似春溫),
Sorrow locks brows, showing sleep traces (愁鎖眉峰著睡痕).
Small plum in the vase, cheeks half-opened (膽甁小梅腮半吐),
Shyly silent, embroidering paired mandarin ducks (含羞不語綉雙鴛).
Cutting frost wind sweeps the north forest (剪剪霜風掠北林),
Cold bird cries at the moon, truly concerns the heart (寒鳥啼月正關心).
Before the lamp, tears for the thought-of person (燈前爲有思人淚),
Drip on the small thimble for threading silk (滴在穿絲小挫針)."

Beside it was a separately designated room. The curtains (幃帳), mats (褥), quilts, and pillows were all clean and tidy. Outside the curtain, musk incense (麝臍香) burned, its scent fragrant, and the lamps shone together, bright as day. Yi Saeng experienced all sorts of fun and joy with Ch'oe Nang, staying for several days without realizing the passage of time. One day, Yi Saeng said to Ch'oe Nang:
"The ancient sages said, 'When parents are alive, if you travel, you must let them know where you are going.' It has now been three days since I missed paying my morning and evening respects (定省). My parents must be waiting at the gate; this is not the way of a dutiful son."
Hearing this, Ch'oe Nang nodded, her face tinged with sadness, and helped him over the wall to leave. After this, Yi Saeng went to Ch'oe Nang's house every evening without fail. One evening, Yi Saeng's father said to him:
"You go out in the morning and return at dusk to learn the wise maxims of the Former Sages. But why do you leave in the dark and return at dawn? Aren't you surely engaging in frivolous behavior, climbing over someone's wall to pick flowers? If this gets out, won't everyone scold me for failing to teach my son properly? If that woman is from a renowned noble family (名門 右族), your mad actions will disgrace her family name. This is no small matter of incurring blame from others. Go immediately to Yŏngnam (嶺南), supervise the farming with the servants, and never return."
He scolded and reprimanded him with terrifying severity. The next day, Yi Saeng had no choice but to leave for Uljju (蔚州). Ch'oe Nang waited for Yi Saeng in the flower garden every evening, but after more than a couple of months passed and he didn't come at all, she worried he might have fallen ill. She secretly sent Hyang'a to Yi Saeng's neighborhood to inquire. A neighbor said:
"Yi Rang (李郞) offended his father and was banished to Yŏngnam; it's already been a couple of months."
Hearing this, Ch'oe Nang suddenly fell ill and couldn't get up. She couldn't even take a sip of water, didn't speak, and became terribly thin. Her parents found it very strange and asked about her illness, but she choked up and couldn't answer. So they looked through the young woman's chests and boxes, and the poems Yi Saeng had exchanged with her previously caught their eye. Slapping their knees, they exclaimed:
"We almost lost our daughter!"
They asked who Yi Saeng was. Only then did the young woman stop hiding it and, in a faint voice barely above a whisper, said the following:
"Father, Mother, the grace of raising me is<58> deep, so how could there be anything to hide? Quietly reflecting, mutual longing between man and woman is the most profound of human emotions (人情之至重). Thus, the auspiciousness of the falling plums (標梅) led to reciting the 'Odes of Zhou Nan' (周南), and the inauspiciousness of the Xian hexagram's hamstring (咸腓) was guarded against with the 'Yi Jing' (羲易). I, too, with my willow-like frailty (蒲柳之質), did not consider the 'Song of the Mulberry Leaves Falling' (桑落之詩) and let the 'dew on the path' (行露) soak my clothes, inviting ridicule (喧笑) from bystanders. Like dodder vine (絲蘿) clinging to a tree, I engaged in the conduct of a bridesmaid (媦兒). Thus, my sins have piled high (貫盈), and disgrace has befallen our family. However, that 'cunning boy' (狡童), having once tasted the fragrance, stirred a thousand regrets. My young and weak self endures the sorrow alone, but my thoughts grow ever more intense, my illness worsens daily, and I am about to become a vengeful ghost (怨鬼). If my parents grant my wish, I might preserve my remaining life. But if you go against my feelings, I would rather die and roam with Yi Saeng in the underworld than ever marry into another family."
Hearing these words, her parents understood her intentions. They didn't ask about the illness anymore but simply comforted and consoled her to ease her mind. At the same time, they arranged for a matchmaker to formally inquire at Yi Saeng's house. Mr. Yi asked about the standing of the Ch'oe family, saying:
"Although our son is young and perhaps unrefined (風采는 사나우나), he is well-versed in learning and shows promise. We hope he will become a dragon or phoenix in the future, so we do not wish for him to marry hastily."
The matchmaker returned and reported this. Mr. Ch'oe sent her back again, saying:
"All our friends say your esteemed son's talent (才華) surpasses others. Even though he might be coiled like a dragon now (蟠屈), wouldn't it be good to quickly set a joyful meeting date and attain the happiness of uniting our two families (二姓之合)?"
The matchmaker relayed this message to Yi Saeng's father. Mr. Yi said again:
"Since my youth, I held books and studied the classics, but I achieved no fame even in old age. My servants have all fled, relatives offer little help, my livelihood is bleak, and my household is struggling. Why would a renowned and noble family want to take a humble scholar from a poor background as a son-in-law (贅壻)? Perhaps some busybody (好事者) exaggerated our family's status and deceived your esteemed house (高門)."
The matchmaker conveyed this to Mr. Ch'oe as well. Mr. Ch'oe said again:
"I will bear all costs for the betrothal gifts (納采) and wedding preparations (裝束). It is best to choose an auspicious day and set the date for the wedding ceremony (華燭)."
The matchmaker relayed this to Mr. Yi. At this point, Mr. Yi changed his mind and sent someone to ask his son. Yi Saeng could not contain his joy and wrote a poem:

"A broken bell becoming round again has its time (破鍾重圓會有時),
Magpies at the Tianjin ford aid the auspicious date (天津烏鵲助佳期).
From now, the Old Man in the Moon ties the red thread (從今月老纏繩去),
Don't resent the cuckoo towards the east wind (莫向東風怨子規)."

Hearing this, Ch'oe Nang's illness slightly improved, and she also wrote a poem:

"Bad fate turns out to be good fate (惡因緣是好因緣),
Vows must ultimately be fulfilled completely (盟語終須到底圓).
When will be the day we pull the deer cart together (共輓鹿車何日是)?
Help me up to arrange my flower ornaments (倩人扶起理花鈿)."

Thus, an auspicious day was chosen, and the wedding ceremony was held, rejoining the severed connection. Afterward, the couple respected each other like guests. Even Liang Hong and Meng Guang (鴻光鮑桓) could not speak of greater marital virtue (節義). The following year, Yi Saeng passed the high state examination (高科) and his fame spread throughout the court and country (朝野).

However, in the Sinch'uk year (辛丑年, 1361), the Red Turbans (紅賊) occupied the capital city (京城, Kaesŏng). The king moved to Pogju (福州, Andong), houses turned to ash, and people and livestock were butchered (臠灸). At this time, Yi Saeng's family, his wife's family, and relatives could not stay together and fled in all directions. Yi Saeng took his family (家內) and hid in a remote cave. When one of the bandits came chasing with a sword, Yi Saeng fled and escaped death, but his wife was captured by the bandit. The woman cried out:
"Tiger spirit, eat me! I would rather die and be buried in the belly of a wolf (豺狼) than become the mate of a dog or pig!"
The bandit flew into a rage, killed her, and tore her apart.

Yi Saeng hid in the rough fields and barely survived. Hearing that the bandits had been defeated, he returned to find his parents and his old home. Heartbreakingly, the house had been completely burned down in the fires of war (兵火). He went to his wife's family home, but there too, in the desolate corridors (廊廡), only mice squeaked and birds<59> chirped. Overcome with grief, he went up to the small pavilion (小樓), wiped his tears, and let out long sighs as the day suddenly grew dark. With a heart full of sorrow, he sat alone, thinking of the times he had enjoyed extreme happiness and play with Ch'oe Nang there; it seemed like a spring dream (一場春夢). As the second watch (2更) approached, the faint moonlight began to illuminate the roof beams (屋樑). Suddenly, there was the sound of footsteps from the corridor, gradually drawing nearer. Who could it be? It was Mrs. Ch'oe, his beloved wife. Yi Saeng knew she had died, but his fervent love dispelled all doubt.
"Where did you take refuge to survive?"
Yi Saeng asked. The woman took Yi's hand, wept bitterly (慟哭), and said:
"I was originally from a good family (良族). From childhood, I followed family teachings, learned embroidery, sewing, and other tasks, studied poetry, calligraphy, benevolence, and righteousness, knowing only the governance of the women's quarters (閨門). How could I have known about affairs beyond the borders (境外)? However, after once glimpsing outside the wall draped with red apricot blossoms, I offered the pearl from the blue sea (碧海之珠). With one smile beneath the flowers, we pledged our lives. Indeed, we met again within the bed curtains (帳裏) and were to grow old together for a hundred years. Speaking of this, it is hard to suppress my sorrow... How could I have known I would suffer a violent death (橫折), tumbling into a pit, my body refusing to yield to a beast-like man, smearing my own flesh and blood on the ground? This stemmed from natural character (天性), not something human feeling (人情) could bear. After parting in the pit, I finally became a lone wild goose separated from its mate. My home is destroyed, my parents gone. I grieve that my dead soul (넋) has no place to rest. However, righteousness (義) is heavy and life (命) is light; I count myself fortunate not to have suffered disgrace upon my body. Who will mend my heart, broken piece by piece? Who will reconnect my intestines, rotten coil by coil? My skeleton lies scattered in the fields, my liver and gallbladder (肝膽) smeared in the dirt. Thinking of past happiness feels like today's sorrow and resentment (愁寃). Now, the Pipes of Zou Yan (鄒律) have blown in the幽谷 (secluded valley), and the spirit maiden (倩女) has returned to the world of the living (陽間). The promise of twelve years at Penglai (蓬萊 一紀) is binding (綢繆), and the fragrance of three lives at Juku (聚窟 三生) is rich (芬郁). Let us now renew our efforts, uphold our former vows, and never forget. Would Master Yi (李郞) permit this?"

Yi Saeng, both joyful and moved, replied:
"That is indeed my wish (固所願)."
They expressed their complex feelings of sorrow and joy, exchanging endless intimate talk. When the conversation turned to the plundered family property, Mrs. Ch'oe replied:
"Not a penny was lost. I buried it in a certain mountain valley."
Yi Saeng asked again:
"Where are the remains of both our parents?"
Mrs. Ch'oe replied:
"They lie abandoned in a certain place."
After concluding their multifaceted intimate conversation (萬端 情話), they slept together, their joy like before. The next day, she went with Yi Saeng to the place where the treasure was buried. After retrieving the gold, silver, and other valuables, they went to where the remains of both sets of parents lay scattered, collected them, sold the treasures, and buried them together properly at the foot of Ogwan Mountain (五冠山), performing the rites with due ceremony. After this, Yi Saeng no longer sought public office (仕宦) but found pleasure only in the joy of living together with Mrs. Ch'oe. Servants who had fled and survived returned. From this time on, Yi Saeng became negligent in worldly affairs (人事). Even when relatives or guests came for celebrations or condolences (賀吊), he kept the gate firmly shut and did not emerge, only exchanging words with Mrs. Ch'oe; their marital harmony (琴瑟) could not be greater.

Time flowed by, and several years passed before they knew it. One evening, Mrs. Ch'oe suddenly said to Yi:
"We have met for three auspicious periods (佳期). Worldly affairs falter (蹉跎), and joy turns to sorrow. The sad time of parting has suddenly arrived."
She choked up and wept. Yi Saeng, taken aback, asked in astonishment:
"What is the reason for this?"
Mrs. Ch'oe replied:
"Fate cannot be avoided. However, Heaven (하누님),<60> acknowledging that the bond between you and me was not severed and that I committed no crime, allowed my illusory form (幻體) to meet with you temporarily to alleviate your sorrow. I cannot remain long in this world and mislead the living of the Yang realm (陽界)."
She had a servant bring wine and recited a verse to the tune of 'Yùchūnlóu' (玉春樓):

"Weapons clash, filling the eyes (干戈滿目交揮處),
Jade shattered, flowers scattered, mandarin ducks lose their mates (玉碎花飛鴛失侶).
Scattered remains, who finally buried them (殘骸狼藉竟誰埋)?
Blood-stained wandering soul with no one to talk to (血汚遊魂無與語)?
Once descended from Gaotang (高唐), the woman of Mount Wu (巫山女),
Broken bell restored, yet heart grieves bitterly (破鍾重兮心慘楚).
From this parting, both vanish into茫茫 (vastness) (從玆一別兩茫茫),
Between heaven and earth, messages are blocked (天上人間音信阻)."

Each time she sang a line, sobs welled up twice, making it almost impossible to form a melody. Yi Saeng, unable to suppress his anguish (悲愴), said:
"I would rather return to the nine springs (九泉, underworld) with you than meaninglessly preserve my remaining life alone. After the chaos of war (兵亂), when relatives and servants were all scattered and the bones of my deceased parents lay exposed on the hillsides, if it weren't for you, who would have buried them properly (勘葬)? The ancients said, 'Serve them with propriety while living, bury them with propriety when dead.' Wasn't it you who accomplished all this? I am moved beyond words by your pure filial piety (純孝) and profound kindness (篤厚), and at the same time, I cannot bear my shame. Please stay long in this world, and after a hundred years, let us become dust (塵土) together."
Mrs. Ch'oe replied:
"Your lifespan (壽) still has remainder, but my name is already recorded in the register of ghosts (鬼籙), so I cannot linger long. If I cling obstinately to the human world, I will violate the rules (條令). This would not only bring blame upon me but also implicate you. My only wish is that my remains (遺骸), scattered in a certain place, be gathered and buried, so they might be sheltered from wind and rain."
They looked at each other, weeping beyond words. After speaking her precious words, Mrs. Ch'oe gradually faded away, disappearing without a trace. Following her words, Yi Saeng collected her remains and buried them near his parents' mountain tomb (親山). However, after the burial was finished, Yi Saeng fell ill from the pain of remembrance and died within a couple of months. Everyone who heard this news lamented and sighed, and none failed to admire their righteousness (義理).

(Appendix) This is a translation of "Yi Saeng Kyujang Chŏn" (李生窺墻傳) from Maewŏltang Kim Si-sŭp's (梅月堂 金時習) "Kŭmo Sinhwa" (金鰲新話). The original text contains many quotations from difficult classical Chinese texts (Book of Songs, etc.) not commonly used today. As I translated hastily (忽忽히) without much time to consult the original sources, there may be many points that are inadequate, and I believe there might be outright errors (誤錯). I hope the reader will understand (惠諒).
July 25th, at the study
in Nuhadong (樓下洞)

**Summary**

This article is a Korean translation of the classical story "Yi Saeng Kyujang Chŏn" from Kim Si-sŭp's 15th-century work "Kŭmo Sinhwa". It recounts the passionate love between Yi Saeng and Ch'oe Nang in Songdo (Kaesŏng), their secret meetings, forced separation, eventual marriage, and tragic parting during the Red Turban invasion (1361). Ch'oe Nang is killed but returns as a ghost to reunite with Yi Saeng for several years before finally departing for the afterlife, after which Yi Saeng dies of grief. From a colonial history perspective, the publication of this classic Korean romance in 1931 is noteworthy. It represents an effort to preserve and disseminate traditional Korean literature and cultural narratives during a period of Japanese assimilation policies, potentially serving as a subtle assertion of distinct Korean identity and heritage. The translator's note about the difficulty of classical Chinese also reflects the linguistic shifts occurring during this period.

李生과 崔娘, 金鰲神話奇談
						한결生 譯
松都 駱駝橋 곁에 李生이란 자가 살앗섯다. 늘 國學에 댕기면서 길꺼리에서 시를 읊엇다. 18의 年紀로서 얼골 좋고 才操 잇기로 일컬엇다.
또한 당시 善竹里에는 宮門 貴骨에 태어난 방년 二八이 될락말락하는 崔娘이 살앗는데 자태가 또한 艶美한 중에 자수 잘 하고 詩賦 능하기로 일컬엇다. 그리하여 사람들이 이르기를
『風流李氏子,
窈窕崔家娘.
才色若可餐,
可以療飢腸』.
이라 하엿다. 李生은 책을 끼고 학교에 갈 때마다 崔娘의 집 뒤 담 밖으로 지나게 되는데 거기에는 질질 끌릴만치 가지가 땅에 드리운 수양 수십 株가 물러섯는 고로 李生은 그 밑에 쉬는 것이 예사엿다. 李生이 하로는 담 안을 엿본 즉 名花가 만발하고 벌과 새들이 그 사이에 노래하고 그 옆에는 한 적은 다락(樓)이 잇어서 꽃떨기 속에서 隱映하며 珠簾은 반쯤 걷히고 깁 휘장(羅幃)은 낮윽히 드리웟는데 그 속에서는 한 미인이 수를 놓다가 바눌을 멈추고 턱을 고인 채로 시를 읊고 잇엇다-
『獨倚紗窓刺繡遲,
百花叢裏囀黃鸝.
無端暗結東風怨,
不語停針有所思.
路上誰家白面朗,
靑衿大帶映垂楊.
何方可化堂中燕,
佤掠珠簾斜度墻』.
李生은 이것을 듣고 마음이 간지러움을 이기기 어렵엇지마는 큰 문은 높고 험하며 중문은 깊고 겹겹이어서 할 일 없이 섭섭이 갓다. 돌아올 때에 李生은 백지 한 폭에 시 세 수를 적어서 조악돌을 매어 담 넘어로 던지엇다-
『巫山六六霧重回,
半露尖峯紫翠堆.
惱却襄王孤枕夢,
肯爲雲雨下陽臺.
相如欲挑卓文君,
多少情懷已十分.
紅粉墻頭桃李艶,
隨風何處落繽粉.
好因緣邪惡因緣,
空把愁膓日抵年.
二十八字媒已就,
藍橋何日遇神仙.』
崔娘은 侍婢 香兒를 시키어 가지어다가 보니 그것이 곳 李生의 시엿다. 두 번 세 번 거듭 읽고 마음에 기뿜을 이기지 못하여 곳 조히 쪽에 여덟 자를 적어 던지니 그 말은 곳
『將子無疑, 昏以爲期』
라 함이엇다. 李生은 그 말대로 어두울 때를 타아 가아보니 桃花 한 가지가 담에 걸치엇는데 흔들흔들 그림자가 빛이어 보이엇다. 가까이 가니 이는 그네줄에 대 앉을깨(竹兜)를 달아 드리운 것이엇다. 李生은 곳 그것을 타고 담을 넘어 들어갓다. 때마침 달은 동산에 떠 오르게 되어 꽃 그림자가 땅을 수놓고 맑은 향기가 코를 움즉이엇다. 李生은 생각하기를 내가 仙境에 들어옴이나 아닌가 하여 마음에 그윽히 기쁨을 이기지 못하엿으나 비밀한 사정이라 모발이 치솟는 듯 하엿다. 좌우를 돌아 보니 그 여자는 이미 꽃떨기 속에서 香兒를 데리고 꽃을 꺾어 머리에 꽂고 으식한 곳에 氈褥를 깔다가 李生의 들어옴을 보고 빙그레 웃으면서
『桃李枝間富貴
鴛鴦枕上月嬋娟』
을 먼저 부름으로 李生은 이에 화답하에
『他時漏洩春消息,＜56＞
風雨無情亦可憐.』
이라 하매 崔娘은 變色하면서 말하기를
『본대 군으로 더부러 종신토록 箕箒를 받들면서 환락을 같이 하려 하엿는데 이제 무슨 말을 그처럼 하십니까? 나는 비록 여자지마는 마음이 태연한데 君은 장부의 意氣로서 어찌 이러한 말슴을 하실 수 잇습니까? 후일에 만일 규중의 일이 친정에 누설되더라도 책임을 나에게 지우시오. 내가 감당하리다』
하면서 香兒를 방으로 보내어 酒果를 차리어 오라 하엿다. 香兒는 그 명령대로 방으로 갓다. 사방은 고요한데 人氣좇아 들리지 않앗다. 李生이 묻기를
『여기는 과연 어떠한 곳입니까?』
한즉 崔娘이 대답하기를
『이곳은 우리 뒤뜰(後園) 중 적은 다락(小樓) 밑이외다. 부모께서 내가 외딸이라고 극히 사랑하시어서 芙蓉못(池)가에다 이 다락을 따로 짓고 봄날 萬花가 아름답게 필 때면 나로 하여금 侍兒를 데리고 이곳에서 재미잇게 노닐게 하고저 한 것입니다. 부모님이 거처하기는 도장(閨閣)은 멀고 깊어서 여기서 이야기하고 웃고 깔깔대더라도 들리지 아니합니다.』
하고 綠蟻酒 한 잔을 가득이 부어 李生에게 권하면서 입으로 읊기를
『曲闌下壓芙蓉池,
池上花叢人共語.
香霧霏霏春融融,
製出新詞歌白紵.
月轉花陰入氍毹,
共挽長條落紅雨.
風攪淸香香襲衣,
賈女初踏春陽舞.
羅衫輕拂海棠枝,
驚起花間宿鸚鵡』.
라 할 때에 李生은 술잔을 받으면서
『誤入桃源花爛熳,
多少情懷不能語.
翠鬟雙綰金釵低,
楚楚春衫裁綠紵.
東風初折並蔕花,
莫使繁枝戰風雨.
飄飄仙袂影婆娑,
叢桂陰中素娥舞.
勝事未了愁必隨,
莫製新詞敎鸚鵡.
라고 和唱하엿다. 마시기를 마치매 崔娘이 李生에게 이르되
『오늘의 일은 필시 적은 인연이 아니니 그대는 잠간 나를 딸아 와서 情疑를 펴게 하십시오』
하면서 말을 마치자 곳 北窓으로 들어갓다. 李生도 딸아 들어갓다. 방 가온대 다락으로 올라가는 층계가 잇는 고로 그리로 올라가니 곳 다락방이엇다. 그 안에는 文房几案이 극히 整齊하고 깨끗하게 놓이어 잇엇다. 한편 벽에는 煙江壘嶂圖와 幽篁古木圖가 걸리엇는데 이는 다 名畵엿다. 작자는 누군지 모르겟으나 그 우에 語가 각각 적히어 잇는데 그 첫재는,
『何人筆端有餘力.
寫此江心千疊山,
壯哉方壺三萬丈.
半出縹緲烟雲間,
遠勢微茫幾百里.
近見崒嵂靑螺鬟,
滄波淼淼浮遠空.
日暮遙望愁鄕關,
對此令人意蕭索.
疑泛湘江風雨灣』.
이라 함이고 그 둘재는
『幽篁蕭颯如有聲.
古木偃蹇如有情.
狂根盤屈惹苺苔,
老幹矢矯棑風雷.
腦中自有造化窟,
妙處豈與傍人說.
韋偃與可已爲鬼,
漏洩天機知有幾.
晴窓嗒然淡相對,
愛看幻墨神三昧』.
라 함이엇다. 또 한편 벽에는 四時景의 네 수 시가 붙어 잇는데 그것도 누구의 詩ㄴ지 알 수 없으나 그 글시는 松雪眞字를 본뜬 것으로서 그 體가 극히 精硏하엿다.
제1폭 『芙蓉帳暖香如縷,
窓外霏霏紅杏雨.
樓頭殘夢五更鍾,
百舌啼在辛夷塢.
燕子日長閨閤深,
懶來無語停金針.
花底雙雙蛺蝶飛,
爭趂落花庭院陰.
嫩寒輕透綠羅裳,
空對春風暗斷腸.
脉脉此情誰料得,
百花叢裡舞鴛鴦.
春色深藏黃四家,
深紅淺綠映窓紗.
一庭芳草春心苦,＜57＞
輕揭珠簾看落花』.
제2폭 『小麥初胎乳燕科,
南園開編石榴花.
綠窓工女幷刀饗,
擬試紅裙剪紫霞.
黃梅時節雨廉纖,
鸚轉槐陰燕入廉.
又是一年風景老,
棟花零落箏生尖.
手拈靑杏打鸚兒,

『干戈滿目交揮處,

天上人間音信阻』.
한 번 노래를 읊으면 두 번 울음이 북받히게 됨으로 자못 곡조를 이루지 못하게 되엇다. 李生도 悲愴함을 억제하지 못하면서
『차라리 당신과 함께 구천에 돌어가는 것이 낫지 어떠케 부질없이 나 혼자만 殘生을 보전하겟니까? 向者 兵亂 뒤에도 친척과 종들은 다 각각 흩어지고 亡親의 해골이 들 언덕에 뒤널리엇을 때에 만일 당신이 아니엇더면 누구가 이를 勘葬하엿겟습니까? 옛 사람이 이르기를 살아서는 예로써 섬기고 죽어서는 예로써 장사한다고 하엿는데 이것을 다 한 이는 당신이 아닙니까? 천성이 純孝하고 인정이 篤厚하심을 감격하여 말지 않는 동시에 부끄럼을 이기지 못하는 바외다. 당신은 오래 이 세상에 머물어 잇다가 백 세 후에 나와 함께 塵土가 되기를 바람니다.』
하엿다. 崔씨는 이에 대하여 이르되
『당신의 壽는 아직 나마지가 잇거니와 나는 이미 鬼籙에 이름이 오른 고로 오래 지체할 수가 없습니다. 만일 인간에 굳이 애착하게 된다면 條令을 어기게 됩니다. 이는 나에게 죄가 될 뿐 아니라 누가 당신에게도 및이게 됩니다. 단지 바라는 것은 나의 유해가 아무 곳에 흩어지어 잇으니 그것이나 글어 묻어주시면 비바람이나 막겟습니다.』
하고 서로 마주 보고 느끼어 울기를 말이 아니하엿다. 珍重히 말을 마치자 崔氏는 차차 사라지더니 온대 간대를 모르게 종적이 없어지엇다. 李生은 그의 말대로 그 유골을 걷우어 親山 믿에 장사하여 주엇다. 그러하나 장사가 끝난 뒤로 이생은 추억의 쓰림이 병이 되어 두어 달만에 죽고 말엇다. 이 소문을 들은 사람마다 설어하고 탄식하며 그 의리를 사모하지 않는 이가 없엇다.
(附記) 이것은 梅月堂 金時習의 『金鰲新話』 중의 「李生窺墻傳」을 번역한 것입니다. 원문에는 현대에 보통으로 쓰이지 아니하는 난삽한 한문 고전(詩傳 기타)에서 인용함이 많은데 元출전을 별로 참고할 겨를도 없이 忽忽히 번역하엿기 때문에 미흡한 점도 많거니와 전혀 誤錯된 점도 없지 않으리라고 생각됩니다. 독자는 惠諒하심 바을랍니다.
