Article Title: 願과 念과 勤 (Aspiration, Remembrance, and Diligence)

Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

Aspiration, Remembrance, and Diligence

Young men and women must certainly establish a lifelong aspiration (願). They must firmly set a goal, saying, "I will accomplish this work in my lifetime." If this aspiration cannot be achieved in this life, they must resolve to achieve it even if it takes reincarnating through three, four, up to a hundred or a thousand lives (三生 四生 내지 百生 千生). They must resolve not to close their eyes in death (暝目) before achieving it. The great figures and patriots (志士) of ancient times (古來) were all people who set such aspirations.

"In my lifetime, I will surely make Chosŏn a Chosŏn of knowledge (知), wealth (富), harmony (和合), and peace and comfort (安樂)."

Though there may be countless aspirations depending on the individual, is this not a fitting aspiration for the youth of Chosŏn today? If the number of people establishing this aspiration increases, wouldn't the land of Chosŏn be greatly stirred (掀動) by that power of aspiration (願力)? The first thing for the youth of Chosŏn to do today is to establish an aspiration. "Even if heaven and earth collapse (天地가 문이지더라도), my aspiration will remain unchanged!"

However, even if one establishes an aspiration, what good is it if forgotten? Just as a hen incubates her eggs day and night, keeping them warm, one who has established a great aspiration (大願) must remember (念) it in the morning, remember it in the evening, remember it while sitting, and remember it while walking, never forgetting it for a moment. Only then will that aspiration gain more fervor and strength.

Chosŏn people tend to be forgetful (乾忘性); many things sworn with tied coat strings (옷고름 맺고 맹세한 것) are forgotten before long. It's a rat's mind (쥐 정신). They see something and instantly forget it; they cry and wail and play around (작난하고) one moment, and forget it the next. This is why there is a need to constantly (時時로) remember the aspiration once set. One must remember it without pause to renew the power of aspiration.

However, no matter how great an aspiration one sets, and no matter how constantly one remembers it without dozing off, what is the use if there is no action? One must establish a plan (計劃) to achieve that aspiration, and like an ant digging the earth, like a bee gathering honey, one must labor (勞役) diligently (勤) without rest, even if only little by little (조곰식이라도), following that plan to achieve the aspiration. This is called diligence (勤). An aspiration without diligence is like a boat without water.

A person without aspiration is a person without a soul (혼); a person with a great aspiration is a person with a great soul. Isn't the time of youth precisely the time to develop a great aspiration? Isn't it the time to set a great aspiration that, if not achievable in one lifetime, will be achieved through three or four, even nine or ten lives? Oh, brothers (兄弟) and sisters (姉妹) who set great aspirations, remember them greatly, and practice diligence greatly, rise up by the thousands (千) and tens of thousands (萬)! (Ch'unwŏn) <1>

**Summary**

The article, titled "Aspiration, Remembrance, and Diligence," exhorts Korean youth to cultivate three essential qualities for a meaningful life and national progress. First, they must establish a strong, lifelong aspiration (願 - Wŏn), ideally focused on creating a knowledgeable, wealthy, harmonious, and peaceful Chosŏn. Second, this aspiration must be constantly kept in mind (念 - Yŏm) to maintain its power, countering the perceived Korean tendency towards forgetfulness. Third, diligent, persistent effort (勤 - Kŭn) through planned action is crucial to actually realize the aspiration. The author, Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu), frames this as essential for both personal fulfillment ("a person without aspiration is a person without a soul") and collective uplift, urging young people to embrace this path with unwavering resolve, even across lifetimes if necessary.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects a common theme in colonial Korean intellectual discourse: emphasizing inner strength, self-cultivation, and persistent effort as means for Koreans to improve their situation under Japanese rule. While not overtly political resistance, the call to build a better "Chosŏn" clearly carries nationalistic undertones. The blend of personal development advice, Buddhist concepts (reincarnation, mindfulness), and national aspiration provides insight into the ideologies shaping the youth of the time, particularly the emphasis on moral and mental fortitude in the face of external constraints. Historians might note the focus on internal qualities and long-term vision as a form of non-confrontational nationalism or cultural self-strengthening advocated by prominent figures like Yi Kwangsu.

願과 念과 勤
남녀청년은 반듯이 일생의 願을 發할 것이다. 『나는 일생에 이러한 일을 하리라』하는 願을 굳게 세울 것이다. 지금에 이 願을 못 일우면 三生 四生 내지 百生 千生을 윤회하더라도 이 願을 일우고야 말리라. 일우기 前에는 暝目하지 아니하리라 하는 願을 세울 것이다. 古來의 위인, 志士들은 다 이 願을 세운 이들이다.
『나는 일생에 朝鮮을 知와 富와 和合과 安樂의 朝鮮을 만들고야 말리라.』
개인을 딸아 무수한 願이 있으려니와 오늘날 朝鮮 청년으로 이것이 맞당한 願이 아닐가. 이 願을 세우는 이가 많아진다 하면 그 願力으로 朝鮮 천지가 掀動되지 아니할까. 오늘 朝鮮 청년이 할 일은 첫째로 願을 세우는 것이다. 『天地가 문이지더라도 내 願은 변함이 없으리라!』
그러나 한 번 願을 세우더라도 잊어버리면 무엇이 되랴. 닭이 밤낮으로 알을 품어 그 알을 식지 않게 하는 모양으로 한번 大願을 세운 자는 아침에 念하고 저녁에 念하고 앉으매 念하고 걸으매 念하여 일쯕 잊어버림이 없어야 비롯오 그 願은 더욱 열을 얻고 더욱 힘을 얻을 것이다.
朝鮮人은 건망성이 있어 옷고름 맺고 맹세한 것도 어느 새에 잊어버리는 일이 많다. 쥐 정신이다. 금방 보고 금방 잊고 금방 울고 불고 작난하고 금방 잊는다. 時時로 한번 세운 願을 念할 필요가 이래서 있는 것이다. 잊을 새가 없이 念하여 願力을 새롭게 할 것이다.
그러나 아무리 大願을 세우고 또 시시로 念하여 졸지 아니한다 하더라도 함이 없으면 무엇하랴. 그 願을 달할 계획을 세우고 땅을 파는 개미 모양으로, 꿀을 모우는 벌 모양으로, 쉬지 않고 꾸준히 조곰식이라도 그 계획을 딸아 願을 達하기 위하여 勞役함이 있어야 할 것이다. 이것을 勤이라고 한다. 勤 없는 願은 물 없는 배와 같다.
願이 없는 사람은 혼이 없는 사람이요 願이 큰 사람은 큰 혼을 가진 사람이다. 청년 아이때가 바야흐로 大願을 발할 때가 아닌가. 일생에 못 달할 願이어든 三生 四生으로 九生 十生에 달할 大願을 세울 때가 아닌가. 큰 願을 세우고 크게 念하고 크게 勤하는 형제와 자매야 千으로 일아나고 萬으로 일어날찌어다.(春園)＜1＞
