From Troy
						Kim Yŏje, in America

Ch'unwŏn Hyŏng,

I am taking a moment from work to write these few words. About two weeks ago, I left New York City and came to Troy City. I came this far following a job opportunity. This place is upstream on the Hudson River and is one of the old cities in the New England states. It is about 600 *ri* away from New York. I will work here until early September and then head to Columbia University in New York City on the opening day of school, September 15th. On the way, I am thinking of visiting the Sesquicentennial International Exposition being held in Philadelphia to commemorate 150 years of American Independence. When I get to Columbia, I will enroll in Teachers College. It is the best in the country for Education. Among our countrymen in the Education Department, those specializing solely in an M.A. are very rare. Most study hoping for a degree but quit midway. The more I go on, the more I realize how difficult English is to learn. From my perspective, it seems that among the students studying in North America, the path to becoming a true scholar (大學家) is becoming increasingly distant day by day. There are a few people studying for a Ph.D., but it seems most, due to difficulties with the language, are not truly engaged in research work. I wonder if they are barely managing to avoid failing. The prevailing illness among North American students is egoism. I do not know who among them strives to seek the Truth. (Remainder omitted - original text) <69>

**Summary**

Kim Yŏje writes a personal letter to "Ch'unwŏn Hyŏng" (likely Yi Kwangsu) from Troy, New York, in 1926. He details his temporary work, plans to attend Teachers College at Columbia University after visiting the Sesquicentennial Exposition in Philadelphia, and reflects on the difficulties of studying English. He critically observes fellow Korean students in North America, noting their struggles with advanced studies (Ph.D. research) due to language barriers and what he perceives as a widespread "egoism" rather than a pursuit of truth. For historians, this letter offers a valuable glimpse into the life and mindset of an early Korean student intellectual in the United States during the colonial period, including academic aspirations, financial realities (moving for work), observations on the Korean student diaspora's challenges, and connections within the Korean intellectual network (addressing Yi Kwangsu).

春園兄
일하는 집에서 짬을 타 數字들임니다. 아마 약 2주간 전에 뉴욕市를 떠나 Troy市로 왓슴니다. 일자리를 딸아 이까지 왓슴니다. 이곳은 Hudson River의 상류요 New England states의 오랜 도시중의 一이외다. 뉴욕서 약 600里 相離외다. 이곳서 9월 초순까지 일하고 9월 15일 開學日에 뉴욕시 컬럼비야 大學으로 향하겟슴니다. 가는 길에 필라델피아에 개최될 美國 獨立 150년 紀念 博覽會에 참관하려고 생각함니다. 컬럼비야에 가면 Teachers College에 입학하겟슴니다. 敎育科로는 국내에 제일이외다. 敎育科에 本國 사람으로 M. A.만을 전공하는 이 매우 드무외다. 대개는 學位를 바라고 공부하다가 중도에 그만둠니다. 弟 역 갈쓰록 英語의 難學임을 깨닷겟슴니다. 나 보기에는 北美 留學生 중에서 大學家가 날날이 매우 遼遠한 듯 하외다. Ph. D.를 공부하는 이 멧 사람 잇스나 대개는 語學 困難에 참으로 Research Work을 하는 이 없는 듯 하외다. 겨우 落第를 면할 뿐인가 함니다. 北美 留學生의 流行病은 利己主義외다. 眞理를 찻기 위하여 노력하는 이 그 누구인지 모르겟슴니다.(下略-원문) ＜69＞
