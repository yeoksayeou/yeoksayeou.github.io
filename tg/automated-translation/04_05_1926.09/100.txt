A Fragment of Wandering
						T'aehŏ

Leaving such a tragic scene behind me, I ended up following the pockmarked man who was going to buy liquor. Unsure where the liquor store was, and as the night seemed already deep, we wandered through the dark streets for quite a while until we finally found an *udong* house that hadn't closed yet and went inside. Though they had liquor at that house too, the pockmarked man, realizing that the price of *udong* was less than the price of liquor, ordered fourteen bowls of *udong*. Among them, the two of us ate four bowls, and we returned carrying ten bowls. Despite this, the only complaint was, "Why wasn't there any liquor?" and it lasted only as long as the words were spoken. As soon as the pockmarked man said, "Be quiet, let's buy some and drink when it gets bright," everyone started eating.

Sweet Sleep in Dirty Bedding
Maktong, who had shown me favor from the beginning, had even prepared my sleeping spot and was waiting. However, there was another pockmarked man among us who was arguing with Maktong over the sleeping spot. But following my insistence, I lay down at the very edge, had Maktong lie next to me, and chased the other pockmarked man away. Maktong was a young man from Chinju, younger than me. Although I was still clothed, I didn't have the courage to actually lie down. Yet, sleepiness was overwhelming, and I couldn't just not sleep. Lying down meant I couldn't refuse Maktong's kindness, nor was there any other way even if I could refuse. Helplessly, I sat and then lay down on that bedding. At first, I tried lying down supporting my whole body with my heels, elbows, and a small part of my buttocks, but I couldn't keep straining my entire body like that for long. Unable to bear it, I began to let my back touch the bedding. My closed eyes squeezed shut once more, forcefully. It wasn't long. The coldness relentlessly creeping in from all directions also made me grope for the blanket—a smelly, dirty, damp blanket like a winter cow's rump, so worn out that the cover was almost gone, revealing clumps of black cotton stuffing. Careful not to wake Maktong, who seemed already asleep, I gently pulled one edge and lightly covered only my stomach. Covering just my stomach wasn't enough. One leg at a time, I covered both legs. My two arms felt left out. I tucked both arms in as well. But inside, I couldn't just lie still. So, whenever I changed position and a previously untouched spot made contact,<31> I shuddered. Wrestling with these troubles, enduring the hardship, and making resolutions, I eventually reached a state of indifference. The clammy dampness clinging to my stomach and back from the blanket and mattress dried up thanks to my body heat, and a warm feeling emerged. As it grew warm, my head also felt at ease; my head had been needlessly stubborn.

Midnight Steam Whistle Sound
Within the makeshift hut (假屋), even the remaining candle flickered out, and it became deathly quiet. Lying there on a straw mat under ragged bedding in such a house, located in a desolate field amidst marshy reeds, alongside rough, unfamiliar laborers (*nogada*), a feeling of sorrow surged up, so much that I truly felt like crying. Moreover, since it wasn't far from the train station, whenever the steam whistle blew, it felt as if the very source of my seven emotions (七情) was being wrenched out. No matter how experimentally one tastes it, it's natural for a person with sound nerves to feel the unpleasantness of bitterness as bitterness.
Amidst this, on one hand, out of curiosity, I listened intently to Maktong's breathing beside me, another person's snoring, the sound of someone turning over, the creaking of the floorboards at that moment, the sound of frogs croaking—crying then stopping in the East, starting then ceasing in the West, then in the North, taking turns like this—and the buzzing sound of a mosquito passing by my ear. I listened intently to all these sounds. The drowsiness that seemed like it would make me sleep through anything, even if a tiger carried me away, vanished somewhere, and instead, my mind became clear (灑落). As my mind cleared, its activity fled to the land of memory.

People I Remember
The master of the house in Pinheungni had gone far, far North. Whether eating well or poorly, when he was there, at least one's heart felt strong. But he was gone. Mr. Yi, who had trudged all the way from Vladivostok (海蔘威), driven by destitution (否連), sat smoking his long, crooked pipe, just gazing at the ceiling as his routine. Mr. Yu, who had become even more melancholic after receiving the sad news of his father—a key figure in the Communist Party—being assassinated by someone from a certain country at Suhangnyŏng (蘇項嶺), had recently quit his job, wasn't even going to the park, and spent his days flipping through books or writing in his diary. Upstairs were Mr. Ch'a, Ms. Hwang, Miss Ch'a, and Miss Kim. Mr. Ch'a went out in the morning and returned late at night, consistently putting effort into his public duties (公務). Ms. Hwang would say, "Since the teacher left, even making my own meals is bothersome. Eh, what should I make today? Let's just make whatever, shall we?" Whether she cooked rice or made porridge (粥), whenever the sound of Ms. Hwang rattling something in the kitchen was heard, everyone, without exception, felt a trusting sense of relief. Miss Kim was groaning in bed from the lingering effects of her journey from Taegu (大邱), while Miss Ch'a was busy with preparations for her departure to America (米洲) and nursing the sick... The night I left, when I visited Sinjamno, the last person I saw was Miss Yi, whom I platonically admired, Miss Im who was ill, and Miss Ha, a Chinese person... That day I visited Reverend S's house. I walked past his house, though I'm not sure why. Just then, I met him strolling outside. He said, "When you go to Tokyo (東京), please visit the YMCA there, find Reverend O, give him my regards, and also, please be sure to tell him that my child (兒) is leaving for America (米洲) on the twenty-third..." Hearing that I was going to Japan (日本), for some reason, he seemed to instantly assume someone was sending me to study in Tokyo. Anyway, what kind of person is Reverend O?... When I was a middle school student, coming back from my hometown, I remember feeling quite homesick. But<32> until this time, I had never felt such an intense, incessant, deeply, almost pathologically unbearable longing for the past—the recent past. Countless recollections and imaginations, imaginations and recollections, chased each other's tails like a game of tag. Joy and sorrow were the illuminations shining upon it. I came to connect thoughts that had stopped. Thoughts began to fragment...

The "Bride" Greeting the New Day
Morning sunlight streaming through the gaps in the board wall (板墻)! It felt as refreshing, cool, and smooth as if I had taken a sweat-inducing remedy for a cold and sweat profusely, washing away all the disgusting grime that had seeped into every nook and cranny of my body, leaving only a clean mass of flesh. The warmed blanket was wrapped tightly around my body. Looking at anyone's face, I felt indifferent. The morning sunbeams shooting like golden arrows through the gaps in the board wall poured a new spirit (靈) into the "Bride" who greeted the new day.
If this is the case, perhaps it is precisely when a person enters "Hell" (地獄) that they return to their inherent humanity. Conversely, when ascending to "Heaven" (天堂), inhumanity will reach its zenith. Thus, "Heaven" will be filled with humans possessing pride and similar inhuman qualities, while "Hell" will be filled with humans possessing humility and analogous human qualities. Just like the qualities of people living a "Heavenly Life" and those living a "Hellish Life" in this world. And while those who go straight to "Heaven" will "reign as kings" (王 노릇) for a "thousand years" (千年 동안) in the "first resurrection" (첫재 復活), "the rest of the dead" who go straight to "Hell" will wait for the "thousand years to be completed" (千年 차기를 기다리어) and then "reign as kings" forever.

Heaps of Rice in Bowls
"Wow, what a fine day! If we'd rolled the carts (*guruma*), we'd probably have earned a good bit of shit money (똥깨나 껄쌍 시프다)!" The foreman (상노), about to fold the blanket, holding its corner, stretched both arms high in the air, yawned deeply, and spoke. He hurried the old pockmarked man, Wŏngi (元伊), (or rather, the old pockmarked man) to set up the rice pot in the corner, while he himself went to the village and returned with mackerel, green onions, soy sauce, and the like. The rice was ready. They sat in a circle around the rice container, filled to the brim with firm rice, and the soup pot with chunks of fish tumbling about. Seeing this, for me, who for several years had lived acutely aware of the comparison between the amount of rice and the number of people at mealtimes, there was a feeling of abundance, abundance, even if it was just a few items, and an attitude of no worry for tomorrow even if it was all eaten today. I felt I had escaped the constrained and fussy society where one had to weigh even a fraction (分重), measure even an inch (寸袤), and measure even a small volume (合量), and entered a different society, unsparing yet leisurely generous. Truly, even if I dished out an exceptionally large amount of rice for myself, there was no one to glare disapprovingly. There was no need to go through the trouble of distributing portions supposedly fairly, like picking out this piece and that piece, saying large pieces are two each, small pieces are three each. However, this was certainly not just because food was plentiful or their senses were dull; I saw that humility (謙德) prevailed. Especially Kwidori and Wŏngi, while being urged, "Eat up, hey, eat up, hey," merely replied, "Don't worry about us, don't worry," and only picked at things like the head or tail, starting to eat freely only after others had put down their rice spoons. That day, because the *oyakata* (什長 - foreman), Tallo (姜達浩), came late from Torikai (鳥飼), they couldn't even roll the carts (*guruma* - 도로) which they expected would earn them some money.<33>

Sweat Money: 1 Won 20 Chŏn (1圓 20錢也)
"June 29th. Cloudy then Rain. Work! *Guruma* 8 times. 2 times *tonbori* (トンボリ - turnover?). Sweat (汗). 1 won 20 chŏn." This is the diary entry for that day recorded in my notebook back then. The work involved digging earth from a place about 10 *chō* (町 - approx. 1 km) away, loading it onto a cart (*gurutma*) called a *toro* (도로), rolling it along a track, dumping it to fill depressions near the station, and leveling it out – what they called *toro-osi* (도로오시 - pushing the cart). Maktong and I picked out a cart (*guruma*). However, the workers from Nakamura's (中村) crew had already taken all the good-rolling carts, leaving only the ones that didn't roll well, so all the workers from our house were complaining. We took down the box (*hako* - 木箱), turned the cart bed (車板) upside down on top of it, wiped it, oiled it, set it back up on the rails, placed the box (*hako*) on top, loaded two levers (당가래), and then, one hand on the hip, the other on the corner of the box (*hako*), we pushed towards the digging site. Moving my feet clad in straw sandals (*waraji*) forward, listening to the sound of the cart (*guruma*) rolling, feeling the vibration (顫動) of the cart body (車體) suddenly jolt my arm – ah, labor!
We loaded it up fully and set off. The first section was downhill. Following Maktong's directions, like Maktong, I climbed onto the back. Maktong yelled at me to sit down, grabbed the two corners of the box (*hako*) with both hands, twisted his body sharply to one side as if flying, and flattened himself down. The heavy cart (*guruma*) shot like an arrow through the tunnel (*tŭnneru*), barely higher than the road itself, curved, and rushed down. I sat there stunned, clutching a handful of water in each hand.

First and Second Hurdles
This thing called the tunnel (*tŭnneru*) was a hole just big enough for the cart (*toro*) to pass under a railway bridge where a small stream flowed during the long rainy season (長霖). On the accelerating cart (*toro* - 구루마), if one didn't gather their wits before reaching the hole, one's neck could get caught on the railway bridge, or the body could get jammed between the cart body (車體) and the embankment (堤綠), or a slight mistake in steering (操繰) could cause derailment and overturning at the curve, leading to collisions with carts coming down behind – it was a dangerous first hurdle (第一 雜關) where tragedies occurred. Escaping that danger, a mentally jarring first hurdle, the speed began to drop, and from there, there was an uphill section (올림바지) where one had to push with all their might. That was the exhausting second hurdle (第二 雜關).
The first cart (*guruma*) didn't seem particularly strenuous. But as the number of trips gradually increased, my strength began to dwindle. On the sixth and seventh trips, we derailed and overturned repeatedly. Once that happens, not only does the effort multiply several times, but it's unbearable to hear the resentment, and in severe cases, the reprimands (질타) and curses (慢罵), from those loading behind and those unloading ahead. Maktong blamed it on me. But Maktong must have had a change of heart, for seeing me become soft as cotton and red as fire, he didn't try to complain further. By the time we wrestled with the eighth cart (*guruma*) at the second hurdle, I was running a fever. Though I hadn't wasted my strength by eating and playing for over 20 years, I couldn't match the strength of Maktong, who had expended his strength working and eating for over 20 years.
Sweat! I sweated profusely. When I thought there was no more sweat left to come out, the thirst (口渴) became unbearable. Picking tree leaves (木葉) to make a cone-shaped cup (곡갈), catching water seeping from rock crevices to replenish the sweat, I 'rolled' eight times until the rain came and forced us to stop<34> – with two turnovers (*dombohudajŭ* - 轉覆二次). Even though I hadn't read to the point of 'wearing out the leather bindings three times' (韋編三絶) while receiving the teacher's caning (楚撻) until blisters rose like welts on my buttocks, I had read enough to wear out the cheap paper books. Only on that day, 20 years later, did I truly grasp the meaning of the phrase 'Hoeing sprouts beneath the midday sun, Sweat drips down onto the soil below the sprouts' (鋤荷에 日常昨허니 汗滴下禾土라).

Daily Earning: Eight Wooden Tallies (木牌 八個)
Lifting the cart (*guruma*) off the track and turning it upside down beside it, we shouldered the levers (당가래) and returned to the makeshift hut (假屋). Each time we dumped the earth, we received wooden tallies (木牌) from the Japanese supervisor. I gave the 8 tallies to the *oyakata*. Originally, it was 33 chŏn per cart (*guruma*), but after deducting 3 chŏn for the *oyakata* as the cost of oil for the wheels, the total of 2 won 40 chŏn was to be split evenly with Maktong. Thus, under my name in the account book (치부책), an income (入) of 1 won 20 chŏn was recorded. 1 won 20 chŏn! Truly, truly, a laborer's earnings are hard-won. Since the accounting (*kanjyo* - 간죠) was done only once every fortnight, and the cost of food (食價) I had already consumed over 4 days amounted to 2 won 80 chŏn, I naturally couldn't see any cash. It was the same for Maktong, and since it was the rainy season (梅雨期) with very few clear days (晴天), a day's earnings couldn't even cover two days' food costs, so it was the same for everyone. But that day, one could see that the low ground near the station had risen considerably higher than the previous day.
Amidst the 'music' (風樂) of raindrops drumming 'udŭrak ttukttak' on the tin roof (함석 집웅), we ate the 'evening meal' (晩餐) bought with blood and sweat. I ate an enormous amount. After eating, my whole body felt feverish and hot, yet the damp, cold evening breeze coming through the gaps in the board wall (板張) gave me goosebumps, and my limbs began to ache uncontrollably (四肢가 조변을 못하게 쑤시기를 시작하엿다). I felt sick (몸사리엇다). Ah, the ragged blanket! Try touching the bundle of a beggar passing by over there. He will surely get angry. For him, who must endure cold and hunger (寒頓) on the ground without support, what does the wealth of Mr. Min (閔某), Mr. Yun (尹某), or Mr. Yi (李某) matter? I pulled the ragged blanket tightly up to my chin and, overcome by the fever, lost consciousness.

Unable to Move a Limb
If I say I cried, I know many readers might laugh. 'Evening came and morning came—the second day' arrived. "Get up! Hey, Kwidori!" I opened my eyes at the shout waking Kwidori. I tried to get up. But it was impossible. I couldn't move my body even slightly. My entire spine (全 척추), from the tailbone (태루) to the top of the neck (응문이 끗), felt stiff and constricted (꽉꽉해 졋다). My arms and legs had become stiff (뻣뻣해졋다). All my joints were rigid (강직되엇다). Overnight, I had become a living mummy (木乃伊). Not only that, but if I tried to move even a little, there wasn't a single spot that wouldn't hurt, wherever I tried to exert force (勳할래던지). Trying to lift an arm, my shoulder hurt even before the arm moved. My hands felt suddenly heavy, so I tried to clench them. The finger joints felt like they would burst, and my palms (손ㅅ掌心) throbbed and ached. I couldn't even attempt to move my legs. Feeling so pathetic (안탓갑던지), I just burst into tears. Whether it was because of the tears, the activity of my heart, or because I had exerted myself beyond my usual effort, eventually, I became able to 'turn over' (뒤일) like an infant. After being propped up (업대엇다가) for a while, again like a small child, I managed to kneel, drag my body forward (쭉-끌어다가), and sit up. And still, I had to stand up and fold my bedding, had to stand bent over (꺼끕) at the ditch to wash my face, had to eat breakfast, and had to do the 'day labor' (날일)<35> that the *oyakata* seemed to have specially considered for my constitution. Whenever I recall the pathetic helplessness and pain of that time, my whole body tingles (자릿자릿해 져셔), and I can't sit still. Even for me, one of the 'descendants' (자손) who for countless millennia have 'obtained food until returning to the earth only by laboring throughout their lives' (終身토록 수고하여야 흙으로 돌아갈 때까지 먹을 것을 어드), if it was like this, the suffering of 'Eve' (이브) and 'Adam' (아담) on the morning of the second day after 'tilling the ground' (밧갈) must have been considerable. They must have surely felt some discontent with the 'Creator's' (造物主) ungenerous and heartless new life 'system' (制度).

Difference Between "Day Labor" and "Cart Pushing"
"June 30th. Cloudy then Rain. Day labor from 6 AM to 6 PM. 1 won 60. Interpretation." This is the diary entry for that day. "Day labor" (*naril*) referred to miscellaneous tasks other than *toro-osi*. With *toro* work, the construction contractor would estimate the maximum daily wage for a Korean laborer and the number of carts (*guruma*) an ordinary laborer could push in a day at full effort. Based on this, they would divide the money by the number of carts to set a price per cart and agree with the labor contractor (often the *oyakata*). The labor contractor would secretly deduct a so-called "head fee" (*adama dorikking* - 頭取金) and then agree on the piece rate per cart with the laborer – essentially a piece-rate wage system (「出來高」 賃銀 제도), similar to a sawyer's "per line" (*chulssik* - 줄씩) pay. However, work not suited for this system was called *naril* (날일 - day labor) by the Korean laborers. We all preferred to get *naril* because, compared to the *toro* work here—which involved surprisingly long distances, dangerous spots, mostly uphill sections, and frequent rain making the work messy, the earth heavy with water, and the rails slippery, resulting in fewer earning days and more effort for less than the cost of food—*naril* was different. If rain stopped the work, you'd get paid for half a day if it was in the morning, or a full day if in the afternoon. Moreover, it was looser work (헌그러온 일) where you could sneak breaks while avoiding the supervisor's eyes and still receive the full day's wage.

Thumb Crushed, Nail Lost
Such "day labor" came my way. Perhaps my clumsy, crawling movements caught the *oyakata*'s eye. Carrying those steel rails (鐵ㅅ갓) and hauling railroad ties (枕木) was certainly not easy, but it was more manageable than pushing the cart (*guruma*). If I had been made to push the cart again that day, who knows what kind of disgraceful scene I might have caused on the railway line.
The next day was rain. The following day, the *naril* finished in half a day. The day after that, rain. "July 4th. Clear." As the fifth cart (*guruma*) plunged downhill into the tunnel (*tŭnneru*), my thumb (拇指) got crushed. It felt like the envoy from the underworld (閻羅國 使者) grabbed the back of my head, like I 'crossed the line of death...' (死線을 넘...). Almost... I stopped working and went back to the makeshift hut (假屋). If I had had my way, I would have gone straight to the village and bought something like iodine water (沃度水) to apply, but remembering the advice from the smaller pockmarked man since the first day I arrived in Takatsuki (高槻), I just waited for the *oyakata* to buy and apply a plaster (膏藥). The smaller pockmarked man's advice was this: when he first asked if I had money, I didn't know what he meant and said no. He then told me never to show any sign of having money, even ask the *oyakata* to buy *waraji* for me, and indebt myself to the *oyakata* for even a single penny if possible, so that if things got difficult, I could just leave without paying (빼야한다는 것이엇다). After nagging and nagging, I got the plaster three days later, but it was already too late. The nail came off. That nail is surely buried under the tracks at Takatsuki (高槻) station, mingled with the soil soaked in my sweat.
The next day was also rain. "July 6th. Clear." With my injured hand wrapped up, and Kim Tŏksam unable to go to work due to a boil (腫處) on his leg, we were rolling around on the floor of the makeshift hut (假屋), talking about this and that. We reached an agreement, put on our *happi* (합비 - laborer's short blue coat with white lettering), and stealthily left Takatsuki (高槻) station, heading towards Torikai (鳥飼) village. (Continued) <36>

**Summary**

This memoir excerpt by T'aehŏ recounts his harrowing experience as a manual laborer (*nogada*), likely a Korean working in Japan around Takatsuki station in 1926. He describes the squalid living conditions in a makeshift hut with other laborers, the initial shock and physical agony of grueling cart-pushing work (*toro-osi*), the meager pay (1 won 20 chŏn for a day's work), the camaraderie and subtle hierarchies among the workers, and his eventual injury (a crushed thumb) leading to his decision to flee with another injured worker. The narrative contrasts the narrator's educated background and sensitivities with the harsh realities of physical labor and poverty. From a historical perspective, the article offers a raw, personal glimpse into the lives of Korean migrant laborers in Japan during the colonial period. Historians might find valuable insights into the piece-rate labor system (*dekidaka*), the role of the foreman (*oyakata*), the dangerous working conditions, the coping mechanisms (shared food, dark humor, eventual escape), the interactions between Korean workers, and the psychological toll on individuals thrust into such circumstances, reflecting broader themes of colonial exploitation and displacement.

第一 第二의 雜關
이 「튼네루」라고 부르는 것은 長霖통에 나물이 흘러보는 작은 개천자리 우로 철도가 지나간 다리 밋 「도로」가 겨우 통과할 만한 구멍이다. 가속도로 닷는 「도로」(구루마) 우에서 그 구멍에 당도하기 전에 정신을 챙기지 못하엿다는 철도다리에 목이 걸리던지 車體와 堤綠 사이에 몸이 끼이던지 하거나 操繰에 깟닥 실수를 하면 꾸부러지는 곳에서 탈선 전복이 되며 뒤밋쳐 나려오는 「도로」와 충돌이 되거나 하여 참극이 일어나는 위험한 제1 雜關이엇다. 그 위험은 하나 정신이 펄덕 드는 제1 雜關으로 벗어나서 속도가 떠지기를 시작하는 것에서부터는 전력을 다하여 밀다가 올라와야 하는 한 곳 올림바지가 잇섯다. 그것은 힘드는 제2 雜關이엇다.
첫재 「구루마」에는 별로 힘드는 것 갓지 안핫다. 그러나 차차 番數가 만하 갈쓰록 기력은 줄어들기를 시작하엿다. 여섯번째와 일곱번째에는 거퍼 탈선 전복을 하고야 말앗다. 한 번 그리되면 노력이 몃 갑절 더 들게 될 뿐더러 뒤에서 실꼬 오는 사람 아페서 부리우고 오는 사람들의 원성 심한 자로는 질타 慢罵를 듯게 되는 것이 질색이다. 莫童이는 그것을 나의 탓이라고 하엿다. 그러나 莫童이도 맘을 가졋는지라 솜가티 부드러워지고 불 가티 뻘개진 나를 보고는 더 불평을 말하려고 하지 안핫다. 여들번째 「구루마」와 제2 雜關에서 다툴때는 나는 열병을 알핫다. 나는 20여년을 먹고 놀아서 힘을 허비하지 안핫것만 20여년을 먹고 일해서 힘을 허비한 莫童의 힘을 당할 수 업섯다.

「날일」과 「도로오시」의 구별
『6월 30일 曇 后 雨. 날일 전 6시로 후 6시까지. 1원 60. 통역.』 이것이 그 날의 일기. 「날일」이라는 것은 「도로오시」외의 잡역인데 「도로」로 말하면 공사 청부업자가 가령 조선사람 勞働者 는 매일 최고 얼마면 사용할 수 잇다는 것과 보통 노동자로서 최선을 다 하면 하로에 몃 「구루마」나 구을릴 수 잇다는 것을 짐작하여 가지고 「구루마」수로 돈을 쪼개어셔 한 「구루마」에 얼마식 주겟다고 인부 청부업자와 相約이 되면 인부 청부업자(흔히는 「오야가다」)는 거긔서 소위 「아다마도리낑」(頭取金)이란 口文을 비밀히 제하고 인부와 한 「구루마」의 품갑슬 相約하는 즉 「出來高」 賃銀 제도 다시 말하면 톱질꾼의 「줄씩」과 가튼 것이지만. 성질이 그럿치 못한 일은 조선 사람 노동자들의 부르는 「날일」이라는 것이다. 그런데 우리들이 모도 그 「날일」 어더 하기를 원하기는 이 곳 「도로」는 거리가 놀날만하게 멀고 위태한 곳이 잇고 올림바지가 대부분인 대에 비가 자조 와서 일을 번지게 되거나 한다 하여도 흙이 물을 먹어서 무거워지고 선로가 비를 마자서 미츠러지는 까닭에 돈벌이 할 날이 적고 힘은 더 들이고도 밥갑도 못 엇지만 「날일」은 그와 달라서 일을 하다가도 비가 와서 못하게 된 때 그 때가 오전이면 半日치 오후면 온군날치들 밧게 될 뿐 아니라 틈틈이 감독의 눈을 속여 가면서 쉬이도 제 갑슬 다 바들 수 잇는 헌그러온 일인 까닭이다.

손을 치어 손톱이 빠저
그리한 「날일이 나에게로 왓다. 아마 나의 엉긔 엉긔 게는 꼴이 「오야가다」의 눈에 띄엇던 모양. 그 鐵ㅅ갓을 메어 나루며 枕木을 지어 옴기는 일이 비록 수월치는 안핫지만 「구루마」를 구을리기 보다는 용이하엿다. 그 날 만일 또 다시 「구루마」를 구을리게 되엇던 덜 線路 상에서 무슨 추태를 들어 내엇슬른지 모를 것이다.
이튼 날은 비. 다음 날은 半日 간에 「날일」이 끗낫고. 그 다음 날은 비. 『7월 4일 晴.』 다섯번재 「구루마」가 급전직하 「튼네루」로 들어가는 순간에 拇指가 치엇다. 閻羅國 使者한테 뒤통수를 집피는 듯 『死線을 넘... 』는 듯 하엿다. 하마트면... 일하기를 그만 두고 假屋으로 돌아갓다. 내의 생각만 가핫스면 이어 마을로 가서 沃度水 가튼 것이라도 사서 발랏겟지만 高槻로 간 첫날부터 작은 곰보의 충고가 잇서서 「오야가다」가 膏藥 사다 부터 주기만 기다렷다. 작은 곰보의 충고람은 처음에 나더러 돈이 잇느냐 뭇기에 무슨 의미인지 몰라서 업노라고 한즉 결코 돈 잇는 기색을 보이지 말고 「와라지」도 「오야가다」더러 사 달라고 해서 할 수 잇는 대로 한푼이라도 「오야가다」의 돈을 지고 잇다가 여차직하면 빼야한다는 것이엇다. 조르고 졸라서 사흘만에 膏藥을 어더 부릴 때는 볼서 느젓다. 손톱이 떨어졋다. 그 손톱은 확실히 高槻역 선로 밋 내 땀에 배인 흙 속에 뭇쳐 잇을 것이다.
