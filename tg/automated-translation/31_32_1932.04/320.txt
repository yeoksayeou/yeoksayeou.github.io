**Body Text Translation**
Poems of Youthful Days and 3 Others
						Han Hŭk-ku in America

Poem of Youthful Days

Oh, you!
Write down the poem of youthful days
in crimson red!
That is merely your poem,
without deception.

Writing, revising, revising, and gathering,
You are anxiously making money.
Haven't you already deceived the world
and bent your own heart!
—Though money may cover your coffin
with fragrance,
before the grave, merely
a lightless night will keep watch—.

Oh, you!
Write down the poem of youthful days
in crimson red!
Beautiful emotions!
The pursuit of passionate blood (血慹)! And disillusionment!
This is the true life of youth!
Isn't this merely the symbol of youth!

Day by day, your blood cools,
You will wander day and night,
trying to deceive the world!
At that time, with poems that are not poems,
with affection that is not affection,
and with all falsehoods,
you will try to come out before the backdrop and dance.<120>
Laughing but not laughing,
sad but not crying,
agonized but not angered,
you merely cling to the world
and carve your idols...
You will be unable to feel
any life as a poet—.

Oh, you!
Write down the poem of youthful days
in crimson red!
Even if you cannot wear the poet's crown
and lie behind a marble tombstone,
only the poem of youthful days
will remain forever—
in the young hearts of the world,
remaining forever—
burning crimson red—.
1931. 5. 18. -In Sh'ik'ago-

Niagara Falls! (Draft)

The time I heard your name
was from my elementary school geography teacher
(1 line unreadable - National Institute of Korean History)...
Now, as a solitary figure in a foreign land (異域),
I have come to stand beside your feet.
Ah! Your magnificent sound!
Your masculine face!
You are the eternal symbol of manhood.

Towards the lake,
descending, gathering, pooling, and descending,
joining, flowing, flowing, and joining,
from the high mountain slope,
through the deep valley...
Oh! Crash...
Crash, crash, crash, crash...
Refreshing crash, crash...
Ah! Your magnificent sound!
Your majestic face!

Oh, poet!
Come here!
Oh, painter!
Come here!<121>
Whether philosopher, religious figure, or musician,
whether hero or commoner,
all people moving in the world!
You come here!

You will all bow your heads,
you will all, in silence,
be unable to say any words, any descriptions.
Merely from eternity to eternity,
only the magnificent sound of Niagara
will mock and roar
past your near-sighted eyes and
below your ears hearing only five li away—.

Great! Niagara!
You are solely the masculinity of this land.
Flowing, joining, gathering
to form your body...
This truth! This precious lesson—
I have now realized it for the first time.
1931. 8. 11. —Passing "Niagara"—

The Candlelight of My Heart

The candlelight of my heart
burns at both ends (량 끝에),
Will it fail to fully brighten
this dark winter night?

However, comrade (동모여)!
Before my small light expires,
if I search, knocking on this night—
if I search, knocking on this night—.
1931. 7. 15. -In "Sh'ik'ago"—

Waking Up (Short Song)
"Cock-a-doodle-doo!" The sound of the rooster crowing,
though the dream was just a dream,
waking up and sitting on the bed,
this lonely morning, thoughts of the forgotten hometown (故鄕) arise.
1930. 8. 15. —In "Sh'ik'ago"—.<122>

**Summary**

This collection comprises four poems written by Han Hŭk-ku while in Chicago and visiting Niagara Falls between 1930 and 1931. The first poem urges young poets to write with raw, truthful passion, contrasting it with the compromises and deceptions of those seeking worldly success. The second poem marvels at the masculine power and natural grandeur of Niagara Falls, seeing it as a humbling force and a symbol of collective strength. The third uses the metaphor of a candle burning at both ends to express a sense of urgency or fleeting life. The final short song captures a moment of homesickness upon waking in a foreign land. From a historical perspective, the poems offer insight into the experiences and mindset of a Korean intellectual living abroad during the colonial period. The emphasis on youthful passion versus compromise, the awe inspired by American natural landscapes, and the underlying sense of exile and longing for home are particularly noteworthy for historians studying the Korean diaspora and artistic expression under colonial constraints.
