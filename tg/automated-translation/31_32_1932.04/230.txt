Record of Resentment Towards Doctors, A Gentleman's Sorrow
						By Kim Tongin

Dragging my aching back, afflicted with neuralgia, I once visited a certain hospital. It was a hospital called O西OO. There was a hospital run by an acquaintance of mine called O亞 Hospital (O亞醫院). That place was originally in OO-ri (OO里), but it later moved somewhere else. I didn't know where it had moved. But afterwards, while occasionally passing through XX-t'ong (XX通), I saw the O西OO hospital there. As if a crow flies just as a pear drops, the O西 hospital appeared in XX-t'ong just as the O亞 hospital moved from OO-ri.

The first character (O) was the same. The second character, at a glance, was also similar: 亞 (A) and 西 (Sŏ/West). I mistook the newly opened hospital in XX-t'ong for the O亞 Hospital. And I assumed it was my acquaintance's hospital. My visit to the hospital that day was due to this connection.

However, inside, there was a doctor I didn't know. Feeling a bit puzzled, I asked if he was the director. He said he was.

Realizing I had mistaken the place and entered the wrong building, it would have been awkward for both me and the doctor if I just turned around and left. So, having no other choice, I said I had come to be treated for an illness and sat down on a chair.

"Where does it hurt?"

"Yes. The neuralgia in my back is a bit severe..."

"I see."

After this single word of response, the doctor abruptly stood up and went into another room.

I assumed the doctor had gone out because he needed to urinate or perhaps remembered some other urgent matter. After all, the conversation between doctor and patient hadn't finished, let alone the examination, and he hadn't even asked for the patient's address or name yet...

While I was waiting alone in the consultation room for a short while, the doctor came out. He placed powdered medicine (散藥) and liquid medicine (물약) in front of me.

"That will be 1 wŏn."

I was dumbfounded. For a moment, I just stared at the doctor's face.

But, the sorrow of a gentleman—I picked up the medicine bottles, put them in my pocket, took out 1 wŏn, and gave it to him. In my heart, I was thinking,
"Thief!"...<84>

Cash 50 Chŏn

My wife had breast surgery.
It cost 30 chŏn each day for washing and changing the wick (심지). 30 chŏn wasn't a huge sum, but not knowing clearly whether this expense would last for two months or three months was an excessive burden given my financial situation. As I also had almost no income due to illness, it was difficult to continue paying this cost.

Having no choice, I decided to try another approach. I wrote a letter to a close doctor friend (親友). And I decided to send my wife to his hospital with the letter.

"She has been receiving treatment at the XX hospital until now, but since it's a large hospital, they mostly leave it to the nurses, so recovery is slow, and paying 30 chŏn daily is burdensome. Therefore, I'm asking this favor of you."

That was the gist of the letter.

However, my wife, upon returning from that hospital, immediately started nagging (박아지를 긁는다 lit. scratches the gourd dipper). Asking for the details, she said they didn't even treat her as well as the hospital she had been going to, and they demanded exactly 50 chŏn in cash right then and there.

I blushed (赤面하엿다) in front of my wife. Because when I sent her to that hospital, I had introduced the doctor as my close friend...

He probably wouldn't cheat me, but I couldn't protest, asking, "Why did you demand cash on the spot?"—Playing the gentleman (紳士) is painful.

My wife didn't go back to that hospital again.

The Doctor Who Gives No Medicine

Suffering severely from insomnia (不眠症), I knocked on the door of Dr. K.

"My greatest wish (所願) right now is just to experience, even for one day, the refreshing feeling of waking up the next morning after sleeping without taking sleeping pills (催眠劑)."

This was my appeal to Dr. K.

Dr. K nodded his head.

"Why don't you go to the mountains? Hot springs (溫泉)—that's right, hot springs in the mountains. Yangdŏk (陽德). It's good, truly good. Yangdŏk Hot Springs has beautiful scenery, it's quiet, and it would be the best for nerve-related illnesses (神經病). If you go there and rest deeply for two or three months, your insomnia will vanish without a trace." Dr. K praised Yangdŏk without saying a single word about his main profession (本業), medicine and pharmaceuticals.

"Wouldn't it be even better to take some medicine concurrently while staying at the hot springs?"

Even to this question, he replied,
"Just exercise and soak in the hot springs, that's enough. For nerve problems, there's no better medicine than moderate exercise and rest."
He persistently refused to give medicine.

I cannot forget his goodwill (好意) and kindness. I can only be grateful for his heart, which transcends profit (營利).
Medicine is a benevolent art (仁術). It should not be solely about seeking compensation (報酬).

Explanation Fee 2 Wŏn

To receive treatment for insomnia, I visited a certain hospital.
Although it occupied a corner in the busiest part of Kyŏngsŏng, the inside of the building was incredibly gloomy. It was the kind of gloomy place where you felt you might faint if you bumped into someone on its narrow, steep stairs.

The initial consultation (初診) was done by an assistant doctor (助醫). Perhaps having seen my name in print, he introduced my profession and symptoms to the head doctor (主任).

"You can't sleep?"
"No. I can't sleep at all without using sleeping pills, so I came here hoping to be treated so I can sleep without them."
"What medicine do you use?"
"Allonal or Adalin."
Thus, the questioning began.

"You write novels?"
"Yes."
"You're famous."
"I am famous."
"No worries?"
"It's the human world—doesn't everyone have worries?"
"No major worries?"
"Making a living is a major worry. Writing weakens my nerves (神經). If they get weaker, I won't be able to write. If I can't write, I won't have food."
"Edison—American Edison worked without sleeping. Two or three hours is enough."
The questioning proceeded like this.<85>

The questioning continued for 30-40 minutes. He said that duodenal worms (十二指腸虫) can sometimes cause insomnia, so I should have a stool examination tomorrow.
He told me to sleep alone in a quiet place.
He told me to empty my mind of distracting thoughts (雜念) at night.
He told me to keep my head cool and feet warm.
"It's fine to take Allonal. It's a good medicine. It's okay to take it regularly."
This is what he said about sleeping pills.

That I might have duodenal worms was new knowledge (新智識) to me. But other than that, everything else was advice I already knew and practiced—there was no particularly novel recommendation. Moreover, encouraging the use of sleeping pills to me, who wanted to be able to sleep without them, was worse than giving an irrelevant answer to a question (東問西答).

Saying there was no need to give medicine since I apparently had my own, the doctor wrote out some kind of slip for me, indicating the consultation was over.
When I gave the slip to the accounting department (會計科) downstairs, they told me to pay 2 wŏn (金 2圓也). When I asked what the 2 wŏn was for, they said it was an "explanation fee" (說明料).

Being a gentleman (紳士), I paid the 2 wŏn without another word. It was an expensive gentleman's tax (紳士稅).
Courtesans (기생) charge for their time. We writers receive payment for our writing. Lawyers receive payment for their words. And I had always thought doctors received payment for consultations and medicine. But here was a doctor who charged for his words.
"You might have duodenal worms, so let's test tomorrow."
For this single piece of new information, I had paid a consultation fee of 20 chŏn and a speaking fee of 2 wŏn.

"How Westernised."
A certain gentleman (某君), upon hearing my strange tale (奇談) that evening, remarked thus.
"In the West, if doctors recognize someone as upper class (上流人), they sometimes slap on exorbitant charges..."
If so, thanks to that doctor, I had become upper class, at least for a moment. In that case, perhaps I should have given about 20 wŏn...

Thought It Was Free, But...

On the day I was relieved of the gentleman's tax, I met an old friend (舊友), Mr. X (某君). Mr. X, with whom I attended middle school during my Tokyo (東京) days, was now a physician (醫員).

Mr. X asked about my symptoms. I explained my symptoms to Mr. X. And I also told him the story of just having been relieved of the 2 wŏn gentleman's tax.

Mr. X listened to my story, tilted his head for a moment, and then said,
"Well then, let me give you some good medicine."

In this world where people often don't give readily even when asked, for someone to offer voluntarily is truly appreciated. There, I felt anew the preciousness of friendship (友情).

Mr. X wrote a prescription (處方) and gave it to me.
"Take this medicine, take one pill at nine tonight, wait an hour, and if you still can't sleep, take one more pill. You'll definitely sleep. And if you plan to go on a trip (旅行) somewhere, I'll give you a bit more then. Come back then."

I thanked him for his kindness (好意). And we parted ways.

However, having been deceived once before, while cursing my own base thoughts (卑劣한 심사), I secretly unfolded the prescription slip (處方箋) Mr. X had written. And just as I suspected, beside the name of some medicine (ten pills), a price of 80 chŏn was marked.

"Thief!"
I barely managed to swallow these words that had reached my lips. While I, who had hoped for the medicine free of charge (無代), was also shameless, the friend who volunteered to give medicine I hadn't even asked for, and then put a price on it, was also an interesting fellow.

It's a frightening world.
Being a gentleman (紳士), forced to swallow tears and eat mustard (울며 겨자먹기로 - idiom meaning doing something unwillingly), is truly a painful class (階級).

Treated Like a Mental Patient

Once again, seeking treatment methods for insomnia, I went to the affiliated hospital of the highest educational institution in Chosŏn (朝鮮 最高學部의 附屬病院).

When I knocked on the door of the Neurology Department (神經科), students conducted the preliminary examination (豫診).
Since the Neurology and Psychiatry Departments (精神科) were combined, regardless of whether the patient belonged to Neurology or not, they performed a full preliminary examination including psychiatric questions.

Are your parents alive? If deceased, what illness did they die from? What is your financial status?<86> Have you ever suffered a serious illness (重病)? What is the age difference with your wife, and how many times a week do you have sexual intercourse (性交)? What school did you graduate from, and what were your grades like? Do you drink alcohol? Do you smoke tobacco? Were there any heavy drinkers (大酒家) among your parents or ancestors? Do you have children, and are they all healthy (健全)? They asked all sorts of questions.

The person conducting the preliminary examination was a student (실습생 - trainee/intern), so when asking questions like "How many times a week do you have sexual intercourse?", he himself snickered first, losing his own dignity. Then another student who was there would advise him,
"You must ask with a solemn attitude; it's not right to laugh like that yourself."

After the question-and-answer session ended, I lay down on the examination table (診察臺). About ten students (who were in the room) began to handle my body. One student poked my stomach. Another student poked my feet. They also ran their hands up and down my legs. They looked inside my mouth. They shone a light and examined my eyes, turning the lids.

Consulting with each other, and opening textbooks when they found something unusual, they rolled this large guinea pig (몰못트 - marmot/test subject) this way and that.

Ignoring the Prescription Slip

After the preliminary examination finished, Professor △'s formal consultation began.

"You can't sleep without medicine?"
"No. If I don't take medicine for three or four days, I can't sleep at all."
"What kind of sleeping pills do you use, and how much?"
"I first take about 1 gram of Adalin, wait for about an hour, and if I can't fall asleep, then I just take some by hand, guessing the amount, and take more and more until I can fall asleep."
Professor △ cast a look of astonishment (驚異) at my reckless (란폭한) method of using medicine.

"Using medicine so irregularly is why you've reached the point where you can't sleep without it now. Generally, sleeping pills are all potent drugs (劇藥), and your environment, where you can freely purchase such drugs, has created your current condition."

When I asked what he thought about hiding away in some remote hot spring resort where I wouldn't have sleeping pills and couldn't buy them, the professor tilted his head.

"That might be one strategy. But could a person with insomnia as severe as yours stay in such a place? After not sleeping for 4 or 5 days, wouldn't you be so tormented that you'd just run back? In my opinion, I don't think you could endure it."
As expected of an expert (專問), his words were all understandable (首肯).

"Receive my treatment. Throw away all the sleeping pills you have, use the sleeping pills I prescribe, and follow my treatment. Get a Brocal injection (브로카ㄹ 주사) daily, and I'll also give you pills (丸藥). Take one of those pills after each meal. And I will prescribe sleeping pills for you. Take that single-dose medicine tonight and sleep. Tomorrow, get the injection and take the pills again, but do not use the sleeping medicine. Plan on not sleeping for one night and definitely do not use the sleeping medicine. You might sleep a little due to the sedatives taken during the day, but even if you can't sleep, do not use the sleeping medicine. After passing several days like this, you will eventually be able to sleep well even on days without using sleeping pills. Once you can sleep sufficiently without sleeping pills, then skip the sleeping pills every other day. You must fix this gradually (漸進的); trying to fix it suddenly is likely to only increase your distress (苦悶)."

I nodded my head several times. If they charged an "explanation fee" here like at that other hospital (某病院)—and if that hospital's "explanation fee" was 2 wŏn—then this explanation was worth 2,000 wŏn.

Since my current circumstances didn't allow for continuous treatment, I decided to receive Professor △'s treatment after my personal affairs were settled. With the prescription slip (處方箋) the professor gave me still in my pocket, I left the hospital without stopping by the pharmacy (藥局).

Thinking about it, this hospital's consultation ticket (診察券) cost of 25 chŏn seemed too cheap for an "explanation fee." Perhaps it was offset by the fee for being a guinea pig (몰못트代).
When I was relieved of the gentleman's tax at that other hospital, I left with a dignified posture. But leaving this hospital, I looked back over my shoulder and left as if fleeing. Playing the gentleman is also difficult.
(February 11th)<87>

**Summary**

The author, Kim Tongin, recounts several frustrating and costly encounters with doctors in Kyŏngsŏng (colonial Seoul) while seeking treatment for neuralgia and severe insomnia. He describes doctors who seem primarily interested in money, prescribing unnecessary or overpriced medicine, charging exorbitant "explanation fees," or failing to provide effective care. He contrasts these experiences with a seemingly more benevolent doctor who advises against medication for insomnia and a very thorough, albeit impersonal and somewhat dehumanizing, examination by student doctors and a professor at a major university hospital. Throughout the anecdotes, Kim highlights the "gentleman's sorrow"—the social pressure to maintain composure and pay fees without complaint, even when feeling cheated or poorly treated, making the experience of seeking medical help particularly agonizing.

For historians, this article offers a valuable first-person perspective on the medical system and patient experiences in 1930s colonial Korea. It sheds light on the perceived commercialization of medicine, the economic struggles faced even by intellectuals like Kim, the social dynamics between doctors and patients (including the concept of the "gentleman's tax"), the contrast between private clinics and institutional hospitals (like the one likely attached to Keijō Imperial University), and the state of treatment for conditions like neuralgia and insomnia at the time. The detailed description of the university hospital examination, including invasive questions and being treated like a "guinea pig" by students, provides insight into medical training and practices.

醫師 怨妄記, 紳士의 悲哀
						金東仁
신경통의 아픈 허리를 끄을고 어떤 병원을 찾아간 일이 잇엇다. O西OO이라는 병원이엇다. O亞醫院이라는 나의 知人의 병원이 잇엇다. 그 집이 본시는 OO里에 잇엇는데, 뒤에 다른 곳으로 이사를 갓다. 어디로 갓는지는 나도 몰랏다. 그런데 그 뒤 간간 XX通을 지나다닐 때에, 거기서 O西OO이라는 병원을 보앗다. 가마귀 날자 배 떠러 진다고 O亞의원이 OO里에서 이사하자 XX通에 O西의원이 생긴 것이엇다.
웃 字는 같은 자엇다. 아렛 자도 얼는 보아서는 비슷한 亞자와 西자엇다. 나는 XX通에 새로 생긴 병원을 O亞의원으로 알엇다. 그러고 나의 知人의 병원이거니 하고 잇엇다. 이날 그 병원에 찾아간 것도 그런 관게상이엇다.
그랫더니, 거기는 아지 못할 의사가 잇엇다. 좀 의아한 나는 당신이 원장이냐고 물엇다. 그랫더니 그러타고 한다.
집을 헛 알고 잘못 들어왓노라고 하고 도로 나오자니, 나도 무안하고 의사도 무안한 노릇이라, 할 일 없이, 병을 뵈려 왓노라고 하고 의자에 걸터 앉엇다.
「어디가 아프세요?」
「네. 허리에 신경통이 좀 과해서...」
「네」
이 한 마디의 응답이 잇은 뒤에 의사는 벌떡 일어서서 다른 방으로 가 버렷다.
나는 의사가 小便이나 그러치 않으면 다른 급한 일이 생각나서 나갓거니 하고 잇엇다. 의사와 환자로서의 문답이 아직 끝나지 않엇으며 진찰은 커녕 환자의 주소 성명도 아직 묻지 않엇아니깐...

處方箋을 黙殺
豫診이 끝난 뒤에 △敎授의 정식 진찰이 시작되엇다.
「藥을 안 먹으면 자지를 못하오?」
「네. 사흘이고 나흘이고 藥을 안 먹으면 자지를 못하오.」

△교수는 이 란폭한 나의 藥使用法에 驚異의 눈을 던젓다.
「그러케 규측없이 약을 쓰니깐 지금같이 藥 없이는 잠을 못자게 됏소. 대체 催眠藥이란 다 劇藥인데 그런 약을 자유로 購入할 수 잇는 당신의 환경이 오늘날 당신의 증세를 낳엇소.」
혹은 催眠劑를 가지지 않고 催眠劑를 購入할 수 없는 궁벽한 溫泉場에라도 가서 숨으면 어떠켓느냐고 물으매 교수는 머리를 기우렷다.
「그것도 혹은 한 방책이 될 지는 모르겟소. 그러치만 당신만치 强度의 不眠症의 사람이 그런데 가 잇을 수가 잇겟소? 4,5日間을 잠을 못 자 놓으면 너무 괴로워서 도로 뛰처 돌아오게 되지 않겟소? 내 생각 같아서는 당신이 견디지 못할 것 갓소.」
專問인 만치 그의 말은 모도 首肯를 할만 하엿다.
「내 치료를 받으시오. 당신이 가지고 잇는 催眠劑는 죄 버리고 내가 處方해 주는 催眠劑를 쓰며 내 치료를 받으시오. 매일 주사를 브로카ㄹ 주사를 맞으며 또 丸藥을 드릴께. 그 환약을 매 식후마다 한 알식 먹으시오. 그러고 催眠劑를 내 處方해서 드릴께. 그 돈복약을 오늘 밤에 먹고 즈므시오. 내일은 역시 주사를 맞고 丸藥을 먹지만 최면약은 쓰지 마시오. 하롯밤은 자지를 않을 예산을 잡고 결코 최면약은 쓰지 마시오. 그래도 낮에 쓴 진정제 때문에 조금은 자게 되겟지만 못 잔다 할지라도 최면제는 쓰지 마오. 이러케 며츨을 지나노라면 최면제를 안 쓰는 날도 넉넉히 잘 수가 잇게 되겟소. 최면제를 안 써도 넉넉히 자게 되면 그 뒤에는 최면제를 이틀씩 건너 쓰시오. 이러케 漸進的으로 곤처야지 갑작이 곤치려다는 외려 苦悶만 느리게 되기가 쉽소.」
나는 몇 번을 머리를 그덕엿다. 여기서도 某病院과 같이 說明料를 받는다면-그러고 某病院의 「說明料」가 2圓이라면 이 說明料는 2,000圓의 價値가 잇엇다.
시재는 게속적으로 치료를 받지 못할 형세에 잇는 나는 私事가 끝난 뒤에 △교수의 치료를 받기로 마음먹고 교수가 주는 處方箋을 주머니에 넣은 채로 藥局에는 들리지 않고 그 병원을 나왓다.
그러다 보니 이 병원의 진찰권 25錢은 「說明料」로는 너무 싸다. 몰못트代와 相殺가 되는 모양이다.
某病院에서 紳士稅를 빼앗긴 때는 그 병원을 나설 때는 당당한 태도로 나섯다. 그러나 이 병원을 나설 때는 뒤를 돌아보며 도망하듯이 나섯다. 紳士 노릇도 하기 어렵다.
