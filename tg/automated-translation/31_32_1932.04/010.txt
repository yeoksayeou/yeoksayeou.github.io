Magazine Title: 동광 제32호
Publication Date: 1932년 04월 01일
Article Title: 봄비 (Spring Rain)
Author: 安基永(曲), 朱耀翰(歌) (An Kiyŏng (Music), Chu Yohan (Lyrics))
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

1. Spring rain with wind blowing,
Fluttering like threads.
Though it sprinkles all day long,
It shows no sign of stopping.
The old night will wear new clothes,
So come as much as you please.

2. To the thirsty branches,
So that sweet sap may rise,
Sprinkle to your heart's content.
Permeate and enter, please.
From the roots that were dry,
New sprouts seem likely to emerge.

3. Frogs, wake from your sleep!
Pussy willows, you come too!
Butterflies and honeybees,
All kinds of creatures, come out!
Sweet spring rain comes to Chosŏn,
Shall we go out to meet it? <5>

**Summary**

This poem, titled "Spring Rain," celebrates the arrival of revitalizing spring rain. It depicts the rain nourishing thirsty plants, awakening dormant creatures, and bringing new life after a period of dryness ("old night," "dry roots"). The poem explicitly welcomes the rain to "Chosŏn" (Korea) and calls for all creatures to come out and greet it, suggesting a theme of renewal and collective awakening. From a historical perspective, the poem's emphasis on awakening, renewal ("new clothes," "new sprouts"), and the specific mention of "Chosŏn" during the Japanese colonial period could be interpreted as a subtle expression of hope for national revitalization and an end to a period of hardship, much like nature's revival in spring. Historians might find the blend of natural imagery with potential nationalistic undertones noteworthy for understanding cultural expression under colonial rule.

봄비
						安基永(曲), 朱耀翰(歌)
1. 봄비에 바람치어
실같이 휘날린다.
종일두고 뿌리어도
그칠줄 모르노네.
묵은 밤 새옷 입으리니
오실대로 오시라.

2. 목말은 가지가지
단물이 오르도록
마음것 뿌리소서.
스미어 들으소서.
말랏든 뿌리에서도
새싹 날까 합니다.

3. 개구리 잠깨어라.
버들개지 너도 오라.
나비도 꿀벌도
온갓 생물 다 나오라.
단 봄비 조선에 오나니
마중하러 갈거나.＜5＞
