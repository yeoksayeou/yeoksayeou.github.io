Song of the Farmer
						By Hŭrima, Translated by Kim Ansŏ

I am a boy, become twenty years old,
Having lost my mother as a suckling babe,
Until now, on beans, young dreams, and foolishness,
I am one who has grown up unknown to others.

I cannot remember my childhood,
Like a slave, this body suffered in hard labor,
Life was only harsh to my body.
As time went by, my body was pressed down by hard labor.

I must have been nine, with my own hands, this young me
Came to seek tasteless food.
And then, holding a whip in hand,
Driving a black ox, I farmed.

Wandering the whole field, alone, all by myself,
I worked driving two oxen.
Barefoot, I walked the muddy furrows,
Plowing the black earth, ceaselessly plowing the black earth.

When evening came, in the rough bosom of the Earth,
I slept peacefully.
All the celestial bodies with frowning faces
And the shining stars seemed to mock this body.

There was no joy, no happiness for this body,
No joyous holidays of celebration.
In this poverty and misery, without sunlight,
This body lived alone like a dog.

From the age of nine, I cursed the world—
The landlord (田畓主人), fat as a pig,
Ah, how I trembled in fear at his command!
This life quickly became poison to my body.

In the cold winter, this body slept upon the snow,
In the warm spring, I passed time on the rough fields.
The sleeping place on summer nights injured my back,
On the unevenly high and low earth and stones.

By cold and ice, I suffered as much as one could suffer,
And so now, this body knows neither heat nor cold.
Anger fills every joint of my bones,
This flesh is stone, and the bones are steel.<126>

These resilient muscle fibers
Were tempered by the gentle breeze in the dead of night,
And my will hardened in rough fate,
To support this body, there is only the strength of this world.

The wide, rough fields are my mother,
My father is the solemn mountains.
My home is this world, everywhere,
This body is a universal being (宇宙人), a ceaseless wanderer (漂泊者).

In the dim evening, lying on the field and looking up at the sky,
How welcome, the whistle the shepherd boy blows!
Ah, my heart is seized by boundless emotion,
By the shepherd boy’s thin, beautiful whistle.

When the whistle's ecstatic melody
Drifts through the quiet valley as if sleeping,
This body feels a mystery in the emotion,
In the softest, dream-like song, I lose myself.

Only at this time, the stars forget to mock,
The cows and sheep eat grass peacefully,
And the sky too, in the leaves of the ecstatic song,
Seems to shine joyfully with each phrase.

The spirit flies freely about,
Over the valley, over the quietly resting land.
When this body is caught in a soft, ecstatic sleep,
The peaceful mind and body just fall silent.

Then, waking from sleep, even tasteless food
Is sweet to this body. And the green grass and trees of the valley
Appear ecstatic. But why is it
That I cannot live on this as a free man?

Driving the oxen like this, I
Will wander from this furrow to that under the sunlight,
Until this body, embracing its final suffering (苦惱),
Returns to the grave of the black Earth.

I am a boy, become twenty years old,
Having lost my mother as a suckling babe,
Until now, on beans, young dreams, and foolishness,
Namelessly grown, I am this person.<127>

**Summary**

The poem "Song of the Farmer," written by "Hŭrima" and translated by Kim Ansŏ, depicts the bleak and laborious life of a twenty-year-old farmer who lost his mother in infancy. He recounts his childhood spent in slave-like toil from the age of nine, enduring poverty, hunger, harsh weather, and the oppression of a landlord. His body has become hardened like stone and steel, finding kinship only with nature. Brief moments of solace and transcendence arrive through the sound of a shepherd boy's whistle in the evening, offering a temporary escape into peace and beauty. However, this respite highlights his lack of freedom, and he resigns himself to a life of unending labor until death.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the poem, though possibly a translation, reflects the widespread hardship and suffering of the rural population under Japanese rule. The farmer's sense of powerlessness, relentless toil, and alienation, along with the mention of an oppressive landlord figure, could resonate with the economic exploitation and social conditions faced by many Koreans during this period. Historians might find the poem's portrayal of rural despair, the longing for freedom, and the specific vocabulary used (reflecting 1930s Korean) of interest for understanding the social consciousness and cultural expression of the time. The choice to translate and publish such a work suggests an implicit commentary on the Korean situation.

農夫의 노래
						흐리마(作), 金岸曙(譯)
나는 少年, 스므살 되엇노라,
젓먹이로 어머니를 잃어버리고
지금까지 콩알과 어린 夢想과 어리석음에서
남모르게 자라난 사람이로라.

나는 어린 때를 記憶치 못하노라,
奴隷처럼 이 몸은 苦役에 시달렷거니,
生命이 내 몸에는 苛酷햇을 뿐.
갈사록 苦役에 내 몸은 업눌렷섯노라.

아홉 살이엇으리, 내 손으로, 어린 나는
맛없는 밥을 求케 된 것은.
그러고 채찍을 손에 잡고서
검은 소를 몰며 나는 耕作햇노라.

왼들을 헤매돌며, 외로이 외나마나로
소 두 마리를 몰고 일햇노라.
맨발로 질컥거리는 밭이랑을 걸어단녓노라,
검은 땅을 갈면서, 쉬지 않고 검은 땅을 갈면서.

저녁이 되어 大地의 거츨은 품에서
나는 平和로이 잠을 잣노라.
얼골을 쯩그린 모든 天體와
빛나는 별들은 이 몸을 비웃는 듯 햇거니.

내 몸엔 기쁨도 없고 幸福도 없엇노라,
즐겁은 祝賀의 名日도 없섯노라.
가난과 悲慘, 日光없는 이 속에서
이 몸은 홀로 개와 같이 살앗노라.

아홉 살 때부터 나는 세상을 辱햇노라―
도야지처럼 살진 田畓主人,
아아 나는 얼마나 그 號令에 무서워 떨엇든고!
이 生은 어느 덧 내 몸에 毒藥이 되엇거니.

추운 겨울엔 이 몸은 눈 우에서 잠을 자고
다사한 봄철에는 거츤 들 우에서 지냇노라.
여름밤의 잠자리 내 등을 傷햇노라,
울툭불툭 높나즌 흙과 돌에.

치위와 얼음에 나는 시달릴대로 시달렷노라,
그리하야 지금 이 몸은 더위와 치위를 모르노라.
憤怒가 뼈다귀 마듸마듸에 찻을 뿐,
이 肉體는 돌이요, 뼈다귀는 鋼鐵이로라.＜126＞
彈力잇는 이 筋肉의 纖維를
아닌 밤 중의 微風이 이겨주엇고
나의 意志는 거츨은 運命에 굳어젓나니,
이 몸을 支持할건, 오직 이 세계의 힘이 잇을 뿐.

넓고 거츨은 들들은 나의 어머니,
나의 아버지는 崇嚴한 山들이로라.
나의 집은 이 세계, 곳곳마다요,
이 몸은 宇宙人, 끈임없는 漂泊者로다.

어득한 저녁, 들 우에 누어 하늘을 우르려면
반갑고나, 牧童의 부는 추파람!
아아 나의 맘은 限없는 感動에 잡히노라,
牧童의 가늘고 아름다운 추파람의.

추파람의 恍惚한 曲調가
자는 듯 고요한 골작이에 떠돌제,
이 몸은 感動에 神秘를 느끼노라,
가장가같은 보드랍은 노래에 나는 나를 일노라.

이 때만은 별들도 비웃기를 잊어버리고
소와 羊은 平和스러이 꼴을 먹으며,
하늘도 恍惚한 노래의 잎파리의
가락가락에 기쁜 듯이 빛나는 것을.

精神은 自由로이 날아 돌나니,
골작이 우를, 고요히 安息하는 땅 우를.
보다러운 恍惚의 잠에 이 몸이 붓들리면
平和로운 맘과 몸은 그대로 잠잠코 마는 것을

그러다가 잠이 깨면 맛없는 밥조차
이 몸엔 단 것을. 그러고 골작이의 프른 草木도
恍惚이 보이는 것을. 그러나 어인 일가
나는 이 우에서 自由롭은 사람으로 살 수 없노라

이라 외나 소를 몰으며, 나는
해볓 아레서 이 이랑 저 이랑을 헤매들오리,
이 몸이 마즈작 苦惱를 안은 채로
검은 大地의 무덤속으로 돌기까지는.

나는 少年, 스므살 되엇노라,
젓먹이로 어머니를 잃어버리고
지금까지 콩알과 어린 夢想과 어리석음에서
남모르게 자라난 사람이로라.＜127＞
