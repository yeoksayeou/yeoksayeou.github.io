Man Who Died at Sea and 1 Other Poem
						Min Pyŏnghwi

Man Who Died at Sea

Because the fish weren't biting,
they laid us off for over a month.
Whether we starved or froze,
how could they care when the loss was only ours!

But today they put us back on the ship,
on a worn-out wreck unused for two years.
The Maritime Affairs Bureau inspector in his stiff Western suit
laughs and chats with the company owner, puffing on Haet'ae brand cigarettes.
The insurance company clerk also declared it safe,
and the third-class engineer also climbed aboard.

The steam whistle blows!
We, bearing a dark fate on our backs, gaze out at the sea
from the deck.
A seagull falls dead onto the forecastle deck (?).
An ill omen! Fear of death!
The sailors fall into nihilistic self-forgetfulness.
Old ship! Wind! Disaster! Shipwreck!
All these ill omens come to mind.
Sitting on watch, they just stare at the sea.
The sea is dark, and stars twinkle in the sky.
The engine screams a wretched sound, and the wind sweeps across the deck.

Night! Rough waves surge on the dark sea!
From the watch, the cry "Where are we?" struck the ears of those off-duty! Their hearts sink.
The rolling grows worse following the rotation of the engine's crank!
Three feet of seawater on deck—the lighthouse flickers—<123>
The sailors have turned pale with terror.

"Pump the bilge (漏水)!" shouts the young second engineer.
Sailors grabbing the Washington pump (?),
Sailors watching the gauge (壓力計),
The crowd rushing about before the furnace (爐口).
The sound of the bilge water going out!
The crowd, unwilling to die, rushes about!
But isn't the ship's rolling only getting worse?

The sailors, like patients with fever, grind their teeth and struggle against nature.
But the pressure in the steam dome (蒸汽溜) rises,
and the safety valve is blowing off steam.
In the glass pipe of the water gauge (水位計) of the rotten boiler (뽀일러), steam fills it completely.

The water gauge pipe ruptures!
One hundred pounds (?) of steam, having found an exit, erupts.
Before the pipe, a sailor screams and collapses.
When moved to the cabin! He was already dead.
The wind blows, and the rolling worsens further.
All the comrades standing before the sailor's corpse mourn their departed comrade. Tears! Rage!

The ship meant to catch fish caught a man.
XX against the XXX who sent out an old ship unable to withstand such rough waves to test the fishing!
The Maritime Affairs Bureau official drank XX and X.
And thus helped conceal the sailor's X.

The sailors couldn't catch fish; they brought back their dead comrade on the broken ship.
The sailors who fight the sea went to the company.
To the dead sailor came the president's condolence money of 2 Won.

In the house of the man who died at sea, XX against XX was organized.
The sailor's funeral! The procession came before the company gate.
Shouts of furious indignation! Tears! XX stains the fishing village of X
with a grim atmosphere.
—December 1931—<124>

Seeing My Wife Cry -On the Day I Was Taken Away-

Wife, did you not cry that day!
Did you cry in sorrow because I was defeated in the struggle!
When, entangled by X, I boarded the train
with my comrades, I saw your tears.

You who vowed to me
to become a strong woman in daily life, who didn't spare yourself in work,
you who were fearless—why on that day alone
were there tears?

Did you cry because you were sad to leave your embrace?
Or did the tears well up in the eyes of a strong woman, our comrade,
out of anger that I was caught, defeated in the struggle?
I do not know why you cried.

If you cried out of sentimentality
at parting from your husband leaving your embrace,
Ah, I shall be sad! I shall be angry!
You, who were never like that in daily life, why did you cry on that day alone?

My wife, surely you must have cried out of anger.
Surely you must have cried out of anger that our strong comrade, our leader,
was caught and taken away like that!
But we were saddened, seeing the traces of tears on your weak face.

Why did you shed tears that day, tears long dry,
making our comrades going to the X of the X feel regretful?
My wife, those tears on that day, at that time, gave us despair.
How could you, our wives, shed tears at that time?
—Lunar New Year's Day, 1932— At Songdo.<125>

**Summary**

The first poem, "Man Who Died at Sea," vividly portrays the death of a sailor due to corporate negligence—sending workers out on an unsafe, old ship. It highlights the collusion between the company, an indifferent government inspector (Maritime Affairs Bureau), and an insurance clerk, culminating in a fatal boiler explosion. The poem contrasts the workers' fear and struggle for survival with the callousness of the owners, who offer a paltry sum in condolence money, leading to organized protest and collective grief among the sailors ("마도로쓰"). The second poem, "Seeing My Wife Cry," reflects the speaker's complex emotions upon being arrested and taken away (likely for labor activism or political reasons, given the context and censored parts). He questions whether his wife's tears stem from personal sadness (sentimentality) or righteous anger at the injustice of his capture, expressing disappointment if it's the former, as he expects strength and political solidarity even in hardship.

From a historical perspective, these poems are significant for their raw depiction of labor conditions and exploitation under Japanese colonial rule in the early 1930s. They showcase the dangerous working environments, the perceived corruption or indifference of authorities (海事局 官吏), the power imbalance between capital (회사 주인, 사장) and labor (마도로쓰, 동무), and the nascent forms of worker solidarity and protest (XX에 대한 XX가 조직되엇다, 葬式 行列, 激憤의 高喊). The use of the term "동무" (comrade) and the context of arrest suggest involvement in socialist or nationalist labor movements, which were active but suppressed during this period. The censorship (XXX, XX) itself speaks volumes about the political climate and restrictions on expression. The second poem's focus on the expected political consciousness of women ("굳센 여성," "굳센 동무") provides insight into gender roles within activist circles of the time.
