Friend
						Kim Yŏ-je

Friend, if you are for Chosŏn,
If you truly love Chosŏn,
First wash your hands.
Those hands of yours,
That applied the powder of pretense,
That held the cup of lewdness,
Wash them first.
With those dirty, foul-smelling hands,
Do you intend to lay the foundation of Chosŏn?

Friend, if you are for Chosŏn,
If you truly love Chosŏn,
First break your mouth.
That mouth of yours,
That slandered brothers,
That praised fame and gain,
Break it first.
With that dirty, foul-smelling mouth,
Do you intend to shout the name of Chosŏn?

Friend, if you are for Chosŏn,
If you truly love Chosŏn,
First mend your heart.
That heart of yours,
Instigating and weak,
Vile and corrupt,
Mend it first.
With that dirty, foul-smelling heart,
Do you intend to become a worker for Chosŏn?
...From an old manuscript...<1>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Yŏ-je is a stark call for self-reflection and moral purification directed at fellow Koreans who claim to love and work for Chosŏn (Korea). The author argues that genuine patriotism requires inner cleansing: washing hands dirtied by pretense and vice, silencing mouths used for slander and greed, and mending hearts filled with weakness and corruption. Only through such personal reform can one truly contribute to building the nation. For historians, this piece offers insight into the internal critiques and moral discourse within Korean society during the colonial period. It reflects anxieties about hypocrisy and moral failings potentially hindering the nationalist cause, suggesting a focus on character and integrity as prerequisites for national service.

친구여 그대가 조선을 위하거든
진정으로 조선을 사랑하거든
먼저 그대의 손을 씨스라
가식의 분을 바르고
음탕의 잔을 들던
그대의 손부터 씨스라
더럽고 냄새나는 그 손가지고
조선의 긔초를 세우려 하나뇨

친구여 그대가 조선을 위하거든
진정으로 조선을 사랑하거든
먼저 그대의 입을 부쉬라
형제를 훼방하고
명리를 찬송하던
그대의 입부터 부쉬라
더럽고 냄새나는 그 입 가지고
조선의 일홈을 웨치려 하나뇨

친구여 그대가 조선을 위하거든
진정으로 조선을 사랑하거든
먼저 그대의 마음을 고치라
교사하고 약하고
비루하고 부패한
그대의 마음부터 고치라
더럽고 냄새나는 그 마음 가지고
조선의 일군이 되려 하나뇨
