Random Critiques of Magazine Reporters
						Kim Man (金萬)
A magazine is a commodity. To put it nicely, even among commodities, it has its own life, which is its assertion, but it cannot preserve that life unless it keenly captures the curiosity and interest of its readers according to the times. Not only does the difficulty of magazine management lie here, but it is also why many magazines collapse after only three issues.

The business department, of course, must have business acumen. However, the editorial department must skillfully capture the readers' hearts without compromising its own assertions. This is where the reporter's intelligence, foresight, and wit must come into play. Just as one changes bait according to the season for fishing, how could a magazine reporter lack the skill to subtly guide the readers' minds? When you think about it, there is perhaps no job more difficult than that of a magazine reporter.

Moreover, in a place like Korea (朝鮮), there are difficulties with censorship (檢閱難), difficulties securing manuscripts (原稿難), and even difficulties with readers (讀者難). Therefore, no matter how renowned a reporter may be, they cannot fully display their skills. Although general curiosity might demand the publication of certain articles, censorship often forces their cancellation. Furthermore, for certain specialized articles, finding a suitable writer is impossible, which is truly pathetic. And because the general readership's level of education is low, there is no choice but to publish articles that cater to their interests, even if they are lowbrow. How, then, can a reporter fully demonstrate their skills? The fact that Korean magazines inevitably appear inferior compared to foreign magazines is precisely due to this point. Moreover, the reason why talented reporters fail to fully develop and wither away also lies here.

Despite these three difficulties, we get a glimpse of the reporters who diligently strive and agonize for the future of this field. While it may not compare to the taste of ice cream on a hot day, it surely won't hurt to look.

Pang Chŏng-hwan (方定煥)
In the editorial office on the second floor of Kaebyŏk-sa (開闢社), which could be called Korea's magazine kingdom, he sits alone like a polar bear in the Arctic, chain-smoking cigarettes. Day after day, he sweats over the table of contents for each issue of "Hyŏsŏng" (혜성), "Sin Yŏsŏng" (신여성), "Pyŏlgŏn'gon" (別乾坤), and "Ŏrini" (어린이). While one might view him as the Prime Minister of the Kaebyŏk magazine kingdom, his stout build and diligence evoke the image of a "hard-working bear." This impression is likely not unique to the author. Mr. Pang could be called a jack-of-all-trades, but in reality, that's not quite right. If he were forced into a single specialty, as is inevitably required despite appearing to run a one-man general store, there isn't one specific thing one could point to. If there is one, it would be children's stories, which both he and others acknowledge. However, as the author has not seen his original creative works in this genre, it is difficult to place him at the forefront of the children's literature scene (童話壇). Nevertheless, the author holds Mr. Pang, who works steadily, calmly, and passionately, in high regard and deeply respects him.
The consistent monthly publication of the Kaebyŏk kingdom's magazines, putting aside special cases like "Pyŏlgŏn'gon," with such substantial content for readers, is certainly not without the efforts of others. However, it is primarily the result of Mr. Pang's bear-like diligence, knowing only hard work.
Therefore, while his editing may lack dazzling novelty, there is nothing in it that would cause indigestion. If steady work is the foundation of success, then a considerable brightness awaits the future of that kingdom.
(How ephemeral the ways of the world (世相) are. Mr. Pang Chŏng-hwan passed away (別世) after I wrote this piece.)

Ch'a Sang-ch'an (車相讚)
Wielding his sharp pen name under various pseudonyms like Ch'ŏng-o (靑吾), Kwansangja (觀相者), or Ch'a Ch'ŏn-ja (車賤者), Mr. Ch'a is truly a standout figure in the Kaebyŏk-sa magazine kingdom. Had his wit not assisted Prime Minister Pang Chŏng-hwan, the miscellaneous editing of the Kaebyŏk kingdom's magazines might have lacked novel and curious content.
His prominent forehead, becoming bald, and his sharp chin characterize his face. He never breaks into a wide grin (破顔大笑), only the area around his lips moves when he smiles. Anyone can guess from his appearance that he has distinctive features. The fact that his pen <61> often becomes too sharp and offends celestial eyes is one aspect of this distinctive face, but this should rather be seen as the thorn on a fragrant (芳香) rose. Some might interpret his revealing of unknown personal histories (內歷) under pseudonyms like Kwansangja or Ch'a Ch'ŏn-ja as malicious (惡意), but it is more appropriate to view it as goodwill (善意) stemming from his distinctive character.
This January's "Pyŏlgŏn'gon" featured his biography of Hong Kyŏng-nae (洪景來傳). Behind this lies a story revealing his wit, worthy of a chuckle. Having discerned that a certain Mr. Mun would not easily provide the materials (事蹟) on Hong Kyŏng-nae he had collected over many years, Mr. Ch'a visited Mr. Mun and engaged him in a debate about Hong Kyŏng-nae's history. Mr. Mun, never suspecting he was being tricked (업히우는 줄은 생각지 아니하고), showed the sources (出處) of his materials (材料), saying, "Look, there is clear evidence (證據) in the documents (文獻) like this, how could I be wrong?" At that moment, Mr. Ch'a, saying, "Indeed, I'll note this down (記錄) and look into it again," deftly copied the essence of the information ("엑스"를 깜작같이 옴겨다까) and quickly wrote the Hong Kyŏng-nae biography. If Mr. Ch'a were an investigative reporter (訪問記者), imagine the resourceful skill he would have displayed!

Ch'ae Man-sik (蔡萬植)
Mr. Ch'ae is also a reporter for the Kaebyŏk-sa magazine kingdom. He occasionally publishes short stories (短篇), plays (戲曲), and the like, using the kingdom's monthly publications (月刊物) as his home base. However, as the author has not read any of his works, I cannot comment on them, nor is there a need to discuss such literary works (文藝物) here. Of course, seasoned veterans (百戰老將) like Pang Chŏng-hwan and Ch'a Sang-ch'an sit above him, directing (指揮) everything, which might give the impression that Mr. Ch'ae's skills as a magazine reporter have not had a chance to emerge. In reality, in some sense, it seems there is little to observe regarding his skills as a magazine reporter.
While he may have aspects of a literary man, his skills as a magazine reporter have yet to manifest. Just as he is young, Mr. Ch'ae as a magazine reporter belongs to tomorrow (明日), not today. This is by no means intended to suggest he lacks talent or ability (無才無能).
Therefore, we should not disturb Mr. Ch'ae, who is quietly growing within the Kaebyŏk kingdom, for now. Only by awaiting the promise (約束) of tomorrow will his true aspects emerge.

Yi Sŏng-hwan (李晟煥)
Opposing Kaebyŏk-sa, which styles itself as the magazine kingdom, stands a place that has raised the dazzling banner of a publishing kingdom: Haebang-sa (解放社). Although the Chŏn Chosŏn Nongmin-sa (全朝鮮農民社) and Haebang-sa have no inherent connection in terms of their nature, the dominant figure in both companies is none other than Mr. Yi. Thus, it is quite enjoyable to fully observe his skills as both a reporter and a manager here.
He changed the monthly "Chŏn Chosŏn Nongmin" (全朝鮮農民) to the thrice-monthly "Nongmin Somun" (農民所聞) and continues to publish it steadily with his bare, bright red fists clenched. On the other hand, despite having material patrons ("페트론"), he manages the magazine "Haebang" (解放) to such an extent. One can gauge his courageous fighting spirit (分闘力), and his great passion (誠熱) cannot be forgotten. He might receive criticism for not considering the means (手段) for the sake of the objective (目的), but what can be done when one, like Mr. Yi, has nothing but two fists? In any case, he is persistent. Without this persistent passion (誠熱), Haebang-sa is nothing.
The fact that "Sinsosŏl" (신소설), once a pure literary magazine, changed its clothes to become "Haebang," and couldn't help but wield sensational curiosity-driven articles focusing on eroticism and grotesqueness (에로 그로), miscellaneous gossip (雜同散異), and exposé-style writing without hesitation, allows us to glimpse Mr. Yi's determination to overcome the painful management difficulties (괴롭은 경영난). At the same time, it can be seen that he has certainly captured the interest of the general reader. However, the question of what substance lies therein remains. That is, does the so-called principle (主義) or assertion (主張) of the magazine truly lie in "Haebang"? Boldly exposing the rights and wrongs (是是非非) of an individual's private life (個人의 私生活) to the world (世上) is not entirely without its cathartic satisfaction (통쾌한 일). But beyond that, a magazine reporter should not forget (아니될 일) that there are many better paths (밟아나아갈 좋은 길) for a magazine to follow.
Indeed, there is nothing quite as satisfying as striking down (뚜드려대는) an unpleasant, detestable figure. But if one intends to crush them (뚜드려 부시고저), how much more dignified would it be to enter through the front gate (正門) openly and squarely (正正堂堂하게) and challenge (難詰) them, rather than sneaking through the back door (뒷문)? <62> The author does not intend to challenge (難詰) Mr. Yi but merely points out an aspect of his tendency to disregard the means too much for the sake of the objective.
Just as this flaw exists, so does this virtue: Mr. Yi pushes forward managing "Nongmin Somun" with his two fists, and it seems even material hardships cannot overcome his passion (誠熱). Mr. Yi tackles all adversities, not considering difficulties as difficulties, simply pushing through them with his passion alone. A day of reward will surely come before this seemingly foolish passion. (Editor's note: Mr. Yi recently severed his ties with Haebang.)

Kim Kyu-t'aek (金奎澤)
Whatever others may say, Mr. Kim is an enigma as a reporter for the publishing kingdom Haebang-sa. He inherently wants to drink as much alcohol as he pleases, but knowing he cannot work the next day without a hangover cure, he forces himself to stop drinking once his face turns crimson. However, needless to say, nine times out of ten, he drinks far beyond his limit and makes mistakes. In any case, judging by his personality, being a magazine reporter seems like a misstep (誤入) for him. Within the realm of writing and art, wielding a paintbrush (畵筆) seems like a more suitable profession for him. Seeing Mr. Kim, who seems neither rushed nor slow, the author is unconsciously reminded of the artistic style (藝術家적 風采) of bygone days. This reflects his lack of concern for present reality (現實). For someone uninterested (關心치 아니하는) in this reality to work as a magazine reporter cannot but be called a misstep (誤入).
Although the author doesn't know the details of Mr. Kim's situation and circumstances (境遇), I believe that if he were to further cultivate his skills with the paintbrush (畵筆), he would surely establish himself as a notable cartoonist (漫畫家). As a magazine reporter, his skills merely involve writing this or that under someone else's coaching (코취), so his true aspects (面目) cannot be seen. To put it briefly, his entirety lies in tomorrow (明日), not today. And if any trace of him appears in "Haebang" today, it is not Mr. Kim the magazine reporter, but rather fragments of a cartoonist (漫畫家) who has talent (소질) but has not yet found his place. However, a cartoonist and a magazine reporter are different things. (Editor's note: Mr. Kim Kyu-t'aek reportedly recently left Haebang-sa.)

Chu Yo-han (朱요한)
Mr. Chu is a curious figure who gives everyone the impression that it is surprising he writes poetry (詩作) while holding a pen in one hand and never forgetting the calculations (利害打算) of an abacus (珠板) in the other.
Seeing how he takes the magazine "Tonggwang" (東光), which possesses its own principles (主義) and assertions (主張), and spices up (藥念) its content (內容) enough to attract general readers' interest, one can readily see how he possesses a so-called commercial brain (商賣的 頭腦).
If it's true that one cannot live ignoring reality (現實), then Mr. Chu's excessively commercial brain can sufficiently compensate (補充) for his own shortcomings (缺點). However, as a poet, his shortcomings cannot be considered small. Needless to say, being a poet doesn't mean being unrealistic; rather, emotion (感情) should prevail (勝) over reason (理智). Yet, in Mr. Chu's case, this is not so; emotion seems weaker than reason. Herein lies the point where his poetry conflicts with his nature as a poet.
In any case, when Mr. Chu resumed publication of "Tonggwang," he employed an unusual tactic for recruiting readers: a 200-won prize contest for a "Tonggwang" slogan (表語募集), aiming for three benefits with one stone (一石三鳥) while also providing sufficient profit (利益) and interest (興味) to the readers. This too, undoubtedly, is the product (小産) of his clever (總明) commercial brain. If "Tonggwang" did not advocate its principles and assertions so strongly, Mr. Chu's skills as a reporter would likely have been demonstrated much more effectively.

Kim Tong-hwan (金東煥)
While working as a reporter for the Chosŏn Ilbo-sa (朝鮮日報社), specifically a society desk (社會部) reporter visiting government offices (官廳) and police stations (警察署), he started the small magazine "Samch'ŏlli" (三千里) several years ago.
It is regrettable (有感) that there are no records (記錄) of outstanding achievements regarding his skills as a society desk reporter. However, one cannot help but be newly astonished by his talent as a magazine reporter and manager (經營者).
After publishing "Samch'ŏlli" sporadically like beans sprouting in a drought, once it gained some foundation (基礎), he abruptly submitted his resignation (辭表) to the newspaper company and devoted all his passion (誠熱) single-mindedly (一意專心) to his own magazine. <63> Seeing this, one can again surmise that his time working at the newspaper company had a different purpose. Having built today's "Samch'ŏlli" from scratch (맨주먹), one certainly cannot dismiss him lightly as merely a poet of the rustic wild (野聲).
While the content of "Samch'ŏlli" is not entirely free from lowbrow sections, it effectively satisfies the preferences of the general readership. Mr. Kim is clearly a reporter who has grasped the heart (心像) of the Korean reading public, just as he grasped the core (核心) of his own poetry (詩歌). His foresight (先見) is evident in his championing of low prices (廉價) for both the magazine and standalone books (斷行本).
Mr. Kim concurrently holds all positions at Samch'ŏlli-sa – president (社長), reporter (記者), publisher (發行者), errand boy (小使), and external relations agent (外交員) – demonstrating his versatility and capability (多精多能). Struggling with poor finances, he cannot afford manuscript fees (原稿料), yet he cannot omit necessary articles. Thus, he inevitably has to write them himself (自將擊之). Setting out to gather materials (材料蒐集) with this mindset, Mr. Kim never directly asks the interviewee (被訪者) to provide material. Instead, after discussing various topics, he steers the conversation (話題) towards his required material, asking questions humbly one by one. He then mentally organizes the results (結果) obtained through this question-and-answer (問答) process and publishes them in "Samch'ŏlli." Those who know him understand how systematic his mind is.
Publishing something like "Ch'unhyangjŏn" (춘향전) in the context of modern Korean literature (近代文學) seems like a significant mistake (失手) for the otherwise clever Mr. Kim.

Yi Ŭn-sang (李殷相)
If one's profession (職業) should be chosen according to their personality (性格) and talent (才能), then poet Yi Ŭn-sang's role as a reporter for "Sinsaeng" (新生) must surely be called a misstep (誤入). While we hear that necessities like food, clothing, and shelter are predestined, how often are people forcibly drafted (强徵) into jobs they dislike due to these three inherent difficulties (難)? Mr. Yi is one such reporter experiencing this sorrow (悲哀).
Needless to say, since the magazine "Sinsaeng" has its own principles (主義) and advocacy (표방), perhaps there is no need to capture the heart (心像) of the general readership. Even so, readers who must endure the same unchanging fare day after day, 360 days a year, cannot help but suffer from the same difficulties as Mr. Yi. Monotony (單調) of the cookie-cutter variety (천편일률) surely requires a fresh song (清新한 노래) to revitalize (一新) it, yet "Sinsaeng" seems utterly oblivious to this, which is regrettable.
Although the detailed circumstances (內容) of this tendency are unknown, even if the primary responsibility (責任) lies with the publisher (주재자), poet Mr. Yi, as a magazine reporter, cannot avoid his share.
In short, Mr. Yi is someone who, if provided with food and clothing, should quietly sit in his room immersed in poetry (詩作) or research, not venture onto the bustling streets of reality as a reporter. It is no mistake (誤入) that this gentle (溫厚), sincere (誠實) poet expresses his feelings in sijo poems (詩調).
(Editor's Note: Thanks to the reviewer's concern, Mr. Yi Ŭn-sang has left Sinsaeng-sa and taken up a position as a professor (敎授) at Ewha Womans College (梨花專門).)

Kim Tong-hyŏk (金東赫)
The fact that the magazine "Chosŏn chi Kwang" (朝鮮之光) has persisted to this day despite various tribulations (波瀾) is entirely due to Mr. Kim's tenacious grip (묻고 느러진 힘). However, honestly (正直), one cannot suppress the feeling that he is not at all suited (適任) to be either a reporter or a manager. Firstly, Mr. Kim is utterly incompatible with the principles (主義) and ideology advocated by "Chosŏn chi Kwang." Secondly, despite managing it for many years, the fact that he still suffers material hardship (물적 고통) at his age indicates a lack of management skill (경영술). Thirdly, as a reporter, he has failed to capture the heart (心像) of the readership and sufficiently arouse their interest.
Considering these points, perhaps it would be better for Mr. Kim not to cling (붓잡고) to "Chosŏn chi Kwang" but to hand it over (내어 주고) to someone deemed suitable and wash his hands of it. His destined path seems to lie elsewhere (별다른 곳), not in such a magazine.

Ch'oe Sang-dŏk (崔象德)
Confining (拘束) a novelist who published modern erotic-flavored (에로味) stories in newspapers and magazines to the single magazine "Sinmin" (新民) inevitably gives the impression of caging (鳥籠) a free-flying bird.
Mr. Ch'oe is likely now forced to pick up his pen again at "Sinmin," against his will, due to the undeniable burden of making a living (生活). Furthermore, since "Sinmin"'s readership is fixed and predictable (판에 박아 놓은), there is no need to engage in reader acquisition battles (奪取戰) against other magazines. Consequently, there is no opportunity for Mr. Ch'oe to display (發揮) his talents as a reporter; indeed, it hardly matters whether he has them or not. Therefore, seeking his skills here is unreasonable.
If someone were to newly manage a magazine specializing in witty and biting writing (才筆 毒筆) with an erotic flavor, they should, above all, recruit Mr. Ch'oe and place him in the highest position. Not only does his satirical writing (漫文) overflow with wit (才致), but his occasional infusion of poison (毒藥) into eroticism (에로) would surely capture all interest in this area.
Although Mr. Ch'oe currently eats the bread of "Sinmin," he is by no means a person who will be satisfied (滿足) there; he will surely flee (다라날) whenever he gets the chance. Therefore, his skills are unlikely to be prominently displayed in the current "Sinmin." <64>

**Summary**

This 1931 article by Kim Man critiques prominent magazine reporters and editors in colonial Korea. It laments the difficulties faced by the press, including censorship, securing good content, and appealing to a readership with limited education, which often forces compromises between journalistic ideals and commercial survival, sometimes leading to sensationalism. The author assesses individuals associated with major publications like Kaebyŏk-sa, Haebang-sa, Tonggwang, Samch'ŏlli, Sinsaeng, Chosŏn chi Kwang, and Sinmin, evaluating their skills, personalities, suitability for their roles, and contributions, offering both praise and sharp criticism. Several postscripts note editors leaving their positions or passing away, highlighting the precarious nature of the field.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its candid portrayal of the media landscape in 1930s colonial Korea. It explicitly names the "three difficulties" (censorship, manuscript scarcity, reader limitations) that constrained journalism under Japanese rule. The individual critiques offer insights into the personalities, perceived strengths and weaknesses, and operational methods of key figures in the Korean literary and publishing world of the time, including their struggles to balance ideological stances (or lack thereof) with the need for readership and financial stability. The mention of specific magazines, internal dynamics (like Ch'a Sang-ch'an's method of acquiring information), and the acknowledged trend towards sensationalism or catering to lower tastes reflect the complex socio-cultural and political environment.

雜誌記者 漫評
						金萬
잡지는 상품이외다. 좋은 해석을 하자면 같은 상품이라도 그 자신의 생명이라 할 주장이 잇는데다가 어듸까지든지 독자의 好奇的 흥미를 시절 따라서 가장 예민하게 붓잡지 아니하고는 생명을 보존할 수가 없게 되니 잡지의 경영난이 이곳에 잇을 뿐 아니라 3호 단명으로 꺼꾸러지고 마는 것이 다 이 때문이외다.
영업국에서는 영업으로의 수완이 잇을 것은 물론이외다. 마는 편집국에서는 그 자신의 주장을 허물내이지 아니하면서 묘하게 독자의 맘을 붓잡아 놓아야 되니 이곳에 기자로의 총명과 선견과 기지가 잇서야 되는 것이외다. 고기도 시절 따라서 미끼를 갈린다고 하니 어떠케 잡지 기자로서의 독자의 맘을 묘하게 이끌만한 수완이 없을 것입니까. 생각하면 잡지기자처럼 어렵은 일은 없는 것이외다.
게다가 조선 같은 곳에는 檢閱難이 잇고 原稿難에다가 讀者難까지 겸해 놓앗으니 아모리 명기자인들 자기의 수완을 충분히 발휘할 수는 없는 일이외다. 일반적 好奇로는 이러한 기사를 실어야될 것이언만 검열 때문에 할 수 없이 중지치 아니할 수 없는 일도 잇거니와 또 어떤 특수한 기사 같은 것에는 원고의 적임자조차 없고보니 한심한 일이외다. 그러고 독자 대중의 교양이 낮기 때문에 어쩔 수 없시 저급하게라도 그들의 흥미를 끄을만한 기사를 실지 아니할 수가 없으니 어떠케 기자로의 충분한 수완을 발휘할 수가 잇겟습니까. 조선 잡지의 외국 잡지에 비하야 손색이 잇지 아니할 수 없는 것은 실로 이 점에 잇거니와 또 명記者가 명기자로의 충분한 발육을 하지 못하고 그대로 말라 빠지고 마는 것도 이 점에 잇다고 할 수밖에 없는 일이외다.
이러한 세 가지 난관을 그대로 꾸준이 이 방면의 明日을 위해 노력 고심하는 기자들의 일면을 엿보게 되니 뜨겁은 날의 「아이스크림」 맛에다는 비할 것이 아니라해도 또한 해롭지는 아니할 것이외다.

方定煥
조선서는 잡지왕국이라 할 開闢社 2층에는 편집실에 북극의 白熊모양으로 혼자 들어 앉아서 連해 연방 담배를 피여 물고는 「혜성」, 「신여성」, 「別乾坤」, 「어린이」들의 매호 편집 목차에 하로같이 땀을 흘리는 同氏는 開闢 잡지왕국의 總理라는 觀도 없지 아니하거니와 그보다는 몸뚱이가 뚱뚱하고 부즈런한 것이 「노력하는 곰」이라는 감을 금할 수 없는 것은 필자만의 특수감이 아닐 것이의다. 氏야말로 만물상이라 할만하외다마는 그 실은 그러치도 아니하야 혼자서 만물상을 버려 놓은 것만차 1人 1技의 피할 수 없는 곳에다 앉히운다고 하면 이것이라고 붓잡아 말할 곳은 없는 이외다. 잇다고 하면 자타가 모도다 許하는 동화가 잇을 것이나 창작으로의 씨의 동화를 보지 못한 필자는 童話壇 우에다 씨를 맨 첨 올라 앉히우기도 어렵은 노릇이외다. 그러나 꾸준하고 침착하고 熱이 잇게 事爲해 나아가는 씨를 필자는 무엇보다도 귀엽게 생각하며 깊이 존경하는 바외다.
開闢왕국의 잡지들이 꾸준이 달마다 別乾坤같은 것은 별문제라 하고라도 그만한 내용을 독자에게 내여 놓게 되는 것은 물론 다른이들의 노력이 없는 것이 아니외다마는 씨의 「곰」같이 노력만 아는 부즈런의 결과에 지내지 안는 것이외다.
그러기에 씨의 편집에는 눈부시게 새뜻한 것은 없으나마 먹어서 설사가 날만한 것은 하나도 없는 것이외다. 꾸준이 일한다는 것이 성공의 근본이라 하면 明日의 同 왕국에는 적지 아니한 광명이 잇는 것이외다.
(世相은 무상합니다. 方定煥씨는 필자가 이 글을 쓴 후에 별세햇습니다)

車相讚
靑吾니 觀相者니 車賤者니 하는 아호의 익명으로 뾰족한 붓대를 도처에 휘둘으는 씨야말로 잡지왕국 開闢社의 이채라 할만하외다. 만일 씨의 기지가 方定煥 總理를 보조치 않앗드란들 開闢왕국의 잡법편집에는 好奇로의 새뜻한 목차가 보기 어렵을는지 몰을 것이외다.
대머리가 되어가는 도두라진 이마에다가 하탁이 뾰족 나온 것이 웃어도 破顔大笑하는 법이 없고 입살 근처만 음직이는 것이 씨의 얼골이니 인물로 보더라도 도두라진 곳이 잇는 것은 누구나 짐작할 것이외다. 그의 붓끗이＜61＞ 가다가다 너무 날카롭아서 하늘 눈을 울리는 때가 많은 것도 이 얼골의 도두라진 일면이어니와 이와 같은 것은 차라리 芳香 많은 장미꽃의 가시라고 할 일이외다. 觀相者니 車賤者니 하는 익명으로 알려지지 아니한 남의 내력을 붓끗으로 점점히 오려내는 것을 악의로 해석할 이도 잇을 것이외다마는 그보다는 그의 도두라진 일면으로의 선의라고 보는 것이 온당할 일이외다.
금년 정월 「別乾坤」에는 씨의 洪景來傳이 실리엇거니와 이 이면에는 破顔一笑할만한 씨의 기지가 잇엇으니 그것은 여러 해 두고 모하 놓은 洪景來 사적을 容易히 제공치 아니할 것을 간파한 씨가 文씨를 차자보고 洪景來 사적에 대하야 일대 설전을 한 것이외다. 文씨는 꿈에도 자기가 업히우는 줄은 생각지 아니하고 「자 문헌에 이러케 일일히 증거가 잇는데 내 말이 잘못이야」하면서 재료의 출처니 하는 것을 보여주엇든 것이외다. 이 때에 씨는 「그러타니 나도 기록해 두고서 다시 알아보겟소」하면서 기사의 「엑스」를 깜작같이 옴겨다까 잠간동안에 洪景來傳를 쓰고 말엇으니 씨가 만일 방문기자엿든들 얼마나 기지로의 수완을 발휘하엿을 것입니까.

蔡萬植
씨도 잡지왕국 開闢社의 기자외다. 씨가 잡지왕국의 월간물을 보금자리로 단편, 희곡같은 것을 가끔 발표합니다. 마는 작품들을 하나도 읽어 보지 못한 필자로는 말할 것이 못될 뿐 아니라 또한 이곳에서는 그러한 문예물을 이아기할 필요도 없는 것이외다. 물론, 方定煥, 車相讚 같은 백전 노장들이 우에 앉아서 모든 것은 지휘하는 때문에 잇어야 할 씨의 잡지기자로의 수완이 나타나지 못한다는 감도 없지 아니하거니와 실제 어떤 의미로는 잡지기자로의 씨의 수완이란 엿볼만 한 곳이 없는상 싶습니다.
문인으로의 씨의 일면은 잇을망정 잡지기자로의 씨의 수완은 아직 나타나지 아니하엿을 뿐 아니라 씨의 나이가 젊은 것 만치 잡지기자로의 씨는 明日에 잇는 것이요 금일에 잇는 것은 아니라 해도 결코 씨가 무재무능하다는 뜻으로는 생각지 아니할 것이외다.
그러면 고요히 開闢왕국에서 자라나는 씨를 지금은 건드릴 것이 아니요 明日의 약속을 기다려서뿐 비로소 나타나는 일면이 잇을 것이외다.

李晟煥
잡지왕국을 표방하는 開闢社와 대립하야 출판왕국이라는 눈부신 간판을 들고 이러선 곳이 잇으니 이것은 解放社외다. 全朝鮮農民社와 解放社와는 그 성질로 보아서 아모러한 관련이 없는 것이나마 이 두 사의 지배인물은 씨를 내어놓고는 다른 사람이 없고 보니 이곳에서 씨의 기자로의 또는 경영자로의 수완을 충분히 엿볼 수 잇는 것은 대단히 유쾌한 일이외다.
