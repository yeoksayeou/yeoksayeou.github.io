Gandhi Autobiography

We resume the serialization of Gandhi's autobiography, which had been suspended due to circumstances. This part depicts the crisis as Gandhi leaves his boyhood behind and enters his youth.

I passed the high school entrance examination in 1887. This exam was held in two regions, Āmedābād and Bombay. Since the whole country was poor, students from Kāthiāwār naturally tended to choose the place that was relatively closer and less expensive. As my family was also poor, I had no choice but to use this selection method. This was my first journey from Rājkot to Āmedābād, and I traveled alone.

After the entrance exam, my elder brothers intended for me to continue my studies in college. There were colleges in both Bhāvnagar and Bombay, but since the former school was less costly, I decided to go there and study at Sāmaldās College. I went, but everything was difficult, and I found no enjoyment in it. It wasn't the professors' fault; the professors at that school were first-rate at the time. However, I lacked the cultivation to receive it. At the end of the first semester, I returned home.

We knew a man named Mavji Dave (부디, 데부), a thoughtful and learned Brahmin, who was a long-time acquaintance and advisor to our family. He continued his relationship with our family even after my father passed away. He visited us during my vacation and, while talking with my mother and eldest brother, asked about my studies. Learning that I was at Sāmaldās College, he said,
"Times have changed. None of you can expect to inherit your father's position without receiving a considerable education. Since this child is still continuing his studies, you must intend for him to inherit that position rather than yourselves. It would take four or five years for this child to obtain a B.A. degree, but that would qualify him best not as a prime minister, but as a lawyer. If he studies law like my son, it will take even longer, but by then, lawyers will be vying for the prime minister's position. I think it would be better for you to send this younger brother to England. The cost will only be four to five thousand rupees. Think about the Barristers who have returned from England now. How high-class they live. If they want to become prime ministers, <44> it's easy. I intend to strongly urge them this year to send Mohandās to England. My son Kēshavram (케부아르람) has many friends in England. He will give him letters of introduction, and Mohandās will benefit from them there."

Without hesitation, the old advisor turned to me and asked, "Wouldn't it be better for you to go to England than to study here?" No welcome could have been greater for me. I was suffering through studies I found overwhelming, so upon hearing this, I didn't know what to do and said the sooner I could go, the better. However, quickly passing the various hurdles was not so easy. Could I perhaps go abroad to study to obtain a doctor's qualification?

My elder brother objected, "Father would never have liked that. When he said that we Vaishnavas (바지나바) should not dissect corpses, he was thinking of you. Father intended to set you up in the legal profession."

At this point, the old advisor mediated, saying it wasn't about opposing medicine. "Our scriptures have no words opposing it. However, the title of doctor might prevent you from becoming a prime minister. I wish for you to become a prime minister. Only that path will enable you to protect and provide for your large family. Times are changing rapidly; things will get more difficult as days go by, so I think becoming a Barrister quickly is the wisest course." The advisor departed, asking me to think it over carefully.

I began to build castles in the air. My eldest brother became worried. How would we afford the expenses? Would it be alright to send such an inexperienced person alone? Mother was very reluctant about me studying abroad. Thus, trying to postpone my departure as much as possible, she said, "Right now, the eldest in our family is your uncle; we must consult with him first. If he permits this, I will think about it."

My elder brother had another idea. He generally said, "We should inquire with the state of Pōrbandar. Mr. Lely (레리-) is an elder who (one line illegible) is very fond of us. Our uncle is also very beloved by that elder. Perhaps he could recommend you for some state scholarship for your studies in England."

I agreed to everything and prepared to go to Pōrbandar. There was no railway there at that time. It was about a five-day journey by bullock cart (거세우차). I mentioned before that I was quite a coward, but at this time, my desire to go to England eliminated that fear. I hired a bullock cart only up to Dhorāji (디오라디) and from there, to reach Pōrbandar even a day earlier, I hired a camel. This was my first camel journey.

I finally arrived and first went to see my uncle to discuss everything. After careful consideration, he said, "It is uncertain whether one can stay in England without being swayed from the religion I revere. Whenever I meet students who have studied in England, I see no difference between their lives and those of Europeans. They have no scruples about food. Cigars (려송연) seem never to leave their mouths, <45> they act like Englishmen without any shame, and have not the slightest thought of preserving our family customs. I will soon depart on a pilgrimage and won't live much longer. Standing at death's door, how can I permit you to travel so far? However, I will not obstruct your path. Ultimately, it depends on your mother's permission. If she permits it, then I too will bless your success. Tell your mother that I am not interfering."

"I cannot expect more from you than that. Then I will try to persuade Mother. But couldn't you recommend me to Administrator Lely?" I asked.

"How can I know that? But he is a good man. Explain your connection and ask for his help. He will surely arrange and assist you," my uncle said.

I cannot say why my uncle did not give me a letter of introduction. I think he hesitated to give direct cooperation to my 'irreligious' act, that is, going to England. I sent a letter to Mr. Lely and received notice to visit his home. So I went, but he saw me on the stairs and said, "First pass the B.A., then come see me. I cannot help you now," coldly turned around, and went upstairs. I had painstakingly prepared to meet him again. I had carefully memorized two or three sentences. I bowed low and raised my hands in salute to him. But it was all in vain.

Since all seemed lost, I thought about selling my wife's ornaments to go. I wondered if my most trusted eldest brother could help somehow. He was generous to a fault and loved me like his own child. I returned from Pōrbandar to Rājkot and told him everything that had happened. I had also spoken with the old Brahmin advisor (네-브 로인 - likely referring back to Mavji Dave), who advised me to take out a loan if possible.

Selling my wife's ornaments would likely raise two or three thousand rupees. My brother also promised to arrange some money.

Mother still did not consent and made various inquiries. Someone said that young men go bad if they go to England – they eat meat freely and cannot live without alcohol. I told my mother, "Mother, don't you trust me? I will not deceive you. I vow I will never fall into such temptations. If there were such dangers, the old Brahmin advisor (레-브 로인) would not have encouraged me to go."

"I trust you. But how can I trust you when you are in a foreign country? I don't know what to do. I will ask the Swāmi (스와미)," Mother said. The Swāmi was also an advisor to our clan. He said, "I will allow him to go after receiving three vows from him," and he administered the vows to me. I solemnly swore not to touch wine, women, or meat. And so, permission was granted by Mother as well.

The high school gave me a send-off. Going to England was not a common occurrence for a young man from Rājkot. Receiving the blessings of many elders, <46> I headed for Bombay. My brother accompanied me, but significant difficulties arose here.

Receiving my mother's blessing and leaving behind my wife and infant child of several months, I set off for Bombay. However, upon arrival, various friends told my brother about the roughness of the Indian Ocean for six or seven months and said that since it was my first voyage, it would be difficult, so it would be better to wait until November. Someone else also told him about a steamship that had sunk due to a large storm at that time. This caused my brother anxiety, making him forbid me from leaving immediately, deeming it dangerous. My brother entrusted me to a friend in Bombay and returned to Rājkot for his own affairs. He entrusted my travel expenses to my brother-in-law and asked two or three friends to provide me with necessary assistance before leaving. The time in Bombay was truly tedious.

During this time, the people of my caste (性階) became very agitated, thinking ill of my going abroad. If I were to go, I would have to be expelled (lit. registered out). A general meeting of the caste was held, and I was summoned there. I don't know why I suddenly gained courage. Without any hesitation and completely unyielding, I attended the meeting. The chairman of the meeting, Mr. Seth (세스), who was distant from me but had been on good terms with my father, gave me his instructions.

"In the opinion of the caste, your going to England is not considered proper. Our religion forbids overseas travel. It is said that in England, it is impossible to live without defiling our religion. This is because one cannot avoid eating with Europeans there."

To this, I replied, "I believe that going to England does not violate our religion in the slightest. I intend to go there to study. And I have already firmly vowed to my mother to abstain from the three things you most abhor. I believe that vow will keep me safe." Seth replied to this,

"But we tell you, it is impossible to firmly maintain our faith there, so what is to be done? You surely know the relationship between your late father and myself. Then you must listen to my counsel." I replied again,

"I know the relationship well. And you are an elder to me. However, regarding this matter alone, I cannot help it. I cannot change my decision to go to England. Even the learned Brahmin priest, my late father's friend, approved of my going to England, and my mother and brother have already given their permission."

"But will you not obey the order of the caste?"

"I cannot. I believe the caste should not interfere in this matter."

This angered Seth. He reprimanded me. I sat still without moving. And so, Seth issued the order.

"From today, this child must be treated as someone outside the caste (성계 밖의 사람). Anyone who helps him or sees him off at the docks shall be fined 1 Rupee and 4 Annas."

This order had no effect on me. I bid farewell to Seth. However, I worried about how my brother would view this. Fortunately, he was not swayed. <47> Despite Seth's order, a reply came saying, "I permit it, so go." However, this incident pained my heart considerably. What would have happened if they had pressured my brother as well?

Just then, I heard that a solicitor's agent from Junāgadh (듀나가디) was going to England by wooden ship on September 4th to obtain a barrister's license. My brother's friends who were arranging things for me said I shouldn't miss this opportunity. There wasn't a moment to delay. I obtained permission from my brother by telegram. I then asked my brother-in-law to return my travel funds (려비됴). However, he hesitated due to Seth's order, saying he couldn't risk losing his caste standing. I had no choice but to ask a family friend to arrange only my passage fare and miscellaneous expenses. He gladly agreed. With that money, I bought my ship ticket and prepared for departure. I also prepared sufficient food supplies. Through the arrangements of several friends, I was placed in the same cabin as the solicitor's agent. He was an experienced man in his prime, while I was still an eighteen-year-old youth with no experience. On September 4th, I finally left Bombay and became a passenger on the Indian Ocean. <48>

**Summary**

This excerpt from Gandhi's autobiography, published in the Korean magazine *Dong-gwang* in 1931, details his decision to study law in England after finding college in India difficult. It recounts the advice he received, the significant opposition from his family (mother, uncle) concerned about religious and cultural integrity abroad, and particularly the threat of expulsion from his caste (Vaishnava). Despite financial difficulties and intense social pressure, Gandhi secures permission after vowing to abstain from meat, alcohol, and women, and finally departs from Bombay in September 1887. For Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it shows the circulation of Gandhi's story and ideas in colonized Korea, likely resonating with Koreans facing their own struggles against imperial power and perhaps contemplating foreign education or dealing with social conservatism. The detailed account of navigating family, tradition (caste rules), and personal ambition towards education abroad might have struck a chord with Korean intellectuals and youths of the era.

간디 자서전
그동안 사정에 의하야 게속치 못하든 간디의 자서전을 다시 실림니다. 이것은 간디가 소년 시대를 벗어나 청년시대로 드러가는 위기를 그린 것입니다.

나는 1887년에 고등학교 입학 시험에 급제하얏다. 이 시험은 「아-메다밧드」와 「봄베」의 두 지방에서 행하얏다. 온 나라가 가난하니까 자연히 「카시아와르」의 학생들도 비교적 가깝고 학비가 덜 드는 데를 택하게 되엇다. 나의 집도 가난하기 때문에 나도 그 선택방법을 아니 쓸 수 없엇다. 이것은 「라지코트」에서 「아-메다밧드」까지 첫 여행이오 또 독행이엇다. 나의 형들은 입학 시험 후 고등학교에서 나의 공부를 게속 시키려고 하얏다. 「바브나갈」에나 「봄베」에나 다 고등학교가 잇엇지마는 전 학교가 비용이 덜 들기 때문에 나는 거기에 가서 「사말다스」고등학교에서 배우기로 결실하얏다. 그리하야 가기는 하얏지만 모도가 어러워 아모 자미도 몰랏다. 그는 교수들의 과실이 아니니 그 학교의 교수들은 당시 일류들이엇다. 그러나 나는 그것을 받을 만한 교양이 부족하얏다. 일학기 말에 나는 귀가하얏다.
우리들은 「부디, 데부」란 사람을 알앗으니 그는 사려 깊고 학문 잇는 「파라문」 중이니 우리 집의 세대구교이요 또 조언자이엇다. 그이는 나의 부친이 도라간 뒤에도 우리집과 관게를 게속해 왓다. 그이는 나의 휴가 중에 우리들을 찾아와 나의

이 때에 마츰 「듀나가디」소송 대리인이 변호사 면허를 얻기 위하야 구월 사일에 목선으로 영국 간다는 말을 드럿다. 나의 주선을 맡은 형의 친구들은 이 기회를 노처서는 안된다고 말하얏다. 일각도 유예할 수가 없엇다. 나는 형에게 전보로써 허가를 얻엇다. 그리고 의형에게 려비됴를 돌려 달라고 말하얏다. 그러나 그이는 「세스」의 명령에 꺼리껴 성게를 잃어 바려서는 안되겟다고 말하얏다. 나는 할 수 없이 그 가족의 친구에게 부탁하야 나의 선표 갑과 잡비만을 매련하야 달라고 하얏다. 그이는 기쁘게 허락하얏다. 그 돈으로 나는 배표도 사고 떠날 준비도 하얏다. 식료품도 충분이 예비하얏다. 여러 친구의 주선으로 그 소송 대리인과 같은 방에 잇게 되엇다. 그이는 성숙긔에 잇는 경험가이요 나는 아직 아모 경험도 없는 십팔세의 청년이엇다. 나는 구월 사일에 마츰내 「봄베」를 떠나 인도양 우의 한 선객이 되엇다.＜48＞
