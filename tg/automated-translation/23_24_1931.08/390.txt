**Body Text Translation**

End of the World

In T'ŏigi's (Turkey's) old capital, K'ŏnsŭt'ant'inop'ŭl (Constantinople), several thousand people recently gathered outside the city walls, waiting for the sky to break apart. This was because a certain fortune-teller had prophesied that the end of the world would come that night. It is said that an assistant at the K'ŏnsŭt'ant'inop'ŭl (Constantinople) Observatory (天文臺) could not sleep a wink all night because people kept calling by telephone, asking to see if the face of the angel who would judge the world had yet appeared in the sky.<71>

**Summary**

The article reports on an incident in Constantinople (modern-day Istanbul) where thousands gathered based on a fortune-teller's prophecy that the world would end that night. An observatory assistant was inundated with calls asking if the judging angel had appeared. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article highlights the flow of international news, even seemingly trivial or sensationalist stories, into Korea during this period. It might reflect a popular interest in apocalyptic narratives, perhaps as a form of escapism or mirroring underlying societal anxieties, or simply the nature of global news dissemination at the time.

世界의 末日
토이기의 옛서울 컨스탄티노플에서는 최근 수천명의 군중이 성 밖으로 모여서 하늘이 끊어지기를 기다리고 잇엇다. 그것은 어떤 점쟁이가 이날 밤에 세계의 끝날이 오리라고 예언한 까닭이엇다. 컨스탄디노플 天文臺의 助手는 밤새도록 세계를 재판할 천사의 얼골이 아직도 하늘에 나타나지 않앗는가 보라고 전화로 뭇는 사람이 잇어 한잠도 자지 못햇다고 한다.＜71＞
