**Body Text Translation:**
Review of the Additional Salary Problem, Discriminatory Treatment in Official Circles
						Ham Sanghun

1
Even though it is said that since the promulgation of the new government organization system in Taishō 8 (1919), Korean military police assistants (헌병 보조원) and police assistants (巡査輔) were eliminated, and regulations concerning the status and salaries of Korean civil officials were abolished, the discriminatory policy of keeping Korean officials in lower positions and using other forms for salary disparities still remains.

First, looking at the position of Korean officials among general officials, as of the end of Shōwa 3 (1928), out of a total of 45,900 central and local officials, Korean officials numbered only about 16,400, merely one-third of the total number of officials. Even among high-ranking officials (고등관리), the backbone of Chosŏn, there were only 6 Koreans compared to 158 Japanese, occupying just 1/26th of the total high-ranking officials.

Now, the number of Koreans in executive-level positions in each department of the central government offices is shown below. (End of Shōwa 3)
Secretariat executives: 14, Koreans: None
Internal Affairs Bureau: 35, Koreans: 1
Finance Bureau: 9, Koreans: None
Industry Bureau: 32, Koreans: 1
Justice Bureau: 3, Koreans: None
Education Bureau: 13, Koreans: 4
Police Affairs Bureau: 22, Koreans: None
Forestry Department: 12, Koreans: None
Land Improvement Department: 16, Koreans: None

Furthermore, looking at the regulations regarding salaries, even for officials of the same rank, Japanese officials receive significant benefits under names such as additional salary (加俸) and housing allowance (舍宅料).

The content of the decree concerning additional salaries for employees of the Government-General of Chosŏn (朝鮮總督府) and its affiliated offices, promulgated as Governor-General's Decree No. 36 in March, Taishō 2 (1913), is as follows:
Article 1: Additional salaries for employees serving in the Government-General of Chosŏn and its affiliated offices shall be paid according to the following rates:
High-ranking officials (고등관) and those treated as high-ranking officials: 4/10ths of the base salary (for professors and assistant professors at Kyŏngsŏng Imperial University, 4/10ths of the base salary and duty allowance)
Junior officials (判任官) and those treated as junior officials: 6/10ths of the base salary

Thus, even for a provincial governor with the same annual salary of 3,000 won, a Japanese receives an additional salary of 1,200 won (4/10ths), while a Korean can only receive the base salary. Even for an elementary school teacher with the same monthly salary of 50 won, a Japanese receives an additional 30 won (6/10ths of the base salary), while a Korean can only receive 50 won.

Now, to investigate the total amount of additional salaries for Japanese officials within Chosŏn, let us first show the number of personnel and salary amounts for all Japanese and Korean officials in Chosŏn. (Shōwa 3) <15>

Calculating the additional salaries for officials of junior rank (判任官) and above based on the Additional Salary Decree (加俸令), it is approximately as follows:
Imperial Appointees (勅任) (40% of base salary): 135,450 won
Mid-Rank Appointees (奏任) (Same): 1,336,151 won
Treated as Mid-Rank Appointees (奏任대우) (Same): 100,987 won
Junior Appointees (判任) (60% of base salary): 6,763,350 won
Treated as Junior Appointees (판임대우) (Same): 4,168,596 won
Total: 12,504,534 won

Separate from the above Additional Salary Decree, the additional salaries for the Governor-General of Chosŏn and the Vice Governor-General for Political Affairs (政務總監) are set to be 5/10ths of their annual salary, so the total additional salary for all Japanese officials would be 1,500 won more than this. Furthermore, according to Article 3 of the same Additional Salary Decree, those serving in the following prefectures (府), counties (郡), and islands (島) are to receive an additional salary equal to 1/10th of their base salary on top of the Article 1 additional salary, meaning another 400,000-500,000 won in additional salaries will be added. Also, according to Article 4 of the same decree, in the counties of Musan in Hamgyŏng Pukto; P'ungsan, Kapsan, Samsu, and Changjin in Hamgyŏng Namdo; and Pyŏktong, Ch'osan, Wiwŏn, Kanggye, Chasŏng, and Huch'ang in P'yŏngan Pukto, an additional salary of 1/10th of the base salary is granted, adding several tens of thousands more won. That is, the additional salary according to Article 1 is over 12 million won, the additional salary according to Article 3 is about 400,000-500,000 won, and the additional salary according to Article 4 is about 20,000-30,000 won, reaching a total of truly over 13 million won.

Chŏlla Namdo: Chejudo Island, Sorokto Island <16>

In other words, Japanese officials, even of the same rank, receive an additional salary of 6/10ths to 8/10ths more than Korean officials if they are junior appointees (判任官), and 4/10ths to 6/10ths more if they are mid-rank appointees (奏任官) or higher.

Is it only officials? Among government employees, almost every province grants Japanese individuals 6/10ths of the base salary as additional pay, following the example of local treatment allowances for Chosŏn employees, and even for municipal clerks (府吏員), Japanese are granted an additional salary of 4/10ths to 6/10ths of the base salary.

For Government-General policemen (巡査), Imperial Ordinance No. 303 of April, Taishō 14 (1925) applies the Civil Official Additional Salary Decree mutatis mutandis. Therefore, even a policeman who starts service at the initial salary of 30 won, if Japanese, receives an additional salary of 18 to 24 won (6/10ths to 8/10ths), resulting in an income of 48 to 52 won, whereas a Korean can only receive 30 won.

Japanese officials and policemen also have something called a housing allowance (宿舍料). The regulations for housing allowance payment have detailed rules regarding region, family status, etc., but typically for families, the following housing allowances are paid, divided into Grade 1, Grade 2, and Grade 3 areas. Grade 1 is Kyŏngsŏng, Grade 2 includes prefectures (府) or designated towns (面/邑), and Grade 3 covers places not belonging to Grade 1 or 2. Therefore, if two female teachers, one Japanese and one Korean, who graduated from Kyŏngsŏng Normal School, are simultaneously appointed to a school in a designated town, these two individuals who studied side-by-side until now face a situation where one receives only the base salary of 40 won, while the other receives an additional salary of 26.40 won to 35.20 won and a housing allowance of 13 won on top of that, totaling 83.40 won to 120.20 won – approximately double.

How many Korean county magistrates (郡守) receive a salary of 83.40 won to 120.20 won?

The regulations regarding housing allowance payments for Japanese policemen are structured as follows: 1st grade area 10 won, 2nd grade 9 won, 3rd grade 8 won, 4th grade 7 won, 5th grade 6 won, 6th grade 5 won, based on factors like cost of living and transportation conditions. Adding the additional salary and housing allowance to the initial 30 won salary results in roughly more than double the amount.

3
Originally, these regulations for additional salary and housing allowance were established because Chosŏn had inconvenient transportation and lacked fully developed educational and recreational facilities, naturally causing inconvenience to outsiders, and thus were intended to compensate them. However, Chosŏn today, after 20 years, is no longer the Chosŏn of the past. Now, in terms of transportation, culture, and everything else, it is not inferior to that of Japan. Moreover, the cost of living is lower than in Japan. Why is this "impartial benevolence" (一視同仁), this principle of non-discrimination that is always mentioned, still left here? Furthermore, it is a fact that the economic strength of the Chosŏn people (民力) is currently extremely depleted and cannot bear the present burden, while necessary expenses for compulsory education and other needs are piling up. Shouldn't this 13 million won in additional salaries and several million won in housing allowances be abolished to eliminate the factual discrimination between Japanese and Koreans and be used for more urgent expenses? Abolish the additional salaries and similar allowances quickly. <17>

**Summary**

This 1931 editorial by Ham Sanghun critically examines the discriminatory salary system implemented by the Japanese colonial government in Korea (Chosŏn). It highlights the significant disparity in pay between Japanese and Korean officials of the same rank, primarily through the mechanisms of "additional salary" (加俸) and "housing allowance" (舍宅料), which were granted exclusively or at much higher rates to Japanese employees. The author provides detailed statistics showing the low representation of Koreans in higher official posts and calculates the substantial total cost of these additional payments to Japanese officials (over 13 million won annually), arguing that this system starkly contradicts the official rhetoric of "impartial benevolence" (一視同仁). The editorial calls for the immediate abolition of these discriminatory allowances, citing the modernization of Korea (rendering the original justifications obsolete) and the severe economic hardship faced by the Korean populace, suggesting the funds be redirected to more essential needs like education.

For historians, this article offers valuable quantitative data and contemporary critique of the economic dimensions of colonial discrimination. It starkly illustrates how seemingly neutral bureaucratic regulations (salary scales, allowances) were used to enforce ethnic hierarchy and extract wealth, directly contradicting colonial propaganda. The detailed breakdown of allowance rates, regional variations, and the calculation of the total financial burden provides concrete evidence of systemic inequality within the colonial administration. The author's appeal to both fairness and fiscal responsibility, grounded in the changed conditions of Korea and the people's suffering, reflects a reasoned argument against colonial policy from within the educated Korean elite.
