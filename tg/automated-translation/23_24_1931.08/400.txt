**Wedding Ceremony**
						By Kim Dong-in (金東仁)

One day, at some gathering, several people were chatting when a friend named K asked me this—
“Do you know a man named Kim Ch’ŏlsu?”
“No.”
I tilted my head as I answered. Of course, the surname “Kim” and the given name “Ch’ŏlsu” are incredibly common, so I might have heard them somewhere, but a “Kim Ch’ŏlsu” worthy of being brought up in this conversation didn’t immediately come to mind…
“You probably wouldn’t know him. He’s currently a student at Chosŏnjŏn University…”
“I don’t know him.”
“Do you know a woman named Song Sŏnbi?”
“No. Ah, wait a minute. What does she do?”
“She’s a nursery teacher at X Kindergarten.”
“Ah. I remember now. Quite the stylish one.” I recalled seeing her at a joint kindergarten sports day sometime ago, remembering a certain nursery teacher who paraded around with unnecessary flair in front of the large crowd, and nodded.
“That’s right. Stylish indeed—truly, in Chosŏn. Then you wouldn’t know about the wedding comedy involving Kim Ch’ŏlsu and Song Sŏnbi, I suppose.”
“How would I know?”
“Truly, why are there so many marriageable girls in Chosŏn? Swarming around, five or six for a penny.”
“Isn’t that only natural? Men who used to marry at eleven or twelve now wait until they’re well past twenty. While those eleven or twelve-year-old girls grow up to their twenties, there will naturally be a surplus of girls. At this rate, hundreds of thousands of marriageable virgins must be left over each year. Right now, even if you assigned three student concubines to each man, it probably wouldn’t be enough…”
“It’s a pitiful situation. That’s why such comedies happen.”
“So, what’s the story you’re trying to tell? Is it like the one or two stories that appear in the newspaper every day, that Song Sŏnbi also unknowingly went off to be a concubine to that Kim fellow?”
“Wouldn’t that be nice? Kim Ch’ŏlsu almost became Song Sŏnbi’s concubine, you see. Hahahaha.”
“So, you mean Song had a husband already?”
“Hahahaha. Just listen to the story.”
K took a sip of the tea placed before him. Then he began his tale.

This fellow, Kim Ch’ŏlsu, comes from humble origins but from a family with a bit of money. Thanks to that bit of money, he’s now at Chosŏnjŏn University—studying what?—well—law or literature, or whatever subject is easy to handle for the time being, putting future goals aside. He’s twenty-two years old. A single man who has been previously married [and divorced/widowed]. An atheist who attends a Christian church. <72>
Speaking of his personality, he’s a bit impatient and lacks decisiveness, yet he’s spirited, easily embarrassed but also quite bold—especially when it comes to relationships between men and women, he’s quite forward and neurotic…
Well, this fellow came back home during summer vacation and got engaged, didn’t he? His counterpart was none other than Song Sŏnbi.
Originally, this woman Song Sŏnbi, her family wasn't much in terms of wealth, as her mother made a living as a *wŏlja* matchmaker (월자거간). But she managed to finish girls' high school (高普) here and even attended XX Women's School in Seoul. Moreover, whether studying here or in Seoul, her attire and everything about her always followed the—so-called—cutting edge. She was the first one here to wear her skirt with the lower hem turned up (that is, she was the first to import the Seoul fashion). That was Song Sŏnbi. She was the first to import and practice the trends of skirts getting longer then shorter, jackets getting bigger then smaller. (Of course, she couldn't do that while attending school). Always draped in nameless Japanese silks, she was at the forefront of trends among girls floating in vanity. Though I didn't see it directly, they say when she attended XX Women's School in Seoul, she was the most stylish and the most 'high-collar' (하이칼라). A platinum wristwatch on her arm, a ring set with a large (though only one) diamond on her finger, always wearing thin silk stockings through which her skin peeked—where on earth did that money come from? Actually, there's a rumor that while attending XX Women's School, all her expenses were covered by the school's principal, Mr. Q. Furthermore, there's even talk that she went with Mr. Q to some countryside place to get an abortion.
Mr. Q, as you know, isn't he a notorious lecher? Isn't he famous for using his healthy physique and his position, which allows him many encounters with women, to engage in seduction, adultery, rape—all sorts of acts contrary to human morality? So, it would almost be natural that he somehow got involved with Sŏnbi, who was quite brazen-faced, also quite bold, and had strong sexual desires.
According to hearsay (傳聞), the affair between Mr. Q and Sŏnbi seemed to have become quite passionate. Moving from woman to woman without a moment's pause (next 2 lines illegible)… whether it was because Sŏnbi was extremely jealous, or because the intensity of their sexual desires matched perfectly, one cannot judge, but it seems that while Sŏnbi was enrolled at XX Women's School, he apparently didn't lay a hand on any other woman.
Somehow or other, Sŏnbi graduated from that school and came down here to become a nursery teacher at X Kindergarten. Of course, there must have been a tearful, dramatic scene of parting. Huh? That ‘Oh let go, no I won’t let go’ kind of thing, you know.
Although Seoul and here are over 500 *ri* apart, with a thousand coming and a thousand going daily, the romance between Mr. Q and Sŏnbi spread so widely that there was hardly anyone here who didn't know about it. And every three days, Mr. Q would come down to P'yŏngyang, call Sŏnbi out, stay with her at an inn, and then go back up. The engagement with Kim Ch’ŏlsu happened right around that time.
Even now, I can't figure out Sŏnbi's intentions. If she was that deeply involved with Mr. Q, why did she get engaged to Ch’ŏlsu? Of course, Mr. Q did have a wife. But for the so-called modern girl (모던껄) Miss Song Sŏnbi, to whom love knows no borders, no class—love transcends everything—whether Mr. Q had a wife or not shouldn't have been an issue, right? In a life-or-death situation, what does a legal wife matter? What? Huh? Right. That seems the only way to interpret it. "Let's leave it to fate"—this is a common trait among Koreans. Perhaps she decided to compartmentalize: love is love, fate is fate, and resolved to marry the fellow (놈팽이).
But the story of how they got engaged is also quite a spectacle. Our talented Mr. Kim Ch’ŏlsu went with the matchmaker to the prospective bride's house for the viewing (선), didn't he? After they were seated, the bride's mother asked a couple of questions, then said,
“In the new style (신식), the two should talk alone.”
She then winked at the matchmaker, and they both stepped outside. After a little while, someone brought in a platter of sliced oriental melons. Then, a princess-like, clear voice spoke,
“Please come closer and have some.”
The fellow’s mind went half blank. This guy, who had only ever dallied with café waitresses or *kisaeng*, was bewildered hearing such words from a new-style, high-collar prospective bride.<73>
“Not at all, not at all.”
He just stared holes into the floor beneath him. Head bowed deeply… After that, a long silent play (黙言劇) ensued. Whenever the groom-to-be timidly glanced up at the bride, the bride, with a smile on her lips, would be staring intently back at the groom. Meeting her gaze, the groom would once again stare holes into the floor… Then suddenly, hearing a rustling sound, he looked up to find the bride had come close to him.
“Please move down a bit,”
she said, even boldly grabbing his hand. The fellow, utterly startled, stammered, “Yes, yes,” and as he slightly shifted his body, the bride suddenly let go of his hand, lunged at the groom, and started showering him with kisses. Letting out sounds like “Ung-ya,” “Ung-ya,” she rained down kisses on his cheeks, nose, lips—everywhere. And after clinging to him like that for a while, she slyly slipped her hand into the groom's waistband and felt around.
And so, the engagement was sealed. In the fellow's eyes, there was no woman in the world like this one. Someone like us would find such a vulgar girl repulsive, but in the eyes of the fellow, who was a step more enlightened than us, it all seemed innocent and lively. Moreover, he thought that since she acted this way upon their first meeting, she must already be completely smitten with him.
After that, the fellow was constantly hanging around the girl's house. Being a shy fellow still, he couldn’t bring himself to pester her about sleeping over at the bride-to-be's house, but during the day, her parents would give them privacy, leaving them alone together, which seemed quite enjoyable. He would go to his future in-laws' house as soon as his eyes opened and only return reluctantly to his own home late at night.
During this time, of course, Mr. Q came down several times to meet the girl. When he did, the girl would deceive her fiancé, saying she was going to the mineral springs or a cold spring bath, and go out to stay overnight for a day or two. But the fellow, completely smitten with the girl, simply trusted her implicitly.
Meanwhile, at some point, the girl apparently told her new fiancé everything about her relationship with Mr. Q.
“Now seems like a good time to tell you about my past secrets,”
she must have thought, and so she told him. And in conclusion, she declared that she had abandoned Mr. Q for his sake and would henceforth love and cherish only him, punctuating this, as was her custom, with a thunderbolt of kisses.
Ch’ŏlsu could only say, “Uh-huh, uh-huh,” unable to utter anything else. What could he say? Especially now that he was completely captivated by Sŏnbi; he would follow her even if chased away with a stick. When she said she abandoned that man for him, what could he possibly object to? If anything, he felt grateful that she had given up such a well-known figure like Mr. Q for him. Since he himself was not a virgin, demanding virginity from Sŏnbi felt inappropriate…
Truly, in situations like this, women are formidable. While men capable of flawlessly managing two wives are virtually non-existent, women often manage two men while deceiving them perfectly… To Ch’ŏlsu, she swore she had broken off ties with Mr. Q and loved no one else in the world but him. Yet, to Mr. Q, she claimed she was only engaged to someone else due to her parents' command but would absolutely not go through with the marriage. She kept both sides placated.
‘Frailty, thy name is woman’—some fool named Shakespeare said that, didn't he? Not at all, not at all. Strength, thy name is woman. Foolishness, thy name is man. One fellow rejoices that his fiancée abandoned a famous man in Chosŏn for his sake, while the other fellow internally delights that even a promising student and single groom-to-be cannot match his ability to handle women. Meanwhile, the woman manipulates both fellows as she pleases.
“I am your lover.”
“I am your wife.”
These two different statements, delivered distinctly to each man, satisfied them both.
In the meantime, the summer vacation ended, and Ch’ŏlsu had to return to Tokyo. They planned for him to come back during the winter vacation to hold the wedding ceremony. Ch’ŏlsu departed, shedding genuine tears of farewell.
Sŏnbi, saying she would see her departing beloved off, skipped work at the kindergarten and accompanied him all the way to Seoul.<74> Ch’ŏlsu's heart was so heavy that he couldn't utter a single word on the train, they say. He would occasionally gaze at the distant mountains, sigh, and then steal glances at his future wife…
Sŏnbi, too, only occasionally pinched Ch’ŏlsu’s thigh with her hand and remained silent all the way to Seoul. Then, during the 20-minute stop in Seoul, avoiding the eyes of other passengers, they exchanged several kisses, and then, sayonara.
As Ch’ŏlsu was leaving, he mumbled like a half-mute,
“Three months—three months—”
unable to finish his sentence. After glancing back at him once with the most dramatic, tragic expression, Sŏnbi hurried out of the station gate. Before the spot where she kissed Ch’ŏlsu could even dry, 20 minutes later, she was already offering those same lips to Mr. Q.
“I suddenly missed you so much that I came all the way here.”
Mr. Q melted all over again.
Napoleon supposedly wrote to his lover, “I’m too busy to write more than two letters a day.” Ch’ŏlsu must have been busier than Napoleon, as he could only manage one letter per day. And upon returning [to Tokyo], the fellow boasted.
“Mr. Kim (or Mr. Lee, or Mr. Park, or Mr. Choi), do you know Mr. Q?”
They mostly knew Mr. Q. After all, Mr. Q, being a so-called celebrity of sorts, often had his name appear in newspapers and magazines, so the name was familiar to their ears. When they replied that they thought they had heard the name, Ch’ŏlsu would puff up his chest.
“I snatched his lover away. I got engaged when I returned home this time, and the young lady was originally Mr. Q’s lover.”
He would say this, puffing his chest again as if asking, ‘How about my skills?’ Then, like someone out of his mind, unprompted and without preamble, he would add,
“She plays the piano well.”
Or,
“We’re having the wedding ceremony during winter vacation.”
Or,
“How can a man with a beautiful lover endure being so far away, worrying?”
He would ramble on like this.
Time flows like water, and the eagerly awaited winter vacation arrived. Ch’ŏlsu requested a hefty sum for travel expenses. Then, after wandering through Mitsukoshi (미츠꼬시) and Matsuzaka (마츠자카) buying heaps of gifts for his bride, he set off on the journey home for the wedding ceremony.
“When I return this time, I’ll be marrying Miss Song Sŏnbi—that beauty who was the lover of the famous Mr. Q.”
“The date has been roughly set through letters with Miss Song; we decided on January 3rd by the solar calendar—New Year’s banquet day.”
“Mr. Kim (or Mr. Lee, or Mr. Park, or Mr. Choi), aren’t you returning home for winter vacation? Let’s go together. On your way, stop by P'yŏngyang and attend my wedding.”
“If you don’t at least send a congratulatory telegram (축전), I’ll resent you.”
After deliberately taking a day off to visit friends and profusely publicize his wedding with such greetings, leaving behind the celebratory ‘banzai’ cheers of several friends, he bravely departed from Tokyo Station.
However, when returning home, the fellow concocted all sorts of schemes and deliberately didn't notify his bride of the exact return date. He merely hinted that he might arrive perhaps two or three days before the wedding. This was the plan hatched by his meager brain, calculated to utterly surprise and delight his lover.
But unexpectedly, he ran into Sŏnbi at Kyŏngsŏng Station. The man was startled. The woman was startled too.
“Oh my goodness!”
The woman let out such a cry, her eyes wide as she stood there stunned. But when faced with such situations, women are indeed better. Immediately composing herself with a smile, she said,
“Well, I thought you might arrive around today, so I came all the way here to meet you.”
And she plunked herself down in the empty seat next to Ch’ŏlsu.
Deeply moved—what else could Ch’ŏlsu feel but deeply moved? With eyes overflowing with emotion, Ch’ŏlsu gazed blankly at this goddess.
“I… I…”
He was an idiot. Mumbling like a half-mute.<75>
“You arrive, and yet no word?”
“I… I…”
“I don’t know. Men are all like that. So heartless.”
“I… I…”
“If I hadn’t rushed out, I might have missed meeting my lover (she whispered) arriving.”
“I… I…”
He was like a half-wit from a new-wave drama (신파희극).
Anyway, let’s just say they rushed from Seoul straight down to P'yŏngyang.
Ch’ŏlsu, being a fellow with some money, and one who had divorced his previous wife to get a new-style spouse—for this wedding ceremony, he wracked his meager brain, contemplating all sorts of ways to make it truly grand. What? He decided to hire first-class attendants (후행) for the procession? That meant fourteen people, men and women combined. The ceremony was to be held at the OO Church (례배당), and he had laid out all sorts of plans for it. For the processional music, an organ was deemed too shabby, so he arranged for an orchestra. As soon as the car carrying the bride and groom appeared at the street corner, Boy Scouts (뽀이스카웉) would blow trumpets in welcome, kindergarten children would sing congratulatory songs, a movie camera would be set up to film the scene—he squeezed out every possible idea his brain could conceive. And he sent invitations to all prominent figures, known or unknown…
From the beginning of January, presents (푸레젠트) and congratulatory messages started pouring in from friends and various quarters. The fellow’s nose rose even higher in the air.
Now, don't they have something called a step practice (보조련습) the day before the wedding? It's practice for walking into the ceremony hall in time with the music. On the fourth day of January, His Excellency the Groom transported his precious self (옥보) to the bride's house. They had agreed to meet at the OO Church at 5 PM for the step practice together (동부인하다), so he went to the bride's house around 4:40 PM. But the bride, with whom he had made a firm promise, wasn't home. The groom's eyes widened, and after waiting at the bride's house for a while, feeling bored, he came out. Just as he was about to step out the main gate, the bride's aunt, an old woman, followed him out. She pressed her lips right up to the groom's ear and whispered,
“Go check the OO Inn. She’s probably there.”
Then, as if talking to herself, she added,
“That fellow named Q is apparently here again,” and went back inside the house. Judging by this, the aunt seemed to disapprove inwardly of her niece's bad conduct.
It seems our friend had some jealousy in him too. No matter how famous the other party was, Ch’ŏlsu, who until now had proudly thought she had abandoned that celebrity for him, found these words grating.
—She said they broke up, but they were still attached. Furious, he stormed off, breathing heavily. He burst through the door of the OO Inn, and right opposite him were familiar women's shoes. Heaven gave man one trick when creating him; judging by the fellow's initial fury, it seemed he would barge straight into the room and cause a scene, but he suppressed his anger and stood quietly outside the door, listening. Inside, all sorts of things could be heard, such as:
“This is farewell forever, then.”
Or,
“Please write often to my parents’ home.”
Or,
“That person will be going back to Tokyo in a few days, so please come meet me again then.”
Oh, it was sickening. After that, there was all sorts of struggling and carrying on.
*“Tōnan mitsuketa. Sono ba o ugoku na!”* (Found the thief! Don't move from that spot!)
In *kabuki* terms, this would be the scene for striking a pose (*mie*). But does the fellow know *kabuki*? What caught his eye was a shoe. One shoe flew through the air, broke the paper door screen, and landed inside the room. Then another shoe, and another, and another—after throwing all four shoes inside, having run out of shoes, this time he threw his own body into the room. And there, a scene of action unfolded.
“Celebrities aren’t all that. When I hit him, his nose bled.”
This was the fellow’s recollection. Anyway, Mr. Q, the principal of XX School, the celebrity gentleman, got thoroughly beaten up at an inn bustling with people from all thirteen provinces of Chosŏn, and the stylish, high-collar Miss Song had her skirt torn and fled home in her undergarments (잠뱅이).<76>
Naturally, the engagement was off. But the bride's family was quite shrewd too. They returned the wedding gifts (폐백) and presents they had received that very night. The clothes and bedding must have already been made, as the feast was the next day, right? They apparently unstitched everything and sent it back as bolts of cloth (감으로 돌려 보내다).
At the groom's house, although the engagement was broken, it was a huge crisis. Setting aside the prepared food, they had informed all relatives and acquaintances about tomorrow's feast, diligently received monetary gifts (부조), and now, if they couldn't hold the feast, what a disgrace it would be. Among them, the groom was especially worried. He had told all his friends in Tokyo, and tomorrow at least forty or fifty congratulatory telegrams would arrive. If he returned to Tokyo still a bachelor without having gotten a wife, how would he look? More than anything else, it was a major concern for his reputation (체면상). Now, what was to be done?
But just as there is a god who abandons, there is a god who saves. That night, amidst the uproar and commotion, a friend of the groom's father came visiting, heard about the predicament, and came up with a brilliant idea, saying:
“I have a daughter who is working as a teacher (훈도) at the OO County Common School. Her looks are quite decent, and being a school teacher, her knowledge is considerable. How about it?”
Necessity is the mother of invention (窮則通). How could they have known such blessings would fall from the sky? On the verge of utter disgrace, they would have accepted anyone willing to marry him, but on top of that, she was described as decent-looking and educated—what more could they ask for?
An agreement was reached, and that very night, the prospective bride's father sent a telegram to his daughter.
Unaware of the circumstances, the daughter arrived the next day, didn't she? After giving his daughter, who arrived at dawn, a lecture, the father straightforwardly told her to hold the wedding ceremony today.
—The groom has wealth.
—He is a student at Chosŏnjŏn University.
—He is good-looking too.
Listing these conditions, the father persuaded his daughter. The daughter sat there blankly, then finally said,
—There’s nothing lacking in other aspects. However, the date is too sudden. For appearance's sake, how can we arrange a wedding today after just mentioning it today? So, if we could just have one week…”
In other words, if the wedding date could be postponed by just one week, she had no other objections. But the father, knowing the groom's family's situation, insisted she marry today. Go today! I'll go in a week! After arguing back and forth for a while, the father helplessly gave in to his daughter and went to report the matter to the groom's family. The groom's side, however, stubbornly insisted on today. It wasn't that postponement was impossible. But while they could postpone for a day or two, a whole week was out of the question—this was the groom's side's stance. It made sense. Even winter food, prepared with today as the target date, how could it last a week? Besides, while one could make up some excuse to postpone a wedding for a day or two, finding an excuse for a week-long postponement wouldn't be easy. The bride's father tried several times to mediate between his daughter and the groom's family, but failed to reach a compromise. The daughter inevitably returned to her school. But even as she left, she seemed quite reluctant.
“Even if it’s just four days postponement…”
She muttered this to herself, loud enough for her father to hear, as she departed.
After that, the groom's family had no choice but to try other means. They gathered every matchmaker (매파) in the city and the entire household threw themselves into searching for a bride. But my, were there ever so many unmarried women! Four or five family members went out and viewed every marriageable student available, but the date remained the issue. To represent the relationship between brides and dates numerically:
More than 3 months notice – Eight women
About 1 month notice – Thirty-one women
About half a month notice – Thirty-six women
One week notice – Sixteen women
Five days notice – Sixteen women
Total: 107 women
That’s how it was. Not a single person was willing to come within two days. They sent matchmakers back several times to the thirty-two women requiring the shortest notice (one week and five days), trying to persuade them to marry tonight or tomorrow, but perhaps they couldn't bring themselves to agree, as there was no clear positive response. That makes sense too. Even a *kisaeng* rarely consents on the first night they meet; wouldn't marriage be even more so?
At the church (례배당) <77>
“Congratulations on your wedding!”
“Kim Ch’ŏlsu and Song Sŏnbi, Banzai!”
“You must be happy!”
Such congratulatory telegrams (축전) were flooding in, while the groom’s household was in turmoil trying to choose a bride. Moreover, the men, women, young, and old who had gone to the OO Church for the wedding ceremony scheduled for 6 PM, finding only a dark building, inquired about the reason and came over to the groom’s house. Besides the embarrassment, what could be done? The house, meant for celebration, didn't feel celebratory at all; instead, people whispered in corners here and there, making it feel like a household struck by some misfortune. But they were helpless. Nothing could be done.
At that moment—indeed, God is gracious—a ray of light shone down from heaven. An unfamiliar matchmaker came bustling in, saying there was a bride willing to marry even tonight. What a windfall! This was no time for idle chatter. They asked if it was true, and the matchmaker swore oaths, saying she could bring the bride right away.
Whew—. After that, what else was there to discuss? The marriage proposal and acceptance happened like lightning. Then it was chaos: decorating the church with flowers, spreading cotton cloths, calling the Boy Scouts, recalling the attendants (후행) to put on their formal attire, requesting the pastor…
Because it was so sudden, finding female attendants was difficult. Please find four from the groom’s side—if you can't, we'll proceed with however many we have. We also can't find anyone suddenly in the middle of the night. Thus, the attendants, originally intended to be a first-class group of eleven, dwindled to three. Other arrangements also didn't go according to plan. The OO Church didn't have electricity yet; the original plan was to set up temporary wiring just for this day, but even that was abandoned. The swift wedding ceremony took place under dim kerosene lamps. Speed, speed—even saying it, such speed is hard to come by.
“The husband shall not abandon the wife.”
“Yes.”
“The wife shall not abandon the husband.”
“Yes.”
“Do not quarrel.”
“Yes.”
“Do not hit [each other].”
“Yes.”
Hahahaha. The fellow hadn't even seen the bride's face yet, but wasn't there the so-called exchange of wedding gifts (예물 교환)? The wedding ring exchange. At that moment, as he slipped the ring onto her finger, he glanced sideways. Her head was bowed low, so he couldn't see everything through the veil, but her white forehead and the bridge of her white nose looked quite pretty, apparently. The fellow's nose rose even higher.
Anyway, the wedding ceremony thus ended safely—no, rather grandly. So, the fellow must have been at the peak of joy, right? But this joy didn't last even an hour before plunging into the abyss of despair. To put it simply, after finishing the wedding ceremony, embracing the bride, returning home, and removing her veil, he found it was his former wife (古妻), whom he had kicked out several years ago. In a manner of speaking, the fellow had held his silver anniversary. He had remarried, new-style, the old-fashioned wife he had abandoned years ago.
The fellow, perhaps out of embarrassment, fled to Tokyo the very next day. Without even savoring the joys of newlywed life…
But according to news coming from Tokyo, the fellow makes no mention whatsoever of remarrying his former wife (古妻). Instead, he goes around bragging about his marriage to Song Sŏnbi and extolling her virtues (美德). Furthermore, he apparently spins various tales about how his former wife came and interfered with the wedding, causing trouble. If he's that good at making things up, he'd succeed as a novelist. But from the fellow's perspective, he probably needs to tell such lies to deceive even himself; otherwise, how could he live with the embarrassment?
Anyway, it’s an interesting story. <78>

**Summary**

The story, narrated by a character named K, recounts the farcical wedding attempts of Kim Ch'ŏlsu, a somewhat naive university student from a moderately wealthy family. Ch'ŏlsu gets engaged to the fashionable but duplicitous Song Sŏnbi, unaware she is still involved with her former lover, Mr. Q. On the eve of their meticulously planned "new style" wedding, Ch'ŏlsu discovers Sŏnbi with Q, leading to a violent confrontation and the engagement's cancellation. Desperate to avoid social disgrace and save face, especially after boasting to his Tokyo friends, Ch'ŏlsu's family scrambles for a replacement bride. A last-minute arrangement is made, and the wedding proceeds hastily. Only after the ceremony does Ch'ŏlsu realize he has unknowingly remarried his own 'old-fashioned' first wife, whom he had divorced years prior. He flees back to Tokyo in embarrassment, continuing to fabricate stories about marrying Sŏnbi.

This story offers a satirical glimpse into Korean society in the early 1930s, particularly the clash between traditional values and emerging "modern" or Westernized ideals regarding relationships and marriage. Historians might find interesting the commentary on the perceived 'surplus' of unmarried women due to changing marriage ages for men, the critique of both the supposedly liberated "new woman" (represented by Sŏnbi) and the hypocritical, status-conscious "modern man" (Ch'ŏlsu), the depiction of social pressures surrounding marriage, and the casual references to Japanese department stores (Mitsukoshi, Matsuzaka) and colonial institutions (Chosŏnjŏn University). The narrative's tone reflects anxieties and judgments surrounding rapid social change during the colonial period.

結婚式
						金東仁
어떤 날, 어떤 좌석에서, 몇 사람이 모여서 잡담들을 하든 끝에 K라는 친구가 내게 이런 말을 무럿다-
「자네, 김철수라는 사람 아나?」
「몰라」
나는 머리를 기우리며 대답하엿다. 무론 「김」이라는 성이며 「철수」라는 이름은 흔하고 흔한 것인지라 어디서 드른 법도 하되 이 좌석에서 새삼스러히 이아기꺼리가 될만한 「김철수」가 얼른 머리에 떠오르지 않음으로...
「아마 모르리. 지금 조도전대학 재학생이니까...」
「모르겟네」
「송선비하는 녀자는 하나?」
「몰라. 아 가만잇게. 뭘 하는 녀잔가?」
「X유치원 보모」
「응. 생각나네. 아주 멋쟁이」나는 언젠가 유치원 련합 운동회에서 본 긔억을 이르키며, 그 많은 관중 앞에서 필요 이상의 멋을 부리며 도라가든 어떤 보모을 머리에 그려보면서 머리를 그더졋다.
「그러치. 멋쟁이지-참, 조선엔. 그럼 자네는 김철수하고 송선비하고의 결혼희극도 모르겟네 그려」
「알 수 잇나」
「참 조선엔 웬 과년한 게집애가 그러케도 많은지. 우글우글 한 돈에 다섯 여섯 식」
「그게야 당연한 일이 아닌가. 보통 열 한 두 살이면 장가를 가든 사내들이 인제는 스물이 썩 넘어야 가게가 됏으니깐, 열 한 두 살 난 어린애들이 스물 몇 살까지 자랄 동안은 게집애가 남아 갈게지. 1년에 몇 십 만 명씩은 과년한 처녀가 남아가리. 지금같아서는, 사내 한 명에 녀학생 첩 셋 식을 배당한대두 부족은 없을걸...」
「딱한 일이야. 그러니깐 그런 희극도 생기지」
「대체 자네가 할려는 이아기는 어떤겐가. 매일 신문에 한 두 개씩 나는 것같이 송선비도 역시 모르고 그 김몬가 하는 사람에게 첩으로라도 갓단 말인가?」
「그러면 좋게? 하마터면 김철수가 송선비의 첩이 될 번 햇네 그려. 하하하하」
「그럼 송모에게 본남편이 잇엇단 말인가?」
「하하하하. 이아길 듣게」
K는 앞에 놓인 차를 한 잔 드려마섯다. 그러고 이아기를 꺼냇다.

김철수라는 사람은 근본은 보잘 것이 없으나 돈냥이나 잇는 집 자식일세 그려. 그 돈냥의 덕으로 지금 조도전 대학에-무슨?-그-법과라나 문과라나 좌우간 장래에 목적은 둘째두고 시재 감당하기는 쉬운 과목을 닦는 중이야. 나히는 스물 두 살. 기처한 독신자. 예수교회에 다니는 무신론자.＜72＞
성질로 말하자면 좀 조급하고 과단성이 없으면서도 결긔 잇고 부끄럼을 잘 타고도 그만하면 비위가-더구나 남녀관게의 일에는 비위가 척척하고 신경질이고...
그 자가 녀름 방학에 귀국햇다가 혼약을 하지 않엇겟나. 그 상대자가 송선비네 그려.
본시 송선비라는 녀자는 집은 자긔 어머니가 월자거간을 해서 먹어가는 집안이니깐 재산 형편으로는 보잘 것이 없는데 여기서 녀고보(高普)를 고이 마추고 서울 XX녀학교에까지 다녓는데 더구나 여기서 공부할 때나 서울서 공부할 때나 그 옷차림이며 무엇에던 가장 그-소위 첨단을 거른 녀자란 말이지. 여긔서 치마에 아렛폭까지 대림처 닙기를(즉 서울 류행을 제일 먼저 수입한겔세 그려) 그것도 송선비지. 치마가 길엇다 짧엇다, 저구리가 컷다 작앗다 하는 류행을 제일 먼저 수입해서 실행한 것도 송선비지. (무론 상학할 때는 그러케 못하지만)늘 이름 모를 일본 비단을 몸에 검고 허욕에 뜬 게집애들의 류행의 선봉을 선 것도 송선비지. 내가 즉접 보지는 못햇지만 서울 XX녀학교에 다닐 때도 제일 멋쟁이고 제일 하이칼나댓다나. 팔에는 백금 팔뚝시게, 손까락에는 (단 한 개지만)커다란 금강석을 박은 반지, 언제던 살이 꿰보이는 엷은 비단 양말-대체 그 돈은 어디서 낫느냐 말야. 하기는 XX녀학교에 다닐 때는 그 비용이 모도 그 학교 교장 Q씨에게서 나왓단 말이 잇어. 뿐더러 Q씨와 함께 낙태를 시기려 어떤 시골까지 다녀 왓단 말까지 잇기는 해.
Q씨라는 사람은 자네도 아다시피 유명한 색마가 아닌가. 건강한 육체와 녀자와 많이 사괼 수 잇는 제 지위를 리용해 가지고 유혹, 간통, 강간-온간 인륜에 어그러지는 일을 해 나가는 것으로 유명한 사람이 아닌가. 그러니깐, 그만하면 얼굴도 빤빤하고 역시 버위도 츠근츠근하고 성욕도 세인 선비하고 어느덧 이러케 저러케 됏다는 것도 차라리 당연한 일이겟지.
전문(傳聞)에 들자면 Q씨하고 선비하고의 새는 꽤 열렬하게까지 됏든 모양이야. 여자에서 여자로 잠시도 끊임없이 옴가다니(이하 2줄 판독 불가) 선비가 샘이 너무도 세서 그랫는지 혹은 두 사람의 성욕의 강도(强度)가 꼭 맞아서 그랫는지 그건 판단을 나릴 수가 없지만, 사실 선비가 XX녀학교 재학 중에는 다른 녀자에게는 손을 안대인 모양이야.
이러구러 선비는 그 학교를 졸업하고 이곳 X유치원 보모로 나려 오게 됏네. 무론 울며불며 작별의 일장의 비극이 잇엇겟지. 응? 그 에라 놓아라 난 못놓겟다 양산돌세 그려.
서울하고 예하고가 5백여 리 상거가 된다 하나 매일 가는 천 명 오는 천 명, Q씨하고 선비 새의 로맨쓰도 이곳서 모르는 이가 없으리만치 쪽 퍼젓지. 그러고 사흘 거리로 Q씨가 평양을 나려와서는 선비를 불러다가는 려관에서 묵고 도로 올라가고 햇네 그려. 김철수하고의 혼약이 꼭 그때야.
지금도 나는 선비의 속을 알 수가 없어. Q씨하고 그만치 정분이 낫으면 웨 철수하고 혼약을 햇는지. 무론 Q씨에게야 안해가 잇기야 하지. 하지만 소위 련애에는 국경도 없고 게급도 없고-련애는 온갓 것을 초월한다는 모던껄 송선비양에게야 Q씨에게 안해가 잇고 없는게야 문제가 안될게 아닌가. 죽자사자판에 본처가 다 뭐야. 뭘? 흥? 그랜. 그러케 밖에는 해석할 수가 없겟지. 「운명에 맡기자」 이게 조선 사람의 공통성이니깐. 애정은 애정, 운명은 운명, 이러케 두곤대로 갈라 부치고 놈팽이한테로 시집을 가기로 결심을 한 게겟지.
한데, 그 혼약을 하든 이아기도 장관이야. 수재 김철수군이 매파와 함께 선을 보려 색시 집을 가지를 않엇겟나. 가니깐 좌정을 한 뒤에 이러구 저러구 색시의 어머니가 두어마미 말을 무러 보드니
「신식은 단 둘이서 이아길 하야지」
하더니 매파에게 눈찌름 해서 함께 밖으로 나가드라나. 그런 뒤에 좀 잇다가 참외를 깎아서 한 대접 드려 보내드라나. 그러니깐 공주 낭낭한 음성으로 말슴하시기를,
「좀 가까이 와서 잡수세요」
놈팽이 정신이 절반이나 나갓지. 카페의 웨이트레쓰나 기생이나 데리고 놀아본 녀석이 신식 하이칼라 색시한테 이런말을 듣고보니깐＜73＞ 어리둥절햇단 말이지.
「천만에 천만에」
밑구녕으로 담만 뚜르네. 머리를 푹 숙으리고... 그런 뒤에는 한참 묵언극(黙言劇)이 련속됏네. 신랑 간간 룡안을 구을려서 신부를 보면 신부는 입에 미소를 띠고 뚜러지게 신랑만 바라보겟지. 그 눈을 만나면 신랑은 또 한 번 밑구녕으로 담을 뚫고... 이러다가 갑작이 벗석하는 소리가 들려서 보니깐 신부가 신랑의 가까히 왓드라나.
「좀 내려가세요」
하면서 손까지 덤석 잡으면서. 놈팽이 혼비백산해서 네, 네, 하면서 몸을 조곰 움지기랴니깐 신부는 잡앗든 손을 덜석 놓고 와락하니 신랑에게 달려들더니 키쓰를 퍼부웃기 시작햇다. 「엉야」 「엉야」 소리를 련방 내이면서 뺨, 코, 입, 할 것 없이 키쓰의 소낙비를 나려 부읏는다. 그리고 한참 매달려 그러다가 슬몃이 손을 신랑의 허리춤으로 너어서 쓸어보더라나.
이러케 혼약이 성립됏네 그려. 놈의 눈에는 년과 같은 색시는 이 세상에 다시 없게 비최엇지. 우리같아서는 그런 천박한 게집애는 다시 상종키도 싫겟지만 우리보다는 한층 개화한 놈팽이의 눈에는 그게 모도 천진스럽고 활발하게만 뵐 뿐더러 초면에 이만치 구는 것을 보니낀 벌서 자긔한테 잔뜩 반햇너니라, 이러케까지 생각됏단 말이야.
그 뒤에는 놈, 만날 년의 집에 무처 잇네. 놈은 아직 부끄럼을 타는 놈이라, 색시네 집에서 밤잠까지 자겟다고 졸라보지는 못햇지만 낮에라도 부모는 피해주고 단 둘이 잇으니깐 그 재미가 괜찬튼 모양이야. 눈만 뜨면 처가에 갓다가 밤이 들어야 할 일 없이 어슬렁어슬렁 제집으로 도라오네 그려.
그 동안에도 무론 Q씨야 몇 번을 년을 만나려 나려 왓지. 그러면 년은 약수에 감네 냉청에 감네 하고 약혼자를 쇠기고 하로 이틀씩 나가 자고 드러오고. 그러나 색시한테 잔뜩 반한 놈은 거저 왓작 색시를 신용만 하고 잇지.
그러는 동안에 언젠간 색시는 자긔와 Q씨의 관게를 새서방에게 다 이아기 햇다나.
