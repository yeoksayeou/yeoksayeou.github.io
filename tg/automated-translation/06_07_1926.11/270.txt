My Kŏmun'go
						Ri Ŭn-sang

My kŏmun'go
My heart's kŏmun'go
I have hung it on a tree branch atop the mountain
Wind, you too blow and listen to a tune
Moonlight, you too flow and make the strings resound

I have also laid it by the sea
Waves, you too come and go
With beautiful new melodies each time
Make resound, make resound my kŏmun'go's strings

I have also hung it in the empty air
Flocks of birds, you too, with the tips of your wings
Strike my kŏmun'go and make it resound
So that heaven and earth are buried in joy

Whether on a deep night street piled with snow
Or in the fields on spring, summer, autumn days
My pitiful companions in the world
Pluck pluck my kŏmun'go and make it resound

Sound of Rain

In the middle of the night
The sound of falling rain
A whispering sound

A sound right there
A sound telling me to listen
All night long, until dawn
Its meaning
There's no way to know, but
A sound I cannot bear not to listen to

Sound of rain
A sound I want to listen to
All night long, until dawn

Climbing Namsan (Old Draft)

On Namsan in Seoul
The falling rain
A thousand streams, ten thousand streams
Nine hundred million streams

On the green pine field
Is mischievous rain
On the worn roof of Changch'ungdan
It is sorrowful rain

Looking afar at Norobŏl
A distant road of ten ri
Rain falling on the sand
Rain falling on the river

A boat going and returning
Who is the boatman?
His hemp clothes soaked in sweat
Will get wet again

Looking out at the street
With no one there
Only the falling streams of rain
Are so lonely

Thirty ri wide
This great capital city
Where has the master gone
And only rain falls? <81>

**Summary**

This publication presents three poems by Ri Ŭn-sang (Yi Ŭn-sang). "My Kŏmun'go" personifies the poet's heart or spirit as a traditional Korean zither (kŏmun'go), placed in nature (mountain, sea, sky) and played by natural elements (wind, moonlight, waves, birds) and fellow humans, expressing a desire for connection and resonance. "Sound of Rain" captures the intimate, meditative experience of listening to rain at night, finding its ambiguous sound compelling. "Climbing Namsan (Old Draft)" depicts a melancholic scene of rain falling over Seoul, particularly Namsan, highlighting the desolate state of Changch'ungdan (a patriotic memorial) and the empty streets, evoking a sense of loss or absence in the capital. For historians, the poems, especially "Climbing Namsan," offer insight into the atmosphere of colonial Seoul in the 1920s. The imagery of a deserted capital, a dilapidated national monument (Changch'ungdan), and pervasive sorrow under the rain could reflect sentiments about the loss of sovereignty and the prevailing mood under Japanese rule. The use of older spellings is also of linguistic interest.
