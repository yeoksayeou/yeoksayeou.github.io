Wronged Spirit in a Foreign Land
						Ch'oe Sŏhae

The wretched night had come again. As dusk began to fall, the stars opened their eyes.

Even when her husband was alive, that fellow Yuga would show a suspicious attitude whenever they met quietly at the edge of the field or by the stream. However, with her mountain-like husband beside her, although she felt scared and worried, one side of her heart felt secure. But now, that secure feeling was all gone, and worry, anxiety, and fear occupied her entire heart.

Since her husband passed away, she couldn't sleep alone at night. In the large, isolated house, even the rustling of a mouse made her hair stand on end, and the suspicious behavior of the Chinese landlord, Yuga, gave her constant indigestion. Because of that, Ponggil's grandfather from across the stream slept in the side room every night. Ponggil's grandfather had come from the same hometown as her, and he was also a close friend of her deceased husband Hyŏnsŏn's father. Although such a close old man slept in the side room, her heart did not feel as reassured as when her husband lay beside her. Like a person startled by a pot lid after being frightened by a turtle, she more than once found herself suspecting even Ponggil's grandfather, stealthily creeping over to peek through a crack in the door to check on the quietly sleeping old man's movements, and then letting out a long sigh.

Even so, it would be nice if he came early every day, but he was later than at first. Initially, as soon as the sun set, the old man (Ponggil's grandfather) would come into the room, pipe in mouth, hair tied in a topknot, and lie down. Now, he was often late. It might be because it was the busy autumn season for farmers, but to her, waiting, it felt inconsiderate.

"Aigo, why hasn't he come yet?"
She muttered to herself as she carried the memorial meal offering for her husband's spirit altar into the room. It wasn't that late yet, but the events of the evening flashed through her mind like lightning, and the intimidating face of Yuga, who had been pestering her more aggressively than usual, flashed before her eyes, causing feelings of sorrow <63> and resentment, mixed with a dreadful fear, to well up.

Carrying the meal tray, she entered the dark room, placed the tray before the spirit altar, and opened the window. Outside, the twilight glow descended, making it dim, but the sky was clear, making the room brighter than before. Was the moon already rising? Moonlight began to flow over the peak of the high mountain across the stream. A firefly drew a straight line across the dim yard as it passed. The sound of the rapids, the chirping of insects, the sound of the wind rustling through the millet field by the yard – all sounded desolate.

She placed the rice bowl, soup bowl, side dish plates, and spoon from the tray onto the spirit altar. After sticking the spoon into the coarse mixed-grain rice, she slumped down helplessly before the altar and covered her face with both hands. Her shoulders began to tremble gently like waves, and a choked sob escaped her lips. Was she crying?

The rising moonlight had already bathed half of the opposite peak, but because there was a mountain behind the house, this side was still in shadow. The room felt even more desolate. The ceiling, blackened with soot and dangling with cobwebs, and the walls, stained with dirt and bedbug blood, blended with the dim light as if brushed with ink wash. Only the faint outlines of the spirit altar, hung on the wall like a bird's nest, and her pale shadow sitting below it were visible.

The sounds of insects, the wind, and the rapids were still loud.

Her shoulders, which had been trembling gently and slowly, began to heave more violently, and the choked gasps turned into heartbroken sobs. She forgot everything. The thought of clearing the meal offering, the thought of waiting for Ponggil's grandfather, whether it was night or day – she didn't know. Only sorrow overwhelmed her. Her body and mind seemed submerged in endless, deep blue sorrow, drifting into a distant void. She was always like this when she cried before the spirit altar. Her heart never opened, and her tears never dried. Whether sad or pained, she knelt before her husband's spirit altar and wept. There was no other solace.

After losing her crops to drought and her fields to flood, she had barely survived on bran gruel and *t'osŭrae* (토스래 - food scraped together with a shovel) clothes. Unable to bear the extreme poverty any longer, she and her husband Hyŏnsŏn had come to this Kando region two springs ago. Even after coming to Kando, they had barely sustained their lives until this day as tenant farmers for a Chinese<64> man, enduring all sorts of mistreatment. More than other hardships, the landlord, Yuga, was a widower, and whenever that fellow met her alone, his eyes would glaze over, he'd reveal his yellow teeth in a smirk, and his suspiciously approaching manner was unbearable. However, she couldn't utter a single word of displeasure to Yuga and forced herself to show a pleasant face while subtly avoiding him. There was no other choice. Since they rented fields from Yuga and borrowed grain to eat, if they were kicked out, they would starve. In the wide, wide world, it was so difficult to find a place for two young people. After she started avoiding Yuga like that, his attitude became extremely tyrannical. He would find fault without reason, saying she wasn't weeding properly or demanding repayment of debts immediately. This fault-finding affected not only her but also brought misfortune upon her husband Hyŏnsŏn. From then on, her heart secretly broke. Thinking that her blameless husband was being harassed by that man because of her, she wished she could just disappear. Fearing her husband would be even more upset if he noticed, she never spoke of it, nor did her expression ever betray her feelings. When her husband was severely harassed by that man, she sometimes thought of quietly offering herself to make things easier for her husband, but her firm moral principles did not allow it. Moreover, deceiving her husband was something she couldn't do even if her own flesh were torn apart. Her tenacious life was hard to end, she couldn't tell her husband, and the more she resisted Yuga, the harsher the relentless oppression became day by day... She had no way to resolve her tormented feelings. Amidst this, her husband, whom she had trusted like a great mountain, passed away from illness.

After her husband's death, Yuga's attitude became much freer, and she couldn't even go out to the fields alone during the day. She tried desperately to leave quickly or find a house in the village across the stream to live in, but Yuga wouldn't allow it. Yuga's condition was that she harvest in the autumn, repay the borrowed grain, and then leave his land. Yuga's house was about three *majang* (unit of distance) away, around the mountain bend down below.

Thinking about this and that, her helpless situation felt like a stray dog's leftover rice, and the path ahead seemed pitch black. She didn't know where or how her thread-like life would end. She longed for her homeland. She wanted to live in her familiar hometown, whether starving or fed, but even that was not possible <65> according to her wishes. Being ignorant of everything, even if set down there, she wouldn't be able to find her way; she didn't know where her homeland was or how the roads went. She only thought that beyond Paektusan lay Chosŏn, the land that bore and raised her. That too was because
"That's Paektusan. Beyond that is Chosŏn (朝鮮)."
Her deceased husband had pointed it out while alive, weeding in the field on the mountain behind the house; the memory remained. But what joy would there be in going back to her homeland? Could she bear to turn her back and leave after burying her husband in this remote land, thousands of miles away? This evening, while weeding in the field on the back mountain, she thought of her husband's words and wept sorrowfully, watching the clouds drift over the peak of Paektusan. Just then, Yuga came up behind her and grabbed her waist. She shuddered as if a snake were crawling up her spine and shook him off. Yuga didn't let go easily. At that moment, someone approached from the other side, and Yuga slipped away. Until just a moment ago, that man's shadow lingered before her eyes.

Now, sitting before the spirit altar, all those sorrows surged at once. She let out a wail. She thrashed and wept before the spirit altar. Her chest and throat, which felt constricted as if by smoke and jaggedly torn by a dull knife, seemed to find release, and each hot tear that fell seemed to soothe her burning heart. And it felt as if a gentle man's hand emerged from the dim spirit altar and softly embraced her. It was all an illusion. The thought that her husband was buried in the earth of the lonely forest pricked her heart, and she just wept and wept.

The full moon rising over the eastern hill flowed over the village across the stream and encroached upon this yard as well. The damp grass and grain stalks, wilted by the descending dew, gleamed coldly under the water-like moonlight.

The sound of the rapids, like smoothly flowing tears or perhaps choked, gurgling sobs, and the sound of insects, like a blend of heart-wrenching *tanso* (bamboo flute) and *hojŏk* (conical oboe), still cried out insistently.

As the moonlight flowed over the roof and shone on the yard, the room became bright again.

2

As she writhed and sobbed, a sound from behind startled her, and she turned her head. <66> A pale figure stood blocking the doorway. Her heart sank. In her eyes, it appeared as Yuga with his protruding cheekbones. She didn't know what to do.

"Why are you crying so much?"
Ponggil's grandfather stood facing the door in the yard, looking up at the sky. When she realized it was Ponggil's grandfather, her tense nerves relaxed, but her heart still pounded, and her limbs felt drained as if paralyzed.

"Hegu, Grandpa (클아배)! Heung."
She laughed a hollow laugh. People often laugh inanely after a severe fright, perhaps meaninglessly, or perhaps mocking their own weakness.

"Ho ho, worrying so much won't do!"
Ponggil's grandfather offered comfort, looking up at the moon flowing over the roof. The moonlight flowing over his wrinkled old face looked excessively cold.

"Aigo, Grandpa (클아배)! Hyu... How am I supposed to live?"
Putting the rice bowl from the spirit altar back onto the tray, she lamented her own fate, like a lonely reed under the cold frost.

"How to live? You just live, that's how! Come now, don't be sad. The living must go on living... Hyu..."
Does the old man's back hurt? Stepping onto the wooden floor, he pats his lower back.

"Aigo, God is so heartless! Should have taken me instead of the only son for three generations... *sob*..."
Her words trailed off as she sniffled. Sorrow welled up again. Her husband Hyŏnsŏn was the only son for three generations. When her husband lay ill, she always prayed.
"May the mountain spirit and God look down and have mercy. Even if you take me instead, please help our master, our husband who is the only son for three generations, so that his line (代數) is not broken."
Every dawn, she prepared rice and went to the back mountain to pray. But in the end, she – who wished for death – lived, and her husband's life, which she had wished and wished for, ended. <67> Her wish to save her husband, offering her own life, was her true heart.

"What can be done? It can't be helped... You saw too, will I live? What son of mine... because this wretched throat is like paste... still trying to live, came to this wretched land (地), and died by the knife of that bandit (*hŭngŭjŏk* 마적) scoundrel... And yet, I'm still alive like this..."
The old man, sitting inside smoking his pipe, let out a deep sigh and looked outside. Perhaps the horrific scene from that time flashed before his eyes; he frowned his brow, dimly visible in the gloom, and blinked as if wishing everything would disappear. Truly, two years ago, after Ponggil's father (the old man's son) was stabbed to death by bandits while cultivating opium, the old man's hair had turned whiter.

"Aigo, Grandpa, I just wish I were dead!... Why was I born with such a fate (八字)? I have no desire to live at all..."
She sat near the door after finishing the dishes, speaking listlessly, and gently touched her lower abdomen. Her belly was not empty. That was what kept her alive. A listless encounter when her husband was ill had eventually planted a new life inside her belly. The reason she had dragged her life along until this day was because of that. Her sole devotion was to carefully raise this one speck of her dead husband's bloodline so that his lineage would not end. And the reason she couldn't run far away despite being harassed by Yuga was because of her husband's grave. If she died, she would die here and be buried next to her husband; she couldn't bear to leave his grave desolate.

"Is it that easy? Dying isn't easy!"
The old man's voice resonated like the admonition of an enlightened sage.

There was silence for a while.
The wind, rustling through the field in front of the yard, blew into the house.

"Grandpa, let's sleep now!"
Sitting quietly, she looked at the old man sitting in the side room and closed and latched all the open doors.

"Ung, let's sleep... Uhum... Ung..." <68>
The old man also closed his door and lay down.

"Grandpa, lock the door securely."
She instructed as she closed the door between the rooms and lay down on the heated floor, still dressed.

"Ho ho, what thief would come to our house!"
The unknowing old man just laughed it off.

All around was quiet. Had the moon already climbed to the middle of the sky? Light, clear as water, began to flood the lower part of the window. The room was bright like the approaching dawn. The chirping sounds – *ttoruruk jjiljjil ssaekssaek* – of nameless insects from front and back, and the sound of the rapids in the front stream, sounded one pitch higher.

Exhausted from the day's troubles and prolonged weeping, she felt drowsy as soon as she lay down, her limbs heavy. But once she lay down, the awaited sleep did not come; instead, this thought and that kept her eyes wide open. She closed her eyes. Her husband's ailing appearance came to mind, and the scene of his death appeared.

"Yeobo!" (term of endearment for spouse)
Her husband, lying painfully propped on a pillow, barely suppressed the phlegm rising in his throat, called her, then grasped the hand of her, who was sobbing again, saying,
"Yeobo, why are you crying? Don't cry... Ung..."
He forced a painful smile. Even as his breath nearly stopped, her husband told her,
"When I die, please return to your home country! I won't resent you at all, so find another husband, please, please, have sons and daughters and live well... Yes? Unghŭ... Ung... I truly did too many wrong things to you! Bringing you to this barbarian land and making you suffer... Hyu... If we are reborn in the next life, perhaps then we can meet..."
And with a gasp, his breath stopped. When all this rose before her eyes, she reached out her arms to embrace her husband tightly and opened her eyes. But what her arms embraced was her own chest, and what her eyes saw was the window. Had her husband truly died? It felt as if he had just gone on a long trip. But the scene of his death resurfaced, and the memory of burying him in the cold, cold earth did not <69> affirm that he was alive. She turned over, closing her eyes tightly, trying not to see or think about anything. Now, the pillow felt heavy, a burning heat spread through her body, and her eyelids felt unbearably heavy, like thousands of pounds. She snapped her eyes open again.

The moon already shone halfway across the window. Was that a snake hanging like lace from the shadow of the eaves? It swayed whenever the wind blew. The wind rustled softly, shaking the ears of grain in the millet and sorghum fields. The sound of the ears brushing against each other evoked a deeper sense of sorrow. The grain ripens. Nature is always nature. Whether people die or grieve, nature proceeds on its path without the slightest hesitation. The seeds sown and cultivated with her husband had already grown and ripened. Oh, where had her husband gone? Would I eat this ripened grain alone? Thinking this, her heart felt heavy, and tears welled up. She didn't bother wiping the tears that dripped down, hotly soaking the pillow, but gazed blankly at the window. The moonlit window (月窓), reflected in her tear-filled eyes, looked like a lake under a sorrowful moon, a mysterious world vast and endless. Her body and spirit seemed submerged in it, drifting endlessly into some world neither cold nor hot, neither bright nor dark. There was nothing there. Sadness, joy, pain – all sensations vanished. It was like a dream. The tears that had poured like a spring from her eyes stopped. Her eyes gradually dried. But her vision did not perceive anything clearly. The moonlight on the window, which had been visible, albeit dimly through tears, was now unseen. Only something – something black, like a club – seemed to wriggle. Her eyes focused intently on the shadow. As her pupils converged towards her nose (鼻), the shadow multiplied and grew immense, then it spread out dead and gathered, gathered and spread out. When it wriggled up, down, forward, backward, and sideways, it looked like a disgusting, terrifying snake, and when it contracted suddenly, it looked like a hideous face. As this happened, her consciousness, which had for a moment forgotten even its own existence, gradually became aware of something. Her eyes widened fully, her lips twitched convulsively, and her face turned pale. <70>

Protruding cheekbones, red eyes, yellow teeth... As the thought reached this point, she cried out,
"Uung!"
Shivering violently, she shot upright. As she sat up abruptly, fire flashed in her eyes, the world suddenly went black, and she collapsed on the spot.

"Uhm... Ung..."
Perhaps hearing the sound of her collapsing in his sleep, Ponggil's grandfather in the side room stopped his snoring abruptly, coughed, and then resumed snoring intermittently.

Regaining consciousness after a while, she looked around from where she had fallen. Moonlight still flowed through the window, and the room was still bright. It was all an illusion. What her eyes had misread was the shadow of the dangling decoration (*saekki*) at the edge of the eaves projected onto the window. She hadn't realized it. She felt simply as if she had woken from a terrifying dream. A renewed sense of fear arose. All the shadows in the room felt like menacing eyes and mouths.

She closed her eyes again, trying to fall back asleep.

Struggling and struggling, just as she was about to drift off to sleep, she was startled awake by some sound. She couldn't see anything, nor could she hear any sound. Forcing herself to calm her inexplicably pounding heart, she carefully looked around the room again while still lying down.

The moonlight flooding the window was brighter than before. Now the shadow of the eaves had disappeared. Seeing the moonlight streaming thickly through the open window frame, falling near where she lay at the warmer end of the floor, she knew the night was nearing dawn. The house was bright enough to thread a needle. Nothing else was visible. The only sounds were the usual chirping of insects and the roar of the rapids. Even the sound of the frequently blowing wind was now absent.

She closed her eyes, but then, driven by a nagging thought and a terrifying feeling, she instinctively opened them again and scanned the room. Her fear intensified, making her reluctant even to breathe loudly. The sound of Ponggil's grandfather snoring in the side room was now inaudible. The snoring, which had been annoying earlier in the evening, was now something she longed for. If only that sound <71> were present, it would still be a human sound, offering some sense of companionship, but even that was gone, making the space beside her feel terribly empty (*hisŏnghŏsŏng*), and the absurd thought that the old man might have died also arose. As that thought arose, the image of Ponggil's grandfather's corpse, with his broad forehead and sunken eyes, seemed to appear before her, making her even more frightened. Her nerves were stretched to the limit. It felt as if terrifying hands and eyes were moving and watching from this dim corner and that, and all the horrible and frightening stories stored in her memory—stories of dead people, thieves, ghosts, goblins (*tokkap*)—rushed forth, making it unbearable. When a great, unknown calamity approaches, a person's spirit grows heavy beforehand.

"Grandpa..."
She started to call the grandpa (클아배) sleeping in the upper room but stopped abruptly. She felt sorry to wake the soundly sleeping old man. If her husband were beside her at such a time, how secure she would feel, and what would it matter if she woke him a thousand, ten thousand times? The memory of him escorting her even to the outhouse when he was alive surfaced clearly. The sorrow of widowhood surged again.

"..."
Hearing a strange sound, she strained her ears again. It really felt like something was somewhere. A rustling sound, perhaps footsteps, perhaps the wind, she couldn't tell. Then, did it fade away, barely audible? Keeping her ears strained, she watched the window, which seemed almost torn by the excessive moonlight. It was so quiet she could hear the pulsing throb in the arteries near her ears. After a long moment—
"Rustle."
Along with the sound of footsteps, a black shadow—a human head—flickered against the window and disappeared.

"Aha..."
Her heart pounded, the blood in her limbs seemed to rush and congeal thickly, and the realization of what it was flashed instantly through her mind.

"Grandpa! Ponggil's Grandpa (봉길너 클아배)!" <72>
Her calling voice trembled violently. It lacked strength. It was the voice of someone losing their mind.

"Aigo, Grandpa!"
She crouched down as if sinking into the ground, trembling uncontrollably.

"Ung-ŭ... Ung... Uhm... What's wrong?"
The old man's voice, woken from a light sleep, was drawling.

"Something's outside!"

"What's come? Go back to sleep. What would come while I'm here? Uhm."
The old man still replied slowly and then cleared his throat deeply.

"No, really, something came..."
Her voice was on the verge of tears.

"What came... Ek... Go back to sleep."
The old man mumbled as if annoyed. At his words, she couldn't say anything more. She fretted alone. Would a chick that saw a hawk flash in the sky be more frightened than this? Would a puppy placed before a lion be more terrified? Even with a person sleeping in the next room, it was useless! As she agonized alone for some time, the window darkened, and this time the shadow of a full human figure blocked it completely. She yanked desperately at the door latch.

"Heung, Aigo... Grandpa! Aigo, look!"
Her eyes, screaming half in tears—her eyes watching the window where the shadow flickered—were already half rolled back.

"What is it, what's wrong?"
The old man opened the door between the rooms, seemingly annoyed. At that moment, the shadow lingering outside the door grabbed the door and entered the house. The door latch snapped off with a clang and fell.

It was Yuga—the Chinese landlord. Her intuition was correct.

"Who is this scoundrel..."
As the old man (Ponggil's grandfather) started to rise, his head was split in two by the flashing axe (*tokki*) in Yuga's hand.

"Kkŭk... Ŭŭk..."
Letting out a mournful cry, the old man collapsed on the threshold, hot blood pouring from his head. Seeing this, she, governed by a strength she didn't know she possessed, ran out into the yard. But before she could step down from the wooden veranda, she was seized by Yuga's strong hands. Yuga, trembling fiercely, grabbed her waist.

"You bastard! You barbarian!"
Her fear turned into fury. Malice surged up to her throat; she wanted to chew Yuga up and swallow him. But Yuga held her waist and dragged her towards the room.

"Kill me, you bastard! Barbarian!"
Trying not to be dragged inside, she collapsed onto the ground, bracing her feet against the earthen floor and screaming furiously. Seeing her collapsed on the ground and struggling, Yuga smirked and threw his weight onto her. As Yuga pressed down on her, wrestling, he suddenly cried out,
"Aya... ya..."
Screaming as if his bones were numb, he jumped away.

"Ung... You barbarian bastard... Does your bitten-off nose hurt that much? It's not over yet! To avenge my grudge against you scoundrel!"
She chewed fiercely on the piece of Yuga's nose she had bitten off. Yuga, clutching his nose with both hands, writhed in agony, then suddenly turned and went back inside the house. When he reappeared, his hand held the axe that had split the old man's head. The axe, following Yuga's hand, descended onto her lower back.

"Ŭngŭk... Kill me! Kill me... Barbarian! I don't resent my death, but it's unbearable that my husband's bloodline will disappear! Aigo, my master (husband)! Ŭngŭk, kkŭk..."
Cut in two, she cried out one last time, and her breath stopped.

Yuga's shadow disappeared. The hot blood flowing onto the cold ground steamed in the chilly moonlight and congealed.

All around was quiet. It was not yet dawn. The moon setting in the western sky, along with the choked sound of the rapids and the crying insects, seemed to mourn the lonely, helpless, wronged spirits. All the sounds and the light were that desolate. - October 3, 1926, 2 AM - <74>

**Summary**

This short story by Ch'oe Sŏhae depicts the tragic fate of a young Korean widow living as a tenant farmer in Kando (Manchuria) in the 1920s. After losing her husband, she lives in constant fear of her predatory Chinese landlord, Yuga. Despite the nightly presence of an elderly neighbor for protection, her vulnerability increases. The story culminates in a brutal night attack where the landlord murders the elderly protector and then kills the widow after she fiercely resists his assault, biting off his nose. She dies lamenting the loss of her unborn child, her husband's only heir.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly illustrates the extreme hardships and dangers faced by Korean migrants in Manchuria during the Japanese colonial period. It highlights their economic precarity as tenant farmers, their vulnerability to exploitation and violence (specifically sexual violence against women) by local landowners, the lack of effective protection, and the profound sense of isolation and longing for their homeland, coupled with the inability to return. The intense focus on the widow's psychological terror and desperate resistance underscores the personal trauma within the broader context of displacement and colonial subjugation.

필자	崔曙海
기사형태	소설

異域寃魂
						崔曙海
원수에ㅅ 밤은 또 닥처왓다. 땅ㅅ검이 들기 시작하면서 별들은 눈을 떳다.
남편이 잇슬 때에도 그 놈의 유가가 밧머리나 개울가에서 조용이 만나면 수상스런 태도를 보엿다. 그러나 태산가튼 남편이 겨테 잇스니 무섭고 걱정은 되면서도 마음 한 편이 든든하엿지만 지금은 든든한 마음은 다 슬어지고 걱정과 근심과 두려움이 온 마음을 차지하엿다.
그는 남편이 세상을 떠난 뒤로 밤마다 혼자 자지 못하엿다. 크고 외따른 집에서 쥐만 밧삭해도 머리끄티 줏볏하는데 지주되는 중국사람 유가의 행동이 수상스러워서 체증이 내리지 안핫다. 그때문에 밤마다 개울 건너 잇는 봉길의 할아버지가 방에서 잣다. 봉길의 할아버지는 그와 한 고향에서 들어 왓고 또 그의 죽은 남편 형션의 아버지와 막역한 친구엿다. 그처럼 친한 녕감이 방에서 자것마는 그의 가슴은 남편이 겨테 누엇슬 때처럼 누굴하지 안핫다. 자라 보고 놀란 가슴이 소뚜겅을 보고 놀란다는 셈으로 봉길의 할아버지까지 의심이 벗석 들어서 가만 가만히 기어가서 문틈으로 고요히 자는 녕감의 동정을 살피고는 한 숨을 화- 쉬인적이 한 두 번이 아니엇다.
그런대로 매일 일쯕이나 왓스면 조흐련만 처음 보다는 떠젓다. 처음에는 해만 떨어지면 늙은 녕감(봉길의 할아버지)이 기단 대를 물고 민상투 바람으로 방에 와서 들어 눕더니 이제는 느저서 오는 때가 만핫다. 때가 농가의 밧분 가을이니 그러키도 하겟지만 기다리는 그에게는 야속스럽게 생각되엇다.
『에구 어째서 지금도 안오는가?』
그는 남편의 령좌에 올릴 상식을 들고 방으로 들어가면서 혼자 뇌엇다. 아직은 그리 늣지 안핫것만 저녁편 일이 머리속에 번개처럼 언득하자 다른 때보다 더욱 우악스럽게 졸르는 험상스런 유가의 나티 눈 아페 언득 떠올라서 설ㅅ고＜63＞ 윈통한 가운대도 무시무시한 생각이 치미는 까닭이엇다.
상식상을 들고 컴컴한 방에 들어선 그는 령좌아페 상을 노코 창문을 열엇다. 밧게도 황혼비치 내려서 으스름하나 한울이 맑아서 방안은 아까보다 훤하여 젓다. 벌서 달이 오르는가? 개울건너 노픈 산봉오리 끄테 달비치 흐르기 시작하엿다. 반듸ㅅ블이 어스름한 마당에 일ㅅ자를 그으면서 지나갓다. 여울ㅅ소리, 버레소리, 마당ㅅ가 조바틀 스처오는 바람 소리가 처량이 들럿다.
그는 상에서 밥그릇, 국그릇, 반찬접시 수저를 령좌에 올려노앗다. 우시시한 조밥에 술까락을 박아논 그는 령좌아페 시름업시 주저안자서 두 손으로 나틀 가렷다. 그의 두 억개는 고요히 물ㅅ결을 치더니 목메인 느낌이 입속으로 흘러 나왓다. 그는 우는가?
점점 솟는 달비츤 건너편 봉오리를 절반이나 물들엿 것마는 집 뒤에 산이 잇서서 이편은 아직도 그늘이다. 방안은 한층 으슥하엿다. 거으름에 깜아케 된 거미줄이 넌들넌들한 천정과 몬지와 빈대 피가 얼룩얼룩하던 벽은 수묵을 끼언진듯이 으슥한 한비체 조화가 되엇다. 비닭의 집 가티 벽에 달아 노흔 령좌와 그 아래 주저안즌 그의 희슥한 그림자만은 륜곽이 흐미하다.
버레소리 바람ㅅ소리 여울ㅅ소리는 의연히 요란하엿다.
고요히 천천히 물결치던 그의 억개는 점점 몹시 올으내리고 흙흙하던 느낌은 목메인 울음으로 변하엿다. 그는 모든 것을 이젓다. 상식을 물릴 생각 봉길 할아버지를 기다리던 생각 지금이 밤인지 나진지 몰랏다. 그저 설음이 복밧첫다. 자기의 몸과 마음은 끗업는 끗업는 풀은 설음속에 쌔어서 아득한 속으로 들어가는 듯 하엿다. 그는 령좌 아페서 우는 때마다 이리하엿다. 가슴 열릴 때가 업섯고 눈물 마른 때가 업섯다. 설으나 괴로우나 그는 남편의 령좌아페 다리를 꿇고 안자서 울엇다. 그밧게 위로ㅅ거리가 업섯다.
그는 가물에 곡식을 일코 홍수에 바틀 일은 뒤로 겨죽과 토스래(삽으로 만 것) 옷으로 겨우 목숨을 이어 가다가 넘우도 기한을 못이겨서 그 남편 현션이와 같이 재작년 봄에 이 간도로 왓다. 간도에 와서도 이 날 이때까지 중국사람의 소작인으로＜64＞ 별별 구박을 다 바드면서 겨우 목숨을 이어왓다. 다른 구박보담도 지주되는 중국사람 유가는 홀아비인데 그 녀석이 늘 고요한대서 만나면 두 눈이 스르르 흐리고 누런 닛발을 들어 내어서 벙긋 웃으면서 수상히 달려 붓는 꼴은 볼ㅅ수 업섯다. 그러나 그는 유가에게 불쾌한 소리 한마디 못하고 억지로 조흔 나츨 보이면서 슬슬 피하엿다. 그럴 수박게 업는 일이다. 그 유가에게서 바틀 어더 부티고 량식을 꾸어 먹는 판이니 쫏겨 나는 때면 굶을 것이다. 넓으나 넓은 천지에 두 청춘을 용납 시키기 그처럼 어려웟다. 그가 그러케 유가를 슬슬 피하게 된 뒤로 유가의 태도는 한껏 횡포하엿다. 기음을 잘못 맨다는 둥 빗을 어서 가프라는 둥 하여 일 업는 생트집을 잡앗다. 그 트집은 그에게만 미칠뿐 아니라 남편 형션이에게까지 앙화가 미치엇다. 그는 그때부터 은근히 가슴이 찟겻다. 자긔때문에 애꾸진 남편까지 그 놈에게 쪼들리는 것을 생각하면 자기 한 몸이 업서저 버리고도 시펏다. 그런 눈치를 남편이 알면 더욱 심사가 상할까 보아서 입밧게 내지도 안핫거니와 얼골빗도 변해 보인 적이 업섯다. 그 놈에게 남편이 몹시 쪼들릴 때면 슬그머니 몸을 허하여 남편의 몸이나 편케 할까하는 생각도 업지 안핫스나 굿세인 그의 정조 철학은 그것을 허락지 안핫다. 더구나 남편의 눈을 속이는 것은 자긔의 고기가 찟겨도 할 수 업섯다. 모진 목숨이 끈키는 어렵고 남편에게 말키도 안됏구, 유가를 대항튕�면 할쓰록 무도한 압박은 나날이 심하구... 그는 민민한 정희를 풀길이 업섯다. 그리던 중에 태산가티 밋던 남편이 병으로 세상을 떠낫다.
남편이 죽은 뒤로는 유가의 태도가 한껏 자유로와서 나제도 동무 업시 바트로 못나갓다. 어서 바삐 떠나던지 그러치 안흐면 물건너 촌에 가서 집을 어더 가지고 살아 볼까하고 애를 썻스나 유가는 허락지 안핫다. 가을에 추수를 하여 꾸어먹은 량식을 갑고 자기 땅에서 떠나라는 것이 유가의 조건이엇다. 유가의 집은 그의 집에서 삼마장쯤 떨어저서 저-아래 산모롱이에 잇섯다.
그런생각 저런생각을 하면 의지가지업는 자기 신세가 개밥에 도토리 갓기도 하고 만흔 압ㅅ길이 캄캄하엿다. 실낫가튼 목숨이 어대서 어떠케 될런지 몰랏다. 고국이 그리웟다. 굶으나 먹으나 낫닉은 고향에서 살고 시펏스나 그조차 뜻대로＜65＞ 되지 안핫다. 아무것도 모르는 그는 고국이 어대 부텃는지 길이 어떠케 낫는지 들어내 노아도 못차저갈 것이다. 백두산 아페는 자기를 나하서 길러준 조선이 잇거니 생각할 뿐이다. 그것도
『저게 백두산이오. 저 아픈 죄선(朝鮮)이오』
하고 죽은 남편이 살아서 집 뒤 산 바테서 기음맬때 가르처 준 기억이 남아잇는 까닭이엇다. 그러나 고국으로 간다한들 무슨 재미 잇스랴? 천애만리에 남편을 뭇고 참아 발ㅅ길이 돌아질까? 그는 오늘 저녁때 뒤ㅅ산 바테서 기음을 매다가 남편의 말을 생각하고 백두산 머리에 넘는 구름을 보면서 설ㅅ게 울엇다. 그런데 유가가 뒤에 와서 허리를 안앗다. 그는 등ㅅ골에 배암이 오르는 듯이 몸서리를 치면서 몸을 뿌리첫다. 유가는 좀처럼 노치안엇다. 그때 마츰 저편에서 인적이 잇서서 유가는 슬적 가버렷다. 아까까지도 그 놈의 그림자가 그의 눈 아페 어른거렷다.
이제 령좌 아페 안즈니 그 모든 설음이 한꺼번에 치밀엇다. 그는 목을 노하 울엇다. 령좌 아페서 몸부림을 하면서 울엇다. 연기가 팽팽 돌고 무듼 칼로 찍찍 찟는 듯하던 가슴과 목구녁이 시언이 풀리는 듯하며 뜨거운 눈물이 빠지는 족족 뜨거운 마음을 눅이는 것 가탓다. 그리고 어득한 령좌에서 부드러운 사내의 손이 나와서 슬그머니 안아 주는 것 갓다. 모든 것은 한 공상. 남편은 적적한 숩 속 흙에 무치엇거니하는 생각이 가슴을 뜩금거리게 하여 그저 그저 울엇다.
동산우에 솟으는 보름달은 건너편 마을에 흐르고 이편 마당까지 범하엿다. 축은이 내리는 이슬에 후질근한 풀과 곡식ㅅ대들은 물가튼 달빗 아래 싸늘이 빗낫다.
철철철 순스럽게 나오는 울음 갓기도 하고 꺽꿀렁 꽐꽐, 목메인 곡소리가튼 여울ㅅ소리와 애끈는 단소와 호적을 어울타는 듯한 버레소리는 의연이 우지짓는다.
달비치 집웅에 흐르고 마당에 비최임을 딸아서 방안은 다시 훤하여젓다.

어느새 창문에는 달이 절반 넘어 비최엇다. 레스꼿 가튼 첨하 그림자에 구렝이처럼 달린 것은 색기가 드리운 것인가? 바람소리 나는 때마다 흔들거렷다. 바람이 스르르 시치어서 조와 기장 파테서 곡식 이삭이 흔들린다. 그 이삭과 이삭이 머리를 치는 소리에는 아쉰 생각이 더 떠올랏다. 곡식은 익는다. 자연은 언제나 자연이다. 사람은 죽거나 설어하거나 자연은 조곰도 주저치안코 제 걸음을 것는다. 남편과 가티 갈고 뿌린 씨가 어느새 자라서 익엇다. 오오 남편은 어대로 갓는가? 저 익은 곡식은 나 혼자 먹는가? 생각하니 가슴이 뿌지지 하면서 눈물이 핑그르 돌앗다. 그는 방울방울 흘러 나려 벼개를 뜨겁게 적시는 눈물을 씨스려고도 하지안코 창문을 물그럼이 보앗다. 눈물 어린 눈에 비최인 달창(月窓)은 우수달아래 호수ㅅ물 가티 창망하여 가도 업고 끗도 업는 신비의 세게 가탓다. 자긔의 몸과 정신도 거긔 쌔여서 춥지도 더웁지도 밝지도 어둡지도 안흔 어떤 세계로 끗업시 끗업시 쌔여드는 것 가탓다. 거긔는 아무 것도 업섯다. 슬픔도 기쁨도 괴롬도-모든 감각은 스러젓다. 꿈 속 가탓다. 두 눈에서 샘가티 쏘다지든 눈물은 거첫다. 두 눈은 점점 말랏다. 그러나 그의 시각은 모든 것을 깨닷지 못하엿다. 아까는 눈물에 어리어서도 희미하게나마 보이던 창문의 달비치 지금은 보이지 안핫다. 다만 무엇이-방맹이만침한 검은 것이 꿈틀꿈틀하게 보일뿐이엇다. 그의 두 눈은 그 그림자를 점점 노렷다. 노리던 두 눈동자가 코(鼻)를 중심으로 모아들어서 모듧떠진 때에는 그 그림자가 수업시 만하지고 커지더니 그 놈이 죽 퍼젓다가는 모아들고 모아들엇다가는 퍼젓다. 그것이 꿈틀거리면서 우로 아래로 아프로 뒤로 양여프로 퍼질 때면 슧�글징글하고 무시무시한 구렝이 갓고 그것이 확 모여든 때면 험상한 얼굴이 돼 보엿다. 이러케 되자 한참 자기의 존재까지 이젓던 그의 의식은 점점 무엇을 의식케 되엇다. 그의 눈은 한껏 커지고 입ㅅ술은 경련적으로 실룩하면서 나비치 푸르럿다.＜70＞
불숙한 광대뼈, 벍언 눈, 누-런 닛발...생각이 이에 미츠자 그는
『으응』
부르르 떨면서 벌떡 일어섯다. 벌떡 일어슨 그는 두 눈에 불이 번적하자 갑작이 천지가 앗득하여 그 자리에 쓸어젓다.
『으흠...응...』
그가 쓸어지는 소리를 잠ㅅ결에 들엇는지 방에서 자던 봉길의 할아버지는 골던 코를 뚝 끄치고 기침을 하더니 다시 코를 드믄드믄 골앗다.
한참만에 정신을 채린 그는 쓰러진채 사면을 돌아보앗다. 창문에는 여전히 달비치 흐르고 방안은 여전히 훤하엿다. 모든 것은 착각이엇다. 그의 눈에 엇보인 것은 창문에 비최인 첨하 끗 색기 그림자엿다. 그는 그런 줄 몰랏다. 그는 그저 무서운 꿈을 깬 것 가탓다. 새삼스럽게 무서운 생각이 일엇다. 방안의 모든 그림자는 흉악한 눈 갓고 입 가티 느껴젓다.
그는 다시 잠을 들려고 눈을 감앗다.

3
애쓰고 애써서 겨우 잠이 들락말락 하엿던 그는 무슨 소리에 소스라처 깨엇다. 아무 것도 보이지 안커니와 아무 소리도 들리지 안핫다. 그는 공연히 울렁거리는 가슴을 억지로 진정하면서 누은채 조심스럽게 또 한 번 방안을 돌아 보앗다.
창에는 달비치 아까보담 더 밝게 넘치엇다. 이제는 첨하 그림자도 스러젓다. 뚤허진 창구멍으로 굵게 흘러드는 달비치 그가 누운 웃목 자리아페까지 떨어진 것을 보아서는 밤도 새벽이 가까웟다. 집안은 환하여 바늘귀라도 뀔 것 갓다. 그 밧게는 아무것도 보이지 안핫다. 소리래야 여전한 버레소리와 여울소리뿐이다. 자주 불든 바람ㅅ소리도 지금은 들리지 안핫다.
그는 그만 눈을 감앗다가 그래도 하는 생각과 무시무시한 마음에 본능적으로 또 눈을 떠서 방안을 돌아보앗다. 무서운 증세가 점점 고조되어서 숨도 크게 쉬고 십지 안핫다. 방에서 자는 봉길의 할아버지의 코고는 소리는 지금은 들리지 안핫다. 초저녁에는 귀치 안턴 코고는 소리가 지금 와서는 그리웟다. 그 소리나마＜71＞ 낫스면 그래도 사람의 소리인지라 의지가 될 것 가튼데 그것조차 업스니 겨티 몹시 히성허성하고 또 그 녕감이 죽지나 안핫나 하는 얼토당토안흔 마음까지 치밀엇다. 그런 생각이 치미니 눈 아페 이마가 넙적하고 눈이 쑥 들어간 봉길 할아버지의 죽엄이 뵈는 것 가타서 더욱 무서웟다. 그의 신경은 극도로 긴장되엇다. 어득한 이 구석 저 구석에서 무서운 손과 눈이 움즉이고 노리는 것도 갓고 죽은 사람의 이야기, 도적놈의 이야기, 귀신의 이야기, 독갑의 이야기하여 기억속에 남앗던 모든 흉하고 무서운 이야기는 다 줄달음으로 떠올라서 참을 수 업섯다. 아지못할 큰 변이 닥치는 때에 사람의 령감은 미리 무거워지는 것이다.
『클아...』

『...』
무슨 이상한 소리에 그는 다시 귀를 기우리엇다. 암만해도 어대 무에 잇는 것 갓다. 부시럭하는 소리는 자최ㅅ소리 갓기도 하고 바람ㅅ소리 갓기도 한데 알 수 업다. 그러며 그것이 들린둥 만둥하고 살아저 버렷는가? 그는 귀를 기우린채 달비치 넘우도 실려서 찌저질듯한 창문을 주의하여 보앗다. 툭툭하고 귀밋 동맥치는 소리가 들리도록 고요하엿다. 이윽해서엇다.-
