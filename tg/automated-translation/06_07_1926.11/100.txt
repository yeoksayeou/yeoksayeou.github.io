필자	春園
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

禪婆
						春園

I went to Sŏgwang Temple (釋王寺) to rest my ailing body. It was Ch'usŏk time, when the pine mushrooms (松茸) were in full season, though the autumn leaves had not yet turned. Although they said the "medicinal waters had ripened," there were no visitors.

The owner of the inn where I stayed was an old woman (老派), perhaps sixty years old. Known for being very kind, she was said to have experienced quite a lot in her life. She mentioned that eight years prior, she had lost her son, who was over thirty, and had wanted to die herself.

That night, the old woman came to my room and, after lamenting her various hardships, told me the story of how she lost her eldest son, wanted to follow him in death, but ultimately did not die. Her story went roughly like this:

With no husband and no property, relying solely on her one son, losing him made the whole world seem empty. She had absolutely no thought of living, only of dying. So, she resolved to die and held the poison, but then remembered the debt her son had incurred before his death.

The thought occurred to her, "They say if you don't repay debts from this life, you have to repay them tenfold in the next. If I die too, who will repay his debt?" At that thought, she threw down the poison cup she held and decided to live. From then on, her single-minded focus became, "I must quickly repay the debt and then die."

She lived on like this for some time. Then one day, the thought suddenly struck her:

"What exactly is a person? What is living, and what is dying? What is this thing that sits here thinking these thoughts?"

From that day on, day and night, in all her activities—walking, standing, sitting, lying down (行住坐臥)—she pondered this question. Sometimes, lost in this thought, she would forget the passage of time and even herself.

Then one day, while crossing the pass behind Unhyŏn Palace (雲峴宮), still absorbed in such thoughts, she suddenly realized that everything before her became bright, and she collapsed on the road.

"Oh, everything is mind (맘)! It is only the mind! What feels pain is this mind; what feels joy is this mind; living and dying are ultimately this one mind! Paradise and hell are not separate places; they are ultimately this one state of mind (맘ㅅ자리)!"

This she realized. And then, her heart filled with overflowing joy.

Continuing on her way over the pass, the son of Lord OOO came by, pulling a rickshaw.

The thought came to her: "The one riding looks like an ox, and the one pulling looks like a bear."

It was this state of mind she realized! And then, her heart filled with overflowing joy.

Continuing on her way over the Kwalli Pass, the son of Lord OOO came by, pulling a rickshaw.

The thought came to her: "The one riding looks like an ox, and the one pulling looks like a bear." All those people bustling back and forth on the main streets of the capital, chasing after money <26> or fame, biting and scratching, weeping and chattering, unable to realize this state of mind—they all seemed laughable and pitiful.

"Ah, Śākyamuni Tathāgata (서가여래) must have seen sentient beings like this, which is why he cast aside even the glorious throne like a worn-out shoe and aroused the great compassion (大慈悲心) to save these pitiable sentient beings. The mind of Śākyamuni Buddha (서가무니부처님) is mind, and my mind is mind, so I too can become like the Buddha. Ah, so this is what 'All sentient beings have Buddha-nature' (一切衆生皆有佛性) refers to!"

she thought.

The old woman said this and smiled faintly.

"Isn't it wonderful? How can even the position of a king compare to this? Someone like me hasn't yet reached the state of Great Awakening and Seeing the Nature (大道見性), but my mind is always peaceful and joyful. Because my mind is at peace, as they say 'A broad mind makes the body healthy' (心廣體胖), my body has become like this too." She touched her plump, healthy-looking body and the new black hair growing in. "My body, which had withered away like a stick after my son died, gradually put on weight like this, and my hair, which had fallen out completely like my knees were bare, is growing back like this. Even my loose teeth have become firm again like this—it's truly wonderful!"

"Therefore, the mind is the most important thing. Aren't all good and evil born solely from the mind (唯心所生)? Sir, you too are suffering from illness like this now, but if you just firmly grasp this state of mind, then with Mind Harmony and Qi Harmony (心和氣和), all hundred illnesses will naturally disappear. If the state of mind is firmly settled, even if a thousand lightnings and a thousand thunderbolts strike upon your head, you wouldn't even blink an eye—so what are mere illnesses? Isn't that right?" She looked at me for a moment.

"What is the root of all sin? Greed (貧), anger (嗔), and ignorance (痴), these three. Lust for wealth, lust for fame, lust for work—that's all greed (탐). Humans can't live completely without desire, but just going along gently, as things come, without being lazy, is enough. Frantically scrambling to make things mine, suddenly, a lot, hastily—it doesn't work that way. Even if it does become mine, what is all that in the end? If you have two million yang of money, can you take it with you? Even if you die as an emperor of ten thousand chariots (萬乘天子), don't you just rot the same? No matter how high your name is, does it reach the three thousand great thousand worlds (三千大千世界)? Yet foolish sentient beings, like swarms of ants, strain their heads trying to make things theirs, and through that, it becomes sin and illness. They cast aside everything they struggled to make theirs and just leave empty-handed—isn't that right? Don't anger (嗔心) and ignorance (痴心) ultimately arise from greed (貪心) too? Getting angry, hating, resenting..."

This old woman says she has been practicing Zen meditation (참선) for eight years.

"These words are not meant to be spoken lightly. Just because I say them to someone like you, Sir, does everyone understand? This state of mind is not something given by others; it is something only oneself can find and know. Only those who have accumulated good roots (善根) in past lives and strive for good in this life can find it—isn't that right?"

I received a profound lesson from this old woman. My going to Sŏgwang Temple, this old woman speaking such words to me, and my being moved upon hearing these words—perhaps it was all due to karmic connection (인연). <27>

**Summary**

The author, Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu), recounts his encounter at Sŏgwang Temple with an elderly innkeeper. Devastated after her son's death, the woman contemplated suicide but chose to live to repay his debts. This led her to question the nature of existence, eventually culminating in a sudden enlightenment experience where she realized that all phenomena, including suffering and joy, life and death, heaven and hell, originate solely from the mind. This realization brought her profound peace, joy, and even physical rejuvenation. She explains her philosophy, rooted in Buddhist concepts like the mind being paramount, the three poisons (greed, anger, ignorance) being the source of suffering, and the universality of Buddha-nature, contrasting worldly pursuits with inner peace.

From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it showcases a personal narrative of finding solace and meaning through a folk interpretation of Buddhist/Zen philosophy during a period of significant upheaval in Korea. Written by a prominent intellectual like Ch'unwŏn, it reflects an engagement with spiritual themes as potential responses to personal loss and perhaps the broader anxieties of colonial modernity. The old woman's critique of worldly desires (wealth, fame) might resonate with sentiments of disillusionment or a search for alternative values under Japanese rule.

그 로파가 밤에 내 방에를 차자와서 여러가지로 신세타령을 한 끝에 자긔가 장남한 아들을 잃어버리고 딸아죽으려 하다가 안 죽은 이야기를 하였다. 그 이야기는 대강 이러하다.
남편도 없고 재산도 없고 오직 아들 하나만 믿고 살던 몸이 아들을 잃어버리니 세상이 모도 비인듯하여 살ㄹ 생각은 털끝만콤도 없고 죽을 생각만 있었다. 그래서 죽으랴고 약을 들고 생각하여 보니 아들이 지고 죽은 빗이 있는 것을 생각하였다.
