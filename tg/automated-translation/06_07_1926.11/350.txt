Bloodline of the Korean People
						Chŏng Il-u

Tan (檀) means "Han," and the ethnic group is "kyŏlle" (결레); thus, the lineage of the Hangyŏlle (한결레, Korean people) is broadly divided into three branches: Southern (南北), Northern (正宗), and Orthodox (正宗).

What is called the Orthodox lineage (正宗) refers to the direct line ethnic group. Hae Tongmyŏng (解東明), the founder of Koguryŏ (高句麗), was the son of Tan'gun (檀君) "Han'gŏm" (한검) Haemosu (解慕漱) of Pukpuyŏ (北扶餘). Taejo Choyŏng Taewang (大震 太祖 祚榮大王) "K'ŏhan" (커한) of Taejin (大震) was a blood descendant of Koguryŏ. The ancestors of Yi T'aejo (李太祖) of Chosŏn originated in Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道), which was the Southern Capital (南京) of Parhae (渤海).

The Southern Branch (南族) refers to the Han people (韓族) of the Peninsula, who were divided into the Samhan (三韓), passed through the Three Kingdoms of Silla (新羅), Paekche (百濟), and Karak (駕洛), and then Koryŏ (高麗) reunified and ruled the Peninsula.

The Northern Branch (北族) includes the Yemaek (濊貊), Suksin (肅愼), and Okchŏ (沃沮), who established themselves separately after the Tan'gun of Puyŏ (扶餘).

Yemaek (濊貊)
The commentary in the *Kangmok* (綱目註) states, "Ye (濊) is precisely Maek (貊)," and the *Kang'yŏk Ko* (彊域考) states, "Among the nine types of Maek, Yemaek is one." Thus, Ye and Maek are not two separate states but one ethnic group, sometimes called Ye and sometimes called Maek. The Yemaek centered around the Greater Khingan Range (大興安嶺) and resided in the regions of Chahar (察哈爾) and Rehe (熱河). Among them, the Han'ye (寒濊) gradually moved south, expanded far into the areas of present-day Zhili (直隷), Shandong (山東), Jiangsu (江蘇), Anhui (安徽), and Zhejiang (浙江), and established the Great Xu Empire (大徐帝國), which lasted for 1105 years from the *kabin* year of King Xiao Yi (小乙) of the Shang Dynasty (商王) of China to the *muin* year of Qin Shi Huang (秦始皇). Among their rulers, King Yan (偃王) was the leader of the alliance of Eastern ethnic groups. In Ku County (衢縣), Kŭmhwa Prefecture (金華道), Zhejiang Province (浙江省), there exists the Stele of the Temple of King Yan of Xu (徐偃王廟碑) by Han Yu (韓愈).

Suksin (肅愼)
The *Book of the Later Han* (後漢書) states, "The Suksin were subordinate to Puyŏ (扶餘)." The commentary in the *Records of the Grand Historian* (史記註) states, "Siksin (息愼) is sometimes called Suksin (肅愼)." The commentary in the *Lost Book of Zhou* from the Ji family tomb (汲家周書註) states, "Chiksin (稷愼) is Suksin (肅愼)." The *Yìshǐ* (繹史) states, "Sukkŭn (肅謹) is Suksin (肅愼)." The *Book of Jin* (晋書) states, "Suksin is also known as Ŭmnu (挹婁)." Therefore, Siksin, Chiksin, Sukkŭn, and Ŭmnu are all Suksin. Later, Yelü Abaoji (耶律 阿保機), initially called Khitan (契丹), established the Liao state (遼國). Yelü (耶律, Yeryui) is a phonetic change from Ŭmnu (挹婁, Yeryui), so it actually means "Abaoji the Ŭmnu person."

Okchŏ (沃沮)
The *Yitongzhi* (一統志) states, "The land of Okchŏ is northeast of Chosŏn (朝鮮)." The *Shengjing Tongzhi* (盛京通志) states, regarding Okchŏ Kingdom (沃沮國), "The *History of Liao* (遼史) notes that Haizhou (海州) was originally Okchŏ land." The *Book of the Later Han* states, "North Okchŏ is 800 *li* from South Okchŏ." Although the old location cannot be ascertained today, it is generally considered to be Ningguta (寧古塔). Okchŏ is precisely the Jurchen (女眞), and T'aejo of the Jin Dynasty (金太祖) arose from the Jurchen.

The lands of the Yemaek, Suksin, and Okchŏ are collectively called Manchuria (滿洲) today. T'aejo of the Qing Dynasty (淸太祖) rose suddenly from here.

**Summary**

This article by Chŏng Il-u argues for a unified Korean ("Hangyŏlle") identity rooted in the mythical figure Tan. It posits three major branches of this lineage: the Orthodox (tracing Koguryŏ, Parhae, and Chosŏn founders), the Southern (Samhan, Silla, Paekche, Karak, Koryŏ), and the Northern (Puyŏ descendants like Yemaek, Suksin, Okchŏ). The author connects these northern groups to vast territories, including Manchuria and parts of ancient China (the Xu state), and links them to later powers like the Liao (Khitan), Jin (Jurchen), and Qing (Manchu) dynasties. The piece emphasizes a broad, ancient, and powerful Korean ethno-national lineage extending well beyond the Korean peninsula. For historians, the article is interesting as an example of early 20th-century Korean nationalist historiography during the colonial period. It constructs a narrative of shared ancestry linking Koreans to various Manchurian groups, implicitly challenging Japanese colonial narratives by asserting a distinct, expansive, and historically significant Korean identity and sphere of influence predating modern borders. The specific historical connections drawn (e.g., Yemaek to Xu, Yelü to Eupnu) reflect particular interpretations aimed at bolstering this nationalist view.

한결레의 피줄
						鄭一雨
檀은 「한」이요. 종족은 「결레」니 한결레의 혈통을 南北과 正宗의 三派에 大別하니라.
正宗이라 함은 정통 종족을 이름이니 高句麗의 시조 解東明은 北扶餘 檀君「한검」 解慕漱의 子며 大震 太祖 祚榮大王 「커한」은 高句麗의 血孫이며 조선 李太祖의 先世는 渤海의 南京인 咸鏡道에 發祥하엇고,
南族은 半島의 韓族이 三韓으로 난우어 新羅, 百濟, 駕洛의 三國을 지나 高麗가 半島를 다시 통치하엿고,
北族은 扶餘 檀君의 後에 濊貊, 肅愼, 沃沮가 分立하엿나니
濊貊
綱目註에 曰 濊는 곳 貊이라하고 彊域考에 曰 貊이 九種에 濊貊이 其一이라 하엿스니 濊와 貊이 二國이 아니라 一族을 혹 稱 濊요 혹 稱 貊이라 하엿도다. 濊貊이 大興安嶺을 中心하여 察哈爾와 熱河의 地方에 거하다가 其中에 寒濊가 점점 남하하여 今 直隷, 山東, 江蘇, 安徽, 浙江 등지를 遠拓하고 大徐帝國을 建하여 中國 商王 小乙의 甲寅으로부터 秦始皇 戊寅까지 1105년간을 歷하엿는대 其中에 偃王은 東方 諸族의 盟主이엇나니 浙江省 金華道衢縣에 韓愈의 徐偃王廟碑가 잇나니라.
肅愼
後漢書에 曰 肅愼이 扶餘에 臣屬하엿다 하고 史記註에 曰 息愼을 혹 謂之 肅愼이라하고 汲家周書註에 曰 稷愼은 肅愼이라하고 繹史에 曰 肅謹은 肅愼이라하고 晋書에 曰 肅愼은 一名이 挹婁라 하엿스니 息愼, 稷愼, 肅謹, 挹婁가 다 肅愼이며 후에 耶律(初稱契丹) 阿保機가 遼國을 建하엿스니 耶律(예뤼)는 挹婁(예뤼)의 轉音이니 실은 挹婁人 阿保機라는 말이라.
沃沮 一統志에 曰 沃沮地는 朝鮮의 東北이라하고 盛京通志에 曰 沃沮國은 遼史에 海州가 本 沃沮地라하고 後漢書에 曰 北沃沮가 去南沃沮 8百里니 今에 舊地가 無考나 대저 寧古塔이라 하엿고 沃沮가 곳 女眞이니 金太祖가 女眞에서 起하니라.
濊貊, 肅愼, 沃沮의 地를 今에 통칭 滿洲라 하나니 淸太祖가 이에서 崛起하엿도다.
