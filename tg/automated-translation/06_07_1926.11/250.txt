**Body Text Translation:**
Among All the Winds
						Scotland Burns (Original), Yohan (Translation)

"Robert Burns" is a folk poet of Scotland, famous for the simplicity and innocence of his songs. The translations here are things that the people of that region always sing as songs.

(Of A' the Airts the Wind Can Blaw)

Among all the winds, East, West, South, North,
I like the West wind,
For there lives the maiden
Whom I love the most.
Though woods grow, rivers flow,
And mountains block the way,
Day and night, my heart’s thoughts
Fly to "Jean" (쨘).

Her face, beautiful even like a dew-kissed flower,
Is visible to me,
And the sound of singing birds, to my ears,
Is my beloved’s voice.
Whether by the spring’s edge or seeing petals <76>
Sprouting in the grassy field,
Or when a sweet-sounding bird is heard,
Thoughts of "Jean" (쨘) keep arising.

(Auld Lang Syne)

Old Friends
Should old friends all be forgotten
And not even remembered?
Should old friends and bygone days
Even fade from memory?

Let's commemorate the old times, friend,
Commemorate the old times.
Let's raise a welcoming glass again
And commemorate the old times.

In days past, we climbed the hills
And played picking flowers,
But how much the hardships of life
Have troubled us.

In days past, we frolicked morning and evening
In the river waters,
But the wide sea between us
Now keeps us apart.

Then your hand in mine, <77>
My hand in yours,
Let's hold them together for old times' sake
And raise this glass.

(My Heart's in the Highlands)

Where My Heart Is
My heart is in the Highlands, not here,
My heart is in the Highlands, the land where deer are hunted.
Only there, where the wild deer go chasing the mountain goat,
Wherever I go, the Highlands are where my heart is.
Farewell, my homeland behind, farewell my beloved Highlands,
My land where heroes arose, my magnificent homeland.
Wherever this body may wander, wherever it may go,
Mountains of the Highlands, I will love you forever.

Farewell, mountains of home, covered white with snow,
Farewell, valleys and rivers flowing beneath them. <78>
Farewell, vast forests, deep woods,
Farewell, swift-flowing streams and overflowing floods.
My heart is in the Highlands, not here,
My land where heroes arose, my magnificent homeland.
Wherever this body may wander, wherever it may go,
Mountains of the Highlands, I will love you forever. <79>

**Summary**

This article presents Korean translations of three famous poems by the Scottish poet Robert Burns: "Of A' the Airts the Wind Can Blaw," "Auld Lang Syne," and "My Heart's in the Highlands." The translator, identified as Yohan (요한), introduces Burns as a renowned folk poet of Scotland. The poems explore themes of longing for a loved one separated by distance, nostalgia for past friendships and times, and a deep, enduring love for one's homeland (the Scottish Highlands).

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as an example of the introduction and reception of Western literature, specifically British poetry, in 1920s Korea. The choice of Burns, a poet celebrated for his connection to folk traditions and national identity (Scottish), might have resonated with Korean intellectuals grappling with their own cultural identity under Japanese rule. The themes of longing, separation, and love for a homeland, though set in Scotland, could have held indirect significance for a Korean readership experiencing colonial occupation. The translation style and orthography also provide linguistic data from that period.
