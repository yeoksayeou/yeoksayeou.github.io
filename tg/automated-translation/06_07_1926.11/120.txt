A Brief Study of Blue Widow Folk Songs
						By Yi Ŭn-sang (李殷相)

Foreword

When listening to any melancholic little song (小謠) among the traditional folk songs possessed by our people, we often glimpse the lives of our ancestors within the simple lyrics and plain melody. We can imagine the nature of that time, and not infrequently, we develop a love for history, a reverent heart toward our homeland, and fervent thoughts for our nation.

The saying that poetry is the literature of literature, and folk songs are the poetry of poetry, is something we have long known. Regardless of the form or imagination of any poetry, folk songs should not be treated lightly. I believe that only when literature develops based on this foundation can it attain elegant literary value.

Therefore, if someone claims to like poetry but does not know folk songs, they are merely skimming the surface of poetry. Those who truly wish to savor the inner taste of poetry must seek out the villages (洞里) of folk songs and become intimate with them.

Although we often hear the term "cosmopolitan," I believe that the word "cosmopolitan" cannot exist in reality apart from one's own nation, homeland, and history.

Who would deny that Tolstoy loved Russia (露西亞) and Whitman loved America? Who would not affirm that Bjørnson revived Norway (諾威) and Lessing served Germany?

Did not the Western sage Socrates worry about Greece (希臘), and was not the Eastern sage Confucius (孔子) a man of Lu (魯)? Did not Christ weep for the people of Israel (以色列)?

However, if they had not been one with their own people, their own history, their own land, how could they have shown us the reality of being "cosmopolitan"?

If we grant the foremost position among all arts to folk art (향토예술), then it is undoubtedly true that the foremost position within folk art should be granted to folk songs. Therefore, those who walk the path of art and discuss "cosmopolitanism" should treat these folk songs with even greater reverence.

Ever since the phrase "Literary Market" (文藝市場) emerged, I have always thought this way:
-All arts are like the five grains (五穀).
They grow in the fields and are sold in the market.-
"Art is cultivated in the homeland (鄕土) and sold in the market." This should not be taken lightly.
We cannot help but believe this is an undeniable fact.

There is no need to write an excessively long introduction here, but I want to state beforehand that folk songs are the chief of folk art, and anyone advocating any form of literature <33> must regard these folk songs with respect.

It is doubtless that collections and studies of our traditional folk songs will emerge in the future. This humble writing under the present title aims merely to briefly examine two representative long folk songs (長謠) among the Blue Widow (靑孀) folk songs: "T'an'ga" (嘆歌, Song of Lament) and "Sibiwŏlga" (十二月歌, Twelve-Month Song).

I. Study of T'an'ga (嘆歌考)

If asked where the standard for the "literary value of folk songs" lies, I would answer that I wish to place it "in the emotion from which the folk song, or rather the poem/song, originates."

Why? Because the term "literary" can broadly be rephrased as "emotional," I assert that the literary value can only be considered by discussing the nature of the emotion that produces the folk song.

Therefore, to discuss the literary value of these Blue Widow folk songs, "T'an'ga" and "Sibiwŏlga," the first priority must be to discuss the emotions from which "T'an'ga" and "Sibiwŏlga" originate. Only secondarily and tertiarily can other issues be discussed.

However, for the convenience of argument, I intend to naturally reveal their literary value while briefly examining both their emotions and forms together.

Selling hairpins, selling hair switches,
I bought medicine, they said,
Hanging the medicine pot (약탕관),
I didn't realize his breath was fading.

The entire "T'an'ga" consists of 4-line stanzas based on a 4-4 syllable count like this one. The confession revealed in the first stanza clearly shows a family suffering extreme hardship in reality. It is a family so poor that she had to sell even her own daily personal belongings for her husband's illness. Medicine could not overcome Heaven's will (天運), and finally, her beloved husband departed. This is sung at the beginning—

Though I cry out to the vast heavens (호천망극),
Can the departed soul (魂魄) of my beloved know?
Like stabbing a knife into an unripe watermelon (션수박),
We parted meaninglessly.

No matter how much I call his soul (招魂), no matter how much I grieve (測怛), it seems the soul (魂魄) of my beloved, having already crossed the River of Oblivion (忘河) and become a citizen of the afterlife, does not recognize me who remains. Like cutting into an unripe watermelon (未熟한 西瓜), misfortune (惡運) came to a love that did not last long, leaving nothing but the solitude shared through suffering.

Lying down, can sleep come?
Sitting up, will my beloved come?
Neither sleep nor my beloved comes,
How am I supposed to live?

Her life's desolation and pain continue day and night (晝宵에 連하엿스니). With no joy and no hope, how can she sustain her life? She sings of the difficulty (難) of her remaining years—

The grass blooming on the rocky mountain (석산),
Blooms again every year,
But my departed beloved, why
Cannot bloom again like the grass?

Here, the meter changes to 3-4 syllables. It becomes sadder, sung with a slower rhythm (調子) than the 4-4 meter. When gazing daily at the indifferent nature, one cannot help but feel a darkening (惛暗) of the heart along with the slow weeping (鳴咽) lament. As this emotion and form find their perfect match (맛당함), like the most beautiful gold sand within gold sand (金砂), this single stanza among the long folk song particularly catches our eye (emotion 情感).

My heartless beloved,
Leaving flower-like me behind,
Why does he not come?
For what reason does he not come? <34>

The first two lines use a 3-4 meter, connecting the emotion to the previous stanza's 3-4 meter. The last two lines use a 4-4 meter, pulling the emotion towards the following stanza's 4-4 meter. At the same time, the repetition (反復) of "an'osin'go" (안오신고), meaning "why does he not come?", strengthens the force of the sorrowful tune (애조).

Is it twilight on the wild plains,
He cannot come because the day is ending?
That high peak visible in the distance,
Is the mountain too high for him to come?

Is there white snow on the mountain crest,
He cannot come, blocked by snow?
Is the water too deep for him to come?
Why, oh why, can he not come?

Looking at these two stanzas, they are interpretations of the "an'osin'go" used in the preceding stanza. It is the pain of speculating on various reasons why the departed beloved cannot come. The use of parallelism (대구법) here is skillfully done. Furthermore, to avoid monotony, the last two lines of the second stanza are slightly altered, creating a flawless song (謠) that is worthy of admiration (賞嘆).

Did he meet his parents (父母) on the road,
And is serving (奉養) them?
Did he find an infant's grave (애기장 무덤),
And is preparing medicine of Sambekch'o (三百草 약)?

Here too, the first two lines are in 4-4 meter, continuing the rhythm (調子) of the preceding stanza. The last two lines are in 3-4 meter, foreshadowing the later 3-4 meter section. Simultaneously, it shows an ethical or moral perspective (倫理觀念 내지 道德觀念) that values filial piety (봉양) towards parents, and a humanitarian spirit or sympathy (人道心 내지 동정심) for tragic premature death (夭死), seeking Sambekch'o medicine (三百草 藥) to tend an infant's burial (兒葬) and save lives. It is a stanza that immerses us in desolate sorrow.

In the deep valley of the gold-panning site (채금터),
Did he go to pan for gold (금캐러)?
Finely grinding five-colored stones,
Did he go off as a merchant (장사로)?

This stanza arises from their difficult life (생활난). Did he go to pan gold (採金) to enrich their current life (現實生活)? Or did he finely grind five-colored stones to become a merchant (商賈) and return after earning enough money to escape the poverty (貧窮) of his former life (前生)? This hints at the difficulty of their real lives, while also being considered as another reason why the departed beloved cannot return.

During the seven-year drought (7년대한 가물음),
Is he praying for rain near the Milky Way (銀河水께)?
Seeing the rain preparations (비빹보고) by the mountains and streams (山川까에),
Is he seeking a rain hat (갈모씨)?

Where has he gone that he cannot come?
Did he fall ill again after leaving?
Wearing a cangue, in prison garb (칼을쓰고 옷속에서),
Is he weeping blamelessly (허물업시)?

What is this?
Why can he not come?
On a windy, darkening day,
Only his spirit (혼백) weeps sorrowfully (슬피우네).

Gradually—following the progression of the song (謠)—the process by which the Blue Widow's sorrowful resentment bursts forth uncontrollably with tears becomes fully apparent.

The wind blows high,
High wind, what is the matter?
Deep night of the eleventh lunar month (동지섯달),
Deep night, for what reason?
(4 4) (2 4) (4 4) (2 5) Such a chaotic rhythm (亂調) is created. However, the mixing in of the 4-4 meter emotion shows the difficulty of sudden change, and it also seems to foreshadow (豫示) the sorrowful chaotic rhythm emerging against the backdrop of fierce wind and deep night (風急夜深). <35>

Come, why do you not come?
Come tonight.
In the deep pine forest,
Lying there, why are you silent?
(2 1 4) (3 2) (2 4) (2 1 4) It becomes even more chaotic (亂調) than the previous stanza. However, the fact that the first and fourth lines have the exact same (2 1 4) meter shows that a musical rhythm is naturally established. This is a point that draws particular attention.

Though the road is dark
And the fourth watch (4更) has passed,
Why couldn't your spirit (혼) come?
Come tonight.
(2 4) (2 4) (3 1 4) (3 2) Similar to the previous stanza, it is chaotic (亂調). Here, I find it profoundly moving to discover that while the previous stanza only appealed to the departed beloved's physical remains (골육), this stanza appeals also to his spirit (靈). This love is not merely a sensual, physical love (육적愛) confined to the flesh (골육), but reaches the high, clean, clear soul (영혼), boasting a yearning for sacred love (愛). How much more deeply does this poignant emotion (悵然한 감회) pierce our feelings (정감)?

A tree fallen in the wind,
Does it rise again when it rains?
My beloved, called away by heaven,
Will he return because I weep?
(3 5) (4 4) (5 4) (4 4) This stanza gathers the chaotic rhythm (亂調) of the previous stanzas and shows a return towards the basic 4-4 meter (本格 調子). It is a song of self-consolation (자위) from intense grief (배통). After pondering various reasons why the departed beloved does not return—that is, the reason of "Once dead, one never returns" (人生死去不復返)—she finally realizes he simply does not come for no specific reason. After lamenting the intense grief with a chaotic rhythm, she now enters a turning point, a stanza of self-consolation and regaining composure (자중), as in the saying "To whom can I appeal my pent-up feelings?" (愊憶誰訴).

Water flowing from the mountain valley,
Has no way to return again.
The sun setting in the western mountains,
Does it set because it wants to?

Our lives, like morning dew (초로),
How can we avoid dying?
That he died and does not return,
Who can blame anyone for anything (뉘라무삼 탓잡으리)?

Clearly returning to the 4-4 meter, she sings a song of self-consolation based on a view of life and death (생사관).

My love, my love, go in peace.
Go in peace beyond Unjang Pass (운장넘어).
Do not worry about your flesh and blood (골육),
Go in peace, even if just your soul (혼자나마).

There is an issue with this stanza. I have considered various meanings for "Unjang nŏmŏ" (운장넘어) in the second line, but the most plausible interpretation seems to be "Unjŏng nŏmŏ" (雲井넘어, beyond the clouds). However, since the renowned folk song reciter (民謠多誦者), the old man of Kimhae (金海老人) (in Masan 馬山), said it was "Unjang nŏmŏ," I wrote it as such, though I suspect it might be "Unjŏng nŏmŏ." (I hope readers who know will inform me.) Also, regarding "honjanama" (혼자나마) in the fourth line, some say it means "honinama" (혼魂이나마, even if just your soul), but this too follows the words of the Kimhae elder who sang this folk song. In any case, regarding this point, "honinama" seems fitting, and "honjanama" is also fine, so I have marked it with a side line (側線) along with "Unjang." It seems necessary to convey this with caution when transmitting it further. Setting this issue aside, however it may be, this stanza concludes (終) "T'an'ga," making it weighty and significant. The fact that it does not end simply with a song of self-consolation, but tenderly <36> laments one more stanza for the departed beloved, shows its precious value. I assert that among Korean Blue Widow folk songs (靑孀民謠), this, along with "Sibiwŏlga" which I will examine next, forms a pair of masterpieces (쌍벽). I trust this is not mistaken.

II. Study of Sibiwŏlga (十二月歌考)

This "Sibiwŏlga," which can be called one of the two great masterpieces (쌍벽) of Blue Widow folk songs along with the preceding "T'an'ga," what is it like? I intend to examine it stanza by stanza. This folk song gathers the laments (恨歎) of a young widow (靑春寡婦) throughout the twelve months of the year into one song.

Notably, within this "Sibiwŏlga," song lyrics (謠詞) similar to those in "Sach'in'ga" (思親歌, Song of Longing for Parents) are found used month after month. There is no way to prove which came first and which came later, but judging from the structure of the words and also their external form, it seems appropriate to consider them works of the same era.

Perhaps the authors were the same, or if not, it is clear that one of these two folk songs, "Sibiwŏlga" and "Sach'in'ga," purely imitated the other. It is also undeniable that when later generations lay these two folk songs with similar lyrical meanings side-by-side for comparative study, even if they cannot clearly distinguish which came first and which later, they could name them sister folk songs or brother folk songs without objection.

Therefore, I assert that I cannot attempt a historical examination of these two folk songs here, nor is there any basis for a regional examination, and I dare to declare these two folk songs as sister folk songs.

If space permitted, I would record "Sach'in'ga," albeit somewhat lengthy, and undertake a comparative explanation. However, I do not feel a particular need for it, and I also intend to make "Sach'in'ga" naturally understandable through this brief study of "Sibiwŏlga." Thus, I will now proceed directly to the brief study of "Sibiwŏlga."

While no folk song exists that does not incorporate folk customs (民俗) or national character (民性), this "Sibiwŏlga" particularly allows us to find many folk customs. Moreover, I believe this folk song contains "something" that especially needs to be preserved.

On the fifteenth day of the first month,
It's the New Year, the New Year!
Resplendent five-colored clothes,
Changing into them layer by layer,
Playing in groups,
It's the New Year, the first month.
Climbing high on the mountain,
Oh, youths gazing at the moon (망월)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's the first full moon (상월)?

This song consists of 10-line stanzas in 4-4 meter like this one. We can find the folk custom (民俗) of moon-gazing (望月) on the 15th of the first month. Even facing the joyous time of the first full moon (上元) of the New Year, she cannot suppress her sorrowful sighs (愁嘆) and painfully remembers (追憶) her departed beloved.

Barely passing the end of that month,
The second month, Hansik Day (한식일),
Spring arrives in nearby and distant mountains,
Burnt grass sprouts new shoots,
Every house eats cold food,
A soul lost for Kyejat'u'i (개자추에 넉시로다).
On this desolate spring day,
Oh, youths riding horses!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Ch'ŏngmyŏng (청명)?

This Ch'ŏngmyŏng Festival (청명절) in the second month is one of the 24 solar terms (24氣), following the vernal equinox (春分), around April or May in the solar calendar (陽曆). The day after Ch'ŏngmyŏng is called Hansik Day (寒食日), <37> a day primarily associated with the story of Kyejat'u'i (介子推 Jie Zitui). There is a Chinese (支那) poem line: "Encountering Hansik on horseback, mid-journey falls in late spring" (馬上逢寒食途中屬暮春). Perhaps the song phrase "Oh, youths riding horses!" originated from this. It is an undeniable fact that observing Hansik Day in the second month was a folk custom (民俗). Through such times, she also remembers her departed beloved.

Barely passing the end of that month,
The third month, Samjin Day (삼진날),
Swallows (연자) fly in,
Seeking their old homes,
Butterflies (호접) too flutter about,
Boasting of the past rain (옛비?).
On the spring breeze-filled paths outdoors,
Oh, youths strolling!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Tapch'ŏng Day (답청절)?

Spring is definitely advancing. Seeing the youths treading the green (踏靑) on the fresh blue paths outdoors with the spring breeze, she again remembers (懷憶) her departed beloved. It is a line indicating that the representative day of the third month, in terms of folk custom, was Samjin Day (三辰日).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The fourth month, the eighth day (초파일),
On the highest peak of Samgak Mountain (삼각산 제일봉),
A phoenix (봉황) sits and dances,
In the deep waters of the Han River (한강수 기픈물),
The fisherman's song (어옹의 노래) is mournful.
In every house of the capital's myriad households (장안만호),
Oh, youths lighting lanterns (관등)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's the Lantern Festival (관등절)?

It deviates somewhat from the strict 4-4 meter rule. "Ŏong ŭi noraega ch'ŏryanghalche" (어옹의 노래가 처량할제) (6-4) is one example. However, there is sense in avoiding excessive monotony here. Also, substituting (6) for (4) with "Ŏong ŭi noraega" (어옹의 노래가) might even be considered better for conveying a light, mournful, clear scent.
This Lantern Festival (觀燈節, also 看燈節) is a Buddhist custom (佛教의 俗), but in our past, on the night of the 8th day, Buddha's birthday (釋迦의 생신일), every house in the capital (장안) lit lanterns to celebrate. There is a text: "One lamp, two lamps, three lamps, ... six, seven, eight, nine, ten lamps. Must change again into ten thousand lamps. The five-watch bell sound falls upon the human world" (一燈二燈三燈, 一”六七八九十燈, 須更變作千萬燈, 五更鍾聲落人間). The spectacle of Lantern Day (看燈日) was indescribably grand.
Even facing such a time, we can see the sorrow of the Blue Widow, sitting and envisioning the phantom (환영) of her departed beloved.—

Barely passing the end of that month,
The fifth month, Tano Day (단오일),
Eating herbs (나물), drinking water,
Lying down using an arm as a pillow,
Summer clouds are clouds,
The cuckoo's cry (자규ㅅ새울음) is a cry.
On the long, long trees of pine, cypress, and willow (송백양류),
Oh, youths swinging high!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Swing Festival Day (추천절)?

Expressing her feelings (叙懷) through the Tano swing (鞦韆), the desolation from the third to the sixth lines blends with the seventh and eighth lines, creating a stanza of exquisite skill (妙).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The sixth month, Yudu Day (류두일),
Welcome is the favorable wind (순풍)!
My skirt hem flutters,
Every breeze is a sigh,
Every sigh is a song.
Weeding fields, pounding grain,
Oh, youths bathing!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Yudu Day (류두절)?

Referring to Yudu Day (流頭日) in the sixth month, it expresses the feeling of remembrance (懷憶의 情) as if painting a picture of favorable winds (順風章).—<38>

Barely passing the end of that month,
The seventh month, Ch'ilsŏk Day (칠석날),
The half-moon over Mount Emei in autumn (아미산월 반륜추),
Is the pure delight (청홍) of I Chŏksŏn (리적선 Li Taibai)?
Gazing at the Herdsman and Weaver stars (견우직녀),
I am of an age to shed tears.
Amidst the autumn waters sharing the sky's single color (추수공장 천일색),
Oh, youths with bamboo staffs and straw sandals (죽장망헤)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Ch'uo Day (추오절)?

Using the phrase "The half-moon over Mount Emei (峨嵋山) in autumn (月半輪秋) is the pure delight (청흥) of I Chŏksŏn (李謫仙)" (Li Taibai 太白) eloquently demonstrates Korea's deep connection (관계) with Chinese poetry and prose (詩文). It can also be seen as an attempt to introduce the excitement of the autumn season through the writings of this "Immortal Poet" (酒仙). Gazing at the Herdsman (Kyonu 견우) and Weaver (Chingnyŏ 직녀) stars, said to meet once a year by crossing a bridge over the Milky Way (銀河水) on the night of the 7th day of the 7th month (칠석), and shedding tears is attaching her own sorrow to the story. Borrowing the phrase "Autumn waters share the single color of the vast sky" (秋水共長天一色) from Wang P'al's (王勃 Wang Bo's) writing further enhances the autumn mood. Seeing the pure scholars (淸士) of autumn setting out on a pilgrimage (巡禮) of a thousand li of rivers and mountains (千里江山) with bamboo staffs and straw sandals (竹杖芒鞋), she remembers her beloved who has gone, never to return (一去不復來), not even knowing Ch'uo Day (秋梧節), derived from Paeng Nakt'yŏn's (白樂天 Bai Juyi's) poem "When autumn rain falls on the paulownia leaves..." (秋雨梧桐落葉時,...). It is a stanza of remembrance (懷憶).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The eighth month, Ch'usŏk Day (추석날),
The hundred grains (백곡) are abundant (풍등),
Joyful is the autumn sound (추성),
Who knows this fate of mine,
Drenching my collar with a handful of tears (일국루)?
Following the chilly wind and the season,
Oh, youths going to clear weeds from graves (벌초)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Ch'usŏk Day (추석절)?

Even during the Ch'usŏk (秋夕) of the eighth month when the hundred grains (百穀) are abundant (豊登), she speaks of her lonely feelings (情懷), unable to hold back a handful of tears (一掬淚). It also shows the folk custom (民俗) of going to clear weeds (벌초) from ancestral graves (山塋).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The ninth month, Chunggu Day (중구일),
The thousand peaks (천봉) seem higher again,
Surrounded as if by clouds,
Maple leaves color the myriad valleys (만학),
As delightful as blooming flowers.
On Ch'ŏnhwang Peak (천황봉) of Chiri Mountain (지이산),
Oh, youths climbing the mountain (등산)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Chunggu Day (중구일)?

On the Double Ninth Day (重九日) of the ninth month, when the thousand peaks and myriad valleys (千峯萬壑) clearly show autumn colors, seeing the youths coming and going following the crows of Chiri Mountain (智異山), she again expresses unbearable sadness (不勝感悵).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The tenth month, Ch'ŏnma Day (천마일),
Wild geese (기럭이) in the empty mountains (공산),
Is this body of mine sleeping alone in an empty room (독숙공방)?
Oh, high moon in the freezing sky (저리궁천),
What use is shining alone?
Sitting with the lamp extinguished,
Oh, youths reciting poetry under the moon (풍월읇는)!
Where has my beloved gone,
Not knowing it's Ch'ŏnma Day (천마일)?

It brings to mind a line from a Farmer's Song (農夫歌): "The bright moon in the empty sky (空天) is the pride of the tenth month." It is a quiet, mournful verse (哀詞), as if hearing the chilly wind turn and the sound of wild geese.

Barely passing the end of that month,
The winter solstice month (동지ㅅ달), Tongji Day (동짓날),
More famous for filial piety (효자)
Than Wang Sang (왕상) or Maeng Chong (맹종)?
Did he follow his departed parents (가신부모),
To offer his remaining filial devotion (남은효성)?
Sitting alone, drinking wine (술),
Trying to soothe my sorrow (설음진정),
From where does the sound of reading (글소리)
Wrench my heart anew (남의애를 다시끈노)?

Wang Sang's (王祥) "Fresh fish on cold ice" (寒氷鮮魚) and Maeng Chong's (孟宗) "Bamboo shoots on snow" (雪上竹笱) are <39> world-renowned tales of filial piety (孝誠道). But did he, famed as even more filial, follow his departed parents to offer his remaining devotion? Her aching heart tries to find solace (鎭定) in the inappropriate wine cup (酒杯), but the sound of someone reading aloud (讀書聲) brings back the sorrow and pain of a broken heart (膓一斷). The ending differs from the songs of the previous months (January to October), which avoids tediousness (支離함) and monotony (單調함), and is simultaneously commendable (應服할 점).

Barely passing the end of that month,
The last month (섯달), New Year's Eve (제석날),
The year-end cold wind (설한풍) blows fiercely,
Oh, pitch-dark night!
How can I live alone?
How can I live alone?
The returning ninety days of spring (구십춘광),
With whom shall I greet them?
Sorrowful is this chastity of mine (이내정절),
I will uphold it high, high!

This is the final stanza. Throughout the twelve months, the first line of the lyrics always alternates between "Barely passing the end of that month" (그달 그믐 겨우보내) and "Passing the end of that month" (그달 그믐 다 지나고). While the repetition in every stanza might be seen as a flaw, the fact that the feeling of "barely passing" is consistently attached shows the depth of her suffering.
Looking towards the coming ninety days of spring (90春光), she harbors no thought that harms her memory of the departed beloved. Instead, she renews her vow (盟誓) to uphold her chastity (정절) highly, concluding the song with the thought that the remaining life (餘生) of a Blue Widow is nothing but sorrow. This reflects old morality (옛 도덕). It is the freedom left to the widow's conscience (양심). Therefore, it also becomes an unchanging morality. However, this is not the place to discuss it, so there is no need to talk about its moral character.
Thus, the laments of the twelve months gather to become the Blue Widow's "Sibiwŏlga." It can certainly be counted among Korea's proud folk songs, alongside "San Nŏmbul" (山念佛), "Yusan Minyo" (遊山民謠), "Nollyang" (놀량), "Tŭnggi" (등기) (Farmer's Song 農夫歌), "Kin Sant'aryŏng" (긴산타령), "Chajŭn Sant'aryŏng" (자즌산타령), "Ŏga" (漁歌), and together with "Sach'in'ga" (思親歌) and other "Blue Widow Folk Songs" (靑孀民謠). Among these Blue Widow folk songs, these long songs (長謠), "T'an'ga" and "Sibiwŏlga," cannot but be considered central pillars (中望).

Epilogue

As we have briefly examined these folk songs above, we can see that Korean folk songs are generally composed in a 4-4 meter (4 4조). At the same time, we have seen the characteristic that they can adopt all sorts of tunes—calm (平), sorrowful (哀), joyful (樂), agitated (激)—according to the meaning of the words. We have also seen that they can possess all sorts of tunes—calm, sorrowful, joyful, agitated—according to the number of syllables (음절).
We have learned that the rhythm (리듬) of Korean folk songs, and indeed Korean poetry, is fundamentally different from that of the West (西歐), having little relation to the highness or lowness (高低) of sounds, but much relation to the length or shortness (長短) of syllables (음절).
Furthermore, we have learned that our poetry does not gain acoustic effect (음향적 효과) from rhyming points like head rhyme (頭韻), internal rhyme (腰韻), or tail rhyme (尾韻), but rather gains acoustic effect plainly and continuously through repetition or through Eastern-style (東洋的) parallelism (대구).
I read with approbation a section of the discussion on Korean folk songs written in Ch'unwŏn's "Nollyang Study" (놀량考). I feel even greater joy in finding common ground between the explanation of the characteristics, nature, foundation, and development of Korean folk songs presented in "Nollyang" and what I have gathered through this "Brief Study of Blue Widow Folk Songs." This joyful feeling, I believe, is the attitude we gain, we who stand on the path of Korean folk song research, unable to let go of bewildered and pained hearts.
I believe that as research into Korean folk songs becomes more complete in the future, good theories of poetry (詩論) will emerge among us, and Korea's traditional folk songs will make no small contribution (공험됨) to the future of Korean poetry. (End) <40>

**Summary**

This essay by Yi Ŭn-sang analyzes two Korean folk songs, "T'an'ga" (Song of Lament) and "Sibiwŏlga" (Twelve-Month Song), categorized as "Blue Widow Folk Songs." Yi argues for the profound literary and emotional value of folk songs as the bedrock of national literature and identity, countering cosmopolitan ideals detached from homeland roots. He examines the structure (predominantly 4-4 meter), emotional depth, and cultural context of the two songs, highlighting their portrayal of a widow's grief, hardship, remembrance of her husband, and references to traditional customs and seasons throughout the year, ultimately praising them as masterpieces of Korean folk tradition. From a colonial history perspective, the article is significant for its emphasis on preserving and valuing indigenous Korean folk culture (민요, 민속) as a core element of national identity and artistic expression during a period of Japanese rule. Yi's passionate defense of folk songs and their connection to the "homeland" (향토) reflects a subtle assertion of cultural distinctiveness and continuity.

우리 민족이 소유한 전래 민요중의 어느 것이던지 을프지는 한 小謠를 들을 때에 우리는 그 순박한 가사와 단순한 調子속에서도 때때로 우리 선조의 생활상태를 엿보게 되는 것도 잇고 그 때의 자연을 상상할 수도 잇는 동시에 역사를 사랑하는 마음, 국토를 향하는 경건한 마음, 미족을 위하는 뜨거운 생각을 가지게 되는 일이 적지 아니한 것이다.
문학의 문학을 詩라하고 詩의 詩를 민요라 하는 말은 우리가 일쯕 아는 말이어니와 어떠한 형식 어떠한 상상을 가진 詩임을 불구하고 민요를 예사로히 하지 못할 것이며 이것을 기조로 하여 발전한 이상이라야 우아한 문학적 가치를 띄게 될 것이라고 생각한다.

二, 十二月歌考
前項 「嘆歌」와 병합하여 靑孀民謠의 2대 쌍벽이라 할만한 이 「十二月歌」는 어떠한 者인가. 節을 딸아 小考해 보려는 것이어니와 이 민요는 靑春寡婦의 1년 十二月歌의 恨歎을 모아 한 노래를 만들어 노흔 것이다.
특히 이 十二月歌 속에서는 「思親歌」의 謠詞와 가튼 謠詞가 달달이 쓰이어 잇슴을 발견케 된다. 어느 것이 먼저요 어느 것이 뒤라고는 증거할 곳이 바이 업스나 말의 조직이나 또한 그 외형으로 보아서 동시대의 作이라고 간주함이 맛당하다고 생각한다.
아마 작자가 갓거나 그러치 안흐면 十二月歌와 思親歌 이 두 민요중에 어느 것 하나이 어느 것 하나를 순전히 模擬한 것일 것임은 분명한 일이요 後人이 詞意가 가튼 이 두 민요를 동시에 눈 아페다 펴노코 호상 비교연구해 보는 때에 그 何者先 何者後를 判然히 구별해 노치 못하는 것에도 불구하고 姉妹民謠라 혹은 兄弟民謠라 명명한다 할찌라도 異議할 수 업는 일이라고 생각할 수 밧게 업다.
그럼으로 나는 이에 이 兩 민요의 역사적 고찰을 시험할 수도 업고 또한 지방적 고찰도 憑籍할 곳이 업슴을 단언하는 동시에 이 두 민요를 姉妹民謠라고 敢言해 두는 바다.
지면만 허락한다면 좀 장황하나마 思親歌를 기록하고 비교 해설을 취하겟스나 별로히 필요를 느끼지도 안는 바요 또한 이 十二月歌 小考에서 자연히 思親歌를 알 수 잇게 해 보려는 생각이기 때문에 이로부터 바로 十二月歌를 小考해보려 한다.

餘言
우리가 이상에 이 민요를 小考해 보앗거니와 조선의 민요는 대개로 4 4조로 되어 잇슴을 알 수 잇는 동시에 말의 뜻을 딸아서 平, 哀, 樂, 激의 온갓 調로 될 수 잇는 특색을 보앗스며 음절의 수효에 딸아서도 平, 哀, 樂, 激의 온갓 調를 가지게 될 수 잇는 것도 또한 보앗다.
조선민요 내지 조선시가의 「리듬」은 결코 西歐의 것과는 달라 音의 고저에 관계됨이 적고 음절의 장단에 관계됨이 만타는 것을 알게 되엇다.
그 뿐 아니라 우리의 시가는 頭韻, 腰韻, 尾韻 이와 가티 韻다는 點에서 음향적 효과를 엇지 못하고 반복에나 혹은 동양적인 대구에서 평범하게 연속적으로 음향적 효과를 엇게되는 것도 알게 되엇다.
나는 더욱 춘원의 「놀량考」에 쓰인 조선민요 이야기의 一端을 讚意로 읽엇거니와 「놀량」에 나타난 조선민요의 특질 특성 내지 基調 발달을 설명할 것과 공통된 점을 이 靑孀民謠小考에서 조합해 엇게 됨에서 더욱 기쁜 마음을 가진 것이다. 이 기쁜 마음이라 조선민요 연구의 길에 서서 망연하고 괴로운 마음을 노치 못하는 우리가 어든바 태도라고 생각하는 것이다.
후일에 조선민요 연구를 완전히 하여 감을 딸아 우리에게서 조흔 詩論이 생겨지며 조선의 전래민요란 조선 시가외 장래에 공험됨도 적지 안흐리라고 밋는 바다.(完)＜40＞
