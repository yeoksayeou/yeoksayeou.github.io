Magazine Title: 동광 제6호
Publication Date: 1926년 10월 01일
Article Title: 入院中에 (While Hospitalized)
Author: 長白山人 (Changbaeksanin)
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

While Hospitalized
						Changbaeksanin

Lying sick,
What good are blooming flowers?
What good is the rising moon?
Being a young body,
I grieve even more.

On the Kyŏngwŏn Line Train

Rain falls on the empty fields,
The traveler's clothes are all soaked.
With water dripping from my clothes,
I travel this endless road.

Today too has grown dark.
In which house shall I find lodging?
If I cannot find a human dwelling,
Let's walk through the night.

The Bothersome Mountain Stream Sound

Oh, bothersome stream,
Did you not sleep even at night,
Waiting for my eyes to open?
When my weary soul
Turned over at dawn,
Did you startle it awake?
So annoyingly, clamorously shouting,
Trying to ruthlessly sweep away the traces of the dream
Preciously gathered during the single night!

Tomb of King Kungye
(Beside the road entering Yaksup'o from Sambang Station, there is a stone-piled tomb covered in kudzu vines (츩렁굴), and beside it, a small shrine built by locals for offering prayers. In front of the tomb stands an old pine tree (젓나무).)

Where shall I go, alone on horseback?
In the deep, deep mountains, wind and rain are frequent.
What became of the ambition to save the world?
Oh, old hero wandering along the stream.

A thousand years after you departed, the old populace
Built a small house, hung a shaman painting (탱그려),
And calling daily upon the soul dozing in the afterlife,<55>
They plead for blessings, plead for sons.

The pine tree soaring beside the royal tomb,
Surely it couldn't have lived a thousand years, yet
Its spirit soaring loftily at dusk,
Seems like the hero, making me look up again.

Cows Said to Be Going to Sŏul
(Every early morning at Sambang Yaksup'o, ten or several tens of pairs of cows pass by. Often, people wearing rain hats (갈모) drive the cows up the cliff pass of Ch'ŏnjinbong Peak (天眞峰, also called Kigakpong 奇角峯).)

Up the Sambang pass, steep as if carved,
Yellow cows are driven onward.
Tossing their curved horns,
Swishing their thin tails, they go.

Mooing, mooing, continuously, as they turn
Their heads back, they misstep,
Fall to their knees, then lift their heavy bodies
One step up, turn back again, and moo.

Cow trader in your rain hat, whip in hand,
Don't say they cry because the mountain path is rough.
Know that they cry because the suckling calf<56>
Left behind lingers in their eyes.

Beyond Sambang pass, Sep'o, Kŏmbullyŏng pass,
The road leads endlessly to Sŏul.
Though humans may return by this road,
The cows from the Kosan land, who left calves behind, cannot come back.

Those wide fields of Anbyŏn and Kosan
Were the ancient grounds you plowed for generations.
The soreness on your back, worn raw by the yoke,
Is the final price you carry.

Mountain Moon
(After a long rain briefly cleared, for just one night in Sambang, the mid-July full moon appeared. Just then, the sound of a woman singing was heard from somewhere, and all who heard floated in an indescribable excitement. I too, leaning on my cane, went with friends Liu (劉) and Kil (吉) to seek out the deserted streamside (湲邊).)

"Moon on Kigakpong Peak!"
After 40 long days of rain,
The moon of the twelfth night!
Between the two dark peaks,
That moon looking down! <57>
From where does it call?
The song of a young woman.
Is she alone, or are there two?
Drawing out the long notes of "Sabisu" (泗沘水),
A song arriving, barely audible.

Is she singing from the front mountain?
If the front mountain, her back is to the moon.
Is she singing from the back mountain?
If the back mountain, she embraces the moon.
Is she sitting on a rock by the stream?
Is she leaning against an old pine on the slope?
Here and there alike,
A song from an unknown place.
Perhaps a song sung by eyes filled with tears.

Let's go out.
Cold wind on an ailing body—
Still, let's go out!

Black water,
Gray rock,
White rapids.<58>
Blue wind coming from the green mountain,
Yellow moonlight coming from the yellow moon.
Going against the sound of the water,
My heart, sharply red.

East wind blows on the land.
West wind blows in the sky.
Therefore,
The misty rain spray flies west, westward,
And torn cloud fragments fly east, eastward.
Though the moon is bright, following the moonlight,
Misty rain shimmering like ice!

The mountain sits,
The water goes by, making sound.
The ailing person huddles on the back of a rock,
Listening to the sound of the flying clouds,
The sound of the flowing moonlight,
The sound of the misty rain,
The sound of the white rapids in the black water flowing underfoot,
The sound of the wind rushing toward the sky. <59>

**Summary**

This collection of poems by Changbaeksanin, published in 1926, captures moments of introspection and observation during travels, likely while recuperating from illness. The poems move from personal sorrow ("While Hospitalized") and the melancholy of travel ("On the Kyŏngwŏn Line Train") to reflections on nature's sounds ("The Bothersome Mountain Stream Sound"), historical sites ("Tomb of King Kungye"), the harsh realities of rural life ("Cows Said to Be Going to Sŏul"), and a profound connection with a moonlit landscape ("Mountain Moon"). The imagery often contrasts human fragility and suffering with the enduring or indifferent presence of nature and history. From a historical perspective, the poems offer glimpses into early 20th-century Korea under Japanese rule. The depiction of cows being led to Sŏul, likely for slaughter, hints at economic pressures and the disruption of traditional rural life. The reflection on King Kungye evokes a sense of lost sovereignty and historical memory. The detailed descriptions of specific locations (Kyŏngwŏn Line, Sambang, Kosan) provide valuable context for the period's geography and travel.
