Waves
						Yŏsim

They surge in.
Tens of thousands of water droplets, soaring and breaking apart
Onto the jet-black rock
That hadn’t moved for a thousand years,
Leaving water stains,
All day long.

Surging in and surging in again.
In the evening, foam roars,
And over the broken stone fragments crashing and grating against each other,
It shouts and surrounds them.

Though no one watches through the night, the waves
Shout and surge in,
How foolishly.

Ah! But as the night of the Century dawns
And the roosters of the fishing village<60>

Sing of the Victory of Light in the morning,
I see,
How the gentle waves
On the rustling fine sand
Ripple and dance, I see.

Ah! Who would say the foam is powerless?
Who would call the mad waves foolish?
Behold,
Within my heart now
The waves are about to soar,
And tomorrow,
Onto the jet-black rock
They intend to leave water stains.

At the Ch'ingtao Seashore

Evolution

A mother had three sons.
The eldest,
Shouting, “I will make those born low and those born high all the same people!”
Went out, and came back with an arrowhead
Stuck in his chest.
The second,<61>
Crying out, “I will stop white people from hitting black people!”
Went out, and came back with a long sword
Stuck in his back.
The youngest,
Running out, shouting, “I will prevent idle people from taking things from working people!”
Was shot in the head and
Came back.
Now the heartbroken mother alone
Stands at the threshold every evening
And sees countless young girls
Holding nameless flowers
Going to visit the graves of the three champions.
1925, 3, 10

Freedom

Mother, I must let that bird go.
But Mother,
You just laugh, saying, “Foolish child!”?
Mother, if an enemy had locked me, your son,
In a cage like that,<62>
What would you do, Mother?
I really must let that bird go.
Still, Mother,
You just laugh, saying, “Foolish child!” <63>

**Summary**

This document presents three poems by Yŏsim published in 1926. "Waves" uses the imagery of relentless waves crashing against rocks to symbolize persistent struggle leading to eventual transformation and victory, reflecting the speaker's own determination. "Evolution" is an allegory of three sons martyred for fighting social, racial, and economic inequality, suggesting the continuation of their ideals by a younger generation. "Freedom" portrays a child's simple yet profound desire to free a caged bird, juxtaposing it with the mother's dismissive reaction, highlighting the yearning for liberation. From a historical perspective, these poems, written during the Japanese colonial period, are significant. They subtly yet powerfully convey themes of resistance against oppression ("Waves," "Freedom"), critique social injustices likely mirroring colonial realities ("Evolution"), and express hope for eventual liberation ("Waves") and the passing of ideals to future generations ("Evolution"). The allegory and symbolism likely served to navigate censorship while expressing nationalist or anti-colonial sentiments.
