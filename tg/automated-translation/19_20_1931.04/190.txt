Quiet Thoughts, Continued: Several Additional Chapters, Quietly I Think of It, Waking and Sleeping I Beat My Breast
						By Yang Chu-dong

1. Know that I am the enemy first
Makeshift measures, goodwill and hesitation,
Egoistic calculation and also conservatism,
Fine-sounding neutrality, aloofness, in reality evasion,
The true form of conscious or unconscious reaction—
His patriot-like, theorist-like, detestable demeanor—

Ah, this is the inevitable fate of the modern intellectual class,
Of those possessing a petty bourgeois mentality.

Sophistry, falsehood, obfuscation,
Wasted influence and its lack of substance, lack of vigor,
Daring not to create history,
The pitiful "fellow traveler" dragged along as things proceed—
The "chameleon" whose own theories change when faced with action—

Ah, this is the undeniable true nature of the passing century,
Of the retreating class.

Towards the procession rushing into the dawn,
Knowingly or unknowingly trying to shut their eyes,
Loitering, loitering, trying only to follow behind,
A worthless parasite of the era, of the masses—
A cripple of progressiveness, actually hindering the advance—

Ah, my son, if your father is like this,
Know that I, first, am your enemy of the new world. (1930, 1)

2. Historical Treatise (Deleted)

3. People Are Not Yet Round/Complete
The six continents are infinitely vast,
In how many places does human smoke connect?
Looking out, one cannot see the limit,
Climb that high mountain peak.
Eighteen thousand years have passed,
The Earth is round, but people are not yet round/complete.
Laughable are the plans of the masses,
Confined to a few acres of land on the earth. <86>

Spring Water
Cette eau de diamant avait un gout de mort!
—Théophile Gautier

By the graveside
A spring flows,
Trickling out from the crack in the rock,
Clean spring water, jewel-like spring water.

Shall I wash my hands in the spring?
How cold that spring water is,
Just drawing near feels chilling to the bone,<86>
Icy cold spring water, jewel-like spring water.

Ah, suddenly I shudder,
Could it be that this spring water
Connects its vital energy with "Death"?
Spring water by the grave, jewel-like spring water. (Old Manuscript) <87>
<86-87>

**Summary**

This article presents several poems by Yang Chu-dong. The first poem is a harsh self-critique directed at the intellectual class of his time, accusing them of hypocrisy, inaction, conservatism, and ultimately being obstacles to progress—enemies of the "new world" represented by the next generation. The second poem, a *Hansi*, reflects on the vastness of the world compared to the limited scope and incompleteness of humanity. The third poem contemplates a cold spring by a graveside, evoking a sense of death lurking beneath the surface of life's purity, referencing Gautier. From a historical perspective, the first poem is particularly revealing. It showcases the intense self-reflection and generational conflict among Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, highlighting disillusionment with passive or conservative stances and a yearning for decisive action and societal transformation. Historians might find the poem's critique of the "fellow traveler" and "chameleon" intellectual archetypes valuable for understanding the pressures and ideological struggles within the Korean intelligentsia under Japanese rule.
