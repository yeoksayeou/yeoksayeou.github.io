The sound of a housewife shaking out an empty rice bin with a broken gourd,
From inside a cave-like room,
The sound of children, skipping meals, stamping their feet and crying fretfully—
High civilization does not even lend an ear.

Flocks of sparrows coming and going, skylarks singing...
Surely the beautiful rural scenery is no different now than before,
But the grub digging in, the rough life, suffocates and gasps.

**Summary**

This poem excerpt by Song Sun-il paints a grim picture of Korean rural life in 1931, contrasting the outward beauty of nature (sparrows, skylarks, scenery) with the harsh reality of poverty and toil. It highlights the sounds of deprivation – an empty rice bin, a broken gourd, hungry children crying – and notes the indifference of "high civilization" to this suffering. The poem depicts the physical labor of peasants whose lives seem suffocating, like a grub digging into the earth. From a historical perspective, the poem offers a poignant glimpse into the hardships faced by Korean farmers during the Japanese colonial period, suggesting economic distress and a disconnect between the lives of ordinary people and the forces of modernization or colonial administration. The mention of farmers with ploughs hints at agricultural labor struggles.
