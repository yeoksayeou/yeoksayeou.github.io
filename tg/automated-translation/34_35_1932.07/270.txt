**Collected True Stories**

"Our Village's Strange Story" Mr. Chang the Rich Man's Jade Button
By Yŏn'gi Pyŏksansaeng

The idea that officials are revered and the populace lowly is already a dream of the past, and one would think that the selling and buying of government posts wouldn't exist in today's civilized world, but depending on the region, there are occasionally those who sell posts and those who buy them. How can one not chuckle? In our village these days, it seems the number of people wearing official hats, claiming to have held posts like *T'ongjŏng Taebu* (Senior Third Rank Official), *Chungch'uwŏn Ŭigwan* (Privy Council Advisor), or *Taesŏngjŏn Chigwŏn* (Official of the Great Sage Hall), is increasing daily. Looking into who arranges these posts, it's almost all decadent Confucian scholars (腐儒). It seems these types somehow create slips of paper stamped with the seal of the former Korean Empire's Minister of Finance (舊韓國 度支部大臣) and sell them for one hundred won per sheet, or perhaps even down to ten won. In our village, there is one household, that of Mr. Chang the Rich Man (張富者). Even as the hundred or so other households in the village are dying out, this house alone grows steadily richer, harvesting several thousand sacks of rice, they say. But that rich man, perhaps because it was his lifelong regret that his ancestors couldn't wear a *t'ang'ŏn* (horsehair indoor cap), recently held a feast, claiming that three generations of the Chang family had now become *T'ongjŏng*, *Ŭigwan*, and *Chigwŏn*. It's said he spent about 300 won to buy these three official-sounding titles. For the feast, he butchered a dog, bought some pounds of noodles, got a cask of *t'akchu* (unrefined rice wine), and invited only the likes of Mr. Yi Ch'ambong from across the water, Mr. Kim Chusa from over the pass, and some councilor, feeding them until their bellies were about to burst and receiving their congratulations. If he was going to hold such a fine feast anyway, it would have been nice if, during this spring famine, he had at least fed the villagers one meal. But that dreadful Mr. Chang didn't even give his own servant living in the outer quarters a single cup of wine or a bite of soup, they say. So, from the very next day, wearing an official hat (*kamtu*) over his *t'ongyŏng* gat, with a jade button (*okgwanja*) firmly attached, he apparently went off to Choch'iwŏn Market to boast about having attained his post. Truly, it's a situation where even the village dogs would laugh. They say if you go to the Northern Provinces (北鮮), you see people with suddenly acquired official hats (*pyŏrak kamt'u*); now this sort of thing has appeared in the Southern Provinces (南鮮) too.

"Our Village's Strange News" The Man Who Returned from the Afterlife
By P'ungch'ŏn Kŏsa

I intend to write a riddle here.
A young man named Chŏng X-kyun (real name) who lives in our village, under 20 years old, is currently attending a certain middle school in Seoul. Last autumn, that student was ill and likely missed school, but his dear mother (慈親), upon hearing the news that he was lying sick in bed, went all the way to Kyŏngsŏng, where he was studying abroad, to see her son. At that time, Chŏng X-kyun saw his mother and said this—
"Mother, I've been to the afterlife and back. When I went there, I was able to meet my deceased grandmother, and I could also find my dead younger sibling. Then, when I went somewhere, some important-looking person was flipping through a thick book and <80> asked my name. So I answered, 'I am Chŏng X-kyun from P'ungch'ŏn.' Then that person said,
'Your time to come here is still far off, so leave. And it is the turn for Sŏ X-ho (real name) from P'ungch'ŏn to enter.' Indeed, who is this person Sŏ X-ho? Mother, you would know, right?" he asked.
Hearing these words, Chŏng X-kyun's dear mother, being a woman, couldn't immediately figure out who this Sŏ X-ho person was. On the way back home to their village with her son, she heard people talking in the train carriage—
"How could Dr. Sŏ pass away so quickly?"
Only then did she realize that Dr. Sŏ had truly died, and Chŏng X-kyun's mother, hearing the name 'Doctor,' finally identified who it was.
That Dr. Sŏ was a famous doctor here and was very popular. One day, he carelessly performed external surgery on a wound on his hand from a plowshare, and the bacterial toxin (菌毒) entered, causing poisoning. He went to another hospital, but things did not go well (不如意), and he tragically became a visitor to the Yellow Springs (黃泉客 - i.e., died). When this happened, there wasn't a single person who didn't say that a good doctor had passed.
Well then! Does a place called the afterlife exist, where there is a register (名簿) of all humans in the world, and people go there one by one in order?
Is the story above true? Or false? Even Emperor Qin Shi Huang (泰始皇), who sought eternal life, died, so it is something everyone will experience once. Whether it's true or not will simply remain a question.

Youth Sold Away
By Ahyŏnni Yakobo

"Oh my, ma'am? Why, your daughter got married last month, didn't she? But she's come back with her hair still braided, what's happened?"
"That's right. Far from being married, she just stopped by for a short visit."
The mother, driven by money, married off her only fifteen-year-old daughter to an old bachelor over thirty, receiving fifty won.
However, the mother thought fifty won was far too little, and she was always scheming—how could she get her daughter back and marry her off somewhere else for a hundred won? Just then, about a month after getting married, the daughter came for her first visit home.
The mother consulted with her daughter, coaxing her by saying there was another, better groom, and she should go to him.
"But still, Mother? My husband is quite mature, and I receive a lot of affection—"
"Are you going to defy me and do as you please? Then I'll just die!"
The mother apparently threw a fit.
A month after braiding her hair again, the groom came to the bride's house.
"Why is her hair braided?"
"What's your worry? She said her mother-in-law treats her harshly. Am I supposed to send my precious daughter into that?"
"Damn you, you dog-like women!" "Look at this punk! Cursing at his mother-in-law. You bastard, let me cut off <81> your balls... Trying to kill someone else's daughter for no reason, you murderous punk!"
The groom was so dumbfounded that he just turned around and left.
In this way, after marrying her off, deceiving the husband, and sending her elsewhere several times, even today, she keeps the daughter's hair braided long and flowing and is supposedly looking for another well-behaved son-in-law prospect...

Whenever 17 or 18-year-old unmarried women gather:
"They say Yŏng-sun has had eight husbands—"
"Kŭm-suk, she's truly the daughter of a millionaire, yet she fell for some guy stuck in a rented room, had her wedding ceremony five months pregnant, and apparently her hands, fair as powder, are getting all rough. But her family, who had planned to marry her into a rich house, didn't give her any of the valuable trousseau items they had prepared—"
"They say Sŏng-sil graduated from X-myŏng School and went to be a concubine for some rich man—but apparently, she went because she wanted to—living comfortably without working—"
"The villagers drove Ŭn-sun out of the village, saying her conduct was improper—"
"Pretty Ippun-i, who always stayed indoors, ran off with some vagrant fellow, and apparently, she's working as a hostess in a bar (色酒家) in Inch'ŏn—" These are all facts that can be seen happening all at once just in our area, they say.
Is that all? There's a tavern every other house, so aren't there quite a lot of taverns?
In just this small village, there are over 40 taverns, they say. (End)

"Strange News" - The Story of Almost Being Disgraced While Looking for an Inn
By Maengsan Okch'ŏn Hagin

This happened one summer when I was at the X Company (X社) in P'yŏngyang. I was traveling with Mr. U, the company's Managing Director (常務理事), who was nearly 60 years old (六旬), on business in the Wŏnsan-Hamhŭng area. On our return journey, at the Sambang Mineral Springs place (三防 藥水浦), an innkeeper asked where we were headed, and we replied that we were going to Kyŏngsŏng. He urged us several times, "If you are going to Kyŏngsŏng, please make sure to go to the M Inn (M여관)." Arriving at Kyŏngsŏng Station, we hailed a taxi. When we asked the driver for the M Inn, he said he didn't know it, so we asked him to guide us to a good inn that he knew. He stopped in front of the T Inn (T여관). Looking down the alley, the building was low and looked very shabby from the outside. Mr. U expressed his dissatisfaction with the inn. Feeling it would be rude to immediately reject the inn the driver recommended as good, I proposed that I quickly check the inn myself, and if it was acceptable, we would stay (投宿), otherwise, we would go elsewhere. Under this agreement, I went into the alley alone, and without looking for the owner, I stepped onto the threshold of the main gate and glanced around the inner courtyard (內庭). As it was summer, a person who seemed to be the owner was sitting on the wooden floor (마루) and asked whom I was looking for. I unconsciously blurted out that I was looking for a guest from Inch'ŏn. The owner followed up, asking which guest from Inch'ŏn I was looking for. Again, without the slightest hesitation, <82> I properly asked, "Isn't a guest named Kim Ch'un-sik staying at this inn?" The owner immediately called an errand boy (使喚者) and asked, "Is Mr. Kim Ch'un-sik, the guest from Inch'ŏn, staying here?" "Yes— he's resting in room X right now." "Tell him a visitor is here looking for him." As he gave this order, the errand boy went off towards that room to find Kim Ch'un-sik. I felt strangely flustered, as if I had committed some crime. I had intended only to quickly assess whether the inn was good or bad, but when the owner asked whom I was looking for, I had blurted out "Kim Ch'un-sik from Inch'ŏn" completely at random (억막주악에 䊻�川손님 金春植이라고 되는대로 불러버린 것이), and bizarrely, it turned out that an Inch'ŏn Kim Ch'un-sik actually was staying at that inn at that exact time! It was an incredibly strange event, beyond anything I could have dreamt (千萬 夢外). Of course, I wouldn't recognize his face if he came out. And if we met, I wouldn't have any excuse ready, plus the taxi was waiting on the main road, there were time constraints, and if that person came out, it felt like my country bumpkin nature would awkwardly reveal itself. So, whether the inn was good or bad didn't matter anymore. Deciding to pick up my dropped ears later (proverbial saying for embarrassment), I employed the 36th stratagem: run away (36計에 走字). I ran out of that alley, breathless, unable to speak, just gesturing wildly with my hands to signal that the inn was wrong. Feeling as if someone was chasing me from behind, I yelled "Fire! Fire!" (idiomatic for urgency) to get the car moving, and we drove out to Chongno. Once we reached Chongno, the sign for the M Inn caught my eye. I immediately got out of the taxi, rushed into the inn, entered a guest room, but I was still out of breath. Mr. U looked puzzled and asked why I was so worked up (氣高萬丈). When I revealed what had happened, Mr. U, despite being an old gentleman, burst into such hearty laughter (破顔大笑) that anyone watching might have found it suspicious. Even now, I laugh when I dream about this incident.

**Summary**

This collection presents four short anecdotes depicting Korean society in 1932. The first satirizes the lingering obsession with status and the practice of buying fake aristocratic titles among the newly wealthy. The second explores superstition and the belief in the afterlife through a young man's near-death experience story. The third reveals the harsh realities of poverty and patriarchal control, where a mother repeatedly sells her young daughter into marriage for financial gain, alongside gossip reflecting the limited, often grim, prospects for young women. The final story is a humorous personal account of an embarrassing coincidence encountered while traveling, highlighting awkward social moments and perhaps the density of social networks even in large cities like Kyŏngsŏng. For historians, these stories offer valuable glimpses into social stratification, economic desperation, prevailing folk beliefs, changing gender roles and expectations, and the texture of everyday life (urban travel, communication, village dynamics) in colonial Korea. The critique of buying status, the prevalence of taverns, and the gossip about women's fates provide specific insights into social anxieties and realities of the period.

「우리고을의 珍車」 張부자의 옥관자
燕歧 碧山生
官尊 民卑도 벌서 녯날의 꿈이엇으며 賣官買職도 문명화한 오늘날에는 없을 줄로 알지만 지방을 따라 간혹 벼실을 팔고 벼실을 사는 자가 잇으니 어찌 코웃음이 나아오지 안하릿까. 우리 동리에는 요새 通政大夫中樞院議官大成殿直員, 이 따위 벼실을 하엿다고 감투를 쓰는 자가 날로 늘어나는 모양입니다. 그 벼실을 시키는 지를 돌아보면 거이 다 腐儒들입니다. 이런 무리가 어대서 舊韓國 度支部大臣의 印을 찍은 쪽지를 만들어 가지고 한 장에 백원 또는 機 10원까지라도 賣買가 되는 모양입니다. 우리 동리에 張富者 한집이 잇는데 동리 백여호가 다 죽어가도 이 집만은 점점 부자가 되어 몇 千石받는답니다. 그런데 그 부자는 선대로 탕건 못써본 것이 한이던지, 요새 장부자 三代가 通政, 議官 直員을 하엿다고 잔치를 하엿지요. 이 벼실 이름 셋사기에 한 3백원 들엇답니다. 잔치를 하는 대는 개한마리 잡고 국수 몇근 사고, 濁酒 한통 받아서 물건너 李參奉 재넘어 金主事 무슨 議員 그따위들만 불러다 배가 툭 터지게 먹이고 致賀를 받엇다나요. 기왕 좋은 잔치를 할 것 같으면 이런 춘궁에 동리사람이나 한밥 먹엿으면 좋으련만 지독한 장부자는 제집 행낭 아범도 술한잔, 국한먹음 아니 주엇답니다. 그래 그 이튼날부터는 통영갓에다 감투를 쓰고 옥관자를 딱 부치이고 鳥致院市場으로 벼실 햇노라 자랑을 나아 갓더랍니다. 참으로 동리개가 다 웃을 지경이지요. 北鮮 지방에를 가면 벼락감투를 본다더니 南鮮에 이 따위가 생겻습니다.

「우리고을 珍聞」 저승에 갓다온 사람
豊川居士

나는 여기서 수수꺾이 하나를 쓰려 합니
다. 우리 고을에 사는 鄭X균(本名)이란 者는 20未滿의 청년으로 지금 서울 某중등교 재학중입니다. 그 학생이 작년 가을 病患이 잇어서 결석도 하엿겟지만 그가 病床에 누어잇다는 소식을 들은 그의 慈親은 그 아들을 보려고 유학간 京城까지 찾아 갓습니다. 그때 鄭X균이가 自己어머님을 보고 하는 말이-
「어머니 나는 저승에 갓다 왓어요. 거기 가보니까 돌아가신 할머니도 맞나 볼 수 잇엇으며 죽은 동생도 찾아 볼 수 잇엇습니다. 그래서 어데를 가니까 무슨 큰 사람이 두터운 책을 뒤지며 내＜80＞ 이름을 묻습디다. 그래서 『나는 豊川 鄭X균이올시다』하고 대답하니까 그 사람이 말하기를
『너는 아직 이곳 올을 날이 멀엇으니 나아가거라 그리고 豊川사는 徐X호(本名)가 들어올 차례다』하옵디다. 과연 徐X호란 사람이 누구입니까? 어머니는 아시겟지오? 하엿다.
이 말을 들은 鄭X균 慈親은 여인인 고로 갑작이 徐X호라는 사람을 알아 내지 못하고 母子서 고향으로 돌아오는 길에 車間에서 사람들이 하는 소리-
「徐醫師가 그러케 빨리 돌아가다니?」
이때에야 비로소 정말 徐醫師가 죽은 줄을 알엇으며 鄭X균 母親도 醫師라는 이름을 듣고야 누구인지도 판명되엇다.
그 徐醫師라는 사람은 이곳에서 유명한 선생이어서 인기를 많이 끌든 사람이다. 어떤날 쟁기한데 손우에 傷處가 생겨 잇음을 무심코 外科수술을 하다가 그 菌毒이 침입하야 중독되어 他병원에까지 가서도 不如意하야 비명의 黃泉客이 되고 말엇다. 이 사실이 잇엇을 때 누구를 물론하고 좋은 醫師가 갓다고 말하지 않는 사람이 없엇다.
자! 그러면 저승이란 곳이 잇어 온 세계인간의 名簿가 잇어서 차례차례로 그 곳으로 가는 것일까!
以上의 말이 참말인가? 거짓인가? 長生 不死하려든 泰始皇도 죽엇으니 누구나 다 한번 경험할 일이다. 이 眞否는 다만 숙제일 것이다.

阿峴里 野孤步
