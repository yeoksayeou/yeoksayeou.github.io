Newspaper Management and Editing Roundtable Discussion

Causes of Newspaper Management Failure

Chu: In Korea, it seems assumed that starting a newspaper business will inevitably lead to failure. Where do you think the causes lie?
Kim: The money wasted trying to run newspapers in Korea, does it amount to about 500,000 wŏn?
Ch'un: 500,000 wŏn? It must be a million wŏn. If you include all the money put in under various pretexts for running newspapers, it easily exceeds one million wŏn. Among the reasons for newspaper failures, perhaps one is the tendency for newspaper company funds to end up in the personal pockets of individuals...
Chu: If you add the losses incurred by branch managers, it would likely be several million wŏn.
Yi In: It's probably 3 or 4 million wŏn... (The discussion continued for some time on the issue of newspaper companies consuming money and the matter of the Chosŏn Ilbo's publishing rights [版權] being transferred to Mr. Im Kyŏngnae). (1) The inadmissibility of using newspaper publishing rights as collateral for mortgages, and (2) issues regarding the character of those in charge of newspaper companies were discussed, but due to circumstances, they cannot be published here.
Wŏn: The reason so much money is spent and publishing rights are bought and sold is that the right to publish is limited to one or two individuals, preventing just anyone from starting a newspaper. We must first clarify that publishing rights cannot be the object of a mortgage. What is the legal situation? <40>
Yi In: Of course, newspaper publishing rights cannot be the object of a mortgage.
Wŏn: Buying, selling, and mortgaging newspaper publishing rights – these are phenomena unseen outside of Korea.
Chu: Doesn't the reputation [聲價] of a newspaper have a certain value? For example, the name "Tong-A Ilbo" or "Chosŏn Ilbo" certainly has a value corresponding to its reputation.
Wŏn: But the recent Chosŏn Ilbo issue wasn't about reputation, it was about publishing rights [版權].
Yi In: While the right to both author and publish can be mortgaged or sold, it's not possible here because it's based on a permit system [許可制].
Chu: That's why they talk about transferring [讓渡] publishing rights, isn't it? Who formally says they are buying or selling?
Wŏn: Even a permit for a car can't be sold, yet they sell the publishing rights of a public newspaper institution...
Yi In: Under a permit system, whether it's a newspaper or a car, the permit is granted based on the character of the applicant. The transfer should also be granted based on the character of the person receiving the transfer.
XX: Looking at the recent Chosŏn Ilbo incident, it seems the authorities either disregard the Korean press, hold the Korean populace in contempt, or fail to recognize the gravity of the situation – one of the three.
Sŏl: It's unlikely they wouldn't thoroughly investigate the responsible party of a media organization, which is at least a public instrument [公器], so ultimately, they must have approved of him.
XX: Another reason Koreans fail in the newspaper business is their complete lack of experience. How can someone who has no idea how to run a newspaper, no management policy whatsoever, possibly avoid failure? Among them, few possess a statesmanlike spirit [國士的氣魄] or grand ambitions; many are the sort who think becoming a newspaper president is like being the prime minister of a nation. If you count everyone who failed in the newspaper business, there must be about 30 people.
Sŏl: It's true that failures occur due to lack of knowledge, money, and experience in managing newspapers, but there are other conditions hindering newspaper development, such as the small number of readers of Korean script [朝鮮文]...
Ch'un: The absence of political life in Korea is another major reason for newspaper failures.

The Mission and Current State of Newspapers

Chu: Let's change the subject. What is the reason for running newspapers in Korea? In other words, please tell us whether current Korean newspapers are harming or benefiting the public. Around the time of the March First Movement [己未年], when the three newspapers emerged, I believe the publishers and reporters, regardless of their political leaning, published under a certain conviction, but I think the meaning has changed today.
Wŏn: In Japan, they talk about purifying the Diet, but in Korea, we need to purify the mission of the newspapers. Before the Eulsa Treaty [乙巳], didn't the populace regard newspapers as a kind of bible [聖經]? The expectation Koreans had for the Tong-A Ilbo during the March First Movement was extraordinary. But now...
Yi In: Back then, besides being news organizations, they truly served the role of a bible.
Ch'un: They still do things beyond being just news organizations.
Chu: Ultimately, because of the permit system [許可制], there's no way around it... If the authorities say no, there's nothing to be done, and since the newspaper exists only because it received that permit, what choice is there...
Sŏl: It's said that newspapers were the public's bible, but that contradicts the newspaper business itself. As a commodity [商品], it needs to attract popularity, while a bible must be a mirror. Just as a bible and a commodity are contradictory, as newspapers become news-centric [「뉴쓰」 本位] and commercialized, naturally, significant changes have occurred over the past 10 years.
Ch'un: Why, aren't they still a wooden bell [木鐸, symbolizing a moral guide]? <41>
Chu: Are there many readers who look at the newspaper editorials [論說] now?
Wŏn: Recently, it seems magazines rather than newspapers carry more authority in terms of editorials.
Yi Chong: Even now, many people in the countryside read newspaper editorials. Therefore, even if newspapers are becoming commercialized and censorship is severe, simply acknowledging and conforming to it is not right.
Sŏl: That's difficult. Even with "Tonggwang," regardless of its content, if you just want to sell it, that's one thing, but maintaining a certain dignity makes management difficult, doesn't it?
Ch'un: That's right. You can't keep pouring money into it...

New Plans for Newspaper Management

Chu: Mr. Hong, if you were to launch a new newspaper now, please outline a concrete plan for how you would do it.
Hong: It's difficult to name names here, but someone said that if they had one million wŏn, they could create a major newspaper.
Sŏl: If one were to start managing a newspaper from scratch now to make it a major one, besides the financial issue, it would require the 10 years that the Tong-A Ilbo has gone through. I would start a different kind of newspaper.
Chu: Why, even in Europe, America, or Japan, similar newspapers can compete in the same city or region, can't they? If you were to do it differently, specifically, what could be done...
Sŏl: For example, publishing a morning edition [朝刊] instead of an evening edition [夕刊]...
Chu: Right, newspapers in other countries are primarily morning editions, but why are they evening editions in Korea?
Sŏl: Koreans tend to sleep late. (Laughter)
Ch'un: Korean newspapers are not centered on the capital but aim for same-day delivery throughout Korea. So, if it's an evening edition in Kyŏngsŏng, it becomes a morning edition in the provinces.
Chu: The reason for evening editions is because of the market price reports [「相場」記事]. You can't meet the deadline for market reports with a morning edition.
Sŏl: Do major Japanese newspapers have morning and evening editions? No. They continuously publish regional editions, with a new edition every two hours...
Wŏn: To start a new newspaper now that could compete with the Tong-A Ilbo wouldn't take 10 years. Four or five years now would be equivalent to 10 or 20 years in the past.
Chu: If the Chosŏn Ilbo were properly reorganized now, it could be run without additional investment.
Wŏn: You mean if it were revived as it is?
Chu: Yes. If it were handed over unconditionally as is, who could run it? Realistically, who are the people capable of managing the Chosŏn Ilbo company?
Sŏl: Specifically? Even if such people exist, it's hard to say.
Kim: I believe the Chosŏn Ilbo issue is not just about money. Doesn't the Chosŏn Ilbo company have over 70 directors involved?
Yi In: That might be the case...
Kim: If just paying money makes you a director, how can it succeed? We need capable people who don't just see running a newspaper as a great honor, but who can actually manage it.
Yi In: That's right. Newspaper specialists need to step forward.
Chu: How is it in Japan? In America, the editor (主幹) seems to be involved in both management and editing...
Sŏl: The Kokumin Shimbun (國民新民) has, besides the editorial, business, and printing departments, a general affairs department, under which the planning division, secretariat, accounting department, and public relations department [廣務部] directly report. The business department primarily handles sales and advertising. And each department head also serves as a director [取締役].
Hong: If a newspaper lacks a 'businessman,' it's bound to fail... <42>

Regarding Newspaper Editing

Chu: In Korea, which section is considered more important, politics or society?
Kim: The society section is probably considered more important.
Yi In: These days, the politics section is on page one, but usually, of course, the society section is read more.
Chu: Then, isn't placing the politics section on the first page and the society section on the second page technically imperfect?
Sŏl: Of course.
Yi Chong: Then why is the society section put on the second page? Ch'unwŏn and you two, why don't you try to reform it?
Sŏl: Korean newspapers can be seen as imitating Japanese newspapers, and in Japan, initially, the society section didn't exist. This is because early Japanese reporters were a type of political operative [政客], and thus political news [政治記事] was the important news. Social issues, which were neglected, began to be covered on page three, digested in an interesting and easy-to-read manner, which can be seen as the development of the society section. Korea, lacking politics, imitated Japan, which has politics, resulting in a kind of deformity. We haven't broken that tradition and just leave it as is.
Wŏn: The society section is always filled with arrests [檢擧], imprisonment [懲役], suicides [自殺], famine [飢饉], etc., making it too gruesome to look at.
Sŏl: That's true. It's also a fact that there isn't enough material to make the society section the front page. However, putting the politics section on page one is a contradiction in production. That's why when the Shanghai Incident or the Manchurian Incident occurred, we actually put them on page two to better inform the general readership. In reality, articles of general interest should be on page one, but instead, they end up on page two, which is a major contradiction. Some Japanese newspapers don't even have titles like Society Editor or Politics Editor; instead, they are divided by page, like First Section Chief, Second Section Chief.
Hong: The politics section in Korean newspapers mixes Chinese characters [한문] and Korean script [朝鮮文], making it difficult for many people to understand everything.
Sŏl: That's a matter of tradition, so it can be changed.
Sŏl: Didn't the Tong-A Ilbo face so much criticism for writing its editorials [社說] in Hangul?
Chu: What do you think will happen to newspaper language in the future? Mr. Yi Sanghyŏp said it will likely become Korean script mixed with easy Chinese characters.
Sŏl: Writing purely in Korean script [순조선문] might be difficult to read at first, but once you get used to it, it's fine. During the state funeral of Emperor Sunjong [純宗因山], we tried publishing the article entirely on the front page for the first time. I believe articles of primary interest should be published on the front page in Korean script.
Hong: Then articles about places like the Sin'ganhoe would end up on the front page. Honestly, the current front-page articles don't seem to have direct relevance; it feels like watching a gambling game.
Sŏl: The Osaka Asahi, in its Korean edition, puts so-called "headline stories" [頭記事] about Korea at the top, and articles unrelated to Korea are pushed down to the third or fourth column.
Wŏn: Writing purely in Korean script is good, but then "opportunism" [日和見主義] would have to be called "sunflower-ism" [해바라기主義], wouldn't it? There are so many incomprehensible Korean words these days.
Sŏl: Our newspaper absolutely does not use such words.
Wŏn: Then I'll pick them out and send them to you. (Laughter) Articles appearing in newspapers and magazines these days require annotations [註釋] to be understood. Please, just write in Korean...!
Sŏl: It will gradually become like that.
Hong: There are many incomprehensible words, right? "Until somewhere goes away" [어디서 얼마 去하기까지], translating the Japanese 'tonikaku' (とにかく - anyway) as 'tokkippul' (토끼뿔 - rabbit horn), 'totan-ha' (トタンハ -> totanban トタン板 - corrugated iron sheet) as 'yangch'ŏlp'an' (양철판 - tin plate)... (Laughter)
Chu: Translating 'Kane ga naru' (鐘ガナル - the bell rings) as 'chong-i toenda' (종이 된다 - becomes paper)... (Laughter)
Yi Chong: This is turning into an exposé of ignorant reporters.
Sŏl: It's not just due to ignorance; there are many reasons. When editing time is tight, 'totan-ha' becomes 'yangch'ŏlp'an'. (Laughter)² <43>
Hong: Also, there are many errors in wire reports [通信], but because things are rushed, these errors often get overlooked. It's difficult to have the leeway to correct errors in wire reports...
Chu: That's why there should be a department specializing solely in that. Another thing, I don't understand the reason for stretching newspaper articles that could be five lines long into twenty lines. For instance, "There wasn't even room to stand [立錐의 여지가 없엇다]," "The security [警戒] was formidable [어마어마 햇다]." At a certain Japanese newspaper company, a reporter wrote "Keikai ga monomonoshii" (警戒ガ 物物シイ - Security was imposing/formidable), and apparently got severely scolded by the editor-in-chief. They asked, "If you say security was 'monomonoshii', how many people gathered, how many plainclothes and uniformed police were there, how many cars were there, write that so we know why it was 'monomonoshii'; how else can we know?"
Ch'un: That's why reporters often write articles about lecture meetings without even attending. "Before the scheduled time, the venue was packed to capacity [大滿員], leaving no room to stand [立錐의 餘地가 없엇고]. The speaker delivered a profound and impassioned speech [意味深長한 熱辯을 吐하야], receiving rounds of applause [박수 갈채를 받고], and the meeting concluded peacefully [무사히 散會하엿다더라]." (Laughter)
Chu: The local news sections seem to be entirely filled with such embellished descriptions [條飾].

Reporter Activities and News Gathering

Chu: Korean newspaper articles are always monotonous [千變一律]. In terms of news gathering, isn't there more room for development in areas like the society section?
Wŏn: Currently, reporters don't even go to places like Ch'ŏndogyo or the Sin'ganhoe unless asked to publish something, right? Even when asked, not everything gets published...
Sŏl: There's little worth writing about among the articles people ask us to publish. We don't send reporters because there's no news material, not because we don't want to. If there's material, we send reporters anywhere. They always go to the police station or the courthouse because that's where they can gather the most news in the shortest time with the least effort, so they have no choice but to go.
Ch'un: If there's newsworthy material at Ch'ŏndogyo, reporters go there often too. But there needs to be a significant event.
Chu: Even the Ch'ŏndogyo schism incident was only treated as news after the split had already occurred, wasn't it? If reporters visited regularly, they would have known the signs [徵兆] before such a major event happened.
Wŏn: That's right. You need to constantly visit and stir things up to generate topics for discussion [말꺼리]. If they don't even show up once, how would they know if something happens?
Ch'un: The important news items for newspapers are the movements of organizations and the movements of people. But where are the organizations in Korea, and who are the people newsworthy enough? Even if there are people, you can only praise them; if even slightly negative news [나뿐 記事] comes out, it causes a huge fuss! Who should we feature in the newspaper? Mr. XXX? Mr. XX? It would only cause trouble and be of no benefit.
Chu: If there were competing newspapers, things would develop.
Sŏl: Of course. Without competing papers [경쟁紙], stagnation is inevitable...
Ch'un: There's fundamentally a lack of events in Korea...
Chu: If someone like Pa Il-n [巴人 - pseudonym] creates totalitarianism or something, it can become news anytime. Stagnation occurs because there are no competing papers.
Yi In: Aren't there reporters covering the social sphere? If they were made to visit various organizations like the Chamber of Commerce and Industry and individuals just once a week, considerable news would likely emerge.
Chu: In my opinion, even now, if we assigned 2 or 3 trainee reporters [見習記者] to each field reporter [外勤記者], significant news could be generated. Maybe raise salaries for getting lots of scoops [특종].
Sŏl: That might work.
Chu: Reporters should feel honored if just one of the stories they bring back gets published, but if everything they write gets printed, how can there be vitality [生氣]...
Hong: Also, reporters should occasionally change their beats and the regions they cover <44> to avoid stagnation.
Kim Yŏ: I think there are too many reporters in Korea. Perhaps compared to foreign countries, but in one place I saw with a population of 30,000, there was only one 'reporter' (field reporter - 外勤記者).
Sŏl: Was there a news agency [통신사] there?
Kim Yŏ: Yes.
Sŏl: That explains it. If Korea also had a Korean-language news agency, the kind of news anyone can get by going to the police station or the Government-General, what they call 'front-door seed' news [玄關種], could be obtained from the agency. If that happened, two reporters serving as a so-called 'guerrilla unit' [遊擊隊], just wandering around gathering news, would be sufficient. However, because Korea has Japanese news agencies but no Korean-language agency operating from a Korean perspective, each company sends out many people individually to do the work that a news agency should be doing.
Hong: Actually, Korean newspapers have too few reporters.

New Newspaper, New Plan

Wŏn: Mr. Yi In, since you're planning to start a newspaper, please make it purely in Korean script.
Sŏl: Mr. Wŏn Sehun says 20,000 or 30,000 wŏn would suffice, but I think a minimum of 500,000 wŏn would be needed.
Yi In: The XX Ilbo only needs 150,000 wŏn. If you carefully examine how the XX Ilbo has been managed, it struggled due to debt, but its monthly income and expenses balanced out. I can't go into details here, but if 20,000 wŏn is used to pay off minor debts, 30,000 wŏn to sort out advertising and branches, and 100,000 wŏn to operate while expanding advertising in Tokyo and Osaka, the budget can be balanced within six months.
Chu: As long as no disputes arise.
Ch'un: If I were to start a newspaper, I would do this: establish branch offices in each district [洞支局] of Kyŏngsŏng, publish news like "Sŏdaemun News" or "Tongdaemun News," make the paper size [紙型] slightly larger than "Tonggwang," a 4-page newspaper, set the price [定價] at around 25 chŏn. Doing this would guarantee success. Didn't the "Franklin Newspaper" succeed that way too?
Chu: If only the Newspaper Law [新聞紙法] is revised and newspapers can be published freely, all sorts of newspapers could emerge and succeed.
Sŏl: If I were to start a newspaper, I'd make it a small-format newspaper, changing the layout for different editions like an intra-city edition [市內版], extra-city edition [市外版], and an Inch'ŏn edition [仁川版]. Doing so could attract more than double the current readership of the Tong-A Ilbo. Firstly, people in a particular region are interested in news from that region...
Ch'un: Doing it that way (like my plan - reporter's note) firstly makes collecting subscription fees easy, and secondly, attracts many readers. People within the walls of Kyŏngsŏng have tastes unimaginable to people in the provinces; for instance, something like Mr. Yi XX's reception room [사랑] could be featured continuously for many days with `tachikiri` (lead story status) and receive a great reception. People within Seoul have their own unique world, so if a newspaper centered purely on the Seoul system—including places like Kaesŏng, Inch'ŏn, Ansŏng, Ŭijŏngbu—were created focusing on Seoul, I think it could sell over 30,000 copies in Seoul alone.
Sŏl: Doing it that way (according to my plan - reporter's note) also makes it easy to get advertisements. Papers like the `Daimai` (likely Ōsaka Mainichi) do this. For example, an advertisement limited to P'yŏngyang doesn't need to pay the expensive rate for the All-Korea edition [全鮮版], but if a P'yŏngyang edition is created, it can be published there for a cheaper rate. Whether to publish that ad in other regional editions can be decided at the newspaper's convenience [便宜]. For instance, when changing the P'yŏngyang edition to, say, an Inch'ŏn edition, the P'yŏngyang ad space could conveniently contain Inch'ŏn ads or articles. Also, a smaller format means less empty space, offering the convenience [便利] of flexibly adjusting [伸縮] articles and ads.
Chu: Time has run too long, let's stop here for today. Thank you all.
(Transcribed by one reporter; please understand [海諒] that not everything said was included.) <45>

**Summary**

The roundtable discussion from 1932 explores the persistent failures of Korean newspaper businesses, attributing them to financial mismanagement, lack of owner experience, the restrictive government permit system (許可制), limited readership, and the absence of vibrant political life under colonial rule. Participants debated the newspaper's role – balancing being a public 'bible' (聖經) or moral guide (木鐸) with commercial viability. They criticized current editing practices, like prioritizing politics over more popular social news on page one (an imitation of Japanese models despite different contexts), the difficult mixed script, sensationalism in social reporting, and formulaic writing. Future plans discussed included creating more localized newspapers (city/district-specific editions), potentially morning editions, using pure Hangul, and improving news gathering beyond reliance on official sources, possibly spurred by competition if the restrictive laws were eased.

From a historical perspective, this discussion is highly revealing about the challenges faced by the Korean press under Japanese colonial rule. The explicit mention of the permit system (許可制), censorship (検閲), and the difficulty of covering sensitive topics highlights the lack of press freedom. The debate over language (Hanja-mixed vs. pure Hangul) reflects ongoing cultural and nationalistic currents. The financial struggles and comments on inexperienced management provide insight into the nascent state of Korean capitalism and media enterprise during this period. Historians would find the candid discussion on operational difficulties, the critique of imitating Japanese models without adaptation, and the proposed strategies for future newspaper development valuable for understanding colonial media economics, cultural politics, and nationalist aspirations.

신문편집에 관하야

洪. 朝鮮신문은 정치면은 한문과 朝鮮文을 섞어 쓰게 되어서 여러사람이 다 알아 보기에 곤란합니다.
薜. 그것은 전통문제이니까 고칠 수 잇지오.
薜. 東亞日報에서 社說을 한글로 쓴다고 오즉 말이 많앗습니까.
朱. 장차 신문용어는 어떠케 될 것 같습니까. 李相協氏는 쉬운 한자를 섞어서 朝鮮文으로 쓰게 될 것이라고 합디다.

洪. 그러면 新幹會같은데 記事를 一面에 싯게 되겟구만요. 사실 지금 一面記事는 직접 리해관게가 잇는 것이 아니고 꼭 노름구경하는 것 같습니다.
