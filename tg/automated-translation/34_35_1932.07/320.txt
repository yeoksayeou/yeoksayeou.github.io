Song from the Mountaintop
						Yi Ŭng-su

One warm afternoon when the wind was still,
To cure my boredom (無聊), I climbed Samyŏngsan (四明山).
There, upon a particularly high, distant peak,
As my clothes fluttered in the vast sky (萬里의 天空),
My heart, unbeknownst to me, began to unfold its coiled spirit
And spread its wings over every corner of the boundless expanse (茫茫) below my eyes (眼下).

Before me lay the rough waves of the East Sea (東海),
Behind me, mountain peaks (뫼부리) stretched endlessly as far as the eye could see,
Above my head, the blue sky (하눌) arched over,
And my heart spontaneously began to produce grand thoughts.
While my voice flew out to the sea and echoed commands (呼令) across the mountain ridges,
My shrunken spirit (기삀¸) grew ever more boastful.

My steps, once accustomed to the lotus walk (蓮步) and the ox walk (牛步), <92>
Now strode boldly across the mountaintop,
And my song, which used to groan (呻吟) in the quiet (靜寂) inner room,
Shouted, "A grand plan (雄圖) to turn the world around (回天) with one decisive throw (乾坤一擲)!"
Then I recited "Standing a horse on the highest peak of Osan (立馬吳山第一峯)"
And muttered the phrase, "Looking down (瞰下) from the highest peak of the Alps (「아루푸스」)".
—End— <93>

**Summary**

The poem describes the author's ascent of Samyŏngsan mountain to escape boredom. Reaching a high peak, the expansive view and lofty position inspire feelings of grandeur and power. The author contrasts their previously timid movements and quiet groans with bold strides and loud shouts of ambitious phrases drawn from history and literature, expressing a newfound, perhaps exaggerated, sense of strength and potential. From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, is interesting for its expression of escape from confinement (boredom, the inner room) to a place of freedom and power (the mountaintop). The shouts of "turning the world around" and standing atop the highest peak, even referencing foreign mountains like the Alps, could subtly reflect suppressed nationalistic aspirations or a desire for agency and liberation in a restrictive political climate. Historians might find the contrast between the constrained self and the liberated, empowered self on the mountain a poignant reflection of the psychological landscape of intellectuals under colonial rule.

嶺上의 노래
						李應洙
바람이 잔 어느 따스한 날 오후에
내 無聊를 醫하려 四明山에를 올랏습니다.
그곳 그중 높은 봉오리에 아득하게 올라서
옷자락을 萬里의 天空에 펄럭거리고 잇을 새에
내 마음은 나도 몰래 감앗은 기세를 펴기 시작하야
茫茫한 내 眼下의 구석구석에 나래를 펼쳐 놓앗습니다.

앞에는 東海의 거츠른 물껼이 누엇고
뒤에는 눈길이 모질도록 뫼부리가 이어 잇엇으며
머리우에는 푸른 하눌이 덮인 곳에서
마음은 空然이 壯한 것을 내기 시작하고
소리는 바다로 날고 山嶺에 呼令을 거듭하는 동안에
꿈추러들엇든 내 기삀¸는 더욱 더 뽐내엇습니다.

蓮步와 牛步를 일삼든 내 걸음은＜92＞
아주 勇한채 山上을 뚜벅거려 보고
靜寂의 골방속에서 呻吟하든 내 노래가
『乾坤一擲에 回天의 雄圖』를 웨첫습다.
그리고는 立馬吳山第一峯을 읊어보고
『「아루푸스」의 최고峯에서 瞰下』의 一句를 중얼거렷습니다.
―끝― ＜93＞
