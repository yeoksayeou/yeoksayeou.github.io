Song Heard from the Farming Village, Student Literary Scene
						Fushun Middle School, Kim Ch'angsik
Respectfully sending this song to the unknown elder brother, Sŏl Chŏngsik-kun

Get up, get up, get up!
Quickly, stand, stand! Little brother.
Hey! You little kid, is your leg broken?
When your big brother urges you on impatiently, stand up briskly!
So, that rotten, seemingly broken, crippled nag, and draped over it, the hard bridle rope that makes one stumble – throw it away!
And keeping step with my shout, chest thrust out like a plowshare,
Run forth—run forth!—to the yellow field of grass you see ahead!
So learn the strength of the hoe grasped in your brother’s arm!
And learn the vibrant vitality (意氣) of your brother who dashes across the plains, hoe on his shoulder, whenever he receives the city’s scorn! <62>
—Your brother’s vitality, which will not be taken by anyone.

When the light from the sun’s dynamo, spinning into action in the early morning,
Casts a shadow beneath the riverside willow by the embankment,
I met a companion walking listlessly along the embankment path.
『The girl next door went off to marry,
shedding bitter tears,
The bachelor in the back house tears at his aching chest,
and goes to hang himself』
As he listlessly muttered thus,
I glared and shouted out this song—

**Summary**

This poem fragment by a student, Kim Ch'angsik, is a passionate exhortation addressed to a younger figure, urging him to rise above rural despair and hardship. The speaker contrasts the despondency represented by a "crippled nag" and a companion mourning local tragedies (forced marriage, suicide) with his own fierce vitality ('意氣'), symbolized by the strength of his hoe and his resilience against urban contempt. He calls for action, urging the younger person to cast off burdens and run towards the future, learning the speaker's unyielding spirit. From a historical perspective, the poem is interesting for its raw depiction of rural suffering potentially exacerbated by colonial conditions, its expression of youthful Korean energy and defiance ('意氣') even while residing in Manchuria (Fushun), and its direct address to the established poet Sŏl Chŏngsik, suggesting an engagement with the broader literary scene and perhaps nationalist sentiments.

삼가 未知의 兄 薜貞植君에 이 노래를 보냄

일어나라 일어나라 일어나!
냉큼 서거라 서라! 동생아
야! 이 어린 것아 다리가 부러젓느냐?
어서 바삐 형이 재촉하고 섯을 때 선뜻 이러서거라!
그래서 그 썩어 부러질뜯한 병신 숫당말을 그리고 걸처 넘어지게 굳은 말재갈 노끈을 던져버리고
내 고함에 발맟어 정겡이 부리걷은채 가슴버리고
앞에 보이는 누른 풀밭으로 내여 달어라-내여 달어라!
그래서 형 팔뚝에 쥐인 광이의 힘을 배우라!
그리고 때로는 도시의 흘림을 받을 때마다 광이를 멘 채로 原野를 내닷는 형의 산 意氣를 배우라!＜62＞
-누구에게도 빼끼지 않을 형의 意氣를.
첫아츰 핑글핑글 發動되는 태양 發光機의 빛이
최뚝옆 개버들밑에 그림자 그려놀 때
나는 맥없이 최뚝길것는 동모를 맛낫노라
『앞집처녀 쓰린 눈물 흘리며
시집을 가고
뒷집총각 앞은 가슴 뜯으며
목매려간다』
그는 맥없이 이러케 중얼대기에
부릅뜨고 웨처 들려준 노래잇으니-
