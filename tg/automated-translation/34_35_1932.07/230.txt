**웃고 우는 내마음 (My Laughing and Crying Heart)**
**Pak Ch'ŏn-juk (朴天竹)**

"There is always sorrow in those who always laugh." Someone must have said this. It clearly doesn't seem to be my own words, but I can't recall whose words they are either. If this statement is true, then my friends who call me a "laughing person"! Does that not mean I am designated as a "sad person"?

That would be my fatal flaw, if it is one. Why should I have to be a sad person? And I do not wish to be included among my friends in such a position.

When I laugh while facing others, I hold not even the smallest sorrow. If there is someone who interprets my laughter as a "perverse expression of sadness," how sorry my laughter must be to them. I do not laugh to aid others' pleasure, nor do I laugh to eliminate my own sadness. When I laugh, it is simply "laughing," nothing more.

Then, will someone ask if I have no sorrow? Of course, there is sorrow. Sometimes there is the sorrow of being human, among which there is the sorrow of being a Korean (朝鮮人), and sometimes there is the sorrow of being a woman, and among that, there is also the sorrow of being "me."

There are times when the sorrow enveloped in this situation, this environment, this fate makes me cry. However, I absolutely do not engage in the painful work of transforming that sorrow into laughter to show others. <64>

When sad thoughts arise, I do not meet others. I strive to avoid people. And I simply wait alone for time to pass.

However, here I have another thought. Times of laughter are good. Times of crying are also good. After laughing, I feel refreshed. After crying, I also feel refreshed. Like after the wind has blown, like after the rain has fallen, my heart rather becomes clear and cool (爽凉).

But sometimes—when there is no laughter and no tears, when a desolate facial expression appears in the mirror, there is no time my heart aches more than then. After that time passes, the memory of it rather makes my heart ache.

If you want to laugh, laugh. And if you want to cry, cry. If you are not laughing, then rather cry. Although you may cry, please let there be no "time when one neither laughs nor cries." That time, indeed, makes my heart more desolate and painful.

- June 3, 1932 -

**群統 (Group Traditions/Lineages)**
**Pak Hwa-sŏng (朴花城)**

Ch'unhyang, purely as a Chosŏn woman appearing in a work, possessed three special types of traditions (統). First, the *kisaeng* tradition (妓統); second, the beauty tradition (美統); and third, the virtuous woman tradition (烈女統). Since all three are beautiful traditions, it is perhaps understandable that there is a widely disseminated Ch'unhyang tradition (春香統). However, the advertisements prominently featuring "Ch'unhyang Festival (春香祭)" and "Ch'unhyang Festival Sponsorship Association (春香祭協贊會)" in front of a certain train station's main gate and in newspapers, with their special large lettering, seem unlikely to be the 1932 style, especially today when there are Labor Festival (勞働祭) and Revolution Festival (革命祭) traditions. Granted, it's a world where disciples of the absolute idealist Christ tradition (基督統) and students of the absolute materialist Marx tradition (맑스統) even fall in love, but no matter how I think about it, this seems to be a propagation tradition (普及統) of a special class tradition (特殊階級統) unique to Chosŏn. It is both frustrating and lamentable (憤且嘆).

**두 對照 (Two Contrasts)**
**[No Author Listed, but context suggests Pak Hwa-sŏng continues]**

The time is early summer—the place is above and below the high stone embankment surrounding the spacious flower garden of a certain Japanese residence. The lush green shade seems to cherish and embrace the fragrances of various flowers—red roses, yellow roses, orchids, etc.—that have reached their peak beauty. Three or four girls in Western attire are playing cheerfully amidst the flower bushes. Below the stone embankment, along the roadside, old men with white hair sit in a row, breaking stones with hammers (마치). The midday sun mercilessly beats down on them, and also on young women nursing babies while diligently lifting and dropping their hammers. "How much do you earn if you break stones all day?" "Oh dear, my back... At best, ten *jŏn* (십전), otherwise eight *jŏn* (8錢)!" The old man speaks with a sigh, barely lifting the heavy hammer again. They frequently waste time washing out the stone dust that gets into their eyes. A three or four-year-old child, dressed in tattered clothes stained with grime, with a face showing malnutrition, who had been playing alone with the small broken stones, rubs their eyes and cries with a sound like being squeezed. The nursing mother pulls her breast away abruptly, looks back, and then she too rubs her eyes and cries, perhaps because stone dust got into her own eyes. The mother puts down her hammer, sighs, and tries to soothe the children, but when they don't listen, she screams and hits them. The thorns of various hardships in daily life have turned her into a person with sharp nerves. The children cry even louder. The green shade of the flower garden sways, and children rush over, tossing leftover snacks down below the road. The crying child stops crying instantly, eyes wide open as if never having been hurt, <65> searching for and eating the snacks. The smaller child holds out a hand, asking for some too. The crying has stopped. The mother sends eyes of gratitude upward and once again grasps the hammer in her right hand and the round, wheeled handle (to prevent stones from scattering) in her left hand. Above and below the stone embankment—what do these two contrasts signify?

**벌과에 헤매는 내마음 (My Heart Wandering in the Field)**
**Son Ch'o-ak (孫初岳)**

To be bound or restricted in any matter must be truly unpleasant. For my recent thoughts, I was assigned a limit of three manuscript pages, but writing this essay brings up frustrating distress. What one intends to do seems incredibly significant before writing it. One feels compelled to find a clear resolution to the issue somehow. One must push the investigation forward most vigorously and refine it most meticulously to break through the impasse (유끼쓰마리 - *yukitsumari*, Japanese for deadlock/impasse). Even if one picks up the pen with such demands, looking at what was written later, dissatisfaction arises not just from failing to achieve the expected or intended results, but also from seeing that the original issue itself was nothing more than a trivial matter (𤨏未事). One realizes it would have been fine not to write it. And one realizes that the most important thing that absolutely must be written still remains unwritten.

The important things cannot be spoken, no matter how hard one tries. They cannot be written. If spoken, they transform into something entirely different, and if written, they immediately become falsehoods. This makes my state of mind unbearably tormented. Finally, I threw down my pen and ended up wandering onto a wide field with a solitary (孤寂) and empty (空虛) heart.

So today too, as I was wandering the field, a story—I can't recall exactly when, perhaps during elementary school—flashed into my mind like lightning (電光). At that time, I didn't understand its meaning in detail.

A magpie picked up a few peacock feathers and stuck them among its own black feathers, appearing proudly before the crowd of birds in the forest.
"How about this? Aren't my feathers magnificent?"
All the birds spared no cries of admiration (讚嘆) for the beautiful feathers, and right then and there, they elected the magpie president of the forest nation. And so, when the celebration banquet (祝賀宴) was held, the magpie, while dancing wildly (허무진 舞蹈) with a swan, ended up dropping every single one of its magnificent feathers.
The forest birds immediately raised a fuss (야료) and, rushing in unison, pecked this swindler (협잡군) to death.

After that, this time a real peacock came strutting into the forest.
"How about this? Aren't my feathers truly magnificent?"
The peacock spread its rainbow-colored tail feathers like a fan. However, the forest birds unanimously showed suspicion. But was that all? An owl remarked, "That one must be of the same kind as the swindler," and all the birds simultaneously rushed in and pecked this peacock to death as well.—

As the sun was about to set and my sorrow was about to deepen further, from the distant forest, the plaintive song of a shepherd boy drifted, and a calf, holding a mouthful of green shade (綠陰), crossed the hill. Oh! Today too, <66> having come to this field, thinking of the father who will not return, my heart returns, embracing an unceasing, heart-rending grief!

**市場과 專門學生아가씨 (The Market and the Vocational School Miss)**
**Kim Cha-hye (金慈惠)**

Today too, after returning from the company (社), I changed into plain clothes and went out to the market to bargain for side dishes. I could have sent the servant (下人), but to change the suffocating feeling accumulated throughout the day, I went myself to the market where vegetables and fish (魚物) are piled up like mountains and the shouts of sellers ring deafeningly in the ears. "Excuse me! How much is a *kŭn* (근 - approx. 600g) of spinach?"
"Yes—ma'am, just five *jŏn* (오전)."
"Five *jŏn*? Oh—why is it so expensive?"
"Yes—ma'am. Spinach is just expensive these days. Then, how much will you offer?"
"Let's make it three *jŏn* (삼전)."

This kind of haggling (에누리) and bargaining was awkward at first, and the words wouldn't come out, but once I got used to it, the fun of bargaining is quite something. During my vocational school (전문학교) days, even when buying small items at the department store, I would ask for delivery, but now I carry armfuls of spinach, crown daisy, and fish, striding briskly across intersections where taxis speed by.

If you feel like committing suicide, come out to the market. Merchants trembling to save even one more *p'un* (푼 - smallest unit of currency), bargain hunters clutching tightly, trying to buy even a little cheaper—competitors shouting at the top of their lungs, each trying to sell only their own goods—enter among them, look around in all directions, and the attachment to "living" surges up. Those entering with a full load of vegetables, joking around; those who have sold everything, going to a corner, rummaging through grimy pouches, counting coins by rule of thumb (주먹구구), frowning—everyone seems to be clinging to the rope called "Life" (生), straining their muscles until veins bulge. They do not ask "Why live?" They just try to live, working hard until their arms, legs, and faces turn copper-colored and become hard as iron. My weak heart, which used to be discouraged by trivial matters, gets swept away by their vibrant current, gaining new courage and strength (强力) before returning home.

**때묻은 치마와 파란치마 (The Stained Skirt and the Blue Skirt)**
**Hong Ka-ŭn (洪加恩)**

It was last Sunday when I was crossing the Chongno intersection (鐘路네거리). From behind, hearing a voice ask, "Excuse me, where do I catch the streetcar (車) to Kwanghŭimun (光熙門)?"—<67> I turned around to see a woman of about thirty, wearing a worn-out black cotton skirt (광목치마) and a cotton blouse (무명저고리) shiny with grease stains, looking up at me.

As I walked her closer to the stop (정류장), the woman lifted the hem of her black skirt to wipe the sweat from her forehead, which resembled oiled paper (장판지), and asked again. "If I take the Kwanghŭimun-bound (光熙門行) car, will it go to Hwanggŭmjŏng (黃金町)?" "Is there a job information center (직업안내소) in Hwanggŭmjŏng?" "Will they help me find any job easily there?" etc... I answered her, albeit without much energy.

Turning back, wondering if she got on the right streetcar—I saw her still there, grabbing another woman and speaking in her strong Kyŏngsang Province (慶尙道) dialect. Gesturing with her hands—among her words, hearing, "Are you sure this is where I catch the streetcar to Kwanghŭimun?" I flinched. She hadn't fully trusted my words. Why must she doubt?—Walking along, it was a thought of no great use and nothing new, yet I thought it again. Even today, I repeat it. Isn't she the representation (表象) of us?

Those eyes, clouded with anxiety stemming from solitude, hunger, ignorance (無知), and doubt—isn't that woman our picture? Our mirror—yes. She revealed it starkly with those eyes full of great anxiety and fear, her worn and stained skirt and blouse, and her unreserved manner of speech, while we merely concealed it with brand-new white silk blouses (사비단 저고리) and blue silk skirts with fine pleats (파랑비단 잔주름치마). We merely hid it on faces like oiled paper with powder (분) and rouge (연지). She was unconsciously truthful, and we were unconsciously fabricating falsehoods; that is the only difference.

**中國野菜商人 (Chinese Vegetable Merchant)**
**Ch'oe I-kwŏn (崔以權)**

Whether it rains or the wind blows, without fail, diligently and faithfully, a Chinese vegetable merchant visits our house every morning. One day, he put down the basket (광주리) he carried on his back and, while resting, unfolded a torn piece of paper (조히조각 - *johi jogak*, older form of 종잇조각) and mumbled something to himself. Going closer, I saw it was a map of Manchuria (滿洲地圖) printed on an old newspaper scrap that had been used to wrap the bananas he sells. Pointing his finger at 'Pongch'ŏn' (奉天 - Mukden) and 'Changch'un' (長春), he read them and then, with an expression that seemed both very nostalgic and pleased, he was still smiling. Someone standing nearby asked, "What's that?"
"This is our China country, good thing," he said, smiling again.
"Do you have a wife?" "Have." "Where?" "In our China country." "Children?" "Children also all there." "Then why did you come here alone?" "Our people earn money, earn lots of money, I also go China, good!"

I heard this exchange (問答). I don't know his name (姓名) or his background (來歷). I only know that he comes every day carrying a heavy vegetable basket and exchanges it for one or two *p'un*. But besides that, I have seen something else in him. While sweating profusely, even when his fingers are chapped from the cold, he comes without missing a single day, with diligence and industriousness, showing an expression absorbed in the pleasure of earning money, even if it's just one copper coin earned through his own effort. The way he values (貴) and cherishes it, and how he puts all his effort into what he does, feeling satisfied, showing no signs of boredom, but rather <68> living with a face seemingly smiling with great hope. One day, I took his account book (치부책) and wrote in it with a fountain pen. He was startled and seemed disappointed that I hadn't used a pencil. Some time later, when I received his book again, except for what I had written with the fountain pen the previous day—"Month X, Day Y, Cabbage 5 *jŏn* (배추5錢)"—everything from the beginning to the end had been cleanly erased. Only then did I realize the reason he asked me not to use a fountain pen, and at the same time, I understood that his account book (a small notebook that looked like it wasn't even worth 5 *jŏn*) was used for at least 4 years or more.

From this, I learned. When accumulating money, no, when seeking profit (利), instead of scheming through deceitful tricks and envy (시기), it is most appropriate to devote all passion to the work one can do, be satisfied and faithful in that work, respect and protect one's own work gravely, and seek profit (利) and gain benefit (益) from within that.

This morning too, that Chinese merchant is wiping the beads of sweat falling on his forehead with the black towel tucked at his side, trudging up the hill path.

**비오는 날 밤 (A Rainy Night)**
**Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi (崔貞熙)**

When I woke up to the sound of a baby crying for milk, it was two o'clock deep in the night. Outside the window, the rain was still falling, the sound of dripping rainwater sounded mournful, and the ticking sound of the clock by my head, along with my mother-in-law's (싀어머님) snoring, made my scattered thoughts even more confused.

The dream I had woken from lingered so vividly before my eyes that I wondered if it might have been reality. I looked around the room, but as my weary sleep receded and my mind cleared, I recognized that it was definitely a dream, not a hallucination (환영). But if it was a dream, it was an exceedingly long one. I recounted this story to K.

"When I was six, I attended a village school (서당), and the teacher (훈장) cherished and loved me very much. So, when the boys couldn't recite their lessons (강), he invariably had me go around and slap all their cheeks. They would remain still while being slapped, but after the teacher went out to the latrine (변소) or somewhere, the class leader (접짱) and everyone else would gang up on me and hit me. Knowing I had done nothing wrong, I would just shed tears without being able to resist at all. Even though I wielded such power when the teacher was beside me! It seems just like one aspect of my life these days." My dream ended when the story reached this point. Without hearing a single word from K. Even though it was a dream, I had anticipated what words of comfort K would offer if I told this story.

Reliving the broken dream, I felt that perhaps everyone feels desolate and loses strength if they lack someone who understands (리해) and defends (?) them.

The falling rain soaked the ground and soaked my soul. My melancholic soul, thoroughly drenched by the fiercely falling rain, wandered the rainy streets deep in the night as if mad, feeling solitude (고독) and sorrow (비애). (On the night of June 13th) <69>

**Summary**

This collection, "Women's Essays," features short personal reflections by several Korean women writers in 1932. The essays cover a range of themes: Pak Ch'ŏn-juk contemplates the nature of her laughter and sorrow; Pak Hwa-sŏng critiques the commercialization of tradition (Ch'unhyang) and observes stark class contrasts between Japanese residents and Korean laborers; Son Ch'o-ak reflects on the difficulty of expressing profound thoughts and uses an allegory about borrowed feathers; Kim Cha-hye finds vitality and a will to live in the bustling energy of the marketplace, contrasting it with her past self; Hong Ka-ŭn sees a reflection of hidden anxieties and societal pretense in a poor, distrustful woman encountered on the street; Ch'oe I-kwŏn admires the diligence, frugality, and hopeful spirit of a Chinese vegetable seller; and Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi recounts a dream connecting childhood experiences of power and vulnerability to her present feelings of isolation. For historians, these essays offer valuable insights into the inner lives and social observations of educated Korean women during the colonial period. They reveal personal struggles with identity, emotion, and societal constraints, alongside sharp commentary on class disparity, colonial realities (Japanese residence vs. Korean laborers), cultural shifts, and the search for meaning and connection in a challenging environment.

웃고 우는 내마음
朴天竹

「늘 웃는 사람에게는 늘 슬픔이 잇느니라」고. 어느 분이 이런 말을 하엿든지. 분명히 내 말은 아닌 것 같은데 그러나 뉘말인지도 생각나지 안는다. 만일 이 말이 진리라 하면 날더러 「웃는 사람」이라 부르는 벗들이어! 나는 그대로 「슬픈 사람」이라는 指目을 받는 것이 아니냐.
그것이 내게 致命傷이라면 致命傷일 것이다. 웨 내가 슬픈 사람이 되어야 할 것인가. 또 나는 그러한 지위로 벗들 속에 끼이고 싶지는 아니하다.
나는 남과 대하여 웃을 때엔 조고마한 슬픔도 가지는 것이 없다. 만약 내 웃음을 「슬픔의 변태적 표정」이라 해석하고 보는 이가 잇다하면 내 웃음이 그에게 얼마나 죄송하냐. 나는 남의 유쾌를 도으려고 웃는 것도 아니요 내 슬픔을 제거하려고 웃는 것도 아니다. 내가 웃는 것은 다만 「웃는 것」 그것뿐이다.
그러면 내게 슬픔이 없느냐고 뉘가 묻겟지? 물론 슬픔은 잇다. 어느 땐 사람된 슬픔도 잇고 그 중에도 朝鮮사람된 슬픔도 잇고 또 어느 땐 여자된 슬픔도 잇고 그 중에 또 「나」된 슬픔도 잇다.
이 처지, 이 환경, 이 운명에 싸인 내 슬픔이 나를 울리는 때도 잇는 것이다. 그러나 나는 결코 그 슬픔을 웃음으로 化하여 남에게 보이는 괴로운 사업은 하지 안는다.＜64＞
슬픈 생각이 일때면 나는 남을 대하는 일이 없다. 힘써 남을 피해 버린다. 그리고 혼자서 시간이 지나기를 기다릴 뿐이다.
그런데 나는 여기 다른 한가지 생각하는 것이 잇다. 웃는 때도 좋다. 또 우는 때도 좋다. 웃고나도 시원. 또 울고나도 시원하다. 바람분 뒤 같이 비온 뒤 같이 오히려 내마음은 맑고 爽凉해진다.
그러나 어느 때-웃음도 없고 눈물도 없이 적막한 얼굴빛이 거울속에 나타날 때 나는 그때처럼 마음 아픈 때는 없다. 그 시간이 지난 뒤에는 오히려 그 기억이 내 마음을 아프게 한다.
웃으려거든 웃어라. 그리고 또 울려거든 울어라. 웃지않거든 차라리 울어라. 비록 울지언정 「웃지도 않고 울지도 않는 그러한 때」는 부대 없거라 그때야 말로 도리어 더 내마음을 적막케 하고 아프게 한다.

中國野菜商人
崔以權

비가 오거나 바람이 불거나 하로같이 쉬지않고 부즈런하고 충성되게 매일아츰 우리집을 찾어오는 中國人 야채商이 잇다. 어떤날 그는 메고온 광주리를 나려 놓고 쉬이면서 찢어진 조히조각을 펴들고 무어라고 혼자 중얼중얼 가까히 가서 보니 그가 팔러 다니는 빠나나 쌋던 헌신문지조각에 박힌 滿洲地圖. 『奉天』 『長春』 이것들을 손가락으로 찔러가며 읽고서 매우 그립은 듯 반가운듯한 표정으로 역시 싱글싱글 하고 잇다. 옆에 섯던 사람이 물엇다. 「그게 뭐야?」라고.
「이거 우리 중국나라 좋은거시」하고 그는 또 웃엇다.
「당신 마누라 없어?」 「잇오요」 「어데?」 「우리 중국나라 잇어」 「아이는?」 「아이도 모도 다 잇소요?」 「그럼 웨 혼자 여기 왓어」 「우리 사람이 돈이 벌어 돈이 많이 벌어 나도 중국이 갓소 좋소네!」
이러한 問答을 나는 들엇다. 나는 그의 姓名이 무엇인지 그의 來歷이 어떠한지를 모른다. 다만 날마다 무거운 채소광주리를 메고 와서 한푼 두푼 바꾸어 가는 줄만 안다. 그러나 그 밖에도 나는 그에게서 본 바가 잇다. 바지땀을 흘려가면서도 손가락이 얼어 터지면서도 하로도 빼지않고 열심과 부즈런함으로 다니며 돈버리에 자미부친 표정을 동전한푼이라도 제힘으로 얻는다. 그것을 貴해 하고 아끼는 樣을 그리고 저하는 일에 힘을 다하야 만족해 하면서 조금도 실증을 내지않고 도리어＜68＞ 크다란 희망에 웃고 사는듯한 얼굴을 어떤날 나는 그 치부책을 받아서 만년필로 써주엇섯다. 그는 깜짝 놀라며 연필로 쓰지 않은 것을 섭섭히 여겻다. 얼마후에 또다시 그의 책을 받을 때는 前日 내가 만년필로 쓴 「某月 某日 배추5錢」이라고 쓴것만 除하고는 처음부터 끝까지 말끔 지워놓은 冊을 받엇다. 그때야 나는 그가 나더러 만년필로 쓰지 말기를 請한 이유를 깨다른 동시에 그의 치부장(5錢짜리도 못될듯한 조그만 공책)이 적어도 4년以上 쓰는 것이람을 알엇다.
나는 이에서 알엇다. 돈을 모으되, 아니, 利를 바라되 간사한 속임과 시기에서 꾀하는 代身 제가 할 수 잇는 그 일에 열정을 다 하여하며 그 일에 만족하고 충성되어 제가 하는 일을 스사로 貴히 역이고 重하게 보호하는 중에서 利를 바라고 益을 取하는 것이 가장 적당한 일이라는 것을.
오늘 아츰도 그 中國상인은 니마에 떠러지는 땀방울을 옆에 찻던 시컴언 수건으로 씻으며 언덕길을 더듬어 오르고 잇다.

비오는 날 밤
崔貞熙

젓먹고 싶어 우는 어린애 소리에 눈을 뜬때는 깊은 밤새로 두시엿다. 창밖에 비는 아직도 나려서 락수 물소리가 구슬프게 들리고 머리맡에 짹깍이는 시게소리와 싀어머님의 코고는 소리도 흩어진 내 머리를 더 한층 어지럽게 하엿다.
나는 깨어진 꿈이 너무도 똑똑하게 눈앞에 어려서, 혹 생시나 아닌가 싶어 방안을 휘둘러 보앗다마는 고단한 잠이 멀어지고 정신이 맑어질수록 그것은 환영도 아닌 확실한 꿈이엇음을 인식하엇다. 그러나 꿈이라면 너무도 긴 꿈이엇다. 나는 k에게 이러한 이야기를 들려주엇다.
『내가 여섯살적에 서당에 다녓는데 훈장이 퍽 귀해하고 사랑하엿으므로 남자들이 강을 못밭히는 때면 으레히 내가 돌아다니면서 모조리 뺨을 때리도록 하엿는데 뺨맞을 적엔 가만 잇다가도 훈장이 변소에나 어디 좀 나간뒤면 접짱이하 모다가 나를 몰아주고 때렷답니다. 나는 잘못한 일이 없는 줄 알면서도 눈물만 뚝뚝 흘리며 아모 반항도 못햇지요. 훈장이 내곁에 잇을 때는 그러케 세력을 쓰면서도! 마치 요새 내 생활의 일면이 꼭 그와 같구려.』 여기까지 이야기가 게속 되엇을 때 내꿈은 깨어젓다. k의 말한마디 들어보지도 못하고. 꿈이면서도 내가 이런 이야기를 하면 k가 나를 어떠한 말로 위로해주리라는 것까지 짐작은 하고 잇엇다.
나는 깨어진 꿈을 되푸리하면서 사람은 누구나 자기를 리해하고 옹호(?)해주는 자가 없으면 적막을 느끼는 동시에 힘이 줄어지는 것인가부다고 느꼇다.
나리는 비는 땅을 적시고 내 혼을 적시엿다. 우울한 내 혼은 몹시 나리는 비에 폭젖어 밋친 듯이 깊은밤 비오는 거리를 헤매며 고독과 비애를 느꼇다. (6월 13일 밤에)＜69＞
