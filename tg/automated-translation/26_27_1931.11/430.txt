기사제목	革命詩人 하이네(1), 理性과 浪漫의 二元苦와 哲學 (Revolutionary Poet Heine (1), The Dual Suffering of Reason and Romanticism, and Philosophy)
기사형태	문예평론 (Literary Criticism)

Revolutionary Poet Heine

Let's pick out one poem, "Questions," from his poetry collection "The North Sea."

By the sea, the desolate, dark sea,
A young man stands alone.
His heart full of sorrow, his head full of doubt,
He opens his mouth plaintively and asks the blue waves.
"Ah, solve the riddle of life,
That sorrowful, ancient riddle."
...
...
The blue waves surge endlessly,
The wind blows fiercely, the clouds scatter,
The stars shine indifferently in the cold sky,
And the fool awaits the answer.

Heine, from the abyss of doubt, seemed to leverage the "World-Weariness" (Weltshmerz), a general atmosphere of the time, with romantic yearning and sorrow, yet at some point, he would express a decisive stance towards society and humanity in a refreshingly appealing manner. The thunderous rebellion, like the roaring waves of the great ocean arising from his rich poetic imagination, resulted from the racial contempt he faced as a Jew and his ceaseless mockery and negation of the exclusive, aristocratic life of the professorial class. However, on the other hand, he also strove tenaciously to obtain a professor's position, and while recuperating in Norderney, he tried to make known that he was an aristocrat by using "Von Heine" (in Germany, adding "Von" to a surname indicates nobility). These seemingly contradictory and conflicting directions within him ultimately prepared his tragedy and his heartbreaking disappointments. His rhythmic attachment to the sea, while strolling along the sand dunes under the light, contained a certain tragic beauty in its lyricism, thinking of his unforgettable lover "Amalie," yet his mind would soon steer towards the harbor of reason.

His unrestrained imaginative power, crisscrossing his mind, sought to purify the oppressive life and the suppression of speech by the ruling class into a bright, free new era.
These two ideals within him were steeped in a revolutionary spirit, as his external life was impoverished and his university life experienced chivalrous expulsion, while conversely, his contemplative mood pursuing ideals was also intensely strong. Reason, for him, found solace in the subsequent era of joy and conviction following the February and July Revolutions, deriving the "World-Weariness" (Weltshmerz) that could extract its appropriate and poignant form. Romantic sentiment, centered around Schelling and Schlegel, imprinted the historical and social *Guǐtóngnǚ* (貴童女, lit. 'noble child-woman', perhaps referring to a muse or ideal) "Sturm und Drang" <86> (狂風怒濤, lit. 'mad wind, angry waves') with more than enough passion in his heart, branding the anguish over illness (he suffered from severe headaches and in his later years, eye disease and hemiplegia, which afflicted him until death) and the lost love for the "Amalie" sisters like a flame.

Now is the time to use reason
And cast off all dullness.
For a long time, as a comic actor,
I have performed comedy with you.

The splendid backdrop
Was drawn in too romantic a style.
My knightly attire shone with gold,
And I felt too exquisite an emotion.

Now I shall be circumspect
And abandon mad garrulity.
But even more than when I played comedy,
I feel my own unhappiness.

Ah, the heart unconsciously
Says, what did I feel?
Like death within my breast,
I performed a feeble sword fight.

Amidst the dual suffering of reason and romanticism, he looked everywhere for a place to position himself. He attempted to aspire to a professor's position and visited Goethe hoping to gain some new impulse. However, for him, Bismarck's unified empire of blood and iron, then nearing completion, offered only the bared, sharp teeth of persecution and hostility from the various ruling institutions of reactionary Germany. As the trend of liberal civil rights passed its mature peak with the July 14th attack on the Bastille, the various social forms of the first half of the 19th century and the diverse ideological forms dependent upon them stood at a major turning point, influenced by the aftermath of the revolution. The processes of class formation for the emerging bourgeoisie as a class-for-itself consequently provoked the revolutionary movement of the proletariat. The era called "enlightened" by Kant (this is a historiographical method of idealist philosophers centered around certain specific figures) was succeeded by French materialism – which was the matrix of French sociology, and it is well-known that Marx was also influenced by it. The dialectical view of society that followed provided a proper scholarly method for understanding historical and social phenomena. Facing such times, Heine's longing for the discarding of the old world and its replacement by a new one, although not consciously directed towards action based on scientific understanding, necessitated, above all, a desire for the abolition of the private property system, stemming from his martyrlike passion as a poet and his worldview as one of the oppressed class. However, even if this amounted to no more than baring his heart welling up from his poetic sentiment, he indeed had another direction of thought capable of possessing the firm theory of diamond-like reason.

Nature did not create
Property. Without anything,
Without pockets attached to our bodies,
We are all born into the world.

The salvation of human life had to be realized first through the improvement and transformation of material conditions. Imitating the tone of idealists, talks of self-reform or guidance of human nature are all nonsensical sleep-talk. A fistful of bread gives more vitality to a weary body than a hundred thousand prayers. No matter how much one contemplates the *Hoengbyŏn Sibang* (橫遍 十方, pervading the ten directions horizontally) and *Sugung Samje* (竪窮三際, penetrating the three times vertically) from a spiritual excess, the power to return the growing unemployed masses and economic mechanisms suffering from the poverty of hunger to the right path must be the gospel of matter. Thus, for him, bestowing aesthetic value upon the "power of things" was unavoidable. In this respect, modern materialist <87> social thought undoubtedly flowed beneath the surface in him. This modern materialist social thought is inseparably related to the liberation movements of the 19th century. The protagonist of his epic poem "Atta Troll," the bear "Atta Troll," proclaims:

Unity, unity alone
Is the foremost demand of the age. Individually,
We become slaves. But united,
We can defeat the tyrant.

Unity! Unity! If we ripen,
The rule of dirty monopoly will fall.
We shall establish
The righteous kingdom of animals.

From this passionate poetic vision, how much admiration and praise did he offer for the revolutionary actions of the emerging forces against the old dominion! The Revolution of 1830 gave a great impulse to Heine, who was spending disillusioned months gloomily in his homeland Germany, which he loved so much yet which ultimately abused him. To Paris! That was his unsurpassed passionate longing. Paris! Paris! His impatient excitement dreaming of Paris, the capital of the newly rising, bright red masses amidst disillusionment and hostile mockery!
"I had a beautiful homeland,"
lamented this poet of betrayal and conversion, voicing his resentment.
"Ah Germany, my distant beloved,
When I think of you, I almost weep,"
he confessed, loving the Germany of his dreams more than cheerful France. However, on the other hand, the persecution by detestable authorities and his tragic pastoral song, and moreover, his rationalistic inclination that rebelled against Classicism and Romanticism and sought connections in the reality of the liberation movement, caused him to toss and turn in the pain of two contradictory directions. Thus, it was natural that he could not forget Paris, seeking a new turning point and new endeavors there.
"Freedom is the new religion. Paris is the new Jerusalem, and
the Rhine is the Jordan River dividing the holy land of freedom from the land of the profane." This yearning of his for something new, even if it did not crystallize into clear action—and thus, although in his later years he received guidance among German exiles in Paris—the turbulent and mutually repulsive dual suffering within him was natural. With reason, he affirmed the inevitable movement of history and society, driven towards social revolution, but with romanticism, he could not detach himself from reality and sever ties with the world of fantasy. Heine, who was (according to Franz Mehring) the first proclaimer of the world proletarian literary movement, stood, on the other hand, at the apex of a transitional period, concluding German Classicism and Romanticism and transmitting their legacy to a new generation—a figure rarely seen. **Summary**

This article discusses the German poet Heinrich Heine, focusing on the profound internal conflict between reason and romanticism that shaped his life and work. It portrays him as a "revolutionary poet" whose Jewish background and critique of aristocratic German society fueled his rebellious spirit, yet who also harbored personal ambitions and deep romantic sensibilities, often leading to contradictory actions and inner turmoil ("dual suffering"). The author highlights Heine's engagement with the political and social upheavals of his time (like the July Revolution), his exile in Paris, his critique of private property, and his leanings toward materialist thought, positioning him as a transitional figure between Romanticism and a new, socially conscious era, even citing Franz Mehring's view of him as a precursor to proletarian literature.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it was published in 1931 Korea under Japanese rule. The sympathetic portrayal of Heine as a "revolutionary poet," his critique of oppression and reactionary forces (Bismarck's Germany), his focus on social change, materialism ("power of things," "fistful of bread"), and the call for "Unity" against "tyrants" and "monopoly" would likely resonate with Korean intellectuals grappling with colonial subjugation and exploring various ideologies, including socialism and nationalism. The discussion of Western revolutionary thought and figures like Heine, Kant, and Marx indicates the intellectual currents circulating among Koreans seeking models for resistance and envisioning a different future, even within the constraints of censorship. Historians might find the emphasis on Heine's dualities (reason/romanticism, revolution/personal ambition) and his ultimate embrace of social critique particularly relevant to understanding the complex ideological landscape and aspirations of Korean intellectuals during this period.

革命詩人 하이네
그의 시집 「北海」에서 「疑問」이라는 시 하나를 집어내보자

바다가에 쓸쓸하고 컴컴한 바다가에
젊은이가 혼자 서잇다.
가슴은 슬픔에 차고 머리는 懷疑에 가득한데
그는 애달게 입을 열어 滄波에 물엇다.
「아 生의 수수꺾기를 풀라
그 애닯은 예로부터의 수수꺾기를」
...
...
滄波는 끝없이 출렁거리고
바람은 거칠고 구름은 허터진다
별들은 무심히 찬 한울에 빛나는데
어리석은 자는 그 대답을 기다리고 잇다.

하이네는 회의의 심연에서 당시의 일반적 분위기이든 「世界苦」를 戀情과 哀愁로 지레질하는 듯이 보이다가도 어는틈에 사회와 인간에 대한 결정적 태도를 통쾌하게 호소적으로 표명하는 것이엇다. 그의 풍요한 시상에 오는 대양의 노도같은 우렁찬 반항은 유태인이라는 민족적 멸시와 교수계급의 배타적 귀족적 생활에 대한 間斷없는 嘲弄과 否定으로 결과하엿다. 그러나 그 반면에 그는 또한 교수의 지위를 얻으려고 악착하엿고 「놀데르네」에서 靜養하고 잇을때에는 Von Heine라고 하야 자신이 귀족이라것을 알리려고 하엿다. (독일에서는 姓에 Von이 붙으면 귀족임을 표시하는 것이다) 그에게 잇어서의 일견 모순되고 반발하는 두 개의 방향이 끝끝내 그의 비극과 그의 안타가운 실연을 장만하는 것이엇다. 바다에 대한 운율적 애착이 일종의 비장미를 내포한 서정에서 光下의 砂丘를 거닐며 잊히지 안는 애인 「아말리」를 생각하다가도 어느듯 그의 두뇌는 이성의 항구로 배질하는 것이엇다.
뇌리에 종횡으로 교차하는 無碍한 구상력은 逼迫한 생활과 지배계급의 언론 강압을 명랑한 자유로운 새 세대에 정화하려 하엿다.
그에게 잇어서의 이 두가지 이념은 그의 외부생활이 곤궁하엿고 그의 대학생활이 의협적 추방도 겪엇든이만치 혁명적 기개에 浸潤되엇섯고 반면에 이상을 추구하는 관조적 정조도 지지않게 강렬하엿섯다. 이성은 그에게서 그 적절하고 절실한 형태를 지버낼 수 잇는 世界苦를 2월 혁명과 7월 혁명에서 환희와 신념의 다음 시대에서 스스로 위안시키는 것이엇고 낭만적 정서는 쉘링과 슐레겔을 중심하야 洄卷을 틀고잇든 역사적 사회적 貴童女 「狂風怒濤」를＜86＞ 폭발하고도 남을 열정의 胸裡에 烙印하야 病苦(그에게는 심한 두통의 지병이 잇엇고 晩年에는 眼疾과 半身不隨로 終身하고 말엇다)와 사라진 「아말리」 姊妹에 대한 애상을 火焰같이 이리 집어보는 것이엇다.
