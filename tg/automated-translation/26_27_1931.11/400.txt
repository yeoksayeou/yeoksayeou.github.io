**Body Text Translation:**

Life of German University Students, Swordsmen and Women, Nude Life, Cutting-Edge Love
						By To Yuho

Among German university students, one sometimes sees acquaintances with scars on their cheeks. These acquaintances are invariably swordsmen. For some reason, it is said that the more traces [scars] one has on both cheeks, the higher one's reputation grows. Among these acquaintances, there are reportedly sometimes those who mimic others using a sword. Scars are also sometimes found not just on the cheeks but also on the foreheads of some individuals. In Germany, there are two types of student caps; one has a visor, and the other does not. The caps worn by these swordsmen are generally the round ones without a visor. This is said to be exclusive to the swordsmen. These facial sword-scars were apparently quite common before but are gradually decreasing now. It is said that when these swordsmen reach 60 or 70, their pleasure in later years is always telling stories of their youthful martial exploits to their children. These swordsmen turn out for university ceremonies. Certain "groups" of swordsmen wear special uniforms, carry flags, travel by car, and occupy special reserved seats upon entering the ceremony hall. The uniform resembles the military attire of the warriors seen in the motion picture "The Three Musketeers." While people talk about the "decline of capitalism" today, these acquaintances alone still play before the halls of feudal lords. Also, there are a great many "groups" among German university students. It is common for a specific "group" to wear caps or shoulder sashes of a certain color. Among these groups, the aforementioned swordsmen's group, taking the example of Frankfurt University, gathers in one place every day between 11 AM and 12 PM. This is common to German universities, and the time between 11 AM and 12 PM is said to be standard. These swordsmen constitute, so to speak, a special "caste" (social stratum) among university students. It is said that swordsmen will now also begin to emerge among heroines [women]. I saw in a motion picture the other day that a certain girls' school teaches swordsmanship to young female students. However, if scars appear on the cheeks of young lady students, a major change will occur in the world of male swordsmen. It is, of course, difficult to say what the result would be if swordsmanship becomes popular in the Land of Women (女人國), but it is a fact that some heroines in the Land of Women have begun to take up the sword handle. Since the topic of female swordsmen in the Land of Women came up, let me add one more odd story. The other day, I heard from Mr. Cook (쿡 君), an American student in the Philosophy Department, that he had received a letter a few days prior from a female British student in Berlin (伯林) about her interest in co-ed nude gymnastics. <77> This British female student is a friend of his and was reportedly practicing it currently. Just hearing about it sounds barbaric. Men and women gathering together completely nude and frolicking about! This must mean the end times have arrived. ...Moreover, since "boys and girls should not sit together after the age of seven" (南女七歲不同席) was the etiquette of the East (東洋), is it right for such a barbaric custom (蠻風) to enter the land of propriety, the East? Even if we have to sell our house to buy straw mats (멍석), we must block this wicked trend. And furthermore, those fellows who return after catching this damned trend should be gathered together, driven into a steam room (한정간), and made to sweat profusely. ...Thinking about this makes me anxious about how this sweat will be shed in the future. ...Just as co-ed nude gymnastics is becoming popular, Germans also dislike covering their bodies. When going to a swimming pool on a summer day, it is extremely rare to see men or women covering their upper bodies. Last summer, I once went with my upper body covered and was met with a snort of derision from the acquaintance I went with. Semi-nudity (半裸體) at swimming pools is similar to Japan (日本).

K Coffee Shop is a den for university acquaintances. However, the acquaintances who come here are generally the same ones. I sometimes join them there. An American student "gang" (깽) also frequents the place. The characteristic of the American acquaintances is that they always sit amongst themselves. A cup of coffee is 25 Pfennig! Nowadays, 2 Pfennig more for tax! Total 27 Pfennig. Since everyone's pockets are not ample, they make do with one cup. But once seated, one gets terribly bored. There's nowhere else to go, and it feels a bit too early to go home and open a book... During this time, various stories emerge.

Mr. A (A君) from Persia (波斯) sometimes brings up complaints about Worker-Peasant Russia (勞農露西亞). His uncle is still in Moscow (모스코), and his father was apparently a wealthy merchant before the revolution. Mr. Peter? (뻬드 군) and a Bachelor of Divinity (神學士) (American) discuss the philosophy (哲理) of the universe (宇宙). Their conclusion always ends with "like this time" [implying uncertainty or resignation]. The German fellow Mr. Arndt? (아른트 군), who is proficient in multiple languages (多國語), recently passed the state examination (國家試驗) and is now planning ways to go to America to take up a teaching position. One of his companions failed two subjects during the exam and is waiting for the next chance. However, since only two subjects remain, he can relax and play around. What troubles his mind now is choosing a "Frau" (푸라우). The literary youth (文學靑年) always hatches dragon eggs [has grand, unrealistic ideas]. Occasionally, "that girl from his London stay" comes up. The poet-student (詩人 學徒) Mr. Schindelmann? (쒼더만 군) still goes around wearing a messy student cap. The scene at K Coffee Shop sometimes shifts to the university cafeteria. A young British student dreams of becoming a great future writer, and his friend once got into trouble for introducing nonsensical elements into his view on love (연애관). Among the literary youth, just like back home, there are those who cry out for "that pure and noble love." On one side, there are acquaintances floating meaninglessly high in the air, saying they care nothing whether the world turns upside down or mountains crumble, seeking eternal beauty and eternal love; on the other side, there are also acquaintances descending to the lowest level, shouldering pickaxes and rolling up their sleeves. Those floating high in the air, attached to the ordinary, remaining most aloof, perhaps because the ground they roam is too high, are like perpetual white snow year-round, their complexions always pale. When I was back home, I saw among so-called literary figures those who seek this eternal beauty, this eternal love. They say: so-called proletarian (푸로레타리아) writers discuss how class consciousness (階級意識) relates to love, but isn't love always just pure love? They say: revolutionary literature (革命文學), focusing on this small part of human emotion, is not the true path (本道) of literature. However, in seeking such universal things, one ultimately fails to seize the opportunity to engage with life itself. Life always exists in specific manifestations (特殊相). The fullness of life lies in faithfulness to these specific manifestations. To them, adhering rigidly (拘泥) to specific manifestations, which are the most momentary phenomena (刹那的 現象), appears most foolish. Therefore, in their eyes, even unemployed beggars (實業쟁이 거지) must understand the purity of love. The young ladies of Kaiser Street (카이저- 거리) must emulate Ch'unhyang (춘향). They cite the example that Shakespeare's (쉑쓰피어) plays, which used kings, dukes, and marquises (王公候) as story material centered around them hundreds of years ago, are highly acclaimed even in Worker-Peasant Russia (勞農露西亞). <78> Literature, they say, must transcend like Shakespeare's, encompassing all of human nature. However, while seeking such eternal things, one gets struck by nonsensical misfortune amidst the dithering. During my time in Yanjing (燕京), I heard an interesting joke. As part of an annual event in early 1930, the great historian (史學大家) Professor Teggart? (떼바거쓰 교수), who is now teaching at Harvard [likely Berkeley], gave a lecture series on "1929" in the style of a monograph. It happened to be held in the Geology Department (地質學部), so a professor from the Geology Department gave the opening remarks (開會辭). At that time, he jokingly said that the historian's bluster sounds like nonsense (개소리) to a geologist like himself. That is true. When comparing the long, vast changes over time in the natural world (自然界) to this short history of humankind (人類 歷史), this human history, being the most specific manifestation (特殊相) and change within the shortest period, appears too foolish to consider. In geology, the Psychozoic Era (理性動物期), the period when humans built their highest civilization (最高文明), is calculated to span 300,000 years already. The history of just a few thousand years is an extremely small part of that! And furthermore, it is limited to humanity, a part of living beings, which are themselves a part of the natural world! Thinking this way, it is natural that what historians say sounds like nonsense. This 300,000 years compared to Earth's beginning! This compared again to the eternity of the universe! Thoughts become increasingly distant. However, life becomes too nihilistic this way. Focusing on these 300,000 years, the shape of human life disappears before one knows it. Similarly, when focusing only on love, which is common to all humanity, East and West, past and present, one becomes most distant from the reality of life. Ultimately, life adheres to the specific manifestation (特殊相) until the end. The fullness of life lies in faithfulness to this specific manifestation.

The young socialist (쏘시알리쓰트) Wang Chop'yŏng (王兆平君) came to visit with his lover. He asked me why I seemed to have a causelessly melancholy (멜랑콭리) air about me. Both of them attacked me with the same words.

His lover, H, preached her philosophy of life to me. She said those who only read books day and night are idiots (바가). "How many days does life last? You must learn how to enjoy life," she lectured vivaciously. Of course, for me, books are what gather dust in my room. But I didn't even try to make excuses to them that I don't read my books. When they came in, I was writing this impressionistic piece. Soon, Mr. Wang made a suggestion to H. He said since Mr. To looks so gloomy lately, she should give him a kiss (키쓰). I thought it was just a joke, but with her lover standing beside her, H gave me a sweet kiss. This was, without lying, my first kiss since arriving in Europe. I was so taken aback by their revolutionary behavior that I didn't know what to do for a moment. When I told H that I received my first kiss since staying in Germany from her, her eyes widened, and she called me an "idler/parasite" (식충). "What are you living for in this world?" I had no particular answer then. Wang stood beside, smiling broadly. On one hand, I thought of Mr. Ilgil's (日吉先生) letter. –
I thought again of Mr. Ilgil's letter quoting, "Joy, happiness, satisfaction are passive." ... Mr. Wang rebels against everything traditional. He challenges conventional sexual morality (性道德) to the end. He confronts me, asking what love is, what chastity is. I protested to him. However, compared to his attitude, I unconsciously felt that I was merely someone lagging behind him. He says he loves H. H also says she loves him. Yet he asks H to give me a kiss for my sake. When H gave me the kiss, of course, I did not refuse. Mr. Wang is a young socialist. He is a nihilist (니힐리쓰트). He utterly rejects traditional relationships between men and women. However, regarding himself, he is a heroist. He marches into the Land of Women (女人國) with this heroism. The citizens of the Land of Women are hero-worshippers. Wang is a hero through nihilism (니힐리즘). Wang is a hero through gold. When these two glitter together, the citizens of the Land of Women bow their heads and worship. The psychology of hero-worship is, frankly speaking, <79> primitive. I think of another text. *While one is surprised, it is enjoyable; women are fortunate.* ...He thinks this attitude towards the Land of Women is what a revolutionary should properly take. I protested. However, I could not protest against his assertive attitude, his uncompromising rebellion. I could not help but feel that he was many steps ahead of me. Frankly speaking, of course, the reason I could not march into the Land of Women was not because I was governed by my moral views. I did not possess the weapons for conquest. For a bum (건달) like me, of course,

the only chance to see women's undergarments (단속곳) is on a mannequin displayed in a shop window. However, in my view, I lack the courage to have my lover kiss a friend. I looked up at H and then at Mr. Wang. The hyacinth (히야신쓰) placed on the table continued to emit its fragrance. H said to me, "You love this flower"... I answered "Yes, yes" (야- 야-) several times. And I again stared blankly at the nude paintings (裸體畵) on the wall, "Farewell" (離別 - Adschied) and "Water Play" (물작란 - Wellensspiel). I am not a pessimist like Schopenhauer (쇼펜하우르). But I thought again – "Joy (喜び), happiness (幸福), satisfaction (滿足), are passive (消極的である)." [Note: this quote is in Japanese in the original text]

While I was writing this, the year 1930 passed before I knew it. On New Year's Eve night (섯달금음밤), at midnight (零時), people in every house opened their windows, and from the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th floors facing the street, they lit fireworks (烟火) and threw them down. The fireworks exploded fiercely. For a while, explosion sounds were heard continuously from all directions without cease. (Greeting the New Year with fireworks is a custom (風) from China (中國).) Various flashes of light (閃光) flickered continuously from house to house. While greeting the New Year (新年) like this, the sound of bells (종소래) from a distant temple or cathedral (사원) rang out powerfully. I greeted the New Year with this bell sound.

Since the story has become a bit rambling, I intend to end this piece with a laugh by posing a riddle (수수꺽기) at the end. –
It is said that nowadays in America, brevity is preferred in all matters because times are what they are. Once, I saw an exchange of letters between a father and son in an American magazine. It was a letter from an acquaintance short on tuition money to his father, containing only the following phrase:
No mon, no fun, your son.
(돈 없소. 滋味없소. 아들 말이요.)
The father who received this reportedly replied as follows:
Too bad, very sad, your Dad.
(안됏소. 未安하오. 아비말이오.)
However, it is said there was recently another similar letter. This was a letter from some young master requesting money again from his father after spending all the money sent, containing only the following:
SEND
+ MORE
MONEY (Meaning = Send more money)
This, however, signifies a specific amount. Roman letters (羅馬文字) each represent a specific number. Readers who are interested, let's try to find that amount.
(January 14, 1931, from Frankfurt (德國佛郞府), Germany) (End)
P.S. = Having written it, I see many omissions and shortcomings. However, due to time constraints for now, I don't have the opportunity to revise it, so I am sending it as is. I intend to elaborate further later and end it here for now. (Author) <80>

**Summary**

This essay by To Yuho, written from Frankfurt in 1931, describes his observations and reflections on German university student life. He discusses the fencing fraternities with their facial scars and formal rituals, the shocking (to him) trend of co-ed nudism ('Nacktkultur'), the atmosphere of student cafés filled with diverse discussions ranging from politics (criticism of Soviet Russia) and philosophy to job prospects and love, and contrasts different approaches to life and relationships – the traditional, the idealistic literary types seeking eternal love, and the radically modern, nihilistic socialists challenging all conventions, exemplified by his friends Wang and H. The author juxtaposes these Western experiences with Eastern (specifically Korean/Confucian) values, expressing both fascination and conservative disapproval, particularly regarding nudity and changing sexual morality. He reflects on the vastness of geological time versus human history and the importance of engaging with the 'specific manifestations' of life rather than abstract universals. The article concludes with a lighthearted cryptarithmetic puzzle.

For historians of colonial Korea, this article offers valuable insights into how a Korean intellectual abroad perceived and grappled with Western modernity, liberalism, and social customs in the early 1930s. It reveals the cultural clashes and internal conflicts experienced when encountering vastly different norms around the body, relationships, and ideology. The author's conservative reactions, his comparison of German student life with Korean norms ('literary youth' types, Ch'unhyang as a moral exemplar), and his interactions with other international students (American, Persian, British, Chinese socialist) provide a window into the complex intellectual and cultural landscape navigated by Koreans during this period, reflecting anxieties about tradition, modernity, and identity formation under colonial rule.

獨逸大學生의 生活, 劍客男女 裸軆生活 尖端戀愛
