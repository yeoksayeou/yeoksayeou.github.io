Cartoon: Scenes of the Latest Fashionable Clothing
						Art by Yi Ch'an-yŏng

(1) Two things saved because skirts went up rapidly and simultaneously came down. 1) The *chŏgori* (upper garment), and 2) The curve appeal (曲線美) of legs becoming ignorantly revealed (面無識되는)!

(2) I am a middle school student (中學生). My hat has horns, and my trousers have lumps. My studies are at the 3rd-year level.

(3) The modern (모던) master's suit trousers just flap loosely up and down.

(4) The self-proclaimed proletariat (푸로레타리아) young master; his hair is like a chestnut burr, and his clothes are workwear (勞働服). But his "labor" is dancing (딴스), Mahjong (麻雀), and cafes (카페). Don't buy it. <95>

**Summary**

This 1932 cartoon by Yi Ch'an-yŏng satirizes contemporary clothing trends and social types in Korea. It comments on the practicality (or lack thereof) of rising hemlines and shorter traditional tops, mocks the appearance of middle school uniforms and overly loose Western-style trousers, and critiques the affectation of a "proletariat" youth whose lifestyle involves leisure activities like dancing, Mahjong, and frequenting cafes rather than actual labor. From a historical perspective, the piece vividly illustrates the adoption and adaptation of Western fashions, changing social norms (especially regarding women's attire), and the emergence of new urban youth cultures and social critiques within colonial Korean society. Historians might find the specific critiques of different social groups (students, 'modern' men, pseudo-proletarians) and the reference to popular leisure activities like dancing, Mahjong, and cafes revealing of urban life and social commentary during this period.
