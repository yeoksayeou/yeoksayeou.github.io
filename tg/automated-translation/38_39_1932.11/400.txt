**Body Text Translation:**
Mocking the Optimist
						Yu Un'gyŏng

Oh, cotton candy!
Oh, puffed corn!＜91＞
Thus, like a scarecrow with arms spread wide on the paddy ridge,
its front flap fluttering in the April breeze.

Your form, merely puffed up without substance,
provokes a bitter, pained smile from the onlooker.
Bang! With just one blank shot,
I want to tear apart your senselessly arrogant face.

Without the desperate shock of needing to grab
a child slipping and falling off a cliff,
you drone on about fatalism, like sleep-talking.

Oh, cotton candy!
Oh, puffed corn!
Thus, like a scarecrow with arms spread wide on the paddy ridge,
its front flap fluttering in the April breeze.＜92＞

**Summary**

This poem by Yu Un'gyŏng vehemently criticizes optimists, portraying them as hollow and superficial figures like "cotton candy" or a "scarecrow." The speaker expresses disdain for their puffed-up arrogance and detachment from harsh realities, comparing their reliance on fatalism to sleep-talking while ignoring immediate dangers like a child falling off a cliff. The poem conveys a sense of frustration and anger towards this kind of unfounded optimism. From a historical perspective, the poem likely reflects the socio-political atmosphere of colonial Korea in the 1930s. Historians might find it interesting as a commentary on attitudes within Korean society – potentially criticizing those who maintained naive hope, collaborated, or preached fatalistic acceptance under Japanese rule, ignoring the suffering and urgency of the national situation.
