Theory of Poetry Translation
						Kim Ŏk
3
Since one is already a translator, unless the translator thoroughly digests the work, makes it their own, and then expresses it anew, there is no significance to be given. Even flowers change their colors when the region changes. If they do not, they cannot become something unique to that region. The translation of poetry is no different from this.
"Singing with resentment, the voice chokes; overwhelmed by sorrow, the dancing sleeves are slow.
The choking voice, the slow dancing sleeves are due to longing for the beloved.
At Sŏrŭng (西陵), the sun is about to set; it feels as though my heart is breaking."
A sijo like this, though it follows the sijo form, is in reality neither sijo nor anything else; it merely possesses the taste of the original Chinese poem (漢詩) itself. Therefore, this kind of "sijo-ification" of Chinese poetry not only lacks any novelty but also fails to deliver the subtle charm that a sijo should possess or the emotional impact of poetry. The reason there are few purely Korean sijo reflecting genuine Korean sentiment (朝鮮 情趣) among Korean sijo, and many that carry the scent of the Chinese mainland (漢土), is that poets failed to sufficiently digest Chinese poetry and make it their own, instead swallowing it whole and regurgitating it as is. This likely has its roots in the ideology of worshipping the Chinese mainland. That even individuals who could not help but roam in the creative world of poets had this tendency is regrettable in some respects.

When Ko Kyŏngmyŏng (高敬命) took Tu Fu's (杜甫) poem "Night Mooring by the Qinhuai" (夜泊秦准),
"Smoke envelops the cold water, moonlight envelops the sand; Mooring at night by the Qinhuai, near the taverns; The singing girls know not the sorrow of a fallen kingdom; Across the river, they still sing 'Flowers in the Rear Courtyard'."
and rendered it into the sijo form as
"Mooring the boat at Chinch'un (秦准), seeking a tavern,
The singing girls across the river, unaware of the sorrow of a fallen kingdom,
As smoke envelops the water and moonlight the sand, sing only 'Flowers in the Rear Courtyard'."
can this truly be called a translation? The translator's effort merely amounts to rearranging the sentences of the original text for their own convenience. Unless there is some meaningful creative effort in such works, this transplantation into the sijo form, given the existence of the original poem, has absolutely no value or reason to exist. Instead, it is far better to read the original poem character by character (though, of course, if one can appreciate the original poem as it is, nothing is better). How can the value of poetry be discovered in a translated poem where the translator's own essential individuality does not appear? To call such a thing poetry is unbelievable unless one is infatuated with the Chinese mainland. It is merely a momentary disturbance, a pastime for the sake of convenience. Only when there is creative effort filtered through the translator's own life, whether good or bad, does it acquire the significance worthy of evaluation.

Furthermore, the Chinese poem <63>
"In Weicheng (渭城), the morning rain dampens the light dust; At the inn, the willows are green and fresh; I urge you, sir, to drink one more cup of wine; West of Yangguan Pass (陽關), there are no old friends."
was made into the sijo
"In Wich'ŏng (渭城) morning rain, the willow color is fresh.
I urge you, have another cup of wine.
Westward, beyond Yanggwan (陽關), you will find no old friends."
Compared to the translation of "Night Mooring by the Qinhuai," this shows considerable effort by the translator, yet one cannot help but wish the translator had employed bolder and freer expression. Saying this should not be considered overly demanding of me, because in the realm of challenging poetry translation, the translator's own bold individuality often brings the poetry to life, sometimes with a meaning different from the original text. The more clearly the translator's individuality is revealed, the greater the effect, regardless of success or failure. In the end, in a word, the translation of poetry depends on the translator's individual effort.

4
"A poor scholar (布衣寒士) who only wrote for over forty years,
You had no wife or children while alive; now dead, there is no one to perform ancestral rites.
When the bier departed, your lone mother wailed,
On the Kwangnyuk (廣陸) land in March, only apricot blossoms fluttered down."
If the original poem for this translation by someone is Sin Kwangsu's (申光洙)
四十文章一布衣 生無妻子死無依 旅櫬歸來慈母哭 廣陸三月杏花飛
(Forty years of writing, a commoner dressed in cloth; Alive without wife or child, dead without support; The temporary coffin returns, the loving mother weeps; In Kwangnyuk, third month, apricot blossoms fly.)
everyone would surely be surprised. This is because although the translated text conveys the meaning of the original, it lacks value as poetry. In other words, it cannot be called a translated poem but is merely a prose piece interpreting the original poem. No matter how one punctuates lines or arranges verses specially, prose remains prose; it can never become poetry because of such things. Conversely, even if poetry is put into prose form, it does not become prose but will reveal its original nature, needless to say. I stated that the above translation is merely a prose piece interpreting the original poem. That is correct. Interpretation and explanation cannot provide the deep emotion and boundless suggestion of poetry, thus they are the most dreadful things that must be avoided at all costs in poetry. Who would drink flat "beer" and clap out of rhythm?

Since it is an interpretation, it might seem to faithfully convey the meaning of the original poem, but such interpretive faithfulness to the original in a translated poem only promotes its worthlessness as a translated poem.
This translator failed to see the original poem through their own individuality. That is why the translated poem ended up being merely an interpretation. It is easy to think that translation can be done easily by anyone with sufficient linguistic ability. But in reality, it is not so; sufficient linguistic ability means a good understanding of the meaning, which often leads one to lean towards interpretation. It is very good when a translator with sufficient linguistic ability and abundant poetic sensibility translates poetry, but the translation of poetry does not depend solely on linguistic ability. Unless the translator sees through their own individuality, the poetic life of the translated poem cannot be captured. Therefore, it must be said that this translator forgot the most crucial life force that should not be neglected through creative emotion. <64> In other words, the issue is what kind of poetic emotion this translator received upon reading the original poem and 담아 놓앗는가 (captured) in their translated poem, which could almost be called their own creation. As in original creation, in translated poetry too, the translator cannot produce poetry worthy of the name unless they grasp the core of the poetry and the sharpest point of creative power. One cannot be a poetry translator without being a poet and simultaneously an appreciator with deep insight. Herein lies the translator's struggle, which is greater than that of the original author.
The emotion this translator received from the original poem is mundane, making it impossible to find even a narrowly defined three-dimensional focal point. In a word, the translator failed to derive poetic feeling from the original poem, thus could not place any emphasis in the translated poem, merely adding an interpretation to the original.

Returning to the original poem, if it can evoke emotion in the reader, it can be divided into at least three kinds. One is to feel greater sympathy for the poor scholar (寒士) who, older than his aged mother, spent over 40 years playing with brush and ink, truly never even married, knew nothing of worldly pleasures, lived in poverty, and then died. Another is to feel more pity for the sorrowful mother who, having lost the son she relied upon, must now spend her frail remaining years alone in sighs and sorrow, than for the scholar who lived poorly without wife or child and died, his spirit having nowhere to rest. And yet another is to regard both son and mother with equally sad eyes of sympathy. It might seem unnecessary to distinguish these, but for the poetry translator, such points cannot be overlooked because it is a matter requiring much care in expression depending on which side the translator's own sympathy leans towards. Since this attitude is adopted without any particular consciousness in the translator's expression, one might say there's no need for conscious preparation, but as an attitude towards expression, the translator must naturally adopt one.
"Forty years of writing plagued by poverty,
This childless scholar's body has departed.
As the mother clings to the bier, shuddering,
Apricot blossoms fall in the March spring wind."
If translated like this, it seems the translator's sympathy lies more with the remaining mother than the deceased poor scholar. And the contrast with indifferent nature is clearly revealed. As this translated poem shows no interpretation or explanation, it is arguably better as something resembling poetry than the aforementioned translation by someone else.

There is one thing that must be mentioned here. It concerns how to handle proper nouns and the like in translated poetry. The original poem contains the place name Kwangnŭng (廣陵). I believe the translator may dispose of such things as they see fit. Before thoughtlessly including "Kwangnŭng" in the translated poem just because it is in the original, one must consider how much realism it will impart to the reader. If it adds realism, include it by all means; if not, it is better to omit it. So, what about this translated poem? In my opinion, this "Kwangnŭng" seems unlikely to add any realism to the translated poem. Rather, to impart realism, something like "the house next door," "the back hill," or perhaps "the roadside" would be far better. It is embarrassing, but I, the translator, do not even know if Kwangnŭng is simply a place name or the name of a cemetery. And since it says apricot blossoms fall, it goes without saying that the season is spring. <65> Thus, if necessary, even "March" could be omitted, but to aid the rhythm (語勢), phrases like "spring March," "it being March," or "even in March" might be fine. In any case, I think one should never resort to awkward phrasing (語塞) deliberately. If awkward phrasing in newspapers is unbearable, how much more so in verse?

Since poetry must be a complete expression using the most concise language in the briefest possible form to deliver deep emotion, suggestion, and stimulus (剌戟), poets or poetry translators cannot help but think deeply about this point. Therefore, I believe that unnecessary words, no matter how beautiful or difficult to discard, must be boldly thrown away without the slightest hesitation. Because of this, one often loses the cat while trying to catch the mouse.
"Poor man's studies, of no use.
Lived without wife or child, died a soul without owner.
As the old mother weeps, clinging to the bier,
Apricot blossoms flutter wildly in the wind."
If translated like this, where does the translator's sympathy lie more? It can be seen as being more with the deceased commoner (布衣) than with the lone, old mother left behind. Just as various similar dishes can be made from the same ingredients, from the same original poem, translations like this can place the emphasis in different places while conveying the same general meaning.
"This scholar who spent 40 poor years with writing,
Childless, his body gone, a lonely soul with nowhere to rely.
Pitying the old mother, she weeps clinging to the bier,
It being March, apricot blossoms tremble, petal by petal."
In such a translated poem, the translator's sympathy can be seen as resting equally on the deceased scholar and the old mother. Needless to say, the meaning of these translated poems, regardless of which one, is the same overall. However, when viewed again in terms of expression and nuance (語感), one can discover different poetic flavors (詩味) within them. This shows that the taste of poetry lies not only in its meaning but also in the melody of language use (言語驅使).
"Poverty-stricken, forty years spent with writing.
Dying childless, where can the soul go?
As the mother weeps, clinging (붇안고) to the head of the bier,
It being March, apricot blossoms whirl in the sky."
In the expression of such a translated poem, one might detect sympathy tinged with the translator's mockery (嘲笑). However, even in that mockery-tinged sympathy, one cannot forget the overflowing sorrowful sentiment.

Talking like this might seem overly meticulous. But the translator cannot disregard such matters carelessly, just as a tiny crack can become the cause that brings down a large house.
"Writing for 40 years, poverty never departs,
Childless, his state, a soul without owner.
As the old mother weeps, sending off the bier,
Apricot blossoms fall in the March spring wind."
It is also possible to translate like this. However, regardless of the translated poem, in a word, where the translator's sympathy lies is the main point for both the reader and the translator <66> (remainder of original text missing - National Institute of Korean History)

**Summary**

Kim Ŏk argues that poetry translation demands more than literal accuracy; it requires the translator to fully "digest" the original, infuse it with their own individuality (個性), and recreate it as a new poetic work in the target language. He criticizes translations, particularly of Chinese poems into Korean sijo, that merely rearrange words or offer prose interpretations, lacking genuine poetic life and the translator's creative effort. Using examples from translations of Tu Fu and Sin Kwangsu, he illustrates how different translators, by emphasizing different emotional aspects (sympathy for the scholar vs. the mother, etc.) and making choices about details like proper nouns, can create varied poetic effects from the same source, underscoring that translation is a creative act akin to original composition. From a colonial history perspective, Kim Ŏk's essay reveals the ongoing negotiation of Korean cultural identity under the heavy influence of Chinese literary tradition, even as Western influences were also arriving. His call for translators to create uniquely Korean poetic expressions from foreign sources, rather than simply mimicking or interpreting, reflects a desire to assert cultural autonomy and artistic originality within the constraints of the era. Historians might find the specific critiques of translating Chinese classics into the native sijo form particularly interesting, as it highlights the complex relationship between received tradition and the drive for modern, national literary expression.

譯詩論
						金億
3
이미 번역인 以上에는 어데까지든지 譯者는 그것을 소화하야 자기 것을 만들어 놓은 뒤에 다시 토하지 아니하는 한에서는 아무러한 의의 줄 것이 없습니다. 꽃이라도 지방이 달라지면 그 색채까지 변하는 것이외다. 그러치 아니하고는 그 지방의 고유한 것이 될 수 없습니다. 시가의 번역도 이에서 지내지 아니할 것이외다.
『恨唱하니 歌聲咽이요 愁飜하니 舞袖遲라
歌聲咽 舞袖遲는 임 그린 탓이로다.
西陵에 日欲暮하니 애끊는 듯 하여라』
이와 같은 시조는 형식은 時調刑을 밟앗을 망정 그 實은 시조도 아무 것도 아니고 漢詩 그대로의 그 맛에 지내지 아니하는 것이외다. 그러기에 이러한 漢詩의 時調化에는 조금도 신기한 것이 없을 뿐 아니라 시조로의 가저야 할 묘미와 또는 시가로의 감동을 줄 수가 없는 것이외다. 朝鮮 時調 속에는 순전한 朝鮮 情趣의 시조가 적고 어데까지든지 漢土 냄새가 나는 것이 많은 것은 다 漢詩를 충분히 소화하야 자기 것을 만들어 쓰지 못하고 그대로 삼켯다가 그대로 토해 놓앗기 때문이외다. 이것은 漢土崇拜思想에 그 원인이 잇을 것이외다. 시인으로의 창작세계에 逍遙하지 아니할 수 없는 人士들에게까지 이러한 경향이 잇엇든 것은 어떤 점으로 섭섭한 일이라 할만 하외다.

杜甫의 「夜泊秦准」의
「煙籠寒水月籠沙, 夜泊秦准近酒家, 商女不知亡國恨, 隔江猶唱後庭花』 같은 시를 高敬命이가 時調體로
『秦准에 배를 매고 酒家를 차자가니
隔江商女는 亡國恨을 모르고서
煙籠水 月籠沙 할 제 後庭花만 부르더라』
하고 시침이를 떼어 놓앗으니 이것을 번역이라고 할 수가 잇겟습니까. 譯者의 노력으로는 원문의 문장을 자기 사정 좋을대로 뒤바꾸어 놓은데 지내지 아니합니다. 이러한 것에도 오이려 의미할 수 잇는 창작적 노력이 잇다 하면 모르거니와 그러치 아니하는 限에서는 이 時調體 移植이야 말로 原詩가 잇는 이상 아무러한 존재할 만한 가치와 이유가 없는 것이외다. 도리어 이와 같은 것은 原詩 그대로 한 자 한 자를 (물론 原詩 그대로 감상할 수만 잇으면 그 이상 좋은 일은 없는 것이외다 마는)뜯어서 읽는 것이 훨신 나은 일이외다. 譯者로서 가지지 아니할 수 없는 譯者 그 자신의 개성이 나타나지 아니한 時譯에서 어떠케 詩歌로의 존재가치가 발견되겟습니까. 이와 같은 것을 詩歌라 함은 漢土心醉者가 아니고는 믿을 수 없는 말이외다. 한 때의 作亂이요 姑息의 유희에 지내지 아니할 뿐이외다. 좋건 나쁘건 譯者 그 자신의 생명을 거친 창작적 노력이 잇으리라야 비로소 평가받을 만한 존재의의를 가지게 됩니다.

또 그러고 漢詩의 ＜63＞
『渭城朝雨浥輕塵, 客舍靑靑柳色新, 勸君更進一杯酒, 西出陽關無故人』
을 時調化하야
『渭城 아침 비에 柳色이 새로워라
그대를 권하노니 一杯酒 나오소라
西으로 陽關에 나가면 故人 없어하노라』
하엿으니 이와 같은 것은 『夜泊秦准』의 譯에 비하여는 대단히 譯者의 노력이 잇다할 만도 하나 역시 이 譯者로 좀 더 대담하고 자유롭은 표현을 가젓드라면 하는 생각을 금할 수 없습니다. 이런 말을 한다고 필자를 過慾이라고 할 수 없는 것이 불가능한 詩譯에는 譯者 그 자신의 대담하다 할 만한 개성의 표현이 가끔 원문과는 다른 의미로 詩歌를 살려 놓기 때문이외다. 譯者의 개성이 단적으로 들어나면 들어날스록 성패는 여하간 그만큼한 효과는 얻을 수가 잇는 것이외다. 필경 한 마듸로 하면 詩歌의 번역이란 譯者의 개성적 노력여하에 잇는 것이외다.

4
『마흔이 넘도록 글만 쓰든 布衣寒士여
그대 살아선 처자가 없드니 죽어선 제사 지낼 사람이 없구려.
상여가 떠날 때 홀로 떠러진 그대의 어머니 통곡하는데
廣陸 땅 3월에 살구 꽃만 풀풀 날라는구려』
