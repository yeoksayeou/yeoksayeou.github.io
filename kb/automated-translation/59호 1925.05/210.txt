잡지명	개벽 제59호
발행일	1925년 05월 01일
기사제목	十字橋 우에서 (Above Sipchagyo Bridge)
필자	金基鎭
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

十字橋 우에서
						金基鎭
You are here. After encircling the clearly visible palace walls below the Puagak of bygone days and laying out the Six Ministries Street (六曹大路), when the crossroads were named East and West, the stone bridge laid by the hands of the people of that time was removed, and in its place, you are now here. You are here.

I stand upon you. Gazing at the newly adorned Kwanghwamun Gate, gazing at the newly erected, grand granite building of the Chosŏn Government-General (朝鮮總督府), gazing at Puagak—Pukaksan Mountain (北岳山)—which has maintained its old form for decades upon decades, gazing at Mongmyŏk—Namsan Mountain (南山)—with its tall green pines, changing day by day, and also picturing before my eyes the form of the Haet'ae statues, whose whereabouts became unknown after the bustle of the Kongjinhoe (Industrial Fair)—I now stand upon you. I stand upon the place called Sipchagyo (十字橋 - Crossroad Bridge), paved with cement and solidified with stone blocks. Sipchagyo, do you know the origin of your name, Sipchagyo, or perhaps not?

Ah, Kwanghwamun! Pukak, still as before! Namsan, changing daily! The nature of Chosŏn, being reconstructed! Buildings! People! My throat is choked with aimless thoughts now. Choked.

Kyŏngbok Palace (景福宮)! Built from April of the 2nd year to July of the 5th year of Emperor Kojong (李太王), truly with the enormous sum of over 7.4 million *yang* made from the people's blood and sweat, and having endured numerous hardships for the 57 years since then—the ruins of the old capital! Ah, Kwanghwamun! Great gate that guarded the royal palace with a solemn posture, showing a marvelous harmony with the skyline of Pukak and Inwang (仁旺)! When you were built back then, how much of the people's blood, sweat, and oil flowed for you, unable to overcome some coercion? And today, to trample, tear down, and rebuild you, how much of the people's blood, sweat, and oil is flowing for a new XX(?)—do you know, or perhaps not? Do you remember that some years ago, a certain foreigner mourned and praised you, poetically lamenting your destruction and seeking to save you? Oh! Kwanghwamun, since then, how many times has your face been made up? How many times <28> have you been forced into prostitution? And now, once again, you have put on unwanted makeup and are forced to sell smiles for some Kongjinhoe being held within you. Your shape, changed as much as it can be! The ironic contrast between the unresponsive Pukaksan Mountain, the new Government-General building, and you! When I look at you, having become like this, I feel a sorrow that comes from all sorts of facts, facts too clear, solemn facts. Yes, it is not denial, but the sorrow of acknowledging facts as they are.

The nature of Chosŏn changes day by day. And isn't this already a fact, an inevitable trend that transcends right or wrong, likes or dislikes, something that cannot be helped? The sorrow arising from affirming the facts is by no means small. Oh, white-clad people! Korean people (朝鮮民族)! I know you well. I know your current state well...

Even if the historical facts before Kija Chosŏn (箕子朝鮮) are not known in detail, receiving the mythical legends anyway, from the so-called Tan'gun (檀君), through Kija's 40 generations, Wi's (衛氏) 3 generations, Emperor Wu's (武帝) Four Commanderies and the Three Han (三韓), crossing the Three Kingdoms Period (三國時代) to Silla (新羅), Koryŏ (高麗), and Chosŏn (朝鮮)—not only do I certainly not know the so-called half-myriad-year history well, but I also think there is no need to bring such things up anew now. Whether the people here are a single ethnicity or a mix of Mongol and Han peoples, I also think there is no need to investigate the roots (採根) now. However, people of this place, be ashamed! That you have been the perpetual losers throughout history! Let's not talk about the distant past; just looking at the historical facts from the founding of the state by Yi T'aejo (李太祖) in 1392 through 27 kings over 519 years until the Korea-Japan Annexation (韓日合倂) in 1910, you must realize that you yourselves were perpetual losers.

I have not the slightest thought now of wanting to record the 500-year history of the Yi Dynasty. Moreover, I have absolutely no desire to record the history of the filthy factional strife of the Four Colors (四色). I only know that the people of this place, then and now, are a race that forgets the bigger picture due to dirty factional strife. That much I know.

Indeed, look. From 1392 until August 22, 1910—to be more precise, until 3:30 PM on the 4th [likely mistake in original, should be 22nd or 29th]—what fills the numerous pages of the 500-year history of the Yi Dynasty? I find nothing there other than the Tongin (東人), Sŏin (西人), Namin (南人), Pugin (北人), Noron (老論), Soron (少論), and slightly modified factions and factional struggles derived from these. What was politics? It was merely a symbol, material for their power <29> struggles. What were the people? They were merely donkeys (驢馬) that made their storehouses more abundant. How could Chosŏn not perish under such circumstances? Therefore, Chosŏn perished. Chosŏn perished.

Yes. Chosŏn perished. This is a fact. But did Chosŏn perish entirely and completely?

However, one must not forget another fact: that Chosŏn is rising. One must know that Chosŏn's perishing meant the loss of its national polity (國軆).

Chosŏn has not perished. While my ears hear the chatter of many people saying, "Chosŏn is no more. The Chosŏn that once existed in this land is already gone!", I see before me that Chosŏn which many people say "is no more." Yes—Chosŏn is decidedly not "gone." Just as Blok (쁘록크) once saw Russia amidst the XX(?) and the intellectual class, I now see Chosŏn.

All things, all existence, are fated to experience suffering, submission, and dispersion. However, amidst this submission, Chosŏn must emerge as something new—and in a new sense, great. It must emerge as something new that could not have been expected 16 years ago. There must be the kind of expectation Blok spoke of: "That plan, which had melted into the soul of humanity, into the soul of the nation, breaks the fences that bound them, tears down the walls, demolishes a part of the useless hill, and charges forth like a mad wave."

Ah, but I cannot help but be pessimistic. Because the exact same motif that tarnished the 519-year history is now also tarnishing the current state of Chosŏn. The season is spring. This time when the Queen of May comes with a brilliant smile, this time when all living things are moved by new expectations (Ah—how desolate, Chosŏn!), my throat is choked with aimless thoughts.

Oh, Kyŏngbok Palace! Sipchagyo! Pukak! Kwanghwamun! Kongjinhoe grounds! White robes fluttering in the spring breeze! Filthy rags! Poor beggars! Poor beggars! Dirty Seoul...

Sipchagyo, you were built in the days called civilized. It's true. You were indeed constructed with civilized tools. Be grateful to today's civilization that you stand so splendidly. <30>

Oh, nature of Chosŏn! Isn't it good for you too to be grateful? That you have been remade in myriad forms, radiating the brilliance of civilization...

Truly, I see it. Civilization permeating everywhere. On the faces of workers with poor complexions, among those who commit suicide by taking rat poison, among those who abandon their parents' homes and run away, among the crowds heading to Manchuria (滿洲) with only their bare fists—everywhere, I can see the forms of civilization. Is there anything, this or that, which is not a blessing of today's capitalist civilization?

Why leave one's hometown and head to a foreign land with only bare fists? Therein lies the sorrow of uncivilized people conquered by modern capitalism.

Why do the numbers of workers with poor complexions increase every year? There too lies the suffering of a conquered people.

Why must one die by taking rat poison? Because there lies the agony of a loser who rejects life.

Why must one leave the home of one's parents? Because there lies the conquest of capitalist civilization that inevitably makes it so. Simply put, it is because the traditional family system (在來의 家族制度) is collapsing day by day.

Yes, the world today is completely a capitalist society.

And this is an inevitable trend. It is a fact that transcends issues of right or wrong, likes or dislikes. Just as the history of Chosŏn came to a close in 1910, the patriarchal family system (家長家族制度) of Chosŏn, along with the collapse of its national polity, is losing its vestiges day by day due to the capitalist system of production. The reason for leaving the parents' home lies here.

Then why couldn't the patriarchal family system of Chosŏn endure without collapsing? Such talk has no meaning. Why, you ask? It is because the family economic system is changing to an individualistic economic system. In other words, individual life naturally came to be practiced in order to manage a household adapted to an individualistic economic system.

The division of the family is caused by the individualization of income. When the family system existed in its complete form in Chosŏn, it was a time when the entire family was supported by the strength of the patriarch alone, and consequently, because the patriarch had the power to support (扶養力), he also had sufficient authority (支配力). However, the patriarch's authority, that is, <31> his power to support, gradually diminished. Among the Korean people, more than half, the vast majority of the propertyless class (無産階級者), whether men or women, parents or children, all had to go out to the place of production to earn wages. Whatever the type of labor, it became impossible to live unless each person earned at least some amount. Whereas previously, the patriarch alone brought in income for the whole family, today the income of a household has become individualized. Thus, as income, the foundation of life, becomes individualized, the patriarch's authority in the home could not remain as it was without gradually diminishing. Discord in the family arises because trust in and obedience to the patriarch diminish. The family cannot trust and obey the patriarch's support and authority. Why? Because the family's livelihood can no longer be sustained solely by the patriarch's income. When even the sum of the entire family's income is barely enough for the household's subsistence, how can the patriarch's authority possibly persist any longer?

Leaving the parents' home is, in essence, an absolute avoidance of the authority of the patriarch, the parents.

Besides the propertyless class, many among the middle-class intellectuals (中産智識階級者) are also leaving their parents' homes now. And even if this cannot be called the individualization of income, there is no doubt that it is essentially due to the permeation of the individualistic spirit following the transition from the era of family economy to the implementation of an individualistic economic system. Children who have received the baptism of the so-called new thought of the new era cannot possibly live in harmony with parents who cling stubbornly (固*) to the ignorance of familism. There, one can see the most profound reaction against the family system, triggered by the natural economic organization.

And today, when the father goes to the township office (*myŏnso*), the son to a factory or company, the daughter to a girls' school or perhaps a shop, scattering in all directions, how can the collapse of the family system be prevented? This is the general trend. An inevitable tendency. And this transcends the realm of our value judgments.

All the misery of reality lies where the traditional social system is replaced in turn by another system. And this tendency comes with a great, unstoppable force that inevitably arises as human society evolves economically and socially. Just as familism could not rule forever in the past, there is no guarantee that today's individualism will rule forever in the future. In other words, just as <32> the patriarchal family economic structure that supported the society here collapsed, today's capitalist economic organization will also collapse before long. All this is possible in the course of history.

Truly, who can guarantee that today's social system will be sustained into the eternal future? It must inevitably collapse. However, we cannot wait until that time naturally arrives. History shifts. But people cannot remain bystanders until that history shifts. Isn't the logical contradiction where those who speak of calm historical materialism (唯物史觀) also forcefully advocate for passionate class struggle (階級鬪爭) truly due to this? It is not only Marx's (맑쓰) contradiction but also that of his disciples.

Chosŏn is chaotic (混沌). And truly chaotic in a new sense. I am merely hearing a small amount of noise. Yes. It is nothing more than noise. It is nothing other than factional strife of the Four Colors (四色)—truly, Four Colors in a new sense. It is nothing systematic (씨스티마틱크한). Nothing refined (리파인된). It is merely noise.

Just as a person cannot live with only a nervous system, a person also cannot live with only blood components. But Chosŏn is a cripple possessing only one of these two. First and foremost, we must live. Yes. We must live.

Reality is miserable. Today is chaotic. But there must truly be great expectation and effort in a new sense. There must be a new departure into the life of a new era. Just as sufficient recognition of reality is indispensable, criticism of values is also indispensable. Recognition of reality involves explaining and pointing out existence, while judgment of value determines action; therefore, these two must coexist. This is because life is not merely the explanation and pointing out of existence, and where recognition of reality is insufficient, there is no determination of action. And life truly lies in action, in practice, and nowhere else. Thus, when one lacks both this recognition and action, life becomes lame. This is not said from the perspective of the misunderstood, shallow proponents of 'life-ism' (生活至上主義者). Truly, I say, where is the substance of human life apart from living?

I repeat: just as a person cannot live with only a nervous system, a person also cannot live with only blood components. <33> However, the civilization of today's intellectualism (主知主義) tends to make people conduct their lives relying solely on the nervous system. There is a tendency for people who content themselves solely with explaining and pointing out existence to increase. They know existence. But they forget significant action. They forget that action based on value judgment, departing from sufficient recognition of reality, is indeed the substance of human life.

I wonder: Did our predecessors truly have sufficient recognition of reality? And how much action are today's seniors and friends actually taking?

But I hesitate to say these words: "Everything is despair!"

I once spoke of moving beyond the period of disillusionment (幻滅期) in Chosŏn and truly acting upon what we must do. After that, I received the following poem from a certain friend.

(The Donkey)

At a house in the Chinese quarter,
A gloomy, dirty, small house,
Standing before the gate opening onto the street,
I, along with three or four passersby, am looking in.

Its eyes covered on both sides to only look forward,
A donkey, seemingly just skin and bones,
Is tied to what looks like a heavy mortar (臼).
A little later, the mortar
Is filled to the brim with wheat (小麥).

With its long tail tied tightly around its head,
A Chinese man in dirty clothes
Starts whipping it, as if keeping time.
The sound of hitting a bamboo stick (대쬬가리),
*Katchiri, katchiri katchiririn*

The donkey began to turn the millstone. Believing [?] this,
A person standing next to me said, "Ah—how
pitiful an animal! 365 days a year,
Always going round and round in the same place!"

Then another person said, <34>
"That's why it's an animal,
Because it has no feelings."

Hearing this, the last person spoke,
"There are many idiots like that among humans too."
Chattering like this, the three people
Laughed heartily as if greatly amused.

But the donkey walks around without a word.
The ground flour, like spring water,
Trickles out continuously.
The donkey, without a word,
Just turns with all its might.

Idiot?
Pitiful animal?
But it doesn't forget how to grind flour,
And that flour
Is important to people.

Oh yes, for people,
Flour is necessary—I too
Must grind flour, and to grind it,
Let me become even a donkey,
Let my eyes be covered too,
Let me become even an idiot.

The donkey diligently keeps turning.
The ground flour, like spring water,
Continuously wells up from below.

In my ears, still
Remains the sound of the bamboo stick being struck,
*Katchiri, katchiri, katchiririn*.

I did not record this here with the intention of discussing the poetic value of this poem. Moreover, since I translated it myself. I merely present something like this for reference.

Fine. Even if the meaning of this poem is hypocrisy, I favor this kind of intention. And this kind of <35> intention is, of course, a "determination of action" that has trodden the path of sufficient recognition of reality. When considered intellectually (主知的), it might be nothing more than a kind of foolishness, but nevertheless, I see there the action of will. I see the passion for life.

What is necessary for us now is how we should act, how we should live. Above all, the loss of will is to be lamented. But look. How many people now truly have not lost that will? Great expectations in a new sense cannot be obtained without the power of will. But now, there is a feeling that Chosŏn is being tarnished by the exact same motif that tarnished its 519-year history. What is all this talk of great expectations in a new sense? Oh, people of this place! Perpetual losers of history! Is this not a fact worthy of sorrow?

Sipchagyo! Standing upon you now, facing the spring breeze, I feel the emptiness of past affairs and the gloom of the coming destiny. Oh, adorned Kwanghwamun! Bride of the ruined capital! How many times have you put on makeup until now?

Ah, dirty Seoul! Ruined capital Hanyang (漢陽)! My throat is choked with aimless thoughts now.
April 18, 1925 <36>

**Summary**

Kim Kijin stands on Sipchagyo Bridge near Kwanghwamun, reflecting on the drastic changes in Seoul under Japanese rule. He laments the altered landscape—the newly built Government-General building contrasting with the old palace gate (Kwanghwamun) and mountains (Pukak, Namsan)—seeing it as a symbol of Korea's tragic history and current state. He harshly criticizes the Chosŏn Dynasty's legacy of factionalism (사색당쟁) as a root cause of its downfall and fears similar divisions plague contemporary Korean society. Kim analyzes the breakdown of the traditional patriarchal family system as an inevitable consequence of encroaching capitalism, which forces individuals into wage labor, disperses families, and fosters individualism. While acknowledging these changes as perhaps historically necessary, he despairs over the suffering they cause (poverty, suicide, emigration) and the perceived lack of unified will or effective action among Koreans, comparing their situation to a state of chaos and paralysis. The included poem "The Donkey" serves as a complex metaphor, perhaps suggesting the necessity of tireless, even unthinking, labor for survival and progress, urging a move beyond intellectualism towards determined action despite the bleak reality.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its raw expression of colonial-era Korean intellectual anguish. It captures the complex feelings towards modernization—recognizing its inevitability while decrying its destructive impact on tradition, culture (symbolized by Kwanghwamun), and social structures (the family). Kim's critique of pre-colonial history (Chosŏn factionalism) as a contributing factor to Korea's subjugation, and his fear of its repetition, reflects common internal debates among Korean intellectuals of the time. His analysis of capitalism's impact on the family structure provides insight into social transformations occurring under colonial rule. The reference to Blok and the mention of class struggle (阶级鬪争) alongside historical materialism (唯物史观) hint at the burgeoning influence of socialist ideas, mixed with nationalist sentiment and profound disillusionment. Historians might find the blend of cultural lament, sharp social critique, historical reflection, and the call for pragmatic action (even if allegorized as donkey-like labor) particularly revealing of the intellectual currents and deep anxieties within colonized Korea in the mid-1920s. The ambiguity marked by "XX(?)" likely points to censorship regarding direct political critique or mention of specific oppressed groups (like the proletariat).

그리고 이것은 必然의 事勢이다. 是非好惡의 問題를 超越한 事實이다. 朝鮮의 歷史가 1910年에 閉幕되엿슴과 가티 朝鮮의 家長家族制度가 그 國軆의 崩壞와 더부러  資本制度生産 때문에 그 殘影을 나날이 업시 하여가는 것이다. 父兄의 집을 버리고 나오게 되는 그 原因이 여긔에 잇다.
그러면 무슨 까닭으로 朝鮮의 家長家族制度는 崩壞하지안코서 견듸지 못하엿드냐? 이따위 말은 아모 意義가 업다. 무슨 까닭이라니. 그것은 家族經濟制度가 個人主義的 經濟制度로 變하기 때문이다. 밧구어 말하면 個人主義的 經濟制度에 適應한 살님을 하기 爲하야 自然히 個人的 生活이 實行되엿다는 것이다.
家庭의 分裂은 所得의 個人化에 原因한다. 일즉이 朝鮮에 잇서서 家族制度가 完全한 形態로 存在하엿슬 때는 왼家族이 家長의 힘 하나로 扶養되엿슬 때이엿스며 따라서 家長에게 扶養力이 잇섯슴으로 말미암아 充分한 支配力이 잇섯든 때이다. 그러나 家長의 支配力은 卽 ＜31＞ 扶養力은 漸漸 주러 드러갓다. 朝鮮百姓 中의 반나마 大多數의 無産階級者는 사내나 女子나 어버이나 子女나 할 것 업시 모도 다 生産場에 나서서 賃金을 바더오지 아니하면 아니되게 되엿다. 그 勞働의 種類는 如何하엿든지 何如間 各 사람이 얼마식이라도 버러오지 안으면 生活할 수 업게 되얏다. 일즉이 家長되는 사람 하나가 全家族을 爲해서 所得을 가저오든 것이 오늘날 와서는 한 집안의 所得이 個人化하엿다. 그리하야 生活의 基礎인 所得이 個人化하는 以上 家庭에서의 家長의 支配權도 漸次로 減退되지 안코서는 그대로 잇지 못하게 되얏다. 家庭의 不和는 家長에 對한 信賴와 服從이 減退함으로 말미암아 이러나는 것이다. 家族은 家長의 扶養과 支配를 信賴하고 服從할 수 업다. 웨 그러냐하면 一家의 生活은 이미 家長의 所得만을 가지고서는 到底히 하여나아갈 수가 업게 되엿스닛가. 全家族의 所得의 合計로도 오히려 一家의 生計가 어려웁거늘 엇지 家長의 支配力이 이 以上 더 持續될 것이랴.
父兄의 집을 버리고 나간다는 것은 要컨대 家長인 父兄의 支配에 對한 絶對의 廻避다.

無産階級말고 지금 中産智識階級者 中에도 父兄의 집을 버리고 나오는 사람이 만타. 그리하야 이것은 所得의 個人化라고 말 못한다하야도 要컨대 家族經濟時代가 지나가고 個人主義的 經濟制度가 施行됨에 따라서 個人主義的 精神이 浸潤한 까닭임은 疑心 업다. 새 時代의 所謂 새 思想의 洗禮를 바든 子弟가 家族主義의 頑冥을 固*하는 父兄과 相和할 수는 업는 일이다. 거긔에 當然한 經濟組織이 불러을리는 가장 深刻한 家族制度에 對한 反動을 볼 수가 잇다.
