Magazine Title: 개벽 제59호
Publication Date: 1925년 05월 01일
Article Title: 가난한 사람들 (Poor People)
Author: 李箕永
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Poor People
						Yi Ki-yŏng


Sŏng-ho left the house upon seeing there was nothing for tomorrow morning's meal. However, he didn't set out intending to get money or rice from somewhere. There was nowhere to beg, and besides, he hated doing it.
Since coming back from Tokyo last September, he hadn't earned anything, so how he had eaten and survived until now was certainly not through his own efforts. The food had run out three months ago already; how had they managed to live until now! He thought the skill with which his family had survived was remarkable, perhaps like poor people whose belt is their sustenance, just tightening their belts to live.
During that time, looking back now, he had drifted around Seoul pointlessly, trying to accomplish things that were never going to work out, so he had remained utterly ignorant of the household situation. Still, he hadn't forgotten that he had the solemn responsibility of being the head of the household.
His stomach felt heavy from the sour taste rising from the thin gruel he ate for dinner. He had eaten the whole bowl because he was hungry, but it felt unpleasant, like taking some kind of medicine. That people live eating such things? A miserable thought began to stir gently in one corner of his mind, but then the realization that even this would be gone from tomorrow struck him like lightning, making him wish he still had it. Ah! If only he had a lot of it, couldn't he sell that stuff and buy rice mixed with barley? Thinking such ridiculously foolish thoughts, he chuckled hollowly to himself.
But still, something felt regrettable. When he thought about eating that watery gruel thick with mallow greens—blowing on the incredibly hot stuff, hoo-hoo—he felt it was natural the children would fuss and refuse to eat it, and he wanted to tear apart his own mouth that had eaten it as if it were food. <59>
Then, Sŏng-ho vividly recalled meals he used to enjoy. He remembered eating delicious croaker soup at someone's house the other day, and tasty lettuce wraps at another's, and then, in order, what delicious things he had eaten at which inn. And as if to savor the leftover taste, he swallowed hard.
He again mocked his own pathetic thoughts. Just because he starved for a few meals or ate gruel for a few days, should he have such base thoughts? Is a person this weak? Am I this weak? He lamented endlessly, but still, his self-respect concluded that anyone in such a situation would naturally feel the same.
Pondering these thoughts, or trying not to, Sŏng-ho walked step by step and soon reached his intended destination. It was the town center of this county, about 10 *ri* from Sŏng-ho's house. In the early summer green fields, the hazy barley heads swayed like waves in the cool evening breeze, and the dim twilight crept over them. Towards the train station, a couple of electric lights twinkled within the haze! Twinkle! Twinkle! As if boasting of their solitary brilliance, a rarity in such a rural place. Or perhaps they shone quietly like the tear-filled eyes of a girl who has just cried, feeling some sorrow of solitude.
Sŏng-ho went straight to the post office and mailed a letter. He had 1 *wŏn* and 13 *chŏn* in his pocket; after buying a 3-*chŏn* stamp, the remainder was 10 *chŏn*, requiring no effort to calculate. This was his entire fortune! No, the entire fortune of his household. Indeed, while he himself had 10 *chŏn*, none of the other ten members of his family had even a single copper coin. So, in his house, he was considered the richest.
The letter was nothing special, just a final reminder to a friend who had promised to arrange a job—a job he had been trying to secure for several months in Seoul, which seemed perpetually close yet never materialized, like a 1,000 or 10,000 *wŏn* lottery ticket.
"I'm really counting only on you. It will be no small disaster if this doesn't work out. So please, do your absolute best to arrange it. Any job will do as long as I can earn a living. But please try hard for the one we talked about recently. Let me know as soon as possible," he pleaded repetitively, laying bare the desperate state of his household affairs, hoping his friend would feel sympathy upon reading the letter and thus strive his utmost—it was full of solicitous words, like a petition or a statement of appeal. <60>
Sŏng-ho carefully affixed the stamp to the letter, dropped it into the postbox, and thought, if this letter goes out tonight, it should arrive tomorrow morning. Then, maybe a reply will come the day after tomorrow, or the day after that. Or perhaps even sooner? And if, at that time...
How happy he would be if a telegram arrived saying, "Hired, proceed to capital immediately"! But then, where would he borrow the travel expenses? Surely, if the job came through, he wouldn't be unable to go for lack of funds. He could even walk if necessary. But what if the job doesn't materialize? What if this letter doesn't even arrive? Thinking such destitute thoughts and needless worries, he left the post office.
Sŏng-ho intended to go straight home but, wanting to avoid seeing his family's tearful faces even for a moment, he sought out a friend's house where he felt they wouldn't suspect him of coming to ask for something.
This friend worked at some government office; a young couple lived alone like a pair of pigeons, solitarily—no, perhaps cozily—renting a single room. He welcomed Sŏng-ho warmly.
As it happened, his wife had gone to their main family home in the countryside for an ancestral rite, and he was home alone. He said he was bored anyway and that Sŏng-ho had come at a good time, so Sŏng-ho did not hesitate to enter the room.
Indeed, perhaps because this young man was enlightened? Or perhaps it was kindness, intending not to make his wife observe the custom of avoiding male guests. Sŏng-ho remembered visiting once when the wife was present. At that time, unsure what greeting to offer to someone he was meeting for the first time, he hesitated briefly, then decided to just go in and see how things went. The wife stood shyly like a new bride, head bowed slightly, with an uneasy smile. Forget greeting her, Sŏng-ho lacked the courage even to look up and just plopped down. Then, feeling uneasy and bewildered, he indirectly suggested to the host:
"Let's go outside. She must be uncomfortable inside..."
But the friend, seemingly comfortable himself, replied with a grin,
"It's fine. What's wrong with staying?"
At that time, Sŏng-ho thought to himself—why does encountering another person cause such unease? Especially since she is my friend's wife? Though she acts shyly towards me now, <61> if I weren't here, she would likely laugh and talk freely with her husband. Perhaps even strip naked and slip into his bedding... And Sŏng-ho chuckled inwardly at the thought. He felt that if they could engage in interesting conversation without constraint, instead of this dull and awkward situation where they regarded each other like a cow looking at a chicken, how much more harmonious and human the gathering would feel. He thought that Elder Kim's family, the "Jesus goblins," were enviable in this regard alone.
After urging his friend outside that time, he never again entered the room when the wife was present. Even now, if she had been in the room, he had intended to exchange a few words outside and then leave.
However, since this friend was the only one who allowed his wife's face to be seen, and this wife was the only one who would meet her husband's friend, Sŏng-ho admired their courage and also considered it an honor. Speaking of which, the wife was by no means a beauty. Did she have a few freckles, or not? Having seen her only briefly then, her overall face was now faint in his memory, but anyway, she seemed just over twenty, possessing the usual shyness of a young woman, her cheeks reddish—just an ordinary face. Truthfully, any young woman looks pleasant enough if passable. She seemed so too, but her mouth was definitely large... he remembered her mouth distinctly even now.
"They asked me to join the ancestral rites in the countryside, but—" the host began, breaking the silence. People like him cannot bear silence even for a moment, so finding a conversation partner at such a time is a great relief.
"Ah! What's the point of holding ancestral rites in a household starving to death? Rites should be performed with sincerity; otherwise, it's better not to hold them, lest you deceive the spirits of your ancestors. Do spirits even eat anything! It's just setting out offerings and bowing before nothingness—"
He laughed heartily, "ha ha," his wide, slit-like mouth stretching towards his ears, looking as if it might tear. In contrast, his eyes were small and deeply set, not matching his large mouth. He always looked amusing, but anyway, the big-mouthed pair had met well.
"Indeed! That's right." <62>
Sŏng-ho agreed emphatically and sat down. Truly, he himself hadn't performed ancestral rites in who knows how long; he was now almost forgetting the dates. But it wasn't because of poverty that he didn't perform them. It had been several years since he embraced Christianity and burned the ancestral tablets and spirit placeholders; now, he had burned away Jesus too and was playing the part of the faithless, propertyless, ignorant—just playing with the character "無" (nothing/none). Being poor even in words, he often just listened to his friend speak.
The host first launched into a lengthy tale of his own hardships—how his salary was only 30 *wŏn*, making it impossible to live, how he himself could somehow manage to make ends meet, even if it meant working as an office boy (고쓰까이) at a Japanese company late at night, but oh! what were his elder brothers thinking, clinging to the bad habits of the old *yangban* days of idling and eating, doing nothing constructive. How he couldn't bear to see their sullen, crying faces, and how all he heard were stories of skipping meals for days or running out of food—and that his own household, too, was worried about having nothing for tomorrow morning. It was, again, a tale of hardship.
Sŏng-ho listened with great sympathy on the one hand, thinking "That must be how it is," but on the other hand, a resentful thought crept in: Was he deliberately saying these things because I came, to preemptively block any requests? Is this how this friend treats me now? He wondered if his worn ramie coat and faded, dented felt hat made him look too destitute, prompting such talk.
But is it because the host earns 30 *wŏn* while I earn nothing that this class consciousness arises? Sŏng-ho couldn't help but mock his own pettiness.—Are people this wretched? Am I this wretched? He felt this thought again, but forcibly dismissed it and started listening to the host's story again.
"If Kim-sang had just stayed at the company back then, he'd probably be making around 50 *wŏn* now, wouldn't he?"
The way he looked up suggested he meant that if Sŏng-ho had stayed at a certain company he quit years ago, his salary would have reached that amount. The host continued,
"Now, there's nothing else to hope for, just need to latch onto a junior official position (*p'anim'gwan*) to manage my livelihood, but ah! That's utterly <63> difficult. Pak Kun has been lobbying the Provincial Governor and Councilor with endorsements from some Noble Lord and Bureaucrat for ages, and still hasn't succeeded, so what hope is there for someone like me with no connections? Honestly, when I think about doing this for the rest of my life... why am I living? Is this a human life? Or a machine's life? Such pathetic thoughts arise, but what can I do? Not to say the unsayable, but should I resort to theft or robbery? Ah! Wrestling with the brush day after day, this repetitive work makes me sick now—indeed, some Westerner said it, didn't they? The office is a prison. Truly, it's like serving a hard labor sentence. Maybe high-class hard labor... ha ha ha."
Sŏng-ho remained silent, just smiling faintly. The host, as if remembering something forgotten, quickly took a couple of deep drags from his cigarette, savoring the smoke, then said,
"Ah—and—"
As he spoke, smoke curled out of his nostrils. "Ah! And! Serving superiors who rotate in and out (*ppyŏmdori*) is incredibly difficult. When I think about those petty, unpleasant, good-for-nothing sorts lording it over me just because they have the title (가다가끼) of 'superior,' making me feel small—Hey! You damned thing! Will I die if I don't do this job!—the urge to quit and punch them surges up! But then, thinking about tomorrow, the pressure of livelihood bears down, and I swallow it again. So, a person's lot is dead, what else is there?"
He laughed his hollow laugh again. Sŏng-ho couldn't help but laugh along. It wasn't that the host's words were funny, but his facial expressions and gestures while speaking were already amusing, and his laugh made it even more so. A single lamp dimly lit the room, and moonlight already faintly brightened the east window.
"Ah! If I can't even manage my own mood, how can I cater to others'?"
The host glibly continued his tale.
"Still, the one who left recently was easygoing and had some flair, we drank together and messed around, so it was alright! Ah! But the one who came recently, where did such a creature come from? So nitpicky, quite the pedant, catering to his moods is incredibly difficult. <64> Ha ha ha. But we have to stick to those in power; what rights do we have?"
Then, as if trying to convey some meaning, he stared intently at Sŏng-ho for a while, then composed his expression back to normal and mentioned who had been promoted to which section chief and who had received a prestigious transfer, adding that since they liked him, they surely wouldn't treat him coldly if he visited them later—ultimately, it was a request for Sŏng-ho to write several letters for him in Japanese. Sŏng-ho just laughed vacantly, "ha ha."
"Ah! Who knows how to write that damned 'sorobun' (書類文 - formal document style)? I can barely manage the official draft format ending with 'konodan shōkai sōrō nari' (此段照會候也 - Thus we inquire regarding this matter). Ha ha ha. It's getting hot, so I should send summer greetings. A servant's first duty is loyalty to the master. Ha ha ha," he laughed loudly, seeming like his self-deprecation stemmed not from humility but from some servile thought, forcing a hollow laugh. Perhaps it was a wry smile about his entire hope resting on a single junior official position, pricking his conscience.
Sŏng-ho thought.—In the prime of youth, becoming a slave merely to one's belly, what a terrifying hungry ghost (餓鬼)! Ah, they manage to obtain a piece of bread (*p'ang*) for today to barely survive, but what about tomorrow? Next year? When he truly considered what might happen, he couldn't help but shudder. He also pictured countless poor families like his own, unable even to get thin gruel, men and women, old and young, clinging to each other, trembling at the terror of death, and unconsciously shed hot tears inwardly.
The host seemed lost in thought for a while, then laughed a laugh of despair, "ha ha," and said,
"What! To save one's life, one must kill the person within." He looked meaningfully at Sŏng-ho's face.
"To save one's life, one must kill the person within?" Sŏng-ho, bewildered by the meaning, repeated the question.
"Ah! Isn't that so? Can one truly live in this world as a person? Truly, I too have lost much spirit lately. Am I getting old already, though not that big? Sometimes such sudden thoughts occur, but recently I was so angry I beat up one of my superiors—the fellow showed absolutely no respect for me as a person. Whatever happened afterwards, I just cursed him out, 'Damn you, you wretched fellow!' Ha ha ha."
"Ha ha ha, and then?" <65>
Sŏng-ho, intrigued, finally urged him to continue.
"Ah! But that became an issue, and I almost lost this," he gestured to his neck. "So, what choice did I have? I ended up groveling and begging again, and finally, things settled down." At this, Sŏng-ho burst out laughing again.
"Ha ha. So where was the original intention in beating him? Where was the righteous spirit of defiance? That's why I later thought, 'To save one's life, one must kill the person within.' This is my originally conceived philosophy of life. Ha ha ha."
Hearing the host's explanation, Sŏng-ho finally learned this grand philosophy.
"They don't use people, only machines. Indeed, since modern civilization is one of machines, perhaps using people like machines is the true nature of civilized people—so they kill the person and use only machine-like automatons, not people who live by reason or emotion. Such people are either killed and used, or if they don't die, they're just snapped! Broken! Only those who obey orders blindly, like slaves, like cattle, are considered very good people. Isn't that ridiculous?"
"Otherwise, they're called rebellious Koreans (不逞鮮人)?"
"Right! Right!" the host exclaimed, as if Sŏng-ho had read his mind.
"But is killing the person within while saving one's life truly happiness?"
Sŏng-ho suddenly asked this.
"Well, of course, it's suffering, but..."
The host trailed off, as if caught off guard.
"Then wouldn't it be better to save the person? And kill the life—"
"Ha ha ha. Ideally, of course, that's right, but a weak person like me may kill the spirit but cannot kill the body."
"Then what would you do if both the spirit and the body were to be killed?"
Sŏng-ho asked again. The host blinked his eyes for a moment, then <66>
"What do you mean, what to do? Then one simply dies completely. If one must die anyway..."
He looked at Sŏng-ho. Sŏng-ho smiled faintly and asked,
"Are you quite sure about that?" pressing the point. The host, unsure why he was pressing the matter, looked at him with suspicion in his strangely wide eyes. Sŏng-ho moved a step closer, raised his voice slightly, but still smiling, asked again like this:
"Haven't you killed both your life and the person within?"
"Huh! Me?—"
The host looked startled for a moment. "Yes, you," Sŏng-ho said, continuing with the following words.
"Brother O (吳兄), you receive a salary of 30 *wŏn* a month, so perhaps you think you're comfortably saving your life, but fundamentally, how miserable a figure is 30 *wŏn* a month for a household? Didn't you just say yourself, Brother O, that it's impossible to live on that? That's not saving a life; it's merely staving off death. It's not the living of life, but merely the continuation of existence."
The host listened intently, and as Sŏng-ho finished speaking, he nodded repeatedly. Suddenly, his large mouth opened wide.
"Ah-ha ha ha," he laughed like a braying donkey, startling Sŏng-ho.
"If you put it that way, that's true too."
The host laughed as if embarrassed and amused by his own contradiction, making Sŏng-ho regret having needlessly shattered his philosophy of life. So he changed the subject, and they exchanged various stories. The thought, 'What is the purpose of living?' arose anew...
Night seemed to have deepened considerably. Sŏng-ho was about to return home but was persuaded by the host to stay and collapsed where he sat. Before falling asleep, he pondered tomorrow's affairs, others' matters, and the question of life or death, but failed to find a solution and fell asleep. <67>
Startled awake, he saw the morning sun shining red through the east window. The dew-laden earth's energy flowed into the room, breathing new life into his newly awakened soul. Sŏng-ho sprang up and went outside, inhaling the fresh air deeply. It felt like mother's life-giving milk! The more he breathed, the more vigorous his spirit became. Ah! The grace of Nature! At this moment, everything seemed to exist for him.
Looking up again, the blue sky was clear, and distant mountain peaks rose like paintings. Leaves danced joyfully in the gentle breeze, birds in the air beat their wings enthusiastically, singing their morning songs. The sun, like an emperor adorned in golden robes, rose majestically in the eastern sky...
Sŏng-ho stood transfixed for a while, watching the spectacle of all things on earth greeting the new morning, then, as if remembering something, went back inside. The host was still not up, mumbling from under the covers in a voice thick with sleep,
"Why, you're up already... Sleep more = huh!"
He turned over. Drawn by his words, Sŏng-ho lay back down but couldn't sleep anymore. So he lay there, playing with the sunlight streaming like golden threads through a hole in the papered door. Countless dust motes danced in the beam. They seemed to be having fun, performing a round dance. He blocked the light beam with his hand. His fingers were beautifully dyed by the light, pleasing to look at. Like a small child engrossed in a fun game, he moved his hand away and covered it again, lying there, when someone entered abruptly without even coughing. It was a young relative of the host.
"Ah! Still sleeping? I was asked to write this, but I don't have any paper..." he said, pulling something out. A quick glance revealed it seemed to be his résumé. It appeared he too was looking for a job.
He had previously worked as a military police assistant (憲兵 補助員), but after the March First Movement, when the military police system was reformed into a regular police system—changing military rule (武斷政治) to the nicely named cultural rule (文化政治)—he shed his yellow uniform for a black one. However, complaining that the abuses by the ******* were the same as before, he participated in a strike or something and ended up being dismissed (馘首).
Sŏng-ho bowed to him and compared their respective hardships. Immediately, an indescribable anger and vengeful feeling <68> surged up like dark clouds gathering for a storm—suddenly his mood turned foul. So Sŏng-ho left the house right away. The host, looking apologetic, called after him,
"You should have had breakfast before leaving... There's cold rice, but no side dishes..." Sŏng-ho heard this from behind.
"Even cold rice would be fine," he shouted inwardly, mocking his own base animal nature again.


Sŏng-ho's feet unconsciously turned towards home. It seemed his feet knew first that there was nowhere else to go. Did they manage to cook breakfast at home? Or not? He pondered both possibilities, then chuckled hollowly at the baseless thought that they must have managed somehow. He knew for sure there was no way they could have procured anything when he left, so how could any means have appeared overnight just because he slept out? Rather, the family would likely look intently at his face when he returned, as if wondering if he had found some solution. That was, after all, the correct order of things.
At home were his uncle and younger brother. Besides them, only women and children. The younger brother farmed, but the uncle and he himself were idlers. No, he wasn't even a proper idler. Perhaps an idler-in-training? Since returning from Japan last year, he had become such an aimless person. Still, the family seemed to trust us idlers, and among them, they seemed to trust him the most. So when his uncle and he went out dressed up and returned, they would look back and forth between uncle and nephew, as if wondering if some solution had arisen. But it wasn't just them; uncle and nephew also looked at each other that way. Wondering if the other had found some solution...
Even now, when he went in, they would look at him like that again. That moment, however brief, made Sŏng-ho feel unbearable suffering. No, it was a terrifying fear. It felt like exposing his incompetence and wretchedness, or like suffering the greatest insult. They seemed to heap upon him all possible scorn and contempt, saying, "Give up playing the man who cannot save his wife and children!"
With what face could he face them now? Feeling anxiety, shame, despair, sighs, anger—all sorts of negative emotions—he arrived <69> in front of his house. A palpitating heart, as if embarking on a great adventure! Did they eat breakfast? Or not? He was curious, eager to know which it was, yet also afraid to find out, making his heart pound. His conviction that they hadn't eaten grew stronger. In that instant, his heart clenched as a realization struck him like lightning: he saw his seven-year-old son, not at school, standing listlessly in a corner of the yard. This proved they hadn't eaten breakfast.
Sŏng-ho's face flushed hot as he entered the brushwood gate. His wife, her eight-month belly protruding, looked intently at him with the usual expression expecting some news. Fearing her gaze, he quickly entered the room. The wife, perhaps already resigned, changed her expression and asked,
"How was your morning?"
Her voice was unusually gentle and soft. It was a tender question, deeply concerned about whether he had eaten breakfast somewhere or not. Sŏng-ho had definitely skipped breakfast, but wanting to test his wife's sympathy, he deliberately fabricated a story, tossing out the irresponsible words,
"I ate before coming, but I suppose the household didn't have breakfast!" The wife's sallow, yellowish face clearly showed signs of hunger, her forehead wrinkled like the pleats of her skirt! Her deeply sunken eyes opened vacantly, her listless posture looked as if a puff of air could knock her over. As if lost in thought, she lowered her eyes and stared at one spot. As Sŏng-ho's words fell, a faint smile, like a withered flower, suddenly bloomed on her thin lips.
"I thought you must have starved too—where did you eat?" she asked, her eyes widening slightly. Her pupils shone differently, as if she felt some satisfaction. Sŏng-ho was utterly dumbfounded by this unexpected reply and expression.
He had expected her to shriek in a metallic voice, "Is it enough that you alone ate? Knowing full well there was nothing for breakfast, what do you mean 'I suppose you didn't have breakfast'?" crying bitterly and scratching at the gourd dipper. But that she would love him with such generous sympathy, he truly hadn't known.
Sŏng-ho was two years older than his wife. He mistreated her as much as possible. She always looked hateful to him. <70> No, he hadn't found her so hateful before, but as he aged, she became increasingly distasteful. Already nearing 30, her face was sallow and puffy like an opium addict's, her forehead was receding, and her hair was thinning, the part looking like a newly paved road. Moreover, the balding spot on top of her head was ugly, like a patch of turf torn from the end of a path in a communal cemetery on a mountain ridge. Seeing her dressed shabbily in countrified clothes, speaking bluntly without charm, especially looking at her mole-like hands (calloused from harsh labor), even the affection he had would vanish.
'Living with *that* as my wife.'
Whenever he thought this, he cursed her, yet somehow they had lived together until now. (He endured even greater frustrations, but thinking of the four children she bore made him want to beat himself as much as he hated her.)
Actually, being not yet 30, she shouldn't look so old, but malnutrition, childbirth, labor, illness, and poverty had worn her down, making her age prematurely. If only her mouth were shaped a bit better and her face slightly narrower, she might look quite pretty. Indeed, when she first married, even in his hazy memory now, she seemed lovely. She was sixteen then! Her plump, round face had the bloom of youth, her cheeks rosy like ripe fruit, looking tempting. When she smiled, dimples formed in her cheeks, and her dark eyes held affection, seeming visible even now. But back then, he neither disliked her nor found her pretty. He was just an ignorant child then, unaware of what a wife was...
Sometimes, Sŏng-ho would reminisce about the past, picturing his days as a young groom (초립동이), and casually ask his wife about those times. She would suddenly flare up, her voice sharp and biting, invariably saying:
"Why ask that? Just thinking about it is dreadful!"
Tears would well up in her eyes. She seemed unable to contain her sorrow, as if a forgotten grief had resurfaced.
"You wanted to kill me back then, didn't you?" <71>
"Aigo, what a horrible thing to say!"
"No, you definitely hated me, right?"
"Ho!..."
"Then my hating you now is payback for that time," he'd say. The wife would sit there silently, unable to bear her anger, breathing heavily, then retort,
"What! You're a man, so you did whatever you pleased!..."
Her voice choked, she bit her lip. Back then, she harbored the sorrow of an empty room; now, having fallen out of favor and aging year by year added to her heartache. How many times had she cried secretly while looking in the mirror? As she often said, if not for the one son still living, she would have swallowed lye or run away long ago. But that wasn't all. Looking into Sŏng-ho's heart revealed feelings that caused her even greater unhappiness.
Whether she did it when he was away, he didn't know, but whenever Sŏng-ho was home, she always applied white powder thickly. So,
'Try applying powder or anything else,' Sŏng-ho would scoff inwardly. Of course, they fought a lot too. He would suddenly slap her cheek hard enough to make sparks fly, grab her by the hair, beat her severely, then lock the door and kick her out. At such times, the wife would go out crying, threatening to drown herself, but would sleep at a neighbor's house and return before breakfast the next day. Anyway, although he himself was greatly upset by her, she too seemed to have caused him considerable distress. Her hair thinning so badly was apparently due to falling out after a severe illness last year while he was away in Japan.
When he returned from Japan, she looked even worse. Still, she seemed most eager to see him. Thinking he had died in the Great Kanto Earthquake, she had spent a month crying and wailing. Then, when he suddenly reappeared alive, she was overjoyed. Indeed, Sŏng-ho, now tall and slender with a fair complexion, looked perhaps 26 or 7, but smooth-faced without a beard, appearing quite young. Not only his appearance but also his personality was feminine. However, his actions were often eccentric. Perhaps such a person would be called someone with a dual personality. Normally quiet, <72> when he did speak, he was shy like a new bride, but at other times, strangely, he would pour out passionate eloquence. Not only this, but he had been known as an unusual person in his hometown since childhood. Known for being calm and maidenly yet frequently running off somewhere without notice, unable to settle in one place, wandering here and there. Thus, he even earned the nickname "maidenly playboy."
It was long ago, but his grandmother, now deceased, whenever she saw Sŏng-ho sitting idly—which he often did—would remark,
"Why is that boy like that! His wife (댁) is steady and clever like the four seasons—" then add, "You should switch places with your wife!" and cackle. Still, when he returned after being away for a long time without notice, or when he got thoroughly drunk and caused trouble, she would exclaim anew in surprise, clicking her tongue,
"Who knew such a gentle boy would become a playboy?"
Worried she might not see a great-grandchild in her lifetime because Sŏng-ho wandered so much, she nagged her son incessantly to bring him back. Indeed, the child born after that is the now seven-year-old boy. So, the rumor spread widely in the neighborhood that this child was conceived after being fetched from Chŏlla Province.
He and his wife married in early spring when he was fourteen. It was the auspicious year of his grandmother's 60th birthday (還甲), and his father, out of filial piety, arranged it so she could celebrate seeing her grandchild's wife too. In effect, he was sacrificed like a young lamb for the scapegoat offering (贖罪祭) at his grandmother's birthday feast. Thus, like young men forced into early marriage who now clamor about divorce issues—arguing, "I didn't marry of my own free will. I was forced into marriage by the evil custom of early marriage, so I have the right to divorce. If divorce is a great sin, society should bear the responsibility."—he resonated with such theories, flavored with Ellen Key's ideas on the sanctity of divorce, and suffered headaches along with them. Now, especially, the hatred stemming from his wife being older, not beautiful, uneducated, and an old-fashioned woman reeking of castor oil grew stronger as his knowledge increased and his perspective broadened. Whenever this happened, the words of someone, "He who has not experienced love has no value in life," would suddenly come to mind, piercing his heart, and the yearning and impulse to experience love himself became unbearable without at least reading romance novels to taste the sweet flavor of others' romances. So, when walking the streets, if he encountered a beautiful woman who caught his eye, <73> he would think inwardly,
"Hey! Wouldn't you like to have an affair with me?"
and then secretly laugh at his own impurity.
But the wife, whatever abuse she suffered, seemed only to plead not to be divorced.
"Look! I really can't live with you. Your world (mind) and my world are different, leading only to misunderstanding and conflict. How can I endure this day after day? Therefore, divorcing promptly would be happiness for both of us. It's not like the old days; nowadays, divorcing and remarrying is certainly not a sin. No, even in the old days, it shouldn't have been a sin. If it's not a sin for a man to take a concubine, what sin is there in separating because you dislike living together? Rather, not divorcing in such a situation would be the sin."
Sŏng-ho had conveyed this sentiment to his wife sometime before.
"Stop it. Am I some kind of loose woman (환냥년)? A kisaeng or prostitute (갈보년)? My parents arranged this husband for me, and he's alive and well right before my eyes. Am I crazy to marry again? Aigo! Such disgraceful talk... If that's not a sin, then there are no sins in this world. What! A man is a man, so it's okay for him to take a concubine. They say a proper man wouldn't take a concubine for that reason. If you say that, don't think I'll be fooled; I'm shrewd about these things too. 'A quarrel between husband and wife is like cutting water with a knife,' didn't the old folks say that knowing full well? If you're going to live together for life, of course, you'll quarrel sometimes. Ah! If you divorced every time you quarreled, you'd end up doing nothing but divorcing day and night."
The wife had refused like this back then, smiling faintly. Infuriated by this, he retorted,
"What should I do if I dislike you!"
"What should I do if I like you!" the wife shot back, mimicking his tone. Flabbergasted, he laughed hollowly and pointlessly,
"Look! You're quite something too. Why? Am I the only man around? Wouldn't it be better to remarry? I want to get married every day. Why insist on living together when the other person says they dislike it? You wouldn't eat food you dislike either, right? Similarly, <74> why won't you grant my request not to live with you, whom I dislike?"
The wife stared at him blankly again, then
"Why? Am I food?" she laughed again.
"No, I'm not saying you're food, but the principle (리치) is the same."
"What principle is what principle? Your parents and my parents paired us up, wishing us to live well together. What's the point of saying you like or dislike it now? You have to endure it even if you dislike it, and if you like it, that's good. That's just fate, what can you do! Then who told you to marry me in the first place?"
"Because I was an ignorant child back then."
"Then why have children?"
"That's why I had them too."
"What! 'That's why I had them'? After having children and all, what nonsense are you talking now? Make me a virgin again! Then I'll gladly remarry. You made me a used woman (헌 게집)."
"Didn't I become a used man (헌 산애) because of you too?"
"Ho ho! Then it's tit for tat. A used man and a used woman should live well together, shouldn't they? It's a sin for a used man to desire a new woman, and a sin for a used woman to desire a new man, so isn't it best to live as we are? Besides, how long have we actually lived together that you keep saying you dislike living together! Always just wandering around... Stop talking such crazy nonsense and let's just sleep. Ah, I'm sleepy!"
The wife yawned, haa-, ultimately dismissing his words as crazy talk. Back then, realizing the issue wouldn't be resolved with such pointless dialogue, he stopped talking. Of course, if he had been determined to divorce, it wouldn't have been impossible. However, he had a bigger issue preoccupying him, constantly yearning for and pursuing it with fervor, so that matter had been put aside for the time being.
This was none other than the studies he wished to pursue. He had attempted to study abroad several times, <75> but failed each time, and his current presence at home was because he had barely escaped with his life during the earthquake disaster. He had, of course, been studying while working arduously (苦學) there.
Therefore, although the divorce issue still lay dormant in his mind, hearing his wife's words now—the astute reader will guess what Sŏng-ho heard that left him dumbfounded—could hardly make her suddenly seem beautiful. Yet, hearing such words in this situation, he perceived a certain indescribable human warmth in her sallow face that transcended physical appearance. It evoked a sympathetic human feeling even in his cold heart. Thus, rather than the dislike he felt for her as a wife, he felt a sense of justice and humanity, thinking, "She too is human!"
Sŏng-ho suddenly rushed forward, hugged his wife tightly, and gave her a long kiss on her withered-flower-like lips. The wife, experiencing such an action for the first time in her life, showed an expression of not knowing what to do; her faint smile seemed to convey a kind of startled joy.
"Oh! God of Justice! God of Humanity! Please deliver all humanity from human suffering!"
Sŏng-ho offered this prayer inwardly...


The wife sat blankly for a while, then hesitantly brought up the story of going to borrow rice the previous evening and receiving harsh words.
"With the younger uncle here and the children, I couldn't bear to just sit and starve, so I went to the main house (큰집) to ask, though it was difficult."
Then, she suddenly let out a soft sigh. Sŏng-ho, sensing something amiss, urged her on,
"And then?"
"So, I asked for just one *mal* of rice on credit, promising to repay it when the cocoons were sold, but that elder cousin brother (兄님) jumped up and made a scene—'Isn't it enough that we paid for his meals while that brother-in-law (서방님) was in Seoul! What more do you want! Shameless inside and out! We can't live <76> because of our sixth cousins (六寸)! From now on, not a single grain of rice, not a single grain of unhulled rice, absolutely nothing!'—he complained endlessly. I was so embarrassed and angry—'What sin is it to pay for a sixth cousin's meals with money spent on drinking and wasteful rituals (뭇구리)? You say you can't live because of your sixth cousins? How much have you actually helped? You won't even give one *mal* of rice on credit... Who asked you to pay for anything, just to brag about it later?'—I turned sharply and walked out, but tears blurred my vision so much I could barely walk!..."
Then, as if the humiliation she suffered then resurfaced, the wife lifted the hem of her skirt to wipe away the flowing tears. A couple of teardrops had already fallen on the front panel of her jade-green ramie skirt, creating coin-sized circles. She lifted her bloodshot eyes again.
"So, how much did they pay for the meals that they talk like that? If they weren't going to give it, they should have just said so, why say all sorts of things to hurt people..."
She looked up at him. Sŏng-ho listened to his wife's words, trembling like a prisoner hearing a death sentence. Each phrase felt like an electric shock, a tingling sensation running through his chest—was it anger? Pain? An indescribable anguish. His mind went dizzy, as if he had hit his head hard somewhere, and he could only stare blankly ahead.
"Ah— Is that so! Is that really so? What's the point of living after suffering such an insult? If I had 25 *wŏn* in my hand right now, I would throw it down immediately and take revenge on that dirty money!!"
He was so furious he clenched his fists and ground his teeth. And he blamed his wife again, saying if they were going to starve, just starve, why go and say such things.
"I didn't know it would be like that either. I was just so desperate—and since I could pay it back as soon as the cocoons were sold—I thought perhaps it would be better than going to someone else's house..."
The wife couldn't finish her sentence and wiped her tears again. She seemed to feel class consciousness more acutely than kinship ties.
Indeed, how much had their family relied on the main house? They were rich, and we were poor, so it was true we had received some favors, but surely it wasn't to the point where they couldn't live because of us. Within the same village, within the same family, for them alone to eat well and dress warmly <77> must have troubled their conscience, so they gave a bit of money, a measure of rice, a bowl of food. But to say they couldn't live because of that—isn't that too harsh? Throwing away thousands of *wŏn* in one go gambling, wasting hundreds of *wŏn* in a single night at restaurants—that's living well externally? And performing rituals (뭇구리하고 살푸리하고), making offerings at temples (불공), pouring out devotion to shamans (녹으메 정성드린다고), scooping out grain by the *sŏm* and cutting silk by the bolt to waste—that's living well internally? Is it only because of the pennies and grains shared within the family that they claim they can't live? If so, they shouldn't have given anything in the first place! No matter how much one begs, if they refuse, they won't be robbed. Whether the sixth cousins starve to death or the fourth cousins freeze to death, isn't it enough if they themselves live well? Since the right of ownership is established by sacred and inviolable law, there's no fear of losing even a hair's breadth. When did we take anything from them? When did we beg for anything? And when did we tunnel through their wall? What does it mean, saying we made it impossible for them to live? he cried out inwardly...
Giving first as if performing some charity, only to brag about it later! Why, who asked them to give in the first place? With what intention? He wanted to vomit again at the base psychology of the wealthy.
During last year's disaster relief efforts, thinking I was dead, that cousin brother came to meet me, traveling nearly 100 *ri* on the day I returned. Believing my survival was a stroke of luck, seeming genuinely to cherish my life infinitely, he held my hand, again and again, stroking it—that cousin brother back then! The impression of that time is still vivid in my eyes. Ah!—That this cousin brother's household refused a loan of one *mal* of rice to sustain my life, instead bringing up past favors and heaping insults upon us? What contradiction is this? Was that love back then worth less than the price of one *mal* of rice? How much is one *mal* of rice worth anyway? 1 *wŏn* 50 *chŏn*! Isn't the market price 1 *wŏn* 50 *chŏn*? Ah! Then does my life amount to less than 1 *wŏn* 50 *chŏn*? Ah, Brother! Brother! Sŏng-ho wept sorrowfully in his heart.
Sŏng-ho, unfortunately lacking an elder brother, respected and loved his sixth cousin brother like his own. Personally, too, from this so-called meal money issue, he had received material sympathy a few times. He also occasionally had the honor of being a companion in amusement. Grateful for his generosity, he had tried his best to share whatever mental benefit his shallow knowledge could offer. Of course, it was intangible and insignificant, but being poor in material things, he had nothing else to offer him.
Humans are emotional animals. And they are governed by environment and circumstances. No matter how great the past kindness, at a time like this—<78> when facing the terror of 'I'm utterly doomed,' reaching out a hand for salvation to that benefactor—experiencing both despair and insult simultaneously, emotions cannot remain unchanged. A full person doesn't appreciate delicacies, but a hungry person eats even cold rice with relish. Giving one *mal* of rice at such a time, even if not given freely, would be appreciated more deeply than giving several *sŏm* or hundreds of *nyang* freely at other times. Thus, unless one inherently has the heart of a thief, one knows to repay such kindness. Therefore, when faced with such a refusal in such circumstances, regardless of whether past favors were as high as Mount T'ai or as deep as the sea, emotions cannot remain as they were.
Isn't there a saying, "Don't chase a cornered thief"? It means that even an enemy is forgiven when life is cornered by death. Pheasant meat is tasty, but they say you don't catch a pheasant that has entered your house. Why? Because that's human sentiment (人情). In winter, when snow piles deep, pheasants come down to human dwellings because there's nothing to eat. "We're starving because there's nothing to eat, please save us—let us eat the grain scattered in your yard"—they approach people who hunt them, unafraid. Hunger (餓鬼) is that terrifying. Life, even entering a place of death like this, still tries to find a way to live. Anyone who lifts a stone by a wall in spring will see something coiled there like bean sprouts. Unable to sprout green shoots like others because pressed down by the heavy stone, its life force, striving to live, cannot shoot upwards but coils upon itself. How much more so for humans? There should be a limit to giving a stone when asked for rice cake.
He realized that circumstances and situations could turn benefactors into enemies, and enemies into benefactors. This could be the beautiful emotion of human life, and also the evil emotion, but humans cannot transcend this, he thought.
One *mal* of rice! 1 *wŏn* 50 *chŏn*! It's trivial, but if that trivial thing brought tears of blood to his eyes, he would never be able to forget that memory his entire life. If humans have souls, he would never forget it eternally. The more trivial, the more infuriating. The more painful. Thinking this, Sŏng-ho shed tears along with his wife, weeping silently.
He wanted to go anywhere right now, find employment, and at least repay that money first. He closed his eyes and thought about places where he could find such employment.
Of course, this incident was the doing of the cousin's wife (아주머니), the mean-spirited act of an ignorant and stingy woman, hardly worth dwelling on, <79> so feeling this way towards the cousin brother himself was unreasonable, but this incident created something like a sharp knife severing the relationship between him and his cousin brother. It was clearly class consciousness. This is the absolute divide of the capitalist era, where the haves and have-nots are separated like the South and North Poles. Even between parents and children, brothers and sisters, they are divided according to "having" and "not having." Therefore, class antagonism supersedes ethics. Isn't this why there is killing and enmity between parents, children, and siblings? The haves are the enemies of the have-nots. The have-nots are the enemies of the haves. What is family? What are relatives? All that matters is the struggle between having and not having, until one side wins or loses, settling the score. However! It's class struggle! he cried out. He intuitively realized that without a Great Revolution bringing the baptism of a New Humanity, there would never be happiness for humankind.
The so-called meal money wasn't something he had begged for either. The issue arose from a mutual plan: if he went to Seoul, found a job, and continued his studies, his cousin brother would also come to Seoul to do something together. Since he had no money for meals, he was unavoidably unable to refuse his cousin brother's payment. Be that as it may, since he hadn't used the money improperly, what could be done? Not wanting to sniff out any socialist scent, but fundamentally, he didn't attach significant value to the concept of ownership—'mine' versus 'others'.' Since 'I' myself came from 'others,' and 'my' life depends on the efforts of 'others,' 'I' too have a duty to do something beneficial for 'others.' Only after fulfilling that duty would 'I' have the right to benefit from the labor of 'others.' Therefore, if his life did no harm to society and contributed beneficially to himself and others, he felt his conscience allowed him to accept anyone's help for his right to life, and furthermore, he had the right to seek it. Moreover, the main house's wealth wasn't earned by that cousin brother, nor did his grandfather earn it honestly; it was said to be money squeezed from the people's blood while serving as a magistrate (守令). Even in the past, it was said, "If benevolent, one cannot be rich (爲仁이면 不富라)." Who, past or present, could be both benevolent and rich? However, he wasn't saying this to avoid repaying the meal money.
The wife went outside, brought in a mulberry leaf basket, and started trimming the leaves on the porch. The silkworms had just finished their baby molt. The son, hungry, whined for something, glancing at his mother, then, seemingly unable to bear it any longer,
"Mother! Hey!—" <80>
He pleaded for something. As soon as the sound escaped, suddenly the wife's voice, cursing through clenched teeth, and the thudding sound of her hitting the son were heard.
"Knowing full well we didn't have breakfast, where would there be rice that you're whining? Really! Huh—. Born without luck, but damn quick to want to stuff your face!"
Amidst the hitting and screaming,
"Aigo, Mother... uhhh! Uh! Aigo, aigo, ah! Ah!"
The son's weak, mournful cries were heard. Again, Sŏng-ho felt a dizzying, bone-chilling sensation, as if fire were poured onto his face.
'Living like this is simply impossible!'
He resolved on some terrifying thought. He clenched his fists, gritted his teeth, and trembled violently. He wanted to scream "Ak—!" rush out, beat his wife, throw his son down, hit and kill anyone he encountered. He wanted to set the Earth on fire and watch them burn to death. He wished for a great earthquake like the one in Tokyo, for active volcanoes to erupt violently, to see these hungry ghosts (餓鬼) perish.
He wanted to laugh the Guffaw of the Devil.
"Ah—Still not stopping? Huh! Still!"
The wife berated the son.
"Stop crying and trim the mulberry leaves quickly! An unlucky brat should at least work hard! From tomorrow, go gather firewood! Does going to that damn school put food in your mouth first? Ah! Still not trimming! Still! Huh! If you don't want to work, get out and die! If you die, you won't need to eat!"
Her venomous eyes and punching fists seemed visible. The son, trembling in terror of the violence, choked back his sobs and fell silent, trimming the leaves, or perhaps not. Afterwards, only a deathly silence continued, through which the distant, mournful cry of a cuckoo <81> could be heard...
"Mother! Trim this too?"
The son's innocent, weak voice drifted out.
"O-nya (Oh yes), trim that too!"
The mother's reply, stemming from warm affection, immediately followed. The son let out another choked sigh, "Heuk! Heuk!", pitifully.
Sŏng-ho contrasted the earlier scene of mother and son hitting, being hit, cursing, and wailing with this current exchange of warm affection, feeling anew the transience of human emotions. Perhaps like a clear morning after lingering rain! It evoked a certain strange sentiment, both sorrowful and beautiful. However, in them, he glimpsed again the psychology of the conqueror and the conquered. Ah! He saw the epitome of life between the strong and the weak even in this mother and son. He was newly astonished at human ignorance.
From the inner room (안방), a cousin's child whimpered and then seemed to get hit by its mother too. Startled by this, the nursing baby screamed shrilly—as if touched by fire. The older child's cry, weak like a starving calf's. The relentless screaming of the infant being beaten like laundry! And the mother's curses! This was utterly not a human home, but a valley of ghosts (鬼谷)! A den of demons (魔窟)! Sŏng-ho's heart felt slashed by a knife again.
The younger brother (아오), with disheveled hair, came in from outside carrying an A-frame carrier (지게), dropped it with a thud in the yard, sighed deeply, "Hoo-," then
"Brother (兄님)! You couldn't arrange any money, right? We need to weed the fields, but we need food..."
He looked at Sŏng-ho with the usual expectant gaze, his expression one of despair. Sŏng-ho, having nothing to say, remained silent.
"Other people's barley is turning yellow, but ours is still green. Even the barley hates our family!"
This was the aunt's lament.
"Why? Couldn't you even bring firewood? Even if we get food, we won't be able to cook it without firewood!" <82>
This was the sister-in-law (弟嫂) blaming the younger brother, worrying about fuel. Hugging her full-term belly, she barely supported her weak, starving body, yet still waddled around like a duck. The wife,
"Here's another one!"
as if to say, pushed out her similar belly and sat facing her. A pair of pregnant women! Well-timed pregnancies, indeed. As if the demon of poverty weren't enough.—
Suddenly! A figure in a yellow uniform appeared at the gate, calling out, "Letter delivery—" dropped a sealed postcard, and left. Sŏng-ho felt both anxiety and joy simultaneously as he quickly tore open the letter handed to him by his brother. It was the awaited reply from Seoul, written in the customary formal Sino-Korean style starting with inquiries about his well-being (謹詢日來의 兄軆萬旺), with the phrase "Affairs did not go as intended, regretfully what can be done (事不如意 未安奈何)" neatly explaining that the job had fallen through. Powerlessly, he dropped the letter onto his seat and closed his eyes silently. He lacked the energy even to answer his brother's question about where it came from...
Ah! It's over now. My desire to study is utterly hopeless! Hopeless! Suicide! Hand-to-mouth existence (苟且偸生)! Family! Wandering! Hopeless! Suicide!?!
He repeated these thoughts over and over.
Sŏng-ho wrote an IOU for 25 *wŏn*, gave it to his brother, asking him to deliver it, then changed into borrowed clothes and went outside. Heading towards the town center, he met his uncle on the road, who had managed to borrow 2 *wŏn* for urgent needs (장변) somewhere and was carrying home one *mal* of rice he had bought first. He continued straight down to the town center.
His intention was to visit the friend working at the government office, explain his family's situation, and ask him to help secure some salaried position.
However, before reaching the house, his mind changed again. He didn't know if such a position was even available, and even if it were, it would be monthly employment (月雇) for about 20-odd *wŏn*. Could a family of over ten really survive on that? Of course, if that were the sole objective, they could somehow keep the family alive, and he could repay the debt, but would his own life find satisfaction in that? <83> What satisfaction would there be in taking that kind of revenge? Ah! It's a base revenge. A stingy revenge, just like the stingy actions of the rich. Rather than preserving life through such petty revenge, how much more valuable would a magnificent death be? For a cursed life, death is happiness...
Changing his mind again with these thoughts, he decided to go to Seoul to find a certain comrade (同志). As he walked, step by step, the sound of his son crying seemed to follow him from behind. The sound of his cousin crying! The sounds of cursing and screaming followed, and images of their plight appeared vividly. Then, before his eyes, appeared the sister-in-law, collapsed from starvation right after giving birth. The newborn wailed relentlessly, "Ngae! Ngae!" and the delivery room reeked of blood. Next, his own wife gave birth and lay there in the same state. The uncle sat hunched over, tears falling heavily from his sunken eyes, and the aunt sat beside him, legs stretched out, wailing and lamenting. Amidst this, the groans of the birthing mothers and the relentless, demonic cries (鬼哭聲) of the newborns could still be heard intermittently. And at the gate, the loan shark (2圓장변노리) stood menacingly like a messenger of death, demanding payment.
Suddenly, the wife sprang up, snatched the newborn, slammed it onto the yard ground, rushed into the kitchen, grabbed a kitchen knife, and ran outside. The newborn gave a gasp, its limbs trembled violently, and it stopped breathing. Red blood flowed from its crushed head. The wife, in one leap, reached the moneylender (高利貸) and stabbed him fiercely in the throat. The moneylender staggered, let out a terrible groan, and fell with a thud like a log. The wife, her hair unbound and wild, brandished the blood-stained knife and ran leaping down the path. Then, the sister-in-law also sprang up, picked up her baby, and threw it onto the yard. Grabbing the sickle stuck in the A-frame carrier, she dashed out in one stride, stabbed the bleeding loan shark's corpse fiercely in the belly several times, then brandished the sickle and followed her sister-in-law. Blood spurted into the air, splattering her face, making it a mask of gore. The uncle and aunt, eyes wide, seemed to follow them! The children had all fainted, eyes rolled back, left behind. In the front yard, the corpses of the two infants lay tragically, like featherless baby sparrows fallen dead—one child's neck broken, the other's intestines spilled. At the gate lay the terrifying corpse with the mustache (구레나루), eyes glaring, teeth clenched, pouring blood like a basin. From afar— <84>
"Crazy! Crazy!"
The shouts of the villagers could be heard. Sŏng-ho's mind went blank, and he collapsed unconscious on the spot... This was his terrifying fantasy.
"Ah! That's right! This world is ruled by demons (惡魔). So, to live, become a demon! Slaughter the demons—those who try to live well by making others miserable—with a demon greater than theirs! That's right! Kill! Die! Let the hungry ghosts (餓鬼) kill the hungry ghosts!"
He clenched both fists and stood up again. Gritting his teeth, he let out a cry, "Uak!" The feeling of revenge smoldered and flared. Night was falling quickly, and the sound of dogs barking from a distant village drifted faintly. He put strength into his legs and started walking again.
Suddenly, unexpected thunder rumbled, ururu, then lightning flashed here, flash! then there again, flash! illuminating everything brightly before plunging back into darkness, followed by another thunderclap, ururu—ttak—signaling an imminent downpour from that direction. Dark clouds like a demon's den (伏魔殿) covered the entire sky in an instant, and shortly after, hail-like raindrops began to fall, hudakdak! hudakdak, wa—and then heavy rain poured down. Regaining his senses, he waved his fists.
"That's right! Pour! It's a storm! Strike with lightning! Let the earth quake! Die! Kill!"
He shouted, leaping about like a madman, piercing through the darkness.
Storm! Darkness! Thunder and Lightning! U—wa—! Ururu! Ppŏntchŏk!

**Summary**

The story follows Sŏng-ho, an unemployed man recently returned from Tokyo, grappling with extreme poverty in rural Korea under Japanese rule. His family, including his wife, children, brother, uncle, and aunt, is starving, having run out of food months ago. Sŏng-ho feels immense pressure and shame as the head of the household but is powerless. He attempts to find hope through a potential job arranged by a friend in Seoul, only to be disappointed. He witnesses the similar struggles of another friend working a demeaning low-level government job. A visit to wealthy relatives for a small loan of rice results in humiliation and exposes a bitter class divide even within the family, pushing Sŏng-ho further into despair. The narrative culminates in Sŏng-ho experiencing intense rage, horrific visions of violence born from desperation, and a mental breakdown amidst a brewing storm, symbolizing the overwhelming social and economic pressures crushing the poor.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a raw depiction of the destitution faced by many Koreans during the colonial period. The economic hardship, lack of opportunity, feelings of powerlessness against both the colonial system (implied through the friend's bureaucratic struggles and fear of being labeled "rebellious") and the traditional social hierarchy (represented by the indifferent wealthy relatives) are starkly portrayed. Historians might find interest in the detailed descriptions of daily survival (gruel, foraging), the psychological impact of poverty (despair, anger, suicidal thoughts, breakdown of family harmony), the critique of class disparity even among Koreans, and the protagonist's struggle between traditional obligations and modern disillusionment, reflecting the complex social realities of 1920s colonial Korea. The mention of specific Japanese terms used in bureaucratic contexts (katagaki, shorui-bun) also reflects the linguistic landscape of the time.

잡지명	개벽 제59호
발행일	1925년 05월 01일
기사제목	가난한 사람들
필자	李箕永
기사형태	소설

가난한 사람들
						李箕永


성호(成浩)는 내일 아츰거리가 업는 것을 보고 집을 나섯다. 그러나 어대가서 돈이나 쌀을 어더오랴고 나선 것은 아니다. 그런 구걸은 할 데도 업고 또한 하기도 슬혓다.
작년 구월에 동경에서 나온 이후로 그는 아모 버리도 한 것 업스니 지금까지 먹고 산것도 물론 그의 주선으로 된 것은 아니엿다. 양식이 떠러지기는 벌서 슥달 전 일인데 이제껏 엇더케 살어왓는지! 가난한 사람은 허리띄가 양식이라 허리띄만 졸나매고 살앗는지 그는 식구들의 살아온 재조가 용하다고 생각하엿다.
그동안은 지금 생각해보면 하나도 되지 안을 일을 해보랴고 공연이 서울 가서 두류한 까닭에 집안 형편은 도모지 모르고 지낫섯다. 그래도 자긔는 훌늉한 호주(戶主)의 책임이 잇는 줄은 이저버리지 안엇다마는.
저녁에 조죽을 먹은 것이 생목이 올나서 속이 거북하다. 배가 곱파서 한그릇을 다 먹기는 먹엇지마는 무슨 약을 먹은 것가티 불쾌하엿다. 사람이 그걸 먹고 살다니? 하는 운수은 마음이 한편에서 실그머니 닐어나다가 그게나마 내일부터는 업다!하는 번개가티 머리를 치고 닐어나는 생각은 다시 그게라도 잇스면 조켓다 하엿다. 아! 그게라도 만히 잇스면 그놈을 팔아서 이밥을 사먹을 수가 잇지 안은가 하는 어림업시 어리석은 생각을 하고 그는 제풀에 허허 우섯다.
그러나 그는 암만해도 무엇이 섭섭한 게 잇섯다. 그 멀건 조죽에 아옥 건더기가 뻑뻑이 든 것을-뜨겁기는 경치게 뜨거운 걸 후-후-부러가며-먹던 생각을 하면 원체 아해들이 안 먹을라고 트적트적할 만도 하다하고 그걸 음식이라고 먹은 자긔의 주둥이를 지찟고 십헛다. ＜59＞
그러자 성호는 그젼에 잘 어더먹던 생각이 새록새록 난다. 일전에 누구의 집에서는 생선 조기국을 잘 먹고 또 누구의 집에서는 상추쌈을 잘 먹엇다는 것으로부터 엇던 여관에서 무엇무엇을 맛나게 먹엇다는 것이 차례로 생각난다. 그리고 그때 나문 입맛을 다시랴는드시 그는 허춤을 꿀떡 삼켯다.
그는 다시 자긔의 이런 누한 생각을 비우섯다. 가령 몃 때를 굴멋기로 몃 칠을 죽을 먹엇기로 이런 비루한 생각을 내게 하나? 사람이 이러케 약한 것인가? 자긔가 이러케 약한가? 하고 그는 무한히 애닯어하다가 그래도 그의 自尊心은 누구던지 이런 경우를 당하면 의례히 그럴 것이라고 結論을 짓고 말앗다.
성호는 이런 생각을 하랴거니 또는 하지 말랴거니 하며 한발 두발 거러나간 것이 어느듯 그의 가고저 하는 곳에 다달앗다. 그는 성호의 집에서 한 10里남짓한 이 고을 읍내이엿다. 첫 녀름 푸른 들에 부-연 보리이삭이 서늘한 저녁바람에 굼실굼실 물결을 치는데 蒼暗한 저문빗이 그 위로 가몰가몰 기여온다. 정거장 편으로 두어개 뎐등불이 그 속에서 빤작! 빤작!. 그것은 이런 시굴은 珍奇한 일이라고 저의 光明을 홀로 자랑하랴는 것처럼. 또는 무슨 孤獨의 悲哀를 늣기는 갓으로 울고 난 少女의 눈동자처럼 고요히 빗난다.
성호는 그길로 우편국에 가서 편지 한 장을 부첫다. 주머니에는 一金 13錢이 드럿는데 3錢으로 우표 한 장을 사부치고나니 남어지는 수판질할 수고도 들 것 업시 10錢이엿다. 이것이 그의 전재산! 아니 자긔집의 전재산이다. 그러타니 말이지 자긔에게나마 10錢이 잇섯지 왼집안 열식구 중에 다른 이들에게는 동전 한 푼도 업섯다. 그래 그의 집에서는 자긔가 제일 부자라 하엿다.
그 편지라는 것은 별 것이 아니라 그동안에 서울 가서 몃 달동안 운동하던 職業을-마치 천원 만원판가티 된다 된다하며 안되는-주선해주랴는 친구에게 그것을 마지막 재촉하는 편지엿다.
『꼭! 자네만 밋네. 안되면 여간 낭패가 안일세. 그러니 아모쪼록 극력 주선을 해주게. 아모게라도 밥버리만 되면 하겟네. 그러나 요전에 말하던 것을 힘써주게. 그러면 곳 긔별해 주게.』하는 의미로 중언부언 부탁하고 집안 형편의 난처한 사정을 드러서 아모쪼록 그 친구가 이 편지를 보고 동정하도록 그래 자긔 힘껏 주선해 주도록-이를 터이면 歎願도 갓고 陳情書도가튼 苟苟한 소리엿다. ＜60＞
성호는 편지에다 우표를 정녕히 부처서 포스트에 집어느코 저 편지가 오날 저녁에 올나가면 내일 아츰에는 드러가렷다. 그러면 모레나 글피 쯤은 답장을 보렷다. 아니 그 안에 올는지도 모르지 하며 만일 그때에.
『채용 승낙 즉 상경』이라는 電報가 오면 얼마나 반가울가? 아니 그러면 여비를 어듸가 꾸나? 그야 되기만 하면 설마 여비 업서 못 갈나구. 무얼 그까지껏 거러가지. 그러나 일이 안되면 엇저누? 또 이 편지가 안 드러가느니? 하는 어듸까지 窮한 생각과 쓸띄업는 염려까지 하면서 그는 우편국을 나섯다.
성호는 그길로 바로 집으로 도라가랴다가 식구들의 우는 상을 잠시라도 안보랴고 차저가도 『무엇을 달나러 오지나 안엇나?』하지는 안을 듯한 엇던 친구의 집을 차저갓다.
그는 어느 관청에 다니는 친구인데 젊은 내의가 단 둘이 비둘기 함쌍가티 고적하게 아니 호젓하게 셋방 한 간을 어더가지고 사는 청년이다. 그는 성호를 반가히 마저 드렷다.
마침 그의 부인은 촌에 잇는 큰집으로 제사지내려 갓다하며 자긔 혼자 집을 보는 참인데 그러지안어도 심심하던 차에 잘 왓다고 하는 바람에 성호는 방에 드러가기를 사양치 안엇다.
하기는 이 청년은 개화를 해서 그런지? 아니 자긔에게는 그의 부인을 내외를 식히지 안으랴는 친절인지도 모른다. 그 언제던가 성호는 그 부인이 잇슬 때에 한 번 드러가 본 적이 잇섯다. 그때 무에라고 처음보는 인사를 할 지 몰나서 잠깐 머뭇거리다가 좌우간 드러가서 저쪽의 태도를 보아가며 하랴하엿더니 그 부인은 마치 숫색시가티 부끄리며 고개를 다소곳하고 불안한 미소를 띄고 섯는데야 인사는커녕 처다볼 용긔도 업서서 그냥 털석 주저안젓다. 그래 불안하고 어리뻥뻥한 생각에서 그때 주인에게 이런 말을 間接으로 건넷다.
『밧그로 나갑시다. 안에서 불편하실 터이니...』
그러나 그 친구는 자긔 혼저 편안한 드시
『괜찬어. 잇스면 엇던가』
하고는 빙끗 우섯다. 그때 자긔는 속으로 생각하기를-사람과 사람이 마주 對하는 것이 엇지해서 이가티 불안을 늣기게 할가? 더구나 저 부인은 자긔의 친구의 부인이 안인가? 저 부인이 자긔는 이러케 부끄러워하지마는 만일 ＜61＞ 자긔만 업스면 그의 남편 압헤서는 아모거리낌 업시 웃고 이야기하렷다. 아니 빨개 벗고 그의 이불 속으로 쏙 드러 가렷다...하고 그때 자긔는 속으로 혼자 우섯다. 그리고 이러케 몰취미하고 덤덤한 짓으로. 서로 소 닭보듯하고 잇느니보다 만일 재미잇는 이야기를 간격 업시 할 수 잇다면 이 좌석이 얼마나 어울리고 사람사는 것 가트랴하야 예수독가비라는 金長老집 식구를 이것만은 부러워할 만하다 하엿다.
자긔는 그때 그 친구를 졸너서 밧그로 다리고 나간 뒤에는 다시는 그 부인이 방에 잇슬 때는 드러가지 안엇다. 지금도 그 부인이 방에 잇스면 밧게서 數語하다가 가랴하던 참이다.
그러나 자긔 부인의 얼골을 보이기도 이 친구 뿐이요 남편의 친구와 상면해 주는 부인도 이 부인 뿐이엿슴으로 자긔는 그들의 용긔를 감복하엿고 또한 그를 영광으로 생각하엿다. 참 말이 난 김에 말이다마는 그 부인은 결코 美人은 아니엿다. 죽은꽤가 약간 잇던가 업던가? 그때 잠깐 본 까닭에 지금은 그의 얼골 전양도 희미하다마는 엇더턴지 스물을 갓으로 넘은 녀자의 의례히 잇는 症인 처녀의 羞態를 띄고 두 뺨이 붉으레한 게 그저 平凡한 얼골이엿다. 하긴 젊은 녀자는 우연만하면 밉지안케 보이는 것이다. 그래 그 부인도 그러케 보엿다마는 그러나 그의 입은 確實히 컷다...하는 생각은 지금도 그의 입만은 똑똑이 긔억 난다.
『안에서 가티 제사참사를 가자고 합데다마는-』하고 主人은 위선 말끗을 끄내엿다. 이런 사람은 잠시라도 입을 담을면 심심해 견듸지 못하는 터임으로 이런 때 말벗이 생기면 여간 반갑지 안은 것이다.
『아! 먹을 것이 업서서 죽어가는 집에서 제사는 지내서 무엇하오. 제사도 정성으로 지내야지 그러치안으면 차라리 지내지 마는 것이 도로혀 조상의 魂靈을 소기지나 안케되지요. 靈魂이 무엇을 자시기나 하는게요! 공연히 무엇을 차려노코 헛것보고 꾸뻑거리는 것인데-』
하고 그는 허허 웃는데 그의 쭉 찌여진 큰 입이 귀밋까지 도라가며 아주 찌여지지나 안을가 겁이 난다. 그런데 눈은 그와 반대로 쪼고마케 쏙 드러가 박힌 것이 그의 큰 입과는 어울리지 안엇다. 언제보아도 우수은 사람이다마는 암커나 입 큰 이들끼리 잘덜 만낫다.
『암! 그러치.』 ＜62＞
하고 성호도 맛방망이를 치고 주저안젓다. 참 자긔도 제사를 지내본 지가 언제인지 지금은 제사날도 거의 이저버려간다. 그러나 그는 가난하다고 제사를 아니지낸 것은 아니엿다. 예수를 밋고서 제청과 혼백을 불사르기도 수년 전 일인데 지금은 예수도 불사르고 無信者 無産者 無識者 맨 「無」字로만 노는 판이다. 말까지 가난한 자긔는 언제던지 그의 말을 듯고 잇슬 적이 만헛다.

忽然! 누런 服裝이 門 압헤 나타나며 『편지왓소-』하고 봉함엽서 한 장을 떠러트리고 간다. 성호는 불안과 깃븜을 동시에 늣기며 아오가 집어다 주는 편지를 얼른 뜨더 보앗다. 그는 기다리던 서울 답장인데 謹詢日來의 兄軆萬旺이니하는 例套의 漢文式으로 중간에 「事不如意 未安奈何」라는 文句가 보기조케 職業은 틀녓다고 설명하엿다. 그는 힘업시 그 편지를 집어서 자리에 던지고 아모 말 업시 눈을 감엇다. 어듸서 왓느냐고 아오의 뭇는 말도 대답할 용긔가 업는드시...
아! 인제는 고만이다. 나의 배우고저 하든 학문은 영영 絶望이다! 絶望! 自殺! 苟且偸生! 家族! 放浪! 絶望! 自殺!?!』
그는 이런 생각을 되푸리 하엿다.
성호는 25圓의 借用証書를 써서 아오를 주며 傳하여 달나 부탁한 後 옷을 떼여입고 밧그로 나갓다. 읍내가는 길노 나가다가 三寸을 길거리에서 맛낫는데 어듸서 장변 2圓을 어더서 위선 쌀 한 말을 사온다고 둘너메고 온다. 그는 그 길로 읍내로 내려갓다.
가는 뜻인즉 엇던 관청에 다니는 친구를 차저보고 집안형편을 사정하야 무슨 월급자리 하나를 就職케 하여달나고 청하랴함이엿다.
그런데 그는 그 집을 채 못 다가서 다시 마음이 변하엿다. 그는 그런 자리가 마침 잇슬넌지도 모르고 잇다하더래도 20餘圓의 月雇이다. 그것 바더가지고 십여 식구가 과연 살 수 잇슬가? 그야 全目的이 이뿐이라하면 엇더케던지 家族을 連命케 할 수 잇슬 터이요 그 돈도 갑흘 수가 잇겟지마는 그러면 그것으로 자긔의 生命이 만족할가?하엿다. ＜83＞ 그의 復讐를 한다하면 상쾌할 것이 무엇인가? 아! 그는 卑劣한 復讐이다. 부자의 인색한 행위와 가튼 역시 인색한 復讐이다. 이런 구구한 復讐로 生命을 부지하랴는 것보다 차라이 壯快한 죽엄이 얼마나 價値잇슬가? 咀呪바든 生命은 죽엄이 幸福이다...
그는 이와가튼 생각을 다시 돌녀먹고 서울 엇던 同志를 차저가랴 하엿다. 한발 두발 것는 등 뒤에서는 아들의 우는 소리가 들니는 듯하다. 四寸의 우는 소리! 咀呪하고 악쓰는 소리가 들니고 그들의 꼴이 일일히 보이는 듯하다. 그러자 눈 압헤는 弟嫂가 어린애를 금방 나노코 굴머느러진 形容이 또 보인다. 갓난애는 응애! 응애 악착히 울고 해산방에는 비린내가 탁 터진다. 그 담에는 안해가 또 해산을 하고 그 모양으로 누은 것이 보인다. 三寸은 잔뜩 찌푸리고 안저서 헤멀거코 쑥 드러간 눈에서 눈물을 텀벙텀벙 흘니고 叔母는 그 엽헤서 두 다리를 쭉 뻣고 안저서 사설을 하여가며 우는 양이 보인다. 그 사이로 간간 呻吟하는 産母의 통성과 갓난 아해의 鬼哭聲가튼 악착한 우름소리가 여전히 들닌다. 그리고 門 압헤는 2圓장변노리가 使者가티 버틔고 서서 빗을 조르는 양이 보인다.
별안간 안해가 벌떡 닐어나더니 갓난애를 번적드러 마당에다 패며 부치고는 부억으로 우루루 드러가더니 식칼을 들고 밧그로 뛰어간다. 갓난애는 깩하더니 고만 사지를 바르르 떨며 숨이 끈어진다. 그의 으서진 머리에서 빨간 피가 흘너나린다. 안해는 한거름에 달녀드러 高利貸의 산멱을 푹! 찔넛다. 高利貸는 비척비척하더니 무서운 呻吟聲을 發하며 장나무 토막가티 쾅!하고 자뻐진다. 안해는 머리를 푸러 산발하고 피무든 식칼을 휘두르며 저편 길로 껑충껑충 뛰여간다. 그러자 弟嫂도 벌떡 닐어나더니 아해를 드러서 마당에다 며내친다. 그리고 지게에 꼬친 낫을 빼가지고 한다름에 내닷더니 목에서 선지피가 철철 흐르는 빗장의 송장의 배를 두어번 콱! 콱! 찍고는 낫을 휘두르며 또 동세를 쪼처간다. 피가 탁 튀여 공중으로 뿌려헤치는 바람에 그의 얼골은 피투성이가 되엿다. 三寸과 叔母는 눈을 흡뜨고 또 그덜을 쪼처가는 모양! 아해들은 모다 긔절하야 눈을 뒤여쓰고 뒤처젓는데 안마당의 두 嬰兒의 송장은 마치 털 안난 참새색기 떠러저 죽은 것가티 凄慘하게 한 아해는 목아지가 부러지고 한 아해는 창아리가 터젓다. 문 압헤는 구레나루 난 무서운 송장이 눈을 흘기고 이를 앙당그려 물고 피를 동의로 쏫고 누엇다. 저-긔서는 ＜84＞
『미첫다! 미첫다!』
하는 동리 사람덜의 고함치는 소리가 들닌다. 성호는 정신이 앗질하야 고만 그 자리에 昏倒하엿다...이것은 그의 무서운 幻想이엿다.
『아! 그러타! 이 세상은 惡魔가 사는 세상이다. 그래 살랴면 惡魔가 되여라! 저 잘 살랴고 남을 못살게 구는 놈들의 惡魔 以上의 惡魔를 처쭉여라! 그러타! 죽여라! 죽어라! 餓鬼가 餓鬼를 죽여라!』
하고 그는 두 주먹을 불끈 쥐며 다시 닐어섯다. 이를 악물고 「으악!」소리를 첫다. 復讐의 感情이 모락모락 타올랏다. 어느듯 밤은 어둑어둑한데 遠村에서 개짓는 소리가 은은히 들려온다. 그는 다리에다 힘을 주어 다시 거름을 내거럿다.
별안간 난데업는 천둥소리가 우루루하더니 번개불이 예서 번적! 다시 제서 번적! 눈 압히 환-하엿다가 도로 캄캄한 뒤에는 또 우루루-딱-하는 천둥소리는 미구에 저쪽에서 쏘내기가 몰려오랴는 것이다. 伏魔殿가튼 거먹 구름이 왼한울을 삽시간에 덥퍼오더니 족음 잇다가 우박가튼 비방울이 후닥닥! 후닥닥 와-하고는 큰비가 퍼붓는다. 그는 다시 精神을 차리여서 두 주먹을 휘두르며
『그러타! 퍼부어라! 暴風雨다! 벼락처라! 地震해라! 죽어라! 죽여라!』
웨치고는 狂者와가티 펄펄 뛰며 暗黑을 뚤코 나간다.
