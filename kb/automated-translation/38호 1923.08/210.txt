The Great Forest of the Cold Border Region, A Great Treasure House of the East
The Mountains and Rivers Remain, But Whose Treasure House Is It?
When speaking of the Treasure House of the East, probably everyone knows it refers to the great forests along the banks of the two great rivers, the Amnok (Yalu) and Tuman (Tumen), centered around Paektu-san (Mt. Paektu). Even when discussing the situation in P'yŏngbuk (North P'yŏngan Province), this forest is the most noteworthy feature, and similarly, when discussing the situation in Hamnam (South Hamgyŏng) and Hambuk (North Hamgyŏng), there seems to be little else worth mentioning besides this forest. As I have not yet surveyed the important areas, I cannot provide a detailed explanation, but having visited the Forestry Bureau (營林廠), the direct authority for forest management, and seen Sinŭiju, the central gathering place for timber, the materials to introduce the general outline are vividly clear.
The great forest near Paektu-san, the great timber flowing down the Tuman River, the great timber flowing down the Amnok River – this border forest, a world treasure house, inexhaustible for billions of years, makes one feel full just hearing about it, and even children and women drool. However, when asked if it belongs to the Korean people, one is suddenly choked up. The mountains are Korea's mountains, the rivers are Korea's rivers, and the forests are Korea's forests, yet when asked who manages them, who utilizes them, and who profits from them, one can only beat one's chest and tear at one's hair. It may be a treasure house, but what treasure is it for Koreans? A thousand beautiful trees are worth less than a foot of kindling (부드갱).

First, let's talk about the Forestry Bureau (營林廠)
The Forestry Bureau is a special Japanese government office managing the vast forest of over 2 million *chōbo* (町步) belonging to the Korean side in the basins of the Amnok and Tuman rivers, one of the largest forests in the East. Its predecessor was established as the Military Lumber Office (軍用木材敝) in Meiji 38 (1905) after taking over the Russian operations as a result of the Russo-Japanese War. Subsequently, separating from the Chinese side, in Meiji 40 (1907), the Sino-Japanese joint venture, the Yalu River Lumber Company <92> (covering only the forests on the Chinese bank), was established on the Chinese side. On the Korean side, the Forestry Bureau of the Residency-General (統監府營林敞) and the Northwest Forestry Bureau (西北營林敞) were announced under the government system. Following the Japan-Korea annexation (日鮮合倂) in Meiji 43 (1910), it was promulgated under the government system as the Forestry Bureau of the Government-General of Chosŏn (朝鮮總督府營林敞), which continues to this day. What is the Area Under its Jurisdiction?
The forest area under the Forestry Bureau's jurisdiction spans three provinces: Hamgyŏng Nambukto (North and South Hamgyŏng Provinces) and P'yŏngan Pukto (North P'yŏngan Province). Within this, the area designated as reserve forest reaches approximately 2.2 million *chōbo* (Amnok River: 1.75 million *chōbo*, Tuman River: 450,000 *chōbo*), truly exceeding half the total area of Japan's national forests. Among this, the total area of mature coniferous forest land is said to exceed 1 million *chōbo*. And,

The Main Tree Species
are cold-zone types, with coniferous trees occupying the majority. The main types important for utilization are:
One. Red Pine (Korean Five-Needle Pine - 朝鮮5葉松): Diameters of 3 *ch'ŏk* (feet) or more are common. The trunk is straight, the color is lustrous, and it shrinks and expands little, making it easy to work with. It is widely used for various construction materials, furniture materials, railway sleepers, etc.
Two. Fir/Spruce (杉松): The quality is inferior compared to Red Pine, but it is also used for construction, furniture, box materials, packaging, telephone pole materials, matchsticks, and paper manufacturing.
Three. Larch (落葉松): With dense annual rings, strong and durable material quality, it is suitable for construction, bridges, ships, sleepers, piles, utility poles, and coffin materials.
Four. Hinoki Cypress (亦栢松): Its material quality is so beautiful that it has been called the king among coniferous trees since ancient times. Suitable for fine crafts such as tea shelves, desk boxes, braziers, tea utensils, canes, chopsticks, combs, etc.
One. Birch/Ash (檀木): Also strong in material quality, suitable for vehicle wheels, floor posts, canes, and measuring instruments.
Others are mostly broad-leaved trees (潤葉樹), suitable for miscellaneous uses, but logging has not yet begun. And,

Regarding Forest Condition and Stock
The majority of the mature forest land consists of natural forests in good condition. Large specimens of Fir/Spruce, Red Pine, and Larch mostly reach a breast-height diameter of 3 *ch'ŏk* 4-5 *ch'on* (inches) and a height of over 20 *kan* (fathoms). The Tuman River basin is mostly Red Pine, while the Amnok River banks are mostly Fir/Spruce and Larch. The total stock is said to be several hundred million *ch'ŏkche* (尺締 - a timber volume unit), but the exact number is unknown for now. The Fir/Spruce in the Amnok River basin alone, famous as a raw material for paper manufacturing, is said to amount to no less than 250 million *ch'ŏkche*. (1 *ch'ŏkche* is 1 *ch'ŏk* square (square foot) and 2 *kan* [12 *ch'ŏk*] long).

Afforestation Instead of Logging
No matter how abundantly the forest is stocked, if there is only logging and no afforestation, it is a fact that it will be depleted in just a few years. Therefore, the Forestry Bureau is in the process of carrying out afforestation on devastated land, non-wooded land, and logged areas. According to one bureau official, the current accumulated forest stock alone can be harvested for no less than 200 years, and with some afforestation going forward, there will be no shortage of timber for billions of years to come. This is because trees become usable timber after 100 years or more of afforestation. The total volume of timber logged and processed from Meiji 40 (1907) to Taishō 10 (1921) is reported as:
Amnok River Basin (direct management and others): 6,128,142 *ch'ŏkche*. Tuman River Basin (direct management and others): 467,767 *ch'ŏkche*. Total: 6,595,909 *ch'ŏkche*. (Breakdown by tree species omitted).

How is Timber Transported?
Regardless of whether it's government-run or private, timber transport requires complex procedures. After entering the mountain forest land and felling the trees, branches are removed or bark is stripped. Then, using manpower or ox/horse carts, the logs are gathered at a suitable place where the terrain levels out and the mountain ends, bringing them to the raft assembly site (編筏場). The most convenient time for transport is from November each year to March of the following year, utilizing the frozen season. During the summer thaw, rafts are assembled (編筏) and floated down the river. The famous Amnok River Raft Flow (鴨綠江筏流) and the Raftsmen's Song (筏節歌) refer to exactly this. The Amnok River Raft Flow on June 9th each year is truly a world spectacle. Then,

What Happens to the Floated Raft Logs?
There is a log storage area (貯木所) at Pukha-dong, about 1 *ri* upstream from Sinŭiju on the lower Amnok River. Logs are stored here. There is also a log storage yard within the premises of the Sinŭiju Lumber Mill (新義州 製材所). Logs are placed in this storage yard and either processed into lumber or sold as raw logs. From Meiji 40 (1907) to Taishō 10 (1921), the stored log volume in the Amnok River basin is reported as 3,846,738 *ch'ŏkche*. And the lumber processed just at the Sinŭiju Lumber Mill up to Taishō 10 (1921) is 1,715,891 *ch'ŏkche*. <94>

What are the Timber Sales Figures?
Up to Taishō 10 (1921), logs sold as raw timber amounted to 1,163,912 *ch'ŏkche*, valued at 5,120,712 Won. Lumber processed and sold amounted to 2,714,730 *ch'ŏkche*, valued at 16,330,590 Won. The total is 21,451,312 Won. And,

is reported as 5,010,740 *ch'ŏkche* in the Amnok River basin and 101,529 *ch'ŏkche* in the Tuman River basin.

What is the Relationship Between the Forestry Bureau and Koreans?
Isn't it like giving away the pear and begging for the core? It's enough to make you choke up, ha ha, you can only laugh bitterly. In the severe cold and snow of winter, wearing *kuri* shoes (꾸리 신발) and ragged clothes, shouldering an axe (독기), sharpening a sickle (낫), stumbling (덥더지며) and falling (잡빠지며), rolling and crawling on the ice and snow, entering deep mountains and valleys where human traces cease, following the sounds of tigers and seeking the footprints of bears, clutching empty stomachs and tightening belts, blowing on fingertips and toes, they chop down trees, trim branches, strip bark (껍줄을 볏기고), and then pull, drag, push, and carry. After pouring their blood and sweat into the timber, they barely receive a few pennies (멧푼) in wages for their hardship (艱辛히) getting it down the mountain. Then, in the scorching heat of May and June, they assemble the timber into rafts (編筏) and set out on the thousand-*ri* long river carrying food for the dead (使者밥), hearing ghostly cries, fighting and fighting against swift currents and fierce waves for a week up to a month, transport the timber, and barely earn a few pennies each. Even getting that much means one has the fate to pass the state exam (科擧); one wrong move and you end up buried in a fish's belly (魚腹에 葬事). Oh, how dreadful! The raftsmen (筏夫) - even then, it's difficult for Koreans to get their turn, and most are Japanese. And even if one gets permission from the Forestry Bureau to log freely, in the end, one has to sell it to the Forestry Bureau. The taxes are high and expenses are many, so it is said that 10 out of 10, or even more, end up losing money. That's why there isn't a single raft made with Korean capital and labor on the upper or lower Amnok River, they say. When I asked a raftsman, he said they transport logs from Kosan-chin (高山鎭) to Ch'angju-gu (昌洲口) for 8 *chŏn* per *ch'ŏkche*. One raft holds a total of 800 *ch'ŏkche*.
So, two raftsmen would earn 64 Won. When asked how many days the round trip takes, he replied that if the water flow is good (조코) and there are no obstacles, it takes two weeks. Otherwise, it can take over a month, or two months, and if not, you just become a soul in the water. He deeply lamented his fate of being a raftsman. <95>

**Summary**

The article details the immense timber wealth of the forests along the Amnok and Tuman rivers near Paektu-san, describing it as a "Treasure House of the East." However, it starkly contrasts this natural wealth with the reality of Japanese colonial control, exercised through the Forestry Bureau (Yŏngnimch'ang), which manages logging, transportation via river rafts (a dangerous process highlighted by the description of raftsmen's work), processing in places like Sinŭiju, and sales. The author expresses deep bitterness that Koreans, despite the forest being theirs, only provide the grueling and perilous labor for meager wages, while the Japanese authorities and companies reap the profits. The piece laments the complete lack of Korean-owned logging operations due to prohibitive costs and the necessity of selling to the Japanese bureau. For historians, this article offers a valuable contemporary Korean perspective on Japanese resource extraction during the colonial period. It vividly portrays the economic exploitation, the dangerous working conditions forced upon Korean laborers (specifically raftsmen), and the palpable sense of loss and resentment over the control of Korea's natural resources by the colonizers. The mention of specific practices, locations, figures (like timber volumes and prices), and the description of the Forestry Bureau's operations provide concrete details about the colonial economy.

採伐의 代에 造林
森林이 如何히 豊富하게 蓄積되야 슬지라도 採伐만 하고 造林이 업스면 不過 幾年에 盡할 것은 事實이다. 故로 營林廠은 廢荒地 未立木地 採伐地에 造林을 施하는 中만에 在하다. 엇던 廠員의 言에 依하면 現在 積蓄林만 해도 無慮 200年은 採用할 터 인바 今後 造林을 若干만 하면 億萬年 來來 無窮토록 材木의 不足은 無하겟다 한다. 造林後 100年以上이면 能히 材木用이 되닛가- 그리고 明治 40年으로 大正10年까지의 伐木造材材積의 計가
