布哇의 夏
						布哇生
布哇羣島 (Pukwa Kundo) are sometimes called the "Sandwich" Islands (산도위잇치 羣島). They extend from 19 degrees North latitude to 22 degrees North latitude, and from 156 degrees West longitude to 166 degrees West longitude. Judging by their location, they are entirely within the tropics. Therefore, people who have not actually visited Hawaii (布哇) might easily think that Hawaii is very hot, and especially in summer, it would be unbearably hot for people. However, because the ocean surrounds it on all four sides, constantly moderating the climate, there is little severe heat, and as it is also a region of much rain, there is hardly a day when the rain stops. The cool breeze blowing gently from the Pacific coast naturally refreshes the mind and body, making one wonder even in May if it is perhaps the clear autumn. The white snow piled high on Mauna Kea (만나키야山) (altitude 13,808 feet, Hawaii's highest mountain) makes one forget the scorching heat of the hottest summer days just by looking at it. The coral reefs scattered like sparse stars and leftover Go stones (踈星殘碁) provide natural bathing spots everywhere, and the Mauna Loa (모로아) Volcano, with an altitude of over 13,760 feet, is from time to time a paradise on earth. Hot springs are abundant, and trees are dense, making it suitable for escaping the heat and taking walks. Honolulu (호노루루), located on the southern coast of Oahu Island (오아푸島), is the capital of the Hawaiian Islands (布哇羣島) and has a climate most suitable for health. The average temperature is 22 degrees Celsius in winter and 25 degrees Celsius in summer. In the summer season, noon reaches 86 to 85 degrees Fahrenheit, and nighttime is around 70 degrees Fahrenheit. This place is bustling and its scenery is exceptionally beautiful. Those sailing the vast ocean, after struggling with huge waves and rough seas for ten or several tens of days, rejoice when they make a port call here, just like finding an oasis <50> in the middle of a desert. There is not a speck of dust in the city streets, the roads are extremely flat, and the buildings are grand. Facilities such as electric lights, telephones, and electric trams are fully equipped, not to mention various other civilized institutions. For a person sightseeing in Hawaii, missing Honolulu would be like a person sightseeing in China missing Yueyang and Dongting (岳陽洞庭). Honolulu is good in all four seasons, but summer is especially better. Among its places, "Ten Acre Square" (덴에카스꽈크) is lush with greenery and fragrant with myriad flowers, attracting an endless stream of visitors. On holidays and other public holidays, many people gather, some playing music, some dancing. Among them are beauties with skin like snow, faces like flowers, wasp waists, and blonde hair, gentlemen in high collars and short jackets, laborers, the rich, the poor, the old, and the young. There are the natives, who lost the homeland passed down from their ancestors for thousands of years, were driven from place to place, live a wandering life, and sing the sad songs of a ruined nation; Americans, who boastfully stick out their chests with their big noses, showing off their money as if they own the world; Chinese, who, as long as they are paid well, completely disregard any insult or suffering and only do labor; Japanese, who, since the Pacific Conference, strut around dragging their geta sandals, pretending their rather short stature is tall, saying they too are people from a nation occupying a position among the world powers; and Koreans (朝鮮人), who left their homeland thousands of miles away by land and sea, are helpless and friendless, earning meager wages in farms or factories, living in poverty, yet shedding tears of sorrow and indignation for their homeland – people of all shapes and colors are present. Honolulu is truly a city of summer. Who would not praise the stylishness and cleanness when looking at the Territorial Building (地方廳), Queen's Hospital (皇后病院), and the statue commemorating Kamehameha (카메하메하) (the unifier of the Hawaiian Islands)? Who would not envy the sight of young and old, men and women, in light summer attire, leisurely strolling under the occasionally scattered mango (망고) trees, waving palm-leaf fans (草扇)? Climbing Pali Mountain (파리山) and Tantalus Mountain (탠틀라스山) to gaze upon the distant waters and nearby mountains at a glance is also a grand and exhilarating experience; washing away the dust and heat that trouble everyone at Waikiki (와이키키), the world-famous bathing spot, is also an entertainment. The sight of Kilauea (길나위아) Volcano, the world's second most active volcano with a crater three miles deep and wide, emitting hazy smoke and steam, creating a scene of darkness on all four sides, and the spectacle of the lava lake (燃湖) located north of this crater, which is obscured by black clouds stretching endlessly during the day and becomes a sea of fire at night, is also one of the greatest wonders. In our Korea (朝鮮) too, during the summer, refreshing fruits not seen before, namely pineapple (파이나풀 - 鳳梨) and banana (빠나나), become popular. Hawaii is truly the origin place of these fruits. They are available throughout the four seasons, but they are especially abundant and consumed a lot in summer. Besides these fruits, Hawaii also produces many papayas (파이이아페아 - 梨). <51> Since sugar is also a specialty product, many iced desserts (氷菓子) and other refreshing items are manufactured and supplied for drinks. Summer in Hawaii is truly different from what one might think. Although its name suggests the tropics, it is cooler than temperate Korea. Among our compatriots (同胞), those who have the means should consider visiting at least once, perhaps to escape the summer heat. <52>

**Summary**

This 1923 article describes summer in Hawaii ("布哇"). Despite being in the tropics, the author, "布哇生" (Hawaii Resident), emphasizes that Hawaii's climate is moderated by the ocean and rain, making it surprisingly cool, even cooler than temperate Korea. The article highlights the natural beauty (Mauna Kea's snow, Mauna Loa, coral reefs, lush vegetation), the modern and clean capital city of Honolulu (호노루루) with its amenities and attractions like Kapiolani Park(?), Pali, Tantalus, and Waikiki beach. It notes Hawaii's famous fruits like pineapple and banana, and the Kilauea volcano. A significant portion describes the diverse population of Honolulu, including Hawaiians mourning their lost nation, boastful Americans, hardworking Chinese, proud Japanese, and notably, Koreans (朝鮮人) living in poverty but feeling patriotic sorrow for their colonized homeland. The article concludes by recommending Hawaii as a summer retreat for affluent Koreans.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its portrayal of the multi-ethnic society in Hawaii in the early 1920s through a Korean lens. The specific description of the Korean immigrant community – poor, working in manual labor, yet filled with "sorrow and indignation" (悲憤慷慨) for their homeland under Japanese rule – offers a valuable glimpse into the early Korean diaspora's experience and nationalist sentiments during the colonial period. The juxtaposition of Koreans with other groups (Americans, Chinese, Japanese, Native Hawaiians) and the distinct characterizations provide insight into contemporary ethnic perceptions and hierarchies.
