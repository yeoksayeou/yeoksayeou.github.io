발행일	1923년 11월 01일
기사제목	셰 번이나 본 共進會 (The Co-prosperity Exhibition I Saw Three Times)
필자	想涉 (Sangsŏp)
기사형태	세태비평 (Critique of the Times)

셰 번이나 본 共進會
						想涉

"Where is there anything worth seeing! To see this kind of thing, I came having spent money like water!"
"Shh, shh"
This is the hushed exchange between gentlemen wearing *kat* (traditional hats) with colorful group badges pinned to their chests, right in front of the exit of the Exhibition Hall (陳列舘) from which thousands of spectators were pouring out. It seems plausible for one to heave a deep sigh (長太息), saying they came to see such a thing with money they didn't have, and it is also a pitiable and thankful aspect of the times (世態) that the other widens his eyes and hushes him, stopping his words. For someone whose stomach aches even after being robbed of just the 20 *chŏn* entrance fee, there's nothing more to say, but it seems only natural to regret having engaged in useless bowl-farming (사발농사, i.e., futile effort) when, having pulled dozens of *wŏn* for travel expenses from a meager purse (囊橐) and scattered it at the railway bureau, inns, or restaurants, one turns back crestfallenly (悄然히), lugging a bundle of old clothes. In Seoul, there is a popular phrase, "Co-prosperity Exhibition bundle," which carries a certain sense of contempt. However, before scoffing at the "Co-prosperity Exhibition bundle," when I picture in my mind the desolate figures of those people turning back, I feel apologetic and sorry. What more if, among them, there were people forced to come, eating mustard while weeping (i.e., doing something unwillingly)? I don't know about financial matters, but after suffering from instability and hardship for over a year amid cries of "Money panic! Money panic! (錢慌)," it seems the earthquake disaster (震災) in Tokyo also had some effect, and perhaps more than the actual blow to the financial world, there was needless public agitation or panic (恐慌). Creating an opportunity to draw in hundreds of thousands of people from the provinces at such a time might be one way (一方途) to invigorate the city and towns (市町), but perhaps among them were circumstances of even greater hardship (困乏). It would be a mistake for me, a completely ignorant layman (門外漢) regarding such financial or market conditions, to repeat myself (重言復言), so I'll stop, but seeing the bewildered faces of the country gentlemen shuffling along,<61> following behind a large banner (?) on the street, I felt a strange kind of irony. Wouldn't it be more urgent and effective to first create the brains capable of viewing the Co-prosperity Exhibition before showing them the Side-Business Co-prosperity Exhibition (副業共進會)? That thought also occurred to me.

Though it was only about time to put on layered autumn clothes, the weather was extremely gloomy, as if snow might pour down any moment.
As I was still lying under the covers, half-asleep (非夢似夢間), with my eyes dimly closed, the roaring sound of a propeller buzzed loudly near my ears. It seemed as if An Ch'ang-nam had arrived with four or five planes. The children were shouting "Airplane! Airplane!" as if they had met the world. Not just the children, but young and old alike rushed out noisily onto the edge of the porch and down into the yard. When asked what day it was, I was told it was October 5th, the opening day of the Side-Business Co-prosperity Exhibition. The intention was to extend the Co-prosperity Exhibition mood even into the sky first. Indeed, the entire city was immersed in the Co-prosperity Exhibition atmosphere. It was true that throwing the stone called the Co-prosperity Exhibition into the flow of the monotonous lives of Koreans—who lack popular or social annual events (年中行事), recreational facilities (오락기관), or amusements (遊興物)—caused ripples, small waves spreading out in circles. Although the stone wasn't that heavy or large, because life was originally so dry and tasteless (乾燥無味), those small ripples slid far and lingered long. Out on the streets, the number of men and women in new clothes noticeably increased, the trams were packed, and the Six Ministries Avenue (六曹大路) seemed to greet the sun and moon, bustling with people pouring in and out. Curious about what it was like, I let myself be pushed by the crowd towards Kwanghwamun Gate, and what strikingly caught my eye was the triple gate covered with straw mats (돗자리), befitting the name Side-Business Co-prosperity Exhibition. This gate has been a source of much trouble until now. Although the public naturally insists on preserving it, rumors say the authorities consider it a nuisance because even demolishing it costs a lot. Perhaps there's no rule against it being disposed of unexpectedly easily through auction (競賣). However, I don't know about such things. Just stating honestly the random thought (偶感) that popped into my head: if Mr. Yanagi Sōetsu of Japan had come and seen it, wouldn't he have perhaps clung to the remains (殘骸) wrapped in straw matting and wept aloud (放聲痛哭)? However,<62> regarding the hosting of the Co-prosperity Exhibition, a new record was set when the Haet'ae (mythical sea-lion statues), which had faithfully guarded the palace day and night without fail (闕하는 일업시) for nearly fifty years while gazing at Chamtubong (蚕頭峰, likely a peak on Namsan), were served eviction orders due to the Co-prosperity Exhibition craze. But there's no helping it. It's something that can't be helped. The Co-prosperity Exhibition fattened the belly of the Kyŏngsŏng Electric Company. Thanks to that, the sound of tram wheels could be heard closer even in Pukch'on. And thanks to that, the Haet'ae vanished overnight. Disappeared forever. As living standards improve and new houses are built, dusty old antiques (骨蕫品) can only be tossed onto dusty shelves or into moldy closets. But still, I miss the pair of stone beasts more than the tasteless, winding single line of rail. As they say, "You don't notice what you have until it's gone." When they were there, they didn't catch the eye much, but now that they're gone, I still feel sad. Now, they merely leave their solitary shadows (孤影) on the blue wrappers of the Monopoly Bureau's (專賣局) products, but even that will soon be swept away by the vanishing smoke and won't remain even in our memories... Where are they sitting now, sniffling? Our companion of nearly fifty years, Haet'ae!
For twenty days, Seoul's Changan (長安, capital city) melted into the Co-prosperity Exhibition mood. Wherever one went, the Co-prosperity Exhibition mood drifted. On the main streets (巨里), at gatherings (集會), in inns (旅舍), homes, inner rooms (內室), outer rooms (舍廊)—everywhere one went, it was the Co-prosperity Exhibition mood; everything one heard was talk of the Co-prosperity Exhibition.
"Hey, how many times have you been?"
"I've been three times."
"I've been five times."
Going many times and going early was a source of pride.
"What did you see?"
"I saw the airplane!"
"Was it flying?"
"It was just sitting there."
Of course, it would just be sitting there. And aviation, in our agriculture-based nation (農本國)—no, in any nation—is surely not a primary occupation (正業); it must be a side-business (副業). But what's most regrettable is that the first thing to catch the eyes of the 400,000 spectators amounted to nothing more than a single airplane, at best.
"What did you see after that?"
"I saw people."<63>
"Were they different from the people you see on the street?"
"They were different. Since they had no worries about food or clothes, they looked different, wandering around carefree and full of excitement."
"That's fortunate. That too must be thanks to the side-business. But weren't they drunk on the liquor, the side-business of an agriculture-based nation?"
"Who knows! But there were certainly many pubs. Restaurants, udon shops... even standing bars. Plus, it was teeming with kisaeng (기생). They shoot arrows, jump on seesaws, dance, and probably sing too, but anyway, for 20 *chŏn*, it wasn't too expensive."
"So, did you only spend 20 *chŏn*?"
"Nonsense! Would you sightsee without eating lunch? And after eating lunch, unless you're a complete boor (벽창오), could you resist having a drink?"
"So, how much did you spend in total?"
"About three *wŏn* just disappeared each time. And that was being frugal."
"Then you spent nine *wŏn* in three visits, and this person spent fifteen *wŏn* in five visits."
"Seems about right."
"So, was that all there was to see?"
"Well... Oh, right, dried croaker, dried pollack... and wooden clogs (딸국나막신), blue-nosed shoes (청혜)... what else was there?... Oh, that thingamajig was there too!"
"What do you mean?"
"Wooden shoes, leather shoes... what else was there!"
This person tilts his head, trying hard to recall what he eagerly saw.
"Are you saying you only saw footwear?"
"Ah, right, there were hats too. Official's hats (紗帽), *kat*, silk hats too!"
"Now you're moving upwards, aren't you."
"What else was there?"
"There were thermos flasks (마호병), walking sticks (단장), and bags too."
"Now you've dropped down a bit. Hahaha. Are all those things side-businesses of Koreans?"
"Are you mocking me?"
"Hahaha..."

I intended to go see it at least once, sometime. If possible, I wanted to go on the very last day. But I wasn't determined to go. Being someone who tends to laugh first before getting angry when picked on, I <64> am the type to just laugh even when seeing others make a fuss and carry on senselessly. But I went three times. The first time, of course, I went to see the exhibits (陳列品), but from the second time onwards, thinking, "I must be a crazy guy with nothing better to do too!", I still went three times—to see the airplane, see the kisaeng, go to the pubs, and people-watch. Not a single penny (分) came out of my own pocket for the entrance fee, but somehow, the sum of sixty *chŏn* came out of someone's pocket.
The first time, I was dragged along by a friend from the countryside. Whenever you go to see something that's been loudly hyped up and has a glittering exterior (外華), you are usually disappointed. The Co-prosperity Exhibition, which had stirred up 400,000 people from the countryside and Seoul, was no exception. What I saw was no more than what various friends had seen during that time. Being pushed from behind and poked in the ribs, getting swept in and pushed out without effort, was perhaps the only gain among it all. Still, I went a second time. But that was due to a friend's persuasion that one could see beautiful women and have a drink (一杯酒) for free, in other words, without paying the amusement tax (遊興稅). And the third time was because I received a request to write this manuscript.
It was the last day, when the twenty-day Co-prosperity Exhibition mood would come to an end. Whether it was a precursor (先通) to the coming three winter months, or an attempt to silently spread a bundle of early winter chill beneath Samgak Mountain, it was an afternoon when a chilly wind, absent yesterday, relentlessly rustled near the foundation stones of Kyŏnghoeru Pavilion (慶會樓), then swept up handfuls of the remaining excitement of the Co-prosperity Exhibition, and vanished tracelessly and silently, escaping through Kŏnch'unmun and Yŏngch'umun gates. On the dull (魯鈍한), weary faces of people tired from drunken revelry (취흥), only laughter seemed to prevail. But I didn't know what that signified. Whenever the costume parade (가장행렬), performing the ugliest displays (醜態), staggered past, dragged along by drunkenness (醉氣), the surging crowd greeted and sent them off with cheers. Watching for a while, I turned to Mr. R and Mr. H standing beside me and remarked,
"It's frightening that humans bear the duty to laugh even if it means resorting to this!" Both gentlemen chuckled wryly. But perhaps everything appeared dark to my eyes, wearing yellow glasses at the time. If there's anything wrong with this writing of mine, perhaps it's not my fault but the fault of my glasses.<65> Lastly, one thing I cannot omit: as I passed by the row of Korean restaurants (朝鮮음식점) behind Kyŏnghoeru, a gentleman in formal attire (禮服禮帽) with a white flower pinned on, holding a bowl of loach soup (鰍湯) inside a standing bar, seemed flustered (갈팡질팡하다가), perhaps afraid of being spotted by others, and hastily ran out, disappearing somewhere—a desolate rear view. Seeing that gentleman, I thought,
"It's very chilly, isn't it? You must be hungry," and offered a word of greeting, but when I came to my senses, it was just a voice from within my own stomach.
However, esteemed 400,000 spectators! I indeed visited three times, but due to my ignorance, all three visits were meaningless like this. Yet, that doesn't mean the Co-prosperity Exhibition itself was meaningless, nor does it mean that because I viewed it meaninglessly, you too must have inevitably viewed it meaninglessly. I know that you, wise sirs, will understand (諒察하실). Rather, I too declare myself one who believes that the promotion (獎勵) of side-businesses and the revitalization (振興) and development of general livelihoods is the fundamental principle (根本義) for the economic self-rescue (自救) of our people.
...(October 25th entry)... <66>

**Summary**

The author, Sangsŏp, provides a critical and satirical account of the 1923 Side-Business Co-prosperity Exhibition (副業共進會) held in Seoul. He recounts his three visits, initially prompted by curiosity and friends, later by an obligation to write about it. He observes the superficiality of the event, the forced enthusiasm, the financial burden on visitors (especially those from the countryside), and the prevalence of mere entertainment (food stalls, kisaeng, drinking) over meaningful exhibits promoting actual side-businesses. He laments the displacement of cultural heritage, specifically the Haet'ae statues near Kwanghwamun, removed to facilitate tram lines likely expanded for the exhibition, seeing it as a symbol of loss amidst imposed modernization. For historians, this article offers a valuable contemporary critique of a colonial-era public spectacle, revealing skepticism towards state-promoted economic initiatives, highlighting the perceived exploitation of the populace, the tension between modernization and cultural preservation, and the socioeconomic realities faced by Koreans under Japanese rule.

「쉬쉬, 쉬쉬」
수천의 군중이 쏘다저 나오는 陳列舘 출구 압헤서 가슴에 울긋붉읏한 團體徽章을 붓친 갓 쓴 분네의 숙은숙은하는 酬酌이다. 업는 돈에 이까짓 것을 보랴 왓드란 말이냐고 長太息을 하는 것도 그럴듯한 일이어니와 두 눈을 뚱그러케 뜨고 쉬쉬하며 말을 막는 것도 가업슨 일이요, 고마운 世態다. 입장료 二十錢만 빼앗기고도 배를 알는 사람으로 보면 더 말할 것도 업지만 빈약한 囊橐에서 數十圓의 旅費를 끄러내어서 鐵道局에나 旅館에나 음식점에 뿌려버리고 헌 옷 보때리를 질머지고 悄然히 도라슬제 쓸데업는 사발농사만 지엇다고 후회하는 것은 당연한 일일 것 갓다. 서울에는 소위 共進會ㅅ보따리라는 일종의 輕蔑의 뜻을 품은 유행어가 잇다. 하지만은 나는 共進會ㅅ보따리라고 흉을 보기 전에 그네들의 돌아가는 쓸쓸한 뒤ㅅ모양을 머리ㅅ속에 그려볼 제 미안하고 가엽슨 생각이 난다. 더구나 그들 가운데에는 울며 겨자 먹기로 하는 수 업시 끌려 나선 사람도 잇다 할디경이면 어떠할까. 財界의 일은 나는 모르지만 錢慌錢慌하며 일년 이상이나 불안과 곤경에서 허덕어리든 차에 東京의 震災로도 얼마쯤은 영향을 바든 모양이요 또 財界의 실제의 타격보다도 공연히 인심의 동요나 恐慌이 업지 안은 모양이다. 이러한 때에 수십만의 지방인민을 끄러 올릴 기회를 만든 것은 어떠한 의미로는 市町의 활기를 도읍는 一方途가 될지도 모르지만 그 중에는 한층 더 困乏한 사정이 잇섯슬지도 모를 것이다. 이러한 財界라든지 市井의 사정에 대하야 공연히 暗昧한 門外漢인 나로서 重言復言하는 것이 실수이기로 고만두지만 길거리에서 大*을 압헤 세우고 비쓸비쓸하며＜61＞ 주레주레 따라가는 촌 서방님네의 어리둥절한 얼굴을 볼제 나는 일종의 이상한 「아이로니」를 늣기엇다. 副業共進會를 그들에게 보여주기 전에 共進會를 叅觀할만한 두뇌부터 만드러 주는 것이 더 급하고 더 큰 효과가 잇지나 안이할까? 하는 생각도 낫다.

인제야 막 겹옷을 입을 때쯤 밧게 안이 되엇건만 몹시 음산하고 눈이라도 퍼 부을듯한 일기이엇다.
