A Story of How I Also Had the Glory of Sightseeing
						Yu Kwangnyŏl

The Chosŏn Subsidiary Business Joint Exhibition (朝鮮副業共進會)—chaired by Ariyoshi, the Government-General's Director of Political Affairs (政務總監), who holds the rank of Vice Governor-General, with Yi Wan-yong-kun? No, His Excellency Yi Wan-yong as vice-chairman, hosted by the Chosŏn Agricultural Association (朝鮮農會), and mobilized with the full power of the Government-General (總督府) down to the county governors and village heads—whizzed by in the blink of an eye over twenty days. Now, the sight of country gentlemen (yangban) with topknots, traditional hats (kat), straw sandals, and flintlock guns has become rare on the main streets. Let me tell a little story of how I, mixed among them, had the glory of sightseeing.

What Kind of Joint Exhibition Was It?

Joint Exhibition! Joint Exhibition! What kind of joint exhibition was it? A troublemaking friend came to me, who was lying down sick of the world, and pestered me endlessly, saying,
“Hey, you should see something like this at least once.”
“My friend, what is there to see? I’m already lying down, cutting ties with the many upsetting and eye-sore affairs of this world. What refreshing sight would I see if I went?”
“Still, you should go see it. Just as ‘Good and evil are both my teachers’ (善惡이 皆吾師), it’s not irrelevant to witness upsetting things once in a while…”
He urged me so strongly that I reluctantly followed him.

Pretending to Yield is Best!

The autumn sky was covered with gloomy clouds, frowning heavily, while every streetcar heading towards Hwangt'ohyŏn was packed with gentlemen wearing *kat*. Looking towards Kwanghwamun from Hwangt'ohyŏn, it seemed as if white awnings had been set up to block the sun (白木遮日), with white-clad people swarming like ants.
“My goodness, what are they hoping to get, coming in such numbers?”
My companion, K-gun, replied,
“Hey man! Why wouldn’t they come to a sightseeing event where travel expenses are paid? If they didn’t come, they’d face scrutiny from certain quarters… Pretending to yield is the best way.”

“Probably a Japanese Gentleman, I suppose” <67>

Arriving in front of Kwanghwamun, it was several layers better than the Military Rule (武斷政治). Perhaps it was a scheme by the Government-General, advertising the Cultural Rule (文化政治) like a magician’s trick telling people to stay still. The ancient palace gate, with its old colors, was decorated gaudily with Kanghwa flower-patterned mats (花紋席), and country gentlemen lined up like students on a school trip. After managing to buy three tickets with difficulty and entering the gate, delivery men from the *Maeil Sinbo* (每日申報), the Government-General's mouthpiece, were dressed in official robes (冠) and calmly distributing extras about the Joint Exhibition, which was neither urgent nor desired. Seeing this, a country gentleman remarked,
“Oh my, that gentleman is wearing Japanese clothes and an official robe. He must be a Japanese gentleman, I suppose—”

The Seven Million Yen Skyscraper

Entering the gate, the grounds of Kyŏngbokkung Palace, always desolate to behold, remained desolate even when welcoming tens of thousands of white-clad people. The Government-General building (總督府廳舍), built over several years at a total construction cost of seven million yen, was nearly complete, with the scaffolding removed—truly a skyscraper (摩天閣) of Chosŏn. It was fortunate that the construction cost alone was seven million yen; if the land and materials had been purchased entirely, it would likely have cost over ten million yen. Did the Government-General authorities who built this intend to construct the finest government office in the East? Do they think that having the best government office in the East amounts to anything when seventeen million people are wandering homeless, unable to survive?
When elementary school children cannot be educated, what does this seven million yen skyscraper intend to give to the Korean people? The country folk, unaware of the inner workings, gaped and exclaimed,
“My goodness, what a magnificent building!”
Ah, pitiful compatriots! Please know that your blood and sweat flow within this building.

Korean Women Like Impurities in White Rice

Entering the Second Reference Hall following the route, the confectionery making and belt weaving were all run by Japanese management. Across from that, the textile section of the central experimental station was also unrelated to Korean businesses. Shall we call the woman weaving cotton with a foot-treadle machine amidst this, an impurity (뉘) in white rice?
Next, what I saw was mostly the same. On one side, chemically applied manufactured glass vessels sparkled, while on the other, a barefooted Korean woman with a pale face slowly wove hemp cloth (멱사리)—a truly pitiful sight.
Next to the Ch'ŏrwŏn silk weaving, the splendid flower-patterned mats of the Chosŏn Industrial Trading Company (朝鮮産業貿易會社) (Japanese-managed) shone brightly. Next to the Anju sheer silk (亢羅) weaving, Western sock machines clattered away. I went towards a dense bamboo grove, only to find a Japanese-style bamboo house with tatami mats built next to it. <68>

The Sound of Machines Killing People

From this corner, *ttokttak*—from that corner, *dŏlgŏk*—on one side, an old man with a topknot and *tanggeon* hat leisurely makes ash trays, while on the other side, the noise of Japanese-managed machinery roars.
Help! Save me! This machine sound is killing the Korean people. For us, who lack the sound of machines in our hands, each time this sound rings out, it feels as if our blood dries up and our flesh is scraped away. However, as the saying goes, "ignorance is brave" (無智는 勇敢), the country gentlemen are mesmerized by this loud noise, their eyes glazed over. It seems they don’t even know the purpose for which they came to see this spectacle. Some just smile blankly, pleased—he-heh. But since this place shows things actually being done, even without explanation, they might at least grasp ‘that’s just how it’s done.’ But what about the main hall?

Most Exhibits by Japanese

Entering the main hall, there were hundreds of varieties of fish products. However, anyone who carefully looked at the exhibitors would realize that there were almost no exhibits by Koreans. With the Chosŏn peninsula surrounded by sea on three sides, nearly all fishing rights are in Japanese hands, so this is hardly surprising.
Among the colorful fruits, one might hope to find exhibits by Koreans. Even in sericulture, said to be the most promising industry in Chosŏn, the majority of exhibits were Japanese. Next to a few straw mats (*satjari*), a few straw sacks (*mŏngsŏk*), and a few pairs of straw sandals (*chipsin*) laid out, the splendid equipment of Japanese products glittered.

Japanese Korean Speech That No One Can Understand

In the First Reference Hall, since they were demonstrating things practically, even the farmers who saw and understood could no longer comprehend things starting from the main hall. The item names were written in Chinese characters (Hanja), and the explanations were in Japanese. That figures! Under the Government-General’s rule, where the forty-odd thousand Japanese are the focus, not the seventeen million Koreans, asking for explanations in Korean is like asking a bandit (盜蹠) to be virtuous. Still, perhaps out of some leftover scrap of conscience, there were Korean explanations next to the Japanese ones. However, that Korean was Japanese-style Korean, not real Korean. Could the country gentlemen possibly decipher such high-level Korean? Since they earn translation fees for such Korean, how about cutting the Korean language promotion budget or translation fees from the already penniless Government-General's budget next year?

Improved Ondol That Will Kill Farmers

Leaving the main hall, people were crowded around something. I went to see what it was, and it was the Improved Ondol (改良溫突) designed by Kyŏnggi Province. Regarding this design, the author <69> once confronted an official from Kyŏnggi Province. "If you light the fire and completely seal the furnace door, gas could leak into the room through the imperfect ondol floors in Korean rural homes, which would be truly dangerous—if all Korean ondol floors were perfect, implementing this might be acceptable, but doing so with cracks in the floor would pose a major health hazard," I had argued. Even the designers tilted their heads and considered this point after hearing my words.

Even the Wise Slip Once in a Thousand Thoughts

As the saying goes, ‘Among a thousand chickens, there is one phoenix,’ and ‘Even the wise slip once in a thousand thoughts’ (智者千慮 必有一失). Those folks, who annoyingly wrote explanations in Japanese, strangely wrote the forestry product explanations primarily in Korean, occasionally adding Japanese. Isn't this a truly bizarre event? And I saw many products made in prisons. Thinking of the circumstances of those who made them while eating prison fare (콩밥 - kongbap, lit. beans and rice), these subsidiary business exhibits didn't feel very heartwarming. This Second Reference Hall was almost entirely filled with so-called mainland (Japanese) products. A few bolts of cotton cloth laid out next to their splendid machine-woven fabrics! Is this a joint exhibition of royalty and beggars? A joint exhibition of master and servant?

The Prostrate, Smiling Japanese Woman

Walking further, I saw a model of the three-thousand-*ri* land of Chosŏn, with flags of the Chosŏn Mail Steamship Company (朝鮮郵船會社) stuck all over like stars, indicating various sea routes. Above it, a Japanese woman from the Edo period (江戶時代) lay prostrate, spinning round and round, and the boats below her also moved back and forth accordingly. Seeing this, one farmer exclaimed,
“Wow, they made that woman so well. Just like a fairy maiden (仙女)!”
Pure-hearted compatriot! “Seeing how the Japanese have seized the shipping routes along every port and inlet (津津浦浦) of this land handed down by our ancestors, if there were a Korean fairy maiden, she would surely weep. Fortunately, it’s a Japanese fairy maiden, so perhaps it’s all well… but what do you mean by ‘made so well’? Please, regain your senses. No matter how breathless we are…”

These are Chosŏn Local Products

Leaving the Second Reference Hall and entering the Cotton Pavilion, the mountains of broadcloth piled up were all Japanese products. As for Korean products, there were only the products of the Kyŏngsŏng Spinning Company (京城紡織會社) and a few bolts of homespun cotton from the countryside in one corner. What was even more laughable was that during the Korean Products Promotion Movement (物産獎勵運動) among Koreans last Lunar New Year, the slogan was “Koreans, Use Korean Goods” (조선사람 조선 것). Yet, on Japanese-made broadcloth, they had printed slogans in Korean like “Koreans, let’s use only Korean goods,” “These are Chosŏn local products (조선토산),” and “These are our goods.” This allows a glimpse into how meticulous they are with commercial psychology (商學心理). Then, after watching the free motion picture showing silk production conditions in Japan, India, <70> and China, a heavy feeling pressed down on my entire body. The despair that competition with them is utterly impossible deepened further.

What Use Are Empty Dreams?

Entering the Sericulture Pavilion, at the entrance, on the Fugoku-maru exhibit from the Imperial Gift Production Center (恩賜授産場), a banner proclaimed “Japan’s Two Great Pillars of Trade,” stating “Annual Raw Silk Exports: 600 Million Yen” and “Annual Cotton Imports: 400 Million Yen.” Though it says annual cotton imports are 400 million yen, in reality, that cotton all becomes woven cloth, continuously draining our blood year after year. The total value of raw silk exported to the United States is 600 million yen annually, and they say Chosŏn is also suitable land for sericulture. If only 100 million yen worth of Korean raw silk could be put onto the foreign market annually, life might become a bit easier, but what use are empty theories (空論)! In sericulture, the products of the Chosŏn Silk Reeling Company (朝鮮製絲會社) seem to be the only exhibit from the Korean side. Apart from that, the silkworm egg advertisements featuring gaudily dressed Japanese female mannequins were spectacular.

Purely Our Exhibits!

Next, I visited the Ch'ungnam Ramie Fabric Special Exhibition Hall. Among the many exhibits there, this place alone featured products made purely by the hands of our people. What if the machinery were improved and the authorities actively provided subsidies to encourage it—will this voice reach their ears?

The Terrifying Tōtaku Special Pavilion

The Oriental Development Company (東拓 - Tōtaku) Special Pavilion—more terrifying than snakes and scorpions, intending to roll up the land of Chosŏn like a mat, chase Koreans out to the fields of Manchuria or Siberia, and bring in Japanese immigrants—featured a display titled "Rural Villages Past and Present" (農村의 今昔). It contrasted the former Korean Empire era with the period after the Government-General's rule, boasting that all aspects of rural life had been renewed. They conveniently omitted mentioning that farmers, who used to settle down quite well before, are being driven out year after year thanks to the Government-General and Tōtaku. I want to ask them, who climbed into the mountains upon seeing America's black ships (墨船 - kurofune), whether their present development occurred because the Government-General existed.

The Only Benefit of the Joint Exhibition

Besides this, at the display of weights and measures (度量衡), meddlesome friends were busy measuring their heights. There were countless special pavilions for various shops and companies, but since they were unrelated to the lives of Koreans, there was no need to make an effort to see them. Turning around back, I saw Chosŏn's special product, cattle (牛). Yellow-furred, plump cows stood blinking their small eyes. Thanks to this once-in-a-millennium Joint Exhibition, even these cows were dressed better than people. The decorations <71> around their necks glittered, and the blankets draped over them were colorful. It seems Mr. Cow should take charge of praising the Subsidiary Business Joint Exhibition, but in reality, perhaps displeased at leaving the countryside where they roamed freely to stand in one place all day, they frequently mooed mournfully. After briefly viewing examples of poultry and pig farming behind them, I went to the pond by Kyŏnghoeru Pavilion, where innocent students were busy rowing boats, as if proclaiming, "This world is ours."

Since When Did You Acquire a Taste for Udon?

Most of the stalls inside the venue were also Japanese. The Korean stalls tucked away here and there had hardly any business. For example, the Korean beef soup (*chang'gukbap*) stalls were sparsely visited, while the Japanese *udon* stalls were bustling with white-clad people. According to restaurant owners, even people who came up from the countryside would enter a Korean beef soup restaurant and ask for *udon*.
"Compatriots!" Since when did you develop such a taste for *udon*?

For Whom Was This Joint Exhibition Held?

After finishing the tour, what did I see? What did I gain? Firstly, whose joint exhibition was this? Was it a Japanese joint exhibition? Or a Korean joint exhibition? The fact that the exhibits from the seventeen million Koreans living in Chosŏn were inferior to those from the forty-odd thousand Japanese residents—isn't this living proof, no matter how the authorities try to excuse it, that they encourage and develop the commerce of the minority Japanese more than the industry of the majority Koreans? Secondly, for whom? For which people was this Joint Exhibition held? If it was truly a joint exhibition for the development of Korean subsidiary businesses, as the authorities claim, why couldn't they hire adequate explainers to inform the farmers, who came all the way after spending considerable travel expenses, in a way they could understand? Couldn't they at least have written the explanations in Korean for people to read? What is a Japanese explanation reflected in the eyes of a village farmer, if not a painting (丹靑) to a blind man? Fundamentally, for whom, and for whose benefit, were those Japanese explanations written? I ask again, for whom was this Joint Exhibition held?

The Effect of Spending Three Million Yen

According to the authorities' explanation of the results of this strange Joint Exhibition, the number of exhibitors was 9,724, the number of exhibit items was 11,821, and the number of visitors over twenty days was 400,000.—So far, so good. But the amount spent by rural farmers due to this event was over two million yen. And their impression? "Like a blind man viewing fine paintings (소경 단청구경이라)." As the proverb goes, "Keep your wits about you even when dragged away by a tiger." No matter how loudly the authorities boast and promote, what words will they use to respond to this final conclusion? <72>

**Summary**

The author, under the pen name "Yu Kwangnyŏl," provides a scathing critique of the 1923 Chosŏn Subsidiary Business Joint Exhibition held in Kyŏngbokkung Palace under the Japanese Government-General. Presented as a personal account of a reluctant visit, the article argues the exhibition overwhelmingly showcased Japanese businesses and products, marginalizing Korean contributions despite being held in Korea for Koreans. The author highlights the vast expense of the new Government-General building contrasted with Korean poverty, the displacement of traditional Korean crafts by Japanese machinery, the language barrier preventing Korean farmers from understanding exhibits, and the promotion of entities like the Oriental Development Company (Tōtaku) which facilitated Japanese colonization of Korean land. The author concludes the exhibition, despite attracting large crowds (many seemingly coerced or incentivized to attend), was primarily a propaganda piece for Japanese colonial achievements and economic dominance, offering little real benefit or insight to the Korean populace while costing them significantly.

From a historical perspective, this article is valuable for its firsthand, critical Korean perspective on a colonial event often portrayed positively in official narratives. It vividly illustrates the economic disparities, cultural friction, and underlying resentment felt by Koreans under Japanese rule. The specific details about Japanese dominance in various sectors (fishing, textiles, trade), the critique of colonial institutions like the Tōtaku, the mention of the Korean Products Promotion Movement, and the observation of cultural shifts (like preferring *udon*) offer concrete insights into the socio-economic realities and nascent nationalism of 1920s colonial Korea. Historians can use this to understand the lived experience of colonial subjects and the methods of critique employed against the colonial regime.

누구를 위하야 연 共進會?

구경을 다하고 나니 본 바가 무엇인가. 어든 바가 무엇인가. 첫재 이 共進會가 엇던 사람의 共進會인가. 日本人의 共進會인가. 朝鮮人의 共進會인가. 一千七百萬이 사는 朝鮮사람의 출품이 四十餘萬이 사는 日本人의 출품만 못한 것은 당국자가 아모리 변명할지라도 다수인 朝鮮人의 산업보다 소수인 日本人의 상업을 더- 獎勵하고 더 發達식힌 活證據가 아닌가. 둘재 누구를? 엇던 사람들을 위하야 연 共進會인가. 당국자가 말함과 가치 진실로 朝鮮人의 부업발달을 위한 共進會라면 웨- 상당한 설명원을 새웨 不少한 路費를 쓰고 못처럼 올나온 농민이 아라듯도록 일너주지 못하엿든가. 적어도 朝鮮文으로 설명을 써서 읽어보게나 못하엿든가. 향촌농부의 눈에 빗취는 日文說明이 소경의 눈의 丹靑이 아니고 무엇이랴. 대체 그 日文說明은 누구를 본위로, 누구를 위하야 써 논 것이냐? 更問하노니 누구를 위하야 연 共進會인가?

三百萬圓 소비의 효과

이 괴상한 共進會의 성적을 당국자의 설명대로 말하면 출품인원이 九千七百二十四人이요. 출품점수가 一萬一千八百二十一점이요. 二十日동안 입장자가 四十萬이요.-여긔까지는 조흐나 지방농민이 이로 인한 소비금액이 二百餘萬圓이요. 그들의 감상은 曰 『소경 단청구경이라.』 속담에 호랑이에게 물려가도 정신을 차린단 말과 가치 아모리 떠들고 선전을 잘하는 당국도 이 말단의 결론에는 무슨 말로 代辯하려는가?＜72＞
