Publication Date: 1923년 11월 01일
Article Title: 돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이지!! (Money! Is Your Name Money?!)
Author: P. S. L 生
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

Money! Is Your Name Money?!
						P. S. L 生
Money! Is your name Money?
Just hearing your name makes children jump about, laborers hold out their hands, desk workers' eyes widen, and seniors glance sideways.

Money! Is your name Money?
Paper money, gold coins, silver coins, Baekdong coins (백동전 - cupronickel coins), copper money—your forms are truly numerous. Within one country or across various nations, you are truly diverse and varied.
Despite having such various forms, how is it that your nature is so precisely the same?
In England, in France, in Germany, in Japan, in China, and in Korea, whether you appear as paper, as gold or silver, or as copper, how is it that your nature is so exactly the same? If only things like the encouragement of domestic products (물산장려), the People's University (민립대학), large factories, and cottage industries could become reality as instantly as your nature is consistent!

Money! Is your name Money?
No matter how I look, you have no eyes, yet how do you see so well? Do you only see and follow into the safes of grand, splendid houses, into the silk vests (비단좃기) and crocodile wallets (악어지갑), but leave cotton vests (무명좃기) and worn cloth bags (헌겁가방) before even entering?
You have no nose, yet how do you smell so well? Do you escape from hands smeared with oil grime or hands soiled with dung fertilizer before even being <11> grasped, only to circulate among hands fragrant like a well-combed head of hair?
You have no ears, yet how do you hear so well? Do you hide your brave self deep within a chest even before hearing the footsteps of a thief—a thief coming to steal you?
You have no feet, yet how do you travel so well? Within a few hours, from Korea to Japan, to England; from Japan to Germany, from Germany to America—you walk so well!
You have no hands, yet how do you grasp so well? Do you hold hundreds of millions, tens of millions of people captive in factories, bind them in farms, and seat them fixedly at desks? You have no eyes, no nose, no ears, no feet, no hands, so how could you have a spirit? Yet, how is it that you possess the power to suppress politics, ignore morals, destroy ethics, and even oppress sacred religion?

Money! Is your name Money?
Before you, kings bow, generals collapse, aristocrats kneel, and all people submit. What are you that you are so great? You make all people and all nations fight and also ally, make them cry and also laugh. You make all families and all peoples peaceful and also disheartened, make them rise and jump and also sit and weep.

Money! Is your name Money?
To obtain you, almost all people cast away everything—material goods, noble thoughts, esteemed family lineage, precious bodies, immaculate flesh, and even life, which cannot be had twice.

Money! Is your name Money? <12>
Ah—is your name Money? If only one has you in hand, there is rice and meat, clothes—silk, cotton, calico—many friends, and many beautiful women. If only one possesses you, one can kill people—many people—and also save them. They say where the spirit reaches, gold and stone crumble, but where money (gold and diamonds - 황금과 금각석) reaches, never mind the spirit, even the vessel containing the spirit—the body—is crushed.

Money! Is your name Money?
Money, when were you born, and what path have you trod to reach your present state? Because I lacked the leisure to think of you, I have left your true identity (正體) unexamined. But, but, the more I think of you, the farther away from you I become. So, I wanted to know you, who are gradually moving farther away. Just like a wife contemplating divorce needs to examine her husband more closely—though it's useless—perhaps it might serve as a reference when choosing a new husband. No—in truth, it's the difficult sentiment of truly abandoning my dear, cherished husband forever; perhaps it might work, attaching all sorts of flaws—flaws never seen or heard—to his honey-like words, his Jesus-like conduct, his grape-like flesh, and everything else, in an attempt to obliterate the departing husband from my concern—though ultimately unable to obliterate him.

Money! Is your name Money?
Were you in this world before humans appeared? Or were you born at the same time as humans?
As soon as humans appeared, they lived on natural fruits and the roots and stems (根莖) of plants. This is the so-called primitive era. Thus, each person obtained their own food with their own hands. Therefore, there was nothing to exchange for you. Nature was freely available to all of them without any price (代價). At that time, there was no need for you to exist. Afterwards, as time gradually passed, the human population increased, and natural fruits and plant roots and stems became scarce. So people began catching beasts in the mountains and fish in the sea to eat. At this time too, each person obtained <13> their own food with their own hands and lived. This is the so-called hunting and fishing era (어렵시대). At this time too, there was no need for you to exist. Isn't it clear even now when looking at households living in remote mountain valleys that have little need to use you, money, and within a single family, you are not used?
While humans caught and ate beasts and fish, the population also increased greatly, and on the other hand, since the acquisition (獲得) of beasts and fish was uncertain, they naturally learned to domesticate and raise animals. Consequently, due to the need to survive the winter (過冬), they began cultivating grains to supply food for the animals. This cultivation of animal feed indeed became the ancestor of our later agriculture. This time is known as the pastoral era (목축시대).
At this time, no matter how much people scattered, they couldn't keep up with the proliferation of sheep (羊, sheep are said to be the first livestock, perhaps because they are docile and gentle), and by raising a large number of sheep, a surplus naturally occurred. Accordingly, a surplus of food gave people surplus time. Thus, they began weaving simple cloth (쳔), making simple tools, and starting grain cultivation.
However, due to gender relations and regional factors, these tasks naturally formed a division of labor. At the same time, the social structure of that time consisted of scattered large-family villages (大家族的 촌락) united by kinship (혈속관계). Within these large families, things were still communal (공산). However, between villages, there was no communal economy. It goes without saying that many such villages arose as the population increased, and between these villages, a system of mutual exchange (유무상통) for surplus goods naturally developed. However, it was still a direct exchange of goods for goods, like sheep for rice, rice for cloth, cloth for sheep, and so on. This is called barter (物物交換).

Money! Is your name Money?
You were conceived at this time. It was time for you to be born. And so, you were first born as a four-legged beast called sheep (羊). Then, in some countries, you were born as shells (貝), and in some regions, as rice plants (稻). That <14> evolved (進化) into iron, copper, silver, gold, and paper.
You are a historical product. You are an economic historical product. What inconveniences there were in the exchange of goods for goods! Firstly, the difference in the goods desired by both parties; secondly, the difference in the quantities usable by both parties; thirdly, the difference in the timing of exchange desired by both parties; fourthly, the inconvenience of the difference in easily exchangeable locations for both parties. Therefore, some medium was required. Direct exchange was truly difficult. Now, look at all kinds of goods. What item could eliminate these four inconveniences? Firstly, it must satisfy everyone's desires; secondly, it must be divisible into any large or small quantity; thirdly, it must be preservable for any length of time; and fourthly, it must be exchangeable anywhere. If such an item existed, it would be truly ideal (리상뎍) as a medium for barter. However, such an item was not discovered at that time. At that time, only sheep could satisfy everyone's desires. Therefore, despite enduring the other three inconveniences, you were born as sheep. Of course, this was because exchange was infrequent and its scope was narrow (혐소한), but it was your inherent destiny from birth that your appearance would change along with the progress of productive forces and the development of the division of labor.

Money! Is your name Money?
You were not only destined for your appearance to change, but you were also born with an even greater destiny besides that. That destiny, contrasted with your great power, is truly a fate too pitiful to speak of. Ah—Money, do you truly know one of the destinies you were born with? It is your death. You were already born with the destiny that you must inevitably die. You possess the fate that you will eventually, inevitably disappear from this world. Thinking this way, your situation is also pathetic. But this is inevitable. You will disappear not because of our society's hopes or hatred, but because of the destiny you yourself were born with.

Money! Is your name Money? <15>
You know how you came into being, don't you? You inevitably arose from the economic process called commodity exchange. There is a prerequisite for exchanging goods. That is, exchange happens only after an individual, or a family combining several individuals, possesses their own exclusive property.
If this prerequisite disappears, wouldn't you, Money, also disappear?

Money! Is your name Money?
Money, shortly after you were born as sheep, you came to acquire your faithful servants. Before them, even though you mediated commodity exchange, you were merely one of the goods in various exchanges, and your form still directly satisfied people's desires. Thus, both parties in the exchange were always the direct producers of the goods.
However, the new servants you acquired cannot survive unless you feed them. Since they always make your appearance beautiful and elevate your status (디위) higher than heaven each time, perhaps it's not considered wasteful.
Your servants are merchants, traders whose purpose is the exchange of goods, responding to each person's needs in terms of material goods, location, time, and quantity—moving surplus goods from A (甲) to B (乙), B's surplus goods to C (丙); surplus goods from place A (甲地) to place B (乙地), place B's surplus goods to place C (丙地), place C's surplus goods to place A (甲地); this month's surplus goods to the next month, this year's surplus goods to the next year; gathering small surpluses for large demands, distributing large surpluses for small demands. These people also emerged right after you were born. However, these people were not originally created in your form. Just as you would later change your appearance, these people also transformed from being ordinary people among others.

Money! Is your name Money? <16>
At this time, you had to change your appearance. Now, it became acceptable for you not to directly satisfy human desires. Your servants would handle such tasks. You only needed to transform into a substance that eliminates the four inconveniences of commodity exchange—material, quantity, time, and place. You have undergone change upon change to become so. Today, don't you fulfill your duties adequately with just a piece of paper?

Money! Is your name Money?
Money, born as sheep, you were not suitable for the demands of your servants. Your servants had already changed your appearance. And so, from the beginning, you were changed into metals. This was because, besides being able to satisfy human desires like you, metals also eliminated the inconveniences of quantity, time, and place. However, even then, your face was not as subtle as it is today. But you, having changed your appearance to metal, fulfilled your duties considerably well even in your rudimentary form. Once you change your appearance, your servants also increase in number. Thus, economic activities, which were merely exceptions (례외) when there was a surplus but no easy way to exchange it, become the norm (상사) due to your emergence and that of your servants. People began to produce many goods they would not use directly. Accordingly, the increase in production and the development of the division of labor progressed even further. Thus, money, merchants, division of labor, and production became mutually cause and effect, leading to unprecedented economic development.

Money! Is your name Money?
Even while changing like this, your great power has always been demonstrated.
Firstly, you broke down the large-family villages united by kinship based on blood ties (혈족뎍 관계). Secondly, you destroyed the so-called feudal system (봉건제도). And you also disrupted the order of production relations.
Moreover, entering the modern era, with the rise of large-scale industry due to the development of machinery (긔게), you wield even greater power. And so <17> you further disrupt the industrial world (문장이 중도에 끊김 - Sentence breaks off mid-way - National Institute of Korean History)

Money! Is your name Money?
But do you know your destiny?
The world's production system has undergone unprecedented development since your appearance. However, each time you widely exert your power, you hasten your own destiny. The reason is as follows. Since the time you began circulating widely in the production system, people have mostly engaged only in work they found interesting and were skilled at, producing goods beyond their own needs. Thus, they have lived by exchange. This naturally became the starting point where one could not control one's own production. In other words, the person who makes the goods does not know the future path of those goods. Therefore, if everyone produces goods for sale limitlessly and without restriction, at some point production will exceed demand, leading to an explosion. Thus, the industrial world temporarily comes to a standstill (뎡톄). Factories close, and shop doors shut. This is the so-called economic crisis (經濟恐慌). In modern times, such crises have become increasingly larger in scope and more frequent. Indeed, we experienced this twice, after the Russo-Japanese War and after the recent World War. There's no need to look far back into the past.

Money! Is your name Money?
At times like these, you become even harder to find, wherever you may be. How could you, so clever and so powerful, not know this principle (리치)? Have you perhaps prudently fled somewhere? Or hidden? Right, fearing the thief who doesn't pay after using more than the thief who steals, you must have hidden yourself away in a safe.

Money! Is your name Money?
Think again about the motive for your birth. Think about the premise for your birth. You presuppose individual property <18>. Thus, your motive was the exchange of goods between individuals.
Go look at a home where individual property is not distinguished. Among those people, there is nothing to exchange. No—go look at the society of prisoners in jail. Where is exchange there? Truly, because there is no exchange, one never gets to see your face in prisoner society.

Money! Is your name Money?
Do you know the cause of the economic crises arising from the disorder of production?
They say production is excessive. Do you think that sounds true? Doesn't "excessive production" mean that there are leftovers after everyone has used and eaten? Isn't it strange then, considering the meaning of "excessive production," that during a crisis, people's struggle for livelihood becomes even more severe?
However, the phrase "excessive production" today means that the supply of goods is excessive because there are few demanders who possess you, Money. It does not mean that the general populace has such an abundance of means of living that goods are leftover.

Money! Is your name Money? You should know this. Know this principle (리치). The factories and machines (긔게) that produce are all the property of a few people. The goods produced are also the property of the owners of those factories and machines. Therefore, even if production increases and goods are plentiful, they are not given away for free. To anyone. Therefore, explosions occur sporadically. Isn't this proof that as long as a few people own the means of production, that is, the so-called capital (자본), productive forces cannot develop beyond this point?

Money! Is your name Money?
Do you know the purpose of the act of production? The act of production aims at the unlimited increase of production. Therefore, from primitive times <19> to the present day, everything that hinders the increase of production has been shattered. This was not done by humans; humans merely served as soldiers (병뎡). And you, Money, truly stood above humans and acted as an officer (장교).

Money! Is your name Money?
You must know what hinders the increase of production. Let's shatter it. You be the officer. We are the soldiers.

Money! Is your name Money?
We will surely win. Haven't you seen this happen with your own eyes in this battle? Thinking that your traces will thus disappear makes me very sad, but in a place without goods to exchange, we cannot invite you anyway.

Money! Is your name Money?
You should rather die in battle. Die at the vanguard, struck by a cannon. We will gather all your dead bodies, arrange them nicely side-by-side in a museum for viewing, and we and our descendants, for myriad generations to come, will praise your meritorious deeds!

Money! Is your name Money!! (Night of September 27th) <20>

**Summary**

The article personifies money, addressing it directly. It marvels at money's paradoxical nature (formless yet powerful, eyeless yet seeing, etc.) and its pervasive influence over all aspects of life, including politics, morality, and human relationships. The author traces the historical evolution of money from barter through various forms (sheep, shells, metals, paper) driven by economic necessity and the division of labor. Critically, the text argues that money, born from private property and exchange, fuels capitalism and leads to inevitable economic crises (공황). The author, adopting a socialist perspective, identifies private ownership of the means of production (자본) as the root cause of these crises and the ultimate barrier to further productive development. The article concludes by predicting the eventual demise of money as society evolves beyond private property and exchange, urging money to "die heroically" in the struggle for a new economic order, promising its "exploits" will be remembered.

From a colonial history perspective, this article is significant for several reasons. Written in 1923 under Japanese rule, it reflects the influx of socialist and critical economic thought among Korean intellectuals grappling with the rapid, often disruptive, changes brought by colonial capitalism. The critique of money's power and the analysis of economic crises (mentioning the post-Russo-Japanese War and post-WWI downturns, which impacted Korea) offer insight into contemporary economic anxieties and intellectual responses. The call for the abolition of money and private property aligns with radical critiques of the existing colonial and capitalist order, suggesting a desire for a fundamentally different societal structure. Historians might find the blend of historical materialism, moral critique, and revolutionary anticipation particularly revealing of the intellectual currents within the nationalist and burgeoning socialist movements of the era.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이지!!
						P. S. L 生
돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
네 일홈만 들어도 어린이들은 뛰놀고 막일(勞働)하는 이들은 손 내밀고 책상일군들은 속눈뜨고 선배님들은 겻눈질을 흘깃한다.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
조희 돈 금전 은전 백동전 구리돈 네 형태는 참으로 여러 가지다. 한 나라안에서나 각 나라를 통하야서나 참으로 다종다양하다.
그가티 여러 가지 형태임에 불구하고 네 셩질은 엇지도 그가티 곡 가튼냐.
영국에서도 불란서에서도 독일에서도 일본에서도 즁국에서도 조선에서도, 조희로 나타나던지 금은으로 나타나던지 구리로 나타나던지 네 셩질은 엇지도 그리 꼭 갓흐냐. 이리 셩질이 네의 그것과 가티 꼭 갓흘 것 갓흐면 물산장려도 민립대학도 대공장도 가내 수공업도 당장에 되련마는!

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 아모리 보아도 눈이 업건만은 엇지도 그리 잘 보느냐. 고대광실 화려 찬란한 집 금고만, 릉라주의 비단좃기 악어지갑만 보고 다라드러가고 무명좃기 헌겁가방에는 드러도 가 보기 전에 나와버리나뇨.
너는 코가 업건만은 엇지도 그리 내임새를 잘맛노. 기름검댁이 발닌 손 똥거름 발닌 손에는 쥐워도＜11＞ 보기 전에 버서나서 향기가 모록모록 나는 빗질갓흔 손에만 도라단니나뇨.
너는 귀가 업건만은 엇지도 그리 잘 듯노. 도적- 너를 채랴오는 도적의 발자최 소래도 듯기 전부터 그 용자를 깁흔 장 속에 숨기냐뇨.
너는 발이 업건만은 엇지도 그리 잘 단니냐뇨. 몃 시간 내에 조선에서 일본으로 영국으로, 일본에서 독일로 독일에서 미국으로, 잘도 것는구나.
너는 손이 업건만은 엇지도 그리 잘 붓잡노. 멧 억 멧 천만의 사람을 공장에 붓잡아놋코 농장에서 붓잡아 매고 책상 우에 붓잡아 안치나뇨. 너는 눈도 코도 귀도 발도 손도 업거늘 졍신이 엇지 잇스랴. 그러하거늘 너는 엇지하야 정치도 내려 누루고 도덕도 무시하고 륜리도 파괴하고 심지어 신셩한 종교까지도 압박하는 힘을 가젓나뇨.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
네 압헤는 군왕도 절하고 장군도 꺼구러지고 귀족도 꿀어안고 만민이 굴복하는 고나. 너는 무엇이관대 그리도 위대하냐. 모든 사람들과 모든 나라를 싸우게도 하야보고 뎨휴하게도 하야보고 울리기도 하고 웃기기도 하느냐. 모든 가뎡과 모든 민족을 화평하게도 하야보고 락심하게도 하야보고 일어서 뛰게도 하야보고 안자 울게도 하야 보느냐.

돈아! 네 일홈이  돈이냐.

너는 사람이 생기기 전부터 이 세상에 잇섯더냐. 아니 사람과 한때에 생겻더냐.
사람이 생기자말자는 텬연의 과실과 초목의 근경(根莖)으로 살엇다. 이것이 소위 원시시대이다. 그래서 각각 자긔의 손으로 자긔의 먹을 것을 어더 살앗다. 그럼으로 너를 주고 밧구어 가질 아모 것도 업섯다. 자연은 모다 그네들께 아모 대까(代價) 업시 해방되얏다. 그 때에는 너는 생길 필요가 업섯다. 그 후에 차츰 세월의 감을 따라서 사람은 만해지고 텬연의 과실과 초목의 근경은 적다. 그래서 사람들은 산 속에 즘승을 바다에 고기를 잡어 먹기 시작하얏다. 이 때에도 역시 각각 자긔 손으로＜13＞ 자긔 먹을 것을 엇고 살엇다. 이것이 소위 어렵시대다. 이때에도 역시 너는 생길 필요가 업섯다. 즉금 시대에도 산간유곡에 사는 집에서는 돈아 너를 쓸 필요가 젹고 한 가정 안에서는 너를 쓰지 안는 것을 보아도 알 것이 아니냐.
사람은 즘승과 고기를 잡어 먹는 동안에 역시 인구가 만히 증가되며 한편으로 즘승과 고기의 획득(獲得)이 불정확함으로 자연 즘승을 길듸려 양하는 법을 배왓다. 따라서 과동(過冬)할 필요로 즘승의 식물을 공급하기 위하야 곡물을 재배하게 되얏다. 이 즘승의 식물 재배야말로 후일에 우리의 농사의 시조가 되얏다. 이때가 즉 목축시대라고 하는 때이다.
이 때에는 사람들이 아모리 뿌러나가도 양(羊, 양은 최초의 축물이라한다. 아마 유순하고 온양한 까닭이겟다)의 증식만은 못할뿐더러 다수의 양을 양함으로 자연 여유가 잇게 되얏다. 따라서 음식물의 여유는 사람에게 시간의 여유를 주엇다. 그래서 간단한 쳔도 짜게 되고 간단한 연장도 만들며 곡물의 재배도 시작하게 되얏다.
그러나 이러한 일은 남녀의 관계로 디대의 관계로 인하야 자연 분업의 형식을 이루엇다. 동시에 그때의 사회 조직은 혈속관계의 친족으로 결합한 대가족뎍(大家族的) 촌락이 산재(散在)한 것이엿다. 이 대가족 안에서는 역시 공산하엿다. 그러나 촌락과 촌락 사이에는 공동경제가 아니엿다. 이러한 촌락이 인구의 증가를 따라서 만히 생긴 것은 말할 필요도 업거니와이 촌락과 촌락과의 사이에는 자연 여유잇는 물건의 유무상통의 법이 새겻섯다. 그러나 역시 양과 쌀, 쌀과 쳔, 쳔과 양 등과 가티 물건과 물건이 서로 직접 밧구어가게 되엿다. 이것이 소위 물물교환(物物交換)이다.

이 세상의 생산게는 네가 나온 후로 미증유의 발달을 하얏다. 그러나 네가 넓히 위력을 부리면 부리는 때마다 네의 운명을 너로 재촉하얏다. 그 연유는 이러하다. 네가 생산게에 넓히 돌아단일 때부터 사람들은 모다 자긔의 요구 이외의 물건으로 자긔 취미잇고 익숙은 일만 하얏다. 그래서 교환에 인하야 살아왓다. 이것은 자연이 자긔가 자긔의 생산을 지배하지 못하는 초문열이 되얏다. 다시 말하면 물건을 만드는 사람은 그 물건의 압길을 모른다. 그럼으로 사람마다 팔기 위한 물건을 무한량 무제한으로 만들면 어느 때에던지 생산이 수요에 넘어서 한번은 폭발을 한다. 그리하야 산업게는 일시 뎡톄된다. 그리하야 공장을 닷치고 상뎜문도 닷친다. 이것이 소위 경제공황(經濟恐慌)이다. 이런 공황이 근대에 이르러서는 더욱더욱 범위가 커지고도수사 자자온다. 그래 우리도 일로전쟁 후와 금번 세게대전란 후에 두 번식 당하얏다. 멀리 옛날은 살펴볼 것도 업시.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 이럴 때마다 어대가 잇는지 더욱 볼 수 업두구나. 그리도 령리하고 그리도 위력잇는 네가 이 리치를 알지 못할 리가 잇느냐. 너도 좀 점즉하야 어대로 도망하얏드냐 피하얏든가. 올타 훔쳐가는 도적보다도 쓰고 내지 안는 도적을 무서워서 금고 속에 잠복하얏지.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 네의 생겨난 동긔를 다시 생각하야 보아라. 네의 생겨날 전뎨를 생각하야 보아라. 너는 각자의 소유물을＜18＞ 전뎨로 한다. 그래서 각 사람의 물건 교환함을 동긔로 하엿다.
너는 각 사람의 소유물을 구별치 안는 집에 가서 보아라. 그 사람들 사이에는 아모 교환할 것이 업다. 아니 감옥 안 죄수 사회에 가서 보아라. 어대 교환이 잇드냐. 참말 교환이 업기 따문에 죄수 사회에서는 네 얼골을 구경한 일이 업다.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
생산의 무질서로 일어나는 경제공황의 원인이 무엇인지를 아느냐.
생산이 과다하다고 한다. 그것이 참말가튼 줄 아느냐. 생산이 과다하다는 것은 모든 사람이 쑤고 먹고 남아지 인사는 말이 아니냐. 엇진 일인지 공황 때에는 사람사람이 생활난이 더 심한 것은 긔이한 일이 아니냐. 생산과다라는 뜻에 비춰 보아서.
그러나 오늘날 생산과다라는 말은 돈아 너를 가진 수요자가 적어서 공급 물건이 과다하다는 말이다. 일반 사람이 생활자료가 넘우 풍부하야 물건이 남아잇다는 말은 아니다.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐. 너는 이것을 알어라. 이 리치를 알어라. 생산하는 집과 긔게는 모다 몃 사람의 물건이다. 만들어 나온 물건도 그 집과 긔게의 주인의 물건이다. 그럼으로 생산은 증가하야도 물건은 만허도 그것을 거저 주지는 안는다. 누구에게던지. 그럼으로 드문드문 폭발하야진다. 이것은 몃몃 사람이 물건을 생산하는 물건 즉 소위 자본을 가지고 잇는 때까지는 생산력이 이 이상 더 발달할 수는 업다는 증거가 아닌가.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 생산 행위의 목뎍을 아느냐. 생산 행위는 생산의 무한한 증진을 목뎍한다. 그럼으로 원시시대부터＜19＞ 오늘날까지 생산의 증가를 저해하는 모든 것을 깨틀여왓다. 그것은 사람이 한 것이 아니라 사람은 다만 병뎡 노릇을 하얏다. 그리고 너도 참으로 사람 우에 잇서서 장교 노릇을 하얏다.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 생산 증가의 저해하는 것이 무엇인지를 알겟다. 그것을 깨틀여보자. 너는 장교가 되야라. 우리는 병뎡이다.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
우리는 반듯이 익인다. 이 싸홈에도 네 눈으로 네가 보아 온 일이 아니냐. 그리하야 네 자취는 업서지겟다고 생각하면 매우 섭섭하다, 만은 교환물건 업는 마당에 너를 청해 올 수도 업고.

돈아! 네 일홈이 돈이냐.
너는 차라리 전사하여라. 너는 선봉에서 대포에 마저 쭉어라. 네 죽은 시톄랑은 모조리 모아다가 박물관에 보기좃케 사이좃케 늘어 놋코 우리가 우리의 자손이 억만대를 나아가면서 네의 전공을 찬송하야주마!
