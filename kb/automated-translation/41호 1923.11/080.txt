Grade of Each Train Station

The Keijō Management Bureau of the South Manchuria Railway recently revised the grades of each station, considering factors such as station duties (驛務) and personnel capacity (定員), as follows.

First Class Stations: Kyŏngsŏng, P'yŏngyang, Yongsan, Pusan

Second Class Stations: Taejŏn, Taegu, Sinŭiju, Inch'ŏn, Chinnamp'o, Wŏnsan

Third Class Stations: Kunsan, Iri, Ch'ŏngjin, Hoeryŏng, Mokp'o, Sinmak, Chŏngju, Pokkye, Yŏngdŭngp'o, Ch'oryang

Fourth Class Stations: Songjŏngni, Choch'iwŏn, Hamhŭng, Masan, Ch'ŏnan, Kimch'ŏn, Samnangjin, Sariwŏn, Suwŏn, Kaesŏng, Sinanju, Ch'uhyŏn, Hwangju, Nanam

Fifth Class Stations: Pusanjin, Kup'o, Ku Masan, Miryang, Kyŏngsan, Waegwan, Ch'up'ungnyŏng, Nonsan, Kanggyŏng, Kimje, Chŏngŭp, Sŏnghwan, P'yŏngt'aek, Ch'ŏngnyangni, Ch'ŏrwŏn, Kosan, Kyŏmip'o, Sŏnch'ŏn, Sŭnghori, Sŏn'gyori

Sixth Class Stations: All stations other than those listed in the above classes.

Seventh Class Stations: Each signal station, each simple station.<59>

**Summary**

The Keijō (Seoul) Management Bureau of the South Manchuria Railway has reclassified train stations throughout Korea into seven grades based on operational duties and staffing levels. Major cities like Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), P'yŏngyang, Yongsan, and Pusan are designated First Class, with progressively smaller towns and facilities categorized into lower grades, down to signal and simple stations in the Seventh Class. This article provides historians with a snapshot of the hierarchical structure of the colonial railway network in 1923, reflecting the administrative priorities and perceived importance of different locations under Japanese control via the South Manchuria Railway Company. The specific listing reveals the extent of the railway network and the systematic classification imposed by the colonial authorities.
