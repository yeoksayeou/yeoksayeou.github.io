Attending the National Disarmament Movement Rally, a Great Mass Movement of All China
						By A Reporter in Beijing

The National Disarmament Movement Rally (國民裁兵運動大會), jointly initiated by various new figures across China and established in early October, saw the participation of over 70 public and private organizations in Beijing. On October 10th, the Double Ten Festival (China's National Day, commemorating the success of the Wuchang Revolution), at noon sharp, they staged a demonstration march divided into East, West, South, and North contingents. Each contingent appointed one general commander to exclusively manage all its affairs, with Boy Scouts maintaining order within each group. The banners for each contingent were made of white cloth, prominently displaying the eight characters: "Reduce Military Strength, Save the Nation, Save the People" (裁減兵額, 救國救民). There were approximately 350 different types of flyers and leaflets from various organizations, mostly advocating for disarmament. Besides this, countless other slogans of general awakening were written on small flags, such as "Down with International Capitalism," "Equal Rights for Men and Women," "Abolish the Public Order Police Law," "Anarchism," and "People's Revolution." At 5 PM that day, after marching throughout Beijing, the participants from all routes gathered together in the square outside Tiananmen (the central gate of the former Qing Palace's main entrance) to hold the National Disarmament Movement Rally. Including President Li Yüan-hung (黎元洪), who joined in his capacity as a citizen, along with celebrities from all walks of life, truly more than fifty thousand people gathered. A Disarmament Committee was promptly organized to petition the Presidential Office, the State Council, and the National Assembly to realize the progress of disarmament. The full text of the petition is as follows:

Lin Ch'ang-min (林長民) (former Minister of Justice) and others hereby represent the public will to petition for the organization of a National Disarmament Committee. The matter of disarmament is extremely crucial for our country's future, requiring no lengthy explanation from the likes of Ch'ang-min and others. However, this matter values only practical execution, and execution relies on the power of organization. Therefore, we wish that the government swiftly convenes the committee and sets about creating the executive body. We hereby list the articles of the petition: <70>
1. The government shall adopt the will and demands of the people, convene a National Disarmament Committee, and organize an executive body for disarmament.
2. The government shall promptly dispatch several commissioners to consult with designated representatives from various organizations and organize a provisional committee.
3. The National Disarmament Committee shall be established within the shortest possible period from now and prepare for the following matters:
    A. Draft a disarmament plan to serve as discussion material when the formal committee convenes.
    B. Solicit public opinion from all citizens for all formal and informal procedures, organize the formal committee with designated representatives from various quarters, making it an active body of public opinion and practical strength.
The above plan is extremely simple and feasible to implement. We request that the President make a resolute and clear decision and promulgate it immediately. After the above items were discussed and decided upon at the rally, they were immediately sent to the government. Mr. Li Yüan-hung was the first to take the stage, earnestly advocating the necessity of disarmament, followed by speeches from first-rate new figures such as Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei (蔡元培), Li Shih-tseng (李石曾), Lin Ch'ang-min (林長民), Ting Chin (丁錦), Ma Chün-wu (馬君武), Chang Chi (張繼), Hu Shih (胡適), Li Ta-chao (李大釗), and other politicians and scholars. Besides these, others spoke freely, truly presenting an unprecedentedly grand spectacle.

My Reflections

Brothers, do you remember China's Double Ten Festival? You might well remember America's Independence Day or France's Revolution Day, but I fear you might not remember this day in China. This statement isn't just because it's the day China's republic was established or its revolution succeeded. Rather, it's the day the child of republicanism was first born on the great continent of East Asia. It's the day the people's movement began. Moreover, as a Korean, although it is another nation's celebration, when we distantly celebrate this day together, brothers, what arises in your hearts? What comes to your eyes? Regarding this disarmament movement beginning on the National Day, Mr. Lin Ch'ang-min said, "Celebration is a sign of not forgetting a kind of suffering." This is truly factual. If we were to say we too have a day to celebrate, it would naturally be so, but moreover, if there's any association with news like this, it can only be a sign of suffering. Eleven years ago today in the Republic of China, in Wuchang, Hubei Province, those revolutionary soldiers who rose up, shattering the Hubei Governor-General's office with bare hands!! How much suffering must they have endured at that time? That sacrifice, that suffering, must have brought this great day to the Chinese people. I wish to briefly write my minor reflections based on the article above. Disarmament!! It may not offer much reference for us, but on this day, I participated in the North Contingent march centered around Peking University (北大), wearing a small badge on my lapel reading "National Disarmament Movement" (國民裁兵運動), and followed along to the grand rally site outside Tiananmen. <71> Until I went to join the contingent, my consciousness was functioning normally. However, upon arriving at the First Compound of Peking University, seeing the brave men gleaming with light and hope, as if setting off to a place of honor or the front lines of revolution, even my momentarily gloomy heart leaped with some joy. Then, forming ranks, as the thousands of people were about to exit the compound gate, when the military band at the head began to play, I completely lost my consciousness; my spirit felt paralyzed, my chest tightened, and tears of sorrow poured out. As the crowd, stretching several hundred steps long, moved out, I seem to have stood there alone. Like waking from an anesthetic, I gradually regained consciousness and, swept up by the crowd, went along, but my body, already alternating between chills and fever, gradually felt an even deeper, more profound sort of illusion constantly welling up inside, while inwardly I wept limitlessly as if something was breaking, and made a deep resolve. Arriving at the rally site, there was indeed a state of joy celebrating the National Day, but they too, since the founding of the Republic, were still amidst much suffering, and the sounds of discontent and cries of grief pierced my heart even more. Finally, I gained some courage from the magnificent cries of "the people's self-awareness" and "the people's self-creation" born of great unity, but after barely getting through this day that caused me the most anguish, on the way back after dispersing, my endlessly tense mind inevitably turned back and prepared to write this piece. Before I even finished my evening meal, I sat at my desolate desk intending to write this. However, I could only wonder how best to begin the topic; when I tried to write, there was nothing, and it seemed impossible, my fervent thoughts only making my head ache. Truly, I have no reflections to offer before you, brothers. Ah, brothers, what could have been the reason for the strange state of my nerves earlier? I cannot express it well. But I know that if you, brother, were to encounter that moment, you too would unknowingly feel the same. Moreover, for someone like me, living a fugitive life without achieving anything significant, the feeling was truly accidental and indescribable. However, I do not intend to reveal that feeling here. I merely wish to speak of one ordinary thing. The modern era is called the era of the masses. Accordingly, the masses awaken and seek to live as the masses. Thus, striving and struggling day by day to bring about that which is ultimately desired, <72> namely happiness, seems to be a principle that cannot be neglected if human society is to be maintained. However, looking back at the history of past human life, despite the long ages since human life began, has not that happiness desired by the masses failed to materialize? Not only have we endlessly received suffering as the price for that happiness until now, but much human suffering remains ahead, and it seems an awareness from the present perspective that, aiming for human life, suffering cannot be avoided one way or another. The Chinese people endured the suffering of past revolution to bring about that happiness. Again, to overthrow the warlords and expand civil rights, they voluntarily take on the yoke of suffering, initiating disarmament movements like today's, and intend to sacrifice their lives and shed blood against their adversaries in the future. Thus, within suffering, they try to cast off suffering and seek happiness. If viewed from the traditional Eastern perspective of acceptance (達觀主義), human endeavors might indeed seem laughable, but as humans, if one does not know that suffering and does not voluntarily seek to taste it, then humans are truly unconscious. Therefore, I earnestly wish to advocate the philosophy of suffering here. Brothers, do we have no suffering? Will we not step forward once to taste suffering? As you can see from the article above, this mass movement in China this time seems to encompass all classes of the populace, from the President above to the laborers below. Therefore, this movement might be the first mass movement since the Chinese Revolution, a great manifestation of the ultimate suffering. The daily progress of the Chinese people is truly astonishing. Progress means tasting suffering well. From this, I gained a feeling and wish to briefly discuss the meaning and value of mass movements. A mass movement is a function that universalizes the will of an entire nation or populace, starting perhaps in one city or region. Indeed, looking at 19th-century European political history, it is the product of mass movements, and the victories of oppressed nations and the proletariat are entirely obtained through these means. Therefore, every kind of ideal and principle, from the day of its inception until the day it fully manifests as reality, generally passes through several, or even dozens, of mass movements. Thus, state politics is entirely <73> built upon the total consciousness of the nation; national consciousness is that which acutely judges the good or bad of state politics. If such daily expressions of national consciousness, namely mass movements, do not persist, the nation becomes detached from politics, numbed, and is akin to neglecting its own life. Accordingly, while there are not a few methods to awaken national consciousness, among them, the ultimately most fierce and most universal is none other than the mass movement. When one has discontent or assertions but shows no such expression, that is a dead nation. Therefore, if humans intend to live even one day of political life, they cannot avoid receiving political education and training, and mass movements certainly provide universal political training for the common people and bring about universal political effects. Look! Were not universal suffrage in Europe, labor legislation, and other satisfactions of human grievances obtained through several mass movements of citizens? Of course, no matter how good the principle or ideal, when it first arises, the majority of the populace generally has no keen awareness of its pros and cons, thus paying little attention. Or, there are a few influential people at the time who, due to their own interests, vigorously oppose it and obstruct it in a hundred ways. However, once a mass movement occurs regarding that principle, those who paid no attention pay attention, those who did not sympathize sympathize, and what could not be boldly asserted becomes boldly asserted. Therefore, it can be called the sole method for the masses' expression regarding politics. <74>

**Summary**

The article, written by a Korean reporter in Beijing, describes the large-scale National Disarmament Movement rally held in Beijing on October 10, 1922 (China's National Day). It details the organization of the march, the diverse slogans, the mass gathering at Tiananmen Square involving figures like President Li Yuanhong and intellectuals such as Hu Shih and Li Dazhao, and the petition submitted to the government demanding the formation of a National Disarmament Committee. The reporter then shares personal reflections, deeply moved by the event, contrasting the Chinese people's active struggle and suffering for progress (disarmament, overthrowing warlords) with a perceived lack of such active engagement among Koreans. The author emphasizes the importance of mass movements as expressions of national consciousness, political training, and the means by which progress and rights are achieved, drawing parallels with European history and urging Koreans to embrace suffering as a path to progress. For historians, this article offers a valuable contemporary Korean perspective on early Republican China's political turmoil and mass mobilization. It reveals the emotional impact of witnessing such events on a Korean observer living under colonial rule, highlighting themes of national consciousness, suffering, and the perceived necessity of collective action, likely reflecting aspirations for Korea's own situation.

全中國의 大民衆運動인 國民裁兵運動大會에 參하고
						在北京 一記者
中國 各 新人界의 聯合發起로 10月 初에 成立된 國民裁兵運動大會는 北京 公私 各 70餘 團體의 加入으로 10月 10日 雙十節(中國 國慶日 即 武昌革命 成功日)로 期하야 同日 正午에 東西南北 四隊로 分하야 遊行의 示威運動을 擧行케 되엇는데 每隊에 總指揮 一人式 推擧하야 本隊一切의 事務를 專司케 하고 各隊에는 童子軍이 잇서 秩序를 維持케하며 各隊의 旗幟는 白布로 製하야 「裁減兵額, 救國救民」의 八個字를 大書特書하고 各 團體의 傳單 及 삐라가 各其잇서 約 三百五十餘種인데 大槪는 裁兵을 主張함이고 그 外에 打倒 國際資本主義, 男女同權, 撤廢治安警察法, 無政府主義, 民衆革命 等의 小旗에 쓴 一般 警醒의 單語는 그  數를 다 헤아릴 수 업스며 同下午 5時에 北京 全市를 遊行한 後 各路의 參加運動者는 天安門(前 淸宮 正門의 中門)外 廣場에 一齊히 會集하야 國民裁兵運動大會를 開한 바 國民의 資格으로 加入된 大總統 黎元洪氏 以下 各界 名人을 合하야 實로 五萬 以上의 群衆이 모이게 되엇다. 即히 裁兵委員會를 組織하야 裁兵의 進行을 實現할 事를 大總統府 國務院 及 國會에 請願케 하얏는데 그 請願의 全文은 左와 如하니
林長民(前 司法 總長)等은 玆에 衆意를 代表하야 全國裁兵委員會組織의 事를 請願하나이다. 裁兵의 一事는 우리나라 前途에 極히 主要함은 長民 等의 贅述할 바를 待치 만흘 것이지마는 다만 일은 오즉 實行이 貴한 것이요. 實行은 또한 組織의 力을 賴하는 것인즉 願컨대 政府는 速히 委員會를 召集하고 實行의 機關에 着手하소서. 玆에 請願의 各 條를 列擧하노니 ＜70＞
1. 政府는 國民의 意思와 要求를 採取하야 全國裁兵委員會를 召集하고 裁兵實行의 機關을 組織할 事
2. 政府는 곳 委員 若干人을 特派하야 各 團體指定代表와 商議하야 臨時委員會를 組織할 事
3. 全國裁兵委員會는 이제로부터 最短期內에 成立하야 左列의 各 事를 籌備할 事
甲 裁兵의 草案을 擬定하야써 正式委員會 開會의 時에 討論의 資料가 되게할 事
乙 正式 及 非正式의 一切 手續은 全國民의 公意를 徵求하야 各 方面의 指定한 代表로써 正式의 委員會를 組織하야 輿論 及 實力의 有動機關이 되게할 事
以上의 案은 極히 簡易하야 實行에 可能하오니 請컨대 大總統은 毅然히 明斷하시와 卽日 公布하야 주소서. 以上의 件을 大會에서 討議議決한 후 곳 政府에 送呈케 하고 黎元洪씨는 가장 먼저 登壇하야 裁兵의 必要를 切實히 主張한 後 蔡元培, 李石曾, 林長民, 丁錦, 馬君武, 張繼, 胡適, 李大釗 其他 政治家 學者의 一流新人들이 演說하고 그外에는 自由로 演說케하야 참으로 空前의 大盛況을 呈케되다.

나의 所感

兄弟여 中國의 雙十節을 記憶하는가. 美國의 獨立紀念日이나 佛蘭西의 革命紀念日은 잘 記憶하실지라도 中國의 是日은 或 記憶치 못하실가 저어합니다. 이 말은 中國의 共和가 成立되며 中國의 革命이 成功된 날일 뿐만이 아니외다. 如何커나 東亞의 大陸에 共和의 兒가 처음으로 産出된 날이외다. 民衆의 運動이 開始되던 이날이외다. 더구나 우리 朝鮮人으로 남의 慶日이지마는
