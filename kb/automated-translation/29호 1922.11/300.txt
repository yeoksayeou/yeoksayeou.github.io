The Selfish Giant
						By Oscar Wilde of England (Original Author), Sopa (Translator)
This piece is one of the famous fairy tales by Oscar Wilde (Born 1856, Died 1900), renowned as the author of the famous "Salome" or the proponent of "The Decay of Lying." The fairy tales he wrote in his lifetime are entirely contained in two books, "A House of Pomegranates" and "The Happy Prince and Other Tales," totaling only nine short and long stories. All were written during the peak of his artistic activity, and most exude the profound fragrance of a romance elevated by religious, pious "love." They should not be seen merely as old tales. In some ways, they seem too allegorical and difficult for fairy tales, with many points whose true meaning might be hard for even general adults to grasp. However, this one piece, which strongly teaches the power of love, is deeply allegorical yet very gently written, which is why we introduce it to the readers now.
(Note) This piece is included in the book "The Happy Prince and Other Tales," published in 1888. I take this opportunity to mention that "The Prince and the Swallow" included in my translation "Love's Gift" is the translation of "The Happy Prince," which is the title of that book.

Every day around two or three in the afternoon, when they returned from school, the children would customarily go and play in the garden of the "Bearded Strongman's" house.
That garden was truly so lovely and fine, as magnificent, large, and wide as a place where even celestial fairies might play. Soft, fine green grass was spread out, and here and there in the lawn, bright red and yellow flowers like twinkling stars were smilingly blooming. There were also twelve peach trees, which, every spring, bloomed with pinkish,<43> pearl-like blossoms, and in the fall, bore large, delicious, wonderful peaches in abundance.
Moreover, the songs of the various small, lovely birds perched on the branches were truly delightful and sweet, so much so that the children would stop their play and quietly listen and listen to their songs.
"Isn't this garden truly a wonderful place?"
"Could there be such a fine yard anywhere else?"
The children whispered among themselves, rejoicing.
Then one day, the owner of this house, the Bearded Strongman (so called because he had a lot of facial hair and was strong), returned after a long time. The Bearded Strongman had gone to visit his friend, an ogre named "K'onnol," and had stayed, slept, and played at his house for seven years. Having spent seven years chatting and playing with his ogre friend, he ran out of stories to tell and things became dull, so he simply wandered back to his own house after seven years. When he returned, he saw his garden full of children playing freely.
"You rascals, what mischief are you up to here?" he yelled. Suddenly, the frightening-looking bearded owner shouted fiercely, startling the children, who immediately fled in panic.
"My garden is my garden, always." he muttered, "It's always been the rule... No one but me can enter my garden." And so, the Bearded Strongman immediately built a high wall all around so no one could enter, and even put up a notice board that read:
TRESPASSERS WILL BE
PROSECUTED (處罰).
This Bearded Strongman was a very ill-tempered and stubborn person.

Sadly, from that day on, the children had absolutely no place to gather and play. Having no choice, they tried playing<44> on the street, but the street was too dusty and the road surface had too many sharp stones, making it impossible to play. Thus, with nowhere to play, after returning from school, the children would just circle around the high-walled house of the Bearded Strongman, talking about the lovely flower garden inside the wall.
"That garden was truly wonderful, wasn't it?"
"We really had fun playing in that garden..."
The poor children would say such wistful things, looking up at the wall.

Soon, spring returned, and new grass sprouted on every mountain and field, and the warm spring sunlight shone brilliantly every day. Flowers bloomed brightly on every branch, and small, lovely birds began to sing. The whole world seemed intoxicated with spring, as if slightly drunk.
But for some reason, inside the wall of the ill-tempered Bearded Strongman's house, spring did not arrive, and it remained "winter" indefinitely. Because there were no children, the birds didn't come to sing, and the trees didn't even dream of blossoming. Only once, a small wildflower cautiously pushed its head and face out from the lawn, but upon seeing the sign that said trespassers would be punished, it quickly recoiled back inside and fell asleep.
The only ones who rejoiced were the Snow and the Frost.
"It seems Spring has forgotten this flower garden. Anyway, thanks to that, we can stay in this house all year round,"
they whispered to each other, setting up their domain. The Snow covered the lawn with its great white cloak and lay down, and the Frost painted every single tree silvery-white.
Not satisfied, they even invited their friend, the North Wind, "to come visit with the intention of staying for many days."<45> So the North Wind came immediately. Upon arrival, wondering, "Why hasn't spring come to this house yet?" he declared, "This place is truly our world!" and spent the whole day shouting and running wildly inside the walls of the house. By evening, he had managed to break off the chimney (烟突) standing on the roof. Every time the North Wind shrieked and rampaged, the Bearded Strongman, unbearably cold, couldn't even open his door and huddled by a roaring fire.
The Snow, Frost, and North Wind grew increasingly excited.
"This is truly a splendid place for us. Since things are going so well, let's invite that fellow Hail (雹) too."
"Yes, let's. That's a fine idea." And they immediately invited the Hail, who rushed over at once. Every day, for three hours, the Hail battered the roof of the house, eventually breaking almost all the roof tiles (瓦). Still full of energy, it leaped down from the roof and dashed through the flower garden like lightning. The Hail wore grey, and its breath was colder than ice.
"This is really strange. Why won't spring come?" lamented the Bearded Strongman, sitting by the glass window in his room, looking out at the cold, white, snow-covered yard. "I wish the weather would change quickly..."
But spring never came. Summer also did not come. Elsewhere, autumn had already arrived, with golden-yellow ripe fruits hanging heavily in clusters, but not a single one grew in the Bearded Strongman's garden.
"That fellow is too ill-tempered and stubborn," said Autumn.
Thus, only winter perpetually surrounded the house, with the North Wind, Hail, Frost, and Snow dancing among themselves between the trees.
Then, one morning.
Wondering when spring would ever arrive... the Bearded Strongman, who couldn't leave the side of the hearth and lived only in the cold, opened his eyes in bed and heard, though he didn't know what it was, a soft, delicate sound of music (音樂) from somewhere. The music sounded somehow<45> welcoming and joyous, so he thought perhaps the king's musicians (樂師) were passing by. But in reality, it was the sound of a small, lovely bird perched outside the bedroom window, singing a song. Because he hadn't heard birdsong for such a long time, this birdsong sounded so welcoming and joyous that it seemed like the finest music in the world.
As the bird sang its long-overdue song, the Hail stopped dancing above the Bearded Strongman's head, and the North Wind ceased its howling rampage. And through the casement window, opened refreshingly after a long time, a fragrant scent wafted in profusely.
"Ah! Spring has come at last!" he cried out in overflowing joy, quickly getting out of bed and looking outside.
What did the Bearded Strongman see then?
What the Bearded Strongman saw at that moment was truly astonishing. There was a hole in one part of the high wall he had built, and through that hole, children had somehow crept in and were sitting perched on the tree branches, playing. On every tree in sight, a child was sitting on a branch. The trees, too, were extremely happy that the children had returned after such a long time, and they burst into full bloom all at once, smiling broadly and waving their slender arms gently above the children's heads. Small birds flew joyfully from tree to tree, singing happy songs. Lovely flowers smiled brightly upon the green lawn. All the beautiful things of spring gathered at once, creating a truly lovely and charming scene. However, there was just one spot, in a far corner, where spring had not yet arrived and winter lingered. It was near the wall in a corner of this flower garden; the tree standing there had no blossoms and remained wintry, and beneath that tree stood a small, cute child, all alone. This child was very small, his height not reaching the branches, so he couldn't climb up. He was weeping loudly, pacing anxiously beneath the tree. Poor tree, it still had frost and snow frozen upon it, and the North Wind was sniffling<47> among its upper branches.
"Little sir! Please climb up!"
The tree tried its utmost, bending its branches down as low as it could, but still, the child was too short to reach.
Seeing this sight, the Bearded Strongman felt as if something blocked and pressed in his chest had suddenly cleared.
"Ah, I didn't understand until now. Now I know the reason why spring wouldn't come. Yes! I will go and lift that child onto the tree! And I must tear down that high wall and make this flower garden a playground for the children forever!"
The Bearded Strongman shouted this with a joyful and relieved heart, regretting what he had done until then.
The Bearded Strongman quickly went down into the flower garden. Seeing the Bearded Strongman coming, the children who had been happily playing perched on the tree branches were startled and all jumped down and fled. And as the children ran away, the garden instantly turned back into winter. Only the small child from before, who had been crying so hard that his tears blurred his vision, did not notice the Bearded Strongman approaching. The Bearded Strongman quietly went behind him, gently lifted him with both hands, and placed him on a tree branch. Instantly, the tree burst into blossom, and birds flew over and began to sing. At that moment, the child stretched out his two arms, clasped the Bearded Strongman's neck, and kissed him from above. Seeing this, the other children realized that the Bearded Strongman was no longer fierce and all came back again. Flowers bloomed anew, birds gathered, and spring returned. The Bearded Strongman, overjoyed, didn't know what to do.
"There now, children! From now on, this is your yard!"
He immediately took a huge axe and knocked down the wall he had built. The dazzlingly beautiful and brilliant flower garden became visible even from afar, and the entire neighborhood could smell the fragrance of the flowers. And the neighborhood became even more splendid than before. <48>
From then on, the Bearded Strongman became friendly with the townspeople, and at night, people returning from the market passing by the garden would always see the Bearded Strongman playing among many cute children in the strangely brilliant flower garden.
Thus, day after day, the children played happily with the Bearded Strongman, and when evening came, they would all together at once say to the Bearded Strongman,
"Good night."
And depart.
"But do you know where that little friend of yours went? He hasn't come back at all since then... I mean the little boy I lifted onto the tree!"
The Strongman asked this because, as the child had been the first to kiss him, he cherished him more than the other children.
"We don't know. Maybe he's at his house,"
the children replied.
"Well then, tell him to be sure to come and play tomorrow!!"
He requested this, but they didn't know which house the little child belonged to, nor had they ever seen such a child before, so the Bearded Strongman became disheartened and very sad.
After that, too, day after day when school let out, the children gathered and played happily. However, the little child the Strongman longed to see never appeared again. The Strongman was friendly and fond of all the children, but he could never for a moment forget the child who had first become his friend, especially that lovely child who had first kissed him, and he always worried, mentioning him at every turn of phrase.
"Ah, how I wish I could somehow meet that child."
He often sighed, saying such words. <49>
Some years passed after that.
The Bearded Strongman gradually grew old, and year by year, his body weakened. So he no longer had the energy to go out into the yard and play with the children; he would sit on a large sofa-like chair and watch the children play and gaze at the garden's flowers.
"In my garden, countless beautiful flowers bloom together. But those children are the most beautiful flowers among them!"
He was always saying words like these.

One winter morning. The Strongman was getting dressed and looked out the casement window. The Strongman no longer hated winter so much. He knew it was merely the time when "Spring" was sleeping, and the flowers and birds were resting from fatigue.
While getting dressed, he looked out the window and suddenly! He saw a strange sight. A tree standing in a corner of the garden had somehow burst into full bloom, and all its branches were made of gold (黃金), and from these branches hung heavy clusters of fruit shining like silver (銀). And beneath that tree stood the very lovely, small child he had always longed to see, all alone.
How happy and overjoyed he was! He immediately went down and into the garden. But when he came close to the child, his face suddenly turned red, and his anger (怒氣) seemed to pierce the heavens.
"Who— who wounded (傷處) you so terribly like this!" he shouted, asking.
Truly! On the lovely child's two hands and feet, there were wounds (傷處), gruesome to behold, and marks where red blood had flowed were visible.
"Who wounded you like this? Huh? Tell me. I will kill that person with a sword." <50>
The child smiled brightly and said,
"N-no. It's not like that. These wounds are the marks of Love (愛)."
"Who are you?"
The Strongman asked, feeling strange. And somehow feeling struck by an unknown, mysterious force, he simply knelt before the child.
At that moment, the child looked at the Strongman, smiled sweetly, and spoke thus:
"You once let me play in your garden. Today, you must come play with me in my flower garden. That flower garden is the flower garden of Paradise (極樂)."

That day, after lunchtime, when the children returned from school as usual to play, they found the Bearded Strongman lying dead under the tree, his entire body covered in white blossoms. (The End) <51>

**Summary**

This article is a Korean translation of Oscar Wilde's fairy tale "The Selfish Giant," published in the magazine *Kaebyŏk* in 1922. The translator is identified as "Sopa" (Bang Jeong-hwan). The introduction praises Wilde and explains the allegorical nature of his tales, particularly highlighting the theme of love in this story. The main text follows the familiar plot: a giant forbids children from his beautiful garden, causing perpetual winter; he repents when he sees the children sneak back in, bringing spring with them, except for one small boy unable to climb a tree; the giant helps the boy, kisses him, tears down the wall, and welcomes all children; years later, the aging giant sees the boy again, bearing wounds (stigmata), who reveals they are marks of love and invites the giant to his garden, Paradise; the giant is later found dead under the tree, covered in blossoms. From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as an early example of Western children's literature translation in colonial Korea, undertaken by Bang Jeong-hwan (Sopa), a pivotal figure in the Korean children's rights and literature movement. The use of early 20th-century Korean orthography and vocabulary also provides valuable linguistic data.

털보장사
						英國 오스카-·와일드 (원작자) 小波(번역자)
이 一篇은 彼 有名한 「사로메」의 作者 또는 「架空의 頹廢」의 論者로 有名한 오스카-·와일드 (1856年 生 1900年 死)의 名作 童話 中의 一이다. 그가 一生 中에 지은 童話는 全部 「柘榴의 家」와 「幸福한 王子와 其他의 物語」라는 두 冊에 실려잇는 大小 9篇뿐인데 모다 그 藝術的 活動의 全盛期에 쓴 것이어서 大槪는 宗敎的 敬虔한 「사랑」을 高潮한 「로- 맨쓰」의 香내가 그윽한 것이라. 다만 한 녯날 이약이로만 보지 못할 것이다. 어떠케 보면 童話로서는 넘우 寓意가 깁고 難解할 點이 만하서 一般 壯年들에게도 그 眞意를 捕捉키 어려운 것이 만흔것 가트나 그 中에 사랑의 힘을 몹시 强하게 가르친 이 1編은 寓意가 깁흐면서도 몹시 보들업게 씨운 것이라 이제 讀者께 紹介하기로 한 것이다.
(거듭) 이 1篇은 1888年에 公刊된 「幸福한 王子와 其他의 物語」라는 冊에 실려잇는 것이요, 拙譯 「사랑의 선물」에 실려잇는 「王子와 제비」라는 것이 그 冊의 題目인 「幸福한 王子」의 譯인 것을 이 機會에 말해둔다.

날마다 오후(午后) 두 세시쯤 되어, 학교에서 돌아오면 어린이들은 의례히, 「털보장사」의 집, 꼿동산에 가서 놀앗습니다.
그 동산이야말로 어떠케 어엽브고 조흔지 한울의 선녀들이라도 노는 곳가티 훌륭하고 크고 넓은 동산이엇습니다. 보들업고 고흔 잔듸가 파-라케 깔러잇고 그 잔듸밧에 군대군대 빤작빤작 하는 별(星)가티 빨가코 노-란 꼿이 빵긋 웃고 잇섯습니다. 그리고 복사나무가 열두나무나 잇서서, 해마다 봄에는 붉으레-＜43＞하게 진주가튼 꼿이 피고 가을이면 크고 맛잇는 훌륭한 복사가 듬븍듬븍 열리엇습니다.
그리고 그 보다도 더 여려가지 조그만 어업븐 새들이 그 나무가지에 안저서 부르는 노래소리는 참말로 재미잇고 귀여워서 어린이들도 작난을 그치고 조용하게 그 노래를 듯고 잇고 듯고 잇고 하엿습니다.
「이 동산은 참 조흔 곳이지?」
「다른 대도 이러케 조흔 마당이 잇슬가?」
하고 어린이들은 서로 속살거리면서 질거워 하엿습니다.
그랫더니 하로는 이 집 주인인 털보장사(얼굴에 털이 만히 나고 긔운이 세여서 털보장사라 합니다.)가 오래간만에 돌아왓습니다. 털보는 그 동무 「콘놀」이라는 마귀의 집에 놀너가서 닐곱해 (7年)동안이나 그 집에서 자고 놀고 잇섯습니다. 닐곱해 동안이나 그 동무 마귀와 이약이나 하고 놀고 잇스니까 그만 이약이 미천이 끈허저서 할 이약이도 업고 재미가 업스니까 그냥 닐골해ㅅ만에 자긔 집으로 휘-적 돌아온 것이엇습니다. 돌아와서 보니까 자긔 집 동산에 어린이들이 긋득 모여서 마음대로 놀고 잇는 고로,
「요놈들, 여긔서 무슨 작난들을 하니」하고 소리를 질럿습니다. 별안간에 그 무섭게 생긴 털보 주인이 소리를 버럭 지르니까 어린이들은 깜작 놀라서 그냥 도망을 하야 다라낫습니다.
「내 동산은 어느 때던지 내 동산이다.」하고 중얼거리면서 「의례 그런 법이지... 내 동산에는 나 밧게는 아모도 들어오지 못한다.」하고 털보장사는 곳 아모도 들어오지 못하게 놉다라케 담을 둘러싸코, 게다가 널빤지에 써다 부치기까지 하엿습니다.
이 안에 들어오는 자는
처벌(處罰)하리라. 고... .
이 털보장사는 대단히 성질이 사납고 고집이 세인 사람이엇습니다.

가엽게도 어린이들은 그 날부터는 영영 모여 놀 곳이 업섯습니다. 하는 수가 업서서 길거리에 가서 놀아도＜44＞ 보앗지마는 길거리는 몬지만 만코 길바닥에 돌뿌리가 만하서 놀 수가 업섯습니다. 그래서 아모대도 놀 곳이 업서서 학교에서 돌아오면 어린이들은 놉흔 담으로 에워싸힌 털보장사의 집 근처를 빙빙 돌면서 그 담 안의 어엽븐 꼿밧 이약이만 하고 지냇습니다.
「참말로 그 동산은 조핫지?」
「참말로 그 동산에선 재미잇게 놀앗것만...」
이러케 가여운 어린이들은 그 담을 쳐다보면서, 섭섭한 말을 하고들 지내엿습니다.

이윽고 봄철이 돌아와서 어느 산 어느 밧헤던지 새 풀이 솟고, 따뜻한 봄볏이 날마다 화려하게 빗낫습니다. 가지마다 환하게 꼿이 피고 조그만 어엽분 새가 노래를 부르기 시작하엿습니다. 술이 얼근하게 취한 것가티 세상이 모다 봄에 취하엿습니다.

그 중에 뎨일 질거워 하는 것은, 눈(雪)과 서리(霜)뿐이엇습니다.
