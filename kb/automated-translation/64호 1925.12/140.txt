발행일	1925년 12월 01일
기사제목	兩西 五十日 中에서 (Fifty Days in the Two Wests)
필자	春坡
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

Fifty Days in the Two Wests
						Ch'unp'a
With "Kaebyŏk" suspended, frustration flaring up, and nothing to eat – how could I not leave? I decided to set off for various reasons: to get meals, to sightsee, to look up friends, and to spread the Doctrine. Until the suspension is lifted and an urgent telegram summons my return to the company, I would wander for a month, two months, half a year, a year. No matter how incompetent I might be, surely I could get meals. Let's go. Let's go anywhere. "To pick mulberries and see my beloved," as they say. Let's first go towards my hometown, both to get food and to see my parents. I'll stop by Hwanghae Province to look for several friends, visit a few places in South P'yŏngan Province, spend a few months in North P'yŏngan Province, and hopefully, if the good news of the suspension being lifted arrives, I'll come straight back up. If not, I'll go to South Manchuria or South Chosŏn – that's what I'll do. Thus, I listed my planned destinations. First, travel through Kimch'ŏn and Koksan, then via Suan and Sŏhŭng to Pongsan and Hwangju. From there, to P'yŏngyang and P'yŏngwŏn, then through Anju and Pakch'ŏn. Spend the Ch'usŏk holiday in my hometown T'aech'ŏn, then head to Kusŏng and Chŏngju, Sŏnch'ŏn and Ch'ŏlsan, Yongch'ŏn and Ŭiju. If my route changes by then, I'll go to South Manchuria. If not, I'll travel through Sakchu, Ch'angsŏng, Pyŏktong, Ch'osan, Wiwŏn, and Kanggye – the seven towns along the river – then come out to Hŭich'ŏn, see Unsan and Nyŏngbyŏn, arrive in Kaech'ŏn and decide again. If there's still no news of the suspension being lifted by then, I'll make another detour through Sunch'ŏn, Tŏkch'ŏn, Yangdŏk, Maengsan, Sŏngch'ŏn, and Kangdong, return to P'yŏngyang and decide again. If there's still no news even then (though surely it wouldn't take that long, I thought), I'll head to Chinnamp'o, tour the various towns of Haesŏ like Ŭllyul, Changyŏn, Ongjin, and Haeju, then go up to Seoul via Kaesŏng and Changdan. If the suspension still isn't lifted by then, this time I'll head down to the three southern provinces – Ch'ungch'ŏng, Chŏlla, and Kyŏngsang – and run around here and there. Yes, I will. No worries at all – thinking this, I set off towards Sŏsŏn (West Chosŏn) first, on September 7th. It's true that when I left, I felt a bit sad, like a defeated soldier. (Looking back over the 50 days in Yangsŏ [the Two Wests] day by day is truly too much. I'll jump through it quickly.)

The Driver and the Oxcart Driver
On the day I left, I stopped in Kimch'ŏn to visit Mr. Yu Ik-su and talk about church matters. The next day, I got off at Namch'ŏn Station, savored a bowl of cold noodles after a long time, and then headed straight the 17 *ri* to Koksan by automobile. About 3 *ri* from Namch'ŏn, there is the Yesŏng River ferry crossing. Here, the first act of sightseeing unfolded. (These kinds of incidents popping up make travel enjoyable.) What was it? A conflict between the oxcart drivers and the automobile driver.
Originally a newly constructed direct road between Namch'ŏn and Koksan, and also a ferry crossing point, it was incredibly busy, not only with pedestrians but also with oxcarts, automobiles, and bicycles. When the automobile reached the riverside, already about ten oxcarts loaded with cargo had arrived and were jostling to cross first. The driver maneuvered the automobile past the many oxcarts that had arrived first, parking it in the third position from the river mouth. As there was no more room to move forward, he had no choice but to wait for the two oxcarts ahead to cross before it was the automobile's turn. It looked like it would take about an hour. Behind the automobile, the mood of the oxcart drivers turned sour. One oxcart crossed, and another was loaded onto the ferry. We firmly believed the automobile would surely be next after this trip. But what happened? An oxcart that had been behind us brushed past the automobile and drove down. Two oxcarts nonchalantly parked in front of the automobile. An argument erupted between the driver and the oxcart drivers. "Hey, what is this? Driving your oxcart down while seeing the automobile ahead?" the driver said, eyes wide open. The oxcart driver pretended not to hear and just whipped the ox, saying "Hey, hurry up-" and continued pulling it down. The driver got angry. The passengers in the automobile also got angry. I, too, initially felt displeased. "Hey, the automobile goes first. Pull back," the driver shouted, almost ordering, as he approached the oxcart driver. "What's this nonsense? You think just because it's an automobile, it's special? We've been waiting here for three, four hours. What's wrong? You just arrived and you're making all this fuss," the oxcart driver retorted, tightening his belt and glaring. "Hey, tell the oxen behind to hurry down too," he said, looking ready for a fight. "Regardless of the circumstances, the automobile is supposed to cross first. Initially, under the witness of the police chief, county magistrate, and township head, it was agreed with the ferry owner that the automobile would always cross first... Hey, boatman? Is the ferry owner here?" the driver demanded, eyes wide open again. "What ridiculous talk is this? Who's scared of the county magistrate or the police chief? First come, first served is the eternal rule. What's wrong? We arrived four hours ago. Are you telling us to spend the day here? You<77> can go to Koksan twelve times even if you cross later. We have to travel for days. Just honk your horn a bit more often. Ridiculous... Just because you're carrying some Western-suited folks. Hope the automobiles overturn into the river..." one oxcart driver said, tucking up his clothes and squaring off as if to charge, while all the other oxcart drivers voiced their support in unison. The driver, helpless, now started arguing vehemently with the boatman. Shouting about not being able to cross, demanding the ferry owner be brought, he caused a commotion. While the boatman mumbled that the owner had gone far away, the oxcart was already loaded. "Don't waste time, let's cross. What's the problem with the first person here crossing first?" they shouted, and with a "Heave ho," pushed the boat off. No matter how much they yelled, what could be done? The boat was already in the middle of the river. The driver fumed alone, the passengers chuckled, and I, honestly, sympathized with the oxcart drivers. I sympathized with their words: "Is an automobile all that? Are we supposed to fear the county magistrate or police chief? Just because you carry Western-suited people? Honk more, and you can go twelve times. First come, first served is the eternal rule." More than that, seeing this also as a kind of class struggle made it even more interesting. After waiting for two ferry trips and crossing, I saw the oxcart drivers already enjoying lunch at the Kitan inn (歧灘店), some boasting, some laughing.

High Mountains, Deep Valleys, Also Have Affection
About 3 *ri* from Koksan town, at a place called Ch'udong, I got off the car, and my acquaintance, Mr. Kim Ch'ang-hyŏn, along with three church members, came to meet me. The kindness of meeting like old friends, the innocent village fathers and brothers, truly made me feel they were my own father and brothers. Like the words of a certain French priest, the warmth of Korean people's affection is indeed admirable. Though walking the grassy path at night was not inconvenient, they guided me from the front with torches and guarded me from behind – where else could such consideration be found? Although the village house's inner room might seem modest, the host and hostess were both so kind, could even one's own family home be better than this? Moreover, catching a chicken, boiling *miljebi* soup (wheat dough flake soup), and even serving rice wine – where else could such deep affection be found? Murmuring "May you be blessed" as a silent prayer, I spent the night, and the next day, I came to Koksan town. I met Kim Yŏng-hwan, Kim Myŏng-ho, and several other acquaintances.
Koksan town hadn't changed much. Only the feeling of having come during the planting season and returning during the harvest season was different. The yellowing rice in the upper and lower fields was the change, if any. Talking back and forth about the suspension details, church disputes, etc., with several acquaintances, I didn't notice the night deepening. The next day, I went hunting along the Yongbong Stream and explored other scenic spots nearby, unaware of the day ending and night deepening. The day after that, I sought out the old Munsŏng fortress and enjoyed time with Song Mun-hwan and several village brothers and juniors. Again, we ate chicken and *miljebi*, and even better were the wild grapes and peaches, and also the corn. With high mountains and deep valleys, and even the autumn rain falling steadily, how could there not be traveler's sorrow? But I didn't feel it at all. The pleasure of associating with the<78> elders and brothers of the farming village was truly like lying in a sweet dream in my old home, warm and caring, making it difficult to describe the state of my heart. After spending a day, and then another, I reluctantly left that place, and with two companions, traveled 40 *ri* further to Sinp'yŏng. It's a major village between Koksan and Yangdŏk, with a Public Common School, a Financial Cooperative, a police substation, a township office, several hundred houses, and rocky mountains and clear streams – truly a scenic spot. "Beautiful scenery, beautiful people" could be seen here too.

When Police Ride, There's No Passenger Limit
Regardless of the place, the lawless swaggering of the long-sword faction (長釰派 - derogatory term for police/officials) has long been an issue, but this time in Koksan, I saw that unpleasant sight again. They said there were military exercises on the Koksan pass. Majors, captains, privates – they were all pouring in, causing a commotion among the officials and people of Koksan. Reaping grain not even half ripe, collecting money almost door-to-door to butcher cows and pigs, going out 3 or 5 *ri* to welcome them – truly, as if greeting victorious troops, everyone was bustling about without really knowing why. I had planned to walk through Suan to Sŏhŭng, but unfortunately, due to illness, I had to take an automobile back to P'yŏngsan. Since I had a pre-purchased ticket, I boarded without hesitation. More people boarded, and then more, until the car was packed beyond capacity. Despite the car being full, about three members of the long-sword faction boarded again. The car owner mentioned it was full and politely asked them to take the next car. But they acted as if they hadn't heard. "We are police officers. And we're going to meet Imperial soldiers, so shut up," they seemed to say, just pushing their way in. The car owner, as if thinking, "Whose order is it? Don't say anything, or you'll get hit," just let them be. Even if they had reservations, aren't they the ones responsible for cracking down on those who exceed the passenger limit? And wasn't there enough time to take the next car? In the end, including the driver, 12 people were aboard. Before going even 1 *ri*, a tire burst. On even small hills, everyone had to get out and push. I was so angry; how hateful those sword scabbards looked. Look here. Automobiles always stop in front of the police substation (駐在所). A policeman inspects it. If it's over capacity, he immediately yells "Baka!" (idiot) and tickets the driver. And he pulls out the excess passengers. That's how it is. Exceeding the limit is against the law. But look. That day, 12 people rode, so does that mean the capacity was 12? No. Perhaps the constitution of the Great Japanese Empire has a clause saying, "Policemen may board unconditionally, exceeding capacity or regardless of any circumstances." It must be so. Such sights are seen everywhere. This wasn't the first time. <79>

Is the Governor Responsible, or the County Magistrate?
Just as fast as automobiles and trains are, my travelogue jumps along just as quickly. Arrived in Sariwŏn. Saw hundreds of students lined up in the station square. Are they going on a trip? No. Their attire doesn't suggest that. Are they welcoming someone? Right. It seems so. Did some big shot come and go? The Governor-General? No. Then the school principal? No. They say the Provincial Governor came and went. That must be it. His Excellency the Provincial Governor! In old terms, the Observer, His Excellency the Provincial Chief! Eek, scary. Of course. They must cancel all classes, mobilize all male and female students, and respectfully see him off for ten *ri*, a hundred *ri*. That's the duty of the people and the students' role. If they don't, big trouble will follow. Later, it would be difficult to get even one clerk position, one township secretary job. It absolutely must be done...
But listen. When I came into town and inquired, I found out he didn't come specifically for Sariwŏn, nor to the county office, nor to the school. He was just passing through on his way to Hwangju or somewhere, stopping briefly at the County Office. And for such a governor, they caused such a huge fuss, canceling classes, dragging hundreds of students through the dusty streets back and forth – there was much criticism. They say it was an order from the county office. A call came from the county office to the agricultural school too: "Governor departing, lead students for send-off." The authorities at the agricultural school, perhaps having a bit more sense, reportedly refused.
Regardless of the personage, welcoming warmly and sending off sincerely is not necessarily a bad virtue. However, canceling classes and dragging out numerous young students to make such a fuss is better avoided. The Governor himself should restrain this. Doing so doesn't add to or subtract from the Governor's dignity. The people, on the contrary, laugh. There is no better official than one who eliminates hardships for the people, and no worse official than one who imposes burdens on the people. The County Magistrate is the same. Perhaps he did such a thoughtless thing thinking it was a subordinate's responsibility towards a superior, but doing so doesn't guarantee a promotion, nor will failing to do so get him fired. Just following the normal path and fulfilling one's actual responsibilities is enough. No need for lengthy sermons, let me just ask once more. Did the Governor ask for this to be done? Or did the County Magistrate do it voluntarily? That is the question. Let me ask once more to all the so-called officials throughout Chosŏn.

Passing Through Four Counties to Hometown
My body feels extremely weary. The Chinam chicken given by Mr. Kim Chin-*<80> tastes bland, the opposite sex introduced by Mr. Kim Mun-hwan holds no interest, and the famous Pongsan apples from Mr. Ch'oe Yong-hwan aren't refreshing. Climbing Kyŏngam Mountain offers no particular pleasure, and the yellowing rice fields of Yŏmulp'yŏng don't particularly inspire desire. The feeling of having nothing outside oneself arises strongly. Let's go quickly. Let's go to the hometown. Let's be an idler there for a bit. - A brief stop in Hwangju and P'yŏngyang, then I must go quickly. - Thus, I arrived in Hwangju. As soon as I settled in the inn, a fever started. My limbs began to contract. I had to tell the friend I had met after so long to leave. The pain intensified minute by minute. "Ah! I'm dying!" a tragic feeling arose. But then, the final courage of "Me, die? Does that make sense?" emerged. I clenched my fists and abruptly stood up. But I immediately collapsed. There was nothing to be done. A midnight guest in Hwangju Castle, already aimless, now afflicted with illness too. What to do! Eventually, I woke the person sharing the room, woke the owner, and finally sent someone to the Chŏngbang Clinic to borrow two injections of "mohi" (morphine?) from Dr. Yi To-je, and only then did I revive.
Now, let's go. Let's go straight to the hometown village and be done with it. Still, I couldn't just pass by P'yŏngyang and Anju. But what pleasure could there be for an ailing body even if I stopped by? Still, I forced myself to visit both places. But I have no impressions, no observations. Even if I did, I don't feel like writing them.
With my body suffering, the dream of the hometown village isn't sweet either. The Ch'usŏk holiday is also bothersome. However, the memory of eating early-harvest rice cakes with my younger siblings under our mother's roof – that alone remains fresh.

Wandering for Thirty Thousand Years
Perhaps because it's the place where I was born, there seems to be much affection and kindness. My body felt a bit better. I had no choice but to leave again. On the 17th day of the 8th lunar month, I went to T'aech'ŏn town. I spent a pleasant day with friends from the town. What pleased me most was that a Youth Association had newly been initiated where there was none before. I covered the 60 *ri* to Kusŏng on foot. "Wandering for thirty thousand years" (身遊三萬年) refers precisely to this. Kusŏng is commonly called "Kumanmyŏn" (Turtle Ten Thousand Years - 龜萬年), so there are many things in Kusŏng with the prefix "Man-nyŏn" (Ten Thousand Years). The inn is Mannyŏn-gwan, the bridge is Mannyŏn-gyo, and the temple is Mannyŏn-sa. Drinking three large bowls of Mannyŏn wine with Messrs. Paek Ŭng-gyu and Wŏn Myŏng-*, crossing the Mannyŏn Bridge with comrades Chŏn Ŭi-ch'an and Kim To-hyŏn, going to Mannyŏn-gwan, and then sightseeing at Mannyŏn-sa was quite delightful, feeling like I was "Wandering for thirty thousand years." Talking day and night with the 60-70 students of the Kusŏng training institute is also an unforgettable memory. Here too, there is still no news of tenant farming issues, labor problems, or even the youth movement. Only the beauty of human affection and the beauty of mountains and water exist.

Visiting Chŏngju, Kwaksan, Sŏnch'ŏn, Ch'ŏlsan to Yongch'ŏn
Arrived in Chŏngju, a famous place in North P'yŏngan. More than anyone, the famous General Hong Kyŏng-nae comes to mind. The Imjin War (Japanese invasions of 1592-1598) is also recalled. Inside and outside the South Gate<81> have somehow become Japanized. The feeling of "How turbulent the winds and waves, bringing much sorrow to the visitor" arises. The line "Where to seek the General's heroic spirit? Inside and outside the castle walls, Japanization" also forms in my mind. I looked for Mr. Kim Kong-sŏn. I visited the Ch'ŏndogyo Central Administrative Office (宗理院). I met Hong Chong-hyŏn,
Sŏ In-hwa, and several other comrades. Discussing church issues, rural problems, youth and children's issues day and night, I left for Kwaksan the next day.
Originally an Independence Army county (獨立郡), now living as a dependent of Chŏngju, Kwaksan evokes a special, poignant sympathy. Meeting comrades Kye Yŏn-jip
and Yi Kuk-yŏng at the station head, everything seemed peaceful again. Moreover, encountering the nonchalant Kim Sang-sŏl and the comrade Shin U-kwŏn, who spares neither himself nor others, in succession, my shoulders shrugged with the thought of having a good time. Added to this, the host was kind, the octopus tasted good, and the *soju* was also fine, so I opened my heart wide. Acting like a traveling peddler (돌님장사), I spent a day with several comrades, repeating the stories I told in other counties, and then dashed off again to Sŏnch'ŏn.
Arrived in Sŏnch'ŏn, a famous center for Protestant Christianity (耶蘇敎). I visited the Ch'ŏndogyo Central Administrative Office and looked for Mr. Yi Kun-o's house. The neatness of the streets and the distinct Japanese style give visitors a favorable impression. Seeing more students in school uniforms and women with braided hair compared to other counties is especially welcome. It seems to be thanks to the church. Commerce is prosperous and there are many wealthy people, which is said to be a characteristic of Sŏnch'ŏn. Again, playing the role of the traveling peddler, I left and went to Ch'ŏlsan.
Arrived in Ch'ŏlsan, said to have fierce people, and also known as Chŏng-ga's (鄭哥 - likely refers to Jeong Yeo-rip or a similar figure?) town. It's the day of the Shinto shrine enshrinement festival (神宮鎭座祭). Five-colored flags hang like cobwebs in the town. Perhaps because it's a fierce town, the shrine festival is also celebrated with fierce enthusiasm. The Public Common School is holding a large athletic meet. It's also bustling. With Messrs. Pak Yŏng
and Chŏng Ch'ung-sŏk, I toured the town, and at night, fulfilled my possessed mission by talking extensively. After getting drunk on Ch'ŏlsan white liquor, I left early in the morning for Yongch'ŏn.
At the Kaebyŏk Branch Office in front of Namsi Station, I heard the good news of Kaebyŏk's suspension being lifted through the newspaper and went to Yongch'ŏn, the granary of North P'yŏngan. Passing through villages, seeing two or three large tile-roofed houses (瓦家) each time, and seeing the endless yellowing rice fields when passing the Fuji Farm (不二農場) were new records.
The town of Yongamp'o is not yet a well-organized town. However, the boundless grain in the upper and lower fields, its position pillowed by the thousand-*ri* long river, and its view overlooking the great sea make it a place of considerable value in both quality and quantity. It's a corner of West Chosŏn, and the lack of convenient transportation is regrettable, but with both water and land routes available, what more could one ask for? Congratulations to Yongch'ŏn. I heard no news of ideological movements or youth movements.
At the Ch'ŏndogyo church, I performed the usual act (例の 一幕 - likely referring to giving talks or spreading his views) and enjoyed myself until late at night in Mr. Kim Sa-t'aek's room with Messrs. Yi Yu-jŏng,
Kim Chŏk-hong,
and Yi Pŏn-kwan. <82> The next day, I became a passenger boarding a boat on the Yalu River, heading for Sinŭiju.

Seeing New and Old Ŭiju, and the Return Journey
Arrived at the border city, Sinŭiju. Checked into the Sinsŏng Inn. The owner, Mr. Yi Chin-ja, wasn't there. Asking "主人何處去" (Where did the host go?), I was told he went to watch the Public Common School athletic meet. Thinking I might meet acquaintances and also watch, I went to the athletic field and met Messrs. Im Hyŏng-kwan
and Chŏn Tŭk-hyŏn at the entrance. Shortly after, they announced a break for lunchtime. On the way back, I met the branch manager, Mr. Paek Yong-gu, for the first time. Leading three or four acquaintances back to the inn, the same maid from three years ago recognized me and welcomed me warmly. Following the principle of "丈夫 逢輒飮" (A man should drink whenever he meets someone), we each drank 6 or 7 cups, and host and guests became cheerful.
After dinner, we went to Andong Prefecture (安東縣 - Antung, now Dandong, China) and watched the border youth joint speech contest. The speakers' lack of content and the audience's off-target heckling ("yaji" - 야지) were somewhat disappointing compared to expectations. More than that, Mr. Kim U-yŏng's announcement from the podium regarding "Notes on judging" (番判에 관한 주의) (as it was an audience vote) was truly embarrassing, like porridge instead of rice. When the meeting ended, it was 11 o'clock. I urged returning to Korea, but my friends protested, saying how could a visitor to a foreign country for the first time leave just like that, and eventually dragged me to some restaurant. We ate heartily, and still feeling it wasn't enough, sought out some *kisaeng* house (妓房) to see the female entertainers. Oh, how shocking. Ten, twenty, however many there were, all came in to present themselves. If you can't say who you like, they tell you to point with your finger. When I pointed to one among the dozen or so, the remaining rejected girls ran off as if embarrassed. Five or six foreign men sharing drinks with one foreign woman – how laughable must it be? Laughing and talking for a minute, we gave 1 Won and left. Should this also be called worldly experience (經世)?
The next day, I went to Old Ŭiju (舊義州). Only the provincial office was gone; the town streets were the same. The T'onggun Pavilion (統軍亭) was also intact. However, it seemed to have an air of old age and decline. After glancing around the streets with Messrs. Kim Cha-il and Hwang Ha-sik, I stopped by the branch office to discuss the future with Mr. Pak Yun-wŏn, then accompanied Hwang Kŭm (?) for a bowl of the border specialty, cold noodles (凉麵), whose taste was just so.
At night, I spent time with many comrades discussing the usual topics, and the next day, returned to Sinŭiju. From here, the journey home begins. <83>

Great Ignorance Amidst Great Knowledge
It's okay not to mention this, but let's just have a mutual laugh. Thinking that the lifting of Kaebyŏk's ban might generate some significant sympathy, I decided, swallowing my pride, to try and solicit congratulatory advertisements. It wasn't that there wasn't a telegram from the head office urging my immediate return, but there were so many hardships that I was putting on a brave face. Forcing the busy Mr. Paek to accompany me, I went around seeking out certain influential figures (有志) in Sinŭiju. The one going is hesitant, but how difficult must it be for the hosts? (Originally not possessing great wealth, and then being pestered every other day for some donation, some sympathy, some advertisement – how could they endure it? Truly, the hardest role is being an influential figure. Let's assume both sides understand this situation.)
After visiting several influential figures, we sought out Mr. Ch'oe OO, reputed to be one of the leading figures in Sinŭiju's legal circles. With words that wouldn't come out easily, I finally stated our purpose and asked for some congratulatory contribution, regardless of the amount. Perhaps it was a momentary misunderstanding, but his reply was, to put it impolitely, enough to make one's jaw drop. Namely, "Is there even a congratulation for a suspension being lifted? Wouldn't it attract suspicion from the authorities (當局)?" So, I explained that couldn't be right, it was common practice everywhere, almost providing evidence. But he insisted on his ignorance, saying, "Still, I haven't seen congratulations for a ban being lifted. It really seems like it would attract suspicion from the authorities." This is what I mean by great ignorance amidst great knowledge. He was a former judge, a current lawyer, and also one of the prominent figures in Sinŭiju's legal community. I believe it was a momentary misunderstanding. Let's both have a laugh.-<84>

**Summary**

The author, Ch'unp'a, recounts his 50-day journey through Hwanghae and P'yŏngan provinces (the "Two Wests") in late 1925, undertaken after the temporary suspension of the magazine "Kaebyŏk". Motivated by a mix of necessity (finding food) and purpose (visiting friends, spreading "the Doctrine" - likely Ch'ŏndogyo), he details his travels, observations of local life, and encounters. The narrative includes anecdotes like a conflict between an automobile driver and oxcart drivers highlighting class tensions, criticism of the obsequious mobilization of students for visiting Japanese officials, and the arbitrary behavior of the colonial police ("long-sword faction"). He interacts with various individuals, including Ch'ŏndogyo members, discusses local conditions, and notes the nascent state of social movements in some areas while appreciating the natural beauty and human warmth he finds. The trip concludes with the news of Kaebyŏk's ban being lifted and reflections on encounters in the border city of Sinŭiju, including a frustrating meeting with an overly cautious lawyer.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into Korean society under Japanese rule in the mid-1920s. It vividly portrays the friction between modern transport and traditional livelihoods, the heavy-handedness of colonial administration and policing (including arbitrary power and demands for shows of loyalty), the activities and network of the Ch'ŏndogyo movement in the provinces, and the general atmosphere of surveillance and caution, even among the educated elite. The author's personal reflections and critical tone provide a contemporary Korean perspective on the colonial experience beyond official narratives.

기사형태	기행문

兩西 五十日 中에서
						春坡
『開闢』은 停刊되고 心火는 불숙 나고 먹을 것은 업고-하니 안이 떠날 수 잇스랴. 밥도 엇어 먹을 겸 구경도 할 겸 친구도 차자 볼 겸 主義도 선전할 겸 겸사 겸사로 떠나게 되얏다. 解停이 되야 歸社의 急電이 잇기까지는 한 달이고 두 달이고 반년이고 일년이고 그냥 도라단니리라. 아무리 못낫기로 밥이야 못 엇어 먹으랴. 가자. 아무데든지 가자. 「뽕두 딸 겸 님두 볼 겸.」이라고 밥도 엇어 먹을 겸 부모님도 뵈올 겸 고향 근처로 먼츰 가자. 黃海道에 들녀 멧멧 친구를 찻고 平南에 들녀 멧멧 곳을 보고 平北에 가서 멧달을 지우다가 幸히 解停의 喜報가 오면 즉 上來하고 그럿치 안으면 南滿洲로 가든지 南朝鮮으로 가든지― 그러케 하리라. 이리하야 나는 예정지를 꼽아 보앗다. 먼츰 金川 谷山을 단녀 遂安 瑞興으로 해서 鳳山 黃州로 그리하야 평양 平原으로 安州 博川을 단녀 가지고 泰川 鄕里에서 추석 名日이나 지내가지고 龜城 定州로 宣川 鐵山으로 龍川 義州로- 여긔까지 갓다가 路次變하게 되면 南滿洲로 가고 그럿치 안으면 朔州 昌城으로 碧潼 楚山으로 渭原 江界 등 강변 7읍을 단녀서 熙川으로 나와서 雲山 寧邊을 보고 价川에 와서 다시 결정하자- 여긔까지에도 解停의 報가 업스면 내다른 거름에 順川, 德川, 陽德, 孟山, 成川, 江東 등지를 도라 평양에 와서 또 결정하리라. 여긔까지에도 소식이 업스면 (설마 이 때까지야 안되랴 하면서도) 이제는 鎭南浦로 해서 殷栗, 長淵, 瓮津, 海州 등 海西 諸邑을 구경하고, 開城 長端으로 해서 上京하리라. 이때까지에도 解停이 못된다 하면 이번에는 忠淸 全羅 慶尙 등 3南 3道로 내려가 이리 뛰고 저리 뛰리라. 그러리라. 아무 걱정이 업네- 하고 爲先 西鮮을 향하야 떠나니 그 날이 9 월 7일이엿다. 떠날 때에 敗北한 졸병 갓치 엇재 좀 섭섭한 것은 사실이엿다. (兩西 50일을 追日 회고하자면 실로 未遑하다. 후덕떡 후덕떡 뛰기로 한다.)

運轉手와 牛車軍
떠나든 當日에 金川에 들녀 柳益秀씨를 訪하야 敎會事를 말하고 翌日 南川驛에 내려 오래 간만에 냉면 한 그릇을 맛보고 곳 자동차로 谷山 17리를 直入케 되얏다. 南川서 3리 쯤 가닛가 禮成江 渡船場이 잇다. 여긔서부터 구경의 第一幕이 열닌다. (이런 수가 툭툭 떠러저야 구경 단닐 맛이 잇겟다.) 무언고 하니 牛車軍 對 運轉手 항쟁이다.
원래 南川 谷山 간 直通 新作路요 兼 渡船場인지라. 去來 徒步客은 毋論 牛車, 자동차, 自行車-실로 여간한 복잡이 안이다. 자동차가 강변에 닷고 보니, 발서 荷物牛車만 10여 척이 와서 爭先하야 渡船하는 중이다. 運轉手는 先着한 만흔 牛車를 요리 죠리 피하야 江口 제3위까지에 운전해노앗다. 더 드러갈 여지가 업슴에 부득이 압선 牛車 2척 만은 건너 간 뒤래야 자동차 차례가 되얏다. 約 한 시간은 걸닐 모양이다. 자동차 뒤에선 牛車軍들의 氣色이 이상해진다. 牛車 한아가 건너가고 또 한아 저 배에 실엇다. 이번 갓다 오면 의례 자동차라고 튼튼히 미덧다. 웬걸 뒤에 섯든 牛車가 자동차를 슷치고 내려 몰닌다. 자동차 압흐로 2척이나 넌즈시 와 노인다. 운전수와 牛車軍 사이에 言爭이 니러난다. 「여보 이게 무슨 즛이요. 압선 자동차를 보면서 牛車를 내려 모는 것은」하고 운전수가 눈을 딱 바로 떳것다. 牛車軍은 이 말은 드른 체도 안하고 「여보게 어서-어」하고 소를 챗직질하야 그냥 내려 끈다. 운전수 골이 낫것다. 자동차에 탓든 손님들도 골이 낫것다. 나 亦 처음은 불쾌한 생각이 낫다. 「여보, 자동차가 먼츰 건너가요. 뒤로 내뽑아요.」 하고 운전수는 牛車軍 각가이가며 호령하다십히 삑 소리를 친다. 「이것 왜 이래. 자동차면 그만인 줄 안담. 우리는 세 시간, 네 시간 전부터 와서 기달려. 왜 이래. 시방 온 것이 별 걱정 다 만흐이」 하고. 牛車軍은 허럿띄를 졸나매며 눈을 흘기며 「여보게, 뒤엣 소들도 어서 내려 모라」하며 톡톡히 덤비여 볼 작정이다. 「如何한 경우임을 不拘하고 자동차가 먼츰 건네는 법이야. 當初에 경찰 서장, 군수, 면장, 立會 下에 언제든지 자동차는 먼츰 건네기로 배ㅅ주인과 結約을 햇서... 여보 사 공? 배ㅅ주인 업소」하고 운전수 한 번 더 눈을 딱 바로 떳다. 「이게 무슨 어림 업는 소리냐. 군수, 경찰서장이라면 누가 무서워 하드냐. 먼저 온 놈이 먼저 건네는 것이 萬古通例야. 왜 이래. 우리는 네 시간 전에 왓서. 그래 여긔서 날을 지우란 말이냐. 너는＜77＞ 잇다 건네도 谷山 열두 번도 가-. 우리는 멧츨을 가야 돼. 고동만 좀 자조 틀려무나. 어림도 업시... 양복쟁이나 태웟노라고. 자동차들 江에 뒤업흘나...」하고 한 牛車軍이 옷자락을 것어차며 달겨들 듯이 對立을 하닛가 모든 牛車軍은 일시에 聲援을 한다. 運轉手 할 수 업시 이제부터는 사공과 야단이다. 배를 못 건네느니 배ㅅ주인을 불너 오라느니 들구 야단이다. 사공은 배ㅅ주인 멀니 갓다고 어물어물하는 새에 牛車는 발서 실엇다. 「시간 지체말고 어서 건네요. 먼츰 온 사람 먼츰 건네는데 무엇이 걱졍이야」하고 「으얏차 드얏차」 배를 뗀다. 아무리 아우성을 한 들 奈何오. 배는 발서 중류에 떳다. 운전수 혼자 불눅거리고 손님들은 희죽 희죽 웃고 나는 정말이지 牛車軍들에게 동정해섯다. 「자동차면 그만이냐. 군수서장이라면 무서워 하드냐. 양복쟁이나 태웟노라고. 고동만 좀 더 잽시 틀면 열두 번도 가. 先着者 先渡는 萬古通例」 등 말에 동정 하얏다. 그것보다도 此亦 일종의 階級戰으로 보아서 더욱 흥미가 잇다. 두 배나 묵어서 건너와 보닛가. 牛車軍들은 발서 歧灘店에서 맛잇게 점심하면서 혹은 壯談하고 혹은 웃더라.

山高谷深 亦有情
谷山邑 距 3里許 楸洞이라는 곳에 하차하니 知友 ㅁ,金昌玄군이 세 분 敎友와 갓치 마저 준다. 一面 如舊의 친절 無垢한 村父村兄은 실로 我父我兄의 感이 생긴다. 佛國 엇던 神父의 말과 갓치 朝鮮 사람의 인정미란 과연 감탄치 안을 수 업다. 草路夜行이 거북치 안은 바 안이나 횃불로써 압흘 인도하고 뒤를 경계해 주니 이런 感心할 데가 어대 잇스며 村家內室이 비록 체면 적은 듯 하나 主父主母가 아울너 친절하니 親家인들 이에서 더하랴. 더구나 닭을 잡고 밀제비국을 끌이고 飯酒까지 주니 이런 厚情이 어대 또 잇스랴. 「多福하옵소서」하고 곱집어 暗祝하면서 一夜를 지내고 翌日은 谷山邑에 왓다. 金泳煥, 金明昊 외 여러분 知友를 맛낫섯다.
谷山邑은 별로 변함이 업다. 播種시절에 왓다가 秋收시절에 또 온다는 感뿐이다. 上下 坪의 黃禾가 즉 變態라면 변태란 말이다. 停刊顚末, 敎會紛糾 등 說往說來로 멧멧 知友와 더부러 夜深함을 몰낫섯다. 翌日은 龍峯川邊에 川獵도 하고 시내 別處에 探景도 하면서 日暮夜深함을 몰낫섯다. 그 翌日은 舊文城中을 차자 드러 宋文煥 외 여러 村兄村弟와 즐기게 되얏다. 역시 닭을 먹고 밀제비를 먹고 더 조흔 것은 다래와 복숭아요 또 조흔 것은 玉수수이엿다. 山高谷深한데 秋雨까지 霏霏하니 客愁인들 업스랴 만은 도무지 몰낫섯다. 農村의＜78＞ 父老 형제와 사괴는 맛은 실로 故原의 달콤한 꿈자리에 누은 듯 알들 살들하야 그 心緖 여하를 形言하기도 어렵다. 하루 지내고 또 하로 지내서 哀然하나마 그 곳을 떠나 3인 동행 40리로 더 멀리 新坪을 갓섯다. 谷山 陽德間의 大巨里인데 公普校, 金融組合, 駐在所 面所 등 人家도 數百戶 이며 石山이며 淸川이 실로 山水勝區이다. 山水美人情美란 이 곳에서도 볼 수 잇다.

巡査탈 때는 無定員
何處를 불문하고 長釰派들의 無法하게 껏떡대는 꼴이야말로 발서부터 문제거리이지만 이번이 谷山에서도 또 그런 안이꼬운 꼴을 보앗다. 谷山 마루에 軍事연습이 잇다 한다. 대장이니 소장이니 졸병이니 막 드리 밀닌다고 谷山의 官民은 두루 법석인 이 때이다. 半實도 안된 곡식을 비며 戶別하다 십히 돈을 모아 牛豚을 잡으며 3里 5里를 불구하고 出迎을 하며 실로 勝戰軍이나 맛는 것 갓치 모다들 멋도 모르고 떠들석하는 이 때엿다. 나는 도보로 遂安을 단녀 瑞興으로 가랴든 것이 불행히 신병 때문에 다시 자동차로 平山으로 가게 되얏다. 豫買하얏든 표가 잇는 지라 서슴지 안코 승차하얏다. 또 타고 또 타고 하야 발서 정원 이상으로 차안이 빡빡하다. 車中이 만원임에 불구하고 長釰派가 적으만치 3명이나 또 오른다. 차 주인은 滿員을 말하며 다음 차로 가시라고 간절히 청한다. 그러나 드른 쳬도 안 한다. 「우리는 警官이요. 또 제국 軍人을 마주 가는 터이야 말 말어」하는 듯 그냥 비비고 드러 온다. 차 주인은 「누구의 令이냐 말 마라. 따구 마즐나」하는 듯 그냥 내버려둔다. 설사 예약이 잇섯다 해도 그네들은 정원 이외에 승차하는 자를 取締하는 책임자가 안이냐. 또 다음 차로도 시간 不及은 안이엿지? 결국 운전수 아울너 12인이 탓다. 1里를 못 가서 박퀴가 터진다. 조고만 고개도 반듯이 내려서 모다 드러븟터 밀고 간다. 골이 엇지나 나든지 그 놈의 칼자루가 엇지 그리 밉던지. 자 보란 말이다. 자동차는 언제든지 駐在所 前에는 정류하겟다. 巡査가 檢点을 하것다. 만약 滿員 이상이면 단바람 「빠가」하고 운전수에게 붓치것다. 그리고 정원 외의 인원을 끌어내리것다. 이럿컷다. 정외에는 못 타는 법이것다. 그런데 말이다. 읊� 날은 12인이 탓스니 정원이 12인이란 말이냐. 안이다. 大日本제국 헌법에 「巡査는 정원 이상 又는 如何한 경우임을 불구하고 무조건 乘車함」이란 그런 문구가 잇나 보다. 그런가 보다. 이런 꼴은 到處에서 보게 되것다. 이번이 처음 만은 안이다.＜79＞

責在知事乎郡守乎
자동차, 기차의 속력이 그만콤 빠른 것과 갓치 나의 紀行도 이만콤 뛴다. 沙里院 왓다. 停車場 광장에 列立한 수백명 학생떼를 보앗다. 여행을 가나? 안이다. 行裝들이 그것은 안이다. 누구를 환영하나? 올타. 그런 듯 하다. 엇던 굉장한 놈이 왓다 가노? 總督인가? 안이다. 그럼 교장인가? 안이다. 知事가 왓다 간다고 한다. 그러렷다. 道知事閣下 이젼 말로 觀察使, 道長官閣下! 엑크 무서워. 암 그럿치. 학과를 全廢하고 남녀학생을 총출동 식켜서 십리고 백리고 졍성것 奉送해야지. 그래야 백성의 도리요 학도의 본분이지. 만약 그럿치 안으면 큰일 나지. 잇다가 雇員 한아 面書記 한아도 어더하기 어럽지. 꼭 그래야 되야...
그런데 말이다. 시내에 드러와 아러 보닛가 沙里院을 위하야 온 것도 안이요, 군청에 온 것도 안이요, 학교에 온 것도 안이요, 黃州인가 어대로 가는 길에 잠간 郡廳에 들녓다 가는 知事를 그러케 굉장 야단스럽게 학과를 全廢하고 멧 백명 학도를 끌고 塵煙萬丈의 시가로 왓다갓다 한다고 비난이 만타. 군청의 名命이라고 한다. 농업학교에도 군청으로부터 「知事 출발 학생인솔 奉送」의 전화가 왓드란다. 農校당국자들은 그래도 좀 節이 낫든지 거절햇다고 한다.
如何한 인물임을 막론하고 졍성것 마저주고 졍성것 보내줌이 미덕이 안인 바는 안이다. 그러나 학과를 폐하고 多數한 어린 학생을 끌고 그러케 야단스럽게 할 것은 업는 것이 조켓다. 知事 자신이 制止해야 된다. 그런다고 知事위엄이 더하고 들할리 업다. 人民은 도리여 웃는다. 백성에게 폐단을 업시하는 것만콤 善官이 업고 백성에게 폐를 끼치는 것만콤 惡官이 업다. 郡守도 그럿타. 上官에게 대한 下官의 책임이라고 그런 主着업는 즛을 한 듯 하나 그런다고 한級 더 올나가는 것 안이요 그럿치 안는다고 목이 떠러질 리는 업다. 오직 常道를 밟아 실지 책임만 다하면 그만이다. 煩說을 다 할 것 업고 한번만 더 무러 둔다. 知事가 그러케 해달나고 하얏느냐. 郡守가 자진하야 그러케 하얏느냐? 이것이다. 한번 더 全조선 소위 官吏배들에게 무러둔다.

四郡을 것쳐 鄕里에 들다
몸이 몹시 괴롭다. 金振*군이 주는 진암닭도 맛이 업고＜80＞ 金文煥군이 소개하는 異性도 자미 업고 崔龍煥군이 주는 鳳山의 名苹果도 시원치 안타. 景岩山登도 별 快味가 업고 餘物坪 黃禾도 그닥 欲氣가 안난다. 身外 無物의 感이 밧작 생긴다. 어서 가자. 鄕里로 가자. 그 곳에서 閑良을 좀 하자.- 黃州 平壤을 잠간 단녀 어서 빨니 갈 밧게 업다.- 이리하야 黃州에 왓다. 여관을 정하자마자 身熱이 생긴다. 사지가 옴으려든다. 못처럼 맛난 친구를 가라고 하얏다. 分一分 더해 진다. 「아! 나 죽엇구나!」의 悲感이 생긴다. 그러나 「내가 죽다니 말이 되나」의 최후 용기가 생긴다. 주먹을 쥐고 벌덕 니러낫다. 그러나 당장 쓰러졋다. 별 수가 업다. 黃州城中 夜半客이 가지도 無聊한데 또 病苦 로구나. 엇지 할고! 결국 同宿人을 깨우고 주인을 깨우고 그리다 못하야 正方의원에 사람을 보내여 李道濟군의 「모히」 注射 2放을 빌고서야 살아낫다.
이제는 가자. 直去 鄕村하고 말자. 그래도 平壤과 安州는 그져 지낼 수 업다. 들닌둘 병 든 몸이 무슨 快事가 잇스랴. 그래도 억지로 두 곳 다 들니긴 햇다. 그러나 소감, 소견은 아무 것도 업다. 잇다 해도 쓰기가 실타.

身遊 三萬年

大有識 中 大無識
이 말은 안해도 죠흐나 彼此에 한 번 웃고 말자는 것이다. 開闢해금이라는 데서 무슨 큰 동정이나 생길가 하야 염치 무릅쓰고 축하광고를 어더 보기로 하얏다. 本社로부터 急上來의 전보가 업는 바는 안이지만 하두 고통되는 점이 만하서 生비위를 내세는 판이것다. 無暇한 白군을 억지로 끌러가지고 新義州시내 某某 有志를 찻는 판이다. 가는 자도 구구하지만 주인된 인들 여간 괴로우랴.
