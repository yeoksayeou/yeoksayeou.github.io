Holding a Corpse
						Pa In

Ice sheets are splitting. Upon the Tuman River's water,
In the pu-re whi-te snow, tofu-like sheets of ice split finely.
In the bl-ue starlight, buffeted by the ice flow, they froth and stream.

Perhaps because dawn has already broken, the wind is harsher by the riverside. In the mountain valley across the river, the sound of dry branches splitting never ceases.
How to cross? Hiding footprints, over this river? Though by day, even the ice sheets might melt.

Would there not be soil somewhere? Just a handful of soil?
If not for those words, wanting even the bones to go to their own land,
Couldn't this corpse be buried even in this sandy field? So it wouldn't be unearthed even by the wind.
Lifting my eyes, the old mountains and rivers still beckoning<6>
Seem to call all, the dead souls and the living corpses.
My friend, who will be embraced in that bosom in broad daylight, how many could there be?

Ah, my friend's corpse grows colder and colder. In the arms holding it, strength drains away.
Must I truly step on ice fragments and cross on a single log bridge?
These footsteps, unable to go forward or back! On the corpse, only the snowstorm
Piles up.

When it piles, I shake it off; when shaken, it piles again.
Does the white snow intend to become earth and hold the funeral in this heart?
This heart-wrenching scene, trembling and trembling all night long!

Now even the dawn brightens. This dawn sky, where one cannot even stand by the riverbank.
Upon the river water, the stars still sit, playing on the glacier.
Am I meant only to sprinkle tears on my friend's dead face?
Ah, sorrowful river water!<7>
<6-7>

**Summary**

This poem depicts the desperate and sorrowful journey of a speaker carrying the corpse of a comrade across the frozen Tuman River at dawn. Battling the biting cold, the treacherous ice, and overwhelming despair, the speaker laments the inability to reach their homeland ("old mountains and rivers") to bury the friend in native soil, as desired. The corpse grows cold, strength fails, and the only solace is the falling snow, which seems to mock the desire for burial. The poem powerfully conveys themes of loss, displacement, the harshness of exile, and a deep yearning for home. From a historical perspective, the poem reflects the grim realities faced by Koreans during the Japanese colonial period, possibly hinting at the struggles of exiles, refugees, or independence fighters operating near the Manchurian border (Tuman River). The intense imagery of cold, death, and thwarted homecoming provides insight into the emotional and physical hardships endured under colonial rule.

屍體를 안고
						巴人
어름짱 갈닌다. 두만강 물 우엔
보-얀 힌 눈속에 두부모가튼 어름짱이 잘도 갈넌다
파-란 별비체 氷流에 쌔여 거품처 흐르면서

벌서 새벽이 된 타신가 강영엔 바름도 더 심하다. 강 건너 산골에는 마른아지 째지는 소리는 끗치지 안는데
어이 건너랴 발자최 감추며 이 강 우를, 대낫에는 어름짱도 풀니련만

흙이야 어대 업스랴 함 줌 흙즘이야
뼈라도 제땅에 가고십다는 그 말 아니면
이 백사벌엔들 시체야 못 무드랴 바람에도 안 패이게
눈을 들면 아직도 손질하는 녯산천이＜6＞
죽은 목숨 산송장 모다 오라 부르는 듯 한데
백일에 저 품속에 안길 내 동무야 그 몃치랴

아하, 동무의 시체는 점점 식어간다 안은 나팔로 맥이 풀려지며
아모래도 어름조각 드듸고 외자곡다리 건너단 말가
가도 오도못하는 이 발자최여, 송장에는 눈보래만
싸히는데

싸히면 떨고 떨면 또 싸히고
흰눈이 흙이되야 이 가슴에 장사 지낼셈가
밤내 떨고 또 떨고 하는 애끗는 이 마당이여

인제는 새벽조차 밝어진다 강역에도 섯지 못할 이 새벽 한울이
강물우엔 여전히 별이 안저 氷河를 놀질하는데
동무의 죽은 낫에 눈물뿌리기로 일삼을셈가.
아하, 애닯은 강물이여!＜7＞
