기사제목	雪上逍遙 (Strolling on Snow)
필자	卞榮魯
기사형태	시 (Poem)

雪上逍遙
						卞榮魯

곱게 비인(虛) 마음으로
눈 위를 거르면
하얀 눈 위를 거르면
하얀 눈은 눈(眼)으로 들어오고
머리로 숨여들어가고
맘속으로 기어 들어가
붉든 사랑도 하얘지게 하고
검든 미움도 하얘지게 하고
푸르든 생각도 하얘지게 하고
누르든 의심도 하얘지게 한다ㅡ

어느듯 나도 눈이 되어
하하얀 눈발이 되어
꿈가티 이상한 춤을 추는
눈무리(雪群)에 싸히어
어느 붉은 봄나라를
어느 푸른 여름나라를 덥흐려
하얀 미친 노래를 부르며 나려간다.

幻怪한 곡선을 大空에 그리(劃)우면서
아름답든 『폼페이』을 내리덥든
『뻬스뷰스』의 재(灰)가티 ＜112＞

Strolling on Snow
						Pyŏn Yŏngno

With a heart beautifully empty (虛)
If I stroll upon the snow
If I stroll upon the white snow
The white snow enters through the eyes (眼)
Permeates into the head
Crawls into the heart
Making even reddish love white
Making even blackish hate white
Making even bluish thoughts white
Making even yellowish doubt white—

Before I know it, I too become snow
Become stark white snowflakes
Enveloped by the snow-crowd (雪群)
Dancing a strange dream-like dance
To cover some red spring country
To cover some blue summer country
I descend, singing a white mad song.

Drawing fantastic curves in the Great Sky (大空)
Like the ash (灰) from 『Pesŭbyusŭ』
That rained down, covering beautiful 『P’omp’ei』 <112>

**Summary**

The poem "Strolling on Snow" by Pyŏn Yŏngno describes a transformative experience while walking in the snow. The speaker's mind, initially empty, is purified by the whiteness of the snow, erasing strong emotions and thoughts symbolized by colors (red love, black hate, blue thoughts, yellow doubt). The speaker then merges with the snow, becoming part of a collective, dancing flurry intent on covering the vibrant "spring country" and "summer country," singing a "mad white song." The poem concludes with a striking simile comparing this descent to the volcanic ash from Vesuvius burying Pompeii. From a historical perspective, written during the Japanese colonial period, the poem's intense desire for purification and transformation through the overwhelming, almost destructive force of whiteness could reflect a yearning for a radical societal reset or an escape from the complex and often harsh realities of the time. The ambiguous ending, comparing the beautiful yet obliterating snow to volcanic ash, might interest historians exploring artistic expressions of underlying anxieties or potentially destructive impulses veiled in natural imagery during this era.
