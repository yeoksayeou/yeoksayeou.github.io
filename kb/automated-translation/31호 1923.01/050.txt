Article Title: 中國의 政治的 現勢와 社會的 現勢(現勢的 政治의 破産과 革命的 氣分이 漲溢한 社會의 現態) (China's Political Current Situation and Social Current Situation (The Bankruptcy of Current Politics and the State of Society Brimming with Revolutionary Sentiment))
Author: 北旅東谷
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Article)

China's Political Current Situation and Social Current Situation
						Pukryŏ Tonggok
One

It is an undeniable fact that the general situation in present-day China is facing the utmost danger. It is no exaggeration to say that the political current situation has reached utter bankruptcy, facing extreme entanglement and disorder that cannot be easily resolved. The social current situation, besieged by the heavy pressure of resistance and revolutionary sentiment stemming from political severity, seems poised for some kind of explosion or progression. Truly, there is an undeniable sense that we have reached a final moment where the situation is urgent and the winds and clouds are rapidly gathering day by day. Before describing the current situation, if we briefly review the general state of political changes since the revolution to see where the fundamental cause of the present entanglement originated, we can concisely glimpse the circumstances behind the current political scene. As the state of society, which is a reflection of political changes, will naturally be revealed, let's briefly divide the overview of political changes after the revolution into four periods:

(1) The Era of Power Struggle among Political Parties: This period extends from the establishment of the Nanking Government in the first year of the Republic until Yüan Shih-k'ai (袁世凱) formally assumed the presidency and dissolved the National Assembly. Regarding the Chinese revolution, I always feel not satisfaction and exhilaration, but rather displeasure <35> and inadequacy. Not only that, but I wish the moment of revolution had been a bit longer, or that it had received a more genuine baptism of revolution. After the Wuchang Uprising suddenly succeeded within a month, a provisional government was established in Nanking. Through a makeshift peace negotiation with the Ch'ing court (淸廷), the Ch'ing Emperor abdicated, and the Republic was ostensibly established. However, the fact that the revolutionary army, following Huang Hsing's (黃興) advocacy for war, could not disregard negotiations and directly charge into Peking to carry out a true revolution is truly regrettable for all time, especially when considering the current situation. When such negotiations, which could be called a defilement of the revolution's sanctity, began, the demonic hand of Yüan Shih-k'ai, a great sinner against the Republic, had already infiltrated. Under harsh conditions, deviating from the revolution's original intent and lacking a spirit of popular rule, Yüan Shih-k'ai formally assumed the presidency. When he became the leader of the Republic, the fundamental poison leading to the current entanglement was already conceived. Consequently, new figures with the spirit of the times and revolutionary vigor were forced into retirement, while the corrupt bureaucratic faction of the former Ch'ing (前淸) increasingly grasped political power. Political parties proliferated and vied with each other. Among the parties at the time, the Kuomintang (國民黨) (the successor to the Hsing Chung Hui (興中會) and the central organ during the revolution) and the Progressive Party (進步黨) were the largest. This was the time when these two parties contended for political power. For a while, the Kuomintang's influence was extremely strong, and many cabinet members were Kuomintang members, leading to a temporary appearance of a new atmosphere in the Republic. However, as the two parties struggled against each other, Yüan Shih-k'ai, harboring even greater ambitions, used cunning (奸滑) tactics to exploit the situation for his own gain, eventually leading to the outbreak of the Second Revolution. The Kuomintang was forcibly dissolved, the Progressive Party also fell into the tragic situation of mutual collapse, and the National Assembly effectively dissolved.

(2) The Era of Imperial Restoration Preparation—The Era of Warlord Embryogenesis: As the National Assembly was thus illegally dissolved, the power of the Kuomintang collapsed beyond recovery. Not only were many new figures from the revolutionary circle harmed by Yüan's poisonous hand, but Yüan himself monopolized all power in China. Although there was dissatisfaction among revolutionary figures, they were utterly unable to resist. Therefore, even though the Second Revolution occurred, it achieved no success. Instead, Yüan's power and base became even stronger. The blow to the revolutionary circle was beyond words, becoming even more severe than the oppression during the former Ch'ing era. Yüan, exercising pure dictatorship, threw the political situation into turmoil, increasingly eager to realize his fundamental ambition. Therefore, after the dissolution of the National Assembly, seizing the opportunity, he convened puppet political conferences (政治會議) and constitution conferences (約法會議) to enact a new constitution (新約法) and reform the official system, placing his confidants in key positions. He utilized warlords, <36> deploying them in various provinces and strategic points, solely aiming to secure his selfish interests, thereby fostering the fragmented situation of warlord power. These became the so-called current Provincial Inspectors General (巡閱使), Military Governors (督軍), and Garrison Commanders (鎮守使). Finally, when he attempted to forge the people's will (民意) to establish an imperial system, the political scene was purely dominated by the old bureaucratic faction of the former Ch'ing, effectively becoming a revival of the corrupt former Ch'ing government, though nominally the Republican government post-revolution. The Kuomintang faction and revolutionary figures like Sun Yat-sen (孫逸仙) and Huang Hsing were once again forced into exile abroad. Truly, not even a trace of the revolution could be seen. However, the revolutionary spirit was not dead. Ts'ai O (蔡愕) and Li Lieh-chün (李烈鈞) first rose in Yunnan (雲南), raising the banner of righteousness against the imperial system. The entire nation responded, ultimately shattering Yüan's "Hsin-hua Palace" (新華宮) spring dream. But even then, their righteous army failed to directly assault Peking and eradicate the den of the so-called pseudo-non-revolutionary faction. This was also because those who responded to Ts'ai and Li's army were largely motivated passively by their inability to defy their own power bases and interests, or the trend of the times and public sentiment, rather than being a truly intentional uprising. The harm caused by that imperial system attempt was immense. Internally, the order of society and the people fell into extreme chaos. Externally, the encroachment upon territory by Japan and foreign powers posed a great danger to the overall situation, especially the unreasonable plunder and harsh concessions made to Japan. Thus, although the imperial system was somehow overthrown, the internal and external situation remained critical. Moreover, the so-called Peiyang (北洋系) warlords were truly conceived, and thereafter, political power and all influence fell solely into the hands of the warlords. These are the current warlords of China.

(3) The Era of Warlord Ascendancy—This period spans from Li Yüan-hung's (黎元洪) succession until the Chihli (直隷派)-Anhwei (安徽省派) war. When Yüan Shih-k'ai attempted to establish the imperial system, he placed his confidants in key positions, utilizing remnants of the former Ch'ing, unreasonable military men, shameless politicians, and other corrupt old figures from the former Ch'ing. At the same time, not only was the spirit of popular rule lacking, but the temporary political chaos was compounded by the nurturing of warlords who would cause great harm in the future. It is a fact that the fragmented situation of provincial military governors (督軍), the major cause of the current entanglement, was unknowingly and naturally fostered. At that time, under Yüan Shih-k'ai's sharp maneuvering, the central government's orders could still be executed to some extent, preventing absolute tyranny. After Li Yüan-hung succeeded him, Tuan Ch'i-jui (段祺瑞) followed in Yüan's footsteps, monopolizing power. At that time, Chang Hsün (張勳), serving as the Yangtze Inspector General (長江巡閱使), and Ni Ssu-ch'ung (倪嗣沖), the Anhwei Military Governor (安徽督軍), meddled in state affairs, <37> leading to the second dissolution of the National Assembly. The result was the fostering of a "Restoration" (復辟) movement behind the scenes, culminating in the reappearance of the Ch'ing autocracy in the Republic of China, even if only for three days. President Li Yüan-hung was forced to resign, and China's political situation entered an era of indescribable extreme chaos following the revolution. At this time, the National Assembly moved south to Kwangtung (廣東), raising the banner of "Protecting the Constitution" (護法). Sun Yat-sen, finding no other recourse, established a Military Government (軍政府) in Kwangtung to maintain the "Constitution-Protecting National Assembly" (護法國會) and sought to fulfill the original revolutionary intentions. This marked the beginning of the North-South split in China. Sun Yat-sen, with a few fervent comrades and empty hands, boldly carved out the six southern provinces (Kwangtung 廣東, Kwangsi 廣西, Yunnan 雲南, Kweichow 貴州, Szechwan 泗川, Hunan 湖南), creating a temporary power base. However, due to the influence of warlords and the maneuvering of several ambitious individuals, he was unable to realize his original aspirations. Meanwhile, in the North, after the failure of the Restoration movement, Tuan Ch'i-jui returned to the cabinet. He maneuvered to have Hsü Shih-ch'ang (徐世昌) accept the presidency as a puppet. The Anfu (安福俱樂部, organized by Tuan Ch'i-jui) Parliament replaced the Old Parliament. Attempting to implement a policy of military unification, he sent his confidant Wu Kuang-hsin (吳光新) to Szechwan to stir up trouble and appointed Ch'uan Liang-tso (傳良佐) as Hunan Military Governor, aiming to directly subjugate the Southern Military Government. From this point, the North and South engaged in mutual struggle for several years. Interrupted by warnings from the diplomatic corps, a North-South Peace Conference was convened in Shanghai (上海). However, due to mutual intransigence, it dispersed without any conclusion. At that time, China's political situation resembled the Warring States period or the era of the Five Hu and Sixteen Kingdoms, with warlords carving up territory. The overall situation inevitably descended into the greatest danger. The Anfu Clique in the Northern government, relying on Peiyang warlord leaders Tuan Ch'i-jui and Hsü Shu-cheng (徐樹錚), engaged in truly traitorous acts by contracting huge foreign debts, especially massive loans from Japan. Despite the nation's situation being driven daily to the brink of survival, such loan money not only filled private coffers but was entirely consumed in internecine struggles. In the Southern Military Government, the so-called influential Political Study Clique (政學系) (led by figures like Ts'en Ch'un-hsüan 岑春煊), relying on the power of Southwestern warlords Lu Jung-t'ing (陸榮廷, then Kwangsi Military Governor) and Xiong Kewu (態克武, Szechwan Military Governor), imposed unreasonable taxes and extorted wealth. Other provincial military governors and commanders-in-chief, under the pretext of unification and protecting the constitution, largely fulfilled their greedy ambitions at will. Thus, in China, anyone holding military power was supreme. Truly, it reached a nadir where even the authority of ancient emperors paled in comparison. The ascendancy of warlords reached its absolute peak. However, the Southern Military Government also suffered from daily internal strife and power struggles, its declining state becoming untenable. Moreover, it deviated from the original spirit and purpose of establishing the military government under the banner of protecting the constitution. Consequently, Sun Yat-sen moved to Shanghai. The military government at that time was directly bought off by the North <38> and even its name was abolished. Meanwhile, in the North, although Hsü Shih-ch'ang maintained the semblance of governing under the title of President, the mutual antagonism between the Chihli (直隷) and Anfu (安福) cliques finally erupted into war. As a result, the Anfu clique was completely crushed. Tuan Ch'i-jui was stripped of his posts and titles, and Hsü Shu-cheng and others fled into exile. The Anfu clique's maneuvering was utterly extinguished. However, the tyranny of the Chihli and Fengtien (奉天派) cliques continued unabated, if not increased, compared to before. The political situation remained entangled, with no possibility of resolution in sight.

(4) The Era of the Federal Provinces Movement: At that time, regardless of who won or lost, who fell or rose, the overall political situation in China was merely a chaos of power struggles, offering little hope for unification or order, no matter how the situation might change. In the North, the Anfu clique, which had solely disrupted the political scene, was completely defeated. The Southern Military Government, which had raised the banner of opposition, was also abolished, at least nominally. The activities of Sun Yat-sen's party (民黨) ceased. Superficially, it seemed North and South were unified under the command of the Northern Government. However, the overall situation only grew more chaotic. After Sun Yat-sen went into hiding in Shanghai, he quietly observed the times and foresaw renewed trouble in Kwangtung. He encouraged his comrades, secretly planned the recovery of Kwangtung, and ordered Ch'en Chiung-ming (陳炯明), who was lying low in Ch'ang-chou (漳州), Fukien (福建) province, commanding only a few battalions, to attack and occupy Kwangtung. He then revived the Military Government, seeking to achieve the original constitutional protection goal. Thus, within the two provinces of Kwangtung and Kwangsi, the influence of the Political Study Clique (政學系), i.e., the pro-North faction, was completely eliminated. Denouncing the illegal Northern government, Sun declared himself Extraordinary President and prepared directly for a Northern Expedition. In this way, the power of the Min Tang (民黨) was re-established in several southern provinces, forming a temporary power base, and the formally unified North and South were once again completely divided. At that time in the North, following the defeat of the Anfu clique, the rivalry between Chang Tso-lin's (張作霖) Fengtien clique and Ts'ao K'un's (曹鯤) Chihli clique persisted, resembling the situation of the Chihli-Anhwei struggle before the Anfu clique's defeat. Thus, the entire situation offered no prospect of stability, with warlords carving up territory as if several nations coexisted within the Republic's borders. Therefore, due to their own partitioned interests and conflicts, many remained unaffiliated with any side, maintaining a stance of independence and mutual observation. Consequently, the notion of Federal Provinces Autonomy (聯省自治) arose under this fine name. Movements for provincial constitutions and autonomy sprang up in Hunan (湖南), Chekiang (浙江), Szechwan (泗川), and Kwangtung (廣東). In Hunan province, the provincial constitution was already drafted, reviewed, and nearing implementation. This autonomy movement, ostensibly aligning with the times and public sentiment, was undeniably driven by the ambitions of various warlords seeking to solidify their own power bases and territories. <39> Thus, using the fine name of autonomy, they aimed only to realize their private schemes of partition. In this manner, the entire North-South situation was merely an era of partition by those holding military power and the contention of warlords. In the North, the Fengtien-Chihli struggle, continuing after the Chihli-Anhwei conflict, finally led to the recent Fengtien-Chihli War. In the South, due to Ch'en Chiung-ming's partitionist ambitions and his disagreement with Sun Yat-sen over the Federal Provinces Autonomy principle, coupled with the failure of the Northern Expedition forces, Sun Yat-sen was eventually expelled by Ch'en Chiung-ming and fled again to Shanghai. In the Northern Government, Hsü Shih-ch'ang was also ousted by the Chihli clique when the Fengtien clique was defeated. Simultaneously, under the temporary, obfuscating tyranny of the Chihli warlords, Li Yüan-hung was installed as the current President, seemingly perpetuating the current chaos in a state of ambiguity. At this point, the overall situation in China seems to have reached the pinnacle of extreme entanglement and disorder. It is a fact that this final state of chaos cannot be sustained as it is. Furthermore, considering the facts soon to be exposed, Chang Tso-lin, having already established the autonomy of the Three Eastern Provinces (東3省) and severed ties with the Northern Government, is actively preparing for war to decide the final outcome with the Chihli clique. In the South, Hsü Shu-cheng and Hsü Ch'ung-chih (許崇智, Sun Yat-sen's subordinate) invaded Fukien (福建) last month, establishing a base for some remnants of the Anfu clique and Sun Yat-sen's forces, poised to advance. Moreover, within the Chihli clique currently dominating the political scene, the opinions of Wu P'ei-fu (吳佩孚) and Ts'ao K'un (曹錕) are reportedly diverging daily, raising the possibility of internal struggle. Thus, this final moment is indeed fraught with imminent, drastic changes, the nature of which remains unknown.

Two

The above brief account outlines the political changes since the 11th year of the Republic. The first period was from the first year to the third year; the second from the third year to the sixth year; the third from the sixth year to the ninth year; and the fourth from the ninth year onwards to the 11th year, i.e., the present time. These periods of change, each lasting about three years, have followed one another. Seeing the increasing urgency and frequency of changes recently suggests that if this is not the final collapse, some rapid transformation is likely imminent. My observations on another country's politics might seem overly harsh or unsympathetic, but even based on the fair observations of the Chinese people themselves, there is little to add beyond this. Rather, it is natural that their situation, being in a revolutionary transition period, cannot avoid such chaos and entanglement. Furthermore, <40> without such extreme chaos during the transition, the foundation for complete new construction might not be solid. Having described the past trends, the current political situation can also be clearly understood. In the North, the power of the Chihli clique, essentially the domain of Ts'ao K'un (曹錕) and Wu P'ei-fu (吳佩孚), prevails, with figures from the President down to the cabinet ministers all emerging under their patronage. In the South, under the fine name of Federal Provinces Autonomy, a state of mutual partition currently exists. Since Ch'en Chiung-ming regained power in Kwangtung, he has joined hands with Wu P'ei-fu due to his territorial interests. Other southern provinces also seem to offer no significant opposition to the Chihli clique's commands, appearing relatively stable for now. Thus, the entire political power in China today, excluding Chang Tso-lin in the Three Eastern Provinces and a few other provinces, is monopolized by the Chihli clique. As for the cabinet, its stability cannot be trusted even for a few months, making it difficult to comment. However, the current Wang Ch'ung-hui (王寵惠) cabinet seems to have been formally established a few months ago as a victory for Wu P'ei-fu's exclusive backing and the so-called scholar/diplomat clique (슙�교계) (led by Yen Hui-ch'ing 顔惠慶, Wang Cheng-t'ing 王正廷, Wang Ch'ung-hui 王寵惠, etc.). Its members are predominantly "scholars," earning it the nickname "Cabinet of Doctors." In any case, this is the first time since the Republic's founding that a cabinet has been surrounded by new figures. While it might be capable of maintaining temporary precarious peace in the current political situation to some extent, it faces extreme financial distress and a host of other complexities. Moreover, the rampancy of various warlords leaves it powerless. Furthermore, the lack of clear, absolute approval from Ts'ao K'un's faction poses a major obstacle, both now and in the future, and it already seems to be suffering fatal blows repeatedly. Additionally, regarding the current situation, not only is the northern strongman Chang Tso-lin in a state of open challenge, but in the South, Chekiang (浙江) Military Governor Lu Yung-hsiang (盧永祥), originally from the Anfu clique and currently isolated, holds some power by joining hands with Chang Tso-lin and other anti-Chihli factions due to shared interests. Even elements of Sun Yat-sen's Min Tang possess intangible influence. The recent occupation of Fukien by Hsü Shu-cheng and Hsü Ch'ung-chih is a major issue in the current situation. Other numerous anti-Chihli activities operating in the shadows are most noteworthy. Truly, there is a sense of "The wind fills the tower before the mountain rain arrives" (山雨欲來 風滿樓).
From this, if we speculate on the future trends of the political situation, we can understand how the political scene might unfold now and in the future. First, regarding the Hsinhai Revolution, as briefly mentioned above, it is a fact that it did not receive a true revolutionary baptism nor achieve revolutionary fruition. Furthermore, since the founding of the Republic, the emergence of demons like Yüan Shih-k'ai, fundamentally contrary to the revolutionary spirit, prevented healthy progress, causing absolute harm both internally and externally. Moreover, they nurtured the Peiyang warlords, the main axis of the current political turmoil, <41> creating a situation of partition. Among these factors, the greatest cause is that the general populace had not universally awakened, which solely led to the present chaos. However, China's revolution has imperceptibly become a chronic revolution, allowing the period from Hsinhai to the present day to be viewed as one revolutionary transition. Why? Because the Wuchang Revolution of Hsinhai was a racial revolution directly opposing centuries of foreign conquest. The period from then until today has been an experimental process of self-renewal, aiming for a substantive revolution. This can be understood by reviewing the overview of political changes above; after the revolution, there was no direct new construction. All subsequent actions seem like processes during the revolution itself, or perhaps a maturation towards a true revolution. Since the current political situation is still climbing towards the peak of turmoil, it is impossible to permit even a precarious optimism about China's political future. Therefore, it can be predicted to some extent that a major transformation of the entire political scene will certainly occur before long. Firstly, the entire North-South situation can be broadly divided into two factions: the Chihli clique and the anti-Chihli clique. Further dividing these, the Chihli clique includes the Wu P'ei-fu faction (the so-called Loyang 洛陽派) and the Ts'ao K'un faction (保定派). Although Ts'ao K'un is formally the leader, his authority is nominal. The anti-Chihli clique primarily consists of: first, the old Anfu remnants (still a central force); second, the Chang Tso-lin faction; third, the Southern Min Tang faction. Besides these two main opposing forces, there are neutral factions (small part) and Federal Provinces Autonomy factions (small part). The future conflict will arise between the Chihli and anti-Chihli cliques. Within the Chihli clique, Wu P'ei-fu holds central power, monopolizing all authority in the current political scene. The anti-Chihli forces—namely, Sun Yat-sen's Min Tang, Tuan Ch'i-jui's Anfu remnants, and Chang Tso-lin's Fengtien clique—have already unified their stance and adopted an attitude of resistance. Apart from these, there are no other prominent voices or movements yet, but a major issue for the future lies with the various new figures, such as the Ch'en Tu-hsiu (陳獨秀) faction and other Peita (北大學派) cliques, who are directly based on the youth, students, and masses and constitute a significant hidden force. The clash between the Chihli and anti-Chihli cliques is clearly inevitable, sooner or later. Who knows, perhaps through this, China's old factions will self-destruct through mutual ruin, bringing the first act of entanglement mostly to a close. It seems not far off that the power of the new figures' faction, possessing contemporary awareness and based on the youth, students, and masses, will replace them. <42>

Three

As political entanglement has thus continued for ten years since the founding of the Republic without a single day of peace, growing increasingly severe, it is natural that all aspects of society would stagnate. How great must the suffering of the people have been? Consequently, the accumulated resentment, wrapped in curses and discontent, would inevitably demand resistance and revolution once more. Looking at the trajectory of societal change over time, society has a continuous relationship with politics. Therefore, it would not only suffer greatly from the influence of such bad politics but might even face the same miserable fate. However, Chinese society, in reaction, paradoxically achieved genuine awakening. On one hand, it has progressed considerably, showing very good results in inverse proportion to the political situation. This can be attributed to historical relations and national character. Historically, especially when the Ch'ing dynasty ruled the mainland of Zhongyuan, the Han Chinese, being conquered by a foreign race, fundamentally despaired of politics. Interest in politics was often viewed merely as a means to wealth. Moreover, political influence had little significant connection to the lives and evolution of the common people. Therefore, even after the revolution, it can be said that the general pure populace remained largely uninvolved in politics. Regarding national character, people were overly preoccupied with their own lives; as long as they could earn money and sustain their livelihood, there was little resistance against grievances. Only when unbearable suffering made even that precarious existence impossible to maintain, and unless unreasonable sacrifices were avoided, they couldn't help but make some final gesture. Additionally, as they gradually realized through the turning of the times and their collective identity that they could not remain purely detached from politics, they inevitably had to find a path forward for self-preservation and defense, unable to remain passive until the end. Because of these past historical traditions and inherent weaknesses, even though a democratic political system emerged after the revolution, national politics became like a plaything for a few politicians. The populace remained detached from politics, focusing solely on accumulating money to expand their own livelihoods. However, the fundamental principle of politics lies with the people themselves. Unlike the autocratic and paternalistic politics of the former Ch'ing, the quality of political influence directly shatters even their monetary lives. Coupled with the lessons of the era, the atrocious politics of the ten years since the Republic's founding inflicted unspeakable suffering on the people. <43> From this, they began to gain an awakening more precious than during the revolution itself. As mentioned above, instead of suffering the same destruction as the bad politics, they paradoxically achieved good results. After over 200 years of peace under the Ch'ing dynasty with no great suffering, the dangers and hardships of the ten years after the Republic's founding served as a period of great trial for the Chinese people. Consequently, looking at the recent progress of society in general and its rapidly advancing positive aspects, one can assert that Chinese society and the Chinese people are truly beginning to enter genuine reform. In the early years of the Republic, when their own suffering was not yet so severe, figures like Yüan Shih-k'ai, traitors to the Republic, could run rampant. The Second Revolution was initiated mainly through the use of the Revolutionary Party's army. Nominally, it might be called a movement of the general society and masses, but it cannot be called a popular resistance stemming from genuine awakening. Later, even when Yüan Shih-k'ai's imperial restoration attempt occurred, resistance was mounted through the military actions of the Ts'ai-Li faction, but it was not based on a movement of society (社會) and the masses. Similarly, when Chang Hsün's Restoration movement arose, resistance was expressed through Tuan Ch'i-jui's use of military force, employing both overt responses and covert attacks, but throughout, the general society and masses failed to rise up directly in resistance. This indicates that the suffering of society and the masses was still relatively limited, and due to this lack of trial, their final resolve and unity were insufficient. During that time, less than six years from the first year of the Republic to the sixth, society and the masses endured considerable suffering and testing through major upheavals like Yüan Shih-k'ai's imperial restoration, Chang Hsün's Restoration, and Tuan Ch'i-jui's tyranny. Then, the "May Fourth Movement" (5·4운동), worthy of special mention in the history of recent Chinese reform, erupted. This movement was truly based on the self-power of society and the masses, representing a resistant expression of the masses against the government and an autonomous action born of the masses' self-awareness. From this point, a new social force began to germinate in China, and the foundation for the new power of the Chinese masses was initially laid. Around this movement, the rise of new intellectual circles, the propagation of new thought, the new culture movement, and all other popular activities marked a major starting point for the movement of the Chinese masses and society. Among these, the significant activities of the Peita Clique (北大學派) and the propagation of new thought by the Ch'en Tu-hsiu faction not only brought about the smooth victory and success of the May Fourth Movement but also raised expectations for further progress towards a final resolution. Consequently, with the active efforts of new movement proponents and the rapid progress of general society, there is truly a sense of advancing with lightning speed. Meanwhile, the political situation, having no connection with society and the masses, became even more regressive. Its crimes multiplied daily, and the suffering of the masses increased, pushing their resentment, resistance, discontent, and curses gradually towards the extreme. <44>

Four

While the turmoil caused by all old forces, namely the tyranny of warlords and old bureaucratic cliques, has reached its peak, the progress of the general populace has also reached a level where they will not passively (無*히) endure such turmoil and tyranny. The May Fourth Movement erupted, serving not only as a sharp blow (當頭痛棒) to the old forces but also as a solemn declaration by society and the masses. Consequently, the old forces, i.e., the warlords, gradually began to face the unfortunate fate of decline. Feeling somewhat that they could no longer easily deal with society and the masses, they could not avoid facing constraints and were compelled to seek favor regarding the psychological trends of the populace. Therefore, although internally harboring malicious intentions, externally they peddle false labels of 'the masses' and 'society'. Lately, phrases like 'autonomy' and 'popular rule', and even the concept of Federal Provinces Autonomy, have emerged from the mouths of warlords. Slogans like 'social reform' are frequently propagated by old bureaucrats. Viewed this way, it might be a strategic maneuver by the old factions themselves to prop up their declining fortunes, but in reality, it serves to further promote the rise of society and the masses. Looking at the history of struggle between the masses and the old forces, the victory of the masses is often significantly fostered and aided from within the old forces themselves. Why? Because old forces fight among themselves, leading to self-elimination, and when they try to oppress each other using the banner of the masses, the value and trials of the masses are greatly elevated. During this process, the new power of the masses is naturally nurtured, eventually leading to the complete annihilation of the old forces, regardless of whether they are somewhat progressive or not. This is proven by history. Observing the current state of struggle between society and government, masses and the old cliques in China, it seems increasingly governed by this principle. In the five years from the May Fourth Movement in the 6th year of the Republic to the 11th year, the maneuvering of the old forces and the progress (進步) of society and the masses seem to have increased and decreased in direct proportion. From one perspective, the maneuvering of the old forces, representing the extreme tyranny of their final days, seemed to plunge the entire Chinese situation into even greater chaos. Before the recent Fengtien-Chihli war, the tyranny of the Fengtien faction's Chang Tso-lin had truly reached its peak, seemingly controlling almost all decrees of the central government. The rampancy of the Chihli clique, with its open interference in politics and other unreasonable brutalities, was no less wicked than that of the Fengtien faction. This precarious situation, seemingly unable to last the day, culminated not in bloody suppression by society and the masses, but in a direct struggle between the old forces themselves, leading to <45> the downfall of one side. This can still be seen as a victory for society and the masses. Thus, even the victorious Chihli clique offers little transcending the confines of the old power structure. Their continued errors and maneuvering will surely fail to win the hearts of society and the masses, and their final miserable fate is as clear as day. Therefore, however (如何»¤나) the Fengtien old forces might fare, if not eliminated by self-attrition, they will inevitably face destruction by society and the masses in the end (究竟). All this can be seen as a convenience (利便) for the people. Thus, as the Fengtien old forces depart and the Chihli old forces take their place, although there might be some slightly better aspects compared to the Fengtien faction, and despite raising many banners like 'troop reduction,' 'unification,' 'restoration of legal authority,' 'convening the Old Parliament,' and others to win the favor of society and the masses - starting with the restoration of legal authority by expelling Hsü Shih-ch'ang (suspected of aiding the Fengtien faction) and installing Li Yüan-hung (who had voluntarily dissolved the Old Parliament and resigned the presidency in the 6th year of the Republic under duress from the military governors' clique) as a temporary stopgap measure, and reconvening the Old Parliament - legally speaking, Li Yüan-hung was the direct protagonist of the Old Parliament's dissolution (even if under duress). Furthermore, even if his term continues from that time, it could be argued that it has already expired. As for the Old Parliament, after being dissolved in the 6th year, it was formally convened and held sessions in Kwangtung in the 8th year, exercising its functions for some time. Now, the Chihli clique's self-serving act of reconvening the parliament from the 6th year is also a major issue. Firstly, under the fine slogans of 'troop reduction' and 'unification,' they have so far received much grudging tolerance from society and the masses. However, far from reducing troops, they seem to be increasing them. Unification is out of the question; instead, division increases daily, and the political situation descends into extreme turmoil. Moreover, the lack of solidity within the Chihli clique itself increasingly causes societal suspicion and fear. Ts'ao K'un, whose wealth and power have reached their zenith, seemingly having no further ambitions, still dreams of the presidency. Wu P'ei-fu, drilling and expanding his forces daily in Loyang, seems intent on dominating the central plains. The opinions between these two diverge daily. Ts'ao K'un, through marital ties, maintains contact with Chang Tso-lin, exchanging envoys for reconciliation. At times, he attempts rapprochement with the Anfu faction or Sun Yat-sen's faction. Thus, it seems inevitable that another struggle between old force versus old force will erupt. Society and the masses, watching for opportunities in the interstices, will certainly not remain idle.
In the current situation of China, no matter what changes occur among the old forces, even partial reform is impossible. Unless there is a replacement by new forces based on society and the masses, that is, a true popular revolution, the current situation cannot be resolved, and a new China cannot emerge. <46> Therefore, we must look at the current state and potential of society and the masses to foresee China's future. From this perspective, the new popular foundation began to be laid after the May Fourth Movement. Observing the progress since then and the underlying currents of the present situation, there indeed seems to be potential. Furthermore, spurred by the recent Fengtien-Chihli war, they have reached a final resolve and determination. Since Li Yüan-hung assumed the presidency, the government's actions, unable to be purely dismissed as empty talk, have led to daily cries for troop reduction and abolition of military governors. The government's weaknesses are laid bare, and indeed, the time for a popular uprising seems to be gradually maturing. Moreover, the masses' resistant will has already been expressed significantly, and they seem to be holding on through the final active moments. Among them, the declaration titled "Our Political Proposals" (我們的(우리의) 政治主張), jointly signed by 16 individuals including Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei (蔡元培), Wang Ch'ung-hui (王寵惠), representing the Peita Clique and Northern new figures, was published around April this year in the "Nuli" (努力) weekly review, edited by Hu Shih (胡適), purely aiming at "Political Critique and Popular Advancement." Although not an extremely radical assertion, but rather moderate proposals—first, fundamental troop reduction; second, thorough public disclosure of finances; third, amicable North-South unification—it received significant societal response. The Kuomintang, led by Sun Yat-sen, which had truly lacked a central organ for the masses and society, has now adapted to the changes in times and public sentiment, recognized the need for internal purification, resolutely reorganized, and is regrouping its forces. Ch'en Tu-hsiu, in an article titled "My Views on Reforming Present-Day China" (對於現中國改 造之我見) published in the Nuli weekly review in August this year, called for gathering sound elements of the domestic democratic revolution to form a major political party, aiming first for a true popular revolution, and subsequently advocating for a proletarian revolution. Since early September recently, as a vanguard for the revolutionary movement, he launched the "Xiangdao Weekly" (嚮導週報, The Guide). Its inaugural statement briefly declared its emergence as a guide for the popular revolution. According to Ch'en Tu-hsiu's "Theory of Nation-Building" (造國論), present-day China cannot be called an independent nation but is in a time of "nation-building," i.e., revolution, and a time of political warfare. Thus, talks of restoring legal authority, hastily enacting a constitution, decentralizing power to localities, reforming finances, or cleaning up elections are all irrelevant to the true demands of the situation, akin to scratching an itch through one's boot. Therefore, an authentically independent Republic of China must be constructed through the means of political warfare. If this problem is solved, the second question is by what method to achieve "nation-building." The answer to this is the necessity of forming a true national army. Thus, summarizing our progression, it is as follows: Step 1, organize a national army; Step 2, through national revolution, eliminate all internal and external oppression; Step 3, <47> organize a popular-based unified national government; Step 4, adopt state socialism to develop industry. This essay advocating direct revolution was published in the 5th issue of the Xiangdao Weekly. From this, we can see that the revolutionary opportunity is considerably ripe. The National Troop Reduction Movement on the Double Tenth festival last October seemed to show clear signs of an impending explosion. All the unstable, perilous signs across the country suggest the situation cannot last much longer.
Ah! For rebirth, for reconstruction, there must be destruction. Revolution must explode. In China's current state, without it, there is only decline. The Chinese people, possessing the spirit of resurrection, are proceeding along an inevitable path. The Chinese people, having endured many trials and gained capabilities through the first and subsequent revolutions of the past, intend to receive the baptism of revolution again in its true sense. When this sacred fire of revolution spreads across the continent of East Asia, its repercussions will surely be felt by the Chinese people. Furthermore, how can we not predict that the great storm clouds over all of East Asia will arise from this? Herein lies the overflowing new life of the Chinese people on the continent, and the acceleration of East Asia's new destiny... (End) <48>

**Summary**

This 1923 article by "Pukryŏ Tonggok" analyzes the political and social chaos in China following the 1911 Revolution. It divides the post-revolutionary period into four stages: party struggles, Yüan Shih-k'ai's imperial ambitions and warlord emergence, warlord ascendancy leading to North-South division, and the era of failed federalism movements and intense warlord conflict (Fengtien vs. Chihli). The author argues that China's revolution was incomplete, leading to chronic instability dominated by self-serving warlords and corrupt politicians. However, this very chaos and the suffering it inflicted paradoxically spurred a genuine awakening among the Chinese populace, particularly visible after the May Fourth Movement. The author sees the burgeoning social consciousness, student movements, and calls for radical change (citing figures like Ch'en Tu-hsiu) as signs of an impending, true popular revolution necessary for China's rebirth and reconstruction, predicting this upheaval will have significant repercussions across East Asia.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting because it shows a Korean intellectual closely observing and analyzing the turbulent situation in neighboring China. The author's emphasis on the necessity of "destruction" for "rebirth," the critique of incomplete revolution, and the focus on popular awakening likely resonated with Korean readers facing their own struggles under Japanese rule. The anticipation of a revolutionary "sacred fire" spreading across East Asia suggests a hope that transformative change in China could inspire or influence events in Korea and challenge the existing colonial order. Historians might find the detailed account of Chinese warlord politics, the specific mention of contemporary Chinese intellectual movements and publications (like "Nuli" and "Xiangdao Weekly"), and the Korean perspective on the potential for region-wide upheaval valuable.

現 中國의 일반정세는 최대의 위험에 처하야 잇슴은 부인할 수 업는 사실이다. 政治的 現勢는 일체의 極紛紏와 極紊亂에 容易히 수습할 수 업는 전혀 破産에 臨하얏다 하야도 과언이 아니며 社會的 現勢는 그의 정치적 험악에 인한 반항과 혁명적 기분의 重壓에 잔득 포위되어 장찻 무슨 폭발과 진전이 잇스려 하나니 참으로 형세가 迫緊하고 風雲이 日急한 최후의 찰나에 임한 감이 不無하다. 今에 現勢의 如何를 서술키 전에 혁명 이후의 정치적 변천의 槪況을 暫言하야 現下 紛糾의 근본적 원인이 어대로부터 비롯하얏는가 함을 一觀하야 보면 현 정치적 이면의 情態를 간략히 窺知할 수 잇는 때에 정치적 변천의 반영인 사회의 現態도 자연히 표명될지라 혁명 후 정치적 변천의 槪况을 略 四期로 分하야 보면
(1) 政黨의 爭權時代  此는 民國元年 南京政府 성립한 때로부터 袁世凱가 정식으로 대총통에 취임하야 國會解散한 때까지니 나는 항상 中國의 혁명에 대하야는 壯快하고 만족한 늣김을 가지는 것보다 오히려 불유쾌하고 ＜35＞ 미흡한 생각이 잇슬 뿐만이 아니라 혁명의 순간이 좀 더 길엇더면 또는 혁명의 진정한 洗禮를 더 바닷더면 하는 것이다. 武昌의 혁명이 旬月의 間에 문득 성공한 후로 南京에 임시정부가 성립되어 苟且한 對淸廷의 議和로 淸帝가 퇴위하고 민국이 居然히 성립은 되엇스나 당시 黃興의 主戰論에 의한 혁명군이 議和고 무엇이고 할 수 업시 直히 북경에 衝入하야 진정한 혁명을 하지 못하게 됨은 참으로 더욱 現下의 情態에 鑑하야 千古의 遺憾이라 할 것이다. 그가튼 혁명의 神嚴을 浼瀆한다 할만한 議和가 개시될 때에 벌셔 民國의 대죄인인 袁世凱의 魔手가 此에 침입되어 가혹한 조건 하에서 혁명적 本義에 違背되며 民治的 정신에 薄弱한 袁世凱가 정식으로 대총통에 취임하야 民國의 수령이 되는 때에 現下의 紛糾를 致하는 근본의 病毒이 此에 이미 胚胎케 되엇다. 그리하여 시대적 정신과 혁명적 氣魄이 잇는 新人物들은 退野케 되고 돌이어 前淸의 부패한 관료파가 日로 정권을 秉執케 되며 정당이 분립하야 서로이 跳梁케 되어 당시의 정당으로 말하면 國民黨(卽 興中會의 後身 혁명시대의 總機關) 及 進步黨이 여러 정당 중에는 가장 큰 정당이엇섯는데 곳 이 兩黨이 정권을 쟁탈하든 때이엇다. 그 얼마 동안에는 國民黨의 세력이 극히 盛하야 入閣의 인물로도 多數로는 國民黨員이엇슴으로 一時는 民國의 新氣象이 나타낫스나 그 두 정당이 서로 투쟁케 되는 동시에 袁世凱는 더욱 야심을 包含하야 奊�滑한 수단으로써 그가 온대서 漁人의 計로 이용하야 마츰내 제2차의 혁명이 일어나게까지 되고 國民黨은 無理히 해산을 當케 되는 때에 進步黨도 共倒同망의 悲境에 陷케 되며 국회는 無形中 解散케 되다.
(2) 帝政準備時代―軍閥胚胎時代 如斯히 국회는 不法히 해산되는 때에 國民黨의 세력은 一蹶不起케 되고 혁명계의 新人物은 만히 袁氏의 毒手에 被害되엇슬 뿐만이 아니라 당시 全中國의 全勢力은 袁氏가 獨專케 되어 혁명계 인물들의 불평이 업다 아니할지라도 도저히 반항할 수도 업게 되다. 그럼으로 제2차의 혁명이 비록 잇섯다 할지라도 무슨 成效를 내지 못하얏슬 뿐더러 돌이어 袁氏의 세력과 地盤은 더욱 튼튼케 되엇스매 혁명계의 타격은 더 말할 것 업시 前淸時代의 압박보다도 더 심하게 되며 袁氏는 이에서 純然한 독재로서 당시의 정국을 요란케 하는 때에 더욱 그의 근본적 야심을 실현하기에 급급하얏섯다. 그럼으로 국회해산 후에는 此를 奇貨로 思하야 御用의 정치회의, 約法會議를 소집하야 新約法을 제정하고 官制를 개설하야 자기의 腹心을 安置하고 군벌을 ＜36＞ 이용하야 각 省及 각 要隘에 배치하야 專혀 自私의 安固를 圖하랴는 때에 군벌의 할거적 형세를 양성하야 所謂 현재의 각 省巡閱使 督軍 及 鎭守使가 그것이엇다. 최후에 民意를 僞造하야 帝制까지 하랴는 때에 당시의 정국에는 純然히 前淸 舊官僚派가 다시 정권을 잡게 되어 말하자면 이름만 혁명 후의 民國政府라 하지마는 부패한 前淸 政府의 부활이라 하야도 과언이 아니며 國民黨 一派 及 革命界의 孫逸仙 黃興은 다시 국외에 망명케 되엇슬 뿐만 아니라 참으로 혁명의 흔적은 족음도 볼 수 업게 되다. 그러다가 혁명의 정신은 오히려 죽지 아니하야 蔡愕 李烈鈞 兩氏가 首先히 雲南에서 일어나 帝制 反對의 義旗를 들매 전국이 響應하야 마츰내 袁氏의 「新華宮」의 일장춘몽이 無形 中에 타파케 되엇스나 이 때에도 그의 義軍은 北京을 直衝하야 所謂 ꊷ� 非혁명파의 소굴을 蕩滌치 못하고 말앗 버렷나니 이 역시 그 당시에 蔡李兩軍에게 響應한 자―또한 각자의 地盤 及 勢力에 어쩌지 못하고 시대와 인심에 반역치 못하는 被動이 참으로 有意의 奮起가 아님이엇다. 그 후에 그 帝制로 인한 해독은 더 말할 것 업스니 안으로는 사회와 인민의 질서가 극히 紛亂에 陷하엿든 것이오 밧그로는 日本 及 外國에 疆土의 侵呑을 바든 것은 더욱 당시의 대세에 큰 위험을 준 것인데 더구나 日本에 대한 無理의 强奪과 苛酷의 증여를 한 것이다. 그리하야 帝制는 그럭저럭 타파는 되엇다 할지라도 내외의 형세가 이가티 위급함은 고사하고도 소위 北洋系의 군벌이 正히 胚胎되어 그 후의 정권과 일체의 세력은 또한 군벌의 手에 專擅케 되고 말엇나니 이이론 현재의 中國 각 군벌이다.
(3) 軍閥滿盛時代―이는 黎元洪氏의 繼任時로부터 直(直隷派)皖(安徽省派) 전쟁 시대에 이르는 동안이니 袁氏가 帝制를 하랴는 때에 자기의 腹心을 각 중요의 위치에 두기 위하야 前淸의 遺老와 無理한 武人과 無恥한 政客 등 기타 前淸의 일반 부패한 舊人物을 이용하는 동시에 民治의 그 정신에 부족함도 물론이요, 또는 일시 정국의 혼돈을 致함도 姑舍하고 압흐로 큰 해독을 끼치는 군벌이 양성되며 더욱 現下 紛糾의 大原因이 되는 각 省督軍의 할거적 형세가 不知不覺의 중에 자연히 양성되어 왓슴은 사실이라. 그 때는 오히려 袁世凱씨의 신랄한 수완 하에서 중앙정부의 行號 施令이 어느 정도까지 행사되어 십분 전횡케는 안 되얏섯다. 黎元洪씨가 繼任한 후로 段祺瑞씨가 袁氏의 뒤를 蹈襲하야 專權케 되매 당시 長江巡閱使로 잇든 張勳씨와 安徽督軍으로 잇든 倪嗣沖 등이 공연히 정국에 ＜37＞ 간섭케 되어 국회는 제2차의 해산을 맛나게 됨에 이르러 그 결과는 『復辟』의 운동이 그 이면에 양성케 되어 마츰내 3일 천하라도 大淸의 專制가 다시 中華民國에 출현케 됨에 대총통 黎元洪씨는 퇴위케 되며 中國의 정국은 이로써 혁명 후 더 말할 수 업는 極紛亂期에 들어가게 되다. 이 때에 국회는 南으로 廣東에 가서 「護法」의 기치를 들고 孫逸仙씨는 최후에 어쩔 수 업시 廣東에 軍政府를 置하야 그 「護法國會」를 유지케 하고 다시 혁명의 初衷을 관철하랴 하얏나니 이가 곳 中國 남북의 분열된 처음이엇다. 孫逸仙씨가 이가티 赤手空拳의 몃 열렬한 동지로 더불어 壯快하게 남방 6省(廣東, 廣西, 雲南, 貴州, 泗川, 湖南)을 분할하야 일시의 형세를 이루엇스나 이 역시 그 군벌적 세력과 그 幾個 야심가의 跳梁으로 자기의 素志를 펴짊� 못하게 되엇다. 이 때에 북방은 復辟運動이 실패된 후 段祺瑞씨가 내각에 復任하야 徐世昌씨를 運動하야 괴뢰적으로 대총통을 바든 후 安福(安福俱樂部 段祺瑞의 주관)派 國會가 舊國會를 代하야 소집되고 무력통일 정책을 실행하려 함에 자기의 腹心인 吳光新으로 泗川에 보내여 소요케 하고 傳良佐로 湖南督軍을 낸 후 직접 南方軍 政府를 토멸하려 함에 이로부터 남북의 互相鬪爭으로 수년 동안 爭持하야 오다가 중간에 외교단의 警告에 의하야 上海에서 南北和會가 소집되엇스나 마츰내 互相의 不成條件으로 이무 귀결이 업시 분산되는 때에 당시 中國의 정치 형세는 마치 5胡 7國 시대의 군웅할거와 同樣이며 일체의 대세는 더 말할 것도 업시 최대의 위험으로 들어가게 되엇다. 그 때의 북방정부의 安福部는 北洋軍閥의 거두 段祺瑞 徐樹錚 등을 憑藉하야 외국에 대한 債欸 더욱 日本에 대하야 거대한 債欸으로 實의 매국적 행동을 함에 국가의 형세는 日로 존망을 未判할 境에 驅入됨을 不拘하고 그러한 債金은 가저다 私人의 充腹만 되엇슬 뿐이 아니라 純然한 골육의 투쟁에 소비되고 말앗스며 南方 軍府에는 소위 유력한 정당인 政學系(岑春煊 등이 수뇌)는 西南 軍閥派 陸榮廷(당시 廣西督軍), 態克武(泗川督軍) 등의 세력을 依賴하야 無理한 苛稅와 暴歛을 징수하고 기타 各省 督軍 及 總司令이 다 통일과 護法의 名義를 빙러 거의 임의로 그 野慾을 다 채웟다. 그리하야 中國의 천하에는 軍力만 쥔 者이면 그만이엇다. 참으로 古時 황제의 권위로 그를 돌이어 불비할 만 最境에까지 이르러 軍閥의 滿盛은 이에 더할 수 업시 극에 달한 모양이엇섯는 데 南方의 軍政府도 互相의 세력쟁탈과 內訌이 日起하야 그 頹局은 도저히 유지튕� 수 업슬 뿐만 아니라 護法의 義旗를 들어 軍府를 設한 本精神과 本目的에 위반됨으로 孫逸仙씨는 上海로 오게 되자 당시의 軍府는 직접 北方의 買收로 ＜38＞ 그의 名義까지 취소하게 되엇스매 그 때에 北方도 徐世昌씨가 所謂 대총통의 名義를 가져 정국을 그냥 유지한다 하얏스나 直隷 安福 兩派의 互相 반목은 마츰내 전쟁까지 일어나 그 결과 安福派가 일망타진되어 段祺瑞씨는 奪官褫職을 당하고 徐樹錚 등은 망명케 되어 安福派의 跳梁은 純然히 滅消되엇다 힐지나 이에 直隷派 及 奉天派 專橫은 이전보다 족음도 加滅이 업시 또한 발호할 뿐이요 정국은 依然히 분규로 만들어 가게 되고 다소라도 수습될 가능은 업섯다.

二

현 中國의 이 국면에는 일체 구세력으로는 如何한 변동이 잇다 할지라도 局部의 개조도 불가능이려니와 사회와 민중의 토대로 한 신세력의 교대 즉 진정한 민본적 혁명이 아니면 도저히 現局의 수습과 신中國의 출현을 못 시킬 것이다.＜46＞ 그러면 현재의 사회와 민중의 정도 여하와 現勢 여하를 보아 中國의 장래를 볼 것 밧게 업나니 이로써 보면 「5·4운동」후에 비롯오 민중적 신토대가 始創케 되엇는데 伊來의 진보된 景況과 現勢의 이면을 깁히 보건대 확실히 가능성이 잇는 것 갓다. 더구나, 今番의 奉直 전쟁을 인하야 최후의 각오와 결심을 하얏거니와 黎元洪씨 대총통 취임 이래로 정부의 행동도 純然히 虛言할 수 업는 점에서 裁兵과 廢督을 日로 絶叫하야 잇는 중 정부의 약점은 적나라하게 들어나서 果是 민중적 奮起의 시기가 점차 성숙하야 오는 모양이다. 더구나 민중의 반항적 의사는 이미 만히 표시도 되엇슬 뿐더러 적극적 최후의 幾分間을 撑持하야 잇는 것 갓다. 그 중의 北大學派 及 北方新人들의 網羅로 한 蔡元培, 王寵惠…등 16인의 連署한 『我們的(우리의) 政治主張』이라 한 선언이 금년 4월 경에 純然한 「政治批判 及 民衆進行」을 목표로 하고 출판된 胡適씨 주간의 「努力」 週報上에 발표되엇는데 此는 그러케 極烈한 주장은 아니고 오히려 온화한 주장으로 제1 근본적 裁兵, 제2 재정의 철저공개, 제3 南北의 원만한 통일 등인데 만히 사회의 反響을 바닷스며 孫逸仙씨를 수령으로 한 國民黨은 참으로 伊來 민중과 사회의 총기관이 엇섯는데 今에 시대와 인심의 변천에 순응하야 또는 내부의 廓淸을 필요로 하야 斷然히 개조한 후 기치를 重整하야 잇는 터이며 陳獨秀씨는 금년 8월 努力週報 上에 「對於現中國改 造之我見」이라는 논문에 刻下에 곳 국내의 민주혁명의 健全한 分子를 收羅하야 일대 정당을 조직한 후 제 1 着 진정한 민본의 혁명을 期成하고 그 次로 무산계급의 혁명을 絶叫하얏고 최근의 9월 초부터는 혁명운동의 선구로 「嚮導週報」 “The guide”를 출간하야 그의 創刊詞에 간단히 此報는 민중혁명의 선도로 출현하얏다 하얏고 陳獨秀의 「造國論」에 의하면 현재의 中國은 독립의 국가라 할 수 업스며 또는 「造國」 즉 혁명의 時機에 在하며 政治戰爭 시대에 在한 즉 무슨 법통을 회복하느니 憲法을 속히 제정하느니 지방의 分權을 하느니 재정을 整理하느니 選擧를 廓淸하느니 하는 것이 시국의 진정한 요구에 대하야는 다 不成問題이요, 이는 구두 신고 가려운 대를 긁는 것과 일반이다. 그런즉 政治戰爭의 수단으로써 일개 진정한 독립의 中華民國을 건설하여야 할지니 만일 이 문제가 해결된다 하면 제2의 문제는 如何한 방법으로써 「造國」하랴 함이 문제일 것이니 此의 답안은 진정한 국민군을 조성하여야 할 것이라 함이다. 如斯히 우리의 進程을 총괄하야 말하면 알에와 갓나니 제1步은 國民軍의 조직, 제2步는 국민혁명으로써 국내 국외의 일체 압박을 解除할 것, 제3步은 ＜47＞ 主民的 全國統一政府를 조직할 것, 제4步는 국가사회주의를 採用하야 實業을 개발할 것, 이상의 直接革命 고취에 대한 논문 그 響導週報 제 5호에 발표되엇는데 이로써 보면 혁명의 機가 자못 성숙됨을 알겟스며 최근의 去年 10월 雙十節의 國民裁兵運動은 완전히 폭발의 預兆를 보인 듯하며 일체 전국의 불안한 險象은 그 終日을 마치지 못할 것 갓다.
아아! 新生을 위하야 改造를 위하야는 破壤가 잇서야 할 것이다. 혁명이 爆裂되어야 할 것이다. 中國의 現狀으로는 그것이 업고는 도태될 것 밧게 업다. 부활의 魄力을 가진 中國民은 필연의 道程으로 나아간다. 과거의 1차 3차의 혁명에 대한 만흔 시련과 능력을 가진 中國민은 진정한 의미에서 다시곰 혁명의 세례를 바드려 한다. 그런데 이가튼 혁명의 聖火가 장차 東亞의 대륙에 펴쳐질 때에 그의 反響은 中國民에게는 물론일 것이다. 아울러 全東亞의 대풍운이 그로부터 일어날 것을 어찌 預占치 아니하랴. 이에서 中國民의 新生命이 대륙에 橫溢하야 잇스며 東亞의 新機運이 촉진될 것이다…(完)＜48＞
