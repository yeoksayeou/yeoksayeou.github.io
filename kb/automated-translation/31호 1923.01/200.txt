잡지명	개벽 제31호
발행일	1923년 01월 01일
기사제목	十七圓五十錢, 젊은 畵家 A의 눈물의 한 방울 (Seventeen Won Fifty Jeon, A Drop of Tears from the Young Painter A)
필자	稻香 作
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

First
To my beloved Teacher C, I offer a word from my young heart, a boundless feeling that wells up without reason.

Like the flowing stream of time, our lives are boat rides upon it. Is there anyone—old or young, heartbroken or grieving, singing hymns of happiness aloud or crying out odes of success—who does not play at boating in this stream called time?

This young A, who offers this letter to you today, has also set adrift a small boat on the stream called time, floating along towards an unknown shore (浦口).

Some ride swift steamships, while others raise high sails and glide along with the fair wind. Yet others precariously row dilapidated boats with holes in the bottom, tossed this way and that.

From some boats, sounds of yawning and stretching can be heard. From others, drums beat and merry songs are sung. From some boats, the whispers of blushing intimacies (情話) are audible. From others, the heart-wrenching cries of a woman arise. In some boats, skeletons (髑髏) dance, and in others, the snoring of gamblers is heard. <56>

But do you know what sounds are heard from the boat this A rides? Unceasing melancholy, indignation, disappointment, suffering, and resentment have formed clumps and masses, seemingly blocking the listener's ear canals, leaving only a ringing headache.

There are hundreds, thousands of boats floating alongside mine, passing through the same places. They merely look at each other and laugh, choked up. And they merely shed tears for each other.

Teacher. This boat is moving. Whether it goes five *ri* an hour or just one *ri*, it moves. But surely, the boat will not go backward. What purpose does this forward-moving boat, carrying us, intend to achieve? Flowing time is silent and purposeless; it proceeds according to fixed rules. But upon this stream called time, silent and purposeless, what ripples should this A sketch?

Are they the lovely ripples dancing “chalrŭdŏk ch’alsak” in the frosty dawn wind? Are they the murmuring ripples of the tide, rolling in from afar in the morning and evening, then receding far away? Are they the cunning wave patterns, bluish then whitish, reflecting the crescent moon askew? In any case, I must sketch some ripple upon that stream called time. Even if it must be mere foam rising “p’u-hak” upon the dark water, I must leave it behind.

Teacher. However, though I try to draw those ripples, I cannot. The wind from the heavens is too fierce, and the surging waves are too powerful. When the wave of convention tries to drive this small boat (片舟) away, and when all the dark waves of the encroaching environment try to swallow this small boat called A, which intends to proceed, I try to weigh anchor, row the oars, and go. But though I try to set a course, my arms lack strength, and though I wish to go, I do not possess the powerful engine that could pull me forward. Is that all? Sometimes torrential rain pours down, and sometimes fierce winds blow, trying mercilessly to cast this barely bobbing small boat into the blue waves.

Ah, Teacher. That is not all. Sometimes it becomes a dark night. Angry, churning waves leap here and there in the pitch-black darkness. Not a single star of hope is visible in the sky. Even the flickering light of the lighthouse, like a sesame seed, dimly visible over there at the entrance <57>, sometimes goes out.

But I intend to go. With weak, powerless arms and legs, I intend to go towards that unseen shore (浦口), drawing ripples of various shapes and colors. The canvas of ripples drawn today will give birth to the ripples drawn tomorrow, and the canvas of ripples drawn tomorrow will give birth to those of the day after, and when I reach that shore over there, upon the powerful current flowing out to the great ocean (大洋), I intend to draw the grand and beautiful ripples of success.

Ah, at that time, neither the mad waves dancing in the darkness, nor the ceaseless storms and downpours, nor the surging waves of convention, nor all the harsh waves of the environment will be able to erase or swallow that one grand and beautiful ripple. Whether this small single-leaf boat (一葉片舟) shatters against the rocks or is washed away by the waves, even through my dying throat, I shall sing the song of eternal life with overflowing joy and surging delight.

Second
For some reason, the weather today is gloomier than I've ever seen. I tried hard to make my frustrated mind and melancholic feelings cheerful and clear, but it was a failure.

After eating breakfast, I lay on the damp room floor until noon. Coming and going fantasies sometimes made me laugh, sometimes made me cry. My young wife, with a sewing box covered in colorful paper (五色 조희) beside her, blinked her star-like eyes as she attached the ties to the *turumagi* (overcoat) I would wear out. Whenever I look at my wife, I feel pity. Whenever I think of my young wife's hardships, my heart aches for some reason. The young wife sitting beside me is certainly not the wife who fulfills my ideals. We are not a couple joined by love meeting love. It is not the harmonious love of a self-aware lover and a self-aware lover. I cannot tell what she trusted in to become my wife, nor what awareness she has in loving me. If the meeting of lover and lover is the greatest, most daring act, then the union of these two people, neither lover nor lover, is also perilously daring. <58>

This A, who committed the same perilous act, is a pitiful brave man, but my young wife, who still does not know this, is also a foolish brave woman. Have our two bodies truly been entwined harmoniously with the thread of love (愛)? What is it that pours mighty strength into the red blood of both of us?

However, my young child calls me "Appa, Appa" (Daddy, Daddy). And calls my wife "Ŏmma, Ŏmma" (Mommy, Mommy). Whenever I hear those calls of "Ŏmma, Appa," my heart somehow becomes pure, and before I know it, a warm affection fills my chest. When the child laughs, I laugh too. Then my wife also laughs. My wife's laughing eyes invariably gaze upon my face.

When the innocent child laughs playfully, my laughter and my wife's laughter entangle and kiss in the unseen air. At that time, all dissatisfaction, all suffering is driven out of the room.

Today too, sunlight streamed warmly through the south-facing window. The innocent child, in a corner of the room with a ruler, its chubby legs bare, sniffling “saegŭnsaekkun” as it played, finding something so amusing. Though my frustrated feelings made me frown without reason, when my wife, with her ticklish skin and soft breath, stitched the quiet silence with a fine needle, for some reason, all the senses of my whole body, with eyes closed, felt weary as if having lost their strength.

As my unsleeping mind was submerged in a daze (昏膿), my wife, perhaps thinking of something, said,
"Darling, it's getting colder. When you get your salary, please buy a hat for the child." Hearing this, I pretended not to hear. And inwardly, I thought, "I need to buy painting supplies (畵具) and some books too; the child's hat can wait," feeling needlessly annoyed at my wife's words. This annoyance was certainly not because her words were unreasonable or because I begrudged buying the child's hat, but rather, suffering from economic pressure, whenever I hear the word 'money,' the accumulated dissatisfaction tangles up even good feelings and throws them away. <59>

My wife, having mentioned such things several times before, seemed embarrassed by my lack of response and was silent for a while, then said,
"Why don't you answer when someone speaks?" I still lay there without replying. My wife again,
"What's so difficult about buying one hat for the child?" then, without another word, she shook out the finished *turumagi*, fluffing it, and tossed it onto my legs where I lay.

The child, who had been playing with the ruler, hearing the word 'hat,'
"A pretty hat? Uh-huh, Mommy?" grinned widely, looked up at my wife, and ran towards her.

Seeing this, my wife, perhaps smoothing over her annoyed expression, said,
"Look at this, will you? He's saying 'hat, hat'," and gently shook my chest with her soft, smooth hand as I lay there with my arm over my eyes, saying nothing. As my wife's smooth fingers fidgeted on my clothes, the fine nerves beneath my skin seemed to transmit a sensation, as if intoxicated, into my bloodstream.

I merely said,
"Why are you being so bothersome?" nudged her hand away with my elbow, and turned over again. My wife, knowing my inherently nervous temperament, showed not the slightest sign of anger but, smiling brightly, said, feigning great annoyance,
"Never mind. Just get up, put on your clothes, and go out. Lying around in broad daylight is making me feel suffocated." For some reason, her voice did not allow my feigned anger, weak-hearted as I am, to last long. I just sat bolt upright, glanced once at my wife's face, and said,
"Aish, how can I lie down with all this nagging? What about the *turumagi*?" unable to suppress a smile, I grinned broadly. My wife too, her face adrift with laughter mixed with feigned annoyance, <60>
"What else could it be?" pointing to my *turumagi* lying on the floor.

Feeling both embarrassed and amused, I simply put on the *turumagi* without a word and muttered to myself,
"What time is it now?" then picked up my hat and put it on.

I went outside. My heart, having come out after a loving quarrel with my young wife, felt a longing and affection for the events that had just transpired. When the fresh air and blue sky seemed to melt my needlessly sentimental feelings and soften them, for some reason, the face and expression of my young wife, mixed with half-laughter and half-annoyance, seemed to captivate (魅醉) my heart indescribably.

As I headed towards MW Company (MW社) to find my friends, my thoughts were solely of my wife, and the new hat for our child that she had requested. I decided I would buy the hat when I received my salary. Thus, I would not only delight the child's heart but also bring joy to the heart of the young wife, the child's mother.

Third
I arrived at MW Company (MW社). D, H, W, and C were looking at each other, seemingly worried about something. For some reason, a dark blue shadow of anxiety flickered back and forth in that not-so-spacious room. I asked,
"What's wrong with everyone? What are you worried about?" The dark-faced D, H said,
"We were just waiting for you. It's about N C's wife; word came that she's ill. He's already struggling, how worried must he be? So we were thinking of going to N C's house today and were waiting for you."
"What? N C's wife?"
"Yes."
"That's terrible. Then when should we go? Are the travel expenses ready?" <61>
"I've prepared them."
"Then let's go." I simply couldn't bear the feeling of sympathy for my friend's unfortunate situation. N C's home is in the countryside (시골). Moreover, it's a secluded village. His life is not affluent but impoverished. He now farms with his own hands. In the morning, he takes his hoe and goes to the fields. In the evening, he returns home without worries. Once back, he reads tiny-print books under a flickering brass lamp (鍮檠). And he writes poetry. As you, Teacher, may have seen, not a few of his poems truly achieve perfection. I envy N C's quiet life. I envy his life lived under his firm faith, without the slightest complaint, without the slightest change.
I have not seen his tears. His sighs have not chilled my ears.

Fourth
Beloved Teacher, if humans have tears, would there be anyone who wouldn't cry in such a situation? If tears are truly tears, then tears like these must be true tears.

It was this evening. The three of us arrived at the countryside village where N C lives. As we walked the ten *ri* from the station, the three of us truly held a common consciousness, a common emotion in our minds and hearts.

The small stars visible in the distance seemed to guide us like the Magi of old towards Bethlehem, twinkling playfully as they watched us. The tightly clustered Pleiades huddled together as if ticklish. The night was dark and the road rough, but beyond the end of the line of whatever force led us lay the paradise of shared feeling (反情). There lay the longed-for "Eden" of camaraderie.

Walking without words, without sound, the three of us, while seeking out N C's desolate, lonely, quiet, and tasteless (無味淡淡) house, intended to paint the heavy air surrounding that secluded farming village with our boiling blood and burning passion, making it endless and brilliant. <62>

However, it happened when we reached N C's house. In the darkness surrounding the edge of the thatched house, there was a person pacing back and forth, coming and going alone. At that moment, he had his eyes closed, looking up at the sky. We recognized him as N C. We simply called out,
"N C!" and extended our welcoming hands. Seeing this, N C, without a sound, extended his pale hands and replied in a weak voice tinged with melancholy,
"Hey, how did you all manage to come down here?" As we heard N C's voice, we sensed some vague realization within our three hearts. Like hearing life's elegy (哀歌), a song of heartbreak and sorrow, a blueish chill emanated from N C's voice.

N C said nothing, merely gripping the hands of each of us three in turn, firmly. Then he said,
"My wife went to the land of eternal resolution (解決) 30 minutes ago." Tears, never before seen, flowed from N C's eyes. Tears, the crystallization of grief (哀嘆) that tore at N C's chest and chilled his red blood, simply soaked his cold collar and cooled without a trace.

As someone once said, there are stars in the sky. There are flowers (꼿) on the earth. There are pearls in the sea. And we humans have hot, sparkling tears. Seeing this, who could refrain from crying? Seeing this sight, who could hold back tears? The three of us stood there crying for a long while. Seeing the death of the beloved wife of a close friend, a cherished comrade (同志者), the collective sorrow of our human existence freshly made us, weak-hearted as we were, weep.

Fifth
I haven't seen you for a long time. I stayed at N C's house for a whole week. N C's wife's funeral <63> was two days after we arrived in the countryside.

Early autumn was chilly. Tree leaves danced above the bier carrying the corpse, as if wilting. The long, drawn-out, mournful funeral dirge (韭歌) sung by the pallbearers stretched out over the mountain pass towards the public cemetery.

Ah! N C's wife is gone forever. Passing through the village, past the mountain bend, she is gone forever. But her apparition, endlessly weeping in N C's mind, how long will it make our N C cry through the long years? Her shadow, dancing listlessly in the memories of retrospection, how many times will it fill N C's eyes with deep emotion?

On frosty, ice-cold nights, on evenings with a slanted crescent moon, where and when will the heart-aching old memories, the heart-rending old thoughts, make our N C weep?

When I attended N C's wife's funeral, I too felt as if standing on the borderline between death and life. What difference is there between death and life? Does being alive mean that blood circulates in the body, that one is conscious of everything, feels everything? Does death mean that all senses lose their activity along with the decay of the body? I could not help but feel infinite sorrow.

Sixth
I came up from the countryside yesterday. For some reason, the weather today was clear. As the thin, sweet air passed through my nose into my constantly expanding lungs, my blood, which seemed withered until yesterday, felt purified again.

While painting a picture titled "Despair" (낙망), fearing despair itself, I ceaselessly filled my heart with beautiful hope. When moving the brush and palette (Palette/調色板) in deep meditative bliss (法悅), I was alive and tasted the truth of life. Only when I held the palette board and sat facing the canvas (간바스) was my true life (生). While painting a picture with the motto "Despair," <64> there was infinite future and endless joy (愉悅). Like holding a lover's hand, shedding tears below her ear, and pouring out one's heart, a pure and sweet taste colored my entire being.

For some reason, my mind was clear (淸澄) today. Perhaps it was because I spent nearly a week in the countryside with little stimulation, but in any case, I spent today in weary ease with a serene (閑雅) spirit.

However, even ease has its boredom, and meditative bliss (法悅) was not without its waking moments. Bodily weariness made the mind weary too. And it shattered even the bliss.

I stretched once and threw down the palette board. I put away the canvas, put on my overcoat, put on my hat, and looked at my watch. It indicated 2 o'clock. I realized I had two hours free. So, to melt away the boredom, I decided to go to S O's house.

S O is a pitiful woman. She is disabled, lacking one leg. She is 20 years old. Dragging her missing leg, she attended school for over 10 years, rain or shine, hot or cold. When I was in the fourth year of middle school, meeting her every morning at the same street corner became the connection (緣) that led to our acquaintance, and we have known each other for three years now.

She has only an elderly mother. Morning and evening, when S O went to and from school, her mother would invariably watch and wait for her daughter to go to school and come back. If something at school made her late, her old mother would always come to the school gate and wait for her daughter.

Ah, Teacher, the life of the disabled mother and daughter is truly pitiful and wretched, unbearable to witness or even think about. Their material life is the most miserable in this world. They live day to day by doing needlework for hire in a rented side room (겻방) of someone else's house.

Today too, I came looking for that pitiful disabled woman. Stepping inside the gate, I coughed a couple of times. But for some reason, <65> the disabled woman, who always used to greet me gladly, her shadow was nowhere to be seen today.

My heart, standing at the entrance, felt desolate, like wandering in a mountain valley at dusk. The fact that the poor disabled woman did not greet me made my heart weep.

I coughed again and was about to open the room door, which had holes and flapping paper (문풍지). But I couldn't open it. The sound of the disabled woman and her mother crying, leaking through the gaping cracks in the door, colored my feelings with pity (연민). I just stood there blankly, speechless. Around me, standing silently, the desolate air seemed to swirl, carrying the cries of the disabled woman and her mother, as if wilting.

After a short while, the door opened, and the person who came out was her old mother. Wiping her tears with the hem of her skirt, she looked at me and said,
"Did you see? Please come into the room," turning away to blow her nose. Having nothing to ask, nor anything more to say, I simply asked,
"Yes. Is S O in?" peering into the room. S O's mother replied,
"Yes, she's here," answering my question, then looked back into the room and said,
"Dear, the teacher is here."

Inside the room, S O was sitting turned away, still crying perhaps, unable to turn her head, just wiping her tears with her skirt tie. But seeing me, she kept her head down, shifted her body to turn around, and said,
"Please come in," looking up at me with eyes flushed red, then cast her eyes down to the floor again. I hesitated to enter. But I couldn't just turn back either. I took off my shoes and entered the room. As I was about to enter, the wooden leg (나무대리) that served as S O's leg, lying at the edge of the *maru* (wooden floor), caught my foot with a clatter (덜컥). At that moment, feeling an itchy, creepy sensation, as if someone beside me had yelled "Aebi!" <66> and thrown something disgusting at my neck, I shuddered and jumped into the room.

S O said,
"You don't have much time today, do you?" looking at me intently, differently than usual. I replied,
"I have time until about 4 o'clock. I just came by for a short visit," settling into a seat.

"Has something unfortunate happened at your home?" I tried to find out the reason for her tears, but she just said,
"No," blushing, and fell silent.

Unable to ask anything further, I just looked around the four walls, saying nothing.

S O remained quiet for a long while. Then, in a half-trembling voice,
"Teacher," she called me, then fell silent again, staring at her fidgeting fingers for a while, until my prompting
"Yes?" seemed to hasten her, and again she said,
"Teacher." I said,
"Yes," looking only at the black hair on her bowed head.

"I am disabled," she said, and the tears she had held back until now fell again, rolling silently onto the floor. Hearing this, I wanted to cry with her.

"I am disabled," she repeated the words again in a forceful tone, then collapsed face down on the floor, weeping, and said in a choked voice,
"Even though I'm disabled, I have blood and feelings. I have hot tears and bright red passion. But pitiful me, I cry alone with those tears, and no one understands them; I burn alone with that passion, but there is no one to receive it. The world only tries to push pitiful people into even more pitiful pits." She wept, sobbing. <67>

"Do you pity me, Mr. A? If you truly pity me, please understand my heart too," she pleaded, leaning against my knee and crying.

Teacher, who hearing these words would not cry? Who would not feel pity? I just held S O close and cried for a long while.

"Miss S O, please don't cry. I pity you. I truly sympathize."
"Then, will you love me, a disabled person with one leg missing, forever and ever?"

Hearing these words, I just said,
"Yes!?" and fell silent. I could not answer that question. What appeared before my eyes was my young wife. It was the young child playing with the ruler. S O pleaded,
"Yes, Mr. A, please answer me," looking up at me, her eyes filled with tears, imploringly.

Ah! Teacher, I truly pity this S O. I truly sympathize. When she sheds tears, I shed tears too. When she frets, I try to fret too. This disabled S O, fidgeting on a speck of Earth under the heavens, I feel it wouldn't be enough even if I held her tight and cried with all my might. But Teacher, in the very instant that this feeling of pity arises, hasn't love already passed by? Holding her hand and crying together with her, wasn't that already love?

However, this disabled woman tries to love me, but receiving a woman's love, my heart conversely ached. Receiving genuine love, I couldn't help but reject it.

I am too unfortunate a person to receive the fervent love of a disabled woman.

Teacher, could I have ever dreamt that the physically disabled person would become disabled in love because of me, who pitied her disability? Love is straight, not crooked; I am already a person standing on that straight path. It wasn't that I did not love my wife. <68> Then, I could not deliberately bend the straight love directed towards my wife to love this disabled woman. Just because the disabled woman is disabled, while I sympathize with her, I could not make my own love disabled. But this disabled person's tears, no, not from the moment they fell on my knee, but from the moment her love turned towards me, already made the love entwined in her heart disabled. Like her missing leg, her love was a one-sided love.

I could only say,
"Miss S O, please don't cry. My heart feels like it's melting because of Miss S O's tears. Each time a drop of Miss S O's tears falls upon my heart, it hurts and stings as if pierced by some arrow."

"Mr. A, I thought only you, Mr. A, would truly love me," S O said, then fell silent as if waiting for some answer. I could only say,
"Please stop crying. Come now, get up," wiping away uncontrollable tears.

Until yesterday, I had called those who received the love of many women fortunate. But today, hearing this disabled woman's plea, my heart of affection (情) truly ached. I knew the pain of trying to tear a single love into two parts. I knew the sting. I understood the heartbreaking misfortune of one person with a single love receiving the multiple loves of several people.

Ah! But that disabled person will become more and more disabled, won't she? From the abyss (深淵) of despair and resentment, she will look up to heaven and cry out her misfortune, won't she? How much will the pitiful sound of that cry chill my blood? Will that sound weep sorrowfully beneath my ears forever?

Teacher, should I cruelly reject the love of this woman I truly love? Should I accept it forever? Should I listen to the weeping of the pitiful one? Should I embrace the disabled woman's tearful plea (哀訴) until it is buried in my heart? <69> I merely wander at a crossroads, unable to steady my weak heart, lost.

"Yes. I understand. But I cannot answer Miss S O's words so quickly."
"Then when will you give me an answer?"
"Yes. I'll do that slowly." This exchange of question and answer passed between us.

S O, as if doubting, asked,
"Then you mean you absolutely won't love me? You mean there is absolutely no love for me in Mr. A's heart?" she looked up at me resentfully. I didn't know what to answer. Did I truly have absolute love at that moment? Truly, I did not. There might have been absolute sympathy and pity, I don't know, but there was no absolute love. There was calculation (打筭); there was hesitation. Sometimes, the itchy pronoun 'disabled person' even made me shudder.

S O, seeing my lack of response, said,
"I understand. I am eternally disabled. A possessor of love with one corner chipped away. Not only that, I..." then, in her eyes, where resignation and resentment tangled, the foolish tears dried up in an instant, leaving only what seemed like a cold smile and curse. Hearing this, I somehow felt a chill, my body trembled, and I wanted to see her tears again. And seeing her expression, seemingly filled with resignation, resentment, cold smiles, and curses, I again wanted to unravel her heart, making her weep weakly and gently. I said,
"Miss S O," taking her hand,
"I will never be able to forget Miss S O." She merely replied,
"Yes, please don't forget me. I probably won't be able to forget Mr. A either, until I close my eyes."

Seventh <70>

Leaving S O's house, I headed towards the school. My mind (心神), which had been clear (淸澄) until moments ago, became hazy and vague after visiting the disabled woman's house, turning gloomy and sentimental.

I go to school. Not so much to teach an hour of drawing (圖畵) but seeking the remuneration for it. If there is a most unfortunate crime in the world, perhaps it is this kind of person. Knowingly doing something unintended, something not in one's heart, for a lump of rice and a few pieces of kimchi to fill the stomach (充腹), how much better is that than a person driven by hunger and cold (飢寒) who steals another's property knowing it's a sin? If the conscience of one who steals another's property trembles, then my conscience must have trembled proportionally. If one sympathizes with the craving for life (生) of a person who steals due to unbearable hunger and cold, then perhaps this painter A, who deceives his own conscience to sustain his life, can also seek sympathy?

I entered the school main gate. Just then, Principal M (M敎主), perhaps on his way out from visiting the school, was about to get into a car. At that moment, that sycophantic Mr. Lee (이선생) supported Principal M's arm and guided him into the car. Seeing this, I laughed loudly. Mr. Park (박선생), seeing me laugh nearby, said,
"Why are you laughing?" glaring, "What kind of rude behavior is that?" I laughed out loud once more and said,
"Mr. Park, you don't understand the meaning of my laughter," and then,
"It's a doll. A doll. A doll with two arms and two legs that it can't use. Since it's a doll, there's nothing to say, but seeing the foolish person supporting the doll, I can't help but laugh." Then I turned around and went into the classroom.

Today is the last day of the month. Payday. Entering the office, all I could see was the accountant's demeanor. And I was just thinking about how to spend that money. Today too, I thought I should buy a hat for the child and give a scarf to my loving wife. <71>

Waiting for the salary of 25 Won, my heart felt bitter for some reason, and I felt pity for myself. And I felt needlessly annoyed.

Entering the classroom and picking up the chalk to draw on the blackboard, even all the students seemed hateful. And it felt as if those students had created my fate like this. I couldn't help but endure the hour that wasn't in my heart.

That day was the day students were supposed to bring in homework. Among the nearly 40 students, five had not brought their homework. Among them, the youngest and most shabbily dressed student, when I asked,
"Why didn't you draw the homework?" just stood there silently for a long time, then shed hot tears and kept crying. The other students tried to deceive me, the teacher, with various excuses. Looking at the crying student, and seeing his torn socks again, I felt a pang of pity and asked,
"Why aren't you answering, just crying?" As I placed my hand on his shoulder, the teacher's hand caressing his shoulder perhaps loosened his emotions further, as he sobbed even more. Then, along with a burst of sobbing, he said,
"At home, they said they don't have money and wouldn't buy me drawing paper."

Teacher, do I have the right to punish this student? Or not? I just looked at the young student with sorrowful eyes and said,
"Look here. Having a sincere heart is enough. I wasn't trying to reprimand you for not bringing the drawing, but rather to reprimand you if you lacked sincere effort. Your single drop of tear is worth many times more than the drawing you couldn't bring today."

After school (下學), I went to the office. The accountant saw me and, seemingly very kindly, said,
"Mr. A, please come here for a moment," calling me to his side. He put the salary in an envelope, placed it in my hand, and said, <72> "Use this for cigarette money." Receiving it somehow felt shameful. So I said,
"Yes. Thank you," and put it in my pocket without looking at it.

As the day grew darker and the gray evening light enveloped the entire city street, only after leaving the school gate did I take out the envelope again and check the money inside.

Inside, there was 17 Won 50 Jeon. 17 Won 50 Jeon. I stopped short. And thought,
"Why is it only 17 Won 50 Jeon?" Puzzling over it for a moment, I suddenly remembered going to N C's house. The pay for the week's effort, spent visiting a friend who had lost his wife, had been deducted by the school.

Ah! Teacher, in my hand, there is 17 Won 50 Jeon. The reward for one month's effort is 17 Won 50 Jeon. The price for the sin that shamed the conscience of a poor young painter is 17 Won 50 Jeon.

I had no choice. Holding the money placed in the gray envelope, I unconsciously walked to S O's house. Through gaps in the clouds in the sky, small stars, appearing and disappearing, seemed to look down, mocking this ridiculous young A. Gray emotions made my heart swell with indignation (鬱憤) and resentment without reason.

Why did I come to S O's house? Even I didn't know. Only upon reaching the gate did I wonder, "Why did I come here?" and thought of turning back home. But some harmony, crying (鳴) unceasingly in my chest, pulled my feet towards S O's house. However, I couldn't just enter directly without hesitation as before. The heavy air flowing out from the small house, the small door, seemed to rush towards my chest like a fast-flowing stream.

I just stood at the gate, hesitating like a thief (도적놈), peering inside.

Teacher, when I intended to give neither love nor anything else, I used to come and go without hesitation, without any anxiety. Why today did I hesitate to enter like a guilty person, my heart pounding? <73>

Intending to give love, which is not a sin, I felt my heart tremble; I hesitated to enter the house of the person to whom I would give love, which is not wrong.

I stood there for about 10 minutes. Then, I heard the cries of the disabled mother and daughter again. Those cries seemed to pierce and carve my heart even more than before. And they felt heavily sorrowful, as if loading all grief onto my chest. But there were no tears in my eyes. The 17 Won 50 Jeon, the reward for a month's labor received at school, which filled me with indignation (鬱憤), had apparently blocked even my tears.

I listened to the crying for a long while. Mixed with the weeping, the trembling voice of the old mother was faintly audible:
"S O, now you've truly become a street ghost (한길귀신)," a pitiful sound that seemed to freeze the flesh.

Only then did I understand those tears. The disabled mother and daughter had no place to shelter their bodies. Today, because of a few paltry *푼* of rent money (貰錢), they were being kicked out of this house.

In the pocket of this A, listening outside the window, there is 17 Won 50 Jeon. This A won't be wandering the streets just yet, right? I took out the 17 Won 50 Jeon from my pocket. Then, with a pencil, I wrote 'A' on the envelope. In that instant, absolute sympathy surged within my heart. It made my blood boil, hot and strong.

I silently opened the door, gently placed the money on the *maru*, and then, like a thief (절도), quickly ran out the gate on trembling legs. And without looking back, I headed towards my home.

At home, my wife will be waiting eagerly for my return. My young child, told that Daddy would buy a pretty hat when he came, must be resting his chubby hand on his head, anticipating this young father's return, right?

But today, payday, my pocket is already completely empty (탈탈이), without a single coin. <74> To my young wife, who greets me more gladly than other days upon hearing the sound of the gate opening, I have nothing to satisfy her heart. I will only deflate the joyful, leaping heart of my young child.

However, walking through the dark neighborhood as if digging into the darkness, this A's heart, for some reason, felt a satisfied joy and a vibrant pulsation of life (生).

I came again to MW Company (MW社). There, D H and W C were huddled, reading some book, and seeing me, they asked,
How did it go?" They were asking about my salary. Taking off my hat and shoes, I smiled bitterly as if choked up and said,
"Heung, the reward for my month-long effort has indeed been spent valuably." ...(End)... <75>

**Summary**

This fictional story, written as a letter from a young painter 'A' to his 'Teacher C', details A's struggles with poverty, artistic aspirations, and complex relationships in 1920s colonial Korea. He laments his difficult life, comparing it to a precarious boat journey. He describes his strained relationship with his wife, born not of ideal love but circumstance, yet finds solace in their child. His financial hardship leads to tension at home and shame at his teaching job, where he feels like a fraud. A pivotal moment occurs when his salary is docked for taking time off to attend the funeral of his friend N C's wife. Later, he encounters S O, a disabled woman he pities, who confesses her love for him. Torn between sympathy, societal judgment, and his existing family, A ultimately uses his entire meager salary (17 Won 50 Jeon) to anonymously help S O and her mother avoid eviction, finding a complex sense of fulfillment despite returning home empty-handed.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the economic hardship and social pressures faced by intellectuals and artists in colonial Korea. The narrator's constant struggle for money, the feeling of compromised integrity in his work, the stark poverty depicted (N C's rural life, S O's situation, the student unable to afford paper), and the complex emotional landscape navigating traditional expectations (marriage) versus personal feelings offer historians valuable insights into the lived experiences and psychological burdens of Koreans during that era. The mention of specific currency (Won/Jeon) and social settings (school, rented rooms) grounds the narrative in its time.

첫재
사랑하시는 C선생님께 어린 심정에서 때업시 솟아오르는 끗업는 늣김의 한마듸를 올리나이다.
시간이란 시내가 흐르는 대로 우리 인생은 그 우에서 배놀이를 하고 잇습니다. 늙은이나 젊은이나 마음 압흔 이나 가슴 쓰린 이나 행복의 송가를 놉히 외우는 이나 성공의 謳歌를 길게 부르짓는 사람이나 이 시간이란 시내에서 배놀이 하지 안는 사람이 누구입니까?
오늘 이 편지를 선생님께 올리는 이 젊은 A도 시간이란 시내에 일엽편주를 띄워 노코 끗모르는 浦口로 향하여 둥실둥실 떠갑니다.
어떠한 이는 쾌주하는 汽船을 탓스며 어떠한 이는 놉다란 돗을 달고 순풍에 밀리어 갑니다. 또 어떠한 이는 밋구녕 뚤어진 거루배를 이리 뒷둥 저리 뒷둥 위태하게 젓고 갑니다.
어떠한 배에서는 하픔하고 기지개 켜는 소리가 들리입니다. 또 어떠한 배에서는 장고를 두드리고 푸른 노래를 부르기도 합니다. 어떠한 배에서는 붉으레한 情話의 소근대는 소리가 들립니다. 어떠한 배에서는 여자의 애끈는 울음소리가 납니다. 어떠한 배 속에서는 髑髏가 춤을 추고 어떠한 배 속에서는 놀음꾼의 코 구는 소리가 납니다.＜56＞

나와 가튼 배를 띄워 가튼 자리를 지내가는 배가 몃 백 몃 천이 잇습니다. 그들은 다만 서로 바라보며 긔막혀 웃을 뿐이외다. 그리고 서로 눈물지을 뿐이외다.
선생님. 이 배가 가기는 갑니다. 한 시간에 5리를 가거나 단 1리를 가거나 가기는 갑니다. 그러나 그 배가 뒤거름 칠 리는 업슬 터이지요. 가기만 하는 배는 우리를 실어다. 무엇을 하랴 할가요? 흐르는 시간은 말이 업고 뜻이 업스매 다만 일정한 규칙대로 가기는 가겟스나 뜻업고 말업는 시간이란 시내우에 이 A는 무슨 파문을 그리어 노하야 할가요.
새벽 서리 찬바람에 차르덕 찰삭 뛰어노는 어여쁜 물결입니까? 아츰 저녁 멀리 밀려왓다 멀리 밀려가는 밀물의 스르렁 거리는 물결입니까? 초생달 갸웃두름하게 비추인 푸르럿다 희엿다 하는 깜찍한 파문입니까? 어떠튼 저는 무슨 파문이던지 그 시간이란 시내 우에 그리어 노하야 할 것이외다. 하다 못하여 시컴한 물결 우에 푸-하게 일어나는 거픔일지라도 냄겨 노코야 말 것이외다.
선생님. 그러나 그 파문을 그리랴 하나 그릴 수가 업습니다. 한울의 바람은 넘우 장하고 몰려오는 물결은 넘우 힘이 잇습니다. 인습이란 물결이 이 작은 片舟를 몰아낼 때와 육박하는 환경의 모든 시컴한 물결이 가랴하는 이 A라는 족으마한 배를 집어 삼키랴 할 때 닷을 감아라 노를 즈어라 가랴고는 합니다마는 방향을 정하랴 하나 팔에 힘이 약하고 가랴 하오나 나를 이끌어 나아가게 하는 힘잇는 발동기를 갓지 못하엿습니다. 그나 그 뿐입니까? 어떤 때에는 폭우가 내려붓고 어떠한 때에는 광풍이 몰려와 간신히 댓동거리는 이 작은 배를 사정업시 푸른 물결 속에 집어너랴 합니다.
아아, 선생님. 그나 그뿐이 아니외다. 어떠한 때는 어두운 밤이 됩니다. 울멍줄멍하는 노한 파도가 다만 시컴한 암흑 속에서 이리 뛰고 저리 뚭니다. 한울에는 희망의 별 하나 보이지 안습니다. 저-쪽 어구에＜57＞ 희미하게 비추이는 깨알가튼 등대의 깜박어리는 불도 꺼질 때가 잇습니다.
그러나 저는 가럅니다. 약하고 힘업는 두 팔 두 다리로 저-보이지 안는 浦口를 향하야 형형색색의 파문을 그리면서 가기는 가럅니다. 오늘에 그리어 노흔 파문의 한 폭이 내일에 그리일 파문을 나코 내일에 그리어 노흔 파문의 한 폭이 모래의 그것을 나하 저-쪽 浦口에 이를 때에는 大洋으로 나아가는 힘잇는 여울 물결 우에 거륵하고 꼿다운 성공의 파문을 그리려 합니다.
아아, 그 때에는 암흑에 날뛰는 미친 파도나 때업는 폭풍폭우나 밀려오는 인습의 물결이나 모든 환경의 그 모진 파도가 그 거륵하고 꼿따운 파문 하나는 지어버리지 못할 것이며 삼키어 버리지 못할 것이지요. 이 적은 一葉片舟는 그 때가 되어 바위에 부듸처 깨어지거나 물결에 씻기어 살아지거나 저는 다만 죽어가는 목구녕 속으로라도 넘치는 환희와 북바치는 깃븜으로 영생의 노래를 불을 것이외다.

돌재
오늘은 왼일인지 日氣가 전에 보지 못하게 음침합니다. 답답한 심사와 침울한 감정을 陽氣 잇고 淸澄하게 하랴 애를 썻스나 그것은 실패하엿습니다.
아츰에 밥을 먹은 저는 열 두시가 되도록 습기 찬 방바닥에 누어 잇섯습니다. 오고가는 공상이 어떠한 때는 저를 웃키더니 어떠한 때는 울리더이다. 저의 젊은 안해는 오색 조희로 바른 반지 그릇을 엽헤 노코 별가튼 두 눈을 깜박어리며 저의 입고 나아갈 두루막이 끈을 달고 잇섯나이다. 저는 저의 안해를 볼 때마다 불상한 생각이 납니다. 나히 젊은 안해의 고생살이를 생각할 때마다 저의 심정은 왼일인지 쓰립니다. 제 엽헤 안저잇는 그 젊은 안해가 과연 저의 이상을 체우는 안해는 아니외다. 사랑과 사랑이 결합하여 된 부부가 아니외다. 자각잇는 애인과 자각잇는 애인의 조화잇는 사랑은 아니외다. 그는 무엇을 밋고서 나의 안해가 되엇스며 무슨 각성을 가지고 나를 사랑하는지 알 수가 업습니다. 애인과 애인이 서로 맛나는 것이 가장 큰 대담한 일이라 하면 애인도 아니요 애인도 아닌 이 두 사람의  서로 결합된 것도 위태하게도 대담한 것이외다.＜58＞

철업는 아이가 재롱 부려 웃을 때는 저의 웃음과 저의 안해의 웃음 소리는 보이지 안는 공중에서 서로 얼크러저 입을 마춥니다. 그 때에는 모든 불평 모든 고통이 그 방안에서 내쫏기어 버립니다.
