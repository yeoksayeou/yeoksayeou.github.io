Account of Meeting Chinese Communist Party Leader Mao Zedong

Last autumn, a certain major magazine in Tokyo held a vote for the ten greatest figures of China in the last hundred years.

As a result, Hong Xiuquan, Zeng Guofan, Li Hongzhang, Kang Youwei, [obscured], Sun Yat-sen, Yuan Shikai, Chen Duxiu, Chiang Kai-shek, and Mao Zedong were selected. Not only in Japan [obscured], but also [obscured]'s "L'Humanité" [『■마니테』 - likely referring to the French communist newspaper] once featured Mao Zedong, writing in large letters, "The Great Leader of the Chinese Proletariat." Judging from this, it is indeed a fact that his [obscured] reputation internationally has already overwhelmed that of Chen Duxiu. However, for someone who knows him well, there could be no greater surprise. I was his colleague, and we lodged and ate together in the same room for several months, yet I still cannot figure out which part of him is great.

"Summer of 1924." At that time, I was working as a factory worker in Hangzhou when I was arrested following a strike incident [ten characters omitted]. I immediately wrote a letter to the Kuomintang<125> Shanghai Executive Department and requested to join the party.

At that time, Mao Zedong's signature was on that letter. Afterwards, through his introduction, I joined the party, and he occasionally sent me propaganda materials such as "[obscured]" and "New Youth" [『新靑年』]. Thus, I began corresponding with him and came to know that he was a Central Committee member of the Chinese Communist Party and also a candidate for the Kuomintang Central Committee.

The first time I met him face-to-face was the following year, in the winter of 1925, when I was at the Whampoa Military Academy [黃浦軍官學校]. Hearing the news that Mao had assumed the position of Acting Head of the Central Propaganda Department, I quickly went to find the Central Party Headquarters at No. 30 Dadao Road [大東路 - likely refers to Dadong Road or a similar name in Guangzhou where the KMT HQ was]. The Propaganda Department was on the right side floor. Inside a place as dilapidated as a crumbling temple, several rattan chairs were placed, and two shabbily dressed men in Western clothes were chatting idly. One of them was Chen Chunpu [陳春圃] — the younger brother of Madam Chen Bijun [陳璧 女史], Wang Jingwei's [注兆銘 - Wang Zhaoming] wife.

"Director Mao is out. He will return soon, so please wait."

——A man wearing wide clothes of light gray patriotic cloth [愛國布], without a hat, his long hair let down untidily, walked in steadily.

He wore worn-out leather shoes, torn here and there, making a sorry sight. His complexion was haggard, like that of a sick person. As soon as he entered the door, he hurriedly went into the next room and began reading a newspaper.

That was Director Mao, Mao Zedong. Through Chen Chunpu's introduction, I finally met him face-to-face.

"I am ○○. I apologize for causing you much trouble in various ways until now."

"Oh— Comrade! I am truly glad to meet you."

"You must be very busy with the work of the Propaganda Department."

"Not at all. Is there anything to be particularly busy about? But it's too uninteresting. Right now, I am researching the peasant problem. Without the uprising of the 320 million peasants, the so-called revolution is empty talk. Therefore, the Chinese revolution will be a peasant problem."

He continued to lecture on the peasant problem for two hours. Through this fateful first meeting, I was transferred to the Propaganda Department and came to live together with him in the same house.

His daily life was truly unreliable. He never woke up before noon each day. Yet, it wasn't that he was sleeping. He would be awake but wouldn't get up, reading the newspaper instead. And after getting up, newspaper; in the toilet, newspaper; on the rickshaw, newspaper. From morning till night, he only read newspapers, and I never saw him doing any of his actual work for the Propaganda Department. Feeling frustrated, if I even proposed a reform plan for the Propaganda Department,

He would scold me from the start, saying it was a useless thought [five characters omitted]. I even began to wonder how a leader could be like this. In fact, no matter how you looked at it, he lacked the dignity of a leader. He quarreled readily even over small matters.

Once, Shen Yanbing [沈雁氷] (now the famous Mao Dun [茅盾]), Xiao Chunu [蕭楚女], Mao, and I were playing Mahjong, and he argued with Xiao for ten minutes over a mere five cents [五仙].

His strong sense of regionalism was a major flaw.

He had a nature of completely ignoring anyone who wasn't from Hunan [湖南]. And his personality was also excessively strong. He considered everything he did to be right and everything others did to be wrong. He had a strong desire for leadership. However, he was neither a theorist nor an agitator. His writing was clumsy, and he wasn't good at public speaking either. From what I saw, he was nothing more than an official [官吏]. If he had any strengths, they were his organizational ability and his skill in meticulously reading newspapers and observing social phenomena. Not just newspapers, but also all<126>—sorts of magazines and monographs, even popular songs, he liked to read. He often said things like this:

"No matter how poor a publication is, if it is selling, we must pay close attention to it. Even books that don't sell have already exerted some social influence."

During the six months he served as Acting Head of the Propaganda Department (the official head was Wang Jingwei [注兆銘]), he produced absolutely no results. Thus, after the [obscured] Central Plenum, both he and I left the Propaganda Department at the same time. He briefly served as the head of the Peasant Movement Training Institute [農民講習所長] before leaving shortly after to inspect the peasant movement in the north.

In 1927 — during the Wuhan Government [武漢政府] era, he was still the head of the Peasant Movement Training Institute. The lack of talent in this field gave him a good opportunity, allowing him to concurrently serve as the Central Peasant Department Head [中央農民部長].

I frequently interacted with him afterwards as well, but his personality and eccentricities remained the same. As someone said, he is an "eternal patient" [永久的 病人].

Hearing that this man had returned to Hunan and started a peasant movement, knowing his character, I scoffed. But suddenly [omitted] he organized an army, and its power grew like a snowball, expanding rapidly, which greatly surprised me. Thus, he has now become the Chairman of the Central Executive Committee of the Chinese Soviet [中國 싸베트 中央執行委員 主席] and a world-famous figure. In any case, he is an extraordinary man [傑物]. Although there must surely be some reason for such an unreliable person to suddenly change so drastically, I think it is not accidental that he became such a different person.

Postscript [附■] — I state that this is a translation of the full text of an account of meeting Mao, published anonymously by a certain Japanese person from the mainland [日本 內地人 某].

**Summary**

This 1934 article presents a highly critical personal account of Mao Zedong by an anonymous Japanese author who claims to have been his colleague and housemate in Guangzhou around 1925. The author portrays Mao as lazy, undisciplined, lacking leadership qualities, quarrelsome over trivial matters, regionally biased (favoring Hunanese), arrogant, and generally unimpressive as a writer, speaker, or thinker. His only perceived strengths were organizational skills and diligent newspaper reading. The author expresses astonishment at Mao's later rise to become a major leader of the Chinese Communist movement and head of the Chinese Soviet, attributing it to unexplained drastic changes or perhaps opportune circumstances like a lack of talent in peasant mobilization.

From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects a Japanese viewpoint on a rising Chinese Communist figure, published for a Korean audience under Japanese rule. It presents a negative image of Mao, potentially aligning with Japanese anti-communist interests and attempting to shape Korean perceptions of developments in China. The anonymity of the Japanese author and the somewhat dismissive yet surprised tone towards Mao's eventual success offer insights into contemporary Japanese views and potentially propaganda efforts concerning China's internal conflicts.

처음으로 그를 面對하기는 그 翌年 즉 一九二五年 겨울 내가 黃浦軍官學校에 잇슬 이다 毛가 中央宣傳部長 代理로 着任하엿다는 消息을 듯고 나는 急히 大東路 三十號 中央黨部를 차저갓다 右側 層이 宣傳部 더럽기가 다 씨러저가는 절(寺) 갓튼 속에 藤椅子가 여러 개 노여 잇고 洋服을 입은 헙수룩한 男子 두 사람이 閑談을 하고 잇다 그 中에 한 사람이 陳春圃― 注兆銘 夫人 陳璧 女史의 令弟이다

——淡灰色 愛國布의 넓다란 옷을 입고 帽子도 안 쓰고 길다란 머리를 되는대로 느리트린 男子가 벅 벅 걸어 들어왓다

그는 게속해서 農民問題의 講議를 두 時間이나 게속하엿다 이 初對面이 奇緣으로 나는 宣傳部로 ■任되고 그와 가튼 집에서 同居케 되엿다
그의 日常生活은 참으로 信實치 못하엿다 每日 正午가 되기 前에는 起寢치 안한다 그럿타고 잠을 자고잇느냐 하면 그럿치도 안타 이기는 하고도 일어나지를 안코 新聞을 읽는다 그리고 일어나서도 新聞 便所에서도 新聞·人力車 우에서도 新聞 아츰부터 밤지 新聞만 읽을 本職인 宣傳部 일은 조곰도 하는 것이 보이지 안엇다 하도 답답해서 宣傳部 改革案이라도 ■내면
쓸데업는 생각이라고 처음부터 핀잔만 준다 (五字略) 首領이 으 이 모양인가하고 생각지 낫섯다 그는 事實 어대로보던가 袖領의 風格이 업다 그는 조고만 일에도 싸홈을 잘한다
어 沈雁氷 (지금은 有名한 茅盾) 蕭楚女·毛· 나 네 사람이 麻雀을 하는데 단 五仙問題로 蕭과 더부러 十分동안이나 口論하엿다
同鄕觀念이 强한 것은 그의 큰 缺點이다
湖南人 以外에는 全혀 아른척을 안하는 性質이다 그러고 個性이 한 너무 지나치게 强하다 自己가 할 일은 모다 正當하다하고 남의 할 일은 모다 不正當하다고 한다 그는 領袖慾이 强 하다 그러나 그는 理論家도 煽動家도 아니다 文章도 섯투르고 講演도 잘 못한다 내가 본 바로는 한 個의 官吏에 지나지 안엇다 그에게 長點에 잇다면 組織的 能力과 克明하게 新聞을 읽고 社會事象을 잘 보는 點이다 新聞 아니라 모＜126＞—든 이 雜誌와 單行本지도 심지어 流行歌지도 읽기를 조와한다 그는 각금 이런 말을 한다
『어 拙劣한 出版物이라도 팔리고 잇다는 거기에 우리는 섬세한 注意를 하지 안으면 안 된다 설사 팔리지 안는 書冊이라도 社會的으로 발서 얼마만한 影響이 밋처지고 잇기 문이다』
代理 宣傳部長(本職은 注兆銘이엿다)으로 잇섯든 半年間은 純全히 아모런 成績을 내이지 못 하엿다 그리하■ ■中全會 後 그도 나도 同時에 宣傳部를 낫다 그는 잠간 農民講習所長에 잇다가 얼마 후 北方農民運動의 視察을 낫다.
一九二七年 ― 武漢政府時代에도 그는 依然히 農民講習所長이엿는데 이 方面의 人材의 缺乏이 그에게 조흔 期會를 주어 中央農民部長을 兼하게 되엿다.
나는 그 후에도 자조 접촉해 보앗스나 그의 個性과 癖質은 如前하엿다 누가 말한바와 갓치 그는 『永久의 病人』이다.
이러든 그가 湖南에 도라가 農民運動을 始作하엿다는 말을 듯고 나는 素性을 아는지라 冷笑하엿스나 갑작이 (略) 軍을 編成하고 눈사람과 갓치 그 勢力이 볼사록 더나는데 작 놀■엿다 그리하야 지금은 中國 싸베트 中央執行委員 主席이요 ■界的으로 有名한 人物이 되엿다 何如間 傑物이다 그럿케 信實치 못하든 그가 갑잭이 突變한데는 確實히 어 原因이 잇겟스나 이러틋 사람이 되엿다는 것은 偶然치 안은 일이라고 생각한다.
