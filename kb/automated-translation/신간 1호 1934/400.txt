Endless Sorrow
						Ch'unwŏn

In early spring twilight, when the thawed ground freezes again,
After singing the sick children back to sleep,
My heart, endlessly, knows not where to turn.

Shall I rest my weary body? Lying down with eyes closed,
Yet sleep won't come, each cell tingling,
The little one turns over, I must help him pee.

When wild chives appear at the side dish shop, alongside clams,
They say shepherd's purse sprouts on the sunny side, too,
But the sorrow hidden in my heart also awakens with the spring.
(March 1932, at Sungsam-dong) <17>

My forty-first birthday passed the day before yesterday,
At this age, one should be able to settle down,
But is it because my nature is murky? I feel even more restless.

In quiet moments alone, life grows distant.
As if I alone am left behind after my generation has passed,
Hope and the energy to follow seem all drained away.

XXX work? Ah, I must have said I would do XXX work.
Did doing what I did set XX right?
I want to throw these forty years of futile life into the fire.

When unknown friends cherish me,
That is precisely when I most want to die.
Whenever they might praise me, it feels like being beaten. <18>

Having done nothing by forty, what more could I wish for?
Though it wasn't deceit, it feels just like I deceived.
To speak plainly, it is a shameless me.

Lying alone sleepless in the station building,
From somewhere, a familiar insect cries, "kwi-ttul kwi-ttul."
The pause in its crying sounds so very lonely.
(September 4, 1932, at the Sinŭiju Station hotel)

Are the mountains deep, is spring late? It's May, yet cherry blossoms fall.
Blooming unseen on a thousand-fathom cliff,
Now that this traveler arrives today, shall I watch them scatter?

The sound of the waterfall crying, what meaning does it convey?
If I listen closely, each sound has its own rhythm.
Shall I try to hear the unfulfilled will of Creation?
(May 1934, at Punsŏlt'aek, Kŭmgangsan) <19>
<17-19>

**Summary**

This poem by Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwang-su) expresses profound melancholy, disillusionment, and a sense of existential drift. Structured in sections dated 1932 and 1934, it reflects on aging (reaching forty-one), the weariness of daily life (caring for sick children), feelings of failure regarding past actions (potentially censored), shame despite others' kindness, and loneliness found even in nature (the cricket's cry, the waterfall). The poet feels disconnected from life and hope, viewing his past forty years as futile. From a colonial history perspective, the poem is significant as it reveals the inner turmoil of a prominent intellectual during the Japanese occupation. Written by Yi Kwang-su, who later became known for collaboration, the deep sense of regret, purposelessness, and shame expressed here offers historians a glimpse into the personal conflicts and psychological pressures faced by Korean elites navigating the complexities and compromises of life under colonial rule, possibly hinting at his internal struggle before or during his shift towards collaboration. The censored lines are particularly intriguing regarding unspoken regrets.

하욤업는 슬픔
						春園
이른 봄 초어스름 녹인 땅이 다시 얼제
앓고난 어린 것들 노래 불러 재고 나면
내 마음 하욤없이도 지향할 바 몰라라.

곤한 몸 쉬어볼까 눈 감고 누엇서도
세포 알알이 저려 잠을 못 이룰 제
어린 것 돌아누으니 오좀 누어주리라.

반찬가개에 모시조개 달래 나면
양짓짝 냉이싹도 돋아난다 하건마는
내 맘에 숨은 슬픔도 봄을 따라 깨어라.
(1932. 3월 崇三洞에서)＜17＞

마흔 한 째 돌도 그저께 지냇으니
나이 이만하면 맘잡을만 하건마는
타고남 흐려 그런가 더욱 들떠 하노라.

고요한 혼자 때면 인생이 멀어지네.
항렬 지난 뒤에 이 몸 혼자 떨어진 듯
희망도 따를 긔운도 다 풀린 듯하여라.

XXX일? 아아 XXX일 하노라고 하엿것다.
내 한다 하야 XX 바로 잡혓던가.
四十年 헛된 살음을 불에 넣고 싶어라.

모르는 친구들이 나를 앗겨주을적이
그적이 더더구나 죽고 싶은 적이로다.
혹시나 칭찬줄 때면 매맞는 듯 하여라.＜18＞

四十에 한 일 없이 더 바랄 것 무엇이리?
속임 아니언만 속인 것만 같은지고.
툭 털어 말슴하오면 염체 없는 내외다.

호울로 驛舍에 누어 잠 못일워 하올 적에
어디서 예 들던 벌레 귀뚤뒤뚤 우노매라.
울다가 끊는 도막이 하도 외뤄하노라.
(1932. 9월 4일 新義州驛 호텔에서)

山 깊어 봄 늦은가 五月인데 벚꽃지네.
千仞絶壁에 남모르게 피엇다가
오늘에 客子 이르니 흩어볼까 하노라.

落瀑의 우는 소리 무슨 뜻을 아뢰는고.
가만히 들어보면 소리소리 제 가락을.
造化의 다 못편 뜻을 들어볼까 하노라.
(1934. 5월 金剛山 噴雪澤에서)＜19＞
