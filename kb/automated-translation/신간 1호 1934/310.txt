◇ Alcohol and Chosŏn
Last year, the volume of alcoholic beverages manufactured in Chosŏn was 1,754,855 sŏk (石), which averages out to slightly over 1 to (斗) 6 sŭng (升) per person. The alcohol tax alone amounted to over 11.7 million Wŏn (圓), and if imported liquors are added to this, the figure would be even more astonishing.
Among these are 130 sŏk of Chosŏn-made whiskey and 283 sŏk of wine. Makgeolli (막걸리) accounts for over 1,286,000 sŏk, making up more than 70% of the total manufactured volume.

◇ Number of Mining Claims in Chosŏn
Looking at the changes in mining claims last year, which can be seen as one aspect of the Chosŏn gold mining industry (朝鮮金鑛業) in numbers, it is as follows:
The total number of mining claims as of January 1st last year was 3,714. During last year, the number of newly established claims was 733, and the number of extinguished claims was 709. Thus, there was a net increase of 624 claims, making the total number of mining claims as of January 1st this year 3,338 claims. <120>
Approximately 86%, or 630, of the 733 newly established mining claims were for gold and silver mines. Placer gold (砂金) was next with 70 claims, while others accounted for only 33 claims, it is said.

**Summary**

This 1934 article from the magazine "Kaebyŏk Sin'gan" presents a statistical overview of colonial Korea. It covers demographics (total population, households, occupational breakdown, age distribution, urban populations), economic indicators (alcohol production and tax, mining claims focusing on gold), social statistics (medical personnel, marriage and divorce rates, students studying in Japan, the decline of traditional Sŏdang schools versus modern schools, libraries), and unemployment figures. The data compares figures from the early 1930s, highlighting changes like a significant decrease in marriages and Sŏdang, alongside increases in modern schooling and gold mining activity. For historians, this article provides valuable quantitative data reflecting social, economic, and demographic conditions and transformations in Korea under Japanese rule, such as urbanization, shifts in education, the impact of economic conditions (implied by decreased marriage rates), and resource exploitation (gold mining boom). The specific categorization of occupations, medical roles, and the definition used for unemployment also offer insights into the colonial administration's framework for understanding Korean society.
