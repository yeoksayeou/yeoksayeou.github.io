**Summary**

This text from the November 1934 issue of *Kaebyŏk Sin'gan* consists of two main news items. The first details the activities of major record companies operating in Korea (Victor, Siren, Okeh, Columbia, Taihei, Polydor), including signing artists like Hong Nan-p'a and Kim Pok-hŭi, plans for new recording studios and buildings, artist tours, the release of new records ("Aesanggok," "Imja ŏmnŭn narupae," "Nado mollayo," "O Nae Sarang"), the introduction of deluxe and budget records, and competition between companies. The second item provides detailed statistics on the severe flood damage that occurred in the southern three provinces (Samnam) and Kangwŏn-do in 1934, listing figures for agricultural land loss, flooding, crop damage value, and infrastructure (roads, bridges) damage costs, province by province, as reported by the authorities.

For historians, the article offers insights into the burgeoning popular music and recording industry in colonial Korea, showing the active competition among Japanese and possibly Korean-affiliated companies, the star system involving singers and composers, and promotional strategies like tours and differentiated record formats (standard, deluxe, budget). The mention of a "popular song purification movement" at Taihei hints at cultural anxieties or controls. The detailed flood statistics provide valuable quantitative data on the impact of natural disasters and the colonial administration's documentation of such events, reflecting the scale of hardship faced by the rural population.
