기사제목 長篇 無絃琴(第一回) (Feature-Length Story: Muyeon'geum (The Stringless Zither) (Part 1))
필자 廉想涉
기사형태 소설 (Novel)

Feature-Length Story: Muyeon'geum (The Stringless Zither) (Part 1)
						Yŏm Sangsŏp

The day after the snow, the weather cleared brightly, and the sunlight felt warm, so I thought it was thawing a bit. But perhaps because I had been inside the warm office all day, the evening wind seeping into the nape of my neck felt unexpectedly harsh.
Ch'ansu pulled up his overcoat collar to cover his front, put on his gloves, and turned into the side alley, nearly slipping. The snow that had been ankle-deep when he went to work in the morning had half-melted during the day, only to freeze solid in the evening wind. Although he wore rubber overshoes, a slight misstep felt like it could send him sprawling on his backside.

5:20 PM – Even in this mid-winter when the sun didn't spread its light until well after 7 AM, the office workers, who had to report at 8 AM, work attached to their desks until 5 PM with only a 30-minute lunch break and barely a moment for a cigarette, would rush out the door as if hearing a fire alarm the moment the 5 o'clock bell rang. By the fifth chime, everyone, without exception, would be outside pulling out their cigarette cases.<75> It was the daily habit of these office workers. But Ch'ansu was 20 minutes late, emerging alone only now. He absolutely needed 30 won by tomorrow, but afraid that the ill-disposed Western general manager might overhear, he had hesitated for two days. Every day after closing time, he lingered behind, peeking into the general manager's office. Today, he finally called out An Chindal, who acted like a secretary running errands before the general manager, to negotiate indirectly. Last month, due to a trivial matter, the general manager had issued an expulsion order, and he had almost been kicked out. Asking for an advance of more than half his salary now would likely earn him nothing but a scolding. He disliked speaking directly to the general manager, and An Chindal was the type who liked being approached for such requests, so he had called him out and asked.

"Hey, I was wondering why you've been wandering around alone lately when leaving. Was it because of this?"
Before even fully hearing Ch'ansu's urgent situation, An Chindal's chiding remark made Ch'ansu lose heart. Did he mean to imply there was some other reason? It felt unpleasant, as if An Chindal suspected him of loitering because he wanted to catch the eye of the beautiful typist in the general manager's office. But truthfully, it wasn't as if he hadn't been reprimanded for loitering around the general manager's office when that beautiful typist first arrived the month before last.

"I waited for a chance to talk, but the day before yesterday the general manager wasn't visible, and yesterday you seemed busy too, so I couldn't say anything. The deadline is urgent, and I absolutely must have the money today to avoid trouble. Sorry to bother you, but please put in a good word for me."
He offered this as an explanation for his recent loitering while making his plea. But of course, he didn't get a clear answer. After a long string of complaints, delivered as if dispensing his own money with reluctance and self-importance, An Chindal said it was difficult right now and told him to check again tomorrow.

On one hand, it was merely asking to use money early that he would earn in half a month. It would have been more straightforward <76> to offer to pay half a month's interest on the 30 won. But having begged and pleaded, laying out all his desperate circumstances like a beggar, felt, upon later reflection, like utterly crushing his self-respect. He felt like scratching his head vigorously out of frustration. Still, seeing a glimmer of hope that it might work out tomorrow, Ch'ansu felt a bit encouraged.

As if just remembering, Ch'ansu pulled out a cigarette and lit it, fumbling for matches in his pocket with his gloved hand, pausing as he struck one. Just as he was about to take a satisfying drag, a woman's voice suddenly called from behind,
"Oh my! Excuse me," accompanied by the sound of someone about to fall. Holding the extinguished matchbox, Ch'ansu startled and turned around. It was the typist he had just seen putting on her overcoat in the general manager's office. Slipping and nearly falling forward, she barely caught herself, her face flushed red as she smiled.

"The ground is so slippery! I almost pushed you over, sir," she said, still laughing cheerfully.

"That would have been alright... I almost fell myself just coming out the door," Ch'ansu replied, laughing along as he relit his cigarette.

However, what immediately came to mind was An Chindal's earlier question about why he was loitering around the general manager's office. Could it be that this woman thought his loitering for the past two or three days was because of her, and had followed him? When Ch'ansu parted ways with An Chindal and came out, he had indeed seen through the glass window that this woman was taking down her coat, adjusting her hat, and looked ready to leave soon. It seemed she had seen him turn into this alley and followed him, making him wonder, "What's this about?" feeling a sense of suspicion.

Although he had never spoken with her before, whenever they encountered each other in the company, she would offer a subtle greeting and a slight smile. Once, when he was called into the general manager's office about to be fired, she had looked at him with what seemed like pity and sympathy as he sat there crestfallen. But he had dismissed that as mere feminine courtesy, not any special favor. Even now, <77> it was probably meaningless; perhaps she just didn't want to walk alone and followed him for company. In any case, having only seen her from afar, her voluntarily approaching him like this, becoming his companion and initiating conversation, was unexpected and felt somewhat novel.

"Are you heading this way home?" Ch'ansu spoke first.

"No. Is your home this way, sir?"

"N-no. By the way, why 'sir'?"
Office workers in sales didn't usually use the honorific 'sŏnsaengnim' (sir/teacher) amongst themselves. Even if it were used like barbers, cobblers, tailors, or, for women, café girls used to use 'chusa' (an old title), it sounded unfamiliar to Ch'ansu's ears, and if it didn't carry that meaning, it felt somewhat embarrassing to hear.

"What does it matter? I call you 'sir' because you seem deserving of it."
Ch'ansu tilted his head but didn't press further.

Turning through the narrow alley, Munhŭi's high-heeled shoe slipped, and she almost fell again, but Ch'ansu quickly caught her.
"Why am I so clumsy today? It's fortunate this is just an alley. What would I have done if I'd fallen flat on my back on the main street?" Munhŭi said, laughing sheepishly.

"You would have provided some comfort to the citizens (市民)," Ch'ansu's remark sounded a bit cynical.

"Pardon?..." Munhŭi chuckled, glancing sideways.
"Seriously though, imagine falling sprawling on the main street, and just then a newspaper photography team passes by and snaps a picture – that would be quite a sight!... You know how they publish photos of modern girls holding umbrellas awkwardly in the rain, <78> forward, backward, sideways? Similarly, they could title it 'Street Skating of a Pitiful Typist Released After Nine Hours Confined in a Brick House'... hahaha. But 'confined in a brick house' sounds too gloomy! That wouldn't be comforting at all, would it?" she chattered on, laughing as if struck by a girlish fantasy. Although her chatting so freely, like someone she'd known for years, was a bit surprising, it was bright and pleasant.

"Do you like being in the newspaper that much? If it comes to that... In America, a certain young literary aspirant took his poems to the chief editor of a major newspaper and pestered him to publish them. The editor reportedly said, 'If you really want them published, carry this poem in your bosom and commit suicide by train. Then I'll publish it.' Following that logic, although the Han River is frozen now, you could wait until next spring... hahaha."
Ch'ansu laughed off his own rambling, wondering why he was spouting such pointless nonsense.

"Even then, they say a lone death is a dog's death, so they only publish a small notice. If you're going to do it, it should be a double suicide (情死)."

"Do as you please..."
After a brief pause, Munhŭi asked again, as if anew,
"Really now, where are you going that you take this way every day?"

"How did you know that?"

"I can see you from the upper floor (우칭); you always go this way."
Hearing that, it seemed she had indeed seen him from the upper floor today and hurried after him.

"So, were you curious about where this path leads – to heaven or hell – and followed me, stumbling and falling like this, just to find out?" Ch'ansu asked, smiling pointlessly again.

"Even if not, yes! I was curious whether it was the road to heaven or hell, so here I am, following you, slipping and tripping," Munhŭi replied, also laughing pointlessly.<79>

Turning into the alley right next to the company led through a winding path whose name Ch'ansu, despite walking it daily, still didn't know, but it eventually led to the library he frequented. After work, Ch'ansu almost always stopped by the library via this nearby route to read the newspaper and, if time permitted, a book.

As they emerged from the alley and Ch'ansu crossed the street, Munhŭi followed, but upon hearing Ch'ansu say he was going into the library, she exclaimed,
"Are you going to the library now?"

"Why?..."

"On a cold day like this, shouldn't you hurry home, eat something warm, and sit cozily enjoying your wife's (아씨님) company and your baby's cute antics?"
Munhŭi, though likely over twenty and looking intelligent and seemingly demure, said such things.

Ch'ansu just smiled and was about to part ways when she said,
"Won't you accompany me just over there? The ground is slippery, you should protect me a little..."
She seemed reluctant to part ways, acting with a boldness somewhat unexpected for a young woman, as if they were old friends.

"Where are you going?..."
Ch'ansu found it hard to just brush her off and go in. Politeness aside,
"What on earth is this woman thinking?" His curiosity suddenly flared.

"My nephew's (족하놈) first birthday is the day after tomorrow, so I was going to Missŭgosi (미쓰고시) to buy him something. It's cold, and you won't get much reading done going into the library so late anyway. Let's go together, have some tea, and talk."
In truth, Ch'ansu wasn't idle or cheerful enough to be gallivanting with a young woman, but thinking it wouldn't hurt, he silently followed her.<80>

"Do you go to the library every day?"

"Yes, every day."

"What books do you read?"

"Where's the time to read books? I just go to read the newspapers."

"Newspapers? Can't you read them at home?" Munhŭi scoffed, as if it were a pointless remark.

"In my circumstances (형세), can I even afford newspapers? Even if I could, I don't have the time."
In fact, his father had cancelled the newspaper subscription, saying the monthly fee of 1 won was too difficult, but even if he wanted to read one, he also wanted to read a Japanese newspaper. Yet, on his salary of 50 won, after setting aside 10 won for his own pocket money to cover cigarettes, socks, and other miscellaneous expenses for the month, he couldn't afford newspapers. Leaving early in the morning and returning after dark, some days he was too tired to even properly read a single newspaper. So, his routine was to stop by the newspaper reading room at the library on his way home daily and skim through them. Among these, he had developed a taste for reading serialized novels (連載小說), keeping up with three Japanese ones simultaneously. This was his sole hobby and, at the same time, a form of study. If he wasn't hungry, he would go upstairs to browse magazines or spread out his own books that he carried with his lunch box (벤도갑), but he couldn't really study diligently.

Munhŭi dragged Ch'ansu into Missŭgosi and spent a while buying a baby suit, underwear, and tiny thimble-like socks for the first birthday celebration (돌잡이). Then she suddenly asked,
"Sir, how old is your baby?"

"He'll be one year old when he's born,"
Ch'ansu, who wasn't even married, chuckled inwardly.<81>

"Well then, let me know when he's born. Invite me for the twenty-first day celebration (삼칠일)," she volunteered, setting the date.
It seemed she had intended to buy something for his child while shopping for baby items, but Ch'ansu just replied, "Sure," noncommittally. After buying everything, she dragged him to the restaurant, sat him down, and then, as if struck by a sudden thought, asked,
"Sir, you write poetry (詩), don't you?"

"If a laborer (고공사리) in an ironworks (철공장) wrote poetry, the poetry itself would weep," Ch'ansu replied with a cold smile (랭소). In fact, his colleagues did tease him, calling him "poet." They did so because they saw him carrying literary books along with his lunch box, but also mockingly (조롱) called him "poet" when he sometimes made absurd remarks or didn't join in their boisterous laughter, looking deeply melancholic.

"Well, what does it matter what you do? Anyway, they say you study a lot."

"Who says that?"

"I'll tell you who said that next time," Munhŭi replied with a gentle smile.
Ch'ansu just looked up at Munhŭi with a puzzled expression (낫빗).

"Even though I know nothing, please show me something you've written sometime."

"Hahaha..."
Ch'ansu just laughed loudly as if dumbfounded (기가 막히다), but guessing she might be a "literary girl" (문학소녀), he felt a sense of gladness stemming from the pure heart (순정) of youth yearning (동경) for literature. He also began to suspect that her motive (동긔) for seeking closeness might lie there. Her subtle greetings before, and her deliberately following him today to chat like they had been friends for years – perhaps it all stemmed from her own sense of camaraderie and trust (신뢰), based solely on their mutual love for literature.<82> However, her hint that she had heard about him from someone else remained puzzling. In any case, the way she seemed to regard him as a quite substantial writer (작가) was rather embarrassing.

Munhŭi silently sat, watching the steam rise from the fragrant teacup (차ㅅ잔), occasionally lifting the cup to her moist (촉촉히), red lips, her face beaming with a gentle smile.
"I've actually been thinking for a while that I'd like to meet you once and talk. Today is truly delightful!"
She brought this up, glancing subtly at the man.

"Your words are more than I deserve (과분한 말슴). To think you regard someone like me so highly..."
Ch'ansu replied verbally with a somewhat idle (실 업슨) tone, but he couldn't help being drawn into the woman's cheerful mood.

"It's not because I've known you for a long time, sir. Honestly, I'm very lonely. Whether I go home or come to the company, I don't have a single person to talk to. My friends have all gotten married and scattered. Even if I meet them after not seeing them for a few years, we naturally feel distant (설면설면)..."
Ch'ansu sympathized with this too. The feeling of loneliness both at home and at work – how similar it was to his own. He wondered if she too had lost her mother. And the way old school friends naturally drift apart as their paths diverge and interests change – he had experienced that himself. In any case, their meeting under such similar circumstances seemed to rapidly deepen (독구어) their friendship (友情).

However, Ch'ansu found it difficult to express that someone like him couldn't be a cheerful or beneficial friend, so he remained silent.
Munhŭi drank her tea again and sat quietly for a moment, then said,
"Although I see you every day, please come visit my home sometime when you're bored. It's XX Nagwŏn-dong (락원동). Come eat first-birthday rice cakes the day after tomorrow."

"Well..."<83>

"I'd like to visit your home too and borrow some good books, but I wonder what your wife (아씨) would think? Even with friends, it feels awkward visiting them once they're married."

"There's no wife at home to think anything, and no books worth seeing, but it's really not a place for you to visit."
In truth, Ch'ansu dreaded the thought of this woman visiting and being seen by his father, which would immediately cause an uproar. Besides, he disliked showing his meager living situation.

"Why?... Did your wife go away to give birth?" she asked, bringing up the wife again. When Ch'ansu just smiled and remained silent, Munhŭi, as if finally understanding, blushed slightly with a maiden's instinct and nodded lightly.

Ch'ansu couldn't figure this woman out. If she simply wanted interaction based on a shared literary interest, the matter was simple. But her probing about whether he had a wife and child seemed to suggest she sought something more. Yet, her attitude and conversation were too open for a typical young woman seeking love.

"So you're still single (獨身)?"
Finally, the blunt question came. Perhaps this was how modern young women were, but it still didn't seem like words that should come from a maiden's lips.
She seemed more like a married woman or one who had failed in marriage.

"That's why you shouldn't ask someone like me to come visit," Ch'ansu said, smiling pointlessly.

"Why? Isn't it because you're a bachelor that you visit a maiden, and because I'm a maiden that I seek out a bachelor? Otherwise, it would be rather risky. It would look strange to others. Imagine a married woman visiting a bachelor, or a maiden visiting a married man. Of course, as purely platonic friends, it wouldn't matter, but isn't there still more risk than in the association between a purely single man and woman? Look at the news these days about poison suicides (陰毒自殺) and whatnot. Where does the cause lie?"<84>

Ch'ansu was once again surprised by her bold words. If this woman hadn't graduated from a girls' high school and studied English or English literature in Tokyo, hearing such uninhibited talk at her age might make anyone doubt her propriety. But thinking about it calmly, her words weren't entirely without reason.

"If that's the case, why did you invite me over earlier when you still thought I had a wife and child?"
Ch'ansu tried to pin her down with this.

"That's different. I've transcended things like marriage and romance (戀愛), so at least for me, there's no such risk. Whether you're single or not doesn't matter for my association with you. Just wait and see. Hahaha."
Munhŭi's laughter was bright.

"In that case, I'll come visit. I'll come visit with an easy mind."
Ch'ansu laughed along, but felt somewhat dazed, as if he'd been outmaneuvered by her.

"Please do come visit. But why would you come without an easy mind?"

"I meant I wouldn't go, fearing you might think I was visiting often with ulterior motives."

"Hahaha... That shows your strong pride and extreme fastidiousness (潔癖), but on the other hand, it also stems from having too little spirit (覇氣). It means you lack the passion and spirit to conquer a woman – no, if 'conquer' sounds wrong, let's say to make a woman's love = heart and body your own. You worry about every little thing a woman might think, so you won't even visit when invited. At this rate, you'll be a lifelong failure in romance. And a failure in romance is likely to be a failure in other endeavors too. If you lack passion and spirit even in romance, won't that be even more true for business?"
Ch'ansu felt like he was listening to a sermon on romance.<85>

"But you shouldn't confuse romance and business."

"Who confused them? I was speaking about passion and spirit. That's why a businessman, if he decides not to pursue romance, simply won't, but if he does, he will succeed."
Munhŭi, seemingly pleased with her own eloquence, flushed slightly and drank her tea deliciously before changing her tone to a gentle one,
"Anyway, it's fortunate that you haven't married yet." Again, she spoke in riddles.

"Why?..."

"For various reasons. I'll tell you later..."
The words coming from her small, pretty, red lips remained riddles.
(Does she mean to suggest a match? Or does she mean she wants a long association with me?...)
Whatever the reason, her saying it was fortunate he remained single was a puzzle. Especially coming from a young woman's lips – even if she weren't a maiden, but a similarly single woman – it was hard to just let it pass. But then again, it also felt like a game of hide-and-seek. While claiming to have transcended romance and marriage, her words urging him about lacking the spirit or passion to conquer a woman seemed like taunting him to catch her while running away.
(Perhaps most men grow old prematurely, their minds consumed by such things...)
While thinking this, Ch'ansu realized his own mind was consumed with trying to understand the woman's true intentions.
(Should I tell her I'm engaged? Or not?...)
It seemed waiting to see what she said next and revealing it then wouldn't be too late, but it also felt like deceiving her, making him look foolish. Considering the possibility of being toyed with (롱락) by the woman, struggling, and then collapsing, it seemed smarter (령리한) to reveal everything <86> and withdraw early on.

"Actually, I'm already..."
Ch'ansu started, looking up at her face. Munhŭi blinked and looked back, then asked with a gentle smile,
"Why?... Are you saying you're already engaged?" Ch'ansu had expected surprise or disappointment, but seeing Munhŭi smile gently without any change in expression – though perhaps she was hiding her true feelings – he felt deflated and just smiled weakly.

"Who is she?... Where is she from?"

"Does her origin matter?"

"Of course, it must be a love marriage (戀愛 結婚)?"

"Well - we'll have to wait and see."
Ch'ansu, finding it tiresome to answer, just smiled it off.

"They say marriage is speculation (投機), but sir, are you really going to gamble your whole life away like some top-knotted country bumpkin selling off his fields to pay the matchmaker, without knowing anything?" Munhŭi asked sneeringly.

"Hmph!..." Ch'ansu rested his chin on his elbows on the table, then leaned back, stretching his shoulders before sitting up straight again.
"Then what can I do? Things like romance, poetry, literature... maybe they'll be possible if I'm born again..."

"That sounds gloomy. Why do you speak so listlessly?"
Munhŭi felt pity for the man. He seemed like a man suffering from heartbreak (실련한), unable to resolve his pent-up feelings, with no prospect of dedicating himself to literature and achieving so-called success. He seemed resigned to throwing everything away – his body and soul included – and just living day by day.<87> His despair was profound, and she felt sympathy.

"If that's the case, why did you get engaged? You should have studied more."
Munhŭi changed the subject after they sat facing each other for a moment.

"Can one study empty-handed? It's not that I don't want to, but family circumstances urgently require a breadwinner, and as the eldest son (맛아들), I have duties to fulfill. Can I avoid it? In the end, our kind of marriage is like acquiring a lifelong laborer woman (永年勞役婦) who comes along based on the simple condition of guaranteeing meals for life. But can even I trust that those meals will be guaranteed for life? They say it's a world of deception, but isn't it truly about deceiving and being deceived? Once the yoke of marriage is placed upon us, we don't know why we live, but we must eat and dress to survive. To do that, we fuss and fret, have children, and raise them. First off, it's cheap. Hahaha... If that's what living is, then so be it. We just live day by day."

"Thinking about it realistically, perhaps married life ultimately boils down to such results, but still, shouldn't there be flowers, poetry, and dreams? Shouldn't there be ideals?"

"Saying there *should* be is theory, isn't it? That's truly a dream, isn't it?... What did you say earlier? That you were released after being confined (갓첫다가) in a brick house for 9 hours? Are there flowers in that brick house? Is there poetry? Are there dreams? When I go home and lie down on the heated floor, my body might stretch out, but I do have chaotic, disturbing dreams. Hahaha..."
Ch'ansu, having nowhere else to vent his frustration, deliberately used coarse language like "lying down on the heated floor" to express his sneering dismissal.

"If you only think in such terms, the world seems dreary (신푸녕스럽고) and life loses its pleasure (자미). Try to think more cheerfully. Try to make your life splendid."<88>

"It might be partly my innate personality, but trying to force things that aren't meant to be just means living according to others' tune (장단에 사는 수). What can be done about that?"

"This is the first time we've talked like this, but sir, you seem prematurely old at heart (마음이지레 늙으섯서). On top of that, if you get married and have children, it'll be even worse..."
Munhŭi said this, perhaps lamenting his fading youth or worrying that he might simply wither away (자즈러질까보아).

"Even though you say that, aren't you also happy about getting married?"
Munhŭi changed tack, teasing the old bachelor (로총각).

"Happy? I'm so thrilled I can't sleep," Ch'ansu replied, again with a cold sneer.
In reality, he couldn't sleep due to money worries.

"Anyway, I'm disappointed!"

"Why?"

"Just because."

"Someone else is getting married, and you don't even offer congratulations (치하)..."

"Congratulations are congratulations..."
Munhŭi showed that enigmatic smile again.
Ch'ansu silently stood up.

As they parted ways outside Missŭgosi, Munhŭi urged (똥기엇다) him once more to come eat the birthday rice cakes the day after tomorrow. As she boarded the streetcar, Munhŭi whispered in his ear again,
"I'm disappointed..." Ch'ansu just smiled, but he felt disappointed too.<89>

Getting off the streetcar, Ch'ansu walked towards his home in Hyŏnjŏ-dong (현저동), facing the Mohwagwan wind. His head felt somewhat dazed (띙한), yet his mood was good. It was as if he had been fuzzily intoxicated by a fine, fragrant wine and was now sobering up in the cold wind – pleasant amidst the confusion, yet also regretting (앗갑기도) the fading warmth of the moment. It felt like his first love from five years ago had returned, or like his long-dormant youth had reawakened. However, thinking back on the events of five years ago, they now seemed like mere child's play (솟곱작난). Comparing that experience with the impression and emotion of meeting Munhŭi now, it felt as if he was only just truly understanding what a woman was.

But the woman certainly hadn't said she loved him. Rather, there were hints of manipulation (롱락) that felt somewhat unpleasant. He didn't think he loved her either. And yet, just thinking of her sparkling eyes and cheerful voice felt like emerging into the open ground after crouching long in a dreary (을쓴연스러운), gloomy shadow, feeling the spring breeze and gazing at the spring sky for the first time (비롯오).
(Why did she insist so sincerely that I visit? Was it to tell me that story she mentioned – the one she'd tell 'later'? What could it be?)
Ch'ansu found it hard to wait for the day after tomorrow. But ultimately, he felt an anxiety that he would somehow be deceived by her.
(But what if I hadn't been engaged?...)
Empowered by the woman's word "disappointed," he entertained this hypothesis. His imagination took flight, and just as Munhŭi had said, a world of bright, splendid flowers, poetry, and dreams spread out limitlessly (번져나가는). The more he thought this way, the more he lamented and resented not having met her sooner. But rather than not meeting sooner, the fact that he hadn't had the chance earlier despite seeing her every day nearby seemed like fate, something best resigned to. Moreover, looking at the practical issues, it wasn't likely such a person would come live in this house and manage the household, nor would it work even if she did. So, perhaps it was better this way, he thought.<90>

Indeed, as he reached his front gate and pushed the door open, the creaking sound shattered the beautiful dream.
The absence of a mother in the house was not a recent development, but it always felt desolate.
His younger sister hurried out, set the dinner table, and left.
"Has Father (아버니) come home and gone out again?"
"He hasn't come back yet."
It was a perfunctory greeting; there was nothing more to say. His younger sister, worn down by hardship, was already withered before her time, even before marriage, her expression bleak. Since the stepmother (게모) passed away last year, this sister had taken charge of the household. If the stepmother hadn't died, this year would have been her turn to marry. But plans had been altered (밧고아서), and now the sequence dictated that Ch'ansu had to bring in a homemaker (살림꾼) first before his sister could marry.
(Ask Chang Munhŭi to come live in this house!)
Ch'ansu, still feeling the lingering excitement from earlier, thought this inwardly while eating, then reproached (남으래는) himself for entertaining such fantasies when no one had offered.

In the inner room, his sister sat with the younger siblings, likely sewing, without a sound (찍소리). Of the four siblings, the youngest brother and sister were half-siblings (이복). It wasn't about discriminating between full (동복) and half-siblings, but when Ch'ansu came home, the children scattered like mice finding holes. Though Ch'ansu might internally pity and cherish his younger siblings, he remained silent like a mute who'd eaten honey (꿀먹은 벙어리), so the children naturally avoided him (꿀치). Even the sound of the youngest sister reading her textbook (독본) ceased, leaving the house eerily quiet (괴괴하다) except for the sound of Ch'ansu's spoon.

If only he acted cheerful and lively, perhaps installing a radio (라듸오) or playing the gramophone (유성기), the motherless children wouldn't seem so withdrawn, and the house might brighten a little. But somehow, Ch'ansu couldn't muster the mood. Having lost his mother at nine, <91> even with a stepmother who was reasonably decent, he had grown up lonely. His father, having aged in commerce since youth, lived by diligence and frugality. He was neat and strict, and with finances worsening in old age and having lost his second wife, his temper had sharpened recently. Warm affection or understanding between father and son was simply unattainable. Ch'ansu's melancholy likely stemmed from these various causes, settling heavily (묵업게) in his heart like ash settling (앙금) in water.

After the dinner table was cleared, his father came in. Ch'ansu went and stood by his father's place at the head of the table (상머리).
"Sit there. So, did the money come through?"
"It should be ready tomorrow. If it is, I'll send it the day after tomorrow."
Even as he said this, thoughts of Munhŭi surfaced in Ch'ansu's mind.

"You say you'll send it the day after tomorrow if it's ready tomorrow! Always 'tomorrow, tomorrow,' procrastinating (청처짐하게), letting good opportunities slip by. What do you intend to achieve acting so sluggishly?"
"It will definitely be ready tomorrow."

"If it's too late, it'll be embarrassing for the other side. By the way, someone came again today. They said they absolutely can't manage, so we need to send everything, including the cotton padding (솜), all wrapped up (싸가는). We budgeted 150 won, but can you manage at least 100 won? How much will your year-end (년말) bonus (상여금) be this time?"

"I won't know until they give it, but how much of a bonus could it be? Maybe just a few tens of won (멧 십원)."

"Hah- this is a big problem."
His father, having hastily arranged the marriage pending the stepmother's memorial service without regard for budget, wasn't without some funds himself,<92> but he worried (로심) about money. In marriages between needy families, the groom's side provides betrothal gifts (납채), while the bride's side provides the dowry/trousseau (선채), making it harder for a poor groom.
"By the way, since you're getting married, will they collect contribution money (부조ㅅ냥) at your workplace? Roughly how much do you think it will amount to?"
His father was already factoring even this into his calculations.

After discussing such matters for a while, Ch'ansu returned to his room and slumped into his chair at the desk as if throwing his body down.
Why was he going through with such a paltry marriage? If he had his way, he wanted to break it off immediately and flee somewhere far away. But opposing his father's plans would only lead to shouting, and his circumstances didn't allow for opposition. He had been passively (다수긋이) dragged along until meeting Munhŭi today, which had undeniably stirred waves (출렁거려) in his heart.
(What if I didn't go through with this marriage?...)
Ch'ansu tried hypothesizing this way now. But weighing the pros and cons, considering the family situation, there seemed no way to avoid it (모 피할). If he truly wanted to escape, the only way was to leave home. Like a bride running away the day before her wedding. But for whom? For what reason? Even if a way to study suddenly materialized, there was no reason he couldn't study after getting married, supporting his elderly father, raising his young siblings, and freeing himself from worries about dependents (후고의 렴려).
(But what if Munhŭi agreed to come to me?...) He hypothesized this once more. In that case, running away from home would seem justified, unavoidable. But that was a dreamlike fantasy.
(I don't know! I just don't know!...) Ch'ansu clasped his head with both arms and threw himself onto the floor.

However, when leaving the house for work the next morning, though his head felt heavy from a night of fantasies and torment, he felt refreshed after a good stretch (긔지).<93> As he entered the company, the usual sound of steel being hammered in the back factory (뒤ㅅ공장), which always made him frown (찝흐려지든), seemed unheard today, his mood so good. Glancing sideways (겻눈질) as he passed the general manager's office, he saw Munhŭi give a slight acknowledging smile. If no one else were watching, he would have responded, but he passed by.

During the noon (오정때) lunch break, he met An Chindal and pressed him about yesterday's request.
"Huh? What?..." An Chindal feigned ignorance as if hearing it for the first time, then said, "Let's meet when you leave," and hurried off.
Ch'ansu felt annoyed at his indifference to another's plight, but since he was told to wait for a reply (하회), he decided to linger behind again today, partly to catch up on some slightly delayed work. He wasn't without a secret hope that Munhŭi might accompany him again today.

While others repeatedly (련해련방) glanced at the clock (시게), sweeping documents from their desks into drawers (설합), waiting for the five o'clock bell, Ch'ansu alone sat leisurely (유산 태평), dipping his steel pen (철필) into the inkwell again and again (너코). Seeing this, the colleague sitting opposite him remarked,
"Mr. Kim, what's gotten into you lately? But it won't do any good. It'll still be exactly 50 won, not a penny more (우수리)," he sneered. Ch'ansu lowered his head and snorted, but truthfully, it felt a bit awkward to be the only one working while others had finished and were just waiting for the minutes to pass.

"Hey, isn't Christmas (크리쓰마쓰) about half a month away?" the person next to him laughed again.
Until now, this company hadn't given year-end bonuses, merely paying the month's salary early, before Christmas (크리쓰마쓰), perhaps adding 10 won or so on the last day of the month (금음 날), and dealing with it cursorily (쓱싹하는).
This company imported various industrial machines directly from Germany (독일) and America (미국), assembled them here, and sold them. Thus, it had a fairly large assembly plant (구미다데공장) in the back. On one side, it also managed the wholesale (도매업) of Russian (로서아) petroleum and aviation/automotive gasoline (까솔린), so it also had petroleum and gasoline warehouses. Anyway, despite operating on such a large scale, perhaps the income and expenses (수지) truly didn't balance yet, <94> resulting in no bonuses. Or perhaps they assumed that Koreans would scramble desperately even for a mere two or three won, so just appeasing them slightly would prevent major complaints. Whatever the reason, the remark implied Ch'ansu was suddenly feigning diligence hoping to snag even a penny more of the paltry sum given at Christmas, which made Ch'ansu purse his lips.

"But Mr. Kim is a quasi-bell (준 방울), so even without going to such lengths, he's more secure than us."
'Bell' (방울) was the nickname for An Chindal, the one Ch'ansu had asked for the advance. Because he was the general manager's trusted favorite, always tagging along (뀌어하고), a witty colleague had coined the nickname, and it stuck. His short, plump figure, rosy complexion (혈색), and handsome white face (투상) suited the name 'Bell'. Seeing him standing face-to-face with the pole-like (바지랑대) general manager, acting charmingly, or walking side-by-side, one looked attached (매여달려 바리는 것 갓고), the other carried (뀌어찬 것 갓타야), making 'Bell' seem an even more roughly (어수히) apt nickname. However, as in any society, those close (긴한) to superiors often aren't well-regarded by peers. Perhaps because An Chindal, though outwardly smooth, wasn't entirely straightforward, besides 'Bell,' he had another nickname derived from his name: dropping the 'Jin' (진) left 'Andal' (안달), which, when misheard or twisted, became 'Seondal' (선달). An Chindal – Seondal – Andal – Bell... they called him these names not just secretly (뒤ㅅ구멍) amongst themselves (저이끼리), but some shameless ones even used them to his face (당자).

"Quasi-bell (준방울)? How about 'stiff bell' (뻣건방울)? But speaking of which, if we were to rank the handsome men among us, after Bell, it would surely be Mr. Kim. What he lacks in facial features, he makes up for in figure (풍신)."

"Hey, you shut your mouth! It's not the squirrel's (다람쥐) turn (나올막). Shut up (답치고) and sit down," someone retorted to a short, dark-complexioned (까마잡잡한) colleague who tried to protest (타내랴니까). Then the person who called Kim Ch'ansu 'quasi-bell' proceeded to outline the qualifications for being a 'Bell.'<95>

"First, to be a Bell, one must be handsome and small (체소). One must have an effeminate manner (녀태). One must practice abstinence while following the Donkey (떵키-)..."
"Enough-"
Someone cut him off. 'Donkey' (떵키-) was the nickname for the general manager.

"Now, speaking of our Mr. Kim, though not small, he certainly counts among the handsome. Though not effeminate, being a poet, he is refined. As for smoking, seeing as he no longer makes 'business trips' (출장) to the Tŭngsŏn'gak (登仙閣) these days, he seems to have resolutely quit smoking (단연 단연(斷然, 斷煙)). And seeing him display such sacrificial spirit, working overtime without pay with such diligence (근념), it seems likely he'll catch the Donkey's eye."

They spent the remaining five minutes idly chatting like this, seeming restless. When the bell finally rang, they shot up as one (왓작 니러나서), jostling for position (압흘 다토아), grabbing their coats and hats, and streaming out (물려나간다). Their most urgent business (정사) was cigarettes.

The company enforced a smoking ban because the petroleum and gasoline warehouses were right next door, and gasoline was constantly used in the factory, making fire a serious risk. However, if they were truly concerned, they could have set up a smoking room (끽연실), even if not far away (멀직 안이라도), but the lack thereof was likely due to the general manager's personal preference, as he himself was not a smoker (애연가). The earlier pointless remark about Ch'ansu no longer making 'business trips' to Tŭngsŏn'gak referred to nothing other than the cramped back toilet (뒤ㅅ간). Whether one went there intending to relieve oneself and sneaked a puff, or whether the craving was so strong that one endured the stench just for a drag, sitting there stubbornly – who knows? But after a period of comings and goings, entering that thick (자욱한) haze might make a troublesome (요망스런) person demand the fire brigade (소방대). However, the fact that this building was still standing solidly (성하게부터 잇는) clearly meant no such mishap had occurred yet. (Continued - 계속)<96>

**Summary**

This first installment of "Muyeon'geum" introduces Kim Ch'ansu, a financially struggling office worker in 1930s Seoul. He feels trapped by his low salary, demanding job, family obligations (including an impending arranged marriage), and gloomy home life following his stepmother's death. His monotonous existence is disrupted by an unexpected, bold encounter with Munhŭi, a modern, enigmatic typist at his company, who seems interested in him despite (or perhaps because of) his melancholic, "poetic" nature. Their conversation sparks dormant feelings of youth and possibility in Ch'ansu, contrasting sharply with his dreary reality and causing internal conflict about his engagement and future. The story ends with Ch'ansu feeling both intrigued and wary of Munhŭi, while simultaneously burdened by financial worries and office politics.

From a historical perspective, the story offers valuable insights into the daily life, economic anxieties, and social dynamics of educated Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. The depiction of office culture (strict hours, low pay, interactions with a likely foreign manager nicknamed 'Donkey', workplace gossip and nicknames like 'Bell'), the financial pressures surrounding arranged marriage, the contrast between traditional expectations and emerging "modern" figures like Munhŭi (the "literary girl," typist, aware of Western concepts), and the pervasive sense of ennui and limited opportunity reflect the constraints and cultural shifts of the era. The mention of Japanese department stores (Mitsukoshi), serialized novels in Japanese newspapers, and loanwords further highlights the cultural milieu.

기사형태	소설

長篇 無絃琴(第一回)
						廉想涉
눈온 이튼 날 날이 번쩍 들고 해ㅅ발이 땃듯 하기에 좀 풀리는 줄 알앗더니 온종일 훈훈한 사무실 속에만 잇다가 나와서 그런지 목덜미로 슴여드는 해ㅅ머리 바람이 어이는 듯 모질다.
찬수는 외투깃을 펴올려서 압흘 염이고 장갑을 끼면서 엽골로 드러서다가 하마트면 밋그러질 번 하얏다. 아츰 출근할 때에는 발목이 쎅지든 눈이 당저가며 것만 녹다가 저녁 때 바람에 드윽 어러부터 버렷스니 고무덧신은 끼엇건마는 깟닥 잘못하다가는 엉덩방아도 찔 것 갓다.
5시 20분- 아츰 7시가 훨신 넘어도 해ㅅ발이 퍼지지 안는 이 심동에도 8시에 출근을 해서 담배 한 목음 못먹고 쉬일 시간이라고는 점심시간 30분 밧게 업시 오후 5시까지 매달려 잇는 회사원들은 5시 치는 뗑 소리만 들리면 「불야」소리나 들은 듯 십히 다섯 번째 종소리가 날 때에는 벌서 너나할 것 업시 문밧게 나와서 댬배갑을 끄내드는 것이＜75＞ 이 회사원들의 날마다 한결가티 하는 버릇인데 찬수는 20분이나 느저서 인제야 혼자 나온 것이다. 아모리 느저도 래일까지는 30원 돈이 잇서야 피일터인데 감정이 조치 못한 서양사람 총무가 들을까 십지 안허서 이틀재 나두고 파해나 올제마다 뒤처저서 총무실을 기웃거리기만 하다가 오늘은 춍무압헤서 비서처럼 심부름하는 안진달이를 불러내서 간접으로 교섭을 해 보고 나오는 터이다. 지난 달에 사소한 일로 총무의 축출령이 나려서 하마트면 쫏겨날번 햇든 일도 잇섯든 끗친데 월급의 반 이상을 선대해 달라면 핀둥이 밧게 마질 일이 업서서 총무에게 즉접 말하기도 실커니와 안진달이는 이 일이라도 자긔를 거처서 청하는 것을 조화하는 위인이기에 그 사람을 불러내서 청해 본 것이다.
「자네, 요세 웨 나갈제면 혼자 빙빙 도나하얏더니 그것 때문인가」
찬수의 시급하다는 사정을 다-듯지도 안코 안진달이가 이러케 핀잔을 주는 바람에 찬수는 긔가 줄엇다. 그 때문이 아니면 또 무슨 때문이 잇는 줄 알앗다는 말인지? 총무실에 잇는 미인 「타이피스트」와 눈이라도 마출랴는 때문으로 기웃거리든 것으로나 알앗다는 말눈치 가타야 불쾌도 하다. 그러나 사실인즉 그 미인 「타이피스트」가 지지난 달 처음 드러왓슬 당시에 총무실을 기웃거리다가 혼이 난 축이 업든 것도 아니엇다.
「긔회를 타서 말을 부처보자고 벨럿스나 그적게는 총무가 눈에 안띄우고 어제는 자네도 밥븐 눈치기에 말을 못하고 말앗는데 날자는 급하고 오늘은 꼭 가지고 나가야만 랑패가 안될테니 자네를 성이 가시게 해서 미안하나 힘 써주게 그려」
그 동안 기웃거리든 변명겸 이러케 간청을 한 것이다. 그러나 물론 시원한 대답은 못들엇다. 제 돈을 제 권리로 나주는 듯 십히 주짜와 것생색을 뒤석근 잔소리만 한참 느러노코 나서는 지금 당장은 어려우니 래일이나 보자는 것이다.
한편으로 생각하면 반달 후에 쓸 것을 닥아쓰자는데 지나지 안흐니 차라리 30원에 대한 반달 변리를 물라고 하얏스면＜76＞ 떳떳한 일이나 비럭질이나 하듯이 가진 간구한 사정을 느러노하가며 간청간청을 한 것이 나종에 생각하면 자존심을 일시에 보째 빠트려 버린 것 가티 불쾌하고 저절로 손이 머리로 올러가서 북북 긁을 지경이나 그래도 래일 될 듯한 눈치를 보고 찬수는 긔운이 낫다.
찬수는 인제야 생각난 듯이 담배를 한 개 끄내서 물고 장갑 낀 손으로 성냥을 포케트 속에서 더듬어 차저가지고 주춤하며 그어대엇다. 한 목음 마악 달게 빨랴할제 별안간 뒤에서
「에그머니! 용서하서요」하고 녀자의 목소리가 나며 업흐러지랴는 긔척에 찬수는 꺼진 성냥갑이를 든채 깜짝 놀라서 뒤를 돌려다 보앗다. 금방 나올제 총무실에서 외투를 입고 잇는 것을 본 「타이피스트」양이다. 밋근둥 하며 압흐로 업흐러지랴는 것을 겨우 간우며 얼굴이 발개서 웃어 보인다.
「땅이 여간 미끄러워야죠. 하마트면 선생님을 떼밀번 햇서요」하고 그래도 명랑하게 긴긴 대소를 한다.
「그럼 엇대요... 나두 문간에 나스자 넘어질번 하얏세요」하고 찬수는 마조 웃으며 담배를 다시 부첫다.
그러나 선 듯 머리에 떠올으는 것은 조금 전에 안진달이가 무슨 때문에 총무실을 기웃거리는가? 하엿다든 그 말이 다 이 녀자야 말로 그 동안 이삼일을 두고 총무실을 기웃거리든 것을 자긔 때문인줄 알고 뒤쪼차 나온 것이나 아닌가? 찬수가 안진달이와 헤어저 나올제 류리창으로 비처보니까 이 녀자가 외투를 떼어 입고 모자를 만적어리며 곳 나올 눈치 이든 것은 사실이나 자긔가 이 골목으로 드러서는 것을 보고 뒤를 쪼차 온 듯 십허서 웬일일구? 하며 의아한 생각도 드는 것이다.
이 녀자와 이야기를 해 본적은 업서도 회사 속에서 마조치면 은근히 인사를 하고 미소를 먹음어도 보이고 한번은 쫏겨날번 해서 총무실에 불려드러 갓슬 때도 넉슬 일코 안저서 가엽고 민망한 듯이 자긔를 바라보든 일도 잇기는 잇섯다. 그러나 그것은 녀자의 인사성일 것이요 그것을 무슨 특별한 호의로 볼 것은 아니라고 생각하얏고 지금도 아모 의미업시＜77＞ 혼자 가기 실흐니 동무 삼아 가티 가자고 따라오는 것일지도 몰을 것이다. 하여간 이 녀자를 원광으로만 보다가 이러케 제 풀에 자청하야 와서 동행이 되고 수작을 건네는 것은 의외의 일이요 신긔한 일 갓기도 하다.
「댁을 이리로 가서요?」하고 찬수가 먼저 말을 부첫다.
「안요. 선생님 댁은 이리 가요?」
「아-ㄴ요. 그런데 선생님은 무슨 선생님입니까」
영업판의 화사원끼리 선생님이란 경어를 쓰는 일도 업지만 그것이 설혹 리발소나 구두방이나 양복점이나 또는 녀자로 말하면 카페-껄이 예전에 「주사」라고 하는 대신으로 쓰는 것이라 할지라고 찬수에게는 귀에 서툴으고 더구나 그련 의미가 아니라면 그대로 듯기에 부끄럽기도 하얏다.
「아무러면 엇덧습니까, 선생님이라고 엿줄만 하기에 선생님이라는 거지요」
찬수는 고개를 기웃하며 더 탄하지는 안헛다.
좁은 골목을 휘둘다가 문희는 굽 놉흔 구두가 삣긋하며 또 한 번 넘어질번 한 것을 찬수가 얼는 붓드러 주엇다.
「내가 오늘 웨이러케 허둥대누? 이런 골작우니게 망정이지 큰 길에서 엉덩방아를 찌엇든들 엇절번 햇세요」하며 문희는 무색한 듯이 웃는다.
「시민(市民)의 위안이 되엇지요」
찬수의 말은 좀 「히니구」로 들렷다.
「녜?...」하고 문희는 눈웃음을 치며 흘겨보다가
「아닌게 아니라 큰길바닥에 네 활개를 치고 나동그라질 때 고때 마츰 신문사 사진반이 지나다가 딸각 백여다가 내엇스면 볼만하기도 한걸요!... 웨 비 오는 날 우산바든 모던껄을 압흐로 백이고 덜미로 백이고 꼭지로 백이고＜78＞ 해서 신문에 내지안하요. 그것처럼 아홉시간 벽돌집에 갓첫다가 풀려나온 가련한 「타이피스트」의 로상스켓팅(路上滑氷)이라고 제목을 부처서 냇스면...하하하 그러나 벽돌집에 가첫다 나왓대서야 우울하군! 위안도 아모것도 안되죠?」하고 소녀다운 공상이 떠올러왓든지 혼자 잿거리고는 웃는다.
언제 친햇든 사람처럼 거침업시 수작을 부치는 것이 좀 의외이기는 하나 명랑하고 유쾌하얏다.
「신문에 나는게 그러케 조흐세요? 그럴 지경이면... 미국에서 엇던 문학청년이 시를 지어가지고 대신문의 주필을 차저가보고 내얼달라고 졸으니까 주필 말이 정 내고 십거든 이 시(詩)를 품에 품고 철도 자살을 하우. 그럼 내어주리라고 하얏다는 세음으로 요새는 한강물이 어러부텃지만 래년 봄까지 참앗다가... 하하하」
찬수는 무슨 객적은 실업슨 말를 함부로 하는가 하는 반성을 하자 웃어버리고 말앗다.
「그것두 혼자 죽으면 개죽엄이라해서 그런지 조고마케 내드군요. 이왕이면 정사를 해야죠」
「그건 맘대로 하시구...」
잠간 말이 끈첫다가 문희는 새삼스럽게
「참 그런데 어딀 가시게 매일 이리 가세요?」하고 뭇는다.
「그건 어떠케 아서요?」
「우칭에서 내려다보면 꼭 이리 가시드군요」
그 말을 들으니 오늘도 우칭에서 내려다보고 부리나케 쪼차온 모양이다.
「그래서 이 길이 어듸가는 길인가-하고 궁굼해서 쪼차와 보섯습니까?」하고 찬수는 또 다시 실업시 웃어 보이니까
「그러치 안어두 그래요! 천당 가는 길인지? 지옥 가는 길인지? 궁금해서 이러케 업흐러지며 곡그라지며 따라오는거랍니다」하고 문희도 시럽시 웃는다.＜79＞

이런 구구스런 혼인을 무엇하자고 허는 것인가? 하는 생각에 자긔 마음대로 하면 당장 빠그러트려 버리고 어듸로 훨훨 다라나 버리고 십흔 것이다. 그러나 부친의 하는 일에 반대를 하얏다가는 큰 소리만 날 것이요 사정이 반대를 하랴야 할만한 형편이 못되고 보니 이때 것 다수긋이 되는대로 끌려오든 차에 오늘 문희를 맛낫다는 것을 찬수의 마음에 물결을 출렁거려 노흔 것이 사실이다.
(만일 이 혼인을 안한달 지경가트면?...)
찬수는 이번에는 이런 가정을 하야보앗다. 그러나 이리 대보고 저리 대보고 하여야 집안일을 생각한달 지경이면 모 피할 도리는 조금도 업섯다. 그래도 구지 피하랴면 집을 버리고 나가는 것 밧게 도리가 업다. 마치 혼인전날 도망가는 신부처럼 그러나 누구 때문에? 무엇 때문에? 비록 공부할 방도가 별안간 생겻다기로 장가 들어 늙은 아버니 모시게 하고 어린 동생 길으게 하야 후고의 렴려가 업게 하고서 못할 공부가 잇슬 리 업다.
(또 그러나 만일 문희가자긔에게 오겟다면?...)하고 다시 한 번 이런 가정을 하야 보앗다. 그 때 가서는 집을 뛰어나온대도 리유가 잇고 신세 부득이 할 것 가티 생각이 든다. 그러나 그것은 꿈 가튼 공상이엇다.
(몰으겟다! 몰라!...)하고 찬수는 두 팔로 머리를 얼싸고 방바닥에 나동그라저 버렷다.
그러나 이튼 날 출근 시간에 집을 나올 제는 간밤에 밤새도록 공산과 번민에 지친 머리가 묵어우면서도 기지가 낫다.＜93＞ 회사에 발를 들여노흘제 언제나 뒤ㅅ공장에서 강철 두드리는 소리에 눈살이 저절로 찝흐려지든 것도 오늘에 한해서는 귀에 들리지 안는 듯이 귀분이 조타. 총무실 압흘 지날 때 겻눈질로 드려다보니 문희가 생긋하고 알은체를 해보인다. 보는 사람만 업스면 이편에서도 대꾸를 하겟스나 그대로 지나첫다.
오정때 점심시간에 안진달이를 맛낫기에 어제 부탁한 것을 채근해보니까
「응? 무엇?...」하고 금시초문이란 듯이 딴청을 하다가 잇다가 갈제 맛나자 하고 다라나 버린다.
남의 일이기로 그러케 무심하거이 불쾌하얏스나 하여간 하회를 기다리라고 조금 밀린 일도 정리할겸 오늘도 또 뒤처지기로 하얏다. 혹시 오늘도 문희가 동행이 될까하는 은근한 긔대도 업지는 안핫다.
남들은 련해련방 시게를 치어다 봐가며 책상 우에 빌려노핫든 서류를 설합에 휩쓰려 너코 다섯 시의 종소리가 나기만 기다리고 안젓는 판에 찬수 혼자서는 유산 태평으로 안저서 철필을 잉크병에 너코 또 너코 하는 것을 보고 마조 안즌 동료가
「김군 요새 웬일인가? 하지만 그래야 별 수 업네. 아모래도 한 푼 우수리 업는 50원밧게」하며 비웃는다. 찬수는 고개를 숙인채 코웃음을 처보엿스나 사실 남들은 손을 탁탁 탈고 일분 이분 시간 가기만 기다리고 안젓는데 혼자만 일을 하기가 좀 열적기도 하얏다.
「이 사람 크리쓰마쓰가 한 보름 남지 안핫나」하고 엽사람이 또 웃는다.
여긔서는 이때것 년말 상여라는 것도 업시 다만 크리쓰마쓰 미처에 가서 이달만은 금음 날에 줄 월급을 닥아서 주고 그 길에 돈 10원씩이나 언저주고 쓱싹하는 것이다.
이 회사는 각종 공업용의 긔계를 독일과 미국에서 즉 수입하야 여긔서 꾸며서 내주는 고로 뒤에는 꽤 큼직한 구미다데공장(紐立工場)도 잇고 또 한엽흐로는 로서아 석유와 비행긔 자동차용의 까솔린의 도매업도 경영하기 때문에 석유와 가솔린 창고도 가지고 잇는 터이다. 엇잿든 이만치도 크게 영업을 하는데 정말 아즉도 수지(收支)가 맛지를 안혀서＜94＞ 상여금이라는 것이 업는 것인지 조선 사람들이란 단 이삼원 짜리에라도 머리를 싸매고 덤비는 터이니까 그만 정도로 입만 씻기면 별 불평은 업스리라고 넘보고 그러는 것인지는 모르겟스나 엇잿든 크리쓰마쓰에 주는 잣단돈량을 한푼이라도 더 어더걸릴까 해서 안나오는 정성을 별안간 부를는 줄로 아는 말눈치에 찬수는 입을 실룩하얏다.
「하지만 김군은 준(准) 방울은 되니까 저러케까지 안해도 우리보다는 튼튼할걸세」
방울이란 찬수가 돈부탁한 안질달이의 별명이다. 총무의 심복으로 뀌어하고 다닐 지경이라해서 입담 조흔 친구가 지어내인 별명이 통용이 되어 버린 것이지마는 키가 작으마하고 오동통한데 보글보글 하고 혈색 조흔 하얀 투상이 미남자인 것도 방울이란 별명에 어울리지만 바지랑대 가튼 총무와 마조서서 아양스럽게 수작을 한다거나 나란히 것는 것을 보면 한편은 매여달려 바리는 것 갓고 한편은 뀌어찬 것 갓타야 더욱히 방울이란 어수히 마처낸 별명이라고 누구나 생각하는 것이다. 그러나 어느 사회에서나 그러틋이 웃사람에게 긴한 사람은 동료간에 평판이 그리 조치 못한 모양이어서 안진달이도 싹싹은 하면서도 그리 선선치 못한 탓인지 방울이란 별명외에 또 한 가지 안진달이란 일홈에서 진자를 쑥 빼버리고 「안달」이라고 불러보앗다가는 또 엇먹어서 「선달」이라고도 부르는 것이다. 안진달-선달-안달-방울... 저이끼리 뒤ㅅ구멍으로만 부르는게 아니라 실업슨 축은 당자를 맛대서 불를 때도 이러케 부르는 것이다.
「준(准)방울이라니 뻣건방울이 엇던가? 하지만 그러케 치고보니 안닌게 아니라 우리 중에서 미남자를 꼽자면 방울 다음에는 김군 일걸세. 얼굴로 모자라는 것은 풍신으로 보태면 될걸세」
「이 사람 딴소리말게. 미남자를 표준으로 수석방울 차석방울을 정한다면 어듸로 보든지 내가 방울 중에도 금방울이 돼야할껄세」하고 키가 짝달막하고 얼굴이 까마잡잡한 친구가 타내랴니까.
「요빤빤아! 다람쥐는 나올막이 아니야. 답치고 드러가」하고 나서 김찬수를 준(准)방울이라고 한 사람이 방울의 자격심사를 한다.＜95＞
「위선 방울이 되자면 미남자여야고 체소해야 할 것 녀태(女態)여야 할 것 떵키-를 딸아서 단연을 해야할 것...」
「됏서-」
누구인지 장단을 마춘다. 떵키-라는 것는은 총무의 별명이다.
「그런데 우리 김군으로 말하면 체소하지 안흐나 미남자 축에 끼이긴 할 것이요. 녀태는 아니라도 시인이라 단아하시고 담배는 요새는 등선각에를 출장 아니시는 것을 보니 단연 단연(斷然, 斷煙)을 하신 모양인데 또 저러케 희생적 정신을 발휘하셔서 시간외에 근무를 무보수로 하시기에 근념하시는 걸 보면 어듸로 보든지 떵키-의 눈에 듭시리라는 말일세.
남은 5분 동안을 이런 객설로 약약한 듯이 보내고 안젓다가죵소리가 나자 어듸나 처나가는 듯 십히 왓작 니러나서 압흘 다토아 제각긔 외투 모자를 떼어 입고 물려나간다. 이들의 무엇보다도 급한 정사가 담배인 것이다.
이 회사에서 금연을 식히는 것은 바로 엽헤 석유와 까솔린 창고도 잇고 공장 속에서도 늘 까솔린을 쓰기 때문에 화재가 무섭다 햐야 그러는 것이지마는 의연만 하면 끽연실 한아쯤은 멀직 안이라도 설비를 해노흘 것이로되 그것조차 업는 것은 물론 총무란 사람이 애연가(愛煙家)가 아니니만치 제고집으로 그러는 것일 것이다. 아까 실업슨 말끄테 찬수가 요새는 등선각(登仙閣)에 출장을 안한다는 등선각이란 달은게 아니라 넓지도 못한 뒤ㅅ간 말이다. 뒤를 보랴 간 길에 한목음 빠는 것인지 한목음 맛이 신통해서 그 구린 것도 참아가며 안나오는 것도 짜고 안젓는 것인지는 모르겟지마는 한동안 들고나고 나고들고 한 뒤에 자욱한 그 속에를 드러간대면 요망스런 사람은 소방대라도 불러대라고 서둘 것이다. 그러나 이 집이 이러케 성하게부터 잇는 것을 보면 아즉까지 그런 실수는 업든 것이 물론이다.(게속)＜96＞
