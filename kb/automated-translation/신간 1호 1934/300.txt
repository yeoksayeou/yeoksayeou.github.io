Literary Scene Comings and Goings

Mr. Yi Sŏk-hun (李石薰) sighed deeply one day, saying, "I haven't produced a single work worthy of the name, so what kind of writer am I?" However, while the person who produces a masterpiece is undoubtedly a great writer, the person who strives until they produce a masterpiece is also a noble writer. Guy de Maupassant (가이―드―모팟상) before and after publishing "Boule de Suif" (脂肪) is one example. Needless to say, those who have works comparable to "Boule de Suif" are great, but those who possess Maupassant's fighting spirit and diligence are also equally our fine writers. O humble Mr. Yi, take heart and be optimistic!

×

It is a sociological law that people form associations and groups. Therefore, countless "groups" exist in officialdom, the literary world, and among the merchant and industrial classes. In our literary world too, there are KAPF (캅프), the Nine-Person Society (九人會), the Theater Arts Research Association (劇藝術硏究協會), etc., and there could be more. Nevertheless, singling out only the Nine-Person Society, like someone did, and blindly citing factionalism (黨派性) to demand its dissolution—that itself is the talk of someone with factional tendencies... It's good to say we should unite grandly in a Korean way, but that too is something achieved when individuals join with individuals, and "groups" merge with "groups."

×

Pak T'ae-wŏn (朴泰遠)'s "Novelist Mr. Kubo's Day" (小說家 仇甫氏의 一日) has been serialized in the *Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo* (朝鮮中央日報) for a month. Shouldn't it be "Mr. Kubo's Month" (仇甫氏의 一個月) instead of "Mr. Kubo's Day" <118>? Joking aside, Mr. Pak's "Sullen People" (한 사람들) in the September issue of *Chungang* (中央) is a valuable harvest for this autumn's literary scene.

×

Recently, the literary section of *Sintonga* (新東亞) has been exceedingly poor. There are no names of anticipated writers one wants to read, and after reading, I haven't seen any resonant works. The critique discussing trends in the literary world by someone called SK Saeng (SK生) in the September issue was also foolish drivel that couldn't be published without using the pseudonym KS Saeng (KS生). Writing irresponsible articles is already wrong, but hiding one's name and wearing a mask makes it even more detestable.

×

Regarding Chu Yo-han (朱耀翰)'s joining Hwasin (和信), many people say he has gone to a place distant from literature. However, literature, in principle, encompasses politics, economics, commerce, industry—all of it. A writer is truly distant from literature when, even while in their study, they do not pick up their brush or show interest in it. Therefore, if one comments that Mr. Chu has gone to a place distant from literature, saying conversely that he has gone to a place close to literature is not merely a paradox. Whether managing society or engaging in business, a writer should produce literature; whether sitting in an automobile or standing in an elevator, Chu will sing of the "beautiful dawn."

×

Highlighting only the strengths and covering up the weaknesses when criticizing a work is a corrupt Confucian moral custom deeply ingrained with the harmful practices of empty courtesy and self-righteousness; it's talk fit only for followers who wish for the second coming of Mencius and Confucius (孟孔). At this time when there are many pseudo-critics who cannot speak truthfully because they only watch others' faces, appear, oh refreshing critic for our literary scene, one who practices stating things plainly and directly—good as good, bad as bad!

×

Mr. ■■ Chun (■■俊氏) is suffering from a foot ailment. "Lying down, the feet of people coming and going catch my eye quite often. The foot is the most vital thing for a person, yet it is always hidden; it works the hardest but is always least rewarded." I recall that Mr. ■■ wrote a beautiful essay titled "Foot" (발) some years ago, which instilled in everyone a love for feet. I pray for his speedy recovery.

×

Slander (辱說) is a wicked thing. However, the target deserving of slander is perhaps even more wicked, and scheming with lies and ornate words (虛言麗辭) in the shadows while trying to maintain one's reputation in the light is an utterly abominable existence. Slander is violent but possesses a rather straightforward courage; this, however, is cunning and base. In the past, our literary scene had many slanderers, but even more people tried to harm others by framing them as slanderers. Just as one needs to beware of counterfeit goods (僞造商■■) when buying items, henceforth, be even more careful of those who slander others by calling them slanderers.

×

Mobilizing the elite of the literary world—Pak Yŏng-hŭi (朴英熙), Chang Hyŏk-chu (張赫宙), Yi Ki-yŏng (李箕永), Yi Mu-yŏng (李無影), Cho Pyŏg-am (趙碧岩), Yi Sang-hwa (李相和), Yi Sŏk-hun (李石薰), Pak T'ae-wŏn (朴泰遠), Pak Hwa-sŏng (朴花城), Song Yŏng (宋影), Ŏm Hŭng-byŏn (嚴興變), An Hoe-nam (安懷南), Yi Hyo-sŏk (李孝石), Kim Ki-rim (金起林), Ch'ae Man-sik (蔡萬植)—to serialize short stories is an initiative by the *Chosŏn Ilbo* Company (朝鮮日報社) that others have praised, but it is a meaningful event worth mentioning again. I hear that this is not stopping after just this round but is planned to continue for several years ahead, centering on young, elite writers.

×

Looking at the recent editorial actions of journalists ("저낼리스트"), their common knowledge regarding the literary world seems far too shallow. The prevalence of disorderly actions that hinder the organization and progress of the literary system is due to their ignorance of even the psychology, currents, trends, and atmosphere of the literary world. They should, of course, gain a deeper understanding in the future and learn to be ■■ashamed■■ of their own ignorance, which invites others' ridicule.■■■<119>

**Summary**

This article compiles short pieces of news and commentary about the Korean literary scene in late 1934. It touches upon individual writers' self-doubts and activities (Yi Sŏk-hun, Pak T'ae-wŏn, Chu Yo-han), defends literary groups like the Kuinhoe against charges of factionalism, criticizes the quality of literary sections in magazines like *Sintonga* and anonymous reviews, calls for honest literary criticism, and praises the *Chosŏn Ilbo* for featuring prominent writers. It concludes by lamenting the lack of understanding of the literary world among journalists. From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the internal dynamics, anxieties, and debates within the colonial Korean intellectual community. Mentions of specific writers, groups (KAPF, Kuinhoe), the intersection of literature and commerce (Chu Yo-han's move to Hwasin), and critiques of journalistic practices reveal the concerns and landscape of the *mundan* (literary world) under Japanese colonial rule.
