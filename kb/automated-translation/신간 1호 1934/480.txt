Extreme Poverty (赤貧)
						Paek Sin-ae

Ever since her second son married into a mountain village called Maech’on (梅村), everyone called her “the old woman of Maech’ont’aek.” The reason they specifically attached “t’aek” after “Maech’on” and before “old woman” was because this old woman was from the Ŭnjin Song clan (恩津宋氏), supposedly descendants of Master Song U-am (宋尤奄), a family that had long maintained a semblance of yangban status in that village, making the title an unavoidable form of respect due to her family connections. However, nowadays, nobody thought of the “t’aek” as a sign of respect. To anyone, “the old woman of Maech’ont’aek” had become a contemptuous pronoun for a dirty, pitiful old woman, poorer than a beggar who did odd jobs for others. Not only that, but recently some people had even started omitting the “t’aek” altogether, just calling her “the old woman of Maech’on.” Even so, the old woman’s attachment to her yangban past had worn so thin that she didn’t feel any anger or resentment about it.

A few years ago, the son of the village headman, for whom she often did chores,<62> asked her playfully at the end of a joke,
“Old woman, what’s your name?”
“Hee-hee, you mean me? What name does an old woman have!”
“Come on, why wouldn’t you have one? Was it Ttongdŏk? Kaettong?” he teased.
She felt a surge of anger at the young man’s insolence in asking an elder’s name.
“What? I was raised preciously in the old days too! My grandfather was a descendant of Master Song U-am! He was renowned for his writing! My grandfather named me Kwinam, meaning precious daughter, because I was so cherished!”
She boasted of her yangban lineage, implying that in the past, commoners like them wouldn’t have dared to enter her house so casually. Thinking back on it now, it all seemed ridiculous.
“In today’s world, if you have no money and are poor, this is how it is.”
This truth only became clearer with each passing day.

If one spoke of poverty, no one could be poorer than this old woman of Maech’ont’aek. Her eldest son was a dimwit, described as a “pig,” just like her long-dead husband. Like a pig, he was dull in all matters, greedy, reckless, and senseless, yet when it came to drinking and smoking, he was a match for a hundred men. Consequently, whenever he earned money from day labor, he would run straight to the tavern as soon as the money touched his hand, so the clothes on his body were often so ragged they barely concealed his private parts. His younger brother, at twenty-eight, had saved a few ryang from working as a hired hand, married into Maech’on, and intended to use the little money left over to get his older brother, the “pig,” married too. But no one, unless they were blind, would give their daughter to him. Yet, there was someone willing to take such a fool<63> as a son-in-law: the father of a mute girl who had remained unmarried until the age of twenty. The pig, being incapable of considering muteness a flaw, was simply overjoyed at the prospect of “getting a woman,” grinning widely, his broad nostrils flaring, as he got married.

The old woman thought her worries were over after marrying off both sons, but her worries only multiplied after they were married. The “pig” spent every single day just looking for alcohol, and the mute daughter-in-law would come to the old woman, who lived with the Maech’on wife, crying of hunger.

Since Maech’on-i (the second son) continued to work as a hired hand even after marriage, his wife and the old woman survived by going from house to house every day, working for food. Thus, the money earned from his hired labor was saved. Even though it amounted to only about 10 wŏn per year, the old woman treasured it like a million-dollar fortune, never thinking about the fact that she didn’t even have a change of clothes to wear. When she absolutely had no clothes left, she would go around the mountain villages doing day labor weaving cotton cloth. In the mountain villages, people still wove their own cloth by hand, just like in the old days. After weaving a bolt, she would receive several ch’ŏk of cotton cloth as wages and make one set of clothes for her daughter-in-law and one for herself.

As if receiving wages, the three of them worked themselves to the bone, going from place to place, but they had no major worries.
“If only we can save a few hundred yang, we can rent someone else’s rice paddy (논) or field (밧) as tenant land (貸地) and farm for ourselves.”
This was the sole hope of Maech’on-i, his wife, and the old woman.

Four years after Maech’on-i got married, the money they had painstakingly saved, bone by bone, reached 58 wŏn, just 2 wŏn short of 60. Maech’on-i took 15 wŏn from that money, bought a completely dilapidated hovel, and for the first time in his life, owned something he could call his home. The old woman rejoiced too. But then, the remaining 43 wŏn, meant for tenant farming, vanished in an instant. Maech’on-i, known in the village as honest<64> and capable, found himself overwhelmed holding so much money at once. Lured by the scent of money, village gambling sharks swindled him, and he lost it all in a single night. Maech’on-i’s eyes blazed, and feeling a gut-wrenching pain as if his bones were melting, he furiously smashed his innocent tobacco pipe. Their dream of farming, which they had believed in as surely as if it were in their grasp, had tragically turned into bubbles. The old woman cried all night, clinging to her son, wailing as if she wanted to die.
“Damn thieves! Robbers! They won’t get away with eating my 43 wŏn just like that!”
Maech’on-i sat rigidly, grinding his teeth. But no matter how much he lamented, the money once lost was unlikely to return to his hands. Nevertheless, Maech’on-i frequented the gambling dens daily, trying to win his money back. In the process, without realizing it himself, he soon transformed into one of the village’s penniless gamblers. Being a simple man, his transformation was easy and swift.

After Maech’on-i, whom the old woman and her daughter-in-law had trusted like a mountain, became like this, and their only hope of farming their own land turned into a dream, they were caught in a situation where they could neither live nor die, continuing to survive solely by going to others’ houses for food. Since they ate food cooked in others’ pots twelve months a year, even though they worked for it, the villagers treated them with contempt, as if feeding freeloaders. It was from this time that the “t’aek” was dropped from the old woman’s title, and she came to be called just “the old woman of Maech’on.”

The elder son, the pig, though long past the age where he should have gained some sense, was the sort who would cut off his own head for a drink. He would work himself to death but come back empty-handed after being paid only in alcohol. The mute daughter-in-law, for her part, worked to the point of collapse just for food. But another misfortune befell them: the pig was kicked out of the village. Having gone several days without alcohol and feeling desperate, the pig devised a plan. He went to a tavern and promised, “Give me a drink, and I’ll bring you a load of firewood.” He got his drink first. Then, having no firewood to bring, he went to an area under a sand control project (沙防工事), where cutting trees<65> was strictly forbidden, cut a full load, and was caught by the work crew leader on his way down. The firewood was confiscated, he was beaten severely, and the tavern owner was also subjected to endless insults. Because of this, he was expelled from the village. He went straight to Maech’on-i, but Maech’on-i, now also a penniless gambler, could do nothing. So, he had no choice but to move to a village about five ri away, taking shelter in a side room of someone’s house. The arrangement was that he wouldn’t pay rent but would help with chores when the household was busy. However, with nothing to eat immediately, he would beat the mute woman, demanding she go find food. But the mute woman, pointing to her large, heavily pregnant belly, would just cry sorrowfully. Still, the pig couldn’t think of any way to get food himself; instead, he would pester the mute woman until he got tired, then just lie around, dreaming baseless dreams that his mother, the old woman, might bring something.
Occasionally, when he craved tobacco, he would go out to the fields, pick the flowers of the “ssinraengi” plant (씬랭이 - possibly wild greens), stuff them into his pipe stem, and suck on it, making squeaking sounds like a mouse.

When the mute woman felt the fetus (胎兒) squirming inside her belly, she would shudder with fear.
“Damn beggar woman! Why are you scared? Isn’t that what babies do?”
He would shout like thunder, but she couldn’t understand, only whimpering “Kkŭng-kkŭng…” and clutching her belly tightly. Since they couldn’t even get one meal a day, the mute woman’s large eyes looked fierce, like those of a cow.

One night, the old woman dreamt of two large tigers. The next morning, she told Maech’on-i’s wife about the dream.
“Perhaps both of you are going to give birth to sons today or tomorrow…”
She looked at her daughter-in-law as if marveling at the premonition. Maech’on-i’s wife was also heavily pregnant, just like the mute woman.
“If both give birth at the same time, what are we going to do? The younger daughter-in-law at least has some food prepared for after childbirth, but what about the mute one…?”<66>

The old woman just mumbled to herself, continuously shaking her head side to side. The younger daughter-in-law had prepared five toe of rice and one mal of barley for her postpartum recovery, but the mute woman was starving right now; the situation was desperate.
Unable to bear thinking about it alone, she tied her sparse hair – a mix of gray, white, and black strands – into a knot with her fingers, put on her raggedly patched jacket and beggar-like short skirt, and quickly left the house. She went straight to the houses where she regularly worked, explaining the situation and begging them to lend her something, promising to work it off later. But not a single house gave her a clear answer.
“Honestly, that old woman worries about children like them. Why did they even have a baby if they knew they couldn’t feed it?”
They would mock, scold, and tease her. The old woman, revealing her few remaining tooth roots in her broken smile, would force a cat-like laugh,
“Hee-eh-.”
When she laughed, the fine, deep wrinkles covering her face, like a gnarled, warped tree root, would smooth out slightly, revealing patches of skin underneath that hadn’t seen sunlight, looking like dried gourd strips scattered here and there.
“That’s why I say it, the children are wretched. I’m just doing this because I pity the mute girl…”
She would force another “Hee-eh-” laugh and turn away.

Hoping against hope, she stopped by one last house. Having endured contempt and abuse for so long, the old woman no longer felt sadness or resentment at others’ coldness. And she didn’t know how to despair. At the last house she visited, they readily showed sympathy.
“Oh, how pitiful. Of all things, for a child to be born into such circumstances…”
Without much fuss, they gave her one toe of rice, two toe of barley, a bowl of soy sauce, a piece of seaweed, and one dried pollack (명태). This old woman, whose entire spirit would melt with gratitude for a single bowl of rice, was paradoxically unable to fully grasp the kindness of such excessive charity. Her capacity to feel gratitude<68> was simply too limited. Her nerves were overwhelmed with emotion, and she lacked the capacity to fully appreciate all these various acts of kindness individually.

The old woman just shook her head back and forth, repeatedly wiping her runny nose with the back of her hand as if catching it. Without any words of thanks, she put the various items into her basket and lifted it onto her head.
After leaving the house and walking for a while towards the village where the pig lived, she glanced back once, then quickly pulled the dried pollack out of the basket and hid it in her bosom.
“I’ll give this to the younger daughter-in-law when she gives birth.”
Feeling that she was favoring the mute daughter-in-law too much, she decided to hide the pollack and give it to the younger one later.

When she flung open the sliding door of the room where the pig was staying, a wave of hot steam and a musty smell billowed out. The pig, who had been lying alone in the room, stirred and sat up.
“What’s that? I’m hungry!”
He glanced sideways up at the old woman from below. He looked so much like a pig that the old woman gave a bitter smile inwardly. Not only did the room resemble a pigsty, but his slow movements and the way his small eyes squinted sideways made him look exactly like a sick pig. The only difference was that he wasn't plump like a real pig.

The old woman, weary of lamenting or hating her utterly worthless and ruined sons, had grown tired of it all and entered the room without a word.
“What’s that!”
He wiped the corner of his mouth, where dried saliva had formed white patches, with the back of his hand, asking again as if unbearable hunger gnawed at him.<67>
“What is it? It’s nothing. A young person is about to give birth, what will you feed her if you just lie around like this all night?”
The old woman spoke gently, her voice small as if stuck in her throat, not angry but softly chiding.
“I wanted to go work, but I’m too hungry…”
“Even so, does food fall from the sky if you just lie there? Where did the young woman go?”
“Maybe she went to the back hill to gather greens…”
The old woman scratched the back of her head with four fingers, then abruptly stood up as if unable to bear the frustration.
“This is medicine (藥) to feed her after she gives birth. Don’t you touch it!”
She pushed the basket she had brought into the upper corner of the room, went outside, grabbed a handful of thatch, and pressed it down on top.
“Seriously, don’t touch this. It’s medicine to feed her when the baby is born.”
The old woman repeated the warning ten, twenty times.
“Mmm-hmm, alright, stop nagging…”
The pig’s entire being, except for the skin on his body, was focused on that basket; the old woman’s words were like the passing wind to him. The old woman could clearly see into the pig’s heart. She knew well that no matter how much she warned him, the pig wouldn't follow her words, but she repeated the request several times anyway, hoping he might at least eat it sparingly. Yet, even a brainless “beast” (축신이) like the pig, a man nearing forty, found it pathetic that she called food “medicine.” And for them, wasn’t food indeed a precious and vital medicine that sustained their lifeline? The thought occurred to her – weren’t they people who had to eat food like medicine? – so the old woman didn't say anything more and left the room. On the way back home, worried she might run into the mute daughter-in-law, she kept the dried pollack hidden in her bosom, pressing down on it with her left hand, and hurried back. The younger daughter-in-law<69> was out working, so the old woman opened the lid of the rice jar buried in a pit beside the kitchen hearth and buried the pollack deep inside the rice. Then, thinking she too should go somewhere to work a bit and get lunch, she left the house again.

She went straight to the village headman’s house.
“Old woman, welcome. What’s wrong with this child!”
The headman’s wife was holding her three-year-old daughter on the wooden porch, looking distraught.
“What? Is she sick somewhere?”
The old woman quickly climbed onto the porch and expertly felt the child’s forehead and chest.
“She was just playing in the yard, and suddenly she’s like this!”
The child indeed had a fever and was crying painfully.
“It’s nothing serious. Let me see.”
The old woman took the child, pursed her sunken lips even more tightly, and gently massaged her chest and stomach. Having spent her life caring for other people's children, the old woman had seen many illnesses and also seen and heard many remedies. Now, she was trusted more in the village than a novice doctor, often prescribing simple remedies, performing rituals to ward off evil spirits (“겍귀”), and treating indigestion. Therefore, the headman’s wife entrusted the child to the old woman with confidence.

Indeed, the child soon began to vomit undigested food and, after a while, fell asleep just like that. The old woman sighed with relief, “Hoo-,” sat down on the edge of the porch, and said,
“Let her sleep soundly for a bit, then give her some thin, watery rice gruel (조당수). Don’t feed her dinner, just let her sleep, and she’ll be fine.” The wife, relieved, prepared a meal for the old woman. The old woman took the bowl of rice and side dish scraps and sat on the edge of the porch,<70> swallowing gulp after gulp without chewing.
“Goodness, old woman, couldn’t you eat a bit slower? What if you choke and die swallowing like that…”
The wife watched the old woman eat and cautioned her.
“Hee-eng-”
The old woman gave her characteristic affectionate laugh, bit off a piece of salted pollack tail (간청어), bones and all, chewed briefly, her mouth puckering, then swallowed with a gulping sound.
“My goodness, she’s eating the bones too!”
“Hee-eng- Don’t worry. Isn’t it a blessing to die while eating?”
She let out her habitual cat-like “Hee-eng-” laugh once more, and her fist-sized scoops of rice continued their rapid journey up and down.
“That old woman’s intestines must be made of cast iron!”
The wife, swallowing her own saliva, took some leftover kimchi scraps from the cupboard and gave them to her. The old woman, who had never refused food offered to her, readily accepted the kimchi and slurped down the juice first. Though her body was withered and twisted, her intestines alone seemed tough and sturdy as cast iron. She had never suffered from a stomachache in her life.

That day, after helping with various chores and receiving dinner as well, as she was leaving, the wife poured about two toe of barley into the hem of the old woman’s skirt.
“Oh, what’s this for?”
She accepted it without refusal and went straight home. She took the barley, wrapped it in a scrap of old rag, and hid it under the firewood pile in the kitchen, unbeknownst to her daughter-in-law. She intended to take it to the mute woman when her food ran out…<71>

A few days later, hearing word that the mute woman was lying down with signs of labor, she rushed over frantically. It was well past noon when she arrived.
When she flung open the door, the mute woman was banging her head against the corner of the room, groaning “Kkung-kkung…” and clawing at the wall as if dying. The pig sat there anxiously, sucking on his smokeless pipe, making squeaking mouse sounds.
“How long has she been like this?”
The old woman entered the room, sat down, and asked her son.
“I don’t know. Since last night, she hasn’t even had a sip of water!”
The old woman understood the mute woman’s suffering well. She hadn’t eaten anything and lacked the strength to give birth quickly. Realizing this, she asked,
“Where is the stuff I brought the other day?”
“What stuff? We ate it all.”
“What did you say?”
The old woman was dumbfounded. How much could one toe of rice and two toe of barley possibly amount to? Barely enough to wet their lips. But hearing that they had eaten the seaweed too sent a surge of anger through her.
“Damn beggar! Eating all of that…”
With the mute woman groaning weakly before her, unable to give birth quickly due to lack of strength, the old woman’s heart felt bewildered. First, she took the little remaining soy sauce, added a ladle of cold water, boiled it, and fed a couple of spoonfuls to the mute woman. Suddenly,
“Abŏba-.”<72>
With a scream, a bright red lump of flesh fell onto the floor, letting out a strong first cry, “Ŭaa-.” The old woman needed to cut the umbilical cord, but there were no scissors or anything. While she was thinking, the pig rushed over and bit the cord cleanly with his teeth. Though it was the floor, only a palm-sized patch of worn-out straw matting covered the area near the door, so the baby lay directly on the bare earth, wiggling its bright red arms and legs, its lips moving slightly. The old woman and the pig quickly parted the baby’s legs to look. A tiny appendage clearly proved it was a boy. Suddenly, the old woman’s arms started trembling violently. She looked around frantically, then, seeing no other option, quickly took off her own skirt, wrapped the baby in it, and laid him on the mat. The mute woman lay sprawled out as if dead. The pig muttered meaningless words incoherently, fidgeting with his pipe, touching the mute woman, then fidgeting again. The old woman sat still for a moment. Perhaps because this was the first grandchild she had seen at the age of sixty-three, her heart swelled with emotion, and for some reason, tears streamed down her face.

Soon after, the afterbirth (胎) came out. Unable to handle the amount of blood, she scooped the afterbirth onto a torn piece of straw sack (가마니) and instructed the pig to take it outside near the yard and burn it.
“What will I feed her!”
The old woman remembered the two toe of barley hidden under the woodpile at her house. But going to get it now meant leaving, which she couldn't do. Asking the pig to go worried her that the younger daughter-in-law might find out. She felt like cutting off her own arm in frustration. Just then, the landlord’s wife, realizing the situation, gave them a bowl of rice.

The old woman cooked the rice, gave a large bowlful to the mute woman, and scraped the leftovers into a gourd dipper (박아치).
“That damn woman! Doesn’t even seem to hurt after giving birth. Eats like a horse. I’m starving to death too, damn it!”
The pig yelled from the yard where he was burning the afterbirth. The old woman,<73> muttering,
“Damn beggar, like a beast (축신이),”
scooped some rice from the gourd dipper and gave it to the pig, while she herself just picked off and ate the grains stuck to her fingers. Between tending to the baby and caring for the mute woman, the day drew to a close. She fed the remaining rice to the mute woman. Unable to bring herself to take back the skirt she had used to cover the baby, she waited until dark, clad only in her tattered undergarments, to go fetch the barley from her house.
She worried that if the younger daughter-in-law found out, she would ask,
“Whose barley is that? Why did you hide it and take it?”
and feel hurt. So, she slipped quietly into her own house. Maech’on-i must have gone to the gambling den again; the daughter-in-law was alone, picking fleas off herself under the flickering castor oil lamp, her clothes untied. Soundlessly, the old woman entered the kitchen, reached under the firewood pile, and gently pulled out the bundle of barley. Just as she was about to leave, she hesitated slightly, thought for a moment, and then, with the skillful movement of a thief (쓰리), reached into the rice jar. The dried pollack (명태), which she had previously hidden under the rice, was now lying exposed on top.
“Oh dear, the daughter-in-law must have seen it.”
Realizing this, she quickly withdrew her hand from the jar and slipped out of the house.

Clutching only the barley bundle at her side, she ran like lightning back to the pig’s place.
“Make porridge with this. You eat only a little bit, and feed the baby’s mother!”
She repeated the instructions several times, then started back towards her own house.
Her completely empty stomach felt stuck to her back. Her mouth and throat felt coated with pine resin (송정); when she tried to moisten her lips, it felt like they would tear, stinging sharply. The thought, “How many days can they possibly survive on just two toe of barley?” drained the strength from her legs (누다리), and the uselessness of both the pig and Maech’on-i felt freshly detestable. But before her eyes (continued on page 44 of Vol. 3)<74> flashed the image of the tiny baby, the sign of its manhood appearing and disappearing intermittently.
“As if meeting an enemy on a single-log bridge, what if the younger daughter-in-law also goes into labor tonight…”
At this thought, her vision blurred, and she felt she would collapse forward at any moment. Continuously clutching her falling undergarments with one hand, her elephant-hide-like skin showing through the tears, she kept running desperately.
-“End”- <44>

**Summary**

This story, "Extreme Poverty," depicts the harsh life of an elderly woman, "Maech'ont'aek," descended from yangban lineage but now living in utter destitution. Her two sons are sources of constant despair: the elder, "Pig," is a dim-witted alcoholic, and the younger, Maech'on-i, loses their painstakingly saved money to gambling and becomes a gambler himself. The narrative focuses on the old woman's desperate efforts to provide for her sons' families, particularly the elder son's mute wife, culminating in the simultaneous births of grandchildren under conditions of extreme deprivation. The story highlights the brutal reality of poverty, the breakdown of family and social structures, and the protagonist's resilience mingled with resignation.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a stark portrayal of rural poverty and social decline during the Japanese colonial period in Korea (1934). It illustrates the erosion of the traditional yangban class status in the face of economic hardship and changing social dynamics. Historians might find valuable insights into the struggles of the rural populace, the prevalence of issues like alcoholism and gambling as coping mechanisms or symptoms of despair, the lack of social support systems, and the tension between traditional values (like lineage) and the harsh economic realities of the time. The detailed depiction of daily survival, including bartering labor for food and cloth, provides a glimpse into the material culture and subsistence strategies of the poor.

그의 둘재 아들이 매촌(梅村)이라는 산골에 장가를 간 후로는 그를 부를 때 누구든지 「매촌댁 늙은이」라고 부른다. 「늙은이」라는 우에다 「매촌택」이라고 특히 「택」자를 붓처 부르는 것은 이 늙은이가 은진송씨(恩津宋氏)인 고로 송우암(宋尤奄) 선생의 후예라고 그 동리에서 제법 양반 행세를 해오든 집안이 친정으로 척당이 됨으로서의 부득이한 존층이다. 그러나 지금에 와서는 존층으로 「택」자를 붓처준다고는 아모도 생각지 안엇다. 아모라도 「매촌택 늙은이」하면 의레히 더럽고 불상하고 남의 일 해주는 걸어지보다 더 가난한 늙은이다. 하는 멸시의 대명사로 녁이는 것이엿다. 그 뿐 안이라 요즈음에 와서는 「매촌택 늙은이」라고 「택」자를 쑥 빼고 부르난 사람도 잇어젓다. 그래도 늙은이는 그것을 노엾게 생각할만한 양반에 대한 애착심이 낡어빠저서 아-모런 생각도 늣기지 안엇다.
몃 해전 그가 늘 허드래 일을 해주러 단이는 그 동리 면장의 집 아들이＜62＞ 장란말 끗헤
「늙은이의 일홈이 뭐요?」
하고 물엇다.
「히힝 내 말인가. 늙은이가 무슨 일홈이 잇어!」
「그래도 왜 업서요. 똥덕이엿소 개똥이엿섯소?」
하며 놀려대는 것이엿다. 그는 젊은 놈이 당돌하게 늙은이의 일홈을 뭇는다는 것이 와락 분해저서
「왜? 나도 예전에는 다- 귀하게 큰 사람이요 우리 할아버지는 송우암 선생의 자손이요 글이 문장이라오 내 일홈도 할아버지가 귀한 딸이라고 귀남이라고 지엇다오!」
하며 자긔도 옛 세월 갓헛으면 너희들은 감히 나의 집에도 만만히 못들어올 상놈들이다 하는 뜻을 암시하야 양반 자랑을 한 것도 지금 생각하면 다- 우수운 일이엿다.
「돈 업고 가난하면 지금 세상은 이런 것.」
이라 하는 것만은 날이 갈수록 더 똑똑하게 알니여질 뿐이엇다.
가난하다면 이 매촌댁 늙은이보다 더 가난할 수는 업는 것이다. 그의 맛아들은 오래 전에 죽어바린 자긔 남편과 마찬가지로 「도야지」라고 설명을 듯는 멍텅이엿다. 모든 일에는 도야지 갓치 둔하고 욕심 굿고 철닥선이 업고 소견 업는 멍텅이면서도 술 먹고 담배 피우는대는 일당백 이엿다. 그래서 남의 집에서 품파리라도 하면 돈이 손에 들어오기 밧부게 술집으로 좃차가는 것이엿음으로 몸에 입은 옷이라고는 자칫하면 감후는 물건이 벌눔 내다보일 지경이엿다. 그 동생은 스물 여덟에 남의 집에서 고용사리로 모앗든 몃 량 돈으로 매촌으로 장가를 들고 얼마 남은 것으로 형되는 「도야지」도 장가를 드려줄야고 햇으나 눈 빠진 사람이 안이고는 그에게 딸을 내여줄 사람이 업섯다. 그러나 이럿케 못난이 「도야지」라도＜63＞ 사위를 보려는 사람이 잇엇다. 그는 스무살이나 먹도록 싀집 못보내고 둔 벙어리 색시의 아버지다. 도야지는 벙어리라고 험으로 생각할 인물이 못되여 「게집 엇는다」는 것만이 조화서 싱글벙글하며 넓적한 코구멍을 벌눔거리며 장가를 들엇다.
늙은이는 아들 둘을 다 장가 보내고 나니 이재는 걱정할 것이 업다고 생각햇으나 장가를 보내고 나니 걱정은 더 만허 젓엇다. 「도야지」는 한날 한시로 술만 차저단이고 벙어리는 매촌의 안해와 갓치 잇는 늙은이에게 와서 배곱흐다고 우는 것이엿다.
매촌이는 장가든 후에도 고용사리를 하는 고로 그의 안해는 늙은이와 날만 새이면 남의 집으로 돌아단이며 일 해주고 밥 어더먹고 하야 살어옴으로 고용사리로 밧은 돈은 그대로 남겨두게 되엿다. 남겨둔다드라도 1년에 10원 내외이나 늙은이는 백만 재산갓치 귀중히 녁여 몸에 걸칠 옷 한가지 밧구어 입을 것이 업는 것은 생각할 줄도 몰낫다. 아주 옷이 업서지면 산골로 돌어단이며 무명베 짜는대 품파리를 한다. 산곡에서는 예전과 갓치 아즉까지도 제 손으로 옷감을 짜는 것이다. 한 필을 짜면 무명베 몃 척식을 삭으로 바더가지고 며누리와 한 가지 자긔 한 가지식 옷을 해입는 것이엿다.
품싹이나 밧는 것 갓치 그들 셋은 뼈가 부서지도록 일을 해주고 돌어단엿으나 그래도 별 걱정은 업섯다.
「어서 몃 백양 모이게 되면 그것으로 남의 「논」이나 「밧」을 대지(貸地)로 어더서 제 농사를 해보리라.」
하는 것만이 매촌의 부부와 늙은이의 유일한 희망이엿다.
매촌이가 장가든 지 4년만에 이럭저럭 뼈를 깍거 모은 돈이 2원 모자라는 60원이나 되엿다. 매촌은 그 돈 중에서 15원을 띄여 일간 토옥 다 허물어저가는 것을 싸가지고 생전 첨으로 자긔의 집이라는 것을 가지게 되엿다. 늙은이도 깃버햇든 것이다. 그랫드니 남은 돈 43원으로 대지를 하기 전에 흘낙 날너보내고 말엇다. 동리에서도 뚝뚝하고＜64＞ 잘하는 신용잇는 매촌이엿으나 한거번에 만흔 돈을 쥐고 보니 갓득이나 마음이 벙벙한데다가 돈냄새를 맛고 달려든 동리 알부랑 노름꾼들에게 속아 넘어서 하로밤에 휘ㅅ닥 날려보내고 만 것이엿다. 매촌은 두 눈에 불이 켜지고 뼈가 녹은 것 갗이 스라리게 앗가워서 죄 업는 담배대만 힘것 두들겨 부수엇다. 손에 쥐인 것 갗이 밋고 밋엇든 농사한다는 그들의 꿈은 그대로 애처럽게 물거품으로 돌어가고 말게 되엿음으로 늙은이는 왼밤이 새도록 아들을 졸느며 죽는다고 목을 놋코 우는 것이엿다.
「죽일 놈들 도적놈들 내 돈 43원을 그대로는 못먹을 것이다.」
매촌은 딱버틔고 안저 니를 갈엇다. 그러나 한번 낙기운 돈이 아모리 간장을 녹인들 도로 제 손안에 들어올 리가 업는 것이엿으나 그래도 매촌은 제 돈 차지러 매일갓치 노름판에 드나들엇다. 그러는 중에 그는 제 자신도 몰은 사이에 어느듯 동리 알탕 노름꾼으로 변하고 말엇다. 단순한 매촌이엿엇는 만큼 그의 변화는 쉽고 빨은 것이엿다.
늙은이와 며누리는 태산갓치 미덧든 매촌이가 그 모양이 되고 오직 하나 희망이엿든 제 농사 짓는다는 것도 꿈으로 돌어간 후 죽지도 살지도 못할 판에 끼이워 한결갓치 남의 집에 단이며 입만은 살어갓다. 1년 12달 남의 솟헤 익혀 낸 밥만 어더먹는 그들이라 비록 일은 해주고 먹는 것이라 해도 동리 사람들은 공밥을 먹이는 것 갓치 그들을 천대하는 것이엿다. 늙은이에게서 「매촌택」의 「택」자를 숙 빼고 「매촌택 늙은이」로 불니우게 된 것도 이때부터이다.
큰 아들 도야지나마 이제는 심을 채일 나이도 된 지 오래엿건마는 그는 술 한 잔이면 제 목이라도 비여줄 작자엿음으로 죽도록 일을 해주고도 술만 어더먹고 그대로 오는 것이엿고 벙어리는 또 제대로 밥만 어더먹고는 죽을동 살동 일을 해 주는 것이엿엇다. 그러나 이중에 또 불행이 하나 더 덥처 도야지는 그 마을에서 쫏겨나게 되엿다. 그것은 몃날 술을 먹지 못하야 못살 지경에 이른 도야지가 한 꾀를 생각해 가지고 술집에 가서 「술 한 잔 주면 나무 한 짐 해다주겟다. 는 약속으로 먼저 술 한 잔을 어더먹엇다. 그리고는 갓다줄 나무가 업서 나무버히기를 엄검하는 사방공사(沙防工事)해 노은대＜65＞ 갓치 한짐 잔득 버혀지고 내려 오다가 일꾼 패장에게 들키워 나무짐은 나무짐대로 다- 빼앗기우고 죽도록 어더맛고 술집 마누라까지 무한 욕을 먹고 한 까닭에 그는 그 동리에서 쫏겨난 것이엿다. 그 길로 매촌에게 왓으나 매촌이 역시 알부랑 노름쟁이라 하는 수가 업엇다. 그래서 그는 하는 수 업시 오리(五里)가량 떠러진 동리에 가서 남의 집 겻방사리로 들어갓다. 방세는 내지 안트라도 그 집의 밧분 일은 거들어 주겟다는 약속이엿다. 그러나 당장에 입에 너을 것이 업섯음으로 벙어리를 두들기며 밥 어더 오라고 하는 것이엿으나 벙어리는 임이 당삭이 된 크다란 배를 가르키며 설업다는 듯이 우는 것이엿다. 그래도 도야지는 엇더케든지 해서 량식을 어더올 궁리는 하지 못하고 벙어리를 졸느다가 지치면 그의 어머니인 늙은이가 무엇이나 가저다 주지나 안나! 하는 턱 업는 꿈을 꾸며 딍글딍글 구울기만 하는 것이엿다.
잇다금 담배 생각이 나면 들에 나가서 「씬랭이」의 꼿을 따다가 대에 너어가지고 쥐색기 소리를 내며 빠러대는 것이엿다.
벙어리는 자긔 배 속에서 꿈틀 꿈틀 하며 태아(胎兒)가 놀면 몸서리를 치며 무서워햇다.
「비러먹을 년 어린애가 그리지 안나 겁은 왜 내여?」
하고 벼락갓치 소리를 질으나 알아듯지 못하고 끙긍...하는 소리로 울며 자긔 배를 쿡 쥐여질으는 것이엿다. 하로 한끼도 어더먹지 못하는 그들이라 벙어리의 크다란 두 눈은 쇠눈깔갓치 험악하엿다.
늙은이는 어는 날 밤에 큰 호랑이 두 마리가 꿈에 보이드라고 하며 그 잇흔 날 아침에 매촌의 안해를 보고 꿈이약이를 하는 것이엿다.
「아마도 오늘 래일 간에 너이 둘이다 아들을 나을랴는가 보드라...」
하며 신긔하다는 듯이 며누리를 바라보는 것이엿다. 매촌의 안해도 벙어리와 갗이 당삭이엿든 것이다.
「한꺼번에 둘이다-해산을 한다면 이 일을 엇절가. 적은 며누리는 그래도 해산 후에 먹을 것이나 준비해 두엇지마는 저 벙어리를 엇더케...」＜66＞
나 한도가 적엇든 까닭이다. 그의 신경은 모조리 감격에 차고 이 여러 가지에 대한 감사를 일일이 다- 늣기기에는 그의 신경이 모자랏든 것이다.
늙은이는 채머리만 쩔내쩔내 흔들며 연방 헤끗으로 코물을 잡아뜻드시 닥것다. 아-모 고맙다는 인사도 업시 그는 여러 가지를 바구니 속에 너어 가지고 머리에 엿다.
그 집을 나와 한참 도야지 잇는 마을을 향해 걸어가다가 그는 힐금 한 번 뒤를 돌어보고는 얼는 바구니에서 명태를 끄집어 내여 품 속에 감추엇다.
「이것은 적은 며누리 해산하그든 주지.」
그는 벙어리만 중하게 생각하는 것 가태서 명태는 감추엇다가 적은 며누리를 주려는 것이엿다.
도야지가 잇는 방 지게문을 덜컥 열어재치니 방안에서는 더운 짐과 퀴퀴한 냄새가 물신 소삿다. 도야지는 혼자 방에 누엇다가 부시직 이러나 안젓다.
「그것 뭐요. 배곱혀라!」
하며 힐금 아래서부터 녑흐로 늙은이를 치어다 보는 것이엿다. 그 모양이 정말 도야지 갓허서 늙은이는 속으로 쓴우슴을 첫다. 방안 모양도 도야지 우리 갓헛거니와 그의 느린 동작과 조곰안 눈이 실그먼히 흘겨보는 상은 병들은 도야지 그대로엿다. 다-만 한 가지 참도야지처럼 살이 툭툭 찌지 안은 것만이 다를 뿐이엿다.
늙은이는 지긋지긋하게도 못나고 망란이인 두 아들을 원망이나 미워하는 것도 이재는 그만 지처서 그대로 잠잣코 방으로 들어갓다.
「그것 뭐요!」
입가장자리가 보야케 침이 타 붓흔 것을 손등으로 슬적 닥그며 배곱허 못견대겟다는 듯이 재처 뭇는 것이엿다.＜67＞
늙은이는 혼자 중얼거리며 연방 채머리를 쩔내쩔내 흔드는 것이엿다. 적은 며누리는 해산하면 먹는다고 쌀 다섯 되 보리 한 말을 준배해 두기라도 햇거니와 벙어리는 지금 당장에 굼고 잇는 판이니 그 일이 난감하엿다.
혼자 생각다못해 노린 것 흰 것 검은 것이 한 대 석기인 몃가락 안되는 머리를 손가락으로 감어서 꽁처매고 누덕누덕 집은 적삼에 걸내 갓흔 몽당치마를 입고 빨니 집을 나섯다. 그는 그 길노 바로 단골노 단이며 일해주는 집들을 돌어단이며 사정 이약이를 하고 얼마만치만 뀌여주면 나종에 그만치 일을 해주리라고 애원을 해도 한 집도 시원케 대답하지 안엇다.
「모다 그 늙은이는 참 그런 이들을 자식이라고 걱정을 해. 먹을 것도 업슬 줄 알며 어린애는 왜 만들엇서?」
하고 비웃고 핀잔주고 놀여주고 할 뿐이엿다. 늙은이는 이즈러지고 뿌리만 남은 몃 개 남지 안은 니빨을 들어내며
「히 에-.」
하고 고양이 갗이 웃어보이는 것이엿다. 웃으면 고러 배틀어진 우붕뿌리 갓흔 그 얼굴에 누비질한 것 갓치 잘게 깁게 잡힌 주름살이 피이며 주름 사이에서 해빗흘 보지 못한 살이 밧고지운 것 갗이 여긔 저긔 들어나는 것이엿다.
「그러기에 말이지 자식놈들이 몹슬 놈이지 그저 벙어리가 불상해서 그리는 거요...」
