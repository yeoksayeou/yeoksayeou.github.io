Publication Date: 1925년 07월 01일
Article Title: 中國의 排外運動에 關聯하야 (Regarding China's Anti-Foreign Movement)
Author: 淸進學人 (Progressive Scholar)
Article Type: 소식 (News/Report)

Regarding China's Anti-Foreign Movement
						Ch'ŏngjin Hagin

1. Agitation and Life Facts

In the movements of the proletariat or any other conquered peoples, a tendency can be found within the privileged class not to recognize the causes and progress of the movement based on the facts themselves. Instead, they seize upon secondary events, sometimes those with no organic connection whatsoever, and believe them, in an exaggerated manner that puts the cart before the horse, to be the fundamental motive of the movement itself. This is a conscious or unconscious tendency within the privileged class because recognizing the natural facts as they are—in other words, recognizing the inevitable state of the oppressed class's movement for the recovery and improvement of their lives, rebounding against the life-oppression by the privileged class—would explain the irrationality of the fundamental basis of the privileged class's own existence or the process of its collapse.

Therefore, they try as much as possible not to touch upon those fundamental facts, and by placing those facts on a permanent or ideal foundation, they seek, on the one hand, to ensure the security and interests of the privileged class's existence. On the other hand, they exercise methods of force, law, and morality to rationalize the prevention and suppression of the opposing class's rise. However, when these methods and efforts fail, and they witness the rebound movement deepening, purifying, and continuously erupting, the privileged class, even more unconsciously, believes in and clings to the fundamental foundation of its own class. They tend to view the motive for the rebound as a movement arising from the instigation and "agitation" (煽動) of socialists, communists, or radical elements with such tendencies—in the present day—and believe that the movement can be stopped by cracking down on these agitators. This is a phenomenon commonly observed among the privileged class's politicians, capitalists, scholars, newspaper reporters, or within what is called the public opinion of the privileged class. Thus, concerning the outbreak of the general strike incident in Shanghai, China, and the spread of its sparks throughout China, the politicians, scholars, reporters, etc., of privileged nations like Britain, Japan, the United States, and others <79> unanimously, with one voice, observed it as resulting from the agitation of Russia and the Third International, or China's radical communist elements and students. They focused their countermeasures on these agitators, believing that suppressing them held the possibility of ending the movement. Perhaps so. A revolutionary state, uprising, or disturbance is not a normal psychological state of human life but a state of war where the psychology of life is extremely tense and excited. This state of war is a temporary phenomenon in the entire process of human social life—economic and political—and not a permanent state. Therefore, it is also true that temporary states of disturbance can sometimes be stopped by suppressing the agitators and leaders. Thus, the privileged class believes that by exposing the "wrongdoings" of the agitators and leaders and enlightening them, they can prevent such states of war beforehand, and they think they can quell a state of war that has erupted temporarily. When the target identified as the agitator is a foreigner, they often delude themselves into thinking that national slander can achieve that enlightenment or suppression of the movement. This can be seen today in the attitude of capitalist nations like Britain, Japan, and the United States towards Russia and the Third International concerning the turmoil in China.

While the state of war—uprising or disturbance—marked by excited and tense life psychology may be a temporary phenomenon in the entire process of social life and not a permanent state, this does not mean its motive is accidental. Nor does the suppression of the temporarily erupted state of struggle mean the movement will completely cease, unless the object of the struggle disappears and its goal is achieved. It means that what had been steadily and continuously struggled for through peaceful means within a peaceful state takes on a revolutionary character—a state of bloody conflict—when some direct emotional stimulus occurs or an organized plan for a certain goal is achieved. In the case of China, it would be appropriate to view it as a nationwide state of uprising-like struggle that erupted due to some incident providing a direct emotional stimulus (刺衝) to the Chinese masses. However, the driving force behind the emotional stimulus of an uprising or disturbance does not lie in the agitation or instigation of foreign and Chinese agitators; the fundamental driving force lies in the deterioration (沮喪) of the life facts that make such emotional stimulus possible. That is, in China, slogans like "anti-imperialism," "abolition of capitalism," "expulsion of Japan and Britain," or "recovery of national rights" mean that the life facts of the Chinese people are threatened and insecure due to capitalist British and Japanese nations. The demand for the recovery of national rights, such as the abolition of extraterritoriality (治外法權) and the return of leased territories (租借地), originates from these life facts. <80> Without recognizing these natural facts as they are, one cannot grasp the true picture of the turmoil. Here, the politicians, scholars, reporters, capitalists, etc., of the privileged class consciously or unconsciously try to pinpoint the cause in the agitation of rash and reckless (輕擧妄動) left-leaning elements (左傾分子) from Russia and China.

(Even if the Chinese masses or students are moved by the agitation of agitators, their life psychology is not excited solely by that agitation; it is merely that the deficiencies in their life facts, which allow them to directly and emotionally accept the agitation, make the agitation possible. Of course, agitation can be misused by politicians. In this case, the discernible distinction lies between the conscious effort of the agitator and the unconscious action of the agitated. Where deficiencies in life facts that enable agitation strictly exist, the masses being moved by the conscious effort of the agitators is a function of sharp human life instincts. If the causes—ethnic, political, economic—that reveal the possibility of agitation in life facts do not exist, the conscious effort of the agitator will not yield results. In the opposite case, the life instincts of the masses operate advantageously. As this experience accumulates through struggle, the initial unconscious actions gradually become conscious and衝擊 (clash/impact) against the opposing party based on the sense of gain and loss in life. Therefore, as long as the potential for being agitated潛在 (lies hidden/is latent) in life facts, the agitated, unconscious actions of the masses do not harm the process of recovering their livelihood; on the contrary, it becomes an experience that benefits the process of life recovery—all the more so in the case of conscious actions by the agitated, where life instincts operate in a more advantageous direction. In such circumstances, feeling fear towards the agitator's agitation is tantamount to forcing the permanent state of ignorance upon the masses, and simultaneously, it can be seen as asserting the establishment of permanent privilege through it, which means the permanent establishment of a barbaric state for humanity.)

This attitude of the privileged class is not unique to the China situation; it can be observed even more clearly in the attitude of the bourgeoisie (뿔으쪼아), bourgeois critics, and officials (官憲) when labor disputes or tenant farmer disputes (小作爭議) arise in worker and peasant movements. For example, when a general strike (同盟罷業) by workers occurs, the act of striking is merely a means of tactics (戰術). Yet, they perceive it as originating from socialist or radical, progressive thought manufactured by agitators, and believe that the dispute can be eradicated by cracking down on the socialists and strike leaders identified as agitators. In other words, they do not see the workers' use of the tactic <81> of a general strike as a life-action that inevitably arises in the process of struggling against the opposing class. Instead, they believe that the radical and subversive idea of a general strike pre-exists the workers' lives and leads them to carry out the strike. This is an inverted (顚倒的) mode of thinking (思惟態度) where ideas, tactics, and means precede life, rather than the life process producing the means or tactics of life. They attempt to discover the rationality and permanence of the privileged class's life within this inverted mode of thinking. This life attitude of the privileged class stands on the opposite footing (立脚地) from the life attitude that human existence determines human consciousness, not that human consciousness determines human existence. Therefore, they misinterpret struggle actions like general strikes, which inevitably arise due to the deterioration of life facts, as having their cause in agitation or instigation, instead of seeing them as means in the process of life recovery or evolution. That is, they place importance on the means or results and evade (迴避) or fear consciousness of the cause.

Observing it this way, while agitation or instigation may have the potential to provide a secondary motive that encourages tension and excitement in revolutionary and life-struggle actions, it is not the primary, fundamental driving force that generates and governs the struggle actions and emotions.

Therefore, as long as the social causes that induce a state of war in social life exist, the rebellious struggle actions of oppressed nations and classes possess an inevitability that cannot be removed from life facts. Thus, although the masses may sometimes be moved by agitation, human struggle instincts do not activate unless they feel the social deficiencies that make agitation possible—that is, threats to their livelihood. Wherever these struggle instincts operate, the driving cause of deficiencies in human life strictly exists. This is a solemn fact that can be uniformly observed in human social life from ancient times to the present, something well known even without hearing reports from biologists.

2. Questions and Answers Regarding the Movement in China

"A": It seems the Shanghai strike incident served as a fuse, ultimately expanding into a nationwide anti-Japanese and anti-British movement with the character of an uprising or riot. How do you view this recent anti-foreign incident in China?

"B": How do I view it? Regarding what aspect?

"A": Foreign newspapers and many critics related to the Shanghai incident seem to describe the movement as one for the recovery of national rights or a national movement, observing it as a pure nationalist movement without any disagreement. Do you also feel the same? <82>

"B": Answering like that is a bit difficult, but anyway, the fundamental driving force behind this movement in China is indeed the national background (民族的 背景), and it is a fact that it is rising up (厥起) centered around that. However, it cannot be seen purely as just a national movement or a movement for the recovery of national rights. Complex elements are included, and different currents (潮流) of tendencies intersect, forming the current shape of the movement, making it difficult to quickly grasp the true picture. Even if one vaguely calls it a movement for the recovery of national rights, it seems plausible. There also appear to be people who want to see it as a proletarian movement with anti-imperialist colors, led by workers, students, and communists. However, among the critiques I've read on this issue, the one I read with the most interest was the critical essay by Japan's Hasegawa Nyozekan (長谷川如是閑) titled "Labor-Capital Conflict and National Conflict." According to his view, roughly:

"Today's China has not reached a state of capitalist industry capable of forming labor-capital conflict (勞資對立關係) within one country and has not yet escaped the medieval industrial state. Consequently, the class structure of the proletariat under a modern capitalist industrial state has not formed there. Therefore, it stands to reason that there cannot be a class movement based purely on proletarian consciousness in an industrial state where the proletariat lacks class formation. Thus, circumstances exist where the Chinese people are more moved by national instinct (民族的 本能). This is the capitalist invasion of China by advanced capitalist countries like Japan, Britain, the US, etc., and consequently, the establishment of capitalist industry can be seen. In the case of China, the invasion of capital from Japan, Britain, the US, etc., simultaneously takes on the same nature as national conquest by Japan, Britain, etc., creating a situation of confrontation. This is because capital belongs to other nations like Japan and Britain, while labor belongs to the single Chinese nation, establishing a relationship where the traditional, instinctual national relationship is added to the labor-capital conflict relationship. However, while the development of capitalist industry and the progress of transportation are accompanied by global cooperation in life, which can regulate national instincts, in China's case, the confrontational relationship between capitalists and the proletariat also involves a national (民族的) confrontational relationship. Unlike India (印度) or Egypt (埃及), where the power of a certain strong capitalist nation leads to a colonial conquest state where national conquest/subjugation and labor-capital conflict coincide, in China, the invasion of foreign capital establishes capitalist industrial zones in localized areas like Shanghai (上海), Qingdao (靑島), or Hankou (漢口). <83> The class sentiment of labor-capital conflict in these localities is no reason for national sentiment to disappear. As long as the situation exists where the sense of self-preservation and development of one's own nation can be pursued based on national sentiment against the invasion of other nations, that national instinct and sentiment will not be extinguished by the class sentiment of labor versus capital. This is because, in China's case, the labor-capital conflict relationship becomes national, and that conflict forms capitalist industrial zones only in limited local areas, making it seem as if small capitalist states have formed within China. China is still in a medieval state where the central and local governments are in continuous confrontation, and sometimes situations arise where they fight each other. On the other hand, foreign capital invades China, and its capitalist industrial state forms in a small local part. Thus, the Chinese proletariat is simultaneously in a state of confrontation with capitalists as proletarians and in a state of confrontation between national conquerors and the conquered. National sentiment is by no means extinguished by the class sentiment of labor versus capital. Therefore, in China, proletarian consciousness combines with national consciousness to form a state of confrontation with the collective that is both the ruling capitalist class in China and also a foreign nation. Thus, the Chinese case possesses the characteristic feature of resistance based on national sentiment against modern economic conquest, added to the resistance of proletarian consciousness." I'm not sure if I've summarized the main points well, but it seems Mr. Hasegawa observes something like the above in the Chinese phenomenon. To rephrase it, because a capitalist industrial form has not been established in China, if capital had not become global in nature, China's industrial state would still be dominated under militaristic oppression. Consequently, if the proletariat did not have an international character, just as there were supposedly no proletarians in the feudal era in China, there would be no class composition of proletarians. They would merely be merchants, farmers, and artisans subject to government extortion (誅求), not an exploited class under capitalist industry. However, foreign capital invades certain locations like Shanghai and Qingdao, creating a confrontational relationship that is, on the one hand, a labor-capital relationship and, on the other hand, a national confrontational relationship. This is not a pure colonial state of national conquest and subjugation. It can be seen as foreign capital being established in a part of China, forming separate small capitalist state forms. Therefore, the Chinese case is different from other capitalist nations and also different from the form seen in colonies where labor-capital conflict and national conquest/subjugation are combined. <84> It is simply a phenomenon where a national confrontational relationship is added to the local labor-capital conflict relationship... In this way, it is neither a purely national movement nor has a capitalist industrial state formed sufficiently for it to be a purely proletarian class movement. In essence, it is resistance by the proletariat against the invasion of foreign capital, and at the same time, a national confrontational state based on that. That seems to be the appropriate view.

"A": Do you think so too?

"B": I generally agree, but there are a few points I question. It's nothing else but this: Mr. Hasegawa said something like this: "As capitalism develops internationally, national sentiment can be defeated by economic cooperation (協同). Also, despite capital gradually transcending national borders and developing into an international character, today's capitalist society requires a strong national collective, i.e., a state background. This is because accompanying strong state coercive action is convenient for fully pursuing the interests of capitalist society internationally, hence capitalism demands a state background." I have no disagreement with this view either, but he didn't mention whether the British proletariat and the Chinese masses or proletariat are in a relationship of national confrontation, or whether there is a possibility for them to cooperate on a class basis through economic cooperation. I see this as an important issue too, but since he didn't address it, isn't it impossible to clarify this problem without explaining what kind of confrontational relationship exists between the British proletariat, belonging to the state background, and the Chinese masses and proletariat, belonging to a different nation? If there is even some possibility for the two to cooperate currently, to what extent can the British capitalists' state background maintain its strength? And if cooperation between the Chinese masses and the British proletariat is possible, isn't China currently in a situation where national instincts can be regulated? This is what I question. Also, if the development of capitalism becomes global and national collectives split and develop along class lines, can't we see the development (發犔 - likely a typo for 發展/발전 - development/emergence) of irregular (變則的) functions in industrial life forms and consciousness forms? That is, in underdeveloped small nations and colonial countries with backward capitalist industries, proletarian consciousness reaches the same level of intensity as the consciousness of the proletariat in highly developed capitalist countries. Isn't this an inevitable irregular effect arising from the irregular exploitative relationships of global capitalist development? <85> Can't this effect be seen operating in China as well? Isn't it also an inevitable phenomenon arising from the global nature of capitalism and the life cooperation accompanying the progress of transportation? And thus, isn't there a possibility that national sentiment can be regulated or coordinated by class sentiment? Of course, Mr. Hasegawa pointed this phenomenon out, but in the case of China, there's a sense that his observational focus was concentrated on national sentiment, so isn't there a concern that he might have overlooked it? That's what I think. Besides that, I could bring up a few minor questions, but overall, I express my agreement with his view. And I think it's best to leave it at that for now.

"A": No, then what is your so-called correct observation? Let's hear it.

"B": Let's postpone that for the next opportunity. Honestly, it's beyond my capacity, but—

"A": Well then, I'll trouble you again when the opportunity arises.

"B": Alright then, I need to lie down for a bit. <86>

**Summary**

The article critiques the interpretation of the 1925 anti-foreign movement in China (sparked by the Shanghai strikes) as merely the result of external "agitation" by powers like Britain, Japan, and the US. The author, "Ch'ŏngjin Hagin," argues the root cause lies in the oppressed "life facts" (saenghwal sasil) of the Chinese people under imperialism and capitalism. A dialogue then discusses Hasegawa Nyozekan's analysis, which posits that China's unique situation—undeveloped domestic capitalism but localized foreign capitalist presence—blends national conflict with nascent labor-capital conflict. While largely agreeing, the author questions whether transnational class solidarity (e.g., between British and Chinese workers) might override national divisions and points out that global capitalism can foster advanced class consciousness even in less industrialized nations. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its sophisticated Marxist-influenced analysis of imperialism and class struggle in a neighboring colonized/semi-colonized nation, written by a Korean intellectual in 1925. It reflects the global spread of socialist ideas and their application to anti-colonial struggles, showing a Korean perspective on the complex interplay of nationalism and class consciousness in resisting foreign powers, themes highly relevant to Korea's own situation under Japanese rule. Historians might find the critique of the "agitation" narrative, the engagement with contemporary Japanese analysis (Hasegawa), and the specific focus on "life facts" versus ideology insightful.

1. 煽動과 生活事實
無産者運動이나 또다른 모든 被征服者의 運動에 잇서서 運動의 發生原因과 進行過程을 事實그대로 認識하지안코 第2 義的, 境遇에 依해서는 何等의 有機的 聯結이 업는 事件을 捕捉하야 그 運動自軆의 根本的 動機처럼 本末顚倒的의, 誇大的으로 信仰하는 思考의 傾向을 特權階級에서 發見할 수 잇다. 그것은 特權階級에게 在한 意識的 或은 無意識的 傾向인데, 自然한 事實그대로 認識하는 것은, 밧구아 말하면 特權階級의 生活壓迫에 因하야 反撥하는 被壓迫階級의 生活恢復 乃至 向上運動의 必然的 狀態를 事實 그대로 認識하는 것은 特權階級自軆의 存立의 基本的 事實의 不合理性 또는 崩解의 過程을 說明하는 것이 되는 까닭이다.
