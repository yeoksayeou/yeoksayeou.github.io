발행일	1925년 07월 01일
기사제목	黎明以前 (Before the Dawn)
필자	金麗水
기사형태	시 (Poem)

黎明以前
						金麗水
Are you only coming now, you people?
I stayed up the whole long night waiting for you.
When the crows and magpies hopped about, chirping of the morning,
How many times did I go outside the village entrance to see your arrival?

You wouldn't know.
In this desolate ruin, this rough ground,
The ill-tempered wind struggled in the empty air last night.
Ah, how many of the flower-like young folk,
Do you know how many bled and died innocently in this very place?

The crowd who died without seeing the bright morning,
While waiting for you to come,
Ah, the crowd that departed while waiting for the righteous people to come<122>
Do their blood-stained clothes not
Shine red in the rising morning sun?

All that has passed is but a jumbled dream.
When the small bell in the village over the hill rings,
Mourning the sufferer who departed on the road of eternity,
Behold the sun and rainbow above my head and yours!
Like they know nothing of the ruins, the tragedy of midnight.

You people I waited for all night,
Has that tedious dark night now passed?
Has it passed far away, a thousand, ten thousand *ri*?
Ah, the tediousness of the night that passed!<123>
<122-123>

**Summary**

The poem expresses the speaker's long, weary wait through a dark night for the arrival of "you people," symbolizing comrades or perhaps liberation itself. The speaker laments the sacrifices of the young who died ("bled and died innocently") before witnessing the "bright morning" or the arrival of the awaited "righteous people." Despite the sorrow and the memory of past tragedies ("desolate ruin," "tragedy of midnight"), the poem ends with the hopeful arrival of dawn, represented by the sun and rainbow, questioning if the long, "tedious dark night" has finally passed. From a historical perspective, this poem, published in 1925 under Japanese colonial rule, powerfully captures the intense longing for liberation ("黎明" - dawn), acknowledges the immense suffering and sacrifice endured by Koreans ("died without seeing the bright morning"), and expresses a fragile hope for a future free from the darkness of oppression. Historians would find the poem's allegorical language and its emotional weight indicative of the national sentiment and the subtle ways resistance and hope were expressed through literature during that period.
