Overseas and Domestic

P'yŏngyang Tidbits
By a P'yŏngyang Reporter

P'yŏngyang, being the second city, is naturally complex. In complex places, many strange things happen. Among them, public opinion is reportedly widespread that the clerks of the P'yŏngyang Electric Company or the telephone exchange operators are unpleasant and laughable. Listening to the reasons, when one calls about an electrical fault, they typically use informal language (banmal). When collecting fees, they act like detectives, cutting off small talk, opening doors, and demanding money. When entering every house for so-called "electric light inspections," they swagger around self-importantly, roll their sharp eyes, use informal language, and act high-handedly. This is said to be unpleasant. They say it's laughable that those without authority or decent character, working for less than the cost of feeding a Western dog, look down on others. The writer has also frequently clashed with this company. For example, the other day, our branch office was cut off mid-call while talking to someone on the phone. When we asked the operator why they cut off the call while we were talking and requested they be careful, the operator replied, "How insolent for a Korean." Feeling incredibly displeased, I clenched my fists on the spot and rushed breathlessly to the P'yŏngyang Post Office. It was past 9 PM, and only one or two staff members were there. Thinking quickly that there must be an acting director, I requested the acting director and questioned him breathlessly, wasting about 15 minutes. He said he didn't know and told me to go upstairs to the second floor and ask the telephone supervisor. Agreeing to do so, I left the post office and went to the Honmachi police substation, told a police officer the whole story, and requested his presence as I was going to question the telephone supervisor. The police officer immediately agreed. Going up to the second floor of the post office, we found the supervisor (Sakuma) and reprimanded him for allowing such unreasonable words and actions (the accompanying officer also told him to fix his way of speaking). Mr. Sakuma turned red and apologized profusely, saying, "I never said such a thing. Perhaps an operator misspoke. Please be patient while I investigate." What material does this make, and what value does it have as an article? However, as mentioned earlier, it's both unpleasant and laughable, and I feel nauseated by their unwarranted arrogant behavior, so I write this one word urging them to abandon their bad habits.

A Spectacle Among Fights
As everyone knows, the water supply system using taps has changed to a metered system. Households are busy putting up signs, and on the other hand, people sit like posts selling water. On top of that, the spectacle is the fight between a water seller and Mr. Kim, who lives next to the water barrel. Around 2 PM on June 2nd, at the Saiwaichō water barrel, the water seller was sitting under the shade of the neighboring house's wall to escape the heat. Seeing this, the homeowner remarked, "Hey, you're selling the Prefectural Office's water, so it's appropriate for you to sit on Prefectural Office land and sell it." The water seller smacked his lips and said, "My goodness, how can people be so heartless?" calling the homeowner someone impossible to deal with (水火不通, lit. unable to pass water or fire). The homeowner retorted, "Hey, you call me someone impossible to deal with? You're the fine gentleman (兩班, Yangban) who marks the water barrel with a red line and doesn't give even a drop during drought, you're the one impossible to deal with! Why would you sell Prefectural Office water on my land? ... Since you're being so precise, I'll be precise too." Their back-and-forth argument – what is it if not a spectacle?

Notes from Attending the Third Sinŭiju Oratorical Contest
By PYK

This contest, held on the border and subjected to excessive interference from the police, yet having already held its second iteration, now sees its third開催, bringing immense joy to this reporter. At the scheduled time of 8:30 PM on May 18th, the venue, the Public Hall auditorium, was truly packed beyond capacity with over a thousand spectators, allowing one to gauge the general popularity of this field. Thus, we can only hope that amidst the desolate border region, this event, growing steadily with each iteration, will yield many harvests.
The oratory began with Mr. Yi Ye-yong as the master of ceremonies.
1. Ills of Modern Society. Kim Tŭk-rin.
He is a passionate speaker, reputed as a rising orator representing the Sinman Youth Association. First pointing out all the flaws of this society, his main point was that the priority solution lies solely in preventing the tyranny of capital in the capitalist era, just as the tyranny of warriors was prevented in the feudal era. The contemporary color was vivid.
2. Let Us First Use Domestic Products. Kim Chŏng-sŏ.
Representing the Sinin Association, his talk on using domestic products seemed oddly contrasting. While earnestly advocating for the necessity of promoting domestic products, his statement that authorities view promoting domestic products as anti-Japanese behavior and treat it as subversive action shows a lack of observational power and is contradictory. His baseless theories and forced advocacy for domestic product use rather induced weariness in the audience.
3. Righteous Men Will Ultimately Conquer the World. Yi Ki-hyŏk.
He represented the Ŭiju Yongsan Christian Youth Association. Citing historical examples of justice ultimately achieving victory, much like an elementary school lesson, he mentioned the story of Abraham Lincoln, drawing jeers. He concluded mundanely with the point that if the element of justice were removed from all affairs in our society, everything would end in failure.
4. Poverty and Responsibility. Yi Yong-un.
Representing Sinyŏng School, he argued that poverty arises from not fulfilling responsibilities. His main point was that only by fulfilling responsibilities amidst poverty can the final victory aimed for be achieved. As he spoke ramblingly, the hall became somewhat chaotic.
5. On Success. Yi Myŏng-sik.
Representing the Sinŭiju Christian Youth, his topic itself seemed unusual. However, upon taking the stage, he unexpectedly deviated from the main subject, using contradictory metaphors that provoked antipathy from the general audience, leading to repeated jeers and chaos in the hall, forcing him to step down.
6. We Are Not Cripples. Kang Che-hwan.
Our nation is not crippled; rather, we are a people possessing outstanding national characteristics unmatched in the world. As examples, were there not athletes competing under the Chinese flag at the Oriental Olympic Games, and are there not students studying abroad achieving excellent results everywhere? Let us, being such people, strive even harder and tear out the shameful pages from our half-a-myriad-year history with our own hands – this was his main point.
7. What Our Youth Should Do Today. Pak Ch'un-gŭn.
Representing the Chŏngju Osan Student Youth Association, this young man of 16 this year, chosen as representative over students from grades 2, 3, 4, and 5, hints at his genius. We have not created any aspect of this world's civilization with our own hands. Therefore, we must first strive for education and learn; second, promote industry to secure stability in food, clothing, and shelter; and third, reform the deficiencies in all cultural systems with the hands of our youth. As he passionately argued, the hall became quiet as if water had been sprinkled, <93> and everyone was captivated with curiosity.
8. Our Path to Survival? Kim Pyŏng-sun.
Representing the Sinŭiju Second Christian Youth Association, his main point was that our path to survival lies solely in the propertied class sympathizing with the plans laid out by the intellectual class, starting from humble small factories like陋近 (nugŭn) to find our occupations.
His demeanor was passionate. Due to his tone resembling a Christian prayer, jeers like "Thank you" and "Amen" erupted repeatedly, causing general laughter.
9. The Cleaning Brigade. Kim Pong-jun.
Representing the Ch'ŏndogyo Youth, as he took the stage, the hall filled with laughter. This was because he is known for telling funny stories. All systems, people, or actions that are harmful to this human society will be put into the cleaning cart (淸潔구루마) of the Cleaning Brigade and removed. Society has joint responsibility. Just as the ears, eyes, mouth, nose, limbs, and the entire body cannot survive in isolation, so too is human life in society. Exceeding the time limit while passionately delivering this main point, he added a remark: The Sinŭiju First Christian Church building was constructed with donations from the general public (including non-believers), yet after completion, the church authorities' attitude changed completely. Under the pretext that the church is sacred, they forbid its use for any general public gatherings and even prohibit its use by the church's own youth association, forcing them to spend considerable money each time they need to gather and use cramped spaces. This is an unfair measure. Religion that distances itself from society like this is a harmful substance that our cleaning cart cannot forgive. This phrase was a refreshingly famous line.
Thus, the nine speakers concluded. The judges were exclusively lawyers: Ch'oe Ch'ang-jo, Yi Hŭi-dŏk, and T'ak Ch'ang-ha. Mr. Yi Hŭi-dŏk announced the results: 1st place, Pak Ch'un-gŭn; 2nd place, Kim Pong-jun; 3rd place, Kim Pyŏng-sun.
Furthermore, according to the votes distributed by our Kaebyŏk branch office, the following result was obtained:
Valid votes: 109. 1st: Kim Pong-jun (40 points), 2nd: Pak Ch'un-gŭn (35 points), 3rd: Kang Che-hwan (30 points).

Friendship in Name, Enmity in Reality
By a Reporter from the Anju Branch Office

The Private School Joint Field Day, hosted by the Anju Private School Staff Friendship Association on June 8th and 9th, had the primary spirit and purpose of mutual friendship and promoting physical education. However, during the actual running of the competition, not a single expression of friendship could be seen. Several individuals among the officials selected from the staff of each school blatantly displayed actions of envy and animosity (反猜反目), which even woodcutters and shepherds would disdain to do. To display such ugliness in front of over a thousand innocent children and a crowd forming a sea of people – is this not excessively degrading one's own position and character? As the saying goes, "Only after a man degrades himself do others degrade him" (人必自侮以後에 人이 侮之). Since this is self-inflicted, do not take offense at this writing. Instead, put more effort into moral cultivation regarding sports, so that from the first event onwards, like a mother and daughter making tofu (豆腐앗듯) in a harmonious family, strive to make it deeply interesting, allowing the general spectators to realize the true flavor of private school education, while ensuring your efforts do not end in vain.
And Mr. Yi, the general secretary: indulging in sweet and spitting out bitter (甘呑苦吐) – whether this is your usual tactic (常套手般) is unknown, but claiming there were some unfair points in judging regarding Taesŏng School where you work, you declared a withdrawal and simultaneously resigned from the position of general secretary citing illness, only to finish the event as a badgeless, red-faced general secretary. Unable to win the championship flag, you couldn't issue an order to disband the field day, only declaring withdrawal. You say you are invincible before the strong (强者), but the strength of the north (北方之强) is not strength to be adopted. If slapped on the right cheek, turn the left cheek also – had you practiced this holy teaching more faithfully...
Furthermore, authorities of Taehŭng School: regardless of the measurement, starting line, or judging departments, one staff member from your school was involved in each duty from beginning to end. What were you complaining about? After the scores were calculated and the championship flag was about to be awarded at the very end, why did you collect the prizes that the students recognized as their own <94> and return them to the organizing committee, and what was the reason for raising an objection? If you had complaints about the measurements, there was Mr. Cho; if about the starting line, Mr. An; if about judging, Mr. Chŏng. Were you three just acting like scarecrows (꾸어 은 麥 노릇) for two days? The students competed well, but failing to win the championship flag is your responsibility. You should be begging forgiveness from your school's founder, parents, and students. What kind of reckless behavior is this? Please exercise more self-restraint.
Next, regarding Yusin School, due to an article in the Chosun Ilbo claiming they won the glory of championship through the feat of fielding ineligible athletes, public opinion in society is divided, let alone between the concerned parties. While we cannot believe all articles in newspapers and magazines are entirely accurate, we acknowledge them to some extent. Unless the Chosun Ilbo reporter is a complete fool (全然天痴), it's difficult to dismiss the article as entirely groundless rumor. As for Yusin School, it is an extraordinary school belonging to the management of a religious organization that teaches the Bible for several hours a week, and accordingly, all school personnel are either clergy or devout believers. Considering this relationship, it is also difficult to acknowledge it as fact. We can only hope for a clear resolution clarifying right and wrong as negotiations between the two parties proceed.

Hamnam Branch Office

Consequently, among the middle schools, general parents have traditionally revered the local Higher Common School (高等普通學校) above all else. Even the students of that school receive all sorts of praise. Students who barely manage to enter this school wear the school cap costing 300 units (presumably Wŏn or sen) and, amidst the congratulations and envy from the general public, naturally hold their shoulders high and are said to be incredibly foolish. Not everyone is like this, but May 13th was the Silver Wedding Anniversary (of the Japanese Emperor and Empress), and students from boys' and girls' schools held celebratory lanterns and paraded through the entire city around 10 PM that day. Hearing that a large fight would resume between the students of the two schools on this occasion, the principal of Hamhŭng Higher Common School, unable to bear the anxiety, reportedly called the principal of the Agricultural School and confessed one by one that his school was at fault. Thus, the general public and the student community in the area say that Higher Common School students are extremely arrogant (나마이키, namaiki - Japanese loanword) wherever they go. The Agricultural Association is a Mockery Association (弄會), the County Magistrate is a County Enemy (郡讎)
By a Reporter from the Kowŏn Branch Office

The Kowŏn County Agricultural Association (高原郡農會), aiming to promote the industry of the county residents, took the opportunity of the joint silkworm cocoon sale starting June 17th. With a budget of 150 Wŏn, they plan to give lottery tickets only to top-performing sericulture farmers within the county based on excellent results. Those chosen by lottery will receive prizes: 2 special prize winners get 1 farm cow each, 5 first prize winners get 1 improved pig each, and 20 second prize winners get 1 improved breeding chicken each. This policy aims to encourage county residents to compete for excellent results in industry. Looking at this, one cannot help but praise it as an administration genuinely promoting industry.
However, looking beneath the surface, while there might be some points worthy of praise, the so-called primary policy of industry promotion, flying the banner of cooperative administration focused on silkworm cocoon joint sales, is solely limiting the sale to Japanese buyers, which is extremely harmful to the people (人°‘ -> 人民). Allowing one person to buy everything makes it absolutely impossible to raise the price appropriately; it will only lead to a price drop. Regardless of who the merchant is, it is a principle to lower the price as much as possible for goods bought at a suitable price, as there is no profit constraint. Looking at last year's example, the facts are clear. How can this be seen as a sudden, isolated incident? Therefore, the county residents held a large meeting, elected 5 executive committee members, and entrusted them with negotiating with the county authorities to change this policy. The executive committee thoroughly demanded the policy be changed to sales via competitive bidding, but the county authorities argued that they had already signed a contract with one Japanese person, and furthermore, had borrowed a large sum of 2,600 Wŏn from that contractor to build a cocoon drying facility (乾繭場), so they absolutely could not comply with the demand this year. If there were truly no benefit whatsoever, why would a Japanese person lament the small size of the drying facility and lend such a large sum to the County Agricultural Association interest-free for 3 years? Whatever the case, covering that amount means robbing the profits of the sericulture farmers. No matter how eloquent the arguments, with clear facts and evidence, what explanation can there be? The gains and losses will be known when the time comes. If there is only harm, the county residents are watching closely, intending to demand compensation from the head of the County Agricultural Association. Seeing this, the county authorities only stick to their stubbornness, acting deaf to the resentful voices of the county residents. Furthermore, under the guise of promoting the residents' interests, they are instead skimming the fat <96> off the county residents' industry only to fill the private pockets of one Japanese person. Head of the County Agricultural Association, Kim Hyŏng-t'ae! County Magistrate, Lord Kim Hyŏng-t'ae! Please come to your senses.

A Year in a Foreign Country
From the USA, by Y. H. Kim
I receive the Kaebyŏk magazine once a month and read it faithfully. Already, a long time, one year, has passed in this white man's country.
Brother Kim! I left Kyŏngsŏng last January. On February 8th, I boarded a steamship at Kobe port. When I landed in San Francisco on March 6th, I met and bid farewell to Mr. Yu Paek-hŭi and Mr. Son Kŭm-sŏng. Then, coming up from New Orleans, the primary port and bustling city in the southern part of North America on the Gulf of Mexico, I arrived in Jackson City, the capital of Mississippi state, on March 14th last year. I immediately looked for a school and enrolled, but how can I record the suffocating feeling with just one brush? Look! First, I lacked the most necessary thing anywhere – money. Don't think I had paper currency. Second, I couldn't communicate in English, the most necessary thing in this land. Among all this, I didn't know a single person, yellow, white, or black. So, I knew no people, knew no writing, knew no language, knew no customs – I knew nothing at all. However, shamelessly, I sat among white men and women every hour, holding my textbooks. Listening to the teacher's lecture was like reading scriptures to a cow's ear (牛耳讀經), but there's a limit. The good-natured blue-eyed, white-haired youths chattered, laughed, asked questions, and giggled as they pleased. I spent a year in their midst.
Recently, I understand a little and can speak a little. It feels like I've seen and lived in a different world of human life. Amidst this, I passed several subjects. Among those I chose, one subject's grade was poor. I am putting a lot of effort into mathematics among them. However, I don't know what changes might occur in the next 2 or 3 years; for now, I just focus on studying without knowing anything else. Last summer, I studied at a summer course, and I plan to do the same this summer. Anyway, I need to study quickly so that living here won't be too difficult. Perhaps it's my fate not to die but to live; an American who worked at the Anglican Church (聖公會) in Kyŏngsŏng 10 years ago came here as a school teacher. So, he is very sympathetic. During the past year, I briefly met one Korean person on the street. Other than that, I haven't seen even a shadow. Brother! Write much and disseminate much to bring great happiness to our people. <97>

Korean Fan and Indian Fan (Buddha Image)
From Ceylon, India, by Kong T'ak
Leaving Shanghai on April 23rd, I stopped here and there and have now reached Ceylon, India. Wherever I go, the sound of the strong devouring the weak is frightening. The Buddha images (부체님, archaic/dialectal for 부처님) I see here are very different from those in Korea. The Buddha images I saw in Korea have eyes like thin threads, are clear, flow with curved beauty, and seem to evoke blue clouds. However, the Buddha images I see here have large eyes, and like the actual form of Indians, their expressions seem very stuffy and troubled. Korea's sky is high, and its water is clear. Therefore, its Buddha images are also bright and clean. India's sky is a lump of fire, and the earth burns. Therefore, they yearn for the lotus flower (蓮花) and consequently set paradise (極樂世界) against that backdrop.
I saw in the newspapers here reports that the saint Gandhi (칸듸), who came to approve violence from non-violence, went to Calcutta (칼캇타), and several thousand people kissed his feet. India's revolutionary receives kisses on his feet. But India still faces difficulties. It's because they have too many religious sects. What about Korea?

Seconding Brother Ch'unp'a's Motion
From Tokyo, by Pyŏkto

Brother Soch'un! Only yesterday was I able to browse through the contents of the May issue of Kaebyŏk. =Borrowed briefly from a friend=.
Didn't it contain a short letter sent to you, brother, from Brother Ch'unp'a in Hwanghae Province?
There it is! Right there. Isn't there that final, poignant motion?! "It would be good to establish a Farmers' Section in Kaebyŏk"... that's the one.

Brother Soch'un! I absolutely agree with Brother Ch'unp'a's motion. How many times, I wonder, have I discussed with you, brother, over the past few years, the idea of creating an institution for farmers – farmer-centric guidance, public opinion, education, practical benefits, and recreation?! You likely know well that while harboring such thoughts, I couldn't start due to the lack of that wretched "money," always burning with impatience in my narrow chest. That Pyŏkto has now kicked off a government career like worn-out shoes (弊履) and abruptly set foot on Tokyo's soil can, in a sense, be seen as preparation for realizing this method.
Now, hearing Brother Ch'unp'a's motion, I feel an even more pressing and profound significance.
Brother Soch'un!
Korea of agriculture!
Korea of farmers!
Korea of farming villages!
Korea, with its history as an agriculture-based nation – our Korea, no matter how I think about it, seems to be in different circumstances than other countries. Accordingly, among the various tasks we must undertake, trying to bloom temporary flowers imitatively or trendily, just because others shout "Fire!"...! seems destined to become a kind of fruitless fig (無果花), yielding no gains.
Therefore, shouldn't we consider our circumstances and select our tasks, the ones we should do first?
Brother Soch'un! Even if establishing farmer schools in every nook and cranny (坊坊曲曲) with our strength is difficult for the time being, surely enabling those farmers wandering in ignorance to hold a magazine volume once a month wouldn't be impossible if there's sincerity?.
Moreover, reflecting on the bitter experience that the work we have done so far has been superficial (枝葉的), failing to nurture the roots, we feel the need to halt all ongoing projects if necessary and strive for this fundamental movement of farmer enlightenment. Reaching this point, it feels like we should undertake even greater tasks.
In Korea, an agriculture-based nation, the fact that there was no institution whatsoever for the farmers is a great failure on our own part and simultaneously shameful even to others. Moreover, facing this critical juncture (危急存亡之秋) where the lives of the farmers, the entirety of the nation, are appearing moment by moment on the brink of starvation, how urgent must be the wait for the establishment of an institution to guide them!
Brother! Such talk needs no borrowing of Pyŏkto's mouth; you, brother, surely know this even better, making further words unnecessary.
How about realizing Brother Ch'unp'a's opinion as the very first step? Of course, an independent farmers' magazine should eventually be born, but let's consider that as being in gestation (孕胎 中) and not yet full term. In the meantime, how about taking about 10 pages from Kaebyŏk's editorial section and about 10 from the literary section, thus creating a section of around 20 pages in total, specifically naming it the "Farmers' Section" (農民欄)? We could systematically publish, month after month, in plain explanations, the opinions and theories of seniors well-versed in rural affairs, making it somewhat like a lecture series?

I think: "Kaebyŏk" itself, to the extent that it doesn't bend its own consistent principles, rather than striving to solidify a high-flown, difficult-to-implement idealistic ground, how noble would it be if it could instead contribute to the pressing life issues of the time...?

In this respect, even if the entire space of Kaebyŏk were turned into a pure "Farmers' Magazine" for the 14 million masses of Korea, the farmers, it wouldn't be regrettable. Rather, the magnanimity (雅量) should be praised for its preciousness.
Brother Soch'un!
I second Brother Ch'unp'a's motion. <99>

**Summary**

This extensive "Overseas and Domestic" section from the July 1925 issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine compiles news reports and letters from various locations within Korea (P'yŏngyang, Songdo/Kaesŏng, Sinŭiju, Anju, Hamhŭng, Kowŏn, Yŏnghŭng) and from Koreans abroad (USA, India, Philippines, Manchuria, Japan). The articles cover a wide range of topics: complaints about the arrogance and poor service of employees at the P'yŏngyang Electric Company and Post Office; disputes within organizations like the Songdo Shop Clerks' Association (embezzlement) and between schools (Anju field day conflicts, Hamhŭng student brawls); social commentary on drunken teachers, perceived elitism of Higher Common School students, and attempts by Confucian groups to hold traditional events resisted by youth groups; reports on a speech contest in Sinŭiju; economic grievances regarding water meters and alleged collusion between local authorities and Japanese businessmen in cocoon sales (Kowŏn); and personal letters reflecting the experiences and thoughts of Koreans living and studying overseas, as well as a strong plea from Tokyo to include a dedicated "Farmers' Section" in *Kaebyŏk* to address the needs of Korea's predominantly agricultural population.

Historians would find this collection valuable for its diverse snapshot of Korean society under Japanese rule in 1925. It reveals everyday friction points (public services, inter-school rivalries), internal conflicts within Korean organizations, the emerging consciousness and activism of youth groups challenging traditional norms (Confucian events), economic exploitation concerns involving Japanese interests and Korean collaborators (Kowŏn cocoon sales), the lives and perspectives of the Korean diaspora, and debates about the direction of Korean society and media (the plea for a farmers' section). The raw complaints, local disputes, and ideological clashes provide a ground-level view often missing in official narratives.

5. 成功에 就하야. 李明湜君.
新義州基督靑年代表인 君의 演題부터 異彩를 띄엿드니 及其也 登壇하야는 意外로 本問題를 離脫하야 矛盾된 比辭로 一般聽衆의 反感을 사기 때문데 『야지』가 連發하고 場內가 騷亂遂下壇.

우리 民族은 不具者가 아니요 도리여 世界에 比類가 업는 優秀한 民族性을 가진 民族이다. 그의 例로서는 오리엔달오림픽大會에 中國旗발 아래로 出場選手가 잇스며 各國留學生으로 到處에서 優秀한 成績을 獲得하지 아니하느냐. 이러한 우리로서 加一層奮發하야 半萬年歷史를 羞恥되는 페지를 우리 손으로 때여 버리자라는 主旨이엿다.
