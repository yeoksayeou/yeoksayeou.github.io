Publication Date: 1925년 07월 01일
Article Title: 땅파먹는 사람들 (二等) (People Who Dig the Earth to Eat (Part 2))
Author: 朴吉洙
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction)

People Who Dig the Earth to Eat
						Pak Kil-su

One

From the high peak of Kosalmi, two mountain ranges snaked and stretched out—like a blue dragon and a white tiger diverging left and right, drawing a grand semicircle before converging again, gently settling down to form a plain in front. Water seeping from the mountain valleys gently washed the earth's surface, carrying the soil's richness, stream by stream, rivulet by rivulet, merging to form a river that flowed forward in curves. Standing by this water, Noma, who had been busy building embankments along the curves, paused his work, looked at his older brother who was taking a short break, and called out, "Sŏng! Sŏng!"

Noma's brother took the pipe from his mouth, stood up, and asked with a startled look, "What is it? Are you hurt somewhere?"

"No, no! I'm not hurt—." <49>
Putting his brother's panicked heart at ease after his overly urgent cry, Noma spoke again,
"Look there! Look there!" pointing to the dike of the paddy field across the way.

On the bank where fresh green sprouts of grass dotted the darkish earth, red pasqueflowers (할미꼿) bloomed, reflecting the smiling sunlight in the clear morning dew, bathed in the rising sun's rays.

"The pasqueflowers have bloomed—Sŏng!" Noma repeatedly pointed at the bank.

Noma's brother stood, rolling his large dark eyes, looking at the bank to find the red flowers.
"Where? I don't see them—"
"There! Right next to the clump of green mugwort sticking up, don't you see the red—?"
"Yes! Yes! I see it now. On the upper paddy dike, right?"
"Uh-huh, on the upper paddy dike—. I'll go dig them up." As soon as Noma finished speaking, he took the hoe he was working with, splashed through the water, dug up the pasqueflowers, picked some mugwort, and returned, calling his brother again.

"Sŏng, Sŏng! There's only one flower—. But look how much mugwort there is. Isn't this flower really pretty?"
"Ah, it truly is pretty!"

Noma's brother, who had started working again, looked at the flower with its fine yellow pollen (花粉) scattering from within the red petals (花瓣), as if seeing a flower he had never seen before.
"Ah, truly pretty," he remarked again, looking closely.

"If you smell it, the scent is so sharp, it feels like needles pricking inside your nose! Here—Sŏng, smell it."
Noma held the flower right under his brother's nose. As the sharp, golden-dust-like pollen entered his nostrils with his breath,
"Ah, the smell is truly nice too!" Noma's brother shook the flower. The fragrance intensified. <50>

"It smells even better with this mugwort—."
Noma held up the mugwort.
"The smell is even nicer, right? Sŏng."
"Uh-huh."
"Let's take this flower home, plant it, and watch it grow nicely. Okay—Sŏng?"
"Uh-huh. Let's take it and grow it well."

Noma placed the flower and mugwort on the bank and started working again.
The cuckoo, which had been singing since before sunrise from this mountain and that, announcing the arrival of spring, was still carried away by its excitement.
"Cuckoo, cuckoo," it sang, stirring the gentle breeze, and its melody intertwined with the breeze, circling around the two brothers' ears.

The sound of the murmuring water flowing by. The voices of people plowing fields here and there. The faint sound of chickens crowing from the village. All these sounds mingled with the gentle breeze, softly striking Noma's ears.
However, he didn't seem to hear any of these sounds. Instead, some faint, powerful, sharp sound seemed to ceaselessly prickle his nerves—a sound emanating from the yellow-green buds (萌芽) pushing through the earth, from the tree branches where new sprouts were bursting forth, pulsating (波動). Noma felt his life leap with an unknown impulse (衝動).

It wasn't the first time he had seen this sight—the grasses and trees that had withered, dried, fallen, and turned yellow and sickly in the autumn frost, having endured the cold, terrifying, gloomy winter, now vigorously sprouting new buds and blooming flowers, continuously growing. But strangely, today it gave Noma a great impulse.

Noma stopped working and pulled out a blade of grass.
It pulled out without effort, swaying even in his breath—a very weak blade of grass, no different from what he had seen before.
Noma examined the grass from different angles, then placed it on his palm and rubbed it vigorously. Green juice emerged as the grass was crushed, <52> and the fragrant grassy smell pricked his nerves, giving only a pleasant sensation (快感); there was nothing particularly unusual.

Noma looked again at the grassy field and up at the tree branches. The grass and trees seemed to convey a faint, sharp, powerful sound to Noma's ears, showing him some great force, pulsing and growing larger.

"Sŏng! Sŏng! This grass is so small, yet how strong and resilient it is... It didn't freeze to death even in that cold, and now that it's met spring, it's growing tremendously—"
Noma pulled out another blade of grass, showed it to his brother, his face expressing wonder (驚異).

"It's a living thing too, wanting to live, somehow surviving that cold and growing like that again!"
A solemn look crossed Noma's brother's face.

"Sŏng! Seeing this grass and these trees survive that cold, there must be some strange power in them, right? Isn't that so, Sŏng?"
"Aren't all living things like that? If you think about it, it's all strange!"
"Sŏng! When the leaves fall in autumn, there's something the size of a mung bean stuck where the leaf fell off, right?"
"Yeah! You mean those bud-like things stuck on every tree branch."
"Uh-huh, that's it. Last fall, I picked one to see what was inside. Looking closely, there was a tiny new sprout, the size of a sesame seed, inside. I picked one from another tree and tore it open, and it also had a new sprout inside—. Maybe that's what grows into leaves and flowers when spring comes?"
"Yeah! From inside those, some grow into leaves, and some grow into shoots. I saw that too when I looked closely before."
"Sŏng! Sŏng! Then isn't that even stranger? If that tiny sesame-seed-sized thing were in the ground, it might at least be less cold, but how does it survive on a bare tree branch without freezing to death, and then sprout like that—?"
"Even during that cold time, there's sunlight, so maybe it gets strength from there to survive, and then when spring comes, it grows big like that—" <52>

"There might be sunlight, but in such cold weather, could it even be lukewarm? Yet, seeing them survive so fresh, it seems like there's some great creation even inside something as tiny as a sesame seed—Sŏng! What kind of creation allows it to live like that—?"
"..."
Noma's brother looked at Noma without a word, and Noma just carefully examined the blade of grass.

The two started working again. After a while, Noma felt thirsty and put his mouth to the gushing stream, gulping down the water.
The fragrant earthy smell of the slightly bitter water, flowing with dissolved soil nutrients, seeped into Noma's whole body, stirring his life force, making it leap.
Noma drank his fill, stood up, swung his arms around, and looked up at the village.

The Kosalmi peak rising behind the village embraced the village with its two sprawling mountain ridges drawing a semicircle on either side, like a mother holding her child to her breast. A blue haze of morning cooking smoke hung low over the village, and the sound of chickens crowing drifted faintly. Haze shimmered over the mountains like a veil woven from spider silk, tinged with green. The sun, shining brilliantly above the eastern mountain ridge, looked down like the eye of a father smiling at his nursing child, upon all the creatures on the earth just beginning to grow.
Receiving this feeling from the earth and the sun, Noma continued his work.
The two men were absorbed in their work, silently building the embankment.

After a while, Noma's brother sat on the paddy dike and started talking again.
"Noma! Noma!"
"Uh-huh." <53>
Noma tightened the belt around his waist over his sinking stomach as he answered.
"It must be time for breakfast, right? Noma!"
"I wonder if it hasn't passed already."
"Everyone else is going too. Shall we go?"
"Sŏng! There's only that little corner left. Let's finish that bit and then go."
"Right, let's do that. There isn't much left—."
Noma finished the work he was doing and set out with the flower and mugwort.

"Sŏng, let's go now."
"Okay, let's go."
"Sŏng, you must be very hungry. You didn't eat much last night either..."
"No, I'm alright, but you must be very hungry."
"I was hungry before, but now I don't feel hungry—"
"It's because the time has passed. Mother will be waiting. Let's hurry home."
"Uh-huh... By the way, Sŏng, when are you planning to leave?"
"I plan to go after about ten days."
"Then there are only about ten days left to work and talk together with you, Sŏng. When you leave then, when will you come back?"
"Exactly. My heart wants to come back soon, but things don't always go as planned."
"That's true, but come back often, okay, Sŏng?"
"..."
"Are you planning to go to Taep'an (Osaka) again this time?"
"I'll have to see whether I end up in Taep'an or somewhere else. I'll go here or there depending on the circumstances." <54>

"Taep'an... they say there are many strange things there, and life is interesting... Is that right?"
"There are strange things and interesting things, sure, but since I have nothing, what use is it? Sometimes seeing those things makes my chest ache with anger, and I can't stand it."
"Still, living in a place like that, you learn a lot and understand what the world is like, right? Isn't it? It must feel like truly living, more than living here. Right, Sŏng?"
"It might seem so, but for someone with nothing, it's all the same."
The two continued talking ceaselessly as they returned home.

Two

"Mother! Mother!"
Upon returning home, Noma called for his mother from the entranceway.
"Is that Noma? You must be starving." From the low kitchen (정지), where dark soot hung like drapery from the eaves (쳠하) and ceiling, and the walls and doors were all stained reddish-black from smoke, a reddish face appeared. Red lips parted, and the mother's voice, red and hot as blood, came out between white teeth.
"Why are you only coming now?"
"We finished the embankment work, so we're late. Mother, Mother! Look at this red flower, and the green mugwort."
"Truly pretty. Where did you dig it up?"
"From the paddy dike. Here! Smell it. Doesn't it smell nice?"
"Truly, the fragrance is lovely too."
"Mother! Let's boil this mugwort right away, and plant the flower to look at. Okay—." <55>

"That would be nice, but where is there a place to plant flowers?"
Noma's mother knew there was no place, but she looked around the yard anyway. Hearing this, Noma also frowned and looked around. The small yard was more than half taken up by the manure pile; there was no place to plant flowers.
"Mother! Where would be a good place to plant it?"
"There's no place inside the house, so do as you please."
Noma thought about where to plant it, smelling the flower's fragrance, then asked,
"Mother! Where did Father go?"
"Isn't today the day for the 'meeting' (뫼)? So he left without even eating breakfast."
The mother's voice came out weakly.
"You mean the meeting called by the company (회사)?"
"Yes!"
As these words ended, shouting sounds from afar reached Noma repeatedly.
"Mother, Mother! What's that noise?"
"..."
The two listened intently.
As the shouting stopped, now whispering sounds from inside the room, barely audible, seeped through the window cracks, faintly striking their ears before passing.

"You must be very hungry? Your face has become pale!" – a thin, frail woman's voice.
"I might be hungry, but my complexion wouldn't change." – a man's laughter accompanying his words.
"No! It's true. I wish there were a mirror (석경) so I could show you your face. I'll come early today. Yesterday I just uselessly <56> wandered around, didn't even pick many greens, came back late, and only made you hungry like this..."
"Is breakfast ready?"
"It's been ready for a while, but we have to wait for Father to eat. If you want to eat first, I'll bring it to you—."
As the sounds from the room ended, shouting was heard again from afar.
Noma, startled, faced the room and cried out.
"Sŏng, Sŏng! Listen to that noise, something's happened—."
Noma's sister-in-law burst open the door, carrying a baby on her back. "What noise?" she asked in a startled tone, looking at Noma. Noma's brother followed her out, smiling faintly.
"Looks like another fight broke out," he said, putting on his shoes and heading out.
"Is it another fight with the company?"
"Looks like it. Let's go see."
Noma left the flower behind and ran towards the shouting.

From the Kosalmi peak, the mountain range stretching to the left—the Blue Dragon—curved around the village and gently sloped down to a wide field covered in grass. In the center stood a large mound (뫼), almost the size of a small mountain. The owner of this mound was someone who used to hold sway over the people here in the past, but now, somehow, even his descendants were nowhere to be seen, and only the village children came here to play.
Countless farmers had gathered here. On top of the mound stood a man in Western clothes, waving his arms in time with the farmers' shouts, yelling at the top of his lungs. Noma, along with the crowd, felt his blood boil as if his body would catch fire, listening to the man speak.

"Instead of you all making such a racket, listen carefully to what I have to say."
"We don't want to hear a long speech, just tell us straight out whether you're taking the paddies away or not." <57>
The man in Western clothes smiled and opened his mouth.
The crowd fell silent again, as if dead.

"You all raise cows, don't you? And you lend your cows to other people too, right?"
"We don't want to hear that kind of talk."
The crowd shouted again. The man in Western clothes, his smiling face flushing red, shouted back.
"Everyone, when you lend out your cows, you either receive wages or you do labor exchange (품아시), don't you? In the same way, our company lends you paddies and receives rent (도됴). That's how it is."
As his words reached this point, shouts arose from the crowd.
"We don't take excessively large amounts like you do."
The man in Western clothes continued speaking without interruption.

"When you lend out your cows, suppose one person offers fifty jeon for a day's use, and another person offers one won. To whom would you lend it? It goes without saying, even a three-year-old child knows it's a given principle to lend it to the person offering one won. Doing so, as you all know, is human nature.
Therefore, our company also demanded more rent, and decided to give the tenancy rights to those who pay more. You all say our company takes excessively large amounts, but you wouldn't refuse if someone offered you more, and being human yourselves, you would also demand more.
Yet you curse only us, calling us thieves. Isn't this truly baffling? If the person offering fifty jeon were to curse you, asking why you didn't lend it to him, what would you think of that person? Isn't that person either crazy or a thief?
I will say no more on this. And if what I am saying is a lie, then I will acknowledge <58> your demands as right. However, as you yourselves admit, what I have said is not that. If there is anyone among you who would give to the one offering fifty jeon instead of the one offering one won, please step forward.
Therefore, our company will assert our position to the very end. If you do not listen, we will give the rights to someone else. If it comes down to it, immigrants will even come from the mainland (내지), so I hope you will think carefully and settle this amicably for both sides."
Pausing his speech briefly, the man in Western clothes leaned on the cherry wood (사구라) stick he held and looked down at the crowd for a moment.
"I am leaving now. Those who agree by tomorrow, please bring your seals to the company office. For those from whom we hear nothing by tomorrow, we regretfully will have to revoke your tenancy rights."
He then turned, swinging his stick, and strode away.

Noma followed him out.
"Sir, sir!"
"What is it, boy?"
"Uh— I have something to say, sir."
"What is it?"
"Earlier, you said it's human nature not to lend a cow to someone offering fifty jeon, but to lend it to someone offering one won, right, sir?"
"That's right."
"And you said even a three-year-old child knows that, right, sir?"
"That's right."
"You definitely said that, right, sir?" <59>

"Yes! I definitely said that."
"Then what you said is wrong, sir."
"Why do you say it's wrong?"
"Why it's wrong is because I don't do that, so it's wrong, sir. And among my friends too, there are many who don't do that—."
"Then what would you boys do?"
"We, sir, what we do is, even if someone offers fifty jeon, no, even if someone can't pay a single푼 (pun - penny), if that person is in greater need, we definitely give it to that person, sir. And if I need something, I ask someone else and use it...
Among us friends, we've never once taken money for lending things. So isn't what you said wrong, sir? Even we don't do that, yet you said even a three-year-old child does..."
"How old are you?"
The man in Western clothes smiled and asked Noma this.
Noma, his eyes flashing like sunlight, rolled them this way and that, looked up at the man, and replied.
"I'm twelve, sir. Why do you ask that, sir?"
"Truly a bright child... But you are still young, how would you know about worldly affairs or the human heart!"
"That gentleman thinks a child isn't a person."
"Hey, you brat! Stop it and go away."
"That gentleman told a lie, and now he's embarrassed, so he's trying to cover it up and dismissing me as a child, not even treating me like a person. Why isn't my heart a human heart, sir? Why are you chasing me away?"
"..."
"Why are you just running away without saying anything, sir? Telling lies in broad daylight—" <60>

"Why don't you treat a child like a person and just chase me away, sir?"
Noma shouted, but the man in Western clothes didn't answer, only walking away faster.
The noisy crowd was now quiet, perhaps calmly plotting their next move regarding the future. The surroundings became strangely quiet, like the lull before a storm, and only Noma's shouting echoed through the mountains.
"Why isn't a child a person, sir?"

Three

The sun, which had poured out its growing energy onto all living things throughout the day, was now setting. Although it was dinnertime in the village, apart from a few scattered houses, there was almost no sign of cooking smoke.
When the sun had completely set and a deathly quiet dusk arrived, even those few wisps of evening smoke had already vanished. The village was desolate, like an uninhabited land, with only the shining stars and moonlight in the sky profoundly illuminating people's hearts.
Noma, his heart darkening like the deepening twilight, tried to brighten it with a single red star shining far away in the eastern sky, waiting for his mother who had gone to gather wild greens and had not yet returned.

Noma's father, who had gone twenty li away without eating breakfast to try and keep the tenancy rights the company was taking away, had returned. He lay in the room, his face pale, his bloodshot eyes blinking open and shut. Noma's nephew—the baby—who had cried all day without getting milk, was now exhausted, unable even to cry, just sleeping in his father's arms.
A rumbling sound continuously rose from their stomachs.
Noma, who had been gazing at the distant stars, imagining his mother's face, and drifting off, was jolted back to awareness by the rumbling sound and finally called his brother. <61>

"Sŏng! Sŏng!"
"Uh-huh?"
"Father hasn't eaten since morning... he must be starving—. Mother hasn't come back yet, shouldn't we try to make dinner somehow—?"
"Well! I've been thinking about it since earlier, but if it were rice, we could just cook it, but it's green wild-vegetable porridge, how can we possibly make it? We just have to wait for Mother—there's no other way."
"I wish she'd come quickly, what if she comes late!"
"..."
"Sŏng! Sŏng! The moon is already setting and it's getting pitch dark. Why is Mother so late today? Sŏng! Shouldn't we get a torch (횃불) and go look for her?"
"Yes, let's go look."
Noma's brother held his son, and Noma lit a bundle of bush clover (싸리비) to use as a torch, and they set out to find their mother.

At the village entrance (어구), other children stood in groups, calling for their mothers.
"Mother—. Mother—."
The mothers they called didn't answer; only the mountains echoed gloomily.
"Mother—. Mother—."
Noma, along with these other children, trod the narrow, rough mountain path, heading deep into the valley to search for their mothers.

"Noma, yesterday she came when the crescent moon was setting, but why hasn't she come yet today?"
"As long as she comes back safely, even if late..."
"Hey kids, let's ask around here if anyone's seen them coming."
"Let's do that." <62>
The children shouted together,
"Mother! Mother!" But the mothers they called didn't answer; the echoing mountains cried "Mother! Mother!" as if weeping.

Searching for their mothers like this, Noma finally met his mother, along with the other children, just as the torch burned out.
"Mother! Why are you so late today—?"
"Not only did I leave home late, but I took the wrong path, suffered all day, couldn't pick many greens, and ended up this late too. Is Father back?"
"Uh-huh, he came back around sunset."
"He hasn't eaten since morning, he must be starving. What happened with the paddy fields?"
"I asked, but he didn't say anything. I guess it probably fell through."
"..."
"Mother, give me the bundle (보구름). My shoulder hurts, I'll carry it!"
"It's alright, leave it."
"I'll carry it, give it here—."
"It's alright. Where is your sister-in-law (아주머니)?"
"She's coming behind with Sŏng, nursing the baby and talking—."
"Call her to hurry. We need to go quickly and make Father's meal!"
"Leave them be. They're having a nice chat right now... Don't call them, okay—Mother."
"Then let's just us hurry."
Noma and his mother arrived home while chatting back and forth. <63>

Noma's mother went into the room, while Noma rummaged through the gathered wild greens, searching for cuckoo flower roots (뻐꿈때) and other edible roots, chewing on them as he sat.
Suddenly, shouting erupted from the room—
"Coming back late, why don't you just hurry up and make the meal...
What's a woman (게집년) going to do asking about the paddy field work—"
Hearing his father's gruff shouting, Noma then heard his mother's gentle, trembling voice follow.
"As if I wanted to come back late—. No matter how much I wanted to come back early, how could I just bring back an empty basket... So..."
"I don't want to hear it, what nonsense is this? Before my fist flies, get out and cook the meal—"
Hearing the word "fist," Noma shot up.
—Ah! What if they start fighting again? Forcibly suppressing his pounding heart, Noma glared at the room.

Noma's mother came out of the room, chased out, muttering—
"It's always fists! Fists! What more could happen than being beaten to death by fists? I haven't done anything wrong—"
"What did you say, you bitch (이년)? I told you to come early today, but you defied me once and came even later, and still have so much to say—, you lightning-struck bitch (베락 마질 년)."
"Even if I'm struck by lightning or worse, I don't have a speck of fault—You take out your frustrations on me after being oppressed elsewhere... Am I supposed to just die?"
"You bitch, who do you think you're talking back to? You bitch deserving a thousand cuts (천참만육 할 년)."
"Why, what's wrong with saying something? Heavenly King, Earthly King, Human King, Household Gods (神主竈王 木德王)? Always calling me this bitch, that bitch..." <64>

"Alright, you bitch, say it one more time. I'll give you lightning with my fist... Still talking back, you bitch?"
"What more could happen than death?"
Noma's mother clenched her teeth, washing dishes in the kitchen.
Noma's father grabbed a wooden pillow, burst out the door, faced the kitchen door, threw the pillow, and yelled.
"Go on, die, you bitch deserving a thousand cuts!"
The pillow hit a tin bucket near the door, making a loud clang—a sound that seemed to pierce the bones in this frightening night, causing a violent tremor (激動).

"I need water to cook rice."
With these words, Noma's mother put an end to the fight and said no more.
Noma's father also went back into the room, just muttering—
"Eh! Bitch deserving a thousand cuts."
Noma stood glaring at his father, his eyes lit with red, fiery blood. Like an angry tiger facing its enemy (敵), holding its breath and preparing its body to fight, he stood firmly planted on the ground, looking up resentfully at his father in the room.

Like the lull before a storm, silence and stillness followed each other, and the surroundings grew deeper into darkness.
After a while, breaking this terrifying stillness, Noma called out in a soft voice,
"Mother! Mother!"
"Uh-huh." <65>
Her voice trembled, choked with anger and sorrow (슯흠).
"There's no water? Shall I fetch (기러오지) some?"
"Leave it, I'll tell your sister-in-law to fetch it!"
"I'll fetch it—"
Noma's mother saw Noma entering the kitchen with a smile, and amidst her anger and sorrow, she felt a deep joy (깃븜) welling up and mingling like blood.
Since getting married, Noma's mother had never heard a single affectionate word from her gruff husband, nor had she been able to speak one. She had poured all her love onto her two sons and lived on. But after her older son married, that love too was snatched away by her daughter-in-law. Thus, love naturally turned into resentment, and resentment, in turn, sparked fights, leading to endless secret struggles between mother-in-law and daughter-in-law. Living as if a knife were stuck in her chest, she felt she would have long departed this world if it weren't for Noma, who, whenever things got like this, would act like a child, calling "Mother! Mother!" and comforting her.

"Leave it be and just call your sister-in-law."
Noma's mother was determined to make her late-arriving daughter-in-law do the chore.
"Mother! Can't I fetch it?"
"It's not that you can't... but how can you fetch it in this dark night?"
"Don't worry. I'll fetch it—."
Just then, Noma's sister-in-law entered the yard.
Noma's mother picked up the water bucket and threw it down in the yard like lightning, shouting at her daughter-in-law.
"Hurry up and go fetch water! What were you doing coming only now?"
The baby, startled, kept crying.
"I was late because I was nursing the baby." <66>
The daughter-in-law answered respectfully, but her eyes were full of venom (독긔).
"Giving birth to one useless girl... Just leave that thing whether it dies or not, and hurry up and fetch the water."
The mother-in-law became even more vicious.
Noma's brother took the daughter from his wife, saying,
"Why! Isn't a girl a person too—. Girls and boys are all the same—."
He looked at his mother and smiled.
But the mother did not cease her venomous words, clenching her teeth and shouting—
"You damned brat, what kind of person are you, always taking your wife's side? Huh, you brat."
"It's not taking sides, it's just that girls and boys are the same... Really, Mother... Stop it and just hurry up and put the rice on. I'll tend the fire—"
Noma's brother smiled again and went into the kitchen.

When the first cock crowed, they sat around the porridge bowls, laughing and talking amongst themselves.

Four

The night passed, the sun rose again, and the terrifying murderous aura (살긔) of the past night vanished. The songs of cuckoos crying from this mountain and that evoked gentle human feelings. In a sloping field (밧고랑) halfway up the high Kosalmi peak, three people dressed in white were planting cabbage and radish (무수) seeds.

"When you leave this time, when will you come back again? You won't be able to come even when the cabbage and radish we're planting now grow big enough to harvest, right?
Not even then."
As the woman's trembling voice ended, a man's choked voice was heard—
"I'll come back before then, before then—." <67>
Noma's child-like voice was heard—
"Sŏng! Sŏng! You'll definitely come back then, right—?"
The three voices rose and fell, following one another—

"Last summer, when we planted the radishes, you said you'd come before the autumn kimjang season, but you only came this spring..."
"No matter how much I wanted to come, things have to work out!"
"Because you (이편) planted them, even during that drought last summer, the young master (도련님 - Noma) and I carried water every single day and grew the radishes plumply. And then, time after time, I just waited for you to come, but you never showed up..."
"Sŏng! Sŏng! Remember when sister-in-law (아주머니) and I carried water and grew the radishes? Back then, sister-in-law kept asking me like this—. Seeing how only the radishes Sŏng planted grew plumply like rising clouds even in that drought, she said Sŏng must be working energetically without any trouble and be healthy. And sometimes, around sunset, she would gaze far away, sigh, and just cry. Still, Sŏng didn't come. This time, please come back early, okay! Sŏng."
"Yes! I'll definitely come."
"It's true—. When I saw the radishes growing plumply, I thought you must be like that too—. And if I saw even one radish eaten by bugs (버러지) or looking diseased (뜨물이 인걸), I worried if you might have gotten hurt while working—. So I killed all those bugs and took even better care of the bug-eaten radishes than the others. Then, seeing them grow even better than the others, I thought you must have become like that too—"
"Sŏng! Back then, sister-in-law wouldn't let me pick even one radish leaf (닙사구), she'd scold me—. And if Mother picked one, she couldn't say anything, so she'd just glare sideways like this—. Look."
"Hahaha," the three laughed together.
"But one day, Father said someone came to buy the radishes, so let's sell them all. That was the most <68> bewildering moment I've ever experienced. So after a while, I asked him not to sell just one row, to leave it. Then Mother asked, 'We have enough for us to eat in the other field, what are you going to use those good ones for?' not knowing my feelings. I was saving them to give to you, but somehow the words wouldn't come out in front of Father, so I couldn't say anything and just stood there blushing—."
"Sŏng! I was beside her then, and I teased Mother like this. Gosh! Mother... Save them and give them to Sŏng when he comes—. And then when I looked at sister-in-law, her cheeks were bright red—. Sister-in-law! You were embarrassed then, right?!"
"When Father heard the young master's words, he said, 'Alright,' and looked at me, I was so embarrassed... On top of that, Mother kept staring at me strangely..."
"It's alright even if I don't eat them. It's disappointing if the person who planted them can't eat them, but... even if I come back late this time, you don't need to do that again—."
"Sounds like you might be late again this time—."
"No. I mean, just in case I happen to be late."
"Sŏng! Sŏng! Definitely come back early. Sister-in-law and I will grow them even better than last year—"
"Okay, if I can possibly come, I definitely will."
"Hey. Are you going to Japan again this time?"
"I have to go there again. Still, there are more places to work there than here."
"Don't go there."
"Why?"
"What if there's an earthquake like that time, and those Japs (왜놈들) stab Koreans (조선사람) to death again? Why go to a place like that?"
Hearing this, Noma let out a hollow laugh.—
"Eh! Just stab those bastards right back! When I grow up, I'll travel around places like that too. And if I encounter <69> such times, I'll just deal with those bastards however I want—."
"Are all the people there like that? Some people treat us very kindly—.
For starters, the house where I stayed treated us with great sympathy—. So there's no need for such worries. Even if something like that happens again, a person dies once, not twice. Whenever you die, you die once. Just face it together, try fighting back, live if you live, die if you die. What's the point of worrying!"
The three people, speaking in rather excited tones, were finishing their work.
"Sŏng! Shall we go home now?"
"Uh-huh, let's go."
As they descended the mountain towards home, they started talking again.—
"Last year, you went down that road visible far away, right?"
Noma's sister-in-law looked at her husband and pointed to the southern edge of the wide plain spread out before them. A long, long road snaked, drawing curves on the plain, stretching out towards the horizon (디평선).
"How do you know that?"
"After you left, I came to this field and watched only you walking away until you disappeared from sight. That's how I know."
"..."
"Hey, this time, leave without me knowing, okay—. If I don't see you when you leave, maybe my heart will hurt a little less than if I watch?"
"If you watch me leave, your heart hurts right then, and tears come after a while, but after that, it's okay. But if I leave without you seeing me, it might hurt less at that moment, but from then on, that deeper aching heart never goes away until we meet again. I don't know about others, but that's how it is for me!"
"Then what should we do!"
The words stopped there, and tears welled up in their eyes, rolling and falling onto the ground. <70>
The sound of the cuckoo evoked mournful feelings, ringing in their ears as it passed.

Five

Noma's brother made various arrangements to enroll Noma in school and then left.
Noma would study, return home, and together with his sister-in-law, go to the field halfway up the mountain to tend the radishes and cabbages, waiting for Sŏng's return. Even with hungry stomachs filled with cold water, they did not pull up and eat these radishes.
However, even when the radishes and cabbages were fully grown, Noma's brother, who had planted them, did not return. After some time, the person who bought them came and pulled up the radishes and cabbages, leaving only one row behind.
Even after that, whenever Noma and his sister-in-law came to work in this field, after finishing their work, they would climb to the high peak of Kosalmi and gaze towards the distant horizon, towards some unseen world (세게), longing (憧憬), and speak to each other like this.
"Why isn't Sŏng coming? If I weren't attending school, I would go looking for Sŏng..."
"If only the young master could go, I would go searching right now—."
And they would soothe the child, saying,
"Baby! Baby! You miss your Daddy, don't you?"
And they would just gaze far away towards the horizon.

**Summary**

This fictional story, "People Who Dig the Earth to Eat (Part 2)," depicts the hardships of a Korean tenant farming family in 1925. It follows a young boy, Noma, and his older brother, Sŏng, as they face poverty, exploitative land tenancy practices by a "company," and the forced emigration of Sŏng to Japan for work. The narrative highlights the emotional toll of separation, the family's connection to the land despite their struggles, tensions within the family arising from poverty, and Noma's awakening sense of injustice when confronting the company representative. The story ends with Sŏng gone and Noma and his sister-in-law waiting longingly for his return, symbolizing the uncertain future of many Koreans under colonial rule.

From a historical perspective, the story is significant for its portrayal of rural Korean life under Japanese colonialism. It vividly illustrates the economic pressures forcing Koreans into tenancy and migration to Japan, the power imbalance between Korean farmers and landowning companies (potentially Japanese or collaborators), and references the trauma of anti-Korean violence in Japan (likely the Kantō Massacre). Noma's naive yet pointed questioning of the company agent's capitalist logic also subtly critiques the imposed economic system. Historians might find the depiction of daily life, family dynamics under stress, the specific mention of "내지" (Japanese mainland) and the aftermath of events like the Kantō earthquake valuable for understanding the social and economic conditions of the period.

고살미 놉흔 봉으로부터 꿈틀거리고 뻐더 나아간 두 갈래의 산맥-청룡과 백호로 좌우로 갈라저서 한바탕 반원을 그리여 노코도 다시 접건하야저 가지고 사르르가라 안저서 압헤다 평야를 이루어 노앗고, 산골작이에서 스미여 나오는 물은 땅바닥을 아지리 씨츠며 땅기름을 몰고서 한 줄기 두 줄기 한 개울 두 개울이 서로 합하야 내를 이루어 곡선을 그리며 압흐로 흘너가는데 이 물가에 서서 그 우에다 곡선을 이리저리치며 방천을 하느라고 활동(活動)하는 놈아는 잠간 쉬고 잇는 자긔의 형을 바라다보며 「성! 성!」하고 불넛다.
놈아의 형은 입에 물엇든 곰방대를 빼며 닐어나자,
「왜 그라냐! 어듸를 다쳣냐?」하고 놀래는 빗을 띄우고 놈아를 바라다 본다.
「아니여, 아니여! 다치지 안엇서-.」＜49＞
넘우나 급하게 나온 말소리에 놀랜 형의 가슴을 가라안쳐 노코, 놈아는 다시
「저것봐! 저것봐!」하며 건너편 논두덕을 가르첫다.
검으스레한 흙 우로 파릇파릇하게 터저 나오는 풀닙의 새싹이 총총하게 깔리여 잇는 그 언덕에는 빨가란 할미꼿이 맑은 아츰의 이슬에다 미소하는 시선(視線)을 반사(反射)식히며 떠올나 오는 태양빗에 싸이여 피여 올나온다.
「할미꼿이 피엿서- 성!」 하고 놈아는 연해연방 언덕을 가르첫다.
놈아의 형은 빨가란 꼿을 차자보랴고 커다란 검은 눈을 휘굴리여 언덕을 바라보고 섯다.
「어듸 말이냐? 보이지 안는데-」
「아갸나! 우북이 소사오른 퍼런 쑥엽헤 빨간이 보이지 안어-?」
「그래! 그래! 인제 보앗다. 웃논 언덕에 말이지?」
「응, 우ㅅ논 언덕에 -. 내 가서 캐올게.」 놈아는 말이 끗나자 일하든 괭이를 그대로 가지고, 툼부덩거리며 물 우로 뛰여가서 할미꼿을 캐고 쑥은 뜨더 가지고 도라 오자, 또 다시 형을 부른다.
「성, 성! 꼿은 꼭하나 밧게 업서-. 쑥은 이러케 만흔데도, 이 꼿 참 곱지?」
「아 참 곱구나!」
놈아의 형은 일을 시작하다가, 빨간 화판(花瓣) 속에서 누런 화분(花粉)이 곱게 날으는 꼿을 보고, 못 보든 꼿이나 보는 듯이
「아 참 곱구나」를 또 차지며 자세히 보고 잇다.
「냄새를 맛하보면 향기가 엇더케 신지 코안을 바늘로 자긋 자긋 찌르는 것 갓허! 자- 성 좀 맛하봐」
놈아는 꼿을 형의 코에다 밧삭 댓다. 날카로운 황금가루가튼 화분이 숨쉬는  바람에 날라서 코안으로 드러가자,
「참 냄새도 조타!」하고 놈아의 형은 꼿을 흔들엇다. 향냄새는 더욱 진동한다. ＜50＞
「이 쑥하고 맛하보면 더 조와-.」
놈아는 쑥을 갓다댓다.
「더욱 냄새가 조치? 성.」
「응.」
「이 꼿을랑 갓다가 심어노코 잘키워감서 봐. 아니 - 성.」
「응. 갓다가 잘키우자.」
놈아는 꼿과 쑥을 언덕에다 노코 나서 일을 시작하얏다.
해 뜨기 전부터 봄이 왓다고 이산 저산에서 노래 부르든 포곡새는 아즉도 흥에 겨워서
「포곡, 포곡」 하며, 실바람을 흔들어 노코, 그 노래가락은 다시 실바람과 얼키여저서 이 두사람의 귀를 싸고서 돈다.
속살거리며 흘너가든 물소리. 이곳 저곳에서 논가는 사람의 소리. 은은히 동네에서 들니여 오는 닭우는 소리. 이 모든 소리가 실바람과 서로 얼키여저서 놈아의 귀를 가늘게 친다.
그러나 이 모든 소리는 들리지 안코, 다만 땅을 뚤고 커 올라오는 누르고 푸른 맹아(萌芽)로부터 파릇파릇하게 새싹이 터저 나오는 나무가지로부터, 파동(波動)을 치는 가늘고 힘잇는 날카로운 그 무슨 소리가 끈일새 업시 신경을 쑤시고 가는 듯하야 놈아는 아지 못할 충동(衝動)에 생명이 날뛴다.
지내간 가을의 서리ㅅ바람에 시드러지고 말라 떠러지고, 누러케 병이 들엇든 풀과 나무가 춥고 무서운 음울한 겨울을 거처서, 지금은 씩씩하게도 새싹이 트고 꼿이 피여 끈일새 업시 작구만 생장하야가는 이 광경이 처음으로 보는 것은 아니나 이상하게도 오날은 놈아에게 큰 충동을 주엇다.
놈아는 일을 멈추고 풀하나를 뽑아 보앗다.
힘도 아니들게 뽑히여지고 코숨에도 흔들거리는 이전에 보든 봐와 다름이 업는 심히 약한 한 개의 풀이다.
놈아는 풀을 이리저리 살펴보다가 손바닥에다 노코서 싹싹 비볏다. 푸른 물과 가티 그 풀은 뭉개저버리고＜52＞ 향긔로운 풀냄새는 신경을 찔너서 쾌감(快感)을 줄뿐이지 아무 이상한 것은 업다.
놈아는 다시 풀밧을 바라보고 나무가지를 치어다 보앗다. 풀과 나무는 가늘고 날카롭고 힘잇는 소리를 놈아의 귀에다 들리여 주며, 움죽움죽 움직이며 커올나 오는 그 무슨 큰힘을 보이여 준다.
「성! 성! 이 풀이 요러케 적은데도 엇더케 강하고 독한지... 그 추위에도 얼어죽지 안코 지금은 봄을 맛나서 굉장하게 커나는데-」
놈아는 풀 하나를 또 뽑아가지고 형을 보이면 경이(驚異)의 표정을 나타낸다.
「그것도 사는 것이라, 살아보것다고 그 추에도 엇더케든지 사라나와서 또 저러케 컷구나!」
놈아의 형의 얼골에는 엄숙한 빗이 떠돈다.
「성! 이 풀과 나무가 그 추에도 사라나온 걸 보면 암만하야도 무슨 이상한 힘이 잇는 갑이지? 그러치, 성?」
「사는 것이란 다 그러치 아느냐. 생각하면 모다 이상하지!」
「성! 가을이 되여서 나무닙이 떠러지면 녹두만한 것이 닙떠러진 자리에 붓허 잇지?」
「그래! 나무가지마다 붓허잇는 눈가튼 것 말이장.」
「응, 그것말이여. 그 속에 무엇이 잇는가하고 작년 가을에 따보앗지. 자시자시 보닝깨 그 속에 깨만한 새싹이 들어잇고 다른 나무에 잇는 놈을 따서 찌저보아도 새싹이 다 드러잇서-. 아마 그것이 봄이 되면 커서 닙사구도 되고 꼿도 되는 갑이지?」
「그래! 그 속에서 엇던 놈은 닙사구도 나오고, 또 엇던 놈은 순도 나오고 하더라. 나도 그 전에 자세히 보닝깨」
「성! 성! 그러면 더 이상 하지 안어? 고  깨알만한 것이 땅속에 나잇스면 춥기나들하지, 빨가버슨 나무가지에서 얼어죽지 안코 엇더케 사라와서는 또 저러케 날고-?」
「그 추운 때라도 해ㅅ빗이 잇스닝깨, 거긔서 힘을 어더서 사라오다가 봄이 오닝깨로 저러케 장성하야지는 갑이다-」＜52＞
「햇빗이 잇기야 잇기만도 그러케 추운 때에야 미지근이나 하리라고. 그런데도 싱싱하게 산 것을 보면 고리케 적은 개꽤알 만한 속에도 무슨 큰 조화가 잇는 것 갓허- 성! 그것이 무슨 조화로 그러케 살고-?」
「...」
놈아의 형은 아무 말업시 놈아를 바라보고 놈아는 풀닙만 자세히 살피고 잇다.
두 사람은 다시 일을 시작하얏다. 한참동안 놈아는 일을 하다가 목이 말라서 촬촬 흘너가는 냇물에다 입을대고 꿀덕꿀덕 드려 마셧다.
땅기름을 녹혀가지고 흘너오는 틉틉한 물의 향긔러운 흙냄새는 놈아의 왼 몸에 스미여 드러가고 드러가서는 생명을 충동이여 날뛰게 한다.
놈아는 물을 흠뻑 마시고 닐어나서 두 팔을 휘휘 둘으며 동네를 치어다 보앗다.
동네 뒤에 소사잇는 고살미봉오리는 좌우에 꿈틀 꿈틀 반원을 그리고 잇는 두 갈래의 산줄기로써 어머니가 아들을 두팔로 안고 젓을 먹이는 듯한 형상으로 동네를 안고 잇다. 동네우 나즈막한 공중에는 아츰밥하는 푸른 연긔가 잠기여 잇고 닭우는 소리가 은은히 들리여 온다. 거미줄 가튼 베일로 가린 듯한 아지랭이가 아른거리는 산에는 초록의 빗이 떠도는데, 동쪽산 머리 우에 황홀이 빗나는 태양은 한참 커올나 오는 땅 우의 모든 생물을 젓먹는 아들을 드려다보고 웃는 아바지의 눈과 가티 나려다 보고 잇다.
땅과 태양에서 이런 늣김을 바드며 놈아는 또 일을 게속하얏다.
두사람은 일에 취하야 아무 말이 업시 망천만한다.
한참 잇다가 놈아의 형은 논두덕에 안즈며 또 말을 시작하얏다.
「놈아야! 놈아야!」
「응」 ＜53＞
놈아는 꺼저드러가는 배를 허리끈으로 졸라매며 대답을 하얏다.
「아츰 먹을 때는 되엿지? 놈아야!」
「지내지나 아니 하얏는가 모르것서.」
「다른 사람들도 모다 간다. 우리도 갈거나?」
「성! 고귀튕이 얼마 안남엇스니, 그곳이나 다하고 가지.」
「참 그러자. 얼마 안남엇스니-.」
놈아는 하든 일을 맛처노코 꼿과 쑥을 가지고 나섯다.
「성, 인제 가지」
「오냐 가자.」
「성, 퍽 배곱흐지. 어제 저녁도 얼마 못먹고...」
「아니다, 나는 괜찬타 마는 너는 퍽 시장할 것이다.」
「앗가는 곱흐더니, 지금은 곱흔 줄 모르겟서-」
때가 지내서 그러치. 어머니가 기두르겟다. 어서 가 보자.
「응... 그런데 성 언제 떠나 갈텨?」
「한 열흘쭘 지낸 뒤에 갈란다.」
「그러면 성하고 가티 일하며 니약이 할 날도 한 열흘밧게는 업네. 그때 가면 언제나 올텨?」
「글세 말이다, 마음이야  곳 오고십드라마는 어듸 마음대로 되드냐.」
「그도 그러치마는 자주와, 아니, 성.」
「...」
「이번에도 대판으로 갈 참이여?」
「대판으로 가게될는지 다른 데로 가게될는지 가봐야 알겟다, 형편에 따라서 이리도 가고 저리도 가고 할 터이닝깨.」 ＜54＞
「대판인가 그곳은 이상한 것도 만코 살기도 자미잇고... 그러타지?」
「이상한 것도 만코 자미잇는 것도 잇기야 잇지 만도, 내가 업스닝깨, 소용잇드냐. 어느 때는 그런 것을 보면 가슴이 미여지도록 화가 나서 못견듸것더라.」
「그래도 그런 곳에 살면 아는 것도 만하지고 세상이 엇던가도 알 수 잇지. 그러치 안어? 이런데 사는 것보다도 참사는 것 가틀 것이여. 그러치 성?」
「그럴듯도 하지만 업는 사람이야 맛찬가지더라.」
이 두 사람은 끈일새 업시 이약이를 하며 집으로 도라왓다.

二
「어매! 어매!」
놈아는 집에 도라오자 문간에서 이러케 어머니를 차잣다.
「놈아냐. 퍽 시장하것구나.」 식검은 끄림이 쳠하와 천정에 드레드레 매달려 잇고, 벽이나 문이 모다 연긔에 끄슬리여 검붉은 빗으로 채색하야 논 듯한 야지막한 정지에서, 붉으스레한 얼골이 나오자, 빨간 입살이 버러지며, 하얀 이새이로 피가티 붉고 뜨거운 어머니의 말소리가 나다라왓다.
「왜 인졔야 오냐?」
「방천하든 것 마치고 오느라고 느젓서. 어매 어매! 이 빨간 꼿 좀 봐, 퍼런 쑥하고.」
「참 곱구나. 어듸서 캣냐.」
「논두덕예서. 자! 맛하 봐. 냄새가 참 조치?」
「참 향내도 조키도 하다.」
「어매! 이쑥을랑 즉 끄려먹고, 꼿을랑 심어 노코 구경해. 아니 -.」 ＜55＞
「그랫스면 조컷다마는 꼿심을 자리가 어듸 잇냐?」
놈아의 어머니는 심을 곳이 업는 줄이야 알지마는 마당을 휘둘너 보앗다. 놈아도 이 말에 눈쌀을 찌푸리며, 이리저리 둘너보앗다. 좁은 마당에는 거름자리가 반이나 넘어 차지하야 버리고, 꼿 심을 자리도 업다.
「어매! 어듸다 심엇스면 조켓서?」
「집안에는 심을 데가 업스니, 네 마음대로 해라.」
놈아는 심을 곳을 생각하며 꼿향내를 맛고 잇다가
「어매! 아버지 어듸 갓서?」하고 무럿다.
「오날이 뫼이라는 날이 아니냐. 그래서 아침도 안 잡수고 가시더라.」
어머니의 말소리는 힘이 업시 나왓다.
「회사에서 뫼이라는 날 말이지?」
「그래!」
이 말이 끗나자, 고함치는 소리가 멀니서 연하야 놈아에게 들렷다.
「어매어매! 저게 무슨 소리여?」
「...」
두 사람은 귀를 기우리고 잇다.

놈아의 형은 여러 가지로 주선하야 놈아를 학교에 입학식혀 노코는 떠나가 버렷다.

그러나 그 무수와 배추가 다 컷슬 때에도 심고 간 놈아의 형은 도라오지 안엇다. 얼마후에는 그 무와 배추도 사간 사람이 뽑아가 버리고 다만 한 두렁 밧게는 남지 안엇다.
그 후에도 놈아와 놈아의 형수는 이 밧헤 일하러 올 때마다, 일을 맛추고는 고살미 놉흔 봉오리 우에 올라서서, 멀니 보이는 디평선 저쪽의 보이지도 안는 어느 세게를 동경(憧憬)하며 이러케 서로 말하얏다.
「성은 왜 이리 아니 오는고? 내가 만일 학교를 아니 댕기는 몸일것 가트면 성을 차자서 가것구만도...」
「만일에 도련님이 가기만 하면 나는 지금이라도 차자가 볼텨-.」
그리고 그들은 어린애를 어루며
「아가! 아가! 아버지 보고 십지?」
하고는 멀니 디평선 저쪽만 바라보앗다
