Publication Date: 1925년 07월 01일
Article Title: 마침내 조선 사람이 자랑이여야 한다 (Finally, Koreans Must Be a Source of Pride)
Author: 권덕규
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Opinion Piece)

Finally, Koreans Must Be a Source of Pride
						Kwon Tŏkkyu
1
I am a Korean. But not a Korean of today, rather a Korean of old. Although the body I inhabit is not in the Chosŏn of old, I am definitely a Korean of old. No matter how much others may call me a Korean of today, not only do I not wish to become a Korean of today, but I am fundamentally a Korean of old. Others might ask, "You were born in the present, and your actions are those of the present world, so what nonsensical talk is this?" However, I am not a Korean of today. Having received the long lineage (bloodline) of the Koreans of old, I was born in the Chosŏn where the Koreans of old lived, and thus the spirit (풍기) I received is the spirit of the Chosŏn of old. The food (飮食) I eat is what the Koreans of old ate; the words I speak are those of the Chosŏn of old. Just as what I eat and wear are what the Koreans of old did, the morals I intend to follow and establish, and all standards, are those of old, not of the present. That is why, when walking down the street, I may want to eat *sŏllŏngguk* with steam rising, but I have no interest from the start in things like "Cheap, cheap, what's better than honey sweeter than Chinkogae candy?" The cry "Softly boiling roasted chestnuts! Mushy and hot roasted chestnuts!" sounds folksy and pleasant to hear, but standing at the corner of a brick house and hearing "*yakiguri*" (Japanese for roasted chestnuts) is truly unpleasant. In this way, I like the old and dislike the present. What is the ugliest sight in the world? Needless to say, I believe there is nothing more detestable than seeing a white-haired old man burning his beard while lighting a rolled cigarette (卷烟) and asking for a *koryŏn* coin. Even those who praise and dance for the present Chosŏn could not possibly like this. Still, the gentleman who says the present is good gets *ilgong* plus *ilgong* for a perfect score (滿滿点). Haha, this is all just jesting. <18>

2
Koreans today have no pride. Let's say one has studied philosophy extensively. Isn't a philosopher who hasn't even delved into the inner feelings of the Korean people a crippled fool of a philosopher? Let's say one is a natural historian (博物學者). So what? What is so great about a scholar who doesn't even know what Korean plants exist and doesn't remember a single plant name in Korean? Furthermore, they might say the *haori* (羽織 - Japanese jacket) is cool and good. But if a madman walks around naked, people would say that fellow is mad. In any case, Koreans today have no pride. If they do have any, it must be something other than Korean pride. Then, what was there to be proud of in the Chosŏn of old? Let's list some of the prides of the old Koreans. Even now, if you go to Ch'wiunjŏng or Hwanghakchŏng, you can see people practicing archery. This too is something the Koreans of old did. While the people of China (支那) still didn't know what helmets or armor were, the people of Ch'ŏnggu (靑邱 - Korea) already wore armor and helmets, slung the *Tan'gung* (檀弓 - birch bow) over their shoulders, and subdued the Chinese. Seeing that we were unharmed even when hit by their arrows while wearing our armor and helmets, they called us *Tongdu Ch'ŏlsin* (銅頭鐵身 - copper heads and iron bodies), *Taegungin* (大弓人 - great archers), and even praised us as benevolent *Inin* (仁人 - humane people). If this counts as pride, it is one. The invention of movable type (活字), a tool for disseminating knowledge widely by facilitating printing for the wide distribution of books (書籍)—who thought of it? Koreans. The time it was put into practical use was around 1230 AD, several hundred years before Koster of Holland (和蘭) or Gutenberg (꾸텐뻬르-흐) of Germany (獨逸). If the contribution of movable type is great, how could its first invention not be a source of pride? That the ironclad ship (鐵甲船 - Turtle Ship) was built and used as early as four centuries ago is already a globally recognized fact. If we take pride in this as a foremost invention, this too is not insignificant. If we enumerate the murals of Koguryŏ, the rain gauge (測雨器) of the Yi Dynasty (李朝), the celadon (磁器) of Koryŏ, the Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵) of Kyŏngju, and, more recently, Yi Tongmu's (李東武) *Sasang Sŏl* (四象說 - Four Constitutional Types Theory) one by one to fill our pride, the list would indeed not be short. But wouldn't it be extraordinary if we had an even greater pride than these? What could it be?

3
Indeed, what is our pride? From a moral perspective, is it pride that someone like Confucius (孔子) of the Zhou (周) dynasty wished to emigrate here? That's an Eastern thing. Or, as a certain Western-educated student said, that although everything Korean is unremarkable <19>, Kŭmgangsan (金剛山 - Diamond Mountains) alone is unparalleled in the world—should we take pride in this? Should we pride ourselves that Master Wŏnhyo (元曉公), the mid-stream progenitor of Buddhist philosophy, emerged here? Should we pride ourselves on possessing the precious and voluminous P'alman Taejanggyŏng (八萬大藏經 - Tripitaka Koreana)? Should we pride ourselves that the miraculous medicine ginseng (人蔘) grows here? These are not devoid of pride, but they are not the pride I wish to speak of here. Since humanity acquired the wondrous tool called letters (글ㅅ字) several millennia ago, there have been not just one or two instances of ugly, messy, and cumbersome scripts. While the Roman alphabet (羅馬字), which currently holds the most power, and Hanja (漢子), which still exerts its remaining influence, stand divided in East and West—one as a meaningless (phonetic) system and the other as a meaning-based (ideographic) system—seeming like ultimate victors, it goes without saying that the meaning-based Hanja was fundamentally not convenient for practical use. Moreover, the phonetic Roman alphabet is also illogical and its sounds are imperfectly represented, failing to fulfill its function in daily use, making it almost a deficient script (不具的 글ㅅ字). Then, is there no script that accords with logic, has no impediment in sound representation, and is so well-structured in form as to be utterly flawless? No, there is. Despised (賤對) by Hanja scholars, the sycophants of the Han slaves (漢奴之走狥), and thus not widely used until today, Ŏnmun (諺文)—namely, Hunminjŏngŭm (訓民正音), created by our own thought—is truly the most advanced, rational script without a single flaw. Its aesthetic beauty of form, the richness of its sounds, its ease of learning, and convenience of use provide, on the one hand, artistic feeling, on the other, educational advantages, and on the other, fulfill practical instincts—lacking none of the requirements demanded of a script. Scholars both domestic and foreign have evaluated it thus: "Chŏngŭm possesses all the sounds and wonders of the universe, so there is nothing it cannot be used to write, and nowhere is it obstructed." (Chŏng Inji - 鄭麟趾); "The vernacular script (諺字) encompasses every possible sound, something unprecedented since the existence of writing." (Yi Sŏngho - 李星湖); "Since the vernacular writing (諺書) emerged, there is no language or sound in the myriad directions it cannot transcribe; this is something only a sage could achieve." (Yi Sugwang - 李晬光); "Though its letters are not numerous, their structure is meticulous, they are convenient to use and easy to learn, capable of depicting all words and sounds in detail; this is something the ancient sages had not conceived of, and is unparalleled under Heaven." (Sin Kyŏngjun - 申景濬); "Ŏnmun is the most modern among the world's two hundred-plus national scripts today, the only alphabetic script in the East, possessing a primary scholarly organization unseen in other national languages; from this perspective, Ŏnmun is truly a product of Chosŏn worthy of global pride." (Kanazawa Shōzaburō - 金澤庄三郞); "In terms of having few strokes yet a precise and regular structure, it is exquisitely made, perhaps unsurpassed among the world's phonetic scripts." <20> (Shiratori Kurakichi - 白鳥庫吉). But look now. Possessing this precious script, a natural gift (天惠) suited to our innate sounds, there is no thought of researching it or using it well. Instead, how many are there who strive to learn from the half-mute who pronounces 'chong' [gun] sound as 'tan' [mimicking Japanese pronunciation difficulties], like awkwardly imitating someone knowledgeable? How many are the types who say Ha-nanim instead of Hanunim, trying to become short Kobos [Japanese?]? Such behavior likely stems from the belief that life's highest ideal lies in merely surviving (pasting up the throat - 목구녁에 풀질하는 대). But is it not a fact that they are thoughtless? This cannot be a source of pride.
O Koreans, who speak of the brilliance of history and desire the glory of life! Compared to others, compared to others' things, nothing was achieved on that day, at that time, without effort. Is it not true that nothing is achieved without toil and exertion? Isn't the person who only sees the fruit of success and only dreams of that a spirit-rotten fool (얼이 석은 머덜이)? Let's talk about Lenin. However, one must remember that countless Lenins preceded Lenin, and Lenin himself achieved what he did by dedicating his mind and energy throughout his life, without wasting a single moment (一刻). And wasn't it also accomplished through opportune timing (運數)? When we encounter an opportunity, we too must have something to present, saying, "We have achieved this." That will be the time when we become a source of pride as a people. Being born in Chosŏn today might feel like a source of regret. However, we have no choice but to cling to this Chosŏn and make it bloom with pride.
<18-21>

**Summary**

Kwon Tŏkkyu argues passionately that Koreans of 1925 lack pride, blindly imitating foreign (Japanese, Western) ways while neglecting their own rich heritage. He contrasts this with the Chosŏn of old, citing historical achievements like early archery prowess, the invention of movable type, the ironclad Turtle Ship, and culminating in the supreme achievement: the Korean alphabet, Hunminjŏngŭm (Hangeul). He extols Hangeul's scientific design, phonetic completeness, and aesthetic beauty, quoting historical figures and even Japanese scholars who recognized its superiority, while lamenting its contemporary neglect and the trend of mimicking Japanese sounds or ways. The author urges Koreans to find pride in their own history and culture, especially Hangeul, and strive to build future achievements worthy of pride, rather than just subsisting or imitating others. From a historical perspective, the article is a potent example of Korean cultural nationalism during the Japanese colonial period, reacting against assimilation policies and perceived cultural inferiority. Historians would find the specific historical claims used to bolster national pride (movable type pre-dating Gutenberg, Turtle Ship, Hangeul's perfection cited even by Japanese scholars) and the sharp critique of contemporary Korean society's perceived lack of cultural confidence particularly revealing about the intellectual and cultural currents of the time.

마침내 조선 사람이 자랑이여야 한다
						권덕규
1
나는 조선사람이다. 조선사람이로되 지금 조선사람이 아니라 녜전 조선사람이다. 이 몸동이가 處한 바는 녜전의 조선이 아니로되 나는 꼭 녜전의 조선사람이다. 남이 아무리 나를 지금의 조선사람이라 하여도 나는 지금 조선사람이 되고도 십지 아니하려니와 본대부터 나는 녜전의 조선사람이다. 남이 또 뭇기를 네가 나기를 지금에 낫고 너의 하는 것이 지금 세상의 하는 짓이니 무슨 열업슨 소리냐 할엿다. 그러나 나는 지금 조선사람은 아니다. 내가 녜전 조선사람의 긴(혈통)을 바다서 녜전 조선사람의 살든 조선에 낫고 그리하야 나의 바든 풍기가 녜전 조선의 풍기요. 나의 먹는 바 飮食이 녜전 조선사람의 먹든 바요 나의 짓거리는 바 말이 녜전 조선의 것이요. 이러케 먹는 바 닙는 바가 녜전 조선사람의 하든 것임과 가티 내가 행하려는 바 세우려는 바 도덕이나 모는 표준이 다 녜전 것이요 지금것은 아니다. 내가 그리하기에 길을 가다가도 김이 설여나는 설렁국은 먹고 십으되 싸구려 싸구료 진고개 사탕보다 단꿀 무엇이 낫구료 하는 것은 애초부터 돌아볼 바가 아니며 설설이 끌헛소 군밤이요 무르고도 덥소 군밤이요 하는 소리는 엇구수하야 듯기가 조흐되 벽돌집 모통이에 가 서서 야끼구리 하는 것은 진정에 듯기 실타. 이와 가티 나는 녜전을 조하하고 지금을 슬혀한다. 천하에 제일 보기 미운 것이 어떤 것이드뇨. 둘말할 것 업시 저 허여케 시인 늙은이가 卷烟 불 때 수염을 태우면서 고련한푼 하는 것보다 더 밉광스러운 것은 업슬줄 안다. 이것은 지금 조선을 아무리 贊頌하고 춤추는 패일지라도 그리조하는 못하리라. 그래도 지금을 조타고 하는 양반은 日公에 日公을 더하야 滿滿点이다. 허허 이것은 다 우순 말이고. ＜18＞
