기사제목	南滿洲行(第一信) (Journey to Southern Manchuria (First Letter))
필자	興京에서 李敦化
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

南滿洲行
						興京에서 李敦化

Thoughts on Crossing the Border - A Night in Musun - Finding Hŭnggyŏng County - Chinese Customs Seen Along the Way - A Big Incident During a Meal - A Written Conversation with a Chinese Youth - Even Barbarian Lands Have Spring - Wang Chao-chün's Ancient Rosy Cheeks - A Forty-Ri Welcome - Tears of Compatriots in a Foreign City - Hŭnggyŏng is the Birthplace of the Independence Group (OO團) - The Past and Present of the Independence Group (OO團) - The Tight Organization of the Chŏngŭibu (正義府) - The Demands of Compatriots in Manchuria

1. Thoughts on Crossing the Border
Invited by the compatriots of Hŭnggyŏng, I set off on a journey to Manchuria. I'll skip the preface and start with the story of crossing the border. It was the morning of May 20th. The train departed from Sinŭiju. As we crossed Andong Station, the thought "So this is a foreign country!" struck me.<105> What makes a foreign country foreign? I paid attention anew. With attention, it somehow looked like a foreign country. First, needing to adjust the clocks, all passengers took out their watches and changed ten o'clock to eleven. Next was the sight of Korean passengers disappearing and Chinese passengers suddenly increasing, policemen in different uniforms walking back and forth through the cars, hearing Chinese spoken among the train conductor's words, and the changing street scenes and house styles outside. This is what makes a foreign country. It's the distinction between your country and my country, created by human artifice. Nature knows no inside or outside. The natural scenery—high mountains, flowing water, cawing crows, chattering magpies, red flowers, and green trees—is no different from Chosŏn. The train car is filled with people from the black-clothed country, while from the white-clothed country, Chosŏn, besides myself, there is only one old man with a topknot. According to the old man, he lives in Tŏgwŏn County, Hamgyŏng Province, his surname is Cho, his given name is Hŭmmyŏng, and he is heading to Harbin. His uncle, who lived in a farming village near Harbin, passed away this spring, and he is on his way to bring back the family. The old man puffs on Chŏngsu tobacco in a long pipe and speaks of policies for saving the world and comforting the people. Filial piety, fraternal duty, loyalty, trustworthiness, benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and wisdom are the core of his governing strategy. Though tiresome to listen to, he was my only companion, so like "weak wine is better than strong tea," our questions and answers went quite well. During this time, the train seemed to be approaching Pongch'ŏn. The beautifully named Ponghwangsŏng passed by in a glance, and the scenic Pon'gyeho passed like a dream. There is one thing I must state clearly here. That is, I had always imagined Manchuria as a boundless plain without a single mountain, but that image was completely wrong. From Andong County to what I think was Sŏkchaja Station or Ojadun Station, we passed 26 or 27 stations. In terms of ri, it's a distance of 600-700 ri, entirely intertwined with mountains and water. The Yodong 700-ri plain, passed down through the ages, hasn't shown even a shadow yet, wherever it might be. Piercing mountains and crossing waters, it's truly no different from the Three Gorges (三防幽峽). The train reached Honha Station. From here, indeed, it's a vast plain. A great plain unseen in Chosŏn. Honha Station is just one stop from Pongch'ŏn Station, and here one changes trains for Musun. As I got off the train, Kim Ŭijong and Ham Insŏk, workers from the Pongch'ŏn Kaebyŏk branch and Chosŏn Ilbo branch office, greeted me warmly. Meeting acquaintances in a distant land thousands of ri away, I was extremely glad. Kim Ŭijong accompanied me to Musun.<106>

二. The Story from Musun to Hŭnggyŏng
From Honha Station to Musun is just six stops. It took about two hours, and it seemed to be around ten at night. At Musun Station, Mr. Chŏn P'yŏngsu, the owner of Yŏngch'angt'ae, came out specially to greet me.
On the 21st, I stayed in Musun. Musun, as everyone knows, is famous throughout the East for its coal. In Musun city, there are about 200 Korean households, all with stable jobs and relatively comfortable livelihoods. That evening, there was a lecture hosted by the local Christian Youth Association. Although it was raining that day, making passage very difficult, the fact that about one person per household attended was enough to understand the brethren's sympathy. I went out to see the coal mining. There are said to be two methods of mining coal. One is like gold mining, digging tunnels and going hundreds or thousands of feet underground. The other is called open-pit mining (露天掘), where the surface soil is removed to extract relatively shallowly buried coal. What I saw was open-pit mining. It was a wide pit, about two chō (町 - approx. 218 meters) long and one chō (町 - approx. 109 meters) wide. The coal mining process looked like stone steps (石階層); coal benches were made layer by layer, narrow gauge tracks were laid on top, and the coal beside them was dug up like earth, loaded onto the narrow gauge cars, and taken up a seemingly distant incline lift (乘降機) on narrow gauge tracks, transferred to an electric railway above, and moved to the coal storage. Next is the situation of the miners. Thousands of Chinese miners, stripped to the waist, attached to the narrow gauge cars, going up and down the incline lift, looked truly dangerous and pitiful. However, their wage is said to be a meager 50-60 chŏn per day. To experience how flawed society is today, or to truly understand what the labor problem is, one must directly encounter such scenes. The grand buildings reaching two, three, four stories high, piercing the clouds in Musun, and the luxury of those capitalists sitting inside, eating delicacies, embracing beautiful women, and enjoying song and dance—when you think about it, what are they but a group exploiting the surplus value poured out from the blood and sweat of those thousands of laborers? Ah, Heaven (蒼天)!
I left Musun. It's the road to Hŭnggyŏng County. Hŭnggyŏng is north of Musun. It was the 22nd.<107> I rode in a Chinese horse cart (中國馬車). Everyone, don't be fooled by the term "horse cart." This wasn't riding a horse cart, but doing horse cart gymnastics. No, it was horse cart labor. My back is sturdy, so I endured it, but if it were the waist of a Western woman, it would surely have broken nine times out of ten. This is a humorous way of saying the horse cart was extremely uncomfortable. Not only the cart, but the driver's behavior was worse than the cart. Because I was wearing distinct Chosŏn clothes and didn't know the language, the mocking tone as they called out "Kkori!" "Kkori!" (a derogatory term for Koreans) in words I couldn't understand was clearly visible. You laugh at me, and I laugh at you, so there's no loss for either side, but what made me somewhat uneasy inside was hearing back home that bandits (馬賊) are rampant in the Manchurian region. Furthermore, Kim Ŭijong, who had accompanied me from Honha to Musun, couldn't come along due to unavoidable circumstances, and my only companion was a white-clothed farmer, originally from Yŏngbyŏn, who had been in Manchuria for ten years but still couldn't communicate well in Chinese. As they say, "There's no one who won't yield if tied up and beaten," so regardless of anxiety or unease, I had to go on. Entering the Hŭnggyŏng region, the mountains become high again. They are almost no different from the mountains and rivers of Chosŏn. Added to that, it was mid-spring; the sound of orioles calling their mates amidst the drooping willows, and the lonely cry of the cuckoo (布*) sent from the distant green mountains—even the heart of a strong man couldn't help but hum a tune of homesickness (患鄕由).

Here are the Chinese customs seen along the way. After traveling all day from Musun, covering only 70 ri, the first night's lodging was at a Chinese inn for horse carts (馬房客主). Upon entering, I looked around for the guest room, but couldn't find any place suitable for people to sleep. My top-knotted compatriot companion said, "Hey, come up here, this is the guest room," which surprised me once again. Let me briefly describe the state of a Chinese house. First, upon entering what serves as the main gate, there is a large courtyard for tying up hundreds of carts and horses. Next, entering through a door that could be called the reception room door, the inner room door, a side door, or any other kind of door, the first thing you see is the kitchen (廚所) for cooking rice and side dishes, exposed in the center.<108> To the left and right of the kitchen area are long bench-like platforms, about the height of a normal chair, stretching out rectangularly, with no doors, just open. On the ceiling, which isn't even plastered, the black soot from the thatched roof (草葺) hangs down like sorghum tassels. To speak of uncleanliness is an understatement. Horse dung from the stables is married to the food in the kitchen, and dust from the courtyard kisses the dust on the sleeping mats. Moreover, the rush mats are in ten or twenty pieces; even "a hundred patches hanging like quail feathers" (懸鶉百結) is no exaggeration. Why is it so chaotic? The noise of several dozen cart drivers shouting "Hao bu hao de!" (好不好地 - Good or not good!) and the loud chatter of the servant boys (使喚軍) truly resemble the scene of thousands of soldiers charging the enemy.
The meal was served. Sitting with the drivers, though hungry, I reluctantly picked at the unappetizing food a couple of times when a thunderous scolding erupted from among the drivers. Not knowing the language, I quietly observed to see what was happening. It turned out they were making a fuss because my table manners were wrong again. In Chosŏn custom, we eat alone at separate tables (獨床), so I had the habit of putting a piece of kimchi I was eating back on the table, and this habit emerged unconsciously. From the opposite table, another thunderous shout arose. This was for no other reason than my top-knotted companion, who was traveling with his 5-6 year old daughter, had ordered only one meal due to lack of travel funds. He was caught by a servant while giving his daughter a spoonful of rice, leading to a huge commotion. In Chinese inns, giving one's own food to another is considered a loss to the owner and is strictly forbidden, just like the rule in prison preventing inmates from sharing food. Ah, are they humans or beasts? And they call themselves the lords of creation! No, they don't even have the self-awareness of being lords of creation. It's just money. Money, money, money, money is all that matters.
It was the next day. From the horse cart, I saw the body of a child, perhaps four or five years old, lying on the road, half severed. The drivers chattered among themselves as if they had seen a good spectacle. They laughed and talked. I learned this was a wicked custom resulting from Chinese bad habits. The custom among Manchurian Chinese is that when a person dies, they are placed in a coffin and left as is at the foot of a mountain or sometimes in a field. An even more unforgivable, major bad custom is that when a child under seven dies, they are wrapped in a straw mat and hung from a tree. Then, creatures like eagles (솔감 - likely 'solgae' 솔개, eagle/kite), finding food, grab the corpse and fly off,<109> but if it's too heavy and dropped to the ground, a pack of dogs rushes in, tearing and fighting over it, claiming "now it's my turn." The child's corpse I saw now was said to be a result of this. Ah, human superstition is so cruel.

Five horse carts traveled together in a line. Almost everyone was Chinese. "A single red dot in ten thousand shades of green" (萬綠*中一點紅 - likely 萬綠叢中一點紅), among them, a Chinese youth caught my eye. He looked like a young man studying in Beijing or abroad. Since we were traveling together for 3-4 days, although we couldn't speak the same language, we both felt a mutual understanding (靈犀) and wished we could have a proper conversation, but that was impossible. One day, while crossing a large mountain pass, we had to walk for several tens of ri, and we began a written conversation (筆談). Here is the gist of the conversation, only the youth's words: He asked, "How are Japan's internal affairs recently?" Next, he asked, "It seems Chang Tso-lin (張作霖) made a secret treaty with Japan during the Fengtian-Zhili War (奉直戰爭). Might you know the details?" He also asked political questions like, "What do you think the future world situation will be?" This was enough to understand the youth's inclinations. Eventually, I learned that the young man was a patriotic young Chinese officer named Ch'oe Ch'unwŏn (崔春園). Mr. Ch'oe treated me to lunch that day and asked various questions about the OO ideology (OO사상 - likely Independence ideology) of Koreans and the situation of Ch'ŏndogyo (天道敎) in Chosŏn.
A brief story about dropping into an elementary school. One day during lunchtime, the house next door happened to be an elementary school, so I went to have a look. There seemed to be about a hundred students. As I entered the gate, lovely and cute boys unhesitatingly clung to my sleeve, asking me via written notes (筆談) to please take a good look around the school. In that moment, I forgot the feeling of being in a foreign country. Children are sacred. There are no borders for children. Their smiling faces, their running and playing, grabbing my wrist wanting to talk to me before other children—their affectionate gestures were no different from Chosŏn boys. The only differences were language and clothing. As I alternately held the boys' hands and nodded my head, they were delighted and led me by the hand into the classroom. The arrangement of desks, the hanging of the blackboard, etc., were common worldwide,<110> nothing different there. On the wall hung portraits of Confucius and Mencius, and portraits of Kuan Yü (關壯謬 - likely 關壯繆, Guan Yu) and Yüeh Fei (岳武穆). Also, as a lesson (敎訓), the two characters "Neatness" (整潔) were written large and posted, followed by "Do not skip class, Do not be noisy" (勿曠課勿暄譁).

Story of Hŭnggyŏng
After four days, we arrived at a place called Nŭngga (陵街). Hŭnggyŏng town was said to be 40 ri from Nŭngga. There's a reason Nŭngga has the character 'Nŭng' (陵 - tomb) in its name. This place is the birthplace of the Qing Dynasty's Aisin Gioro (愛新覺羅) clan, so tombs were built and palaces erected here. Adjacent to Nŭngga is a place called Nosŏng (老城 - Old Fortress); this is where the Qing ancestors, while tribal chieftains, built a fortress and nurtured grand ambitions. In Chosŏn, tombs (陵) would naturally have pine forests, but seeing pine forests in Manchuria is harder than seeing stars on a cloudy day. Therefore, even at this place called Nŭng, broad-leaved trees (濶葉樹) thick enough to embrace grow densely, making it difficult, as they say, to see a star past ten trees. Looking at the old poem, "In barbarian lands, no flowers or grass; spring comes, but not like spring" (胡地無花草春來不似春), I don't know if this is truly the barbarian land where the beautiful Wang Chao-chün (王昭君) was married off, but if it is, then this poem overly scorns the barbarian land. Although flowers and grass might be relatively scarce, spring is still spring. In the spring light ripening amidst green shade and fragrant trees, the harmonious songs of orioles (鶯) and cuckoos (布穀) celebrating nature stir even the traveling guest on horseback to tilt a cup and soothe his traveler's melancholy (客懹). Alas, "Year after year, the flowers are similar; year after year, the people are different" (年年歲歲花相似 歲歲年年人不同). The great park of Nŭngga may be unchanged through ages, but where can one find the glory of the Aisin Gioro's time? Ah, forget it, stop it. Life is transient (無常). Transience is suffering (苦). Suffering is creation (創造). Humanity does not create the future through pleasure (樂) but pioneers the future through suffering (苦). Wang Chao-chün is a beauty who succeeded through suffering. If Wang Chao-chün had grown old and died as a minor concubine in the Han palace, who in later generations would know her name, or where would be the cause to sympathize with her situation?

*** From here, I felt like I had discovered a new Chosŏn. As we entered the outskirts of Nŭngga, dozens of compatriots greeted me.<111> The brethren residing in Nŭngga and Messrs. Hyŏn Ch'angho, Yi Chong'ŭn, Yi Suyŏng, Kwŏn Kyesu, Sŏnu Pin, Kang Yŏng'u, Cho Unggŏl, and others who deliberately came the long 40-ri distance from Hŭnggyŏng to meet me—their arduous journey made me feel too indebted to bear. After receiving a delicious lunch from the people of Nŭngga, we returned to Hŭnggyŏng city. Receiving the fervent welcome of the Hŭnggyŏng city gentlemen, I couldn't help but feel tears of compatriotic sentiment welling up. Everyone, please listen closely to the story of Hŭnggyŏng; there are many things worth hearing, but due to various sensitivities, I will only record what is possible.

Hŭnggyŏng, looking at the map, is bordered by Musun and Hwanin to the south, Kwanjŏn County (寬甸顯) to the east, and T'onghwa County (通化顯) and Yuha County (柳河縣) to the north. It can truly be called the center of northern Southern Manchuria. Given this geography, Japanese influence hasn't reached here, and consequently, many Koreans have immigrated, with about 4,000 compatriot households reportedly in the Hŭnggyŏng vicinity alone. In Hŭnggyŏng town itself, out of a total of about 4,000 households, our compatriots reside in only about 90 households. The primary occupation for the majority is rice milling (精米業). The rest are small retailers and restaurant owners. Thus, it goes without saying that the vast majority of the 4,000 households are absolute farmers. The farming method largely involves managing paddy fields (水田), and because the land is Chinese territory, almost all are tenant farmers, reportedly suffering considerable tyranny from the Chinese.
Regarding the time of immigration, many came several decades ago, but more than half arrived around 1921 (Taishō 10). In terms of origin (原籍地), due to the geography, P'yŏngan North Province is close, so friends from the North Province seem to constitute the entirety.

Now, I will begin the story of the Independence Group (OO團). Anyone entering Hŭnggyŏng first senses that Hŭnggyŏng is the base <112> of the Independence Group (OO團). The history of the Independence Group (OO團) originated here, and its current activity zone is also centered here. That's why upon entering, one strangely hears titles like "Officer So-and-so" (某士官), "Military Person So-and-so" (某軍人) used as commonly as the title "Chusa" (主事 - clerk/official) in Chosŏn. It's truly a different world. A world unimaginable for compatriots within Chosŏn. Currently, a self-governing system is completely organized, and the administrative orders of the Independence Group (OO團) are said to be thoroughly and systematically implemented.
Regarding the history of the Independence Group (OO團), about 15 years ago, an organization called the Pumin-dan (扶民團 - Support the People Group) was first started here, which is said to be the beginning (嚆矢) of Korean ethnic autonomy. The Pumin-dan transformed into three groups: the Hanjok-hoe (韓族會 - Korean Race Association) led by Yi T'ak, the Tongnip-tan (獨立團 - Independence Group) led by Cho Maengsŏn, and the Ch'ŏngnyŏn-dan (靑年團 - Youth Group) led by An Pyŏngch'an. Later, the Hanjok-hoe was reorganized into the Kunjŏngsŏ (軍政署 - Military Administration Bureau), and then groups like the Kwangbokkun Ch'ongyŏng Kwanghan-dan T'ŭkmu-bu (光復軍總營光韓團特務部 - Restoration Army General Headquarters Kwanghan Group Special Affairs Department) arose. Separately, there was also the Tokpan-bu (督辦部 - Supervisory Office), which served as a branch of the Shanghai Provisional Government (上海假政府). Until about 4 years ago, these various bodies existed separately under names like Kunjŏngsŏ, Kwangbokkun Ch'ongyŏng, Tongnip-tan, T'onggun-bu (統軍府 - Unified Military Command), but due to the changing times, they unified into a single organization called the T'ongŭi-bu (統義部 - Unification Headquarters), continuing many activities. Recently, to further consolidate thoroughly, the Chŏngŭibu (正義府 - Righteous Government) was formed. After becoming the Chŏngŭibu, public sentiment has concentrated there even more, and self-governing administration is said to be implemented completely and swiftly. However, a remaining regret is that a faction called the Ch'amŭibu (參議部 - Advisory Council) has not yet unified, which is said to be very disappointing.

The area governed by the Chŏngŭibu aims to cover all of Southern Manchuria, and currently, the number of households completely under the Chŏngŭibu reaches over 40,000.
The organization of the Chŏngŭibu consists of 8 departments: Administration (行政部), Civil Affairs (民事部), Military Affairs (軍事部), Finance (財務部), Education (學務部), Livelihood (生計部), Propaganda (宣傳部), and Justice (法務部). Each department has one chairperson (委員長) guiding it, and below the chairperson are chief committee members (主任委員) and committee members (委員) who handle the work.
How is local administration handled? For every hundred households, there is a Paekka-jang (百家長 - Head of 100 Households), and for every thousand households, there is a Ch'onggwan (總管 - General Manager), who are connected to the Chŏngŭibu.<113> (Recently, it's said the local level also changed to a committee system). The method for selecting personnel is: the Paekka-jang is recommended by the 100 households, the Ch'onggwan is elected by representatives of that district, and then Chŏngŭibu committee members are elected from separate council election districts, with one councilor elected per district, and the entire committee elected by secret ballot. The chairperson is then elected by mutual vote among the committee members.

The duties owed to the Chŏngŭibu by its people include tax payment (納稅義務) and military service (兵役義務). Taxes are 6 Yuan (淸貸 - Qing currency?) twice a year, spring and autumn. Military service obligation is met by maintaining a roster of volunteers.
For education, it's said that every area with over 100 households must have one school, implementing compulsory education. Industry falls under the Livelihood Department's jurisdiction, and all industrial improvement is guided by the Livelihood Department. Communication is extremely swift; if urgent matters arise, like the old courier system (파발) of the former Korean Empire, messages are relayed from this village to that village, and from that village to the next, allowing news to travel easily even over hundreds of ri.
However, there is said to be one thing that is most detested. That is the tyranny of Chinese soldiers. Originally, the Military Affairs Department of the Chŏngŭibu tries its utmost to win the favor of the Chinese, but those Chinese soldiers are a group who see nothing but money in their eyes and will dare to do anything for money. Thus, Chinese troops frequently attack the Independence Army (OO軍), leading to mutual clashes. The reason the Chinese army attacks the Independence Army (OO軍) is nothing other than the objective of seizing the Independence Army's (OO軍) weapons.
In China, it's not just the actions of the Chinese soldiers; the conduct of Chinese government offices is also said to be the same. If you have money, even a serious criminal can be acquitted and released (無罪放免), but if you have no money, even a temporary detention can turn into months of hardship as a matter of course. A single lawsuit involves 6-7 Yuan for the official scribe (官營代書科), wastes several days, and even requires rent (房貰) for lodging during detention. It's said to be exactly like the misgovernment (弊政) of the former Korean Empire era. No, it's said to be even more shameless.<114>
What about news of Manchuria's famous bandits (馬賊)? While this area isn't a bandit stronghold (巢窟地), bandit appearances are frequent, making it impossible to feel completely safe. On the day I entered Hŭnggyŏng, bandits entered Hŭnggyŏng city and kidnapped three wealthy Chinese. And news today reports that Chinese students were captured somewhere.

Compatriots in Manchuria have earnest requests for their brethren back home (內地). It's nothing else but a plea for capable individuals (有爲의 인사) and capitalists (資本家) to come in large numbers. According to the brethren, until last year, the comings and goings of brethren from the homeland were difficult. The reasons were not only intense factionalism (黨派熱) at the time, but also suspicion of being spies (偵探) or the rampancy of hooligans (不良子), making it truly inconvenient for newcomers to move about. But now, not only has the policy of the Chŏngŭibu changed, but unruly elements (不良分子) have also been swept away, and the vigilance of the Chŏngŭibu is thorough, making things much safer. From now on, they intend to welcome compatriots from the homeland as much as possible, seeking cultural and commercial connections between inside (Korea) and outside (Manchuria). Therefore, the reason they welcomed me so earnestly is due to compatriotic feeling, certainly, but in reality, it stems from a sincere desire to establish the substance of internal-external connection.
The most urgent issue is said to be the lack of personnel in education. Although there are schools in every village (洞內), the shortage of educators makes implementation extremely difficult. (Unfinished)<115>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Yi Tonhwa describes his journey in May 1925 from Korea across the border into Southern Manchuria, heading towards Hŭnggyŏng at the invitation of Korean compatriots. He recounts his impressions of crossing into a "foreign" land, observations of the industrial coal mining in Musun and the harsh conditions of Chinese laborers, the arduous and uncomfortable journey by Chinese horse cart, encounters with Chinese customs he finds unpleasant or cruel (like inn practices and treatment of child corpses), a written conversation with an educated Chinese officer, and the surprisingly familiar nature and beauty of the Manchurian landscape. Upon reaching Hŭnggyŏng, he is warmly welcomed by the Korean community and learns about their significant presence (around 4,000 households in the vicinity) and the highly organized self-governing body, the Chŏngŭibu (Righteous Government), which evolved from earlier independence groups (referred to as OO團). He details the Chŏngŭibu's structure, functions (administration, military, education, etc.), tax and service obligations of the 40,000 households under its jurisdiction, and the challenges faced, including conflicts with Chinese soldiers/officials and banditry. The article ends with the community's plea for more educated individuals and capitalists from Korea to join them, emphasizing the improved safety and desire for connection.

For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the Korean diaspora in Manchuria during the colonial period. It provides a contemporary account of the landscape, travel conditions, Korean perceptions of Chinese customs, and interactions between Koreans and Chinese. Most significantly, it details the existence, structure, and functioning of a large-scale Korean self-governing/independence organization (Chŏngŭibu) operating with considerable autonomy in Manchuria, including its administrative reach, military aspects (implied by OO團/OO軍 and conflicts), education system, and challenges – offering a glimpse into the complex political and social reality of Koreans resisting or navigating colonial rule from abroad. The mention of specific historical groups (Pumin-dan, Hanjok-hoe, Tongnip-tan, Kunjŏngsŏ, T'ongŭi-bu, Ch'amŭibu) and leaders provides concrete data points for research on the independence movement's organization in this region.

南滿洲行
						興京에서 李敦化
國境을 넘는 感想 - 撫順의 하로밤 - 興京縣차자들제 - 沿道에서 본 中國人의 風俗 - 食事中에 大逢變 - 中國靑年과의 筆談 - 胡地도 春似春 - 王昭君의 녯 紅頰 - 四十里出迎 - 異城의 同胞淚 - 興京은 OO團의 發祥地 - OO團의 昔今 - 正義府의 緊密한 組織 - 在滿同胞의 要望條件

1. 國境을 넘는 感想
興京同胞의 부름을 입어, 滿洲族行을 떠낫슴니다. 序文은 고만 둠니다. 國境을 넘든 이약기부터 始하겟슴니다. 5월 20일 앗츰이외다. 車는 新義州에서 떠낫음니다. 安東驛을 건너서자, 「여긔가 외국이로구나!」하는 정신이 돌앗슴니다.＜105＞외국이라니 엇던 것을 외국이라느냐 하고, 새삼스럽게 注意를 하얏음니다. 注意바람에 엇전지 외국가태 보임니다. 첫재 시계를 곳처 노아야 한다 하야, 乘客은 누구나 시계를 빼여들고, 열시를 열한 點으로 고침니다. 다음은 朝鮮人乘客이 업서지고 中國人乘客이 갑작이 만하지는 光景이며, 服裝 다른 巡査가 車間으로 왓다 갓다 하는 것이며, 車掌의 용어 가운데서 中國말을 듯게 되는 것이며, 外界의 市街光景이라든지 家屋制度가 달라지는 것 등입니다. 이것이 외국이라는 것임니다. 사람의 작란으로 나온 네 나라와, 내 나라라는 것입니다. 자연에게는 아모 內外가 업슴니다. 산은 놉고 물은 흘으고 가마귀 울고 깟치 짓고 꼿 붉고 나무 푸른 자연의 光景은, 조곰도 朝鮮과 다른 것이 업슴니다. 車안에는 黑衣國사람이 가득 찰 뿐이오, 白衣國의 朝鮮사람은, 나 외에 상투장이 老人 한 분 뿐입니다. 老人의 말을 드른 則, 사는 곳은 咸鏡道德源郡이고, 姓은 趙요 이름은 欽明이라 하고, 가는 곳은 哈爾賓이라 합니다. 자기의 삼촌되는 이가, 哈爾賓 엇던 농촌에서 살다가, 금년 봄에 作故가 되엿는데, 그 家屬을 더리러 가는 길이라 함니다. 老人은 긴 담배때에 長壽烟을 비비여 피우며, 濟世安民의 策을 이약이함니다. 考悌忠信, 仁義禮智가 그의 治世策의 中心임니다. 듯기 시른 이약이지만은, 그래도 동무라 구는 한 사람뿐인 고로, 薄薄酒도 勝茶湯으로 隨問隨答이 제법 잘 되얏슴니다. 그러는 시간에, 車�는 奉天 갓가히 온 모양임니다. 일음 조흔 鳳凰城도 눈결에 지냇고, 경치 조흔 本溪湖도 꿈속 가티 지냇음니다. 여긔에 한가지 톡톡히 말하야 둘 것이 잇슴니다. 그는 무엇인고 하니, 나는 평소에 滿洲라 하면, 一點山이 업는 無邊大野로만 상상하엿든 것이, 그 상상은 왼통 落題가 되엿음니다. 安東縣서 石家子驛인가 吳家屯驛인가 하는 곳까지가 26, 7個의 停車場을 지냇는데 里數로 말하면 6, 7百里나 되는 먼 距雖가 왼통 산수로 얼키여 잇음니다. 자고로 전하야 오는 遼東 7백里 벌판이란 것은 어느 便에 붓헛는지, 아직까지 그림자도 보히지 안이함니다. 山 뚤코 물 건네고 하는 것이, 완연히 三防幽峽과 다름이 업슴니다. 車는 渾河驛에 다앗슴니다. 여긔서부터는 과연 큰 들입니다. 朝鮮서 보지 못하든 大野입니다. 渾河驛은 奉天停車場에서 겨우 한 停車場 새인데, 이곳서 撫順가는 車를 갈아타게 됨니다. 車에 나리자 반가히 마자주는 이는 奉天開闢支社兼 朝鮮日報支局의 일군인 金義宗, 咸麟石兩君이 엇슴니다. 천리타향에 逢故人, 퍽도 깃벗슴니다. 撫順까지는 金義宗君과 동행이 되엿슴니다.＜106＞

二. 撫順서 興京까지의 이약이

趙雄杰 諸氏 등의 고행은, 너무 미안하야 견딀수 업섯슴니다. 陵街 여러분의 맛잇는 午餐을 어더먹고, 興京 서울로 돌어오게 되얏음니다. 興京市諸氏의 열렬한 마즘을 밧고, 스스로 同胞感의 熱淚가 흐름을 깨닷지 못하엿음니다. 여러분 興京이약이를 좀 자세히 들어보시오, 들어볼만한 일이 만습니다만은, 여러 가지로 끄리는 데가 만하서, 될 수 잇는 것만 기록함니다.

興京이란 곳은, 지도로 보면, 남쪽은 撫順桓仁이오 동쪽은 寬甸顯이오 북쪽은 通化顯과 柳河縣이 접하야 잇는 곳임니다. 실로 南滿洲北部의 중심이라 할 수 잇슴니다. 지세가 이러코 보니, 이곳은 日本人의 세력이 밋지 못하엿스며, 그에 따라 朝鮮人의 이주가 만히 잇서, 興京附近만으로도 약 4000戶의 동포가 잇다 함니다. 興京邑內는 總 戶數 4000여戶가량에 우리™포의 거주자가 겨우 90戶가량인 바, 대부분의 業은 精米業이 일등이오. 其餘는 小賣商과 飮食店임니다. 그러고 보니 4000戶의 대다수가 절대농민인 것은 말할도 *슴니다. 농사하는 방법은 대개가 水田을 경영하고 잇스며, 水田은 中國영토인 관계상, 전부가 소작농이어서, 中國人의 橫暴가 적지 안타함니다.
移住의 연령으로 보면, 수십년전에 들어온 이도 만치만은, 반수이상은 大正 10년 내외이며, 原籍地로 말하면 이곳은 지세상 평안북도가 갓가운 고로 北道親舊가 전부를 점한 모양임니다.

인제는 OO團의 이약이를 시작하겟슴니다. 누구든지 興京에 들어서 먼저 觸感되는 일은, 興京이 OO團의＜112＞ 根據地이란 것입니다. OO團의 역사가 이에서 발원되엿고, 現今의 活動地帶도 또한 이곳이 중심될만하야 잇슴니다. 그래서 들어서는 길로 이상히 들리는 것은, 某士官 某士官하며 某軍人 某軍人이라는 稱呼가, 朝鮮에서 主事라는 稱呼와 가튼 통례로 듯게 됨니다. 참으로 별천지입니다. 朝鮮안에 동포로는, 夢想不到할 天地입니다. 現今은 완전히 자치제도가 조직되야, OO團의 行政命令이 徹底且組織的으로 施行이 된다 합니다.
OO團의 역사가 엇지 되야 나려왓는가 하면, 距今 약 15년전에는 이곳에 처음으로 扶民團이라는 단체가 시작되엿는데, 이것이 韓民族自治의 嚆失이라 함니다. 扶民團이 변하야 세가지 단체로 되얏스니, 李鐸을 領首로 한 韓族會이며, 趙孟善을 領首로 한 獨立團이며, 安秉讚을 領首한 靑年團이 잇게 되엿슴니다. 그 후에 韓族會는 軍政署로 개조되고, 다시 光復軍總營光韓團特務部라는 단체가 일어나게 되엿스며, 又別로 上海假政府의 支部格인 督辦部가 잇게 되엿음니다. 距今 약 4년전까지는 이상의 諸團軆가 軍政署 光復軍總營 獨立團 統軍府라는 名辭로 分立되엿든 것이, 시대의 推移에 因하야 統義部라 하는 一機關으로 통일되야, 만흔 활동을 繼續하야 오다가, 최근에 와서는 또 그를 徹底的으로 통일키 위하야, 正義府가 생겻다 합니다. 正義府가 된 뒤에는 민심이 一層 그리로 집중될 뿐 안이라, 자치적 行政이 완전 且敏速히 施行된다 함니다. 그러나 아즉도 遺感되는 것은, 參議部라는 일부분이 합일되지 못하야, 그로서 대단 섭섭한 일이라 함니다.

正義府에서 자치하는 구역은 南滿洲 전체를 目標로 하는바, 時在 완전히 正義府에 소속된 戶數가 4만여 戶에 달한다 함니다.
正義府의 조직은 行政部 民事部 軍事部 財務部 學務部 生計部 宣傳部 法務部의 8부로 되엿스며 各部에 委員長 一人이 잇서, 그를 指導하고, 委員長 이하에는 主任委員及委員이 잇서 일을 보게 된다 합니다.
地方行政은 엇더한가 하면, 百戶에는 百家長이 잇고 千戶에는 總管이 잇서, 正義府와 聯終이 되어 잇슴니다.＜113＞ (최근에는 지방에도 委員制로 변하엿다 함)모든 것에 人選하는 방법은 百家長은 百家의 推薦으로 되고, 總管은 그 區城의 대표가 選擧하게 되며 그러하야 正義府 委員은 別로히 議員選擧區가 잇서, 一區 一議員이 選出되어, 無記名 投票로 委員전부를 選擧케 되는 제도이며, 그리하야 委員長은 委員들의 互選으로써 된다 함니다.

正義府 소속의 인민들이 正義府에 밧치는 義務는 納稅義務 兵役義務가 잇는바, 納稅는 1년 春秋雨期에 淸貸 6圓이며, 兵役義務는 志願者로써 軍籍을 備置하야 두엇다 합니다.
敎育은 百戶이상에는 반듯이 一校를 두어, 강제교육을 실시한다 하며, 산업은 生計部의 所管인데, 일절 산업향상을 生計部의 指導로 되야간다 함니다. 교통은 심히 敏速하야, 급한 일이 잇고 보면, 이전 舊韓國時代의 파발모양으로, 이 촌에서 저 촌에 전하고 저 촌에서 또 이웃촌에 전달하게 하야, 비록 수백리의 遠距離라도 容昜히 소식을 통하게 된다 함니다.
그런데 제일 질색되는 일이 하나 잇다 합니다. 그는 중국군인의 橫暴라 합니다. 원래 正義府의 軍事部에서는 아모조록 中國人에게 대하야 환심를 사고저 하나, 저들 중국군인이란 것은 도모지 眼中에 돈밧게 업는 무리어서, 돈을 위햐야는 엇더한 일이라도 敢行한다 합니다. 그래서 中國軍이 누누히 OO軍를 습격하야 피차 교전이 잇게 되는바, 저를 中國軍이 OO軍을 습격하는 이유는 아모것도 업고, 오즉 OO軍의 무기를 奪取할 목적이라 함니다.
中國은 중국군인의 일이 그러할 뿐 안이라, 중국관청의 행사가 또한 그러하다 합니다. 돈만 잇스면 중죄인이라도 無罪放免이 될 수 잇고, 돈만 업스면 일시 拘留짜리도 멧달 고생이 예사라 합니다. 한번 訟事에 官營代書科가 6-7圓이 들고, 시간이 幾日이라도 허비가 되며 한번 갓기는데 房貰까지 잇다 합니다. 완연히 舊韓國時代의 弊政과 恰似하다 함니다. 안이, 그 이상으로 沒廉恥라 합니다.＜114＞
