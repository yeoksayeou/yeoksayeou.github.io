기사제목	檢事局待合室 (Prosecutor's Office Waiting Room)
필자	廉想涉
기사형태	소설 (Fiction/Novel)

檢事局待合室
						廉想涉
1

On the sunny side, the damp earth had swollen blackly, giving a soft, spongy feel underfoot as I lifted my steps, but the wind still held a harsh bite, making it a day when one was reluctant to take hands tucked into the sleeves of one's *turumagi*.
I had been sitting on the single bench in the waiting room of the Kyŏngsŏng District Court Prosecutor's Office, but feeling a chill, I came out seeking the sun and stood leaning against the doorway with my arms crossed. Just then, a pale-faced young man, looking about twenty-four or five, rushed back and forth before slipping into a group of five or six loitering men, tipped his hat, and asked politely,
"Where is the reception desk for the prosecutor's office?"
"Go in this way," I gestured towards the waiting room, which was about half a *kan* in size.
However, he only peered curiously behind me and then quickly turned back the way he came, rushing off again. He returned, leading a female student. From her round eyes and full lips, to the freckles on her oval face, it was clear they were siblings.
The woman, finding herself among men, and especially in a place like the prosecutor's office—the mere sound of which made one's heart pound and evoked gloomy feelings—seemed uncomfortable, having been summoned here for the first time in her life. Perhaps overwhelmed by some unknown force, she squinted hard, averted her gaze, and upon entering, deliberately skirted the group of loitering men to stand sideways near the corridor on the far side. Yet, even as she moved to that spot, face downcast and avoiding eye contact, <2> the way she tilted her head halfway towards the men and held it high as she walked struck my eye as oddly defiant.
Meanwhile, the young man went into the waiting room behind me, fumbling to take out the summons, and stood waiting for someone to look out from the reception window. An idle curiosity—perhaps unconsciously cultivated by my profession as a newspaper reporter—arose in me, and I followed him back inside, quickly glancing at the name written on the postcard.
—The name Yi Kyŏngok (李景玉) flickered past the edge of my vision. I caught the name of the summoned woman, but before I could look closely at the address, he flipped the postcard over and placed it on the sill of the small glass window.
...Yi Kyŏngok! Yi Kyŏngok! What sort of woman gets summoned to the prosecutor's office? As a witness for Chŏngnam? The wife of a suspect in the frequent Communist Party or Independence Party cases these days? ...If so, would they have left her alone until the summons was casually placed at the prosecutor's office!
Mulling this over, I stepped back outside the door to observe the woman's appearance again.
Wearing a willow-green skirt and a white cotton poplin *chŏgori*, with a purplish winter shawl draped over her shoulders, her rear view seemed modest. Judging by this, she was past her student days. Furthermore, the fact that she wore slightly worn-looking yellowish high-heeled shoes with mud still caked on the heels suggested she was more likely an elementary school teacher or a kindergarten nursemaid than an ordinary housewife. In any case, her posture—her height, broad shoulders, and ample hips—clearly indicated she had the build of a grown woman, I thought to myself.
...But why was she called here? Looking at the man—wearing a clean white *turumagi*, equally neat, slender yellowish high-heeled shoes, and already sporting a light spring hat though the season had barely turned—one could guess she came from a decent family. Moreover, the fact that her older brother accompanied her, unwilling to let the summoned woman come alone, hinted at a respectable household... Could she have been called as a witness for some trivial reason?...
Having arrived promptly at nine in the morning for a matter I knew nothing about, I'd been shivering here for two hours. To pass the time, I stood there indulging in idle speculation. <3>
The young man, having submitted the summons, came out and called,
"Hey, go wait inside there."
The female student, clutching the large shawl as if gathering the folds of a *changot*, tucked her hands inside, lowered her head, and passed by me into the waiting room. I paused, stepped aside, and then moved closer to the door again to get another look at her demeanor. Once inside, the woman took off her shawl, folded it into quarters, draped it over one arm, and stood sideways in a corner, head lowered. Whichever way I looked at her, I decided inwardly that she certainly wasn't the type to be involved as a reference or witness in some sordid affair between a man and a woman. Having drawn this line, I felt reassured, as if there was no need for further speculation.
The chill I had momentarily forgotten crept back, seeping under my armpits. The young man who had been standing beside me was now talking to another man who looked rough, like some laundry delivery agent.
"...Yes, I've seen you often when I go to your house to pick up orders. But why are you here?" the rough man asked with a grin.
"Well, I don't know either. But how long are they going to keep us standing here like this?" the young man replied, stamping his feet as if they were cold.
"If they call you before lunch, that's considered fast. Who's the presiding prosecutor?"
"It says M on the summons. Are there Korean prosecutors too?"
"Well..."
My ears perked up at the mention of M. I was actually here because the editor and publisher of the S Newspaper had been summoned, but circumstances forced me to come in his stead. I had informed the prosecutor's office that a representative had arrived and would wait if the matter concerned the newspaper, but would leave otherwise. They had told me to wait, and so I had been stuck here, unable to come or go.
Noon approached, yet there was no news. In the front yard of the prosecutor's office, overshadowed by the dark, oppressive records depository, <4> the sunlight had already faded. The narrow space grew increasingly crowded, but the atmosphere remained gloomy, like late afternoon. Whenever the silent, chilly wind seeped in, everyone would glance towards the waiting room, but no one seemed inclined to enter readily. It was because of the shy woman—the female student with braided hair and yellow shoes—standing demurely turned away.
My legs now stiff, I couldn't bear it any longer. After hesitating for a while, I strode in. On the wooden bench, large enough for about three people, the woman sat facing away in one corner, and another man sat at the far end. I plopped down in the middle, closer to the man. Of course, there were about two hand spans between me and the woman. Nevertheless, she quickly stood up and turned stiffly towards the outside. I felt apologetic. But then,
...Has this girl never even ridden a streetcar in her life? The thought struck me as peculiar, and she seemed overly sensitive. A little later, the man sitting at the end also got up and left abruptly. Was having the small bench to myself a minor victory? However, I was now left alone with the woman in the narrow waiting room. I considered leaving too, but instead shifted to the far end, rested my chin on my hand, and closed my eyes. The woman seemed to glance my way, then I heard the rustle of her sitting down again softly.
...Why is she being so fussy? —I thought, while also acknowledging that such shyness did seem demure. Waiting for nearly four hours was utterly pointless. I considered going out to call the office (社) and asking someone else to come wait in my place, but hesitated, worried they might call me while I was gone. As I sat there indecisively, the sound of footsteps approached from outside, crunching on the ground.
"...This is about the famous Governor-General assassination plot—the Yun Ch'iho incident... The main building was too small back then, so this was built hastily in eleven days and xx hours..." a voice explained in Japanese.
I jumped up and went out to look. It seemed some people were being given a tour.
...Yun Ch'iho incident! Yun Ch'iho incident! I glanced up at the flat roof of the building I had just been sitting under. In the gloomy corridor, a light whirlwind swirled gently, gathering in a corner, then scattering discarded scraps of paper <5> before vanishing, leaving stillness all around.
...Yun Ch'iho incident! Yun Ch'iho incident! How many Yun Ch'ihos have we faced since the first Yun Ch'iho? I recalled An Myŏnggŭn, or someone like that, a middle-aged man who had reportedly seen the world again last spring after more than ten years due to this incident, coming to the office as a representative. I remembered comforting the dazed man while taking his photograph. ...This is what courthouses are like: summoning people at nine and making them wait five, six hours. Before this absolute power (絶代), even time (時間) hides its existence. Before King Yŏmna, eternity and an instant might weigh the same; what significance could a span of ten-odd years hold for those who control human destiny? Thus, for these esteemed individuals who hold the power of life and death, robbing a person of a day's life—a precious fragment of their existence—is perhaps still a lenient measure.
As I stood there, lost in these rambling thoughts, Y and K from D Company arrived, looking glum.
"Hey—what's this all about?" they greeted me, then looked past me.
"Mr. X! You got dragged here too? Are we having a reporters' convention here?" Y chuckled heartily. I turned and saw Y from D Company.
"What's going on? Did they call everyone here? Are we all involved in the same case?" I asked. "Who knows—" they replied, saying they were called as witnesses and sent in their business cards.
During this exchange, the man with the round eyes, standing in an L-shape with T, stared intently at the four of us, his eyes wider than ever.
"But look here, old chap. There's one behind you too. Does spring come even to a place like this!"
K must have seen the woman peering out from behind me, as he chuckled and said this. Feeling embarrassed, I tried to change the subject.
"Who called you guys?"
"A prosecutor?" K asked. <6>
"Five hours? But you'll probably have to wait longer! You brought the summons, right?" K inquired, his tone implying that since they were called by phone and sent in their cards, they might receive different treatment.
"Well, whether we wait five hours or six, what does it matter? We'll get a good show, maybe find material for a novel..." K added, glancing again at the woman and smiling.
"A good show?"
"Just turn around and look behind you," Y whispered mischievously. The woman seemed to be peering out curiously too, but I pretended not to notice.
"You can have my share of such 'good shows'—just stand here rooted for five hours," I retorted, forcing a laugh.

二

A little later, an office boy called Y and K inside.
However, if I had to wait until after Y and K finished with the same prosecutor, who knew when my turn would come? It was infuriating that they, who arrived later, were called in first. Even when going into a restaurant for a bowl of noodles, it's common courtesy in this world to serve the person who entered first, even by a step. But it seemed the courthouse was a land where the sun rises in the west (西), and prosecutors were the sort who viewed the world from under their legs. Resigning myself to keeping my mouth shut as the best policy, I waited. Then, they came out again, saying they were told to wait a bit longer. About ten minutes later, the three reporters from the three newspapers were called in together. Even if King Yŏmna himself were summoning me, since I was being called anyway, getting there even one step ahead was the smart move, so I followed closely behind the office boy.
"You're not the person himself, are you? Then file a report stating the person is unavailable," the prosecutor said, handing back the summons.
I had already fumed silently about how courthouse people lacked even the basic courtesy of a *changgukbap* peddler. But really, how many hundreds of times must one read the Six Codes of Law to become so miraculously adept? I glared up at Prosecutor M, who seemed puffed up with self-importance, <7> thinking,
"Look here, what did I tell you at the beginning? I explained who I was and said I'd wait if there was something to discuss, otherwise I'd leave. And after making me wait half a day during this busy time, this is all you have to say? I'm not trying to argue, but isn't it infuriating to have been kept waiting all this time?"
I felt like letting him have it, like popping a blister... but then thought better of it. "No, arguing about such clear matters would be more effectively done later with a *sŏllŏngt'ang* restaurant server." Deciding to make a quick exit, I turned to leave, overhearing the prosecutor instructing T from D Company to come back at three in the afternoon as he was going into a public trial now. I didn't know what happened to Y and K, but K, seeing me come out, asked,
"What happened? Are you leaving alone?"
"No," I replied, "Enjoy the show," and hurried away.
Ever since then, whenever I see a *changgukbap* peddler, the question arises: why didn't that person learn the Six Codes of Law? I still haven't solved that mystery.

三

Back at the office, when I mentioned that reporters from three newspapers had all been summoned by the same Prosecutor M, C from the city desk remarked,
"Ah, then it must be the Yi Kyŏngok case!"
My ears perked up again.
"The Yi Kyŏngok case?"
Around that time, distracted by other matters, I hadn't been paying close attention to the daily newspaper editing, just skimming the headlines. So, I had no idea who Yi Kyŏngok was or what the Yi Kyŏngok case entailed. However, remembering that the name on the summons the young man showed at the prosecutor's office reception <8> was Yi Kyŏngok, and convinced that the woman herself was the owner of that name, I felt it was news to me. At the same time, if it turned out I had unknowingly gone to the prosecutor's office because of this Yi Kyŏngok woman—even though I went reluctantly as a substitute for the publisher—it would reflect poorly on me. Moreover, realizing I had been unaware of a scandal within the company until now felt like negligence on my part, making me feel awkward.
"What case is it? You mean she filed a complaint? If something like that happened, you should have told me sooner," I asked, deflecting my own laxity and instead sounding slightly reproachful.
"I didn't know it would go as far as a formal complaint, but why don't you know? About the story of the clandestine prostitute that caused such a stir, like a 'romance'!"
I was startled for the second time at the mention of "clandestine prostitute."
"Well, I handle the main legal matters, but how would I remember every Kim Kyŏngok or Yi Kyŏngok?" I said, still trying not to reveal my inattention to my own newspaper's articles.
"I don't recall the details clearly. So, what happened?" I inquired.
"She's a terrible woman. Apparently, she attends K Academy or somewhere like that, and with a policeman backing her up, she lures any guy who looks like he has money, promising marriage, brings him to her house, fleeces him, and then kicks him out. That's her specialty. She's expected to be arrested by the Ponjŏng Police Station today or tomorrow," said D, the reporter who wrote the story, laughing as if delighted.
"Arrested? But I just saw her at the prosecutor's office. She didn't seem like that kind of woman..." I replied, smiling absently.
"What? You saw her? What did she look like? Was she very demure then?" C leaned forward eagerly, his face brightening as if at some amusing news.
"She looked slender, but her clothes and shy demeanor didn't seem so depraved. Seeing her accompanied by that respectable-looking man, who seemed like her older brother, they looked like children from a decent family," I said, looking at C with wide eyes, as if <10> unable to readily believe his story.
"Naturally, women like that are experts at putting on airs," C retorted, adding that her brother had visited drunk the other day, rambling incoherently. C's tone suggested that when it came to judging women's character, I was just a novice.
However, the fact that the brother came, questioned them, and even filed a complaint might mean she was genuinely wronged. Newspapers might claim to be "leaders of the public" (민중의 지도), "society's wooden clapper" (사회의 목탁), or "moral guardians" (도덕상방부재), but reporters are still human. Being human, not gods, it's impossible for them to know the intimate details of others' private lives. Moreover, who could tell if someone, spurned after pursuing the woman, sought revenge by deceiving a reporter into publishing such a story? No matter how authoritative a newspaper is, obstructing a young woman's promising future—especially considering the public at this time tends to believe anything printed, even that soybeans can be made from red beans (팟으로 메주를 쑨다해도)—is a serious matter. Regardless of the truth, the moment such a story appears in print, it changes the course of that woman's destiny. However important the newspaper's authority or the reporter's dignity may be, meddling with an individual's fate is the greatest sin of all—holding such views, I couldn't help but feel pangs of conscience whenever faced with such issues.
"But where did you get the material for that story?"
I asked after sitting quietly for a moment.
"I heard it from a detective at the police department, but I'm going to write more today," C replied, looking triumphant.
"Write more? How can you write without conducting a direct, thorough investigation, just based on hearsay? Searching for independence activists or investigating activities in certain circles might occasionally involve false reports without major consequences, but articles concerning personal matters or defamation require extreme caution. Aside from the potential for unpleasant problems later, one must consider the situation of the person involved," I stated firmly, my impression of the woman making it impossible for me to believe the story. <10>
"You seem quite taken with that woman."
A, sitting nearby, chuckled.
"What's there to worry about when the facts are clear? Because of what happened yesterday evening, the Ponjŏng Police Station is planning to bring her in for questioning today," C insisted, then recounted the following story.
A young man from the countryside near Yangju sold his cow and came to Seoul intending to use the money as capital for some venture. He was intercepted by an acquaintance from his hometown, a drifter type, who promised to find him a cheap and good inn. He was led, fatefully, to Yi Yŏngok's house. Yi Yŏngok's house didn't have an inn sign; its large gate and appearance didn't suggest poverty. Furthermore, perhaps due to something the introducer said, the reception was overly warm. They insisted he shouldn't worry about the cost of meals. The flirtatious behavior of the young female student inside, while suspicious, wasn't entirely unwelcome. After about two days, the friend who brought him there said, "The young lady of the house is a young widow, studying at a girls' school out of frustration. She's looking for a suitable husband but dislikes the frivolous young men of Seoul and is always searching. Her older brother asked me to help find someone. Why don't you try your luck? She has her own means, so there's nothing to worry about. Give it a shot—if it works out, great; if not, no loss. It looks like she's interested too, so she's unlikely to refuse. And even if you just fool around a couple of times and she doesn't want more, that's fine too. Doesn't cost anything... just passing the time... But later, just buy some Chinese dishes like I tell you, and everything will be settled." Driven by this encouragement, though partly scared, he had about two hundred *wŏn* on him. Tempted by wild fantasies and greed—thinking he could live there for a few months for just ten *wŏn*, and if luck struck, maybe even settle down permanently, what a windfall that would be!—he kept fumbling in his pocket, pulling out his round mirror watch, mulling it over. Eventually, the four of them—the young man, his friend, and the hostess's siblings—played cards late into the night. As instructed by his friend, he ordered Chinese food. Slightly drunk, he pretended to be intoxicated and let himself be led wherever they took him. For the next three days, he was completely captivated, gradually drawn deeper, losing track of day and night. <11> By the time he came to his senses, the gambling debts owed to the female student's brother amounted to nearly half the money he had brought. Only then did his friend say,
"Hey, what kind of situation is this? Why are you playing games like that? Now that things have come to this, do you think they'll let you leave? Let's just slip out, pretending we're going to arrange for money. If her mother finds out while you linger, who knows what hell will break loose?" Scaring him thoroughly, the friend led him out. Still, wanting to see the girl one last time, he consulted his friend.
"Alright, wait in front of Pagoda Park. I'll bring her right out," the friend said, but then disappeared and didn't show up even as evening fell. Realizing he'd been tricked, he wanted to go back, but the winding streets and uncertain address made it difficult. Besides, he feared what trouble he might face from the brother and mother if he went back rashly. Still dithering, he went to the place where his friend supposedly stayed, only to be told,
"That gentleman might come by to gamble, but you can't count on finding him." At his wit's end, enraged, he went into a standing bar near Changgyo and started spending the rest of his money, drunkenly ranting,
"Can such things happen in this world?" A policeman from the Ponjŏng station overheard him and decided to call the woman in for questioning, or so the story went.
C briefly recounted the gist of the article he intended to write.
"Such incidents are commonplace, but the fact that she brazenly goes around threatening to sue the newspaper is what makes me want to teach her a lesson," he added, laughing cheerfully.
"But that story also came from the same detective you mentioned earlier, right?" I pressed again, still skeptical.
"No. After it appeared in all three newspapers, it became an issue at the Ponjŏng station, and they're keeping an eye on her. Then this new incident was discovered, and the judicial police were buzzing about it. Besides, when the victim's name is clear, there's no room for doubt, is there?" C countered, seeming to regard my persistent questioning as either naive or perhaps indicative of someone foolish enough <12> to sell a cow (牛) and fall into such a trap himself.
However, the phrase "such incidents are commonplace" struck me as newly alarming, and I found it difficult to understand the mentality of the judicial police who were "buzzing" about discovering a "new fact." I decided to let the matter drop.

Since then, whether Yi Kyŏngok sat in the detention cell at Ponjŏng station in her soiled skirt and *chŏgori*, licking her lips over bean rice, or whether she was still savoring Chinese dishes nestled beside some other fool, or perhaps sitting demurely under the blackboard in the third year at K Academy—I never found out. The lawsuit fizzled out, and I never had the chance to ask C about it again. But even now, whenever I see a female student on the street with freckles and braided hair, wearing a willow-green skirt—a "New Woman" who quickly lowers her head when she sees a man—I find myself standing there staring blankly for a moment, a habit I seem to have developed.
(May 27th) <13>

**Summary**

The narrator, a newspaper reporter, recounts his experience waiting at the Kyŏngsŏng District Court Prosecutor's Office where he observes a young woman, Yi Kyŏngok, and her brother. Initially intrigued by her demure yet defiant demeanor and respectable appearance, he speculates about why she was summoned. Later, back at his newspaper office, he learns from a colleague, C, that Yi Kyŏngok is the subject of scandalous articles depicting her as a "clandestine prostitute" who swindles men, allegedly backed by police connections. Despite C's confidence based on police sources and a recent victim's testimony, the narrator remains skeptical due to his impression of Yi and concerns about journalistic ethics and the devastating impact of potentially false accusations. The story ends unresolved, leaving the narrator haunted by the image of Yi Kyŏngok and the ambiguity surrounding her true nature and fate.

Historically, the story offers insights into colonial Korean society, particularly the workings (and inefficiencies) of the Japanese colonial legal system, the power and potential irresponsibility of the press, and prevailing social anxieties surrounding "New Women" like Yi Kyŏngok, whose actions deviate from traditional norms. Historians might find the depiction of the prosecutor's office, the casual attitude towards ruining reputations based on hearsay (especially police sources), and the specific social types (reporter, swindled country youth, modern woman) valuable for understanding the period's social dynamics and tensions.

양지쪽은 물긔먹은 흙이 검어케 부프러 올라서 떼어놋는 발 밋이 푸근푸근히 부드러운 감촉을 주건만은 아즉 바람은 모진 맛이 남아서 두루막이 귀에 찔은 손을 내노키가 실흔 날새엿다.
나는 경성 디방법원 검사국 대합실문에 단 한 개 밧게업는 걸상에 걸어안젓다가 으스스한 증이 나서 양디를 차자나와 문턱에 팔장을 끼고 섯스랴니까, 스믈 너덧쯤 되어 보이는 햇그무레한 청년이 허둥허둥하며 왓다갓다 하다가 오륙인이 어정버정하는 가운데로 빠저드러서며 모자를 들먹하더니,
「검사국 접수구가 어대얘요?」하고 공손히 뭇는다.
「이리 드러가슈」하고 나는 반간통 쯤 되는 대합실을 몸짓으로 가르처 주엇다.
그러나 그는 내 뒤를 기웃이 드려다 보기만 하고 다시 휙 돌처서 오든 길로 허둥허둥 나가더니 어떠한 녀학생 한아를 끌고 드러왓다. 눈이 똥그러코 입술이 도틈한 것이라든지 갸름한 상판에 죽은깨가 난 것까지 남매인 것이 분명하다.
녀자는 남자들 틈에, 더구나 검사국이라는 듯기만 하야도 가슴이 울렁거리고 우주충한 늣김을 주는 데에 난생처음으로 호출을 바다온 것이 불쾌하고 무슨 알 수 업는 긔운에 압두가 되여 그러하는지 눈을 몹시 찝흐리고 한 눈을 팔며 드러오더니 남자들이 모혀서, 서성대이는 테 밧그로 멀리 도라서 저쪽 복도 엽헤가서 모로 섯다. 그러나 지금 섯는 자리에까지 가는 동안에 얼굴을 응등그려뜨리고 한 눈을 팔면서도＜2＞ 고개를 남자들이 잇는 편으로 반쯤 돌려서 번쩍 치어들고 것는 것이 나의 눈에는 이상하게 거슬렷다.
그 동안에 남자는 내 뒤로 대합실에 들어가서 호출장을 훔척훔척 끄내면서 접수구에서 내어다 보기를 기다리고 섯다. 나는 상관업는 호긔심-그것은 신문긔자라는 직업이 부지불식간에 길러준 것일지도 모르나-이 나서 그 남자를 따라 돌처 드러와서 눈치 빠르게 그 엽서에 씨인 일홈을 흘끈 보앗다.
―리경옥(李景玉)이라는 일홈이 시선 끄트로 스처갈 제 호출된 녀자의 일흠은 알앗지만 주소를 채 자서히 보기전에 그는 엽서를 뒤집어서 조그만 류리창 압턱에 노아버렷다.
...리경옥! 리경옥 어떤 종류의 녀자길네 검사국에 호출을 당하얏누? 정남의 증인으로? 요사이 빈빈한 공산당 독립당의 혐의자의 안해?......그러면야 청처짐하게 검사국에서, 호출장을 노흘 때까지 내버려 두엇슬라구!
나는 이런 생각을 하며 그 녀자의 행색을 다시 한 번 보랴고 문 밧그로 나섯다.
유록빗 치마에 흰옥양복 저고리를 입고 자지빗 겨울「숄」을 걸친 뒤ㅅ모양이 수수한 것으로 보면 녀학생은 면하얏고 더구나 좀 낡은 듯한 노랑목다리구두 뒤축에 흙이 무든 것을 그대로 신은 것을 보면 여염집 녀편네라는 것보다는 소학교 교원이거나 유치원 보모가타얏다. 그러나 저러나 버틔고 섯는 몸가지는 틔라든지 떡버러진 억개쪽지와 펑퍼짐한 엉덩판으로 보와서 벌서 어른꼴이 박인 것은 분명하다고 혼자 생각하얏다.
......그러나 웨 불려왓슬구? 깨끗한 흰두루막이를 입고 역시 얍부장한 노란목다리구두를 단정히 신엇슬 뿐 아니라 벌서 철 차저서 봄모자를 삽붓이 쓴 남자를 보면 웬만한 집자녀일 것이요 더구나 호출당한 녀자를 혼자 보내기가 안되어서 오래비까지 안동해 보낸 것을 보면 가도도 짐작할 만한데...까닭업는 증인으로 불려왓슬까?...
고지식하게 아츰 아홉시부터 와서 영문도 모르는 일에 두 시간이나 으르를 떨고 섯는 판이라 심심파적으로 식이지 안는 궁리률 혼자하고 섯섯다. ＜3＞
남자는 호출장을 접수식히고 나서 나오더니,
「얘, 저리 드러가 잇거라.」 하며 소리를 치니까,
녀학생은 장옷자락을 염의듯이 커다란 「숄」미테들은 두 손을 안흐로 너허서 염의여 고개를 속옷하고 내 엽흐로 지나 대합실 안으로 드러갓다. 나는 멈츳하며 비켜섯다가 거동을 다시 한번 보리라 하고 문 이편으로 닥아서며 그 녀자를 거듧떠 보앗다. 문 안에 드러섯는 녀자는 「숄」을 벗어서 네골에 접어 량수거지를 한 손우에 걸고 고개를 속옷이 모로 구석에 서잇다. 어느 모로 보든지 추한 남녀관계에 참고인이나 증인으로 올 위인은 아니라고 마음 속에 단정하야 버렷다. 이러케 금을 치고 나니까 인제는 안심이 된 것처럼 다시 궁리를 해볼 생각이 업섯다.
한참동안 이젓든 찬 긔운이 오수수하고 겨드랑이 미트로 기어든다. 내 겻테 섯든 남자는 어느 틈에  어떤 세탁집 주문거간가튼 헙수룩한 남자와 이약이를 한다.
「...네, 댁에 늘 주문도루(取)하랴 가서 여러 번 뵈엇지요. 그런데 웨 오섯세요?」 하며 그자는 빙그레 웃엇다.
「글세, 나 역시 몰라. 그러나 언제까지 이러케 세워두랴누?」 하며 남자는 발이 실인 듯이 두 발은 통통 구른다.
「점심전에 불으면 속한 편이외다. 주임검사는 누구얘요?」
「주임에 M라구 썻든데 朝鮮사람두 검사가 잇는지?」
「글세요-.」
M이라는 소리에 내 귀가 반짝하얏다. 오늘 내가 온 것은 실상 S신문의 편집겸 발행인을 불은 것이지만 부득이 내가 대신 오게 되어서 면저 대리인이 왓스니 신문에 관한 것이면 기다리겟고 그러치 안흐면 가겟다고 검사에게 련통을 하얏더니 기다려달라고 해서 이 때까지 가도오도 못하고 잇는 터이엇다.
오정이 갓가워 와도 아모 소식이 업다. 싯검어코 유착한 서류창고에 압히 가리운 검사국의 압마당에는＜4＞ 벌서 양디도 다 지나가서 좁은 속에 사람은 졈졈 부러가나 음산한 긔운은 석양판갓다. 소리업는 쌀쌀한 바람이 슴여들 때마다 누구나 대합실안을 기웃거리기만 하고 선 듯 드러가는 사람이 업다. 수줍어하는 녀자― 트레머리하고 노란 구두를 신은 녀학생이 단정하게 도라서 잇기 때문이다.
나도 인제는 다리가 뻣뻣하야 더 참을 수가 업서 한참 망서리다가 썩 드러섯다. 세 사람쯤 안즐만한 널판지 걸샹의 구석편으로는 그 녀자가 외면을 하고 안젓고 저 끄테는 어떤 남자 한 아가 안젓다. 나는 그 한가운데에 남자편으로 닥아서 펄석 끼어안젓다. 물론 녀자와는 두어 뼘통이나 떠러저 잇다. 그러나 녀자는 삽붓이 닐어나서 밧갓쪽으로 바드심히 도라선다. 나는 미안하다고 생각하얏다. 그러나,
...... 이 계집애는 생전 뎐차도 타보지 못하얏나? 하는 생각을 하야보니 야릇하기도 하고 맵살마저 보이엇다. 조곰 잇다가 끄테 안젓든 남자도 닐어나서 훌적 나가버렷다. 조고만 걸상은 내 차지가 된 것은 조고만 승리라 할까? 그러나 좁다란 대합실에는 녀자와 단둘이 남께 되엇다. 나도 나가버릴까 하다가 저편 끄트로 비켜 안저서 턱을 괴이고 눈을 감엇다. 녀자는 이편을 돌려다보는 듯하더니 다시 살그머니 안는 긔척이다.
...... 웨 이리 요사를 뜨는구? ―하는 생각을 하면서 그처럼 수집어 하는 것이 얌전하기는 하다고 생각하얏다. 거진 네시간이나 기다려야 꽁 구어먹은 수작이다. 나가서 사(社)에 뎐화를 걸고 누구든지 대신 와서 기다려 주엇스면 하는 생각이 업지 안엇스나 뎐화를 걸려 간 동안에 불을까 보아서 머믓머믓하고 안젓스려니까, 밧갓헤서 버적버적 발자최 소리가 나더니,
「...이것이 그 유명한 사내 총독 암살 음모사건― 윤치호사건 말슴이야요-. 당시에 본관이 좁아서 십일하고 xx시간에 불야불야 지은건데...」 하며 日本말로 설명하는 소리가 난다.
나는 벌떡 닐어나서 나가 보앗다. 서너사람이 견학하랴 온 것을 구경시키는 모양이다.
... 윤치호 사건! 윤치호 사건! 하면서 나는 지금 들어안젓든 납작한 집웅우를 휙 하번 훌터 보앗다. 우중충한 복도속에서는 가벼운 회리바람이 살살 기어서 한구석으로 모히다가 너저분히 허트러진 조희조각을＜5＞ 펄펄 날리우며 온데로 다시 갓는지 사방은 잔잔하야젓다.
... 윤치호 사건! 윤치호 사건! 윤치호를 첫 마지한 후 멧 윤치호를 마젓노? 하며 작년 봄에 이 사건으로 열 몃해 만엔가 세상구경을 하게 되엇다는 안명근 인가라는 중늙은이가 반장님이 되어서 사에 차저 왓슬 때에 어릿어릿 히는 그를 위로하면서 사진을 박든 생각이 난다. ...아홉시에 사람을 불너노코 다섯시간 여섯시간을 기다리게 하는 것이 재판소란 곳이다. 절대(絶代) 이 압헤는 시간(時間)도 존재를 감춘다. 염라대왕 압헤서는 영원과 일순간이 비겨 떠러지겟거늘 열 몃해란 시간이 사람의 운명을 좌우하는 그들에게 하상 무엇이랴. 그러면 한사람이 귀중한 생명의 몃 토막인 하로의 생활을 빼앗기로 생살여탈의 권을 가진 갸륵한 그네들로서야 그래도 관대한 처분이 아니냐.
이런 생각 저런 생각을 밋도 끗도 업시 하고 섯스려니까 D사의 Y, K가 우중우중 드러온다.
「야- 이게 웬일이야?」, 하며 그들은 나를 보고 인사를 하다가 다시 저편을 보고
「X군! 로형두 붓들려왓소? 예서 신문긔자대회를 할 세음인가」 하며 Y는 너털웃슴을 웃는다. 도라다 보니 D사의 Y다.
「웬일이야? 모조리 불러다노코 어떠케 할 모양이야? 가튼 사건인가?」 하며 내가 무르니까 글세― 하며 그들은 참고인으로 오란 것이라고 명함을 끄내드려 보냇다.
그러는 동안에도 T와 기윽자로 섯는 아까 그 남자는 똥그란 두 눈을 한칭 더 크게 뜨고 우리 네 사람을 번가라가며 유심히 치어다 보고 섯다.
「그러나 여보게. 자녀 뒤에두 한아 잇네 그려. 이런데두 샨이 오나!」
K는 내 뒤에서 내어다보는 그 녀자를 보앗는지 이런 소리를 하며 웃엇다. 나는 면구스런 생각이 나서
「자네들은 누가 불렷나?」 하며 말을 막느라고 딴전을 부치엇다.
「검사라니?」 한다. ＜6＞
「다섯시간? 하지만 좀더 기다려야 할껄! 호출장을 가지고 왓다지?」 하며 뭇는 K의 눈치가 자긔들은 뎐화로 불러와서 명함을 드려 보냇스니까 대우가 다르리라는 것가티 들리엇다.
「그러나 저러나 다섯시간을 기다리든 여섯시간을 기다리든 상관잇나? 조흔 구경하것다, 소설자료도 생길거얏다... 하며 K는 또 녀자를 건너다보고 웃엇다.
「조흔 구경이라니?」
「뒤를 좀 도라다보게.」 하며 Y도 겨오 들리게 실업슨 소리를 하얏다. 녀자도 갸웃이 내어다보고 섯는 모양이나 나는 모른 척하고,
「그런 구경을낭은 내 대신에 다섯기간만 붓박이로 실컨들 하게」 하고 나도 웃어버렷다.

二

조금 잇다가 Y와 K를 사환아이가 불러 드려갓다.

「하지만 그 이약이 역시 처음에 이약이 하든 형사의 말이겟지요?」 하며 그래도 나는 또 한번 다저 보앗다.
「안요. 세 신문에 일제히 난 뒤로 본정서에서 문제가 되여서 주목중인데 또 그런 사실이 발각되엇다고 사법계에서 조화서들 하든데요. 그 뿐 아니라 피해자의 성명이 분명한 다음에야 의심날 여디가 업지 안허요」 하며 자고 캐이는 나를 돌이어 어림업는 사람이거나, 그러치 안흐면 족히 소(牛)라도 파라다가＜12＞ 데어밀만한 사람가치 녁이는 모양이엇다.
그러나 「그런 일이야 례상사이지만...」 이라는 그 례상사가 나에게는 새삼스럽게 놀라운 일갓고 새 사실을 발각하야 조화서들 한다는 사법계순사의 심리를 리해하기 어려운 것 가티 생각이 되어 잡잣고 마랏다.

그 후에 리경옥이가 본졍서 구류장에서 고름업는 치마저고리를 입고 콩밥에 혀를 빨며 안젓섯는지 여전히 청료리ㅅ 맛에 또 어떤 놈을 끼고 누엇섯는지? 그러치 안흐면 지금 K학원 삼년급인가에 가서 얌전을 빼이고 칠판미테 안젓는지? 고소사건도 흐지부지 되고 C나에 다시 무러볼 긔회도 업시 지금까지 지내오지만, 요사이에도 길거리에서 죽은깨잇는 트레머리-유록치마 입은 녀학생! 남자를 보면 고개를 금시로 다소곳하고 지나치는 신녀자를 보면 나는 얼빠진 사람처럼 한참 바라보고 섯는 버릇이 생기엇다.
