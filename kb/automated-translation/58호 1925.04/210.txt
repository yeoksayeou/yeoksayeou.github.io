Prelude Offered from "The Sword of Darkness"
						Cho Myŏnghŭi

Sword of Darkness! Sword of Darkness!
Surely for you, there could not be such rambling words like the foul stench of these humans of the Last Days?
Could there be words?
I believe you possess only silence, like the color of the black sky.

Sword of Darkness! Sword of Darkness!
Surely for you, there could not be the laughter of fools who leap about like gobies in dishonest satisfaction?
Could there be laughter?
I believe your eyes merely blink like suns that have lost their light, with tears flowing like candle wax.

Sword of Darkness! Sword of Darkness!
Surely for you, there could not be the kind of tasteless happiness that only mouse-like humans favor?
Could there be happiness?
I believe you are only like a great man clad in black clothes of blood, standing upon the guillotine.

Sword of Darkness! Sword of Darkness!
Drive away all the words, colors, and happiness of this immature world.<41>
And lock up this world only in painful silence.
So that only the soundless lamentation of truthful souls pierces deeply upon the earth... -2, 3-
<41-42>

**Summary**

This poem by Cho Myŏnghŭi is a dark, intense invocation addressed to the "Sword of Darkness." The speaker contrasts this entity with the perceived corruption and superficiality of the "Last Days," rejecting human speech ("rambling words"), false laughter ("dishonest satisfaction"), and base happiness ("tasteless happiness"). Instead, the Sword of Darkness embodies silence, sorrow (tears like wax, dimmed eyes), and tragic nobility (a figure on the guillotine). The poem culminates in a plea for the Sword of Darkness to obliterate the world's superficialities, leaving only "painful silence" and the "soundless lamentation" of authentic souls. From a historical perspective, the poem likely reflects the profound despair and disillusionment felt by Korean intellectuals under Japanese colonial rule in the 1920s. The "Sword of Darkness" can be interpreted as a symbol of necessary destruction, purification, or perhaps a nihilistic rejection of a world seen as irredeemably corrupt, offering historians insight into the psychological and emotional landscape of the era's resistance or suppressed anguish.
