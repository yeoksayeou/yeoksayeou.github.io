**Body Text Translation:**
Various Types of Kyŏngsŏng Student Appearances

My goodness – there seem to be quite a lot of students in Kyŏngsŏng these days. Around 8:30 in the morning or 3 or 4 in the afternoon, the people bustling back and forth, packing every alleyway, are all students. Among them are elementary school students with torn and stained *turumagi* (overcoats), their hats tilted back, scarves wrapped loosely, book bags slung jauntily over one shoulder; there are girl students in black skirts and white *chŏgori* (upper garments), wrapped in colorful woven carriers like straw mats, swinging their brass-bowl-like bottoms and chattering like sparrows; there are vocational school students in neat navy serge Western suits, their four-cornered caps pulled down low, staring only at their shiny shoes; there are middle school students in short Western-style jackets, wearing leggings (脚絆), their plump buttocks bouncing like a horse's rump; there are also beaming graduates looking like newborn calves, with smooth hair, stylishly wearing the newly fashionable spring caps, sporting new cotton *turumagi* and gold-rimmed glasses, swaggering as if to say, "I'm a gentleman now"; and there are new students in greenish *turumagi*, wearing silk scarves tied loosely like a calf's tether, their caps without school badges worn askew like drunkards, mouths agape, wandering from bookstore to bookstore. If I were to charge these common students for physiognomy readings (觀相), even with a 50% discount, I'd become instantly wealthy, so rich that Minister Min Yŏng-hwi would come looking for a servant's quarters job, bowing and scraping. But since I’ve hung out my physiognomy shingle for over a year now, offering readings for free, how could I possibly charge a special fee just for students? Ah well – forget it, let’s just do it for free again. However, I can't possibly read all these numerous students at once, so let's exclude the elementary and girl students and first look only at the male students from middle school and above. Even they number in the thousands, so there's no time to examine each individual. Let's gather them on the wide Hullyŏnwŏn training ground, use a telescope and a mind-reading mirror (照心鏡), and apply the latest, most wondrous grand physiognomy method to read their overall appearance. Attention! Let's begin. Today's students, compared to the past, indeed seem quite neat in appearance. Their ages are similar, heights similar, complexions similar. Unlike before, you don't see old students in their 30s or 40s with scraggly beards. There are no hunchbacks, no one with distorted mouths, no one walking in a zigzag. Not just appearance, but their studies also seem comparatively more advanced than before. Japanese language, of course, they are all fluent like market vendors, and they seem quite proficient in English and doctrine too. However, when viewed closely with the eyes of a physiognomist, there are many strange trends among some segments of the student population. To mention just the main points:
Their cap badges all look like misshapen gourds, as if they were pawned off to a taffy seller and then retrieved, making it impossible to tell which school they attend (though I can’t be sure, perhaps they do it for style and secret activities). Their mouths seem swollen from learning only Japanese, as it's routine for them to go to the latrine for a smoke as soon as the dismissal bell rings. Whenever three or four gather, they make it their business to critique teachers and female students. During lunchtime, dozens form groups and head to the *hottŏk* (sweet pancake) stalls. Posŏng High Common School students go to the place opposite Yŏnghaeru in Kyŏnji-dong; Chungdong students go opposite Kim Kyu-jin's house in Susong-dong; Hwimun High Common School students go opposite a certain Western tailor shop in Kwanhyŏn or opposite the Hygiene Center (衛生所) in Kye-dong; Chungang High Common School students go to the snack shop outside the school's main gate. Kyŏngsin is the same, and Paejae, Yangjŏng – every school without exception is the same. This, of course, only applies to students with money; they treat these *hottŏk* stalls as their regular haunts, calling them "such-and-such hotel," and go daily <38> According to this physiognomist's statistical survey, the daily income of one stall ranges from a high of 57 Wŏn 60 Chŏn to a low of 24 Wŏn 30 Chŏn, just from students. Ah – isn't this astonishing? They could get by just eating their packed lunches (*bento*), so what's this habit of snacking on *hottŏk*?

Moreover, those extremely wicked (*窮凶*) Chinese *hottŏk* vendors, to curry favor with students and sell cigarettes, even prepare secret smoking rooms and lure students daily. Among them, vocational school students, feeling too embarrassed to eat *hottŏk*, flock to the Japanese light Western-style restaurants in Hwangggyo, or else to the loach soup restaurants in Hwagae-dong, or the secret eateries in Tubangmaeng'igol (Songhyŏn-dong), or the restaurants inside and outside Chaam near Namdaemun Gate. (Of course, not all of them do.) And though they might face suspension for not paying tuition fees, they habitually go to watch motion pictures. They rarely write letters to inquire after their parents and siblings, yet correspondence with female student "sisters" (who are not relatives) is frequent. Also, if you visit a boarding house, no matter how poor the student claims to be, you won't find the oil-box desks of the past; they invariably have desks worth 5 or 6 Wŏn, complete with bookshelves, pen holders, and photo albums. Even if they lack a single textbook or reference book, they all have a romance novel and a book of popular songs. In their drawers, you'll sometimes find flowery envelopes and stationery for writing to female students, and even spring pictures (*ch'unsŏdo* - erotic pictures). On the wall, a violin (빠요링) and racket (라겟트) are ostentatiously hung. Once out on the street, they lose track of time teasing (히야까시) female students, and when they return home, they are often late for meals because they are busy shaving (面刀). Furthermore, if you look inside their brains and stomachs, they are filled with the vanity of wanting to live comfortably and well-fed, even if it means becoming a slave to a Government-General (總督府) official, some bank or company employee, or some wealthy person. They are busy seeking employment even before taking their graduation exams, and after graduation, their first orders of business are dressing up in Western suits, frequenting restaurants, and divorcing their original wives. (Limited to wealthy students.) There's much more to say, but let's stop here and await the next installment. Ah – is this trend, even if only a part of the Kyŏngsŏng student world today, truly a sign of our good fortune or misfortune? If this physiognomist's words turn out to be utterly wrong, and everyone blames me, saying, "That physiognomist fellow is just like Ch'a Ch'ŏnja, only telling lies," then it would be fortunate for our people. But if the physiognomist's words prove uncannily accurate, there could be no greater misfortune for our people. I merely hope that, for those students described, the physiognomist's words become falsehoods. Finally, I feel extremely regretful and also offer my apologies if my words have inadvertently offended the pure and blameless students who have no connection to these behaviors. Let's disperse now... <39>

**Summary**

This 1925 article from *Kaebyŏk* magazine offers a sharp critique of male high school and vocational school students in colonial Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). The anonymous author, posing as a physiognomist, observes students' appearances and behaviors, lamenting what they perceive as a decline in moral character and seriousness. The students are depicted as increasingly adopting superficial aspects of Japanese and Western modernity – fashion, consumer habits (snacking at stalls, frequenting cafes), smoking, dating ("teasing" female students), and valuing material comfort and bureaucratic/corporate jobs over national consciousness or academic rigor. The author contrasts this with an idealized past and worries about the future implications for the Korean nation. For historians, the article provides rich detail on colonial-era youth culture, social anxieties surrounding modernization and Japanese influence, changing gender dynamics (interactions with female students), class distinctions among students, and the specific urban landscape of student life (schools, hangouts like *hottŏk* stalls and restaurants in named locations). It reflects contemporary adult concerns about the direction of the younger generation under colonial rule.

形形色色의 京城 學生相
