News from Home and Abroad

Sinŭiju Burning with Public Anger
Red Fist

Mr. Miyake (三宅君), the newly appointed police chief of Sinŭiju, who, upon taking office, seemed to feel that even a whole day was insufficient for improving the treatment of Japanese prostitutes under the golden signboard of "protecting the weak," revised the traditional income distribution ratio between brothel owners and prostitutes from 6:4 to 5:5. He basked得意揚揚 (tŭkŭi yangyang - triumphantly) in the cheers and applause of the "Red Skirt Group" (紅裙團 - referring to the prostitutes). Unexpectedly, however, he faced general criticism and the snickers of officials wondering if the very first step of his administration was prostitute protection. Flustered and confused (周章狼狽), he resorted to sophistry (詭辨), claiming, "My path lies solely in protecting the weak," thereby covering up the immediate situation. Whether it was a desperate measure to regain popularity is unknown, but recently, he suddenly arrested 22 members of the Sinŭiju Labor Union (新義州勞働組合), concentrating the world's astonishment upon himself.
For what crime such a large number of workers were arrested simultaneously was initially unclear. However, according to an announcement in the pro-government Japanese newspaper about ten days ago, it was claimed that Sinŭiju Labor Union members had seized goods being transported by members of the local Freight Transport Group (荷物運搬組), who were fellow workers, and extorted the transport fees.
However, from our fair perspective, it is clear that the Labor Union members did not seize the cargo. In reality, the Freight Transport Group members, jealous (猜忌) of the Labor Union and unable to counter the well-organized and disciplined union through strength, resorted to a kind of self-destructive act. They violently seized the goods being transported by Labor Union members, instigated a fight between the two groups, and then, using grey elements (회색분자 - collaborators/reactionaries) behind the scenes, fabricated a story completely opposite to the facts, shifting the blame (傳嫁) onto the innocent Labor Union members. It is common knowledge that the Freight Transport Group is an impure organization, born with the goal of destroying the Labor Union, jealous of its sound development, and backed by grey elements parasitic on government authority (官權). Therefore, further lengthy discussion is avoided here, but it is also not surprising (無怪) that the authorities, considering the face of these grey elements, would foster their activities both overtly and covertly.
However, as a police authority whose lifeblood is fairness and impartiality, and as you, Sir, who were so busy advocating for prostitutes from the very beginning of your appointment, claiming to protect the weak – despite the facts being contrary, you have detained 22 workers who are on the verge of succumbing to starvation and poverty. You have caused their hundreds of family members to weep on the streets, shivering in the north wind (朔風) with empty stomachs and ragged clothes. Sir! Where lies the true meaning of your declaration at the start of your post to protect the weak?
Ah! Perhaps it's because you, Sir, with your stubborn brain that feels the very name "Labor Union" (勞働組合) is dangerously subversive (不穩), compounded by nervousness, believe that among the equally weak, simple-minded workers are excluded from this protection. Is your true intention of protecting the weak merely to protect the prostitutes, whose every frown and smile makes your mind and body entranced (恍惚), thereby earning the honorable title of "Dannasama" (旦那*さま - Master/Sir) from the ingratiating women who sell smiles (賣笑婦)?
But Sir! Where there is strong pressure, a volcanic vent (噴火口) like *arasawa* (아라사와 - unclear reference, possibly meaning eruption/resistance) is bound to form. <49> Even when beating a dog (캐를), one should check for an escape hole (구녕) before striking. Do you not anticipate that they, like a cornered cat (窮捌*貓), will resist you with sharp teeth?
Do you not perceive, Sir, the fearsome reaction that will come in the future from the hundred-odd laboring masses pushed into a desperate situation (絶對絶命)?
Furthermore, colluding with the pro-government Japanese newspaper, you headlined it as "Violence by the Labor Union," adding the insulting and flattering talk (侫說) that "this is an exposure of the innate *indolent nature* (*惰牲 - likely meant 惰性, tasŏng - laziness/inertia) of Koreans," writing it large for all to see. Ah, what wicked people!
However, evil is never the enemy of righteousness. Sir! Do you know or not that the day is near when all this evil will be judged with gunpowder?
Sir! How about deep reflection right now, before the judgment comes?
(3,8)

P'yŏngyang Tidbits
One Reporter

For some reason, strikes (同盟罷業) never cease in P'yŏngyang. Whether the oppression by the propertied class is particularly severe (尤甚) or the consciousness of the proletarian army is advancing, strikes simply don't stop. The P'yŏngyang Printers' Strike, which astonished the world, was indeed the largest scale reaction movement in recent Korean history. Issues of treatment and wages were, of course (毋論), the main causes. Employers are human, workers are human; they are the same humans, so how could dissatisfaction not arise from such mistreatment and low wages! Eventually, the volcano erupted. However, Employer Sirs – as the proverb goes, "One receives superior virtue, not inferior virtue," we state that the employer side should concede. And isn't the continuation of the strike during busy times, setting aside your own losses, a loss for society? How about yielding (讓步如何)?

Also, it seems that undesirable trend of kisaeng cutting their hair short (妓生斷髮) is becoming fashionable in P'yŏngyang. If they have a bit of money (돈分) and a smooth face (얼골 작대기나 빤빤하면 - lit. if their face stick is smooth), the bloodsuckers (吸血鬼 - meaning patrons) slyly latch on, acting as if it's life or death, and as the final stage of bloodsucking, they skillfully arrange for the hair cutting (斷髮). Previously, there were no fewer than 12 or 13 such hair-cutting demons (斷髮鬼), but recently, a bloodsucker named Usanwang (禹山王) from the Kisŏng Kwŏnbŏn (箕城券番 - P'yŏngyang Kisaeng Association) reportedly got involved with some rich idler from Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul), cut her hair, and even stopped going out. Her parents are apparently making a huge fuss (앙알앙알 야단벅석), complaining that she's staying home instead of earning money. It's a general laughingstock – what's with a bloodsucker cutting her hair?

Whether the passion for education has cooled or the difficulty of living has become particularly severe (尤甚), P'yŏngyang, which used to bustle every year with difficulties in school admission, saw something strange this year. Among the six common schools in P'yŏngyang city, all except Chongno Common School failed to meet their admission quotas. Ah, this terrifying difficulty of living!

Songdo Higher Common School, Where Cornered Dogs Fight
S A

The Hideous and Wicked Old Guard Faction
Songdo Higher Common School (松都高等普通學校), that Christian hypnotizing institution (基督敎催眠機關), which began to shake after being hit by the bombshell of a student strike (同盟休校) in May last year, has seen another round of dark struggles centered around the conspiracy and misdeeds of the so-called Old Guard Faction (元老泒), composed of cornered dogs (窮狗) whose usefulness has expired. The leader of the Old Guard Faction, Yi Man-? (李萬0), who had been the head of academic affairs for many years, found his position and influence diminishing compared to before, and his credibility among students weakening. Consequently, he submitted his resignation to the principal, Mr. Yun Ch'i-ho (尹致昊씨), which was accepted. However, taking advantage of Principal Yun's absence while in Kyŏngsŏng, a certain gentleman named Hong ?-kuk (洪0國), who had returned from America, used his knowledge of English as leverage with the so-called vice-principal, a mentally ill Westerner named Sin Ae-do (申愛道). Thoroughly acting as Yi Man-?'s stooge (走狗), he employed all sorts of strange and unfathomable crafty schemes (奇怪岡測한 奸策) on behalf of the already resigned Yi Man-?. Mr. Sin, the Westerner unfamiliar with the ways of the world, readily believed him and came up with the brilliant idea <50> of withdrawing the resignations of the aforementioned Mr. Yi and certain other teachers, all without the principal's knowledge.

Principal Yun's Anger?
After becoming principal under less than honorable circumstances, having spent over 20,000 won of 'money' that was being squeezed out of him again and again, receiving only social criticism, and considering resigning due to various personal reasons, this incident occurred, disregarding him. The temperamental Principal Yun reportedly resigned, saying "good riddance," but due to the efforts of some students, he is said to have agreed to stay on for the time being.

Infighting Among Teachers?
The Old Guard elders, jealous of the current head of academic affairs (as of this writing; likely to change?), Yu ?-min (劉0敏), and seeking personal gain in the sacred field of education, behaving like profiteers (謀利輩), were looking for ways to somehow restore their old influence when the student strike erupted last May.
Ah! Who, with a conscience, could have imagined using this unfortunate incident as a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity?! The sinister Old Guard elders falsely accused Yu ?-min and others, who had only been there a few days, of inciting the students. After causing a frantic disturbance (發狂亂動), they committed the hateful and abominable tyranny (可憎可惡하 專積) of ordering the expulsion of 38 leading brave students from the 3rd and 4th grades, becoming a major target of criticism in Kaesŏng (開城) society.

Mr. Yi's Hateful Words
As the misdeeds of the Old Guard party were clearly reported in newspapers and magazines, despite carrying on with their conspiracies, they seemed concerned about their reputation. Mr. Yi reportedly went around the school grounds spreading malicious propaganda (惡宣傳) to students, telling them not to believe magazine and newspaper articles about the school. Did they think the newspapers and magazines would defend them? Truly the mad barking (狂吠) of a cornered dog (窮狗).

Denunciations Between Teachers and Students on Graduation Day!
On March 6th, after the graduation ceremony, the entire graduating class gathered in the tea room for a farewell ceremony between teachers and students, intending to share their heartfelt feelings. Suddenly, a voice arose from among the graduates... starting with talk about how the supposedly sacred educational institution had split into factions fighting over 'rice bowls' (밥 - food/livelihood), it mercilessly denounced (大聲疾討) the wicked and base crimes of the Old Guard members Yi Man-? (李萬0), Wŏn Hong-? (元洪0), Hong ?-kuk (洪0國), Kim ?-ryŏl (金0烈), etc. Teacups and chairs were thrown about, turning the scene into pandemonium (殺風景의 수라장). The cornered dog elders (窮狗輩), having anticipated this, had hidden themselves away (追邸隱身). Anyway, this is how modern 'Christians' practice the Bible. If God truly cares...

Future Outlook Watched Closely
As the school, fraught with talk and reasons, increasingly becomes a stage for the mad barking of cornered dogs (窮狗狂吠場), the students, bewildered about which rhythm to dance to, finally saw this unfortunate, ugly drama exposed. What shadows will flicker in the minds of students facing their desks at the beginning of the school year?... (Recorded March 12th)

Shanghai Dispatch
Yosŏp

From morning today, a light rain was falling, but around lunchtime, it turned into heavy snow, pouring down all afternoon. Snow in mid-February is truly a rare miracle (긔젹 - 기적) to see in Shanghai. At least from what I've seen, in the past five years, last winter had the most snow, but seeing snow in this spring season is a first for me.
While the snow falls, the 29,000 pitiful workers currently on strike (同盟 파공 - 동맹 파업) in Shanghai, awaiting a resolution, must be shivering, hit by the snow! All thirteen Japanese-managed textile factories in Shanghai city have closed their doors due to the strike. The fuse was lit on the morning of February 14th when 7,000 workers at one factory went on strike (파공), igniting sympathy strikes (同情파공) by workers in other factories, now reaching 30,000 people.
The crucial demands are:
1. Improvement of treatment <51>
2. Increase in wages (료금 - 요금)
3. Prompt payment of wages (급금 - 급금)
There is little hope for resolution yet.
Particularly noteworthy in this strike is the great activity (大活動) of students, especially female students. At gatherings of striking workers, one can always hear the jade-like voices of 2 or 3 female students delivering passionate speeches, shouting for them to fight to the end (끗까지).
Still, some students have been arrested on suspicion of agitation (선동의 혐의). From this, one can see how much students serve as the backbone (中堅) in the labor movement and other movements here.
Another point of attention is the clash (츙돌 - 충돌) between Japanese and Chinese workers. The reason is unclear (未詳), but according to newspaper reports, Japanese workers were beaten to death by Chinese workers who worked in the same factory and thrown into the river. Seeing this, it is said that the slogan "Workers of the world, unite!" is merely an ideal, practically impossible in reality. The reason is inevitably that the interests of Japanese workers and Chinese workers are different (相異). If this is the case within China, how much more so in our own country (本國 - Korea)? Could this serve as a great lesson to some so-called influential figures who ignorantly clamored, "Korean workers must unite with Japanese workers too"? Even if both are in the exploited class (被착취게급 - 피착취계급), as long as one receives special treatment and the other receives more brutal treatment, it is a fixed principle (정한 리치 - 정한 이치) that these two classes can never truly unite, right?
The strike seems likely to expand further (압흐로도), but it's still uncertain. According to the Dong-A Ilbo (東亞日報), there's a major strike by Kyŏngsŏng tram employees in our country, right? And in the countryside, there's the anti-Oriental Development Company (東拓 - 동척) movement! Anyway, troubled times (多事之秋) have come to this house and that house alike. Won't there be a day of great harvest before long? I see the current battles merely as preliminary skirmishes (예비젼 - 예비전). Sorry for rambling on too long. I'll stop here.

Hwangju Miscellany
Chŏkpyŏksaeng (赤壁生)

In the Presbyterian Church (야소교 쟝노교회), they made a big fuss for the starving people (饑餓者) across Chosŏn (全朝鮮), weeping and praying with lectures and prayers. I believed (미덧더니 - 믿었더니) there would be much sympathetic help through collected donations, but then they suddenly disappeared without a trace (쥐도 짝도 업시). Awakened by criticism from foreigners (外人), they reportedly sent a mere 20 wŏn (圓) check from the Sunday offering (主日獻金). Is there such a lack of substance between their words (言) and actions (行)? Also, the pastor of the same church, Yi Hak-pong (李學鳳), is said to be harsh in his criticism, insults, and curses of others during every sermon (講道). Believers who came seeking peace (和平) and comfort (慰安) reportedly return intimidated (悚懼히). Does he intend to compensate for this with 60 wŏn a month? Such a sermon (講道) is robbery (强盜 - pun on 講道 'kangdo' and 强盜 'kangdo').

Influential young people (有志靑年) sought permission from the authorities (當局) to establish the Hwangju Library (黃州圖書舘), which is now nearing realization. Cooperating with the Hwangju Youth Association (黃州靑年會) for its management is even better. I hope those entrusted with the duties (執務) will exert themselves (一曆 奮發). However, it's said there are individuals who pledged donations but are now trying not to pay. If they persist to the end, their names will reportedly be revealed clearly (톡톡히).

The Hwangju Christian Women's Youth Association (黃州 基督敎 女子靑年會) has established a kindergarten (유치원). One cannot help but admire (感服) the management of this great enterprise by women who lack power, financial resources (財力), and rights (權利). However, they cannot succeed without borrowing the strength of influential people (有志). Although they have barely managed to continue (근근득생) for the past year, they now face the fate of a candle in the wind (風前燈火). Oh, influential people of Hwangju! I hope you will lend your support, and at the same time, I pray for the continued activity of the women.

Editor (編者). Although there are many other dispatches from various places worthy of publication, due to lack of space, we cannot include them all, which is deeply regretted. <52>

**Summary**

This collection of news reports from April 1925 covers social and political unrest across Korea and in Shanghai. In Sinŭiju, the new Japanese police chief faces criticism for prioritizing Japanese prostitutes' welfare over Korean workers, leading to the mass arrest of union members on dubious charges. P'yŏngyang experiences continuous labor strikes, notably a large printers' strike, alongside commentary on kisaeng adopting bobbed hair (seen negatively) and declining school enrollment due to economic hardship. Songdo Higher Common School is plagued by internal conflict between factions of teachers and administrators, involving alleged conspiracies, manipulation, and student expulsions, culminating in a confrontation at graduation. A dispatch from Shanghai details a massive strike by Chinese workers in Japanese-owned textile factories, noting student support (especially female students) and reflecting skeptically on the potential for solidarity between Chinese/Japanese and Korean/Japanese workers due to differing interests and treatment under colonialism. Hwangju sees efforts to establish a library and a women's association running a kindergarten, alongside criticism of a local church pastor and donation issues.

From a historical perspective, these articles vividly illustrate the tensions of colonial Korea: labor activism and strikes met with suppression (Sinŭiju, P'yŏngyang printers), the complex role of colonial authorities (Sinŭiju police chief), social changes and moral commentary (P'yŏngyang kisaeng), economic hardship impacting education (P'yŏngyang enrollment), internal conflicts within Korean institutions under pressure (Songdo school), and early reflections on class struggle intertwined with national/ethnic conflict (Shanghai strike analysis). The raw, often angry tone, especially regarding the Sinŭiju police and the Songdo school infighting, offers insight into contemporary Korean perspectives on injustice and institutional failings during the colonial period. The Shanghai piece provides a valuable contemporary Korean viewpoint on international labor movements and the difficulties of cross-ethnic worker solidarity in an imperial context.

海內海外
公憤에 타오르는 新義州

有志靑年으로 黃州圖書舘을 設立하랴고 當局에 寄附許可까지 맛하 지금 거의 실현을 보게 되엿슴에 黃州靑年會와 協同하야 경영한다 함은 더욱 잘 되는 일이다. 執務의 任을 당한 諸君은 一曆 奮發함을 바란다. 그런데 기부를 적어놋코 아니 내랴고 하는 人士가 잇다고 한다만 일끗까지 고집하면 일흠까지 톡톡히 들어내랴고 한다.

黃州 基督敎 女子靑年會에서는 유치원을 설립하얏다. 힘업고 財力과 權利업는 女性들의 위대한 사업을 經營함에는 感服지 아니치 못하겟다. 그러나 有志의 힘을 빌지 안으면 안 되겟다. 1年이 되여오는 지금까지 근근득생으로 계속하여 왓지마는 이 압프로 風前燈火의 運命을 맛낫다. 黃州有志여 後援의 힘을 주기를 바라는 동시에 女性諸君에 끗끗내 活動이 잇기를 빈다.
