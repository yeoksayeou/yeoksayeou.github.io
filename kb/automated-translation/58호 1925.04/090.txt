Impure Graduation Ceremonies with a Mix of Japanese and Western Styles, Observations of Graduation Ceremonies at Paejae, Posŏng, Chungang, Kyŏngsin, Yangjŏng, Chungdong, and Hwimun Schools
						Pak Tori

Starting with the Overall Impression of Seeing the Graduation Ceremonies
"Let's discard form and take only the substance—" This seems to be the common sentiment in Chosŏn society currently. Looking at graduation ceremonies as just one example, it certainly seems so.
Just years ago, it was customarily like this: "It's a glorious graduation ceremony, so let's do it as well as possible. Invite many guests, decorate the venue splendidly, prepare plenty of tea and snacks—even if we have to go into debt, it must be done that way. So, let's erect pine gates. Let's hang flags of all nations. Let's call the military band. Let's order food from Myŏngwŏlgwan. And prepare for the appreciation party, initiate a farewell party—" Indeed, the schools or the student bodies were bustling with excitement before and after the graduation ceremonies. But what happened?

This year is not like that. Is it due to the pressure of the economy, or is the general sentiment to take only the substance? This year's graduation ceremonies are quite desolate. It's common to just gather the graduates in the school auditorium, hold a simple certificate presentation, and finish. Some schools even conduct the ceremony while holding regular classes on one side. It was very desolate. Discarding form and taking only the substance is not necessarily bad. However, because it's too desolate, a poor feeling arises. A feeling of decline, a feeling of withering, a feeling of being dispirited, the feeling of a feast in a house that has lost public favor—many unfavorable feelings are apparent. Wouldn't emphasizing splendor as much as possible on this one day, the glorious graduation day, be better for the school, the graduates, or the current students?

Paejae High Common School Graduation Ceremony

(Including the elementary school) March 4th, 3 PM. Location: Chŏngdong Church. - They study at school and receive their graduation certificates at the church. Wow—this is truly strange. But there's nothing strange about it. It's common practice in many places for Christian schools run by Westerners.
Blonde-haired, blue-eyed individuals casually entering is a matter of course, and the fact that there are more female guests than male guests in the guest seating is unthinkable if not for a Chŏngdong-side graduation ceremony. At the exact time, led by the graduates, the crowd of students rushes in noisily. Since it's graduation day and promotion day, everyone looks happy. As it's the school uniform, the clothing style isn't remarkable, but their faces all look quite decent. Even at a glance—it seems they wrestled with shaving this morning. Almost all of them—look like graduates from 10 years ago.
Principal Appenzeller's (阿扁薛羅) university cap and gown look particularly solemn, perhaps because it's a church and Western style.
Will they start the ceremony with "Kimigayo" as written in the program? Or will they start with the usual hymn? Ah, that's right! It was a hymn.
"Let's begin with Hymn No. 5," the command was given, and the sound of "Hallelujah" vibrated throughout the hall. Following that, the usual prayer came out. Even the strong-willed Western noses seem to have no choice against official power. Since the Governor-General's representative and the Governor's representative came, they were reverently escorted to the stage. They sat down squarely at the front of the stage, saying, "Ahem. That's right. Ehem." The Western style, or rather the Christian style, briefly receded, and now the truly Japanese style was about to begin. "Kimigayo" and the "Imperial Rescript on Education" were, of course, in order. But unfortunately, an unexpected comedic act burst forth. From among the students on the west side before the stage, a voice suddenly shouted "Principal?!" in anger. The atmosphere in the hall instantly tensed, and all eyes turned there. The angry shout "Principal?!" was heard again, and that student addressed the entire student body, delivering a speech. "Everyone—we, whose future is like ten thousand miles... from such a bad teacher..." He seemed to be speaking quite indignantly and emotionally from his perspective, but it was hard to understand what he was saying. Cries of "Grab that guy and take him out" arose from all sides. The glorious graduation ceremony, the solemn venue, instantly turned into a scene of chaos. As if anticipating such a situation, a detective nearby quickly rushed over and dragged the student out. The hall became peaceful again. However, everyone was left wondering, "What happened?" To resolve this question, Teacher Kang came forward with an apology speech to the guests. "Everyone... I apologize. That student was recently caught forging documents after stealing the school seal. Although his five years of accumulated effort are regrettable, according to school regulations, we decided not to give him a graduation certificate. It seems that's why he acted this way," he said.

Resolving the question is fine, of course, but mentioning the theft of the seal and such on the spot seems rather detrimental to the reputation of the school and students. Just<42> saying simply, "That student couldn't receive his graduation certificate due to an unpleasant incident..." might have seemed better. Anyway, he is an honest (シヨチキ - shōjiki) teacher in Japanese terms.
Following the order: Kimigayo, Respectful Reading of the Rescript, Certificate Awarding, Prize Awarding, Principal's Address, Governor-General's Message, Governor's Message, Guest Congratulatory Address, Graduate's Response—like clouds clearing to reveal the sun again, it finished smoothly.
However, what remains memorable here is Principal Appenzeller's fluency in Korean. Without rambling, and ditching the usual Principal-style admonition, he concluded with a section on educational theory, citing examples from Eastern educational mottos like "Leaving sons gold and treasures is not as good as teaching them one classic" and Western ones like "Jade unwrought will not become a vessel; man unlearned will not become accomplished" (玉不啄 不成器 人不學 不成器 - Western also same), which seemed more meaningful than endless admonitions. And during the guest addresses, old Yi Sang-jae's opening remark, "I don't know Japanese, so I have no choice but to speak in Korean... By the way, do you all understand Korean?" was such a profoundly meaningful, humorous satire that while the hall burst into laughter, everyone felt like they received a new injection. Councillor Kim Yun-jŏng's (金潤晶閣下), the "Rice Bucket" (밥통), reading of the message was so ridiculous that everyone clutched their mouths and kept poking each other's sides. "How can he read Japanese like that? Wow—he really is a rice bucket," a voice was heard saying. Whether he said "Taishō" (大正), "Taisō" (體操 - gymnastics), "Kyōiku" (教育 - education), or "Gyūniku" (牛肉 - beef), it was impossible to tell. And how he butchered the phrases! — Let's leave it at that. The farewell medal pinning by the current students for the graduates seemed quite affectionate. Finally, who were the top students of this graduation? Yi Ŭn-ŭl, Kil Yŏng-yang,
Im Pyŏng-hwan were the three gentlemen among 137 graduates (107 from High School, 30 from Elementary).

Posŏng High Common School Graduation Ceremony
March 5th, 10 AM. At the school. "They say the Taedong River thaws around Usui and Kyŏngch'ip, but what's with this year's severity? Ah, forget it..." muttering continuously, I passed the Andong intersection and arrived at the school's main gate in a desolate mood. More than anything, this question arose first. "Did I come knowing wrongly? Does it look like graduation day anywhere? The invitation clearly said today... Could it be this desolate?" That was the question. However, there was a table (テ불 - teburu) at the entrance, guiding, "It's the graduation ceremony. Please come in."
It seemed only the graduates came, not all the current students. Upstairs and downstairs were quiet. Indeed, only the graduates had come. This is truly a graduation ceremony focused solely on graduates. Why would currently enrolled students be at a graduation ceremony—this is truly the latest style.
The time arrived. With 59 graduates from the 5-year system and 32 graduates from the 4-year system as the main attendees, along with a dozen or so guests and a dozen or so staff members, the venue was quite monotonous. There wasn't even a shadow of a female guest, but instead, the presence of 2 or 3 Buddhist monks (和尙) was unprecedented at other schools. Monks? Don't even mention it. They are said to be the managers of this school. Somehow, they were seated in the chief seats.
Since it's the standard Chosŏn graduation ceremony nowadays, could there be anything different? As usual, it opened with "Kimigayo" and closed with the graduates' reply. Perhaps forgotten, there was no "Imperial Rescript." There was no school song or graduation ceremony song either. It was very simple. However, it seemed somewhat lacking compared to a more bustling one. At this ceremony too, the "Rice Bucket" Councillor's reading of the message provided some relief from boredom. Listening to Yu Sŏng-jun (兪星濬令監), the lord with the fox-fur scarf (狐皮목도리), deliver his farewell speech, whether it was about Kangdong or something, was ticklishly unbearable. Principal Chŏng Tae-hyŏn's (鄭大鉉) admonition, however, left a friendly memory.
The lasting impressions of this graduation ceremony are: first, the absence of current students; second, its simplicity and plainness; and third, the graduates' immediate cap-changing ceremony right after the ceremony ended. How they must have longed for caps (캡), how tedious the school cap (校帽) must have been, that as soon as the closing order was given, they simultaneously executed the "School cap off! Cap on!" change. The 5-year school cap must have been tedious, but anyway— The laurel wreath of this graduation was taken by Yi Kang-guk
and Ko Yu-sŏp. <43>

Chungang High Common School Graduation Ceremony
March 5th, 1 PM. At the school. — Although a bit cramped compared to downtown Kyŏngsŏng, the Chungang School graduation ceremony was held at its well-located site. Perhaps because there was a decision like, "Let's not crowd into a narrow classroom; the location is good, so let's just do it refreshingly in the schoolyard," the ceremony was held in the schoolyard in front of the main hall, even though a chilly wind still nipped at the ears on this day. Led by the 48 graduates, with the entire student body as the main participants, along with guests and staff, the venue seemed quite bustling compared to other schools. The ceremony opened under fluttering flags of all nations, accompanied by the lively sound of a military band. The manner in which Hyŏn Sang-yun (玄相允), who is sometimes called "Mr. Saengwŏn" (生員님 - title for classics licentiate), known for his gentleness and also holding the title of "boy principal," delivered his address was, forgive the impoliteness, endearing to see.
There's nothing more to say about the usual order of the ceremony—the regret of this ceremony might be the absence of honors students. However, they worked hard and received their graduation certificates, so that's enough. The "Rice Bucket" lord's (狐皮영감) "leather craft" (희닉구 - 皮革?)—might it not be a case of the pot calling the kettle black (五十步笑百步)? The fox-fur lord's prime days as Kyŏngsŏng Councillor seemed clearly visible. Teacher Im Kyu (林圭), who would insist on being second if told he wasn't the skinniest, delivered a congratulatory address that was just the usual kind of exhortation. Kim Sŏng-su (金性洙), acting for the school founder, whose Japanese-style (倭形) metallic voice (鐵音) lacked any particular distinction, seemed perhaps better than the school founder Min (閔校主). After finishing the ceremony with the school song, there were tea and snacks in a separate room. Is eating the most important thing (食而爲大販)? There was no need to worry about appearances.

Kyŏngsin School Graduation Ceremony.
March 5th, 3 PM. At the school. — When thinking of Kyŏngsin School, one immediately recalls its principal, Mr. Kun Yebin (君芮彬 - likely Junkin). Never mind the sharply sloping lower jaw—the nose occupying two-thirds of his face—despite such a grotesque appearance, he speaks Korean well and has a humorous streak, so he always comes to mind when Kyŏngsin is mentioned. And one bursts into laughter. This graduation ceremony was, of course, also a tripartite mix of half-Western, half-Japanese, half-Korean styles. Opening with a hymn, followed by a prayer and even a Bible reading, then Kimigayo, respectful reading of the Rescript (Christian schools doing it particularly intensely), certificate awarding, etc., proceeded in the usual order. Fortunately, the "Rice Bucket" Councillor was absent, which was somewhat refreshing, but Lord Yu (兪令監), the contractor for fox-fur scarf congratulatory speeches (狐皮목도리 祝辭請負業者), starting with his absurd denial of romance, followed by praise for God the Father, was so rambling and incoherent it made one's mouth dry. In contrast, Dr. Ki Il's (奇一博士) brief congratulatory remarks using Edison's example of success sounded plausible.
The special feature of this graduation ceremony is that among the 15 graduates, led by the two honors students Pyŏn Chae-sŏng (卞在成) and Yi *ch'ŏl (李*喆 - name unclear), there were 6 students with perfect attendance, which is quite endearing. The principal revealing the graduates' academic records and publicly announcing them at the ceremony seems like self-advertisement, but in reality, it seems like a good thing. The mention that Pyŏn君’s 5 years of perfect attendance were unfortunately broken by 3 days due to his father's death is exceptionally endearing and regrettable news. Moreover, he is said to be impoverished.

Yangjŏng High Common School Graduation Ceremony
March 6th, 10 AM. At the school. — Yangjŏng Educational Foundation (財團法人養正義塾) — As a private high school in Kyŏngsŏng, its foundation is substantial, and according to reputation, this school is said to have a good location.
Grumbling alone about the poor weather in the chilly wind, I went around Pongnae-chō (蓬萊町) outside Namdaemun and arrived in front of the main building of the school. It seemed I was the first among the guests. "Agh, that lord is coming again. That contractor of congratulatory speeches," as soon as I spotted the fox-fur scarf, the large, flabby figure of the "Rice Bucket" Excellency (「밥통」閣下) appeared right behind. "This graduation ceremony will be the same again..." Guided by a student, I entered the guest room, and it seemed much more Japanese-influenced than at other schools. That is, like the Western influence at Paejae and Kyŏngsin, this school has a strong Japanese influence. There seem to be more Japanese teachers than Korean teachers. Being served tea and snacks immediately upon entering the room also seems like a Japanese custom.<44>
Indeed, the graduation ceremony venue was purely Japanese style. The national flag was crossed on the stage, the Imperial Rescript was respectfully presented on the table, except for the principal and Lord Yu, everything was in Japanese, and except for 2 or 3 guests in Korean attire, everyone else wore Western suits or Japanese clothes. Principal Ŏm Chu-ik (嚴柱益) seemed not to know Japanese, and contractor Yu (兪請負業者) also seemed the same. Yet, this lord, despite billing himself as a specialist in congratulatory speeches, routinely put forth the excuse, "I came reluctantly due to the principal's forceful command." A certain Western-suited man's Japanese congratulatory rambling (祝辭), though he tried to act sophisticated, looked quite awkward. During the usual "Rice Bucket" Excellency's reading of the message, the students' bursts of laughter were mutually embarrassing. Anyway, the honors students of this graduation are said to be Song Pong-gŭn (宋鳳根),
Kim Tal-chun (金達俊),
and Cho Tong-no (趙東老), three individuals among 37 graduates. —

Hwimun High Common School Graduation Ceremony
March 7th, 1 PM. At the school. Surprisingly, there were many female guests. Ah, they must be the Min family members. The Governor-General's representative and the Governor's representative were present as a matter of course, and Lord Yu (兪영감) also came as usual. In place of the troublesome Kongsan Myŏngwŏl (空山明月 - "Empty Mountain Bright Moon," nickname for former principal Im Kyŏng-jae 任璟宰), the new principal Kim Hyŏng-bae (金亨培) took the stage. "Kimigayo" aside, the Rescript reading was not performed. During the ceremony, the sound of a car was heard, and the school founder Min Yŏng-hwi (閔泳徽) entered the hall wearing a winter cap (반이쓰 - bonnet?), holding a short cane in his left hand and a Luzon cigar (呂宋煙) in his right, accompanied by a young boy. All eyes turned towards him. Well-fed, well-dressed, and seemingly without worries, he looked like a millionaire at first glance. He took a seat on the stage.
As he is the school founder, though lacking coherence, a few words of admonition were delivered. During the Governor's message, everyone again clutched their mouths and poked each other's sides. During Lord Yu's congratulatory speech (祝辭), feeling utterly bored, low "boos" (야지 - yaji, from Japanese 野次) sporadically emerged. Min's admonition (訓話) to be thankful tickled the chest. The attack on romance (戀愛攻擊論) and the argument against divorce (離婚不可說) seem to be important items in his contracting business repertoire. Does a person become so unaware of others' psychology when cornered? If that contracting business were limited to this year only, it would be fortunate, but if it continues next year too, I wish someone would pay him off, even using money, to please make him cancel. The principal's introduction, "Lord Yu Sŏng-jun, Teacher" (兪星濬閣下 先生님), might have been a momentary slip of the tongue, but hearing it made my back ache (허리가 시엿다 - felt extremely unpleasant/cringeworthy).
Anyway, the laurel wreath of this graduation was monopolized by Yi Kyu-p'ung (李奎豊) alone, out of 92 graduates.
Postscript. Due to the editorial deadline, I only managed to observe the graduation ceremonies in Kyŏngsŏng city before March 10th. In the observation notes, the frequent inclusion of tiresome remarks from the "Rice Bucket" Councillor, the Fox-Fur Lord, etc., becomes tiresome for the reporter as well as the reader. However, unfortunately, the state of this year's graduation ceremonies is such that they cannot be described without them. What can be done if there's no Councillor other than Mr. Kim, and no one to give a congratulatory speech other than Mr. Yu? It's truly a ridiculous and pathetic society—.

**Summary**

This 1925 article by "Pak Tori" critiques the graduation ceremonies of several prominent private schools in Kyŏngsŏng (colonial Seoul). The author contrasts the current austere and "impure" (mixing Japanese, Western, and Korean elements) ceremonies with the more elaborate ones of the past, attributing the change possibly to economic hardship or a new focus on substance over form, though lamenting the resulting desolate atmosphere. The piece provides detailed, often sarcastic, observations of individual ceremonies at Paejae, Posŏng, Chungang, Kyŏngsin, Yangjŏng, and Hwimun schools (mentioning Chungdong but not attending), noting mandatory Japanese elements like the "Kimigayo" anthem and Imperial Rescript on Education, Christian influences at mission schools, a student protest at Paejae, and the repetitive, sycophantic presence of certain colonial officials mockingly nicknamed "Rice Bucket" and "Fox-Fur Scarf." The author concludes by lamenting the state of society reflected in these ceremonies. From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable ground-level view of cultural negotiation and imposition under Japanese colonial rule, particularly within the education system. Historians might find the descriptions of the hybrid ceremonies, the overt presence of colonial authority (officials, anthems, rescripts), the differing atmospheres between mission schools and others, the student resistance, and the candid critique of collaborating Korean elites particularly interesting.

卒業式을 본 總感想부터
「形式을 보리고 事實만 取하자-」 이것이 아마 現下 朝鮮사회의 共通한 氣分인가 뱀´다. 爲先 졸업식 一例로만 보아도 확실히 그린 듯 십다.
年前만 해도 의례 이랫것다. 「영광의 졸업식이니 될 수 잇는 대로 잘 하자. 來賓도 만히 請하고 式場도 繁華히 設備하고 茶菓도 만히 준비하고 - 빗을 내서라도 그러케 해야만 된다. 그러닛가 松門도 세우자. 萬國旗도 달자. 軍樂隊도 불으자. 明月館에 요리도 주문하자. 그리고 謝恩會 준비 送別會 發起 -」 실로 졸업식 전후의 학교 又는 학생 間은 퍽도 興盛興盛하얏다. 그런데 웬일일가?
今年은 그러치 안타. 경제의 打迫으로 그러함인지 일반 심리가 사실만 취하자는 셈인지? 금년의 졸업식은 퍽도 쓸쓸하다. 학교 강당 안에 졸업생 뿐 모아놋코 간단한 証書授與로 맛치고 마는 것이 보통이다. 엇던 학교는 一邊 上學을 식키면서 式을 거행하기도 한다. 퍽도 쓸쓸하얏다. 형식을 버리고 사실만 취함이 조치 안은 바는 안이다. 그러나 넘우도 슬쓸하닛가 조치 못한 感이 생긴다. 零落한 기분 시드러지는 기분 한풀 꺽긴 기분 人心 일흔 집의 잔치하는 기분 - 두루 조치 못한 기분이 만히 보인다. 이 영광의 졸업식 一日은 가급적 繁華를 主함이 학교나 졸업생 又는 在學生 間에 조치 안을는지?＜40＞

培材高等普通學校 卒業式

(並學堂) 3월 4일 오후3시. 장소는 貞洞禮拜堂. - 학교에서 공부하고 예배당에서 졸업증을 밧는다. 야- 이것 참 이상하구나. 그러나 이상할 것도 업다. 서양인 經營의 기독교 소속 학교는 到處에 그런 예가 만타.
黃髮靑眼子가 슬젹슬젹 입장함은 毋論 男子席 來賓보다 婦人席 來賓이 더 만홈도 貞洞側 졸업식이 안이면 어림도 업다. 정각이 되자 졸업생을 선두로 학생떼가 우루퉁퉁탕 드리밀닌다. 卒業日이요 昇級日이닛가 모다 快해 보인다. 교복인지라 의복 모양은 別數 업지만 얼굴 모양은 다- 그럴 듯하다. 얼핏- 살펴도 이날의 아젹 시간은 면도와 씨름을 한 것 갓다. 거의 전부가- 10년 전 졸업생들의 꼴이 보여진다.
교장 阿扁薛羅씨의 大學帽와 禮服은 교회당이요 서양식이라 그런지 別로 엄숙해 보인다.
순서에 쓰인대로 『君が代』로 開式을 하랴나? 例의 찬송가로 開式을 하랴나? 올치 그럿치! 讚頌歌엿다.
『찬송가 제5장으로 開式합시다』 令이 나리자 『할넬루야』 소리가 場內를 진동한다. 뒤밋쳐 例의 기도가 나온다. 억지ㅅ손 세다는 洋코도 官力에는 별수가 업나 보다. 總督代理 知事代理가 나오닛가 惶感스럽게도 壇上으로 모신다. 그들은 『암. 그럿치. 에헴』하고 壇上 정면에 坐定합신다. 洋式 안이 基督敎式은 잠간 드러가고 이제 졍말 日式이 열니려 한다. 『君が代』 『敎育勅語』가 毋論 순서이다. 그러나 불행히 番外의 滑劇一幕이 툭 튀여진다. 壇前 西側 學生叢 중 엇던 학생의 입으로서 갑작이 『교장?』 하고 怒號하는 소리기 들닌가. 장내의 공기는 일시에 긴장되며 모든 시선은 그리로 몰녓다. 「교장?」하고 怒號하는 소리 겹쳐 나더니 그 학생은 학생일동을 향하야 一場 演說을 吐露한다. 「여러분- 前程이 萬里 갓튼 우리가... 이런 납븐 선생한테...」하고 자기 딴은 퍽 悲憤 感慨하게 떠드는 모양이나 무슨 말인지 잘 알아드를 수가 업다. 「저놈 잡아내라」 소리가 사방에서 이러난다. 영광의 졸업식 엄숙하든 식장은 일시 수라장이 되고 마럿다. 이런 氣味를 예상하얏든지 곁에 刑事가 쏙 달녀들더니 그 학생을 끄러 내인다. 장내는 다시 평온해졋다. 그러나 「웬일일가?」하는 의문은 모다 품게 되얏다. 이 의문을 푸러주기 위하야 姜선생의 來賓에 대한 사과연설이 나온다. 「여러분... 미안함니다. 그 학생은 日前에 學校印章을 窃取하야 僞造文書를 맨들다가 발각된 학생이외다. 5년간 積功은 愛惜하지만 校規에 의하야 졸업증을 안이 주기로 햇슴니다. 그래 그러는가 보외다」 라고 한다.

의문푸리는 毋論 조치만 當席에서 印章窃取 운운하는 소리는 학교나 학생간의 체면상 좀- 조치 못한 듯 하다. 그저＜42＞ 「그 학생은 不美한 일이 잇서 졸업증을 못 밧게 되닛가...」하고 간단한 一言이 오히려 나을 듯이 보엿다. 어든 日本말로 シヨチキ한 선생이다.
순서대로 君が代, 刺語奉讀, 證書授與, 償品授與, 校長訓辭, 總督告辭, 知事告辭, 來賓祝辭, 卒業生答뜻 - 雲飛去日復照格으로 순순히 잘 맛친다.
그런데 여기에 기억이 남는 것은 교장 阿扁薛羅씨의 朝鮮語 融通이다. 支離하지 안케 또 例의 校長式 訓*를 집어치우고 東洋의 敎育標語 西洋의 敎育標 「遺子黃金滿財 不如敎子一經」 「玉不啄 不成器 人不學 不成器」(西洋亦同)의 예를 드러 敎育論 一節로 끗을 막는 것은 訓話萬談보다 의미깁허 보인다. 그리고 來賓 辭에 드러 李商在노인의 「나는 日本말을 모르니 不得已 朝鮮말로 할 밧게... 그런데 대관졀 여러분 朝鮮말 들을 줄 아오」의 劑頭一言은 얼마나 意味深長한 滑橋的 諷刺인지 장내는 一場 우슴판이 되면서도 모다들 새 注射를 맛는 感이 잇다. 「밥통」 叅與官 金潤晶閣下의 告單朗讀은 엇지도 우스운지 모다들 입을 부르쥐고 서로 녑구리만 집어 뜻는다」 엇지면 日本말을 그러케 잘 닑느냐? 야- 참 밥퉁이다.」 소리가 어던 이의 입으로서 전해온다. 「大正」이라는지 「軆操」라든지 「敎育」이라는지 「牛肉」이라는지 분별을 알 수 업다. 句節은 또 엇지 그러케 잘 때는지? - 그만해 두고. 재학생 일동으로서 졸업생에게 채워주는 送別票 폐달은 퍽 友情이 이서 보인다. 최후로 이 졸업의 우등생은 누구인가? 李殷을 吉瑛養
任秉煥 3君이다. 137명(高等 107명 學堂 30명) 졸업생 중에.
