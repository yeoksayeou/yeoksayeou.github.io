Article Title: 妙香山으로부터 다시 國境千里에 (From Myohyangsan, Again to the Thousand-Li Border)
Author: 春坡 (Ch'unp'a)
Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

From Myohyangsan, Again to the Thousand-Li Border
						Ch'unp'a

In the previous issue, while talking about the world's most scenic spot, Myohyangsan's Inhodae, I had to stop due to space limitations. Now, let's set out again from Inhodae, over mountains and waters, towards the thousand-li border.

From Inhodae to Kanggye Town

Clinging to iron chains and arduously crawling up, one reaches the foremost scenic spot of Hyangsan, Inhodae. The top of the terrace is a large flat rock capable of seating a hundred people, upon which four or five old pine trees stand majestically. Below the terrace is a thousand-chang cliff; looking down is dizzying, enough to make one lose their senses. Furthermore, from the hundred-chang steep cliff to the left of Sangwŏn Hermitage, the thousand-ch'ŏk Yongch'u Waterfall tumbles down, and from the thousand-ch'ŏk sheer cliff to the right, the hundred-chang Sanju Waterfall cascades directly down. Truly, it can be said, "Silver rivers fall from the ninth heaven on left and right." It's as if silver pearls are scattered, as if jade fragments are dispersed; misty waves scatter like smoke, dragon scales glitter, and rainbows form. At this place, this terrace, the ultimate extremity of mountains and waters, beyond description or writing, the marvelous Sangwŏn Temple sits serenely atop the terrace, flanked by the silver rivers and bearing the celestial waterfall. What could this be if not a separate world? Whether it's heaven or a fairy terrace, drunk or dreaming, my mind and body were entranced, not knowing what to do.

Having ordered lunch at Sangwŏn, our party of over 30 people freely roamed above and below the terrace, some dipping their feet in the lower pools, others washing their faces upstream, some picking flowers, others breaking branches. In this freedom of coming and going, my body felt transformed, as if I had become an immortal of the Kyŏngdae, completely forgetting the dusty world, not knowing the way back. Moreover, there were strange tales and miracles; should I listen to that or look at this? My hands and feet were busy, like trying to chase two rabbits at once. While I listened to the tale of Master Hyŏnbin visiting the Dragon Palace, others discovered the large characters "Sinsŏn'gulchaek Unhadongch'ŏn" (Divine Immortals' Cave Dwelling, Cloud Mist Grotto Heaven) carved on the rock face and chattered excitedly. While I examined the egg-shaped dragon horn stones based on the legend of retrieving dragon eggs, others passed through the Kŭmgang Gate, critiquing the carved names of worldly visitors, unaware that the day was waning. Soon, lunch was announced; it was 1 PM. Those admiring water, rocks, trees, flowers—all gathered in one room and ate their fill of meat*. <64>

No matter how much Sangwŏn is a separate realm, what can be done? They say Tan'gundae is 20 *li* away, so what can be done? We must go quickly, see it, and return, so what can be done? Before the meal could even settle, we took leave of Sangwŏn; like leaving something unfinished, my feet wouldn't move easily. Many thanks for the kindness of the head monk, Kim Yonghae.

Winding through the dense forest along the mountain slope stone path, we arrived at Puryŏng Hermitage, also said to be one of Hyangsan's scenic spots, rested briefly, and then leaped down again, crossing the Hyangsan valley floor to see Pinbal Hermitage. From here, bounding up through the Tan'gun Grotto Heaven's white stones and clear streams towards Tan'gundae, taking one step and resting, then one step, it became truly arduous beyond measure.

Though our group numbered 30, not a single person knew the path to Tan'gundae. Mr. Yi *hun, who had visited once 7 or 8 years ago, was with us but said the path was unclear due to fallen trees and jumbled rocks. Kim Sŭngbŏp, a monk from Pohyŏn Temple, was also accompanying us but said he had only seen it once as a child and wasn't sure. Dividing into groups of 3 or 4, or 5 or 6, pointing to this slope, that slope, this valley, that valley, just assuming "it must be that peak, below that rock," treating main paths as side paths and side paths as main paths, falling and stumbling, getting pricked and scratched in landslides, on stony paths, through thickets, through thorny bushes, we struggled onward, aimlessly climbing, but ultimately failed to find the path to Tan'gundae. Our group of five ended up mistakenly entering the most difficult impasse among impassable places, cut off left and right, front and back—a desolate spot where even tigers and leopards do not approach. Advancement was difficult, retreat was difficult; reaching a point where loud laughter was useless and long sighs were futile, we looked at each other, up and down, merely waiting to perish in this place.

"Life springs from desperate situations" (絶處逢生) is an old saying. Why would Heaven wish to let us innocent descendants of Tan'gun die of hardship in this place? Tan'gun was spiritual and divine; how could there be no future sign? Surely, he would guide our hands. Mustering a burst of courage, with a final resolve between life and death, tying handkerchiefs together and joining short staffs, connecting hand to waist, we climbed several *chang* up the cliff like squirrels. A faint path seemed to open up; along with a long sigh, a slight smile emerged. "Hey, those ahead! Is there still no path? Save us!" I shouted until my throat was raw. Right above, just a few steps away, someone replied, "Hey, there's a path! Just come forward a few more steps!" "Ah, saved! We're saved now!" Taking a couple more steps, indeed, a faint path appeared.

Thus, we reached Tan'gundae at 6 PM.
On Tan'gundae, there is Tan'gun Cave, with upper and lower sections. There was also Tan'gun Hermitage, but because it was said to be a resting place for the Independence Party (獨立黨), those Japanese policemen (日本巡査) had demolished it randomly. The pitiful sight of rafters and pillars intertwined, tiles and stones <65> resting on each other, thoroughly soured the mood of the evening mountain visitor. After drinking a gourdful of the Jade Spring water that Tan'gun supposedly drank from Tan'gun Well in the lower cave, I stood there pensively, imagining the past and present. A sigh escaped, "The mountain is as before, the cave is as before, but why are the people not as before?" Then, correcting myself, "Wrong! If people were as before, there would be no greater misfortune for humanity than this," I consoled myself. Wandering between the upper and lower caves, contemplating Tan'gun's time and reflecting on our present, fearing the encroaching dusk, I quickly descended the mountain, legs trembling and stomach rumbling. (See previous account of Tan'gundae).

Spending another night at Pohyŏn Temple, recounting the hardships of the journey to Inho[dae], Puryŏng[am], and Tan'gundae, I took leave of Hyangsan early the next morning, June 1st, along with Kwangsŏng's party. We went out to Wŏllim-ni, where Kwangsŏng headed south by boat on the Ch'ŏngch'ŏn River, while I headed north on a mountain road of several hundred *li*. Parting was truly sad. The hunting party on the clear river at Wŏllim with Messrs. Yi Kyŏnghun and Pae Chinyŏng, though brief, was quite enjoyable.

Walking fifty *li* on the Hŭich'ŏn road with Mr. Yi *hun, we arrived at Hŭich'ŏn Town at 7 PM the same day. Old friends were waiting. We conversed about Hyangsan until midnight before retiring to bed, my calves throbbing.

June 2nd (Saturday), clear. This was the scheduled day for the trip to Kanggye. Having anticipated Kanggye for so long, even though the fatigue from the previous day's journey hadn't subsided, I woke up early and bought a bus ticket first. The fare was 14 *wŏn* and some change, and the trunk tax was 3 *wŏn* 90 *chŏn*. Taking a bus is not something one can easily contemplate. However, seduced by the thrill of covering over 300 *li* in 5 or 6 hours, everyone grits their teeth and gets on.

As 9 o'clock approached, the bus started its engine. Just as I was about to board, someone grabbed me, saying, "Let me see you for a moment." When I asked who it was, they said they were from the police station and, although they could have asked right there, insisted I come to the station as if there were some special case. Annoyed, I forced myself to comply, stopped by briefly, and answered a few questions.

The bus departed. Northward, northward, it sped towards distant Kanggye. After traveling for a while, we came upon a large street called Myŏngmun-dong. Backed by a stone mountain where a clear stream flows, over a hundred houses lined the street on both sides; at first glance, it seemed only scorpion (蝎蜅) sellers would profit there. Stopping briefly for inspection by a police officer, we moved on again. We reached the famous Kuhyŏn Pass. This side is Hŭich'ŏn, the other side is Kanggye. This side is the source of the Ch'ŏngch'ŏn River, the other side is the source of the Tongno River. The pass is quite high, but the bus crossed it without too <66> much difficulty. However, danger was ever-present. With many curves and being inherently steep, even the bus strained. "Driver, save us! Our hearts are pounding, we can't stand it!" – these words were truly on everyone's lips. But being full of curiosity, though scared, though realizing life and death hung in the balance each minute, each second, I shifted my buttocks, looked alternately up, down, left, right, and occasionally glanced back. Three or four buses following in a line looked like a spy movie action scene. The people in the rear bus looked exactly like a police squad, and those in the front bus seemed just like villains.

Reaching the summit, I thought it might feel refreshing, but my chest still felt tight. Mountains upon mountains, waters upon waters, the deeper we go into the mountains, what is to be done? From here, we followed the Tongno River downstream. Passing the street of Koin-dong, crossing the Chŏnch'ŏn River, we arrived at Chŏnch'ŏn street. This street is the largest among the mountain towns and a renowned place in Kanggye. With a post office, police station, school, and other official buildings, as well as several hundred private houses, it certainly looked better than some county seats. Resting for about 30 minutes and eating lunch, we set off again, passing Pyŏlga-dong, emerging from the treacherous pass called Hŭgae, and continued straight on until we reached Puji Pass (also called Pulgil Pass), about 30 *li* from Kanggye Town. It was also a steep road and high pass, no less formidable than Kuhyŏn Pass.

Crossing the pass and emerging from the valley entrance, it felt a bit more open. The difficult parts were now all behind us. Only 20 *li* (Korean *li*) of smooth, wide road remained to Kanggye Town. "Drive faster! Blow the horn full blast!" Just as we were speeding along at full throttle, a young man by the roadside raised his hand, stopped the bus, and asked, "Is Mr. Pak Tal-sŏng from Seoul aboard?" He looked like a policeman. "Yes, that's me," I replied, asking who he was. He introduced himself as Yu Hangjun, who had come out from Kanggye Town to meet me. I felt so embarrassed and ashamed; mistaking a newfound friend, who had come out 20 *li* regardless of the distance to welcome me, for a policeman – what else could this be but excessive nervousness? Reproaching myself for my thoughtlessness, I apologized to Mr. Yu for the discourtesy and proceeded directly into town with the moving bus.

Arriving about 3 *li* from the town, about ten people were lined up at a large roadside spot. Wondering who they were, I was about to pass by when I caught sight of the faces of old acquaintances Kim Munbyŏk, Yi Wŏnhaeng, and Kim Tohyŏn. How delighted I was! I jumped off the bus, grabbed their hands, shook them for a while, and then, after introductions by Kim and Yi, exchanged joyful greetings with Messrs. Han Kyŏngha, Yun Ch'angsu, Kang Pyŏngju, Yi Ŭnghwa, and Kim Sehun, who had come far to welcome me. Slowly walking into the town, <67> being my first visit, I felt bewildered and still unfamiliar. Introduced by these gentlemen, I settled myself at the Sŏkchu Inn.

As is customary, the first thing to see is Inp'ung Pavilion. Also, to get a panoramic view of Kanggye Town, it's logical to choose the highest point there for observation, but the West or East Mountains were too far to go immediately. Since Inp'ung Pavilion was said to offer a sufficient view, I headed there first.

Ah, Inp'ung Pavilion! One of the eight scenic spots of the Western Province (西道), Kanggye's representative landmark, Inp'ung Pavilion indeed lives up to its reputation. The pavilion itself isn't particularly praiseworthy, but its location is truly remarkable. Situated atop a cliff at the confluence of the Tongno River (right bank) and the Sŏsŏng River (left bank), it stands loftily. Behind it lies the entire view of the town, before it the green waves of the long river. Furthermore, the fishermen's songs and shepherd's flutes along the Sŏsŏng riverside, the songs of birds and sounds of water from the green banks and lush forests, the talented men and beautiful women on Mangmi Pavilion, the setting sun over Ip-pong Peak, the first moon over Hyang-bong Peak, the evening bell from Chabuk Temple – these are the surroundings of Inp'ung Pavilion. Who would not cite this pavilion when speaking of scenic extremities?

Moving eastward a few hundred yards, I reached a pavilion that seemed like Inp'ung Pavilion's younger sibling, possessing a bit more youthful beauty – this was Mangmi Pavilion, rivaling Inp'ung Pavilion.

"Mr. Pak, let's head back for today and look forward to tomorrow. It's getting dark, and you must be tired..." Following someone's suggestion, I returned to the inn as dusk began to fall. Looking at the dinner table, everything was clean, but the side dishes, simply boiled without much seasoning and served with soy sauce, definitely showed the mountain region style.

After dinner, while lying alone, Messrs. Yi and Kim visited and suggested a walk. As I had come intending to investigate this and that and wander here and there, how could I spend even a moment meaninglessly? A nighttime walk through Kanggye city was originally my wish, so <68> I followed them out. Following the guides through this alley and that alley, then again following the guides, we found a house, took seats, and sat down – it was the room across from a restaurant. Silently, I thought, "Alright, a restaurant... I'll see a bit of Kanggye style. Oh, Kanggye beauties? Might I be able to see them too? What if they don't show them out of propriety?" Asking myself these questions, I rolled my eyes, examining the interior and exterior. It wasn't spotless, but not particularly dirty either. Soon, a beauty entered. Another entered. Yet another entered. Altogether, three beauties came in and sat down nearby. "Kisaeng? Scorpions (蝎蜅; likely slang for lower-class entertainers)? No, in rural towns, there's no distinction between Kisaeng and Scorpions. Right, that's it. What are their looks like, anyway?" I casually observed them; they were neither great beauties nor particularly ugly. They wore no hair ornaments or fancy clothes, just dressed simply like common women, with simple ribbons (자지당기) and skirts and jackets of cotton and hemp – a uniquely characteristic style. When asked, they said the Kisaeng had formed a Saturday Club (土曜會) and were all promoting local products. Regardless of its longevity or whether it was voluntary or forced, it was certainly a commendable effort. The songs were only the 'Sushimga' and 'Nanbongga'; the alcohol was rice wine (눙酒); the side dishes were pork, chicken, and mixed vegetables (雜菜). But it looked a bit dirty.

3rd (Sunday), Clear. After breakfast, guided by Messrs. Kim Mun'gwan and Yu Hang[jun], I visited various government offices and private influential figures, busying myself somewhat to learn about the situation in Kanggye.
Does the title 'County Magistrate' (郡守) inherently mean only guarding the county? Truly, it seems to be just 'guarding' (守直而已); perhaps there's no activity for the sake of the county. Education, industry – those are handled by the Japanese gentlemen, and newspapers, magazines – those are done by the anti-Japanese elements, so it seems enough to just sit guarding a corner of the room like a borrowed sack of barley. In Kanggye Town, with over 90 readers [of Kaebyŏk?] and 30 cultural organizations, a place quite brimming with vitality, for the so-called County Magistrate to ask "What is 'Kaebyŏk'?" – it truly looked pathetic. I looked at his face again. He seemed both detestable and pitiful. Guarding an empty chair just for food and shelter (口腹이 怨讎), content with receiving a few dozen *wŏn*, letting the world unravel or decay, thinking "the mainland gentlemen will handle it as usual" – this must be common among the County Magistrate types. Needless to say. Let that be. On this side, the Independence Party is causing trouble, and on that side, hearing the news that two policemen were killed, I asked about it, but the police side's response of merely "All quiet and safe" (安穩無事) was again only worth a snort of laughter. Though there's talk that Governor-General politics is inherently fraudulent politics, <69> cosmetic politics...

The best impression I got in Kanggye Town was the activity of the young people focused on cultural work. The depth of their perseverance and the duration of their activities are not for a temporary visitor to know, but anyway, just in terms of associations, there are the Cultivation Society (修養會), Athletics Association (軆育會), Local Products Association (物産會), Temperance and Anti-Smoking Society (禁酒斷煙會), Labor Association (勞働會), Education Society (敎育會), Saturday Club (土曜會), etc. – 30 organizations in total (though having many associations might be disadvantageous in some respects). That the young people are all full of vitality and emphasize practical strength created, more than anything, a good feeling. Among them, the most oppressed and resentful are the innocent rural brethren. A certain Mr. X in town, speaking about the local situation – no, the situation between the police and the Independence Party, the Independence Party and the villagers, the villagers and the police – based on what he saw and heard, retracted his own words, covered his mouth, and ended up just sighing deeply and shedding tears. Especially when recounting the final moments of Independence Party member OOO who died in battle in OO-myŏn last early winter, he just struck the ground and stared at the ceiling.
There are many hidden facts, revealed facts, big facts, small facts – much to say and much to appeal, but the sentiment was to swallow it all down. I acknowledged this and didn't try to learn more.

4th (Monday), also clear. I visited various prominent figures in town, and in the afternoon at Kyŏyŏn Pavilion on the mountain behind the town, about 30 Kanggye brothers showed warm affection and provided endless delicious food for their young brother from afar. Being immersed in joyful celebration all day long was a great honor, yet also made me feel ashamed and unworthy.

5th, also clear. To gather more material for articles and perhaps attach more affection to the cherished Kanggye, I stayed another night in Kanggye Town. The kind and thorough guidance of Messrs. Kim Sŏnggil and Yu [Hang]jun was truly appreciated. I had planned to leave for Chasŏng on the 6th without fail, but Heaven, whether out of goodwill or malice, sent down a light rain. For a short distance, I would have gone even in heavy rain, but the 160 *li* between Kanggye and Chasŏng involves truly high passes and long rivers on treacherous mountain roads, so I couldn't dare to even think of starting. "Tomorrow then?" Feeling the delay, I spent five days in Kanggye.

From Kanggye to Chunggang Town

7th (Thursday), Cloudy. Woke up early and checked the weather first; it still looked rainy. But I couldn't not go. Since it's a mountain path, travel by ox or horse is precarious, but nevertheless, I hired a packhorse for ten *wŏn* for luggage and companionship. Mr. Yu Hangjun also became my travel companion. Leaving Kanggye Town at 9 AM, sometimes walking, sometimes riding, heading far, far north, crossing mountains and waters, entering deep mountains and secluded valleys, some places felt desolate, others inspired fear. <70> However, amidst the white stones and clear streams, birdsong and water sounds, strange rocks and bizarre stones, dense trees and lush forests, vast mountains and long valleys – the thrill of striding through it all, holding a staff, unhindered, even when I looked at myself, didn't seem base. Asking the horseman about village tales and mountain stories, listening while slowly walking, resting, then walking again – this was calm and pleasant, more so than a train or bus. Ascending the famously rugged, remote, and frightening P'aryŏng Pass, the clouds that had been lazily drifting since morning gradually turned gray, eventually becoming rain that started falling steadily. Having no rain gear, I got caught in a downpour mid-journey. Reaching the foot of the pass, there happened to be a traveler's inn. To take shelter from the rain, have lunch, and also wait for Mr. Yu following behind on a bicycle, I went inside and ordered lunch. The rain lessened slightly, but the clouds remained dark gray. After much pleading with the owner, I bought someone's old umbrella for 3 *wŏn*. Soon, lunch was ready, and the rain stopped, so we departed immediately. By chance, we met an elementary school student coming from Kanggye Town and crossed P'aryŏng Pass together. Hearing that two days earlier on the pass, two Chinese men had murdered one of their companions (also Chinese) and fled after stealing money made it feel even more terrifying. Moreover, the forest was dense, visibility was less than a foot, clouds and rain intensified, making it impossible to open one's eyes, and the pass was high and steep – truly, my heart pounded, and my hair stood on end. It felt like even a rabbit hopping by could make me fall.

Amidst such fear, climbing to the middle section of the pass, the sound of horse bells rang out loudly, and 5 or 6 postal and packhorses coming over from the Chasŏng direction stood in a line on the path. Passing them and climbing further, more horsemen came over. Asking why there were so many horsemen, I was told that all goods and mail transport between Kanggye and Chasŏng relied entirely on horseback, so it was like this every day.

Crossing the pass and emerging from the long valley, a somewhat refreshing plain came into view. Large tiled-roof villages could be seen, as well as fields stretching over ten *li*. Asking, I learned this place was the renowned Isŏ-myŏn, Chongp'o Town, associated with Kanggye. Hearing that it was the birthplace of Ms. Kim Sŏn, whom I had met once in Seoul, engendered a somewhat fonder feeling. About 5 *li* further from Chongp'o Town, we stayed overnight at the house of Pak Yŏngbin in Hamhŭng-dong; this house belonged to the elementary school student who had accompanied us from the other side of P'aryŏng Pass. The student was so bright and well-behaved that I had arranged to stay at his esteemed home earlier. Mr. Yu, who had left Kanggye 2-3 hours after us and followed by bicycle, now arrived, panting. After coming 90 *li* through treacherous roads in the rain, I felt quite tired. How delicious the rice tasted! And the top-knotted host, despite saying his livelihood was difficult, sending all three of his sons <71> to school was truly astonishing. How glad I was! I wanted to grab him and embrace him, calling him "Big Brother!" Perhaps because of that, among the guest meals of over 20 days, the rice at this house tasted the best.

The next day, the 8th, the weather cleared. We left early. Chasŏng Town was eighty *li* from here, requiring crossing two major passes, Majŏn Pass and Chajak Pass.
Majŏn Pass is the dividing ridge between Kanggye and Chasŏng; the Kanggye side is not very high, but the Chasŏng side is considerably high. It rivals P'aryŏng Pass but is not as rugged. We had lunch at Inp'ung-dong (formerly Chungyŏng), a famous spot in Chasŏng, crossed Chajak Pass in one go, and entered Chasŏng Town at 5 PM. I was deeply moved by the Chasŏng gentlemen who came out 5 *li* from the town to meet me. Lodging at the house of Sŏ Pyŏngguk in town, I immediately met Messrs. Kim Ponggi, Kim Munbong, and Yi Kyŏngsŏn, and under their guidance, climbed the South Mountain of the town to view the entire townscape.

The town proper consists of barely a hundred households. Since the town's layout is said to be only 55 years old, what could have developed? Moreover, it's deep in the mountains... so there's not much to see. The only thing worth seeing is the color of the Chasŏng River winding around the northeast side of the town down to the Yalu River. However, the feeling of quietness, cleanliness, warmth, and like a flower bud about to bloom arises from the mountains and waters here, and the local customs and sentiments. Because these are mountains and rivers yet to be developed, people yet to be refined, existing in their natural state, their original nature. That's how I think of it.

Staying one night in the town, gathering necessary information, I left on the 10th amidst rain, heading towards Chunggang Town, also in Chasŏng territory and a central point on the Yalu River bank. From this point, besides Mr. Yu from Kanggye, Mr. Kim Munbong from Chasŏng also became a companion. Following the Chasŏng River down for 40 *li* from Chasŏng Town, we stayed one night at the mouth of the Chasŏng River on the Yalu bank (also called Pŏp-tong). The next day, the 11th, also enduring heavy rain, the three of us walked towards Chunggang Town. We left our luggage at the mouth of the Chasŏng River.

From the mouth of the Chasŏng River to Chunggang Town is 120 *li*, continuously going up along the Yalu River bank. It requires crossing and circling three major passes – P'imok Pass, Choryul Pass, Ch'asu Pass – and two major slopes – Songdŏk Slope, T'osŏng Slope – so it was also tough. With heavy rain falling as well, the difficulty was beyond words. However, we arrived safely and cheerfully.

Chunggang Town, being a key fortress on the border and the central point of the Yalu River, I imagined it to be quite prosperous. But it's primarily a place targeted by the Japanese for political and industrial purposes related to managing Southern Manchuria. It can be said to have no particular relevance to Koreans. At the entrance of Chunggang-dong, about a hundred Korean households reside, mostly engaged in farming or earning a living by working for foreigners. <72> More than 9 out of 10 residents are Japanese, centered around commerce or forestry operations, including loggers and other officials and clerks.

We found lodging at the house of a fellow Korean situated in the Japanese village, crossed the river to glimpse the town of Moer'shan (Linjiang County), and left Chunggang Town the next day, the 13th. I only went because it's called a border fortress; there was nothing else gained. There are said to be some strange rocks and historical sites on the 70-*li* plateau atop Usu Mountain, but due to boat schedules, I couldn't see them.

From Chunggang Town to Ŭiju

13th (Wednesday), Clear. At 6 AM, our party boarded a steamship named Piryong-maru. It's a scheduled vessel, and missing it means waiting at least a week for the next boat, so we were especially determined to leave that day. With China on the right and Korea on the left along the thousand-*li* long river, the steamship sounded its horn; it was fast and exhilarating. Countless rafts flowed downstream constantly, and Chinese merchant ships were docked at every port, but for Koreans, neither rafts nor merchant ships were visible. Even the few wooden plank boats (나무궤통 木船) they had were allegedly confiscated and destroyed by the police, claiming they were used for Independence Party movements. Speaking of the Independence Party, the Yalu River may be Korea's river, but it is also the river of Korean resentment. Hearing from people along the riverbank, it is said to have claimed countless Korean lives, guilty or innocent. Bodies bound with ropes, bodies with broken limbs, clothed bodies, naked bodies – they float downstream constantly. Ah, how cruel a thing this is! During the ice-thaw season, 5, 6, or even 10 bodies drift into each port, they say. Where else among humans could such a ghastly tragedy exist? People don't speak of it, but... The living conditions of the Chinese on the right bank and the Koreans on the left bank look similar in intangible ways. Only the blue clothes and white clothes differ. <73>

Parting ways with Mr. Kim Munbong at the mouth of the Chasŏng River, retrieving the luggage we had left there, we arrived at Manp'o Town at 4 PM the same day and disembarked. Manp'o Town is not just a famous place in Kanggye but also a renowned spot along the river, targeted by the Japanese. A second-class road from Kanggye comes here, and there's also bus service to this town of over a hundred households. It has traditionally been a place where military commanders (僉使) resided, and Manp'o's Se'gŏm Pavilion is reputed to be a scenic wonder of the world. Finding lodging at the home of Kim Pongsŏk in town, I visited Dr. Kim Sŭngsu of the Inae Clinic, and under his guidance, viewed the town and Se'gŏm Pavilion.
Se'gŏm Pavilion is a lofty structure standing solitarily atop a several hundred-*chang* cliff on the bank of the Yalu River. It is now occupied by the garrison (守備隊). Normally, viewing is not permitted, but perhaps due to Mr. Kim's influence or special treatment for a journalist, the garrison commander, Mr. Chang Ch'ŏn'gu (?), kindly provided guidance.

Although I should have left the next day, there was no boat service, and even the usually plentiful rafts were absent, so I inevitably stayed one more day. During this time, I visited the local Posŏng School and conversed with teachers Yang Hŭije and Kim Sŏkhong. Then, on the 16th, I boarded a raft heading for Ch'osan. Here, I also parted ways with Mr. Yu. <74>

This was my first time riding a raft (떼). Having no fare was economically advantageous, and freely moving about on the wide raft allowed for easy sightseeing, but it was certainly dangerous. Floating downstream is naturally easy, but encountering rapids or rocks is truly liver-chilling. When the raft reaches rapids and the front dips down while the rear lifts up, one feels as if ascending to the clouds, head tingling. When the front lifts up and the rear dips down, one feels as if entering the Dragon Palace, the pit of the stomach prickling. Experiencing this dozens, hundreds of times along the thousand-*li* river – what point is there in describing the hardship? But once experienced, it is exhilarating.

Leaving Manp'o, about 10 *li* away on the Chinese bank at Pisŏk-ri, the tomb and stele of King Kwanggaet'o are said to exist, but landing on the Chinese bank was utterly impossible, so what could be done? I could only gaze longingly and recall ancient history.

The raft only went as far as Kosan Town, so I had to disembark there, spend another day at public expense, and only on the 17th did I find a raft going to Ch'osan, arriving safely at Ch'osan's Sindo-jang. The pass called Razu (岩子; Amja) between Kosan and Ch'osan, the greatest obstacle on the Yalu, made my eyes widen like saucers, thinking I might become fish food.

Entering Ch'osan Town, I stated my purpose and, guided by Mr. Chang Ch'ŏnsŭng, met with local officials, public figures, and influential people while viewing the town's panorama, spending two pleasant days.
It happened to be the Tano Festival (端午日), so watching the splendid attire of Ch'osan women and the vigorous wrestling games (脚戱) of Ch'osan men helped me forget the hardships of travel. Seeing a teacher from the Public Common School (公普訓導) take first place in wrestling and a court clerk (裁判所 書記) take second, both donating their prize money to the Imunhoe society, and as a gesture of thanks, the citizens parading through the town with a band, I praised them, saying, "Wow, quite impressive for a mountain town!" <75>

Hearing about the situation in Ch'osan from Mr. Ham Ilgyu, drinking fragrant cups offered by the citizens' goodwill at Ch'osan's scenic spot, Yŏngho Pavilion, and enjoying the citizens' choir competition, pressed by the need to return, I bid farewell to Ch'osan Town at 7 PM on the 19th.

Spending one night with Mr. Chang Ch'ŏnsŭng at Sindo-jang on the riverbank, from here I traveled by Korean boat via Pyŏktong to Ch'angsŏng, then took a bus from Ch'angsŏng to Sakchu, and another bus to Ŭiju. It felt like I had survived and returned to my hometown – refreshing yet somewhat sad. Visiting T'onggun Pavilion, from which I had parted a month earlier, asking after its well-being, and getting drunk on the comforting wine offered by church friends Ch'oe An'guk, Kim Cha'il, Kim Tŭkp'il, and Hwang Hasik, I stretched out my legs atop T'onggun Pavilion. White clouds drifted by, a clear breeze blew gently – past events seemed like a dream. (End)

Postscript: Due to the inherently long distance, the travelogue is disjointed. There really is a lack of page space. Even after omitting much in the middle, it has become this long. In particular, the accounts of Pyŏktong, Ch'angsŏng, and Sakchu had to be entirely deleted, for which I feel extremely apologetic. Please do not take it as personal bias, but view it with tolerance. <76>

**Summary**

This travelogue by "Ch'unp'a" recounts a journey undertaken in the summer of 1923, starting from the scenic Myohyangsan in north-central Korea, heading north to Kanggye, further north to the border town of Chasŏng, then to Chunggang-jin on the Yalu River, and finally downstream along the Yalu via Manp'o and Ch'osan to Ŭiju. The author vividly describes the natural beauty, the hardships of travel on foot, bus, and raft, interactions with locals (including intellectuals, officials, police, and common people), and provides glimpses into the social and political atmosphere of these remote northern regions under Japanese colonial rule, noting the presence of independence activists, Japanese police actions, local development efforts, and the stark realities faced by Koreans living along the sensitive border with China.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its firsthand account of travel conditions and infrastructure (or lack thereof) in northern Korea during the early 1920s. It offers insights into the Japanese colonial administration's presence and methods (surveillance, suppression, focus on resource exploitation like forestry), the activities and perceived threat of the Independence Movement (獨立黨) in the border regions, the cultural life and aspirations of local communities (e.g., cultural societies in Kanggye, local pride in Ch'osan), and the palpable tension and suffering experienced by Koreans, particularly along the Yalu River, described as a "river of resentment" due to violence against civilians. The author's candid observations about officials, police interactions, and the hushed conversations about sensitive political matters provide a revealing snapshot of colonial society.

前號에 天下絶景-妙香山 寅虎臺 이약이를 하다가 紙面의 關係로 中斷하고 마럿슴니다. 이제 다시 寅虎臺로부터 산 거듭 물 거듭 國境千里에 나아가 봅시다.

寅虎臺로부터 江界邑까지
鐵索을 붓잡고 艱辛히 기여 올나서면 이가 곳 香山의 제1景 寅虎臺이다. 臺上은 百人可坐의 大盤石인데 그 우에 老松 4,5株가 亭亭히 섯고 臺下는 斷崖千丈인데 굽어봄애 앗질하야  精神일켓다. 게다가 上院庵 좌편 百丈 急崖로부터 千尺 龍湫瀑이 구을너 떠러지고 우편 千尺 絶壁으로부터는 百丈散珠瀑이 直下하니 眞可謂 좌우 銀河落九天이다. 銀珠를 뿌리는 듯 玉屑을 헤치는 듯 烟波가 飛散하고 龍鱗이 번득이며 彩虹이 어리엿다. 山極致 水極致 言書俱絶의 此地此臺에 또한 上院妙刹이 좌우 銀河를 끼고 天心長瀑을 이고 臺의 上面에 飄然히 소사 안저스니 此가 別天地가 안이고 무엇이랴. 天上인지 瑤臺인지 취한 듯 꿈인 듯 心身이 恍惚하야 엇덜지를 몰나섯다.
上院에 점심 식키고 일행 30餘名이 臺上臺下로 任去來하면서 下淵에 濯足 上流에 洗面 혹은 꼿을 따며 혹은 가지를 꺽그니 去自由 來自由에 그만 身이 化하야 京臺人이 된 듯 塵界를 頓忘에 도라갈 바를 아지 못하겟다. 게다가 또한 異說이 잇고 奇蹟이 잇스니 彼를 聞할가 此를 見할가 手忙足奔에 二兎追從格이 되고만다. 나는 玄賓大師-龍宮去來說을 聞하는데 彼는 「神仙窟宅 雲霞洞天」의 岩面 大額字를 발견하고 왁자자 떠들며 나는 龍卵取來說에 의하야 卵形의 龍角石을 구경하는데 彼는 金剛門을 출입하면서 俗客流의 刻字評으로 날이 기울 물아지 못한다. 이윽고 점심을 報하니 때는 오후 1시요 水者 岩者 木者 花者- 一室에 모여 肉*을 만족식켯다.＜64＞

6월2일(土曜) 晴. 이 날은 江界行 예정일이다. 두고두고 江界 江界하든 판이라 전날의 路困이 풀니지 안치만 일즉 이러나 자동차 표부터 삿다. 차비가 14圓角數요 도랑크稅가 3원 90전이나 된다. 여간해서는 자동차 행은 念頭도 못내겟다. 그러나 300여리를 5,6時間으로써 돌파하는 멋에 누구든지 껑껑 알으면서도 타게된다.
九9시9시가 되자 자동차는 고동을 틀기 시작한다. 차에 막 오르려 하는데 엇던 者가 「좀- 보입시다」하고 붓잡는다. 누구냐 하닛가 경찰서라고 하면서 당장에 무러도 됴흘 것을 별 사건이나 잇는 듯이 署까지 가자고 한다. 심사가 불둑거리나 억지로 참고 잠간 들녀 두어 마디 대답해 주엇다.
자동차는 떠낫다. 北으로 北으로 저 멀니 江界를 향하야 다라난다. 한참 가노라니 明文洞이란 큰 거리가 잇다. 청천이 흐르는 곳에 石山을 등지고 백餘戶가 좌우로 거리를 지어스니 얼핏 보아도 蝎蜅장사나 이로울 듯이 보인다. 잠간 停車하야 警官의 點考를 맛더니 또 간다. 彼- 유명한 狗峴嶺을 當着하얏다. 이 편은 熙川 저 편은 江界. 이 편은 淸川江 발원지 저 편은 禿魯江 발원지. 嶺은 꽤 놉흔 嶺인데 자동차는 그닥 어럽지안케＜66＞ 넘어간다. 그러나 위험은 毋論이다. 屈曲이 만코 원체 急坂인지라 자동차도 씩얼씩얼 한다. 「운전수야 사람 살녀라 가슴 죄여 못살겟다」 소리가 사람 사람의 입에는 다 담기여슴이 사실이다. 그러나 호기심 만흔 나는 무서우면서도 生死가 일분 일초에 달녓거니 하면서도 엉등이를 들먹거리며 상하 좌우를 번가라 살피이며 간혹 뒤도 도라보앗다. 자동차 3,4臺가 一路에 追從하는 것이 偵探劇 活動寫眞갓다. 뒷 차에 탄 놈은 말금 警官隊갓고 압 차에 탄 놈은 말곰 惡漢가튼 생각이 난다.
嶺上에 올나서면 좀-시원할가 하얏더니 역시 가슴이 답답하다. 山疊疊 水重重 去益深山인데 奈何오. 여기서부터는 禿魯江을 끼고 내내 내려가게 된다. 발서 古仁洞 거리를 지나 前川江을 건녀 前川 거리에 왓다. 이 거리는 山邑중 가장 큰 거리로 江界의 名高한 곳이다. 우편국 경찰서 학교 등 官公舍 또는 民家 수백 여호가 잇서 엇던 읍내보다는 확실히 나어보인다. 약 30分間 휴식하면서 점심을 먹고 또 떠나서 別加洞을 지내 黑崖라는 險口를 버서나 그냥 죽- 가서 江界邑을 距하기 약 30리에 잇는 釜池嶺(一名 不吉嶺)을 넘게 되얏다. 역시 狗峴嶺만 못하지 안은 急路이요 高嶺이다.

十131313日(水曜) 晴. 상오 6시에 우리 일행은 飛龍丸이란 蒸氣船을 탓다. 定期船인지라 이 날을 놋치면 少하야도 일주일을 지내야 船便이 잇다 하닛가 더욱 이 날은 떠나게 되얏다. 右 中國 左 朝鮮 千里長江에 汽船이 고동을 울리니 빠르기도 하려니와 쾌하기도 하다. 무수한 筏木은 無時로 流下하고 中人의 商船은 포구포구에 떠 잇는데 오직 朝鮮人은 筏木도 商船도 볼 수가 업고 간혹 잇든 나무궤통 木船 그것조차 獨立黨 往來物이라 하야 警官이 몰수하야 消盡하고 마럿다 한다. 獨立黨 말이 낫스니 말이지 鴨綠江은 朝鮮의 강이나 그러나 朝鮮人의 怨讎의 강이다. 강변 인사에게 드르니 無罪有罪간 朝鮮人의 생명을 수업시 奪取하얏다 한다. 縳繩진 시체 手足 부러진 시체 옷 입은 시체 빨가버슨 시체 무시로 떠나려 간다하니 아- 얼마나 慘酷한 일인가. 解氷期는 5,6명 10여명 시체가 포구마다 밀려들어온다 하니 인간에 如此한 지독한 慘狀이 어대 또 잇슬가. 말을 안이 한다마는... 右岸의 中國人의 사는 꼴이나 左岸의 朝鮮人의 사는 꼴은 무형으로 보아 비슷비슷하다. 靑衣와 白衣가 다를 뿐.＜73＞ 慈城江口에서 金文鳳씨를 작별하고 任置하얏든 行具를 차자싯고 同 오후 4시에 滿浦鎭에 와서 下船하얏다. 滿浦鎭은 江界의 名地 뿐 안이라 강변의 名地로 日本이 着目한 곳이라. 江界서 제 2等路가 들어와 자동차의 편도 잇는 百餘戶 市街이다. 자고로 僉使를 살리든 곳으로 滿浦의 洗劍亭하면 천하의 절경이라고 떠드는 터이다. 시내 金奉碩家에 숙소를 정하고 仁愛醫院 金勝秀氏를 차자 氏의 안내로 市街 及 洗劍亭을 보앗다.
洗劍亭은 鴨江 畔數百丈 絶壁上에 兀然히 놉흔 樓亭인데 지금은 守備隊 所占地이다. 보통으로 구경을 안이 許한다하나 金氏의 眼目인지 記者의 特待인지 守備隊 長千口씨는 친절한 안내를 준다.
翌日은 곳 떠나야 겟지만 船便도 업고 그-만튼 筏流도 업슴애 할 수 업시 1일을 더 留하면서 當地 普成學校를 訪하야 敎員 楊喜濟
金錫鴻 諸氏와 交하다가 翌 16日은 筏木을 타고 楚山을 향하니 여긔서 兪氏와 또한 分手가 되얏다.＜74＞
떼를 타보기는 이번이 처음이다. 賃金이 업스니 경제상 이익이요 널다란 筏編에 任去來하니 구경이 자유이나 엇잿든 위험하다. 順流야 毋論 편하지만 急流나 혹 岩石을 當할 때는 실로 肝이 마를 일이다. 떼가 급류에 다다러 先頭가 쑥 들어가고 後尾가 들석 올나갈 때는 사람은 완연히 雲霄에 올으는 듯 머리가 옷삭해지고 先頭가 들석 들리우고 後尾가 쑥 들어갈 때는 사람은 완연히 龍宮으로 들어가는 듯 밋구멍이 자릿자릿해 진다. 이러케 하기를 千里長江에 수십 수백번이니 그- 고생이야 말해 무엇하리. 그러나 격고 나면 快하다.
滿浦를 떠나 10리許의 中國岸 碑石里에 廣開土王의 陵及 碑가 잇다하나 中國岸에 下陸키가 도저히 불능하게 되니 엇지하랴. 그저- 瞻望己久에 古事를 추억할 뿐이엿다.
떼가 高山鎭까지 오고 마니 不得己 高山鎭에 下陸하얏다가 또 1일을 公費하고 17일에야 楚山行 筏木을 만나서 楚山의 新島場까지 무사히 왓다. 高山 楚山간의 관문 라즈(岩子)란 鴨江中 최대 難關인바 아조 고기밥이 되나보아 눈이 王砂鉢 만해젓섯다.
楚山邑에 들어 來意를 告하고 張天承씨의 안내로 읍의 全景及 官公有志를 相面하면서 2일을 快하게 지내섯다.
맛츰 이 때가 端午日이라 楚山婦女의 燦爛한 치장과 楚山人士의 壯快한 脚戱를 구경하면서 客苦를 잇게 되얏다. 脚戱에 公普訓導가 일등을 밧고 裁判所 書記가 이등을 하야 2인이 共히 상품으로써 以文會에 기부하야 그의 謝禮로 시민이 樂隊行列로써 市中을 一周함을 보고 「야- 山邑하고는 그럴듯하다」 고 稱揚하얏섯다. ＜75＞
咸一奎씨에게 楚山狀況을 듯고 楚山名勝 映湖亭에서 市民有志의 정으로 주는 香盃를 마시고 시민 성악대회를 滋味잇게 구경하다가 歸路의 促忙으로 19일 오후 7시에 楚山邑을 作別하얏다.
張天承씨와 가티 강변 新島場에서 一泊을 하고 이제부터는 朝鮮船便으로 碧潼을 거처 昌城까지 와서 昌城부터 자동차로 朔州에 왓다가 또 자동차로 義州에 오니 사라난 듯 鄕原에 온 듯 시원하고도 섭섭하얏다. 한 달 전에 작별한 統軍亭을 차자 안부를 뭇고 敎友 崔安國
