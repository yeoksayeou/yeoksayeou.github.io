필자	金素月
기사형태	시 (Poem)

개(渚)여울
						金素月

당신은 무슨일로
그리합닛가,
홀로 개여울에 주저안저서.

파릇한 풀포기가
돗아나오고,
잔물은 봄바람에 해적일때에.

가긴 가도 아주 가지는
안켓노라는
그러한 約束이 잇섯겟지오.

날마다 개여울에
나와안저서
하욤업시 무엇을 닛자 합니다.

가긴 가도 아주 가지는＜147＞
안켓노라-ㅁ이
굿게굿게 닛지말나는 부탁이지요.＜148＞

**English Translation**

Riverside Shoal (개여울)
						Kim So-wŏl

For what reason
Do you do that,
Sitting alone sunken down at the riverside shoal?

When the green clumps of grass
Sprout forth,
And the ripples stir in the spring breeze.

"Though I go, I won't leave
entirely,"
There must have been such a promise (約束), right?

Every day at the riverside shoal,
I come out and sit,
Trying endlessly to forget something.

"Though I go, I won't leave entirely"<147>
That promise—
Is it a plea to firmly, firmly not forget? <148>

**Summary**

The poem depicts a speaker observing someone sitting forlornly by a riverside shoal (개여울) every day as spring arrives. The speaker reflects on a promise made by someone who left – a promise not to leave entirely. The person by the shoal seems to be trying to forget something related to this departure, yet the speaker muses that the promise itself might actually be a request *not* to be forgotten. It evokes themes of longing, waiting, memory, and the pain of separation. From a historical perspective, Kim So-wŏl's work, especially from this period and published in a nationalist magazine like *Gaebyeok*, often resonates with the collective sorrow (*han*) and sense of loss experienced during the Japanese colonial occupation. Historians might find the poem interesting as an expression of personal grief that could also be interpreted allegorically, reflecting the national longing for return, restoration, or the keeping of promises in a time of uncertainty and oppression.
