진달내꼿 (民謠詩)
						金素月
나보기가 역겨워
가실때에는 말업시
고히고히 보내들이우리다.

寧邊엔 藥山
그 진달내꼿을
한아름 따다 가실길에 뿌리우리다.

가시는길 발거름마다
뿌려노흔 그꼿을
고히나 즈러밟고 가시옵소서.

나보기가 역겨워
가실 때에는 ＜146＞
죽어도 아니, 눈물흘니우리다. ＜147＞

Azaleas (Folk Song Poem)
						Kim Sowŏl

When you leave,
Weary of seeing me,
Without a word, gently, gently I shall let you go.

In Nyŏngbyŏn, on Yaksan mountain (藥山),
Those azaleas,
I will gather an armful and scatter them on your path.

Step by step on the path you tread,
Upon the flowers laid before you,
Gently tread and go.

When you leave,
Weary of seeing me, <146>
Though I die, no, I shall not shed tears. <147>

**Summary**

This famous poem by Kim Sowŏl depicts a speaker addressing a lover who is leaving because they have grown tired of the speaker. The speaker promises a quiet, dignified farewell, suppressing personal sorrow ("Though I die, no, I shall not shed tears") and instead offering a blessing for the departing lover's journey by scattering azaleas gathered from Yaksan mountain in Nyŏngbyŏn. The poem beautifully captures the Korean sentiment of *han*—a complex feeling of sorrow, resentment, and acceptance—often associated with separation and loss. From a historical perspective, published in the nationalist magazine *Kaebyŏk* during the Japanese colonial period, the poem's theme of painful separation and suppressed grief, framed in a traditional folk-song style, could be interpreted by historians as an allegory for the nation's suffering and loss of sovereignty, expressing deep sorrow while maintaining dignity in the face of overwhelming circumstances.
