From 「Leaves of Grass」(草葉集)

By Whitman, Translated by Kim Sŏksong (金石松)

"Walt Whitman!" I cannot help but call his name thus with admiration, revering and worshipping him. This is because his poetry is less a poetry of beauty than a poetry of power. He is indeed a poet "as broad and strong as Nature." He is a thorough prophet, seer, and guide, a poet for the future, and a naked apostle who believed firmly in the truth of humanity's upward progress and mutual prosperity.

I trust there will be an opportunity to introduce his life story later and will omit it for now. Taking up my translation pen for the first time, I am glad to be introducing the poetry of Whitman, whom I admire, first and foremost, yet at the same time, I hesitated greatly and also exerted much effort. This is because I originally have no knowledge of English, and there are two or three Japanese translations available. However—since translating poetry (though other things are like this too) is inherently more difficult than creative writing—not only do the translated meanings often differ from the original text, but even among the Japanese translations, there are many completely contradictory interpretations. Consequently, struggling with the original text felt, in retrospect, like going beyond hesitation. However, considering that this is the first introduction of Whitman in Chosŏn (朝鮮), I send this to the readers with the confidence that even if there are errors, not only the readers but also the original author Whitman himself would forgive with a smile from the underworld. Finally, to give a very brief introduction of Whitman:

He was born on May 31, 1819, in Long Island, New York State, USA. Having barely finished elementary school, he became a typesetter in a printing shop at age 13. At 20, he published a weekly miscellany mainly featuring his own poems. From then on, he began working as a contributor, magazine reporter, and speaker in New York, making it impossible to explain his life without mentioning printing, newspapers, magazines, poetry writing, travel, and so on. His later years were very peaceful; for 10 years from 1882, he lived a tranquil life with nature and reading. On March 26, 1892, he ended his persistent 73-year life.

Thus, the vitality of Whitman's poetry lies in the fact that he was born a commoner, sang of commoners throughout his seventy-odd years, and died a commoner. His collection of poems began with the publication of the first volume of "Leaves of Grass" (草葉集) in 1855, and the complete works were published in 1860. Regarding his poetry, rather than me offering separate explanations or introductions, readers will be able to judge for themselves from the several clumsy translations recorded below. I merely wish to call him the "Pioneer of Democratic Poetry"—a "bold free-verse poet" who recklessly disregarded conventional rules. <19>

Pioneers! O Pioneers!

Come my tan-faced children,
Follow well in order, get your weapons ready,
Have you your pistols? have you your sharp-edged axes?
Pioneers! O pioneers!

For we cannot tarry here,
We must march my darlings, we must bear the brunt of danger,
We the youthful sinewy races, all the rest on us depend,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

O you youths, Western youths,
So impatient, full of action, full of manly pride and friendship,
Plain I see you Western youths, see you tramping with the foremost,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Have the elder races halted?
Do they droop and end their lesson, wearied over there beyond the seas? <20>
We take up the task eternal, and the burden and the lesson,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

All the past we leave behind,
We debouch upon a newer mightier world, varied world,
Fresh and strong the world we seize, world of labor and the march,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

We detachments steady throwing,
Down the edges, through the passes, up the mountains steep,
Conquering, holding, daring, venturing as we go the unknown ways,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

We primeval forests felling,
We the rivers stemming, vexing we and piercing deep the mines within,
We the surface broad surveying, we the virgin soil upheaving,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Colorado men are we,
From the peaks gigantic, from the great sierras and the high plateaus,
From the mine and from the gully, from the hunting trail we come,
Pioneers! O pioneers! <21>

From Nebraska, from Arkansas,
Central inland race are we, from Missouri stretching, traversing,
From the North, from the South, the comrades links commanding,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

O resistless restless race!
O beloved race in all! O my breast aches with tender love for all!
O I mourn and yet exult, I am rapt with love for all!
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Raise the mighty mother mistress,
Waving high the delicate mistress, over all the starry mistress,
(Bend your heads all)
Raise the fang'd and warlike mistress, stern, impassive, weapon'd mistress,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

See my children, resolute children,
By those swarms upon our rear we must never yield or falter,
Ages back in ghostly millions frowning there behind us urging,
Pioneers! O pioneers! <22>

On and on the compact ranks,
With accessions ever waiting, with the places of the dead quickly fill'd,
Through the battle, through defeat, moving yet and never stopping,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

O to die advancing on!
Are there some of us to droop and die? has the hour come?
Then upon the march we fittest die, soon and sure the gap is fill'd.
Pioneers! O pioneers!

All the pulses of the world,
Falling in they beat for us, with the Western movement beat,
Holding single or together, steady moving to the front, all for us,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Life's involv'd and varied pageants,
All the forms and shows, all the workmen at their work,
All the seamen and the landsmen, all the masters with their slaves,
Pioneers! O pioneers! <23>

All the hapless silent lovers,
All the prisoners in the jails, all the righteous and the wicked,
All the joyous, all the sorrowing, all the living, all the dying,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

I too with my soul and body,
We, a curious trio, picking, wandering on our way,
Through these shores amid the shadows, with the apparitions pressing,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Lo, the darting bowling orb!
Lo, the brother orbs around, all the clustering suns and planets,
All the dazzling days, all the mystic nights with dreams,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

These are of us, they are with us,
All for primal needed work, while the followers there in embryo wait behind,
We today's procession heading, we the route for travel clearing,
Pioneers! O pioneers! <24>

O you daughters of the West!
O you young and elder daughters! O you mothers and you wives!
Never must you be divided, in our ranks you move united,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Minstrels latent on the prairies!
(Shrouded bards of other lands, you may rest, you have done your work),
Soon I hear you coming warbling, soon you rise and tramp amid us,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Not for delectations sweet,
Not the cushion and the slipper, not the peaceful and the studious,
Not the riches safe and palling, not for us the tame enjoyment,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Do the feasters gluttonous feast?
Do the corpulent sleepers sleep? Have they lock'd and bolted doors?
Still be ours the diet hard, and the blanket on the ground,
Pioneers! O pioneers! <25>

Has the night descended?
Was the road of late so toilsome? did we stop discouraged nodding on our way?
Yet a passing hour I yield you in your tracks to pause oblivious,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

Till with sound of trumpet,
Far, far off the daybreak call—hark! how loud and clear I hear it wind,
Swift! to the head of the army!--swift! spring to your places,
Pioneers! O pioneers!

As I Watch'd the Plowman Plowing
As I watch'd the plowman plowing,
Or the sower sowing in the fields—or the harvester harvesting,
I saw there too, O life and death, your analogies,
(Life, life is the tillage, and death is the harvest according.)

This Moment Yearning and Thoughtful
Alone, seated, this moment yearning and thoughtful,
It seems to me there are other men in other lands yearning and thoughtful, <26>
It seems to me I can look over and behold them in Germany, Italy, France, Spain—or far, far away, in China, or India or Russia—talking other dialects,
And it seems to me if I could know those men I should become attached to them as I do to men in my own lands,
O I know we should be brethren and lovers,
I know I should be happy with them.

Poets to Come
Poets to come! Orators, singers, musicians to come!
Not today is to justify me and answer what I am for,
But you, a new brood, native, athletic, continental, greater than before known,
Arouse! arouse—for you must justify me—you must answer.
I myself but write one or two indicative words for the future,
I but advance a moment only to wheel and hurry back in the darkness.

I am a man who, sauntering along without fully stopping, turns a casual look upon you and then averts his face,
Leaving it to you to prove and define it,
Expecting the main things from you. <27>

To a Common Prostitute
Be composed—be at ease with me—I am Walt Whitman, liberal and lusty as Nature,
Not till the sun excludes you do I exclude you,
Not till the waters refuse to glisten for you and the leaves to rustle for you, do my words refuse to glisten and rustle for you.

My girl, I appoint with you an appointment—and I charge you that you make preparation to be worthy to meet me,
And I charge you that you be patient and perfect till I come.

Till then I salute you with a significant look that you do not forget me.

Masks
Masks—a female's real nature perpetual trick,—
Masks, Masks, Masks, her face, her form, her body—
Changing and metamorphosing every moment
Even when she sleeps, the mask is worn. <28>
<19-28>

**Summary**

This article from the July 1922 issue of *Kaebyŏk* introduces the American poet Walt Whitman and his seminal work "Leaves of Grass" to a Korean audience. The translator, Kim Sŏksong, expresses profound admiration for Whitman, emphasizing the "power" of his poetry, his role as a democratic prophet, and his commoner origins. After a brief biography, Kim presents his translations of several Whitman poems, including "Pioneers! O Pioneers!", "As I Watch'd the Plowman Plowing," "This Moment Yearning and Thoughtful," "Poets to Come," "To a Common Prostitute," and "Masks." Kim acknowledges his lack of English and reliance on Japanese translations, asking for the reader's indulgence. From a historical perspective, this article is significant as an early introduction of a major Western democratic poet in colonial Korea. The choice of Whitman, with his themes of pioneering, progress, democracy, individualism, and the common man, likely resonated strongly with Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule who were seeking inspiration for national identity and advancement. Kim Sŏksong's emphasis on "power" and the "pioneering spirit," especially through the extensive translation of "Pioneers! O Pioneers!", suggests a deliberate effort to instill a sense of agency and forward movement among the Korean readership during a period of oppression.

「윌트·휘트맨」!. 나는 이러케 感嘆的으로 그의 이름을 부르지 아니할 수 업시 그를 敬仰하고 崇拜한다. 함은 그의 詩가 美의 詩인 것보다도, 力의 詩인 까닥이다. 그는 果然 「自然과 가티 寬大하고 强壯한」 詩人이다. 그는 透徹한 豫言者요 先知者요 引導者이오 未來를 爲한 詩人이오 人類의 向上前進과 共存共榮의 眞理를 確信한 벌거버슨 使徒이다.
그의 生涯에 對하야는 後日에 紹介할 機會가 잇슬 줄로 밋고 이제는 畧하는 바이나, 나는 이번에 나의 飜譯의 붓을 처음으로 들어, 맨 먼저 나의 崇拜하는 「휘트맨」의 詩를 紹介하게 됨을 스스로 깃버하는 同時에 나는 매우 躊躇하얏고 또한 努力하얏다. 함은, 나는 元來 英文의 知識이 업는 터이오 日譯된 것이 2, 3種이 잇다. 하나- 元來 詩의 飜譯은 (다른 것도 그러치마는) 創作보다 어려운 것이라- 모다 譯意가 原文과 틀니는 것은 勿論, 日譯끼리도 全혀 相反되는 解釋이 만흠으로서 투른 原書를 노코서 끙끙 대인것이 돌이어 躊躇넘은 줄로 생각되는 터이다. 그러나 「휘트맨」의 紹介는 朝鮮에서 이것이 처음임을 생각할 때에는 設或 잘못이 잇다 할지라도 讀者는 勿論, 原作者 「휘트맨」도 地下에서 微笑로 容恕하리라는 自身으로 이것을 讀者에게 부치는 바이며 끄트로 「휘트맨」의 아조 簡略한 紹介를 하면,
그는 1819年 5月 31日에 米國 『뉴-욕』州 『롱, 아일랜드』에 나헛다. 學校라고는 小學校를 겨우 마치고 13歲에 印刷所 植字工되엇고 20歲에 自己의 詩篇을 主로 한 週刊雜詩를 發行하고 그때부터 『뉴-욕』에서 寄稿家, 雜誌記者, 演說家 노릇을 하기 始作하야 그의 一生은 印刷, 新聞, 雜誌, 詩作, 旅行 等을 떠나서는 說明치 못하게 되엇다. 그의 末年은 매우 平穩하얏스니 1882年으로부터 10年間은 自然과 讀書로 平和한 生活을 하얏고, 1892年 3月 26日에 그는 73歲의 끈긔 잇는 生涯를 마치엇다.
이와가티 「휘트맨」은 70의 一期를 平民으로 나서 平民을 노래하다가 平民으로 죽은 點에 그 詩의 生命도 잇는 것이다. 그의 詩篇은 1855年에 「草葉集」第1輯이 發行되기 비롯하야 1860年에 全集을 發行하얏다. 그의 詩에 對하야는 내가 따로이 說明이나 紹介하는 것보다 다음에 記錄한 拙譯數扁에서 讀者가 能히 判斷할 수 잇슬 것이오, 나는 다만 「民主詩의 先驅者」- 從來의 規約을 함부로 無視한 「大膽한 自由詩人」이라고 그를 부르고 십다.＜19＞
