발행일	1922년 07월 10일
기사제목	故鄕 (Hometown)
필자	치리코프 (作), 憑虛 (譯)
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

故鄕
						치리코프 (作), 憑虛 (譯)
The steamboat carrying me approaches the wharf of a small country town where I spent my younger days, my heart pounding fiercely. Like it was sunk in idle sorrow, the hazy and quiet summer evening softly covered the lazily swaying Volga River water, the mountains along the bank, and the distant view across the river, tinged with the green of the forest. A languid fatigue, an inexpressibly sad feeling, seeped deep into my soul from this evening, from the dreamlike water's surface, from the shadow of the lush forest atop the mountain lying within it, from the setting sun slowly dipping behind the mountain, from the lonely fisherman's small boat, and also from the white seagulls and the distant steam whistle... I can already see the familiar places where I used to wander with a fishing rod, set fires, and catch crabs. On that rock where I always used to cast my line, someone is fishing. A strange thing... sitting in the exact same spot as me, fishing just like me! Suddenly, overcome by sad thoughts, I felt like crying. I started thinking about how I now have white hair and wrinkles, and how my heart wouldn't pound following the movement of the fishing float (浮子), nor would I jump into the water to catch a fish if I hooked one like that person is doing now. Thus, my heart began to ache for the life that, once gone, would never return... I came here expecting joy, but what greeted me was sorrow. Turning a bend, <132> from the high bank of the Volga, the familiar two round tops of the church and a cluster of houses with roofs either green or gray peek into view - tears welled up in my eyes... Since then, this town has suffered fires that could be called near total burns twice; could the old house where we lived still remain? I desperately wanted to see the house surrounded by a green fence where I lived happily with my parents. Father is gone, Mother is gone, not a single sibling is left in this world. Yet, painful recollections flickered before my eyes as if alive. No matter what, I couldn't believe they were not in this world. When I get off this steamboat, I'll take the shortcut with the puddle – I used to take that way often to cut across – go over the hill, pass a couple of houses, and our house's fence will come into view... Such thoughts arose...
"Mother, Father!"
Then, on the steps of the entrance, the joyful faces of Mother, Father, younger brother, younger sister pop out...
"Just arrived!!"
"Yes, I just got here..."
A long steam whistle began to blow. The steamboat, drawing a circle, gradually approached the wharf. The wharf was crowded with people. Hungrily, I scanned face after face, looking for anyone I knew from the old days, but there was no one, not a single person. Strange. Where did all those people go? Ah, but wait, that woman standing there holding a Western umbrella (洋傘) looks familiar. No, that woman is not her! That woman was already twenty-five then, so she must be close to fifty now, whereas this woman doesn't even look thirty yet. So, back then, when I lived in this town, she must have been only five or six years old; there's no way we could know each other. These five or six young women... they probably weren't even born yet when I was here.
"Sir (令監), shall I carry this luggage for you?..."
"Oh, yes, please do."
I can't meet anyone. Not a single soul makes my heart race. No one to kiss me, no one to ask, "Have you arrived?" Only people staring at me, as if hostile, as if distrustful, and full of curiosity. - "Who is that fellow – whose house is he going to?" <133>
"Who should I go to? I don't know. No, I won't go to anyone. Rough and pitiful land that saw my youth, I came here searching for you. No matter how much you've changed, surely we wouldn't fail to recognize each other."

I won't take the shortcut with the puddle. Now I have no need to hurry anywhere; unlike the old days, there's no one waiting for me with joyful anticipation...
"I should get a carriage..."
"That won't do. There are only two carriages in total here, one the Chief of Police (署長) just took, and the other, for some reason, isn't out today. Never mind that, I can carry it. Where are you going, Sir?"
"Me? Oh, there must be an inn (旅館) over there?"
"Of course! There's a splendid one. The Klimova Inn."
"Klimova! Then, is that person still alive?"
"That person died long ago. But people still call it that, same as before."
"So, is his son running it?"
"No. Ivanov runs it. But people still call it the old name. The son also died sometime ago."
Following this porter (막버리군), I thought. Dear land, are you even entirely alive? Is even a single dog left?
"Sir, where have you come from?"
"Me?... I'm just a traveler... Came from Peterburg (彼得堡)."
"Sightseeing must be good where you live, Sir. Or perhaps you came on some business?"
"No."
"I suppose so, the only business here is selling clear powder (맑금가루장사). Then perhaps you came on some official government business?"
"No, just stopped by briefly for sightseeing. I used to live here before, so I wanted to stop by for some reason..."
"Then you won't recognize much, Sir. After the fire, what's left of the old things..."
We walk into the village. Hungrily, I looked for familiar places. The village had changed completely. The new <134> houses did not greet me warmly.
"What is the name of this village?"
"It's called Simbirsk (심빌스크)."
"Simbirsk! Ah, is that really so?"
"Yes."
In Simbirsk, there was the house of the head priest (祭司長), and a young maiden, said to be a relative of the head priest, was staying there. Her name was Sashinka (사신카). A very simple, short romance suddenly comes to mind. A noisy group with fishing gear and a samovar (사모와알) heads towards the watermill... In the stream hitting the riverbed in the fields, there were many trout (鱒魚). Sashinka, wearing a red headscarf on her golden hair, holding a fishing rod, revealing her lovely legs through the grass, ah, how beautiful she must have been! We sat quietly, watching the floats. And so we waited for the call, "Time for tea."
At that time, the samovar took a long time to boil. Sashinka and I had already been heating the samovar with pinecones for a while. Because she disliked worms, I baited the hook with worms for her. Ah, how beautiful she was, that Sashinka...
"Eek, it ate it again... Could you put another one on for me?"
"Sure, sure."
I went over to her side and tried to bait the worm from behind her back. But the worm straightened itself stiffly, then wriggled and coiled, refusing to cooperate. Sashinka turned her head, looked up at me from below.
"Put it on quickly."
"This darn thing, it won't go on properly."
I sat down beside her. Looking at her face from the side, I thought.
"What would happen if I kissed this girl right here..."
Our eyes met. Perhaps she sensed my wicked thought. She blushed. Not long after, I did bait the worm, but I didn't go back to where my fishing rod lay. Sitting right next to Sashinka, I lingered near her neck.
"Go away. Your float is moving." <135>
"I'm not going... I refuse to go..."
"Why?"
"I can't leave your side........."
Silence. Head bowed, mouth shut.
She doesn't tell me to just go.
"Il'eksandora Vikt'orovna!"
"What is it!"
"Guess what I'm thinking..."
"Am I some kind of magician (魔術장)? How would I know what you're thinking!..."
"If you knew what I'm thinking right now, you'd surely be angry..."
"Who forbids anyone from thinking whatever they want..."
"Do you know what I'm thinking?"
"No, what is it?"
"You'll get angry..."
"Just tell me anyway!"
"Have you ever loved anyone?"
"I don't know about such things."
"Then, what about now?"
"Same thing."
She turned crimson like a peony (杜丹).
"Then I..."
"Well!"
"I am in love..."
"With whom?"
"Guess!"
"I don't know..."
But she herself blushed even more, bowing her head lower and lower. I lay down on the grass right next to Sashinka. She didn't move away. Chewing on a blade of grass she had plucked, I myself was tormented by the irresistible desire to kiss Sashinka.
I sighed.
"Why are you doing that?"
"Judge for yourself..."
The girl blushed again. Whatever happens, happens... I got up, leaned over, and kissed Sashinka. The maiden covered her face with both hands and sat silently. I kissed her once more, then asked softly. <136>
"Yes or no?"
"Yes!" Sashinka whispered, barely audible.
"Lower your hands!... Look at me..."
"Don't want to."
Sashinka didn't move a muscle... Sitting beside her, I brushed my head against her lap. Her hand, gently placed on my head, softly stroked it...
"The samovar is boiling!"
Sashinka, as if suddenly awakened from sleep, jumped up and dashed towards the watermill. There, we sat together again and drank tea. Even so, we didn't look at each other. Both of us were afraid to look at each other. Late at night, returning to the town, saying farewell at the gate of the head priest's house, as I helped Sashinka down from the carriage, I looked into her face for the first time. It was a bewildered, anxious face. Her hand, extended to me, trembled. And I could deeply feel her barely managing to respond to my handshake. After that, day after day, eagerly anticipating meeting Sashinka, I passed by the head priest's gate. As the days went by, my affection for her deepened and grew stronger, but she seemed to have vanished into thin air, not even a shadow to be seen, nor any news heard. Some time later, I found out that Sashinka had gone to Simbirsk the very next day. Because a telegram arrived saying her father had passed away...
After that, I never met Sashinka again. Where is she? Surely, she must have married the head priest. By now, she must be the head priest's wife... She must be over forty already...
"There was a head priest named Nikolai (니코라이). Is he still alive?"
"He died."
"Then his house?"
"It burned down. - His house was right there, ... precisely where the Monopoly Bureau (專賣所) is now..."
The building is new, but the stone gate remains. Staring blankly at the gate, suddenly, it felt as if the young and lovely Sashinka, wearing a red headscarf - just like when she went to the watermill - might dash out from within the gate, blush, <137> and ask, "Do you remember catching trout at the watermill?"
A servant came out of the Monopoly Bureau and started knocking the sealing wax off a bottle's neck against the stone pillar of the gate...
"Why are you doing that?... This isn't the place for you to be doing such things, old fellow..."
"What business is it of yours?"
"Indeed... There's no need to tell that person Sashinka stood in this gateway 20 years ago. Ah, my relationship with Sashinka would surely be of no concern to that person!"
The temple (寺院) still retained its old appearance. As before, lush white birches (白樺) surrounded it. Magpies (白嘴鳥) had built nests in those trees, and their chaotic cries echoed throughout the town. Just like the market women. They don't even leave alone the sentimental reflections prompted by memories. The bell for the all-night vigil (徹宵祭) rings out. Tomorrow is Sunday. It's still the same bell sound I heard long ago. The ding-ding ringing evokes in one's soul the sorrow that once gone, one can never return, makes one think how short human life is, and how human affairs are entirely in its grasp... Again, I remember going to the temple to see Sashinka... The bell rang like this then too. But back then, the end of life was not yet in sight. So its ringing was completely different from now.
"Well, we've arrived..."
Alone amidst the welcome. So lonely and desolate, like death. On the gloomy back porch, a clock lazily ticks away. The memory of kissing Sashinka at the watermill recedes further and further away. It doesn't stop, it doesn't end. From the small window, the police tower is visible. Everything is as before. Even the correct greyish color is still yellowish. Surely it didn't burn. It couldn't have burned.
"Come in!"
"Terribly sorry to bother you, but could you please show me your residence certificate (居住證明書), Sir?"
"Ah, the certificate, you mean... This is a travel permit (旅行券) without an expiration date. I can wander around anytime, indefinitely."
"Security (警戒) is extremely tight here these days." <138>
"Is it that tight here too?"
"Yes. Since the revolution (革命) happened, we can't let anyone without a travel permit stay overnight."
"So there was such a revolution here too?"
The manager (管店) gave a slight smile. And, seemingly displeased,
"Well, of course! There was quite a revolution. It was done very orderly..."
"Um, what do you mean by orderly?"
"That means, well, as is customary... they killed the Inspector (監察官), everyone wrapped themselves in red flags (붉은 旗) and wandered around, the Cossacks (코사크) came."
"The Cossacks came... So, did you get beaten up too, old fellow?"
"Sure did, beaten half to death!"
"His spirits are sky-high. He seems very satisfied."
"So, how about now?"
"Everything's calmed down now. This new Chief of Police is a very strict gentleman. A truly good Chief."
"What about the previous one?"
"He was handed over to the court (裁判所)."
"For what?"
"For following behind the red flag..."
I can't make sense of this world. I waved my hand dismissively. The manager left. I sat silently under the small window for a long time, then went out into the street. Huh, there's the bridge (橋) lying in the gully overgrown with nettles (蕁麻). Below that gully, a silvery stream meanders. Its name is "Bul'viska" (불비스카). On the hill across that gully should be the house where we lived, but I'm afraid even to go see it. My heart might just shrivel up. I stopped walking on the bridge. I can barely breathe. Over the bridge railing, the middle of the gully is visible. There, my younger brother was waging war with the nettles. He was cutting down the nettles with a wooden sword. The memory of a boy with mocking eyes and a high-strung, nervous disposition comes back.
"Modya! What are you doing there?"
"Waging war..."
"Dinner's ready, come here!"
"Can't, the enemy forces are in pursuit!"
It seems like yesterday. Where is that boy now? Could the Modya who played war games with nettles in this gully really be the same Modya killed near <139> Bapankoo? An unbelievable thing! I sighed deeply, hung my head low, and moved my powerless feet. Climbing the hill, thankfully, everything remained as it was. Neither flame nor war had touched this place, so precious to my memories. Look, there's that fence! Ah, the forsythia is so overgrown. It's almost blocking the window. Someone is playing the piano there. Stopping across from it, I listened intently. It sounded like an old, broken piano. We used to have one like that in our old house. Hearing that sound, I suddenly felt transported back to the old days of my youth. And it seemed as if Mother was playing that piano. Yes, I'm thinking about Sashinka, whom I kissed at the watermill yesterday. What is she playing? Ah! Right, it seems she's playing some old melody I know. The wind from that time blows over me. What melody could that be? Ah, right, that's "A Maiden's Prayer." Right, yes... Closing my eyes and listening, the 20 years that separated me from my youth vanished in an instant. I imagined myself still a university student, returned home for the summer vacation (暑中 休暇). The house is bustling with relatives gathered together. It's just after we finished drinking tea heartily in the courtyard (中庭). Father, with a rolled cigarette (卷煉) in his mouth, sits beside the already cooled samovar, reading the newspaper. Mother is playing the piano. Bogoyavlenskii (보고야브렌스키), my rival, a theology student also in love with Sashinka, comes and pesters me to go swimming in the Volga (보올카). He was always preparing his proposal (申提) to marry Sashinka. He consulted with me about it. While we were swimming, we talked only of Sashinka. He took off one boot (長靴) and tapped the sole of his shoe (구쓰).
"Getting married isn't like buying shoes."
"Right!"
"So, what do you think?... How does she seem to you?"
"What?"
"Well, Sashinka!"
"Oh, I haven't really thought about it."
"I think she's stunningly beautiful! It feels like offering everything would still be too little. Right now, I don't know whether it's right <140> to speak to her, or if I should postpone it until graduation. I'm just worried someone else will snatch her away. Because she's stunningly beautiful..."
He took off his other boot, tossed it aside with a leap, and
"I've decided. Tomorrow, I'll definitely propose (請婚)..."
Then, he dove backwards into the water from the raft (떼ㅅ목).
That same person came today, coaxing me to go swimming, intending to talk about Sashinka. Crazy fool! He doesn't even suspect that his Sashinka was irrevocably lost at the watermill yesterday.
"Hey, aren't you going swimming?"
"Did you propose?"
"No, not yet. What's the rush?"
"That won't do. By the way, do you believe Sashinka loves you?"
"Her?"
Bogoyavlenskii nodded vigorously, his eyes wide, then slapped my shoulder,
"Beautiful Sashinka is already mine!"
I felt both amused and indignant. Setting aside other things, I felt like cursing primarily for Sashinka's sake. So I wanted to shout, "Yesterday we kissed each other. And Sashinka said 'Yes' to me."
"Hey, go away! I don't want to go swimming. And about Sashinka. Don't face a miserable failure. No matter how confident you are... no matter what..."
"What do you mean?"
"Well, let's wait and see."
"What do you mean 'wait and see'? What will you do if I get her consent?"
"Cut the chatter! Sashinka is already my wife..."
"Ah, this is serious trouble?"
"Go quickly! If you don't, I'll slap your cheek!"
"Ah!... This is serious trouble..."
Where is that Bogoyavlenskii? Perhaps he married Sashinka, became a head priest. But did she tell her husband about what happened at the watermill?
The piano stopped playing. I came back to my senses, and desperately wanted to go into that house and see all the changes. <141> Who lives in this house, who plays the piano, and what have the dining room, drawing room, and study become? However, barging in without preamble and saying,
"Please let me see the house. I spent my youth here once. So, I want to look around this house now and return to my youth, I can't stand it otherwise," felt somewhat awkward.
I couldn't quite make up my mind. After circling the house several times, I slipped into a side alley and peeked over the fence into the yard (뜰). In this yard, I once picked and ate the watery, firm fruits of the fennel (茴香) tree. This yard is also where Mother made jam (쨤) and shared the foam with my siblings. There, amidst the forsythia and strawberry bushes, were many good hiding spots. I often read good books and thought in one of them.
"Motherland (祖國)! I will dedicate my life for your happiness."
My self back then seemed so small and insignificant to my present self. Ah, life passes by in the meantime. Yet, you remain as minuscule and powerless as you were in those distant old days. No, you have become even more insignificant and worthless than before. Why? Because you no longer have the same conviction in your own strength, nor even the hope of seeing your happy motherland in the future. I remember the inn manager who talked about the revolution... And also the Chief of Police who followed the red flag...
"Poor Chief! You probably didn't know everything would turn out so tragically. I too never dreamed things would turn out like this!... Therefore, both you and I have ended up in this situation. You are brought to trial, and I am under police surveillance..."
Carrying depression and sorrow in both spirit and body, I returned to the inn. The manager brought a samovar. Shortly after, he turned to leave, closed the door, and seemed to be quietly eavesdropping...
"Everything is the same! Only I am not the person I was before... I no longer believe in manifestos (檄文), nor do I stain my hands with the black ink of the mimeograph (謄寫版)... Hey, landlord, eavesdropping is futile. Do you suspect I came here to stir up another revolution?... They say there's a strict Chief of Police in this town..." <142>
The same thing again. Early in the morning, a policeman (巡査) came with a summons (呼出狀) - requiring me to appear at the police station today.
"Ah, just seeing a summons gives me a headache. But it's better to go from this side than for them to come. Let's go to the police station. Let's see this 'strict' Chief."
I reached the police station. I was guided to the Deputy Chief's (副署長) office. Contrary to my inner feelings, I entered with a scowl.
"Here, please sit down. I apologize for troubling you to come unnecessarily. However, I would like to know for what purpose you have come here..."
"No purpose at all. I just came because I thought of it. Don't I have the right to freely wander wherever my feet take me?"
"A perfectly reasonable statement... But when do you plan to leave?"
"I haven't thought about that yet."
"Forgive my curiosity, but... aren't you... aren't you a writer (作家)?"
"I am a writer. Unfortunately, I am a writer."
"I am very pleased to make your acquaintance."
"Are you really pleased?"
The Deputy Chief looked embarrassed.
"I was also a university student before. I was at the university with you. When I was in my third year, you were in the graduating class."
"Ah! Is that so!"
"Yes. Have a cigarette. I too was one of those involved in that disturbance (騷動)... with you... Perhaps you don't remember, my name is Penzenskii (펜젠스키)!"
"Penzenskii! That name sounds somewhat familiar..."
"Yes! Didn't I slap the secretary's (幹事) cheek!"
"Was that you?"
"Yes... It was me! I swear it was!"
"You! I really didn't know..."
The Deputy Chief energetically tried to prove to me that he had slapped the secretary's cheek during the disturbance. He seemed to have completely forgotten he was currently working at the police station. Growing more spirited, he began to recount the details of the disturbance. His face changed strangely, devoid of any trace of a policeman's demeanor. Perhaps the university disturbance was his most precious memory... I watched him with unconcealable curiosity, thinking. How did you end up in the police force instead of under police surveillance? Perhaps he guessed my thoughts.
"Look here, please don't look at me like that! Indeed, I am wearing a police uniform now, but such things aren't that significant..." <143>
And then, he brought up the story of the disturbance again. A man with a sycophantic, dog-like face peered into the room. Probably a clerk (書記). The Deputy Chief knitted his eyebrows together and shouted sternly.
"Don't enter my room without permission. I'm busy with official matters right now."
The clerk withdrew.
"Ah, there were all sorts of people in those days..." The Deputy Chief suddenly said this and paced the room excitedly.
"Perhaps, you have torn my heart apart... Do you remember Ulyanov (우리야노브)? The one who was executed (死刑)? I was in the same class (班) with him..."
"By the way, why exactly did you summon me? Please tell me the reason."
"Ah, that... Thinking of my youth, my university days, I wanted to see how you had changed... Because I knew you from university, that's why..."
"So, it was to show respect, then!"
"Were you offended? Please forgive me with a generous heart! I couldn't bear the memories of that time coming back... Also, I've been an avid reader of your writings, so I wanted to meet you..."
He suddenly clamped his lips shut. Then he turned towards the window and stood motionless. I stood up and cleared my throat... He quickly turned back to me, his face clouded. An embarrassed smile played on his lips.
"I won't keep you any longer."
He spoke affectionately and sighed. After thinking for a moment, he extended his hand.
"Well then, my friend (한우님)! May blessings be upon you!... We probably won't have a chance to meet again, unless..."
"Unless I'm summoned to the police station again?"
He chuckled. Then, with an embarrassed smile,
"Our life is short, everything goes away with us..."
I left the police station. For a while, I couldn't organize my thoughts. It is truly incomprehensible that a policeman, installed to abolish and eradicate all disorder, not only fondly recalls the disorder he once participated in but also treasures that memory like a drowning man grasping at straws. Could it be because he, like me, has white hair fluttering on his head, and has lost the flower of life on the weary road of existence to sorrowful bygone days?
...(1921. 6. 15)... <144>

**Summary**

A middle-aged writer returns to his provincial Russian hometown on the Volga after many years. He finds the town greatly changed, recognizes no one, and learns many old acquaintances and landmarks are gone due to fires and time. He nostalgically recalls his youth, particularly a fleeting romance with a girl named Sashinka and interactions with a university rival. His old family home still stands, triggering powerful memories, but he hesitates to enter. Summoned by the Deputy Chief of Police, he is surprised to find the official is a former fellow university student who reminisces sentimentally about their radical student days, revealing a shared sense of lost youth and disillusionment despite their different paths. The story captures the bittersweet pain of homecoming, the irrevocability of time, and the lingering impact of past ideals and events (like the revolution) on individual lives.

From a Korean colonial history perspective, the publication of this translated Russian story in *Kaebyŏk* is interesting. While set in Russia, themes of returning to a changed homeland, disillusionment with the present compared to past hopes (especially those associated with youthful idealism and revolution mentioned near the end), and encounters with authorities (police checks, summons) could resonate deeply with Korean readers experiencing similar feelings under Japanese rule. The Deputy Chief's nostalgic remembrance of youthful "disorder" (student activism) while serving the current regime presents a poignant irony that might reflect the complex compromises and paths individuals took in colonial Korea. Historians might find the choice to translate and publish this specific story indicative of the subtle ways Korean intellectuals engaged with contemporary issues and expressed shared sentiments of loss and change through foreign literature.

故鄕
						치리코프 (作), 憑虛 (譯)
나를 실흔 汽船은, 나의 가슴에 劇烈한 鼓動을 일으키면서, 내가 저믄 날을 보내인 어떤 족으만한 시골의 埠頭에 다긔여가고 잇다. 閑愁에 잠긴 듯, 한다스하고도 고요한 녀름저녁은 게을리 흔들리는 보올가 江물과, 沿岸의 山들과, 江 건너 저편 수풀 푸른내 끼인 遠景을 스르륵 덥헛다. 나릿한 疲勞, 무에라고 말할 수 업는 구슬픈 느낌은, 이 저녁으로부터, 꿈결가튼 물얼굴로부터 그 속에 가루누은 山우의 蒼鬱한 나무 숩의 그림자로부터, 山 저쪽으로 뉘엿뉘엿 넘어가는 夕陽으로부터, 寂寞한 漁父의 扁舟로부터 그리고 또 흰 갈막이와 먼 汽笛으로부터, 나의 넉 속속들이 숨이어 들어온다.... 내가 일즉이 낙시ㅅ대를 가지고 헤매기도 하며, 불을 노키도 하며, 게(蟹)를 잡기도 하든, 낫 익은 場所가 벌서 보인다. 내가 노상 낙시를 드리고 잇든, 그 바위돌 우엔, 누구인지 고기를 낙고 잇다. 이상한 일이다... 내 안젓든 꼭 그 자리에 나 모양으로 안저 고기를 낙고 잇다!. 문득 나는 슬픈 생각이 자아치어 눈물이 날 듯 하엿다. 나는, 나에게 흰털억과 줄음살이 잇는 것, 그리고 또 낙시찌(浮子)가 움즉임을 딸하 가슴을 두근거리기도 하며, 시방 저 사람이 하고 잇든 고기가 걸리어도 물 속으로 뛰어들어 그것을 잡으랴 안흘 것을 생각하기 시작하엿다. 그리하매 나의 深藏은 한번 가고 다시 돌아오지 아니할 生涯로 하여 압프기 비롯하엿다... 歡喜를 期待하고 여긔를 왓거든 나를 맛는 것은 悲哀이엇다. 한구비를 돌우나가니＜132＞ 보올가의 놉다란 沿岸으로부터, 눈에 익은 敎會의 두 둥근 꼭대기와, 或은 초록 或은 灰色의 집웅을 인(戴) 집 한 떼가 엿보인다- 나의 눈에는 눈물이 고이엇다... 그 後 이 고을이 두 번이나 可謂 全燒하다십히 火灾를 격것스니, 우리가 살든 녯집이 그대로 남아 잇슬가? 내가 父母님과 즐겁게 지내든, 그 草綠色 울타리로 에둘리인 집을, 나는 못 견디리만큼 보고 십헛다. 아버지도 업다, 어머니도 업다, 兄弟하나 이 世上에는 업다. 하건만 아닯은 回想은 산 것가티 눈압헤 얼른거리엇다. 암만해도 그들이 이 世上에 업다고는 밋기 실혓다. 내가 이 汽船을 내려, 저 웅덩이 잇는 지럼길을 거처간다 - 그 때 길을 질르랴고 행용 그리로 단이엇섯다 - 언덕을 넘어 두어집만 지나면, 우리집 담이 보인다... 이런 생각이 낫섯다...
『어머니, 아버지!』
한즉 入口의 층층대 우에는 어머니, 아버지, 男동생 女동생의 기븜 찬 얼굴이 뛰어 나온다...

『예, 막 나렷습니다...』

『응, 그래, 갓다주게.』
아모도 맛날 수 업다. 어느 뉘 하나, 나의 가슴을 뛰게하지 안는다. 키쓰해주는 이도, 『나렷느냐?』고 뭇는 이도 업다. 다만 敵對하는 듯이, 못 밋겟다하는 듯이, 그리고 가득한 好奇心으로써 사람사람이 나를 바라볼 뿐이다. - 『저 者가 웬 者야- 뉘 집에 가는 모양이야?』＜133＞
『누구한테 갈고? 나는 모른다. 아니, 나는 아모한테로도 가지 안흘란다. 젊은 나를 본 적 잇는 거칠고 불상한 땅이어, 너를 차저서 예 왓노라. 아모리 變햇기로 우리야 서로 몰라보지 안켓지.』

나는 웅덩이 잇는 지럼길을 거치지 안흘란다. 인제 내겐 아모대도 급급히 갈 必要가 업다, 녯날과 가티 깃븐 念慮로 나를 기다려 주는 이가 하나도 업습이다...
『馬車를 求해야 될텐데...』
『그것 안됩니다. 여긔는 馬車가 통 두 채 밧게 업는데, 한 채는 시방 署長께서 타고 가셧고, 또 한 채는 웬일인지 오늘 안나왓습니다그려. 그릴 것 업시 제가 메고가지요. 令監은 어대로 가십니까?』
『나 말인가, 응, 저 旅館이 잇겟지?』
『잇고 말곱시요! 아주 긔막히게 훌륭한 것이 잇지요. 크리모바 旅館이라고.』
『크리모바! 그러면, 그 사람이 지금 살아잇나?』
『그 사람은 벌서 죽엇습니다. 죽엇지만서도 예전대로 그저 그러케 불읍지요.』
『그러면 그 사람의 아들이 하고 잇나?』
『아닙지요. 이와노브가 하지요. 하지만서도 녯날대로 그러케 부릅지요. 아들도 언제 죽엇습니다.』
나는 이 막버리군의 뒤를 딸하가며 생각하엿다. 情든 땅이어, 넌들 온전히 살아잇슬가? 개 한 마리이니 남아 잇슬가?
『令監께서는 어대서 오셧습니까?』
『나 말인가?... 나는 그저 돌아다니는 사람일세... 彼得堡에서 왓네.』
『구경은 令監게시는 대가 조흘걸입시요. 或 무슨 장사ㅅ일로나 오셧습니까?』
『아니』
『그러키도 하시겟지요, 장사라고 해야 여긔는 맑금가루장사뿐이니. 그러면 저어 무슨 官家일로 오셧습니까?』
『아니야, 구경 次로 잠간 들런 것일세. 내가 이전에 여긔 살앗기 때문에, 어째 들러고 십헛네...』
『그러면 잘못 알아 보실걸입시요. 불에 타고 녯 것이 어대 남앗서야지요...』
우리는 동내로 걸어간다. 나는 줄인 듯이 예전아는 場所를 차저 보앗다. 동내는 모다 變하엿다. 새로운＜134＞ 집들은 나를 情다이 마저주지 안핫다.
『이 동내 이름이 무엇인가?』
『심빌스크래요.』
『심빌스크! 아, 꼭 그런가?』
『그래요』
심빌스크에는 祭司長의 집이 잇섯는데, 그 집에는 祭司長과 일가간이라는 젊은 處女가 와잇섯섯다. 그의 이름은 사신카 이엇섯다. 極히 簡單한 쩌른 로만스가 문득 생각이 난다. 고기잡는 器具와 사모와알을 가진 시끄러운 몃패는 물방아간ㅅ편을 向해간다... 들ㅅ河床에 부디쳐 물소리 요란한 내(川) 鱒魚가 만히 잇섯다. 黃金가튼 머리에는 붉은 머리ㅅ手巾을 쓰고, 손에는 낙시ㅅ대를 들고 사랑스런 다리를 풀 사이로 보이고 잇는 사신카의 모양은, 아아, 얼마나 아름다웟스랴! 우리는 가만히 안저 낙시찌를 보고 잇섯다. 이리고 우리는 『茶 먹어라』고, 불릴 때를 기다리고 잇섯다.
그 때 사모와알은 좀처럼 끌치 안핫다. 나와 사신카는 벌서부터 솔밤숭이로 사모와알을 뎁히고 잇섯슴이엇다. 그가 벌레를 실혀하기 때문에, 나는 그를 爲하야 벌레를 낙시에 꿰엇섯다. 아아, 그는, 어떠케 그러케 아름다웟든가, 그 사신카는... .
『에그, 또 먹어버렷네... 딴 놈을 꿰어 주셔요?』
『그리지요, 그리지요.』
나는 그의 겨테 가서, 등넘어로 벌레를 꿰어주랴 하엿다. 그런데 벌레란 놈이 몸을 꼿꼿이 펴기도 하고, 구불구불 오글어부티기도 하며, 말을 잘 듯지 안핫다. 사신카는 고개를 돌려, 미테서 눈을 치떠 나를 보며.
『얼핏 꿰어 주어요.』
『요런 놈, 어대 잘 꿰어져야지』
나는 그의 겨테 안젓다. 엽흐로 그의 얼굴을 바라보며 생각한다.
『내가 이 자리에서 이 녀자를 키쓰하면 어찌될고...』
우리들은 눈과 눈을 마조 보앗다. 아마 그가 나의 못된 생각을 깨달앗슴이리라. 그는 얼굴을 붉히엇다. 나도 또한 그 後 얼마 아니되어, 나는 벌레를 꿰기는 꿰엇스되 내 낙시ㅅ대가 노힌 대에는 가지 안핫다. 사신카의 바루 겨테 안저서 그의 목에 숨길을 서리고 잇섯다.
『저리가요. 당신의 낙시찌가 꿈적이는구먼.』＜135＞

『祖國이여! 나는 너의 幸福을 爲하야 나의 生命을 바치리라.』
그때의 내 自信이 이다지도 작고 하잘 것이 업는 것으로 지금읫 나엔 생각하엿다. 아아, 生命도 그새에 가바린다. 하거늘, 너는 依然히 저 먼 녯날 그 때와 가티 微小하고 無力한 놈이다. 아니, 너는 예전보담도 加一層 보잘 것 업고 하잘 것 업시 되엇다. 웨? 너에게는 인제 제 힘에 對한 前가튼 信念도 업고 將來일지라도 幸福스러운 제 祖國을 볼 수 잇다는 希望조차 업는 까닭이다. 革命을 이약이하든 旅館의 『管店』 생각이 난다... 그리고 또 붉은 旗ㅅ뒤를 딸하단엿다는 署長도...
『불상한 署長이여! 너는 모든 것이 이다지도 悲哀에 돌아갈 줄 몰랏스리라. 나도, 그 일이 이러케 되리라고는 꿈에도 생각치 안핫섯다!... 그럼으로 나나 너나 이런 境遇에 빠지고 만 것이다. 너는 裁判에 부텨지고, 나는 警察의 監視미테... 』
넉에도 몸에도 悒鬱과 哀愁를 부더안고, 旅館으로 돌아왓다. 管店이 사모와알을 갓다 주엇다. 얼마 아니되어 그는 돌아서 나가더니, 문을 다치고 가만히 긔척을 엿보며 엿듯고 잇는 것 갓다...
『모든 것이 如前하다! 다만 나만이 前日의 내가 아니로군... 나는 벌서 檄文도 밋지 안코, 謄寫版의 검은 먹을 손에 문치지도 안는다... 여보, 主人 엿들어도 헛일이라. 내가 여긔와서, 또 革命이나 反復할가보아 의심하는가.... 이 고을에는 嚴한 署長이 잇다지...』＜142＞
또 가튼 일이다. 아츰 일즉이 巡査가 - 本日 警察署에 出頭할 事 - 라는 呼出狀을 가지고 왓다.
『아아, 나는 呼出狀만 보아도 골머리가 압흐다. 그러나 저편에서 오는 것보담 이 편에서 가는 것이 조타. 警察署에 가자. 그 嚴한 署長이란 者를 좀 보자』
警察署에 다달앗다. 副署長室로 引導되엇다. 나는 胸中과 반대로 얼굴을 찝흐리고 들어갓다.
『이리, 안지십시요. 일업시 오시라고 하여서 罪悚합니다. 그런데, 무슨 目的으로 이 곳에 오셧는지 그것이 알고 십흡니다...』
『아모 目的도 업습니다. 다만 생각이 나길래 왓슬 따름입니다. 나에게는 발길 가는대로 自由로히 아모대나 단일 權利가 업슬가요?』
『맛당히 하실 말슴이올시다... 한데 언제 떠나실 豫定입니까?』
『그런 것은 아즉 생각치 안핫습니다.』
『好奇心가태서 失禮올시다마는, 당신은... 당신은 作家가 아니십니까?』
『作家올시다. 不幸히 作家올시다.』
『서로 알게 되어 매우 깃븝니다.』
『참말로 깃브실가요?』
副署長은 무색한 모양이엇다.
『나도 前者에는 大學生이엇습니다. 당신과 함끠 大學에 잇섯습니다. 내가 3年級에 단일제, 당신은 卒業할 年級에 계셧습니다.』
『아!, 그럿습니까!』
『그럿습니다. 담배나 부티십시오. 내 亦是, 그 騷動에 參預한 한 사람이올시다... 당신과 함끠... 아마 잇지 안핫섯겟지오, 내 姓名은 펜젠스키라고 합니다!』
『펜젠스키! 어쨰 듯든 이름 갓습니다그려...』
『그럿습니다! 그래 내가 幹事의 뺨을 치지 안핫습니까!』
『당신이 그랫습딋가?』
『그럿습니다... 내 올시다! 맹서코 그럿습니다!』
『당신이! 참 몰랏습니다...』
副署長은 豪氣잇게 自己가 그 騷動ㅅ적에 幹事의 뺨을 친 것을 나에게 證明하랴고 애를 썻다. 그는 시방 自己가 警察署에 勤務하고 잇는 것을 아주 이즌 듯 하엿다. 그는 더욱더욱 긔운이 나서, 騷動의 顚末을 仔細히 이약이하기 시작하엿다. 그의 얼굴은, 警官다운 그림자를 차지랴 차질 수 업시 異常하게 變하엿다. 아마 大學의 騷動은 그에게 가장 貴重한 回想이엇슴이리라... 나는 숨길 수 업는 好奇心을 품고 그를 바라보며, 생각하엿다. 어찌하야 너는 警察의 監視미테 들지 안코, 警官의 틈바구니에 끼엇느냐? 아마 그는 나의 생각을 推察하엿슴이리라.
『여보시오, 제발 그러케 나를 보지 말아주시오! 果然 내가 지금 警官의 制服을 입고 잇습니다마는, 이런 것은 그리 대수롭은 것 아닙니다... .』＜143＞
하고는, 또 騷動의 이약이를 끄어내엿다. 개처럼 追從的 상판을 가진 사람이 방안을 기웃이 들여다 본다. 아마 書記이리라. 副署長은 눈섭을 한 태로 모흐며, 嚴格하게 소리를 질럿다.
『許可업시 내 방에 들어오지 말아. 지금 事務가 바뿌다』
書記는 물러갓다.
『아아, 그 時代에는 別別 사람이 잇섯지오...』 不意에 副署長은 이런 말을 하고, 興奮된 듯이 방 안을 왓다갓다 한다.
『아마, 당신이 나의 心臟을 찌저 버렷습니다... 우리야노브를 생각하십니까? 저 死刑에 處한 사람입니다? 내가 그 사람과 한 班이엇습니다...』
『그런데, 대관절 당신은 무슨 일로 나를 呼出하엿습니까, 그 까닭을 일러 주시지오?』
『아아, 그것입니까... 젊을 때, 大學生 때ㅅ일이 생각이 나서, 당신이 어떠케 變하셧는가 뵈옵고 십헛습니다... 나는 大學時代부터 당신을 안 까닭에 그래서...』
『그러면 敬意를 表하기 爲한 때문입니다그려!』
『努하셧습니까? 너그러운 마음으로 容恕해 주십시오! 그 때ㅅ 생각이 나서 견딜 수 업섯습니다... 그리고, 당신이 쓰신 것을 愛讀하고 잇섯기에, 당신을 뵈옵고 십헛섯습니다...』
그는 문득 입슐을 다물엇다. 그리고는 窓을 向하야 不動의 姿勢를 取하고 잇섯다. 나는 일어나 긔침을 하엿다... 그는 急히 나를 돌아보는데, 그의 얼굴은 흐리엿더라. 무안한 微笑가 입슐에 흐르며,
『이우에 더 당신을 붓들고 잇스랴 안습니다.』
그는 다정하게 말을 하고, 한숨을 내여쉬엇다. 잠간 무엇을 생각하고 잇다가, 손을 내여밀며,
『그러면 한우님이시여! 당신에게 福을 나리옵소서!... 인제 다시 만날 機會가 업겟지오, 만일...』
『警察署에 불리어 오지 안흐면 말입니까?』
그는 失笑하엿다. 그리고 무안한 微笑를 떠이우며,
『우리의 生命은 쩔읍니다, 모든 것이 우리와 함께 가버리는 것입니다...』
나는 警察署를 나왓다. 한동안, 나의 생각을 整理할 수가 업섯다. 모든 無秩序의 廢止와 根絶을 爲하야 設置된 警官이, 제가 일즉이 行한 無秩序를 통쾌하게 追憶할 뿐더러, 물로 빠지는 사람이 풀입을 부어잡든, 그 追憶을 貴重히 녀김은 진실로 까닭 몰을 일이다. 그것은 그도 나 모양으로 머리에 白髮이 헛날리게 되고, 支離한 人生의 길에서 生命의 꼿을 애닯은 지내인 날에 일허버린 까닭이 아닐가?.
...(1921. 6. 15)...＜144＞
