발행일	1922년 07월 10일
기사제목	文學과 靈感 (Literature and Inspiration)
필자	江戶學人
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Essay)

文學과 靈感
						江戶學人

Even where a blade of grass sprouts, even where a flower blooms, even where a stone rolls (轉), it seems there is some incomprehensible power. If we watch it mindlessly, it sprouts and withers, blooms and falls, rolls and settles down (坐) again—moment by moment, it is nothing more than a truly meaningless, routinely occurring phenomenon. However, if we observe a little deeper, within its inner surface, amidst the silence and absence of sound, there must be a kind of profound and mysterious activity. There must surely be that incomprehensible power, that formless metaphysical activity without sound or smell (無聲無臭). It cannot be otherwise.

I wish to see life as surely present even in the sprouting grass, the blooming flower, the rolling stone. Whether viewing the object subjectively or observing the object itself objectively, we cannot help but acknowledge its incomprehensible power, or activity. Why is this so? Let us suppose, for a moment, that within such objects, there is no such power or activity. If so, everything manifested in the phenomena of all these objects would have to be denied. Consequently, they would have no life, no vital energy (精氣), no change. Simply put, if this external world, the material world, lacked the power of inner life, that world would become an ice continent, a cold world like the moon.

Similarly, it seems that through all things like plants, insects, fish, tiles, and rubble (草木蟲魚瓦礫 等屬)—that is, through both living and non-living things as defined in science—there is alike a kind of life and vital energy. (At first glance, this seems highly irrational). Is that all? When we observe the universe or human life, we keenly realize such things. Whether it is hypothetical or real, behind the universe, there must be—call it the Creator, God, the Jade Emperor (玉皇), or the Supreme Being (上帝), whatever name is fine—but we deeply, deeply realize the latent presence of such an entity's incomprehensible power and<104> activity. And within human life, there must be—whether called spirit (精神), psyche (心理), or consciousness (意識), it's hard to find a suitable name immediately—but we can also discover a kind of incomprehensible power and activity. Therefore, it seems that in the universe and human life, and also in nature, there exists infinite, vast, eternal, universal life, and a spiritual body (靈體) of harmony, unity, boundlessness, and immutability.

If there is indeed some truth to this, I believe we can take this idea and examine it within the realms of religion, philosophy, and art. If religion lacks a truly religious faith, its belief system becomes mere idolatry or a superstitious faith of primitive people seeking blessings and warding off calamities. It will ultimately be unable to approach the God, Buddha, or Heaven residing on the other shore, over there. It becomes a religion without life, without value. Likewise, if philosophy lacks a truly philosophical observation embodying its true nature, its observation becomes nothing more than the view of an ordinary person (凡人所視) or the opinion of the common crowd (俗衆所見), a kind of shallow observation of primitive people. It decidedly cannot become a philosophy that proclaims metaphysical, trans-human truth (眞理). I believe art is also the same. By the same logic, if art is not an artistic expression for art's sake, it becomes mere playful amusement or an entertaining curiosity (骨董品). Can such a thing ever become art created out of pure aesthetic impulse and the joy of creation? I think it is entirely impossible. Whatever it may be—be it religious faith, philosophical inquiry, or artistic expression—it seems one cannot recognize its true face, profound principles, substance, or origin merely through superficial appearances, empty talk, and formalism. At the very least, unless one removes the colored glasses, takes off the conventional robes, dusts off the dust-covered box, and approaches it with a transcendent and broad-minded eye (心眼)—an eye free from any stain (汚穢), speck of staleness (陳敗), or drop of impurity (混濁); broad enough to encompass the universe, large enough to cover rivers and mountains, bright enough to eclipse the sun, and clear enough to surpass a bright mirror (明鏡)—the results will ultimately return to nothingness, like a water bubble (烏有水泡).

I have taken too many detours and spoken too vaguely. However, to return to the main topic, "Literature and Inspiration," I must mention beforehand the unavoidable circumstances that required spending the above few words.

Literature and Inspiration—in one way, it's easy to talk about, and in another, it's difficult. It can be a matter of common sense and public knowledge, yet at the same time, it seems imbued with extremely secret and unique colors. Mencius (孟子) of China, renowned during the Warring States period as a politician, orator,<105> and philosopher, cultivated his "vast, flowing energy" (浩然之氣) well, yet when questioned by his disciple, he first called it "difficult to explain" (難言). Also, the relationship between men and women is extremely universal and commonplace, yet, on the other hand, it is extremely secret and private (隱諱). Since I am neither a cultivator of the "vast energy" nor a researcher of sexual matters, and certainly lack such qualifications, there is no need to prattle on further. However, to speak fully and without reservation about Literature and Inspiration truly seems extremely difficult (極難且難). Especially as I hold the brush, I simply do not know what to say.

However, to speak of Literature and Inspiration, it seems I must first say a little about what inspiration (靈感) is. What is this thing called inspiration? As mentioned earlier, inspiration itself is, on the one hand, public and common-sensical, while on the other hand, it seems extremely privileged and secret. This is perhaps easiest for those who have experienced it to understand (without needing explanation) and most difficult for those who have only heard of it through blind common sense to comprehend.

We possess perception (知覺), will (意志), and emotion (感情). We utilize these three things to live and act. If the activity of these three ceased, our life and actions would stop right there or perish. In other words, our life and actions seem to be nothing more than the activity of these three. However, our daily actions today have become quite unconscious, unintentional, and unfeeling—natural, mechanical actions. It is not that these three have completely left us and run far away. Rather, our innate activity of these three, having developed and progressed daily since leaving the womb, has ultimately resulted in our actions not necessarily beginning after relying on perception, appealing to emotion, and consulting the will each time. Instead, that developed and trained activity itself gives rise to unconscious, mechanical actions. Thus, our ordinary daily routines—getting up, sitting down, eating, dressing, studying, talking, working—have truly become mechanized. However, in the course of our daily lives, or perhaps in special circumstances, suddenly, or even from long-accumulated experience, a special psychological activity arises—mysterious, hard to describe, hard to shape—transcending the mechanical activity of those three trained aspects. Even if its activity arises in an instant (刹那), it carries infinite eternity; even if its activity erupts momentarily, it already possesses unchanging permanence. And while in our ordinary daily actions, the three aspects of perception, emotion, and will often<106> proceed in parallel, in such cases, setting aside the activity of perception and will, the activity of emotion is likely the most intense. Of course, even if perception and will are active, they are likely already suppressed under emotion. Even if the activity arose from sensory perception or internal impulse, or even if it went through some process involving the selective action of the will, it seems to have already achieved emotional independence. That activity, at that time and place, I believe, is "inspiration" (靈感) for us. It may manifest as sorrow, wonder, joy, strangeness (怪訝), anger, deep emotion (感激), fear, compassion, desire, hatred, or admiration (歎美). Whatever form it takes, our inspiration must possess eternity, universality, immutability, and validity (妥當性). However, if there is even the slightest flaw (虧隙), defect (缺陷), indifference (冷淡), or consideration (顧慮) in its activity, it has already lost the validity to be inspiration. Only by being extremely exclusive, incompatible, and reaching the highest peak can it qualify as inspiration. It is not something we can always obtain, receive anywhere, or that everyone can realize. Without a special place, a special time, a special personality, it is ultimately not an object one can obtain, taste, pierce, and chew. Therefore, for those whose emotions are dull (遲鈍), it is difficult to receive this inspiration. For the lazy (懶惰), it is difficult to taste this inspiration. For those without passion (熱情), it is difficult to realize this inspiration. Furthermore, if the environment and scenery are too familiar to our eyes and ears to evoke any sense of strangeness or wonder (怪異難惑), it is difficult to trigger our inspiration. Consequently, when one simply greets and sends off each day unconsciously, no inspiration whatsoever will arise. This inspiration, indeed, cannot be experienced or perceived solely through the physical senses (肉的 感官). Generally, when we recognize any object, it is, of course, a fact that we must rely on the power of our senses. However, just as we need innate, necessary categories within us to recognize objects, for this inspiration too, we naturally rely on external sensations like sight, hearing, taste, smell, touch, etc. (視聽味嗅觸 等). That is also a fact. Yet, with those sensations alone, it seems impossible to obtain inspiration. If one could receive inspiration through sensation alone, then all humans—children and adults, women and men, the lazy and the neurologically impaired, the uneducated and the knowledgeable—would receive inspiration daily, everywhere they turn. But reality is not so. External sensations—what we see, hear, smell, touch, all sorts of things—come to all of us, regardless of who we are, following time and circumstance, from the moment we open our eyes in the morning. However, does everyone who receives these sensations consequently<107> receive inspiration? That is undeniably something we can refute. Therefore, although inspiration seems like a very privileged, aristocratic item, it is definitely not a prostitute (賣淫女). Nor is it a thief (竊盜者), a beggar child (乞食兒), or a traitor (叛逆者). I believe it is one pure as powdered snow on a moonlit night; bright and clear as the blue waves of the great ocean; upright and incorruptible as the phoenix on the paulownia tree. It is obedient as the shepherd's sheep in the field; pure and lofty as the crystal sun in the autumn sky. Therefore, one who wishes to approach it must not be an ugly person (醜人). One must not be evil, deceitful, or recklessly violent (狂悖者). Since it is itself bright, clear, clean, noble, and great, it also demands an ideal partner like itself. Only after finding such a person of character will it consent to marriage, and not be ashamed or avoid shaking hands and kissing. Therefore, we must shed the vulgar, stale, filthy, corrupt physical appearance and become persons of character possessing a spiritual mind's eye (靈的 心眼) that concentrates all virtues—purity (純一), selflessness (無私), refined beauty (粹美), gentleness (溫和), elegance (優雅), clarity (淸朗), loftiness (高越)—in one aspect. Only then can we seek a union with the maiden of inspiration—graceful, beautiful, elegant, and charming (婉美秀艶)—who resides in her quiet, soft, serene, and clean boudoir. We could live together, and enjoy life together.

Pity us! When a hundred flowers are in full bloom and spring's excitement overflows (滔淊), when green trees are lush and summer shade is deep (濃濃), in a place where clear streams mirror like a woman's looking glass (女鏡) and purple mountains are like a woman's painting (女畫)—there, guarding her chastity alone, like a thousand years in a day, like myriad eons in a moment, plucking and loosening the fine strings of her soft and weak zither at flowering dawns and moonlit evenings—we received cold treatment from that maiden of inspiration. We were rejected. Because we were wicked, because we were ugly creatures. Because we were not persons of character possessing the mind's eye. But for that maiden, it must have been legitimate self-defense. It must have been upright and honorable conduct. I think that was the only path she could take.

Indeed! That inspiration is a maiden possessing trans-human qualities. She is not one easily discovered or offered affection by mere material mechanisms or physical eyes. Only one who is divine, spiritual, and a person of character equipped with the mind's eye can obtain her, can receive her. Therefore, those who did were not ordinary people, not the common crowd. They must have utilized the opportunity of receiving and obtaining at least some partial or temporary inspiration to advance on their own paths. Though I cannot list examples one by one, nor is there a need to, throughout the countless centuries of changing times (物換星移), those various individuals hailed by the world with flashy praise (浮華的 稱頌), false honor (虛僞的 名譽), and blind admiration (盲目的 讚美) as so-called heroes (英雄兒), great figures (豪傑人), remarkable men (奇男兒),<108> or true men (大丈夫)—they were all users of it. I am neither praising, exaggerating, nor commending them. Nor do I think there is any need to. Of course, I am not criticizing the highs and lows, successes and failures, merits and demerits, gains and losses of their deeds, achievements, or actions. Whatever the facts may be, that they came and went for a moment within their own scope and degree, within their so-called space and time, and left some mark—even if it wasn't that profound, lofty, or grand—was spurred by the inspiration received, at least in part, or temporarily, or by chance. It became a stimulus (刺戟). It became encouragement (促進). It led to exertion (奮勵) and accomplishment (成就). Their so-called relics, anecdotes, epitaphs, biographies, histories, and chronicles seem to be booklets recording their level of inspiration. Their so-called great achievements (偉功), meritorious deeds (功勳), great affairs (大事), or monumental works (巨業) are all, it seems, hints and products of their level of inspiration. Regardless of their good or evil, gains or losses, the fact that their empty names, if nothing else, have not decayed and are transmitted to this day, though thousands of years have passed—what could it be but the gift of inspiration received at some opportunity, in a single instant, a single moment back then? To give just 2 or 3 simple examples unavoidably: A word from an elder, a phrase from an old book, the flash of a sword, advice from a passerby, the fall of an apple, the drifting of duckweed—these became the strategies of Alexander (亞歷山) or Genghis Khan (成吉思汗), the grand plans of King Gwanggaeto (廣開土王) or Eulji Mundeok (乙支文德), the great deeds of Washington (와싱톤) or Lincoln (린컨), Newton's (뉴-톤) theory of gravity, or Columbus's (코롬보쓰) discovery of the continent. Who hasn't received a word from an elder? Who hasn't read a phrase from an old book? Who hasn't seen the flash of a sword? Who hasn't heard advice from a passerby? Who hasn't seen the fall of an apple or the drifting of duckweed? But ordinary people saw only with physical eyes and heard only with physical ears. They did not let it pass through the mind's eye (心眼). Therefore, ordinary people saw it as a routine matter. There was nothing strange or shocking about it. Consequently, no inspiration arose in response. Those others saw these things with an extraordinary mind's eye. Consequently, they received a kind of pulsation (鼓動) of inspiration. Ultimately, they did what was appropriate for their level. How powerful indeed is the force of inspiration!?

However, it is not limited only to statesmen or scientists. I believe it is most true for religious figures, philosophers, and artists. Though it may sound like my own dogmatic assertion, I want to forcefully argue and cry out: if you want to become a religious figure, a philosopher, or an artist, first cultivate<109> the capacity for inspiration (靈感性)! Anyone who pontificates about this or that without ever having received inspiration even once—no matter how clever, cunning, or agile they are—I believe falsehood (虛僞), deceit (欺瞞), ambition (野慾), or conspiracy (陰謀) must be lurking and accompanying their inner workings. Where the capacity for inspiration is lacking, I think it results in false religion, pathological philosophy, and dead art. Without me needing to pretend to know and speak at length about this publicly, just look at Shakyamuni Buddha (釋迦如來)! Look at the Holy Teacher Jesus (耶蘇聖師)! Look at Master Confucius (仲尼夫子)! Look at philosophers like Plato (푸라톤) and Kant (칸트), or literary giants like Tolstoy (쓰토이) and Tagore (타콜-)!—The world knows them all. There's no room for me alone to extol their character, their lives, their legacies. But one thing I particularly want to mention is how much the inspiration they received ceaselessly throughout their lives, at various times and places, influenced their religions, their philosophies, their arts. Indeed, their inspiration was great. It was sublime. It was profound. It was earnest and composed, agile and clear. They received all kinds of inspiration—from suffering, from anguish, from life and death, from people, from things, from plants, from birds and beasts, from insects and fish, from mountains, from water, in the morning, in the evening, in cold, in heat. Their inspiration created their philosophies, founded their religions, and taught them their arts. If they had lacked inspiration, no matter how great or monumental they were, they would have been hungry. They would have been thirsty, breathless, cold, and their heads would have spun. But because they constantly received inspiration, they laughed and cried as they pleased. They loved and hated as they wished. They danced and sang to their hearts' content. To the extreme, I see all of this as the product of inspiration. Of course, I cannot know if this is merely the prejudice of one individual. Ah, how great is the power of inspiration!?

I have spoken too digressively in explaining inspiration. Setting aside the length, I am only ashamed that its meaning has not been fully and thoroughly elucidated.

Now, I intend to take the thread of inspiration and enter the domain called Literature. However, to understand Literature and Inspiration, one first needs to know what Literature is. But since I spoke a little about this under the title "The Essence of Literature" (文學의 本體) in a previous issue of this magazine, although it was quite incomplete and ambiguous (曖昧朦朧), I will omit it for now.<110>

However, no matter how hard we try, we cannot smell a fragrance strong enough to pierce our noses from artificial flowers or flower paintings (造花나 花圖). Likewise, we probably cannot discover lovely affection from the corpse of Yang Guifei (楊貴妃) or a portrait of Cleopatra (구레오빠트라). No matter how skillful the making of artificial flowers, no matter how exquisite the depiction in flower paintings, they are lifeless falsehoods (虛僞物). Even the most developed human or animal mind will surely fail in smelling their fragrance. No matter how peerless the beauty (美毛) of Yang Guifei, no matter how outstanding the graceful demeanor (姸態) of Cleopatra, they are already corpses (死骸), relics of the past (故物). They have no life. They have no vital energy (精氣). No matter how filled with passion and ambition, how sharp one may be, one cannot attach affection to them. We must surely smell the sweet and savory scent only from flowers that bloomed on trees that received the warm rays of the sun and drank the moist water from underground. Likewise, it seems we can only exchange hot and thick affection with a maiden who received the virtuous grace (德澤) of loving parents and was nurtured in a harmonious family. Why? Because that flower, that woman, possesses life and vital energy.

Literature is exactly like that. From literature lacking life and vital energy, it seems impossible to recognize its value or arouse interest. Literature with life and vital energy means literature that has received inspiration. From literature that has not passed through inspiration, no matter how meticulous the observation, rich the appreciation, or well-equipped with cultivation and genius one might be, it seems impossible to truly recognize and discover the life and vital energy of literature. It would be like putting one's nose to an artificial flower, waiting for the scent to vibrate; like stretching out a hand to a corpse, hoping for the pulse to quicken. It would be futile effort (徒勞) and yield no gain (所得).

I am not a man of letters (文學家). Furthermore, I am not one who constantly receives and obtains inspiration to engage with literature. I have yet to write even a single short story or a single line of poetry that could be called creative work (創作). Moreover, I have not read that many literary works widely, nor do I have any critical eye (批評의 眼目) for them whatsoever. It is with self-reproach (愧謝) for excessive presumption (猥濫) that I dare to speak so frankly from such a position. However, might it not be fortunate if even a part of this wild talk (狂言) hits the mark?

Just as one must first cultivate inspiration to become a religious figure, and must first cultivate inspiration to become a philosopher, it is of course necessary to cultivate the capacity for inspiration (靈感性) to become an artist. Can one become a literary figure without cultivating inspiration?<111> This inspiration, more than anything else, seems to have the most appropriate, necessary, indispensable connection to literature, unable to be separated even for a moment (須臾). For the author too, a work written without passing through this capacity for inspiration seems like a reappearance of a corpse (死骸). For critics or readers, if they do not approach literature with a capacity for inspiration at least equal to what they have experienced themselves, it will become like a parrot's mimicry or a blind person's groping (모막댁이질).

Indeed, the life of literature lies in inspiration. To remove inspiration from literature is to take away its life. Literature written through inspiration, and literature read and criticized with shared inspiration, possesses eternity. It possesses universality. It possesses validity and immutability. When we look at literary works called masterpieces, we often discover these qualities of eternity, universality, validity, and immutability. Setting aside the works of our own country's ancestors that we all know well, consider, from China, the poetry of Li Bai and Du Fu (李杜) or the writings of the 18 Masters (18家), or from Europe, the plays of Shakespeare (쉑쓰피아) or the novels of Tolstoy (톨쓰토이). Though they are writings of ancient people from thousands or hundreds of years ago, and their customs differ, languages vary, and distances are great, being works of foreigners, why do they evoke such inexhaustible interest and command recognition of absolute value? It is, of course, because their works possess eternity, universality, validity, and immutability. They all presented their works amidst inspiration received from the pulsation (鼓動) of high spirit. Therefore, they possess an immortal, eternal, unchanging life like Heaven and Earth, and hold a universal, valid truth that all people can sympathize with. If their works had departed from the capacity for inspiration, they would have all ended up as waste paper (휴지감) for papering windows and walls, blowing noses, or wrapping things back in their own time.

However, saying their literature has life does not mean teaching its phrases, its theories, or its meanings (辭意). It refers to the capacity for inspiration within its inner surface. If the author's own capacity for inspiration is not active in a literary work, no matter how much lofty discourse (高談峻論) is freely spouted, or how many ornate phrases (綺辭麗句) are arrayed, it becomes like dressing a breathless corpse in burial clothes (歛衣) of fine silk (綾羅錦繡). No matter how splendid or grand those clothes are, their destination is none other than a grave of three feet of earth (三尺抔土). There is no day for it to reappear in this world. Literature lacking the capacity for inspiration inevitably dies right where it is born. No, perhaps it sometimes miscarries even before birth.<112>

Now, regarding what inspiration is, I spoke briefly above, but because I failed to explain it well with examples, there might be difficulties in understanding. Furthermore, as I try to speak of inspiration specifically in the context of literature, a feeling of dissatisfaction arises even more, so I intend to add a few more words.

It is an obvious fact, needless to say twice, and also mentioned frequently above, that those who intend to become literary figures must cultivate inspiration. Now, among human inspirations, the most representative things seem to be, first, tears, and then blood and muscles. Our inspiration, put nicely, possesses a kind of divinity (神性); put badly, a kind of demonic power (魔力). This is something we encounter often. Especially, I know it is frequently experienced by those who pay attention to literature. That is, when observing human life, when viewing the scenery of mountains and rivers, when facing the changes of the seasons, even when reading novels or watching plays. Whenever we come into contact with the myriad things (森羅萬象) in this universe.

To put it concretely: when the red sun rises in the eastern sky and morning haze envelops the village huts (村盧), when the setting sun hangs on the western mountains and evening smoke settles in the riverside trees (江樹), in a mountain temple deep in the night where tigers and monkeys call to each other, where the sea waves stretch for ten thousand leagues (萬頃) and seagulls and ducks fly freely, when frost lies thick on the maple forest (楓林) and wild geese fly under the moon, when flowers bloom in the garden head (園頭) and warblers sing in the courtyard plum trees (庭梅)—one cannot help but shed a line of tears. When passing through ancient battlefields where white bones scatter on the sandy field (沙場) and eerie ghosts wail (哀號) in the dark night, when visiting old capital cities of bygone days where wild grass tangles and stone fragments lie rolling, when vigorous and brave young men flash the vengeful gleam of their swords, when beautiful and elegant young men and women kindle the passionate flames of love (戀火)—one also cannot help but hold back a line of tears. When seeing the final fate of heroes who once soared with spirit to the heavens but were eventually exiled (竄逐) to lonely islands, when seeing the lives of the poor who befriend the furnace even in scorching heat and wear ragged hemp clothes even in ear-numbing cold, when hearing the songs of boys and girls, fine as thread and resonant (嚠喨) as the sound of metal (金鐵), when hearing the speeches of patriots (志士), fervent as blazing fire (燃火) and grand as mountains—one also cannot help but squeeze out a line of tears. In such cases, simultaneously with the flowing tears, hot blood will surely surge within the veins. Consequently, the flesh will quiver. Those tears are truly tears we neither expected nor prepared for. When shedding those tears, one simply cries because the tears come out. There is no reason or reflection (反省) like "Why are tears coming out?" "Why must I shed tears?" "Why can't I help but shed tears?"<113> As mentioned above, it seems that rather than the actions of will and perception, the pure action of emotion alone is intensely active. If one were to ask the reason or reflect, those tears would already dry up. They would vanish in an instant. If, at that moment, while shedding tears, I were to ask myself, "Why am I crying?" or if someone else asked, "Why are you crying?" there would be nothing to explain, nothing to describe. If forced to speak, there would only be words like, "Because I am sad, or happy, or angry." The quivering flesh and boiling blood follow the same principle. Indeed, those flowing tears, quivering muscles, boiling blood—they seem to be the result (所以) of the pulsation of some indescribable, mysterious 'something' that cannot be expressed in words or depicted in sentences. That, truly, seems to be inspiration. However, those tears, those muscles, those drops of blood (血滴)—they are not shed, boiled, or made to jump out, boil, and quiver artificially, deliberately. If one tries to do it deliberately, they become even more rigid. This is something we often experience when attending someone's funeral vigil (靈筵) to offer condolences. At that time, while mourning for the deceased, if one tries, out of a sense of obligation for appearances and social etiquette (體面 人事), to cry mournfully and even shed tears, often, quite the opposite happens: the brow becomes stiffer, and even the wailing sound seems to crawl back inside. Likewise, tears truly cannot be squeezed out by force, blood cannot be made to boil by force, and muscles are difficult to make jump suddenly by force. They must await the command of our inspiration to flow, boil, and quiver. And those tears flow out in various streams (派流). Commonly, we think tears only come from simple sorrow, and consequently, when tears flow, anyone quickly associates it with sadness. But in reality, there is nothing that expresses our expressions in such diverse ways as tears. The tears shed by a child who loses a fight or an attempt to snatch something from another child are tears of desire (欲望), tears of resentment (忿恨). The tears shed by a newlywed woman visited by her family are tears of joy (歡喜). The tears shed after losing beloved parents or bidding eternal farewell (永訣) to a trusted husband are tears of sorrow (悲哀), tears of despair (失望). The tears shed when seeing someone off at the station (驛頭) or leaving home for a distant journey (遠征) are tears of sorrowful parting (悵缺). The tears shed while embracing a friend by a riverside tree (江樹) or thinking of one's home mountains (鄕山) on a moonlit night are tears of longing (戀慕). Besides these, we pour out and sprinkle tears according to various circumstances whenever the opportunity arises. Thus, though the streams of tears can be counted in many ways, their source is one.<114> That is, inspiration. Therefore, if the wellspring (源泉) of inspiration dries up, its streams will naturally cease according to the flow of nature. The boiling of blood and quivering of flesh will also be the same.

Thus, human expression is revealed through tears, and the tears of expression flow out through inspiration. If humans lacked tears, they would be straw puppets (草偶), wooden statues (木像), and simultaneously beings of iron and stone (鐵石). If tears flowed without the pulsation of inspiration, they would be mere squeezed sap (*액) or wrung-out water droplets (水滴). However, by the power of Heaven, the power of God, the power of Buddha, we humans cannot be without tears, and tears cannot but be backed (後見) by inspiration.

Again, I have detoured significantly with the explanation of tears, but unless one approaches literature with the inspiration of that moment, that standpoint, where tears pour or blood boils and flesh quivers, it seems it will not do. If an author takes up the brush without possessing this inspiration, their work will surely become like the wailing of the chief mourner (喪主) at a funeral rite (小大祥), who merely wears the hemp mourning attire (屈巾祭服), holds the mourning staff (喪杖), and stands there making crying sounds. If a reader picks up a book without possessing this inspiration, it will become like tasting only the rind (것만 할는) of a watermelon (水苽) or hearing disconnected fragments of music. If a critic approaches another's work without possessing this inspiration, it will be like handing fire back to someone who has grabbed something hot, or putting a round lid (뚜깨/盖) on an angular (모진/角) vessel. In that creation, that appreciation, that criticism, there will be no eternity, universality, validity, or immutability; there will be no unity, organization, life, or value. Therefore, though it is presumptuous (濫越) of me, when approaching literature, if one has not received the baptism of inspiration, I wish to advise the author not to lift the brush, the reader not to open the book, and the critic not to utter a word. For I fear that a lifeless corpse (死骸) might appear before us, that a harmful demon might be born, or that friction (軋轢) breeding only suspicion (猜疑) might be provoked!... (June 20, Copied by Sin Sŭk [申湜抄]) <115>

**Summary**

This 1922 essay by "Edo Haksaeng" (江戶學人) argues passionately that "inspiration" (靈感, yeonggam) is the essential, life-giving force behind not only nature and human existence but critically, behind genuine religion, philosophy, and especially literature. Inspiration is depicted as a profound, often sudden, emotionally intense, and transcendent experience, distinct from mere intellect or sensory perception, requiring a pure heart and "mind's eye" (心眼). The author contrasts inspired literature, possessing eternal and universal value (citing figures from Mencius to Tolstoy), with lifeless, mechanical works that are mere imitations or corpses dressed in finery. True literary creation, appreciation, and criticism, the essay contends, are impossible without this vital, almost mystical connection to inspiration, often manifesting in spontaneous tears, boiling blood, or quivering muscles.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its intense focus on inner spirit, emotion, and a kind of transcendental insight ("inspiration," "mind's eye") as the source of true value and creation during the Japanese colonial period. This emphasis on non-material, spiritual depth could be interpreted as a subtle assertion of Korean identity and inner resilience against a backdrop of colonial modernity, rationalism, or perceived materialism. The call for moral purity and elevated character in the artist/receiver of inspiration might also reflect contemporary nationalist discourses emphasizing moral regeneration as a path for the nation. Historians might find the blend of traditional East Asian concepts (Mencius, vital energy) with Western examples (Shakespeare, Tolstoy, scientific figures) and the almost mystical tone useful for understanding intellectual currents and cultural self-perception among Korean intellectuals in the early 1920s.

文學과 靈感
						江戶學人
한 풀이 나는 대도 한 꼿이 피는 대도 한 돌이 굴으(轉)는 대도 무슨 不可思議의 힘이 잇는가 합니다. 우리가 그것을 無心히 보고 잇스면 낫다가 마르고 피엇다 떨어지고 굴으다 다시 주저 안는(坐) 瞬間 瞬間의 참 無意味한 依例히 그러할 1個 現象에 지나지 못하지만 조금 속 깁히 觀察하고 보면 그의 內面에는 말도 업고 소리도 업는 그 가온대에 一種의 玄妙 不可思議할 活動이 잇슬 것이외다. 반듯이 그 不可思議의 힘, 無聲無臭의 形而上學的 活動이 잇슬 것이외다. 그가 업서서는 아니 될 것이외다.
저는 싹나는 풀에도 열니는 꼿에도 굴으는 돌에도 반듯이 生命이 잇다고 보고 십흡니다. 主觀的으로 그 對象을 보던지 또는 客觀的 物로 物의 그 자신을 보던지 어느 편으로나 그의 不可思議할 힘을, 또는 活動을 우리는 認定치 아니할 수 업습니다. 왜 그러냐 하면 萬一 그런 物體의 內面에 그의 힘과 그의 活動이 업다고 假定합시다. 그러고 보면 그 온갓 物體가 現象에 나타나는 一切가 다 否認되고 말 것이외다. 딸하서 그에게는 生命이 업고 精氣가 업고 變化가 업슬 것이외다. 簡單히 말하면 萬一 이 外界 即 物質界에 內面的 生命의 힘이 업스면 그 世界는 한 氷洲나, 한 太陰의 冷却한 世界가 되고 말 것이외다.
그와가티 草木蟲魚瓦礫 等屬 即 科學上에서 말하는 生物 無生物을 通하야 그네들에게는 다가티 一種의 生命과 精氣가 잇는 듯 하외다. (얼는 보면 넘우나 不合理하지만). 그뿐이리까 우리가 宇宙나 人生을 觀察할 때 痛切히 그런 것을 깨달을 것이외다. 그는 假定이던지 實在이던지 宇宙의 뒤에는 반듯이 創造者라 할는지 神이라 할는지 玉皇이라 할는지 또는 上帝라 할는지 그의 이름이야 무엇이던지 조치만 그와가튼 누구의 不可思議할 힘과＜104＞ 活動이 潛在함을 깁히 깁히 께달을 것이오 人生의 안에는 반듯이 精神이라 할는지 心理라 할는지 意識이라 할는지 適當한 이름은 얼는 부티기가 어렵지만 역시 一種의 不可思議할 힘과 活動을 發見할 수 잇슬 것이외다. 그럼으로 宇宙와 人生에게는 또는 自然에게도 無限, 廣大, 永遠, 普遍한 生命과 調和, 一致, 無窮, 不變의 靈體가 잇는가 합니다.
이것이 果然 一理가 잇다 하면 이것을 끌고 가서 宗敎나 哲學이나 藝術界에 들어가 한번 考察할 수가 잇는가 합니다. 萬一 宗敎에도 宗敎스러운 宗敎的 信仰이 아니면 그의 信仰은 한 偶像崇拜나 祝福除禍하랴는 一個 迷信的 未開時代人의 信仰이 되고 말 것이외다. 到底히 彼岸에 잇는 那邊에 게신 神이나 佛이나 天이나에 近接할 수가 업슬 것이외다. 生命이 업는, 價値가 업는 宗敎가 되고 말 것이외다. 또는 哲學에도 哲學다운 眞面의 哲學的 觀察이 업스면 그의 觀察은 한 凡人所視나 俗衆所見에 지나지 못하는 一種의 淺近한 原始時代人의 觀察이 되고 말 것이외다. 結團코 形而上的 超人間性의 眞理를 天命하는 哲學이 될 수는 업는가 합니다. 藝術이 또한 그러한 줄 압니다. 藝術에도 가튼 理致로 藝術 때문의 藝術的 表現이 아니면 그는 한 遊戱的 작난거리나 娛樂的 骨董品이 되고 말 것이외다. 그것으로는 아모리 하야도 純美한 衝動 創造의 歡喜로 造成된 藝術이 되겟습니까? 全혀 不可能일가 합니다. 무엇이나 勿論하고 곳 말하자면 宗敎上 信仰이나 哲學上 考察이나 藝術上 表現이나 다가티 外面皮相空談形式만으로는 그의 眞面, 奧理, 實質, 本源을 認識할 수 업슴인가 합니다. 적어도 色眼鏡을 벗고 陳套衣를 벗어노코 蒙塵箱을 털고 一點의 汚穢, 一粒의 陳敗, 一滴의 混濁이 업시 넓기는 宇宙를 싸고 크기는 江山을 덥고 밝이는 太陽을 접어노코 맑기는 明鏡을 凌蔑할만한 超越汎博한 心眼으로 거긔에 臨하지 아니하야서는 그의 效果가 畢竟은 다 烏有水泡에 돌아가고 말가 합니다.

두말할 것도 업시 문학가가 되랴는 이에게 靈感을 양성하여야 될 것은 명백한 사실이오. 또는 이 우에도 자조 말한 바외다. 그런대 인생의 靈感 중에 가장 대표적 물건이 될 것은 몬저 눈물이오 다음에 피와 근육인가 합니다. 우리의 靈感은 조케 말하자면 일개의 神性, 낫게 말하자면 일종의 魔力을 가젓는가 합니다. 이는 우리가 각금 각금 맛나는 경우외다. 그 중에도 文學에 유의하시는 이가 자조 자조 경험하시는 바로 암니다. 즉 인생의 생활을 볼 때에 山水의 풍경을 구경할 때에 時候의 변환에 당할 때에 심지어 소설을 읽을 때 연극을 구경할 때까지에. 이 우주간 삼라만상에 접촉할 제마다.
그를 구체적으로 말하자면 우리가 紅日이 東天에 올으고 朝霞가 村盧를 둘넛슬 때, 落照가 서산에 걸니고 夕烟이 江樹에 잠겻슬 때 山寺에 夜寂하고 虎猿이 相嘯하는 곳에서 海波는 萬頃이오 鷗鳧가 비상하는대서 楓林에 霜高하고 月下에 鴈飛할 제 園頭에 花開하고, 庭梅에 鶯歌할 제 一行의 눈물을 뿌리지 아니할 수 업나이다. 백골이 沙場에 드날리고 怪鬼가 暗夜에 哀號하는 고대의 戰場에 지낼 때 荒草가 엉크러지고 片石이 둥그러 단기는 昔日의 故都를 구경할 때 壯快悍勇한 혈기아가 복수의 검광을 번득이고 姸美端雅한 청년남녀가 열정의 戀火를 켤 제 또한 一行의 눈물을 먹음지 아니할 수 업는가 합니다. 일시에는 기개가 충천타가 필경에는 孤島에 竄逐된 영웅들의 말로를 볼 때 쯔는 듯한 더위에도 熔爐를 동모 삼고 귀가 빠질 듯한 치위에도 남루한 베옷을 글치는 貧人의 생애를 볼 때 가늘기는 실과 갓고 嚠喨하기는 金鐵의 소리와 가튼 소년소녀의 노래를 들을 제 열렬하기는 燃火와 갓고 웅대하기는 산악과 가튼 志士의 열변을 들을 제 또한 一行의 눈물을 짜내지 아니할 수 업슬 것이외다. 그와가튼 경우에는 눈물이 흘느는 동시에 반듯이 혈관 안에 더운 피가 솟을 것이외다. 딸하서 살이 뛰놀 것이외다. 그 눈물은 실로 우리가 예상치도 준비치도 아니한 눈물이외다. 그 눈물을 흘릴 때에는 다만 그 눈물에 나옴으로 흘릴 뿐이오. 왜 눈물이 나오느냐 왜 눈물을 흘려야 쓰겟느냐 왜 눈물을 흘리지 안하면 안되겟느냐 하는 그런 이유나 반성은＜113＞ 물론 업습니다. 저 위에서 말한 것과 가티 의지와 지각의 작용보다 감정이 순전한 작용뿐이 격렬히 활동하는가 합니다. 만일 그 이유를 뭇고 반성을 하게 되면 그 눈물은 벌서 말늠니다. 어늬 결을에 흔적을 감츨 것이외다. 만일 제가 그 당시에 눈물을 흘리면서 「왜 내가 눈물을 흘리나」하고 반문하거나 또는 남이 「너는 왜 눈물을 흘리느냐」 물으면 설명할 말이 업고 형용할 말이 업시 됩니다. 부득이 말한다 하면 「나는 슬어서 혹은 조아서 혹은 憤하야서」 하는 그런 말밧게 업슬 것이외다. 살이 뛰는 것도 피가 끌는 것도 역시 동일한 이치외다. 과연 그 흘느는 눈물 뛰는 근육 끌는 피야말로 언어로는 형용할 수 업는 文句로는 묘사할 수 업는 일종 불가사의의 무엇이 鼓動하는 所以인가 합니다. 그야말로 靈感인가 합니다. 그러나 그 눈물 그 근육 그 血滴이야말로 인위적으로 일부러 흘리고 일부러 끌이고 일부러 처서 나오고 끌코 뛰는 것은 아니외다. 그는 일부러 하랴고 노력하면 도리어 더 구더지는 것이외다. 그것은 우리가 남의 靈筵에 가 조문할 때 흔이 경험하는 바외다. 그때에 남의 죽은 사람을 위하야 弔喪하면서 슬은 표정을 아니하야서는 체면상 人事가 틀리겟다는 심리로 한번 슬께 울고 눈물까지 흘녀보랴고 하면 도리어 정반대로 눈쌀이 더 꼿꼿하야지고 哭聲까지 속으로 긔어 들어가는 수가 잇습니다. 그와가티 눈물은 과연 억지로 짜아낼 수 업는 것이오 피도 억지로 끌케 할 수 업는 것이오 근육도 억지로 뚝 놀게 하기는 어렵소이다. 그들은 반듯이 우리의 일종 영감의 지휘를 기다려 가지고 흘느고 끌코 뛰는 것이외다. 그러고 그 눈물은 여러가지 派流로 난외여 흘느나이다. 세상에서 보통 우리가 생각하는 눈물은 다만 단순한 비애에서만 나오는 줄로 알고 딸하서 눈물이 흘는다 하면 누구던지 얼는 슬음이라는 연상을 하게 됩니다. 그러나 실은 눈물처럼 우리의 표정을 多種多樣으로 표현하는 것은 업는가 합니다. 어린 아해가 싸홈을 하거나 물건을 빼앗으랴다가 다른 아해에게 지고 흘리는 눈물은 욕망의 눈물이오 忿恨의 눈물이외다. 신혼의 여자가 친정일가의 방문을 밧고 흘리는 눈물은 환희의 눈물이외다. 사랑하는 부모를 일코 신뢰하던 남편을 永訣하고 흘리는 눈물은 비애의 눈물이오 실망의 눈물이외다. 驛頭에 손을 보내고 원정에 집을 떠날 때 흘리는 눈물은 悵缺의 눈물이외다. 江樹에 벗을 품고 月夜에 鄕山을 생각하며 흘리는 눈물은 연모의 눈물이외다. 그 외에도 우리는 기회가 잇슬 때마다 여러 사정을 딸하 눈물을 쏫고 뿌리나이다. 그와가티 눈물의 派流야 몃으로 계산할 수가 잇지만 그러나 그 근원은 하나의다.＜114＞ 즉 靈感이외다. 그럼으로 靈感의 源泉이 말으면 그의 派流는 자연의 세로 딸아서 업서질 것이외다. 피와 살의 끌코 뛰는 것이 또한 동일할 것이외다.
그와가티 인생의 표정은 눈물을 통하야 나타내고 표정의 눈물은 靈感을 통하야 흘러나오나이다. 만일 인생에게 눈물이 업스면 그는 草偶의 인생이며 木像의 인생이고 동시에 鐵石의 인생인가 합니다. 靈感의 鼓動이 업시 눈물이 흘넛다하면 그는 찍어 말는 *액이나 쥐어 발는 水滴인가 합니다. 그러나 天의 힘으로 神의 힘으로 佛의 힘으로 우리 인생에게는 눈물이 업슬 수 업고 눈물에는 靈感이 後見치 아니할 수 업는가 합니다.
다시 눈물의 설명으로 경로를 만히 돌앗습니다마는 이 눈물이 쏘다지는 또는 피가 끌코 살이 뛰는 그 찰나, 그 입지의 靈感을 가지고 文學에 대하지 아니하면 아니 될가 합니다. 만일 작자가 이 靈感을 가지지 아니하고 붓을 든다 하면 그의 작품은 꼭 喪家의 小大祥 때에 屈巾祭服만 입고 喪杖만 집고 공연히 서서 울음소리만 내는 喪主님의 곡성이 되고 말 것이외다. 독자가 이 靈感을 가지지 아니하고 책을 든다 하면 그는 꼭 것만 할는 水苽의 맛이나 도막도막 끈어진 음악의 소리가 되고 말 것이외다. 비평가가 이 靈感을 가지지 아니하고 남의 작품에 대한다 하면 그는 뜨거운 것을 잡은 자에게 돌리어 불을 주거나 모진(角) 그릇에 둔근 뚜깨(盖)를 덥는 심이 되고 말 것이외다. 그 창작, 그 감상, 그 비평에는 무슨 영원성도 보편성도 또는 타당성도 불변성도 업고 무슨 통일 무슨 조직 또는 무슨 생명 무슨 가치도 업시 되고 말 것이외다. 그럼으로 저는 濫越한 말이지만 文學에 대할 때 만일 靈感의 세례를 밧지 아니하얏거던 작가에게는 봇을 들지 말어라 독자에게는 책을 펴지 말어라 비평가에게는 말을 내지 말어라 권고하고 습흡니다. 생명이 업는 死骸가 압헤 現出할가 해독을 끼치는 악마가 탄생할가 또는 猜疑만 불으는 軋轢이 야기될가 두려워함으로!...
