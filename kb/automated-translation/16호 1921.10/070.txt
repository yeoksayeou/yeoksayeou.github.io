Magazine Title: 개벽 제16호
Publication Date: 1921년 10월 18일
Article Title: 回顧 夏路七千里 (Looking Back on a Summer Journey of Seven Thousand Li)
Author: 朴達成
Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

Looking Back on a Summer Journey of Seven Thousand Li
By Pak Talsŏng

Student Vigor Departing Tōkyō Station
Mixed Feelings about the State of Youth Associations in Various Regions
Deplorable and Detestable Divisions Between Churches
Unbearable Living Conditions of Many Brethren
Our Ever-Majestic Rivers and Mountains, the More One Sees

I dedicate this writing especially to the Brethren residing overseas who long for the Homeland.
Racing East and West, a summer journey of seven thousand *li*? Ah, one might expect strange tales and bizarre events, bitter winds and harsh rains, moments of joy and sorrow, poems and songs of high mountains, long rivers, famous sights, and historic spots within this span, seeming more worthy of a look than the feeling of sighing longingly while gazing at the autumn sky in vain. However, in writing this, I do not weigh whether it is worth a look or not. I merely intend to record what I saw and heard as it is, to share a moment of leisure with the Reader.
Now, lodging myself in a corner of my hometown and pondering the distances, far overseas and near to where my feet have trod, I cannot suppress a certain feeling of pleasure, amidst which several streams of tears flow uncontrollably. How much more so as the autumn wind rises and white clouds fly! As I caress a chrysanthemum bloom with my hand, cold dew falls upon my feet. The sky is high and the mountains are clear; is that the shadow of a lone wild goose reflected askew in the clear river? Ah—what can one do but feel sorrow? Washing my feet in the front stream and drawing my knees up in a grass hut, I hear the chirping of crickets and see the peach leaves turning yellow. Gazing up at the ceiling, I laugh once; looking toward the vast sky, I sigh once. In that moment, my emotions drift to friends near and far, and unconsciously, the seven-thousand-*li* path unfolds before my eyes. What is even more deeply felt is the situation of the Brethren residing overseas. How much they must long for the Homeland across mountains and seas, across ten thousand *li*, <39> and how earnestly they must desire to know the circumstances of their hometown brethren—this I truly empathize with. Therefore, though insufficient, I offer these few pages to the Brethren, hoping to soothe their poignant sorrow and, at the same time, introduce the situation in Korea during the summer. Consequently, I present my humble impressions to share with the Brethren.
I left Japan for our Land of Hibiscus (槿域) on the afternoon of June 15th. At that time, our brethren in Tōkyō, aiming to make the flower of culture bloom in our Land of Hibiscus this summer, were each gripping their iron staves (鐵棒), firmly resolved on many things. The Student Friendship Association (學友會) had lecture teams, the Comrade Association (同友會) had theatre troupes, and there were also lecture teams from the Ch'ŏndogyo Youth Association (天道敎靑年會), the Buddhist Youth Association (佛敎靑年會), the Women's Youth Association (女子靑年會), and numerous other lecture teams and theatre troupes aimed at specific regions. Seeing these many activities of our brethren in Tōkyō, I silently wished for each of their successes, and as a member of the Ch'ŏndogyo Youth Association, also holding the same objective, I headed for the Homeland earlier than others.
Thus, I directly crossed the two-thousand-*li* Tōkaidō Line and the thousand-*li* Genkai Sea (玄海千里), setting foot in our Land of Hibiscus from that day in June until the end of August. During that time, my path led through major and minor cities of North and South Kyŏngsang Provinces, including Pusan, Kimhae, T'ongyŏng, Chinju, Sanch'ŏng, Tansŏng, Ch'angnyŏng, Ulsan, Kyŏngju, Yŏngch'ŏn, and Taegu; through the Kyŏnggi hubs of Suwŏn and Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul); then breaking through the direct Kangwŏn route via Ch'ŏrwŏn and P'yŏnggang to enter Wŏnsan and Hamhŭng; then seeing the unique scenery of Sŏgwangsa Temple (釋王寺) and Sambang (三防); returning again via Kyŏngsŏng, P'yŏngyang, and Chinnamp'o, passing through the land of Pakch'ŏn to reach T'aech'ŏn.
Ah! A summer journey of seven thousand *li*? The joys and sorrows of these seventy days? For me, they are a lifelong memory and also an honor. Setting aside what I saw and heard, just the feelings and thoughts are enough to make me feel a lack of paper. Pusan, Taegu, Kyŏngsŏng, and Wŏnsan are worth writing about once for their urban flavor; Kimhae, Kyŏngju, P'yŏngyang, and Yonggang also need to be written about once for their historical sites; Sŏgwangsa Temple and Sambang, for their famous scenery, also cannot be left unwritten. However, I cannot present this as a diary, detailing sequentially from the beginning of a day to its end, nor will I list places in order from the starting point to the destination like a travelogue. Bundling the beginning, middle, and end of the days, and the start, middle, and end points of the places, I intend to extract the things that left the deepest impression, one by one.

First, what left the deepest impression on my mind and continuously evoked excitement and emotion was the sight of our fellow students at Tōkyō Station upon first departure, full of heroic spirit (豪氣萬丈) and bursting with vitality (生意充天). Those sending off and those departing <40> were not necessarily just our brethren. Japanese, Chinese, Indians, British, French, Americans—all were sending off and departing amidst their own thoughts and actions, which anyone would perceive as ordinary. Yet, only our brethren transcended the ordinary, with deeply meaningful expressions on their faces, filled with white-hot passion, mutually wishing great success and shouting "Manse!" (萬歲) three times in succession until their throats were sore. The station's air became tense, and the gazes of the thousands coming and going converged on our brethren.
Ah! Korean students! To live well like others, striving to build a new, brilliant culture in Korea, enduring repeated hardships (辛酸) in a foreign land for many years, without even a moment's rest in the summer, rushing east and west on a three-thousand-*li* lecture tour (巡講)? Ah! How magnificent and exhilarating an affair! Anyone watching could not help but wish them success along with the "Manse!" shouts. No matter how much the Japanese prided themselves on being "Oriental," I saw them truly admire (欽羡) us at this moment. People from the West, China, regardless of nationality, all offered blessings for us, exclaiming, "Korean youth! Ah! Korean youth! The spirit of youth! So lively and strong—Korea's culture is bound to flourish!" When they offered such praise, how elated must our minds and bodies have been! In the great metropolis of the East, at the great station of the East, as people from East and West bustled about, the meaningful shouts of "Manse!" echoing Orientally—no, globally! Ah! I keenly feel, again and again, that if it is not Korean, it is global.
Thus, we departed Tōkyō with immense spirit, each pondering cultural strategies, so much so that there was no leisure to view the scenery along the railway line, and it is a fact that we had no particular impressions as we were busy compiling lecture notes. However—
Brethren, amidst such excitement, why did these figures of heroic spirit end up scratching their heads and sighing deeply? Inside and outside the train from Tōkyō to Shimonoseki (下關), there wasn't much sense of difference, but inside and outside the ship from Shimonoseki to Pusan, why was it such a different world? Brethren, what is the reason for this? People in white clothes (白衣人)! Ah—aren't they clearly our brethren? Aren't they our own flesh and blood? Out of joy, isn't it fine to embrace them and cry, or laugh? Isn't it fine to kiss their lips or touch their cheeks? But what is this? A feeling of aversion arises, they become unpleasant to look at, and a kind of inexplicable ill feeling (惡感) floods the chest—what is this? There's a limit even to being changed (換膓 - lit. 'intestine swap'), but for such ill feeling to arise upon seeing the brethren of one's homeland—isn't this the act of someone afflicted with an extreme case of change-sickness?
Brethren, my heart ached. I wept because my brethren were too unbearable to look at. <41>
Laborers with topknots like pine cones, head towels tied tightly; merchants in cotton white trousers (白袴衣), red jackets (赤衫), and straw shoes (메투리); women with heads like glossy paper, brazenly exposing and shaking their green-veined breasts (젓통); children with hair like tangled yarn, exposing their genitals—it's too shameful to detail everything, but aren't they clearly our brethren? Appearing crude (麤率), dull (愚鈍), poor (貧乏), seemingly worthless—ah! Why are there so many like this among our brethren? Setting aside the people coming and going across the Genkai Sea inside and outside the ship, isn't it the same everywhere throughout Korea, from Pusan to Kyŏngsŏng and Ŭiju (義州)?
Brethren, how many topknot-wearers are there among our brethren? How many disheveled-haired youths (總角)? How many women who expose and shake their breasts? How many naked children? Aren't these the very ones who damage the dignity (軆面) of our nation, who make us brethren unable to lift our faces (낫), who are incompatible (不相容) with us—no, whom we cannot leave unattended? Ah—Incompatible? Isn't this a statement deserving heavenly punishment? What kind of talk is it to point at one's own brethren and say "incompatible"? Isn't this something that should make blood clot in the heart? How frustrating and painful a statement!
I truly confess. What I publicly declared from the stage was: the topknot-wearers, the tousled-haired youths, the women exposing their breasts, the disheveled-haired children! They are the ones damaging our nation's dignity, proudly proclaiming "We are such crude, inflexible barbarians," and they must be subjugated (討伐) or assimilated (歸化)—this outcry occurred everywhere I went, with everyone I met. Brethren, I earnestly wish for you to consider whether this is sincere concern for us or hypocrisy.

Second, what excited and moved me, which I unfailingly spoke about to everyone I met, was the youth organizations (靑年團軆) in various parts of Korea. There is no place, regardless of region, without a youth association. At the very least, even a social club (俱樂部) exists everywhere. It is a fact that youth associations arose centered on Korean culture. Although the activities of youth associations in various fields such as education, industry, and religion have achieved much, looking into their inner workings reveals quite a few regrettable points.
The lack of suitable personnel causes considerable harm (弊); namely, under the guise of seeking fame (釣名式) and pretense (假意), factionalism (便黨), disputes (紛爭), and superficiality (浮虛) arise frequently, leading to many tragedies of being famous but ineffective (有名無實), starting strong but ending weak (龍頭蛇尾), rising in the morning and fading by evening (朝起暮消). Due to financial difficulties (財政難), intended projects and even ongoing ones stop midway, resulting in mutual observation (互相觀望), sighing at the heavens (仰天長嘆), and cries of "Money, oh money!" (돈아 돈아)—this is the general inside story <42> of most current Korean youth associations, as everyone agrees.
However, what I admired greatly at first sight was the Kimhae Youth Association. Having only seen it once, I don't know the detailed inner workings, but compared to other associations, I saw no fame-seeking, pretentious superficiality; instead, I saw a genuine advocacy for Korean culture. That is, the entire association (擧會一致) advances together towards one goal; their sole method of progress, I clearly saw, is based on each member's sincerity (衷情), using public discussion (公論), understanding (理解), determining feasibility (可否), compromise (讓步), forgiveness (容恕), and decisiveness (果斷). As one example, executives praise each other, and members yield to one another. Thus, unity between old and young (老少一致) and cooperation between men and women (男女共同) were characteristics of Kimhae. Because they—men and women, old and young, knowledgeable and unknowledgeable—cooperate unitedly, I saw many substantive internal achievements emerge, more so than various things like men's and women's study sessions (男女講習會), labor night schools (勞働夜學), education societies (敎育會), and middle school establishment fund-raising associations (中學期成會). Moreover, Kimhae has capable people and adequate finances. I saw the propertied (有産者) contributing wealth, the propertyless (無産者) contributing labor, the knowledgeable (有識者) contributing knowledge, and the unknowledgeable (無識者) contributing physical strength (軆力), each providing devotedly (誠心껏) for the association. I thought that if all the youth associations in various parts of Korea were like Kimhae, Korean culture would likely flourish.
Among others, youth associations in places like Chinju and Kyŏngju also had many praiseworthy points, but I observed some shortcomings in urban-type youth associations like those in Wŏnsan and P'yŏngyang. Speaking ill of others is inauspicious, but since that work is my work, and that association is also my association, I publicly offer a word out of mutual responsibility and the intention of encouraging goodness (責善).
Gentlemen of the Wŏnsan Youth Association: Regionalism (地方熱) and factionalism (黨派熱) are things we all bitterly resent; is there really a need to divide Wŏnsan further into Upper and Lower Wŏnsan? I heard talk of Upper Wŏnsan and Lower Wŏnsan, with the railway line as the boundary (界線). And I heard that the upper area is propertyless (無産) and the lower area is propertied (有産). I heard there is some conflict (紛爭) between the executive faction (任員派) and the member faction (會員派) due to incompatibility of opinions stemming from this propertied/propertyless divide. I don't know thoroughly if this is fact, but according to what I heard, it is so. Such things might not exist only in Wŏnsan, but isn't it truly an inauspicious matter?
Gentlemen of the Wŏnsan Youth Association, if this is not true, it is fortunate indeed. I will cautiously restrain my reckless writing (妄筆). If it is fact, regardless of propertied/propertyless, knowledgeable/unknowledgeable, upper/lower boundaries, please cooperate mutually and offer your full strength sincerely for Korea and Wŏnsan, according to the fundamental spirit of the youth association. This is truly my earnest plea (哀願). And gentlemen of the P'yŏngyang Youth Association: In P'yŏngyang, rumors seem rife (浪藉) that the P'yŏngyang Youth Association is famous but ineffective (有名無實); is this true? If it is true, <43> setting aside the misfortune for P'yŏngyang, it is a misfortune for Korea. How could the spirit of Tanbong Peak (丹峯) and the energy of the Taedong River (浿江) stop and cease at this? I do not know the detailed circumstances, so I won't elaborate, but with this single phrase "P'yŏngyang Youth Association famous but ineffective," one can glimpse a part of the P'yŏngyang youth scene, and at the same time, I cannot but hope for renewed efforts from all of you.
Speaking of youth associations, let me add one more thing. Regarding the touring activities of lecture groups, theatre troupes, etc., active for Korean culture from various youth organizations, I observed that inauspicious situations sometimes arise involving both the organizers (主催者) and the sponsors/supporters (後援者). I experienced this firsthand, and I also heard it from an official of a certain youth association. To introduce that official's words:
"Ah—sponsoring lecture teams when our association lacks both manpower (人力) and funds (金力), being a body with no real power, even sponsoring just once for two or three days truly brings many points of self-reproach (自愧), but there are many headache-inducing matters. Just this summer, it has already exceeded several dozen times. The Korean Youth Federation (朝鮮靑年聯合會) lecture team, the Struggling Students Mutual Aid Society (苦學生갈돕會) lecture team, the Student Congress (學生大會), the Ch'ŏndogyo Youth Association Tokyo Branch (天道敎靑年會東京支會), the Student Friendship Association (學友會), the Comrade Association (同友會), the Buddhist Youth Association (佛敎靑年會), the Tōkyō Women's Youth Association (東京女子靑年會) lecture teams, plus other theatre troupes (劇團), motion picture teams (活動寫眞班)—truly, countless (枚擧키 難한) groups have passed through. Every time these many groups pass through, they invariably request sponsorship from our association. When requested, we cannot refuse, so we provide support to the extent of our ability. We handle everything from advertising and venue arrangements to even the team members' lodging issues. Thus, rushing east and west for several days, we exhaust our energy yet feel much unease (未安). We provide support according to our ability like this. However, if the venue is inconvenient, advertising insufficient, audience small, or lodging uncomfortable, it invites displeasure (不快) from the sponsored side (被後援側), and the association itself feels uneasy, leading one way or another to headaches. It's not just once or twice, but dozens of sponsorships—truly a difficult problem. This situation seems not limited to one or two places but likely applies to the whole country. We can't avoid being asked for sponsorship, and when asked, we can't refuse; we lack resources, yet the requests are frequent (頻煩)—what is to be done about this matter (此事 將奈何)?" These were his words.
I heard this talk in Taegu, Wŏnsan, and P'yŏngyang. I saw it firsthand. Regarding this, I advocate one thing. Regardless of the association or group, if planning to rely on sponsors for lectures or plays, I say don't even start with that intention. I say, proceed within the limits of your own group's capabilities. Relying on others for manpower or funds, setting aside the poor outcome (不景氣) of the lecture tour, I say it conversely becomes an obstacle (障害) to the cultural movement. Borrowing a name or requesting an introduction might be acceptable, but the detrimental practice (弊) of demanding (强要) even food (食費) and travel expenses (車費) is not insignificant; how could even the <44> wealth of Shi Chong (石崇) or the loyalty of Zhuge Liang (諸葛) suffice? I say again, if you truly sympathize with our youth associations, which are newly arising and just putting forth meaningful, beautiful shoots, and wish for their prosperity, do not ask for sponsorship from the start. Rely on your own strength (自力) from beginning to end (終頭至尾).

Third, what I felt keenly and could not get over during this trip was all the harms (弊害) related to the religious world (宗敎界). Without need for lengthy explanation (張皇), knowledgeable people (識者) all lament (痛論) it, but the relationship between churches (敎會) is too much like the hostile states of Wu and Yue (吳越), maintaining the sentiment of enemies (仇敵)—what kind of unfortunate situation is this? Whether one believes in Christianity (基督敎), Buddhism (佛敎), or Ch'ŏndogyo (天道敎), belief (信仰) is up to individual freedom (自由), but a person is a person, a Korean is a Korean, and brethren are brethren, are they not? Viewing each other with enmity (仇視)? What kind of nation-ruining (亡國) affair is this? Christian believers say that all outside Christianity are a multitude of sinners (罪惡) and followers of heresy (異端), invariably referring to Buddhist monks as "monk bastards" (중놈) and Ch'ŏndogyo followers as the "Tonghak army" (東學軍), fawning obsequiously over Westerners (洋人) while daringly adopting attitudes of exclusion (排意) and actions of rejection (斥行) towards their own people—is this the behavior of people who intend to prosper (興하려는)? Aren't Buddhist believers also the same? And aren't Ch'ŏndogyo believers also in the same state?
The division (間隔) between religions is a great misfortune for the Korean people. Isn't there a fierce cudgel to break down that division? If that sentiment is left ignored (黙過), I fear that in not too many years (不幾年日), a Korean religious war (宗敎戰爭) will arise, and we twenty million will inevitably face tearful bloodshed (淚血相交) amongst ourselves, like worms born from one's own skin (自皮生蟲). How much more so for our brethren, who still retain the old customs (因襲) lacking understanding (理解) and forgiveness (容恕)?
Brethren, believe freely, but let us unite and cooperate (一致協力) for Korean culture, and respect and love each other (相敬相愛) under the principle of being one family (自家一族). The truth (眞理) of religion is not separate from respect and love, and the fundamental duty (本分) of human life is also respect and love. How much more so for brethren of the same family? Christian believers, isn't the principle of your religion universal love (博愛)? What is the teaching of Mr. Jesus (耶穌氏)? Love your neighbor; if someone strikes your left cheek, offer the right cheek too. Or, love your enemy. And, "Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect"—isn't this the teaching of Mr. Jesus? Why have you forgotten that teaching? Can one still be called a believer while acting contrary to that teaching? Rather, you are sinners against Christ and rebels against the church; setting aside Heaven (天堂), how will you escape Hell (地獄)? Setting aside neighbors and enemies, whether you like them or not, whether they are believers or non-believers, brethren are brethren, so love them. This is truly my earnest plea.
You who believe in Buddhism, isn't the principle of your religion also great mercy and compassion (大慈大悲)? Isn't viewing all sentient beings (一切衆生) as one the teaching of Mr. Shakyamuni (釋迦氏)? Setting aside all sentient beings, please do not <45> harm the bonds of righteousness (義分) among your own people.
I also offer a word to Ch'ŏndogyo believers. Don't just pay lip service to fine-sounding slogans like "Man is Heaven" (人乃天), "Serve Man as Heaven" (事人如天), "Spread Virtue Throughout the World" (布德天下), or "Widely Save All Living Beings" (廣濟蒼生) within one enclosure. Practice "Serve Man as Heaven" diligently (躬行盡力), loving everyone equally without creating divisions (間隔) between believers (敎人) and non-believers (非敎人).
Regarding the religious world in Korea, whatever the exterior, I keenly feel that the interior is full of instances that defile (汚瀆) the truth. My hope is that, fortunately, based on religious will, complete perfection (圓滿無缺) will be pursued.

Fourth, what clenches my heart and remains unresolved is the living conditions (生活狀態) of our brethren I saw in both South and North Korea (南北鮮). Unable to properly wear even one set of clothes (even if just cotton - 무명옷), unable to properly build even one room (even if just a thatched hut - 草幕), and furthermore, the scene of not having enough to eat—even after gripping my chest and crying my heart out, the feelings still well up. Among them, the most pitiful (可憐) are the laborers (勞働者) and tenant farmers (小作人), and among them, their elderly parents (老父母) and weak wives and children (弱妻子). Ah—their way of living was too wretched (齷齪), I could not bear to watch (忍見). In the farming villages (農村), the tragic scenes of tenant farmers; in the cities (都會), the circumstances of laborers. Barley (보리) and corn (강냉이) are their high-class meals; acorns (도토리), water chestnuts (물릇 - likely Trapa japonica), Solomon's seal roots (둥구레), and Porphyra seaweed (핏게 - laver/gim) are their regular food (常食物). And they fill their stomachs with rotten melons (참외) and watery bean dregs (비지그릇)—ah! During the long, long summer days of intense heat (苦熱), shedding sweat (비지땀) as much as possible, what is this situation? Even more heart-wrenching (一目悵然) is the sight of those sleeping and eating by the roadside (路傍寢食者類) in places like Kyŏngsŏng, Taegu, and P'yŏngyang—ah, I am ashamed even to speak of it before Heaven.
I limit myself to one word calling for the propertied class (財産家) themselves to reflect (反省). Consequently, to the propertyless (無産者), I offer just one word: you too are only human; live like human beings.

Fifth, I wish to conclude by speaking of our country's famous sights and historic relics (名勝古蹟). Perhaps our Korea is foremost in having many famous sights. Bright mountains and beautiful waters (山明水麗), fantastic rocks and strange stones (奇岩怪石), green pines and verdant bamboo (蒼松綠竹), rare birds and strange beasts (珍禽奇獸), fragrant flowers and orchids (花香蘭芳)—where is not a fairyland separate from the world (別地仙境)? Among them, the area along the Kyŏngwŏn Line (京元線) truly has to be called a world-class scenic route. The view leaving Namdaemun (South Gate), skirting Namsan (終南山), and looking down on the Han River (漢江); the pine wind (松風) of Ch'ŏngnyangni (淸凉里); the cherry blossoms (櫻花) of Uidong (牛耳洞); the exquisite beauty (奇絶) of Samgaksan (三角山); the autumn foliage (丹楓) of Soyosan (逍遙山); the waterfall (瀑布) of Pogaesan (寶盖山); the plateau (高原) of P'yŏnggang (平康); the medicinal water (藥水) of Sambang (三防); the pines and cypresses (松栢) of Sŏgwangsa Temple (釋王寺); the Kalma Peninsula (葛麻半島) of Wŏnsan (元山); the Ch'ongsŏk rocks (叢石) of T'ongch'ŏn (通川); the unique realm (別境) of Kŭmgangsan (金剛山)—this is truly unparalleled in the world (世界無比). I have not tread upon the entire world. However, I have roughly seen and can judge the so-called Jiangnan (江南) of China and the Tōkaidō Line (東海線) of Japan. Compared to our Korea's Kyŏngwŏn Line, they cannot hold a candle (伯仲하지 못합니다 - lit. cannot rival). Speaking also of historic relics, setting aside the ancient tomb relics (古墳遺墟) of Kangsŏ (江西), Yonggang (龍岡), Kimhae (金海), and Ch'angnyŏng (昌寧), <46> Kyŏngju's Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵), Ch'ŏmsŏngdae Observatory (瞻星臺), and the Nine-story Pagoda (九層塔)—what sources of pride are these? Are they not renowned (錚錚) throughout the world as the oldest art of the East?
Without needing to speak at length, our Korea, in terms of rivers and mountains (江山), has no reason to feel inferior (遜色) to the world, and in terms of history (歷史), has much to boast about to the world. Only, the question remains about the state of our culture regarding the present and the future. However—
The Chinese boast of Dongting Lake (洞庭) and the Red Cliffs (赤壁); the Japanese boast of Nikkō (日光) and Itsukushima (嚴島). But in terms of natural scenery (天然의 景) and inherent beauty (自然의 美), how can they match our Sambang and Kalma? It is not exaggeration (誇張) because it is ours; in reality, it is among the world's finest scenery. Besides this, the saying "Born in Koryŏ, one must see Kŭmgangsan" (顧生高麗國 一見金剛山) goes without saying, and I won't speak of the spirit (靈) of Paektu Mountain (白山) or the wonder (奇) of the Three Spirits/Mountains (三神).
Brethren, in this fine land, why can the flower of culture not bloom? In this fine paradise (樂園), why should there not be the fruit of happiness (幸福)? Especially, Brethren residing overseas, you have traveled much and seen much; how does it compare to our rivers and mountains?
With this, I close my writing. My journey this past summer was like this. This rough, unrefined account (糟粕), lacking bones and flesh, is nothing remarkable. However, you may be able to roughly grasp the internal situation (內情) of Korea. May you, who strive for the homeland while residing overseas, be relieved of your curiosity (궁금), even if only for a moment. Ah—autumn has surely arrived. Please stay healthy (健康) and steadily advance to your intended final destination.

**Summary**

Pak Talsŏng recounts his 70-day journey through Korea in the summer of 1921, undertaken as part of a Ch'ŏndogyo youth lecture tour starting from Tōkyō. He expresses mixed emotions: immense pride in the patriotic fervor of Korean students leaving Japan and the stunning natural beauty and rich history of Korea, contrasted with deep frustration and sorrow over the poverty, perceived backwardness of some compatriots, internal divisions within youth movements, and bitter sectarianism among religious groups (Christianity, Buddhism, Ch'ŏndogyo). The travelogue serves as both a personal reflection and an update for overseas Koreans, urging unity, cultural development, and self-reliance. Historically, the article offers a valuable glimpse into early colonial Korea (1921), capturing the nationalist aspirations simmering among the youth post-March First Movement, the stark social realities including poverty and cultural clashes, the nascent challenges faced by civil society organizations like youth groups, and the significant problem of religious factionalism hindering national cohesion. The author's candid, sometimes harsh, critique of fellow Koreans and societal issues provides insight into the internal struggles and reformist zeal within the nationalist movement.

東京女子靑年會의 講演隊 其他 劇團 活動寫眞班 실로 枚擧키 難한 各 團軆가 만히 단여갓습니다. 이 만흔 團軆가 단여갈 때마다 반듯이 本會에 後援을 請합니다. 請하면 아니 應할 수 업서 實力이 미치는 대까지 後援을 하엿습니다. 廣告로 場所로 甚至於 隊員旅舍問題까지 다- 交涉하게 됩니다. 그리하야 東馳西走 몃칠동안에 力을 盡하면서도 未安을 만히 感합니다. 이와 가티 實力대로 後援은 합니다. 그러나 場所가 不便하거나 廣告가 不充分하거나 聽衆이 少數이거나 旅舍가 不便하거나 하면 被後援側의 不快를 招하며 會自軆로서 未安을 致하야 이러케 저러케 그만 頭痛症이 남니다. 一二次도 아니오 數十次의 後援ㅣ 實로 難問題입니다. 此事實이 한 두 곳 뿐이 아니라 全鮮이 다- 그러할 모양입니다. 後援 아니 請할 수도 업고 請하면 아니 應할 수도 업스니 實力은 업고 番數는 頻煩하니 此事 將奈何오」하는 말이엇다.
나는 이 말을 大邱에서도 듯고 元山에서도 듯고 平壤에서도 들엇습니다. 實地로 보앗습니다. 이에 對하야 나는 한 가지 主張합니다. 어떤 會 어떤 團을 毋論하고 講演이나 演劇을 後援側에 依賴하랴거던 當初 生意부터 말라 합니다. 어찌하던지 自軆의 實力이 及하는 限에서 巡行하라 합니다. 人力 金力間 남에게 依賴하겟다 함은 巡講의 不景氣는 姑捨하고 反히 文化運動에 대한 障害라 합니다. 名義를 빌거나 或 紹介를 請함은 可하거니와 食費 車費까지 强要하는 弊ㅣ 적지 아니하니 石崇의＜44＞ 富, 諸葛의 忠인들 어찌하겟습니까. 다시금 말하노니 只今 새로 일어나 바야으로 意味깁흔 고흔 筍을 내어 두르는 우리 靑年會에 實로 同情하야 그 興旺을 祝하거던 當初부터 後援을 請하지 말라 합니다. 終頭至尾 自力에 訴하라 합니다.

셋재 내가 이번 行에 切實히 感하야 마지안는 것은 宗敎界에 對한 모든 弊害엿습니다. 張皇할 餘地 업시 識者로서는 다- 痛論하거니와 敎會와 敎會의 사이가 넘우도 吳越의 勢를 作하야 仇敵의 氣分을 固守함은 是何等不幸의 事입니까. 基督敎를 信하거나 佛敎를 信하거나 天道敎를 信하거나 信仰은 各各 自由에 任하거니와 사람은 사람이오 朝鮮人은 朝鮮人이오 兄弟는 兄弟가 아닙니까. 서로 仇視함? 此ㅣ 何等 亡國의 事이랴. 基督敎 信者는 基督敎 以外에는 다- 罪惡의 무리오 異端의 徒라 하야 言必稱 중놈 東學軍하야 洋人에게는 굽습굽슬 어리대이며 自己民族에게는 排意斥行을 敢作하니 此가 所謂 興하려는 人의 行爲이랴. 佛敎信者도 亦是 一樣이오 天道敎 信者 또한 그 모양이 아닙니까.
敎와 敎의 間隔, 朝鮮民族에게 大不幸事입니다. 그 間隔을 打破할 猛棒이 업슬가요. 그 氣分을 그대로 黙過하다가 不幾年日에 朝鮮的 宗敎戰爭이 起하게 되면 自皮生蟲으로 2千萬끼리 淚血相交를 免치 못하리라 합니다. 하물며 理解가 업고 容恕가 업는 以前의 因襲이 그대로 잇는 우리 兄弟이겟습니까.
兄弟들아 밋기는 自由로 하되 朝鮮文化를 爲하야는 一致協力하며 自家一族이라는 下에서 相敬相愛합시다. 宗敎의 眞理도 敬愛를 離함이 업거니와 人生의 本分이 또한 敬愛입니다. 하물며 一家의 同兄弟이겟습니까. 基督敎 信者여러분, 貴敎의 主義가 博愛가 아닙니까. 耶穌氏의 敎訓이 무엇입니까. 이웃을 사랑하며 남이 왼뺨을 따리면 바른뺨까지 내어 대어라. 또는 원수를 사랑하라. 그리하야 한울에 계신 아버지가 完全함과 가티 너이도 完全하리 함은 이 耶蘇씨의 敎訓이 아닙니까. 그 敎訓을 왜 이젓습니까. 그 敎訓과 背馳하고도 信者라 할가요. 돌이어 基督의 罪人이오 敎會의 反逆者니 天堂은 그만두고 地獄을 어떠케 免하겟습니까. 이웃 원수는 그만두고 밉거나 곱거나 信者거나 非信者거나 兄弟는 兄弟라 하야 사랑하시오. 이 實로 나의 哀願입니다.
佛敎를 밋는 여러분, 貴敎의 主義 또한 大慈大悲가 아닙니까. 一切衆生을 同一視한다 함은 釋迦氏 敎訓이 아닙니까. 一切衆生은 그만두고 제 民族끼리의 義分은＜45＞ 傷하지 마시기 바랍니다.
天道敎 信者에게 또한 一言을 呈합니다. 人乃天이니 事人如天이니 布德天下니 廣濟蒼生이니 하야 이름 조흔 한 울타리로 입으로만 떠들지 말고 事人如天을 躬行盡力하되 敎人이거나 非敎人이거나 間隔을 두지 말고 一切로 사랑하시오.
朝鮮에 對한 宗敎界, 外面은 如何하얏던지 內面은 眞理를 汚瀆함이 만흠을 痛切히 느낌니다. 幸히 敎志에 依하야 圓滿無缺을 圖하면 나의 希望입니다.

넷재 나의 가슴이 매치고 매처 풀리지 안는 것은 南北鮮에서 본 우리 兄弟의 生活狀態입니다. 옷 한 벌(무명옷이나마)을 변변히 못 입고 집 한간(草幕이나마)을 변변히 못 짓고 더군다나 못 먹는 情景 꼭 븃잡고 실컷 울어도 오히려 느낌이 북바침니다. 그 中에도 可憐한 것은 勞働者, 小作人, 그 中에도 그들의 老父母 弱妻子, 아- 그 生活하는 꼴 넘우 齷齪하야 忍見치 못하겟습디다. 農村에는 小作人의 慘景, 都會에는 勞働者의 情況, 보리와 강냉이는 그들의 上等飯이오 도토리 물릇 둥구레 핏게 等이 常食物입니다. 그리고는 썩어진 참외 알풀어진 비지그릇으로 배를 채우니 아- 苦熱의 長長夏日에 비지땀은 흘릴대로 흘리며 이것이 웬일입니까. 더욱이 一目悵然한 것은 京城 大邱 平壤 等의 路傍寢食者類ㅣ 아- 말로저 한우님이 부끄럽습니다.
나는 財産家 自身이 스스로 反省이 잇기를 一言으로 呌하고 已합니다. 딸아서 無産者에게 一言으로써 *하는 것은 그대들도 오즉 사람이거니 사람답게 살라고 할 뿐입니다.

다섯재로 나는 우리 나라의 名勝古蹟을 말하고 마치려 합니다. 名勝이 만키로는 아마 우리 朝鮮이 第一인 듯합니다. 山明水麗 奇岩怪石 蒼松綠竹 珍禽奇獸 花香蘭芳 어느곳이 別地仙境이 아닙니까. 그 中에 京元線 一帶는 實로 世界的 名勝이라 할밧게 업습니다. 南大門서 떠나 終南山을 끼고 漢江을 굽어보는 光景, 淸凉里의 松風, 牛耳洞의 櫻花, 三角山의 奇絶, 逍遙山의 丹楓, 寶盖山의 瀑布, 平康의 高原, 三防의 藥水, 釋王寺의 松栢, 元山의 葛麻半島, 通川의 叢石, 金剛山의 別境, 이 實로 世界無比입니다. 나는 世界를 다 밟아보지 못하얏습니다. 그러나 所謂 中國의 江南, 日本의 東海線은 대강 보아 斟酌합니다. 우리 朝鮮의 京元線에 比하야 伯仲하지 못합니다. 또 古蹟으로 말하면, 江西, 龍岡, 金海, 昌寧의 古墳遺墟는 그만두고＜46＞ 慶州의 石窟庵, 瞻星臺, 九層塔, 이 얼마나 자랑거리입니까. 東洋的 最古藝術로 其名이 天下에 錚錚하지 아니합니까.
길게 말할 것 업시 우리 朝鮮은 江山으로써는 世界에 遜色이 업스며 歷史로써는 世界에 자랑할 만합니다. 다만 現在 及 將來에 대한 우리의 文化가 그 如何한가 함이 問題 中에 잇습니다. 그런데
中國人은 洞庭赤壁을 자랑하며 日本人은 日光, 嚴島를 자랑합니다. 그러나 天然의 景自然의 美로야 어찌 우리의 三防, 葛麻를 當하겟습니까. 우리의 것이라 하야 誇張이 아니라 그 實은 世界風景의 一입니다. 此外에 「顧生高麗國 一見金剛山」은 말할 것도 업스며 白山의 靈三神의 奇도 말하지 아니합니다.
兄弟들아 이 조흔 江山에 왜 文化의 꼿이 못 피며 이 조흔 樂園에 왜 幸福의 果가 업겟습니까. 特히 海外에 계신 兄弟들아 만히 단이고 만히 보앗나니 우리 江山과 比하야 그 어떠합니까.
나는 이로써 꼿을 막습니다. 今年 夏期의 小弟의 行路는 이러합니다. 뼈도 업고 살도 업는 어섬푸러-한 糟粕의 此行 아무 보잘 것이 업습니다. 그러나 朝鮮의 內情은 대강 窺知할 수 잇스리이다. 海外에 계셔서 故國을 위하야 애쓰시는 여러분 一時의 궁금이나마 免하소서. 아- 가을은 確實히 왓습니다. 健康하셔서 目的하신 終點까지 꾸준히 나아가소서.
