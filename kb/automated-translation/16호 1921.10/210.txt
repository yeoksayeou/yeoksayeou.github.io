**Body Text Translation:**
The rising of the moon 달뜰 때 (The Rising of the Moon)
						Lady Augusta Gregory, 海兒 (Haeah) (Trans.)
Written in the Western Year 1907

Characters
Police Sergeant
Police Officer D.
Police Officer B.
Man in Ragged Clothes.

Stage: The side of a wharf in some port town. Stone pillars and iron chains. One large barrel. Three police officers enter. Moonlit hour.
The Police Sergeant is a bit older than the other two officers. He goes to the right side of the stage and looks down the stairs. The other two put down a water container (?) and untie a bundle of posters.
Police Officer B. It would be good to paste it there (Points to the barrel).
Police Officer D. Let’s ask him (Turns to the Sergeant). How about pasting it here? (No answer)
Police Officer B. Is it alright to paste the notice on this barrel? (No answer)
Police Sergeant. There are steps leading down to the river here. We must pay attention to this. If that fellow goes down this way, his comrades might have a plan to come here with a boat and take him away.
Police Officer B. Isn’t that barrel good for pasting the notice?
Police Sergeant. Yes, it’s good. Paste it there. (The officers paste the notice)
Police Sergeant. (Reading) Hair: darkish. Eyes: darkish. No beard. Height: five feet five inches. How can anyone possibly know him just from this? <130> If I had known he would break out of prison, I should have taken a look at his face. Anyway, he’s said to be a strange character. The group’s actions follow his commands. There’s probably never been anyone in this ‘Ireland’ who could break out of prison like him. It seems there were probably his kind among the prisoners too. Definitely.
Police Officer B. The government is too stingy offering only a 100-pound reward for him. The policeman who catches him will surely get promoted, at least.
Police Sergeant. I’ll watch here. I somehow feel he’ll try to escape this way. Slipping past over there (Points to the side of the wharf), then his comrades would have arranged to be waiting on that side. (Points below the steps) So if we lose him here once, we won’t even be able to think of catching him again. Ah, it looks like he might be hiding in that small boat piled with seaweed. But it seems there isn't a single person in the world willing to help a man with a wife who wants the reward money.
Police Officer D. And even if we manage to capture him just in time, the people, maybe even our relatives, will likely just curse us.
Police Sergeant. Yes, yes. We just have to fulfill our duty as policemen. Isn't the responsibility for maintaining the nation's law and order entirely on our shoulders? Without our efforts, the lower would become the upper, and the upper would become the lower. Now, now, hurry along. You need to paste notices in other places too. After you've pasted them all, come back here. You can take the lantern. Don't stay out too late; it's just too lonely staying here alone looking up at the moon.
Police Officer B. We regret that we cannot stay with you. It's not that the government isn't increasing the number of police on the streets, especially since that man was in prison and it's also the circuit court season. Well then, please take care.
Police Sergeant. (Paces back and forth a couple of times, then looks at the notice again) Is this an order for a 100-pound reward and a promotion? With 100 pounds... (A Man in Ragged Clothes appears from the left and tries to pass in front of him. The Police Sergeant turns to look.)
Police Sergeant. Where are you going?
Man. Yes, yes, sir. I'm just an idle singer. (Shows a bundle of songbooks) I'm just trying to sell these to those boatmen. (Tries to leave)
Police Sergeant. Don't go. Stop there. You can't go. <131>
Man. Oh, oh, how heartless. There's nothing as bitter as poverty. It seems the whole world is an enemy to the poor.
Police Sergeant. What's your name?
Man. Yes, yes, sir. My name is Chinmi Wŏlshyu (찐미이·월슈), the singer.
Police Sergeant. Chinmi Wŏlshyu. I've never heard that name before.
Man. Ah, really, officer sir? In Ennis (에니스), there's no one who doesn't know me. Have you ever been to Ennis, sir?
Police Sergeant. What for? Why did you come all the way here, from so far away?
Man. Well, sir, I came to make a bit of money during the circuit court sessions. I can still earn 2 or 3 shillings, can't I? I came in the same carriage as the judges.
Police Sergeant. Alright, hurry up and go. Don't stay here.
Man. I'm going, I'm going. I'll go as I intended. (Goes towards the steps)
Police Sergeant. You can't go that way. Tonight, nobody is allowed down there.
Man. Then I'll sit here, sir. Maybe the boatmen will come up to buy songbooks. I need to earn money for dinner at least. They usually return late. In K'ŭlk'o (크-ㄹ코), boatmen often come to the wharf with handcarts.
Police Sergeant. What's all this chatter when I told you to go? I said nobody is allowed to come and go around here tonight.
Man. Yes, yes, I'm going, I'm going. Nothing is as bitter as poverty. How about it, sir? Won't you buy a copy? There are some pretty good songs here. (Takes out books one by one.) 'Contentment and the Flute' – this is useless. 'The Policeman and the Goat' – you probably won't like this either. 'Chyonni Hat'ŭt' (쪼-ㄴ니!하트) – this is a really good song.
Police Sergeant. I told you to go.
Man. What, just listen for a moment. (Sings)
In Loss Tongnae (롯스洞里), there's a rich farmer's daughter.
The handsome young master is Chyonni Hat'ŭt, an Ireland (Hairanttŭ, 하이란뜨) soldier.
The mother tells her daughter, if you forcibly bring home a soldier in Scotch (Sk'och'i, 스코치) clothes as a husband,
I'll simply go mad.
Police Sergeant. Be quiet. Stop that.
Man. (Gathers the songbooks and slowly tries to go towards the stairs)
Police Sergeant. Hey, where are you going?
Man. You told me to go, so I'm going. <132>
Police Sergeant. Are you mocking me? Did I tell you to go there? I meant go back to the town.
Man. You mean go back to the street?
Police Sergeant. (Grabs the Man's shoulder and pushes him forward) Hey you, I'll push you towards the street. Go. What are you dawdling for?
Man. (Looks at the notice and points with his hand) Sir, I understand now. I know what you are waiting for here.
Police Sergeant. What business is that of yours?
Man. Well, sir, I know the man you're waiting for well. Yes, I know him very well indeed. Oh, right, then I'll be going back. (Starts to leave hesitantly)
Police Sergeant. You know the man? Come here. So, what kind of man is he?
Man. Come here? Sir, are you going to cut off my head?
Police Sergeant. Hey, what kind of trickery is this?
Man. No, it's alright. I'll be going. Even if you offered double the reward, I couldn't stay with you, sir. (Goes towards the left side of the stage) Even ten times the reward, I couldn't do it.
Police Sergeant. (Following behind him) Come here. Won't you come? (Drags him back) What does the man look like? Where did you see him?
Man. I saw him in my hometown in K'uree Kun (쿠레에 郡 - County Clare?). Ah, he truly has a terrifying face. You can't even sit together with that man. He's too scary. There's nothing he doesn't know how to use. And his strength! Truly, that man's physique is probably as wide as this plank. (Hits the barrel)
Police Sergeant. Is he that fearsome?
Man. That's how he looked when I saw him then.
Police Sergeant. Are you sure?
Man. In my hometown, there's a man who became mute... an Inspector from P'ari-bon (파리-보-ㄴ)? They say he was hit with a large stone by him.
Police Sergeant. That's the first I've heard of it.
Man. Well, sir, it's not like that. Can every little thing in the world possibly be reported in the newspapers? And there are plainclothes policemen too, sir. - That man went to Raimŭt'ik (라이무틱 - Limerick?) too. They say it was probably after the attack on the police barracks in K'ilmarok (키마록 - Kilmallock?). On a moonlit night, just like tonight. By the riverbank... the exact date seems uncertain.
Police Sergeant. Is that true? This is unpleasant. <133>
Man. Of course. Sir, you're standing there looking towards that side, thinking he'll come out on this side of the wharf (Points). But who knows, maybe that man will come up from that side (Points). If that happens, he'll rush at you before you even have a chance to act.
Police Sergeant. Well, to catch a man like that, the entire police force should mobilize.
Man. Still, sir, if you permit me to stay here, I'll watch this side. I'll sit on the barrel and keep watch.
Police Sergeant. Do you remember that man well?
Man. Yes, yes, sir. I'd know him instantly, even from a mile away.
Police Sergeant. But if that happens, you'll expect to share the reward money.
Man. Well, sir, no matter how much I wander the streets all day long, going from pub to pub singing songs like a poor beggar, I don't want to hear that I'm blinded by reward money. It seems I'm useless. It would be better to go back to the street.
Police Sergeant. No, it's fine for you to stay here.
Man. (Climbs onto the barrel) Heave ho! Sir, aren't you tired from walking back and forth like that?
Police Sergeant. Tired? I'm used to it!
Man. Sir, you'll have hard work tonight, you should rest your body even for a short while. There's enough room to sit together on this barrel. If you're even a little higher up, you can see farther.
Police Sergeant. That's true. (Climbs onto the barrel and sits to the Man's right. They sit back-to-back, each looking in opposite directions) Somehow, your words are a bit strange.
Man. Sir, please give me a match. (The Sergeant gives him a match. The Man lights a cigarette) Would you like one too, sir? It clears the head. Wait, I'll give you a light. Don't turn around. Whatever happens, you must keep watching the wharf.
Police Sergeant. Don't worry, no need to worry. (Lights his pipe. The two men smoke) Ah, truly, the job of a policeman is quite hard. Out here in the dead of night, facing any kind of danger, yet not a single person shows appreciation. On top of that, the pay is low and we get cursed by the people. And yet, we have no choice but to obey orders. Even for a man with a home and family, <134> they don't show the slightest consideration, just keep sending us out to dangerous places.
Man. (Sings)
"Leaping from hill to hill, coming out into the clover-filled field,
Flowers bloom in every crevice of the rocks. Beneath those flowers, water flows.
Sitting in the mountain valleys, listening to the maidens' soft songs, the mute old woman K'uraniel (쿠라니엘 - Granuaile), suffering unlawful pain..."
Police Sergeant. Stop it. That's not a song for a time like this.
Man. No, truly, sir, I was just trying to gather some courage. Thinking about that fellow makes my legs tremble. While we're sitting here like this, what if he comes up the wharf and maybe rushes at me first?
Police Sergeant. Hey, watch carefully!
Man. Don't worry. And without getting any reward money either, I'm truly a fool. But somehow, I have this nature where I can't bear to see others suffering without helping. Eek, what's this? Something hits my chest. (Rubs his chest)
Police Sergeant. (Taps the Man's shoulder) You'll get your reward in Heaven.
Man. I know that too. Still, life is precious.
Police Sergeant. Alright, alright, if singing helps you gather courage, then sing.
Man. (Sings)
"The old woman's head is like a basin, her limbs bound in iron chains,
Sad laments, sounds of moaning, carried on the evening wind. Singing a sorrowful tune, I am the K'uraniel of old times
My lips kissed by the Great King...."
Police Sergeant. That's not right. "The good clothes worn of old, now wearing bloodstained rags" - that's how it goes. Your song is wrong.
Man. Yes, yes, oh, right, I made a mistake. (Sings the song again) But, truly, I never dreamed someone like you, sir, would know this song.
Police Sergeant. Life involves knowing many things. But <135> often those things turn out to be useless.
Man. Then, excuse me, but when you were young, sir, did you ever sit perched on a stone wall, just like we are sitting on this barrel now, singing "K'uraniel" in unison with other children?
Police Sergeant. Yes, of course I sang it.
Man. Then how about "Chan, P'uen, P'ok'ŭ" (쟌, 푸엔, 포오-크)?
Police Sergeant. I've sung that too.
Man. Then "The Grass on the Cape"?
Police Sergeant. I know that one too.
Man. Then maybe the man you are trying to catch tonight also sat perched on a stone wall when he was young, singing the same songs. Truly, the world is a strange place.
Police Sergeant. Shh.... I thought someone was coming... Was it a dog?
Man. Yes, sir, isn't it a strange world? Perhaps that man was one of the friends who held your hand and sang with you in the old days. The very man you might catch tonight or tomorrow and put in jail....
Police Sergeant. That's true. Yes,... truly....
Man. And perhaps, when you were singing those songs, other children came and talked to you about some future plans. Maybe plans to restore the nation's freedom? If they had talked to you, sir, you might have become one of their party back then.... And now you might be facing various hardships....
Police Sergeant. That's right. That could be. I can't know. Back then, I too held great ambitions in my own way.
Man. Sir, it truly is a strange world. Even a mother cannot know what circumstances her child, crawling on the floor, will face throughout a long life, or what state they will be in at the very end, without living through that life.
Police Sergeant. That's truly so. What a strange thought. It's true. Let me think for a moment.... Truly, if I hadn't had sense, if I hadn't had a wife and children, or if I hadn't become a policeman back then, maybe I too would be breaking out of some prison now, <136> hiding in the darkness and suffering. And the man hiding now, the escaped prisoner, might conversely be sitting on this barrel right now, trying to catch me.... If that were so, he would be the officer, and I would be him, I would be struggling to evade him, and he, conversely, would be upholding the national law while I, conversely, would be breaking the national law. Maybe I would have thrown a bomb over his head, or perhaps I would be holding that large stone they say that man carried.... Yes. I definitely would have done that... Ah. (Sighs. After a while) What's that? (Grabs the Man's arm)
Man. (Jumps down from the barrel, listens intently, looking at the water surface) It's nothing, sir.
Police Sergeant. It sounds like a small boat... I really think his comrades are going to bring a small boat to this wharf.
Man. Sir, I think when you were young, you were one with the people, not someone who chased the national law.
Police Sergeant. Of course, back then I might have been a fool, but that time has long passed.
Man. Perhaps, sir, even though you wear a sword and a uniform, sometimes the thought still occurs that you might not follow K'uraniel's path from your youth?
Police Sergeant. Whatever thoughts I have are none of your business.
Man. Ultimately, I believe the day is not far off when you too will surely fight for the country.
Police Sergeant. (Getting down from the barrel) Stop that kind of talk right now. I have my duty, and I will just fulfill it. (Looks around) That's a boat. I hear the sound of rowing. (Goes to the steps and looks down)
Man. (Sings)
"Teach us, Sho Op'aarerŭ (쇼- 오·프아-레루 - Seó Ó Farail?)
Where is the meeting place for all? By that familiar riverside (邊)
That you and I both know well."
Police Sergeant. Stop it. I told you to stop.
Man. (Sings louder and louder)
One more word is the sign to pass the code,
Blow the whistle for the march.
Shoulder your spears, <137>
The time is perfect. The day the moon rises.
Police Sergeant. If you don't stop, I'll arrest you.
A whistle sounds from below, repeating the song's melody.
Police Sergeant. That's the signal. (Stands blocking the way between the Man and the steps) Hey, you can't go down this way.... Step back.... Who are you? Huh? You're not just a singer, after all.
Man. There's no need to ask who I am. That poster explains it. (Points to the poster)
Police Sergeant. You're the fugitive we're looking for.
Man. (Takes off his hat and wig; the Sergeant snatches them) It's me.
There's a 100-pound reward on my head. Down below in the small boat, my friends are waiting for me. They came here to take me to a safe place.
Police Sergeant. (Looks closely at the hat and wig) I'm speechless. Utterly speechless. You deceived me. You deceived me well.
Man. I am a member of K'uraniel's band. There's a 100-pound reward on my head.
Police Sergeant. Speechless. Utterly speechless.
Man. Will you let me escape? Or will you forcibly arrest me?
Police Sergeant. I am a police officer. I cannot let you go.
Man. I tried to loosen myself from your grasp with the tip of my tongue. (Puts hand on chest) What's that?
Voice of Police Officer D (Offstage): Well, we've reached the place where we parted from the Sergeant.
Police Sergeant. My colleagues are here.
Man. I trust that you will not betray me... the members of K'uraniel's band. (Hides behind the barrel.)
Voice of Police Officer B: This is the last of the posters.
Police Officer D. (Entering) If he had escaped, how could we not know?
Police Sergeant hides the hat and wig behind his back.
Police Officer B. Has anyone passed by here?
Police Sergeant. (After a pause) No one passed by.
Police Officer B. Not a single person?
Police Sergeant. No. Not a single person passed by.
Police Officer B. Since we haven't received orders to return to the station, we can stay here with you like this.
Police Sergeant. Ah, there's no need for that. What does it matter even if you're not here?
Police Officer B. Earlier, you asked us to come here and watch together.
Police Sergeant. No, it's better to be alone. Even if someone passes by here, <138> if you fellows are talking... It would be better for it to be quiet here.
Police Officer B. Well then, anyway, we'll leave the lantern. (Offers the lantern)
Police Sergeant. No need. Take it with you.
Police Officer B. No, why no need? Clouds are starting to gather, so it will get dark soon. I'll leave it here on this barrel. (Starts to approach the barrel.)
Police Sergeant. I told you to take it with you, no arguments.
Police Officer B. No, I'm thinking of you. I always carry this and can see clearly even in dark corners. (Swings the lantern while talking.) Doing this makes me feel just like sitting by the fireplace inside a room, watching the firewood blaze up and then die down.
While talking, he lifts the lantern and shines it around the vicinity. The light goes over the barrel and then onto the Police Sergeant.
Police Sergeant. (In an angry voice) Won't you leave? Take the lamp too.
The two officers exit. The Man emerges from behind the barrel and faces the Police Sergeant.
Police Sergeant. What are you still waiting for?
Man. My hat, of course. And the wig too. The weather is so cold like this, I need them so I don't freeze to death. (The Sergeant gives them to him)
Man. (Walking towards the steps) Well then, sleep well, friend. Thank you. You worked hard for me tonight. I appreciate it. Perhaps someday, when the small become high and the great become low, I will be able to repay this favor... When the moon rises, that moon which reverses all our positions (Waves his hand and disappears).
Police Sergeant. (Turns his back to the audience) The hundred-pound reward...
One hundred pounds. (Turns to the audience) Am I such an old fool as I now think myself to be? I really cannot tell.
Curtain (End) <139>

**Summary**

The play depicts a Police Sergeant in Ireland tasked with capturing an escaped nationalist fugitive. While waiting at a moonlit wharf, he encounters a ragged ballad singer. Through conversation about old patriotic songs and shared youthful ideals, the singer awakens the Sergeant's dormant sympathies for the nationalist cause. The singer eventually reveals himself as the fugitive just as his escape boat signals. Faced with a conflict between duty and revived national feeling, the Sergeant chooses to let the fugitive go, questioning his own choices as the curtain falls. For historians of colonial Korea, the publication of this Irish play, translated by "Haeah" (Child of the Sea), in *Kaebyŏk* magazine in 1921 is highly significant. It represents a clear example of using foreign literature with themes of national resistance against a colonizer (Ireland vs. Britain) as a veiled allegory for Korea's own struggle against Japanese rule, subtly promoting nationalist sentiment under censorship.

西紀 一千九百七年 作

人物
巡査部長
巡査  D.
巡査  B.
襤樓한 衣服을 입은 男子.

舞臺는 어떤 港町波止場의 側面, 石柱와 鐵鎖, 큰 桶이 한 개, 巡査 3人 登場, 月明時.
巡査部長은 他二 巡査보다 좀 늙엇다. 그는 舞臺右便으로 가서, 階段을 나려다 본다. 他 2人은 물그릇을 나려노코, 揭示紙 뭉텅이를 푼다.
巡査B. 저기 부텨스면 조켓구먼(桶을 가르친다)
巡査D. 무러보지(部長을 向하야) 여긔 부텻스면, 어떨까요. (對答이 업다)
巡査B. 이 桶우에 揭示를 부텨도, 좃습니까. (對答이 업다)
巡査部長. 여긔 江으로 나려가는 층층대가 잇다. 이것을 注意하여야 되겟다. 그 놈이, 이리로 나려가면, 그 놈의 동무들이 이리로 배를 가지고 와서, 다려갈 計策일는지도, 몰라.
