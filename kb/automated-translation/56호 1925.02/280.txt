Regarding Preparations for Hosting the World Children's Works Exhibition - To Provincial School Teachers and Esteemed Parents
						By the Kaebyŏksa Children's Department

We trust you are already aware that our company's publication, the magazine "Ŏrini" (Children), though its strength is meager, has been endeavoring for the youth movement of Chosŏn. This coming spring, around May, we plan to host the "World Children's Works Exhibition," and have already requested submissions not only from the United States and Great Britain but also from Russia and China. We believe this event will surely provide much stimulation and impulse to the boys and girls of Chosŏn, and expecting no small educational effect, we are hosting it, dedicating considerable time, effort, and resources. We hope that you will widely advertise this to the "young people" (어린사람) who cherish this cause and also strongly encourage many submissions.

1. Submission Categories: Free Painting (Paper and tools optional) (Copies are absolutely invalid)
Handicrafts (Embroidery, Knitting, Woodwork, Others)
1. Submission Location: Kaebyŏksa Children's Department, Kyŏngsŏng
1. Submission Deadline: By March 30, 1925
1. Submission Notes: Do not write address and name on the painting surface; write it neatly beside the picture.
(Submitted works will be kept in storage even after the exhibition concludes and will not be returned.)
Prizes, judging, and other details will be announced later.
<89>

**Summary**

This article is an announcement from the Children's Department of Kaebyŏksa, the publisher of the "Ŏrini" (Children) magazine, regarding their plans to host a "World Children's Works Exhibition" in May 1925. They appeal to provincial school teachers and parents to publicize the event and encourage children in Chosŏn (Korea) to submit paintings and handicrafts. The organizers have solicited entries internationally, including from the US, UK, Russia, and China, believing the exhibition will provide significant educational stimulation for Korean children. Historically, this article is interesting as it shows a Korean cultural organization actively promoting children's development and international cultural exchange during the Japanese colonial period. The ambition to host a *world* exhibition and solicit entries from major global powers, including Russia, despite the political constraints of the time, highlights a strong nationalist desire to connect Korean children with the world and foster their talents on an international stage, reflecting cultural resistance and aspirations within the colonial context.
