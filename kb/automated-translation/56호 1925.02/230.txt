Artificial Laborer
						Čapek (Writer), Pak Yŏnghŭi (Translator)
The original title of this play is "R.U.R," which is the name of the artificial laborer manufacturing company, "Rossum's Universal Robots." "Robot" means "laborer" or "unpaid laborer," and Mr. MacCoan interprets it as "a laborer given life by being made by machines." This word is Bohemian.
The great physiologist Mr. Rossum departed for a remote island in the South Seas in 1920 to study marine animals. During his research, he gained confidence in chemically producing something similar to protoplasm, and in 1950, he artificially created a human. However, it died after three days, resulting in his failure. It was his son who finally succeeded in creating a complete human and used these artificial humans as substitute laborers. But these artificial laborers, lacking souls and sensations, could work without wages. Consequently, countries ordered tens of thousands of artificial humans, and governments planned military expansion using them, eventually leading to a global revolution. It is a great masterpiece depicting the end of human society developed by machine civilization in a future isolated island. It is said that countries competed to stage it.

Act One
Inside the sales headquarters of the Rossum's Universal Robots factory.
An entrance at the rear right. On the front wall, a window showing the factory buildings. To the left, several office doors. Tŏmin sits in a swivel chair before a large desk. On the desk are an electric lamp, telephone, letter scale, and various other items. On the left wall hang a railway network map, a large calendar, and a clock showing a few minutes past noon. "Cheap Labor"
"Rossum-style Workers"
"Tropical Worker 30 Pounds"
"Get Your Workers."
"Get Rossum-style Workers to cut your production costs."

Tŏmin sits beside the desk, and Sŭra is typing on a typewriter.

"This company does not guarantee against damage to goods during transport. Rossum's Universal Robots Company."

It was in 1920 when the famous physiologist Mr. Rossum came to this desolate, remote island with the purpose of studying marine animals. At that time, he was a very young scientist. While here, he began to chemically create a living thing called protoplasm. Thus, he discovered something exactly like a living organism but different from chemical composition. That discovery was in 1932, exactly 400 years after the discovery of America. <60>
Helena - Then why are you laughing about that discovery?
Tŏmin - Yes, because biology is not my specialty. Shall I continue the story?
Helena - Yes, please tell me.
Tŏmin - So, Mr. Rossum wrote this among his chemical samples: "Nature has only one way of making living things. But that there might be another, simpler, faster way at will, Nature surely never dreamed. The second method, showing the progress of human life, has been discovered by me today." That's what it says. Look at this. Think about Mr. Rossum, who wrote such astonishing words about colloidal waste that even a dog wouldn't look back at. Think about Mr. Rossum, sitting before a test tube, absorbed in seeing how the entire tree of life lived within that test tube, or how all animals, from beetle-like things to humans—though humans of a different substance than us, of course—developed within that test tube. The time when he was researching like this was truly an astonishing time.
Helena - What is?
Tŏmin - So, the question now is how life arises in this test tube, how it evolves to form a heart, skeleton, and nerves, and how the contacts of enzymes and conjugates were discovered—do you understand?
Helena - I can't understand it well, but...
Tŏmin - Please be patient and listen. Anyway, just think that Mr. Rossum could make whatever he wanted to make. Whether it was the brain of Socrates, a monster like Medusa, or a worm 50 yards long, there was nothing he couldn't make. Putting aside idle talk, he first planned to make vertebrates, specifically humans. And so, this artificial creature was something that longed for life and consequently dreamed and destroyed things, without any thought. Therefore, it could not be made from nature's proteins. <61> So he thought about it.
Helena - What is it?
Tŏmin - I mean imitating nature. First, he tried to artificially create a dog. But after struggling for several days, what he made turned into a calf. Even that died after a few days. I'll show it to you now in the museum. And after that, he started making humans.
Helena - Is it okay to tell this story to others?
Tŏmin - Please keep it absolutely secret from others.
Helena - But isn't that sort of thing in school textbooks in Europe or America?
Tŏmin - Yes. But do you know the facts beyond the textbooks? The fact is, Mr. Rossum was mad. You must know that an old madman brilliantly created humans.
Helena - But you can make humans too, can't you?
Tŏmin - Regardless, Mr. Rossum truly made a wonderful human.
He tried to become a scientific substitute for God. And he was an extreme materialist. That's why he did such things. His aim was merely to establish proof that God was now unnecessary. Do you have knowledge of anatomy, madam?
Helena - Just a little.
Tŏmin - I'm the same way, but... Anyway, he was determined to make something exactly like a human.
I'll show you the failed samples he struggled with for 10 years. Because the thing he made, calling it human, could only live for three days. But then his son, who was working as an engineer, appeared. His son is also a very capable man. So, seeing the human his father made incorrectly, the son said this: "It's foolish to take 10 years to make one human. If it can't be done faster than nature, it's much better <62> to quit." That's what he said. So the son immersed himself again in the study of anatomy.
Helena - There's no mention of that in the school textbooks.
Tŏmin - Textbooks only record what advertising fees have been paid for, so the writing there is just bland. And the textbooks say Mr. Rossum and his son worked together, but that's like a children's story. Father and son always cursed each other. Mr. Rossum had no thought for industrial needs, but his son threw himself into the laboratory for industrial purposes and researched with truly astonishing energy. So eventually, from the position of an engineer, he intended to do business. Mr. Rossum cursed himself. He even said he would take two lives before he died. Then one day, while the two were researching, he died. That's all the story about him.
Helena - So what did the son do?
Tŏmin - Then let me tell you about that person too. Anyone who has seen the dissection of a human body would think that humans are too complex, and some famous engineer would try to make it much simpler. Therefore, Mr. Rossum's son began to revise anatomy. That is, he tried to simplify it by omitting things. For example—but isn't this too tedious?
Helena - No, not at all. Please continue the story. It's really interesting.
Tŏmin - So he said this: "Man is an animal that tries to do anything unnecessary, like knowing happiness, playing the piano, or taking a walk."
Helena - Really.
Tŏmin - Such things are not needed when weaving fabric or calculating. Madam, can you play the piano?
Helena - Yes.
Tŏmin - Good. But a labor machine must not be able to play the piano. It must not know happiness. Nor must it demand anything else. <63> There's no use decorating a gasoline motor with alcohol. So, making an artificial laborer is the same as making a gasoline motor. The method of making it must be the simplest and, from a practical standpoint, the best product possible. If it's a practical laborer, what kind of laborer do you think would be best, madam?
Helena - What?
Tŏmin - From a practical standpoint, what kind of laborer do you think is best, madam?
Helena - Really, someone honest and hardworking, perhaps?
Tŏmin - No. It means the cheapest one. The person who requires the least. So Mr. Rossum's son invented the laborer who required the least. He simplified the laborer. Thus, he threw away everything unnecessary for advancing labor. That is, he threw away the human and created the Artificial Laborer. Madam! The artificial laborer is not human. To be precise, the artificial laborer is far more perfect than we humans. Artificial laborers all possess highly advanced intelligence. But they do not have souls.
Helena - How do you know artificial laborers don't have souls?
Tŏmin - Have you ever seen the inside of an artificial laborer, madam?
Helena - No.
Tŏmin - The inside is truly simple. It's truly a magnificent creation. There is nothing unnecessary, and everything maintains its order perfectly. An engineer's creation is far more mechanically perfect than nature's creation.
Helena - But humans are said to be God's creation?
Tŏmin - That's why they're imperfect. God knows nothing about modern mechanics. Do you believe Mr. Rossum's son worked in place of God, madam?
Helena - What? <64>
Tŏmin - He started making special-order artificial laborers. Yes, they were all giants of the same height, twelve feet tall. But that was a failure, as you must know, madam?
Helena - A failure?
Tŏmin - Yes. Their hands and feet broke too easily. Certainly, this earth of ours is too small for giants. Therefore, this company now only makes laborers of the first-class civilized type.
Helena - I have seen first-class artificial laborers in my country. Because the provincial government official bought artificial laborers.
Tŏmin - Bought artificial laborers? That's right. Those laborers are bought and sold.
Those laborers were bought as street sweepers. So I saw those people sweeping the streets. It was truly strange and dignified.
Tŏmin - Rossum's Universal Robots Company doesn't just make one type of person. We make various types, from high-grade to low-grade. The highest quality artificial humans live for about 20 years.
(Rings an electric bell to summon Mariyusŭ.)
Helena - Then after that, the artificial laborer dies?
Tŏmin - Yes. It can no longer be used.
(Mariyusŭ enters.)
Tŏmin - Mariyusŭ, bring two samples of manual labor artificial laborers!
(Mariyusŭ exits.)
I'll show you the standard and low-grade products. The first-class ones are relatively cheap and can be produced in large quantities.
(Mariyusŭ enters with two artificial men.)
Yes, you are the strong ones. And insured models with average intelligence. You may go now?

Helena - Because you already know I came to incite the artificial laborers—
Tŏmin - Madam! We have met about a hundred rescuers and preparatory figures here. Two or three come with every ship. Missionaries have come. Anarchists have come. The Salvation Army has come. All sorts of people come. I am truly amazed that there are so many churches and foolish people in the world.
Helena - So, did you let those people see the artificial humans?
Tŏmin - Of course, we showed them. The artificial humans know everything well. But that's all; there's nothing else special. Those artificial humans don't even know how to laugh when people tell funny stories. It's truly incomprehensible. Madam! If you wish, I can show you the artificial human warehouse. There are about three hundred thousand of them.
Ppasŭk'al - Four hundred forty-seven thousand.
Tŏmin - Is that so. Madam! Please say whatever you wish. Read the Bible or recite the multiplication tables, as you please. Or preach about human rights, it doesn't matter at all.
Helena - Truly, I thought you had at least a little love for the artificial humans—
P'aburi - Using the word 'love' is impossible. There is nothing as unpleasant to look at as an artificial human. <72>
Helena - Then for what reason do you make artificial humans?
Ppasŭk'al - Hahaha, that's an interesting question. You ask why we make artificial humans?
P'aburi - We make them to make them work. Because one artificial human does the work of two laborers. And looking at it, the human machine is quite imperfect. The human machine will disappear sooner or later.
Ppasŭk'al - Because the human machine is utterly uneconomical.
P'aburi - And yet, firstly, its effectiveness is poor. The human machine is already inadequate for the demands of modern mechanics. Nature does not try to keep pace with modern labor. For example, from an industrial perspective, childhood is utterly absurd. It just wastes time needlessly. And also—
Helena - No. That's not true!
P'aburi - Please don't misunderstand. What can be done? Please tell us a bit about the true purpose of your League... uh, the Humanity League.
Helena - The true purpose of the Humanity League is—to protect artificial humans and... to ensure they are treated well.
P'aburi - In any case, that purpose isn't bad. Machines must be handled well. I personally agree. I dislike seeing goods broken. How about letting us join the Humanity League, perhaps as contributing members, regular members, or even founding members?
Helena - No, madam. You misunderstand. What I want is the liberation of artificial humans.
Haremiël - How do you propose to liberate them?
Helena - They must be treated like humans.
Haremiël - Haha, you mean giving artificial humans the right to vote? Letting them drink beer? Letting them command us?
Helena - Can't artificial humans drink beer?
Haremiël - And you mean paying them wages for their labor?
Helena - Of course, they must be paid.
Haremiël - Well, just think about it! What on earth <73> would they do if they received wages (삭)?
Helena - Artificial humans might have things they want to buy. They might have things they want to possess.
Haremiël - It would be nice if they had things they wanted to possess. But they have nothing they want to possess. So what would they buy? You mean we should nurture them with pineapples or straw (藁 - likely a typo or misunderstanding for something else, maybe 'food'?) or other things? Yes. But doing such things is useless. They have no appetite. And they have no interests. Therefore, artificial humans have nothing to smile about.
Helena - Why didn't you make the artificial humans happier?
Haremiël - It cannot be done that way. Because artificial humans are only laborers.
Helena - Oh dear, but don't artificial humans have intelligence?
Haremiël - Yes. But they have nothing else. Artificial humans have no will of their own. No soul.
Helena - Don't they have love?
Haremiël - Love? Of course. Artificial laborers don't know love. They don't even know how to love themselves.
Helena - Don't they have rebellious spirit?
Haremiël - Rebellious spirit? Well, perhaps. Sometimes there might be occasions.
Helena - What?
Haremiël - No, madam. It's nothing worth specifically mentioning. Occasionally, they have seizures. We call it "robot's cramp." At that time, they suddenly throw away whatever they were holding and grind their teeth—then they go into the crusher. That, of course, is a defect in the machine's structure.
Tŏmin - Such things are useless.
Helena - No— no. That is precisely the soul! <74>
P'aburi - Then does the soul first grind its teeth?
Helena - Grinding teeth is precisely their rebellion. It's a sign of the suffering within their hearts. Yes, can't you improve them somehow?
Tŏmin - Absolutely. Dr. K'al is currently researching it.
Dr. K'al - Tŏmin! What I am researching is not that. I am currently creating pain nerves.
Helena - Pain nerves, you say?
Dr. K'al - Yes. Artificial humans cannot perceive physical pain. What Mr. Rossum's son created limited the nervous system too much. Therefore, we must make them aware of pain.
Helena - Why is pain necessary for artificial humans too?
Dr. K'al - From an industrial principle perspective, madam. Because artificial humans don't know pain, they break themselves. They put their hands into machines, break their fingers, bash their heads, and feel nothing. So we must make them feel pain. That will be an automatic protection against damage.
Helena - If they become aware of pain, artificial humans will surely become happier.
Dr. K'al - No, madam. On the contrary, they will become unhappy. Only from an industrial perspective will they become more perfect artificial humans.
Helena - Why don't you create souls for the artificial humans?
Dr. K'al - It is beyond our power.
P'aburi - Because they have no interest in such things.
Ppasŭk'al - The production cost is too high. In short, our company makes artificial humans cheaply. Including clothes, one artificial human costs about 15 pounds. Fifteen years ago, it cost 100 pounds. That was because things like clothes were bought elsewhere back then. But now, our company has a textile mill, and we export woolen cloth (羅紗) five times cheaper than others. Madam, how much did you pay per yard for woolen cloth?
Helena - I don't know. I've forgotten. <75>
Ppasŭk'al - You don't know? And yet you organized the Humanity League. Madam, woolen fabric can now be bought for one-third of the previous price. Nowadays, the price of everything has dropped by one-third compared to before. Prices are now gradually starting to fall.
Helena - Why is that?
Ppasŭk'al - Why, you ask? Yes, it's because laborers' wages have become cheaper. For one artificial laborer, including food and all other expenses, it costs only three-quarters of a penny per hour. This is a significant issue. If any factory doesn't use artificial humans, that factory will soon go bankrupt.
Helena - Then human laborers will become unnecessary?
Ppasŭk'al - Of course, yes. In any case, our company sent 500,000 tropical artificial humans to cultivate grain on the Irosin Plain (이루샌진野原). Do you know how much one pound of bread (팡 - from French 'pain' or similar) costs?
Helena - I don't know.
Ppasŭk'al - Then let me tell you. In Europe, it now costs two pence. One pound of bread for two pence. And the Humanity League knows nothing. Yes. Madam, have you had no thoughts about such valuable things? Within five years, surely—
Helena - What?
Ppasŭk'al - All prices will become one-fifth of what they are now. Why, you ask? Because within five years, there will be more than enough rice and everything else to eat, with plenty left over.
Alk'isŭt'ŭ - Yes. And all the laborers in the world will be laid off.
Tŏmin - That's right, Alk'isŭt'ŭ! Exactly. So within ten years, Rossum-style artificial humans will produce vast amounts of grain, woolen cloth, and everything else. Thus, prices will become very cheap. Then, needy people will disappear. All work will be done by living machines. Consequently, all human suffering will also disappear. And liberation from the degradation of labor can be obtained. Everyone will be able to live solely to perfect themselves. <76>
Helena - Including the artificial humans, you mean?
Tŏmin - Of course. It's impossible now, but when that time comes, there will be no more humans employing humans, nor humans submitting to material things. Of course, there might be fearful things at first. That's unavoidable. But only when that time comes, no one will struggle to obtain bread as objects of life and hatred. Artificial humans too will escape their beggar-like state and live comfortably in their own homes.
Alk'isŭt'ŭ - Tŏmin, Tŏmin! Aren't you talking just like it's paradise? But isn't service a good thing, and isn't humility also admirable? Isn't there value in toil and fatigue?
Tŏmin - That may be so. But there's no use thinking again about what we lost when we remodeled the world. When that time comes, humans will be free and superior. Humans will have no purpose other than self-perfection, no toil, no worries. Humans will not submit to other humans or to material things. Humans will not be production machines, nor means to an end. Humans will become the gods of creation.
Ppasŭk'al - A very thankful thing.
P'aburi - That's right.
Helena - I feel as if I've been completely charmed. I wish it could really be like that.
Dr. K'al - Madam! Since you are younger than us, you will live long enough to see it.
Haremiël - Absolutely. Madam, what will you do? How about having lunch with us?
Dr. K'al - Please do so. Yes? Tŏmin! Ask her on our behalf!
Tŏmin - Madam! Will you accept?
Helena - If you knew why I came here—
P'aburi - For the Humanity League?
Helena - Therefore—
P'aburi - Alright. Excuse us for five minutes.
(Everyone stands up.)
Dr. K'al - I'll also be excused for a moment.
Ppasŭk'al - I'll be right back. <77>
Haremiël - We are very pleased you came, madam.
Ppasŭk'al - I'll definitely be back in five minutes.
(Everyone exits, leaving only Tŏmin and Helena.)
Helena - Why did everyone leave?
Tŏmin - To prepare the food.
Helena - What food?
Tŏmin - Lunch. Artificial humans make our meals, but they have no sense of taste.— Haremiël makes good stews, Dr. K'al makes sauces, Ppasŭk'al makes excellent omelets.
Helena - Oh my, what a grand feast. That gentleman—the Architect—what is he good at?
Tŏmin - You mean Alk'isŭt'ŭ? That fellow doesn't know how to cook. He just sets the table. And P'aburi gathers the fruit. The cuisine here is quite extraordinary.
Helena - I have something I'd like to ask you.—
Tŏmin - I also have something I'd like to ask you. (Looking at his watch) Five minutes!
Helena - What do you want to ask?
Tŏmin - No, you ask first!
Helena - It might sound foolish, but why did you make female artificial humans? Uh—
Tŏmin - You mean, is gender meaningless to artificial women?
Helena - No.
Tŏmin - There is still a need for them. Because the world uses female artificial humans as maids, female clerks, stenographers, and the like—.
Helena - But still, why is it that male and female artificial humans don't— uh—
Tŏmin - They have no thoughts about it. They possess absolutely no affection.
Helena - Oh dear! How shocking!
Tŏmin - Why?
Helena - But isn't that too unnatural? I can't quite understand it. Whether it's better to love or hate, or—
Tŏmin - Or whether it's better to feel sorry for them, you mean?
Helena - It's more than that. And what was it you wanted <78> to ask me?
Tŏmin - How about it, madam? How about marrying me?
Helena - What did you say?
Tŏmin - Won't you become my wife?
Helena - I haven't really thought about such a thing.
Tŏmin - (Looking at his watch) Three minutes left. If you don't marry me, you must definitely marry one of the five of us.
Helena - Why is that?
Tŏmin - Because they will all propose marriage in turn.
Helena - Why do you all do such things?
Tŏmin - They all love you.
Helena - Please tell them not to do that. I— I'm leaving soon.
Tŏmin - Miss Helena! Are you rejecting me?
Helena - But— but I can't marry six men.
Tŏmin - That's not it. Just one among them. If you dislike me, will you marry Mr. P'aburi?
Helena - No. I don't want to.
Tŏmin - Then Dr. K'al?
Helena - I dislike them all.
Tŏmin - (Looking at his watch) Only two minutes now.
Helena - Haven't you married any of the ladies who came here?
Tŏmin - Many have come, but—.
Helena - Young women?
Tŏmin - Yes.
Helena - Then why didn't you marry them?
Tŏmin - There wasn't a woman whose appearance charmed me enough. Until today. But from the moment you removed the veil from your face—
(Helena looks around.)
Tŏmin - Only one minute left now.
Helena - But I really don't want to marry you.

Helena - (Looks at Tŏmin, but quickly turns back) You are quite persistent.
Tŏmin - What can I do then? Every man is persistent. That's what men do.
Helena - Are you mad?
Tŏmin - Every man is a little bit mad. That's the good thing about men.
Helena - (Turning back again) Hey! You're going too far.
Tŏmin - Did you understand what I said? Is it alright?

Tŏmin - Helena! Is that really your answer?

**Summary**

This text provides an introduction and the complete first act of Pak Yŏnghŭi's 1925 Korean translation of Karel Čapek's play "R.U.R." The introduction explains the play's premise: the invention of artificial laborers ("Robots") by the Rossums, their soulless efficiency leading to global adoption and societal upheaval. Act One introduces Helena Glory, daughter of a president, visiting the isolated island factory of Rossum's Universal Robots. She meets the general manager, Domin, and other senior staff (all human men), initially mistaking them for Robots due to her mission from the Humanity League to advocate for the Robots' rights. The act explores the creation, nature, and purpose of the Robots through dialogue, highlighting their lack of feeling, soul, and pain (though pain is being developed for practical reasons), contrasting this with the efficiency and economic benefits they bring. It ends with Domin impulsively proposing to Helena, revealing the other managers also intend to, and securing her ambiguous acceptance just as the others return.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as an early example of modern Western literature being translated and consumed in Korea during the Japanese colonial period. The themes of industrialization, dehumanization of labor, class struggle, and the potential dangers of technology, while universal, might have resonated differently in a colonized nation experiencing forced modernization and exploitation. The translation itself, using contemporary Korean spellings and vocabulary (including loanwords like 'Robot'), reflects the linguistic landscape of the 1920s. Publishing a play discussing revolution and the overthrow of humanity, even in a fictional context, in a censored environment like colonial Korea is also noteworthy.

大生理學者인 롯심氏는 1920년 南洋孤島로 출발해서 해양동물을 연구하는 중에 원형질과 유사한 물건으로 화학적으로 제작할 확신을 가지고 1950년에 인공적으로 인간을 제작하엿다. 그러나 3일만에 죽어서 그는 실패를 하고 그의 아들이 비로소 완전한 인간을 제작하여 가지고 그 인조인을 노동인으로 代用하엿다. 그러나 영혼이 업고 감각이 업는 인조노동자는 무임으로 노동할 수 잇섯다. 그리함으로 각국에서 수만 명의 인조인을 주문하며 또한 각 정부에서는 이것으로 군비확장을 계획해서 종말에는 세계적 혁명이 비롯되며 또한 기계문명의 발달된 인류사회의 말세를 보이는 未來孤의 일대 걸작이다. 각국에서는 닷투어 가면서 상연하엿다 한다.

第一幕
롯섬 우주 인조인 제조회사 공장 내 영업본부.
右便 後部에는 입구. 정면 벽에는 공장의 건축물이 보이는 창. 左便으로는 수 개의 사무실. 떠민은 큰 책상 압헤 회전의자에 걸타안젓다. 책상 우에는 전기스탠드, 전화, 편지다는 저울 등 여러 가지 물건이 노엿다. 左便 벽에는 철도기반의 교통지도, 큰 日歷, 正午 후 數分이 지내고 잇는 시계가 걸렷다. 또한 몃 장의 포스터가 붓텃다. 거긔는 이러한 말을 써서 잇섯다.
「갑싼 노동자」
「롯섬식 노동자」
「열대 노동자 30磅」
「귀하의 노동자를 구하시요.」
「귀하의 생산비를 절감하기 위해서 룻섬식 노동자를 구하시요」
이와 가튼 무취미한 장식과는 조화되지 안케 마루 우에는 찬란한 土耳其製의 紙氈을 깔엇다. 右便으로 원형테불, 쏘파, 가죽으로 싼 肱掛椅子, 책 대신으로 酒甁을 벌려 논 冊棚. 左便에는 금전출납의 床. 떠민은 책상 엽헤 안졋고 스라는 타입으라터 (寫字本)을 누르고 잇다.
떠민-(書取를 시키면서) 자아?
스라-네.
떠민-英國 소삼푸돈市 이-엠 막비카 회사 御中.
『本 회사는 운송 중에 파손한 물품에 대해서는 보증하지 안슴니다. 本 회사는 화물을 선적할 때에 그 기선이 인조인을 수송함에 부적당하다는 것만을 귀하의 선장에게 주의만을 식히고. 本 회사는 荷物 파손에 대해서는 아모 책임도 업슴니다. 롯섬 우주 인조인 회사』
(書取할 때에 꼼짝업시 안젓든 스라는 멧 분 동안에 신속하게 타입으라이터로 박이엿다)
떠민-다 되엿니?
스라-네.＜57＞
떠민-또 하나. 「米國 育紐市 이 삐 하이손 대리점 御中」 『귀사에서 인조노동자 5,000인의 주문하신 것은 拜受하엿슴니다. 귀사 기선이 다시 올 때에는 본사용 경연탄을 보내주시면 前記 석탄대금은 本 회사의 지불액의 일부로 기입하여 주시요. 롯섬 우주 인조인 회사』
(스라는 신속히 박이엿다.)
떠민-다 되엿니?
스라-네.
떠민-또 하나. 「獨逸 함뿌룩크市 후리도리쓰히 공장 御中.」 『귀사에서 주문하신 인조노동자 15,000인의 주문은 拜受하엿슴니다.」
(전화가 왓다.)
떠민-여보시요! 여긔는 본부요. 그래 꼭 음 전보를 노아라 그만두자.
(전화를 끗첫다.)
어듸서 끗첫지?
스라-『인조노동자 15,000인의 주문은 拜受하엿슴니다.』에서요.
떠민- 인조노동자 15,000인. 15,000인!
(마리우스가 드러왓다.)
떠민-응 무슨 일야?
마리우스-네 엇더한 부인께서 뵈이시겟다구요.
떠민-부인? 누구야?
마리우스-누구신지는 모르겟슴니다마는 이 소개장을 가지고 오섯서요.
떠민-(명함을 보고서) 응. 구로리 대통령의 소개로군. 드러 오시라구 해라.
마리우스-(문에서) 이리 드러오십쇼.
헤레나, 구로리 등장. 마리우스 退去)
헤레나-안영하심니까?
떠민-네 안영하심니까. (일어서면서) 무슨일로 오섯는지요?
헤레나-당신이 총지배인 떠민氏심니까?
떠민-그럿슴니다.
헤레나-나는-
떠민-구로리 대통령 각하의 명함을 가지고 오섯지요. 네 좃슴니다.
헤레나-구로리 대통영은 나˜ 아버지가 되심니다.
떠민-각하의 따님! 우리가 대통영 각하의 따님을 맛게된 것은 본 회사로서는 대단한 영광이올시다. 그러나-
헤레나-그러나 나를 쫏차내지는 안을 터이지요?
떠민-자 안즈십쇼. 스라야 너는 저리 가거라!＜58＞
(스라 退去)
떠민-(걸터안즈면서) 말슴하시고 십흔 것이 게시거든 염여말고 하세요.
헤레나-내가 온 것은-
떠민-사람을 맨드는 우리들의 유명한 공장을 구경하시려고 오섯슬 터이지요? 다른 손님들처럼 알엇슴니다. 거절은 안이하겟슴니다.
헤레나-나는 거절하실 줄 알고도 왓슴니다.-
떠민-공장예 드러가는 것 말슴임니까? 에 그것은 물론 거절임니다. 다른 사람들도 다 누구의 소개짱을 가지고 오기는 하지만.
헤레나-그래 여러분에게 다 보이여 드리엇을 터이지요?
떠민-다만 한 부분뿐만 보여 드림니다. 인조노동자를 맨드는 데는 비밀한 방법이 잇기 때문에-.
헤레나-그러면 나에게는-
떠민-보여주겟느랸 말슴이지요. 안이 구라파 전체가 지금 이 공장 이야기뿐만 한대요.
헤레나-당신은 웨 남의 말을 꼭 잘너서 말슴을 하심니까?
떠민-실례햇슴니다. 그러면 다른 말슴이 하실 것이 계시단 말슴임니까?
헤레나-내가 말슴하고 십흔 것은-
떠민-네 그러면 부인께는 특별한 대우로 공장까지 보여 들이지요.
헤레나-엇더케 당신은 내가 원하는 것을 그리도 잘 아심니까?
떠민-여긔 오시는 이는 누구든지 가튼 소원이 잇스니깐요. 그러나 부인께는 특별이 다른 사람에게 안이 보이는 것을 보여들이지요.
헤레나-고맙슴니다.
떠민-그러나 그 비밀은 절대로 루설되지 안토록요.
헤레나-(일어나면서 손을 내밀고) 네 그것은 결단코.
떠민-고맙슴니다. 그 베일(面紗)를 좀 버스시는 것이 엇더하실까요-?
헤레나-그것은 쉬운 일이지요. 내가 탐정이나 안인가 하고 그것을 알으시 량으로 그러시지요. 용서하세요.
떠민-무엇이요? (별안간 헤레나의 손을 잡으면서)
헤레나-손을 좀 노아주세요.＜59＞
떠민-(손을 노코) 아 용서하십쇼.
헤레나-(面紗를 버스면서) 당신은 매우 주의를 하심니다 그려 그럿치요?
떠민-(매우 만흔 흥미를 가지고 헤레나를 보면서) 네 물론 우리들은-그-
헤레나-그 무엇이예요? 무슨 말슴을 하시려다가?
떠민-우리들은 매우 즐겁게 생각함니다. 배에서는 무사하섯슴니까?
헤레나-네.
떠민-무슨 괴로우섯든 일은 업섯슴니까?
헤레나-웨 그리세요?
떠민-안이 나는 다만 부인께서 저러케 젊으시니깐 말슴예요.
헤레나-곳 좀 공장을 볼 수 업슬까요?
떠민-네 알엇슴니다. 스물둘 가령은 되엿겟지.
헤레나-무엇이 스물들이란 말슴예요?
떠민-부인의 년치가 말슴예요.
헤레나-스물한 살이예요. 그런데 엇재서 그런 것을 물어보심니까?
떠민-엇재서요.-(열심으로) 오래동안 이곳에 게시겟슴니까? 엇더심니까?
헤레나-구경할 공장이 끗나는 대로 결정하려고 하는데요.
떠민-인저 공장 이야기는 그만 두시지요. 안이 안이올시다. 모조리 구경시켜 들이지요. 모조리 보여 들이겟슴니다. 엇더심니까? 안즈시지요.
헤레나-(長椅子 압헤 가서 안즈면서) 고맙슴니다.
떠민-그런데 맨 먼저로 인조노동자를 발명하든 이야기를 좀 드르시렴니까?
헤레나-네 들여주세요.
떠민-(황홀한 모양으로 헤레나를 흘깃거리면서 별안간 짓거리기 시작한다.) 유명한 생리학자이엿든 롯섬씨가 해양동물 연구를 목적하고 이 절해의 외로운 섬으로 오게 되엿든 때는 1920년이엿슴니다. 씨가 그때에는 매우 젊은 과학자이엿슴니다. 그러는 중에 씨는 원형질이라고 하는 생물을 화학적으로 맨들기를 시작하엿슴니다. 그래서 그여히 발견된 것이 化學上 조직과는 틀린 생물과 꼭 가튼 것을 발견하엿슴니다. 그 발견은 1932연의 일인데 그때가 꼭 아메리카 발견한지 400년이＜60＞ 됨니다.
헤레나-그러면 당신은 그 발견한 것을 왜 우슴니까?
떠민-네 생물학은 나의 전문이 안이니깐요. 그리고 또 이야기를 계속 할까요?
헤레나-네 들려주세요.
떠민-그래서 롯섬씨는 화학상의 표본 가운대 이러한 것을 써노앗서요. 『자연이 생물을 맨드는 법은 다만 하나 밧게는 업다. 그러나 그보다 더 간단하게 뜻대로 더 신속하게 되는 다른 방법이 잇슬 줄이야. 자연은 꿈에도 생각지 못하엿스리라. 인생의 진보를 보이는 제2의 방법은 오날 나로써 발견되엿다.』 이러케 써 잇서요. 이것 보세요. 개(犬)까지도 도라다 보지도 안튼 膠質의 雜物에 대해서 이러케 놀나울 말을 써노은 氏를 좀 생각해보세요. 시험관 압헤 안저서 모든 생명의 나무가 그 시험관 속에서 엇더케 살게되며 또는 甲虫과 가튼 물건에서 사람에게까지-물론 우리와는 다른 물질의 사람이지만-그 모든 동물이 그 시험관 속에서 엇더케 되여 가는 것을 몰두해서 보고 잇섯든 롯섬氏를 생각해 보십쇼. 그가 이러케 연구하엿든 그때야말로 참으로 놀날 만할 시간이엿슴니다.
헤레나-무엇이요?
떠민-그래서 인제 문제라는 것은 이 시험관에서 엇더케 생명이 생기며 또한 진화해서 심장과 골격과 신경이 생기며 또는 효소와 접합소들의 접촉물이 엇더케 발견되엿느냐는 것이지요-아시겟슴니까?
헤레나-잘 알 수는 업슴니다마는.
떠민-좀 참으시고 들으십쇼. 엇지햇든 롯섬氏가 맨들여고 하엿든 것은 무엇이든지 할 수 잇섯다구만 생각하세요. 소크라데스의 두뇌나 메데유사와 가튼 怪物이나 50야드나 되는 長虫이나 무엇이든지 그가 맨들지 못하든 것은 하나도 업섯서요. 잡담은 그만두고 그는 위선 脊椎動物 더욱이 사람을 맨들려고 게획햇슴니다. 그래서 이 인공적 생물은 생활을 열망하는 것이며 또한 따라서 꾸매기도 하게되며 깨트러 부시기도 하게되는 것을 아모러케도 생각하지 안은 물건이엿슴니다. 그런 故로 자연의 단백질로 만들 수는 업슴니다.＜61＞ 그래서 그가 생각을 하게 되엿음니다.
헤레나-무엇 이예요?
떠민-자연을 모방한단 말이예요. 위선 처음으로 氏는 인공적으로 개(犬)를 만들려고 하엿슴니다. 그러나 몃 칠 동안 애쓰고 만든 것은 고만 송아지가 되고 말엇슴니다. 그나마 나달후에 죽엇슴니다. 그것을 지금 박물관에 가서 보여들이지요. 그리고 그 후로 그는 사람을 만들기 시작햇슴니다.
헤레나-이런 이야기를 남에게 말해도 관게찬슴니까?
떠민-염체 남에게는 아조 비밀로 하세요.
헤레나-그러나 그까진 것은 歐羅巴나 米國이나 학교 교과서에도 잇지 안슴니까?
떠민-그럿슴니다. 그러나 그 교과서 이외에 사실을 아심니까? 롯심氏는 정신이 미첫다는 것이예요. 늙은 밋치괭이가 훌륭하게 사람을 만들엇다는 것을 꼭 아세야 함니다.
헤레나-그러나 당신도 사람을 만드실 줄 아시겟지요.
떠민-그것은 엇지햇든 롯섬氏는 참으로 훌륭한 사람을 만들엇슴니다.
그는 신의 과학적 대용물이 되려고 하엿든 것임니다. 그리고 氏는 극단에 물질주의자이엿서요. 그래서 그런 일을 한 것임니다. 그가 목적한 것은 신이라는 것은 인제는 불필요하다는 것의 증거를 세우려고 한 것에 불과한 것이지요. 부인은 해부학의 지식이 게심니까?
헤레나-그저 조금쯤은 잇슴니다.
떠민-나 역시 그러키는 함니다마는. 엇지햇든 氏는 무엇이든지 사람과 가튼 것을 만들려고 결심하엿슴니다.
그가 10년 동안이나 애쓰고 만들다가 잘못된 견본을 보여들이지요. 사람이라고 만들어 논 것이 단 사흘동안 밧게는 더 살지 못하여섯든 까닭에요. 그런데 기사노릇을 하고 잇든 그의 아들이 나타낫지요. 그의 아들도 매우 장한 사람이예요. 그래서 그 아들 되는 이가 그의 아버지가 잘못 만든 사람을 보고는 이러케 말하엿슴니다. 『사람 하나를 만드는데 10년식이나 걸린다는 것은 어리석은 일이예요. 자연보다 더 빨리 못될 것이면 그만두는 것이＜62＞ 훨신 남니다.』라는 말이엿서요. 그래서 그 아들이 다시 해부학 연구에 몰두하엿슴니다.
헤레나-학교 교과서에는 그런 말이 업는데요.
