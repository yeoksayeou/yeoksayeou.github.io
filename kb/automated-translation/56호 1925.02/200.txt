기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

땅속으로
						抱石
一

When I left Tōkyō this spring and came to what I call my home near S Station (though it’s actually my elder brother’s house), I found the family situation truly beyond words. A large family of ten… no, fifteen or sixteen members, were crammed together in a house that wasn’t very spacious. Just seeing the young children clustered in a narrow room corner looked like chestnuts packed into a gourd dipper. Among them were a couple of my own offspring.

As for what this multitude eats to survive, it’s millet rice in the morning and millet gruel in the evening. Occasionally, maybe rice mixed with sorghum, or sometimes gruel with green vegetables mixed with a bit of millet grain. Do they even manage to eat consistently? Not even that. If there's food for two days, there's none for one. If they eat two meals, they skip one; if they eat one meal, they skip two. They generally get by like this. This is only possible because they farm about ten *turak* (斗落 - a unit of land) of someone else's land as tenant farmers, allowing them to borrow a *mal* (斗 - a unit of volume) of grain at high interest (長利) or a few pennies (邊錢) from others.

The faces of everyone in the household look like yellow flowers have bloomed. They all seem utterly devoid of vitality. Yet, their eyes hold not so much a murderous glint (殺氣) that pierces one's gut, but rather a look of weakness (즘약미), an oddly unpleasant, tenacious expression. (One often sees this in people, especially nervous (神經質) ones, who have suffered misfortune for a long time and cannot inwardly digest the pain.)

They say impoverished homes have many quarrels, and perhaps because everyone is left with only malice, adults and children alike start fights at the slightest provocation. What is to become of this household? The house has become utter chaos. Adults don't act like adults, and children <30> don't act like children.

Seeing this scene for several days after rushing home (it certainly wasn’t this bad five years ago when I was last here), I was reminded of some kind of "living hell," a "realm of hungry ghosts and warring demons" (餓鬼修羅場). I came to grasp, by inference, the living conditions of the vast majority of Korean people. (Though it's common to see even more惨혹한 scenes than this in our Korea.)

Apparently, a new sense of harmony (和氣) entered the house after my arrival, compared to before. The reason (리유) for this harmony might partly be the joy of seeing me after a long time, but a bigger factor was the hope that now that I had returned, some great means of saving the entire household would arise.

After I came home, sat down, and exchanged greetings with everyone, I recounted, at my elderly mother's (老母) request (소청), the gist of my life abroad. Then, at my request, I asked about the family's situation.

Fragments of stories emerged from this person's mouth and that person's mouth in turn. Among them, my sister-in-law (형수씨), known for being the most talkative, spun a rather long tale, ending with these words:

『Oh, now our family has no worries at all! When the new master (referring to me) goes to Seoul and becomes something like a lawyer (辯護士), he'll earn at least tens of thousands of *nyang* a month. Even if he were to become a county magistrate or a provincial governor (도장관), he could do it as easily as turning his hand over... Why wouldn't a university graduate *yangban* (량반) be able to do anything?... Hey, new bride (looking towards my wife), you've really lucked out now. You'll live in comfort. As they say, joy comes after hardship, now truly...』

I found these words so absurd and laughable that the smile starting to form vanished abruptly. My lips clamped shut tightly, and my inwardly rolling eyes stared only at the dirt-stained paper floor.

『It’s unlikely the boy would do such things, but anyway...』 My elder brother trailed off. He knows a little about current affairs (시채사정). However, behind that word "anyway" lay hidden the meaning of great expectation (긔대). <31>

『Be that as it may, given the family situation and now that you've finished your studies, shouldn't you start thinking about a way to make a living?』 These were the words of my aged mother, her hair white as snow and wrinkles etched deeply, considered rather dignified for a woman of the house (안부인).

Through all this, I just sat silently listening, offering no response (말대척).

When night came, the rest of the family was crammed into the main room (안방 - about half a *kan* in size), resembling soybean sprouts grown packed in a steamer. I went down to the room below the courtyard.

In the dim room, where the smells of grime and dust from the newspaper-pasted walls stung my inner nose, I sat facing a flickering kerosene lamp placed in a corner, sometimes sitting, sometimes lying down. Eventually, thinking, 『Whether I sleep or not, I'll just lie here until dawn,』 I lay staring up at the ceiling, crisscrossed with cobwebs and dotted sparsely with flies. My head throbbed, my mind felt distant. It was as if I had woken from a sweet dream to find myself lying on the iron grid of reality (現實). Indeed, the train that had carried me from Tōkyō Station (東京驛) and then Pusan Station (釜山驛) had brought and deposited me in this scorched-earth zone (焦獄地帶).

Lying there thinking, the resolve I had always maintained hardened further at this moment. Suddenly, my upper lip pressed down over my lower lip, my head shook semi-convulsively (半痙彎的), and I muttered to myself,
『There’s no helping it. If we all starve to death, so be it.』

Having made this decision, there was no need to worry or think, no matter what sights appeared before my eyes. Yet, my heart could find no peace. I clenched my jaw once more, reaffirming,
『Ugh! May all thoughts just vanish!』

I glanced around the room. Only then did I realize I was alone in this quiet, desolate room. (The main room, of course, was noisy with human sounds.) For me to occupy such a room alone (my disposition (성벽) favors quiet, <32> so in the past, I always insisted on having a room to myself, no matter what) felt excessively unfair to the others. So, I thought that when my brother, who had stepped out, returned, we would naturally sleep together, but for now, I should go to the main room, bring my mother and perhaps the younger children down here, and alleviate the overcrowded situation somewhat for those in the main room. Just as I was about to get up, a voice called from outside the door,
『Father.』

It was the voice of my nine-year-old eldest daughter. I gave no answer.

『Father.』

She called again, opening the door and appearing. Right behind her, holding our five-year-old second daughter, stood the person known as W.

They entered together in a line. My immediate thought was,
『Are they already pushing them onto me as my family?』 I found the impatience of the people in the main room rather odd. Though there were other reasons too, of course...

『Why did you come in?』 I asked, looking at the mother and two daughters huddled together like a mountain (뫼ㅅ산자) in the upper part of the room (웃묵). They gave no answer. W sat facing the lamp sideways (칙면), and on the wall, the shadow of her face showed protruding cheekbones slanted (엇비슷하다) sideways, drawing the gentle slope (문수룻하다) of a mountain peak.

『She’s gotten quite thin,』 I thought inwardly.
『Why did you come down? I was planning to sleep here with Grandmother and Eldest Uncle...』

『Even after meeting again after so long...』 The wife (마누라장이) glanced at me resentfully with her large, sunken eyes, then turned her head back towards the wall.

『When did we ever sleep affectionately in the same room?』 I snapped brusquely (퉁명스럽게), then immediately regretted letting out such hateful words yet again.

Perhaps because she had endured considerable abuse (학대) and hatred from me since long ago, <33> her nerves (神經) had become numb (痲痺) to such things, making her indifferent to minor incidents. Or perhaps, thinking of the decorum towards a so-called husband she hadn't seen in a long time, she forced herself to endure. She just sat silently, offering no retort (말 대꺼리). I couldn't stand it any longer (enduring the presence of someone you loathe is truly no small torment).

『Go back inside. Quickly,』 I urged.

『Why go back inside? I’ll sleep here,』 she replied with such insensitivity (무신경) and shamelessness (몰념치).

『Sleep? Sleep here? Don’t be like that. Just leave the children, perhaps... The main room is crowded. You should go back quickly,』 I said, trying to coax her away, wanting somehow to get rid of this nuisance.

『Why should I go back? I’ll sleep here. And if you go to Seoul tomorrow, I’ll follow you too. It’s better to die than to live like this.』

『What? What did you say? That’s truly astounding. Living or not, how could you not know? I have enough trouble just trying to do my own things, who am I supposed to support? Anyway, I don’t want to hear it, and I don’t want to see you.』 『If you don’t know, who does?』

Finding it extremely irritating (긔치안어서) to listen, I frowned.
『I don’t want to hear it. Go back inside, now!』 I raised my voice slightly. This time, she too grew somewhat angry.

『Damn it all! There’s a limit to how much you can hate someone!』 The way she spoke showed the prickly temper that used to flare up occasionally in the past was starting again. My own anger surged.

『If I tell you to go, then go! What’s with all the chatter? I can’t stand the sight of you, get out!』 I yelled.

Her resistance (反抗) grew stronger, and turning her face towards me, she asked,
『Now you say you can’t stand the sight of me again, what do you expect me to do?』

『That’s why I told you long ago to go somewhere else.』

『Go where?』<34>

『Where? To marry someone else, that's what I mean!』 I added with a sneer.

Having reached this point, she seemed utterly dumbfounded. Unsure whether words, tears, or laughter would come out, she seemed at a loss. The young children looked at me fearfully, glancing sideways as if facing some unfamiliar foreigner (異邦人). This scene was not the behavior between a husband and wife or parent and child, but rather like confronting an enemy or adversary.

Anticipating that loud words would follow, she, perhaps finding it absurd, instead spoke in a low voice,
『Even cruelty has its limits. After nearly ten years of marriage, enduring all sorts of hardships, my youthful flesh (살끠) has all withered away. And now, you start this anew... Moreover, if I didn’t have the children (자식색기), I could just go off to the mountains or the water by myself...』 she pleaded, almost begging.

『I don’t know. Children or whatever... Did I ask to have them? Did I want to get married?』 I muttered to myself, 『What a cursed, ill-fated connection (惡緣) there is in this world. What wretched fate.』

『Then are you telling the three of us, mother and daughters, to just die?』 Her voice trembled slightly as it rose.

『I don’t know! Die or live, I don’t know! Truly, I don’t know! Truly...』 My lower jaw trembled uncontrollably.

As if asking if I truly meant it, she stared hard at me.
『Ah, are you serious?』 she asked insistently.

『Serious? Is there any doubt? Would I say something I don’t mean?』

『Truly?』 she pressed again. Her voice was choked.

『Truly,』 I replied sternly (嚴絶). Now she seemed unable to bear it any longer. Sitting precariously like a tombstone (碑石) erected on a steep slope (急傾斜地), about to collapse any moment, her face terrifyingly tragic, she stared down at the floor, tears dropping heavily from her eyes.

『Then, let the three of us die!』 Her voice scraped out of her throat, trembling as it did. <35>

The two children, who had only been looking at their mother's face, successively cried out, 『Mother!』 『Mother!』 and clung to her skirt. This only intensified her grief. The lamp, which had been flickering dimly until now, seemed to quietly raise its small pupils, waiting for the scene (場面) about to unfold. She struck the floor with the hand resting on her knee, slumped sideways, and cried out, 『Oh dear, what am I to do!』 letting out a sob. The cry seemed to well up from the bottom of her stomach (배ㅅ속 밋). The elder daughter started crying too. The second daughter, seemingly more startled, shrieked 『Mother, Mother!』 and cried.

I leaped to my feet.
『Ugh, damn it all! Why are you crying? I can’t stand this sight, I’m leaving for Seoul right now, this very night! Ugh!』 Angrily, I darted out the door.

Outside, it was extremely dark. Clouds that had gathered in the sky before dark were now falling as a drizzling rain, silently.

Walking a few steps out and looking around, everything suddenly seemed darker. A feeling abruptly arose: 『Let everything on earth be locked tight in the destiny of darkness.』 Heading towards the main room, I reached the edge of its stone step only to realize the lights were out inside. The snoring sounds of several people mingled together. The sound of crying from the room below the courtyard grew louder. More than anything, I wanted to escape that crying sound, so I circled around the corner by the main room's chimney and crept under the eaves (쳠하). The crying could still be heard here. I worried that the people in the main room, especially my mother whose sleep was precious, might wake up and hear it, causing a commotion in the dead of night. But fortunately, they seemed deeply asleep.

I took a few more steps towards the back, stood stock-still facing the darkness. Across the fence, a bright lamp shone from a house in the neighboring village. Obscured by tree branches, it disappeared and reappeared countless times. Facing the darkness, I let out a long sigh, "Hoo—". The sound trembled violently. My chest, too, felt constricted. The sound of crying <36> faintly came and went. As I recalled this small, headache-inducing drama, repeated countless times in the past (녯날), being replayed anew tonight immediately upon my arrival, I felt regretful—『I shouldn't have gone that far』—and also resentful. It was painful, but also resentful. A wave of frustration surged through me.

『Ugh, let’s just go somewhere! Let’s just wander off aimlessly tonight!』 I muttered under my breath and started walking towards the brushwood gate (사리문). (Ignoring the light rain.) I opened the gate and stepped out. The night was dark and quiet. Even the sound of the fine rain wasn't falling. The only sounds were the occasional dripping from tree leaves and the distant, faint sound of water falling from a water mill. They only served to deepen the stillness of the spring night. I walked one step, two steps, passed the house in front, then one house, two houses, reached the edge of the village, and arrived at the millstone shed located midway across to the next village. The crying shouldn't reach this far, but whenever I thought of it, it seemed to echo faintly in my ears. The children's crying, especially, tugged at my heart. Just as I was about to move my halted feet, I was startled by the sight of the pitch-black "old tree" (노성나무) standing before the millstone shed. Beneath that tree was a well. It wasn't visible in the dark, but the thought of the well sent a chill through my chest.

I quickly changed direction, took a different path, chose an even smaller lane, and walked on. Things that happened long ago all came floating back to my eyes. Suddenly, feelings of terror (恐怖) and pity (憐憫) exploded within me.
『That well—』 I whispered softly to myself.

The story about that well is this:
Ten years ago, driven away by me (my chasing her out was because, at fifteen, my sexual awareness began to awaken; when we married, she was sixteen and I was twelve, and I had actually liked her then, boasting to others), she had gone to her parents' house and stayed for four years. On the very day she returned, I harangued her to leave again, pushing her out. She went straight to that well and jumped in. It was only by chance that people who had come from her family home carrying a palanquin, while out sightseeing in the village, looked into the well wondering about its depth and discovered her. When they pulled her out then, everyone thought she was dead. <37> What a sight she was then! Disheveled hair, bloated flesh, water pouring from her nose and mouth, eventually turning to blood.

Later, after she regained consciousness lying in the lower part of the side room of our house (at that time, it was a bigger, better house than the current one, located four or five houses down from that millstone shed. Now, because this area has become somewhat bustling, a Japanese general store owner (雜貨商) cum moneylender (高利貸金業者) occupies that house. The house my brother is currently in is also said to be mortgaged to him.), she opened her eyes and looked up at me in the upper part of the room (the look in her eyes then... how best to describe it... if one were to speak of the deepest resentment (怨恨) and plea (哀願) in this world, one could only point to that gaze). Tears streamed down from her lifeless, dark eyes, her spirit (넉) drained. Even though, before she jumped into the well, I had always inwardly wished, 『If only she would die,』 now, instead, I felt regret and unbearable pity. I rushed forward, embraced her prone chest, and pressed my cheek against hers. At that moment, several drops of my tears, however precious they were, must have fallen on her face. In silence (無言), the two of us forged an emotional reconciliation (講和). My eldest daughter was also the product (所산) of that peace treaty signing (講和條約締結). But even after that, marital affection (夫婦間愛情) remained a separate issue.

The deep spring night is cold. My body, already chilled by the fine rain, began to shiver (옷솔옷솔하다). After the memory of the well passed like a film, another thought arose.—Could such a thing happen again, or something worse—mother and daughters dying together........... The image of the three of them collapsed in tears in that room flashed before my eyes. The sound of crying seemed to echo in my ears again. My chest tightened. I turned abruptly, about to head back towards the house. Then I stopped short and thought for a moment:
『Why am I being dragged down by such weak sentiment, binding my own destiny? Why can't I possess eyes of tragic (悲痛) will (意志), like fragments of shattered steel?』 Thinking this, I tried forcibly to calm my constricting heart. But it was useless. The emerging feeling of pity (憐憫) could not be stopped. I stamped my foot. <38>

『Oh, I can't help it! I am a weak person.』 And I quickly strode back towards the house.

Closing the brushwood gate I had left open, I turned and looked at the room door. The light was still on. Walking silently, I stood before the door and listened carefully. The crying had completely stopped. Only the sound of breathing, perhaps a child's small breaths, could be heard. Occasionally, a long sigh, as if startled, escaped. The room was quiet. But the lingering echo (餘韻) of the earlier crying seemed to drift enduringly in the room. I quietly opened the door, entered, and observed their state.

They all looked as if struck down by some terrible blade or bullet. In the center of the room lay the elder daughter, next to her, her, and next to her, the younger daughter. She, wearing only a thin cotton jacket (홋적삼), had taken off her padded jacket (솜저고리) and draped only the upper body of the daughter lying face down beside her, cheek pressed to the floor. The youngest daughter, lying in the uppermost part of the room, was covered by her own right arm and skirt hem, her left hand placed on her forehead as she lay face down (업푸러저).

『Maternal love (母性愛) is truly immense,』 the thought quickly passed.

Her body seemed to sink deep into the ground, as if crumbling. The posture (姿態) of a fading soul (魂). I wanted to rush over and embrace her tightly. But I changed my mind, picked up the blanket spread in the lower part of the room (아랫목), and gently covered them.

Then, I took off my rain-soaked Western-style jacket, hung it up, and sat down just as I was, in my shirt (삿스 바람), in the lower part of the room. It felt as though a great whirlwind (回旋風) that was about to rise in my chest had subsided right there, and the black sky that was about to pour down a torrential rain remained just staring blankly. Only the occasional lingering echo (餘韻) of the crying (now their breathing sounded like the melody of weeping) or the intermittent sight of their pitifully lying forms brushed against my heart, causing large raindrops to fall, it seemed, within my darkened chest.

I could think of nothing. There was no reason to think. There was only a pitch-black future, uncertain whether it would collapse or explode. Not just the future, but the past and present too were nothing but pitch-blackness (캄캄하다). Only the occasional sound of some mournful (悲痛) whistle echoed from this side to that side of the dark world. <39> It was the lonely (孤寂) breath of life (生), no, of the soul (靈魂), born from suffering (苦痛) and despair (絶望), with nowhere to land.

I closed my eyes. My soul (魂) was about to sink into the world of bitter meditation (瞑想). I remained like this for some time.

Spring nights are short. The sound of chickens crowing rises from a distant village. Following that sound, a neighbor's chicken flaps its wings and crows too. Time turns a corner. Thus, eternity (永遠) greets another new day and sends off the old. Along with the sound of the chickens, I let out a long sigh and broke free from my meditation (瞑想). The room was still maintained by the dimly flickering lamplight. I turned onto my side and tried to recover the lost meditation.

I must have dozed off at some point. Whether I slept for a full hour or half, I don't know, but when I woke and opened my eyes, she had placed the younger daughter beside me, the elder daughter at my feet, and covered even me with that same blanket. And she herself was huddled alone, asleep, in the upper part of the room (우ㅅ목). I sat bolt upright, rushed over, embraced her, pulled her down to the lower part of the room, spread the blanket wider to cover us, and we ended up sleeping together in one spot.

Although I should have gone straight up to Seoul the very next day, I endured and stayed home a bit longer, wanting to withstand this painful taste a little more. There was also the persuasion of my elderly mother (老母), who wanted to see the son she had longed for for so long (especially since I was the youngest son (망내아들), born when she was in her fifties and known for her affection), for even one more day. Thus, I ended up leaving for Seoul the day after. As I left home,
『Please, think of some solution quickly. Think of the family situation...』 This plea also came from my elder brother. (續 / To be continued) <40>

**Summary**

The narrator, an educated man returning from Tōkyō, finds his family in rural Korea living in extreme poverty and dysfunction. Overwhelmed by the squalor, hunger, and constant quarreling, he feels detached and resentful, particularly towards his wife and children, whom he sees as burdens. A confrontation with his wife, stemming from his coldness and her desperation, escalates to the point where he tells her and their daughters to die, driving her to despair. He flees the house, haunted by memories of her previous suicide attempt and tormented by conflicting feelings of anger, pity, and responsibility. Ultimately, his compassion overrides his detachment, and he returns to find his wife and children huddled together; he covers them and later shares a blanket with his wife, signifying a fragile, temporary truce born of shared misery. The story ends with him leaving for Seoul, pressured by his family to find a solution, but with his internal conflict unresolved.

From a historical perspective, the story powerfully illustrates the grim reality of rural poverty and social disintegration in 1920s colonial Korea. The stark contrast between the narrator's education abroad (Tōkyō) and the "living hell" he returns to highlights the disillusionment faced by Korean intellectuals who found their learning offered no easy solutions to the deep-seated problems of their homeland under colonial rule. Historians might find the detailed depiction of tenant farming struggles, family dynamics under extreme stress, the mention of Japanese moneylenders, and the psychological toll of poverty and hopelessness particularly valuable insights into the socio-economic conditions and personal despair prevalent during that era.

땅속으로
						抱石
一

내가 올 봄에 동경을 떠나나와 S역(驛) 근처에 잇는 내 집이라고 와서 보니 (그 집이란 것도 실상 내 집이 아니요 내 형님 집이다) 집안 형편이 참 말이 못된다. 식구는 십여 명 아니 15.6식구가 되는 대가족(大家族)이 과히 넓지 못한 집구석에 웅긔중긔 모여 산다. 좁은 방구석에 어린아이들만 모여 안진 것을 보아도 쪽박에 밤 담어 노은 셈이다. 그 속에는 내 소생이 두어 개 끼여잇다.
그레 그 다수식구가 무엇을 먹고 사느냐 하면 아침에는 조밥 저녁에는 조죽 수 조화야 쌀밥 엇던 때는 좁쌀깨나 석긴 풋나물죽 그것도 끈이를 이어가느냐 하면 그도 그러치 못하다. 양식잇는 날이 잇흘이면 업는 날이 하로 두끼 먹으면 한끼 굼고 한끼 먹으면 두끼 굴머 대개 이 모양으로 사라나간다. 그것도 남의 땅마지기나 십여두락(十餘斗落) 어더서 소작하는 덕택에 남에게 장니(長利) 말(斗)이나 변전(邊錢)냥이나 어더오는 까닭이엿다.
왼 집안 식구의 얼골꼴이라니 노랑꼿들이 피여잇다. 모다 생긔라고는 족음도 업서들 보인다. 그리하면서도 눈에는 무슨 사람의 오장을 쏘는 듯한 살긔(殺氣)보다는 즘약미(弱味)를 가진 보기에도 이상히 실케하는 악착한 표정들을 띄이고 잇다. (오레ㅅ동안 불행에 처하야 고통을 속으로 삭이지 못하는 사람들에게 그 중에도 신경질(神經質)인 사람에게 이런 것을 흔히 본다.
가난한 집에는 싸홈이 만타더니 사람들이 모다 악만 남어 그러한지 아이 어른 할 것 업시 걸핏하면 싸홈질을 일으킨다. 엇지 되여 나가랴는 집안인지 집안이 그만 난장판이 되고 마럿다. 어른도 어른노릇을 못하고 아이도＜30＞ 아이노릇을 못한다.
달녀들며 멧칠동안에 이러한 광경을 본 나는(다섯 해전 내가 집에 잇슬 때에는 물론 이 지경은 아니엿다) 무슨 「산 지옥」 아괴수라장」 (餓鬼修羅場)을 연상(連想)하게 되얏다. 다대수의 조선사람 생활이란 것을 미루어 짐작하게 되얏다. (우리 조선에서 이보다 더 참혹한 광경을 보기는 예사이지마는)
내가 온 뒤에 예전보다는 새로운 화긔(和氣)가 집안에 돌게 되얏다는 꼴이 이 모양이다. 그 화긔가 돈다는 것은 나를 오레ㅅ동안 보지 못하다가 만나보게 됨으로 반가운 생각들이 나서 그리한다는 것도 얼마ㅅ간 리유는 되지마는 그보다도 더 큰 것은 내가 도라온 뒤에는 왼 집안을 살닐 무슨 큰 수가 생기리라는 희망에서 나온 것이엿다.
내가 집에 드러와 자리에 안자 여러 사람들과 인사를 마치고 나서 그 다음은 내 로모(老母)의 소청으로 그 동안 내가 객지에서 지내던 이약이를 대강 말하고 난 뒤에 이번에는 내 청으로 집안형편 이약이를 무럿다.
이 사람 입 저 사람 입에서 번가라 가며 도막친 이약이가 나온다. 그 중에도 제일 수다하다는 내 형수씨 한 분이 남보다 좀 길게 이약이를 끄내여 노터니만 말 끗헤 하는 말이다.
『아이고 인자 우리 집에는 아모 걱정도 업서요. 새 서방님(나)이 무얼 서울 가서서 변호사(辯護士)가튼 벼슬을 하시면 적어도 한 달에 멧 만냥식 버실걸. 하다 못하야 군수고 도장관 가튼 것을 하시자면 여반장으로 하실걸... 대학교 졸업한 량반이 무엇을 못하실나구... 여보게 새댁(소의 내 안해되는 군을 처다보며) 자네는 참 인자 수 난네. 호강하네. 고생끗헤 락이 온다고 인자 참 다...』
나는 이 말이 하도 어이가 업고 우숩기도 하야 빙긋이 나오던 우숨끗이 선뜻 잘나지며 엄연히 입은 꽉담으리고 속으로 끄러 드러가는 눈알이 때무든 기직자리 방바닥만 바라다보게 되얏다.
『그 아이가 무슨 그런 것을 할 리는 업지마는 좌우간...』하고 말끗을 멈추는 이는 내 형님이엿다. 그는 시채사정을 족음 안다는 이다. 그러나 그 「좌우간」이라는 말끗헤는 무슨 만흔 긔대(期待)를 한다는 의미가＜31＞ 감최여 잇다.
『그러나 저러나 집안 형편도 그러코 공부도 다 맛추엇스니 인자 좀 살 도리를 생각하여야 할 것이 아니냐』하고 말하는 이는 안부인으로는 좀 점잔타는 머리가 하얏케 시고 주름살이 여지업시 잡힌 내 늙으신 어머니의 하시는 말슴이엿다.
이러고 저러고 나는 아모 말대척이 업시 안저 듯기만 하엿슬 뿐이다.
밤이 되여 여러 식구는 안방(간 반이나 되는)에다 한 대 모라 늣코(모여 안저 잇는 모양만 보아도 시루 속에 자라난 콩나물 대구리 갓다) 나는 뜰아래ㅅ방으로 내려가게 되엿다.
신문지 쪽으로 발는 찌드른 냄새와 먼지냄새가 마음의 코를 찌르는 침침한 방안에서 까막까막 하는 한구석에 노인 셕유등불을 대하고 안젓다 누엇다 하다가 나종에는 그만 『잠이야 자거나 말거나 누어서 밝히리라』고 생각하고 거미줄이 이리저리 얼키고 파리가 듬성듬성 부터 잇는 천장만 바라다보고 누엇다. 머리가 띙 하고 정신이 아득하다. 마치 단꿈을 깨치고 보니 몸이 철망의 현실(現實)에 누은 셈이다. 실상 동경역(東京驛)에서 또는 부산역(釜山驛)에서 나를 태우던 긔차가 나를 실어다가 이 초옥지대(焦獄地帶)에다 내려노앗다.
드러누어서 생각하던 나는 늘 하여오든 결심이 이때에 더 구더지며 별안간 우t입슐이 아랫입슐을 내려 덥흐며 머리를 반경련적(半痙彎的)으로 흔들거리며
『하는 수 업지. 모다 굴머 죽으면 죽엇지』하고 혼자 이런 말을 하엿다.
이러케 결심한 이상에는 비록 엇더한 꼴이 눈압헤 보인다 하더래도 걱정할 것도 업고 생각할 까닭도 업다. 그러나 종시 마음이 개운할 수는 업다. 나는 한번 더 재처
『엑기-모든 생각이 다 사러저가고 마러라-』하고 입을 또 꽉 담으리엿다.
방안을 휘 한번 돌나 보앗다. 그때에 비로소 자긔 혼자몸이 이 적적하고 쓸쓸한 방에 잇슴을 깨달엇다. (안방은 물론 사람소리로 요란하다) 자긔가 이런 방을 혼자 쓴다함은(자긔의 성벽이 조용한 것을 조화하는＜32＞ 까닭으로 예전에는 세상업서도 꼭 방 하나를 독차지하고 지내엿다) 여러 사람에게 대하야 너무나 미안적다. 그래서 지금 나는 출입하신 형님이 도라오시면 물론 가티 자려니와 우선 안방에 드러가 우리 어머니와 그 외에 어린아이들이나 끌고 내려와서 너무나 업처덥처 지내는 안방사람들의 사정을 좀 보아주리라고 생각하고 자리에서 일어나랴 할 즈음에 문 밧게서
『아버지』
하고 부르는 목소리는 아홉 살 먹은 내 큰딸의 소리엿다. 나는 아모 대답도 업섯다.
『아버지』
하고 또 부르며 문을 열고 나타난다. 바로 그 뒤에 다섯 살 먹은 둘재 딸을 안고선 사람은 소위 W엿다.
그들은 한거번에 쭉 들어온다. 나는 얼는 생각하기를
『저것들을 내 식구라고 발서부터 내게로 내민단 말인가』하고 안방사람들 인심이 너무 조급함을 좀 이상히 생각하엿다. 물론 다른 리유도 잇지마는...
『왜들 드러왓니』하고 나는 웃묵에 뫼ㅅ산자로 한 덩어리저 안저잇는 삼모녀를 보고 말하엿다. 그들은 아모 대답도 업다. W라는 사람은 등잔불을 칙면(側面)으로 대하고 안저서 벽에 빗친 그 얼골 그림자 중앙(中央)에는 쑥 내민 광대뼈가 엽흐로 엇비슷하고 문수룻한 산 봉오리를 그리엿다.
『어지간이 말럿군』하고 나는 속으로 생각하며
『왜,들 내려왓서? 나는 할머니와 큰아버지나 모시고 잘나고 드는대...』
『그처럼 오래간만에 맛나는대두...』하고 마누라장이는 크고도 쑥 드러간 눈으로 원망스러운 듯이 흘끗 처다보고는 다시 벽으로 고개를 돌닌다.
『누가 언제는 한 방구석에서 정다웁게 자본 적이 잇섯나?』하고 나는 퉁명스럽게 말을 하고 나서는 인제 와서 또 새삼스럽게 미워하는 말투를 내인 것을 뉘우첫다.
그는 옛날부디 내 학대(虐待)와 미움을 어간치 안케 바더 왓슴으로 여간한 일에는 시들하게 생각하도록＜33＞ 신경(神經)이 마비(痲痺)가 되여 그러한지 또는 오래ㅅ동안 보지 못하던 소위 남편에게 대한 체모를 생각하고 억지로 참음인지 아모 말 대꺼리도 아니하고 잠잠히 안젓슬 뿐이라. 나는 더 견대일 수가 업서서(이 세상에 보기슬흔 사람을 억지로 참고 대하기라는 것은 참 여간한 고통이 아니다)
「드러들 가. 어서』하고 재촉하엿다.
『드러가긴 왜 드러가요. 여긔서 잘걸』이러한 무신경(無神經) 몰념치(沒廉恥)한 따위엿다.
『자? 여긔서 자? 그러지 말고 아이들이나 두고 가던지... 안방이 좁고 하니. 당신이나 어서 들어가오』하고 엇지 하엿던지 귀치안은 것을 떼여쪼차 드려보내랴고 타일르는 말조로 하엿다.
『드러가긴 왜 드러가요. 나는 여긔서 자고. 내일이라도 서울 가신다면 나도 따러갈걸. 사람이 이 노릇을 하고 사느니 죽어바리지』
『무엇 엇재? 참 긔막힌 소리도 다한다. 살고 말고 그런 것을 다 몰라. 나는 나 혼자 할 일만 다하자하여도 긔막힌데 살리기는 누구를 살려. 그러고 저러고 다 듯기도 실코 보기도 실혀.』『모르면 누가 안단 말이요』
나는 듯기가 몹시 긔치안어서 상을 찝흐리며
『뜻기실혀. 어서 드러가』하고 좀 놉흔 소리를 첫다. 이번에는 저편에서도 성이 좀 나서
『앗다 제기. 사람을 실혀해도 분수가 잇지.』하고 말하는 품이 넷날에도 드문드문 나오던 거슬거슬한 성미를 또 부리기 시작한다. 나는 화증이 더럭 나서
『드러가라면 드러가지, 무슨 잔말이냐. 보기실혀 어서 들어가』하고 소리를 버럭 질넛다.
그는 반항(反抗)하는 긔세가 더 놉하지며 얼골을 내게로 돌리고
『인자 와서 나를 또 보기실타면 엇지 하잔 말이요?』
『그러닛가 진작이라도 어대로 가라고 그리하엿지.』
『가기는 어대로 가?』＜34＞
『어대로? 다른 사람에게로 시집가라는 말이야』하고 나는 비웃는 말투로 하여 부치엿다.
여긔까지 이른 그는 긔가 막힌 모양이다. 말이 나올지 울음이 나올지 또는 우슴이 나올지 엇지할 줄을 모르는 모양이다. 어린아이들은 나를 무슨 낫선 이방(異邦) 사람이나 대한 것처럼 무서운 듯이 흘금흘금 처다들 본다. 이 광경은 부부나 친자(親子)사이에 하는 태도가 이니요, 무슨 원수나 대적을 하는 셈이다.
큰 말소리가 나옴을 나는 미리 짐작하엿던 그는, 어이가 업던지, 그 반대로 도로혀 나지막한 목소리를 내며
『그리 사람을 박대하여도 분수가 잇지. 시집온 지 근 수십 년에 가진 고생을 다 하여 가며 젊은 살끠가 다 빠젓는데. 인제 와서 또 새삼스럽게... 더군다나 자식색기나 업스면, 나 혼자 몸이야 산으로 가거나 물로 가거나...』하고 애걸비스름하게 말한다.
『나는 몰라. 자식색기고 무엇이고 다... 누가 나코십허 난나. 장가들고 십허 들엇나』 혼자말로 『세상에 참 망할 악연(惡緣)도 잇다. 고약한 놈의 운명도 잇다.』
『그러면 우리 삼모녀가 다 죽으란 말이요』하고 조곰 말소리가 놉하지며 떨렷다.
『믈라, 죽던지 살던지 다 물라. 참 몰라. 참...』하고 나는 아래턱이 부르르 떨렷다.
과연 그러냐고 뭇는 듯이, 나를 노려보고 잇는 그는
『아, 참말슴이요?』하고 다지는 말투로 뭇는다.
『참말이고, 여부가 잇서 내가 뷘 말을 할가』
