Script: On the Eve, Five Acts
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl (Translated and Supplemented)

The script *Kyŏgya* (隔夜 - On the Eve) is an adaptation of one of the six major novels, a representative work, by Ivan Turgenev (1818-1883), one of Russia's three great novelists. It was adapted in the 4th year of Taishō (1915) as a performance script for the Geijutsu-za (藝術座) by Mr. Kusuyama Masao (楠山正雄), then a teacher at the Drama School (演劇學校). The original novel adapted for this script was published in 1859; the English translation is *On the Eve*. In Japan (日本), there was also a Japanese translation of the novel by Mr. Sōma Gyofū (相馬御風), also a teacher at the Drama School, titled *Sono Zen'ya* (其の前夜). Mr. Kusuyama also titled this script *Script: Sono Zen'ya* (脚本 其の前夜), and taking its meaning, I have given it the title *Script: Kyŏgya* (脚本 隔夜). I hope the readers will understand this intention.

I will not record the inner meaning of the work in detail here, only that readers should be able to understand it if they read it carefully and savor it.

Mr. Kusuyama wrote the following preface to the original script: "The difficulty of dramatizing a novel increases the richer the original work's artistic value and the deeper its flavor. Moreover, attempting to stage this particular work by Turgenev, enveloped in a uniquely pastoral and tender sentiment, might be a grand 'recklessness' never before attempted in any theater in the world..." Now, as I translate such a script in our Chosŏn (朝鮮), where there is not even the slightest understanding of what art or theater is, I realize something is silently stirring within my heart.

Among the many representative plays of the world, the reason I specifically chose this script to undertake is that there are various points that resonate with us, and also because the current state of Russia (露西亞), geographically closest among many Western countries, does not leave my mind. I feel as though all the characters appearing in this script explain the Russia of today. The fact that the heroine Elena<151> is a pioneer, a type of active new revolutionary of 1850s Russia, her father's stubborn ideology, the young sculptor, philosopher, patriot—all these personalities seem to me to possess a value worth reading ten, even a hundred times. Moreover, even Tolstoy (톨스토이), who generally did not hold such favor towards Turgenev's art, did not spare words of praise for a character like the sculptor Shubin.

The reason I have undertaken the translation of this script this time is indeed due to a request from a certain alumni association (校友會) with plans to stage it later, hence the prohibition of unauthorized performances. The original adapter of this script, Mr. Kusuyama, was not only a teacher from whom I personally received instruction while enrolled at the Geijutsu-za's affiliated Drama School, but at that time, the principal Shimamura Hōgetsu (島村抱月), Nakamura Kichizō (中村吉藏), Sōma Gyofū (相馬御風), Kusuyama Masao (楠山正雄), and Akita Ujaku (秋田雨雀), among other teachers, had especially given permission to me, a foreigner, to translate their works. Furthermore, Mr. Nakayama Shimpei (中山晋平), the music teacher at the time, composer of the song *Katyusha ga waiya* from *Resurrection* (復活 가튜샤가 와이야) and also the composer of several songs within this script, had given similar consent at that time. Obtaining such consent back then was in preparation for offering some help to our theater world upon returning home, but several years have passed with not a single translation completed, nor half a plan realized, greatly betraying the goodwill of these teachers. As I take up my pen to begin translating the script, I cannot suppress all feelings of shame. Since this script was performed at the Tokyo Imperial Theater (東京帝國劇場) when I was studying as a research student at the Geijutsu-za after graduating from the Drama School, I had many internal connections to it, and not only did I offer some assistance on stage, but I also personally heard a lecture on the original author by Mr. Nobori Shomu (昇曙夢), an expert in Russian literature. Therefore, I have some confidence in translating this script today. I especially thank the editorial staff of the *Kaebyŏk* Magazine (開闢 雜誌) for their kindness in allowing the publication of this translated script, and I intend to publish it sequentially with each subsequent issue.

Act One
In the center, an ancient linden tree, large and lush, casting a dark shade. Beneath it, summer wildflowers bloom profusely, and there are 2 or 3 large and small discarded stones.
The distant view shows a windless, blue-waved lake, forests of poplar and birch, and the ruined walls of an old castle.
Evening scene in hot July, clear sky.
Five or six German workers (獨逸 勞動者) dressed in factory work clothes pass by, all very drunk, singing loudly, some shouting, making noise.

Song
You folks are having a drink.
We folks are having a drink too.
Drink up, drink up, till it fills your throat
Drink lots, till you drop dead drunk.

When gambling, we play till the end
Even without paying a single penny
Let's do it, let's do it, keep doing it, all kinds of gambling
Till our fists and bare bodies are stripped naked.
(Insarov and Elena emerge from the thicket, talking. The workers pass in front of them, making various comical gestures, laughing, swearing, greeting, each chattering as they please, and exit.)
Insarov: Quite noisy, aren't they?
Elena: Those people? They're German workers. Lately, they've come to work in all sorts of factories.
Insarov: What about your mother? She's quite late.
Elena: She must be stopping at every point to admire the scenery. It’s her first outing in a long while, so she's making a big fuss. She'll probably be ill for ten days after this.
Insarov: Bersenev seems to have fallen behind too.
Elena: Oh dear, we were too absorbed in our conversation—
Have you known Mr. Bersenev since long ago?
Insarov: Yes, we were classmates at Moscow University. He was<154> in the Philosophy department, and I was in History.
Elena: I heard he was quite talented.
Insarov: That's right. He graduated third in his class, a brilliant student.
Elena: Although what he says involves very difficult academic topics, somehow, when I listen to him talk, I feel an indescribable pleasantness, as if I'm walking the streets of some bright and beautiful foreign city.
Insarov: He is a person with great, noble ideals. The new life for Russians will be pioneered by the hands of such people.
Elena: I often heard a lot about you from Mr. Bersenev and sometimes wanted to meet you.
Insarov: Bersenev is someone who always speaks highly of his friends.
Elena: (As if reconnecting a conversation interrupted by the workers' noise) So, since that time, have you not known where those scoundrels went? (Sits down under the linden tree.)
Insarov: (Standing) Yes? What was it? Those scoundrels, you mean?
Elena: The Turkish officer you mentioned earlier—the one you called your mother's enemy—
Insarov: (Interrupting) You mean that scoundrel? I don't know. I never tried looking for him again.
Elena: So... asking this might be rude... but I heard from Mr. Bersenev that your family was ruined because of that officer—
Insarov: That's correct. That scoundrel dishonored my mother. He killed my father. That Turkish pig—
Elena: Oh—(Pause) Ah, please sit down. Sit down.
Insarov: Yes, I will. (Sits down beside her.)
Elena: (Still drawn by curiosity) How old were you then?
Insarov: It was before I turned 18. I was eight years old. From then on—later, I followed my aunt to Russia. Russia is a homeland that is not my homeland.<155>
Elena: Have you never been back to your home country since then?
Insarov: No. I went to Bulgaria (불가리아) once when I was twenty.
Elena: Did you return to Russia right after visiting?
Insarov: Yes. I suddenly longed to see my homeland, so I went. I wandered around Bulgaria for two years, but... wherever I went, there were only things that offended the eye. A home country ruled by foreigners is truly harder to live in than a foreign land.
Elena: During those two years, how did you not manage to find that enemy?
Insarov: Miss Elena. Taking revenge is not a difficult task. But what occupies my mind right now is an issue much larger than a small family vendetta—the people's enemy... no, perhaps saying it like this might be misleading... That is, the question of whether the entire Bulgarian nation can gain freedom or not is immensely important. It's a big matter before which the small matter pales. If only that great goal is achieved, my small matter will resolve itself... Yes. It will resolve itself.
Elena: (Softly, emotionally) Truly... you are a patriot who sincerely loves your country.
Insarov: No. One cannot know that without facing it later. Only after laying down one's life for that country can one perhaps say that person loved their country.
Elena: Then, if you were in a situation where you could not return to your homeland, would you consider living your whole life in Russia without concern?
Insarov: Well... probably not... I hear distant shouting. (The sound of many people singing and shouting is heard from afar.)
Elena: It must be those people again... (Pause) Your language... Bulgarian, is it very difficult? Somehow, I want to visit Bulgaria too.
Insarov: What's difficult about it? There's nothing difficult at all. It's shameful for a fellow Slav, a Russian, not to know Bulgarian. You should learn it.<156> It's very easy. I'll lend you a couple of Bulgarian language books. Bulgarian songs are by no means inferior to Serbian (세루비아) ones. I'll translate one for you sometime. The sentiment of the song... Not just that, do you know the general outline of Bulgarian history?
Elena: No, not at all.
Insarov: Then a book that gives a general overview of the history... Anyway, here... listen to the song for a bit... Perhaps a book would be better. Read it sometime. You will surely come to think well of our people. After all, you are someone who loves those who cannot endure oppression... (Becoming suddenly agitated) Do you know? Do you know what kind of blessings the country called Bulgaria originally received? Such a country is now trampled under the hooves of foreigners, a sight cruel even to behold... Yes. Our people have been robbed of their land, religion, laws, everything passed down through generations from our ancestors. On top of that, those utterly ignorant Turkish scoundrels drag our compatriots around like cattle, kill them if they wish, fathers, sons, brothers... all scattered to the four winds...
Elena: Look at me...
Insarov: Forgive me. Whenever I talk about my homeland, I just speak mindlessly. You just told me that I truly love my country. To me, after God, there is nothing as certain and trustworthy as this...
Elena: Ah, for you, there is such a trustworthy God, and a country, but for me...
Insarov: Yes? Why is that...
(The voices of Shubin and Bersenev are heard from the thicket.)
Elena: Never mind... They're all coming. Let's go that way.
Insarov: It sounds like Bersenev is coming. They must be looking for us.
Elena: It doesn't matter. Even though Mr. Bersenev is nice, Mr. Shubin<157> will tease us if he comes. Let's go. Yes, let's take a walk over there. I still have many things to ask you...
Insarov: (Stands up and offers his arm.)
Elena: (Smiling brightly, takes the man's hand and stands up) Are you really not going to stay long in this country?
(As the two, shoulder to shoulder, are about to head left, Shubin sees them and shouts.)
Shubin: Hey! Elena! Where are you going...? I've been looking all over, wondering where you were buried.
Elena: (Shrugging) Going where?
(The four meet.)
Insarov: (Explanatorily) Bersenev, what kept you? We were just talking about what you two might be doing... I was just telling Miss Elena a little about Bulgaria.
Elena: (Goes to Bersenev, puts her hand on his shoulder, in a friendly tone) Mr. Bersenev, are you feeling unwell somewhere? You look very pale.
Bersenev: (Forcing a smile) Perhaps the earlier seasickness hasn't worn off yet.
Elena: So what will you do?... Mr. Bersenev, I heard various stories about Bulgaria from Mr. Insarov earlier.
Bersenev: Insarov's stories are interesting, aren't they?
Elena: Yes, they are all gripping stories.
Shubin: Gentlemen! I don't know how pressing the Bulgarian issue is at this moment, but why must we stand here talking as if tied by a rope? Let's rest a little.
Bersenev: Ah. (Saying this, he sits down with Elena under the linden tree in the center. Insarov also joins them, all three sitting with Elena in the middle.) What happened to those coming behind? (End of Act 1, incomplete)

We had planned to publish one act of this script in each issue of our *Kaebyŏk* Magazine, but this time, due to the rush to print, we regrettably could not finish the end of Act 1, for which we feel truly sorry to our readers. However, in the second issue, we will continue the incomplete part of Act 1 and also complete the translation of Act 2, so we ask for your understanding... Translator.<158>

**Summary**

This article presents the first installment of a Korean translation of a Japanese stage adaptation of Ivan Turgenev's novel *On the Eve*. The translator, Hyŏn Ch'ŏl, introduces the work, explaining its origins and his personal connection to the Japanese theater figures involved in the original adaptation. He highlights the perceived relevance of the play's themes—nationalism, foreign oppression (specifically Bulgarian suffering under Turkish rule), and revolutionary spirit (embodied by Elena)—to contemporary Russia and, implicitly, to colonial Korea. The article includes the character list, setting details, and the beginning of Act 1, featuring a conversation between Elena and the Bulgarian nationalist Insarov, interrupted by other characters. The translator apologizes for not being able to include the entire first act due to printing deadlines.

For historians studying colonial Korea, this article is interesting because it shows a Korean intellectual engaging with foreign literature (Russian via Japanese adaptation) and explicitly drawing parallels between the struggles depicted (Bulgarian nationalism against Ottoman rule) and the perceived situation in Korea under Japanese rule, albeit subtly. Hyŏn Ch'ŏl's mention of needing permission from Japanese figures to translate and stage the play, his connections to the Tokyo-based Geijutsu-za, and his framing of the play's themes highlight the complex cultural interactions and the veiled expressions of nationalism possible within the colonial context of 1920. The choice of Turgenev's *On the Eve*, with its focus on a Bulgarian patriot seeking liberation, was likely deliberate and resonated with Korean aspirations for independence.

脚本 隔夜 五幕
						玄哲 (譯補)
脚本 隔夜는 露西亞의 3大 小說家의 一人 이완·툴게넵(1813年-1818年)의 가장 代表作인 6大 小說 中의 一을 大正 4年度에 藝術座의 興行脚本으로 當時 演劇學校 先生 楠山正雄씨가 脚色한 것이다. 이것을 脚色한 原作小說은 1859年의 出版한 英譯- ON THE EVE- 요 日本서는 小說로 亦是 演劇學校 先生인 相馬御風씨의 日文 飜譯의 『其の前夜』가 이섯다. 楠山 先生도 亦是 이 脚本을 『脚本 其の前夜』라 한 것을 余는 그 意味를 取하야 『脚本 隔夜』라는 名稱을 주엇다. 讀者- 此 意를 諒解하기 바라며.
余는 篇中의 內意를 詳細히 記錄지 안이한다. 다만 讀者 諸씨가 精讀 喫味하면 可解할 것임으로-.
楠山 先生은 原作 脚本에 이런 序文을 말하엿다. 『小說을 劇化하는 困難은 原作이 藝術的 價値가 豊富할사록 濃厚한 感味가 만코 濃厚한 感味가 만흘사록 더욱 甚하다. 더구나 特殊한 牧歌的 柔軟한 情趣에 싸인 툴게넵의 此 作을 舞臺에 올린다고 하는 것은 或은 世界 어느 劇場에서던지 일즉이 試驗치 못한 一大 『無謀』가 안인지 모르겟다...』고 하엿다. 이제 藝術이 무엇인지 演劇이 무엇인지 一毫半點의 理解가 업는 우리 朝鮮에서 이러한 脚本을 譯出할 적에 나의 가슴 가운대서 소리업시 지꺼리는 그 무엇이 잇슴을 깨다랏다.
世界 代表的 戱曲이 만흔 中에 特히 이 脚本을 選擇하야 着手한 것은 가장 여러 가지가 우리에게 共鳴되는 점도 잇고 또는 만흔 西洋 中에도 가장 地理的 關係가 갓가운 露西亞의 今日의 狀態가 마음에 떠나지 안이하는 까닭이다. 이 脚本에 나오는 모든 人物이 오늘날 露西亞를 說明하는 것 가튼 마음이 키인다. 女主人公 에레나가＜151＞ 1850年代의 露西亞의 活動的 新革命의 타입의 先驅者임과 그 父親의 頑固한 思想, 靑年의 彫刻家, 哲學家, 愛國者 모든 性格이 우리로 하야곰 十看百讀의 價値가 잇슬 줄 生覺한다. 더구나 彫刻家 슈빈의 性格과 가튼 것은 平生에 툴게넵의 藝術에 그 가티 好意를 가지지 안이한 톨스토이도 讚揚의 言辭를 아끼지 안이 하엿다.
余가 今番에 脚本 飜譯에 着手한 것은 實로 엇더한 校友會의 囑托을 바다 後日 이를 舞臺에 올리랴하는 經營이 잇슴으로 無端興行을 禁한 것이라. 이 脚本의 原 脚色者 楠山 先生은 余가 藝術座 附屬 演劇學校 在學 當時에 親히 授業한 先生일 뿐 안이라 其 當時에 校長 島村抱月, 中村吉藏, 相馬御風, 楠山正雄, 秋田雨雀 諸 先生에게 特히 異國人인 余는 그 著作에 飜譯할 수 잇다는 許諾이 잇는 然故이라. 그 뿐 안이라 當時 音樂敎師 『復活 가튜샤가 와이야』하는 作曲者 兼 此 脚本 中에 잇는 唱歌 數3을 作曲한 中山晋平 先生에게도 同樣의 承諾이 當時에 잇섯다. 이러한 承諾을 當時에 어던 것은 歸國始初에 우리 劇界를 爲하여 무슨 도음이 잇슬가하여 準備한 것이 遷延幾個星霜에 一個 飜譯이 업고 半個의 成算이 업스니 諸 先生의 好意를 저바림이 만은지라. 붓을 들어 脚本을 飜譯하랴는 劈頭에 스스로 모든 慚愧한 情緖를 禁치 못한다. 이 脚本은 余가 演劇學校를 畢業하고 藝術座에 硏究生으로 在學할 當時에 東京帝國劇場에 上演한 것임으로 內部的 關係가 만핫고 또 多少의 舞臺上 助力도 한일이 이섯슬 뿐 안이라 露西亞 文學通 昇曙夢씨의 原作者에 對한 講演도 親히 들엇는 故로 今日 이 脚本을 飜譯함에 當하야 多少의 自信이 잇다. 이 脚本의 譯載를 特히 許諾하여 주는 開闢 雜誌 編輯 同人 諸씨의 好意를 特히 謝禮하고 自此로 號를 隨하야 順次 揭載코자 하노라.
