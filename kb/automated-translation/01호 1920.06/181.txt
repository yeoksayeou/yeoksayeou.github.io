Oh, Sir! If you had not lost this city, I would not have had the pleasure of recapturing it... I thank you.
This is what Maksimasŭ (Maximus) said to Piyasŭ (Fabius?), who had previously lost said city (該都市), after recapturing a certain city (某 都市) from Hannibal. Indeed. If there is true pleasure in the world, it must be that of recapturing something already lost (旣失), at some unexpected future time (不期何日).

Even if it be by the spears and guns (槍銃) of the enemy (仇讎), dying bravely in one breath is always superior to constantly fearing death (死) and seeking only to prolong life (生).
This was Sija's (Caesar's) reply (答言) to his subordinates' (臣下) earnest plea (懇請), "Your Excellency (閣下), please strengthen your guard (警衛) more strictly." What can one possibly say to the likes (徒輩) of those who merely desire to live (生을 是欲하며) meekly and tremble in fear (戰戰히) of death (死를 是懼하는)?<63>

**Summary**

This short piece presents two anecdotes from Roman history. The first recounts Fabius Maximus's remark upon recapturing a city, highlighting the unique joy found in regaining what was lost. The second quotes Julius Caesar, who dismisses the need for heavy security, arguing that a brave death is preferable to a life lived in constant fear. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, these themes likely resonated deeply. The idea of recapturing something lost could be interpreted as a veiled reference to regaining national sovereignty, while Caesar's quote promotes courage and defiance against overwhelming power, potentially encouraging resistance or endurance under Japanese rule. Historians might find the use of classical Western examples to convey nationalist sentiments, likely as a way to circumvent censorship, particularly interesting.
