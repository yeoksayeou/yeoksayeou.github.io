Author: 靑友 (Ch'ŏng'u)
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Feelings of Nature
						Ch'ŏng'u

One
As the moon's shadow setting at the sea's edge disappears in the twilight glow,
And the sound of the temple gong, floating from the temple, awakens lazy sleep,
With a bright and clear face
And hot and vigorous energy,
Rolling roundly over that high mountain,
The morning sun arrives, daily creating a new world.

Two
The green mountain struck by evening frost turns transiently yellow,
And fallen leaves in the empty yard are soaked in tears of blood,
With a bitter, sharp, and salty heart and a persistent, steadfast life,
Standing long, long, and lonely on that perilous cliff,
The winter pine does not meaningfully change our great purpose.

Three
The sun and moon that make night and day have their own cycles,
And this creature and that creature moving in mountains and fields rest when it is time to rest,
With endlessly offered devotion and uniquely strong faith,
Winding and flowing through that deep valley,
The single cup of spring water finally creates the valleys and the sea.

Four
The river surface, frozen hard after the cold snow, is brushed by ice,
And the light on the distant hill in the setting sun longed for newness,
With butterfly dances and oriole songs everywhere,
Raising up various things into that unique world,
The spring wind bestows their play as they wish.

Five
Wind blows, snow falls, the year draws to a close, the moon passes, the sun changes,
Many years, past years, again years, living the life they wish to live,
Truth and rightness become worn and old, everything becomes confusion,
But the world's will, like the winter pine, flowing long like the spring water,
Comes together with the morning sun and spring wind at the end of the confused ancestors.
Ah, how great is that resonance! Opener! New Opener! <86>

**Summary**

This poem by Ch'ŏng'u uses vivid imagery from nature—the rising sun, the resilient winter pine, the persistent stream, the renewing spring wind—to depict cycles of hardship and renewal. Each stanza focuses on an element of nature embodying endurance, steadfastness, or the power to create anew. The final stanza synthesizes these themes, acknowledging a time of decay and confusion ("truth and rightness become worn," "everything becomes confusion") but affirming that the enduring spirit of nature, aligned with the forces of dawn and spring, heralds a powerful new beginning or 'opening' ("열님! 새 열님!"). From a colonial history perspective, the poem is interesting for its likely allegorical nature. Written in 1920, shortly after the March 1st Movement, the imagery of endurance through harsh conditions (winter pine, frozen river) and the ultimate triumph of renewal (morning sun, spring wind, the final 'opening') can be interpreted as a subtle expression of Korean national resilience and hope for liberation from Japanese rule, carefully veiled in natural metaphors to evade censorship. The final call, "Opener! New Opener!", resonates strongly with the title of the magazine itself, "Gaebyeok" (Opening a New World/Creation).

自然의 느낌
						靑友
一
바다 끄테 저가는 달그림자 놀비테 사라지고
절 집으로 떠오는 쇠북소리 게으른 잠 깨우치자
빗나고 뚜렷한 얼굴과
뜨겁고 씩씩한 긔운으로
저 놉흔 메우로 둥실둥실 구을러 올라
날마다 새로운 누리를 맨드러 오는 아츰 해.

二
저녁서리 부드친 푸른 메는 덧업시 누러지고
빈 뜰에 떨어진 입새 피눈물에 저졋는데
쓰고도 맵고 짠 마음과 줄차고 꿋꿋한 목숨으로
저 위태한 바위에 길이길이 외로히 서서
뜻답게 우리의 큰 뜻을 박구지 안는 겨을 솔.

三
밤과 낮을 만드는 해와 달은 질적이 서로 잇고
메와 들에 움즉이는 이몬 저몬은 쉬는 때 쉬는데
끗 업시 드리는 정성과 남달리 튼튼한 미듬으로
저 깁흔 골 사이로 구비구비 감도라 흘러
마츰내 골이랑 바다를 일우어 노흔 한잔 샘.

四
찬 눈뒤에 얼고 언 가람낫은 얼음에 스쳐잇고
저몬 해에 아득한 언덕 빗은 새로움 그립더니
이곳 저곳마다 나비춤과 꾀골노래
저 남다른 누리로 가지가지 이르켜 세워
뜻대로 그들의 노리를 베풀어 주는 봄바람.

五
바람 불고 눈오고 그리는 새 달가고 해가 박궈
열해 온해 또 골해 하고 십흔 살림하느라고
참과 올흠 헐고 낡아 모든 것이 헷저음 되는데
겨울솔가튼 누리의 뜻 샘물가티 길게 흘려
헷저음 조상 끄테 아츰 해 봄바람 한께 오니
아아 그 울림 크도다. 열님! 새 열님!」＜86＞
