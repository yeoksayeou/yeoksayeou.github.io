잡지명	개벽 제1호
발행일	1920년 06월 25일
기사제목	筆頭에 臨한 感 (Feelings Upon Taking Up the Pen)
필자	李東園
기사형태	논설 (Editorial)

筆頭에 臨한 感
						李東園
A person is a person because they are alive, and because they are a living person, therein lies value, and therein lies meaning. If we use the word "death" to describe a person, they are no longer a person, but merely a worthless, meaningless object, unusable for anything. Humanity reaches the necessity of removing from the surface of the world the entire existence of this thing that has lost value and meaning. To dispose of the person who loses meaning and value in order to eliminate themselves, we eternally bury them under a handful of red earth (一抔의 赤土) and wait until, through the action of oxidation beneath it, they turn to yellow earth and mix with the great atmosphere of the sky. Could it be said that humans receive life under the premise of the eternal terror that is death, and are born into an eternal chain to enjoy life, feel, weep, rejoice, and sorrow? Therefore, before we cry out for the absolute nature of life, shouldn't we properly cry out for the absolute nature of death? If one says only death is absolute and life is not, we, who possess the greatest and strongest desire in this world to demand life, would want to voice our disagreement. However, within the world of sensation, reasoning, and experience, isn't the possibility of asserting the absolute nature of death slightly greater than asserting the absolute nature of life? But if one only asserts the absolute nature of death, it would be easy to call it the tone of a pessimist or a world-weary person with suicidal tendencies. If we consider that Shakyamuni Buddha (釋迦如來) personally witnessing the process of old age, sickness, and death was the motive that taught humanity his profound, meditative Buddhism, <53> then it seems difficult to say that anxiety about human life and pessimistic facts, and the lamentation thereof, only lead people upwards to be inclined solely towards pessimism. When we see that Jesus Christ did not say, "The kingdom of heaven is near, come enter," but rather, "The kingdom of heaven is near, repent," it is implicit in his cry that the people within Jesus's range of observation could only enter the kingdom of heaven by repenting. When the state establishes the public agreement called law to make communal life smooth, before establishing that saving a person will be rewarded, we see the threatening phrase written first, that killing a person will result in being killed, revealing the shadow of the devil latent within human life. Doesn't the fact that the state repays murderers with the death penalty, written at the head of social life, imply that all people possess a murderous nature, wanting to kill others? If we suppose we could live in an era where saving people was a crime, then wouldn't the phrase "A murderer shall be killed" be changed to "A rescuer shall be killed"? Because we have breathed for a long time within the unwritten law and custom where only a murderer is killed, and a rescuer is revived or rewarded, even imagining "A rescuer shall be killed" creates a feeling like something unprecedented (破天荒). However, even if we lived in a world that was the exact opposite of the present situation, it wouldn't feel that strange. As we who take living as an immovable premise and tread the path of life, while we cannot expect absolute perfection from people without deviation or mutual strife, we still hope for even half-completeness and try to live. But when we realize that behind the curtain of the present situation lies an abyss of doubt, and the more time and effort we invest in reflecting on others' lives and our own, the more the threat of doubt about life and the pressure of emptiness rush towards us like a lion roaring from hunger and thirst, we realize our hearts become filled with the fear of anxiety (杞憂) about the entirety of human existence. The demand for life in humans is stronger and greater than anything else. It gives orders to us all. The cry "To live!" has the mighty power to move our entire being, like the god of strength in mythology, and it has been called the treasure-like material that explains the entire meaning of a person from the first moment of life to the <54> first moment of death with the single premise "to live." Without this one thing, "to live," the entire meaning of our existence would return to emptiness and void. Yet, amidst the effort "to live," when we feel the stench of much human bloodstain (血㾗) and hot blood (血氣), it makes us spontaneously voice laments, asking if it must be so cruel. When we reflect now on the process of life we have passed through, there may be materials to gain, things that make us wail lifelong laments as if unaware that crying won't bring relief, and many things that are vexing or infuriating. When the present cherishes the past and strives to make it valuable, we cannot help but look back at the past. The past was long. There are many unknowable things and countless ambiguous points, but still, it is likely not as long as the future. While no one can assert where the end of the future lies, the possibility of believing even a hypothetical beginning of the past is greater than that for the future. Within this long past, there is our past, others' past, and pasts that belonged to others but were related to us. As we strive to live in the present, harmonizing with everything around us, and struggling to break through when harmony fails, let's also look back at the past. If we evoke our memories of the past, how much joyful material might there be? Like the phrase "unknowable Buddha-body" (佛體), perhaps it would be better to let all our past memories sleep forever? We who have passed through have continued a life of death and suffering in the plain of death, the desert of suffering. Our past was truly a past of curse (咀呪). There are records by ancient Chinese people saying our ancestors excelled in art and music, harmonizing the music of the East, but where have the sounds of such music faded to now? The Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵) and Ch'ŏmsŏngdae Observatory (瞻星臺) in Kyŏngju (慶州), barely remaining after enduring unbearable abuse, only faintly whisper to us of the yearning and deep thought (深古力) of ancient people regarding art and astronomy. It is said that water soon decays if it does not flow; perhaps human life is like the flow of water, and it decayed because it could not flow. Will something arise from the decayed past? Are there other places where new things have emerged from decay? It would be fortunate if there were, but even if not, it is an indefinite hope that perhaps our case could create an exception in human history.<55> Who would desire and be satisfied with a stale life? Perhaps it was merely our lack of self-awareness and reflection that made it so, or perhaps external circumstances forced us into it? We must choose one of the two – either blame ourselves or resent the external circumstances. In any case, shouldn't we let the past bury the past, and from the present towards the future, have no choice but to try living again, and have no choice but to strive to live? Can we, after striving, abandon the impatience that seeks to question the outcome, and create a future of blessing from the cursed past? We ask ourselves but try not to answer. While some powerful command, "To live," seems poised to push us forward impartially, the fact that a feeling of hesitation (躊躇) arises spontaneously – is the demand to advance an external force, and the hesitation an internally generated force? We ask but cannot answer. It feels as if the abyss of death lies horizontally before us, and we don't know if the force pushing from behind is pushing us to live or pushing us to die. It's the abyss of death. One cannot say it pushes us to live, yet the cry "To live!" does not disappear from the heart, so it is difficult to conclude that what intends to go forward intends to go towards death. If it were a force pushing us to go die, we should exert all our strength (死力) at this very moment, cry out for salvation, and change our direction. Should we advance, or sit and cry out for help, or follow the pushing force and accelerate even faster forward? Is this the hesitation and wandering of one person, or is it perhaps a collective illness of the era? We do not know. Regarding the future, we have only the privilege of having one of two things: either anxiety (杞憂) or hope; either weeping or laughter; laughter or weeping. The commander called life does not listen to the tyrannical demand to possess both weeping and laughter simultaneously. Is the future we see hope or laughter, weeping or anxiety? Again, we see it ourselves but cannot answer. In any case, whether the plain of death or the wilderness of life lies before us, we do not enjoy the condition of asking about the reality of what lies ahead and choosing based on that; it is simply like being told unconditionally, "Go forward." By the command of life, "Go forward," we came from the mother's body into the midst of a village inhabited by unfamiliar people, and by that same command, have we not trod the path of life until today, without becoming victims of disease and <56> pestilence? Our path of life is where the flower called life blooms abundantly, and the bitter spring (苦泉) called life flows forth. The beautiful flower blooming beside the bitter spring intoxicates us, making us forget the pain of death, and we sway and jostle amidst all the frustrations (悶鬱). It has been cried out that a person is alive, and through living, a person has value and meaning, but what exists within this living? Recent medical scholars have declared that drawing a boundary line between life and death is impossible, shattering the notion by pointing out the commonality of life and death, the state of their mixture. They say that even among the dead, there is cellular activity or signs of life, and among the living, there are states of death, so the cessation of heartbeats or breathing is not the premise for death. Then, when does our life begin, and when does death commence? Do life and death run parallel, or do they struggle against each other? This too must be entered into the realm of the unknowable. Then, perhaps the time is coming in the not-too-distant future to destroy the boundary line between life and death, one of the legacies we inherited from the past regarding them. But even if we consider future dreams as merely natural empty dreams, would anyone not be astonished if the entire meaning and value of life were found in a life subordinate to inactive, incapable, stagnant, lazy ease (無爲無能 沈滯한 怠惰的 安逸) in the present? For life in the biological sense, blood circulation and air respiration might be deemed sufficient, but for those who are part of the living world yet do not cling to its simple meaning, who speak of life beyond the biological, yearning for the life of the spirit, the life of the mind – when they reflect on their present life, aren't there many who cannot hold back tears unknown to others? The English poet Coleridge said that one minute of reflection holds more value in life than three minutes of teaching from a famous professor at university. Teaching is merely an stimulus (願激物) coming from the outside, while reflection is the true voice of self-confession cried out by a person from within themselves. Since we are destined to find the meaning of life and gain its value in the field of the self's outcry, we have no choice but to choose myself and the reader, right?
Finally, I will not explain how this became lengthy as feelings upon taking up the pen, nor where the intended meaning lies, but entrust it to the readers who can appreciate the external stimulus (外界의 願激) upon the writer. Recently, the fact that the Cheondoist Youth Association (天道敎靑年會) obtained permission right at the outset (劈頭) for the magazine named Kaebyŏk under the Newspaper Law also gives the feeling that the magazine's name, meaning "Creation" or "New Era," has been manifested. At the same time, I hope that through this publication, a great page contributing to the history of Korean culture (朝鮮文化史) will be written with the praising penmanship of future historians, and I, as one part of the whole, also anticipate the efforts of all gentlemen in Korea (朝鮮) and others involved in the management. <57>

**Summary**

This editorial by Yi Tongwŏn for the inaugural issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine is a deep philosophical reflection on the intertwined nature of life and death, suffering, and meaning. Yi grapples with the inherent pessimism arising from the inevitability of death and suffering, questioning the value of existence if not actively lived with purpose and self-reflection. He laments Korea's "cursed past," marked by decay and loss of cultural vibrancy, symbolized by neglected relics, but holds onto a fragile hope for renewal, urging readers to strive forward despite uncertainty. The essay connects personal existential angst with a collective, possibly national, condition, concluding with anticipation for the magazine's role in fostering cultural progress and a new era for Korea. From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its expression of profound existential and national despair under Japanese colonial rule, subtly weaving critiques of stagnation and calls for self-awareness and effort ("To live!") into philosophical discourse. The references to Korea's past glory and present decay, the invocation of both Eastern (Buddha) and Western (Jesus) figures for universal relevance, and the connection to the Ch'ŏndogyo youth movement highlight the intellectual and spiritual currents seeking pathways for Korean identity and progress during a period of suppression. Historians might find the blend of philosophical pessimism, cultural lament, and cautious hope a revealing insight into the Korean intellectual mood of 1920.

筆頭에 臨한 感
						李東園
사람은 산 것이 사람이고 산 사람임으로 거긔에 價値가 잇고 그것에 意味가 잇슴니다. 사람에게 죽엄이라는 말로 써 形容을 하게 되면 인제는 사람이 아니고 價値업는 意味업는 物品으로도 所用되지 못할 用處업는 무엇에 不過합니다. 사람은 價値를 일허바리고 意味가 업서진 이 무엇을 벌서 世界의 表面으로부터 全 存在를 업시할 必要에 至함니다. 스스로 업시하기 爲하야 意味와 價値를 일는 사람을 우리는 處置하기 爲하야 一抔의 赤土 下에 永遠히 葬事하고 그 아래에서 酸化作用으로써 黃土와 化하야 空中의 大氣에 석기기까지 기다림니다. 사람은 죽엄이라는 永劫의 恐怖인 前提아래서 生을 바다가지고 生을 享樂하러 느끼고 울고 깃버하고 슯허하려 永遠히 一連鎖에 난 것이라 할는지오. 그럼으로 우리는 生의 絶對를 絶叫하기 前에 맛당히 死의 絶對를 絶叫하게 되지 아니하는가오. 死만 絶對이고 生은 絶對가 아니라 하면 生을 要求하기에 이 世上에서 무엇보다 더 大하고 더 强한 우리에게는 不服을 言하고 십흘 것이오. 그러나 感覺, 推理, 經驗의 世界 內에서는 生의 絶對를 主張하기보다 死의 絶對를 主張할 可能性이 좀 더 잇지 아니합닛가. 그러나 死의 絶對만 主張한다하면 悲觀的 或은 自殺的 傾向을 帶한 厭世者의 語調라 하기 쉽겟지오. 釋迦如來가 老, 病, 死에 對한 그의 過程을 親히 目擊한 것이 그 深遠한 冥想的 佛敎를 人類에게＜53＞ 가라친 動機엿다하고 보면 人生에 對한 杞憂와 悲觀的 事實 그것에 對한 感歎性도 人生으로 하여곰 向上을 引下하야 悲觀에만 傾倒시킨다고 말하기 어렵겟습니다. 예수 그리스도가 天國이 갓가왓스니 들어오너라 하지 아니하고 天國이 갓가왓스니 悔改하라 한 것을 보면 예수의 觀照의 範圍 內에 在한 사람들은 悔改하여야만 天國에 入할 수 잇섯다는 것이 其 絶叫의 裡面에 伏在하얏습니다. 國家에서 共公한 生活을 圓滑하게 만들기 爲하야 法律이라는 公約物을 定할 時에 사람을 살리면 賞을 준다고 定하기 前에 그러케 定함이 업시 사람을 죽이면 죽인다는 威脅의 文句를 먼저 써노흔 것을 볼 때에 人生의 裡面에 伏在한 魔의 그림자가 보임니다. 殺人者에는 國家의 權力으로 死刑의 報酬를 준다고 社會的 生活의 筆頭에 써노흔 것은 모든 사람은 다 남을 죽이려는 殺人性을 가졋다는 것이 裡書되지 아니 합니다. 우리가 萬若 사람을 救濟하는 것이 罪惡이라는 時代에 生活할 수 잇다고 假定을 하면 그 때는 殺人者는 殺이라는 文句를 고쳐서 救人者는 殺이라고 할 터이겟지? 아지 못하는 佛體라는 말과 가티 우리의 過去의 모든 記憶은 차라리 永遠히 잠이나 재우는 것이 조흘것이 아닌가요, 지내온 우리는 死의 平原, 苦의 沙漠에서 死와 苦의 生活을 繼續하얏습니다. 우리의 過去야말로 참말 咀呪의 過去이엇습니다. 우리의 祖先은 美術과 音樂에도 優勝하야서 東方의 音樂을 조화하던 사람이라고 古代 支那人의 記錄이 잇다 하는데 그런 音樂의 音響이 只今은 어대로 슬어젓는지?
