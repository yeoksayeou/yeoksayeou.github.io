발행일	1920년 06월 25일
기사제목	今後의 朝鮮工業 (Future Korean Industry)
필자	工友俱樂部議事員 劉斗燦 (Yu Tu-ch'an, Member of the Workers' Friendship Club Council)
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Opinion Piece)

The Future of Korean Industry
						By Yu Tu-ch'an, Member of the Workers' Friendship Club Council

The unprecedented calamities of destruction and slaughter, and the poison gas of injustice and brutality in history, have been washed away by the harmonious winds of heavenly principle and righteousness, and the world now faces a bright new destiny of social reconstruction. However, we must not forget that the future holds an intangible economic battlefield, hundreds of times more significant, determining the rise and fall, prosperity and decline of nations and peoples. Looking back at the past, we were fortunate to have peacefully passed through such cruel, armed warfare due to heaven-sent fortune. Yet, it is a fixed rule of heavenly principle that joy follows suffering, and suffering follows joy. We cannot help but think of the tragic fate of economic warfare, where our beloved parents, siblings, spouses, and children will scatter in all directions, enduring wind-blown meals and sleeping under the dew in scorching heat and bitter cold, finally unable to bear the hunger and cold, shedding tears of blood in barren wildernesses, their spirits becoming ownerless vengeful souls, and their parents' flesh and blood becoming food for birds and beasts. No, thinking about this is not what's necessary; correspondence and preparation are needed. What good strategy is there to solve and prepare for this grave problem? Victory in the former [military warfare] was undoubtedly achieved through the five elements of justice, righteousness, human harmony, geographical advantage, and weaponry (干戈), but concerning the latter [economic warfare], while justice and righteousness are necessary, the primary determinant will be the quality and quantity of productive power. Even in ancient times, it was said that one knows propriety only when food and clothing are sufficient; how much more so in today's material age, where further superfluous words (贅言) are unnecessary. Generally, production has many types, but among them, the main subjects and masters (主翁) are agriculture and industry. Thus, if we first observe concretely the future of agriculture and industry, the masters of production: regarding agriculture, even if there were great changes on Earth due to geology, such that oceans become continents, this is a future spring dream. And even if there were advanced research and limitless<87> development in agricultural science, expecting a vast increase in yield beyond imagination from a fixed area of land is unreasonable. Conversely, as world civilization advances, the gradual increase in population is a natural, fixed principle. If this rate of increase depends entirely on agricultural development, what will the result be? Ultimately, the cruel slaughter and struggle for survival, like in the past, cannot but arise again. Let us now consider the death of human life through sentiment. Even when facing parents or others who inevitably depart the world due to depleted blood and weakened energy, we cry out to heaven and weep bitterly (呼天痛哭), grieving ceaselessly. How much more tragic is the situation of orphans crying in the cold night after losing parents, or the feelings of parents separated from their children, unable to bear the loneliness in old age? This is not something one wishes for, nor is it something one wishes to inflict. It is due to unavoidable circumstances. However, looking at the future from the perspective of the latter, industry, our hopes seem distant and vast (遙遙茫茫하다). If its research and development become complete, even if the population increases hundreds or thousands of times compared to now, it goes without saying that people will enjoy comfortable lives through justice and righteousness. To prove this definitively, one can easily understand it by considering the principle of the indestructibility of matter. Also, if we listen to recent examples from Germany (獨逸), there is a wondrous tale that they supplemented military provisions by manufacturing starch from the grasses and trees of mountains and fields, which we originally thought of only as fuel or timber. Observing this from an industrial perspective, it seems not an exaggeration but a fact. That is, the components of rice grain and the components of grasses and trees share the same principle. Seen through such examples, if industry develops and flourishes, what suffering could there be in our human lives, and what could be impossible for human power? Could our ancestors have even dreamed that today, human power would manufacture trains and steamships to provide convenience in transportation, flying ships and airplanes that fly through the sky, electricity that provides light like daytime in the deep dark night and operates huge machinery, all utilizing coal and hydraulic power? Thus, it is a natural principle that in the future, wondrous objects beyond today's imagination will appear, providing infinite convenience to human society. Furthermore, looking at it from the perspective of livelihood, there are not a few people who consider only large-scale production by numerous laborers within enormous capital and magnificent buildings as industry. However, the reality is different. Even if a single individual weaves an inch of rope or a pair of straw shoes, this too is industry. The person who made it will receive a corresponding reward for their labor, and the resulting beneficial money will grant comfort to their own life, and also contribute proportionally to the national interest and people's welfare. Therefore, if our seventeen million<88> compatriots, according to their respective occupations, utilize just thirty minutes of their daily leisure time and perform labor worth an average of one jeon, the additional production amount per day will be 170,000 won, and in one year of 360 days, it will clearly be 61,200,000 won. How can this not be enormous? If this amount were used to operate actual manufacturing industries, setting aside the amount of profit, and considering only pure labor, assuming that 1,000 won of capital requires one person's labor power, it could accommodate 61,200 people. If these 61,200 compatriots, having no fixed property and no occupation (無恒産 無職業), were to leave their beloved parents and siblings, depart far from their native land and mountains, and go to a foreign country with vastly different sights, sounds, and customs, how great would their suffering be? Therefore, various civilized nations advocate that an industrial colonial policy is preferable to acquiring colonies by initiating warfare, and as preparation for economic warfare, policies for industrial development are becoming increasingly fierce. With measures like industrial protection policies, industrial development policies, and industrial relief policies, governments protect private industry in this way. In the United States (米國), under the name of chemical industry development policy, chemical knowledge is disseminated to the general public, technicians and laborers are given special treatment, research institutions are fully equipped to provide convenience for specialists, and even private factories are equipped with research facilities through enormous subsidies to encourage research. In the United Kingdom (英國), they have begun import prohibitions, limiting the import of foreign goods based on certain standards. Therefore, I wish to briefly discuss (畧論코자) our current industrial situation, protection and encouragement (奬勵), and our enthusiasm and desires.

Generally, productive industry is formed by the combination of four classes (階給): capitalists, managers, technicians, and laborers. However, among those called capitalists and industrialists in our society, how many pay attention (留意하는) to industry? How many possess skilled techniques, and how many research academic technology? Furthermore, what goods manufactured by our own hands are exported to foreign countries? It is truly lamentable. What can be called our major industry today is originally nothing more than household industry, namely silk, hemp, and cotton spinning. Looking at the statistics, the annual production value of silk, hemp, and cotton cloth combined does not exceed 6,000,000 won, but the import value exceeds 15,000,000 won. Thus, the amount of money lost to foreign countries each year just for clothing materials (衣次地) amounts to 9,000,000 won. The rest can be inferred from this. Furthermore, regarding protection and encouragement, it's not that there is no protection or encouragement. Rather, it is because there is no one to receive it. Even in a single family, no matter how much enthusiasm the parents have for nurturing and educating, there must be children capable of receiving it,<89> and they must be virtuous and sound. If there are children but they are delinquent, it cannot be done; if they are not sound, failure midway is unavoidable.

We are a people with a history of industry and possess a genius suited for industry. What was the standard of Koryŏ's civilization, and by whose hands and feet was this civilization achieved? Was this not the development of industry, and were those who developed it not our ancestors? For a historic family that thus enjoyed fine clothing and food, laughing and talking in harmony, has today not reached a state where even subsistence living (糊口) is barely possible?

We have a genius for industry, and we have abundant, limitless materials. According to a certain Japanese specialist, Korea has three major drawbacks (缺点) for industrial development related to fuel, hydraulic power, and climate. However, this is a view that knows only one side and not the other. Perhaps it is plausible that a lack of fuel and hydraulic power, and unfavorable climate relations, could be obstacles (障害) to mechanical industry. However, there should be no pessimism regarding chemical industry and artistic industry, which are comparatively unaffected by these factors. That is, our genius and natural disposition are suited for these, and the abundant, inexhaustible (無盡藏) raw materials are also primarily for chemical and artistic industries.

According to the investigation by the Central Laboratory (中央試驗所), the inexhaustible sandy soil on the southern coasts of North and South Chŏlla Provinces (全羅南北道) and South Kyŏngsang Province (慶尙南道) is suitable as raw material for glass (硝子). The soil quality south of Kyŏnggi Province (京畿道) is most suitable as raw material for pottery and porcelain (陶磁器). Regarding other fiber industries, eight-tenths of the cotton produced throughout the provinces is exported to various places in Japan (日本), and it seems more than half of the hemp varieties are also exported. Looking at cowhides and other skins, even though there are various leather companies in places like Taejŏn (太田), Yŏngdŭngp'o (永登浦), Yongsan (龍山), Ch'ŏngnyangni (淸凉里), and P'yŏngyang (平壤), three-fifths of the total production (raw cattle, cowhides, and other miscellaneous skins) is exported. Furthermore, regarding Korean specialty sedge (莞草, wanggol), the Mat Manufacturing Company (製莚會社) outside Tongdaemun (東大門) in Kyŏngsŏng (京城) has been manufacturing Western-style goods for the past three or four years to great acclaim, and there's talk that it will recently undergo a major expansion into a joint-stock company worth 2,000,000 won. Regarding other bamboo products, papermaking, and raw materials for chemical products, although difficult to record each one, there is considerable production in any case. We also hear that precious metals rare in the entire world are gradually being discovered. Considering these relations, we can comprehend the limitless elements necessary for the development of chemical and artistic industries. Therefore, let us awaken and strive. Regarding pottery raw materials, although there is a Ceramics Company (陶磁器會社) in Pusan (釜山), there is no glass business. In the fiber industry, there is the Chosŏn Textile Company (朝鮮紡織會社) and the Kyŏngsŏng Textile Company (京城紡織會社),<90> building factories in Pusan and Yŏngdŭngp'o, but there is still a much more promising hemp manufacturing business with hundreds of times more potential. Looking at leather, although there are the five production sites mentioned above, except for the Yŏngdŭngp'o Leather Company (永登浦皮革會社), they have yet to escape infancy (幼穉).

Allow me to add a word concurrently (兼하야) as a conclusion. Generally, although there are many gorgeous clothing materials like various silks, cottons, and hemp cloths in our society today, what is designated as suitable for our clothes and as our clothing fabric? Regardless of the country, doesn't each nation have clothing fabric of a specific color and quality suited to it? However, isn't our clothing fabric plain white cotton cloth (廣唐木)? Speaking of this plain white cotton cloth, it was originally used for tablecloths, chair coverings, window curtains, and bedding covers in various countries around the world. How can we, who wear this as clothing, not feel shame (羞耻)? The upper class may not know, but there is likely no nation whose lower class displays such a ragged (襤褸한) appearance in white clothing. This is likely not because they want to use it or wear it, but due to unavoidable circumstances. Observing from this perspective, the establishment of a large-scale dyeing industry for the purpose of changing colors is urgently needed at this moment.

Therefore, we should not hope for a mechanical industry unsuited to our natural disposition and natural conditions, but anticipate a chemical industry and artistic industry suitable for us. Furthermore, the abundance of raw materials for chemical and artistic industries, and our genius and natural disposition, are a great benevolence (鴻恩) bestowed by heaven. I believe this will become the great commander-in-chief in our future economic war and grant us a great victory in our lives.<91>

**Summary**

The author, Yu Tu-ch'an, argues that Korea, having avoided the worst of recent military conflicts, now faces a critical economic war that threatens national survival. He contends that agriculture alone cannot sustain the growing population and prevent future conflict. Instead, Korea must prioritize industrial development. However, he dismisses large-scale mechanical industry as potentially unsuitable due to perceived deficiencies in fuel, water power, and climate. He strongly advocates focusing on chemical and artistic industries, citing Korea's abundant raw materials (sandy soil for glass, clay for ceramics, cotton, hemp, hides, sedge) and the inherent "genius" and "natural disposition" of the Korean people for such crafts, referencing Koryŏ's historical achievements. He laments the current state where Korea exports raw materials and imports finished goods (especially textiles), and criticizes the use of plain white cotton (廣唐木), originally for household furnishings, as clothing, urging the development of a dyeing industry. He concludes that focusing on these suitable industries is key to economic victory and national prosperity.

*Historical Significance:* This article provides a fascinating glimpse into early Korean nationalist thought under colonial rule (1920). It reflects a deep anxiety about Korea's economic vulnerability and a desire for self-sufficiency through industrialization. The emphasis on "chemical" and "artistic" industries, framed as uniquely suited to Korean "genius" and resources while downplaying "mechanical" industry (perhaps influenced by perceived Japanese dominance or colonial limitations), is particularly interesting. The critique of common clothing materials (Gwangmok) highlights issues of national pride, poverty, and the desire for cultural distinction even in everyday life. The call for national awakening and collective effort resonates with the broader independence movement's spirit.

今後의 朝鮮工業
						工友俱樂部議事員 劉斗燦
