If I (余) have performed good deeds (善事), my (予) memory will remain ever new in the world (世間). If I have not done so, even if substituted with memorial statues (紀念像) from the whole world (全世界), what would that be to me (於我에 何有)?
This is the answer (答言) given by a King of Sparta (스팔타) in ancient (古昔) Greece (希臘) to a subject (臣下) who said they would erect a statue (像) for him as he faced his deathbed (臨終). If there are any fraudsters (詐欺漢) in this world (世間) who do not reflect on their own achievements (事功) but vainly scheme (是圖) for the noisy propagation (喧傳) of empty fame (虛名), they should shed cold sweat (寒汗) at these words left by the great figures of old (古偉人).

Besides knowing the one thing (一事) that he himself (스사로) knows nothing (不知), he (彼) knows not a single thing (一事).
These are the humble words (謙語) with which Socrates (소크라테쓰), one of the Four Sages of the World (世界 四聖), evaluated himself (自己). Knowing one's own ignorance (不知) must indeed be great knowledge (大知).<66>

**Summary**

This short piece presents two anecdotes emphasizing humility and the value of substance over superficiality. The first quotes a King of Sparta who believed his legacy depended on his good deeds, not memorial statues. The second quotes Socrates' famous claim that true wisdom lies in recognizing one's own ignorance. Published in 1920 under Japanese rule, these philosophical reflections on true worth versus empty fame could be seen by historians as a subtle commentary relevant to the Korean situation. It might reflect an encouragement towards genuine self-improvement and meaningful action rather than seeking external validation, potentially serving as an indirect form of cultural or intellectual resistance during a period of suppressed national identity.
