기사제목	人類學界의 泰斗 「스타」 博士의 朝鮮觀 (View of Korea by Dr. Starr, a Great Authority in the Field of Anthropology)
기사형태	소식 (News Report)

View of Korea by Dr. Starr, a Great Authority in the Field of Anthropology

Dr. Frederick Starr, an American Doctor of Anthropology, Doctor of Philosophy, and also Doctor of Science, and a professor at the University of Chicago, who visited Korea four times before in 1911, 1913, 1915, and 1917, visited Korea again this year, arriving in Kyŏngsŏng on April 19th and staying at the Chŏndong Hotel. On the morning of the 22nd, when I visited the Professor at the Chŏndong Hotel, he had just finished his breakfast and was arranging his luggage to head for Kaesŏng on the 9:50 AM train departing from Namdaemun. At this time, the reporter briefly stated the purpose of the visit and returned to the same place to see the Professor again on the morning of the 23rd. Although the Professor is now the owner of an advanced age close to sixty, his round face was filled with warmth, and when he repeatedly uttered the affirmative word "Yes, yes," it seemed as though everything in this world submitted under his single word of affirmation. Moreover, his white hair, seeming to deceive silver, barely covered his scalp, appearing whiter than snow, resembling something covered with clear glass, through which all worldly phenomena seemed to reflect, and all the pride the Professor possessed seemed to radiate through it. The Professor began with polite words, saying, "I felt much joy when I visited your Church and your Magazine Company the day before yesterday," and then spoke of his impressions from seeing Korea this time, stating:

"My impressions are numerous, but to mention a few first, the first is the rise of press organizations. When I previously visited Korea, the Korean press organizations consisted only of several newspapers managed by Japanese and American missionaries. I did not see a single press organization managed by Koreans. At that time, the oppression of speech was so severe that Koreans could not manage such things no matter how they tried. However, visiting this time, the prosperity of press organizations is truly astonishing; it is said that magazines alone number over 20 types. As you all know, Japan still does not have sufficient freedom of speech even within Japan itself, so even if press organizations have emerged within Korea, it is推推 (obvious without asking) how narrow the scope of freedom they possess must be. However, those like you who are involved in press organizations, if you do your best within that scope,<66> I sincerely believe and rejoice that the future path for the Korean people will open day by day because of it, and I also hope that fellow Korean brothers and sisters will be prepared to fully receive all the benefits such organizations provide," he said, turning the conversation. "When I met Governor-General Saitō the other day, I heard that Mr. Son, the leader of Ch'ŏndogyo, is unwell. How is his condition?" (Reporter's reply omitted - original text) He continued, "Is that so? I merely heard he was unwell and wondered about his state, but is it such a serious illness? Then I will cancel my visit this time. By the way, I once saw Mr. Son at a certain archery pavilion. I always have a good feeling about Korea's Ch'ŏndogyo. The reasons are: first, the religion has no room for recognizing idols; second, its tenets are pure and rational; and third, its rituals are extremely simple – that is, a single bowl of clear water can substitute for all rituals. Ch'ŏndogyo has not learned from any religion in the world," he said, "it is a flawless religion formed from the clear minds of the Korean people, and also, this religion will not be the least bit inferior when compared to any religion in the world. Therefore, I have avidly read the history and doctrines of this religion, and also, when I visited Korea previously, I put a photograph taken at the former Central Headquarters (scene of a commemorative ceremony) into a magic lantern, and when touring and lecturing in my home country, I always explained the full picture of this religion. And especially, in Korea, which has experienced many twists and turns,<67> the fact that the influence of this religion can steadily progress like this allows one to glimpse that there is something within that religion that enables it to do so, doesn't it?" "Regarding Korea's food, clothing, and shelter, what are their strengths and weaknesses?" "Well, I have thought about various things regarding that, but let's leave it for the article I promised the other day. And other things I felt when meeting Korean brothers and sisters, as I also mentioned when other newspaper reporters visited, are that people below the middle class seem too leisurely in their attitude, which is uninteresting. They need to be filled with more vitality and show a spirit of striving." Regarding "What is the Korean people like from an anthropological perspective?", he said he would also write about it concretely in the next article. (However, no matter how busy I am right now (because that morning there was an appointment to visit Mr. Ch'oe Namsŏn at Kyŏngsŏng Prison), there is one thing I must say based on my feelings from seeing Korea this time:

Before I saw Korea, I first heard through American friends in Korea that Koreans are extremely gentle, even to the point of being devotedly loyal in others' affairs, possessing some points similar to us Americans that are absent in Chinese or Japanese people, and also that eloquence is particularly a genius of the Korean people. However, when I actually met Korean people 10 years ago, what made me feel deeply regretful in my heart was that although Koreans were gentle, possessed different points absent in Chinese or Japanese people, and also had the disposition of natural orators, they seemed unable to fulfill the various missions laid before them, and I had the thought, 'Are these people truly the descendants of those ancestors who built a sacred past?' But please do not misunderstand this statement. At that time, the various unfavorable circumstances Koreans faced likely led us outsiders to such observations. However, coming here this time, today's Korean people are not the Korean people of the past; they are all people filled with new spirit and strength, showing the vigor to capably undertake any duty placed before them. This is not said from a political standpoint; it simply means that today's Korean people have the strength to seize all opportunities that come before them and capably open their own future path. I am deeply gladdened by this and pray for even greater happiness in the present and future of the Korean people." Etc.

[Postscript] The Professor stayed in Kyŏngsŏng for one week, observing the current state of Korea from many aspects, and departed for his home country from Kyŏngsŏng on the 27th. The Professor has specifically arranged to write concretely about everything he saw and felt in Korea this time after returning home, for the readers of this magazine, and send it to our company through his acquaintance in Kyŏngsŏng. We, along with our readers, thank the Professor for his kindness. <68>

**Summary**

This 1921 article reports on an interview with American anthropologist Frederick Starr during his fifth visit to Korea. Starr notes the surprising growth of the Korean press, albeit under Japanese restrictions, and encourages journalists to maximize their efforts within those limits. He expresses admiration for Ch'ŏndogyo's rationality, simplicity, and indigenous origins, mentioning his lectures about it in the US. He observes a perceived lack of dynamism among the lower classes but contrasts this with a newfound vigor and capability he sees in Koreans overall compared to his visits a decade prior, expressing optimism for their future. Starr promises a future article detailing his anthropological views and observations on Korean life.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for Starr's outsider perspective on changes in colonial Korea between the early 1910s and 1921, particularly the emergence of a Korean press following the 1919 March First Movement (part of the so-called 'cultural policy' era). His positive assessment of Ch'ŏndogyo, just two years after its leader Son Pyŏnghŭi was imprisoned for his role in the 1919 movement (which Starr seems aware of, asking about Son's health), is notable. His observation of a change in the Korean people's spirit – from seemingly incapable to possessing "new spirit and strength" – reflects the impact of the nationalist awakening and resistance efforts, even under continued colonial rule. Historians might find Starr's nuanced views on Korean potential, the press, and religion, filtered through an early 20th-century American academic lens, valuable.
