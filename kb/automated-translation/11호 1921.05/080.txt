Discussing Our White Clothing Problem and the Future Dyeing Industry of the Peninsula
						Yu Tu-ch'an

1. Dr. Miyama's Theory of White Clothes Destroying the Nation (白衣亡國論)
Before discussing the main topic, we first introduce a part of Dr. Miyama's theory on white clothes, which stimulated our minds and brought astonishment to our spirits.
The Doctor, currently a vigorous man not yet 50, studied abroad for many years, and upon returning to his country, he was appointed head of the Applied Chemistry Department at the Tokyo Industrial Experiment Station. He became a Doctor of Engineering as a result of pouring all his efforts into research with the determination to artificially manufacture natural lacquer liquid, which is felt to be insufficient throughout the world today. Two years ago, the Doctor was appointed Principal of Kyŏngsŏng Industrial Professional School and Director of the Central Experiment Station, positions he holds to this day. Not many weeks after taking up his post at that time, he specifically cried out the "White Clothes Destroyed the Nation" theory in a certain magazine while commenting concretely on the industrial development of the peninsula, causing us no small amount of wonder.
His cry of the "White Clothes Destroyed the Nation" theory did not merely signify the past. He lamented, considering the future, shouting with pain; the past is the past, not worth mentioning, but in this era where the difficulty of living worsens daily and the struggle for survival intensifies daily, it is lamentable that we, still unawakened, rather revere the white clothes that could be said to have destroyed the nation, and he also listed points hindering various industries. We find his critique quite pertinent and cannot cease offering a handful of gratitude.

2. The Economic State of Awakened Nations
We always hear and know that Paris, France, is the most <58> splendid metropolis in the world and the place that reveres luxury the most. It is heard that one cannot go to the theater or even for a walk in the suburbs without one's wife, and one cannot go without a horse-drawn carriage or automobile. It is also heard that even when going to see something like a theater, they consider only the upper seats as their own. However, according to what a certain gentleman who recently came from Paris conveys, even those who prided themselves on luxury and revered splendor like this, have today reached a point where the affectionate accompanying wife has become a lonely, uninteresting single person, and the horse-drawn carriage and automobile, swift as arrows, have changed to the two legs bestowed by heaven. Their tiered upper seats have become flat lower seats, and the most severe thing is that even though one could watch comfortably in a splendid seat by spending only about 1 chŏn 5 ri more, the ratio of those requesting this is not even 1 in 100 people.
Recently in Germany, it is said they satisfy their needs with paper-based clothing material costing no more than 2 wŏn 50 chŏn per suit of Western clothes. It is not that they do not like beautiful clothes, nor is it because they cannot endure the difficulty of living. Is it not because they think of the future and do so for the sake of the nation? However, our people, who have heard the pertinent "White Clothes Destroyed the Nation" theory, pay not the slightest attention to whether land prices fall, no matter how low, or into whose hands our fields and paddies (田與畓) pass, or how violently grain prices plummet, or whether there will be food in the future or not. They only consider the 1 or 2푼 reduction in the price of wide cotton cloth (廣唐木) as a rare opportunity and marvel only at the falling prices of daily necessities. Is this not truly lamentable?

3. Clothing Reform is a Pressing Issue
Since two years ago, in our narrow peninsula's white-clothed society, responding to the world's great trends and accompanying the tides, there have been numerous theories of reform, and also fervent new movements have arisen like bees; namely, the cries of various gentlemen for lifestyle reform, women's liberation, education, labor, freedom of speech, culture, and what not, truly too numerous to enumerate. However, it is not that we do not hope for these. We are苦待 (earnestly waiting) and fervently hoping day and night for their realization. Yet, more than reforming anything, more than hoping for anything, we say the most urgent thing for us is the reform of clothing, food, and shelter (衣食住), and resolving these three first is our most pressing, urgent task. They talk of women's liberation, cultural movements, and what not, but if we feel a lack in even one of these three – clothing, food, and shelter – at that time, will not all the various things we hoped for and expected entirely return to bubbles?
In the severe cold of winter snow, what if one has no clothes?
In the severe cold of winter snow, what if one has no shelter? <59>
What if one does not eat for even a day?
This is something that always avoids our doubt. Even the sage of high antiquity, who purely advocated morality and was indifferent to material desires, said that only when clothing and food are sufficient does one know propriety. How much more so in today's world where the struggle for survival intensifies daily? Seeing this, we can well infer what kind of relationship the three – clothing, food, and shelter – have in our daily human life, and we who live in this age cannot but research it. In the New Year's issue of this magazine, Mr. Pak Tal-sŏng argued that in guaranteeing our livelihood, in guaranteeing the civility of society, in guaranteeing the dignity of the nation, there is no more urgent issue than solving these three. However, what we hope for is to set aside livelihood, civility, and dignity for now, and reform in a practical, economic, easy-to-understand, and easy-to-implement way. That is, if we are to demand something or reform something, there is no more urgent issue than solving the problem of clothing, food, and shelter first. Suppose our 20 million people entirely revere only culture and advocate only speech and freedom, being indifferent to industry. What will our future be? Or in a family without constant means, if all family members only loudly advocate culture and are indifferent to the family affairs, will that family be able to live a comfortable life in the future or not? Will it become a harmonious family or not? This is a problem worth our deep research and thought. Suppose there is now a great talent whose knowledge is outstanding and personality surpasses others. But if he unfortunately has not obtained freedom in clothing, food, and shelter, will he really be able to go out into society and perform great activities or not? Also, will various societies welcome him or not? He will ultimately just become a pitiful child dreaming of vanity and weeping in agony. Not only this, but if all our 20 million people are likewise indifferent to industry as above, we will also not avoid such circumstances. At that time, who will recognize our existence, and where would be the place to even test culture when our existence is not recognized? When we see the so-called Han scholars today, dwelling idly in thatched huts, seeking only "Confucius said," "Mencius said," and old and young doctrines, even if his moral sense is abundant and his learning is ample, does the thought arise that he will do great things for us or not? Also, does a feeling of sincerely trusting him arise in us or not? Seeing this, we cannot but have some sort of awakening regarding the future now. Currently, the whole society loudly clamors about culture, humanity, and justice, but that seems entirely to be just a temporary slogan, and ultimately, it feels like it will conclude in America's mammonism (黃金萬能主義). I don't know if this statement will hit the mark exactly, but even if not, it won't be far off. <60>

4. Brief Opinion on Reforming Clothing, Food, and Shelter
Among these three – clothing, food, and shelter – we say the most urgent to reform is <60> clothing, and the easiest to reform is also clothing. This is not about reforming the system itself, but just about resolving the white clothing problem.
Generally, the wish to abolish white clothes does not come merely from a sentiment to overthrow white clothes. It is to prevent the pain and sorrow in life that will push upon us like a mountain wave in the future. Regarding this important issue, various esteemed gentlemen of ambition will have lofty opinions and research, but Mr. Pak Tal-sŏng advocated in the New Year's issue of this magazine that it is appropriate to change white to black and change the "tanip" (neckband lining?) and "otkorŭm" (coat strings) to a button system. However, what we hope for is not necessarily to change white to black and the "tanim" (neckband?) and "otkorŭm" to a button system, but to leave the "tanim" as "tanim" and "otkorŭm" as "otkorŭm," and just change only the white clothes to a color suitable to our character. That is, simply put, the clothing system remains as before, and we wish to change only the white color to the color we like best. What difficulty could there be in doing this? We are confident that our clothing system has not the slightest flaw no matter which country or society it is presented in, and hearing the words of general critics, it also seems so. Then, what color should we choose next? This is a problem worth deep research and thought. Should we perhaps choose black, or yellow, or green? Or does our nation perhaps originally have its own inherent color or not? It is said that China has blue (靑藍色) as its people's inherent color, and Japan has floral patterns (華紋色) as its people's inherent color. How could it be that we do not have an inherent color? Just like China's blue, we say red or green is our inherent color. However, regarding choosing a color, we say there is no need to adhere strictly to any formal issue. Speaking of color, this is something where demand constantly changes according to the emotional shifts of the people of the time, so it is not something to be predetermined by setting a standard. We say, whatever color it changes to (excluding white), it is acceptable to wear only colored clothes normally and designate white clothes as formal wear, to be worn only on holidays or memorial days.
If we are to reform clothing, what method would be best to implement it? In this regard, borrowing the power of various organizations and other institutions is one strategy. However, in our opinion, we are convinced that there is nothing faster than borrowing the power of religion. If every member of every church were to abolish white clothes and wear only colored clothes, the clothing reform problem we anticipate and aim for would be achieved imperceptibly. Currently, not only are there many religions and other organizations in our peninsula, but even if we cannot interpret the profit relationship, it is a fixed principle that, like the proverb "he who touches pitch will be defiled" (近墨者黑), it will naturally be put into practice. <61>

5. Regarding the Management of the Dyeing Industry Arising from the Clothing Reform Problem
As a conclusion to the clothing reform problem, I wish to briefly state my humble opinion regarding the future management of the dyeing industry. If we are to reform our clothing, the first prerequisite issue will be the dyeing industry. That is, no matter how much we wish to abolish white clothes and adopt only colored clothes, if there is no suitable product that meets our demands, it is practically impossible. Then, we cannot rely entirely on foreign countries as before; there is no other way but self-production and self-sufficiency (自作自給). However, for self-production and self-sufficiency, the three elements of management, capital, and technology are necessary. Observing our current situation, which of these three elements can be said to be abundant? We think of it as follows: through the efforts of ambitious gentlemen, manage a dyeing industry with a capital of at least 5 million wŏn. On one hand, first produce textiles suitable for our clothing colors, and on the other hand, establish connections with Kyŏngsŏng Textile or Chosŏn Textile Company. Besides this, make special contracts with various large cloth merchants to purchase all the cloth imported from Japan or China at this factory, dye and bleach it, or even process it, so that, like fine cotton cloth (玉洋木), we can buy it for clothing and directly tailor clothes without having to put in the hand labor (手功) of refining, bleaching, or dyeing. Also, regarding sales methods, it goes without saying that special contracts should be concluded with large cloth merchants in various regions. Furthermore, negotiate with various organizations and churches to reform the clothing of their members first. In any case, it lies in our awakening and effort. (End) <62>

**Summary**

This 1921 editorial by Yu Tu-ch'an argues strongly for the abandonment of traditional white clothing (백의) by Koreans in favor of colored attire. Citing economic inefficiency (cost of upkeep), hindrance to industry, and the example of "modernizing" nations like France and Germany adapting to economic realities, the author echoes Dr. Miyama's controversial "White Clothes Destroyed the Nation" theory. Yu advocates for practical reform, suggesting retaining the traditional clothing style but changing the color, keeping white garments only for ceremonial occasions. He stresses that this reform is a prerequisite for national progress, more urgent than debates on culture or politics, and proposes developing a domestic dyeing industry capitalized at 5 million wŏn to achieve self-sufficiency in colored textiles, leveraging existing textile companies and mobilizing social organizations, particularly religious groups, to promote the change. The article reflects the anxieties and debates surrounding modernization, economic survival, and cultural identity in colonial Korea. Historians might find the explicit link between clothing color, national economic health, and perceived backwardness particularly interesting, as well as the reference to a Japanese official's (Dr. Miyama's) critique influencing Korean discourse and the proposed strategy of using religious organizations for social change.

一, 三山博士의 白衣亡國論
吾人은 本題를 論하기 前에 爲先 우리의 頭腦를 刺激케 하고 우리 精神에 驚愕을 招來한 三山博士 白衣論의 一端을 紹介한다.
博士는 至今 50未滿의 壯年으로 多年間 外國에 留學하얏고 歸國하야는 곳 東京工業試驗所 應用化學科長으로 被命되엇다. 그는 今日 全世界에서 不足을 感하는 天然的 漆液을 人造的으로 製造하겟다는 決心을 품고 그 硏究에 全力을 傾注한 結果로 工學博士가 되엇섯다. 博士는 再昨年에 京城工業專門學校長과 中央試驗所長으로 被命되어 今日에 至하얏다. 그는 그 때 赴任 後 幾旬을 不過하야 某 雜誌에 半島 工業發展에 對하야 具體的으로 論評한 中에 特히 白衣亡國論을 絶呌하야 우리로 하야금 적지 아닌 驚異를 품게 하얏도다.
그의 絶呌한 바 白衣亡國論은 非但 過去만 意味한 것이 아니라. 將來를 遠慮하고 痛叫하얏나니 過去는 過去라 말 할 것도 업고 生活難이 日甚하야가고 生存競爭이 日激하야 가는 이 時代에 處하야 尙未覺醒하고 오히려 國家를 滅亡하얏다 할만한 白衣만 崇尙함이 可歎할 바라 하얏스며 또한 諸般産業上에 妨害되는 點을 列擧하얏도다. 吾人은 그의 論評이 자못 至當하다하며 一掬의 感謝를 不巳하는 바이다.

二, 自覺한 國民들의 經濟狀態
우리는 恒常 듯고 알기를 佛國 巴里라 하면 全世界에서＜58＞ 第一 華麗한 都會地이며 第一 奢侈 崇尙하는 대라하야 勿論 劇場 又는 郊外에 散步를 가더라도 同夫人이 아니면 못 갈 줄 알고 馬車 自動車가 아니면 못 갈 줄 안다함도 聞하얏스며 劇場 等에 구경을 가더라도 上席이 아니면 自己들의 坐席이 아닌 줄 안다 함도 들엇섯다. 然이나 近者에 巴里에서 온 某氏의 傳하는 바에 依하면 以上과 가티 奢侈를 自矜하고 華美를 崇尙하던 그들도 今日에 至하야서는 愛情이 津津한 同夫人은 寂寞 無滋味한 單身이 되고 迅速하기 살과 갓던 馬車 自動車는 天與된 兩脚으로 變하얏다 하며 그들의 坐席인 層層한 上席은 平平한 下席이 되고 第一로 甚한 것은 約 1錢5厘만 더 虛費하면 華麗한 坐席에서 便安하게 구경을 하는 것을 此를 要求하는 者가 百人 中에 1人의 比가 못된다 하는도다.
近者 獨逸에서는 價格이 洋服감 1襲에 2圓50錢에 不過하는 紙類衣服감으로서 그들의 要求에 滿足을 得한다 하나니 그들은 美麗한 衣服을 조하하지 안하는 것이 아니라 또는 生活難에 못견디어 그러함도 아니라. 將來를 생각하고 國家를 爲하야 그리함이 아닌가. 然이나 白衣亡國은 當한 白衣亡國論을 들은 우리 民族은 土價야 如何히 低落하던지 우리의 田與畓이 뉘 손으로 들어가던지 는 穀價는 如何이 暴落하던지 將來에는 먹을 것이 잇던지 업던지 此에 對하야는 秋毫만큼도 關念치 아니하고 다만 廣唐木 價格이 1分 2分式 低廉하야 가는 것만 奇貨로 여기며 日用雜貨의 低落하는 것만 神奇히 아는도다. 眞實로 可歎할 바가 안닌가.

三, 衣服改良은 焦眉의 問題
再昨年 以來로 우리 狹小한 半島 白衣社會에는 世界의 大勢에 應하고 風潮에 伴하야 改造論도 許多하얏스며 또한 熱烈한 新運動이 蜂起하얏나니 卽 諸氏의 絶呌한 바 生活改造니 女子解放이니 敎育이니 勞働이니 言論自由니 文化니 무엇이니하야 實로 枚擧키 難하도다. 然이 나 吾人은 此를 希望치 안하는 바가 아니라. 實現되기를 星夜로 苦待하고 熱望하는 바로다. 然而  吾人은 무엇을 改造함보다도 무엇을 希望함보다도 第一 우리에게 緊急한 것은 衣食住 改良이라하며 爲先 此 3者를 解決하는 것이 우리의 焦眉한 急務라 하노라. 女子解放이니 文化運動이니 무엇이니 하지마는 萬若 衣食住 3者 中에 其一이라도 우리에게 不足을 感하는 그 때에는 우리가 希望하고 期待하던 바 諸般일은 全혀 水泡로 歸할 것이 아닌가.
