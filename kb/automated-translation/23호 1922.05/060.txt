발행일	1922년 05월 01일
기사제목	모든 行動을 좀더―무겁게 가지소서, 昨今에 起한 우리 社會의 現象을 보고 (Please Carry Out All Actions More Gravely—Observing the Recent Phenomena of Our Society)
필자	朴達成
기사형태	논설 (Editorial)

Please Carry Out All Actions More Gravely—Observing the Recent Phenomena of Our Society
						By Pak Tal-sŏng (朴達成)

Elders used to tell us these words from early on:
"A person must have gravity (weightiness)."
"A person shouldn't be frivolous," they said.
And when they evaluated people, they would certainly say these words:
"That person has gravity," and
"That person is not shallow," and
"That person should be looked at in multiple layers," they said.
And also,
"No matter how you look at that person, they lack substance," and
"Everything about that person seems spineless," and
"That person altogether doesn't weigh even one *doe* (升) of millet (稷)," they said.
I have always heard these words from my father and mother, and I have also heard them many times from neighborhood elders, school teachers, and leading figures in society. When I heard them, I thought, "What does that mean?" and regarded them lightly, but now that I myself have entered society and personally experienced social phenomena, I indeed find myself thinking anew of the words the elders taught me.
A person with gravity, a person who carries themselves heavily, a person whose actions are heavy—ah, how precious such a person is! A person who thinks once more when facing a matter, a person who becomes even more composed and remains unyielding and unshaken when encountering difficulty—ah, how fine such a person is! I have come to earnestly long for this kind of person. Especially having witnessed firsthand the recent phenomena of our society <113> I think of this even more keenly.

How could the desire to love one's own things not be great, but inevitably I have come to tear at them. How could speaking of one's own faults not be uncomfortable, but inevitably I have come to expose them. Even knowing for sure that spitting (涎) towards the sky will land on one's own face, out of necessity I have come to spit.
Many times, I have placed a mirror (鏡) before me, looked at my own face, and grabbed and pulled at my face with my own hands. Whether ugly or handsome, my face is my face, so how could the desire to love and cherish it not be great, but that broad face, that puffy flesh—no matter how I look, it's not as good as others'. There isn't even a needlepoint's worth of beauty (美妙). Anger flares up, and thinking "This darn face," I grab to pull at it, but then restrain myself, thinking, "Still, it's mine, what can I do?" Then, thinking, "There must still be some well-formed part," I looked closely again at my ears, eyes, nose, and mouth. No matter how I examine them, the basic quality is what it is, and the structure is formed that way, so what can be done? Like the saying, "Trying to remove a wen, one adds another wen," trying to find beauty, I kept discovering ugliness. Bowl-like eyes, a flat nose, a catfish mouth—ah, how disheartened I must have been. Inevitably, I spread my five fingers and scratched and pulled hard. After pulling at it, I just cried and cried. "To pull at my own face with my own hands," I regretted it considerably. I have also hit my own head with my own hands many times. When thoughts should naturally come but my mind is blank and they don't, when something I thought would work turns out not to work in practice, when I absolutely must not forget something but forget it right then, when I rush into something foolishly and face embarrassment (狼狽), when I am shamed (昌皮) by others, when I suffer humiliation from others—at these times, no matter that it's my own head and my own fault, I can't help but grab my head hard with both hands and pound it, thinking, "Damn it, this stupid head—" and thump it repeatedly. After grabbing and pulling for a while, I end up sniffling and crying. To grab my own head with my own hands—where could there be such a perverse (怪惡한) thing? I have also regretted many times, thinking, what would my heart feel if another's hand had touched my head?
I have scolded and hit my own child two or three times. Isn't the heart that loves its own child something that all animals possess? Surely there is no hateful feeling. However, when their appearance is not like others', when their character and intelligence are not like others', and when their actions and conduct are not like others', <114> suddenly, thinking "Damn this child," my hand strikes out. After hitting them, when I see them sniffling and crying, I think, "Ah—it's my fault. Why did I hit them with my own hands?" and regretting it again, I cried along with them.
I have also had such times with my wife, and with my younger siblings. I love them, but there have been times I resented them; there have been times I stroked them and acted kindly, but there have also been times I found fault and struck them. This is probably the case for most everyone.
Why am I saying these seemingly crude (醜率한) and worthless (갑업는) words, like a confession of my own inner affairs (內情)? It is because I intend now to speak of how our brothers' psychology is, how their actions are, and how the public morals (風紀) of our society are, how its phenomena are, that I have come to say these things. I don't know if I might spit on my own face with my own mouth and then shed tears of regret again.

Brothers, how can your psychology be so weak, and how can your actions be so thoughtless? Why are the morals of our society so disordered (紊亂), and why are the phenomena of our society so sordid (醜雜)? It feels like spitting on my own face with my own mouth, but out of necessity, I say this.
First, looking around at child society, their psychology, their actions—one cannot bear it. Setting aside the ugliness (醜雜) of their external appearance, and without mentioning the good or bad of their mischief (作亂) (because speaking of that would attach various other problems), their psychology, their actions are truly astonishing.
Whether two gather or three gather, what they want to do is mainly playing coin-tossing or playing with dirt, and the words coming from their mouths are only the lowest class words, utterly commoner's words. Only perverse (怪惡한) words, full of poison and bristling with thorns, come out. And whether they like someone or not, it's insults (辱); whether someone does well or poorly, it's insults; after making trouble mainly with insults, in the end, they punch with fists, kick with feet, scratch faces, pull hair, until blood flows and faces are broken, wrestling together, not knowing if they'll live or die. Friends try to stop them, but they don't listen; elders try to restrain them, but they don't listen; even when their own parents scold them harshly, they don't budge. We can see such phenomena morning and evening on every street, in every neighborhood.
"Bees (蜂) about to perish sting fiercely," they say. Is this a sign of impending doom? Or a sign of prosperity? Could children's psychology be so full of murderous intent (殺氣 騰騰) that they enjoy fighting? No matter how I look at it, it seems like an inauspicious (祥瑞롭지 못한) matter. <115>
Since the psychology of children, the actions of children are like this, the psychology of adults, the actions of adults are also similar. Having grown up like that since childhood, it becomes a habit, and even as adults, they enjoy fighting.
One evening, I witnessed fighting scenes (求景) five times. I saw a shop owner and a customer fighting at a general store in Chongno (鍾路); I saw laborers fighting with laborers at the entrance to Sadong (寺洞); I saw children fighting with children in front of Kyodong (校洞) Public Ordinary School; I saw farmhands fighting with farmhands at a standing bar in Chin'gol (진골). Every time I saw such scenes, I thought, "Koreans in general must have some connection to fighting. Perhaps Heaven gave them a special command, saying 'Go forth into the world, make a living by fighting, and then return,'" and frowning (눈쌀을 찝흐리며), I thought deeply in various ways.
When they fight, they can't manage big fights, only small ones; they can't fight cleanly, but fight messily. It looks like chickens fighting in spring, or dogs fighting on a muddy day. And it's only fights among their own companions; they can't even engage with others. There is plenty of evidence seen many times that when Japanese people and Korean people fight, the Korean person invariably loses, and against Western people, they can't even conceive of fighting from the start. If, by chance, they happen to confront them, they customarily bow their heads and just turn away, saying, "Yes, sir, I was wrong." Looking at this, they are probably first in being pathetic. "A rooster that was quite spirited (豪氣) in its own house just tucks its wings (쭉지를 끼고) and shuffles around when it goes to someone else's house"—the way those who attack (팩팩 달겨드는) their own companions so fiercely can't even open their eyes wide towards others is truly heartbreaking to see.
Thus, I saw four fights from Chongno to Chin'gol and returned home with these thoughts and worries. As I went around the back of the house to relieve myself, I heard a tumultuous (擾亂한) noise from over the wall, "You bitch, that bitch," and overlapping sounds of furious screaming (發惡), "Kill me, save me." This seemed to be a fight between husband and wife.
Thus, within a short few hours, I saw five fights. These were only the ones that caught my eye; how could there be only five? Perhaps there were five hundred, five thousand. Just within Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul) city in one night.
At first glance, this seems like an ordinary matter, but it has a great impact on that nation, that society. By observing the psychology of that people, the actions of that people, one judges the civilization or barbarity (文野) of that nation, that society.
Here, I will speak frankly (露骨) about a few things without hesitation. <116>
Among those who consider themselves so-called possessors of common sense (常識家), who consider themselves the backbone (中堅) of society, who think they are the only workers in this world, I have seen many whose psychology is utterly perverse (怪惡한) and whose actions are utterly base (卑劣한). That is, among those who fancy themselves speakers or writers, who constantly frequent (無常히) some association or organization and enjoy making noise, I have discovered quite many. Among them, regardless of whether their own insides are completely empty or not, whether they have credibility or not, whether others call them loafers (乾達) or scoundrels (難捧), whether they call them scoundrels fit to be killed or spared, they ignore it all and act extremely smart on their own, act like heroes on their own, act as if only they know anything, sticking out their bellies and swaggering very insolently (건방지게). Their banner (標榜) is so-called humanity, justice, equality, freedom, claiming to work for the people, for the nation, even for the world. However, their inner heart is completely different. A messy desire for fame (釣名心), a grubby desire for profit (取利心) alone fill their guts (膓子). They desperately demand, eyes red, for their worthless name to appear on the third page of the newspaper or on billboards, and many rush in, drooling, to get even a penny or two, even if it's just the price of a bowl of *sŏllŏngtang* or a glass of liquor. (There might be some who do so out of necessity to survive). And among them, there are many who possess unfathomable (叵測한) intentions like a high official's concubine, with jealousy (嫉妬心) and divisive scheming (離間心), wanting to devour others.
If something of another's seems to be going well, they casually think, "Should I go check that place out? Watching for an opportunity, maybe get some money, and if not, maybe stir up trouble (방망이질) and cause its collapse (瓦解)—" and slip in, at first acting very subserviently, sticking out their snout hoping to get a bite. Then, if they see there's no prospect of getting anything, their malice is triggered, and they spread slander (毁謗) all around, incite trouble (衝動질), stir things up (방망이질), shaking and bringing down what others had painstakingly built up to that point.
However, if they see a very solid atmosphere (氣味) where their troublemaking causes no disturbance (搖動) and things continue steadily forward, then they inevitably droop their shoulders slightly and flee (逃兦) over the wall. There, they again look around shiftily, searching for an opportunity to gain something. After searching all around, if they really can't find anything, they wander around aimlessly like madmen through mountains and fields, picking up and eating this and that.
The other day, passing through Kyodong (校洞), I heard such words. One person in Western clothes and one person in Korean clothes were walking together towards Chaedong (齋洞) and said:
Western Clothes Wearer: Hey, they say there's trouble (紛亂) at that place. (Pointing to some building <117>)
Korean Clothes Wearer: Well, looking at the newspaper, it seems so.
Western Clothes Wearer: Should we go into that place, join (加擔) whichever faction, and stir things up (야단을 처볼가)?
Korean Clothes Wearer: What business do we have in someone else's church (敎會)?
Western Clothes Wearer: Is there any business? What does it matter? Anything goes.
When I heard these words, I thought, "What despicable (괘심한) people," and my hands trembled uncontrollably. Look—at this person's psychology. Is it towards evil or good? Are they truly workers or troublemakers (作亂군)? Ah—there are all sorts of strange fellows (怪漢) in the world.
There is a saying, "Fanning the flames (키질) of a burning house." Isn't this person exactly that? Hearing that misfortune (不祥事) has occurred in a great organization of Chosŏn (朝鮮), a gathering of our people, instead of worrying, they act as if a great opportunity has arisen, suggesting joining (加擔) a faction to stir things up. What kind of malicious thought is this?

The cries for freedom and equality became too prevalent (流行), and before we even obtained freedom and equality, we instead came to possess discontent and anxiety. We stated early on that how foreign ideas are digested determines the happiness of that people. Now I ask the brothers, does our level truly possess the strength (實力) to protect (護得) freedom and equality as they are, and do we have the fortitude (根氣) to enjoy (享有) them as they are? Also, have you truly understood that freedom and equality are the correct principles and rules of the universe and human life? Can you not even consider once that lack of freedom and inequality might be its principle? Some say, "It is unfree and unequal because freedom and equality are not given." Where could there be such ignorant (節不知) reckless talk (妄發)? That phrase, "not given"—is it truly not laughable? We shout (呌號) much for political freedom and equality. However, that too has not yet been resolved. Yet, among us, many are now shouting for religious freedom and equality. Freedom and equality—that must be the most important among the issues we desire. However, setting aside (姑捨) the political aspect, isn't religion inherently free and equal from the beginning? Religion, in its faith, in its truth (眞理), is truly free and equal. If one wants to believe, one believes; if one doesn't want to believe, one doesn't believe. That is, joining a religion is free, leaving a religion is free—what is unfree? The heart seeking truth in a life of sincere faith—that is truly freedom and equality. When heart and spirit are harmonious (心和氣和) and mind and body are joyful together (心神이 俱樂), when the family is harmonious and neighbors are friendly (家和隣睦) and brothers are joyful together, that is, loving others as oneself (愛人如己) and viewing the four seas as one (四海一視), achieving the Way and establishing virtue (道成德立) and enjoying Heaven and the world (樂天樂世)—what part of that is unfree or unequal? However, recently among us, many misunderstand this, acting as if freedom in religion, republicanism in religion are new <118> creations, jumping about wildly, and their psychology, their actions have become very troublesome (까다로웁게).

The disturbances that arose in certain churches (敎會) from this spring were surely not caused (基因) by freedom and equality, right? I certainly surmise that they arose from the sincerity of the general believers aiming to further develop and improve those churches, with the system, propagation methods (布敎), and finances (財政) being the main issues. However, quietly observing their words (言辭) and actions, they seem greatly captivated (魅醉한) by the recent theories of so-called freedom and equality.
"You are human, I am human; you are Heaven, I am Heaven," making no distinction between old and young; "You have a say, I have a say; you have a share, I have a share," with rights and balance (均衡) reaching an extreme; even (甚至) to the point of telling one's own father, "You are human, I am human," let's speak and act the same way; and there are signs of intending not to trust (信任치 안케되랴는) even the religious founder (敎祖) whom tens of thousands believed in (信之仰之), revered (師之尊之), and trusted like Heaven for thousands of years. That is to say, Sakyamuni (釋迦) is human, Jesus (耶蘇) is human, the Great Divine Teacher (大神師) is human, so they are not inferior to them in the slightest. (Some people act this way). They are, of course, human. The name "human" is certainly the same. However, just by the name (名辭) "human," one is not a complete human. (Those who thoroughly understand this point are true believers).
Now, such morals (風紀), such phenomena are seen daily in organization after organization, even (甚至) becoming prevalent among elementary school students. It is a fact that this issue arose in a certain elementary school, and it is said that executives in a certain association are greatly troubled (勞心) by this issue. Now,

The Drum Beating Incident (嗚鼓事件) in Buddhism (佛敎) is known to the world. Setting aside (姑捨) the myriad opinions of myriad people, I personally consider it improper. Regardless of who is right or wrong, I say that believers in Buddhism, founded on the great principle of great mercy and great compassion (大慈大悲), would not dare do such a thing. Even if circumstances necessitated it, they would not dare do it under the watchful eyes of all (萬目所視). Especially as religious figures, they cannot do it. This can only be called being Buddhist in name (名色) only, but in fact, violating the will of the World-Honored One (世尊). Responding to evil with evil, responding to good with good—this is not the original principle of the Buddha. The Buddha's principle is to survey the entire universe and view all good and evil, beauty and ugliness, large and small, great and minute within the universe with the same compassion (一視 同慈). Setting aside the Buddha's principle, even as ordinary people in worldly affairs, they would not dare. Due to a momentary conflict of wills, a momentary lack of understanding, momentary emotions, or some contradiction in systems or rituals, what is the point of beating drums and parading (嗚鼓而循) in broad daylight on the main street (白晝四巨里), violating the Buddha's principle, <119> attracting society's attention, and advertising the carelessness of each individual?
I bluntly (唐突) say, gentlemen, your psychology, your actions are reckless acts (妄作), lacking common sense (無常識). Even if the sins of the other side are severe, why should this side commit an even greater sin to attack (攻키) that sin? This is truly the small matter (細音) of adding a wen while trying to remove one.
Gentlemen, just as I pulled at my own face and then shed tears of regret, I know that tears of regret will surely fall for you too.
Matters of the world are definitely not accomplished only by fists or kicks, nor by momentary anger or a single day's hasty action. You often speak of Luther's (루터) Reformation, but religious reform is not that easy. It is harder than anything else, and also a great matter. I believe it is best to act with weightiness, ensuring no violation of the religion's principle, its truth (眞理), adding understanding upon understanding twice and thrice, adding forgiveness upon forgiveness, and considering the dignity (體面) of the church, the dignity of the people, one's own dignity, thinking and thinking again. And

The incident that occurred at a certain Christian church (耶穌敎堂) in Taegu (大邱)—though the world commonly knows of it, where could there be such heartless (沒情) people who would push the back and drag the hands of someone offering prayers (祈禱) to God and seeking blessings (福) for all people, expelling them like a grave sinner right on the spot? Where is the rule that those who daily speak of loving others as oneself (愛人如己), who constantly desire atonement and salvation (贖罪救靈), who desire Heaven and fear Hell (天堂地獄 是願是懼), should, in a moment of agitation (奮激) over a single issue of fame and profit (名利問題), bury their entire lives and even (甚至) defile (汚損) the face of the church?
Setting aside (姑捨) the teachings of Christ, even by the bonds of compatriots (同胞之義), the affection of brothers (兄弟之精), they would not dare. Doing so absolutely does not cause a great matter to fail or succeed. They might receive the reproach (呵責) of their own conscience, but I personally certainly cannot call it proper conduct. You would know if you asked your own conscience, but tears well up even in the eyes of a third party like me, so how much more so for you who were involved? And

Members of Ch'ŏndogyo (天道敎)—I too am one among the believers (信徒) of Ch'ŏndogyo—was that a good thing or a bad thing? Calling themselves the New Faction (新派) or the Old Faction (舊派) (that very character '派' [faction] must be the product of malice from a few individuals), after bowing to, serving, and uniting in heart for 10, 20, 30 years, promising to be together for 50,000 years, how could there be such thoughts of turning against each other (反目) overnight, distrusting, rejecting (排斥), even (甚至) gathering factions (羣黨), <120> engaging in agitation (煽動) to greatly disturb (撓動) the church, and committing sins against their own conscience? (The recent dispute (紛糾) in the Ch'ŏndogyo church was, of course, not the entire church. This can be known by the fact that the dispute did not last two weeks before the clouds scattered and the blue sky appeared (雲捲天靑).)
I will not speak much. I leave it to each individual's conscience and ask for judgment by common sense. As a final piece of advice (忠告), one word is:
Each person should not deceive their own conscience.
Each person should not defile (汚損) their own dignity (體面).
Each person should gauge (料得) their own strength (實力).
Each person should not damage (毁損) their past merits (功績).
Think of the greater cause (大義) and do not act rashly (輕動) out of small emotions.
Next is:
Fully understand (諒解) the past, present, and future of Ch'ŏndogyo.
Know by whose power Ch'ŏndogyo was built and by whose power it will develop.
Certainly know where the considerable prestige (威信) of Ch'ŏndogyo lies.
And finally:
Fully understand the present and future of Ch'ŏndogyo in relation to Chosŏn (朝鮮), and Chosŏn in relation to the world, that is, the entire big picture (大局).
Worldly affairs are definitely not accomplished by momentary emotions or momentary discontent, nor by one person's wave of the hand or one person's reckless talk. Seeing the general trend, seeing the times, seeing the level of the people (民度), progressing step by step (步一步 漸進) is said to be the most appropriate (適實한) method (方便).

Brothers, please carry out all actions with weightiness. When facing matters, think once more—and the more difficult the situation, pass through it with even greater composure (鎭重히). In every word and deed, definitely reconsider and think deeply (再思深量), making your actions befitting a person with gravity (重數). "Water once spilled is hard to gather again," isn't it long known that in the space of one minute, one second, there can be a difference of ten thousand or a thousand *li*? That among our brothers currently, many have destroyed (毁失) a lifetime's accumulated merit (積功) in a single morning or evening is something we keenly lament (哀惜히) for a moment, but how can we guarantee (期必) that such lamentations of regret (哀惜之嘆) will not occur again in the future (압흐로)?
Brothers, I say again and again, become people of weight (重量). To gain gravity (重數), cultivate (修養) yourselves much more—physically, intellectually, morally—and temper (鍊鍛) yourselves much more.
Given that the weight of any object depends on its substance (體質), structure (組織), position (位置), and age (年度), how much more so for the gravity of a person? By seeing, hearing, and learning much more, reaching a weight of thousands and tens of thousands of *jūn* (鈞) that cannot be lifted (千鈞萬鈞 不可擧), freely handling (自由處斷) perilous and smooth, flat and rugged situations like Mount T'ai (泰山) or the great sea (大海), and remaining unshaken even in great winds and sudden storms (大風急兩), that would truly be the happiness of us all. (I have dared to wield my clumsy brush (拙筆). Criticize me as you will.) <121>

**Summary**

The author, Pak Tal-sŏng, laments the lack of "gravity" or seriousness in the actions and thoughts of Koreans in 1922. He contrasts the wisdom of elders valuing composure and depth with the contemporary trend of thoughtlessness, impulsiveness, and internal conflict he observes—from children's vicious quarrels to adults' petty disputes and factionalism. He criticizes the superficial adoption of concepts like "freedom and equality" without true understanding or societal readiness, pointing to recent disruptive incidents within major religious organizations like Buddhism (the "Drum Beating Incident"), Christianity (an expulsion in Taegu), and Ch'ŏndogyo (New vs. Old faction strife). Pak urges his "brothers" (fellow Koreans) towards deeper self-reflection, self-cultivation (physical, intellectual, moral), and measured, thoughtful action based on understanding the larger context (大局) and historical trajectory, arguing that true progress comes from gradual, deliberate steps, not rash impulses. Historically, this article is interesting for its candid critique of Korean social behavior and anxieties during the colonial period. It reveals internal conflicts and factionalism within significant nationalist and religious groups (Buddhism, Christianity, Ch'ŏndogyo), reflecting the pressures and challenges of modernization, ideological importation (like 'freedom and equality'), and societal cohesion under Japanese rule. Historians might find the specific examples of internal strife and the author's plea for 'gravity' and self-cultivation particularly revealing about the intellectual and moral concerns preoccupying some Korean elites in the early 1920s.

기사제목	모든 行動을 좀더―무겁게 가지소서, 昨今에 起한 우리 社會의 現象을 보고
필자	朴達成
기사형태	논설

모든 行動을 좀더―무겁게 가지소서, 昨今에 起한 우리 社會의 現象을 보고
						朴達成
어룬들은 일즉 우리에게 이러한 말슴을 일러 주엇습니다.
「사람은 重數(무게)가 잇서야 쓴다」
「사람은 촐랑촐랑 하여서는 못 쓴다」라고.
그리고 그들은 사람을 評할 때 발듯이 이러한 말슴을 하얏습니다.
「그 사람은 重數가 잇는 사람이라」하며
「그 사람은 홋 사람이 아니라」하며
「그 사람은 여러 겹으로 볼 사람이라」하얏습니다.
그리고 또
「그 사람은 아무리 보아도 씨가 안든 사람이라」하며
「그 사람은 모든 것이 뼈가 업서 보인다」하며
「그 사람은 도대체 피(稷) 한 되 (升)무게가 아니된다」라고 말슴하얏슴니다.
나는 이 말슴을 나의 아버지 어머님께도 늘―들어 왓스며 이웃 어룬 學校先生님 社會의 先進들도 여러 次例 들어 왓습니다. 들을 때는 「저것이 무슨 소리인가?」하고 尋常히 여겻더니 이제와서 이 個身이 社會에 나서 보고 社會의 現象을 親히 격고 보니 果然 어룬들의 일러준 말슴이 다시곰 생각키웁니다.
重數가 잇는 사람, 몸을 무겁게 가지는 사람, 行動을 무겁게 하는 사람 아-얼마나 귀여운 사람입니까. 事에 臨하야 한번더-생각하는 사람 難을 當하야 一層 더―沈着하야 不撓不動하는 사람 아-얼마나 조흔 사람입니까. 나는 이 사람을 切實히 그립게 되엇습니다. 더구나 昨今에 對한 우리 社會의 現象을 實見한데서＜113＞ 더― 懇曲히 생각키웁니다.

제 것을 사랑하고 십흔 마음이야 如干하겠습니까마는 할 수 업시 물어 뜻게 되엇습니다. 제 허물을 말하기야 如干 거북하겟습니까마는 할 수 업시 들어내게 되엇습니다. 한울을 보고 침(涎)을 배트면 제 얼굴에 떨어지는 줄 確實히 알면서도 不得已 하야 침을 뱃게 되엇습니다.
나는 거울(鏡)을 압헤 노코 내 얼굴을 내가 보며 내 얼굴을 내 손으로 잡아 뜨든 적이 여러번입니다. 못생겻거나 잘생겻거나 내 얼굴은 내 얼굴이니 사랑하고 애끼고 십흔 마음이야 如干하랴마는 그―넙적한 얼굴 두실두실한 살빗 아무리 보아도 남만 갓지 못합니다. 美妙란 바늘끗 만큼도 가진 데가 업습니다. 火症이 벌컥 나서 「이놈의 얼굴하고 」잡아 뜨드려다가 「그래도 내것이니 어찌하랴」하야 抑制를 하야 노코 「그래도 좀 잘생긴 곳이 잇스려니」하고 귀와 눈 코와 입을 다시금 有心히 보앗습니다. 아무리 살핀들 素質이 그러하고 組織이 그러케된 바에 어찌합니까. 「혹을 떼려다가 혹을 부친다」는 말과 가티 美를 차즈려다가 醜를 자꾸 發見하게 되엇습니다. 눈은 사발눈 코는 平코 입은 메기입 아―얼마나 落心되엇겟습니까. 할수업시 다섯 손가락을 벌이어 박박 잡아 뜻게 되엇습니다. 잡아 뜻고는 그만 왕왕 울엇습니다. 내 얼굴을 「내 손으로 잡아 뜻다니」하고 後悔도 적지 아니 하얏습니다. 나는 또 내 머리를 내 손으로 따린 적이 여러 번입니다. 依例히 생각이 날 것인데 캄캄하야 생각이 아니 날때, 그러케 하면 되려니 한 것이 實地에 나아가서는 그러케 아니 되는 때, 꼭 이저버리지를 안하야 되겟는데 今時 곳 이저버리게 되는 때 잘못 덤비다가 狼狽를 當할 때, 남에게 昌皮를 當한 때, 남에게 屈辱을 當한 때, 이때는 아무리 내 머리요 내 잘못이지만 어쩔 수 업시 두손으로 머리를 박박 잡아 뜨드며 「에익기 이놈의 머리―」하고 콕콕 박아내입니다. 한참 잡아 뜻고 나서는 그만 쿨적쿨적 울게됩니다. 내 머리를 내 손으로 잡아 뜻다니―이런 怪惡한 일이 어대 잇슬가. 만약―남의 손이 내 머리에 다앗던들 내 마음이 어떠하랴 하야 다시금 後悔한 적도 만핫습니다.
나는 나의 子息을 辱하고 따린 적이 두세 번입니다. 제 子息을 사랑하는 마음이야 動物치고는 다―잇는 것 아닙니까. 決코 미운 마음은 업겟지요. 그러나 그―생김생김이 남만 갓지 못하고 그―性品 그―知力이 남만 갓지 못하고 그―行動 그―處身이 남만 갓지 못한데서＜114＞ 어느덧 「에익 이 子息하고」 손길이 언뜻합니다. 따리고나서 그가 훌적훌적 우는 것을 볼 는 그만 「아―내가 잘못이다. 저것을 왜 내손으로 때렷는가-」하고 다시곰 後悔를 하면서 나도 딸아 울엇습니다.
나는 나의 안해에게도 이러한 적이 잇섯고 나의 동생에게도 이러한 적이 잇섯습니다. 사랑하기는 사랑하지만 미워한 적도 잇고 쓸어주고 고맙게 군 적도 잇지만 훙보고 따려준 적도 잇섯습니다. 이것은 누구던지 大槪는 다―그러하겟지요.
나는 이 醜率한 듯하고 別로 갑업는 말을 即 自己 內情의 自白가튼 말을 왜 하는고? 하니 이제 兄弟들의 心理如何 行動如何를 말하려 하며 우리 社會의 風紀如何 現象如何를 말하려  함에서 이런 말을 하게 되엇습니다. 제 손으로 제 얼굴을 뜻고 또 後悔의 눈물이나 아니흘릴는지 모르겟습니다.

兄弟시어 어찌면 그리 心理가 薄弱하며 어찌면 그리 行動이 輕率합니까. 우리 社會의 風紀는 왜 이러케 紊亂하며 우리 社會의 現象은 왜 이리 醜雜합니까. 제 얼굴에 제 입으로 침을 배앗는 것 갓습니다마는 不得已하야 이 말을 하게 됩니다.
爲先 兒童社會를 둘러 본다 하면 그 心理 그 行動―참을내야 참을 수 업습니다. 그―外形의 醜雜은 그만두고 그―作亂의 善惡도 말할 것 업고(그것을 말하랴면 또 여러가지 問題가 부트니까) 그―心理 그―行動이야말로 果然 놀낼만 합니다.
둘이 모엿거나 셋이 모엿거나 그들의 하고저 하는 것은 돈치기 흙작난하기가 爲主이며 그들의 입에서 나오는 말은 極下等의 말 아조 常놈의 말 뿐입니다. 毒이 차고 가시가 다닥다닥 부튼 怪惡한 말뿐 나옵니다. 그리고 고와도 辱 미워도 辱, 잘해도 辱 못해도 辱 長―辱으로 作亂하다가 結局은 주먹으로 찌르며 발길로 차며 얼굴을 허비며 머리털을 뽑으며 피가 나게 얼굴이 깨어지게 막 어울러 죽을지 살지 모르게 싸웁니다. 동무가 말려도 아니 듯고 어룬이 制止해도 아니 듯고 제―어미아비가 야단을 처도 꼼작도 아니합니다. 이런 現象은 우리가 아츰저녁으로 巨里巨里 洞里洞里에서 볼수 잇습니다.
「망하려는 벌(蜂)이 잘 쏜다」고 이것이 亡하려는 徵兆입니까. 興하려는 徵兆입니까. 兒童들의 心理가 그러케도 殺氣가 騰騰하야 싸우기를 조하할가요? 아무리 보아도 祥瑞롭지 못한 일이라 합니다.＜115＞
兒童의 心理 兒童의 行動이 이러한지라 어룬의 心理 어룬의 行動도 그와 비슷합니다. 兒童때부터 그러케 자랏는지라 그것이 習性이 되어 어룬이 되어서도 싸우기를 조하합니다.
나는 하로 저녁에 싸우는 求景을 다섯 번이나 한 적이 잇습니다. 鍾路 어떤 雜貨店에서 店主와 顧客이 싸우는 것을 보앗고 寺洞於口에서 勞働者와 勞働者가 싸우는 것을 보앗고 校洞公立普通學敎 압헤서 兒童과 兒童이 싸우는 것을 보앗고 진골 어떤 선술집에서 머슴과 머슴이 싸우는 것을 보앗습니다. 그러한 모든 光景을 볼 적마다 大體 朝鮮사람이란 싸움과 무슨 因緣이 잇는게다 아마 한울이 너의들은 世上에 나아가 싸움이나 해먹다가 돌아오너라 하고 特命을 부텨 준 모양이다 햐야 눈쌀을 찝흐리며 여러가지로 생각을 만히 하얏습니다.
싸우되 큰 싸움은 못하고 적은 싸움뿐이며 快하게 못 싸우고 너저분하게 싸웁니다. 봄날 닭의 싸움갓기도 하고 진날 개싸움 갓기도 합니다. 그리고 제 동무끼리만 싸우는 싸움이오 남과는 어울리지도 못합니다. 日本 사람과 朝鮮 사람과 싸우면 朝鮮 사람이 番番이 지게됨은 여러번 본 証驗이 잇거니와 西洋 사람과는 當初부터 싸워볼 生意도 못합니다. 或 境遇에 그들과 맛서게 되는 때면 依例이 머리를 숙여 「네 영감상 잘못햇습니다」하고 돌아 설 뿐입니다. 이것을 보면 못나기로는 아마 第一일 것입니다. 「제 집에서는 꽤 豪氣잇든 숫닭이 남의 집에 가면 그만 쭉지를 끼고 비슬비슬한다」고 제 동무끼리는 고러케 팩팩 달겨드는 것들이 남을 對해서는 눈도 크게 못뜨게 됨이야말로 속이 傷해 못보겟습니다.
나는 이러케 鍾路서 진골까지에 네 번 싸움을 보고 이러한 생각 이러한 걱정으로 집에를 돌아왓습니다. 小便을 보려고 집 뒤를 돌아가노라니 울넘어로 「이년아 저년아」하는 擾亂한 소리가 들리며 「죽여라 살려라」하는 發惡의 소리가 겹쳐나옴을 들엇습니다. 이것은 아마 夫婦間의 싸움인 듯 합니다.
나는 이러케 短 한 몃 시간 내에서 다섯 번 싸움을 보앗습니다. 내 눈에 띄운 것이 이것 뿐이지 어대 다섯 번뿐이겟습니까. 아마 五百番 五千番은 되리이다. 一夜間 京城市內로만 하야도
이것이 얼핏보면 尋常한 말 갓습니다마는 이것이 그 國家 그 社會에 큰 影響이 밋는 것입니다. 그 民族의 心理 그 民族의 行動을 보아 곳 그 國家 그 社會의 文野를 判斷하는 것입니다-.
나는 이에 서슴거리지 안코 露骨로 몃가지 말을 하겟습니다.＜116＞
所謂 常識家로 自處하고 社會의 中堅으로 自處하고 이 世上의 일군이란 저 하나 뿐이라는 이들 中에 心理가 아조 怪惡한 이―마흐며 行動이 아조 卑劣한 者가 만흠을 보앗습니다. 即 말마디나 한다 하며 글씨줄이나 쓴다 하야 어떤 會 어떤 團體에 無常히 出入하며 떠들기 조하하는 이 中에 퍽 만흠을 發見하얏습니다. 그들 中에는 自己의 속이 탱탱 비엿거나 말앗거나 自己의 信用이 잇거나 업거나 남이 自己를 乾達이라거나 難捧이라거나 죽일 놈이라거나 살릴 놈이라거나 不計하고 自己 딴은 아조 똑똑한 체하며 自己 딴은 아조 英雄인 체하며 自己뿐 아는 체하야 배때기를 내밀고 아조 건방지게 꺼떡대는 이가 만습니다. 그 標榜은 所謂 人道니 正義니 平等이니 自由이니 하야 民族을 爲하며 國家를 爲하며 世界까지 爲한다 합니다. 그러나 그 內心은 아조 딴판입니다. 너저분한 釣名心 구지레한 取利心 뿐이 膓子에 가득찻습니다. 그 못난 이름일 망정 新聞三面이나 廣告板에 오르기를 눈이 벌거케 要求하며 한푼두푼 비록 설렁湯 한 그릇 술 한 잔 갑이라도 어드려고 침을 생켜가며 덤비는 이가 만습니다.(먹고 살랴니까 할 수 업서 그리는 이도 잇겟지만) 그리고 그들 中에도 大監의 妾년 가티 嫉妬心 離間心으로 남을 잡아먹겠다는 叵測한 心思까지 가진 이가 만습니다.
남의 무엇이 좀 잘된다 하면 얼픗이 생각하기를 「그놈의 데나 좀 가볼가. 機會 보아가며 돈푼이나 어더먹고 그러치 안흐면 방망이질이나 하야 瓦解나 시키지―」하고 슬적 들어가 처음은 아조 굽실굽실하며 한푼 먹을가하고 주둥이를 내어 두릅니다. 그리다가 못먹을 形便만 보이면 그만 惡心이 發動하야 두루 毁謗을 노흐며 衝動질을 하며 방망이질을 하야 남의 모처럼 그만큼 맨들어 노흔 것을 뒤흔들어 떨어 칩니다.
그러나 自己의 방망이에 搖動이 업시 그냥 꾸준히 잘 나아가는 아조 튼튼한 氣味만 보면 그제는 할 수 업서 슬그먼이 억개를 늘이고 울 넘어로 逃兦갑니다. 거긔서 또 눈을 휘휘 내두루며 먹을 구멍을 찻습니다. 두루 찻다가 정―할 수 업스면 미친 사람모양으로 산으로 들로 막 돌아 단이며 이것 저것을 막 주어먹습니다.
나는 日前 校洞을 지나다가 이런 말을 들엇습니다. 어떤 洋服입은 者 하나와 어떤 韓服입은 者 하나가 서로 어우러져 齋洞께를 向하야 가며 하는 말이
洋服者. 여보게 이놈의 데 紛亂이 낫다지.(어떤 집을＜117＞ 가리키면서)
