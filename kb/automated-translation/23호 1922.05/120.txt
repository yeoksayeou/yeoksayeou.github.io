New Year's Eve -
						Yŏm Sangsŏp
Six
Wasn't it around the end of July, when it felt intensely hot, that we finished our nearly three-week trip to T'okyo and started a life, calling it a family, in a corner of Taegu?

The branch manager's chair awaiting its new master, the newly decorated company house ready to welcome the newly married young couple—settling in there, forgetting all the bitter and dark memories and old wounds of the past, planning to build a new life with ten times, a hundred times the courage—your future must have seemed boundless, a dream shining with light and hope. Moreover, seeing in your arms what you had only looked upon as a pie in the sky at church, longed for and yearned for, and finding her unexpectedly chaste and gentle, you must have felt you could only thank God. But how could you, not being God, have known what that chastity, that gentleness, that obedience signified?

Even when we went to T'okyo, didn't you ask me about everything that caught my eye, down to even a single picture postcard, "Don't you want to buy it? Don't you want to have it?" and buy them for me one by one? You even ordered a piano, paying a deposit of 1,000 wŏn, which might have been considered excessive for your status. But didn't I always firmly refuse, as if I had no desire for anything I saw? Yet, I was an ordinary woman. Perhaps I was a woman with stronger vanity than the average woman. How could I dislike such beautiful things? But in that situation, I had no choice but to refuse. Seeing this, you, on the one hand, might have felt displeased, thinking I either didn't trust your financial ability or was too discerning, but you also seemed to find it commendable and even more lovable. However, <21> my refusal was not because I was too discerning, nor was it out of consideration and sympathy for your financial means. It was because the more I received such kindness, the more it felt like exposing my own sin, and I feared that your disillusionment and sorrow would become even more intense.

Nevertheless, you, while not buying even a decent necktie or undershirt for yourself, bought things for me without regard for cost, even things that seemed irrelevant, as soon as they caught your eye.

When I stopped you in a solemn tone, saying, "Please stop. That's too much," you seemed to take that lovely sentiment, unseen in modern women who are embodiments of vanity, as more precious than the words themselves, feeling grateful yet sorry, and ordered two or three items when you might have bought just one. To be honest, rather than satisfying me with special items suited to a lover's taste, you were moved by the beauty and goodness of my feelings revealed through those material things, and you relished that. But for me, it was an unbearable, heavy burden. Sometimes at night, when you suggested going to the Imperial Theater or an opera, if I refused and just lay down, you seemed to think even that was a womanly virtue. But the more it happened, the more your kindness felt like needles pricking my heart, and there were times when I blushed alone or felt like crying. Yet, even those aspects appeared to your eyes only as the attitude of a pure and innocent maiden.

Because of this, when Mr. P came to visit and spoke with an overly familiar attitude, or when, on the day before leaving T'okyo, we went out together saying we were visiting former colleagues' houses as a farewell, you, unaware of the complex and subtle intricacies between the sexes, either didn't notice or perhaps trusted my character; in any case, you permitted it without any suspicion.

On that day, when I returned an hour later than the promised time, you, having waited anxiously, seemed just relieved that I had returned safely and hugged me so tightly it felt like my body would break. You, experiencing for the first time the joy of meeting a lover after anxious waiting, must have surely tasted the highest happiness then. But for you, it was obtained by paying a price higher <22> than that.

Ah, this wicked woman who sold herself (鬻女), whom even death cannot purify! The more I regret, the more I try to be prudent and cautious, telling myself I cannot trust myself, the stronger the power of temptation sings its victory song. Ah, it's fearsome. It's audacious... Ah, God forbid!

However, the first time in my entire life that I experienced truly human emotions was then. Truly, the only time I regretted was then. And the only time my throat choked up, unable to speak, trembling with the passion to love you, even for just one minute, was then. Perhaps it was the first and last time in my whole life. I wanted to cling to your chest and weep. I wanted to confess everything nakedly and then beg you to kill me. In the end, your pure love had indeed subdued the demon that had dug a deep nest in my heart. But reason whispered that confessing right here and now would save no one. Truly, regret was only for that moment. Instead, embraced by your warmth for a month after marriage, even the sprouts of conscience that were beginning to awaken withered away at the roots.

Even on the way back to Korea, you were overjoyed, almost ready to devour me, seeing me gradually become cheerful, chatting, and playing around with teasing, childlike remarks. But you never dreamed that it was the worsening conversation of a cunning person.

And so, somehow, we arrived safely at Taegu Station. Meeting my mother, who had arrived the day before, at the station platform, fear preceded any feeling of gladness. From that day, I became the mistress of a new household. The house wasn't particularly large, but there was enough room for each of us to have a separate study; there was no inconvenience. For a newlywed setup, it could even be called too splendid. Even a young maid brought specially from Sŏul for me was there; preparations for everything were so complete that we just needed to move in. But how could you, not being God, know that it was merely a dream lasting only two weeks? Isn't it natural that a home built on falsehood, no matter how magnificent, is worth less than a mirage <23> built on sand?

After about five or six days, Mother left. In truth, she wanted to stay longer, but I hurried her to leave as soon as possible. The week after that! That too was a savory time. But what happened after that? What cannot be resisted in the slightest is the force of life. Despite worrying so much and taking every possible measure and precaution for nearly a month, your suspicious eyes soon became fixed on my body. Sometimes you would stare at me blankly as if lost in thought, then suddenly ask,

"Since when did you know P?"

When you asked like that, it wasn't that my heart didn't jump, but haven't there been times when I mumbled something dismissively, as if it were a slip of the tongue? You seemed to connect the abnormality of my body with P. Moreover, the fact that I hadn't allowed you to touch my body for any reason, even though it had been about a month since our marriage, seemed to have increased your suspicion recently. But you, being composed, only watched for signs and never uttered a single word about it. I, too, had prepared myself for some storm eventually, but I didn't want to bring it up myself before you said something first. Now, rather than feeling anxious or afraid, I thought it would be refreshing for both of us to get it over with quickly, clear everything away, and start anew with a fresh mood and spirit. Indeed, I realized I had become surprisingly calm and bold. However, I never once thought I would be kicked out.

"There will be some turbulence. You'll be heartbroken and perhaps disheartened for a time. And I'll surely face shame before the servants and various people for a while. But if we keep it secret, maybe it can be managed somehow... I could go to Sŏul and come back, replace all the servants... say it happened after returning from Sŏul, entrust it somewhere, and there won't be much noise. If we do that, surely you wouldn't be able to kick me out... If it really comes to the worst, didn't I say from the beginning I couldn't do it? Forcing it unreasonably like that, and now trying to abandon me just because this happened..." <24>

Although not entirely reassured, I trusted your character optimistically, on the one hand. I hoped for a kind of miracle. The thought of resorting to scientific means (likely meaning abortion) did cross my mind, but I immediately rejected it as even more difficult than before marriage. Even before the wedding, I had considered it, but there was no suitable person to consult, and ultimately, it would only benefit the newspapermen (新聞匠이), besides costing 10 or 100 wŏn, which was utterly impossible given my circumstances back then. Moreover, here in this completely unfamiliar place, I couldn't even dare to attempt such a rash act.

Not only that, but even if I were to lose your protection, it wasn't a dead end. Meeting Mr. P in T'okyo had given me a final point of hope. Mr. P, still unaware of my relationship with Mr. E, after hearing the whole story, promised, "If you just make up your mind firmly and go through the proper divorce proceedings, I'll make sure you have nothing to worry about." Mr. P himself was struggling to get a divorce and was one of those young men of today who, even after graduation, lingered under the pretext of further research and didn't return home. But that promise was made so I could prepare a backup plan; it goes without saying that there was no sincerity or uncontrollable passion involved. Still, I showed as much enthusiasm as possible and satisfied all his demands.

However, the strange thing was that recently, feelings for you suddenly began to sprout in my heart. As your attitude grew colder, the desire to be close to you became increasingly earnest, even as I tried to hide my body from your sight. There were times when I felt that if I could just run into your study right now, embrace you, and cry to my heart's content, I would feel relieved. I would go as far as the doorway, lingering there aimlessly. Then again, I would think of confessing everything in writing and just running away somewhere, but that too was not an easy task.

But the biggest headache was the eyes of Suni and the inside servant woman (안잠재기). Not only was it suspicious for a newly married young couple <25> not to sleep together, but they also seemed to find my recent physical state questionable. Occasionally, Suni or the servant woman would ask,

"Why has the master been cooped up in his study lately?" or, with a meaningful smile,

"Since when have you known the master? Did you study together in Japan?" Whenever they asked, I felt fortunate and told them we got married two years after our engagement, or that he was ill.

"You, look when you clean the master's room! It's full of medicine bottles (藥甁), isn't it?... He looks demure, but behind the scenes, it's quite a mess. It's an illness he got while attending school, and it acts up like this every summer. Hahaha, he can't even go to the hospital and stays alone in this heat with the door tightly shut... hahaha."

When I told them this, young Suni, not fully understanding the meaning, would ask with wide eyes,

"What kind of illness is it that he can't go to the hospital?" But the middle-aged servant woman, though raised in the countryside, already seemed to guess and would just smile.

However, a gloomy atmosphere soon began to permeate the stuffy heat inside the house. In the household where five or six people bustled about, apart from the clatter of dishes being washed in the kitchen morning and evening and the sound of sweeping, it was dead silent, not even a loud breath could be heard. The gloomy pantomime, with no end in sight, dragged on like the summer heat. This abnormal state had already lasted two weeks, and August slipped by. My body, now over five months along, could no longer be hidden even under two or three layers of ramie clothes; it was protruding and heavy. My nerves were quite frayed. My mind had no more capacity for further deliberation.

"Let it be what it will be!" I could only spend the long days rolling around in my room. You too, seemingly unaffected, went back and forth between your study and the office at the appointed times each day as before, but the traces of agony clearly etched on your face only grew darker day by day. It seemed you were almost in a state of collapse, unsure of what measures to take. But the time was ripe. The program was all set.

September 15th! For you too, it must be an unforgettable day in your life. It was the day that swallowed <26> the 75 days of married life in one gulp. At the same time, for you, a young man of 25 like a budding leaf, it was a gracious day when you laid down the heavy burden you couldn't bear and took a deep breath.

- It must have been around midnight. That day, even as night fell, there wasn't a breath of wind; it was humidly hot, the last sultry day. Since you hadn't returned even after 11 o'clock struck, I told Suni to go to bed, and I also went under the mosquito net and lay down. You never went to restaurants (料理집), even when entertaining guests, preferring to buy food instead. Wondering why you were so late today, I began to doze off. But perhaps five or six minutes later, startled by a thudding sound at the edge of the wooden floor (maru), I got up and looked out. You had entered sometime unnoticed and were sitting on the edge of the maru, still wearing your shoes (구쓰), head bowed deeply as if out of breath. Seeing you, I couldn't help but be shocked. I woke Suni and sent her out, following behind her, but I didn't have the courage to speak. You, who would turn redder than wine even after a single glass of medicinal liquor (藥酒) or perhaps just wine (葡萄酒), must have drunk so much somewhere that day that you seemed unable to collect yourself, sitting there with eyes closed for some time. Seeing you then, I genuinely couldn't overcome my pity. But on the other hand, the fact that you had broken your firm faith and achieved a worldly release felt like finding a path to survival (活路) for me, and it also seemed that the opportunity I had been waiting for had finally arrived tonight, so I felt somewhat relieved.

You sat like that for about 20 minutes. Perhaps feeling sorry for Suni's pleading (哀乞), you suddenly stood up. I thought you would go straight into your study, but instead, you came into the main room (anbang) and plopped down in the warmest spot (알엣목) that Suni had busily cleared. Seeing you still sitting with your head bowed, I hid in the study. But when Suni came to call me, although I had anticipated it, my heart couldn't help but pound.

"What should I do? If he brings up the issue, what should I say? It's best to seize this opportunity and pour everything out frankly, but how should I frame my words?..."

Thoughts swirled in my head like a whirlwind, but I couldn't quite make up my mind as I stepped onto the threshold of the main room <27>. You remained seated as if petrified, just letting out heavy, pained breaths. However, when I entered and sat facing the front window, you finally lifted your head, told Suni to go sleep in the opposite room, and then, once again, sat with your head bowed for some time, didn't you?

A terrifying silence pressed down on my head. One minute, two minutes, five minutes, six minutes... Only the ticking sound of the clock above my head kept time with your difficult, sighing breaths. Ten minutes had already passed. Twelve o'clock struck loudly. But the death-like silence continued again. My mind gradually calmed, but the feeling of oppression grew increasingly severe. Unable to bear it any longer, I opened my mouth.

"You should sleep now."

"..."

"Shall I make your bed here?"

But you remained seated with your eyes closed as before. Perhaps overcome by drunkenness, your brow only furrowed more and more. I too bowed my head and sat. Another two or three minutes passed. My chest felt prickly, like prodding an old sore, but on the other hand, I realized a feeling of self-abandonment (自抛心) – 'let it be what it will be!' – was arising, and at the same time, my heart felt lighter. However, in an instant (瞥眼間애), you lifted your head sharply,

"Hmph, sleep?... Maybe now is really the time to sleep."

You uttered this one phrase as if talking to yourself and looked straight at me. I, too, inadvertently lifted my head, but I had no choice but to lower it again to avoid meeting your gaze. Yet, I sensed your fiery gaze piercing the eternal mysterious thing (謎物) stirring in my belly, shooting towards me. The heavy silence continued again. But could a dense, unmoving black cloud disperse without a single downpour? The stifling air in the room, hot like the inside of a boiling cauldron, wrung out sweat until the blood ran dry. ... Finally, <28> the dreadful silence was broken, irretrievably. -

"Everything is my fate (運數). Born with such destiny, what can be done?... There's no need to say anything. And no need to hear anything either... Leave tomorrow! That's all I can do with my own strength. Think about it so it's best for both of us. I don't know what you'll do once you go to Sŏul... It might all be my fault, my lack of discernment, but I cannot sacrifice my honor (名譽) beyond this point, so you must consider that deeply."

Whenever your voice choked up, you cleared your throat with a loud cough, barely finishing your words. Then you bowed your head again, seemingly sobbing (흙흙 늑기시는), but I could only sit there looking down at my knees. My mind was in chaos, like a water wheel with broken limbs, all thoughts seeming to stop at once. Yet, the thought, "He's really not drunk on medicinal liquor," repeated itself several times. The idea that throwing myself down, clinging, crying, and pleading (號訴) would bring relief, that all problems would be washed away, now seemed merely a fantasy.

Even as I sat watching, with sidelong glances, the teardrops like bile of anguish (懊惱) fall onto the knees of my white Western trousers (洋服 바지) and soak in instantly, my own eyes remained dry. It wasn't that my tear ducts (淚線) were blocked; they had dried up completely at the roots (송도리ㅅ재). Or perhaps it would be more accurate to say my soul (靈魂) had become parched. Having lost its elasticity and moisture, my soul was so dry it felt like it would crumble into dust if touched.

"I'm not saying this because I'm drunk. It's a decision I barely reached after agonizing for a month, so don't take it lightly (凡然히). Prepare to leave on the night train (밤차) tomorrow," you said, then abruptly went into the study. Even after you left, I sat blankly in the same spot, unable to think of anything.

Even after Suni came over, made the bed again, and left, I still sat in the warm spot (알엣목), but no thoughts came to mind. Besides a self-abandoning, somewhat absurd sense of defiance – the idea of staying up all night like this – I couldn't discern whether I wanted to laugh or cry; I couldn't fathom my own heart. The thought that once this night passed, I would have to leave this house forever <29> didn't even surface in my mind. And yet, the thought occurred that even if Suni and the others in the next room sensed the current situation (光景), they, believing my pregnancy resulted from relations (交接) with you before marriage, would likely interpret it as a jealousy quarrel between a young couple. I chuckled inwardly at this. While my mind seemed to have some leeway, an inexplicable anxiety arose, making me lie down and sit up repeatedly. I simply couldn't think of what measures to take next.

When the electric light (電燈 불) went out and the birds began to chirp, I managed to close my eyes for a moment. After sleeping lightly for two or three hours, I woke with a start; the sound of rainwater dripping from the eaves sounded desolate (凄凉히). It had been stuffy all day yesterday, and finally, it had turned to rain. The distant clattering sounds from the kitchen, footsteps rushing back and forth, mumbled talk about needing to polish shoes, calling a rickshaw puller (人力車軍)... after a period of bustle, the house became quiet again (從容하야젓습니다). Lying still in the dim room, I felt as if I were seriously ill (重病), making me feel even more desolate (凄凉), and pitying my own circumstances, tears welled up, my chest feeling tight for no reason.

"If I died, would anyone cry?"

Thinking this, a sudden urge to see Chŏngŭi arose, and the thought of running off to Sŏul right away occurred.

After breakfast was finished, Suni came in stealthily (살금어니), as if to gauge the situation (動靜). When I caught her and asked what the master had said, she replied,

"He didn't say anything," and took out a folded banknote (紙幣張) from her waistband, placed it before me, and looked down at me with a tearful face, sitting there.

"So, what did he say this money was for?"

"... He just said to give it to you... And he said, if you're going to Sŏul today, to take good care of you," Suni said, her eyes brimming with tears. As if ashamed to cry, she turned her head slightly and bowed it deeply. <30>

"Why are you crying? Did your mother die?"

Though I snapped like this, comparing her to myself, who wouldn't shed a single tear even if my parents died now, I couldn't help but find her adorable.

"Wouldn't it be nice to go to Sŏul?... We'll come back again."

Offering this one word of comfort (慰勞), I got up and decided to get ready (治裝).

The rain poured down all day without cease, and you didn't come back until dark. So, instead of leaving on the 7-something train, we delayed and delayed, and finally, we boarded the train around 2 o'clock in the morning.

Ah, the more I think about it, I have never experienced a night as desolate and miserable (悲慘) as that one. I don't know where you went that day, but unable to wait any longer, I left the lonely house entrusted only to the inside servant woman and the outside servant couple (밧갓下人內外). As I set out with only two suitcases in the rickshaw, drawn by the suppressed sobs of Suni, I sat in the rain gear (雨備), bit my lip hard, yet couldn't help but hold back tears (먹음지). (Incomplete) <31>

**Summary**

This installment of Yŏm Sangsŏp's novel "New Year's Eve" is narrated by a woman recounting the painful early days of her marriage. She describes her trip to T'okyo with her new husband ("You"), her internal conflict over accepting his lavish gifts due to her premarital past (including a likely pregnancy resulting from a relationship with "P"), and their move to Taegu. The narrative details the growing tension as her husband becomes suspicious, her own vacillation between confessing and hoping for a miracle, and the eventual confrontation where he, after much anguish, tells her to leave. The story ends with her desolate departure by night train, highlighting her emotional numbness mixed with profound sadness. From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the social constraints and personal dilemmas faced by women in colonial Korea, particularly concerning issues of chastity, arranged marriage dynamics, the conflict between tradition and emerging modern sensibilities (like seeking divorce or dealing with premarital pregnancy), and the stark emotional landscape of individuals caught in such circumstances. The mention of specific locations like T'okyo, Taegu, and Sŏul, along with details like company housing, servants, and train travel, grounds the personal drama within the socio-economic realities of the 1920s.

除夜-
						廉想涉
六
우리가 3週日 가까운 東京旅行을 마치고, 大邱 한구석에서 家庭이랍시고 살림을 始作한 것은, 한참 찌는 둣한 7月 그믐께가 아니엇습니까.
새로운 主人을 기다리는 支店長의 椅子, 神婚한 젊은 夫妻를 마즈랴는 새로 꾸민 社宅, 거긔에 들어안저, 過去의 쓰고 컴컴한 記憶 낡은 傷處를 다-이저버리고, 10倍 100倍의 勇氣로 새롭은 生活을 세우랴고 貴君의 前途는, 洋洋하고 광명과 희망에 빗난 꿈이엇겟지요. 더구나 교회에서 畵中의 떡으로 바라보며, 戀慕하고 熱望하던 것을, 及其也 품에 안고 보니, 意外에 貞淑하고 溫順한 데에는, 다만 한우님께 感謝치 안흘 수 업스셧겟지요. 그러나 그 貞淑, 그 溫柔, 그 服從이, 무엇을 意味한 것이엇든가를 神이 아닌 貴君이, 어찌 아셧겟습니까.

그러나 어느덧 집안에는 鬱畓한 더운 속에, 陰散한 氣運이 돌게 되엇습니다. 上下 5, 6人이나 들어 업들엿는 집안에서는, 朝夕으로 부억에서 서름질하는 그릇 부듸치는 소리와 집 치우는 비질 소리 外에는, 쥐 죽은 듯이 숨소리 하나 크게 들리지 안핫섯습니다. 언제나 끗이 날지 모르는 陰鬱한 無言劇은, 여름날 더위와 가티 支離하얏습니다. 이러한 異常한 狀態가 벌서 2週日이나 지나서, 8月은 훌적 넘어갓습니다. 滿 5個月이 넘은 나의 몸은, 모시옷 두 세겹으로는 아모러케 하야도 감출 수 업슬만치 들버티고 묵업엇습니다. 神經도 왼만큼 疲勞하얏습니다. 머리는 그 以上 더 窮理할 餘裕가 업섯습니다.
「될대로 되렴으나!」하며, 긴긴 해를 房 속에서 듸굴듸굴 굴며 보낼 수 밧게 업섯습니다. 貴君도 每日 如前히 定한 時間이 되면, 書齋로 會社에, 會社로 書齋에, 아모 일 업는 것 가티 드나드시는 貌讓이엇스나, 언굴에 歷歷히 씨운 苦悶의 痕跡만은, 나날이 컴컴하게 흐려갈 뿐이요, 어떠케 措處해야 조흘지 거의 失神狀態이엇섯던 貌樣이엇습니다. 그러나 때는 익엇습니다. 프로그램도 다-되엇습니다.
9月15日! 貴君에게 對하야도, 一生涯에 이츨 수 업는 날이엇겟지요. 結婚 生活 75日이란 時間을, 한 입에＜26＞ 삼켜버린 날이엇습니다. 同時에 25歲의 떡닙 가튼 貴君으로서는, 지고 견딀 수 업는 묵업은 짐을 내려 노코, 큰 숨을 쉬이시던 恩惠롭은 날이엇습니다.
-그 때가, 아마 子正이나 갓가웠섯지요. 그날은 밤이 들어도 바람 한 點 업시, 훅근훅근하며, 마즈막 덥은 鬱陶한 날이엇습니다. 11時를 처도 아니 들어오시기에, 順伊더러도 자라 하고, 나도 모기帳으로 들어가 들어누엇섯습니다. 손님 待接을 하야도 飮食을 사다가는 하셔도, 決코 料理집에는 아니 가시는 貴君이, 오늘은 왜이리 느지시나 하며 , 어느덧 잠이 어린어린하얏섯습니다. 그러나 5, 6分間이나 지낫슬지 마루 끗에서 털석하는 소리에 깜짝놀라 일어나며 내다보니까, 어는 틈에 들어오셧는지, 貴君은 구쓰를 신으신 채 마루 뎐에, 숨이 찬 늣이 고개를, 푹 숙으리시고 안즈셧는 것을 뵙고, 나는 놀라지 안흘 수가 업섯습니다. 順伊을 일으켜 내보내고, 나도 뒤따라가 나는 입을 벌일 勇氣가 업섯습니다. 藥酒라고는 葡萄酒 한 盞을 잡스서도, 葡萄酒 빗보다도 더 밝애지시는 貴君이, 그날은 어대서 얼마나 잡스셧던지 氣神을 못 차리시는 것처럼 눈을 감고 어느 때까지 안즈신 것을 뵈올 際, 眞情으로 가엽슨 생각을 못 이겻습니다. 그러나 한便으로는, 굿센 信仰을 깨트리시고 世間的擺脫을 하셧다는 것이, 돌이어 나에게는 一條의 活路를 어든 것 갓기도 하고, 적어도 오늘밤에는 이때까지 苦待하던 機會가 온 것 갓기도 하야 多少 安心도 되엇섯습니다.
그 가티 20分이나 안즈셔섯지요. 順伊가 哀乞하는 것이 未安하야 그러셧던지, 벌덕 일어나서 올라 오시는 길로 書齋로 들어가실 줄 알앗던 貴君은, 안房으로 들어오셔서, 順伊가 분산히 치운 데에 알엣목에 펄석 주저안즈신 뒤에도, 亦是 고개를 숙이고 안즈신 것을 보고, 나는 書齋로 숨어버렷습니다. 그러나 順伊가 와셔 부를 때에는, 豫測은 하고 안젓섯지만 가슴이 두근대지 안흘 수가 업섯습니다.
「어떠케 할가? 그 問題를 끌어내이실 地境이면, 무어라고 對答을 해야 조흘가. 이 機會를 노치지 말고 시원하게 죄다 쏘다 버리는 것이 得策은 得策이지만, 어떠케 말을 꾸며내대야 조흘가. ...」
머리속에서는 旋風이나 일어난 듯이, 이 생각 저 생각이 삥삥도나, 채 決心을 하지 못하고 안房 門지방에 발을＜27＞ 걸쳣습니다. 貴君은 그대로 化石이 된 것처럼 괴로운 숨만 씩은씩은 쉬이고 안즈셧슬 뿐이엇습니다. 그러나, 내가 들어가서 압 窓門을 向하야 안즈니까, 비롯오 고개를 드시고, 順伊더러 건넌방에 가서 자라고 한 마듸 하신 後, 또 亦是 어느 때까지 고개를 숙이시고 안즈셧지 안핫습니까.
무섭은 沈默이 머리 우를 壓倒하야 옵니다. 1分 2分 5分 6分... 내 머리 우에서 때깍거리는 時計소리마는, 수-수- 하시는 괴로운 숨소리와 長短을 마칩니다. 벌서 10分이 지낫습니다. 12時가 떵떵 첫습니다. 그러나 亦是 死와 가튼 沈默은 또다시 繼續합니다. 어느덧 나의 마음은 沈靜하야 왓스나, 沓沓한 症은 漸漸 甚하야 질 뿐이엇습니다. 참다 못하야 나는 입을 벌엿습니다.
「인제 주므시지요.」
「...」
「자리를 예다 펴 드릴까요.」
그러나 貴君은 如前히 눈을 감고 안즈셧슬 뿐이엇습니다. 醉氣에 못이기어 그러셧던지, 이마ㅅ살은 더욱더욱 찝흐리실따름이엇습니다. 나도 고개를 숙이고 안젓섯습니다. 또 2, 3分은 지낫습니다. 나의 가슴은, 묵은 부스럼을 건드리는 것 가티 조릿조릿하얏습니다마는, 한便으로는, 인제는 될대로 되렴으나!하는 自抛心이 생기는 同時에, 돌이어 마음이 가벼워지는 것을 깨다랏습니다. 그러나, 瞥眼間애 고개를 번적 드시며,
「흥, 자라구?... 인젠 定말 잘 때가 되엇는지도 모르겟소.」
혼자 말슴처럼 한 마듸 하시고, 나를 똑바로 치어다 보실 제, 나도 不知 中에, 고개를 번적 들엇스나, 마조치는 視線을 避하랴고, 또다시 고개를 숙이는 수 밧게 업섯습니다. 그러나 貴君의 불길 가튼 視線만은 나의 배 속에서 움지럭어리는 永遠한 謎物을 찌르는 듯이 이리로 쏘다지는 것을 깨다랏습니다. 또 묵업은 沈默이 繼續되엇습니다. 그러나 꼼짝도 아니하고 껌어케 덥흔 密雲이 한 소낵이 하지 안코 허터질 리가 업지 안습니까. 끌은 가마 속 가티 훅근거리는 房 안의 鬱陶한 空氣는, 피가 마르도록 땀을 쥐여짜내입니다. ... 듸디어＜28＞ 두렵은 沈默은, 다시 주어 담을 수 업시 깨지고 말앗습니다.-
「모든 것이 내 八字요. 그런 運數를 타고 난 以上에야 하는 수 잇소. ... 아모 말도 할 必要업소. 또 들을 必要도 업소. ... 來日 떠나주! 그 밧게는 내 힘으로 하는 수 업스니까. 생각해서 彼此에 조토록 하는 수 밧게 업소. 서울로 가서 어떠케 하는 것은 나도 몰르우. ... 모든 것이 내 잘못 내 不察인지 모르지만, 이 以上 名譽까지 犧牲하는 수는 업스니까, 그 點은 깁히 생각해 주어야 하겟소.」
목이 메여올 때마다, 큰 기침을 하셔가며 겨우 말슴을 맷고, 고개를 다시 숙이시고 흙흙 늑기시는 貌樣이엇스나, 나는 그대로 무릅 우를 내려다 보고 안젓슬 수 밧게 업섯습니다. 머리 속은 마치 四肢가 어그러진 물레바퀴 貌樣으로, 모든 想念이 一時에 停止한 것 가티 混沌한 中에서, 「正말 藥酒에 醉하신게 아니로군」하는 생각만은, 몃번이나 反復하야 떠올랏습니다. 어느 때던지, 몸을 내던저서 실리우고 울며 號訴하얏스면 시원하리라, 모든 問題는 다-씻기어 흘러가 버리리라고 한 것도, 只今와서야 다만 空想에 不過하얏던 것을 깨다랏섯습니다.
懊惱의 膽汁 가튼 눈물 방울이, 하얀 洋服 바지 무릅 우에, 떨어지기가 무섭게 슴여들어가는 것을, 겻눈으로 노려 보고 안저서도, 내 눈은 如前히 보송보송하얏섯습니다. 淚線이 막힌 것이 아니라, 송도리ㅅ재 말라버렷섯습니다. 或은 눈물이 말랏다는 것보다도, 靈魂이 보송보송하야젓다는 것이 穩當할지도 모르겟습니다. 彈性과 潤氣를 일허버린 靈魂은, 건듸리면 보삭보삭 바스러저버릴만치 말랏섯습니다.
「내가 술이 醉해서 한 말은 아니요. 한달 동안 머리를 썩이고 겨우 決心한 것이니까, 凡然히 생각치 말고, 來日 밤차로 떠날 準備를 하오.」하시며, 훌적 서재로 들어가 버리신 뒤에도, 나는 아모 것도 생각할 수 업시 안즌 자리에 그대로 멀건이 안젓섯습니다.
順伊가 건너와서 자리를 다시 펴주고 간 뒤에도, 亦是 알엣목에 안젓섯스나, 머리에는 아모 생각도 떠오르지 안핫습니다. 이대로 안저서 밤을 새여 보리라는, 自抛的 一種의 우습은 反抗心 外에는, 웃고 십흔지 울고 십흔지, 自己의 마음을 自己도 헤아릴 수가 업섯습니다. 이밤이 새이면, 이 집을 永永 離別하지 안흐면＜29＞ 안되리라는 생각조차, 머리에는 떠오르지 안핫섯습니다. 그러면서도, 只今 이 光景을, 건넌房에서 順伊들이 눈치를 채엿다 하드래도, 나의 姙娠이 結婚하기 前부터, 貴君과 交接이 잇서서 된 것으로만 斟酌하고 잇는 彼等에게는, 젊은 夫婦의 嫉妬 싸움으로 알리라는 생각이 나서, 속으로 우서 보기도 하얏습니다. 마음에는 餘裕가 잇는 것 가트면서도, 空然히 焦燥한 症이 나서, 누엇다 안젓다 할 뿐이요, 인젠 어떠케 措處를 하겟느냐는 것은, 아모리 하야도 생각이 떠오르지 안핫습니다.
電燈 불이 나가고, 새소리가 재절대일 때에, 暫間 눈을 부텻습니다. 2, 3時間쯤 자고 선잠에 소소라처 깨이니까, 落水 물소리가 凄凉히 들리엇습니다. 어제 왼終日 찌더니 其於히 비가 되고 말앗습니다. 부억 속에서 멀리 들리는 떼그럭 소리며, 황황히 도아 단이는 발자최, 구두를 닥가야 하느니, 人力車軍을 불너 오느니 하는 속은거리는 소리, ... 한참 부산하더니, 집안은 다시 從容하야젓습니다. 으슥한 房 속에 가만히 들어누엇스랴니까, 自己가 重病이나 든 것 가타야, 더욱이 凄凉하기도 하고, 自己 신세가 불상한 듯도 하야 눈물이, 나을 듯 나을 듯, 空然히 가슴이, 뻐근하얏습니다.
「내가 죽으면, 울 사람이나 잇슬가?」
이런 생각을 하다가, 貞義를 맛나보고 십흔 症이 와락 나서, 今時로 서울로 뛰어 올라갈가 하는 생각이 일어낫습니다.
아츰을 다-해치은 뒤에, 順伊가 動靜을 살피랴는 듯이, 살금어니 들어오기에, 붓들어가지고 나리가 무엇이라 하시드냐고 무러보니까,
「암 말슴도 아니 하셔요.」 하며 허리춤에서, 네골에 접은 紙幣張을 꺼내서, 내 압헤 노코, 울 듯울듯한 相으로 나를 내려다 보고 안젓섯습니다.
「그래 이 돈은 뭘 하라고 하시던?」
「... 근냥 갓다 드리라고만 하셔요. ... 그리구, 오늘 서울 가신다거던 잘 모시고 가라구 하셔요.」하며, 順伊는 눈물이 글성글성하야 우는 것이 부끄럽다는 듯이 고개를 살작 돌리며 폭 숙으렷습니다.＜30＞
「울기는, 웨 운단 말이냐, 네 에미가 죽엇니?」
나는 이가티 소리를 빽 질럿스나, 父母가 돌아갓다고 하야도, 눈물 한點 나오지 안흘 只今의 自己에 比하야 얼마나 貴엽은지 몰라서,
「서울 가면 좀 조흐냐. ... 인제 또 온단다.」
이가티 慰勞 삼아 한 마디 하고 일어나서, 洗嗽한 뒤에 治裝을 차리기로 決心하얏습니다.
비는 왼終日 끈힐 새 업시 쏘다지고, 貴君은 어둡도록 들어오시지를 안키 때문에, 7時 몃分 車로 떠나랴다가 머믓머믓하고, 及其也에, 밤, 새로 2時 頃에 車에 오르게 되엇습니다.
아-, 생각할스록 그날 밤처럼 쓸쓸하고 悲慘한 經驗을 하야 본 일은 업섯습니다. 貴君은 그날 어대로 가셧던지 모르겟습니다마는, 기대리다 못하야 쓸쓸한 집을 안잠재기와 밧갓下人內外에게만 맛게 두고, 가방 두 個만 人力車에 노하가지고 나슬 제, 辭氣 업는 順伊의 울음소리에 끌리어서, 나도 雨備 속에 안저서, 입슐을 악물고도, 不知中 눈물을, 먹음지 안흘 수 업섯습니다. (未完)＜31＞
