Article Title: 嗚呼 地方農村의 衰頹 =(鄕里에 갓든 이약이)= (Ah, the Decline of the Provincial Farming Village =(A Story from a Visit to My Hometown)=)
Author: 朴春坡
Article Type: 세태비평 (Social Commentary)

Ah, the Decline of the Provincial Farming Village =(A Story from a Visit to My Hometown)=
						Pak Ch'unp'a
=Such misery is buried among our neighbors=
=Our hometown people are suffering such pain=
=Brothers! Do not look up (above), please look down (below)=
=Brothers! If you have tears, please shed them here=

My journey to my hometown is like this

Unless one is an extreme individualist, as a member of human society who holds a part of affection, connects with parents, siblings, and the nation, hopes for everyone to live well together, and strives for everyone to live well together, how could there be a moment without homesickness, no matter how heartless or busy one is? How much more so in the circumstances of our people today, when localism (nationalism) is surging?
I was truly a fellow full of homesickness. That place where generations have lived continuously, the place where this body was born and raised, clothed and fed, the place where parents and siblings, relatives and old friends reside, that is, my hometown! When would I not miss it, when would there be a time I could forget it?
When I sat quietly in my room on a sunny morning, the scene of my mother with her bent back and worried face, cooking rice and drawing water, appeared vividly. When I gazed blankly at the western sky on a moonlit evening, the actual sight of my father with his chapped hands and trembling body<74> twisting straw ropes and making straw mats appeared just as it was. When facing a meal, the millet (粟) rice and soybean paste (土醬) placed on their dining table were clearly visible; when facing my office desk, the reality of them carrying A-frames up the mountain and entering the house carrying firewood was clearly visible. Are they all well? Have there been no natural disasters, deaths, or illnesses? How much has the general thought progressed, and how much has the general life improved? My hometown, missed and missed like this, over and over! Thinking "some day," "some day," led to changing the year and changing it again due to this and that matter, doing "a little more," "a little more." Thinking "this day," "that day," doing "just one more day," "just one more day," led to passing a fortnight and then another fortnight.
Ah, there are many petty things in the human world. It was one Sunday night, in the study of my most beloved friend, Elder Brother Kim. While discussing human suffering and the pain of life back and forth, two urgent telegrams arrived consecutively through Elder Brother Cho's hands. This was the news of my father's passing (家親還元).
My hometown, so dearly missed, I finally got to enter only after sending my father to another world. This is petty. One might call it an ordinary event in life.

My hometown is like this

If you board a train at Namdaemun and travel for a while, then a while longer, you cross the Taedong River. After crossing the Taedong River and traveling a bit further, you cross the Ch'ŏngch'ŏn River. After crossing the Ch'ŏngch'ŏn River and traveling a little more, you cross the Taeryŏng River. Get off right after crossing the Taeryŏng River, and that place is called Yŏngmi. From here, if you walk northward along a mountain path for 60 ri (Korean ri), you enter a valley surrounded by mountains on all sides, open like a chicken's nest; this is my hometown. The population is merely 15 or 16 households, and the people are entirely farmers, but only tenant farmers. Among the mountains, Kajibong Peak is slightly high, and for water, the Wŏnp'ungch'ŏn stream just murmurs along. Fields and paddies are about half and half, and the forest is just enough to supply firewood. There is, of course, no school; over one pass, there is a church or a private village school (私塾). It's generally like this. A very wretched (糟粕) farming village.

A Glimpse of My Self-Consolation

Regarding him who had passed away (還元), I did not particularly wail (哀號) nor was I particularly distraught (蒼黃). Who among humans does not die, and who among humans can attain long life? Just as he died, I too will die; just as he suffered in the physical world (肉界),<75> I too will suffer in the physical world; just as he will find joy in the spiritual world (靈界), I too will find joy in the spiritual world. It is all the same (一律), so what is there to grieve? It is a common destiny (同歸), so what is there to mourn? There is only the difference between today and tomorrow. There is only the depth or shallowness of affection. This too is the sole judgment (獨斷) of the living; the dead will have no discriminating view (分別觀) whatsoever. I do not insist on living, nor do I insist on dying. That is, I just entrust myself to the natural law of the universe. I myself do not keep life and death in mind, so even if it is my father, what concern is death, and even if it is me, what concern is death?
I resigned myself like this and remained quite calm. However, this too is the assertion of reason (理智). Emotionally (情的), how could it be so? Something was certainly stirring in the middle of my chest. It is a fact that I faced great difficulty in controlling this. Moreover, the sorrowful winds and miserable rains (悲風慘雨) coming from all around! Truly, my body shivered. The choked sobbing of my 80-year-old grandmother. The sight of my young siblings fainting. Ah, not being wood and stone (牧石), not being dead ashes (死灰), what could I do?
Who among humans will not face this pain? The one who would escape this pain is surely a fool who does not understand life. This is a common occurrence and a fixed rule of life. Let those who weep, weep; let those who faint, faint. I alone will remain silent (默默), and I alone will remain composed (自若). Thereupon, throwing away the emotional knots in my heart (胸海) and cutting off the surrounding circumstances, this body transformed and communed with the solitary moon among the clouds; now, finally, it felt as if I had become a single, flawless gem shed of ten thousand dusts and kalpas.
This is a glimpse of my method of self-consolation. From this point, my actions returned to normal.

Seeing the Reality of the Hometown As It Is

Living like this and that, 10 days passed, then another 5 days, and now, 5 days before leaving my hometown, I came to observe the real situation of the village. I visited people one by one (歷訪) and homes one by one. First, I sought out my neighbor's house.
Passing the dilapidated stable (馬廊) and the collapsing bush-clover gate, I stepped over the earthen threshold (土階) and entered the inner room (the main living room; there was, of course, no separate men's quarter). The head of the household was twisting straw rope, his wife was mending socks, and two shaggy-haired children held corn (玉薥) cakes, sniffling. Cattail rushes (북草) were scattered about the room, and dung-stained rags filled this corner and that, while some sort of murky, stale smell pierced the nose. On the ceiling where rafter beams (椽木) peeked through, spiderwebs (網) were stretched, and the uneven earthen walls (土壁) looked as if they had been flogged with bedbug blood. The floor mat was all worn out, with dust<76> rising in clumps, and on top of a clothes chest the size of a kerosene box (石油箱子), a couple of ragged bedding items (夜具) lay.
However life may be, could human feelings be different? On one hand happy that the gentleman who went to Seoul had returned, on one hand honored, on one hand apologetic, on one hand seemingly awestruck, the head of the household, smiling and welcoming me yet with a tone mixed with sighs, stopped twisting the rope and swept the room briskly with a broom (箒), saying, "This is how we live," and urged me to sit. His wife also stopped her sewing, saying, "The young master must be sitting in such a place for the first time," and retied her skirt ties. The children just stared at me with wide eyes. Before sitting, I asked, "Where is the elder?" and based on his reply, went straight up to the upper room and sat down to inquire after (慰問) the master old man's illness.
The master elder (令監) was an old man of 70, currently suffering from infirmities of old age (老患). He lay on his sickbed, clad in worn-out, dirty, flimsy cotton trousers and jacket, stained with grime and with flesh peeking through here and there, chewing listlessly on a coarse corn cake that could make one's throat choke and gums peel. Truly a pitiful old man. He couldn't afford a single packet of medicine or a doctor's visit, nor could he wear a set of warm clothes or eat sweet food. He groaned (呻吟) amidst this. Tears streamed down uncontrollably from my eyes.
"Do you know who I am?" I asked, sitting right up close. Even in his hazy state (朦朧), he vaguely recognized me and, waving his hand as if pleased, said, "Ah, it's the nephew (姪) who went to Seoul... I'm going to die now," and tears welled up in his eyes—so thin, so thin, so lacking in vitality. Finding the scene so pitiful, I offered a few words of comfort (慰安) and then went down to the lower room and opened a conversation (談話) with the young master of the house.
The more I listened to his words, the heavier my heart became, and no means of comfort came to mind; I could only smack my lips.
"My friend, what's the point of living like this?"
"Why, what's wrong?"
"What's wrong? Just look at this miserable state. Does it look like human life? If one must live like this..."
"Village life is mostly like this. What's wrong?"
"What's wrong? My insides hurt, I can't live. Just because there's breath, they call it a person..."
"You're saying strange things. Village life is all like this."<77>
"All like this? Where else are there people living like this? As you can see, even though my father is ill, I couldn't get him a single packet of medicine or a bowl of rice porridge. What's the point of living like this?"
His voice began to tremble, and tears welled up in his eyes. I too felt numb, just sighing. Continuing his line of thought, yet perhaps slightly differently, I asked this:
"So, how are the farm provisions (糧食)? They wouldn't be excessively insufficient, would they? Between millet and sorghum..."
"You ask even while hearing my words. If provisions weren't insufficient, why couldn't I get a single packet of medicine for my sick father? It's already been two months since the food ran out. By making straw mats, we barely manage to survive (連命), but how we'll live from now on is uncertain (茫然). Who will lend us money? Who will let us borrow rice?... Ah, really!"
"What happened to all the farming you did? No matter how bad the harvest (凶年), didn't it even yield half a season's food?"
"For a poor fellow, there's neither bad nor good harvest. What good would come from cultivating a few days' worth (날가리) of that slash-and-burn mountain farming (山耕/火田)? And even that belongs to someone else... Ah, don't talk about it. Anyway, this place has bad land and the people's hearts are on edge, so poor people can't live here. We have no choice but to move (移徙) elsewhere."
"Do you think other places are better? That place is also this place. Is there really a different kind of place?"
"Still, they say North Jiandao (北間島) and West Jiandao (西間島) are a bit better... Anyway, where could there be a place worse than this?"
"Any place where people live is like this. Whether this place is better or that place is better, when you face it, it's all the same."
"Whatever the case, this place is bad, so we have to leave."
To these words, I could find no further reply; only my heart ached. He blames everything squarely on the place. Poverty is the place's fault, not being able to live is the place's fault, and everything else is the place's fault. My head ached and my chest felt blocked, so without saying anything more, I claimed I had some business to attend to and returned home.

It was the next day. I visited the house of Kim OO, one of my childhood friends, who had passed away the previous month. The way they lived was similar to the house I visited yesterday, but perhaps slightly better. Kim's mother, having lost her beloved son, saw me and held onto me tightly,<78> letting out a bout of sorrowful yet welcoming tears, as if her own son had come back alive. I comforted her in every way possible to calm her down, and then we talked (談話) about this and that for nearly an hour.
"The dead are dead, so let's forget them pettily and let the living live well. Who among humans doesn't die?"
"Live well? Your situation (境遇) is different from mine... I have no choice but to leave this place. With the place being like this, how can people not die? And as for poverty, this village is the worst."
"What crime has the place committed? It's all people's fault."
"People's fault? What kind of talk is this? No, was my son's death also people's fault?... He was sick for a whole year, could I call a proper doctor (醫師) even once? I asked everywhere they said was spiritually potent, performed every ritual (祭祀) there was, did every spirit protection (防神) there was, but in the end, he died anyway... Why did we live in this cursed (常놈) place? The more I think, the angrier (忿하야) I get. I must leave quickly (밧비)..."
Again, I became numb. No matter how much I explained reasons to the contrary, what effect could it have on her,激しく悲憤し、習慣が根深い病となった彼女に激しく悲憤し、習慣が根深い病となった彼女に? Having nothing else to do, I slipped out quietly. Kim's death was from tuberculosis. Yet, they offered substantial money (錢兩) to shamans (巫黨) and blind fortune-tellers (杖任), leading to the person's death, accumulating debt, and ultimately reaching a state where even the living could not survive. And the final blame was directed at the place, with the innocent mountains and waters taking the fall for all the sins.

Brothers! Search every nook and cranny of the three thousand li. Is such a miserable state found only in my place? Ah, it's suffocating.

Then, the day after that. I visited the house of another village friend, Kim somebody. (Living conditions were the same in every house). It was a household with quite a large family (食口). There were undoubtedly over ten people. Looking around the lower and upper rooms, the men had gone somewhere, and there were 6 or 7 women and children. But one strange and bizarre (奇怪罔測) sight was a tiny young bride (새악씨님), about 12 or 13 years old, sitting in the upper room, sniffling. I was so dumbfounded, I could only smile faintly. Then I asked Kim's mother (related to me through marriage (姐戚) but not closely) like this:
"That young lady?"
"Isn't that the child named Hwakshil?"
"Oh. When did she put her hair up (get married)?"<79>
"They say she put it up last fall."
"How old is she now to have her hair put up already?"
She sighed deeply, looking quite ashamed.
"They say she's twelve now. Did she put it up because it was time? It happened because we had no choice."
"What do you mean, you had no choice?"
"We had much debt and nothing to eat, so having no choice, we sold her off to Yi Village (李村) in Chŏngju (定州) last July."
"How old is the new husband (새서방)?"
"They say he's twenty-two."
"Asking in such detail (仔細) is rude, I know... but what harm is there between us... How much did you receive?"
"How much? Did we receive much? Her father, drunk and stumbling, handled it somehow... I think it was 800 nyang (兩) (80 Won)."
"It must have happened because you had no choice, but selling your child? Where else is there such an unthinkable act? And the new husband is twenty-two..."
"People better off than us do it too. Because the local custom (風俗) is like that... Do I not know it's a sin (罪)? We do it because we have no choice. Over the pass, Hong OO bought a seven-year-old for 800 nyang, they say. And across the way, Yi OO brought in a daughter-in-law for 1,500 nyang. Would others do it just because? This place's customs are like that."

Receiving word that a guest had arrived at my house, I left immediately. Look at this. Selling one's child is also blamed on the place. Of course, it wouldn't be entirely unrelated to the place. Speaking so openly about my local affairs is somewhat regrettable for the people of my hometown, and also for my own heart, but how distressed and pained must I be to publicly reveal such things? Unable to do anything through my own means and methods, I appeal widely to my brothers and compatriots to at least stir public opinion.

It was the day right before I left my hometown. There was some event at my house, so the elders from the neighborhood (隣里), one from each household, all gathered at my home. Taking this as an opportunity, I talked about various things. Talk about farming, talk about forestry (森林), talk about housekeeping, talk about education, talk about religion, talk about rites of passage (冠婚祭禮),<80> talk about improving customs and habits, talk about newspapers and magazines. Interspersing examples from foreign countries, or cities, or other regions, I spoke very sincerely, making it easy to understand. They too have ears and eyes (視覺), so they acknowledged my words. However, in the end, as if from one mouth (如出一口), they said this place is bad, so it's utterly impossible (到底히) to do anything. Poverty is the place's fault, death and illness are the place's fault, customs and habits are the place's fault, inability to educate is the place's fault, and everything else is the place's fault. And so, they all say they will leave. At this time, how much my lonely heart must have ached. Nevertheless, I argued my case as much as possible. We talked about the place for a while longer.
I explained at length, until my mouth was dry: that in the world, there are many places uninhabitable due to extreme cold with only icebergs and glaciers (氷山氷河), and many places uninhabitable due to extreme heat, seeming to boil all year round (四時長節); places where snow piles up like mountains (山덤이), places where it always rains cats and dogs, places where the sun cannot be seen, places where the stars cannot be seen, places that eat people, places where people live only by hunting animals, places where only wind blows, places with only mountains, places with only sand, places uninhabitable due to fierce beasts and poisonous snakes (猛獸毒蛇), places uninhabitable due to volcanoes and earthquakes (火山地震), places where people live only on sweet potatoes (甘藷), places where people live only on oats (귀리)—that there are many truly strange and outrageous (異常孟浪) places. But our Chosŏn lacks all these things; it's a place where the mountains are bright and waters beautiful (山明水麗), the climate mild and winds gentle (氣溫風和), mountains and fields are half and half (山野相半), the land fertile (土地肥沃), all five grains grow (五穀俱全), surrounded by sea on three sides (三面環海) so marine products (海産物) are abundant, gold, silver, copper, and iron are plentiful (만하야) for industry (工業) and daily use (日用), the people's hearts are sincere (渟厚) and customs virtuous (善良)—making it the best place in the world for people to live. I also mentioned that people of the world call Chosŏn a world paradise (樂園), and that Chosŏn's landscape is called embroidered rivers and mountains (錦繡江山). Finally, I strongly argued (力說) that our hometown, among all places, is a most vital land (緊要之地). That is, the mountains are beautiful, the water is clear, there are many paddies and also many fields, lacking nothing; moreover, it's free from natural disasters and any other kind of harm—a paradise within the paradise of Chosŏn, our hometown. I talked quite enthusiastically (興趣잇게). Thus, I explained two, three times that everything is people's fault. And I added that foreigners greatly desire to live in Chosŏn once, and people from other regions hope to live in our region once, asking how much regret there would be later if we all leave saying this place is bad, and then foreigners or people from other regions come here and live well. I spoke with great effort.
The general villagers were all silent (黙黙), as if acknowledging the plausibility, when my fifth cousin uncle quite forcefully declared (宣明) that his nephew's words were indeed true.<81>

Leaving the Hometown

Spending my time like this, my hometown life lasted merely 20 days. Leaving the hometown before dawn (未明) on the 17th, I felt sad and pitiful, and my feet hesitated (敢히) to turn back. Leaving behind my innocent young siblings, and at the same time, leaving behind relatives and old friends in the hometown whose future was uncertain—the scene made me linger (戀戀) and wander (彷徨) even more. It felt like leaving a beloved companion alone on an uninhabited, remote island (無人絶島). Would there be a path to regeneration (再生) for them, or would they forever wander there alone in sorrow and tears, only to collapse as they are, or perhaps drift (漂流) to an even more desolate (寂寞) and perilous (險惡) place? Indeed, it was a farewell whose outcome was hard to foresee (期티 難한 作別). The ancients said that a true man (丈夫) may not be without tears, but he does not shed them at parting (離別). However, upon leaving my hometown, I could not help but realize that a kind of worried and sorrowful tear flowed swiftly.
Ah, Brothers! How can this be said only of my hometown, only of my relatives and old friends? Lift your eyes and look thoughtfully at every corner of our land (江山). Every direction is like this, every house is like this, every person is like this. I have surveyed (實踏) many regions. Everywhere (到處) is like this.

Ah, Brothers! How can we give them life, give them light (빗), give them laughter, give them happiness? Everyone in the world with purpose, please shed a stream of tears.

Where mountains are bright and waters clear, only the people are dark and murky.
Ah, where does the sin (罪) lie?!<82>

**Summary**

The author, Pak Ch'unp'a, recounts his visit to his impoverished rural hometown in northern Korea for his father's funeral. He describes the dire living conditions, malnutrition, illness, lack of education, and despair among the tenant farmers. He details conversations where villagers attribute their suffering—poverty, disease, death, harmful customs like selling young daughters into marriage—to the inherent flaws of the "place" itself (地方탓), leading many to consider migrating to places like Jiandao. The author tries to counter this fatalistic view, arguing that Korea, and their region specifically, is naturally blessed and that their problems stem from human factors, not the land itself, urging them to improve their situation rather than flee. He leaves filled with sorrow and concern for their future.

From a historical perspective, this article provides a poignant first-hand account of the devastating poverty and social conditions in rural Korea during the 1920s under Japanese rule. Historians might find the pervasive fatalism ("place blame" or 地方탓) expressed by the villagers, the mention of migration to Manchuria (Jiandao) as a perceived solution, the critique of reliance on superstition (shamanism) instead of medicine, and the intellectual's struggle to instill agency and modern thought against deep-seated despair particularly revealing about the socio-psychological landscape of colonial Korea. The description of child marriage driven by poverty offers a stark glimpse into social pressures.

나의 鄕里行은 이러합니다

極端의 個人主義者가 아닌 以上 情의 一部를 次持하고 父母兄弟 民族 民族과 連結을 하야 다-가티 잘살기를 希望하며 다-가티 잘살기를 爲하야 努力하는 人間社會의 一員으로서 아무리 沒情하고 아무리 奔忙하단들 어찌 一時나마 思鄕心이 업겟습니까. 하물며 鄕土主義(民族主義)가 澎湃한 今日 우리 民族의 處地이겟습니까.
나는 實로 思鄕心이 만흔 놈이엇습니다. 代代나 두고두고 살아오든 그 곳 이 몸이 나고 자라고 입고 먹고 하든 그 곳 父母兄弟가 잇고 親戚 故友가 잇는 그 곳, 即 나의 故鄕! 어느 때 그립지 아니하며 어느 때 이즐 때가 잇겟습니까.
해 돗는 아츰에 고요히 房안에 안젓노라면 어머님의 굽은 허리 愁心만흔 얼굴로서 밥 지으며 물길으시는 情景이 歷歷히 보엿스며 달 밝은 저녁에 無聊히 西天을 바라보노라면 아버님의 부르튼 손 떨리시는 몸으로＜74＞ 새끼 꼬시며 집석이 삼으시는 實景이 그대로 보엿습니다. 밥을 對할 때 반듯이 그들의 食卓에 노힌 조(粟)밥과 된장(土醬)이 낫나치 보엿스며 事務床을 對할 때 반듯이 그들의 지게 지고 山에 오르며 나무-메고 집에 드시는 實情이 낫나치 보엿습니다. 다-들 安寧하신가 或-天災地變 死亡疾苦나 업스신가 一般의 思想은 얼마나 進步 되엇스며 一般의 生活은 얼마나 向上이 되엇섯는가? 이러케 이러케 두고두고 그립고 그립든 故鄕! 언제 한번 언제 한번 한 것이 此事 彼事 좀더-좀더-하다가 歲을 換하고 또 換하얏스며 此日 彼日 하루만 더-하루만 더- 하다가 보름을 지내고 또 보름을 지내엇습니다.
아-人間世上에는 齷齪한 일도 만습니다. 어느 日曜日 밤中인데 가장 愛經하는 金兄의 舍廊이엇습니다. 人間苦 生活痛을 說往說來하는 즘에 2枚의 急報가 趙兄의 手를 經하야 겹처 떨어집니다. 이가 곳 家親還元의 報입니다.
그러케 그립든 故鄕은 이제 아버님을 딴 世上으로 보내고야 겨우 들게 되엇습니다. 이- 齷齪스럽습니다. 人生으로의 普通事라고도 하겟지오.

나의 鄕里란 이러합니다

南大門서 車를 타고 한참 또 한참 가노라면 大同江을 건너게 됩니다. 大洞江을 건너서 또 한참 오노라면 淸川江을 건너게 됩니다. 淸川江을 건너서 족음 오노라면 大寧江을 건너게 됩니다. 大寧江을 건너자 곳 내리면 그가 곳 嶺美라는 곳이외다. 여긔서 北을 向하야 徒步로 山路60里(朝鮮里)를 들어오면 四方 山中에 닭의 둥지만큼 터진 골이 잇스니 이가 곳 나의 鄕里입니다. 戶口는 15,6戶에 不過하고, 人民은 全혀 農民인데 小作人뿐입니다. 山은 茄子峰이 그 中 족음 놉고 물은 院豊川이 돌돌 흐를뿐입니다. 田畓은 相半이고 樹林은 그런대로 火木이나 보탤만 합니다. 學校는 毋論 업고 한 고개 넘어 敎會堂 又는 私塾이 잇습니다. 大槪 이러합니다. 아조 糟粕한 農村입니다.

나의 自慰의 一端

나는 還元하신 그에 對해서는 別로 哀號하지도 안핫스며 別로 蒼黃하지도 안핫습니다. 人生으로서 누가 죽지를 아니 하며 人生으로서 누가 長生을 어들 수 잇겟는가. 그가 죽음과 가티 나도 죽을 것이며 그가 肉界에서 苦함과 가티 나도 肉界에서 苦할 것이며 그가＜75＞ 靈界에서 樂함과 가티 나도 靈界에서 樂할 것이다. 一律이어니 무엇을 悲하며 同歸일지니 무엇이 애닯으랴. 다만 今明의 差가 잇슬뿐이오. 다만 情의 厚薄이 잇슬뿐이다. 이것도 生-그 者의 獨斷이오. 死-그 者는 下等의 分別觀이 업슬 것이다. 나는 구타여 살겟다고도 아니하며 구타여 죽겟다고도 아니 한다. 即 宇宙의 自然律에 一任할 뿐이오. 나-스스로는 生死를 念頭에 두지 아니하나니 비록 我父인들 死에 何關이며 비록 我인들 死에 何關일는가.
나는 이러케 斷念하고 아조 冷然하엿습니다. 그러나 이것도 理智의 主張입니다. 情的 方面으로야 어대 그리됩니까. 가슴 가운대서 무엇이 움을거리기는 確實히 움을 거립니다. 이것을 制禦하기에 매우 困難을 當한 것은 事實입니다. 더구나 四圍로서 들어오는 悲風慘雨! 實로 몸이 으스스 떨립니다. 80 老祖母의 목맨 울움. 어린 동생들의 氣絶하는 情景. 아- 木石이 아니고 死灰가 아니 어니 어찌 함니까.
人生으로 누가 이 苦痛을 아니 當할 것인가. 이 苦痛을 免하겟다는 그 者야말로 人生을 理解치 못하는 愚者일 것이다. 이것은 人生으로의 常事이며 定則이다. 울 者-울고 絶할 者-絶하라. 나는 獨히 默默할 뿐이며 나는 오즉 自若할 뿐이다. 이에 胸海의 情結을 집어던지고 四圍의 事情을 끈허 던지고 此 身의 化하야 雲間의 孤月과 交하니 이제 비롯오 萬塵千劫을 버슨 一個無瑕의 玉이 된 듯하다.
이것이 나의 自慰策의 一端입니다. 이로부터 나의 動作은 平常을 回復하게 됩니다.

鄕里의 實況을 그대로 봄

그렁저렁 지내는 것이 10日이오. 또 5日이엿고 이제 離鄕前 5日을 期하야 鄕里의 實情을 살피게 되엇습니다. 人人을 歷訪하며 家家를 歷訪하게 되엇습니다. 나는 첫재로 나의 이웃집을 차젓습니다.
찌글어진 馬廊 넘어저 가는 싸리門을 들어서 土階를 넘어 안房을(舍廊이란 毋論업슴) 들어섯습니다. 戶主되는 이는 새끼 꼬고 안해는 버선 깁고 더벙머리 두 아이는 강낭(玉薥)떡을 들고 코를 훌적훌적 합니다. 북草는 房안에 넙너부러-하고 똥무든 걸네는 이 구석 저 구석에 그득한데 무슨 흐리터분하고 시시부러한 냄새가 코를 찌릅니다. 椽木가래가 비죽비죽 보이는 天井에는 거미줄(網)을 죽 늘엿고 우틀두틀한 土壁에는 빈대피로 매질을 한 듯 합니다. 자리는 다 떨어저 몬지가＜76＞ 뭉클뭉클 이러하며 石油箱子만한 衣床우에는 누덕이 夜具가 두어개 노엿습니다.
生活은 如何튼지 人情이야 다르켓습니까. 서울갓든 양반이 돌아온다고 一邊 반갑고 一邊 榮光스럽고 一邊 未安스럽고 一邊 惶恐한 듯이 웃으며 반가워하면서도 한숨을 겸한 語調로써 戶主는 새끼 꼬든 것을 中止하고 비(箒)로 房안을 휙휙 쓸면서 「사는 꼴이 이럿습네」하고 안즈라고 勸합니다. 안해 되는 이도 亦是 「적은 샌님은 이런 자리에 처음 안저 보겟소」하며 바느질하든 것을 멈추며 치마고름을 다시 맵니다. 아이들은 눈이 멀둥멀둥하야 나만 처다봅니다. 나는 안기에 先하야 「어르신네는 어대 계시냐고」뭇고 그의 對答에 依하야 곳 웃방으로 올라 안저 主人 老人의 病을 慰問합니다.
主人 令監은 年 70의 老人이신데 只今 老患 中에 잇습니다. 떨어지고 더럽고 산뜻한 찬자리에 때가 덕지덕지 뭇고 살이 비죽비죽 나오는 무명바지 저고리를 걸치고 病席에 누어서 목이 턱턱 메이고 이ㅅ(齒)몸이 버서질만한 거츠른 강낭떡을 들근들근 씹고 누엇습니다. 果然 불상한 늙은입니다. 藥 한 첩 醫師 한 번 못써보며 단 飮食 따뜻한 옷 한 벌을 못 입어 봅니다. 이러한 中에 呻吟합니다. 나는 抑制할 수 업시 눈물이 좍 흐르더이다.
「제가 누구인지 아르시겟습니까」하고 딱바투 들어 안저 물으니까 그는 朦朧한 中에도 얼픗이 알아보고 반가운 듯이 손을 허위며 「아-서울 갓든 조카(姪)로 구만... 나는 이제는 죽겟네」하며 그-마르고 마르고 氣脈이 업서 보이는 눈에 눈물이 엉킵니다. 나는 그 情景이 하도 딱하야 몃 마디로써 慰安을 들어고 곳 알엣方으로 내려 안저 젊은 主人과 談話를 열어 노핫습니다.
그의 말을 들을스록 가슴이 답답하야 무슨 慰安의 方은 아니 생기고 그저 입맛만 쩝쩝 다실뿐이엇습니다.
「여보게 이 모양하고 살으면 무엇하겟나」
「왜 어떳습니까」
「어떳타니 이 꼴악신이를 좀 보게. 사람사는 것 가튼가. 이 모양으로 살 바에야...」
「村살림이 大槪-다 그러치요. 무엇이 어떱니까」
「어떳타니 속이 傷해 못살겟네. 命이 부터 잇스니 사람이라고 하지...」
「그저 딴 말슴을 다-하십니다. 村살림이 다-그러치요」＜77＞
「다-그러타니 이러케 사는 사람이 어대 또 잇겟나. 보는바와 가티 아버님께서 病으로 계서도 藥한 첩 쌀밥 한 그릇을 못해 드럿네. 이러구야 살아 무엇하겟나」
그의 말은 뗠리기 始作하며 눈물이 핑그레-돌읍니다. 나도 멍멍하야 그저 한숨만 지을 뿐입니다. 나는 그 말의 繼續인 듯 하면서도 족음 다를가 하야 이러케 뭇습니다.
「그래 農糧은 어떳습니까. 過히 不足치는 안켓지요. 黍粟間에...」
「이 사람이 말을 들으면서도 그러는구만. 農糧이 不足치 안흐면 왜 病나신 아버님께 藥 한 첩을 못 지어들이겟나. 糧食 떨어진지가 벌서 두달채일세. 집석이 짝이나 삼어서 艱幸艱幸히 連命은 해가나 압흐로 어떠케 살지 茫然하여 누가 빗을 주나. 누가 쌀을 通用해 주나... 아-참!」
「農事하신 것은 다-어떠케 되엇습니까. 아무리 凶年이기로 半節糧食도 아니 되어요」
「가난한 놈에게는 凶豊도 업다네. 그까짓 山耕(火田)날가리나 며칠 가리부치면 무슨 數가 잇겟나. 그나마 남의 것... 아-말-말게. 어쨰든 이놈의 곳은 땅이 바르고 人心이 끗에 올라서 가난한 사람은 못 살 곳일세. 不得已 다른 곳으로 移徙할 밧게 업네」
「다른 곳은 나흘 줄 아오. 그곳도 이곳이지요. 別다른 곳 잇답듸까」
「그래도 北間島 西間島는 좀 낫다는데... 如何間 이만 못한 곳이야 어대 잇겟나」
「그저 사람 사는 곳은 다-그러탑니다. 이곳이 나흘가 저곳이 나흘가 해도 當하고 보면 다-마챤가지지오」
「어쩃든 이곳은 地方이 낫바 떠나야 되어」
나는 이 말에 다시 무어라고 對答이 아니 나오고 다만 가슴만 쓰렷습니다. 모든 허물이 斷斷然 地方탓이라 합니다. 가난해도 地方탓 못살아도 地方탓 그밧게 무엇무엇이 다-地方탓이라 합니다. 나는 머리가 압흐고 가슴이 뚝 막히어 무어라고 다시 말하지 안코 무슨 볼일이 잇다고 집으로 돌아오고 마럿습니다.
