Fire of the Wind
						Sowŏl

Wind blowing in spring, spring where the wind blows,
The blowing spring wind shakes small branches,
The wind-blowing spring shakes my heart,
Spring and wind and I weep together.
Looking afar, flowers and wine are before you,
For your sake, I am sorrowful.

They say waves change and become mulberry fields.

This sorrow of mine, hard to hold back,
Falling flower petals on a darkening spring evening,
Oh, let the falling flower petals flutter.
Even in the words handed down by ancient people,
They say waves change and become mulberry fields.
So it is. During the dreamlike, beautiful time of Youth,
All things become unfamiliar to the eye, estranged,
Look, is it not sorrowful? You,
On a fading day in March, even in spring,
Scattering down like red rain,
Those flower petals over there, those flower petals over there.

If I were to sit facing the lamplight.

Silently,
If I were just to sit facing the bright lamplight,
Without any thought, I just want to weep.
Though perhaps no one would know why.

If I were to lie alone in the dark night,
Without any thought, I just want to weep,
Though perhaps no one would know why,
Though if I had to assign blame, I could say something.

Spring Night

On the old branches of the willow tree's dark hair,
On the wide wings of the swallow's navy skirt,
On the window lattice of the tavern, look, isn't spring sitting there?

Soundlessly the wind blows; let out a sigh,
This pitch-black spring night, sorrowful and yearning without reason,
Soft moisture drifts and covers the ground.<48>

**Summary**

This collection of three short poems by Sowŏl, published in 1922, explores themes of transience, sorrow, and the melancholic beauty of spring. The poet uses imagery of wind, falling petals, and the changing seasons ("waves change and become mulberry fields") to evoke feelings of loss, the fleeting nature of youth, and an undefined, pervasive sadness, particularly felt during quiet moments like facing a lamp or lying alone at night. From a historical perspective, the poems reflect the pervasive sense of melancholy and loss (han) often found in Korean literature during the colonial period. While not explicitly political, the deep sense of sorrow and reflection on change and fading beauty could resonate with the national experience of lost sovereignty and cultural upheaval, offering historians insight into the emotional landscape of the era as expressed through lyric poetry.

바람의 불
						素月
봄에 부는 바람, 바람부는 봄,
적은 가지 흔들리우는 부는 봄바람,
내 가슴 흔들리우는 바람부는 봄,
봄과 바람과 나는 함끠우노라.
바라보면 꼿과 술은 그대의 압헤,
그대를 위하야 나는 설어하노라.

물결이 變하야 뽕나무밧이 된다고.

붓잡기 어렵은 나의 이 설음,
저무는 봄저녁에 져가는 꼿닙,
져가는 꼿닙들은 나붓기여라.
녯사람의 닐너오는 말에도
물결이 變하야 뽕나무밧이 된다고.
그러하다. 꿈가티 아름답은 靑春의 때의
온갓 것은 눈에 설고 낫모르게 되나니,
보아라, 섧지 안흔가, 그대는
봄에도 三月의 저무는 날에
붉은 비가티도 흐터저 나리는
저기 저 꼿닙들을, 저기 저 꼿닙들을

燈불과 마조 안젓스랴면.

寂寂히
다만 밝은 燈불과 마조 안젓스랴면
아못 생각도 업시 그저 울고만 십습니다.
웨 그런지야 알 사람이 업겟습니다 만은.

어둡은 밤에 홀로이 누엇스랴면
아못 생각도 업시 그저 울고만 십습니다,
웨 그런지야 알 사람도 업겟습니다 만은,
탓을 하자면 무엇이라 말할 수는 잇겟습니다 만은.

봄밤

실버드나무의 검스렷한 머리ㅅ결의 낡은 가지에,
제비의 넓은 깃나래의 紺色치마에,
술집의 窓녑헤, 보아라, 봄이 안젓지안는가.

소리도 업시 바람은 불며, 한숨지여라,
아모런 줄도 업시 설고 그립은 새캄한 봄밤,
보드랍은 濕氣는 떠돌며 땅을 덥허라.＜48＞
