Author: 쉑스피아(원작), 玄哲(譯述) (Shakespeare (Original Author), Hyŏn Ch'ŏl (Translator/Adapter))

하믈레트
						쉑스피아(원작), 玄哲(譯述)
Third Act

Scene Two (Continued from last month's issue)

Hamŭl. Is there relish in my mother's heart?
Queen. Methinks the lady doth protest too much.
Hamŭl. Let us keep this one word.
King. Have you heard the argument? Is there no offence in't?
Hamŭl. No, no, they do but jest, poison in jest; no offence i' the world.
King. What do you call the play?
Hamŭl. The Mouse-trap. Marry, how? Tropically. This play is the image of a murder done in Vienna (뻬엔나): Gonzago (코싸코) is the duke's name; his wife, Baptista (빠푸티쯔다). You shall see anon; 'tis a knavish piece of work... but what o' that? Your majesty, and we that have free souls, it touches us not.
Let the galled jade wince, our withers are unwrung...
(Enter Actor dressed as the villain Lucianus (류샤나쓰))
This is one Lucianus (루샤나쓰), nephew to the king.
O’hwi. You are as good as a chorus, my lord.
Hamŭl. I could interpret between you and your love, if I could see the puppets<49> dallying.
O’hwi. You are keen, my lord, you are keen.
Hamŭl. It would cost you a groaning to take off my edge.
O’hwi. Still better, and worse.
Hamŭl. So you must take your husbands.—Begin, murderer; pox, leave thy damnable faces, and begin. Come: 'the croaking raven doth bellow for revenge...'
(Rusya). Thoughts black, hands apt, drugs fit, and time agreeing; Confederate season, else no creature seeing; Thou mixture rank, of midnight weeds collected, With Hecate's ban thrice blasted, thrice infected (三度毒氣), Thy natural magic and dire property, On wholesome life usurp immediately.
(Pours the poison into the sleeping King's ear)
Hamŭl. He poisons him i' the garden for his estate. His name's Gonzago (콘싸코): the story is extant, and writ in choice Italian (伊太利). You shall see anon how the murderer gets the love of Gonzago's wife.
O’hwi. The king rises!
Hamŭl. What, frighted with false fire!
Queen. How fares my lord?
Pporo. Give o'er the play.
King. Give me some light: away!
All. Lights, lights, lights!
(Exeunt all but Hamŭllet'ŭ and Horresio)
Hamŭl. Why, let the strucken deer go weep,
The hart ungalled play;
For some must watch, while some must sleep:
So runs the world away.
Would not this, sir, and a forest of feathers—if the rest of my fortunes turn Turk with me—with two Provincial roses on my razed shoes, get me a fellowship in a cry of players?
Horre. Half a share.
Hamŭl. A whole one, I.
For thou dost know, O Damon (데에몬) dear,
This realm dismantled was
Of Jove (쪼오바) himself; and now reigns here
A very... very—peacock.<50>
Horre. You might have rhymed.
Hamŭl. O good Horresio, I'll take the ghost's word for a thousand pound. Didst perceive?
Horre. Very well, my lord.
Hamŭl. Upon the talk of the poisoning?
Horre. I did very well note him.
Hamŭl. Ah, ha! Come, some music! come, the recorders!
For if the king like not the comedy,
Why then, belike,—he likes it not, perdy.
Come, some music!
(Re-enter Rooschenk'ŭranch'ŭ and K'ildensŭdaan.)
K'ilden. Good my lord, vouchsafe me a word with you.
Hamŭl. Sir, a whole history.
K'ilden. The king, sir,—
Hamŭl. Ay, sir, what of him?
K'ilden. Is in his retirement marvellous distempered.
Hamŭl. With drink, sir?
K'ilden. No, my lord, rather with choler.
Hamŭl. Your wisdom should show itself more richer to signify this to his doctor; for, for me to put him to his purgation would perhaps plunge him into far more choler.
K'ilden. Good my lord, put your discourse into some frame and start not so wildly from my affair.
Hamŭl. I am tame, sir: pronounce.
K'ilden. The queen, your mother, in most great affliction of spirit, hath sent me to you.
Hamŭl. You are welcome.
K'ilden. Nay, good my lord, this courtesy is not of the right breed. If it shall please you to make me a wholesome answer, I will do your mother's commandment: if not, your pardon and my return shall be the end of my business.
Hamŭl. Sir, I cannot.
K'ilden. What, my lord?
Hamŭl. Make you a wholesome answer; my wit's diseased: but, sir, such answer as I can make, you shall command; or, rather, as you say, my mother: therefore no more, but to the matter: my mother, you say,—
Roo. Then thus she says; your behaviour hath struck her into amazement and admiration.<51>
Hamŭl. O wonderful son, that can so astonish a mother! But is there no sequel at the heels of this mother's admiration? Impart.
Roo. She desires to speak with you in her closet, ere you go to bed.
Hamŭl. We shall obey, were she ten times our mother. Have you any further trade with us?
Roo. My lord, you once did love me.
Hamŭl. So I do still, by these pickers and stealers.
Roo. Good my lord, what is your cause of distemper? You do, surely, bar the door upon your own liberty, if you deny your griefs to your friend.
Hamŭl. Sir, I lack advancement.
Roo. How can that be, when you have the voice of the king himself for your succession in Denmark (덴마아크)?
Hamŭl. Ay, sir, but 'While the grass grows,'—the proverb is something musty.
(Re-enter Players with recorders)
O, the recorders! let me see one. (Goes aside to K'ildensŭdaan) Will you play upon this pipe?
K'ilden. My lord, I cannot.
Hamŭl. I pray you.
K'ilden. Believe me, I cannot.
Hamŭl. I do beseech you.
K'ilden. I know no touch of it, my lord.
Hamŭl. 'Tis as easy as lying: govern these ventages with your lingers and thumb, give it breath with your mouth, and it will discourse most eloquent music. Look you, these are the stops.
K'ilden. But these cannot I command to any utterance of harmony; I have not the skill.
Hamŭl. Why, look you now, how unworthy a thing you make of me! <52> You would play upon me; you would seem to know my stops; you would pluck out the heart of my mystery; you would sound me from my lowest note to the top of my compass: and there is much music, excellent voice, in this little organ; yet cannot you make it speak. 'Sblood, do you think I am easier to be played on than a pipe? Call me what instrument you will, though you can fret me, yet you cannot play upon me....
(Enter Pporooniyasŭ)
God bless you, sir!
Pporo. My lord, the queen would speak with you, and presently.
Hamŭl. Do you see yonder cloud that's almost in shape of a camel?
Pporo. By the mass, and 'tis like a camel, indeed.
Hamŭl. Methinks it is like a weasel.
Pporo. It is backed like a weasel.
Hamŭl. Or like a whale?
Pporo. Very like a whale.
Hamŭl. Then I will come to my mother by and by. (Aside) They fool me to the top of my bent.—I will come by and by.
Pporo. I will say so.
(Exit Pporooniyasŭ)
Hamŭl. By and by is easily said.—Leave me, friends.
(Exeunt Rooschenk'ŭranch'ŭ, K'ildensŭdaan, Horresio, etc., leaving Hamŭllet'ŭ alone)
'Tis now the very witching time of night, When churchyards yawn and hell itself breathes out Contagion to this world: now could I drink hot blood, And do such bitter business as the day Would quake to look on. Soft! now to my mother.— O heart, lose not thy nature; let not ever The soul of Nero enter this firm bosom: Let me be cruel, not unnatural: I will speak daggers to her, but use none; My tongue and soul in this be hypocrites; How in my words soever she be shent, To give them seals never, my soul, consent!
(Exit Hamŭllet'ŭ)
-(End of Act 3, Scene 2)-<53>

**Summary**

This text is a Korean translation/adaptation of Act 3, Scene 2 of Shakespeare's Hamlet, published in the magazine *Kaebyŏk* in April 1922. It covers the "Mousetrap" play-within-a-play scene where Hamlet confirms King Claudius's guilt, Claudius's abrupt departure, Hamlet's confrontation with Rosencrantz and Guildenstern involving the recorder metaphor, his exchange with Polonius about the cloud shapes, and his final soliloquy before going to his mother's chamber. For historians of colonial Korea, this article is interesting as an early example (1922) of the translation and reception of major Western literary works. The translator, Hyŏn Ch'ŏl, used the term "譯述" (yŏksul), suggesting not just translation but adaptation or narrative retelling, reflecting how foreign culture was processed. The specific transliterations chosen for names (e.g., 하믈, 오휘, 뽀로) and the use of early 20th-century Korean orthography and vocabulary offer linguistic insights into the period.

第三幕

第二場(前月號) 繼續

하믈. 어머님의 마음에는 滋味가 잇습니까?
王妃. 劇妃의 말이 넘우 過치 아니한가 하네.
하믈. 한말은 지킵시다.
國王. 이 事然을 아는가? 무슨 拘碍는 업겟지.
하믈. 아니요 족음이라도. 한갓 作亂꺼리로. 戱諧로 毒殺한 것을 한 것입니다.
國王. 異狀도 한 表題이다.
하믈. 쥐 잡는 듯. 아니 比喩이다. 뻬엔나인 弑逆을 脚色한 것인데, 코싸코, 王妃는 빠푸티쯔다, 곳 아시겟지마는 무서운 凶計의 이약이...나...아무치도 아니한 것이야. 陛下이시나 또 다른 사람이나 마음이 潔白한 이는 關係가 업는 것이다.
무릅에 傷處잇는 말은 떼지마는 이 脊骨은...
(惡漢 류샤나쓰를 扮裝한 俳優 들어온다)
저것이 國王의 족하 루샤나쓰라는 者이다.
오휘. 殿下께서는 說明俳優가티 왼갓 것을 잘 아십니다.
하믈. 그러쿠말고. 거리에 서서 놀리는 산듸만 보아도 너와 너의 사랑하는 사람의 사이를 틀림업시＜49＞ 일러 듯기겟다.
오휘. 입도 넘우 빠르십니다.
하믈. 이 빠른 입을 鈍하게 하랴면 꿍꿍거리고 알치 안흐면 아니 될 것이다.
오휘. 조핫다가 글럿다가.
하믈. 그리고도 男便을 맛지 안흐면 아니 되겟지... 아니 사람 죽이는 놈, 始作해라, 곰보딱지의 醜한 얼굴은 그만 찡그리고 速히 始作해라, 『凶報를 모르는 까막까치도 怨讎를 갑흐라고 울고 잇다...』
(루샤). 마음도 검고 밤도 검으며. 藥도 毒하고 손도 굿세다. 때마츰 조흘시고 사람도 업네. 너이는 正밤중 어둠(暗)을 타서, 세 번이나 魔王의 呪咀에 쓸어저 三度毒氣에 적셔 낸 풀국. 두려울사 天然의 그 魔力으로 健全한 生命을 卽時에 뻬아사.
(잠든 王의 귀에 毒藥을 붓는다)
하믈. 저놈은 王位를 빼아스랴고 동산에 잠든 王을 毒殺하고 잇다. 王의 이름은 콘싸코라 하는 者인데 事實이 잇는 이약이다. 그것은 巧妙한 伊太利말로 記錄이 되엇다. 그리고 저 殺人者가 王妃를 꾀여 自己手中에 너흐랴고 한다.
오휘. 아아! 陛下께서 일어 납신다.
하믈. 무엇 거짓불(火)을 두려워 하셔서!
王妃. 어대가 거북하십니까?
뽀로. 演劇을 中止해라, 演劇을!
國王. 燭불 들어라... 저리로!
衆人. 燭불을 들어라! 燭불을! 燭불을!
(하믈레트와 호레시오를 두고 남아지는 다 들어간다)
