Stepping on the Spring Rain and Dew
						Kim Kich'an

There is an old saying: "When the spring rain and dew fall, a gentleman (君子)! steps on them and surely feels a sorrowful heart." I am no gentleman, but when I happen to step on the spring rain and dew, I inevitably have not only a sorrowful heart but also various thoughts. I write them down to trouble those with similar intentions for a glance.

Anxiety, Unrest—The Crisis of Rural Farming Villages

A compatriot from Namsŏn specifically sought me out and told me the following:
『A major incident has occurred in the Namsŏn region this spring. The poor young men (壯丁) of the farming villages are all leaving their homes and heading towards factories in Japan. Not only are the numerous middle-class farming households, which relied on their hands for farming, in disarray, but also many of the tenant farmlands (田畓) they cultivated themselves are on the verge of falling into ruin (荒廢).
How many are leaving, you ask? It is regrettable that I do not have statistics on this, but I tried counting the number of Japan-bound laborers I didn't know on the southbound express train at Miryang station one day (in mid-March), and the count was 221. At the stations south of Taegu, it's roughly the same situation at every stop. Since this phenomenon began after last year's autumn harvest and continues to this day, one can imagine how vast the number of those leaving is.
What is the cause, you ask? In a word, it stems from the historical exhaustion of the lives of the common people (細民) in the rural farming villages. Throughout generations, politics and morality not only failed to promote the welfare of the common people in general but also did not provide guarantees for their lives and property. Their possessions were mostly monopolized by a few local strongmen (土豪)<41> and other powerful families (權家). They eked out a living amidst near-death struggles with about one-third of the income generated from others' land. Fortunately, they found a refuge in Sŏbuk Kando some years ago, managing the immediate crisis for a time. However, in recent years, that area has become dangerous and untenable, so they have found a safe zone in the various factories of Japan, leading to the current situation. This is the general cause. Observing it more concretely: First, due to the landlords' excessive collection (過歛), farming itself is fundamentally unprofitable, making it impossible to support a family through agriculture alone. Second, because farming is unprofitable, those who farm using hired hands (雇人, mŏsŭmgun) cannot treat them well. Third, factory owners in Japan welcome cheap Korean laborers and devise various methods to recruit them. Fourth, most common people in rural villages are uneducated (無識者) and are easily swayed by the incitement (煽動) of intermediaries rather than relying on their own calculated judgment. Fifth, side businesses (副業) in farming households are not yet widespread, leaving them idle from after the harvest until the planting season, prompting them to try earning even a penny during this downtime.
What will be the effects, you ask? Well, I cannot dare say, but (1) as the management of middle-class farming households, which primarily relied on hired hands (mŏsŭmgun), falls into disarray, there is concern that the foundation of the farming villages will become precarious. Simultaneously, (2) there is concern that this will consequently reduce the harvest of agricultural products, the main produce of Chosŏn. (3) Although the displaced laborers in general might have temporary income, many will waste it, corrupt their character, and end up wandering in that land, or even if they return home, there is concern they could easily become fuses igniting disorder in society.』
Upon hearing these words, I truly do not know what to say. If the state of our farming villages, which form our main strength, is thus shaken and exhausted, where can we find our light? Alas! Even a blade of grass has its roots, but our lives lack them. Even a tiny sprout (엄) receives rain and dew (雨露), but our common people (細民) do not. Ah, the rain and dew to fall upon the common people—is it the self-awareness of landlords? The construction of factories? Or the spread of popular culture? O people with much money! People with much knowledge! People with both much money and knowledge! People with much knowledge and also real power! Please think of this as a fire fallen upon your own feet.
= I dedicate this writing separately to the various brothers of the Hŭngnonghoe'u (興農會友).<42>

Who Will Guide the Activities of Our Nation with Order and Organization?
= To All Graduates Leaving Various Schools

潢潦無根源 朝滿夕已除 (A puddle without a source, full in the morning, gone by evening)
This is a line from a poem the ancient Master Han T'oeji wrote for his children. For some reason I cannot fathom, whenever I think about the activities (云爲) of our nation, such poetic lines naturally come to mind. This, of course, may be nothing more than my personal bias.
To all the male and female graduates leaving various schools around this April, among you, some may continue their studies, and among those studies, perhaps practical business, perhaps politics, etc., your fields of learning will vary. Also, in the endeavors you undertake, perhaps commerce, perhaps culture, etc., your areas will differ. This is not for me to meddle in.
What I wish to beg your consideration for in just a few words is this: (1) Please become scholars as much as possible. (2) Even if circumstances and situations prevent you from aspiring to be scholars, or if you have already embarked on endeavors, please, in your attitude towards learning and in your methods of carrying out tasks, be sincere like scholars and systematic like scholars.
You may have thought about this better than I, but what is most deplorable for us today is that most of our thoughts, words, and actions lack foundation (根底). Therefore, not only do others not trust our activities (云爲), but we ourselves cannot trust our own thoughts, words, and actions. Where does the reason for this entirely lie? It is because we still lack scholars, and we lack scholar-like sincerity. If we had numerous scholars who clearly perceived the origins (由來) and consequences of all problems and phenomena, discerned (辨覈) the meaning and value of all movements, first cultivated a well-founded sincerity, and then conducted all activities based on the activation of that sincerity, how could there be a reason for words to be grand while actions are small, for methods to be crude and the atmosphere debased?
Urgent is the need to teach new order and organization to the activities (云爲) of our nation. Thus, we must build an unshakeable foundation (萬撓不拔之根底). Who, then, will take on this task? It is said to be the graduates leaving the halls of learning each year, and among them, those who strongly aspire to be scholars and those who work with scholar-like pure sincerity and organization.

Alas! The Bankruptcy (破産) of Chosŏn Women

An old saying goes:<43>
國亂思良相 家貧思賢妻 (In national chaos, one thinks of a good minister; in family poverty, one thinks of a wise wife)
The real economic power that clothes and feeds us has long (though not entirely, the majority) passed into the hands of foreigners, so economically, our house is already poor (家貧). Even within a single family, thoughts are unsettled between the new and the old (or East and West), so spiritually, our house is already poor. In such times, even if we try not to think of a wise wife (賢妻), can we succeed?
It is said that the "good mother, wise wife" (良母賢妻) ideology is already an outdated thought, not to be adopted by the new generation. However, theory is theory; in reality, as long as the family system is maintained, the need for good mothers and wise wives will inevitably persist.
Therefore, we must educate (敎養) wise wives and good mothers. To educate them, we must first provide education suitable to our customs (風俗), habits, morals, principles, and systems. Yet, when we look at the facilities for our women's world, what do we see? While there are university departments (setting aside the mismatch between name and reality), higher common schools, common schools, or similar educational institutions, apart from schools run as means of proselytization or for other policy reasons, how many schools truly protect the unique Chosŏn womanhood and provide education suitable for becoming housewives in Chosŏn homes? Furthermore, while there have been magazines and other associations aimed at the social education of our women, most of them stagnated midway, and even those remaining today do not possess any particularly commendable influence. With such facilities for the women's world, how can we expect the sound development of our society—especially the fulfillment of our homes?
Some critics reproach women who received school education, calling them arrogant individuals unaware of their own countrymen beyond foreigners, ignorant individuals who know nothing of household management or caring for relatives, and lacking qualifications in various other aspects to be housewives in our homes. While the criticism might be excessive, it cannot be guaranteed that women who received such education in such surroundings would not turn out that way.
The bankruptcy (破産) of Chosŏn women—this is indeed a great problem. It is a great problem concerning whether we seek our inner life for eternity or abandon it for eternity. There must surely be countermeasures for this relief. Especially since women, by nature, are more susceptible to influence than men, and because they will influence the mindset of our future citizens, there is a need to discuss this more urgently than any other issue.<44>

A Word for the Student World
And Also to the School Authorities

My dear students, the time when everyone universally praised your courage and purity seems like only yesterday. How has it come to pass that those same people now speak of your insincerity and lack of self-control?
Recently, I attended a graduation ceremony at a certain school. The principal's address to the graduates lasted about 10 minutes, and its entire content was cautionary words about student discipline. A few days prior, I met with the head of another school, and the entirety of his words also carried such meaning. Furthermore, I have seen such words in newspapers and heard them from the mouths of other seniors. Students, what is happening?
Of course, I believe that what is rumored in the world is not entirely true to fact. Even if some part of it is true, I contend that the responsibility lies not with the students themselves, but with the society and school authorities who provided the convenience and leeway that fostered such phenomena. Exploring the causes:
1. The ignorance of the masses and the prevalence of pseudo-patriots. The ignorance of the masses inevitably led to demanding cultural propagation from students who are in the learning stage. The prevalence of pseudo-patriots agitated the innocent hearts of students. These two facts drove a small number of students to the rash conclusion that "the affairs of today's Chosŏn society cannot be resolved unless we roll up our sleeves and step forward," simultaneously fostering the illness of grandiose delusions.
2. The proliferation of concerts and lectures. Of course, music and lectures themselves are sacred, but the excessive creation of such gatherings, where those with little cultivation ascend the platform unprepared with the aim of deceiving the masses momentarily, delivering meaningless, disorganized speeches without substance, has somewhat bewildered the thoughts of young students. Also, gatherings of male and female students centered around concerts have naturally contributed to troubling the hearts of the youth. Those who frequently participate in such meetings naturally come to publicly judge the character of societal figures, discuss worldly affairs, and feel as if they instantly become patriots or activists just by stepping onto the podium once, eventually losing their student-like pure sincerity.
3. Lack of foresight by school authorities. Although perhaps less so recently, student strikes (盟休) during last year and the year before constituted one of the most prominent phenomena in our society. The causes were, of course, varied. According to my observation,<45> the usual guidance and foresight of the school authorities were inadequate, leading to situations where issues like the qualifications of instructors or school facilities were left to be debated by the student side. Allowing such flaws on the student side resulted in fomenting student unrest and, in some cases, even led to their deterioration.
Though I cannot point out every example, besides what has been mentioned above, factors such as the inadequacy of student dormitory facilities, the indiscriminate reading of newspapers and magazines (especially literary works), and individuals participating in more than one association likely had significant impacts on students' characters.
Students, I wish to say this for your sake: First, believe that for those who are learning, the act of learning itself is paramount. Recognize that seeking involvement in worldly affairs prematurely is definitely not the path to great achievement. Regarding lectures organized by general society, unless they are academic lectures, there is no need to attend. As for students themselves engaging in cultural propagation, practice this sparingly if possible, and do not speak of anything other than the academic subjects you are researching. Be selective in your reading outside of textbooks, avoiding empty writings focused solely on inspiration and encouragement, and shallow literary works. It would be best to always uphold the principle of self-cultivation first, aspiring to become the backbone of tomorrow's society.

Finally, I will offer a word to the school authorities. (Although the few points mentioned above were formally addressed to students, the main intention was actually to trouble those related to students for their consideration.) Remarks concerning student discipline are heard most often from you, the school authorities. This is likely due to your direct relationship with the students. However, school authorities, you must consider this one thing: precisely because students are students—in other words, because they stand under the direct guidance of teachers and learn everything—students themselves cannot independently be the masters of any problem, and at the same time, the full responsibility for those problems inevitably falls upon you, the authorities who are their direct guides. Strictly speaking, if students have merits, it is merely your own merits manifested through them. If students have demerits, it is merely your own demerits manifested through them. Even if reality presents exceptions contradicting this, you must think in this way. There is no need for lengthy chatter towards you wise individuals.<46> What I humbly suggest is: (1) Schools should esteem moral education (德育) as much as intellectual education (智育), instilling in general students the notion that "school is a place that provides character as well as knowledge." (2) Regarding all potential problems arising within the school, maintain constant vigilance and resolve them proactively. (3) In managing students, do not force their submission through authority, but win their willing obedience through sincerity (誠). That is, make them trust and love (信愛) the school authorities rather than fear and respect (敬畏) them. (4) To achieve this, you, the authorities, must first not forget to become individuals of genuine character and ardent disciples of self-cultivation.

To the Farming Brothers, the Pillars of Chosŏn

When many people speak of destruction and discuss construction, talk of culture and debate politics, each裝扮 as a person of purpose and displaying their existence, you farming brothers alone are immersed in the monotony of the village, heads bowed, holding hoes, providing the food and clothing (衣粮) of Chosŏn.
When people seek better lives for themselves, crossing the Amrok-kang and Tuman-gang to the northwest, the Ilbon-hae and T'aep'yŏng-yang to the southeast, scattering in all directions and becoming displaced, you farming brothers alone guard the homeland of Chosŏn, showing the spirit to fight to the end. Residing at the lowest level of society where official power, financial power, and intellectual power combine forces and dominate, you farming brothers endure (堅忍) all the unpleasantness and hardship (幸酸) as they are, faithfully devoted only to the work at hand.
When I contemplate your circumstances in silence and admire your spirit, my emotions are stirred, my feelings reach an extreme, and I truly do not know what to say. How much more so today, when word coming from the regions, from person after person, unanimously reports the anxiety and exhaustion of the farming villages?
But you! Do not forget your initial resolve: "I will die for my homeland (殉하리라)." Recognize that the shouts for so-called popular culture, freedom and equality, industry, education, etc., being raised in today's world and within Chosŏn are ultimately movements aiming to elevate your status and promote your welfare. The sympathy of the world seeks only to approach your doors. Endure patiently, and strive always with a joyful spirit until you achieve self-sufficiency and self-reliance.
(For details, please refer to the two articles penned by this reporter: "Urgent Motion for Rural Improvement" in Issue No. 6 of this magazine and "Plan Regarding Rural Improvement" in Issue No. 7 of this magazine.)

The Final Path for All to Entwine and Advance Together

Setting aside the various ways one might categorize, I intend to divide our brothers and sisters as follows:
Namely, (1) those with knowledge and money, and those without knowledge and without money<47>; (2) those with knowledge but no money, and those with money but no knowledge; and (3) those with knowledge but no money, whose knowledge enables them to form their own perspective and establish their livelihood thereby, and those with money but no knowledge, who can assert their existence through innate ability or practical experience. It is said that matters such as displaying the nation's existence and constituting the face of society primarily depend on the people belonging to the first category of (1) and the group belonging to (3). Daring to undertake adventurous actions, engaging in radical and reckless propaganda, devising cunning schemes to either agitate the public sentiment, disturb social order, or commit numerous intellectual crimes—such acts are primarily carried out by people belonging to the first category of (2). Those who are utilized by the few while sometimes becoming objects of caution for those same few are mainly people belonging to the second category of (1) and the second category of (2). Consequently, those in power seek to deal with the people belonging to the first category of (2) to reduce their numbers, while society attempts to adopt measures of cautious distance or utilization towards them. However, the people whom both the rulers and general society wish to deal with directly are those belonging to the first category of (1) and the group belonging to (3). As for the numerous others, they seem to be disregarded for now. Among these, the most detestable and fearsome are those elements from the first category of (1) and the second category of (2) who secretly entice or incite people belonging to the second category of (1) and the second category of (2) to fulfill their own ambitions, thereby obstructing or damaging the nation's proper path forward.
The task we must generally focus on today is not merely to speak grandly or act loftily, but to grasp hands with those who lack money and knowledge and those who have money but lack knowledge, giving them industry and knowledge. Furthermore, we must grasp hands with those who have knowledge but lack money, giving them occupations and skills, so that brother joins brother, entwining entirely and discussing the path for the whole to become self-sufficient. However, achieving this primarily depends on the duty of those belonging to the first category of (1) and the group belonging to (3) mentioned above.

Our Joy Unknown to Others

Rain and dew (雨露) fell continuously, and fragrant grasses poked their heads out daily. In late March, today the 23rd, heavy snow suddenly fell, nearly two feet deep. But this snow will vanish within a week, and the falling rain and dew will continue to fall, the beautiful flowers and grasses will remain beautiful. There is no worry, no concern. Our nation still faces remaining ice and snow and unexpected wind and rain, but it is certain that spring has returned. Those who do not understand may look at the old mirror still covered and the remaining snow and say, "Still far off! How pathetic!" But some, seeing the fiercely sprouting spring beneath that old mirror, might be dumbfounded. Now, stepping on the spring rain and dew, feeling the most joyous sentiment as if clearing away the old mirror, I offer these few rough personal opinions.<48>

**Summary**

Kim Kich'an expresses deep concern over the state of Korean society in 1922, focusing on several critical issues. He laments the exodus of poor farmers to Japan due to economic hardship and landlord exploitation, weakening the nation's agricultural base. He calls for intellectuals and graduates to provide sincere, organized leadership, criticizing the lack of foundational thinking in national activities. He worries about the "bankruptcy" of Korean women, suggesting traditional roles are undermined by unsuitable modern education and societal neglect, impacting the home and future generations. He addresses student issues, attributing perceived indiscipline partly to societal pressures and inadequate school guidance, urging students to focus on self-cultivation and authorities to foster trust. Ultimately, Kim calls for unity across social strata—the knowledgeable and wealthy aiding the less fortunate—to achieve national self-reliance and progress, seeing underlying strength and inevitable progress despite surface difficulties, like spring emerging after winter.

From a historical perspective, this article offers valuable insights into the socio-economic anxieties and intellectual currents in colonized Korea during the early 1920s. It highlights the severe impact of colonial economic policies on rural life, driving migration and social instability. Historians might find the author's grappling with modernity—particularly regarding education, national identity (criticizing shallow activism), and the changing roles of women (reflecting patriarchal anxieties amidst social change)—especially revealing about the complexities of navigating tradition and external pressures under colonial rule. The call for unity among different social classes reflects contemporary nationalist aspirations for collective action.

不安, 動搖-地方農村의 危難

南鮮의 同胞 한 사람은 저를 委혀 차자 알에와 가튼 말을 하여 주엇습니다.
『南鮮地方에는 今春에 들며 큰일난 것 한 가지가 잇습니다. 農村의 貧民 壯丁이 모다 流離하야 日本의 工塲을 向往하는데 그들의 손을 비러 農事를 營하던 多數 中 農家의 狼狽는 勿論이오 그들의 自耕하던 小作 田畓도 만히는 荒廢에 돌아갈 地境이외다.
