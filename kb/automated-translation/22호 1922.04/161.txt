The Seller of Lucky Ladles

“Buy lucky ladles... Lucky ladles...”
On the pitch-dark night of the year's last day,
Wandering through alley after alley,
Oh, seller of lucky ladles,
You are the most enlightened observer (達觀者).
Since the history of Humanity (人類) began

That's right, they are hungry for Fortune,
They are trampled by Misfortune.
Your cry is finally
For those about to greet the new year,
Truly a welcome sound.
-(End)-<12>
<9-12>

**Summary**

This poem portrays a vendor selling *pokchori* (lucky ladles), a traditional Korean item bought on New Year's Eve for good fortune, wandering the dark streets. The speaker calls the vendor an "enlightened observer" (達觀者), suggesting a deep understanding of human desires and struggles. The vendor's call resonates with people yearning for luck and escaping misfortune as they prepare to welcome the new year. From a historical perspective, the poem subtly reflects the enduring cultural practices and the quiet hopes of ordinary Koreans for a better future during the early colonial period, despite the hardships implied ("hungry for Fortune," "trampled by Misfortune"). The focus on a traditional custom like buying *pokchori* highlights cultural persistence.
