Magazine Title: 개벽 제22호
Publication Date: 1922년 04월 01일
Article Title: 未來를 爲하야 (For the Future)
Author: 金石松
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

For the Future
						Kim Sŏk-song

One

They are Happiness
—Body and Spirit—
With these two things
They speak of the World

They rightfully
With the Body—
With the Spirit—
Shall gain Eternal Life

And so their
Supreme Happiness—
Highest Ideal—
Upon that, a flower shall bloom

Two

Those whom we, until now<9>
—Wise men, Accomplished scholars, Sages—
Have called by these names,
These purely man-made Idols

With Morality, Ethics, Philosophy...
All sorts of snares,
Who bound our necks,
These Blasphemers of Truth

In the golden mist,
In the fragrant flower garden,
On the master's knee,
Dream the dream of Victory

Three

But, friend

For Humankind,
For Truth,
For the Future,
Walking barefoot<10>
My young friend

The shrewd Ones'
Entanglements, woven this way and that,
A spiderweb-like Canopy,
How many days can it hold?

Endless fear
And matchless Threat
Make us weep
Us, the young ones

Four

For the Future,
Friend who worries in your heart,
Weep! Weep!
Weep until the end!

When the Setting Sun of Tradition
With solemn Radiance
Above Humankind's head
Smiles gently...<11>

All things become one
Yin and Yang unite
Spirit and Body respond
The undying Fixed Star

Shall emit a new light

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏk-song contrasts the perceived happiness and eternal life sought by those who adhere to traditional notions of body and spirit with the critique of established authorities ("Wise men, Sages," etc.) depicted as idols who ensnare humanity with false truths (morality, ethics, philosophy). The poem expresses anxiety and fear ("endless fear," "matchless Threat") but places hope in the suffering ("weep until the end") younger generation ("my young friend," "us, the young ones") who walk "barefoot" for humanity, truth, and the future. It culminates in a vision of unity (Yin/Yang, Spirit/Body) and renewal ("new light") transcending restrictive traditions. For historians, the poem reflects the intellectual climate of 1920s Korea under Japanese rule, criticizing established power structures (potentially coded criticism of collaborators or the colonial system itself) and expressing a yearning for a radically different future, likely fueled by nationalist or socialist ideals prevalent in publications like *Kaebyŏk*. The imagery of "man-made Idols," "snares," and a fragile "spiderweb-like Canopy" suggests a critique of hollow ideologies propping up an unjust order.
