Around the World, Over Mountains and Waters
						Literary Historian Roh Chŏng-il
Sitting amidst the moments of fierce hidden struggles in the cave of life, piled high with waves, this foolish brush, trying to pluck the tangled emotions like strings on a zither, keenly feels the agitation and anguish pressing in from the gathered daily duties and the unstable circumstances of a harsh new environment. Consequently, I deeply regret that I cannot present this so-called adventurous travelogue of a world tour in beautiful prose and fine sentences worthy of satisfying you all. I ask that you read this with an attitude of generous understanding and profound sympathy... (Il-u)

Second Section: The Divine Inspiration of Humor
A shepherd boy, resting his body on the back of an ox on a mountain path at sunset, heading towards a village where smoke rises high, picks a willow leaf and plays a desolate song on it like a flute. Whether drunk on nature or struck by solitude, who can know his feelings? And even if there were a sympathetic person wanting to know, the desolation of the flute's sound itself has the power—a blowing force (吹響力)—to divinely inspire according to the listener's character and circumstances. The common saying, "Laugh off all the world's affairs" (世上 萬事 都付 一笑), might be the reckless words of a wandering optimist, but whatever the case, there is nothing among the myriad things and matters in the world that does not offer some lesson or divine inspiration <76> to one with the power of thought.
Now, speaking of the divine inspiration of humor seems to imply a kind of wondrous feeling. When a solitary wild goose flies south crying on a frosty moonlit night, its sound is truly desolate, yet to the old and young deep asleep, it likely brings no emotion, no meaning, no appreciation. But to a prisoner groaning on a straw mat behind iron bars, wouldn't tears of lament flow without asking the sorrow of the lone goose flying freely in the blue sky? A noble princess sleeping alone in an empty room would fling open the glass window and shed tears of sympathy even before asking the lone goose's sorrow. During the three-odd years of struggle in Edo, every event encountered at any moment or opportunity carried a humorous meaning in itself, yet for me experiencing it, it often evoked tears of admiration rather than tears of sympathy, stirring my blood towards self-improvement. Sometimes, during leisure time, the dormitory students would playfully engage in *sumo* (씨름). Stripping completely naked, wrapping a towel around the loincloth area, they'd shout, "Alright, come on! Let's do it!" Making a fuss, lifting and lowering their left and right legs, stretching and bending them, they'd then take salt in their mouths, spit it out, drink water, and spray it while planting their legs apart, clenching their fists, shouting "Alright, come on!" and the wrestling would begin. I would sit calmly on a high chair nearby and watch intently. They shout words of encouragement loudly. "Alright, Roh-kun, come on! Let's do it!" I also imitate what I see. I move my legs up and down, tense the muscles in my arms, sprinkle salt, drink water, making a fuss. "Roh-kun, be firm!" I give a warning. "Roh-kun seems quite strong!" I also offer praise. "Alright, let's do it!" He charges. First, without much effort, I slap his ear sharply, making him crumple down. "Wow, the Korean style is splendid!" they exclaim in admiration. "Let's go again!" He charges. I hook his inner thigh (안갈 구리) and wrench his back sharply. "Ah, it hurts!" he shouts and falls. "Let me try! What's Roh-kun?" Another fellow charges. This time, calculating to do it with a bit more flair, I quickly thrust my right leg between the opponent's legs, lift his hips (웅덩배기), and with my left arm, press down hard on the back of his neck (뒷더수구니), throwing him down in a cross shape (열 십자), shouting like thunder, "There, he's going down!" Ah! Then all hell broke loose. "What's this about losing to a Korean! We can't lose to the New Japanese!" Two or three guys rushed me at once. I gulped down cold water and said, "Didn't you know all Koreans are strongmen (力士)? They say even the iron hammer (鐵椎) that struck like lightning from the flowing sands (댄 浪沙) was the handiwork of the Korean race!" A fight broke out. They rushed at me as if to kill. I said I wouldn't fight anymore. <77> The enemy army, frustrated, insisted we continue. I slyly turned away, saying, "I need to go study. I'm a student who came to study, not a soldier who came to fight!" Slouching off towards the well, I drew about ten buckets of cold water, poured them over myself, and heading back to my room, recited a line in reflection: "For human nature's癖 is to delight in fine phrases, If words don't startle people, one won't rest even in death (爲人性癖耽佳句 語不驚人死不休)." About 10 years ago, while reading Du Fu's poetry (杜詩), the effectiveness of the 'wrestling' I practiced as a troublemaking exercise was truly magnificent, I thought inwardly, entering my room, stretching out my four limbs, and calmly contemplating the spectacle of the wrestling match from beginning to end. The guys who had warned me at the start, "Roh-kun, be ready!" ended up rushing me as if to kill. Ah-ha! That's how it would be. Those guys are truly short-tempered, but their mentality of hating to lose to others, though strange, is something worth emulating. Though a bit stubborn, in this environment, in this society, if I only maintain the characteristics of a person from the 'nation of etiquette' (禮儀之邦人) with the attitude and appearance of a gentleman, I won't even be able to preserve my life. I must not lose, no matter the appearance or effort. I must win and move forward. Let's fight!" Talking to myself, I abruptly stood up, faced my desk, and 'studied.' After that, that scene and experience constantly came to mind, providing great stimulus in my life.
After the winter term exams finished, an entertainment gathering was customarily held. An entertainment gathering meant not only *dekansho* (데깐쇼 - likely a song/dance) and *korya korya* (꼬랴 꼬랴 - likely another song/dance), but also drinking bitter tea and eating salty crackers (煎餠). Once, a somewhat more sophisticated poetry meeting for personality critique was held. The purpose was to describe the classmates' personalities through poetry. A 'poet' among my classmates stood up briskly, thrust both hands into his side pockets, looking as if he would recite some magnificent verse, cleared his throat a couple of times, smacked his lips repeatedly, and then recited: "負ケヌツつ 花ヵ 笑ク ロ-サン" (Makenu tsuitsu hana ga saku, Ro-san). Translated, it means "On a face unwilling to lose, flowers bloom." He dedicated this line to me. It earned a round of great laughter and applause, as they felt the meaning of that line aptly expressed my character. If that truly captured my personality, it wouldn't be an exaggeration to say it was entirely the realization of the inspiration received from wrestling.
The 00 Academy (00學院) was an educational institution run by Western missionaries. At that time, the buildings and financial support were entirely the effort of Westerners, but its management and operation proceeded under the exclusive control of the Japanese. Although there were many beautiful Western-style houses within the academy grounds, the atmosphere of life had become that of Westerners assimilated into Japanese culture. Consequently, regarding the educational purpose, it was not to train hired hands for Westerners, but thoroughly aimed at educating Japanese youth for a sound Yamato culture <78> and nurturing competent citizens and leaders for Japanese society. Occasionally, influential Western financiers, scholars, religious figures, social activists, etc., who had invested their money and manpower in managing the institution, would visit to investigate the situation and propagate their own principles.
One day, the Dean of 00 University (米國 00大學院長 - likely implying an American university dean) from the United States was scheduled to give a speech at the academy's main auditorium on the topic "The Future and Duty of the Japanese People." All students from every department of the academy were gathered in the large hall, and all students from every department of the Women's Academy also attended. It was early spring, the weather was severely chilly, and the steam heating in the hall was insufficient, making the temperature inside barely different from outside. At the appointed time, an elderly doctor with a receding hairline and a large physique ascended the podium. Perhaps mindful of his health due to the severe cold and his age, he began his speech while still wearing his overcoat. At that moment, all the students filling the large hall began murmuring like buzzing bees, expressing displeasure with phrases like "Impolite old man!" or "He's insulting us!" Sensing the atmosphere, a Westerner quickly went up to the podium and discreetly whispered in the old doctor's ear, making him remove his overcoat.
Whether the elderly man who took the stage was concerned about his own physical well-being or intended to make an impression by wearing the overcoat while attempting the speech, it wasn't just a personal issue for the speaker. The appropriateness of the speaker's attire directly related to showing respect or disrespect to the audience. The Japanese youth, possessing a cultivated sense of self-respect (自敬心), naturally and fiercely displayed their spirit of intolerance towards the impolite and disrespectful old man's breach of etiquette.
At that moment, I felt an indescribable indignation. Especially since returning to Korea this time, I constantly feel a painful, stinging anguish as if stabbed in the heart with a knife. On the pulpit, before the congregation gathered for worship on a holy day, our Korean teachers (先生님들) stand stiffly wearing the same stained *durumagi* (두루막 - traditional overcoat) they wore while working at home, with dirt and dung stuck to their shoes, attempting to preach. Towards the missionary elders (宣敎師 어룬들) who receive blessings with raised hands while still wearing their overcoats, who among the 700, 800, or even over 1,000 worshippers has ever felt displeasure? Our Korean teachers might perhaps do so out of ignorance, so there is room for generous forgiveness. But the missionary elders, who claim to have education and belong to a civilized race, if they say they don't know better, then putting aside their missionary status, they are not civilized people. And if they do know the etiquette of civilized people, are they not insulting our people? It is clearly an insult. Trying to speak of the facts <79> makes my mouth too bitter to bear. We are a nation that loves music, often holding concerts of Eastern and Western music. When listening to Western music, observing the behavior of Western solo performers makes my heart ache, truly unbearable. They wear the same stained clothes they wore while cooking with their wives in their own kitchens and dare to appear before us. Is it music or insult? It's not that I complain they don't wear formal evening attire (夜會服). What is the meaning of having it but not wearing it? Is the evening attire they wear when performing at concerts hosted by Japanese people in the city perhaps too precious to wear before Koreans, for fear of getting it dirty? How could they insult us like that! Recently, hearing a friend's story made me truly angry. About two years ago, when that friend was tying the knot for a hundred years (百年佳綠 - getting married), whether out of adoration or respect for a Western teacher (先生님), he asked a missionary to preside over his wedding ceremony and received consent. The time for the ceremony was clearly stated as 1:00 PM, but until 3:00 PM, the groom, bride, other relatives, and friends anxiously awaited the teacher's arrival. Finally, the awaited pastor (牧師님) came strolling into the ceremony venue wearing the same clothes he wore while pulling weeds in his garden, his face and palms still smeared with dirt, saying, "I was finishing up some work, so I'm late. Well, let's start the ceremony." When I heard that, I reproached my friend, with whom I couldn't be closer. "Weren't there any pastors among our own people? Did you just swallow that humiliation?" Ah! Ah! Our people must also possess self-respect like those Japanese youths. Our leaders must also awaken. Our nation must truly understand what insult is. When standing before the public, attending any ceremony, or making visits, I will, within possible limits, wear appropriate clothing and keep my body clean. Wearing appropriate attire (義服), combing hair, and polishing shoes is not so much about respecting oneself as it is about respecting the public or friends one is meeting. The distinction between a cultured person and a barbarian lies in having or not having the right motivation.
The Western missionaries residing within the academy compound not only exerted effort for the students' sake but their earnest social fellowship and comforting advice were truly an uninterrupted realization of their faith. They held evening parties (夜會) once or twice each academic term, and I truly admired their power to influence students socially. At such times, Japanese or Western music was exchanged, and people performed freely according to their skills, aiming for perfect harmony. Missionary wives and daughters served tea and sweets (茶菓) and also played music (奏樂). Often, among the students, someone would recite poetry (詩吟). Sticking out a red leg, crossing both arms across the chest, <80> leaning back fully in a large chair, face contorted, eyes opening and closing, that posture while reciting poetry was truly magnificent. Yet, the graceful and solemn ladies (夫人들) sat calmly and listened attentively with an attitude expressing extreme respect. Every time I witnessed such scenes, I received divine inspiration descending from heaven. The vigor and spirit of self-respect of the Japanese youth, the generous attitude of admiration from the Westerners—which of these suddenly came and went in my heart? Those who serve must always have a generous attitude and a gentle disposition; those who walk alone must always possess strong vigor and a spirit of self-respect and self-confidence.

Chapter 3: The Crisis of Transition
Has the warm wind (薰風) blown? The tree leaves are pointy; is mist hanging? Ueno Park is in cherry blossom season (櫻花當節); has fine rain fallen? The grayish-yellow school grounds and fields seem to spread a green carpet (靑褥), renewing themselves morning and evening. Is not this transition and renewal of nature a process of evolution and creation, ceaselessly advancing step by step towards truth, goodness, and beauty?
"Ah! Ah! Pitiful is human growth! Ah! Ah! Desolate is the path of human life!" Sighing to myself, I took off my stained round hat (環形帽), held it in my hand, and received my graduation certificate (卒業 證書). More than the joy of having successfully completed years of study (硏學), I felt the relief of a prisoner receiving the order for release upon completing their sentence. Rather than the feeling of eagerly desiring a triumphant return home (錦衣還鄕) paired with youth, the spirit to tread on a sharp sword (利劒) and leap even over the sun boiled up in my chest. I sensed that a change in path, a crisis of growth, had arrived. Entangled in tears, wrapped in sorrow, I returned to my room. The pictures hanging on the four walls, the flowers and plants on the desk—all showed pale faces, adding to the gloomy atmosphere. If something had restrained me, forcing me to spend the long years of five years in a certain department as I had anticipated, I would have fainted. Truly, feeling that youth was precious, a feeling like that of a young woman unwilling to endure the hardships of marriage fiercely arose in my heart.
Through desperate arrangements (懸命的 周旋), I obtained a favorable opportunity for study travel (留學 旅行) to the United States of North America (北米合衆國). I returned temporarily to my hometown to bid farewell to my parents (堂上 兩位) and to see once more the scenery of my homeland with its high mountains and clear waters before departing.
As the second act of the stage of struggle (奮鬪 舞臺) was about to open, the two hundred-odd male and female students, with whom I had shared a deep teacher-student bond (師弟의 分誼) for several years, sang a farewell poem, like jewels, composed of the feelings of parting and the sincerity of hope. <81>

1
(Poem composed and sung by the children)
Parting with the meaning of parting,
What is this? Truly sad.
On the path our teacher takes,
Over two hundred students shed tears.

2
Oh, heartless train crossing that mountain,
Leave our teacher and go alone.
If we young ones were to follow,
Where would we leave our parents and siblings?

3
We pray and wish, O high Lord,
As he goes over mountains and waters,
Protect him with Your great, broad love,
Send him safely wherever he goes.

4
We pray and ask of you, Teacher,
Don't be sad leaving our young hands,
With a firm heart like the integrity of Boyi and Shuqi (伯夷叔齊),
Don't forget us even crossing the vast ocean (萬里大洋).

5
What shall we, like innocent children (赤子), do?
When we think, gazing at the vast ocean,
Unable to forget Teacher's great love,
We will pray, shed tears, and seek.

6
Our teacher crossing the great ocean,
Trusting in God's (한우님 - likely Hananim/God) protection, we pray.
After specializing at the high University of Ohio (오하요 - Ohio?),
Please rescue our Korean youth!

Chapter Three: Second Act of the Stage of Struggle
First Section: To the Long-Desired Destination
Infinite yearning was buried in the name America (米洲), and that continent where the Liberty Bell rang—that was the land of the Republic, the new America (新米國), which I had longed for for years. Now, holding a travel permit (旅行 免狀) that seemed like a single thread of survival, crossing the vast ocean (萬里의 大洋) to see the longed-for mountain scenery and waterscapes (山光水色), and to encounter its culture and civilization (人文 物化), the joy welling up in my heart was truly akin to leaping for joy (雀躍). However, undertaking penniless study travel (無錢 硏學 旅行) to a faraway land (萬里 外域) where faces are different and language is unfamiliar was indeed <82> a terrifying adventure among adventures.
The world situation was shaken by the European war (歐洲戰亂), and the winds and clouds in the Far East (極東) were also in a somewhat perilous state. The Japanese government had already declared war on Germany, and rumors circulated that the sea route between Yokohama (橫濱) and San Francisco (桑港) was unsafe. Therefore, for the reason that "Safety is paramount" (安全이 第一), the northern sea route was chosen.
In the small first-class cabin (no second class) of the *Nippon Maru* of the Osaka Shosen Kaisha (大阪 商船會社), docked at the pier (埠頭) in Yokohama harbor (橫濱港), which also served as the dining room and reception hall (食堂兼 應接室), various flower wreaths (화환) and fruits (果物) celebrating the passengers' departure were lined up. News reporters (新聞 記者) and farewell well-wishers of all ages and genders created a great commotion and hubbub (大雜沓과 喧譁). Along with a few friends, I arranged my luggage (行李) and sat gathered in a corner, observing the scene. Among them, a particularly noticeable young gentleman had a flower-like young woman near his knee, holding her sleeve to her mouth, shedding pearl-like tears, and speaking of secret matters in a way unlike others, a sight truly enviable and lovely.
I called the cabin manager (客室의 主任) to inquire about everything necessary to know during the voyage and asked about the number of passengers. He said there were about 13 passengers in total, including men, women, and children, but I was the only foreigner, a single Korean student (朝鮮 學生). After giving instructions to the boy (뽀이 - boy/steward) regarding luggage and other general matters, we went out onto the deck (甲板), looked out at Yokohama city shrouded in smoke and dust, listened to the clamor (喧㦊) of farewells from the third-class passengers, and while wishing each other good fortune for the future, pledging to share honor and disgrace together—as our heart-stirring conversation (情談) gradually deepened, the ship's whistle signaling anchor weighing (發錨의 汽笛) blew.
Ah! Ah! The moment! The heart-wrenching moment of parting!
Carried by good fortune, seizing the moment in the spring breeze, I was setting off to cross the watery realm dividing the two hemispheres, to become a life of the New World under the free heavens and sun. But my close friends (知己), with whom I had swung together in the pine forest behind the hill, swum together in the green waters of South Lake (南湖綠水), and walked together, sometimes ahead, sometimes behind, were trembling in a frightening environment like lambs trapped in a pen (牢柵), wilting in the dry air like greenhouse flowers (溫室의 花草). Several such friends, bitterly tasting that fate, stood on the pier shedding only tears. They waved handkerchiefs from afar as a signal. "Roh-kun! You've found a way to live... What shall we do! You, who have found the way to live, must save us!"
As the image of the white handkerchiefs waved by the friends sending me off from the Yokohama pier faded away, the *Nippon Maru* carrying me smoothly exited the harbor. There, water police officers (水上 警察官 나리님들) came aboard to search for stowaways (密航者). After anchoring temporarily for about two hours, the grand yet desolate sound of the steam whistle echoed, shattering the surrounding air, and the ship's bow turned north. <83>

**Summary**

This installment of Roh Chŏng-il's travelogue recounts his experiences as a Korean student in Japan (likely Tokyo/Edo) around the early 1920s and his subsequent departure for the United States. He reflects on humorous yet poignant incidents, like a dormitory sumo match that sparked his resolve not to be defeated, and contrasts the perceived lack of self-respect among Koreans (including missionaries' behavior towards them) with the fierce pride and sensitivity to etiquette observed in Japanese students. The narrative culminates with his emotional departure from Yokohama, leaving behind friends in colonized Korea while embarking on an uncertain but hopeful journey to America for further studies, choosing a northern sea route due to post-WWI tensions.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the mindset of a Korean intellectual navigating the complex cultural and political landscape of Japanese colonialism. His observations on Japanese self-respect versus perceived Korean submissiveness, the critique of Western missionaries' condescending behavior towards Koreans compared to their deference towards Japanese, and the intense emotional weight of leaving Korea for opportunities abroad vividly illustrate the psychological impact of colonial rule and the burgeoning nationalist consciousness among Korean students during this period. The mention of specific cultural practices (sumo, poetry recitation) and social interactions provides a textured glimpse into student life and intercultural encounters in early 20th-century Japan.

波瀾이 重疊한 生活의 洞窟에서 暗鬪가 極烈한 分秒의 動間에 안저서 琴을 彈하자는 一愚咄筆은 纏纏한 情緖를 실실이 뽑아내랴 할 대 雲集한 日常의 職務와 辛酸한 新環境의 不安頓한 事情으로부터 迫來하는 動搖와 苦悶을 痛切하게 感함니다. 따라서 所謂 世界 一周의 冒險 紀行도 諸氏의 滿足에 應할 만한 美文瓊章으로써 뵈여 들이지 못함을 甚히 遺憾으로 녁임니다. 寬厚한 諒解의 態度와 深奧한 同情을 가지시고 보아 주시기를 바람니다... (一愚)

第二節 滑稽의 神感
夕陽 山路에 쇠둥에 몸을 싯고 煙氣가 길길이 놉히 떠오르는 村落을 向하는 牧童은 버들닙흘 따서 피리부텨 凄凉하게 노래 한다. 自然에 醉하엿는지 寂寞에 느꼣는지 누가 그의 心情을 알 수가 잇스며, 알아 보랴고 하는 同情의 사람인들 잇스랴마는 피리 소래의 凄凉한 것은 凄凉한 事實로 듯는 사람의 性行과 事情을 應하야 神感으로 줄- 吹響力이 잇다. 「 世上 萬事 都付 一笑」라 한 俗言이 放浪的 樂天家의 妄言인지 모르거니와 엇잿던지 世上에 萬般의 事와 物이 思考力이 有한 者에게 하나이라도 무슨 敎訓이나 神感이나를 ＜76＞ 주지 안는 것이 업다.
이제 滑稽의 神感을 말하자 하매 一種의 神奇한 感을 意味한 듯하다. 새벽달 서리 찬 밤에 南飛하는 외기러기 울고 가니 그 소리 참 凄凉하나 잠 깁히 든 老幼에게야 무슨 感動, 무슨 意味, 아모 趣感도 업슬 터이나 鐵窓 藁席에서 呻吟하는 囚人에게는 蒼空에 自由飛하는 孤鴻의 서름 뭇지 안코 感歎의 淚를 뿌릴지요 空房에 獨宿하는 貴公主는 琉璃窓을 탁 열치고, 孤鴈의 서름 뭇기 前에 同情의 熱淚를 뿌릴 테라. 江戶에서 奮鬪하기 3有餘年 間에 어떤 瞬間이나 或 어떤 機會에나 接觸한 모든 일의 自體는 滑稽의 意味를 帶하엿스나 經驗하는 나에게는 痛切한 神感을 與할 때에 同情의 淚 보다도 嘆賞의 淚를 뿌리게 하얏스며 向上의 피를 끌케 하엿다. 有時乎 閑暇한 때에는 寄宿舍 學生들이 스모(씨름)를 遊戱的으로 하기도 하엿다. 왼 몸을 벌거케 벗고 수건으로 「사추리를 둘너 싸서 허리동이만 돌나매고 「자- 오너라. 해 보자」 氣를 내여서 左右 다리를 드럿다 노왓다 폇다 모왓다 야단 법석을 하고서는 소곰을 물고 뿌리며 물을 마시고 뿜어 비아트면서 두 다리를 벌려 집고 左右 팔과 주먹을 불끈 쥐고 「자- 오너라」 고함을 치자 씨름이 始作된다. 나는 한편 엽헤 놉달한 椅子에 從容히 안저 물끄럼이 傍觀한다. 應援聲을 놉히 부른다. 「자- 로君 오너라. 해 보쟈」 나도 본대로 임내를 낸다. 左右 다리도 上下로 動하며 兩팔에 筋肉도 가다듬고 소곰도 뿌리고 물도 마시며 야단을 친다. 「로君- 단단히 해라」 注意를 하여 준다. 「로君이 꽤 세찰 껄」 賞嘆도 해준다. 「자- 해 보쟈」달녀 든다. 爲先 힘 드리지 안코 귀마리를 탁 처서 팔삭 주저 안게 하엿다. 「참 朝鮮式이 奇麗하고나」 驚嘆올 한다. 「또 해보쟈」 달녀든다. 안갈 구리를 걸고 허리를 밧삭 잡아 꺽것다. 「아- 압흐다!」 고함을 치고 넘어간다. 「나와 해 보쟈- 로君이 무엇인가?」 다른 녀석이 달녀든다. 이 番은 좀 멋시 잇게 해 보리란 心筭으로 바른 다리를 냄큼 敵에 兩 다리 사이에 너흐면서 웅덩배기를 들고서는 왼팔노 적의 뒷더수구니를 칵 눌너서 열 십字로 메따려 뉘면서 「자- 너머간다」 고함을 벽력가티 질넛다. 아! 그때는 야단이 낫다. 朝鮮人에게 지는 것이 무엇이냐! 新日本人에게 저서는 안된다」 두 세 녀석이 한거번에 달녀든다. 나는 冷水를 흠벅 디려 마시고 「朝鮮人이 다 力士인 것을 未曾知냐. 댄 浪沙에서 벼락 친 鐵椎도 朝鮮族의 손바람이란다」 싸홈이 낫다. 죽일 듯이 달녀든다. 나는 더 하지 안켓단다.＜77＞ 敵軍은 애가 타서 그냥 하잔다. 나는 슬적 돌아서면서 「가서 工夫하야 하겟네. 工夫하러 온 學生이지 싸홈 하러온 軍人이 안일세!」 슬먹 슬먹 우물 념흐로 와서 冷水를 限 열암은 통 퍼서 끼여 언고 房으로 들어가면셔 回顧의 一句를 을펏다. 「爲人性癖耽佳句 語不驚人死不休」라 約 10年 前에 杜詩를 닐그면서 作亂 거리로 練習하엿든 「씨름」의 效力이 참 宏壯하고나 혼자 속소리를 하면서 房에 들어가서 네 활개를 활신 펴고 누워서 씨름 하든 始末의 光景을 감안히 생각하여 보왓다. 「씨름」을 始作할 때는 「로君- 단단히 차리게」하고 注意 하여 주든 녀석들이 終末에는 죽일 듯이 달녀 들엇것다. 아-하- 그럴테지- 短氣하기는 그 녀석들이 참 短氣하다 마는 他人에게 지기 스려 하는 心法은 怪하기는 하나 本 밧을 必要가 잇다. 좀 뙤지마는 이 環境에서 이 社會에셔, 禮儀之邦人의 特色으로 紳士의 態度와 體面만 차리다가는 목숨도 보전치 못하겟다. 나도 무슨 貌樣으로 엇던 努力으로던지 지지 안켓다. 이기여 나가야 하겟다. 奮鬪하자!」 스스로 말하면서 화닥닥 니러나서 冊床을 對하야 「工夫」를 하엿다. 그 뒤로는 恒常 그 光景과 經驗이 心頭에 來往하야 生活上에 大刺戟을 주엇다.
冬期 試驗을 終了하면 常例로 娛樂會가 열닌다. 娛樂會라 하면 「데깐쇼」와 「꼬랴 꼬랴」는 勿論이오, 쓴 차와 짠 煎餠도 먹는 法이다. 한번은 좀 高尙하게 人格 批評의 詩會가 열니엿다. 即 詩로써 同窓들의 性格을 말하자는 目的이다. 나와 同級生 中에 一 「詩人」이 웃둑 니러 셔셔, 兩 손을 左右 폭겨트에 집어 녓텨니 무슨 宏壯한 句나 을플 貌樣으로 기침도 두어 번 하고 임맛도 再三 다시더니 「負ケヌツつ 花ヵ 笑ク ロ-サン」 飜譯하면 「지기시린 얼골에 꼿이 핀다」 一節의 詞調를 나에게 주웟다. 그 一節의 意味가 나의 性格을 適切히 表現 하엿다고, 一場 大笑의 喝采를 博하엿다. 果然 그것이 나의 性格을 適切히 表現 하엿다 하면 그것은 全然히 씨름에서 밧은 感激의 實現이라고 하여도 過言이 아니다.
00學院은 西洋 宣敎師들의 經營하는 敎育 機關인데 其時에는 建築物이나 維持力이나 全部 西人의 努力이오 그 管理와 機關 運用은 日本人의 專主로 進行하여 가며 學院 構內에는 美麗한 西人들의 住宅이 만흐나 生活의 氣分은 日本化가 된 西洋人의 生活이 되엇다. 따라서 敎育의 目的에 在하여서도 西人의 雇用軍 養成이 아니요 徹頭徹尾하게 健全한 大和文化를 ＜78＞ 爲하야 日本 靑年을 敎育하며 日本 社會를 爲하야 完全한 公民과 引導者 養成이다. 往往히 西洋에 有力한 財産家, 學者, 宗敎家, 社會 運動者 等이 自己들의 金力과 人力을 投하야 經營하는 機關이니까 來訪하야 情況을 調査도 하며 自己들의 主義를 宣傳도 한다.
一日은 米國 00大學院長이 學院 大講堂에서 「日本人의 將來와 義務」란 問題로 演說을 하갯다고 學院의 各科 學生 全部를 大講堂에 聚集하엿는데 女學院의 各科 學生 全部도 出席하엿다. 때는 孟春이라 日氣는 甚히 凜凜한대 講堂 內에는 蒸氣의 溫度가 未洽하야 堂內의 溫度나 堂外의 溫度가 서로 差別이 업섯다. 正刻이 되매 니마가 훨신 버서진 대야머리의 軆軀가 肥大한 老齡의 博士가 登壇한다. 日氣가 甚히 치운대 兼하야 老人이라 調攝에 注意를 하려 함인지 外套를 닙은대로 그냥 서서 演說을 시작한다. 其 瞬間에 그 大講堂에 가득히 안젓든 學生 全部가 「失禮의 老爺」니 「侮辱을 한다」니 하야 수성 수성 벌의 소래가티 不快의 態度를 表現한다. 그 光景의 氣脈을 차린 一 西洋人이 急히 登壇하야 老博士의 귀에 暗然히 말하야 外套를 벗게 하엿다.
登壇한 老人은 自己의 身體의 調攝에 注意 하엿던지 貌樣 뵈기 爲하야 外套를 닙고 演說을 試 하랴 하엿던지 演士 自己에게 對한 個人의 問題가 아니엿고 演士의 衣服의 正不正은 即 聽衆에게 對하야 尊敬을 表하고 表하지 아니하는 데 잇다. 自敬心의 素養이 有한 日本 靑年들은 無作法 無敬意의 老人의 失禮를 寬容치 안을 氣慨를 自然히 猛烈히 表示하엿다.
나는 當場에 말할 수 업는 痛憤을 感하엿다. 더욱 今番 還國 以後로 나는 恒常 칼노 가슴을 찌르는 것 가티 압흐고 쓰린 苦痛을 感한다. 敎壇 우에서 거록한 날에 參拜하는 敎人들 앞헤 自己 집에서 일하면서 닙던 때 무든 두루막을 그냥 닙고 흙과 똥이 뭇은 구두를 그냥 신고 우둑허니 서서 傳道講演을 試하는 우리 朝鮮 선생님들과 外套를 입은 채로 두 손을 드러 祝福 祈禱를 밧드는 宣敎師 어룬들에게 向하야 7,8 百名 或은 1千 餘名의 參拜者 中에 뉘가 일즉 不快를 感하엿는가? 우리 朝鮮 선생님들께서는 아지 못하여서 그럴 지도 모르니까 寬大하게 容恕할 餘地가 잇다마는 敎育이 잇다 하고 文明한 人種이란 宣敎師 어룬들이야 아지 못한다 하면 宣敎師는 둘재치고 文明人이 아니요 萬一 文明人의 作法을 안다 하면 우리 民衆을 侮辱하는 것이 아니냐. 侮辱하는 것이 分明하다. 事實談을 하자니까 ＜79＞ 입이 써서 못 견듸겟다. 우리는 音樂을 愛하는 民族이라 往往히 東西 音樂會를  開催하고 西洋 音樂을 드르랴 할 때 西洋人 獨唱家의 作法을 보면 마음이 쏘와서 참 못 살네라. 입은 衣服은 自己 廚房에서 自己 妻와 가티 料理하면서 닙던 그 때 무든 衣服 그냥 입고 敢히 우리 압헤서 出演하니 音樂이냐 侮辱이냐 夜會服을 꼭 입지 안은 것이 不平이 아니다. 잇고도 안 입는 것이 무손 意味냐? 市內 日本人 主催의 音樂會에 出演할 때 입는 그 夜會服은 朝鮮人 압헤서는 더러울가 念慮하여서 안 입느냐? 우리를 侮辱한들 어찌 그와 가트랴! 日前에 어떤 親舊의 이악이를 드르니 참 분하엿다. 그 親舊가 約 2年 前에 百年佳綠을 매즐 때, 西洋 先生님을 崇拜하엿던지 尊敬하엿던지 自己의 婚禮式을 主張하여 달나고 한 宣敎師에게 請하야 許諾을 엇엇다. 禮式을 擧行할 時間은 午後 1時라고 分明히 말하엿섯는대 午後 3時까지 新郞과 新婦와 其他 親族과 朋黨들이 先生님 오시기만 苦待하엿다. 마츰내 期待하엿던 牧師님 께서는 自己 庭園에서 풀을 뽀부면서 입든 그 衣服을 그냥 입고 얼골과 손빠닥에는 흙이 뭇은 그대로 어정 어정 式場에 드러 오면서 말하는 수작은 「무슨 일 보던 것을 맛추고 오너라구 느저 젓소. 자- 禮式을 합시다」 나는 그 말을 드를 때 내 親舊하고는 또 더 親할 수 업는 友人을 責하엿다. 「우리 사람 中에는 牧師가 업더냐? 그 恥辱을 忍呑하엿느냐?」 아! 아! 우리 사람들도 뎌 日本 靑年들과 가티 自敬心을 가저야 하겟다. 우리의 引導者들도 覺醒해야 하겟다. 우리 民族들도 侮辱이 무엇인지 참으로 알아야 하겟다. 公衆 압헤 설 때나 무슨 禮式에 參席할 때나 訪問을 할 때 나는 될 수 잇는 範圍 안에서는 相當한 衣服을 着할 것이요 身體를 淨하게 할 것이라. 相當한 義服을 着함과 머리를 빗고 신을 닥가 신는 것은 自身을 尊重히 녁임 보다도 自己가 對할 公衆이나 親友를 尊重히 녁임이 아닌가. 그 動機를 바로 가지고 못 가지는데 文化人과 野蠻人의 別이 有하다.
