잡지명	개벽 제20호
발행일	1922년 02월 08일
기사제목	除夜(第一回) (New Year's Eve (Part 1))
필자	廉想涉 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
기사형태	소설 (Novel)

除夜 (New Year's Eve)
						廉想涉 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
一 (One)

The final moment fulfills the most important mission. And it announces the absolute end. Then, what remains behind? It is merely emptiness. Does not eternal peace, absolute liberation, pure truth, sanctity, and supreme good reside there, flowing from emptiness into emptiness?

But for what purpose am I writing this letter (片紙) now? I myself question it. I do not know what connection or relationship it has with the final conclusion, that the thought of writing this letter came to me in a fleeting moment (瞥眼間). While hesitating whether it is better to write or not to write, the tip of the brush still runs across the paper. Perhaps one might say that therein lies humanity, but it is still a laughable, meaningless act. Or perhaps one might say it is to elicit your sympathy through this letter, or to indicate that even I still possess a fragment of conscience capable of understanding and sympathizing with your feelings, but that too is not only meaningless but also something I definitely do not desire. What is meaningful today? Hasn't everything gone as far as it can go? Continuing this play any further would not only be laughable but also too cruel, wouldn't it? Firstly, for me, it is too severe a torture. Unless we can obliterate each other's memories, is there any other way? Isn't all that remains for us the wreckage of decaying memories? Understanding, sympathy, pity, anger, forgiveness... everything is meaningless, empty; we are merely forced to repeat conventional words (常套語) to each other. However, let us withdraw this condition of 'being forced' as much as possible. Let us just obey <35> as things unfold. At the very least, I am prepared to accept every lash that fate brings down, without refusal or defense. Seeing the unexpected letter you sent on the 24th, I became even more firmly resolved in this thought. After receiving that letter, I thought for three or four days and nights. And I don't know how many times I picked up the brush to write this reply. But finally, I am writing it. I don't know what or how I should write, but the thought that I must finish it by tomorrow night is clear in my head. It is said that Karl Marx (카-ㄹ, 마-쓰), while engrossed in writing Das Kapital, slumped over his desk and died peacefully, but how happy I would be if, while writing this letter, the first and last of my life, I could lay my cheek on this paper before dawn breaks and pass away just like that. But will such happiness come my way?

However, what I ask in advance is that after reading this letter, you must burn it right there. At the latest, it must not survive past tomorrow's sunset.

My life of 25 years has been a short yet rambling (支離) period during which fate, in order to make me write this letter today, sat holding the end of a brush in the soap suds called space, blowing the bubbles called time. Fickle fate, like a mischievous child (作亂 구럭이 兒孩) blowing soap bubbles, held the mouthpiece of a pipe dipped in soapy water and sat blowing one drop, two drops per second, ten drops, a hundred drops per minute, even a thousand, ten thousand drops, huff huff. And as the empty soap bubbles piled up, piling up and gradually swelling like a mountain heap (山ㅅ 뎀이), the mischievous child of fate, with a troubled heart, perhaps looked up at my peach-blossom-pink, radiant complexion, or perhaps my wax-like pale face, with a satisfied smile. Even now, I hear the sound of laughter, oo-hoo-hoo. — Truly, this was my half-life. But the time has finally arrived for me to write this letter. If I finish writing this letter and throw down the brush, in the next moment, from the lips forced open by laughter choking in my throat, I will likely let go of that brush-end. And that accumulation (堆積) of bubbles will vanish without a trace, forever swept away by the pressure of the air vibrated by the sound of laughter. But why, in general, is this mischief being done? Do people have the duty <36> to sit quietly and endure this meaningless and cruel mockery?

Essentially, what is life? I am not saying this to put on the airs of an inept philosopher, but truly, what is life? Is it the soap bubbles meant to satisfy the mischief of fate? — though even that is merely a term based on a dubious and imprecise concept (觀念). Then what is death? Does it refer to the momentary disappearance of the bubbles? What is emotion? What is love? What is procreation? What is God? What is morality? What is chastity? What is marriage? What is conscience? What is right and wrong? Good and evil? Sin? Atonement (贖罪)? ... And what is society, family, bloodline, destiny (緣分)?

Today, everything seems clear, yet also shrouded in a thick fog (五里霧中). Everything seems like an empty notion that can be disregarded, or the delirious talk (譫語) of a dreamer, yet also seems like an indispensable requirement for life. Lying still and meticulously examining, pondering, and comparing everything experienced so far, facts seen with my eyes, rumors heard with my ears, ultimately, it seems there is neither good nor evil, right seems not right, and wrong seems not wrong; there were even times I wanted to affirm everything as good and right. But then again,

"Everything is nothing more than toys made by a small child. What authority or meaning is there in them? Subjectivity is absolute. Isn't one's own subjectivity the only standard? If one's own subjectivity permits it, that's enough. What need is there to listen whether society says this or that, whether morality raises objections, whether God warns of destruction? The way worldly commoners (俗衆 雜輩), regardless of the matter's importance, act self-righteous about justice and whatnot, is ultimately like hiding in shelters called God, common purpose, society, state, etc., to conceal their own sins, thrusting out and waving a flag attached to a long bamboo pole over the wall. In this sense, they are, before anyone else, hypocrites (僞善者)."

When I thought like this, fueled by hatred and the heat of rebellion, wanting to deny, trample, and discard everything, I struggled alone, wanting to go out onto the Chongno (鍾路) street right now, give a grand speech, scrape off their masks, <37> and shout, "Let's carefully measure the distance between you and me."

Especially when Father looked askance at me with unusual eyes, there were many times I wanted to say just one word, "And you?...", but gritted my teeth and endured.

"However, even if ten million people are all hypocrites, hypocrisy will remain hypocrisy. If it's not the public way (公道), it's a sin. No matter how much one exposes the hypocrisy or sins of ten million people and mocks them as being 'fifty steps or a hundred steps' apart, it cannot serve as a single word of defense for one's own filthy sin, can it? Under any new society, any new morality, you surely cannot be tolerated." When I thought this, I cried in despair and pleaded for God's salvation. And my self-loathing reached such an extreme that my own body seemed a symbol of ugliness. I thought even dismembering my limbs and mincing my flesh bit by bit wouldn't be satisfying enough. When it felt like some foul odor emanated from my own body, I couldn't even look directly into the mirror (面鏡). There were many times when, after reflecting my face in the mirror and gazing at it intently, unable to bear the anguish and fury of despising and coldly scorning my own persona, I threw the mirror onto the floor. But then again,

"Essentially, who is there to cast a stone? What is sin? Degeneracy (墮落)? Is that just the name for the hood placed over a condemned prisoner on the scaffold, forced upon a young maiden yearning for free love?
But what about those who lament the so-called 'ways of the world and the hearts of men' (世道 人心)? — Taking a concubine under the pretext of preventing divorce. Courtesans (藝妓) under the fine names of socializing for businessmen, comfort for patriots (志士), human studies for third-rate writers (三文文士), aestheticism for artists. Doesn't the wrong path become the right path, degeneracy become social policy, a means of business, and good material for study? But do they have any reason to know that the most frightening thing is the fundamental degeneracy of human nature? At least I have the confidence that I haven't sold off the most beautiful part of human nature."

When I stepped forward, dismissing everything with a single phrase like this, I felt as if there was still light and hope for me, and I was brazen enough to stand tall, believing that no authority could lay a finger on my body. If you had heard such words, let alone sending such a letter, <38> you would probably have cursed me, saying, "Still haven't opened your eyes? Oh, audacious viper! May you fall on the blizzard-swept plains of Siberia (西伯利亞) and become food for magpies, you viper! The gates of Heaven must be eternally shut for you." But whatever you say, I have no objection. I have a duty to listen.

However, it was a fact that I sought even temporary solace through such self-defense. More than anything, you will know this if you see that even today, already four months after receiving that declaration—simple and mundane, yet like words from a chaplain guiding someone on the scaffold, flowing from blue lips that had been tightly sealed for a month, trembling convulsively—two lives are still stirring.

Ah, four months? It was a time when one life was inwardly condensing, and another life was expanding and growing outward with irresistible force. Great is the power of life. Don't they say life is tenacious? Situated between finite contraction and infinite expansion, enduring all sorts of agony, all sorts of severe punishment, I have lived for nearly half a year. Still, I managed to find and eat three meals a day at their proper times. When night came, I knew how to sleep. But is life this dark and gloomy? As the proverb goes, sins from a past life lead to a harsh fate in this life; I don't know if I'm undergoing this ordeal to pay for past sins or to atone for sins in this life.

The sun has set. The moon is still far from rising, and the multitude of stars are beautiful, but too distant, too small. The dove let out into the field (野) shivers in the cold frost. And the peacock, tethered by the neck, stamps its feet and wails bitterly.

Ah, four months that compressed the entire aspect of life and let me taste it all at once! I suspect I should have gone mad. Why does consciousness remain? Sensation? Conscience? Why does God still keep me alive? Is it grace? Punishment? ...

However, I never intended to die. It wasn't because I was a foolish believer in the superstition that suicide is a sin, nor because I couldn't overcome the attachment to life. Nor was it because I feared the surveillance of my family. I was, of course, aware that I was a splendid living corpse (生屍軆). But I gritted my teeth and resolved that I must <39> somehow live on.

"Death compensates for nothing. Death may cloud and distance people's memories, but that is too cowardly an act."

While thinking this, on the one hand, I took it upon myself that I had a great mission. Yes, a great mission. Revenge against myself, revenge-like success against one man, and rebellion, challenge, revenge against this society... this was it.

But suicide is cowardly. One must pay one's own debts. When I thought this, I was a purified warrior who had repented and realized the principle of atonement (贖罪), but when I plotted revenge against everything, I was a demon, grinding my teeth, trembling with fury like a wild beast. Not only that, but I thought my current situation was more than enough to atone for my past sins. Even just the torment and agony, like my heart being pierced through and through with the point of a needle, would surely earn God's forgiveness. At the very least, my own conscience could forgive me, I reasoned to myself. In any case, this painful life has continued until today. For whom, for what reason, I live so precariously, whether indeed for revenge or for the new bud of a life continuing its ceaseless expansion, I myself did not know.

However, that unexpected letter you sent on Christmas Eve gave me the wisdom, clarity, and determination to judge myself. The gospel delivered by a small white hand! It was the judgment text of the final verdict conveyed by an angel. It was the gospel of God that came from the lips of the Son of Man, heard only once on earth. Ah! At the same time, it was the cross that purified.

二 (Two)

The dark shadow surrounding us both is too gloomy and cruel (慘酷). Even if called the mischief of a troubled fate, it cannot but be considered excessively cruel and malicious torture; even if called God's providence, it cannot but be considered excessively tragic severe punishment. That such a trial was inflicted upon you, gentle and pure, and that we had to meet, cannot but be called a kind of ironical destiny (宿命). <40> Perhaps this might be called merely a coincidental event, but for a coincidence, it was one that reached the extreme of elaborate artifice.

First, it is an undeniable fact that our married life had a thorough defect (缺陷) from its very first step. The thorough sin of conventional marriage (因襲的 婚姻)—based on nothing more than the matchmaker's (媒婆) words, each other's photographs, and the advice of Mr. X, the manager of your company, without any other motive, means, or condition—which we, too, had to repeat as an heirloom (遺物) from our ancestors, was the first defect. Of course, this defect did not directly cause the disastrous breakdown (破綻) we face today, but as a defect, it was fundamental, and consequently, if we had thoroughly recognized, considered, and avoided this defect, there would have been no question of marrying me from the beginning. In this sense, the responsibility for this lies almost entirely with you.

Firstly, as a failure in married life who had tasted such bitter experience, you lacked reflection on the cause of that failure. Secondly, when seeking to heal (慰瘉) that wound (瘡疲) and obtain some measure of domestic happiness, you took the same means and form, which was your inescapable responsibility. Of course, being still unfamiliar with worldly affairs and, like ordinary Korean (朝鮮) youths, having no rebellious thoughts against conventional morality, besides being good-natured and upright, and also worldly, calculating, and passive, it is likely true that regarding the past breakdown, apart from opening your eyes in astonishment and swallowing tears of grief, you had no reflection or criticism. However, although I haven't heard the detailed story, didn't your former wife come into the family as a so-called child bride (밋메느리) and grow up together with you? Later, when both her parents passed away and she had no family home to rely on, you lived apart temporarily until your studies in Tokyo (東京) were finished, and then you married. But by then, wasn't she no longer the lovely dove seeking solace in your arms, but rather a peacock cursed by the profligate son of an aristocrat? If this is true, how could you remain completely indifferent, without a shred of reflection on that defect? It is suspicious no matter how I think about it. Moreover, didn't you say you spared no affection (寵愛) for her?

If you grew up together under the same parental roof for nearly ten years like siblings, there must have certainly been sibling-like affection. As you both became aware and gradually understood what marriage meant, mutual desires and hopes would arise, and consequently, in light of those desires <41> or hopes, you would have examined and understood each other as partners. Thus, weren't you able to permit ardent love? Yet, didn't she betray (背反) you? Not only had she already lost her chastity and rebelled against you during the temporary separation, but although she fulfilled the marriage promise out of obligation, didn't she ultimately deceive (騙詐) you for her lover and finally abandon you?

Then what was the main reason? Of course, there might have been various reasons. One could speculate she had a prostitute-like character, or points like the aristocrat's prestige or the man's appearance, or since finances were reportedly not as good then as now, such material issues might certainly have been involved. But the most powerful factor would be love. If her love for you had been stronger than that for the aristocrat's son—since there are rumors she separated from him shortly after, it seems true she had no love for the aristocrat either, so perhaps it shouldn't be compared to that love—she wouldn't have been able to betray you so easily. Looking at it this way, it goes without saying that the lack of any mental, that is, internal understanding, prevented finding a point of resonance and accord in personality and a line of connection in soul, and consequently, the result of true love not existing, the lack of love, declared the breakdown; isn't this self-evident? If so, the failure of the first marriage could rather be called natural, and even if it had continued, it would have been merely a dangerous and fragile union possessing the possibility of destruction or separation at any time.

Thus, as a result of lacking reflection and criticism regarding the true cause of that breakdown, you entered into another conventional marriage with me through the same means, only to achieve a second breakdown. When you could have resolved things most easily and smoothly and enjoyed lifelong happiness according to the means available, the shackles (桎梏) of so-called conventional old morality stuck like octopus tentacles in your brain, causing you to repeat a breakdown that could have been fully avoided.

Your position today, in fact, makes you happier than any other young man in present-day Chosŏn, in that you fully possess the conditions to practice free marriage and free love. And this free marriage based on free love is not just rational and advantageous in theory; to recover from the mental blow you suffered at that time, and as long as you desired remarriage, <42> there would have been no means other than seeking a fully qualified spouse based on love. But you lacked that much courage.

Moreover, there seems to be an unheard-of rumor these days that the first wife was let go because threatening with the signboard of being the leftover of a so-called aristocrat was bothersome. If this were true, putting aside the right or wrong of the matter, doesn't it show how much you lacked confrontational power and had no reflection on slave morality (奴隷 道德)? Therefore, even if you could agree with free marriage in theory, I can guess that you lacked the spirit to take an active stance.

Truly, herein lies the weak point in your character, and consequently, isn't it that a pitiful tragedy keeps the tears of the lonely and weak you from drying? The virtue of silent endurance (忍黙), not blaming or detaining the rebellious one, respecting their free will, thereby allowing them to freely choose the person they wish to love, might be the true way of loving them and a wise method, but more crucial than that is becoming strong oneself. Unless one first becomes strong, even such a sublime moral motive will unexpectedly produce the exact opposite result. I apologize for saying such disrespectful things, but since these are my final words, you will forgive and listen, won't you?

However, regarding this, some might pass this judgment: "Ultimately, it's your fault. Isn't it your immoral (悖德) behavior, you malicious hypocrite, that induced today's breakdown?" Of course, looking only at the result today, the direct fatal wound would be that fact, dreadful even to mention. However, the cause that forced me, who was in such desperate straits at the time, to resort to such hypocritical means, wasn't it also the conventional marriage system you adopted, and Father's so-called abuse of patriarchal authority (家長權) and tyrannical pressure? Furthermore, even if that fact hadn't existed, could our married life have been guaranteed smooth sailing? I avoid a clear answer to this.

In any case, you seem to have been almost captivated by the matchmaker's sweet words, my somewhat beautiful appearance, my certain reputation known in the world, and my modicum of knowledge. But ultimately, what did you gain? Wasn't it a lump of maggot-infested meat that must be thrown away <43> immediately? Of course, considering that you were remarrying, you must have had various calculations. The most suitable thing for your requirements was perhaps that I was the child of a concubine. Whether I was a concubine's child or an illegitimate child, such things were not of particular concern to me at the time, but to you, whose head was deeply ingrained with old conventional notions, you must have thought that was my weakness, while at the same time thinking that consenting to marry such a person was a great concession and generous act on your part. Besides that, there's the saying that a woman's 21st year is like her 60th birthday (回甲), but I was already 25, the same age as you; the reputation in student circles abroad or in Seoul society, regardless of the truth about my conduct, was not particularly good; and the fact that in today's Chosŏn, it's extremely difficult for a woman of 25 to marry a suitable young man, etc., must have conversely been advantageous conditions for you. This wasn't due to any malice on your part, but rather, based on past experience and calculation, a person of your character understood well that to dominate a woman, it was more advantageous to grasp her weaknesses rather than rely on one's own strength.

Thus, like a man in love, you seem to have become quite infatuated, yearned, and made various clandestine moves. Moreover, hearing the rumor that I strongly opposed from the beginning, you must have felt greatly insulted, and also seemed quite agitated like someone suffering from unrequited love, which can be seen from the conditions you proposed. Among them, the most advantageous for me was that upon marriage, you would immediately send me to Tokyo to study further at a women's university. However, becoming impatient as things didn't easily proceed, didn't you make Mr. X, the manager of your company, roll up his sleeves and step in? Whether you did so knowing that my father couldn't disregard Mr. X's words, or for whatever reason, bringing Mr. X into it was a great success. Father, who initially refused, saying you had no wealth, suddenly changed his attitude and proceeded with the matter autocratically, almost coercively, barely receiving my consent (?) or not receiving it at all, all due to the efforts of Mr. X, who was a friend of the former wife's father and had introduced her as a child bride. But who knew that everything was rushing headlong towards the final conclusion point? Clutching my seemingly breaking heart, fidgeting <44> and agonizing, was only me. Thinking about it, this Mr. X might indeed be the incarnation of fate, destined to follow and dominate your entire life. In any case, thus the second ruin gnawed at your weak heart.

However, I certainly do not intend to shift the entire responsibility for this breakdown solely onto you. Although I might bear some responsibility for the fundamental error and defect, regarding the point of directly lighting the fuse, I do not intend to evade the full responsibility one bit. Facing today, we are prepared to accept甘受 any moral judgment, any accusation or sentence without a single word of resistance, rebuttal, or explanation.

You—not just you, but anyone in such a situation—would likely first rebuke by saying, "As a new woman belonging to the so-called intellectual class..." and so on, or, "Having lived a life of faith for over 10 years and held a teaching position..." That would be typical. And, "If you had even a speck of conscience left, you would have resisted resolutely, even unto death. In the sense of yielding to conventional marriage, you lost the value of a new woman, and in the sense of exploiting marriage and deceiving your partner and those around them, you are an enemy of humanity (人道), deserving of heavenly punishment (天誅)." This would be the central point of the accusation. Indeed, it is most fitting.

However, there were circumstances here that theory alone cannot resolve. Moreover, if one probes its source, one cannot overlook the fact that a strange power, which even the firmest force of conscience could not control, was constantly at work.
It is this: a soul similar to the so-called "soul of the Karamazov family" in "The Brothers Karamazov" has been passed down through generations in our Choe (崔) family.

Even today, I do not intend to commit the sin of filial impiety again by boldly and rudely exposing my parents' flaws, but it is an undeniable fact that my life began its first step of germination from an illicit (不倫) union, and the nutrients that nurtured that life's young bud were the scent of flesh and the green wine (綠酒) of pleasure. <45>

It's shameful to mention, but needless to say about my grandfather, my father's extraordinary vigor most accurately proves him to be his father's son. Even now, approaching 60, he has two young mistresses (小室). Among them, it's said, is even a former young female student who could pass for his own granddaughter. However, the one called my mother was certainly not a virtuous woman either. For me, her child, to utter such words is a bone-chilling shame (羞恥) and, on the other hand, a sin. But since I feel I have a duty to tell you everything without omission, please forgive me, read on, and for the sake of my living parents, I trust you will keep this confidential.

In any case, you must not forget the fact that I am her daughter. Indeed, I was a symbol of injustice, kneaded between a father who was a bedrock of flesh and a mother who had a strange interest and habit regarding immoral, especially sexual, clandestine affairs. I was the child of karma cursed by the flesh. Ah—I am an illegitimate child (私生兒). Resolved to commit the final sin once more, I cry out one phrase: I am a cruel lump of flesh made by an adulterer and adulteress. Indeed, until today, I have gritted my teeth and tried to shed this disgraceful name (陋名). But what form does this take? The more I tried to shed the disgraceful name, the darker and gloomier my path became. The loathsome blood of the Choe family! Ah! The soul of the Choe family, playing beneath the thin skin of a young woman, whispers ceaselessly. Perhaps I representatively embodied all the characteristics and, simultaneously, flaws of the Choe family.

But it wasn't just that. The rich colors familiar to my eyes from childhood made my emotions mature earlier than my constitution. In other words, the crucial factor that cannot be overlooked is that the atmosphere of the home was precisely my atmosphere. If you imagine what kind of home it must have been, centered around a young woman who left her husband, 15 or 16 years old, 6 or 7 years her junior, you can probably guess the general picture. Our house was a sort of hangout for a group of aunts (아즈머님) and a place for young uncles' nightly visits. Not only that, but there was a kind of tacit agreement (墨契) in our house—that is, without jealousy, conflict, or restraint, in other words, without sacrificing mutual happiness and feelings or monitoring each other's actions, each person's particular pathological pleasure was unconditionally guaranteed. <46> Therefore, in such a society, being chaste (貞操) was meaningless, not even worth a bundle for three pennies. It became an unconscious notion that chastity was ultimately the golden rule of impotent Confucian scholars (道學生員) or the foolish jealousy of a fool trying to control the indifference of the other party when affection still lingered on one side.

Thanks to that, my behavior from childhood had complete freedom (?), without a shred of supervision or guidance. If I hadn't persistently gone to school, my fate would likely have been fixed on one path. Isn't it logical that a mother, harboring the strange regret of not having spent her youth in the splendid world of flowers and willows (花柳界), would have sought absolute control over my fate, if only to alleviate anxiety about her livelihood in old age? In fact, if Father hadn't continued to look after us, mother and daughter, my fate would likely have changed, at the latest, the year I graduated from girls' school. However, the reason Father started interfering somewhat recently wasn't for any particular reason. It was merely because he provided some money, and now, presenting me as his daughter wouldn't be too shameful, plus, having entered old age, he had come to his senses a bit. That's why he was enthusiastic about this marriage too, but it was a fact that this final interference and supervision plunged me into misfortune far worse than the misfortune induced by the neglect (放任) of my childhood and adolescence.

Indeed, just as the saying goes that wit emerges only when it's not needed, life's tragic events are staged everywhere because necessary intervention and guidance are neglected when indispensable, while extreme interference, guidance, and slavish obedience are demanded unconditionally when not only unnecessary but actually a cause of misfortune and harm. Do you know how many young men and women weep in misfortune due to the lack of necessary parental interference and guidance, past and present? Conversely, do you know how many parents cause their children to suffer and die in tragic circumstances due to unnecessary supervision and interference? But parents in the world still do not realize. You can see it just by looking at our parents right now. Truly, I am a worthless sacrifice who has received both these misfortunes, but my parents have no reflection whatsoever on this.

Anyway, freedom exceeding the ability to control oneself, along with various <47> circumstances such as the surrounding dense atmosphere and the early maturation of emotions, drove me towards a certain point. I graduated from xx Girls' School at the age of 18, but by then, I was already not an innocent maiden. Carrying a strand of the Choe family soul in my mouth, embracing a heart full of wonder, curiosity, joy, hope, and expectation, I emerged, a maiden burning with the desire to see everything, know everything, obtain everything—if you think that such a girl, just weaned from her mother's nipple, seemingly ready to burst if touched, would have been left alone carelessly in the capital city of Seoul or among the Tokyo student circles, then the thinker is mistaken.

In fact, everywhere, I discovered myself as the queen. The conceit that there was no man who did not obey my slightest gesture became firmly nailed down before long. My unrestrained passion knew no bounds. But I could not adhere to and sustain just one love affair. From red lips to red lips! Rather, I desired that. But starting last year, I began to grow weary of even that. Listing words fixed like articles in the legal code (六法全書), talking about love, romance—I began to feel nauseated by it. Love like blood! Blood, fire, death... I tried to picture such dark, bitter, strong love in my imagination. Rather than trifling with the feeble love of weak and base men whom a woman could manipulate at will, I wanted to capture a man with strong pride, fiery passion, and human suffering like death and blood, completely indifferent to both love and fame, and this time, love him to death from my side. Love that cannot be turned away from despite rejection, insult, and being trampled! Love without which one cannot live! In short, I tried to proactively and aggressively obtain love more valuable than death. It was not a plea to be loved, but a desire (慾望) to love. But that was fantasy. It was a kind of reactive phenomenon to the dissipated life I had continued until today. Even if I had the chance to obtain such a man, failure was certain. In fact, I failed. And what I obtained instead of such desire was only the impulse of intense sexual desire, stronger than anything experienced before, flaring up like fire... Thus, I entered the second wheel of destruction (滅落).

But even amidst that, I did not abandon religion. I too estimate <48> that this point becomes the focus of further attacks against me. I knew there were criticisms, such as it being protective coloration to conceal my ugly conduct, or the general bad reputation of new women who supposedly just attend gatherings to meet young men. Of course, mostly I attended because it was pleasant to be at gatherings where crowds gathered, especially where young men and women teemed, and also due to the responsibility and enjoyment of leading the choir; whether in Japan or Korea (朝鮮), I always went to church. But occasionally, there were times when I genuinely called upon God, thinking I should enter into a life of faith. However, it wasn't because I reflected on my actions or repented. In a way, perhaps God and Jesus Christ were also, for me, lovers in a mundane sense. I never needed to call upon God unless it was after abandoning all worldly lovers or after sobering up from indulgence in pleasure, as a substitute.

Nevertheless, I managed to graduate from school with relatively good grades. However, regarding my desire to study further in the field of literature, which I had started midway, permission was ultimately not granted. Therefore, I had no choice but to abandon my six years of Tokyo life in March of this year, return to Korea, and decide to take up teaching at my alma mater, somehow. Of course, regarding this employment too, I had no reflection or concern. Only the assignment to teach ethics (修身) in the class I was in charge of was a bit of a headache.

However, upon returning home, I found many difficult problems awaiting me. Among them, the primary obstacle was the marriage issue. Although I had resolved from the beginning that I must continue to struggle, it didn't seem likely to be settled so easily.

But a second force, constantly tempting and pressuring me, began to follow me around. <49>

**Summary**

The text is the first part of a fictional story presented as a long, confessional letter written by a 25-year-old Korean woman on New Year's Eve to a man, presumably her estranged husband. She reflects on her life, marked by a troubled family background (illegitimacy, parental promiscuity, lack of guidance), her own complex emotional and romantic history, and a disastrous conventional marriage arranged despite her initial objections. The letter delves into themes of fate, societal hypocrisy, the failures of traditional marriage, personal responsibility, sin, atonement, and despair, culminating in a state of crisis apparently triggered by a recent letter from the recipient. She struggles with self-loathing, justifies her past actions, critiques the societal norms and the specific individuals (including the recipient and her father) she believes contributed to her predicament, and contemplates the meaning of life and death, hinting at a tragic resolution.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the social and psychological landscape of colonial Korea in the early 1920s. It vividly portrays the struggles of a "new woman" caught between traditional expectations (arranged marriage, patriarchal authority) and emerging modern ideas (free love, individual desire, education). The candid discussion of female sexuality, family dysfunction (including illegitimacy and parental behavior considered scandalous), criticism of conventional marriage practices, and the protagonist's internal conflict regarding morality, religion, and societal judgment provide a window into the personal and social tensions of the era. Historians might find the text significant for its representation of female consciousness, its critique of patriarchal structures and societal hypocrisy (particularly regarding sexual mores), and its exploration of themes like fate versus free will ("崔哥의 피" - Choe family blood) within the specific context of early modern Korean society under Japanese rule.

除夜
						廉想涉
一
最後의 瞬間은, 가장 重大한 使命을 遂行합니다. 그리고 絶對的 終結을 고합니다. 그러면 그 뒤에 남는 것은, 무엇일가요. 다만 空이 올시다. 空으로 부텨 空에 흘러가는 거긔에 永遠한 安住가 잇고, 絶對的 解脫이 잇고, 眞純이 잇고, 神聖이 잇고, 至善이 잇지 안은가 하나이다.
그러나 只今 이 片紙는, 무슨 必要로 쓰랴는가. 自己도 疑問이올시다. 最後의 結末과 무슨 連絡이 잇고 關係가 잇기에, 이 片紙를 쓰랴는 생각이 瞥眼間에 낫는지 모르겟습니다. 써야 조흘지 쓰지 안어야 조흘지, 망서리면서도, 亦是 붓 끗은 紙面 우로 다라나 갑니다. 或은 그러한 대에, 오히려 人間味가 잇는 것이라고 할지도 모르겟스나, 亦是 우스운 無意味한 일이외다. 또는 이 편지로 말미암아, 貴君의 同情을 사랴거나, 或은 나에게도, 아즉 貴君의 心情을 理解하고 同情하야 드릴 만한 良心의 片影이 남아 잇다는 것을 表示하랴 함이라 할지도 모르나, 그 亦 無意味 한 일일 뿐 아니라, 나의 決코 願하는 바가 아니외다. 오늘날 와서 무엇이 意味잇습니까. 모든 것이 갈데 까지 가지 안핫습니까. 이에서 더 演劇을 繼續하는 것은, 우슴거리도 되려니와, 넘우 慘酷한 일이 아니오니까. 第一에 나에게는 넘우 甚한 酷刑이외다. 彼此의 記憶力이나 撲滅시키기 전에는, 아모 수단도 업지 안습니까. 우리에게 남은 것은 오즉 썩어 문드러저 가는 記憶의 殘骸뿐이 아니오니까. 理解도, 同情도 憐憫도 憤怒도, 容恕도... 一切가 無意味한, 空虛한, 常套어를 不得已하야 彼此에 反復함에 不過 하지 안습니다. 그러나 우리는 될 수 잇는 대로, 이 不得已하야 라는 條件을 撤回하십시다. 우리는 오즉 되어 가는 ＜35＞ 대로 順從하십시다. 적어도, 나는, 運命이 나리우는 채찍의 낫낫을, 하나도 拒絶하지 안코 防禦하지 안코, 바들 覺悟입니다. 24日에 주신 意外의 글월을 뵈옵고, 더욱이 이 생각을 굿게 決心하게 되엇습니다. 그 편지를 바든 後, 3,4晝夜를 생각하얏습니다. 그리고 이 答狀을 쓰랴고 몃 번이나 붓을 들엇던지 모르겟습니다. 그러나 듸디어 쓰게 되 엇습니다. 무엇을 어떠케 써야 조흘지는 모릅니다마는, 來日 밤 안으로 끗을 내야 하겟다는 생각만은 머리 속에 明瞭합니다. 「카-ㄹ, 마-쓰」는 資本論에 執筆하다가, 書案에 업드러저 永眠 하얏다 하오나, 나는 나의 一生에 最初요, 最後인 이 片紙를 쓰다가, 새벽녁 東이 트기 전에 이 조희에 뺨을 대이고 그대로 絶命하얏스면 얼마나 幸福일지 모르겟습니다. 하지만 나에게 그러한 幸福이 차례에 오겟습니까.
그러나 미리 付託하야 두옵는 바는, 이 片紙를 보신 後에 그 자리에서 반듯이 태여 줍시사 함이외다. 느저도 來日 해를 넘기지 마라 주서야 하겟습니다.

25年 間의 나의 生涯는, 運命이, 오늘날 나에게 이 片紙를 씨우기 위하야 空間이라는 왜 비누물에, 붓두겁을 박아 물고 안저서, 時間이라는 거픔을 불고 잇던, 짧으나 支離한 歲月이엇습니다. 實 업슨 運命은 作亂 구럭이 兒孩가, 비누 물방울을 나리우듯이, 대ㅅ개피 물부리를 비누 물에다 박아 물고, 一秒에 한 방울 두 방울, 一分에 열 방울 百 방울, 乃至 千방울 萬 방울 훅훅 불고 안젓습니다. 그리하야 空虛한 비누물 방울이 쌔우고 쌔워 漸漸 山ㅅ 뎀이 가티 부러 올라갈스록, 心事 사나운 作亂軍의 運命兒는, 或은 나의 紅桃花色의 艶艶한 顔色을, 或은 蠟燭 가튼 蒼白色의 얼굴을, 滿足한 微笑를 띄우며 치어다 보고 안젓섯습니다. 只今도, 우후후, 하며 웃는 소리가 들립니다.― 實로 이것이 나의 半生이엇습니다.  그러나 나에게는 듸디어 이 편지를 쓸 時間이 當到하야 왓습니다. 萬一 이 片紙를 다- 쓰고 붓대를 던지고 나면, 그 다음 瞬間에는, 목이 메여 터져 나오는 우슴에, 못 이기어 열리는 입슐로부터, 그 붓두겁을 떼여 버리겟지요. 그리고 그 거픔의 堆積은 우슴소리에 波動 되는 空氣의 壓力으로, 痕跡도 업시 永遠히 슬어저 버리겟지요. 그러나 이것이 大體 무슨 까닭으로 하는 作亂인가요. 사람에게는 이 無意味하고, 慘酷한 이 愚弄을, 가만히 안저서 바다야만 할 義務가 잇는＜36＞ 것입니까?
大抵 生이란 무엇입니까. 空然히 哲人의 서투른 입내를 내느라고 하는 말슴이 아니라, 果然 生이란 무엇입니까. 運命? ― 그것도 疑心나는 不正確한 觀念에 不過한 술어이지만, ―의 作亂을 滿足시키랴는 비누물의 거픔입니까. 그러면 死란 무엇입니까. 거픔의 瞬間的 消滅을 이름입니까. 感情이란 무엇입니까. 戀愛란 무엇입니까. 生殖이란 무엇입니까. 神이란 무엇입니까. 道德이란 무엇입니까. 貞操란 무엇입니까. 結婚이란 무엇입니까. 良心이란 무엇입니까. 正邪란? 善惡이란? 罪란? 贖罪란? ... 그리고 社會란, 家庭이란, 血統이란, 緣分이란 무엇입니까?
오늘날 와서는 모든 것이 明瞭한 것 갓기도 하고, 亦是 五里霧中에 싸인 것 갓기도 합니다. 모든 것이 무시 할 수 잇는 空虛한 關念이나, 空想家의 譫語 갓기도 하고, 업스면 안 될 生活의 要件 갓기도 합니다. 가만히 누어서 오늘까지 經驗한 것, 눈으로 본 事實, 귀로 들은 所聞 들을, 낫나치 硏究하야 보고, 吟味 하야보고 比較 하야 보면 結局 善도 업고, 惡도 업고, 正도 正이 아닌 것 갓고, 邪도 邪가 아닌 것 가틀 뿐 아니라, 모든 것을 善이라 하고 正이라 하야, 肯定하랴고 할 때도 잇섯습니다. 그러나 또 한 便으로는,
「모든 것이 어린 兒孩가, 만들어 노흔 玩具에 不過하다. 거긔에 무슨 權威가 잇고, 意味가 잇느냐. 主觀은 絶對다. 自己의 主觀만이, 唯一의 標準이 아니냐. 自己의 主觀이 容許하면 고만이다. 社會가 무엇이라 하던지 道德이 무엇이라고 抗議를 提出하던지, 神이, 滅亡하리라고 警告를 하던지 귀를 기울일 必要가 어대 잇느냐. 世間의 俗衆 雜輩가 일의 大小를 莫論하고, 正義니 무엇이니 하며, 혼자 잘난체 하는 것은, 結局 自己의 罪科를 隱蔽하기 爲하야 所謂 神이니 共同 目的이니 社會니 國家니 하는 等 避難處에 숨어서, 기다란 대ㅅ 개피에 매어달은 旗발을, 墻 外에 내여 밀고 휘두르는 것 가튼 것이다. 이러한 意味로, 彼等은 누구보다도 먼저 僞善者이다」
이가티 생각할 제, 나는 一切를 否定하고, 蹂躪하고, 睡棄하랴는 憎惡의 念과 反抗의 熱에 떼워서, 혼자 몸부림을 하며, 今時로 鍾路 바닥에 나가서 大演說이라도 하야, 彼等의 假面을 박박 긁어 버리고, ＜37＞
「너와 나의 距離가, 얼마나 되는가 仔細히 재여 보자」고 부르짓고 십헛습니다.
더욱이 아버님께서 異常한 눈으로 흘겨 보실 際,
「당신은?...」 하며, 한마듸하고 십흔 것을, 이를 악물고 참은 때도 만핫습니다.
「그러나 千萬人이, 다- 僞善者라 하더래도, 僞善은 僞善이고 말 것이다. 公道가 아닌 以上 罪惡이다. 千萬人의 僞善이나 罪惡을 暴露하고, 50步 100步라고, 얼마나 嘲笑한 들, 自己의 더럽은 罪惡에는 一言의 辯護도 될 理가 업지 안흔가. 어떠한 새 社會, 어떠한 새 道德 알에서라도 너는 到底히 容納 될 수 업스리라」고 생각할 際는 絶望에 울며, 神의 救援을 哀願 하얏습니다. 그리고 自己 憎惡가 極하야 몸둥아리까지 醜惡의 象徵으로 보엿습니다. 四肢를 발기고, 살을 點點히 점여도, 시원치 안타고도 생각하얏습니다. 自己 몸에서 무슨 醜惡한 냄새가 나는 것 가튼 때에는 面鏡을 바로 볼 수도 업섯습니다. 面鏡에 自己 얼골을 비추어 노코 가만히 드려다 보다가, 自己의 人格을 自己 自身이 輕蔑하고 冷罵하지 안흐면, 自己의 容貌도 볼 수 업는 若痛과 憤怒에 못 이기어, 面鏡을 房바닥에다 내 던진 때도 만핫습니다. 그리하다가도,
「大體, 돌을 던질 者가 누구냐? 무엇이 罪냐. 墮落? 그것은 自由 戀愛를 渴望 하는 어린 處女에게만 씨우는 絞首臺 上의 死刑囚의 覆面巾을 이름이냐.
그러나 所謂 世道 人心을 慨歎한다는 彼等은, 어떠하냐?― 作妾은 離婚 防止하는 名目 下에. 藝妓는 實業家의 社交, 志士의 慰安, 三文文士의 人間學硏究, 藝術家의 耽美라는 美名下에, 非道는 正道가 되고, 墮落은 社會 政策이요, 事業의 手段이요, 學問의 好材料가 되지 안는가. 그러나 가장 두려운 것은, 人間性의 根本的 墮落인 것을, 厥者들이 알 까닭이 잇느냐. 적어도 나는 人間性의 第一 아름답은 部分만은 팔아먹지 안핫다는 自信이 잇다」
이 가티 모든 것을, 一言而蹴하고 나설 때에는 나에게도 아즉 光明이 잇고 希望이 잇고, 어떠한 權威도 손가락 하나 自己 몸에 건듸리지 못하리라고, 悠悠히 버틔고 나설 만치 뻔뻔 하얏습니다. 萬一 貴君이, 이러한 말슴을 들으셧드면, 그런 片紙를 주시기는 姑捨하고,＜38＞
「그래도 아즉 눈을 못 떳느냐. 오! 大膽한 毒婦야. 눈보라 치는 西伯利亞 벌판에 걱구러 저서, 까치 밥이나 될, 요 毒婦야! 天堂의 門은, 너를 爲하야 永遠히 다치엇스리라」 고 詛呪 하셧겟지요. 그러나 무엇이라 하시던지 不服은 업습니다. 듯지 안으면 안 될 義務가 잇습니다.
그러나 그러한 自家 辯護로, 一時的 慰安이라도 어드랴고 한 것은, 事實이엇습니다. 무엇보다도, 그날 그 宣言― 簡單하고 平凡하나 마치 斷頭臺에 올라선 者에게 引導를 주는, 敎誨師의 입에서 나오는 것 가티, 1個月 동안이나 꼭 담으럿던, 푸른 입슐이 痙攣的으로 떨리면서, 흘러나오던 그 宣言을 바든 뒤로, 별서 4個月이나 되는 오늘날까지 두 生命이, 아즉도 움즈럭 어리고 잇는 것을 보시면, 아실 것이외다.
아!, 4個月 동안? 한 生命은 內面的으로 凝縮하야 가고 한 生命은 外部로 向하야 不可抗拒의 勢力으로 伸張하고 擴大하야 나오든 時間이엇습니다. 偉大한 것은 生命의 힘이외다. 모진 것은 목숨이라 하지 안습니까. 有限한 縮小와 無限한 擴大 사이에 介在하야, 가진 苦悶, 가진 惡刑을 다- 바드면서도 近 半年을 살아 왓습니다. 그래도 세끼 밥은 제 때에 차저 먹엇습니다. 밤이 되면 잘 줄도 알앗습니다. 그러나 人生이란 이러케 컴컴하고 구중중한 것입니까. 俗談에 前生에 罪가 만하, 今生 八字가 奇薄하다더니, 前生의 罪갑을 하느라고 이러틋 단련을 하는지, 今生의 罪 땜을 하느라고 그런지는 모르겟습니다.
해는 떨어젓습니다. 달은 뜨기 아즉 멀고, 뭇 별은 아름다우나, 넘우도 멀고, 넘우도 작습니다. 들(野)에 내노핫던 비둘기는, 찬서리에 떨음니다. 그리고 목을 매어 노흔 孔雀은, 발을 굴으며 痛哭합니다.
아-, 人生의 全局面을 縮小하야다가, 一時에 맛보게 한 4個月 동안! 내가, 왜 아니 미첫던가 疑心 남니다. 어쨰 意識이 남아 잇는가. 感覺이 남아 잇는가. 良心이 남아 잇는가. 어찌하야 한우님을, 아즉 것 살려 두시나? 恩寵인가. 刑罰인가...
그러나 나는, 決코 죽으랴고는 아니 하얏습니다. 自殺은 罪惡이라는, 어리석은 迷信의 徒이기 때문이거나 生에 對한 執着力을 이기지 못하야 그러한 것도 아니엇습니다. 또는 집안의 監視가 무서워서 그런 것도 아니엇습니다. 自己가, 훌륭한 生屍軆인 것도, 勿論 自覺하고 잇섯습니다. 그러나 나는 이를 악물고 어대까지 ＜39＞ 던지 살아야만 하겟다고 決心 하얏습니다.
「死는 아모 것도 代償치 안는다. 死는 사람 사람의 記憶力을 흐리우고 멀게 할지 모르나, 그것은 넘우 卑怯한 짓이다」
이가티 생각하는 一便에, 나에게는 큰 使命이 잇다고 自任 하얏습니다. 에-에, 큰 使命이올시다. 自己 自身에 對한 復讐, 한 男子에게 對한 復誰的 成功, 그리고 이 社會에 向한 反抗, 挑戰, 復讐...이것이올시다.
그러나 自殺은 卑怯하다. 自己의 負債는, 自己가 辦償하여야 한다고 할 때에, 나는 悔改하고, 贖罪의 理를 깨다른 淨化한 勇士이엇지만, 一切에 對하야 復誰를 企劃할 때에, 나는 惡魔 이엇고 野獸와 가티 悤怒에 떨면서 이를 북북 갈앗습니다. 그 뿐만 아니라, 現在의 境遇는 나의 過去의 罪를 代贖하고도 充分하다고 생각하얏습니다. 心臟을 바눌 끗으로, 속속 들이 찌르는 것 가튼 苛責과 苦憫만 하야도, 神은 赦罪 하시리라. 적어도 自己의 良心마는 容許하야 줄 수 잇다고 스스로 辯明하야 왓습니다. 何如間 이가티 하야 괴로운 목숨은 오른 까지 부터 왓습니다. 누구를 爲하야, 무엇을 爲하야, 이처럼 苟苟히 살랴는지, 果然 復誰를 爲함인지 不斷의 伸張을 繼續하는 한 生命의 새롭은 萌芽를 爲하야서 인지는 自己도 몰랏습니다 마는.
그러나 크리쓰마쓰·이-브에 보내신 그 意外의 글월은 나에게 스스로 自己를 裁斷할 만한 睿智와 聰明과 決心을 주엇습니다. 족으만 하-얀 손이 쥐어 주고 간 福音! 그것은 天女가 傳하는 最後의 審判의 判決文이엇습니다. 地上에서 꼭 한 번 들은 人子의 입으로서 나온 神의 福音이엇나이다. 아! 同時에 淨케 시킨 十字架이엇나이다.

二 우리 둘을 에워싸고 도는 暗影은, 넘우도 陰酸하고 慘酷하외다. 心事 사나운 運命의 作亂 이라 하야도 넘우 殘虐 惡毒하고, 神의 攝理라 하야도 넘우 悲慘한 酷刑이라 안흘 수 업습니다. 溫順하고 純潔한 貴君에게 그 가튼 試練을 하고, 우리가 맛나지 안흐면 안 되게 한 것이 벌서 一種의 아이로니캘한 宿命이라고 아니 할 수 업습니다. ＜40＞ 或은 이것을 다만 偶然한 事相에 不過하다 할지 모르나, 偶然으로서는 넘우 技巧의 精緻를 極한 偶然이엇나이다.
爲先 우리의 結婚 生活에는 그 第一步서부텨 徹底한 缺陷이 잇섯슴은 다툴 수 업는 事實이엇나이다. 媒婆의 입과 彼此의 寫眞과, 貴君의 會社의 支配人이라는 X氏의 助言 以外에는, 아모 動機도 手段도 條件도 업는 因襲的 婚姻이라는 徹底한 罪惡이, 先祖의 遺物로서, 우리도 또 한번 反復치 안흐면 안 되엇든 것이, 第1의 缺陷이엇나이다. 勿論 이 缺陷은, 今日에 當한 無慘한 破綻에 對하야 直接 原因이 되지는 안핫스나, 缺陷으로서는 根本的이요, 딸아서 이 缺陷에 對하야 透徹한 自覺으로써, 考慮하고 廻避 하얏슬진대, 처음부터 나와 結婚 問題도 업섯슬 것이외다. 이러한 意味로 이에 對한 責任은 거의 그 全部가, 貴君에게 잇다 하겟습니다.
第1에 貴君은 그러한 쓴 經驗을 맛본 結婚 生活의 失敗者로서, 그 失敗의 原因에 對하야 反省이 업섯고, 第2에 그 瘡疲를 慰瘉하고 幾許의 家庭的 幸福을 엇고자 할 제, 亦是 同一한 手段과 形式을 取하셧슴이, 忌避할 수 업는 貴君의 責任이엇나이다. 勿論 世事에 아즉 어둡고 普通 朝鮮 靑年과 가티 因習 道德에 對하야 何等의 反抗的 思想이 업는 外에 善良하고 純直한, 그리고 世俗的이요 打筭적이요 消極的인 貴君으로서는, 過去의 破綻에 對하야, 驚異의 눈을 뜨고 悲歎의 눈물을 먹음은 外에는, 아모 反省이나, 批判이 업섯슬 것은 事實 이겟지요. 그러나 別로 仔細한 말슴은 듯지 못 하얏거니와, 前夫人은, 世間에 普通 잇는 바와 가티, 所謂 밋메느리로 들어와서 가티 자라나셧다 하지 안습니까. 그 後 兩親이 俱沒하시고, 家庭이라고 依支할 대가 업게 되엇슴으로, 東京 留學이 끗나기까지, 一時 別居 하섯다가 成禮하시니까, 그 때는 벌서, 貴君의 품에 安息을 求하랴는 아릿다운 비둘기가 아니라, 貴族의 難棒 子息에게 詛呪 밧든 孔雀새가 되엇드라고 하지 안습니까. 萬一 이것이 事實이라 하면, 어찌하야 그 缺陷에 對하야, 一毫의 反省이 업시, 全然히 無關心의 態度로 지내실 수가 잇섯드냐는 것이 암만하야도 疑心이외다. 더구나 寵愛를 아끼지 안흐섯다 하지 안습니까.
近 十年 동안을 한 父母 膝下에서, 男妹와 가티 자라낫스면, 거긔에 반듯이 同氣와 가튼 友愛도 잇섯슬 것이요, 서로 知覺이 나서 夫婦란 무엇인 것을 次次 알게 되면, 彼此의 慾求와 希望이 생길 것이요, 딸아서 그 慾求나 ＜41＞ 希望에 비추어서, 서로 相對者를 吟味하고 理解하게 되엇슬 것이외다. 그리하야 貴君은 熱愛를 許諾하실 수가 잇섯지 안핫습니까. 그러하면서도 彼女는 貴君을 背反하지 안핫습니까. 貴君과 暫時 別居하얏던 동안 이미 貞操를 깨털리고 貴君에게 反逆 하얏슬 뿐만 아니라, 義理에 못 이겨서 一時 結婚의 先約은 施行하얏스나, 結局 情夫를 爲하야 貴君을 騙詐하고, 듸디어 버리지 안핫습니까.
그러면 그 主要 原因은 무엇에 잇습니까. 勿論 여러 가지가 잇섯겟지요. 娼婦的 性格이 잇섯드라고도 推測할 수 잇고, 貴族의 威風이라던지 男子의 風彩라는 點, 또는 當時에는 生計가 只今 보다 如意치 못하얏다 하오니까, 그러한 物質 問題도 勿論 加味하얏스리라고 할 수도 잇겟지요. 그러나 무엇보다도 가장 有力한 것은 愛라 하겟습니다. 萬一에 貴君에게 對한 愛가, 그 貴族의 子息에게 對함 보다 强하얏더면―그 後 얼마 아니 되어 곳 離別하얏다 하는 所聞이 잇다 하오니, 그 實은 貴族에게 對하야도, 愛가 업섯듬은 事實이엇겟사오니까. 그 愛와 比較 할 것은 아니겟지요 마는―그와  가티 容易히 背反하지는 못하얏슬 터이지요. 하고 보면 두 말 할 것 업시, 精神的 即 內面的으로 何等의 理解가 업섯다는 것이, 個性의 共鳴 協致點과 靈魂의 結合線을 엇지 못하게 하고, 딸아서 眞正한 愛가, 存在치 못한 結果, 愛의 缺乏이 破綻을 宣告 하얏다 함은 自明의 理가 아니오니까. 그러하면 第1次의 結婚 生活이 破産은 차라리 當然한 일이라 할 수 잇고, 비록 持續 하얏다 할지라도 그것은 어느 때든지 破壞하거나, 分離할 可能性을 가진 危險하고 脆弱한 結合에 不過하얏겟지요.
이와 가티 그 破綻의 眞因에 關하야 反省과 批判이 업스섯던 結果는, 同一한 手段으로써 나와 第2의 破綻을 엇기 爲하야 다시 새롭은 因襲的 結婚을 하셧습니다. 取하시는 바 手段에 딸아서는 가장 容易히 그리고 圓滿히 解決하야 一生의 幸福을 누리실 것을, 所謂 因襲的 舊道德이라는 桎梏가, 烏賊魚의 발과 가티 腦裡에 粘着하엿기 때문에 充分히 回避할 수 잇는 破綻을 또 反復하시게 되엇습니다.
貴君의 今日의 處地는 自由結婚 自由戀愛를 實行함에 充分한 條件이 具備하야 잇다는 點으로, 事實 現今의 朝鮮 靑年 中 누구보다도 幸福합니다. 그리고, 이 自由戀愛에 依한 自由結婚이란 것이 다만 理論上으로 合理的이요 有利하다는 것이 아니라, 貴君의 當時의 精神的 打擊을 恢復하랴면, 그리고 再婚을 要求하는 以上, 戀愛를＜42＞ 基礎로 한 充分한 資格잇는 配偶者를 求하시는 外에, 아모 手段도 업섯슬 것이외다. 그러나 貴君에게는 그만한 勇氣가 업섯습니다.
더구나 初娶 夫人은, 所謂 貴族의 부스럭지라는 看板을 가지고 威脅을 하는 것이 구치 안하서 내주엇다는 等, 只今 世上에는 들어 보지 못할 所聞이 잇는 모양이오니, 萬一 이것이 事實일진대, 일의 正否는 姑捨하고라도, 貴君이 얼마나 對抗力에 缺乏하시고, 奴隷 道德에 對하야 아모 反省이 업스셧슴을 알 수 잇지 안흔가 하나이다. 함으로 貴君이, 理論上으로는 自由結婚에 同意하실 수 잇드래도, 到底히 積極的 態度를 取하실 만한 氣力이 업섯스리라고 推測 할 수가 잇습니다.
