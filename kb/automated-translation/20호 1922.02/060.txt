Research on the Livelihood Problems of Koreans, Part One
						Sŏnu Chŏn

In the New Year's issue of your esteemed magazine, I published a part of my research under the title "The Economic Meaning of Arms Reduction and Its Direct Impact." Unfortunately, this piece touched upon matters sensitive to the authorities and had to be deleted. Regarding this, I feel my own carelessness as the author, and at the same time, I feel quite apologetic towards the readers. Now, upon receiving another request for a contribution, and to express gratitude for the goodwill of many readers and the patronage of the journalists, I have undertaken to discuss the following issue. What I, the author, earnestly request from the readers directly is that while such a problem is the most important one for us Koreans, due to the lack of reference materials and insufficient research, I can only present a few facts and cannot introduce it fully, which is deeply regrettable. If any readers have reference materials or facts relevant to this research, I earnestly implore you to provide the author with research materials either by writing directly or by publishing your opinions and facts in this publication, "Kaebyŏk." Furthermore, what is discussed here is limited to the internal causes of the Korean people's livelihood difficulties; other various causes are not discussed. I hope you will understand this as well.

1. Social System of the Old Era and General Causes of Livelihood Difficulty
Today, the problem of livelihood difficulty is a common issue in any country and any society, but the causes leading to the onslaught of livelihood difficulty are not necessarily identical. Recently, as Korean society changes moment by moment, the livelihood difficulty that was not minded in former days has gradually intensified, reaching a point where it has now become a most worrisome and urgent <45> problem. Among the various reasons why livelihood difficulty has arrived so rapidly in Korea, there is a special kind of cause, the likes of which are hard to find in other countries and other societies, and it seems to be the most significant internal cause of the livelihood difficulties of Koreans today. Namely, the past Korean society was formed with various social systems under the backdrop of an autocratic system (專制制度), and even down to individual households, the autocratic authority of the head of the household (家主專制權) was strictly exercised. Of course, it is not that social systems based on the principle of absolute sovereignty did not exist temporarily in any country, but in other countries, social systems based on an economic foundation like today's were established early on, the autocratic social system was destroyed in a short period, and the whole society was able quickly to shed its old shell and transition to a new phase. However, in Korea, that transformation was extremely slow, so upon transitioning to a new phase, it has reached a situation where it must inevitably contend with many severe obstacles. That is, the lingering roots of the stubbornly cultivated old system have caused numerous difficulties in our material life today, becoming an important contributing factor that further exacerbates livelihood difficulties. Simply put, comparing this to advanced countries, in other countries, the people's economic efforts developed and production increased, but flaws arose in the distribution system, leading to the emergence of various social problems. In contrast, in Korea, it seems livelihood difficulty has arisen because economic production efforts are rudimentary, rather than issues with the distribution system of produced goods. Then, what does it truly mean to say that the social system, a product of the old culture, has become an important internal cause of livelihood difficulty today?
Under this system, society in general did not place excessive importance on "economic effort," and through all social classes, certain special sovereigns commanded and ruled those under them. At the same time, those subordinate to them not only took upon themselves the duty of absolute obedience to those sovereigns and authority figures but also relied on those rulers and authority figures for their own existence and livelihood, so the concept of independent living was weak from the beginning. That is, under the one sovereign who ruled the entire society were various officials and large families; under the large families were small families; and under the small families were individuals. Those in each special class ruled those subordinate to them and also arranged their means of living.
According to a tax investigator from Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture (京城府), while surveying the ability of Koreans to bear school expenses planned last year, he visited various Korean households within the prefecture and personally witnessed a household with over 60 dependent family members (寄 食口). Of course, it is likely that not all of these numerous dependents were direct descendants of the household head; it seems factual that they were relatives near and far, socially connected close friends facing livelihood difficulties, and others relying on the household for support due to various <46> relationships. In any case, the head of the household took upon himself the difficult task of supporting numerous people, which was hard to bear alone. It is self-evident that a single household head cannot support numerous people for long, so it is an inevitable consequence that they will be struck by livelihood difficulties. Of course, such households may be few, but even in many ordinary households, if there is, say, a family of five, one person, the head of the household, bears the duty of supporting the entire family, while the other four dependents naturally lack the power to earn a living and become idle mouths (徒食 人口). Therefore, it is a clear fact that the content of that family's life will be impoverished and miserable. That is, the statement that the social system of the old culture constitutes a significant internal cause of today's livelihood difficulty refers to this.
Of course, it is recognized as fact that today's various social systems and the large family system in Korea are gradually changing from old to new, adapting to social changes over time. However, it cannot but be recognized as fact that where the old system, with its deep roots and immense power, was strictly enforced, its reflection today is severe, giving rise to numerous social problems. For instance, in the Northwest region (西北地方), not only are the economic conditions of the entire society relatively equal, but even the family system has evolved towards the division into small families rather than large ones, and consequently, the wealth of the entire region is distributed. In contrast, in the Southern region (南鮮 地方), while a few large capitalists have emerged in society as a whole, a large number of poor and assetless people have also emerged. For instance, the fact that the tenant farmer problem (小作人問題), arising from the unequal distribution of land division, and the evils of land annexation (土地 兼倂) first raised their voices in the Southern region, and that the migration of the poor to Kando (間島) and Manchuria (滿洲) due to the difficulty of maintaining a family's livelihood, disregarding geographical proximity, is highest from the Kyŏngsang region (慶尙 地方) – are these not indeed reflections of that old system? Also, the Southern region, formerly called the "Confucian scholar region" (儒林地方), had a tendency to disdain muscular labor (筋肉的 勞働) and material effort. That is, from Kyŏnggi (京畿) centered southward, among the middle class and above, let alone the so-called descendants of Confucian scholars (儒林子孫), one rarely saw scenes of other indoor ladies shouldering plows and hoes (犁鋤) and going out to the fields for cultivation (耕野). The general notion seemed to be that muscular and material effort was the responsibility of lower-class men. In contrast, in the Western region (西洋地方 - *likely means 西部地方, Western region, i.e., Northwest*), women's farming and women's labor were rather common circumstances. As a result, today, not only has a difference in superiority/inferiority arisen between the two regions regarding women's labor rate and physical development, but it has also consequently had a significant impact on the economic aspect of family life. The result of this disparity in muscular effort and economic effort has had no small effect on the occupations of the residents of the two regions today. <47> That is, it has resulted in many unemployed people (無職者) and vagrants (遊民) in the Southern region, and many employed people (有職者) in the Northwest region.

Just as it is common for livelihood difficulties to first emerge in large cities in any country or society, in Korea too, Kyŏngsŏng (京城) became the place where livelihood difficulties first appeared, and the cause seems attributable to the same reasons mentioned earlier.

2. The Content of the Lives of Kyŏngsŏng Residents
Kyŏngsŏng, the birthplace of the old culture, where the old system was practiced to the extreme, is now experiencing the bitterness of livelihood difficulty more than other cities. Originally, it was generally known that among the residents of Kyŏngsŏng, excluding the merchant class, many individuals held positions in the officialdom (官界), maintaining their livelihood through official salaries (官祿). However, as society took a sharp downturn in recent decades, bureaucratic figures accustomed to idleness (無爲徒食) faced mass dismissals from official posts. Many Kyŏngsŏng residents who had planned to live off official positions found it difficult to make ends meet (糊口) in Kyŏngsŏng and inevitably began to flee <48> to their manors (莊園地) in the provinces. This became the cause of the drastic decrease in the Kyŏngsŏng population (Koreans) over the last 10-odd years, truly presenting the strange phenomenon of Kyŏngsŏng breaking the general characteristic of urban population growth. According to the above, while all cities show population growth, only Kyŏngsŏng shows the opposite phenomenon, a decrease of about 8 percent (8分減). So, what kind of people are the current residents of Kyŏngsŏng generally? They can be broadly divided into (1) former Kyŏngsŏng capitalists who belonged to the traditional special classes, (2) provincials who moved in from various regions over the last 10-odd years, (3) impoverished old Kyŏngsŏng residents who, due to social displacement, wish to relocate from their hometowns but cannot move due to personal circumstances and traditional ties, and (4) lower-class people belonging to various labor classes. Looking at Kyŏngsŏng's business world, intellectual circles, or the locus of social vitality today, those holding the most power are largely provincials belonging to the second category. Those who have become society's refuse (屑物), whose living conditions are miserable and insecure both presently and for the future, seem to be mostly old Kyŏngsŏng residents belonging to the third category. Among them, there seem to be quite a few unemployed individuals, and their lifestyle content is more old-fashioned and romantic than modern. Their income is uncertain and unreliable, and while they struggle to maintain social standing due to financial hardship, various evils arise, seemingly posing no small obstacle to society's general progress. However, class-wise, they are by no means lower-class people.
According to a recently published survey, the Korean households and population in Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture are: Households 38,978, Population Male 97,176, Female 93,218, Total 189,394. Regarding their income, according to the Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture School Fee Household Assessment Ranking Table (京城府 學校費 戶別 割 等級表) implemented in 1921 (Taishō 10), out of a total of 38,978 Korean households, 9,765 households had an annual income of 400 yen or more, up to a maximum of 106,500 yen. In contrast, 29,213 households belonged to the category of annual income below 400 yen, accounting for about 75% (7割 5分) of the total households. If this truly explains the living reality of Koreans residing in Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture, isn't it an astonishing fact? Let's examine the details. <49>

While the standard for social class distinction may involve occupation, status, knowledge, power, or numerous other criteria, in an economic society like today's, where one can attain lifelong comfort by satisfying material desires, the "income of wealth" (財의 收入) that guarantees livelihood likely serves as the general <50> standard for class distinction. That is, through the income of wealth, one can measure the expected quantity of desire; through the expected quantity of desire, one can measure the standard of living; and through the standard of living, one can distinguish social classes. Thus, the standard of living is truly an important condition that cannot be disregarded in the study of social problems, and it will be directly impacted depending on the income of wealth. The fundamental theory regarding this most meaningful standard of living will be shifted to a later discussion. Now, distinguishing the social classes within Korean society based on annual income, it roughly turns out as follows:

This class distinction was created by the author based on actual income (according to the 1921 Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture School Fee Household Assessment Ranking Table) rather than idealizing an improvement in the quality of life, for convenience. Therefore, it cannot be called a perfect class distinction. For example, the income of the Korean upper class is low compared to the income of the middle class in other countries. However, if one were to use the income rates of other societies' classes as a standard for the Korean situation, the social classes of Koreans would fall to middle class or below, the poor class, leading to a mismatch in class distinction.
The livelihood problem we now seek to study will be that of the middle class and below, the poor class. Assuming the aforementioned class distinction is suitable for the Korean reality (the author believes so), observing the income content and household count of each class: the number of households in the Special Class <51> is 192, which is merely 0.49% (1厘 9毛) of the total households. The number of households in the Upper Class is 775, representing 1.99% (1分 9厘 9毛) of the total. The number of households in the Middle Class is 3,239, representing 8.31% (8分 3厘 1毛) of the total. The number of households in the Lower Class ("Upper Lower" and "Middle Lower") is 5,564, representing 14.27% (1割 4分 2厘 7毛) of the total. The number of households in the Lower Class ("Poor") is 29,208, accounting for a staggering 74.94% (7割 4分 9厘 4毛) of the total households. A sad feeling arises that Kyŏngsŏng might actually become a den (窟\*) of the poor. Isn't it truly strange that society is maintained under such income conditions? Then, what is the actual state of their lives?
As mentioned before, the social system of the old era in Korea was, on one hand, a significant cause of livelihood difficulty, while on the other hand, it also served as a means to alleviate today's livelihood difficulty. The author feels no need to overly expose the inner workings of Korean life here, and indeed, feels a sense of shame in disclosing such unfortunate facts. However, rather than the pain of maintaining appearances by deceiving society—saying what is not, is, and what is, is not—I felt the need to report the facts to society and explain them to the authorities, so that after understanding the true state of life, a plan for the solid livelihood of the general Korean populace could be devised. Therefore, the author intends to present a part of the inner situation of Korean life. Traditional customs that contradict the new life today have permeated various aspects, and can often be seen exposed even in social language. When encountering relatively close acquaintances on the street or in some gathering, if asked, "What have you been doing lately?" the reply might be, "I have nothing to do, just idling aimlessly." (Actually unemployed). Or, meeting a friend and asking, "What are you doing these days?" the reply is, "Just getting by like this." (Has a job but lacks sincerity or enthusiasm for it). Meeting another friend and asking, "What are you up to these days?" the reply is often, "Just going to such-and-such place, such-and-such company, and 'playing' (노네)." (Someone who thinks highly of their own status compared to their job). Meeting another friend and asking, "What are you doing lately?" the reply is, "I report to (社進) such-and-such place, such-and-such company every day." Yet another friend, when asked, "What are you doing lately?" replies, "I am eagerly bustling about trying to accomplish a certain project, but various obstacles arise, so it's still difficult."
To comment on these five answers in a word: the first, second, and third answers stem from the habits inherited from the social system of former days. They show a tendency to rely on others rather than fostering self-reliance, a desire to maintain former appearances haughtily and boastfully rather than showing sincerity and enthusiasm towards one's occupation, and a habit of treating any undertaking casually, born from an undisciplined and insincere disposition that prefers relying on luck and opportunity ("if it works out, it works out; if not, not") rather than completing and seeing through a mentally or materially intended project with a steady and strong will, facing any difficulty <52> with one's own strength. If one were to carelessly utter such answers to an experienced person or businessman who is tenacious in whatever they undertake and possesses practical ability, one would immediately lose credibility on the spot, even if one possesses certain abilities or knowledge in their head.
What was meant by the earlier statement that the old cultural social system is both a cause of today's livelihood difficulty and, simultaneously, a means of alleviating it?
The fortune-seeking riffraff (求官雜輩) who frequented the houses of powerful families (權門大家) seeking appointments (求仕 次로 獵官의 目的으로) in the previous era, now, in order to save themselves from livelihood difficulty, are often found visiting the gates of the powerful and wealthy. That is, if one peers into the reception room (舍廊) of a noble household (貴人大家) – people of high status and rank who possessed considerable influence in the old days and still have substantial wealth today – one can often see numerous visitors quietly surrounding the seating area, murmuring amongst themselves. Their appearance and demeanor mimic those of dignified upper-class figures. However, probing into their substance reveals them to be idle individuals without fixed occupations. Their conversation consists merely of slandering others, saying "so-and-so did such-and-such," and other useless, insubstantial chatter. Their hand games involve nothing more than playing with the *changgi* (Korean chess), *Go*, or *ssangnyuk* (backgammon-like game) prepared in the reception room. And if the host says wine is rice, they readily agree (唯唯應是). Yet, the master of the house neither rejects their visits nor orders them to leave. Instead, he seems to treat them well and welcome them. Why? Because not only have they had deep-seated social connections (世來的 交分) for generations, but they also belong to factions (黨派的) that always side with the host, thus earning his favor. If the host were to reject them and incur their ill will, they would roam various sectors of society, spreading malicious rumors about the host everywhere, causing unexpected harm. Also, among the hosts themselves, many seem to be people who held high official positions in the past but are now outdated in terms of modern skills and knowledge. Possessing some wealth, they lie low within society, specializing in high-interest moneylending (高利貸金). Responding to solicitations from canvassers (勸誘員) fashionable at the time, they seem desirous of obtaining some honorary position like president or director by investing a bit of money in some company or association (某社, 某組合). Given the host's circumstances and inner workings, these dependents (食口) of his reception room, deeply connected to him, might introduce borrowers for the host's loans, arranging introduction fees and commissions from the borrower. Or, <53> regardless of their suitability or skills, they might request a clerical position (一窠의 事務員) in a company or association the host is involved with. Or, they become retainers (家臣) of the host, assisting with his administrative affairs. Or, acting on the host's behalf, they might travel to the provinces to collect land rent (土地作用) from income-generating land, or to buy or sell land and real estate. Or, in whatever business the host manages, they become the host's frontman (압자리가 되어), serving the host in various capacities like his own hands and feet. The relationship between the host and these individuals, though different in surname (姓 則 異姓), forms a bond as close as family in terms of intimacy (親分) or connection (交分). They truly become dependents belonging to the host (主人에 隷屬된 食口), and the phenomenon is that the host directly or indirectly bears the greater part of their living expenses through various connections. This is the living situation of a portion of the unemployed vagrants. The people who receive titles like "so-and-so's man" (아무개의 사람) or "so-and-so's reception room man" (아무개의 舍廊사람) are entirely these people; the Secretary Kims (金主事) and Secretary Lees (李主事) are also among them. Their numbers are surely not small, and in terms of class within Korean society, they would belong to the upper-lower class or above, up to the middle-middle class. Wasn't the aforementioned statement by the Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture tax investigator, about witnessing a household of an old noble family with over 60 dependents, describing a part of this reality? Another interesting point is, when asked how much their living expenses are, their answer is that if their income is large, they eat and spend a lot, and if it's small, they eat and spend little, so there's no limit (限量). Then, when asked if they have a fixed income, they say they don't. When asked how their numerous dependents manage, they say they don't know either. Isn't this truly the incomprehensible way of life (살림살이) of Koreans?
Thus, becoming like servants (使僕) in the house of another influential person, a retainer or guest (家人, 食客), engaging in the household affairs (家務, 家政) of that house while relying on it for the greater part of their living expenses – although it's likely true that such parasitic livers (寄生的 生活者) are gradually decreasing with the changing times, considering the fact from the aforementioned Kyŏngsŏng resident income statistics that households with annual incomes below 400 yen or no income constitute 75% of the total, it's hard to deny that they are still quite numerous. Furthermore, even if there are some discrepancies between truth and falsehood regarding the actual examples discussed above, it cannot but be acknowledged as fact that a large number of unemployed people among Koreans in Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture continue this kind of romantic (浪漫的) life. However, our purpose is not to critically discuss the inner workings of Korean life, but rather there is a need to research it thoroughly. And we should not ridicule or mock their inner lives, <54> but must fundamentally research ways to remedy this situation. The purpose of the author's research lies in this point.
Here, if we classify the Koreans within Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture based on their means of livelihood, we can distinguish: (1) those who maintain their livelihood through their own property, regardless of whether they have a regular job, (2) those who have a fixed occupation and thus a somewhat reliable income, maintaining the greater part of their livelihood with it, and (3) those who lack a fixed job and consequently have no reliable income, relying mostly on others for their means of maintaining life. Regarding the first class, those living off income from their own property, there is no need to discuss them here. However, considering the nature of the inner lives of those belonging to the second and third categories: the former, while having differences in living standards depending on the amount of income, have a lifestyle content that is modern, organized, standardized, and progressive. In contrast, the latter lack a budget or standard for living expenses, making their lives undisciplined, wandering, old-fashioned, and merely sustaining, thus their future is most uncertain and dangerous. However, the former have the disadvantage of facing immediate panic and livelihood difficulty if they temporarily lose their job, whereas the latter have the advantage of possessing unexpectedly strong resistance to livelihood difficulty, being able to endure hardship... **Summary**

Sŏnu Chŏn argues that a primary internal cause of Korean economic hardship in 1922 stems from the lingering social structures of the "Old Era," specifically the autocratic family system and a traditional disdain for manual labor, particularly prevalent in southern Korea. This system fostered dependency over self-sufficient economic activity. He contrasts this with the Northwest, suggesting better adaptation, and highlights Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) as an epicenter of hardship, citing statistics showing 75% of Korean households earn less than 400 yen annually. He describes a significant segment of the unemployed (often former elites) living a "romantic," dependent existence reliant on wealthy patrons, contrasting their resilience to hardship, despite lacking stable income, with the modern but precarious lives of those with regular jobs. For colonial historians, this article is valuable for presenting a contemporary Korean analysis linking socio-economic problems to pre-colonial structures, offering specific income data for Kyŏngsŏng, and providing qualitative descriptions of class dynamics and cultural attitudes towards work and dependency in early colonial Korea. The regional comparison and the author's call for empirical data also reflect the developing state of Korean social thought.

「貴誌 新年號에 余의 硏究의 一部「軍備縮少의 經濟的 意味와 그 直接 影響」이라는 問題 下에 鄙論을 揭 하엿던 바 不幸히 此가 當局의 忌諱에 觸한 바 되어 削除케 됨에 對하야는 論者의 經忽함을 自感하는 同時에 讀者 諸君에게 對하야서도 한 갓 未安히 생각하는 바이외다. 이제 다시 寄稿의 請求에 對하야 多數 讀者의 好誼와 記者 諸君의 愛護를 謝키 爲하야 左와 如한 問題를 論叙케 됨에 臨하매 論者가 直接으로 讀者 諸君에게 懇求하는 바는 이와 가튼 問題가 朝鮮 사람된 우리의 가장 重要한 問題인 것을 參考資料의 未備와 硏究의 不足으로 寡少의 事實을 擧함에 不過하고 充分히 紹介치 못하는 것이 甚히 遺憾이오니 讀者 諸氏 中에 此의 硏究에 對하야 參考 될 만한 資料와 事實이 有하면 論者에게 直接 書面으로나 或은 本 「開闢」에 其 意見과 事實을 揭載하야 論者의 硏究의 資料를 供給하여 주기를 懇求하옴는 바이외다. 再次 此에 論하는 바는 朝鮮人 生活難의 內部的 原因에 限하야 論한 바 이고 其他 諸 原因에 對하야는 論한 바 아니니 此 亦 諒解 하시기를 望 하옵니다.

1. 舊時代의 社會制度와 生活難의 一般的 原因
今日 生活難 問題는 어느 나라, 어느 社會에 在하야서나 通有한 問題이나 生活難이 襲來하는 그 原因에 就하야는 必히 同一치 아니하다. 近來 朝鮮社會도 刻刻으로 變化됨에 隨하야 前日에 氣介치 아니 하얏던 生活難이 漸漸 急을 加하야 現下에 至하야는 가장 憂慮할 만한 焦愁의 ＜45＞ 問題가 되엇다. 그리고 朝鮮에 在하야 이와가티 急激히 生活難이 迫來케 된 諸原因 中에는 他國, 他社會에서는 그 類例를 見키 難한 一種 特別의 原因이 有한데 그는 今日 朝鮮人 生活難의 內部的 原因으로 가장 重大한 것인 듯 하다. 即 過去의 朝鮮社會는 專制制度의 背景下에 各般 社會制度가 組成되어 個人家庭에 至하야서도 家主專制權이 嚴密히 行使되엇다. 勿論 主權 萬能主義의 社會制度는 어느 나라를 勿論하고 一時 存在치 아니하얏던 바는 아니나 다른 나라에 在하야는 일즉이 今日과 如한 經濟的 基礎를 本位로 한 社會制度가 早速 樹立되어 專制的 社會制度가 短其間에 破壞되어 社會全般은 迅速히 舊殼을 解脫하고 新生面에 轉換키 能하엿다. 그러나 朝鮮에 在하야는 그 變革이 極히 遲遲 하엿슴으로 新生面에 轉換됨에 際하야 事實 多嚴한 障壁과 相戰치 안흐면 아니될 現狀에 至하엿다. 即 頑强히 培養된 舊制度의 餘根은 今日 우리의 物質的 生活에 幾多의 困難을 惹起하야 生活難을 一層 助長케 하는 重要한 加勢的 原因이 되엇다. 此를 先進國에 比하야 簡單히 말하면 他國에 在하야는 國民의 經濟的 努力이 發達되어 生産은 增大하엿스나 分配制度에 缺陷이 生하엿슴으로 各種의 社會問題가 胚生케 됨에 反하야 朝鮮에 在하야는 生産物 分配制度 보다도 經濟的 生産努力이 幼穉함으로 生活難이 發生한 듯 하다. 그러면 舊文化의 所産인 社會制度가 今日에 至하야 生活難의 重要한 內部的 原因을 作하엿다 함은 果然 何를 意味한 것인가?
此 制度下에 在하야는 社會一般이 經濟的 努力이라는 것을 過히 重視치 아니하엿스며 또 社會 各 階級을 通하야 如何한 特殊의 主權者가 그 部下에 對하야 命令하고 支配하는 同時에 此에 隷屬된 者는 그 主權者 權能者에 對하야 絶對로 服從하는 義務를 自負하엿슬 뿐 아니라 自己의 存在生活에 對하야서도 그 支配者 權能者에 依賴하야 獨立生活이라는 觀念이 最初부터 薄弱 하엿다. 即 社會 全般을 支配하는 一個人의 主權者에 諸臣僚와 大家族이 有하얏고 大家族下에 小家族이 有하얏고 小家族 下에 個人이 有하야 各 特殊階級에 在한 자가 自己에 隷屬된 者를 支配하엿고 또 그의 生活 方便도 處辨하엿다.
京城府 課稅調査員의 所傳에 依하면 客年에 計劃한 朝鮮人 學校費 負擔能力을 調査키 爲하야 府內 鮮人 各 戶를 巡訪하얏던 바 어떠한 家庭에는 一家 寄 食口 60 餘名이 잇는 것을 實見하엿노라 하엿다. 勿論 그 多數人의 食口 全部가 同家主의 直統 子孫은 아닐 것이고 或은 遠近의 親戚 或은 生活難을 當迫한 世交的 親友 其他 種種의＜46＞ 關係로 同家에 扶養을 依賴한 者일 것은 事實이 될 듯 하다. 如何間 그 家主된 者는 獨力으로 堪當키 難한 多數人의 扶養의 難事를 自負하엿다. 一個人의 家主가 長久히 多數人을 扶養키 不能할 것은 勿論일 터 임으로 生活難의 襲擊을 當할 것은 事必 當然한 結果가 될 것이라. 勿論 이와 가튼 家庭은 少數에 不過할 것이나 多數 普通家庭에 至 하야서도 假令 一家族 5人이 有하다 하면 其中 家主된 一個人이 全體家族의 扶養의 義務를 負 하엿고 其他 4人의 食口는 自然的 生活力이 缺無한 徒食 人口가 될 터 임으로 그 家族生活의 內容이 貧弱, 慘憺할 것은 明確한 事實일 것이다. 即 舊文化의 社會制度가 今日이 生活難이 重大한 內部的 原因을 成한다 함은 此를 云함이다.
勿論 今日 朝鮮 各般 社會制度 及 大家族制度가 社會的 變化를 順應하야 舊로부터 新에 漸進하야 時時로 變化하여 가는 것은 事實로 認識하는 바이다. 그러나 根據가 깁고 勢力이 魁大하엿던 舊制度가 嚴格히 行使 되엇든 地方에 今日 그 反影이 甚하야 數多의 社會問題를 胚生한 것은 事實로 認識치 아니키 不能하다. 即 西北地方에 在하는 社會 全部의 經濟的 條件이 比較的 平均케 되엇슬 뿐만 아니라 家族制度에 至 하야서도 大家族 보다도 小家族의 分立에 進化하얏고 딸아서 地方全體의 財富가 分割的 임에 反하야 南鮮 地方에 在하야는 社會全體로 少數의 大 資産家 發生된 一面에 多數의 貧民 無資者가 發生 되엇다. 即 最近 朝鮮의 社會問題로 土地 分割의 不平均을 基因하야 發生된 小作人問題 土地 兼倂의 弊害가 南鮮 地方에서 그 初聲을 發케 된 것과 一家族의 生活維持 困難으로 間島 滿洲 方面에 貧民의 移住가 地理上 關係를 無視하고 慶尙 地方에 最多함과 如한 事實은 果然 그 舊制度의 反影이 아닐가? 또 所謂 儒林地方이라고 일컷든 南鮮地方은 筋肉的 勞働과 物質的 努力을 輕視한 風이 有하엿다. 即 京畿를 中心하야 南鮮 地方에 至하여는 中流以上의 階級은 勿論, 所謂 儒林子孫, 其他 室內 夫人이 犁鋤를 荷 하고 耕野에 出하는 光景은 過히 見치 못하엿고 一般의 觀念이 筋肉的 物質的 努力은 下層 階級의 男子의 當負한 것이라 思察 하얏든 듯 하다. 此에 反하야 西洋地方에 在하야는 婦人農業, 婦人勞働은 寧히 一般的 事情 이엇슴으로 今日에 至하야는 兩 地方間에 在하야 婦人의 勞働率 及 身軆의 發達에 兩者間 優劣의 差異를 生케 하엿슬 뿐만 아니라 因하야 家族生活의 經濟的 方面에 至하야서도 多大한 影響을 波及케 하엿다. 此等 筋肉的 努力, 經濟的 努力의 優劣의 結果는 今日 兩地方 住民의 職業에 不少한 影響을 及 하엿다.＜47＞ 即 南鮮地方에 無職者와 遊民이 多하고 西北地方에 有職者 多한 結果를 現하엿다.

各 地方 有職者 無職者 別 ( 大正 9年 10月 1日 施行 國勢 調査 )

右表에 依하면 舊文化의 中心地라 할만한 京畿地方을 爲先하야 忠淸南北道, 慶尙南道 及 咸鏡南道 地方에 無業者가 最多하고(5割 以上) 此에 反하야 舊文化의 影響이 比較的 稀薄한 平安南北道, 黃海道, 江原道 全羅道 地方에 在하야는 無業者의 수가 少할 뿐만 아니라 (5割 以下) 各種의 産業이 比較的 平均히 發達된 感이 有하다. 어느 나라 어느 社會를 勿論하고 生活難이 大都市에서 先發되는 것은 通例인 것과 가티 朝鮮에 在하야서도 京城이 生活難의 先發地가 되엇고 그 原因이 前述한 바 同一한 理由에 歸할 듯 하다.

2. 京城府民의 生活의 內容
京城은 舊文化의 發祥地로 舊制度가 極端으로 行使되엇더니 마치 今日에 至하야서도 他 都市以上의 生活難의 辛酸을 極 하얏다. 元來 京城住民으로 말하면 商人階級을 除한 以外 多數 人士는 官界에 職籍을 置하야 官祿으로써 生活을 維持 하든 것을 一般이 周知하든 事이엇섯다. 그러나 最近 數十年間에 社會가 急轉直下케 되매 無爲徒食적 官界人物은 官職의 大罷免을 當하야 官職으로 生活을 圖하든 多數 京城人은 京城서는 糊口의 策을 辨키 難하야 할 수 업시 地方의 自己의 莊園地로＜48＞ 逃出키 始作하얏다. 이것이 最近 10餘年 間 京城 人口(朝鮮人) 激減의 原因이 되어 實로 京城은 都市人口 常增이라는 一般的 特徵을 打破하는 奇現象을 現出하얏다. 即 最近 9個年間 朝鮮 內 四大都市의 戶數 人口의 增減을 (朝鮮人) 示 하면

右에 依하면 어느 都市를 勿論하고 人口의 增殖을 示하나 唯獨 京城에 在하야는 約 8分減反對의 現象을 示 하엿다. 그리고 現在 京城住民은 大槪 如何한 人物이냐 하면 (1) 從來 特殊階級에 屬하얏던 元 京城의 資産家 (2) 最近 10數年間 各 地方에서 移入한 地方人, (3)社會的 淘汰로 因하야 鄕土地方에서 移轉코저 하나 身邊의 事情과 因襲的 關係로 動搖키 不能한 辛酸한 舊 京城人 (4) 各種 勞動階級에 屬한 下級人民에 大別할 수 有한데 今日 京城의 事業界로 보던지 知識方面으로 보던지 社會的 活力의 抱負點으로 보던지 가장 勢力을 把持한 者는 大部分 第 2에 屬한 地方人으로 人間 社會의 屑物이 되어 生活의 狀態가 現在 及 將來에 對하야 悲慘 不安한 運命을 當한 者는 第3種에 屬한  舊京城人이 大部分을 占한 듯 하다. 그네들 中에는 無職者로 相當히 만흔 모양이며 그 生活內容이 近代的 보다도 舊式的이오 浪漫的이며 그 收入이 不確不實 하야 生活困難으로 社會上 體面을 保키 難한 反面에서는 種種의 弊毒이 發生하야 社會 一般的 進步에 對하야서도 不少한 障碍를 끼치는 듯 하다. 그러나 그네들은 階級的으로 보아 決코 下級人民은 아니다.
最近 發表한 調査에 依하면 京城府內의 朝鮮人 戶數 人口는, 戶數 38,978號 人口 男 97,176 人, 女 93,218 人, 計 189,394人 인데 그 收入은 大正 10年度 施行 京城府 學校費 戶別 割 等級表에 依하면 朝鮮人 總 戶數 38,978號 中에 年 收入 400圓 以上 最高額 106,500圓의 收入을 有한 者 9,765戶에 對하야 年 收入 400圓 以下에 屬한 者 29,213戶, 即 總 戶數의 約 7割 5分을 占 하얏나니 이것이 果然 京城府에 在住하는 朝鮮人의 生活實況을 說明한다 하면 奇驚할 만한 事實이 아닌가. 그러면 그 內容을 吟味하여＜49＞ 보자.

朝鮮人에 對한 京城府 學校費 戶別 割等級 表    (大正 10年度 施行 京地府 調査)


社會의 階級區別의 標準이 職業, 品位, 知識, 或은 權力 其他 數多의 標準이 有할지나 物質의 慾望 滿足으로써 一生의 安樂을 得키 能한 今日과 如한 經濟社會에 在하야는 生活을 保障할 「財의 收入」이 階級區別의 大凡의＜50＞ 標準이 될 것이다. 即 財의 收入으로써 慾望의 可期的 分量을 測定키 能하고 慾望의 可期的 分量으로써 生活의 標準을 測定키 能하고 生活의 標準으로써 社會的 階級을 區別키 能하니 生活의 標準은 實로 社會問題硏究에 度外치 못할 重要한 條件이 되고 또 財의 收入 如何에 依하야 此가 直接 그 反響을 受케 될 것이다. 이 가장 意味잇는 生活標準에 關한 根本的 理論은 後論에 移하고 이제 朝鮮人 社會에 在하야 그 社會的 階級을 그 年收入을 標準하야 此를 區別하면 大槪 左와 如케 된다.

收入을 標準한 京城府 內 朝鮮人의 階級別
