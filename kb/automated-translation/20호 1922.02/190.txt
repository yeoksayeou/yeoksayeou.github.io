Article Title: 近代文藝(7) (Modern Literature 7)
Author: 岸曙 (An Sŏ)
Article Type: 문예평론 (Literary Criticism)

近代文藝
						岸曙
A pathological record! Of course, in a sense, one could say it only recorded the pathological aspects, or the ugly and汚 (dirty) aspects, the bestial side of human life. It seems there wouldn't be a speck of the so-called metaphysical or spiritual point in the standpoint that denies anything spiritual in humans, allowing only the material – in other words, a materialistic state. Only a desolate, ordinary machine was visible. Indeed, just as they say everything in the world depends on how you look at it, it seems unavoidable that the Naturalist writers saw things differently from how the previous Romantic writers saw them, because their respective assertions and ways of seeing were different. However, I do not try to forget that there is something in humans that is different from other things. I won't try to define it definitively as spirit, mind, or something else. But anyway, I cannot forget that there is something different. Even when I view human life from the same perspective as a natural scientist, I still hold the thought that humans are the lords of creation. Of course, the term 'lords of creation' has a slightly different meaning than what people used to say. Putting such talk aside, there is no room for doubt that the Naturalist writers made the world ugly. In other words, they broke down all traditional customs and delusions and tore off the masks people wore deeply. Then, the Naturalist writers found joy in pulling out the bestiality (獸性) hidden beneath those masks and exposing it publicly. Thus, the kingdom of God became bestialized, giving rise to a new phrase worth calling "The bestialization of The holy Kingdom." For example, love, which had been considered sacred, even to the point of saying "Love is best," is described by biologists as the satisfaction of ancestral instincts or carnal satisfaction, a phenomenon of the animal <10> instinct for species preservation. It is not considered poetic or sacred at all. As Count Tolstoy also said, humans cannot live even a day without faith, and faith, which was regarded as life itself, is taught by natural scientists to be a pathological function of the nerves, giving it the name Hysterical hallucination.

And they say that things like the satisfaction of human instinct or religious faith ultimately bring nothing but sin to humans. Truly chilling words. In a word, Naturalist writers tried to uglify, prosaicize, and bestialize everything that had been beautified, poeticized, and sanctified, as much as possible. They looked deep into the essence of things, and if there was anything smelly inside, they wouldn't leave even a bit of it alone but pull it out, hold its smell to the noses of many people, and hold it to their own noses as well. Thus, the bestialized aspect came to occupy an important position in their works. For those who sought not the bright places but the dark places hidden behind the bright ones, it seems to have been an unavoidable task. As someone said, Dante wrote the Divine Comedy (神聖한 喜劇), Balzac wrote the Human Comedy (人生의 喜劇), and Zola wrote the Bestial Comedy (禽獸의 喜劇) – this doesn't seem like an ordinary remark.

However, regarding the pathological records of Naturalist writers, one cannot simply blame the Naturalist writers alone. This is because, in the modern era of natural science, everything was inevitably governed by the laws of natural science. Here, the famous Russian proverb, "We must not blame the mirror if the face looks ugly" (얼굴이 밉게 비추인다고 明鏡을 허물하여서는 안된다), strongly defends the Naturalist writers. Indeed, that is so. Romantic writers did not try to peek into the inner workings of things at all, but were satisfied with everything on the surface and tried to praise it endlessly. And they failed to capture honest human life. However, this does not mean that the Naturalist writers captured all of true human life. Naturalist writers ran to an extreme, viewing everything as ugliness and also only as suffering. That was also how Naturalism <11> failed to capture true human life. To capture true human life, I believe one must allow for both spirit and matter, soul and body, and record both aspects. However, if these two aspects become too extreme, one will also be unable to capture human life. From my current viewpoint, nothing seems more mistaken than natural science that denies the spirit. Again, if I may add one more word, though it's superficial, true science must coincide with true religion. If science and religion are false, they certainly cannot coincide, but if science and religion are true, they will surely coincide. This is because the truth of religion and the truth of science cannot but be one, and what religion and science seek cannot but be one. Therefore, it is as clear as day that what literature seeks must also be that which religion and science seek. It seems that Naturalist literature, having received the baptism of science (this science, of course, is science that does not coincide with religion), recklessly observed human life with the scientific method (사이엔퇵 메또드로). I say recklessly observed. But, of course, one cannot scold the Naturalist writers. That famous Russian proverb defends them again. One should scold natural science, but cannot scold Naturalism.

Needless to say, the bright side is a fact of human life, and the dark side is also a fact of human life. Both are equally facts of human life. But there is no need to fix one's eyes on only one side. In other words, seeing both together with the same eyes and letting them supplement each other is not some temporary stopgap measure, but seems to be the true way. Of course, writers like Ibsen or Bernard Shaw seem to reject harmony and the like entirely, asserting "All or nothing." But there is no need to elaborate on such stories here. I should just introduce them honestly and not adopt the perspective of a critic at all.

Now, in Naturalist literature, there is the term "Human document" (人生 記錄). This means nothing other than a fragment of human life. It is writing down facts hidden in daily life or ordinary places without any plot or anything. Whatever catches the writer's eye is captured and depicted exactly <12> as it is; in other words, description was the priority. Therefore, it becomes like a diary of daily life without unity. The fact that many Naturalist works are dry and tasteless, earning the name of literature utterly lacking so-called enjoyment, also lies in this point. And it is a characteristic, if you call it that, of Naturalist works that it is unclear what is the main character and what is subordinate. But that cannot be helped. Since it's not about writing the entirety of human life, but only capturing and writing one part, it inevitably becomes a fragment, and also something without a plot or anything. If one were to write about a complete human life, one would have to capture and describe a person's entire life. That's why works like Maupassant's "A Woman's Life" (女子의 一生) become inevitable. And, like the so-called impression, Naturalist writers often wrote honestly about some fragment of human life as impressed upon their minds, without exaggeration or satire, and without any emotion. Also, from the perspective of the Naturalist writer, nothing more could be done. This is because it is produced purely by the natural scientific method. In other words, whether it is evil or good, they did not care at all; as long as they wrote down what was reflected in their eyes, they fulfilled their responsibility. A photographer just captures whatever is there as it is; they cannot alter or remove things. Why? Because if they alter and remove, the true reality of things would disappear. The perspective of the photographer and the painter differs here. If we call the attitude of the Naturalist writer that of a photographer, then the painter must inevitably be something else. I would like to call the Neo-Romantic writers, whom I intend to discuss below, painters. It is too early to talk about this here.

One can easily understand this by listening to writers like Ibsen. When we hear Ibsen say that his plays are fragments of everyday life that could happen anywhere, and that the facts or scenes in his plays could all be found if one just removed the wall of anyone's house anywhere and looked inside, we can understand how they drew material from everyday life. And in the famous "Lower Depths" (日釋 『夜와 宿』) by a writer like Russia's Gorky, only the scenes change, and characters just come and go. <13> There is certainly no plot to the events, and one cannot tell who the main character is. There is certainly no tendency to solve any problem. Therefore, it offers no interest to the audience and rather makes them unable to understand the reason why. However, the fault does not lie with the author; rather, such things exist in human society and real life, so it can be said to be an unavoidable matter. In Naturalist works, there are certainly none of the so-called novels like Changhanmong (長恨夢) or Ongnumong (玉樓夢), or whatever strange and varied things they are. As mentioned before, how could those low-grade works that needlessly try to squeeze tears out of others become material for Naturalist works? Currently in Chosŏn, there are many various novels, and the three-penny "cheap stuff" (싸구려)판 fills the bookstore shelves, covered in dust. Yet, even after reading such things, there is much talk of this and that, a separate world of side-splitting laughter. How surprised they would be, and how they would call them non-novels, if they were shown Naturalist works! Of course, one cannot dismiss all Chosŏn novels as being outside the category of novels. Even looking at the works of some reliable author or one with a naturalistic self-confidence as a great writer, it is often impossible to understand what they are about. Thinking about this, I have an earnest desire to speak a bit more detail about Naturalist or modern works. But let this suffice. Of course, Romantic writers had unity and thought that permeated their works. That cannot be denied. Regarding this, it is enough to know the term "fragment of human life." The life portrayed by Naturalist writers was one described as it is from a natural scientific viewpoint, shattering all the formal conventions of Romantic works at one stroke.

Now, speaking of novels, there are three elements. From the Romantic viewpoint, they would be character, plot, and setting (周圍). But from the Naturalist viewpoint, the order would be setting, character, and plot. From the Naturalist perspective, setting would be paramount, while character and plot would be mere appendages.

Regardless of the country, if you look at ancient paintings, the main subject is depicted large, occupying the entire canvas. It was the same in novels. In Romanticism and Classicism, more than anything else, only the protagonist of the novel was written about in various ways, while the protagonist's surroundings (周圍), or any specific plot, were not detailed. To put it directly, even when describing the protagonist in various ways, <14> if it was a woman, she was invariably a peerless beauty, and if a hero, invariably an unprecedented, superhuman hero was written. Therefore, the entire piece was just superhuman accounts of the bizarre protagonist, and an unnatural plot of events following those accounts. Regarding the protagonist's situation, that is, the setting (周圍), they did not try to say anything at all. To prove such things, common old sayings like "Once upon a time, in a certain place, there was a person" can be said to indicate the lack of attention paid to describing the setting. Just as the main subject alone occupied the entire canvas in ancient paintings, novels also had only the protagonist and the plot of events. Some have called such pictures "Figuring up in the air" (空中으로 올라가는 形狀), but this idea is not absent in novels either. Just as the background came to occupy the most important position in modern painting, it is a clear fact that the setting (周圍), which is the background, came to occupy the most important position in novels. Furthermore, for Naturalist writers, it was indispensable. For example, let's say a painter tries to paint the joy of a spring day. If they simply painted only a little girl smiling, how much joy would that give the viewer? Of course, it would give the viewer nothing more than the fact that a little girl is smiling. Therefore, if they painted a scene where green grass has sprouted fresh and blue, and under a flowering tree, a little girl sits with a smile, playing mischievously, how much more deeply would it impress the viewer with the joy of a spring day? Naturalist writers adopted methods like this. It seems that 반드시 (necessarily) writing in detail about the background, which holds the most important position, is no different from the joy of a spring day in a painting. Why? Because that background not only explains the joy better than anything else but also speaks to all the attitudes of the main character. Moreover, as mentioned before, it barely needs saying that people change in various ways according to their surrounding circumstances (周圍의 境遇).

According to the laws of evolutionary theory, all living things are said to change according to their environment (境遇). And it has even been said that the human body and spirit (精神) ultimately arise according to the surrounding environment (周圍의 境遇). To borrow the words of the famous master of the experimental novel, Zola: "The environment which determines and completes the man." (사람을 完成케 하며, 決定시키는 周圍) <15> Because Naturalism focused only on describing the setting (周圍), it was not without various shortcomings. But we should try to remember that this was also one of the characteristics of Naturalism that could not be helped.

If one observes and dissects everything with the attitude of a natural scientist, there will not be a single identical object. Things like Shakespeare's "Comedy of Errors" (comedy false) can only be called obsolete old tales fit for eternal burial. Where could there possibly be people whose appearances are so indistinguishable that they are mistaken for each other? Of course, the saying "ten people, ten colors" (十人 十色) means not only psychological states but also appearances differ. The saying that nature never creates the same object twice provides a sufficient explanation, I believe. Romantic writers took universal abstractions about things, used them as ideals, and were self-satisfied. But Naturalist writers took direct experience as their foundation, depicted the individuals (個體) visible before their eyes concretely, and expressed characteristics that would allow easy distinction from other individuals. Therefore, in works before Naturalism, there were types and fates that could be said to be all fixed. But in Naturalist works, more than anything, there was the particular point of the individual, so the types were not entirely the same. This was, needless to say, because they used scientific observation in depicting individuality (個性). The so-called "Local colour" (地方色) in literature existed before Naturalism. But in the Romantic school, these often had fictional elements. In modern works, more than anything, even when writing about social conditions or climate and customs, they tried to clearly and accurately reveal the specific local color.

Even a reader who has only read the work can easily understand the scenery, life, and everything else. Here, the author's writing style (筆致), in other words, the prose (文章), has a great relationship. In Naturalist prose, there was something like a free artistic style, frank and full of attractive power, which the Romantics could not match.

As everyone knows, regarding prose (文章), no writer agonized more than Flaubert. Indeed, his brush added caution upon caution, reverence upon reverence; it was prose of the utmost care (惟心), worth twin streams of tears (雙淚 兩行). Not only Flaubert, but modern writers generally broke conventional forms and had a writing style (筆致) that created distinctive prose.

Now, Flaubert's words about prose (文章) are interesting. Namely, there are no exactly identical things in the world. Therefore, the writer <16> must first discern the individuality (個性) of any object. And when trying to write about it, it must not appear to the reader as an object similar to others. It must be made distinct (明晳). After doing this, one must naturally allow the true aspects of life to move actively within the work. For this, the most important thing is the word. Needless to say, for one object, there is only one word. But if one misuses the one word that exists for one object, it is truly easy to confuse it with other objects. Therefore, the writer's agony is to find the "mot unique" (하나이요, 둘도 업는 말 - the one and only word). This is a famous saying. Flaubert agonized to obtain this unique, irreplaceable word (唯一無二의 文字). It is said that he routinely revised manuscripts more than 10 times. Flaubert's prose, which flows with good rhythm and has absolutely no unnecessary words, was created under such assertion and agony. Also, the Goncourt brothers wrote prose no less skillful than Flaubert's. Their assertion was to make impressions clear (明瞭) and make events and characters come alive like paintings (繪畵), so they used grammatical structures that ignored grammar (文法) and words not commonly used, thereby giving unknown deep impressions while simultaneously embedding subtle allure.

Even a writer like Maupassant possessed unsurpassed skill in prose (文章). It was clear (明晳), concise prose. To summarize all this in one word, it is said to be a characteristic that Naturalist writers, relying on a scientific viewpoint or method, had to possess. This is because without distinctive prose, it is difficult to achieve precise description of surroundings, regions, life, or individuals.

One thing I want to mention here is that when discussing Naturalism, one cannot avoid talking about Impressionism and modern drama. But it has become too lengthy, and also difficult to proceed according to the initial plan, so regarding Naturalism, I will be satisfied with this much. Next, after outlining Neo-Romanticism, I will spend a few words on poetry, and then conclude this series on Modern Literature. <17>

**Summary**

The article by An Sŏ critiques Naturalism (자연파) in modern literature, characterizing it as a "pathological record" focused on the ugly, material, and "bestial" aspects of life, influenced by natural science. It contrasts this with Romanticism's (로맨파) idealism, arguing Naturalism rejects the spiritual. An Sŏ discusses Naturalism's emphasis on objective observation ("like a photographer"), the concept of the "human document" (fragments of life), the deterministic role of environment (周圍), detailed depiction of individuality (個性), and the importance of precise language (like Flaubert's *mot unique*). While acknowledging Naturalism's shortcomings (extremism, dryness), the author sees it as an unavoidable product of its scientific era, using the Russian proverb about not blaming the mirror for an ugly reflection. The author ultimately suggests a true understanding requires acknowledging both material and spiritual aspects.

From a historical perspective, this 1922 article is significant as it reflects the direct engagement of Korean intellectuals with contemporary Western literary theories like Naturalism during the Japanese colonial period. It shows the process of introducing, analyzing, and critiquing these movements, implicitly contrasting them with existing Korean literary traditions (mentioning works like *Changhanmong*). Historians might find the author's specific framing of Naturalism (pathological, bestialized, scientific), the references to European authors (Zola, Tolstoy, Ibsen, Flaubert), and the commentary on the state of contemporary Korean novels particularly interesting for understanding intellectual currents and the reception of modernity in colonial Korea.
