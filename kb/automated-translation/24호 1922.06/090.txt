Around the World, Over Mountains and Waters
						Il-u

Section 6: Late Spring, New Greenery

Ah! It has come! Spring has come! The lively scenes of the one-legged races and butterfly dances by the Monnett (Women's Hall name) girls, who were skating on the mirror-like ice rink of Green Lake (綠湖 Kŭrinreik), wander fondly in memory. Violet and rose bouquets proudly declare themselves the stars of the social world upon the chests of young women. Ah, the departed spring has returned, and the green shade deepens, but when will the spring of life come! The peerless spring of life, that spring which, once gone, cannot return—has it come? Is it coming? Is it going? That spring, which seems to intend to leave even as it arrives, is appreciated dearly.

As I greeted the late spring of university life, when the third act of the stage of struggle was about to open, the aspiring fighter, having ascended the podium as a contestant in the exhilarating drama of the academic sea—the intercollegiate debate competition—passionately argued over the issue, "Is it a flower garden or a battlefield?" That vivid performance, shouting down prejudice, was as comical as the announcement of victory was indeed a gratifying reality.

What ambition drives us humans, who idealize a world-like paradise where rare flowers bloom and wondrous herbs sway in clear fragrance, to create scenes of carnage with mountains of corpses and rivers of blood under explosive smoke and raining bullets? What mentality underlies the phenomenon where races, yearning for friendship like a gentle spring breeze, glare at each other, grind their teeth, and exchange iron fists! Ah, I want to go "to a garden filled with smiling flowers and fragrance, to a land of dancing and singing"! At the very least, I want to envision and see such a world, and breathe such air. Thus, transcending circumstances and situations,<61> I resolved to sing and enjoy a pleasant university life. While pursuing the regular university course, I entered the Conservatory of Music. I hesitate to say I fully attained the anticipated joy. But! But! The feeling of failure was not even in my thoughts. After finishing the graduation exams, the night before the farewell banquet was held, I became the proud performer selected to appear in the concert featuring students of the music school, the Conservatory of Music. By reciting poems like Longfellow's (롱펠너 Rongp’ellŏ) "Alla" (알나 Alla) and Ppŏsyŏm's (뻐쇰 Ppeosyŏm) "Clear Wind," my life as a Carnegie boy (칼네지 뽀이 K’alnejibboi) came to an end.

Chapter 4: The Third Act on the Stage of Struggle
Section 1: Twilight on the Shores of Lake Michigan

The beautiful city of Delaware (뗄나웨아 Ttellawae’a)! It feels like only yesterday that I found my way here, straightening my collar, deeming it the arena where I would come to know myself, yet three years (有 星霜 yu sŏngsang) have passed in this small town, endeared through tears and laughter. Looking up at the chapel spire soaring into the blue sky, I clasped my hands, shedding tears, and made a pact with myself, saying, "Let it not be a shameful departure; grant that I may graduate from university even if I die, and bid farewell with glory and success." That moment is recalled with sharp clarity. Gracious city of Delaware, the chapel spire that gave me much divine inspiration, the trustworthy professors who helped me know myself—as I prepared to leave Ohio Wesleyan University (웨을네안大學 Weŭllean Taehak), which had nurtured me, I felt a sense of gratitude, having gained much but contributed nothing. It made me feel the emotions of leaving a second homeland.

The time was precisely May 15, 1918. Departing from the memorable Pennsylvania Station in Delaware city, I headed towards Evanston (에반스톤 Ebansŭt’on) city in the state of Illinois (일니노이주 Illinoiju). My purpose in heading to Evanston was not definitively set. Making money was certainly not the objective. If I had intended to earn money,<62> Chicago (쉬카고 Swik'ago) city was the place. It seemed to be for study, but that too was not clearly decided internally. If study had truly been the objective, I would have headed further east, even by one step. It seemed the purpose was leisurely recuperation (閒養 hyanyang). But! But! I had not yet acknowledged myself as a fortunate child who could solely pursue leisure. In any case—it naturally became clear that the purpose was to rest while quietly thinking about the work ahead.

Evanston city is a residential village for the wealthy, adjoining Chicago, one of the foremost major cities in the United States, and a scenic spot (paradise) nestled along the shores of the great Lake Michigan (미시칸 大湖 Misik’an Taeho). In the educational world, it is famous for the scholarly prestige of Northwestern University, and in the religious world, it is renowned for the theological authority of Garrett Biblical Institute (깨랫聖經學院 Kkaeraet Sŏnggyŏng Hagwŏn). I had previously been introduced to President S (S氏 S Ssi) of Garrett Biblical Institute and Professor H (H氏 H Ssi) of that institute, so I felt no sense of unfamiliarity when heading towards Garrett. Although I hadn't thought of enrolling in that school, I was allowed to stay in the beautiful Garrett dormitory under the pretext of recuperating during the summer period.

Lake Michigan is vast and boundless, with no shadow of the opposite shore visible, yet entertainment boats come and go, drifting like pictures on the mirror-like lake surface. When the western sky turns red and the eastern sky brightens—when gentlemen and ladies from esteemed households stroll along the shore, matching their steps and dragging their short canes—I sit on a bench placed in a corner of the shore—in the woods—welcoming the clear breeze that blows gently, dreaming and sketching in my mind only the contents of the "script" for which I will appear as the protagonist on the new stage of struggle! Is it Lake Michigan under the moon! Or the moon in a mirror! The lake water ripples gently enough to make one doubt—but the fierce waves and counter-currents rising in my heart surge, forming a vast expanse. Is a gentle breeze trying to blow?—the tree leaves rustle; is a torrential rain about to fall?—frog croaks echo here and there, 'gaegol gaegol'; as if conveying some good news, as if foretelling some fortune or misfortune, drunk on hope—buried in the fog of doubt, I grip the bench with both hands, bow my head deeply, feeling sad and happy by turns, reading my own record to myself. "What happened with the scholarship application sent to C University (C 대학 C Taehak)? It probably failed. No matter how good my grades in the 3rd and 4th year courses are, I, with my unremarkable qualifications from the 1st and 2nd years, am truly a foolish child to hope for acceptance. But—still—won't there be some kind of news? Surely! And what does Mrs. J (J 婦人 J Puin) think! Does she think I'll just melt away in this corner of the institute, in some corner of this region! But! She will surely offer some kind of opinion! Surely she won't say nothing at all!" "Hello! What are you doing here!" I snapped awake at the voice of a friend slapping my back. "Were you<63> sleeping or dreaming! Eh?" "Neither sleep nor dream, if you say so! So, did the mailman come?" "He already came! Mr. Ro (로君 Ro Kun), didn't you get your letters?" "Letters—plural! You mean several came?" "So you were just sleeping without even noticing those letters? The mailman came carrying a load that could break his shoulder! What letters do you receive so many of every day?" "And how about you! You're saying what I should be saying!" "Stop the chatter and go see your letters! Look here, Mr. Ro! Won't you come preach at our church this Sunday? You absolutely must come this time! Homemade pie (홈메드 파이 Hommedŭ p’ai)—chicken pot pie (치큰-팟-파이 Ch’ik’ŭn-p’at-p’ai)! Mmm—it's truly good! Would even the millionaires of New York ever taste such flavor?" "Pie pot pie—you only talk about pies day and night! You ask me to preach, yet keep talking nonsense about pies!" "Aha! If you don't even taste the pie, what pleasure is there in being a village church pastor (牧師 moksa)! Don't you know that pleasure? Cherry (櫻桃 aengdo) season, cherry pie; strawberry season, strawberry pie; peach (복숭아 poksunga) season, peach pie—you get to eat whatever you want according to the season! Look at my face; would the top pastor in Chicago city, earning an annual salary (年俸 yŏnbong) of $10,000, be as well off as me, a village pastor earning $1,000?" "Hey—stop the nonsense; pastors like you with bellies like Mount Namsan go to the Slaughterhouse (屠獸場 tosuch’ang) Hell when they die! Stop the talk about eating! If you're not prepared to have a heart that sheds tears for others and boils with blood for society (社會 sahoe), then give up the title of Pastor! If you dislike the first seat in Hell! Even if I go preach, it won't be different words; these words I'm saying to you now will be those words!" "Hey! You really sound like a revival meeting (復興會 puhŭnghoe) pastor, don't you? You seem like a disciple of Billy Sunday (뻴네 선데이 Ppelle Sŏndei), don't you?" "You know, but you're slightly wrong! Instead of 'lay believer (平信徒 p’yŏngsindo) revival pastor,' how about saying 'Pastor's revival meeting pastor'!" "Go look at your letters! Don't make noise, but you absolutely must come this Sunday. A church member said they'd come with a horse carriage to meet us both!" "Sunday's matter isn't something to worry about, is it! Is it true? There are letters for me?" "If you don't believe me, go see! Not believing is also a sin (罪罪 choeak)!"

I slowly head towards the dormitory. I think! From whom, what letters! From C University! From Mrs. J! From whom, what letters have come! Ah! They really came! From C University, from Mrs. J! My heart pounds, and the feeling of doubt intensifies. Tearing open the envelope, I enter my room and scrutinize it like a juggler examining the '알' (al) character on an 8-character divination board. What does the University say! Aha! Success! As much as my heart<64> feels relieved, I also sense the heavy responsibility demanded by C University. What does Mrs. J say! Whether it works out or not isn't a major concern, but desire is naturally limitless, so I open it with anticipation! Aha! Brocade added upon embroidery (錦上疊繡 Kŭmsangjŏmsu - idiom for adding splendor to splendor)! With this, the final stage of struggle in North America began to open.

Before leaving Evanston, there is an article that I cannot hesitate to add, borrowing a portion of this page.

Evanston city, as mentioned, is a residential village connected to Chicago city. Chicago city is a renowned commercial and industrial center located in the central part of North America. Our students, studying in various places, gathered in that city during the summer period with the aim of preparing funds for school expenses. Far away from our homeland, having come to a land with different waters and unfamiliar mountains, hoping for the same glory and undertaking the same struggles, we indeed shared the same feelings. To console and encourage each other, we held a summer gathering (夏令會 haryŏnghoe) on the shores of Lake Michigan. Rather than detailing the schedule of our student summer gathering, I want to talk about how joyfully we played. When about 30 young compatriots gathered together, feelings of sorrow were aroused more than a mood of joy, so even when we spoke, the words were of mutual consolation and encouragement, and even when we sang, it was the sorrow of homesickness, thinking of the homeland. Our desires, facing the adverse fate of the times, were efforts like seeking an oasis in the desert or trying to see radium on a deeply overcast day. In the vortex of such an atmosphere, each person suppressed their own sorrow, "concerned about provoking the grief of their brothers," striving to draw out courage from each other and wear expressions of delight. After enjoying outdoor sports and boating on the lake, we gathered on the golden sands of the Lake Michigan shore, watched the setting sun in the western sky, collected scattered pieces of wood, and lit a bonfire (뽄파이아 pponp’aia). That bonfire, finding its moment in the twilight dusk, blazed higher, its force reaching for the heavens. Our bodies, chilled by the cool breeze coming across Lake Michigan, drew near the red flames, creating a kind of harmonious family gathered around a fireplace (파이아풀네쓰 p’aiap’ullessŭ). Having thus consoled each other's accumulated feelings of solitude, late at night on the lakeshore, as we covered the red bonfire embers with sand and exchanged farewell handshakes, we blessed each other with health and success.

Before heading towards Chicago (市俄古 Siŏgo) and New York (紐育 Niyuyok) cities, I wish to introduce Chicago city. Chicago city is the second largest metropolis in America. It is a city of great prosperity comparable to New York City. Although its harbor (港灣 hangman) facilities are not excellent, it lies sprawled across a vast, boundless plain, and its population indeed reaches 3 million. I was inclined to doubt how a city without a good harbor could prosper so greatly. However,<65> upon examining the location of the metropolis, I discovered the reason. While it embraces the great Lake Michigan to the southwest, stretching 180 miles (哩 ri) like a vast ocean, it is also the center of the American continental railroad (美州大陸鐵道 Miju Taeryuk Ch'ŏldo), serving as the hub for the collection and distribution of products such as grains (穀物 kongmul), livestock (家畜 kach'uk), lumber (材木 chaemok), and iron materials (鐵材 ch'ŏljae), amounting to over 500 million dollars annually.

The city, being the center of railway lines radiating and connecting east, west, south, and north, sees 3,000 trains arriving and departing in a single day and night. Originally, Chicago is famous as the world's number one city for soot and smoke (煤煙 maeyŏn). Throughout the city streets, black smoke envelops the space. An elevated railway (高架鐵道 kogach'ŏldo) extending over 2,000 miles, a subway (地下鐵道 chihach'ŏldo) (섭웨이 sŏpwei) stretching 70 miles, and streetcars (電車 chŏnch'a) covering 1,000 miles disperse in all directions, reaching everywhere. Adding to the various railways that transport mountain-like crowds to and fro, the horns of automobiles, almost too numerous to count, startle the spirits of the diverse pedestrians.

From the lakefront park, gazing at the vast, blue expanse of the great lake, I shift my steps towards the lush forests, art museum, and museum (博物館 pangmulgwan) of Jackson Park (짹손公園 Tchaekson Kongwŏn). The extensive park, covering an area of about 600 pyeong (approx. 1983 sq meters), is adorned with ponds where fish swim peacefully, fountain statues (噴水銅像 punsu tongsang) scattering silvery droplets, strange rocks, and gardens (까든 kkadŭn) in full bloom with myriad flowers, interspersed with old cedars and ancient cypresses (古杉老檜 kosam nohoe), and tall buildings and large pavilions (高樓巨閣 koru kŏgak). The sight of beautiful women clad in light, fine clothes (錦衣 kŭmŭi), accompanied by young gallant men (俠士 hyŏpsa) who appear to be their lovers, strolling side by side, was a living scene (活景 hwalgyŏng) surpassing a famous painting (明畵 myŏnghwa).

The Great Slaughterhouse (大屠畜場 Tae Toch'ukchang). Anyone who enters Chicago city even once cannot neglect to visit and inspect the famous slaughterhouse. At the renowned stockyards' (스탁야드 sŭt'akyadŭ) Great Slaughterhouse, several thousand sturdy men (壯漢 changhan), whose actions make one doubt whether they are demons (鬼神 kwisin) or possess human nature (人性 insŏng), labor (勞働 nodong).
It is a place filled with the air of killing, where 210,000 cattle (牛 u), 20,000 pigs (豚 ton), and tens of thousands of other calves (犢牛 togu), sheep (羊 yang), goats (山羊 sanyang), etc., are slaughtered (屠殺 tosal) daily. The slaughtering also utilizes machinery (機械 kigye). Numerous living creatures (生物 saengmul) are driven into pens (柵 ch'aek)—the cattle, sheep, and pigs, forced to move forward, are struck dead with large iron hammers (鐵槌 ch'ŏlch'wi) and thrown out of the pens. Elsewhere, throats (咽喉 inhhu) are slit, hides (皮 p'i) are stripped, heads (首 su) are cut off, and meat (肉 yuk) is carved. It is transported urgently (星火的 sŏnghwajŏk) by train (汽車 kich'a), and before an hour passes, it is turned into canned goods (鑵詰 kwanjil) and transported to the market (市場 sijang). It does not seem like human work. Hearing the screams (悲鳴 pimyŏng) of the cattle and sheep, while cursing (咀呪 chŏju) human cruelty (殘酷 chanhok), the feeling that meat-eating (肉食 yuksik) is a sin (罪惡 choeak) was also intensely aroused. I proclaimed to myself that Tolstoy's (톨쓰토이 T'olssŭt'oi) vegetarianism (菜食主義 ch'aesikchuŭi) is indeed the great way (大道 taedo) of human ethics (人倫 illyun).<66>

**Summary**

This installment of Il-u's travelogue covers his transition from Ohio Wesleyan University to Evanston, Illinois, near Chicago, in the spring/summer of 1918. He reflects on his university experiences, including participating in debates and studying music, before moving to stay temporarily at Garrett Biblical Institute. While contemplating his future, he receives positive news about a scholarship application to "C University" and encouragement from a "Mrs. J," setting the stage for his next academic pursuit. He describes a poignant summer gathering of fellow Korean students by Lake Michigan, marked by shared feelings of homesickness and mutual encouragement amidst their struggles abroad. The piece concludes with vivid descriptions of Chicago's bustling industry, transportation networks, parks, and a particularly graphic and critical account of the city's massive slaughterhouses, which evokes strong feelings of repulsion and prompts reflection on vegetarianism.

From a historical perspective, this article offers valuable insights into the life of a Korean student studying in the US during the Japanese colonial period. It highlights the academic aspirations, financial challenges (scholarship need), and the strong sense of camaraderie and shared national sentiment among Korean students overseas, who gathered for mutual support despite being far from their occupied homeland. The author's observations on American modernity—university life, urban development (Chicago), industrial scale (slaughterhouses)—provide a Korean perspective on the West at the time, and his moral critique of the slaughterhouse reflects personal and potentially broader cultural reactions to industrial processes.

第6節 暮春新綠
아! 왓고나! 봄이 왓고나! 그린레익 「綠湖」의 거울가튼 얼음마당에서 스케팅하던 모네(女子館 이름)껄들의 외다리 競走와 나비춤의 活景은 사랑스럽게 記憶에 徘徊하고 바이올네트와 薔薇꼿 보궤트는 靑春女子들의 가슴 우에서 社交界의 明星인 것을 스스르 자랑한다, 아아! 갓든 봄 다시 와서 綠陰이 저믈어 가는데 人生의 봄 언제 오나! 둘업는 人生의 봄, 한번 가면 다시 못 오는 그 봄, 왓는가? 오는가? 가는가? 오면서도 가랴는 그 봄 貴엽게도 感賞된다,
大學生活의 暮春을 마즈면서 奮鬪舞臺의 第3幕이 열리랴할 때 學海의 快劇인 校際辯論競爭의 選手로 登壇한, 奮鬪兒는 「花園이냐 戰場이냐?」하는 問題로 偏見을 絶叫하는 그 活劇이야말로 滑稽的인것만치 得勝의 公布도 亦是 痛快한 現狀이엇다.

市俄古市 紐育市를 向하기 前에 쉬카고市를 紹介하고저 한다. 市俄古(쉬카고)市는 美國에 第2의 大都會다. 니유욕市를 副할만한 大繁盛의 市로서 港灣의 便利는 良好치 못하나 廣茫無際한 大平野에 橫臥하야 人口는 實로 300萬에 달하엿다. 良港을 가지지 못한 市로서 어찌 그와 가티 繁盛하냐 疑心하려 하엿다. 그러나 그＜65＞ 都會의 位置를 살필 때 그 理由를 發見하엿다. 西南部에는 180哩나리는 恰然히 大洋과 如한 미시칸大湖를 끼고 잇는 日時에 美州大陸鐵道의 中心地가 되어 1年 5億萬弗 以上에 達하는 穀物, 家畜, 材木, 鐵材 等의 産物이 此處를 中心으로 하야 集散한다.
市는 鐵道線路의 中心이 되어 東西南北에 散布連絡하엿슴으로 一晝夜에 3,000의 汽車가 發着한다. 元來 市俄古는 世界 第一 煤煙의 都會로 有名하다. 市街 到處에 黑煙이 사모친 空間을 延長한 2,000여哩에 高架鐵道와 70哩에 亙하는 地下鐵道(섭웨이)와 1,000哩에 及하는 電車가 四方에 散通하야 八方에 達한다. 山과 가튼 群衆을 運去運來하는 各種의 鐵道를 더하야 殆히 그 數를 헤지 못할만한 自動車의 喇叭소리는 雜多한 通行人의 氣血을 놀래게 한다.
湖畔에서 公園에 碧波渺茫한 大湖를 바라보면서 짹손公園의 鬱蒼한 森林과 美術館, 博物館을 向하야 步調를 옴긴다. 面積 約 600坪이나 되는 廣濶한 公園은 靜波魚游들과 銀屑을 뿌리는 듯한 噴水銅像과 怪石이며 百花爛漫한 까든으로 古杉老檜와 高樓巨閣의 建物들을 間入丹粧하엿다. 輕快한 錦衣를 몸에 두른 美人들은 情人으로 보이는 靑年俠士들의 그림자를 가티하야 雙雙히 散步하는 光景은 1幅의 明畵보다 勝한 活景이엇다.
大屠畜場 누구나 1次 市俄古市에 入한 以上에 반듯이 訪問視察을 度外로 示치 못할 곳은 곳 有名한 屠畜場이다. 著名한 스탁야드의 大屠畜場에는 鬼神이냐 人性이냐 疑心할만한 幾千名의 壯漢들이 勞働한다.
每日 21萬頭의 牛, 2萬頭의 豚, 其外 幾萬의 犢牛, 羊, 山羊 等을 屠殺하는 殺氣騰騰한 곳이다. 屠殺도 機械應用이다. 多數의 生物을 柵內에 몰아들여- 할수 업시 前進하는 牛, 羊, 豚畜 等을 大鐵槌 打殺하야 柵外에 投出하면 他方에서 咽喉를 지르고 皮를 剝하며 首를 切하고 肉을 割하야 汽車로 星火的 運送을 하면 一時間을 지나기 前에 鑵詰에 化하야 市場에 運搬케 된다. 人間의 作業가티 보이지 아니한다. 牛羊의 悲鳴을 들을 때 人間의 殘酷을 스스로 咀呪하는 同時에 肉食은 罪惡이라는 感까지 激發하엿다. 「톨쓰토이」氏의 菜食主義가 실노 人倫의 大道가 아니냐고 自呼하엿다.＜66＞
