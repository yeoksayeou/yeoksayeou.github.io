Magazine Title: 개벽 第24호
Publication Date: 1922-06-01
Article Title: 먼저 有識有産者側으로부터 反省하라 (First, Reflect from the Side of the Educated and Properited)
Author: 起瀍
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Opinion Piece)

First, Reflect from the Side of the Educated and Properited
						起瀍
I do not know about the times of great antiquity when there were no records. Nor do I know about the matters of 10 years or 100 years from now. However, from the time since records of life existed until today, and from today for some time into the future, there have certainly been the educated (有識者) and the uneducated (無識者), the propertied (有産者) and the propertyless (無産者); they existed, they exist, and they will exist again. It would certainly be good if such distinctions did not exist from the beginning, but what can be done about what already existed, and what can be done about what exists now? This is, of course, a temporary, transitional phenomenon produced by the intersection of the intelligence of the few and the lack of intelligence of the many in a certain course of human development, and it is an undeniable fact—and must become a fact—that it possesses no permanence whatsoever. If, for some period, our society cannot be without distinctions like the educated and the uneducated, the propertied and the propertyless, and consequently, those with real power (有實權者) and those without real power (無實權者), what would the state of society be? Needless to say, when the educated and the uneducated interact, the side of the educated is superior; when the propertied and the propertyless interact, the side of the propertied is superior; and when those with real power and those without real power interact, the side of the powerful is superior. This, regrettably, is the current reality that cannot be denied.<4> In the current situation, the first thing the uneducated, the propertyless, and the powerless can put forward is their majority number. However, even that majority cannot become any kind of organized force unless it achieves disciplined unity (團結), and to achieve disciplined unity, they must first possess the self-awareness (自覺) and undergo the trials (試鍊) sufficient to build it. But self-awareness further requires the development of intelligence (智能), and trials further require the passage of time (時間). If, without adequate self-awareness or adequate trials, they merely rely on being the majority and seek to achieve equality in intellect or wealth, they will first fail to demonstrate the power (威力 - the force befitting the majority) needed to seize and obtain it. Even if they were to manifest it in some form, it would be nothing more than a type of violence (暴力), unable to restrain the organized power of the minority. Conceding a step further, even if they manage to restrain it, what is gained through violence can be disturbed again by violence, ultimately only causing the catastrophe of mutual collapse (彼此의 共倒) for humanity.

Mutual collapse! Since I cannot live well, you shouldn't either. Since it's rice cake I can't eat, let me just stuff my belly full of chaff there! Such things are decidedly acts that one cannot dare commit under the name of 'human.' At least, until one becomes a villain wearing the skin of jackals and wolves (豺狼) and possessing the heart of dogs and pigs (狗彘), one cannot dare do such things.
However, what is the reality? Humans are not necessarily animals that choose only righteous deeds, nor are they clean animals that live solely by the dictates of reason (理智). Humans are capable of committing any act when impulsively driven (衝動) by circumstances, and humans are capable of daringly perpetrating any reckless deed when corrupted this way or that. In this sense, we cannot simply be content with broadly saying, "Such and such is wrong. Those who commit such acts are thoughtless,"<5> thereby distinguishing good and evil (善惡) or offering cold criticism. We must absolutely take a step further and devise measures to prevent this society and its people from being driven to the necessity or opportunity of committing such reckless acts.

Then, who are those who will devise and implement such measures—in other words, who will bear that responsibility? Of course, the answer will vary depending on the commentator. However, I assert in one word that it will not be other people, but rather the few people in our society today who possess comparatively more knowledge and property, and who, with that knowledge and property as their background, possess relatively more of the real power (實權) in the current society. To put it urgently, if all our affairs go well, it is their merit; if they go poorly, it is their fault. They are the ones who can simultaneously incur merit and guilt towards us, and who can simultaneously receive praise and curses. The broad masses (多數의 民衆) must always promise to respond cooperatively to their well-intentioned guidance while also scrutinizing their words and actions (云爲). I now wish to urge consideration from the educated (有識者) and propertied (有産者) among our brethren with these few brief words, reflecting on the current state of our society and proposing for the future of our nation.

To the Educated (有識者) Sirs
Educated Sirs. In this land where we still lack a system of compulsory education (義務敎育) and thus opportunities for developing the intelligence (智能) of the general populace are extremely scarce, a few of you alone were blessed with good fortune and favorable opportunities. You became those who know what the majority does not (in terms of thought - 思想) and those capable of what the majority is not (in terms of skill - 技術). Regardless of whether some of you remain quietly at home<6> or are prominent in society, the words you speak, the actions you take, the writings you publish—even if they are just one word, one step, or one short phrase—become that which awakens (警醒) many people, that which they follow as a rule (遵則), and that which they pay attention to (注視). Your position relative to the people of society is truly noble (尊貴), and your duty (責務) concerning the rise and fall of the nation is truly grave. The work you do cannot but be exceptionally excellent (卓越); the attitude you adopt cannot but be exceptionally sincere (誠實); the views you hold cannot but be exceptionally wise (聰明). However, as I observe you, not only are there those among you who fail to show evidence of excellence, sincerity, or wisdom in their work or attitude, but it reaches the extreme where some twist their tongues, falsify their writings, and wear false masks, manipulating the ignorance of the masses with their own petty cleverness (小慧), content and arrogant with the slight fame and fortune (名利) and measure of conceit (自惚心) gained thereby. Speaking of this truly makes one's hair stand on end (毛骨이 悚然).
Then, what is it that I ask of the educated today? I would answer thus:

First, I ask that you be thorough in the attitude you adopt. Once you have established a certain purpose (目的), whether for your own self or for the future of society and the nation, you must proceed straight ahead on the path towards that purpose, even if there are guns (銃) and knives (칼) along the way. You must not be seduced by the illusory flowers (虛花) or false scenery (假景) appearing on both banks of that path and stray onto a side track (橫路), nor should you hesitate (躊躇) midway anticipating the many difficulties (多艱) ahead. Recently, among some intellectuals, some vigorously promote certain ideologies (主義) or strive for the progress of certain movements (運動). They all say this is the shortcut (捷徑) to reach the original goal<7> of planning for the life and prosperity of our nation. If it truly is a shortcut, there is nothing more to say, but I wonder if this isn't the beginning of taking a divergent path, mesmerized by the illusory flowers and false scenery on the banks, or if it isn't a sign of despairing at the future difficulties and giving up (暴棄) on oneself? Although the reporter himself wishes to believe that surely there's no reason for such things, somehow I still feel concerned.
In all matters, one must certainly take the proper path (正經大道). Empty displays or schemes (策略) are not so valuable. This is even more true in affairs planned together with the less intelligent masses. If one succeeds in one out of ten cases, it is luck (僥倖), but what if nine out of ten end in crushing defeat (慘敗), either inviting irreparable destruction (破滅) or inducing a sweet slumber (甘眼) from which one cannot awaken? Matters like 'what ideology' or 'what movement' require much consideration in their promotion or progress.

Furthermore, the educated class must be even more thorough in all things concerning themselves and their own families (家庭). There are those who frequently shout for others to earn their bread by the sweat of their brow, yet they themselves lack a fixed occupation (職業) and wander about, becoming a kind of irregular vagrant (浮浪者). There are also those who, whenever the opportunity arises, put forth the slogan (標榜) of being servants (奴僕) of society, yet in reality, they continue the old-style luxurious life (豪奢生活) (going out with carriages, coming in giving orders—to their family or servants). This is decidedly not the way to be faithful to one's principles, nor is it the way of one devoted to the work of society. We today must first act ourselves and practice, possessing the truth of sages whose own bodies become the example (率) and whose own voices become the measure (度). Mere words or writings are useless. Although this sounds commonplace, I believe many of our shortcomings—especially those of the educated—lie here.<8>

Next, the educated should possess the humble virtue (謙遜한 心德) befitting the educated. Just as the likely shortcomings of the uneducated are rudeness (無禮) and disorderliness (亂暴), the likely shortcomings of the educated are arrogance (驕傲) and self-satisfaction (自滿). Both arrogance and self-satisfaction arise from not possessing the virtue of humility. Just as water in a small vessel makes an unusually loud sloshing sound when only half-full, the arrogance of the educated will be greater the smaller their innate disposition (心性) and capacity (器局), or the lesser the amount of knowledge they possess; the degree of arrogance increases proportionally to how small or insufficient it is. Therefore, people with shallow dispositions, or intellectuals at the initial stage of entering civilization (文明), are more easily afflicted by the tendency towards arrogance and self-satisfaction.
Quietly observing the intellectuals among our people recently, it seems quite many lack the virtue of humility. Among them, the most severe is the tendency to forcibly impose (强制) upon others what one has felt, realized, or wishes to do—excessive promotion (宣傳). Even those with or without the knowledge equivalent to a middle school graduate, when they open their mouths or take up a pen, all say, "Arise! Awaken!" each seeking to become the leader of all people. Or, if they have even a vague opinion (主見), they immediately write it down or speak it out, seeking the approval (贊同) of the majority. The statements (聲明書), denunciations (聲討文), drum-beating incidents (鳴鼓事件), etc., that frequently appear on the surface of society recently, reveal one aspect of this tendency. And another detestable thing is using the newspapers, magazines, or other writings one is involved with to dare write slanderous (中傷) articles about individuals or groups, or even if not necessarily slander, to willfully distort (誣曲) the writing. Thus, the recent intellectuals, increasingly flowing towards arrogance, fail to gain the trust (信任) of the masses, are either kept at a distance (敬遠) or cursed (咀呪) by them, and it feels as though even the few<9> intellectuals who exist cannot become close friends with the masses. Is this all? Intellectuals themselves, content with their current possessions and pleasures, do not engage in further contemplation (思索) and research (硏究). They intend to muddle through life with whatever little or much they once acquired. Consequently, even those who initially had considerable reputation become objects generally discarded after a few years. Society lacks fresh vitality (生新氣), and the words and actions of the masses lack an academic (學術的) basis. Is this not truly pathetic? Our intellectuals must first cultivate the virtue of humility (謙德) befitting intellectuals and share (共分) the benefits arising from that humility with the general society.

Next, what I want to say is that among our intellectuals (mainly referring to scholar-type intellectuals), there is too little contemplation and research concerning our own affairs. For example, those interested in medicine (醫學) do not conduct scientific research on the local diseases (土疾) endemic to various parts of Korea (朝鮮) or on things like Sasang medicine (四象學), which is a part of traditional Korean medicine. Thinkers (思想家) show no signs of systematically observing or researching matters concerning Korea's inherent ethics (倫理) and morals (道德), or the prevailing thought tendencies (思想 傾向) among Koreans today. Regarding social issues (社會問題), they mostly engage in vague discussions about the women's issue (婦人問題), the labor issue (勞働問題), or communism (共産主義), anarchism (無政府主義), etc., lacking systematic introductions and organized plans. They seem to pay little attention to practical issues (實際問題) inherent to Korea, such as the landlord-tenant issue (地主 小作人 問題), the Yangban-commoner issue (兩班 常人 問題), the master-servant issue (主人 下人 問題), the old-young issue (老少 問題), and numerous others. To force a point, the landlord-tenant issue appears occasionally in discussions and actual movements, and the Korean Labor Mutual Aid Society (朝鮮勞働共濟會) is attempting to find a solution (解決) for it, but research and countermeasures (對策) are still not thorough.<10> Putting aside the issues of the adequacy of its content (內容) and the rightness of its claims (主張), there have only been the research by Sŏnu Chŏn (鮮于全씨) on the problem of Korean people's livelihood and the discussion by Yi Ch'unwŏn (李春園) on the reconstruction (改造) of the Korean national character (民族性), published in this magazine (本誌), which aroused some public discussion. In literature and art (文藝), most works are either translations (飜譯) or adaptations (飜案). If there is any original creation (創作) among them, nine out of ten are shallow works concerning the love issue (戀愛 問題)—(I do not call them shallow because they write about love)—. Thinking about the point that there seems to be not a single truly artistic work (藝術的 作品) worth seeing that captures some major inherent Korean problem or unusual situation, one can gauge how unfaithful our intellectuals have been towards us. I especially wish to ask for the reflection of intellectuals on this point. There are still one or two more things I want to say regarding the educated. However, I will first conclude here and now turn my words

To the Propertied (有産者) Sirs.
Propertied Sirs. While a significant number of your brethren cannot find a place of employment (就職處) or a place for farming (作農터), unable to produce cooking smoke (炊烟) twice a day, supporting the old (老) and carrying the young (幼), crossing the Tumen River (豆滿江) to the north, the Yalu River (鴨綠江) to the west, and the Hyŏnhae Sea (玄海) to the east, wandering (流離) westward; you, the propertied class, whose storehouses (庫間) alone are abundant and whose seats (座席) alone are comfortable. Not only that, while boys and girls aged 7 or 8 are crushed under factory machinery and rot in rural straw huts, your children alone are wrapped in brocade clothes (錦衣) and get to attend kindergarten (幼穉園); you, the propertied class. Towards such sirs as you, there is only one thing we have to say. Namely,<11> "Utilize the wealth (富) you currently possess most effectively (最有効), and do so spontaneously for your own happiness."
Then, what is the method to utilize that wealth most effectively? Propertied Sirs, you must not associate "utilizing effectively" with the commonly practiced usury (高利貸金法). According to what I've heard, a certain wealthy person in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), still finding his current wealth insufficient, engages in usury under the name of retainers (門客), sometimes even standing directly before the telephone mouthpiece (電話口) himself to guard (警戒) and supervise (監督) it. Furthermore, not only certain other propertied families, but also merchants (市商者) or bank employees (銀行會社員) who possess some idle money (遊金) (saying this, of course, does not mean all possessors of idle money do so), there is almost no one today who does not engage in the lending business as a kind of sideline (副業) besides their main occupation. In such times, trying to suggest something other than the lending business as the most effective way to utilize wealth might first invite a scoff (鼻笑) from you, the propertied sirs. However, I cannot refrain from speaking. I cannot refrain from speaking my mind.

Propertied Sirs, first, establish organizations for meeting (會合) and fellowship (親睦) among yourselves. According to what I have heard, the current propertied people, who lack no discomfort in food, clothing, or other matters, each think "you are you, and I am I," having no interaction with each other. Their households are frequented only by local elders/officials (舍音?), other dependents (附食人), and scriveners (代書業者), with few others coming and going. Consequently, their wealth becomes increasingly sealed off (封鎖), and their views become increasingly obstinate and narrow-minded (固陋). While I cannot suddenly propose concrete methods here, first, Propertied Sirs, you must establish some kind of meeting organization, in whatever form, where you can first communicate your intentions and foster fellowship. I cannot enumerate examples here, but in other countries, there are meeting organizations for the propertied<12> in various forms. Currently, we do have things like Chambers of Commerce, but those are meeting organizations established for specific business people to discuss specific business matters, inherently differing in significance from what is being discussed here. In short, if there were an appropriate fellowship organization in some form, allowing for occasional meetings, it seems the path to effectively utilizing wealth would naturally open up.

Next, as the first step in utilizing their wealth, our propertied people must make active investments (積極的 投資) in various enterprises (各種 企業). Of course, in the current situation, planning new businesses and making active investments in them likely involves various reasons for hesitation (躊躇). The profit generated from such investments will certainly be inferior to the income from the lending business, and sometimes they might end in complete failure. However, if things remain as they are, all our economic rights and interests (經濟的 利權) will be entirely confiscated (沒收) by foreigners (外人). Already, more than half have been confiscated. In such circumstances, we cannot invest solely for the purpose of receiving dividends (配當) of a few percent. We cannot but do it with the mindset of repairing our own fortress walls (城壘) to defend against foreign aggression (外侮), and the immediate losses incurred (if losses occur) must be considered as the defense cost (防禦費) for that purpose. If we do this for a certain period, the businesses undertaken will establish a foundation (基礎), and the general economic world (一般 經濟界) will gradually revive (復活). If we do not act with such a mindset and merely calculate the presence or absence of immediate dividends when deciding to invest, our industrial world (産業界) will never be stimulated (振作).
Look! For several years now, how fervent has the Japanese (日本人) investment in Korea been, and how fervent is the American (美國人) investment enthusiasm towards the Far East (極東方面)?<13> Didn't recent newspapers report on American management of Siberia (西伯利), involving the migration (移植) of 150,000 people, with an advance party (先發隊) of 100-person groups already departed for that land? And what about the Genoa Conference (「께노아」會議) held earlier? Wasn't its main purpose to restore (恢復) the economic world of Europe (歐洲)? At this time when people all over the world are engrossed in acquiring (獲得) and retrieving (挽回) economic power, can our propertied class alone afford to sleep soundly (高寢安眠)? Do we have nothing to retrieve or acquire? Such thoughts are surely not absent in anyone, but at this time when we don't know what to do because we have nothing in hand, you, the Propertied Sirs, who alone possess the funds to act, please reconsider with your sense of responsibility.

And again, regarding the Propertied Sirs, there is one bitter thing I must say: your attitude and actions concerning public works/charity (公益事業).
Of course, not all of you, but a great majority among you, if you donate (寄贈) some amount of money or goods to a public cause, you do not consider it a moral duty you should naturally perform, but rather attempt to use it as (1) a means to angle for your own reputation (名聲), or (2) a scheme (策略) to protect (擁護) your already acquired position and honor. It's something that occasionally appears even on page 3 of the newspapers: a certain wealthy person (富豪) in Kyŏngsŏng recently invented a most curious new plan (新案). If there is a filial son or virtuous wife (孝子烈婦) somewhere (limited to the old-style filial piety and virtue), he immediately sends a gold half-ring (金半指?) or gold cup (金盃), and has this published (揭載) in the newspaper, thereby first seeking the favor (歡心) of the recipient and those around them, and then seeking the admiration (嘆賞) of people everywhere.<14> The technique (其術) is indeed clever. But the intention (心事) is base (陋) and wicked (奸). Considering his psychology, regarding public benefit, he intends to first maintain his social standing (體面) and fend off criticism arising from various quarters with that level of effort, and then do as he pleases with all other income. Thinking a bit deeper, it's likely an intention to protect the traditional moral thought system (道德 思想 制度) for even one day longer through such means, thereby further solidifying (堅固) his own base (地盤). What impure conduct (擧措)! Such thinking is likely not limited to one wealthy individual but is a psychology common to many propertied families. Even when donating (捐出) 1 or 2 won, they worry that their benevolence (慈惠) might be buried and obscured (埋沒). For someone with an annual income (輸入) of at least 100,000 won, what boast is there really in donating a few won or tens of won? Yet, they act as if performing great charity (施善) with something as insignificant as one hair from nine oxen (9牛の一毛). We absolutely cannot overlook such things.
Propertied Sirs, take a step forward from your past practices and truly allocate the surplus (剩餘) of your wealth to society for the common welfare (共同福利) of the people. If we were to see public facilities (公的 施設) such as museums (博物館), sports grounds (體育場), charity hospitals (慈善醫療院), youth centers (靑年會館), children's centers (少年會館), and others brilliantly equipped (燦然히 具備) due to your special benevolence, what a splendid thing it would be! Would this only be the happiness of a third party excluding yourselves? Would it not also directly be your own happiness? Without mincing words, I will add a couple more general remarks to conclude this record for the propertied.<15>

Of course, there were reasons why things became this way, but anyway, the thinking of many people up to today has been abnormally misguided in many respects. The most<15> abnormal psychology among them is the belief that one can be splendid alone, no matter how ugly (醜惡) one's surroundings are, and the extreme belief among some that one's own goodness and beauty (善美) are magnified precisely because the surroundings are ugly. To put it colloquially (卑近하게), it's like thinking that even if the room one lives in (居住) is squalid (醜陋), it's fine as long as one sits adorned in brocade clothes (錦衣) on a brocade mat (錦席). To put it even more closely, it's like thinking that even if one's hands and feet are buried in filth (汚泥), it's fine as long as one's face (顔面) is kept clean. Thus, they not only failed to strive to maintain balance (均衡) between their surroundings and themselves, but they also sought to preserve their own dignity and prestige (威位) by making the general surroundings even more inferior (低劣) than themselves. The old-style family system (家庭制) with numerous servants' quarters (下人房, 行廊房) housing messy male and female attendants (隨從者), and other actions of the superior classes towards the masses, can all be said to be expressions of such a spirit.
However, let us think calmly. Can any single individual be magnificent alone, transcending their surroundings? Can they be good and beautiful alone? When all shapes and sounds of ugliness constantly prick one's five senses (五官), how can one pretend to be unrelated? Logically speaking, the master's greatness is not enhanced because the surroundings are base and inferior; rather, the master's greatness becomes even greater because the surroundings are splendid.
Propertied Sirs, if you look around your surroundings with such new feelings, what do you see? Your gardens (庭園) might be clean, but what is the appearance (貌樣) of your brethren living outside those gardens? And what is the appearance of the various subsidiary people who attend you within those gardens? We must live together. We must make it so that we can live together. Are these words solely for the sake of your surroundings?<16>

Conclusion (結言)
Having digressed, I have thus far stated several points of my feelings towards the educated and the propertied. From now on, I will reveal the true intention that compels me to write this article and turn to the conclusion.
I don't know the reason, but observing the way our society has been developing recently, there is undeniably a new tendency towards mutual suspicion (猜疑) and, consequently, mutual collapse/rupture (潰裂). It seems like some bond (맷기) that connected people is beginning to break (끈허지기), and the evil situation (惡現狀) of some past time, where each acted for their own interest (各自 爲心) and each acted for their own faction (各自 爲黨), seems about to repeat itself. How can we not fear (恐懼) and be cautious (戒愼)? If this is an unfortunate fact, the causes (原因) for it are certainly not just one or two. Even common sense (常識) would count several causes. However, one significant cause among them can be counted as the arrogance (驕傲) (even if arising unconsciously) possessed by those people in today's society who have knowledge, who have property, or who, because they have knowledge and money, grasp the real power in various aspects of the current society. In other words, those who possess knowledge, in the position of distributing that knowledge to the masses in various forms, are suspected (嫌) of failing to add sincerity (眞誠) and fairness (公平) on top of the distributed knowledge. As for those with property, it is not only rare (稀少) for them to use that property for the sake of the masses, but even among those rare few, many mix in crafty schemes (奸策) aimed at maintaining or boosting their reputation or fending off criticism against themselves with some small favor (施惠). Such errors (過誤) cause the masses to harbor terrible antipathy (反感) towards the educated and propertied.<17> Taking it a step further, it gives opportunities for some impure elements (不純潔한 輩流) — who, given any chance, would surely employ some scheme or other to sweep away the central figures of current society and replace them — to seize the opportunity and cause chaos (乘機作亂), confusing (眩惑) the minds of the societal masses, promoting (促成) the division (分裂) into factions (黨類), and hindering (沮傷) the steps (步武) on the proper path forward (正當한 進路) we should be taking. It truly seems a point where patriots (有志者) cannot suppress beating their chests and weeping long (撫胸長哭).
There is no other way. First, the educated and the propertied must reflect (反省). They must utilize (運用) the knowledge and property they possess together with their surroundings and the masses. They must do so with sincerity (誠意). They must reject (排) all means (手段) and schemes (策略) and act solely with the passion (情熱) of martyrs (殉敎者) for the survival (生存) of our entire nation (民族 全體). In this way, those who know and those who do not, those who have and those who do not, must truly intertwine and become one mass (한 뭉텡이). If it becomes like that, won't we all become educated and propertied together?

We have nothing left. In this starkly visible state of ruin (零落한 形勢), what more do we have besides the small sentiment (心情) called compatriot feeling (同胞의 心情)? If we lose even that—no, if we cut it off ourselves (스스로 끈허버린다)—what work is left for us to do?

Compatriots (同胞)! Let us use all the feeling, all the knowledge, and all the property we have to mutually enlighten (啓發) and intertwine (서로 엉키기로). Let us start first with the educated and the propertied, but let us do it with the spontaneous feeling (自發的 氣分) of greatest joy (最喜悅), like the passion of martyrs. The true intention (眞意) in my drafting (草) this text lies solely here.<18>

**Summary**

This editorial by Ki Ch'an implores the educated (有識者) and propertied (有産者) elites of early 1920s Korea to engage in self-reflection. He argues that their privileged position brings immense responsibility for the state of society and the nation's future. He criticizes their frequent arrogance, lack of sincerity, failure to research Korea-specific issues, preference for usury over productive investment, and use of charity for self-promotion rather than genuine public good. Ki Ch'an warns that this detachment and self-interest fosters resentment among the masses and risks societal division. He urges these elites to shed their arrogance, act with sincerity and humility, share their knowledge and wealth for the collective welfare, invest in Korean enterprises despite risks, and unite with the masses with the "passion of martyrs" for national survival and mutual progress.

From a historical perspective, this article is significant as it reveals the internal class tensions and intellectual currents within colonial Korean society. It highlights the critique directed towards the Korean elite by fellow Koreans, urging them towards national consciousness and social responsibility amidst Japanese colonial rule. Historians might find Ki Ch'an's specific criticisms—the lack of focus on Korean problems (like Sasang medicine or landlord-tenant issues), the critique of unproductive capital (usury), the call for investment in Korean industry despite foreign competition, and the fear of social fragmentation—particularly interesting as they reflect the socio-economic anxieties and nationalist aspirations of the period.

잡지명	개벽 제24호
발행일	1922년 06월 01일
기사제목	먼저 有識有産者側으로부터 反省하라
필자	起瀍
기사형태	논설

먼저 有識有産者側으로부터 反省하라
						起瀍
記錄이 업던 太古의 그때에는 모르겟습니다. 또 이제로부터 10年 後 或은 100年 後의 일은 모르겟습니다. 그러나 生活의 記錄이 잇슨 後로부터 오늘까지, 또 오늘로부터 얼마동안의 來日까지에는 반듯이 有識者와 無識者가 잇섯스며 有産者와 無産者가 잇섯스며 또 잇스며 다시 잇슬 것이외다. 當初부터 이러한 差別이 업섯스면 勿論 조핫겟지요마는 이미 잇섯는 것을 어찌하며 또 現在에 잇는 것을 어찌합니까. 이는 勿論 人間發展의 한 途程에 잇서 少數者의 聰明과 多數者의 不聰明이 交接하야써 나아노흔 一時의 過度的 現像으로서 何等의 永遠性을 가지지 못한 것은 거의 異論치 못할 事實이며 또한 事實이 되지 아니하야서는 안될 것입니다. 얼마동안의 期間이나마 우리가 사는 이 社會에 이와(右)가티 有識者와 無識者, 有産者와 無産者, 또 이에 따르는 有實權者와 無實權者의 差別이 업지 못하야 잇다하면 이때 社會의 形便은 어떠할가? 別하게 말할 것도 업시 有識者와 無識者가 相交하면 識者의 便이 優하고 有産者와 無産者가 相交하면 有産者의 便이 優하고 有實權者와 無實權者가 相交하면 有權者의 便이 優하게 되는 것은 섭섭하나마 現下의 事實로써 否認할 수 업는＜4＞ 것이외다.  只今의 形便에 잇서 無識者와 無産者와 또 無權者의 側으로서 맨 처음으로 내여세울 것은 多數인 그것이외다. 그러나 多數인 그것도 規模 잇는 團結을 어든 後가 아니면 何等의 組織 잇는 勢力이 되지 못할지며 規模 잇는 團結을 엇고저 하면 먼저 그것을 짓기에 足한 自覺과 試鍊을 가저야 할 것입니다. 그런데 自覺은 更히 智能의 發達을 要하고 試鍊은 更히 時間의 經過를 要합니다. 만일 相當한 自覺과 또는 相當한 試鍊도 업시 다못 多數者인 그것을 밋고 智 或 富의 平均을 엇고저 하면 爲先 그것을 取하야 어들만한 威力(多數者다운 勢力)을 나타내이지 못할지요. 設或 어떠한 形式으로 나타낸다 할지라도 그것은 一種의 暴力에 지나지 못할 것인바 少數者의 規模 잇는 勢力을 牽制할 수 업슬 것이요. 更히 一步를 讓하야 牽制함을 엇는다 할지라도 暴力으로써 어든 것은 다시 暴力으로써 壤亂될 수 잇는 것인바 結局은 彼此가 共倒하는 人間으로의 破綻을 惹起할 것 뿐이외다.
彼此의 共倒! 내가 못 살으니 너도 못살아라. 내가 못먹는 떡이거니 거긔에는 춤이나 슬컷  배아터 두어라 ! 이 따위의 일은 決斷코 人間이란 이름을 가지고는 敢히 못할 일이외다. 적어도 豺狼의 가죽을 쓰고 狗彘의 心腸을 가지는 惡漢이 되기 前까지에는 敢히 못할 일이외다.
그러나 事實에 나아가 보면 어떠합니까. 사람이란 반듯이 義로운 일만 가리어하는 動物도 아니며 반듯이 理智의 命하는 바에만 사는 깨끗한 動物도 아니외다. 境遇境遇에 衝動만 되면 아모러한 짓이라도 能히 行하는 것이 사람이며 이러케 저러케 惡化만 되면 무슨 無謀의 일이라도 敢히 做出하는 것이 사람이외다. 이 意味에서 우리는 그저 汎博하게 但曰 「이러이러한 것은 올치 못한 일이다. 그와가튼 일을 行하는 者는 덜 생각한 者이니라」＜5＞하야 그에 對한 善惡을 分別 或은 冷評함으로써 自足할 수가 업습니다. 반듯이 一步를 更進하야 이 社會의 이 人間을 몰아써 그와 가튼 無謀의 일을 做出할 必要 또는 機會를 업게하는 策을 講하지 아니하면 안될 것이외다.
그러면 그리할 策을 講하고 또 實行할 者- 다시 말하면 그 責에 任할 者가 누구이겟습니까. 勿論 論者에 依하야 그 對答을 달리할 것입니다. 그러나 나는 한 마듸로써 斷言하되 그는 다른 사람이 될 것이 아니라 오늘의 우리 社會에 잇서 比較的 知識이 잇고 財産이 잇스며 그 知識 그 財産을 背景으로 하야 現 社會의 實權을 比較的 만히 所有한 少數의 그 사람들이라 합니다. 逼切하게 말하면 우리의 모든 일이 잘 되어도 그들의 탓이며 못되어도 그들의 탓입니다. 그들은 우리에게 對한 功과 罪를 아울러 지을 수 잇는 者들이며 賞讚과 咀呪를 아울러 바들 수 잇는 者들입니다. 多數의 民衆은 恒常 그들의 云爲를 鑑視하는 一面으로 그들의 善意의 指導에 互應할 約束을 가저야 할 것이외다. 나는 이제 이를 우리 社會의 現狀으로써 鑑照하고 우리 民族의 將來로써 議案하야 길지 못한 몃 마듸의 말로써 우리 兄弟 中의 有識者와 또는 有産者 여러븐의 一考를 促하고저 합니다.

有識者 여러분에게
有識者 여러분. 우리에게 오히려 義務敎育의 制가 업고 딸아서 一般으로 우리의 智能을 啓發할 機會가 甚히 적은 이땅에서 당신들 몃 사람이 호을로 福이 만흐고 機會가 조하. 多數者가 모르는 그것(思想의 便)을 아는 者가 되엇스며 多數者가 能하지 못하는 그것(技術의 便)을 能하는 者가 되엇습니다. 몃 분은 그 몸이 가만히 집에＜6＞ 잇스나 또는 뚜렷이 社會에 잇슴을 不關하고 그 하는 바 말, 하는 바 行動, 發表하는 바의 글은 비록 그것이 한마디, 한거름, 또는 한 句節의 적은 것이라 할 지라도 만흔 사람의 警醒하는 바 되며 遵則하는 바 되며 注視하는 바 되나니 社會의 民衆에 對한 여러분의 地位야말로 尊貴하며 民族의 興替에 對한 여러분의 責務야말로 重大하외다. 여러분의 하는 바 일은 남달리 卓越하지 아니할 수 업스며 여러분의 取하는 바 態度는 남달리 誠實하지 안흘 수 업스며 여러분의 가지는 바 所見은 남달리 聰明하지 아니하여서는 안될 것이외다. 그러나 나로써 여러분을 보건대 여러분 中에는 그 하는 바의 일에서나 或은 取하는 바의 態度에서나 聰明, 誠實, 卓越한 實跡을 보이지 못하는 者가 잇슬 뿐 아니라. 甚함에 至하야는 그 舌을 曲하며 그 文을 誣하며 그 面을 假하야 自己의 小慧로써 民衆의 無智를 飜弄하며 其間에서 어더지는 若干의 名利와 多少의 自惚心으로써 自滿, 自驕하는 者도 업지 아니한 바 말하야 이에 이르매 實로 毛骨이 悚然합니다.
그러면 오늘의 有識者에 對한 너의 바라는 바는 무엇이냐 하면 나는 곳 이러케 對答하겟습니다.

먼저 당신들의 取하는 바 態度에서 徹底하라 하겟습니다. 自己의 一身이나 或은 社會民族의 將來를 爲하야 어떠한 目的을 한번 確立한 以上은 그 目的의 압길을 거러 나아가는 途中에 비록 銃이 잇고 칼이 잇다 할지라도 한 모양으로 나아갈 것입니다. 그 길의 兩岸에 나타난 虛花나 假景에 誘惑되어 그만 橫路로 脫走하거나 前途의 多艱을 豫想하고 中途에 躊躇하여서는 안될 것이외다. 近來 一部의 識者 中에서는 或 무슨 主義의 宣傳을 盛히 하며 或 무슨 運動의 進捗을 力圖합니다. 다-말하되 이것이 곳 우리民族의 生榮을 圖하는 本來의 目的에＜7＞  到達하는 捷徑이라 합니다. 事實로 捷徑이 된다하면 더 말할 것도 업거니와 이것이 或 兩岸의 虛花假景에 心醉하야 벌서 岐路를 取하는 始作은 아닌지, 그러치 안흐면 前途의 多艱에 落望하야 스스로 暴棄하는 兆朕은 아닌지 혈마 그럴 理야 잇겟느냐고 記者부터 自信코저 하는 바이나 어찌해 그런지 그래도 念慮가 됩니다.
무릇 일이란 반듯이 正經大道를 取할 것이외다. 虛張이나 策略이란 그러케 貴한 것이 아니외다. 더욱 聰明치 못한 民衆과 더블어 圖謀하는 일에서 그러하다 합니다. 10에 1로 成功하면 僥倖이어니와 10에 9로 慘敗하야 或은 收拾치 못할 破滅을 招하거나, 또는 깨이지 못할 甘眼에 취한다 하면 어찌하겟습니까. 무슨 主義이니 무슨 運動이니 하는 것은 그 宣傳이나 또는 進捗에 잇서 만흔 考慮를 要할 것이라 합니다.

그리고 識者階級에서는 그 自身이나 또 自己의 家庭에 잇서 모든 것이 一層 徹底하여야 하겟습니다. 이마에 땀을 흘리어 써먹으라고 툭하면 부르짓는 그 사람도 爲先 自己부터 一定한 職業을 가지지 못하고 四處로 流浪하야 一種 變則의 浮浪者를 짓는 사람도 업지 못하며, 社會의 奴僕이라는 標榜을 機會만 잇스면 내여놋는 그 사람도 實際에 잇서서는 舊式의 豪奢生活(나아가면 車馬, 들어오면 號令-自己의 家族이나 使役者에 對하야-)을 그대로 繼續하나니 이것은 決코 自己의 主義에 忠實하는 道도 아니며 社會의 事業에 盡瘁하는 者도 아니외다. 오늘의 우리는 먼저 躬行하고 實踐하야 自己의 身이 곳 率이되고 自己의 聲이 곳 度가 되는 聖者의 眞實을 가지지 아니할 수 업습니다. 말 뿐이나 글 뿐으로는 所用이 업습니다. 이것이 비록 말은 平凡하나 우리의 缺處-特히 識者의 缺處-는 만히 이에 잇는가 합니다.＜8＞

다음으로 識者는 맛당히 識者로의 謙遜한 心德을 가저야 할 것이외다. 無識者에게 잇기 쉬운 缺處가 무엇이겟는가하면 無禮, 亂暴이 될 것임과 가티 有識者에게 잇기 쉬운 缺處는 轎傲, 自滿이 될 것이외다. 驕傲나 自滿은 다- 가티 謙遜한 心德을 所有치 못한데서 생긴 것이겟습니다. 작은 그릇에 물이 담기우되 半쯤 되고 말면 그 물소리가 異常하게 출렁거리는 것과 가티 識者의 驕傲도 그 稟賦한 心性 器局이 크지 못하거나 또는 그 所有한 知識의 量이 만치 못하면 크지 못하고 만치 못한 그만콤 그 驕傲의 度가 더 놉흘 것이외다. 故로 淺薄한 心性을 가진 사람으로서 또는 처음으로 文明에 나아가는 그 때의 識者로서는 一層 驕傲·자만의 風에 걸리기가 쉽습니다.
