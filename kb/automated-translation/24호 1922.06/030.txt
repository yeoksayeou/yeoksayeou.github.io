Regarding Our Clothing Costs, Housing Costs, and Entertainment Costs: Research on the Korean People's Life Problems, Part Four
						鮮于全 (Sŏnu Chŏn)

1. Clothing Costs 2. Housing Costs 3. Entertainment Costs

In the previous installment, we discussed food costs (see this magazine, April issue, page 12 onwards). Following this, we must discuss clothing costs and housing costs, which are most important for managing material life. Finally, we will discuss entertainment costs.

Generally, clothing costs and housing costs are certainly not of lesser importance compared to food costs for managing material life. However, if we observe human material life from a physiological perspective, humans, like other animals, must first eat above all else to sustain their biological lifespan even for a moment. Therefore, the primary necessity for maintaining physiological life (or material life) is foodstuffs. Following that, the preparation of clothing, housing, entertainment, and other necessities arises, as evidenced by the foundation of human physiological life and what we call the origin of today's civilized life.

The development and laws of this physiological life can be explained with a diagram. If we let 'ba' represent income and 'ba' represent desire, satisfaction arises when a certain income fulfills desires. Thus, cc, dd, ee, ff, etc., would represent satisfaction. And as income increases, such as ac, cd, de, ef, fg, etc., one can satisfy the desire for food (ac), the desire for clothing (cd), the desire for housing (de), the desire for entertainment (ef), and subsequently, as income further increases (fg, gh, hi, etc.), one can satisfy the desires represented by fg, gh, hi, etc. In this diagram, income is represented by a vertical line, while desire is represented by a diagonal line, signifying that desire always has an expansive nature. As the income 'ab' <19> grows, the desire 'ab' extends indefinitely, meaning human desire has an infinite and limitless nature.

This diagram explains human physiological life observed under general standards. In a real situation, if person A has an income of 'ag' and person B has an income of 'ad', person A can pursue satisfaction beyond food, clothing, and housing, including entertainment and others. However, person B, while able to seek satisfaction in food and clothing, will be in a situation where satisfaction in housing cannot be fully obtained. In that case, B will inevitably have to allocate the income 'ad' to expenditures for the three major essentials indispensable for physiological life: food, clothing, and housing. Consequently, it becomes inevitable that B will try to economize on food and clothing expenses. Ultimately, B will fall below the standard level of living, and due to insufficient food and clothing expenses, it will be difficult to maintain physical health.

This irregular result explains the difference in the content of material life between the poor and rich classes. To achieve eternal peace in society and the common happiness of people, we must aspire to maintain the same standard for each class regarding at least the three major items: food, clothing, and housing.

As mentioned before, the desire for material goods arising from the need to sustain physiological life is successively satisfied according to the increase in income and earnings. Furthermore, observing the nature and types of these material desires reveals differences in importance, leading to distinctions between the first and the last priorities, as briefly explained earlier. That is, while the desire for food has an absolute and unchanging nature, the desires for clothing, housing, and others are relative and variable. Consequently, regarding the expenditures required to pursue these desires, food costs become absolute expenditures, while expenditures related to others become relative expenditures (see this magazine, April issue, pages 9-10). Observing this again from the perspective of income, food costs do not significantly change based on the amount of income and earnings. However, expenditures related to clothing, housing, entertainment, and others, beyond a certain level, may increase or decrease depending on the amount of income and earnings, showing much fluctuation. <20> Moreover, when general income and earnings increase, the increased income is consequently allocated to newly arising expenditures other than foodstuffs. Therefore, the ratio of food expenditure to total income for those with low income differs from that of those with high income, creating an inverse relationship between income increase and the ratio of food expenditure. This relationship, shown statistically, is as follows:

Having briefly outlined the nature and expenditure variation relationships of food costs, the most crucial item for human physiological life, and other items like clothing, housing, and entertainment costs, we will now proceed to discuss each cost item—clothing, housing, and entertainment—individually, similar to the previous measurement of food costs. However, what makes discussing these living cost items difficult is that, as discussed previously (this magazine, February issue, pages 50-55), the income and earnings of Koreans are uncertain, leading to uncertain expenditures for various living costs. Furthermore, as the very structure of social organization wavers, all scales and styles related to material life are fragmented and inconsistent. For instance, some live a purely Korean-style life, some live a Japanese-style life, some blend Japanese style into Korean-style life, and some blend Western style into Korean-style life. Just as the entire Korean society currently lacks scale and standards, the content and style of life have lost scale and standards. Korean society in such a phenomenon can truly be said to be in a state of chaos, causing considerable concern among intellectuals about its future.

Since the social phenomenon is in such a state, we, colleagues in social problem research, even when discussing the minor issue of life problems, find that researching the very core of Korean society and its organizational structure would be the foremost important research and an urgent issue. However, research on improving social organization and the study of life problems have a very close relationship. Considering that the study of life problems constitutes the substance of social problem research, it goes without saying that the study of life problems will greatly benefit research on the problem of improving social organization.

As the entire social organization wavers, fixed <21> standards and scales in all aspects of life—individual life, family life, social life—loosen. Consequently, clothing costs, housing costs, and entertainment costs, which inherently have high variability, become even more unstable, making the measurement of standard amounts for these cost items a very difficult problem. However, to complete this discussion, we must somehow determine the standard clothing and housing costs required by a standard middle-class Korean family. Therefore, based on the data obtained by the author, I intend to establish the standards for these living costs as follows.

1. Clothing Costs
(a) Costs Related to Korean Clothing (朝鮮服): Clothing costs refer to expenses directly related to protecting the body. Clothing, its quality, structure, and style, develops and progresses based on the local climate and environment, the inherent customs of the residents, the development of culture and technology, the characteristics of local products, and the nature of the people. Therefore, it is easy to discern the characteristics of a nation's situation (國情) by observing the clothing of its residents, representing the local people's nature, customs, characteristics, technology, climate, environment, and products. Consequently, a nation's clothing comes to represent the characteristics of its national situation. However, behind the recent great tide of world thought and world civilization circulating globally, there is a perception that the unique culture and systems of each nation are assimilating into a globally common culture, leading to the assimilation and unification of each nation's clothing. For example, as European civilization spread to various countries worldwide, Western clothing (洋服) becoming international common attire sufficiently proves this fact.

While we cannot fail to recognize the fact that clothing, representing a nation's unique culture, artifacts, and systems, is unifying globally, on the other hand, the inherent national character, customs, systems, and the influence of nature (a region's unique climate, environment, and products necessitate specific clothing for its residents) of each nation, which strongly resist ideological and systemic unification, have necessitated and maintained the unique clothing of the local residents. That is, just as Chinese, Japanese, Koreans, and other nations influenced by European civilization emulate European systems in various aspects while finding it difficult to dismantle and destroy their inherent systems, thus practicing a dual-system approach, they also use both Western clothing and their traditional clothing concurrently. For Chinese, Japanese, or Koreans, who are in a situation of emulating European civilization rather than understanding and internalizing it (of course, the author confesses not to be a proponent of national essentialism - 國粹論者), bearing a double burden regarding clothing costs is disadvantageous from the perspective of alleviating living difficulties and national economic consumption. However, as long as circumstances prevent us from completely dismantling and destroying our nation's inherent systems and customs <22> to fully assimilate into another country's systems, we cannot avoid practicing the dual use of Korean and Western clothing, even if it entails some disadvantages and points of imperfection.

Before calculating the costs for Korean clothing under this dual Korean-Western clothing system, a brief critique of Korean clothing: its structural style, viewed externally, possesses elegant characteristics, a point acknowledged even by foreigners who lack a habitual taste for Korean clothing. However, viewed from an economic and practical standpoint, whether regarding quality or system and style, it has wasteful drawbacks, a fact acknowledged even by us Koreans. Thus, improving the fabric, dyes, and certain aspects of the style is currently urgent. However, regarding these matters of quality, color, and design (意匠), which relate to technology, it is difficult to formulate concrete proposals without specialized knowledge and research. Therefore, this is left to specialist researchers. What the author wishes to propose as a personal opinion on the clothing issue is:
1. Even when improving any aspect of Korean clothing, its inherent elegant characteristics should be maintained.
2. The materials for a nation's clothing should be its specific local products.

The reason the author presents such opinions is, firstly, that in today's era where improvementism (改良主義) is highly advocated, many traditional Korean systems and artifacts have been improved supposedly out of temporal necessity. However, looking at the results of these improvements, whether in design or practicality, they are often inferior to the original state, frequently evoking feelings of ugliness even for us who use them regularly. The cause of such improvement leading to regression lies in the lack of design and technical skill for the improvement. Therefore, at the very least, improving clothing that represents a nation's unique civilization requires significant research and insight regarding its design and technology. The second reason for proposing this opinion is that even though domestic division of labor has reached international division of labor due to the development of the international economy today, as long as nations are separate and the national economy based on the nation as a unit has not been dismantled, the commonly used foodstuffs and clothing materials for the people must rely on local products. In Europe, the clothing cost burden for Germans is light, whereas for Americans and British it is heavy. In the East, the clothing burden for Koreans is relatively heavier than for Japanese. This is because German and Japanese clothing materials rely on their domestic products, whereas American, British, and Korean clothing materials rely on foreign goods. In this sense too, promoting domestic products is extremely necessary.

Regarding the issue of improving Korean clothing, which arose from temporal necessity, <23> there is no need for lengthy discussion here. Therefore, following the main purpose of this discussion, we will limit ourselves to calculating the standard clothing costs for a standard middle-class family (refer to the standard middle-class family described in the April issue, pp. 14-15) based on the following standards. Although Korean clothing may be simpler compared to Western clothing, the following table will demonstrate that the types and costs are substantial.

II. Housing Costs
Of course, observing the current state of social development, as the scope of human activity expands and progresses from the individual to society, and from society to the international sphere, the scope of our lives also expands its outline from family life to social life, and from social life to international life. <29> Nevertheless, if the family unit, which forms the basic unit of the social group, cannot obtain satisfaction regarding the dwelling called its paradise, the adverse effects, both spiritual and material, that the family will suffer due to this will surely not be small. The teaching of "Mencius's Mother's Three Moves" (孟軻之母 三遷之敎), a lesson regarding the location of residence, has been heard since ancient times. The fact that the extreme suffering of urban life in any city today arises from housing difficulties is frequently heard.

What I intend to discuss below as housing costs refers to expenditures related to housing. Before discussing housing expenditure costs, attempting a common-sense discussion on housing structure and style also seems necessary for Korean society at this time when reconstruction and improvement are loudly advocated.

Of course, to provide a scientific explanation and concrete plan regarding housing structure and style, it is difficult to attempt discussion without specialized research and particular knowledge. However, considering it from a common-sense perspective, a dwelling—not a shrine, temple, public office, or shop, but a place where a family lives together in harmony and is called a microcosm of society—must meet the following conditions in its structure and style: First, regarding the local climate and environment, the structure must be capable of defending against external intrusions. Second, regarding hygiene, the location and the content of the structural style must be conducive to health. Third, regarding the nation's economic spending capacity, it must not require excessive management costs for the operation, preservation, and repair of the dwelling. Fourth, its structural style must possess artistic color and solidity. Fifth, it must be suitable for the general social system and family system of the region. Sixth, the structural style must provide great convenience in use. Seventh, since today's social system and organization constantly change and progress, the structural style must be capable of being improved and altered according to the progress and improvement of the social system. Do the Korean houses (朝鮮家屋) we inhabit conform to these points? It is commonly acknowledged that there are many points of non-conformity. Now, let's briefly compare and discuss the shortcomings and strengths of the structure and style of Korean-style houses.

First, Korean-style houses, in their external appearance, form, and structural style, are large-scale and expansive. Indeed, their exterior and interior appearance are imposing, and the scale of their enclosing walls, with expanded outlines, is rarely seen in the architecture of other countries. Observing this historically, the structural style of Korean houses has many points modeled after castles (據城) developed during the feudal era, <30> making them more territorial and invasive than neighborly.
The reason Korean houses possess such characteristics originates from the large family system (大家族制度) that arose against the backdrop of the royal hierarchical system (王建制度). In our ancestors' time, the scope of a family included multiple generations—two, three, or four—of numerous blood relatives. Thus, the total number of family members reached several tens, and a family formed a union group of close blood relatives. Therefore, there was a need to make their dwellings expansive. However, in our era today, the number of members in a family has become no more than four or five, including husband, wife, and children. Consequently, vast, large buildings resembling barracks are not only unsuitable for achieving the ideal of harmony for our small families but also cause significant disadvantages in terms of maintenance and repair, as we keenly feel. This is the drawback that fails to meet the third condition mentioned earlier. Next, a notable strength of Korean houses is that their structure is solid and firm, and they exhibit the beauty of antiquity rather than the beauty of new construction. The lifespan of Korean buildings surpasses that of Western buildings and is double that of Japanese buildings. If geometric design were added by applying advanced architectural techniques to the unique solidity of Korean buildings, their solidity would be further enhanced. This solidity is a unique characteristic of Korean buildings. This characteristic, secondly, renews a special kind of beauty, a solidity rarely seen in other buildings. That is, while Western or Japanese buildings initially evoke a certain "delicate" (デリケット - likely 'delicate') aesthetic appeal with their artificial new beauty, as years pass, their dilapidated and worn-out ugliness truly evokes unpleasant feelings, requiring considerable expense for improvement and repair to maintain their existence. In contrast, although Korean buildings may be inferior to Japanese or Western buildings in terms of elegance and grace, their unique solidity ensures that even if the beauty of new construction disappears after a certain period, the building's main body and framework have endurance. Thus, its lifespan continues without requiring major repairs, and after the beauty of new construction fades, it further displays a special antique color, renewing a certain kind of beauty. Such characteristics are rarely seen in other buildings, and this point allows us to recognize the value of Korean buildings.

While Korean buildings possess such characteristics, they also have a rigidity that makes it difficult to meet the needs of the times in today's social situation where economic pressure is severe. The first is that they require large construction costs and extensive land area. That Korean buildings require comparatively larger construction costs than those of other countries is commonly stated by professionals in the civil engineering and construction industry. This directly <31> raises the rental price of Korean houses, causing general tenants to bear a significant housing cost burden, as discussed later. Also, the fact that Korean houses require a larger area than any country's architecture has become a significant cause for further increasing the burden of urban living costs today, when urban land prices are gradually rising. This is the drawback that fails to meet the third condition mentioned earlier.

Second, today's Korean society, seeking to assimilate into world civilization, sees its social system changing constantly. Therefore, housing, which has a significant relationship with the general social system, must be able to easily adapt and harmonize with the new system without requiring enormous costs and revolutionary reconstruction. However, the structural style of Korean houses, developed historically, finds it difficult to harmonize with the new systems produced by the new era through minor stylistic changes. This is the drawback that fails to meet the seventh condition mentioned earlier.

The primary reasons why changing the structural style of Korean-style houses is difficult are: first, our lifestyle is based on the ondol (溫突) heating system; second, the family system is unique; third, the distribution system for daily necessities is still not fully implemented; fourth, Korean buildings lose their inherent beauty when other styles are added. These various points are incompatible with changing the style of Korean houses. Detailed research is needed to offer concrete evaluations on these points, but the overall hope regarding style change is to add improvements with new taste without losing the inherent characteristics of Korean buildings, adapting them to new systems and new demands.

Some foreigners or even some Koreans comment that Korean houses are unclean or unhygienic, but the author does not wish to concur with this assessment. Generally, regardless of the system or cultural artifact, there are those who criticize or mock it without conducting a fundamental examination or long-term observation. Some misjudge due to incompatible tastes or lack of insight. Although it has been many years since Korea was introduced overseas, it seems there are truly not many foreigners who have sufficiently studied and examined the content of Korea's inherent culture and systems—that is, those who know Korea well. The assessment that Korean houses are unclean or unhygienic is largely due to not knowing Korea well, the uncleanliness of the surroundings, and the unhygienic practices of the inhabitants inside. The house structure itself, I believe, certainly does not possess an inherently unclean or unhygienic nature. Regarding this point, we must pay attention to hygiene and cleanliness both in public places and within the home, and <32> be more mindful of cleaning facilities around the exterior of dwellings to avoid foreign criticism.

The main point of the above critique is that as our society today assimilates into world civilization, the dwellings of families, which form the basic units of the large structure called society, must also be improved. When discussing the housing issue, the author feels the need to implement improvements to the structural style and, at the same time, keenly feels that the rental fees for Korean houses are relatively high compared to buildings in other countries.

As discussed in the section below, the ratio of housing expenditure to total living cost expenditure is higher for Koreans than for foreigners or Japanese. For Japanese, if a family's total monthly living expenses are 100 Won, the housing cost, i.e., rent (家賃 - yachin/kach'im), is only 10 to 15 Won, or 10%-15%. For the middle class, it is 35 to 40 Won, or 18%-20%. For foreigners, Americans, 19% is common. In contrast, for Koreans, a family with a monthly income of 100 Won pays 20 to 25 Won, or 20%-25%, for monthly rent. This heavy burden of housing costs will inevitably affect food and clothing expenses, harming health.

The rental cost (借賃 - ch'aim) for such a type of house is roughly calculated as follows.

In Kyŏngsŏng, there are two customary rental systems (借家制度 - ch'akka chedo): (1) Monthly Rent Payment System (家貰月拂制度 - kase wŏlbul chedo) and (2) Chŏnse (傳貰). The so-called Chŏnse system is mainly practiced in Kyŏngsŏng, seemingly accounting for about 20% to 25% of all rental households. The reasons for the emergence of this system will be discussed in a separate article later, but legally observing the nature of this system, it is merely a House-Collateral No-Interest Lease Agreement (家屋擔保無利子賃貸契約). (Author's own term) Specifically, between the homeowner (家主 - kaju) 'A' and the tenant (借家人 - ch'again) 'B', 'A' borrows a certain amount of money (about half the market value of the collateral house) from 'B' without interest, providing their own house as collateral. In return, 'A' transfers the right to use the house to 'B', allowing 'B' to use the house rent-free. Viewed from the outcome, it is an exchange contract between the house rent (貰金 - segŭm) and the interest on the debt (債權 - ch'aegwŏn). From the tenant's perspective of gains and losses, the Chŏnse system seems more convenient than monthly rent.

Distinguishing the housing cost burden of the aforementioned standard middle-class family based on whether they use Chŏnse or monthly rent: In the case of Chŏnse, standardizing the total house size at 25 kan, the Chŏnse price (傳貰價額 - chŏnse ka'aek) would be 3,750 Won (assuming a house located outside the city center with a sale price of 250 Won per kan). The tenant's gain/loss relationship from this lease, i.e., the tenant's housing cost burden, would be:
一. 375 Won (Annual interest at 10% on the principal).
一. 18 Won 75 Chŏn (Registration fee for the mortgage value of 3,750 Won). <34>
一. 150 Won (Interest cost, commission, and other miscellaneous fees, estimated at 4% of the Chŏnse amount).
Total 543 Won 75 Chŏn (Annual total).

3. Entertainment Costs, Other Cultural Living Costs
Entertainment costs, even among nations with the same income, will not be identical due to differences in the nation's cultural level, national taste, development of entertainment facilities, social system, national habits, and various other factors. Even within the same nation, as briefly discussed in the initial section, the ratio of entertainment cost expenditure for each class will not be the same depending on the amount of income.

Among relative living costs (as opposed to absolutely necessary living costs), besides entertainment costs, there are social expenses (交際費 - kyojeppi), self-cultivation expenses (修養費 - suyangkpi), insurance premiums, and other miscellaneous cost items. However, to conclude the discussion quickly, these various cost items will not be discussed separately but under the comprehensive name of Entertainment Costs or Cultural Living Costs. What makes discussing this extremely difficult is, as mentioned before, that the nature of the content of these cost items is extremely variable compared to the previously discussed food, clothing, and housing costs, making it hard to set a standard. To attempt a sufficient discussion for setting the standard amount for these cost items, one would need to consider at least the <36> nature of entertainment costs, the income of our various classes, inherent tastes and habits, social systems and the content of cultural life, the nature and development of entertainment facilities traditionally maintained, considerations regarding the types and facilities of entertainment institutions necessary for future cultural life, the need for entertainment facilities appropriate for each class and society, and various other aspects. However, here, to hasten the conclusion of the discussion, the deliberation on the above is entirely omitted, and only the result based on the following standards will be presented.

Conclusion
Just as all movements of objects and changes of facts produce dual results concerning their surrounding space, world civilization does not deviate from this grand principle and seeks to dominate our cultural life from two sides. That is, under the dual influence resulting from the development of world civilization, on one hand, we are invigorated and advance fiercely, finding joy in things, while on the other hand, we are placed in a situation where our energy wanes, leading to self-pity, and we cannot help but feel sorrow towards things. This drama of two sides is precisely the result of world civilization and the aftermath of civilizational development.

Regarding the dual results of this civilizational development, let one side's result be called the good fruit (善果) and the other side's result the bad fruit (惡果). On the side of the good fruit are all the civilized systems and matters of happiness that world civilization supplies in abundance to our lives, both spiritually and materially. On the side of the bad fruit are all the tragedies arising from the intensification of the struggle for existence—that is, the hardship of living (生活亂).

Although we cannot control or alter the natural laws of object motion, just as we can harmonize and moderate its dual aspects of movement to prevent conflict, although we cannot change the driving force of civilizational development, regarding the results it produces, while not losing the good fruit, we must somehow harmonize and adapt the bad fruit through some method to prevent conflict in society. That harmonization and adaptation are precisely the various methods for relieving living difficulties, policies for alleviating living difficulties, poverty relief methods, and other social policies.

These methods for relieving living difficulties and social policies are common to all countries worldwide where living difficulties exist, and thus each country is diligently researching them with utmost sincerity and intention. In our Korean society, what methods and policies would be effective in rescuing the Korean people from living difficulties, and can they be adapted and implemented under the current Korean system? This will be researched in a separate article later. With the above discussion, this part of the "Research on the Korean People's Life Problems" concludes. (End)

**Summary**

This 1922 article by Sŏnu Chŏn analyzes the costs of clothing, housing, and entertainment/culture for a standard middle-class Korean family in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). It follows a previous piece on food costs. The author argues that Koreans face a heavy burden, particularly with clothing (due to needing both Korean and Western attire) and housing (traditional Korean houses being large, expensive to build/rent, though durable). These high costs, coupled with lower incomes compared to Japanese or Westerners, lead to significantly less spending on cultural and entertainment activities. The author laments the "chaotic," unstandardized state of Korean lifestyles influenced by foreign contact and calls for research, improvement (especially in clothing/housing design and cost-effectiveness), and promotion of domestic products to alleviate living difficulties and improve cultural life.

For historians, this article offers a valuable quantitative and qualitative glimpse into the economic pressures and lifestyle dilemmas faced by urban middle-class Koreans during the colonial period. It highlights the tangible costs of navigating modernity and colonial influence, such as the dual clothing system. The author's frank discussion of the perceived "chaos" and lack of standards reflects anxieties about cultural identity and social change. Furthermore, the detailed breakdown of living expenses, the discussion of housing systems like Chŏnse, and the explicit comparisons with Japanese and American living standards provide concrete data points for understanding socio-economic conditions and aspirations in 1920s Korea. The editor's concluding remarks also underscore the contemporary shift in focus from nationalist rhetoric towards practical problem-solving.

1. 被服費
(a) 朝鮮服에 關한 費用  被服費라 하면 直接 身體 保藏에 關한 費用인데 被服이라는 것은 그 地方의 氣候와 風土, 住民의 固有한 習慣, 人文과 技術의 發達, 産物의 特色, 民性 如何에 依하야 그 品質과 構造樣式이 發達 進步되는 것임으로 그 地方 住民의 民性, 習慣, 特色, 技術, 氣候, 風土, 産物을 住民의 被服에 依하야 測知키 容易하며 딸아 一國民의 被服은 그 國情의 特色을 代表케 된다. 그러나 近來 世界思想, 世界文明의 大潮가 全世界를 廻流하는 後面에는 各 國民의 固有한 文化 及 制度가 世界共通的 文化에 同化케 됨을 從하야 各 國民의 被服도 同化統一되여 가는 觀念이 有하니 例컨데 歐洲의 文明이 世界各國에 傳播케 되매 洋服이라는 것이 世界國際被服, 共通服이 된 것은 此 事實을 証키에 足하다 하노라.
一國의 固有한 文物과 制度를 代表하는 被服이 世界的으로 統一되어가는 事實을 認識치 아니키 不能한 他面에 思想統一, 制度統一에 對하야 가장 抵抗力이 强한 各 國民의 固有한 民性, 固有한 習慣, 固有한 制度 及 自然의 支配(一地方의 特有한 氣候 風土 産物은 그 住民에 對하야 固有한 被服을 必要케 하는 事有함)는 그 地方 住民에 對하야 그 地方 固有한 被服을 必要케 하얏스며 維持케 하얏나니 即 歐洲 文明에 同化된 中國이나 日本이나 朝鮮이나 其他의 國民은 各種 制度에 對하야 歐洲의 制度를 模擬하는 他面에 그 固有한 制度를 解體 破壤키 難하야 兩制幷行主義를 行하는 것 가티 被服에 對하야서도 洋服과 其他 그 固有한 被服을 倂用케 되엇다. 歐洲文明을 理解하야 自國化되게 하느니보다도(勿論 論者는 國粹論者는 아닌 것을 自告함) 此를 模擬코저 하는 境遇에 處한 中國人이나 日本人이나 朝鮮人이 被服費에 對하야 2重의 負擔을 荷케 된 것은 生活難 緩和上으로 見하야 國民經濟의 消費 方面으로 見하야 不利益을 受하는 바 多하다 할지나 吾人이 自國의 固有한 制度와 習慣을 全部 解體 破壤하야＜22＞ 他國의 制度에 全部 同化키 不能한 事情이 有한 以上에는 多少의 不利益과 不徹底한 點이 有하다 할지라도 朝鮮服, 洋服幷用主義를 行치 아니키 不能하다.
此 朝鮮服洋服幷用主義 下에서 朝鮮服에 對한 費用을 計出키 前에 爲先 朝鮮服에 對한 短評을 下하면 朝鮮服의 構造樣式이 外樣上으로 觀하야 其優美한 特點을 有한 것은 朝鮮服에 對하야 常習的 趣味가 無한 外國人으로서도 共認하는 바이다. 그러나 經濟上 及 實用上으로 觀하야 品質에 對하야서던지 制度樣式에 對하야서던지 浪貝的 缺點이 有한 것은 吾人 朝鮮人으로도 共認하는 바인即 服地의 改良, 色素의 改良, 一部 樣式의 改良은 目下 時急을 要하는 바이나 此等 品質 色素 意匠 等 技術上에 關하야는 專門的 見識과 硏究가 無하고는 具體的 定案을 下키 難함으로 此는 專門硏究者에 移하고 論者가 被服問題에 對하야 個人의 意見으로 提出코저 하는 것은

勿論 社會 發達의 現勢에 依하야 觀하면 사람의 活動力의 範圍가 個人으로부터 社會에 社會로부터 國際에 擴張되고 進展됨을 從하야 吾人의 生活의 範圍도 家族生活로부터 社會生活에 社會生活로부터 國際生活에＜29＞그 輪廓이 擴張된다 할지라도 社會團體의 單位를 成하는 家族團體가 그 樂園이라 稱하는 住宅에 對한 滿足을 求得치 못하면 그 家族이 此로 因하야 精神上으로나 物質上으로나 受할 바 惡影響은 果然 少치 아늘 것이다. 「孟軻之母」의 「三遷의 敎」라 云하는 居住地位置에 關한 敎訓은 녜로부터 들어오는 바이며 今日何地方의 都市를 勿論하고 都市生活의 最極의 苦痛이 住宅難으로부터 生하는 事實은 屢屢히 傳聞하는 바이다.
以下 論코저 하는 바 居住費라는 것은 卽 住宅에 關한 支出을 云함인대 住宅에 支出費用을 論키 前에 住宅構造樣式에 對하야 常職的 論을 試하는 것도 改造改良을 高唱하는 此時에 朝鮮 社會를 爲하야 한갓 必要할 듯하다.
勿論 住宅의 構造樣式에 對하야 科學的 說明과 具體的 考案을 下하자면 專門的 硏究와 特殊한 知識이 無하고는 試論키 難하나 그러나 常識的으로 考察하야 神社, 寺院, 公廨, 商舖가 아니고 一家族이 混居團欒하며 一社會의 小天地라 稱하는 住宅은 그 構造와 樣式이 下의 條件과 相合치 아느면 不可타할지니 即 第1은 그地方의 氣候風土上으로 關하야 外部의 侵害를 防禦키에 可能한 構造가 되지 아느면 不可하며 第2는 衛生上으로 關하야 그 位置와 構造樣式의 內容이 保健에 可能치 아느면 不可하며 第3은 國民의 經濟 支出能力上으로 關하야 住宅의 經營保存修繕에 對하야 多大한 經營費를 要치 아니하는 者가 되지 아느면 不可하며 第4는 그 構造樣式이 美術的 色彩와 堅固性을 有치 아느면 不可하며 第5는 그 地方의 一般社會制度, 家族制度에 適合한 者가 되지 아느면 不可하며 第6은 使役上 多大한 便宜를 供하는 構造樣式이 되지 아느면 不可하며 第7은 今日의 社會制度와 組織은 時時로 變遷進步하는 故로 그 構造樣式이 社會制度의 進步改良을 딸아 改良變更키에 可能치 아느면 不可할지니 吾人이 捿居하는 朝鮮家屋이 此等諸點과 合致하느냐하면 勿論 不合致한 點이 多할 것은 共認하는 바이다. 이제 朝鮮式 家屋의 構造와 樣式이 如何한 點에 短處가 有하며 長處가 有한 것을 相比하야 略論할 진대
第1. 朝鮮式 家屋은 그 外觀上 形體 及 構造樣式이 大規模的이오 擴張的이다. 實로 그 外觀 及 內觀이 莊嚴하며 防築의 輪廓이 擴大된 그 規模는 他國建築에 至하야 類見키 難하다. 此를 歷史的으로 觀察하면 朝鮮家屋의 構造樣式은 封建時代에 發達되얏던 據城을 規擬한＜30＞ 點이 多하야 交隣的보다도 領土的이오 侵略的이다.
朝鮮家屋이 이와 가튼 特質을 有한 것은 王建制度를 背景으로 하야 起出된 大家族制度에 源因한 바이니 吾人先祖時代에 在하야는 一家族의 範圍는 2代 3代 4代의 多數血族을 包含하얏슴으로 總家族員數는 實로 幾十數名에 達하야 一家族이라하면 近親血族의 聯合集團을 形成하얏슴으로 그 住宅을 擴大히 할 必要가 有하엿스나 今日 吾人時代의 一家族員數는 夫婦子女  共하야 4,5人에 不過케 되얏슨즉 兵營을 模擬한 듯한 廣濶한 大 建物은 吾人 小數家族의 團欒의 以上을 達하기에 不適할 뿐만 아니라 保管 修理上에도 至大한 不利益이 生하는 것은 吾人이 實感하는 바이다. 此가 卽 前記 第3條件에 不合하는 缺點이오 其次에 朝鮮家屋의 長處로 特記할 것은 그 構造가 堅實固確하며 新工의 美觀보다도 古物的 美觀을 發揮하는 그것이니 朝鮮建物의 壽命은 西洋建物에 比하야 勝하고 日本建物에 比하야 倍勝하다. 朝鮮建物의 그 獨特한 堅實性에다가 發達된 建築學의 技術을 加하야 幾何的 設計를 加하엿스면 그 堅實性은 一層發揮될 것이다. 此 堅實性은 朝鮮建物 固有의 特色인대 此의 特色은 第 2로 他建物에 在하야는 見키 難한 一種特殊의 美觀을 更新하는 固性이 有하다. 即 西洋建物이나 日本建物에 在하야는 新築初期에 그 人工的 新美는 一種 「데리겟트」的 趣味를 感起하나 年이 久함을 從하야 그 廢老退破된 醜色은 實로 不美의 觀感을 起하야 改良 修繕에 多少한 經費를 要치 아니하면 存續을 維持키 難함에 反하야 朝鮮建物은 그 淸雅, 優美한 點에 關하야 日本建物이나 西洋建物에 比하야 遜色이 有타 할지나 그 特有한 堅實性은 一定한 時期가 經過되면 그 新工의 美觀은 消失된다할지라도 建物의 本體와 骨格이 持久性이 有함으로 大工의 修繕을 加치 아니할지라도 壽命이 存續되며 新工의 美觀이 消失된 後面에는 更히 特殊의 古物的 色彩를 發揮하야 一種의 美觀을 更新케 한다. 이와 가튼 特色은 他建物에 在하야는 類見키 難한 者이니 此點으로써 朝鮮建物의 價値를 認케된다.
朝鮮建物이 이와 가튼 特色을 持有한 反面에는 經濟的 壓迫이 甚한 今日의 社會形便에 當하야서는 時代에 應合키 難한 固性이 有하니 그 第一은 多額의 建築費와 擴大한 面積을 要하는 그것이다. 朝鮮建物이 他國의 建物에 比하야 建造費가 比較的 大한 것은 土木業 傳問業者가 共唱하는 바인데 此는 直接 朝鮮家屋의 賃貸價格을＜31＞ 高貴케하야 後論에 說한 바와 如히 一般借家人으로 하여금 多大한 居住費를 負擔케 하엿다. 또 朝鮮家屋이 何國의 建築에 比하여서나 廣大한 面積을 要하는 것은 市價地代가 漸騰하는 今日에 當하야 都市生活費의 負擔을 一層增加케 한 重要한 原因이 되엇다. 此가 前記第3條件에 不合되는 缺點이오.
第二는. 今日의 朝鮮社會는 世界文明에 同化되려 하야 社會制度가 時時로 變化하여 감으로 一般社會制度와 重要關係를 有한 住宅도 多大한 費用과 革新的 改造를 要치 아니하고 容易히 新制度에 順應調合되지 아느면 不可타 하노니 그러나 歷史的으로 發達된 朝鮮家屋의 構造樣式은 若干의 式樣變化로는 新時代가 産出한 新制度에 調合되기 難하니 此가 前記 第7條件에 應合치 아니하는 缺點이다.
朝鮮式 家屋의 構造樣式을 變更키 難한 原因의 第1은 吾人의 生活이 溫突式 生活인 그것, 第2는 家族制度가 特殊한 그것 第3은 生活 用品에 對하야 아즉도 配分制度가 完行되지 못하는 그것, 第4는 朝鮮建物은 他의 樣式을 加味하면 그 固有한 美觀를 消失케되는 그것 等諸點이 朝鮮家屋의 樣式變化에 對하야 相容치 아니하는 者인데 此等諸點에 對하야는 細密한 硏究가 無하고는 具體的 定評을 下키 難하나 樣式變化에 對하야 總括的으로 要望하는 바는 朝鮮建物의 固有한 特色을 消失치 아니하고 新味의 改良을 加하야 新制度, 新要求에 適應케 하라함이다.
外人 又는 一部朝鮮人間에서 朝鮮家屋에 對하야 不潔타 하며 不衛生的이라 評하는 者 有하나 論者는 此評論에 同肩코저 하지 아니 하노니 大槪 如何한 制度와 文物을 勿論하고 그 制度와 文物에 對하야 根本的 審査와 年久를 施치 아니하고 或은 非難하며 或 嘲評하는 者 有하며 或은 趣味의 不合, 見識의 不足으로 誤評하는 者 有하다. 朝鮮이 海外에 紹介된지 年이 久하엿다 할지라도 外國人으로서 朝鮮의 固有한 文物制度에 對하야 그 內容을 充分히 硏究하엿스며 審査한 者, 即 朝鮮을 잘 아는 者, 實노 多치 아니한 듯하다. 朝鮮家屋에 對하야 不潔타 하며 不衛生的이라 評하는 것도 朝鮮을 잘 아지 못하는 것과 그 主位의 外觀이 不潔한 것과 內部에 住居하는 者가 不衛生的인 것과에 原因된 바 多하고 住屋의 本體 그것은 決코 不潔하며 不衛生的인 本質을 有치 아니 하엿다 信하노니 此點에 對하야는 吾人이 各히 公開한 場所에 在하여서나 假定 內에 在하여서나 衛生과 淸潔에 意를 注하며 住宅 外界附近의 淸潔施設에 對하야＜32＞ 一層用意하야 外人의 非評을 兔하라 하노라.
以上 論評의 要點은 今日 吾人의 社會도 世界文明에 同化되여 감을 從하야 社會라는 大 建物의 單位 基本을 形成하는 家族의 住宅도 改良되지 아느면 不可타 함에 在한데 論者가 住宅問題를 論할 때에 그 構造樣式에 對하야 改良을 施할 必要를 感하는 同時에 朝鮮 家屋의 賃貸料가 他國建物에 比하야 比較的 高한 것을 切感한 바 多하엿다.
下段에 論한 바와 如히 總生活費 支出額에 對한 居住費 支出額의 割合이 外國人이나 日本人보다도 朝鮮人에 在하야 大하니 日本人에 在하야는 一家族月拂生活費總額이 100圓이라 하면 居住費 即 家賃은 10圓 乃至 15圓 即 10%-15%, 中流階級者에 至하야 35圓 乃至 40圓 即 18%-20%에 不過하고 外國人 米國人에 在하야 19%이 普通임에 對하야 朝鮮人에 在하야는 月收入 100圓을 有한 家族이 每月 家賃으로 支拂하는 金額이 20圓 乃至 25圓 即 20%-25%를 占하엿나니 此居住費의 重課가 直接 食料費, 被服費에 影響을 及하야 保健을 損케 할 것은 必生의 事實이 될 것이다.

3, 娛樂費, 其他文化生活費
娛樂費는, 同一한 收入을 有한 國民間에 在하야서도 그 國民의 文化程度, 國民的 趣味, 娛樂機關의 設備發達의 如何, 社會制度의 如何, 國民的 習性 其他種種의 相異에 依하야 同一치 아늘 것이고 同一한 國民間에 在하야서도 論의 初段에서 略論한 바와 如히 그 收入多寡에 의하야 各 階級의 娛樂費 持出額의 割合은 同一치 아늘 것이다.
相對的 生活費(絶代必要生活費에 對하야) 中에는 娛樂費 以外, 交際費, 修養費, 保險料, 其他雜多의 費目이 有할 터이나 論을 速結키 爲하야 此等各費目을 別個로 論치 아니하고 娛樂費又는 文化 生活費되는 總括的 名稱 下에서 此를 論할 터인데 此를 論함에 대하야 至極困難한 것은 前說한 바와 如히 此等費目의 內容性質이 旣論된 食料費, 被服費, 居住費에 比하면 變動性이 極히 多함으로 그 標準을 定키 難한 것이다. 此等費目의 標準額을 定키에 充分한 論을 試하자면 적어도 娛樂費의＜36＞ 性質, 吾人 各階級의 收入, 固有한 趣味 習性, 社會制度 及 文化生活의 內容, 從來로부터 固持하여온 娛樂機關의 性質及發達, 今後 文化 生活를 營키에 必要한 娛樂機關의 種類 及 設備에 對한 考察, 各階級 各社會에 適宜한 娛樂機關의 必要, 其他 各方面으로부터, 試論치 아느면 아니될지나 玆에는 論의 速結을 期키 爲하야 右에 關한 論은 全部約하고 左의 標準에 依하야 그 結果만 表示코저한다.

娛樂費 又는 文化生活費測定의 標準의 期一

一. 朝鮮人의 文化程度는 歐米人에 比하야 幼穉함으로 文化生活費의 支出이 少할 것,
一. 社會生活의 鍛鍊이 少함을 從하야 社會生活費가 少할 것,
一. 朝鮮에는 文化的 娛樂機關의 設備가 無함으로 娛樂費의 支出은 自然自感될 것,
一. 一般階級의 收入의 退減은 娛樂費支出에 影響될 바 多할 것,
一. 家庭制度 上으로 觀하야 主人以外에는 娛樂費 交際費를 要하는 家族이 少한 것,

結論
온갖 物體의 運動과 온갓 事實의 變化가 그 空間周圍에 對하야 兩面의 結果를 伴生케 하는 것과 가티 世界文明도 此 大原則에서 버서나지 아니하고 吾人 文化生活에 對하야 兩面으로 支配코저 한다. 即 吾人은 世界文明의 發達된 結果 兩面的 影響 下에서 一面에 在하야는 氣를 活하야 猛進하며 事物에 對하야 樂하되 他一面에 在하야는 氣를 衰하야 自通하며 事物에 對하야 悲치 아니치 못할 境遇에 立하엿나니 此 兩面의 劇이 即 世界文明의 結果오 文明發達의 後跡이다.
此 文明發達의 兩面的 結果에 對하야 一面의 結果를 善果라하고 他面의 結果를 惡果라 할지니 그 善果方面에 在하야는 世界文明이 吾人 生活에 對하야 精神方面에 對하야서나 物質方面에 對하야서나 多數供給하는 온갓 文明制度, 幸福의 事物 그것이오 그 惡果方面에 在하야서는 生存競爭이 激烈케 됨을 從하야 生하는 온갖 悲慘 即 生活亂 그것이 될 것이다.
吾人이 物體運動의 自然的 法則을 支配變則키는 不能할지나 그 兩面的 運動方面을 善調緩合하야 衝突이 生치 안케하기에 可能한 것과 가티 吾人이 文明發達의 原動性을 變化키는 不能할지나 其生하는 바 結果에 對하야는 善果를 失치 안을 同時에 如何한 方法으로 던지 그 惡果를 善調應化케하야 社會에 衝突이 生치 아니하기에 可能할지니 그 善調應化는 即 各種의 生活難救濟法 生活難緩和策 貧民救濟法, 其他 社會定策 그것이 될 것이다.
此等 生活難救濟法, 社會政策은 生活難이 世界 各 國의 通有的이니 마치 各 國이 精心精意로 專意硏究하는 바인데 吾人 朝鮮社會에 在하야는 如何한 方法과 政策이 朝鮮人의 生活難을 救키에 有効하며 朝鮮現制度에 應合施行키 不能할가? 此에 對하야는 後日 別稿로 硏究키로 하고 以上의 論으로써 「朝鮮人生活問題硏究」의 一部를 終하노라(終)
