기사제목	悅樂 (Joy / Delight)
필자	素月
기사형태	시 (Poem)

悅樂
						素月
A darkly, deeply choked heaven.

Rolling out from the bosom of dreams
The gaze of a mournful, sleepless Ghost (幽靈).

Upon the shadow-black willow tree
The streams of pouring rain
Are the sound of a chanted, slanted Spell (呪文).

With pitch-black hair disheveled
The daughter goes, wailing.

Naked worms unravel dreams,
Sea of Black Blood (黑血), Cave (洞窟) of Withered Trees (枯木)
The woodpecker's (啄木鳥)
Pecking sound, pecking sound.<39>

**Summary**

The poem "Joy" (悅樂) by Sowŏl uses stark and somber imagery—a choked heaven, sleepless ghosts, pouring rain like a spell, a wailing figure, naked worms, black blood, and the incessant pecking of a woodpecker—to create a mood of deep sorrow and despair. The title stands in stark, ironic contrast to the bleak content of the poem. For historians studying colonial Korea, this poem from 1922 reflects the pervasive atmosphere of anguish, confinement, and suppressed suffering under Japanese rule. The juxtaposition of the title and the dark themes might signify a veiled critique of the era or a personal expression of pain presented under a seemingly innocuous label, highlighting the psychological landscape of the period.
