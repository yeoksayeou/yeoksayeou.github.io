Thirty Folk Songs from Ryonggang
						Hong Chongin

In the previous issue of this magazine, I read with great interest over 50 folk songs from the people of Cheju Island, introduced by Mr. Kang Pongok. Although small, our Chosŏn spans three thousand *ri* north to south, and there are differences in climate and natural scenery between the south and the north. Consequently, the character, language, and customs of the inhabitants also differ considerably. Thus, when one mentions Cheju Island, one imagines quite a southern atmosphere.
Now, speaking of the sentiment of the North, I intend to introduce folk songs from the Northern Chosŏn region to connect the concepts of south and north among our people.

These are tragic songs. These folk songs are often sung by woodcutters in the mountains and farmers in the fields. Among them, those sung by women almost all despairingly express dissatisfaction with the past family system. For those of us unfamiliar with the past family system, especially when we see the wrinkles on the foreheads of our mothers and grandmothers along with these folk songs, who could refrain from shedding tears and not resolve anew for improvement?
Here, I state that the new poets who will come in the future for the world's people, for the Korean people, for our nation that will live with our language in our land, must first firmly shake hands with our folk songs and children's songs.

Planting *hŭnga* on the front hill
Planting indigo on the back hill,
Planning to go see Mother,
I prepared for three years,
But news came in an obituary
That Mother was gone.

Turning around a corner stone,
The sound of wailing echoes through the mountains.
With the front gate open wide,
Why did they call me when the burial was happening... .

Brother-in-law, brother-in-law,
My older sister's husband, brother-in-law,
Stay overnight, stay and rest,
Eat some pine needle rice cakes before you go.

Should I stay overnight, should I rest and go? <84>
Should I eat pine needle rice cakes and go?
Look at the face of my wife's younger brother's wife.

Mom, Mom, you raised a daughter,
Don't give her to Samhwa or Kangnyŏng.
The water is rough, the currents fast,
It's a place where married life is unbearable.
Barley rice like dog vomit,
I hate eating flower stalk side dishes.
The pestle handle (文來) I sleep hugging,
The tail end I grasp and sleep. (Handle of a *Mullae* pounding stick)

Though my older brother's handwriting is good,
They say there isn't a single letter from him.
Though my older sister's skills are good,
They say there isn't a single tobacco pouch from her.

Cousin, cousin,
How is life as a married woman?
A length of fine hemp cloth for a skirt, dyed half-black (반물),
It's all worn out from wiping tears.
A ten-*nyang* silver ring,
It's all melted away from wiping my runny nose.

The sun is setting, the sun is setting.
The sun is setting over Irwŏlbong Peak.
The setting sun over Irwŏlbong Peak,
Receive it on a brass tray.
Today is also the winter solstice,
Who will go to perform the solstice rites?
No matter how much of a young lady she is,
In the prime of youth, she cannot draw water,
She cannot cook rice on boiled beans.

On the laundry stone (쟁깃돌) on the front Namsan mountain,
Pound the fine hemp skirt (세뵈초매).
Even if you can't wear it half-dyed,
Pound it until it's smooth.

Under the bright moon at the third watch,
A lone wild goose cries as it goes.
Wild goose flying in broad daylight,
Where are you going, crying alone?
Night has fallen again tonight,
It's time for the young master of the study to come.
The spinning wheel (文來) my father-in-law decorated,
It doesn't turn smoothly, wobbling side to side.<85>
It's past the time for the evening bell (멍엣담을 means three *ri* away),
It's time for the young master of the study to come, but he hasn't.
If you're not going to pass the Chinsa or Taekwa exams,
Stop studying all night.

My longing for my mother
Is tied up like a silkworm cocoon (참실).
The affection tied like a silkworm cocoon,
I want to see it unravel.
My one and only older brother,
I planted him hoping he would sprout.
Neither sprouting nor budding,
Rotten or decayed,
I truly cannot bear this sorrow and live.

Why is a lover like this?
Before falling asleep, I cannot forget.
I try to forget after eating,
But with every sip of alcohol, thoughts of my lover.
I try to forget by sleeping,
But even in dreams, thoughts of my lover.
This uninteresting life,
Let's try raising a gourd vine.

Through spring, summer, autumn, winter, the four seasons,
Thoughts of my lover are all there is.
Fetching sweet water,
I place it on the stream bank (둑천)
And sigh as I turn away.

Does my lover come if I sit?
Does my lover come if I lie down?
The sound of crickets (蟋蟀셩) behind the back wall
Melts away a person's insides.

The skylark flying in broad daylight,
May it rise high like this heart of mine.
The bonfire for good fortune (복덕불) in the front yard,
Like this heart of mine, only the inside burns.

Do I leave you behind?
It is the soul (넉) of an egg-laying bird.
Even if I say I go, do I go for good?
Even if I go for good, could I forget?
When burning the ten thousand volumes of poetry and prose (萬卷詩書),
Why was the character for parting (離別離字) included?
Burn the character for parting (離),
And I want to write the character for meeting (逢).<86>

The evening sun crosses the mountain pass,
The road ahead is a thousand *ri*.
Don't urge the thousand-*ri* mule,
Won't I get there even if I go now?
The friend I made as dusk fell,
Says let's part as the sun sets.
Holding my head kerchief (의닙수건),
Let's meet again tomorrow morning.

Citron, corn (강뉴), pomegranate bird (石榴새),
Whose heart are you trying to melt?
Why were you born so exquisitely beautiful?
What beauty do I possess?
The love-me-not flower (相思不思꼿) is beautiful.

Though the branch is high, I cannot break it,
Let me at least leave my name.
No return, no return, never return (不歸不歸 다시 不歸),
Let me at least write my name.

Oh, it's hot, give me a fan,
Song Hae-wŏn's fifty-year-old fan.
Fan it, fan it,
Fan it towards my parents.
A wind like crystal in the ravine,
Blow just once more now.
Though the ravine wind blows,
Hwangjangsan Mountain is blocked.

Yi T'aebaek, with five *don* of money,
Went to fetch alcohol.
Drinking on the way there, drinking on the way back,
He drinks it all himself and doesn't return.

It's already noon today,
The lunch basket (덤심광지) is coming over the hill.
In a brass bowl (놋바리) like the morning star,
Comes the wife (妻子) like the half-moon.

Hamjong, Chŭngsan, Maewŏgae Mountain,
How can I cross them alone?
With a new *tanggu* flute and a *saengp'iri* pipe,
Let's cross while playing music (전주르며).

Eating breakfast and going to communal work (수여), <87>
The first plowing (길매기) is difficult.
Setting up the spinning wheel plow (물렛쟁기),
Plowing half a field section (반특헤기) is difficult.

Master, strike the flint (부쇠),
I can't tie my waistband (허리압하).
To buy flint, I go to Kaech'ŏn,
To buy tobacco, I go to Sŏngch'ŏn.
If you want to prepare our lunch (뎜심),
Prepare a simple meal (얼두반상).
To cook rice, there's no rice,
To make porridge (죽), there's no firewood (새).

Live well, live well.
Have sons and daughters and live well.
Living well is also one's fate (팔자),
Living poorly is also one's fate.
How should one live to be called living well?
Even one's own heart can discern (가리슴하다).

What is written above are folk songs and children's songs from their respective origins in Sŏmyŏn Oŭp - collected with the invaluable efforts of several young companions while I was in Ryonggang from the autumn of two years ago until last spring. There are still several more pieces besides these, but many contain dialects or old words whose sounds have changed over a long time, making their meanings quite difficult to understand, so they are unavoidably omitted here.
Also, the few pieces below were collected near Sŏkkujittangnolgo and other places along the Taedong River, about 30 *ri* from P'yŏngyang. These are mostly about love affairs. I have about 50 such pieces in my possession.
These, indeed, most explicitly express the flame-like love in the hearts of passionate young men and women, like the feeling of new buds and shoots sprouting again in spring, amidst the bitter life of marriage in meaningless homes - serving indifferent parents-in-law - and undeserved mistreatment. I would like to introduce all of them now, but in our Chosŏn, where love is still immature and understanding of art is lacking - a society where seeing just one nude painting (裸体畵) causes people to click their tongues or call it scandalous - it still requires waiting for a future time. Therefore, I will record only 4 or 5 of them and conclude.

In the desolate east wind, the wind over the pass,
My white silk ribbon (白갑사댕기) flutters.

When did I ask you to stay long?
I was in a hurry on my way and rushed off (활기쎄햇구나).

I went to sharpen my sickle on the whetstone,
And she puts a hemp cloth (뫼추단) pouch in my mouth (닙딜리주누나).

The peony (함박꼿) blossom outside the back gate,
Stooping low (소구둥하구도), it only watches for my lover.

In the smiling lips, slightly parted (햇쥭햇쥭),
It seems like holding a full balsam flower (봉솨).

Eyebrows-forehead, not knowing how she was born,
She carries a water jar (물똑) and spins around and around.
1923. 2. 11.
Chŏngyong At the foot of Sŏgŭmsan<88>

**Summary**

This article by Hong Chongin introduces thirty folk songs primarily from the Ryonggang area of northern Korea, collected between 1921 and 1923. The author contrasts these northern songs, often expressing the sorrows and hardships of life (particularly for women under the traditional family system), with the southern folk songs of Cheju Island mentioned in a previous issue. Hong emphasizes the importance of these folk songs for future Korean poets seeking to connect with the national spirit. The article presents the lyrics of several songs dealing with themes like parental loss, the difficulties of marriage ('sijipsari'), longing for family or lovers, the passage of time, hard labor, and resignation to fate. It concludes by mentioning another collection of love songs from the Taedong River area near P'yŏngyang, noting their passionate nature but lamenting that societal conservatism prevents their full publication at the time.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its documentation of early 20th-century Korean folk songs from a specific northern region (Ryonggang), offering insights into the language, customs, and emotional lives of ordinary people, especially women experiencing 'sijipsari'. The author's commentary reflects contemporary intellectual currents regarding national identity, the importance of folk traditions, and the perceived backwardness or conservatism of Korean society concerning expressions of love and art (like nude paintings) during the colonial period. The comparison between northern and southern cultural expressions (Ryonggang vs. Cheju) also highlights regional diversity within Korea.

本誌 前號에 康奉玉씨의 소개로 濟州島人 민요 50여 수를 자미잇게 닑엇다. 적으나미 우리 조선도 남북 삼천리에 亙하여 남과 북과의 기후와 산천의 相異가 잇스며 딸하셔 그 주민의 성품이며 언어이며 풍속 등의 相異도 적지 안타. 그리하여 濟州島라면 그래도 퍽으나마 南國氣分을 상상하게 한다.
이제 나는 北國의 風情을 말하면 北鮮地方의 민요를 소개하여 우리 민족의 남북의 관념을 서로 연락하려 한다.
悲曲이다. 이 민요는 흔이 산의 樵竪와 野의 농부들이 불으는 것인데 그 중 여자들의 불으는 것은 어느 것이나 거진 과거의 가정제도에 대한 불평을 절망적으로 부른 것이다. 과거 가정제도에 詳通치 못한 우리들로써야 더욱이 이 민요로 더불어 우리들의 어머니와 한머니의 니마의 주름살을 볼 때에 누가 눈물 안흘리며 다시 개선에 뜻하지 안흘 이가 잇슬까.
이에 다시 세계인류로 조선사람으로 우리 강산에 우리의 말로써 살 우리 민족의 장래에 올 새 시인은 먼저 우리의 민요 동요에 튼튼히 악수하여야만 할 것을 말해둔다.
