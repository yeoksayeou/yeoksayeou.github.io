잡지명 開闢 제34호
발행일 1923년 04월 01일
기사제목 우리의 足跡ㅡ 京城에서 咸陽까지 (Our Footprints - From Kyŏngsŏng to Hamyang)
필자 車相瓚
기사형태 기행문 (Travelogue)

Our Footprints - From Kyŏngsŏng to Hamyang
						Cha Sangch'an
The "Basic Survey of Korean Culture," which our Kaebyŏk Publishing Company had planned as a new project for the year Kyehae [1923], has now come to fruition. First, we turned our attention to Kyŏngsangnam Province to investigate its actual conditions, and the company's chief editor, Kim Kijŏn, and I were selected as the investigators. Our party had intended to leave Kyŏngsŏng sharp on February 1st for our destination, but the myriad affairs of this floating world naturally kept us busy, and it was only on the afternoon of the 2nd that we could finally depart. At 6 p.m., I gathered my travel gear and became a body upon a handcart, heading towards Kyŏngsŏng Station. Mr. Soch'un, full of preparedness, and several others including Ch'unp'a, Ilyŏn, and a few junior members who came to see us off, were already waiting inside the station grounds. Miss Ch'oe Pongsu, a student from Kimhae studying in Kyŏngsŏng, was also there, planning to accompany us to return home for treatment (Miss Ch'oe was pregnant at the time). Although the two of us held second-class tickets, we secured seats in a corner of a third-class car to travel with Miss Ch'oe. While chatting about this and that with those who came to send us off, the heartless time had already reached 7:20 p.m. At the sound of the whistle urging people on, we released their warm hands, saying farewell words, "Please stay well. We'll return around the sunny third month," and entered the train. The train puffed, emitting smoke, gradually picking up speed, and with a final shriek, it left Kyŏngsŏng Station behind. Although Kyŏngsŏng is not my hometown, it is my second home where I grew up and wandered for nearly twenty years, a place full of connections, tears of sorrow (恨淚), and affection. Even leaving for a short while fills my heart with many emotions. While lost in these thoughts, the train had already passed Yongsan Station and was crossing the Han River Railway Bridge. It was exactly the 17th day of the 12th lunar month. A bright moon rising in the east shone upon the Han River, making the sky and water appear as one color. The snow piled on Kwanak Mountain formed a silver world. Looking out from the train, it was truly "Moon white, snow white, heaven and earth white." Amidst the quietude, seeing this naturally lifted my spirits, and I sometimes sang songs or recited poems, chatting about various things with Soch'un. As the train reached Suwŏn, the moonlight grew brighter, making the world seem like a mirror, and the ice on West Lake looked like vast sheets of glass spread out. If the train hadn't restricted our freedom, I would have wanted to see the night scenery of Hangmijŏng<52> and Panghwasuryujŏng, and explore the historical sites of Hwahongmun and Hwasallŭng. But the heartless train, adopting the strategy of fleeing as the best option among the thirty-six stratagems, didn't stop and just kept running away. Quickly passing through stations like Osan, P'yŏngt'aek, Sŏnghwan, Chŏnŭi... we reached Choch'iwŏn as the night grew deeper and fatigue naturally set in. Entrusting my body to the berth, I fell asleep. Stations like Pugang, Okch'ŏn, Yŏngdong, Ch'up'ungnyŏng, Kimch'ŏn, Waegwan, Taegu, Kyŏngsan, Ch'ŏngdo, Yuch'ŏn, Miryang... half a thousand *li* of road passed by in a fleeting spring dream. The train arrived at Samnangjin, and the time was already five o'clock in the morning. As we two had to change to the Masan train here, we bid farewell to Miss Ch'oe Pongsu and, rubbing our sleepy eyes, gathered our luggage and got off the train. Since the "pass" (파스) I had was for travel between Kyŏngsŏng and Pusan, I made special arrangements with the station master to allow passage to Masan as well. The train departed at 5:20 a.m. and reached Naktong Station. The east was gradually brightening, and the Naktong River had already thawed and was flowing abundantly. Last night when leaving Kyŏngsŏng, I saw the Han River ice still solid enough for people and horses to cross, but the Naktong River was already thawed. The difference in climate was naturally apparent without needing words, I remarked to Soch'un. Quickly passing Yurim, Chinyŏng, and Ku Masan, we arrived at Masan Station, the terminus of the Sam-Ma Line. Gathering our luggage, we disembarked. Guides from various inns in Masan rushed towards us as if showing some kind affection, shouting loudly in the Yŏngnam dialect, "This inn is good," "This lodging house is convenient," making the station seem ready to burst. I recalled a line from someone's travel poem, "Seeing the mountain scenery, I suspect it's my old land; suddenly hearing people's speech, I realize it's a different region." Indeed, it was true. Just by hearing their speech, one could tell it was the Yŏngnam region. Since we were from Kyŏngsŏng and had some idea of local conditions, we didn't find it particularly strange, but if it were one's first visit, it would be easy to mistake their loudness for people about to start a fight. Smiling, I thought that since I was paying for the night, any inn that treated us well would do, and entrusted our luggage to one of them as I saw fit. However, they started arguing amongst themselves, shouting, "I spoke first," "No, it was me," creating another uproar. We two were bewildered and speechless. Eventually, they resolved the issue themselves, and we ended up going to the inn of the person we had initially chosen. Embracing the dawn breeze blowing gently from Koundae, watching the flickering streetlights of Masan city, we followed the guide to the inn. Hurrying to catch the automobile bound for Chinju, we hastily washed our faces and ate breakfast. The car was already about to leave, urging passengers on. We gathered our luggage again and became passengers on the automobile. Although it was my first time in Masan,<53> due to time constraints, I couldn't even see the city streets properly and left as if in a dream. Gazing only at the distant views of Masan Bay, Wŏryŏngdae, Tuch'ŏksan, and Kŭnwigu, we headed towards Chinju. As the red sun rose higher in the sky, the southern landscape refreshed the traveler's spirit. The snow on the mountains had all melted, the grass by the water was already turning green, and along the road, every house was adorned with lush green bamboo (綠竹) swaying gracefully (猗猗하다). This was a strange sight, certainly not seen in Kyŏngsŏng. However, seeing the small, dilapidated houses of the poor along the roadside naturally brought tears of sorrow. Ah, though all are people of freedom and equality, how is it that the social system is so unfair and imperfect? Some build grand mansions and live comfortably, while others live in two or three-*kan* huts with broken windows and walls, unable to properly shield themselves from wind and rain, worrying day and night for their daily bread (糊口之策). Isn't this the poison of capitalism and classism (班閥主義) that has come down to us? Of course, there is a gap between rich and poor in North Chosŏn (北鮮) too, but I haven't seen such a stark difference as in South Chosŏn (南鮮). Seeing this naturally filled my heart with discontent. If my discontent had weight, the automobile might not have been able to move. As we continued, the car reached Chungni. Along the road, groups of laborers working on the Mamoksŏn railway line, soon to be opened, were walking by. The road was rough, and the old automobile felt very precarious. After traveling for a while, we saw dozens of oxcarts heading towards Chinju resting on the roadside. As our car approached, they tried to move aside, but just then, an automobile coming from Chinju towards Masan rushed like wind and rain, and its wheel (박쿠?) seemed to collide with one of the oxcarts. The driver jumped out and, without a word (댓자곳자업시), grabbed the oxcart driver's topknot, kicked him, slapped his face (뺌), and dragged him along the road like catching a dog. The oxcart driver couldn't say anything, just pleaded for mercy, begging to be spared, but the wicked and unreasonable driver kept hitting him. Though dozens of other oxcart drivers were present, they just stood by blankly as if it were none of their business. Seeing this, we two were utterly aghast, exclaiming, "Those fellows are all dead inside! The laborers of South Chosŏn have no heart! If this were Kyŏngsŏng or P'yŏngan Province, the other oxcart drivers would have already beaten that driver to death!" Unable to contain our anger, we jumped out of the car intending to say at least a word. Perhaps the driver had hit him enough (따릴망콤), or perhaps his conscience returned, as he finally stopped. As the saying goes, "He who strikes with a pestle gets hit by a pestle." If even one of us had the strength, regardless of whether the man stopped or continued, we wanted to give him a beating for humanity's sake. But since our strength was insufficient to subdue him, there was no need to provoke further trouble. Suppressing (抑制) our anger, we climbed back into the car. Truly, the laborers of South Chosŏn are too docile and powerless. This<54> stems from the hereditary trait of absolute obedience to the yangban in former days, where they couldn't utter a word even when their precious lives and property were taken (빽기여도), and also from the recent bad habit (弊習) formed under constant oppression by military police assistants and policemen, where they tremble in fear even at the sight of a ragged person (흔텰방이?) in Western clothes, and address even ordinary elementary school students with shaved heads as "Master" (나리님). Among Korean laborers, those in South Chosŏn are truly pitiful and wretched. How can we guide these compatriots towards the path of light? The two of us lamented endlessly. From there, the car proceeded again, reaching the Chinju area. The road gradually became flatter, but the thawed mud, churned up by oxcarts and horses, turned it into something like a mire. An oxcart carrying dried pollack (靑魚바리), trying to avoid our automobile, couldn't manage due to the muddy road and ended up colliding with the car, completely smashing my trunk (도랑크). The driver stopped the car and jumped out. Fearing he would hit the cart driver again, based on what I had just witnessed, I intervened, saying, "Hey, the trunk is mine. Even if it's damaged (破傷), it's my loss, so don't say anything." I stopped him (가로마터) and called the cart driver over, jokingly demanding compensation for the damaged item. The cart driver, without a word, asked how much the damages were, saying, "If you ask me to pay for the damages, what can I give you?" I said it was about 15 *wŏn*. The cart driver replied, "The pollack I have is worth only 7 *wŏn*, so I can't arrange (變通) the rest until I get home." Smiling, I told him to forget about the damages but to be especially careful next time to avoid causing loss to others or facing insults, and let him go. These people are often careless, but they are very simple and honest (淳實하다). Urging the car onward again, we traversed the fertile plains (沃野) of Chinju, crossed the Namgang Bridge, and entered Chinju Fortress. Arriving at the bus stop, there was no one we knew, and everything felt unfamiliar. Having no choice, I asked the driver to recommend an inn, and he pointed us to the Chungang Yŏgwan in Taean-dong. Entrusting our luggage (行具) to a porter (인부), we headed towards the inn. On the way, we happened to meet Mr. Sin Yonggu, the local T'iandaojiao (天道敎) leader (宗法師), and Mr. Pak T'aehong, the chairman of the Youth Association, who had come to greet us. Unable to contain our mutual pleasure, we warmly grasped hands and exchanged greetings after a long separation (隔阻된 말), sharing various affectionate stories (情話) as we entered the inn. A little later, Mr. Pak Pongŭi, the general secretary of the Chinju branch, also arrived. Host and guests sat facing each other, chatting for a while, then decided to see the city streets and left the inn. Guided by these gentlemen, we entered a restaurant and heartily ate a bowl each of Chinju's specialty, *pibimbap*. Then, changing our route (路를 轉하야), we visited the local T'iandaojiao Main Parish (大敎區), Youth Association (靑年會), and Labor Mutual Aid Society (勞働共濟會). Afterwards, we crossed over to the foot of Pibong Mountain and reached Pibong Pond (飛鳳潭). The thousand-foot deep pond water, vying in depth with the friendship of our four or five companions, was rippling with spring waves, flowing vast and mighty.<55> Southern belles (佳人), loving the spring light, gathered in groups of three and five, leisurely doing laundry. We lingered on the bank for a while, enjoying the "pleasure on the Hao Bridge" like Master Chuang, then changed our pace and headed towards the city streets. I had heard a Chinju saying, "See three flies in one spoonful, meet one kisaeng every three steps." Due to the weather, we couldn't see flies, but kisaeng indeed seemed numerous. Red skirts and blouses flickered in every alley, dazzling the eyes (眼을 현란케 한다) of passersby (行客). Strolling slowly (緩步) towards the banks of the Nam River, Ŭirang Rock (義娘岩), boasting the eternal fame (萬古芳名) of Nongae (論介娘), stood alone and imposing (嵬然이) by the riverside. The long river's waters flowed with a mournful cry (鳴咽이), mourning (吊喪) the thousand-year loyal souls (千載忠魂) of the three heroes (壯士) Kim Ch'ŏnil, Ch'oe Kyŏnghoe, and Hwang Chin. Ascending Ch'ŏksŏngnu Pavilion and looking out over Chinju Fortress, every nook and cranny lay within view. Ha Ryun's record of Ch'ŏksŏngnu states: "Pibong Mountain rests to the north, Mangjin Mountain bows to the south, Ch'ŏngch'ŏn Stream winds to the west, the Long River flows between them, and the eastern and western mountains gently encircle it." This truly depicts the topography of Chinju Fortress. Chinju is the center of Kyŏngsangnam Province and the seat of the provincial government. It is a place filled with the most sorrow (恨), tears, and blood for our Korean people. During the Imjin War (宣廟龍蛇亂), when the fortress fell, over 60,000 ancestors of our Chinju compatriots perished here at once. No matter how steely-hearted one might be, ascending Ch'ŏksŏngnu, one cannot help but shed tears filling one's robe. Leaning on the railing, I sang the song of the three heroes' heroic stand (立節時), eternally magnificent and tragic: "Three heroes in Ch'ŏksŏngnu Pavilion, raising a cup, laughing, point to the Long River's water. The Long River's water flows滔滔兮, its waves unending兮, their souls undying." Then, following the rhyme scheme (韻을 次하야) of Sin Yuhan's poem beginning "East of Chinju Fortress, the water flows east, clusters of bamboo and fragrant orchids greenly reflect the province," I composed a poem:
千尺朱欄枕碧流, 登臨怳若上瀛洲. 山河百戰餘空郭, 風雨重經有此樓.
江水無窮壯士恨, 岩花尙帶美人愁. 居民不識當年事, 但醉笙歌月夜遊.
(A thousand-foot red railing rests on the blue stream, / Ascending here feels like reaching the Isles of the Blest. / Mountains and rivers saw a hundred battles, leaving an empty fortress, / Wind and rain have passed again, yet this pavilion remains. / The river's water holds the heroes' endless sorrow, / The cliff flowers still bear the beauty's grief. / The residents know not the events of that time, / Only revel in music and song, playing under the moonlit night.)
Our party then descended Ch'ŏksŏngnu, visited the Exhibition Hall (陳列館), turned west to see Sŏjangdae (West Command Post), Pukchangdae (North Command Post), Ch'ungnyŏlsa Shrine, and Hoguksa Temple, passed behind the provincial office, and returned to the inn. Feeling tired, I lay down for a bit. Dinner was already served, so the gentlemen who guided us returned home, leaving just Soch'un and me to eat. In the evening, several prominent figures (有志 諸氏) of Chinju visited us. We talked about various things until around 11 p.m., when everyone left, and we too went to sleep.
4th, Clear. As today was Sunday, I went to the Parish (敎區) at 11 a.m. to observe the Sunday service (侍日禮式). Soch'un, though middle-aged (장년), has a special fondness (조하하야) for youths and loses track of time when talking about them. He again ended up speaking to the local T'iandaojiao Youth Association (少年會員) members. It was around 2 p.m. when he finished speaking. We left, filled our midday stomachs (午腸을 充하고) with *pibimbap* again, did some more sightseeing in the city, and returned to the inn. The gentlemen of Chinju who cherish <56> our party, disregarding their busy schedules, visited us again in the evening. They shared many beneficial stories about local customs, history, and legends, returning home late at night.
5th (Monday), Clear. Around 11 a.m., guided by Mr. Yun Pyŏngŭn, a reporter from the Tong-A Ilbo branch office, I went to the Provincial Office (道廳) to obtain various materials for articles. After returning and having lunch, Mr. Pak Pongŭi and I visited some prominent private citizens again. At 7:30 p.m., the prominent figures of Chinju hosted a dinner party for the two of us at Kyŏngsŏnggwan. We went there and, amidst warm affection and cheerful laughter, enjoyed a lavish feast (滿盤의 盛饌), returning to the inn around 11 p.m.
6th (Tuesday), Clear. After breakfast, Soch'un and I split into two teams. Guided by local gentlemen, we investigated various aspects of the private sector.
7th (Wednesday), Clear. Having mostly completed our work in Chinju, we intended to leave for another county today. However, due to some unfinished business at the branch office, we couldn't leave again. Today happened to be Chinju's market day, so we went out to the market (장날) to have a look. Chinju market is truly bustling (번창하다). The place is spacious, and the variety of goods displayed (출품된) is vast. Agricultural products, marine products, industrial goods, livestock, various miscellaneous goods, fruits, foodstuffs – there was nothing missing. There were even small drums (小鼓), waist drums (腰鼓), gongs (錚), tops (팽쉬?), flint and steel (부시돌), and even yellow-marked *karisyŏkyak* (?) – things unseen in the markets of North Chosŏn. Furthermore, as the end of the lunar year (歲末) was approaching, traveling merchants (행상) from all regions had gathered, and village farmers (村農民) hoping for a prosperous day (各日興成) had all come, totaling tens of thousands, bustling like boiling water (물끌틋). Just as the people of West Chosŏn (西鮮) sing Farmer's Songs or recite novels in a mournful tone (愁心歌調), the people of South Chosŏn (南鮮) call out to sell their goods in either a six-beat rhythm (六字拍이 調) or a short song style (短歌調). Truly a magnificent (壯觀) and curious sight (奇觀). We two wandered around aimlessly (정신업시), asking prices and looking around as if intending to buy something. After wandering for about an hour, we went to the Parish, where they served us rice cake soup (떡국) for lunch. We ate heartily and, accompanied by Messrs. Pak T'aehong and Kim Ŭijin, and several leaders of the Youth Association, went to see Chŏkpyŏk (Red Cliff) downstream on the Nam River, the most famous scenic spot in Chinju. On the way, we entered the market again and bought octopus (文魚), dried persimmons (乾柿), and white sugar (白糖). Passing through Okpongni, famous as a peach blossom spot (桃花勝地), we followed the Nam River downstream (順流하야) and reached Chŏkpyŏk. Indeed, the rock formations (岩石) were bizarre, and the scenery (景槪) was superb (絶勝). It was no wonder (無理의 事가 안이다) that last autumn, on the 16th day of the 7th month (秋 7월 旣望), many pseudo-Su Tung-p'os (假者 蘇東坡) appeared, drawing general criticism. Since we are not literary men, we couldn't compose beautiful prose like Su Tung-p'o's "Red Cliff Fu," but deciding to eat octopus instead of writing beautiful prose, we shared the octopus we had brought. Descending the winding stone path (石逕斜路), the inscribed name of Yi Chaehyŏn (李載現) was clearly visible (뵈인다) on the cliff face (岩壁上). This was the Yi Chaehyŏn of the royal family (皇族), who, as governor (觀察使) in former days, committed all sorts of misrule (惡政), plundered (橫奪) the people's property, and inspired the folk song <57> about eating three *chi* of Chinju's soil. During his prime as governor, enjoying great luxury, he would daily visit Ch'ŏnggoksa Temple, about 20 *li* east of Chinju town, reveling riotously (질탕이) with kisaeng entertainment (妓樂). When returning at night, he forced the people along the road (沿路人民) to hold torches (炬火, also called *pambaragi*), turning the 20-*li* stretch into a city that never slept (不夜城) every night. When he went boating (船遊) on the Nam River, hundreds of pairs of boats (船雙) were commandeered (執留) for days on end, linking them across the entire river (全江을 連環) so that the river water (江水) sometimes couldn't flow. Not only that, he selected famous kisaeng from various counties to be directly attached to the Governor's Office and gathered dissolute youths and profligates (蕩子浪輩) from the capital and countryside, engaging in revelry day and night (晝夜遊逸). As goblins (독갑이) flock to the forest, kisaeng from various places kept gathering, so that their number, originally less than 20, reached over 700 within a year. The fact that Chinju still has many kisaeng is said to be entirely due to the "legacy" (遺德) of that Mr. Yi. Indeed, how much of the innocent peasants' lifeblood (고혈) did he squeeze out (짜먹엇스며), and how many sins against the state did he commit? It is said that Heaven repays the accumulator of evil with misfortune. Seeing him now, lacking food and clothing, relying on his younger brother's charity, wearing a worn-out dung-colored hat (쇠똥벙거지) and ill-fitting mismatched rubber shoes (짝짝이 고무신짝), his mouth twitching (실룩~) from palsy (風病마진), wandering the streets of Kyŏngsŏng – truly, Heaven's will is not indifferent. Having done all sorts of wicked deeds, did he intend to leave behind a stench (遺臭) for hundreds of years? Seeing his inscription is truly laughable and pathetic. While we talked about this and that, the evening sun was already reflecting on Okpong Mountain, and flocks of crows seeking their nests flew towards the foot of Pibong Mountain. We turned back, walking sometimes ahead, sometimes behind the market-goers dispersing in groups of three and five, and returned to the city. Today was truly enjoyably spent. After eating dinner, we discussed our departure for tomorrow with several visitors and chatted about other things. After the guests left, feeling tired, I went straight to sleep.
Leaving the affectionate and moving Chinju Fortress
Towards the ancient county of Tansŏng, with its bright waters and purple mountains
Our party, with a daily schedule (日割), intended to quickly finish our work in Chinju and leave for another place, but the affectionate and moving (多情多感) Chinju Fortress naturally caused us to spend four or five days there. Regardless of the circumstances, we resolved to leave without fail on the 8th. From the evening of the 7th, we organized our luggage (行李). To expedite our work, Mr. Soch'un and I decided to split into north and south teams. Soch'un, accompanied by Mr. Pak Pongŭi, would go to the coastal counties (沿海諸郡) like Sach'ŏn and Namhae, while I, with Mr. Sin Yonggu, would head towards the mountainous counties (山峽諸郡) like Sanch'ŏng and Hamyang.
8th (Thursday), Clear. After breakfast, I finished the necessary investigations at the County Office (郡廳) and Township Office (面所) and returned to the inn. Several gentlemen from Chinju, who came to see us off, were already waiting at the inn. After expressing my deep gratitude (禮辭) to them, I hurried with my luggage (行具) to the automobile stop. Since Soch'un was leaving in the afternoon, he also came to the stop to see me off. <58> I held the loving hands of the Chinju gentlemen and Soch'un, bidding them a sorrowful (悵然이) farewell, and boarded the vehicle. With a *bbong-bbong* sound, the car departed, and I bid farewell to Chinju Fortress, where I had briefly grown attached. A folk song goes, "Getting attached everywhere I go, I can't live with these frequent partings." Truly, my partings are frequent (잣다), I thought to myself. The car, racing like the wind (질풍갓치), quickly passed the foot (밋을) of Pibong Mountain. Ch'ŏksŏngnu gradually receded beyond the clouds. After traveling about 40 *li*, we reached T'ohyŏn Pass, marking the end of Chinju county (郡界); from here began Tansŏng. Tansŏng truly lived up to its name (meaning Cinnabar Fortress): the mountains were red, and the fields were red. Just as Hwangju is called Hwangju because its soil color (土色) is yellow (黃), Tansŏng is called Tansŏng because its soil color is red (丹). In an instant, the car arrived at the Sinan Station stop. We got off, rested briefly, then hired a porter to carry our luggage, crossed Sinan Station, and headed towards the old Tansŏng town center. Reaching Sin'gyŏp'yŏng, Mr. Kang Iksu, the local T'iandaojiao missionary (傳敎師), was waiting there to greet (마지랴고) us. We met happily, exchanged greetings (寒暄), and then, guided by him, entered Sŏngnaeri. After having lunch at an inn, guided by Mr. Yi Handong, an executive of the Tansŏng Youth Association (丹城靑年會), we first inspected the entire town streets and visited the Township Office (面所), Youth Association, and T'iandaojiao Mission Hall (傳敎堂). Tansŏng, originally a small town, became even more dilapidated (凋殘) after becoming subordinate to Sanch'ŏng (March 1, Taishō 3 [1914]), evoking feelings of desolation and stillness (寂寞蕭條) in the observer. However, in terms of natural scenery (景槪), production of notable figures, and industrial development, it is by no means inferior (손색이 少無하다) to other large villages or counties (雄村巨郡). This is because the Sinan River, the upper stream of the Chin River, meanders (灣回) here, with Mt. Ŏmaksan rising precipitously (屹立) above it. To the west lies Mt. Naesan, to the north stand the scenically renowned Mt. Myŏngwŏl and Mt. Paengma, embracing it from afar (遙遙擁立). To the northeast, Chŏkpyŏk (Red Cliff) lines up like a brocade screen (錦屛), and to the south rises Mt. Nigusan like soaring wings (聳翼). Mukkok silk (黙谷), *myŏngju* silk (明紬), Mundae whetstones (文臺의 礪石), Ch'ŏnggye porcelain (淸溪磁器), Paegun pottery (白雲陶器), and Sŏngnae green onions (城內靑葱) are the special products (特産物) of this township, famous in Kyŏngsangnam Province. The filial piety (出天의 孝) of Lord Kang Sŏng (Mun Ikchŏm) and his virtue in transplanting (移種) cotton (大綿) are admired (추모하며) by all. The wealth (富力) of the people includes 6 individuals with over 3,000 *sŏk* [of rice equivalent] and 5 or 6 people with 500 to 1,000 *sŏk*. It is indeed a place with substantial inner worth, even in decline (죽어도 실속이 만은 곳이다). I heard various stories about Tansŏng from Mr. Yi Handong and also obtained (어더서) the county gazetteer (郡誌), learning clearly (瞭然이) about its famous sites, historical relics, notable figures, customs, etc. I felt truly grateful.
Exploring Paeyangdong, the Origin of Korean Cotton,
and Visiting Mukkok-ri, the Foremost Model Village in Kyŏngsangnam Province.
9th (Saturday), Clear. At breakfast, accompanied by Mr. Sin Yonggu and Mr. Kang Iksu, I went to the Township Office to visit the Township Head (面長), Mr. Son Pyŏngguk. This Mr. Son is also the Vice-Chairman of the local Youth Association. I asked Mr. Son to guide us to Tansŏng's famous places, and despite his many official duties (公務의 多端), he agreed. We set off immediately, first exploring (踏査) Paeyangdong (Cultivation Village, now Sawŏlli), the birthplace of Korean cotton. This Paeyangdong is the old home site (古基) of Lord Kang Sŏng (ancient name for Tansŏng) <59> Mun Ikchŏm. His filial piety monument (孝子碑) still stands there. South of its pavilion (碑閣), there is a rectangular field of about fifteen or sixteen *p'yŏng* (called 培養田 Paeyangjŏn, Cultivation Field). This is the field where the master hand-planted (手植) cotton and conducted experimental cultivation (시험배양). However, a newly constructed road (新作路) now passes through its middle, turning about a quarter of it into road and dividing the field east and west. Seeing the master's monument pavilion and its surroundings filled me with immense emotion. Today, the fact that our Korean people wear cotton clothes (木棉衣) and fully protect their bodies is entirely due to the master's virtue. Yet, times are distant (踈遠), people's hearts have become coarse (杇朴), the master's monument pavilion is dilapidated (퇴락) from wind and rain, and his bequeathed field has been encroached upon by a road, yet there is not a single person trying to preserve and commemorate it. This is truly lamentable (한심하고) and regrettable (可惜). Especially (況 且) in this era of promoting domestic products (국산), we need to revere the master nationally and preserve his relics (遺跡). Lamenting endlessly (개탄을 不禁), our party left the site and headed towards Mukkok-ri, known as the foremost model village (模範村) in Kyŏngsangnam Province. On Pip'ado Island, with its ten *li* of bright sand (明沙) where green pines (蒼松) and green bamboo (綠竹) grow sparsely (간간이 叢生), we mourned the lonely souls (寂寞孤魂) of exiled transients (流入遷客) (it was formerly a place of exile, 流謫地). Crossing (渡하야) the Sinan River and detouring about one *chō* [approx. 109m], we found a wide valley (山谷이 平濶) with houses lined up (즐비) (170 households). The village surroundings were densely covered (鬱密) with bamboo groves (竹林) and chestnut groves (栗林), and the sounds of crowing roosters and barking dogs (鷄鳴犬吠의 聲) reached the four neighborhoods, making it seem just like T'ao Yüan-ming's village of Ch'ai-sang (柴桑村). At the village entrance, we met Mr. Ch'oe Tusu and entered his house. He seemed to be an influential person (有力한 人) in the village, and his family circumstances (家勢) appeared not poor (不貧). The house was neat and clean (淸楚), and the garden (庭園) was considerably adorned. What first caught my eye was a pair of intertwined trees (連理樹, also called 相思木, love tree). I had previously only read the line "On earth, we vow to be trees with branches intertwined" in Pai Lo-t'ien's "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" and had never seen the real thing. Now, seeing it in reality, I found it even more curious and asked the owner about the tree's characteristics. The owner explained that the tree's leaves and flowers are entirely similar (相似) to those of the plum (李), bearing yellow fruits (황색의 實) that serve as a miraculous medicine (靈藥) for women in difficult childbirth (難産). Its branches always grow straight up facing each other (相對直上), and no matter how one tries to separate them artificially (人工으로 분열케하야도), they have the characteristic of returning to face each other. It is said to be a rare plant (희귀한 식물) even in Kyŏngsangnam Province (Later, I saw this tree again in Ch'angnyŏng). Full of thoughts of longing (相思의 情), I lingered in contemplation (相思하다가) near this love tree for a while before entering the owner's guest room. I asked the owner about the village's customs, habits, and the reason for being a model village. The owner replied, "Our village has nothing else worthy of being a model, except that the residents are diligent and frugal (근검하며). Since long ago, we have had many side businesses (副業). Annually, we produce about 80 rolls of *myŏngju* silk, worth about 6,000 *wŏn*; over 30 *sŏk* of chestnuts (栗), worth about 900 *wŏn*; and bamboo products (竹物) worth about 1,500 *wŏn*, totaling about 9,400 *wŏn*. Among these, the *myŏngju* silk (quality is sturdy) ensures that no one is poor (貧寒). Also, people pay their taxes voluntarily (자진납부), so there are no visits from government officials (官公吏) for collection enforcement (督促出張)." Under the current revenue-focused administration (收入主義), where simply paying taxes well makes an individual or group recognized as a sole model, it is indeed worthy of being a model. In any case, it is a very good thing <60> that the people put effort into production. As time was pressing, our party immediately bade farewell to the owner and sought out the house of Yi Chinsa (his name was Chin-dong), said to be the wealthiest man in the village and also the stingiest, earning him the nickname "pig" (되야지). Even if the main house (본치) was absent, the height (崇高) of the walls (墻垣) and the grandeur (宏大) of the buildings, seen even from the outside (外面), clearly indicated wealth. We entered the guest room. The host, possessing many characteristics of suspicion, gloominess (陰徵), and stinginess, seemed displeased (不吉) by our arrival. Moreover, the plainclothes detective (査公) from Tansŏng who had been tailing (미행) us was already present (來房) in the room, furtively glancing around. We asked our questions, ate the tea and snacks offered, and then left again. Crossing the Sinan River, we visited Mr. Cho Hyŏnjang in Sonamch'on. Then, wanting to see Namsari, the concentration point of Tansŏng's yangban, scholars, and wealthy families, we entered Namsari along the sunset mountain path, accompanied by shepherds walking in pairs. Guided by Mr. Son Pyŏngguk, I visited the home of Mr. Ch'oe Inhwan, the wealthiest man in Sanch'ŏng County and the Chairman of the Tansŏng Youth Association. Because he was the Youth Association Chairman, I expected him to have some modern knowledge. However, he greeted us wearing a fist-sized topknot under a Chŏngjagwan cap, swaying his buttocks in wide silk trousers, and holding a long bamboo pipe with a jade mouthpiece. As he himself said, he was indeed a chairman appointed solely due to his wealth. While chatting about this and that, the time had already reached about 7 p.m. Mr. Son Pyŏngguk and Mr. Kang Iksu, being busy, returned to Sŏngnaeri, while Mr. Sin Yonggu and I stayed overnight at Mr. Ch'oe's house.
10th (Saturday), Clear. I woke up around 7 a.m. Yesterday, dusk had already fallen (日邑이 己暮), so I couldn't see the village scenery well. Feeling curious, I took a walk alone through the village (洞內) before breakfast. Namsari was indeed, as I had heard, a concentration of wealthy families. In this large village of several hundred households, tile-roofed houses (瓦家) of 50-60 *kan* were lined up (즐비). Among them, the house of one Ch'oe Sŏnho was said to be the finest mansion (甲第) in Kyŏngsangnam Province. Having only seen small houses along the Kyŏngbu railway line or within Chinju Fortress, I had always felt a sense of inadequacy. Seeing the huge houses of the Namsari rich, I was astonished (놀낫다). Even in Chinju city, the seat of various provincial offices, I hadn't seen such houses. However, looking at their clothing, food, etc., I wondered how they could have conceived of building such dwellings, talking to myself as I returned to the host's house. It was already past 10 a.m., but in the carefree host's house, breakfast was still not ready. Pressed for time, we waited anxiously, finally eating breakfast around 11 a.m. and preparing to leave. The detective (査公) who had been tailing us since Mukkok yesterday, having slept somewhere, showed up again at Mr. Ch'oe's house. Mr. Ch'oe, who was supposed to place an advertisement celebrating the publication of our magazine issue (道號發行祝賀廣告), got scared upon seeing the detective and hesitated to give the advertising fee properly. He gestured to the detective, whispered (수근~) with him in another room, and only then came out and gave it. This is, of course, a common occurrence (常事) for village wealthy men. We expressed our thanks (禮謝) to him, bid farewell, and then visited the neighboring Mr. Yi Pyŏnggwŏn. He was ill in bed, <61> and his younger brother (季氏), Mr. Yi Pyŏnghwa, met us with a haggard (초췌한) face. As he was a graduate of Kyŏngsŏng Posŏng Chŏnmun School (京城普成專門學校), we had known each other previously through school ties (校友의 親誼). Mr. Yi, being quite conservative (보수성이 만은), had returned home after graduating from the law department, grown his hair long (髮을 長하고) again, and had been secluded (蟄伏) at home for nearly 10 years. Truly stubborn (頑固) amidst the enlightenment (文明) - a real piece of stubbornness (찰 頑固). While I was eating the tea and snacks offered by Mr. Yi, the sharp-nosed (냄새 잘맛는) detective entered again. A little later, two sword-wearing officers (칼치장사, likely policemen), the detective's superiors, passed by the front of Mr. Yi's guest room. The detective went out again, then came back in. According to what I heard later, they were conducting night patrol (夜警) to monitor us. Truly, their nerves are overly sensitive. It's rather pitiful (가업다?) that we caused them to lose sleep. As we were about to leave, Mr. Kwŏn T'aehan, the general secretary of the Tansŏng Youth Association, who had promised yesterday to accompany us to Tŏksan, arrived just in time. We bid farewell to Mr. Yi and, guided by Mr. Kwŏn, headed towards Tŏksan. The detective who had protected us for two days, perhaps finding it too boring, simply slipped away (뺑손이 치고 간다). If he had come with us to Tŏksan, I had intended to give him a wooden cup (木盃), abundant in Tŏksan, as a reward (賞주랴고) for his peculiar service, but the unlucky fellow (福철리이?) just left. From now on, it was just the three of us companions. Passing Pukpau Rock, we walked along the flat, broad road (탄탄대로) at the foot of Mt. Nigusan, chatting about various things. We rested briefly at Ch'iljŏngni, then set our compasses (콤라스?) again. From here, the Sich'ŏn Township (矢川面) office was said to be about 10 *li* away. Following the Tŏksan River (德山江), famous for its mandarin fish (鱖魚) (Mandarin fish are abundant in Tŏksan River, and their taste (其味) is superb (極佳), leading to a folk saying that if a Tŏksan tenant farmer (소작인) doesn't give mandarin fish sashimi (鱖魚鱠) to his landlord (지주), he loses his leased land), passing Paeksŏltong, we reached Ipdeok (Entering Virtue). This Ipdeok Gate is where the master Nammyŏng Cho Sik (南冥先生 曹植), upon retiring to Tŏksan, personally wrote and carved (삭인 곳이라) the three characters 入德門 on a rock face. He was not only a scholar of Daoist learning (道學君子) but also a renowned calligrapher of our East (我東). The three characters 入德門 he wrote are grand and powerful (雄建) in strokes (畫), like dragons and snakes soaring (龍蛇가 飛騰함과 如한고로). Therefore, visitors to this place invariably made rubbings (模寫) of it to take away. However, some years ago, when a new road was being cut (鑿할 시에), that rock was destroyed, so the master's calligraphy vanished like gunpowder smoke (爆烟). Only a copy (模寫) transferred (移刻) onto another rock remains. Seeing this, I lamented unceasingly (慨歎不己) before moving on. Until entering Ipdeok Gate, although it was mountainous, there was much worldliness (俗臭). But after entering Ipdeok Gate, the valley (洞府) became increasingly deep and secluded (深邃), with white stones and clear streams (白石淸溪) winding everywhere (曲曲이), making it feel truly like entering a different world (別有天地). Following the winding path (峰回路轉), we entered Kyeridong. Mt. Chiri (智異山), with an elevation of 6,600 *ch'ŏk* [feet?], its white snow gleaming (皚皚), seemed to welcome me. Fond of joking (戱言), I looked at Mt. Chiri and said to Mr. Sin Yonggu: "Previously, Ch'a Kyŏngsŏk came to Mt. Chiri and deceived many people by claiming that the Son of Heaven Ch'a (車天子) had descended.<62> Since my surname is also Ch'a (車哥), I too shall call myself the Son of Heaven Ch'a, and you, being tall with a fine beard, shall be General Sin (申將軍). Let's deceive the residents at the foot of Mt. Chiri once more." Just kidding (却說). Our party entered Kyeridong. The valley opened up flatly, like the inside of a gourd (壺中), with several hundred houses lined up, and pine, bamboo, and persimmon trees (柿木) standing densely (林立) everywhere. Guided by Mr. Kwŏn, we entered Tŏksan Yŏgwan (Inn). The proprietress (主婆), Kang Kŏgak, welcomed us warmly and led us to the guest room. This proprietress, originally a woman from a good family (良家女子) in Hadong, had somehow ended up (淪落) in this mountain place, living the life of an innkeeper, bidding farewell to the old and welcoming the new (送舊迎新) through spring breezes and autumn moons (春風秋月). Although her age was nearly 50, her skillful social arts (交際手段), honed through countless battles (百戰) in the demimonde (花柳巷裹), could easily captivate (魔殺) young travelers. Seeing her, I wondered if the Magu immortal (麻姑女) who breathed mist and clouds (烟霞) on the 36 peaks of Mt. Tiantai (天台山) had moved to Mt. Pangjang (方丈山中). Shortly (少焉), lunch (午饌) was brought out. The style (범절) of the cooking was truly a rare skill (善手) in the mountains. After heartily eating (飽喫) this, braving the fierce north wind blowing from Mt. Chiri, we crossed Tŏkch'ŏn Bridge and went to the Sich'ŏn Township Office (矢川面所). Although office hours had passed, we specially requested the Township Head (面長) and investigated the general situation of the township and the status of the University Practice Forest (大學演習林), then immediately returned to the inn. After dinner, the owner prepared bathwater and invited us to bathe (목욕하라). Feeling even more grateful, we bathed. Our bodies naturally tired, we spent a night (留宿) in this separate world (別有天地).
11th (Sunday), Clear. I rose early (早起), washed my face, and visited Mr. Aoki (靑木?), the caretaker (看守) of the University Practice Forest, to hear about the forest. On my way back, I viewed (拜觀) Sanch'ŏnjae Pavilion (山天齋), where Master Nammyŏng used to read (built by the master, 先生所築), and his tomb (the tomb is on the mountain behind Kyedong; the epitaph on the spirit path stele (神道碑文) was composed (撰함) by Hŏ Misu and Song Uam). Returning to the inn, I ate breakfast and then reviewed (一覽) the various documents collected in Tŏksan. Externally, Tŏksan appears beautiful (佳麗) with its mountains and waters, forests, orchards, and fields, leading one to easily assume its residents live a paradise-like existence (극락생활) without any worries. However, investigating its inner circumstances (내막) from various angles reveals a truly miserable and pitiful reality. Mt. Chiri is called one of the Ten Victory Sites (十勝之地) in the Chŏng Kam Nok, but it uniquely suffered severe calamities (酷禍) during the Kabo Peasant Uprising (甲午民衆亂) and the subsequent Righteous Army (義兵亂). Furthermore, after the University Practice Forest was established and their lifeline (생명인), Mt. Chiri, was taken away (被奪) (the University Forest is discussed in detail separately, so omitted here, 畧함), their means of livelihood completely disappeared. Added to this are the pressures from the numerous Cho clan members who parasitically exploit (썩은 뼈를 울게 먹는 떼만은 曹씨 등살) Master Nammyŏng's legacy, and the Japanese moneylenders (고리대금업자) who suck the blood of Koreans, making life unbearable. Its name sounds nice, Tŏksan (Virtue Mountain), but in reality, it's Toksan (Poison Mountain, 毒山). Thinking of the true plight of the Tŏksan people, I lamented endlessly and prepared to leave. Mr. Kwŏn T'aehan came again and gave us two bamboo canes (竹杖), local products, as souvenirs for the two of us. We accepted them gratefully, bade farewell to him and the inn owner, and went back out towards the village entrance. Swinging my bamboo cane, passing through Ipdeok Gate, I unexpectedly <63> composed a line (吟하얏다):
客入桃源非別界, 仙歸方丈亦人間
(The traveler enters Peach Blossom Spring, yet it's not another world; / The immortal returns to Mt. Pangjang, yet it's still the human realm.)
My poetic inspiration (詩思) dried up (索莫), so I couldn't complete the verse (一首) and left it at that. Traveling together (作伴) with charcoal sellers (숫쟝사) emerging in groups of three and five, chatting about various things, we soon passed Ch'iljŏngni and reached Namsari. Resting our feet (歇脚) by the roadside, we set off again. After walking about ten *li*, we saw a village at the foot of a mountain with deep bamboo groves, where dozens of thatched huts (茅崖이?) were faintly visible (隱暎). This was Sŭngp'yŏngni, where Kwak Chong-sŏk, a great Confucian scholar (巨儒) of recent Yŏngnam, used to live. We briefly observed the village appearance (村容) from under a pagoda tree (槐樹下) by the road, then left again. Treading (발고) the summit of Ch'wijŏk Peak (吹笛峯頭), we reached Salgonae. This was the place where Lord Kang Sŏng (Mun Ikchŏm), a man of exceptional filial piety (出天의 효자), set weirs (살을 노와) to catch fish to serve (奉親하랴고) his parents. From here, the old town of Tansŏng was fully visible. Passing Pip'ado Island, we looked once more at Lord Kang Sŏng's Filial Piety Monument and entered the inn in Sŏngnaeri. Thus, the 11th was also spent entirely in Tansŏng.
Arriving in the Beautiful Sanch'ŏng County
and Mourning the Tomb of King Kuhyŏng, the Last Ruler of Karak.
12th (Monday), Clear. After breakfast, we immediately departed. Guided by Mr. Kang Iksu, we went to Kangnuri to visit Mr. Kwŏn Chaehyŏp. Crossing the Sinan River, we reached the San-Chin Automobile Stop (山晉自働車停留場). The car had just arrived and was waiting for passengers. We bought tickets for Sanch'ŏng and, after bidding farewell to the prominent figures of Tansŏng who came to see us off, boarded the car around 11 a.m. Leaving Wŏnji Station, the car raced along the flat road at the foot of Mt. Myŏngwŏl, passing by the site of Hudie Pavilion (蝴蝶樓, also called Sinyo-ru), with the scenic Chŏkpyŏk and Paengma mountains behind us, the spring breeze gently blowing. Passing Sinan and Oesong, we reached Pŏmhangni. The mountains grew successively higher (놉고), the water clearer (맑은데), and the snowscape (雪景) in the gorges (兩峽) became even better, truly resembling the post-snow scenery of Shan-yin in China (as Sanch'ŏng's old name was Sanŭm, 山陰 故로 云). The car dashed at full speed, leaving the villages of Chŏnggok and Chŏngjŏng behind in an instant (片時에 물너가고). Soon, the Sanch'ŏng County town stop was ahead. Getting off the car, I looked around at the overall view of the county town. As I had heard, Sanch'ŏng is truly a land of mountains and water (山水의 鄕). To the south stands Mt. Sambong, to the west Mt. T'aesŏk rises precipitously. To the north, the Kyŏngho River (鏡湖江), famous for producing silverfish (銀魚), flows southward, encircling Mt. Hoegye (the mountain behind the county town) like a vast white silk ribbon (萬丈白練). On the south bank of the river, Hwan-a Pavilion (換鵝亭, now the specimen room of the Common School) stands gracefully (翼然이 臨) on a cliff top shaded by old, lush trees. To the east, Mt. Tŭnggye and Mt. Hwangmae (source of smokeless coal, 無烟産地) face each other from afar (遙遙相對). However, due to the overlapping mountains and inconvenient transportation, it seemed unlikely to develop much beyond its current state. Guided by Mr. Sin Yonggu, we entered Kyŏnghŭng Yŏgwan in Saedong, had lunch, and then, mustering our courage again, decided to investigate T'ŭk-ri in Kŭmsŏ-myŏn, promising as a porcelain production site (磁器産地). Leaving the inn, equipped with bamboo canes and Western shoes (竹杖洋鞋), we crossed over the ridge of Mt. Hoegye and traversed the Kyŏngho River. On the way, I composed a poem (吟하얏다):<64>
鏡湖春水碧於烟, 白鳥雙飛夕照邊, 晩踏換鵝亭下路, 竹林深處有孤船.
(Kyŏngho's spring water, greener than mist, / A pair of white birds flies by the evening glow. / Late, I tread the path below Hwan-a Pavilion, / Deep in the bamboo grove, a lone boat rests.)
Disembarking from the boat, we headed towards T'ŭk-ri along the sunset mountain path. The stone path (石逕) was rugged (崎嶇) and perilous. Moreover, the half-melted snow was extremely slippery (滑膩), causing us to fall once every three steps. My shoes were coated in mud (진흙칠), and my hat soaked with sweat (땀투셩이다). Panting (허위~), we crossed the pass of about ten *li*. Wangsan Mountain, behind T'ŭk-ri, grew steadily closer (각가워 온다). This Wangsan Mountain (King Mountain) was named by people because King Kim Kuhyŏng (金仇衡), the tenth king of the Karak Kingdom (駕洛國), surrendered to King Pŏphŭng of Silla (新羅 法興王), came to Hwasan-dong north of this mountain to spend his remaining years (餘年), and after his death, was buried (葬한 고로) on this mountain. From afar (遙遙히), I mourned (吊) the ancient tomb (古陵) of this fallen king (亡國王) and entered T'ŭk-ri. Mr. Kim Wisang, an instructor (訓導) at the local Common School (普通學校), met us on the way. Guided by Mr. Kim, we visited Mr. Min Yŏnggil, then accompanied Mr. Min to visit Mr. Min Yŏngjik, who had devoted great effort (노력) and financial resources (金力) to porcelain research (磁器硏究). Although confined to his sickbed (病석에 在하여서도), he was still reading books related to porcelain manufacturing research. After discussing various aspects of porcelain with him and carefully examining the results of his experiments, we left promptly due to time constraints (此에 관한 상세한 事는 別錄에 在함 - Detailed matters concerning this are in a separate record). Messrs. Min and Kim urged us to stay overnight, but we firmly declined (固謝) due to our busy schedule. Messrs. Min and Kim then kindly accompanied us all the way back to Sanch'ŏng town. We returned to the town (歸城) around 7 p.m., had dinner together with the gentlemen, and then, feeling exhausted (困하야), immediately went to sleep (취침하얏다).
Passing Sagŭn Station, an Ancient Korean-Japanese Battlefield,
Towards Hamyang Fortress, Rich in Famous Sites and Historic Relics.
13th (Wednesday), Clear. Messrs. Min and Kim left before breakfast due to school commitments. Guided by Mr. O Chusŏk and Mr. Kim Yŏngjŭng, we visited the County Office, Township Office, and other prominent figures, then departed Sanch'ŏng at 11 a.m. Around 3 p.m., we reached Sagŭn Station (in Hamyang County, 咸陽郡). This Sagŭn Station is where, in the 10th year of King U of Koryŏ (麗朝辛禑), the Commander of Three Provinces (三道元帥) Pae Kŭngnyŏm led a large army to resist Japanese invaders (왜구) attacking from Sangju, but was defeated (敗績). Generals Pak Sugyŏng and Pae Kŭgŏn made their last stand (立節), and over 500 brave soldiers (장정) tragically perished here (其 時 溪水가 盡赤하야 仍名 血溪라 - At that time, the stream water turned completely red, hence it was named Hyŏlgye, Blood Stream). Reciting (誦함애) Yi Ch'ŏm's poem of remembrance about this place, "Blood splattered the grass on Hamyang Plain" (血濺咸陽原上草), tears of sorrow (비장한 눈물) flowed involuntarily. Ah, compatriots of Hamyang! Do you know this sorrow (恨), or not? Even though it happened five or six hundred years ago, thinking about it truly makes my bones ache (뼈가 매치고) and my blood boil (피가 끌는다). Forcing back my tears, I looked west again towards Sujibong Peak, and suddenly (뭇득) thought of Wŏlmyŏngch'ong (Moon Bright Tomb), composing a poem of remembrance (懷古詩):
月明塚上月惟明, 環珮無聲蜀魄鳴, 此是人間膓斷處, 街童莫唱望東京
(On Wŏlmyŏng Tomb, only the moon is bright; / Jade pendants are silent, the cuckoo cries. / This is a place where human hearts break (人間膓斷處); / Street urchins, do not sing "Gazing Towards Tonggyŏng.")
(Folk tale: Long ago, a merchant from Tonggyŏng (Kyŏngju) loved Wŏlmyŏng, a beauty of Sagŭn Station. The merchant left. Wŏlmyŏng longed for him day and night, fell ill (相思成病), and died (遂死). She was buried on Sujibong Peak. Later, the merchant heard of her death, came, wept (哭) at her grave, and also died. People buried him in the same grave (同穴). Scholars like Kim Chongjik (佔畢齋) and Yu Hoin (兪濡溪) all wrote poems (題詠) about it. "Gazing Towards Tonggyŏng" (望東京) is the name of her song.) <65>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Cha Sangch'an, written for the magazine *Kaebyŏk* in 1923, chronicles a journey undertaken with Kim Kijŏn from Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) to Hamyang in Kyŏngsangnam Province as part of a "Basic Survey of Korean Culture." The author details their train and automobile travel, vividly describing landscapes, local customs (like the loud Yŏngnam dialect and market scenes), food (Chinju *pibimbap*), and historical sites (Chinju Fortress, Ch'ŏksŏngnu, Mun Ikchŏm's cotton cultivation site, King Kuhyŏng's tomb). Cha expresses deep emotional responses to historical tragedies, particularly at Chinju Fortress and Sagŭn Station, and reflects on social inequalities, contrasting the luxurious homes of the rural wealthy with the dire poverty of peasants, which he attributes partly to capitalism and classism. He encounters various local figures, including T'iandaojiao members, youth association leaders, officials, scholars, and ordinary people, noting both their positive traits (simplicity, diligence in model villages) and negative ones (passivity of laborers facing abuse, stinginess of the rich).

From a colonial history perspective, the article is revealing in its depiction of social conditions in southern Korea. Cha's observations highlight the stark economic disparities, the perceived docility and powerlessness of laborers (which he links to historical oppression and potentially contemporary colonial pressures via figures like police assistants), and the exploitative presence of Japanese moneylenders in rural areas like Tŏksan. The mention of the University Practice Forest in Tŏksan taking away local livelihoods points to resource exploitation under colonial administration. The constant surveillance by a plainclothes detective (査公) underscores the atmosphere of suspicion and control. Historians might find Cha's candid commentary on social injustice, his nationalist sentiments evoked by historical sites, and his interactions with various strata of Korean society under colonial rule particularly interesting.

						車相瓚
우리 開闢社에서 癸亥年 新事業으로 계획하던 「朝鮮文化의 基本調査」는 이제 이것을 실현하게 되얏다. 면저 手를 慶南에 着하야 그 實況을 조사할새 本社主幹 金起廛와 나는 그 조사원으로 被選되얏다. 우리 일행은 2월 1일에 京城을 꼭 떠나 목적지로 향하랴고 작정하얏스나 多端한 浮世의 생활은 자연 분망하야 2일 오후에야 비로소 출발하게 되얏다. 나는 오후 6시에 行裝을 수습하고 腕車上의 몸이 되야 京城驛으로 往하얏다. 예비성 만흔 小春형과 우리 일행을 전송하랴는 春坡, 一然 及 少年會員 몃 분이 벌서 정차장구내에 와서 나오기를 고대하고 잇고, 金海학생으로 京城에 유학중인 崔鳳守양도 우리를 동반하야 歸省, 治療(其 時 崔孃 有身羔)하러 가랴고 또 와서 잇다. 우리 두 사람은 二等乘車券을 가젓스나 崔양과 동반하기 중에도 南鮮의 노동자는 참 비참하고 가련하다. 엇지하면 이러한 동포를 광명의 길로 인도할고하며 두 사람이 무한의 개탄을 하얏다. 이로부터 車를 다시 몰아 晋州地境에 이르니 길은 점점 평탄하나 解凍된 흙이 牛馬車 등에 유린되야 마치 수렁논가티 되얏다. 靑魚바리를 실고가는 荷車는 우리 탄 자동차를 만나 피하랴고 하다가 길이 하도 질어서 마참내 피하지를 못하고 자동차와 충돌되는 바람에 나의 「도랑크」는 왓삭 바서젓다. 운전수는 차를 멈치고 뒤여나린다. 나는 아까 광경으로 보앗기로 운전수가 또 荷車軍을 따릴가 하고 염려하야, 여보 도랑크는 내것이라 破傷이 되야도 나의 손해가 될 뿐이니 아모 말하지 말나하고 내가 가로마터 荷車軍을 불너가지고 戱弄的으로 破傷된 물건의 손해를 무러내라 하 얏다. 그 荷車軍은 아모 말 업시 손해를 물어달나시면 무어들이지요 하고 그 손해액을 뭇는다. 나는 15원 가량이라한즉 荷車軍은 말하기를 저의 가진 靑魚는 7원 가격 밧게 못 되온즉 집에 가기 전에는 變通할 수가 업다 한다. 나는 우스면서 손해는 그만두고 이 다음에는 특별 주의하야 다른 사람에게 손해를 끼치거나 욕을 당하지 말게 하라하고 그대로 돌여보냇다. 그 사람들은 부주의하는 일이 만치만은 淳實하기는 매우 淳實하다. 다시 차를 재촉하야 晋州沃野를 밟아보고 南江橋를 건너 晋州城內로 들어갓다. 정류장에 이르니 아는 사람은 하나도 업고 여러 가지가 다 생소할 뿐이다. 부득이 운전수에게 여관을 하나 안내하라고 청한즉 운전수는 大安洞 中央旅館을 指示한다. 우리는 行具를 인부에게 직히고 여관으로 가다가 途中에서 마츰 우리를 영접하랴고 오는 當地 天道敎 宗法師 申鏞九씨와 同靑年會長 朴台弘씨를 만낫다. 피차에 반가움을 익이지 못하야 따뜻한 손을 잡고 그간 隔阻된 말과 여러가지 情話를 하면서 여관으로 들어갓다. 조곰 잇다가 晋州支社 總務 朴鳳儀씨가 또한 왓다. 主客이 마주 안저 한참 이약이를 하다가 시가지를 구경하랴고 여관을 떠나 나섯다. 諸氏의 안내로 엇더한 음식집에 들어가서 晋州의 명물인 비빔밥 한 그릇식을 잔득 먹고 다시 路를 轉하야 當地 天道敎 大敎區, 靑年會, 勞働共濟會를 방문한 후 飛鳳山下로 건너가서 飛鳳潭에 이르니 우리 4,5인 동지의 交情과 深淺을 다투는 千尺의 潭水는 春波가 方生하야 洋洋浩浩＜55＞ 흐른는데 春光을 사랑하는 南國의 佳人들은 삼삼오오 隊를 지여 한가히 빨내한다. 우리는 潭上에서 暫時間 莊叟의 濠上之樂을 취하다가 다시 步를 變하야  시가지로 향하얏다. 晋州俗語에 「一匙見三蠅이오 三步逢一妓」라는 말을 들엇더니 기후관계로 파리는 볼 수 업스나 기생은 과연 만은 모양이다. 골목마다 紅粧羅裙이 오락가락하야 行客의 眼을 현란케 한다. 쳔쳔이 緩步하야 南江연안으로 향하니 論介娘의 萬古芳名을 자랑하는 義娘岩은 江畔에 嵬然이 홀로 섯고 3壯士 金千鎰, 崔慶會, 黃進의 千載忠魂을 吊喪하는 長江水는 鳴咽이 흐른다. 矗石樓에 올나가서 晋州城內를 발아보니 방방곡곡이 다 眼中에 잇다. 河崙矗石樓記에 「飛鳳山이 北에 止하고 望晋이 南에 拱하며, 菁川이 西에 繞하고 長江이 其間에 流하며 東西諸山이 宛轉四環」이라 한 것은 참 晋州城의 지형을 畵한 것이라. 晋州는 慶南의 중심지오 도청소재지다. 우리 조선민족이 가장 恨이 만코, 눈물이 만코 피 만이 흘닌 곳이다. 宣廟龍蛇亂陷城할 시에 우리 晋州 동포의 祖先 60,000여명이 일시에 순절한 곳이다. 아모리 剛腸鐵肝의 人이라도 矗石樓에 오르면 滿衿의 淚를 뿌리지 안이치 못할 곳이다. 나는 난간에 의지하야 千古에 壯絶悲絶한 3壯士의 立節時 노래 「矗石樓中三壯士, 一盃笑指長江水. 長江之水滔滔兮, 波不窮兮魂不死」를 부르다가 우연히 申維翰씨의 「晋陽城外水東流, 叢竹芳蘭綠暎州」 운운의 韻을 次하야 一首의 詩를 作하얏다.
천千              천              千尺朱欄枕碧流, 登臨怳若上瀛洲. 山河百戰餘空郭, 風雨重經有此樓.
江水無窮壯士恨, 岩花尙帶美人愁. 居民不識當年事, 但醉笙歌月夜遊.
우리 일행은 다시 矗石樓를 나려 陳列館을 구경한 후 西으로 廻하야 西將臺, 北將臺, 忠烈祠, 護國寺를 보고 도청 後를 經하야 여관으로 歸하얏다. 몸이 피곤하야 좀 누엇더니 夕飯이 벌서 나옴으로 우리를 안내하던 여러분은 집으로 돌아가고 나와 小春과 두 사람만 밥을 먹게 되얏다. 밤에는 晋州의 有志 諸氏가 來訪하야 여러가지 이약기를 하다가 11시경에 各 歸하고 우리도 역시 취침하얏다.
4일, 晴. 本日은 일요일인 고로 11시에 敎區에 가서 侍日禮式을 보왓다. 연령은 장년이지만 소년과는 특별이 조하하야 소년에 관한 말을 하면 해가고 날새는 줄을 모르는 小春은 또다시 當地 天道敎少年會員에게 말을 하게 되얏다. 그럭저럭 오후 2시나 되야 말을 畢하고 떠나서 또 비빔밥으로 午腸을 充하고 다시 시가지구경을 하고 여관으로 돌아왓다. 우리 일행을 사랑하는 晋州의 人士들은＜56＞ 일의 분망함을 不顧하고 밤에 또다시 차저와서 인정풍속과 역사, 전설에 관한 여러가지 유익한 이약기을 하야주고 심야에 歸하얏다.
5일(월요), 晴. 오전 11시경에 나는 東亞日報支局 記者 尹炳殷씨의 안내로 道廳에 가서 각종의 기사자료를 어더가지고 와서 점심을 먹고 朴鳳儀씨와 다시 民間有志를 방문하얏다. 오후 7시 반에 晉州의 有志 諸氏는 우리 두 사람을 京城館으로 招하야 만찬회를 開함으로 우리는 거긔에 가서 따듯한 사랑과 질거운 우숨 쇽에서 滿盤의 盛饌을 飽喫하고 11시경에 여관으로 歸하얏다.
6일(화요), 晴. 朝飯을 먹은 후에 小春과 나는 두 隊로 논아 當地 諸氏의 안내로 민간측 諸 狀況을 조사하얏다.
7일(수요), 晴. 우리는 晉州의 일을 대개 畢하얏슴으로 今日에는 他郡으로 가랴고 하얏스나 支社의 일이 미흡한 것이 잇서 또 다시 못떠낫다. 오날은 마참 晉州의 장날인 고로 우리는 장구경을 하랴고 시장에 나갓다. 晉州의 시장은 참 번창하다. 장소도 널거니와 출품된 물건도 만타. 농산, 수산, 공업품, 축산 기타 각종의 잡화, 과실, 식료품 등이 업는 것이 업다. 北鮮地方 장시에서는 보지도 못하던 小鼓, 腰鼓, 錚, 팽쉬, 부시돌, 黃찍은 가리셕약까지 다 잇다. 더구나 음력 歲末이 임박한 고로 각지의 행상들도 다  들어오고 各日興成을 하랴는 村農民들도 모도 와서 무려 수만여이 와글와글 물끌틋 한다. 西鮮地方의 인민이 農夫歌를 하던지 소설을 보와도 愁心歌調로 하드시 南鮮의 인민은 물건사라고 외는 소리까지도 모다 六字拍이 調가 안이면 短歌調이다. 참 壯觀이오 奇觀이다. 우리 두 사람은 정신업시 이곳저곳을 단이며 무슨 물건이나 살 듯시 가격도 물어보고 구경도 하얏다. 한 시간이나 돌아단이다가 敎區로 가니 敎區에서는 우리에게 떡국으로 점심을 준다. 우리는 맛잇게 먹고 朴台弘, 金義鎭 兩氏와 少年會 간부 몃 분을 동반하야 晋州에서 제일 名區인 南江下流 赤壁 구경을 하러갓다. 往路에 또한 장시에 들어가 文魚, 乾柿, 白糖 등을 사가지고 桃花勝地로 유명한 玉峯里를 지나 南江을 順流하야 赤壁에 다다르니 과연 岩石도 기괴하고 景槪가 絶勝하다. 작년 秋 7월 旣望에 假者 蘇東坡가 만이 생겨 일반의 비평까지 듯게 된 것도 無理의 事가 안이다. 우리는 문장이 안이닛가 蘇東坡의 赤壁賦 갓튼 美文은 짓지 못하지만은 美文 대신에 文魚나 먹자하고 사가지고 간 文魚를 논아 먹으며 石逕斜路로 나려가니 岩壁上에 抝�載現의 題名한 것이 두렷이 뵈인다. 이것은 前日에 觀察使로 別般 惡政을 다하고 인민의 재산을 橫奪하야 晋州의 흙을 세 치까지 먹엇다는 동요를＜57＞ 읏던 皇族의 李載現이다. 그가 觀察使로 한참 잘 호강할 따에는 晋州邑에서 東으로 20여리 되는 靑谷寺를 날마다 가서 妓樂으로 질탕이 놀다가 밤중에 돌아올 때면 沿路人民으로 炬火(밤바라기)를 들게 하야 20여리가 每夜 不夜城을 이르럿고 南江에 船遊를 하면 수백의 船雙을 幾日式 執留하야 全江을 連環함으로 江水가 흐르지 못한 때가 잇섯다. 그뿐 안이라 各郡의 名妓를 선택하야 觀察府에 직속케 하고 京鄕의 蕩子浪輩를 모도 모와 晝夜遊逸함으로 수풀에 독갑이 끌틋시 각지 기생들이 작구 모와들어 본래 20여명에 불과하던 妓가 1년내에 700여명에 달하얏섯다. 지금까지 晋州에 기생이 만은 것은 全혀 그 李씨의 遺德이라 한다. 과연 그는 무죄한 농민의 고혈을 얼마나 짜먹엇스며 국가에 죄악은 얼마나 지엇는가. 惡을 積한 者는 天이 禍로써 報한다고 그가 지금에 衣食거리가 업서 아우의 집에서 신세를 지우며 다 해진 쇠똥벙거지에 맛지 안는 짝짝이 고무신짝을 끌고 風病마진 입살을 실룩~하고 京城市街로 돌아단니는 것을 보면 참 天意가 무심치 안이하다. 못된 일을 다하고도 몃 백년이나 遺臭를 전하럇던지 그의 題名한 것을 보면 참 가소롭고 가련하다. 우리는 이런 말 저런 말하는 중에 석양은 벌서 玉峯山에 반사되고 집을 찻는 가마구무리는 飛鳳山下로 날어든다. 우리는 그만 거름을 돌여 삼삼오오로 흐터저가는 場軍과 압서거니 뒤서거니 하고 성내로 돌아왓다. 참 오날은 유쾌하게 잘 놀앗다. 夕飯을 먹고 우리를 방문하러 온 여러 손과 明日에 출발할 의논도 하고  다른 이약기를 하다가 손님이 간 후에 몸이 곤하야 즉시 잣다.
