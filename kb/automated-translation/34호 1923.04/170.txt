Basic Survey of Korean Culture - Partial Publication
First, let us speak about the general situation of Kyŏngnam (South Kyŏngsang Province)

Map of South Kyŏngsang Province [Implied, not shown in text]

1. Location and Topography. This province is located at the southeastern tip of the Korean peninsula. To the west, it borders North and South Chŏlla Provinces, to the north, it adjoins North Kyŏngsang Province, and to the east and south, it faces the sea. Mountain ranges run crisscross within its territory, resulting in varied topography, but generally, the northwest is elevated, and the southeast is low-lying. The Naktong River flows through the center of the province, and the plains along its basin have fertile soil, making agricultural production abundant. The southern coastline is indented and winding, with numerous bays and harbors. Many islands are scattered like stars in the sea, suitable for fish habitats, making the fishing industry the province's major industry after agriculture.

2. History. This province was the territory of the ancient Pyŏnhan confederacy. Later, the Five Kaya states divided and occupied it until they were destroyed by Silla. After Koryŏ unified the territory, in the 14th year of King Sŏngjong, the area under Kimju jurisdiction was called Yŏngdong-do, and the area under Chinju jurisdiction was called Sannam-do. In the first year of King Myŏngjong, it was renamed Chinhapchu-do. In the first year of King Ch’ungsuk<3>, it was combined and called Kyŏngsang-do. The Yi Dynasty (Chosŏn) continued this name. In the 14th year of King Chungjong, it was divided into Left and Right provinces, using the Naktong River as the boundary, but this was soon abolished. In the 25th year of King Sŏnjo, it was again divided into Left and Right provinces, reunified the following year, divided again in the 28th year, and reunified again the following year. A military headquarters (營) was established in Taegu (initially established in Sŏngju). It was moved in the 31st year, moved back to Taegu four years later. In the 32nd year of Emperor Kojong, it was divided into North and South provinces, with the provincial office (道廳) placed in Chinju, where it remains to this day.

3. Administrative Districts, Households, and Population. The area of this province is 790.783 square *ri* (方里). This is divided into 2 *pu* (府 - urban prefectures), 19 *kun* (郡 - counties), and 257 *myŏn* (面 - townships). The average area of a county is 37.909 square *ri*, and the average area of a township is 3.104 square *ri*. The households number 339,055 Korean, 17,308 Japanese, and 204 foreign, totaling 356,567 households. The population is 1,738,049 Koreans, 69,750 Japanese, and 765 foreigners, totaling 1,808,561 people. Per square *ri*, the ratio is 447 households and 2,266.9 people, making the population density the highest among all provinces. (Surveyed end of December, Taishō 10 [1921])

4. Climate. Korea's climate is continental, so the difference between heat and cold is distinct. However, the climate of this province, except for the northwestern mountainous region, is moderated by the ocean and is mild compared to the rest of the peninsula, with relatively small temperature differences. The annual average temperature ranges from a high of 32 degrees Celsius to a low of minus 5.3 degrees Celsius. Furthermore, sunny days are frequent, and the air is dry, making it most suitable for residence.

Overview of Religion in this Province
Kyŏngnam was originally a center (淵藪) of Confucianism, so it goes without saying that there are many believers, i.e., scholars. However, adhering rigidly to old customs and falling behind the times, it faces rejection from the general public and is gradually declining. Buddhism temporarily flourished during the Silla and Koryŏ periods, and even today, many temples built during that time are truly astonishing in their grandeur and magnificence. But as a result of the Yi Dynasty's policy of promoting Confucianism and suppressing Buddhism (崇儒斥佛), it declined severely and remains stagnant to this day. Recently, religions founded in Korea such as Ch’ŏndogyo (天道敎), Taejonggyo (大宗敎), T’aeŭlgyo (太乙敎) (number of followers for the latter two unknown), and Christianity introduced from abroad have become quite prevalent. Additionally, many Japanese residents adhere to Shinto (神道). The overview is as follows.

Agriculture, Fisheries, Industry - The Three Major Industries of this Province
The Three Major Industries of this Province
□ Agriculture: 15 million won to 73 million won
□ Industry: 6 million won to 21 million won

Among the provinces of Chosŏn, this province, like South Chŏlla Province, has a mild climate suitable for cultivating temperate plants in agriculture and forestry. Particularly, the coastline extends over 1,200 *ri* (海里 - nautical miles?), making the production of fish and algae abundant. Furthermore, historically and geographically, it has a close relationship with Japan, and the industrial significance arising from maritime transport is especially great. Now, we wish to introduce the industrial situation by sector to draw the attention of our brethren in Kyŏngnam and the general compatriots.

Agriculture
Agriculture in this province, along with fisheries, constitutes the mainstay of industry. As of the end of Taishō 10 (1921), there were over 160,257 *chŏngbo* (町步) of paddy fields (沓) and over 116,164 *chŏngbo* of dry fields (田), totaling over 276,322 *chŏngbo*. The number of farming households was 277,163, with a population of 1,466,026, accounting for about 80% of the total households. The arable land area per farming household is 5 *tan* 8 *myo* (反畝) of paddy field and 4 *tan* 2 *myo* of dry field, equivalent to 1 *chŏngbo* in total.
Among agricultural products, rice, barley, and beans are famous. The average annual production of rice reaches 1.7 million *sŏk* (石), barley also 1.67 million *sŏk*, and soybeans about 400,000 *sŏk*. Of these, about 400,000 *sŏk* of rice and about 100,000 *sŏk* of soybeans are exported annually.
Among special agricultural products, upland cotton (陸地綿), ramie (苧麻), paper mulberry (楮), and rush grass (莞草) are renowned for both quality and quantity. Among fruits, persimmons (柿) and apples (苹果) are also notable.
This province has numerous islands and has been famous for livestock farming since ancient times. The fine horses of Yokchido (欲知島), presented by Kyŏn Hwŏn (甄萱) to Wang Kŏn Taejo (王建太祖), are specially recorded in history, but they are now gone. There is only the raising of cattle, pigs, sheep, etc., by farming households.<14>

Forestry
The total forest area in this province is about 881,000 *chŏngbo*. Of this, about 160,000 *chŏngbo* has been established forest for some time, about 480,000 *chŏngbo* is land with young trees, and the remaining 210,000 *chŏngbo* is unforested land. The provincial authorities have endeavored in afforestation for over ten years, and by Taishō 10 (1921), more than a quarter of the unforested land had been planted. For reference, the actual state of afforestation is shown below:

1. Provincial Forests (道有林): According to regulations, up to about 500 *chŏngbo* of national forest land can be transferred. This province has received transfers exceeding this limit and has already completed afforestation. The situation is as follows. [Table implied]

2. Township Forests (面有林): To cultivate financial resources for the townships, each township is allowed to manage 50 to 300 *chŏngbo* of township forest. The land allocated for this includes Type 1 unnecessary national forest land transferred or leased, village forests within the township consolidated into the township forest, and privately owned forest land requiring special facilities like erosion control works, which were transferred to the township. The township forests in this province already amount to 28,024 *chŏngbo* (average over 100 *chŏngbo* per township).

3. School Forests (學校林): Schools of common level are permitted transfers of up to 20 *chŏngbo* of national forest land, and secondary level schools up to 40 *chŏngbo*. Based on this, 66 schools have established school forests, covering a forest area of 760 *chŏngbo*.

4. National Leased Forests (國有貸付林): Of the approximately 67,000 *chŏngbo* of Type 1 unnecessary national forest land in this province, over 60,200 *chŏngbo* has already been leased. Excluding the portions mentioned in (1), (2), and (3) above, the rest is leased to organizations and individuals aiming for forest management. Furthermore, as of the end of Taishō 10 (1921), about 6,500 *chŏngbo* of national forest land remained undisposed, but it is said that this too is scheduled to be leased sooner or later.

How the bald mountains (禿山) of Kyŏngsang Province shock people passing through on the Kyŏngbu Line is generally acknowledged, but now, young trees are seen sprouting almost all over the mountain faces, truly giving them a new look. The authorities often boast about the good results of afforestation. Furthermore, the provincial authorities formulated a 20-year afforestation plan in Taishō 8 (1919), aiming to complete afforestation of the entire province by Taishō 27 (1938), and are proceeding with it today. The forest survey, ongoing since Taishō 6 (1917), is set to be completed by Taishō 13 (1924), clarifying forest ownership rights. Concurrently, starting with the counties where this assessment is finished, a basic forest survey will be gradually conducted to ascertain the supply and demand relationship for timber and firewood, and along with designating conservation forests (保安林), a concrete plan <15> for promoting afforestation is planned.

Fisheries
Among the 2 *pu* and 19 *kun* of this province, 2 *pu* and 9 *kun* border the sea, meaning more than half of the administrative area is deeply related to fisheries. The number of affiliated islands is 278, and the coastline length is 2,310 *ri* for the mainland and 3,390 *ri* for the islands, totaling 5,700 *ri*. The coast is indented and winding, with capes, bays, and coves linked together. Numerous islands are scattered like Go stones. The water depth is generally around 50 *chim* (尋 - fathoms?). Situated at the confluence of cold and warm currents, various aquatic species are abundant, including warm-current fish like sardines (鰮), mackerel (鯖), and Spanish mackerel (鰆), and cold-current fish like cod (鱈) and herring (鰊). The climate is particularly mild, suitable for year-round operations.
Sardines, mackerel, and cod are referred to as the province's three major fisheries. Among them, the sardine fishery holds the top position, with an annual production value of around 4 million won, accounting for about one-third of the province's total catch value. Its success or failure has a truly significant impact on the province's economy. The mackerel fishery, due to the rise of fixed net fishing methods in recent years, extended fishing seasons, and development of transportation infrastructure, has an annual production value of 2.5 to 3 million won, with much exported fresh to Japan. The cod fishery mainly belongs to Korean fishermen, with an annual production value of 1 to 1.5 million won.

Look at the Murai Farm in Chinsu
When one speaks of Japanese farms in Korea, one immediately thinks of the Tōyō Takushoku (東洋拓殖), operating with a huge capital of 50 million won, followed by the Chōsen Kōgyō (朝鮮興業) and Mori Farm (森農場) in Hwangju, Tōsan Farm (東山農場) in Suwŏn, Ukon Farm (右近農場) in Mokp’o, Murai Farm (村井農場) in Ch’angwŏn, Kumamoto Farm (態本農場?) in Chŏngŭp, Ishikawa Prefecture Agricultural Stock Company (石川縣農業株式) in Kimje, Hosokawa Farm (細川農場) in Iksan, Saitō Farm (齊藤農場) in Chinnamp’o, and Nichikai Kōgyō Stock Company Branch Office (日海興業株式出張所) in Sariwŏn. Looking at the recent situation of one of these, the Murai Farm in Chinsu, Ch’angwŏn County: the farm was established in January, Meiji 41 (1908), engaging in both ordinary farming and fruit cultivation. The land it occupies spans from Ch’angwŏn to part of Kimhae, covering an area of about 3,000 *chŏngbo*. The tenant farmers associated with it are 100 Japanese households and 1,300 Korean households. The total harvest the year before last was 27,000 *sŏk* of rice paddy, 1,700 *sŏk* of barley, and 1,000 *sŏk* of soybeans. Its owned arable land consists of 1,500 *chŏngbo* of paddy fields, 500 *chŏngbo* of dry fields, and 600 *chŏngbo* of other miscellaneous land. Additionally, there is some amount of forest land. Indeed, they are doing quite well for themselves.

Industry
The industry of this province was traditionally a subsidiary occupation for farming households and not much to look at. In recent years, due to enthusiastic investment by Japanese, large-scale enterprises such as the Chōsen Bōseki Stock Company (朝鮮紡績株式) in Pusan<16>, the Nippon Kōshitsu Tōki Stock Company Branch (日本硬質陶器株式支店) in Mokto (牧島), and the Chōsen Sen'i Kōgyō Stock Company (朝鮮纖維工業株式) in Kup’o (龜浦) have emerged.
Taking a look at the province's industrial production value for a recent one-year period, the total production value was over 21,769,000 won. Among this, textiles accounted for over 1,992,000 won, paper and paper products for over 958,000 won, lacquerware (漆器) for over 54,000 won, and straw mats (莚席) for over 455,000 won, these being the main items. Besides these, the brewing industry (釀造業) managed by Japanese is particularly prosperous.
The weaving industry (機業): The seven counties of Hapch’ŏn, Kŏch’ang, Hamyang, Sanch’ŏng, Namhae, Sach’ŏn, and Kosŏng have long been famous production centers for hemp cloth (麻布) or ramie cloth (苧布). In Taishō 10 (1921), they produced a large amount of 892,000 *p’il* (反 - bolts) of fabric, valued at over 1,956,000 won.
The paper industry (製紙業) is also prominent in this province, with an annual production value reaching 400,000 to 600,000 won. Each year, 150,000 to 350,000 *kŭn* (斤 - catties) of paper are exported from Korea to China, and over 80% of this is produced in this province.
The lacquerware of T’ongyŏng (統營) has been famous since ancient times, but in the interim, a trend of crude and excessive production occurred, greatly damaging its reputation. The annual production value is calculated at about 50,000 won.

Commerce
Geographically, this province is close to Japan, resulting in frequent maritime traffic. The three ports of Pusan, Masan, and Chinhae typically handle about 25% of the total export-import trade volume of all of Korea. Among them, Pusan port serves as the gateway to Korea. Major import items include cotton piece goods (金巾類), petroleum, coal, sugar, timber planks, cotton textiles, paper products, ropes and straw bags (繩叺). Major export items include rice, soybeans, fish, live cattle, cocoons, fertilizer, raw hides, raw cotton, and seaweed.
There are 122 regular markets (시장) held every five days within the province, among which Hadong Market and Chinju Market are well-known. There are also 16 special markets, such as fish markets, belonging to individual or company management, and 1 exchange (取引所), totaling 139 locations.<17>

Min Yŏngjik (閔泳直), a Man of Diligence in Sanch’ŏng Porcelain Research
Mr. Min was originally a technician (技手) at the Land Survey Bureau (土地調査局). During his time in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), he visited the Central Laboratory (中央試驗所), observed porcelain manufacturing and its raw materials, and developed a boundless interest. After returning home, he inspected various regions to discover raw material sites for porcelain himself. He found a raw material site of about 3 square *ri* near his home (currently T’ŭk-ri, Kŭmsŏ-myŏn, Sanch’ŏng County). Initially, utilizing existing pottery workshops, he experimented diligently for about five years, obtaining over 2,000 test pieces through 24 trials. As the results were particularly good, he took them to the Central Laboratory in Kyŏngsŏng for evaluation. The verdict was that the raw material was the best among those found in Korea and also abundant. After the Central Laboratory conducted an on-site inspection, Mr. Min requested support for this manufacturing business from the Provincial Office. The Provincial Office, as part of its local industry development policy, established a Ceramics Cooperative (窯業組合) at the Sanch’ŏng County Office, dispatched a specialized technician, and provided a subsidy of about 7,000 won to install a 12-chamber climbing kiln (登窯). Although 7 chambers were already completed, construction has now stopped due to lack of capital. Mr. Min exhausted his entire family fortune on this porcelain manufacturing research, sacrificing about 5,000 won. Later, together with supporters (有志), he initiated the Sanch’ŏng Porcelain Stock Company (山淸磁器株式會社), but due to the poor economic climate, this also failed to materialize. As a result of agonizing day and night, he fell ill, even becoming deaf, and is still sick today. According to Mr. Min, the kaolin clay (高嶺土), the raw material for Sanch’ŏng porcelain, covers about three or four square *ri*. Not only is the quantity the largest in Korea, but its quality is also not inferior to the famous Hadong Ch’ŏngsong product. Other materials like silica stone (硅石), feldspar (長石), and clay (粘土) are also abundant. Fireclay (耐火土), essential for porcelain manufacturing, is plentiful in Hamgyŏng Province, but transportation is inconvenient, so it is currently imported from Japan at a high cost of about 5 won per *kwe* (簣 - basket/container?). However, this region also has abundant fireclay, reducing this difficulty. Also, with Chirisan nearby, fuel is plentiful. Thus, from any perspective, the Sanch’ŏng porcelain manufacturing industry is not only promising for the future, but also, a prime section of about 40,000 *p’yŏng*, which had been licensed by the Tōkyō Jiki Stock Company (東京磁器株式會社), has now been relinquished due to circumstances. Therefore, if any capitalist among us Koreans were to pay attention to this, there would be great benefits for the future industrial world, he says.<19>

Endangered Eggs Under a Fallen Nest - The Decline of Korean Industry in Kyŏngnam
How could there be reason for endangered eggs under a fallen nest (覆巢危卵)? The land of Kyŏngnam has already become a hunting ground for outsiders. Moreover, Kyŏngnam, because (1) the climate is mild and good for living, (2) the land is fertile and harbors are good, making products abundant, (3) it is close to Japan, making transportation convenient, and (4) the people's hearts are simple, making it easy for foreign races to approach, has attracted foreign labor more severely than any other province in Korea.
Among the industries of this province, the prominent ones currently are agriculture, industry, and fisheries, and the promising one for the future is forestry. However, witnessing how feeble Korean efforts are and how vigorous Japanese efforts are in managing these four major industries, our hearts cannot help but feel a renewed chill.

First, looking at agriculture:
Even according to the statistics of Taishō 9 (1920), among the total 277,681 farming households, Japanese farming households numbered 1,932, and Chinese farming households numbered 6. Looking at the numbers, it doesn't seem particularly significant. However, among Korean farming households, about 80% are tenant farmers (小作農戶) who have no choice. It goes without saying that they cannot exert any conscious effort towards farm management. Even owner-farmers (自作農戶) or large landowners mostly practice conventional, stopgap farming (姑息的 營農). In contrast, those Japanese farmers not only exert much conscious effort themselves but also generally have the constant support of individuals or society behind them. Compared to Koreans, they are truly advancing today with the force of one against a hundred.
The land management of the aforementioned Tōyō Takushoku Company (東拓會社), for example, concentrates its efforts heavily in this province. The Tōtaku Pusan Branch (東拓釜山支店), established in Pusan's Sōsei-chō Gochōme (相生町五丁目 - Aioi-chō 5-chome?), currently employs 42 staff members and is busy with land management, immigration projects, and procuring colonization funds (拓植資金) in Kyŏngnam. The branch's current land holdings span 2 *pu* and 18 *kun*, reaching a total area of over 8,656 *chŏngbo*. The amount of colonization funds provided <20> also exceeds 3,760,000 won, and the prosperity of its business shows marked growth year after year, it is said.

Besides the management of such companies, there are quite a few individual farm managements of considerable scale. For example, the Murai Farm (村井農場) in Chinyŏng (進永), Ch’angwŏn County of this province, started its business way back in Meiji 41 (1908), and by around Taishō 4 (1915), it was already managing over 2,850 *chŏngbo* of land, continuing to this day. Furthermore, the southern coast of this province has deep tidal influence, and there is much state-owned unreclaimed tidal flat land (國有未墾地의 干潟地). The sharp-eyed and well-funded Japanese build dikes (防築) to create new paddy and dry fields, receiving leases or grants. During my recent visit to the southern coast, I often saw commemorative steles erected praising these dike-building Japanese along the roadsides, sufficient to gauge their painstaking efforts in land management.
Despite the vigorous efforts of the Japanese in land management, all aspects related to this on the Korean side are conspicuously silent. Many tenant farmers struggle for daily meals and wander abroad as an annual event. The few landowners are solely preoccupied with how to extract even one more *sŏk* of rent, having no other concerns, seemingly awaiting a day of mutual collapse between rich and poor, which is truly lamentable beyond words.
That's roughly the situation in agriculture.

Now, let's glance at the fisheries sector.<21>

How is it?
Fishery management differs greatly in nature from land management. Simply catching the natural fish resources makes them one's property. Compared to agriculture, industry, or any other business, it yields greater returns with less effort. The approximately 200,000 brethren residing on the Korean peninsula, surrounded by sea on three sides, centering on the 43,950 *ri* coastline (combining mainland and islands), have actually lived a more comfortable life than any other brethren. The various marine products derived from it have been transported domestically and abroad, supplying the delicacies of the sea (海錯).
Climbing even to the peak of Paektu Mountain (白頭山), searching even the streams of the Yalu River (鴨綠江), trying to scoop up even fallen grains of rice – this is the economic management policy of Japan and the Japanese towards Korea. How could their black hands neglect such a field as fisheries? Do not be surprised. Their hands and feet have already encircled the coastline like a spider's web. Now, let's briefly describe the power dynamics between Japanese and Koreans regarding fisheries in this province.
At the end of Taishō 10 (1921), the total number of fishery operators residing in the province was 15,019 households, with 13,151 being Korean and 1,868 Japanese. In terms of households and population count, the Korean side always wins overwhelmingly. But what use is that? Just as the entire Korean nation, with a population of over 17 million, lost all political and economic real power to a mere 300,000 Japanese (residing within Korea), the over 13,000 native fishing households of Kyŏngnam are trampled by just 1,800 foreign fishermen. That is, looking at the catch value for Taishō 10 (1921), out of a total value of 17.53 million won, the Japanese catch value reached 12.29 million won, while the Korean catch was merely 5.24 million won. Thus, among the province's three major fisheries – sardines, mackerel, and cod – only in the cod (大口魚 - large-mouth fish?) catch do Koreans hold the main role. The catch of sardines and mackerel, with annual productions reaching 3 million and 1 million won respectively, largely falls into Japanese hands. Furthermore, looking at marine products, the total product value in Taishō 10 (1921) was 10.59 million won, of which the Japanese share reached 6.63 million won, again showing superiority. Moreover, looking at aquaculture (魚類養殖業), there is nothing noteworthy under Korean management, while Japanese endeavors like the eel farming (鰻養殖) by Nippon Shokusan Stock Company (日本殖産株式) manage an aquaculture farm of about 50,000 *p'yŏng* on the banks of the Naktong River.

The province's industry, as briefly mentioned in a separate section, still hasn't escaped the primitive handicraft era for traditional industries. Meanwhile, Japanese-managed enterprises like the Chōsen Bōseki in Pusan and the Kōshitsu Tōki Company in Mokto are newly emerging today. The Japanese-run brewing industry centered in Pusan is also famous in this province.<25>

Commerce is centered around Pusan.
For a time around Taishō 8-9 (1919-1920), commercial institutions of individuals or companies like Paeksan Trading (白山貿易) and Chuil Company (周一商會) emerged rapidly, presenting a prosperous scene attracting many customers (萬客其來). However, since the summer of Taishō 9 (1920), along with the financial crunch, everything collapsed, and now there is nothing much to see. Although on a small scale, the Korean commercial sector in Masan seems to be achieving rather steady development.
In short, just as the Korean commercial sector in Kyŏngsŏng revolves around the Japanese in Nihyŏn (泥峴 - mud hill?), the Korean commercial sector in Kyŏngnam also revolves around the Japanese in Pusan. They are like the warp of a net (網) or the collar of a garment (領). For example, individuals like Katori Gentarō (香推源太郞?), Hakama Bōtarō (迫問房太郞), and Ōike Chūsuke (大池忠助) residing in Pusan are all millionaires (*paengman changja* - 百萬의 長者), and the economic power they possess is not necessarily confined to the Kyŏngnam region alone.

At Japan's Farmers' Convention (農民大會),
an agenda for land nationalization was recently passed, and they decided to campaign for its implementation. This was because if it happened, it would bring equal welfare to the general populace. However, in present-day Korea, whether it's fields, paddies, or forest land, if it becomes state-owned (國有), 99% of it ends up being utilized, no, granted (讓受), exclusively to the Japanese side. There are, of course, various reasons for this. Issues of capital and technology certainly exist. But among them, the unique reason is that it is done as a matter of national policy from the perspective of Japan and the Japanese. Looking primarily at the state-owned unreclaimed land (國有未墾地) in this province, by the end of Taishō 9 (1920), over 3,000 *chŏngbo* of wilderness, grassland, wasteland, tidal flats, and marshland had been leased. This leased land is used for paddy fields, dry fields, afforestation, orchards, forest gardens, and other miscellaneous purposes, with over 2,600 *chŏngbo* reclaimed as paddy fields. Whether paddy fields, dry fields, or horticultural plots, these leased areas were naturally granted exclusively to Japanese. Since this phenomenon has continued rapidly after the end of Taishō 9 (1920), the power gained by the Japanese through the reclamation of state-owned unreclaimed land must be truly immense.
Also, looking at forest land: the total forest area in this province as of Taishō 10 (1921) was over 881,000 *chŏngbo*. By the same year, over 108,000 *chŏngbo* had been leased. Thus, it must be assumed that much of this has also entered the sphere of influence of the Japanese side. Given the particular negligence of the Korean side regarding forestry, the power of foreigners over forest land must also be immense. To think that at one time, they mindlessly turned those mountains into bald mountains, and now, they are again mindlessly entrusting those bald mountains to foreigners, leaves one speechless.<26>

To speak of the power of foreigners in terms of industry, there are still various aspects, but it's impossible to cover everything. One final point to make concerns the power of the Japanese in this province's transportation and communication industry. Generally, when an immigrant group with different customs, clothing, manners, and language intends to move into another region, the first priority is the opening of transportation and communication facilities. Without them, neither a single individual nor a multitude of people can transport the goods from their home country needed for clothing, food, and shelter. Look! Once the Kyŏngbu Line (京釜線) was opened, major areas along the line, starting with Pusan and Taejŏn, were occupied by those Japanese overnight. After the March First Movement (萬歲運動) erupted in Taishō 8 (1919), they immediately focused on the economic occupation of Korea. The primary focus of this plan was first the completion of a railway network covering all of Korea. As the first step, private railway companies (私鐵會社) were initiated in various places, and the authorities strongly encouraged their completion. This truly shows they grasped the knack of colonization (拓植). Looking at Kyŏngnam Province, the Nansen Tetsudō Stock Company (南鮮鐵道株式), with a nominal capital of 10 million won and paid-in capital of 1.5 million won, has its head office in Masan and is steadily proceeding with its business. This company plans to build a light railway (輕鐵) starting from Masan, passing through Chinju, Hadong, Kurye, Koksŏng, Sunch’ang, and Kwangju to Songjŏng-ri, and also from Wŏnch'on in Namwŏn to Chŏnju in North Chŏlla, thus traversing the southern part of Korea. They expect to see the opening of the section in Puk-myŏn, Masan County around October of this year. Once these lines are opened, the face of southern Korea will surely be renewed, and with that renewal, we will see a corresponding development of Japanese power.<27>

Chirisan University Forest, the Object of Kyŏngnam People's Resentment (怨府)
Chirisan (智異山) is called the treasure house (寶庫) of southern Korea because its land is fertile and its products are abundant. The people living nearby have farmed here, pastured animals here, hunted pheasants and rabbits here, and also made and sold woodenware, charcoal, bamboo items, etc., for thousands of years, living a free and contented life. Therefore, people of the world all called this Chirisan an ideal land (理想の鄕) or a mystical den (神秘の窟), envying it and singing its praises. If the people nearby were to lose their rights to Chirisan, it would be like fish losing water or living beings losing air – such is the vital connection. In other words, Chirisan is the lifeblood of the people nearby. However, their lifeblood was already stolen by others over ten years ago. That is, the Government-General of Chosŏn (朝鮮總督府), under the name of a so-called University Practice Forest (大學演習林), leased Chirisan free of charge for a long term of 80 years to Japan's Kyōto and Kyūshū Universities, effective December 12, Taishō 1 (1912). Looking at the content of the lease directive now, it is as follows:

This huge tract of land, 43,570 *chŏngbo*, is <28> of course state-owned (國有) from a legal perspective, and the Government-General can freely dispose of this state-owned land by leasing or selling it to others. However, in reality, not all of it is state-owned; below the middle peak (中峯), that is, more than two-thirds, is privately owned (民有). Originally, when the Government-General surveyed the land, it notified the people to prove their ownership rights within a certain period. But how could the ignorant mountain people know what rights were, or what state ownership was? While they were peacefully sleeping, the merciless deadline quickly passed, and the paradise (樂土) pioneered by the sweat and blood of their ancestors was lost overnight. The heavens are silent (蒼天無語), the mountain spirits are indifferent (山靈無心), so even if the people lament and grieve, who can possibly understand? Moreover, the resident Japanese, who suck the blood and scrape the bones of Koreans, saw this as an opportunity. Ostensibly claiming to utilize forest products and promote the common interests of the residents, they organized so-called Common Benefit Cooperatives (共益組合). They demand payment of a certain amount, and if one does not become a member, they do not issue an entry permit (入山證), preventing the making of woodenware, charcoal, etc. Furthermore, the University designates a so-called raw timber price (原木代金) (This price is not a certain amount per tree, but a few *sen* (錢) per piece of woodenware). The cooperative collects the designated price; for example, if a pair of wooden clogs (木履) has a market price of 40 *sen* and the university-designated price is 10 *sen*, the cooperative collects 20 *sen*, giving 10 *sen* to the university as revenue and keeping 10 *sen* as cooperative revenue. Ultimately, the maker has to pay out about 50% of the actual product price. Also, charcoal makers are not allowed to produce charcoal under their own management; they must produce it under the name of the cooperative, receiving only a small wage (質金?) for producing a certain number of *sŏk* of charcoal. When this cooperative was first established, Koreans were also members, but due to the power of money and politics, they were naturally pressured, and all Koreans lost their membership qualifications, making it the exclusive domain of a few Japanese. (Regarding this, there was much popular discontent, so last year in Sanch’ŏng, the county office intervened, allowing woodenware makers to directly organize a Woodworkers' Cooperative (木工組合), supervised by the township head (面長), and severing ties with the former Common Benefit Cooperative. The burden on makers was reduced by about 5 *po* (步?), but charcoal production is still directly managed by the Common Benefit Cooperative.) Besides this, the University also forces the residents at the foot of the mountain to form so-called Forest Protection Cooperatives (山林保護組合), collects cooperative fees, and prevents anyone who is not a member from gathering even a single blade of grass (including mountain vegetables and medicinal herbs) or a piece of wood. Even this Protection Cooperative is not freely organized by the people; the township organizes it, the township head must be the cooperative head, and the permit application must go through the county office to the University Practice Forest Management Office (大學演習林管理事務所) in Hadong County. This office then reports to the University to obtain permission. Because such useless procedures are complicated, <29> time naturally drags on, and the people often miss the timing for cutting even the firewood essential for their livelihood, causing immense hardship. What is also alarming here are the so-called brokers (紹介業者) and moneylenders (高利貸金業者). Rootless vagrants (無賴漢) who have wandered into these mountains from various places, lacking fixed assets, collude with the so-called Common Benefit Cooperative, acting as brokers or patrons for woodenware makers, greedily demanding unreasonable commissions (口文) and food and drink. The Japanese members of the Common Benefit Cooperative, on the other hand, also engage in usury, sucking dry the blood of the woodenware makers. In places like Tŏksan (德山) (Sigak-myŏn (矢川面?), Sanch’ŏng County), there was a case where a Japanese member of the Common Benefit Cooperative lent 2 won to a Korean and received 60 won back after three years. There was also a case where someone who borrowed 20 won, after repaying 15 won, had compound interest charged on the remaining 5 won, and after three years, even after selling household goods and furniture (家庄什物), paid 100 won but still could not clear the debt. As seen above, since Chirisan was leased as a so-called University Practice Forest, the residents at its foot have been deprived of their interests in various ways, naturally leading to hardship in their lives. The result is the commission of appalling criminal acts such as robbery, theft, and gambling. Looking at the crime statistics for all of Kyŏngnam, Sanch’ŏng County has the most robberies and thefts, and within that county's statistics, the areas near Chirisan, namely Samjang (三莊) and Sigak (矢川) townships, have the most. Ah, whose fault is this truly? Though all under heaven is the king's land (普天之下 莫非王土), why do the people at the foot of Chirisan particularly suffer this tragic calamity? The Chŏng Kam Nok (鄭堪錄) said Chirisan is one of the Ten Victory Sites (十勝之地), but this turned out wrong; only the phrase "Who is killing me? The small-headed, footless one" (殺我者誰 小頭無足) proved correct. That is, they are fated to die by the hands of shaven-headed foreigners (外人). What was the intention of the Government-General when it initially leased this Chirisan to the two universities? And what was the meaning behind the two universities accepting the lease? There is no need to further discuss the underlying details, but ostensibly, the purpose was to protect the forest, supply materials needed for academic research, and consequently set an example for Koreans. However, since leasing it, what kind of facilities have the two universities actually established to set an example for the general public? They merely cut down the existing forests, collect the raw timber fees, and, as a token gesture (塞責上), transplant a few black pine seedlings onto about 1.5 *chŏngbo* (1町 5反) of poor soil, calling it the Practice Forest of such-and-such University. Is this truly the original intent of the Government-General in leasing it, and the original intent of the two universities in managing the practice forest? However, these two universities also merely drain the lifeblood (膏澤) of the residents to fill the private pockets (私腹) of the Common Benefit Cooperative, gaining no actual benefit for their own universities. Looking at the recent annual income for the 16,991 *chŏngbo* of practice forest in Sanch’ŏng County belonging to the two universities, it was 686 Won 64 Chŏn 2 Ri (from April, Taishō 10 to March, Taishō 11), <30> with the highest annual amount being 151 Won 51 Chŏn. Calculating proportionally for the entire practice forest area of 43,570 *chŏngbo*, it comes to about 20,000 won annually. However, after deducting the salaries of the managing staff and guards and other expenses, there is not only no profit, but it is said that when personnel are dispatched from the universities for so-called inspections or whatever each year, the university's expenses are covered instead. Then, what need do the universities have to persistently hold onto this lease right? And why does the Government-General calmly overlook actions contrary to the original purpose of the lease and not cancel it? Whether it benefits the universities or not is not our concern, but in this era professing cultural rule (文化政治), if industry or education is to be centered on Koreans, why lease Chirisan, the treasure house of Korea, to Japan's Kyūshū University or Kyōto University, located tens of thousands of *ri* away and having no connection whatsoever? Britain annexed India, but we have not heard that the Governor-General of India leased the Himalayas (雪山) to London University or Cambridge University (釰橋大學?). France made Annam its territory, but we have not heard that it leased the Cloud Range (雲嶺?) to Paris University (巴里大學). We can only fervently hope that the Government-General, for the sake of the happiness of the people of the various counties at the foot of Chirisan and considering the discontent of the general Korean populace, will realize the unreasonableness and inconsistency of this past action and cancel the lease right as soon as possible, leasing it instead to operators of Korean educational projects.
(There are many such University Practice Forests in various parts of Korea, but since this issue of the magazine is the Kyŏngnam Province issue, we specifically discuss only Chirisan.)

Wooden Clog Seller's Cold Spell (木屐商寒)
The cold spell on the 22nd day of the 12th lunar month (臘月 22日) is called the "wooden clog seller's cold spell" (나무신 장사 치우) in Chinju. According to folk tales, a certain person in Chinju Castle wanted to kill someone he hated. He sent a letter from places like Tŏksan, instructing, "Kill this person as soon as he arrives," and entrusted the letter to that person. As that person was carrying the letter, he happened to give it to a wooden clog seller. The wooden clog seller delivered the letter and was unjustly killed. Therefore, it is said that it definitely gets cold on that day. This is close to fiction, but it is similar to how the cold spell on the 20th day of the 10th lunar month near Kyŏnggi province is called the Son Sŏk Hang (孫石項 - Sondolmok) cold spell.<31>

**Summary**

This 1923 article from *Kaebyŏk* magazine provides a detailed socio-economic survey of Kyŏngsangnam-do under Japanese colonial rule. It covers geography, history, demographics, climate, and extensively details the stark disparities in education, favoring Japanese residents overwhelmingly in terms of school access, funding, and types of institutions available. The report analyzes the province's major industries—agriculture, forestry, fisheries, manufacturing, and commerce—highlighting the region's natural wealth but emphasizing the systemic marginalization of Koreans and the aggressive economic penetration by Japanese individuals and corporations like the Tōyō Takushoku and Murai Farm, who dominate land ownership, resource extraction (especially fisheries and forestry), and emerging industries. The article expresses deep resentment towards colonial policies, exemplified by the controversial leasing of vast tracts of Chirisan (Mt. Jiri) to Japanese universities, which dispossessed local Koreans and facilitated exploitation through intermediary organizations, leading to social distress and crime.

For historians, this article is valuable as a contemporary Korean perspective offering rich statistical data (though sometimes incomplete or presented narratively) and qualitative descriptions of colonial realities in a specific region just a few years after the 1919 March First Movement. It vividly documents the mechanisms of economic colonization, the stark educational inequalities fueling resentment, the environmental impact of resource exploitation (both deforestation and reforestation efforts under Japanese control), and the palpable sense of loss and anger among Koreans, particularly regarding land alienation and the Chirisan University Forests, offering insights into the socio-economic roots of anti-colonial sentiment.

朝鮮文化의 基本調査 - 一部發表
먼저 慶南의 大體로부터 說去하면

경상남도 관내도

1. 位置 及 地勢.  本道는 조선반도의 東南端에 在하니 西는 全羅南北二道를 접하고 北은 慶尙北道를 隣하고 東南二方은 海에 瀕하얏다. 海內에 산맥이 종횡으로 亙함으로 지세가 不一하나 대개 北西가 융기하고 東南이 低下하며 洛東江은 道의 중앙을 貫流하야 其流域에 沿한 평야는 地味가 비옥함으로 농산이 풍부하고 남방일대의 해안은 叅差屈曲하야 항만이 파다하며 다수의 島嶼는 海中에 巷布星列하야 魚族의 서식에 適한 고로 수산업은 농업에 次한 本道 주요의 산업이 되나니라.
경남의 청년단체(대정 12년 2월 調)

2. 沿革.  本道는 古弁韓의 地니 후에 五伽倻國이 分據하얏다가 신라의 멸한 바 되고 고려가 통합한 후 成宗 14년에 金州 소관으로 嶺東道라 하고 晉州 소관으로 山南道라 칭하다가 明宗 元年에 晉陜州道라 개칭하고 忠肅 元年에＜3＞ 慶尙道라 合稱하야 李朝에서 仍稱하고 中宗 14년에 左右道로 分하야 洛東江으로 界를 作하얏다가 즉 罷하고 宣朝 25년에 又 左右道로 分하얏다가 翌年에 復合하고 28년에 又 分하얏다가 翌年에 復合하야 大邱에 設營(初設於星州)하고 31년에 移營하얏다가 4년에 大邱에 復移하고 高宗太皇帝 32년에 南北道로 分하야 道廳을 晉州에 置한 후 今에까지 至하니라.

3. 管區 及 戶口人口.  本道의 면적은 790方里 783이라. 此를 2府 19郡 257面에 分하얏스니 1郡의 평균면적은 37方里 909, 1面 평균면적은 3方里 104에 달하며 戶口는 조선인 339,055戶, 일본인 17,308戶, 외국인 204戶, 계 356,567戶, 인구는 조선인 1,738,049인, 일본인 69,750인, 외국인 765인 계 1,808,561인이니 1方里에 대하야 戶數 447戶, 인구 2,266人 九의 비례가 되는 고로 人戶口가 各道中 제일 조밀하니라.(大正 10년 12월 말 調)

4. 氣候.  조선의 기후는 대륙적인고로 寒暑의 차가 현저하나 本道의 기후는 西北의 산간부를 除한 외에는 해양의 조절관계로 반도 중 온화하야 最溫度가 비교적 차이가 少하니 연중평균온도는 최고 섭씨 32도, 최저 영하 5도 3이오 且晴天이 多하고 공기가 건조하야 거주에 最適宜하니라.

慶南은 원래 유교의 淵藪인 고로 其信徒 즉 학자가 다수됨은 물론이나 舊習을 膠守하고 시대에 낙오되야 일반인의 배척을 受함으로 점차 쇠퇴의 경향이 有하며 불교는 신라, 고려시대에 일시 盛旺하야 今에도 당시 건축한 사찰 등을 見하면 其壯大雄傑함이 실로 可驚할 者가 多하나 李朝에서 崇儒斥佛한 결과로 극히 쇠퇴하야 當今까지 萎靡不振의 상태에 在하고 근래에 至하야는 조선에서 창설된 天道敎, 大宗敎, 太乙(兩敎 敎徒數 未詳) 등과 외국에서 유입한 기독교가 頗히 성행하며 且日本人間에 神道를 신앙하는 자가 多하니 其槪況은 左와 如하다.

本道는 朝鮮諸道의 중에 全羅南道와 同樣으로 기후가 온화하야 農林業上으로 온난대의 식물을 栽植하기에 適好하며 특히 해안선의 延長이 1,200여 해리에 달하야 魚族 及 藻類의 출산이 풍부하며 특히 역사적, 지리적으로 일본과 밀접한 관계가 잇서 해상교통으로부터 오는 산업상의 의의는 別로 중대함이 잇나니 이제 방면을 分하야 산업형편을 소개하야써 慶南의 형제 또는 일반동포의 주의를 促함이 잇고져 한다.

農業
本道의 농업은 어업과 아울너 산업의 大宗을 일우엇다. 大正 10년말의 현재로 沓이 160,257町步餘, 田이 116,164町步餘, 합계 276,322町步餘로서 농업자 戶數 277,163호, 인구 1,466,026인인 바 총호수에 대하야 약 8할을 점하엿스며 농가 1호에 當할 경지면적은 沓이 5反8畝, 田이 4反2畝, 計1町步에 상당한다.
농산물은 米, 麥, 豆類가 유명한대 米는 平年産額 170만석, 麥이 亦 167만석, 大豆가 약 40만석에 달하는 중 米는 매년 약 40만석, 大豆는 약 10만석이 年輸移出을 행하게 된다.
농산품 중의 특용작물인 陸地綿, 苧麻, 楮, 莞草와 가튼 것은 其質與量에서 共히 有數한 것이며 果物 중의 柿와 苹果가 亦然하다.
本道에는 島嶼가 甚多하야 古來로 목축업에 유명하야 甄萱으로브터 王建太祖에게 헌상한 欲知島의 良馬는 역사상에 特記된 바이나 이제는 업스며 다못 농가의 牛, 羔, 羊, 등의 飼畜이 잇슬 뿐이다.＜14＞

林業
本道의 임야총면적은 약 881,000町步인 중 일즉브터 成林된 地가 160,000여町步, 稚樹 등 生地가 480,000여町步이며 남아지 210,000町步는 無林木地인대 道當局으로브터 其間 十有餘年間 造林에 노력하야 大正 10년까지 無林木地의 4분의 1이상의 植林을 행하얏다. 이제 참고로써 造林의 實況을 示하면,

1. 道有林은 약 500町步까지 국유임야의 讓與를 受하는 規例인대 本道에서는 該 제한면적 이상의 讓與를 受하야 旣히 造林을 了하엿스니 其狀況이 如左

2. 面有林은 面의 財源涵養으로 一面에 50町步 내지 300町步의 面有林을 경영케 하는대 此에 충당하는 임야는 第一種不要存國有林野를 양도 又는 대부한 者와 面內의 각 部落林을 面에 통일한 者가 잇스며 또는 사유임야 중 砂防工事와 如한 특수시설을 要할 者를 面에 양도케 한 者 등이 잇는 바 本道의 面有林은 旣히 28,024町步(一面 평균 100町이상)에 달하엿다.

3. 學校林은 보통 정도에 학교에는 20町步, 중등 정도의 학교에는 40町步까지 國有林里의 讓與를 許하는대 此에 의하야 學校林을 설치한 자가 66교에 임야면적 760町步에 달하엿다.

4. 國有貸付林.  本道內의 第一種不要存國有林野 약 67,000町步 중에 旣히 貸付한 것 60,200여町步인대 其中 前記의 (1) (2) (3) (4)의 分을 除한 외에는 營林을 목적하는 단체 及 개인에게 대부한 것이며 尙又 大正 10년말까지 처분 아니한 국유임야가 약 6,500町步가 有하엿난대 是亦 조만간에 擧皆 貸付할 예정이라 한다.

慶尙道의 禿山이 如何히 경부선 통과의 人人을 喫驚케 하는가함은 일반이 認하는 바이나 지금은 거히 산악의 全面을 亙하야 稚樹의 발생을 見하게 되야 사실로 면목을 一新하게 된 바 당국에서는 動輒造林成績의 양호를 自誇하난 바이라. 尙又 本道當局에서는 大正 8년에 造林業 20년계획을 成하야 大正 27년까지 道內全部의 造林을 완료할 계획으로써 진행하는 今日이며 大正 6년브터 행해오는 임야조사도 大正 13년으로써 완료케하야 산림의 권리관계를 명료케 하는 동시에 此의 査定이 종료된 郡으로브터는 점차 삼림의 기본조사를 행하야 木材及薪炭의 수급관계를 분명케 함과 共히 保安林編入 기타 植林促進의＜15＞ 구체안을 수립할 방침이라 한다.

水産
本道 2府 19郡 중 海에 瀕한 것이 2府 9郡이나 나리는고로 管內의 半分 이상은 수산에 관계가 深하며 所屬島嶼의 수가 278로서 해안선의 연장은 本陸이 2,310里, 島嶼가 3,390리, 계 5,700리에 至하며 해안은 叅差届曲, 岬角灣澳가 相連하고 다수의 島嶼가 碁布한 중 수심은 대개 50尋 내외로 寒暖 兩海流의 유역에 際하야 난류성의 鰮, 鯖, 鰆 한류성의 鱈, 鰊 등 각종 水族의 분포가 풍부하며 특히 기후가 온화하야 四時의 操業에 適하다.
鰮, 鯖, 鱈은 道의 3대 어업으로 지칭되며 중에도 鰮漁業은 本道 어업의 首位를 점하야 年産額이 4백만원 내외에 달하야 本道總漁獲高의 약 3분의 1에 上하야 此의 풍흉이 本道 경제계에 及하는 영향은 실로 지대한 것이며 鯖漁業은 近年 중 着網漁法의 발흥, 漁期의 연장 及 수송기관의 발달에 의하야 年産額 250만원 내지 300만원에 上하야 生鮮 그대로 일본에 수출됨이 多하며 鱈漁業은 주로 조선인 어업에 속한 것인대 年産額 100만원 내지 150만원에 上한다.

보라 進水의 村井農場
조선 안에 일본인의 농장이라 하면 문득 5,000만원의 巨資로써 활동하는 東洋拓殖을 始하야 黃州의 朝鮮興業과 森農場, 水原의 東山農場, 木浦의 右近農場, 昌原의 村井農場, 井邑의 態本農場, 金堤의 石川縣農業株式, 益山의 細川農場, 鎭南浦의 齊藤農場, 沙里院의 日海興業株式出張所 등을 연상할 것이다. 그런대 이 중의 하나되는 昌原郡 進水의 村井農場의 근황을 見하면, 該農場은 明治 41년 1월에 창립되야 보통농사와 과수재배를 兼營하는대 그의 점유한 토지는 昌原으로부터 金海의 일부에 跨在하야 약3,000町步의 면적을 취하엿스며 此에 관계하는 소작인은 일본인이 100호, 조선인이 1,300호이며 재작년 중의 收穫全高는 稻租가 27,000석, 麥이 1,700석, 大豆 1,000석이며 소유의 경지는 沓 1,500町步, 田 500町步, 기타 雜種地가 600町步이며 이외에 幾何의 임야가 잇다. 과연 잘들 해먹는다.

工業
本道의 공업은 일종 농가의 부업에 속하야 可觀할 것이 無하던 중 近年 일본인의 열심투자로 釜山의＜16＞ 紡績株式, 牧島의 日本硬質陶器株式支店, 龜浦의 朝鮮纖維工業株式 등이 有하야 규모가 宏大하다.
試하야 최근 1개년 간의 本道 工産額을 見하면 總産額이 21,769,000여원인 중 직물 1,992,000여원 紙及製造品 958,000여원, 漆器 54,000여원, 莚席 455,000여원이 其主要한 者이며 此外에 別로 일본인 경영의 釀造業이 왕성함이 잇다.
機業은 道內의 陜川, 居昌, 咸陽, 山淸, 南海, 泗川, 固城의 7郡이 古來로 麻布 又는 苧布의 산지로 유명하야 大正 10년도에는 직물 892,000反, 價額 1,956,000여원의 多額을 産하여섯다.
제지업은 역시 本道에 有數한 것이니 年産額이 40만 내지 60만원에 달하며 매년 조선에서 중국에 수출되는 것이 15만 내지 35만근에 至하는 중 8할 이상이 本道産이다.
統營의 漆器는 古來로 유명한 것이나 중간에 粗製濫製의 風이 행하야 만히 신용을 失하엿스며 年産額 약 5만원을 算한다.

商業
本道는 지리상 일본과 近하야 해상교통이 빈번한 중 釜山, 馬山, 鎭海의 3항은 輸移出入貿易은 조선 전貿易高의 약 2할 5분을 점함이 보통이다. 그 중에도 부산항은 조선의 관문으로서 수입품 중의 주요한 것은 金巾類, 석유, 석탄, 사탕, 목재판, 棉織物, 紙類繩叺 등이오 수출품 중 주요한 것은 米, 大豆, 魚類, 生牛, 繭, 비료, 生皮, 綿花, 海藻 등 류이다.
道內의 시장은 5일 5일을 격하야 보통 시장이 122處인 중 河東市場, 晉州市場 가튼 것이 저명하며 기타의 개인 혹은 회사의 경영에 속한 어시장 가튼 특별시장이 16處 取引所가 1處 계 139개소이다.＜17＞

覆巢下의 危卵 ㅡ 慶南에 在한 朝鮮人産業의 沒落
覆巢危卵이 엇지 其理가 잇스리오, 慶南의 천지도 벌서 外人의 獲場이 되고 말엇도다. 더욱 慶南은 (1)기후가 온화하야 사람 살기에 됴코, (2)토지가 肥하고 海港이 良하야 産物이 富하고, (3)일본과 근접하야 교통이 편하고, (4)인심이 순박하야 외래의 족속이 來接하기에 무방한 등 事는 조선의 엇더한 道보다도 더 심하게 外人의 勞力을 초래함이 되여섯다.
本道의 산업 중 현재에 유수한 것은 農, 工, 漁의 三業이 될지며 장래에 유망한 것은 林業일지라. 그러나 이 4대의 산업경영에 在하야 조선인의 努力이 如何히 빈약하며 일본인의 努力이 如何히 왕성한 것을 목격할 때에 우리의 心膽은 새로히 서늘해짐을 느끼지 아늘 수 업섯다.

요컨대 京城의 조선인상업계가 泥峴 일본인을 중심삼아 運轉되는 것과 가치 慶南의 조선인상업계도 역시 釜山의 日人을 중심삼아 運轉된다. 그들은 마치 網의 網과 갓고 衣의 領과 갓다. 例하면 釜山 거주의 香推源太郞, 迫問房太郞, 大池忠助와 如한 人은 모다 百萬의 長者로서 그들의 소유한 경제력은 반다시 慶南一圓에 팽배한 것뿐이 아니라.

慶南人民의 怨府인 智異山大學林
智異山은 토지가 膏沃하고 물산이 풍부함으로 自來南鮮의 寶庫라 한다. 그 부근의 인민은 幾千年 간을 此에서 경작하고 此에서 蒭牧하고 此에서 雉兎하고 又 木器木炭, 竹物 등을 제조판매하야 자유로운 낙천의 생활을 하얏다. 그럼으로 세상의 人은 다 이 智異山을 理想의 鄕이니 신비의 窟이니 하야 此를 羨望하여 此를 구가하얏다. 만일 그 부근의 인민으로 하야금 智異山에 대한 권리를 失한다 하면 魚類가 水를 失하고 생물이 공기를 失함과 如한 절실의 관계가 잇다. 즉 智異山은 그 부근 인민의 생명이다. 그러나 그의 생명은 벌서 10여년 전에 타인에게 被奪되얏다. 즉 朝鮮總督府에서는 大正 元年 12월 12일부로 智異山을 소위 大學演習林이라는 명의 하에 일본에 京都, 九州 양 대학에 무상으로 80개년 長期貸付를 하얏다. 今에 其貸付한 지령의 내용을 見하면 左와 如하다.
