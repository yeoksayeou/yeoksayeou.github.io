A Born Poet
						Kim Sŏksong

When I was reciting poetry,
A small bird in a cage, too,
With a voice like silver bells,
Recited poetry in response.

Biriri biribibi,
Biribiribiribi... .

Ah! Small bird! Born poet!
Your poetry is truly poetry,
More so than the stereotypical human poetry,
A crystallization of affectation, exaggeration, stagnation.

Ah! Small bird! Born poet!
I intend to learn from you, <81>

Your poetry of purity, freedom, abandon,
Your feelings without deception.

Bibibi biribi,
Bibibibi biribibi... .

Cage Bird

A rectangle, one *ch’ŏk* by five *ch’on*!
Inside a three-dimensional bamboo cage,
From this perch to that perch,
From that perch to this perch,
Hopping and hopping,
Eating the feed in the container – given by the master,
Knowing it as its own share,
- The small bird's life!

On days of monotony and flatness,
With threads of infinite limitation,
The silk of your life, woven out,
Is cared for by your Master until the end.
(And I, profoundly, <82>
Must gratefully receive
The cherished love of the great Master
Who raises both you and me).

To the Land of Children

I was absorbed in reading.
Startled by the sound "Father!",
I glanced back quickly,
And my three-year-old, having woken from sleep,
Rushed towards me with open arms.
I held the child,
Exclaiming "Waya!".

While the mother, deep in exhausted sleep,
Her breathing could be clearly heard,
From afar, the sound of a train arrives.
My spirit suddenly becomes vacant,
And together with the sound of the rolling train wheels,
It departs far, far away somewhere.
Carrying fantasies of life, growth,
To the land of children, never to return again. <83>

**Summary**

This work by Kim Sŏksong consists of three short poems. The first, "A Born Poet," contrasts the natural, pure "poetry" of a bird's song with the artificiality of human poetry. The second, "Cage Bird," describes the limited life of a bird in a cage, reflecting on its confinement and dependence on a master, which the poet extends to his own situation, expressing gratitude for a "great Master." The third, "To the Land of Children," depicts a moment where the poet, interrupted by his child, feels his spirit drift away with the sound of a distant train towards an idealized, irretrievable past symbolized by childhood. From a historical perspective, the poems might interest historians for their themes of confinement ("Cage Bird") potentially allegorizing the colonial condition, the yearning for purity and freedom ("A Born Poet"), and the escapism towards an idealized past ("To the Land of Children"). The ambiguous reference to a "great Master" caring for both the bird and the poet could also invite interpretations related to power dynamics or religious/philosophical resignation in the colonial era.

天生의 詩人
						金石松
내가 詩를 읇고 잇슬 때,
籠 속에 들은 작은 새도,
銀방울 가튼 목소리로,
詩를 읇허 화답하엿다.

비리리 비리비비,
비리비리비리비... .

아! 작은 새! 天生의 詩人!.
너의 詩야말로 참 詩이다,
虛飾, 誇張, 澁滯의 結晶인,
판박이 사람의 詩보다.

아! 작은 새! 天生의 詩人!
나는 너를 배우랴 한다,＜81＞

그 純眞, 自由, 奔放의 시를,
속임성 업는 너의 감정을.

비비비 비리비,
비비비비 비리비비... .

籠鳥
一尺과 五寸의 長方形!
立軆로 된 竹籠 속에서,
이 홰에서 저 홰에,
저 홰에서 이 홰에,
깡충깡충 뛰다가,
통에 들은 모이- 주인의 준 것을,
제 차지로 알고 먹는 ,
-작은 새의 生活!.

單調와 平面의 날에,
無限한 局限의 올로,
짜어내인 너의 生의 비단은,
끗까지 너의 主人이 보살핀다.
(그리고, 나는 그윽히,＜82＞
너와 나를 기르시는,
거륵한 主人의 寵愛를,
感謝히 바드랴 한다).

어린이의 나라로

나는 글읽기에 精神이 업섯다.
「아버지!」하는 소리에 놀나,
뒤를 훌적 도라본 즉,
세 살 된 어린 것이 자고 깨어서,
팔을 벌리고 달녀든다.
나는 어린 것을 안엇다,
「와야!」소리를 하며.

困한 잠이 깁히 든 어미의,
숨소리가 똑똑히 들일 제,
머얼니서 막 車소리가 들린다.
나의 얼은 空然히 휀하야,
車박휘의 굴으는 소리와 함끠,
어대론지 멀니멀니 가고 만다.
生命, 成長의 幻想을 실코
다시 못 오는 어린이의 나라로.＜83＞
