The Urgent Need for the Creation of a National Central Force

Chosŏn lacks a political or social central force. A populace without a central force is not even a nation (民族). Furthermore, it is certainly not a *people* (國民). During the Korean Empire era, the yangban class centered around the royal family was the central force, but after annexation, Chosŏn's national central force disappeared entirely. Because there is no central force, the people of Chosŏn are merely a multitude of individuals gathered in the region called Chosŏn. They are not an organized group. They are like the four limbs and the whole body without a central nervous system. Just as the arms move as arms and the legs move as legs with no unification whatsoever, the Chosŏn nation still lacks a national consciousness, and consequently, there is no national ideal, national will, or national opinion.

National consciousness is the conviction that our 20 million people are a single organized body heading towards the same ideal. It is the national consciousness that allows one Korean, upon meeting another Korean—even if they are strangers who have not spoken or exchanged names—to trust that they share a common point of ideal. And the national ideal, national will, or national opinion refers to the ideal, will, and opinion of the group that constitutes the central force. The central force is like the North Star in the heavens or the sun in the solar system; just as the multitude of stars revolve around the North Star as their standard, and just as the various planets of the solar system orbit the sun as their standard, each individual of a unified nation wills and acts with that central force as their standard. Only through this can national life and national endeavors be achieved. <4>

A national central force does not necessarily mean the sovereign ruler or the government. In ancient times, indeed, the monarch or the ruling class became the central force of the nation. Even now, a government can be the central force as long as it represents the will of the people (民意). However, speaking from correct theory, it is not the government that creates (作) the national central force, but the national central force that produces (産) the government. The national central force is the mother of the government.

However, the national central force is not necessarily limited to the governmental sphere. Historically, religion could also become a central force, and in places like present-day Russia (아라사), a group united by a social ideal has become the national central force. In any case, the group that firmly unites with a definite plan to achieve the ideal most fitting for that nation—in other words, the ideal most appealing to the heart of that nation—becomes the central force of that nation.

Therefore, the central force is precisely a strong and large organization. Without forming an organization, one can never become a central force. Without a central force, a nation can never be unified to achieve national life and endeavors. Some might think that an individual hero or sage can become the central force of a nation, but this is ignorance. Historically, there is no hero who created a central force without gathering (紏合) comrades, without gathering numerous comrades, training them, and forming a powerful great union. Even if they did not personally form the union, they utilized an already established union. The most prominent examples are the political organizations of modern nations.

However, in Chosŏn, in the strict sense, there is still no organization that can become such a central force. This is one of the important peculiarities among the unique circumstances of the Chosŏn nation. The Philippines (比律賓) has a central force. The Philippine independence movement is the movement of this central force. India has a central force. The movement of Gandhi's (깐듸) faction is the movement of this central force. To understand this as the movement of Gandhi <5> the individual is a mistake. It is not the movement of Gandhi the individual, but the movement of a large group (一大團軆) that Gandhi represents. Ireland (愛蘭) has the Sinn Féin Party (신펜黨). De Valera (데 발레라) is the leader of the Sinn Féin Party. Russia (아라사) has the Bolshevik Party (볼세비키黨). Lenin (레닌) is the representative of the Bolshevik Party. The Jewish people (猶太민족) have the Zion Party (시온黨) based on Judaism. Therefore, if you want to know the will of the Indian people, you just need to ask Gandhi. This is because Gandhi is the leader of the National Congress, the central force of the Indian people. If you want to hear the ideal of the Irish nation, go to de Valera. His words are precisely the words of the Irish nation. But to whom should one ask the will of the Chosŏn nation? Should one ask Yi Wanyong (李完用)? Should one ask Kim Myŏngjun (金明濬)? Or should one ask the president of the Tong'a Ilbo (東亞日報) or the editor-in-chief of Tongmyŏng (東明), who advocate independence-ism (獨立主義)? Or should one ask the president of Sin Saenghwal Magazine (新生活雜誌), who advocates socialism (社會主義)? If asked, each would likely give an answer. However, it would be an individual's answer. No one would accept their answer as representing the Chosŏn nation.

If Yi Wanyong or Kim Myŏngjun were representatives of a large organization capable of becoming the central force of the Chosŏn nation, their opinions could be called the opinions of the Chosŏn nation. But they are individuals. An individual, solely in their capacity as an individual, can never represent the nation. Independence advocates, autonomy advocates, or socialists are the same; if their opinions are to become the opinions of the Chosŏn nation, they must first create a large organization with their ideology (主義) that can become the central force of the Chosŏn nation. Only when a newspaper or magazine publishes the opinions of an organization that is the central force can it be said to represent public opinion.

Moreover, the Chosŏn nation is in an era where it must fully express its national will. Whether it be assimilationism (同化主義), autonomism (自治論), independence-ism (獨立主義), or socialism (社會主義), the people of Chosŏn must choose one of these at the core and create a national will. Otherwise, Chosŏn will forever remain a scattered multitude of individuals who are nothing, and unified national life and <6> endeavors will be impossible to achieve. Even if debates rage day and night with pro and con arguments (甲論乙駁), they are merely individual disputes. National will cannot be decided through them. No matter how much each person claims to represent the twenty million people of Chosŏn, they never become representatives. It is said that 13 individuals, each claiming to represent the Chosŏn nation, gathered simultaneously in Moscow (모스크바). The Moscow government did not know which among them should be considered the true Chosŏn nation. All 13 carried credentials (信任狀) in the name of some organization. However, these organizations were mostly ones without even membership lists (命薄) or dues income. The 13 fought amongst themselves to be recognized as the official representative, but in the end, none became official. This is a natural outcome. It is because none of them were representatives of an organization that was the central force of the Chosŏn nation. No matter how ingenious the plans and stratagems one possesses, one cannot represent a nation without doing so in the name of an organization that is the central force.

Therefore, are you an assimilationist? If you wish to achieve your goal, form an organization. Are you an autonomist? An independence advocate? If you truly wish to realize your ideology (主義), create an organization that can become the nation's central force. All actions taken without doing so are futile.

However, in Chosŏn, where such an organization to become the central force is needed, there is no such organization. People clamor about as if something can be achieved even without an organization. In our view, the people of Chosŏn seem not yet to have realized the necessity of an organization. Otherwise, those who claim to be leaders in various fields of the Chosŏn nation would surely strive to create organizations.

Some might say, "Aren't the leaders of Chosŏn already creating organizations?" Leaving aside the past, even currently, aren't there organizations like the Minuhoe (民友會), the Chosŏn Youth Association Federation (朝鮮靑年會聯合會), and in other spheres, the Yuminhoe (維民會), the Tenant Farmers' Mutual Aid Society (小作人相助會), and in yet other spheres, the Taedong Comrades' Association (大東同志會), the National Association (國民協會), etc.? Right. Indeed, such organizations exist. And perhaps those organizations were formed with the self-confidence (自負), or at least the hope, of becoming the <7> fundamental force of the Chosŏn nation. But why do those organizations lack power? Why can't they flourish? Some might also say, "Firstly, the people of Chosŏn lack the capacity for unity, and secondly, organizations advocating independence or socialism cannot flourish due to government oppression (壓迫), while organizations advocating assimilation or autonomy cannot flourish due to the people's lack of response (不響應)." There is indeed some truth to this answer. It is true that the people of Chosŏn have not yet grasped the essentials of organizational life, and it is also true that advocates of independence, assimilationism, or socialism each face great obstacles in realizing their ideologies. However, leaders! Are you not excessively shifting the responsibility for the lack of organizational development solely onto the government and the people? Are you not excessively avoiding your own introspection and self-reproach (內省自責)? If there is a new ideology to be realized, it will inherently contradict the current state of society. If it were an ideology that did not contradict the current state of society, there would be no need to realize it anew. If there were an ideology that the government approved of and the people unanimously supported, there would be no need for your painstaking efforts and tireless devotion (磨頂放踵). Because the obstacles are many, great strength is required to realize it, and because great strength is required, a large organization (大 團體) is needed. If you cannot overcome these difficulties and fail to form a large organization, it would be an admission of your own incompetence in realizing that ideology. In that case, it would be appropriate to shut your mouth, put down your pen, withdraw, and study more.

Why do all the existing organizations in Chosŏn start with a dragon's head but end with a snake's tail (龍頭蛇尾)? Why is the day of inauguration their golden age, only to be forgotten as time passes? We do not attribute the responsibility for this solely to the fault of the Korean people. Nor do we attribute it solely to government oppression. We assert firmly that the greatest responsibility truly lies with the leaders who initiate (發起) the organizations. The reason why all organizations in Chosŏn fail to flourish lies in the fact that the initiators lack the insight (明) to perceive the unique conditions of Chosŏn and the wisdom (智慧) to formulate plans responsive to these unique conditions, merely stopping at imitating (模倣) what others do. <8>

Regarding organizational life, the people of Chosŏn still lack deep awareness and experience. Thus, without considering the reasons for consenting to become a member of an organization or the duties and rights one holds after becoming a member, they simply add their name to the roster (名簿) if it sounds like a good cause (조흘 뜻). After adding their name, they forget about it. They have never even once read through the organization's rules. Let alone critically examined its ideology and plans. They might have attended meetings a couple of times and paid dues for a couple of months. But after that, they completely forgot about it. This is the concept of organization among our compatriots today.

And what is our method of creating organizations? First, two or three, or four or five people gather by chance. By chance, the topic of conversation turns to a certain matter. "Good," they all agree. Without trying to ascertain precisely the extent of their agreement, they try to think that their opinions somehow match. And the agreed-upon purpose consists of ideas unclear to the majority. They did not come with a prior commitment (素志) to necessarily dedicate their lives to this ideology; they just happened to hear about it, it sounded plausible, and they consented out of momentary excitement. Then, whether their ideologies precisely match or not, if they know each other, they help organize an inaugural meeting (發起會). There, a rules drafting committee is formed, another meeting is held, the rules are passed by majority vote, officers are elected, and thus the organization is formed. A prospectus (趣旨書) is circulated, newspaper articles appear, and then, having already passed its golden age, it enters a period of decline. Isn't this the typical history of our organizations?

Because the organization's initiators (發起人) lack clear awareness and well-ordered plans, and are not individuals who have mutually decided to wholeheartedly dedicate themselves to the organization after clearly and definitively agreeing on these points, disagreements arise from the second day of the organization's formation, and members start dropping out one by one <9> from the fourth day. When recruiting new members, they prioritize simply filling the roster (名簿) without confirming whether the recruit's awareness and sincerity (實心) align with the organization's purpose, so even if that member joins, it brings no benefit (効益) to the organization. Let me ask: which organization today has members who fulfill the duty of attending meetings, paying dues, and obeying the rules? It seems the more aloof one is from attending meetings, the more dignified; trying to collect dues is considered undignified from the start. Association expenses should be covered by donations, and proposing to use dues for them is laughable from the outset. — Isn't this the current state of our organizations? How can such organizations grow, and even if they grow, what strength would they have? What is the fundamental purpose (本義) of organizing? We do not organize for the sake of organizing; we organize for the sake of getting work done. Great work requires great strength, and we organize to obtain that great strength.

Why does an organization have great strength? Is the membership roster (會員名簿) the strength? If the membership roster were the strength, the census register (戶口調査表) would be the most powerful organization. Is the prospectus (趣旨書) the strength, or the rulebook (規則書) the strength? If so, we could write four or five prospectuses and rulebooks a day during our leisure time. Is having "Mr. So-and-so, Chairman of the So-and-so Association (모모회장 모모씨)" appear in the personal news section of a daily newspaper the strength? Is holding lectures or speeches under the name of the So-and-so Association (某某會) the strength? Is being invited as the "So-and-so organization" during holiday celebrations (慶節祝賀) the strength? Lectures and speeches can be held if one has the 35 won fee to rent the Youth Association Hall (청년회관), and even the Kisaeng Association (기생조합) can participate in holiday celebrations.

The strength of an organization is not that. Nor is it some vague (瞬昧한) abstract ideology (主義) or platform (綱領). Then what is the strength of an organization? It is the single-mindedness (일심) of each member for the same ideology, and the members' money (金錢) and time contributed to achieve that ideology. Unchanging though time passes, unchanging though persecution comes, unchanging despite difficulties and losses, unchanging even if one goes to prison, unchanging even if one ascends the execution scaffold <10> for the ideology, the single-mindedness of the member who declares "I am one who practices the ideology of such-and-such organization," and the contribution of fixed membership dues to achieve the organization's goals, and responding to the organization's call by abandoning all private matters if it is a legally decided resolution of the organization, even selling one's house and cauldron (가마솟)—this is the strength of an organization. Because the members are single-minded in the same ideology, the membership roster becomes a symbol of strength, and because it can generate financial power far beyond what individuals can muster, the membership roster becomes a symbol of strength. What strength can arise from having millions or tens of millions of members who are not single-minded in the ideology and do not contribute the required money? Such members are like inconvertible banknotes (不換紙幣). An organization with such members is like a rich person sitting with a safe full of only inconvertible banknotes. It seems to have strength, but in reality, it's completely broke (맨꽁문). Alas, how many completely broke organizations are there?

Then the reader might ask: Wouldn't every organization want to become such a powerful organization? Everyone wants it, but what can be done about the lack of organizational training among our compatriots? This too sounds plausible, but it is actually shifting the responsibility away from oneself onto other Koreans. "Alas, the people of Chosŏn are hopeless! Even when an organization is formed, they are not single-minded and only engage in mutual envy and conflict (爭鬪). The people of Chosŏn are hopeless!" Such are the words (口吻) of pseudo-patriots (사이비志士) who stand aloof, sighing deeply as if they have done all they could. If it were truly impossible for the people of Chosŏn to build an ideally solid organization, that would be tantamount to saying that the people of Chosŏn can never lead a national life.

No. No. It is possible to build a solid, large organization! The method is as follows:

First, you must possess a clear conviction. Decide to dedicate your entire life to the realization of that conviction, and from that day forward, while practicing it yourself, formulate the plan you believe is most perfect for achieving that conviction. Once these three elements—conviction, plan, and feasibility of execution—<11> are established, find one comrade who agrees precisely with these three elements. Once you have confirmed that this comrade is clearly your comrade, the two of you should then seek a third person. Continue in this manner until you gain the confidence that the foundation of your organization (the size of the foundation depends on the scale of the intended project) is firmly established. After that, organize the group and recruit members widely, but again, do so one by one, only after confirming that their intentions precisely match your conviction, plan, and resolution to act. If you do this, there will surely be no conflict of opinions, and single-mindedness will surely prevail. However, if there is anyone who violates the contract (rules are a contract), expel them without any leniency. Isn't losing one member better than letting the organization die? The essential requirements for organizational life are (1) unity of ideology (主義), (2) single-mindedness (一心), (3) strict adherence to rules. If even one of these three is lacking, the organization will perish. Among these, non-adherence to rules, i.e., violation of the law, is the cause of death for organizations, from large states down to small student alumni associations. Besides these three requirements, there is another factor that seems intangible yet has terrifyingly tangible consequences: mutual trust (信) and love (愛) among members. Comrades trusting each other without any doubt and loving each other with mutual forgiveness is the fundamental morality of organizational life.

If done this way, you will not see the flourish of gaining hundreds or thousands of members within a month of initiation. However, you also won't see the farce (滑稽) where the initiation day becomes the golden age. Instead, membership will grow bit by bit, accelerating, growing geometrically (기하급수적으로), and as members increase, strength will increase. In this way, it will become an organization in the true sense, a large organization that is the national central force.

The day such a large organization emerges will be the day the national life of the Korean people begins. Whether it be nationalism (민족주의) or socialism (사회주의), <12> or assimilationism (同化主義) or autonomism (자치주의), I am not speaking of its substance. Whatever it may be, the one who forms such a large organization and becomes the national central force is the one who will represent the Chosŏn nation. Only then will the national ideal, plan, and opinion of the Chosŏn nation be known to the Koreans themselves and be communicable to others.

Some people say things like, "Such a large organization can be formed overnight if the right opportunity (機運) comes and the right person (인물) appears." Alas, this is the saying that has deceived the Korean people until now. Opportunity and personality! How much precious time has been wasted waiting idly for these! What use are a hundred thousand opportunities if there is no strength to respond and act upon them? What use are several great figures if there is no force to respond to them? For a ship whose engine preparations are complete, both high tide and low tide are opportunities (機運) to set sail, but for a ship without even preparations for sails, high tide and favorable winds have no meaning. The same applies to people. A captain exists after there is a ship; what use is a captain without a ship?

Also, a prominent figure (인물) is nothing special. It is the person who gains trust within that organization. That person is the representative of the organization and also the representative of the nation. Who would dare deny that the leader of an organization possessing the practical strength of the nation's central force is the representative of that nation's will? Therefore, I declare emphatically: Forming a large organization that serves as the central force is what brings about the opportunity (機運) for achieving national endeavors and produces great figures (인물). There is absolutely no other way to bring about opportunity and produce great figures.

Alas, it is late. Realizing this only now is extremely late. Because it is late, it must be done immediately. We must run. <13>

**Summary**

The author argues forcefully that the Korean people (Chosŏn) desperately lack a unified "central force" – a cohesive, large-scale organization with a clear ideology, plan, and dedicated membership – which is essential for achieving any national goals, whether political, social, or cultural. The piece criticizes the current fragmented state of Korean leadership and organizations, dismissing existing groups as ineffective, prone to internal conflict, lacking genuine commitment from members (equated to "inconvertible banknotes"), and failing due to poor leadership methods rather than just external oppression or popular apathy. The author outlines a meticulous, bottom-up method for building a truly strong organization based on shared conviction, rigorous vetting of members, strict adherence to rules, and mutual trust, asserting this is the only way to create a representative force, generate leaders ("인물"), and seize historical opportunities ("機運").

From a historical perspective, this article is significant as it reflects the intense debates and self-criticism within the Korean nationalist movement in the early 1920s, following the widespread but ultimately suppressed March 1st Movement (1919). It highlights the deep frustration with the fragmentation of leadership (mentioning figures like Yi Wanyong alongside independence and socialist advocates) and the perceived lack of effective organizational strategies. The call for a disciplined, ideologically unified, and financially self-sustaining organization, capable of acting as *the* representative voice of the Korean people, speaks volumes about the challenges faced under colonial rule and the search for a viable path forward, implicitly critiquing both existing moderate and radical factions for their perceived shortcomings in organization building. Historians would find the detailed critique of contemporary organizational failures and the proposed methodical approach to building a "central force" particularly interesting, revealing contemporary analyses of the movement's weaknesses and desired solutions.

곳 해야할 民族的 中心勢力의 作成
조선에는 정치적 또는 사회적 중심세력이 업다. 중심세력이 업는 민중은 민족도 아니다. 더구나 한 국민은 아니다. 한국 시대에는 왕실을 중심으로 하는 양반계급이 중심세력이엇스나 합병 후에는 조선의 민족적 중심세력은 업서지고 말앗다. 중심세력이 업슴으로 조선인은 조선이란 한 지역에 모혀 사는 數多한 개인의 떼다. 그네는 조직된 단체는 아니다. 比컨대 신경중추 업는 四肢百軆다. 팔은 팔대로 발은 발대로 놀 뿐이오 거긔는 아모 통일이 업는 것과 가티 朝鮮 민족에게는 아직 민족적 의식이 업고 딸하서 민족적 이상 민족적 의지 민족적 의견이란 것이 업다.
민족적 의식이란 우리 2천만인은 가튼 이상을 향하야 조직된 단일체라 하는 신념이다. 엇던 조선인이 다른 조선인을 대할 때에 피차에 생소한 사람이라도 서로 談話도 안이 하엿고 통성명도 안이 하엿더라도 서로 공통한 이상의 一點을 가젓스리라고 미들 수 잇는 것이 민족적 의식이다. 그리하고 민족적 이상이라던지 민족적 의지 의견이라던지는 그 중심세력되는 단체의 이상이요 의지요 의견을 가르친 것이다. 중심세력이란 天의 북극과 갓고 태양계의 태양과 가튼 것이니 衆星이 북극을 표준으로 회전하는 모양으로 태양계의 諸遊星이 태양을 표준으로 도는 모양으로 통일된 민족의 각 개인은 그 중심세력을 표준으로 意志하고 활동하는 것이다. 이러함으로 비로소 민족적 생활과 민족적 사업이 성취되는 것이다.＜4＞
민족적 중심세력이란 반듯이 그 통치주권자나 또는 정부를 의미하는 것이 안이다. 古代에는 과연 군왕이나 治者階級이 민족의 중심세력이 되엇다. 지금도 그 정부가 民意를 대표하는 동안 중심세력이 될 수 잇다. 그러나 정당한 이론으로 말하자면 정부가 민족적 중심세력을 作하는 것이 안이오 민족적 중심세력이 정부를 産하는 것이다. 민족적 중심세력이 정부의 母다.
그러나 민족적 중심세력이란 반듯이 政府的에 한한 것은 안이다. 역사적으로 보건대 종교로도 중심세력이 될 수 잇고 또 現今의 아라사 가튼 데서는 사회적 理想으로 뭉친 단체가 민족적 중심세력이 되엿다. 엇더턴지 그 민족에게 가장 합당한 다시 말하면 가장 그 민족의 마음에 드는 理想을 理想으로 하고 그 理想을 달하기 위하야 일정한 계획을 가지고 굿게 단결된 단체가 그 민족의 중심세력이 되는 것이다.
그럼으로 중심세력은 곳 굿고 큰 단체다. 단체를 이룸이 업시 결코 중심세력이 되는 법은 업다. 중심세력이 업시 결코 一民族이 통일되어 민족적 생활과 사업을 성취하는 법은 업다. 혹 개인인 영웅이나 성인이 一民族의 중심세력이 되는 것처럼 생각하는 이가 잇거니와 이것은 무식이다. 역사상에 동지를 紏合함이 업시 다수의 동지를 紏合하야 훈련하야 힘잇는 대단결이 됨이 업시 중심세력을 作한 영웅은 업다. 자기가 몸소 단결을 짓지 안이하엿스면 임의 일워진 단결을 이용이라도 하엿다. 그 가장 현저한 실례는 근대 각국의 정치적 단체다.
그런데 조선에는 嚴正한 의미에서 아직까지 이러한 중심세력이 될 단체가 업다. 이것이 조선민족의 특이한 처지 중에 중요한 特異의 하나이다. 比律賓에도 중심세력이 잇다. 比律賓 독립운동은 이 중심세력의 운동이다. 인도에도 중심세력이 잇다. 깐듸 일파의 운동은 이 중심세력의 운동이다. 이것을 깐듸 一個人의 운동으로＜5＞ 알아서는 잘못이다. 깐듸 一個人의 운동이 안이라 깐듸가 대표하는 一大團軆의 운동이다. 愛蘭에는 신펜黨이 잇다. 데 발레라는 신펜당의 수령이다. 아라사에는 볼세비키黨이 잇다. 레닌은 볼세비키黨의 대표자다. 猶太민족에는 유태교를 기초로 한 시온黨이 잇다. 그럼으로 인도의 국민의 의사를 알랴거든 깐듸에게 무르면 된다. 깐듸는 인도국민의 중심세력인 국민의회의 수령인 까닭이다. 愛蘭민족의 理想을 들으랴거든 데 발레라에게 가거라. 그의 말은 곳 愛蘭 민족의 말이다. 그러나 조선민족의 의사를 누구에게 무르뜀´. 李完用에게 무르랴 金明濬에게 무르랴. 또는 獨立主義란 東亞日報社長이나 東明主幹에게 무르랴 또는 社會主義라는 新生活雜誌社長에게 무르랴. 무르면 각각 대답은 하리라. 그러나 그것은 개인의 대답이다. 그네의 대답을 아모도 조선민족을 대표한 대답이라고 허락하지 안이할 것이다.
만일 李完用이나 金明濬이 조선민족의 중심세력이 될 만한 대단체를 대표하는 자라 하면 그의 의사를 조선민족의 의사라 할 수 잇슬 것이다. 그러나 그네는 개인이다. 개인은 오직 개인의 자격으로는 결코 민족을 대표하지 못하는 것이다. 독립주의자나 자치주의자나 사회주의자도 그와 가타서 그네의 의사가 조선민족의 의사가 되랴거든 爲先 그네의 主義로 조선민족의 중심세력이 될 만한 대단체를 만들어야 할 것이다. 엇던 신문이나 잡지도 그것이 중심세력되는 단체의 의사를 발표하는 것일 때에 비로소 그것이 여론을 대표하는 것이 될 것이다.
더욱이 조선민족은 민족적 의사를 충분히 표시하여야 할 시대에 잇다. 그것이 동화주의든지 자치론이든지 독립주의든지 사회주의든지를 물론하고 조선인은 이 중심에 어느 것 하나를 취하야 민족적 의사를 만들어야 할 것이다. 그러치 안이하면 조선은 영원히 아모 것도 안인 흐터진 개인의 무리이고 말 것이오 통일적인 민족적 생활과＜6＞ 사업은 성취할 수 업슬 것이다. 甲論乙駁에 밤낫 논쟁을 하더라도 그것은 개인의 논쟁에 불과한다. 그것으로 민족적 의사를 결정할 수는 업다. 아모리 저마다 이천만 조선민족을 대표하노라 하더라도 결코 대표가 되지 안이한다. 모스크바에 조선민족이라고 자칭하는 자가 同時에 13인이 모혓더란다. 모스크바 정부에서는 그 중에 어느 것을 진정한 조선민족으로 알아야 할지를 몰랏다. 13인은 다 엇던 단체 명의의 信任狀을 가졋다. 그러나 그 단체는 대개 命薄도 업고 회비수입도 업는 단체엿섯다. 13인은 서로 자기가 正式되기를 다토앗스나 결국은 하나도 正式이 되지 못하엿다. 그것은 당연한 일이다. 그네는 다- 조선민족의 중심세력되는 단체의 대표자가 안인 까닭이다. 아모리 신통한 경륜과 術法을 가진 이라도 중심세력되는 단체의 일흠으로 함이 안이고는 一民族을 대표할 수는 업는 것이다.
그럼으로 네가 동화주의자냐 네 목적을 달하랴거든 단체를 만들어라. 자치주의자냐 독립주의자냐 진실로 네 主義를 실현하랴거든 민족의 중심세력이 될 단체를 만들어라. 그리하지 안이하고 하는 모든 일은 허사다.
그런데 이러케 중심세력될 단체가 필요한 조선에 단체가 업다. 단체가 업시도 무슨 일이 될 것 가티 떠들고덜 다닌다. 우리 보기에 조선인은 아직 단체의 필요를 깨닷지 못한 것 갓다. 그러치 안이하면 조선민족의 각 방면의 지도자로 자처하는 이가 단체 만들기를 힘쓰지 안이할 리가 업다.
혹은 말하리라. 조선의 지도자들이 벌서부터 단체를 만들고 잇지 안이하냐고. 지나간 것은 말 말고라도 현재로부터라도 民友會
朝鮮靑年會聯合會 또다른 방면으로는 維民會
小作人相助會 또 다른 방면으로는 大東同志會
國民協會 등의 단체가 잇지 안이하냐고. 올타. 과연 그러한 단체가 잇다. 또 그 단체들이 각각 조선민족의＜7＞ 근본세력이 되랴는 自負로 적더라도 희망으로 생겻기도 할 것이다. 그러나 그 단체들이 웨 힘이 업느냐 웨 떨치지를 못하느냐. 혹은 또 말하리라. 첫재 조선인은 단결력이 업고 둘재 독립주의나 사회주의의 단체는 정부의 압박으로 동화주의나 자치주의의 단체는 인민의 不響應으로 떨치지 못하는 것이라고. 이 대답에도 과연 일리가 잇다. 조선인은 과연 아직 단체생활의 要義를 깨닷지 못하는 것도 사실이오 독립주의나 동화주의자나 사회주의자가 각각 그 主義의 실현에 큰 장애를 가진 것도 사실이다. 그러나 지도자들아 諸君은 넘우 團軆不發展의 책임을 정부와 인민에게만 전가하는 것이 안이냐. 넘우 諸君 자신의 內省自責을 피하는 것이 안이냐. 새로 실현할 主義가 잇다 하면 그것은 성질상 현재의 사회상태에는 모순될 것이다. 만일 현재의 사회상태에 모순되지 안는 主義라 하면 그것은 새삼스럽게 실현할 필요도 업슬 것이다. 정부에서도 찬성, 인민들도 일치 찬성할 그런 主義가 잇다 하면 구태 諸君의 노심초사와 磨頂放踵을 요구할 필요가 업슬 것이다. 장애가 만흔지라 실현하기에 큰 힘을 요하고 큰 힘을 요하는지라 大 團體를 요하는 것이니 만일 이 만한 곤란을 이기지 못하야 대단체를 成하지 못한다 하면 이는 諸君이 그 主義를 실현하는데 무 능력자인 것을 자백함일 것이다. 그럴진댄 맛당히 입을 닷고 붓대를 집어너코 물너가 공부나 더 하는 것이 맛당할 것이다.
지금 조선에 잇는 모든 단체가 웨 모다 龍頭蛇尾가 되느냐. 웨 發起하는 날이 전성시대가 되고 세월이 갈스록 니저버려지고 마느냐. 우리는 이러하게 한 책임을 조선인민의 죄만이라고 아니한다. 정부의 압박만이라고도 안이한다. 진실로 그 최대한 책임은 단체를 發起하는 지도자들에 잇다고 단정한다. 發起者들에게 조선인의 특이한 상태를 간파하는 明이 업고 이 특이한 상태에 응할 계획을 세울 지혜가 업시 다만 남들이 하는 양을 모방하기에＜8＞ 止하는 곳에 조선의 모든 단체가 띨치지 못하는 원인이 잇는 것이다.

그런데 우리가 단체를 만드는 방법이 엇더하냐 하면 爲先 2, 3인 또는 4, 5인이 우연히 모힌다. 우연히 話頭가 엇더한 일로 향한다. 「조타」고 모다 일치한다. 엇던 점까지 일치하엿는지 피차에 정확하게 그 정도를 알아보랴고도 안이하고 아모조록 피차의 의견이 일치한 것처럼 생각하려 한다. 그리하고 그 일치하엿다는 主旨는 다수에게는 불분명한 관념들이다. 그는 그 主義를 위하야 반듯이 일생을 헌신하리라는 素志를 가지고 온 것도 안이오 우연히 말을 들어본즉 그럴 뜻 하야 일시적 흥분으로 응낙해 버린다. 그러는 그 主義가 정확하게 일치되거나 말거나 아는 사람이면 도와 들어서 發起會를 연다. 거긔서 규칙제정위원이 생기고 다시 會가 열려 從多數로 규칙이 통과되고 임원이 선거되고 이리하야 단체가 조직된다. 趣旨書가 돌고 신문기사가 나고 그리고는 벌서 전성시대를 지내어 쇠퇴기에 入한다. 이 이것이 우리 단체들의 모형적 역사가 안이냐.

그러면 독자는 무르리라. 어느 단체는 그러케 힘잇는 단체가 되기를 원치 안이 하겟나냐고 다 원하건마는 우리 동포의 단체적 훈련이 부족한 것을 엇지 하느냐고. 이 역시 일리잇는 듯 하나 기실 책임을 자기 이외 조선인에게 전가하는 말이다. 아아 조선인은 할 수 업는 백성이로다. 단체라고 만들어야 一心은 안이하고 서로 시기하고 爭鬪만 하니 조선인은 할 수 업는 백성이로다 하고 가장 자기 할 일을 다한 듯이 高處에 서서 長嘆하는 사이비志士의 口吻이다. 만일 진실노 조선인이 이상적으로 견고한 단체를 지을 수 업다 하면 그야말로 조선인은 영영 민족적 생활을 할 수 업단 말과 가틀 것이다.

첫재로 네가 명확한 신념을 가저라. 그리고 일생을 그 신념의 실현에 바치겟다고 결심하고 너 혼자 그날부터 실행하면서 그 신념을 달하기에 가장 완전하다고 밋는 계획을 세워라. 이리하야 신념과 계획과 실행의 가능성과,＜11＞ 이 3요소가 확정되거든 꼭 이 3요소에 동의하는 동지를 하나 구하여라. 그 동지가 분명히 너와 동지인 것을 확인하거든 너희 兩人이 또 제3인을 구하여라. 이 모양으로 하기를 이제는 우리 단체의 기초(목적한 사업의 大小를 딸하 기초의 大小가)가 확립하엿다는 자신이 생길 때까지 하여라. 그런 후에 단체를 조직하고 넓히 회원을 모집하되 역시 하나씩 하나씩 꼭 그 뜻이 너희의 신념과 계획과 실행의 결심에 合致한 것을 확인한 뒤에 하여라. 이러하면 결코 의견의 충돌이 잇슬 리도 업고 一心 안이할 리도 업슬 것이다. 그러나 만일 계약(규칙은 계약이다)에 위반하는 자가 잇거든 결코 사정을 두지 말고 제명하여 버려라. 일개 회원을 失하는 것이 단체를 死하는 것보다 낫지 안이한냐. 단체생활의 요건은 (1) 主義의 일치 (2) 一心 (3) 규칙의 엄수다. 이 3者 중에 1을 缺하여도 단체는 망한다. 그 중에도 규칙의 불엄수 즉 법의 違犯은 大로는 국가와 小로는 학생동창회에 이르히 단체의 死因이다. 이 3요건 외에 또 하나 無形한 듯 하면서도 그 결과는 무섭게 有形한 것은 회원 상호간의 信과 愛다. 동지 서로 미더 결코 의심함이 업고 서로 사랑하야 피차에 용서하는 것은 단체생활의 근본도덕이다.
