Magazine Title: 개벽 제34호
Publication Date: 1923년 04월 01일
Article Title: 各自爲大將, (現下 우리 思潮界의 裡面의 一部를 論함) (Each Becomes a General, (Discussing a Part of the Inner Aspect of Our Current World of Thought))
Author: 八克生
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

各自爲大將,
						八克生
The most detestable are those who practice hypocrisy, those with thick self-esteem who possess a self-aggrandizing habit lacking self-confidence, and the failure to unite where unity is needed. At least, that is what I think.
However, observing closely, we recently fail too much to unite, engage too much in hypocrisy, and have too much of a habit of exaggeration. Each claims to be a genius; each is a hero and a great man. Of course, I do not think this isn't a precursor to producing geniuses or that it won't serve as a nursemaid fostering heroes and great men, nor do I deny that the entire universe is currently buried in hypocrisy, and consequently, that this might not be the bait leading to happiness. But, on the other hand, we cannot help but ponder how great the resulting harm truly is. My close friend D-gun once uttered the satirical masterpiece that the happiest life in the world lies in becoming a megalomaniac. Indeed, I cannot but say this is a masterpiece mocking the current thought trends of our Korean people. Truly, for oneself as an individual, a life without anguish, only pleasure, might be unobtainable except through megalomania, but for society, which is a lump formed by individuals gathered together, this cannot be tolerated. And the habit of exaggeration, backed by hypocrisy, ruled by a sound mind that is not insane, not megalomaniac, is a poisonous, gut-wrenching delusion. Hypocrisy, exaggeration, and the failure to unite add fuel to the fuse leading to all sorts of conflicts like antagonism, jealousy, curses, cunning, envy, mutual insults, mutual strife, leading even to mutual killing and destruction, and by evoking empty thoughts like castles in the air built in one's chest or mirages, they cause people to wander endlessly in a labyrinth.

What disaster lies hidden in our current world of thought? Since the independence movement arose exactly four years ago from now, stimulated by it, our world of thought has developed rapidly enough to be remarkable, and various social movements arose all at once, gradually intensifying. This is truly something we cannot contain our joy about, but if we examine its inner side, it is not something that ends merely in joy; rather, flaws emerge that make us unable to suppress our dismay <47>. This is precisely the crudeness (麤態) produced amidst the extreme prevalence of failure to unite, detestable hypocrisy, and exaggeration. The entire world of thought has lost its track and entered a labyrinth, surging like a tide that has missed its time, and accordingly, all social movements have fallen into a state of fashion, like a kind of decorative item that becomes momentarily popular. And even more chilling among them is that all movements are based on egoism, and all kinds of thought originate from self-centeredness (爲我主義).

In one corner, the founding of a religion; in another corner, the publication of a magazine; on the right, a lecture meeting; on the left, a concert; in the East, a drama festival; in the West, a new poetry reading; here, a relief society; there, a fellowship meeting, a temperance society, an anti-smoking society; regionally, lecture tours, music tours, drama tours; male student associations, female student associations, etc., this association, that association; for children, for offspring, for women, for wives, this project, that project—truly, all these countless social movements become fleeting trends in the blink of an eye. Of course, I am not saying these are not excellent movements or tremendous undertakings, nor that they shouldn't be done, but considering that the majority of them arise from fleeting emotions of the moment, from shallow thoughts without foundation, the sorrow is immeasurable.

Looking only at the surface, it seems as if philosophers, religious figures, writers, artists, musicians, orators, philanthropists, and benefactors occupy the vast majority of our people, and great development enabling us to stand ahead of others appears imminent. However, gamblers are also religious figures, even within the mouths of public prostitutes are religious figures; one smokes a cigarette and then founds a religion; one lifts a cup of alcohol and then propagates a doctrine. Elementary school students publish magazines, middle school students are poets, elementary school students are novelists, kisaeng are thinkers, female students are philosophers; even in kindergartens, there is art research; in restaurants, music research; in high-class restaurants, literary magazines; in brothels (靑樓), journals of opinion; prostitutes (賣淫女) are orators; thieves and robbers are philanthropists; swindlers (詐欺漢) are benefactors; drunkards (酒酊軍) join temperance societies; tobacco sellers promote anti-smoking. How much great harm will this shameless emergence, this self-aggrandizement lacking self-confidence, steeped in thick hypocrisy, inflict? Or how much of a laughingstock will it become to others? Now, let's dissect their minds and look into their ideals—Ch'ŏndogyo seems to be wielding great influence, so let's found a religion; some type of magazine is said to have many readers, so let's publish a magazine; poets and novelists are gaining fame, so let's write poetry, let's write novels; concerts are said to bring in much income, so let's hold a concert; endeavors for women make it easy to get married; regional lecture tours broaden connections and gain influence, helping one acquire advantageous power for one's future <48> life, etc. Everything is self-centered, yet under the pretext of it being a grand project for society, they think it cannot but be done or that they have a duty to do it, without reflecting one bit on their lack of self-confidence. Their ideals are like this, and where did this arise from, and what has produced this state of chaos? Or what kind of phenomenon doomed to die young is this? Indeed, if we think about how many tenths possess truly confident aspirations, setting aside the habit of exaggeration and hypocrisy, it is a clear fact that we cannot but be astonished.

There are barefaced individuals (철면피) who put up a sign calling a movie magazine a purely literary magazine; there are also "scholars" (샘님) who attach the annotation "mechanical art" to airplanes and submarines and present it as a theory of art, whom we cannot but praise as unprecedented great artists; there are bold playwrights who call Japanese Rakugo translations artistically creative plays; mandolin playing with whistling is called a music festival; kindergarten recreational gymnastics (遊戱軆操) are called opera galas; there are students who take high-class pimps (高等뚜쟁이) and clandestine prostitutes (密賣淫女) around as speakers for lecture tours; there are female orators who deliver the "Nanbongga" (난봉가 - Song of the Playboy) from the podium of a women's lecture meeting, calling it a lecture; there are drunkards who hide temperance pamphlets in their pockets while downing 300 cups a day. What contradiction is this, what confusion, what hypocrisy, what habit of exaggeration?

The conflict arising from this grows fiercer day by day. Without reflecting one iota (一毫半些) on their lack of real ability (實力不充), only envy expands, trying to bury anyone slightly superior to them, and slandering peers comparable to themselves to suppress them. Meeting Kim, they slander Lee; meeting Lee, they speak ill of Kim; Pak's pen refutes Ch'oe; Ch'oe's pen criticizes Pak—this is the daily routine in a part of our current world of thought.

This kind of envious infighting is not limited to one or two but is countless in number; this is the partial state of our current society. Of course, I am not denying that I too acknowledge it is inevitable for any society entering a period of reform and creation to fall into such phenomena, but I am expressing my hope that these chaotic dark clouds will clear away quickly, even if for a moment.

Finally, I wish for us to remember Shakespeare's words: "To thine own self be true, And it must follow, as the night the day, Thou canst not then be false to any man." <49> <47-49>

**Summary**

The author, P'alkŭksaeng, writing in 1923, delivers a sharp critique of the Korean intellectual and social scene four years after the March 1st Movement. He laments the pervasive hypocrisy, boastfulness ("each becomes a general"), lack of unity, and superficiality plaguing various emerging movements—from religion and publishing to arts and social reform. He argues that these activities are often driven by self-interest (egoism, fame, profit, personal connections) rather than genuine conviction or ability, leading to chaos, infighting (mutual slander and envy), and a society full of hollow posturing that invites ridicule. The author sees this as a potentially unavoidable but harmful phase in a period of change and urges self-reflection and sincerity, quoting Shakespeare. For historians, this article provides a valuable contemporary perspective on the internal challenges and growing pains within the Korean nationalist and cultural movements during the colonial period. It reveals disillusionment with the fragmentation and perceived lack of substance among intellectuals and activists, highlighting the social dynamics, anxieties, and rampant self-promotion that followed the initial surge of post-March 1st Movement energy, possibly hindering unified progress.

이로부터 일어나는 갈등은 나날이 熾盛하야 간다. 자기의 實力不充은 一毫半些도 도라보지 안이하고 한갓 시기만 팽창하야저서 자기보다 조곰이라도 우등한 자는 葬事지내려 하고 자기와 비견하는 同類는 중상하야 壓頭코자 한다. 金을 만나면 李의 중상 李를 만나면 金의 험담 朴의 붓끗은 催의 논박 催의 붓끗은 朴의 악평 이것이 現今 우리의 일부 思潮界의 일과이다.
이와 가튼 시기적 암투가 한둘에 끈이지 안이하고 그 수효를 헤일 수 업는 것은 現今 우리 사회의 일부상태이다. 물론 나 역시 엇더한 사회이던지 개조와 창설의 期에 入하야서는 이와 가튼 현상에 빠질 것은 필연한 事이라고 시인하는 바가 안인 것은 안이지만은 이와 가튼 혼돈의 검은 구름이 일시라도 속히 것쳐가기를 기대한다는 것을 말하는 것이다.
