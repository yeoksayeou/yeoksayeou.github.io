Modern Literature and Art
						An Sŏ

No matter how tumultuous external life may be, if only ideals and faith possess some deep foundation, it would truly be a world of peace and tranquility. However, as long as external life is complex and a combative existence with no room for leisure, it goes without saying that difficult-to-solve economic problems inevitably trouble the minds of many people. Thus, considerable anxiety and pressure in internal life drive everyone towards the battlefield of desperate struggle (惡戰苦鬪). In modern times, it has become impossible to hide alone in the sole refuge of faith and ideals as people of the past did, building walls against the outside world, cutting oneself off, forgetting the fleeting dreams of the mundane world (塵世), and leisurely wandering (逍遙自適) in a quiet, separate world (別乾坤的 世界). This is none other than because modern civilization, which takes natural science as its foundation to some extent, denies and rejects all ideals, all faith, and even the standards of morality. In other words, the minds of people in this chaotic world do not know the way back home (歸路) and merely struggle, embracing thoughts of anxiety and fear. In such minds, there must inevitably be pessimism (厭世), sorrow (悲哀), doubt (懷疑), and anguish (煩悶). Where there is distress (懊惱) and despair (絶望), there cannot but be a kind of pathological, unhealthy mood (情的) of the era. Truly, the internal and external life of modern Europe was miserable (慘憺) beyond description. It seems that the bright hope that floated before one's eyes momentarily became the dark despair of a dream (夢想). Now, this tendency towards doubt and agony is called the so-called La mal du siècle (the chronic malady of the century). This pathological chronic malady (痼疾) also differs somewhat in its purport (趣義) according to the times; the malady of the age of scientific omnipotence and the malady after the age of scientific omnipotence passed are slightly different. Such things, of course, being related to the internal life of humankind, are different from historical matters based on concrete facts, so, strictly speaking, one cannot define <126> precise boundaries. It merely amounts to saying that there are some distinctions depending on the era. In other words, as the eyes of humanity, which had lived a slave-like existence receiving limitations and restraints, gradually turned towards some new place and sent forth their gaze, the scene that had been full of only light (光明) gradually began to show its dark side (暗黑面), and when it reached the era called 'scientific omnipotence' where everything was said to be merely mechanical, it became even darker. Thus, doubt deepened further, and thereafter, doubt begot more doubt, and agony begot more agony, proceeding step by step towards the abyss (深淵) the further one went. Accordingly, the doubt and agony in their hearts (胸中) deepened their foundations, and a certain clear scope (明白한 範圍) also emerged. Thinking about this historically again, there was the French Revolution. Needless to say, the unprecedented Great Revolution in France caused Europe, which had carried the old burden of convention (因習), to completely uproot its conventional social system. It struck down the authority of religion and morality, as well as all standards, in one blow, and sought only to highly establish the spirit of freedom (自由). Because of this, the people of Europe lost their way back home (歸路) and were anguished. The result of anguish cannot but be doubt and pessimism. Next, there is a political cause. That is, the twenty-odd years from the early period (初期) of the Great Revolution until the downfall (滅亡) of that hero of egoism (利己主張), Napoleon, saw war year after year, month after month. In the end, the turmoil of war (戰亂) that destroyed the lives of the people gave nothing more to all of Europe than extreme fatigue (疲勞) and extreme consumption of energy (精力消費). It is the way of things that after passing a certain degree (度數) of anything, one inevitably falls into deep thought and contemplation (沈思黙慮). After passing through the balmy spring days and flower-like summer nights, the coming of autumn is the sequence (順序) of the four seasons. In autumn, more than anything, one engages in meditation and contemplation (黙想冥思). Similarly, the result of hitherto holding blades against each other and continuing the turmoil of war caused the people of Europe to quietly look back on what they had passed through and to think about the future to come. That thought would not have been such a good one. Thinking about having cried out for political freedom and advocated (標榜) for the fundamental improvement (改善) of the social system must also have been desolate, like a fleeting spring dream (春夢) one wakes from into loneliness (寂寞). It was fine as long as they were fighting for ideals, but when these were not realized (實現), their hearts (心胸) must have been pained. Just as there is loneliness in success (成功), so too is there the disappointment (落望) of failure (失敗) in failure. If the former can be called loneliness amidst joy, the latter cannot but be called the despair of despair. What they obtained was the despair of despair. <127> Needless to say, the mind, which had hitherto been tense (緊張) in this way or that, would suddenly lose its vigor (元氣), and only the boundless feeling of pessimism (厭世) and sorrow (悲哀) would remain. Thus, this chaotic, pessimistic tendency filled the world of Europe.

This mood of pessimism and world-weariness (悲觀, 厭世) first appeared in poetry and song (詩歌). Of course, in the poetry of England, France, and Germany, these songs of chaotic pessimism were prominent. The poetry of Byron, called the king of the poetic world, began passionate pessimism (情熱的 厭世) in England and later raised its voice (風聲) high on the European continent (大陸). With just the poetic work "Manfred," a masterpiece (絶調) of agony and the sorrow of pessimism, he must have stirred the hearts of the people at that time. One by one, such poets emerged on the European continent: in France, poets like Lamartine, and in Germany, poets like Heine, all received the influence (影響) of the demonic, passionate English poet Byron, and they responded loudly in concert (和動). Appearing around the time of the French Revolution and resonating (共鳴) with the acute sorrow (痛切한 悲哀) of the people's hearts of that era were works such as Goethe's "The Sorrows of Young Werther" in Germany, and in France, the elegant de Musset's "Confession of a Child of the Century" or Chateaubriand's "René." Besides these, there was the novel "Obermann" written in epistolary style (書翰文軆) conveying thoughts of sorrow and pessimism, Senancour, or the lyric poet (敍情詩人) de Vigny, Leopardi, the poet called Lenau, known as the German Byron bearing the name "Poet of Weltschmerz (世界苦)" – to speak of such poets of sorrow, they would not be few. Somehow, the world of Europe was filled with the literature of pessimism and sorrow. Speaking from the perspective of philosophy (哲學), the representative of the trend of thought (思潮) in this area is the pessimistic philosophy of Germany's Schopenhauer, who is almost called the origin (本源) of the modern era. This philosopher said: The world is the manifestation (發現) of the Will (意志). Because that Will is blind (盲目的) and unconscious (無意識的), it is not subject to the control (支配) of reason (理性) or intellect (智力) at all. That water tries to pierce hard stone, that metal and stone (金石) try to fall to the earth – none of this is not a manifestation of the Will. And that plants and animals (動植物) use all sorts of means (手段) to grow (生長) and develop (發育) to preserve (保全) their lives (生命) is also all a manifestation of the Will. Planning the propagation (繁殖) of the species (種族) even by sacrificing (犧牲) one's own life is likewise all a manifestation of the Will. However, that thing called Will is an effort (努力) to assert (主張) itself and display (發揮) its individuality (個性) without any purpose (目的). In humankind (人類), this Will is most developed and progressed (發達進步), yet that thing called Will never knows satisfaction (滿足), always wanting to obtain something else like 'having obtained Long, one covets Shu' (得隴望蜀). This blind and unconscious Will has absolutely no ultimate (究竟) satisfaction. Accordingly, anguish (煩悶) adds to anguish, and sorrow (悲哀) adds to sorrow. Thus, one needlessly always feels deficiency (缺乏), competes (競爭), and fails to attain stability (安定). As long as this thing called Will exists, seeking happiness (幸復) and comfort (安樂) in the present world is foolish. However, through sympathy (同情) alone, humans can avoid experiencing suffering (苦患) for a while, but this too, of course, cannot continue for long. Thus, more than anything, fundamentally (根本的) eliminating the Will is paramount. <128> As long as Will and individuality exist, pain is unavoidable. If one wishes to eliminate pain, one must first eliminate the Will. This requires asceticism (禁慾). Suppress (制抑) everything and enter into the realm of solitude/nirvana (寂寞鄕), the liberation (脫解) of *nirvana* – this was the gist (大意) of his philosophy.

Now, the influence (影響) this philosophy had on all of Europe was not small. From here, I intend to speak about Religion (宗敎). Of course, since it has a great influence, directly and indirectly, in controlling (支配) the hearts of the people of an era more than anything else, one cannot but know the general outline. The Religion spoken of here is Christianity (基督敎). Everyone knows that Christianity controlled the hearts of all European people for a long time. Until now, everything had been followed within the scope of its doctrines. However, regarding those doctrines, first Strauss's "Life of Jesus" (基督傳) added a great attack (攻擊), and next, Feuerbach's "The Essence of Christianity" (基督敎本質論) launched a fervent (熱烈) attack. Of course, in a word, it can be said to be a conflict (衝突) between the new scientific spirit and the old faith (舊信仰). Now, these two men criticized (批評) Christianity refreshingly from a scientific and historical perspective. They asserted that the Gospels (福音) in the Bible (聖經) were not facts at all but rather the fantasies (空想) and falsehoods of ancient peoples. Things like the poetry of the Old Testament (舊約) were said to be nothing more than expressions arising from the pointless, unconscious zeal (熱心) of believers (信者). They claimed that miracles (異蹟) or unnatural (不自然) facts absolutely do not exist in reality (實際), and that Christ, who connected (連結) the infinite and the infinite, God and man, ultimately (畢竟) amounted to nothing more than the old Christ of faith, and was not a historical fact by any measure. Furthermore, they argued that what is originally called God is a hypothetical personification (假想的 擬人) created within the human mind (頭腦), and that such an omniscient and omnipotent (全智全能) God certainly could not exist. In this sense, they stated that apart from humanity, there is no great God, and only oneself becomes God. They argued that if people realized this, they would certainly not believe in the hypothetical personification that is God, but would come to believe in themselves – this was the gist (大意) of their assertion.

Of course, in European society (社會), which came to hear such words, it must have seemed as if the heavens were collapsing and the earth splitting. Consequently, as their faith, which had continued up to that point, gradually weakened, the anguish (煩悶) of their internal life grew ever greater. However, if I were to speak further on such matters, there would be no limit (限定), so selecting only the very outline (大網), I intend to speak only of what must be known concerning its relation to modern literature and art. In any case, the internal life of the European mind could not help but enter the crucible (坩堝) of instability, agony, doubt – in a word, the modern mood of pessimism (近代的 悲觀의 情調). Next, just as there is inevitably a result (結果) after a cause, I intend to speak of the tendency (傾向) to enter the inner side (內面) of the pessimistic mind, not believing in anything, but only doubting and believing in and asserting oneself to the utmost. (To be continued) <129>

**Summary**

This article by An Sŏ discusses the pervasive mood of pessimism, doubt, and anxiety ("mal du siècle") in modern Europe, tracing its roots to the complex and demanding nature of modern external life, the rise of science challenging traditional faith and ideals, the disillusionment following the French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars, and the influence of key literary and philosophical figures. It highlights how thinkers like Schopenhauer and critics of Christianity like Strauss and Feuerbach dismantled old certainties, leaving individuals grappling with a sense of meaninglessness, internal conflict, and a turn towards radical skepticism and self-reliance, all reflected in the era's literature (Byron, Goethe, etc.). The article portrays modern European consciousness as trapped in a "crucible" of despair, setting the stage for further discussion (indicated by "未完"). From a colonial Korean history perspective, this article is interesting as it shows an intellectual engaging deeply with contemporary European philosophical and literary trends in 1921. It reflects the influx of Western thought into Korea and attempts by Korean intellectuals to understand and perhaps apply these concepts to their own situation, even if the direct connection to Korea isn't made in this unfinished segment. The focus on disillusionment and the crisis of meaning could resonate with the colonial experience, though the author frames it purely in a European context here.

近代文藝
						岸曙
