Magazine Title: 개벽 신간 제3호
Publication Date: 1935-01-01
Article Title: 母子 (Mother and Son)
Author: 姜敬愛
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

This morning, as snow fell thick and fast, Sŭngho's mother bundled up the young Sŭngho, groaning with whooping cough, and went outside. As she passed the Chinese shop, she looked back on herself just days before, having quit her job as a hired woman and rushed distractedly back to her parents' home like someone chased out. And today, having fought with her stepmother, she was leaving her parents' home again, feeling like she truly had nowhere left to go. She had thought, never mind her stepmother, but surely her father, seeing his own daughter, would let her stay for a few months, if not a few years, at least until Sŭngho recovered from his whooping cough. But she tearfully realized he was no better than Aehi's family, who were complete strangers. Where could she go? She stopped abruptly. People hurried past her. Staring blankly at the sky, she realized she had no choice now but to bow her head and enter the house of her husband's older brother (sihyŏng), whom she had treated like an enemy. Just thinking about it made her whole body tremble like a cow entering a slaughterhouse,<11> and she truly couldn't bring herself to take a step. But then again, although her husband was dead, wasn't Sŭngho here to carry on his line? Wasn't Sŭngho a more advantageous factor at her brother-in-law's house than at her parents'? A nephew is still a child, isn't he? Yes, let's go! she thought, forcing her feet forward. Moreover, since her brother-in-law had just opened a pharmacy, though it was difficult to enter, it seemed Sŭngho's cough might heal quickly if they just went inside. She gained courage. Whatever insults they hurl, let's just swallow them, she resolved, putting strength into her slowing steps. But the hook-like eyes of her sister-in-law (tongse) and the pumpkin-seed eyes of her brother-in-law kept trying to turn her feet back.

Before the Manchurian Incident, her brother-in-law had revered her husband as if he were heaven, and so he had uncomplainingly provided for their living expenses. But once the Manchurian Incident occurred and Yongjŏng society drastically changed, her brother-in-law's heart changed too. He cursed his terribly cherished younger brother day and night and treated her and her son just the same. Because he stopped providing any living expenses, Sŭngho's mother had had to become a hired woman for others. Especially when news came a year ago that her husband had died in a distant land, the brother-in-law had actually seemed pleased, which made Sŭngho's mother flare up with all her anger, leading to a huge fight. After that, she had completely cut off contact. Knowing full well that her brother-in-law and his wife would certainly not be pleased to see her bowing her head and entering today, she couldn't help but hesitate and waver like this.

Sŭngho, who had been lying quietly on her back, suddenly lifted his head and let out that awful cough again. Then he made a sound as if he couldn't catch his breath, overcome by the coughing fit. She quickly shifted Sŭngho to her front, held him close, pressed her cheek against his, and trembled violently.

"Sŭngho! My baby!"

Distraught, she pressed her lips to Sŭngho's mouth like this and inhaled deeply. She did this whenever Sŭngho coughed, wishing his whooping cough would transfer to her. Then,<12> if Sŭngho didn't cough for a while, she would anxiously wonder if her inhaling his breath had worked, only to inevitably face another coughing fit. Only after Sŭngho's coughing subsided a bit did she start walking again. Before she knew it, she had turned the corner by her brother-in-law's wall. She stopped short. What should she say if her brother-in-law asked why she came? "I've come to live..." Should she say that? Or what? Should she just stay silent? No, she had to tell them she quit her job as a hired woman. But what if they didn't know about that house either? She felt dizzy as if falling into some abyss and turned back. If it was going to be like that, maybe it was better not to go in at all. But then there was nowhere to go. Should she just find someone's house to sleep at tonight and go somewhere else once the snow stops? Recalling the scene of being chased out of Aehi's house, she thought, who else would let us mother and son stay even one night? Who besides me would welcome my Sŭngho, afflicted with this dreadful cough? Considering the child's value, perhaps the brother-in-law is still the better option. Let's go and try talking to them. Surely they won't kick us out. She moved her feet again. Her steps grew heavier, increasingly hesitant. And what if Sŭngho coughed while she was sitting face-to-face talking with her brother-in-law? What if he felt a little sympathy and was about to let them stay, but then got alarmed by the cough and flatly refused? Maybe she should wait until Sŭngho coughs and then go in. She stood stock-still, waiting eagerly for Sŭngho to cough quickly.

She wasn't unaware that staying outside for long on such a windy, snowy day was harmful to Sŭngho, but she couldn't help hesitating and feeling anxious like this.

"Sŭngho, you have to hold back your cough in front of your big uncle. You must."

She pleaded almost desperately, lightly patting the back of Sŭngho, who lay on her back as if asleep. She stopped short. Her brother-in-law's gate seemed to flash before her eyes. And the newly painted gate seemed to tell her she hadn't been here in a long time. Clutching her pounding chest, she hesitated again. At that moment, the gate suddenly opened, and her brother-in-law's daughter, Chilyŏ, a teacher at H Common School, glanced back and forth<13> as she came out. Sŭngho's mother flinched and stepped back.

"Oh, auntie, it's been a long time..."

The soft fur collar of her coat brushed lightly against her snow-white face.

"Have you been well..."

Just looking at Chilyŏ made her bow her head, her words catching in her throat.

"Come on in. Is Sŭngho sleeping?"

Chilyŏ made a motion to come closer but then stepped back again.

"I have to run an errand over there. Auntie, please go inside. Don't leave before I get back, okay, auntie?"

Chilyŏ's voice was exceedingly cheerful. At that moment, she thought of grabbing Chilyŏ and explaining her situation, but Chilyŏ finished speaking, smiled brightly, turned, and left. Having no choice, she stepped inside the gate. Perhaps hearing her footsteps, her sister-in-law's hook-like eyes appeared at the glass window. Her face flushed hot, feeling as if confronting an enemy.

"Well, what is this? Is there ever a time you come to our house?"

Sliding open the door (미다지) and looking out, the sister-in-law greeted her with thinly veiled anger. Without a word, she entered the room and sat down. The smell of medicine wafted strongly, and the room's warmth enveloped her cold cheek. Worried that Sŭngho might cough, she pulled the ragged blanket over his head and sat anxiously.

"So, you said you were going to earn money, you must have saved a lot... People are useless. Do you still think you did the right thing?"

The sister-in-law picked up her long pipe and filled it with tobacco.

"I was wrong."<14>

"And even if you fight, once the anger subsides, the younger person should apologize. Is that any way for a junior to treat a senior, not setting foot here for over a year?"

Hearing the apology, the sister-in-law seemed somewhat mollified and said this. Sŭngho's mother barely suppressed the urge to cry aloud, feeling her own heart was indeed narrow. She was moved. From the upper room (웃방), the loud voice of her brother-in-law could be heard amidst the voice of a Chinese person who seemed to have come to buy medicine. She decided to confess everything and plead her case now that her sister-in-law's anger had softened slightly. She tried to bring up the subject, but only tears came first, and she couldn't open her mouth. Just then, Sŭngho lifted his head and let out another coughing fit. His mother didn't know what to do and fidgeted helplessly.

"Isn't that child's cough whooping cough?"

The sister-in-law's eyes narrowed instantly. She had guessed why Sŭngho's mother had come.

"There's no medicine for whooping cough. When did he get sick with it?"

Hearing there was no medicine, Sŭngho's mother was terrified, her face turning deathly pale. So, Sŭngho has no choice but to die! The thought made her head spin.

"You should have taken better care of the child. Does anyone in the house you were staying at have it (알른가)?"

"N-no."

"Then wouldn't they dislike it at that house too?"

"I've left for good!"

As she finished speaking, she finally burst into tears. The sister-in-law turned away sharply.

"Whooping cough is contagious, who would like that?"<15>

At first, the sister-in-law had thought she only came for medicine, but now she realized she had come because she had nowhere else to stay. Suddenly, a feeling of contempt arose in her, along with the anger that had been subsiding.

"Hmph, you didn't set foot here when times were good, but now that your brat is dying and you have nowhere to stay, you come? We don't know you. Why did you storm out so haughtily, only to come back less than a year later? We can't stand such sights. You have your parents' house, don't you? Go there, or get remarried. Didn't we sever ties back then?"

She tapped the ashtray sharply with her pipe. Sŭngho's mother felt as if her cheeks had been slapped; her whole face hurt. She bit her lip firmly, intending to plead one more time.

"What am I to do! Please forgive me just this once."

"Hmph! Forgiveness, forgiveness, how much is that worth? We don't know you."

At that moment, the sliding door to the upper room opened, and the brother-in-law's face appeared.

"Why are you making such a racket!"

He shouted, his eyes rolling.

"Well, she acted like she'd never face us again, but now that her brat is sick and she has nowhere to stay, she's come again."

"I don't want to hear it!"

The brother-in-law snapped and slammed the sliding door shut again. Even her brother-in-law, her last thread of hope, was like this. She jumped up abruptly.

"Live well."

She rushed outside as if crazed. She reached the street distractedly. The snow was still falling silently, heavily.<16> She stopped short. She couldn't help but resent her husband. But she immediately regretted it. Her husband, who hadn't slept properly or eaten a satisfying meal, wandering through mountains and fields until captured and killed by the enemy—to resent him, she felt herself to be a truly pitiful woman.

What had her husband said before he went to the mountains? He had said that no matter how hard we try to live well, we cannot. At the time, she hadn't understood, but the more she lived (사록), the more his words seemed right. No, they were right. We must teach Sŭngho the same... her husband's words. Ah, what should she do, having lost that husband? When her husband was alive, no matter the hardship, hope never left her. But now, what hope could she have? Only darkness lay ahead.

She stood stock-still. Thinking these thoughts, her husband's eyes, his mouth, his face kept appearing, and she wanted to cry out loud. She stared blankly at the falling snowflakes. Then, wondering if perhaps the snowflakes could be medicine for the cough, she opened her mouth wide and caught them. At that moment, she vaguely recalled the smell of medicine from her brother-in-law's room and felt the snowflakes, sharp on the tip of her tongue. Then, recalling the heartless words of her sister-in-law, her eyes opened fiercely. In the next instant, gripped by the fear that Sŭngho's cough would worsen from exposure to this dreadful wind, she took off the scarf from her head and covered Sŭngho with it. And she walked. Where to? Anywhere. Let's just get out of Yongjŏng. This heartless Yongjŏng, no, this Yongjŏng inhabited only by money-grubbers! This Yongjŏng that drives out mother and son! Just leave this Yongjŏng! People in the same plight as her and her son wouldn't treat them cruelly, she thought. Thinking this, the mountain her husband first left for suddenly came to mind (생각키운다). "No, where are you going? Just tell me," she had asked anxiously. Her husband sat silently for a moment, then said, "To the mountains." "Which mountain?" "Just know it's the mountains..."<17>

From then on, she had looked affectionately at the distant mountains, and her heart would pound whenever she heard anyone mention anything about mountains. Mountains! Her husband must have gone to some mountain, though she didn't know which one, that much was certain. And so, until his death, he must have moved from mountain to mountain before being captured by X [likely referring to Japanese authorities or collaborators]. She lifted her eyes. That mountain, barely visible, buried in snowflakes, that mountain appearing hazily like a dream—it seemed the only place for her and her son to go was to follow her husband to that mountain.

"Let's go, Sŭngho! Follow your father!"

Overcome with emotion, she spoke thus. Thinking this way, it felt as if she might encounter her husband's remains if she went to that mountain, and perhaps hear the last words he left for her and her son. Strength surged through her. Snowflakes fell and fell again on her burning cheeks.

After a while, she looked around. All she could see was the endless field buried in snow; it seemed there was no one else around. Only her and her son, and the mountain that gave them hope. But for some reason, the further forward she went, the hazier the mountain seemed. And then, her face, battered by the snowstorm, stung unbearably. Following that, the fever that seemed to burn throughout her body vanished without a trace, and regretting that she had been chasing a pointless illusion, she looked back. Yongjŏng was no longer visible; it seemed she had already come about 2 or 3 *li*.

She considered turning back. But it felt like she would freeze to death before reaching Yongjŏng. She turned around again. Let's go as far as we can. If there's a house, let's sleep there and figure out tomorrow, but for now, let's just go. She moved her feet, searching for where a house might be. Now she realized it was definitely getting dark, making her feel even more rushed. And she wandered, searching for houses. Sŭngho coughed severely several times. Paying no attention to the cough, she only searched for houses.<18>

This wasn't her first time on this road; she knew it well. She expected a village after this pass, but somehow, even after crossing the pass, there were no houses, just fields buried in snow. After wandering like this for quite some time, realizing she must have taken a wrong turn, she opened her eyes wide and looked around carefully, but she couldn't figure out where she was. There was only the white snow, shining enough to cause dizziness in the terrifying wind, no, shaking her head. She stopped abruptly. And she brought her hand to her eyes. She meant to rub them. But her hand felt like it was made of wood and wouldn't move as she willed. She came sharply to her senses. The thought flashed like lightning: wasn't she dying right now? She tried moving her hands and feet, calling Sŭngho's name. But realizing this was no time for that, she walked forward. Just then, she thought she saw something like houses far away and ran frantically towards them. But it wasn't houses after all, just a few pillars covered in snow. She was startled. She recognized this site as the ruins of a horse-cart station (마차 정유소). But only a few pillars remained like this? She quickly remembered hearing that most houses in the countryside had burned down during the subjugation raids (토벌난), and all the strength drained from her body. She didn't know what to do. She looked for the Chinese house that used to stand nearby with its high earthen wall. That house wasn't visible either. She took a few steps forward and looked, but it still wasn't there. Definitely gone.

The wind seemed to be dying down a little, but the snow fell heavier. And the snow piled on the ground passed her knees. Looking at the pillars, wondering what to do, she thought, "Ah well, if I die, I die, if I live, I live, let's go!" She clenched her teeth and walked. But somehow, her vision darkened, and she kept feeling like she was about to fall. And her rubber shoes, who knows when or where they came off, she was now barefoot in just her socks (버선발), and snow clung to them like sticky rice cake, heavy as a thousand pounds (천근), impossible to shake off no matter how hard she tried, only accumulating more bit by bit, leaving her helpless. And snow dust whitened her hair and eyebrow tips, and her lips too. She ran. Only her thoughts ran; her body stood still in the same place.<19>

Suddenly feeling faint, she slipped, and snow rushed into her eyes, nose, and mouth, suffocating her. She instantly realized she was falling into some ditch or stream, and the thought struck her—I'm dying! I'm really dying! She flailed her arms, trying to grab onto something. But she only grasped soft clumps of snow; nothing solid could be found. She tried to scream, straining her throat. But it seemed she had fallen as far as she could, and she finally came to a stop.

First, she tried to make a space to breathe by pushing the snow away from her head with her hands. But the more she cleared a hole, the more snow pressed down from above. At that moment, suddenly fearing Sŭngho might have suffocated and died buried in the snow, she held Sŭngho tight and kept using her head to push against the snow near him, making a space. Snow seeped under her hair, turning into icy water that coiled down her neck like a snake. Fearing this water would seep onto Sŭngho, she shook her head left and right so it would soak into her jacket collar. But the streams of water seeped in here and there. Her strength gave out completely. And the thought came again: We, mother and son, are truly dying! At that moment, she thought of her husband's death. His death wasn't from being unable to escape from snow like this, nor from the sea, nor from some ditch or stream. "No matter how hard we try to live, in the end, we cannot live, and we die." Her husband's words. Yes! That's right! How hard had she tried to live? Still, since one is human, surely one can live. Surely one won't die—clinging to such foolish hope, day after day, she struggled, only to end up dying in snow like this. Her husband's death and the impending death of her and her son—what a different kind of death it was.

In the heat of the moment (엇껼에), she called her son's name, resolving never to let this son become a person like herself. And she resolved that this son would complete the work his father left unfinished.

"Sŭngho!" Her heart overflowing, she couldn't help but cry out (불우지) his name like this. And she suddenly felt strongly that something like being trapped in snow was nothing to fear... (End)... <20>

**Summary**

A destitute mother, Sŭngho's mother, is forced to leave her parents' home with her young son who is suffering from whooping cough. Having previously fallen out with her deceased husband's brother (sihyŏng) and sister-in-law (tongse) after her husband's death (implied to be related to anti-colonial activities), she reluctantly seeks shelter at their house in Yongjŏng, which now includes a pharmacy. They reject her due to the son's contagious illness and her past defiance. Desperate, she leaves Yongjŏng, heading towards the mountains her husband disappeared into, hoping to follow his path. Caught in a blizzard, she falls into a snowdrift and, facing death, reflects on her husband's words about the impossibility of living well under their circumstances and resolves that her son will live differently and fulfill his father's purpose.

From a historical perspective, the story starkly portrays the harsh realities faced by Korean migrants in Manchuria (specifically Yongjŏng) during the Japanese colonial period, exacerbated by the political turmoil following the Manchurian Incident (1931). It highlights the breakdown of traditional family support systems under duress, the extreme poverty, the fear of contagious diseases like whooping cough (백일기침), and the dangers posed by Japanese "subjugation" campaigns (토벌난) that destroyed rural settlements. The husband's implied involvement in resistance ("went to the mountains," "died in enemy hands") and the mother's final resolution for her son connect personal suffering to the broader anti-colonial struggle, suggesting that survival itself is resistance and that future generations must carry on the fight.

눈이 펄펄 나리는 오늘 아침에 승호의 어머니는 백일 기침에 신음하는 어린 승호를 둘러 업고 문 밖을 나섰다. 그가 중국인의 상점 앞을 지나칠 때 며칠 전에 어멈을 그만 두고 쪼끼어 나오듯이 친가로 정신없이 가던 자신을 굽어보며 오늘 또 친가에서 의모와 쌈을 하고 이렇게 나오게되니 이전 갈 곳이 없는 듯하였다. 그나마 그는 의모는 말할 것 없지만 아버지만 처다보고 그대로 딸자식이니 몇 해는 그만두고라도 몇 달은 보아주려니 보다도 승호의 백일기침이 났기까지는 있게되려니 하였다가 그 역시 딴남인 애히네보다도 못하지 안음을 그는 눈물겨웁게 생각하였다. 어디로 가나? 그는 우뚝 섰다. 사람들은 부절히 그의 옆으로 지나친다. 그는 멍하니 하늘을 처다보면서 이제야말로 원수같이 지나던 시형네 집에나마 머리 숙여 들어가지 않을 수가 없었다. 그렇게 생각하고나니 자신은 도수장에 들어가는 소 모양으로 왼몸이 부루루 떨리고＜11＞ 참아 발길을 떼놋는 수가 없었다. 그러나 한편으로 생각하면 비록 그의 남편은 이미 죽었지만 남편의 뒤를 이을 이 승호가 있지 않은가! 이 승호야말로 친가에서보다도 시형네 집에서는 유리한 조건이 되지 않는가. 족하 자식도 자식이지 오냐 가자! 하고 그는 억지로 발길을 떼어놓았다. 더구나 시형네는 방금 약방을 펼처 놓고 있으니 들어가기가 어려워서 그렇지 그가 들어만 가면 승호의 이 기침도 곧 나질 것 같았다. 그는 용기가 났다. 아무러한 모욕을 주더라도 꿀꺽 참자하고 느려지는 발길에 힘을 주었다. 그러나 동세의 그 낙시눈과 시형의 호박씨 같은 눈이 작고 그의 발길을 돌리려고만 하였다.
만주사변 전만 하여도 시형이 자기의 남편을 하눌같이 떠받치었으며 그래서 자기들까지도 시형이 군말 없이 생활비를 대주었던 것이나 일단 만주사변이 일어나고 그러고 이 용정사회가 돌변하면서 부터는 시형도 맘이 변하야 끔직하게 알던 그 아우를 밤낮으로 욕질을 해가며 역시 자기네 모자를 한결같이 대하였다. 그래서 일절 생활비도 대주지 않는 까닭에 승호의 어머니는 남의 어멈으로 들어가게 되었던 것이다. 그러고 특히 일년 전에 남편이 객지에서 죽었다는 기별이 왔을 때 시형은 오히려 좋와하는 눈치를 보이었기 때문에 승호의 어머니는 있는 악이 다 치밀러서 큰 쌈을 하게 되었으며 그 후로는 발길을 아주 끊고 말았던 것이다. 그런데 오늘 이렇게 그가 머리 숙여 들어간대야 시형네 내외가 물론 덜 좋아할 것을 뻔히 아는 터이고 해서 그는 이렇게 주저하고 망스리지 않고는 견디지 못하였다.
가만히 업디어 있던 승호는 갑작이 머리를 들며 그 몹쓸 기침발을 또 내놓았다. 그러고 기침에 못 이겨서 숨이 꼴깍 너머 가는 소리를 한다. 그는 얼른 승호를 앞흐로 돌려 아느며 승호의 볼 우에 볼을 맞대고 몸을 부루루 떨었다.
「승호야! 아가!」
그는 안타가워서 이렇게 부루루 승호의 입에 그의 입술을 대고 입김을 흠뻑 빨았다. 그것은 아들의 백일기침이 자기에게로 옮아오고 말었으면 하는 생각으로 그는 언제나 승호가 기침을 내놀 때마다 이렇게 하군 하였다. 그러고는＜12＞ 승호가 기침한 지가 한참이 지나도록 하지 않으면 그가 입김을 빨은 효과가 나는가 하야 가슴을 태우다는 번번히 그 기침발을 또다시 맞나군 하였던 것이다. 승호의 기침이 좀 진정한 뒤에야 그는 다시 걸었다. 어느듯 시형네 담모통이로 들어섰다. 그는 멈칫 섰다. 시형이 웨 왔느냐? 물으면 뭐라구 하나? 살러왔읍니다 ...그리나? 뭐라나 그만 잠잠꾸 있을까. 아니 어멈 그만둔 것은 말해야지. 그러나 그 집에서도 모른다면은 어쩌나? 그는 어떤 지하에나 떨어지는 듯 아찔하야 그만 돌아섰다. 차라리 그렇게 될 바에는 아이에 들어가지도 말었으면도 하였다. 그러나 그러고보니 갈 곳이 없다. 그만 오늘 밤이나 누구네 집에 가서 자구서 눈이나 끊치거던 어디로든지 갈까? 그 때는 애히네 집에서 쪼끼어 나오던 광경을 머리에 그리며 남이야 다같이 누구라 우리 모자를 하루 밤인들 재울소냐 이 몹쓸 기침에 걸린 우리 승호를 나외애에야 누가 좋다고 할 사람이 어디 있어 그나마 자식 값에 가니 그래도 시형이 났겠지 가서 말이나 해보자 설마한들 내쫏을까. 그는 다시 발길을 옴겼다. 발길은 점점 무거오며 작구 망스리게 된다. 그러고 승호가 시형과 마주 앉아 이야기 할 때 그 기침을 하면 어찌나 그래서 다소 불상한 맘이 들어 집에 두랴고 했다가도 그만 그 기침에 놀라 딱 잡아떼면 어떻거나! 좀 기다려서 승호가 기침을 한 후에 들어가지 그는 우뚝 서서 승호가 어서 기침을 하기를 고대하였다.
이렇게 바람차고 눈오는 날에 밖에서 오래 있는 것이 승호에게 해롭다는 것을 모르는 것은 아니건만 그는 이렇게 망스리며 가슴을 조리지 않을 수가 없었다.
「승호야 너 큰아버지 앞에서 기침을 참아야 한다. 그래야 한다.
자는 듯이 업디어 있는 승호의 등을 가볍게 두다리며 이렇게 애원하다싶이 하였다. 그는 멈칫섰다. 시형네 문이 눈에 선듯 띠웠던 것이다. 그러고 새로 뼁기칠을 한 시형네 대문은 그가 오래간만에 왔다는 것을 말해주는 듯 그는 뛰는 가슴을 쥐며 또다시 망스렸다. 그때 별안간 문이 열리며 H보통학교에 교사로 있는 시형의 딸이 앞뒤를 굽어보며＜13＞ 나온다. 그는 흠칫하야 물러설 때
「아이 작은 어머니 오래간만이네...」
눈같이 힌 얼골에 보더러운 그의 외투 깃털이 살작살작 스친다.
「잘 있었니...」
그는 질여만 보아도 머리가 숙어지며 말문이 꾹 막힌다.
「어서 들어가요. 승호는 자우?」
질여는 곁으로 오는 체 하더니 도루 물러난다.
「난 저기 다녀 올께. 작은 어머니는 어서 들어가요. 나 오기 전에 가면 못써 웅 작은 어머니.」
질여의 음성은 몹시도 명랑하였다. 그때 그는 질여를 붙들고 이런 사정을 해볼까? 하는 생각도 들었으나 질여는 말을 마치자 생긋 웃어 보이고 돌아서 간다. 그는 하는 수 없이 문 안으로 들어섰다. 신발 소리를 들었음인지 맞동세의 낙시눈이 유리창으로 나타난다. 그는 얼골이 확근 달며 마치 원수를 대하는 듯하였다.
「이거 웬 일이어? 자네가 다 우리 집엘 올 때가 있나?」
미다지를 드르르 열고 내다보는 동세는 은연 중에 노기를 띠우고 그를 대하여 준다. 그는 아무 말 없이 방으로 들어가 앉았다. 약내가 물쿤 스치며 훈훈한 방 기운이 그의 다는 볼 우에 칵 덮씨운다. 그는 승호가 기침을 할까 하야 누덕이를 승호머리까지 뒤집어 씨우고 앉어서 가슴을 조리었다.
「그래 돈버리 한다더니 돈 많이 모아겠구먼...사람이 못 쓰느니 자네 아직도 자네가 옳게한 것 같으나?」
동세는 장죽을 당기어 담배를 담는다.
「잘못했서요.」＜14＞
「그러구 말이지 싸울 때는 혹 싸왔더라도 성이 까라지면 잘못했다고 비러야 허는 게지. 그래 일년이 넘도록 발길하지 않으니 아랫사람으로 웃사람 대하는 법이 어디 그런가.」
잘못했다는 말에 동세는 성이 좀 풀린 모양인지 이러한 말을 한다. 그는 목을 놓아 울고 싶은 것을 겨우 진정하며 자신의 맘이 참말 좁은 것 같았다. 그는 감격하였다. 웃방에서는 중국인이 약을 사러 왔는지 중국인의 음성 틈에 시형의 굵단 음성이 들린다. 그는 동세가 성이 좀 풀린 때에 모든 것을 탁 털어놓고 사정하리라 하였다. 그러고 무슨 말을 꺼내렸으나 앞서는 것은 눈물뿐이요 입을 떼는 수가 없었다. 그러자 승호가 머리를 들더니 기침발을 또 내놓았다. 그의 어머니는 어쩔 줄을 몰라 쩔쩔 매었다.
「아니 그애가 백일 기침이 아니라구?」
동세는 금시로 눈이 샐쭉해진다. 승호어머니가 어째서 온 것을 짐작하였던 것이다.
「백일 기침에는 약이 없다네. 언제부터 그 병에 걸렸나?」
승호어머니는 약 없다는 말에 기가 질리어 얼골이 새하야케 되었다. 그러면 승호는 죽는 수밖에 없구나! 하는 생각이 그의 머리를 아뜩하게 하였던 것이다.
「애를 잘 간수할 것이지. 자네 있는 집에 누가 알른가?」
「아아니유.」
「그러면 그 집에서도 싫여하지 않겠는가.」
「아아주 나왔어요!」
말 끝에 그는 울고야 말었다. 동세는 횡 돌아앉는다.
「백일 기침은 전염병이니 누가 좋와하겠나.」＜15＞
동세는 처음에 약을 얻으러 온 것으로만 알았으나 지금 생각하니 있을 곳이 없어 온 것임을 알았다. 동세는 갑작이 멸시하는 생각과 함께 까라앉으려던 분이 치받친다.
「흥 좋을 때는 발길 안 하더니 새끼가 죽게 되구 있을 데가 없으니 온단 말이어. 우리는 모르네. 아 자네 웨 그리 기세 좋게 떠들고 나가더니 일년이 못되어 돌아오는가. 우리는 그런 꼴 못 보와. 자네 친정집 있지. 그리 가던지 시집을 가든지 우리와는 그때부터 인연을 끊지 안았나!」
담배대로 재터리를 땅땅 친다. 승호의 어머니는 볼을 쥐여 박힌 듯 왼 얼골이 안 아픈 곳이 없었다. 그는 입술을 꼭 다물여 다시 한번 사정하리라 하였다.
「어쩌겠유니까! 한번 용서하십시요.」
「흥! 용서 용서라는 게 몇 푼짜리 가는 게야. 우리는 몰라.」
그때 웃방 미다지가 열리며 시형의 얼골이 나타난다.
「이거 웨들 이리 싱커럽게구니!」
소리를 지르며 눈알을 굴린다.
「글세 생전 면대하지 않을 것 같이 굴더니 새끼가 병들고 있을 곳이 없으니 또 왔구려.」
「듣기 싫여!」
시형은 소리를 냅다 치며 미다지를 도루 닫는다. 그나마 실끝같이 믿었던 시형조차도 저 모양이다. 그는 벌덕 일어났다.
「잘들 살아요.」
그는 미친 듯이 밖으로 튀어 나왔다. 그는 정신 없이 행길까지 나왔다. 눈은 여전히 소리 없이 푹푹 쏟아진다.＜16＞ 그는 우뚝 섰다. 남편을 그는 원망하지 않을 수 없었다. 그러나 그는 곧 후회하였다. 잠 한잠 뜻뜻이 자지 못하고 밥 한끼니 달게 먹어 보지 못하고 산으로 들로 돌아다니다가 적에게 붙들이어 죽은 남편을 원망하는 자신이야말로 너머나 답답한 녀자 같았던 것이다.
남편이 산으로 가기 전에 그를 붙들고 뭐라구 말했던가. 우리는 아무리 잘 살고저 하나 잘 살수가 없다고 하던 남편의 말 그때는 무슨 말인가 하였으나 그가 살아올 사록 남편의 말이 옳은 것 같았다. 아니 옳은 것이다. 승호에게도 우리는 그렇게 가르처야 하오...남편의 말 아아 그 남편을 잃은 자신은 어떻게 해야 좋을까. 남편이 살았을 때는 아무러한 고생을 하여도 그래도 히망이 떠나지 않더니 지금에야 그는 무슨 히망이 있으랴. 그저 앞이 캄캄한 것 뿐이었다.
