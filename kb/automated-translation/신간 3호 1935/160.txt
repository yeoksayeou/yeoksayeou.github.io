Article Title:	削髮하고 長衫입은 金一葉女史의 會見記 (Interview Record with Ms. Kim Il-yŏp, Tonsured and Wearing a Monk's Robe)

Interview Record with Ms. Kim Il-yŏp, Tonsured and Wearing a Monk's Robe
						By Reporter B

Ms. Kim Il-yŏp (金一葉 女史), who had built a love nest with her husband, Mr. Ha (河氏), in a quiet part of Sŏngbuk-tong (城北洞) outside Kyŏngsŏng City (京城) and dreamt of sweet happiness, suddenly cast aside her home like old shoes a year ago and disappeared. She shaved her head, became a Buddhist nun, and set off on a path of ascetic practice, moving from temple to temple. Perhaps seeking to forget all worldly affairs by becoming a Buddhist practitioner, she made a final, great renunciation. She once entered the literary world as a female writer of Chosŏn (朝鮮) and received much affection, but on the arduous path of life, she repeatedly faced turbulence and anguish.

Thus, she gradually drifted away from the pen, and for a while, even news of her could not be heard. The public was greatly relieved to hear she had made a happy home and awaited her return to the literary scene. Who would have thought she would become an ascetic nun!
Recently, she suddenly appeared at the Sŏn Academy (禪學院) in An'guk-tong (安國洞), Kyŏngsŏng, as part of her pilgrimage itinerary. Taking this opportunity, I decided to ask about a piece of her state of mind.

On November 16th, near noon, traces of late autumn still remained; although the leaves had fallen from the branches, the green hue of the fine twigs looked like spring. The reporter sought out Ms. Kim Il-yŏp at the Sŏn Academy in An'guk-tong. Being a temple in the city center, it lacked extensive groves and didn't feel much like a typical temple complex. Like any temple, it was extremely quiet and serene. I stated the purpose of my visit to a monk and requested an interview with Ms. Il-yŏp. He respectfully took the name card I offered and went inside, returning shortly after to invite me in. This area appeared to be the monks' quarters on the east side of the temple. <12> Though not very large, its cleanliness and tranquility seemed to possess a certain dignity. The monk guided me to a room, said, "Please wait just a moment," and withdrew. I sat alone in the room, waiting for Ms. Il-yŏp to appear.

After some time, she came out. As expected, her head was closely shaved, and she wore grey monk's robes. Her face was the same face from four years ago. However, the change in her heart must have been immense.
"It's truly been a long time."
Il-yŏp: "Have you been well?"
"Yes. I hadn't heard any news for a while, then recently heard you came here – I wanted to visit you anyway, so I came, combining it with magazine work. Are you very busy?"
Il-yŏp: "Well, if I were busy, time would be precious, but..."
"Then, excuse my imposition, but I would be grateful if you could grant me about 30 minutes for a conversation."
Il-yŏp: "For something like that, I can give you as much time as you need."
Relieved by these words, I first took out my fountain pen and manuscript paper. Her hitherto rigid expression seemed to soften slightly as she said,
Il-yŏp: "This room isn't heated well and is cold. Let's go to the lower room—"
I followed her guidance and went down to sit in the lower room. She sat formally before me. Her black nickel-silver framed glasses were the same as before.
"Since you seem busy, I'll ask briefly."
Il-yŏp: "It's alright. Formal approval [for my monastic status] hasn't been granted yet."
"From our perspective, you seemed to be living happily in Sŏngbuk-tong. For what reason did you end up getting divorced?"
Il-yŏp: "It was to enter the path of Buddhism (佛道)."
"Was there absolutely no discord between you as husband and wife?"
Il-yŏp: "Absolutely none. Our home life was extremely harmonious. It was quite happy, actually."
"Then how could a divorce happen? So, as you mentioned earlier, did you do so out of a desire to master the way of Buddhism?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes, that's right."
"In that case, couldn't you pursue ascetic practice while keeping the home intact?" <13>
Il-yŏp: "Since one has to make pilgrimages from temple to temple for about three months at a time, naturally, there's no time to look after the home, is there? So, the divorce happened under a very amicable agreement."
"Nevertheless, do you sometimes still think of him (your husband), or perhaps harbor feelings of resentment?"
Il-yŏp: "Absolutely no such thoughts. He was an agreeable man, a good man."
"Leaving him and embarking on the pilgrimage must have brought joy in fulfilling your wish, but if your beloved husband lives in loneliness (孤寂) and resents you..."
Il-yŏp: "Yes. That's why I suffered mentally for a while. But then I heard news that he got engaged to another woman, and I was truly happy. It felt like a burden had been lifted from my mind."
"Basically, since when did you come to believe so deeply in Buddhism?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes, it was about 8 years ago. The Central Buddhist Association (中央佛敎會) was publishing a magazine called 'Buddhism' (佛敎), and starting to write for its literary section at that time became a connection (緣). Through that, I also attended the church [temple meetings] and tried to learn about the path."
"Ah. Ms. Il-yŏp, you attended a Christian church before, didn't you?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes, that was when I was attending school."
"So, which religion do you think holds more truth?"
Il-yŏp: "Well, a religion from when one is immature doesn't leave a deep impression, does it? I now believe Buddhism holds more truth."
"What aspect of Buddhism captivated you so deeply?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes. Are you asking about the point that resonates with me? It's the point of transcending life-and-death (生死) and suffering-and-joy (苦樂). That is, attaining enlightenment (득도). For instance, if we talk and move like this all day long, but then happen to die, the physical body shows no difference or change in its outward form at all, yet it cannot speak or move, can it? In other words, the body remains, but life is gone. That is to say, there is something that governs this physical body—the soul (영혼)—and this practice is about unifying the spirit through single-minded concentration (一念)."
"Then, what was the crucial motive for becoming a nun so thoroughly?"
Il-yŏp: "As the path deepens, shouldn't one become faithful to this truth?"
"So, have you had any great awakenings (大覺) during this time?"
Il-yŏp: "Since I am currently in training, I cannot speak of such things yet. My master (스님) instructed me to completely separate from the secular world before attaining enlightenment (得道), prohibiting communication with outsiders (外人相通), even letters, and told me to observe silence with my mouth. Yet here I am, talking so much. However, this is only for a few days; from the day the approval <14> comes down, meeting outsiders will be strictly forbidden."
"I almost missed the chance to see you! Right! So, after entering Buddhism, how do you view life in the secular world?"
Il-yŏp: "It is all anguish (苦懊) and misery (悲慘). People are wandering in darkness (어둠)."
"What about things like the women's movement (女性運動)...?"
Il-yŏp: "It's merely a temporary, momentary (一時的 瞬問的) emergency measure. It cannot be an eternal, infinite (永遠한 無窮한) truth."
"Which school did you attend...?"
"Il-yŏp": "I attended Ewha Hakdang (이화학당)."
"Me": "Excuse me, but your first love (初戀)...?"
Il-yŏp: "Why ask such things?"
"My apologies. However, what do you consider your failures in the secular world?"
Il-yŏp: "Viewed from my current perspective, my life was anguish (苦懊) and confusion (混亂). I cannot single out one thing and call it a failure."
[I feel bad constantly asking about secular matters, but have you experienced heartbreak (失戀)?]
Il-yŏp: "Heartbreak or fulfillment in love (득연), it's all confusion arising from anguish and mental afflictions (苦懊와 번뇌) born of material desire (물욕). One must escape that."
"After entering Buddhism, who is your guiding master (法師)?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes, there is one. It is Venerable Man'gong (滿空스님) of Sudŏk Temple (修德寺) in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province (忠淸道)."
"His reverence's age is...?"
Il-yŏp: "He is 64."
"What is your pilgrimage itinerary?"
Il-yŏp: "First to Chikchi Temple (直指寺), from there to Sŏbong Hermitage (棲鳳庵), from there to Mahahaeng (摩訶洐), and then I came to Kyŏngsŏng."
"The duration?"
Il-yŏp: "About three months in each place, but I expect to stay in Kyŏngsŏng a bit longer."
"In that case, have you completely given up literature (文藝)?"
Il-yŏp: "During ascetic practice, there is nothing else. However, after completing the practice and attaining enlightenment (修道得道), I will discover a new realm (경지). At that time, I might freely express my thoughts."
[When did you start in literature?]
Il-yŏp: "What's so special about someone like me? When I wrote something back then, everyone, without exception, praised and showered me with compliments, putting me on a pedestal (비행기를 태우드니만), but then when I got divorced, the newspapers and magazines all raised a fuss, calling me terrible names (죽일년 살릴년). That's just how the secular world operates. One must not let the heart be led astray by that."
"You also ran a magazine back then, didn't you?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes, a magazine called 'New Woman' (新女子)." <15>
"Right. Among the works you published in the past, which do you feel confident is a masterpiece (力作)?"
Il-yŏp: "Well... It's laughable that whenever I published anything, people made a fuss saying it was well done, but thinking back now, it was all just wandering in darkness without any real truth."
"Even after entering Buddhism, have you occasionally taken an interest in literature?"
Il-yŏp: "One shouldn't think about things from a time when one's ideology (사상) was immature."
"Then, after attaining enlightenment, you will pioneer a new realm! Right!"
Il-yŏp: "Yes. Like Śākyamuni (釋迦)..."
"Do your parents and relatives not oppose this lifestyle?"
Il-yŏp: "Both my parents passed away when I was young. So I am quite free. I have no relatives either."
"So, are you studying Buddhist scriptures (불경)?"
Il-yŏp: "No. It's not about learning scriptures. It's about receiving a hwadu (話頭). It's about solving one great question. It's the practice of concentrating the mind under a single thought (一念)."
"General reading (讀書)?"
Il-yŏp: "I do no reading whatsoever."
"Visits from acquaintances (親知)...?"
Il-yŏp: "Absolutely not allowed."
"Do you never feel loneliness (孤寂) in your heart?"
Il-yŏp: "It's only about concentrating the single thought, so how could such idle thoughts (잡념) arise?"
"Then you wouldn't envy the happy family life of the world, would you?"
Il-yŏp: "Of course not. It's all temporary (一時的)."
"So, do you intend to entrust your body to Buddhism from your youth until the end of your life?"
Il-yŏp: "Of course."
"Celibate for life?"
Il-yŏp: "Yes."
"Monks nowadays seem different from the past, eating meat and having wives and children?"
Il-yŏp: "In Buddhism, there are two main traditions: Doctrinal School (敎宗) and Meditation School (禪宗). The Doctrinal School lives like that. However, for the Meditation School, it is absolutely forbidden."
"What name are you called by here, Il-yŏp?"
Il-yŏp: "I am called Ha'yŏp (荷葉)."
"What are the rules of life here?"
Il-yŏp: "In the morning, we wake up at 3 AM. Then at 6 AM, we practice meditation (참선). We meditate three times a day (하로). One hour after finishing meditation (放禪), we re-enter meditation (入禪). Until the formal decision comes from headquarters (본부), there isn't much that keeps me busy."
As the conversation reached this point, the sound of a bell ringing <16> from the Dharma hall could be heard.
"Doesn't this bell sound signify some ceremony?"
Il-yŏp: "No. It's the call for lunch."
"Oh, then I am being too impolite! Right!"
Il-yŏp: "Not at all. It's fine if I eat lunch a little late. Please continue talking."
"But this is communal living; wouldn't it be wrong to miss the mealtime?"
Il-yŏp: "It really doesn't matter."
Just then, someone impolitely slid open the door, stuck in a shaved head, and seemed to indicate something with their eyes—likely signaling the meal. Feeling apologetic, I quickly stood up.
"I don't know when I might see you again next time, and I wish we could talk more, but I've been too discourteous, so I will leave now."
Il-yŏp: "No, really. It's fine if you talk more."
Leaving words of thanks, I exited the door. The temple at noon was exceedingly tranquil. On the street outside, countless human shadows intertwined and moved. <17>

**Summary**

This 1935 interview catches Kim Il-yŏp (pen name Il-yŏp, later Dharma name Ha'yŏp), a famous early feminist writer, shortly after she abandoned her marriage and literary career to become a Sŏn (Zen) Buddhist nun. She explains her decision was driven solely by a desire to pursue Buddhist enlightenment—transcending life, death, suffering, and joy—and not by marital discord. She describes her former life and literary efforts as "wandering in darkness" and views worldly pursuits like the women's movement as temporary fixes. She details her strict monastic routine, emphasizes detachment from secular ties (including her ex-husband, whose remarriage relieves her), and anticipates finding a "new realm" of understanding after attaining enlightenment, possibly leading to future writing.

For historians, the article offers a fascinating glimpse into the life choices of a prominent female intellectual in colonial Korea. Kim Il-yŏp's turn from modern feminist ideals and literature towards traditional Sŏn Buddhist asceticism reflects the complex currents of thought during the period, where individuals navigated modernity, colonialism, and indigenous traditions. Her dismissal of her previous activism and writings as immature highlights a personal transformation possibly influenced by disillusionment or a search for deeper meaning beyond contemporary social and political movements, within the specific context of Japanese colonial rule and the revival of Korean Buddhism. Her comments on different Buddhist sects (Doctrinal vs. Sŏn) also provide context on religious practices of the time.

削髮하고 長衫입은 金一葉女史의 會見記
						B記者
京城市外 城北洞 고요한 곳에 夫君 河氏와 사랑의 보금자리를 만들고 달디단 행복을 꿈꾸든 金一葉 女史는 1년 전에 돌연이 가정을 헌신같이 내여 버리고 어디로인지 그 姿態가 사라젔다. 그는 삭발하고 여승이 되여서 사원에서 사원으로 수도의 行程에 올낫다. 그는 불교의 수도자가 되여서 모든 世事를 잊으려 함인지 최후의 큰 失心을 하였다. 그는 일즉이 朝鮮의 女流文士로 文壇에 등장하야 寵愛를 받었었으나 고닯흔 생의 행로에서 여러 번이나 파란과 苦懊를 거듭하였었다.
그리하야 점점 붓과는 멀어지고 한동안 그 소식조차 들을 수 없드니 행복한 가정을 만들었다 하야 일반은 즈윽히 안심하였고 그의 문단의 재출발을 기다렸드니 누가 뜻하였으랴! 그가 修道僧女가 되고만 것을!
近日 그의 순례의 과정의 차례로 京城 安國洞 禪學院에 자태를 돌연이 나타내였음을 기회로 그의 심경의 한 조각을 뭇기로 하였다.

11월 16일 거진 정오 갓가히 晩秋의 자최가 아즉도 남어있어 잎들은 가지에서 떠러젔으나 細枝의 푸른 빛이 봄날 같엇다. 기자는 安國洞 禪學院으로 金一葉 女史를 찾엇다. 시내 중앙에 있는 사원이라 樹林도 없고 그다지 寺院氣分이 적게난다. 어느 사원이나 마창가지로 대단히 閑靜한 곳이다. 한 僧에게 나의 이곳 온 목적을 말하고 一葉 女史의 면회를 청하니 그는 내가 준 명함을 공손히 받어들고 안으로 드러가드니 小許에 안으로 드러오기를 청한다. 이곳은 寺院 東側으로 승려들의 숙소인 듯이＜12＞보인다. 그다지 크지는 않으나 청결하고 한적함이 일종에 위엄이 잇는 듯도 하다. 그 僧은 어떠한 방으로 안내하드니 「잠간만 가다리십쇼」하고는 물러간다. 나는 혼자 방에 앉어서 一葉 女史가 나오기를 기다린다.
얼마만에 그는 나왓다. 예기한 바와같이 머리를 박박 깎고 회색의 승려복을 입엇다. 그의 얼굴은 4년전에 그 얼굴이다. 그렇나 그의 마음의 변화는 짐듯 컷을 것이다.
「참 오래감만입니다.」
