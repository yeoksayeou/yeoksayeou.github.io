上海夜話 (Shanghai Night Stories)
Shanghai Special Correspondent Kang Sŏng-gu

The night streets of Shanghai are streets filled with the sinister smile of terrifying demons. The strange scents emanating from the gentlemen of the night and ladies of the night who roam the streets pierce everyone's noses, and bang! bang! the activities of daring gangsters who race through the night streets as if they own them, firing pistols repeatedly, begin.
The sound of the jazz band flowing from the dance hall is a withered dirge (挽歌), lacking melody, mourning the pitiful destruction of humans who have lost their "tomorrow."
In every foul-smelling, dark alley, prostitutes with faces plastered like flour dough lie sprawled out, casting cheap, flirtatious glances. One paper yuan is surely O.K. No, even three 20-sen silver coins are enough. Their bodies are utterly decayed, fly-infested masses of corruption.
The faint melody of the *hugu* (胡弓) rises from house after house—. Somehow, it makes people's hearts inexplicably sorrowful.
I, a young bohemian, enter a Russian restaurant to fill my hungry stomach. Tossing back a shot of hard liquor (火酒) and picturing my faraway hometown before my eyes, singing a lonely wanderer's song wouldn't be so bland.
Ships, having finished preparations for departure, sound their powerful whistles loudly from the Huangp'u River.
It seems the Shanghai night is gradually entering its main phase.
Pushing through the hazy night air, I looked at my wristwatch indicating 10 o'clock and stepped into the Chinese tavern "T'ient'ien" (天天). In that Chinese tavern, Mr. Wang, a Chinese man well over the age of thirty (설흔), was waiting for me. Mr. Wang greeted me warmly. But somehow, strangely, his face showed a slightly stronger excitement than usual.
I came to know Mr. Wang this spring when I visited the Chinese Art News Company (中國藝術新聞社) on H Road. There, <74> Mr. Wang, who was running errands, poured me a cup of tea and offered it. That day, a spring drizzle was falling steadily. Perhaps because of that, thoughts of alcohol kept coming, but I forcibly suppressed them with the hot cup of tea Mr. Wang brought. Mr. Wang was what they call a good person (好人). When I asked, "How did you end up running errands here?" he just smiled faintly and avoided answering.
This was a story I heard later one Sunday afternoon, also at that Art News Company, from a Chinese reporter named Ch'en. He told me that Mr. Wang had previously been a major (少佐) in the Chinese army.
The next moment, I was startled by the reporter's words and blurted out involuntarily, "What? An army major?" The reporter's story continued.
"In the early spring of 1932, the infamous Shanghai Incident (上海事變) occurred, didn't it! At that time, Mr. Wang belonged to the 19th Route Army (19路軍), and when the incident broke out, he was active on the front lines! But somehow, perhaps struck by fancy, he apparently deserted with a beautiful female student named Liying (麗英), a member of the so-called female student volunteer corps (義勇軍). Afterwards, when the Shanghai Incident ended and the 19th Route Army had withdrawn far from Shanghai, Mr. Wang was wandering the streets desolately. By then, Liying, perhaps disillusioned with their extremely perilous, deviant love affair, had left Mr. Wang alone and disappeared somewhere. It seems the love that bloomed on the front line withered when transplanted elsewhere. Hahaha. Anyway, after that, even though love turned out that way, he had to make a living (입에다 풀칠을 해야), but where could he find a suitable job? So, having no choice, he worked as a houseboy (뽀이) for Westerners and as a doorman at a cafe before rolling into this place. Since he's naturally strong-bodied, he works three times harder than others and has a very cheerful personality."
The reporter's story ended there, but from then on, I developed a great interest (多大한 흥미) in Mr. Wang. So, I often brought him to my room, we ate meals together (가치), and we also went to Chinese pubs and drank together several times.
Then, one morning, while I was busy wielding my brush, trying to finish a novel I had started writing, a postcard arrived. It was a postcard sent to me by Mr. Wang. It was written (씨워 있었다) that he had grown tired of being a servant at the Art News Company and had left to look for another job. After reading the postcard, I wore something close to a bitter smile (苦笑) on my face, but I also interpreted it this way: an army major who had once galloped amidst thousands of troops and horses (千軍萬馬) couldn't possibly remain a newspaper company servant for long. Intoxicated by sweet love, <75> holding hands, they secretly deserted the front line, but once the sweet dream (甘夢) of love shattered, Mr. Wang suddenly must have longed for his old military life again, wanting to ride a warhorse (軍馬) and charge vigorously once more across the blood-soaked front lines where bullets (實彈) rained down like hail.
For about a week after receiving that postcard that morning, I knew nothing of Mr. Wang's whereabouts. Then, suddenly, around 6 o'clock this evening, a postcard flew in; it was from Mr. Wang, from whom there had been no news until now.
"I have something to tell you, Teacher. Please be sure to come to the 2nd floor of T'ient'ien Tavern (天天酒) on B Road around 10 PM on X month X day (i.e., today), without fail." That was, in short, the content of the postcard. I had been worried, not knowing his news, so I was quite pleased (저윽이 반가워했다) to receive the postcard from Mr. Wang. And so, waiting for nightfall, I came to this T'ient'ien Tavern at the time Mr. Wang mentioned in the postcard.
Mr. Wang greeted me affectionately, seated me in a chair, and ordered *laojiu* (老酒) and side dishes (안주). Soon, the alcohol and dishes arrived; he poured alcohol into my glass and his own, said, "Well, let's drink!" and urged me to drink.
Mr. Wang downed his drink in one gulp and then first (위선) recounted his life over the past week in detail. Then, letting out a long sigh, he proceeded to tell me a story that was truly one hundred percent "ero" and "gro," a story chilling enough to give one goosebumps just hearing it.
"Teacher Kang! Can such a world really exist? They call Shanghai the Demon City (魔都), and truly, it's horrifying. This isn't a story I made up; made up? I'm not that kind of cheap novelist. Aigoo, the more I think about it, the more I tremble with rage! Teacher Kang, don't be shocked either. Last night, I directly experienced something incredibly bizarre! Perhaps this could only be seen in Shanghai..."
Mr. Wang stated this preface in a quite agitated tone, then sighed deeply again, "Hoo—."
Looking out the glass window, the night street was still clamorous.
The story flowed from Mr. Wang's lips. An exceedingly, exceedingly bizarre story. So, what indeed was the story that flowed from Mr. Wang's lips? Dear readers, please trouble yourselves to read the following true story narrative titled "Night Club 'White Eagle' (白鷲)" to the end.

Night Club "White Eagle" (白鷲) Night Club White Eagle

Damn this rotten world! Where else could such a world exist! Humph! Even the sun (太陽) scoffs at it.
Mr. Wang is washing dishes, bathed in the warm autumn sunlight. <76> The more he thinks, the funnier, yet on the other hand, the more profoundly serious, the trick (작란) of fate seems, Mr. Wang thought. It's already been a week since he left the Chinese Art News Company. Initially (당초에), when he left the Art News Company, he had resolved never again to be someone else's servant, but right after (턱) leaving, the worry about food took precedence. He wandered the city streets for several blocks (멫 박구씩), but no one greeted him saying, "Come into our restaurant and have a meal." Harsh street! He yelled with all his might, but it only made his throat sore and was utterly useless.
Night comes. Night comes. The autumn night in Shanghai is annoyingly chilly (대기(大氣)가 차다). What he wears is a worn-out (다 떠러진) summer suit that came out of a pawnshop (전당포) this summer. He bought that summer suit for 80 cents (대양(大洋) 원 80전), but now that autumn has arrived so abruptly (턱), it seems difficult to endure with just that thin summer suit.
The bright autumn moon silently appeared above the building (삘딩) over there. Somehow, Mr. Wang felt a pang of homesickness (鄕愁) in a corner of his heart. It was vastly different from the kind of homesickness—tinged with tension (긴장미) and flowing with a fresh feeling—he felt on the battlefield (戰場), where bullets, devil-like bullets, rained down, as he silently gazed at the bright moon rising quietly from beyond the horizon (地平線) and thought of his hometown's mountains and rivers (山川). This was a homesickness soaked in despair, welling up in his heart after losing hope.
Mr. Wang dragged his weak legs towards S Road in the International Settlement (공동조게). Many Westerners lived on S Road. Among the Westerners, British people lived there in particularly large numbers.
From a splendid interior (室內), the sorrowful weeping sounds of a piano and violin, decorating the night of this street, quietly seeped out. Mr. Wang, steeped in homesickness, unconsciously pricked up his ears (귀를 기우렸다).
Tonight, strangely, everything makes Mr. Wang's heart sorrowful.
From the bright moon floating gently in the sky (천공) to the sounds of the piano and violin—
He walked again.
At the entrance (出入口) of a Western-style house (양옥집) visible ahead, some white paper was posted. Mr. Wang walked towards it. And he looked up at the white paper.
Servant Wanted (下人入用).
It wasn't a welcome piece of writing (글발) for Mr. Wang. However, to solve the immediate problem of bread (팡문제), although he didn't like it, he had no choice but to become someone's servant again (아니하면 아니되었다). He opened the door and entered the house. And from that night, he became the servant of that house (a Frenchman (佛蘭西人) lived there).
However, in that house lived something that ignited Mr. Wang's heart with a mix of gladness and hatred (증惡)—it was the beautiful figure of Liying (麗英), who had run away (다라난) <77> leaving Mr. Wang behind. Liying, Mr. Wang's former lover, was working as a servant in that house. Mr. Wang and Liying were quite surprised by this unexpected reunion (邂逅). And the next moment, a strange feeling, a mixture (뒤섞긴) of gladness and hatred, welled up in Mr. Wang's chest. But somehow (어떻게 된 셈인지), that night, Mr. Wang and Liying embraced tightly and kissed each other in the same bed (寢臺). The dead love had revived (사라난).
On the fourth day (나흘째) after Mr. Wang came to that house, the master of the house (밖앝주인) left for T'ienchin (天津) on business (商用). Perhaps because of that, the mistress of the house seemed to have become somewhat wanton (바람이 난 모양이다). Suddenly, her comings and goings outside (밖앝출입) became frequent (자자지며), and male friends often came to visit.
One night (하로밤), the mistress returned late after going out somewhere, brought Mr. Wang to her room, and thoroughly savored Mr. Wang's robust physique.
The master returned home this morning after spending three days in T'ienchin. He drank a cup of milk tea and sat slumped in a chair, smoking a cigarette. Unaware that his wife had savored Mr. Wang's body in his absence—.
After washing all the dishes, Mr. Wang wiped his hands and went into his room. He lit a cigarette and silently pondered something.
—No matter how I look at it, the master's attitude towards Liying is somewhat strange... Giving Liying a 20-yuan jeweled ring (보석반지)... It's definitely strange...
Night came. The clock (시게) mournfully struck one o'clock. Ding!
Mr. Wang suddenly woke up. The next moment, he clearly heard Liying's scream (비명) coming from right outside his door, "Let go! I don't want to! Let go!" Mr. Wang quickly got out of bed and kicked (찻다) his door open.
Oh, Liying! As the door swung wide open, Liying's figure appeared clearly before Mr. Wang's eyes. And the face of the master, standing there holding (붓잡고 섯는) Liying's arm— The master seemed to be trying with all his might to drag the unwilling Liying to his room.
Anger (憤怒) surged in Mr. Wang's chest. This rotten bastard! Mr. Wang's iron fist (鐵拳) flew like a tiger (비호) towards the master's cheek. Hit by Mr. Wang's iron fist, the master collapsed on the spot. Then Mr. Wang quickly hoisted Liying onto his back (등에 업더니) and ran outside like a madman (뛰여나갔다).
The night wind is fiercely cold. Not a single copper coin (동전 한 닢) in hand (수중). Where should they go? Anyway, it would be good if this night would just pass quickly. Ah, I long for the sun. The sun in the blue sky.
Morning flowed in. In the clear autumn sky, the longed-for sun shines vigorously. Gradually (차츰차츰), hunger sets in. Damn it (제-기), where can we go eat! Having no choice, <78> they skipped breakfast. And lunch too.
Night approaches again.
Mr. Wang and Liying wander the streets listlessly, clutching their hungry stomachs.
As they were about to turn the street corner over there, a White Russian old man (백게로시아노인), who had been following Mr. Wang and Liying with snake-like eyes, called out to them.
"Hey there! I have something to say, please stop there for a moment (게시요)."
The Russian old man shouted this from behind them. His voice was sharp as a knife blade (칼날같이).
Mr. Wang and Liying quickly turned around at the sound.
"Why did you call us? Do you have business with us?"
Mr. Wang looked the unfamiliar Russian old man over once (훌터보고) and then asked in a weak voice.
"I called because I have something to discuss!"
The Russian old man, even displaying an unidentifiable smile on his face, spoke calmly in fluent Chinese, as if they were friends of ten years.
"Discuss? But we've never met before (초면인데)..." Mr. Wang hesitated slightly (주저주저).
"It's nothing else, but seeing as you two definitely look like unemployed people (실업자), I wanted to find you a suitable job, so I called. Please forgive my rudeness (실례를 용서하시요)."
At these words flowing from the Russian old man's mouth, a smile unknowingly spread across Mr. Wang's face, and he felt quite pleased.
"Thank you! But what kind of job will you find for us?"
Mr. Wang took a step closer (닥어섰다) to the Russian old man.
"Wouldn't you like to be actors (俳優)? The income (수입) is 90 yuan a month. So, that's 3 yuan a day. How about it? Are you willing to do it?" the old man smiled once more.
"Hmm, 90 yuan a month! Yes! Of course, we'll do it!"
Mr. Wang clenched his fists, vaguely feeling something akin to excitement. He then shuddered (부루룩 하고 떨었다) his whole body once. It was a pleasant shiver (질거운 전률) full of fresh hope. Oh! Heaven (하날) is so gracious! Now I too can live a luxurious life! Oh, who would let go of fortune (행운)!
"Hmm! Then follow me."
Mr. Wang and Liying followed the Russian old man. The short (짜른) autumn sun had already set, and dim twilight (어슴푸레한 황혼) crept into the street (기여든다).
Mr. Wang's party entered a magnificent Western-style house with a small sign (조고만 간판) that read "White Eagle" (白鷲). In that house, there was a large room capable of accommodating (수용할) about 150 people. And in one corner of that room, there was a small stage (舞臺).
Night came. Around nine o'clock, men and women of the leisured class (有閑階級), dazzlingly dressed up (성장들을 한), <79> gathered at this "White Eagle." They were all Westerners.
When the clock struck ten, a naked (빨거숭이) Russian woman (로서아여자) came onto the stage, performed a nude (裸體) dance, and went back in.

The people applauded in unison and chattered wildly. Finally, a play (연극) began. The story (스토리) of the play was about a mentally ill (정신병) young gentleman who, while strolling down the street, suddenly shoots and kills a couple (한 쌍의 부부) passing in front of him for no reason (리유없이)—a very ordinary and simple plot. And the casting (配役) was such that Mr. Wang and Liying would play the unfortunate (불행한) couple, and the role of the mentally ill gentleman was to be played by a middle-aged gentleman (중년신사) named "William," who was among the people visiting "White Eagle" tonight.
First (위선), "William," who took the role of the mentally ill gentleman, appeared on stage and wandered around.
Mr. Wang and Liying waited anxiously backstage for the command (명령) "Go on stage!" from the Russian old man who had brought them here.
"Got it (아랏나)? First, Ms. Liying, you will be hit by the bullet and collapse. Then, the husband, Mr. Wang, will try to attack the mentally ill gentleman who shot Ms. Liying, but instead, he will be shot and fall down right there. Since it's the first time, I've arranged it very simply (꾸몃소). Now then, go out onto the stage. And this is today's daily wage (일급이요)."
Saying this, the Russian old man placed a 5-yuan bill and a 1-yuan bill into Mr. Wang's hand.
"Now, go out!"
And the Russian old man pushed (떠밀었다) Mr. Wang and Liying.
Mr. Wang and Liying went onto the stage. They were treading the stage for the first time in their lives, facing the splendidly dressed gentlemen and ladies. Their hearts kept pounding (자꾸 가슴이 두근거린다). The waves of their hearts (심장) rose high.
"William" walked closer. "William" pulled a pistol (피스톨) out of his pocket (포켓트). The pistol glittered and flashed under the electric light (전기불). "William," the mentally ill gentleman, aimed the pistol at Liying (겨누었다).
"William's" eyes shone menacingly.
Bang! The gunshot rang out. And Liying fell. Oh, but this is definitely not a dream. Look! Open your eyes and look! At the red blood (붉은 선혈) flowing from the beloved Liying's chest!
Woo! Woo! Woo!

A strange sound, chilling to the bone (소름이 쪽끼칠), reached Mr. Wang's ears. It was the sound made by the approximately 150 gentlemen and ladies sitting in the large room of this "White Eagle." They even wore terrifying smiles on their faces.

**Summary**

This article, written by a Shanghai correspondent, paints a grim picture of Shanghai nightlife in 1935, describing it as demonic, filled with gangsters, desperate prostitutes, and melancholic music. The correspondent recounts his encounter with Wang Sŏbang, a former Chinese army major who deserted during the 1932 Shanghai Incident for love, only to be abandoned and fall into destitution, working menial jobs. The main narrative follows Wang's recent harrowing experience: after reuniting with his former lover Liying and fleeing an abusive employer, they are lured into a morbid "night club" called "White Eagle" run by a White Russian. Promised acting jobs, they unknowingly become part of a spectacle where Liying is shot with a live bullet on stage for the perverse entertainment of wealthy Western patrons who revel in the sight of real blood. The story ends abruptly as Wang leaps off the stage after witnessing Liying's murder. From a historical perspective, the article offers a Korean colonial-era view of Shanghai as a dangerous, decadent "Demon City." It highlights themes of exploitation, desperation, the stark class divide (impoverished Chinese vs. wealthy Westerners), moral decay, and the potentially perilous lives of those on the margins in a treaty port city. The portrayal of Westerners engaging in sadistic entertainment at the expense of Chinese lives is particularly striking and could be of interest to historians studying colonial dynamics, urban social history, and inter-ethnic relations in Shanghai.

上海夜話
						上海特派員 姜聖九
상해의 밤거리는 무서운 악마의 氣味 나쁜 미소로서 충만된 거리다. 거리를 횡행하는 밤의 신사들과 밤의 숙녀들의 뿌리는 이상야릇한 내음새는 뭇사람들의 코를 찌르고 탕! 탕! 하고 피시톨을 연발하면서 제 세상 같이 밤거리를 질주하는 대담한 깽들의 활동이 개시된다.
땐스홀에서 흘러나오는 재쓰밴드 소리는 「明日」을 잃은 인간들의 애닭은 멸망을 애도하는 선율을 잃어버린 시드른 挽歌이다.
악취나는 식컴언 골목마다 얼골에 밀가루 반죽을 한 매춘부들이 드러배켜서 갑싼 추파를 보낸다. 지폐 1원만 있으면 틀림없이 O·K이다. 아니 20仙짜리 은전 3枚만 있어도 넉넉하다. 그들의 육체는 썩을대로 다 썩은 파리 엉긴 부패물이다.
집집에서 일어나는 가느다란 胡弓의 멜로디-. 어쩐지 사람들의 마음을 까닭없이 구슬푸게 맨든다.
나 젊은 보헤미안이 주린 배를 채울랴고 露西亞 식당으로 들어간다. 火酒나 한 잔 드리키고 멀리 떠러져 있는 내 고향을 눈 앞에 그리며 쓸쓸히 방랑가를 부르는 것도 그리 싱거운 것은 아닐 것이다.
出帆 준비를 다 마친 선박들이 뚜-하고 黃浦江에서 우렁차게 기적을 울린다.
상해의 밤도 점점 본부대로 들어가는가보다.
흐린 밤공기를 헤치며 나는 10시를 가르친 팔뚝시계를 보고 중국주점 「天天」으로 발을 듸밀었다. 그 중국주점에는 설흔(30세)의 고개를 훨신 넘은 중국인 왕서방이 나를 기다리고 있는 것이다. 왕서방은 나를 반가히 맞었다. 그러나 어쩐지 이상하게도 평상때보담은 좀 가느다란 흥분을 얼골에 띠우고 있었다.
내가 왕서방을 알게 된 것은 금년 봄 H路에 있는 中國藝術新聞社에 놀러갔을때 거기서 잔심부름을 하고 잇는 ＜74＞ 왕서방이 차를 한 잔 따라서 나에게 권했기 때문이다. 그날은 봄비가 부실부실 나리고 있었다. 그래서 그련지 자꾸 술생각이 나는 것을 억지로 왕서방이 갔다준 뜨거운 차 한 잔으로 땍이여 버렸다. 왕서방은 소위 好人이었다. 어떻게 되서 여기서 심부름을 하게 됫소? 하고 물어도 그저 싱긋싱긋 웃기만 했지 대답을 피하였다.
이것은 그후 어느 일요일날 오후 역시 그 藝術新聞社에서 陳이라는 중국기자한테 들은 이야긴데 이전에 왕서방은 중국육군소좌로 있었든 사람이라고 나에게 이야기해 주었다.
다음 순간 나는 그 기자의 말에 감짝 놀래며 「뭘? 육군소좌?」 하고 나도 모르게 소리치기까지 하였다. 그 기자의 말은 게속되었다.
「1932년 첫봄에 例의 上海事變이 일어나지 안햇소! 그때 왕서방은 19路軍에 속하고 있었는데 사변이 일어나자 왕서방은 최전선에서 활약햇대요! 그런데 무슨 바람이 불었는지 소위 여학생 의용군의 일원인 麗英이라는 미모의 여학생하고 그만 탈주를 한 모양이야요. 그뒤 상해사변도 끝나고 19路도 멀리 상해에서 물러갔을 때 왕서방은 쓸쓸히 거리를 헤매고 있었드랍니다. 벌서 그때는 麗英이도 자기네들의 그 위험천만의 탈선적 연애에 그만 실증이 났든지 왕서방을 홀로 남겨놓고 어듸로인지 몸을 감추어 버렸드랍니다. 전선에 핀 사랑을 다른 데로 떠다옴겼드니 그만 시드러저 버린 모양입니다. 하하하. 그런데 그후 좌우간 사랑은 그렇게 되였드라도 입에다 풀칠을 해야 되겠는데 어디 맛당한 직업이 있어야지요. 그래 할 수 없어서 서양사람들의 뽀이노릇도 하고 카페의 문직이 노릇도 하다가 이곳으로 굴러왔다고 합디다. 워낙 건장한 몸이 되노니까 일도 남보다 세 배는 더 하고 퍽 쾌활한 성격의 소유자입니다.」
그 기자의 말은 여기서 끝낫지마는 나는 그때부터 왕서방에게 대하야 多大한 흥미를 가지게 되었다. 그래 여러 번 내 방에 데려다가 밥도 가치 먹었고 또 중국 술집에 가서 가치 술도 여러 번 마시었다.
그런데 어떤 날 아침 나는 쓰기 시작한 소설을 써서 끝마칠랴고 한참 붓을 놀리고 있었는데 엽서 한 장이 드러왓다. 그것은 왕서방이 나에게 보낸 엽서였다. 그 엽서에는 藝術新聞社의 하인노릇도 그만 실증이 나서 다른 직업을 구하려 거기를 나왔다 라고 씨워 있었다. 나는 그 엽서를 읽고 나서 그 무슨 苦笑에 가까운 것을 얼골에 띄웠으나 과거에 있어 그래도 千軍萬馬의 사이를 달리고 있든 육군소좌가 신문사 하인노릇을 길게 할 수는 없을 것이다 라고 이렇게 해석해 보기도 했다. 달착지근한 사랑에 도취하야 손에 손을 ＜75＞ 마주잡고 남몰래 전선에서 탈주는 했으나 그 사랑의 甘夢이 깨여지고 보니 갑작이 왕서방은 옛날의 군인생활이 도루 그리웠으며 軍馬를 타고 실탄이 비오듯 날러오는 피에 젖은 전선을 또다시 기운차게 달려보고 싶었을 것이다.
그 엽서를 받은 그날 아침부터 약 1주일이 지나도록 나는 그 뒤의 왕서방의 소식을 조금도 몰랐는데 돌연 오늘 저녁 6시경에 엽서 한 장이 날러드러왔으니 그것은 이때껏 아무 소식이 없든 왕서방이 보낸 것이었다.

사람들은 일제히 박수를 보내며 제멋대로 짓거렸다. 마지막으로 연극이 시작되었다. 그 연극의 스토리는 정신병에 걸린 나 젊은 신사 한 사람이 거리를 거닐다가 자기 앞을 지나가는 한 쌍의 부부를 아무 리유없이 돌연 피스톨로 쏘아 죽인다는- 아주 평범하고 단순한 내용이었다. 그리고 배역(配役)은 왕서방과 여영이가 그 불행한 부부를 맡어 하게 되고 또 그 정신병자 신사는 오늘밤 「白鷲」에 놀러온 사람들 가운데서 「윌리암」이라는 중년신사가 맡어하기로 된 것이었다.
위선 정신병자 신사의 역을 맡은 「윌리암」이 무대에 나타나서 이리저리 돌아다닌다.
왕서방과 여영이는 무대 뒤에서 자기네를 이곳으로 데리고 온 그 로서아노인의 무대에 나가라! 는 명령이 내리기를 마음 조려 기다리고 있었다.
『아랏나. 먼저 여영씨 당신이 총알에 마저서 쓰러지게 됩니다. 그러면 그 남편인 왕서방이 여영씨를 쏜 그 정신병자 신사에게 덤벼들라다가 도리여 총을 마저서 그 자리에 너머집니다. 처음이고 해서 아주 쉽게 꾸몃소. 자아 그러면 무대에 나가보우. 그리고 이것은 오날 일급이요.』
하고 그 로시아 노인은 5원짜리 한 장과 1원짜리 한 장을 왕서방의 손에 쥐여주었다.
『자 나가시요!』
하고 그 로시아노인은 왕서방과 여영이를 떠밀었다.
왕서방과 여영이는 무대로 나갔다. 화려하게 차린 신사 숙녀들을 눈 앞에 두고 생전 처음으로 무대를 밟고 있는 것이었다. 자꾸 가슴이 두근거린다. 심장의 물결이 높하진다.
「윌리암」이 가까이 걸어온다. 「윌리암」은 포켓트에서 피스톨을 끄집어냈다. 피스톨은 전기불에 비치여서 번쩍번쩍 번쩍이고 있다. 「윌리암」은, 정신병자 신사는 피스톨로 여영이를 겨누었다.
「윌리암」의 눈이 무섭게 빛난다.
