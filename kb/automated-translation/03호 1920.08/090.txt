The Flower Palanquin of a Smile
						Hwang Sŏk-u

Oh, flower palanquin (花輿) of your smile (微笑) that carries my soul (靈),
My soul is swept away by your smile,
Releasing (놋코) love’s (愛) small torch (炬火),
Over the weary light (光) of the setting sun (太陽),
Over the hill of strange moss-covered (苔덥흰) granite (花岡石),
Over the seemingly weeping young pasture grass (牧草),
Over the panting wind, like the gasping (喘息) of a tuberculosis patient (肺患者),
Like a kite (鳶) whose string is cut,
It goes staggering aimlessly (向方업시).

My dear, in the serene shade of your large eyes,
Round and sunken like the forest-colored (森色) windows
Of a gas-lit (瓦斯燈) convent (女修道院) quiet in the dead of night (深更),<34>
Surely some lovely person lies curled,
Sweating in the pure (淨) dream of a saint’s (聖者) first pilgrimage (巡禮).
(Singing, singing, singing)
Merely in my eagerness to meet them,
My helpless, pitiable soul,
Drunk on your smile, like a drop of grape liquid (葡萄液)
Poured out and abandoned by the roadside (路傍),
Is swept away so aimlessly.

My dear, in your pomegranate-like (石榴) mumbling lips,
I think perhaps you intend to spray and sow
The fitting words meant for some lovely person
Into the wide furrows plowed (가러올닌) in my heart’s field (밧고랑).
Merely for the sake of gathering them,
I leap onto your smile like a drifting boat (流船).
Ah, flower palanquin (花輿) of your smile that carries my soul. <35>
<34-35>

**Summary**

This poem by Hwang Sŏk-u uses the metaphor of a "flower palanquin of a smile" to describe the overwhelming, intoxicating effect of a beloved's smile on the speaker's soul. The soul is depicted as helplessly carried away, like a stringless kite, over landscapes imbued with melancholy and decay, yet drawn towards an image of purity associated with the beloved's eyes and the promise of meaningful words from their lips. The speaker feels intoxicated and driven solely by the desire to connect with the beloved or the essence they represent. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in *Kaebyŏk* in 1920, is interesting as an example of lyrical modernism exploring intense personal emotion during the early Japanese colonial period. Its blend of romantic yearning, slightly decadent imagery (panting wind, patient's gasping), and spiritual/religious allusions (convent, saint, pilgrimage) reflects the complex cultural currents of the time, showing the adoption and adaptation of international literary trends within a Korean context under duress. Historians might find it useful for understanding cultural production, expressions of interiority, and the influence of Western aesthetics in early colonial Korea.
