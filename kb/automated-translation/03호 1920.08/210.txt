Children's Song
						Mul

The Lamplighter
During the lo-ong daytime, having attended to company affairs (社務)
People pack their lunch boxes and return home;
When it's time to eat dinner and shut the main gate (大門),
Shouldering a ladder, carrying a kerosene lamp (석냥),
Lighting the streetlamps (장명燈) of house after house,
There is a person running along.

My father, renowned as a banker (銀行家),
Will surely gather money as skillfully as he pleases...
My older brother will become the Minister (大臣) he desires,
My older sister will surely succeed as a writer (文學家)...

Ah-when I grow up later,
What should this body do that would be good?
When I can choose for myself <88>
Ye-s, this body, like that person,
Will wander from street to street,
Lighting the streetlamps (장명燈) of house after house.

And no matter how humble the house,
I will light the lamps brightly for them.
Then the streets will become brighter,
And everyone to-gether will become happy.

Following the streets end to end,
Gradua-ally going into the mountains,
I will also light lamps in the desolate poor villages (寂寞한 貧村).
Then the world will become brighter still...

Hey there, lamplighter passing by,
Do not feel lonely on that weary road.
O lamplighter walking lonely,
This body is your companion.
(August 15, 1961... At Chaetkol House... Trans.)<89>
<88-89>

**Summary**

This poem, written from a child's perspective, contrasts conventional ambitions like becoming a banker, minister, or writer with the desire to emulate a humble lamplighter. The child observes the lamplighter bringing light to homes after dark and resolves to take up the same task, extending this service even to poor, remote villages. The ultimate goal expressed is to brighten the world and bring collective happiness, concluding with a message of solidarity to the lonely lamplighter. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in *Kaebyŏk* in 1920, subtly reflects the aspirations of the era following the March 1st Movement. The act of "lighting lamps," especially in poor and desolate areas, symbolizes enlightenment, social upliftment, and bringing hope during the difficult colonial period, valuing community service over personal status or wealth accumulation within the colonial structure. The later annotation (1961) suggests its enduring resonance.

어린이 노래
						물
불켜는 이
기-나긴 낫동안에 社務를 보던
사람들이 벤도끼고 집에 돌아와
저녁먹고 大門다칠 때가 되면은
사다리 질머지고 석냥을 들고
집집의 장명燈에 불을 켜노코
다름질 해가는 사람이 잇소.

銀行家로 이름난 우리아버지는
재조껏 마음대로 돈을 모겟지...
언니는 바라는 大臣이 되고
누-나는 文學家로 成功하겟지...

아-나는 이 담에 크게 자라서
이 몸이 무엇을 해야 조흘지
나 홀로 選擇할 수 잇게 되거던 ＜88＞
그-럿타 이 몸은 저이와 가티
거리에서 거리로 돌아다니며
집집의 장명燈에 불을 켜리라.

그리고 아모리 구차한 집도
밝도록 환-하게 불켜주리라.
그리하면 거리가 더 밝아져서
모도가 다-가티 幸福되리라.

거리에서 거리로 끗을 이어서
점-점점 山속으로 들어가면서
寂寞한 貧村에도 불켜주리라.
그리하면 世上이 더욱 밝겟지...

여보시요 게가는 불켜는 이어
고닯흔 그 길을 외로워 마시요.
외로이 가시는 불켜는 이어
이 몸은 당신의 동무임니다.
(61年 8月 15日... 잿골집에서... 역)＜89＞
＜88-89＞
