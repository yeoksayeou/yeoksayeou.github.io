Script: Overnight
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl (Translator/Adaptor)
-From the Author to the Reader- It was planned to finish up to the Second Act of this script in Kaebyŏk No. 2, but unfortunately, I was struck by illness at that time, and it is deeply regrettable that I could not proceed as planned according to my wishes. In this Third Issue, I intend to complete up to the First Scene of the Second Act. Also, the song "Lorelei" (로오레라이) in the First Act has a melody and lyrics famous throughout the world. The musical score for that song is published in this issue. Let me briefly tell you the origin of that song. -As some of you may know, there is a legend passed down from old times on the banks of the Rhine River (萊因河邊) in Germany (獨逸國). "Lorelei" is the name of a cliff near the town of Sankt Goar (센트 코-아 町) in that country. Long ago, a bewitchingly beautiful woman of unrivaled beauty sat on this rock, combing her disheveled golden hair while singing in a beautiful voice that captivated people's hearts. Boatmen (船人) and passengers (船客) passing carefully through the swift currents winding by the rock were enchanted by the beautiful maiden's (處女) appearance (姿態) and song, gazing only at the rock. Unknowingly (不覺中), their boats (船體) were gradually swept into the rapid current (急流), struck the Lorelei rock wall (石壁), and finally, boat and people together became vengeful spirits (怨魂) of the Rhine River. Since ancient times (自古), German poets (獨逸詩人) who have put this legend into poetry are not just one or two, but among them, Heine's (하이네) poem is the most famous (有名), and the melody (曲調) by Friedrich Silcher (후리-드릿히질헤룸) is said to be the best. (By custom, the musical score should be inserted here, but due to unavoidable printing circumstances (印刷上 不得已한 事情), it could not be inserted. Readers, please forgive (恕諒) this.) <137>

Somehow, though I don't know why, it pierces my heart,
The tale passed down from old times soaks my body.
The Rhine River flows quietly,
The setting (入) sun, wrapped in evening glow, shines beautifully.

A lovely maiden's (處女) body stands upon the rock,
Holding a golden comb (梳) in hand, her loosened hair
She combs while singing, her song's voice
Strange and wondrous, my soul takes flight.

The passing boat passenger (船客) is captivated by the song,
Not seeing the rock cliffs, looking only upwards,
Engulfed in the waves, boat and person.
Strange is the witch's song, Lorelei.

Please excuse any mistranslations (誤譯). With the author's humble talent (才操), I cannot translate it any better (善譯) than this. As briefly mentioned in the first issue of our magazine (雜志), I obtained approval (承認) for the translation rights (譯權) from the original playwright (原脚色者), Mr. Kusuyama (楠山先生), while I was a student (在學當時). However, a long time (長久한 時日) passed, and I didn't know what had become of the matter. When I started the translation, Mr. Kusuyama's address was unknown (住所不明), so I sent a letter (書面) to Mr. Akita (秋田先生), explaining the reason for the translation and entrusting (付託) him with conveying the message to Mr. Kusuyama. At the end of last month (去月 晦間), I received a reply (回報) from Mr. Akita stating that Mr. Kusuyama had gladly consented (喜諾). The translator expresses his gratitude (謝意) to Mr. Kusuyama Masao (楠山正雄) and Mr. Akita Ujaku (秋田雨雀).

Second Act
First Scene: Moscow (모즈코). Stakhov's (스담홉읍) House
A study decorated in an old-fashioned (古風) Russian style (露西亞軆).
On the right side (右便) towards the audience (觀客席), a room door (房門) leading to the outer door (外門). On the inner right side (內橫側), the dining room door (食堂門). The interior decoration (內部의 裝飾) is visible. On the left side (左便), a large wooden door (板門) leading to the parlor (客室). The stage front (舞臺正面) has large glass doors (琉璃門). Beyond them is a veranda. Following its steps down leads to a vast flower garden (花園) with dense trees (樹木이 鬱蒼). Windows (窓) are on the left and right (左右) of the front.
Indoors (室內): A round table (圓机), a flower vase stand (花投机), a writing desk (寫字机) and chairs (椅子), a sofa, a Turkish chair (土耳其椅子), a piano, a book table (書卓), sculptures (彫刻像), oil paintings (油畵像), a full-length mirror (體鏡?), etc. <138>
The overall decoration (全體) reflects an old-fashioned military taste (軍人的嗜好) and an imitation (模倣) of Paris style (파리스타일).
A hot and clear (晴明) day, around afternoon (午後頃).
Elena. (With a cheerful expression (快活한 表情), puts down the book (冊) she was reading and, mixing sighs and yawns (합폐음?), speaks) Ahh, I'm tired. They say Bulgarian is originally the same as Russian, but it's still quite difficult. He just says it's easy for no reason; he's quite a liar too. (Takes out her pocket watch (懷中時計) and smiles brightly) It's already half-past one. It's time for him to arrive. I still don't know anything, what should I do? The teacher (先生님) must be worried now. Why would he do that? Mr. Insarov (인사롭 先生님). (Takes out a photograph (寫眞) from the desk drawer (冊箱) and kisses (接物?) it) Still playing innocent (시치미 뚝 낫고 잇다눔). How hateful. (Puts the photograph in the desk drawer (冊床설합)) - But still, it's strange. For the past few days, I've just been talking with that Madonna in the painting. (Goes to the oil painting (油畵)) Ah, you are always there alone, looking so stifled. As for me, I haven't been bored lately. Dimitri Insarov—that's right. I have a wonderful friend named Dimitri Insarov. I can always be alone. No friends, no siblings (兄弟). How have I, your dutiful child, survived all this time? Bersenev (벨세넵) and Shubin (슈빈) are good people, but still, they're not as good as him. Ah! I think he's coming. I hear footsteps. Maybe he's here. What should I do? If I stay hunched over like this, he'll come in, right? (Goes before the Madonna painting and bows silently (黙禮))
(Old Man Uval (우왈老人), smoking a long pipe, opens the door and enters. Seeing Elena's back, he looks surprised and makes a comical gesture, stepping back.)
Elena. You've come. Do you know where I am? (Raises her head and sees the Old Man) I thought it was someone else, Uncle (아제시).
Old Man Uval. Hmm? It's Uncle.
Elena. Why did you come? I was completely fooled. (Rushes over and pushes the Old Man's swaying body into the Turkish chair (土耳其椅子).)
Old Man Uval. Playing tricks again.
Elena. So what? It doesn't matter, does it? Uncle, I did this for you, right? (Rubs her head against him) Let me kiss you here. Your bald patch is showing nicely. (Kisses the back of his head.)
Old Man Uval. Hmph! (Waves both hands strangely) What is this behavior? <139>
Elena. (In an increasingly soft tone) Um. Uncle? Were you nicely bald like this when you were young too?
Old Man Uval. What, young? When I was a Second Lieutenant in the Army, participating in the Napoleonic (나파륜) War—
Elena. Your bearing (態度) was manly, and your dancing (舞蹈) was superb. I've heard it often. But your hair, then and now—
Uval. One needs a bald forehead to fall in love (戀愛).
Elena. What? Uncle, have you ever been in love?
Uval. Of course, I have! That's why I know the look in the eyes of a woman in love.
Elena. They say roses (薔薇꼿) bloom in the eyes of a woman in love.
Uval. That's right! And that's why roses have bloomed in your eyes too.
Elena. You're just lying for no reason— I'm not in love or anything like that.— I just feel happy in my heart for no reason. Everything makes me so happy I can hardly bear it. I don't know if I've gone mad.
Old Man Uval. Why wouldn't you be?
Elena. Think whatever you want. La la la, la la la. (Taps her foot to the rhythm (拍子).)
(Shubin opens the parlor door and looks out.)
Elena. (Seeing Shubin) Ah! Pavel (바벨). I have something to tell you.
Shubin. It's someone different. (Shrugs his shoulders and goes back inside.)
Elena. Why is he angry? (Makes a pouting face.)
(Looks at the hanging clock (걸닌 時計).)
Elena. (Suddenly tense) Oh my, it's already two o'clock. What's happened to him? Maybe he's not coming after all. But there's no reason (理) for that. After making such a promise (約條). He's not the kind of gentleman who would break a promise.— Oh dear. Did something happen? Yesterday when I saw him, his face looked strange somehow. I hope nothing bad happened. It seemed like he was deliberately hiding feeling unwell. I hope he hasn't fallen ill.— I'm so worried I can't stand it. Should I go now? What if I miss him on the way? I can't go, what should I do? (Goes towards the door with an anxious attitude.)
(Laughter and talking sounds are heard from outside the door.)
Elena. (Stops suddenly, thinking something is strange.) <140>
(Stakhov (스담호웁), in First Lieutenant's (中尉) uniform (軍服), ushers in Secretary (書記官) Klutovsky (클라트우스키이).)
Stakhov (스담호든). (Seeing Elena) Elena. A guest has arrived. - First Secretary (一等書記官), this is my daughter (女息). Please, make yourself acquainted.—
Klutovsky (클나트위스키이). It's a pleasure to meet you for the first time. I am Klutovsky, First Secretary of the Senate (元老院 一等書記官). (Offers his hand.)
Elena. (With a stiff, reluctant expression. Wordlessly offers her hand.)
Stakhov (스담호들). Where did your mother go?
Elena. I don't know (알 수 업세요).
Stakhov (스담호들). What do you mean, you don't know? Hmm?
Elena. I said I don't know because I don't know.— Please excuse me for a moment (잠간 失禮합니다). (Starts to leave.)
Stakhov (스담호든). (Roars like a tiger) Stay still! Where are you going? Where? We have a guest. Where are you going—
Elena. My apologies. I thought there was nothing important to do.
Stakhov (스담호듯). Show the guest into the parlor (客室).— What are you all doing? (Bustles (紛走이) back and forth, shouting loudly (大聲으로)) Anna! Uval! (The Old Man, who was dozing in the Turkish chair with his pipe in his mouth, is startled by the noise and comes forward. Stakhov spots him. He stands stiffly with a strange attitude. Stakhov just continues shouting) Pavel (바위엘)! Zoia (소오아)! Vasily (빠시리)! Minna Petrovna (민나삐요틀)! Masha (마아샤)!
(Responding to the call, Anna (안나) leads the way as six or seven men and women enter from various doors, whispering among themselves and lining up (整列).)
All. (Almost simultaneously (同時에)) Yes, we're here.
Stakhov. (Frowning (양미간을 쩝푸리고)) First Secretary, this is my wife (室人). (Introduces Anna.)
Anna. Welcome.
Klutovsky. Madam (貴婦人), I am First Secretary Klutovsky. (Shakes hands (握手).)
Stakhov. Elena, guide (引導) him in.
Elena. (Shows a pained, agonized (苦悶的) expression during this) Yes.
(With a reluctant (不得已한) attitude, leads the way into the parlor.)
(The Secretary follows behind.)
(Anna and the others start to leave gradually.)
Stakhov. Anna, wait a moment. It's alright if you don't go. (Stands apart.)
(The others have gone into the parlor or out the outer door.) <141>
Anna. (Sitting in a chair) What is it? Who is this guest who just arrived? (Gestures towards the parlor.)
Stakhov. (Striding back and forth with large steps) That's it. That's why I told you to stay. (Hurries over to his wife's side and speaks in a low voice) As I mentioned earlier, that gentleman's name is Egor Andreevich Klutovsky (에고오울 안트레위치 클라트 위스키)—he's the First Secretary (一等書記官) of the Senate (元老院)—
Anna. I knew that already. But why is that gentleman at our house—
Stakhov. Just listen without saying anything. There's an order to things when speaking. The reason I've started socializing (交際) with such a gentleman is this: even now, there isn't a single moment (一時 一刻) when I don't think (生覺) about family matters— Why are you looking down on people? You think I just wander around playing all my life without caring for the household, just saying things like this. It's not like I enjoyed dance parties or women's gatherings from the moment I was born. When I come home, my wife (녀편네) just complains she's sick with a pale face, devoid of a drop of blood as if her heart has burst—
Anna. So what am I saying? Just do as you please.
Stakhov. Well, let's not talk about that kind of thing. Whether what I'm doing is right or wrong, time (時日) itself will naturally judge (判斷), so there's no need to argue this or that. But one thing to remember (記憶) is that I am a person who not only knows my duty (義務) in any situation (境遇), but also knows what must be done as the head of the household (一家의 家長). (Gradually shifts to a speech-like tone) So, the matter to consider right now (目前) is Elena. Isn't it time to find her a match—that is, I think she must get married. How old is she now?
Anna. She's twenty.
Stakhov. So, what's a twenty-year-old girl doing? Acting like some great philanthropist (慈善家), loving stray dog-like beggar children (子息), sleeping with beggar girls, and not only that, but lately, talking about philosophy (哲學) or literature (文學) she doesn't even understand, flipping through difficult books (冊張) distractedly (精神업시), chasing after artists (美術家) or some philosopher (哲學者). And is that all? Hanging around day and night with some foreign fellow from Montenegro (몬대네그로) or whatever, whose seed (種子) he is I don't even know, just causing trouble— So, if she keeps this up, will nothing happen? In my opinion (生覺), if she doesn't start acting a bit more like a human being, eventually this Stakhov house will be ruined. <142>
Anna. Don't say such things.
Stakhov. Just listen to me for a bit. But let's not get sidetracked. To put it simply. I'm thinking of deciding on that gentleman over there (저기 잇는) (pointing to the parlor) as our son-in-law, and therefore, I intend to cultivate a close relationship (親密이 交際) with him. I don't know what you think, wife (마누라), but in my opinion (生覺), such a position is not easy to come by, and it doesn't seem like a rash act either. That man is probably thirty-three this year. You can't say he's young (졈-다고), but you certainly can't say he's old. Not only that, he graduated from the highest law school, his manners (禮義) and behavior (行動) are dignified, he's a First Secretary, a council member of the university, and he even received the Order of St. Stanislav (스다니스라 頸章).— However, let me make one thing clear: I'm not blinded by his position (地位) or decorations (動章). I'm definitely not like other common parents who don't look at the person involved but only consider the family background, wealth (財産), or status. As you always say, wife, Elena likes men who are active (活動) in real society (實社會), so I think (生覺) there couldn't be a more ideal (理想的) husband than Mr. Klutovsky, who has wide connections (交際가 널고) and is well-versed in real society. Not only that, Elena likes charity (慈善), right? Mr. Klutovsky too, limiting himself to what doesn't hinder (障碍되지 안이하는대 限해서) his own living (生活) from his income (收入). Do you understand? Understand? After securing enough for his living, he plans to distribute (分配) the entire (全部) inheritance (財産) from his father to his siblings (兄弟). Isn't he a splendid philanthropist?
Anna. Really? By the way, what does his father do...
Stakhov. His father, you ask? This gentleman is also a splendid man. He's a moralist (道德家) with a stern (嚴肅), stoic nature. That spirit (氣象) has been passed down (傳해 왓지) to his son. Originally a retired Major (退職少佐)—
Anna. But now—
Stakhov. Now, you mean? Now he resides as the general manager (總營理人) managing (營理) the estate (領地) of Count Ssky (스키이 伯爵) in the countryside—
Anna. Heh. <143>
Stakhov. What 'heh'? After I went to the trouble of explaining—
Anna. Who said anything?
Stakhov. What do you mean you didn't say anything? Well, never mind that. Anyway, just know that my intention is such. By the way, have you prepared something for entertaining (待接) him? You must pay special (特別) attention (注意) not to be negligent (疎忽하지 안토록). This guest is different from the one who rolled in from Montenegro (몬데네구로).
Anna. (Looking into the dining room) It's already prepared. I told the cook (料理하는 반가) to make it French style (佛蘭西세).
Stakhov. I don't know about such things.— Anyway, I've been too rude (失禮) leaving the guest with Elena or Pavel (바우엘). I should go see him myself.
(Suddenly makes the sound of his sword (佩劍) clanking absurdly and strides into the parlor.)
Anna. (With a dumbfounded look, watches him go for a moment, sighs, looking worried, stands up, looks into the dining room, says something, then comes back out, looks around the room, straightens chairs and furniture (什器갓튼 것), and also enters the parlor. From the parlor, laughter has been heard occasionally (間間이), and gradually the conversation (談活) picks up. As Anna enters, laughter is heard, and then Secretary Klutovsky, Lieutenant Stakhov, and Anna emerge, walking in a line. Behind them, Shubin and Old Man Uval, and Zoia (괴쇼오야? typo for 소오아/Zoia) also come out.)
Anna. Please come into the dining room.
Klutovsky. I'm imposing too much— (Turns his head and looks at the framed oil painting (油繪額面)) Haha, Great Ancestor Konstantin (큰스단 太公?), splendidly painted.
Stakhov. Are you speaking of that? To me, I don't know what art (美術) is, but I know it's splendidly painted.
Klutovsky. I feel the same way. Things like art— (Glancing disdainfully at Shubin) I mean, for people like us who are absorbed (沒頭) in practical affairs (實務), it's truly a pitiful object (物件). Hahaha.
(A servant (給仕) in white clothes opens the dining room door. Klutovsky goes first, followed by the others.)
(Shubin shrugs his shoulders, mimicking Klutovsky's attitude (態度) making Zoia laugh. Old Man Uval follows behind with strange gestures.) <144>
(The scattered sounds of everyone taking their seats (着席), the clinking of glasses (琉璃그럭) and silverware (쇠 그것), and the laughter of many people create an elegant rhythm (閑雅한 리슴) with a lonely (寂寥) mood (氣分).)
(The stage is empty (空虛) for a brief moment (暫間동안).)
Voice of a slender (細) girl. (Comes from among the garden trees (庭園樹木裡).)

Does it think there's another unknown world (世界)?

Miss, miss. Miss Elena (에레나 아가씨).
(From the garden trees (庭園樹林), a blind (盲目) girl, Kaja (가아쟈), emerges. She climbs the terraced steps (層層臺), wanders here and there, calling sensitively (銳敏하게) in a low voice.)
Miss, miss. Miss Elena (에레나 아가끼 - typo for 아가씨). (Receiving no answer no matter how she calls, she loses heart and goes back down the steps. She disappears into the trees along that path.)
(Shortly after, the dining room door opens, and Elena appears, looking around as if avoiding someone's eyes.)
Elena. (Towards the garden, in a low voice) Kaja (가야쟈), Kaja. Kaja (가아쟈). Where did she go? She must have gone back already. Without Kaja— Really, Mr. Insarov (인사롭氏) isn't coming. (Smacks her lips and goes out onto the veranda.)
(Collides with Bersenev (벨세엡) coming up onto the veranda.)
Elena. Ah, Andrei Petrovich (안드레이베트로위치), where are you going?
Bersenev. Where are you going?
Elena. Nowhere. I'm not going anywhere. Just now, Kaja came by singing the song she always sings, but I can't see where she went.
Bersenev. Yes, you mean the beggar girl. I just met her on the street. She probably left because no one was here. You must have guests today. It seems very busy (紛雜).
Elena. What guest? Just a passing visitor came. <145>
Bersenev. Then I'll come again. (Makes to leave quickly.)
Elena. (Hurriedly grabs Bersenev's hand and pulls him inside.)
No. It's alright. It doesn't matter. They're busy eating right now— Here, sit down. (Forces him into the Turkish chair and sits beside him.)
Elena. (Sitting beside him, tightly grips his hands and speaks forcefully) I have something to ask you. Andrei Petrovich, you came because you had something to tell me, didn't you?
Bersenev. Yes,— How did you know that?
Elena. You don't need to know that. Just tell me. No, I roughly know already. That you came because you had urgent news. From the moment you arrived—
Bersenev. Is that so?— Then I'll tell you. It's nothing else but this, Miss Elena. Did Insarov come here today?
Elena. No. He promised (約束) to come today, but—
Bersenev. Insarov is leaving on a trip (旅行) today. Right away, leaving this land. Leaving my master (主人).
Elena. (Convulsively (痙攣的으로)) What? Dimitri Nikarovitch (지미도리, 니가로윗티?) is going on a trip?— Today? Right away, leaving this land— Without even coming to say goodbye.— What is this? Why is that? For what reason (까닭)? (Stands up and paces back and forth, unsure what to do.)
Bersenev. As you know. That man doesn't talk about this or that when he does something. But in my opinion. Come and sit here. Why does your face look so pale? But in my opinion, deciding to go on a trip so suddenly must mean there's some reason—
Elena. Yes? What reason? Did something happen? What is it? (Sits beside him, grips Bersenev's hand tightly, looking thoughtful) Ah! Previously, didn't Dimitri Nikarovitch once go somewhere without a word of coming or going, and was gone for quite a while?
Bersenev. That's right. But at that time, there were two people who went with him.
Elena. That's right. They were also Bulgarians, weren't they? Dimitri himself mentioned they weren't such good-looking people, and something funny happened. They were comrades (同志) too, weren't they.— At that time, a conflict (衝突) arose among the comrades <146> and he went to mediate (仲裁).
Bersenev. That's what he said.
Elena. Yes. And also about planning (計劃) a great movement (大運動) with his comrades. Saying it makes it sound simple, but it's truly a spine-chilling (肝膽이 서늘할) matter.— But that time, though he left suddenly, he came back after three days. Maybe this time too, he'll come back after three or four days.
Bersenev. I wish it were so, but— perhaps it won't be like that.— This time, there's no one else, he's alone. Not only that, recently, no Bulgarians have come, and other than visiting your house daily and reading Bulgarian books, he has been spending peaceful and uneventful (平穩無事) days (歲月).
Elena. (Her face changes (變), speaks listlessly (氣脉업는)) Then it must be so.— Who can know?
Bersenev. (Smiling sorrowfully (愁心)) Should I say 'unfortunately'?— There's this story. It's already past, it was this spring. When I first got to know Insarov, one evening, I went with Insarov to my relative's house. At that time, there was a young lady in the relative's house who was quite a beauty (美人). Even the iron-willed (鐵石) Insarov seemed captivated by this maiden's beautiful appearance, so I asked him about it once. Insarov just smiled and said, 'Your thoughts are mistaken.' He added, 'As you think, my heart hasn't been drawn yet, but if it really were to become like that, I would have no choice but to immediately leave the danger zone (危險地帶).' When asked why, he said it was because he couldn't sacrifice (犧牲) the work (事業) for the nation (國民) to which he had dedicated his life for the sake of his own private pleasure (私事快樂) and happiness (幸福). He then continued, adding, 『There is not the slightest (毫末도) reason (理由) for a Bulgarian youth to love a Russian girl,』 thus concluding his remarks.
Elena. But— what does that incident have to do with me—
Bersenev. Are you saying it has no connection (關係)? Why is that? I think that what happened then might really be happening this time—
Elena. But... that's... to me... what... Please don't tell me such things.
Bersenev. Is that so? Truly. Insarov has now fallen in love with a Russian girl. Therefore <147> I think he has probably decided (決心) to leave this land immediately, intending to keep the oath (盟誓) he has spoken of all his life.
Elena. Ah, Andrei Petrovich—
(Suddenly, the laughter of three or four people comes from the dining room, sounding like people are emerging.)

Elena. (Hurriedly follows, grabbing his hand) Mr. Bersenev! You are wrong about many things. You are truly a good person, like an angel.— (Moves her lips as if finding it hard to speak) But... but... will he not come even once more?
Bersenev. (In a grave tone) He might want to come.— But if he does come, all his resolve will vanish.— Miss Elena, do you care for Mr. Insarov that much?
Elena. (In a tearful voice) Yes. Mr. Bersenev— Please forgive (容恕) me.

Elena. Mr. Bersenev! Look at me. Look— Is it really true that Mr. Insarov won't come even once more? (Dejectedly, her body sways, and she collapses onto the long chair.)
(Noise comes from the dining room, sounding like people are getting up from their seats. Simultaneously, Elena collects herself, stands up, goes into the parlor, puts on her hat, and, still wearing her summer coat, slips out into the garden. Clouds lower gradually, the sky darkens, and the roaring sound of wind and distant thunder (雷聲) are heard.)
(As Elena leaves, the dining room door opens immediately, and everyone comes out talking about something.)
Klutovsky. (Walking out alongside Stakhov) That's right. The primary weakness (缺點) of us Russians is always the idea (思想) of acting under some definite principle (確實한 主義). The idea of discarding all pretense (虛飾) and acting with one's own strength as capital (資本) <148> is it. Generally, the great flaw (흠) of Russians is being drawn to such vast empty fantasies (浩濶空想) as scholarship (學問) or art (藝術).
Stakhov. People who babble about art or whatever are often fellows with no principles (主義) or anything for today, right?
Klutovsky. That's right. Overall, art (藝術) is a poison (毒物) that leads the nation (國民) to extravagance (浮華). It's only because the state (國家) somehow manages to govern that such useless things (物件) remain.
Shubin. But artists, no matter how useless you say they are, don't take many bribes (賂物), whereas useless officials (官員) do take bribes.
Klutovsky. (Overbearingly) Taking bribes doesn't necessarily make one a criminal (罪人). There can be unavoidable circumstances (境遇) for that too.
Shubin. Oh? So bribes are also taken based on principle (主義)?
Klutovsky. Where did such talk come from? I just meant that even in taking bribes, there can be sympathetic (同情) circumstances. No matter the circumstances (事情), once discovered (發覺), one receives punishment without pardon (容赦), that's what I meant.
Shubin. So you mean punishing someone who hasn't sinned?
Klutovsky. That's right too. That's also because of principle (主義).
Shubin. This might be rude (失禮), but could you perhaps explain (說明) that principle (主義) clearly?
Klutovsky. (Mumbling) That's nothing other than— no, there's really no need to explain.
Stakhov. Of course (勿論)! Of course, no need for explanation, it's obvious.— Anyway, where did Elena go? I haven't seen her for a while. How can she be so rude (失禮) to a guest?
Anna. She's probably in her room. She said her head hurt, maybe it's quite bad. Shubin, go check on her.
(Shubin, Uval, Zoia (소오아) all understand and exit.)
(The wind grows stronger, and large raindrops fall.)
Stakhov. What's wrong with the weather? You won't be leaving right away, will you? Ah, right, Mr. Klutovsky, there's something I absolutely must get your critique (批評) on. I acquired it from America recently. (Hurries towards the parlor, looking at his wife) Anna, tell them to bring some tea— (Exits.)
Anna. (Nods politely to the guest and exits towards the dining room.) <149>
(Klutovsky sits alone in a chair and yawns (합흠). Just then, the dining room door opens, and Zoia pokes her head in.)
Zoia. (Showing only half her body (半身)) Miss Elena isn't here. (Notices the guest is alone) Oh— Excuse me (失禮햇슴니다). (Starts to leave.)
Klutovsky. Look here. Look here. (Calls her.)
Zoia. (Hesitates, then assumes an attentive tone) Is something wrong?
Klutovsky. Come here, come here.
Zoia. Yes. (Comes closer.)
Klutovsky. Here, this way. (Quickly grabs the girl's hand and seats her beside him) What's your name?
Zoia. Zoia Nikitishna Müller (소오아, 니키디이수나, 미유루럴).
Klutovsky. Oh. So your hometown is Germany (獨의)?
Zoia. No. My father is German.
Klutovsky. I see. Then you must be good at music. Let me see your hands. (Takes Zoia's hand firmly in both of his.)
Your hands are pretty. You probably play well. (Kisses her hand.)
Zoia. Eek. Why are you doing this?
Klutovsky. It's not 'why are you doing this'. It's true. (Stands up, holding the girl's hand) I like you. Why is the young lady of this house such a prude?— Let's play together. (Forces Zoia to the piano, opens the keyboard lid, and spreads out the sheet music (譜表).)
Zoia. (With a happy expression (喜色)) What should I play?
Klutovsky. Anything. Play your best piece (第一 長技) for me.
Zoia. Then... Pe... Pēpē? Or should I play Beethoven (베에트벤)? (Presses the piano keys to test the sound.)
Klutovsky. Pēpē or Beethoven, whatever. I prefer S— (Suddenly (突然), a powerful (强大) clap of thunder (雷聲) sounds. Zoia, startled, cries out and stands up. Klutovsky puts his arm around her neck, presses his face against hers, and kisses her. At that moment, the dining room door opens, and a boy (뽀이) brings in tea. **Summary**

This excerpt is Act 2, Scene 1 of the play "Overnight," translated/adapted by Hyŏn Ch'ŏl for the Korean magazine *Kaebyŏk* in 1920. It includes a preface discussing the Lorelei legend and acknowledging the original Japanese playwright Kusuyama Masao. The scene, set in the Stakhov house in Moscow, depicts Elena anxiously awaiting the Bulgarian nationalist Insarov. Her father, Stakhov, introduces a potential suitor, the pompous official Klutovsky. Elena learns from Bersenev (who secretly loves her) that Insarov plans to leave suddenly, likely because he has fallen for Elena but prioritizes his national cause. Distraught, Elena leaves as a storm begins. Klutovsky, left alone briefly, makes unwanted advances on the young German-Russian woman Zoia just as thunder crashes.

From a historical perspective, the publication in *Kaebyŏk*, a prominent nationalist and intellectual magazine during the Japanese colonial period, is significant. The translation itself reflects efforts towards cultural modernization and engagement with foreign literature (possibly Russian via Japanese adaptation). The play's themes, particularly Insarov's conflict between personal love (for Elena, a Russian) and national duty (for Bulgaria), could resonate with Korean intellectuals grappling with similar dilemmas under colonial rule. Klutovsky's character, representing arrogant and possibly corrupt officialdom, might also offer a veiled critique applicable to the colonial context.
