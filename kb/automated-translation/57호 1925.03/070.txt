A Dialogue Between A and B
						Tonggwan Saeng

A. It’s been a long time. It feels like I’m offering New Year’s greetings after the peach blossoms have bloomed, but how has this year been treating you?
B. You’re different now that you’re getting older, aren’t you? Knowing how to offer greetings and all, haha…
A. Hey—what kind of disrespectful talk is that? Let’s put that aside. Are you still indulging in gloomy thoughts (窮想) like before? I came today to bother you a bit—
B. Gloomy thoughts?
A. I mean poor thoughts. You always seem to indulge in truly poor thoughts. Anyway, I'm thinking of editing this March issue of the magazine in an XXXXXXXXXXX way, and actually, I came to respectfully hear your unique perspective—yes, that unique perspective—on the changes and transitions in Korean society or the world of thought since the Kiminyŏn (1919) year.
B. Oh, is that so? My unique perspective, that’s welcome news. But I have no choice but to decline. In truth, not only have I not grasped clear enough concepts to discuss the issue, but moreover, publishing it publicly in something like a magazine is even more out of the question. Still, XXXXXX was a well-thought-out idea. Have you gathered plenty of good material from various fields? But won't there be trouble if it goes beyond a certain extent?
A. Of course, there will be trouble. Just looking at the XX makes one’s blood rush, but anyway, regarding that issue, I absolutely must hear your view before I leave. If your concepts haven't formed yet, speaking disjointedly is fine too. Come on now, time is a bit tight. I can only be with you for two hours. I'll listen respectfully.
B. If you're short on time, you can leave. I don't want to be constrained by time on an issue I'm already reluctant to talk about.
A. Don’t be like that, just talk.＜41＞
B. Am I getting dragged along by this reporter fellow again? Fine, ask what you want to ask. I'll answer one by one.

A. How has the trend in the world of thought changed since the Kiminyŏn year?
B. —From what I've seen, I don't view it much differently from other countries. Although there might be differences in timing, sooner or later, I believe the trend of thought is the same. However, what evolved and changed gradually over a long period of 50 or 100 years in other countries seems to have been experienced rapidly in Chosŏn within just 10 years, centered around the Kiminyŏn year. This signifies that Chosŏn's social conditions irregularly underwent rapid upheaval and change. Since thought always depends on facts for its origin and development, the transition of society reflects the changes in thought.—The XXXXXX Movement, of course, was influenced by the waves of the Great European War's impact on the world. Taking that opportunity, like all other weak and small nations, it was a political XXXXXX movement. The European War began as a war of aggression by capitalism, and its conclusion merely symbolized the agony of capitalist nations. On the other hand, the proletarian revolution in Russia marked a turning point in world history, creating astonishment. Waves emanated from proletarian Russia; Poland gained a superficial political independence under the protection of capitalist nations; Czechoslovakia did likewise; Ireland can be seen as having achieved halfway success; India failed; the Philippines also failed; Egypt also failed due to British betrayal and oppression; and Chosŏn, too, while leaving behind significant waves, sacrifices, and contributions, saw little change in its political, economic, or social systems. I believe the result gave a great lesson to the Korean people and the world's weak and small nations. And taking that opportunity as a turning point, the Korean people's ideological awakening, the goal of political liberation, the international position of colonies, and their perspective on the world expanded.

A. Wait a moment. You said the result of the XX Movement brought no change to political demands or social systems, but gave a great lesson to the Korean people and the world's weak and small nations. What kind of lesson do you mean?
B. Hmm, that lesson? I see it in three parts. First, it means recognizing the clear facts about the motives for conquest by the state that seizes political rights—the conquering state.＜42＞ Second, it means gaining the experience that the XX of weak and small nations must be done in a certain way, or at least that succeeding in *this* way is impossible. Third, it means realizing where the goal of XX must lie.

A. Alright, I understand. But one thing to note, please try to use moderate language as much as possible. This is my predicament. I know you understand that much...
B. Yes, yes. Is this a warning from you? But I am saying what I think; whether it's moderate or not has nothing to do with me.
A. Yes, that may be so, but just a slightly more moderate wording—truly, this job is impossible. Humph...
B. —However, it's undeniable that the high-pressure policy in Chosŏn's actual social and political spheres reached its peak around the time of Kiminyŏn, and in reality, there has been no change even now. But at that time, the thought of the Korean people was very childish, viewable as an enlightenment period. The fact that the goal of this XX Movement demanded national political XX—that is, political XX—as mentioned above, shows this. Before Kiminyŏn, Chosŏn's political attitude could only be seen as pessimistic, fearful, and skeptical towards politics. It's a fact that thought was in just such a transitional period. Therefore, the ideological awakening after the XX Movement can largely be seen as the awakening of bourgeois liberalism. This is naturally related to the progression of the times. To understand this, one only needs to look at how the movement's goal at the time was proclaimed as purely national; this clearly shows the lack of distinct consciousness regarding class relations within the single nation. Now, the XX Movement, in principle, always explodes from life's realities—in other words, from conflicts of interest. At that time, the consciousness of conflict of interest against the conquering state reached extreme excitement, but the consciousness of conflict of interest within the single nation was vague, so there was a sense of being intoxicated by the slogan "national." Actually, nowadays—this might be a separate issue—the interpretation based on evolutionary theory seems to be the main trend regarding XX. This, of course, is based on the historical view and social evolution view according to historical materialism, and today it seems to have become the universal principle. However, I believe that while the goal of XX is success, even if it ends in failure, it still leaves a great historical significance and naturally impacts real life. Furthermore, unless one grasps the true meaning of evolution, similar to sudden mutation (突然 變種) in biology, a true interpretation of XX is＜43＞ difficult to achieve. Therefore, even if the failure of that XX results in temporarily fostering reactionary forces...

A. No, please leave out your theory of XX. Let's continue with what you were saying earlier.
B. Huh—another warning? You truly are an impossible person—. Anyway, some people might get angry if I say the XX Movement was a movement to acquire political freedom for the bourgeoisie, and that the ideological awakening after Kiminyŏn was bourgeois liberalism, but that reflects a childish understanding of the social circumstances and social evolution of that time. Since the movement during the XX Movement period was just that, despite the lack of nationwide, organized planning, its magnificence proves it fulfilled a significant historical role and provided crucial lessons for the direction of thought and movement.

A. Stop repeating the same point and summarize as much as possible.
B. Shall I stop then...
A. So what are you saying? The XX Movement was the ideological awakening of bourgeois liberalism for Koreans, and after that?
B. You’re determined to suppress it, aren’t you? Fine, I yield. Perhaps yielding to someone like you is my honor. So, the most prominent thing that arose in various regions after the Kiminyŏn year was the Youth Associations (靑年會). They all advocated nationalism, and as that thought spread, it can be seen as becoming fashionable. Consequently, one or two types of press organs, which hadn't existed before Kiminyŏn, emerged. They too can be seen as propagating bourgeois liberal thought and the nationalist movement. Meanwhile, we saw coordination between some progressive bourgeois liberal youth and elderly individuals advocating stubborn nationalism. Then, on another front, a central organization for various regional Youth Associations—the Federation of Youth Associations (靑年會聯合會)—was intermittently formed, centered around liberal youth. In other areas, various groups, gatherings, speeches, etc., became popular, but they too did not deviate from the liberal thought that promoted the national spirit. Thus, it seems the Tong-a Ilbo (東亞日報) as a press organ and the Federation of Youth Associations as an organization served as central institutions. And the attitude of the movement, previously passive denial and skepticism, shifted towards a proactive stance, attempting to push forward, leading to preparations being planned "movement-wise."＜44＞ However, amidst this, there is an ideological trend and the budding of a movement that we should not overlook: socialist thought based on the proletariat (無産階級). This became clearly recognized two years after the XX Movement occurred. Here, the Russian Revolution provided a tremendous impetus. But of course, its ideological system was not distinct. Within it were anarchists, communists, state socialists, adherents of a mix of these, and nationalists merely tinged with these colors—a situation where one or two youths in various places were tentatively poking their heads up. Generally speaking, they emphasized sentiment, humanitarianism, and concepts of justice, leaning towards a so-called radical anarchist coloration. At that time, it would not be an exaggeration to say they were so few throughout Chosŏn that you couldn't count them on ten fingers. Simultaneously, a deformed labor organization with strong tones of labor-capital cooperation, the Labor Mutual Aid Society (勞働共濟會), emerged. This, of course, was not a group that arose with laborers as its background; it was mostly organized centering on progressive bourgeois youth (among whom were a few real traditionalists, sangt'ujaengi - 상투쟁이). They published a magazine called "Mutual Aid" (共濟) as their organ at the time, whose contents were also a jumble of anarchism, communism, humanitarianism, sentimentalism—all sorts of things. Following the occurrence of these few facts, youths with newly sharpened spirits from the proletariat, awakened to socialist thought by social forces, emerged. Ideological groups with this socialist coloration arose in various places. In recent years, their influence has grown such that the movement centered on this faction has become the leading trend. The Federation of Youth Associations, which advocated nationalism, was dissolved, and the General Federation of Youth (靑年總同盟), advocating the proletariat, was founded. The General Federation of Laborers and Farmers (勞農總同盟) was founded and has already been active for a year—isn't this the current situation? Therefore, in their movement, being proletarian, the goal of XX is pointed out not to be merely political but economic. They recognized that their demand for political rights is ultimately based not on the encompassing meaning of "national" but on class-based acquisition of proletarian political power. They realized that the greatest weapon of this movement lies in class solidarity and that the goal can only be achieved through the concentration of the movement's organizational power. Thus, we can see the promotion of international spirit consciousness aiming for international XX. Consequently, this proletarian consciousness found points of convergence with Marx's communism. So, this is roughly how the trend of thought in Chosŏn has flowed until today,＜45＞ as I see it. To summarize, it has transitioned from liberal thought to socialist thought. And what remains unchanged and has intensified throughout this period is the oppression by the authorities.—

A. Let's talk about that separately.
B. Another warning—huh?

A. Alright. Listening to you so far, two things come to my mind. Aren't you always neglecting the masses and focusing on a small group of intellectuals, or perhaps the vanguard? Second, isn't the force of the movement advocating nationalism for the acquisition of political liberties still standing implicitly? Aren't you dismissing it too much as belonging to the past?
B. Of course, those doubts are natural. There hasn't been even a dream of any relaxation or improvement in the lives of the Korean people from before Kiminyŏn to now; rather, falling into further distress is the current situation. Therefore, the suffering of the entire masses within this situation due to capitalist exploitation is proportionally increasing—that is the current reality. Accordingly, the reflection of their lives, the intensification of labor disputes and tenant farming disputes, is also today's reality. Despite this, saying I neglected the masses means that the focal point in the awakening of class consciousness was placed on the small number of proletarian intellectuals; that is unavoidable. Of course, it doesn't mean I disregarded the masses viewed as the center and object of the liberation movement. In essence, it means they are at the vanguard of the masses. Also, it might be true that the forces of both the national movement and the social movement still stand. However, I foresee the inevitable necessity of cooperation up to a certain class between the progressive national movement and the social movement in the future. Therefore, the left-wing elements of that national movement will eventually jump under the banner of the proletariat. The centrist or right-wing elements have degenerated into an autonomy movement or a movement for suffrage rights. Thus, their movement currently has strong reactionary colors, and in the future too, they will likely become absorbed in reactionary power-acquisition movements. However, the mass movement will gradually progress in accordance with social conditions. Regarding the national movement and social movement, you can understand my views well by referring to my articles published in Kaebyŏk: "Towards the Left (왼편을 向하야)," "Standing on the Left (왼편을 밟고서)," "The Goal of Liberation (解放의 目標)," "Rights to Liberty and Rights to Survival (自由權과 生存權)." So I'll stop here for now. Shall we stop? My head is pounding. (Continued on page 50)＜46＞
(-Continuation from page 46- original text)
A. That's roughly understandable. Besides this, I'd like to ask about your views on the lessons of the March 1st movement, the goal of XX, and the international position of colonies, but let's stop here for today. Eh—thank you very much for your trouble. I'll treat you sometime soon, huh...
B. Hey. Let's not publish that in the magazine.
A. You're talking nonsense again. I endured all sorts of unpleasant talk just to write this for the magazine—does that make sense?
B. Right, but the more I think about it, the less appealing it seems. Then at least let me see the draft—
A. That's not difficult. Well then, I'll be going for today. Let's meet again soon...
B. Take care. Ugh—my head hurts—＜50＞

**Summary**

The article presents a dialogue between A (a journalist) and B (an intellectual, likely the author Tonggwan Saeng) in March 1925. A seeks B's perspective on the changes in Korean thought and society since the 1919 March First Movement (referred to euphemistically as the Kiminyŏn or XX Movement due to censorship). B argues that Korea underwent an accelerated ideological transformation compared to other nations, shifting from a bourgeois nationalist awakening triggered by the 1919 movement to a growing socialist consciousness influenced by the Russian Revolution and focused on class struggle and economic liberation. B critiques the initial movement's limitations (lack of class awareness) and charts the rise of youth groups, labor organizations (like Nodong Kongjehoe, Ch'ŏngnyŏn Ch'ongdongmaeng, Nonong Ch'ongdongmaeng), and socialist thought, viewing it as the dominant progressive trend, while predicting that nationalist factions would either join the proletarian cause or degenerate into collaborationist autonomy movements. A repeatedly urges caution and moderation, highlighting the risks of expressing such views under colonial rule.

From a historical perspective, the article is highly valuable. It offers a contemporary intellectual's analysis of the ideological shifts in colonial Korea just six years after the March First Movement, capturing the disillusionment with Wilsonian self-determination and the rising influence of Marxism/socialism following the Russian Revolution. The candid discussion of the transition from purely nationalist goals to class-based analysis, the formation of specific socialist-leaning organizations, and the explicit mention of censorship (XX) provide insight into the intellectual currents, political suppression, and evolving strategies within the Korean independence and social movements of the mid-1920s. Historians would find the detailed tracing of ideological evolution and the critique of bourgeois nationalism from a proto-socialist viewpoint particularly interesting.
