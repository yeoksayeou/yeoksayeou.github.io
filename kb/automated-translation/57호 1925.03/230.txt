Waiting Heart for a Storm, and others
						Yi Sanghwa

From long, long ago
Ah, from hundreds, thousands of years ago
Its flesh stripped away by hoe and spade,
Its inner fat stolen by potato and millet,
Upon the bare ground of a mountain village left with only bones,
Ah, people still hope for a harvest.

On this slow spring day that brews idleness,
"I have suffered so..."
The paddies and fields reveal their stones –
There, as if dozing, hoeing,
I see the life of the farming person.

Knowing it is earth without heart or mouth,
Yet meticulously digging, asking for even a little more,
In their breasts, deserving of curses,
There is still the self-contentment that fate provides. <53>
There is still the submission cooled by self-contentment.

Lazy white clouds drift even in the sky,
And weary silence spreads even on the land,
Oh – on such a day, at such a time,
To shatter the gloom of this land and my heart,
I pray – let a storm pour down from the East Sea.
- From "Gift of Escape" -

Song of the Sea
- A song where my spirit mingled with the waves and brought back the heart of the East Sea -
Come to me, O person, come to me.
Like the delirium of a sick child,
Forget all the old philosophies and worn-out holy teachings,
And just come to me, bearing your sorrow.

Here is the freedom to scoff at God,
And here too is youth that does not age.
Leaving the tear-soaked world, come to me who laughs,
Ah, and realize that life exists only in change.
- From "The Same" -<54>
<53-54>

**Summary**

These two poems by Yi Sanghwa express a profound sense of despair, stagnation, and yearning for radical change or escape. The first poem depicts the bleak, unchanging suffering of rural Korean farmers under harsh conditions, culminating in a desperate prayer for a destructive storm to break the oppressive silence and gloom. The second poem offers the sea as an alternative—a place of freedom from old doctrines, eternal youth, and the acceptance of life as constant change, inviting the suffering individual to find solace there. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, these poems are revealing. They articulate the deep-seated frustration and hopelessness felt under Japanese rule, where the land and people are depicted as exhausted and exploited ("stripped away," "stolen"). The intense desire for a violent "storm" can be interpreted as a metaphor for a wish for upheaval or liberation, while the call of the sea represents a longing for escape from the unbearable reality of colonial subjugation. Historians might find the raw expression of suffering, the critique of passive acceptance ("self-contentment," "submission"), and the yearning for cataclysmic change or complete escape significant indicators of the psychological landscape of intellectuals during that period.

暴風雨를 기다리는 마음 外
						李相和
오랜 오랜 녯적부터
아 몃 백년 몃 천년 녯적부터
호미와 가래에서 등심살을 빗기이고
감자와 기장에게 속기름을 빼앗기인
산촌의 뼈만 남은 땅바닥 우에서
아즉도 사람은 수확을 바라고 잇다.

게으름을 비저내는 이 느진 봄날
「나는 이러케도 시달렷노라... 」
돍맹이를 내보이는 논과 밧 -
거게서 조으는 듯 호미질하는
농샤짓는 사람의 목숨을 나는 본다.

마음도 입도 업는 흙인줄 알면서
얼마라도 더 달나고 정성끗 뒤지는
그들의 가슴엔 저주를 바들
숙명이 주는 자족이 아즉도 잇다. ＜53＞
자족이 식힌 굴종이 아즉도 잇다.

한울에도 게으른 힌구름이 돌고
땅에서도 고달픈 침묵이 까라진
오 - 이런 날 이런 때에는
이 땅과 내 마음의 우울을 뿌술
동해에서 폭풍우나 소다저라 - 빈다.
- 「遁竄의 선물」에서 -

바다의 노래
- 나의 넉, 물결과 어우러저 동해의 마음을 가저온 노래 -
내게로 오느라 사람아 내게로 오느라
병든 어린애의 헛소리와 가튼
묵은 哲理와 날근 聖敎는 다 이저버리고
애통을 안은 채 내게로만 오느라.

한우님을 비웃을 자유가 여게 잇고
늙어지지 안는 청춘도 여게 잇다.
눈물저즌 세상을 바리고 웃는 내게로 와서
아 생명이 변동에만 잇슴을 깨처 보아라.
- 「仝上」 -＜54＞
