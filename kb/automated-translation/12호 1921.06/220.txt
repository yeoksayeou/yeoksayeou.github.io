잡지명	개벽 제12호
발행일	1921년 06월 01일
기사제목	未知의 世界(對話) (The Unknown World (Dialogue))
필자	林蘆月
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Other)

未知의 世界
						林蘆月
第一部

Ch’an-ho: Hey, what are you thinking so hard about? Caught by the demon of lust again, I see.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Ah—who is it that I truly love? Can loving love itself be this passionate? Do I love all the heroines of novels? Hah—
Ch’an-ho: What kind of crazy talk is that? Stop it and just confess. Who is this person, anyway? Ah, right, it must be your landlord's daughter, isn't it? Was her name Sŭng-ja?
Pyŏng-sŏn: No. I have been deceiving you until now. My saying that I love OO-ja, or that my mother wasn't my birth mother, or that I currently have consumption—it was all lies. But I truly am an orphan without a mother, a sufferer of an incurable disease, and someone's lover, always living among sorrowful people touched by mournful melodies. Should this life be called false? No, for me alone, it is true life. I gather fantasies into reality like this, and gather reality into fantasy. This is my art.
Ch’an-ho: You really are a liar.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Do you think fantasy is falsehood? No, there is nothing as creative and wondrous as fantasy. What else are the numerous artistic works that ignite our passion, if not the products of fantasy? And what else caused modern life to plunge into extreme doubt and sorrow, if not the fictional character named "Hamlet" (하무레트)? Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, this chaotic life... receiving the limitations of blind fate, our inherently imperfect actions within existence, the most abstract of all, how do you seek to discover something more eternal, more wondrous, by rejecting fantasy and imagination?... Do you really think a life of fantasy is false? No. Why is it that unrequited love and heartbreak stir our passion more intensely than actual love? Hey, fantasy becomes reality for the artist. Your thinking of God<119> is a fantasy. That's why God truly became existent. Do you think of Goodness and Truth? These too are fantasies. But aren't Goodness and Truth governing your actions right now? In this way, what is all existence if not a fantastical, psychological existence? Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, do you wish to become a saint? Then, after calming your mind, immerse yourself in reading something like the *Biography of Shakyamuni* (釋伽傳). Then, like Shakyamuni, how many tears of compassion will you shed to save humanity from birth, old age, sickness, and death? Do you also wish to become the protagonist of a tragedy? Then read *"The Sorrows of Werther"* (엘텔의 悲哀) or perhaps *"The Tale of Ch'unhyang"* (春香傳). You will ultimately devote your entire being to profound passion. Action truly prevents us from becoming saints or lovers, but fantasy and imagination allow us to become saints and someone's lover. The actual protagonist of a tragedy enjoys nothing, but the spectator of a tragedy can enjoy everything. Therefore, our virtue lies entirely in fantasy and imagination. Only in fantasy and imagination can we beautify everything. If we lived in the age of Rome's "Nero" (네로), how we would hate "Nero"! However, when criticizing "Nero" within imagination, we can sympathize even with such a tyrant. He set the entire city of "Rome" (로마) ablaze to compose a poem of fire. This can be called an innocent game born entirely from artistic impulse. However, when "Nero," from atop a high platform, saw the people of "Rome" crying and screaming, the splendid art museums and public halls all burning, and the fierce flames rising like red coral in various forms, destroying the entire city of "Rome," "Nero" must have surely awakened in panic and repented. And the hot tears shed in agony must have flowed ceaselessly, finally making him cry out "O God!" (한우님) and regret his sin. I believe the profound tears "Nero" shed then were capable of washing his sins clean. Yes, the point where Jesus (耶蘇) forgave the sins of all mankind lies here: in imagining that the sinner's agony is greater than their sin. Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, the entire concept of the artist must reside wholly in fantasy and imagination. Ah—fantasy is not as worthless as worldly people say.
Ch’an-ho: Your words are entirely specious paradoxes.
Pyŏng-sŏn: True paradox is the truth of the superman.
Ch’an-ho: So, are you defending criminals who poison society? If there were just two pathological deviants like you in our Korea (朝鮮), it would be ruined, ruined...
Pyŏng-sŏn: But I love illness. Because it stimulates our imagination.<120> But don't call me pathological. I love illness, yes, but I am decidedly not pathological myself. And earlier, you criticized me for defending criminals... Those called evil by the people of the world were all people who were completely true to themselves.
Ch’an-ho: You truly are a heretic who ignores truth and morality.
Pyŏng-sŏn: As long as the law of evolution exists in human society, truth and morality are constantly negated. If we are to seek truth, we should seek it within art. Art, being the most perfect among all existences, is not governed by the law of evolution. No matter how human society changes, the skeptical thought of "Hamlet" (하무레트) will remain eternally unchanged, and even if Jesus (耶蘇) were to return and build paradise on earth, the pessimistic thought of "Schopenhauer" (소펭화-엘) or "Dostoevsky" (떠쓰도에푸스키) would remain just as unchanged.
Ch’an-ho: Then should we devote our entire existence to art?
Pyŏng-sŏn: If you wish to be eternally intoxicated by beauty and sorrow, you must certainly do so. Only then can you forget the ugliness of space and the clichés of time, and enter the most harmonious realm of ecstasy. Why hesitate when this astonishing world of magic allows our souls to enter the perfect realm of ideals? Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, are you still trying to compromise with reality? That is utter failure. No matter how much you try to enjoy life, life gives us nothing but boredom, falsehood, and regret—no happiness whatsoever. Unless we treat life as raw, unprocessed material, change its form, and create it anew, we can never enjoy it. Only then can we turn this whole world and humanity into art. Then, can we not perform astonishing miracles through wild dreams?
Ch’an-ho: Then you must certainly be opposed to the socialization of art, I suppose.
Pyŏng-sŏn: The phrase 'socialization of art' is a complete insult to the artist. If an artist truly has a world of their own creation, there is absolutely no need to discuss such issues. Those who speak of such things are pitiful common folk who have not yet created their own world to live in and are wandering lost in a fog.
Ch’an-ho: But doesn't art for art's sake poison life?<121>
Pyŏng-sŏn: Hey, that's a misunderstanding. Why? Because any art is entirely unrelated to action. Even if there were a connection, actions resulting from artistic impulse are decidedly dramatized. Therefore, even if one commits some evil, since it is an action entirely devoid of feeling (regarding the evil), the sin cannot harm the person. Rather, art for art's sake elevates life. Why? Because life generally becomes self-aware, awakens, and is reformed through fresh wonder...
Ch’an-ho: You, an advocate for ignoring morality and praising evil—I've truly never seen anyone like you.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Hey, people like us, born in such an astonishing era, must enjoy everything possible without hesitation. I argue that we attain happiness by immersing our souls in strange life. I praise evil because there is beauty and wonder within evil itself. I praise love suicides and adultery because there is passion like fire within them.
Ch’an-ho: It sounds plausible, but your words are too unconventional for me to understand well.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Who likes conventionality, anyway? For me, rationalism that stinks of common sense, or the philosophy and ethics of Confucius and Mencius which only approve of strict logic, are entirely useless. They all reject our development and bind our freedom. The greatest purpose of the artist must be solely the creation of mood and beauty, the revelation of wonder, the sublime, and the absolute. Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, let us, without the slightest hesitation, without being oppressed by the rigidity of morality or the violence of convention, devote our entire existence to fresh artistic passion.
Ch’an-ho: Your words are a kind of magic.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Hey, the time we encounter sublime sorrow is not during a real emergency, but when we have thrown ourselves into sublime dreams. Then, even hearing a dog bark on a desolate night sounds like a mournful lament for life, and seeing the shadows of falling leaves in late autumn makes one anticipate the destruction of the universe. Ah—Ch’an-ho my friend!! When we see a dark rock cave, we will want to cover the shame of our naked bodies; when we see a small, secret valley, the thought of crying freely will arise. And when night falls, the thought of using the stars and moon to travel to the ends of the world will come. And so, we will believe that desolate gardens and deserts will peacefully bury us. When we experience this great sorrow of the universe, we realize an infinite poetic nirvana.<122> Do you think such sublime sorrow harms us? No. Through such sorrow, we can be purified again. But reality mercilessly drags us out of such sublime sorrow and replaces it with despair and pain. Ah—for everything, we must turn towards art. Art makes us infinitely happy. Art is the easiest of all existences to appreciate.

第二部
Ch’an-ho: Let's stop talking about life. Since you said it's terribly difficult to enjoy life, let's enjoy nature instead. Come now, Pyŏng-sŏn my friend, come over here. Look. Just now, the sorrowful sunset lies on the horizon. Isn't this the time when the ears of rice pray silently and golden waves drift everywhere? Hey, let's truly enjoy nature. Ah—where could there be anything more beautiful than nature? Not only that. Nature truly possesses eternal youth, fresh joy, and perfect will. Hey, whenever I engage in pastoral life, I feel like I've returned to my boyhood, my heart becomes young. Seeing this, nature always keeps us young. If we are to rescue modern literature from the urban disease of this decadent final age, we must certainly return to nature.
Pyŏng-sŏn: The main reason modern literature has fallen ill with extreme clichés and conventionality is, contrary to your words, because it returned to nature. You say nature possesses eternal youth, but there is nothing more senile than nature... Today's stars are the stars of tens of thousands of years ago in antiquity, and today's pines and bamboos are the pines and bamboos of thousands of years ago. Ah—what is nature? Isn't the surface of the earth mostly decayed, teeming with numerous maggots? And the youth you spoke of is often feeble-mindedness. It is a corpse with no will, no unity, no vitality. You often say you go play near the villages, but look closely at the people living in nature. First, their appearance is coarse and ugly, and if you observe their inner lives, they probably lead entirely unchanging, monotonous, desolate lives. Their sole intellectual stimulation is limited in scope to the outhouse and the stream, and their religious feeling is confined to superstition and fear, oppressed by the blind, violent force of nature. Can literature written under such ugly and ignorant influence be an expression of fresh wonder, as you say?
In this sense, I declare the literature of naturalism a complete failure in its style.<123>
Ch’an-ho: But isn't nature the great mother who bore us? As far as I can see, the origin of art also seems to have come from an admiration for natural beauty.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Thinking so is your fundamental misunderstanding. Why? Because we certainly... feel boundless aesthetic emotion when we see violets living as companions to white butterflies in the wide fields of the green kingdom. But it's not because the violets are truly beautiful. It's simply because this flower is a symbol of love, and because the poet-saint "Shelley" (세-레-) composed and recited poems about it. And also, when we see pasqueflowers blooming near graves, the reason we feel sorrow is not because this flower called pasqueflower directly sheds tears towards us, but because the Tang dynasty poet Liu Xiyi (劉希夷) spoke of sad circumstances regarding the pasqueflower. Not only this, the reason we generally praise the lotus flower is because it is the ideal type envisioned by Buddhist painters of the East. Ah—Ch’an-ho my friend, nature is not the great mother, but the dream of styles created by poets. If, as you say, nature were the mother of life and art, Ch’an-ho my friend, wouldn't the farming of that rustic fellow in the field also be an artistic work, and consequently wouldn't those people be aesthetes? But unfortunately, they are neither artists nor aesthetes. Even if a farmer owns ten thousand fields, he cannot be the owner of the beauty of that scenery. This is the point that distinguishes us, the educated people. They don't know to lament the sunset passing over the peak like a colorful dream. And in the middle of an early autumn night, when the moon princess, dressed in blue, soars into the infinite heavens, ah—when all the reality entangled in conflict falls soundly asleep in the blue light, and all creation is recreated in mystery, wonder, and sorrow, they will probably be sleeping ignorantly. Isn't the nature they possess entirely dead nature? From this viewpoint, I declare that since the emergence of art derived thus, nature has imitated this art. Ah—it is as clear as day that nature is decidedly not the great mother who bore art. And pure natural beauty can never exist. True beauty escapes from nature and finds eternal life in art.
Ch’an-ho: It sounds plausible. So, must I concede that nature lacks beauty, will, and unity? Alright—let's grant that. But nature must be the god of peace, right? Though there's no room for debate on this, I always find that friendships become closer not when socializing in the corners of the city, but when friends go on excursions to places like the countryside; they seem much more affectionate. From this perspective, if we are to find eternal peace, we must inevitably return to nature.
Pyŏng-sŏn: Oh, Ch’an-ho my friend, how can you make such superficial observations?<124> That's entirely childish talk. No, nature loves war more than peace. Why? Because isn't there the law of the struggle for existence? Didn't nature crudely produce life in excess, ultimately causing even the Great European War? It is said that war occurs because food increases arithmetically while people increase geometrically. If nature possessed a perfect will, it should have limited the number of people. But nature, being inherently feeble-minded, probably lacks such knowledge. Wasn't it us, not nature, who advocated for population control? Ch’an-ho my friend, nature is truly cruel, merciless, and feeble-minded. It crudely produces life in order to massacre numerous lives, and builds houses on sand in order to destroy them. When I think about how it treats us like puppets for its amusement, we should bravely raise the flag of rebellion against the demon called nature, but among us pitiful beings, we are too ignorant and lack the courage to know this. On top of that, the evil current situation where we serve nature like a benefactor and act as slaves is truly a great disgrace in human history. You've spent some time in Tokyo too, haven't you? In places like the slums, due to overpopulation, malnutrition, and disease germs, what慘酷 (ch'amhok - cruel/miserable) lives people lead! In this way, nature abuses humanity with debilitating diseases in the cities, and mocks us by making us feeble-minded in the villages. However, through art and science, we have escaped from the demonic grasp of nature. Art has extended our lives to the gates of heaven, and science has saved us from disease. Isn't the recent new invention regarding rejuvenation and eternal youth by the Austrian scientist Dr. "Steinach" (스타이나하) truly worthy of admiration?
Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, do you love peace? Then we must concentrate all our efforts towards art even more. Art never harms us. Suppose you are crying now watching a tragedy. But there is absolutely no pain in those tears. Ah—Ch’an-ho my friend, art never harms us. Isn't it truly art, and nothing else, that always lets us live in fresh emotions? The day the whole world, all humanity, escapes nature and life and becomes artistic, only then can we live a completely peaceful, elegant, and wondrous life. Ah—Art! Art!! What in this world is more astonishing than art?
Ch’an-ho: You are an advocate of art for art's sake, I see... What you've said just now is truly the truth. But there's one more thing I want to bring up: language. Looking at the origin of language, which is not the child of thought but the mother of thought, isn't language a sign for the affairs of nature? If so, aren't the special affairs of nature<125> the symbol of all our spirit? Therefore, didn't nature invent human culture?
Pyŏng-sŏn: Hey, Ch’an-ho my friend, you must remember that when language returned to nature, we were savages. If you carefully examine the languages of primitive peoples, they are truly limitlessly bizarre. Language consisted merely of cries expressing emotion and irregular noises. Then, gradually moving away from nature, language came to possess lofty poetry and song, beyond merely sketching pictographs. Thus, the origin of language becoming the mother of thought and poetry lies not in its return to nature, but in its emergence from nature. Isn't this clearly seen by comparing regional dialects and urban speech? Contrary to your words, nature is the great enemy of culture. Ah—what does nature signify, anyway? I declare that nature is nothing more than an ignorant laborer.
Ch’an-ho: You're unusually excited. So—is nature a complete failure then?
Pyŏng-sŏn: Ch’an-ho my friend, let's stop talking about nature. Nature is truly inherently imperfect and nothing more than an unconscious, blind phenomenon. It is a feeble-minded entity that constantly flounders, contradicts itself, and plays jokes. It is a delinquent that always enjoys cruel games. Ch’an-ho my friend, for everything, we must turn towards art.
Ch’an-ho: You've told me many astonishing things tonight. Well then, shall we go out to the Tama River (多摩川) for an excursion and some quiet relaxation?
Pyŏng-sŏn: Haven't I been saying this whole time? Contact with nature makes the mind dull (酩酊), and one loses all the thoughts carefully considered... Ch’an-ho my friend, let's not do that. Let's go together to Miss Ch'un-hŭi's place and listen to music instead. She truly has a genius for music. The only person who can realize our dreams and show us the unknown world is Miss Ch'un-hŭi...
...(Written March 5th)... <126>

**Summary**

This dialogue features Pyŏng-sŏn passionately advocating for aestheticism, arguing that imagination, fantasy, and art are superior to reality, morality, and nature. He views fantasy not as falsehood but as the source of creativity and true existence, citing examples like Hamlet and religious belief. He dismisses reality as boring and deceptive, and nature as senile, cruel, monotonous, and an enemy to culture, claiming art actually shapes our perception of nature, not the other way around. He champions "art for art's sake," seeing it as the path to true elevation, purification, and happiness, even defending controversial ideas like praising evil or love suicide for their inherent passion and beauty. Ch'an-ho acts as a foil, representing conventional views, questioning Pyŏng-sŏn's paradoxes, defending reality and nature, but ultimately seems swayed by Pyŏng-sŏn's "magic."

From a historical perspective, this 1921 article reflects the influx of Western philosophical and literary ideas, particularly aestheticism and decadence (reminiscent of Wilde or Baudelaire), into colonial Korea. Pyŏng-sŏn's radical rejection of conventional morality, reality, and even nature, in favor of an idealized, self-created artistic realm, could be interpreted as an intellectual escape from the constraints and frustrations of colonial life. His emphasis on individual artistic creation and the "superman" echoes Nietzschean thought, also circulating at the time. For historians, the text offers insight into the intellectual ferment among young Korean writers grappling with modernity, Western influence, and the role of art in a society undergoing profound change and political subjugation. The extreme nature of Pyŏng-sŏn's views highlights the intensity of these debates.

未知의 世界
						林蘆月
第一部

讚皓. 여보게 무엇을 그리 생각하나 또 色魔한테 붓잡혓네 그려.
炳鮮. 아- 나는 大軆 누구를 사랑하는고? 사랑을 사랑함이 이러케도 熱烈할 수가 잇나. 모든 小說의 女主人公을 사랑하는가? 하-
讚皓. 그게 무슨 미친 말인가. 그리지 말고 어서 自白하게. 大軆 相對者는 누군가. 올치 자네집 主人딸일세 그려, 이름이 勝子던가?
炳鮮. 아니야 나는 至今까지 자네를 속이어왓네. 내가 OO子를 사랑한다는 말과 또는 내 母親이 生母가 아니라던가 또는 내가 方今 肺病에 걸리엇다던가 한말은 다-거짓말일세. 하나 나는 정말 어미업는 孤兒로서 또 不治의 病者로서 또는 누구의 戀人으로서 恒常 哀曲에 느끼는 愁人 가운대서 살아왓다네. 이러한 生活을 虛僞라할가, 아니어 내게 限하여는 참 生活일세. 나는 이가티 空想을 現實로 收集하며 현실을 공상으로 수집하네. 이것이 내 藝術이라네.
讚皓. 자네는 참말 虛僞家일세.
炳鮮. 자네는 空想을 虛僞로 아는가? 아니어 空想처럼 創造的이고 驚異的은 업다네. 우리의 熱情을 태우는 許多한 藝術的 作品이 其實은 空想의 産出이 아니고 무엇이며 近代生活을 極度로 懷疑와 悲哀에 들어가게 한 이는 『하무레트』란 架空的 人物이 아니고 무엇인가. 여보게 讚皓군, 이 渾沌한 人生을. 盲目的인 運命의 制限을 밧고 本質的으로 不完全한 우리의 行爲를 모든 存在 中에 가장 抽象的인 生에서 空想과 想像을 排斥하야 어떠케 자네는 一層 永續的이고 一層 驚異한 것을 발견코저 하는고...자네는 실상 空想生活을 虛僞로 아는가 아닐다. 失戀과 片戀이 實際의 사랑보다도 더-우리의 情熱을 高潮케 하는 것은 무슨 까닭인가. 여보게 空想은 藝術家에게 實在가 되네. 자네가 神을 생각하는 것은＜119＞ 한 空想일세. 그러기에 神은 참말 存在가 되엇네. 자네는 善과 眞을 생각하는가. 이것도 한 空想일세. 하나 善과 眞은 方今 자네의 行爲를 司配하지 안는가. 이와가티 一切의 存在는 空想的 心理的 存在가 아니고 무엇인가. 여보게 讚皓君, 聖者가 되고저하는가. 그리면 마음을 沈靜시킨 後 釋伽傳가튼 것을 耽讀하게, 그리면 자네는 釋伽와 가티 人生을 生老病死에서 救濟하기 爲하야 얼마나 慈悲한 눈물을 흘릴고. 자네는 또한 悲劇의 主人公이 되고저 하는가. 그러면 「엘텔의 悲哀」나 或은 「春香傳」을 읽게, 자네는 畢竟 深刻한 熱情의 全存在를 다-바칠터이지. 行爲는 實로 우리를 聖人이나 愛人이 되지못하게 하네마는 空想과 想像은 우리로 하여금 聖人이 되게하며 누구의 情人이 되게하네. 實際의 悲劇主人公은 아모것도 享樂치 못하지마는 悲劇 觀光者는 모든 것을 享樂할 수가 잇다. 이럼으로 우리의 德行은 全혀 空想 과 想像에 잇다하네. 우리는 空想과 想像 가운대서만 모든 것을 美化할 수가 잇서, 우리가 萬若 羅馬의 「네로」時代에 산다하면 얼마나 「네로」를 미워할고. 그러나 想像 가운대서 「네로」를 批評할 때에는 이러한 虐主라도 同情할 수가 잇다. 그는 火災의 詩를 짓기爲하야 『로마』全市에 불을 질러 노앗다. 이는 全혀 藝術的 衝動에서 나온 無邪氣한 遊戱라 할 수 잇네. 그러나 「네로」가 高臺우에서 『로마』市人의 울고 부르지즈는 소리며 華麗한 美術館과 公會堂이 다-타지며 熾烈한 불은 朱珊瑚가티 가지가지로 일어나 『로마』全市를 滅亡케 할 때에 「네로」는 반듯이 창황히 깨닷고 悔改하엿슬 것이다. 그리고 苦悶에 쌔운 뜨거운 눈물은 끈침업시 흘러 마츰내 「한우님」하고 自己의 罪를 뉘우첫슬 것일세. 이때 「네로」가 흘린 深刻한 눈물은 能히 「네로」의 罪를 깨끗하게 씨츤줄 밋네. 올아 耶蘇가 萬人의 罪를 容恕한 點은 여긔에 잇다. 즉 罪人의 苦悶이 그 罪보다도 더-큰 것을 想像함에 잇다하네. 여보게 讚皓君, 藝術家 全軆의 槩念은 全혀 空想과 想像에 잇서야만 되네. 아-空想은 俗人이 말하는 바와가티 그러케 無價値한 것이 아니야.
讚皓. 자네의 말은 全혀 似而非한 逆說일세.
炳鮮. 眞正한 逆說은 超人의 眞理라네.
讚皓. 그래 자네는 社會를 害毒하는 罪人을 辯護한단 말인가. 자네가티 病的 畸形兒가 우리 朝鮮에 두사람만 잇서도 亡하네 亡해...
炳鮮. 그러나 나는 病氣를 사랑해. 그는 우리의 想像力을＜120＞ 刺戟시키는 까닭이야. 그러나 나더러 病的이라고는 말게. 내가 病氣를 사랑함은 사랑햇지 決코 내가 病的은 아니야. 그리고 먼저 자네가 나더러 罪人을 辯護한다고 非難햇지마는... 世上사람들에게 惡人이라고 불리엇든 사람은 다- 自己한테 忠直을 다한 사람들이라네.
讚皓. 자네는 참말 眞理와 道德을 無視하는 異端者일세.
炳鮮. 人類社會에 進化란 法則이 잇는 以上에는 眞理 道德은 恒常 否定이 된다네. 우리가 萬若 眞理를 求하랴면 그는 藝術가운대서 求할 것이야. 藝術은 모든 存在 中에 가장 完全함으로 進化란 法則의 司配되지 안는다. 人類會社가 如何히 變化할지라도 「하무레트」의 懷疑的 思想은 永遠히 不變할지며 耶蘇가 再臨하야 地上樂園을 建設한다하여도 「소펭화-엘」이나 「떠쓰도에푸스키」의 悲觀的 思想은 그냥 不變할 것이다.
讚皓. 그러면 우리는 藝術을 爲하야 全存在를 바처야되겟나?

炳鮮. 讚皓君, 自然에 對한 말은 그만두세. 自然은 實로 本質的으로 不完全하고 無意識的 盲目的 現象에 不過하다. 그는 不絶히 狼狽하며 矛盾하며 諧謔을 弄하는 低能兒일다. 恒常 殘忍한 遊戱를 조하하는 不逞漢일다. 讚皓君, 우리는 모든 것을 爲하야 藝術로 向하여야만 되네.
讚皓. 자네는 오늘저녁 여려가지 驚嘆할말을 만히 해주엇네. 자-遠足兼 多摩川에 나아가 閒養이나 하세 그려.
炳鮮. 이때껏 말하지 아니하엿나? 自然에 接하면 頭腦가 酩酊해지며 모처럼 考察한 것도 다-일허버리기에... 讚皓君, 그러지말고 나하고 한께 春姬孃한테 가서 音樂이나 듯세. 그이는 참 音樂에 天才가 잇는걸. 우리들의 꿈을 實現하야 未知의 世界를 보여줄 사람은 오즉 春姬氏뿐이야...
