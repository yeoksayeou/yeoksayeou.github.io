The Proletariat's Cry
						Kim Sŏk-song
I am a Proletariat!
One who possesses nothing.

But I—
Gold, Land, Housing,
Status, Honor, Comfort,
Communism,
Socialism,
Democracy,
Ah! I do not want them!

Love, Family,
Society, Nation,
Anything of the Present,
Ah! I curse them!

And only
A rational life of the Future
Ah! I demand it!

And so, I—
Even if the women of the whole world
Were entrusted to my body,
Even if the wealth of the entire world
Were placed in my hands...
Ah! I would abandon it!

I am a Proletariat!
One who possesses nothing.

But I merely
The asset called "Human,"
The "Human" in its true meaning...
Demand! Cry out! <116>

And next,
The "Rights" of the "Human"
Let them be in my hands, so
I myself (as a human)
Can recognize and manage myself...

Ah! I see it!
Ah! I see it!
A flock of scarecrows.

Though they wear human forms,
Though they wear fine clothes,
Regardless of man or woman, old or young,
Though they possess sturdy bodies
Equipped with bone, flesh, and blood,
Though they possess imperfect intellect, emotion, and will,
They do not possess the true soul of a human.

Ah! I see it!
A flock of scarecrows.

They, let alone their daily actions,
Even lack awareness of "Life" and "Death,"
The greatest facts of living beings.
Just like machines,
They are a flock of scarecrows! <117>

The Groan of the Wounded
The Groan of the Wounded!
It is the eternal
Agony of Humankind!

Those wounded in the soul—
Even with the medical arts of the 20th century,
Even with the embrace of pure love,
Cannot possibly, possibly
Be healed!
No, Love—that very thing
Only makes their wounds fester more.

Ah! The Groan of the Wounded!
It is the eternal
Agony of Humankind! <117>

The Person Seen in a Dream
The person seen in a dream—
But as I
Open my eyes and move my legs
Walking into the City,

His head is the size of a fist,
His body is three times
The size of a person.
And he wears splendid
Silk clothes.

The person seen in a dream—
But as I
Open my eyes and move my legs
Walking into the Mountain Village,

His head is the size of a large gourd,
His body is horribly
Just gaunt bones.
And he wears tattered
Hemp clothes.

Ah! What a contrast!
Ah! What a hallucination! <118>

Oh! Coffin-Making Carpenter!
Oh! Coffin-making Carpenter!
Whose hair is graying,
Kind-hearted old Carpenter!
Into the coffin you make,
A person's corpse will go!
(You know this clearly)

Oh—Coffin-making Carpenter!
But you too
In the near future, certainly,
Will need such a coffin.
The one you now make with diligence,
And the coffin you will enter—
Whose hands, indeed, will it borrow!
-Excerpted from (Under the Great-Grandmother's Grave)- <118>

**Summary**

This collection of poems by Kim Sŏk-song expresses the despair and demands of the proletariat ("無産者"). The speaker rejects all forms of existing wealth, status, ideology (including Communism, Socialism, Democracy), and social structures like family and nation. Instead, the core demand is for recognition as a true "Human" with inherent "Rights" and a future "rational life." The poems critique contemporary society, portraying people as soulless "scarecrows" lacking true self-awareness, even regarding life and death. They touch upon the unhealable "wounds of the soul," the stark social contrasts between the city rich and the rural poor (seen as a "hallucination"), and finally, reflect on mortality through the image of a coffin maker who will eventually need a coffin himself. For historians, this work is significant as an early example of proletarian consciousness and social critique in colonial Korea. It reflects disillusionment not just with the colonial situation but with existing social systems and ideologies, expressing a deep existential angst and a yearning for fundamental human dignity and rights, possibly influenced by burgeoning socialist or humanist thought, yet explicitly rejecting specific political labels in favor of a focus on the 'human' itself. The stark imagery of inequality ("Person Seen in a Dream") offers a poignant glimpse into the social realities and perceptions of the time.
