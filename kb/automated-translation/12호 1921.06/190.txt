讀者交情欄 (Reader's Interchange Column)

To Mr. Sin Sŏkkap (辛錫鉀君) of Puan-ŭp (扶安邑)! Our editorial department has also thought for some time that it would be good to provide simple and clear explanations of the newly coined terms frequently used in magazines these days, but we have not yet started because we don't know how the general readership would perceive it. However, in the future, after specifically discussing this, we will either include it in the magazine each month or publish it separately as a single volume from Kaebyŏksa (開闢社).

To our dear readers (讀者諸位)! Many of you often think it would be good if our magazine had a local correspondence column and send us communications about it, for which this reporter is grateful. However, local correspondence cannot happen unless those of you residing in the localities record and send it in. Therefore, those of you who wish to know about local correspondence, please write down interesting news from your respective regions and send it to the Reader's Interchange Desk (讀者 交情係), and we will include it. We hope you will all make an effort.

讀者文壇 (Reader's Literary Forum)

Having Been Jilted
Pak Tonghun (朴東勳), Chinnamp'o (鎭南浦)

Ah, the lover I truly loved,
More beautiful than the rose boasting its grace in the morning mist,
More than the full moon rising over the Eastern Ridge,
More, more beautiful and lovely was my lover,
You were truly my lover.

With a smile on your red cheeks,
You gave me kisses hotter than fire,
Saying, "I live trusting you,"
"And you live trusting me." At that time,
There was no suffering in life.

Looking up at the bright moonlight,
Inhaling the beautiful fragrance of flowers,
Holding your soft hand tightly,
When praising the natural beauty of the Creator,
I never thought we would part forever, forever.

Ah, heartless and truly vulgar!
A firm love that even a fierce bomb could not break,
The lovely person (伊人) who swore by Heaven and Earth,
That she could change so much overnight,
Ah, I truly did not know.

Ah, what a pity, how vexing!
Your sacred and pure body and blood,
Your soft lips (朱脣), your sacred words,
From now on, they are forever sullied.
Ah, where can I find the sanctity of former days again?

Resentful, sorrowful, and vulgar.
The lovely love that was embraced in my arms,
The hot kisses given to me,
My beautiful ideal lover <101>
A hateful enemy has snatched away.

Ah, woman who abandoned me and turned away,
With whom shall I cherish the plum blossoms after the snow?
With whom shall I inhale the chrysanthemum fragrance before the frost?
Knowing your heart today, I see
The love of former days was a love of the lips.

You who snatched away my love,
You must be happy with that love.
You female Satan, slave to bestial desires (獸慾),
Indulge your bestial desires to your heart's content.
Ah, you two, both of you are heartless.

As a Struggling Student to My Brother
Sang Yŏngman (尙永萬), Talsŏng (達城)

Ah, Brother! Have you been well? I have become a struggling student in a foreign land where the mountains and waters are unfamiliar. On the night of the 14th day of the 3rd lunar month, when the clear wind blows and a hundred flowers are in full bloom, as the bright moon shines on the study window, I lean on my desk, gaze at the moonlight, ask generally after your well-being, and write to report my situation. Brother, since leaving my hometown that day and time, coming here, and enrolling in school, I am studying well and am in good health. Are you also well? Ah, truly, Brother, as you know, what is this thing called money? Truly, one can say it is both affectionate and heartless.
Those who are affluent use it like water, but I suffer hardships because of this money and supplement my school expenses by delivering newspapers. Brother, my situation is lonely and pitiful. I go to school at 8 in the morning, finish classes at 1 o'clock, come out with newspapers tucked under my arm, and distribute them house to house until 12 o'clock at night. After distributing until 12, when I am hungry, my legs hurt, and fatigue is severe, I see youths around 20 years old, wandering dissolutely in the bustling streets, frequenting taverns and brothels (酒肆靑樓), spending money like water (用錢如水), enjoying the spring breeze and autumn moon, staggering around. Seeing this, I feel how much discontent there is in this society. Seeing this and that, after distributing newspapers to every house, I return to my lodging, eat two or three spoonfuls of cold dinner, and when I dream a dream of rest in the remaining warmth of the ondol floor (알에목), I take the flickering lamp as my companion, diligently review my daily lessons, often wake up two or three times, look at the moon alone, and sing songs freely.
Oh, our compatriots!
Three thousand leagues of embroidered rivers and mountains (三千里 錦繡江山),
Products are abundant,
And the scenery is also fine.
In such a world,
If people are born,
Should we not also, like this,
Strive diligently (勉勉兢兢),
In today's 20th century,
Become promising youths (有爲靑年)?
Some people have much fortune,
In the warm part of the ondol floor,
Taste delicious food,
Dream under warm quilts (煖衾).
Some people suffer much hardship,
Where mountains and waters are unfamiliar,
Making dark nights their days,
Braving snowstorms,
But our mindful compatriots,
Do not idle in taverns and brothels,
With utmost sincerity and effort (盡心竭力), all together,
If we drive away OO,
To the Three Thousand Leagues of Embroidered Rivers and Mountains,
Fortune and blessings (福祿) will come.
Ah, Brother, singing such songs, I strike the desk, sigh once, weep once, then scold myself, thinking that if we too advance forward, comfort will not be far away. I pick up the book I had put aside again and gaze at it absently. Who could possibly know my heart is rotting inside? That bright moon of the 14th day of the 3rd month seems to know and yet not know my private feelings. Along with that bright moonlight, I confess my frustrations to you, Brother.

A Poet's Weeping
Kim Yonggap (金瑢甲), Ch'ŏngam (晴庵)

Poet, weep!
Poet, weep!
His pale face
Held up blankly,
Not for beauty and love <102>
But drawn to the ideal,
When he gently closes his sightless eyes!
The sigh he lets out,
Do you know? Do you not know?
The weeping sorrow of the heart rains down!

Poet, weep!
Poet, weep!
When the spring breeze is warm
And descends upon the fields!
Wandering along the distant horizon...
When the autumn moon is clear and bright (皎潔) in the vast sky!
Singing of the desolate rivers and mountains...

Poet, weep!
Poet, weep!
The great principle of the universe! The mystery of creation!
The life of great nature?
With beauty and love...
It embraces all things (森羅萬像)!
But there is no one to understand!
There is no one to listen!!
This world that twists wishes...
Who is sad and grieving?
Ah—Poet,
Weep, O Poet.

Poet, weep!
Poet, weep!
This world adorned with formality and masks!
This world drowsing in empty shells and vain desires!
People, sing, dance, drink wine.
Embrace beauties and roll around!
Weep, weep, that—Poet,
Weep silently,
Shedding heart-wrenching tears of blood
From the mind's eye...

For the Late Mr. Kim (故金先生)
Songhŏn Saeng (松軒生), Ŭiju (義州)

Alas! A person's life is but a fleeting dream in a single night.
Here yesterday, gone today; joy yesterday, sorrow today—this is the constant principle of Heaven's Way (天道). Furthermore, within each part, there is a principle of its being so, operating within its appropriate scope, principle, and duration, alternating accordingly. If one stands within this appropriate measure and corresponds to it, what regret would there be, what resentment, what lament, what worry? However, contrary to this, there are reverse principles where long becomes short, wide becomes narrow, high becomes low, etc., sometimes causing resentment and lament, sometimes causing worry and concern.
For example, if a lifespan is 100 years, its length might sometimes be limited to 50 years, or limited to 30 years. If one contracts an incurable, natural illness (順病) and meets their end, even one who hasn't reached half the age of rarity [Confucius said 70 was rare, so perhaps meaning <35] could be said to have reached their lifespan. However, if one entrusts their natural life to the manipulation of others (人의 手弄) and meets their end, then even someone over the age of rarity [>70] has not reached their lifespan but met a reverse principle. In this situation, who would not resent and lament, who would not feel pity (惻隱)? Which person possessing spiritual awareness (靈覺) and eyes and ears, encountering this, would not feel resentment and worry, would not feel pity, would not feel bitterness (辛酸)?
Furthermore, there is a way of rewarding and punishing based on judging the good and evil of actions. If one incurs harm as punishment for a crime that cannot appropriately be avoided, then they are guilty, and death may be deemed fitting. But if harm is inflicted as punishment upon an innocent person (曖昧者) who should rather receive reward and does not deserve punishment, then concerning this, there will be none who do not feel pity, none who do not lament and worry. Alas, what a pity! Oh, our Sŏnsaeng (先生)! Was the harm you suffered meant as reward or as punishment for a crime? If it was punishment for a crime, did fulfilling the duties of a human being born among humans become a crime? Did serving the obligations of a religious believer become a crime? Did fulfilling the duties of a member of society become a crime? Is the present age an era contrary to the past, where reward is treated as punishment? If <103> reward is repaid as punishment, perhaps it could be acceptable, but if it is said that there was a crime and the crime was punished, I personally think this is truly a misunderstanding. Oh, how painful! Our Sŏnsaeng, who parted from us forever under ambiguous circumstances (曖昧永訣). Sŏnsaeng, you were less than half the age of rarity [i.e., young], and moreover, you left not even a single drop of descendant blood (遺血). How could this not be a cause for painful lament, how could it not be a cause for sorrowful regret?
Sŏnsaeng was from Taesan-dong, Kosŏng-myŏn, Ŭiju-kun, P'yŏngan Pukto (平安北道義州郡古城面臺山洞). His nature was upright and strong (剛直), his spirit noble and pure (高潔), his heart resolute, firm, and solid (果敢堅固). In dealing with people, his face was like the spring light; in quiet contemplation, his demeanor was like a rock; in serving people, he was like heaven. His aspirations were a great model (大模範) for the youth world; his firmness was something heroic men (雄士) could admire; his honesty was such that the old man of Lu [Confucius] (魯翁) could commend. In the neighborhood, he took harmony as his principle, punishing evil and promoting good. When sitting with family members, he always spoke of public matters (與公), advising the wealthy not to become ruffians (不汗黨) or misers (守錢奴), and encouraging the poor to practice thrift and saving (勤儉貯蓄). He admonished the delinquent to turn towards good, helped the unemployed find work, and always spoke of striving for education when children grew up, making these public concerns the substance of his conversations. He was particularly fervent and diligent in youth education. Because he disregarded household affairs, his elderly parents, nearing the age of rarity, could not avoid carrying loads on their backs and barely managed to eke out a living (糊口). Nevertheless, Sŏnsaeng, disregarding this, sometimes solicited aid from others to educate poor students. He always lamented that his own eyesight was imperfect for handling public (與公) affairs satisfactorily, and regretted that people were not interested in public matters, lamenting to himself, "When will they know the public good? When will they fulfill their duties?" In recent years, for the sake of his elderly parents, he temporarily engaged in family business, so he could not personally dedicate himself to public society or education. However, he sometimes agreed, sometimes contributed funds, and often provided support internally. When the Ŭiju Youth Club (義州靑年俱樂部) existed, he contributed 10 won, more precious than 100 or 1,000 won from others. He also voluntarily contributed 10 won to the Ŭiju Ch'ŏndogyo Youth Association (義州天道敎靑年會). Until the time of his death, he strived for public matters. Unfortunately, around 8 PM on the 2nd of this month, he met an unexpected misfortune (不意의 變) and finally offered his life to the manipulation of others (人의 手弄). Oh, how painful! Sŏnsaeng, who parted from us forever under ambiguous circumstances. If this incident was not the doing of wicked villains (惡漢黨), then he seemed like a public figure working for the public good (與公). Why then, for an unimaginable, trivial suspicion (不可擬의 毫末嫌), was such an undoable deed (不可爲의 事業) committed against someone so capable in public endeavors? Whichever way one considers it, there is truly no one who does not painfully lament Sŏnsaeng's ambiguous final parting. Thus, with a few words, I inform our brethren on the peninsula of Sŏnsaeng's ambiguous circumstances.

Appended Song (附歌)
How cruel, our teacher (스승), what more can be said?
How regrettable, our teacher, that is all the regret there is.
What use is sighing? It is absurd, the teacher's incident (事變).
What use is blaming? This matter deviates from reason (理致).
How heartless, this resentment! After the teacher departed,
Unrelated compulsory measures (强制手段), who will carry on the teacher's work (師業)?
Forever forbidden to rise in the world (出世不許), his precious body like gold and jade (金玉),
A strange commotion arose. Through inhumane methods (仁치못한 그手段),
Boundlessly ambiguous matters, his life offered unnaturally (順치안케獻命),
I declare and clarify! The teacher's situation (身勢) is lamentable (可歎하다).

A Blind Person's Farewell Letter (失明者의 遺翰)
Yŏsa Cho Yunsuk (曹允淑)

Now, gathering my spirit, weary even from weeping (慟哭), I send this final letter to you, who always understood me well throughout my life (at least, I believe so). However, I am not particularly seeking your pity (憐情). It is certainly not a hateful prank intending to cast a demonic cloud of melancholy (悲鬱) upon your peaceful and happy heart. Know that this is a stream of hot, fresh blood gushing forth uncontrollably from my young heart, pierced by the sharp point of a spear of misfortune too sudden and too great. What unpleasant sights, or what sublime, or pitiful, or sorrowful feelings you might sense from this short letter (短翰) is truly beyond my imagination.
After completely losing my sight, for about 4 weeks, caressing the skeletal dust (骸粉) of all my shattered, brilliant hopes, <104> embracing my crumpled eyes—they must truly be unsightly—that have forever departed from vision, weeping and sighing, can you possibly imagine my profound sorrow (悲哀) during those four weeks of curse that dragged its immense cruelty and threatened me?
Thinking back, it was around the end of the month before last. Two days before you went up to Seoul (上京), late at night, under a rather clear moon, though one side was slightly chipped, passing through the quietly sleeping streets, strolling along the new road lush with roadside trees (路樹) on both sides near the gate, the moonlight filtering through the leaves and branches, and the moonlight reflected on the thin waves of the S stream (S川), splintering into hundreds, thousands of pieces... each forming sparkling curves like embers (甲火), appearing and disappearing with agility, and after disappearing, appearing again, matching the lovely, thin melody of the waves, ceaselessly twinkling—do you remember seeing this? I went out there yesterday evening—led by D—searching for this past beauty. Ah! But instead of seeing the moonlight and the sparkle of the wave scales (波鱗) that gave us joy (喜悅), only a dark, vast iron curtain (鐵布帳) blocked my eyes.
"Weep! Die! Right, die! That is the only remaining hand of salvation (救濟)." There, I shed two or three drops of silent tears (暗淚) and turned back.
Two or three days after parting with you, I began to feel my eyes itching. At first, I just thought it was bloodshot due simply to fatigue. (I had often suffered eye problems before due to excessive use of my sight.) After two or three days passed, the pain gradually worsened, and on the fifth day, realizing it wasn't simple bloodshot eyes, I saw a doctor. But it was already too late. Medication, poultices (濕布), and the doctor's sincere care (看護) were ineffective. Around the seventh day, people coming and going looked like actors' movements seen through two or three layers of gauze (寒冷紗). By the tenth day (第10日詐 - likely 第10日째, 'on the 10th day'), no matter how I rubbed my eyes, I couldn't see anything at all. Ah! I will not speak at length here of how sorrowful I was and how much I wept at this time.
However, C-kun, even if weeping is an impulsive expression of irrational emotion, it did not come out when I clearly realized my decided fate.
Even in this state, too dreadful even to speak of, time flowed without delay (遲滯). Ten days, fifteen days passed, and even in my mind, which had been so shattered and scattered (裂滅亂散), a kind of resignation (斷念) began to form, and composure (沈着) also started to arise slightly. Sometimes, I could even shift to the position of a third party and objectively view the situation I faced. (Of course, you probably know that I have undergone some training in the habit of becoming calm about anything and calmly applying scientific observation even to matters concerning myself.) But at every such moment, some large, dark disappointment covers my entire thoughts. It is because the misfortune that befell me is undeniable and too great. Also, occasionally, I tried to build a world of beautiful illusions (夢幻) as much as possible, even if only through concepts, to gladden my feelings. Then, always, the flames of hot sorrow, sulfurous in color (硫黃焰色), seemingly poisonous just to look at, burn it away without a trace.
When sunlight hits my eyes, the entire view before me appears blueish (파-ㄹ하게). Staring at it for a while, countless spherical objects of dark red color (暗赤色) sparkle and fly around. Is this the dancing sleeve of a celestial maiden (天女) comforting the blind? Or is it the eyeballs of wicked demons (妖鬼) mocking and rejoicing at my downfall?
Sometimes, D comes and asks, "Why are you sitting out there!" I quickly turn my face towards the direction of the voice. But of course, I cannot see D's face. Tears of heartbreak (傷心) flow again from my two eyes.
Ah—there must be countless changing phenomena (事物의 變狀) in the world. There will be stars twinkling in the blue sky (蒼天), and waves will dance in the sea. Crimson maples (丹楓) will fall, and snow will come. On T mountain (T山), there will be groups of hunters (獵夫) releasing hawks towards fleeing pheasants, and on the frozen S stream (S川上), groups of children, punching holes with picks (괭이), pulling out small fish (小魚), and jumping around gleefully (嬉嬉) will also appear. But the time for me to perceive and enjoy (享樂) such phenomena has passed forever. All that remains before me is just one path: death (死)—there is no pleasure or beauty there, but there will not be the bitterness of anguish (悲痛) like here. Why, before taking that step, do I fill my desire to express my sorrowful feelings by sending this short writing to you, the most and only trusted person in my life, and at the same time, substitute it for a final farewell greeting (離別의 人事)?
The difficulty of writing this letter by guesswork, unknown to anyone, was not small. I apologize that the messiness of the characters (文字) will probably trouble you greatly.
Reporter's Note (記者 曰): This is an excellent piece (佳作). The disappointment and emotion of the blind person flow with a feeling that seems to pierce the young heart. <105>

**Summary**

This collection from the June 1921 issue of *Kaebyŏk* features editorial responses and reader contributions under the "Reader's Interchange Column" and "Reader's Literary Forum." The content spans personal expressions of lost love, the hardships of a struggling student balancing studies with newspaper delivery, a poet's lament on the state of the world, a eulogy for a respected community figure whose death is shrouded in suspicion, and a poignant farewell letter from someone who has lost their sight. The pieces collectively paint a picture of individual suffering—romantic, economic, existential, and physical—alongside social commentary on inequality and calls for collective effort, set against the backdrop of early colonial Korea. Historians might find the student's letter particularly interesting for its depiction of social stratification, the hardships faced by aspiring youth, and the veiled patriotic sentiment ("drive away OO"). The eulogy for Mr. Kim offers a glimpse into local community leadership, social activism (related to Ch'ŏndogyo), and the potential dangers faced by such individuals, hinting at suppression or violence beyond ordinary crime. The final letter poignantly captures the devastating personal impact of disability.

先生은 平安北道義州郡古城面臺山洞人이니 其性이 剛直하고 그 氣宇가 高潔하며 其心이 果敢堅固하야 待人接物에 其顔이 春光이오 閑居黙思에 其儀 岩石이며 事人에 如天이니 그 有志함은 靑年界의 大模範이요 그 强硬함은 雄士의 欽慕할 바요 그 正直함은 魯翁이 可謝할 바이리라. 隣里에서는 和睦을 主張삼아 惡은 懲하고 善을 揚하며 家人으로 同坐하면 恆常與公을 言하되 有財者에게는 不汗黨되지말며 守錢奴되지 말기를 勸하고 無財者에게는 勤儉貯蓄을 勸하며 不良者는 戒하야 善에 就케하며 失業者는 勸하야 業에 就토록 周旋하며 子孫이 生長하면 敎育에 努力하기를 常言하야 此等與公을 爲함으로 談話의 資料를 삼으며 靑年敎育에는 자못 熱誠努力하며 家事를 不顧하는 故로 稀年老父母는 負戴를 未免하고 겨우 糊口의 策을 圖하얏스나 先生은 此를 不拘하고 貧困한 學生에게는 人이 救助를 乞하야 敎育시킨 事도 有하며 與公 事業幹務함에 遺憾되는바 自己 眼目이 不完함을 恒常 恨歎하얏스며 世人이 與公에 有意치 아니함을 遺憾으로 思하야 何時에야 與公을 與知할가. 義務를 自盡할가 自嘆不己하다가 近年에는 老父母를 爲하야 臨時 家業에 從事함으로 與公 社會에나 敎育에 親히 獻身치는 못하얏스나 或 贊同도 하며 或 投財寄附도 하며 或 內容的으로 援助한 事도 多有하니 義州靑年俱樂部時에 人의 100圓이나 1,000圓보다 貴한 10圓金을 寄附한 事 有하며 義州天道敎靑年會에도 10圓金을 自願 寄附한 事-有하얏스며 臨終時까지라도 與公에 努力하시더니 不幸히 本月2日 午後8時頃에 不意의 變을 當하야 終也에 人의 手弄에 獻命하시니 嗚呼痛哉라 曖昧永訣한 先生이여. 今般 事變이 惡漢黨의 所爲가 아니어던 與公을 爲하는 公人갓거늘 어찌 與公事業에 有力하신 이를 不可擬의 毫末嫌으로 不可爲의 事業를 行하얏는가. 何方面으로 料測하던지 先生의 曖昧永訣함은 實로 痛歎치 아니할 이 無키로 半島同兄에게 一筆半語로써 先生의 曖昧함을 告하노이다.

失明者의 遺翰
여사 曹允淑

나는 이제, 慟哭에도 疲勞한 精神을 가다듬어, 平生 나를 잘 理解하여주던 君에게 (적어도 나는 그러케 밋노라.)最後의 書簡을 부치노라. 그러나, 나는 구타여 君의 憐情을 求하는 것은 아니다. 君의 和平하고 幸福스러운 마음에 悲鬱의 魔雲을 띠워보자는 可憎한 작난은, 勿論 아니다. 넘우도 不意요 큰 不幸의 날카로운 槍 끄테, 찔린 나의 어린 心臟에 엇찌할 수 업서, 느끼어 내뿜는 뜨거운 鮮血의 一條가 이것줄 알라. 이 短翰에서 어떠한 보기실흔 或은 어떠한 崇高한 또는 憐憫의 或은 셜음의 느낌을 感할는지는 實로 나의 想像外이다.
視力을 完全히 일흔 後엔 約 4週間 微塵에 부서진 모든 燦然하던 希望들의 骸粉을 어루만지며＜104＞ 길이 視覺의 떠난 나의 우그러진-그것은 참말로 보기실흘 것이다.-兩眼을 붓안고 울며 嘆息함으로채인 絶大의 殘酷力을 이끌고 나게 脅迫한 呪咀의 4週間에 深刻한 나의 悲哀를 君은 能히 생각할 수 잇는가?
「생각하면 前前달 그믐께이다. 君이 上京하기 2日前 밤 늣게 오른 한편이 좀 이즈러젓스나 그러나 꽤 맑은 달알에 고요히 잠든 거리를 지나 門近處 兩側에 路樹의 茂盛한 新도로를 逍遙하며 葉枝사이로 새어오는 달빗과 S川 가늘은 물결에 비추인 달빗이 100갈래 200갈래로...저저서 各各 부숫불(甲火)가튼 曲線과 敏滑이 낫다 사라지며 사라진 後에는 또 생기어 사랑스럽고 가늘은 曲調의 물결소리에 調于마처 반작임을 쉬지 안는 것을 본 일을 君은 記憶하는가? 나는 이 지나간 아름다움을 차저 어제 저녁에 그곳에를 나갓섯다.-D에게 이끌리어-아아! 그러나 우리에게 喜悅을 주던 달빗과 波鱗의 빗이 보일 代身에 캄캄하고 그나마한 鐵布帳이 내 눈압에 가로막헛슬뿐이다.
「울어라! 죽어라! 올치 죽어라. 다만 하나 남은 救濟의 손이다.」 나는 거긔 두서너방울의 暗淚를 떨어털이고 도라끗다.
나는 君과 作別한 2, 3日 後에 어쨰 눈이 가려움을 感하기 始作하엿섯다. 처음에는 單히 疲勞로 爲하여 充血한 것이겟지 하고만 생각하엿다.(나는 그前에 視力의 過度의 使用으로 因하여 자조 눈을 알엇다.) 2, 3日을 經過하여 次次 압흠이 甚하여저 第5日에는 單한 充血 아닌 줄을 알고 醫師에게 보엿다. 그러나 때는 벌서 느젓섯다. 施藥과 濕布와 醫師의 誠篤한 看護도 效力업시 第7日쯤하야는 사람들의 來往하는 것이 마치 2, 3重의 寒冷紗를 隔하여 俳優의 動作을 보는 것 갓게 되더니 第10日詐에는 아모리 눈을 부비나 아모 것도 볼수업시 되엇다. 아! 나는 여긔 이때에 내가 얼마나 설어하며 울엇슴을 길게 말하려하지 안는다.
그러나 C君 울음이 아모리 分別업는 感情의 衝動이라 할지라도 決定된 運命을 確實히 自覺한 때에는 나오지 안핫다.
이 말하기조차 慘然한 狀態에도 時間 遲滯업시 흘러갓다. 10日, 15日이 지나가며 그리 裂滅亂散하엿든 마음에도 一種의 斷念이 생기며 沈着이 또한 적이 나기 始作하얏다. 어떤 때에는 가만이 第三者의 位置에 옮아 나의 當한 경우를 客觀하여도 볼수잇게까지 되엇섯다.(물론 내가 무슨 일에던지 冷靜히 되여 自己가 當한 일에라도 가만히 科學的 觀察을 加하는 性格의 訓鍊을 多少間 지나온 것을 君은 알 것이다.) 그러나 그 瞬間마다 어떤 크고 시컴한 失望이 내 생각의 全體를 덥는다. 그는 틀림업고 넘우큰 내계 當한 不幸인 까닭이다. 또 잇다금 될 수 잇는대로 아름다운 夢幻의 世界를 지이 觀念으로라도 내 感情을 깃브게 하려하엿다. 그리면 언제던지 보기만하여도 毒이 잇슬듯한 硫黃焰色의 뜨거운 슯흠의 불길이 자최도업시 그를 태워놋는다.
눈에 해빗이 비추이면 眼前一面이 파-ㄹ하게 보인다. 한참, 그곳을 처다보노라면 暗赤色의 球形의 무손 物件들이 數업시 반작이며 날아단인다. 이것이 失明者를 慰勞하는 天女의 춤소매일가? 그를 비웃고 그가 디된 것을 깃버하는 몹슬 妖鬼의 눈瞳子일가?
이릴적에 或 D가 와서 「웨, 거기 나와 안젓나!」하면 나는 얼굴을 急히 돌려 말 들리는 便으로 向한다. 그러나 勿論 D의 얼굴은 보이지 안는다. 나의 두눈에서는 傷心의 눈물이 또 흐른다.
아아-世上에는 수업는 事物의 變狀이 잇슬 것이다. 蒼天에 반작어리는 별이 잇슬 것이고 바다에는 물결이 뛰놀 것이다. 丹楓이 지고 눈이 오겟지. T山에는 나라가는 꿩을 向하여 매를 노흐려하는 獵夫의 幾團이 잇슬것이며 얼어부튼 S川上에서 괭이로 구넝을 뚤코 小魚를 견저내며 嬉嬉히 날뛰는 아이들의 물이도 생길 것이다. 그러나 내가 이런 現象을 認識하며 享樂할 때는 永遠히 지나갓다. 나는 내 앞헤 남은 것은 다만 한줄기의 길인 死-거긔는 快樂도 美도 업지만은 여긔와 가튼 悲痛의 쓰라림은 업슬 것이다-웨 거름을 옴기기前에 生前에 가장이요 唯一의 信賴를 무던 君에게 이 짤은 글을 부팀으로 나의 섧은 感情의 表現慾을 채우며 아울너 마즈막 離別의 人事에 代하노라.
내가 아모도 모르게 손짐작으로 이 편지를 쓴 困難은 적지 안핫다. 文字의 어지러움 아마 君을 勞케함이 만흘 것을 謝하노라.
