Creeds, Assertions, and Rejections of Various Notable Figures

Let's call it good. Let's even call it evil. Regardless, the atmosphere of this society is formed according to the thoughts of those with knowledge and real power. If this statement is true, then it is by no means a useless endeavor for us today to inquire into the thoughts of the various notable figures residing in the class of the knowledgeable and powerful. Feeling this keenly, our company posed three questions:
1. What is your lifelong creed?
2. What do you wish to assert above all?
3. What do you wish to reject above all?
We troubled many notable figures for their answers, the general scope of which is shown below. Although it may be impolite to say, by doing this, our company hopes that (1) the words and actions of those who responded gain further weight, and (2) the words and actions of our general brethren gain new significance, so that the society we inhabit becomes a more sincere society, and all the work we do takes on a more poignant flavor and possesses greater certainty. If so, our wish is fulfilled.

Reporter's Note: The initial thought was to survey notable figures from all fields in the capital and the provinces to present their Creeds, Assertions, and Rejections. However, contributions from the provinces mostly failed to arrive by the manuscript deadline, and even among those in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), nearly half evaded responding day after day, resulting in failure to achieve the expected results, which is regrettable.<100>

**Summary**

This 1921 article from the magazine *Kaebyŏk* presents the results of a survey asking prominent Korean figures about their personal creeds, core assertions, and main rejections. The respondents span various fields including education (principals, professors), journalism (editors, reporters), religion (Christian, Buddhist, Ch'ŏndogyo leaders), social organizations (youth groups, YMCA), and business/administration. Their answers cover a wide range of philosophical, ethical, social, and nationalistic concerns, frequently emphasizing sincerity, justice, education, national unity, self-reliance, equality, and the rejection of falsehood, class divisions, injustice, and outdated customs.

From a historical perspective, the article provides a valuable snapshot of the expressed ideals and concerns of the Korean elite during a critical period of Japanese colonial rule. The emphasis on education, national character (民族性), unity (團結), and self-strengthening (實力) reflects common themes in nationalist discourse of the time. The diversity of opinions, ranging from religious faith to secular humanism and even anarchist-leaning individualism (Hwang Sŏk-u), is also noteworthy, indicating a dynamic intellectual landscape despite colonial constraints. Historians might find the specific individuals included, their affiliations, and the nuances of their brief statements useful for understanding the intellectual currents and social networks of early 1920s Korea.

諸名士의 信條와 主張과 排斥
善이라 하자. 비록 惡이라 하자. 如何間 이 社會의 空氣는 有知識, 有實權者의 心思 如何에 依하야 써지어지는 것이다. 이 말이 참이라면 오늘날에 잇서 우리가 그 有知識, 有實權의 階級에 居在한 諸名士의 心思 如何를 叩見함과 如함은 決코 無益한 일이 아니다. 本社는 이에 늑김이 切하야
1. 平生의 信條 如何
2. 어대까지 主張하고 십흔 것
3. 어대까지 排斥하고 십흔 것
은 무엇이냐? 하는 세 가지 물음을 發하야 幾多名士의 對答을 煩한 바 그 一般을 左에 보이노니 비록 缺禮의 말이나 本社가 이것을 함으로 因하야 (1) 對答한 그네의 所云爲에 一層 氏가 들며 (2) 一般 兄弟의 所云爲에 다시 새로운 意義가부터 우리들의 깃들이는 社會가 더 좀 純摯한 社會가 되며 우리의 하는 모든 일이 더 좀 辛酸한 맛이 잇스며 더 좀 確實性이 잇게 되엇스면 우리의 所望은 達이라.
