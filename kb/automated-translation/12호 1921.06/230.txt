破睡漫草
						梁白華(拔) (Yang Paekhwa (Pal))

I. 金雲翹傳 (Jin Yunqiao Zhuan / The Tale of Kieu)

Washing the Yanzi Pavilion in wind and rain, there is no way to mourn the old tale of Guan Panpan (關盻盻); locking up the Pear Blossom Courtyard in spring grass, where today can one find the visage of Li Shishi (李師師)? Nevertheless, at the Qinhuai (秦淮) ferry crossing, new willows are like smoke, a soft wind blows through the lattice blinds (丁字簾), a lovely throat trills like jade, and the bright moon shines on the swastika-patterned railing (亞字欄) as dancing shadows sway. Truly, it is a land of splendor. "In Jiangnan (江南) flowers bloom, the water flows leisurely; people arriving at Qinhuai (秦淮) all forget their sorrows. Regardless of the wind and smoke of homes thousands of miles away, at the fifth watch, singing throats warble in embraces." (From *Taohua Shan Chuanqi* / *The Peach Blossom Fan*). Alas! Is the land of Qinhuai truly a land without sorrow (莫愁之鄕)? However, what can be done about it ultimately becoming a gathering place for all the faithless beauties under heaven? Do not judge harshly. The creatures stirring within the Spring-Moving Railing (移春檻) were not originally born as demons, nor is it the original intention of those who sell their looks (色を鬻하고) and trade in charm (媚を賣하는 者) to do so. Fate and circumstance force them to throw themselves into the sea of song and music. I reject those who would make this land without sorrow an impure realm (不淨濊土). Yet, observing the circumstances of those who sell their charms, I cannot help but shed a tear.

The work *Jin Yunqiao Zhuan* (金雲翹傳) was written by Qingxin Cairen (靑心才人). Among Chinese novels, it cannot exactly be called a masterpiece (傑作). However, its depiction of a pure and innocent young girl who, dragged down by her surrounding circumstances, finally falls into the world of pleasure quarters (烟花風月地), experiences widespread hardship (苦楚), and endures various difficulties (艱難), is not without things worthy of appreciation (可觀). Only, the ending, which patches up the flawed heaven of sentiment to strike out a grand finale (大團圓) as a happy event, is a common device (常套 手段) in such novels. For readers whose eyes have perused modern novels and whose hearts cannot be comforted unless by something tragic and profound, there will likely be great dissatisfaction. No, creating an ending that should not have been is something the fraternity of good writing cannot deeply sympathize with, but when it comes to the skill in describing the twists and turns of her experiences and the heartlessness of worldly obligations, bringing her step by step closer to utter ruin, there are indeed aspects worthy of appreciation. In China, where the elegance (綺麗) of prose is <127> esteemed and the structure of the plot (脚色) is disregarded, there is something rather outstanding about it. Compared to the complexity (錯雜함) of *Honglou Meng* (紅樓夢), or the descent into lewdness (淫猥) of *Jin Ping Mei* (金甁梅), I would rather choose the brevity and consistent thread (脉脉一貫한 것) of the *Jin Yunqiao Zhuan* style, and recommend it to scholars who wish to appreciate (玩賞코저) Chinese novels beyond *Shuihu Zhuan* (水滸傳) and *Xiyou Ji* (西遊記). Works like *Pingshan Lengyan* (平山冷燕), merely filled with poetry, and *Yujiao Li* (玉嬌梨), which is flat and commonplace, are originally not worth considering. What then of *Wuse Shi* (五色石) or *Jingu Qiguan* (今古奇觀)? To call *Zashi Mixin* (雜事秘辛) mysteriously beautiful (幽艶하다) and *Feiyan Waizhuan* (飛燕外傳) marvelous (奇絶하다) – I cannot help but lament this for Chinese literature.

The character '情' (chŏng, sentiment/love) is the warp (大經) of this book, and the character '苦' (ko, suffering) is its weft (大緯). However, sentiment inevitably arises depending on circumstance (境), and suffering inevitably arises depending on encounters (遇) – this is stated in a commentary signed 'Jin Shengtan' (金聖歎) at the beginning (冒頭) of the first chapter.

Bringing forth one Liu Danxian (劉淡仙) to serve as an introduction, and revealing (托出) Cuiqiao's lifelong circumstances of sentiment and suffering from amidst vague forms and faint shadows, is the plot structure (脚色) of this first chapter. Observe how it is told.

Wang Cuiqiao (王翠翹) is a beauty (佳麗). She is skilled with the Huqin (胡琴, a Chinese fiddle) and adept at poetry and prose; she is the protagonist of *Jin Yunqiao Zhuan*. One day during the Qingming festival, she takes her younger brother and sister for a spring outing (踏靑). As they walk on and on, they see a solitary grave mound. The sister does not know whose it is. The brother informs them it is the grave of the famous courtesan Liu Danxian (劉淡仙), saying, "In life, she was the wife of ten thousand men; in death, she is a ghost without a husband." Cuiqiao's deep sigh is indeed no accident. Thereupon, she inscribes a poem, saying:
"Where are beauty and fragrance now? / Paying respects, my heart aches. / The cold moon chills the mandarin duck quilt, / Hidden dust seals the mirror stand. / Though jade is buried in yellow earth, / Her name is not yet buried by white clouds. / There is still wine like the River Min, / But no one to offer a cup."
Cuiqiao! Standing still for a long while, unable to leave, suddenly a strange gust of wind blows, whirlingly scattering around Cuiqiao's body. This indicates that the connection (因緣) between Danxian and Cuiqiao is not shallow, allowing the reader to know the origin of the karmic debt (孽債). The conception is similar to the 108 demon stars escaping from the Demon-Quelling Hall (伏魔殿). Inserting the principle of karma (因果主義) as the warp is not uncommon in Chinese novels; even works like *Yinshu Juehou Chan* (淫書覺後禪) have emulated this. As for Eastern novel writers, they rigidly adhere (株守하야) to this principle, setting a useless precedent in Korean literature, the beginning of which has long been transmitted to our literary circles. However, the principle of karma is excellent for so-called didactic novels (勸懲的 小說); it can make women and unlearned folk believe it as truth, and for those who call teaching the primary meaning of the novel, it is undoubtedly a golden rule. Yet, when even lewd books incorporate this to conceal their ugliness, is it not also a pitiful matter for the karmic novel? <128>

In a state of茫然自失 (muddled distraction), the clatter of hooves sounds, and a handsome young man appears riding a horse. This is a scholar from the same village, surnamed Jin (金), named Zhong (重), a classmate of Cuiqiao's younger brother Wang Guan (王觀). A talented man and a beautiful woman meet, thus creating the foreshadowing (楔子) for the later sections.

After returning home, Cuiqiao speaks to her younger sister Cuiyun (翠雲), saying, "Generally, a woman's body, if valued, is like Mount Tai; if taken lightly, it is like a goose feather. A fly on white jade affects one's entire life; how can one not be careful?" Perhaps this could serve as a motto for all women under heaven.

The second chapter is also a foreshadowing (伏線) for the later sections; it too is about the theory of karma (因果說). The theory of karma reaches its extreme here. Cuiqiao's entire life's destiny is largely foretold (卜了하얏스니) here. Cuiqiao meets a beautiful woman in a dream. With light makeup and plain clothes, apricot cheeks and peach complexions, graceful and elegant (娉娉婷婷), she is truly like an immortal maiden. She says, "Today, at the Heartbreak Gathering (斷腸會), I heard of your great talent. The host of the Heartbreak Gathering was greatly pleased, knew you would become a member of the gathering, and sends you 10 Heartbreak themes (斷腸題目) to request your verses. Please compose them quickly." The themes are: Lamenting Much Talent (惜多才), Lamenting Ill Fate (惜薄命), Grieving Parted Ways (悲岐路), Remembering Old Friends (憶故人), Thinking of the Beloved Slave (念奴嬌), Mourning Youth (哀靑春), Deploring Misfortune (嗟蹇遇), Bitterly Drifting (苦零落), Crying for Lost Love (哭相思). Cuiqiao takes up the red-trimmed brush (剪紅之筆) and composes on all themes. The beautiful woman praises her effusively and prepares to leave. Cuiqiao asks the day of their reunion. She replies, "We shall meet on the Qiantang River (錢塘江)." Wind chimes jingle, the Nanke dream (南柯夢) shatters; Cuiqiao is still in bed. The moon is bright as day; it is precisely the third watch. Alas! Was it a dream? This serves as foreshadowing (襯染) for Cuiqiao's entire life, making Cuiqiao resign herself to karma (因果) and submit to the whims (簸弄) of fate, also for this reason. How could this be a good method for a novelist seeking to reveal the subtleties of human life and pluck the heartstrings of human emotion? However, since ancient times, not a few have used dreams to implicitly reveal future paths; that authors of Eastern novels and plays often use this method needs no further mention now.

That young man on horseback has now become a prisoner of love. Eventually, after devising various plans, he finds a garden behind the Wang family residence and lives there, trying to communicate his feelings. As they say, if you wait, even persimmons fall. After living there for over a month, one day he sees a gold hairpin (金釵) hanging on a branch of a peach tree atop an artificial hill. Alas! This is the magpie bridge (烏鵲橋) of love. From this point, Jin Zhong (金重) finally succeeds in conveying his feelings to Cuiqiao; the process does not need detailed explanation here. There is no particularly ingenious structure descended from heaven, nor is there value in boasting about the author's skill, but love was achieved. However, that achievement was like the morning dew, like a spring dream; heartless and unfulfilled, it is all a matter of heartbreak and sorrow.

The third chapter is a scene of these two being overjoyed. Finding a small hole in a secluded corner of the garden, they make a tunnel-like path. The two meet and pour out their hearts; truly, they are those who bore a hole (穴을 鑽하야) to meet secretly. How different is this from the clandestine affairs (野合) of vulgar ruffians in the marketplace? If so, this book would ultimately have not even a trivial (些個) value, <129> and it would be difficult to create the many tragic stories in the later sections to make the reader shed (濺케 하기) tears of sympathy. Therefore, the author devised another means of bringing it to life; do not say this is something that shouldn't exist. Because of this, all the events in the later sections come alive; the reader weeps for her, and I too sigh for her. Listen to Cuiqiao rejecting Jin Zhong's desire for intimacy, lamenting Cui Ying (崔鶯, Cui Yingying), and setting forth her own innermost thoughts (所懷). "I wish, milord, that you pledge for life, and I, your concubine, will guard my chastity. Let us both play the flute and perform melodies, admire the moon and compose linked verses, perfecting the sentiments of a talented man and a beautiful woman, not falling into the wicked ways of adulterous women and men (淫婦奸夫). Then we two shall become pillars (榜柱) of virtuous conduct celebrated through the ages. Would this not be a commendable event worthy of transmission and emulation?" Thus, Cuiqiao was able to preserve her maidenly purity; her insight surpassed the vulgar. Look at the so-called pledge they made after establishing their covenant.

The pledge reads: "We, kindred spirits Jin Zhong and Wang Cuiqiao, born on such-and-such year, month, day, and hour, respectfully offer a stick of heart's incense and a cup of water-wine to the spirits of High Heaven and Thick Earth. We have heard that husband and wife value righteousness (義), and righteousness lies in remaining unchanged throughout life; young women are full of sentiment (情), and sentiment demands loyalty in life and death without betrayal. Previously, Cuiqiao wished for a home, Zhong wished for a family. Admiring talent and longing for beauty have already deeply united our hearts. Today, Zhong considers the beginning, Cuiqiao considers the end. Pouring out our hearts and souls, we dare to pledge for a different day. After this pledge, the man promises no change even through nine deaths, the woman vows chastity for life. Even if external circumstances bring unforeseen events, the heaven of our hearts is absolutely fixed. Should we violate this pledge, may spirits and heaven bear witness."

Wine flows freely, night deepens. Responding to Jin Sheng's request, Cuiqiao plays the huqin (胡琴). With each pluck, sometimes it is slow like a gentle breeze, sometimes rapid like a sudden shower, seeming to lament, seeming to weep, seeming to plead. After making Jin Sheng almost mad with joy, she enchantingly and gracefully presents the huqin to Jin Sheng. At this moment, what was Cuiqiao's demeanor like? Starry eyes hazy, red lotus cheeks flushed, like smoke enveloping peonies, like rain moistening peach blossoms. Jin Sheng, unable to restrain his amorous thoughts and finding the fire of desire hard to suppress, finally grabs Cuiqiao and holds her in his embrace. Cuiqiao resolutely rejects him, saying, "Milord, are you possessed again?" and with a scoff, proceeds to berate him eloquently: "A woman guarding her body is like guarding a vase; if the vase breaks once, it cannot be made whole again. If a woman is stained once, how can she regain purity?" Her words would make anyone, not just Jin Sheng, feel ice-cold. Cuiqiao's determination to preserve her maidenly purity, daring not to yield her body even to her lover before the proper wedding ceremony under bright candles, is truly a rare gem in the world. Since she valued her body and guarded her chastity thus, Cuiqiao's intent at this point was pure and untainted, completely different in kind from those who bore holes (穴을 鑽하는 者). Schiller said that love is the most precious phenomenon in the world; who would equate pure love with frivolous and fickle carnal desire? <130>

Maidenly purity is the ultimate beauty in the world. It's not that flowers aren't gorgeous, nor that the moon isn't beautiful. But they cannot compare to maidenly purity. Between Heaven's covering and Earth's bearing, all beauty is gathered, all essence extracted, to create maidenly purity; it is perhaps like the brilliance of precious gems. Cuiqiao knew its value. Holding onto it, she aspired eventually to become a model (龜鑑) for all women under heaven. However, fate was different from what she thought. It drove her into a ditch, intending to crush her flesh and bones together into mud. The author shows her firmly holding onto her maidenly purity, ultimately reflecting the circumstances of bitter wind and tragic rain in the later sections to come. At this, the reader cannot help but shed a drop of sympathetic tears for the pitiful Wang Cuiqiao.

When joy reaches its peak, sorrow multiplies. How can the morning of smiling flowers and singing birds turn into an evening of black winds and white rain? The fourth chapter is entirely written with a brush of sorrow and pain. The talented man and beautiful woman finally bid each other farewell, Cuiqiao's hopes all scatter like clouds, creating the beginning of the profound and tragic later sections. Jin Sheng rushes to Liaoyang (遼陽) for his uncle's funeral. The Wang family suffers disaster due to involvement in an affair concerning an aunt's husband; misfortune strikes suddenly, and Cuiqiao's father and brother are subjected to cruel punishment together (倂하야). Alas! What is needed now is 300 taels of silver; without it, father and brother cannot escape their fate. Cuiqiao, originally possessing a man's spirit, cannot help but cast aside the maidenly purity she revered and held fast; her lifelong aspirations must eventually be sacrificed for her family and father. She finally tells her parents of this intention. There are many tragic words here. However, this is a scene common in the world, a standard device of storytellers since ancient times. Cuiqiao tells her sister Cuiyun about Jin Sheng and begs her sister to entrust her feelings to Jin Sheng in her stead; looking up and down (俯仰에), she cannot contain her emotions. In her letter to Jin Zhong, she says:
"Cui Cui Qiao, ill-fated, disaster arises within my own home (蕭墻). Not acting like Ti Ying (緹縈) who pleaded for her father's pardon, I merely follow Li Ji's (李寄) example of selling oneself solely to relieve the family's hardship. Selling myself is surely a humiliation, shame, and regret for you, milord. Recalling my rejection under the lamplight, throwing the shuttle, it has finally become proof of my heartlessness. Thinking of milord's feelings, how could there not be resentment? Thinking of my heartlessness, I cannot continue. I humbly offer my younger sister to serve you; if milord, by chance, does not reject her and graciously (俯하야) agrees to this, perhaps it will somewhat repay your overflowing affection. Setting off soon for the ends of the earth, the oath under the moon has already become a vain thought. One huqin, one set of sorrowful tunes, one packet of lingering fragrance – if someday, with my sister, you burn incense, tune the instrument, and play melancholic songs, and fragrant smoke curls and a mournful wind rustles, know that my soul is present. If you would kindly offer a cup of tea to my wronged soul nearby (瀕한즉), <131> I shall have received much grace. The parting of life and death is barely concluded here. Words are short, feelings long, unable to express all. I only hope you take good care and fortunately do not think of me. To my faraway milord Jin, offered under oath, your beloved sister Wang Cui Cui Qiao bows."
The matchmaker arrives, informing that a guest from Linqing (臨淸) seeks a beautiful concubine. Cuiqiao tearfully agrees. Then, the guest in scholar's cap and fine clothes appears. Responding to a request, Cui Cui Qiao inscribes a line on a golden fan and also plays the huqin. What is the tune? "Hongyan Yuan" (紅顔怨, Lament of a Beautiful Face). It is mournful and heartbreaking; all listeners shed tears.
"That person said, 'Truly superb skill! How much bride-price is required?' The matchmaker said, 'She needs to save her father; nothing less than five hundred taels will do.' That person said, 'The bride-price is too much. Three hundred taels.' Cui Cui Qiao said, 'If selling myself cannot accomplish the task, what use is selling?' That person said, 'Then four hundred taels.' Cui Cui Qiao said, 'It must be five hundred taels.' That person added another fifty taels."
The flower-like, jade-like young woman Wang Cui Cui Qiao has now become a commodity for sale. The pitiful Wang Cui Cui Qiao, who firmly guarded her maidenly purity and refused her body even to her lover, has become an item sold off cheaply (歇價放賣物). From 300 taels to 400 taels, from 400 taels to 450 taels. Maidenly purity, at this point, is like a ball of mud. Alas! Poverty! From ancient times to the present, it makes heroes weep and virtuous women cry; their integrity and virtue are trampled (蹂躪) by the rich. Under the half-dimmed lonely lamp, whose tear-filled eyes are blurred? Is it only Wang Cui Cui Qiao? Go look into the inner workings of society. Are not all those places of narrow alleys and pleasure quarters (狹斜烟花) streets of soul-binding and heart-breaking (鎖魂斷腸)?
That evening, Cui Cui Qiao dreams she sees Jin Sheng. Jin Sheng throws down 300 taels to rescue Cui Cui Qiao, chooses an auspicious day, and holds a wedding banquet. The sounds of pipes and drums are loud as the two enter the bridal chamber. Suddenly, a group led by that guest from Linqing bursts into the bridal chamber and snatches Cui Cui Qiao away. Cui Cui Qiao, supported on horseback, is driven rapidly away. Wanting to wait for Jin Sheng on the road, just as she tries to dismount, she looks – what is this? It is not level ground but an extremely large pit of fire! Fierce flames leap and fly, ten thousand fathoms of fiery energy roll upwards, and a lump of fire the size of a bushel basket strikes Cui Cui Qiao fiercely on the face. "Ah! It's burning me to death!" she cries out and awakens startled – it was a monstrous dream. Cui Cui Qiao takes up her brush, trims the lamp wick, and composes eight poems on "Awakening from a Startling Dream." Having finished, she cannot return to sleep but only weeps. Who knows what state will be created next? The chapter ends with the conventional phrase, "Read the next chapter for the resolution."

Chapters 5 and 6 are the so-called "Sorrow and Lamentation Scenes." The parents cannot bear to make their child another's concubine, Cui Cui Qiao wishes to substitute her body for the family's hardship, the parental love and filial piety are fully depicted, presenting life's tragedies, such as the father refusing to sign the marriage contract, leaving no lingering implication (餘蘊) unexplored. However, <132> this does not differ greatly from the brushwork commonly used by storytellers East and West, past and present, so there is no need to recount the outline (梗槪) specifically here. The so-called *Shengtan Waishu* (聖歎外書, External Commentary by Shengtan) says: "Nothing disturbs a person's heart more than sentiment (情). And when sentiment disturbs a person's heart, what is most difficult to arrange is deep affection (至情); even a single strand is already hard to bear, so how much more when Jin Sheng's affection (恩情), parents' filial love (孝情), siblings' fraternal bonds (手足情), the bitter feeling (苦情) of selling oneself to follow a scoundrel (匪人), the parting sorrow (別情) of leaving home and the world, and the worldly sentiment (世情) of using bribes to resolve grievances all rush together at once, stirring Cui Cui Qiao's small heart? How could one like Cui Cui Qiao easily manage this?" Indeed. Cui Cui Qiao, amidst the confusion of a hundred emotions, is about to encounter the ultimate sorrow of the human world. The society of that time takes the pitiful Cui Cui Qiao and makes her suffer all the hardships (苦楚) under heaven, shatters her maidenly purity, entrusts her to a pack of wolves (豺狼), casts her into the mire (泥塗), and transforms her into a shameful demon (媿魔) in the pleasure quarters (烟花場裡). Alas! Society today is like the past, and the East is like the West, yet people forget society's crime and point at society's victims, sparing no painful curses or cold ridicule. Even if I am not You Gou (?), I too will soon cry out to declare society's crime.

Alas! Oh, azure Heaven! Why is Heaven so heartless? It intends to make a virtuous woman fall (駝酪) and suffer in the mire (淤泥). Flowers barely bloom before a mad wind tears them into fine powder; the moon barely becomes full before floating clouds cover it, creating deep darkness. Reaching Chapter 7, the reader cannot help but close the volume and sigh deeply (喟然長嘆). In the quiet, Cui Cui Qiao thinks of the past, imagines the future; recalling the scene of the pledge the other day, the scene of selling herself today, and the scene of longing in the future – thinking of them all together, her small heart nearly shatters. To whom can she complain of her sorrow? Only she knows her longing. Old Heaven ignores people's憔悴 (haggardness), tears add to the nine bends of the Yellow River's overflow, regrets weigh down the three peaks of Mount Hua. "Late, I arrive at the west tower and lean to watch; that setting sun, ancient road, withered willows, long embankment." (*Xixiang Ji* / *Romance of the Western Chamber*). Cui Cui Qiao's feelings surpass even Cui Yingying's sorrow back then. Finally, she composes a letter in blood. When her sister wakes startled and they talk, the pitiful girl's heart is instantly thrown into turmoil, and she faints (昏到) to the ground. "My sentiment is wide as the sea, my love is deep as the sea" – these are words uttered by a protagonist in one of Shakespeare's plays upon meeting her lover. Cui Cui Qiao did not even experience one night of joy. Yet, in entrusting a piece of her sentiment, even in dreams, her heart cannot forget Jin Sheng. However, worldly obligations force this pitiful child to turn her love towards another; the pain pierces her bones, and thinking upon it, she enters a state of unconsciousness. Parents and siblings rush over and revive her. Cui Cui Qiao recovers and finally recounts the matter of Jin Sheng. Her parents and those listening shed unbearable tears once more. The next day, that guest comes with a sedan chair seeking to marry (娶) her. Cui Cui Qiao weeps and what does she say? <133>
"Jin Sheng, Jin Sheng, your wife is separated from you today. In this life, we cannot join as intertwined branches; I wish to continue our fated connection in the next life. I, Wang Cui Cui Qiao, am so ill-fated! Unable to enjoy a dashing, talented husband, I instead go to marry a mad brute (狂且). Pitiful, a delicate flower carelessly stuck in the mire (淤泥). Since Heaven did not grant me a good fate, why vex a talented man? Since I met a talented man, why could this talented connection not be tied?"
So sorrowful, so mournful, weeping and wailing. But what can be done? Pitiful! A peerless beauty is paired with this brute like a horse or cow (馬牛蠢物).

What kind of person is this horse-cow brute (馬牛蠢物)? His surname is Ma (馬), immersed in debauchery, reduced to wearing paper clothes, he drifted to Linzi (臨淄). He formed a partnership with the wicked procuress Ma Xiu (馬秀) and came here to buy people. Pitiful! The virtuous Wang Cui Cui Qiao has fallen into his grasp (掌中). Creation's workings are exquisite, deceiving so cleverly! What a painful, hateful matter! Cui Cui Qiao arrives at his house, sees it clearly with her discerning eyes, and is filled with suspicion. That night, sitting alone, unable to suppress her grief and anger, she finally resolves to kill herself. Danger! Her hand reaches for the dagger on the table. A split second! She will preserve her integrity and end her life here. Will she end her life? The tale of Cui Cui Qiao would end here too, but suddenly Ma Gui (馬龜) bursts into the room. Alas! I cannot bear to write this; the author also sighed. Pitiful! Tender fragrance suddenly hit by jealous wind and rain; how could roving bees and frivolous butterflies know to cherish jade and pity fragrance? That night, Cui Cui Qiao composes nine verses of the song "Nai Jian Kuang Qie" (乃見狂且, Then I Met a Mad Brute) in indignation. On the day of departure, Cui Cui Qiao spoke her mind. But her decision was already made. Comforting her parents and siblings, the father, blinded by tears, parts with her. Though her heart is full, Cuiqiao sets out on a journey of ten thousand li.

Fate and circumstance do not allow Cui Cui Qiao to preserve her maidenly purity, causing her to fall into the places of pleasure quarters and narrow alleys (烟花狹斜). The author depicts the scene of Cui Cui Qiao's fall with a refined brush. In the so-called *Shengtan Waishu*, it is said: "A woman's body has no master; suffering and joy depend on others." How could I not feel pain and indignation for women?

Fate relentlessly drives Cui Cui Qiao, pushing her towards tragic and profound realms. The carriage arrives at a certain place in Linzi (臨淄). She sees only a fat, tall procuress (婆) of about 40 or more, who sees Cui Cui Qiao and calls out, "Cuiqiao dear, get down from the carriage!" Following her, she finds it is a shrine worshipped in brothels (娼家供養之淫祠). Cui Cui Qiao, ignorant of the world, how could she understand (曉得) this? Doing as told, she pays respects. The procuress continuously chants prayers for a thousand guests, ten thousand arrivals. "Show her to father!" Who is this father? It is that OOO Ma Gui (馬龜) whom Cui Cui Qiao thought was her good husband and married. Calling her husband 'father' – what is this? Cui Cui Qiao <134> is shocked (驚呀). The procuress is enraged. Glaring at Ma Gui, she curses him as a beast (業畜), and the remaining fury leaps out, grabbing Cui Cui Qiao's hair for a session of beating. Cui Cui Qiao realizes it is a brothel, loses all awareness, grabs a razor, and stabs herself once in the throat. Alas! The pure and innocent maiden has been deceived and trapped in a demon's den. Thinking about it, with Cui Cui Qiao's temperament, how could she endure this? The wicked procuress is also troubled. Promising to marry her off elsewhere after her wound heals, she lodges her in the Ningbi Pavilion (凝碧樓). One calamity seems to have passed, but misfortune follows closely, about to fall upon the pitiful girl's shoulders.

While composing poems in the Ningbi Pavilion, she suddenly hears someone in the next building reciting:
"Outside the pavilion, whose young lass is she? / Her long chant sounds through the green peach blossoms. / Sorrow invades the brush tip, low voice seems to choke, / Resentment towards the wind cries out in bitter sighs. / Distantly catching sweet fragrance, annoyed by butterfly powder, / Subtly conveying hidden feelings, pleased by the window gauze. / You must pity my abundant talent, / I pity you, still an unopened flower (未破瓜)."
There is a scholar in the next building, wearing a fluttering scarf and fine clothes. Although this scholar's recitation might not be high art (陽春白雪), it still belongs to the vein of poetry and books. Perhaps this offers a convenience for me to escape the fiery pit? Though intelligent, she is a woman; seemingly meticulous but actually quite reckless. Does she know or not know that her sole focus on escaping the demonic realm will, on the contrary, cause her to fall into an even more bitter situation? Cuiqiao's karmic debt (*債) is deep and heavy. This is the plot structure (脚色) of the eighth chapter.

He bows, she curtsies; heart communicates with heart. Cui Cui Qiao becomes acquainted with the scholar in the next building, named Chu Qing (楚卿). Cui Cui Qiao finally composes a letter, entrusts it to a mute servant girl, and has it delivered. The next day, a letter arrives. Opening it, she finds only the two characters '昔越' (seok wŏl) written. She deciphers it to mean 'come see me by climbing over the wall on the 21st day at the hour of Xu (戌時, 7-9 pm)'. Indeed, he comes. They talk, and she obtains his promise to help her escape the fiery pit. However, she who once refused her body even to her lover, suddenly upon this strange encounter (*), without leisure to consider the consequences, lightly gives her affection to him. Having once broken her integrity for her father, she now sullies her womanly virtue for herself. But it is all because circumstances made it so; what can be done? Now, her pitiful heart, desperate to escape the world of suffering, without deep thought or farsightedness, falls for Chu Qing's trick (口計) of "of the thirty-six stratagems, fleeing is best," and escapes by climbing out the window late one night. However, she ultimately fails to escape and is suddenly captured. The beautiful woman becomes stark naked (赤裸裸) and is beaten relentlessly for 2 or 3 *nyŏn* (年 - maybe 'times'? Or unit of measure for beating? Context suggests severe beating). One lash, one turn, reaching the extreme of misery. Chapter 9 narrates this miserable scene, showing that this misfortune, this disaster, is Cui Cui Qiao's own fault. ...(The rest follows in the next issue)...<135>

**Summary**

This article by Yang Paekhwa is a critical review of the classic Vietnamese epic poem written in Chinese, *Jin Yunqiao Zhuan* (The Tale of Kieu), attributed to Qingxin Cairen. Yang praises the novel's poignant depiction of the heroine Wang Cuiqiao's suffering and descent into prostitution due to cruel fate and societal pressures, despite finding fault with common narrative devices like overt karmic causality and a conventional happy ending. The reviewer contrasts it favorably with the complexity of *Honglou Meng* and the lewdness of *Jin Ping Mei*, appreciating its focused narrative and emotional depth in portraying the destruction of female purity and virtue. Yang explicitly critiques the societal hypocrisy that forces women like Cuiqiao into tragic circumstances while condemning them, extending sympathy to her plight. From a historical perspective, the article reveals Korean intellectual engagement with classical Chinese/Vietnamese literature during the colonial period. It highlights contemporary interests in themes like fatalism (karma), the critique of social injustice, particularly the suffering of women under patriarchal structures and poverty, and the tension between traditional literary conventions and emerging modern sensibilities demanding tragic realism.

一. 金雲翹傳
風雨燕子樓를 洗하야 關盻盻의 古事를 吊할길 바이 업고 春草梨花院을 鎖하니 李師師의 面影을 今에 어대가 찻잔말가. 遮莫, 秦淮의 渡頭에 新柳는 如烟한데 軟風은 丁字簾을 吹하야 嬌喉는 玉을 轉하고 璧月은 亞字欄을 射하야 舞影이 婆娑하도다. 誠是豪華의 地로구나. 江南花發水 悠悠한데 人到秦淮盡解愁를 不管風烟家萬里하고 五更懷裡囀歌喉라(桃花扇傳奇). 嗚呼라 秦淮의 地가 果然 莫愁의 鄕일런가. 그러나 終乃 天下薄倖佳人의 都會場됨을 奈何오 酷評을 마소라. 移春檻裡에 蠢爾하는 物이 원래 妖魔로 生함이 아니요 色을 鬻하고 媚를 賣하는 者-본대 彼輩의 本意는 아니러니라. 運命과 境遇가 彼等으로 하야곰 歌吹海에 投치 아니치 못하게함이라. 余는 不淨濊土로써 莫愁의 鄕을 삼는 徒輩를 排하노라. 그러나 賣色輩의 境遇를 觀하고는 마츰내 一掬의 淚가 업지 못호라.
金雲翹傳 1篇은 靑心才人의 作한 배라 中國小說 中에 그다지 傑作이라 稱할 것은 못되도다. 그러나 純潔無垢한 少女가 周邊의 境遇에 끄들리어 마츰내 烟花風月地에 떨어져 널리 苦楚를 맛보고 가추 艱難을 격는 것을 叙한 것이 敵이 可觀할 것이 업지안토다. 다만 結末이 缺陷의 情天을 補하야 大團圓을 慶事로 打出한 것은 此等 小說의 常套 手段이라 저 現代의 小說을 看過한 眼으로 悲慘深刻한 것이 아니면써 心을 慰치 못하는 讀者에게 對하야는 아마 不滿足함이 甚하리로다. 否라 有함이 不可한 結末을 作한 것은 好文의 徒의 깁히 同情을 表할수업는 것이나 그 經歷의 曲折이 잇고 浮世義理의 無情한 것을 述來하야 步步大破裂에 近케 하는 手腕에 至하야는 또한 可觀할 것이 업다 못하리니 文辭의 綺麗를＜127＞ 尙하고 脚色의 何如를 不顧하는 中國에 在하야는 돌이어 卓絶한 것이 잇도다. 紅樓夢의 錯雜함, 金甁梅의 淫猥에 失함, 余는 차라리 金雲翹傳 一流의 短하고 脉脉一貫한 것을 取하야 水滸傳, 西遊記 以外에 中國小說을 玩賞코저하는 士에게 推讚코저 하노니 平山冷燕의 한갓 詩句로써 滿載함과 玉嬌梨의 平平하야 凡調임과 如한 것은 原來에 足히 取할 것이 못되도다. 况五色石이며 况今古奇觀이랴 雜事秘辛으로써 幽艶하다 稱하고 飛燕外傳으로써 奇絶하다 云하니 余는 中國文學을 爲하야 此를 悲치 아니치 못하노라.
情의 一字는 卽 此書의 大經이요 苦의 一字는 즉 此書의 大緯라. 然하나 情은 반듯이 境을 待하야 生하고 苦는 반듯이 遇하야 生한다 함은 第1回의 冒頭에 金聖歎이라 署名하고 評한 言이라.
一劉淡仙을 끄을어 내어 引子를 作하고 虛形淡影中으로부터 翠翹의 終身情苦의 境을 將하야 八九로 托出한 것이 이 第1回의 脚色이라 觀하라 如何히 說하얏는가.
王翠翹는 一個의 佳麗라 胡琴에 妙하고 詩文에 巧하니 卽 金雲翹傳의 主人公이라 一日淸明에 弟妹를 拉來하야 踏靑을 試할새 行行重行行하다가 一座의 孤塚을 見하다, 姊는 不知왜라 弟 告하되 名妓 劉淡仙의 墓로써 하니 生하야는 萬人의 妻가 되고 死하야는 無夫의 鬼가 되도다. 翠翹가 長太息함도 實로 偶然이 아니로다. 이에 一詩를 題하야 曰
色香何處也  憑吊痛心哉  明月冷鴛被  暗塵封鏡臺  玉雖黃土瘞   名未白雲埋  尙有如澠酒  無人奠一盃
翠翹! 佇立良久에 能히 去치 못하는데 忽然 一陣怪風이 잇서 滾滾히 翠翹의 身을 吹散하는 도다. 이는 卽 淡仙과 翠翹와의 因緣이 不淺함을 示함이니 讀者로 하야곰 孽債의 由來한 바를 知케 함이라. 저 108의 妖星이 伏魔殿으로부터 走出하는 것과 그 搆想이 相似하도다. 因果主義로써 經을 삽음은 中國小說에 不少한 터이라 淫書覺後禪과 如한 것도 오히려 此를 效顰하얏도다. 東方의 小說 作者에 至하야는 此主義를 株守하야 朝鮮文學上에 부질업슨 例를 끼쳐 其俑이 오래 우리 文壇에 傳來하얏도다. 그러나 因果主義는 所謂 勸懲的 小說에는 絶好한 것이니 婦女子 不學의 徒에게는 정말가티 밋게 할 수 잇슬 것이요 敎訓으로써 小說의 第一義라 稱하는 者에게는 勿論 金科玉條일지로다. 然하나 淫猥의 書까지 이를 配合하야써 醜를 掩코저 함에 至하야는 因果小說을 爲하야 또한 가여운 일이 아닌가.＜128＞
茫然自失하는 際에 蹄音이 戞戞하고 一美少年이 騎馬하야 來하는 者-有하도다. 이는 同里의 書生 姓은 金, 名은 重, 翠翹의 弟王觀과 同窓의 友라 佳人才子가 相遇하야 玆에 後段의 楔子를 作하도다.
