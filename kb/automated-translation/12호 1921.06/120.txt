기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

Ideal Child's First Steps
						Ch'unp'a
With this piece, I offer it to you, readers, and while feeling dissatisfied myself, I am not without certain expectations towards you all. Readers—perhaps you know the writer's expectations.

1. Kim Chung-sik and One Night in His Hometown
Having filled his belly with the long-missed "koch'ujang tchigae" (red pepper paste stew) of his homeland and resting his hand on the knee of a dear friend, Kim Chung-sik, recounting the affairs of his hometown, the well-being of friends near and far, his own experiences, and current overseas situations, breaks through half ten thousand *ri* of land and sea in two days and nights. Whether it was due to harming his physical health, he strokes his thin face two or three times and shows signs of exhaustion to all his friends. However, he tries his best not to show any signs of fatigue. But all his friends, predicting his exhaustion, depart early, leaving the recounting of sentiments unfinished, to be continued later. Kim Chung-sik, guessing that his friends left early because his signs of exhaustion were revealed, repeatedly regrets it as he pulls the wooden pillow and lies down. And sighing a long, meaningful sigh, he thinks, "Now I've entered the homeland, but..." He then repeatedly reviews his long-held ideal plan, sequentially comparing the path to its realization. Then, as if seeing success, he sometimes smiles, and as if facing failure, he sometimes furrows his brow. However, although he foresees some obstacles, he is confident that he will surely succeed. Moreover, he seems to implicitly expect many obstacles to arrive, saying that the presence of obstacles is, rather, the standard of value for a promising child. Also, whether success or failure, his thorough self-commitment, from his school days until now, has been that he cannot but realize that ideal as it is. Not only for himself but for all his comrades, all his compatriots, and even down to the generations of descendants, he intends to gather, propagate, and actualize that ideal.
What Kim Chung-sik's ideal is and where his path lies is still unknown, but his ideal plan, which he repeats and commits himself to on the night of his arrival in the homeland, is about to be expressed into the world of facts moment by moment. <72>
The warm *ondol* floor gave him a peaceful sleep. Kim Chung-sik, sitting with the morning sun on his chest, holding the OO Daily News, silently reads the title of the article on page 3, "Chosŏn's New Figure, Mr. Kim Chung-sik," two or three times and smiles faintly. The article is as follows:
"Mr. Kim Chung-sik (28), who 6 or 7 years ago crossed the sea with the great ambition to become a worker for our society and tasted much bitterness and sweetness at the snow-covered window of unfamiliar Tokyo, Japan, has now graduated with honors from the English Literature Department of C University. He returned to Korea yesterday afternoon at 7 o'clock on the train arriving at Namdaemun Station and is staying at the Kongp'yŏng Inn. He plans to stay in Kyŏngsŏng for 2 or 3 days before heading down to his hometown OO."
Kim Chung-sik feels rather ashamed by this article, considering it undeserved excessive praise for himself. However, a secret smile rises and falls in his chest.

2. Kim Chung-sik and the Helmsmen (of the Press)
A young man wearing Western clothes and glasses, short and with a gaunt face, sporting a so-called artist-style haircut, came looking for Chung-sik. Following him came another young man, wearing a white *choui* (traditional jacket) over Western clothes, with a round face and also with his hair swept back, looking somewhat composed. The one in Western clothes identified himself as a newspaper reporter, and the one in the *choui* as a magazine company employee. Chung-sik, perhaps having picked up Japanese customs, bows his head 5 or 6 times and waves his hand 4 or 5 times. Then, with a face full of affection, smiles, and joy, he says, "Thank you so much for coming all this way to see me," and offers cigarettes. The guests offer words of comfort, asking, "How much did you suffer through the many years of wind and frost in a foreign land?" The conversation between the two parties proceeded very orderly and unfolded with passion.
Chung-sik: Right now, there are about four kinds of newspapers published in our Korean language, aren't there?
Reporter: That's right. But are they even complete? It's just in name only.
Chung-sik: The reason for that incompleteness is likely a financial problem, right?
Reporter: Financial problems are an issue, but primarily, it's a personnel problem. There's no money and no people, so what can be done?
Chung-sik: I suppose so. Is the scope of speech still about the same? Has it become a bit more lenient?
Reporter: It's still the same. Yesterday's edition of our newspaper was also confiscated.
Chung-sik: For what article?
Reporter: There wasn't any particular article. They said there was a subversive <73> phrase in some contributed piece on page 4?
Chung-sik: How many kinds of magazines made in our language are there?
Employee: Probably 13 or 14 kinds, including the monthly reports from various churches...
Chung-sik: That's still impressive. Compared to 2 or 3 years ago...
Employee: That too is mostly name without substance (有名無實). Most cease publication after the inaugural issue. Setting aside the freedom of publication, due to economic and personnel problems, it's impossible to do things ideally. On top of that, because we follow the continuous publication law, due to manuscript censorship, monthly publications sometimes become seasonal or even annual. And sometimes, they are discontinued altogether.
Chung-sik: But are there many readers? About how many copies does your magazine sell?
Employee: How can there be many readers? Perhaps that too is an economic problem, but there aren't many readers. And it seems the desire for knowledge hasn't fully emerged yet. As things stand now, our magazine, which sells the most, barely reaches 6,000 copies. Compared to the publication figures of magazines in other countries, it's probably not even 1/100th or 1/50th. The level of demand for knowledge in our country's youth circles is still lagging. Though, it seems to have progressed considerably these days.
Chung-sik: Is the collection of payments going well?
Employee: How could it go well? Most are on credit. We incur many losses. It would be good if income and expenses could at least break even, but forget breaking even, the ratio is 100 won of income to 200 won of expenditure.
Chung-sik: How can you run a magazine or anything else like that?
Employee: That's why it's a phenomenon of rising in the morning and disappearing by evening (朝起暮消).
Chung-sik: Truly, our country's press world (操弧界) is in dire straits. But it will get better in the future, right? Let's just proceed steadily with the hope that things will get better. Let's ignore the current flaws and deficiencies and just focus on devising the best strategies (最善의 方策) and relentlessly accumulate effort (勞力).
Employee: That's right. We believe our society will also advance with the momentum of a long stride, like water from a deep source (深源), from now on. Last year was different from the year before, and this year is different from last year. But as I mentioned earlier, it's really a personnel problem. We need more and more capable individuals with practical abilities.
Chung-sik: Indeed. The figures demanded by the modern era are people of practical ability. They must also be people of action who strive for practical implementation (懋實力行). However, it seems there are still many among us who are superficial speculators dealing in empty theories and fantasies. It seems there are many who act falsely and recklessly, claiming to make a fortune overnight (一擴千金) <74> or leap ten thousand *ri* at once (一超萬里). Are there still many among our people today dreaming of wealth and status or seeking fame and profit (名利)?
Employee: Of course, there are many. Forget other things, just look at the scramble for *ch'ampong* posts during the enshrinement ceremonies at the Royal Ancestral Shrine (祔太廟時의 參奉運動)... First and foremost, the fever for official positions (벼슬熱) still exists in our society. Those so-called prominent figures (名望家), businessmen (實業家), and intellectual class (識者階級) members—they've all pretty much held positions equivalent to governing a district (고을하나 式). Never mind that, even becoming a township council member (面의 評議員) or a county councilor (郡의 參事官) makes them act as if they've ascended to heaven; that attitude persists. It's likely mostly due to livelihood issues (口腹問題), but in this modern era emphasizing diligent practical action (懋實力行) and self-sufficiency (自作自給) based on labor, what kind of dirty behavior is that? And the antics (꼴악신이) of the so-called capitalists (資産家) are even more spectacular (可觀). When requests for donations or support are made under the noble cause of school building construction (校舍 建築) because they can't accommodate all school-age children (就學兒童) and teachers and students shed bitter tears, they claim to be planning for their descendants for ten thousand generations. They hold secret meetings in basements and bury their safes (金庫) underground so others can't see them. Although some enlightened individuals (先覺者) don't act like this...
Chung-sik: I believe that's not such a big issue. We shouldn't criticize them. We should only rejoice and welcome the fact that when the spring breeze blows gently (春風이 和蕩하야), all living things (群生之物) will coexist and prosper together (共存共榮). We certainly shouldn't complain about the remaining snow in the mountain valleys (山谷의 殘雪) or the fragments of ice in the ditches (溝壑의 片氷). How long will their life sustain itself? Rather, we can only mourn (哀吊) for them.
There is a brief pause in the conversation. The guests seem inclined to change the subject slightly. They each light a cigarette.
Reporter: Excuse my impoliteness, Mr. Kim, but in which field are you interested? Since your knowledge of literature is abundant, it must surely be that field, right?
Chung-sik: Are you asking about my employment?
Reporter: Yes. In what field do you plan to undertake work?
Chung-sik: Yes. There is something I have been interested in since my school days, and also something my nature, my innate disposition, most wants to do. That is, simply put, the intention to return to the farming village (農村).
Reporter: To the farming village?
Chung-sik: Yes. In our society, there's work to be done everywhere, whether in the city or the countryside, but in my opinion, rural development (農村啓發) is the more urgent issue. I mean returning to the farming village and living together with all the farmers, that is, the elders and youth of my native place (鄕土의 父老子姪). <75> All issues—such as industrial problems, educational problems, or improvement of customs, enhancement of life, etc.—cannot but take the farming village as their starting point.
Reporter: Yes. That's a fine thought. The rural problem—indeed, it is the urgent issue of the present. However, it contradicts what we expected of you, Mr. Kim. Our expectation for Mr. Kim, if you were to truly become the most valuable worker for our society, was that you would definitely take up a powerful pen (健筆) and elevate the world of hypocrites (僞善者 作界) first. Contributing to the literary world, the source of knowledge (知識의 源泉) and the focal point of cultural improvement (文化 向上의 主點), is the demand our society expects. Because there are hardly any textbooks for schools, no self-cultivation books (修養書類) for the youth, and nothing to open the eyes and ears (耳目) of society. And first, to awaken and stimulate all perspectives universally and socially, we expected you to propose an ideal iron plan (理想의 鐵案) for the newspaper or magazine world and wield a powerful pen. The rural problem is understandable, but first, to give universal new life (普遍的 新生明) to our society, it seems better for you to become a figure of the city and strive in social institutional work. My words might be too localized (局部的) and perhaps biased, but first, speaking for our newspaper company, all the staff truly had high expectations for Mr. Kim.
Chung-sik: Yes. That is also understandable. Of course, I know that many of you had such expectations for me. However, although my background is in so-called literature, in reality, I have no literary accomplishment. If I say this, you might call it modesty... And constitutionally, I cannot help but become ruralized. And speaking of work, it is not so valueless or commonplace either. Also, I am a step-by-step gradualist. In other words, I intend to practice the principle of cultivating oneself, regulating one's family, governing the state, and bringing peace to the world (修身, 齊家, 治國, 平天下 主義) literally. First, I don't know how I should live my own life. I have no definite principles for my individual self (個身). Also, I have not yet reformed my own family. All unreasonableness and imperfection remain as they are. And the village where I live, the township where I live, the district where I live—all are like that. Broadly speaking, these might all be universal problems of our society, but... Therefore, lacking principles and convictions as an individual, with unreasonableness and imperfection persisting in my family, and with the village and township all being thus, <76> conscientiously and practically, I absolutely cannot stand on the universal line blindly. So, under the ideal of step-by-step progress, I first aim to become a loyal servant (忠僕) to myself, a public servant (公僕) to my family, and then—ideally speaking—a public servant to the village, township, county, nation, and ultimately to the billions under heaven. Therefore, under the demand of my conscience, under the welcome of my innate nature, under the allowance of my skills, I commit myself henceforth to becoming a public servant of the farming village. Thus, I intend first to fulfill the duties of a public servant myself, have my family do likewise, and then propagate this to the village, the township, my fellow brethren, and all under heaven. I intend to carry out this propagation method not with brush or paper, nor with words or writing, but solely through action with this physical body (肉體), through actual practice (實行). If my ideal becomes fact through this, it will be fortunate for me, fortunate for my family, and fortunate for all aspects of society. And even if it doesn't succeed, I will have no remorse (愧) of conscience. Under the self-recognition of having fulfilled my own duty, I believe it will at least provide the solace of having lived genuinely (虛世의 慰安).
This is truly my principle. Recently, regarding all the issues being clamored about—and all the figures blindly acting (盲動) on those issues—many lack principles and convictions for themselves, their own families are unreasonable and imperfect, yet they dash about outside their scope, rattling on emptily (虛風) about socialism (社會主義), anarchism (無政府主義), the labor problem (勞働問題), the women's question (婦人問題). Those who cannot even liberate their own wives—that is, those who approve of their wives having to cover their faces or observe strict separation of sexes when going out or meeting people—single-handedly discuss world women's liberation. Those who themselves avoid (厭避) labor single-handedly speak on the global labor problem. For those who don't even educate their own children, what use is worrying about the education of others' children? It's either empty talk on paper (紙上空論) or outright hypocrisy (正面 假心). Therefore, I make the fulfillment of the self (自我의 充實) the main point of all endeavors.
Reporter: ...
Employee: That is truly so. That is what our society demands right now. We have heard many good words today. There is work at the office, so we must be going now.
The reporter and the employee promised to meet Chung-sik again later and departed. After seeing the guests off and entering the room, Kim Chung-sik knelt on both knees, thought deeply for a moment, then put on his Western clothes. He intended to go somewhere. He headed towards his alma mater, P School. It seemed his thoughts were unceasing.

3. Kim Chung-sik and the School Principal
The time Kim Chung-sik entered the main gate of the school was exactly noon. It must have been recess, as all the students were actively running about on the athletic field. There seemed to be about 500 or 600 of them. Overcome by nostalgia (感舊의 懷), Chung-sik felt an earnest desire to go straight into the office (事務室) and see the teachers, but he stood for a while on the athletic field, watching the students run about. While doing so, seeing all the students running energetically, he thought, "Ah, my beloved brothers—" and vividly acknowledging them as victors wherever they might go, he slowly entered the office. All the teachers stood up at once and greeted him. The principal, smiling broadly, came forward to meet him, extended his hand to shake his, and said warmly, "From this morning's newspaper introduction, I knew that you, Mr. Kim, had returned safely and gloriously. I was planning to visit you as soon as school let out, but I'm very glad you came by chance. So, have you been healthy all this time?" Chung-sik reported that his journey was uneventful and blessed the health of the various teachers, pouring out the accumulated feelings from their separation. The bell for the afternoon classes rang. The teachers each went into their classrooms. The principal and Chung-sik sat facing each other.
Chung-sik: The school has improved greatly compared to before.
Principal: It's gotten a bit better than before, but... the school building (校舍) is cramped (狹窄), which is a big worry. This year too, judging by the applications for admission, I would have liked to select about 500 students and expand significantly, but due to the school building issue, we only recruited 200.
Chung-sik: I heard from the newspapers and correspondence (傳便), but the enthusiasm for schooling (就學熱) seems quite considerable, doesn't it?
Principal: Considerable—it's astonishing! Take our school this year, for example; for the 200 spots, there were over 1,300 applicants.
Chung-sik: That's truly an astonishing phenomenon. So, what happened to the 1,100 who couldn't get in...?
Principal: What could be done? Some returned to their hometowns (還鄕) just like that, some attend places like private institutes (講習所), and some are probably just idling away their time (虛送歲月).
Chung-sik: If the enthusiasm for education is that high, then the issue from now on is the schools, isn't it?
Principal: That's right. This year alone, 4 or 5 schools have sprung up just within Kyŏngsŏng. It will gradually improve. With such enthusiasm for education, can we fail to provide schooling due to lack of schools? In any case, <78> this year's situation was truly pitiful. Do you know how many students who came from the provinces couldn't get admitted and returned empty-handed (空手), shedding bitter tears? There wasn't a single teacher at our school who didn't shed tears. We may not be able to teach those who come seeking learning well, but having to turn them away immediately upon arrival without question, think how the hearts of those sending them away felt, let alone those leaving.
Chung-sik: The more such tears are shed, the more they will become the light (빗) and value (갑) of our society. Those tears will surely not dry up powerlessly, tracelessly, and meaninglessly.
The principal remained silent for a long while, seemingly lost in thought. After some time, he turned to Chung-sik and asked with these words:
Principal: Now that you have completed your studies (業) and returned, in what field do you intend to be active?
Chung-sik: Well. I plan to return to the farming village and do something for the rural community.
Principal: The farming village? That's fine too. But not just myself, the entire school had high expectations for you, Mr. Kim.
Chung-sik: You expected things of me? For what work?
Principal: Most schools are probably like this, but our school has a big problem with finding lecturers (講師). In any case, you must help with the school's affairs. Even if you can't do other things—especially out of love for your alma mater—
Chung-sik: I am grateful that you speak so highly of someone as unqualified (不資格者) as me, but since I already have separate principles, I cannot accept your words as they are.
Many words went back and forth between the principal and Chung-sik. Urging him to take on school work, refusing, going to the farming village, not being able to go, principles being too stubborn (固執), and so on—there was much debate-like conversation (立論的 談話). Even with the principal's fervent persuasion, he could not bend Chung-sik's principles.

4. Kim Chung-sik Entering His Hometown
Chung-sik stayed in Seoul for one week. During that time, he received many requests (請) from various sectors of society and also faced many difficulties. He was even criticized as being heartless (沒情漢) and heard himself called a negativist (消極主義者) many times. The newspaper companies, magazine companies, schools, and society—each tried hard to entrust Chung-sik with important responsibilities (重任). Thus, there were 5 or 6 banquets (宴會) and several informal discussion meetings (懇談會). <79> Whenever there was a meeting, he often struggled immensely to overcome the difficulties. Chung-sik cast off all thousand and ten thousand matters and set off towards his native place (鄕土) according to his original intention. However, he unavoidably took on the responsibility for the magazine company. He did not particularly evade (回避) the external responsibility (在外 責任) for the magazine company. There was an implicit (隱然) sense of welcome (歡迎).

The distance between Anju Station (安州驛) and Chung-sik's hometown was 50 *ri*. Although there were dangers of mountain paths (山路) and bends of ravines (溝壑), Chung-sik did not take a horse or a rickshaw (人力車) but proceeded simply on foot (徒步). He only had his belongings loaded onto the men and horses (人馬) dispatched (委送) from his family home. Chung-sik was determined not to rely on the labor (勞役) of men and horses except in urgent and unavoidable circumstances. His principle was to traverse mountains and waters, a thousand *ri* or ten thousand *ri*, using his own hands and feet (手足). During the 50-*ri* walk, Kim Chung-sik had thousands and tens of thousands of thoughts.
First, he reminded himself again to be extremely humble (下心) when entering the village and meeting all the elders and brethren, taking care not to show any arrogant (倨慢) air. And he resolved absolutely not to reveal (現露) any foreign airs. He intended to definitely bow when meeting woodcutters and shepherd boys (樵夫牧童) and to definitely exchange polite greetings (相禮) when meeting servants (下人 使喚). And he intended to definitely use honorifics (敬語) when speaking to his wife and treat her with true love (眞愛), and definitely not use abusive language or teasing (辱說弄談) when addressing his own children and nephews (子姪). And he intended not to ask others to do things, big or small, but to carry them out himself. He intended to become an unconditional public servant to the innocent brethren (天眞兄弟) of the farming village and pour out his sincerity (誠) and blood (血) to the extent his mind and body (心身) could endure. He was determined to baptize (洗禮) all the brethren with sincerity and blood, truth and love and tears, and to make the native land radiant (光明化).
He reviewed his family improvement plan again. He also continuously thought about the rural development plan (農村 啓發案). And he repeatedly considered (重復重考出) all rural development institutions such as Education Associations (敎育會), Agricultural Cooperatives (農會), Youth Associations (靑年會), Women's Associations (婦人會), Boys' Associations (少年會), etc., but decided not to adopt the radical (急進的) methods (手段) of contemporary youth (時俗靑年). Slowly, after becoming ruralized himself and sufficiently understanding (洽足히 아른) the surrounding circumstances, and then slowly becoming peasant-like (農民化), he would open the path of improvement step by step, gauging the farmers' favor (歡心 與否) according to their level, ensuring there would be no conflict (衝突) between old and young, and no separation (隔在) between the classes (階級) of the knowledgeable and ignorant (有無識), thereby truly building an ideal heaven (理想의 天國).
With these thoughts, Chung-sik arrived at the entrance (門압흘) of his hometown garden. The mountains and rivers (左右山川) on either side seemed to welcome him, implicitly holding affection and wearing smiles. Step by step, he entered through the verdant, moist mountains and waters (潤澤이 즈르르 흐르는 무르녹은 山水), exchanging warm greetings with the farmers (農夫님들) of the upper and lower plains (上下坪), and was embraced (담싹 안기엇다) by the innocent and artless (天眞爛漫) nature of his homeland. <80> Mountain birds sang (啼), appearing and disappearing in the green shade (綠陰), while yellow calves lowed (嘶), roaming freely on the green slopes (靑坂). Skylarks (雲雀) rose and fell, and orioles (黃鶯) called to friends (喚友). Young girls (少女) gathered herbs, and an old man (老翁) held a child (抱兒). Chung-sik, overwhelmed with emotion (感激情極), his mind and body divinized (神化), exclaimed, "Ah—this is my native garden (鄕原)! Ah! This is the paradise of freedom (自由의 樂園)! Ah—this is the land where my navel was buried (배꼽 그린 땅), and also the land where I will die! The land to enjoy forever, the land I can never abandon forever! Ah—my deeply affectionate paradise of freedom, embrace me forever. I will love you forever." And so, he entered the gate of his long-missed ancestral home (故家). (Continued in the next issue)
Apologies, but please wait until the next issue. The ideal of the protagonist, Kim Chung-sik, will emerge in the next issue. How does his ideal plan for the farming village compare with the ideals of you, esteemed readers? Perhaps it won't be entirely uninteresting. <81>

**Summary**

The article introduces Kim Chung-sik, a young Korean intellectual returning home in 1921 after graduating with honors from a university in Japan. He grapples with societal expectations from the press, magazine publishers, and his former school principal, all urging him to use his talents in urban centers for journalism, literature, or education. However, Chung-sik firmly believes his path lies in returning to his rural hometown. He advocates a gradual, "step-by-step" approach, starting with personal and familial reform and then dedicating himself as a "public servant" to rural development through practical action rather than rhetoric, aiming to build an "ideal heaven" from the ground up.

This article is historically interesting as it portrays the dilemma faced by Western-educated Korean intellectuals under Japanese colonial rule: whether to engage in urban-centric, potentially more visible (but also censored and constrained) activities like journalism or education, or to focus on grassroots rural development. It highlights the perceived inadequacies and challenges of the colonial press and education system, the persistence of traditional status-seeking ("official post fever"), and critiques superficial activism. Chung-sik's emphasis on personal example, practical action ("懋實力行"), self-sufficiency, and gradual rural reform ("步一步主義") reflects one response to the conditions and debates within the nascent nationalist and social movements of the period.

기사형태	문예기타

理想兒의 初步
						春坡
나는 此一篇으로써 讀者 諸君에게 提供하며 스스로 不滿의 感을 抱하면서도 諸君에게 對한 무슨 期待가 업지 아니합니다. 讀者-或 記者의 期待를 알으실른가.

1. 金重植과 故園의 一夜
오래 그리웟던 故國의 「고추장찌개」에 배를 불키고 情다운 親舊의 무릅에 손을 언고서 鄕園의 事情, 遠近知友의 安否, 自己의 經驗談, 現下의 海外事情을 두루두루 叙懷삼아 말해가는 金重植은 水陸 半萬里를 2晝夜에 突破하노라. 身體의 健康을 傷함이엇던지 여윈 얼굴을 두 세 번 쓸어 만지며 모든 親舊에게 支離의 感을 보인다. 그러나 自己로서는 어대까지 支離의 氣色을 아니보이려 한다. 그러나 모든 親舊는 그의 支離할 것을 預測하고 叙情이 未洽하나마 後期를 두고 일즉이 허여저 간다. 모든 親舊가 일즉이 허여저 감을 自己의 支離의 色이 露出됨으로 自測한 金重植은 後悔를 거듭하면서 木枕을 당기어 자리에 눕니다. 그리고 「이제 故國에 들긴(入) 들엇다마는...」하면서 意味 깁흔 한 숨을 길게 쉬인다. 그리고 自己의 前부터의 理想案 그것을 다시금 다시금 反覆하면서 實行의 彼岸을 順序的으로 計較해본다. 그리다가 成功이 보이는 듯이 或 웃기도 하며 失敗가 當하는 듯히 或 이맛살도 찝흐린다. 그러나 그는 多少 支障이 잇기로써는 自測하나 確實히 成功하리라고 自處한다. 그리고 그는 支障이 잇슴이 돌이어 有爲兒의 價値的 標準이라하야 만흔 支障이 當到하기를 隱然히 期待하는 듯 하다. 또 自己로서는 成功이든지 失敗이던지 그 理想을 그대로 實現치 아니치 못하겟다는 徹底한 自期가 學校時代로부터 이때까지엿다. 自己뿐 아니라 모든 동무 모든 同胞에게까지 또는 子孫 代代에 至하기까지 그 理想을 集湊시키며 宣傳하며 또 事實化케 하려고 한다.
金重植의 理想이 무엇이며 그의 行路가 어대인지는 아즉 모르나 故國 纔到의 夜反復自期하는 그의 理想案은 刻一刻事實界로 表現이 되려한다.＜72＞
따뜻한 溫突이 그에게 安眠을 주엇다. 아츰해를 가슴에 바드며 OO日報를 들고 안즌 金重植은 3面 記事 「朝鮮의 新人物 金重植군」이라는 題를 두 세 번 暗讀하면서 빙글에-웃군 한다. 그 記事는 이러하다.
「이로부터 6 7年전에 우리 社會를 위하야 일군이 되겟다는 큰 뜻을 품고 멀리 바다를 건너 산달코 물설흔 일본 동경의 설창(雪窓)에서 쓰고 단 것을 만히 맛보든 金重植(28)군은 이번에 C대학 영문학과 (C大學 英文學科)를 우등으로 졸업하고 작일 하오 칠시 남대문 착 렬차로 귀국하야 공평녀관에 투숙하얏는데 군은 2, 3일간 경성에 두류하다가 곳 자긔고향 OO로 내려간다더라.」고.
金重植은 이 記事로써 自己에 對한 非分的 過贊이라고 돌이어 부끄러워한다. 그러나 그의 가슴에는 남모르는 웃음이 오르락 내리락 한다.

2. 金重植과 操弧者
洋服입고 眼鏡 쓰고 키가 작달막하고 얼굴 갤죽하고 所謂 藝術家式 머리한 靑年 하나이 重植에게로 차저왓다. 뒤 미처 洋木 힌 周衣 입고 얼굴 둥글고 亦是 뒤쓸어 넘긴 머리로 좀 沈着해 보이는 靑年 하나이 왓다. 洋服者는 新聞 記者라 하며 周衣者는 雜誌社員이라 한다. 重植은 그나마 日本風을 바든지라. 5, 6次 고개를 굽슬굽슬하며 손은 4 5番 내여 젓는다. 그리고 含情含笑喜滿한 얼굴로 「모처럼 이러케 차저 주시니 感謝합니다.」하면서 卷烟을 내여 勸한다. 客으로부터는 「異域의 多年 風霜에 얼마나 苦生이엇느냐」고 모든 慰辭를 보낸다. 兩便의 談話는 매우 順序的이오. 또 熱情으로 열리엇다.
重植. 지금 우리나라 글로 하는 新聞이 두루 다하야 네種인가 되지오?
記者. 그럿습니다. 그나마 어대 完全합니까. 名色 뿐이지오.
重植. 그 不完全의 理由는 大槪 金錢問題 이겟지오?
記者. 金錢問題도 問題려니와 첫재는 人物問題이지요. 金錢 업고 人物 업스니까 어찌 할 수 잇습니까.
重植. 그러켓습니다. 言論의 範圍도 그저 그 모양이지오? 좀 너그러워 젓는가요?
記者. 그저 그모양입니다. 우리 新聞 昨日分도 押收이엇습니다.
重植. 무슨 記事로요?
記者. 別 記事가 업섯습니다. 第 4面의 어떤 寄書에 不穩의＜73＞ 句가 잇섯다던가요?
重植. 우리 글로 만드는 雜誌는 몃 種이나 되는가요?
社員. 아마 13, 4種 이지오. 各 敎會月報까지...
重植. 그나마 壯합니다. 2, 3年 前에 比해서는...
社員. 그 亦 有名無實입니다. 大槪는 創刊號 나고는 그만입니다. 出版의 自由는 且置하고 經濟問題 人物問題로 到底히 理想대로 할 수 업서요. 게다가 繼續 出版法에 依하니까 原稿檢閱 때문에 月刊이 或 節刊도 되고 或 年刊도 되게 됩니다. 그리고 或 -廢刊도 잇구요.
重植. 그런데 讀者는 만흔가요? 貴 雜誌는 몃 部 假量이나 팔립니까.
社員. 讀者가 만흘 수 잇소. 그도 亦 經濟問題 인지는 모르나 讀者가 만치 못합니다. 그리고 知識慾이 아즉 덜 난 모양이야요. 우리 雜誌가 只今 形便으로는 第一 만히 나아간다는 것이 겨우 6千部입니다. 남의 나라 雜誌 出版高에 比하면 100分의 1, 50分의 1도 못되는 모양이지오. 우리나라 靑年界의 知識要求의 程度야 말로 아즉이야요. 그나마 이즈음은 매우 進步된 모양이지오.
重植. 代金 回收는 잘됩니까.
社員. 잘 될 리가 잇소. 대개는 외상이지오. 만히 밋짐니다. 收支相殺라도 되엇스면 조켓는데 收支相殺는 且置하고 收入 100圓에 支出 200圓 比例가 됩니다.
重植. 그래서야 어대 雜誌고 무어고 할 수가 잇겟소.
社員. 그러니까 朝起暮消의 現像이지오.
重植. 참말 우리나라 操弧界야 말로 말안입니다. 그러나 압흐로 잘 되겟지오. 우리는 잘되리라는 希望만 가지고 꾸준히 나아갑시다. 現在의 缺點 非點은 그대로 默殺하고 오즉 最善의 方策만 講究하면서 不絶히 勞力만 싸하봅시다.
社員. 그럿습니다. 우리 社會도 이로부터는 深源한 물과 가티 長足의 勢로 進步할 줄 압니다. 昨年이 再昨年과 다르고 今年이 또 昨年과 달습니다. 그런데 아까도 말슴햇지요마는 實로 人物問題입니다. 자꾸 자꾸 實力的 人物이 만히 생기어야 될 터인데요.

4. 鄕園에 入하는 金重植
重植이가 서울서 留하기는 一週日間이잇다. 그동안에 社會 各 方面으로 여러 가지 請을 만히 바닷고 또 難關도 만히 當하얏다. 심지어 沒情漢이라는 評도 듯고 消極主義者라는 말도 만히 들엇다. 新聞社는 新聞社로 雜誌社는 雜誌社로 學校에서는 學校로 社會에서는 社會로 다 各其 重植으로써 重任을 막기려고 만히 애를 썻다. 그래서 宴會도 5, 6次 이엇스며 懇談會도 몃 번이엇다.＜79＞ 會談이 잇슬적마다 難關을 免하노라 無雙 애를 쓰군 하얏다. 重植은 千事情 萬事情 다-떨어버리고 本來의 뜻대로 鄕土를 向하야 떠낫섯다. 그러나 雜誌社의 任은 어찌할 수 업시 마타가지고 갓다. 그는 雜誌社의 在外 責任으로는 그다지 回避 하지 안핫다. 隱然히 歡迎의 味가 잇섯다.

安州驛과 重植의 鄕里와는 50里의 相距이다. 山路의 險이 잇고 溝壑의 曲이 잇지마는 重植은 말도 人力車도 안이타고 그냥 徒步로 가게 되엇다. 本家로서 委送하얏던 人馬에게 行具를 실렷슬 뿐이다. 重植은 緊急 不可避의 事情外에는 決코 人馬의 勞役에는 依賴치 아니하겟다는 決心이다. 山이고 물이고 千里고 萬里고 自己의 手足으로써 跋涉하려는 主義이다. 50里路 中을 徒行하는 그 這間의 金重植은 千思萬慮 생각이 만핫섯다.
爲先 그는 鄕里를 들어서서 모든 父老나 弟兄을 對할 때 極히 下心하야써 倨慢의 風을 아니보이려고 다시금 注意한다. 그리고 外國의 味를 絶對로 現露치 안흐려 한다. 樵夫牧童을 對할때 반듯이 절하려하고 下人 使喚을 對할때 반듯히 相禮하려한다. 그리고 自己婦人을 보고 반듯히 敬語로써 하며 眞愛로써 하려고 하며 自己의 子姪을 對할 때 반듯히 辱說弄談을 아니하려한다. 그리고 大小事間 남을 시키려 아니하고 몸소 實行하려 한다. 農村의 天眞兄弟에게 無條件으로 公僕이 되어 心身이 盡하는 대까지 誠과 血을 부어노흐려 한다. 그는 정성과 피, 또는 참과 사랑과 눈물로써 모든 兄弟에게 洗禮하며 鄕土로 하여금 光明化케 하려는 決心이다.
그는 家庭 改善案을 또 다시 反覆한다. 農村 啓發案도 繼續하야 생각한다. 그리고 敎育會, 農會, 靑年會, 婦人會, 少年會 等 모든 農村 啓發的 機關을 重復重考出한다하되 時俗靑年의 急進的 手段을 取치 아니한다. 徐徐히 自己부터 農村化가 되어 周圍의 事情을 洽足히 아른 뒤에 또 徐徐히 農民化가 되어 程度에 맛도록 農民의 歡心 與否를 보아가면서 한 가지 두 가지 곳 步一步로써 改善의 途를 開하되 老少의 衝突이 업도록 有無識의 階級이 隔在치 안토록 하야 眞實로 理想의 天國을 建設하겟다고 생각한다.
重植은 이러한 생각으로 故園의 門압흘 到達하게 되엇다. 左右山川이 그를 반겨주는 듯 隱然한 가운대 情도 먹음고 웃음도 띠웟다. 潤澤이 즈르르 흐르는 무르녹은 山水사이로 步一步들어가 上下坪의 農夫님들과 따뜻한 人事를 밧고 주고 하면서 天眞爛漫한 自然의 鄕愿에＜80＞ 담싹 안기엇다. 山鳥는 啼하야 綠陰에 出沒하며 黃犢은 嘶하야 靑坂에 縱橫하는데 雲雀이 上下하고 黃鶯이 喚友한다. 少女-나물캐고 老翁이 抱兒하엿다. 重植은 感激情極에 心身이 神化하야 『아- 이것이 나의 鄕原이로구나! 아! 이것이 自由의 樂園이로구나! 아-이것이 나의 배꼽 그린 땅이오. 또 죽을 땅이로구나! 永遠히 즐길 땅이오. 永遠히 버리지 못할 땅이로구나! 아-나의 情 깁흔 自由의 樂園아 네 永遠히 나를 품어다고. 내 永遠히 너를 사랑하리라.』하면서 오래동안 그립고 그립던 故家의 門을 들어선다.(來號繼續)
