人類學에 對한 槪念
						一記者
人類學과 動物學
Just as there is Zoology for animals and Botany for plants, it is quite natural that there should be Anthropology for us humans. Broadly speaking, since we humans are also a type of animal, there would be no reason for Anthropology to exist separately when Zoology already does. However, who is it that makes such a fuss classifying things as Zoology or Botany? Isn't it we humans who do so? Since we are the ones doing it, what need is there to deliberately place ourselves within a category of animals and mingle together with them? Not only that, but whether considering the complexity of our organization or the breadth of our distribution, there is a practical need to study us humans differently from other animals. That is, we must differentiate the categories. Therefore, Anthropology must necessarily exist independently. And indeed, it does exist independently.

人類學 硏究의 動機
Then, what kind of thing is Anthropology? What about humanity does this field of study research?
Humans—this thing called "us humans"! At first glance, it seems quite simple. What do we look like? Head round, feet square (趾ㅣ方하고), a little body hair, speaking, laughing, drawing, quite cunning—nothing special. Generally, this is true, and just as others are like this, people from P'yŏngan Province
<78>
are like this, and people from Chŏlla Province are like this. Also, how did we come into being? Our parents gave birth to us, and our parents' parents gave birth to our parents, and going back generation after generation, it's still the same, nothing special. Just as I am like this, others are like this; just as Japanese people are like this, Chinese people are like this. In short, there seems to be nothing problematic. However, let's not just look narrowly at "people" or "P'yŏngan Province" or "Chŏlla Province," but let's look broadly at the world's humanity. And let's not just think of my parents or my parents' parents, but let's think about the very beginning of human existence. If we do so, doubts will surely arise. A desire to research what humanity generally is will emerge. Isn't there a saying like this in our language? When there is a solar or lunar eclipse, people say, "A fire dog comes and bites the sun/moon and takes it to the dark country." This is, of course, merely a kind of folk tale (野語), but didn't we imagine that while there is this bright country, there must be people living dimly in a dark country? Also, don't we commonly say (恒語) that in some places there are people with four eyes, and in other places, people have a hole passing through their chest, front to back, through which they thread carrying poles to carry each other? This too is merely a kind of amusing story (笑話), but didn't we conjecture that if we exist like this, there must surely be other people who exist like that? Furthermore, if we look at the first volume of the *Chinasaryak* (支那史略 - Brief History of China), which we read from the first day we enter the village school (書堂), doesn't it say that T'aeho Pok'ŭi (太昊伏羲) had a snake's body and a human head (蛇身人首), and Yŏmje Sinnong (炎帝神農) had a human body and an ox's head (人身牛首)? Thinking about this, shouldn't we imagine that we humans were not neatly formed like this from the beginning, but evolved from something, and evolved again, to become human like this? These things—imagining that there are people living dimly in a dark country, that there are four-eyed people, that there are those with snake bodies and human heads, or human bodies and ox heads—are all concepts related to Anthropology.
If we had thought about it that much and stopped, that would be one thing, but if we had taken one step further and systematically added research to conjecture, we would have become distinguished anthropologists. However, back when we thought there was no world beyond the East, it was factually impossible to fully research Anthropology. Anthropology formed a complete academic discipline at the earliest only after the utilization of the steam engine. Therefore, even if someone asks, "Why haven't you had anthropological research until now?" we have nothing particularly to be blamed for. The issue starts from now.

人類學의 眞正性質
Then, what is the true nature of Anthropology?
Pinar (피나-) of France (佛蘭西) says:
Anthropology is a branch of natural history (博物學) that initiated research concerning humanity as a whole and the subdivisions of races (人種) within humanity.
Tylor (다이라-) of England (英吉利) says:
<79>
Anthropology is the natural history (自然史) of mankind.
Weiss (와이스) of Germany (獨逸) says:
Anthropology, as its name suggests, is the science (理學) concerning humanity as a whole; strictly speaking, it is the science concerning the nature of mankind.
Reconciling the above various theories, if we venture to define Anthropology,
It would be most appropriate to say that Anthropology is the science (理學) concerning humanity as a whole and the habits (習性) of humanity. To explain this further:
A. Anthropology is the science concerning humanity as a whole. That is, since it broadly refers to humanity, specific research on one individual or the people of one nation would not be Anthropology. Since it concerns the whole of humanity, a study focusing only on certain parts of the human body (肢軆) is not Anthropology. At the same time, within this world—no, within this universe (if, for example, there were people on Mars, that too)—any race (人種) that can properly be called a race, and even things that are not human but resemble humans, such as anthropoid apes (類人猿), which are classified within humanity, will equally fall within the scope of anthropological research.
B. Anthropology is the science concerning the whole of humanity and its habits (習性). It is the science concerning human habits, not the science concerning humanity's original mental nature (心性). Therefore, the study researching the human mental nature may be Ethics (性理學) or Psychology (心理學), but it is not Anthropology.
C. Anthropology is the science (理學) concerning the whole of humanity and human reason [Translator's note: The text says 理性 (reason) here, but given the context of point B distinguishing from 心性 (mental nature) and the overall definition focusing on 習性 (habits/behaviors) and 總軆 (whole entity), 理學 (science/principle study) seems intended, consistent with earlier usage. The sentence structure suggests "science concerning the whole and habits," paralleling the definition. The inclusion of 理性 seems like a potential typo or a slight conceptual inconsistency by the author, possibly confusing 理學 with 理性. The translation follows the likely intended meaning based on context]. It is not chemical; being a science (理學), it differs from decomposing and researching the constituent elements (元素) of the human body or dissecting and researching the joints (骨節) of the human body. Rather, it studies humanity as it is, researching its overall entity and habits. To research its overall entity and habits, one naturally comes to research the present state and history (沿革) of humanity.
To state this more systematically, the objects of anthropological research would be as follows:
1. What does the word "human" mean?
2. What is the scope of synonymous terms for "human" in various countries?
3. What are the common characteristics (通有性) of humanity encompassing these?
4. What position does such humanity occupy in relation to nature?
5. What are the similarities and differences between humans and apes (猿類)?
6. Is all the world's humanity of one species or several species?
    a. What is the geographical distribution of humanity?
    b. What are the similarities and differences among humans in various regions?
    c. How should humanity be classified?
    d. What is the present condition (狀況) of various races and their mutual relationships?
    (A) What is the situation regarding human migration (移住)?
    (B) Is the place of origin (本源地) of humanity a single location or multiple locations?
    (C) What are the causes of race formation (人種形成)?
    (D) Are the ancestors (祖先) of humanity a single pair of man and woman or numerous men and women?
    (E) From when has humanity existed, and where was the place of humanity's first appearance?
The scope of anthropological research is this broad. It is impossible to explain even the outline (梗槪) in just ten-odd pages. If the opportunity arises, I will speak more concretely, but here, as indicated by the title, I will merely show the concept of what Anthropology is and stop. <80>

**Summary**

This article defines anthropology as the scientific study of humanity as a whole, including its physical characteristics, distribution, origins, classification, and habits (distinguishing it from psychology or anatomy). The author argues for anthropology's necessity as a distinct field from zoology due to humanity's unique complexity and self-awareness. The piece traces the motivation for anthropological inquiry to innate human curiosity about diversity and origins, referencing folk beliefs and myths, while noting its formal development as a discipline is relatively recent, tied to advancements like the steam engine. It outlines a wide range of research questions encompassing human evolution, classification, migration, and the definition of humanity itself. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects an early 20th-century Korean intellectual engaging with Western academic concepts like anthropology, attempting to define its scope and relevance for a Korean audience. The author's justification for Korea's late entry into the field and the blending of references to Western scholars (Pinar, Tylor, Weiss) with East Asian traditions (Chinese creation myths) highlight the complex cultural and intellectual landscape of colonial Korea.
