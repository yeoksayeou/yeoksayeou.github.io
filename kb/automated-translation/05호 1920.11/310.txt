Roar of the Great Man
(Part Two)
						Soch'un

The Point That Makes a Great Man Great = Carlyle
The great English thinker Carlyle, along with the great literary figure Shakespeare, formed the treasure house of English literature. Moreover, how he encouraged and roused innate sincerity with his ardent flame, scolding the darkness and calling out the righteous courage of the age, is truly enough to make a coward stand and a warrior leap for joy. In a passage from his work *On Heroes*, where he explains the essential quality of a great man, he stated as follows:
"Regarding great men, I specifically assert the following: namely, the sole quality of a great man is simply the one thing called 'Truth' (眞). This Truth indeed forms the cornerstone of the various mental states coiled within his psyche. Anyone who could accomplish great deeds, like Mirabeau, like Napoleon, or like Cromwell, there is no instance where they were not passionate about their cause. Therefore, I call them men of utmost sincerity (至誠). This so-called utmost sincerity—profound, great, and pure—must be called the primary characteristic of all great men. However, the utmost sincerity spoken of here does not refer to self-proclaimed sincerity. So-called self-proclaimed sincerity belongs to the base and most contemptible,
that is, it is another name for shallowness and exaggeration; in other words, it is self-conscious sincerity, which merely pertains to conceit. The great man's sincerity is not so; it is something the mouth cannot speak of, and the heart cannot know. As I conjecture, he would rather be aware of his own insincerity; how could he possibly be aware of his own sincerity? I declare: the great man's sincerity does not originally belong to himself;
<112> his sincerity emerges because he cannot help but be so. More than anything, the great fact of the establishment of this myriad universe is the greatest thing to him. Even if he wished to escape, he could not flee from the presence of this sublime, stern, and certain 'Reality' (實在), because the structure of his psyche is made that way from the start. Is this not the reason for his greatness? The source of his overflowing, seemingly boiling, heartfelt emotion is precisely the 'Universe,' its reality like life, its truth like death. The teeming masses forget the truth of this Universe and sink into false superficiality, but the great man does not do so. The blazing reflection of truth illuminates him from beginning to end, and as soon as its reflection enters his eyes—nay, as soon as any falsehood or injustice of the world enters his mirror—he immediately becomes a thunderbolt, a living fire, compelled to investigate its root. Gentlemen, please take these words and make them the primary definition concerning the great man."
Furthermore, the passage praising the courage of the Northern European people is truly powerful writing for the ages. He stated:
"Courage is the duty immortal through all ages. This duty was so in the past, and will be so in the present, the future, and for ten thousand generations. Not only that, courage is supreme value. Our primary duty still lies in conquering fear today; if we cannot eliminate this, we cannot succeed in anything else. If we do not overcome fear, control fear, and gladly trample it underfoot, our actions will be base, inevitably becoming like bats flying at night; our thoughts will be false, and our thinking will become that of slaves and cowards. I say again: we must first be brave. We absolutely must be brave. Entrusting ourselves solely to our original mission and choice, facing death as if returning home, we must forge straight ahead and strive manfully. In essence, never fear. Regardless of the era, the degree to which one conquers fear is sufficient to judge the stature of a person."

That is One, This is One = Liezi
Liezi was a man from the ancient Chinese state of Zheng (鄭), whose name was Yük'ou (禦寇), and lived between Confucius and Mencius. His teachings are based on Laozi and have many similarities to Zhuangzi. As the *Shiji* (史記) does not contain his biography, his deeds cannot be traced. There are eight chapters transmitted as the writings of Master Lie, and looking at the full text, it is clear he was a splendid thinker. Now, a passage from his *T'ien-jui* (天端篇 - Chapter on Heavenly Signs) chapter states:
Someone lamented the ennui of the present world and discussed the existence or non-existence of the afterlife, and the conversation turned to the theory of the destruction of heaven and earth. Liezi heard this and laughed, saying, "To say heaven and earth will collapse is false, and to say they will not collapse is also false. Whether they collapse or not is not something we can know, nor need we know it. That is one, and this is one, are they not? (Meaning they are independent and unrelated.) Therefore, living, one does not know death, and dead, one does not know life; coming, one does not know going, and going, one does not know coming. How does the destruction or non-destruction of heaven and earth concern us?"
Alas! The past is already past. There is no trace. It is like a spoonful of rice already
<113> swallowed down the throat. The future is yet the end times. Not even a sprout is visible. It is like fish not yet caught. Today's people! Though their number is 1.7 billion, who can trace back and live in the past of the 19th century? And who can leap forward to the future of the 21st century? There is only the present world. Only the present world. In other words, only Today. What ideals do you have? If you have them, you must realize them Today. Also, what desires do you have? If you have them, you must acquire them in the present world, during your own time. Alas! To say heaven and earth will collapse is wrong, and to say they will not collapse is also wrong. That is, the afterlife is wrong. Only the present is certain. Whether it succeeds or fails, it is only this present world. Ah, Master Liezi's view of life, how keen it is!

I Came to Bring Division = Jesus
Jesus Christ, born 1919 years ago in Bethlehem (벳을네헴) of Judea (猶太), as soon as he uttered the cry of a newborn, immediately incurred the wrath of King Herod (헤롯) at the time, and fled with his father Joseph (요셉) to Egypt (埃及). After Herod's death, he returned to the land of Israel (이스라エル), but Archelaus (이켈나오), who succeeded Herod, also hated him, so he unavoidably departed to the region of Galilee (갈닐리). Afterwards, from Galilee, he went to the Jordan (요단) River, received baptism from John (요한), and being greatly moved, fasted and prayed for forty days in the wilderness. Conquering various temptations, he finally achieved great enlightenment, concluding that "Man shall not live by bread alone, but by the truth of God; do not test God, but only worship and obey him." While preaching this gospel to the multitudes, he was first rejected by the Jews, and tragically met his end as dew on the Cross at the prime age of 33, as we well know. However, his gospel, every word is tears of blood and challenging.
Here are one or two examples:
Mr. Jesus entered a house, summoned the scribes who came from Jerusalem (에루살렘), and spoke in parables. During this, one person informed him, saying, "Your mother and brothers are outside looking for you." Jesus replied, saying:
"Who is my mother, and who are my brothers? Whoever does the will of God is my sister and my mother."
Another day, addressing
a rich household, he said:
"I came to cast fire upon the earth; what is my desire? It would have been good if the fire were already kindled. Do you think that I have come to establish peace on earth? I tell you, no, but rather division. From now on, five in one house will be divided, three against two and two against three; father against son and son against father, mother against daughter and daughter against mother, mother-in-law against daughter-in-law and daughter-in-law against mother-in-law."
The seeds he sowed will not be in vain. They will sprout and bloom everywhere in this world. That is, this world will now fight intensely. More than wars between nations, class will fight against class, the new against the old, and family against family. Thus, there will be a day when the organization of the world will be fundamentally reformed. <114>

**Summary**

This article, "Roar of the Great Man (Part 2)," presents excerpts and interpretations of the views of Thomas Carlyle, Liezi, and Jesus Christ on the nature of greatness, truth, and action. Carlyle emphasizes sincere truthfulness (not self-proclaimed) and courage (conquering fear) as the defining qualities of great men. Liezi advocates focusing entirely on the present reality, dismissing concerns about the past or future (including cosmic destruction or the afterlife) as unknowable and irrelevant to current life and action. Jesus is portrayed as a challenger of the status quo, stating he came not to bring peace but division (fire and conflict), predicting strife within families and society as a necessary precursor to fundamental change, driven by adherence to God's will over earthly ties. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article's emphasis on inner truth, courage against fear, the urgency of present action, and the acceptance (even welcoming) of conflict and division as forces for radical change could resonate strongly with contemporary readers facing oppression. Highlighting figures who defied convention and advocated for conviction-based action, even at great personal cost or causing societal upheaval, might have served as an indirect commentary or inspiration for Koreans navigating their own challenging circumstances under Japanese rule.
