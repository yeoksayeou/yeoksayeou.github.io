新詩
						吳相淳

疑問
White-haired,
Eighty-year-old grandmother (八旬 老한머니)
Barely taking hesitant steps,
A child with its first teeth (初齒),
Face (面) and face (面) meet each other,
When eyes and gazes (視線) collide,
I wanted to ask.
To the grandmother:
"Do you know—that child?"
I wanted to hear.
From the child:
"Do you know—that grandmother?"

Cloud
Flowing cloud,
My eyes following it,
Vanishing without a trace by itself,
In the moment of seeing her disillusionment (幻滅),
Gently dissolving,
Indifferently (無心) bursting into laughter,
And then
Shedding tears—.

創造 (To a certain Young Sculptor)
One thumb,
For three years,
Pouring all passion (熱情), life (生命), and energy (精力),
You have been making,
And from now on,
Your entire life (一生涯),
You pledge to dedicate to its completion (完成)—
Oh, young friend of passion,
I fear (畏) you.
I see, I feel within your "core,"
The pulsation (躍動) of the God of Creation (創造의 神).

To a Certain Friend
When evil thoughts (邪念)
And delusions (妄想)
Invaded (侵襲),
Drawing a famed sword (銘刀)
Like autumn frost (秋霜),
My old friend of the same path (同道),
Where are you now,
Fighting well (健鬪)? Striving (努力)?
For life (生), for the realization (實現)
Of a more valuable life,
My longing (追慕) for you is deep.
Especially now (昨今), when I feel
The keen spirit (氣銳) of devotion (精進)
Has dulled (鈍).

My Suffering
Following laughing people,
Being unable to laugh,
Is suffering (苦痛).
However,
For weeping people,
Being unable to weep,
Is greater suffering.

Philosophy of Life
The essence (本質) of all things in the universe (宇宙萬有) is all
Leben
"Life" (生)—
Within the impulse (衝動) of philosophical intuition (哲學的 直覺),
To a stone, I placed my ear
Quietly,
To iron, I placed my hand
Gently.
As if crazed—. <77>

**Summary**

This collection of six short poems by O Sangsun, titled "New Poetry," explores themes of existential questioning, the passage of time, disillusionment, artistic dedication, camaraderie, suffering, and the fundamental nature of life. The poems juxtapose old age and infancy, fleeting beauty and sorrow, intense artistic creation and the poet's awe, the memory of a striving friend, the pain of emotional disconnect, and a philosophical search for life's essence in the inanimate. From a historical perspective, these poems offer insight into the introspective and often somber mood of some Korean intellectuals under Japanese colonial rule. The themes of suffering (苦痛), striving (努力), and philosophical searching (using the German word "Leben") reflect the complex emotional and intellectual landscape of the era, grappling with modernity, identity, and the human condition amidst political constraints. The use of Hanja alongside Hangul and older Korean spellings also provides linguistic data for the period.
