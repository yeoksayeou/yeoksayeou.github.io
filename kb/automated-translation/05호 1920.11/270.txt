丁 A Scene of Tragedy (A Child's Death) by Tongŏm Saeng, Kyŏngsŏng

On the day of the full moon (望日) in July, the bright moonlight fills our yard, all things are quiet, and the grass and trees are deathly still, utterly silent. The sound of wild geese, crying "kkiyak kkiyak" as they fly high above, provides a desolate accompaniment to the tune I play on my short flute (短嘯). The only sounds heard from time to time are the "ttarŭrang" bell of the newspaper deliveryman running by and, for reasons unknown, the barking of the neighborhood house dogs... The time is half past 10 in the evening, and among the leaves of the trees in the silent yard, dew has formed thickly, making them glisten as if oiled. I blow away the fatigue in my brain from working all day with a single note from my short flute and enter the inner room (內房), take off my clothes, and cover myself with the bedding. From where...? Suddenly, the boisterous wailing sound of several women arises, chattering like a shaman (巫黨) performing a soul-calling ritual (넉하다) or a blind man reciting scriptures.

"Aigo- aigo- Oksŏng-ah!"
"Where have you gone!? Leaving your father, your mother, your younger siblings, where have you gone!? Aigo- *sob sob* - Aigo Oksŏng-ah! The moon has brightened, that moon I used to see you under has risen again, but where have you, who saw that moon, gone!? The policeman (巡査) is my enemy! Why did you seize an innocent child, *sob sob*? When you sent my precious, gem-like Oksŏng to that hellish Sunhwa Wŏn (順化院), how could I have known you would die in there!? *sob sob*... Aigo, the smell is awful too. They sprayed everything – inside the room, the wooden floor, the kitchen, inside the wardrobe – saying they were disinfecting with something called 'Aijeru' (아이제루) or 'K'aijeru' (카이제루). Ah- the smell..."

Already unable to fall asleep due to the sound, clear tears unknowingly fall from my eyes, wetting the pillow I lie upon.
I mutter to myself, "Ah! There are so many cruel things in the world!!"
"Someone else's child..." and my words trail off, leaving me momentarily silent.
The pendulum clock (掛鐘) ticking away by itself on the wall reports 3:00 AM through the dark room, striking my ears with "ttŏng ttŏng ttŏng".
Ah! It’s already been four hours since I lay down. Opening the east window, the bright full moon shines on my face, and in the empty sky, only the stars twinkle. The desolate sound of crickets seems to present nature’s music, and the faint Milky Way tilts towards the west.
Ah- I must sleep. I have to leave early tomorrow. Covering myself with the bedding, I force myself to invite the sleep that does not come. The women's weeping sound has not ceased by this time and comes disturbingly through the covers...

乙 Autumn Rain by Sulmyŏng Saeng, Taegu

The rain that fell all day seems to have lessened its intensity.
<101> The window suddenly brightens, and perhaps the wind has changed direction, as the sound of fine rain striking the west window is heard. A chilly feeling arises. Listening intently, the loud sound of water rushing down the stream flowing west is heard. "Perhaps dawn has broken," I think, and upon opening the room door, a cold wind hits my face as if piercing it. Clouds are flowing from the northwest, so the cold is understandable. On the persimmon tree standing before the yard, only one ripe persimmon (紅柿) hangs lonely, and the raindrops clinging to the end of each branch look like white beads strung together. The sound of them gathering and falling, gathering and falling, strikes the fallen leaves, making a listless noise.
The wild chrysanthemums (野菊) blooming on the ridge of the radish field (무수밧 두덕) have become pitifully withered by the overnight rain. Within the grassy patch, the chirping of crickets (蟋蟀) is heard intermittently.
Not a single person passes along the sparse country road. In the water pooled over the rice field, the black dots of harvested rice stalks look strangely disheartening.
Looking towards the distant village, the houses, looking like stacked grain piles (露積), appear grey, blurred by the rain.
Another gust of wind blows fine rain into the room.
I close the window and turn my gaze back to the poetry book I was reading.
A sharp chill runs from my neck down my back. I thought about grabbing my overcoat (두루막), but remained seated, hunched over.
From the inner room, the sound of my mother ironing can be heard.

乙 The Outskirts by Yukchŏng Saeng, Kyŏngsŏng

The grass, like spread silk,
is fine and clear,
and in the western part of the high sky,
like snow, white,
a single cloud
wanders lonely.
The sun shines steadily,
providing cover,
and the river, swollen after the rain,
visible from afar,
embracing the mud strongly,
flows quite swiftly.
"Ferryman, ferryman!"
A shepherd boy's (牧童) voice, raised high,
calling out,
makes the rivers and mountains ring.
In the poplar (포풀라) forest,
embracing the dense green,
with a clear voice,
"Ssŭrŭram! Ssŭrŭram!"
Is it welcoming the beauty
of the evening glow with song?
Or is it weeping (욺인지)
for the hazy vagueness (漂渺함) of twilight,
startled (아차러위)?
Greenly, beautifully,
finely wrapped in pretty clothes,
made up flawlessly,
the wide and flat
fields
are even more luxurious.
In the clear, slanting wind,
the pale rice blossoms sway precariously,
gently emitting fragrance (香氣),
coming and going fitfully,
in the rain that falls without cease,
growing vigorously together,
then, innocently tied up at home (집헤 묵기어),
going for sightseeing inside the gate at dawn,
"Farewell, clear water!"
they say, splashing foolishly<102>
the muddy waters.
They load and pull like mountains.
Seeing the sting of the whip,
with gentle and calm faces,
stepping slowly,
one step, two steps,
their two large eyes on either side,
perhaps proud of exerting themselves
in their heavenly duty (天職), blink, blink.
Holding a fishing rod and
a round basket (둥그미) in his right hand,
and a scoop net (반두)
in his left hand,
on the dry ground,
the sound of wooden clogs
goes "ttubŏk ttubŏk",
with a face flushed with hot blood (血潮),
he follows his long shadow.
A group of lads (놈아들),
carrying baskets (삼태),
legs rolled up in the stream
trickling between the green rice paddies,
with faces like negroes (니그로),
on slender sticks,
catch minnows.
From that mountain corner,
into the clear empty sky,
spewing black smoke,
the whistle sound (박휘소리) is loud,
not deviating by a moment,
heads leaning out dimly
from the train window coming from Wŏnsan (元山)
gradually become clearly visible.
One plume, two plumes,
from the farmhouse chimneys,
smoke billows up vigorously,
the turquoise-clad water bird,
shining again in the sunlight
that had been hidden,
lightly sings a cool song (生凉의 曲)
and flies.

**Summary**

This article presents three short literary pieces followed by an editorial note. The first piece depicts a tragic night scene where a mother mourns her son, Oksŏng, who died after being taken to the Sunhwa Wŏn (a colonial institution), possibly hinting at police brutality or harsh conditions, alongside the unsettling smell of disinfection. The second piece offers a melancholic description of an autumn rainy day in Taegu, focusing on sensory details and a sense of chill. The third describes a vibrant rural scene near Kyŏngsŏng, contrasting pastoral images of nature, farming, and children playing with the intrusion of a modern train. The editor's note thanks readers for submissions but urges them to improve the quality and clarity of their writing, providing submission guidelines.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its glimpses into Korean colonial life. The first piece explicitly mentions the police (巡査) and the Sunhwa Wŏn, reflecting the harsh realities of colonial control and institutions. The mention of forced disinfection ("아이제루/카이제루") points to public health measures implemented by the authorities, perceived negatively by the narrator's neighbours. The other pieces offer insights into the atmosphere and daily scenes of the period, while the editorial note reveals the state of literary activity and literacy among the magazine's readership during that time.
