望鄕
						在江戶 잔물

Whether I go to the mountains
Whether I go to the sea
The whispering sounds
I hear with envy

Over the mountain crest
Beyond there
The evening sun sets
Ah, my body alone
Its shadow is lonely.

If this body were a flying bird
I would not weep like this, but
Ah, my homeland is behind the distant mountain
It lies beyond those white clouds.<110>

In Sŏul, the grassy field
I often trod upon
The fallen leaves
Must be dancing now.

Ah, my dear, my love (愛人)
When strolling on the grass
The footprints I sought for you
Look for them within your heart.

With red lips like a blooming rose (薔薇)
Listening to sweet words like songs
My passionate young heart, seemingly bursting, played freely
How many happy days I spent there.

Mountain passed, field crossed, I came to this place
Beneath the trees shedding leaves, I think alone
The sight of you weeping silently appears before my eyes—.
Ah, a traveler (旅人) who has left a lover hurts even more.<111>
<110-111>

**Summary**

This poem, "Manghyang" (Longing for Home), expresses the deep homesickness and sorrow of a Korean individual living in Edo (Tokyo), identified by the pen name "Chanmul in Edo". The speaker feels alienated from nature and longs for their distant homeland, specifically recalling cherished memories of walking with a loved one on grassy fields in Seoul. The poem contrasts past happiness and connection with the present state of lonely exile, concluding that the pain of separation is amplified for a traveler who has left a lover behind. From a historical perspective, the poem is significant as it voices the personal displacement and nostalgia experienced by Koreans in Japan during the colonial period. The specific mention of Edo and Seoul, the sense of being far from one's '故國' (homeland), and the melancholic tone reflect the emotional landscape of colonial subjects living away from home, possibly as students or laborers.
