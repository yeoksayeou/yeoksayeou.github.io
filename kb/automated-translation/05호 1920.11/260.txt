讀者交情欄 (Readers' Friendship Column)

To the reporters and all the readers across the world, are you well during this change of seasons? I have been an avid—no, an ardent—reader of this Kaebyŏk since the special issue. More than anything, the most welcome news was the establishment of the Readers' Friendship Column starting from issue No. 4. Let us make good use of such a fine column to exchange knowledge and deepen our friendship. Are we humans not animals that live in groups? I sincerely hope that we help and guide each other to become healthy members of our society. (Hanch'ŏnsaeng from Kimpo-gun)

Esteemed elder brothers, are you perhaps all in good health? Your younger student is a young, poor scholar secluded in a remote village in Kanggye. I wish day and night to become a true person through the excellent guidance of the reporters and various elder scholar colleagues, so I earnestly pray that you all become fine people. (Hansa from Kanggye)

To all readers under heaven! I have participated in the last ranks of devoted readers since Kaebyŏk No. 3. Although several months have passed since Kaebyŏk roared its mighty lion's roar (獅子吼), I, being deaf and dim-sighted, remained completely unaware until about 10 days ago when I went to a certain bookstore. While looking through this book and that, amidst the vast blue waves, perched atop a sharply risen rock, was an eagle with its two wings stretched wide, its two legs firmly gripping the rock. Its eyes were piercing (구리구리하고), and its beak was wide open, looking as if it would sweep through the myriad winds and clouds in a single breath at dawn, or as if the sun, moon, and stars would lose their light on the spot should it strike. Goodness, what book is this? Wiping my eyes and looking closely, the two characters 開闢 struck my gaze. Quickly grabbing it and flipping through, the contents also startled my heart. I thought, "Ah, this is completely different from those recent magazines that just put on airs and lack substance," emptied my pockets, and bought it in a flash. To all readers under heaven, as one of the readers of Kaebyŏk, I wish to borrow the right to speak within this column and say a word or two, whether it be dog barks or chicken squawks. Lately, I have observed what is called human life. Looking at it, everyone seems like drunkards or like people startled awake from sleep by a shout. How is it that they boast so unreasonably about being great? Parting their hair in a high-collar (하이칼라) style, wearing gold-rimmed (金禮) glasses even without nearsightedness, donning tailored (?) Western clothes (채대리가튼 洋服),<99> riding around in automobiles on clear days in good health, lifting their heads high as if they were the sole masters under heaven. And when a young and pretty woman passes by, they clear their throats with an "Ahem!" as if to say "Look at me!" and stroke their mustaches, thick as a toad's tail, with a hand adorned with a gold ring.—Not that everyone is like this—but how is this different from a drunk person shouting wildly as they please, regardless of whether others laugh or point fingers? Lectures promoting the Human is Heaven principle (人乃天主義) or the Cultural Movement (文化運動) stir up the land of Chosŏn, magazines like Kaebyŏk, Sŏgwang, or Seoul raise high the lion's roar (獅子吼) of their respective principles and assertions, and newspapers like the Tong'a Ilbo or Chosŏn Ilbo work intensely like a downpour (비빨치듯) for the nation and for culture, crying "Wake up! Rise up! Act!" Is it because people's minds are bewildered? They dither, unable to decide whether this way or that way is good, deciding something today only to change it tomorrow, starting something now only to quit a moment later. They blink their eyes blankly without spirit, lacking vitality. How similar they are to someone just woken from sleep! We, the readers of Kaebyŏk, are all youths with self-awareness and feeling, so I expect without doubt that you will strive and struggle, and through struggle achieve success. But those who are not like this cannot but face my reprimand and cannot avoid receiving admonishment. Imitating someone, I too say "Ahem," in the spirit of "Keep your wits about you even if dragged away by a tiger." Everyone, just sharpen your spirits, look at the world situation, respond to the trends of the times, and with appropriate self-awareness and self-appointment, each will find their own path. Oops, having written this, my own face feels hot [with embarrassment]. Anyway, I pray for the health of the reporter gentlemen and fellow readers and end here.— (Chang Yŏpchu from Kyŏmip'o)—

Have sympathy for struggling students (苦學生). What is meant by sympathy (同情)? It is putting one's own body and mind into the thoughts and circumstances of others; various virtues such as charity, public good, tolerance, etc., are all sympathy. We Korean people, from ancient times to the present, have lacked the heart of sympathy. Whether for a society, a nation, or an individual, the presence or absence of sympathy can clearly distinguish between civilization and barbarism. Sympathy is directly proportional to spiritual development. No matter how much modern material civilization has reached its peak, material civilization not based on spiritual civilization gives little happiness to our human lives and instead becomes harmful poison, as can be clearly proven by looking at the past European War. Indeed, where there is no sympathy, human society lacks the zest for life and is merely desolate; it feels like suddenly stepping on a poisonous snake or encountering a ferocious lion in the desert, utterly cold-hearted. Only where there is sympathy is there also the zest for life in human society; it is like the harmonious spring breeze, soft and warm. Furthermore, the distinction between a gentleman (君子) and a petty person (小人) can be made based on the presence or absence of sympathy. Let us reflect on the histories of the various sages and worthies like Confucius, Sakyamuni, Jesus, etc., whom we always admire and worship. (Omitted - original text) Everyone, please express your full sympathy. Please sympathize with the struggling students who strive day and night, thirsty for funds to pay for their education. (Omitted - original text) Moreover, are they not the ones striving to fully display the ample strength they have stored and the self-generating internal characteristics when their own unique stage for lively activity unfolds in the future? Who would not express full sympathy for this? "If one eats one's fill, wears warm clothes, lives idly, and receives no education, one is close to being a beast (近於禽獸)," thus spoke an ancient Chinese sage, creating a golden maxim (金科玉條) regarding the value of education for later generations; seeing this, we can understand how necessary education is for human life. They are the struggling students who, having realized this early on, disregard myriad difficulties and hardships and strive to learn, to learn. What heartless person would not sympathize with this? Truly, this world is unfair. Why is there so much suffering for them? Why is it that profligates, who make it their daily routine to drink, chat merrily, and enjoy themselves in bars and brothels (酒肆靑樓), spend money like water (用錢如水) even in times of financial panic?<100> One cannot help but cry out against the unfairness of the world.
For those who, facing adversity, brave myriad hardships and strive day and night to secure funds for education, please moisten their dry throats with tears of sympathy....(Chŏng Tong'yun, 2nd Year Student, Posŏng High School)...

Mr. Ch'oe Chongbŏm of Yongch'ŏn-gun? Your passion is truly appreciated. Your excellent poem is admirable, admirable indeed. However, due to space limitations on the page, we cannot publish it, which is deeply regrettable. (One Reporter) <101>

**Summary**

This article compiles several letters from readers of the magazine *Kaebyŏk* in a dedicated column. Readers express enthusiasm for the magazine and the new column, emphasizing the importance of mutual support and intellectual exchange. Some letters offer social commentary, criticizing the superficiality and lack of direction among some contemporaries, contrasting them with the ideals of the Cultural Movement and publications like *Kaebyŏk*. A significant portion pleads for sympathy and support for *kosaeng* (苦學生), students struggling financially to pursue education, highlighting their dedication despite hardship and decrying social inequality. The column concludes with a polite rejection notice to another reader due to space constraints. For historians, this column offers valuable insights into reader engagement with nationalist/intellectual publications during the colonial period, contemporary social critique (particularly of Westernized affectations and apathy), the perceived role of media in national awakening, and the stark realities of economic hardship faced by aspiring students, reflecting broader social stratification and the high value placed on education.
