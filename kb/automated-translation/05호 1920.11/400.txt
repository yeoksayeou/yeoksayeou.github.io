脚本 隔夜
(第三幕)
						玄哲
Stadamhup House Study. (Same as Act II, Scene 1)
Afternoon. Drizzling.
Anna, Old Man Uwal, the girl Shōya, etc., are gathered around a Hwatu table, playing Hwatu (flower cards).
Shubin is leaning back on a Turkish chair, reading the "Moscow Gazette".
An atmosphere of boredom and listlessness seems to fill the room.

Uwal: Alright, hearts—

Anna: You again, Uncle! You've been winning all by yourself since earlier.

Uwal: (In a drawling voice) The ten of hearts—

Anna: (To Shōya) Look at this, can we even compete like this?

Shōya: (Just smiles)

Shubin: (Nodding to himself) Haa! Hoah! (Taps his foot to a rhythm while reading the newspaper.)

Uwal: Alright, hearts—

Anna: Hey! Again! Ah, I think I'll just quit. There's no end to this. (Shouts to Shubin) Pawiēl, Pawiēl!

Shubin: (Absorbed in the newspaper, distractedly) Yes, yes?

Anna: Come over here. Uncle is cleaning us out all by himself. Hanging them up, nine of hearts, ten of hearts—

Uwal: The nine of hearts came out again.

Anna: Look at that! Is there any point in playing—

Shubin: That’s really too bad.— But setting that aside, Auntie, something terrible has happened. Something terrible! <143>

Anna: Terrible? What terrible thing? I'm the one having a terrible time.

Shubin: Now listen. It seems war is inevitable.

Anna: Really! Where?

Shubin: Where— you ask? You're too complacent. They say war has started between Russia and Turkey.

Anna: Really.

Shubin: It's not just ‘really’. Big trouble has started (惹端 natssŭmnida). Haven't you seen the newspaper? Shall I read it to you?

Anna: Uh-huh. (Listless reply)

Shōya: (In a soft voice) Yes, please read it to us.

Shubin: Shall I read? (Reads) Eh, where is it...
"Turkey Declares War." The Turkish Emperor has set a deadline of October 27th, noon, demanding the withdrawal of garrison troops stationed in the Principality of Bulgaria from the Russian Emperor. If the troop withdrawal is not completed within that deadline, he has urgently notified that he will immediately take hostile action.
"Border Mobilization." The Russian Emperor has ordered the border defense corps to mobilize within 24 hours. Now, this is the big trouble.— Listen carefully. (Reads) "Sofia Riots and the Rise of the Bulgarian National Party." As the signs of war starting between Russia and Turkey become more pressing by the moment, the Christian populations of the various Balkan states, who had hitherto lain dormant, harboring tears of sorrow and resentment under the despotic oppression of the Turkish government, have risen up all at once. Amidst the fierce momentum to overthrow the Turkish government in one fell swoop and recover their national independence, what can be considered the first shot of this anti-Turkish movement was a major riot that occurred in Sofia, the capital of Bulgaria, on the night of October 20th. Although detailed reports have not yet been received, a group of rioters centered around the country's National Party reportedly broke into the official residence of the Turkish Governor, carrying out a horrific massacre, and clashes between Turkish authorities and Bulgarian revolutionary party members are resulting in tragedies everywhere.

Shōya: Hee. Something terrible has happened. Bulgaria is Mr. Insarop’s home country, isn't it?

Shubin: That's right.

Shōya: Is it different from Turkey?

Shubin: It's Bulgaria within Turkey.

Anna: Then Mr. Insarop will go to war too, won't he?

Shubin: He might go. But don't worry. They won't come to attack Russia. <144> Bulgarians are people trying to defeat the Turks.

Shōya: Then a revolution will start now.

Shubin: Don't talk nonsense. Do you even know what a revolution is?

Shōya: Of course, I know. It's about overthrowing the Turkish government and recovering Bulgaria's independence, right? The land of my beloved ancestors has been trampled by the tyrannical Turkish bastards. Our poor compatriots are chased around like cattle and horses by the vicious Turkish bastards.... Everyone, rise up!— (Mimics Insarop.)

Uwal: Alright, hearts— (Shouts loudly.)

(Everyone bursts into laughter)

Anna: Ah, I'm quitting. There's no way I can beat Uncle. I feel sluggish. (Yawning, she throws down her Hwatu cards.) Ah, my head is starting to hurt again. (Lays her head down on the table.)

Uwal: Haa, haa (Blinks his eyes and wiggles his fingers.)

Anna: Is it still raining?

Shōya: It might even snow. It's gotten very cold.

Anna: Rain, then snow, October is almost gone already. The winter solstice, the twelfth month, the long winter has come again— Ah, my head. (Holds her head.)

Shubin: (Smoking a cigarette) Auntie, you seem to be feeling quite regretful.—

Uwal: (Twirls his fingers and makes a strange smiling face.)

Anna: (In a listless voice) Shōya, why don't you play something?

Shōya: Yes. (Sits down at the piano.) Shall I play Beethoven? Or something fitting for this mood?—

Anna: Play Beethoven. (Shōya plays the piano.)

Shubin: By the way. Uncle. Our house's General.

Shōya: (Looking back) He went out last night—

Shubin: You mean he hasn't come back. Hmm.— Right now, at that woman's house, 'My dear sir, my here (presses hand to heart) here feels like it's going to burst like this,' she must be acting cute, the worthless thing. That's why Auntie will have a headache her whole life. Getting involved with a woman devoid of any charm.— (Humming)
Not everything that burns is wood.<145>
Not everything that melts is oil.
I want to dissect this heart of mine
Which only ever burns and melts.—
(As he says this loudly and stands up to leave)

Stamhoup: (Enters wearing a Lieutenant's uniform and a large overcoat.) Hmm. What are you muttering about alone? (Looks around left and right.)

Shubin: Hah! Welcome back. (Everyone comes out to greet him.)

Stamhoup: (Frowning) What is this. Looking down on others. (Looks down sideways at everyone and paces the study like a tiger. The others stand lined up distractedly, watching.)

Anna: You're back now? (Lightly kisses the Lieutenant's hand and takes off his overcoat.)

Shubin: (Sits alone by the window, smoking with an expression like sneering at others.)

Stamhoup: Hmm, Karuda again. (Glares at Old Man Uwal) No dignity for your age. (The old man twirls his fingers.) Shōya, what's that? Touching the piano like some young lady. Go to the kitchen and help with the meal.
(Shōya shrugs and exits. Old Man Uwal follows behind her, swaying his hips.)

Anna: Bring some coffee. (Exits holding her husband's overcoat as if stealing it.)

Stamhoup: (Also pacing back and forth in the room) Shubin, just lazing around as usual.

Shubin: As you can see. However, the item you requested—

Stamhoup: Hmm, Augustina's bust is finished?

Shubin: It's almost finished.

Stamhoup: Almost finished? I knew it.

Shubin: No, really, it's almost done. Just that nose and cheek are a bit—

Stamhoup: What about them?

Shubin: Yes. They turned out a bit Greek style (크리이키 스타일). If possible, if the nose were about an inch higher—

Stamhoup: What did you say? If it were an inch higher than that, she'd look like a goblin (도깝이).

Shubin: (Intentionally in a low voice) Did you meet her last night?

Stamhoup: Who?

Shubin: Miss Augustina. This one. (Points to his heart.)

Stamhoup: (Casually) Far from meeting her, she hasn't even come yet.— October is already almost gone. Gradually <146> winter is coming, what on earth is she doing in Reual? (Shows some signs of heartbreak.)

Shubin: Maybe she's knitting socks for the winter preparations. Her own socks, that is. Right, probably not your socks.

Shubin: [Translator's note: This line seems misplaced and attributed to Shubin, but context suggests it's Stamhoup speaking.] Go ahead and babble as you please. But there's no woman like her. Noble character, honest nature, and on top of that, a woman without greed—

Shubin: But didn't she cash the promissory note (手形) she took from you sometime ago?

Stamhoup: (Intentionally in a loud voice) No, I've never seen such a greedless woman before. She's the kind of woman you wouldn't find easily even if you searched high and low.— Tell me if there is one anywhere— Just look at how she captivates me like this, like this— But forgetting me, who thinks of her like this, and not even sending a single letter is too much.

Shubin: Heh. You seem quite taken with her. By the way, I have an excellent, clever method. Would you like to try it?

Stamhoup: What is it?

Shubin: Really? When Augustina comes.—

Stamhoup: Yes, when she comes—

Shubin: When you meet her... Will you do exactly as I suggest?

Stamhoup: Of course, I will. I definitely will. How—

Shubin: Suddenly, take one Western cake (양떡), and smack her across the nose and cheek— And then we'll see what that wench does.—

Stamhoup: What did you say? You wicked fellow! I thought you really had some good idea,— just making fun of me. Hmm, it's my fault for taking seriously the words of a mere sculptor like you, a fellow with airs but no principles (主義).

Shubin: Heh, a fellow with no principles? Right, acting according to principles (主義) was the favorite phrase of Secretary Kŭllat'ŭwisŭk'i, whom you've become close to recently.— By the way, that master of principles, he took 100 rubles from you yesterday too. What kind of principle is that?

Stamhoup: You worry too much. Do you think someone like you could possibly understand the great venture he's undertaking now, aiming to make a fortune (一攫千金)?

Shubin: Anyway, whatever it is, it's certainly not a bad business method for an official (官人) who definitely doesn't accept bribes (賂物) like 100 rubles, is it?

Stamhoup: I don't want to hear it. Stop blabbering. Can someone like you truly understand the value (價値) of such a person? If he were such a figure (人物), I would definitely not be ashamed to choose him as a son-in-law (사위). Think about it. He has a noble disposition (貴人性), is clever (怜悧), has wide social connections (交際) in upper-class society (上流社會), and has influence (勢力) in the Prefectural Office (府廳)—

Shubin: What, at best, maybe the Director (長官) likes him enough to hold his nose and let him get away with things?

Stamhoup: Perhaps that might be the case. Even the Director has high hopes for him. Isn't that proof (證據) of how talented (才能) he is in practical affairs (實務) and knowledgeable about worldly matters?

Shubin: He's also good at Karuda.

Stamhoup: Of course, he is. At anything. If I were a woman, I'd marry a man like that. But our Elena— she's my daughter, alright, but I don't know how she turned out like this. I can't pin down what's wrong with her. One day she's suddenly jumping around like a freshly caught fish (生鮮), and the next day she's lethargic (沈滯) like a dead person. Can a sane person understand her? Just when you think she's withered away beyond recognition, suddenly she has a face full of plump amiability (和氣). She's utterly indescribable, like a madwoman you don't know what to do with.—
(A servant with unpleasant features brings a tray with coffee, cream, and sugar. Stamhoup finally sits down in a chair, adds cream and sugar to his coffee, and speaks while stirring with a spoon (砂匙).)
Basically, a son-in-law is for the parents (父母) to decide (定하기에). As long as the parents approve, it doesn't matter what the daughter (딸자식) thinks. There's no need to be restricted (拘碍할 것은 업는 것이다) by the immature feelings (感情) of a naive young child. Hmm, and yet, in the past (以前), we lived respectably. Now the world (世上) is turned upside down. Whatever criticisms (是非) others may have. (Takes a sip of coffee and starts his argument (辯論) again.) That's right. Nowadays, young women talk to any man they fancy, buy newly published books on their own, go wherever they please day or night without companions. No, it's utterly outrageous. So this is Paris style (巴里風) or Moscow style (모기風), they say. If you don't act like this, you're not considered an upper-class lady (上流 婦人) or a new-style lady (新式 婦人), they say. It's truly a damned world (亡한 놈의 世上)— Just two or three days ago, Elena disappeared. When asked where she went, they said she went out (出入하엿다고). When asked where she was going, nobody knew. What kind of business is this? Should she really behave (行實) like that? <148>

Shubin: (Smiling faintly, smoking his cigarette) Please finish your coffee quickly and send it away. You always used to say not to speak pointlessly (空然한 말을) in front of the servants. (Says this looking at the servant. The servant glares maliciously at Shubin without him noticing.)

Stamhoup: (Stirs his coffee with the spoon, finishes it. The servant takes the cup and exits.) Basically, even I, her so-called father (所謂 아비), don't know where or with what kind of men Elena associates (交際). Ugh, one I do know among them is that philosophy student built like a goblin's hind leg, right?

Shubin: Be quiet. Pelsen'ep is my close friend (親友). He might seem a bit clumsy, but he's a fine young man.

Stamhoup: I don't know how fine a man he is, but they all look the same to me. In the first place, you, who call yourself his close friend, are a prime example of a worthless fellow. I only tolerate you because you're Anna's nephew (甥姪), turning a blind eye.

Shubin: Thank you very much for the kind words.

Stamhoup: Still, at least one can grasp the true nature (本性) of that student somewhat. But besides him, that friend of his, that Montenegrin or Bulgarian or whatever, a foreign (外國) beggar ronin (거어지 浪人) whose origins (種子) are unknown— if anything happens from associating with such creatures, don't you know the name of this Stamhoup house will be ruined?

Shubin: Poor Insarop— that Bulgarian ronin, he's dying of tuberculosis (肺病).

Stamhoup: What, tuberculosis? You mean he's set to die from it? It's pitiful, but thankfully, he won't be coming to our house anymore. It's good. Having someone of unknown background (根本) visiting (出入) our house brings dishonor (不名譽) to the family. (Pacing) Basically, the one who allows such people into the house is your auntie— Anna is the mistaken one. She doesn't discuss anything with me, the head of the family (家長), and always tries to keep me in the dark (뒤ㅅ전에 둘랴고). Anna doesn't take me seriously, Elena doesn't, you don't. Looking at it, everyone in the house, man or woman, even Shōya the maid, doesn't treat me like the master (主人). Is there a welcome (迎接) when I enter, a farewell (餞送) when I leave? What is all this? When I enter the room, Anna is wrapping her head, looking like she's dying. Elena just turns her head away whenever she sees someone's face and goes off somewhere. Is that all? When I call a servant, do they come readily? I don't understand my place in this house. I, the master of the house, am treated exactly like <149> an old man in the back room (뒤ㅅ방 늙은이). Is this the Paris style family (巴里式 家庭)? Is this the modern style family (現代式 家庭)? It's truly laughable. (Takes large steps.)

Shubin: So that's why you can't stand not seeing the high-collar (하이칼라) face in Reual every evening. ... Poor Auntie treats you like an old man in the back room? Does she even have the energy for that? Please stop talking like that and think differently. In five or six days, it will be Auntie's birthday (生辰). It would be better to think about getting her some gift (物件). No matter how modest the gift you give, Auntie will like it very much.

Stamhoup: That's right. If you hadn't mentioned it, I would have forgotten. That's right, it might be modest, but I bought a jewel-encrusted box (寶石 박은 箱子) from the shop (廛) in P'erunŭst'ol a while ago. Should I give her that? Will she like it?

Shubin: That one? The one you bought to give to the high-collar lady in Reual, right?

Stamhoup: Hmm. Perhaps so.

Shubin: Haha, Auntie will probably like that one too. (Stands up.)

Stamhoup: Shubin, are you going there tonight?

Shubin: Well.— No, I won't go. I have work I must do tomorrow. Staying up all night is strictly forbidden (嚴禁). I'm not in a position to just play around like that. (Exits.)

Stamhoup: Tsk. (Clicks his tongue, then paces the room again. After a moment, he opens a desk drawer, takes out a velvet-covered box (氈으로 表裝한 箱子), wipes the outside with a silk handkerchief (비단 手巾), checks inside the box, takes out a standing mirror (다리 잇는 거울), and tidies his hair, turning his head left and right, striking various poses and combing his hair.) (The previously seen servant enters stealthily and gives a light cough.)

Stamhoup: (While fixing his hair) Who is it? (Turns around, startled.)

Servant: (With a cunning, sycophantic smile) Master.

Stamhoup: Yes. Is something wrong?

Servant: Master. I am in this house, am I not?—

Stamhoup: Who said you weren't? Well?

Servant: Master, I am distressed if you get angry. Haven't I grown up in this house since I was young? Because of that kindness (恩惠), there is just one thing I must tell you. It's nothing else but-<150>

Stamhoup: What is it, what?

Servant: Master. You said earlier you didn't know where the young lady went. I know exactly where she went.—

Stamhoup: Shut up! Like dog barking! What is it?

Servant: No, Master. It is distressing that you scold me so, but today, I definitely saw the young lady entering a certain house.

Stamhoup: Whaat? Where? Whose house?

Servant: It's near P'owalsŭk'i-chŏng (뽀왈스키町). I heard in detail about the status of the person renting the house from the old man who looks after the house.

Stamhoup: (Angrily kicks the table) I don't want to hear it! You idiotic fool! Spouting such words carelessly. The young lady is full of charitable spirit (慈善心). She might be visiting the house of needy people for comfort (慰問), don't you know— Get out— you fool!
(As the servant exits hesitantly, utterly crestfallen, Stamhoup follows him, grabs his wrist, and pulls him back).

Stamhoup: Stay there. You, you fool. What did that old man tell you? The name of the person living in that house—

Servant: Nothing, nothing at all. Just that he's a stu-, a student (學生)—

Stamhoup: (Shoves the servant's wrist away) I don't want to hear it, you idiotic fool! Listen here! If you dare speak even one word of this nonsense to anyone else, what do you think will happen?

Servant: Th-that couldn't happen.

Stamhoup: I d-don't want to hear it! You fool, if anyone else finds out even one word you just said, I'll deal with you myself. I'll make it so you can't even beg for food anywhere. Remember that. Understand? Hmm? You fool, that's enough, get out.
(The servant exits.)

Stamhoup: (Paces around even more frantically.) What is this? (Stops abruptly) What was that idiotic fool babbling about? Hmm, I must go see for myself if she's in that house. That's right. I have to see. Then what? It's already come to this. Even the servant... hmm. (Smacks his lips) What calamity (變) is this? What is happening? (Loudly) Even the servant knows? Is there such an outrageous misfortune (逢變)?— (In an angry voice) Anna, Anna! (Calls out, puts the mirror back in the drawer, and once more) Even the servant? What kind of outrageous misfortune is this?— Anna, Anna! (Calls.)
(Anna enters, with a wet compress (濕布) on her face, looking like a collapsing patient. Stamhoup clicks his tongue.)
What is it? Sick day and night. Have some sense of shame (廉恥). Anna, where did Elena go?
...(Act III Unfinished)...
To the reader... Stamhoup asks Anna where Elena went. What kind of conflict (衝突) will arise next... (In the next issue (次號))...<151>

**Summary**

Act III of Hyŏn Ch'ŏl's play "Overnight" unfolds in the study of the Stamhoup household amidst a dreary afternoon. While Anna, Old Man Uwal, and Shōya play cards, Shubin reads news of the escalating Russo-Turkish War and Bulgarian uprisings, sparking anxiety, particularly concerning the Bulgarian nationalist Insarop, who is connected to the family. The tense domestic atmosphere heightens with the return of the authoritarian patriarch, Stamhoup. He expresses disdain for his family members, criticizes his daughter Elena's mysterious behavior and associations (especially with Insarop and the student Pelsen'ep), and laments his perceived loss of authority. A servant reveals Elena's secret visit to a student's house, infuriating Stamhoup, who then confronts his ailing wife, Anna, about Elena's whereabouts as the act abruptly ends.

From a historical perspective, the play, written by a Korean author during the Japanese colonial period, is interesting for its choice of a European setting (Russia) and conflict (Russo-Turkish War, Balkan nationalism). This might reflect a way to explore themes of nationalism, foreign oppression (Turkish rule over Bulgaria), and resistance indirectly, perhaps resonating with Korean audiences experiencing colonial rule. The portrayal of the patriarchal Stamhoup's anxieties about modernity ('Paris style', 'New-style lady') and his daughter's independence also mirrors social changes and tensions of the era.

國境動員이라. 露西亞 皇帝는 國境守備軍團에 對하야 24時間 內로 動員을 行하기로 命令하얏다더라. 자아 이것이 큰일입니다.— 잘 들으시요. (읽는다) 소뷔아府 暴動과 불가리아 國民黨의 
崛起라. 露西亞와 土耳其 
間에 戰端 開始의 兆朕이 時時刻刻으로 急急함을 從하야 從來 土耳其 政府의 專制的 壓迫下에서 怨嗟의 悲淚를 含하고 蟄伏하얏던 빨칸 諸邦의 基督敎 國民은 一齊이

슈빈.  헤에, 主義도 업는 년석이요. 올치 主義를 쪼차서 
行動한다 하는 近者에 親하신 클나트위스키 書記官의 단골 文字이엇섯지요.— 그런데 그 主義의 先生님, 어저께도 100루불이나 돈을 당신에게 가저가더니. 그것은 무슨 主義인가요.
스담호웁.  걱정 퍽 하내. 그 사람은 只今 一攫千金할 큰일을 하고 잇는 것을 네까지 따위가 알기나 하겟니.
슈빈.  아무러턴지 무엇인지 모르지마는 100루불이라고 하는 돈을 決코 賂物로 받지 아니한다고 하는 官人에는 해롭지 아니한 商法이지요.
스담호웁.  듯기 실타. 떠들지 마라. 제까진 것이 정말＜147＞ 그런 사람의 價値를 알 수 잇나. 그런 人物이면 사위를 定해도 決코 부끄러움이 업다. 생각해보지. 귀인 性이 잇고 사람이 怜悧하고 또 上流社會에도 交際가 넓고 府廳에서도 勢力이 잇고—
슈빈.  무엇, 쭉해야 長官이 조하서 코를 쥐고 뺑손이나 칠는지.
스담호웁.  或 그럴는지도 모르지. 長官도 그 사람에게는 한목 보고 잇다. 그만큼 그 사람이 實務에 才能잇고, 세상일에 밝다는 證據가 아니냐.
슈빈.  가루다도 잘하고.
스담호웁.  그러코 말고, 무엇이던지 내가 만일 게집이 되엇스면 그런 사람을 남편 삼지. 그런데 우리 집 에레나라고 하는 것은— 내 딸 아닌 바는 아니나, 어떠케 생긴 것인지, 모를 일이야. 이것은 어떠타고 질정할 수가 업서. 오늘 별안간 갓 잡은 生鮮 모양으로 펄펄 뛰고 놀다가도, 내일이면 또 죽은 사람가티 沈滯해지고. 성한 사람이 그를 理가 잇나. 볼 수 업시 말라비틀어진 줄 생각하고 잇스면, 어느덧 북슬북슬한 和氣의 얼굴이고. 도모지 무어라고 말할 수 업는, 어쪌 줄 모르는 미치광이가태.—
(人相이 조치 못한 下人, 쟁반에다 珈琲茶와, 크림과, 모당을 담아가지고 왓다. 스담호웁, 비롯오 椅子에 걸터 안저 珈琲에 크림과 砂糖을 너코. 砂匙로 저으며서 말한다.)
大體 사위라고 하는 것은 父母가 定하기에 잇는 것으다. 父母의 마음에만 들면, 딸자식이야 어떠케 생각하던지. 철업는 어린 아이의 未熟한 感情에 拘碍할 것은 업는 것이다. 으응, 그리고도 以前에는 우리들이 훌륭이 살아왓다. 只今은 世上이 거꿀우 되엇다. 남이야 무어라고 是非를 하던지. (珈琲를 한 목음 마시고, 다시 辯論을 始作한다.) 그러치. 只今 世上에는 젊은 게집들이 제 마음대로 마음에 맛는 산아이만 보면 말을 부티고. 새로난 冊이라도 저 혼자 사보고. 가티 가는 사람도 업시 제 맥대로 밤중에 갈대 못 갈대 다 단이고. 아니 도모지가 망칙한 일이야. 자아 이것이 巴里風인지 모기風인지라나. 이러케 아니하면 上流 婦人이라 아니하고, 新式 婦人이라고 아니한다지 참 亡한 놈의 世上이지— 當場 요 2, 3日 前에도 에레나가 보이지 안케, 어대 갓나냐 무르니까, 밧게 出入하엿다고 하기에, 
어대로 간다더냐고 무르니까, 아모도 아는 사람이 업섯지. 이게 무슨 놈의 일이야. 그래도 그따위 行實이 잇서야 하겟나.＜148＞
슈빈.  (육장, 웃음을 입슐에 띄고 담배를 피우며서) 速히 잡수시고 그것을 보내시지요. 下人들 압헤서 空然한 말을 하지 말라고 당신이 늘 그러케 하셧지요. (라고 하고 下人을 보고. 下人은 心術구진 눈으로 슈빈 모르게 흘겨본다.)
스담호웁.  (珈琲茶를 砂匙로 저어, 다 마시고. 그릇은 下人이 들고 나간다.) 大體 에레나라 하는 것은 어대서 어떠한 산아이들과 交際를 하는지. 所謂 아비 되는 나도 모른다. 으
용 그 중에 내가 하나 아는 것은 第一 그 도깝이 
뒤ㅅ다리 가튼 哲學書生이지?
슈빈.  가만이 게셔요. 벨세넵은 나의 親友이입니다. 사람은 좀 어설푼 것 가트나 훌륭한 산아
이입니다.
스담호웁.  어떠케 얼마나 훌륭한 사람인지는 모르나 내 눈에는 다가티 보인다. 대관절 親友라고 하는 너부터 변변치 못한 놈의 代表다. 안나의 甥姪이니까 어쩔 수 업서 눈을 감고 두기는 하지마는.
슈빈.  大端이 고마운 말슴이올시다.
스담호웁.  그래도 그 書生은 얼마간이라도 本性을 알 수 잇지마는. 그자 말고 그자의 親舊라고 하는 몬데네크로인지 불가리아
ᅟᅵᆫ지 하는 어대 어떤 놈의 種子인지 모르는 外國의 거어지 浪人, 그런 물건을 가까이 하다가 萬一 무슨 일이 생기면 이 스답호웁의 집 이름이 亡하는 줄 모르니.
슈빈.  가엽시 인사롭은— 그 불가리아의 浪人은, 肺病으로 다 죽어간다오.
스답호웁.  무어, 肺病. 글로 죽게 되엇단 말이지. 불상은 하지마는 다행이 우리 집에 오지 안케 되겟스니 고맙다. 그런 根本도 모르는 사람이 우리 집에 出入을 해서는 집안이 不名譽이다. (단이며서) 大體 그런 사람은 집에 단이게 하는 것은 너이 아지머니가— 안나가 틀린 사람이다. 家長되는 내게는 한마듸 議論도 업고 무슽 일이던지 나를 
뒤ㅅ전에 둘랴고 하는 까닭이다. 안나가 나를 대수로이 아니 여기지, 에레나가 그러치 네놈이 그러치, 보자 보자 하니, 집 안에 산아이나 게집이나, 쇼오야의 下女까지도 主人가티 여기지 아니하지 들어오니 迎接하나 나가니 餞送을 하나 이게 다 무엇이야. 房 안에 들어오면 안나가 머리를 휩싸고 다 죽어가는 얼굴을 짓고 잇지 에레나는 남의 얼굴만 보면 고개를 뒤틀고 어대로 가버리지 그뿐인가 下人을 부르니 下人이 잘 오나. 내가 이 집에서 무엇인지 까닭을 모르겟다. 한 집에 主人인 내가 꼭＜149＞ 
뒤ㅅ방 늙은이의 
대접을 밧고 잇다. 이것이 巴里式 家庭이라고 하는 것인가. 이것이 現代式 家庭인가? 참 웃으운 일도 만타. (크게 거름을 것는다.)
슈빈.  그러니까 저녁마다 레우알의 하이칼라 얼굴을 아니 보고는 못 견대신단 말슴이지요.

뒤ㅅ방 늙은이가티

스담호웁.  참 그러타. 네가 그 말 아니하엿더면 이즐 번하엿다. 참 그러치, 내게는 변변치 못한 것이나 요 前에 뻬룬스톨의 廛에서 산 寶石 박은

슈빈.  그것이요. 레우알의 하이칼라 주랴고 사신 것이지요.
스담호웁.  흐응. 그럴는지도 모르지.
슈빈.  하하, 그것이면 아마 아지머니도 마음에 들겟지요. (일어선다.)
스담호웁.  슈빈, 너 오늘 저녁에 거긔 가니.
슈빈.  글세요.— 아니 안 가겟서요. 來日 일을 하지 안할 수 업스니까. 밤새우는 것은 嚴禁이올시다. 그러케 놀고 지낼 處地가 못 되니까요. (박그로 나간다.)
스담호웁.  쯔. (라고 혀를 차더니 또 室內로 돌아단인다. 조금 잇다가 冊床 설합을 얼더니 氈으로 表裝한 箱子를 끄어내더니 비단 手巾으로, 것을 훔친 뒤에 箱子 안을 調査해보다가 다리 잇는 거울을 끄어내여 머리를 손질하면서 고개를 외로 틀엇다가 오리로 틀엇다가 여러 가지로 모양내는 흉내를 하면서 빗질을 한다.) (前에 나왓던 下人이 몰래 오는 것가티 오더니 輕한 긔침을 한다.)
스담호웁.  (머리를 만지며서) 누구냐. (하며 깜짝 놀라며 돌아다본다.)
下人.  (狡猾한 媚笑를 먹음고) 영감마님.
스담호웁.  우에. 무슨 일이 잇니.

스담호웁.  누가 안잇다니, 그래.

스담호웁.  무어야, 무어.
下人.  令監마님은. 아까 아가씨가 어대로 가시는지 모른다고 하셧지요. 저는 가신 곳을 잘 압니다.—
스담호웁.  시끌어워, 개소리가티, 무어야.
下人.  아닙니다. 令監마님. 그러케 꾸중을 하셔서는 惶悚하오나, 오늘 小人이 아가씨가 어떤 집으로 들어가시는 것을 꼭 보앗습니다.
스달호웁.  머어, 어대서. 누구 집이야.
下人. 뽀왈스키町의, 가까운 곳이올시다. 그 집을 빌어 든 사람의 身分까지 그 집 보는 늙은이에게 仔細히 들엇습니다.
