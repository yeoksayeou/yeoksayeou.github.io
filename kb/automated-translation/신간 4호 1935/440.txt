**Body Text Translation:**
A Soybean Patch Where Gold is Dug
						Kim Yu-jŏng

Deep beneath the earth, it is always gloomy.
The weary lamp flame flickers weakly, tinged with blue. Unlike at night, it seemed dimmer during the day.
A narrow pit, tightly enclosed on all sides by rough walls of yellow earth. It felt as oppressive as the inside of a tomb. A chilly silence. Only the stuffy smell of earth and a horrid dampness filled the space.
The pickaxe bites into the earth. Striking down fiercely,
Thud, thud, thud—
Only this dull sound, but occasionally the wall crumbles with a rustle.
Yŏng-sik stops working and pulls up his sleeve to wipe the sweat from his face. He was fed up, wondering when this vein would ever be found. He picks up a handful of dirt, brings it close to his nose, and examines it minutely with his fingers. It certainly seemed like the waste rock (버력) had changed a bit. <51> But the useless waste rock wasn't entirely gone yet. They said gold comes with *maldong* waste rock (말똥버력 - lit. horse dung waste rock), so why wasn't it appearing?
He picks up the pickaxe again. Kneeling on the ground, buttocks raised high, he pants heavily. The pickaxe strikes down relentlessly. Water seeps from the bottom, soaking his knees thoroughly. Clods of earth fall from the ceiling above, rolling down the nape of his neck. Sometimes, a chunk of the upper wall breaks off, hitting his back with a thud and shattering.
But he doesn't even blink. He had ruined a whole soybean patch trying to dig for gold. Furious, he threw himself into it, life or death, like a man possessed. He spits on his palms, readjusts his grip on the pickaxe handle, and doesn't know when to rest.
Behind him, the sound of scraping earth goes *dŭŭk, dŭŭk*. It seems he hasn't cleared all the waste rock yet. Is this fellow working or just pretending? While he himself was burning up inside, how could this guy be so thick-skinned?
Yŏng-sik turns his head, his gaze sharp with anger. He glares at Su-jae without a word. Only then does Su-jae sluggishly fill the carrying frame (바지게) with earth, hoist it onto his back, and climb the ladder.
The pit frame seems unstable, the wall trembles. Earth crumbles down. In the past, he would have been terrified of being buried alive here without even getting married, his hair standing on end. But now, he almost wished for it. If he could be buried in an earthslide with that fellow Su-jae and die together, that would actually be better.
He hated him that intensely.
This fellow, swayed by talk, had ruined a precious soybean patch. Not only that, everything was a disaster. He couldn't even weed the three rice paddies. The grass on the paddy ridges grew tall and lay tangled. Knowing this, the landlord (지주) flew into a rage. He stamped his feet, saying not to even think about farming from next year. No matter how much they dug, there was no end to it. It must be well over five *kil* (길 - approx. fathoms) deep by now. He hesitated, wondering whether to dig deeper or push northward. He was a novice at gold prospecting (금점). Until now, he had worked under Su-jae's direction, and to find gold, <52> he would have to continue doing so. But he wouldn't do such a detestable thing.
"Come here and dig this."
He issued the command with forced authority. Then he stood up, dusted off his hands, and stepped back.
Su-jae obeyed without complaint. As instructed, he knelt, scraped away the waste rock stuck to the wall, and then started digging again. Yŏng-sik shouldered the remaining waste rock. Staggering under the large carrying frame (걸때), he climbed the ladder. As he emerged from the pit entrance (굿문) and was about to dump the earth onto the waste pile,
"Why are you digging again? Are you crazy!"
He ran straight into the landlord's agent (마름) coming down from the mountain. His mind went blank, and he just stood there dumbfounded. What kind of tyranny would he have to endure today?
"I told you not to, so why are you digging again?" the agent snapped, poking the back of Yŏng-sik's carrying frame sharply with his cane. "This field is for farming, not for digging holes! You crazy fools, what gold do you think you'll find in a soybean patch, doing this nonsense?" he shouted, veins popping in his neck. If the field was ruined, it would be his fault for mismanagement. He came every day, raised hell, and forbade them, but the next day they would be digging again.
"You'd better fill this hole back in today. Know that you'll go straight to jail tomorrow!"
He was so overcome with emotion that his words stumbled out. His fist trembled by his side as if about to strike.
"We'll just try for today and then stop,"
Yŏng-sik managed to say, his face flushing red. And he pleaded desperately.
The agent ignored him and walked away.
Yŏng-sik stared blankly at his retreating figure. Then, looking at the face of the soybean patch, he felt utterly devastated. Proper field <53> now had holes punched everywhere.
Waste rock was piled up in heaps. It looked desolate and rather eerie, like a communal cemetery after a landslide. The soybean plants, which had been growing so well, were mostly buried under the waste piles, with only a few survivors here and there nodding their heads. Seeing that sight was worse than watching his own child die; it was an unbearable scene.
He would lose all his farmland. But how on earth was he going to pay this year's rent (도지) for the field, two and a half *sŏm* (섬 - approx. 5 bushels each) of hemp cloth? On top of that, since he ruined the field, he might even go to jail.
When Yŏng-sik returned to the pit, his companion was sitting on the ground, resting. He was calmly puffing on a cigarette, utterly unconcerned.
"When are we going to hit the vein?"
"It should start showing up soon."
"Showing up soon," Yŏng-sik scoffed, sneering. After a short pause,
"You bastard!"
He grabbed a clod of earth and struck him on the head.
Su-jae cried "Ouch!" and collapsed forward. Then he scrambled back up. Grabbing the nearest pickaxe, he immediately charged. But their strength was unequal. Pushed back by a brawny arm, he crashed against the wall with a thud. In that instant, he saw the pickaxe he had lost flying towards his forehead. He jerked his head aside. The pickaxe struck the earthen wall with a thud and bounced off.

Just hearing Su-jae's name made Yŏng-sik's teeth grind. He had clearly been completely fooled.
Yŏng-sik originally had no experience with gold prospecting. Nor any interest. He just hunched over in the field furrows, sweating, <54> diligently working away. This year, the soybeans had unexpectedly yielded well, and he felt somewhat relieved.
One day, while he was weeding alone,
"Hey there, isn't it hot? Take a break."
He looked up; it was Su-jae. He didn't farm but just wandered around prospecting; what brought him here now, grinning like that? Thinking maybe he'd struck it rich, Yŏng-sik asked,
"Made a lot of money? Treat me to something."
"Of course, I did! Ate my fill, spent my fill."
His face flushed from alcohol, he boasted grandly. Then he squatted at the edge of the field and, after chatting idly for a while, said,
"Don't you want to make some money? There's gold buried in this field, gold..."
"What?" Yŏng-sik asked.
Just over this mountain, in the big valley (큰골), there was a mine. A real bonanza (노다지) employing over three hundred miners, producing more than seventy *nyang* (냥 - traditional unit of weight, about 37.5g) of gold daily. That's over seven thousand *wŏn* (원). The main vein (줄맥) supposedly ran through the mountainside and extended right into this soybean patch. If the two of them dug, they'd hit the vein in just ten days and could expect to extract at least three *don* (돈 - 1/10th of a *nyang*) a day. Even just thirty *wŏn*, how much was that? Enough to buy half a cow, wasn't it?
But Yŏng-sik didn't listen intently. Gold prospecting was like jumping with a knife in your mouth. If it went well, fine, but if not, you'd ruin your life. He had heard such things before.
The next day, Su-jae came again, trying to persuade him, then left.
The third time, he came to the house, holding a bottle of makgeolli (막걸리 - rice wine). He had come again because he was eager. Sitting on the earthen floor veranda (봉당), <55> he stared at the dinner table, rambling on about how pollack soup nourished the body, how laborers needed to eat well, how others were buying rice paddies and fields while Yŏng-sik was just living like this—was he going to quit like this? he chattered incessantly.
"Auntie (아즈머니), please let us have some of this."
He finally offered the wine bottle to Yŏng-sik's wife.
They sat around the dinner table, drinking cheerfully. After a few cups, Yŏng-sik's mind began to change. Rather than suffering for a whole year just to get a few meager *sŏm* of soybeans, digging for gold seemed wiser. If he could just strike it rich in one day, the profit would far exceed the harvest from a year's hard work. He was constantly hounded because of the seven *wŏn* debt from fertilizer costs and labor wages incurred last spring. If he was going to live so wretchedly, then as a man, he might as well try his luck, win or lose.
"Let's start digging tomorrow. If we just had money, who cares about soybeans like these?"
When Su-jae eagerly pressed him, he readily agreed.
"Let's do it. Damn it all, if it doesn't work out, we just stop."
However, if his wife, who was drinking porridge behind him, hadn't nudged him sharply in the ribs, he might have hesitated a bit longer.
The wife had her own quick calculations.
These days, gold prospecting was all the rage. If you just farmed foolishly, you'd end up nothing more than a beggar. Soon enough, mountains, paddies, and fields would all be riddled with holes by gold diggers, turned upside down, a complete mess. What would they eat then? Look! Farmhands, as if by agreement, would suddenly abandon their work and run off to the gold mines. The village was buzzing with talk about not being able to farm this year due to the lack of workers. And tenant farmers, following suit, threw down their hoes and ran off to the riverbanks <56> and streams to pan for placer gold (사금). Then, a few days later, weren't they strutting around in *tabi* socks (다비신) and fine cotton clothes (옥당목), showing off (히짜를 뽑는)?
The wife had never dreamed that gold could come from their soybean patch. She was surprised and also delighted. Just thinking, "This year, I'll finally get to eat my fill of that dried pollack (코다리) I've only been drooling over," made her insides tingle as if tickled. She was terribly envious of Yanggŭn-daek (양근댁) from the house behind, who walked gracefully in the white rubber shoes her husband bought her thanks to gold prospecting. If only gold would pour out for them too, she would also wear white rubber shoes and put powder on her face.
"Let's give it a try. If we just do as that gentleman says, surely it will turn out well—"
She had encouraged her hesitant husband like this.

At the crack of dawn, they gathered at the soybean patch.
Su-jae muttered this way and that, as if chanting an incantation. He bustled about, coming here and going there. He was trying to estimate the path of the vein lying underground.
After wandering the field for a while, he stopped at a corner near the mountain and explained, pointing with his finger. A major vein always follows the mountain. Since this vein was a bonanza, it must surely lie slanted in this direction. So, they should start digging from here.
Yŏng-sik didn't grasp the meaning of his words. But since Su-jae was supposedly skilled in gold prospecting, he just firmly believed that if he did as Su-jae said, gold nuggets would undoubtedly appear. Without a word, he plunged his shovel into the indicated spot and began digging.
Gold was gold, but the soybeans painstakingly cultivated were also soybeans. The nearly grown, healthy-looking plants were crushed by the shovel blade and buried in the earth. Seeing this hurt him deeply. When poignant thoughts surged, he would occasionally put down the shovel, bend over, and brush the dirt off the soybean leaves (콩닢). <57>
"Hey, man, why bother with that? Let's dig for gold!"
"No, my back just hurts a bit—"
He felt a little embarrassed after being scolded. Indeed, if gold just gushed out, who cared about a soybean patch like this? He could even turn this field into a rice paddy. Closing his eyes, he flung the dirt from his shovel haphazardly onto the soybean leaves.

"Just farm the damn land, what kind of nonsense is this—"
An old man from the village frequently came by, spewing harsh words.
They had already dug three holes in the field. And were continuously digging. Was it gold or a curse? Because of it, farming was abandoned. This must surely be a sign that the world was ending. Digging holes in such precious fields, this madness—would those fellows remain unscathed?
The old man, in his own fury, couldn't help but gesture threateningly with his cane.
"You'll be struck by lightning, struck by lightning—"
"Don't worry. Who's going to know?"
Yŏng-sik retorted bluntly each time. In his anger, he threw dirt carelessly, spat sharply, and entered the pit. But a corner of his heart always felt uneasy (끈-하엿다). He had turned the soybean patch upside down searching for the vein. And it was still completely uncertain when the vein would appear. He couldn't weed the paddies, couldn't check the water; he didn't even know how the rice was doing. At night, sleep wouldn't come, and he lay awake tormented.
Su-jae showed no sign of discouragement, always the same. Crouched on the ground, he idly dug, humming to himself. <58>
Can't we find even a single speck of gold after all this? Eh, eh, what useless men. Unconsciously, shallow sighs escaped him twice in succession.
Late at night, the two men went out to make rice cakes (떡). The husband pounded the rice in a mortar, *thump thump*. But they had no sieve. The wife's legs were burning from running around the village borrowing one.
"Why are you just sitting there? Light the fire!"
The wife found her husband, who was spacing out while steaming the rice cakes, infuriating. While she was worrying herself sick, what was he thinking, just sitting there? She sharply chopped kindling with a sickle, threw it to him, and implicitly urged him on.
The rice cakes were finally done after the rooster had crowed twice.
The wife balanced the steamer (시루) on her head, and the husband tucked a straw mat under his arm. They climbed the dark mountain path.
After climbing the slope for some distance, they reached the soybean patch. It was a place enclosed by a towering black mountain in front. Along the edges, zelkova and jujube trees spread their branches like dishevelled hair.
Just before reaching the edge of the field, the husband stopped and looked back at his wife.
"Wait here. And stand still—"
Taking the steamer and holding it in one arm, he climbed alone into the soybean patch. Just as he was about to turn past the piles of earth heaped in front, he must have tripped on a stone. He staggered, about to fall, and his wife, terrified, rushed up and supported him.
"You wretched woman, why did you come up here? You'll bring bad luck!"
As soon as the husband regained his balance, he shouted and slapped his wife across the face. Things were bad enough already, and this ill-omened woman... He grumbled discontentedly as he entered the field. <61>
He spread the mat in the middle of the field and placed the steamer on it. Then, facing the steamer, he bowed deeply, respectfully and sincerely.
"Please save us. If the mountain spirit (산신) does not help us, we have no choice but to die,"
he prayed, hands clasped together.
Watching this scene, the wife's anger flared up. Claiming to prospect for gold, yet unable to find a single speck, his bad habits were only getting worse. He never used to, but recently he had developed the nasty habit of hitting her frequently. Did he mean to dig for gold or slap her face? For goodness sake, she hoped that damned gold wouldn't appear. Resentful from the slap, she rebelled with all her might.
Indeed, things turned out just as the wife wished. More than ten days passed, but the mountain spirit remained silent. Her husband spent day and night buried in the pit, eyes wide with desperation. On the rare occasions he came down to the house, his face was haggard, his shoulders slumped; he looked almost like an invalid. Then he would silently throw his large frame onto the heated floor (방고래) with a thud.
"Damn it all, I wish I could just die—"
He would sometimes sigh like this.

The wife set out from the house, carrying lunch in a gourd dipper (밖아지). The nursing baby on her back gurgled happily, drumming its feet.
Now, thoughts of white rubber shoes and dried pollack were tiresome. Just hearing the word 'gold' made her mouth taste sour. Setting that aside, if only they weren't hounded for the food they had borrowed and eaten, that would be good enough.
Autumn descended yellowly upon the paddies and fields. Farmers, faces joyful, exchanged cheerful jokes when they met. But her husband had only ruined the precious field and failed even to manage (건살) the paddies; what would he harvest this autumn, what joy could he find? Ashamed of the villagers' <62> gazes, she took the mountain path.
Emerging from the pine forest, she looked out into the distance and saw both men were outside the pit. They must have fought again today. One sat on a dirt pile here, the other on a pile there, facing away from each other, silently puffing on cigarettes.
"Come have lunch."
She placed the gourd dipper down in front of her husband and subtly checked his condition.
Her husband's shirt was torn, and his face was scratched. He sat silently, arms crossed, gazing at the distant mountains. Su-jae looked like he had been buried in dirt and just crawled out; not just his face, but even inside his ears were full of soil. Dried blood crusted under his nose, and fresh blood still trickled down slightly. Seeing Yŏng-sik's wife, he seemed embarrassed. He turned his head away sideways, clicking his tongue.
Were they supposed to dig gold or just draw blood day after day? Hounded by debt, her insides were churning, yet what luxury allowed them this nonsense? The wife, displeased, narrowed her eyes.
"When are we going to repay the money borrowed for the mountain offering (산제)?"
She directed the sharp end of her words towards her sullen husband. But the husband didn't even lift an eyebrow. This time, raising her voice slightly,
"Why did you tell me to borrow it if you couldn't repay it?" she demanded, almost shouting.
These words rekindled her husband's barely subsided anger. He jumped up, clenched his fist like a ripe chestnut, and struck his wife hard enough on the head to make her see stars.
"Impudent woman—"
She feared nothing else, but his fists were terrifying. If she foolishly resisted, her skull would crack. Suppressing her resentment (암상), trembling, <63> the wife finally untied the baby carrier from her back. She shoved the baby towards her husband; the child let out a choked cry.
Then the wife turned away and muttered to herself,
"Imagine a simpleton (숭맥) trying to dig gold from a soybean patch," mocking him pointedly.
"What did you say, woman?" The husband instantly lunged at her and landed another solid punch on her cheek. Even if just a little, she was his wife, he should console her, but she only provoked his anger. Damn it all, it's sink or swim now.
"I won't live with you. Leave today!"
He shoved his wife violently, knocking her onto the paddy ridge, and kicked her hard in the side. The wife gasped, her mouth wide open.
"When was it you nudged my ribs, telling me to do it? You woman who will ruin this family!"
And he kicked her again, hard. Then again, hard.
Seeing this, Su-jae grew anxious. He sensed that Yŏng-sik's fury might soon turn back towards him. If he got caught now, he'd be killed. He edged away nervously and, at some point, vanished stealthily into the pit.
The sun radiates the warm fragrance of autumn. The soybeans, ownerless, roll their heavy pods onto the earth. From the foot of the opposite mountain comes the joyful song of farmers harvesting rice.
"It's hit! It's hit!"
Su-jae bursts out of the pit entrance, eyes wide, shouting. A handful of earth was clutched tightly in his hand.
"What?" Yŏng-sik gasped, then,
"Found the gold vein! The gold vein!" "Yes?" leaving behind a single cry, Yŏng-sik darted towards Su-jae. Frantically, he took the earth and sifted through it <64>—it was reddish-yellow soil, unlike anything he had seen before. Tears welled up in his eyes.
"Is this the main vein?"
"Yes, this is the *kopsaek* vein (곱색줄). You'll easily get about six *don* per sack (포대)."
Yŏng-sik felt choked up, more than joyful. Should he laugh or cry? He just stood there, mouth half-open, staring blankly at Su-jae's face.
"Come here, look. He says this is gold."
Finally, the husband called his wife. And, "What did I tell you? I told you to try it," he said, finding his wife, who approached sheepishly, suddenly lovely. He wiped away her tears with his thumb and then, leaping about, entered the pit.
"Is there gold in that dirt?"
As Yŏng-sik's wife, overjoyed, began imagining dried pollack and a house as big as a whale's back,
Su-jae replied breezily,
"Yes, about fifty *wŏn* per sack!" — and thought to himself that tonight, he absolutely, definitely must run away. Lies don't last long. It was best to disappear completely before his true colors (뽕) were revealed and he couldn't even save his bones (뼉따구). -(End)- <23>

**Summary**

Set in rural Korea during the 1930s, the story depicts the devastating impact of "gold fever" on a poor farming couple, Yŏng-sik and his wife. Persuaded by his friend Su-jae with promises of quick riches from a supposed gold vein under their soybean patch, Yŏng-sik abandons his farming duties, neglects his crops, incurs debt, and ruins his field. His obsession leads to conflict with the landlord's agent, village elders, and escalating violence towards his initially supportive wife. The story culminates with Su-jae falsely announcing the discovery of gold, planning to flee after deceiving Yŏng-sik one last time, leaving the couple facing ruin.

From a colonial history perspective, the story vividly illustrates the socio-economic desperation and disruption caused by the gold rush phenomenon in 1930s Korea. It highlights how rumors of easy wealth (potentially fueled or exploited by external factors) could lure farmers away from sustainable agriculture, leading to land destruction, debt, social disintegration, and heightened interpersonal conflict within impoverished rural communities. The narrative implicitly critiques the speculative frenzy and false hopes that preyed on the hardships of the peasantry during this period.

땅속 저 밑은 늘 음침하다.
고달픈 간드렛 불. 맥없이 푸르끼하다. 밤과 달라서 낮엔 되우 흐릿하였다.
거츠로 황토장벽으로 앞뒤 좌우가 콕 막힌 좁직안 구뎅이. 흡사히 무덤속같이 귀중중하다. 싸늘한 침묵. 쿠더브레한 흙내와 징그러운 냉기만이 그 속에 자욱하다.
고깽이는 뻔찔 흙을 이르집는다. 암팡스러히 나려쪼며
퍽 퍽 퍽-
이렇게 메떠러진 소리뿐 그러나 간간 우수수하고 벽이 헐린다.
영식이는 일손을 놓고 소맷자락을 끌어당기어 얼골의 땀을 흘는다. 이놈의 줄이 언제나 잡힐는지 기가 찼다. 흙 한 줌을 집어 코밑에 바짝 드려대고 손가락으로 삿삿이 뒤저본다. 완연히 버력은 좀 변한 듯 싶다. ＜51＞ 그러나 불통 버력이 아주 다 풀린 것도 아니엇다. 말똥버력이라야 금이 온다는데 왜 이리 안나오는지.

「왜 이리 앉엇수 불 좀 지피지」
떡을 찌다가 얼이 빠저서 멍허니 앉엇는 남편이 밉쌀스럽다. 남은 이래저래 애를 죄는데 저건 무슨 생각을 하고 저리 있는건지. 낫으로 삭정이를 탁탁 죠겨서 던저주며 안해는 은근히 훅닥이엇다.
닭이 두홰를 치고 나서야 떡은 되엇다.
안해는 시루를 이고 남편은 겨드랑에 자리때기를 꼇다. 그리고 캄캄한 산길을 올라간다.
비탈길을 얼마 올라가서야 콩밭은 놓엿다. 전면을 우뚝한 검은 산에 둘리어 막힌 곳이엇다. 가생이로 느티 대추나무들은 머리를 풀엇다.
밭머리 조꼼 못미처 남편은 거름을 멈추자 뒤의 안해를 도라본다.
「인내. 그러구 여기 가만히 섯서-」
실루를 받아 한 팔로 껴안고 그는 혼자서 콩밭으로 올라섯다. 앞에 쌓인 것이 모두가 흙덤이 그 흙덤이를 마악 돌아슬랴할제 아마 돌을 찻나보다. 몸이 씨러질랴고 우찔근하니 안해는 기급을 하야 뛰여 오르며 그를 부축하엿다.
「부정타라구 왜 올라와 요망맞은 년」
남편은 몸을 고루잡자 소리를 뻑 지르며 안해를 얼뺨을 부친다. 가뜩이나 죽으라 죽으라 하는데 불길하게도 게집년이. 그는 마뜩지 않게 두덜거리며 밭으로 들어간다. ＜61＞
밭한가운데다 자리를 퍼고 그 우에 시루를 놓앗다. 그리고 시루앞에다 공손하고 정성스리 재배를 커다랗게 한다.
「우리를 살려줍시사. 산신께서 거드러주지 않으면 저히는 죽을 밖에 꼼짝수 없읍니다유」
그는 손을 모디고 이렇게 축원하엿다.
안해는 이 꼴을 바라보며 독이 뾰록같이 올랏다. 금점을 함네하고 금 한톨 못캐는 것이 버릇만 점점 굴러간다. 그 전에는 없드니 요새로 건뜻하면 탕탕 때리는 못된 버릇이 생긴 것이다. 금을 캐랫지 뺨을 치랫나. 제발 덕분에 고놈의 금 좀 나오지 말엇으면. 그는 뺨맞은 앙심으로 망껏 방자하엿다.
하긴 안해의 말 고대루 되엇다. 열흘이 썩 넘어도 산신은 깡깜 무소식이엇다. 남편은 밤낮으로 눈을 까뒤집고 구뎅이에 묻혀 있엇다. 어쩌다 집엘 나려오는 때이면 얼골이 헐떡하고 어깨가 축 느려지고 거반 병객이엇다. 그리고서 잠잣고 커단 몸집을 방고래에다 퀑하고 내던지고 하는 것이다.
「제이미 붙을, 죽어나 버렷으면-」
혹은 이렇게 탄식하기도 하엿다.

안해는 밖아지에 점심을 이고서 집을 나섯다. 젖먹이는 등을 두다리며 좋다고 끽끽 어린다.
인젠 힌 고무신이고 코다리고 생각좇아 물렷다. 그리고 금 하는 소리만 드러도 입에 신물이 날만큼 되엇다. 그건 고사하고 꿔 다 먹은 양식에 졸리지나 말엇으면 그만도 좋으리마는.
가을은 논으로 밭으로 누-렇게 나리엇다. 농군들은 기꺼운 낯을 하고 서루 만나면 흥겨운 농담. 그러나 남편은 앵한 밭만 망치고 논좇아 건살 못하얏으니 이 가을에는 뭘 걷어드리고 뭘 즐겨할는지. 그는 동리 사람의 ＜62＞ 이목이 부끄러워 산 길로 돌앗다.
솔숲을 나서서 멀리 밖에를 바라보니 둘이 다 나와 잇다. 오늘도 또 싸운 모양. 하나는 이쪽 흙뎀이에 앉엇고 하나는 저쪽에 앉엇고 서루들 외면하야 담배만 뻑뻑 피운다.
「점심들 잡숫게유」
남편앞에 박아지를 나려놓으며 가만히 맥을 보앗다.
남편은 적삼이 찟어지고 얼골에 생채기를 내엇다. 그리고 두 팔을 것고 먼 산을 향하야 묵묵히 앉엇다. 수재는 흙에 밖혓다 나왓는지 얼골은 커녕 귓속드리 흙투성이다. 코밑에는 피딱지가 말라 붙엇고 아즉도 조곰식 피가 흘러나린다. 영식이 처를 보드니 열적은 모양. 고개를 돌리어 모로 떨어치며 입맛만 쩍쩍 다신다.
금을 캐라닌까 밤낮 피만 내다 말라는가. 빗에 졸리어 남은 속을 복는데 무슨 호강에 이  지랄 들인구. 안해는 못마땅하야 눈가에 살을 모앗다.
「산제 지난다구 꿔온 것은 은제나 갚는다지유-」
뚱하고 있는 남편을 향하야 말끝을 꼬부린다. 그러나 남편은 눈섭하나 까딱하지 않는다. 이번에는 어조를 좀 돋으며
「갚지도 못할 걸 왜 꿔 오라햇지유」하고 얼주 호령이엇다.
이 말은 남편의 채 가라앉지도 못한 분통을 다시 건디린다. 그는 벌떡 일어스며 황밤주먹을 쥐어 창낭할 만치 안해의 골통을 후렷다.
「게집년이 방정맞게-」
다른 것은 모르나 주먹에는 아찔이엇다. 멋없이 덤비다간 골통이 부서진다. 암상을 참고 바르르하다가 이윽고 ＜63＞ 안해는 등에 업은 언내를 끌러들엇다. 남편에게로 그대로 밀어던지니 아이는 까르륵하고 숨모는 소리를 친다.
그리고 안해는 돌아서서 혼잣말로
「콩밭에서 금을 딴다는 숭맥도 있담」하고 빗대놓고 비양거린다.
「이년아 뭐」 남편은 대뜸 달겨들며 그 볼치에다 다시 올찬 황밤을 주엇다. 적으나면 계집이니 위로도 하야주련만 요건 분만 폭폭 질러노려나. 예이 빌어먹을거 이판새판이다.
「너허구 안산다. 오늘루 가거라」
안해를 와락 떠다밀어 논뚝에 제켜놓고 그 허구리를 발길로 퍽 질럿다. 안해는 입을 헉하고 벌린다.
「네가 허라구 옆구리를 쿡쿡 찌를 제는 은재냐 요 집안 망할 년」
그리고 다시 퍽 질럿다. 연하야 또 퍽.
이 꼴들을 보니 수재는 조바심이 일엇다. 저러다가 그 분풀이가 다시 제게로 슬그머니 옳며올것을 지르채엇다. 인제 걸리면 죽는다. 그는 비슬비슬하다 어느 틈엔가 구뎅이 속으로 시납으로 없어저 버린다.
볕은 다스로운 가을 향취를 풍긴다. 주인을 잃고 콩은 무거운 열매를 둥글둥글 흙에 굴린다. 맞은 쪽 산밑에서 벼들을 비이며 기뻐하는 농군의 노래.
「터젓네, 터저」
수재는 눈이 휘둥그렇게 굿문을 튀어나오며 소리를 친다. 손에는 흙 한줌이 잔뜩 쥐엇다.
「뭐」하다가
「금줄 잡앗서 금줄 」 「응」하고 외마디를 뒤남기자 영식이는 수재 앞으로 살같이 달려드럿다. 헝겁지겁 그 흙을 받아 들고 샃샃이 헤처보니 ＜64＞ 따는 재래에 보지 못하든 붉으죽죽한 황토이엇다. 그는 눈에 눈물이 핑돌며
「이게 원 줄인가」
「그럼 이것이 곱색줄이라네. 한포에 댓돈식은 넉넉 잡히되」
영식이는 기뿜보다 먼저 기가 탁 막혓다. 웃어야 옳을지 울어야 옳을지. 다만 입을 반쯤 벌린 채 수재의 얼골만 멍하니 바라본다.
「이리와 봐. 이게 금이래」
이윽고 남편은 안해를 부른다. 그리고 내 뭐랫서. 그러게 해 보라구 그랫지하고 설면 설면 덤벼오는 안해가 항결 어여뻣다. 그는 엄지가락으로 안해의 눈물을 지워주고 그리고 나서 껑충거리며 구뎅이로 들어간다.
「그 흙속에 금이 있지요」
영식이 처가 너머 기뻐서 코다리에 고래등같은 집까지 연상할 제
수재는 시원스러히
「네 한포대에 오십원식 나와유-」하고 대답하고 오늘밤에는 꼭 정연코 꼭 다라나리라 생각하엿다. 거즛말이란 오래 못간다. 뽕이 나서 뼉따구도 못 추리기 전에 훨훨 벗어나는 게 상책이겟다. -(끝)- ＜23＞
