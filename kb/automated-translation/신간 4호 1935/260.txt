One Hundred People, One Hundred Stories
One Hundred People, One Hundred Stories

Mathematician Mr. Yi Myŏngch'il recently went to Onyang Hot Springs to attend the Standard Language Assessment Committee meeting hosted by the Korean Language Society. He took off his overcoat at the bathhouse, forgot it, and returned home. Upon remembering, he sent a letter to the bathhouse, and fortunately, they had kept it safe and sent it back by mail. It seems that although he is good at mathematics, he wasn't so good at keeping count of his own clothes.

★

Since Mr. Yŏ Unhyŏng cannot drink alcohol, when he goes to the provinces for lectures, he is often urged to drink cider instead. Before the New Year, while touring the Hwanghae and P'yŏngan provinces [Hwangp'yŏng Yangsŏ ■ region], he drank so much cider that he got diarrhea. When he burped, the smell of cider gushed up from inside, causing him much suffering. Because of this, afterwards, even just passing by a shop and seeing cider bottles gives him a headache.

★

While Hong Mokch'un is famous for having a lot of chest hair, Mr. Hong ■shik also has an unusually hairy chest. Is there some fateful connection (有■因緣) between the Hong family and chest hair (胸毛)?

★

Mr. Chŏng Inbo's "The Spirit of Korea Through Five Thousand Years," published in the *Tonga Daily* (*Tonga chi*), is admirable for the depth of its research and the grandeur of its prose. However, it's regrettable that there's a critique suggesting it has only three readers: the first reader is the author himself, the second reader is the ■ book ■ censor, and the third reader is the newspaper's ■ proofreader. What a pity, what a lament! Couldn't it be written a bit more ■■-ly?

★

Ms. Ch'oe Ŭnhŭi, at some point, secretly gave birth to another son and reportedly held the 100-day celebration on January 30th. Although unlucky in husbands, she seems very blessed with children!

★

Former Provincial Governor Kim Yunjŏng, upon the arrival of Police Affairs Bureau Chief Ikeda, went out with his entire family to greet him. As he presented name cards one by one, Mr. Ikeda looked at them and remarked, "Are there really that many Kims in Korea? We don't have the surname Ikeda [among Koreans]." Kim, the typical socialite (交際客), replied, "Nee— [Yes—] there are none. However, I will change my surname to Ikeda from now on." It's said they shared a laugh over this. Now that there's talk of Mr. Ikeda being transferred, what will Mr. Kim change his surname to next?

★

Mr. ■ Kwangnyŏl always keeps his hands in his trouser waistband, and Mr. Pak ■■ spits out phlegm only to chew and swallow it again. People's ■■ [habits?] are truly varied.

★

Although Mr. Chŏng In■ is small in stature (體小), he is particularly famous for the length of his speech and writing. Recently, a certain magazine requested a short essay (隨筆) from him, and—don't be surprised—it turned out to be a long piece (長文) of 45 manuscript pages! The magazine's editor was reportedly so overwhelmed that they dared not publish it and could only sigh deeply. If every newspaper and magazine were to meticulously pay manuscript fees based on the number of pages, his fees would undoubtedly rank first in the Korean literary world!

★

The father of Pak Hŭngshik, owner of Hwashin Department Store, shares the exact same name, Kŏbok, with lawyer Kang Kŏbok, though they have different surnames. Because of this, when people talk about Mr. Kang Kŏbok, Pak Hŭngshik feels truly awkward (거북스러워서 - a pun on 'kŏbuk', meaning turtle or awkward) and cannot speak well<74>. He even refers to Turtle Ship brand (龜船票) rubber shoes simply as 'Kwisonp'yo' (turtle-sound brand). People from his hometown still don't really know him as Pak Hŭngshik but know him well as "Pak Kŏbok's son."

★

Mr. Shin Albert, whose face hadn't been seen for quite some time, was recently spotted at Anguk-tong intersection rummaging through the new and old novels spread out by a bookseller peddling his wares. He then bought a copy of *Changhanmong* (*Song of Everlasting Sorrow*) and left. How bored must this tearful and sorrowful widowed old man be on long winter nights to buy such a book? If there is anyone sympathetic, they should buy and send him a pair of reading glasses!

★

Mr. Hong Myŏnghŭi's *Pak Kŏjŏng Chŏn* (*The Tale of Pak Kŏjŏng*) is the first long novel of its kind seen in the Korean creative world. Although Mr. Hong is said to be exhausting his mental energy writing the manuscript daily, he always ■laments, saying, "Even though I put so much effort into writing this novel, it's a novel that cannot even be published after its serialization in the newspaper. That is what's regrettable."
The reason is none other than that the novel is originally a long serial with many installments, making it impossible to publish in a single volume. And if they were to publish it in ■ volumes, the book price would be high, making it likely that few people would buy it.

★

Because Mr. Yi In's own legs are disabled (不仁), when he sees beggars who also have disabled legs, he feels special sympathy and gives them money generously. This is quite the opposite (판) of Zen Master Han Yongun, who generously gives alms to robust (이) begging monks.

★

Messrs. Song Chinu, Yi Sanghyŏp, and Cho Pyŏngsang, along with Mr. Ch'oe Ch'anghak, were unfortunately injured in a car accident at the very beginning of the New Year while heading to Paekch'ŏn Hot Springs. Although [something like fortune telling?] is ■■■■ unreliable (虛■), the saying "wealth does not approach" (富不近) seems quite true.

★

The *Tonga Daily*'s reconnaissance plane [likely a gossip column] harshly criticized this magazine (本誌) for ■ [publishing?] the Kim Il■ interview. It's too late now (時已晩■져). It would have been better if Kuk ■nyŏl, when he was the political editor at that newspaper and living with that woman, had first given advice, asking why he was living with that woman whose reputation was so foul (惡臭紛紛).

★

Messrs. Shin Hŭngu and Hyŏn Tongwan, who worked diligently for many years at the Central YMCA (中央基靑), have come to resign from the association. It's said they left not because of some other incident, but due to mutually unpleasant matters, which makes it even more regrettable. Oh, beloved Lord, why do you have two sons who cannot love each other, and among them, allow one son to receive too much other love, thus causing such turmoil out of nowhere (平地風波)? Amen!

**Summary**

This article, "One Hundred People, One Hundred Stories," is a collection of short anecdotes and gossip about various prominent Korean figures in 1935, including intellectuals, businessmen, politicians, and writers. The pieces cover personal quirks (forgetfulness, drinking habits, chest hair), professional activities (writing, attending meetings), social interactions (dealing with Japanese officials, relationship issues), and general news (births, accidents). It concludes with a brief mention of the scientific advancements in the "Zephyr" train and an advertisement for the relaunch of the magazine *Pyŏlgŏn'gon*.

From a historical perspective, the article offers glimpses into the social dynamics and cultural milieu of colonial Korea. The anecdote about Kim Yunjŏng interacting with the Japanese Police Chief Ikeda humorously illustrates the pressures and sycophancy some Koreans exhibited towards Japanese authorities. The mention of censorship (檢閱官) affecting Chŏng Inbo's readership highlights the constraints on expression. The difficulties faced by Hong Myŏnghŭi in publishing his long novel reflect the economic challenges within the colonial literary market. The reference to the *Tonga Daily* and internal disputes within organizations like the Central YMCA also shed light on the media landscape and civil society activities of the time.
