Secret Stories of the Shanghai Incident, Appendix="The Cause of Defeat and Chiang Kai-shek's Side's Statement"
						Shanghai Correspondent Kang Sŏng-gu

Somehow, on the bone-chilling cold nights of February, I inevitably find myself thinking. Thinking about what, you ask? Yes, it's understandable that our dear readers would ask this out of curiosity. Well, I'll tell you right now. It's nothing other than the scenes from the night of the Shanghai Incident (上海事變), which makes one shudder just thinking about it, and the truly terrifying "episodes" lurking within it, enough to make one's teeth chatter.

As you readers may know, it seems Chinese soldiers have historically been adept at so-called night raids (夜襲). Perhaps because of this, during the Shanghai Incident, the number of bombs flying from the Chinese lines at night was far greater than during the day.

It was the night of January 28th, three years ago now. Around midnight that night, an unexpected gunshot rang out—*tang*—lonely yet carrying a sense of tragedy under the ice-sheet-like blue January night sky. Following that, small gunfire erupted like popping beans, occasionally mixed with the sound of cannons, violently assaulting people's ears. Of course, by then, all the citizens of Shanghai had already risen from their beds, trembling with blood-stained, pale fear. I, too, was one of those people. It was the morning of the next day, the twenty-ninth. To get a clear look at the scenery of that day, something I was experiencing for the first time in my life, I mustered up my vanished energy and stepped out into the streets, which had turned into a scene of utter chaos, a veritable pandemonium. It was truly miserable. Every scene that entered my eyes seemed to hold murderous intent, blood, and finally, the terrifying death that was inevitably approaching. Self-defense groups—I was able to witness self-defense groups armed in various colorful ways <46> with my own eyes for the first time that day. Some of them carried Japanese swords (日本刀) with gleaming blue blades, others shouldered old-fashioned long rifles, and still others held things like bamboo spears (竹槍) or wooden swords (木刀), standing guard firmly on every street corner. And even small children—mere five or six-year-olds—had small daggers (短刀) perfectly suited to their small bodies and height tucked into their waists, standing steadfastly behind their fathers and older brothers.

"Sāi dé huān qù? (Where are you going?)"

They would bellow fiercely like this, thrusting out long rifles and Japanese swords towards the Chinese people wandering aimlessly through the streets, fleeing the terrifying chaos of war. The children standing behind them would also shout at the top of their lungs.

Usually, that thunderous shout alone would startle people into almost mechanically putting their "hands up." Honestly, I still don't know when or how the Chinese people learned that "hands up." You, the reader, probably don't know either. Regarding the fact that they learned "hands up," one of the "new forms" introduced from the West, before anything else, I'd like to offer a brief "new" interpretation here. It's nothing particularly special. Shall we call it an instinctive action of a person for the "desire to live (欲求)"? Anyway, regardless, I couldn't help but be surprised and admire their swift "hands up."

The self-defense group members would make the Chinese people with their "hands up" stand safely aside and conduct strict body searches. The reason for the body searches was none other than to arrest the plainclothes troops (便衣隊) who were constantly infiltrating the Hongkou (虹口) area to carry out rear disturbance (後方攪亂) against the XX army.

Speaking of plainclothes troops, they were truly elusive characters.

Usually, they disguised themselves as rickshaw (人力車) pullers to infiltrate. They would enter some dimly lit place, fire a pistol shot—*tang*—and then vanish without a trace. Observing their actions closely, it seemed to me that their goal wasn't so much to XX the XXX, but rather simply to unsettle the public and disrupt the rear of the XXX by any means necessary; that seemed to be enough for them.

There were also "group plainclothes troops" (集團便衣隊). They would gather in groups of twenty to thirty, or even seventy to eighty, occupy large churches or big movie theaters, and from there, fire shots into the streets—*tang tang*— seemingly out of boredom. And when XX self-defense members or XXX heard the gunshots and rushed to the scene shouting, not even a mouse, let alone a plainclothes soldier, would be stirring. If this isn't being elusive <47>, what is?

While we're on the subject, let me tell you another story about the plainclothes troops. This one is about a particularly daring plainclothes soldier among them.

It was late February, when the spring in war-torn Jiangnan was deepening. At the Shanghai Japanese Club (上海 日本人俱樂部), Japanese refugees who had fled the danger zones to the Hongkou area, considered relatively safe, were accommodated, and rice balls were distributed to them daily.

However, mixed among these Japanese refugees, a Chinese plainclothes soldier was brazenly chewing and swallowing a rice ball with great composure. This plainclothes soldier had secretly placed bombs at key points throughout the Japanese Club and was eating the aforementioned rice ball while waiting for the bombs to explode. But things didn't proceed so easily, and eventually, someone discovered all the bombs. Of course, none of the bombs exploded. Now that things had turned out this way, the Japanese Club was thrown into an uproar. While the refugees rejoiced endlessly at the good fortune of the bombs not exploding, they also felt a premonition of sudden tragedy creeping into a corner of their hearts, somehow seeping in.

The refugees, each roaring in anger, searched frantically with bloodshot eyes for the plainclothes soldier who had planted the bombs. Eventually, the plainclothes soldier was caught. And right there on the spot, he was beaten by the crowd. Then, readers might ask: Why didn't the plainclothes soldier flee immediately after planting the bombs, instead lingering so leisurely, eating a rice ball? But even I don't know the answer to that, no matter how much I think about it. Therefore, I have no way to provide any definite answer to your question, readers, which is extremely regrettable.

Next, shall I tell a story about a "spy," a byproduct of the Shanghai Incident? However, the spy story I'm revealing here is a very ordinary one. But for those of you who have already read and heard many complex and intricate "spy" stories, listening to a very bland and ordinary spy story, the exact opposite, might not be entirely meaningless. <48>

I can't recall exactly, but it must have been around midnight on a certain day in mid-February.

A Chinese young woman, appearing to be about thirty years old, appeared on the deeply night-shrouded, heavily guarded street, holding an eight-year-old girl on one side and a seven-year-old boy on the other.

For a Chinese woman to suddenly appear in the dead of night, where not even a shadow stirred except for the Japanese marines guarding the streets near the "barricade," was undoubtedly strange.

And was that all? That strange Chinese woman was carrying a large sum of 3,500 yuan. She was led by soldiers to a nearby barricade.

There, at the barricade, she was interrogated by an officer. The officer, too, harbored great suspicion, above all else, about the fact that the woman was carrying the large sum of 3,500 yuan. Consequently, the interrogation grew increasingly severe. However, even after searching her body thoroughly, nothing else came out besides the 3,500 yuan. Still, the officer's suspicion was not dispelled. Finally, the officer examined the Chinese woman's XXX. And there, hidden in her XXX, was a secret report (密報) for the Chinese army. This method is extremely commonplace and outdated for a spy, but it seems that Chinese female spy was still using such a method at that time.

Rather than shouting "Search for new forms!" and trying to emulate spies like Mata Hari and Agent X-27, there is a need to first return to the A-B-Cs of spycraft (spy 學 A·B·C). No, that would be the best course of action. And only after mastering the A-B-Cs of spycraft should one learn about Mata Hari and X-27. That's right! Everything has an order and steps. Isn't that so? Yes, my dear readers!

Now then, let's put aside the trivial spy story and tell another one.

The story I'm about to tell is somewhat amusing, but it could also be called a comedy (?) too serious for laughter.

Around sunset, about one battalion of the XX army <49> repulsed about two thousand Chinese soldiers and, seizing the momentum, began marching proudly. As they marched, they noticed that almost all the Chinese soldiers were lying on the ground, seemingly hit by the army's bullets. So, the XX army nonchalantly continued their march. Then, after the army had passed the area with the Chinese soldiers' bodies and gone about one or two *chō* (町 - approx. 109 meters), what should happen but bullets suddenly rained down on them from behind like falling rain. These were bullets fired by the Chinese soldiers, who had pretended to be corpses but had suddenly turned back into living people. Saying it like this might not be clear to you readers, but in other words, it seems those Chinese soldiers, repulsed by the XX army, pretended to be shot and lay prone on the ground, eagerly waiting for the XX army to pass. When things went according to plan, they thought, "Alright, this is it!" and started firing live rounds—*tang! tang!*—repeatedly from behind. What do you think? This, too, could be called a clever stratagem (妙策), if you will. Hahahaha.

Shall I stop now?

What? Were there people who made money during the incident? Of course, there were! And quite a lot of them, too! Among them, those who made money through deviant methods were the most numerous, and they probably earned the most. If circumstances permitted, I would like to list the deviant methods they used one by one, but unfortunately, I have no choice but to avoid doing so here. Shall we call this too the "author's sorrow"?

Of course, many "madame groups" (娘子羣 - likely referring to prostitutes) also came then. It seems they also managed to scrape together some money thanks to the incident boom. But isn't it sickening to hear that those frail women, feeling heart-wrenching nostalgia (ノスタルヂア - nosutarujia) in their chests as the blood-red sunset of warm Jiangnan flowed silently, had to XX an average of fifty times a day?

Anyway, when the incident ended and so-called "peace" arrived, it felt good. There might be some among you readers who have experienced "war," but I believe that among tragedies, the most profound and miserable tragedy is none other than war. However, the world is clamoring that the sickening war will break out again before long. Seeing this, one cannot help but conclude that true peace with permanence is not produced between the instructors of the "peace makers."

Now I will really stop writing. Due to the aforementioned "author's sorrow," I have no skill to write more even if I wanted to, so what can I do? The night seems deep. Well then, sleep well.

『The Cause of Defeat in the Shanghai Incident - Chiang Kai-shek's Side's Rebuttal Statement Regarding Cai Tingkai's Attack-』＜50＞

Cai Tingkai (蔡廷楷), the valiant general on the Chinese side during the Shanghai Incident who recently received an enthusiastic welcome in the United States and later became a central figure in the Fujian Independence Movement, delivered a fierce speech in America regarding the Shanghai Incident. The crux of his speech attributed the defeat of the Chinese side in the Shanghai Incident entirely to Chiang Kai-shek's responsibility. In response to this, Chiang Kai-shek's representative issued a lengthy statement from Hankou (漢口), delivering a scathing counterattack against Cai Tingkai. This statement reveals new facts previously unknown to the general public, sparking our interest considerably. Here, we present the main points of the statement, hoping you will read it to the end. (Correspondent Kang)

Cai Tingkai is attacking Mr. Chiang Kai-shek, but the gist is: "Chiang Kai-shek used a series of cunning schemes during the Shanghai Incident to destroy the core elite part of the Chinese resistance against the Japanese attack." And, "The so-called government at the time did not send a single soldier towards Shanghai, nor did it supply the necessary munitions." Cai Tingkai probably wanted to attack Mr. Chiang Kai-shek because he was defeated in the Fujian Rebellion (福建叛亂) (emphasis - Kang), but we didn't expect Cai to tell such bold lies so recklessly. The "so-called government" Cai mentioned was, in fact, formed by Cai's faction at that time, and Mr. Chiang Kai-shek held no power among them. Cai Tingkai does not know English. Therefore, when he returns to his country, the meaning of the Chinese text he used for his speech in America will become clear, but the English translation is too far removed from the facts.

Firstly, it was not only the 19th Route Army that defended Shanghai. The 87th and 88th Divisions (i.e., the 5th Route Army), trained by Mr. Chiang himself, fought most bravely, and one-third of them died in battle.
Besides this, Mr. Chiang, despite being merely a private citizen at the time, never neglected daily contact with Chiang Kuang-nai (蔣光鼎) and Cai Tingkai (蔡廷楷). If you read the newspapers from that time even now, you will clearly see that Mr. Chiang was forced to resign and formally left his post on December 20, 1931 (Showa 6). From the end of 1931 to the beginning of 1932 (Showa 7), the Guangdong faction claimed that Chinese unification was impossible as long as Mr. Chiang remained in office and did not participate in the government.

Mr. Chiang, out of patriotism, threw away all key positions and intended to accept the recommendation to travel abroad. However, as soon as he returned to his hometown in Zhejiang Province (浙江省) to prepare for his trip abroad, he faced another attack from the new government. At that time, the new government cast the following barbed words: - If Mr. Chiang has patriotic sincerity, isn't it natural for him to quickly return to the capital and assist the government! Because of this, Mr. Chiang returned to the capital again.

When the Shanghai Incident broke out on January 28, 1932 (Showa 7), although Mr. Chiang was in Nanjing (南京), he could not issue orders to the government troops because he had left public office. However, facing this crisis, Mr. Chiang intended to take on emergency duties, but the government rejected him, stating that Mr. Chiang was merely a member of the Military Affairs Commission. <51> Furthermore, he volunteered to go to the front line to encourage the soldiers, but this too was buried due to the jealousy and fear (emphasis - Kang) of the opposing faction. Thus, Mr. Chiang Kai-shek was placed in a peculiar position where he would be impeached for failure if it occurred, yet he could not prevent that failure.

Suppressing Mr. Chiang Kai-shek, who was about to depart for the front line, they said: - The officers and soldiers will not welcome Mr. Chiang. If you still try to force your initial intention, there is a risk that the 19th Route Army, instigated by Chen Mǒu (陳某) (a figure implicated in the China Merchants Steam Navigation Company scandal - Kang) and others from the opposing faction, will clash with the 5th Route Army. -
At that time, on the other hand, Mr. Chiang ordered the officers and soldiers of the 5th Route Army to do their utmost to assist the 19th Route Army, even saying, "The glory of the 19th Route Army is the glory of China!"

Thus, Mr. Chiang Kai-shek had no choice but to be satisfied with advising Generals Chiang Kuang-nai (蔣光鼎) and Cai Tingkai (蔡廷楷) only by telegram. He cautioned them not to take an offensive stance until a certain time came and advised them to remain faithful to defensive tactics until reinforcements arrived. At that time, there were numerous Japanese warships on the Yangtze River (揚子江), so reinforcements could not be transported quickly using the river. And while the existing forces might have been insufficient for an attack, there was no shortage for defense.

We now introduce the telegrams exchanged between Mr. Chiang Kai-shek and the two generals.
First, Mr. Chiang advised them not to adopt an offensive posture until the day reinforcements arrived. Second, he pointed out that the defensive line at Liuhe (劉河) on the Yangtze River bank was extremely weak because not a single soldier was stationed there, and strongly recommended sending at least two regiments (聯隊) to prevent a Japanese landing. Third, the return telegram, jointly signed by Mr. Chiang Kuang-nai and Mr. Cai Tingkai, clearly stated their intention to act according to Mr. Chiang Kai-shek's advice.

However, because they did not implement this, the Japanese army was able to land without meeting any resistance. And the two generals carried out a retreat on their own accord, without waiting for any permission from the central government. Even when carrying out the retreat, they gave no notification whatsoever to the Fifth Route Army, so the Fifth Route Army continued fighting <52> and suffered considerable losses.

During this period of national crisis, Mr. Chiang Kai-shek was blamed from various quarters, either by telegram or letter, for not extending a helping hand.
Let me say it again: the critics are completely unaware that Mr. Chiang had been stripped of all key positions at the time. Mr. Chiang did his utmost merely as a private citizen, believing his advice had been accepted by the other party, and dispatched reinforcements urgently. And the advance units of those reinforcements did indeed arrive in Shanghai on the scheduled date. However, this was two days after Generals Chiang and Cai had carried out their retreat.

During and after the Shanghai Incident, those around Mr. Chiang Kai-shek advised publishing the truth, but Mr. Chiang refused all such suggestions. Mr. Chiang dispatched Zhang Zizhong (張自忠), the commander of the 5th Route Army at the time, to Chiang Kuang-nai and Cai Tingkai, requesting that they reveal the truth directly themselves. Although the two generals immediately agreed to this request, they clamped their mouths shut once the incident ended.

Therefore, it is perhaps understandable that the outside world remains completely unaware of the true state of affairs to this day. However, on the pages of a certain Chinese newspaper in Shanghai, the two generals stated clearly several times: - Mr. Chiang Kai-shek fully supported the two generals and did not withhold any necessary assistance. - Considering the points mentioned above, the so-called statement by Cai Tingkai is utterly baseless, and it can be said to have no value beyond the stereotypical ** [censored text likely referred to propaganda or slander] of reactionary politicians' fabrications. - End of Statement Summary <53>

**Summary**

This article by Kang Sŏng-gu, a Shanghai correspondent for the magazine *Kaebyŏk Sin'gan*, provides anecdotal accounts of the 1932 Shanghai Incident. It describes the chaotic atmosphere, the actions of Chinese self-defense groups (including children), the elusive tactics of plainclothes Chinese operatives (便衣隊), a failed bombing attempt at the Japanese Club, and a story of a female spy using conventional methods. It also recounts an incident where supposedly defeated Chinese soldiers feigned death to ambush the advancing XX army (presumably Japanese). The latter half of the piece presents a translation of a statement attributed to Chiang Kai-shek's faction, refuting accusations by General Cai Tingkai that Chiang was responsible for the Chinese defeat, claiming Chiang was powerless at the time and that Cai and his 19th Route Army ignored advice and retreated prematurely. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its first-hand (though potentially biased) Korean perspective on a major Sino-Japanese conflict, the casual mention of Japanese military presence (often censored as 'XX'), the portrayal of Chinese resistance and espionage tactics, and the inclusion of internal Chinese political disputes (Chiang vs. Cai) filtered through a Korean publication under Japanese rule. The censorship (XX, XXX) itself highlights the constraints on reporting sensitive military or political information in colonial Korea.

上海事變秘話, 附=『敗因과 蔣介石側의 聲明』
						上海特派員 姜聖九
읏전지 살을 에워내는 듯한 일, 2월의 추은 밤이면 의레이 생각나드군요. 뭣이 그러케 생각나느냐고요? 그럿습니다. 친애하는 독자 여러분께서 궁금해하는 나머지 이러케 무르시는 것도 무리는 아닐 것입니다. 그러면 시방 곳 대여드리죠. 다른것이 아니라 그것은 생각만해도 소름이 쪽 끼치는 상해사변(上海事變)의 밤의 광경이며 그리고 그속에 꿈튼거리는 역시 치가 떨닐만한 너무나 무서운 『에피소-드』 말입니다.
독자 여러분께서도 아시겟지만 옛날부터 중국 군사들은 소위 야습(夜襲)을 잘하는 모양이 드구만요. 그래 그런지 상해사변 때도 밤이 되면 중국군 진영에서 날너오는 폭탄의 수가 낫보다 훨신 만습듸다.

『上海事變의 敗因-蔡廷楷의 攻擊에 對하야 蔣介石側의 反駁的聲明-』＜50＞

얼마전 미국에서 열광적 환영을 밧든 상해사변 당시의 중국측의 용장 그후의 福建獨立運動의 대인물이엇든 19로 군장 蔡廷楷는 미국서 상해사변에 관한 격렬한 연설을 하엿는데 그 연설의 골자는 상해사변에 잇서서의 중국측의 敗北을 전혀 蔣介石의 책임으로 돌려버렷다는데 잇다. 여긔에 대하야 蔣介石의 대표는 漢口에서 장문의 성명을 發하고 蔡廷楷에게 통열한 반격을 가햇다. 그 성명중에는 종래 일반이 알지 못햇든 신사실도 폭로되여 잇서 자못 우리들의 흥미를 자아내는 점이 잇다. 여긔에 其 성명의 大要를 揭載하니 끝까지 읽어보기를 바란다. (姜特派員)

蔡廷楷는 蔣介石씨를 공격하고 잇스나 그것은 요컨대 『蔣介石은 상해사변 당시 일련의 간책을 써서 일본의 공격에 대한 중국측의 저항의 최정예 부분을 破壤하엿다』 『당시의 소위 정부는 상해를 향하야 일인의 병정도 보내지 안코 또 필요한 군수품도 공급치 안햇다』 라고 한데 잇다. 蔡廷楷는 아마 福建叛亂(傍點姜)에 패한 까닭에 蔣介石씨를 공격하고 싶엇슬 것이다만은 그러타고 蔡가 이러케도 대담한 거즛말을 함부로 할줄은 몰낫다. 蔡가 말한 『소위 정부』란 것은 그당시 실로 蔡의 당파로서 형성되여 잇섯고 蔣介石씨는 그들 중에서 아무런 권력도 가지고 잇지 안햇다. 蔡廷楷는 영어를 모른다. 따라서 그가 귀국하면 미국에서 연설할 때 쓴 그의 중국어의 의미가 판명될 것이다만은 英譯은 너무나 사실과 相遠가 잇다.
제일로 상해를 防護한 것은 19路軍 뿐이 아니엇다. 蔣씨 자신의 손으로 훈련된 87師, 88師(즉 제 5路軍)는 가장 용감히 싸웟스며 그리고 其 삼분지 일은 전사햇다.
또 이런일 외에 蔣씨는 당시 일개의 시민에 지내지 안햇슴에도 불구하고 매일 蔣光鼎과 蔡廷楷와의 연락을 조곰도 겨을리지 안햇다. 당시의 신문을 지금이라도 읽어보면 확실이 알것이다만은 蔣씨는 辭職의 강요를 바더 1931년(즉 昭和 6년) 12월 20일에 정식으로 그 職을 떠나버렷든 것이다. 1931년 말부터 32년(즉 昭和 7년) 초에 이르기까지 廣東派는 蔣씨가 其職에 잇는 이상 중국의 통일은 불가능하다고 하엿스며 그리고 정부에 참가하지 안햇다.
蔣씨는 애국의 지성으로 일체의 요직을 내던지고 外遊의 권고를 감수할나고 하엿다. 그런데 氏가 外遊의 준비를 하려 浙江省의 향리에 도라가자마자 이번엔 또 별다른 공격을 신정부한테 바덧다. 그때 신정부는 아래와 가티 가시품은 말을 던진 것이다.-만약 蔣君에게 애국의 지성이 잇스면은 하로밧비 귀경하야 정부를 조력함이 당연치 안느냐!고. 이통에 蔣씨는 또 귀경하엿다.
1932년(즉 昭和 7년) 1월 28일 상해사변이 발발한 당시 비록 蔣씨는 南京에 잇기는 햇스나 氏는 공직을 떠난 까닭에 정부의 군대에게 명령을 발할수 없엇다. 그러나 蔣씨는 이 난국에 직면하야 救急의 임을 맛흘나고 하엿스나 정부는 蔣씨가 일개의 군사위원에 지내지 안는다고 배척해 버렷다.＜51＞ 또 氏는 자진하야 전선에 나가서 병사들을 독려하겟다고 顧햇스나 이것도 역시 반동파의 질투와 공포심(傍點-姜) 때문에 매장되고 말엇다. 이리하야 蔣介石씨는 실패를 하면 그 책임의 彈劾을 받고 그리고 또 그 실패를 회피치 못한다는 말하자면 奇妙한 지위에 노여 잇섯든 것이다.
전선으로 출발할나고 하는 蔣介石씨를 억압하고 그들은 말하엿다. -將士들은 蔣君을 환영안할 것이다. 만약 그래도 억지로 初志를 관철할나고 애쓸것 갓흐면 19路軍은 반동파의 일인 陳某(招商局疑獄事件에 連坐한 인물이다-姜)등에게 선동되어 五路軍과 충돌할 염려가 있다라고-.
그때 한편으로 蔣씨는 五路軍의 將士들에게 전력을 다하야 19路軍의 원조를 명하고 『19路軍의 光榮은 중국의 光榮이다!』 라고까지 말햇다.
이리하야 蔣介石씨는 蔣光鼎, 蔡廷楷 兩氏에게 다만 전보로 충고하는 것으로 만족할 수밧게 없엇다. 氏는 일정한 시기가 오기까지 공세적 태도를 취하지 말기를 주의식히고 원군 도래까지 오로지 방어전술을 취함에 충실하라고 충고하엿다. 당시 양자강엔 다수의 일본 군함이 잇섯기 때문에 江上을 이용하야 급속이 원군을 수송할 수 업섯다. 그리고 당시의 병력은 공격엔 불충분할는지 모르지만 수비엔 부족할 점이 업섯다.
우리들은 지금 여긔에 蔣介石씨와 二將軍間에 교환된 전보를 소개한다.
第一로 蔣씨는 원군 도래의 날까지 공격적 태도를 취하지 말나고 충고하엿다. 第二로, 양자강안의 劉河에는 一兵도 주둔하지 안한 까닭으로 防禦陣이 극히 徵弱하다는 점을 지적하고 일본군의 상륙을 阻止하기 위하야 적어도 2聯隊를 보낼것을 극력 권고하엿다. 第三으로 蔣光鼎씨와 蔡廷楷씨의 공동서명이 잇는 返電에는 蔣介石씨의 충언대로 행동한다는 뜻이 뚜렷이 기록되어 잇섯다.
그러나 그들은 이것을 실행치 안햇기 때문에 일본군이 아무런 저항도 밧지안코 상륙할 수 잇섯다. 그리고 二將軍은 중앙의 아무 허가도 기다리지 안코 제마음대로 퇴각을 단행해 버렷다. 퇴각을 단행할때도 第五路軍에겐 아무런 통지도 없었기 때문에 그들 五路軍은 여전이 전투를 게속 하엿스며＜52＞ 또 다대한 손해를 받은 것이엇다.
이 국난기간중 蔣介石씨는 각 방면으로부터 혹은 전보로 혹은 편지로 구원의 손을 펴지 안한 책임을 詰責당햇다.
또한번 말하지만은 비난자들은 당시 蔣씨가 모-든 요직을 빼앗긴 것을 조곰도 모르고 잇는 것이다. 蔣씨는 단지 일시민의 자격으로 전력을 다하엿스며 자기의 충언이 상대자에게 용납된줄만 밋고 원군을 급파하엿다. 그리고 그 원군선발대는 사실로 예정의 기일에 상해에 도착되엇다. 그러나 그것은 蔣蔡 二將軍이 퇴각을 단행한지 2일후엿다.
상해사변중 及 其後에 잇서 蔣介石씨의 주위에선 진상의 발표를 권햇스나 蔣씨는 이것을 모다 거부햇다. 蔣씨는 당시의 5路軍長 張自忠을 蔣光鼎과 蔡廷楷에게 派遺하야 직접 양 장군의 손으로 진상을 발표하기를 요구하엿다. 양 장군은 이 요구를 卽座에 승낙햇슴에 불구하고 사변이 끝나자 그만 입을 담으려버린 것이엇다.
따라서 현재에 이르기까지 외부는 사실의 진상에 관해서 도모지 모르고 잇는것도 무리는 아닐 것이다. 다만 상해의 모 중국신문 지상에다 양 장군은 멧번인가 명백이 말한 것이 잇다.-蔣介石씨는 양 장군을 충분이 지지하엿고 일체의 필요한 원조를 앗기지 안햇다, 라고. 이상 말한 점으로 미루어보면 소위 蔡廷楷의 성명이란 것은 전혀 근거가 업고 그것은 다만 반동정치가의 날조에 지내지 안는 상투적 **의 가치 이외엔 아무 것도 아니라고 할 수 잇슬 것이다.- 성명서 대요 끝 ＜53＞
＜46-53＞
