**Body Text Translation:**
White and Red Fleets

It is reported that Priboi (뿌리보이), who occupies a unique position in the Soviet literary world having published works based on "Tsushima" (對馬), "The Rout" (敗走), and the Battle of the Sea of Japan (日本海海戰), is again preparing to publish [missing text]. At this time, Priboi has published his motives for writing "Tsushima" in the Pravda (프라우다) newspaper, roughly as follows, showing that Soviet [sentiment] towards Japan is still far from ordinary. Quoting a certain news report (某■通信):

"In my life, I could not remain silent as a seaman and as a writer during such an [intense] period. Before my very eyes, the [fleet] (浮■) of thirty-eight ships carrying 12,000 crew members, unprecedented in world history, was annihilated. With the October Revolution, my wandering life (■■浪生活) came to an end, and I entered this life as a writer.

The artistic task of "Tsushima," so to speak, is to depict the [conditions] (■■ 모양) of the Baltic Fleet (빨틱 艦隊), which were entirely different from the life within the Red Fleet (赤色艦隊). Of course, I also have the [intention] (■■) to depict the Red Fleet, but even without me, there are plenty of people suitable for that task, and any Soviet writer (쏘비엣트 作家■) can understand the Red Fleet. However, the fleet life of the Tsarist era (帝政時代) is not so easily understood. The manuscript for the second edition of "Tsushima" was completed a few days ago and is currently being printed (印■中)."

**Summary**

This news report from 1935 discusses Soviet author Aleksey Novikov-Priboy, famous for his novel "Tsushima" about the Russo-Japanese War. It quotes Priboy (via Pravda) explaining his motivation: to document the disastrous Tsushima battle he witnessed as a sailor and contrast the inept Tsarist-era Baltic Fleet's conditions with the (implied superior) Soviet Red Fleet. Priboy notes that while others can write about the Red Fleet, understanding the Tsarist navy requires firsthand experience like his. He mentions a second edition of "Tsushima" is being printed. For Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it appears in a Korean magazine ("Kaebyŏk"), reflecting Korean awareness of international affairs, particularly Soviet-Japanese tensions. It highlights the Soviet perspective on the Russo-Japanese War (a pivotal event influencing Korea's annexation) and subtly underscores the ongoing geopolitical friction between the USSR and Japan in the 1930s, a dynamic keenly observed in colonized Korea.
