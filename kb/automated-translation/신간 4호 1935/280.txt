Overall Assessment of Student Conduct
Deviant behaviors among middle school and elementary school students during December showed a tendency towards gradual increase, totaling 1,212 cases<78> as follows. This is a phenomenon drawing attention at a time when student conduct issues have become a topic of discussion.
The most common offense is visiting motion picture theaters, with 550 cases (Elementary: 98, Middle School: 452). Next is improper attire with 398 cases, all concerning male middle school students.
Tardiness accounted for 43 cases, also mostly middle school students (38 cases), including some female middle school students.
Next is visiting cafes, restaurants, and tea rooms with 22 cases, which includes 6 female students. Street eating accounted for 30 cases, skipping meals for 12 cases, insulting women, and beating younger students. Broken down by elementary/middle school and gender:

**Summary**

This article from March 1935 compiles various statistics reflecting conditions in colonial Korea. It covers increases in tobacco sales and gold mining applications, details allocations for the third round of public works/poverty relief projects across different regions and categories (river management, ports, water supply, roads), presents data on rising student disciplinary issues (movie-going, attire, tardiness, cafe visits), and provides extensive statistics on film censorship, including breakdowns by Japanese production companies and genres. The piece offers a snapshot of economic activity, government spending priorities, social concerns regarding youth, and the tightening control over popular culture, particularly cinema, under Japanese rule. Historians would find the specific financial data on relief projects, the categories used for student misconduct, and the detailed film censorship numbers (showing the dominance of Japanese studios and genre popularity) particularly valuable for analyzing colonial administration, social control mechanisms, and cultural consumption patterns.
