Germany's National Lottery (國營富籤)

There is something that makes the hearts of five million Germans constantly pound from the end of the year through the New Year – it is the National Lottery. For the upper and lower classes of the German populace, this National Lottery is sweet and provides no small amount of comfort (慰安).

The long yearning (憧憬) spanning three hundred sixty-five days may or may not be realized (實現) in merely [illegible] minutes, a short time. However, the populace (民衆) says that whether the dream is realized or not is a secondary issue, and they are greatly satisfied just being immersed, as if drunk, in the thrilling feeling of anticipating that fortune, even if only for ten minutes. Truly, those ten minutes are a time of perfect peace under heaven.

The final drawing (抽籤) day for the lottery is December 12th, but there is a ten-day break before this final drawing day, so the actual drawing period lasts about one and a half months.

During this month and a half, Germans throughout the country test their luck for the year, their blood pumping vigorously [illegible], so this season is quite lively, regardless of whether in the city or the countryside (莫論).

There are various sales methods for this lottery, but generally, lottery [illegible - sellers?] energetically go to places like gathering spots to sell them.

Around the end of October, when these lottery [illegible - sellers?] appear on the streets, people suddenly reflect (回顧) on their own luck for the year.

As it is a national lottery, there are 262 public drawing locations across the country. Nowadays, the convenient radio seems to be working hard for this lottery. It is said that there is no time when radio listeners (聽取者) are more thankful than during the public drawing period.

However, although this German lottery is nationwide, it is not a worldwide public lottery. It is somewhat difficult for foreigners to buy this lottery ticket and obtain fortune. Also, heinous criminals (兇罪者) in prison are denied the right to buy it.

During this lottery season, there are also quite a few [illegible] smaller lotteries, such as the Hamburg lottery and the [illegible] lottery.

The number of lottery participants was around 320,000 after the [illegible - War?] (■爭後), but today it seems to have increased to 800,000.

If you ask what the cheapest prize money among these lotteries is, the minimum is the 120 Mark lottery.

Among these lotteries, the most interesting and popular ones are those of Prussia (풀로입센) and Southern Germany. The prize capital (賞■資本) here was initially 62,770,740 Marks, but it has now increased (增資) to 113,960,100 Marks. The net profit (純利益) from one lottery drawing is said to be 20,475,744 Marks, which is a [illegible] large amount. Also, the lotteries of Prussia and Southern Germany are held twice a year <82> and are divided into five classes (階級) for the public drawing, so they seem quite popular.

Thus, each year, [illegible] lucky winners emerge from among the five million lottery purchasers.

Last year's darling (寵兒) of the lottery was Mr. Bandipakeu (반듸빠크), the son of a [illegible] shop in Berlin (伯林), still in his twenty-[illegible] years. His dream of [illegible] minutes was realized, and he instantly became a millionaire (金滿家) worth several million Won [Note: Won likely used loosely for a large sum, context is German Marks]. His father had bought lottery tickets for over ten years but never won, yet this time his son secretly bought one without his father's knowledge and instantly became a man of property (財産家) worth several million Won. Because of this, not only did Mr. Ban succeed splendidly in a marriage his father opposed, but his mother, who had left home due to disagreements with the father, also returned. Seeing this, the magic power (魔力) of this lottery seems quite significant.

Appearance of a Jewish Mayor in Jerusalem!!

Jews driven out (쫏겨난) of Germany mostly fled (逃亡) to Palestine (파래스치나), causing various problems and even staging (演出식힌) bloody tragedies in the so-called Holy Land (聖地), but recently things seem to have calmed down (鎭靜) somewhat.

Now, it is said that the Jerusalem (에루살렘) mayoral election (選擧) will be held (擧行) soon, and a Jew (猶太人) has put himself forward as a candidate (立候補), with his election (當選) viewed optimistically (樂觀). If a Jewish mayor of Jerusalem appears, he will naturally take control (掌握) of the city's administration, which would be the first time since the expulsion of Jews from the same place in 70 CE [Note: Refers to the destruction of Jerusalem and diaspora]. Thinking of this as a cycle of history (歷史의 循環) is not without some interest.

**Summary**

The article covers two main topics: the widespread popularity and social impact of the German national lottery, detailing its operation, prizes, and a story of a recent young winner whose fortune transformed his life and family situation. The second part discusses the potential election of the first Jewish mayor in Jerusalem since antiquity, contextualizing it with the recent flight of German Jews to Palestine and framing it as a significant historical turn. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article reveals a Korean interest in international affairs, particularly in Germany (a modernizing power) and the situation of Jewish people (a minority facing persecution and displacement, perhaps drawing parallels). The detailed description of the lottery might reflect aspirations or comparisons regarding wealth and fortune in constrained colonial circumstances. The focus on the Jerusalem election highlights awareness of geopolitical shifts and historical narratives of diaspora and return.
