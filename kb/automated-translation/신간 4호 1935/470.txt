“My mother was pouting because your salary is low, so I bluffed a bit, saying it’s forty-five *wŏn* as an apprentice, and that if it goes up by ten *wŏn* each year, it’ll be a hundred *wŏn* within five years. Keep that in mind.”
“It’s like the mercury column (水銀柱) rising in the height of the summer heat. If it goes up too much, it’s bound to fall!”
Though they parted laughing like this, Ch’ansu, from that moment, not only felt ashamed of his thirty-five *wŏn* salary but also engraved in his mind the fact that one of the motivations that finalized the marriage issue was the ten *wŏn* verbally added through his friend's goodwill. That's why, even today, he tells Munhŭi he was a "fifty-*wŏn* groom." It was unavoidable due to the practicalities of life, but it wasn’t a pleasant matter.

At any rate, the following three or four months were Ch’ansu’s happy times. Though he couldn't go to professional school, there was the interest and freshness of encountering real society, and added to that, his engagement to a beauty like Chŏngnye proceeded smoothly, so it felt as if the light of life was suddenly shining upon him. As he visited Chŏngnye’s house and was called over, love grew and deepened. Affection as childhood friends changed into affection as lovers. Moreover, while the groom was still young, the bride’s side, with a widowed mother reluctant to send her daughter off hastily and thinking she would suffer if married young, decided to hold the wedding the next spring or autumn. This seemed rather fortunate for the young man and woman, as it extended their so-called courtship period. And so, the sweet dream only deepened.

However, that was all while Sim Wŏnsŏk was alive. That he died suddenly in an accident was a bolt from the blue, but for Ch’ansu, it was followed by a second thunderbolt.
The declaration of the broken engagement came less than a month later. When Ch’ansu visited frequently to offer condolences, Chŏngnye's mother would cling to him, weeping at the thought of her son, to the point where it became exasperating. Eventually, he reflected that his attempts at consolation seemed only to freshly stir up thoughts of her son, so his own feet naturally grew distant. On their side, too, they gradually became colder. Chŏngnye herself, crushed by the too sudden blow and sorrow, <99> was likely not composed enough to whisper words of love by the seashore. As for her mother, from the very beginning of the engagement, she had been dragged along by her son’s insistence, despite several points she disliked—his stepmother situation, his being the eldest son, his poverty. Now that the son who had arranged the engagement was gone, and with only her daughter left to rely on, her primary condition became finding a live-in son-in-law (*terilsawi*) who earned well. Even if not a live-in son-in-law, she wanted someone who could at least rent a separate house and live independently, perhaps a second or third son, or someone suited to be the husband of the youngest daughter. However, Ch’ansu met none of these requirements. If he were wealthy enough to ensure his mother-in-law's comfort for the rest of her life, perhaps being the eldest son wouldn't matter, but he couldn't forcibly take away a daughter she claimed she couldn't give up, nor was he in a position to move in with them. Furthermore, the daughter herself, facing the prospect of leaving her helpless mother, likely lacked the firm resolve at her young age of around eighteen to commit strongly to love for a man, and thus couldn't defy her mother's words.

To console her grief-stricken mother, submission was the only way. She had no means to evade her mother's strict orders and surveillance forbidding her to meet him.
“Seeing you, just when I’ve managed to calm down, my sorrow surges up again. She also doesn't want to meet you for a while.”
—Having shamed him like this to prevent him from coming at all, she would take any letter that came for Chŏngnye and, right before Chŏngnye's eyes, throw it, still sealed, into the fireplace grate. If Chŏngnye uttered even a single word in protest,
“Is this wench acting up because she thinks she won’t get a husband?” she would retort, and inevitably end up crying after venting her anger, making Chŏngnye cry too. Perhaps it was to sever the attachment, but in her need to vent her frustrations on everything, Ch’ansu and his letters got caught in her wrath and were treated with even greater cruelty. For a while, that lady was certainly not in her right mind, and being not in her right mind, she handled matters impulsively without considering consequences, like an angry child smashing everything within reach. Among those things, her daughter's engagement was the first to suffer.

However, having heard words harsher than the declaration not to visit, Ch’ansu waited nearly a month. His letters of consolation turned into love letters, and then <100> again into letters of reproach, demanding, demanding a reply, any word, black or white. Finally, wanting to hear just one word from her own lips, he suggested meeting times and places two or three times, but still received only dead silence. This, too, was undoubtedly the mother's doing. Then, what was called the engagement ring arrived by parcel post. He was indignant at the utterly senseless way things were handled and considered reprimanding them by letter, but decided to wait for the lady's inflamed temper to cool. Whether the ring was returned or not, considering it a matter of great human importance (*illyundaesa*), especially given the affection shared over four or five years of acquaintance and the duty not to betray the deceased friend's wishes, he couldn't treat it so lightly. He felt a sense of responsibility that he must absolutely take care of the mother and daughter for life, and he hadn't lost hope at all. It was just that visiting them immediately might worsen the situation through unpleasant exchanges, so he maintained a prudent silence and observed from afar.

However, after a couple of months passed, he heard rumors that Chŏngnye had appeared in the Chongno area. He discovered Chŏngnye working as a clerk at a large general store that did business with the company Ch’ansu used to work for. It seemed the widow, feeling her own strength suddenly depleted after losing her son, felt she could no longer manage the household alone and had sent her daughter out to work. This wasn't unreasonable. When Ch’ansu, with a determined heart, sought her out, Chŏngnye, perhaps out of embarrassment, looked more ashamed and shy than pleased to see him. Even if she hadn't been able to send a single letter or make a phone call during that time due to her mother's surveillance and interference, her attitude—remaining aloof while freely moving about, especially working nearby—suggested her heart had already drifted away. Nevertheless, he treated her with sympathy, thinking that perhaps, after her mother had ruined things, she couldn't bring herself to seek him out, worried he might be angry, or that her hesitant steps were due to having done too many things wrong from her narrow girlish perspective. But Chŏngnye attributed everything solely to her mother. She told him to go talk to her mother, to negotiate with her mother, refusing to reveal her own inner feelings. He went to see the mother. But the mother shook her head. She scolded him, asking why he was bringing upเรื่อง anew something that was already settled long ago. Still, <101> Ch’ansu did not yield. He believed he could definitely change her mind and succeed. He vowed he would succeed. From then on, he visited Chŏngnye whenever he had time. Guessing her leaving time, he would walk with her, reasoning with her, reprimanding her, and cajoling her. During this time, Chŏngnye too seemed to miss their past affection and appreciate Ch’ansu's goodwill and passion. Anyway, he had managed to pull the woman's heart to a point where it seemed almost within his grasp.

But that too ended in a final breakdown in less than a month.
One chilly winter night, Ch’ansu paced the street, waiting for Chŏngnye to come out. However, Ch’ansu didn't know there was another dark shadow there, just like him. The woman he awaited with eyes straining stepped out of the shop door, glanced casually at the display window, and gave a smiling glance to an unfamiliar man standing there before heading towards the streetcar stop. The man followed her, stood beside her, and they began talking. Watching this unfold from behind, Ch’ansu’s eyes suddenly burned. He thought of turning back, but driven by youthful pride, he followed and stood beside the woman. Chŏngnye was startled. Her expression of surprise was like that of a girl brushing off a bug or a millipede from her neck. Ch’ansu despaired. The other man turned away.

When the streetcar arrived, Chŏngnye hastily said goodbye and boarded. Ch’ansu lacked the courage to follow her onto the streetcar and stood blankly as if waiting for the next one. The man glanced scrutinizingly at Ch’ansu’s face and then jumped onto the departing streetcar. That evening, when Ch’ansu deliberately went to a Chinese restaurant to borrow the phone and called to ask her out, Chŏngnye refused, saying she had no time today. He had gone anyway out of a desire to see her, even briefly, since it had been too long, but only after witnessing that scene did he understand why she had said she couldn't meet him.

The next day, Chŏngnye’s mother came to Ch’ansu’s company and made a scene. Why was he interfering with the marriage prospects? she demanded. Given Chŏngnye’s thoughts, age, and reputation, it would have been better if she had spoken calmly, but she caused a commotion so that everyone at the company knew. It was, of course, <102> because of the previous night's incident; that man was apparently the prospective son-in-law, and he must have said something unpleasant after Ch'ansu's appearance.

Ch’ansu, his disappointment turning into anger and resentment, gave up completely. He left the company at the end of that month. He wasn't fired, but he was embarrassed by the rumors spreading not just within the company but throughout the Chongno area, and also because he had been scolded by his father. The explosive result of the elderly widow's fierce, inflated temper combined with hysteria had utterly destroyed a perfectly fine young man.

Around the time Ch’ansu left the company, Chŏngnye also vanished from the Chongno area. Rumor had it she went to Japan, but he heard no word of her getting married. And after that, he heard no news at all. Nor did he try to find out.

But today, coming to this house, Chŏngnye had suddenly appeared before Ch’ansu after almost five years, in a photograph, and he heard the news that she wanted to meet him.

Ch’ansu, rather than complaining about how carelessly and insultingly they had treated him, or how much he had suffered not only mentally but also materially and in reputation, continued his story to Munhŭi as a form of self-justification.
“The reason I abandoned my promise to my deceased friend and the dream of becoming a great industrialist, and instead began avidly reading literary works, was partly because I had the time during the year I idled around after leaving that company, but it was also because, having suffered such blows in various ways and grown pessimistic about the world, I found solace there…” he began, trying to unburden himself.
“I suppose that’s understandable. But you should also sympathize with and understand their side of the story. Given the circumstances back then, it was easy for the old widow to act that way, and wouldn’t Chŏngnye have felt the same?”
“Yes, that’s true… But when did you meet her? She went to Japan quite some time ago.” <103>
“She went in the autumn the year before last and came back last winter, but then couldn't return because of her mother’s illness and stayed put. Something about shock, they said. She was terribly frightened, and it apparently turned into a sickness from suppressed anger (울화증 - ulhwajeung), but she wouldn't talk about the details, and it’s quite pitiful to see.”
“Huh—, really? That could happen, but that’s unfortunate…”
Despite everything, drawn by old affection, Ch’ansu couldn't help but be surprised.
“So, what did she do in Tokyo? That means she went twice in total…”
“Yes— Before, she attended a women's commercial school for about a year, then came home because her tuition ran out. Then she worked again to save money and went back; that’s when I first met her.”
“Worked to save money?…” Ch’ansu scoffed. He had thought so back then too: it was unlikely the mother would send her daughter so far away to Tokyo unless some scoundrel had enticed her, promising to pay for her studies, only to abandon her. Then, having tasted Tokyo and become restless, she probably went back again somehow, only to fail a second time and return. That seemed plausible. An old widow clinging to her only daughter, unwilling to let her go but wanting to benefit from her—it was typical for such situations to drift into ambiguity, ultimately ruining the child. Thinking this, Ch’ansu felt both resentment and pity.
“So what is she doing now?”
“Doing what? It seems she racked up quite a bit of debt nursing her mother. So they sold their house to pay the debt, and with what was left, they rented the detached servant's quarters (*sarangchae*) of someone else's house in Hyojadong on deposit. There might be two or three hundred *wŏn* remaining, so for now, the two of them can manage without starving, staying indoors.”
The hopes for a live-in son-in-law or marrying into a rich family as the youngest daughter-in-law had all come to nothing. Now, the circumstances of that lady, who was just waiting for death, were a thing of the past. Anyway, it was unfortunate and pitiful.
“It looks like once the mother passes away, Sim Chŏngnye will be completely alone and dependent,” Ch’ansu said, his tone softening, drawn by old feelings despite everything. <104>
“Exactly. I wish she could get married before the mother passes, while she’s still a year younger, but now that they're ruined and the mother is like that, who would even consider helping them out?”
That was indeed the reality, but Ch’ansu felt no inclination now to get involved in such matters anew, nor did he feel like visiting them out of courtesy or sympathy.
“By the way, how did my story come up?”
“She already knew you were working at the company. When she heard I was going to work there, she looked startled, almost pleased, and said, ‘Ah, then there must be a gentleman like this and that there,’ which seemed suspicious, so I probed carefully, and she told me the whole story.”
“Did she also mention that I study or write poetry?”
“Yes— She knew it well, though I don’t know from whom she heard.”
No matter how he thought about it, there was no one who would convey news about him to her side.
“Anyway, thinking of my deceased friend, I feel I can’t just stand by and watch his family end up like this, but my own situation is dire, so there’s nothing I can do. All I can do is offer empty blessings, hoping a good match appears and she finds a happy home. What else is within my power? Even if we met, it would only extend the tragedy of five years ago into the present; there would be no benefit for either of us…”
Leaving these final words, Ch’ansu stood up. Munhŭi made no further attempt to persuade or detain him. -(Continued)- <105>

**Summary**

Ch'ansu explains to Munhŭi the painful history behind his broken engagement to Chŏngnye five years prior. The engagement fell apart after the sudden death of Chŏngnye's brother, leading her distraught mother to oppose the match due to Ch'ansu's financial status and role as an eldest son (making him unsuitable as a potential live-in son-in-law). Chŏngnye eventually distanced herself, seemingly becoming involved with another man, culminating in a public humiliation for Ch'ansu orchestrated by her mother. He reveals he recently learned from Munhŭi that Chŏngnye is back from studying/working in Japan, now impoverished and caring for her terminally ill mother. While feeling some pity due to past affection and his connection to her deceased brother, Ch'ansu concludes that meeting her again would serve no purpose but to revisit past pain.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the social and economic pressures faced by young Koreans in the 1930s under Japanese rule. It highlights the significant weight of family considerations (parental approval, financial stability, eldest son's duties vs. the *terilsawi* option) in marriage decisions, the economic vulnerability that could push women into difficult situations (working, relying on potentially unreliable male support for education abroad), and the intense emotional consequences of navigating these complex social expectations within a changing colonial society. The mention of specific locations like Chongno and Hyojadong, details about salaries (*wŏn*), and the discussion of studying in Tokyo ground the narrative in the realities of the period.

「우리 어머니께서 자네 월급이 적다고 실죽해 하시기에 견습으로 사십오원이요. 년년히 십원씩 올러가면 다섯해 안에 백원이라고 풍을 좀 첫네. 그쯤 알아두게」
「삼복지경에 수은주(水銀柱) 을러가는 셈일세 그려. 넘우 올러가다가는 떠러지는 법이니!」
피차에 이러케 웃고 헤어젓스나 찬수는 그때부터 삼십오원이란 월급에 창피도 늣겻지마는 결혼문제를 최후에 결정해 준 동긔의 한아가 친구의 호의로 입으로만 올려준 십원에 잇섯다는 것을 머리에 삭엿고 그러기에 오늘날까지도 오십원짜리 신랑이라는 말을 문희에게 하는 것이다. 그것은 실제 생활분제로 부득이한 일이나 œ 쾌치는 못한 일이다.
아무턴지 그 후 삼사삭은 찬수의 행복한 시절이엇다. 전문학교에 못갓서도 실사회에 접촉하는 흥미와 신선미가 잇고 거긔다가 정례가튼 미인과 약혼이 순조로 되엇스니 인생의 광명이 일시에 비치는 듯 십헛다. 정례의 집에 차저가고 불려가고 하는 동안에 사랑은 자라가고 깁허갓다. 동모로의 애정이 련인으로의 애정으로 변하야 갓다. 더욱히 신랑도 아즉 나이 어리지만 신부편에서도 홀어머니가 딸을 급히 내노키도 실코 일즉 시집보내면 어린것을 고생시킨다고 래년 봄, 가을에나 혼인을 하겟다는 것이 두 남녀에게는 소위 교제하는 동안을 늘려주는 것 같어서 도리어 조핫다. 그리하야 단꿈은 점점 깁허갈 뿐이엇다.
그러나 그것도 심원석이가 살아 잇슬 동안의 일이엇다. 그가 불시에 횡사를 하얏다는 것만도 청천벽력이엇지마는 찬수에게는 뒤를 니어 두번재 벼략이 떠러젓든 것이엇다.

화긔에 뜬 모친을 위로하자면 순종하는 수밧게 업섯다. 맛나지 말라는 모친의 엄명과 감시를 어기고 피하는 도리가 업섯다.

치운 겨울밤 찬수는 거리를 오락가락하며 정례가 나오기를 기다리고 잇섯다. 그러나 거긔에는 자긔와 똑가튼 검은 그림자가 또한아 잇는 것을 찬수는 몰랏다. 눈이 빠지게 기다리든 녀자는 상점문밧글 나서자 진열장을 무심히 드려다 보며 섯는 보지 못하든 남자에게 눈웃음을 처보이고는 정류장으로 가는 것이엇다. 그대로 남자는 뒤따라가서 나란히 서며 비롯오 이야기를 하얏다. 뒤에서 이 거동을 보든 찬수의 눈은 별안간 뜨거워젓다. 돌쳐스랴다가 그대로 젊은 객긔에 쪼차가서 녀자편으로 나란히 섯다. 정례는 깜짝 놀랏다. 그 놀라는 표정이 마치 총충이나 노래기를 목덜미에서 떠러버리는 처녀의 긔색가타얏다. 찬수는 절망하얏다. 그 남자는 저편으로 도라섯다.

그런데 오늘 이 집에 와서 거진 오년만에 정례가 사진으로서 찬수압헤 홀연히 나타낫고 맛나고 십다는 소식을 들은 것이다.
