Emergence of the Maxim Gorky

The details [內部內容 - inferred] of the Soviet Union's propaganda airplane ANT-20 "Maxim Gorky," which was famously built to commemorate the 40th [四十 - inferred] anniversary of the literary career of world-renowned literary giant Maxim Gorky, born of Soviet Russia, have recently been photographed and made public (recently officially announced).

This aircraft was designed with the utmost devotion by Chief Engineer A. N. Tupolev [아·엔·츠─폴레푸 - inferred] of the Moscow Central Aerohydrodynamic Institute. It is a new model aircraft [新型機 - inferred] reported to have a wingspan [翼長 - inferred] of 64 meters, an overall length [全長 - inferred] of 35 meters, eight engines (7,000 horsepower), and a speed of 220 [二百二十 - inferred] to 240 kilometers per hour.

The special feature of this aircraft lies in the point [點 - inferred] that it is richly equipped with propaganda tools; not only does it have cinema and radio capabilities, but even a rotary press is installed inside the fuselage to print newspapers [新聞 - inferred], and it can accommodate a large number of 83 passengers. It is a very novel concept [構想 - inferred]. With this, it will be possible to frequently spread socialist [社會主義 - inferred] education to every corner of the vast territory, so it is imagined that the future activities of this aircraft will be considerably [相當 - inferred] effective. Now, dissecting the interior of the sky giant, the Maxim Gorky, reveals: "Salon," "Navigator's Seat," "Cockpit," "Passenger Cabin," "Radio Transmission Room [無線發信室 - inferred]," "Internal Telephone Exchange [機內電話局 - inferred]," "Dining Room [食堂 - inferred]," "Typewriter Room," "Bar [酒房 - inferred]," "Reading Room [讀書室 - inferred]," "Cinema Room," "Bathroom," "Radio Reception Room," "Printing Room," "Cloakroom [衣裳室 - inferred]," "Lower Deck [階下 - inferred]," "Central Power Plant," "Photographic Darkroom [寫眞用暗室 - inferred]," "Bedroom," "Baggage Room," "Fuel Tank," "Flight Engineer's Seat," etc. [等 - inferred].

**Summary**

This 1935 article from the Korean magazine *Kaebyŏk* reports on the Soviet ANT-20 "Maxim Gorky" aircraft, built to commemorate the writer's 40-year literary career. It details the plane's specifications (size, engines, speed) and highlights its extensive propaganda features, including a printing press, radio, and cinema, designed to spread socialist education across the USSR. The article lists numerous compartments within the large aircraft. For historians, this piece is interesting as it shows a Korean publication under Japanese colonial rule reporting relatively neutrally, even with a hint of awe, on a major Soviet technological and propaganda achievement. It reflects an awareness of international developments and perhaps a subtle interest in powers other than Japan, despite the prevailing anti-communist atmosphere enforced by the colonial government.
