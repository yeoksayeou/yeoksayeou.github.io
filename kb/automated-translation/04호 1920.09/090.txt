Article Title: 自然觀 (View of Nature)

Article Type: 잡저 (Miscellaneous Essay)

The lark's cry comes not from its mouth, but from its entire soul (魂全軆). Among the manifestations of the soul's vibrant voice, nothing is as vigorous as that. (Kusamakura)

Nature, entering spring, is truly a benevolent mother (慈母). Humans merge with Nature, embraced in its bosom, grieving their finite lives and yearning for limitless eternity. That is, wrapped in the benevolent mother's bosom, one feels a kind of sweet sorrow. (Shizen to Jinsei)

Beneath the frozen ice, the lake's heart beats. Beneath the falling, piling snow, the volcano's passion burns. (Omoide no Ki)

The two characters for eternity (永遠) are found, I think, more readily in the bends of a great river (大河) than in the sea. Try standing perhaps by the shore of the great sea (大海); you might not grasp the sound of the gushing river water (河水). Behold the flow that quietly, ceaselessly flows, flows, and flows without limit. 『逝者如斯』 (The passing [is] like this [river]) – truly, think of the flow as it flows from hundreds of millions of years past to hundreds of millions of years hence, flowing through infinite space (스페-쓰), flowing endlessly. (Shizen to Jinsei)

Great Nature can make humans look back despondently at their own empty shadow and weep. However, the human subjective view offers salvation from these painful tears. (Doppo Shizenkan)

Nature that engulfs (吐呑) all humankind, infinite and boundless Nature! I am within you. Living, I am within you, and dead, I shall also be within you.<54>

**Summary**

This article presents a collection of musings on nature, portraying it as an eternal, powerful, and all-encompassing force, often using metaphors like a 'benevolent mother' or an endlessly flowing river to represent eternity. It contrasts nature's infinite character with the limited span of human life, evoking feelings of sorrow and human insignificance, yet also suggests that the human subjective perspective can provide solace or salvation from this existential angst. Historically, this piece from the early colonial era (1920) published in *Gaebyeok* is notable for its focus on nature, eternity, and the human spirit. This inward turn towards philosophical and natural themes, while heavily citing Japanese literary influences, might represent a subtle search for enduring values or national identity amidst the political suppression of the time, making it interesting to historians studying cultural responses to colonialism.

自然觀
종달의 우는 것은 입으로 우는 것이 아니라. 魂全軆가 우는 것일다. 魂의 活動聲에 나타난 속에서 저와가티 元氣잇는 것은 업다. (草枕)

自然은 봄에 들어서 正이 慈母일다. 사람은 自然과 融合하야 自然의 품속에 싸여서 限잇는 人生을 슬퍼하며 限업는 永遠을 사모한다. 即 慈母의 품속에 싸여서 一種 단 것 가튼 悲哀를 感하는 것이라. (自然과 人生)

얼어부튼 얼음미테 湖水의 맘은 뛰고. 내려 싸이는 눈미테 火山의 情은 탄다. (思出의 記)

永遠의 二字는 바다 보다도 차라리 大河의 구비에 잇다고 생각한다. 試컨대 或 大海의 구비에 서서 決決한 河水의 音을 모를 것이다. 고요히 쉬지 아니하고 흐르고 흘러 흐르기 限이 업는 흐름을 보시요. 『逝者如斯』 實로 億萬萬年의 예로부터 億萬萬年의 뒤에 이르기까지. 無限의 스페-쓰를 흐르고 흘러서 限업시 흘러갈 때의 흐름을 생각하라. (自然과 人生)

큰 自然은 能히 人生으로 하야곰 黯然히 自身의 空影을 둘아다 보고 울게 한다. 그러나 人類的 主觀은 이 괴로운 눈물로 부터 救함이라. (獨步自然觀)

왼 人間을 吐呑하는 自然, 無限無窮의 自然이어. 나 너의 속에 잇다. 살아서 너의 속에 잇고. 죽어서 또한 너의 속에 잇슬 것이다.＜54＞
