**Body Text Translation:**
The Foot-Injured Pilgrim Girl
                        Hwang Sŏgu

O girl, girl hobbling with an injured foot,
O girl, the evening sun, over the high pass lush with grapevines like iron screens (鐵屛)
and sandalwood leaves,
like being snatched by a hungry kite
wandering over the corpses of those who died in the fields,
has gone off, carried away to whose bosom,
to a place whose name is unknown.
Ah, the earth bustles clamorously like a house of mourning (喪家), impossible to collect oneself.
Ah, girl, where are you going at such a time?

O girl, girl hobbling with an injured foot,
You are the flame of life's highest candle (燭).
Ah, you are like the gleam in a saint's (聖者) eye
shining on the clean streets, the fortress walls of Heaven,
An eternal lamp (常夜燈) of soul (魂), love (愛), strength (힘).

O girl, girl hobbling with an injured foot,
O girl, in the sticky breeze of the summer dusk, like the deep, growling laughter of the devil's (惡魔) jealousy (嫉妬),
the small cross (十字架),
worthy of being called the key to a maiden's (處女) fair breast,
hanging on a silver chain (銀쇠사실) ring (指環),
shaking forlornly
like a pilgrim's (巡禮者) bell (鈴), as if boasting,
The evening sun has completely hidden its tracks (蹤迹).
At such a time, where are you going, ah.

O girl, girl hobbling with an injured foot,
O girl, dispatched by God, heading somewhere,
as if chasing after someone,
or as if going to meet someone, bumping into anything yet
with a great, cautious (愼) silence (沈默) that does not bow,
carrying one small, red ticket (틕켓트) of destiny,
The evening sun has completely hidden its tracks (蹤迹).
O girl, where are you going at such a time? <82>

**Summary**

This 1920 poem by Hwang Sŏgu depicts a young pilgrim girl with an injured foot, walking alone near Munsan Station at dusk. Despite her suffering and the surrounding chaotic, dark world (likened to a house of mourning, filled with devilish jealousy), she is portrayed as a symbol of purity, spiritual strength, and enduring hope – described as the "highest candle," an "eternal lamp of soul, love, strength," and carrying a "ticket of red destiny." The poem contrasts her vulnerability with her silent, unbowed determination as she proceeds toward an unknown destination while the sun sets. For historians, the poem offers a glimpse into the Korean psyche during the early colonial period. The imagery of suffering, resilience, uncertain journeys, and the potentially allegorical figure of the injured pilgrim girl could reflect the national situation under Japanese rule. The use of Christian symbolism (pilgrim, cross, Heaven, saint) within this context is also noteworthy.
