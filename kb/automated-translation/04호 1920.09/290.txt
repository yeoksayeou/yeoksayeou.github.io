기사제목	上海로부터 漢城까지 (From Shanghai to Hansŏng)
필자	天友
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

From Shanghai to Hansŏng
                        Ch'ŏnu

The cry of the lone wild goose, *jjururuk kiruruk*, flying towards the northern sky, abruptly awakens my longing bundle of hometown dreams. In the dim, empty room as dusk falls over the west wall, the crescent moon, like a broken-off piece, endlessly illuminates the faces of my beloved parents. Ah, no matter what, there is none more beloved than parents; ah, no matter where, there is no place more yearned for than home, they say. It has already been several months since I left my hometown for Kangnam (江南), and some time has passed since I left my parents to drift like duckweed on water (萍水). The leaves that sprouted green marked spring on the Huangpu Bund (黃浦灘), and the drizzling rain was summer at Mochou Lake (莫愁湖). A cup sending off feelings while mooring at night on the Qinhuai (秦淮夜泊) cannot compare, no matter how fine, to gazing across a piece of silk brocade (一片綾羅) from atop our home's Ŭlmil Terrace (乙密臺); the Three Pools Mirroring the Moon (三潭印月) amidst the elegance of West Lake (西湖風流) cannot compare, no matter how fine, to viewing the confluence of three rivers (三江合水) below our home's T'onggun Pavilion (統軍亭). The perch of Songjiang (松江鱸魚) and the plain boiled vegetables of Wujun (吳郡素灼), no matter how fine, are not as good as the kimchi and thin soy paste soup (沉菜干漿) eaten at my home; the iron bed with gauze netting (綃紗鐵寢床) and the brocade-decorated couch of an official residence (官房錦裝榻), no matter how fine, are not as good as the thin quilt and single mattress (薄衾單褥) I slept on at my home. No matter what, if it's mine and from my place, it becomes ever more welcome, missed, beautiful, and beloved. An infinite affection (戀情) and love (愛情), beyond words, surges and stretches from the Black Dragon River (黑龍江) to the Tsushima Strait (對馬峽), from Paektu Mountain (白頭山) to Halla Mountain (漢拏山). Thus, my hometown, even if white snow covers the mountains (白雪滿山) or barren forests line every path (荒林遍逕), is more cherished than the spring light in another's garden, than the flowering trees by another's roadside. And my parents, no matter how dark their faces (얼골이 껌읏껌읏할 지라도), no matter how white their beards (수염이 희꼿희꼿할 지라도), are more beloved than another father's lively demeanor (活潑態度) or another mother's vibrant appearance (生氣로운 姿色).
Ah, my father, my mother! Ah, my countryside, my village! Ah, the scenery of my hometown that I always draw! Ah, the affection of my parents that I always love! I want to go, I want to see them, I cannot bear it,<115> I cannot forget. So, using the summer vacation (暑暇), I planned to return to Korea (還國), hoping and praying to leave quickly, as soon as possible.

Ah, tomorrow I leave! How shall I pass this one night, filled with extreme joy and extreme sorrow? My fellow students pack my luggage (行李) until late at night and shed tears of farewell (別淚) until their eyes swell. I affectionately received a cup of Shaoxing wine (紹興酒) from Hŏju-kun's firm hand, and tearfully engraved in my memory a farewell poem (送別詩) recited from Sosŏk-kun's red lips.

Knowing you return to your country tomorrow,
Tonight is the time my heart breaks.
Remember this one word at parting:
Gazing at clouds, walking under the moon, stir thoughts of each other.

I then harmonized with his rhyme:

The most unbearable thing in this human world,
Is seeing off a guest while being a guest oneself—precisely that time.
Wanting to weep but unable, unable to sing,
Mountains and passes stretch ten thousand li; upon what can I rely for thoughts?

The time was precisely August 2nd. As soon as the morning sun rose, I rubbed my eyes and got up. Without even washing my face (洗手), I just gathered my clothes haphazardly and put them on, then headed to the train station (停車場). At that time, a major conflict had erupted between the Zhili (直) and Anhui (安) cliques in Chinese politics. Xu Shuzheng (徐樹錚) of the Beijing Duan faction and Wu Peifu (吳佩孚) of the Baoding Cao camp faced off against each other. Consequently, Li Xiushan (李秀山), the Governor of Jiangsu (江蘇督) belonging to the Zhili clique, and Lu Yongxiang (盧永祥), the Governor of Zhejiang (浙江督) of the Anhui clique, also engaged in battle. The Shanghai-Nanjing line (滬寧線) from Shanghai to Nanjing and the Tianjin-Pukou line (津浦線) from Pukou to Tianjin were temporarily disrupted, plunging into a state of chaos (混沌狀態) for a while. Only recently had news spread through the newspapers that train services had resumed. Having come out so early, I found upon arrival that the morning express train (早快車) was still not running, and only the night express (夜快車) offered direct service. Since I had already come out, I couldn't just turn back, yet setting off meant facing considerable trouble (故障) midway. So, I spent that day at the New World Amusement Park (新世界遊戱場), Shanghai's finest—no, arguably China's finest—both for sightseeing and killing time, enduring the utter tediousness (支離함) and tolerating the commotion (紛雜함). Before I knew it, the day drew to a close. As the powerless sun (白日) on the horizon slowly set, lamps throughout the city (滿城燈花) flickered like flying dots, and clear songs from the upper floors (一樓淸歌) echoed intermittently. Men in red and women in green (紅男綠女) walked in pairs, matching steps, while southern and northern tunes (南腔北曲) flowed from everywhere. People bustling about, coming and going, standing around—of course, this is expected in a major world metropolis like Shanghai, but indeed, China truly has all sorts of sights. There are many idle wanderers (放蕩客) seeking pleasure, and many lewd and decadent customs (淫謔迷累 惡風俗). Perhaps this is one reason why China has become so weakened (疲弱). At ten o'clock at night, I went back to the train station.<116> Seeing few people buying tickets at the ticket window (賣票口), it seemed the departure time was near. I bought a third-class connecting ticket (三等聯絡票) from Shanghai North Station (上海北站) to Tianjin Main Station (天津總站) for 14 yuan, 7 jiao, and 5 fen. Hŏju and Sosŏk, holding platform tickets (入場券), briskly entered the platform (月臺). Only then did I truly feel like a train passenger (車中客). Soon, as 11 o'clock struck, the *peep-peep* of the steam whistle (汽笛) endlessly shattered the quiet night sentiment (沉寂 夜情), and the *hwak-hwak* sound from the iron smokestack (鐵筩 굴똑) continuously spewed out immense plumes of smoke (萬丈 氣焰).

Meanwhile, Hŏju-kun and Sosŏk-kun took off their hats and pulled out handkerchiefs, waving them with both hands, their boundless expression of affection (無限 情表) wishing me a safe journey and return, visible even from afar, felt like some kind of club striking my chest, making me shed hot tears I couldn't hold back.

Ah, I am going. I am going. Leaving behind friends who came with me,
I go alone. I go alone.
Who among my companions wouldn't want to go home?
Which child wouldn't want to see their parents?
But wanting to go, they cannot go; wanting to see, they cannot see,
Alone and lonely, they can only wring out a single stream of tears.
Ah, I am going. I am going. Leaving behind friends who were with me,
I go alone.
I will go find your home and deliver your news.
I will meet your beloved and convey your feelings (정지).
Though I convey the news well and tell of your feelings well,
The listener's heart will only be pierced by a handful of sorrow (서름).
Ah, I am going. I am going. Abandoning friends I played with,
I go alone. I go alone.
Still, still, do not cry.
I will return soon (곳 단여오리라). The long, long road to Hanyang Castle (漢陽城),
When I come back and see you, and seeing me, you rejoice, telling of our homeland's situation (故國事情),
Will you then, listening, cry!? Or laugh!?

And that night, whether I slept or not, whether I was going or coming, I knew nothing, just sat there numbly. The next day, at 6 AM, we reached the Xiaguan river mouth (下關 江口) on the Nanjiang River. Immediately boarding a connecting ferry (聯絡船), I crossed the Yangtze River (楊子江) and arrived at Pukou Station (浦口驛). The train bound for Tianjin (天津行 列車) was already waiting for passengers (乘客). Hurrying my steps, I entered the train car (車內). The irregular seating (不規則 坐席) and untidily arranged luggage (沒整頓 行李) were placed haphazardly. However, being small-bodied, where couldn't I squeeze in? I settled into a narrow gap in a corner. Opening the train window (車窓) and looking ahead, the tightly encircled<117> Iron Urn City (鐵甕城) over there was indeed the scenery of Jinling (金陵), famous site of the Six Dynasties (六朝名勝). Seeing the finest place in Jiangnan (江南 甲地), how could I spare a single phrase of writing?

The high tower at the North Pole receives the clearing rain,
The kingly aura of Jinling remains bright.
Ancient ivy-covered trees stand unevenly dense,
One melody from Qin Lake evokes ten thousand li of feeling.

The train departed from Pukou around 9 AM. After eating breakfast (朝飯) in the car, I sat holding a newspaper. The jolting journey soon brought us to Xuzhou Station (徐州站). A few hundred li east of Xuzhou lies Huaiyin County (淮陰縣), the birthplace of the ancient Han Xin (韓信). The Shrine of the Bleaching Woman (漂母祠) and the Crawling-Under-the-Legs Bridge (跨下橋) are there. At this, I thought: that Han Xin was truly great. Though I never saw him nor spoke with him, across several thousand years to this day, I hear his name, Han Xin, and can think about and speak of the history he left and the legacy (遺功) he bestowed. This is truly an interesting aspect of the human condition (人生觀 興味). Lost in thought, unaware of the train's movement, I found myself looking at Bengbu (蚌埠). Bengbu was the garrison base (屯兵處) of Ni Sichong (倪嗣忡), the Military Governor of Anhui (安徽督軍). General Ni, who had faced life and death situations, still held his position, but now found himself completely surrounded (四面楚歌), seeing enemies in every bush and tree (八公草木), unable to advance or retreat. Observing this, one wonders whether the contemporary currents of the time (現代 時勢) would permit such warlord cliques (軍閥波 무리), whose power was once imposing (威風 凜凜) and spirit dignified (氣槪 堂堂).

Before long, the sun set, and the distant mountains grew dark. A strange wind began to blow softly. Ah, this is Shandong Province (山東省), they say. Years of floods (水災) and warfare (兵災) have left millions impoverished (貧民). Some head to North Manchuria (北滿), others to the South Seas (南洋). Seeing their bluish pallor, unable to hide a single red leg, their gaunt appearance mirroring their ash-colored faces (검옷테테한 얼골빗), I vividly imagine my own elders (父老), brothers (兄弟), sisters (姊妹), and relatives (親戚), holding onto each other, parting from each other, weeping as they go. Carrying loads on their backs and heads (男負女戴), crossing the Yalu River (鴨綠江) and Tumen River (豆滿江) over the ice—their hands and feet frozen and cracked, staggering this way and that, aimlessly wandering (정처를 못하다가), sometimes collapsing on the road (道路에 거꿀어지기도), sometimes falling into ditches (溝壑에 업드러지기도)—I feel as if I can clearly see and hear it all.

The next day was August 4th. In the afternoon, we reached Jinanfu (濟南府). The terminal point of the railway extending east from Jinan is Qingdao city (靑島市) on the Jiaozhou Bay (膠海岸), which caused a major issue for East Asian peace (東洋平和), or rather, staged a great tragedy for both China and Japan (中日兩國 悲劇). I do not intend here to elaborate (敷說) on the political interests concerning the Qingdao issue (靑島 問題).

Around 4 PM that day, we arrived at Tianjin Main Station (天津總站). Dust flew so thickly one could hardly open their eyes, and the heat pressed down, making it hard to lift one's head. As soon as one steps off the train at Tianjin Station, there's one thing that truly confounds a first-time visitor (初行者):<118> the clamor of inn servants (旅館下人) employing all sorts of tactics (手段) to solicit guests (손님네 모시려고). Some grab luggage (行李를 껴드는 놈), some pull at sleeves (袖衫을 당기는 놈), some rush ahead (압서는 놈), some follow behind (뒤서는 놈)—a real commotion (야단법석) erupts. However, if you stay alert, keep your eyes wide open, and walk just a little way out of the station, you'll find inns (旅館) and lodges (客棧) standing densely packed here and there. Thus, one has considerable freedom (自由) and convenience (相當) to choose whichever place they wish. But if you follow one of those inn servants, you might face incredibly unpleasant situations (아니꼬운 꼴을 턱업시 볼 때). No matter what, while not all Chinese people—or rather, not every single Chinese person—may be like this, that one major bad habit (弊習) of deceiving others financially and being evasive (어름어름하는 것) is undeniably an uncivilized (不文明) and unscrupulous (不徹底) behavior in anyone's eyes. I once heard someone jokingly say—what was it?—that for Chinese people, regardless of status (上下 勿論), theft is their main occupation (本業), while farming, industry, and commerce (農工商) are side jobs (副業). This sounds excessively harsh, but I personally wish this inherited bad habit (遺傳性 惡習) could be fundamentally eradicated (根本的 撲滅).

Around 12 AM that night, as I prepared to depart Tianjin for Fengtian (奉天), due to the military curfew (軍事戒嚴中), the luggage (行李) of all arriving and departing passengers (來人去客) was inspected. My luggage contained only a few books I studied with, some clothes I wore, and a student ID card (學生證). Without further ado, I was allowed to pass, but others seemed to undergo truly harsh scrutiny (刻甚 査實) without any regard for feelings (客氣업시).

The next morning, we reached Shanhaiguan (山海關). Shanhaiguan is said to be the starting point (起點) of the Great Wall built during the Qin Dynasty (秦時 萬里長城). A little further in is Qinhuangdao (秦皇島). Just hearing the name Qinhuangdao gives a general idea (大畧) of its meaning. According to popular legend (俗談), Qin Shi Huang (秦始皇) traveled there, held back the sun (白日 挽), and whipped the blue stones (靑石 鞭). Opening the train window and looking out, I saw white sand exposing bones (白沙露骨) and green grass covering withered mounds (靑草枯塚). Originally, the Beijing-Fengtian line (京奉線) had the widest gauge (軌道) in East Asia. Thus, the train speed (火車速力) seemed comparatively fast. Ah, the sun hanging on the western mountains (西山) was setting slowly. The evening smoke (져녁 煙氣) surrounding the Tianhou Temple Pagoda (天后宮塔) in Jinzhou City (錦州城) dissipated along with the cries of birds returning to Bailu Temple (白鹿寺) on Guangling Mountain (廣陵山).

It was around 7 PM that day. Stepping off at Fengtian Station (奉天驛), I couldn't find a single friend, nor anyone to rely on. So, I hailed an easy-to-find and compliant rickshaw (人力車) and went to an inn (旅館). That evening, during the long night, I couldn't sleep if I tried, nor could I stop if I wanted to. From a house across the way, the sound of a small hourglass drum (새장고 소리) carried on the wind, pleasantly stimulating my eardrums, weary from days of travel (連日 疲困 耳膜). Then, somehow, I fell asleep and woke up after a while. It was now August<119> 6th. Being in Fengtian, one must visit the Northern Tomb (北陵). The green pines (蒼松) were dense (鬱密), and the stone paths (石逕) were deserted (寥落). Traces of 200 years of emperors (帝王 蹟) still remained. The stone figures, stone horses, stone lions, stone jackals, and tigers (石人 石馬 石獅子 石豺 虎) truly allow one to sufficiently imagine (足히 推想) how developed the craftsmanship (工藝) of that era was. Embracing the setting sun (夕陽), I descended the mountain path (山路). Suddenly struck by a thought (무슨 생각 갑작히 突出인지), I plopped down on a rock (巖上), pointed towards the southern sky (南天), and spoke words of endless sorrow:

Where do they send people after killing them?
Is it Heaven (天堂)? Hell (地獄)? Hometown (故鄕)? Or a foreign land (客地)?
Old and young, all follow the same path,
Resentfully and sorrowfully shedding tears of blood (피눈물),
Their limbs cut off (손발은 잘리이고), their marrow pierced (뼈ㅅ속을 쑤시이되),
But the soul (魂) that cannot even be seen, who can know!?

Then, standing up, I thought for a long while before returning to the inn with weary steps. After spending several days like this, it was suddenly August 11th.

Taking the 9 AM train directly to Kyŏngsŏng (京城), my lonely self, without companions, sat on the train. Now it was farewell even to Fengtian. Just seeing the black smoke of Shenyang Castle (瀋陽城) felt dreadful.

The dog that rushed forward
As if to bite and tear with gnashed teeth,
When shown the intent to give it
A piece of meat, two pieces of candy, saying "Here!",
Wags its tail, turns around,
And grants passage ahead.

Meanwhile, we quickly passed Benxihu (本溪湖) and Qiaotou Station (橋頭驛), and around 2 PM, we arrived at Jiguan Mountain (鷄冠山). Military police (憲兵) and regular police (巡査) stood stiffly here and there.
Looking up at that Jiguan Mountain, I felt limitless emotion (限업는 늣김). I recalled the history (古事) of the Russo-Japanese War (日俄戰爭). Indeed, it is a mountain where countless bloody corpses (血尸) are buried. It was 5 PM. Ah, we had arrived at Andong County (安東縣). Ah, I crossed the Yalu River (鴨綠江)! The hazy mist on Yuanbao Mountain (元寶山), the clear air of Zhenjiang Garden (鎭江園)—I felt them endlessly, saw them fleetingly. How many tears shed, how much sweat dropped, drop by drop (點點滴滴), rusts the large iron bridge red.

Ah, one Mugunghwa flower (無窮花 한송이)
Fell from somewhere,
Beaten by rain and blown by wind,
Rolling this way and that,
Is it a flower (꼿) or dirt (흙)?
Who could possibly know?<120>

Ah, now I have finally stepped onto the soil of my homeland (故國 땅). Sinŭiju Station (新義州驛). The wide fields of Kwangsŏngmyŏn (光城面) are impressively vast. Paengma Station (白馬驛).

Ah, welcome, welcome! Paengma Mountain Fortress (白馬山城)! Have you been well?
And General Im (林將軍) too. Ah, Paengma Mountain Fortress!
With what meaning do you bow your head low to me?
What plea (陳情) are you trying to make to me so earnestly?
The one who cut down every single tree on your body and ate them clean,
And the one who drove triangular nails (三角釘) firmly into your head—
Can you remember them? Tell me what you know!
I will make a life-or-death decision (死生決斷하오리라), to what end?

As I spoke these words, we passed Sŏnch'ŏn (宣川) and Chŏngju (定州), arriving near Kasan (嘉山). "Perhaps this vicinity is T'aech'ŏn county (泰川 고을)? Is the home of my beloved P-hyŏng in front of or behind that Chajabong Mountain (茄子峯山)?" I opened the train window (車窓) and gazed out with interest (趣味). Gazing endlessly, I didn't even notice the coal dust (石炭가루) settling on my head. Turning my head away, tired and weak, I closed my eyes and fell asleep. It was around 11 PM. We arrived at P'yŏngyang Station (平壤驛), they said. Only the rumbling sound of crossing the Taedong River Iron Bridge (大洞江 鉄橋). Just going and sleeping, sleeping and going.

The next day was August 12th. The weather was clear (淸朗), and pieces of cloud like white cotton (白綿) formed shapes, sometimes cotton balls, sometimes dogs, rising lazily from the head of the elegant and beautiful Samgak Mountain (三角山). Around 7 AM. We arrived unceremoniously at Namdaemun Station (南大門驛). Since my arrival was unexpected (期約치 못하엿던 터), no one came out to meet me (迎接). Immediately boarding a rickshaw (人力車), I briskly entered the hometown house I always thought and spoke of. Overcome with joy (넘우나 반갑던 김). My father (아버니) and elder brother (兄님) were out somewhere; only my mother (어머님) received my affection-filled greeting. She was too choked up (억색하야) to speak, but how could she suppress her longing affection (정리)? From that day on, I became part of my hometown. I became a child of the house. Eating my fill of white rice and plain vegetables (白飯素菜) deliciously prepared by my mother's hands, I played outside during the day (낫이면) and came home to sleep at night.

August 15th was a Sunday. Receiving an earnest invitation (招待) from my friends, we packed wine (술) and side dishes (안주), filled with soaring spirits (逸興 陶陶), and went out as a group of 10 (10人 隊) to Ch'ŏngnyangsa Temple (淸凉寺) for a picnic. A gentle breeze (薰風) blew softly (徐來), and the tree shade (樹陰) was sparse (稀薄). It was a refined gathering (깨끗한 坐席) with Messrs. O, L, T, P, etc., known for their elegant writing and demeanor (文章 雅儀). Mr. O gave me a delightful poem (글 한 首):

A distant traveler drunk in the temple—
How does it compare to roaming Shanghai (滬上游)?

At that time, whether fitting or not, I regret (遺憾) not being able to immediately compose a harmonizing reply (和答).<121> Perhaps due to being drunk and forgetting everything (醉中除萬事), I became unaware of my surroundings. However, engraving it deeply in my heart, the memory surfaces again now as I write this.

At parting, I always pondered talks for when we meet;
On another day, I will further recall this day's excursion (此日游).

And that night, I returned home extremely drunk (몹시 醉하야). Somehow (엇지된 판인지), I couldn't lift my head and just collapsed onto my bedding. "Ah, what happened? You said you were going home, have you returned already? Did you have a good trip? How wonderful was your hometown? How happy were your parents?" Hearing Sosŏk-kun's words, I couldn't answer, and only after a while did my mouth open. Grasping Sosŏk-kun's wrist, I said, "I saw your father too. The whole family (渾家) is well," and as I was about to continue speaking, the arm resting on my forehead slipped off onto the pillow, and my eyes flew open. Ah! It was all a dream! It seems it was a dream! <122>

**Summary**

This article by Ch'ŏnu recounts his emotional journey from Shanghai back to his hometown, Hansŏng (Seoul), during the summer vacation of 1920. He expresses deep nostalgia for his home, parents, and Korean culture, contrasting them favorably with sights and experiences in China (Jiangnan, West Lake, Qinhuai). The journey itself is marked by disruptions due to warlord conflicts in China, observations on Chinese society, and reflections on historical sites like the Great Wall and places associated with Han Xin and Qin Shi Huang. Crossing the Yalu River marks a poignant return to Korean soil, evoking memories and sorrow related to Korea's situation, particularly symbolized by Paengma Mountain Fortress and the Mugunghwa flower. The narrative culminates in a joyous reunion with his mother, leisurely days at home, a drunken outing with friends, and a final dream sequence where he interacts with his friend still in Shanghai, highlighting the lingering connection and perhaps the unreality of his fulfilled longing.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into Korean student life abroad in the early colonial period, the difficulties of travel between China and Korea (including political instability in China and inspections), and the strong sense of national identity and longing for home experienced by Koreans under Japanese rule. The references to Chinese warlord politics (Zhili-Anhui conflict), the Qingdao issue, Japanese police presence in Korea (憲兵巡査 at 鷄冠山), and the symbolic reflections on historical sites like Paengma Mountain Fortress provide glimpses into the broader political and social context of the time. Historians might find the detailed descriptions of travel conditions, the author's emotional responses to landscapes imbued with national significance, and the subtle commentary on both Chinese society and the Korean situation particularly interesting.

上海로부터 漢城까지
                        天友
北天으로 날아오는 쭈루룩 끼루룩 외마리 기럭이 소리는 나의 그리워하는 고향 꿈뭉치를 덧업시 깨치도다. 西城으로 넘어가는 어스름 뷘 방, 한조각 귀떨어진 달은 나의 사랑하는 부모님 얼골을 끗업시 빗치도다. 아- 무엇무엇 하여도 부모밧게 더 사랑할 이 업스며 아- 어대어대 하여도 고향밧게 더 그리운 곳 업스리라 한다. 내가 지금 고향을 떠나 江南을 온 지도 아마 몃달이 넘엇으며 부모를 여워 萍水를 딸은 지도 또한 얼마가 되엇도다. 파릇파릇 피던 입새 黃浦灘의 봄날이며 부슬부슬 오던 비는 莫愁湖의 여름이다. 秦淮夜泊의 一杯送情은 암만 하여도 우리집 乙密臺 우에서 一片綾羅를 건너봄만 갓지 못하며 西湖風流의 三潭印月은 암만 하여도 우리집 統軍亭 아레로 三江合水를 구경함만 갓지 못하다. 松江의 鱸魚와 吳郡의 素灼은 암만 하여도 우리집에서 먹던 沉菜干漿만 못하고 綃紗의 鐵寢床과 官房의 錦裝榻은 암만 하여도 우리집에서 자던 薄衾單褥만 못하다. 암만암만 하여도 제것과 제해라면 더욱더욱 반갑고 그립고 아름답고 사랑스런 無限의 戀情 愛情의 무엇이라 말할 수 업시 저ㅣ 黑龍江으로 對馬峽까지 白頭山으로 漢拏山까지 쏘치고 뻐치도다. 그리하야 나의 고향은 白雪이 滿山일지라도 荒林이 遍逕일지라도 남의 동산의 봄철 빗보다 남의 길가에 꼿나무보다 그리고 또 나의 부모는 암만 얼골이 껌읏껌읏할 지라도 암만 수염이 희꼿희꼿할 지라도 남의 아버지의 活潑한 態度보다 남의 어머니의 生氣로운 姿色보다.
아- 나의 아버지 나의 어머니 아- 나의 시골 나의 마을 아- 나의 일상 그려하는 고향의 경치 아- 나의 매양 사랑하는 내 부모의 정디 가고 십고 보고 십허 참을 수 업고＜115＞ 이즐 수 업다. 그래서 나는 暑暇임을 利用하야 還國키를 圖謀하니 速히 하로 바삐 떠나기를 바라고 또 바랏더라.
아- 來日은 떠나도다. 깃븜도 極하고 서운도 極한 오늘밤 하로를 엇더케 지내일가? 나의 同學 친구들은 밤이 맛도록 行李를 묵거주고 눈이 붓도록 別淚를 뿌려준다. 虛舟군의 구든 손으로 주는 紹興酒 한 盞을 정답게 바드으며 素石君의 붉은 입으로 읇는 送別詩 한 首를 눈물로 새겻도다.
