公園情操, 夏夜의 各 公園 (Park Sentiments, Various Parks on a Summer Night)
						잔물 (Chanmul)
塔洞 公園 (T'apkol Park)
The sun has set!
What welcome and energizing news this is for the citizens who have sweltered all day long in the boiling intense heat and rotten steam. When the sun, which seemed to test endurance by shooting down its fire from the center of Seoul Chang'an, nestled in the basin between Namsan and Pugaksan, sinks behind the head of Kŭmhwasan outside Saemun Pakk, people emerge bustling onto the streets of Seoul, and the number of pedestrians suddenly increases.
"That fearsome sun has finally set, hasn't it!" they seem to say with their faces, donning cool, new ramie *choui* (outer coats) and walking slowly, unhurriedly.
Then, as if saying, "Come on in—," the round electric lights of T'apkol Park, the house of green shade, flicker on.
For the citizens of Kyŏngsŏng, who suffer through the boiling heat in narrow, complex, and dusty alleys, lacking even a nearby place to cool off, how truly dear and cool, though small, is this treasured space, T'apkol Park.

It was nearly nine o'clock, quite dark, when K and I bought fruit at the night market and entered the park.
As soon as we entered the gate, our bodies were already in the green shade, and a light, refreshing coolness began to gently permeate our minds. Walking under the shade of the cherry trees stretching out straight from the left and right, stepping on the shadows of countless leaves spread across the path leading straight to the P'algakchŏng (Octagonal Pavilion), felt like swimming in the cool water of a pond beneath willows. <96>
The electric light hit the leaves, drenched in moisture as if water droplets were about to fall, making them shine bluer than phosphorescence. Yet, the shade behind the leaves and under the trees was as dark as could be, which alone created a deep atmosphere and added layers of coolness. Seeing the shadows of those trees and leaves softly and lightly floating on the ground, as if they would fly away if blown upon, made one feel like simply sitting there and playing. Walking carefully, lightly, with delicate steps as if they might break if stepped upon, we saw a young woman, who must have arrived some time earlier and was already on her way back, accompanied by a young girl, approaching gracefully from the corner of the P'algakchŏng. The way those soft leaf shadows coolly flickered upon her neat white clothes, sometimes even dancing across her pale face, was an indescribably beautiful scene.

At night, the P'algakchŏng looked larger and more ancient, standing tall and somber, holding a dozen or so people dressed in white. On the lawn spread around it, the electric light flowed, pooling brightly. Young middle school students, vocational school students, gentlemen, men wearing *kat* (traditional hats), men in Western suits—various sorts of people strolled slowly, circling around, some dragging walking sticks, others holding fans, some carrying transverse flutes, as if looking for someone. Whenever I come here, this park always feels like the garden of some mansion. The scarcity of places to sit and places to go makes the people wandering back and forth outside the lawn or under the trees reinforce this feeling. If only the sound of a piano could be heard from some corner of this park, the feeling of strolling through a rear garden would be even more complete.
We turned west in front of the P'algakchŏng, slipped into the tree shade, crossed the bridge over the pond, and sat on a bench under the vines by the pond's edge. It's rather amusing that Seoul students call this pond Purinji (Pond of Intolerance) and the bridge Kwanwŏlgyo (Moon Viewing Bridge), like the one on the cover of Ch'u Wŏlsaek's novel; it seems fitting for young students. They supposedly often arrange to meet here, saying, "Let's meet at Kwanwŏlgyo tonight at such-and-such time," and then gather here before heading out again. Today, too, a student in a Hwimun school uniform was teasing the goldfish by the pond.
While we sat there briefly, countless people passed before us, walking slowly, hands clasped behind their backs, dragging walking sticks, crossing the bridge.
Suddenly! In the corner near the restaurant on the far side of the pond, under the shade of a tree, a young woman with *t'ŭre* hair (traditional chignon) in a black skirt and a tall gentleman in a Western suit were sitting affectionately <97> on a rotten pine log, catching our eye. Their presence made the scene feel profoundly fitting. But then, for some reason, the woman frequently wiped her eyes with a handkerchief. "What could be wrong?"—doubt and curiosity surged within us, but there was no way to know more. The Western-suited gentleman, who looked quite tall even sitting down, seemed embarrassed and frequently glanced around. The woman just kept her head down, rubbing her eyes with the handkerchief. While it was an interesting scene, being by the pond, the gentleman's frequent glances towards us made us feel uncomfortable, so we left.
The two-story Japanese house on the north side of the pond is a Western restaurant called "Sŭngni" (Victory). During the summer season, they sell ice cream and iced beer, and a Japanese woman with powdered neck smiles brightly from the glass window, but besides being expensive, the place somehow feels deep and stuffy, not refreshingly open, so few people go in. As we rounded the corner and were about to emerge onto the path leading clearly to the P'algakchŏng, Ch'oe the photographer and Kim from Hamhŭng, sitting on a plank bench there, saw us and stood up, saying, "Hey, where are you coming from?" The four of us gathered together and sat on the plank bench, beginning to eat the fruit.
Here, in front of the restaurant, sits a small pine tree, and next to it, under a tall tree with small leaves whose name I don't know, is where we sat, with the P'algakchŏng visible at an angle. In front of us, two pine log stumps were embedded by the roadside...
Sitting here and looking at the P'algakchŏng, one can discover another world, secretly delicious and full of charm. Inside the P'algakchŏng, an old-style lamp hangs from the ceiling. Having not been cleaned for a long time, it's covered in cobwebs and dust, thoroughly aged and musty, casting a dim glow. Viewed from this spot, sitting on the plank bench under the tall tree next to the pine near the Sŭngni restaurant, a small part of the lamp is visible through the gaps in the decorative railing above the thick beam resting horizontally on the pavilion pillars. In the dimness, seeing about half of the lamp's glass looks just like an affectionate autumn lamp subtly shining through the paper sliding door (*miadaji*) of a lonely thatched cottage at the foot of some mountain or a separate cottage in the rear garden of some great house. The more I look, the more it seems so, reminding me of the stories I often heard as a child—about a young, talented scholar who came to Seoul for the state examination and found love with a maiden studying in a cottage in the rear garden of a great house. The subtly visible lamp looks exactly like a brightly lit lamp seen through a cottage's paper door, and it almost feels as if a pure maiden is inside, and her clear, resonant voice reading aloud can almost be heard. And somehow, it even seems like <98> two pairs of shoes, a man's and a woman's, are placed before the cottage door, revealing a separate, affectionate, poetic world of stories. Ch'oe gazed blankly and said, "Ah, it reminds me of my country home!" Sitting here, looking like this, a sentimental, autumn-like feeling, like falling leaves, already envelops the heart.

The night deepened considerably.
After ten o'clock, as if by agreement, more people pour into this park. This is because many people come after wandering through the night market or finishing their evening tasks. Among them, prostitutes soliciting customers and cheap *kisaeng* also flock in around this time.
We left that spot, passed by the Stone Pagoda (Sŏkt'ap), and peeked into the desolate and dark pavilion standing in the weed patch in the western corner near the park's North Gate. Because this place is secluded, lonely, and rough, during the day, laborers from the vicinity gather here, naturally forming a spot to exchange stories about workplaces and experiences elsewhere. Tonight, too, it was another world; about ten laborers, odd job workers, and struggling students were gathered, holding court. A man in a Western suit, neat among them, about 40 years old, was talking incessantly about his time earning money in America, while the others listened intently. Though a pitch-black electric wire hung from the ceiling, there was no light bulb, just a white fixture dangling. From amidst the seated group, questions like "So, what do people eat to live there?" and "How much can you earn per day?" were earnestly exchanged in frustrating question-and-answer sessions, making one's heart feel somehow sorrowful. Within this small park, containing the P'algakchŏng and the Western restaurant, this pavilion distinctly opened up a different world.
From there, we turned east again, circled behind the Stone Pagoda, and went towards the Music Hall. The cool-looking Music Hall and its electric lights stood empty and vast, while many people in ramie clothes sat under the wisteria vines behind it. The branch stall of the Sŭngni-ya (Victory House), occupying a part of the wisteria arbor yet feigning ignorance, still looked somewhat cool. We sat on a plank bench in front of it, also. Looking over, we saw the *t'ŭre*-haired female student and the Western-suited gentleman sitting affectionately on a plank bench opposite the Sŭngni branch stall, in front of the Music Hall. Seeing the tall stature of the gentleman, we realized it was the pair who had been shedding tears by the pond earlier. How strange! The woman, who had been wiping her tears with a handkerchief, nudged the gentleman's arm. He quickly took out a cigarette, offered it to her, even struck a match for her, and smoke <99> curled up from the tip of the woman's nose. "What could it be?"—suspicion arose instantly, but I concluded she must be a prostitute and let it go. Why was she crying? There was no reason to know, but the tall gentleman's not overly vulgar face looked quite pitiful. Every passerby in a Western suit looked intently at this couple as they went by. The couple paid them no mind. The gentleman sat with his long legs stretched out, holding his walking stick between them with both hands, just looking at the ground, while the woman, sitting shoulder-to-shoulder with him, whispered incessantly.
Wondering what they were talking about made me unreasonably curious, but sitting there with no way to know, a youth in a cap and *choui* coat passed by. Seeing the scene, he approached the couple as if he had something to ask, stopping about three feet in front of them in an attention-like stance (氣着姿勢), staring intently. Then, another student passing behind him also stopped and watched. The couple simultaneously saw the unexpected intruders. However, the tall gentleman's head drooped again, and only the woman stared back with wide eyes. After staring at each other for a while, the capped youth continued on his way. Then the student who had arrived later also stealthily went on his way.
Both Ch'oe and Kim chuckled softly.
Only the electric light of the empty Music Hall shone indifferently.
"Let's go around this way," we said and left that place too.
The area under the eastern wall of this park is dark and sunken, and occasionally, interesting stories arise here.
From near the park's East Gate to the place where the Turtle Stele (Kubi) stands, the dark area allows electric light to seep in only barely, making it a true realm of cool shadows (凉影), filling the ground. With *choui* coats hanging from branches and people filling every shady spot, it's here that young women, coolly powdered and made up, gather in groups of three or five, settling in the shade and asking unknown men for a cigarette light, initiating flirtations (酬酌). Whether men are drawn here because of this, or whether these women gather because men are drawn, in any case, it's said that pimply-faced men in Western suits or powdered prostitutes head straight for this side upon entering.
We could have gone straight out the Main Gate from there, but there were still two more charming spots left in this park.
Between the Main Gate and the P'algakchŏng, there are two paths opening to the west. The first path goes past the Greenhouse (花草溫室) towards the toilet and the pond. Whether entering or leaving through the Main Gate, turning onto this path reveals another world within this park, the brightest, quietest, <100> and coziest. Though not a pavilion or a shaded spot, right after turning onto that path, on the right-hand side, inside an iron fence, is a patch of grass brilliantly and pleasingly illuminated by electric light. There, in a space about two *kan* wide (approx. 12x12 ft) surrounded by tall miscellaneous trees, stand one maple tree and a couple of pine trees. Beneath them, several clumps of orchids, grown several feet tall and gracefully drooping like trumpet mouths, rest cozily and quietly. The electric light from the lamp post standing right beside seems to focus solely on this spot, not leaking elsewhere, making the light incomparably brilliant. Since no one gathers nearby to chat loudly, it's profoundly quiet and profoundly cozy. Within this complex park, this spot alone seems to sink softly into a pleasant dreamland, or perhaps it's as if these fortunate grasses, born into this blessed world, are whispering head to head. Thus, gazing at it for a while makes me want to keep listening to the quiet whispering of their special world, a world I cannot participate in.
We stood here for a long while, holding onto the iron fence, looking in enviously. Then we moved on, walking a little further down that path past the Greenhouse to the pavilion on the right side of the path. This pavilion was where, years ago, a certain eloquent friend would come every single night to tell old stories (古談), attracting people who had developed a taste for his tales, gathering nightly as if keeping office hours. The storyteller from previous years faced police crackdown (取締) and hasn't been seen since, but even now, people gather in this pavilion, taking off their *choui* coats and hanging them casually, settling down as if in their own homes' detached quarters (*sarang*), and begin discussing current events (時事). Topics range from the price of rice (米價), lightning strikes (落雷) causing harm to people and livestock (人畜) somewhere, to debating the pros and cons of Prohibition in America at the time, or discussing how France has many widows (寡婦) and which country has better-looking people, sending the conversation westward. Then the topic might return home, discussing the current movie at Tansŏngsa Theater or the circus troupe in Meijichō... Thus, the idle chatter of these anonymous guests (無名客) jumps around aimlessly. The people gathered there listen attentively, as if taking it all to heart. Around midnight, as if quitting time has arrived, they put their clothes back on and disperse. In this way, rumors (風說) spread throughout the city (市中) from the mouths of the departing people. And the next night, they gather again one by one, two by two, greeting each other as if they'd always been close friends—"Did you sleep well?" "You're here now?"—and start their talks again. Among <101> those who gather here, one often sees elderly men over 50, and sometimes people bring someone who plays the *tanso* (short bamboo flute) or *sahyŏn'gŭm* (four-stringed instrument) to listen to a clear and cool piece (1曲). Here, a separate world with this kind of unusual sentiment (情緖) opens up daily.
After visiting this pavilion too, we turned back towards the Main Gate. By the time we came out, it was quite late, but even then, there were couples resembling husband and wife entering hand-in-hand, and in the night market outside the park gate, many merchants were preparing to leave.

獎忠壇 公園 (Changch'ungdan Park)
Compared to T'apkol Park, this park feels quite park-like.
Around six in the afternoon, getting off the tram in front of the Hullyŏnwŏn (Military Training Center) grounds and heading south along the path between fields for a while, past the police substation, the distinctly park-like, clear, and cool atmosphere already rushes out to welcome you.
The very sound of women doing laundry by the considerable stream of clean water flowing down beside the path to the park is remarkably invigorating and cooling.
Passing a few Japanese houses with flower gardens, the distinctively pretty scenery of the park comes into view, and you reach the entrance marked by posts prohibiting vehicle traffic. Before even reaching those posts, stepping onto the green-painted bridge, you see the water flowing from the park's pond into the stream, sliding down a stone wall nearly eight feet high. It falls in white, noodle-like strands, making clear, distinct sounds that refresh the mind and body, instantly dispelling the sweat that had broken out all over. Standing midway on the bridge, shifting my gaze from the water, I saw weeping willows ignorant of summer, their deep shade providing a backdrop for the clear water flowing out with the cool breeze, forming a calm pond. The Western-style house and benches glimpsed through the trees, along with everything else, reflected upside down in the mirror-like surface of the pond—a rare and cool sight. It felt as if my own body was inside the pond, and I forgot the sunset there.
After a while, I crossed to the other side of the bridge and went to sit on a bench by the pond.
The sun had set, but the leisurely floating evening clouds, reflecting the afterglow, had sometime turned violet.
The old trees in front of the old Changmudang hall stood tall against the darkening sky like giants in their twilight years. The lone willow at the park entrance swayed listlessly like an aging *kisaeng*. Japanese women in three and fours, wearing summer clothes mixed with blue hues and carrying powder boxes (粉박?), came down hand-in-hand from the Sinch'ŏng/Shinmachi hill—clearly prostitutes from the red-light district (遊廓). <102>
Even they, living the painful life of having their flesh (고기?) and blood forcibly sold night after night, bearing the fearsome and tragic label of "vampires" from others, seem somewhat purified (淨化) when, every day at dusk like this, they bathe, cleanse their bodies, apply cool makeup, cross the hill, and walk by the clear pond under the drooping willows.
Clearly, it's true. Seeing them like that, longing for the world of people outside their own world, coming to stand leaning on the pond railing or strolling across the wide lawn, reveals none of the pain of the flesh or the sting of blood. The dark shadow of their life beyond the hill hasn't followed them here. They are just women. Just human beings who are women.
As dark shadows seemed to creep out bit by bit from between the willow groves, deepening the twilight mood of the park courtyard, young women strolling in threes and fours created a scene like a landscape painting—a unique charm of this park, utterly unobtainable in others.
Soon, electric lights began to turn on from the Sinch'ŏng hill, gradually spreading inwards until the lights at the park's restaurant also lit up. Then, as if signaling "Time's up!", one group of courtesans, needing to report for their night work (夜業), gathered again and listlessly climbed back up the hill they had come down.
"If only we could play in this park a little longer!" How pitifully they must have thought this in their hearts (心中).
The park began to grow thoroughly dark.
Passing the flower garden in the front yard of the restaurant Yoshino (與志野), located below the sports field in the eastern corner of this park, and heading south, you find the newly made road (新作路) curving out from the pine forest (松林) at the foothills of Namsan. Cherry trees line both sides of this road. To the east of the road lies the park's flower bed (花壇), and to the west stands a pretty Western-style house serving Western food. Being a newly made road, with sparsely lined cherry trees, this path has a remarkably fresh feel, and I walked back and forth along it several times.
The southern mountainside (山기슭) of the park grew a little darker.
Heading up the newly made road towards Namsan for a short distance, on the right side of the path, you see young men, two or three at each bend, bathing naked in the water that flows down, trickling and winding this way and that. Finding a quiet spot between them, taking off my socks (洋襪) and dipping my feet in, a fresh and intensely strong coolness, better than anything else, chills me from toes to head.
Sitting there absentmindedly, feet dipped, absorbed in the cool sensation, the sound of a long-drawn-out song emerges from the darkening pine forest of the foothills. Echoing in the empty mountain (空山), it sounds profoundly clear, elegant, secluded, and quiet, like hearing a woodcutter's song in a deep blue forest (深山蒼林) far from human dwellings (人家). Drawn by the sound, I turn my head towards the pine forest. The long-echoing song gradually comes closer, and finally, three men emerge from a path disappearing like a thin thread into the dark pine grove. They look like laborers, carrying dangling bento boxes (벤또). One of them, red-faced, continued singing as they approached. Then those three also rushed to the water's edge and began bathing. The song that filled the mountain and pine forest ceased, and only the quiet sound of water could be heard again.
This, too, must be one of the valuable sentiments of this park. The day grew quite dark. Gathering my socks and putting them back on, I started back down the path I had come. The Western restaurant, with its door open midway along the cherry tree path, looked even more brilliant and cool as darkness fell. Like an underwater magic palace (水中의 魔宮), this place lures people in, and I, too, was inevitably drawn gently inside. The jade-like white stone tabletop (食卓) felt cool even to the slightest touch, and the stylish appearance (맵시) of the beauty in blue clothes (靑衣美人) wearing an apron, combined with her gentle voice, was quite cool.
The beer poured while waiting for the few bowls of meat (고기?) I ordered was just frothing. The only other customers besides me were a Japanese couple (日人夫婦). They occupied a table by the north window on the far side, one table away. The woman looked perhaps 23 or 24, with simple tied-up hair (束髮), large, roundish eyes (어글어골하고?), and a round, modern face (新型). The man looked like a subordinate (쭐네나?) from the Government-General (總督府) or a clerk (事務員) from the Tram Division (電車課), but wearing only a yukata (浴衣), it was hard to tell for sure. He wore eyeglasses (眼鏡) and had a short figure-8 mustache (8字 수염) under his nose (코밋), which wasn't overly disagreeable. Their affectionate whispering was about the flowers in this park's flower bed across the path from the restaurant door. Hearing them say the dahlias (따리아) weren't as good as the ones in their own garden suggested they also grew flowers at home, and the couple seemed to have quite an interest (趣味) in flowering plants (花草). After listening with interest for a while, my consciousness returned to myself, and as if suddenly remembering, I quickly picked up my beer and drank, thinking how shameful (羞恥) it felt to have come in and sat alone.
Outside the projecting window (들窓), thin willow leaves swayed gently. The brilliant (輝煌하는) light from the ceiling lamp filtered through the nasturtium (旱蓮?) petals on the table, shining in the beer mug.
My eyes and face felt somewhat flushed, and my heart felt cozy. My soul (魂) wandered in the air filled with a strange foreign feeling.

By the time I left the restaurant, the night was very deep, and the crisp night air (夜氣) <104> touched my face. People were in the park courtyard, the lights shimmered tremblingly in the pond, and the sound of nameless insects chirped somewhere like autumn.

漢陽公園 (Hanyang Park)
There are several shortcuts to Hanyang Park, but there is a special charm (趣味) in deliberately treading the old castle site (城터) from the base of Namdaemun (南大門턱).
Walking up, treading the dilapidated site of the old castle that boasted the dignity (威嚴) of the imperial capital (帝都), one passes by several places where only the form of the castle wall (城壁) remains, climbing the stone stairs (돌層階) beside them.
With each step, the city streets (市街) appear lower, and with each step, the sky appears shallower. Climbing alongside the old castle, we feel as if we are seeking some holy (거륵하고) and ancient historic site (舊跡) or holy ground (聖地). Climbing and climbing, panting (허위허위) until sweat soaks our backs and breathing becomes painful (苦롭게까지), we emerge onto a flat area, where a refreshingly cool breeze rushes towards us as if it had been waiting. It feels almost like the ascetic practice (苦行) of a monk (修道者), suggesting that to attain this refreshing breeze and fine view (眺望), one must endure the hardship (苦難) of the climb. However, on this cool ground, the old pines (老松), pavilions (亭子), and cherry trees have all vanished without a trace. Instead, construction work (工事) for a shrine (神宮建築) has begun. Earth carts (흙車) are parked, crude warehouse-like (倉庫) buildings stand—perhaps lodging houses (合宿所) for laborers (役夫) or something else—and unworked stone materials (石材) are piled here and there. Sentiment (情緖) and charm (興趣) took refuge (避難) and fled long ago.
Hanyang Park's life (生命) as a park was over.
Since we had climbed all this way, we decided to cool off (드려가자) our sweat for a moment. The wind, at least, was inherently cool. Sitting with our chests unbuttoned (풀어허치고), we completely forgot the fatigue (疲困) of our bodies.
From outside Namdaemun (南大門 밧게서부터) and Sŏdaemun to Tongdaemun and Tongsŏmun, the countless electric lights of the entire Kyŏngsŏng city streets (全市街) sparkled brilliantly (燦爛히 빤적이어), forming a city that never sleeps (不夜城), a single tableau spread beneath our feet. The dark blue sky seemed so close (갓가와) that reaching out could touch it, feeling as if the tail of the Big Dipper (北斗) touched our foreheads (이마에 다흔 것 갓다).
Standing solemnly (儼然히) amidst the stars of the sky and the lights of the earth (따우의 電光) seemingly competing in brilliance, the stars seemed extinguishable with a single breath, and the lights on the ground seemed sweepable away with a single foot, stirring (動한다) a grand thought that I alone am master (主人) of heaven and earth. However, when my eyes fix on the largest light among the city lights, the one that quickly catches the eye, my heart sinks again. The largest light in the city—that is the light in the grounds of Sŏdaemun Prison (西大門 監獄) at the city's northwest end (西北끗). What is it thinking? That large, solitary light <105> sparkles in the dark shade of Kŭmgyebong. Ah, the world is a dream! A dream! This transient (덧업는) thought envelops my heart. Kyŏngsŏng, too, is now in a dream. All falsehood (虛僞), all fraud (挾雜), all strife (爭鬪) are now submerged in dream. A chain (連鎖) of dreams! Humans bustle (떠든다) in between! Look, the deep dream within the darkness frighteningly emerges from the foot of the dark Pugaksan and covers Chang'an. Flickering, flickering—it is the trembling of a dream state. And the electric lights of the Ŭiju Road outside Saemun Gate form a long dream path, disappearing into the darkness between Inwangsan and Kŭmgyesan. The long-chaining dream confronts some fate, creating a long dream, treading the dream path towards the foot of Inwangsan and Kŭmgyesan.
Ah, it is a dream. Kyŏngsŏng is now in a dream. The beautiful night covers and wraps all ugliness (醜) and all evil (惡), so the streets of Kyŏngsŏng lie within a dream.

The night was as deep as could be (깁흘대로). On the way back (歸路), we looked behind us. There too, clusters (무덕이 무덕이) of dreamlike lights flickered in Noryangjin and Yŏngdŭngp'o.

Experiencing such feelings is perhaps another distinct charm of this park. With my chest still open (헤친대로), my *turumagi* coat draped over my shoulders, I strode (휘적휘적) down the path towards Chin'gogae.
Through the high and deep pine forest, the wide, smooth road (坦坦大路) lay whitely. Walking coolly along that road, occasionally peeking (엿보는) at the city lights between the pines, was interesting (興趣). Moreover, whenever the path curved, although there was no moon, seeing the ridge (마루(宗)) of this mountain brightly illuminated by the city's electric lights was a very amusing (滋味잇는) thing.
Our group of four, all seeking water but unable to find any, encountered a Japanese maiden and a student-like man. Forgetting even the water due to the suspicious (殊常한) couple's conversation, we fortunately encountered a restaurant (飮食집) midway up the mountain (山中턱). The name "Beauty Teahouse" (美人茶屋) was old Japanese style (日本古式). We went in and requested two bowls of iced water (氷水) each. Behind the two-story house seen from outside, in a truly separate one-*kan* thatched cottage (草堂), a young woman opened the paper sliding door (障子), wiping her lips with her apron, and came to grind ice. Although a male customer's (男子客) wooden clogs (나막신) were placed by the cottage, only the electric light shone through the paper door, and not even a cough was heard.

In the desolate (씀쓸하고) and vast mountain, silently, the night deepened as much as it could. <106>

**Summary**

The author, "Chanmul," describes visits to three parks in Kyŏngsŏng (colonial Seoul) on summer nights in 1922: T'apkol Park, Changch'ungdan Park, and the former Hanyang Park on Namsan. Each park evokes distinct moods and observations. T'apkol is a small, precious refuge for citizens, filled with diverse people, whispered stories, and contrasting scenes from the elegant P'algakchŏng to a rougher pavilion where laborers discuss work. Changch'ungdan offers more natural beauty with its pond and weeping willows, attracting Japanese prostitutes seeking brief respite, bathers cooling off in streams, and diners in a Western restaurant. Hanyang Park, however, is described as having lost its life as a park due to ongoing construction for a Shinto shrine; its main offering now is a panoramic night view of the city lights, prompting reflections on the dreamlike, transient nature of life and the imposing presence of Sŏdaemun Prison's light. The article captures the atmosphere, sights, sounds, and social strata encountered in these urban green spaces during a specific historical moment.

From a historian's perspective, the article is valuable for its depiction of everyday life and social landscapes in colonial Seoul. It reveals the distinct functions and atmospheres of different public spaces, the mix of Korean and Japanese residents (and their interactions, or lack thereof), the presence of colonial infrastructure (like the shrine construction displacing Hanyang Park), the visibility of different social classes (students, gentlemen, laborers, prostitutes), and the underlying sense of melancholy or commentary on the era, subtly hinted at through observations like the imposing prison light or the prostitutes' fleeting moments of peace. The detailed descriptions of clothing, activities, and specific locations provide rich texture for understanding urban life in 1920s Korea.

公園情操, 夏夜의 各 公園
						잔물
塔洞 公園
해가 젓다!
이 소리는 찌는 듯한 苦熱과 썩은 蒸氣 속에서 왼終日 복기든 市民에게 얼마나 반갑고 氣運나는 消息이랴. 南山과 北岳山, 고사이 바닥에 노혀 잇는 서울 長安의 복판 우에서 견디어 보라고하는 듯이 불발을 나리 쏘든 해가 새門밧 金華山 머리를 넘으면 서울거리에는 사람들이 우적우적 나와서 行人의 數爻가 卒地에 만하진다.
그 무서운 해가 인제야 젓습니다 그려! 彼此에 이런 말을 하는 듯한 얼글로 서늘한 새모시 周衣를 닙고들 나서서 느럭느럭 천천한 거름으로 것는다.
그리면 『자아 들어오시요-』하고 綠陰의 집 塔洞公園의 둥근 電燈은 반짝 켜진다.
좁고 複雜하고 먼지만흔 훗흇한 속에서 삼는 듯한 더위에 苦로이 지내면서도 갓갑게 땀들일 곳조차 가지지 못한 京城市民에게 참말로 이 塔洞公園은 좁으나마 얼마나 貴엽고 서늘한 重寶로운 마당이랴.

K와 내가 夜市에서 果物을 사 가지고 公園으로 들어가기는 아홉時 가까운 꽤 어두운 때이엇다.
門을 들어서면서, 벌서 몸은 푸른 그늘에 들고 가벼운 상긋한 凉味가 마음에 솔솔 숨기기 始作하는데, 八角亭을 向한 中央의 고든 길로, 左右에서 쭉쭉버든 벗(櫻)나무 그늘 미트로 길에 가득한 나무닙 그림자를 밟으면서 것는 맛은 마치 서늘한 버들 밋의 못(池)물을 헤염치는 것갓다.＜96＞
물방울이 떨어질듯이 水氣에 저즌 닙헤 電氣불빗이 부듯드려서 燐火보다도 푸르게 빗나면서도 닙뒤 나무 그늘은 캄캄할대로 캄캄하야 그것만으로도 情趣가 깁고 凉味가 뚝뚝 덧는데 그 나무 그 닙희 그림자가 보드럽게 가볍게 불면 날라 갈듯이 땅 우에 어른어른 떠잇는 것을 보면 그냥 그곳에 안저서 놀고 십게 마음이 키인다. 밟으면 바서질 듯 십허 아차러운 거름으로 삽풋삽풋 거러가느라니까 어느 때 왓던지 벌서 歸路를 밟는 젊은 婦人 한분이 少女 한 사람을 다리고 八角亭 모통이에서 이리로 向하야 姿態조케 아장아장 거러나오는 淸楚한 흰옷 우에 그 보들어운 나무닙 그림자가 서늘하게 어른거리다가 때때로 하-얀 얼굴에까지 어린거리는 모양은 어떠케 形容할 수 업는 佳景이엇다.

밤이니 만큼 八角亭은 더 커보이고 더 古物가티 보이며 우중츙하게 白衣人 十餘人을 태우고 웃둑히 서잇고 그 둘레에 둘러깔닌 잔듸 우에는 電燈 불빗이 흘러서 질펀-하엿다. 젊은 中學生, 專門學生, 紳士, 갓쓴이, 洋服장이, 가지가지 욋사람이 短杖도 끌고 부채도 들고 橫笛도 들고 누구를 찻는 것처럼, 천천히 빙빙 돌고들 잇섯다. 아모때 와보아도 이 公園은 어느 邸宅의 庭園가티 생각되는 곳이다. 안즐 곳이 적고 갈곳이 업고 하야, 잔듸 밧가이나 나무 그늘로 오락가락 하는 사람들이 더욱 그 생각을 두텁게 한다. 이 公園 어느 구석에서 피아노 소리나 들려왓스면, 後園을 거니르는 氣分은 더 充分할 것이다.

이곳에 暫間 안젓는 동안에, 몃 사람인지도 모르게 우리의 압흘 지나 느릿 느릿한 거름으로 뒷짐 진 손으로 短杖을 껄면서 다리를 지낫다.

여긔는 料理집압, 족으만 소나무 한 株가 섯고 그 엽헤 이름 모를 닙잘고 키큰 나무 밋에 안저서 八角亭이 비슷이 보이는 곳이다. 압헤는 길가에 말뚝가튼 소나무 토막이 두個가 박혀잇고... .
여긔 안저서 八角亭을 바라보면 거긔 남모르는 그윽히 맛잇고 風情잇는 딴 世上을 發見할 수 잇는 것이다. 八角亭 속 天井에 오래된 古式의 燈이 달리어 잇고 오랫동안 掃除를 아니하고 두어서 거미줄이 엉키고 먼지가 긋득이 안고 묵을대로 케케묵은 古燈이 隱隱-하게 비취고 잇다. 그것이 이 勝利라는 料理집앙 소나무 엽 키큰 나무 밋 널조각 걸상에 안저서 보면 亭子 기둥(柱) 위에 가로 노힌 굵은 나무 위 裝飾欄干 틈으로 족음 보여서 컴컴한 속에 그 燈의 琉璃가 半分쯤 보이는 것이 마치 어느 山밋 외로운 草堂이나 어느 大家 後園에 따로 떨어진 草堂 미다지에 多情한 秋燈이 隱然히 비추이는 것가티 보인다. 보면 볼스록 그러케 보여서 어린때에 자조 듯던- 科擧 보러온 少年 才士가 서울 어느 大家의 後園에서 草堂에서 工夫하던 處女와 因緣을 맷는다던- 이약이가 생각나서 隱然히 보이는 그 燈이 꼭 草堂 미다지에 燈불이 환-하게 비친 것가티 보이고 그 房 속에 깨끗한 處女가 잇서서 글 읽는 琅琅한 소리가 들리는 듯 들리는 듯도 심고 또 어떠케 하면 그 房 미다지＜98＞ 압헤 男女의 신발 두켜레가 노혀 잇는 것 갓기도하야 거긔에는 따로운 情다운 詩가튼 이약이의 世上이 보인다. 崔는 물그름히 보다가 아아 시골집 생각이 난다! 하엿다. 여긔서 이러케 작고 보고 잇스면 벌서 落葉지는 感傷의 가을가튼 氣分이 가슴을 덥는다.

열時가 지나면 이 公園에는 約束한 듯이 사람들이 더 만히 쏘다저 들어온다. 夜市를 한바퀴 돌아오거나 저녁일을 보아 노코 오는 사람이 만흔 까닭이다. 그 중에는 客을 낙구는 賣淫女나 갑싼 妓生도 이 時間쯤 되어 싸여 들어오는 것이다.

우리는 거긔서 다시 東便으로 石塔 뒤를 돌아서 音樂堂 엽흐로 갓다. 서늘해 보이는 音樂堂과 電燈은 덩그런히 비어잇고 그 뒤에 藤덩굴 밋헤는 모시옷 닙은 이가 마히 안저 잇섯다. 藤덩굴의 한 部分을 차지하야 마당을 덥고도 시침을 떼고 잇는 勝利屋의 出張店은 그래도 족음 서늘하여 보엿다. 우리는 그 집의 압헤, 亦是 널조각 걸床에 안젓다. 보니까, 音樂堂 압, 勝利出張店 마즌 쪽에 노힌 널조각 걸床 우에 틀에머리 女學生과 洋服紳士가 多情히 안저 잇는데 洋服紳士의 키 큰 것을 보니까 아까 연못가에 안저서 눈물을 흘리던 패이다. 異常도 하지, 手巾으로 눈물을 씻던 女子가 紳士의 팔을 툭치더니 紳士는 얼른 卷煙을 꺼내여 女子에게 바치고 석냥까지 그어 바치이고 女子의 코끗에 煙氣가＜99＞ 모락모락 오른다. 무얼가? 하는 疑心이 금방 나다가 賣淫女이다 斷定하고 말엇다. 울기는 왜 울엇슬가. 그것은 알 까닭 업거니와 키 큰 紳士의 過히 상스럽지 안흔 얼굴이 퍽 딱해 보엿다. 지나가는 洋服쟁이 마다 이 두 男女를 有心히 보고들 지나갓다. 두 男女는 그런 것은 본테도 아니하엿다. 紳士는 기나긴 다리를 쭉버든 사이로 두 손으로 短杖을 집고 안저서 땅만 보고 잇고 어깨를 맛대고 안즌 女子는 쉴새 업시 속살속살하고 잇섯다.

公園의 東門 附近으로부터 龜碑가 섯는 곳까지의 컴컴한 곳에는 電光이 艱辛히 새여 들어올 뿐이여서 凉影이 滿地하야 참으로 그늘의 天地라. 가지마다 周衣가 걸렷고 그늘마다 사람이 열리여 잇는 中에, 서늘하게 粉粧한 젊은 女人이 三三五五 떼를 지여 그늘 속에 자리를 잡고 모르는 男子에게 담베불을 請하야 酬酌을 거는 것도 이곳이라. 그래서 男子가 꼬이는지 남자가 꼬이니까 그것이 모여드는지 何如튼, 여드름 흐르는 洋服장이나 粉 바른 賣淫女는 들어만 서면 이便으로 쏠린다 한다.

이 公園은 塔洞 公園에 比하야 자못 公園다웁다.

公園으로 가는 길 엽흐로 적지 안흔 깨끗한 물이 흘러 나려오고 그 물가에서 婦人네 빨래하는 소리가 들어오는 것부터가 몹시 精神나고 서늘하게 한다.
꼿밧 가진 日人의 집 몃을 더 지나가면 벌서 色다른 公園의 어엽분 景致가 눈압헤 보이고 車馬通行을 禁止하는 말둑 박은 於口에 닥드리게 된다. 그 말둑 於口까지 채 가지 말고 綠色 칠한 다리로 올라서면 公園이 연못에서 개천으로 나려오는 물이 近 한길이나 되는 놉히에서 돌壁을 밋그러저 나려오느라고 희고 흰 국수발가티 갈태갈태 맑은 소리를 치며 나리는 것이 心神을 爽決케하야 全身에 소슨 땀이 一時에 거친다. 다리 中턱에 선채로 서서 눈을 물에서 옴기면 녀름을 모르는 수양버들의, 그늘 깁흔 숩새로서 凉風과 함깨 흘러 나오는 맑은 물이 潺潺한 연못을 니루어잇고 숩사이로 보이는 洋屋과 뻰취가 아울러 거울 낫가튼 못물에 걱구로 비치우는 것도 보기 드문 서늘한 景致라. 내몸까지 못 속에 잇는 것 가태서 해지는 것을 여긔시 니젓다.
한참이나 후에 다리 저便으로 돌아 연못가 뻰취에 가서 안젓다.
해는 젓스나 閑假히 뜬 저녁 구름은 餘紅에 비추어 어느틈에인지 보랏빗으로 變하야젓다.
壯武堂 녯집 압헤 늙은 古木은 晩年의 巨人 가티 저므는 한울에 웃둑히 서잇고 公園 於口에 외따로 선 버들은 다 늙은 妓生가티 풀업시 흥청거리고 잇섯다. 新町 고개로서 푸른줄 석긴 夏衣를 닙고 粉박을 쓴 日女가 3人式 4人式 作伴하야 손목을 잡고 나려오는 것은 分明히 遊廓의 娼妓일다.＜102＞
밤마다 밤마다 고기와 피를 無理로 팔리우는 苦로운 生活을 하면서도, 남에게는 吸血鬼라는 무섭고 慘憺한 指名을 밧는 그네도, 이러케 날마다 黃昏時마다 沐浴에 닥근 몸에 서늘한 化粧을 베풀고 고개를 넘어와서 물 맑은 연못가, 늘어진 버들 밋에 거를 때만은, 저윽이 淨化되는 것갓다.
分明히 그러타. 저러케, 저이의 世上外의 사람의 世上을 그리워와서 연못가 欄干을 집고 섯거나, 넓다란 잔듸밧 우를 逍遙하는 것을 보면 거긔에는 아모 고기의 苦로움도 피의 쓰림도 잇지 아니하다. 고개 저 넘어 生活의 어두운 그림자가 따르지를 아니 하엿다. 다만 女子일 뿐이다. 女子라는 人生일 뿐이다.

公園의 南便 山기슭은 족음 더 어두어젓다.
新作路로 南山을 向하고 족음 올라가느라면 길 右便에 쫄쫄 소리를 치면서 이리 굽을 저리 굽을 흘러나리는 물 우에 굽이굽이마다 2, 3人式의 젊은이가 벌거벗고 沐浴을 하고 잇는 것을 본다. 그 틈에 조용한 中間을 차저 洋襪을 벗고 발을 담그고 안젓스면 아모것 보다도 淸新한 强烈한 氣運이 발끗으로부터 머리끗까지 식혀 올러온다.
발을 담근 체로 싀원한 맛에 精神업시 안젓스면, 어두어 가는 山麓의 松林 속으로서, 길게 뽑는 노래 소리가, 사람＜103＞ 업는 空山에 울리어 그윽히 淸雅하고 幽閑히 들리어 마치 人家도 업는 深山蒼林 속에서 나무 찍는 樵夫의 노래를 듯는 것 갓다. 그 소리에 한 맛을 더어더 고개를 돌리어 松林을 바라보면 길게 울리던 노래는 漸漸 갓가이 들려오다가 이윽고 어두운 솔숩헤 실낫가티 사라진 길로서 어대 役夫인지 벤또를 늘어털여 들고 세 사람이 나아오면서 그 中의 한 사람이, 붉은 얼굴로 노래를 繼續하여 불면서 나아왓다. 그리더니 그 세사람도 물가로 달겨 들어 沐浴을 始作하엿다. 山과 松林을 울리던 노래는 그치고 다시 물소리만 조용히 들린다.
이것도, 이 公園 情緖의 갑잇는 한가지일 것이다. 날이 저윽히 어두엇다. 洋襪을 다시 거두어 신고 가든 길로 나려오기 始作하얏다. 벗나무 길 中間에 門을 열어노코 잇는 洋食집은, 날이 어두어 오니까 더 燦爛하고 더 서늘하여 보엿다. 水中의 魔宮가티 사람을 꼬여드리는 이 집에 期於코 나도 솔솔 꼬이어 들어갓다. 玉가튼 흰 돌로 된 食卓은, 손끗만 다하도 서늘하고 靑衣美人의 행주치마 입은 맵시는 상냥한 목소리와 어울려 저윽이 서늘하엿다.

漢陽公園은, 公園으로의 生命은 죽은 後엿다.
올러왓든 길이니 暫間 땀이나 드려가자 하엿다. 바람 뿐만은 原來 시원하엿다. 가슴을 풀어허치고 안저 잇스면 몸의 疲困을 전혀 닛는다.
西大門 南大門 밧게서부터 東大門 東小門에 이르기까지의 京城 全市街의 數업는 電燈은 燦爛히 빤적이어 不夜城을 이루운 1幅이 발밋에 깔려잇고 검푸른 한울은 손만 들면 어루만질 듯이 갓가와 보여서 北斗의 꼬리가 이마에 다흔 것 갓다.
한울의 별 따우의 電光이 서로 빗을 다투는 듯한 中에 儼然히 서서보면 한울의 별은 한 입으로 불어 꺼질 것 갓고 따우의 電燈은 한 발로 휩쓸어 버릴 듯 십허 한울과 따에 내가 홀로 主人이라는 커다란 생각이 動한다. 그러나 市街의 불 中에 第一 큰 불이라서 얼른 눈에 뜨이는 불에 눈이 멈추면 다시 마음이 폭 가라안는다. 市街에서 第一 큰불 그것은 市街의 서북끗 西大門 監獄 마당의 불이다. 무엇을 생각는지 외따로 큰 그 불은 金鷄峰＜105＞ 컴컴한 그늘에 빤작빤작하고 잇다. 아아 世上은 꿈이다! 꿈이다! 하는 덧업는 생각이 가슴을 덥는다. 京城도 只今 꿈속에 잇다. 모든 虛僞, 모든 挾雜, 모든 爭鬪가 只今은 꿈속에 잠겨잇다. 꿈과 꿈의 連鎖! 그 사이에 人間은 떠든다! 보라, 어두운 속 깁흔 꿈은 무섭게도 컴컴한 北岳山 기슭에서부텨 나와서 長安을 덥고 잇다. 깜박깜박, 그것은 꿈결의 떨리는 것이다. 그리고 새문 밧 義州通 길의 電燈은 기나긴 꿈길을 지여 仁王山과 金鷄山 사이 어두운 곳으로 사라지고 말앗다. 오래두고 連鎖되는 꿈은, 어느 運命과 맛닥드려 오래인 꿈을 지어 仁王山 밋 金鷄山 밋으로 꿈길을 밟아간다.
아아 꿈이다. 京城은 只今 꿈속에 잇다. 아름다운 밤은, 모든 醜와 모든 惡을 덥고 싸주어서, 京城의 市街는 꿈속에 들어 잇다.

밤은 깁흘대로 깁헛다. 歸路에선 우리는 뒤를 돌아보앗다. 거긔도 鷺梁津 永登浦 무덕이 무덕이 꿈결의 불이 깜박깜박하고 잇섯다.

이런 느낌을 갓게되는 것도 이 公園의 別다른 情趣일다. 가슴은 헤친대로 두룩막이를 어깨에 걸고 진고개 나려가는 길로 휘적휘적거럿다.
놉고도 깁흔 松林의 사이로 坦坦大路는 허여케 깔려 잇는데, 그 길로 서늘하게 거르면서 솔과 솔 사이로 間間이 市街의 電燈을 엿보는 것도 興趣 잇거니와 길이 굽을 때마다, 달도 업것마는 市街의 電光에 비춰서 훤하게 이마를 비취고 잇는 이 山의 마루(宗)가 보이는 것은 몹시 滋味잇는 일이엇다.
一行 네 사람이 다가티 물을 求하면서도 엇지 못하고 가다가 日人의 處女 하나와 書生가튼 男子의 한패는 맛낫다. 殊常한 男女의 이약이에 물도 닛고 가다가 多幸히 山中턱에서 飮食집을 닥드렷다. 美人茶屋이란 이름이 日本古式이엇스나, 들어가 氷水 두 그릇式을 請하엿더니 밧게서 보던 2層집 뒤에 따로 떨어진 이야말로 1間 草堂에서 障子를 열고 젊은 女子가 행주치마로 입술을 씨스며 와서 얼음을 가는데 草堂에는 男子客의 나막신은 노혓스면서 障子에는 電燈만 비추고 기침 소리도 나지 안핫다.
