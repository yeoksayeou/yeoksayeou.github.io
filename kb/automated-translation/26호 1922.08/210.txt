Agonizing Summer Night
						Kim Sŏk-song (金石松)

Night has come, night has come, the agonizing summer night,
With a warm breeze and sticky sweat,
Drawing in all sorts of bugs, pestered by swarms of mosquitoes,
The suffocating night has returned again.

He, whose face is even distorted,
Hurriedly clears the dinner table,
Quickly gets inside the mosquito net,
And lies down as if saying, "Now, I've survived."

But the room is hot, and even bedbugs bite,
He begins to fidget.
Tossing aside the fan he was tapping with,
He finally dashes outside.

Ah! Life conquered by mosquitoes and bedbugs!
What is your pride, then?
The pride of the human offspring,
The weakest and most foolish among all things. <12>

**Summary**

This poem vividly depicts the misery of a hot, humid Korean summer night plagued by heat, sweat, mosquitoes, and bedbugs. The subject seeks refuge under a mosquito net but finds no relief, eventually fleeing outside in defeat. The poem concludes with a lament about human frailty and the hollowness of human pride when faced with such basic discomforts, questioning what mankind can boast of when easily overcome by tiny insects. For historians, the poem offers a glimpse into the everyday physical hardships faced by Koreans during the colonial period, reflecting a sense of powerlessness and perhaps subtly mirroring the larger national predicament under oppressive rule, where even basic comfort is a struggle. The focus on common suffering and the questioning of human significance could resonate with the broader social and existential anxieties of the time.

괴로운 여름밤
						金石松
밤은왓다 밤은왓다, 괴로운 여름밤이,
훈훈한 바람에 끈끈한 땀나는,
온갓버레 꾀어들고, 모기떼 등쌀대는,
답답한 밤은 또 돌아왓다.

얼굴까지 틀린 그는,
저녁床을 물니기가 急하다고,
모기장 속으로 얼는 들어가
「인제는살엇다」하는듯이 누엇다.

그러나 방은더웁고 빈대조차 물어,
그는 부시대기를 시작한다.
툭탁거리든 부채도 집어던지고,
그는 마츰내 밧그로 뛰어나왓다.

아! 모기와 빈대에 征服된人生아!.
너의의 자랑은 그래 무엇이드냐,
모든것중에 가장弱하고 미윽한,
사람의 새끼의 자랑거리는.＜12＞
