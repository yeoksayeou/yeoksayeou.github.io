When Autumn is About to Come
						Kim Sŏksong

Still, summer is summer.

But in the daytime, cicadas cry among the groves,
And at night, in the grassy patches, the insects,
Crickets, katydids, grasshoppers,
Their beautiful Trio begins.

It is clear that autumn will come soon.

The worker's busy time,
The farmer's harvesting time,
Summer's ripening time,
Is already before our eyes.

But what time is it now? <10>

In the old field called Life,
Have the seeds been sown?
Has the fruit already ripened,
Become time to harvest?

Ah! I do not know, along with them. <11>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏksong uses the transition from late summer to autumn as a metaphor. While nature clearly signals the coming harvest season with the sounds of insects and the anticipation of ripened crops, the speaker turns inward, questioning whether their own life has reached a similar stage of fruition or readiness. The poem contrasts the certainty of nature's cycles with the speaker's personal uncertainty about purpose and achievement. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in *Kaebyŏk* magazine in 1922, might reflect the introspective mood and perhaps a sense of deferred national or personal fulfillment among Korean intellectuals during the early colonial period, using the natural cycle as a backdrop for questioning individual and collective progress.

生命이란 묵은밧에,
씨는 뿌리어 젓는가.
여믈은 이미 익어,
거둘때가 되엇는가.
