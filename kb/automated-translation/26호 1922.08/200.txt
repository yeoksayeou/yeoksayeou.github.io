Summer Day's Work
						Kim Sŏksong

The sun, which had been shining brightly until now,
Suddenly, like the pupils of a crying person,
Is covered by dark clouds.

A suffocating wind begins to blow,
And streaks of rain like the Devil's arrows,
Follow down endlessly.
Ah! The unbelievable summer weather!

However, a person's life is also like the summer weather.
When the downpour of misfortune will come,
Which prophet can know,
And even if known, who could avoid it? <11>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏksong compares the abrupt and unpredictable nature of summer weather—shifting from bright sunshine to sudden, fierce rainstorms—to the unforeseen misfortunes that strike in human life. The poet laments that just as one cannot predict or escape a sudden summer downpour, no one, not even a prophet, can foresee or avoid the arrival of unhappiness or disaster in life. The poem conveys a sense of fatalism and helplessness in the face of life's unpredictable hardships. For historians studying the Korean colonial period, this poem offers insight into the prevailing mood of uncertainty and perhaps suppressed anxiety. Published in 1922 under Japanese rule, the theme of uncontrollable, negative forces could resonate with the experiences of Koreans facing the unpredictable nature of colonial policies and societal changes, reflecting a broader sense of powerlessness.

여름의 날일
						金石松
이때까지 쨍쨍하든 볏이,
猝地에 우는 사람의 눈瞳子가티,
거문구름으로 가리어 버리고.

답답한 바람이 불기 시작하더니,
惡魔의 살대가튼 비줄기가,
數업시 뒤조차 나려온다.
아!미들수 업는 여름의 날일!.

그러나 사람의一生도, 여름의 날일과 갓다.
不幸의 소낙이가 언제올지를,
그 어느 先知者가 알수 잇스며,
안다 하면 避할者는 누구이랴.＜11＞
