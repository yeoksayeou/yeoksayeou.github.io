Article Title: 가을 (Autumn)
Author: 素月
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Autumn
						Sowŏl

The long vines, frost-stricken with black thorns,
Above the bent branches of the lacquer tree,
Withered and stretching even to the gray honeycomb holes,
The aching autumn has come more bitterly.

Oh, sorrowful, imprinted heart of ours!
Outwardly, beneath the feet of love's dream,
Ah! At my beautiful red waterside,
May only the new tide wash and surge forth. <26>

**Summary**

This poem by Sowŏl (Kim Sowol) depicts the arrival of a harsh, sorrowful autumn, symbolized by frostbitten, withered vines and bent branches. The speaker expresses a deep-seated sorrow ("imprinted heart") hidden beneath outward appearances ("beneath the feet of love's dream"). However, there's a final plea for renewal, hoping for a "new tide" to wash over the "beautiful red waterside," suggesting a desire for cleansing and a fresh start despite the bleakness. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in the influential magazine *Kaebyŏk* during the Japanese colonial period, resonates with the prevalent sense of national sorrow and suppressed longing. The imagery of a bitter autumn and the hidden pain could reflect the harsh realities of colonial life, while the faint hope for a "new tide" might hint at aspirations for liberation or change.

가을
						素月
검은가시의 서리마즌 긴 덩굴들은
시닥나무의 꿉을어진 가지 우에,
灰色인 蜜蜂의구멍에도 버더말라서
압히는 가을은 더쓰리게 왓서라.

서러라, 印눌린 우리의 가슴아!
거츠로는 사랑의 꿈의 발알에
아! 나의 아름다운 붉은 물가의,
새롭은 밀물만 시처가며 밀려와라.＜26＞
