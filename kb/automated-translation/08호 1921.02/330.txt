Script - Overnight (Act 5)
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl
Venice, Italy
-About half a year has passed since Act 4, April of the following spring-

Act 1, Scene 1

The Grand Canal Embankment
(This scene should have an ethereal atmosphere, like a dream yet also reality.)
As the curtain rises, the stage is briefly dark.
Only a single crimson lamp shines in the darkness, like the eye of a monster.
The sound of calm sea waves from afar.
A song
A clear boy's voice

Life is like dew on the grass, love, maidens,
Before those pale red lips fade completely,
Before that hot blood cools completely,
The passing sun and moon will not return.

Life is like dew on the grass, love, maidens,
Let's hold these hands and cross on that boat,
Your cheek burning with a crimson blush against mine,
No one comes here.

Life is like dew on the grass, love, maidens,
On those waves fluttering like waves,
Your young hand on my shoulder,
This is a place where no one sees.<141>

Life is like dew on the grass, love, maidens,
Before white hairs mix with black,
Before the fire burning in the heart goes out,
Today will never come again.

(This gondola song is sung to an Italian folk melody)
As this song is sung, the stage gradually brightens. On the distant sea, 4 or 5 reflections of stars. The lamps lit on the boats also look mysterious.
Amidst the dim scenery of a hazy moonlit night, the shapes of overlapping buildings emerge. - Grand Renaissance architecture, faded of its gold and azure colors, covered in moss, ivy, and vines - Stone gates, balconies, bay windows - mixed with rough modern small houses, the beautiful scene of an ancient city transmitted from the brushes of all modern Western landscape painters,そのまま rendered by the moonlight.
A young boatman is sitting near the mast on the gondola.
A pale shadow of spring moonlight fills the entire stage.
Around 10 PM
Male Voice. (During the song) Isn't the wind cold?
Female Voice. (Also during the song) No. It's refreshing.— It's a spring breeze.
(The boatman's figure disappears. After a short while, two figures, a man and a woman, dimly appear on the bank. This is Insarop and Elena. Both are wearing light travel clothes.)
(Insarop's cheeks are terribly thin.)
(Elena's eyes are sparkling brightly.)
Elena. (Energetically dusting off the right side) Sit here for a bit. Let's rest a little.)
Insarop. Uh-huh. (Sits down wearily.)
Elena. (Standing on the bank, looking at the sea, with a cheerful maiden's tone) The night is so quiet too. I can hear the sound of the waves - ah, seagulls are flying around. It's such a quiet night. - Look, the place where the red flames flared up on the way here is the entrance to St. Mark's, right? We were just watching a play in that bright place until now.
Insarop. Elena, aren't you cold?
Elena. No, I'm not cold. Just standing like this, my body feels warm, and I feel like I'm about to sweat.<142>
Insarop. When will I ever be like that? For some reason, my body is shivering, and I feel strangely feverish.
Elena. What should we do? How can you still not be fully recovered? What if it gets worse? Come on, let's go now.— And the play we saw tonight wasn't good. We really saw a terrible play. A play where a young woman dies—
Insarop. That sharp cry the woman playing Violetta let out when she died is still ringing in my ears— That woman singing in the opera probably had consumption. It felt like she was already dying herself.
Elena. Don't say such dreadful things. Come on, let's quickly go to the inn. I'm starting to feel cold too, for some reason.
Insarop. Let's watch the sea a little longer. Tonight, I feel like staying up all night watching the sea like this.
Elena. Why?
Insarop. Coming out to the seaside like this makes me feel, even for a moment, as if I've come closer to our country. It feels like I'm heading towards our country just like this.
Elena. Which way is the homeland from here?
Insarop. East. Look, there's a steamship passing by—
(The sound of a steam whistle faintly heard)
Elena. The wind from the homeland blows from that direction, right? You are waiting for the ship that wind sends, aren't you? Ah, I see a white sailboat. Isn't that the boat coming to meet you?
Insarop. I wish it were that boat— Renchichi's boat should be coming to meet me.
Elena. Renchichi? What kind of person is he?
Insarop. He's a Dalmatian fisherman. Although he's a fisherman, he works together with our comrades and helps out a lot in various ways. He was supposed to prepare a boat and secretly take me back to the homeland. Right now, all other paths are blocked for me, so I can't go.
Elena. Even as a fisherman, he's quite a grateful person.
Insarop. Grateful indeed. The people of Dalmatia are now suffering under Austrian tyranny, just like our Bulgarian people are suffering under Turkish tyranny. Using this war to throw off the yokes of Turkey and Austria and achieve the great movement of freedom by uniting all Slavic peoples—they have a passion no less than ours burning within them.<143> Isn't it admirable? The Dalmatian fishermen are said to have gathered the lead weights from their nets to make bullets and sent them to the revolutionary army. Fishermen who preserve their lives day by day solely by catching fish, having saved nothing, yet for the sake of their country, they do not spare even their nets, their only asset, and do not worry about starving tomorrow—it's truly a splendid thing. Something to emulate. Ah, should I be dawdling like this? Oh, Renchichi, Renchichi—
Elena. Look at me. Getting excited like that again is bad for your health. Thinking about the homeland again?— It won't do, it won't do. You promised me that tonight, just for one night, you would forget everything—homeland affairs, war, revolution—and we would just enjoy ourselves together, didn't you?
Insarop. True, I did. Forgive me. My heart naturally gets drawn to it. It's already been two months, November and December, since we left Moscow, wandering through snowy roads. As a result, I fell ill in the loathsome enemy country (Austria/Vienna?), and though we somehow made it to this Italian port, all paths except the sea are blocked, and the only ray of hope ahead rests solely on one person, Renchichi. Fierce fighting has begun on the Russian and Turkish borders. England and France have dispatched troops to Crimea and are planning to help Turkey surround Sevastopol. There are rumors that Italy will also join the war, but what are all the Balkan countries doing? Are they just going to sit and watch Russia die alone? Are they going to just watch this golden, once-in-a-thousand-years opportunity pass by? And how long will they continue to cry under the oppression of the Turkish government? Cowards, cripples, dirty dogs, codfish-like fools, what will they do in the future?
Elena. Ah, look at me, don't be like that. (Affectionately raises her hand and strokes the man's head)
Insarop. (Forcing a laugh) Haha. Elena. I tried not to, but... I went against your wishes again.
Elena. Come on, let's really not talk about such things anymore. Let's just think about the two of us living together like this, breathing this warm spring evening air, being one body forever—yes? Okay? If not, I'll really get angry.<144>
Insarop. Yes, let's do that. (Gently pulls Elena closer and kisses her. Elena remains in his embrace, leaning against the man. A long kiss.)
Elena. (Pulls her face away from the man's lips, smiling softly.) Don't do that, don't. Stop it. (Fixes her disheveled hair) By the way. I also want to go see that country, Bulgaria, soon. I hate being in this stuffy town of Venice even for a moment now.
Insarop. Aren't you afraid of the war?
Elena. What's there to be afraid of when I'm going with you? - Besides, everywhere we've gone so far, we've been investigated, risked our lives with all our might, barely crossed borders, passed through deadly crises, and now we're about to have a free boat trip, so somehow, even though it's precarious, it's exciting. Ah, from now on, it's a honeymoon trip, isn't it?
Insarop. A dangerous honeymoon trip indeed.
Elena. I like it, I like it. They say precarious things or painful things are more flavorful when you're alive. I think there was a line like that in a play. When you're alive. When you're alive. Whether long or short. - Ah, I want to sing. For some reason, tonight I just want to make noise by myself. Young people are singing on the boat again. Ah, that sound. What were they saying?
(Song)
Life is like dew on the grass, love, maidens,
Before those pale red lips fade completely,
The passing sun and moon will not return.
Insarop. (During this, his expression becomes extremely gloomy)
Elena. Hey. Yes. What's bothering you? You're withdrawn again. Are you not feeling well? Yes? - Forgive me. - But I felt so lonely.
Insarop. No, it's not that. I just felt an unbearable emotion watching you, drunk on the life pouring out of you, singing. You are now holding onto my life tightly, pouring it into the cup of your own young life. You live solely for love, drawing endless springs of life from it. But what about me? Until now, I was intoxicated by vague, beautiful fantasies like homeland, freedom, revolution, yet I thought I was walking firmly on the earth<145> with something heavy placed on my whole body. And then you appeared. Like a light catching the eye of someone groping in the pitch-dark night trying to find the way, you inexplicably came to me, tough as I was, and whispered soft words. You breathed warm breath onto me. My love. My wife. Every time I say it, I feel as if the pores on my chest open one by one. Even so, I still wonder if my life up to now was true life, or if the life after meeting you is true life. Precarious—compared to the feelings you have—truly, while I spent my days with a precarious heart, a severe illness rapidly carried me to the brink of the grave. Floating in that fever, wandering between life and death for several tens of days, the form reflected in my visions wandering between this body and that body was no longer Bulgaria. It was truly you. Only you. And when, after more than forty days of fever, my mind's eye finally cleared and I first saw your smiling face by my sickbed, I truly felt as if I had been saved.
Elena. (Interrupting) Look, don't say such things—.
Insarop. No, that's the truth. Insarop's falling soul was saved by you. Your fiery woman's life burned away the dirty, impure things lurking in my heart. Only now do I truly feel like I can work for the country with an honest, uncontradictory heart. If the work I intend to do from now on produces anything for Bulgaria, without a doubt, Elena, it is you who produced it.
Elena. Look at me.—(Sobs, burying herself in Insarop's chest)
Insarop. But when I think about it, Elena, I feel that this body of mine won't last long anyway. I feel my illness is heavenly punishment (天罰).
Elena. Why do you say such disturbing, sad things? Heavenly punishment—
Insarop. No, it is heavenly punishment. You are too much for me. As heavenly punishment, my illness worsened rapidly. And so, death tries to tear us apart. Yet, miraculously, I am alive, and you are held tightly in my chest. How can this be? It's nothing else, Elena, but your strength. <146>
Elena. That's a lie, a lie. Why would God kill someone like you? Your illness is the Devil playing tricks for a moment, jealous of the two of us. From now on, if we both strengthen our hearts and hold hands firmly, the Devil will not be able to find a gap again.
Insarop. (In a pained voice) Elena. Forgive me.
Elena. Why are you like this? Needlessly— Ah, your complexion looks terrible. Ah, perhaps it's from being in the night wind too long. Let's stop watching the sea indefinitely and go back to the inn. And let's wake up early tomorrow and wait for Renchichi's boat.
Insarop. Ah, Renchichi.
(The sound of the distant sea waves grows fierce. The wind noise also increases)
Elena. Ah, the waves are rough. Judging by the loud sound of the tide.
Insarop. (Standing up) The wind has gotten quite strong. I hope there won't be a storm tomorrow—
(Loud sound of seagull wings. A fading, mournful cry)
Elena. Seagulls. Seagulls.
(The two stand side-by-side as if embracing).
(Another gust of fierce wind. Faintly heard, the roar of the violent sea).
-(Curtain)-
If possible, black out.
Even when lowering the curtain, darken the stage first before closing the curtain completely.<147>

**Summary**

In Venice, the Bulgarian revolutionary Insarop, weakened by illness, and his Russian wife Elena wait by the Grand Canal for a boatman, Renchichi, to take them secretly to Bulgaria. Insarop reflects melancholically on his failing health, his past dedication to revolution versus his current deep love for Elena, viewing his sickness almost as a punishment for his past life or the intensity of their love, while Elena tries to reassure him and focus on their future together. The scene ends with growing wind and waves, hinting at the perilous journey ahead, mirroring Insarop's internal turmoil and the external political dangers surrounding the Crimean War. For historians, this play offers insight into how Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule might have expressed nationalist sentiments allegorically, using the setting of European conflicts (Bulgarian struggle, Crimean War) to explore themes of homeland, sacrifice, foreign oppression (Turkish, Austrian), and the tension between personal life and patriotic duty, themes highly relevant to their own colonial context. The longing for national liberation and the critique of inaction resonate strongly.

인사롭.  그래 그런 것이 아니요. 나는 只今 그대의 끌어 나오는 그 生命에 醉해서 노래를 부르고 잇는 模樣을 보고서 견딜 수 업는 마음이 생겻소. 그대는 只今 나라고 하는 목숨을 꼭 쥐고서, 自己의 젊은 生命의 잔ㅅ대에 담아잇소. 그대는 오죽 한갓 戀愛에 살아서 거긔에 끗업는 生命의 샘물을 길어내요. 그러나 나는 어쨰. 나라는 사람은 이 때까지 祖國이니 自由이니 革命이니 하는 向方도 업는 아름다운 空想에 醉해서 잇스며서도, 계다가 自己로는 五體에 무엇인지 묵에 잇는 물건은 너허서 確實히 힘잇는 발자최로＜145＞大地를 밟아 단이는 것가티 생각하엿소. 거긔에 온 사람은 卽 그대이다. 이것이 꼭 캄캄 어더운 밤중에 갈팡질팡 단이며서도 길을 차즈러 가랴고 하는 사람의 눈에 띠인 불빗가티, 그대는 까닭업시 단단한 나에게 와서 보들아운 말로써 나에게 속살거렷다. 따뜻한 숨을 내게 불어 주엇다. 나의 사랑이다. 나의 안해라고 할 때마다 나의 가슴의 털구멍이 하나씩 하나씩 생기는 것 가튼 마음이 든다. 그리하여도 나는 아즉까지 只今까지의 나의 生活이 참의 生活인지, 또는 그대를 맛난 뒤 生活이 참의 生活인지, 위태 위태- 그대가 생각하고 잇는 그 마음에 比해서- 참으로 위태위태한 마음으로 歲月을 보낼 동안에, 激烈한 病勢가 급히 나를 穿壙터까지 실허다 노앗다. 그 熱에 떠서 死生의 사이로 彷徨한 지 幾10日間 나의 이 몸과 이 몸 사이에 떠돌아 단이는 나의 幻影에 비치는 形態는 벌서, 불가리아는 아니다. 정말 그대이다. 오즉 그대 뿐이엇다. 그리고 慹이 난지 四十餘 日에 겨우 마음의 눈이 떨어저서 처음으로 그대의 웃는 얼굴을 病床염헤서 볼 때에 나는 참 마음으로 救함을 바든 것 가튼 마음이 들엇다.
에레나  (막으며서)보셔요, 그런 말슴은-.
인사롭.  아니 그것이 정말이다. 인사롭의 墮落해가는 魂靈은 그대에게 救함을 바닷다. 그대의 불꼿가튼 계집의 生命이 나의 마음에 잠겨잇는 더러운 물건을, 純潔치 못한 것을, 태워 준 것이다. 只今 비롯오 정말 나의 正直한 矛盾이 업는 마음으로 나라를 爲하야 일을 하는 것 가튼 마음이 난다. 萬一 내가 只今으로부터 하랴고 하는 일이 불가리아를 爲하여 무엇을 産出하랴고 하면 두말할 것업시, 에레나 그대가 産出해 준 것이다.
에레나.  나 보셰요.-(인사롭의 가슴에 폭 안겨 훌적어린다)
인사롭.  그런데 생각해 보면, 에레나, 나의 이 몸은 아모래도 오래 가지 못할 것 가튼 마음이 든다. 나의 病은 天罰이라고 하는 마음이 드오.
에레나.  우에 그런 도섭스러운 슯흔 말을 합니까. 天罰이라니-
인사롭.  아니 天罰이요. 그대는 내게 넘우 過하다. 그 天罰로써 내가 病이 急햇다. 그래서 죽음이라고 하는 것이 그대와 나의 사이를 떼어 노흐랴고 한다. 그래도 異常히 살아서 그대가 내 가슴에 폭 안겨 잇다. 어쨰 그리한지. 다른 것이 아니다, 에레나 그대의 힘이다. ＜146＞
에레나.  거짓말이야요, 거짓말이야요. 당신가튼 사람을 무슨 일로 한우님이 죽일 리가 잇나요. 당신의 病患은 당신과 나의 두 사이를 猜忌하여 暫時동안 惡魔가 弄絡하는 것입니다. 이로부터 우리 둘이 마음을 단단히 하여 握手만 튼튼히 하면 다시는 惡魔가 틈을 비집지 못할 것이 올시다.
인사롭.  (沈痛한 소리로)에레나. 容恕하시오.
에레나.  웨이리 하셔요. 쓸대업시 -아아, 大端히 얼굴빗이 안 되엇습니다. 아아 넘우 오래 밤바람을 쐬어서 그런 것인가 봐요. 언제까지 바다를 보고 잇자는 것은 그만 두고 旅館으로 돌아갑시다. 그리고 來日 일즉이 일어나서 렌치치의 배를 기다립시다.
인사롭.  아아 렌치치.
