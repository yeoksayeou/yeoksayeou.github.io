I Don't Know

In a certain village, there were two men, Kap and Ŭl, who were very close friends. Normally, they pledged integrity to each other, but one day they went out for a walk together in the outskirts. On the roadside lay a pouch, half-buried in the dirt, though whose it was, they didn't know. Kap, seeing it, quickly picked it up. Lifting it, he found it was very heavy. It seemed likely to contain solid treasure. Ŭl, standing beside him, was also delighted and said,
"Hey, you hit the jackpot! Isn't this truly unexpected fortune? It seems Heaven must have blessed us both."

Kap's face suddenly flushed with anger as he said,
"I don't know about that; this is mine. Because I picked it up first. Surely, Heaven would bless just one person. Why say two?"

Ŭl had nothing to say and just followed behind. Before they had gone far, they encountered a band of thieves. Kap looked back at Ŭl and said frantically,
"Oh no, thieves have come! Hey, what should we do? Disaster has struck! Disaster! It seems the fate (卦) of us both dying has come upon us!"

Ŭl shook his head and turned away. "I don't know, I don't know..."
"This is surely one person's calamity, why say two?"

Finally, the band of thieves rushed over, gave Kap the sword [i.e., attacked him], and gave Ŭl a kick.
When profit and loss cloud the eyes, righteousness is forgotten.<21>

**Summary**

This short fable tells of two friends, Kap and Ŭl. Kap finds a valuable pouch but refuses to share, claiming sole ownership because he found it. Later, when confronted by thieves, Kap seeks solidarity in shared misfortune ("we are in trouble"), but Ŭl ironically throws Kap's selfish words back at him ("This is one person's calamity, why say two?"). Kap suffers the main attack while Ŭl is only kicked. The story concludes with the moral: self-interest blinds people to righteousness. For historians studying colonial Korea, this piece, published in *Gaebyeok* (a significant nationalist magazine), might reflect commentary on selfishness versus communal solidarity during a difficult period. It could implicitly criticize collaborators or those prioritizing personal gain over collective well-being, using a simple story to convey a moral message relevant to the socio-political climate.

나는 몰라
어떤 村에 甲乙 二人이 잇는데 매우 親한 사이이다. 平時에는 서로 氣節을 期約하더니 하로는 郊外에 散步를 가티 나가게 되엇다. 길ㅅ가에 어떤 사람의 것인지는 모르지만 주머니 하나가 떨어저 흙에 半쯤 무텨 잇다. 그것을 본 甲은 얼른 집이 들엇다. 들어보니 매우 무겁다. 아마 단단한 寶物이나 들어잇는 듯하다. 乙이 겨테 잇다가 또한 깃버서 말하되
여보게 땡잡앗네. 그야말로 意外福이 아닌가. 아마 한울이 우리 두 사람을 福하엿나 보이그려.
甲이 勃然 作色하야
나는 몰라 이것은 내 것일세. 내가 먼저 집엇스니까 말이야. 바로 한울이나 하나를 福함이겟지. 웨 두 사람이라나.
乙이 할말 업시 그저 뒤를 딸아갈 뿐이다. 얼마를 못가서 도적의 무리를 맛나게 되엇다. 甲이 乙을 돌아다 보며 惶忙히 하는 말이
아차 도적이 왓다. 여보게 이를 어찌 하나, 變 낫네, 變 낫서. 우리 두 사람의 죽을 괘(卦)가 낫네 낫서.
乙이 머리를 흔들며 돌아선다. 나는 몰라, 나는 몰라...
이게야 한 사람의 禍變이지 웨 두사람이라나
마츰내 그 도적의 무리가 달려와서 甲에게는 칼를 주고 乙에게는 발ㅅ길 주엇다 한다.
利害가 눈에 가리우면 義理를 이저 버리나니라.＜21＞
