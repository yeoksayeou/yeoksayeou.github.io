社會諷刺 銀파리
						목성(記)
This household's "Master" (書房님) is the son of the Lord's (大監) second concubine (妾). He turns 16 this new year, has two older sisters, and his nose seems unusually large compared to others.
Fly: Master, did you have a peaceful New Year's (過歲)? Goodness, what's with the flower cards (화투) on New Year's Day? In all the long months and years (歲月), at what other time could you not play, that you must handle such things from the morning of the very first day of the first month (正月 初하루날)?
Master: Why, just because I have flower cards, do you think I'm gambling? I'm using these to see my fortune (운수) for the year.
Fly: How pitiful. Forgetting even the ancestral rites (祖上 차례) and sitting there, what fortune is there to see? If lucky, you become a Count (백작)—no, you get a hundred sheets (백장) [of cards]; if unlucky, a volcano (火山) erupts in the Lord's bedroom (寢室). What else is there to know beyond that?
Master: Ancestral rites? Father doesn't perform them, so how can I?
Fly: Heung. You didn't forget, but because the adult forgot, you followed suit and didn't perform them—!?
Master: When I was woken for breakfast (아츰), I got up and saw my eldest uncle's wife already in the main hall (大廳) making a fuss (야단), saying, "Does this house not even know its ancestors (祖上)? Is the concubine (妾) number one (第一) in this house?" How could I forget? Is it possible to forget?<59>

Saving money
And making your children vagrants (浮浪者),
Don't do it...
... ... ... ...
Buying a concubine (妾)
And causing household discord (家亂),
Don't do it...
...
If you spent it honorably
On worthwhile work (事業),
You yourself would be
Like a proper person
And society (社會) too
Would be fortunate (多幸)...
... ...(목) <60>

How fair the world (世上) is.
The person who works hard
Grows thin and wretched,
While the guy lying sprawled out doing nothing
Only gets plump!
Though one pours out sweat and effort all day long (왼종일),
All the money earned (생기는 돈) is swallowed (삼켜) by that guy.
Ah, the world
Is fair indeed! (셩)<61>

Fly: Stop talking such nonsense. Starting from the New Year, please, either attend school (學校) or hire a private tutor (獨先生) and learn something. At 16 years old, look at your handwriting! And regardless of the handwriting, you should at least know how to write a letter (便紙). Yet you still put on airs like nobility (貴族 行世), knowing how to deal with Kisaeng (妓生)... The sight of you asking someone else to write a letter to a mere Kisaeng, buying them drinks to do so, is truly a spectacle (可觀)...
Master: Such a trivial letter—there's a scribe (書寫) for that... Are you telling me to study just to write letters?
Fly: Just relying on the scribe... Why, you think if you just have money and a title (爵), everything in the world (世上) will automatically work out?
Master: In the world today (只今 世上), where is there anything money can't do? People say humans are the most precious (貴하다) thing in the world, but right now, money is number one (第一), more so than people. Didn't you see them coming to our house to receive that student (學生) [as a concubine]? At first, they made a fuss, but once the money started flowing, didn't she come in the end (기어코)?
Fly: How insensitive. Buying a concubine (妾) with money... But even if you bought her body, did you buy her heart too? Forced to come, dragged by the power (勢力) of money without her heart in it, how is that different from a wooden statue (木像)? On the outside (것으로는), she might be silent, but inside she hates you like an enemy (怨讐가티). Yet you still call her a concubine, thinking it's great, just setting up a house (집작만, 세간 작만) for her, setting up furniture, and pushing heaps of money at her. But with that concubine and that money, there's someone else living in luxury (호강) in between. Money and heart, heart and body (肉身)—so, which is better? Is it meaningful (수인 가요) to just tie down the body and call it yours, whether the heart is elsewhere or not? If that's the mindset (맘보) of the wealthy, then why not spend a lot of money to make a beautiful doll (人形) and cherish (貴애하지요) it as a concubine? Then you could make a beauty (美人) perfectly matching your taste in face (얼골) or body, and she wouldn't hate you like an enemy either...
Master: Is a doll the same as a person?
Fly: My point is, if you drag someone without a heart here using the power of money, she's worse than a doll. People who only know money can never truly act like decent human beings. Don't just stay cooped up at home seeing and learning only such things; study something. You need to know more to act like a human being. Will you spend day and night just bolting the door (빗장이질), scraping the blood of the poor (貧民), and raising concubines only to incite fights (싸움만 부틸 터이요)?
Master: What's the point of studying? Even as things are, once my father passes away (돌아 가시면), I'll be a Viscount (子爵)...
Fly: Ha-ha—The Viscount title is your disease (病)! Is that title (爵) so good (좃소)? That dirty thing. Maybe they'll flatter (떠 바칠가) you if you go to a Kisaeng house (妓生房).<62> Go ahead, try presenting yourself anywhere as Viscount So-and-so right now. See what kind of reception (待接) you get (밧나). What kind of honorable (榮譽스러운) title is yours? It's all the merit (功) for selling out OOO (OOO먹은 功) entirely (송도리째), that great work (事業), the great meritorious family (大勳家) to be eternally commemorated (永久 紀念) for a thousand autumns and ten thousand generations (千秋萬代)—is that nameplate (名牌) so enviable (부럽소)? Or let's say it is a truly (眞情한) valuable (갑잇는) position (位). Let's say it's a position gained through honor (榮譽) for accomplishing meritorious work (有功한 事業) for society (社會) and the people (民衆). Even so, that honorable position (榮位) is precious (貴하고) and important (重한) for the person concerned (當者)... But here, the son inherits the father's honorable position, whether the son is capable or not (잘낫던 못낫던). The son occupies (차지한다) the father's place, whether he has comparable merit (功勞) or not (업던). Where else is there such a rotten (썩은), outdated (時代에 뒤진) system (組織)? Let's say there's a school here, no, forget a school, let's say there's a Hangeul school (한글房). Because the teacher (先生) was a famous (有名한) scholar (學者), many disciples (弟子) gathered from far and near (遠近). But after that teacher dies, would his son inherit (잇겟소) the teacher's position, whether he is knowledgeable (有識하거나 말거나) or not? Would that school (書堂) last long (길게 가겟소)? It's impossible, right?! How is this any different? Currently, people live quite well even under such foolish (어리식은), unreasonable (不合理한) systems. And this isn't something happening in the lowest society (下等社會), among the lower classes (低級間); rather, the highest class (最高階級), the central structure of a people, operates like this, yet they live on without complaint (不平), without a word of protest. Isn't it pitiful? While calmly carrying out such unreasonable acts, you people still think you're the most intelligent (最靈), don't you? Just because someone was a famous teacher, where is the rule (法) that his son must (반듯이) be as famous as his father (아비만콤)? Or where is the rule that there can be no one more knowledgeable (有識한 者) outside (外에는) the teacher's family (家族)? If an incompetent son sits in the teacher's position in a village school (書堂) just because it was his father's place, the disciples will surely seek (求해) another teacher. Why necessarily (何必) just a village school? It would be the same for a school, for society, or even for a country (나라). Any awakened (깬) person would surely do so. If we extrapolate (밀우어 가면) like this, the current property inheritance system (財産 相續制度), where property earned (번 財産) by the father necessarily becomes the son's, can also be called unreasonable.
Master: I don't want to hear it (듯기실타). You little brat (족으만 놈이), talking all sorts (別) of arrogant (건방진) nonsense.
Fly: Yet your greed (慾心) is great... Why don't you feed the old Lord (老大監) some poison (毒藥)? And quickly (速히) try living like nobility? Isn't it the usual practice of nobility to kill even brothers (兄弟) and poison kings (상감) for the sake of one's own glory (一身의 榮華)? If you want to become nobility too, you should learn the art of medicine/poison (藥妙理) well from now on.<63> Only then will you have hope (希望) of becoming a Marquis (후작) like others later on (이담에), won't you? If you know how to use poison well, are good at selling off (팔아 먹기) land, small or large, to others, know how to keep concubines (妾), and are good at calculation (數盤질), then you have ample capital (미천은 넉넉하니까) to become nobility. If you can't become nobility, you can at least become a clown (어릿 광대)...
Master: Hey, you damned fly (밸어먹을 놈의 파리야)! What kind of talk is that (고게 무슨 소리냐)?
Fly: Why, do you think I'm talking nonsense (허튼 소리)? The Lord still doesn't know, but the scribe knows everything, he was talking (이악이 합디다) about it the other day. Your mother's seamstress (針母) told him.
Master: Told him what...?
Fly: That you have a biological father (原아버지) other than the old Lord... ...
Master: Damn you, you insolent brat (발칙한 놈 가트니)! (He throws a punch.)
Fly: Ouch! I'm leaving without even getting hit (어더 맛지도). Sorry (未安하지만), but I'll tell you clearly: know that your biological father, or whoever he is, still can't escape the Kwangmudae (廣舞臺) stage floor.
It wasn't even a lie (거젓말), but he seemed furious, striking out violently with the gold pocket watch (懷中金時計) he held in his hand, only succeeding in shattering (깨털어 젓다) the watch uselessly. This time, shall I go to Miss Kim (金女史), the advocate of single life (獨身主義)?!

Hey, you!

A certain county magistrate (縣官) tried appointing the man known as the richest person (富者) in the county, sending him appointment notices for Chunggun (中軍帖) and even for Hyangjang (鄕長帖), but the man refused (拒絶) them all without consenting (應諾). The magistrate, greatly angered (憤하야), issued an immediate order (卽 日發令) commanding the rich man to be arrested and brought (捉致하라) outside the main gate (三門 밧그로).
Magistrate: Hey you! Do you not know your crime (罪)?
Rich Man: Eh—I do not know, my Lord (城主님)—
Magistrate: Hey you! I hear you commit unfilial acts (不孝) against your parents.
Rich Man: That is not so. That is not a crime I could commit (當할 罪). My father passed away (作故하시고) before I was born, and my mother passed away when I was three years old (三歲).
Magistrate: Hey you! I hear you cause discord (不和) in your neighborhood (隣里).
Rich Man: Eh—Regarding that too, my house is in a most secluded (幽辟한) place. For ten ri in all directions (四方 十里), there is only my house.
Magistrate: Hey you! I know your crime for sure, so speak truthfully.
Rich Man: Then what is this crime?
Magistrate: Hey you, listen! Deceiving (속이고) your pure heart (淸白한 本心) and harboring dark ambition (컴컴한 野心) is precisely the crime!
Rich Man: Eh—Then my only crime is eating white rice with black red beans (검은 팟) mixed in (두어먹은 罪).
Magistrate: Hey, you damned fellow—Then are you saying it's right (올탄 말이냐) that I shouldn't get your money? The disgusting sight of an official (官吏) making absurd (턱도 안닷는) threats (威脅) was truly unbearable.<64>

**Summary**

The article "Social Satire: Silver Fly" uses a dialogue between a metaphorical 'Fly' and a young, idle nobleman ('Master') to critique the Korean aristocracy during the colonial period. The Fly mocks the Master's ignorance, laziness, obsession with his inherited title (Viscount), and reliance on money, contrasting it with the meaninglessness of buying affection (a concubine) or inheriting status without merit. It criticizes the hereditary peerage system as irrational and outdated, comparing it unfavorably to succession based on competence. The Fly implies the Master's family's title stems from collaboration ("selling out OOO") and even hints at the Master's illegitimate birth. Interspersed are poetic verses lamenting wasted wealth and social inequality, and a concluding anecdote satirizes a greedy official trying to extort a rich man. Historically, the piece is interesting for its sharp critique of the aristocracy established/maintained under Japanese rule, its explicit condemnation of hereditary privilege, the veiled reference to pro-Japanese collaboration as the basis for some noble titles (OOO먹은 功), and the general social commentary on wealth, status, and morality in colonial Korea. The critique of inherited status and wealth points towards modernist or potentially socialist leanings common in intellectual circles of the time.
