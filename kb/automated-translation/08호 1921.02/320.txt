Novel - That Night (Final Part)
						牧星 (Mokseong)
Part One of the Conclusion

November 18th!!
Although he had told his father countless times that he would not go, his father paid him no mind, and after consulting with her family (or rather, doing as they wished), the wedding date was set for that day. Upon receiving Hŏ's letter confirming this, Yŏngsik already felt as though his heart was being chased by something.
"Do you really think the wedding will happen on that day?" he muttered fleetingly, yet he still took down the calendar hanging above his desk and found the date November 18th. It was a Thursday. Today was already November 5th! Not even two weeks remained.
What should he do?! This thought had been tormenting him for some time, but now, even if his family alone consented, it was useless. Now, the only path left was to run away!
But how could he possibly plot an escape now... Where could he go, and how could he survive... The more he thought, the heavier and more suffocating his chest felt, as if it would burst. If only he had graduated... this thought repeated endlessly with sighs. <133>
The following Sunday, he waited for Hŏ at the church more eagerly than usual, but for some reason, she never came. Why?... This thought made Yŏngsik's heart even more troubled. Was she heartbroken and shut up at home? Were her parents keeping her from leaving until the wedding? Her family must be busy with wedding preparations now. Ah, buried in all that, how Hŏ's heart must be burning. She must be desperately waiting for my letter, having thought of every possible plan. Thinking this, as he sat holding Hŏ's photograph above his desk, the image of the two of them talking in the royal tomb forest behind Kŭmhwassan Mountain last time floated in his mind. He could see her expression, looking heartbroken as she yanked out a handful of grass, saying, "I wish we could go live somewhere far away where there are no people," and her voice seemed to echo in his ears. How much must she be lamenting my incompetence right now, tormenting the heart of such a tender woman... A man who can't even come up with a single plan!! This thought, conceived by himself, tightened its grip on Yŏngsik's heart, making it ache even more.
A resourceful man! This phrase made Yŏngsik's heart pound, boil, and burn even more. For the second time, he opened his father's document box and fumbled with the Hansŏng Bank passbook and checkbook, but he couldn't manage the seal kept in the pouch tied to his father's belt, feeling disheartened each time he touched them. That's also impossible! As he voiced his despair, his mind was already nearing its breaking point.
Barely a week remained. The day of destruction was imminent. Agonizing endlessly, Yŏngsik even had the thought: Oh, if only she would just get married quickly!! But, but!! When he suddenly thought of that night's events, a chill ran down his spine.

It's a sin! His head trembled, struck by this electricity. Ah, what should he do... A resourceful man!! This phrase drove him into a corner again. He had to resolve this within the week, by any means necessary. Whatever the sacrifice...
Driven to his wit's end, he finally thought of his uncle's iron safe in the house next door. If I just take it and run away, and then send a detailed letter explaining, surely he wouldn't report me... At that house, there's a money chest in the pawnshop attached to the Sarangbang (men's quarters) and an iron safe in the inner room (Anbang). If I watch for an opportunity, if there's just a chance... Once he settled on this thought, his heart felt a little lighter.
Although he hadn't visited often before, especially after his cousin had told his grandmother about his affair with Hŏ, even though they lived in the same Mohwagwan neighborhood, he hadn't gone at all. Going now felt awkward and strange, but he had no other choice. <134> To avoid only his cousin, he went every day near evening when his cousin was out collecting daily payments. When his uncle asked, "Haven't you visited at all while studying?" and his aunt said, "Oh my, it's been a long time since I saw you. But why do you look so unwell?" he naturally hesitated, but he keenly felt the warm, loving closeness of family and relatives. It seemed they, more than anyone, would sympathize with him in everything. Yet, he remained silent. "When did you buy this chicken?" "It's huge, isn't it? How much did you pay? Does it crow well in the morning?" "Do cats not come here? There are so many cats at the house below... but fewer mice instead," he forced himself to ramble about unrelated things, watching for an opportunity for two or three days. However, the clerk never left the pawnshop even for a moment, and his uncle never went in or out of the inner room, so there was no chance. Still, he wasn't too discouraged, thinking an opportunity might arise if he watched for a few more days.
Though he hadn't visited much lately due to lack of time for leisure, on his way to his uncle's house, he met a friend and went with him to the Sarangbang where they usually gathered. Kim, who loved mischief, was grinning and singing, altering the words of the "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (長恨夢歌, Changhŏn Mongga) even though he barely knew the tune:
Strolling along the fortress wall below Inwangsan Mountain,
Are Ch'oe Yŏngsik and Hŏ Chŏngsuk, the two of them.
Their stroll together is only for today...
As he sang, the others nearby glared at Kim and clicked their tongues, their faces showing deep sympathy for Yŏngsik. After seeing their expressions, he suddenly felt even more pitiful and wretched.
He went daily looking for an opportunity, but none came, and Hŏ's wedding was now the day after tomorrow. Perhaps due to excessive worry and anxiety, his head felt heavy, and he had trouble urinating, worrying it might be some illness.
It's already the day after tomorrow... Everything is futile now... Disheartened, disappointed! Utterly exhausted, with no strength left to do anything, Yŏngsik returned home as dusk fell. His loving grandmother and mother worriedly asked, "My child, why aren't you eating well lately, and why do you look so unwell..." Hearing their words, his chest felt like it would burst, and tears suddenly welled up. He didn't answer, just lowered his head deeply. <135>
He couldn't help but think about how, because his parents were so strict and stubborn, his young self had struggled alone all this time over the most important event of his life. His parents felt terribly unfair. As he thought this, tears were already falling onto his clothes, so he returned to his own room.
That night, lying in bed, the thought "If I died like this..." came to him, and feeling limitless resentment towards his parents, he wept continuously.

Part Two of the Conclusion

While Yŏngsik agonized, Hŏ remained at her home, and the wedding day inevitably arrived.
What will happen... Will Hŏ just go through with it?... Will Hŏ put on the bride's attire and get into that automobile? And go to the church, climb those steps, stand before the pastor... Ah, will she make vows before God and the Lord? A body that is not a virgin, ah, it's a sin. A sin. Burning with fever, he didn't go to school and just muttered. Will she go? Will she, who is not a virgin, go silently? Will she wear the bridal gown? Will she vow before the Lord to live a hundred years with a man she doesn't love? When the pastor turns to the congregation and asks, "If anyone among you has any objection to the marriage of these two people today, please speak now?" Oh, at that moment, at that moment, ah, what if I went then and said, "I do!!" What would happen? And as the eyes of the entire hall focused on me, what if I said, "That bride and I have already been married in practice, more so than any formality, for a long time"? What would happen? Ah, no. Even if we became husband and wife that way, how could we face the world... Ah, will Hŏ wear the bridal gown today? Will she ride in the automobile?... No, she won't wear it. Hŏ will definitely not wear it. Then will her parents force her to? Then what will Hŏ do—ah, still, she won't wear it. What if she can't resist the coercion? Then suicide?! Ah, Hŏ committing suicide... Hŏ's suicide?! Ah, what will happen? She commits suicide, and I follow her in death! Then the parents who insisted on their way would regret it. They'd cry, saying they should have let her marry the one she loved, that they were stubborn for no reason... He abruptly stood up, still in his durumagi (Korean traditional overcoat), grabbed only his hat, and left the house. With nowhere particular to go. He wandered aimlessly and arrived in front of the old provincial prison (監營). He boarded a tram heading into the city gate, closing the door behind him. As the tram passed the Saemunt'ŏk (New Gate Threshold) area, he thought, "The ceremony is at the church here today," <136> "automobiles must be passing this way," and looked down Chŏngdong street. On that street, only two Western women were walking; there was nothing else. As the tram passed the long wall of Hŭnghwamun Gate, Yŏngsik finally wondered where he should go. He considered going to a theater (演劇場), but since it was Thursday, there were no daytime performances. As the tram passed Kwanghwamun Gate, he remembered that the Kirakukan (喜樂館) theater in Honjŏng (本町) had daily daytime shows. He got off right at Hwanggeumjŏng (黃金町), cut through Honjŏng, and entered the Kirakukan. He sat blankly through serial films, old Japanese plays, and the like, unsure whether he enjoyed them or not, yet watching until the end. He had forgotten his torment for a while until 4 p.m., but upon going outside again, the thought of facing his heart-wrenching anguish made him feel utterly desolate. However, this time, he was intensely curious about the wedding, so he headed straight home. But there was nowhere and no one from whom he could hear today's news. Still, thinking "I'll find out somehow," he went home and, surprisingly, found a letter from Hŏ waiting for him. Feeling extremely strange, he hastily tore it open. It was written in pencil:
"It seems the threads of fate that manipulate human affairs are tied in advance. In the end, I have deceived the Lord, my parents, and the world, and am forced to live a life of falsehood and sin. What happiness or peace can this body expect, dragged into a life I did not desire? I wonder how many days I will live. Until the day I leave this world, I will die yearning for you. This is the last time I will ever write to you, who will forever be another's, but I have no time to write, and tears soak the paper, so I cannot write at length. This body, entering a life of hell, can only offer you a single verse from Goethe:
Here lies the sacrificial meat (犧牲의 고기).
It is neither lamb nor ox,
Ah, to think it is the sacrifice of human flesh (人肉)!
On this last day, Your C.S."
Furthermore, the newspaper delivered that night featured pictures of the groom and Hŏ side-by-side, labeled "Bridegroom and Bride."

Part Three of the Conclusion <137>

From then on, Yŏngsik was almost like a man who had lost his mind. What he constantly saw in his mind's eye was the event of that night on Wŏram rock. Ah, she was not a virgin. Not a virgin.
And yet, now she is married... serving her husband... Lying in bed afterwards, agonizing, Yŏngsik sometimes had thoughts like this: She spoke of sacrifice and all, but wasn't she actually eager to go?... Someone back from America! It was a fact that some women among them yearned for America, the land of freedom, and it was also an undeniable fact that they sought an ideal husband among students returned from America. Ah, wasn't Hŏ one of them! Ah, she went eagerly! No, no. Even if all women nowadays are like that, it's not true for Hŏ. Definitely not. I believe that. It was indeed a sacrifice! A sacrifice!! He agonized over this day and night, but to this day, he couldn't tell which view was correct.
He walks the streets like a man out of his wits. His soft, fair, and handsome face has become gaunt and sallow, almost hatefully so. He looks like someone who has recovered from a serious illness. He walks the streets. Like someone who has lost something... And as he walks, he sees women passing by, whether on the tram or on the street. Every time he sees one, the events of that night, momentarily forgotten, come back to him. Ah, she too is a woman. Being a woman, she must have secrets. She passes by nonchalantly, just like Hŏ. But how can one argue she has no secret sin? How can one prove it? Ah, there comes another woman. Who in this wide world can prove that woman's sanctity? Ah, secrets. Secrets. The world is full of secrets.
This obsession of his grows stronger day by day. The events of that night never faded from his eyes.
The following Sunday, around noon, he boarded a tram heading into the city gate. As the tram passed Saemunt'ŏk, many people, perhaps coming from the Chŏngdong church—women with braided hair worn up (틀에머리) and men in fedora-like hats (中山帽)—were waiting and boarded together. Among them was a gentleman in the newest Western suit and, in front of him, a woman in new clothes—it was Hŏ and Hŏ's husband. Yŏngsik froze. His head throbbed, and his heart pounded violently. Seeing the gentleman behind them immediately hold out two tram tickets, he couldn't bear it and turned his head away. Soon, Hŏ, having found a seat, saw Yŏngsik. Yŏngsik, though his heart was pounding, turned his head back towards the couple. As if she couldn't help it, yet composedly, Hŏ half-rose and bowed her head in greeting. Without time to think about this or that, he unconsciously touched <138> the brim of his hat and bowed his head briefly. Then, lacking the courage to look further at what was happening over there, he turned his head away and sat absently, completely blank. When the tram stopped in front of Hŭnghwamun Gate, he jumped off hurriedly.
After the tram had gone some distance, he watched the retreating vehicle and thought, "Ah, indeed, she was a married woman (婦人)!!" He started walking again. Walking back towards the outside of Saemun Gate. He muttered again.
Who knows that she wasn't a virgin before her marriage? Her husband doesn't know. Her parents don't know. Nobody knows. Who can possibly know a woman's secret? Unless that star that shone that night speaks, unless that sky speaks, unless the earth speaks, who can know that secret? Government secrets sometimes leak. Secrets of the Royal Household Agency (宮內省) can be known. But who can know a woman's secret? Ah, there comes a woman. A respectable woman. She wears her hair in a bun (쪽), so she has a husband. But who knows the secrets beyond that?
Muttering, he headed towards the path to Kŭmhwassan Mountain. The sunlight on the mountain was warm, but being high up, the wind was cold. He plopped down. Looking down at the Kyŏngsŏng cityscape spread before his eyes, he thought, "Ah, in this world of secrets, what do they think they know, bustling about like that..." The rooftops, laid out like Go stones—each one a hidden home of sin. All dens of secrets! Ah, the women of the world, who can speak of their sanctity? Who can prove their innocence? My family has arranged a marriage for me. They say I should marry next spring. But who knows about that woman? Her parents might know what she eats, wears, and that she attends school. But how do the parents know anything beyond that? When, where, with which young man, which lover, how she... Ah, who can know a woman's secret? Heaven and earth, stars and lamps—unless they speak, who can know another's secret? Even Hŏ had secrets! Even Hŏ had a secret! Ah, Hŏ was not a virgin. But now she lives well in marriage. The world doesn't know her secret. The world is deceived.
Wherever there are women, sin inevitably follows. Even if they speak of Heaven, Heaven, if there were women there, sin would surely exist there too. Ah, a place without women, a place without women—that would be Heaven!! <139>
About four hours later, as the sun was setting, the distraught body of Yŏngsik was found sprawled face down on the sand of a deserted beach in Inch'ŏn, writing the three characters '許貞淑' (Hŏ Chŏngsuk) on the fine white sand, then erasing it, writing it, erasing it, writing it again.
Earlier, at the Inch'ŏn Station kiosk, he had bought a sealed postcard and written this to his closest friend, Im:
"Im?! I have caused you much trouble and been rude many times. But I am now about to depart on a long journey from the Inch'ŏn seashore. I intend to keep going, wherever it may be, until I see Heaven, until I see a world without women, without sin. I leave everything behind and just go. I hope you remain well, and I depart on this path.
Please convey my words to my family when you receive this letter. I lay down my brush for the last time.
On his last day, departing the world, Ch'oe Yŏngsik"
He soon stood up abruptly. Far out at sea, a sailboat floated serenely, heading somewhere. Ah, if only I could board that boat and go far, far away... endlessly, just keep going... he thought. But immediately, No, no, no matter how far I go, there is no country without women on this Earth. Ah, in this world, there is no country without sin!!
His cry flowed out mournfully. He turned his head and looked towards the city. The sun was setting fast, and the world was growing dimmer bit by bit. At some point, his eyes had become wet with tears.

The sun set completely. He walks step by step towards the sea. Large waves, seemingly capable of swallowing rocks, keep rolling towards him... The End... <140>

**Summary**

This final installment of "That Night" depicts the protagonist Yŏngsik's psychological collapse as the arranged marriage of Hŏ Chŏngsuk, the woman he loves and with whom he shared a premarital intimate night, becomes unavoidable. His attempts to prevent the wedding or elope fail, leading him to agonize over Hŏ's perceived betrayal and "sin." After encountering the newly married Hŏ, Yŏngsik's despair intensifies into a misogynistic worldview where all women are seen as inherently secretive and sinful. The story culminates with Yŏngsik writing a suicide note to a friend and walking into the sea at Inch'ŏn, seeking a "world without women, without sin." From a historical perspective, the story offers insight into the clash between traditional arranged marriages and emerging ideals of romantic love in colonial Korea, the immense social pressure and double standards surrounding female chastity, and the profound despair and existential angst experienced by some youths during that era, possibly exacerbated by the constraints of colonial society. The tragic trajectory and fatalistic tone might reflect broader societal anxieties and limitations felt by Koreans under Japanese rule.

下의 一

11月 18日!!
自己는 안가겟다고 몃 번이나 말슴햇스나 들은 체 만 체하시고 父親께서 그와 相議하야(相議보다도 그가 하자는 대로)婚姻은 그 날로 定하셧다고 許의 便紙를 바든 英植이는 벌서 가슴이 무엇에 쫏기는 것 가탓다.
암만하면 그 날 婚姻이 될 줄 아나? 하고 언듯 이런 소리도 하얏스나 그래도 冊床우에 걸린 日曆을 떼어 들고 11月 18日 날자를 차자 보아젓다. 木曜日이엇다. 벌서 오늘이 11月 5日! 이제 2週日도 남지 못하얏다.
어떠케 하여야 조흘가?! 이것은 벌서부터 속을 태워 오던 생각이지마는 이제는 우리 집에서만 許諾을 한 대도 소용이 업시 되엇다. 只今와서는 오즉 逃亡! 그것밧게 取할 길이 업시 되엇다.
그러나 어떠케 只今 逃亡갈 꾀가 잇나...어대로 가서 어떠케 살아갈 수가 잇슬가...생각할스록 가슴만 뻐겨질 것 가티 묵업고 답답할 뿐이고 卒業만 한 뒤엿더면...하는 소리만 한숨과 한께 덧업시 飜覆되엇다.＜133＞
다음 다음날 日曜日에는 다른 때보다 더 禮拜堂에서 許가 기다려 젓스나 왼일인지 그는 永-오지 안핫다. 왼일일가...하는 생각이 英植의 가슴을 더 煩悶케 하엿다. 속을 썩이어서 집에 파무텨 잇는가. 婚姻 때까지 그 父母가 내어 보내지를 아니하는가. 그 집에서는 只今 婚姻 準備에 奔忙하렷다. 아아 그 숙에 파무텨 잇서서 許가 오죽이나 가슴이 타랴. 別別 궁리를 다하여서 내 便紙만 苦待하고 잇슬 것이다. 이런 생각을 하며서 許의 寫眞을 冊床우에 들고 안젓는 그의 머리에는 去番에 金華山 뒤 陵林 속에서 둘이 이악이 하던 그 모양이 떠돈다. 속傷하는 듯이 연흿 풀 한 줌을 부-ㄱ 뜨드며서『아주 머-ㄴ 사람 업는 대 가서 살엇스면 조켓서요』하던 그 態度가 눈에 보이고 그 말소리가 귀에 들리는 것 갓다. 너글업지 못한 女子의 속을 고러케 태우는 그가 只今 얼마나 나의 無能을 歎息하고 잇슬가...그까짓 주변 하나를 내지 못하는 男子!! 自己가 스스로 생각한 이 소리가 英植의 마음을 더 괴롭게 졸엿다.
