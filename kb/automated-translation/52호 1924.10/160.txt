Students' Real Feelings During Summer Vacation

A Letter Offered to Teacher
Ma Ŭri, Paejae High Common School

Teacher, I dreamt a dream of floating about a *kil* (approx. 6 feet) up in the air. I wonder what omen this is; I had intended to use this year's summer vacation as diligently and beneficially as possible, but all of that turned into a dream.
Quietly sitting and thinking about the events of the past month, it's all just traces of floating in the air. I wondered whether to go to Wŏnsan or Sŏgwang Temple, but Wŏnsan became a dream and Sŏgwang Temple also became a dream. I intended to study English, look at algebra, do geometry, read other books, and write, but this and that all became like floating clouds.

Teacher. This year is a year of bad harvest. Whether it's because it's the year *Kapcha* or because it's the end of the century, why is it that people's hearts are so troubled, regardless of capital or countryside? When I was in Seoul, it was chaotic with the floods that followed the end of the drought, but now that I've come to my hometown (Kanggye), people are crying out for death due to the drought again. Drought in the East Village! A bad harvest even if you go to the West Village! Currently, in the Western Provinces (Sŏdo, referring to P'yŏngan and Hwanghae provinces), there is no place that does not curse Heaven. Even from seeing them clamor about rain rituals (가우제) and heavenly rituals (천제), which were unheard of before, one can guess how much they curse the annoying mischief of the Creator (造化翁). In any case, the willows by the riverside have turned yellow and withered, shedding their untimely leaves with rustling sounds, and the kidney beans planted on the mountain farms have withered up to their stalks from the drought, wringing tears from the eyes of the slash-and-burn farmers who depend on them for food.
"We haven't had anything to eat for two months already, digging up tree roots to eat, so how can we work? The first, middle, and last *pok* days (hottest days of summer) have passed, but we couldn't even do the first weeding. So the weeds are probably a *kil* tall. Is this our sin? Around this time, farmers usually manage <108> to survive on kidney beans, but since that's not possible, how can we work without eating? If you could just lend us about 100 *nyang* (10 Wŏn), it would be enough food for half a season for my family of six... please help," I saw a tenant farmer who came into the landlord's house, crying and lamenting his livelihood like this.
Is it just farming? Business (commerce) is also like that. It's a depression where currently, rural shops (廛房) don't have even a few Wŏn in cash reserves, and the total daily sales, even on a good day, are less than 30 Wŏn. Therefore, laborers, artisans, and salaried workers are all like that, and so are the pubs and restaurants. Schools, companies, everything is on the verge of bankruptcy. Even nursing infants are now prepared for the approaching "death."

With floods, droughts, and diseases spreading (in the Kanggye region, epidemic diseases like indigestion are circulating due to the irregular climate), and the public sentiment being unstable, every kind of superstition is rampant, and female shamans (무녀) and blind fortune-tellers (판수) brazenly roam about in broad daylight as if it's their world. If you go to the river, they search for the Dragon King; if you go to the mountain, they search for the Mountain God; on the streets, street rituals (거리祭); in the fields, Heaven rituals (天祭); on the roads, road rituals (길祭); exorcisms (푸닥거리), shamanic rituals (굿), divination (점), the sounds of small gongs (꽹가리), the sound of Buddhist invocations (불소래), the sound of cymbals (제금소래), the sounds of lamentation – they make a racket day and night without distinction. On top of that, existing rumors, groundless rumors, and strange prophecies further stab at the hearts of the village elders and women. In the end, "We're going to die" is their final word.

We cannot know how great an obstacle the so-called literate youth, haphazardly drunk on Western ways, are to our path ahead. The actions of those who wear sheep's clothing but have the heart of a wolf (狠心) are truly detestable. One such person has appeared in our town (Kanggye).
A brat named Kim O-hwan, currently 23 or 24 years old, acting as a so-called XX Church Missionary (XX敎傳道師), came to our town with some unknown intention. For about 3 years, he held lectures and such, dripping honey from his mouth for a while. Starting the year before last, he established the so-called Ch'ŏngwŏnŭisuk (淸源義塾), gathered married women who were illiterate and young unmarried women who couldn't attend regular school, called it a night school (야학), and appointed himself teacher. But what do you think happened? It's said that among those night school students, he had 6 or 7 romantic partners. Moreover, while clamoring that love is sacred, he reportedly engaged in base behavior. Because of this, other students are said to doubt the church and the night school. So, they say even reading is useless and throw away their book bags.
Teacher. I do not criticize his view on love, whatever it may be. Furthermore, if he were not a church missionary and a night school teacher, it wouldn't even be worth discussing, and I would just spit and discard it. However, regarding the fact that as a so-called church missionary and school teacher, he committed such acts and caused harm, whether small or large, to our future path, I want to impose our sanctions as soon as possible and bury him forever (永水埋葬).

Teacher. I have become too verbose. Finally, to bring even a little order to this world of chaos, ignorance, struggle, and jealousy, I want to cry out for the local youth who know a little better to strive more diligently and sincerely for their own land. And for us who are learning, let's always remember the elders in our hometowns who work, sweat, shed tears, and sigh. Also, to those who write, those who speak, those who sing a little, and those who teach, I presumptuously add a few words, asking them to be more distinct, smell of the earth, smell of sweat, and act vigorously.

Truly, I Saw Extreme Poverty
Hwang Nakchin, Posŏng College

Ah, truly, I saw extreme poverty. <110> That the poorest, most pitiful people in the world are Koreans is something clamored about in newspapers and magazines, in speeches and lectures, so we have unconsciously become aware that we are poor and pitiful people. However, most people only knew about poverty and pitifulness through rumors heard from all around and statistics shown in numbers; few would have seen the actual reality and inside story. In other words, many know it simply because the world clamors about it. This is especially true for youth and students. For us, whose environment and circumstances are unlike those of students in other countries, if we spend four or five years just looking at our coursework, wastefully spending the money our parents provide, without ever knowing the inner workings of our society, even after finishing our studies, we will ultimately fail to achieve beautiful results and end up wandering in a mechanical life. Ah, fellow students who went to your hometowns! Besides seeking out beautiful mountains and wise waters (仁山智水) and visiting famous sites and ancient relics (名勝古蹟) to cultivate your bodies and minds, did you see and think, even a little, about the miserable conditions surrounding you? Ah, did you truly see our heartbreakingly pitiful brethren? I, who had only superficially lived amidst the complex city with its bustling transportation, rows of companies and shops, and imposing buildings, truly realized something unexpected. My current address is in what could be called a small city, the provincial capital of our province. After resting for about two weeks upon returning home, I went to visit my primary hometown, located about 60 *ri* (approx. 24 km) away, a place I left three years ago, where the mountains and rivers were beautiful and the people's hearts were simple and honest (淳厚), living a peaceful life together with 70 or 80 village households. Ah, here I tasted a sorrow that I can never forget. Entering the village entrance, the mountains and rivers remained as before, nature hadn't changed, but how drastically had the circumstances of the people within it changed? Having lost their tenant lands and chased by debt collectors (債鬼), they had scattered somewhere, and the remaining fifty-odd thatched houses were sparsely decayed and crumbling, making foreigners or people from the city who lived somewhat cleanly turn back without even setting foot inside. On the empty house sites here and there, only mugwort and weeds stood sparsely, speaking of sorrow. The people I met were extremely glad to see me and wanted to hear stories about Seoul. But their appearance was extremely haggard. Even if dissected right then, blood and fat would surely not be found. On their dark and sallow faces, their darkish eyes were deeply sunken. I stayed for two or three days for some minor business and to visit a few acquaintances. Ah, why did I come here? They say that with just 10 Wŏn, one can buy a good house of about ten *kan* (rooms/bays). Firstly, here, one cannot see even a single copper coin. There is nothing to sell to them, and there is absolutely no way to earn even a pittance by selling their labor. If by chance they see someone with money <111>, they glare with strange eyes.
Moreover, it is now the *Ch'ilgungjijŏl* (七窮之節), the leanest period most feared in farming villages. What they wear are either hemp rags stained with sweat and mud or cotton rags. Here and there, dark, cold flesh is visible. Of course, since it's hot, they could live even if naked, but primarily, they cannot eat what they should eat. With the income from a year's farming, working themselves to the bone from early last spring until the ice formed, they barely managed to get through last winter. From early this spring, they started carrying rice sacks on their backs to borrow grain at high interest (長利). But that didn't last many days. And the barley and wheat were harvested before turning yellow and didn't provide food for many days. Even potatoes and corn, the summer staples, were not owned by everyone. Ah, do not be shocked. They are currently living day by day on wheat mush (밀범벅 - made by mixing wheat bran and pumpkin) and barley husk cakes (보리개떡 - cakes made from barley husks). Even that seems insufficient. They go out with hoes before sunrise, shed blood and sweat all day long, and when they crawl back into their crumbling huts in the dim twilight, clutching their hungry stomachs, unable to even straighten their backs, the table brought by their scantily clad wives holds only a bowl of barley husk cakes and a small dish of soy sauce. Ah, I cannot write any longer. They are all in such circumstances. Even amidst this, even while starving, they must perform corvée labor (赴役) for road maintenance (治道) (twenty or thirty *ri* away). They must go work on the construction of the new township office, even if it means packing wheat mush. Until a few years ago, this village, though not affluent, did not live such a dreadful life. I tried hard to find the cause. It is not because they are lazy. It is not because they are dissipated. It is only that, starting a few years ago, as economic hardship pressed down on all of Korea (全朝鮮), they couldn't see even a single copper coin, yet various taxes increased. Corvée labor became more frequent, and creditors pressed relentlessly. Pulled and squeezed this way and that, they finally reached today's tragic state (悲境). Their life was a miserable one rarely seen even in primitive times. Ah, miserable! This life! When they have to perform ancestral rites or face special occasions, they go to the landlord elder—twenty or thirty *ri* away, in a big house, eating white rice (白米) with meat side dishes, sitting on a cool wooden floor, rubbing his stomach complaining of indigestion—or to the wealthy who saw the recent drought but won't sell even a *mal* (斗) of rice, just waiting for the price to rise. They tell all sorts of stories as if begging, just to buy a *mal* of rice. Wondering how such a curse had fallen upon this village, I deliberately visited several nearby villages. But there too, empty houses were scattered here and there, and only signs of withering were visible. The neighboring village was like that, and the village across was like that. The entire township (邑) was like that. Only then did I realize that this <112> represented and explained the farming villages of all Korea. At this, a worry I never had before arose in me. Even the thought of returning to the halls of learning (學窓) in the autumn dies away. What effect could there be even after finishing studies in such circumstances? Ah, how long will this unjust and unfair system continue, tormenting those pitiful people?

Unable to Return Home, the Reality of Us Penniless Students!
Yi Sangok, Songdo High Common School

One
The cool wind of early autumn blows. Beneath the foundation stones remaining on the old site of Manwŏldae, the evening cricket songs sound mournful. Ash-gray silence and distant darkness lose harmony, mixing with the untimely, desolate wind without melody, swirling around the eerie air of the guest house (객사). When the silently arriving autumn wind tickles past my warm chest, an unknowable, desolate sorrow made me want to cry my two eyes out. What tears are these?

Two
It was already several months ago. Unfortunately, after the "strike" (스트락이) incident broke out at school, the authorities declared vacation, and the 700-odd students, clutching their aching hearts, all set off on the road home towards the beloved embrace of their parents in their longed-for hometowns. It was this time. However, as a work-study student (半工生), I could not carry out this act. The factory supervisor's words,
"You'll have to stay here for one month and work."
Who could have imagined, even in a dream, that these words would be the fuse that prevented me from ever returning home?
For one month, I endured the pain of homesickness (思鄕) and had to work while hiding the emerging tears. I wound thread, shedding blood and sweat.
Finally, the one-month deadline arrived. My fellow students (同硯) who worked with me packed their belongings, bought gifts for their parents, and were quite busy with preparations for returning home. Their faces overflowed with expressions mingling longing and joy. Laughing and chatting, they spoke in advance of the things they would do upon returning home. But I alone was again <113> unable to go. I could not be embraced by the warm bosom of my beloved hometown. How painful must that heart have been at that time, being left alone, unable to return home? Though I wanted to see my parents, though I longed for my younger sibling, though the mountains and rivers were dear, when I alone could not embark on this road, how much must that heart have ached?

Three
Is my inability to return home due to a lack of parental love? Or a lack of affection for my homeland? No, no. My warm homeland opened its bosom and waited for me long ago, and my mother, full of love (愛), leaning on the gate, has been anticipating my return for some time now. Then what is the reason? Is it because I am unworthy (不肖)? This too is not the case. In the blooming spring and the moonlit autumn, what one longs for is the mountains and rivers of home; facing the distant mountains (遠山) and sitting under the lamp (燈下), what one wants to see is the face of one's parents. When leaving the warm hometown and the affectionate presence (膝下) of one's parents (兩堂), shedding endless tears of parting (석별), and diving into the so-called sea of learning, how could I bear to forget the affection of home and the love of parents at any time? Yet, I could not go. I could not go. What is the reason for this?

Four
The reason that prevented me from leaving was money, that very thing. Money, which even a dog wouldn't eat if thrown away. My hometown is unfortunately far away, in the vast plains of North Manchuria (北滿洲) where the wind blows fiercely and snow flurries swirl. Therefore, with trifling travel expenses of one or two Wŏn, or three or four Wŏn, I absolutely could not achieve my purpose. How much less so at that time, when a financial panic due to floods and droughts everywhere was worsening day by day (日甚)? What prevented me from going was money, that very thing. Money! Money! Money that arbitrarily sways people! But I will not become a slave to money. I will not become a flatterer (阿諛物) of money. Money is a temporary object (物), not an eternal achievement (業). Even if I couldn't return home this time, isn't it something I can do next year or the year after? Yes, it's nothing to grieve over.
Thinking like this, I now recall my brethren in the Western regions (西계형제 - likely 西道兄弟, brethren of the Western provinces) and my compatriots in the city who suffered repeatedly from floods and droughts this year. Having lost their homes and food (農糧), how will they survive the harsh winter (嚴冬)? What will they eat, what will they wear, and how will they fight the fearsome cold that will soon arrive? Ah, do our people have the sincere devotion (血誠) and ability to save <114> these pitiful compatriots, or not?
Ah, fellow students! Let us discard the same narrow egoism as those wealthy people. Let us not think about how to receive a large salary and enjoy a comfortable personal life. Let us contemplate how to break the ignorance and poverty of twenty million people and lead them to a new society of happiness and freedom. And let us diligently strive (刻苦勉勵) to cultivate our character, ability, and knowledge. Comrade, what are your feelings about this? <115>

From a Village Woman – Drought-stricken Samnam, a Field Wife of Samnam
Chu Tonghun, Posŏng High Common School

"Let's try to revive (*) the head rotted under civilization's electric lights with the dew of the fields. Let's leave the unsatisfactory reality behind by seeking out the relics of the ancients," – even this initial resolution for this summer vacation dissolved in the fearsome wind of reality that says, "Share the sorrow with the parched crops. What is recuperation in a world without food, and whose lament is the exploration of ancient sites?" My steps, wandering through the drought-stricken Samnam (Three Southern provinces) – Honam of Samnam – encounter only desolate fields, mere vast emptiness (荒原), whether entering the fields or treading ancient ground (古地). Only the heavens and earth are empty and vast (空漠), where a cluster of white clouds drifts aimlessly, seeming to come and go without strength.
To roughly dig up and carelessly toss away the yellowish, shriveled rice seedlings in the cracked, whitish seedbed (苗床) with a mattock, and the feeling of setting fire to it as a last resort is truly too dreadful to contemplate. Hearing about groups of village women (村婦) climbing high mountains to dig up ancient tombs (古塜) and scatter skeletons under the sunlight makes one's skin crawl. Yet, as if saying, "Leave maturity to heaven. We must just strive to live until the day we die," still holding a ray of hope, I dragged my legs up the mountainside to find my older sister among 4 or 5 village women bent over in the cotton field (*미영 밧테*). My newly worn hemp (*모수*) short jacket was soon soaked and stuck to my back, and the handkerchief held in my hand frequently rose to my forehead. The backs (背後) of the village women, exposed about the width of a hoe handle between their black skirts and white short jackets, directly received the sun, already halfway down the western sky (聊天), and glistened like reddish copper (鳥銅). Sweat, seeping out from under the hemp jackets stuck to their backs, gathered in the hollows of their spines, ran past the glistening copper-like strips of skin, dripped from their towel-covered foreheads, past the corners of their mouths, grazed their chins, and fell drop by drop onto the earth – do they know or not? Only their dark arms holding the hoes <115> thrust into the gaps between the yellowish, gaunt cotton plants. Like putting medicine into the mouth of a dying child and watching over its thread-like life, they gently touch (*만적만적*) the cotton plant with one hand and scrape the earth with the hoe to cover the roots. When city women dance their crystal-like fingers over keyboards next to electric fans, the dark arms of village women make the flower of life bloom in the clumps of earth. When new women under parasols exude the fragrance of weakness (衰弱) in their vain makeup, village women tread the earth under the sunlight and dance the dance of strength (力) to the song of sweat! Knowing it's enviable yet unable to jump in, my body, if not an idle new woman, is surely a modern Korean student.
"Aigo! Why come to the field? You'll dirty your clothes, it's hot, go over there under the persimmon tree."
"Sister, why are you working in the field? If others weed the field, shouldn't I weed too?"
"Aya- what truly crazy (*) talk is this? When did you arrive? Still holding a book-"
"There's no time to play with books. Even though he arrived yesterday, he didn't talk at night, and who knows when he woke up at dawn, he's just looking at books."
"Aigya- Isn't he pitiful too? Someone with a younger brother like that wouldn't worry even in a drought."
"Isn't a cicada's (맴이) fate to be in the shade! Someone like that! Only a penniless, old fate... harsh..." Receiving the words of the village woman next to my sister, an old voice trembles from this end.
"A cicada's fate in the shade!" What an insult this is! Isn't it a phrase that directly pierces through my life of eating without earning?
"Only a penniless, old fate... harsh..." What philosophical hint is this! It points directly, according to God's will, to the future of those who cannot work. Although this may be the "lament of an old woman, feeling sorry for me and grieving for herself," it is undoubtedly the cry of God. It is a heart-wrenching song (斷腸曲) seeing Korea heading towards becoming a "cicada's fate."
However, did this cry of God reach the ears of the thoughtless village woman who has not yet experienced old age, or not? Why envy the "cicada's fate"? Her simple ear may hear the cicada singing in the shade, but it probably cannot hear the cicada crying mournfully in the frosty wind. She may hoe to remove weeds, but she doesn't know she digs the earth to obtain nitrogen (窒素). She may worry about clothes getting wet with dew, but she doesn't know how to sing in the dew that wets the clothes. She may have learned to obey the head of the household (가장), but she has never heard of making the head of the household happy. <116>
Simple, simple! Simple is endearing, but labor, labor! Labor is important, but—since it's not the ancient era (太古) where one could advance with simplicity alone, simplicity must be shattered by the complexity of the 20th century. And since life doesn't end with labor alone, labor must be harmonized with learning (학술). Isn't it even more urgent in Korea, where city civilization is extravagant in learning and village life is primitive (野昧) in labor?
From a firm foundation that recognizes the sanctity of labor, we must wield the strong arms that realize the authority of learning with conviction, making the deeply interesting flower of life bloom. And thus, we must dream of a paradise where we live intoxicated by this interest. To achieve this, the spirit of education must be founded on labor. We must escape from the parrot education (鸚鵡敎育) of present Korea and establish an education where one can tread the earth with a naked body (赤體), stand directly under the sunlight. And thus, let's bring the village women of ancient times into the 20th century and let them play in a land of firm self-respect (自重), freeing them from the self-abandonment (自棄) of merely admiring those with topknots (틀에머리) and Western suits (양복쟁이). Let them sing that they themselves are the happiest. Let these young village women rebuke the baseness of the "cicada's fate." People of present Korea with the "cicada's fate," let's eliminate the lament of that old woman! When going out to the fields, let's wear work pants (잠뱅이), carry a hoe, and sing the hymn of sweat (땀의 공가) with the pulsation of life! When entering the study, let's play in the universe within the test tube and dance in the land of poetry! The life of Korea—no, the life of the world—is this. Where is the paradise of humanity?
To the *sak-sak* sound of the hoe, a patch of cloud briefly covers the sun, but the village woman's back remains unchanged! Only the cicada's song in the persimmon tree lengthens. Isn't there life-giving rain in that cloud, or has it not yet arrived? Oh, rain of Korea, when will you come?
-(End)-

Reprobate Child
SW Saeng

There was one who threw away the insides of a melon, and there was one who picked it up and ate it. The one who threw it away was the son of a well-to-do man, who entered a certain high common school (某高普) last April, spent his ample school funds (學資) extravagantly on food, expenses, and play, failed his grade, and stayed back. The one who picked it up and ate it was the son of a poor man, whose family couldn't even get enough barley rice (조밥) and lately subsists morning and evening on wheat mush (밀범벅).
Ah! The discarded melon insides – they were leftover scraps. But <117> to the poor man's son who picked them up to eat, they must have tasted endlessly delicious. And that poor man, not only unable to send his son even to primary school, but currently having nothing to eat, unable to buy even one common melon for his innocent children who pester him saying "Mother, I want melon...", ended up letting him gratefully pick up and eat scraps left by others. Although this is an ordinary event, it reveals one aspect of the lives of the poor.
The proverb "Giving away the pear and begging for the pear's core" comes to mind. It surfaces in my head.
This crowd of people who "give away the pear and beg for the core" exists everywhere one goes. They are everywhere one looks. They are also found in our Korean farming villages.
They asked me, "Is there any special news in the newspapers these days?" Their manner of asking is earnest. It's like they're asking a secret. It's a question stemming from an earnest anticipation of something. And some say they cannot live like this anymore. They say things must be smashed immediately.
They have now reached a dead end. Unable to advance further, unable to retreat, unable to stand or sit – it is the final cry, "Smash it," brought about by unavoidable circumstances (事勢).
The sound of praise, "That person's family situation isn't well-off, but he raised his one son well," is frequently heard. Behind that statement lies the image of a "32-Wŏn-a-month salaryman." That is, our elders consider that "32-Wŏn-a-month salaryman" a great success and proof of raising a son well—of education (敎育).
Look. Don't they ask me, "After graduating from the school you attend now, what will you become?" with pricked ears, sometimes making me tired of meeting them because of it?
Hoping for that—that is, receiving a salary—they sell off fields, sell off rice paddies, and even sell their bodies to educate their sons and grandsons. This result sadly even influences the minds of innocent boys and girls—when primary school students are asked near graduation, "What will you do after graduating?" the vast majority answer, "I will go to Normal School (師範學敎)." Lacking school funds and wanting to become a salaried employee, they inevitably knock on the doors of the government-funded Normal School. And our elders push them onto that path.
It is all a phenomenon produced by unavoidable circumstances.

I met those people whom I had previously trusted as gentlemen (신사) and respected as the intellectual class. Ah, they were a gathering of cold blood. <118> They were lumps of rotten meat.
Listen to their conversation. "Confucius said this, the Five Relations and Three Bonds (五倫三綱) are that, anyway, the Kim family (金門) is this and the Yi family (李門) is that," or "So-and-so studied with me in primary school, but now he's become a high official (고등관), or has become a property owner worth several thousand Wŏn," – that's all there is. Now, observe their actions. They are thoroughly plastered with the outer shell of hypocrisy, formality (假飾), and convention (因襲). Pretending to be the most patriotic (有志), pretending to be the most virtuous – these are entirely their possessions. They stink. They reek of decay (고린내). The dung beetle in the outhouse doesn't know the smell of dung. They are dung beetles. Therefore, they don't know the smell. I too was a dung beetle. That's why I trusted and respected them in the past.
They hate it when I refer to Confucius as Kong Qiu (孔丘) or Mencius as Meng Ke (孟軻). And they dislike the rejection of all empty formalities (虛禮). Furthermore, they condemn my regarding what they consider golden rules (金科玉條) as worn-out shoes (弊履). They called me "a brat who doesn't recognize the sages (聖賢)," "a brat who doesn't know his own family clan (門族)," "a brat disrespectful to elders (長上不恭)," and finally concluded with the noun "reprobate child" (망난이 자식).
That's right. I am a "reprobate child." At least, that's how they will see me.
Ah! Supreme glory! This term "reprobate child"—if I hadn't encountered it at this time, in this place, it's a great title of honor surpassing that of a duke or prince (公爵 大監) that I would never have heard.
Ah! I must not lose this emblem (徽號) of "reprobate child."

**Summary**

This collection of articles presents five Korean students' reflections on their summer vacation in 1924. The accounts vividly depict the widespread poverty, suffering caused by drought and floods, and social distress in both rural and urban areas of colonial Korea. Ma Ŭri laments unfulfilled plans and observes desperation leading to superstition. Hwang Nakchin gives a harrowing description of extreme rural poverty and exploitation, questioning the value of education amidst such misery. Yi Sangok, a work-study student unable to afford travel home to Manchuria, reflects on the power of money and calls for students to address societal problems rather than pursue personal gain. Chu Tonghun contrasts the hard labor of village women with the detached lives of students and "new women," urging for an education connected to reality and the dignity of labor. Finally, "SW Saeng" critiques the shallowness of both the poor aspiring only to low-level bureaucratic jobs and the hypocritical traditional elite, ultimately embracing the label "reprobate child" as a badge of honor for rejecting outdated values.

Historically, these accounts offer a raw, first-hand perspective on the dire socio-economic conditions in 1920s Korea under Japanese rule, particularly the devastating impact of natural disasters compounded by systemic issues like landlordism and debt. They reveal student anxieties about their future roles, a growing disillusionment with both traditional hierarchies and superficial modernization (represented by the missionary Kim O-hwan and the focus on salaried jobs), and early expressions of social consciousness and critique among the youth. The call for a practical, relevant education rooted in Korean reality and the final piece's defiant tone against established norms are particularly striking.

엉금이 쩍쩍 갈라진 희부현모판(苗床)에 샛놀아케 꼬실어진 벼싹을 광이 잡아 함부로 파 던저버리고, 석냥 그어 마지막 태워버리는 마음은 참아 생각키도 무서우며, 떼지은 村婦들이 고산에 올라 古塜을 파허트리고 해골을 일광 알에 흐터버리는 말은 듯기조차 소름이 끼치거니와, 「성숙은 한울에 맛겨라. 죽으면 죽는 날까지 살도록 힘쓸 뿐이다」는 듯이 그래도 일조의 희망을 갓고 미영 밧테 업드린 4,5의 村婦에게로 누님을 차저 산비탈에 다리를 비틀 그릴 때 갓닙은 모수적삼은 어느듯 둥에 착 들어붓고, 손에 쥐인 항커치는 자주자주 이마에 올라간다. 검은 치마와 힌 적삼의 경계삼아 호미짜루 만큼씩 내어 노흔 村婦들의(背後) 등더리가 四天에 半나마 기울어진 태양을 바루 바다 鳥銅가티 번적인다. 등에 달라부튼 삼베적삼 미트로 숨여 나온 땀이 등꼴에 모혀 鳥銅띄를 번쩍 지나자, 수건 쓴 이마로서 줄줄이 흘러 입가으로 턱을 스쳐 흙우헤 뚝뚝 떨어지는 것을 아는지 몰으는지? 호미＜115＞ 잡은 검은 팔만 샛놀아코 앙상한 미영 틈으로 내젓는다. 죽어가는 자식의 입에 약을 들이우고 실낫 가튼 생명을 들어다 보듯이 한 손으로 미영을 만적만적하고 호미로는 흙을 긁어 뿌리를 덥는다. 都會의 여자가 전기 風扇 겨테서 건반 우흐로 수정가튼 손까락을 춤추일 때에, 村婦들의 검은 팔은 흙덩이 속에서 생명의 꼿을 피워낸다. 신여자들이 패라쏠 미테서 허영의 분장에 수弱의 향수를 풍길 때에 村婦들은 일광 알에서 흙을 밟고 땀의 송가에 힘(力)의 춤을 추노나! 불어운 줄 알고도 뛰어들지 못하는 내 몸도 헐일 업는 신여자가 아니면 현대식  朝鮮學生이로라.
「아이고! 바테를 멋하러 오까 옷 버리고 더운듸 저-감나무 미트로 가거라」
「누님은 웨 바테서 일을 하시오. 남이 밧매면 나도 밧매야 되지오.」
「아야- 참 *광스런 소리도 다 ** 언제 왓단 게라오? 책은 그래도 들고-」
「책은 놀새 업다우. 어적게 와서도 밤에 이약기도 안하고 새벽에도 언제 깻든지 책만 보고 잇셔요」
「아이갸-제우도 안아까? 저런 동생둔 사람은 가물어도 걱정 업겟소」
「그늘에 맴이 팔자 아닌게라오! 저런 사람은! 돈업고 늙은 팔자만...기박...」 누님 겨테 잇는 村婦의 말을 바다 이편 끄트로서 늙은 소리가 떨린다,
「그늘에 맴이 팔자 이 어떠한 모욕인고! 벌지 안코 먹는다는 나의 생활을 바루 꿰뚤은 말이 아니냐.
「돈 업고 늙은 팔자만...기박...」이 무슨 哲理의 암시인거나! 일 못하는 사람의 장래를 신의 뜻 그대로 가르킴이다. 이것이 비록 「노파의 나를 불어히고, 자신을 설허하는 歎息」일망정 이는 틀림업는 신의 울음이다. 「맴이 팔자」가 불어가는 朝鮮을 보는 斷腸曲이다.
그러나 늙어 보지 못한 철업는 村婦의 귀에는 이 신의 울음이 들렷는지 말앗는지? 「맴이 팔자」를 웨 불어하는고? 단순한 그의 귀는 그늘에 노래하는 맴이를 들을찌언정 서릿바람에 설리 우는 맴이를 못들을 것이다. 풀을 제하기 위하야 호미질을 할지언정 窒素를 엇기 위하야 땅을 파는 줄은 몰은다. 이슬에 젓는 옷을 걱정할지언정 옷을 젓는 이슬에 노래할쭐은 몰은다. 가장에게 복종할 것은 배웟슬지언정 가장을 快樂케 할 것은 듯지도 못하엿다.＜116＞
單純單純! 單純히야 귀엽지마는, 勞働勞働! 勞働이야 중하지마는- 단순만으로 나아갈 太古 시절이 아니매 20세기의 복잡으로 단순을 붓어야 할 것이오, 노동만으로 그치는 인생이 아니매 노동에 학술을 조화하여야할 것이다. 도회문명은 학술에 사치하고 향촌 생활은 노동에 野昧한 朝鮮에 잇서 더욱 급하지 아니한가.
노동의 신성을 자각한 튼튼한 입지에서 학술의 위권을 실현하는 검센 팔뚝을 신념잇게 내들러 흥미 깁흔 인생의 꼿을 피워야 된다. 그리하야 이 흥미에 취하여 사는 낙원을 꿈여야 한다. 그러기 위하야는 교육의 정신을 노동에 세워야 겟다. 鸚鵡敎育의 現朝鮮에서 벗어나, 赤體로 땅을 밝을 수 잇는 일광알에 바로 설 수 잇는 교육을 시설하야 그리하야 태고의 村婦들을 20세기에 내세워 한 갓 틀에머리, 양복쟁이만 慕仰하는 自棄에서 신념구든 自重의 나라에 놀게 하자. 자기네가 가장 행복인 것을 노래하게 하자. 이 젊은 村婦로 하여금 「맴이 팔자」의 비천을 꾸짓게 하자. 「맴이 팔자」의 現朝鮮 사람들아 저- 노인의 탄식을 업세자! 들에 나매 잠뱅이 입고 괭이를 매어 생명의 약동에 땀의 공가를 불으며 서실에 들매 시험관 속의 천지에 놀고 시가의 나라에 춤추자! 朝鮮의 생명- 아-니 세계의 생명이 이것이다. 인류의 낙원이 그 어대매뇨.
사각사각하는 호미 소리에 구름 한덩이가 해를 잠깐 가리우나 村婦의 등은 그대로!, 감나무에 맴이 노래만 길게 늘인다. 저-구름에 생명의 비나, 아니 무덧는지? 朝鮮의 비야 언제나 오려노.

망난이 자식
SW生

참외 속을 내버린 자가 잇섯고 그것을 주어 먹은 자가 잇섯다. 내버린 자는 작년 4월 某高普에 입학하야 풍족한 學資로 절탕히 먹고 쓰고 놀기만 하다가 낙제를 하고 주저안즌 잇는 사람의 아들이고 주어 먹은 자는 조밥도 오히려 넉넉지 못하야 요새는 밀범벅으로 조석 운명을 하여가는 업는 사람의 아들이엿다.
아! 내버린 참외 속 그것은 먹다 남은 찍겍이엿다. 그러나＜117＞ 주어먹을 그 업는 사람의 아들은 그것이 한업시 맛잇섯스리라. 그리고 그 업는 사람은 그 아들을 보통학교에도 너흘수 업슴은 勿論 時在 당장에 먹을 것이 업스며 철업는 어린이들에게 「어머니 나 참외...」하며 졸니우면서도 그 흔한 참외 한 개를 사먹이지 못하야 남이 먹다 남은 찍겍이를 감사히 주어 먹게된 것이 다 이것이 한 평범한 일이로되 업는 이들의 살님살이의 일면이 나타난다.
「배주고 배 속 빌어 먹는다는」 속담말이 생각난다. 머리에 떠올은다.
이 「배주고 배 속 비러먹는 사람」의 떼는 발가는 곳마다 잇다. 눈가는 곳마다 잇다. 우리 朝鮮 농촌에도 찻다.
그들은 나에게 「요새 신문 상에 무슨 별소식이나 업다」하고 물엇다. 그 뭇는 태도가 은근하다. 무슨 비밀이나 뭇는 것 갓다. 그 마음부터 은근한 그 무엇을 기대함에서 나오는 말이다. 그러고 엇던 이는 요대로는 살 수 업다. 즉각에서 때려 부셔야 한다고 한다.
그들은 지금 막다른 골목에 드른 것이다. 압흐로 더 나아갈 수도 업고 뒤로 물너날 수도 업고 서지도 못하고 안지도 못할 엇절수 업는 事勢가 지여 주는 최후 부르지짐인 「때려부셔」이다.
「그 사람 가세도 넉넉지 못한 형편에 아들 하나는 잘 각궈서」하는 칭찬의 소리가 자자히 들닌다. 그 말의 뒷 등에는 「32원 자리 월급쟁이」이가 업뎌잇다. 즉 우리의 父老들은 그 「32원 자리 월급쟁이」를 큰 행세거리로 알며 아들 잘 각꾼 것-敎育-으로 안다.
보아라. 그들은 나에게 「지금 너 단기는 학교는 졸업하면 무엇으로 나아가니?」 하고 귀가 솔녀 엇던 때는 그 때문에 그들하고 맛나는 것이 실큼하리만큼 뭇지를 안느냐.
그들은 그것을 바라고-즉 월급푼이나 반는 것-밧을 파라 대이고 논을 파라 대이고 또 몸까지 파라 대이고 아들들과 손자들을 공부 식히는 것이다. 이런 결과에는 섭섭히 도저-천진난만한 소년소녀의 뇌까지 그 化를 식혀노코 만다지- 보통학교 졸업기에 학생들에게 「졸업 후 엇지 하겟느냐?」 물어보면 「師範學敎에 가겟다」고 대답하는 사람이 그 多大數이다. 學資는 업고 월급쟁이는 되고 십흐니까 할 수 업시 관비인 師範學敎 대문을 뛰지는 것이다. 또 우리 父老들이 그들을 그 길로 모라넛는 것이다.
모도가 다-할 수 업는 환경이 나아노은 현상이다.

내가 기왕에 신사라고 밋고 지식계급이라고 존경하든 그 사람들을 맛낫다. 아- 그들은 식은 피의 모임이엿다.＜118＞ 썩은 고기덩어리엿다.
그들의 수작을 들으라. 「공자님이 말슴이 엇지고 五倫三綱이 엇지고 아무톄 金門이 엇지고 李門이 엇지고」 이것이던지 「누구는 小學때에 나와 가티 공부하든데 지금은 고등관이 되엿느니 또는 몃 천원 재산가가 되엿느니」하는 것 뿐이다. 다시 그들의 행동을 살피라. 철두철미 허위의 가식과 因襲의 것 껍대기로 매질을 하여 버렷다. 가쟝 有志인 체 하고 가쟝 착한 자인 체 하는 것 등은 온통 그들의 소유물이다. 그들에게서는 냄새가 난다. 고린내가 난다. 변소의 똥벌레는 똥냄새를 모르는 법이다. 그들은 똥벌례이다. 그런 고로 그 냄새를 모른다. 나도 똥벌례엿섯다. 그럿키에 과거에서 그들을 신임하고 존경하든 것이다.
그들은 내가 孔丘라고 孟軻라고 부르는 것을 미워한다. 그리고 모든 허례배척함을 실허한다. 그 밧게 그들은 金科玉條로 생각하는 것을 나는 弊履와 가티 여기는 것을 辱한다. 그들은 나를 가르처 「성현도 몰나보는 놈」 「집안 문족도 몰으는 놈」 「長上不恭」하는 놈이라고 하더니 맛츰내 망난이자식이라는 명사로 결론을 하엿다.
그럿타. 나는 「망난이 자식이다」 적어도 그들은 그럿케 볼 것이다.
아! 無上의 광영! 이 「망난이 자식」이라는 말은 만일 요때 요곳에 맛나지 못하엿든들 들어보지 못 할 공작 대감 이상의 대존호이다.
아! 나는 이 「망난이 자식」의 徽號를 놋처서는 안된다.
