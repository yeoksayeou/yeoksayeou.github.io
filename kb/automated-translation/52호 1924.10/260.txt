Publication Date	1924년 10월 01일
Article Title	(희곡) 밋처가는 處女』(全一幕) (Play: A Mad Girl (Complete One Act))
Author	金永八
Article Type	희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

A Mad Girl
						Kim Yŏngp'al
- Characters -
Ch'unil (Student at a vocational school) 22 years old
Yŏngae (Ch'unil's lover) 19 years old
Hŭngsik (Ch'unil's friend) 23 years old
Ch'unil's Mother 53 years old
Yŏngae's Mother 49 years old
Inn Old Woman 60 years old

Time: Contemporary (Early spring, one year)
Place: A village below Inwangsan Mountain (A not-so-large inn)

Stage: The rear building of a not-so-large house, seemingly facing Inwangsan Mountain in the distance. Downstage, there is a gate leading to the road, and upstage, a small door used for entering and exiting the house. On the wooden floor (maru), a table and chairs are placed. The room door is slightly ajar, and a distant riverbank is visible.
When the curtain rises, Yŏngae stands center stage, and behind her, Ch'unil stands silently with a strange expression.
Yŏngae: (As if pained) Then I'll be going. I'll come again later (Looks at Ch'unil).
Ch'unil: (Also as if pained) Come again?
Yŏngae: (As if surprised) Ah, then I must come again. This time, I will also speak to Mother.
Ch'unil: (No reply).
Yŏngae: Then please wait for me, okay? (Forces open the door and exits quietly).
(Ch'unil watches Yŏngae leave intently, then goes below the maru, bows his head, and sits down.) — Stage silent for a moment — Inn Old Woman (Enters from upstage) <24>
Old Woman: Is the student here? Or not? Aigoo, you are here. Are you not going anywhere today?
Ch'unil: Yes—.
Old Woman: (Looking down at the bowed Ch'unil) Why, do you have some worries?
Ch'unil: No. Worries, what...?
Old Woman: But you look like you have many worries. It seems young people these days have many worries when spring comes.
Ch'unil: (Head still bowed) Perhaps—
Old Woman: (In a slightly mocking tone) What, is the student also feeling restless because spring has come?
Ch'unil: Perhaps?
Old Woman: What, it must be so. Ha-ha- (Laughs). But look here, student, what should I do? If I were even slightly well-off, I wouldn't say anything, but these days I'm so broke that the things I pawned are all about to be lost. So, even though I'm struggling, I haven't said anything until now, thinking you'd surely take care of it, but I'm speaking now because I'm afraid I'll lose everything at the pawnshop.
Ch'unil: (Finally lifts his head and looks at the Old Woman) I truly don't know what to say, I'm so sorry.
Old Woman: (Before he finishes speaking) No, no, not at all, what is there to be sorry about? Just cut that out. Yes, student— it's because I'm having a hard time these days—.
Ch'unil: Yes, I've known that for some time. The guests who were staying have all left too—.
Old Woman: (As if picking up her own train of thought) Right, that's why it's like this. If I had just a little something, would I talk like this over a few dozen won?
Ch'unil: (Calmly) Please don't worry about anything and just wait a few more days. It will all be paid within a few days.
Old Woman: (In a slightly strange tone) How can I wait like that? Student, you know my situation well, yet you say this? <27>
Ch'unil: (Shakes his head while greeting him) My pleasure is always the same. How is your pleasure?
Hŭngsik: (Sitting down) My pleasure? It’s— great—
Ch'unil: Of course, it must be great (As if envious). How wonderful it would be if I could become a happy person like you!
Hŭngsik: (Laughing heartily, ha-ha) Hey, is this how you tease a happy person?
Ch'unil: No, it's not teasing. You are truly the happiest person in this world, second to none. Even if I were to say that the two words 'your happiness' conquer the entire universe, I would have no choice but to believe it. Ah! Happy person!! (In a very sentimental tone)
Hŭngsik: Hey, Ch'unil, what you're saying sounds just like reciting poetry. No matter what, someone with a poet's disposition is different (As if teasing).
Ch'unil: What? Me, with a poet's disposition? Hey, my head hurts. Are you telling me to become an addict too? These days, there are too many artists, too many poets, and too many writers, so it's impossible to discern what is true. From my perspective, it seems everyone is addicted to art, addicted to literature, and addicted to poetry.
Hŭngsik: (Nodding his head) Hmm— that might be so. But what can you do? It's the journey of civilization. Just as something perfect is discovered amidst imperfection, couldn't it be that a true artist, or a poetic genius like Ta-kol (Tagore?), might emerge from amidst such addiction?
Ch'unil: (Shaking his head) Don't fantasize. Don't fantasize.
Hŭngsik: (Adjusting his posture) So, how is your 'sweetheart's' pleasure these days? Still the same?
Ch'unil: (Bowing his head) How could it be like yours?
Hŭngsik: What's wrong again? It seems you haven't been able to meet lately.
Ch'unil: We meet, we meet every day, but?
Hŭngsik: Then why again? <25>
Ch'unil: (As if apologetic) I know all that, but what can I do? I have no money—.
Old Woman: (Voice slightly sharp) Hey, what kind of talk is this? Huh? Look here— so you can just take female students or whatever kind of students around as you please, spending money, while this widow sheds blood and sweat for a month, rushing around worried about being late, cooks your meals, and then after a month, even two months pass, you don't give a single penny, and when asked, you say, "I need to have money first?" Hey, where in the world is such talk acceptable?
Ch'unil: I too have been under much financial pressure lately. So, I will pay you as soon as my finances allow.
Old Woman: (With an increasingly cold attitude) I don't know. What is this modern talk of financial pressure or economic permission?
Ch'unil: (A bit more kindly) Madam, please don't say anything more and just bear with it for a few more days. Of course, my mother has also come up, so if she sees my predicament, she will likely give at least some amount.
Old Woman: (In a slightly softer tone) Well, that's right. Since your mother has come from the countryside, it seems likely she has some money, but she hasn't given any, that's why I'm saying this.
Ch'unil: How long has it been since Mother arrived? Can I ask for money before even a few days have passed? That's why it's like this.
Old Woman: By the way, where did the madam go?
Ch'unil: My mother? She went out a while ago saying she was going to buy something, but she hasn't come back yet.
Old Woman: (Appearing somewhat subdued, fills her long pipe with tobacco and lights it) Then I'll trust only the student. Don't disappoint me and please be sure to give it.
(From outside, a voice calling "Ch'unil—" is heard. Old Woman: Then I'll trust you and go inside. Exits.)
Ch'unil: Yes, who is it—?
Hŭngsik: (Entering abruptly) Is your pleasure good? Why has it been so hard to see you? <26>
Ch'unil: No… (With a strange expression).
Hŭngsik: (Looking at Ch'unil, as if realizing something) Oh, hey, I heard your mother came up?
Ch'unil: (Casually) That's right.
Hŭngsik: Hey, why do you look so lifeless today? Are you unwell somewhere? If you're not feeling well, you should lie down a bit, eh— hey (Looks up at him).
Ch'unil: Yes, thank you. It’s not that I’m unwell, but it’s not an illness that can be cured by lying down (As if deeply comforted). Hey, Hŭngsik, just between you and me, my spirit has been wounded lately. Of course, you probably don't know that I spend each day clinging to life amidst anguish and sorrow, passing the time with tears. But friends who don't know just think of me as a happy person, no, even you acknowledge me as such (Sighs once). It's not that I haven't tasted happiness at all, but that happiness was momentary, fleeting! In inverse proportion, the sorrow and grief— ah— ah— (As if choking up).
Hŭngsik: (Listening doubtfully to Ch'unil's words) What's wrong, huh—?
Ch'unil: It's not 'what's wrong'—
Hŭngsik: (Even more doubtful) Then tell me the story in detail (Quietly) Is it heartbreak?
Ch'unil: (Forcing a smile) Then, you happy man of the earth, who is experiencing heartbreak? But heartbreak for an unfortunate person like me? Then, does that mean I have no suffering?
Hŭngsik: (Even more doubtful) What kind of ridiculous thing is this person saying - (Laughing) No, hey, I don't get the point of what you're saying. Is this your philosophy?
Ch'unil: (Says nothing).
Hŭngsik: Others say it's unfortunate not to have experienced love, and they say people become pessimistic about the world if they suffer heartbreak, but you strangely say heartbreak means happiness… anyway, what kind of suffering and sorrow is it?
Ch'unil: Don't talk about it. My head aches, I could die. <28>
Hŭngsik: Hey, should a person like me, who has no lover, no, who hasn't even experienced love once, live?
Ch'unil: (As if slightly cheerful) Right, right. What you just said is exactly what I told you earlier. The fact that you haven't yet tread the path of that hardship (苦境) called love is precisely why I called you a happy person earlier.
Hŭngsik: (As if surprised) What? Hardship?
Ch'unil: (Nodding as if agreeing) That's right, it's hardship. I don't know what your view of love is, but from my perspective, I have no choice but to call it suffering. Of course, one can think of love simply in terms of pleasure, but that's what you might call momentary love, without roots. And it would be nothing more than physical love. However, if you take one step further and dissect true love, you inevitably find suffering there.
Hŭngsik: That must be true (As if moved). For young men and women, regardless of who they are, their hearts must burn immensely because of love. But is that suffering? (In a tone of doubt).
Ch'unil: (Waving his hand) Ugh—. It's even more so for us Koreans. We suffer from this and that. As ideals and reality diverge, the bond between father and son breaks, and following the break in the father-son bond, doesn't the sense of morality disappear? When the sense of morality disappears, what is family, what is society? Isn't that so?
Hŭngsik: Well. I know that's the case, but from my perspective, I don't think there's anything to suffer about. Doesn't every youth in the dawn of life taste suffering? But without that suffering, one cannot know the true value of love. Therefore, I think it's necessary to taste that suffering.
Ch'unil: Yes. That's what I mean. It's not about trying to avoid some small scope of suffering. If it's the suffering of one's entire life, isn't that indeed a big problem?
Hŭngsik: Then are you saying you also have such a big problem?
Ch'unil: (Nodding listlessly) I do—
Hŭngsik: (Observing Ch'unil's demeanor) What? You have such a big problem? <29>
Ch'unil: If I didn't, why would I live with such sorrow etched on my face amidst this anguish? Isn't that why I envy you? (As if asking a rhetorical question).
Hŭngsik: (With a solemn attitude) Right. Are you trying to avoid that suffering? (As if having caught on to something).
Ch'unil: Right. That might be the root of my anguish. I just don't know what to do now. I can't judge myself. Ah— how will this hardship unfold?
Hŭngsik: (As if sympathizing) So. Have you tried telling your lover about suffering such pain?
Ch'unil: What could I possibly say? (Both are silent for a moment).
Hŭngsik: Then the money hasn't come yet, I suppose.
Ch'unil: What is money even—
Hŭngsik: Then what are you supposed to do? Starve to death in Seoul?
Ch'unil: That's why they're telling me to come down home right away. They say they'll send the money immediately if only I say I'll come down (As if suspicious) How did they find out about that condition, I wonder—!?
Hŭngsik: That's why arranged marriage is a bag of worries.
Ch'unil: No. It's not just the evils of arranged marriage. It became like this because I did wrong.
Hŭngsik: Hey. Even so, you shouldn't bury your ideals and individuality. Don't you have your own individuality? Then, a revolution of individuality is necessary to express that individuality.
Ch'unil: Yes. I know that. But—
Hŭngsik: What do you mean, 'but'? If you know, you should realize it (Looks at Ch'unil). Someone has to be sacrificed. Just make a decision manfully, whatever it is. Hey— don't just sit cooped up worrying. Your face has changed so much lately. Do you think just worrying constantly will solve anything? So, what did your mother say? Have you even tried talking to her? <30>
Ch'unil: (Angrily stands up abruptly and paces back and forth) She doesn't know the details. Because I haven't said anything at all.
Hŭngsik: Why haven't you told her the detailed story? If you don't tell your mother such things, who do you tell?
Ch'unil: What can I do if I don't want to tell anyone? That's why, before things explode, I have no choice but to run far away somewhere (Bows his head as if in pain).
Hŭngsik: (As if concerned) Hey. Why are you talking like that? Is your character that weak? Let's try to live a bit more stubbornly. Let's assume, as you say, that you run away somewhere. Then your very self wouldn't disappear forever, would it?
Ch'unil: (Listlessly) Maybe it will disappear forever.
Hŭngsik: (Laughing heartily) Cast off such a narrow-minded personality. Yes, only then will your anguish and suffering recede. What kind of absurd talk is that? So, are you saying you have no principles, no convictions?
Ch'unil: That's right, it's been a long time since I lost my principles and convictions. Do I have any reason to have principles and convictions when I'm studying and living off my wife's family's money?
Hŭngsik: (Laughing) Well, where is such logic? Just because your wife's family feeds you and pays for your studies, does that mean you have to submit to them?
Ch'unil: (Just nods his head as if agreeing).
(Both silent for a moment).
(Far away, the bell of a newspaper delivery person is heard, and the electric lights turn on.)
Hŭngsik: (After looking at Ch'unil for a long while) Have you eaten dinner?
Ch'unil: No, are you hungry?
Hŭngsik: It hasn't been long since I ate lunch— But really, what should we do? It doesn't seem like your business anymore. Still, you should somehow manage your mother well— <31>
Ch'unil: Do you think my mother, an elder from the old days, will listen? Moreover, a female student? Is that all? I have a legal wife? On top of that, my wife's family pays for my studies. If I open my mouth even a little to say something, thunder will strike from a clear blue sky. Even without that, she has roughly sensed the situation, and last night there was all sorts of preaching. She said, "Instead of studying like you're supposed to, you seem to just wander around all the time. What kind of behavior is that for a man?" and mentioned that rumors are bad even among the relatives.
Hŭngsik: (In a somewhat excited tone) Then you've regarded women in general as mere playthings, not knowing what ethics or morality are, deceiving others while deceiving your own conscience. Do you think that's a special characteristic of manhood? And you feel no pangs of conscience? Then isn't your suffering something you brought upon yourself? (Glares at Ch'unil).
Ch'unil: (As if ashamed) Forgive me. Perhaps that was my curiosity. No— perhaps it's the psychology of men in general during a transitional period—.
Hŭngsik: (In an excited tone) What? It's not just being drawn by curiosity. It's because there's definitely a base nature involved. Curiosity doesn't exist without desire. Unreasonable desire comes from a base nature.
Ch'unil: But isn't acting without thinking of the consequences, whether it's rash behavior or whatever, the courage of youth?
Hŭngsik: Then why don't you fight it out to the end with that courage? Where did the courage to fight to the end suddenly disappear to?
Ch'unil: (Sits silently, as if deep in thought, without answering) Hey. Then what should I do? Tell me the best course of action, please.
Hŭngsik: (As if somewhat troubled) The best course of action isn't anything special. I believe it lies solely in your determination and decisive action.
Ch'unil: Well, what is that determination and decisive action? I don't know what the truth is! <32>
Hŭngsik: Yes— Then I'll tell you— You've always told me that you love Miss Yŏngae, dedicating your life to her. Since you told me that, I assume you must have said even more honeyed words to Miss Yŏngae herself. When you said those words, you must have surely resolved to divorce your future wife, right? I don't think for a moment that you loved her merely for temporary pleasure or selfish desire, so you must feel the same, right?
Ch'unil: (As if answering reluctantly) Well, yes—
Hŭngsik: (As if anxious) Then just get a divorce.
Ch'unil: (No answer).
Hŭngsik: Why no answer? Can't you get a divorce?
Ch'unil: Because the people in my family feel differently than I do.
Hŭngsik: What, family members? (In a slightly louder voice) No, then did you love Miss Yŏngae for the sake of your family members?
Ch'unil: ... (No answer) ...
Hŭngsik: You talk this way and that way well enough, but your love for Miss Yŏngae is insufficient! (Stands up abruptly as if angry) I'm leaving. Resolve it well. Don't worry too much—
Ch'unil: (Also standing up) Why? Are you leaving? Stay a bit longer. Talk to Mother when she comes, okay?
(Just as Hŭngsik hesitates, about to leave, Ch'unil's Mother enters from downstage carrying a bundle.)
Ch'unil: You're back now, Mother.
Mother: Yes.
Hŭngsik: (As if cheerful) Hello. When did you come up during this time?
Mother: Yes, have you been well? (As if pleased).
Hŭngsik: (Looking at the items) Wow, you bought a lot of things.
Mother: A lot? Aigoo, it's just some fabric I exchanged. Is your pleasure good? (Sits on the maru). <33>
Hŭngsik: (Pacing back and forth while standing) My pleasure is always the same.
Ch'unil: Mother, aren't you hungry?
Mother: No, I stopped by your aunt's house on the way and had a bite of dinner. You must be hungry.
Ch'unil: No, I'm not hungry.
(All silent for a moment).
Ch'unil: (Frowning) Mother, there's big trouble.
Mother: (Slightly surprised) What is it?
Ch'unil: It's because of money.
Mother: What money?
Ch'unil: (Looking up at Mother) I'm constantly embarrassed because of the meal costs.
Mother: How much is it?
Ch'unil: It's about fifty won.
Mother: What, fifty won? How much is fifty won?
Ch'unil: Mother, are you surprised because you don't even know the value of money? Fifty won is two thousand five hundred *ryang*.
Mother: (Nodding as if finally understanding) Ah, two thousand five hundred *ryang*. I don't understand these modern amounts. All this talk of tens of won, several won (After a pause). Well, didn't the wife's family say they'd send money if you came down?
Ch'unil: Wife's family? (As if reluctant to hear it).
Mother: (As if surprised) Why?
Ch'unil: What about school?
Mother: What about school if there's no money? (Looks at him).
Ch'unil: (Stares into the distance, no reply). <34>
Hŭngsik: (Listening to the conversation) Why, was it decided they wouldn't send money?
Mother: (In a disappointed tone) What, apparently a lot of talk arose at that house.
Hŭngsik: What kind of talk?
Mother: If I tell it all, it will never end. I've heard so much (As if angry). Look here, young man. As I said last night, where is such immature behavior acceptable, huh? If he were a completely ignorant person who knows nothing, it might be different, but this guy attends some school? They say it's the highest level school, right? So, a fellow attending such a school, devoid of loyalty and human feeling, comes to Seoul thanks to his wife's family, eats well, dresses well, studies well— shouldn't he show that much sincerity to his wife's family? But sincerity aside, I heard he's running around infatuated with some wench wearing those flimsy, calf-high skirts and waist-length blouses of these modern things, just provoking others' feelings. How could they not be upset? That's why they have no choice but not to send money.
Ch'unil: (As if pained) Mother, I understand. Please stop. I have many thoughts right now too.
Mother: (Glaring) What thoughts? Your father isn't here, and you disgrace the only one left, me, and now what thoughts? (Sheds tears) Because my fate is harsh, now that I'm old, I even got cursed at by my son's mother-in-law for raising my child wrong (Glares resentfully). If you think about it...? I should just die quickly— (Changes tone) I don't know... They insisted I bring you back. You must go tomorrow.
Ch'unil: Bring who back?
Mother: Who? It's you.
Ch'unil: Me? I'll die before I go.
Mother: (As if furious) What? Look at this wicked child! Why do you hurt your mother's heart like this? Did you still not understand what I said last night? Ugh, you child unworthy of being called human! (Stands up abruptly in anger and exits inside).
(Ch'unil and Hŭngsik look at each other, speechless). <35>
Ch'unil: (Stands up abruptly, in a tragic tone) Hey Hŭngsik. Shall we go out somewhere?
Hŭngsik: Where?
Ch'unil: Let's have a drink of alcohol or something.
Hŭngsik: Alcohol?
Ch'unil: Yes, I have no choice but to drink alcohol and calm my heart, even if just for a moment. Right now, my chest is burning like coal in a locomotive (Looks up).
Hŭngsik: (As if frustrated) If you think about everything so hastily like that, you'll often end up failing—
Ch'unil: Failure? I've already braced myself for things like failure. I know that now, nothing but the sharp curse of death awaits me (Sheds tears).
Hŭngsik: (Watches Ch'unil silently for a while, then as if sympathizing) Now, there's no use in advising you further, and I don't think there's anything more to discuss with you. I only intend to say one thing before I go. That is, whichever way you choose, exert the revolution of your individuality. And stand firm on the front line.
(Stands up abruptly, looks at Ch'unil meaningfully once more, and exits).
Ch'unil: (Without glancing at the departing Hŭngsik, clenches his fists as if having made a decision) Yes, revolution of individuality? Then me? Ah! But me?
(Yŏngae and Yŏngae's Mother enter).
Yŏngae: (Entering the door, barely seeing Ch'unil's bowed head) Ch'unil-ssi! (Calls in a trembling voice).
Yŏngae's Mother: (Approaches Ch'unil, who is sitting with his head bowed) Hey, why are you sitting there absently? (In a kind voice).
Ch'unil: (Stands up abruptly as if startled) Ah! You've come (Looks at Yŏngae). Have you eaten?
Yŏngae: (Barely sits on the edge of the maru, looking desolate) Yes.
Ch'unil: (Looks at Yŏngae's Mother, gesturing to the maru) Please sit over there. You must have had a hard time coming all this way. <36>
Yŏngae's Mother: What, far? What's far— And I came to see someone in this neighborhood, so I have to go soon, why sit down?
Ch'unil: Still, please sit just for a moment (Looks up).
Yŏngae's Mother: I'd like to sit, but then again— (Smiling) If I interfere with the two of you talking... hahaha...
(Ch'unil forces a pained smile, Yŏngae's face turns red).
Yŏngae: (Hesitating) Mother, then when I leave, I'll go to that house, please wait for me, okay?
Yŏngae's Mother: Alright, then come there. (Looking at Ch'unil) Then I'll be going. Send her along soon after just a moment.
Ch'unil: (No reply).
(Yŏngae's Mother exits. Stage silent for some time).
Ch'unil: (As if casually) Yŏngae-ssi!
Yŏngae: (Head bowed, slightly startled by Ch'unil's call) Yes? (Looks up).
Ch'unil: (Moves closer to Yŏngae and sits) After you left earlier, did you manage to think of any good ideas? (In a trembling voice).
Yŏngae: (Looks listlessly into the distance, saying nothing).
Ch'unil: (In a tragic tone) After sending you off earlier, I thought about various things and agonized greatly, but it seems my thoughts will have no effect.
Yŏngae: (Says nothing).
Ch'unil: Of course, I roughly mentioned it earlier too, but my life lately feels like I'm treading the path of death. (In an excited tone) But Yŏngae-ssi! You must not misunderstand my words even slightly.
Yŏngae: (Finally lifts her head and looks at Ch'unil doubtfully) Yes? Misunderstand? What?
Ch'unil: No, I mean, please don't think that perhaps my love for you, Yŏngae-ssi, is lacking.
Yŏngae: (Voice sounding tearful) Ch'unil-ssi! Then me—!?
Ch'unil: (Wipes tears with a handkerchief and looks up at the star-twinkling sky). <37>
Yŏngae: (Sobbing) Then you mean you'll cast me off forever like an old shoe? (In an excited tone) With what mouth can you say such things now? When you repeatedly made sweet promises to me in various ways, what did I say? Although I had many words, my one phrase was "A man's words are...?" And didn't I say "A man's love" cannot be trusted? You know well that wasn't just once or twice, right?
Ch'unil: (Bowing his head as if ashamed) Yes, I remember well. And my heart pounds whenever I imagine how precious those words were.
Yŏngae: (At the peak of resentment, in an angry tone) What? Your heart pounds?
Ch'unil: You are too agitated. Don't we have so many things to discuss peacefully? And me too, that's right. The soul of my life truly wants to live in the palace of love forever. And I want to drink the wine of true love and be embraced within the beautiful flowers of love. But unfortunately, what can I do when my widowed mother forbids it? Yes? Mother wouldn't oppose it like this, but what can I do when she says the engagement happened while my father was alive? Therefore, Yŏngae-ssi's warm love will surely not leave my warm heart until the moment my final consciousness disappears (Wipes tears).
Yŏngae: (Sobs and cries).
Ch'unil: (Tries to embrace the crying Yŏngae's shoulders).
Yŏngae: (Pushing him away) Don't do that. Don't torment me like this— I sacrificed everything. Let alone my parents' words, I sacrificed my responsibilities and my own happiness, all for your love— You are a man, aren't you? (Cries).
Ch'unil: (As if frustrated) Ah! I— It's not just Mother's words. (Sighs painfully) There's more. There's something else I truly cannot say.
Yŏngae: (Just keeps crying). <38>
Ch'unil: (As if pleading) Ah! Please forgive me. And please listen to my words. And please curse this vile person. Yes, Yŏngae-ssi.
Yŏngae: (Unable to contain her anger, cries, then speaks) Don't deceive me. You are deceiving me, aren't you?
Ch'unil: (As if defeated) What did I deceive you about?
Yŏngae: You also have a flower-like lover, don't you? And you're deceiving me?
Ch'unil: (As if choked up, speechless).
Yŏngae: (As if speaking sincerely, changes her tone) But I alone have loved purely and with all my heart. I thought that was the root of my life, but I didn't know it would now come to burn my heart.
Ch'unil: Ah! Yŏngae-ssi! Please don't attack me so harshly. I did everything wrong. It was wrong to first deceive my own conscience, ignore society, turn my back on my family, and love you. And please pity and forgive this body being cast out from love.
Yŏngae: (Listens as if not understanding the meaning, no reply).
Ch'unil: (In a desperate tone) It's money that made me like this. Money has made me a cripple. Without knowing anything, just with the promise of sending me to Seoul for studies, I didn't know this body had fallen into lifelong misfortune, I didn't know the joys of Seoul and Tokyo (東京 - likely referring to the broader world/modernity associated with studying abroad or advanced education influenced by Japan) would give birth to today's tragedy.
(Hŭngsik enters quickly).
Hŭngsik: (Seeing the situation of Ch'unil and Yŏngae crying, feigns ignorance) Are you two well? (To Yŏngae) But why has it been so impossible to see you? Yes? Have you been well during this time?
Yŏngae: (Forcing a smile onto her face, desolate) Yes, are you well? (Stands up).
Ch'unil: (Sitting, looks up at Hŭngsik) Have you eaten dinner?
Hŭngsik: (Cheerfully) Of course, would I live without eating— (Sees Yŏngae standing, gestures apologetically to the maru) Please sit there. <39>
Yŏngae: I should be going! Please sit here (Gestures to the maru).
Hŭngsik: I'm terribly sorry. You say you're leaving because I came (Smiling).
Yŏngae: No, it's not that (As if embarrassed).
Ch'unil: (Looking at Hŭngsik and Yŏngae) Please sit there, both of you?
(Hŭngsik sits. Yŏngae also sits as if reluctantly. Momentary silence).
Hŭngsik: (Suddenly) Hey, Ch'unil (Looks up).
Ch'unil: Yes?
Hŭngsik: Will you listen to just one word from me?
Ch'unil: What word?
Hŭngsik: Well, whatever word it is, it won't be harmful to hear.
Ch'unil: (Somewhat suspicious) If it's worth listening to, I'll listen.
Hŭngsik: (As if confirming) Then you really mean it?
Ch'unil: Just hurry up and say it.
Hŭngsik: Then, would you perhaps like to go to Pukkando (North Jiandao/Manchuria) by tonight's train?
Ch'unil: (As if utterly dumbfounded) What, Pukkando?
Hŭngsik: (Observing Ch'unil's reaction) Do you dislike it?
Ch'unil: Well, man, even a bolt out of the blue in the dark night has its limits. What is this sudden Pukkando?
Hŭngsik: (Smiling) So you mean you dislike going?
Ch'unil: (With a slight smile amidst laughter) No, rather than disliking it, isn't it strange? Suddenly— (Looks up).
Hŭngsik: (As if pleading) I myself am surprised, but I suddenly received this telegram. I absolutely have to go, but as you know, I'm not in a position to leave home. So, you immediately came to mind. That's why I ran here in one step. <40> And the living expenses seem considerable too. (Laughing heartily, ha-ha) What are you thinking about? Let's go quickly.
Yŏngae: (Listening to the two men talk, looking back and forth between them) Where is Pukkando? How far is it from here? (As if asking Hŭngsik).
Hŭngsik: Pukkando? Pukkando is China's (支那 - Shina, older term for China) land. It's far, very far.
Yŏngae: (As if surprised) Then how can you go there? To such a distant place?
Ch'unil: (After seeming to think for a while, suddenly as if resolved) Hey, Hŭngsik, I have summoned my final courage.
Hŭngsik: (As if pleased) Ah! Steadfast hero! Then you mean you will go.
Ch'unil: (In an excited tone) Yes, I must go. I have become a person who cannot but go. I will forget everything and go— But even if I say I won't think about anything as I leave now, what will become of the impending fate of my only mother... (Sheds tears) (All maintain deep silence).
Hŭngsik: Don't say anything and just go. It was hard for my eyes to bear seeing you suffer. So, I exerted all my strength to save your character and tell you to live your life together with the life of your beloved Yŏngae-ssi, leaving this bitter human world to live in a world of love. Of course, your mother must follow you there later...
Yŏngae: (Looks up with tear-filled eyes, as if deeply moved).
Hŭngsik: (Energetically) If you just hesitate now, you will throw yourself into the pit of self-destruction. Morality, society, no, even more than that, parents— but isn't there room to think? Ultimately, whatever anyone says, there is nothing other than myself. I believe society exists because I exist. If I cease to exist, what need is there for society or morality? Huh? (Looks up).
Ch'unil: (Standing up, looking most cheerful) Yes, I understood well. That's right, yes, I— but until now I carried rotten morality upon my head, bowed that head, and bound my body to that cold thing called society. But I thought that was the truth. However, although I too have obeyed a gentle disposition until now, it is regrettable, truly regrettable, that I yielded while condemning it as not being the character of us youth standing on the revolutionary front line— <41>
Hŭngsik: (In an excited tone) Ah! Then now you too have opened your eyes to individuality and raised high the flag of the revolution of individuality! Well— then hurry and pack your things— I too will go quickly and make preparations. Come home soon.
(Hŭngsik exits).
Yŏngae: (Looking at Ch'unil) Ch'unil-ssi, then you're going to Pukkando?
Ch'unil: (Smiling, with the joy of hope) Yes, I'm going.
Yŏngae: You're going?
Ch'unil: Why do you say that?
Yŏngae: (Sheds tears).
Ch'unil: (Seeing the crying Yŏngae, as if comforting her) If I go, I'll go together. I will go with Yŏngae-ssi. Yŏngae-ssi, please go with me. Yes?
Yŏngae: (As if doubting) With me?
Ch'unil: (In a trembling voice) Yes, I'll go with Yŏngae-ssi— Leaving my country now, where the mountains are unfamiliar and the waters strange, is solely for Yŏngae-ssi, no, for myself, and even more than that, to continue our love eternally, I cannot but go to Pukkando.
Yŏngae: (Tears streaming down) Ch'unil-ssi, thank you. Thank you for taking even me along. But please don't go for my sake. Because you have an elderly mother, right? Of course, sacrificing love is also great suffering, but leaving behind one mother, who will attend the netherworld (冥界) not long after, for the sake of love is surely not something we should do—
Ch'unil: (As if unable to bear it) Stop, stop, please endure. Stop saying that. Just stop talking about Mother— Now, hurry and get up—
Yŏngae: (Rising quietly, clings to Ch'unil) Ch'unil-ssi, then are you saying you will sacrifice everything for me?
Ch'unil: (Shedding tears, embracing her) Yes, Yŏngae-ssi, I will. For precious love... <42>
Yŏngae: (Still embraced) Thank you. Even if I die—
Ch'unil: Until when— Ah— Ah— Will we live like this?
Yŏngae: If only Ch'unil-ssi's love doesn't change...
Ch'unil: It won't change, but if some obstacle arises again—
Yŏngae: Please don't say such things—
Ch'unil: (Helping Yŏngae up) Now— Yŏngae-ssi, get up. Hŭngsik-gun must be waiting, so I need to go see him quickly. Let's go (Goes into the room and comes out holding a trunk).
(Just as Ch'unil and Yŏngae are about to exit, Ch'unil's Mother enters from upstage).
Ch'unil's Mother: (Seeing the two about to leave) Where are you going?
(Ch'unil and Yŏngae, startled, look back and hesitate).
Ch'unil's Mother: (Snatching the trunk) Where are you two going in the middle of the night? Huh? (Trembles with anger).
Ch'unil: (Says nothing).
Ch'unil's Mother: (Looking at Ch'unil) You brat, you wretched brat! Where are you going, leaving me behind? Huh? Do you think you can walk properly leaving me behind? Ugh, you brat— (Shouts) You wench, who are you? Huh? (Looks at Yŏngae).
Yŏngae: (Just cries, no answer).
Ch'unil's Mother: (Shouting) Go away, you wench! What are you crying for? Is another man's husband, who already has a bride (시약시), tasty, you wench?
Ch'unil: (As if startled) Mother— That word—
Ch'unil's Mother: (In an angry tone) What, 'that word'? Am I telling a lie? What about a fellow who studies and lives thanks to his wife's family?
Yŏngae: (Hearing these words, feels faint, cries "Ah!" and collapses).
Ch'unil: (Catching her and helping her up) Ah! Yŏngae-ssi, please forgive me. I am already one who, however meaninglessly, stood before the sacred rites of marriage— Please forgive me. Yes? Yŏngae-ssi. <43>
Ch'unil's Mother: (Furious) You beggar brat! (Looks at Ch'unil).
Yŏngae: (Face showing a strange smile, stands up) Hahaha, me? Me? Hey, you brat, am I a ghost? Hahaha— It's so funny I could die! (Tries to run out).
Ch'unil: (Embracing her tightly, crying and pleading) Ah! Yŏngae-ssi— Yŏngae-ssi is— (Sobs. Yŏngae pushes him away and exits). Ah, Yŏngae-ssi? Yŏngae-ssi? (Raises both hands) Ah— Yŏngae-ssi? Please forgive me— (Collapses).
(Far away, Yŏngae's cackling laughter and clapping sound are heard).
- Quietly, curtain -

**Summary**

This one-act play, "A Mad Girl," portrays the tragic dilemma of Ch'unil, a student financially supported by his wife's family through an arranged marriage. He falls in love with Yŏngae, leading to conflict with his mother, his financial backers (in-laws), and his own conscience. Pressured by tradition, lack of money, and his mother's disapproval, Ch'unil struggles between his love for Yŏngae and his obligations. His friend Hŭngsik encourages him to choose individuality and escape with Yŏngae to Pukkando (Manchuria). However, the plan collapses when Ch'unil's mother confronts them, revealing his marriage. The revelation drives Yŏngae to madness, and Ch'unil collapses in despair.
From a historical perspective, the play vividly illustrates the social tensions in 1920s colonial Korea: the clash between traditional values (arranged marriage, filial piety, family duty) and modern ideals (individual choice, romantic love, personal identity - '개성'). The financial dependence of students on family or in-laws, the precarious economic situation highlighted by the innkeeper's demands, and the consideration of migration to Manchuria (Pukkando) as an escape route from social and economic pressures in Korea are particularly revealing aspects for historians studying the period. The reference to modern female students ('녀학생') and changing fashion also reflects the societal shifts occurring.

흥식, 사정하는 듯이) 내 자신도 의외로 생각을 하네마는 별안간 이러한 젼보를 바덧네 그려. 내가 꼭 가야만 하야할 터인데 자네 아다십히 나는 집을 떠날 형편이 못 되네그려. 그래 얼는 자네 생각이 나데. 그래서 한 거름에＜40＞ 뛰여왓지. 그리고 생활비도 상당할 듯하데. (너털우슴을 허허 우스면서)무얼 생각을 하고 잇나. 얼는 가지.
영애, (량인의 하는 말을 듯고 번가라가며 치여다 보고 잇다가) 북간도가 어대이야요. 여긔서 얼마나 되여요.(흥식에게 뭇는 듯이)
흥식, 북간도요? 북간도는 지나 땅이올시다. 멀기도 멀지오.
영애, (놀나는 듯이) 그럼 그 곳에를 엇더케 감닛가? 그 먼 곳에를?
춘일, (한참 생각을 하는 듯이 잇다가 돌연 결심한 듯이) 여보게 흥식이 나는 나의 최후의 용긔를 내엿네.
흥식, (깃분 듯이) 아! 굿세인 용사여! 그러면 자네는 가겟다는 말일세 그려.
춘일, (흥분된 어됴로) 응 가야하겟네. 나는 가지 아니할 수 업는 몸이 되엿스닛가. 나는 모든 것을 닛고 가지- 그러나 내가 지금 가는 이 마당에는 모든 것을 생각지 안는다 하여도 오즉 한분 게신 어머님의 닥처오는 운명을 엇지할까... (눈물 짓는다) (일동 깁흔 침묵을 직힐 뿐)
흥식, 자네는 아모 말도 하지 말고 가게. 나는 자네의 고통 밧는 것이 나의 눈으로 참아 보기가 어려웟섯네. 그래서 나의 힘을 다하야 자네의 인격을 살니고 자녜의 일생이라는 것을 사랑하는 영애씨의 일생과 함끠 이 쓰린 인간게를 떠나 사랑의 세게에서 살나는 말일세. 물론 어머님도 뒤를 따라 그곳으로 가시게 하야지...
영애, (감개무량한 듯이 눈물어린 눈으로 치여다 본다)
흥식, (긔운잇게) 만약 자녜가 지금 망사리고만 잇스면 자네의 자신을 스스로 파멸경으로 던지이는 것일세. 도덕보다도 사회보다도 아니 그보다도 부모 그러나 생각할 여디가 업지 아니한가? 오즉 무엇이니 무엇이니 하여도 내 이외에는 아모것도 업네. 내가 잇슴으로 사회가 잇는 줄 아네. 내가 업서지면 사회라는 것도 도덕이라는 것이 무엇에 필요한가. 응 (치여다 본다)
춘일, (가장 쾌활한 듯이 니러나면서) 응 잘 아라들엇네. 그러이 그래 나는 그러나 잇때ㅅ것 썩어진 도덕을 머리 우에다 언고 그 머리를 굽히여 사회라는 찬 것에게 나의 몸을 얼거 매엿섯네. 그러나 나는 그것이 진리인 줄 알엇섯네. 그러나 나 역 자금것 온순한 셩격에 복종하엿지마는 그것은 혁명 전선에 선 우리 청년의 성격이 아닌 것을 비난하면서도 내가 굴하여 온 것은 분하이 분헤-＜41＞
흥식, (흥분된 어됴로) 아! 그리면 인제는 자네도 개성의 눈을 뜨고 개성의 혁명긔를 놉히 들엇다는 말일세 그려- 자- 그러면 어서 행장을 수습하게- 나는 또 빨니 가서 준비를 하여노케 곳 집으로 오게.
(흥식 퇴댱)
영애, (춘일을 바라보고) 춘일씨 그럼 북간도로 가서요?
춘일, (우숨 가운대 희망의 깃븜을 띄우고) 가지요.
영애, 가서요?
춘일, 왜 그리서요?
영애, (눈물 흘닌다)
춘일, (우는 영애를 보고 위로하는 듯이) 나는 가면 가티 가지요. 영애씨와 함께 가겟슴니다. 영애씨 저와 함께 가 주서요. 네?
영애, (의심하는 듯이) 저하고요?
춘일, (떨니는 목소래로) 네 영애씨하고 가지요-내가 지금 산 설고 물 서른 나의 나라를 떠나는 것도 오즉 영애씨를 위하야 아니 나를 위하야 그보다도 더한 우리의 사랑을 영원이 게속하기 위하야는 북간도로 아니 갈 수가 업슴니다.
영애, (눈물 흘닌대로) 춘일씨 감사함니다. 저까지 다리고 가신다니 그러나 나를 위하야는 가시지 마서요. 당신에게는 늙으신 어머님이 게시닛가요? 물론 사랑을 희생식히는 것도 만흔 고통이지요만 사랑을 위하야 얼마 아니한 뒤에 명게에 참석할 어머님 한 분을 놋코 간다 함은 우리의 할 일이 아니겟지요-
춘일, (견딀 수 업는 듯이) 차, 차, 참어주서요. 그 말을 그만 두십쇼. 어머님의 말슴마는 그만두서요- 자 어서 이러나서요-
영애, (가만이 니러나면서 춘일에게 안기면서) 춘일씨 그러면 저를 위하야 모든 것을 다 희생하시겟다는 말슴이야요?
춘일, (눈물 흘니면서 포옹) 네 영애씨 하지요. 귀한 사랑을 위하야...＜42＞
영애, (안기인 채로) 감사함니다. 나는 죽어도-
춘일, (우리는 어느때까지- 아- 아- 이와 가티 지내겟지요?
영애, 춘일씨의 사랑이 변치만 아니하면...
춘일, 변치야 안치요만 또 무슨 마가 생기면-
영애, 그런 말은 말아주서요-
춘일, (영애를 니르키면서) 자- 영애씨 니러나서요. 흥식군이 기다릴 터이닛가 빨니 가서 보와야 하겟슴니다. 자 가시지요(방예 드러가 튜렁크를 들고 나온다)
(춘일과 영애 퇴댱하랴 할때 상수로붓터 춘일모 등댱)
춘일모, (가랴는 량인을 보고) 어대들 가니?
(춘일 영애 깜짝 놀나 돌아다 보면서 주저주저한다)
춘일모, (튜령크를 빼아스면서) 너이들 어대를 이 밤중에 가니 응(분한 듯이 떤다)
춘일, (아모 말이 업다)
춘일모, (춘일을 보고) 이놈아 이 몹슬 놈아 나를 두고 네가 어대를 가니 응 나를 두고 거름이 잘 걸닐줄 아니 으 이놈아-(소리를 지른다) 이 년 너는 누구냐 응(영애를 보고)
영애, (울기만 하고 대답이 업다)
춘일모, (소리를 지르면서) 가라 이 년 울기는 무얼 울어. 남의 집 시약시 잇는 서방이 맛이냐 이년아
춘일, (놀나는 듯이) 어머니- 그 말은-
춘일모, (노한 어됴로) 무엇이 그 말이야 내가 그짓말을 하니 처가의 덕으로 공부하고 지내는 녀석이 무얼.
영애, (이 말을 듯고 정신이 아득하야 아! 하고 쓰러진다)
춘일, (잡아 니르키면서) 아! 영애씨 용서하여 주서요. 나는 벌서 무의미하게라도 신성한 결혼의 례뎐 압헤 섯든 자이여요- 용서하여 주십쇼. 네? 영애씨.＜43＞
춘일모, (분한 듯이) 이런 비러먹을 녀석(춘일을 본다)
영애, (얼골에 이상한 우슴을 띄이고 니러나면서) 하하하 나를? 나를? 에라 이놈아 내가 귀신이야 하하하- 우서서 죽겟네(뛰여나가랴 한다)
