Passing Through P'aju
"Mountains bright, waters beautiful" – these four characters are an apt critique of P'aju. With P'ap'yŏngsan Mountain as the center, the winding peaks extending southwest, and the scenery winding east and west with the Imjin River as its collar and belt, it is no wonder it has cultivated the ideals of many writers, and it is not strange that it transmits many unusual histories and scenic relics. Who could not faintly imagine the dignified appearance and imposing manner of our ancestors at the hallowed ground of the Imjin Ferry Crossing, or not recall with deep emotion the earthen mounds of Myŏlwaech'ŏn stream? Mourning the final days of Kungye at the site of the former temporary palace at Up'o, the autumn sun just happened to spread the red glow of sunset; imagining the grand ambitions of King Injo amidst the sparse woods and lingering paths of Hongsu Spring, a fresh feeling of the vicissitudes of time arises. What turned the wealth and honor of that time into a dream's trace is the Sangsŏdae platform of Yun Kwan (尹侍中瓘); the place where the fragrant soul of antiquity is deeply hidden is the T'aedam pool of the Jurchen beauty. The Yongyŏn abyss, where Yun T'aejae Sin-dal is said to have floated ashore in a wooden chest, and the dolmen where the Yi Yŏ-song brothers are said to have suppressed heroic energy – the local people have already lost the oral memory of these, calling them absurd legends. The twin Maitreya statues at Punsuwŏn, said to have been erected by the Koryŏ Dynasty to suppress the royal energy of Mt. Samgak, and the Natural Maitreya at Yangya-ri, where an old nun is said to have achieved Buddhahood after piling up a hundred stones, are superstitions of ours, past and present, making even their beautiful craftsmanship and elegance seem hateful. It seems best to leave aside legends like the Kŭm Tomb on Munbal Mountain and the Samigyŏ Bridge at Tongp'ae-ri. Pan'gujŏng Pavilion, belonging to Master Hwang Hŭi Pangch'on – who, forgetting household affairs, devoted his energy solely to bringing peace and livelihood to the populace impoverished by the chaotic rule at the end of Koryŏ, and then, letting his silver-thread-like white hair fly in the river wind, strolled about befriending indifferent seagulls – evokes emotions transcending mere appreciation, making tears of longing well up. If those types who strive to build houses called such-and-such Pavilion or such-and-such Hall, acting like retired scholars while treating the populace's starvation or ruin like a calf watching its sibling, were made to climb this pavilion, sweat would surely soak their backs. Among the seven pavilions along the Imjin River banks, the central one in status, whose fame has spread far and wide, is Hwasŏkchŏng Pavilion. Master Yi I Yulgok studied here in his early years and strolled here leisurely in his later years. The genius of the master, who shed bright light on our cultural history through politics and philosophy, was displayed from childhood. I will write down a poem he composed at the age of eight.
At the pavilion, autumn is already late, / The poet's feelings are boundless. / Distant waters merge with the blue sky, / Frosted maples [*] line the path in red. / The mountain spits out the solitary wheel of the moon, / The river holds wind from ten thousand li. / Wild geese beyond the border, where do they go? / Their cries cease amidst the evening clouds.
The evening glow dyes a red core, the cicadas' cries seem to call the clear wind. At the foot of the mountain and in the fields before it, woodcutter boys sing, and at the bow of a small boat, a fisherman casts his line. Forgetting the fatigue of the journey, I cast my gaze absently upon the evening smoke of the riverside village.

Munsan's Dedicated Individual, Mr. Hwang Paek-hyŏn
West of Munsan city, a low thatched house with a narrow yard is Mr. Hwang's pottery workshop. What first caught my eye were the dozens of types of pottery displayed at the shop front. Attracted by the novelty of their shapes and the beauty of their colors and luster, my feet naturally led me into the workshop, and I came to speak with Mr. Hwang. When he was 18, as a second-year student at Posŏng Middle School, he went to the Kyŏngsŏng Industrial Training Institute. After finishing two years of study there, he worked as an assistant at the same school for a year, then went as a government-sponsored student to Japan's Aichi Prefectural Ceramics School for three years. Returning to Kyŏngsŏng, he researched Koryŏ celadon for two years at a Japanese firm called Haeshi Trading Company, then crossed the Genkai Sea again to work diligently as a technician (技手) at the Osaka Seiseikan Crucible Manufacturing Plant for seven years before returning to Korea in March of this year and establishing the current workshop. Due to raw material constraints, the manufactured products are still only earthenware: various types of tableware, bottles, water droppers, drawer knobs/handles, nameplates, and other implements, as well as crucible molds and other items demanded by other pottery makers and glass manufacturers. Everyone who sees the actual items marvels at the exquisiteness of his skill, and his diligence, working day and night without rest, covered head-to-toe in clay, is truly that of a rare, dedicated individual. "I will devote my entire life to this work," his words sound very powerful. His strategy of avoiding large-scale plans for now, in order to accumulate much experience through experimentation, also seems reasonable. <103>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Yŏm Sang-jin describes a journey through P'aju county, highlighting its natural beauty and numerous historical sites associated with figures from the Koryŏ and Chosŏn dynasties, such as Yun Kwan, Hwang Hŭi, and Yi I (Yulgok). The author reflects on legends, dismisses some as superstition, and expresses admiration for historical figures dedicated to the people, contrasting them with contemporary elites. The latter half profiles Hwang Paek-hyŏn, a Munsan potter trained in both Korea and Japan, who diligently works to establish a modern ceramic workshop, drawing inspiration from traditional forms like Koryŏ celadon. From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its blend of celebrating traditional Korean landscapes and history (including anti-Japanese place names like Myŏlwaech'ŏn) while simultaneously showcasing a modern Korean entrepreneur whose career path is deeply intertwined with Japanese colonial institutions and industry. It reflects a complex reality of cultural pride coexisting with the practicalities of operating within the colonial system.

坡州를 지내면서
山明水麗 이 4자는 坡州에 대한 適評이다. 坡平山을 중심으로 서남에 蜿延한 諸峰과 臨津江을 襟帶로 동서에 紆縈한 풍경이 만흔 문인의 이상가를 陶冶함에 유감이 업슬지며 오랜 奇史勝蹟을 傳함이 괴이치 아니하도다. 우리 先民의 당당한 위풍과 늠름한 의용을 臨津渡의 성지에서 彷佛히 상상하며 滅倭川의 土墩에서 감개이 회고치 안을 자 누구인가. 牛浦의 行宮遺墟에서 弓裔의 末路를 憑吊하니 때마침 秋日이 석양의 붉은 놀을 널고 洪水泉의 疎林殘逕 에서 仁祖의 雄圖를 상상하니 새삼스런 滄桑의 늣김이 일어난다. 당년 부귀가 꿈자최로 化한 것은 尹侍中瓘의 尙書臺요 子古芳魂이 깁히 감초인 곳은 女眞 미인의 態潭이로다. 尹太宰莘達이 목궤에 浮來하엿다는 龍淵과 李如松형제가 영웅기를 억압하엿다는 支石은 황당한 전설이라고 그 鄕人이 벌서 口碑를 일웟스며 麗朝가 三角의 王氣를 압도하려고 세웟다는 분수원 雙彌勒과 老尼가 百石堆를 쌋코 성불하엿다는 陽野里天然彌勒은 그 공예와 운치의 아름다운 것까지 밉살스럽게 보이리만콤 昔今을 통한 우리의 미신이다. 文發山 金墓와 東牌里 沙彌橋 등 전설 따위는 그만두는 것이 조흘 듯 려말의 난정의 피폐하엿든 민중을 安堵樂業케 하노라고 가사를 잇고 정력을 오로지 밧친 후에 은사갓흔 백발을 江風에 날니면서 무심한 갈매기로 벗을 삼아 소요하든 黃喜尨村 선생의 반구정은 玩賞을 초월한 감상이 동경의 눈물을 뿌리게 한다. 민중이야 족든지 멸망하든지 송아지 제 형보듯 하고 은사인체 무슨 亭 무슨 軒하는 집짓기에 애쓰는 무리로 斯亭에 올으게 하엿스면 반듯이 등을 적시는 땀이 잇스렷다. 臨津 연안의 7개 亭子에서 位로는 중심이 되며 名이 원근에 파함은 이 花犟�亭이다. 李珥 栗谷선생이 早年에 修硏하엿스며 晩景에 倘佯하엿도다. 정치와 철학으로 우리 문화사에 光明彩를 낫타내신 선생의 천재는 어릴 때부더 발휘하엿든 것이다. 그의 8세때 지은 한 편 시를 써 둔다.
休亭秋己晩, 騷客意無窮, 遠水連天碧, 霜楓*行紅, 山吐孤輪月, 江含萬里風, 塞鴻何處去, 聲斷暮雲中.

汶山 篤志家 黃伯顯君
汶山市 西에 나즉한 草家 좁은 마당은 곳 黃君의 製陶工場이다. 처음 눈에 띄운 것은 그 店頭에 진열한 수십종의 도기이엇다. 그 모형의 기묘함과 色澤이 美麗함에 자연 발길이 공장까지 들어가고 또 黃君과 接語하게 된 것이다. 군은 18세 되던 때 普成中學 2년생으로서 京城工業傳習所로 갓다. 2년간 業을 마치고 該校에서 1년 동안 조수 노릇을 하다가 官費遊學生으로 日本 愛知縣 立窯業學校에서 3년의 業을 맛치고 京城으로 돌아 와서 2개년간 海市商會라는 日本人상회에서 고려자기를 연구하고 다시 玄海를 건너 大阪正盛舘坩堝제조소에 技手로 7개년간 근면하다가 본년 3월에 귀국하야 곳 현공장을 설립한 것이다. 제조품은 원료관계로 아즉 陶器뿐인 바 식기 각종과 병 연적 설합. 명패 등 諸 具와 坩堝型등 다른 陶器業者 及 유리제조업자의 수요품이다. 그 技藝의 정교함은 실물을 보는 자 다가티 경탄하려니와 전신이 흙투성이가 되여서 晝夜不撤하고 근면함은 정말 稀有의 독지가이다. 「나는 一生을 斯業에 밧치겟슴니다」하는 그의 말은 매우 유력하게 들닌다. 아즉은 만흔 실험을 쌋키 위하야 대규모의 설계를 피하려는 그의 책략도 그럴 듯 하다. ＜103＞
