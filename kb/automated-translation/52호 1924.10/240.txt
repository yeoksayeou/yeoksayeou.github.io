Evil Prayer
Baudelaire (Author), Yang Chu-dong (Translator)

O Angel, most wise and most beautiful, whom God
Lured with Fate and concealed with praise,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

O Prince of Exile, misunderstood by men,
Who conquers all and ever stands mightily tall,<2>
O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

O Great Emperor, omniscient and ruling all things underground,
Who personally mends the anguish of humans,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who teaches, with love, the joys of Paradise
To the leper and the cursed pariahs,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who, through your old and steadfast lover, Death,
Begets Hope—that foolish fascination!

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who watches the condemned with clear, high eyes,
Casting them from the gallows into Hell,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who knows in which nook of the earth lie
The noble jewels hidden by a jealous God,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who perceives with an insightful eye
The deep repository where buried treasures slumber,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who, for the sleepwalker wandering on the roof,
Shields the cliff with a great hand,<3>

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who miraculously subdues the withered bones
Of the sluggish drunkard trampled by horses' hooves,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who, to console the suffering weak,
Taught us to mix sulfur and saltpeter,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who stamps the mark of a subtle accomplice, O,
Upon the brow of the heartless and unjust plutocrat,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who places the creed of sorrow and the love of rags
Into the eyes and hearts of young girls,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who is the staff (執行) of the wanderer, the lamp of the explorer,
The confessor of the hanged and the conspirator,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!

Who is the adoptive father of those filled with black wrath,
Who drove the Father from the earthly Paradise,

O Satan, take pity on my long misery!<4>
<2-4>

**Summary**

This article presents a Korean translation by Yang Chu-dong of Charles Baudelaire's poem "Les Litanies de Satan" ("Evil Prayer"). The poem is a series of invocations praising Satan, portraying him not as purely evil, but as a compassionate figure who understands and aids the suffering, the exiled, the cursed, and the rebellious. He is depicted as a source of hidden knowledge, hope born from death, and even a protector of the vulnerable, contrasting sharply with a jealous and punitive God. For historians of colonial Korea, the publication of this translation in *Kaebyŏk*, a prominent nationalist magazine known for its critical stance, is noteworthy. Publishing a poem that elevates Satan and critiques divine authority could be interpreted as a coded expression of dissent against the oppressive colonial regime, aligning the suffering Korean populace with the downtrodden figures championed by Baudelaire's Satan, or simply reflecting a broader intellectual engagement with Western literature that carried potentially subversive undertones.
