발행일	1924년 10월 01일
기사제목	흰 달빗 (White Moonlight)
필자	公州 尹貴榮
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

흰 달빗
						公州 尹貴榮
Today, this morning, I unexpectedly received a letter from B, from whom I hadn't heard for four or five months, and whose circumstances I hadn't known during that time. His sudden disappearance from C city was last autumn, two or three days after he resigned from the bank. Although it's lessened somewhat now, since last summer, all sorts of stories were rife in C city, and around the time of his bank resignation, the various rumors circulating around him and a certain woman (a married woman) were truly numerous. And all of them were quite serious matters for B.

His storming out of his family home was in the early spring of last year. Having stormed out with nowhere particular to go, he had no choice but to come to this C city. He wandered about in C city, and one day when he met me, he said something like this:
"Hey, X! Is there some kind of night school run by the X Youth Association?"
"Yes! There is. It's a women's night school. Why do you ask?"
"Ah! Well! Today at the chapel, Evangelist K mentioned a night school run by his association and asked if I could help out, so I agreed to help for the time being—"
"It might be interesting if you're bored, but it could be quite tiresome!"
"Well..."
From then on, it seemed he started attending the night school.<5>
Originally, he was someone who greatly disliked the position of a teacher. That's why, right after returning to the country, despite numerous offers from various commercial schools in Kyŏngsŏng and requests to be a part-time lecturer, he didn't accept a single one. Because of this, disagreements between him and his father grew ceaselessly day by day. For me, the fact that he, who found such things bothersome, especially a women's night school where ladies gathered, was willingly attending it, indicated a sudden change in his psychology that I couldn't fathom at all.
About five days after he started attending the night school, I met him on the street and asked him this:
"So—are you planning to stay in C city permanently for the fun of attending the women's night school?"
"Yes—there's something else keeping me in C city too. Starting tomorrow, I'll be working at the bank again," he said with a faint smile. I was startled—. He wasn't the type to work in a bank. So.
"What? At the bank? A bank, you say..."
"What's there to be so surprised about? I decided to start working at X Bank yesterday. Isn't it just a matter of making up one's mind about something?"
"Right. So you decided to become a bank clerk!"
"No—from now on, I've decided to just go wherever things lead, wherever my feet take me."
I couldn't understand his words. I just decided to watch how things unfolded.
It was after this. A couple of months after he started at the bank and attended the women's night school, one summer day, I heard from a friend about the stories circulating about B. It went something like this:
—This fellow B is originally the only son of the foremost wealthy man in XX, a type of modern profligate. He supposedly betrayed his father over financial matters and ran off to C city. However, thanks to his parents, he graduated from the Political Economy department of X University in Tokyo. Even after coming to C city, he associated with respectable people, but eventually harbored insidious intentions and volunteered himself as a teacher at the X Women's Night School. There, using various means, he preyed upon the women attending the night school, eventually forming a sordid relationship with the wife of a very close friend who attended the school. As for that woman, it's said her close <6> friend trusted B and let her attend the night school—.
Hearing this from that friend, I vehemently denied it. I explained, citing various reasons, that he was not such a vile person. However, from then on, every friend I met told me stories about him. Each time, I passionately defended him.
As the days went by, the rumors spread to such a height that there was hardly anyone in C city who didn't know the name B. But I alone dismissed them as groundless rumors, though my efforts alone were not enough.
It was autumn, quite definitively, when the tree leaves were turning reddish. One day, I heard from a friend who worked at the same bank as B that B had resigned from the bank several days prior. Finding this too unexpected and harboring a sliver of suspicion, I went to B's inn, only to find no trace of B. All I heard was the innkeeper's disappointing words: "He packed his bags and left somewhere yesterday morning."
I couldn't help but suspect him. It seemed I, who had defended such a heartless person until now, was extremely foolish. So, from then on, whenever I met people, we mutually criticized and ridiculed him.

However, today, with the old year gone and not even two months left before the new year arrives, I unexpectedly received a letter from B. Reading the letter, I cried for a good while. He is the only son of a wealthy man. But he is the one who kicked that aside and ran away. Yet, various misfortunes surrounded his frail life. That's why he tried to turn his back on this world, never to set foot in this monstrous world again—he is now in a faraway land. But still, misfortune pursues him. So he tries immensely to go to an even farther land, yet he is now in that faraway land, besieged by illness.
How can I not seek this out? How can I not offer him comfort? Therefore, in a few days, I have decided to leave for that northern land of Japan where he currently is. And I intend to publish in its entirety to this world the long letter he gave me this time, the crystallization <7> of his "bitterness" and "tears."

—B's Letter—
My friend—
I heap upon you, my only friend in my homeland, the countless lumps of misfortune that arose while I was buried in my past world. Please—read this considering my aching heart.
My first time being in C city, wasn't that last spring? Unable to even finish school—and that school with barely a year left, not many years remaining—forced out by the single vexation of my parents, it was then that I first tasted bitterness. I pleaded earnestly with my parents, tried countless ways, striving immensely to make those stubborn people reflect, but such methods had not a single point of effect on those obstinate ones and returned to bubbles (水泡), so what could I do!
Even if I couldn't make them reflect, my studies weren't impossible without their funding, but ah—that's how it was—I couldn't be bothered. I was sick of it. "What's the point of studying in a world like this..."
From that time, I disliked everything. I just longed for an endless land, wanted to go. I longed for a land of ice; I longed for a place beneath palm trees (椰子樹).
However, at that time, I still harbored a faint hope, thinking, "Perhaps some path will appear." So, without much of a resolute decision—though it was treated as a major issue within the family with much fuss—I left home effortlessly.
The world reveres me as the eldest son of a millionaire. I intensely disliked hearing that. While many people might not know "BXX," there was hardly anyone who didn't know the son of the rich Mr. So-and-so... I deeply lamented this fact. So, without thinking about this or that, I just ran away. If leaving home meant the title "son of a millionaire" would be erased from me, my joy would know no bounds, I thought.—At that time, I immediately came to C city. <8> Even after coming to C city, I couldn't avoid hearing the name "son of a millionaire." I thought if I went to C city, they wouldn't know who I was. I thought I could act solely as the simple "BXX." But upon arriving, "son of a rich man" was still my name, and even if called "B," the qualifier "son of a rich man" was attached.
Yet, in the world, even while hearing scornful remarks like "rich man's kid," "rich bastard," there were so many people who desired wealth. They say that if one just becomes rich, even through any tenacious means, that's enough.
A few days after I arrived in C city, on the third day of teaching at the X Women's Night School, I was introduced at the meeting hall to S, the wife of my friend Yŏngsŏn-gun, by Yŏngsŏn-gun himself. Although she was originally a housewife, upon hearing that I was a teacher there, Yŏngsŏn-gun voluntarily enrolled her and entrusted all matters concerning her to me. I gladly accepted her. And I decided to take full responsibility for her. The strange rumors circulating in the world started from that time.
The students were all simple, honest women. I taught them with all my passion and sincerity. I made it my sole purpose to awaken their ignorance and impart knowledge. And at that time, guiding them felt like my destiny ordained by heaven (한울).
However, a few days after that, I realized an urgent problem lay before me. That was the economic problem—there was no source for living expenses. When I left home, I only had a few pennies in my pocket; I disliked even taking money from home, so I just ran out like that. But now, trying independent living for the first time, I learned for the first time that the very first thing is money. Yet, there was no particular means available.
So, inevitably, swallowing my pride, I had no choice but to seek out the X Bank, which I had been set to enter through my father's introduction previously. At that time, the bank president and several others seemed both a bit surprised and very pleased to hear that I wanted to stay at the bank.
I easily became an employee of X Bank. And with the title of Head of the Research Division (調査課長), I became a slave to time. And my monthly income seemed to be around one hundred twenty or thirty won.
"To tie this neck to the bank for a hundred won a month?"<9>
When I thought like this, this body felt endlessly base and endlessly pitiful.
"But this is also an unavoidable task for teaching those who don't know!" When I thought this, without any anxiety, thinking it was a matter of course, I resolved to sacrifice this body to whatever extent necessary and guide them further onto the path of light.
However, at that time, the world, perhaps out of envy towards me, was ripening groundless rumors. But I, quite the opposite, after consulting with Yŏngsŏn-gun, decided to make S my sworn sister (義妹). As she was two years younger than me, as the days went by, there was no distance between us, becoming closer than real siblings. She came often. And she was the best among the night school attendees. So I cherished her greatly. I wanted to teach her even the smallest things, more and more, and her desire to learn grew firmer.
One evening, after finishing my teaching and stepping out of the hall door, S called out softly from under the hall lamp over there, "Oppa, Oppa." Thinking it strange, I hesitated for a moment, then approached her and asked,
"Yes? What is it?" She, with a flushed face and an embarrassed smile, hesitated for a while, then said,
"A few days ago, didn't you receive a book from oppa?" My consciousness became alert. And the events of a few days ago surfaced in my mind. A few days prior, when I had come out of the bank and was lying down holding some book, the door opened, and the face of the plump innkeeper appeared, tossing something at me saying, "Here!" I had received it. It was wrapped in white paper (조회), and "Mr. BXX" was written on it in neat handwriting (글시). "Nothing should be coming to me from anywhere," I thought, suspiciously unfolding the several layers of paper. Inside, wrapped in a white silk handkerchief, was a "...Poetry Collection" (詩集).
"Huh... a poetry collection? I don't have any friends who would send something like a poetry collection... Ah, let's look inside the book." No matter how thoroughly (삿삿치) I searched inside the book, there wasn't even a name card or anything similar that I expected. Day after day, I struggled immensely to solve its mystery.
After several days like that, today, unexpectedly, the mention of that book came from this sworn sister standing before me.—I looked at my sworn sister suspiciously and said,
"Yes! I have it!" showing my desire to know quickly. And "Did this woman send it..." As this thought flashed through my <10> mind, it felt as if Yŏngsŏn-gun's glaring eyes were visible from the dark place over there. She said,
"Do you know who sent it? You must have been very curious (굼굼하섯지요)," she looked up at me with a teasing smile, and before I could speak, she pulled out something like a letter from her book wrapper (책보 사품). Then she gave it to me, saying,
"Take this and read it. Then you'll understand the details." Then, continuing her words, she added, "Tomorrow is Sunday, right? I'll see you at the chapel (뵈옵겟셔요)," and taking the child servant with her, she went off down that road.
I stood there for a long while. Holding the letter in my hand, I stood there unconsciously for a long time. But when I realized I was holding the letter, my consciousness returned, and I desperately wanted to see the letter's contents. So, holding the letter, I hurried back to the inn. As soon as I took off my shoes and entered, I opened (뜨덧다) the letter first. And illuminated under the electric light, the entire content of the letter was this:
I know you must have spent several very curious days. But the truth is, it couldn't be helped...? I just wish to know if you have kept the book I sent somewhere until now? Also, though it's embarrassing to say, I have been offering (밧치고) my hidden love to you, teacher, for a very long time. Please know that the book and the handkerchief are symbols of that. And I am very grateful that you have made S your sworn sister. She and I are like sworn siblings. It seems to be some kind of fate, one way or another.
I will conclude (주리겟습니나) here this time. Knowing your reply (하회), I wish to meet you sometime.
Month X, Day X, 192X      From L
Seeing this simple letter, my head felt fuzzy as if dreaming, then cleared again, and I thought,
"Ah! Miss L?......"
Ah! My friend.
This is the letter from that Miss L whom you also know. It was the letter from that Miss L who is on the lips of many young men in C city. It is true that she is the Miss L who has even been to America.
Ah! My friend—<11>
Should I believe this? Or should I not believe it? I had no capacity to judge. I was just immensely happy and pleased that such a fragrant, flower-like thing could happen to someone like me, even just hearing about it felt good.
It was at that time. The world was abuzz with utterly nonsensical stories, things that seemed unreal, about some relationship between me and my sworn sister S, about Yŏngsŏn-gun monitoring S, about the night school breaking up—such talk was quite loud in C city society. But at that time, I was in such a dreamlike state, unknown to the world, that I had no room for any other thoughts. However, after S, bearing such unpleasant rumors (루명), was unable to leave her house, naturally, communication between L and me, even exchanging letters frequently, became quite inconvenient.
And for S, who strove to guide me towards the path of happiness for my sake, for my life, and sympathized with me, to hear such unfortunate accusations because of me and even be doubted by her own husband—this was something I couldn't just sit by and listen to. But there was nothing I could do. I wanted to confront Yŏngsŏn-gun directly and offer an explanation (변명) for her, but I lacked the courage. Or rather than lacking courage, I disliked making such tedious explanations to him. In effect, I myself had pushed that sworn sister into a pit of misfortune.
Ah! But at that time, the world slandered me with utterly dreamlike words. Yet, I never offered even a shred of explanation about this to anyone. I thought that even if I didn't explain now, the future society would prove the facts and vindicate me. And at that time, I, like a young master in that dreamlike state, thought, "My life holds happiness. I am carrying (길머지고) flowers." Therefore, even when the world chattered such harsh words (험구), I didn't feel (늣기지) much anxiety.
Ah! But my friend—
Time was brief. After a short while passed, only then did I realize how foolish, still young, and inexperienced in the ways of the world I was. And I cried and lamented. I cursed the world. But what use was any of that?
L and I would invariably meet at the chapel on Sundays. Other than that, there weren't many opportunities (긔회). Even on Sundays, although we exchanged greetings happily several times, we would just smile at each other without any significant conversation and part ways (난호엿슬 뿐이다). Of course (몰논), if I had wanted to create a quiet opportunity, I could have, but my character (위인) was such that I didn't know how to engage in such playfulness, so I just endured the burning impatience <12> alone, passing the time anxiously, only exchanging letters. However, after more than a month passed like that, despite sending several letters consecutively, there was no news whatsoever, and I couldn't even see her at the chapel on Sundays.
I burned with immense impatience. My heart was indescribable. And in my empty head, thoughts like lightning—disappointment in love (실련) (though it hardly qualified as romance)—shook me like crashing waves. Ah! The heart of this young master, suffering such bitterness like a jilted bride, was a sea of raging billows (로도).
— A few weeks passed like this, and then I heard a rumor (풍편) that she was going to America again. So I asked knowledgeable friends about her, but I couldn't get any clear answers—just talk of her having gone to Kyŏngsŏng a few days ago, or her travel permit being issued.
A few days after that, something that made me unable to cry even if I wanted to, unable to speak even if I tried—appeared before me. It was receiving an invitation (청첩) stating that Miss L was holding her wedding ceremony with a certain aristocrat and wealthy man named M at the XX Hotel in Kyŏngsŏng.—
Ah! My friend.
About this, I will say no more. This was the bitter event I intended to hide forever, unknown to anyone. But at that time, society spread such groundless rumors as if there were some indelible relationship with S, all across C city.
I haven't met S since then. I stopped attending the chapel too. But what could I do when society fabricates such rumors? Aren't rumors (浪說) created by society? What power do I have!
My existence, which could no longer live in this world, had become completely transformed due to coming to C city. From now on, I have nothing like hope. And I have become someone whose name "B" is eternally stained, unable to remain attached any longer. I decided to die and forget (니즈리라) the world. There seemed no other way. But doing so felt foolish (어리식엇다). The thought of fighting this society once arose. How strong (굿세인가) is it? I wanted to see how tenacious it was.
But first, I had to disappear from C city.<13>
So, the next morning at dawn, packing a simple bag (행구), as if running away, I secretly boarded a train (긔차) without anyone knowing. The train, of course, went to Kyŏngsŏng. But even going to Kyŏngsŏng, I had nowhere particular to go. Consequently, there was nothing to do; it was, so to speak, a journey without destination. But I felt relieved. Leaving C city seemed to lighten my bitterness more than anything. However, amidst the relief, I felt great sorrow leaving S behind.
She is forever sacrificed because of me. Her innocent, blameless name is withered and buried because of this unjust fellow named B. Ah, should I abandon this C city where S resides, should I betray (저바리야) her? Was it too heartless to leave C city without even offering a word of explanation for her, who, though blameless, suffered society's vexation because I came to C city, rendering her unable to rise?
And I cannot forget the indignation towards Yŏngsŏn-gun's attitude. He is an old friend from my hometown (고우). When he was struggling as a poor student in Tokyo, I sympathized with him greatly. So I let him stay with me at my inn for over three years. During that time, we got along understanding (리해) each other amicably, without any conflict. Only our fields of study differed—he in literature, I in politics and economics—and thus our tastes and goals were different. However, regarding the common point (귀일점) that we were both pioneering the future path for our people, there was no gap between us, and we discussed the future together.
But in recent days, he has forgotten me. He is very different from the Yŏngsŏn of the past. His nerves were too sharp. Excessively, reaching the point of misplaced acuity. He suspected me, this innocent person who remained the same as before, based on nonsensical matters, things he didn't even know the facts of. And he is the one who ostracized (배쳑한) his old friend overnight. And consequently, believing the rumors of the world as truth, he is now attempting to divorce (리혼) his lawful wife.
Ah! Not long ago, Yŏngsŏn was not like that; he was not so frivolous. But today, after time has passed, Yŏngsŏn has changed like that. Worn down (씩기고) and battered (부딋긴) by this society, he could change into someone like the current Yŏngsŏn. I do not blame Yŏngsŏn. I do not intend to condemn Yŏngsŏn as a man without loyalty, a man who doesn't know friendship. I merely think it's an unavoidable fate for anyone who has rolled around much on this earth. And I feel a touch of long sorrow (비애) for Yŏngsŏn's future path.<14>
— While picturing these dreamlike past events in my mind, the train had already reached Y Station. Disturbed by the noise of many people outside, I casually looked out the window, and
Ah! There, I saw Miss L, dressed smartly (사틋한) in Western clothes, boarding the second-class carriage over there with the man named M, her fiancé. Seeing her, my head suddenly felt heavy, as if someone pressed down on it with something; of course, it didn't even reach the point of jealousy that I should have felt.
『Ah! Aristocracy, honor, and, money!』
Those who seek anything else in this world are phantoms (헛개비). Madmen (밋친 놈). What is all this truth (眞理) or ideal (理想)? There is only 『aristocracy and honor and money』 (오즉).
For that, everyone works, bustles, cries, laughs, and moves about. Only people with such a common point (귀일점) are those who can survive in this world. If there is anyone who dislikes such things and seeks something different, one cannot help but sympathize with them. Such people will eventually go mad and lose their minds; they are ones who will sit down and weep. Far from finding new light in their future path, they will only dig up more misfortune— Twenty minutes might have passed since leaving 『Y Station』. The train slowly arrived right at Kyŏngsŏng. Following the crowd of people getting off one after another, I crossed the platform, got down, and reached the exit.
But I suddenly disliked going outside. Outside, the so-called "sound of civilization" was tremendous enough to pierce the ears. Ah! The sound of civilization. The sounds of electric trams, horse carts, automobiles rolling, machines whining, vendors hawking, various people's voices—all merge together, clouding the air of Seoul, where the sun (太陽) hasn't even risen yet. People say the more these unpleasant sounds exist, the richer the nation (富國), and a country with few and weak sounds like these is called a poor country. Thus, even the many unpleasant sounds of our Seoul are said to have a big difference compared to the cities (都市) of other large countries. Because of this big difference, Korea is called a poor country. Ah! What is wealth and power, and what is poverty and weakness? What does it matter if one is rich and strong, and what connection is there if one is poor and weak?
Ah! This is the principle of current society. For this goal, various groups fight amongst themselves. Those people squirming and bustling over there right now—if they were offered honor and lots of money, they would throw away everything they are doing now and come running.<15> So, even those who were digging the earth today, sweating in dirty clothes—if they are told they will be given money and honor tomorrow, they immediately become influential gentlemen (有志紳士) from that day on. Whether they have substance or not, society immediately calls them gentlemen and reveres them.
Ah—wherever I go, it's the same situation. Where in this world could there be a different, special place? Where is there a place where this situation and that situation are not seen?
I left the station. I was unconsciously heading to the tram stop to board when suddenly a car whizzed past right in front of me. As I frowned and watched the speeding car, inside were L and M, laughing and chattering (짓거리며), wandering the streets of the capital. Seeing them, I unconsciously cried out (부르지젓다),
『Ah—filthy Seoul!』 So I disliked being disgustingly mixed (석겨) together with them in this one Seoul.
I let the tram I intended to take go by and turned back towards the station (뎡거장). And again, I resolved to quickly leave this wretched Seoul. The time was eight in the morning. Just then, a station worker shouted loudly, walking around, that there was a train departing for Pusan (釜山).
『There happens to be a departing train... Pusan?... Take a boat from Pusan and drift further down...』
I thought for a long while. Then, after some time,
『Alright, let's go to Keryongsan (鷄龍山)!』 New courage surged in me. Hurriedly, I bought a ticket and jumped back onto the train—
Ah! Keryongsan! Keryongsan is a deep, sacred mountain (령산). There must be places with abundant forests (수림) and many valleys (골작이). There must be large rocks and steep cliffs. I decided to spend my life within it. There are many temples (寺) in Keryongsan. Right—let's become a monk! And through a lifetime of faithful chanting (념불), I would heal even a ten-thousandth part of my great wound (상책이), I decided.
Embraced (포옹되여서) by nature (自然), reciting (읇으며) nature, singing of nature, and then falling asleep in nature (自然). Then, rising early in the morning, with clear water like silver (은수), white and pure, flowing gently down, I would wash my face and limbs (수족) cleanly, without a speck of dust (틔끌), and with quiet (코요한) steps (거름), wearing the monk's robe (장삼), proceed to the shrine hall.
And with a spotless new heart, new spirit, after meditating before the solemn Buddha statue, chanting the invocation with a sincere voice, while the sound of the wooden gong (木鐸) of faith—tok, tok—echoes in the quiet mountain dawn air—ah! Where else could such mystery (神秘) exist again?<16>
Alright—now I've found (어덧다) a place to go. In this world, the places to go are all such places that are sufficient. Therefore, gathering my mind floating in emptiness, ending my life with faith alone seemed the greatest happiness. – My heart was immensely joyful (깃벗다). My heart, awaiting the path of happiness, underwent countless palpitations (고동을 이르켯다). Oh, let me reach the destination quickly! "Ah—slow train, quickly take this impatient me there"— When the train passed Suwŏn (水原) station, I decided to pass the tedious journey even in dreams. So, getting a pillow (벼개), I closed my eyes full of joy anticipating the path ahead, and wandered in the land of sleep.
— I don't know how long I slept. I woke up to the clatter-clatter sound of the train wheels at some station. I wanted to get off (나리고) at this C station. Now that I was awake, the journey felt too tedious to bear. So I politely asked a friend in white clothes sitting opposite me,
『To go to Keryongsan, is it alright to get off at this C station?』 He replied,
『Yes—from this station, you can go to K county by car. From K county, Keryongsan is about forty ri away.』 I thought, "Alright, this works." So, I hastily jumped off the train, which was just blowing its whistle (긔적) to depart. Having jumped off the train, I rushed out of the station anticipating great joy. However, when I hurried to the K county-bound bus stop, I only heard the disappointing news that both buses had already left. My burning, fiery anticipation seemed to break down once upon hearing this news. But then, hearing the clerk's voice say, "You just need to wait for an hour or two," I thought, "Oh well, whatever," and decided to wait. Seeing me hesitating with my bag, he said, "Please go inside over there. There's another person who missed the car," and guided me towards a reception room-like area. Wondering "What kind of person," I followed him inside. Over there, a rather pretty-looking (엽-부상스런) young Japanese woman was reading a book, but seeing us enter, she stood up and bowed. I returned the bow and sat down on the chair opposite her, where the clerk indicated. The clerk left. I addressed the woman in Japanese,
『I hear you missed the car?』 I looked at her and asked.
The woman, with a roundish face and a gentle (온정한) smile, replied in a sweet and clear voice,
『Yes—the capacity was full. I decided to take the next car,』 and then asked, 『Is K county your hometown?』 I merely replied, 『No. My hometown is XX.』 Then she reopened the book she was reading and looked at it. I closed my eyes and pictured the road ahead.<17> Keryongsan, which I had never visited, came to mind, and monks in robes (장삼) standing, chanting (념불), and striking wooden gongs (목탁) appeared. Everything seemed to be pulling me quickly towards it. After closing my eyes and meditating alone for a while, I opened them with a start, and the woman sitting opposite me also had her eyes closed, seemingly lost in some worry. After she opened her eyes, I looked at her and asked,
『Are you staying in K county?』 But the woman simply replied,
『No.』 I pressed again,
『Then do you have some business in K county?』
『No, I am just wandering (표랑) aimlessly from place to place,』
she said weakly, letting out a long sigh. And after saying that, a look of profound loneliness could be detected somewhere on her face.
Unknowingly, the lonely anxiety I had felt before resurfaced. And thinking, "Perhaps this woman is trapped in circumstances similar to mine," I looked at her with eyes of sympathy, and in her flushed (상긔된) eyes gazing yonder, tears seemed about to well up.
Seeing that, a certain unknown sorrow (悲哀) seemed to intertwine (얽키는) with hers, as if contagious (감념). I didn't want to speak further. Especially since I had resolved not to associate with women anymore, it just felt strange (긔이) that something inexplicable seemed entangled between me and this unfamiliar (낫서투른), moreover Japanese, woman whom I was encountering for the first time.
However, the longer I sat facing her, the stranger my heart felt. At that time, trying to calm (안뎡시키려) my mind, I pulled out a book to read, but without really reading, I just kept flipping the pages back and forth. The woman still had her eyes closed, thinking about something.
I stared blankly at her slightly reddish cheek, one hand supporting her chin, and then asked,
『Where in Korea are you from?』 She opened her eyes with a start. I continued,
『Why? You said you have no fixed plans, yet you travel like this?』 I gently tried to probe into her inner circumstances. But the woman* **. <18>
『Yes—it just turned out that way,』 was all she said. I asked again,
『You seem unable to bear your anguish; why do you favor such wandering? Otherwise, well! There aren't any famous sites or historical landmarks (名勝古蹟) to speak of in K county...』 She lifted her bowed head and said,
『If I were in a position to seek out scenic spots and historical sites, I would be a fortunate person. Will there ever be a time when someone like me can visit such famous places and rejoice (깃버할)?』 Her voice trembled pitifully. Then, looking at me weakly, she slowly closed her eyes again.
Hearing her words, sudden sadness overcame me. The sorrow burst forth so strongly that I wanted to just grab her and cry my heart out. So, in a tearful voice,
『This world is such a world. This world is a place where all lumps of dissatisfaction are clumped together!...』 Unknowingly, I cried out like this. And continuing again,
『I don't know how much sorrow you carry, but I am someone who has suffered the greatest possible wound in this world. That's why I am one who intends to turn my back on this world. I am one who plans to spend my life in the deep sacred mountain of Keryongsan— You must understand that this world is like that.』 Having exclaimed this, tears streamed from my eyes (it was the first time in my life I had cried like that).
After a while, when I looked at her with tear-filled eyes, the woman was covering her eyes with a handkerchief, crying with her head bowed on her knees (무롭 우에 업다려). Seeing her, she looked infinitely pitiful. I resolved to rescue her from her misfortune, no matter what. At that moment, Keryongsan vanished, and my own sorrow was forgotten. There was only the thought of rescuing her, nothing else. So I stood up, went closer to her, placed my hand on her shoulder (억개에 손을 언고) (though it was impolite), and said,
『Excuse me... Excuse me. I am extremely sorry for speaking improperly and causing you more distress. It was just my inability to bear my own sorrow that made me speak like that. Please forgive me!』 I gently shook her. After a while, she sat up. And in her eyes, looking weakly at me, tears welled up again.
– We couldn't take the K county-bound car that day. The woman suddenly said she felt unwell, so inevitably, she and I <19> took rooms at an inn there. Then I had a doctor see her and gave her medicine. However, she suddenly fell gravely ill that night. She remained in that critical condition for over two weeks. I sympathized with her immensely. I nursed her illness devotedly. During that time, often delirious from the illness, she repeatedly murmured things like, "Mr. XX, I'm sorry." One day, about two weeks later, when she had passed the critical stage, with a faint smile on her thin face, she told me her entire story— After finishing her story, a look of determination suddenly appeared on her face, and she called out,
『Mr. XX!』 calling my name forcefully. I immediately replied, 『Yes—』 and looked down at her, wondering suspiciously what she was about to say. She smiled faintly again and said,
「Mr. XX, I will never be able to forget you!... How could I possibly receive your love, but if you were to think of me, I would spend my entire life alone, forever cherishing the thought of you in my mind! Ah—what will you do?...』 Her voice trembled away.
Ah—my friend?...
What was the right thing to do? She placed her hand on my knee (무롭 우에). But I couldn't affectionately push that hand away either. Ah—I thought for a long while, then squeezed her hand tightly. And I closed my eyes and said nothing. Only my heart felt completely full of something indefinable—
The woman was originally the daughter of a prominent wealthy family, one of the few in Aomori City (靑森市) in northern Japan. She had sequentially completed elementary school and higher girls' school, and the year before last, when she turned nineteen, she entered X Higher Normal School in Tokyo and continued her studies until last autumn. However, her father intended to marry her off as a daughter-in-law to a certain wealthy man known by the nickname 'miser' (守錢奴). While crushing one's freedom and disregarding one's child is a great fault, behind her father's decision to give her as a daughter-in-law to him, the great name of money was also involved.
However, her mother, learning of this, strongly opposed it, but the father showed not the slightest reflection. He summoned his daughter <20> to hasten the wedding ceremony. But she, through her mother's arrangement, took ample money and left home right then.
She said she first wandered (표랑) around various places in Japan before coming to Korea.
When she first left, her mother had promised to notify her as soon as her father showed signs of reflection, but although she sent letters for over a year, there was no news. Then, a few days ago, she received a telegram in Kyŏngsŏng, but it simply said (急) Come quickly. When she asked for further notification, another telegram arrived saying her father's illness was critical.
However, feeling reluctant to go back just like that, she intended to go as far as K county and wait for another letter there. –
– A few days after that, I, who had intended to go to Keryongsan, left for Japan with her, who hadn't even fully recovered (전쾌치). When we set foot on Japanese soil then, she was extremely happy, saying everything felt new. And we whispered (속살거렷다) about our profound future together. Since her father was sixty-seven this year, she even hinted at anticipating his unfortunate death (사거), saying that when that happened, we would build an earthly paradise.
We left Shimonoseki (하관), stayed for three days in fire-ravaged Tokyo, and a couple of days after leaving Tokyo, we arrived in Aomori (靑森). Upon arriving in Aomori, through her arrangement, she and I threw ourselves into some boarding house (下宿).
Lying in the boarding house for several days, recalling the past events, everything felt like a complete dream. And losing consciousness, in a hazy dream state wondering, 『Isn't this perhaps some monk's quarters (승방) in Keryongsan?』, my consciousness suddenly returned, and I muttered to myself, 『Ah—no, this is Aomori city! And I am looking forward to a happy future!』
Three days after that, she came looking for me in the middle of the night. Indeed, her father's sickbed condition had been critical, but recently his vitality seemed to be recovering gradually, although he still couldn't recognize people and didn't even know yet that I had come... Hearing her mother's words, the future seemed optimistic— Late into the night, she and I parted with a "kiss."
However, for several days after that, there was no news at all. Not even a postcard, nor could I see any sign of her. Unable to bear it, I went looking for her house, guided by the old landlady (로파), and peeked around, but there was no sign whatsoever.
After several days passed in anxiety, one day I unexpectedly heard the <21> tragic news from the old landlady that she had passed away the previous night by taking rat poison. And the next day, I received her final letter from the postman.
Ah! Indeed, it was all in vain— Misfortune is my destiny.
She, who I had known for only a few days—but who was my eternal true friend—imprinted an indelible mark deep within my heart, and at the end of her short life (生), attaching a fragrant "poignancy" (안탑가움), she departed far from this world rife with endless contradictions, becoming forever a guest of no return.
Three days later, carrying her final letter to me in my bosom, I sought out her grave, located about five ri from the city.
I cried my heart out, sighed, called out her name, stared blankly into the distance like someone who had lost their mind—thus I spent the entire day before her new grave.
And as the crimson glow of sunset tinted the evening sky like an "orange," I burned the long condolence message (吊文) I had been reading since morning in front of it, and like someone intoxicated, staggering along this path and that, I wandered into the electrically illuminated streets of Aomori.
– From then on, my mind became utterly simple –
The next day, relying solely on a walking stick, alone, with a listless, haggard complexion (낫빗), I came out to Aomori Port (靑森港).
Aomori is Japan's northern region. If I keep going north from here, I will reach the 『Land of Ice』. I boarded a ship. Not long after, I landed in Hokkaido (北해도). I boarded a train again. Thus I raced across Hokkaido from south to north. There, I boarded another ship that broke through the ice and came as far as Karafuto (화태 - Sakhalin). Landing in Karafuto, there was no train connecting south to north. From that day, I walked. I, who had never walked much, walked ten ri, sometimes twenty ri a day, little by little.
And so, in four months, passing through Nikolayevsk (니코라이에푸으쿠), I managed to reach a certain village in Okhotsk (오후쓰구).
I am now staying at the farmhouse of a kind-hearted Russian (로국인) couple. And now, receiving the white moonlight, seemingly crystallized (저화된듯한) like ice, pouring through the broken glass window onto my face, I lie with my weak, sallow (검누른) hands placed on my chest, imagining the future in a dim state of mind. And the desire to rise quickly and walk endlessly makes me impatient. But the old farming couple absolutely forbids it. <22>
Ah! My friend
The doctor's words reportedly declared my future path dangerous. Such words seem not even to register in my ears. Only, for now, finding it hard to defy the words of that kind old couple, I remain lying down. But my mind is simple, filled with the thought that I will rise and walk someday.

However, my mind grows increasingly hazy. Will I truly be able to rise soon and keep walking, seeking that endless land?... That, I can only await the instruction (指示) of the god of fate (運命의 神).
...The End...<23>

**Summary**

The story, presented as a letter received by the narrator (X) from his friend B, recounts B's tumultuous year. B, the estranged son of a wealthy family, fled to C city, took up teaching at a women's night school, and briefly worked at a bank. Rumors falsely linked him romantically with S, the wife of his friend Yŏngsŏn, causing great distress. Simultaneously, B experienced a fleeting, unfulfilled connection with another woman, L, who ultimately married a wealthy man. Disillusioned and slandered, B fled C city, contemplated becoming a monk in Keryongsan, but instead met a distressed Japanese woman. He traveled with her to her home in Aomori, Japan, only for her to commit suicide shortly after. Devastated, B wandered north through Hokkaido and Sakhalin, eventually reaching a village near Okhotsk in Siberia, where he now lies ill, cared for by a Russian couple, reflecting on his misfortunes and uncertain future. The narrator, moved by B's plight, resolves to find him.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the lives of educated Korean youth during the colonial period. It highlights the conflict between traditional family expectations (wealth, arranged prospects) and modern desires (individual freedom, love, self-determination), the pervasive impact of social gossip and reputation in a constrained society, and the economic precariousness faced even by the educated elite. B's despair, his contemplation of monastic life, his flight across colonial territories (Korea, Japan, Sakhalin, Siberia), and the tragic encounters reflect the profound sense of displacement, disillusionment, and limited options experienced by some Koreans under Japanese rule.

그 녀자는 원래 일본 북쪽 청삼市(靑森)에도 몃 재 안가는 큰 부호의 딸이엿다. 그는 소학과 고등녀학을 순차로 맛치고 그가 열 아홉 되던 재작년에 동경X고등사범에 입학하야 작년 가을까지 공부를 게속 하엿섯다 한다. 그러나 그 부친은 그를 동지에 수전로라는 별호를 듯는 엇던 부호에게 그를 며누리로 주려 하엿다. 자유를 꺽거서 자녀를 물시하는 것도 큰 실책이라 하겟거든 그 부천은 그를 그에게 며나리로 줌에 대하야는 그 내면에는 역시 금전이라 하는 큰 일홈이 드러잇는 것이다.
그러나 이것을 안- 그의 부인은 극력 반대하엿스나 그 부친은 족음도 반성함이 업섯다. 그 딸을 불너올녀 속히 결혼식을＜20＞ 하려 하엿다. 그러나 그는 그 모친에 주선으로 넉넉한 돈을 어디 가지고 고때 곳 집을 버서저 나온 것이다.
그는 처음에는 일본에서 각지로 표랑하다가 이 조선으로 왓다 한다.
처음에 나올 때에는 그의 모친이 그의 부친의 반성하는대로 통지하마 하든 것이 벌서 일년이 넘우 넘도록 편지를 하엿스나 소식이 업더니 몃칠 전에 경성으로 온 뎐보를 바덧스나 그것은 간단히 (急)급히 오라는 말뿐이엿슴으로 다시 통지를 하라 하엿더니 자긔 부친의 병이 위중하다는 뎐보가 또 왓다 한다.
그러나 그는 그대로 드러가기가 무엇 하여서 K군까지 가서 다시 편지를 기다리려 한 것이다. -
- 그 가티 지난 몃칠 후에 계룡산을 가려든 나는 전쾌치도 못한 그를 다리고 일본을 향하야 떠낫다. 그때 일본 땅에- 드러설 때에 그는 모든 것이 새로웁다고 몹시 깃버하엿다. 그리고 그와는 심원한 장래를 속살거렷다. 그는 자긔 부친이 금년 예순일곱이니까 불행히 사거(死去)할 때에는 우리는 지상락원을 건설하자는 말도 자긔 부친의 죽엄을 은근히 긔대하는 말이 빗치여 잇섯다.
우리는 하관을 떠나서 불에 탄- 동경에서 사흘을 묵고서 동경을 떠난 지 두어 날 만에 우리는 청삼(靑森)에 도착되엿다. 청삼에 도착되자 나는 그의 주선으로 그와 나는 엇던 하숙(下宿)에 몸을 던저 바렷다.
나는 하숙에 누어서 몃칠간을 - 지난 일을 회샹할 때에 모도가 전통 꿈속 가탯다. 그리고 의식을 일코서 『여긔가 계룡산 어느 승방(僧房)이나 안인가』 하는 어렴풋한 꿈 가운데 잇슬 때에는 별안간 의식은 새로워지며 『아 - 니 여긔는 청삼시! 그리고 행복의 압길을 바라보는 나!』 라고 혼자 중얼거렷다.
그런지 사흘 후 밤중에 그는 나를 차저왓다. 과연 그 부친의 병샹은 중태에 빠저 잇다가 요사히는 차차 원긔가 회복되는 모양이나 아직까지 사람은 못 아러보며 내가 온 것까지 아직 모른다고... 모친의 말을 들을 것 가트면 압길은 락관이 되겟다고 - 밤이 깁흔 후에 그와 나와는 「키-쓰」로써 난호엿다.
그러나 그 후 몃칠을 지나도록 아무러한 소식도 업섯다. 엽서도 한 장 업시 그의 그림자도 볼 수 업섯다. 나는 참다 못하야 주인 로파에게 그의 집을 차저가서 기웃기웃 하엿스나 아무러한 긔색도 업셧다.
그로부터 불안으로 몃칠을 지난 엇던 날 나는 뜻밧게 주인 로파로부터 그가 지난 밤에 쥐 잡는 약으로 세샹을 떠낫다는＜21＞ 비참한 소리를 들엇다. 그리고 그 이튼날 우체부한테서 그의 마지막 글을 밧게 되엿다.
아! 역시 헛게 다- 불행은 나의 운명이다.
사귄 지 몃칠 안되는 - 그러나 나의 영원한 참 동무이든 그는 나의 마음 가운대 깁히깁히 지어지지 안는 인(印)을 처주고서 그의 짤븐(生)의 끗에는 향끗한 「안탑가움」을 매여 달고서 모순이 끗칠 사이 업는 이 세상을 멀니하여 영윈히 불귀의 객이 된 것이다.
사흘 후 나는 그가 내에게 준 최후의 글을 품에 품고서 시내에서 한- 오리나 되는 그의 묘지를 차저갓다.
나는 힘껏 울고 한숨쉬고 소리질너 부르고 실성한 것 가티 먼- 곳을 바라보기도 하고 이와 가티 그 날에 하로를 전통 그의 새무덤 압헤서 보냇다.
그리고 저녁 한울에 「오렌지」에 붉은 노을이 질 제 나는 아침에 읽던 장문의 조문(吊文)을 그 압헤서 불살으고 무엇에 취한 사람 모양으로 빗틀거리며 이 길 저 길을 도라서 뎐긔로 환-한 청삼의 거리로 허매여 드러왓다.
- 그로부터 나의 마음은 아조 단순하엿다 -
그 이튼날 나는 다만 홀몸에 죽장을 의지하고서 힘업시 초조한 낫빗으로 청삼항(靑森港)으로 나왓다.
청삼은 일본의 북국이다. 이 위로 작고 갈 것이면 『어름나라』에 이를 것이다. 나는 배를 탓다. 그리 얼마 안 가서 북해도에 나렷다. 나는 다시 긔차를 탓다. 그래서 북해도의 남에서 북으로 뛰여 건넛다. 나는 거긔서 또다시 어름을 헤치며 가는 배를 타고서 화태까지 왓다. 화태에 나려서는 남에서 북으로 관통하는 차는 업섯다. 나는 그날부터 걸엇다. 거름을 거러보지 못한 나는 하로에 십리 혹은 이십리 이러케 작고 걸엇다.
그래서 나는 넉달만에 「니코라이에푸으쿠」를 지나서 오후쓰구 엇던 촌락까지 올 수가 잇섯다.
나는 지금 엇던 사람 조흔 로국인(露國人)에 농가에 잇는 몸이다. 그리고 지금 나는 다 깨여진 유리창을 통하야 새여 드러오는 어름으로 저화된듯한 흰 달빗을 얼골로 바드면서 힘업는 검누른 손을 가슴 위에 언저놋코 희미한 정신으로 미래를 상샹하며 잇는 나이다. 그리고 어서 속히 니러나서 끗업시 것고 십흔 마음이 나로 하야곰 조급증을 나게 한다. 그러나 늙은 농부 내외는 절대로 못한다 한다. ＜22＞

의사의 말이 나의 압길이 위험하다고 선고하엿다 한다. 나는 그러헌 말은 귀에도 안 드러오는 것 갓다. 다만 아직은 그 친절한 늙은 내외의 말을 꺽기 어려워서 누어잇다. 그러나 어느 때던지 니러나서 거르리라 하는 그런 마음으로 단순하엿다.

그러나 나의 정신은 점점 몽롱하여간다. 과연 속히 니러나서 끗업는 나라를 차저 작고 갈 수가 잇슬는지?... 그것은 오직 운명의 신(運命의 神)에 지시(指示)만 기다릴 뿐이다.
