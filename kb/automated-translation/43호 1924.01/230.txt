Magazine Title: 개벽 제43호
Publication Date: 1924년 01월 01일
Article Title: 十五分間(喜劇全一幕) 禁無斷上演 (Fifteen Minutes (A One-Act Comedy) Unauthorized Performance Prohibited)
Author: 金雲汀
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Fifteen Minutes
Kim Unjŏng

Characters
Sŏk Saran: Young Entrepreneur, 30 years old
Kim Chinŏn: Critic, 32 years old
Sŏl Kajŏng: Wealthy Widow, 27 years old
Yŏm Hoae: Sŏk's Lover, 22 years old
No Such'ŏn: Certain Bank Clerk, 26 years old
Other: 1 Maid, around 20 years old

Time: Present day. Season: Early Winter Afternoon

Place: Pukch'on, Kyŏngsŏng.

Stage

The scene is a room serving as both a reception room and study for a certain young entrepreneur. On the front wall hangs a framed portrait; slightly away from the wall beneath it stands a large wooden clock, and to its left, a long armchair. In the corners of the room are triangular tables, upon which rest an ancient Buddhist statue, Koryŏ celadon, and a stone clock. On the right side is a small, unlit stove. Slightly to the right of the stage center is a round table, upon which sit a desk phone, a rug, an inkstand, and an ashtray. Around the table are scattered 4 or 5 chairs. The left side of the stage has an entrance leading directly to the front door, and on the right side, a closed door leads to the bedroom. <193> The room's decoration, while hard to call luxurious, features cheap, shiny Western furniture arranged to emulate, as much as possible, the life of the wealthy, revealing the owner's vain inner life. As the curtain rises, Sŏk Saran, wearing a morning coat and gold-rimmed glasses, is leaning over the table, flipping through an account book. He then closes it, picks up a Luzon cigar from the ashtray, relights it, stands up from his chair, and paces the room with a satisfied expression.

Sŏk: The Hanyang Trust should be fully established by tomorrow. No matter how many shares the rural shareholders hold, I can somehow control them with my methods. Some of them might be campaigning for the managing director position, but there should be no shortage of worthy gentlemen who will cast influential votes for me, so the position surely won't fall into someone else's hands. But still, you never know. If some troublemaker starts stirring things up unexpectedly, I might face an unforeseen predicament. If that happens, I'll have to use my last resort. I'll ask Kajŏng for another few ten-thousands of won... (Pause) Anyway, for the time being, I must use all my strength to deceive Kajŏng well, so she doesn't leave my side even for a moment...

(At this moment, the desk phone rings. Sŏk quickly goes to the phone and picks up the receiver.)

Sŏk: Yes, who is it? Yes, that's right. (Sounding pleased) Is that you, Miss Kajŏng? Yes, yes, you said you'd come this afternoon, so I've been waiting eagerly until now. Don't mention it, really. Why would any other woman come to see me? I've turned away all visitors since this afternoon, just waiting for you, Miss Kajŏng. Yes, yes, please come right away.

(As Sŏk finishes speaking and is about to hang up, the maid enters through the left entrance, holding out a name card.)

Maid: A visitor has arrived.

(Sŏk shows a slightly displeased expression, takes the name card, and frowns.)

Sŏk: Didn't I tell you earlier? From this afternoon, turn away all male visitors. (Looks at the name card again) Well, a troublemaker has arrived. (Looks back at the maid) So, what did you say? You probably told him I was in, didn't you?

Maid: ... (Hesitates, unable to answer) Before I could say anything, he just barged into the entryway. What could I do? (Looks back) <194>

(Footsteps are heard from the left entrance, and Kim Chinŏn enters briskly. He has long hair, wears a strange Turkish fez pushed down on it, and a shabby grey Western suit. He holds a Western-style pipe slanted in his mouth. Sŏk, still seated, turns towards him, startled, then quickly changes his expression to a smile and stands up from his chair.)

Kim: Hey there, I'm the kind of person who always comes in like this without introduction. You'll forgive me today too, I suppose, hahaha... (Laughs)

Sŏk: (Walks forward, extending his hand, they shake hands) How long has it been? Come on in.

Kim: I came today to congratulate you. That Hanyang Trust, or whatever junk trust it is, that you've been running around promoting day and night – the shareholders' meeting is tomorrow, right? Anyway, by your outlook on life, it's certainly a great success. (Kim goes to the table and sits facing Sŏk.)

Sŏk: That's right, it's a success. It really has been a long time since we last met. We saw each other briefly at Ch'ŏngnyangsa Temple recently, and this is probably the first time since then. So, how's it going? You're still writing quite fiercely these days.

Kim: I write, and I'll always write. I consider it my lifeblood. And it's the thing I most want to do.

Sŏk: I always admire your writing stamina. I just took a solid hit from you now, but your usual "cynicism" (皮肉 - hinikku) is still there. However, this time, it truly is, to some extent, my success. Among companies of this kind currently, it has the most substantial capital power. Not only that, but at a time when everyone is crying financial panic, the 500,000 won in paid-in capital for this large 2,000,000 won company was somehow all gathered within a week, without any shortfall. Even I think it's a miracle, looking back now.

Kim: A miracle, huh. Right. (Chuckles scornfully)

Sŏk: (Looking at the maid) Hey, bring some warm tea.

Maid: Yes. (Replies and exits through the left entrance) <195>

Sŏk: It might be inappropriate to lump you all together, but these days, the gentlemen residing in the special enclave known as artists make a fuss about miracles only concerning things that don't exist in this world. But my venture this time is truly a miracle plainly visible before one's eyes.

Kim: (Exhaling a long stream of smoke) Such things can be called miracles if you want. When you see these things called humans, who cannot sustain their lives without eating the real substance called rice, spending thousands or tens of thousands of won to buy your lies, which are emptier than water bubbles, and making noise about trust companies and stock companies, that too can be called a miracle in this world. Ha, haha, ha... (Laughs) However, what you call a miracle is probably worth less than the steam whistles (汽笛) constantly blowing at train stations or tobacco factories. The whistle from a factory at least makes a 'toot' sound, but the miracle you just mentioned will likely rot away without even a smell within a few days, inside the heavy shell called reality.

Sŏk: (Face flushing as his emotions suddenly flare up) You force everything from one extreme to the other, so such incomprehensible criticisms always emerge. Take a closer look at the reality of this society. It's difficult to declare everything so easily as your tone suggests. In the end, one cannot satisfy one's life based on such flimsy grounds as falsehood, nor can there be success.

Kim: That's what I'm saying. The prospectus for the trust company you've created this time is ultimately a bundle of falsehoods. I don't know if there's material inside to deceive some foolish simpleton (八朔童), but it can't keep wearing that naive mask forever. Then again, who knows, hail sometimes falls even in May or June. If such anomalies continue, perhaps your bundle of falsehoods might maintain its mask to some extent.

Sŏk: (Voice rising and tone becoming forceful as his emotions heighten further) So, are you saying you deny all economic activity in this society?

Kim: No, (In a calm tone) I'm not denying everything. If you want to eat rice, you have to obtain it, right? If you want to wear clothes, <196> don't you need to make appropriate preparations on the street? If there is economic activity in that sense, I certainly affirm it. However, the so-called economists making noise in Chosŏn these days try to make the raw material for rice out of things like steel or flint, so how can it digest properly...

(At this moment, the maid brings in coffee cups on a Western tray, places them before Kim and Sŏk, and exits.)

Kim: (Takes a sip from the cup, grimaces, and puts the cup back down) Hey, this is truly steel instead of rice for me. Instead of this overly sweet coffee, give me a glass of bracing liquor. I may be wearing Western clothes, but a large brass kettle of traditional rice wine suits me just fine. Guffaws.)

(The maid, heading towards the left entrance, glances back, smiles, and exits.)

Sŏk: (Still unable to dissolve his emotions) Liquor... let's drink liquor some other time. I'm a bit busy today.

Kim: You must be busy. These days, you're preoccupied with piloting an airplane loaded with bundles of lies. Haha, ha... (Laughs)

Sŏk: (Emotions seem about to explode) You seem determined to label all my endeavors as falsehoods today. That's your distorted criticism (曲評). I doubt you yourself, who makes such distorted criticisms.

Kim: (Seeing Sŏk's emotions gradually heating up, speaks in a deliberately mocking tone) Of course, I doubt myself too. However, I don't live a life like a dangling hot air balloon (虛風船), like you people who run around merely talking about being businessmen or influential figures. If you look into the lives of those who wander around with signboards as big as doors attached to their necks, claiming political movements or social movements these days, everything boils down to the two characters for vanity. Their ultimate goal is just to gather material for their name cards; it's all just cheap ambition to get a fancy title (肩書) in size 5 print. Do you think there's even one person who truly understands their own life or their mission? If there is, it would only be the small fraction of critics who dissect and explain the life of humanity. (Carelessly taps his pipe ash into the glass ashtray. Sŏk watches anxiously, afraid it might break.)

Sŏk: Critics. You mean critics with crooked pens like yours? In my eyes, unemployed people like you <197> should all be dragged off together to a tropical place like the South Sea Islands (南洋群島) and used as pioneer laborers until beads of sweat pour down your foreheads – that would be just right.

Kim: Exactly, our mission is always pioneering. You know that well. A critic is a guide who points out the obstacles blocking the path of our human life by criticizing what is dark as dark and what is bright as bright. In that sense, a critic can certainly be called a type of pioneer. Our lives are entirely different from bundles of lies like yours, wrapped in a morning coat. (Stands up from the chair and paces the room.)

Sŏk: (His suppressed emotions burst forth at once, his voice suddenly rising) You came here today just to insult me, didn't you? From beginning to end, you've called everything I do falsehoods and lies, trying to destroy my entire character. I am now a gentleman who has established a considerable footing (地盤) in society. My reputation has no fear of being shaken by your distorted writing (曲筆). If you've seen even the slightest falsehood in my life or my business, as you claim, then find it and show me.

Kim: Hahaha... (Laughs) You seem quite agitated. Calm down a bit. If I were to discover your falsehood, wouldn't it be embarrassing for a gentleman's dignity? Think more calmly before making such assertions.

Sŏk: (Jumps up from the chair) No! There is absolutely no falsehood in my life! All the business I conduct is done with my own conviction. There's nothing for me to be caught out on by others. (Paces the room to calm his agitated state.)

Kim: (Looks at Sŏk once more) Are you sure? About what you just said?

Sŏk: (In a forceful tone) Of course.

Kim: Then, would you like to make a bet with me? What will you do if I discover falsehood within your life?

Sŏk: I'll do whatever you ask. <198>

Kim: (Guffaws) You've gained a lot of courage lately. Alright, let's make a bet, just to test that courage of yours. How shall we bet?

Sŏk: Whatever bet you like. (Goes to the table, excitedly stubs out his Luzon cigar in the ashtray, forcefully pulls a chair, and sits facing Kim.)

Kim: Then, let's bet like this. You money-worshippers (金錢萬能主義者) say that money is the most precious thing in the world. So, you bet your entire fortune...

Sŏk: Then what will you bet?

Kim: Me? I'll bet the most vital thing in my life, my critic's pen.

Sŏk: Your critic's pen? (Looks at Kim's face blankly, as if dumbfounded.)

Kim: If I lose, starting tomorrow, I will absolutely stop writing criticism. And I will surrender, stating that I retract all the criticism I've written in the past.

Sŏk: Really? (As if confirming.)

Kim: Of course. I mean it.

Sŏk: Don't make such rash promises. Then, shouldn't a bet have some standard? If, by twelve o'clock tonight, you monitor my life and discover any falsehood, or if I myself engage in false words or actions, then of course, I lose, and I will hand over my entire fortune as wagered. If no such fact exists, then you lose.

Kim: What? Until ten o'clock tonight? (Chuckles) Why wait that long? In just 10 minutes, I'll surely claim victory. (Shows a mocking attitude.)

Sŏk: (Clenches his fists, trembling with uncontrollable anger) What did you say? In 10 minutes? How dare you insult me like this? Huh? (Clenches his teeth tightly) Don't do that; for the sake of your future critical career, let's make it one hour. <199>

Kim: (Showing firm confidence) No. What's the point of a whole hour? Five minutes would be plenty, but especially to extend the life of your false existence even slightly, let's make it 15 minutes.

Sŏk: Ah, whatever! Do as you please. But don't complain later that the time was too short.

Kim: You're the one who should keep your mouth shut tight for 15 minutes. Lest the bundle of lies packed in your stomach comes tumbling out.

Sŏk: The result is the final issue. Hey, shouldn't we have proof later? Let's write a contract and each put our seal on it. (Pulls paper from the table drawer.)

Kim: (Guffaws) Look at that. What's this about a contract? Trying to adopt such formalities like child's play, isn't that falsehood itself?

Sŏk: Stop making such sophistry (詭辯) with your mouth and come over here and write.

Kim: (Smiling slyly, goes to Sŏk's side) What exactly are you going to write?

Sŏk: ... (Face sullen with anger, head bowed, picks up a fountain pen and writes something.)

Kim: (Peeking curiously at what Sŏk is writing) What? A contract... hmm, hahaha (Laughs out loud, then puts his hands behind his back and paces the room again. He picks up a Koryŏ celadon piece from the triangular table in the left corner and examines it. Sŏk, without looking back, continues writing something, then slams the pen down on the table with a 'thwack'.)

Sŏk: Come here quickly, I'm done writing. Just put your seal on it.

Kim: (Walks quickly towards Sŏk) So, what did you write? Let me see. (Snatches the contract from Sŏk's hand and reads it aloud) Contract.
Kim Chinŏn, 38 Samch'ŏng-dong, Kyŏngsŏng Prefecture
Sŏk Saran, 25 Ch'ŏngjin-dong, ditto
The undersigned parties enter into the following agreement. For convenience, Kim Chinŏn shall be referred to as Party A, and Sŏk Saran as Party B. <200>
1. Terms of Contract: During the 15 minutes from 3:45 PM to 4:00 PM on December 7, 1923 AD, if Party A discovers falsehood in Party B's life, or if Party B himself engages in false words or actions, Party B shall transfer his entire assets to Party A. However, if Party A fails to discover falsehood in Party B within the aforementioned time, or if Party B himself does not engage in false words or actions, Party A shall cease publishing his professional criticism and simultaneously retract all of Party A's previously published criticisms entirely. This agreement is hereby made.
This contract is prepared in duplicate, with Party A and Party B each retaining one copy.
"It seems quite clear."
December 7, 1923 AD
Contracting Parties: Kim Chinŏn, Sŏk Saran

(Kim finishes reading and shakes with uncontrollable laughter. Sŏk, unable to bear the excitement, clenches his fists, his shoulders rising and falling as he fumes.)

(Note: The contract text above should naturally be delivered as Sŏk's lines publicly, but it is necessary to prepare the original contract text on a separate sheet and place it in the table drawer beforehand.)

Sŏk: Stop laughing and hurry up and put your seal on it. I won't forgive you today. I'll make sure the root of criticism is pulled right out of that crooked mouth of yours.

Kim: (Stifling laughter by covering his mouth with his hand) I've never owned a seal. I'm not a false gentleman like you.

Sŏk: Then put your fingerprint on it. (Grabs Kim's wrist and pulls.)

Kim: Do I really have to? Fine, I'll do it.

(Opening the ink pad case (印朱匣) on the table, Kim applies his fingerprint, and Sŏk stamps his crystal (水晶) seal. They each take one copy and stuff it firmly into their back suit pockets.) <201>

Kim: (Suddenly looking at the wooden clock) From what time did we say the 15 minutes start?

Sŏk: Didn't the contract say from 3:45 PM to 4:00 PM?

Kim: (Pointing at the clock) Then it's exactly 3:45 PM now. Well then, shall we really begin the bet? But since it's a fact that I will win, just how much is your fortune worth, anyway? Isn't it perhaps on the minus side? (Sits down facing Sŏk.)

Sŏk: Even if I sold it cheap, it's worth at least 10,000 Won at market price.

Kim: Then this poor critic will suddenly become a bourgeois ('뿔쪼아' - ppuljjoa, likely slang for bourgeois). I'll treat you properly later. We'll call the geishas you like, play the drum too. Alright then, let's start. Begin! However, if before the time is up, you reveal our bet to a third party and take preventive measures, you obviously lose.

Sŏk: I, of course, have no need to resort to such base tactics. You be careful yourself.

(At this moment, the maid enters from the left entrance.)

Maid: A visitor has arrived.

Sŏk: (In an angry tone) A visitor? Who is it? (Inadvertently blurts out) Not in...

Kim: (Quickly catching the end of Sŏk's sentence) Who's not in? You're not in?

Sŏk: (Flustered) No, who... who is it?

Maid: (Observing Sŏk's reaction) It's Miss Kajŏng, who always comes, she just arrived.

Sŏk: (Startled, as if realizing something, then in a weak voice) Miss Kajŏng? (Scratches his head.)

Maid: Shall I ask her to come in?

Kim: (Quickly interjecting) Yes, since she's a guest who has come all this way, of course, ask her to come in. (Turns back to Sŏk) Then, I shall make myself scarce. However, I must watch you for 15 minutes. <202>

(As soon as the maid exits through the left entrance, Kajŏng, dressed in a black Western suit, enters the room. Seeing this, Kim quickly hides behind the wooden clock. Sŏk, flustered, watches Kajŏng enter, then takes a couple of steps forward, pretending to greet her.)

Kajŏng: I meant to come a bit earlier, but I ran into some people from the Women's Association on the way, so I'm late. (Looks around the room once, observing, then suddenly changes her demeanor, quickly goes to Sŏk, and grabs both his hands, shaking them.)

Sŏk: (Glances once towards the clock, maintaining a solemn demeanor) I've been waiting eagerly. Please, sit over there. (Points to a chair on the other side of the table. Kajŏng, noticing Sŏk's attitude is different from usual, feels suspicious, looks around the room once more, then sits in the indicated chair. Sŏk also sits in the chair opposite her.)

Kajŏng: (Looks behind her again, speaks in a flirtatious tone) Why are you being so formal today? This morning, I went to six banks trying to arrange that 30,000 Won loan. The banks are also strapped for cash these days, it's chaotic. Perhaps unable to say they have no money, they made all sorts of excuses, like the manager hasn't arrived yet, or the person with the vault key is sick. I nearly died trying to get it. You don't even know the trouble I went through, and now why are you acting so formal? Come closer. (Stands up abruptly and pulls Sŏk by the shoulder.)

(At this moment, Kim Chinŏn, hidden behind the clock, pokes his head out, sees the scene, grins, and ducks back.)

Sŏk: (Glances again towards the clock) I'm feeling a bit unwell today. (Touches his forehead) But it's nothing serious, so I should feel better later.

Kajŏng: (Stares intently at Sŏk) You drank too much rice wine again last night, didn't you? Men stay up all night at restaurants or clubs, claiming it's for socializing or whatnot, without caring for their bodies, right? If you're really uncomfortable, lie down on that bed for a bit. I'll cool your head for you.

Sŏk: No, it's not that bad. But, the 30,000 Won for the promissory note (手形), has it all been covered (充數)?

Kajŏng: That's right, I spent the whole morning going to six banks because of that. <203>

Sŏk: So, you brought the money here then?

Kajŏng: It's right here in this bag. (Shakes the handbag she's holding.)

Sŏk: Someone from the 16th bank will be coming to collect it soon, so this time, don't deceive them, you must pay it...

(Kim, behind the clock, hears Sŏk mention deceiving and pokes his head out again, then retracts it.)

Sŏk: Then, please give me the money. And, today I have someone I need to meet secretly, so (Quickly glances at the clock) Miss Kajŏng, please return home early.

Kajŏng: (Eyes narrowing) Yes, I'm sure you do. Because the woman you're meeting secretly will be coming.

Sŏk: (As if startled) Absolutely not! What are you saying? Besides Miss Kajŏng... (Quickly looks at the clock) what other woman could there be? Please don't joke like that, just give me the money.

Kajŏng: Yes, (Suddenly adopts a stern demeanor) I will give you this money today. But, in return, I must hear one definite answer from Mr. Saran.

Sŏk: (Startled) What are you suddenly saying? Of course, I've always relied on Miss Kajŏng's support, and I planned the company this time trusting in you, and aren't the results quite good? What else is there for you to hear from me now?

Kajŏng: No, it's not about such mundane things. It's about my final issue.

Sŏk: (Startled) Final issue...

Kajŏng: Our marriage issue.

Sŏk: (Looks towards the clock, quickly stands up, flustered) Let's... let's talk about that tomorrow. Please wait a moment.

(Looks again towards the clock, trying to stop Kajŏng from speaking.)

Kajŏng: (In a sharp voice, raising her tone) Wait? For me, that issue is my very life. What irresponsible words are these, telling me to wait? During the year and a half I've associated with you, the slander about my chastity has made it impossible for me to hold my head up in this world! (Slams her palm on the table. Sŏk jumps, startled.) Not only that, my considerable fortune too, <204> I was deceived by you and lost it all...

(Flustered, goes towards Kajŏng, tries to cover her mouth with his hand, and glances back towards the clock. Kajŏng also turns and looks back.)

Sŏk: Th-that kind of talk, let's discuss it quietly later when we go for a walk.

Kajŏng: A walk? With whom, may I ask? With your third lover? Do as you please. I won't be fooled anymore. All that's left is this 30,000 Won I brought today. (Shakes the bag) This is all that's left.

(The minute hand on the clock has moved forward 5 minutes, pointing to 10 minutes before 4:00. Sŏk looks increasingly unable to control his anxiety.)

Sŏk: Miss Kajŏng, you seem very agitated today. (Pats Kajŏng's shoulder, pointing towards the bedroom) Go lie down in that room for a while. Yes, yes, please do. I have an important matter until four o'clock. (Looks at the clock) The... no, that issue, let's decide on it again later.

Kajŏng: (Screams sharply) What? What did you say? No! Tell me right now, right here! Just as this money is the last of my fortune, this request of mine is also the final one!

Sŏk: (Pointing at the clock, in an urgent tone) Just... just wait for 10, 10 minutes!

Kajŏng: Today, no matter what you say, I won't be fooled. (Stands up abruptly, grabs Sŏk's suit lapel forcefully, and pulls him down into the chair. Sŏk plops down.)

(Kim pokes his head out again, sees the scene, and retracts it.)

Sŏk: (Shakes his body, scratches his head vigorously with both hands) Oh no, this is terrible! What should I do? (Stomps his feet.)

(The maid, hearing the stomping, starts to enter as if responding, then hesitates upon seeing Kajŏng holding onto Sŏk.)

Maid: Did you call? <205>

Sŏk: (Startled, looks at the maid) What is it? Who called?

(While the maid stands bewildered, not knowing what's going on, footsteps are heard outside the left entrance, and Hoae enters. She is wearing a black Western skirt, a pink jeogori (Korean traditional upper garment), and a modern scarf. Sŏk and Kajŏng are startled. The maid bows her head in greeting.)

Hoae: (Enters, then stops abruptly, seeing Sŏk and Kajŏng close together. Her expression changes instantly.) You seem to be having a very interesting time today. I waited at home all this time, but you didn't come, so I wondered what happened. Other times, just one word from me, and you'd be present two or three hours early. (Shoots a venomous glare at Sŏk.)

Sŏk: (Takes a couple of steps towards Hoae) I had guests at home today, and I was busy, so I couldn't go yet. I'm truly sorry.

(The maid stands blankly for a moment, then exits through the left entrance. Kajŏng stands up, stares hard at Hoae, then sits down in the armchair near the clock. Meanwhile, Sŏk guides Hoae to sit to the left of the table, and Sŏk himself sits in the opposite chair - the one Kajŏng had just occupied - quickly grabbing Kajŏng's handbag from the table and stuffing it into his back pocket.)

Hoae: (Pointing at Kajŏng) Mr. Saran, who exactly is that woman? Yes?

Sŏk: (Hesitantly) R-r-relative's w-w-wife... no, a g-guest...

Kajŏng: (Stands up abruptly, facing Hoae) Who exactly are you? Coming into someone else's house without even an introduction, who are you?

Sŏk: (Flustered) P-please, why are you both acting like this...

Hoae: Me? You mean me? I am someone with the right to enter this house like this without an introduction. This house belongs to my future husband. (Sŏk quickly tries to cover Hoae's mouth with his hand.) You should get a grip too.

(Sŏk acts frantically, as if losing his mind, repeatedly glancing towards the clock. The two women, also finding it strange, occasionally glance towards the clock. The clock's minute hand already points to 5 minutes before four. Unable to bear it any longer, Sŏk clasps his hands together, bows his head <206> deeply, as if praying.)

Sŏk: P-please, why are you both doing this? If you knew each other, you wouldn't be in a position to act like this. What is there to fight about like this? If you both act like this, I'll be in big trouble. (Glances again towards the clock.)

Kajŏng: (Grabs Sŏk's arm forcefully) Mr. Saran! Are you letting her say such things in front of me? Quickly, send that woman away. Hurry!

Sŏk: Please, just bear with it for 5, 5 minutes.

Hoae: (Grabs Sŏk's left sleeve forcefully) Who exactly is that woman? Who is she to be so brazen? Say something! (Stomps her feet.)

Sŏk: ...J-just, bear with it, just for 5 minutes...

Kajŏng: (Pulls Sŏk's arm forcefully again. Sŏk loses his balance and stumbles as if about to fall.) Why can't you speak? Say I'm your lover!

Hoae: (Stares directly at Kajŏng) Lover? Lover? Whose lover are you talking about? Whose lover are you?

Kajŏng: I'm the lover of the owner of this house! Mr. Sŏk Saran's lover! Now do you understand? Come here, hurry. (Grabs Sŏk's wrist and pulls.)

Hoae: You're the lover? What kind of lover? The lover who provides money? The shell of a lover? Why don't you take off that widow's garb before you come here, so brazenly. (Turns to Sŏk) Quickly send that woman away! Oh, I'm so furious, what should I do? (Stomps her feet and pulls Sŏk's back suit tail forcefully. Sŏk stumbles backward, about to fall.)

Sŏk: ... (Head bowed, thinking about something.)

Kajŏng: (Looking into Sŏk's face) Speak plainly! Answer me as we promised. Please, I can't stand this frustration!

Sŏk: ... (Remains with head bowed, then, as if having made a decision, clenches both fists tightly, looks towards the clock, and shouts loudly) <207> Damn it, why did you have to come and ruin someone's life like this? Huh!

(The two women, not understanding the reason, are startled, look towards the clock, then stare intently at Sŏk's face again.)

Hoae: Please, why are you like this today? Just tell me straight. Who do you really love?

Kajŏng: (Pulls Sŏk's clothes again) Tell the truth! Can't you say you love me?

(The clock's minute hand has almost reached four. Outside the left entrance, the maid's voice is heard: "He's not here. He went out today. I told you he's not here..." followed by a man's voice: "Even if he's not here, I can't just leave today. Even if it's midnight, I absolutely must see him before I go. **Summary**

"Fifteen Minutes" is a one-act comedy satirizing the superficiality and deceit of a young entrepreneur, Sŏk Saran, in 1920s Kyŏngsŏng. A cynical critic, Kim Chinŏn, bets Sŏk that he can expose the falsehoods in Sŏk's life within fifteen minutes. During this period, Sŏk's attempts to manage his lover (Hoae), his wealthy widow patron (Kajŏng), and a creditor demanding payment (Such'ŏn) unravel spectacularly, revealing his manipulative relationships, fake business dealings (including paying off a debt with blank paper), and the hollowness of his "success" with the Hanyang Trust. Kim ultimately wins the bet as Sŏk's carefully constructed facade collapses. From a historical perspective, the play offers insights into the early stages of Korean capitalism under colonial rule, critiquing the get-rich-quick mentality, speculative ventures like trust companies, and the moral compromises accompanying modernization and the pursuit of wealth. Historians might find the portrayal of social types (the vain entrepreneur, the cynical intellectual, the financially exploited women, the desperate creditor), the commentary on emergent business practices, and the underlying critique of superficiality and "허위 (falsehood)" in colonial society particularly revealing.
