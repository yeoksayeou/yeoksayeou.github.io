**Body Text Translation:**
From a Seoul Mouse to a Country Mouse
						Ch'unp'a
Warning. Big trouble. Mouse Saengwŏn, no, Detective Mouse (鼠揁探), is going to dig up and reveal all the inside secrets of humans. He is a world-famous detective (名揁探) who they say will expose even the greatest human secret, their goings-on in the dead of night. As he reveals them, he will make them public to the world, with no regard for reputation or forgiveness. Beware.

"Year of the Rat, Year of the Rat, Kaptcha Year! Year, year, the Year of the Mouse!! Our year! Ah, how wonderful! Come, let's gather and play. Let's have a drink, and dance. Let's sing. Those human fellows are drinking too. Eating too. The old ones smile fondly, saying 'good', and the young ones jump around with joy. Come, let's gather and play. Let's drink. Gather in the ceiling! Kitchen mouse, come! Storeroom mouse, come! Outhouse mouse, come all! And tell the back-house mouse to come, and the front-house mouse too. Let's gather and play. High and low, old and young, let's throw everything open and play! On this joyous Kaptcha Year morning..."
"Hey there! Big trouble! Big trouble!"
"What? Big trouble? What big trouble?"
"He's dead! He's dead!"
"What? Dead? Who's dead?"
"The young mouse from the floor-room house fell into the rice chest!"
"Oh dear... What a disaster on New Year's morning. So what happened?"
"He got caught by those human fellows and was cruelly killed (Massacre! Massacre is what those bastards do best). Cruelly killed! By that vixen-like woman... I just saw his four legs being lifted and tossed over the wall. Eek, how horrifying—"
"On this auspicious New Year's Day, especially the first morning welcoming our year, what kind of disaster is this? That kid was always reckless. A blind fool with no fear. Damned kid... Aaargh, the dead are dead, the living live on, they say a mouse's fate is decided by Heaven (鼠命이 在天)..." <70>

To Elder Brother Changmi-taek in the countryside, this was the first scene in our house this morning upon waking.

"Ring-ring"
"The phone— Since it's New Year's morning today, there should be quite a few New Year's greeting calls... Yes, yes, who is this?"
"Is this the residence of Sŏ Saengwŏn?"
"Yes, it is. Who is calling?"
"Yes. I am Cho Sŏbang from Pukaksan Mountain. Is this Sŏ Saengwŏn? How is the New Year? Have you received many blessings?"
"Thank you. How is your New Year? Are all your household members well?"
"We are all fine. Are the Inner Room family, the Across-the-Way family, the Ceiling family, the Floor Room family, the Kitchen family, the Storeroom family, the Outhouse family—are they all well?"
"Yes, yes, but... the second young mouse of the Floor Room family died this morning. He was cruelly killed by the human fellows."
"Oh, what a disaster! Especially on New Year's morning... How did it happen? Ah, really!"
"Saying he needed to taste some rice for New Year's morning, and also wanting to get some for his mother, he and the third one clumsily jumped onto the rice chest, slipped on the lid, and tumbled inside. The third one saw it, eyes wide with shock, and ran away alone... And so, he ended up caught by the hands of that vixen-like mistress of the house and was cruelly, brutally killed. He was always so reckless..."
"Aie, horrifying— Well, since he's dead, I'll say it, he was quite reckless, wasn't he? And fearless too. Maybe that's why he alone seemed to incur the hatred of the human fellows. Anyway, was the funeral service carried out properly?... Hello? Sŏ Saengwŏn? The New Year's greeting has turned into a condolence call. Let's just count the dead one as dead, and may all of you who are alive receive many blessings. Especially since this New Year is your Year of the Rat, right? Extra congratulations. Don't hog all the blessings just because it's the Year of the Rat, share some with us birds too, okay?"
"Thank you for saying so. Whether it's Kapcha, Pyŏngja, Muja, or Kyŏngja, any year with 'Ja' (子 - Rat) is our year, but even the human fellows attach special meaning to Kapcha, acting as if some great fortune will arise, going on about 'Kaptcha Year, Kaptcha Year'. But for us too, the mood is definitely different from other years. <71> Anyway, we intend to spend it meaningfully. So, starting from the New Year, we plan to stick closely to the human world and find out in detail about their inner circumstances, outer circumstances, and all their situations. Then, we intend to introduce the inside story of those fellows to the world, without omitting a single thing, just as we see and hear it. Perhaps Cho Sŏbang will get to see it too. Just this morning, I was jotting down the doings of the human fellows in our house as the first diary entry for the New Year when your call came..."
"Haha! Is that so? That's a good thing. Speaking of which, there's a need to examine the human world more closely. The world of those malicious, cunning (奸巧), arrogant (검방지고), and deceitful fellows—those fellows who are famous for their arrogance, thinking there's nothing in the universe besides themselves, not even counting us birds or mice in their reckoning..."
"That's because those fellows, while pretending to be clever, are deluded (迷惑). Originally, the special privilege in this world belongs only to your Cho clan and our Sŏ clan, doesn't it? Didn't the Creator, after creating everything in this world, find supervising it all too busy, and specifically command our Sŏ clan and Cho clan, these two families, to supervise this world? Thus, didn't your Cho clan take charge of supervising daytime matters (晝間事), and our Sŏ clan take charge of nighttime matters (夜間事)? Isn't this a special privilege handed down from the creation of heaven and earth until today? That's why even the human fellows acknowledge this, saying 'mice hear the night words, and birds hear the day words,' fearing and avoiding (忌避) us greatly, don't they?"
"True— indeed... Let's meet sometime. It's too rambling on the phone."
"Yes. Let's do that. By the way, are our cousins living near Cho Sŏbang all doing well?"
"Ah— Squirrel, Mole, House Mouse— you mean them? They are fine. Squirrel Saengwŏn came under the tree where we were sitting just this morning, happy because it's the New Year, did a little dance for a while, and left."
"Ah— thank you. Let's hang up now..."

Elder Brother Changmi-taek in the countryside, this was a passage from my conversation (lit. drunken exchange, 醉酌) with Cho Sŏbang of Pukaksan Mountain over the phone.
Brother, you must be very busy in the countryside too, greeting the Year of the Rat, right? Are the human fellows there also desperately short of money, fretting like that? Are they short of food, glaring (헤를 흘군흘군하오) resentfully? Since it's the countryside, since they are farming <72> fellows, since they have things saved up (버러 노은 것) from good harvests and bad, it's probably not like Seoul yet, right? They probably keep the room floor warmly heated, eat their fill of even just millet rice or sorghum cakes, and just pass the time tapping out rhythms with their feet, right? But
Brother, Seoul is beyond description (말안이외다). The human fellows in Seoul were originally guys who lived by exploiting (뜻어먹고) the country fellows, but since the country fellows have become as shrewd as can be, how can they be easily exploited? Moreover, the Japanese fellows get there first (압즐너) and snatch it all away (가루채여), hoarding it in their own pockets and not letting it out—so the Korean fellows in Seoul are all wailing that they're dying, boiling with frustration. Even on New Year's morning, countless fellows couldn't eat a single bowl of rice cake soup or drink a cup of medicinal wine; they just clutch their stomachs, shivering violently, stare up at the ceiling, sigh deeply, and hearing us scampering about noisily, they say pitifully (哀然), 'What are those mice eating that makes them jump around so happily?' envying us. Some fellows seem jealous of our playing, or maybe they're venting their frustrations about their own miserable lives on us; eyes red like a cornered viper, glaring resentfully, they even throw worn-out broomsticks (모닥비ㅅ자루) at us.
Brother, to tell you the truth, because the human fellows are living poorly, the impact extends to us as well. This New Year, the human fellows are struggling because they can't eat, but it's difficult for us to eat too. Forget rice or beans; even getting things like leftover rice scraps or bean dregs is as hard as getting a drop of rain during a 7-year drought. Those guys should leave something behind (깃치거나) or throw it away! But like starving dogs, they lick their bowls clean—In the old days, when things were plentiful, they would leave leftovers and throw them away, leave things in the kitchen or pantry without covering them, and no matter how much food was made of rice, if it got even slightly old, they'd throw it down the drain hole (水門구멍), so we could follow behind and pick up and eat as much as we wanted. But now, they say they can't afford to waste even millet scraps or bean dregs stew, so as soon as they get anything, they lick it all clean—Even if they do throw something out, that dreadful creature called the cat (猫) gets there first and eats it all. Yes. And those annoying sparrows also block our path—
Brother, the human fellows are at death's door, but our Sŏ clan society is also in a terrible state. Fortunately, the master of our house works at a government office, so every month when he gets paid, about one sack of rice <73> comes in, so we get a taste from time to time, avoiding their eyes. But other friends are in dire straits. However, we suffered a great loss by developing a taste for the master's rice. As I said before, losing the young mouse footman on New Year's morning—what could be more wretched (齷齪) than that?
Brother, perhaps we should move to the countryside too. Whether we go to the mountains or the fields, in the countryside, would we have to worry about food? Even if we have to dig up arrowroot (測뿌리, 葛根) or pick up acorns (櫟實)—we really can't survive in Seoul.
Elder Brother Changmi-taek, please guide us to some place. A rich man's house. One with a spacious storeroom (庫方) and where the master isn't too fussy. And a house without that damned (배라먹을) vicious creature (惡物), the cat. This is an advance request, brother.
I plan to stay in Seoul this year to observe the human society a bit more, and then return to the countryside next year. I'm definitely counting on you. Yes.

Elder Brother Changmi-taek in the countryside. I saw the doings of the human fellows in our house. How ridiculous it was! Look at this—
The master fellow of the house I live in is a pig-like fellow, utterly disloyal (無義理) and inhumane (沒人情). He works at some government office and receives a monthly salary of over 100 won. His savings alone amount to at least several thousand won. Besides the house we live in, he apparently owns three others. And at the end of this year, he supposedly received a bonus (상여금) of over 200 won. Look at what a fellow like that does.
One day, listening quietly from the ceiling, the master fellow came in from somewhere, took off his overcoat, hung it up, and said to the inside fellow (his wife), "Hey, give me the key, let me get the savings passbook... If I get it, I have to deposit it right away, if I leave it lying around, I'll spend it all." Hearing this, the wife's ears seemed to suddenly open; smiling sweetly, she said, "What? The savings passbook? Oh, right, today was the day for the bonus, wasn't it?... So how much was it? Huh?" and getting up, she clung to the master's sleeve. The master, putting on a poker face, said, "What does it matter how much it was—" and pushed her away sharply. The wife, pretending to be angry (샛착하더니만), then put on an affectionate act again, "You're not going to tell me how much, I suppose, but this time, you absolutely must buy me a ring (半指), okay? Please—" she said, looking at the master with narrowed eyes and giggling 'heh heh'. The master, pretending not to hear, <74> took out a bundle of paper money (紙錢) from his suit pocket, wet his finger with saliva, flipped through it this way and that several times, divided it into three piles, and said, "About 100 won for savings, about 30 won for food, buy a sack of rice, and then—here, you take just 10 won to spend," and tossed a 10-won note to the wife. At first, the wife snatched it quickly, thinking it might be several tens of won, but seeing it was only a single 10-won note, her lips suddenly trembled, "What am I supposed to do with this? Seriously. This little bit won't even cover my nose," she grumbled, throwing it back at the master. At this, the master's eyes grew round, "Forget it then. I only gave it because I did—forget it, I'll spend it all myself," he said, picking it up and putting on a stern face. The wife, thinking it wouldn't do to have even that taken back, started smiling sweetly again, "Oh my, how can you be so curt? Really, putting it away like that? Don't do that, just add one more note—give me two, please? Huh?" and clung to the master's wrist. The master grinned and said, "I gave it back because you said you wouldn't take it, so I put it away. That's it then," and gave back the 10 won. Because the wife clung so persistently (이약스럽게도), perhaps unable to refuse, he gave her another 5-won note.
Brother, watching these two husband-and-wife fellows playing with the bundle of paper money, it was so funny that Uncle Anpang next door says his sides still hurt from laughing so hard. But you know what? A few days (멧츨뒤) after being like that, a relative of his, someone named so-and-so from Sŏdaemun-chŏng, came looking for him, spoke of his wife's illness, and asked to borrow just 10 won. He refused flat out right away. Yes, really. The man said he came trusting him completely and pleaded for him to manage (通해) even just 5 won, but the master snapped irritably, "I told you I don't have it, what more is there to say?" So the man, utterly disheartened, sighed deeply and trudged away. Human fellows are such vicious liars. Disloyalty (無義理) and inhumanity (沒人情) aside...
It was yesterday evening. A student, a relative of the master fellow who had come from the countryside, heard news of his mother's illness and said he had to go down immediately, asking for just 3 won for travel expenses, promising to return it right after coming back. The master grimaced, talking about financial crisis (財政恐荒), debt repayment (債務還償), and not being able to celebrate the New Year properly. After a long pause, he asked the wife, as if borrowing from her, "Hey, if you have any spending money, give me 1 won. Let's give it to him—" The wife muttered something under her breath and tossed 1 won from her pocket to the master. The master took it, fiddled with it for a long time, then said, "Here. It happens this person has 1 won. It's not much, but take it," and tossed it carelessly. At this, the student lowered his head, his lips trembling, thought for a while, then stood up abruptly, "Since you are so hard up (苟且하신데), you use it for yourselves. As for me, I can't go with just that anyway..." and <75> ran off. Sitting quietly in the ceiling, watching those fellows' bundle of deceit (?) masked with thin paper (얄븐 종희로 가면 거준의 뭉치), how itchy my insides felt! "You thief, you scoundrel, what's in that bag— you cunning and vicious bastard," I screamed, but alas—our languages are mutually unintelligible (語不相通), so how could they understand?
Brother, isn't it ridiculous? Those same fellows, just because it's New Year's morning today, cooked rice cake soup, bought alcohol, roasted ribs, prepared all sorts of dishes, and the female (암놈), male (수놈), and even the young ones (색기들) gathered together, ate until they couldn't eat anymore, and now they store the leftovers away (묵켜둠니다) in the cupboard. Yes.
Brother, human fellows are mostly like this. Is the master of this house the only disloyal (無義理) and inhumane (沒人情) one?

Elder Brother Changmi-taek in the countryside, I went around sightseeing—no, to see the inside story of human fellows regarding New Year's Day in Seoul—visiting this house and that, street by street. Going out from the alley gate (굴목문) through the ceiling, passing the outhouse, I crossed the back house wall and was spotted by that fellow called the cat; if we had clashed (닷틋하더면), I would have nearly died. Brother, as you know, I'm naturally a fast runner, so I survived thanks to my speed. Hiding under the floor of the back house, the sun was about halfway up (반겻티나) when a girl from this house named T'ŭksil came in through the gate, shivering, holding about half a doe of rice in a paper bag, collapsed onto the wooden floor, and cried "Mommy," sniffling. How pitiful (可怜) she looked! I immediately thought of the young mouse massacred in our house this morning and cried. Whether it's one massacred after going out for food, or a girl crying after coming back shivering from trying to sell rice for food—isn't the situation the same?
Brother, after crying for a while, I moved on to the neighboring house. This house doesn't seem too badly off (苟且치). The people also don't seem particularly malicious (惡意). Quite a few friends gathered, and there was a decent spread of alcohol and meat.
But brother, no matter how much you observe human fellows, they seem to be made up only of cunning and hypocrisy. Looking closely, the master of this house is also one of those types. They say he somehow gathered unjust (不義) money through something akin to fraud (詐欺), and now, fearing the mouths and ears of various people, he hosted this party on the auspicious occasion of New Year's morning to shut them up. Maybe because of that, the master's smile doesn't seem like a real smile from within, but like an outward smile. The master's wife, knowing nothing, just sits on the floor muttering to herself. Let's say the master is like that because he's that kind of eater (exploiter), but the obsequious (야슥야슥한) behavior of those fellows who came as guests—some of it is too dirty to watch, and some of it is too pathetic to watch. <76>
Bought by a cup of wine, bought by a piece of rice cake, and bought by a few pennies later, they force smiles they don't feel, say words they don't want to say, trying to look good to the master; the sight of them fawning all over him, practically licking his asshole (밋멍), bowing and scraping, is quite a spectacle.
It seems this is how all human fellows make a living. It seems their social structure itself is fundamentally made of cunning and hypocrisy. Wherever you go, whichever fellow you look at, you can't find even half a measure of truth or honesty. As you also experience, brother, we know more about the nighttime affairs, don't we? Their secret time—that time when all sorts of lies, all sorts of mischief, come out freely—isn't that the night? When we sit quietly in the ceiling or a corner of the room and listen, don't all sorts of lies—no, all sorts of ugly things—just pour out of their mouths? Whichever house, whichever fellow, there isn't one who isn't a robber, isn't one who isn't a thief (竊盜), isn't one who isn't an adulterer (奸淫), isn't one who isn't a fraudster (詐欺)—isn't that so? They are truly a mass of sin. Yet these fellows, when they go out in society for others to see, pretend to be decent (점지 안은체), pretend to be honest, pretend to be sincere, pretend to be useful workers (일군인체)—they put on all sorts of airs, don't they? Truly ridiculous.
No matter how resourceful, clever, and cunning those fellows are, they can deceive others, but they can't deceive our mouse society. They say their society also has detectives (偵探), but how can they compare to us? We, indeed, uncover even the intimate talk (醉酌) they have inside the bedding in what they consider their most secret den (秘密窟), the inner room! Brother, being in the countryside, you might not know, but having lived here in Seoul for about 10 years, I can expose the inside stories of those human fellows, without omitting a single one, without difficulty. From now on, please watch as I reveal them bit by bit, one by one. You'll see just how many ugly and sordid states of affairs (醜雜狀) there are.
My New Year's greetings have become too long, this won't do. I've just scribbled this down randomly amidst the busyness, so please forgive me and wait for my next letter. And please tell me the news from the countryside often too. In the words of the Japanese fellows, "Sayonara" (사요나라)— <77>

**Summary**

Writing on New Year's Day 1924 (the Year of the Rat), a Seoul mouse named Sŏ Saengwŏn writes to his cousin Changmi-taek in the countryside. He contrasts the supposed abundance and simpler life in the country with the harsh realities of Seoul. He laments the death of a young mouse due to human carelessness and cruelty, highlighting the difficult conditions for mice in the city where even scraps are scarce because humans themselves are struggling economically. The letter fiercely criticizes human hypocrisy, greed, stinginess, and insincerity, using the example of his own master—a relatively wealthy official who hoards money, is miserly even with family, yet hosts parties—and the fawning guests. The mouse vows to use his unique access (mice hearing everything, especially at night) to expose the "ugly and sordid" secrets of human society.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its allegorical critique of colonial Korean society in Seoul during the 1920s. It reflects the economic hardships faced by Koreans ("서울의 朝鮮놈들은 다-죽는다고 쩔쩔 끌코지내오"), explicitly blaming Japanese control for exacerbating the situation ("日本놈이 압즐너 가루채여 고놈들의 주머니에만 홀가녓코 내놋치를 안으닛가"). The portrayal of human vices like greed, hypocrisy, and lack of empathy, especially among the seemingly well-off, serves as social commentary on perceived moral decay and social inequality under colonial rule. The use of animal narrators might have been a strategy to voice such criticisms indirectly, potentially mitigating censorship risks.
