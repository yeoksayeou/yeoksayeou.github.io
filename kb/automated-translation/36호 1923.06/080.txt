六號通信
開城溭筆 (Casual Notes from Kaesŏng)

Having fallen for a young female student from the neighborhood, he satisfied his desires by bribing her parents with wealth, while also plotting to expel his own wife. An opium dealer supplied opium to the student's father, forcing her to withdraw from school. While planning to hold a secret wedding ceremony on a chosen date, the girl, shocked upon seeing the betrothal gifts (納幣), requested rescue. With the help of concerned relatives, she fled, intending to go to Japan. Discovering this, the man supplied travel expenses to have the father bring her back. When he went to see the girl, he suffered a major humiliation. This lecherous man is said to be Kim Kyŏng-bae (金慶培), known as a so-called industrialist and influential gentleman in Kaesŏng. Even after enduring insults heaped upon him by the tiny female student, he drools foolishly, saying, "She's still good," and has her parents persuade her daily. The parents beat her every day, and her cries echo through the neighborhood; it's said there isn't a spot on her young body without bloodstains. Alas! Cruel and unrighteous parents! What crime has the innocent young girl committed? Do you seek to fill your bellies by giving our child, who resists to the death, to such a wicked man?

Unable to bear this, a playwright investigated the inside story and is currently writing a script (*脚色*). When the manuscript is completed (*脫橋*), it will soon be staged by a certain theater troupe in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). The aforementioned Mr. Kim, learning of this and perhaps trusting only in his wealth, tried to use police power to take away the playwright's freedom to write the script, but was reportedly refused. He then lobbied his close friends but was also refused. Unable to contain his anger, he reportedly declares he will launch some campaign if the play is staged.

How many victims of the entrance examination difficulties were there in Kaesŏng city during this year's new school term? Don't be shocked! 1,300! At a citizens' rally promoting the implementation of the municipal system sometime ago, Son Pong-sang (孫鳳祥) and Kong Sŏng-hak (孔聖學) grandly proclaimed (*豪言*) they would campaign to establish one more public school to rescue elementary students facing entrance difficulties, making the citizens ecstatic and grateful, leading to unanimous approval of the proposal. Are both gentlemen still well (*健在乎*)?

It is said that Holston Girls' High School (好壽敦女子高等普通學校), which has made considerable contributions to Korean women's education, has never held a faculty meeting (*聯員會*). Of course, this might be an exaggeration by the teller, as it's unlikely that not a single faculty meeting has been held since the school's founding. However, it seems plausible that even if meetings were held, their frequency was significantly lower compared to other schools, and these meetings might have been akin to the advisory councils under the current Governor-General's rule. With education conducted under the absolute despotism of a Western principal, the submission of over 20 spineless teachers is quite a sight, but the future of the several hundred Korean girls, including those in the kindergarten attached to the same school (*仝* 好普通學校 仝3部 幼穉*), is truly precarious. Educators! Surely you shouldn't only think of your livelihood!

The movement to encourage local products (*物産獎勵*) once swept the entire nation (*全鮮*). In Kaesŏng too, whether voluntarily or passively, an association was organized with 600 members and an audience of over 1,000, electing over 30 executive committee members. Afterwards, they invited some famous orator from Kaesŏng for a grand propaganda lecture, greatly awakening an audience of nearly 300. Since then, the very existence of the association has become vague. Even the first practical requirement, wearing cotton ramie overcoats (*木周衣*), is met with such indifference that you'd be lucky to encounter even one person wearing one per day.

Some argue that people will naturally wear ramie (*苧布*) clothing in the summer, following the season and natural trends. However, looking at recent clothing styles, the new "natural trend" involves wearing ramie overcoats lined with either *samp'alchu* silk (*三八紬*) or *nobangju* silk (*老芳紬*), following Seoul styles – something unseen last year. Even *kisaeng*, who wore native cotton skirts for a while, would be better off wearing ramie (*苧*) skirts, but what is gradually being encouraged and spreading are Japanese *habutae* silk (*羽二重*) skirts, also unseen last year. What dreadful social customs! How detestable are the executives of this association, who boast of initiating something for utilitarian gain, use committee membership as a title (*肩書*), do no actual work, and try to evade responsibility with excuses<49> and charm! Think a bit more!

On May 6th, the Koryŏ Youth Association (高麗靑年會) hosted the Kaesŏng People's Athletic Meet. Since it was called a County People's Athletic Meet, one expected many athletes representing the 16 townships (*面*) to participate. However, though called "County People," in reality, it was no different from the annual City People's Athletic Meet, with all participants being city residents. If this too is called the customary exaggeration, it's understandable, but one thing deserving praise is that all prizes were Korean products. Instead of the commonly used Western handkerchiefs, they used patterned wooden bowls (*斑木*), and for lower prizes, iron spoons, braziers, and basins. This stands in welcome contrast to the noisy but inactive Local Products Encouragement Association.

Speaking of the Youth Association, when newspapers were filled with articles about encouraging local products, perhaps the Youth Association had a similar thought, or maybe they just followed suit without knowing better. They distributed a document with a grand statement of purpose to members, and below it, under "Conditions for Implementation," besides the three conditions of the Local Products Encouragement Association, they added, "Specifically, absolutely prohibit foreign food." This seems motivated by alarm at the 140,000 won annual expenditure at restaurants. However, some members criticize, saying, "Telling members to do this while the executives themselves eat until they pass out at Chinese restaurants for welcome or farewell parties, or even personally – isn't that subversive?" Is this true or not? If it were decided by the members' resolution, it might be understandable, but since the order was issued to members based on a meeting of a few executives, if such talk is true, they cannot avoid taking responsibility.

Recently, someone claiming to be a representative of the "Munbuk Promotion Society" (文北促進社) came to Kaesŏng. When asked what it was about, they said they came to recruit workers for "high-daily-income, completely flawless home-based work" (*內職工*), claiming the head office is in Tokyo (東京). Hearing the word "culture" (*文化*) is sickening enough, but calling the recruitment of home-based workers to make *maki-e* lacquerware (*蒔繒*) or Japanese clogs (*下駄*) "cultural promotion" is truly side-splitting. Moreover, even if there were profit, it's said that quite a few people in Kaesŏng have done this "high-daily-income, completely flawless home-based work" only to end up selling their furniture to repay debts. Therefore, doing such work has come to be called "something that will ruin the household." In short, if the Japanese aim to ruin Koreans by promoting cultural destruction, then their company name is indeed an honest trade name.

On May 10th, the founding meeting for the local chapter of the People's Association (民大地方部發起會) was held at the Koryŏ Youth Association Hall. Originally, invitations were sent for a meeting on the 8th, but due to rain on the 8th and 9th, it was unavoidably held on the 10th. Yet, some who received invitations complained that it was held without postponement notice. Those who didn't attend because of rain and then argue about the lack of postponement notice, and those "businessmen" made of straw who assume it will automatically be postponed due to rain – how could this be such a perfectly matched meeting of blank paper saints (*白紙迅死*)?

In the nostril-sized, shabby Japanese quarter (*日本人町*), a large sheet of Western paper announced a *naniwabushi* (*浪花節*) performance by some star of the *naniwabushi* world (*浪曲界*). The sponsors listed were, first and foremost, the Kaesŏng Police Station (開城警察署), followed by the Kaesŏng Reservists' Association (開城在鄕軍人會). The previous police chief used to issue dispersal orders for lecture meetings lasting less than an hour and considered the founding of a Boys' Association extremely dangerous. Now, the new chief, perhaps attempting an unprecedented (*破天荒*) "popularization of the police," sponsors a *naniwabushi* performance. What a lovely contrast.

After encountering the County Magistrate (*郡守*) on paper four or five times, a real magistrate finally arrived after several months. He succeeds the previous magistrate who received a silver medal for merit from a "Spirit-Worshipper (*祟神人*) Training School," dismissed the devotions of spirit-worshippers throughout Korea (*全鮮*) as no different from a student athletic meet, and responded confrontationally to a citizen representative's question with "What are you going to do if I permitted it?" What kind of sorcery will the newly appointed magistrate employ in his rampant administration? News is still vague. Perhaps he is employing forceful hypnotic commands?

The 3rd Kaesŏng Tennis (*庭球*) Tournament concluded amidst great success, but Han Chin-yŏng (韓鎭永) of Songdo High (松高), winner of the 1st and 2nd tournaments, was reportedly defeated by Kim Myŏng-hwan (金明桓) and Son Hŭng-yun (孫興允) of Hwimun (徽文). It's said people are even more regretful (*憒*) because they are all from Kaesŏng, and especially since Kim Myŏng-hwan was at Songdo High last year and competed alongside Han, but transferred to Hwimun this time.<50> It's anticipated that the 3rd Tennis Tournament to be held in Kyŏngsŏng this autumn will be a revenge match between these two players. Considering this, perhaps losing the championship banner permanently might actually be beneficial for Songdo High?

Since last year, tennis fever has boiled over, and it's being widely encouraged among young children too. Last year alone, 5 or 6 "Wooden Board Tennis Tournaments" (*木板庭球大會*), a unique Kaesŏng championship banner competition, were held. Crowds of over 1,000 gathered each time, creating a great spectacle. But why "Wooden Board Tennis"? It refers to the small rackets that young children make themselves using wooden boards found at home. Rather than dressing children in silk clothes (*綢紗衣*) and making them frail, how about buying them a single racket and a single ball, bringing joy and happiness to the child while simultaneously encouraging their physical education?

April 8th is an annual commemorative day for the Kaesŏng merchant community. Originally, it celebrated Buddha's Birthday, but now that religious connotation is completely gone. Instead, shops and streets are decorated, various attractions are set up, and extensive advertising draws people from various regions to sell a wide variety of goods. It's a day of play and also a day of selling (*파日*). It might be similar in nature to the "Big Sale" (*大賣出*) days often seen among Japanese merchants. Anyway, the bustle on this day is truly magnificent. Everyone knows Kaesŏng is a special city, and it's also common knowledge that it's difficult for Japanese people to make a living there. Since last year, April 8th is no longer celebrated only by Koreans. In the Japanese quarter too, the streets are decorated with Japanese national flags and flags of all nations, and shop fronts are adorned with flower lines and lanterns to observe the day. Of course, this is partly to pursue their own profits, but it also implies that living within Kaesŏng requires living like Kaesŏng people – in other words, assimilating. The recent sight of four Western female musicians, performing at a concert hosted by Songdo High students and the Youth Association, all wearing Korean clothes (*朝鮮服*), including Korean socks (*버선*) and shoes (*신*), should not be seen merely as comical.

晋州漫錄 (Casual Records from Chinju)

Who doesn't know about the founding celebration of the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa (衡平社)? The approximately 400,000 brothers scattered throughout our entire Korea (*全朝鮮*), known as *Paekchŏng* (白丁), have been unjustly and unreasonably subjected to contempt (*賤待*) and abuse (*虐待*) in this society. Their hearts were always wounded, their fists always trembling. Among their grievances, the greatest indignation stemmed from educational discrimination. "When? Which day?" they wondered, eagerly awaiting the arrival of the right time—no, anticipating changes in the situation. Amidst this, suddenly, a loud voice was heard. This was not a voice ringing out from one part at one time, but a great, great voice covering the East and West. It was the voice of humanitarianism and justice (*人道正義*), abolition of human discrimination (*人類差別撤廢*), women's liberation (*婦女解放*), labor movements (*勞動運動*), social revolution (*社會革命*), and the like. Among these, what struck their eardrums most forcefully was "no distinction between noble and base occupations, equality of human rights." From this moment, their chests surged, and their fists clenched. The intensity grew increasingly strong and fervent. Noticing this trend early on, Kang Sang-ho (姜相鎬), Sin Hyŏn-su (申鉉壽), Ch'ŏn Sŏk-ku (千錫九), and several other concerned individuals initiated the movement. Starting from Chinju (晋州), they issued a respectful appeal (*謹告書*) to various counties (*郡*) in South Kyŏngsang (*慶南*), and later to all 13 provinces. In response, the 400,000 individuals rose up in support without a single exception. Thus, the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa was organized. They even dispatched representatives to neighboring counties for extensive propaganda, achieving unexpectedly good results. Consequently, a committee meeting was held, and the founding date was set for May 13th at 1:00 PM. Notices were sent out everywhere, and people from distant places began gathering from the evening of the 11th. On the 12th and 13th, they continued to flock like clouds gathering on a mountain. From 11:30 AM, three automobiles loaded with prospectuses (*趣旨書*) drove around the city for about an hour and a half, conducting extensive publicity. At the scheduled time, the Chinju Theater (*晋州座*) was filled to capacity on all three levels (upper, middle, lower). Under the chairmanship of Mr. Kang Sang-ho, the meeting opened, and the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa founding celebration ceremony was held. Among the guests, Mr. Kang Tal-yŏng (姜達永) and Mr. Yi Chin-u (李珍雨) delivered passionate congratulatory addresses. This ceremony, proceeding in a tense (*緊張*) yet again tense atmosphere, concluded around 5:00 PM. Long live the Korean Hyŏngp'yŏng Movement! <51>

**Summary**

This article from June 1923 presents news reports, primarily focusing on Kaesŏng and Chinju. The Kaesŏng section details local gossip like a scandal involving a wealthy man and a schoolgirl, critiques ineffective social movements (local products encouragement), criticizes school administration (Holston Girls' High) and youth group hypocrisy (Koryŏ Youth Association), comments on local events like athletic meets and commercial festivals, and observes the interactions and assimilation pressures between Koreans and Japanese residents. The Chinju section reports dramatically on the founding ceremony of the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa, a pivotal organization formed by the marginalized *Paekchŏng* community to fight centuries of discrimination, spurred by global ideals of equality and human rights.

For historians, the article offers valuable insights into early colonial Korean society. It showcases local social dynamics, critiques of both colonial structures (police influence) and Korean initiatives/leadership failures, the tangible impact of global ideologies on social movements like the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa, observations on Japanese settlement and cultural interaction/assimilation in a major Korean city, and the specific grievances (like education discrimination) fueling social change movements. The detailed account of the Hyŏngp'yŏngsa's founding is particularly significant.
