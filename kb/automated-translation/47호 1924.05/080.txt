Holding the Dying Farming Village, From the Farming Village Pilgrimage Diary
						Pyŏkto Yi Sŏng-hwan

Pitiful! The Conversation of Two Women
It was a clearly bright Tuesday. I tightened the straps on my straw sandals (초신), grasped a thick short cane (短杖) in my right hand, tucked a bulging book bundle (冊褓) under my left arm, and stepped out of the O Office gate. That is, I was setting out on a business trip for the purpose of a farming village lecture tour (農村巡講).

My feet were headed towards the Hadamyŏn office in Tanch'ŏn County. On the way, the conversation of two women walking not two paces ahead of me was worth listening to.

Woman A: How much did you get at that house?
Woman B: I took two *toe* (되 - a unit of measure) and at first, they offered about four *ton*. So, thinking I might get a bit more, I didn't sell it right away. But then the sun was setting quickly, and I had to come home, so I ended up selling it at a loss for three *ton* and five *pun*.
Woman A: Oh, look at that! How good it would have been if you sold it at first. That's really... So, how much did you buy that fish for?
Woman B: I bought two for five *ton* five *pun*, and another two for six *ton*.
Woman A: You bought them really cheap. I took one *toe* of rice, got one *nyang* for it, bought three pollacks (明太), and had only half a *ton* left. So, I couldn't even buy the *paengnoji* (백로지 - type of paper?) the kids asked for. I had to buy the pollacks because we need them for the ancestral rites (祭祀), what could I do?
Woman B: That's what I'm saying. Why is it that the price of grain (穀栗) never seems to go up, but only the things we need to buy are expensive? It's truly dumbfounding. <55>

The exchange (酬酌) between the two women seems very ordinary. However, here lies the true economic insecurity of the farmers. The pitiful reality of the people who live by selling their grain can be found in these few words of dialogue. From spring to summer, summer to autumn, autumn to winter, they shed blood, wring out sweat, break their bones, and work so hard that their flesh wears away, yet the grain they produce doesn't bring money into their hands while sitting still. So, they carry it on their backs and heads, travel 20 or 30 *ri* to the marketplace, get squeezed by the cunning tactics of merchants and industrialists going up and down, and finally sell it at a cheap price (헐價) that isn't even its proper value. And the paltry sum of money (돈부스럭이) barely grasped is immediately snatched away by the owners of various other goods displayed prominently as soon as it enters the farmer's hands. Thus, the farmer has no money. They produce the raw materials (原料) that become money. But their products have no value. Therefore, the agricultural producer has no money. Consequently, the farmer becomes extremely poor (極貧者). A debtor (債務者). Eventually, the farming village dies. I could not help but shed tears at her words, "It's truly dumbfounding." Ah – I weep, holding the dying farming village. How can we secure economic stability for the farmer? Is there truly no way to guarantee the farmer's livelihood? Ah – the dying farming village cannot be left as it is. Ah – people! People of Chosŏn!

The Misfortune of Farmers Thirsty for Money, Knowing One Thing But Not the Next
My steps reached the bridge over the river called Namdaech'ŏn. One plank of the bridge had collapsed. An ox carrying wood had plunged its forelegs into the hole of the broken bridge. The ox knelt on its front legs, its large eyes wide as if in pain, breathing heavily—kee-kee. The person leading the ox shouts, "Eo-ra- eoriya-ha eoriya-ha!" They cry out in chorus. "This darn bundle of wood (나무단) is all breaking apart! Ira ira eoriya-ha eoriya-ha," they say, sometimes hitting the ox, sometimes pulling it. I couldn't just stand by and watch (垂手傍觀). "Yōshi (よし - Okay!)" I said and rushed over. We unloaded the wood bundle and lifted the ox's neck (멱) to help it stand up. The man didn't even bother to check if the ox was injured or not; instead, lamenting the broken wood bundle, he quickly bursts into complaint, "Hmm – that's just great! A fellow with bad luck (運數 窮한 놈) can't do anything right. It's just straight loss, even by one *pun*! What am I going to do!" I <56> felt more pity for him than blame for his foolishness in driving the ox into such a dangerous spot (危險處) in the first place. Why? Because here too, the sorrow (悲哀) of the farmer permeates deeply. That is, he forcefully dragged the ox through this difficult place to achieve his goal of getting to the market even a minute or a second faster than others, selling well before them, and getting even one *pun* more money. That's why the fact that the wood bundle was broken, hindering his original objective, was more pitiful to him than the large ox being injured. His face, gaunt from worry about money, crumpled in lament over the broken wood bundle as if his own flesh and bones were breaking—this situation forces tears from the child of any conscious person. The farmer, thirsty for *chŏn* (錢) and hungry for *wŏn* (圓), is concerned only with immediate gain or loss when it comes to money, regardless of future or past consequences. That is, when it comes to profit (利), they know the one thing but not the next. Should I call such a farmer unfortunate (不祥) or pitiful (可憐)? Why has the farmer, who works day and night, become so starved for money?! Ah – the more I think, the more tears come. I looked at him once more. His shaggy hair was wildly dishevelled under a small, pushed-down *mokch'ulmo* (目出帽 - a type of hat), his face dark and rough as if frostbitten and eroded by the cold and wind, his torn sock bottoms revealing cracked heels and frozen toes sticking out. And on his body, he wore thin, worn-out cotton clothes, stained and crumpled haphazardly. Seeing his attire and his trembling, agitated state, I couldn't stop the stinging, aching tears from blurring my vision once again. Ah – the farmers of Chosŏn grow poorer day by day. Like the leaves of trees on a deep autumn mountain falling away, only a desolate, lonely, and dangerous fate approaches. Ah – the farming villages of Chosŏn are dying. I weep, holding the dying farming village. How can we give them stability in life? By what method can we ensure their well-being (健在)? Ah – how unfortunate! The plight of the farmer thirsty for money!!

Why So Many Pale Faces? What Will Become of the Future of the Second Generation?
Moving my tearful steps onward, I arrived at Hadamyŏn. <57> From 2 PM to 4 PM, the Sericulture Lecture (蚕業講話) was held in the classroom of Kwangdong School. The audience numbered over 200. When I stood on the platform (壇), the mountain valley wind, sharp enough to pierce flesh, rushed in through the broken glass windows (硝子窓), making my upper and lower teeth chatter. Inside the room, a small stove (난로), about the size of a small water jar, was placed. A fire was lit, but the smoke that should have gone outside filled the entire room, making it impossible for people to open their eyes. Nevertheless, I opened my mouth. Starting with the fact, proven by statistics, that our farming villages are in a state of ruin, the topic shifted to the improvement of the agricultural economy. Then it shifted again to the necessity of side businesses (副業) and points to consider in their selection, citing the advantageous conditions of sericulture. I spoke of hoping for the establishment of a "Quadrilateral Alliance" (四角形同盟) by adequately equipping the four major elements for sericulture development—mulberry leaves (桑葉), silkworm eggs (蚕種), rearing rooms and equipment (種蚕室 蚕具), and techniques (技術)—so that not even one is lacking, and with that, the curtain closed on the lecture meeting. During this time, what was the fact that made me lose heart and feel despair as I looked at the crowd...? It was seeing that the faces (낫치) of so many people were excessively pale (蒼白). Ah – why are there so many pale faces? Among the over 200 men and women in the audience, I couldn't find a single person whose complexion (顔色) glowed with healthy vitality (健狀) or whose posture sitting on the bench overflowed with energy. Almost all showed signs (徵候) of malnutrition (營養不良) in their complexions and symptoms (證勢) of languor and weakness (萎靡衰弱) in their physique. Ah – what is to be done about this in the future? There can be no greater problem than this. It is truly a great problem. If there is anything tear-worthy for the future of the nation, it cannot even be discussed without addressing this issue. Behold! Ponder (詛嚼) once more the golden saying (金言), "A sound mind dwells only in a sound body (건전한 정신은 건전한 신체에만 宿한다)"! It is indeed true. Without a healthy body, one cannot possess a virtuous spirit even for a moment. Therefore, a country or society with unhealthy citizens or people is unfortunate, as it cannot accomplish great undertakings (大事業) that require great determination (大英斷), great effort (大努力), great contribution (大貢獻), great sacrifice (大犧牲), great struggle (大奮鬪), great patience (大忍耐), and great courage (大勇敢).
Our Chosŏn, in its unique situation, needs extraordinary vitality (精力) for the great tasks of demolition and construction that lie ahead (압혜). Alas! In Chosŏn today, this vitality is drying up day by day due to poor physical health. How can we not lament (痛嘆) this for the nation's hundred-year plan (百年大計)? That is not all. When motherhood (母性), continuing irregular and intemperate meals that ignore <58> the fixed laws of nutritional value, and fatherhood (父性), unable to correct unclean and unhygienic daily lives excluding the necessary conditions of natural science, unite, the most crucial reproductive cells (生殖細胞) and nutritional cells (營養細胞) among the combined elements of the two sexes will be incomplete and diseased. Thus, they will inevitably pass down unhealthy constitutions and imperfect vitality to the second generation. What is to be done about this too! Seeing the farmers of Chosŏn, who seem destined to wither and collapse like fading forsythia blossoms, I cannot bear the chilling trepidation (戰慄) that makes me cry out, "Ah – what should be done about this?" while painful tears well up in the corners of my eyes. How can we make their faces (낫헤) flow with lustrous oil (潤氣) and make red blood boil and overflow in their veins? Ah – farmers of Chosŏn! People of Chosŏn, standing in a unique position with the heavy burden of great demolition and great construction slung over both shoulders (억게)! I weep with you, gentlemen (諸君), as if to die together. No. I weep to live together. I sob and weep. I cry my heart out. I weep while walking. I weep while sitting. I weep while lying down. Gentlemen! From now on, let's stop making offerings (佛供) at famous mountains and great places. Let's stop catching cows and pigs for shamanistic rituals (굿). Let's stop relying on preventive measures and restorative medicines (補藥). Instead, let's strive to learn the laws of nutritional value and the hygienic conditions of natural science, which are essential for human life. Thus, by planning for physical health and the perfection of vitality (精力), you must ensure that you do not become disabled yourselves, and at the same time, prevent your descendants (슞�손 - likely 후손/huson) from becoming ill. Farmers of Chosŏn! People of Chosŏn!
Descending from the platform and leaving the school gate, I headed towards the Myŏn office. As the sun that had set behind the western mountain seemed to be taking its final look at your world today, perched on the ridge like a lamp, scattering its reddish light, I threw myself into the Myŏn's night duty room (面宿直室). Though called a night duty room, it was a single *ondol* room, unclean as if not cleaned at all. The door frame didn't fit properly, and there were cracks in the plastered walls.

Who Knew the Sorrows of the Myŏn Headman?
I came face to face (마조안께) with the Myŏn Headman (面長), Mr. Kim Ch'ung-yong (金忠鎔). I asked,
"How busy you must be (얼마나 밧부심니까)." The headman replied,
"It's the same for both of us. It's not that it isn't, but there are just too many things they tell us to collect, so it's really impossible to manage." I continued,
"But the burden (부닥김이) on the farmers must be very heavy (重할 터이지오)." <59> The headman, seemingly agreeing (共鳴), said,
"Of course. Just listen. Land tax (地稅), land tax surcharge (地稅附加), additional tax (附加稅), household tax (戶稅), household tax surcharge (戶稅附加), household levy (戶別割), Agricultural Association fee (農會費), Livestock Industry Cooperative fee (畜産組合費), Hamnam Educational Promotion Society fee (咸南育英會費), Academic Lecture Institute fee (學術講習所費), private school fees (私立學校費), Tokyo Earthquake Relief Fund donation (東京震災義損金), besides that, mulberry seedling costs (桑苗代金), silkworm equipment costs (蚕具代), etc. Aren't there so many? There are even more if you count them all. Time just flies by collecting all these." I said,
"It must be even more difficult because the farmers don't pay readily."
"Pay? They need to have money. Well, among them, there might occasionally be some who have money but don't pay, but there are really many people who genuinely can't afford it. Generally, in a *myŏn* like ours, soybeans need to do well, but although last year is called a bountiful year, soybeans were very lacking. Not only that, but people who couldn't get food during the insect plague (虫災) in the Im Sul year (壬戌年 - 1922) lived by pre-selling their soybeans (콩豫賣) from the spring to summer of the Kye Hae year (癸亥年 - 1923). So now, they've sold off what's worth 14 or 15 *wŏn* per *sŏm* (섬 - unit of volume) for only 7 or 8 *wŏn*. What could have become of them? It's really beyond words," he said, sighing.
"That's truly a dumbfounding situation. Selling goods currently worth 15 *wŏn* for only 7 *wŏn* because they lacked money and had to pre-sell... Heo heo, that's truly beyond words. Even if they received the proper price, today's farm households struggle to survive. How tragic and miserable (悲絶慘絶) is the situation where they are forced to sell their products for less than half the market price (時價). Whenever I hear such stories, I keenly feel that this unfortunate event (不祥事) occurs because we lack specialized agricultural banks (農業特種銀行) and agricultural warehouses (農業倉庫) in Chosŏn. If there were banks or warehouses convenient for our farmers to use, the system of distress selling (穫賣) would disappear. Farm household financing (金融) could be smoothly managed using warehouse receipts (倉庫券) or something like promissory notes (王形 - likely 어음/ŏŭm, promissory note) based on harvest forecasts, and incidents like sacrificing goods at half price would absolutely not occur, wouldn't they?" I remarked. The headman let out another long sigh—whew—and said,
"One way or another, 'Life comes after being pushed into a deadly situation (陷之死地以後에 生).' Only after falling deep into an economic panic (經濟恐慌) will the farmers perhaps open their eyes a bit. So, it might be fitting to interpret today's economic panic as a warning bell (警鍾) for the farmers who are calmly dreaming through it. But in the midst of this, <60> the pitiful group caught in the crossfire (찟비탈이우고 - torn on a steep slope?), having their insides thoroughly tormented, are actually those of us working at the *myŏn* office. With pressure from above to collect (바다드리랴) and resistance from below claiming inability to pay (업다 못 내겟다), people can't live like this. In this situation, what son of a dog (개아들놈) would want to be a *myŏn* headman?" he said. Hearing this, I lost the courage to ask further. I merely thought to myself, "Who ever knew that even the esteemed headman has his sorrows?" sympathizing with him inwardly. The headman continued,
"And the pitiful thing is how farm households misuse (박구어) money. Mr. Yi, you probably know this better than I do. Indeed, during events like weddings and funerals (冠婚葬祭), they really spend lavishly (푹푹). Setting up extravagant feasts with whatever resources they have (밋천), inviting people to come and go—aren't they often doing things that only the truly wealthy (豪富者) could afford? However, when it comes to bearing small educational expenses or investing a small amount in productive enterprises that are clearly known to yield sufficient profit, they genuinely lack the money to put forward and just hem and haw. Seeing this, the regrettable thought that our farm households are poor because they misuse money arises without fail." I felt immense agreement (共鳴) with these words. So I said,
"That's right. That statement is correct. It's clear that the cause of debt in our Chosŏn farm households is largely due to these 'important events' (大事) or ostentatious displays (떠대가리). For agricultural production, which cannot achieve sudden wealth (一攫千金), to squander a large amount of money all at once (一朝에 多大한 金錢을 散盡함) is like the foolish act of one who brings about their own declaration of bankruptcy (破産宣告). Yet, in our Chosŏn, such bad deeds are considered propriety (禮), and anyone who doesn't do such things is regarded as if they haven't fulfilled their human duty. Is there any greater contradiction (矛盾)?" The headman, as if resolved, said,
"Nonsense. We must stop inviting guests (請賓) for funerals, ancestral rites, weddings, and coming-of-age ceremonies (喪葬祭禮와 冠婚嫁娶), and make it shameful for people to prepare many luxurious delicacies (萬般需珍)."
"That's right. Therefore, Headman, please study this point well <61> and bring down an iron hammer (쇠방망이) on the heads of these stubborn (頑固) elders steeped in this darned empty formality (虛禮), striving resolutely to ensure such empty formalities are not revered," I urged. Just then, the dinner table was brought in. The headman said, "Let's meet again," and left.
Farmers of Chosŏn! People of Chosŏn, thoroughly soaked in empty formality (虛禮) and superstition (迷信)! Endure the temporary shame (羞恥). What good does it do to put on airs like someone wealthy when you have nothing? In just a few days, the iron chain (쇠사슬) of debt will be fastened around your necks! Isn't it true that we have lived in such unbecoming falsehood (虛僞), pretending to know when we didn't, pretending to have when we didn't, leading us to today's state of ruin? Yes. Yes. We are ruined. Isn't today the day we have lost almost all the land handed down by our ancestors (祖先)? Ah – the farming village is ruined. Chosŏn is ruined. What will be the final path (末路) of the people in white clothes? My heart aches. My throat tightens. Ah – I weep, holding the dying farming village. I weep as if to die together. No. I weep to live together. Farmers! People! Do you know or not that if we hadn't misused money, education would not have lagged as it does today, and industry would not have failed to flourish as it does today? Please have more enlightenment (깨다름).
After dinner (食後), two or three nearby farmers came, and there was a discussion about side businesses (副業). Around 10 PM, I spent two or three hours coming and going at a tavern (酒肆) with several Myŏn clerks (面書記). When the twenty-something-day-old moon, looking as if it had left half of itself somewhere, rose pale and white like a bow in the eastern sky, illuminating the myriad things (森羅萬象) of the world that had been dark until then, our party returned to the Myŏn night duty room, watching the frost and moonlight compete and sparkle on the toad-back-like roofs (집웅) of the village houses.
Ah – today's pilgrimage diary (巡禮記), filled with so much resentment (恨) and tears, written while holding the dying farming village just as it is, without even wiping away the traces of tears, I write and write, then stop. Let's see what tomorrow brings! (62)

**Summary**

The author, Yi Sŏng-hwan, recounts a journey through a rural Korean area (Tanch'ŏn-gun Hadamyŏn) in 1924. He observes the deep poverty and despair of farmers: they are forced to sell grain at exploitatively low prices, struggle to afford necessities, and suffer from poor health likely due to malnutrition. He notes the burden of numerous taxes and fees imposed on them and laments the societal pressure forcing farmers into debt through expensive traditional ceremonies (weddings, funerals). Witnessing pale faces, collapsing infrastructure, and economic desperation, Yi passionately decries the "dying farming village," connecting its plight to the future of the Korean nation (Chosŏn) and urging farmers to abandon costly traditions and superstitions in favor of education, hygiene, and economic rationality for survival and national strength.

From a historical perspective, this article is valuable for its firsthand account of rural conditions in colonial Korea during the 1920s. It vividly details the economic mechanisms disadvantaging farmers (low grain prices vs. high commodity costs, pre-selling crops at a loss), lists specific taxes and levies burdening the populace (including funds for Japanese causes like the Tokyo Earthquake Relief), highlights public health issues (malnutrition, poor sanitation), and critiques traditional social practices (虚禮 - empty formalities) as contributors to poverty, offering a contemporary critique of both colonial economic structures and internal societal problems.

어느 맑게 개인 火曜日이다. 나는 초신에 신들매를 틀하고 올흔손에 굵직한 短杖을 휘여 집고 왼손에 배부른 冊褓를 불룩하게 끼고 O廳門을 나섯다. 즉 農村巡講의 용무를 띄고 출장을 떠남이엿섯다.

乙. 글세 말이우다. 어째서 穀栗갑슨 올나갈 줄을 모르고 우리가 살 것만 빗싼지요? 참말 기  가 맥혀요.＜55＞

두 婦人의 酬酌은 매우 평범한 것 갓다. 그러나 여기에 참된 농민의 경제적 불안이 드러나 잇다. 낫알을 팔아먹고 사는 백성의 가련한 사실을 이 몃 마듸 대화에서 차저볼 수가 잇다. 봄부터 여름, 여름으로 가을, 가슬로 겨울까지 피를 흘이고 땀을 짜내고 빼를 부쉬며 살이 모조라 떠러지도록 힘드려 지어노흔 곡물을 오히려 안저서는 돈이 손에 드러오지를 아니 함으로 등에 지고 머리에 이고하야 20리나 30리 되는 市街地를 차저 가서 商工業者들의 오르락내리락하는 奸巧한 수단에 찌주물리고야 비롯오 제갑도 못되는 헐價에 팔게 된다. 그리하야 겨우 어더 쥐인 돈부스럭이는 농민의 손으로 드러오자마자 크다만하게 벌이고 잇든 다른 상품의 주인들이 죄-다 빼앗사 가고야 만다. 이리하야 농민에게는 돈이 업다. 돈될 原料를 생산은 한다. 그러나 그 생산품이 갑이 업다. 때문에 농업생산자에게는 돈이 업다. 따라 농민은 極貧者가 된다. 債務者가 된다. 드듸여 농촌은 죽어간다. 나는 그네가 「참말 기가 맥혀요」 하는 말에는 눈물이 안나지 못하엿다. 아- 나는 죽어가는 농촌을 안고서 운다. 어떠케 하면 농민에게 경제적 안전을 엇게 할가? 과연 농민의 생활을 보장해 줄 도리는 업느냐? 아- 죽어가는 농촌을 그대로 둘 수는 업다. 아- 사람들아 朝鮮의 사람들아!

하나를 알고 둘부터 모르는 돈에 목마른 農民의 不祥
나의 발자국은 南大川이라는 江다리에 이르럿다. 다리 한판이 문허젓다. 나무실은 소 하나이 압다리를 허물어진 다리구멍에 빠트려너헛다. 소는 압발을 꿀고 압흔듯이 큰 눈을 부르뜨고 끼-끼- 큰 숨만 쉰다. 소 모든 사람은 어-라- 어리야-하 어리야-하 하고 아우성을 친다. 「이런 놈 나무단이 다 부스러지는구나. 이라 이라 어리야하 어리야하」하면서 소를 때려도 보고 이끌어도 본다. 나도 垂手傍觀할 수는 업게 되얏다. ヨ-シ 하고 닥아들엇다. 나무단을 부리우고 소멱을 처들어 이르켜 주엇다. 그 사람은 소가 傷햇는지 안傷햇는지는 아라보랴고도 아니하고 나무단이 부스러진 것을 아까워하며 「음- 그것 참! 원 運數 窮한 놈은 할 수가 업서. 돈 한푼이라도 곳장 손해뿐이니 어쩐담!」하야 못내 한탄을 速發한다. 나는 ＜56＞ 그네가 당초에 그런 危險處에다가 소를 모라너흔 그 미련함을 責하는 것보다 차라리 그네를 不祥타고 생각하는 감정이 소사오름을 견대지 못하얏다. 웨-? 여기에도 농민의 悲哀는 사모처 잇슴으로써 그러하다. 즉 一分一刻이라도 속히 시장에 가서 남들보다 먼저 잘 팔아 한푼 돈이라도 더 밧자는 그 목적을 達하기 위해서 이Ÿ° 곳으로 소를 뻑뻑 끌고 온 것이다. 그러니까 큰 소가 傷한 것보다도 현재에 나무단이 부서저서 본래의 목적을 達치 못할 것이 더 애처럽어 함이엿다. 나무단이 부스러지는 것을 자기의 살이나 뻬가 부서지는 것과 가티 압하하는 그네의 그 돈 때문에 말나붓흔 얼굴을 찌푸리고 못내 한탄하는 情況이 의식 잇는 사람의 자식으로는 눈물을 내지 아니치 못하게 한다. 錢돈에 목말나하고 圓돈에 배곱하하는 농민이 「돈이라면 뒷일과 압일이야 어떠케 되얏든 目前의 利害에만 汲汲하는도다. 즉 늏�이라면 그 하나를 알지 둘부터도 알지 못하는 농민을 不祥타 하랴 可憐타 하랴. 밤낫을 모르고 움즉이는 농민에게 어찌하야 이러케까지 돈 배가 골아젓느냐?! 아- 생각을 할사록 눈물이 난다. 나는 또 한번 그이를 첫다보앗다. 텁수룩한 머리카락이 왜자자하게 흐터진 머리에 조고마한 目出帽을 눌너 쓰고 얼굴은 치위에 바람에 凍傷되고 蝕傷된 것처럼 식컴헛고 험상스럽게 되얏고 구멍이 뚤어지고 바닥 모조리 떠러진 헌 버선으로는 터진 발 뒤촉과 언 발고락이 내미러 잇다. 그러고 몸에는 때가 뚝뚝 떠는 헐고 열분 미명옷을 규칙업시 꾸개여진대로 휘덥허 노앗다. 나는 그이의 몸차림과 그 떨고 던비는 광경을 보고는 또 한번 쓰리고 아린 눈물이 압흘 가리움을 참지 못하엿다. 아- 朝鮮의 농민은 날마다 더 못사라간다. 떠러저 가는 深秋空山의 나무입 모양으로 簫條落寞한 위험한 운명이 다 닥치여 올 뿐이다. 아- 朝鮮의 농촌은 죽어 간다. 나는 죽어 가는 농촌을 안고서 운다. 어찌하면 저네에게 생활의 안정을 줄 수 잇슬가? 무슨 방법으로 저네의 健在를 圖할 수 잇슬가? 아- 不祥도 하고나. 돈에 목이 마른 농민의 처지여!!

蒼白한 얼굴들이 웨 이리 만흐냐 第二民族의 將來는 어찌나 될고
