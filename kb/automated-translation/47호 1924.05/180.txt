Publication Date: 1924년 05월 01일
Article Title: 아츰비 (Morning Rain)
Author: 金億 譯 (Kim Ŏk, Translator)
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

아츰비
						金億 譯

밤은 밝아오며
별들은 멀어가서
遊女들은 愛人과 함끠
한가지로 집에 들아왓을,
이러한 때에 나는 다만 혼자서
아츰비를 가득히 마즈며,
가이 업시 모래 우 노래를 쓰노라.

나무 입에는 빗나는 비방울이 맷첫고
小路를 에도아 흘으는 냇물은
落葉과 모래알을 몰아 흘으며,
땅 우에 써 노흔 내 노래의 줄에는＜142＞
비방울이 구멍을 파나니.

오오, 나의 孤寂하고 걱졍 만흔 이 땅이여,
절믄이는 나를 돌아보지도 아니하며
늙은이는 나를 니저바렷나니,
아아, 하고십거든 맘대로 하여라.

그러나 그이들과 그이들의 아들의
또 아들 되는 이까지라도 나의 노래를
생각하여 볼 때는 잇으리.

눈 압헤의 光榮을 자랑하는
믹딸이며 따이스며 끄리께라의
아름답은 그 뺨들이 떨니고 들어갈 때,
「나의 죽은 뒤에라도 사랑하는 이는
나의 노래를 함끠는 노래하리!」라고
이럿케 나쳐렴은 혼자 주저리지 안으리.＜143＞

***

Morning Rain
						Translated by Kim Ŏk

The night brightens,
The stars recede,
Courtesans (遊女) with their lovers (愛人)
Have likely come home together,
At such a time, I am merely alone,
Fully meeting the morning rain,
Endlessly, upon the sand, I write a song.

On the tree leaves, shining raindrops have formed,
The stream flowing, winding around the small path (小路),
Sweeps fallen leaves (落葉) and grains of sand as it flows,
And on the lines of my song written upon the earth,<142>
The raindrops dig holes.

Oh, my solitary and care-filled land,
The young do not look back at me,
The old have forgotten me,
Ah, if you wish to, do as you please.

However, even those people, and their sons,
And even their sons' sons,
There will be times when they think upon my song.

When the beautiful cheeks of Miltiades, Thais, and Clytemnestra (믹딸이며 따이스며 끄리께라),
Who boast of the glory (光榮) before their eyes,
Tremble and sink away,
"Even after my death, my beloved ones
will sing my songs together!" — thus saying,
Like me, they will not collapse alone.<143>

**Summary**

This poem, translated by Kim Ŏk, depicts a solitary speaker writing verses on the sand in the morning rain, feeling isolated and forgotten by both the young and the old. Despite this loneliness and the ephemeral nature of the writing being washed away, the speaker finds solace and defiance in the belief that their songs will endure and be remembered by future generations, long after the fleeting beauty and glory of famous figures (like Miltiades, Thais, Clytemnestra) have faded. From a historical perspective, the poem is interesting as an example of Western literature being translated and published in Korea during the colonial period (1924). The themes of isolation, the passage of time, and the enduring power of art might have resonated with Korean intellectuals like Kim Ŏk navigating their cultural identity and aspirations under Japanese rule. The translation itself, using early 20th-century Korean orthography, is also of linguistic interest.
