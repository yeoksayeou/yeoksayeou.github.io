The Summer of the Poor and the Summer of the Rich
By Sangch'an

The haves and the have-nots always differ greatly in their lives, but this difference is particularly severe, especially in the summer. The haves possess large mansions (大厦巨屋) and vast gardens, yet they also build mountain pavilions and waterside gazebos (山亭水閣). During the day, they pass the time playing Korean chess (將棋) or Go, quench their thirst with beer and cider, and feel the breeze from their beautiful concubines' (美妾) fans and electric fans (電風機), sighing deeply as if melting. At night, they lie sprawled like fish caught in a net within raw silk mosquito nets (生綃모긔장), never knowing what a bedbug (빈대) or a mosquito (모긔) is.

The have-nots cannot even freely obtain a single narrow, smelly rented room (貫房) and wander displaced (流離飄迫) east, west, south, and north, crying from the heat and the rainy season. Bitten all over by mosquitoes, bedbugs, and fleas, their entire bodies become covered in sores. The haves eat three meals a day of spicy beef soup (육개장) and steamed tender chicken (軟鷄찜) until they are full, yet still complain about having an appetite or not, switching between Japanese food (日料理) and Western food (洋料理) from time to time (they don't even eat Chinese food (淸料理), saying it's greasy and smells).

The have-nots often cannot even get a bowl of barley porridge or flour paste three times a day and, being hungry, tighten their belts until marks appear. The haves wear clothes made of Hansan fine ramie (韓山細苧), Ch'ŏngyang raw ramie (靑陽生苧), or some kind of gauze (紗) or silk gauze (綃) so thin their skin shows through, yet still complain that the clothes cling to their bodies or are baggy. The have-nots cannot even properly wear clothes made of coarse hemp cloth like four-strand (四繩布), five-strand (五繩布), or broad cotton cloth (廣唐布) and their bare, reddish skin bulges out. Also, the haves go to places like Inch'ŏn (仁川) or Wŏnsan (元山) for sea bathing (海水浴) and drink medicinal water (藥水) in places like Sambang (三防) or Kangsŏ (江西). But the have-nots bathe in the sweat pouring from their own bodies and often cannot even get lukewarm tap water because they cannot afford the water bill. Where in our Chosŏn (朝鮮) today would such miserable conditions not exist? Yet they are particularly numerous in the city called Kyŏngsŏng (京城). The poor in the countryside are mostly farmers (農民), so even if they work weeding for others through labor exchange (품아시), they can eat barley rice freely three times a day. When it's really hot, they can spread a mat under a tree<24> and rest comfortably, and they can freely bathe in the clear, flowing streams, finding natural relief from the heat. However, the poor in the so-called cities are mostly factory (工場) workers, so summer is even harder. Among them, those who labor (勞動) in front of fire from morning till evening face even greater hardship. Here are a few examples of their difficult reality.

The Blacksmith (鐵工匠) Who Lives by Charcoal Fire (炭火)
Among birds, they say there is a fire-eating bird (食火鳥); among our poor, there are blacksmiths (鐵工匠) who make their living by charcoal fire (炭火). If blacksmiths had substantial capital like foreigners, with fully equipped factories and sufficient machinery, they might not face such severe hardship even when laboring on hot summer days. However, our blacksmiths (鐵工匠) have no capital, so they have no factories and no machinery. Most of them build what are nominally temporary workshops (假工場) of just a couple of rooms, and their only "machinery" consists of a single bellows (골풀무), an anvil (모루), hammers (매), and the like. From morning till evening, without rest, they keep the charcoal fire blazing red with the bellows, striking and handling iron hotter than the fire itself with hammers and hands. For them, there is no heat, no hotness. Their resistance to heat is stronger than that of peoples living directly under the equator (赤道直下). But how could they not know the heat? It is because hunger is more painful than shedding blood and sweat. If only there were plenty of work like in spring and fall, there might be the pleasure of earning money, but in summer, all kinds of work are in a state of suspension, a downturn, so blacksmithing (鐵工) work is also said to be scarce. Even laboring to death in front of such a hot charcoal fire, their daily income (日收入) amounts to only 70 or 80 chŏn. That is, they earn less than the price of two bottles of beer for the haves. How miserable their life is.

The Female Rubber Factory Workers (고무 女職工) Whose Heads Ache from the Sulfur (黃) Smell
Ordinary people, just wearing rubber shoes in the summer, complain about the sulfur smell (黃내) or their feet getting sweaty and blistered (the haves don't even wear them). But female factory workers (女職工), braving the pungent sulfur smell and hot steam, toil (勞作) like this on such hot days, sweating profusely. Especially considering Chosŏn customs, women who rarely even stepped outside are driven by hunger and cold (飢寒) to work as factory hands, enduring the factory owners' abuse and insults, just to earn a mere 40 or 50 chŏn. In Kyŏngsŏng (京城) alone, there are over 30 factories where several thousand female workers, unable to get a good night's sleep due to mosquitoes and bedbugs in the early evening, labor (勞動) at the factory from 4 a.m. until noon<25> before returning home. (Work hours are not all fixed; the reporter observed this at one factory.) If they were paid by the hour, they could perhaps rest a bit while working, but because wages are based on piecework, the saying "the throat is the government office" (목구멍이 포도청) truly applies. In their desire to earn even one more p'un, they forget the heat and toil (勞作). Their hardship is beyond words, but compared to the concubine types (妾類輩) who exploit others without lifting a finger (10指를 不動), how sacred it is to earn a living through one's own labor.

The Locomotive Cleaners (機關車掃除夫) Who Labor Covered in Oil Bags
All people under heaven must have their occupation (必有其職). Each finds a different way to make a living; some live as cleaners (소제부) on these hot summer days. The food they eat is a small bento box meal (변도밥). The clothes they wear are oil bags (긔름 주머니). Day and night, they ceaselessly wipe down the locomotives (기관차) coming and going east, west, south, and north with oily rags, and they also stoke the fire in the firebox (火口). Their whole bodies become covered in soot from the coal (石炭) smoke anyway. In ancient times, Yeyang (豫讓), to avenge his lord Chibaek (智伯), "painted his body to appear leprous" (漆身爲癩); these cleaners, to avenge their hungry throats, become charcoal stumps and oil-soaked every day. Moreover, while Japanese people receive high wages, the pitiful Chosŏn (朝鮮) people perform the same labor yet receive wages ranging from a minimum of around 1 wŏn to a maximum of around 1 wŏn 50 chŏn. They fight daily with fire and oil (긔름), and their only pleasures are said to be going home to change into clean clothes and receiving their wages. The haves just pay money and can comfortably ride second or first-class trains, lying down and traveling thousands of li without any worries, but behind the operation of these trains lies the infinite sweat and tears of these people.

The Tobacco Factory Workers (烟草職工) Suffering and Crying in the Tobacco Fumes Hotter Than Fire
People who comfortably sit and smoke tobacco (담배) even on summer days speak idly of tobacco (烟草) as a "worry-forgetting thing" (忘憂物) or "grief-dispelling grass" (消愁草), but each single cigarette (궐연) contains the mingled blood and sweat of many people. Leaving aside the matter of farmers in the countryside cultivating and harvesting it, only to have all the profits expropriated by the Monopoly Bureau (專賣局), just the manufacturing (製造) of cigarettes in the factory involves the intense labor of many people. Originally, tobacco (烟草) not only has a strong smell but also a warming property, so in winter, people who wrap their hands or feet in tobacco leaves do not suffer from frostbite (凍傷). But in summer, with many people in the factory and the steam from the machinery, it is unbearably hot, and the tobacco smell<26> is also intense. Its fumes are warm, making the factory hotter than a steam room (한증막). Yet the workers (職工) labor in this high heat for meager wages. Particularly, there are many young boys and girls in the tobacco factories. In their frail bodies, they labor all day long, sometimes becoming dizzy from the tobacco fumes and fainting (昏倒). Their eyes and noses ache so much that they just cry even after returning home. Oh, hateful money! Save the people! Especially save the young boys and girls!
Looking at just these few examples above, one can roughly understand how difficult summer is for our poor people's lives. Yet, those who work sweating like this find life extremely difficult, while the idle loafer gangs (불한당 무리) who relax live comfortably even in summer. Brothers! Whose fault is this, and what is the reason? When one thinks about it, a lump of blazing fire (熱火) rises in the chest, hotter than the sun of the hottest summer days (三伏). Can we truly not rectify such unfairness? Just because it's summer, we shouldn't merely talk about the heat. If we do not advance with courage many hundreds of times greater than that of Zhuge Liang (諸葛武侯) crossing the Lu River (渡瀘) in the fifth month, a cool breeze will never blow upon our chests. <27>

**Summary**

This 1924 editorial by Sangch'an starkly contrasts the leisurely summer enjoyed by the wealthy in colonial Korea with the grueling and hazardous conditions faced by the poor. The author details the suffering of specific laborers—blacksmiths enduring intense heat for little pay, female rubber factory workers exposed to toxic fumes, locomotive cleaners covered in soot and oil, and tobacco factory workers (including children) suffering from heat and toxic fumes—all while the rich indulge in luxuries. The piece concludes with a passionate call to action against this social injustice, urging readers to fight for change with immense courage. For historians, this article offers a vivid first-hand account of the extreme class disparity and harsh labor conditions in urban colonial Korea (specifically Seoul). It highlights the specific struggles of different working-class groups, the exploitation within factories (including piecework systems and wage disparity between Koreans and Japanese), and the growing social consciousness and resentment against such inequality during the period.

貧者의 녀름과 富者의 녀름
						相瓚
잇는 놈과 업는 놈은 언제던지 생활상 차별이 심하지만은 특히 녀름에는 그 차별이 尤甚하다. 잇는 놈은 大厦巨屋에 광대한 정원을 가지고도 山亭水閣을 또 지여노코 낫이면은 將棋 바둑으로 소일하고 맥주 사이다로 목을 취기며 美妾의 부채바람과 電風機바람에 흑흑 늣기다십히 하고 밤이면은 生綃모긔장안에 그물(網)에 걸닌 고기 모양으로 멀둥멀둥 누어서 빈대가 무엇인지 모긔가 무엇인지 알지도 못하되
업는 놈은 狹窄하고 냄새나는 貫房 한 間이나마 마음대로 엇지 못하고 동서남북으로 流離飄迫하야 더위에 울고 장마에 울며 모긔 빈대 벼룩에게 다 뜻겨서 왼 몸에 만신창이 된다. 잇는 놈은 三時로 육개장 軟鷄찜에 배가 부르고도 입맛이 잇느니 업느니 하고 日料理 洋料理를 때때로 갈어먹되(淸料理는 늑기하고 냄새난다고 먹지도 안는다.)
업는 놈은 三時에 버리죽 양밀가루 범벅 한 그릇도 잘 어더먹지 못하고 배가 곱하서 허리띄를 자국이 나도록 잔뜩 졸나 매며 잇는 놈은 잠자리 날개가튼 韓山細苧靑陽生苧 무슨 紗 무슨 綃 등으로 살이 다 비치도록 입고도 옷이 몸에 갱기느니 휘죽은 하니 하고 잔소리를 하되 업는 놈은 쇠덕석가튼 四繩布 五繩布나 廣唐布옷도 잘 입지 못하고 벌건 살이 울근불근하다. 또 잇는 놈은 仁川이나 元山가튼 데 가서 해수욕도 하고 三防 江西 등지에 가서 약수를 먹지만은 업는 놈은 제 몸에서 쏘다지는 땀으로 제물 해수욕을 하고 미지근한 수돗물도 물갑을 주지 못해서 잘 어더먹지 못하고 한다. 금일 우리 朝鮮에 이러한 비참한 사정이 어느 곳인들 업스리요 만은 특히 京城이라는 도회지에 더욱 만타. 향촌의 빈민은 대개 농민인 고로 남의 품아시 김을 매주고라도 3시에 보리밥은 마음대로 먹고 졍 더운 때는 나무 그늘에서＜24＞ 거적을 깔고라도 편히 누어 쉬고 또 좔좔 흘너가는 맑은 물에 자유로 목욕을 하야 자연의 피서를 하지만은 소위 도회지에 잇는 빈민은 대개 공장생활을 하는 고로 녀름이면 더욱 곤란하다. 그 중에도 아츰부터 저녁까지 불압헤서 노동하는 사람은 보다 더 곤란하다. 이제 그들의 곤란한 실태의 數例를 들면 대략 左와 갓다.

숫불로 生命을 삼는 鐵工匠
조류 중에는 불을 먹는 食火鳥가 잇다더니 우리 빈민 중에는 숫불(炭火)로 생명을 삼는 鐵工匠이 잇다. 철공도 외국인 모양으로 자본이 상당이 잇서서 공장의 설비도 완전하고 기계가 상당히 구비하얏스면 비록 뜨거운 녀름날에 노동을 할지라도 그다지 심한 곤란이 업슬 것이다. 그러나 우리의 鐵工匠은 자본이 업슴으로 공장도 업고 기계도 업다. 그들은 대개가 두어간 되는 명색 假공장을 지여노코 기계라고는 단지 골풀무 한 개와 모루 매 等屬뿐이다. 아츰 저녁까지 쉬일 새가 업시 풀무로 숫불을 벌거케 피워노코 불보다 더 뜨거운 철물을 메로 치며 손으로 만진다. 그들에게는 더위도 업고 뜨거움도 업다. 赤道直下에 사는 人族보다도 열에 대한 저항력이 더 강하다. 그러나 그들인들 엇지 더운 것을 모르리오만은 배곱흔 것이 피땀 흘리는 것보다 더 괴로운 까닭이다. 그나마 봄과 가을 모양으로 공사나 만엇스면 돈버는 취미나 잇지만은 녀름에는 萬般의 일이 모다 휴식상태와 如히 불경기인 까닭에 鐵工도 또한 일이 적다 한다. 그와가티 뜨거운 숫불압헤서 죽도록 노동을 하야도 日收入이 7,80전에 불과한다. 즉 잇는 놈의 맥주 2병 갑도 못 번다. 그의 생활이 얼마나 비참하냐.

黃냄새에 골이 터지는 고무 女職工
보통 사람은 녀름에 고무신만 신어도 黃내가 나느니 발이 물어 터지느니 하지만은 (잇는 놈은 신지도 안는다.) 女職工들은 그 독한 黃냄새와 더운 증기 긔운을 무릅쓰고 이와 가티 뜨거운 날에 구슬땀을 흘리면서 勞作한다. 특별히 朝鮮의 습관으로 말하면 문밧게도 잘 나서지 안턴 여자가 飢寒에 쪽기여서 불과 4,50전의 임금을 어드랴고 공장주의 학대와 모욕을 당해가면서 직공노릇을 한다. 京城 內만 하야도 30여개소의 공장에 수천의 여직공이 초저녁에 도모긔 빈대 등살에 단잠 한잠을 잘 못자고 오전 4시부터 공장에서 노동을 하다가 午正에＜25＞ 집으로 돌아간다. (시간은 다 일정치 안이하니 기자의 본 공장은 如斯함.) 그 중에 시간으로 임금을 주엇스면 조곰 쉬여가면서도 할 수 잇스나 공작물을 표준하야 임금을 주는 까닭으로 眞所謂 목구멍이 포도청이라고 한푼의 임금이라도 더 어들 욕심에 더위도 다 니저버리고 勞作을 한다. 그의 곤란한 것이야 말할 수 업지만은 10指를 不動하고 남의 등골만 빼먹는 妾類輩에 비하면 자기의 노종으로 버러먹는 것이 얼마나 신성하랴.

긔름주먼니를 뒤집어 쓰고 勞動하는 機關車掃除夫
