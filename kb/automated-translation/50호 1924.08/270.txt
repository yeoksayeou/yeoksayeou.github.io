사진구경 (Viewing a Film)
						최빙 (Ch'oe Ping)
The bell signaling 11:20 rang.
The air in the lounge (控所), which had been motionless until this moment, suddenly began to vibrate. The students, who for an hour had been absorbed in the lecturer's voice amidst a drowsy atmosphere, gathered in the lounge seeking a brief 10-minute respite.
The students' energetic commands, "Right face!", "Dismissed!", could be heard. From this corner and that, the sound of students chattering amongst themselves arose, along with cheerful bursts of "Hahahaha" laughter. The "thump-thump" of footsteps belonging to first and second-year students, who treated the lounge as their exclusive domain, echoed as they ran about freely. All these sounds mingled and swelled, large enough to seem ready to spill out of the lounge.
C and K also occupied a corner of the lounge, contributing their part to the clamor. K, who sought to observe all events unfolding around him with amusement, let out a laugh that uniquely pierced through the great din. But C, his exact opposite, who tried to judge everything by reason yet couldn't escape his emotions, explained something earnestly without cracking a smile. Between the two of them, the following conversation took place.
"It's not that I'm saying we absolutely cannot go. What I mean is, if a good film (寫眞) comes along, let's get permission from the school authorities and go properly and respectably. It's not that I particularly wanted to participate in that committee or whatever it was... As you know, when it comes to sightseeing or entertainment, I haven't missed a single film, good or bad...?! But lately, society has been criticizing our school too much... Saying we frequent kisaeng houses too often... or go to the theater (劇場) too much... It was too uncomfortable to hear, that's why that committee, or whatever it was, was formed..." This soft, hurried, yet somehow firm voice belonged to C.<32>
"No, no! What does it matter to us what the world says!? We are us, and they are them... And besides, getting permission from the school and seeking their understanding... that's out of the question... Absolutely out of the question... If we do that, are we going to watch things like 'Ono Satsuki, the Teacher'? Hmph, what's that to us? Maybe it'll serve as a supplement to our ethics professor?! Probably... Haha, but watching things like that makes my head ache, I can't bear it!
Stop your pointless chatter and let's go see this film... A guy like me can't tell if it's good or bad, but people are raving about it... Maybe that's why it looks like a really well-made film to me! And the main actors, 'Rirayangkit'ssu' (리라양킷쑤), 'Tabit'ŭ' (다빗트), 'Sanda-song' (산다-송), they do a really great job... Really great... Perfect casting... They couldn't possibly do better... You absolutely must go see it! Especially someone like you must see it!" These cheerful words were K's.
C was swayed by K's words. The thought "Should I go tonight?" arose in a corner of C's mind, which had been filled with the thought "I shouldn't go." C hesitated.
Finally, C opened his mouth and asked K,
"Do you have the program (푸로그램)?"
"Yeah! I do. What do you want it for? You, who said you shouldn't go... Hahahaha," K replied sarcastically. C's face turned red, and he stood there rubbing his hands together, hesitating.
Seeing this, K let out another loud, sarcastic laugh.
C's head lowered slightly. His face grew increasingly red.
Tiny beads of sweat formed on the bridge of his nose.
C's consciousness became muddled.
K felt a little awkward. He muttered inwardly, "I went too far."
Just then, the bell rang, signaling them to return to the classroom.
The students, who had been chattering away until now, ceased their noise. The lounge suddenly became quiet.<33>
The students who had been running around and making noise as they pleased now found their respective places and lined up.
K and C also moved towards their places. C still couldn't lift his bowed head. A tense silence lay between them. Both were lost in their own thoughts.
K thought, "I shouldn't push him any further." And without a word, he took a neatly folded piece of paper from his pocket (포켓) and slipped it into C's pocket. C also said nothing.
C and K took their respective places and stood in line.
The students' energetic commands, "Right face!", "Forward march!", sounded just like before. Following this, the thumping sound of students marching into the classroom could be heard.
After about five minutes of this, the lounge became so quiet one might wonder, "Where did all that earlier noise go?"
Outside, the spring rain, which hadn't stopped, continued to fall gently.

That period was the class taught by the Hanmun (漢文) teacher, who was over 70 years old.
"Confucius said these words..." the teacher's listless voice continued for about 20 minutes.
Outside, the spring rain still fell silently.
The students, who had been absorbed in the lecture, began to doze off one by one, two by two. The teacher, too, yawned listlessly amidst his droning words. C also initially tried to listen intently to the lecture. But after listening to the teacher's languid voice for about 20 minutes, he began to doze off. C's consciousness grew increasingly hazy.
He finally bumped his head against the desk (冊床). His hazy consciousness momentarily returned. But less than a minute later, his mind became foggy again.
After sleeping for several minutes with his chin propped on his arm, he was startled awake by the teacher rapping on the table (테불). The teacher looked at C, smiled faintly, and said, "Feeling sleepy?!" C rubbed his eyes and looked around. Then he let out a meaningless chuckle.<34>
He tried infinitely hard to stay awake. But the charm of the spring goddess, seemingly capable of melting a person's resolve, pulled him back into unconsciousness. Unable to bear it any longer, he muttered, cursing the teacher, "Ah, let it be! What is this old man droning on about?! Won't he just stop already..."
The teacher's listless voice droned on endlessly, "Confucius did this..." and "Mencius did that..."
C, forcing his sleepy eyes open, tried to pass the time by thinking of this and that.
Suddenly, the thought from the previous break time in the lounge surfaced in his mind. The thought "Ah—I shouldn't go" floated up in a corner of his foggy brain. But he realized that from somewhere unknown, the thought "It doesn't matter if I go" was emerging. The notion that K's words held some truth surfaced.
He unconsciously pulled the piece of paper K had given him from his pocket (포켓트). It was the program for the K-kwan (K舘) theater.
His gaze listlessly followed the lines of text on the program. It was indeed entangled in clever advertising techniques.
C again muttered inwardly, as if asking someone a rhetorical question, "Should I go?!" But the thought "No!" canceled out the "Should I go?!" thought.
"But..."
"No..."
"Should I go?..."
"No!"
A battle between two conflicting desires, to go or not to go, erupted in C's mind. A battle between reason and emotion began. The struggle continued for some time. He thought inwardly, "If, by chance, I go and unfortunately get caught by someone else... especially if the Japanese teachers (日本先生) see me, they'll surely repeat that phrase they love so much, 'Because he's Korean (朝鮮人だから)...'. Ah! I shouldn't go!!"
"But that film...? Who knows! They survive on advertising techniques...! But... ah..."<35>
Finally, he decided, "I shouldn't go." His reason had overcome his emotion by a ratio of 6 to 4. But that 4, the emotion, was not entirely expelled from C. It was merely crumpled up in a corner. One couldn't know when or where it might explode.
The 12:30 bell signaling lunchtime rang. The students, drunk with sleep, suddenly snapped to attention. C, too, like someone waking up, looked around, blinking, and muttered inwardly, "Why did I think such things?"
The teacher's endless lecture finally concluded. With a clatter, students put their books into their desks.
After a merely formal bow, the students headed out to the lounge, seeking a time of rest. C, yawning blankly, walked along with the crowd of students.
Outside, the rain, showing no sign of stopping, still fell silently.

The rain that had fallen silently all day had finally stopped. But dark clouds, like a charcoal sketch smudged across the sky, gathered in one place, stagnant, as if they would never move.
Those heavy clouds seemed ready to turn back into rain at any moment and wash away everything in this world.
C, having eaten dinner earlier than usual, tucked a Western-style umbrella (洋傘) under his arm and set out for his daily walk.
Wanting to escape all the confusion, to follow the fresh air in solitude and forget the world, like seeking the mythical leisure of an immortal in legend, C unconsciously turned his back on the city streets—filled with the smoke of evening cooking fires and the groans of human sin—and directed his steps toward M-tae (M臺).
The curtain of darkness descended silently on all sides.
C stood atop M-tae, gazing blankly down at the city streets, lost in thoughts he couldn't name.
From far below M-tae, the sound of groans, oppressed by suffering, groans for the sake of life, drifted up. C, listening absently to the sound, experienced a slight sense of superiority.
He stood in one place indefinitely, smiling meaningfully. It seemed as though he might never move.<36>
A cool breeze blowing from the west brushed against C's cheek and hurried away. The curtain of darkness deepened further. The groans for life gradually diminished.
Electric lights flickered on in the city streets. Countless lights sent countless beams towards C's eyes, twinkling capriciously.
Among the countless scattered lights, C discovered several hundred gathered together, sending him subtle, flirtatious glances.
He muttered meaninglessly, "That must be K-kwan (K舘)...?!" He repeated again, "That's K-kwan... hmph," just as old men engrossed in Go repeat the same phrase over and over, dozens, hundreds of times, meaninglessly, he repeated it several times.
Just then, the sound of the bell from the G Church (G敎會堂)—"You who have passed this day amidst sin! The day of God's judgment is near! Quickly, quickly step onto the path of firm belief! For eternal life... For immortality... Dong-dang dong-dang dong-dang"—rang out, vibrating the heavy, stagnant air all around. C's mind snapped alert. And then again, he cried out more loudly than before, "That must be K-kwan!—Ah!" and trembled once.
Finally, he asked again, "Should I go?!" The emotion that had been driven into a corner earlier exploded all at once.
"Should I go?!"
"But what if...?"
He felt a slight sense of fear.
The surroundings grew darker and darker. Correspondingly, the city lights shone even brighter, sending tempting beams towards C.
Within C's mind, the battle between the contradictory thoughts "Should I go?" and "Should I not?" intensified.
As the lights grew steadily brighter, his limbs increasingly urged a resolution to the conflict.
He thought, "Should I go ask the teacher (the off-campus supervisor)?!" "But how can I, with what face, say I want to go to the theater...? I, who argued for and initiated the recent matter [the committee against excessive entertainment], and then avoided it...? That won't do! That won't do!" he thought, shaking his head vigorously from side to side.
"Then...?" he muttered inwardly. His gaze unconsciously turned towards the heavy clouds that looked ready to fall at any moment.<37>
The sound of the military band playing at K-kwan and the benshi's (narrator's) voice explaining, "He loved her passionately. But... ah!" seemed to drift faintly right beside his ear.
C, whose mind had been battling itself in a hazy state for a while, finally made his decision.
"Let's go... Surely on a rainy day like today... there won't be any risk of being caught...? Even if I am caught, there's nothing to be done... Ah! Let's go?!"
Realizing he had his wallet, he immediately started walking towards the tram stop. "Am I committing a sin...? But..." he thought inwardly.
As the tram passed through the crowded Chongno intersection, constantly ringing its bell, he clicked his tongue in annoyance. And he muttered inwardly, "Can't it go a bit faster..."
By the time he reached the theater, the actual footage/newsreel (實寫) had ended, and the comedy (喜劇) was also over. He found a seat in a corner of the third-class section, huddled down, unable to lift his head or even cough loudly for fear of being seen by others. Outside, the heavy clouds, unable to hold back any longer, began to shed drops, one by one, then two by two.<38>

**Summary**

This story depicts the internal struggle of a Korean student, C, in 1924 colonial Seoul. Torn between his desire to see a popular Western film featuring actors like "Rirayangkit'ssu" and his fear of societal criticism, school rules, and potential judgment (especially from Japanese figures), C wrestles with his conscience. Encouraged by his carefree friend K, C ultimately succumbs to the temptation, sneaking into the K-kwan theater late at night, feeling guilt and anxiety. The story highlights the cultural changes and social pressures faced by young, educated Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. Historians might find the explicit mention of Japanese prejudice ("朝鮮人だから / Because he's Korean...") and the detailed portrayal of student anxieties regarding behavior, reputation, and navigating colonial authority particularly revealing about the psychological and social landscape of the era.
