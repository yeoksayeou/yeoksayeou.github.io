新局面을 展開하는 金浦郡
(Kimp'o County, Unfolding a New Phase)

Everyone says Kimp'o, Kimp'o, so is Kimp'o called Kimp'o because it has much money? Is it Kimp'o because its geography is good? In any case, it seems doubtless that this region is a fine county for some reason. This Kimnŭng (金陵), which holds the fifth position among the five counties occupying large cultivated areas within the Kyŏnggi (畿內) region, combines the scenic beauty of P'arŭng (巴陵) and the grain-producing richness of T'ongjin (通津). With rivers and the sea surrounding it on three sides and fields opening up in all directions, its geography is certainly not inferior to others. In this land with 16,163 *chŏngbo* (町步) of cultivated area, the 7,786 farming households that have lived off it since long ago earn an average profit equivalent to 2 *chŏngbo* per household, lacking the striking disparities between rich and poor seen elsewhere, making it worthy of boasting, "I too have money." Furthermore, the jointly sold quantity of silkworm cocoons (蠶繭), increasing steadily by 50% or 100% annually like 100 *sŏk*, 200 *sŏk*, 300 *sŏk*, and the recent sharp increase in buyers desiring the improved local breeds of breeding pigs (種豚), breeding chickens (種鷄), and hatching eggs (種卵), especially excluding the Japanese domestic products previously purchased at high prices from various parts within Korea (鮮內), shows how joyful a phenomenon this is for the farm economy of this region. However, out of the total county cultivated area of over 16,000 *chŏngbo*, nearly 6,000 *chŏngbo* is an area without a consistent annual harvest due to flood and drought damage. Thus, preventing the flooding of the Han River (漢江), which devastates 6/10ths of the entire area, this naturally fortified land (天府金湯), while also putting effort into irrigation to achieve stability in the lives of the county's people, this is the county policy (郡是) of Kimp'o. Accordingly, aroused government officials and civilian volunteers (官民 有志), without getting bogged down in trivial minor issues, have devoted their full energy to water control projects (治水事業). Finally, with the establishment of the Yangdong (陽東), Yangch'ŏn (陽川), and Pup'yŏng (富平) irrigation cooperatives (水利組合), the T'ongjin (通津) cooperative still in the design stage, and the Sin'gok Farm (神谷農場) and Nagaoka Colonization Company (長岡拓殖株式會社) competing head-to-head, pouring in vast sums of money and mobilizing thousands of laborers, the entire Kimp'o area currently seems just like a newly discovered world. 金陵雜感
(Miscellaneous Thoughts on Kimnŭng)

而今安在哉
(Where Are They Now?)

In the era of Old Kimp'o, different from the present Kimp'o, there were not a few figures who received the honor of their age. The fidelity (節義) of Cho Chungbong (趙重峰) and his son, who grasped the hands of 700 righteous men and resisted the enemy until the last moment with a single bow from atop a hundred-foot pole; the loyalty (忠烈) of Yun Kye (尹棨) and Kang Wip'yŏng (姜渭聘), who were unyielding through a hundred setbacks, their bodies without an inch of intact skin, finally exchanging their lives with the enemy's blades; the imposing figure (威容) of Kim Chunnyong (金俊龍), whose god-like military tactics in the great battle of Kwanggyo Mountain (光敎山) chilled the *gall* (膽) of the Qing Emperor; the fierce integrity (烈節) of Yun Chip (尹集), who sacrificed himself as a leading advocate of rejecting peace overtures (斥和); the filial piety (孝行) of Kim Hyosin (金孝愼), whose utmost devotion to his parents made even powerful bandits submit whenever encountered; the accomplished retired scholar (名儒) Chŏng Hŭiryang (鄭希良), who knew contentment, did not slack, and had an auspicious fate (八字); the literary works (文章) of Chŏng Kŭgyŏng (鄭克永), whose rare masterpieces startled the literary world of the Song people – even just these old tales (古談) show that Kimnŭng (金陵) in those days was indeed a local community (鄕黨) that could not be trifled with. Alas, those whom we cannot meet again even in dreams, where are they now! (而金安在!)<70>

일홈 파라먹는 模範會
(The "Model" Associations Selling Their Name)

Kimp'o, too, was once part of a defense zone (防地帶). The 3,000-*ch'ŏk* ancient Three Han fortress (三韓古城) and the 15-*ri* (old *ri*) Munsu Fortress (文殊城) were both considerably advantageous for attack and defense by land and water. Now, only the dilapidated remains (殘骸) worn away by wind and rain and the several-storied stone pagoda north of Ŭngbong (鷹峰) give a fleeting impression. And today, when even the strategy of "one man guards the pass, ten thousand cannot break through" (一夫當關當夫莫開) has become obsolete junk belonging to a bygone era, with what shall we confront the enemy at our back and belly (腹背之敵) who seeks to strip us of our right to exist? In short, it must be the united hearts forming a fortress (衆心成城). However, regarding the so-called purely Korean groups currently in this region, such as the Model Youth Association (模範靑年會) and the Model Morals Improvement Society (模範 矯風會), without meticulously criticizing whether the motives for their formation were originally voluntary or provoked (挑發的), the tongues of the local people (鄕人), boasting that the Provincial authorities (道當局), who somehow favor them, praise the performance of these two organizations as very excellent and give large subsidies (多額) for special commendation, are almost rotting with maggots. Even for someone touring the eight provinces (八道江山) and encountering such a sight here for the first and last time, it provides no small amusement (消遺) for a day, but how much must those fellows weep with gratitude (感泣), bearing the heavy burden of another's boundless royal grace (天恩罔極)? However, it is all too regrettable to hear that for them, who make a model of being quiet and gentle (얌전), besides the forced subscription (購讀) to government papers (官用紙), which amounts to mere perfunctory fulfillment of duty (塞責), there seems to be no reading material they voluntarily cherish. How could it be only this? One can know ten things by hearing one. No matter how much it is a [illegible] town (鄕) said to only know money, there are newspapers and magazines. There are Korean texts and foreign texts. There are official organs and popular papers. Despite the fact that if there is something, there is everything, the truly deplorable (彫*) state of this region's culture falling behind (落伍) is indeed beyond words ([illegible]舌이 俱絶). Alas, whose fault is this? Is this the work for model youth, is this something the Model [illegible] Morals (模範*風) should be silent about? If their sole purpose is based only on being gentle and making a living off official [illegible] (官*), how about receiving the model of the Yangch'ŏn Women's Diligence and Savings Association (陽川婦人勤儉貯蓄會), which has already gathered over 4,000 bundles (團) of [illegible] mountain (***山) by saving one egg daily, and trying to reform oneself anew, becoming [illegible] white (白), honest again, and sacred again?

到處本履聲
(Sounds of Footsteps Everywhere)

First, comparing the wealth of large landowners throughout the entire county, there are two county residents (本郡人), Kim Chuyŏn (金周演) and Yi Wanyong (李完用) of Kyŏngsŏng (京城), each owning over 200 *chŏngbo*. Besides them, there are 9 people owning between 50 and 70 *chŏngbo*. Excluding the 63 *chŏngbo* owned by other foreigners, the large Japanese landowners include 2 owning over 200 *chŏngbo*, 1 owning over 150 *chŏngbo*, and 2 owning over 100 *chŏngbo*. Now, indicating the Japanese ownership by land category (地目別), it is like this:
Dry fields (田) over 397 *chŏngbo*, Paddy fields (畓) over 929 *chŏngbo*, Ponds/marshes (池沼) over 1 *chŏngbo*, Miscellaneous land (雜種地) over 297 *chŏngbo*, Total over 1,648 *chŏngbo*.

밀따리 玉子光
(Milttari Okchagwang Rice)

Since old times, when speaking of T'ongjin rice (通津米), although the local residents (本土 居民) mostly use it for white rice cakes (白餠) at year-end and New Year's, Seoul gentlemen (서울 양반들) especially compete to buy and use it regardless of price. Furthermore, the precious item most renowned as an Imperial Household supply (御用品) was precisely this Milttari Okchagwang (밀따리 玉子光). Its appearance is similar to Annam rice (安南米), and it is said to be suitable for cultivation (栽培) in areas where freshwater and saltwater mix. Also, because this Okchagwang yields less harvest compared to other varieties and does not increase in volume at all when cooked, the number of its cultivators gradually decreased, nearly becoming extinct (絕種). However, through government encouragement (獎勵), its production, which was barely less than 50 *sŏk* in Taishō 10 (1921), increased to over 100 *sŏk* in Taishō 11 (1922), and 300 *sŏk* in Taishō 12 (1923), thus increasing year by year (逐年). Now, speaking again of the distinctive features of this Okchagwang: first, it has strong stickiness (粘氣); second, it has strong resistance (堪耐力) to drought and flood disasters (水旱災); third, when cooked, it takes on a pale blue-green hue (淡靑色); and furthermore, because it is very sticky, spoons and chopsticks (匙箸) are easily bent (屈折).

一斗七石
(Seven Sŏk from One Tu)

"Seven *sŏk* from one *tu*" (一斗七石) means harvesting 7 *sŏk* from land requiring one *tu* (斗落) of seed. I have seen my fair share of farming villages everywhere that put much effort into securing fertilizer (거름), but among those I've seen, a place like this Kimp'o region, which establishes 2 fertilizer-making villages ([illegible] 제조부락) in each *myŏn* (面), totaling 18 villages, and has each household in those villages install [the necessary facilities] all at once, thoroughly setting up policies for increasing rice production (山米增殖策), seems qualified to be the leader (座首). Among them, the villagers of Purori (不老里) in Kŏmdan-myŏn (黔丹面) are said to use the fertilizer they produced over one year sufficiently on 2-*sŏk*-yielding fields (2石落田畓) with some leftover, carrying it over for use the following year, resulting in rice yields reaching 6 or 7 *sŏk* per *tu*-area (斗落). This is said to be a truth that sounds like a lie. The raw materials for making this compost (퇴비) are generally livestock manure and urine (糞尿), refuse ([illegible]芥), other filth ([illegible]物), topsoil (生土), etc. It is made under the guidance of county technical advisors (郡技術員), and in autumn, the results for the following year's use are invariably inspected ([illegible]査), and a quality assessment competition (品評會) for this is held annually.

배씸 조흔 人骨商
(Gutsy Human Bone Merchants)

As the proverb goes, a man of Yan (燕人) sold the bones of a dead horse, and someone else makes a profit off their ancestor's bones (뻑다구). Recently in the Kimp'o region, it is said there are many guys running wild, unable to sell their own ancestors' bodily remains (體骨). Regarding the land expropriation (土地受容) within the jurisdiction of the Pup'yŏng Irrigation Cooperative, the budgeted amount, which was merely 80,000 wŏn, jumped to 125,000 wŏn through the county office's mediation ([illegible]旋). Also, the long-standing dispute (係爭) between this cooperative's needed land and the Yun Imgye (尹林溪) gravesite (墓地), which involved a budgeted sum of only 64 wŏn, was finally resolved with the cooperative side paying out 8,760 wŏn, also through the county office's mediation (調整). Hearing such things, how stirred up must be the stomachs of the Mammon worshippers (拜金鬼) who would jump into fire carrying a shovel if it meant money? They rummage and rummage through their ancestral graves (先山), let alone the dry bones (枯骨) in the tombs (塜), even searching thoroughly through ownerless old tombs (古塜) and children's graves (兒塜), claiming that even cow bones and dog bones are the bones of their grandfather, their father. Trying to make a fortune (一攫千金) by latching onto this, those lacking financial luck (財數) get caught by the stern official (딱금나으리) and almost shit watery stools into a Japanese oil barrel (倭油桶). It seems there were many who were beaten and then patched up again, but even now, among them, there are said to be not a few guys who, without causing a big fuss, manage to bribe the Yama's messengers ([illegible]羅使令), cleverly maneuvering and making a profit from bone gathering (취렴).

제자리를 찻는 郡守
(The County Magistrate Finding His Place)

Among all under heaven where "all are viewed with equal benevolence" (一視同仁), who suffers the most sorrow? It must be said that the Korean county magistrates (朝鮮人郡守令監네들) are the most pitiful beings among humankind. If it's a Japanese gentleman (倭양반), they tremble like mice seeing a cat just hearing the word, stammering out single-word answers with a dazed look under the official's chin, and if asked for money for some donation (贊助金) or banquet fee (宴會費), they contribute whether they like it or not, just barely maintaining their flimsy dignity (軆面). However, as the saying goes, a dragon can emerge even from a ditch; Kimp'o's young magistrate (졸부) Insik (仁植) seems to be properly finding his place. He is a young high official (少年堂上), the first such magistrate experienced since the Qing dynasty fell (落清後). Moreover, although not much time has passed since he took office (莅位) in this post, that he has staked his precious self (千金一身) amidst disputes and leads the discordant public sentiment (落落難合人心), displaying distinctly Korean-centered (朝鮮人 本位) good capabilities (良能), is something everyone cannot help but praise (致賀). He eliminates the coercive measures of land expropriation (土地受容), which become the focal point of public grievance (民願) in the annual water control projects, decisively resolves (直裁) all sorts of chaotic ([illegible]*) problems with one stroke, facilitating communication ([illegible]*相通) between initiators (發起者) and between officials and the public. Furthermore, he ensures fair resolution of labor-capital issues (勞資問題) where unity is lacking among the various irrigation organizations, absolutely prevents the immigration of Japanese farmers (日本農民移住), and gives priority to fellow white-clothed compatriots (白衣 동무) even in the hiring of government and public officials (官公吏 採用). Ah, since the husband (夫君) has already rolled up his sleeves and stepped forward, it is natural that he should find his place completely. However, others who do not know him still seem unable to discern his true intentions (衷曲). Alas, a lone boat (一葉孤舟) on the vast whale waves (萬里鯨波) where dangerous reefs (險礁) multiply as one proceeds? <72>

**Summary**

This 1924 article from *Kaebyŏk* magazine describes Kimp'o County as a region undergoing significant change ("a new phase"). It highlights the county's agricultural potential, relative lack of wealth disparity, and booming sericulture and livestock breeding. However, it notes severe issues with flooding and drought affecting a large portion of farmland. Major irrigation projects involving several cooperatives and private companies are underway, transforming the landscape. The article contrasts this development with Kimp'o's past, recalling heroic historical figures. It sharply criticizes contemporary local "model" organizations as subservient and ineffective, merely seeking official favor and subsidies while cultural life stagnates. Details on land ownership reveal significant holdings by Japanese individuals and companies. A specific local rice variety, Okchagwang, is discussed, along with advanced fertilizer techniques boosting yields. The text condemns corruption related to land compensation for irrigation projects, where people fraudulently claim ancestral bones for payouts. Finally, it praises the current Korean county magistrate, Insik, for taking a pro-Korean stance, mediating disputes fairly, resisting Japanese farmer immigration, and prioritizing Koreans in hiring, though acknowledging the difficulties he faces.

The article is historically significant for its snapshot of rural Korean development under Japanese colonial rule. It reveals the complexities of modernization efforts (irrigation), the economic impact (changing crops, land ownership shifts favoring Japanese), social criticism of collaborating or ineffective Korean organizations, persistent local issues like corruption, and the precarious position of Korean officials attempting to navigate colonial structures while advocating for Korean interests. The details on land distribution, specific agricultural practices, and the explicit critique of certain groups offer valuable insights into the socio-economic conditions and sentiments of the period.
