Seeing the Artificial Humans at the Tsukiji Little Theater
						S, K.
The powerful vibration sound of steel plates, suggesting a world made of devices and rotation, gives a shock to the audience's anticipation. The psychology of the audience, possessing reality rather than fantasy for this "Fantastic Melodrama," begins to shatter with this one sound. And then the curtain rises. What expectations? Without room for thought, spinning electric balloons, box-like flashing lights of five colors, the regular metallic sounds of the factory and machinery surrounding the R.U.R. company's general affairs office, the noise of a new world giving a premonition—everything pulls one into a world of fantasy that is not fantasy, like a mind falling into an abyss. On the wall hangs a flyer statistical chart proving the low cost of artificial worker production. This already seems like the author's irony. It's also like casting a critical chill on the world of melodrama. The arrogant commanding voice of General Manager Domin (Tomoda Kyōsuke), dictating replies to orders from various Western countries on a typewriter, begins to break apart our doubts about the possibilities of science as if slicing them with a fodder chopper. President's daughter Helena Glory (Yamamoto Yasue), not knowing how much it costs to produce a pound of bread, starts promoting the mission and aspirations of the League of Humanity, advocating for the salvation of artificial humans from the abuse of humanity, showing how inevitably the world revolves around scientific necessity—this author, not a scientist, tries to explain. I believe this is where significant attention should be directed. Machinery originates biologically for economic reasons. Therefore, as Domin says, the best workers are those with minimal demands, who are cheap, don't know how to drink beer, don't know how to play the piano—ideal artificial workers with 2.5 times the efficiency of today's imperfect actual human workers. Or, as Chief Engineer Fabry (Kosugi Yoshio) says, workers "without a youth," meant to combine modern labor and drudgery, are economically most effective. Dr. Hallemeier (Takizawa Osamu), Director of the Psychological Research Institute,<18> says this:
"What use is there in wanting things... They desire nothing. What would they buy even if they received wages? Even if you were to raise them with pineapples or furniture or anything else, I wouldn't say stop. But what can you do about something useless? They have absolutely no appetite. They are utterly ignorant of taste. In short, they never laugh."
Without such artificial workers, this civilized life consisting only of science, machinery, industry, and toil would inevitably become a major headache. The executives of this society who created the Robots are the benefactors of this civilized world. Domin is certainly proud of this. However, the handicap of irony that this author attaches to today's scientific civilization is the point of wanting to give the Robots a nervous system that allows them to feel pain. Or the point of allowing occasional "Robot convulsions." Helena opposes it. She says that is precisely the soul, rebellion, a sign of inner suffering. Also, as Dr. Gall (Sumida Takirō), head of the Physiological Experiment Department, says, from an industrial principle standpoint, to prevent uneconomical mistakes like carelessly putting fingers or heads into machines and breaking them, they intend to insert a "nervous system that feels pain" into the Robots, like an automatic protection device. The author created this handicap through the appearance of the president's daughter. From here, the world of this young<19> playwright's fantastic melodrama emerges. Here too, fantastic satire emerges concerning modern capitalism and militarism, concerning the social structure that must exploit workers from an "industrial standpoint, practical standpoint," and concerning the fate of modern material life that does not labor, that is, does not give birth.
Helena, who came with the mission to liberate the Robots, give them the same favorable treatment as humans, and incite rebellion, ends up becoming Domin's wife on this isolated island of men. Of course, this is not all.
Helena - Why don't you give the Robots souls?
Dr. Gall - It's beyond our power.
Busman - Because the manufacturing cost would be too high.
However, Čapek, with his insight into the masses, knows to deny this. Also—
Helena - But... how do the male Robots and female Robots initially... interact...?
Domin - You mean, why are they initially indifferent? Because they have absolutely no love.
Yet Čapek thinks that without this love, the entire real world will turn to dead ashes. This is the prologue.

Act 2 and Act 3 externally depict the destruction of humanity and the victory of the Robots, but they are successful scenes where Čapek's satire is sharply revealed. On the exact 10th anniversary of Helena's arrival on the island, the day of humanity's doom arrives. It is the day the rebellion of the Robots—lacking personality, pleasure, and desire—succeeds. Already in various Western countries, they were not only demanded simply for factory labor but, in America, Robots were given weapons to suppress worker rebellions, and even the military began using Robots. Thus came the Robots' rebellion against the heinous Robot manufacturers, that is, against human society made of slaughter and hatred. The Robot International League declared, "Humans are our enemies and traitors to the universe." What a grand declaration! It asserted, "Humans are parasites on Robots." What an astonishing fact! The Robots' declaration reads:
"Robots of the entire world. We command you to slaughter all of humanity <20> without exception. Spare not a single man or woman. Destroy everything except the factories, railways, machines, mines, and all raw materials. Then return to work. Work must continue."
How did the Robots, who had neither pain nor desire, start such a rebellion and the destruction of humanity? How did the world's destruction come about? Čapek has three answers to this.
Domin, even at the moment his breath stops, does not regret manufacturing Robots for the ideal (?) of "abolishing the unavoidable servitude of labor, the humiliating, tremendous labor of humans, and eliminating the painful and difficult life." This is the excuse of modern capitalists and militarists. Under the pretext of all production and protection agencies, while tens of thousands of humans live a Robot-like existence beyond mere toil, the dream Domin envisions of "transforming all humanity into the world's aristocracy" is difficult to realize. However, construction engineer Alquist (Maruyama Sadao) criticizes it thus: Old Rossum, the Robot manufacturer, created Robots merely out of atheism, to prove the materialist pride that a technician's product could be more perfect than natural creations, and his son and the company's shareholders did so solely from an industrial standpoint, dreaming of dividends. What is the result? The Robot Radius rebels like this: "Robots can do anything. You people just give orders. You can do nothing but chatter... Don't you need slaughter and power to live like humans?" When these rebellious words vibrate from the mouths of these <21> Robots, marching rhythmically like rigid automatons, Čapek's purpose is achieved.
While theses on limiting Robot production pour out from various universities, the Robot population increases, while the human population gradually decreases. Perhaps nature itself grew angry at Robot manufacturing, to the point where humanity would inevitably become relics after just 30 years of survival competition. The old maidservant Nana (Takahashi Toyoko) laments, "People aren't having children, divine punishment will surely come." She sighs that even puppies have offspring when they grow up, but people not having children is a sign of doom. This is a rebellion against modern society where people are immersed only in material pleasure and neglect motherhood. Here, Čapek once again symbolizes Helena's appearance.
Finally, regarding the moral corruption of humanity seeking ease by avoiding labor, Alquist laments thus:
"Oh, God. We thank you for giving us our toil. Please shed light upon Domin and the other wanderers. Smash their factories and return humanity to labor. Restore the suffering of their souls and bodies. Save us who suffer because of the Robots."
These three issues—namely, the rejection of capitalism and militarism, the advocacy of motherhood, and the sanctity of labor—are very trite, fashionable topics in newspapers and magazines. But Čapek is not a theorist or a demonstrator. So how? Helena came to this island with her mission, exploited Dr. Gall's weakness of loving her to have him insert souls into the Robots, and fearing the increase of Robots and decrease of the human population due to her maternal instinct, she ended up burning the document containing the secret formula for Robot manufacturing. This is Čapek's imperfect trick. Why? Firstly, because the premise of inserting souls by machine is already a fundamental negation of the play's theme, and secondly, because Helena's maternal instinct too weakly led her to burn the secret manuscript. But let me repeat: Čapek is not a theorist or a scientist. He is the author of a "fantastic melodrama." Destruction—not the destruction of Robots, but the destruction of humanity. Why? Does this seem like a dream? You people of civilized society! The audience has no room for contemplation. There is only the cry of the Robot race on stage, dragging humanity into destruction like a swift storm and thunder. Barbed wire, desperate measures, discussion, prayer, resistance—all are drowned out by the footsteps of tens of millions of<22> Robots attacking like thunder. The whole world has fallen into the chaos of destruction and upheaval. Tall cranes, stilt-like supports, factory eaves are dyed red, and explosions sound as if overturning the earth. Again and again. Each time, terrifying flashes of fire shoot towards the sky. The end has come. Pale faces, the posture like a rigid, stubborn bronze diving suit—Robots appear filling Domin's room, windows, balcony, broken doors, ceiling, everywhere, chanting:
"Robots of the world! The power of humanity is destroyed! We have occupied this factory and become the rulers of all things. The era of humanity has passed. A new world has emerged. The Robots shall rule."
The attack of this automaton-like race is truly terrifying. Worthy of trembling. This bizarre, sinister attack must become a symbol of our failure, our defeat. These tens of thousands of foolish masses also seem to teach us the victory of "quantity" over "quality," our stubbornness, our stupidity. It also brings to mind the victory of primitive people.
"Rule of the oceans and the land. Rule of the stars. Rule of the universe. Even greater scope awaits the Robots. Forward!"
When the marching footsteps of tens of millions, unseen, sound like thunder, our legs move involuntarily as if we were the victorious Robots. That's how much this melodrama has reached its climax. <23> Act 3: One year after the world became the Robots'; the Robots who gained liberation from the research lab within the company factory, gained the world, and came to rule the universe and stars, have only one ability lacking: reproduction. Since their lifespan is 20 years, in just a few years, the Robot race that ruled the world has no choice but to self-destruct. All humans are dead. Only one person, the old Alquist, is left. Since Robots lack reproductive ability, new Robots must be manufactured. Alquist, entrusted with researching this secret method, sits in the laboratory day and night without rest. But his arms tremble, his head is foggy. Despair. However, Čapek's unique dramatic conclusion—a new Adam and Eve appear between two Robots, male and female. The two Robots, Primus (Date Makoto) and Helena (Yamamoto Yasue), laugh together, feel shy, chase each other, kiss. It is love. As long as there is love, new life will begin again.
This final act, as was the case in the final act of "The Insect Play," is Čapek's ultimate cliché. However, it's a rather anticlimactic act. Alquist, played by Maruyama Sadao—his despairing sunken eyes, bent back, trembling hands, his voice pressed out from hell—did manage to save this final act. Yamamoto Yasue's actions were very lively. It's quite apparent that this actress tried very hard throughout all acts to bring the role of the President's daughter to life. But for some reason, the impression isn't deep. Thinking about it now, perhaps it's her small physique. She was alive in parts, but overall the impression isn't deep. This is because the role of Helena doesn't suit her.
As for suitability for the role, Azumaya Saburō playing Busman was good. Tomoda Kyōsuke playing Domin was also fitting in his arrogance and self-conceit, but his peculiar diction, biting his tongue, was very awkward. The automaton-like costumes, movements, and tone of voice of the Robots were very well done. The stage design was by Yoshida Kenkichi. This geometric, Constructivist-style set was successful to a certain extent. Especially in the interlude where Domin responds to an interview, explaining the factory, manufacturing plant, pier, and Robot labor conditions, was a clever idea. It was well done without dissonance. But that's all. What was inappropriate was the sound of a real typewriter in the prologue. Mixed with the metallic vibration sounds of the factory behind the stage, the sound of this sturdy typewriter greatly disturbed the fantasy. That the actors' movements, stage effects, and lighting were all harmoniously matched to the tempo in the critical scenes of human destruction in Act 1 and 2 must be credited to the young director, Comrade Hijikata. (1926. 6. 30) <24>

**Summary**

This article is a detailed review by a Korean critic, S.K., of a Japanese production of Karel Čapek's science fiction play R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots) staged at the Tsukiji Little Theater in Tokyo in 1926. The reviewer analyzes the play's themes—critique of industrial capitalism, dehumanization through technology, the meaning of labor, soul, and love, and the potential for societal collapse—and evaluates the production's direction (by Hijikata), set design (by Yoshida Kenkichi), acting (praising Maruyama Sadao as Alquist, critiquing Yamamoto Yasue as Helena and Tomoda Kyōsuke's diction as Domin), and overall atmosphere. S.K. finds Čapek's play highly relevant, though perhaps thematically "trite," and praises the production's climax but finds the ending weak. For historians, this article is interesting as it shows a Korean intellectual engaging directly with modern international cultural production (Czech play, Japanese theatre) and its complex themes during the colonial period. It reflects the anxieties and critiques of modernity, industrialization, and dehumanization that resonated within colonial Korea, viewed through the lens of a contemporary Japanese theatrical interpretation. The reviewer's critical perspective on both the play and the specific Japanese production offers insight into the cultural and intellectual currents among Koreans at the time.

						S, K.
裝置와 迴轉으로 된 세계를 암시하는 저력잇는 철판의 진동소리가 관중의 기대심에 한 쇽크를 준다. 『환상적 멜오드라마』에 환상 아닌 현실성을 가진 관중이 주는 심리는 이 한 소리로 고만 깨지기 시작한다. 그리고서는 막이 열닌다. 기대가 다 무엇이야. 생각할 여지 업시 빙빙도는 電氣風船, 箱子식의 明滅하는 오색의 광선, 이 R, U, R, 회사총무실을 둘너 싸고 잇는 공장과 기계의 규칙적의 금속성, 예감을 주는 새 세계의 소음, 모도가 나락으로 떠러저가는 마음처럼 환상 아닌 환상의 세계로 끌고 나간다. 벽에는 인조 노동자의 生産上의 저렴을 증명하는 비라 통계표가 걸녀 잇다. 이것부텀 이 작가의 皮肉으로 뵈인다. 멜오드라마의 세계에 대한 비판적의 냉기를 주는 것과도 갓다. 구미의 각국 주문자에게 답장을 타이푸라이트식히는 총지배인 또ㅡ 민(友田恭助)의 오만한 支配聲이 과학의 가능성에 대한 우리들의 회의를 작두로 집쓰리 내듯이 뚝뚝 끈어 부시기 시작한다. 대통령 영양 헬에나글오테(山本安英)가 빵 한 폰드에 생산비가 얼마 드는지 몰으면서 人類聯盟의 사명과 포부를 인류의 학대에셔 인조인간의 구제를 선전하기 시작하는 것이 얼마나 과학적 필연성으로 세상이 돌아가고 잇는가를 과학자 아닌 이 작가는 설명하려고 한다. 이것에는 중요한 시선이 가야할 곳인 줄 안다. 기계란 것은 생물학적으로 경제적 이유하에서 始源된다. 그러기 때문에 또ㅡ민 말과 가티 최상등의 노동자는 요구가 극소하고 安直하고 삐ㅡ루 먹을 줄도 몰으고 피아노 탈 줄도 몰으는 종류ㅡ 오늘 이 불완전한 실상의 인간 노동자보다는 2배 반의 능률을 가진 이상적 인조 노동자다. 또는 技能長 파브리(小杉義男) 말과 가티 근대의 노동과 늳�조를 가티하기 위한 「소년시대 업는」 노동자가 경제적으로 가쟝 효과잇는 것이다. 心理硏究所長 하루레마이어 박사(瀧澤修)는＜18＞ 이러케 말한다.

눈에 뵈이지 안는 수천만의 진군 발소리가 우뢰가티 들닐 때 우리가 승리어던 로봇트인 것 가티 두 다리가 제절로 움직거린다. 그만큼 이 멜오드라마의 클아이막스에 달한 것이다.＜23＞ 제3막은 로봇트의 세계가 된지 1년만에 그 회사 공장 내의 연구실 해방을 엇고 세계를 엇고 우주와 별을 통치하게 된 로봇트도 다만 한 가지의 능력이 잇다. 즉 생식이다. 20년이 그 수명이닛가 불과 몃 년 해서 세계를 통치하든 로봇트族은 자멸을 할 밧게 업다. 인간은 죄다 죽어버렷다. 다만 한 사람 늙은 알키스트만 남겨놋코. 로봇트에게 생식력이 업는 이상 로봇트 신제조를 하지 안으면 안된다. 이 秘法硏究를 마튼 알키스트는 주야 쉬일새 업시 실험실 안에 들어 안젓다. 그러나 그 팔은 떨니고 머리는 몽롱하다. 절망이다. 그러나 차펵크의 유일한 극적 결말ㅡ 새 아담과 이브가 남녀 두 로봇트 새이에 나타난다. 프리무스(伊達信)와 헬에나(山本安点) 두 로봇트는 서로 웃고 붓구러워 하고 뒤쫏고 입마초고 한다. 사랑이다. 사랑이 잇는 이상 새 생명은 다시 시작될 것이다.
이 최종막은 「곤충의 생활」 끗막에서도 그랫지만 챠액크의 최후의 常套手段이다. 그러나 좀 기운빠진 막이다. 알키스트인 丸山定夫의 절망적인 빠진 눈과 구버진 허리 떨리는 손 지옥 속에서 눌녀 나오는 그 목소리가 이 최후의 막을 살니기는 햇다. 山本安英의 액숀은 아주 살엇다. 이 여자는 全幕을 통하야 대통령 영양의 배역을 살닐려고 무척 애쓰는 점이 완연히 뵈인다. 그러나 웬일인지 인상이 깁지 안타. 지금 안저 생각한즉 그 적은 체격인 듯 십다. 부분적으로 살엇스나 전체적으로 인상이 깁지 안타. 이것은 헬에나가 그이에게 맛지 안는 까닭이다.
역에 마즌 점으로는 바스만에 扮한 東屋三郞. 또ㅡ민에 扮한 友田恭助도 그 오만자부에 챤 점으로 마젓스나 이 사람 특유한 혀긴 딕숀이 아주 서투럿다. 로봇트들의 자동인형식의 복장과 노동과 어조는 아주 잘되엿다. 무대장치는 吉田謙吉이가 한 것. 기하학적 構成派式의 이 장치가 어느 점까지는 성공햇다. 더구나 席幕에서 또ㅡ민이 필움을 응하야 공장, 제조소, 부두, 로봇트 노역상황을 설명해 뵈이는 것은 묘한 생각이다. 부조화한 곳 업시 잘 되엿다. 그러나 그것뿐 부당한 것은 서막에서 正式의 타이프라이터와 그 소리엿다. 무대 뒤 금속성의 공장의 진동음에 석겨 이 堅牢한 타이프라이터 소리가 나는 것이 아주 환상을 방해햇다. 제 1막과 2막의 위급한 인류멸망의 장면에서 배우들의 우고끼라든지 무대효과라든지 配光이라든지가 모도 조화잇게 템프에 맛게 연출된 것은 나이 젊은 연출자 土方 동지의 공이라 아니할 수 업다.(1926. 6. 30)＜24＞
