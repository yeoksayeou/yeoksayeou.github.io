Green Shade Cool Talk - To Chŏk
						Yarae

The story resumes from the time To Chŏk (盜跖) fled to Chŭngmŭk (卽墨). In Chŭngmŭk Castle, To Chŏk struck down and killed the newly rich I Sim (李心) and his son, who had come into wealth unjustly, with a single fist. He changed his name to I Chŏk (李跖) and fled immediately, traveling for five or six days by night. Along the way, on every street, notices were posted saying, "A reward of 1,000 nyang of silver will be given to whoever catches the murderous villain Tae Sŏng (大成). Anyone hiding the criminal will be punished the same as the villain." Although it was unknown how they found out, To Chŏk's appearance, height, and mannerisms were described in detail and attached to the notices. Every time To Chŏk saw a notice, he suppressed his pounding heart, disguised himself naturally, and soon entered the territory of Tansŏng (丹城) county, some three or four hundred ri away from Chŭngmŭk Castle. Thinking he had likely escaped the cordon, he breathed a long sigh of relief. One day, while crossing a large mountain pass whose name he didn't know, a man near the summit suddenly suffered from severe stomach pain. He cried out, "Save me!" looked at To Chŏk, and begged for help. Without even asking his name or background, To Chŏk hoisted the man onto his back, carried him over the pass, and reached the head of the Kojin River (古津江), about 20 ri away, where he found an inn. Upon entering, he immediately found the innkeeper, had medicine prepared, and gave it to the man. However, the man's illness did not easily subside. Waiting for the man to recover, To Chŏk ended up staying at the inn for five or six days. One morning, he woke up to find the sick man who had slept beside him gone. At first, To Chŏk thought he might have just gone outside and had no suspicions. But when there was no sign of him even by breakfast time, To Chŏk became suspicious. Looking around the room, he realized his own travel pack was missing. "Look at this!" he thought. As To Chŏk opened the door to go outside <81> and look for the man, he suddenly noticed a piece of a letter on the desk. To Chŏk picked up the unsealed letter and casually looked down at it. Written messily in a careless hand, the message read: "Benefactor, I am originally a thief. Now, this heartless thief cannot repay your kindness with kindness, but instead, due to compelling circumstances, must repay it with enmity. I have stolen your travel money of 200 nyang and the innkeeper's 300 nyang and am fleeing far away. I do not know if there will ever be a time to repay your kindness in the future." To Chŏk read the letter and laughed out loud to himself. He muttered, "It's the natural order of things for what exists to go to where there is nothing. But what a fool. What need was there to steal when he could have just spoken openly?" As he was muttering, the innkeeper came in through the door and asked urgently, "Hey, guest! Are you still sleeping?" To Chŏk replied, "Why, what is it?" and opened the door. The innkeeper said he had lost 300 nyang to a thief the previous night and asked about the sick man's whereabouts. To Chŏk, unsure what to say, mumbled for a moment and then explained the sick man's story. Hearing To Chŏk's words, the innkeeper became furious and said, "Look here, guest! Who knows if you were just helping the thief recover from his illness or if you're a thief traveling with him? In any case, all I care about is getting back my meal expenses and the 300 nyang I lost. If I can't, I will report you to the authorities as a thief for punishment!" He glared fiercely at To Chŏk. To Chŏk remained silent for a moment, thinking, then slowly opened his mouth, "Innkeeper, of course, there's no reason I should cause you loss. It's right that I should repay your money. However, as you can see, that heartless thief ran off with my travel pack too, so I don't have a single penny on me now. It's truly impossible for me to repay your money at this moment. Therefore, I will work off the debt as your hired hand in your house. What do you think?" The Innkeeper replied, "What kind of ridiculous talk is that? What loyalty would someone who travels with a thief have to pay off money with his labor? Stop talking nonsense and pay back my money quickly, then go wherever you please. If the officials find out, you'll be arrested as a thief before I even say anything!" To Chŏk lowered his voice further and spoke politely, "From your perspective, innkeeper, what you say is certainly right. However, please think again. If I were truly part of the thief's group, I would have fled with him during the night. The fact that I didn't proves I am a commoner. Furthermore, even if you accuse me <82> of being a thief, report me to the authorities, and have me killed, your loss remains a loss, bringing you no benefit. So instead, why not hire me and employ me for a few days or months? If it seems unprofitable, it won't be too late to take me to the authorities then. I hope you will consider deeply and grant me this chance." The innkeeper thought about it and found To Chŏk's words reasonable. Having no other choice, the innkeeper agreed to To Chŏk's proposal. From then on, To Chŏk received the innkeeper's pardon and became his hired hand. He was contracted to work for three years to pay off a total of 315 nyang, comprising the 300 nyang owed and 15 nyang for meals. The innkeeper ran both an inn and a tavern at the head of the Kojin River. Since the Kojin River was a ferry crossing point 30 ri from the border between the states of Chin (晋) and Che (齊), business was brisk and customers were plentiful. From the day he became the innkeeper's employee, To Chŏk spared no effort, tackling any arduous task with gusto. Gradually, the innkeeper came to understand To Chŏk's character and began to trust him. Initially confined to household chores and forbidden from going far, To Chŏk slowly earned enough trust to be sent on errands outside, even traveling 20 or 30 ri away with other employees for tasks like debt collection. One day, three new guests arrived. Listening to them, they claimed to be from Imja (臨淄) in Che, but To Chŏk discerned from their accents that they were likely from Chin. To Chŏk, who usually handled errands for the guest rooms, was familiar with the guests' origins and purposes. However, even after staying for 20 days, he couldn't figure out what business the new guests from Imja were conducting. Determined to find out what they were doing, To Chŏk deliberately became curious, observing their movements like a spy. Every two or three days, they would go somewhere else and then return to the inn as usual. One day, following the innkeeper's orders, To Chŏk went to the Kojin River ferry landing, 30 ri away, to fetch sorghum with a pack animal. After buying several sacks of sorghum, he stopped at a roadside tavern for a drink on his way back. While drinking, To Chŏk inadvertently overheard voices from the next room. It was clearly the voices of the guests from Imja staying at his master's inn. To Chŏk put down his cup and listened intently. One man said, "Wouldn't it be better to move to a different inn?" <83> Another replied, "No, the innkeeper is very good. He's a naive old man who knows nothing. There's no need to worry about the innkeeper, but that servant named I Chŏk seems suspicious no matter how I look at him. We shouldn't let him catch on, so I think it's best to move elsewhere." Then, another man took over, saying, "No. What you say makes sense, but in my opinion, although this I Chŏk doesn't seem like an ordinary man, just as you think, he says he's originally from the state of No (魯). Even if our activities are discovered, I Chŏk, being from No, would have no connection to Chin attacking Che. Not only that, but he's indebted to the innkeeper and working as his hired hand. Instead, we should try to coax him into revealing the situation in Che in detail. It might be much better than doing the reconnaissance ourselves. Also, our mission absolutely requires us to stay with this innkeeper, so you two just wait and follow my lead." Hearing this, To Chŏk could now clearly guess their situation. He decided to use this opportunity to cross the border into Chin. To Chŏk finished his drink, led the pack animal back to the inn, and secretly waited for the men to return. Around the third watch (11 PM - 1 AM), he saw one of the three men, the last one who had spoken, enter the inn and go to his room. To Chŏk went to the door and asked, "Guest, you've returned late?" and then, "Shall I bring you dinner?" The guest, unusually friendly, called To Chŏk and invited him into the room. To Chŏk entered the room, saying, "You are quite late today," and bowed. The guest replied, "Business affairs keep me busy," looked at To Chŏk, and motioned for him to sit. To Chŏk said, half-mockingly, "Busy is an understatement, isn't it? Even merchants dealing in goods are busy; how much busier must it be to trade in great nations?" He looked at the guest. The guest was startled by To Chŏk's words but forced himself not to show it. "What do you mean by that? I don't understand." He looked at To Chŏk. To Chŏk snorted once, then spoke again in a mocking tone, "You would say that, wouldn't you? If you look at everything in the world as trade, it's all trade. Small trade deals with goods. Big trade deals with nations. When Chin snatches land from Che, what bigger trade could there be?" He stood up as if to leave. The guest quickly raised his hand to cover To Chŏk's mouth, saying, "My good man, my good man! <84> I will reward you greatly. Please stop talking for a moment and listen to me." He then spoke again, "You must be a celestial spirit, how else could you know our affairs so *well? Indeed, I am not from Imja. I am Chang Chin-su (張陳秀), a military officer from Chin. There is currently friction between Chin and Che, and our state of Chin intends to attack Che. We were ordered by the ruler of Chin to come here and spy on Che's strengths and weaknesses. I heard you are from No, so you have no connection to our affairs. Also, you are indebted to the innkeeper and cannot return home. If you listen to me, there will be a great reward, and also a path for advancement. So, do not speak of this at all." He grasped To Chŏk's wrist and asked to become sworn brothers. Hearing this, To Chŏk now assumed a dignified expression, looked at Chang Chin-su, and said, "I too am a man of honor (好漢); how could I obstruct the general's mission? I was once caught due to the innkeeper's meal expenses after saving a thief, and have been working as a hired hand here ever since. If the general helps me out of goodwill, there will surely come a day when I repay the general with goodwill. If you offer me a great reward simply because I know your secret, that is not the act of a man of honor. So, general, worry not, carry out your mission well, and return." From that day on, Chang Chin-su and To Chŏk became close confidants. During the month Chang Chin-su stayed at the inn, To Chŏk often came to discuss all matters, gaining as much knowledge about Che's strengths and weaknesses as if he had seen them firsthand. Chang Chin-su was deeply impressed by To Chŏk's character. On the day of his departure, he wanted to take To Chŏk as an important retainer (門客) to open a path for his future advancement. After a month, Chang Chin-su completed his mission, paid off To Chŏk's debt, and returned to the capital of Chin together with To Chŏk.

To Chŏk followed Chang Chin-su and stayed in the capital of Chin. Not long after, war broke out between Chin and Che. Che was defeated, and Chin won a great victory. Among the Chin generals in this war, Chang Chin-su achieved the greatest merit. Due to this merit, Chang Chin-su became the commandant (鎭守) of Changmunjin (長門鎭), considered the most strategic fortress on the Chin-Che border. To Chŏk naturally followed Chang Chin-su and remained as his retainer. Chang Chin-su sometimes urged To Chŏk to become an official, but for some reason, To Chŏk stubbornly refused. Chang Chin-su felt sorry about this and said to To Chŏk, "With talent like yours, if you became an official, <85> you would surely achieve great success before long. What thoughts make you stubbornly refuse? Isn't making a name for oneself through meritorious deeds (功名揚裡) the proper path for a man born into this world?" He urged him strongly. Even upon receiving such sincere advice, To Chŏk's usual habit was to laugh heartily without answering. When he did occasionally open his mouth to reply, it was generally like this: "Yes, that's right. When a man is born into the world, achieving meritorious deeds is indeed a fine thing for him. However, human pleasure is not found solely in meritorious deeds. It can be found in seclusion (隱逸), in knight-errantry (遊俠), in studying the Way (道學), and even in banditry (盜賊). Moreover, each person has their own innate nature (天性), so if one does what one wants according to one's nature and then dies, that's enough. Being subservient to others, unable to express one's will or connect emotionally (情), is also not the Way (道). Furthermore, being born into a chaotic world, isn't it more exhilarating for a man to rebel against it and fight righteously (義氣) until death, rather than conforming to it?" Thus, he brushed off Chang Chin-su's advice. To Chŏk's original nature was not only ashamed of being subordinate to others, but he was also exceedingly intelligent and worldly-wise. Therefore, he held dissatisfaction upon dissatisfaction with all affairs of the society and state at the time, thinking that viewing all matters as acts of rebellion was most fitting for his character.

Chang Chin-su's fame grew immensely for a time. While serving as the Commandant of Changmunjin due to his merits in defeating Che, as his reputation grew daily, many among the court officials of Chin became jealous and watched for an opportunity to bring him down. Among them, Kung Tae-ryŏ (弓大呂), a favored courtier (嬖臣) of the Chin ruler, had long been at odds with Chang Chin-su and constantly sought ways to frame him by observing his actions. This happened a year after the end of the Chin-Che war. Chin and Che had restored their former peace. At that time, due to some matter concerning both states, the ruler of Chin needed to send an envoy to Che. Kung Tae-ryŏ suggested to the ruler that Chang Chin-su be appointed as the envoy. When Chang Chin-su arrived in Che, the ruler of Che received him with the courtesy due to a feudal lord (王侯). Chang Chin-su was surprised by the excessive treatment but did not harbor any particular suspicion and returned. A few months after Chang Chin-su's return, accusations arose in the court, instigated by Kung Tae-ryŏ, claiming that Chang Chin-su had conspired with Che to betray Chin. Chang Chin-su was framed for treason and sentenced to death. Originally, after the Chin-Che war, Che feared Chin primarily because they dreaded Chang Chin-su's strategic talent. Thus, wanting to drive Chang Chin-su out of Chin, Che had already sent many spies to investigate Chin's internal affairs. <86> Fortunately, they discovered the enmity between the powerful Chin official Kung Tae-ryŏ and Chang Chin-su. They bribed Kung Tae-ryŏ heavily and, at the same time, had Kung Tae-ryŏ persuade the ruler to appoint Chang Chin-su as the envoy to Che. Che then received Chang Chin-su with the courtesy of a feudal lord, making it seem as if a secret treaty had been made, and spread this rumor in the Chin court. Consequently, Chang Chin-su fell victim to Kung Tae-ryŏ's plot and suffered the unjust extermination of his clan (三族之禍). After Chang Chin-su was executed, To Chŏk cursed the world even more and came to despise the unjust and unrighteous exercise of power by the state at that time. Driven by an irrepressible sense of chivalry (義俠心), he entered the capital of Chin to avenge Chang Chin-su. To Chŏk changed his name and secretly stayed in Chinyang (晋陽), seeking to assassinate Kung Tae-ryŏ. He spent a great deal of money befriending Kung Tae-ryŏ's retainers and waited for an opportunity. Just then, Kung Tae-ryŏ accompanied the ruler on a hunting trip in the Western Garden (西苑). To Chŏk saw his chance as Kung Tae-ryŏ was returning and ambushed him. Unfortunately, Kung Tae-ryŏ was not killed; the shot mistakenly hit the accompanying carriage (副車). To Chŏk resolved to try again later and fled aimlessly into the night.

To Chŏk now had to cross the border of Chin again to survive. This time, it was truly different from the incident of killing I Sim in Chŭngmŭk. By order of the state, notices demanding the capture of the heinous criminal I Chŏk were posted throughout the country, and the national police (巡捕廳) were mobilized, setting up an inescapable dragnet. To Chŏk left the Chin capital at night, slept in forests during the day, and traveled through mountainous regions, advancing over 100 ri each night. After four days of this, he reached a mountain valley in Wiyang County (維陽郡) of Chin. Having lost sleep for several nights and crossed treacherous mountains, even the physically robust To Chŏk now lacked the strength to go further. Moreover, he was hungry and thirsty, forcing him to look for habitation to find food. To Chŏk descended the mountain for several ri and saw a main road running north-south. By the roadside, the flag of a moderately sized tavern caught his eye. To Chŏk felt both relief and fear. However, he felt he couldn't endure without buying a drink, even if it meant risking death. So, he slowly approached the tavern, observed the situation inside, and saw that there were no customers at the moment. To Chŏk relaxed and entered the tavern. He ordered a bowl of rice and a bottle of wine. As he finished eating and was about to leave, a robust man (壯漢) came out from inside, belching and rubbing his large belly. He looked intently at To Chŏk, approached him closely, cupped his hands in greeting, and said, "Excuse my impertinence, sir, but <87> where have you come from, and what is your esteemed name?" To Chŏk suppressed his startled heart and replied, "Yes, I am traveling to Wiyang county. My surname is Yu (柳), and my given name is Chŏng (精). You seem to be the owner; may I ask your esteemed name?" The robust man said, "That's right. I am the owner of this place. My name is Chu Chŏng-yang (朱正陽)." He paused as if wanting to say more, then bowed respectfully and said, "Benefactor, where have you come from? I am the thief who received your kindness years ago at Kojin River in Che. They say 'Men meet everywhere' (男子何處不相逢); meeting you today in my home is truly unexpected beyond measure." With tears welling up, he stood up and bowed again. Only then did To Chŏk realize. He stood up, grasped his hand, and said, "This is indeed unexpected. Meeting here feels like a dream. But anyway, how did this come about?" A look of pleasure appeared on his face. Chu Chŏng-yang urged To Chŏk, as if something urgent had happened, "Quickly, come into the room—let's talk slowly. Lately, a state criminal has emerged in our country, and officials are constantly passing this way trying to catch him?" He led To Chŏk into a room. Chu Chŏng-yang explained his past to To Chŏk, the gist of which was this: In his twenties, Chu Chŏng-yang killed the man who was his father's enemy and fled, joining a band of thieves. He lived as a thief for ten years. Years ago, one of his comrades was caught by officials and imprisoned in Wiyang, facing death. Chu Chŏng-yang tried to raise 1,000 nyang of silver to bribe for his comrade's release but couldn't find a way. He went to seek help from a friend (親故) in Che, but that friend was also suffering from illness, so he couldn't get a single penny. On his way back, he suffered the stomach ache, received To Chŏk's help, and then stole To Chŏk's 200 nyang and the innkeeper's 300 nyang to save his friend's life. Chu Chŏng-yang was now subordinate to Maeng Ch'ŏng (猛靑), the leader of a bandit gang whose stronghold (山寨) was in Mount Kyegwan (鷄冠山) in Wiyang. He served as Maeng Ch'ŏng's lookout (探報役), running this tavern to plunder travelers' valuables (*掠する). Hearing Chu Chŏng-yang's story, To Chŏk <88> in turn explained his own background and revealed that he was the very heinous criminal Chin was trying to catch. Chu Chŏng-yang was astonished again. "Ah! I never dreamed it was you! It was several days ago, sir. Officials came from the authorities and stayed at my place, searching every traveler. I didn't know who the criminal was, but I felt great sympathy and thought that if I ever met him, I would help him with all my might. <88> But if my benefactor is that very person, what worries could there be?" To Chŏk asked, "You say you have a way to save me, brother (賢弟). Do you have some clever plan?" Chu replied, "Benefactor, if you just listen to me, saving your life will be easy. It's nothing else but, as I mentioned earlier, I am a subordinate of the great thief Maeng Ch'ŏng. Maeng Ch'ŏng currently has his stronghold in Mount Kyegwan with 3,000 followers. Benefactor, without further ado, if you join them there, it would be the safest plan (萬全之策)." To Chŏk asked, "Then, what is the method for joining the thieves?" Chu replied, "I also handle that duty. If you go with my certificate, there's no way you'll be rejected. Besides, benefactor, your courage and strength (勇力) are extraordinary. If you join the stronghold, you'll surely be entrusted with important responsibilities soon. Since the matter is urgent, benefactor, it's best not to linger here but go straight up to the stronghold." To Chŏk agreed to Chu Chŏng-yang's suggestion to join the stronghold.

To Chŏk obtained a letter of guarantee and introduction from Chu Chŏng-yang and set off for Mount Kyegwan. Mount Kyegwan was located 60 ri away from Chu Chŏng-yang's place. To Chŏk walked slowly and entered the stronghold that evening. Mount Kyegwan was indeed a naturally fortified location (天險之地). Behind it, the great mountain range of Kyegwan stood like a folding screen. In front, layered cliffs stretched for 20 ri. On the way, To Chŏk was interrogated three times by bandit sentries. Finally, reaching the main gate of the stronghold, he explained his situation to the gatekeeper, obtained permission, and entered. Guided by a soldier (徒卒), To Chŏk first sought out the stronghold's chief administrator (都總), Chŏng Cha-yŏng (鄭子榮), gave him Chu Chŏng-yang's letter of introduction, and sought to meet Maeng Ch'ŏng. Chŏng Cha-yŏng was very pleased, went to see Maeng Ch'ŏng, and returned, saying, "You must be tired from your long journey today, and it's getting dark. The General says to meet tomorrow morning." He then led To Chŏk to his own room, served him wine and meat, and let him stay the night. Around noon the next day, To Chŏk entered Maeng Ch'ŏng's headquarters (營門). Maeng Ch'ŏng greeted To Chŏk warmly, invited him to sit in the guest seat, and after exchanging greetings, slowly began to speak, "Elder brother (老兄), I have heard much about you before, and especially through the introduction of my subordinate Chu Chŏng-yang, I am well aware of your lofty integrity (節操). Meeting you like this today, I am grateful beyond words." He offered a cup of tea. ** To Chŏk raised his eyes, looked Maeng Ch'ŏng directly in the face, then lowered his head again, bowed, and said, "To receive such generous words <89> is too great an honor for a mere beggar wandering aimlessly." To Chŏk continued, explaining his situation, and addressed Maeng Ch'ŏng in a respectful tone, "General, if you would show me favor, I wish you would allow me to stay in the stronghold (由寨) as one of your soldiers. Although I lack talent, I will do my utmost to repay the General's kindness (厚誼)." He pleaded to be allowed to stay in the stronghold. Hearing this, Maeng Ch'ŏng nodded thoughtfully and said, "How could someone like you, Elder brother, stay in a place like this stronghold? A stronghold is inherently a den of thieves; it's no place for a scholar who has learned benevolence and righteousness (仁義) in the world. Therefore, I'm not telling you to go elsewhere immediately given your current predicament as a fugitive, but perhaps you could stay in the stronghold for a while and then slowly return to your hometown to pursue meritorious deeds. That seems more appropriate." To Chŏk was unexpectedly taken aback by these words. He reiterated the sincerity of his intentions and forcefully stated his desire to end his life in the stronghold. Maeng Ch'ŏng, unable to refuse To Chŏk's urgent plea, told him to return to the inn for a while. Three days later, he summoned To Chŏk again and said, "Elder brother, I understand your true intentions and your situation well. However, our stronghold is originally a small place, the number of people is large, and income is scarce, so we cannot*** increase the number of people. It's very regrettable, but I've arranged to give you 500 nyang of silver here. It's a small amount, but please take this and go elsewhere." He took out a money pouch from under his seat. Hearing this, To Chŏk's anger flared to the heavens (怒氣衝天), and he wanted to strike Maeng Ch'ŏng with his fist. However, suppressing his anger, he picked up the money, looked at Maeng Ch'ŏng, and said, "Excuse me, General. I came to see you, not considering the thousand ri distance too far, for reasons other than hoping for 500 nyang of silver. And although the stronghold may be small, accommodating one person like me is like adding a molehill (邱垤) to the mountain. Since the General misunderstands my situation like this, I cannot comprehend your intentions." He spoke forcefully. Before Maeng Ch'ŏng could reply, Chŏng Cha-yŏng looked at To Chŏk and said, "Brother, don't be too angry. You speak without yet knowing the rules of the stronghold. The General's suggestion for you to go elsewhere is not because he doesn't understand your situation, but because he fears you might not be able to comply with the stronghold's 'Entry Regulation' (入寨定例). If you violate the Entry Regulation, the stronghold's discipline will fall into disarray?" To Chŏk asked, "What is <90> this Entry Regulation?" Chŏng replied, "It's nothing other than this: anyone wishing to enter this stronghold must, within 15 days, go down into the world and plunder money from a wealthy household. Only those who do so are admitted. Violating this rule results in death." Hearing this, To Chŏk said, "If such a rule exists, how could I possibly join without complying? Though I lack talent (才操), I will not violate that rule. Please allow me to stay." To Chŏk wrote a pledge not to violate the regulation, presented it to Maeng Ch'ŏng, and was allowed to stay in the stronghold. Originally, Maeng Ch'ŏng told To Chŏk to go elsewhere without mentioning the regulation, and also set an unusually short period of 15 days (when the actual rule was 3 or 5 months), for no other reason than his wicked nature of despising those superior to himself (勝己者). Having heard of To Chŏk's reputation for bravery (勇名) for a long time, and seeing upon meeting him that he possessed extraordinary talent and courage (絶人之才勇), Maeng Ch'ŏng became jealous and schemed to send To Chŏk away.

Seven days had already passed since To Chŏk arrived at the stronghold. Now, according to the stronghold's rules, he had to leave to acquire wealth. Taking four or five soldiers, he descended the mountain, conspired with Chu Chŏng-yang, and effortlessly raided the house of a wealthy person somewhere in Wiyang, obtaining several thousand nyang of silver. To Chŏk's heroic reputation startled the ears of the 3,000 members of the stronghold. To Chŏk himself was newly aware that his courage and strength were so astonishing. As To Chŏk's reputation grew daily, Maeng Ch'ŏng became increasingly jealous of him. To Chŏk, too, not only sensed Maeng Ch'ŏng's attitude but also developed a strong aversion to all of Maeng Ch'ŏng's actions. To Chŏk considered Maeng Ch'ŏng a very contemptible fellow for various reasons, but a couple of examples suffice. First, when To Chŏk brought back 2,000 nyang in gold according to the rules, Maeng Ch'ŏng took half for himself, distributed half of the remainder among those who contributed, and put the rest into the communal funds. Also, he alone kept dozens of beautiful women as concubines while forbidding others from doing so. To Chŏk once heard from a soldier that one of Maeng Ch'ŏng's subordinates had captured a beauty in Che, but Maeng Ch'ŏng killed the subordinate and took the woman as his own concubine. Because of this, the subordinates deeply resented his character but were intimidated by Maeng Ch'ŏng's bravery and ferocity (勇猛) and merely obeyed for now. Thus, one day, Chu Chŏng-yang secretly spoke to To Chŏk: "This <91> sounds very improper, but the truth must be told. In reality, Maeng Ch'ŏng is a very merciless person, and the situation is such that we cannot possibly serve him as the king of the stronghold. Why don't you, my benefactor, win over the hearts of all the men, kill Maeng Ch'ŏng, and become the king of the stronghold?" he said frankly. To Chŏk told him not to speak of such things at all, but secretly, he had already conspired with Chu Chŏng-yang, Chŏng Cha-yŏng, I Am (李岩), and several other brave warriors, planning to act when the opportunity arose, several days prior.

The relationship between Maeng Ch'ŏng and To Chŏk deteriorated day by day. Then, one day, a grand feast was held in the stronghold. This feast was an event held several times a year where the entire group gathered to celebrate together. In the large square in front of the stronghold headquarters gate, the 3,000 members were assembled. Maeng Ch'ŏng sat in the highest seat, followed by several of his key subordinates. To Chŏk was seated in the fifth position. When the drinking reached a halfway point, Maeng Ch'ŏng suddenly addressed To Chŏk, "What merit do you have to sit in the fifth position? You are still a newcomer (新進小生). There is seniority even in the stronghold. How can you be so brazenly presumptuous (傍若無人)? Leave your seat immediately and go down!" he ordered. This, needless to say, stemmed from Maeng Ch'ŏng's intense jealousy. Hearing this, To Chŏk's anger flared, his eyes widened as he glared at Maeng Ch'ŏng, "General, what kind of courteous words are these? I know your intentions, General. Why do you hate me so intensely?" He clenched his fists. Before Maeng Ch'ŏng could speak, a subordinate sitting beside him rebuked To Chŏk and told him to leave his seat quickly. Maeng Ch'ŏng drew his sword (釰) and charged directly at To Chŏk. To Chŏk leaped over a screen behind him, drew his sword, and met Maeng Ch'ŏng's blade. The 3,000 onlookers stood frozen, watching the clash between Maeng Ch'ŏng and To Chŏk. Maeng Ch'ŏng leaped ten chang (丈, a unit of length, roughly 3 meters) into the air, jumped over To Chŏk's head, and struck at the nape of his neck. To Chŏk instantly slipped under Maeng Ch'ŏng's leaping legs and deftly parried his sword. When Maeng Ch'ŏng moved east, To Chŏk was already in the west in an instant. When Maeng Ch'ŏng stood south, To Chŏk appeared in the north before one could blink. The skills (才操) of the two men seemed truly evenly matched (막상막하). However, frankly speaking, Maeng Ch'ŏng was a simpleminded, brave general (愚直한 勇將), while To Chŏk was a versatile warrior excelling in both talent and courage (才勇이 전능한 勇士). For a long while, the bodies of the two men were obscured in the flash of swords. Suddenly, To Chŏk's body soared 10 chang into the air. With a great roar (大喝一聲), Maeng Ch'ŏng's head fell to the ground. After a moment, the flashing swords disappeared. To Chŏk wiped his sweat, faced the crowd, and shouted loudly, <92> "Listen, everyone! I have now killed the unrighteous Maeng Ch'ŏng and become the general of the stronghold! Those who heed my words will follow me and become my subordinates. Those who do not heed my words shall fight me to the death!" At once, the crowd came before To Chŏk, asking him to be their general. Chu Chŏng-yang, Chŏng Cha-yŏng, and all the others supported To Chŏk, seated him in the highest position, and had the crowd perform the formal ceremony. The next day**, To Chŏk gathered the crowd again, held a great three-day banquet, brought out the treasures from the storehouse, distributed them equally among the members, and liberated the women, telling them to return to their respective homes. He then established new regulations for the stronghold, the main points being: All property in the stronghold shall be communal property for common use. Seize from the rich and aid the poor. Do not kill people unnecessarily, but show no mercy and end the lives of unrighteous officials. Love comrades and do not envy them. In dangerous situations, enter before others and leave after others. Anyone caught by official law, regardless of who they are, shall be rescued. Several such articles were established. The members of the stronghold were astonished and overjoyed, pledging absolute obedience to To Chŏk's commands. Within a few months of To Chŏk becoming the king of the stronghold, heroes (好漢) from all under heaven heard the news and began gathering, bringing the number of members to over 9,000. As the numbers grew, the stronghold at Mount Kyegwan became too small to accommodate everyone. So, people were sent out widely to find a new, suitable stronghold, and Mount Yang'o-rŭng (陽五陵山) in T'aesan* (太山), Che, was designated. (*Assuming 太励� is a corruption of 太山 T'aesan, Mount Tai)

After To Chŏk moved the stronghold to Orŭngsan (五陵山), the lands and people within a radius of over 100 ri became his devoted followers. He seized the unjustly accumulated wealth of kings, dukes, generals, ministers, and the rich and powerful, using it to relieve the poor (窮恤). The poor commoners of the lower classes praised To Chŏk like a sage (聖人), while the rich and noble of the upper classes feared him like a wolf (豺狼). Officials began calling him by the name To Chŏk (盜跖, Bandit Chŏk), instead of his real name, I Chŏk. This is how the nickname To Chŏk originated. Occasionally, officials sent spies secretly or attempted to attack the stronghold to destroy To Chŏk's den, but Orŭngsan was originally a naturally fortified place, making it inaccessible due to geography. To Chŏk's 9,000 followers were like his own limbs, making them uncontrollable by the ruler (人君). And To Chŏk's strategic brilliance (勇略) was extraordinarily peerless, making victory unattainable through military strategy. Small states didn't even dare to peek, and even a large state like Che couldn't guarantee victory unless it mobilized its entire national strength to attack him. To Chŏk's strategy <93> and the harmony among his people (人和) were so strong, as exemplified by the following incident. To Chŏk had always harbored the thought of avenging Chang Chin-su by killing the Chin minister Kung Tae-ryŏ after his initial failure, and it never left his mind. He was looking for an opportunity to act. Just then, Che planned to launch a war of revenge against Chin, taking advantage of the opportunity created by Chang Chin-su's death. <94> Thus, war broke out again between Chin and Che, and this time, Kung Tae-ryŏ volunteered to lead the expedition. To Chŏk seized this chance and secretly sent 5,000 of his bravest followers into Chin as volunteer soldiers, positioning them within Kung Tae-ryŏ's army. As the Chin-Che war intensified and reached a critical point, a mutiny broke out within Kung Tae-ryŏ's camp. Kung Tae-ryŏ was killed, leading to a major defeat for the Chin army. This was all To Chŏk's stratagem. When news of this spread, To Chŏk's heroic reputation shook the world. Che even sent an envoy to recruit To Chŏk, but To Chŏk sternly rebuked the envoy and sent him back.

On a certain Year, Month, Day, To Chŏk led his subordinates to Imja in Che, successfully carried out a raid for wealth, and returned to the stronghold. It was two days later. That day, To Chŏk felt somewhat tired, ate breakfast late, inspected the stronghold once, and returned to his room around noon. A gatekeeper came in from outside and reported, "A man named Kong Ch'iu (孔丘) from No wishes to see the General briefly and sends his regards." To Chŏk, who rarely showed anger, visibly changed color upon hearing the name Kong Ch'iu, his displeasure apparent. "Kong Ch'iu, Kong Ch'iu... why would that man come to this dangerous place?" he muttered to himself. Chu Chŏng-yang, standing beside him, looked at To Chŏk and asked, "General, Kong Ch'iu? Do you mean the Kong Ch'iu from No who is called a Sage (聖者)?" To Chŏk grew even angrier. "Yes, that's right! The one the world calls a Sage, whom we can call a Hypocrite (僞者), the Great Deceiver (大詐欺家) Kong Ch'iu, has come." He looked back at the gatekeeper. "Go out and relay my words to Kong Ch'iu. Tell him this: Kong Ch'iu and I originally have different principles (主義) and our paths (道) diverge, so there's no need to meet. Even if we met, I would have nothing to listen to. Not only that, but I have a peculiar trait: once my anger erupts, I only calm down after killing someone. If his life is precious, tell him to return quickly." The gatekeeper waited a moment, then came back in. "General, Kong Ch'iu insists on seeing you just once before leaving. He says he would have no regrets even if he died after seeing you once. He also says he has a message entrusted by your elder brother, <94> Master Yu Ha-hye (柳下惠), and earnestly requests an audience." To Chŏk seemed reluctant. "If that's the case, it can't be helped. Tell him to enter." He fell silent for a moment. Kong Ch'iu, guided by the gatekeeper, entered To Chŏk's room. As mentioned before, Kong Ch'iu had left No to meet To Chŏk at Orŭngsan. After parting with the old man on the way, Kong Ch'iu entered the stronghold the next day. As he entered, Kong Ch'iu observed the 9,000 inhabitants living in orderly and well-structured peace and felt as if he had entered a strange paradise. After inquiring about the detailed situation of the stronghold from several people, he was newly impressed by To Chŏk's character. He thought that if he could somehow turn To Chŏk towards the path of Benevolence and Righteousness (仁義之道), he had the character to become a Great Sage (大聖者). Kong Ch'iu was greatly startled when the gatekeeper initially told him it was best to leave quickly if he valued his life, but having come from afar (不遠千里), he summoned his courage and entered To Chŏk's room. To Chŏk remained seated, legs sprawled, glaring at Kong Ch'iu with round eyes. Kong Ch'iu approached To Chŏk but dared not look him directly in the face. He clasped his hands, bowed deeply from the waist, touching his nose to the ground, and offered a polite greeting. To Chŏk did not return the greeting. "Kong Ch'iu, come closer," his voice was like the roar of a great tiger intimidating beasts in the mountains. His nine-ch'i height, stout build, tiger's head, swallow's chin, phoenix eyes, wolf's waist, bear's back, and monkey's arms truly marked him as a great hero of the world. Kong Ch'iu replied, "Yes," and took a few steps forward. To Chŏk opened his mouth, "Kong Ch'iu! Listen! What do you have to say that you wished to see me? You artificially created a false Way of Benevolence and Righteousness, recklessly invoked the Former Kings, foolishly spoke of King Wen (文王) and King Wu (武王), fashioned a crown like tree branches for your head, wore a belt made from dead ox bones around your waist, ate without plowing, clothed yourself without weaving, wandered uselessly throughout the world speaking of right and wrong, good and evil, confusing the people of the world, deceiving the scholars of the world, making people forget the foundation of heaven, earth, and great nature, causing them to engage in strife and dispute. What is even more detestable is your talk of worthless Filial Piety, Brotherly Duty, Loyalty, and Trustworthiness (孝悌忠信), bowing and scraping before the lords of various states, seeking wealth and honor. Your crimes are extremely grave! With what face do you see me? And what do you have to say to me? Obey my will, and I will let you live and leave. Go against my will, and your life <95> will be ended. Know this, and speak if you have something to say." Hearing To Chŏk's words, Kong Ch'iu realized that To Chŏk was indeed eloquent, clear in reasoning, and possessed a fierce temperament. However, seeing that he must comply with To Chŏk's wishes, he decided to first praise him with fine words and then state his request. After a moment of silence, he slowly began, "General, Kong Ch'iu has heard that there are three kinds of virtue (德) under heaven. The first is that a person is born naturally with a handsome face and a fine, tall physique, admired by young and old, noble and common alike; this is the highest virtue (上德). The second is possessing lofty knowledge, understanding heavenly principles through reason, and articulating the principles of all things through speech; this is the middle virtue (中德). The third is possessing courage and decisiveness, gathering followers and commanding troops to build power; this is the lower virtue (下德). If a person possesses even one of these three virtues, they are worthy to face south and become a ruler. Now, General, you possess all three virtues. Your stature is eight ch'i and two ch'on, your face radiates light, your lips are like bright cinnabar (激丹), your teeth are like rows of melon seeds (박시), your knowledge is capable, and your eloquence is sufficient. For such a person of character to be known by the world with the name 'Bandit' (盜) as in To Chŏk is something I find deeply regrettable. Now, General, if you follow Kong Ch'iu's words, although Kong Ch'iu lacks talent and virtue, I will personally plead your case: south to the states of O (吳) and Wŏl (越), north to Che (齊) and No (魯), east to Song (宋) and Wi (衛), and west as an envoy to Chin (晋) and Ch'o (楚). I can arrange for you, General, to possess land spanning thousands of ri and govern tens of thousands of households, elevating you to the rank of feudal lord (諸侯). At that time, General, you can disband your troops, educate the people, offer sacrifices to your ancestral spirits, and practice the kingly way (王道). Would this not be the conduct of a sage and something desired by all people under heaven?" Hearing this, To Chŏk became greatly enraged, glared at Kong Ch'iu, and said, "Kong Ch'iu! Listen carefully to my words! Those who can be tempted with gain (利) and admonished with harm (害) are ordinary people (凡夫). Such talk is unfit to be directed at me! Listen! You praise my appearance and character. This is a great virtue from nature (天然之大德), which came about naturally, not because of your words. Even if you didn't praise me, I wouldn't be unaware of it myself. I have also heard that those who enjoy praising people to their faces also enjoy slandering them. Your praise of me now indicates you are someone who will slander me in the future. Is this not utterly despicable? Furthermore, you <96> speak of me possessing tens of ri of land and hundreds of thousands of households. This is using the ways of ordinary people to entice me. Listen! No city is greater than the world itself. Yet, even sage-kings like Yao (堯) and Shun (舜), who possessed the world, had no place for their descendants to stand an awl (立錐之地) after their deaths. T'ang (湯) and Wu (武), who became Sons of Heaven, were later extinguished. Is this not because the benefit (利) of possessing the world is immense? Because the benefit is great, those who wish to seize it are many. Therefore, those who take great benefit incur great disaster (禍). This is not the way to nurture life (養生之道)." To Chŏk drank a cup of tea, gave a loud cough, and continued, "I have heard that in high antiquity, beasts were many and people few. The people of that time built nests in trees, ate wild fruits during the day, and slept in the trees at night. Thus, they are called the people of the time of Youchao-shi (有巢氏, Lord of Nests). Later, people discovered fire, warmed themselves by it in winter, and began to cook fruits. These are called the 'people who knew life' (知生之民). In the time of Shennong-shi (神農氏, Divine Farmer), people were transformed by ultimate virtue (至德). They rested when they rested and rose when they rose, all according to nature. People knew their mothers but not their fathers. They plowed to eat, wove to clothe themselves, dug wells to drink, lived together with deer (麋鹿) without harming them. People helped each other, harboring no thoughts of harm or plunder. There were no laws, yet nothing to transgress; no rules, yet nothing to steal. The whole world was at peace (太平). This was the age of ultimate virtue. However, starting from the era of the Yellow Emperor, Xuanyuan-shi (黃帝 軒轅氏), the world began to descend into chaos. The Yellow Emperor fought with Ch'i You (蚩尤) in the wilds of Zhuolu (涿鹿之野), shedding blood for a hundred ri. In the time of Yao and Shun, the distinction between ruler and minister was strictly established, and various laws began to be made. Thus, the world became even more chaotic. King T'ang drove out his sovereign and seized the world. King Wu killed Zhou (紂) and seized the state. From then on, the great devoured the small, the strong preyed on the weak. Large fish ate medium fish, and medium fish ate small fish. The world became a turbid and chaotic place (濁亂世界). Speaking of which, from T'ang and Wu onwards, all can be seen as agents of chaos (亂人). Yet now, you speak of the Way of Wen and Wu, using it to teach later generations throughout the world, misleading the rulers of the world, striving to seek wealth and honor. If we speak of bandits (盜), there is no greater bandit than you, yet the world does not call you Bandit Ch'iu (盜丘) but calls me Bandit Chŏk (盜跖). This is because the world is chaotic and has lost its natural disposition (自然之本性).

Also, listen! You persuaded Cha-ro (子路) with sweet words to accept your teachings, but ultimately, when Cha-ro attempted to kill the Lord of Wei (衛君), <97> the plot failed, and Cha-ro killed himself. Isn't this because your teachings were inadequate? And yet you call yourself a talented scholar and sage? When you traveled among the feudal states seeking wealth and honor, you were expelled from No, driven out of Wei (衛), cornered in Che (齊), and humiliated in Ch'en (陳) and Ts'ai (蔡), unable to find a place for yourself in the world. Many of your disciples also met with disaster. What value does your Way possess?

Also, listen! Among those the world deems lofty, none surpasses the Yellow Emperor. Yet even the Yellow Emperor could not perfect virtue and started wars, killing people. Yao was unkind (無慈), abandoning his son. Shun was unfilial (不孝), having friction with his father. Yü (禹) drove out Shun. T'ang drove out his sovereign. King Wu killed Zhou. King Wen was imprisoned at Youli (羑里). These six men are all deemed lofty by the world, yet they abandoned truth for profit, failed to follow their natural feelings (性情), and troubled the people. Their actions are truly shameful. And those the world calls worthy scholars (賢士): Po I (伯夷) and Shu Ch'i (叔齊) starved to death on Mount Shouyang (首陽山). A man named Shen Tu (申徒), because the ruler would not listen to his remonstrance, drowned himself holding a stone. Chieh Tzu-t'ui (介子推) was burned to death. Such examples are countless. These men utterly failed to consider the fundamentals and did not follow the way of nurturing their allotted lifespan (壽命).

Also, listen! The world calls me To Chŏk. Doesn't 'To' (盜, bandit) mean taking others' property without payment? In this sense, I am not ashamed to bear the bandit name To Chŏk. However, even among bandits, isn't there also a proper way (道)? Taking from the wealthy versus taking from the poor, taking from the guiltless versus taking from the guilty—these have distinctions. Taking ill-gotten gains versus taking what is not ill-gotten—these also have distinctions. Taking intangible spirit versus taking tangible substance—all have distinctions. From this perspective, I am a thief who takes from the rich to aid the poor, who takes from the guilty and the unjust to aid the innocent and the just. Therefore, though called a bandit, I can be seen as a righteous bandit (義賊). But look at the world! The rich take from the poor without labor—is this not unrighteous theft? Officials seize property from the innocent—are they not public bandits? So-called sages like you concoct 'benevolence and righteousness' to rob people of their innate nature—are you not thieves who steal the spirit? Furthermore, states conquer states, the strong subjugate the weak—are these not the greatest of bandits? <98> Think about it! Who is guilty, and who is innocent?

Also, listen! At best, a person lives a hundred years for the upper lifespan (上壽), eighty for the middle (中壽), and no more than sixty for the lower (下壽). Even within that short time, subtracting days spent sleeping, days of illness, days of worry and concern, the days one opens one's mouth and laughs amount to no more than four or five in a month. Heaven and earth are boundless, but human life has its time. Entrusting a life with its time to the boundless heaven and earth is faster than a swift horse passing a crack in the door. Now, if we humans do not understand principles, cannot gladden our natural dispositions (性情), and cannot nurture our allotted lifespan, we are all people who have not attained the Way (道).

To Chŏk finished speaking and looked at Kong Ch'iu. "Go back quickly by this road. And after you return, repent and stop deceiving the people. Properly nurture your innate nature. Hey, Kong Ch'iu! Did you hear me well? Why are you silent?" Kong Ch'iu merely said, "I will do as commanded." He stood up again, bowed twice more, came out to the gate, and mounted his chariot. His face was ashen like death (死灰), and his vision blurred. An Yŏn (顔淵) and Cha Kong (子貢) escorted Kong Ch'iu, driving the chariot back to No, arriving around the end of that month. <99>

**Summary**

This installment of the "Green Shade Cool Talk" series continues the story of To Chŏk (盜跖), a legendary Chinese rebel figure. After fleeing Chŭngmŭk, To Chŏk is betrayed by a man he helps, forced into servitude, but gains freedom by aiding Chin spies against Che. He joins a bandit group on Mount Kyegwan, eventually kills the jealous leader Maeng Ch'ŏng in a duel, and takes command. Establishing new, egalitarian rules focusing on aiding the poor and punishing corrupt officials ("righteous banditry"), his fame grows, attracting 9,000 followers. They relocate to the larger, strategic Orŭngsan. The climax is the famous confrontation where Confucius (Kong Ch'iu) visits, attempts to persuade To Chŏk to adopt conventional morality and seek legitimate power, but is thoroughly rebuked. To Chŏk delivers a powerful critique of established authority, hypocrisy ("sage" kings were flawed, Confucius himself is the biggest "bandit" for stealing people's innate nature), the pursuit of fame and power, and advocates living according to one's natural disposition. Confucius leaves utterly defeated.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article, published in the nationalist magazine *Gaebyeok* in 1926, is highly suggestive. The figure of To Chŏk, the righteous bandit challenging corrupt authority (feudal lords, hypocritical sages) and redistributing wealth, could easily serve as an allegory for resistance against the perceived injustices and oppressive nature of Japanese colonial rule or broader social inequalities. To Chŏk's rejection of established power structures and Confucian morality (often seen as underpinning traditional hierarchies) in favor of innate nature and direct action would resonate with readers seeking alternative models of justice and autonomy. Historians might find the detailed philosophical argument given to To Chŏk, particularly his critique of historical figures and the concept of "righteous banditry," indicative of the intellectual currents exploring rebellion, justice, and anti-authoritarianism among Korean intellectuals during this period. The choice to retell this specific Chinese legend suggests a deliberate use of historical narrative for contemporary commentary.

말은 다시 盜跖이가 卽墨에 도망하든 때로부터 시작되겟다. 盜跖이는 卽墨城에서 불의로 새 富者가 된 李心의 父子를 한주먹으로 따려 죽이고 성명을 고처 李跖이라 하고 그 길로 도망을 처서 5,6일 간을 밤길을 걸엇다. 가는 길에 거리거리마다 「殺人凶漢大成을 잡는 자면 은자 1,000냥을 賞給줄 터이오. 만일 범인을 隱置하는 자이면 凶漢과 가튼 죄를 쓰리라」하는 넘어가는데 고개말게서 엇던 사람 하나이 갑작히 胃腹痛에 걸려서 사람 살니라고 고함을 치며 盜跖을 쳐다보고 구원하야 주기를 애걸한다. 盜跖은 성명과 내력도 물을 새 업시 그 자를 둘우처 업고 고개를 넘어 20리쯤 되는 古津江頭에 이르러 한 客店을 차저들엇다. 들어서는 길로 주인을 차저 약을 지어다가 그 자를 먹엿다. 그러나 그 자의 병은 용이히 낫지
