잡지명	개벽 제40호
발행일	1923년 10월 01일
기사제목	행랑자식 (Child of the Servant's Quarters)
필자	도향
기사형태	소설 (Short Story)

행랑자식
						도향

It was a certain cold and very windy winter night. From the servant's quarters (haengnang) of Principal Pak's house, the sound of someone reading aloud could be heard, but like a dying candle flame, the sound gradually grew fainter. Then, again, with a baby suckling at her breast, a mother's voice, mixed with drowsiness, sharply urged,
"Read quickly!"
And the reading sound grew louder once more.
The boy, named Chint'ae, was twelve years old and in the fourth grade of the common school. He was the son of the servant (haengnang abŏm) at Principal Pak's house.
The droning sound of recitation died out again in less than two minutes. Then, the sound of the neighborhood clock striking eleven was heard, and all around became quiet.

The next day, after the sun rose, it was seen that snow had fallen like large flakes, covering the entire yard, roofs, and tree branches. However, the snow hadn't completely stopped yet, and fine sleet was still sprinkling down.
Chint'ae took the worn-out broom that had been propped behind the door and went out. At first, feeling light-bodied and refreshed, as if rolling on a newly washed and spread white mattress, he lifted the broom. He thought he could easily sweep away all that snow with the worn broom and his weak arms, but after barely managing to scoop and dump two or three basketfuls, his arms felt like they would fall off, and his back seemed ready to break. Yet, he couldn't not sweep. It was his daily duty to wake up in the morning and sweep the yard.<113>
His mother had gone inside to cook rice, and his father had gone out to pull the rickshaw, despite being ill.
After dumping one or two basketfuls into the ditch, he carried a third basket, grunting, towards the ditch. The tips of his fingers felt raw from the melting snow, as if the skin was peeling off like when plucking a chicken, and his toes were numb, feeling as if they were being chopped off.
He forced his feet to move. The snow-filled basket seemed to pull him along. Just as he reached the middle of the path, the strength in his arms gradually faded, and his legs suddenly gave way. He dropped the basket of snow he was holding. Just then, an adult's commanding voice shouted right above his head,
"Look at this!"
Lifting his head, he saw Principal Pak, who must have been returning from an early outing, with a pile of snow covering the top of his foot, looking down at him with an angry face. Chint'ae's face flushed instantly. He couldn't say anything and just stood there blankly. He didn't know how he could make amends for his mistake. Then, seeing the muddy snow clumped on the Principal's clean white socks (pŏsŏn), he thought that if he brushed it off with his own hands, his fault might somehow be forgiven. He bent down and was about to brush the sock with both hands. But the Principal stamped his foot sharply.
"Leave it! You'll make it dirtier,"
he said, then
"Hmph!"
snorted and went inside. Chint'ae felt utterly mortified. His hands were stained black with ink from practicing calligraphy the previous evening. He thought brushing it off would earn forgiveness, but instead, he was told he'd make it dirtier. It seemed like a rebuke, making the Principal even angrier. So, he didn't know what to do and just stood there blankly. Mortified,<114> his face burned, and his hands felt like they were on fire.
Thus, unable to go inside, he started towards his room in the servant's quarters when, from the edge of the main wooden floor (maru), he heard the mistress say,
"Doesn't that brat have eyes? Why doesn't he look where he's going?"
Hearing this, he shrank, wishing he could crawl into a mouse hole. Then, fearing his mother, who seemed to be following him with a raised fist ready to strike, shouting,
"You damned brat, where did you sell your eyes?"
he ducked quickly into his room.
Sure enough, a moment later, his mother, her unpleasant breasts bouncing, chased after him.
"You damned brat, are you blind? What is that on the master's new socks? Why are you so clumsy, you child of a human."
His mother's words were relatively few. Then she went back inside.
Chint'ae, though his heart shrank with fear, strangely felt a sense of indignation. No matter how he thought about it, it didn't seem like his fault. He was carrying the snow basket, and Principal Pak must have bumped into him while lost in thought; it wasn't that he had been careless.
So, feeling wronged and with nowhere to appeal, he threw himself face down on the room floor. Then, burying his head in his arms, he cried quietly. He felt his tears fall onto the floor. He smelled the earthy scent rising from beneath the rush matting. He thought about how his mother would worry, his father would worry, and especially how, if his father found out, he would be beaten by those stone-like hands, which made him shudder. He didn't know the words or phrases to lament his fate. In any case, he felt wronged and resentful. But there was nowhere to appeal, nowhere to vent his anger.
As he lay face down on the floor for a while, sobbing, the door burst open. He was startled but<115> didn't look back. He assumed the person opening the door was his mother. He thought that if his mother saw him crying like this in his weakness, she would surely comfort him. So he just waited for his mother to tell him to get up.
However, after a moment of silence,
"Hey!"
a gruff voice called. It was his father. Instead of receiving comfort, he instantly sensed that lightning was about to strike. His tears dried up immediately, and a chill ran through his body.
This time, in a sharp yell,
"Hey, get up, you!"
His father's angry face appeared in his mind's eye, even while face down. But he couldn't just jump up. Tears stained the corners of his eyes. If his father saw the tears, he would certainly ask why he was crying. If he answered, lightning would inevitably strike. So, unable to get up and unable to stay still, his heart pounded with anxiety.
Two feet seemed to stride into the room, and then a hand like an iron hook grabbed the collar of his jacket and lifted him sharply. He dangled like a piece of beef hanging on a hook.
"Why are you crying?"
Even his father, seeing his child cry, must have felt some paternal affection (慈情), as his voice softened slightly, and there was even a hint of a smile mixed in – an instinct to soothe the tearful heart.
"Why are you crying?"
No answer.
"Seriously, why are you crying?"<116>
His heart burned, but he couldn't answer.
"Did Mom hit you?"
Chint'ae shook his head and sobbed.
"Then why are you crying? Did you get scolded?"
"N...no."
Chint'ae didn't shake his head this time.
"Then why are you crying? Tell me."
The father suppressed his rising anger. Then,
"You brat! Speak up! Why have you become mute? Speechless!"
After trying to coax him several more times, perhaps pondering something, he muttered to himself,
"What's going on!"
and went outside. This was uncharacteristically patient for him recently. Perhaps he intended to ask the mother. The father went outside the door. Then he came back in, asking,
"What happened to the basket? Huh? The basket?"
As he paced in and out, the mother, washing dishes in the inner kitchen, said,
"Why, Chint'ae took it out earlier to sweep the yard," and
"Ask him."
The father, sounding annoyed,
"But he's just sobbing away, what did you say to him?" looked towards the mother.
Just then, the mistress, who had been watching something from the main floor, heard the talk about crying and, perhaps feeling sorry,<117> said,
"Earlier, while cleaning snow or something, he apparently dumped snow on the master's foot. So his mother probably just said a word or two."
The father's eyes narrowed. Then, like a tiger about to pounce on its prey, his head thrust forward, and his shoulders hunched up. Without another word, he headed out to the servant's quarters.
Once outside, the father immediately threw open the door. In his mind, dumping snow on the master's foot was secondary. Losing one basket felt so wasteful and infuriating that he wanted to beat his son to death, the damned child.
"You brat."
He grabbed his son.
"Come out here."
Chint'ae drew his hands and feet close to his chest, trembling, looking up only at his father.
"You damned brat. Why are you crying? Did you kill your father? Did you kill your mother? Whimpering since morning."
Then, a fist like stone landed squarely on his back.
"Ouch, Father! Ouch, Father!"
The sound of being struck came repeatedly, but no further words followed. Even Chint'ae, receiving the beating, couldn't unconditionally say he was sorry.
"What is it! Father! This brat, this damned brat!"
He beat him mercilessly.
As the sounds of crying and shouting grew frantic, the mother ran out from inside, pleading,
"Stop now, stop! It's too noisy!"<118>
"What's wrong with you? Stay still, get away!"
He pushed her away with his elbow.
"You brat, were your eyes missing that you poured snow on the master's socks, and then what were you daydreaming about to leave the basket outside and lose it? Huh? You brat who will ruin this family!"
The father's hand struck mercilessly—buttocks, back, thighs—wherever it landed, blue bruises formed on the young flesh, and blood welled up.
With each blow, the father's voice grew louder, trembled more, laced with sorrow and accusation. As he beat his son, he didn't see the child pleading in his hands, but the master sitting in the warmest part of the inner room. And it felt less like hitting his son and more like he wanted to curse, resent, and punch his master.
"Please stop now,"
the mother snatched the child away. Then she held her son tightly in her arms again.

That day, the sun passed three o'clock, heading towards four. Chint'ae returned from school. As he was about to enter the main gate, he saw a newly bought basket. Seeing the crudely woven, reddish straw basket made the places where he had been beaten ache and throb again.
As he sat perched on the edge of the narrow wooden veranda (t'oemaru), his mother called him into the room, bringing a meal set on a small table. Inside the room, only ashes remained of the small fire, with a flatiron stuck in them, along with charred firewood scraps and a small shovel.
His mother held the baby born last year, wrapped in ragged cloth, and nursed it. The baby suckled, mouth moving, while one hand played with the other nipple.<119>
Chint'ae felt a silent affection for his younger sibling. He poked its cheek with his finger and made cooing noises, "Eokku, eokku," trying to soothe it.
The baby smiled faintly. Then it pulled away from the breast and looked back at Chint'ae.
The mother, with a calm face, just played with the baby's fingers, then said,
"Here,"
holding out the baby,
"Hold him on your back for a bit,"
and lifted the baby upright. Chint'ae asked,
"Without eating first?"
He quickly ate his meal and then put the baby on his back. However, rice had not yet been cooked in Chint'ae's home. His mother intended to go inside to cook rice, but not the rice for their own meal.
After his mother went inside, Chint'ae walked back and forth in the room with the baby on his back, thinking that since she wasn't cooking rice, they must be out of it. He hoped his father would return quickly.
Chint'ae peered out through a tear in the papered window, waiting expectantly, wondering if his father might be coming home alone, pulling the rickshaw, with money to buy rice.
Still unsure, he looked inside the jar where they kept rice. He saw only the empty, blackened bottom of the jar with the rice scoop, stained with bran, lying inside. Chint'ae powerlessly replaced the lid and walked back and forth in the room with a heavy heart. The baby squirmed on his back, rubbing its feet together.
"We won't be able to cook rice again today,"
he thought, opening the rattling door and stepping down onto the small wooden porch (tchongmaru).
He went down and looked under the hearth where the pot hung. There, a couple of leftover pieces of kindling wood remained in the ashes.<120>
He then looked in the kitchen corner where firewood was stored. Not even wood scraps were there.
The wind blew through the desolate servant's quarters, highlighting the clean-swept state of their possessions. The dimming evening light made the prospect of not having dinner weigh heavily on Chint'ae's young heart, bringing tears to his eyes.
A little later, his mother came out hurriedly. It seemed she had lit the fire in the kitchen. Chint'ae could almost see his mother's quick feet kicking at the firewood burning in the hearth.
Coming out, she looked at the baby on his back.
"Oh, it's cold! Put something on him, quick!"
she said.
"What happened to the padded hood (nambawi)? His hands are all out."
She went into the room and brought out a padded hood that Chint'ae had worn as a baby, probably ten years old. The fur was all gone, and the silk was worn thin.
After putting it on the baby, the mother stood outside the door looking out for about five minutes. Chint'ae guessed the meaning of her standing there. Waiting for Father to return.
Then, suddenly, as if startled by someone tapping her neck, she jumped and turned to go back inside. At that moment, Chint'ae asked,
"Aren't we having dinner?"
following his mother inside. His mother, already irritated and anxious,
"You need rice and firewood to cook!"
shouted sharply. The baby on Chint'ae's back startled and burst into tears.
Chint'ae awkwardly bounced the baby to soothe it, unable to say anything, just standing there.<121>
His mother went back inside again. Chint'ae followed her. Then he sat down in front of the kitchen and put fuel on the fire.

Darkness fell, and the electric lights came on, but dinner still hadn't been cooked.
And Father still hadn't returned. Chint'ae's mother was about to come outside after setting the table [for the main house] when the mistress called,
"Ŏmŏm!"
"Yes,"
the mother answered, turning back as she was about to open the door.
"Did you make dinner today?"
The mother hesitated slightly, then said,
"We have something to eat,"
smiling embarrassedly.
"Has the father come home?"
"Not yet."
"Then you probably couldn't cook dinner, I suppose."
The mother said nothing. The mistress, already understanding, said,
"How can you manage like this? How will the little ones bear the cold?"
Then,
"Here, take this at least."<122>
She gathered the leftover rice from various bowls onto the edge of the table.
"Tell that boy to come in too. It seems you haven't lit a fire either!? How will you stand the cold? Adults might be okay, but the children..."
She looked up at the mother.
"Quick, tell that boy to come in."
The mother, relieved, came outside, calling,
"Hey, Chint'ae!"
"What is it?"
Chint'ae, who had been standing outside the door, came inside asking.
"Let's go inside!"
"Where?"
"Inside, I mean."
"The mistress says to come in and eat."
Chint'ae's face immediately turned bright red.
"Why? Let's just cook and eat when Father comes home."
"Where is he coming from?"
"He'll come eventually."
"Come inside, she's calling."
Chint'ae said,
"I don't want to!"<123>
and turned away. In Chint'ae's mind, thinking about dirtying the master's socks that morning made him ashamed to face the mistress again, and he was also resentful that he had been beaten by his father for something that wasn't his fault. On top of that, in the inner room was the Principal's daughter, the same age as him, who attended the girls' division of the same school, and he was ashamed for her to see him after being beaten.
"Hey, you'll hear worse things later. Let's go in quickly."
His mother urged him on.
"Hurry up and go in."
Chint'ae stubbornly retorted,
"I don't want to! I don't want to go in to beg for food!"
he yelled.
"You beggar brat. She's waiting inside."
"Whether she waits or not, I'm not going in."
Even in the mother's heart, her son's words didn't seem wrong. Far from scolding, it was a situation deserving sympathy, but still, there was the immediate hunger to consider, and she needed to eat not just for herself but to nurse the baby. So, she couldn't help but break her son's firm will with the authority of a mother.
"You're not going in?"
As she said this and pretended to look for a fire poker, she felt the sorrow of doing something she knew she shouldn't.
"I don't want to!"
Chint'ae refused in a tearful voice.
"If you don't want to, will you starve?"
"Starving is fine!"<124>
"Let's see. Give me the baby here."
Holding the baby, the mother went inside to get food. But Chint'ae went into his room and lay down in the darkness.
He didn't know why he felt so sad, resentful, and lonely that day. He wondered why things were like this. And he wished his father would just come home quickly.
Less than ten minutes later, his mother came out again.
"Hey,"
she opened the door and poked her head in.
"The mistress says to come in. Hurry, hurry."
Chint'ae, still lying down, turned over again.
"I don't want to. I'm not going in."
"The master is worried."
"I don't want to, I said!"
The mother went back inside. Less than five minutes later, the sound of her coming out was heard again. This time, opening the door, she said,
"Then here!"
holding something out.
Chint'ae, huddled like a shrimp under the covers to block the cold floor and the chilly wind seeping through the door cracks, heard his mother's voice.
"What is it?"
he started to ask, then quickly caught himself.<125>
"Here, it's rice. Eat and lie down. What kind of pathetic behavior is this in this cold?"
A wave of shame, sharp enough to tear his flesh, washed over Chint'ae.
"I said I don't want it! I won't eat! I don't want to eat!"
His mother came in. Shaking Chint'ae back and forth like rolling dough with a rolling pin, she pleaded and coaxed,
"Get up, please! Huh? Get up."
Chint'ae moved closer to the wall.
"I don't want to. I'm not hungry,"
he said, pulling the blanket over his head, saying nothing more.
"Fine. You'll be hungry, not me,"
she said and was about to go back inside when,
"Ah, it's cold!"
a voice said, and the person entering was his father. The mother and father bumped right into each other.
"Are your eyes missing? Good grief, sh-"
the father yelled.
"It was dark, I couldn't see," the mother said in a typically feminine, apologetic tone. This one sharp retort robbed her of the courage to scold her husband for coming home empty-handed, and the father, his pockets presumably empty, likewise had his courage to speak loudly diminished.
"How did it go?"
"What do you mean, how did it go? It's a disaster, a disaster. There's no work. What about dinner?"
"Please, save that nonsensical talk for later. What would we make dinner with?"
The father couldn't say anything and went into the room. Chint'ae got up and sat down. Inwardly, any gladness<126> was gone, replaced by the snapping of even the last thread of hope.
"Then what do we do?"
The father lamented, not even thinking about lighting the lamp.
"So you don't have a single penny?"
"No, woman! If I had money, would I have drunk rice wine (makkŏlli)?"
The mention of rice wine provoked the mother's temper.
"What's this about rice wine? The young children are shivering with hunger in a cold room, and still, it's rice wine? If you like rice wine so much, go find a rice wine seller's wife to live with, or go stick your head upside down in a rice wine crock. Always rice wine, rice wine, you'll surely pay your debt to rice wine someday, acting like this."
"That's enough! Stop nagging like a fox again. Ugh, I hate seeing my wife's face when I come home."
He clicked his tongue in distaste.
Chint'ae just watched this scene from the side, then lit the lamp.
"Well, we need to eat dinner,"
the father said, apparently quite hungry, trying to think of a solution where there was none.
"Eat this at least,"
the mother said, bringing out the rice mixed in soup that she had intended for Chint'ae.
"What's this?"
the father asked, taking a couple of spoonfuls, then
"Did you eat dinner?"
he turned to look at Chint'ae. Before Chint'ae could speak, or even needed to, the mother answered,
"He didn't eat."<127>
She glanced sideways at Chint'ae, seeming both to blame and resent him.
"Why?"
The father paused, spoon still in hand.
"Who knows? He just says he doesn't want to eat."
"Then he must be hungry,"
the father said, putting the bowl down.
"Want to eat some?"
he offered, but Chint'ae said,
"I don't want to,"
and moved further away.
"What's wrong?"
"I don't feel like eating."

About thirty minutes passed. Outside the door, the mother called,
"Chint'ae! Chint'ae!"
Her tone seemed unusually secretive, so Chint'ae answered,
"Yes?"
and went outside. His mother was standing by the gate, holding something thin in her hand.
"Here,"
his mother said, unwrapping the object wrapped in a scrap of cloth.<128>
"Take this to the pawnshop (典當局) and ask for 70 or 80 chŏn. Then go to the rice shop and buy five hops (닷곱) of rice, and buy ten nyang's worth (열냥앗치) of wood. Come right back."
Chint'ae understood immediately. Ah, the silver hairpin. The only valuable things in their family were that hairpin his mother brought when she married and a thick silver ring.
Chint'ae took it. He headed towards the pawnshop. The closest pawnshop was next to the general store (雜貨商), and a little further down was another one above the barbershop (리발소). But he couldn't go to the first one. The pawnshop owner's son went to the same school as him. If he were caught, it would be humiliating and shameful. So, he decided to skip that one and go to the farther pawnshop down the street. He walked with his arms crossed, hunched over. As he was about to enter the pawnshop, he felt as if someone was pointing at him, mocking his poverty. And he felt that even the pawnshop owner would probably scold him for his needy state, which he disliked.
But, steeling himself, he was about to enter when he saw a sign posted on the door: "In Mourning" (忌中), and the door was shut.
"In Mourning."
Realizing someone had died, the tension that had built up in his mind over the past few minutes eased.
Well then, he had no choice but to go to his classmate's father's pawnshop.
Dragging his feet, he entered that pawnshop. His chest felt tight, and heat rose to his head, making him feel dizzy.
Peeking inside, he saw no one. He had worried about running into his classmate, but only one adult clerk was sitting there, nobody else, which was a great relief.
He pushed the silver hairpin through an opening meshed with wire, like a pigeon coop hole or a train station ticket window. The clerk, who had been reading a newspaper, glanced up sharply.
"What is it?"<129>
he snapped.
"Will you take this?"
Chint'ae's voice trembled and was faint. The clerk took it without a word and weighed it on a scale.
Chint'ae worried inwardly, what if they don't accept it? What if they reject it as low quality? Just then, the clerk asked,
"How much do you need?"
asking about the loan amount. Relieved, Chint'ae was about to name a price but hesitated, wondering what if they offered less than he asked. Instead, he asked the clerk,
"How much is it worth?"
After a moment, the clerk said,
"One won."
That was 30 or 40 chŏn more than his mother had told him to get. Finally feeling at ease, he said,
"Give me 70 chŏn, please."
"What's your name?"
Having his name written on the pawn ticket wasn't good, but there was no choice, so he gave his name.
While the clerk wrote out the pawn ticket, Chint'ae paced back and forth. Trying not to look like he was there to pawn something, he glanced around and muttered to himself.
As Chint'ae looked outside, someone behind him called out in a child's voice,
"Chint'ae?"<130>
He quickly turned around. There stood the owner's son, delighted to see him.
"What brings you here?"
he asked, coming forward. Chint'ae wanted to run away. If possible, he wanted to forget the money and just escape.
"Did you do tomorrow's arithmetic homework?"
Why did he have to ask so kindly? But Chint'ae just shook his head.
"No."
His face turned crimson.
"Oh no! I can't figure it out at all!"
His friend's words barely registered in Chint'ae's ears. Forget tomorrow's homework; when he went to school tomorrow, his classmates would surely gossip about him. He would be teased. He pictured big Sunam (壽男), the notorious troublemaker, good at teasing and bullying, that delinquent student.
He received the pawn ticket and the money. Now it was time to go to the rice shop. Selling three or five *toe* or a *mal* of rice might even be something to boast about, but selling just five *hop* (닷곱) was truly shameful. Poverty wasn't a crime, but to Chint'ae, it felt as shameful as committing one. He went to the rice shop and got five *hop* of rice wrapped in a paper cone. The first rice seller snapped at him,
"Why didn't you bring a bag?"
then proceeded to measure out rice for other customers before finally, seeming reluctant, measuring out the five *hop* for him.
He paid and left. The hand holding the rice felt frozen, about to fall off. He warmed one ear with his free hand, then switched hands, holding the rice cone.<131>
Next was the firewood shop. He went there. He got 20 chŏn worth tied up. He slung it over his shoulder using a straw rope harness and tucked the rice under his arm as before. Walking down the main street with his head bowed, his shoulders ached, and his hands, feet, and ears were numb, so he stopped for a brief rest. Looking ahead, he saw one of his school teachers approaching, just before the alley leading to his house.
Chint'ae jumped up. He had to get into the alley before the teacher reached it, he thought. He started running. The teacher, naturally unburdened, walked quickly. Chint'ae was carrying something beyond his strength and his steps were slow. He was almost abreast of the teacher. Without looking ahead, he dashed into the alley and collided with someone coming out.
"Oof!"
The rice he held scattered everywhere, and he fell forward, the wood still on his shoulders.
"You child of a ruined house, are you blind?"
The person who turned to look back was his own father. Chint'ae still glanced behind him. The teacher had already passed by, seemingly without noticing.
"You damned brat, scattering the rice like this, what are we going to do?"
His father gathered the rice from the dark ground with both hands, collecting it in the front of his trousers.
Chint'ae stood there blankly, then was dragged home by his father.
When they got home, his mother asked how much he got, how much he spent, and how much was left. Hearing this, Chint'ae handed her the pawn ticket.
Then he explained the details.
But his mother saw no fault or innocence in Chint'ae. Thinking only of the sole treasure pawned and the resulting rice now spilled all over the ground, she felt such waste and resentment that she couldn't contain herself. So,<132>
"This damned brat, I told you to eat the dinner offered, eat and go to sleep, but because you didn't..."
She raised her fist and lunged at him.
"Here, take this!"
she attacked him again. Chint'ae offered no excuse. But being beaten for the second time in one day,
He felt resentment towards something, wanted to curse something, but didn't know what it was. So, after being thoroughly beaten, he cried alone. There was no one to console him, no one to stroke his back.
He huddled in a corner of the room, cried for a long time, and then fell asleep just like that. In his dreams, he had nightmares of being wronged. -(End)-<133>

**Summary**

The story follows a twelve-year-old boy, Chint'ae, the son of a school principal's servant, through a harsh winter day. Falsely blamed and physically punished by his father for accidentally dropping snow on the principal and later losing the snow basket, Chint'ae feels deep shame and resentment. The family faces extreme poverty, unable to afford rice or firewood. Forced by hunger, his mother sends him to pawn her precious silver hairpin. Chint'ae endures the humiliation of the pawnshop, encountering a classmate, and the shame of buying a tiny amount of rice. Rushing home to avoid his teacher, he collides with his father, spilling the rice and wood, leading to another beating from his frustrated mother. The story ends with Chint'ae crying himself to sleep, trapped in a cycle of poverty, injustice, and lack of solace. The story vividly portrays the stark class divisions and the crushing poverty experienced by the lower classes in colonial Korea. Historians might find the detailed depiction of daily life, the power dynamics between master and servant, the specific economic struggles (pawning valuables for food, buying tiny amounts of rice/wood), and the psychological toll on a young boy particularly revealing about social conditions in 1920s Korea.

행랑자식
						도향

엇더한 날 치웁고 바람 만히 부든 겨울밤이엇다. 朴校長의 집 행랑에서 글 읽는 소리가 나더니 꺼저가는 초불처럼 차츰차츰 소리가 가늘어 간다. 그러다가는 다시 업헤서 어린애입에 젓꼭지를 물니고서 조름석거 꽥 질으는 목소리로
『어서 일거!』
하는 어머니소리에 다시 글소리는 굴거진다.
나희는 열 두살. 보통학교 사년급에 다니는 鎭泰라는 아해이니 그 朴校長의 집 행랑아범의 아들이다.
왱왱 외우든 그 소리는 단 二分이 못되여 다시 살어젓다. 그리고는 동리집 시게가 열 한시를 치는 소리가 들니더니 사면은 고요하엿다.

잇튼 날 날이 밝은 뒤에 보닛가 온 마당 집웅 나무가지에 눈이 함박가치 쏘다젓다. 그런대 아즉까지도 눈이 다 끗나지 안코 보슬보슬 싸랙이눈이 내려온다.
鎭泰는 문 뒤에 세워노앗든 모지랑비를 들고 나섯다. 처음에는 새로 빠러 펼처노흔 하얀 요 우에 둥구는 것처럼 몸 가뱌웁고 마음 상쾌한 氣分으로 비짜루를 들엇스며 모지랑비와 약한 자기 팔노서 능히 그 만흔 눈을 처 바릴 줄 알엇스나 두어 삼태기를 갓가수로 퍼 바리고 나닛가 팔이 떨어지는 것 갓고 허리가 부러지는 듯 하엿다. 그러나 안이 칠 수는 업섯다. 날마다 아츰에 일어나서 마당을 쓰는＜113＞ 것이 자기의 직분이다.
어머니는 안으로 밥을 지으라 들어가고 아버지는 병문으로 인뎍거를 끄을너 나갓다.
한 두 삼태기를 개천에 부은 후에 다시 세 삼태기를 들고서 낑낑하면서 개천으로 간다. 두손 끗은 눈에 녹아서 닭튀해뜨들때 발 허물 벡겨내듯 빠지는 듯하고 발끗은 저려서 토막을 내는 듯하다.
그는 발을 억지로 옴겨노앗다. 눈들은 삼태기가 자기를 끄을고 가는 듯하다. 그러케 그가 길 중툭까지 갓슬 때 그의 팔의 힘은 차차 업서지고 다리에 맥이 홱 풀니엇다. 그래서 그는 손에 들엇든 눈 삼태기를 탁 놋치엇다. 그러자 누구인지
『이걸 좀 봐라』
하는 어룬의 호령소리가 바로 자기 머리우에서 들니자 고개를 처들고 보닛가 校長어른이 아츰 일즉이 어대를 다녀오시다가 발뜽에다가 눈을 한아 잔뜩 덥허 씨우시고 역정나신 얼골노 자기를 내려다보고 게시다. 鎭泰는 고만 얼골이 홧홧하여젓다. 그러고 아모 말도 못하고 그대로 멀거니 서잇섯다. 그는 무엇으로 그 미안한 것을 풀어야 조흘지 아지 못하엿다. 그러다가 하얀 새 버선에 검은흙이 석기인 눈이 무더잇는 것을 보고서 자기의 손으로 그것을 털어듸리면 얼마간 자기의 죄가 용서되리라 하고서 허리를 굽흐려 두 손으로 그 버선등을 털어듸리랴 하엿다. 그러나 校長은 한 발을 탁 구르시더니
『고만 두어라. 더 더럽는다』
하시고서
『엥』
하시며 안으로 들어가시엇다. 鎭泰는 무참하엿다. 손에는 어제 저녁에 습자 쓰다가 무든 먹이 꺼먹케 무더잇다. 떨어듸리면은 잘못을 용서하실 줄 알엇더니 더 더러워진다. 핀잔을 주시고 역정을 더 내시는 것 갓다. 그래서 그는 엇더케 해야 조흘지 아지 못하여 그대로 멀거니 서 잇섯다. 무참을＜114＞ 당하여 얼골도 홧홧하고 두 손에서는 불이난다.
그래서 그는 안으로 들어가지 못하고 행랑 자기방으로 들어가다가 안마루 끗헤서 주인 마님이
『아 그애녀석도 눈이 업든가? 왜 압흘 보지 못해?』
하는 소리를 듯고서는 쥐구녕으로라도 들어가 바리고 십도록 온몸이 옴치라젓다. 그러고는 자기 뒤로 따라나오며 주먹을 들고서 따리랴 덤비는 자기 어머니가
『이 망할 녀석 눈깔을 엇다 파라먹고 다니느냐?』
하고 덤비는 듯하여 즐겁을 하여 방안으로 들어갓다.
아니나 달을가 조곰 잇드니 보기 쉴흔 젓퉁이를 털넉털넉하면서 어머니가 쪼차나왓다.
『이 망할 년석 눈갈이 업니? 나리마냄 새 버선에다가 그것이 무엇이냐? 왜 그러케 질뚱바리냐 사람의 자식이.』
어머니는 그래도 말이 적엇다. 그러고는 그내 다시 안으로 들어갓다.
鎭泰는 간이 콩알만하게 무서운 것은 둘재 처노코 왼 일인지 분한 생각이 난다. 아모리 생각을 하여도 자기잘못 갓지는 안타. 자기가 눈 삼택이를 들고 가는데 校長어룬이 딴 생각을 하면서 오시다가 닥달닌 것이지 자기가 한 눈을 파다가 그리한 것은 안이다.
그래서 왼일인지 호소할 곳이 업서 그는 그대로 방바닥에 업듸러 젓다. 그리고는 고개를 두 팔노 얼싸안고 작고작고 울엇다. 그는 눈물이 방바닥에 떠러지는 것을 알엇다. 삿짜리 깐 그 밋흐로 흙내가 올너오는 것을 맛핫다. 그리고는 어머니도 걱정을 하고 아버지도 걱정을 할터요 더구나 아버지가 이것을 알면은 돌짝가튼 손에 으더마질 것을 생각하매 몸서리가 난다. 그는 신세 한탄할 문자를 몰으고 말도 몰은다. 엇터튼 억울하고 분하엿다. 그러타고 어대 가서 호소할 때도 업섯고 분푸리 할 꼿도 업섯다.
그는 방바닥에 한참 업듸려서 늑겨가면서 울고 잇슬 때 방문이 펄석 열니엇다. 그는 깜짝 놀냇스나＜115＞ 도라다보지도 안엇다. 그의 생각에는 그 문여는 사람이 어머니려니 하엿다. 그래서 약한 마음에 이러케 우는 것을 보면은 어머니는 나를 위로하여 주려니 하엿다. 그래서 어머니가 이러나라고 하기만 기달녓다.
그러나 한참 아무 소리가 업더니
『얘!』
하고 험상스러웁게 불으는 사람은 자기아버지다. 그는 위로를 밧기커녕 벼락이 나릴 것을 그 刹那에 豫感하엿다. 그는 눈물이 쏙 들어가고 온몸이 선듯하엿다.
이번에는 꽥 질으는 소리로
『얘 이러나거라 이것아』
하는 아바지의 성난 얼골이 업듸린속으로 보인다. 그는 그러나 벌떡 이러나지는 못하엿다. 자기 눈 가장자리에는 눈물이 무덧다. 그 눈물을 보면은 반듯이 그 우는 곡절을 무를 터이다. 그 대답을 하면은 결국은 벼락이 나릴 터이다. 그래서 일어나지도 못하고 그대로 잇지도 못하고 그의 가슴은 초조하엿다.
두 발이 성큼 방안으로 들어오는 듯 하더니 무쇠갈구리 가튼 손이 자기저고리 동정을 꾀뚤어 번쩍 처들엇다. 그는 쇠관에 매달닌 쇠고기모양으로 반짝 들니엇다.
『울기는 왜우니?』
하는 그의 아버지도 자식우는 것을 볼 때 엇더튼 그 눈물을 동정하는 慈情이 일어나는지 목소리가 조곰 야저지며 또는 우슴이 석기엇스니 그것은 그 눈물나는 마음을 위로하랴는 본능이다.
『왜 울어?』
대답이 업다.
『글세 왜 우니?』＜116＞
가슴은 타나 대답할 수는 업섯다.
『엄마가 때려주든?』
鎭泰는 고개를 내흔들며 늑겨 울엇다.
『그러면 왜 우니? 꾸지람을 들엇니?』
『안...요.』
鎭泰는 가시 고개도 흔들지 안엇다.
『그럼 왜 우러 말을 해.』
아버지는 화가 나는 것을 참엇다. 그리고는
『이 자식아! 말을 해라 왜 벙어리가 되엿니? 말이 업게!』
하고서는 무슨 생각을 하엿는지 여러 번 타일너 보다가
『왼일야!』
하고 혼자말을 하더니 밧갓흐로 나아간다. 그것은 근자에 볼 수 없는 느러진 성미엇다. 아마 어멈에게 무러볼 작정이엇든 것이다. 아범은 문밧그로 나아갓다. 그러더니 다시 들어오며
『삼택이 엇잿니? 응 삼택이?』
하며 안팍으로 들낙 날낙하는 서슬에 안 부억에서 어멈이 설거지를 하면서
『왜 앗가 鎭泰가 마당을 쓴다고 가지고 나갓는대』하고
『걔더러 무러보구려』
한다. 아범은 화가 나는 듯이
『그런대 쭉쭉 울고 잇스니 무엇이라고 그랫나?』 하며 어멈을 본다.
그러자 안마루에서 마냄이 무엇을 보다가 운다는 소리를 듯더니 미안한 생각이 낫든지＜117＞
『앗가 눈인가 무엇인가 친다고 나리마님 발등에다가 눈을 쏘다뜨렷다네. 그래서 어멈이 말 마대나 한 것인게지.』
아범의 눈은 실눅해젓다. 그러고는 잡어 먹을 즘승에게 덤비랴는 호랑이모양으로 고개가 쓱 내밀니더니 억개가 읏슥 올너간다. 그러고는 아모 말업시 밧갓 행랑으로 나아간다.
밧갓흐로 나온 아범은 닷자곳자로 방문을 열어 젓드렷다. 그의 생각에는 주인나리의 발뜽에 눈 업흔 것은 외레 둘재이다. 삼텍이 하나 일어바린 것이 자기자식을 처 죽이고 십두록 앗갑고 분하고 망할 자식이다.
『이 녀석.』
자기 아들을 움켜 잡엇다.
『이리 나오너라.』
鎭泰는 두 손 두 다리를 가슴에 다 모고서 발발 떨면서 자기 어바지만 치어다 본다.
『이 망할 자식. 울기는 애비를 잡어 먹엇니 에미를 잡어 먹엇니 식전 아츰부터 홀작홀작 울게』
하더니 돌떵이 가튼 주먹이 그의 등쭐기를 보기좃케 올니엇다.
『에그 아버지 에그 아버지』
하며 복가 치는 소리가 줄을 대여 나왓스나 그 뒤 말은 업섯다. 매를 맛는 鎭泰도 잘못햇슴니다를 條件업시 할 수는 업섯다.
『무엇야 아버지! 이년석 이 망할자식』
하고서는 사정업시 듸리팬다.
울고 호령하는 소리가 야단스럽게 나닛가 어멈이 안에서 뛰여나오며
『인제 고만두 고만둬요. 요란스럽소』
하고 말류를 하나＜118＞
『이게 왜이래 가만잇서 저리가요』
하고 팔꿈지로 뿌리치고는

대답 한번에 밧갓흐로 나아갓다. 어머니는 대문간에 손에다가 무엇인지 가느다란 것을 쥐고 서잇다.
『저』
하고 어머니는 험겁에 싼 그것을 풀으더니＜128＞
『이것 가지고 전당국에 가서 七拾錢이나 八拾錢만 달내가지고 싸전에 가 쌀 닷굽만 팔고 나무 열냥앗치만 사 가지고 오너라』
한다. 鎭泰는 얼는 알어채엇다. 올치 은비녀로구나. 자기집안에 갑진 것이라고는 어머니 시집올 때 가지고 온 그 비녀하나하고 국다란 은가락지 뿐이다.
鎭泰는 그것을 바다 들엇다. 그러고는 典當局을 향하여 간다. 典當局이 雜貨商엽헤 잇는 것이 제일 갓가웁고 조곰 내려가면 리발소 웃집이 전당국이다. 그러나 첫재집은 가지를 못한다. 그것은 그 전당국주인의 아들이 자기하고 갓흔 학교를 다니닛가. 만일 들키면 창피할 것이요. 붓그러울 것이라. 그래서 그 집을 남겨노코 멀은 저 아랫전당국으로 가리라 하엿다. 그는 팔장을 끼고 옹숭구리고서 전당국으로 들어가랴 하닛가 엇재 누가 손구락질을 하는 것 갓고 구차함을 비웃는 듯 하다. 그러고 그 전당국주인까지도 자기의 구차한 것을 호령이나 할 뜻이 싈흘 것 갓다.
그러나 눈 딱 감고 드러가랴 하닛가 문간에다가 忌中이라 써 부치고 문을 다더 바렷다.
『忌中』
사람이 죽엇구나 하고서 생각하니 고 몃 분 동안에 자기마음이 긴장되엿든 것은 풀려진다.
그러면 이번에는 하는 수 업시 그 동모 아버지의 전당국으로 가야하겟다.
한 발자국이라도 더듸게 띄워노와 그 전당국으로 들어슬 때 가슴은 거북하고 머리에는 열이 올나와서 흐리멍덩하다.
기웃이 듸려다 보닛가 아모도 업다. 혹시 동모학동이나 맛나지 안을가 하엿더니 사무보는 어른이 한분 안저잇고 아모도 업서 아조 다행이다.
그는 정거장 표 파는 데처럼 철망으로 얼고 또 비둘기장 구녕처럼 뚜러 노흔 곳으로 은비녀를 데밀엇다. 신문을 보던 사무보는 어룬이 한번 흘겨보더니
『무엇이냐?』＜129＞
하고서 소리를 꽥 질은다.
『이거 잡으세요?』
하는 소리는 떨니고 가늘엇다. 사무보는 이는 아모 말 업시 그것을 바더 들더니 저울에다가 달어본다.
鎭泰는 속마음으로 만일 저것을 잡지 안으면 엇더케하나? 납분 것이라고 퇴짜를 하면은 엇더케하나 하고잇슬 때
『얼마나 쓰련?』
하고 돈을 뭇는다. 그는 겨우 안심을 하고서 돈 말 하랴다가 자기가 불으는 돈보다 적게주면 엇더케하나 하고서 도리혀 그이더러
『얼마나 나가요?』
하고 물엇다. 그는 한참 잇더니
『일원이다』
한다. 그러면 자기어머니가 어더오랴는 것보다는 三四十錢이 더하다. 그는 겨우 안심을 하고서
『七拾錢 주세요』
하엿다.
『네 일홈이 무엇이냐.』
전당표에 일홈이 씨는 것은 조치 못하나 하는 수 업시 일홈을 대엿다.
사무 보는 이가 전당표를 쓰는 동안에 鎭泰는 왓다갓다 하엿다. 그러고서 남에게는 전당잡으러 온 체하지 안으랴고 사면을 둘너보며 군소리를 하엿다.
鎭泰가 밧갓흘 내다볼 때 누구인지 덜미에서
『鎭泰냐?』＜130＞
하는 어린애소리가 들녓다. 그는 얼는 도라다 보닛가 거기에는 그 집주인의 아들이 반가워마지며
『엇재왓니?』
하며 나아온다. 鎭泰는 다라나고 십헛다. 그러고는 될 수만 잇스면 돈도 고만두고 피해가고 십헛다.
『내일 산술 숙제햇니?』
엇저면 그러케 다정하게 물으랴? 그러나 鎭泰는
『안이』
하고서 고개를 내저엇다. 그의 얼골은 진홍빗 갓치 붉어젓다.
『얘 큰일낫다. 나는 조곰두 할 수가 업서!』
그의 말소리는 鎭泰의 귀에 조곰도 안들닌다. 내일 숙제는 고만두고 내일 학교에 가면 반듯이 여러 동무들이 흉들을 볼 터이요. 또는 놀녀대임을 당할 것이다. 그러고 그의 압헤는 커다란 壽男이가 보이며 작난에 괴수요 핀잔 잘 주고 못살게 굴기 잘하는 그 불량한 학생이 보인다.
典當票와 돈을 바더 들엇다. 이제는 싸전으로 갈 차례다. 석되나 닷되니 한말 쌀을 파는 것은 오히려 자랑꺼리지만은 닷곱은 팔기가 참으로 붓그러웁다. 구차한 것이 죄악이 안이지만은 鎭泰에게는 죄지은 것처럼 붓그러웁다. 그는 싸전에 가서 조희봉지에 쌀 닷굽을 싸들엇다. 첫재 싸전쟁이에게
『왜 전대를 가지고 오지 안엇서?』
꽥 소리를 한번 질으더니 딴 사람의 쌀을 다 퍼주고야 조희봉지 한아가 갓가운 듯이 갓가수로 닷굽 한되를 퍼주엇다.
돈을 주고 나왓다. 쌀 들은 손은 얼어서 떨어지는 듯 하다. 한 손으로 귀를 녹히고 또 한 손으로는 번가러가며 쌀 봉지를 들엇다.＜131＞
이번에는 나무가가로 갈 차레다. 나무가가로 갓다. 二拾錢어치를 묵것다. 그것을 색기에다 질방을 지어서 둘너메고 쌀은 여전히 엽헤다 끼엇다. 행길노 고개를 수기고 가다가는 억개가 압흐고 손, 발, 귀가 쉴혀서 잠간 쉬다가 저쪽을 보닛가 자기집 드러가는 골목을 조곰 못 밋처서 학교선생님 한 분이 오신다.
鎭泰는 얼핏 일어낫다. 그러고 선생님이 골목까지 오시기전에 먼저 그 골목으로 들어가야 하겟다 하엿다. 그러고는 줄다름질 하엿다. 선생님은 아모 것도 둘너 메시엇슬 리가 업슴으로 거름이 속하시다. 자기는 힘에 닷지 안는 것을 둘너 미엇고 또는 거름이 더듸다. 거진 선생님과 막 다다르게 되엿다. 그래서 압도 보지안코 골목으로 뛰여 드러가다가 거기서 나오는 사람과 마주첫다.
『에쿠!』
하면서 손에 들엇든 쌀이 모다 헛터지고 나무는 억개에 민 채 나가 잡버젓다.

하고 되려다 보는 그이은 자기 아버지다. 鎭泰는 그래도 뒤를 도라다 보앗다. 발서 선생님은 본체만체 지내가 바리서엇다.
『이 망할 자식아 쌀을 이러케 헛트려서 엇더케 해?』
하며 아버지는 두 손으로 껌껌한데서 그것을 쓰러서 바지 압헤다 담는다.
鎭泰는 멍멍히 서 잇다가 아버지에게 끄을녀서 집으로 들어갓다.
