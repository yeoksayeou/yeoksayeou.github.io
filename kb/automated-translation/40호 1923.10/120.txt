Various Aspects of Hwajang[sa Temple]
						One Reporter

South of Samgak[san Mountain], there is a separate realm (別境), where the scenery for climbing is beautiful and the lofty, rugged (崔嵬) shape is wondrous, resembling a Buddha, resembling a person, and also like a crown (冠); its name is called "Kwanak[san Mountain]." Among the many temples (寺) and hermitages (菴) in this mountain, the temple closest to Sŏul is called Hwajangsa Temple (華藏寺).

While Sŏul people all know Ch’ŏngnyangsa Temple (靑凉寺), Yŏngdosa Temple (永道寺), and Anjŏngsa Temple (安靜寺) as places to have lunch, they do not know Hwajangsa Temple as well.

Quickly crossing the Han'gang River from the terminus of the Sinnyongsan-bound tramway, passing through the tunnel (돈네루) of acacia trees (앗가시아나무) along the east side of the river, going through the village, crossing the rice paddy paths, and going over the hill, after spending about thirty minutes, the refined dwelling (精舍) of one hundred and ten *kan* (rooms/bays) that comes right before one's feet is Hwajangsa Temple. There is a sweet spring (甘泉), there are sweet chestnuts (甘栗), and there is the even better Kyŏngp'aru Pavilion (鏡波樓). And as for the members (族屬) of this house, there are about fifteen monks (승려), among whom the master (主翁) of this temple, a certain Ch’oe Wŏnong (崔圓翁), is said to be quite a considerable person in many ways.

However, one must know that the true scenery of Hwajang lies outside this. Herein lies the subtle charm (妙味). That is to say, when the sun is about to set behind the western mountains (牛山), if one goes out to the northern foot (北麓) of the temple, observing the lower reaches of the Han'gang River glistening amidst the vast misty haze (烟霞渺茫) is a magnificent view (장관). Furthermore, with Pugak[san Mountain] and Inwang[san Mountain] forming the inner enclosure (內圍), and the continuous peaks of Pukhan[san Mountain] forming yet another outer enclosure (外圍), the sight of Sŏul in the mountain valley (山谷), nestled layer upon layer, softened by the evening sun mixed with cooking smoke (炊煙), is truly a scene that cannot be described (形喩) by any means. Of course, we can see the full view of Sŏul from the peaks of Pugak[san], from Chamtugol (蚕頭) on Namsan, or from anywhere. But those are all the Sŏul of the metropolis (都會). That is, Sŏul as it is. It is the Sŏul of chaotic worldly stench (世臭 紛紛), where trams shuttle back and forth and pedestrians flicker dimly (아물아물). However, the Sŏul seen from the north side of Hwajangsa Temple is the Sŏul of forests and mountains (山林), the Sŏul of fields and gardens (田園). This is before sunset, but when the sun has completely set and it becomes dark, the spectacle of the sea of lights from Pugak[san] to the Han'gang River creates yet another new scene. This speaks of the evening at Hwajang; moreover, stepping out to the west of Hwajang and gazing at the green mountains of Kwanak[san] softened in the morning sunlight (아츰해ㅅ볏) and the houses below the mountain (山下 人家) offers, truly, a thousand bushels of clear taste (淸味萬斛). Though I won't describe it at length (換寫치 안커니와), in any case, one night (一夜) at Hwajang will surely bring about (齋來할) a great revolution in the mind and body (心身) of a Sŏul person. It is beyond comparison (比) with Anjŏng[sa] or Ch’ŏngnyang[sa]. For this reason (爲하야), I expend these words (一言 費한다).<99>

**Summary**

This article describes Hwajangsa Temple, located near Seoul at the foot of Kwanaksan Mountain, as a hidden gem offering beautiful scenery and tranquility. The author contrasts the peaceful, natural views from the temple – especially the panoramic vistas of Seoul nestled in the mountains at sunset and the sea of lights at night, and the view of Kwanaksan in the morning – with the bustling, "worldly" atmosphere of the city itself. The writer argues that a visit to Hwajangsa offers a refreshing, almost revolutionary experience for the mind and body of Seoul dwellers, surpassing other nearby temples like Cheongnyangsa or Anjeongsa. For historians, the article offers a glimpse into early 20th-century Korean perceptions of urban development versus nature, highlighting a desire for escape and appreciation of traditional landscapes during a period of modernization and colonial rule. The specific descriptions of travel routes (tram, walking paths) and temple life provide valuable socio-cultural details.

華藏百態
						一記者
三角의 南에 한 곳의 別境이 잇서 登臨의 景이 佳하고 崔嵬의 形이 奇하야 佛갓고 人갓고 또 冠과 가트니 그 일홈이 曰 「冠岳」이다. 이 산속에는 寺와 菴이 하도 만은 중, 제일 서울과 근접해 잇는 절이 가론 華藏寺이다.
靑凉寺, 永道寺, 安靜寺라 하면, 서울의 사람들은 누구나 다- 한번 점심 먹을 곳 인줄로 알지마는 華藏寺라 하면 그러케들 알지 못한다.
新龍山行 전차의 종점에서 얼는 漢江을 건내여, 강의 동쪽길로 앗가시아나무의 돈네루를 통하야, 村中을 지내고, 논두렁을 건내고, 고개를 넘어, 약 三十分間을 費하면, 발압헤 닥처지는 百十間의 精舍가 곳 華藏寺이다. 甘泉이 잇고, 甘栗이 잇스며, 보다 더 조흔 鏡波樓가 잇다. 그리고 이 집의 族屬으로는 약 十五人의 승려가 잇는 중, 이 절의 主翁인 崔圓翁가튼 이는 여러 가지로 相當한 사람이란 말이다.
그러나 華藏의 眞景은 이 밧게 잇슴을 알어야 한다. 여긔에 妙味가 잇는 것이다. 즉 해가 牛山으로 떠러지려 할 때에 寺의 北麓에 나서면, 烟霞渺茫의 間에 번득이는 漢江의 하류를 遙望함도 장관이거니와, 北岳, 仁王이 其 內圍가 되고, 北漢의 連山이 또 外圍가 되야, 차곡차곡히 둘너 안기운 산꼴(山谷)의 서울에 석양이 炊煙을 和해서 무르녹은 것은, 정말 무엇으로도 形喩할 수 업는 景色이다. 물론 우리는 北岳의 山峰에서나 南山의 蚕頭에서나, 어대서나 서울의 전경을 볼 수가 잇다. 그러나 그것은 다- 가티 都會의 서울이다. 즉 서울 그대로의 서울이다. 전차 오락가락하고, 행인 아물아물하는, 世臭 紛紛의 서울이다. 그러나 華藏寺의 북편에서 뵈는 서울, 이는 山林의 서울이며, 田園의 서울이다. 이는 해지기 전의 말이거니와, 해가 아조 뚝 떠러저 어두컴컴해지고 보면, 北岳으로 漢江까지의 불바다의 일막은 또 한번 새 경색을 이뤄 놋는다. 이것은 華藏의 저녁을 말함이거니와 華藏의 서편을 나서서 아츰해ㅅ볏에 무르녹은 冠嶽의 靑山, 山下의 人家를 바라보는 맛은, 그야말로 淸味萬斛이다. 길게 換寫치 안커니와 여하간 華藏의 一夜는 서울사람의 心身에 일대혁명을 齋來할 것이다. 安靜, 淸凉의 比가 아니다. 爲하야 一言을 費한다.＜99＞
