Kim Tongmyŏng
If You Would But Open the Gate for Me (To Baudelaire)

Oh, Lord! I believe in you.
Your breast, soaked in the crimson water of cold dew,
Your heart, smiling endlessly in red wine and blue Opium (阿片),
And all the beautiful songs flowing from the wounds of your soul.

Oh, Lord! I believe in your country.
With thick, gray clouds
Covering all the repulsive, seductive light of the sun, moon, and stars,
Beneath the chaotic dance of falling leaves fluttering without direction,
Your country, where the strong rhythm burns high like flames in deep tranquility.

Your country
Of intoxication and pathos, agony and ecstasy,
Silence and cold smiles, hallucination and supreme self-regard.
Your country
Of clouds and waves, white peonies and pure incense,
And yet, where the dawn light of the polar night still ripples.
Oh, Lord! I believe. <134>
Lord— Into your country that I long for,
Receive my body!
The spring of alluring confusion, rank with the smell of raw flesh,
The autumn like a house of mourning busy with shrouds,
Surely cannot be seen in the land where you reside.

Only the white snow piled high as far as the eye can see,
And the dizzying Aurora Borealis swaying tragically to a grand melody— these two mingling together
Become a white-hot kiss,
Become the great prelude to death,
Become blue laughter and black tears,
Become a rose-colored cushion woven with life and death as warp and weft,
Marking the weary footprints of souls abandoned and exhausted—
Into your country, oh— receive my body!

Tender Lord! If you would but open the gate for me
(To enter your country)
And cover my heart with thick, iron-gray clouds,
To your lightning-like songs,
I would dance like a falling leaf.

Affectionate Lord! If you would but open the gate for me
(To enter your sanctuary) <135>
And let me touch the pure incense burning in your breast,
Into your ocean-like sighs,
I would sink like a fish.

Lord! Oh— Lord like the Demon King (魔王)!
If you would but open the gate for me
(To enter your secret chamber)
And caress my body with the light of the Arctic (北極) aurora,
Gathering strength from your resounding laughter,
I would dig my grave where the snow lies piled high.—(End)—

I Stand Watching

A white skeleton drunk on red wine,
Holding the exhausted soul of a person,
Hither and thither,
Like leaves scattering in late autumn,
Wandering, not knowing where to go—
I stand watching this.

As the faintly shining shadows of twilight
Scatter beneath the snow-laden tree branches, <136>
He sings thus,
Like the sound of reeds rustling,
Struck by the mad wind—
"In days gone by,
I too had joyous kisses
And the tender love of a young wife."
At this, he sucks his own lips,
And fiercely shuts his pale eyes.
I stand watching this.

On the mountain slope guarding a young man's grave,
Beneath the black curtain falling silently and still,
As the shadow of twilight, deep in reminiscence,
Smiles faintly and fades away,
The white skeleton, holding the exhausted soul of a person,
Opens its eyes once more.

As the pale moonlight
Pours down onto the field covered by the chilly wind, <137>
He sings again,
Like the sound of ghostly weeping drifting faintly
Over the fog-covered sea on a moonless night—
"In days gone by,
I too had fragrant red wine
And beautifully colored dreams."
At this, he collapses,
The moonlight on his back,
Lying prostrate on the ground.
I stand watching this.

As the pale moonlight
Dances in the middle of the field,
The river water sings.
But the sea alone weeps mightily.
This is the time, the time is dawn (黎明).
The white skeleton, holding the exhausted soul of a person,
Which had collapsed in sorrowful reminiscence,
Just like that, ah— just like that,
Together with the starlight of dawn,
Fades away beneath the dawn (黎明).
I stand watching this. <138>

Sorrowful Memory

In two young hearts,
Red words,
Rose-colored confessions.
Between soul and soul,
Spotless laughter,
Pure embrace.
Ah, this was when muddy water flowed in the Ch'ŏngch'ŏn River (淸川江),
And summer danced in the furrows of the millet fields.

In two young hearts,
Burning passion,
Painful silence.
Between soul and soul,
Sorrowful separation,
Uncomprehending resentment (怨惘).
Ah, this was when the moon was blue over the Paeksang Pavilion (百祥樓),
And winter wept from the leafless branches. <139>

**Summary**

These three poems by Kim Tongmyŏng, published in 1923, express a deep yearning for escape, heavily influenced by the French Symbolist poet Baudelaire, who is directly addressed. The first poem, "If You Would But Open the Gate for Me," paints a picture of Baudelaire's imagined realm—a decadent, artificial paradise characterized by intoxication, sensory extremes, darkness, and intense beauty, contrasting sharply with the perceived ugliness of reality. The speaker begs to be admitted into this realm of "strong rhythm," "polar night," and "white-hot kisses," even desiring death within it. The second poem, "I Stand Watching," presents a desolate scene of a "white skeleton" lamenting lost love and dreams, symbolizing decay and the haunting persistence of memory amidst ruin. The final poem, "Sorrowful Memory," contrasts the vibrant passion of past youthful love with the cold silence and resentment of separation, framed by seasonal changes and specific locations. Historically, the poems are significant for showing the direct influence of Western modernist literature, particularly French Symbolism, on Korean intellectuals during the colonial period. The themes of disillusionment, escapism into aesthetics or death, and the focus on intense, often morbid, sensations may reflect the despair and alienation felt under Japanese rule, offering a psychological landscape shaped by both personal sorrow and the broader colonial context.
