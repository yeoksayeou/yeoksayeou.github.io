The Earthen Pot and the Tin Pot

As the river flood overflowed (에강놀이 창일하야), an earthenware pot and a tin pot were floating away. The tin pot saw the earthenware pot and said, "Look here, elder brother (노형), since you and I are fellows in misfortune (同病相憐), let us go together." The earthenware pot replied, "Your words are kind, but your nature (성품) and mine are different. If we bump into each other, it will be the end of me (결단이니), so let's stay apart (따로 놉시다)."<109>

**Summary**

This short article retells a fable, likely Aesop's "The Earthen Pot and the Brass Pot" (using tin instead of brass here). During a flood, a sturdy tin pot suggests travelling together with a fragile earthenware pot due to their shared predicament. The earthenware pot wisely declines, recognizing that a collision between them would only result in its own destruction. For historians studying colonial Korea, this fable, published in *Kaebyŏk* magazine in 1920, could be interpreted allegorically. It might symbolize the perceived vulnerability of Korea (earthen pot) in relation to a stronger power like Japan (tin pot), cautioning against close association or alliances that could prove fatal to the weaker party, reflecting anxieties about cultural or political assimilation and unequal relationships during the colonial period.
