**Body Text Translation:**
One day, when the wind and the sun were arguing about who was stronger, a traveler wearing a padded durumagi passed by. The wind and the sun made a bet to take off his durumagi. The wind blew with all its might, and it seemed the traveler's durumagi would be blown away, but the person tied the coat strings tightly and held onto the hem with both hands. The more the wind blew, the less it could be taken off.

Then the sun calmed the wind, cleared the clouds, and shone down hot sunlight. The traveler became hot and took off the durumagi. The wind admired the sun's power.

To win people's hearts, the warm spirit of grace is better than the cold wind of oppression. <116>

**Summary**

This short essay retells the fable of the Wind and the Sun competing to make a traveler remove his coat. The Wind fails with forceful blowing, while the Sun succeeds with gentle warmth. The author concludes that benevolence (grace) is superior to coercion (oppression) in winning the hearts of the people. Published in 1920 in the magazine *Kaebyŏk* (개벽), shortly after the suppression of the March 1st Movement, this piece serves as a subtle allegory. Historians would find it significant as an example of indirect criticism of the harsh Japanese colonial rule (압제), implicitly advocating for a more benevolent approach (은혜) to governance rather than brute force.

은혜와 압제
하로는 바람과 태양이 누가 세력이 만흐냐 하고 서로 다툴 지음에 한 행인이 솜두루막을 입고 가거늘 바람과 볏이 그 두루마기 벳기기로 낵이 하자하고 바람은 그 힘을 다하야 불매 행인의 두루막이가 불려떠나갈 듯 하더니 그 사람이 옷고름을 단단히 잡아 매고 두손으로 옷자락을 붓들매 바람이 더 불수록 벳길 수 업는 지라 태양이 바람을 재우고 구름을 물리치며 더운 볏을 나려쪼이매 행인이 더워서 두루막을 볏어바리니 바람이 태양의 권력을 탄복하더라.
인심을 엇기에 은혜의 더운 긔운이 압제의 찬바람보다 낫다.＜116＞
