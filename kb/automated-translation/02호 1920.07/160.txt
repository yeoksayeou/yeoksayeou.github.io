Truly, a great person (偉人) desires no particular appearance (儀容). Crowns (王冠), swords (劒), a marshal's baton (元師의 儀杖), a judge's robe (裁判官의 法衣) – all are unnecessary things.

This is because all such things, compared to the compassion (慈愛) overflowing from their very being (自體), lack even half the power to adorn the body (故이다).

An enemy (敵) is better than an unfaithful (不信實한) friend (友).<58>

**Summary**

This short piece argues that true greatness stems from inner qualities like compassion, rendering external symbols of authority such as crowns or robes meaningless by comparison. It concludes with the aphorism that an open enemy is preferable to a disloyal friend. From a historical perspective, this text, published in 1920 under Japanese rule, might interest historians for its subtle emphasis on inherent virtue over outward displays of power, potentially reflecting a quiet critique of authority or a focus on inner national character. The final sentence about enemies and friends could also hint at the complex political and social dynamics of the time.
