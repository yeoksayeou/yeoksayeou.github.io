探囊으로부터
						强我之
From head to heel, even if I emptied out everything I have on my body, it would amount to no more than 10 Wŏn. Just wooden clogs, trousers, a red undershirt, and a ramie durumagi coat. And I'm just wearing a 1 Wŏn straw hat. And I'm just holding a 10-chŏn fan. Besides that, I have nothing. Oh, if you include what I'm wearing on my feet, it would surely exceed 10 Wŏn. Other than that, there's nothing. Even if you strip me bare, there is nothing. However, I possess one thing that is neither visible nor touchable (**어지지도 안이하는**). That is precisely the Pocket (探囊). This truly is an unparalleled treasure for me. It is better than silver or gold, better than jewels. I wouldn't sell it even if offered the whole Earth, nor would I exchange it for the art of ascending to heaven or entering the earth. This truly is my lifelong guide and my most beloved treasure pouch (寶囊). Wherever I go, whatever I face, whether sad or happy, big or small, as things happen, as I encounter them, I just put them into this pocket, and they are never lost or damaged even after ten thousand years, and no matter how much I rummage through it, they are never consumed. So, whenever I say, "Hey, bring that out," the pocket unhesitatingly presents the fact of that time without omitting a single detail, saying, "Here it is," and even if I search for it in my sleep wanting to know about that matter, it often comes out whole. Isn't this truly a treasure pouch? That's why I cannot part with this Pocket even for a moment. I carry it when I sleep, I carry it when I walk, even when I eat or relieve myself, I carry it. Now, I intend to introduce widely to those without this pocket a few facts obtained from it.

As one lives through this world, one sees all sorts of strange sights. One day, feeling extremely vexed, I went looking for the Sŏngje Well in Samch'ŏngdong, carrying my Pocket on my chest, hoping to get some cool breeze. Just before reaching the vicinity of the well, the sound of a janggu <37> drum and the clanging of cymbals "Kkwangch'aeng" could be heard from somewhere. So I thought, "Is there some kind of immortal's revelry going on?" and pricked up my ears to listen carefully, and the sound was coming from the yard of the house next to the Sŏngje Well. So I thought, "Ah, a spectacle has arisen!" and rushed up in one breath, peeking timidly from outside the gate of that house. Was the doorplate inscribed with "Sungsillyŏn Chohap Ch'isŏngdang" (Hall of Sincere Devotion of the Association of God Worshippers)? It was so absurd that I could only look up at the sky and burst into laughter. However, being a curious fellow whose nature prevents me from eating if I don't go right into the scene and witness the spectacle, I had no choice but to disregard decorum and step right in. Ah—how utterly bizarre and unfathomable! A shaman (무당) held open a fan and hopped about, while perfectly fine-looking profligates (破落戶) beat the janggu drum and clashed the cymbals, shouting "Chihwaja," "Nŏŭira," "Such-and-such year, such-and-such date," causing a commotion. Meanwhile, all the young ladies and misses, mesmerized by the shaman, covered their faces with handkerchiefs and sobbed "heuk-heuk," whether feigning grief or truly weeping. In broad daylight under a blue sky, ghosts and spirits must truly not exist. If they did exist, why wouldn't they seize those who corrupt customs and waste resources in vain? If only the police, in place of ghosts, were a bit more discerning... Pocket's No. 1

There are many prejudiced people and many unforgiving people in the world. One day, I saw in some newspaper that a debate between men and women was being held at some venue. So I thought, "Hey, this is worth seeing," and didn't I rush off without even fastening the ties of my durumagi? The audience had already filled the seats. So, rubbing shoulders, didn't I barely obtain the fortune of a seat? Then, a female orator (女辯士) with a hisashigami hairstyle suddenly stepped onto the stage. She was quite lively. No, not lively, she seemed rather presumptuous. She stuck out her chest, clasped her hands behind her back, and rolled her eyes this way and that. All the listeners seemed to purse their lips and regard her favorably. But wasn't the common desire to hear what words would come from her mouth? She began, right off the bat, "What is the world of men doing? Taking kisaeng around, riding in automobiles..." positioning herself at the forefront of attacking men. Consequently, the male orator (男辯士) had no choice but to also deliver an attack on women—after she descended from the stage shortly after, a male orator ascended and, immediately rolling up his sleeves, shouted hoarsely as if he were about to devour someone, "What about the conduct of the women's world? What about the appearance of the women's world?" and started a whole argument. - No matter how much people talk about women's liberation or equality of the sexes with every mouth, what kind of behavior is this at the dawn of a new era? Isn't it just ignorant fools picking a fight, rather than an exchange of <38> academic or intellectual discussion? From now on, I hope men and women will adopt a policy of self-restraint and proceed gradually, keeping pace with the times and their respective capabilities. By understanding each other and forgiving each other—Pocket's No. 2

Hey there. I saw something like this too. How funny it was! One day, I went to see the night market. As I passed by Pagoda Park, a young man, his necktie fluttering loosely, was walking together with a man wearing a topknot (상투쟁이) and engaging in an open conversation quite nauseatingly, which was truly side-splitting. Holding my mouth, I stealthily followed them, putting their words into my Pocket as they came out. The conversation went like this. It was the high-collar man speaking.
"Hey, you treat today."
"..."
"I spent 90 Wŏn last night."
"..."
"Hey, by the way, OO (Kisaeng's name) said she wants to see you."
"Does she know me?"
"Of course, she does. The day before yesterday, when you spent 130 Wŏn..."
"Let's see her once..."
"Man, spend about 100 Wŏn more. She'll be yours."
"No way. Last time, I got scolded by my father."
"Why? What happened?"
"I couldn't even drop by home for three days..."
"Man, a man... what... just tonight, okay okay?"
"Well... I need to have some cash first."
This was their exchange (酬酌). How amusing it was! The necktie was one thing, but the topknot guy was ridiculous. Saying he couldn't go home for three days—Pocket's No. 3

There are many heartless people in the world too. I must have gone to Namdaemun Station to see off a friend. Didn't I take the tram? How crowded the inside of the tram was! However, I happened to be seated. When we reached Kurigae, it became even more crowded, and two or three young ladies boarded. The sight of them standing squeezed among all the passengers, swaying this way and that, was truly pitiful. So I quickly stood up and, turning to one young lady, said in a warm and respectful tone, "Please sit here," offering her my seat. At that, she glanced sideways and quite heartlessly retorted sourly, "It's alright." How awkward it felt! My intentions were entirely good, so why was she so heartless? At that moment, I felt boundless things for the world of Korean women. And thinking of Western women or Japanese women, I felt it even more deeply. A person who doesn't know how to sympathize is one thing, but a person who cannot receive sympathy seems even more pitiful.— Pocket's No. 4 <39>
<37-39>

**Summary**

The author, Kang Aji, metaphorically describes his memory as an infallible "Pocket" (探囊) where he stores observations of contemporary life. He recounts four anecdotes from this pocket: 1) witnessing a bizarre shamanic ritual held by a "God Worshippers Association" in Samch'ŏngdong, questioning superstition and the lack of intervention. 2) Observing a public debate between a presumptuous female orator and an aggressive male orator, lamenting the confrontational nature over constructive dialogue about gender roles. 3) Overhearing a conversation between a modern "high-collar" youth and a traditional "topknot" man discussing spending lavishly on kisaeng, highlighting generational differences and moral decay. 4) Experiencing rejection after offering his tram seat to a young woman, prompting reflections on the lack of social grace and warmth among Korean women compared to Western or Japanese women.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its candid vignettes of Seoul society in 1920, shortly after the March 1st Movement. It reveals social tensions arising from rapid modernization under colonial rule: the persistence of traditional practices like shamanism clashing with modern sensibilities, the awkward beginnings of public discourse on gender equality, the conspicuous consumption and changing lifestyles of urban youth (both Westernized and traditional), and anxieties about social etiquette and national character. Historians might find the direct social commentary, the specific locations mentioned (Samch'ŏngdong, Pagoda Park, Namdaemun Station, Kurigae), and the portrayal of different social types (shaman, fallen gentry, modern youth, traditional man, female orator, young women) particularly insightful for understanding the cultural and social landscape of colonial Korea.
