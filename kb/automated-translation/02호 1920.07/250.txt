華藏寺의 아츰
						노.덧.물

Even my six-foot body had nowhere to rely, and as the sound of unconsciously chanting "Namu Amita Bul" arose, my eyes flew open. The dark curtains of the legions of Māra had all lifted, and *gogocheonbyeon illyunhong* (杲杲天邊日輪紅 - The red sun wheel shines brightly on the edge of the vast sky). Oh dear, I'm late! I must leave by seven o'clock, but as they say, even the Ŭiju express courier has time to rest. I slipped on my shoes and went down to the yard below. Hearing "yard below" might sound ordinary, but considering I came up leaning on my young legs, one can surmise that this place is not quite so level. Since Hwajangsa Temple (華藏寺) is attached to the forehead of Pongbongsan Mountain (寶鳳山), the yard below this temple must be at least the shoulder of this mountain. The mountain's name is said to come from its resemblance to a phoenix (鳳) dancing. To put it somewhat artfully, it feels as though I have ridden a phoenix and ascended into the cloudy heavens (雲霄).

Indeed, the damp morning clouds pass in layers beneath my feet, and the multitude of mountains, far and near, appear as dots before my eyes. The place may be the same, but it feels as if my body, having soared a thousand fathoms to reach the ninth heaven, is now floating again, arriving above the Southern Sea (南溟). The undulating waves of the vast expanse seem poised to swallow the entire great earth, stretching limitlessly where water meets sky. For a moment, I closed my eyes. What lies within that deep? Is it also filled with those undulating waves? If it's water, is it only that which appears white? They say the sea covers three-quarters of the land; if things live within it, their numbers must also be proportionately three-quarters as many.

Just as land animals live on land, aquatic creatures must live in the watery realm. Just as plants flourish on land, plants must flourish in the watery realm. Just as there is air on land, there must be air in the watery realm. Besides these, there might be things that do not exist on land, and things that exist on land might not be there. Of course, that must be so. Aquatic animals live in the watery realm. Just as we walk stepping on the ground, they live by swimming through the water. There are also those like whales and hippos, which originally did not live in water but gradually adapted to living there from the land. Speaking of plants, apart from floating ones like water chestnuts or algae (水垢), if there are other plants, they must live rooted in the ground at the bottom of the water, so let's leave that aside. Just as we must breathe air, they too breathe air from within the water.

There is no need to compare and calculate one by one what exists and what doesn't in the realms of water and land. But generally, might there be homes within that water where they sit and lie down? Or, like us, could there be defined regions <89> called countries, with governing institutions? According to some novel-like writings, there is someone called the Dragon King (龍王) who rules over the entire water world. Aquatic creatures undoubtedly have homes, but the home of this Dragon King is said to be a magnificent Pearl Palace and Shell Pavilion (珠宮貝闕), grander than our homes. Leaving such tales aside, they must surely have a language to communicate their intentions to each other. Though we cannot hear it, just as we look down and talk, they must look up (or perhaps look down, I don't know) and say something amongst themselves. Oh my, what if those creatures, three-quarters more numerous than us, were to mobilize entirely, drive the water forward, and trample our land realm? As I thought this, my eyes snapped open again.

The vast blue waves undulate as before. It truly looks as if they are about to swallow the great earth. But these waves are somewhat strange. Sometimes they rise like mist, sometimes they sneakily gather and flee like smoke. Thus, islands that were visible only as bluish peaks moments ago now disappear, and sharp-looking peaks, previously unseen shadows, suddenly materialize. Darkish ridges, with nothing above or below, appear and disappear. Above them, birds like magpies or kites occasionally fly past. The sun, which had been peeking over the mountain ridge, has now climbed atop the mountain head, standing on tiptoe. The sunlight strikes the eastern slope of the western mountain, painting a brilliant picture.

We humans, driven by curiosity, pack our bags and deliberately go to the seaside to see the morning sunrise or the evening sunset. I thought I would see one of those two spectacles here. Suddenly turning my eyes, I looked down at the sea – none of those phenomena were there. Only waves kept undulating, steadily climbing up towards my feet. However, they were not damp. One surge of waves rose billowingly, then simply crossed over a mountain ridge on one side. In the place where it rose, drooping green pines were revealed. Truly, strange sea waves exist.

From another corner, cloud-like waves rise and flee over to the other side. In their place, fantastic rocks and bizarre stones are revealed. Truly strange. Oh! This is the morning mist-cloud! This is the Sea of Clouds (雲海), the panoramic view seen from Hwajangsa Temple in the morning. What meaning could there be in phenomena that occur according to the time? Yet, humans perceive them as strange and realize things strangely on their own. Just as I was about to turn back, the bugle call for breakfast sounded. The chanting of "Namu Amita Bul" still continues without cease. <90>

**Summary**

The author, staying at Hwajangsa Temple high on Pongbongsan Mountain, wakes early and observes the morning view. Mistaking the thick sea of clouds filling the valleys below for the actual ocean, the author reflects deeply on the vastness of the sea, imagining the life, societies, and even potential power dwelling within its depths, comparing it to life on land. As the sun rises and the scene changes, the author realizes the "ocean" is actually a magnificent display of morning clouds, prompting a reflection on human perception and the nature of phenomena. The piece ends with the sounds of the temple – the breakfast call and continued chanting – grounding the ethereal experience. For historians, the article provides a glimpse into the literary sensibilities and reflective moods of a Korean intellectual writing in 1920 under Japanese rule. The appreciation for Korean landscapes (Pongbongsan, Hwajangsa) and the imaginative, somewhat philosophical escape into nature observation, possibly tinged with subtle allegorical thoughts about hidden worlds or potential power, might reflect coping mechanisms or quiet expressions of identity during a period of national suppression.
