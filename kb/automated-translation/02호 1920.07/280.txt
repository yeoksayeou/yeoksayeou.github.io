잡지명	개벽 제2호
발행일	1920년 07월 25일
기사제목	「나」라는 것을 살리기 爲하야, 이글을 東京에 멈을러 잇는 東初兄에게 드리노라 (To Revive the Thing Called "Me", I Offer This Writing to Brother Tongch'o Residing in Tokyo)
필자	외돗
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

Brother Tongch'o—I have only now finally found the path I must take. I, who always caused you trouble, agonizing at every turn, asking for whom I live, have finally discovered my path to life today, almost half a year after sending you afar to Tokyo. However, beyond just searching and finding that path, the anguish this body suffered until finding satisfaction in it truly defies description. I have parents, I have siblings, I have close friends like you, Brother, and beyond that, I have a hometown I can never abandon forever, and I have a beloved whom I cannot forget for my entire life. Therefore, I am not just the 'I' of one person, but an 'I' standing opposite various inseparable relations around me; an 'I' tightly holding hands with them,<78> not just the 'I' of one person. However, as someone born in a different era from them, whose inherent personality does not match theirs, whose pursued life and enjoyed tastes tend to conflict, it was difficult for me to demand they be faithful to me, just as I could not play the role of 'I' by blindly following them. When my thoughts were not yet firm and my eyes to see myself had not yet fully opened, even if things went against my thoughts and did not align with my heart, I thought, "Because it's my parents' word... Because it's between siblings... For the sake of what..." Captured by moral conscience and worldly virtue, I had no chance to let shine the treasure called 'me' that I was born with, merely killing my potential self in useless blind obedience. I even had no time to cast aside the sacred duty of contributing to cultural development. But now that I have, even if only slightly, opened my eyes to see myself and have come to harbor the doubt, "For whom do I live?", I can no longer, not even for a moment, bury this precious entity called 'me' in such empty blind obedience and aimless adoration. A flame-like thought, uncontrollable in its force, began to burn in my chest. Thus, whenever I felt dissatisfaction and sorrow towards my almost meaningless life! My life of falsehood! I always provoked the thought, "Why am I so frustrated and sorrowful?" And the result was inevitably falling into a dark, pitch-black, terrifying pit, shivering as if thinking, "Because I try to practice filial piety, fraternal duty, loyalty, and keep trust..."

Brother Tongch'o—I am I, and other than me, there can be no other 'I'. I am not my father's 'I', nor my elder brother's 'I', nor even your 'I'. I am the absolute 'I'. From beginning to end, I am solely my own 'I', so the path of life for me to walk must also be just one single path laid open. Therefore, seeking only that path and steadily walking forward with faithful steps and fierce courage is my life, my duty, and perhaps the heavenly calling that all humankind ought rightfully to perform. Consequently, if there were two or perhaps three paths open for me to walk, I would necessarily have to choose only one among them. Only one path among many!! Choosing this path and walking forward along this path with sincerity is life's unparalleled ideal and humanity's unique progress.<99>

Brother Tongch'o—I am only I. As long as I exist, the world and humanity will also exist. If the individual entity called 'I' were to disappear from this world, then everything in the entire world would merely return to emptiness. That is, only after the thing called 'I' exists can parents exist, siblings exist, status exist, and honor exist. Thus, the most precious thing in this world is only 'I', and the most important thing is also only 'I'. Therefore, before anything else, I will strive to revive the thing called 'me', revive it well, and revive it eternally. Only after doing so can I serve the country and serve my parents. Thus, the call "Strive only to revive the self" will ultimately become the premise for striving to revive even things outside of myself, like the nation or parents.

Brother Tongch'o—However, this 'I' that is mine follows anything. If I have my 'I', the nation will also have the nation's 'I', and parents and siblings will each have their own corresponding 'I'. If they all wholeheartedly strive only to revive the thing called 'me', then this society will merely advance towards a world of resilience with the most vigorous steps. If that is the case, there would be no need to fussily make my simple path ahead unnecessarily chaotic with talk of filial piety, loyalty, righteousness, or benevolence. Furthermore, if one were to kill the thing called 'me' (that is, forcibly doing something my heart does not permit) merely for the sake of performing filial piety, loyalty, or righteousness, then that person would be a criminal against the 'I' who can never forgive, and an enemy to my life.

Brother Tongch'o—Truly, there is nothing more to love in the world than the self. In loving myself, reviving myself, and making myself shine, naturally, filial piety will be there, loyalty will be there, and even trust and righteousness will all well up from within. Therefore, I must simply strive solely to revive myself and make myself shine. If there is anyone who casts aside the self and bustles about, claiming to practice filial piety, loyalty, or act for love, that person has already lost the life called 'I'; they are already a dead person, a witless half-person.

Brother Tongch'o—Until now, we have had no time <100> to strive for the thing called 'me'. That is, taking the one 'I' we possess, we tear off a piece for our parents, tear off another piece for our siblings, and with the remainder, for the sake of the king, for the sake of the teacher, for the sake of something, and finally even for the sake of love, we tear it into pieces and discard it. Only after eliminating the entirety of 'me' have we seemed to make it a virtue to say we have fulfilled our duty as human beings. Therefore, until now, my 'I' was not my own 'I' but the 'I' of my parents, the 'I' of the king, the 'I' of the country, the 'I' of siblings, or perhaps the 'I' of love. It would not be an overstatement to say that until now, we have become bodies that lived not for ourselves but for others. Where else in the world could there be a more regrettable thing than this?

Brother Tongch'o—From now on, I intend to gather the scattered 'I'—the lost 'I'—strive to find it, make the broken 'I' complete again, and then contribute my strength solely for the sake of 'I'. I must first retrieve the 'I' given to my parents, the 'I' given to my siblings, and gather together even the 'I' given to my compatriots and the 'I' dedicated to love. After reorganizing the complete 'I', I must make the springs of greater filial piety, greater fraternal duty, greater love, and greater loyalty begin to flow from within a life centered on 'me'.

Brother Tongch'o—We have given everything away to others. We have made giving things away a virtue. However, we must retrieve it all. Even if we cannot retrieve a single thing, we must absolutely retrieve the one thing called 'me'. Me! Me! What greater treasure could there be in this world than me? I am the source of life, the entirety of the universe; there is nothing that is not within it.

Brother Tongch'o—We must abandon all other deeds and take hold of 'me'. Even if we kill everything else, we must revive the one thing called 'me', revive it well, revive it eternally, and thoroughly realize the meaning of being human from beginning to end. We must make all actions necessarily originate from the place centered on the thing called 'me'. Not just for my own sake, but even for the sake of the whole world.<101>

**Summary**

The author, Oedot, writes to his friend Tongch'o in Tokyo, declaring he has finally found his path: to live for "himself." He critiques the traditional Korean emphasis on obligations to family (parents, siblings), country (king, nation), teachers, and even loved ones, arguing these demands force individuals to suppress their true selves and live fragmented lives for others. Oedot asserts the absolute value of the individual "I" as the foundation of existence and meaning. He argues that by focusing on reviving, nurturing, and completing the self, true filial piety, loyalty, and love will naturally emerge from a self-centered life, leading to personal fulfillment and societal progress. From a historical perspective, this article is significant as an early expression of modern individualism in Korea during the colonial period. It reflects a growing intellectual trend questioning rigid Confucian hierarchies and traditional social structures under the influence of Western ideas, advocating for self-discovery and personal autonomy as the basis for a meaningful life and even a stronger society, a potentially radical idea in the context of 1920s Korea.
