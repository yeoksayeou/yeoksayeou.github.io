Article Title: 祈願 (Prayer/Supplication)
Author: 金東鳴
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

祈願
						金東鳴

Lord Hanu!
Who made the sun and moon and stars!
Lord Hanu!
Who prepared the Flood to destroy wickedness!
The greed and strife <145>
of the wickedly cunning wolf pack,
the withered smiles overflowing
under the gloomy night lamps of the city—
if these are hateful in your sight too...
Ah, do you not have Noah’s Flood?
I believe in your righteous anger and omnipotence.

Lord Hanu!
Who made the heavens and earth and sea!
Lord Hanu!
Who prepared the sulfur fire to burn the unrighteous multitude!
The mischief and insidious harm
of ghost-like monsters,
that sight of them clapping and rejoicing
in a sick, decaying dark room—
if this is hateful in your sight too... <146>
Ah, do you not have in your hand the remaining ashes of Sodom Gomorrah?
I believe in your righteous anger and omnipotence.

Lord Hanu!
Who created Truth and Goodness and Beauty!
Lord Hanu!
Who brought the war-fires of Rome to punish the beastly ignorant masses!
The cunning schemes and reckless violence (暴虎)
of wreckage-like creatures,
those hands busily crafting
a cross in a secluded back garden—
if these are hateful in your sight too...
Ah, did you not see the red drops of blood at Golgotha?
I believe in your righteous anger and omnipotence. (End) <147>
<145-147>

**Summary**

This poem, "Prayer," by Kim Tong-myŏng is a fervent plea to "Lord Hanu" (God) for divine intervention against the perceived evils and injustices of the time. Each stanza invokes God as the creator and as a historical agent of righteous destruction (Noah's Flood, Sodom and Gomorrah, the fall of Rome interpreted as punishment), contrasting this power with contemporary scenes of greed, corruption, decay, and malicious scheming ("wolf pack," "ghost-like monsters," "wreckage-like creatures"). The poet expresses unwavering faith in God's "righteous anger and omnipotence" to enact judgment again. From a historical perspective, the poem powerfully reflects the despair and yearning for justice prevalent in colonial Korea. The intense biblical imagery of divine wrath against oppressors ("wickedly cunning wolf pack," "unrighteous multitude," "beastly ignorant masses," those crafting a "cross") can be interpreted as a thinly veiled cry for liberation from Japanese colonial rule, portraying the oppressors as wicked entities deserving divine punishment.
