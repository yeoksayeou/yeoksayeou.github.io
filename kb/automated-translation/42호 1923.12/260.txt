Exotic Atmosphere: Turkey's Folk Songs
						Kang Un'gok from Cheju

Turkish literature is generally divided into three major categories: classical literature, folk literature, and modern literature.
Among these, folk literature has particularly developed alongside the lives of the common people, boasting many famous writers, holding considerable value as a subject of study, and being quite interesting. While there are numerous excellent works such as novels, plays, folktales, epic poems, and lyric poems, what I intend to introduce here are the folk songs (Tuerki).
These songs are called Tuerki in the Turkish language, which is close to the German Volkslied, and would fall into the category of folk songs (민요) for us.

There are truly many charming examples among Turkish folk songs. Generally, the Turkish people are all passionate lovers of music; this applies equally to these Turks and the various Tatar peoples residing in Eastern and Southern Europe, Russia, and Central Asia (who call their songs e-l or jji-l), which can truly be called their national character. Therefore, regardless of gender or age, whenever they have leisure time, they play instruments and sing songs.
Moreover, those with a bit of artistic inclination compose their own tunes to their own poems, enjoying and singing them amongst themselves. Music is an important part of life. Thus, in large cities like the Turkish capital, Constantinople, concerts are said to be held daily, literary magazines publish articles about music and musical scores, and calls for writers and composers are prevalent. Turkish songs are distinct from those of our Chosŏn, various Western European countries, and Russia, possessing their own unique, special melodies. Generally, they carry a tune common to the peoples of the Near East, similar to the songs of Central Asia, Persia, Arabia, <137> Armenia, and Greece. Furthermore, it goes without saying that each of these peoples possesses its own characteristic national melody.

However, what I wish to discuss now is not the melody of Turkish folk songs, but their form and content.
Since folk songs take the form of poetry, they have rhythm and meter, but they are distributed over a certain limited number of lines and are crafted with various variations according to rules like syllable count, alliteration, and rhyme. But there is another element that makes the songs even more beautiful and harmonious. This is the principle of vowel harmony (協韻法), a characteristic of the Turkish language (especially prominent in languages belonging to the Ural-Altaic family). Vowel harmony is a grammatical rule where vowels are divided into strong and weak, with strong vowels always combining with strong vowels, and weak vowels always combining with weak vowels. There are even classifications where certain consonants attach to strong vowels and others to weak vowels.
Strong vowels are a, y, o, u, and weak vowels are ae, e, i, oe, ue. To give an example:

Strong                                    Weak
Tash            (Stone)                  Dish           (Tooth)
Tashly          (Made of stone)          Dishli          (Having teeth)
Tashdan        (From stone)              Dishden         (From tooth)
Tashlar         (Stones, plural)        Dishler          (Teeth, plural)
Bulajaqsyn      (You will find)        Sewejeksin      (You will love)
Bulmadym-my  (Did I not find?)        Sewmedim-mi   (Did I not love?)

Thus, strong and weak are clearly distinguished. Except for loanwords (even those assimilated into Turkish follow vowel harmony) and one or two exceptions, <138> this rule is followed without the slightest deviation, forming the essence of the Turkish language. Therefore, from daily conversation to formal and informal language, prose, and poetry, everything adheres to this principle. Consequently, poetry, already beautified by other conventions, achieves an even smoother, harmonious musicality through this vowel harmony, resonating beautifully. As practical examples, the first and sixth folk songs are appended with Romanized spellings.
Furthermore, without seeing them in the Arabic script, rich in curved beauty, the full mood of Turkish poetry might not emerge. Here, however, I will try to represent the outline of Turkish folk songs using the closest possible pronunciation.

There are various materials for Turkish folk songs, but generally, many excellent ones sing of love and joy. Others sing of the fate of the world and people, and some take legendary figures as their subject matter.
As examples, I have recorded seven folk songs, sourced from the novel "The Unfortunate Letter" by the modern Turkish writer Ijjet Arepi-ssi, and from the essay "Folklore" by Rida Tep'wi-k'ŭssi, another modern Turkish writer and literary critic. The latter has systematically studied Turkish proverbs and folk songs.
However, the formal characteristics of Turkish songs and the aforementioned vowel harmony are impossible to express in our Korean language. Thus, while keeping the number of lines the same, I have adapted the form to be closer to our folk songs and translated the content as straightforwardly as possible.

Lying on the lap of my beloved,
Gazing quietly into those lapis lazuli eyes,
Overcome by that beautiful form, <139>
Even when the morning light shines, I do not rise.
Indeed, we are in our youth,
Come—let's drink wine... How could you and I ever part?

Perhaps calling it the intoxication of love would be fitting? A richly sensual scene comes to mind. This song—for convenience, written below in Roman letters—uses the past tense, but in the translation, I have deliberately used the present tense to enhance the mood.
Yattum yaryn dizine,
Baqdum ala goezine,
Jivesine dayanamadum,
Guenesh dogmush uyanamapum,
Ufajyq Fydan boylym,
Chaqyrym!… Senden ayrylamadum!…
As shown, vowel harmony is observed. This harmony, the various formal beauties of the poem, the content of the song, its corresponding melody, and if there is accompanying instrumentation, its timbre—all these elements fuse together to form the entirety of a Turkish folk song.

Oh, my nightingale, cry, cry,
On the dew-drenched rosebud.
Ah, my beloved is coming,
And so my heart is joyful…
Like the previous song, this one sings of love, but it differs slightly; this is the joy of waiting for love. <140> It is a song with a bright, cheerful mood, and in Turkish, the melody is extremely refreshing.
The words "nightingale" and "rose" appear in this song; these two provide various materials throughout Turkish literature, both refined and common. The nightingale, called *bülbül* (쁄뮬), is like the nightingale, noted for the beauty of its voice. The rose, called *gül* (뀰), although there is a separate word for flower (*çiçek*, 쳭치크) in Turkish, is used as a general term for flowers.

A small bird flew in,
And perched upon the thorn bush.
Sobbing and weeping,
Its pitiful life will likely end.
The curtain of evening descends,
And again, alas, darkness draws near.
Throb, throb, throb, holes open up
In this wound in my breast.
This time, I wanted to introduce a couple of songs about the sorrow of love. This folk song is one of them, a truly plaintive, sad song. When a young shepherd, thinking of his lover, sings this song softly and high-pitched, trembling alone in the vast wilderness, who would not shed tears of sorrow?…

Smoke rises before the fire in my heart ignites.
In the desolate garden, the nightingale weeps. <141>
The hidden flame of emotion, the anguish within the heart, a beautiful sorrow… fills these two lines completely.

The horse is ready for the journey,
But my heart aches at parting.
Trying to endure, my wife still weeps.
Let's go, my wife, let's go again.
Eyes and voice filled with tears,
The capital becomes a desolate ruin, the nightingale doesn't sing,
My wife is in a distant land, my hand reaches out,
I ask the groom, but the groom won't go.
Come, my wife...

The man with money and the man with power,
Come now—which one is superior?
That one's a lie, this one's a lie too.
It's too narrow, too narrow—…
The sixth folk song is entirely different in nature from the previous examples; it presents a worldview.
There is truly a point of transcending the world through clear insight.
Turkish songs are extremely concise, and furthermore, they are musical, so I will transcribe it in Roman letters. <142>
mal Sahaby! milk Sahaby!
Kimdir bunun ilk Sahaby?
oda yalan buda yalan!
Bir azda, gel, Sen oyalan!

The children of men say,
Though clothes be scarlet red,
Though bones shine like pearls,
In the end, they enter the earth.
Even the parrot in the cage,
When its time comes, will always fly away…
This folk song, like the sixth, expresses a worldview, but it is much more religious and world-weary.
It reflects the color appearing in Islam (回回敎), that is, the principle of emptiness (空). The cage is the body, and the parrot is the spirit. It symbolizes the spirit reaching God's side after death.
Whenever I see this folk song, I feel drawn into a world of darkness, sensing infinite sorrow in the life and fate of a lonely island amidst the battling stormy waves. (End) <143>

**Summary**

This article by Kang Un'gok, writing from Cheju Island in 1923, introduces Korean readers to Turkish folk songs (Tuerki). It outlines the place of folk literature within Turkish literary tradition, emphasizes the innate musicality of Turkish and related Tatar peoples, and notes the influence of neighboring cultures on Turkish music. The author explains structural elements like poetic form and, notably, vowel harmony (協韻法), providing examples. Seven translated folk songs illustrate themes of love (joyful and sorrowful), parting, and philosophical/religious worldviews, including fatalism and Islamic concepts of life and death. The author acknowledges the difficulty of capturing the original's formal beauty, especially vowel harmony, in Korean translation. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as an example of cultural curiosity and introduction of a relatively distant, non-Western/non-Japanese culture (Turkey) within colonial Korea, potentially offering readers a different cultural mirror or escape. The author's location on Cheju Island adds another layer, writing about distant lands from a place often considered peripheral itself.

						濟州에서 康雲谷


土耳其의 문학은 보통, 고전문학, 민중문학 及 근대문학의 3대구별이 잇슴니다.
其中 민중문학은 그들의 민중생활과 함끠 특별히 발달되엿는데, 작가로 유명한 사람도 만히 잇고, 연구의 대상으로 해서도 상당한 가치도 잇스며 흥미도 잇슴니다. 소설, 각본, 동화, 서사시, 서정시 등 우수한 작품이 不少하나 여긔 소개하랴는 것은 가요(튤키)이외다.
이 가요를 土語로 Tuerki라 칭하나, 이것은 獨逸의 volkslied에 갓가운 것인대, 우리게서는 민요의 부류에 들 것임니다.
土耳其의 민요에는 실로 자미잇는 것이 만히 잇슴니다. 일체히 土耳其 其 민족은 누구나 음악의 熱愛者이요, 이들 土耳其人이나 歐洲 로시아의 東部南과 中央亞細亞 등에 거주하는 종종의 타탈인(이쪽에서는 가요를 에-ㄹ, 혹은 찌-ㄹ이라 함니다)들도 同樣으로, 正히 그들의 민족성이라 할 수 잇슴니다. 그럼으로 남녀노소를 물론하고, 여가만 잇스면 악기를 톄우며 노래를 부름니다.
그리고 좀 취미 잇는 자는 자작의 시에 자기의 곡조를 붓처서 상호간 질겨하며 노래함니다. 음악은 생활의 중요되는 일부이외다. 그럼으로 土耳其 首府 콘스탄티노풀과 갓흔 大都會에서는 매일 음악회를 개최한다 하며 문예잡지에는 음악에 관한 기사와 악보가 게재되며, 작가 작곡의 모집이 성행함니다. 土耳其의 노래는, 우리 朝鮮, 西歐 諸國, 露西亞와도 특이하야, 그들의 톡특한 특수의 곡절이 잇서서, 대체 中央亞細亞, 波斯, 아라비아＜137＞ 알맨니아, 希臘 등의 가요와 갓치, 이들 近東의 諸 민족에 공통되는 곡조를 띄어잇슴니다. 더구나 此等 諸 민족이 각자의 특징 잇는 민족적 선율을 가저 잇는 것은 물론이외다.



그러나 지금붓터 말하쟈 하는 것은 土耳其 민요의 곡절이 아니요, 그의 형식과 내용이외다.
민요는 시의 形을 취하야 잇는 고로, 장단도 잇스나, 얼마의 한정한 行數에 분배되야, 字數, 두운법, 각운법 등의 여러가지 약속에 의하야 종종 변화의 잇는 것에 맨드라 잇스나, 他에도 가곡을 일층, 더, 아름답게, 더 조화잇는 것에 맨드러 주는 요소가 잇슴니다. 이것은 土耳其 其語의 특징(더욱, 우랄, 알타이계통에 속하는 언어는 그러하나)되는 協韻法의 법칙이외다. 협운법이라 함은, 모음을 강약에 난후와, 强母音은 항상 强母音에 합하고, 弱母音은 항상 母音에 합하는 문법상의 규칙인데, 자음의 중에도, 어느 것은 强母音에 붓흐고, 어느 것은 弱母音에 붓흔다는 분류가 되여 잇는 것도 잇슴니다.
强母音은 a, y, o, u이고 弱母音은 ae, e, i, oe, ue인데 지금 한 예를 들면,
강                                    약
Tash            (石)                  Dish           (齒)
Tashly          (石製의)              Dishli          (齒의 잇는)
Tashdan        (石으로)              Dishden         (齒으로)
Tashlar         (石의 復數)           Dishler          (齒의 復數)
Bulajaqsyn      (네가 차즐 터이지-)   Sewejeksin      (네가 사랑할 터이지)
Bulmadym-my  (나는 찻지 안햇더냐)  Sewmedim-mi   (나는 사랑하지 안햇더냐)
이러케 劃然히 강약이 구별해 잇서서, 외래어(이것도 土語化한 것은 협운법에 의함)와 1, 2의 예외의 외에는＜138＞ 모도, 이 규칙이 一絲도 配치 안하야 行하야 잇고, 그것이 土耳其語의 본질임으로, 일상의 회화로붓터 雅俗과 산문, 시문의 別이 업시, 엇더한 것일지라도 이 필법에 의함니다. 그럼으로 시가와 갓흔 他의 약속에서, 임의 미화된 것은 이 협운법으로, 일층 더 온순한 조화적 음율이 되야 響動함니다. 그래서 그의 실례로 제1, 제6,의 가요에는 로-마자로 綴하야 添書햇슴니다.
더구나, 곡선미의 풍부한 아라비아문자로 보지 안으면 土耳其 詩의 기분이 충분히 안나올 터이나, 여긔서는 될 수 잇는 대로 갓가운 발음으로써, 土耳其 가요의 윤곽을 표시하갯슴니다.



土耳其 민요의 재료에는 여러가지 잇스나, 대강은, 연애와 환희를 노래한 것에 우수한 것이 만히 잇슴니다. 기타, 세상과 사람의 운명을 노래한 것과 전설에 잇는 인물을 제재로 한 것도 잇슴니다.
실례라 해서는 7장의 민요를 記하얏는대, 그 가운데는 土耳其 근대 작가 이쩻트, 아례쀠-씨의 소설 「불상한 편지」와 기타, 그 나라의 근대 작가요, 또 문예비평가인, 리다, 테뿌휘-크씨의 논문 「민속학」으로부터 발출한 것임니다. 氏는 土耳其 其 俚諺과 민요를 계통적으로 연구한 사람이외다.
그런데 土耳其歌의 형식상의 특징과 전술의 협운법 등은 도저히 우리 조선어로는 表치 못하야, 行數만 그대로 하고, 형식은 우리의 민요에 갓갑게 하야서 내용은 될 수 잇는대로 素直하게 譯하엿슴니다.

가만히 보고 잇는 瑠璃의 눈을,
아름다운 姿軆에 이기지 못하야＜139＞
앗침날 빗취어도 안이러난다.
아무렴, 우리는 젊은 때인데
자- 술먹자…엇지하야 너와 나와 떠나겟느냐.
연애의 도취라 밀하면 조흘넌지? 濃艶한 장면이 눈에 빗춤니다. 이 가요는-편의상 로-마자로 다음에 썻슴니다, 이것에는 과거의 때를 사용해 잇스나 譯文에는 기분을 차게 하기 위하야 일부러 현재로 해 두엇슴니다.

이와 갓치 협운법은 직혀잇슴니다. 이 협운과 시의 여러가지 형식미와 歌의 내용과 그것에 상응한 곡절과, 그리고 반주악기가 잇스면 그의 음색과 諸 分子가 溶合되여서 土耳其 민요의 전체가 되는 것이외다.
