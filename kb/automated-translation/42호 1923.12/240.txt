自由通情
新聞雜誌들아

平壤館後里 尹經式

너히들은 한동안 떠드러가며 열성 열심으로 선전해 주던 일이 잇섯다. 만히 오래지 안이은 일이닛가 너히도 기억할 듯하다. 物産獎勵, 禁酒斷烟, 民立大學, 靑年團軆 등에 대한 소식말이다. 너히들은 한동안 이 모든 아름다운 소식을 네의 품에 한아름式 안고 日刊으로 月刊으로 작구작구 煽傳해 주엇다. 그리하야 甲地에서 土産을 獎勵한다면 乙地에서도 金某가 禁酒斷煙을 한다면 李某도 此郡에서 民大地方部를 설립한다면 彼郡에서도 邑內에서 靑年團軆를 조직한다면 村里에서도 역시 조직하야섯다. 이것은 모다 너히 힘이다. 너희 공로이다. 동시에 우리는 너히게 대하야 감사하엿다. 그런대 近者에는 너히게로부터서 이에 대한 소식은 一切로 들어불 수가 업스니 너히난 죽엇느냐 살앗느냐.
신문, 잡지들아 너히는 무엇하고 잇느냐. 先何心이며 後何心이냐. 처음에 그러케 선전한 것은 무슨 뜻이며 지금 이러케 溟黙하는 것은 무슨 뜻이냐. 처음브터 본심에는 업난 일이지만은 사람들이 하도 떠드니 爲先 그대로 게재하야 일시 독자의 환심이나 사서 新聞張이나 雜誌卷이나 팔아보자 함인가. 그러치 아느면 그때 일은 그때 일이엿거니와 이제는 엇던 새로운 견지에서 새로운 主義를 가지게 됨으로 緣함인가. 내가 그 내면을 모르거니와 여하간 꼬리가 너무도 빠르다. 변덕이 너무 심하다. 開闢아 아마 너는 各道號을 부록으로 발행하기 때문에 紛忙하야서 또난 10월호, 11호에 物産獎勵에 대한 평론도 잇섯다고 답변할 줄을 안다. 그러나 이것도 충분한 변명은 못되겟지. 그러고 東亞日報는 日躍號 편집에 每日申報는 總督政治 선전에 분주들 하는 모양이다만은 朝鮮日報나 기타 잡지들은 또 무엇을 하고 잇는고.
(以下 略)

Article Title: 震災 後의 東京에서 (From Tokyo After the Earthquake)
Author: 姜斌

(先略) 華麗를 자랑하든 東京 사치를 경쟁하든 東京은 지금은 幾 世紀前 옛 일이 되고 板木과 洋鐵로 주서 세운 바락구 집이 江戶 뜰에 点点이 잇슬 뿐이외다. 지금도 이전 大 건물의 燒餘個所를 爆發식히는 소래 故 전쟁에 彷彿하외다. 貧寒의 東京 悽慘의 東京 가운대에서 직접 口腹에 관계업는 공부를 하겟다고 冊褓를 끼고 단이는 우리는 狂氣의 感이 적지 안슴니다. 하여간 東京은 우리의 상상하던 이상으로 別有天地올시다.
자연의 압에는 인생이 人工이 이러케도 脆弱한 것을 生覺할 때에 소름을 끼치지 안을 수 업슴니다.
더욱이 東海道線의 最難工事處 酒勻墜道를 통과할 때에는 실로 恐懼를 초월하야 自暴치 안을 수 엄슴듸다. 前月 28일부터 개통은 되엿다 하나 각 道의 내부가 아직 균열된대로 잇을 뿐 外라. 굴의 中특에서는 天井이 문허저서 天井밧게 人間靑天이 뵈이는대는 汗出沾背치 안을 수 업습듸다. 어려서 儒佛의 混合酒에 취하엿던 몸이 其后 外來新品의 基督에 잠시 傾心이 되엿다가 학교교육을 밧음에 밋처서는 乾坤一變에 과학 만능과 현실 최상주의로 일생을 통과하고저 하든 이 愚者 姜斌도 이제 이르러서는 一段의 懷疑가 업지 못함니다.(中略)
今日天道敎宗理院에 갓섯는데 마참 朴思稷씨는 出他하야 면회치 못하고 其外 諸氏를 방문하엿는대 日問歸國할넌지 알 수 업다고 말합듸다.

Article Title: 忠州의 特産品 (Ch'ungju's Special Product)
Author: 金基鎭

小春兄
떠나올 때에 京城驛에서 붓친 편지는 보셧겟슴니다. 小波兄으로붓터 感親文 「마음의 廢墟」를 보셧슴닛가. 이곳은 두메 속이 올시다. 그래도 전에는 觀察道가 이곳에 잇서서 湖西에서는 엄지손고락 꼽든 곳이라 함니다. 읍내에는 日本人이 거지 반 점령하고 잇슴니다. 이곳에서는 유일의 특산물이 잇는데 그것은 黃色煙草라고 함니다.
黃色煙草는 납세업시 재배한다는대 전답이 대개는 이 黃色煙草 경작지로 사용된다지요. 한집에서는 1년에 수만 원식 생산하는 집도 잇담니다. 黃色煙草는 빗이 황금갓다는 뜻인 듯 합니다. 보통 다른 담베보다 더 줄기가 길고 닙새가 만하서 수획이 만코 그 정제법은 밧해서 담배 닙새를 따서 건조실에 집어느코서는 120도까지의 화력 건조를 한담니다. 그래가지고서는 그것을 밤에는 이슬을 맛처가지고서 다시 추긴 뒤에 한 묵금식 묵금니다. 그 빗이 꼭 황금빛이애요. 總督府 專賣局 出張所가 잇서 그것을 거더 드림니다. 민간에서 재배된 것이 1등 2등으로 14등까지 잇서서 1등 1貫에 7원식 한다지요. 보통 6,7등이 만흔대 大槪은 1,2천 貫식은 한집에서 생산하는 모양이래요. 그런데 1貫 5원식이면 그것도 굉장함니다. 그러나 그것을 민간에서는 끽연하지 못함니다. 專賣局에서 엄금이라지요. 만약에 발각될 時는 30원 이상의 벌금이라지요. 맛은 여간 靑紙煙보다 낫다지요. 이것은 꼭 외국으로 수출된 담니다. 朝鮮내에서는 구경할 수도 업다지요. 이것이 忠州의 특산품이람니다.

**English Translation**

**Free Communication**
**To the Newspapers and Magazines**

Yun Kyŏngsik, Kwanhuri, P'yŏngyang

For a while, you clamored with fervor and enthusiasm, promoting certain causes. Since it wasn't too long ago, you probably remember. I'm talking about news regarding Product Promotion (物産獎勵), Prohibition and Smoking Cessation (禁酒斷烟), the People's University (民立大學), Youth Groups (靑年團軆), and the like. For a time, you eagerly embraced all this wonderful news, continuously propagandizing (煽傳) it daily and monthly in your pages. Consequently, if Place A promoted local products, Place B would too; if Mr. Kim undertook Prohibition and Smoking Cessation, Mr. Lee would too; if this county established a local branch of the People's University, that county would too; if a Youth Group was organized in the town center, one would also be organized in the villages. This was all your power. Your achievement. At the same time, we were grateful to you. But recently, we cannot hear any news about these things from you at all. Are you dead or alive?

Newspapers, magazines, what are you doing? What was your intention before, and what is it now? What was the meaning of propagandizing like that initially, and what is the meaning of being in such deep silence (溟黙) now? Was it something you didn't truly believe in from the start, but since people were making such a fuss, you decided to publish it anyway just to temporarily win readers' favor and sell some newspaper copies or magazine volumes? Or is it that, while those times were those times, you have now come to hold new principles from some new perspective? I don't know the inner situation, but in any case, your tail turns too quickly. Your fickleness is too severe. Kaebyŏk, perhaps you will reply that you are busy because you publish separate issues for each province as supplements, and also that there were critiques regarding Product Promotion in the October and November issues. But this won't be a sufficient excuse, will it? And it seems the Tonga Ilbo is busy editing its Sunday edition, and the Maeil Sinbo is busy propagandizing the Government-General's administration, but what are the Chosŏn Ilbo and other magazines doing?
(Remainder Omitted)

**From Tokyo After the Earthquake**

November 10th, Kang Pin

(Beginning Omitted) Tokyo, which boasted of splendor, Tokyo, which competed in luxury, is now a thing of centuries past. Only barracks (바락구) houses, hastily built from planks and tin sheets, dot the Edo (江戶) plains. Even now, the sound of dynamiting the burnt remains of former large buildings resembles the sound of war. In the midst of impoverished Tokyo, miserable Tokyo, for us, carrying our book bundles (冊褓) intending to pursue studies unrelated to immediate sustenance, there is a considerable sense of madness. Anyway, Tokyo is truly a different world, beyond what we imagined.

When I think about how fragile human life and human creations are before nature, I cannot help but shudder.

Moreover, when passing through the Sakawa Tunnel (酒勻墜道 - likely 酒匂隧道), the most difficult construction site on the Tōkaidō Line (東海道線), I truly transcended fear and could not help but despair. Although they say it reopened from the 28th of last month, the interior of each tunnel section is still cracked. In the middle of the tunnel, where the ceiling has collapsed and the human blue sky outside the ceiling is visible, one cannot help but have sweat soak one's back (汗出沾背). Having been intoxicated by the mixed wine of Confucianism and Buddhism in childhood, then briefly inclined towards the newly arrived foreign import of Christianity, and upon receiving school education, intending to pass through life with the belief in the omnipotence of science and the supremacy of reality after a complete change (乾坤一變), even this fool Kang Pin has now reached a point where I cannot avoid a degree of doubt. (Middle Omitted)

Today, I went to the Ch'ŏndogyo Central Mission (天道敎宗理院), but Mr. Pak Sajik happened to be out, so I couldn't meet him. I visited the other gentlemen, and they said they didn't know on which day he would return to Korea.

**Introducing Taedong Academy**

Person from Ningguta (寧古塔人)

Here in Ningguta (古塔), Jilin Province (吉林省), China, a private school called Taedong Academy (大同學院), roughly equivalent to a middle school, has now been established by the hands of our people and will open starting December 1st.＜114＞ This school is said to reject the conventional, traditional education that forces humans to accept the existence of slavery and rationalizes conquest as its principle. Instead, its purpose is to cultivate and promote the human instincts of freedom, equality, and mutual aid (扶助), thereby fostering talent necessary for the construction of a new social life. Also, those from the Korean mainland (朝鮮 內地) who cannot study due to lack of funds are welcome to come to this academy. If you work at the academy's affiliated farm during the summer, you can earn enough tuition to last for one year. And, though it is impolite to ask, books are extremely precious here. Regardless of the type of book, if you have any books you have finished reading, please donate even one volume to the academy. The curriculum duration is 3 years for the main course and 1 year for the preparatory course. Fees are: entrance fee 2 won, monthly tuition 2 won, monthly boarding fee 3 won 50 jeon. The contact address is: To Mr. Ch'oe Chuhwa, c/o Kongje-ho Store, In front of the Bogut'a Yamen, Jilin Province, China (中國 吉林省 寶古塔衙門前 共濟號內 崔柱華前).

**Ch'ungju's Special Product**

From Ch'ungju, Kim Kijin

To Soch'un Hyŏng,
You must have seen the letter I sent from Kyŏngsŏng Station when I was leaving. Have you seen the touching piece "Ruins of the Heart" (마음의 廢墟) from Sop'a Hyŏng? This place is deep in the countryside. Still, they say the provincial governor's office (觀察道) used to be here, so it was considered the top spot in the Hoso region. The town center is almost half occupied by Japanese people. There is a unique specialty product here, which they call Yellow Tobacco (黃色煙草).

They say Yellow Tobacco is cultivated without taxation, and most fields are used for its cultivation. Some households reportedly produce tens of thousands of won worth per year. Yellow Tobacco seems to mean its color resembles gold. Compared to other ordinary tobaccos, the stalk is longer and the leaves are more numerous, resulting in a large harvest. The refining method involves picking the tobacco leaves in the field, putting them in a drying room, and drying them with heat up to 120 degrees. After that, they expose it to the dew at night to moisten it again, then tie it up bundle by bundle. Its color is just like gold. There is a branch office of the Government-General's Monopoly Bureau (總督府 專賣局 出張所) that collects it. The tobacco grown by the people is graded from 1st to 14th class, and 1st class fetches 7 won per kwan (貫). Grades 6 and 7 are common, and it seems one household generally produces 1,000-2,000 kwan. At 5 won per kwan, that's quite significant. However, the public cannot smoke it. The Monopoly Bureau strictly forbids it. If caught, the fine is supposedly over 30 won. They say the taste is better than ordinary blue-paper cigarettes (靑紙煙). This tobacco is apparently strictly for export abroad. You supposedly can't even see it within Korea (朝鮮). This is said to be Ch'ungju's special product.
(Remainder Omitted)

**Please Publish a Korean 'Kyŏngju Chronicle'**

Person from Who Knows Where (不知何許人)

(Beginning Omitted) I wish you would cut back a bit on things like poetry and novels, and instead provide easy-to-understand summaries and reports on economic and political matters, especially domestic and foreign news. Also, once the basic survey of Korean culture is complete, I hope you will use that as material, supplement other missing parts, and create and publish a separate booklet. The other day, I went to Taegu and saw a booklet titled "Kyŏngju Chronicle" (慶州誌) at a Japanese bookstore in Motomachi (元町), and I was deeply impressed. That book had a panoramic map of Kyŏngju (慶州) and clearly marked the locations of historical sites, making it quite neat. It would be good if there were a book compiling such things in our language. I especially request this of the Kaebyŏk Company (開闢社).

Hwimun School Third-Year Student (徽文學校 三年級生)

If possible, please introduce the inner workings of middle-level schools, regardless of whether they are in Seoul (京鄕) or the provinces, to the world. Please allow them to receive public critique. The current school authorities act too arbitrarily within the school gates.＜115＞

**Summary**

This collection of short articles and letters from the December 1923 issue of *Kaebyŏk* reflects various concerns of Koreans during the Japanese colonial period. Yun Kyŏngsik criticizes newspapers and magazines for abandoning nationalist campaigns like product promotion and the People's University movement. Kang Pin offers a vivid, sobering account of post-earthquake Tokyo's devastation and his own existential questioning. An anonymous writer introduces a new Korean-run school, Taedong Academy, established in Ningguta, Manchuria, emphasizing self-reliance and alternative education. Kim Kijin describes the lucrative but restricted cultivation of yellow tobacco in Ch'ungju under the Japanese monopoly system. Another anonymous writer requests *Kaebyŏk* publish a Korean-language guide to Kyŏngju's cultural heritage, similar to Japanese publications. Finally, a Hwimun School student asks for investigative reporting on the arbitrary power wielded by authorities within middle schools.

From a historical perspective, these pieces are valuable for showing the ebb and flow of nationalist movements and media engagement (Yun Kyŏngsik), the direct impact of major events like the Great Kanto Earthquake on Koreans studying in Japan (Kang Pin), the establishment of Korean educational institutions outside Korea as potential havens (Taedong Academy), the realities of colonial economic exploitation (Ch'ungju tobacco), and the anxieties surrounding cultural preservation and educational conditions under Japanese rule (Kyŏngju guide request, Hwimun student). They offer glimpses into public discourse, economic life, diasporic activities, and concerns about cultural identity and education in 1920s Korea.
