Miscellaneous Notes from a Western Journey
						Ch'unp'a

Brother Ch'ŏngo, did you see the brief impressions I wrote to you from Anju? I have now arrived in Pakch'ŏn. Maengjung-ni seems a bit more prosperous, but Pakch'ŏn town shows little sign of development. A phenomenon unseen before is that the famous dry fields (乾畓) around the old ferry crossing (舊津) have become lushly irrigated paddy fields (水畓化) during this time of great drought. (This is due to the Pakch'ŏn Water Cooperative (博川水組).)

Brother Ch'ŏngo, as soon as I settled into the inn, Mr. Kim Yeyong of Pakch'ŏn Hospital came looking for me. He is a friend I spent time with in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) over ten years ago. I was extremely glad. "Finding old friends everywhere and pouring out one's heart upon meeting"—this is truly a great comfort during travels. After visiting Mr. Ch'oe Chongjŏng, I immediately went up T'aebong Mountain.

Brother Ch'ŏngo, Pakch'ŏn has famous spots like the Taeryŏng River, Wŏnsu Peak, and Tabok Valley, but among them, the most famous is this T'aebong. Just like Moran Peak in P'yŏngyang, Pugak Mountain in Kyŏngsŏng, or Osan in T'aech'ŏn, it is the guardian mountain and main peak right behind Pakch'ŏn town. It's a natural park dense with pine forests. In front lie the thousand-household town streets, resembling a Go board (棋局), and the Kujin Plain; behind, the Hong Kyŏngnae River and the Taeryŏng River wind around. Wŏnsu Peak is directly opposite, and Tabok Valley is right below Wŏnsu Peak. Sitting atop the sky-high cliff (天丈絶崖) on the bank of the Taeryŏng River at the rear foot of T'aebong, looking across at Tabok Valley and reminiscing about the deeds of the hero Hong Kyŏngnae in those days, I am truly filled with deep emotion. The mountains and rivers remain as before, but the lament arises, "Where have the great figures gone?" Looking once more at Wŏnsu Peak, glancing down once more at the Taeryŏng River, and thinking once more of Hong Kyŏngnae, I wound my way along the Taeryŏng River bank back to the inn. Mr. Han Ch'ansŏp came to visit. Gathering Messrs. Ch'oe, Kim, and Han, we exchanged our thoughts until late at night. The next morning, after roughly attending to my business, I headed for Chŏngju on the 1 PM train.

Brother Ch'ŏngo, at Unjŏn Station, I saw a country gentleman fall hard and crumple while jumping off the train, which sent a chill down my spine. I heard he was someone going to visit a certain fortune teller (占卜者) in Kwaksan. Unaware, he had boarded a first-class car, then got frightened and tried to move to a third-class car when it happened. Being a country person, he obviously lacked common sense about boarding and alighting trains, but the fact that he was going to see a fortune teller during the peak farming season (農事方劇) drew my anger before my sympathy. However, <103> the tragic sight of him, unable to avoid his misfortune, lying there pouring blood, was truly chilling. I heard his life is likely not in danger.

Brother Ch'ŏngo, rain is drizzling. It's a welcome rain (喜雨), a sweet rain (甘雨). The farmers (農軍) are extremely happy. I was happy too. When I got off at Chŏngju Station, it was raining heavily. Despite the rain, Mr. Kim Tohyŏn from the branch office came to meet me right at the station head. It's quite a distance from the station to the town center. Being mid-journey, clothing was the biggest issue, and also my luggage (行具). After much thought, though against my will, I took a rickshaw as a temporary measure. My face flushed as if I were committing some crime. As the saying goes, "Tigers appear in fearsome valleys," and indeed, I encountered trouble. When the rickshaw had gone about one block (一馬町), sturdy men (壯漢) from behind shouted repeatedly, "You bastard, walk! Are your legs broken? Everyone else is walking, and you sit there pushing ahead, you bastard!" My heart sank. I felt like they would grab the rickshaw shafts and pull me right out; even my hair stood on end. I wanted to jump out immediately and apologize, but doing so after things had already gone this far seemed even more cowardly (庸拙). I wanted to plead verbally, "It was not my intention, please forgive me. Being mid-journey, I had no choice because of my clothes and luggage," but stuck in the rickshaw traffic (前遮後基의 車獄 - lit. prison of carts blocking front and rear?), I couldn't do that either and just broke out in sweat. I urged the rickshaw puller to hurry, hurry, as pursuers were close, but in such mud, this too was unreasonable. Moreover, he was Chinese and didn't understand my words. It was truly awkward. Upon reaching the branch office gate, I felt my heart calm down as if I had escaped a major crisis.

Hearing Mr. Kim Tohyŏn's story, who came later, those men were farmers (農軍) from outside Chŏngju's South Gate. They were farmers carrying pitchforks (소스랑), wearing hoes (호미), with their sleeves and pant legs rolled up. They were farmers who had worked since early morning, plowing and weeding, shedding hot sweat all day. I deserved to be cursed by them. Rather, I was thankful. I wanted to send a letter of apology (陳謝文) addressed "To the Farmers outside Chŏngju South Gate." These were not false words (假言), but my true feelings. I resolved never to ride a rickshaw again, no matter the circumstances.

Brother Ch'ŏngo, Brother Kim Kongsŏn at the branch office seems to be spending his time quite busily (奔忙). He said they are organizing a Farmers' Federation (農民聯合會) and, sponsored by the Federation, are holding a Large Farmers' Athletic Meet using the Tano holiday (端陽佳節). So, he seems very busy, holding meetings daily with various comrades focusing on the farmers' movement. Hong Chonghyŏn,
Sŭng Ch'ŏnu,
and Sŏ Inhwa came to visit. <104>

Brother Ch'ŏngo, the next day, I went to Koŭp Station with Brother Kim Kongsŏn. I visited Messrs. Ch'oe Ch'angsŏ,
Ch'oe Sŏngje,
and Kim Kihong and gained much sympathy. I am glad that wherever I go, there isn't much hostility. This must be not for the individual named Ch'unp'a, but for the organization called Kaebyŏk Company (개벽사). A distinct sense of responsibility arises to work even more faithfully for the company. I spent an enjoyable night with rural companions at Mr. Ch'oe Sŏngje's house. The taste of ground soybean residue stew (되비지) was better than that of the breeding chicken (種鷄).

Brother Ch'ŏngo, what's most famous here is peat (土炭), locally called *nŏk* (넉). Seeing much peat dug up and drying on every paddy ridge, I initially thought it was just mud (泥土) from digging a pond (못), but seeing mud piled up like mountains in the yards of every house, I realized it was peat. (Only after asking). I heard that near this Ilsin-dong or near Osan, peat is most abundant, and it can be dug up limitlessly from the paddy fields. After digging it up for fuel, they can still plant rice in those fields. This is what they call "eating the pheasant and the egg too"; these fields produce rice and fuel. It's a blessed land (福地). However, because human effort (人工) is still lacking, it cannot be exported elsewhere, and even locally, many say it's less convenient than firewood charcoal (柴炭) from the mountains. It is a good fuel (好燃料) that can be well utilized in the future.

Pressed for time, I couldn't visit Osan School and went into Chŏngju town. <105>

Brother Ch'ŏngo, as the journey becomes tedious, so do the miscellaneous tidbits of news and sights (雜聞雜見). The writing hastily tumbles out like a deer running.
In Kwaksan, I met Yi Kugyŏng,
Sin Yukwŏn,
Yi Chinch'u, and several other companions, and we enjoyed Kwaksan's famous *panjihoe* (반지膾) (similar to Seoul's *ungŏhoe* - icefish sashimi) and fish soup (*ŏbukguk*). Then, visiting farming villages like Imhae-myŏn and Kwanju-myŏn, I heard about rural affairs and saw the educational situation from Messrs. Kim Uisu,
Chi Unghyŏk,
Ch'oe Chunggyŏm,
and Kang Hunch'ae. Among them, the irrigated fields (水畓) of Imhae-myŏn and Yŏngch'ang School in Kwanju-myŏn remain fresh in my memory. I briefly visited Sŏnch'ŏn, had discussions overnight with all the branch members and Mr. Yi Kun'o, then went to Ch'ŏlsan. There, I associated with Pak Yŏng,
Chŏng Yunsŏk,
Kim Myŏngsu,
Song Kŭnju,
Chŏng Yuch'ŏl,
Pak Pongsu, and others in the town and villages for two days. On the way back, I stopped at Ch'aryŏn'gwan, and under the guidance of Messrs. Ch'oe Yongun,
Chŏng Yonghyŏk,
Kim Chŏnghak,
and Yu Yŏngsŏn, I toured the town streets and farming villages before embarking on the return journey. There were no particularly strange occurrences or unusual sights.

What Hell Upon First Entering Kyŏngsŏng

Brother Ch'ŏngo, at the end of writing these *Miscellaneous Notes from a Western Journey*, shall I write about the five days in the detention cell (유치장)? You were there for seven or eight days, weren't you? Soch'un,
Sŏkkye, *Ch'on, Sŏp'a – everyone <106> took part in this gamble, didn't we? Bearing the label of being Korean, and moreover, with the times being what they are, could we possibly avoid it even if we tried?

Brother Ch'ŏngo, this is what they call "leaving is hard, coming is also hard," and "escaping the sand pit only to enter hell." Will Koreans always be in this state?

Brother Ch'ŏngo, that day was precisely June 7th. That was when you, brother, were already getting a taste of the detention cell. How could a traveler from the Western region (西隅) have anticipated this incident? With the times being what they are, there was only the question, "Might something happen?" but it's true I hadn't hit the bullseye regarding this incident. That night, I slept at Mr. Yi T'aeha's house in Sukch'ŏn, and was scheduled to take the 1 PM direct train the next day. I missed Seoul terribly, as if some great solution awaited me there. Perhaps it was because I had been away for over a month. The train time seemed incredibly slow, as if someone were waiting for me. In the end! The solution was that solution, and the person waiting was that person. When I came out to Sukch'ŏn Station to wait for the train, an official (俗所謂 나으리) asked for my residence and name (居住姓名). When I stated my identity (本色), he turned away without further comment. After boarding the train and traveling a short while, two or three officials in Western suits came back and forth through the car, their eyes red, roaming around just like hunting dogs searching for a tiger's den. If anyone looked slightly sharp or suspicious, they would ask, "Who are you? Where do you live? What do you do?", pull out the requisite notebook, ask, write, criticize (힐난), and sometimes even search luggage (行具). I, too, caught their eye. Even after giving my business card, I had to show my luggage. The way they strutted about was truly sickening to watch.

When we arrived in P'yŏngyang, there must have been over a thousand passengers. They seemed to add one or two more cars. Among that crowd, I happened to see Mr. Yi Tan. After greetings, I asked if he was going to Seoul. He said, "I'm going tomorrow," and got off, but then suggested we travel together the next day. So I strongly urged him, "Traveling together tomorrow is not as good as traveling together today." Thus, he bought his ticket on the train and became my travel companion. As we settled into our seats, Mr. Yi said, "It seems something has happened in Seoul. Just now, riding the streetcar past the P'yŏngyang Maeil Company office, there was a clearly posted bulletin with banner headlines. Due to the streetcar's speed, I couldn't read the full text clearly, but I did see '90-something surrounding the Ch'ŏndogyo headquarters.'" We were both curious, not knowing what the incident was. When we reached Hwangju, there was a change of inspecting officials (點人 나으리), and another change occurred at Kaesŏng. After passing Changdan, around Ilsan, they again looked closely at our appearance and asked who we were. Upon hearing our names, they seemed to recognize us, saying, "Is that so?" and turned back without further questions. Finally, upon arriving at Kyŏngsŏng Station, none of the expected welcomers were in sight. Feeling quite disappointed <107>, the suspicion "Has something indeed happened?" flared up. As I came out the exit, two ladies met me outside the station building (構外). While exchanging greetings, that Japanese official (日人 나으리) from the train handed us over to another Japanese person, saying, "This one is Kaebyŏk Company, this one is Chidai Nippo (referring to Mr. Yi)." "So-ka, chotto matte (Is that so? Wait a moment)," he said, and then dragged us to the station police box (驛前 파출소). Three or four young men were already detained at the police box, and more kept coming in after us. We underwent body searches and luggage inspections for nearly an hour and a half. When we said we would leave, they told us to wait a bit, then made a phone call to either Chongno or Honmachi, and then said we had to go to the main station (本署), assigning a Korean official (조선 나으리) to escort the two of us.

Eventually, we arrived at the Higher Affairs Section (高等係) of the Honmachi Police Station (本町警察署). It was past eleven o'clock. They said the chief wasn't in and told us to sit and wait. They ordered dinner, and being bored, I looked at the newspaper lying on the table. As soon as I opened it to page 3, something hugely caught my eye: sensational headlines proclaiming, "Ch'ŏndogyo Hall surrounded by 9 automobiles, numerous subversive documents seized, Ch'ŏndogyo executives So-and-so, Kaebyŏk Company So-and-so, and over 200 others arrested," and so on. "Something has happened," I thought, and as I read down carefully, a Korean official came over, saying, "Please don't look. This is a confiscated newspaper," and took it away. "Take it. I've seen enough," I replied, and while eating dinner amidst compounding questions, the person who seemed to be the chief came in. He briefly questioned each person in turn (besides us, there were 2 others), then said he would ask again tomorrow and sent us one by one to the detention cell. It was past one in the morning.

Thus, upon first entering Kyŏngsŏng, I got my first taste of the detention cell. Only one guard was on duty (守直); all the rooms were asleep. There was a small empty space next to the latrine bucket (똥통), so I went there, collapsed, lay down, pondered the incident this way and that, and being utterly exhausted, finally fell asleep. In my sleep, I heard someone shout, "Fuku dasse yo! (복구닷세어 - likely garbled Japanese, maybe 服を出せよ 'Fuku o dase yo!' meaning 'Bring out the clothes!' or similar police jargon)". Rubbing my eyes, I got up, and a detective (형사 나으리) unlocked the cell door with a click and told me to come out. "What's going on in the middle of the night?" I wondered as I went out, and they tied my waist with a rope. I was dragged up to the Higher Affairs Section. The clock struck four sharp. Being an emergency period (비상시기), and since they suspected me, in their own way, of being a serious criminal (重大犯), they conducted an extraordinary interrogation. I recounted the entire story (자초지종) of my trip to the Western region (西鮮行) chronologically, like reading from a journal (日誌). I had committed no crime, but it was extremely distressing. While the whole of Kyŏngsŏng lay asleep, being interrogated alone at four in the morning felt unjust and unbearably infuriating (분통하다). After about an hour, I returned to the detention cell; the eastern window was already bright, and all <108> the inmates (因徒) seemed to be waking from their light sleep (선잠), stretching and yawning (하폄). Some fellows were perched over the latrine bucket, pooping noisily (뿌지직 뿌지직), while others groaned, "Aigu, aigu (아구데구)."

After cleaning up, wiping my face with a rag, and eating a lunchbox (변도) fit for a cat (괴양이 밥통), I exchanged greetings with my fellow inmates (同留人). Besides Chŏn Ch'ihang from Taeryŏn (Dalian) and Ch'oe Sangdŏk, who had entered the previous night with me, the others were thieves, pickpockets, and opium offenders. Mr. Yi Tan in the opposite cell occasionally looked over and smiled with his eyes (눈우슴).
(In the original text, about 20 lines below this are deleted - National Institute of Korean History)

Brother Ch'ŏngo, in this affair, it was extremely regrettable that Brother Hŏ Ikhwan suffered such harsh beatings and endured hardship with us for 4 or 5 days without reason (though it was the same for everyone), and that Mr. Yi Tan suffered similarly for 4 or 5 days merely on suspicion due to traveling with me. However, being in the same situation, there's no place to ask or answer about regret, is there? We just laughed it off amongst ourselves. What else could we do? Isn't that right, Brother Ch'ŏngo? <109>

**Summary**

This travelogue by "Ch'unp'a," published in 1926, details his journey through northwestern Korea (Anju, Pakch'ŏn, Chŏngju, Kwaksan, Sŏnch'ŏn, Ch'ŏlsan). He describes the landscape, local developments (like irrigation projects and peat extraction), encounters with friends and local figures associated with his magazine (Kaebyŏk), and reflects on historical figures like Hong Kyŏngnae. A significant portion recounts a humiliating experience with aggressive farmers while riding a rickshaw in Chŏngju. The narrative culminates dramatically with his return to Seoul (Kyŏngsŏng) on June 7th, where he and a companion are immediately detained by police amidst a major crackdown targeting Ch'ŏndogyo and Kaebyŏk Company figures, leading to a five-day stint in a police detention cell.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into rural conditions, infrastructure development (trains, irrigation), local economies (peat), and social tensions (rickshaw incident) in colonial Korea during the mid-1920s. Most importantly, the latter part provides a firsthand account of the political repression and surveillance faced by Korean intellectuals and activists, specifically detailing the arbitrary arrests and interrogations following an unspecified incident (likely related to the June 10th Movement of 1926, which occurred shortly after the death of Emperor Sunjong and involved planned protests, heavily suppressed by Japanese authorities). The mention of numerous arrests involving Ch'ŏndogyo and Kaebyŏk personnel aligns perfectly with the historical context of the June 10th Movement crackdown.

西行雜記
						春坡
靑吾형, 安州서 적어드린 소감 一節은 보섯는지오. 지금은 博川에 왓슴니다. 孟中里는 좀더 번창한 감이 잇스나 博川邑은 별로 발전의 기색이 업슴니다. 舊津 전후의 그 유명하든 乾畓들이 이 大旱時節에 綠水洋洋의 水畓化가 된 것이 전에 못보든 현상임니다. (博川水組로 인해서)
靑吾형. 여관을 정하자 곳 博川醫院의 金禮容씨가 차저 줌니다. 그는 10여년전 京城서 가티 지내든 친구임니다. 퍽 반가웟슴니다. 到處有故人逢輒相吐情ㅡ이야말로 旅中의 일대 위로임니다. 崔宗楨씨를 차저보고 곳 台峯山에 올나갓슴니다.
靑吾형. 博川의 명소는 大寧江도 잇고 元帥峯도 잇고 多福洞도 잇지만 其中 명소는 바로 이 台峯임니다. 平壤의 牧丹峯이나 京城의 北岳이나 또는 泰川의 烏山가티 바로 博川邑의 鎭山이요 主峯임니다. 송림이 욱어진 천연공원임니다. 前面에는 棋局가튼 千戶市街와 舊津平野가 노여 잇고 後面은 洪景來江과 大寧江이 휘도라 잇슴니다. 元帥峯은 바로 건너편이요 多福洞은 바로 元帥峯下임니다. 台峯後麓 大寧江岸 天丈絶崖 우에 안저 多福洞을 건너다 보며 洪景來 好漢의 當年事를 追想해 보니 과연 감회무량임니다. 山河는 依舊한데 人傑은 何處去의 嘆이 생김니다. 元帥峯을 한 번 더 처다보고 大寧江을 한 번 더 굽어보고 洪景來를 한 번 더 생각하면서 大寧江邊을 휘도라 여관으로 도라왓슴니다. 韓燦燮씨가 차저줌니다. 崔, 金, 韓 諸氏를 모시고 夜深토록 所懷를 相換하고 翌朝에 볼 일을 대강 보고 오후 1시 차로 定州를 향하얏슴니다.

靑吾형. 雲田驛에서 엇던 촌양반이 飛降을 하다가 몹시 닷쳐 꺽구러진 것을 보고 가슴이 서늘하얏슴니다. 드르닛가 그는 郭山 某占卜者를 차저가든 사람으로 멋모르고 一等車를 탓다가 겁이 왈칵 나서 三等車로 옴겨 타려다가 그리 되얏다 함니다. 촌사람인지라 승차 하차에 대한 상식도 毋論 결여하겟지만 農事方劇에 占卜者를 차저가든 그것이 동정보다 욕이 먼저 감니다. 그러나＜103＞ 當厄을 未免하야 피를 콸콸 쏫으며 넘어져 잇는 그 慘景은 실로 가슴이 서늘함니다. 드르닛가 생명에는 관계가 업스리라 함니다.
靑吾형. 비가 부슬부슬 내림니다. 喜雨요 甘雨임니다. 農軍들은 퍽도 깃버함니다. 나도 깃벗슴니다. 定州驛에 내리닛가 비가 몹시 내림니다. 雨中임도 불구하고 支社의 金道賢씨가 驛頭까지 마저줌니다. 驛에서 성내까지 가기는 꽤 먼 거리임니다. 旅中이라 무엇보다 의복이 문제이고 또는 行具가 문제엿슴니다. 생각다 못하야 본의는 안이지만 임시방편으로 인력거를 탓슴니다. 무슨 죄나 범하는 듯 얼굴이 확근확근해졋슴니다. 「무서운 골에 범(虎) 난다.」고 안인게 안이라 봉변을 당하얏슴니다. 인력거가 약 一馬町쯤 가자 뒤에서 엇던 壯漢들이 「이놈아 거러 가거라. 다리가 부러졋늬. 남 다 거러가는데 혼자 제치고 안저서 이놈아.」 하는 소리가 겹처 들니엿슴니다. 가슴이 뜩금햇슴니다. 인력거 채를 뷔여 잡고 당장 끄러내리는 듯 머리까지 옷삭옷삭햇슴니다. 당장 뛰여내려 사과를 하고 십흐나 임의 그리된 일에 그리하는 것이 더 庸拙해 보이고 말로라도 「소생의 본의가 안이오니 용서하십시오. 旅中이라 의복과 行具를 위해서 부득이 이리되얏슴니다.」하고 간청을 하고 십흐나 前遮後基의 車獄인지라 그리하지도 못하고 땀만 흘닐 뿐이엿슴니다. 인력거군에게 追兵이 急하니 速行速行을 재촉햇스나 如此泥中에 此亦 무리엇슴니다. 그는 더구나 중국인인지라 말까지 몰음니다. 참말 거북하엿슴니다. 支社門 압에 당도하고 보닛가 대난관이나 버서난 듯 가슴이 진정되얏슴니다.
뒤에 오든 金道賢씨의 니약이를 드르닛가 그들은 定州 南門 밧 農軍들이라 함니다. 소스랑 메고 호미 차고 팔다리 거더 붓친 農軍이엿다 함니다. 早朝에 나이가 盡日 더운 땀 흘니며 갈고 메고 하든 農軍들이라 함니다. 그들에게는 의당히 욕을 당해도 좃슴니다. 도리여 감사하엿슴니다. 「定州南門外農夫님 諸氏에게」라 하고 陳謝文을 보내고 십헛슴니다. 假言이 안이라 진정이엿슴니다. 여하한 경우라도 다시는 인력거 안 타기로 햇슴니다.
靑吾형. 支社의 金公善형은 꽤 奔忙하게 지내는 형편임니다. 農民聯合會를 조직하고 그 聯合會 주최로 端陽佳節을 이용하야 農民大運動會를 개최한다 함니다. 그래서 농민운동에 주력하는 모모 동지와 연일 회의하기에 퍽 밧븐 모양임니다. 洪鍾炫
承天佑

靑吾형. 翌日은 金公善형과 가티 古邑驛에 갓슴니다. 崔昌瑞
崔聖濟
金起鴻 諸氏를 차저보고 만흔 동정을 어덧슴니다. 만나는 곳에 그닥 박대가 업슴을 깃버함니다. 이는 春坡라는 개인을 위함이 안이라 개벽사라는 그 기관을 위함이겟지오. 社를 위하야 더욱 충실히 일하자는 책임감이 格別히 생김니다. 崔聖濟씨 宅에서 하로밤 농촌동무들과 자미잇게 지내엿슴니다. 種鷄ㅅ맛보다 되비지ㅅ맛이 더 조왓슴니다.
靑吾형. 이곳에는 무엇보다 土炭(俗名넉)이 유명함니다. 논둑마다 土炭을 만히 캐내여 말니는 것을 보고 처음은 못(池)판 자리의 泥土로만 아럿더니 집집의 마당에서 泥土가 산덤이 가티 싸인 것을 보고야 그것이 土炭인 것을 아럿슴니다. (무러 가지고야) 드르닛가 이 日新洞 근처나 五山 근처에 土炭이 제일 만히 나는데 논바닥에서 얼마든지 캐내인다 함니다. 캐내여 연료에 쓰고도 그 논에 벼는 그대로 심는다 함니다. 此所謂 꿩 먹고 알 먹는다고 이곳 논들은 쌀 내고 연료 내고 함니다. 福地임니다. 그러나 아직 人工이 未及하야 외지 수출은 못하고 當地에서도 산의 柴炭보다는 불편하다는 말이 만슴니다. 장래 잘 이용할 수 잇는 好燃料임니다.
五山學校는 시간이 밥버 방문치 못하고 定州邑으로 드러갓슴니다.＜105＞
(원문에서는 이하 105쪽 하단과 106쪽 상단이 삭제되어 있다-국사편찬위원회)

靑五형. 여행이 지리한지라 雜聞雜見도 지리함니다. 노루글로 후덕덕후덕덕 됨니다.

靑吾형. 西行雜記를 쓰든 끗톄 유치장의 5일간을 적어들일가요. 형은 7일인가 8일인가 된다지오. 小春
石溪 *村 小波 할 것 업시 이번 노름은 다가티＜106＞ 츠럿지오. 다가티 조선놈이란 간판에다가 겸하야 때가 때인지라 면할내야 면할 수나 잇슴닛가.
