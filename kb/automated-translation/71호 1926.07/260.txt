宿命?
						(佛國) 빠르쀼스
A single window pierced the bare, empty wall. Outside, it was dusk—as unchangingly quiet as a painting. And there, the faces of two old friends. Their expressions were as if molded from clay.

They had crawled into the same corner of light and shadow, waiting for time to pass in the same room, intending to finish their lives side by side. Occasionally, the two men would talk.

"It's all in the past. There's only fate," mumbled old Dominik'ŭ, a phrase he uttered following any event he spoke of, or thought he had spoken of.

"No, that's not right," replied old K'ŭro-dŭ.
"Fate, just like anything else, makes many mistakes."

The first speaker looked at his friend, somewhat embarrassed and somewhat mockingly, but showed no sign of surprise. At an age like this man's, slight deviations were common.

This one shook his head, a neck like a bundle of withered sticks with protruding veins, and slapped his knee with a hand like dry wood.
"There are things one can't help, yet somehow can do something about," he added to his previous statement. <41>

"Ah!" Dominik'ŭ mumbled. He turned his weak eyes, sunk in reddish sockets, towards the ceiling. Perhaps he was worried, thinking that he too, before long, would end up nonsensically babbling like that.

"I was married to Pernardŭdan. It was long ago," K'ŭro-dŭ said. "I had completely forgotten her. But recently, I saw a girl who looked remarkably similar. It's as if I saw that woman herself; thoughts of her flooded back into my mind. I married that woman, but two months before that, I shot her father's head, shattering it to pieces."

Dominik'ŭ was suddenly afraid. He thought his friend must be dreaming and talking nonsense, wondering if he was the only one awake and listening in this room.
"Hey, K'ŭro-dŭ! Are you sleeping?" he shouted, trembling.

"No," K'ŭro-dŭ replied. "I'm not sleeping. I'm thinking. I married that girl without any trouble. And yet, I shot a bullet into her father's forehead. First, I must tell you that the girl deeply followed her father, and that the father also loved his daughter.

"So that was a very long time ago, eh?" said Dominik'ŭ, now calm like a child who, relieved, quietly began listening to the story again.

"Yes. It was so long ago that it feels like I'm telling someone else's story, or like it happened before I was even born."

The old man suddenly regained the eloquence of his past. He discovered it as if finding a memory.

"Old man Parŭppo was kind and honest. So, the old man didn't want to give his daughter to me. Because I was a useless fellow. Truly. I knew nothing but how to fall for that girl; that was all I could do. <42> And like anyone who knows only one thing, I did that one thing—falling in love—with passion. There aren't many things that could make one as frantic as I was when my heart was stolen by that girl. That girl, you ask? She lived a very long time, but now she's dead—are you listening, eh, Dominik'ŭ?"

"Yes," Dominik'ŭ said, moving closer.

"At that time, the old man disliked me. Everyone close tried hard to change his mind, but the old man pretended not to hear, or heard and pretended not to understand. So, everyone decided not to press him too hard. Why? Because the old man was quick-tempered and strong. That fellow had arms like a wrestler and hands like iron maces. Yes. But one day, I made up my mind and spoke to the old man directly. Very calmly. And what did that old rascal do but throw me out the door. Meanwhile, dear Pernardin went to a corner of the kitchen, stood stock-still, rubbed her eyes with both hands, and sobbed. Driven mad by my own wretchedness and shame, I decided, 'Right, let's commit suicide!' What's the point of living when I knew the one suppressing the beauty and joy of my life was as malicious as a devil and as strong as a bull? Having all my earnest pleas rejected in front of everyone, I couldn't just sit there. It seemed far easier for me to give up my life. After loading the gun with a bullet and choosing a clear, bright night—like a fellow in love, yes, I was in love back then too—I walked out of the village without looking aside. I finally went to the roadside at the corner of Ppŭrik'e's lane and sat down, intending to do what I had resolved. But just as I was about to pick up the gun, I suddenly heard the sound of cartwheels nearby, followed by the appearance of a carriage. Without even needing to think, I recognized it as old man Parŭppo's carriage, and I felt my heart clench. At the same time, I remembered that tonight was the night the old man was supposed to bring money to Madame T'angp'urie. The horse walked slowly. The carriage passed right before my eyes. I saw the old man, his large, detestable mountain-like body leaning forward, his hooked nose, his large wedge-shaped <43> lower beard, and his barbaric profile like that of a Negro king. Seeing this creature, who had thrown me into this utmost predicament, swagger past before my eyes like this made me burn with an indescribable, fierce anger. I jumped up, aimed at the hateful fellow's forehead, and fired a shot—bang! The old man collapsed without even crying out, his head falling backward against the horse's rump. The horse, startled, bolted, left the main road at the corner, ran about fifty feet, and finally tumbled over in Roppiō-'s field. I fled. Blinded, feeling faint and wretched, I ran away as fast as my strength allowed. Only after running senselessly far away did I barely manage to recall what I had done. But recalling what I had done didn't mean the madness would subside, did it? I just ran, regardless of field or forest. Though I forget most things from the past, I still remember, as if it were yesterday, trampling through thorny bushes that night and mercilessly knocking down fences trying to force my way over them. Amidst this, the thought that I had decided to commit suicide as soon as I got home somewhat calmed my boiling heart and restored some order. But then, as I fled under a curse, I realized I had come to that very house—the house the old man had left, the house where the girl was. Realizing this, I couldn't suppress the desire to see the girl just once more—to catch a glimpse through the casement window of the girl standing blankly, looking outside, a dim shadow receiving the faint red reflection of the hearth fire behind her. I had come so close! I pressed myself against the wall, panting, and crept around the corner as quietly as possible. Ah, the window was open. The girl was standing there, her arms resting on the windowsill! She was there, her face pale as an angel's. It seemed to me something made the girl shine brightly. And the girl was smiling! She saw me standing five or six feet away, gave a small cry, and clapped her hands. And she seemed to grow brighter and laugh more joyfully! 'God sent you <44> here,' the girl said. 'Father has given his permission! He understood how much I was suffering, and to save me, he suddenly said he consented. Just before he went out, just a moment ago, he said so. He said he consented, and he smiled!'

I couldn't even cry. My throat was choked, my eyes saw nothing. I don't know how I fled from that place, how I crawled away to where the girl's eyes couldn't follow. What I remember is returning home, one arm outstretched, fumbling, the other hand tightly gripping the gun—yes, at that moment, the gun was the most precious treasure in the world to me! Without even lighting a lamp in the kitchen, without opening my eyes, I searched for the gunpowder box, found it, and loaded the ammunition with joy. But I had become an enemy in the eyes of cruel fate. Yes, that fate intended to forgive me—but what a harsh method of forgiveness! It didn't even allow me the time to realize I had been saved by fate itself, nor did it give me the chance to realize I no longer had the strength left to commit suicide. So, did the bullet go astray? In any case, I only felt a hot, fierce wind. And only a lock of hair flew off. I staggered and then collapsed. Thinking I was dead—but in reality, I was alive.

I awoke to my own mumbling sound when a ray of sunlight filled the room. My ears were ringing—eeeng. But then a loud noise arose from there—the clamor of many people talking. Just then, Chang·chang pounded on the door with his fist—Chang·chang was my eldest brother, five or six years older, who died in old age. My brother pounded again, opened the door, stuck his head in, and shouted.
'Old man Parŭppo was murdered on the road last night.'
'Ah!' I gasped, my face turning ashen, and stumbled back to the furthest corner of the room. <45>
'Those two vagrants who looked fundamentally evil,' my brother continued. 'They've been caught along with the stolen money pouch. They confessed everything without leaving anything out! They apparently attacked the carriage near the village entrance, right near the old man's house. The old man suffered ten dagger wounds in his back and died on the spot. The scene was just a pool of blood. And those two murderer-robbers apparently seated the old man back where he was initially and walked the horse slowly. Much later, at the corner of Ppŭrik'e's lane, the horse collapsed and fell into Roppiō-'s field.'

'I hadn't killed him! Because he was already dead! One cannot kill a dead man again. Hey. Fate is there. Isn't it? Hey, but that night, fate made one mistake.'
-(Translated by P'albong)-

**Summary**

Two elderly friends, Dominik'ŭ and K'ŭro-dŭ, discuss the past. K'ŭro-dŭ recounts a story from his youth where, rejected by his beloved Pernardŭdan's father (Parŭppo), he decided to commit suicide. While waiting to do so, he saw Parŭppo pass in a carriage, shot him in anger, and fled. Approaching Pernardŭdan's house later, she joyfully told him her father had just consented to their marriage before leaving. Distraught, K'ŭro-dŭ returned home to shoot himself but botched the attempt. The next morning, his brother revealed Parŭppo had actually been murdered by robbers shortly after leaving home, stabbed multiple times, well before K'ŭro-dŭ shot the already dead man. K'ŭro-dŭ concludes that while fate spared him, it did so cruelly and made a "mistake" that night. The translator adds a postscript apologizing for the quality, noting it's a triple translation (French -> English -> Japanese -> Korean).

From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as an example of Western literature (French author Barbusse) being introduced to colonial Korean readers in the 1920s, albeit through multiple translations (English and Japanese intermediaries). The translator's (P'albong) candid postscript highlights the challenges and potential inaccuracies inherent in such indirect translation processes common during this period, offering insight into the circulation of foreign texts and the linguistic hurdles faced by intellectuals. The theme of "Fate" (宿命) itself might resonate with the experiences of Koreans living under colonial rule, grappling with forces seemingly beyond their control.

宿命?
						(佛國) 빠르쀼스
맨숭맨숭한 바람 벽에 뚤니어진 창문 하나 밧겻은 해질 무렵-언제까지든지 한결가티 변함이 업는 그림과 가티 고요하다. 그리고 그곳에는 두 사람의 올애된 친구의 얼굴이 잇다. 그것은 마치 흙으로 비저 노은 것 가튼 표정이엿다.
그들은 빗과 그림자의 똑가튼 구석에 긔어들어가지고 똑가튼 방에서 때가 오는 것을 기다리면서 억개를 나란히 하고서 그 한평생을 마추어 버리려고 하는 것이엇다. 두 사람은 갓금갓금 이약이를 한다.
『모도다 지나간 일이다. 그저 숙명만이 잇슬 뿐이지』 늙은 도미닉크는 자긔가 말한 혹은 자긔가 말하얏다고 생각한 어떠한 사건에든지 잇다러 말하는 말로 이러케 중얼거렷다.
『아니 그러치 안흐이』 늙은 크로-드가 대답하얏다.
『숙명이라는 것도 다른 것과 마찬가지로 잘못할 적이 만치』
처음에 말한 사람은 얼마쯤 민망해 하는 듯이 또 얼마쯤 비웃는 듯이 친구를 바라보는 것이엇스나 놀나는 듯한 긔색은 업섯다. 이 산애만한 나히에 이르르고 보면 족음쯤 탈선하는 것도 예사다.
이편 사람은 머리와 심쭐이 들어난 빗적 말른 막대기를 묵거노흔 듯한 목아지를 흔들흔들 흔들고서 마른 나무와 가튼 손으로 무릅을 첫다.
『어떠케 해볼 수 업는 일이면서도 어떠케든지 해볼 수 잇는 일이 잇거든』 그는 먼저 한 말에 이러케 보태어 륀햇다.＜41＞
『아아!』 도미닉크는 중엘대엿다. 그는 붉으데데한 눈짜위 속에 가러 안진 힘업는 눈을 천졍으로 향하얏다. 아마 자긔도 역시 오래지 안하서 져 모양으로 무슨 소린지 알지도 못할 소리를 짓거리게 되는 것이로나 하는 것을 생각하고 불안한 모양이다.
『나는 베르나르단과 결혼을 햇섯지. 녯날의 일이지만』 크로-드가 말햇다. 『나는 그 녀자를 깜박 이저버리고 잇섯네. 그런데 요지음에 그것하고 썩 근사한 게집아이를 보앗지. 말하자면 나는 그 녀자나 본 것처름 그것의 생각이 흠빡 이 마음속으로 돌아오게 되엇네 그려. 나는 그 녀자와 결혼을 햇섯지만 그 두 달 전에 나는 그 녀자의 아버지의 대가리를 총으로 쏘아서 산산박살을 내엇거든』

『여보게 크로-드! 자네는 자는 모양인가?』 그는 벌벌 떨면서 고함첫다.

『그러치. 너무도 오래된 일인 까닭으로 누군지 모르는 다른 사람의 이약이를 하는 것도 갓고 내가 생기어나기 전에 이러난 일인 것 갓기도 하이』
