惡魔 (Devil)
						(露西亞) 막슴·꼴키 (Maksim Gorky (Russia))

Autumn is a time when sorrowful thoughts seep in all the more. Wherever one goes, transient shadows linger, and living somehow feels just wearisome.
The continuous ash-grey daylight, the cloudy sky seeming swollen, the pitch-black night, the unpleasantly whining wind, the frustratingly tangled gloomy shadows – truly, the shadow of autumn. At such times, a person's mind is also constantly immersed in these clouds of melancholic thought, and without firm roots, just being born, falling away, and dying directionlessly, this perpetually stirring thing called life becomes strangely terrifying and unbearable.
Especially by late autumn, the clouds of thought overwhelm the entire mind, and there are many times when even the courage to fight this disappears. Therefore, for anyone who wants to quickly overcome these painful thoughts, personally stepping forward to confront them is the wisest course. This is indeed the only way for a person to escape the chaotic realm of doubt (懷疑) or anguish and stand on the unshakable (確乎不拔) foundation called self-confidence (自信).
However, this is quite a difficult path, like a thicket overgrown with thornbushes. If one forces their way through, it crushes one's raw courage (生膽) and inevitably makes one bloodied. And on this path, there is a devil, always waiting for us. This is the devil first introduced to us by the great poet (詩聖) Goethe, and among the devils we know, he is the cutest one.
From now on, I intend to talk somewhat about this devil.<30>

The devil was bored.
This creature called the devil was too clever to just be constantly sneering (嘲笑). Thus, he clearly knew there were phenomena in human life that he could not sneer at. For example, he had never tried the sharp blade of his ridicule against a great fact like his own existence. To tell the truth, this cute devil, rather than being clever, had a presumptuous (狂妄) nature, and if you observe carefully, this fellow, like us, seems to waste much time on futile things. But let's leave such matters aside. We are not children. We won't engage in the foolish act of carelessly fiddling with a toy and breaking a fine one. It happened one autumn night. This devil was wandering among the graves, unbearably bored. Quietly whistling, he looked around in all directions, searching thoroughly to see if there was anything interesting. He whistled a tune from a stale, short song – it was a song our father used to sing merrily.

Autumn leaves too
Fall fluttering –
Carried by the wind, they fly away.

The indifferent wind brushed past the graves where black crosses stood, rampaging fiercely, echoing the devil's song. In the sky, heavy autumn rain clouds slowly crept along, wetting the narrow abode of death with cold tears. The pitiful graveyard trees, at first glance, silently stretched their bare, bony branches into the rain clouds, swaying whenever the wind blew, rustling and weeping as if murmuring. As the branches brushed against the crosses, an unpleasant, gloomy scratching sound (擦音) was heard from upon the graves.
The devil whistled and thought.
"How must the dead feel on a day like this? It would surely be interesting to hear. Even a devil has to be in a damp, humid place... Once dead, there'd be no worry of catching rheumatism (僂麻質), but still, the mood wouldn't be good.<31> Shall I pull one of them out and have a chat? It might provide some entertainment. He might enjoy it too... Yes. That's it. Let's call one out. Right, wasn't it somewhere around here I buried a writer (文學家) I knew... We met once or twice when he was alive... At times like this, it's necessary to warm up old acquaintances... Humans terribly respect anyone called a writer, but let's ask him whether he likes the grave or not. Now, where was his grave?"
The devil, called omnipotent, searched here and there and finally found the writer's grave. With hideously long fingernails, he tapped, tap-tap, on the stone tablet covering the dead.
"Hey, get up now," he called.
From within the earth came a thin voice:
"What is it?"
"There's something..."
"I'm not getting up!"
"Why?"
"Well, who are you anyway?"
"You should know me..."
"Are you a censor (檢閱官)?"
"Hehe... I'm not such a person."
"Then are you military police (憲兵)?"
"Not that either."
"Aren't you a critic (批評家)?"
"I am the devil..."<32>
"Ah, is that so? Then I'll come right out..."
The stone tablet moved, the earth split in two, and from between, a skeleton (骸骨) appeared. There was nothing particularly strange about it. It looked like an ordinary skeleton used by university students for anatomy study, only differing in being extremely ugly, lacking pockmarks, having empty eye sockets from which a blue will-o'-the-wisp (燐)-like gleam emanated. The skeleton crawled out of the ground and, intending to shake off the dirt clinging to its body, gave its bones a good rattle, making a dry, clicking sound. Suddenly, the skeleton lifted its head and glared at the darkening sky with a blue, cold, trembling gaze.
"Hey, is that you!" was the devil's greeting.
"Wrong," came the simple, calm, yet chilling sound, like bone scraping against bone.
"Was my greeting amiss?" The devil's tone was extremely friendly.
"No, not really, but do you have other business with me?"
"No particular business... just thought we could take a little walk..."
"Fine, fine. It's a dreary day, but let's walk a bit."
"Surely you won't worry about catching a cold," asked the devil.
"Well, there's no chance of that, but I caught colds terribly often when I was alive."
"Yes. And so you died coldly."
"And... made my life so miserable like this..."
The two matched their steps and groped their way along the narrow path between the stone tablets and crosses. Two streams of blue light fell from the writer's eyes onto the ground, illuminating the devil's path... A light rain was drizzling. The wind blew freely through the ribs of the writer, who had neither heart nor anything else.
"Shall we go to the street?" rattled the bones.<33>
"Is there anything interesting on the street?"
"Life (生活)," the writer explained curtly.
"You still have lingering attachments to the world (世俗), I see."
"Yes, I do."
"Why?"
"I don't know why, but people generally measure all things by the extent of their own effort... Even if it's a worthless stone picked up from the summit of Mount Ararat, to them, it becomes a jewel."
"Pitiful creatures!" sneered the devil.
"But that's why they are happy," the writer retorted coolly.
The devil fell silent and hunched his shoulders.
When the two emerged from the cemetery (墓地), the street was right before them, with houses lined up in two rows. The middle was pitch black. The pitiful color of the lamps clearly proved the lack of light on earth. The two were silent for a moment, but the devil soon spoke.
"So, how was it inside the grave?"
"It's been a long time now, so it's nothing. Extremely peaceful... But it was quite difficult at first. Anyway, it's unbearably ugly, and besides, some fool, hammering nails into my coffin lid, drove one right into my skull. Of course, it wasn't a terrible thing, so it wasn't that painful, but still, there wasn't a moment of relief. I thought at the time, that guy must be some kind of symbolist (象徵主義者)... thinking maybe he intended to smash my brains (腦髓) as revenge (報復) for the many times I slandered people during my life... And after that, worms squirmed and bred, and they devoured all my flesh like this..."
"But you can't blame the worms for that. After all, a body lump formed of bile (膽汁) wouldn't have been a very tasty dish..."<34>
"Stop the nonsense... Do you know how much flesh I had?" retorted the writer.
"However much there was, eating it must have been a thoroughly painful task... If it had been a publisher (出版者), the worms might have swarmed and devoured it all in the blink of an eye."
"Of course, that's true – is their flesh a delicacy?"
"Does it get damp inside the grave in autumn?" asked the devil.
"It's soaking wet, I hate it... But you get used to it, and it's nothing. It's just that sometimes, some damned kids wander around the grave and poke mine, that's really annoying – really, how many years has it been since I entered the grave... Since everything around me doesn't move – the concept of time doesn't register at all..."
"Indeed, it's already been four years since you were buried in the earth. Soon it will be the fifth year."
"Is that so? Good heavens... During that time, three fellows came to my grave and spouted insolent nonsense. One boldly denied my existence, mumbled something while reading the epitaph, and then said, 'What's this? (Was there such a fellow? I've never read this guy's books... But I think I've heard the name. Right, right, when I was young, I think an author with this name secretly practiced usury on our street.)' Can you imagine? How could I not be angry? What do you think, hearing such words? As you know, I wielded my pen for that famous magazine for sixteen years and published my writings (著述) three times..."
"It was published twice more after your death."
"After that, two fellows came together, and wasn't their conversation abominable? Looking at my grave,"
'(Oh, was it this guy?)' one said.
'(Yes, this guy),' the other replied.
'(Right, I think I read something by him once.)'
'(Everyone's still reading him now...)<35>
'(But what did he stand for?)'
'(Just the commonplace... ideas of goodness (善) and beauty (美), and one or two other things...)'
'Right, right, I remember...)'
'(His writing was somehow rough, wasn't it?)'
'(How many guys like this must be buried in the ground – Russia has quite a lot of geniuses...)'
"Saying this, the two left... Those beasts... No matter how many warm words they offered, there's no reason for a cold grave to suddenly heat up, and I don't particularly enjoy such talk either... They're just making a complete fool (쳔치) of me. It would have been better if they had just poured out insults..."
"Right. Since you're accustomed to that, it would have been refreshing," laughed the devil.
"No, it wouldn't work... In this day and age, approaching the twentieth century, for the dead to suddenly start cursing is too foolish... And it's a bit cruel towards materialists (唯物論者)."
The devil felt bored again and thought:
"That fellow, when he lived in the world, whether at a wedding feast or a marketplace, wherever he went, he tried to seem like the master, and even today, when his body is dead and nothing remains, his sense of honor (名譽心) is still burning. But I wonder if humans are really worth living. No, only their souls (靈魂) are precious. Only their souls are worthy of respect... How stifling humans can be..."
The devil was about to suddenly urge the writer to return to his cold grave. But a thought flashed through his cunning mind. At that moment, the two were in a wide plaza (廣場) surrounded by houses. The terrifyingly dark, rain-threatening sky spread low over the plaza, pressing down on the rooftops, seemingly observing this squalid earth with sinister eyes.
The devil leaned towards the writer and, in a deeply affectionate tone,<36>
"Don't you want to see how your wife is doing?"
"Well... I don't know how she is," the skeleton's tone was very composed.
The devil, in a tempting tone:
"Yes, you are truly a dead man!"
"Why?" asked the skeleton, its bones trembling violently. "It's not that I dislike the idea... but what if my wife finds out... Maybe she won't recognize me even if she sees me?"
"Of course, she won't!" the devil assured him.
"But you know... when I left home for a while, she had absolutely no intention of loving me..." explained the writer.
Then, the wall of a certain house disappeared somewhere and became transparent (透明) like glass. And the interior of a large room came into the writer's view. Bright, comfortable, and terribly splendid...
"How can it be such a magnificent house?" squeaked the skeleton. "Truly, the style is excellent. If I had lived in such a house, I wouldn't have died so early..."
The devil laughed.
"Actually, I like it too. But it didn't cost that much money. Right, three thousand..."
"What!... That didn't cost much? My magnum opus (大作) only sold for eight hundred fifty won... and I agonized over it for about a year... But who lives there?"
"Your wife."
"What! H-how... Th-this is too splendid for her... A woman! That woman? Is that woman my wife?"
"Yes... There, there comes that woman's husband (男便)..."<37>
"She's become a truly magnificent woman... What a beautiful voice. What, that's her husband? A truly sturdy and short man. His face looks like a merchant's... He seems like a good person... But his face looks foolish (쳔치). No, rather dull... But that kind of face appeals to women..."
"You seem very tired, won't you rest here for a moment? What do you say?" said the devil, looking at the writer with a venomous face.
The writer was mesmerized by the scene, lost in a dream.
"What cheerful, vibrant faces! Both of them – they seem extremely content... Does she truly love her current husband?"
"Yes, of course. He's excellent..."
"But who on earth is that fellow?"
"He's a clerk (店員) at a fashionable goods (流行物) shop (商店)..."
"A clerk at a fashionable goods shop..." The skeleton repeated weakly and then fell silent for a moment, speechless. The devil, seeing this, laughed derisively.
"Well? Do you like it?" The devil's banter was endlessly sarcastic.
The writer, mustering his strength again:
"I had children... What about them?... They must surely be alive... A son and a daughter... I thought at the time – now that I have children like this, they too will gradually become gentlemen..."
"Gentlemen require too much, complete individuals are needed," the devil said coolly and whistled some urgent-sounding march.
"If he's a clerk, he must be inadequate as an educator... My children too..." the hollow skull trembled sorrowfully...
"Ah, they're embracing. Eh-gi, damn it! How amusing!"<38>
"Really... Is that fellow wealthy?"
"He was poorer than me, but thanks to your wife being rich..."
"My wife? Where did she get the money?"
"By selling your works (作品)."
"Is that true?" The writer shook his bony head back and forth. "Right! So does that mean I worked for a clerk I don't even know?"
"Yes, that seems to be the case," the devil nodded as if enjoying himself.
"Take me back to the grave quickly!" The writer stared blankly at the ground and begged the devil.
The rain drizzled down in the pitch-black darkness. The rain clouds hung heavy. The writer, rattling his bones, rushed straight back to the grave without looking aside... The devil followed behind, whistling cheerfully...

The reader might find this worth seeing. The reader is tired of literature. Even for those who write solely aiming for the reader's favor, it is extremely difficult to produce something that perfectly suits the reader's taste. How much more so for this story, which doesn't mention hell at all, the reader will surely be dissatisfied. The reader is convinced they will certainly fall into hell after death, so they want to know something about hell while alive. However, I cannot speak of what pleases the reader. That is because hell does not exist. Even if one can easily imagine it, in reality, there is no hell of fire. But there is something else, much more terrifying.
The moment the doctor turns to your close relatives and says,
"He has passed away (永眠)..."<39>
at that moment, you enter some infinitely bright, glittering world. It is the world of your mistaken consciousness (意識).
You are lying horizontally in a narrow coffin in the grave. Before you, your wretched life rolls by like a wheel. Not only that, but it passes agonizingly slowly. From the very first step of your lifelong consciousness to the final moment, it passes without omission. Thus, you will discover all that you hid and veiled from your eyes while alive. You will discover the falsehood and ugliness of your daily life. You will rethink all your thoughts. You will discover your unsteady steps (步調), one by one. Your life will be regenerated up to the last second. And seeing others walking the narrow, foolish path you walked, and knowing they collide, panic (蒼惶罔措), scold, and curse each other, you will fear your own suffering even more... And because you realize how shameful it is to live such a disgraceful and pitiful life, you will clearly comprehend that they are doing these things.
But watching them heading towards their own destruction, you cannot warn them. You cannot cry out or move. The hope of helping them will only futilely crush your spirit...
Once your life has passed before your eyes, it returns again. You see your life again from the very beginning... Thus, the activity of your consciousness is infinite. Life too will be infinite... And your chilling suffering will have no end... Forever...
-Translated by Pak Yong-dae (朴容大 譯)- <40>

**Summary**

This translated short story by Maxim Gorky depicts a conversation between the Devil and the resurrected skeleton of a writer in a graveyard on a dreary autumn night. Bored, the Devil reanimates the writer to chat. The writer complains about his grave, the worms, and the disrespectful comments of visitors. The Devil then shows the writer a vision of his former wife, now remarried to a shop clerk, living happily and luxuriously in a house seemingly bought with the money from the writer's posthumously published works. Crushed by this revelation, the writer begs to be returned to his grave. The story concludes with a description of true hell: not fire, but the eternal, conscious reliving of one's flawed life, witnessing its mistakes and the suffering of others without being able to intervene.

From a historical perspective, the publication of a translation of a major Russian author like Gorky in a Korean magazine like *Kaebyŏk* in 1926 highlights the intellectual currents of the time, showing an engagement with international literature and potentially socialist-leaning thought (given Gorky's associations). The story's themes of disillusionment, the vanity of worldly success (especially literary fame), and existential dread might have resonated with Korean intellectuals grappling with colonial realities and modernity. The depiction of the writer's legacy being co-opted for the mundane happiness of others could be interpreted as a commentary on the perceived devaluation of intellectual or artistic pursuits in a changing society.

惡魔
						(露西亞) 막슴·꼴키
가을은 애닯은 생각이 한결 더 숨여드는 때이다. 어데를 가든지 덧업는 그림자가 서리여서 사는 것도 엇전지 그저 고달프기만 하다.
줄창 게속되는 잿빗 해 울음에 틩틩 부은 듯한 흐린 한울 새캄한 밤 불쾌하게 잉잉거리는 바람 답답하게 얼켜진 음산한 그림자-확실히 가을 그림자다. 이런 때에는 사람의 맘도 줄곳 이 음울한 생각의 구름에 잠겨서 확실한 뿌리가 업시 그저 생겨낫다가 떠러저서 지향업시 죽어 가는 것처럼 영구히 움질거리고 잇는 인생이란 것이 야릇하게도 무서워서 견딀 수가 업다.
더구나 느진 가을쯤 되면 생각의 구름이 온 맘을 자어내여서 엇던 때는 이것과 싸울 용기조차 업서지는 수가 만타. 그럼으로 누구던지 이 괴로운 생각을 얼는 격거버리랴거던 손수 나아가 이것을 마추우는 것이 제일 약은 수다. 이것이야말로 사람이 회의(懷疑)라던지 번민의 혼돈계를 버서나서 자신(自信)이라는 확호불발(確乎不拔)한 토대에 설 유일한 길이다.
하지만 이것은 꽤 곤난한 길이여서 가시덤불이 욱어진 숩플과 가튼 것이다. 억지로 나간다면 사람의 날쓸개(生膽)를 짓찌여서 피투성이를 맨들고야 만다. 그리고 이 길에는 악마가 잇서서 항상 우리를 기다리고 잇다. 이것은 시셩(詩聖) 꾀- 테가 비로소 우리에게 소개한 악마인데 우션 우리가 아는 악마 가운데는 뎨일 귀여운 놈이다.
나는 일로부터 얼마간 이 악마에게 대하야 이야기를 하랸다.＜30＞
악마는 갑갑하엿다.

독자는 아마도 볼만할 것이다. 독자는 문학에 염증이 낫다. 오로지 독자의 환심을 목표로 쓰는 사람들까지도 독자의 취미에 꼭 알맛는 것을 내기가 극히 어렵다. 하믈며 이 이야기에 잇서서는 지옥의 일에는 조곰도 말이 밋지 안엇스니 더욱 독자도 불만할 것이다. 독자는 죽은 뒤에 반드시 지옥에 떠러질 것을 확신하고 잇슴으로 살아 생전에 지옥에 관한 것을 다소간은 알고 십허 한다. 그러나 나는 독자의 맘에 드는 것은 말할 수 업다. 그것은 지옥이 업는 까닭이다. 설사 쉬웁게 상상은 할 수 잇더라도 실상은 불의 지옥이라는 것은 업다. 그리나 엇던 다른 더욱 몹시 무서운 것이엇다.
의사가 그대의 일을 그대의 갓가운 친족에게 향하야
「영민(永眠)하섯슴니다...」＜39＞
하는 순간에 그대는 엇던 무한한 밝게 빗나고 번적이는 셰계로 들어간다. 그것은 그대의 그릇된 의식의 세게이다.
그대는 무덤 속 협착한 관속에 가르누어 잇다. 그대의 압흐로 그대의 구차한 생활이 수레박휘와 가티 들들 굴며 지나간다. 뿐만 아니라 괴로우리만치 쳔쳔히 지나간다. 그대의 한편생의 의식뎍 맨 첫거름으로부터 최후의 순간에 이르기까지 남기지 안코 지나간다. 해서 그대는 살아잇든 때에 눈압흐로부터 숨기고 가리엿든 모- 든 것을 발견할 것이다. 그대의 일상생활의 거즛과 추함을 발견할 것이다. 그대의 모- 든 사상을 새로 도리켜 생각할 것이다. 그대의 똑똑치 못한 거름(步調)을 한거름한거름 발견할 것이다. 그대의 일생은 최후의 일초까지 재생할 것이다. 그리하야 그대는 그대의 걸어오던 좁고 어리석은 길을 다른 패들이 것고 잇는 것을 보고 또 그들이 서루 충돌하고 창황망조하고 서로 꾸짓고 욕질하는 것을 알고는 자긔의 고통을 한층 더 무서워할 것이다...그리고 이가티 붓그럽고 가엽슨 생활을 하는 것이 얼마나 수치스러운 것인지를 깨닷끼 때문에 그들은 이러한 일을 하고 잇다는 것을 그대는 똑똑히 해리할 것이다.
그러나 창망히 자긔의 멸망으로 나아가는 그들을 보면서 그대는 그들에게 경고할 수가 업다. 그대는 부르지즐 수도 움지길 수도 업다. 그들을 도으랴는 희망은 헛되히 그대의 정신을 짓찌여 줄 뿐이다...
그대의 눈압흐로 그대의 생활이 일단 지나가면 그것이 또 도라온다. 그대는 다시 제 생활을 맨 처음으로부터 보게 된다...이리하야 그대의 의식의 활동은 무한하다. 생활도 무한할 것이다...그리고 그대의 소름이 끼치는 고통은 끈칠 때가 업슬 것이다...영구히...
