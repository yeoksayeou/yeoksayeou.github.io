Female Factory Worker and Four Other Pieces
						Chŏkku

Though they say spring has come, it still hasn't bid farewell to winter—
Above the slumbering sleep of the old nation,
The first siren sound of the factory announcing the dawn
Still fiercely strikes the dark blue sky
And begins to echo beside the ears of 300,000 people.

The hurried breath of the young wife
Who ran here without even clearing away
The memorial meal table for her husband, who was beaten to death…

Inside the workshop, like a devil's cave,
Just spending those twelve hours kneeling, unable to even turn her head—that alone makes one shudder,
And then the gloomy glances of that detestable supervisor....<110>
This damned world where one still has to accept that bastard's intentions—

Oh, Ancestors! Oh, someone else's husband!
Why did you leave this damned world as it is?
—Stopping the wife, making the children fret...

Victim
When the evening sun crept over the mountain opposite,
My husband, his face contorted with fury,
Along with the village tenant farmers,
Dragging clubs, ran towards the South village.

Now, past the third watch of the night,
In this midnight when even the moonlight fades,
He returns home transformed into a corpse,
Laid on a stretcher, unable even to close his eyes—

Then, just as I finished washing the dishes earlier,
Untimely gunshots echoed repeatedly from the back mountain.
Perhaps that was the sorrowful farewell, the soul-calling sound
That escorted my husband's spirit away.<111>

Alright, you bastard!
You enemy who took my husband in exchange for a single bullet—
Do you think the knot in my heart will ever loosen?!
Not as long as my life remains in this world—

At Younger Brother's Grave
Younger brother! Oh, spirit of my younger brother!
Your elder brother is about to leave this land.
This land, this site, this house,
Whose foundations even our father laid, I am about to leave.
To a place without destination, be it a thousand or ten thousand *li* away—

Yet you, though only white bones,
Still rest upon the site bequeathed by our ancestors...

The Moment (刹那)
Live the life of the moment!
—Beyond the moments and moments,
Do not think of the momentary pain—
Only strive to live in the moment!<112>
Then you will naturally attain the true "life" of Reality...

Pleasure Market (享樂市場)
In the wide, wide hall,
The OO's goods, brought in since early evening,
Already piled up like mountains, numbering over 300.
Outside, automobiles and carriages stand in a long line—

High hats, tailcoats, patent leather shoes,
Gold hairpins, jade rings, silk skirts.
Look! How valuable these items are...

These are all goods disinfected with champagne (삼판주),
Displayed with dancing,
And intended to be sold with smiles.

To the hands that will soon gather,
Hands clad in rags—hands crying from hunger—
Those hands, numbering more than three thousand...
—(End)—<113>

**Summary**

This collection of five short poems by "Chŏkku" (Red Colt) paints a grim picture of Korean society under Japanese rule in 1926. The poems depict the suffering of the marginalized: a female factory worker enduring harsh labor and harassment ("Female Factory Worker"), a widow grieving her husband killed in what seems like a violent dispute, possibly related to tenant farming or colonial suppression ("Victim"), a man lamenting his forced departure from his ancestral home ("At Younger Brother's Grave"), a call to live in the present moment despite suffering ("The Moment"), and a sharp critique of decadent wealth contrasted with extreme poverty ("Pleasure Market"). The poems capture the despair, anger, and sense of loss prevalent among Koreans facing economic exploitation, social injustice, and displacement during the colonial period. Historians might find the poems valuable for their raw depiction of labor conditions (long hours, abusive supervisors), social conflict (violent clashes suggested in "Victim"), the potential theme of forced migration or loss of land ("At Younger Brother's Grave"), and the stark class divisions highlighted by the contrast between the "Pleasure Market" and the starving masses. The reference to "300,000 people" might allude to the population of Seoul or another major city, emphasizing the scale of shared hardship.

女職工 外四篇
						赤駒
봄은 되얏다면서도 아즉도 겨울과 작별을 짓지 못한 채
-낡은 민족의 잠들어 잇는 저 자 우예
새벽을 알리는 工場의 첫 고동 소리가
그래도 세차게 검푸른 한울을 치바드며
三十 萬 백성의 귓 겻에 울어나기 시작할 때

목도 메다 치여 죽은 남편의 상식상을
밋처 치지도 못하고 그대로 달려온
애젊은 안악네의 갓븐 숨소리야 말로...

惡魔의 굴 속 가튼 作業物 안에서
무릅을 굽힌 채 고개 한 번 돌니지 못하고
열 두 時間이란 그 동안을 보내는 것만 하야도 - 오히려 진저리 나거든
징글징글한 監督 놈의 음침한 눈짓이라니....＜110＞
그래도 그 놈의 뜻을 바더야 한다는 이 놈의 世上-

오오 祖上이여! 남의 남편이여!
왜 당신은 이 놈의 世上을 그대로 두고 가셧습닛가?
-안해를 말리고 자식을 애태우...

犧牲者
저녁 볏이 건너ㅅ山을 기여 올을 때
남편은 憤怒에 질닌 얼골로
동네 작인들과 함께
작대를 끄을고 南쪽 마을로 달려가드니

밤은 三更이나 지나서
달빗 조차 낡어 가는 이 한밤에
屍軆로 變하야 집으로 도라온다
눈도 감지 못한 채 들거지에 언쳐서 -

그러면 앗가 막 설거지를 맛치고 날 때
때아닌 총소리가 련겁허 뒤ㅅ山을 울니더니
그것이 내 남편의 靈魂을 모셔가는
애닯은 永訣 초혼 소래이던가 보다＜111＞

오냐 이 놈!
한 개의 탄자로서 내 남편을 밧궈간 원수놈-
아모련들 가슴의 매듭이 풀닐 줄 아느냐?!
내 목슴이 世上에 멈으러 잇는 동안은-

아오의 무덤에
아오여! 아오의 魂魄이여
兄은 방금 이 땅을 버리고 가려 한다
한 아버지가 주추까지 노아 준
이 땅 이 터의 이 집을 버리고 가려 한다
千里나 萬里나 정처도 업는 곳으로-

그래도 그대는 白骨이나마
祖上의 끼친 터를 베고 잇건만...

刹那
刹那의 목슴을 살러라
-刹那와 刹那를 넘어 가는
瞬間의 苦痛은 생각지 말고-
오즉 刹那에 살리기를 힘쓰라!＜112＞
그러면 자연 現實의 참「삶」을 엇게 되리니...

享樂市場
너르나 너른 대청 안에는
초저녁부터 실어다 노흔 OO의 商品이
벌서 300도 넘어 山가티 싸엿다
밧게는 自働車 馬車가 장을 서고-

高帽, 燕尾服, 칠피구두
금비녀, 玉반지, 綾羅자락
보라! 엘마나 갑 나가는 물건인가를...

이것이 모다 삼판주로 消毒하고
춤으로 陳列한 물건이다
그리고 우슴으로 팔랴는 것이라

未久에 모혀들
누덕이 걸친 손-굼주려 우는 손-
三千名도 더 넘을 그 손들에게...
-(끗)-＜113＞
