Article Title: 五妹 둔 아버지 (Father Who Had Five Daughters)
Author: 乙祿
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Father Who Had Five Daughters
						By Ŭllok
1.

The dreadful daylight had come again! He dreaded the daylight.
It was because—when night returned, in the land of sleep, the refuge from agony (苦惱)—he could feel the happiness of rest (安息), the only kind he had left.
No matter what worse things tomorrow might bring, no matter how bitter the scars left by the pain endured as today was etched away, forgetting all such things—or rather, even if he tried not to think further—just lying quietly with eyes closed in the quiet night, this was truly a time when all things seemed peaceful. More than anything, just the fact that he didn't have to worry about food until tomorrow morning was enough to give him comfort (慰安).

But this happiness was not permitted to him for long. The dreadful daylight would soon descend again, snatching away his enveloping night.— It glared with eyes as fearsome as a policeman's gaze, rousing them—all the poor people—and driving them out into the marketplace teeming with hungry ghosts (agwi).

He opened his eyes wide now. The window was already brightly lit.
“The day has dawned again!”
He muttered this, unsure of its meaning. The thought that he hadn't eaten dinner yesterday made him realize his hunger. He suddenly remembered last night's dream and chuckled to himself.
"...I ate some delicious food too—where was that, anyway?... Gently opening that door... even in a dream <24> such a lovely... Ai, what a wicked dream to have!"
He sat thinking this, then suddenly
"Achoo—"
He let out a loud sneeze. After that, a series of sneezes followed, four or five times in quick succession, uncontrollably.
"They say if you eat in a dream, you catch a cold. Bad luck, looks like I'm catching a cold again. Fine! Come what may! Tuberculosis, typhoid fever, whatever illness kills you in your sleep—"
His smile vanished, and anger rising, he cried out like this, rubbing his left chest with one hand, feeling a dull ache—perhaps pleurisy or the start of neuralgia. He coughed again, "Hack! Hack!" a couple of times, spitting phlegm into the ashtray.

On the gloomy wall, even in this cold weather, bedbugs crawled sluggishly. He shuddered, remembering being bitten by bedbugs in this room last summer. He closed his eyes tightly again, sitting quietly as if pondering something.
Before his eyes, all past events and his own miserable fate pressing in on him seemed to play out like a motion picture film.
"How on earth am I supposed to go on from here?"
He wished he could just see the end of it. If there were truly a skillful fortune-teller nearby, he felt like consulting one.

Something fell outside with a thud.
"Letter's here—"
The postman's (체전부) voice was heard. At this sound, his heart instinctively jumped. He rushed out, grabbed the letter, and came back inside.
It was a letter from his home in the countryside—from his uncle. <25>
"—Are you keeping well in this cold winter away from home? Here at home, troubles never cease, and we're utterly overwhelmed. Chinok's mother seems to have recovered from the worst of her illness, but having been sick for so long, it doesn't look like she'll regain her strength easily. On top of that, something unexpected happened. Tŭkch'ŏl moved to his wife's parents' home early this month. Saying, ‘What concern are my brother’s family to me?’—and since he did the farming, he claimed it was his share—he sold off the grain while I wasn't around and left. So, with the harsh winter upon us, we're short of firewood and food (柴糧). What can I say? When I think of that fellow's actions, leaving his sick sister-in-law and young nephews to potentially starve, it's quite infuriating. But perhaps he felt resentful, thinking you were imposing your family burden on him by neglecting the household and wandering about. What's the use of saying this or that now?—
So, you should come down now and stay home, whether you starve or eat. What good is it for you alone to study well while your family starves? The growing children need teaching too. Isn't Hyŏngok already ten this year? Watching them play around is the most unbearable sight. I trust you have some sense now, so I won't elaborate further. That fellow Yŏngjun (cousin) is also sick in bed. With things in turmoil, I'll stop here." —

He stood holding the letter, suddenly like a man who had lost his mind, staring blankly at one spot. The letter in his hand trembled violently. He looked up at the ceiling and laughed emptily once.
"—What good would it do if I went down! My going down wouldn't suddenly make food appear, would it? Does he mean I should work as a servant again like before to earn a living? To hang my neck again for a few pennies of salary? I'll never do that again, even if I die. I'd rather die than live as a beggar of life!"
He cried out, burning with anger. He clenched both fists tightly and gasped for breath. Suddenly, he rushed to the desk and frantically scribbled a reply.
He wrote for a while, then paused as if struck by another thought, and lowered his head.
"Then what will become of them?" <26>
The thought occurred.—‘They will surely starve to death!’ the thought continued.
‘And what will become of me? Even I—don't have the means to live through today, right now! Then I have no choice but to starve too! Studying, everything, it's all ruined!’ The thought of a dead end arose.
‘That's right! I can't live like this! If I cannot live as a human, it would be better to die! Isn't studying also for becoming human!?... Yes! Then... let's kill them! And let me die too! Kill and die! To save the "human," let's end this wretched life! Let's kill the demons (agwi/greedy ones)!!!’
Crying out like this again, he tore the letter he was writing into pieces, "Rip— Rip—". And as if solidifying this resolve, he clenched his teeth and fists tightly. He stood up abruptly.
He snatched his hat and dashed out the door.

2.

It was already the spring before last.— Since he came out during the Tokyo earthquake chaos, spent that winter, and left his home the following spring.— That's how he ended up coming straight to Seoul, struggling until now, trying to continue the studies he had left unfinished in Tokyo through self-support.
Thinking back now, he himself had struggled for three years, but how had they (his family) survived? It seemed like a miracle. In three years, he had only managed to send ten won (manuscript fee). During that time, the fact that his younger brother alone had supported them all by himself—he couldn't help but think so.

His younger brother was now twenty-five. Fortunately, their father had arranged his marriage while still alive, and now he had a four-year-old daughter whom he adored. Although uneducated—though, considering the family's situation, not being educated might have been fortunate—he was stubborn and ignorant but a remarkably hard worker. So, despite only tenanting five *majigi* (unit of land) of someone else's paddy field, he managed to support the entire family by selling firewood in spring and autumn, doing wage labor, <27> and cultivating various crops with his own hands.

Previously, when he (the protagonist) worked at the bank, his monthly salary was nearly 40 won. Since the salary covered the tenant fees (소작료)—the harvest remained intact as their food supply—they lived without a penny of debt, even managing to lend out some money. Back then, whenever his younger brother met him quietly, he would beam with joy, suggesting they buy a cow or lend out grain at interest (장리변), finding real pleasure in managing the household and working even more diligently.

Indeed, at that time, he neither drank nor gambled and had a reputation as a faithful Christian and a steady person. Because he saved every penny of his salary, the villagers started saying that his family was the only one likely to make it in the village—whispering behind his back how, if the brothers kept earning so diligently, they'd prosper within a few years, surely having saved close to a thousand won already! Some would ask him to lend money, others requested help tenanting land, or sought advice on how to manage their lives; the poor began to look up to him. But just then, he quit that salaried job and suddenly ran off to Japan to study, carrying a wicker suitcase one morning.

That event had shocked not only his brother but the entire family and the villagers considerably. Still, his brother believed that since he had gone to Japan to study, upon his return, he would surely become a district magistrate or a provincial governor, or find some other great opportunity. He waited, trusting implicitly. But when he finally returned after three years, it was barely escaping death in the earthquake, having lost even the bedding he took with him, emerging stark naked.

However, having returned like that, everyone expected him to find another salaried position and live as before. But he, as if some special power had come over him, kept refusing offers, saying,
"I won't work in places like that anymore!"
He stayed home all that winter, writing something, and then went up to Seoul the following spring. But what success? Not a single penny materialized. All that happened was the loss incurred from selling firewood to buy notebooks and Japanese brushes. Later, it turned out he was writing stories. He wondered what kind of money could be earned by writing stories, questioning if that's all he learned studying in Japan for three years. <28>
"Really! My brother is pathetic! The villagers are all laughing. I'm too embarrassed to live in this world! The old saying that good storytellers are poor must be true! Ten won in three years! Can anyone live on that..." He could almost see his brother snorting in derision. So, after waiting and waiting for his brother to return, he finally lost hope and grew estranged, running off saying, "What concern are my brother’s family to me?"

"Yes! Good riddance! Wherever he goes, he can surely support his own family. Was there ever a time when I prospered enough to support him separately?..." He thought inwardly, feeling it was fortunate. This feeling suddenly brought tears to his eyes. He had felt this way towards other family members, but this was the first time he had thought so deeply for his brother.
"—How desperate must he have been to do such a thing? It's truly my fault. As the elder brother, what have I ever done for him? Did I teach him even a single character? Did we live affectionately as brothers at home? (Not that I disliked my brother—)... Fine! You go and live well! I have absolutely no right to blame you for leaving like that." The more he acknowledged his faults like this, the more various past instances where he had wronged his brother came flooding back to his memory, as if reflected before a mirror.

"Ah—how much more unfortunate a fate was that brother born with than mine? Losing his mother at seven, raised under a stepmother—(not to say the stepmother was wicked) eating the bread of affliction under watchful eyes, wasn't he a neglected child? Wasn't he the brother who started carrying the firewood A-frame (나무찌개) from that time? While I sat comfortably in the room reading books, I ate the rice cooked with firewood gathered by his frail hands, hands scratched by the rough reeds! ... And what did I give that brother? Nothing! I did nothing for him!"
He questioned his conscience (량심), realizing that it wasn't out of kindness that he thought of his brother this way. And he felt ashamed of his own impure <29> heart, knowing that if this incident hadn't occurred, he wouldn't have sympathized with his brother like this.
"Can I really despise him for being ignorant? Can worldly people also criticize him for being ignorant? It's like bandits (不汗黨) sucking the blood and sweat of laborers, playing around shamelessly—and then blaming those who couldn't learn because they were too busy feeding them, calling them ignorant. It's a contradiction!... But I never intended to make my brother suffer deliberately. I only lament that even if I tried to explain this to him with a thousand words, he wouldn't understand! I just want to say that my wandering like this is definitely not for my own comfort. Even if the whole world says otherwise..."
A tear silently rolled down his cheek.—

He remembered the letter from his second cousin (륙촌) that arrived yesterday evening.—
The letter also expressed worries about being unable to survive—how to sustain the deteriorating economic life? It urged him to come down quickly, saying his family's situation was now beyond words.— It said he, having considerable knowledge, was pointlessly tainted by the morbid thoughts (病的思想) of modern youth—and that it was pitiful—what use was it to neglect his family, even if he were doing some great work?—Even a simpleton like Tŭkch'ŏl knows his wife and children; if we don't know ours, aren't we worse than Tŭkch'ŏl?—it said. Reading the letter then, he had retorted,
"Morbid thoughts!"
and laughed mockingly, "Haha." Hearing from his uncle yesterday morning that his brother had left was less upsetting than these words from his second cousin. It felt like the common greetings from acquaintances he hadn't seen in a long time:
"Ah! Mr. C! It's been a while. So, have you made a lot of money (돈 만히 버럿소)?"
This kind of pleasantry felt extremely unpleasant to him.

Does the capitalist era think there's nothing greater than money? To think that his desire to study—a path he had sought since childhood, <30> battling harsh circumstances, pursuing with what he considered firm resolve and carving his way—in other words, setting literature as his goal and studying while working—was considered being poisoned by morbid thoughts? How upsetting was that? How little they understood him, he thought.— If only they had asked, "How much have you studied during this time?" How welcome those words would have sounded.
"Damn him! Does it cost anything to speak? Why bother asking if I've made money, something I hate hearing?"
He remembered cursing like this once after receiving such a greeting. He stared blankly at the sky visible above P Park (P공원).— The bare branches of the skeletal trees trembled and cried out in the cold wind. He lowered his head again and started walking towards his house. He remembered the words he heard from his father-in-law last summer when he visited his wife's home after a long time. His father-in-law, who had inexplicably acquired a fancy title in the meantime, sat solemnly and spoke.— Later, he found out the reason for the "title" was that he had obtained a sinecure (淸閑之職) as an official at the local Confucian academy (향교, 鄕校直員).
"—It is said that patriots disregard their families, but even among patriots, those who truly disregard their families are rare. Unless—one becomes like Sun Yat-sen (손일선) of the great nation, perhaps. Even then, one cannot escape being a sinner to one's family—much less if one cannot achieve that, what's the use of just sitting idly? So what have you been doing in Seoul? Running some kind of bookstore, they say! A storybook store! Can you earn money running a storybook store like that?..."
At that time, he had said nothing. If he had spoken up, he feared a clumsy argument (모양수퉁한 두발부리) might ensue. He just thought inwardly, how ignorant the words of the titled gentleman (량반) were!

But the words heard this time from his second cousin—though similarly harsh—were more painful coming from a close relation. Hearing such words, he felt his loneliness like a drop of oil floating on water.
My brother has betrayed me. My cousin calls me poisoned by morbid thoughts! Those closest to me are all leaving me.
That's right! I am lonely! Blocked front and back! Sick and without support (팡업고)! No need to think further! Let's go! Let's go kill them! And then take up that knife and step out! Hmph! Then the world will call me even more wicked. They'll put it in the newspapers. <31> Fine! Good! Let them do as they please...
He screamed this internally and entered the room. He shook his head as if there was nothing more to consider.— The sun had already passed its zenith. He threw himself down right there and lay as if dead, eyes shut tight. The floor was ice-cold. Lying in this dark, gloomy room—a cave-like room with only one door—dressed in black, he looked like a wild animal hunted by a tracker, hiding collapsed in its den.— The strong impulse of life denied the right to live freely on earth, and the burning desire of instinct—an unbearable rebellion against the predators—made his eyes flash again with a furious frenzy (憤怒), wanting to roar and leap out like lightning, biting whatever he encountered.—

He sold the only book he had, one he particularly cherished (愛讀), and went to the station that night.

3.

Pitch-black cliff! A moonless night where one couldn't distinguish anything nearby, and on top of that, an unseasonal, drizzling rain fell damply.
Will-o'-the-wisps flickered here and there in the public cemetery. A small mountain path from K village (K촌) to the town center ran through the middle of this cemetery ridge.
From this grave and that, ghosts rose abruptly and came out of their tombs. They were immensely glad to be out in the spacious world after being squeezed into narrow graves. A motley crew of shaman ghosts and rake ghosts began their skeleton dance, making rattling sounds, "Ttuktktak! Ttuktktak!"— Child graves (兒塚) whispered, female graves (女塚) rustled, and the ghost of one who hanged herself cried with a faint voice,
"Oo-hoo-hoo-hoo-..."
Egg ghosts rolled about, "Ttaegol! Ttaegol!" A ghost with disheveled hair, dressed in white mourning clothes, laughed hysterically... <32>
"Heh! Heh! Heh! Heh! Heh! Heh! Heh! ..." Heh! Heh! ..."
The ghost of the girl who would have been fifteen now if alive, also emerged from her grave and whistled for the ghost of her younger sister, whom their father had particularly cherished. The three siblings were buried side-by-side even in death.— The ghost of the youngest sister, who died a month after birth, was buried on the far left.
"Hwi—hwi—hwi—"
"Hwi— Sister, are you up?"
replied the younger sister ghost.
"Hwi— Yes, did you sleep well!"
Then the youngest sister ghost at the far end cries out impatiently and comes chasing after them.
"Hwi— Sister! I want to live among humans again! I want to live with Mother! ..."
she began to cry, choked with sobs.
"Hwi— Little sister! Don't cry! We want to live among humans too! We also want to live with Mother!"
The older sister ghosts comforted their younger sister ghost like this.
"Hwi— Mother still thinks of you and regrets it. Because she used the wrong folk medicine (상약), your illness worsened, and you couldn't recover, dying from infantile eczema (태독), didn't you? Ignorant people still say it was just your fate. They say starving to death is also just fate! Truly, ignorance killed you. Human ignorance killed you!"
"Hwi— I followed sister when I was four, didn't I? Father was in Japan then; that wretched disease (measles/smallpox - 홍억) killed me like that. But what killed me was ‘poverty.’ If Father had been home then and I had gotten proper medicine, I wouldn't have died like that..."
sighed the younger sister ghost, whom the father had particularly cherished, sighing through her bony joints, again and again.
"Hwi— At least you sisters saw Father's face and were held in his arms! I never even saw what Father <33> looked like..."
The youngest sister ghost cries out like this, weeping even more sorrowfully. So the older sister ghosts also shed black tears from their vacant eyes.
"Hwi— You say I was held in Father's arms? That's a blatant lie. I was spanked by Father many times, but never held once!"
exclaimed the eldest sister ghost defensively, finding it absurd.
"Hwi— Father was immature back then. He cherished you inwardly but was embarrassed outwardly... Didn't Father have sister when he was sixteen?"
said the younger sister ghost, smiling faintly with her toothless mouth.
"Hwi— Still, you sisters lived longer among humans than I did! Even if Father had beaten me to death, I wish I could have seen his face just once! ... Why was a child who couldn't even see her father's face ever born! ..."
"Hwi— That's all misfortune arising from human contradiction."
"Hwi— That's right! It's all misfortune arising from ignorance. Because people always think only of themselves and don't know others' situations, conflicts arise, and contradictions emerge? So, those who sing of humanitarianism look down on slums while eating well themselves, and humans, supposedly better than beasts, see beastly, demonic humans (아귀인간) yet still try to suck their blood— But if one opens the eyes of reason (理智) in quiet silence (沈黙), all things in the universe become one harmoniously, so I can live within them, and they can live within me. There, the boundless life of the universe <34> flows continuously (滾滾) like a river! Ah! I want to tell this to humans, but the worlds of the living and the dead (幽明) are different, so what can be done? Ah! Ignorant humans! Ignorant humans!"
"Indeed, you speak the truth! Even if one lives out their so-called full lifespan, how pitifully fleeting (刹那) is it compared to this country—the eternal kingdom of death (死의 王國)? When they say 'Human life reaching seventy is rare since ancient times,' they are actually speaking of a short life! Why engage in wretched battles of snatching, being snatched, hiding, and stealing, all born as fellow humans in such a short life? How foolish is this human behavior?
If one must fight, it should be against birth, old age, sickness, and death (生老病死) to create happy humans. Instead of creating ignorant humans and demonic humans through self-destructive acts akin to a blind man eating his own chicken—if every person had freedom in economy (經濟) and love (戀愛), and gave the best possible human education (人間的 最良의 教育) to the sons and daughters born from such unions—if they pursued spiritual elevation (高尙) through refined tastes and competition (競技) instead of the current demonic struggle (아귀다툼)—then surely the desires of human instinct could be satisfied without resorting to the beastly instinct (獸的本能) of the survival of the fittest (弱肉强食). But ignorant humans do not know this. Truly, how can they be so ignorant?"
chimed in the eldest sister ghost.
"Hwi— What's the use of saying such things anew now?— Everyone who comes to this country has said it at least once. It's something everyone knows well! Didn't we also live among humans? Isn't it exactly so? Only when they come to this country do they finally realize clearly, 'Ah! I didn't know it was like that! I was foolish! I was wrong!' They stamp their feet and regret, but what use is it then? It's like someone who can't swim agonizing on the shore, unable to save a drowning person! Let's stop this talk, like preaching to deaf ears. Isn't Father coming down tonight? He must have just arrived at the station! Ah! I think he'll come this way—"
said the younger sister ghost whom the father had particularly cherished.
"Hwi— True! Father is coming down!" <35>

"...But can I really kill them? ..."
The weak thought arose again. He shook his head.—
‘No, circumstances beyond control will necessitate their killing! Even an executioner, when killing someone for the first time, probably does it under similar circumstances! This is different from a murderous robber committing a heinous act in a moment of passion!... Then if I don't kill them, what am I supposed to do? No, should I let them starve to death? Isn't that too cruel a deed?’
He asked himself this, as if arguing with someone.

"—Among all kinds of death, there is no death as frightening and inhuman as starving to death! They say dying on the gallows after receiving a death sentence is the most terrifying, but that is still a death where a human is killed by a human, recognized as a fellow human. But starving to death... when even birds and beasts have their own food, to die because one lacks sustenance (량식)—or because others have it while I alone do not—to starve to death like that—starving for just a moment invites the hungry ghost (agwi) to inflict all sorts of shame and humiliation upon him. Try starving for a moment, two moments, a day, two days, three days, four days! The mouth becomes parched, the eyes sink deeper, withering away like plants in a drought, but instead, the mind becomes increasingly sharp, 'Malttong! Malttong!' (clear, alert), keenly feeling, with every memory sacrificed, that one is the most unfortunate person in the world. Then, finally, one goes mad, seeing everything as food, grabbing and eating whatever comes into sight, be it one's own child, father, or mother... Ah! A terrifying thing! A terrifying thing! How can I inflict this upon them? Killing them might be a sin. But doing that (letting them starve) is an even greater sin. The sin of killing them is only against the three of them. But letting them starve is a sin against all humanity. Surely, wouldn't she gnaw the little ones starting from the head?... So when I enter, and she says, 'Ah! Why did you come only now? I made soup and waited so long...' and I ask, 'What soup? Ah! Where are the children!' she might reply, 'Huh? <39> The two chickens we had at home? Ate them all! This is the soup! Ate the big one yesterday and the small one today! ... The small one was young, so its meat was tender and so delicious! Here! Try some! Try some!... Goodness! You've turned into a chicken too! No, a cow! An ox... Right! Another one to eat has appeared! Heh! Heh! Heh!'"
The ghosts of his daughters clinging to his body heard these thoughts (words) of their father and trembled with fear, goosebumps rising all over. He broke out in a cold sweat.—
"Hwi— More frightening than ghosts are humans?"
cried the eldest daughter ghost clinging to his left shoulder.
"Hwi— That's right! More frightening than ghosts are hungry demons (agwi)—"
replied the younger daughter ghost clinging to his right shoulder. The youngest daughter ghost just trembled in fear. He thought again.
"Hasn't it already happened during this time?"
This thought instilled a certain horror in him too. He shuddered violently.
Is it so? Is it not? Let's see the definite truth quickly! As if thinking this, he quickened his pace, stumbling through the dark path ahead. In the pitch-black night, the dreary rain continued to fall damply. It was the dead of night! All around was deathly silent.

5.

He coughed a couple of times and entered unhesitatingly through the brushwood gate, flinging open the door to the inner room (anbang). Perhaps even the brushwood gate (삽작문) had been gnawed away, as only the frame remained, wide open.
"Hwi— Is this really our house?"
"Hwi— Yes, it is! You must be seeing this house for the first time!"
"Hwi— It looks completely deserted! No brushwood gate either!" <39>
The ghosts whispered amongst themselves again.
"Who's there!?"
A voice cried out from inside the room, startled and breathless. He didn't answer but coughed loudly once. Inside, they seemed to realize who it was.
"Oh... what brings you here! In the middle of the night... Oh, where are the matches! Oh—don't cry! I'll light the lamp for you! ..." the wife soothed the crying child as she found a matchstick and lit the kerosene lamp. In the lamplight, squinting his eyes, she pushed aside piles of rags and tatters. Her ghost-like appearance instantly made him lose all affection (정나미가 떠러젓다). The hungry ghost (agwi) had thoroughly made its nest.— The young child, quieted by the lamplight, sat blankly staring at the unfamiliar father, then suddenly puckered its lips and crawled onto its mother's lap. He stared blankly at the child. A musty stench pierced his nose.

"Oh— Father! Father!"
The wife smiled wanly and embraced him. The older son, whose clipped hair had grown out untidily, lay sleeping soundly, "Sek! Sek!" at the warmest part of the floor (아래목).
"Hwi— The older brother is sleeping! Is this child Chinok?"
"Hwi— Yes. Quite handsome, isn't he! After being sick for so long, he's still not fully recovered!"
The ghosts moved towards the young child.
"Hwi— Oh, look at these plump hands! Goodness! He's smiling! Maybe he knows we're here!"
"Hwi— Really! Look at those eyes! It's like he's looking right at us! Can babies who can't speak know everything in the world? Perhaps speech is just a moth that eats away spiritual wisdom (靈智)!"
"Hwi— Look at this child's little penis, like a chili pepper! Look at these soft (‘mallang-mangnang’) testicles!"
said the youngest daughter ghost, touching the baby's genitals and laughing. <40>
"Hwi! Eh! His mouth resembles mine! His nose resembles Father! Right? Girls!"
"Hwi! How does his mouth resemble yours, sister? His mouth resembles Mother!"
"Hwi! The eyes and nose are exactly like Father's... Look! The way he opens his eyes... But if he had nursed better and been raised in a clean room, his face would look healthier and prettier! If he were dressed well too!"
"Hwi! Truly, I wish we could dress him in fine clothes! What is this? Like a beggar... Oh! Why has Mother aged so much!"
asked the youngest daughter ghost, suddenly startled as if just noticing.
"Hwi! Of course, she'd age, living in a house like this! Hardship makes you age prematurely!"
replied the eldest daughter ghost.
"Hwi! Truly, it's more hideous than our graves! How can they live like this! And there's nothing to eat, I just want to take Mother away with us. Father's thoughts are understandable. Right? Sister! Huh?"
"Hwi! Who says otherwise! Mother was quite pretty too when she gave birth to me. Plump, with rosy cheeks flushed with color—that was Mother's prime. She was eighteen then, but Father was immature... When they married in the spring when he was fourteen, he was even more like a child. Mother was sixteen then, quite mature, already fully grown... Back then, Mother was heartbroken, and now Father is heartbroken!"
"Hwi— Father doesn't hate Mother just because she's aged."
"Hwi! Still, if Mother looked like she did back then, Father would surely love her. Father has completely forgotten Mother's face from that time! So only hateful thoughts remain..."
"Hwi— Shh— Let's listen to what Father is saying! We've been chattering amongst ourselves and haven't heard a word yet!"
At the youngest sister's words, the older sister ghosts abruptly stopped talking and pricked up their ears. They moved closer to their mother's body, pressing their cheeks against hers! <41>
"...After my brother-in-law left like that, well—my maternal uncle came and gave me about a hundred won, and we've managed with that until now! Firewood is so expensive, we can't afford to buy it... So Hyŏngok and I go to the back hill and gather it..."
"Ah— You make Hyŏngok gather firewood!"
he asked, as if startled.
"What else can we do! We can't freeze to death... Now that you've come down, maybe we can send him to school next spring!"
The wife spoke nonchalantly, a faint smile on her haggard face, rendering him speechless. He blurted out these words.
"I'm leaving again!"
At these words, the wife's face instantly turned deathly pale. Her voice trembled, and tears mixed with her words.

「그럼 엇지해여! 어러 죽을 수는 업고... 인제 당신이 내려오섯스니 래년 봄에나 학교에 늣토록 하오!」
안해는 부숙부숙한 얼골에 열븐 미소를 띄우며 이런 말을 쳔연히 하는데는 그는 긔가 막히지 안을 수 업섯다. 그는 이런 말을 쑥 하엿다.
「나는 또 갈 터이야!」
이 말 한마듸에 안해의 얼골은 별안간 새파라케 변하엿다. 그의 목소리는 떨리고 말소리에는 울음이 석겨 나왓다.
「어데로 또 가... 그럼 무엇하러 왓소? 이번에 또 갈 터이면... 안이 우리 삼모자를 죽이고 가오! 내 죽어도 못가게 하던지 내가 쪼처가던지 할걸! 못 가! 가긴 어데로 가...」
하고 안해는 이를 「박박!」 간다. 두 눈에서는 눈물이 텀벙! 텀벙! 쏘다저 그의 야윈 볼위로 굴너 떠러진다.
「그러지 안어도 죽이러 왓다. 너들도 죽이고 나도 죽고!」
그는 서리빨 가티 냉냉한 태도로 조곰도 서슴지 안코 이러케 말하엿다. 훌적어리든 안해는 별안간 울음을 내노앗다. 그 바람에 어린애도 따러 운다.
「... 아! 아! 한우님 맙시사! 내가 무슨 죄가 잇서서 여태 알뜰이도 고... 고생을 짓시기다가 인제와서는 또 죽이겟다오? ... 시집온 지가 근 이십년되니 옷 한 가지를 하여 주엇나? 잘 먹이기를 하엿나. 자식을 다섯이나 낫스니! 그것들을 하나나 키워주엇뀀˜? ... 흙! 흙! 흙... 아이그... 그것들은 제 신세조케 잘들 죽엇지! 세년들이 그저 다 살앗서보아! ... 자식 둘 낳은 것도 주채를 못하여서 못 가르치고 못 먹이다가 나종에는 그것들까지 죽이겟다고... 글세 - 그것들이 무슨 죄가 잇기에! ... 아! 어미 애비＜42＞ 잘못 맛난 죄로... 아! 아! 아! 아! ...」
「듯기 실혀! 이게 무슨 청승마진 소리냐? ... 이 당장에 뒤여지지 못해서!」
하고 그는 소리를 버럭 내질넛다.
「죽여! 죽여! 자! 어서 죽여!」
안해도 마조 악을 쓰며 대들엇다. 어린애는 더욱 놀내서 저의 어머니를 꼭 붓들고 긔급을 하며 운다. 자던 애도 니러나서 비죽! 비죽 울기 시작하엿다.
「휘- 아버지! 어머니! 이 일을 엇지 하나! 엇지 하나!」
하고 귀신딸들도 안타까웁게  부르지젓다.
「휘! 이 얘들아! 어서 가야겟다. 미구에 닭이 울겟다!」
하고 별안간 큰언니 귀신이 정신이 나서 동생들을 재촉하엿다.
「휘! 이런 꼴을 보고 엇더케 떼치고 가나! 어머니! 아버지! 동생아! 동생아! 그래도 더 좀 살앗스면! 아버지! 아버지! 제발 죽이지는 마러요!」
「휘! 어서 가요! 가요! 가요!」
그들은 황황히 문틈으로 바람길을 타고 나갓다
「휘! 우! 후! 후! 후!」
하고 삼형제 귀신은 모다 울고 갓다. 그들은 어머니와 아버지를 위하야 울고 갓다. 어린 동생들을 위하야 울고 갓다. 안이 왼 인간을 위하야 울엇다!
미구에 건너말 큰말에서 장닭이 홰를 치고 울자 여러 머리 닭들이 그 뒤를 따라서 요란히 울어 드컨다.

6.

그 잇흔 날 저녁 때 K촌으로 가는 공동묘지 산말낭이에는 동조고리 빠람인 머리깍근 웬 청년이 하야손으로＜43＞ 어설피게 낫질하며 말은 풀을 베고 잇는데 그 엽헤는 팔구세 되어보이는 빨간 저고리 입은 아해가 조고만 손으로 갈퀴질을 하고 잇다. 그런데 그 밋흐로는 공동묘지의 점점한 무덤 위에 쓸쓸한 겨을 석양이 빗겻는데 저편 장등이로 세 무덤의 조고만 얘총이 우굿한 마른 플속에 무쳐잇다. 만일 그 압헤 비에 마저 썩은 나무의 조고만 묘표가 서지 안엇스면 무덤인지 무엇인지 알아볼 수도 업슬만치.
손흰 나무군은 우굿우굿한 풀폭이를 쪼쳐 단이며 비다가 이 애춍 압흐로 와서 거긔난 풀을 베엿다. 그리고 이 패말을 정신업시 드려다 보앗다. 거긔에는 어느 해 어느 달에 나코 어느 해 어느 날에 죽엇다는 날짜와 아모개의 묘라는 성명이 간신히 알아볼 만치 씨워 잇는데 그것은 그의 그 중 사랑하던 둘재 딸의 일홈이엿다.
그는 한동안 실신한 사람 가티 이 묘패를 뚜러지게 보고 잇는데 별안간
「아버지!」
하고 날카롭게 부르짓는 소리에 깜짝 놀내여 뒤를 도라다 보앗다.
「아버지! 아버지! 저... 나무 글거다 노은 것이 바람에 불려서 모다 날너가유! 저 봐! 저 봐! ...」
하고 아해는 산 위에서 내리질는 바람에 숨이 턱턱! 막혀서 말을 되채지 못하며 눈물이 글성 글셩! 하니 조곰만 더하면 울음을 내놀 것 갓다. 과연 산 위를 쳐다보니 이번에 또 휘! 하고 니러나는 회호리 바람에 나무찜은 까마케 비행긔 가티 공중으로 떠 올너간다. 그는 그것을 쳐다보다가 제 풀에 허허 우셧다.
「오! 치우냐? 그럼 어서 좀 글거 가지고 집에 가자!」
그는 두 손을 「호! 호!」 불고 셧는 아들의 손목을 이끌고 지계 잇는 편으로 급히 쪼쳐갓다.
해질 무럼에 까치집 가튼 조고만 나무찜이 K촌으로 내려오는데 그는 그것도 무서워서 그러는지 이리비쳑 저리비쳑하야 조곰만 하면 넘겨박힐 뜻한 나무찜을 간신히 곤우면서 작대기를 집허가며 갓갓으로 내려오는 모양이엇다.
이것을 보는 건너말 사람들은 모다 우슴을 내뿜으며 손가락질을 하엿다.
「... 서울가서 여태 공부를 햇다는 것이 인제와서 나무찜을 지랴고 한 공부이던가? 허허!」＜44＞
「이 사람아! 서울 뿐인가? 일본가서도 삼년이나 잇지 안엇는가?」
「허! 허! 참! 밥을 비러다가 죽을 쑤어먹는다더니 웨 제발 오라는데도 다 고만두고는 인제와서 시장하게 저 짓을 한담! 남의 일이라도 딱한 일이야!」
「안이 지금이라도 운동을 하면 무슨 월급자리를 어들지도 모르는데 그런 것은 도모지 안넌다니 참 이샹한 셩미도 다 잇지. 도모지 팔자소관이란 억지로 못하는 게야!」
하고 그들은 제각기 떠들며 흉을 본다. 이웃집 각씨들도 삽작문 귀투리에부터 서서 모다 웃고 내다보앗다.
「어머니! 어머니!」
하고 아들은 나무찜을 압장 서서 급히 뛰여드러가며 어머니를 소리쳐 불느는데 그의 입도 역시 우숨이 괴엿다. 그러자 어린애를 업고 저녁을 짓던 안해가 부지땡이를 든 채로 내다보다가 역시 해죽이 웃고 내다본다. 그는 이런 줄 저런 줄 모르고 드러오다가 고만 안마당에다 나무찜을 며내부첫다. 별안간 우숨소래가 요란히 들니엿다.
그러나 그는 아모러치도 안은 드시 다시 나무를 부억으로 안어드렷다. 그리고 어린애를 바더가지고 방안으로 드러갓다.
양식이라고는 몃 칠 먹을 것박 밧업는데 아모 근심 걱정이 업시 만사태평으로 지내는 남편의 모양이 달려들며 첫 인사가 누구를 죽이느니 어듸로 또 가느니하던 그가... 쳔만 뜻밧게 지게를 지고 나서는 것은 생각할수록 이상한 일이엿다마는 한엽흐로 다시 생각하면 인제는 남편이 마음을 잡어서 살님살이를 알들히 해보랴는 셈이나 안닌가 하는 불안한 중에도 밋브고 깃뿐 마음으로 안해는 그의 눈치를 슬슬 보앗다.
그러나 그의 마음속을 아는 사람은 오즉 그 자신 뿐이엿다.
