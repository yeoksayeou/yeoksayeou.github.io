To the Poet - 1925 Work -
						Yi Sanghwa

Only when you realize that with one poem
You must give birth to a whole new world,
Poet, only then will your existence
Be known to the universe as indispensable—
Just as there must be the cry of the tree frog by the drought-stricken paddy field—

Even within the so-called new world,
See if only the manner emerges where mind and body live separated.
Poet, your life
Is still playing the role of a shuddering cripple.
What does it matter if the eclipsed sun ever rises, or what if it sets?

Poet, your glory
Must become the guileless heart of a child who steps even on a mad dog's tail;
Whether night or day,
It lies in the moment when the traces of fervent effort to birth a new world become poetry—
Look at the moth, beautiful even as it flies into the candle flame and dies.

Lamentation - 1925 Work - <114>

Looking up at the heavens,
Though I weep,
It is not out of longing for the heavens that I weep.
Because this land where I cannot stretch my two feet is sorrowful,
Glancing up at the heavens,
Tears burst forth.
Sun, do not laugh!
Moon, do not rise!
<114-114>

**Summary**

The first poem, "To the Poet," exhorts poets to understand their profound responsibility: to create a "new world" through their art, rejecting detached aestheticism ("mind and body separated") and embracing passionate engagement, even if it means self-destruction like a moth drawn to flame. The second poem, "Lamentation," expresses deep sorrow and frustration, not directed at the heavens, but rooted in the painful reality of "this land where I cannot stretch my two feet," culminating in a desperate cry against the indifference of nature (sun and moon). From a historical perspective, these poems are significant for their intense expression of anguish and constrained existence, reflecting the atmosphere of colonial Korea in the 1920s. The poet's call for art rooted in struggle and the raw despair tied to the physical land ("this land") would resonate with historians studying Korean nationalism and the intellectual response to Japanese occupation.

詩人에게 - 1925年 作 -
						李相和
한 篇의 詩 그것으로
새로운 世界 한아를 나허야 할 줄 깨칠 그 때라야
詩人아 너의 存在가
비로소 宇宙에게 업지 못할 너로 알려질 것이다
감음든 논께에는 청개고리의 울음이 잇서야 하듯-

새 世界란 속에서도
마음과 몸이 갈려 사는 즐 풍류만 나와 보아라
詩人아 너의 목숨은
진저리 나는 절눔바리 노릇을 아즉도 하는 것이다
언제든지 日蝕된 해가 도드면 뭣 하며 진들 엇더랴

詩人아 너의 榮光은
밋친 개꼬리도 밟는 어린애의 쌈 업는 그 마음이 되야
밤이라도 낫이라도
새 世界를 나흐려 소댄 자욱이 詩가 될 때에-잇다
초ㅅ불로 날라드러 죽어도 아름다운 나비를 보아라

慟哭 - 1925作 -＜114＞

한울을 우럴어
울기는 하여도
한울이 그리워 울음이 아니다
두 발을 못 뻣는 이 땅이 애닯어
한울을 흘끼니
울음이 터진다.
해야 웃지마라
달도 뜨지마라
