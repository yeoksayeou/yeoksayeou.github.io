발행일	1926년 04월 01일

필자	大膽生
기사형태	소식 (News/Items)

Things Heard and Seen
						Taedamsaeng

It's said that Yi Kakchong, a commissioned associate (囑托) of the Government-General of Joseon (朝鮮總督府), gathers his usual factions of pro-Japanese and anti-Japanese types, scrapes by on the leftovers from the authorities (當局), puts out one antique piece (骨董品) [likely referring to a magazine issue] each month, yet still squares his shoulders as if proud, foolishly claiming he will unify Joseon's magazines. Indeed, relying on some backing somewhere, he has apparently even taken over "Joseon Mundan" (朝鮮文壇). Is he like a daughter-in-law or a son-in-law in this arrangement?

Speaking of "Joseon Mundan," after a period of clamor with Yi Kwangsu as publisher (主宰) and Pang In'gŭn in charge (濁擔), there was no news for three or four months, making one wonder anxiously, "Did they kill that off too?" But perhaps because warm spring is the season of revival (回生之節), its shadow reappeared in March. Exclaiming, "Oh! It wasn't dead after all!" I opened it to find the publisher's address was 89 Ch'ayach'ŏng (茶屋町八九番地), likely the "Sinminsa" (新民社). Mr. Pang reportedly said nonchalantly, without batting an eye, "We moved the business office to 89 Ch'ayach'ŏng for business convenience." Trying to pull the wool over people's eyes (눈 가리우고 아웅) is something you do in the dead of night. In this situation where everything is already out in the open (귀신 다 된 이 판국), just being honest is best.

They say an ideal communist village (共産村) praising labor (勞働禮讚) has emerged at An'onji Temple (安源寺) in Nagano Prefecture (長野縣), Japan. Seventeen farming households have gathered amicably, living well together, working jointly (共同力作) and sharing food jointly (共同分食). It seems the Japanese authorities (日本 當局者) either don't know about this communist village we know of, or if they do, with their usual hypersensitivity, they surely wouldn't leave it alone. Who knows, maybe diplomatic relations (國交斷絶) have been severed again in the meantime?

In New York City (紐育市), USA (米國), they supposedly ordered the suspension of streetcars, calling them a "traffic hindrance" (交通妨害). Those Americans are truly formidable; perhaps because the world keeps talking about America, America, they are causing all sorts of disturbances with such swift actions (밧삭하는 셈). Perhaps soon they'll declare people themselves a traffic hindrance and confine them to their rooms. Yet, in Seoul (서울), Joseon (朝鮮), citizens complain greatly about the lack of streetcars, and Beijing (北京), China (中國), hasn't even had a taste of streetcars yet. How about shipping the New York streetcars over here?

Thinking it was like before, I confidently offered 5 *chŏn* saying, "I'll buy a ticket to Yongsan (龍山票)," but they brusquely rejected it, saying it was 10 *chŏn*. Humbled (코를 뚝 떼고), I asked, and they said the fare had gone up by 5 *chŏn* three days ago. Now that the Government-General (總督府) is directly managing it, they bestow this kind of 'benefit' (惠澤) (spending money), I suppose. Aside from that, they are also reportedly abolishing the free ride passes (無賃乘車券) for commuting students. They plan to snatch 90 *chŏn* per year (3 times) under the guise of a 'processing fee' (手續費). There's no use talking about the arbitrary actions (自行自止) of those in power, but this is going too far.

Kim Ki-t'ae was sued by his mistress for 60,000 *wŏn* in alimony (慰藉科). Min Tae-sik filed a defamation suit (名譽毁損訟訴) against a certain newspaper (某日 文紙) (regarding gambling, etc.). Paek In-gi's son attempted suicide by poison (飮毒自殺) because he wasn't given money. Yu Yong-jun, a student at the Medical College (醫學專門學校), committed suicide with a razor (面刀自殺) over love. A woman in Puch'ŏn (富川), plagued by conscience after stealing beggars' food (乞食物窃取), committed suicide. Kim Ki-jŏn had a son and a daughter at one go (一擧). Ch'a Sang-ch'an is being sued by his landlord for eviction (家屋明渡訴訟) for non-payment of monthly rent (朔月稅). Pak Tal-sŏng is said to be suffering headaches over the issue of reforming his wife (녀편네 改造問題). Truly an inconsistent (不一) world-<82>
Now that spring has arrived, there seem to be quite a few profligate men and women (蕩女蕩子) inside and outside the city gates. Some pair, on their way back from riotous fun at Yŏngdo Temple (永導寺), apparently shouted "Fire!" (불이야!) for no reason on the main road. Unsuspecting neighborhood people all ran out yelling, "Huh! Fire!" In the end, there was no fire, and realizing it was a prank by passersby, they became furious (골이 萬丈이 나서), shouting, "Hey, you damned couple! Did the fire break out in your mother's crotch?! Just watch, you brats!" As the crowd moved in to beat them, the *kisaeng* girl begged for her life, hands and feet pleading, while the men got beaten black and blue (눈이 빠지도록 엇어맛고) and fled into a streetcar like hiding mice. Those idle people must have been asking for trouble (볼따귀가 가렵든게지).

If you're a medicine peddler (藥行商), fine, but why threaten someone who doesn't want to buy medicine? If you're disabled (병신), fine, but why grab a busy person on the street? And another thing. If you're a policeman (巡査), fine, but what's the need to boast about your uniform (服裝)? If you're a county magistrate (郡守), fine, but what's the need to boast about your official title (職名), saying "I am the County Magistrate"? There are too many such disagreeable (안이 꼬운) people. This is just what I've seen over a few days.

At Jinju Higher Common School (晉州 高普校), it's said they ordered a student to leave despite excellent entrance exam results (試驗成績良好), citing insufficient wealth (財産不足). Thus, the student, planning for a long future (萬里를 計하는 학생), reportedly returned home crying his heart out, appealing to heaven and earth (呼天動地 放聲大哭而歸). When will this sordid (齷齪) and contradictory world where capital is almighty (資本萬能) ever be set right (歸正)? Such incidents surely aren't limited to Jinju Higher Common School. Proletarians (無産者)! Learning is secondary (둘재다). Isn't there something else you must do?

Miss Ross(?) (羅西姐), the American woman at the controversial Kyŏngsŏng (京城) East Gate Women's Hospital (東大門 婦人病院), has finally resigned, reportedly unable to withstand the pressure (攻迫) from many involved parties. It seems even money (金力) and power (權力) are helpless before public opinion (輿論). She should have stuck it out a bit longer, devoured (잡아먹고) a few more nurses, and then retreated-.

Kawai Shigejirō (河合繁次郞), the murderous policeman (殺人巡査) in Songhwa (松禾) (who pointlessly shot dead a 13-year-old Joseon girl named Yi Ip-pun (李立粉) at a tavern on February 11th), is said to have received a sentence of a 200 *wŏn* fine from the Songhwa Branch Office (松禾支廳). The result of the so-called "serious indictment" (嚴重論告) citing the "huge influence on colonial policy" (植民地政策에 影響莫大) is reportedly a 200 *wŏn* fine. A fine of 200 *wŏn* for a flower-like (꼿 가튼) human life—this is truly an unprecedented example of arbitrary action (恣行) among Japanese in the colonies. Aside from the discontent (不平聲) of the local people, anyone who hears this is simply left dumbfounded (입이 벙벙할 외에 他道가 업다).

Regarding the tumultuous Kyŏngsŏng Gyesan Boseong School (京城桂山 普成學校), despite our having placed some hope (囑望) in its takeover by the Japanese Nishidera Yoshihiko (西寺嘉彦), stakeholders of the school are now launching a major opposition movement (大擧 반대운동), claiming that Nishidera will completely ruin (아조 말 못되겟다고) the school. The reason is that, contrary to the initial takeover agreement, Nishidera plans to build a Women's Academy (女子學院), his separate enterprise, on the school's playground (運動場). The arbitrary nature (姿行) of capitalism is thus hateful. His original intention (本意) in the takeover can be known (可知) from this (於斯).

It's reported that another thousand Chinese laborers (中國 노동자) landed at Incheon Port (仁川港) on March 13th. These thousands, tens of thousands of Chinese laborers coming year after year (年復年)! Can small Joseon accommodate them? It's appalling (기막키는 일이다). Joseon laborers are driven out to foreign lands, while foreign laborers come in—there can be nothing more appalling. For every thousand who enter, another thousand must leave, right? Oh, masses of Joseon laborers (朝鮮의 노동자群)! Is there a countermeasure or not? Are you spineless worms (無骨虫) who endure this unknowingly and meekly (甘受)?

Japanese authorities (日本當局者) reportedly hesitate (躊躇) to apply Article 73 of the Criminal Code (stipulating the death penalty for those who inflict or attempt to inflict harm upon the Emperor (天皇), Empress (皇后), Empress Dowager (太后), Imperial Prince (皇子), or Imperial Grandson (皇孫)) to the high traitors (大逆罪人) Pak Yŏl (朴烈) and his wife. The reason given is the "huge impact it would have on the rule of Joseon" (朝鮮統治에 막대한 影響). It seems plausible. For a ruler (爲政者), Joseon is indeed important. Hence, the 'special favor' (特典) of life imprisonment (無期).<83>

**Summary**

This "Things Heard and Seen" column from the April 1926 issue of *Kaebyŏk* presents a series of critical and often sarcastic observations on contemporary events in colonial Korea and beyond. Topics range from the perceived subservience and maneuvering of Korean literary figures under Japanese influence, social issues like poverty leading to suicide and crime, frustrations with colonial administration (rising fares, unfair school admissions), legal injustices (a paltry fine for a Japanese policeman killing a Korean girl), the influx of Chinese labor displacing Korean workers, and the political considerations behind the sentencing of Korean anarchist Pak Yŏl. The overall tone is nationalistic and critical of Japanese rule, capitalist exploitation, and social decay, while expressing sympathy for the plight of ordinary Koreans and laborers.

Historians might find this article interesting for its candid, albeit biased, snapshot of colonial society in 1926. It reveals popular discontent with specific colonial policies (fare hikes, education barriers), reflects anxieties about economic displacement (Chinese labor), highlights the perceived injustice within the colonial legal system, and showcases the critical perspective of a nationalist-leaning publication like *Kaebyŏk* on collaborators, Japanese actions, and international affairs (like the mention of US banning trams or the Japanese communist village). The raw, often colloquial and emotional language provides insight into the sentiments circulating among certain segments of the Korean populace.

隨聞隨見
						大膽生
朝鮮總督府 囑托 李覺鍾군은 例의 親日할 번 宅 排日할 번 宅들을 모라다 놋코 當局의 殘飯이나 엇어먹으며 달마다 骨董品 하나식을 내노으며 그래도 장한 듯이 억개를 들먹거리며 朝鮮의 雜誌은 자기가 통일한다고 주책대드라나. 하긴 어대다 등을 댓다고 兼하야 「朝鮮文壇」까지 마저 드린 판이겟다. 며누리 이 格인지 사위 格인지?

「朝鮮文壇」 말이 낫스니 말이지 한참동안 李光洙 主宰니 方仁根 濁擔이니 하고 떠들든 朝鮮文壇은 웬일인지 서너 달 소식이 업기에 「그나마 죽엿는가?」 하고 몹시 궁금하더니 陽春은 回生之節이라 그런지 3월로써 其 影子가 다시 나타낫기에 「오! 죽지는 안엇구나!」 하고 떠드러 보닛가 發行所가 茶屋町八九番地 「新民」府겟지. 方君은 「營業상 편리로 茶屋町八九番地로 營業所를 옴겻다」고 침도 안 바르고 넌즈시 말햇다든가. 눈 가리우고 「아웅」도 밤 즁에나 할 짓이지. 귀신 다 된 이 판국에 그저 솔직이 제일이야-.

日本 長野縣 安源寺에는 勞働禮讚의 理想的 共産村이 생겻다지. 17戶 農家가 誼조케 모야부터 共同力作 共同分食으로 오손도손 잘도 산다지. 우리가 아는 이 共産村을 日本 當局者는 모르는 셈이야 안다치면 고- 신경질에 그대로 둘 수는 업는데 모르지. 그 사이에 또 國交斷絶이나 안햇는지?
