Article Title: 鳳姬 (Ponghŭi)
Author: 崔承一 (Ch'oe Sŭngil)
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

鳳姬
						崔承一
This year, spring has come early too. The year before last, when I met him in the front yard of Sŏdaemun Prison, and around this time last year when I went to Yongjŏng (龍井) and met him, there was still snow left on the distant mountains, and the wind was terribly cold. But this year, for some reason, the weather is already so warm. The warmth of the day might make a corner of the heart sentimental, but what is this again? His heart is cold. Then and now, the world hasn't changed at all; my body has grown thin as if my blood is being drawn out, and my spirit has withered. The year before last was like last year, and this year is like last year; any chance for us to stretch our legs has long since moved far away from us.
As this year arrived again, as this spring came, she who called me 'Oppa' and followed me – who called me 'Teacher, Teacher' and followed me, who asked me about everything, who wanted and thought to do everything as I told her – I rejoice that 'Now, Korea has one complete woman.' – A comrade (同志). A comrade! Among the many comrades walking the path to move today's Korea – to pull this dark land towards the sunlight – one woman walks. She is the Ponghŭi (鳳姬) whom I loved and looked after.
Kim Ponghŭi – that is the woman's name. As spring comes, I think back on Ponghŭi's affairs again. Ponghŭi is now in a place called Soju (蘇州) in southern China. But that is all I know. What she is doing now, and what path she is treading now, is something I cannot imagine. I don't even try to think about it. But memory is a frightening thing. "Let's live forgetting the past. Why? Because thinking about the past, in some cases, becomes a great obstacle to moving forward towards the future."<1> But what can be done? The memories that arise, the past reflected like a mirror in the heart, I cannot forget this. And so, as spring softly approaches – sitting in the dark corner under the dilapidated window, even though it's falling apart – for her sake, and thus, looking towards the dawn (黎明), with a heart hoping she moves forward sincerely in every way – towards the comrades – with these thoughts, I took up this brush.
I think of Ponghŭi. I think of Ponghŭi, the rebel (叛逆者) of the past.

It was spring three years ago.
At that time, I had deliberately come up to Seoul from my hometown, S-gun. It was for no other reason than that my comrade K-gun had been imprisoned in Sŏdaemun Prison, and I came up to visit him and also to look after his affairs. (Regarding his case, since it has no relation to the story I am writing now, I will omit it here.)
So, on a warm spring day, I went to the courthouse for the first time to visit K-gun, got permission from the preliminary judge (豫審判事), and headed towards Sŏdaemun Prison. Upon reaching the prison gate, which seemed detached from the world under the corner of the back hill of Akpak-kol, surrounded by a red brick wall over two *kil* high, emitting a desolate and tranquil feeling, I knocked on the small gate next to the main entrance. A guard's face peered out from the barred glass window beside it and opened the gate. As I entered, the gate closed behind me again. For some reason, I felt a bit unpleasant. And so, I said I came for a visit and presented the document; the guard told me to go over there and wait. Hearing his words, I dragged my strangely heavy legs and walked towards that direction. My experience with prisons was limited to visiting the C-hang prison when I went from S-gun to C-hang, and this Sŏdaemun Prison was my first time, so I took a general look, at least at the exterior. The prison yard, free of even a speck of dust – the sight of the scorching sun boiling down evoked a kind of strange beauty.<2> Because the back mountain was clear, the front was wide open, and a clean atmosphere circulated everywhere, although it was a prison, I couldn't find any particularly gloomy (陰鬱) feeling (though I didn't know about the system or life inside). Only when I looked at the red bricks everywhere with iron bars attached, my brow furrowed, and rather than questioning whether such a place must exist where people live, I felt an excited emotion, unable to suppress a kind of anger (憤怒). I went to a wooden bench placed under the brick wall on one side and sat there for quite a while. Leaning my back against the brick wall, head bowed, I just thought of my comrade's face (面影), thinking of the joy of meeting him and the resentful feelings that would arise. I sat there, absently staring at the sandy ground of the prison yard. However, hearing people chattering nearby, I naturally, without any conscious thought, simply lifted my head instinctively. Indeed. Whether they were people who were there before I arrived or came after, I don't know, but about five or six people were around me, some pacing, some sitting, mostly talking about visitation methods, prison rules, and besides that, who got in how and why. But what caught my eye again was her, over there on the sandy ground, just plopped down, playing with the sand, glancing sideways at the woman sitting quietly beside her (who seemed to be her companion), and though I couldn't hear clearly what she was saying, it was clear that she too had come to visit someone imprisoned (在監).
The one plopped down on the sand – she was wearing a black silk skirt and a black silk jacket. When she lifted her head, I happened to glance over and saw that her complexion was somewhat dark, her features large, and though she was sitting, she was quite tall. And her hair was rather yellowish than black.
At some point,
"My daddy must be coming out too. Hey, my daddy must be coming out too."
Her words reached my ears.
"So, her father is imprisoned."<3>
I realized this intuitively (直覺的), and after that, I was calm again.
On the curved fortress wall (曲城) of Inwangsan Mountain, visible straight ahead, the hem of a white *turumagi* coat flutters. It flutters under the blue sky – right below it, it seems – on the high old fortress site (城趾). From somewhere, the sound of a whistle faintly drifts over the prison's brick wall. I sat still, just as before, closed my eyes, and inwardly compared my friend inside here with the person strolling on that mountaintop, fluttering their clothes, and I don't know how stifled I felt. Perhaps it was just because I have relatively more movement of reason (理智) and less temperamental feeling, but if I were a temperamental person, how would it have been? I'll stop talking about that here.
From the corner of the brick building over there, prisoners (罪囚) carrying baskets and hoes, bound between iron chains, pass by following a single guard. Perhaps some among them turn their heads, look this way, glancing back as if envious of something, and pass by. Red clothes – the color of the earth, the color of their clothes, the color of their faces – it's hard to distinguish, the colors blend together. "Pigs. They're pigs. Are these humans? Pigs! Pigs!" Following that, I cried out inwardly, "Am I any better than them? It's the same. I can't find life within myself either." This declaration of emotion that arose! Going through moment by moment – like the clanking sound of the iron chains the prisoners wear as they limp along, such fragmented emotions continue. It was clear that my mind was so disordered that I couldn't even distinguish whether what I saw before my eyes was the prison yard or a vast open field.
"Who is Mr. Yi Chŏk (李赤)?"
It sounded faint, as if I heard it, but then the clatter of a sword hilt sounded right near my ear.
"Yes, that's me."
Startled as if waking from sleep, I lifted my head. Before me stood a man, presumably a Korean guard, with a clean-shaven chin where stubble had grown back dark, twitching his jaw.
"Why didn't you answer after being called several times?"<4>
"Ah, I didn't hear you."
I stood up abruptly. The guard, who seemed to be Korean, looked quite displeased.
"Visitation."
His voice softened slightly, as if fulfilling his duty nonetheless. And he walked ahead. I followed behind. Silently.

When I came out after finishing the visit, the people who were there when I entered were all still standing in the yard, without a single one missing. And, whether they knew me from before or not, they greeted me with what could be called a very pleased expression, or perhaps an expression of gratitude that things went well. In any case, they greeted me with a facial expression somewhat different from usual. I also smiled faintly. And walked forward. The guard who had supervised the visit glanced back repeatedly and disappeared towards the central office building over there. But here, something strange happened.
"Ah, what did that teacher say his name was?"
This sound reached my ears. He turned his head sideways. It was definitely her. It was clearly the voice of the woman who had said earlier that her father would also be coming out to the visitation area soon and waiting. So I stopped short. I ended up standing sideways right next to him.
"Yes. I am Yi Chŏk. Who are you?" I asked clearly.
I probably would have just kept going, but when I followed the guard in for the visit earlier, they had almost sent me off with welcoming faces, and now they were deliberately asking, referencing my name; I realized it was not an ordinary matter. (Let me add a word here to clarify: my emotion at that time was not governed by the kind of feeling a man who had never encountered any member of the opposite sex would have, becoming utterly bewildered (困惑) by such a suspicious attitude from a woman, nor was it that some unknown secret of the heart seemed to stir.) Thus,<5> it was clear that either she knew me from before, or she had something to ask me right then and there. Furthermore, her attitude was, to whatever extent, lively and frank (赤裸裸) – I won't say it twice.
"Yes. I am Kim Ponghŭi. You are in S-gun, aren't you?"
Look – she already knows even my hometown.
"Yes, that's right. How do you know?"
I asked him again.
"Yes, I know. I know you well, Teacher."
"Yes? Is that so?"
I smiled faintly. Since it was already known,
"Is your father imprisoned?"
I asked in the tone people usually use to inquire about each other in such places.
"Yes, he is."
"For what reason?"
"It's a long story."
"Ah-."
I could only hesitate like that for a long time.
"Where is your hometown?"
"It's S-gun."
"Ah, is that so?"
At this moment, my mind, which had been doubting and wondering what was going on, relaxed considerably.<6>
"We're from the same area. Where in S-gun?"
"C-myŏn."
"Ah, then it's only about thirty *ri* from where I am."
"Yes, that's right. I've heard much about you, Teacher, from home since long ago. Whenever I passed through the town (*ŭp*), I always thought I should visit you once, but it always ended up like this. I feel rather sorry that I've heard so much about how much you fight, yet I haven't met you even once until now."
She spoke fluently like this and laughed cheerfully.
"Not at all, please don't mention it."
I too, perhaps passively for the first time in a long while, laughed heartily.
"Who is the person imprisoned?"
"It's KXX."
"Ah, Mr. K? The one involved in the S-gun XX incident?"
"Yes, that's right."
"Then did he appeal (控訴)?"
"Yes, it turned out that way."
At this moment, "Ms. Kim Ponghŭi! Ms. Kim Ponghŭi!" the guard's calling voice was heard. As she ran off, she said,
"Please wait just a moment. It won't be more than five minutes. Let's go together."
She threw these words out and followed the guard inside.
Since things had come to this point, I couldn't just leave, so I paced back and forth in the yard, thinking various thoughts.<7>

"So?"
"So, there was a firm mutual agreement with the border guards that if they did XX, they wouldn't be arrested and taken away, and that's why he came in. But due to a report from an informant (密偵) elsewhere, he was caught and is now suffering like this."
In a lower room of a thatched house at the dead end of a corner in Songwŏl-dong (松月洞), she and I sat facing each other, listening to this story of the past. As the warm sunlight gradually crept in through the window, the two of us sat facing each other, spending who knows how many hours with this worry and that concern.
If I were to recount the story in dialogue form (對話體), it would be too long, so I will just write down the gist (槪要) of what I heard, as follows.

Her father was originally the leader (團長) of the XX Association based in XX Prefecture, Manchuria. And so, when the XX movement arose, he began to be active here and there. Consequently, the local residents greeted and sent him off with feelings of reverence (敬慕) and also fear, and the local garrison constantly pursued him. However, being extremely familiar with the area and adept in his activities, he spent four or five years like this, here today, there tomorrow. But a couple of years ago, an incident occurred that made it absolutely necessary for him to briefly visit S-gun. So he deliberately intended to come in after making such an agreement with the police at the border – whatever the outcome might be later – but he was suddenly arrested in XX Prefecture, Manchuria.
Therefore, he received the death penalty (死刑) at the P'yŏngyang District Court and appealed to Seoul. However, let's leave the story at roughly this point and talk about her – Ponghŭi – as that is the main purpose. Ponghŭi, upon hearing that her father had met such a fate, could not contain her anger. And so, she quit the school she was attending and went alone to the military headquarters (軍司令部) of N-gun. She went and requested an audience with the military commander but was refused. However, as a result of pressing her chest against the bayonet tip of the sentry soldier and throwing a fit, the commander<8> finally allowed her to enter. Once the meeting began, she immediately denounced his act of betrayal (背信). However, he explained that it wasn't the responsibility of that place but something that happened elsewhere, so there was nothing he could do. But even though the facts might be so, she absolutely refused to believe his words and just pleaded for him to save her father, grabbing the commander's sleeve and making a scene. Although it feels somewhat like something out of an old novel, since this was truly the fact regarding Ponghŭi, I hope the reader understands that much.
So, perhaps the commander had some intention, or perhaps he felt some flimsy responsibility, but he came up to Seoul with Ponghŭi and visited the courthouse once or twice, it is said.
"Perhaps thanks to him, the death sentence might be avoided."
What I wrote is short, but we talked about this long story until the sun had completely set. And so, she had come up now to await her father's verdict and to handle all the procedures for provisions herself, deliberately finding lodging near the prison, and she said she couldn't bear not seeing her father at least once every day.
"Didn't you have an older brother?"
"I did. But he died."
"How? When? Where?"
"In Manchuria."
Beyond this, I didn't ask further; only my chest felt tight, and consequently, my brow furrowed, and as if my head was struck by a lead weight, it felt heavy, pressing down, and my body trembled convulsively (痙攣的). However, passing that moment, the image of some martyr (殉敎者) appeared. I forgot myself again and saw a dazzling light.
"Where are you staying now?"
"I'm temporarily in K-dong. At XX address."<9>
"Is it alright if I visit?"
"Yes, please come visit."
Please guide me in everything, Teacher. I am still shallow in all things. Please teach me a lot. Please visit me here too."
"What do I know, really?"
I replied out of habit. However, she didn't seem to hear a single word I said carelessly. The way she sat straight up, clasping her oily, darkish hands together, or how her large, gentle eyes sometimes smiled, sometimes cursed – complicatedly revealing her personality, while at the same time, somewhere, one could see that once she trusted (信賴) a person or thing, her will would likely not change easily – possessing a tenacity perhaps even stronger than the character of some intense man.
"Teacher. I want to try living in Seoul. For some reason, being in the countryside feels like falling behind the times, and also, my father's life hangs in the balance, today or tomorrow. Consequently, my family consists only of my mother, and not only is there no foundation for livelihood, but also, at this time when my situation has reached a critical point, what's the use of studying – attending the higher course at the XX academy in the countryside? It would be much better for my heart to enter some factory in Seoul instead."
I don't know what motive (動機) led her to trust (信賴) a person like me, but seeing her open her heart completely and discuss even the changes in her circumstances, although I had never had such an experience before, seeing her talk about her past and also discussing what would be good for her to do in the future, I keenly realized that I couldn't just respond with irresponsible, passing words.
"What will you do for a living? Of course, it's natural to think that much. In today's reality, what's particularly remarkable about doing so-called school studies? Today's Korean culture (文化)<10> is the record of the blood of us who are oppressed by others. Ultimately, it only serves as a premise (前提) for preparing to be a slave (奴隷) to some person."
"That's right. Even in my meager thoughts, I've felt that strongly. Livelihood? As I mentioned earlier, I plan to go to a factory (工場)."
"Yes. Of course, the ideal is admirable. But this reality stands in a position diametrically opposed (正反對) to the society we envision. Suppose you go to a factory. Of course, it would be very good to experience the actual situation or to enter among the masses on the streets (街頭). But ultimately, you'll end up doing nothing but slave labor there. Also, do the women like you there know who you are – and if you say something, even if it's the truth (眞理), do they know how to understand it? But this, of course, might be limited to a certain period (時期) – therefore, while I agree in this sense with not receiving the former reality's education, I also think going to a factory is something to consider further. To go to such places, we need more training and cultivation (敎養). Thus, we must grasp some complete idea capable of enlightening them. Here, you must understand my words clearly. I am not denying (否定) your going to the factory. We need to consider how much strength we will need until something new is discovered in this reality that has carried history for perhaps tens of thousands of years. Of course, even I, speaking here right now, am certainly not saying this out of emotion or mere sentiment."
To this, Ponghŭi had no reply. She was silent for a while. But then, suddenly, she exclaimed impulsively,
"Oh my. What if Father passes away? Father mustn't pass away."
It seemed as if she deliberately wanted to change the subject – but her bright, clear eyes became hazy, like pearls submerged in water, tears welling up, and the expression appearing on her face was as if she had received endless stimulation (刺戟). Whether this stimulation was due to some feeling she got from my words, which she then redirected towards thoughts of her father, I couldn't tell,<11> but in any case, she seemed to be flowing with some unspeakable emotion.
"Of course, you must be thinking about your father too. But he has already become merely one incompetent (無能) person. The only thing left for him, perhaps, is that of the past. That's right. Your father has already become a person of the past. What's the use of holding onto only the memory of your father's past? Of course, I'm not saying you are holding onto only that. It's just the thought that it would be better not to do so in the future either. After your father, aren't you the only one left?"
Again, no words. It was only apparent to my eyes that she was solely immersed in emotion. – Silence again.
"Then how should I stay in Seoul?"
I thought quietly. Having advised her to abandon any dreamlike (夢幻的), sentiment-filled thoughts and be more faithful to reason (理智), I considered various things together in my head. Then, like lightning, the Women's XX Association came to mind.
"This seems like it would be good. Why don't you join the Women's XX Association? Then you could get some convenience, and consequently, if you are active, the 'bread' (팡 - probably 'pang' meaning bread/livelihood) problem shouldn't be too difficult either."
She looked extremely pleased.
"Oh, I forgot. I heard rumors about the Women's XX Association even in the countryside. Can anyone join there?"
"If there's a solid introduction, it should be fine. If you decide to join, I'll introduce you. As for the president of that association, she has deep ties not only with me but also with our S-gun Youth Association."
"Then please do that for me."
"Let's do that."<12>

Respected Teacher,
It has already been over a month since I parted with you at the station, Teacher, and consequently, I haven't been able to send you a proper letter since then. Please forgive me generously for everything. Although that's the case, I occasionally see news of your well-being or your struggles, Teacher, in the newspapers. Also, when I see clearly how much the movement in our hometown S-gun has progressed, I cannot express how glad I am.
Teacher!
Previously, I only admired people like my father. I also thought that was the path we should naturally take. But Father only thought of our nation. Of course, that too is one path we should naturally take in our current situation. However, thanks to the advice and teachings I received from you, Teacher, when I was in Seoul, reading books, actually experiencing things myself, and living through them, I realized that the world today is divided into two classes (階級), and at the same time, I discovered that today's Korea is in a situation particularly different from others, even more so. That's probably why they say the city is global. During that time, I received much help from Sister C of the XX Association, and consequently, I learned many things. Not only that, but when I thought deeply, to the bone, about our situation, which is at the peak of a transitional period (過渡期), I disregarded my own background and future, running east and flying west. But reality is not something to be viewed as carelessly as we think. I don't know how frightening, solid, and dirty it is. Sometimes I feel disillusionment (幻滅), and other times, due to the reactionary force, an even stronger power surges up.
Ah, but Teacher.
Why is a woman's strength so weak? I lament being born a woman. Are all women in Korea like me? "This is<13> me talking nonsense. Of course, everyone must be like me." Our female environment, struggling under the weight of the dominant power, subordinated to the male environment that is moved by it! It is truly sorrowful. Is that all? Of course, since this is a male-centered reality, it might be so, but unless we rely on a capitalist (資本家) called 'man,' our livelihood is zero. But then, what about our lives? Socially, those men who have, "one way or another," so-called activity and foundation – even they lack livelihood, so what more is there to say about us women? The female comrades in the XX Association are beyond words. Two sewing machines, a few magazines. This is the sole production facility that sustains the lives of the twenty people within it.
Teacher Yi! Teacher Yi!
One day, while going around selling magazines, I was overcome with sorrow, returned to the association hall, and cried all night long. This happened not just once or twice. But afterwards, the fearsome whip of livelihood mercilessly strikes my back. Just as a landowner (農主) strikes the red back of a serf (農奴) with a leather whip, it strikes mercilessly. But as long as life clings on, what can be done? I go forward again. I'll stop this long, rambling talk, but I tried my best to proceed sincerely. I tried to live like a human being. Also, with my life dedicated to two liberations (解放)! – against the current economic system and within the sphere of male power (權力圈內) – I intended to fight until my bones broke. Of course, male power also exists because they possess the economic means – I am still fighting even now. However, lately, a big change has occurred in me. Perhaps it's a compromise? I have become a governess in a certain bourgeois home. Through a friend's introduction, I am living a splendid life I never experienced before. The house I live in is a magnificent mansion. It feels like I alone will enjoy wealth and glory. Should I laugh once?
But my heart is endlessly troubled. While seeing myself as a bundle of falsehoods, my life has indeed improved compared to before, but my courage seems to stagnate, and consequently, I often think of death. This must be a terrible thought.<14> I will overcome it. Surely – I will overcome it.
Oh, I heard that a Women's Youth Association was newly established in S-gun. The news that a trench (塹壕) is being dug ahead of others fills me with indescribable emotion. I know it's all because you, Teacher, are firmly established there. Also, when I first heard the news, I wanted to rush down immediately and fight alongside, but firstly, I have no travel funds right now, and secondly, whether it works out or not, I am now bound to someone else, so what can I do? I am just angry and frustrated.
My developed spirit is bound in falsehood. Chased by livelihood. Why don't we have that thing called livelihood? Is this even human?
I heard a rumor that you will be coming up soon. When you come up, please visit me once for sure. Of course, you will come, won't you? I will wait for that time, just digging deeper into this dark earth.
Finally, I pray for the struggle of all the comrades in the XX Women's Youth Association there – and also pray for your valiant fight, Teacher, and end this letter here.

Ponghŭi is
To Teacher Yi
This is a letter I received sometime when I was in S-gun. After reading through the contents, I watched his life unfold like a motion picture, and I could also guess to what extent his ideology had reached. However. It probably turned out that way naturally. And while it wasn't necessarily a bad thing, I couldn't help but feel a somewhat unpleasant emotion. And I felt a little disappointed. Perhaps because of that, or perhaps because I was very busy at the time, even though I received such a long letter, I merely sent back a postcard with a few simple words saying I received the letter and wishing him well.<15>
Two months after that, in late summer, I had business in Seoul again and came up. During that time, letters had gone back and forth, and not only that, but the relationship between him and me became even stranger for another reason: in one letter, there was a passage saying she wanted to call me 'Oppa'. At that time, I knew it wasn't necessarily something that leaned towards ethical (倫理的) matters among comrades like us, and I also wasn't unaware that it wouldn't necessarily make us closer, but since she wanted to address me that way, there was no particular need to refuse it, so I just left it as it was.
The day after I arrived in Seoul, I visited him in C-dong. It was, of course, the house where she was staying as a governess. The house was indeed splendid. The thought that struck my head instantly was "Invasion rather than compromise" – that's how I thought. "Then it might be better" – that's how I thought.
"Oppa!"
Running out, she grabs my hand with all her might. And pulls me into her room. Her room was one room in the back, past the main hall (大廳) of the house. It was a very fine place. Firstly, it was clean, also quiet, and next, it was quiet enough to be almost secluded. According to her, the only work she did was to help the two children of the house review their studies for about two hours after they came back from school and had dinner. And she herself also attended the XX Academy during the day –.
"Who was the man who guided me in with you?"
As I sat down, I briefly exchanged news about hometown and recent events, then changed my tone slightly and asked this.
"Him? He's supposedly the nephew of the master of this house."
For some reason, she seems to be deliberately trying to speak formally, as if talking about a stranger.
"He seemed like a very kind man."<16>
What is this? Her face flushes slightly, and she says nothing. She, who usually earned the nickname 'tomboy' but, if it was about a friend she truly loved, was a person with such strong passion that she would cut off her own flesh to feed them without regard for consequences, she who had received many marriage proposals from various friends but rejected them all, saying – 'to exploit me too' – 'to make me a slave' – she who used to cry out like this, rejecting them, has now completely become a different person – this is faintly felt. Perhaps her current environment made her appear that way to me –.
"What school does he attend?"
Shamelessly, for some reason, that man keeps weighing on my mind, and I keep wanting to ask.
"He attends the medical college, they say."
"Oh –."
I let out a long, awkward reply.
"Why do you keep asking about that?"
"Hey. You – aren't you in love with that man?"
This thought suddenly surged up, and while others might not know, it was my nature not to be able to endure it. These words seemed to cause a huge ripple in the air of this room.
"No! Oppa, you too!"
As if it were truly a sudden, unexpected remark she hadn't dreamt of, she lunges forward and hits my knee.
"Be careful!"
Heavily, I just said this one word.
Ponghŭi's head bows down again. However, she seemed to continuously deny something, which was visible before my eyes.<17>
Changing the tone again –
"You used to say you wanted to work in a factory. How are you now? Haven't you changed?"
It was true that my words came out rather mournfully.
"No. Oppa, somehow you've become scary. Why do you say such things? Do I look like that! If so, I'll leave this house right now."
"Perhaps I was a bit short-tempered (短氣). But you must remain steadfast, no matter what."
"Yes."
It's a convulsive reply.
And so, that day too, I stayed late playing and then came back. That child, saying I liked *injeolmi* rice cakes, went in and out of the inner room bringing them, or sometimes sat, laughed, and played. When I was about to leave, coming out of the house, I saw him again – the man – standing in the front yard watering flowers. He bowed politely to me.
Outside the main gate, I held Ponghŭi's hand and said,
"I don't know if I'll come again. The work back home is busy, so I have to go down soon. You must overcome this life, no matter what."
"Yes."
Again, the same powerless reply as before. For some reason, my heart felt heavy.

A year later. Spring in Manchuria was bitterly cold. I had business that took me as far as Yongjŏng (龍井). Around that time, the cherry blossoms at Ch'anggyŏngwŏn Garden must have been blooming inland (內地), but it was so cold there that the ice hadn't even begun to melt yet. Then, as now, I was busy rushing around here and there, and although it wasn't that the thought of Ponghŭi<18> never occurred to me, by that time it had been so long that I couldn't even clearly remember her address. Even if I knew her most recent address, I would forget it, and sometimes the thought of sending her a letter would arise, only to be abandoned because I forgot the address – this happened once or twice. If you call it indifference, it was indifference.
However, one day when I went to the XX Association there and returned to the inn, a letter from Ponghŭi unexpectedly arrived. I was partly glad, partly surprised, and consequently, also found it strange. "How did Ponghŭi know I came here, and consequently, how did she know my address?" Wondering this, I opened the letter.
"Oppa!"
Time flies, doesn't it? It's already been over half a year since I last wrote to you, Oppa. During this long half-year, you probably didn't know my news, Oppa, but I have been hearing news of you. Truly, no matter how I've ended up in this state, I never forget Oppa, and I truly try to treat only Oppa consciously, even if with a shallow consciousness. Truly, we were like that, weren't we?
Yes, Oppa!
My hand trembles as I write this letter now. But when I feel like I'm facing Oppa, or when I write a letter to Oppa, my heart always feels renewed. It's as if, while being in some dark cave, I suddenly see the sun; feeling that, my mind becomes ecstatic. Truly, just feeling Oppa's presence is like visiting that place I always long for.
Ah Oppa! Oppa!
I want to cry now. What is this? My body, which Oppa used to call a clumsy tomboy, a bundle of firewood, is now all decayed. Why is it so painful? The world is truly hell. Humanity – until its kind becomes extinct, a true human being will not be found among them. I have felt disillusionment with all human movements. I have despaired of humanity at large. I have lost. Fighting against this world, although my life is alive, it's like a broken,<19> dead life.
Thinking about it, it's laughable. Rebelling against some place, getting tired and falling down, and consequently being deceived by people – well, what can I do? I wasn't such an easy woman, but being deceived by the person I trusted most – wouldn't this be despair?
But Oppa. Don't scold me. Even in this utterly decayed, violated heart of mine, there remains an ember of a faintly rising flame. It's the thought of trying to live a bit more faithfully – that remains.
Ah – my beloved Oppa!
Please save me. More than anyone else in this world, only you exist. This place is a village about seventy *ri* from Yongjŏng. I am staying here at my aunt's house. Please come visit just once before you leave. I have something to discuss with you. – And Oppa.
I want to go to Yongjŏng with Oppa and become a nurse (看護婦) at the hospital. What do you think? What I need until death is just livelihood. And I must take revenge for being deceived by someone. Oppa! Please come. I will be waiting.
From XX Village
Ponghŭi is
After reading the entire letter, I was frightened. But the exclamation, "Is this girl crazy? Why did she become like this?" didn't leave my lips for several minutes. Looking closely at the letter, it was clear she had hidden something she should have said frankly. But like lightning flashing, a thought struck my mind, and my body trembled. Inwardly, with a heavy heart – medical college student, love affair, deception, falsehood, pregnancy, hospital – began to appear in fragments, like titles appearing on a screen. My prediction will not be wrong. I was certain of this.<20>
However, I couldn't go see her. My work was exceptionally busy. So I sent a letter. A letter asking her to come to Yongjŏng. But for some reason, she didn't come. And so, we ultimately couldn't meet, and I just returned to S-gun. I felt regretful.

The following spring, I again had business in Seoul and came up. At that time, I heard a rumor from some friend that Ponghŭi was attending some actor training school (俳優養成所). When I heard this news, I wondered if it was true or not. But anyway, if I met her once, I could hear the detailed circumstances, and indeed, even if she had entered an actor training school, I absolutely didn't want to abandon that woman. One day, finding time, I visited the place. The actor training school occupied the entire two-story Japanese-style house outside Dongdaemun Gate.
"Oppa!"
Ponghŭi, greeting me! For some reason, although my heart should be glad, it felt heavy, and she too greeted me with a strange air.
First of all, her lifestyle is different. The whole situation has flipped. Upstairs, on the tatami mats, a group of men stomp around noisily, singing popular songs like "Stomp, stomp." And downstairs, in the so-called reception room, it's dark, and a musty smell strangely hits my head, while Ponghŭi, visible before my eyes, is wearing powder. Applying rouge. Wearing a wristwatch on her arm. Her hair is curled with a curling iron, messy and frizzy; the Ponghŭi of old is nowhere to be found. Is that all? Although she put on makeup, her cheekbones protrude sharply, making her face look unrecognizably different, and her body, though she used to be plump, her healthy physique, looking closely, her waist seems no bigger than a handful. I just sat there quietly. "What happened? What happened?" Only this aimless question surged up from my chest. "You, what on earth happened?"<21> Finally, I asked this in a tearful voice.
"What – what happened? I have fallen. I fell because of Oppa."
Like a final act of defiance, she thrusts her head sharply towards me and lashes out fiercely.
"Hey Ponghŭi. What are you saying? It was my mistake not being able to visit you in Yongjŏng, but what happened after that? And this life of yours, what is this?"
"What is what? I'm an actress. An actress. I'm a splendid actress. I can become a splendid actress, right? Oppa!"
Even hearing her call me Oppa felt uncomfortable for some reason, but I just kept my head down quietly.
"Nonsense. How could I fall because of Oppa? That's just forcing it."
After saying this somewhat mockingly, she goes out. A little later, udon noodles are brought in. She says she bought snacks. Nothing felt right to me. My chest just felt heavy. And I felt an emotion like I had lost something.
"Hey. Tell me the story, at least. My heart is heavy."
Putting the udon and snacks in front of me, Ponghŭi sat opposite me, and I looked across at her and asked this.
"What's there to talk about? What's the use of talking? It's all in the past – Oh, is Brother C from the XX Association well?"
"Yeah."
I gave a listless reply and looked across at her; tears seemed to be welling up in her eyes.
Since things have come to this, what's the use of me hearing the story, and why ask to hear it! I stood up abruptly.<22>
"Why are you getting up? Aren't you disappointed? Since you came all this way, stay and play longer."
I shook off her hand as she tried to hold me back, slammed the entrance door shut, and came back. But even then, seeing that she said nothing about talking if I stayed, nor about visiting me sometime, thinking back now, it seems my visit at that time was rather unwelcome to her.
Her will, like steel! Her faith, like a heavy stone (重石)! Her rebellious strength, hating to be oppressed by others, hating to lose to others! Where has it disappeared now? Thinking about it, that too is thanks to this – meticulous reality. Reality, like ruthless iron wire, like a fearsome boulder, crushed her, who resisted, refused to yield, refused to submit. It snatched away her life. It deceived her. Reality, environment – thinking of the guillotine (斷頭臺), the land of her own father, the reality she stood upon, she appeared on the streets of Seoul for a year or two to fight against it, throwing the arrows of fire rising in her heart at the world, wandering around; where is her life groaning now? Where is her living corpse, robbed of life, moving now? In Soju, southern China, they say. Do you know or not know that the sun shines in the sky of your homeland? Indeed, I hope that your existence, Ponghŭi's life, can move again so that it can be found again on this land. Reality knocked you down once. But you still remain. Reality hopes that you will now return, sweeping the dust before you (捲土重來).
Thinking of Ponghŭi, whom I met outside Dongdaemun Gate around this time last year, while lying curled up under the dim window of my house at this time this year, I cried out like this to myself. Consequently, perhaps passively influenced by her, due to some unseen, faint elasticity, in an instant, my head felt like it hit a high, high castle wall (城壁) with a thud. I shot upright.

**Summary**

The story recounts the narrator Yi Chŏk's relationship with Kim Ponghŭi, a young woman he initially admires as an ideal "complete woman" for Korea and a fellow "comrade." He first meets her at Sŏdaemun Prison visiting her imprisoned nationalist father. Full of patriotic fervor derived from her father's activities, Ponghŭi moves to Seoul seeking purpose and livelihood. Yi Chŏk guides her towards activism, but she struggles intensely with poverty and the limitations placed on women, working briefly for a women's association before taking a compromising job as a governess in a bourgeois household. Over time, Yi Chŏk observes her growing disillusionment, a suspected romantic entanglement leading to betrayal and hardship (implied pregnancy and abandonment), and her eventual transformation into someone seemingly detached and "fallen," working at an actor training school. The narrator laments her lost potential and spirit, crushed by the harsh "reality" of colonial life, ending with a desperate hope for her symbolic return.

From a historical perspective, the story offers valuable insights into the lives of young, politically aware Koreans, especially women, during the 1920s under Japanese rule. It vividly portrays the intersection of nationalism, poverty, gender inequality, and urban disillusionment. Ponghŭi's trajectory from idealistic patriot inspired by her father's anti-Japanese activities to a struggling, compromised individual reflects the immense pressures and limited options available. Historians might find the depiction of Sŏdaemun Prison visits, the mention of nationalist activities in Manchuria, the struggles within activist groups like the Women's Association (economic hardship, reliance on male figures/structures), the class dynamics (bourgeois household vs. factory work), and the deeply personal toll of political engagement and societal constraints particularly revealing about the complexities of colonial Korean society. Ponghŭi's story serves as a microcosm of the dashed hopes and difficult compromises faced by many in her generation.
