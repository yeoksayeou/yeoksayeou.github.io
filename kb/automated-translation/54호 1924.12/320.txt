電車車掌의 日記 몃 節
						稻香
Nov. 15th, Cloudy (曇)
... Today’s work began by taking the first morning streetcar from Tongdaemun towards Sinyongsan.
As the streetcar was about to stop in front of the East Gate (Tonggu), the first two passengers boarded. They were both gentlemen dressed in Western suits. From my perspective, sharpened by several months as a streetcar conductor, I guessed they had caroused heartily at Myŏngwŏlgwan all night yesterday, gotten drunk, collapsed right there to sleep, and were just now emerging. It wasn't just that the early morning air was quite chilly; a cold wind mixed with a frosty air was blowing. Every time I grabbed the trolley pole strap (‘ch’urori’), a piercing coldness seemed to seep into my gloved hand, as if touching an icicle. Their faces were puffy (angp’aengi), and like people passing through some shameful place, their hats were pulled down over their eyes, their overcoats covered their noses, and their shoulders were hunched high on both sides. It wasn't completely light yet; dawn was breaking, and between the western and eastern skies, light and darkness were subtly intermingled. However, like a day threatening snow, grey clouds oppressively covered the sky above Puk’aksan mountain in the north. The motorman drove the streetcar at a speed exceeding regulations down the wide, empty street. The streetcar stopped at the T’apdong Kongwŏn stop. From afar, the sound of crowing roosters drifted on the frosty morning wind. Just then, I saw a woman place a lovely foot onto the step of the conductor’s platform where I was standing. Having prematurely signaled to start, assuming there would be no more passengers yet, I saw her and quickly signaled to stop again. One leg hopped up onto the step like a chick, and then a woman, huddled like an egg, popped up <138> and passed in front of me. Her hair, perhaps tousled somewhere, somehow, was pinned up haphazardly. Whether she had set out intending to be disheveled from the start, I couldn't tell, but she was plainly dressed in an ok’yangmok (cotton) jeogori and some kind of skirt, holding a silk purse in her hand. As she passed by me, the refreshing scent of face powder and perfume, like that which hits my nose when Japanese women or kisaeng board the car, mixed with the cold air and tickled my nose.

The woman went inside the car and, perhaps wanting to avoid the eyes of the young men in Western suits already seated, or maybe feigning modesty, sat right at the very front, facing forward. The two young men, perhaps still basking in the lingering excitement of cavorting with kisaeng the previous evening, nudged each other with their elbows and winked upon seeing the woman. The other man nodded as if acknowledging it, and kept looking only at her.

My own curiosity piqued, I decided to get closer and get a good look at her face. I swung the bag hanging behind me to the front, took the ticket punch and tickets in each hand, and entered the car. After punching the two young men's tickets first, I went to the woman and was about to hold out my hand when I startled. I was so surprised I almost rushed forward asking, “What is all this?” I looked her over again, from the gold hairpin (kŭmbinyŏ) in her hair down to the silk shoes on her feet.

Anyway, as I prepared to punch her ticket, she took out her purse. Inside were two or three bills, maybe one-won or five-won notes. She casually handed one over and said,
“Ŭijut’ong.”
She sat there calmly, as if she had forgotten me. After giving her a transfer ticket for Ŭijut’ong, I went back out to the conductor's platform. By then, the streetcar had already passed the Ch’ŏngnyŏn Hoegwan (YMCA building) and reached the Chongno stop. The woman got off there and walked away in that direction. As I leaned out the window to adjust the trolley pole (‘ch’urori’) for the streetcar heading back towards Namdaemun, the heavy darkness that had covered the sky gradually lifted into a hazy white. In the east, <139> a proper reddish hue played, and the twinkling stars, as if sifted through a sieve, left only the large ones, the smaller ones disappearing. A strange feeling inexplicably came over me, making it unbearable. Like someone trying to solve an unsolvable riddle, I just kept tilting my head. I dredged up a past memory, which follows.

A month ago, exactly one month ago, I was also driving the streetcar and stopped at the Paeugae stop. It was around 1 p.m., and the only passengers in the car were a man who looked like a detective from the Tongdaemun police station, two Japanese women, and someone who looked like a country person from Chosŏn. A woman boarded last.
In her hands, she held medicine bottles and packets. Her clothes were grimy with dirt and the hems were raggedly torn. Her face, perhaps unwashed for several days, was dark and grimy. Her feet were bare, clad only in straw shoes (chipsaegi). If she was nineteen, she seemed a bit mature; if twenty, there was a hint of youthfulness somewhere. Her eyelashes were long, her eyes lively and intelligent; her plump cheeks, roundish chin, and moderately shaped nose – neither sharp nor broad – made her not unattractive from any angle, but her wretched appearance was unsightly and left no good impression.
As is our usual routine, I said,
“Ticket, please (P’yo Tchikgŭsio),”
and held out my hand. She looked bewildered, glancing around nervously. As the streetcar started moving again, she didn’t know what to do. She looked up at the person next to her, then glanced outside, unable to either sit or stand properly, fidgeting. I realized she must have just come up from the countryside or had never ridden a streetcar before. We always find such people bothersome when they board. Why? Because they invariably fail to transfer where they should, miss their stop and then complain, bothering not just us conductors, but also often trying to cheat the 5-chŏn fare, acting as if they are the cleverest people in the world by producing some nonsensical transfer ticket obtained somewhere. So, whenever I encounter such people, I get inexplicably irritated, and my voice becomes harsh. <140>
“Where are you going? Your ticket! Ticket!”
She looked up at me and said,
“Yes? (Nye?)”
Dumbfounded, she paused.
“What do you mean, ‘Yes’? I said give me your ticket!”
She then held out a piece of paper she was holding. I took the paper scrap and looked at it. Written in pencil was: Meijichō Jinji Shōkaijo (明治町 人事紹介所 - Meijichō Employment Agency).
“What’s this?”
I snapped at her. She replied, pleading while asking,
“I’m going here. Where is this? Please let me off here.”
“I don’t know. Pay the fare! (Ton naeyo?)”
At the word ‘money,’ she seemed to understand something.
“I don’t have any (Ŏpseyo),”
she said, looking down at her own hands, then hung her head as if ashamed. Still, as if she had more to say, she continued,
“It’s like this. It’s been a month since I came up from the countryside. I have nothing to eat and nothing to wear, so I was working as a hired hand at a house in Tongmak. But I got sick, so I went to the hospital. They wrote this down for me and said if I go there, it would be alright, so that’s where I’m going.”
I understood everything. She had gone to the Government-General Hospital’s free clinic, and a doctor or someone at the hospital, pitying her situation, <141> had directed her to the employment agency. It was also true that she had just arrived in Seoul and boarded the car without money. In any case, at that moment, an inexplicable sympathy arose in my heart. Being soft-hearted, I couldn't bring myself to kick her off the streetcar. So, what should I do? Letting her ride was against the rules, yet I couldn't force her off. While pondering this, I stepped down onto the conductor's platform. When the streetcar reached Hwanggŭmjŏng (Koganechō), I went back to her, punched a transfer ticket, and gave it to her, saying,
“Why did you board the streetcar without any money?”
I reprimanded her sternly once to teach her the rules, then added,
“Here, take this. Get off at the next stop. I’m giving this to you as a special favor. If it’s discovered I gave you this ticket, I’ll lose my job, pay a fine, and that’s the rule. Just so you know.”
She nodded repeatedly, as if grateful.

The woman I met this morning was that very same woman. The woman who, a month ago, lacking even 5 chŏn, received a favor from me, was today impeccably dressed. I had always thought that being born a woman was an infinite treasure, and that thought seemed proven right. With just one turn of her heart, a woman could become the wife of an instant millionaire; with just one flirtatious glance (ch’up’a), she could receive the desperate love of many men.
A month isn't such a long time, yet the woman who, a month ago, spoke pleadingly at the Government-General's free clinic asking for treatment and received a favor even from a conductor like me, today looked just like a respectable lady from a different household. People like us endure all sorts of mistreatment and hardship, barely earning a few tens of won a month. If we truly want to succeed, we must spend our precious youth in grim hardship and suffering, and even then, success is uncertain.
All day long, while standing on the conductor’s platform or punching passengers' tickets, an emotion I couldn't articulate, one I hadn't felt in my <142> twenty-one years, seemed to permeate my entire body. My young blood still smells raw. That blood had not yet experienced scorching heat (灼熱); it hadn't been purified (純化) or refined (淨化). My blood hadn't been boiled down or filtered into an ‘extract’; it was still, so to speak, in its raw state. For some reason, after seeing that woman today, the blood in my chest started to burn from a corner, as if watching a heart laid out on a grill, sizzling, yet feeling as if some new life had fallen into the fire, intensifying the flames. But she had gone off towards Ŭijut’ong. Just because she went towards Ŭijut’ong doesn't mean she'd always be stuck there like glue, but whenever I drove the streetcar there, came back, or passed nearby, I thought of her and always looked in that direction.

Nov. 17th, Clear (晴)
Yesterday I had a day off, so today I was assigned the night shift (夜勤). Today, I started from Tongdaemun heading towards Ch’ŏngnyangni. It was around 8 p.m., and the weather had turned very cold. The wind also began to blow fiercely, driving dust from afar like fog. During summer, spring, or autumn, there probably wasn't a single pleasure-seeking man or woman in the capital who hadn't had their ticket punched by my hand, nor anyone who boarded the car without my assistance. But today, although it was a Sunday, the tree leaves had already withered and fallen silently. Gaunt trees stood starkly, seeming to pierce the sky, not long after flocks of jackdaws had returned to their nests. Only the voices of firewood sellers and cattle drivers from the countryside, shouting, “Giddy up (Ŏdwiŏ), you beastly cow!” could be heard. The entrance to T'apkol Sŭngbang, Yŏngdosa, or Ch’ŏngnyangsa temples seemed somehow more desolate than before.
Our car returned to Tongdaemun. As I swung the trolley pole around and boarded the car again to prepare for departure, I glanced inside and saw her – the woman I met that early morning a couple of days ago – sitting there. I was both glad <143> and surprised, staring at her blankly for a moment. The blood that had ceased burning in my chest flared up all at once. And inwardly, I thought,
“Hey, I run into her often!”
while realizing, for some reason, that a cold sweat was breaking out on my back.
After the streetcar started moving, as I went inside to collect tickets, the thought of receiving a ticket from her hand again inexplicably made my heart pound, and my whole body felt flushed. After punching a couple of people's tickets, I went to the woman and held out my hand. At that moment, I expected that when I habitually extended my hand, she would, as before, open her purse and take out money. Then, she would take the ticket punched by my hand and given by my hand, sit with it, and later give it to the motorman when getting off. However, the woman acted as if she didn't even see my outstretched hand. Instead, like an angry person, she sat with a sullen face, looking elsewhere.
“Ticket, please.”
I urged her attention, but she remained silent, then glanced at me sideways, looking somewhat arrogant. Then she craned her neck to look across at a man sitting a couple of seats away. That seated man, as if suddenly remembering something forgotten, showed a brief expression of surprise, then took out a wallet from his pocket.
“Here you go!”
he called to me, his dark eyes peering over gold-rimmed glasses.
‘What is this?’
The surprising thought struck me. Having no choice, I started towards the man, but I couldn't just turn away from the woman pretending not to notice her, like any other person. I looked her over once more, then went to the man – the one wearing gold-rimmed glasses, with a dark mustache just starting to grow, <144> the edges of his eyes looking bluish, and a prominent nose bridge, looking to be just over or under thirty, who at first glance resembled either a rice futures speculator (midusijang) or a swaggering gambler (ch’ŏllyangmallyangp’ae). He proudly took out a 1-won note from his wallet, bought a discount ticket book (hwarin sŭngch’agwŏn), and told me to punch three tickets. At the words "punch three," I realized that the young man sitting next to him, neatly dressed in a Western suit with a face like carved marble – a ‘Grecian type’ – was his companion.
After punching all the tickets and returning to the conductor's platform, I felt an unbearable sense of hatred, disgust, and jealousy towards the man who had paid for the tickets.
When the streetcar stopped at the entrance to Yŏngdosa, they got off there. Seeing this, a suspicion began to arise in me. The man who bought the tickets seemed, in my eyes, to have a somewhat vagrant nature (浮浪性), and the corners of his eyes and mouth looked extremely lewd. Seeing him take the woman into a secluded temple, a place relatively frequented by lewd women and profligate men (淫婦蕩子), made me feel that some indecent act was likely to occur there. Inexplicably, I hated the man and simultaneously felt pity for the woman being led away. I know the job of a streetcar conductor is not very prestigious. Being in a relatively lowly position, I meet people from all classes daily, and often see suspicious individuals like these. Whenever I see such suspicious men and women, I feel an urge to curse them and make them miserable, even if just for a moment. But regarding this woman, although her arrogant attitude towards me today, forgetting the small favor she received when she was penniless and in rags, felt detestable, for some reason, it seemed to gently unravel the emotions (情緖) in my heart. So, when I saw her off, my heart felt wistful again.

Dec. 15th, Clear (晴) <145>
Today the weather is warm.
Yesterday, however, I heard a strange rumor from my colleagues. They said that the woman, just like when I first met her early one morning at the Pagoda Park stop, still rides the early morning streetcar towards Ŭijut’ong every dawn. Based on the descriptions of her appearance and behavior from several people, which matched perfectly with the image etched in my memory, I could recognize it was her. Hearing this, a certain curiosity arose in me. Even though it wasn't my shift, I boarded the first morning streetcar driven by someone else. Then, when the streetcar stopped in front of Pagoda Park, I quickly looked outside. Hoping against hope that I wouldn't miss her, expecting a lucky encounter, I looked for her, and indeed, there she was, waiting for the streetcar. Not just her, but next to her stood a man, so close that his shoulder almost touched hers, engaged in what looked like an intimate conversation.
The woman boarding the streetcar was completely different from how she had dressed before. Previously, she wore her hair in a bun (jjok) and wore traditional shoes (sin); now, she had Western-style hair (yangmŏri) and wore Western shoes (kudu). And something I hadn't noticed before, looking at her now and thinking back, the previous country bumpkin air and awkwardness were all gone; instead, she looked worn down by something, as if smoothed over by handling (sonttae). Since it was cold, she wore a manto (cape) over her body. On her fingers, which fidgeted, clenching and unclenching, a gold ring, absent a month ago, glittered under the electric light, reflecting reddish sparks. She glanced up at me once, looking at me with familiar eyes, as if finding our repeated encounters strange.
Then the man also boarded the streetcar. This man was the same young man in the Western suit who had accompanied the other man to Yŏngdosa a month ago. When heading to Yŏngdosa, this young man had lagged behind, following somewhat separately, but today he seemed to possess the woman entirely, displaying the proud demeanor of a victor (勝者).
“I nearly died of boredom!” <146>
The woman sighed, speaking to the man in a voice that could be described as coquettish (ayang) or whining (ŭngsŏng).
“Why didn’t you come sooner? You kept me waiting past the time! You don’t know how I waited.”
The man remained silent, perhaps thinking it better not to talk inside the car where others might hear, offering no reply to her words. The woman, sensing this, closed her mouth, turned her head away as if frustrated, and stared out as if waiting only for the red light of the next stop.
When the car stopped at Chongno, the two stood up and got off. Having a plan today, I followed them off the car. I saw them heading towards the Chaep’anso (Courthouse) stop. Worried they might become suspicious of me, I followed them from a distance. I saw them take each other's wrists when they thought no one was around, and my whole body felt like a ball of fire; I felt ashamed.
They stopped in front of the courthouse. After exchanging some words, they turned into the narrow alley beside the courthouse. This time, deciding to follow closely, I tailed them tightly. Unaware I was following, they walked on, conversing intimately.
“Today, you know, I told him I wouldn’t see him again. He just laughed heartily, saying ‘Alright, alright,’ and agreed immediately.”
“Agreed to what?”
“About you and me… being like this.”
“He must have guessed.”
“But he doesn’t consider others’ feelings at all. If we’re to live together, he should arrange things properly, or else say it’s impossible and we should part ways cleanly. No decisive words, just dragging things on, always ‘today or tomorrow, today or tomorrow.’ How can I rely on someone like that? Every day, he drags me out in the middle of the night and sends me off <147> at dawn, but does he ever even see me off?”
The man was silent for a moment, then said,
“Let’s go to my place and rest a bit before you go…”
“What if it gets too late? Dawn is already breaking.”
“What’s the matter? What else would you do at home? Nothing much to do anyway…”
“Well, there isn’t much, but if I go too often, won’t the guests in the other rooms think it’s strange?”
“It’s fine. Who would know?”
While talking like this, they suddenly stopped in front of a certain inn (ryŏgwan). Reaching through a small opening (kaegunyŏk) in the inn door to undo the latch, the two vanished inside. There was nothing more I could do. I just stood there blankly, as if my path was completely blocked. I knew something suspicious must be happening inside that inn, but I had no way of knowing what. Helplessly, I started back numbly. As I turned the corner of the inn's wall, I heard the sounds of a man and woman chatting from a brightly lit sliding window (tŭlch’ang), followed by the sound of a sliding door closing. ‘Aha, this must be the room,’ I thought, pressing my ear to listen. For a moment, there was silence, making my heart pound anxiously. Then, I faintly heard the sound of clothes slipping off a body, followed by a couple of coughs, and the electric light abruptly went out. Everything felt sordid. All the soft, warm feelings that had been burning in my chest extinguished completely. I spat twice into the nearby ditch (kaech’ŏn) and turned back towards the main road. <148>

**Summary**

This story, presented as diary entries of a Seoul streetcar conductor in 1924, chronicles his observations of a young woman over one month. Initially, he sees her as a destitute, naive country girl whom he helps by giving her a free ride. A month later, he encounters her again, now well-dressed and carrying herself like a lady, seemingly involved with affluent men. His feelings evolve from pity to curiosity, fascination, jealousy, and finally, disillusionment and disgust as he witnesses her transformation and apparent moral compromise, culminating in him seeing her enter an inn late at night with a man. From a historical perspective, the story offers a valuable glimpse into urban life, class distinctions, and social mobility (particularly for women) in colonial Seoul. It highlights the stark contrasts between poverty and emergent modern lifestyles, touches upon changing gender roles and moral anxieties, and depicts everyday scenes and locations like streetcars, entertainment districts (Myŏngwŏlgwan), hospitals, and specific neighborhoods (Tongdaemun, Chongno, Ŭijut’ong), providing social commentary through the conductor's subjective lens.

11월 15일 曇
... 동대문서 신용산을 향하야 아츰 첫차를 가지고 떠난 것이 오늘 일이 시작이엇다.
전차가 동구 압헤서 정거를 하랴닛가 처음으로 승객 두 명이 탓다. 그들은 모두 양복을 입은 신사들인데 몃 달 동안 전차 차장에 익은 눈으로 보아서 그들이 어제 저녁 밤새도록 명월관에서 질탕이 놀다가 술이 취하야 그대로 그 자리에서 쓰러저 자다가 나오는 것을 짐작하얏다. 새벽이라 날이 몹시 선선할 뿐 안이라 서리 긔운석긴 찬 바람이 불어서 「추로리」 끈을 붓잡을 적마다 고두름을 만치는 것처럼 저리게 찬긔운이 장갑 낀 손에 수미어드는 듯 하다. 그들은 얼골에 앙팽이를 그리고 무슨 붓그러운 곳을 지내가는 사람 모양으로 모자는 눈까지 눌러 쓰고 외투로 코까지 싼 후에 두 억개는 양쪽으로 뻣죽 올라섯다. 아직 다 밝지는 안코 먼동이 터 옴으로 서쪽 한울과 동쪽 한울 두 사이 한복판을 두고서 광명과 암흑이 은연히 양색이 젓다. 그러나 눈오랴는 날처럼 북쪽 한울에는 회색 구름이 북악산 우를 답답하게 막어 노앗다. 운전수는 사람이 하나도 업는 널븐 길을 규정외의 마력을 내어서 전차를 달려갓다. 전차는 탑동공원 압 정류장에 와서 섯다. 먼 곳에서는 홰를 치며 우는 닭의 소리가 새벽 서리바람을 타고서 들려온다. 그러자 엇더한 녀자 하나가 내가 서 잇는 바로 차장대 층계 우에 어엽븐 발을 올려 놋는 것이 보엿다. 아즉 탈 사람이 별로히 업스리라고 지레 짐작에 신호를 하엿다가 그것을 보고서 다시 정지하라는 신호를 하얏다. 한 다리가 승강단 우에 병아리 모양으로 깡창 올러오더니 계란가티 옹쿠린 녀자가 톡 튀어 올라서＜138＞ 내 압흘 지내는데 머리는 어대서 엇더케 부시댁이를 첫는지 아모러케 허터진 것을 아모러케 쪽지고 본래부터 란잡하게 놀랴고 차리고 나섯는지는 알 수 업스나 옥양목 저고리에 무슨 치맊�인지 수수하게 차렷는데 손에는 비단으로 만든 지갑을 들럿섯다. 그러고 그가 내엽흘 지낼 때 일본녀자들이 차에 탈 젹이나 기생들이 차에 오를 적에 나의 코에 마치는 분 냄새와 향수 냄새가튼 상긋한 냄새가 찬바람에 석기어 나의 코에 시첫다.
그 녀자는 차안으로 들어가더니 그 안에 안저잇는 양복 입은 청년들의 눈을 피하랴 함인지 또는 내외를 하랴는 것처럼 맨 압헤 가서 압만 보고 안저 잇섯다. 두 젊은 사람은 어제 저녁에 긔생 데리고 놀든 흥이 아직까지도 풀리지 안엇는지 그 녀자를 보더니 한 사람이 팔꿈치로 엽헤 사람을 툭 치면서 눈을 끔적하얏다. 그러닛가 그 사람도 알엇다는 듯이 고개를 끄덱끄덱하며 그 녀자만 보고 잇섯다.
나도 호긔심이 일어나서 그 녀자 갓가히 가서 얼골이나 똑똑히 보리라 하고 뒤로 돌려 미엇든 가방을 압흐로 돌려서 전차표와 가위를 양손에 갈라쥐고 차 안으로 들어 갓다. 우선 두 젊은이에게 표를 찍어 주고서 그 녀자 압헤 가서 손을 내밀랴 하다가 나는 깜짝 놀래엇다. 나는 달려들어 이것이 왼일이오? 할 만치 놀랏다. 그리고 그의 머리에 꼬진 금비녀로부터 발에 신은 비단신까지 모주리 다시 한 번 훌터 보앗다.
엇더튼 표를 찍으랴 하닛가 자긔 지갑에서 돈을 끄내는데 1원자리인지 5원짜리인지 두 서너장 드러 잇는 중에서 한 장을 선선히 내노터니
『의주통(義州通)이요』
하고 저는 나를 니저바렷는지 태연하게 안저잇다. 의주통 밧구어 타는 표 한 장을 주고 나서 나는 다시 차장대로 나와섯슬 때 발서 전차는 청년회관 압흘 지내어 종로 정류장까지 왓다. 그 녀자는 거긔서 나리더니 저쪽으로 가버리엇다. 나는 또다시 남대문을 향하야 돌아가는 전차의 「추로리」를 바로 잡으랴고 창으로 고개를 내밀엇슬 때 한울은 중탁하게 덥히엇든 암흑이 점점 뽀얏케 거두어지며 동쪽에는＜139＞ 제법 불근 빗이 놀고 깜박깜박하는 별들이 체로 치는 것처럼 굴근 놈만 남고 잔놈들은 업서진다. 『나는 공연히 신긔한 생각이 드러서 못견대엿다. 그래서 혼자 해결할 수 업는 무슨 수수꺽기를 풀랴는 사람처럼 고개만 기웃하고 잇섯다. 나는 지내간 생각을 다시 끄집어 내엇스니 그것을 다음과 갓다.

한 달 전 바로 한 달 전에 역시 전차를 몰구서 배우개정류장에 정거를 하엿다. 오후 1시 가량이나 되엇는데 차 안에 승객이라고는 동대문 경찰서 형사 비슷한 사람 하나와 일본 녀자 둘과 또 조선 싀굴사람가튼 이가 잇슬 뿐인데 맨 나죵으로 들어온 녀자가 잇섯다.
손에다가는 약병과 약봉지를 들엇고 입은 것은 때가 지질이 끼고 자락이 갈갈이 찌저진데다가 얼골은 맷칠이나 세수를 하지 안엇는지 색깜앗캐 걸엇는데 발은 버슨 채 집세기 하나만 신엇다. 나희는 열 아홉이라면 족음 노성한 편이오, 스믈이라면 어대인지 어린 틔가 보인다. 속눈섭이 길음한데 정채잇게 도는 눈이라든지 보리퉁한 뺨과 둥그스름한 턱, 날카롭지도 안코 넓적하지도 안코 웬만한 코라든지 어대로 보아서든지 밉지 안은 녀자나 주제꼴이 볼성 사나와서 조흔 인상이 업섯다.
우리의 항상하는 례루로
『표 직그시오』
하고 손을 내미닛가 어리둥절하며 사방을 홰홰 내젓는데 다시 전차가 달아나닛가 그는 엇절 줄을 몰으고 엽헤 사람 얼골 한 번 치어다 보고 밧갓 한 번 내다보고 안지도 못하고 서지도 못하고 쩔쩔매는 것을 보닛가 싀골서 갓 올러 왓거나 당초에 전차 한 번 타보지도 못한 위인인 것을 알엇다. 우리는 항상 그러한 사람이 전차에 올으면 성가스럽다. 왜 그런고 하니 의례히 밧구어 타야 할 곳에서 박구어 타지를 안코 내릴 때를 지내노코 내리고서는 구찬케 굴기는 우리네 차장에게만 구찬케 굴 뿐 안이라 세상에 저밧게 약은 사람이 업는 것처럼 각금 전차표 5전을 떼 먹으랴고 엉터리 업는 밧궈타는 표를 어대서 어더가지고 와서는 속혀 먹으랴고 하기가 일수다. 그래서 그런 사람만 맛나면 공연이 화징이 나서 목소리가 불악불악 해진다.＜140＞
『어대까지 가우? 표내시우! 표요』

『녜?』

『녜가 무엇요. 표 내라닛가!』

『그런게 안이라요. 제가 싀골서 올라온 지가 한 달이나 되는데 먹을 것도 업고 입을 것도 업서서 동막(東幕) 어느 집에서 고용살이를 하다가 몸에 병이 나서 병원에 다녀 오는데 이것을 써주며 그리로 가면 된다고 해서 그리로 가요.』
모든 일은 다 알엇다. 총독부 의원 무료치료실에 갓다가 의사나 병원에 잇는 사람이 정상을 가련히 생각하고＜141＞ 인사상담소를 가르처준 것이오. 또는 갓 서울로 올러와서 돈도 업시 차를 탄 것도 사실인데 엇더튼 그때에 나의 마음에서는 알 수 업는 동정심이 나는 동시 마음이 약한 나는 그를 다시 전차에서 내려 쪼츨 수는 업섯다. 그래서 엇지하면 조흘가? 그대로 태우자니 규측위반이오 그러타고 내려 쪼츨 수는 업는데 하는 생각을 하며 차장대에 나려섯다가 전차가 황금정에 왓슬 때 나는 다시 그 압헤가서 밧구어 타는 표 한 장을 찍어주며

『괜찬어. 누군지 아나.』
이러케 말을 하는 동안에 어느듯 엇더한 려관 압헤 두 사람이 서 잇섯다. 그 려관문 개구녁으로 손을 느어 고리를 벗기더니 두 사람은 종적을 감추어 버리엇다. 나는 다시 엇지할 수가 업섯다. 압길을 탁 막어논 것가티 멀거니 서 잇기만 하엿다. 그 려관속에는 반듯이 무슨 수상한 일이 잇슬 것을 알엇으나 그것을 알 길이 업섯다. 하는 수 업시 멍멍이 돌아올 때 그 집 담 모퉁이를 돌아서랴닛가 불이 환하게 비취이는 들창 속에서 남자와 녀자의 잣거리는 소리가 들리며 미닫이를 닷는 소리가 들리엇다. 나는 올치 이 방이로구나 하는 생각이 들며 귀를 기우려 듯고 잇섯다. 족음은 아모 말이 업서서 공연히 나의 가슴이 아슬아슬하여졋다. 그러더니 옷이 몸둥이에서 밋그러저 버서지는 소리가 연하게 들리더니 기침 소리 두어 번이 나며 전긔불은 확 꺼지엇다. 나는 모든 것이 더러웟다. 내가 가슴 속에서 부드러웁고 따뜻하게 타든 모든 것이 그대로 꺼저 버리고 엽헤 잇는 개천에 침을 두어 번 뱃고서 큰 길로 돌아섯다.＜148＞
