**Body Text Translation:**
A Grand View of South Hamgyŏng's Towns: Yŏnghŭng County, Famous Production Center of Silk

"Everyone wears silk clothes, and every house sells Ssangsong Liquor" – this was a rough verse of my impression upon first entering Yŏnghŭng County. This Yŏnghŭng is a place with many pubs; no, it has long been famous as a production center for Yŏnghŭng silk (永興紬). It's a place where a large sum of over 154,500 won pours out annually from the belly of the tiny bug called the silkworm (天虫). In every village, the clacking sound of the Hamgyŏng wooden shuttle (나무북바듸), which wears down the water-like wrists of young women, can be heard, and even people with narrow heads (?) and hands like rough rakes (?) wear at least one silk *turumagi* (overcoat).

The youth hall has broken windows and walls (風窓破壁), and human figures are hard to see, but the pubs in every alley bustle day and night with the sorrowful songs of adulterous women and dissolute men sounding out. Although it's a silk production area, not a single person manages even a small-scale silk reeling (製絲) or weaving (織組) factory. However, the factory chimney of the Yŏnghŭng Brewery (永興釀造所), which pays 6,000 won in taxes annually while seemingly intending to poison all the youth and elderly of Yŏnghŭng with Ssangsong Liquor (雙城酒), stands tall, soaring into the mid-sky. This Yŏnghŭng not only has many pubs and much silk, but also quite a few mines. Starting with graphite, minerals such as lode gold (石金), placer gold (砂金), iron, and others are all inexhaustible. Currently, the total number of mining claims is over 20, covering an area of over 693,400 *p'yŏng*, with an annual mining output of 5,000 tons (graphite alone), reaching an annual production value of 10 million won. However, this has already <80> fallen into the grasp of cunning Japanese individuals, Hŏksŏknyŏng (黑石嶺), Wŏn Sangsa (原商事), and Samhoesa (三會社), becoming their exclusive domain. Bragging about the horns of a dead calf is useless, but at least be aware of it. Is that all? Regarding land, the so-called Yŏnghŭng Plain (永興平野), one of the five great plains of South Hamgyŏng (咸南), unfolds. It is fertile, with over 3,199 *chŏng* of paddy fields (畓) and over 12,470 *chŏng* of dry fields (田), producing over 70,000 *sŏk* of white rice, over 32,000 *sŏk* of soybeans, 76,000 *sŏk* of barnyard millet (稗), and also considerable amounts of barley and wheat. In fisheries, pollack (명태), mackerel (鯖), sea bream (鯛), Spanish mackerel (鰆), flounder (鰈), oysters (牡蠣), etc., are abundant, reaching an annual production of over 54,000 won. Among these, oysters are an important marine product. Yokoyama Kyōtarō (橫山敎太郞) and Nakamura Tarō (中村太郞), two Japanese from Wŏnsan (元山), have established cultivation grounds covering over 6,227,000 *p'yŏng* and harvest them annually, exporting them to various regions under the name "Wŏnsan Oysters." Look how sharp their eyes for profit are. Also, Yŏnghŭng cattle (永興牛) are famous products, but it is also known as a source of paragonimiasis (肺지스도마, an endemic disease 土疾), which everyone considers dangerous, making it a place even officials, who move around for their salaries, avoid going to. Yŏnghŭng County, possessing all these characteristics, is located 130 *ri* south of Hamhŭng and 150 *ri* north of Wŏnsan. To the north, it borders Chŏngp'yŏng (定平) across Kŭmp'a Pass (金坡嶺). To the west, it borders South P'yŏngan Province (平安南道) with overlapping mountain ranges, which undulate in the northwest corner [?] (북서ꊵ�) of the county, naturally forming a mountainous zone. To the south, it borders Kowŏn (高原) and Munch'ŏn (文川) counties, with small mountain ranges and tributaries of the Yonghŭng River (龍興江) forming the boundary. To the east, it faces the blue sea with a coastline reaching 25 *ri*. The Hodo Peninsula (虎島半島) faces a corner of Munch'ŏn County, enclosing Kowŏn County and guarding the head of Yŏnghŭng Bay (永興灣). The plain in the southeastern belt has fertile soil suitable for agriculture. The famous Yonghŭng River flows through the center of the county, extending over 400 *ri*, providing convenience for irrigation and boating (舟楫). The tip of the Hodo Peninsula has long been famous as a scenic spot (景勝地), but it has now become a Japanese army fortress, and ordinary people lack the freedom to enter or leave. In terms of history, it was Pakp'yŏng County (博平郡) during the former Koguryŏ period. A fortress was first built in the 6th year of King Kwangjong (光宗) of Koryŏ. After undergoing numerous changes, it was called Hwaryŏng Prefecture (和寧府) during the time of King Kongmin (恭愍王). In the 2nd year of Yi T'aejo (李太祖), it was first named Yŏnghŭng Prefecture (永興府). Later, King T'aejong (太宗王) changed it sometimes to a county (郡), sometimes a prefecture (府), or Hwaju Provincial Office (和州牧). King Sejong the Great (世宗大王) changed it to a Great Protectorate Command (大都護府). From the time of Emperor Kojong (高宗 太皇帝), it became a county (郡) and has remained so to this day. The total area is 142,169 square *ri* [unit likely incorrect or archaic, perhaps sq km?], divided into 13 *myŏn* and 282 *dong*. It accommodates a total of 20,121 households (169 Japanese households) and a total population of 116,652 (745 Japanese). The main occupation is agriculture, followed by commerce, industry, and fishing. For transportation, the Hamgyŏng Line passes through the territory, with Yŏnghŭng, Majang, Wangjang, and Sam stations within the county. There are also first and second-class roads, making land transport very convenient. However, the coast lacks good harbors, and Songjŏn Bay (松田灣) is also shallow, making it impossible for large ships to enter, causing much inconvenience <81> by sea. Education consists only of several regular elementary schools, a simple agricultural school, and several private schools. There is nothing particularly noteworthy to record. Especially concerning female education, it has not yet escaped the old ways, and there is a harmful tendency to avoid enrollment even in elementary-level schools. Regarding religion, Christianity has relatively more influence. Ch'ŏndogyo was extremely prosperous for a time, but due to the so-called new-old faction conflict (신구충돌), both factions [?] (양파갊�) are now in a dormant state with no particular activity. This is truly regrettable. Among private organizations, besides the youth association, there is the newly emerged ideological group Sŏkp'ung hoe (朔風會 - North Wind Society). Its central figures are the young men Chang Myŏnghyŏn (張明炫), Cho Ponghŭi (趙鳳熙), Wŏn Yongguk (元容國), Kim Yonggwan (金龍觀), and Kim T'ak (金鐸), who are striving hard. However, their influence is originally weak, and they face many constraints in their environment, appearing to be in difficulty. The notable wealthy individuals are Ch'oe Chekŭk (崔濟克) and U Chongnim (禹宗林) of Changhŭng-myŏn (長興面), but their wealth amounts to only 400,000-500,000 won. Regarding disposition (인정), it's called the Yŏnghŭng "ulttuk" (울둑 - stubborn/blunt?), meaning bluntness combined with much sullenness (陰性). In South Hamgyŏng, when referring to people from Yŏnghŭng, they are considered suspicious [?] (依* 속 안이주고 - not giving their inner thoughts?), deceitful (虛言), and full of schemes (譎計), causing people to shake their heads slowly. Also, early marriage (早婚), heavy drinking (多飮), and debauchery (淫蕩) can be said to be major detrimental customs (弊風) of Yŏnghŭng. As for famous sites and historical relics, there are Yi T'aejo's Stele of Achievements (紀蹟碑) (in Heuksŏk-ri, Sŭnnyŏng-myŏn) and Chunwŏnjŏn (濬源殿) (west of the stele?, Hwanjo's old home site? 在紀**西, 桓祖舊*). However, these are generally known, so I will omit details. Although there are many complaints from the populace towards government offices, the most frequent concern the county magistrate (군수). Next are complaints about the police station chief, who solicited private donations to build a so-called Public Hall (公會堂), but now sits holding the keys like a prison guard, readily permitting Japanese use but not readily permitting Korean use (there was even a citizens' rally about this), and that there are many wrongful lawsuits (枉訟) because the police chief concurrently serves as the prosecutor for the court. However, intending to speak more on that on a later occasion, I will just put down my pen for now. <82>

**Summary**

This 1924 article describes Yŏnghŭng County in South Hamgyŏng Province as a region known for its silk (Myŏngju) production, Ssangsong liquor, and abundant natural resources including minerals (graphite, gold, iron) and fertile plains. It details the county's geography, history, population, transportation, and limited educational facilities, noting a particular lag in female education. The author observes local characteristics, describing the people as blunt ("ulttuk") and somewhat negative, prone to early marriage, heavy drinking, and debauchery. Social issues include the dormancy of Ch'ŏndogyo due to factionalism and the struggles of a nascent ideological group, Sŏkp'ung hoe. Significant criticism is directed at the economic exploitation by Japanese individuals and companies who dominate mining and oyster farming, and at the local administration, particularly the police chief's discriminatory control over the public hall and his dual role as prosecutor leading to perceived injustices.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is revealing about the extent of Japanese economic penetration and control over key industries like mining and fisheries, explicitly naming Japanese individuals and companies benefiting from Korea's resources. It highlights Korean grievances against the colonial administration, particularly the police, citing specific issues like discriminatory access to public facilities and potential abuses within the justice system due to the police chief's dual role. The mention of local characteristics, social problems like drinking and early marriage, and the state of education and social organizations provides a snapshot of Korean society under colonial rule, reflecting both internal challenges and external pressures.
