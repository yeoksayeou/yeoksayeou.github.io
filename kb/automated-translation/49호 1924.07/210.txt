Crossing the Border
						Pak Pom in North Manchuria
(First 10 lines omitted)... With tears of weary sighs, I turned my back on the Motherland and set foot towards the North; it was now five years ago, the winter of 1919. Although I knew vaguely from maps where Manchuria and Siberia were, and had heard they were cold regions, this was, of course, my first real experience. The sun had crossed the western mountains, and it was the time of purple-gold dusk. As soon as I set foot on the ice of the Yalu River, our country's last boundary, gentlemen like messengers from the underworld, as if they had found some beast to devour, shouted, "Hey you, where are you going? Come here!" blowing whistles and causing a commotion. Since my situation was already like fish on the cutting board, I thought, "Fine, devour me if you must, let's see if I can run away," and stumbling and falling this way and that, I barely managed, by sheer luck, to reach the shore of Chinese territory. My whole body felt intensely hot, as if a bonfire had been poured into me, my throat was parched like waking up after being drunk, and I gasped for breath. Helplessly, I collapsed under a sunken bank, and after catching my breath for a while, I lifted my head and looked back at the Motherland one more time. A lump of indescribable grief and indignation surged up in my chest, and tears like fists dropped down. I tried to endure the stinging, painful feeling but couldn't; I tried to stop the pouring tears but couldn't. Like an old father whose only son has died, I wept uncontrollably for some time. Finally, wiping my tears with the hem of my *turumagi*, I stood up and, moving my trembling legs hastily, recited inwardly the first verse of the "Song of World Circumambulation" written by Mr. Ch'oe Nam-sŏn: "Hanyang, farewell, I shall return," and headed towards a house with lights on. Reaching a small thatched house, I called for the owner from outside. A person in blue clothes came out, mumbling something in a language I had never heard in my life. Like two mutes who could not communicate verbally,＜96＞ we used hand gestures, and I barely managed to borrow a spot to rest my weary body for one night. The next morning, as soon as it dawned, as if a dear person was waiting somewhere, as if returning to my own home after being in a foreign land, my young self, all alone, began to walk step by step aimlessly across the desolate Manchurian plains, not knowing the direction. The wind blowing straight down from the peak of the Ural Mountains—ah! It was truly cold. When the fierce wind, blowing snow flurries with a *ssing-ssing* sound, pierced through my thin cotton clothes, my bones tingled sharply, and my flesh felt like it was being carved out and dropping off piece by piece. Wiping away the endlessly flowing tears, I grumbled, "Ah! God of fate, why do you expel a young body of 19 into the endless fields without a home, to become a vengeful spirit in the snow?" But then I thought, "No, no, just as the saying goes, 'Better be the beak of a chicken than the rump of an ox,' rather than barely surviving as a slave without freedom, if I run freely and vigorously across the wide, wide plains as I please and end up buried in the pure white snow, how exhilarating and heroic that would be!" When I thought this way, my legs felt lighter without realizing it, and my steps quickened.

After several weeks like this, I arrived at OOO. Like widows sharing their sorrows only with each other, comrades with similar circumstances and goals complained to and consoled each other about the grief coiled deep within their hearts, waiting only for the chance to stretch their great wings (鵬翼一伸). Then came the late autumn of 1920... Kando Subjugation (間島 討伐)...
(3 lines omitted) Suppressing my grief and indignation, I moved north, further north. What change could there be in the natural seasons? As soon as the first winter arrived, big flakes of cold snow poured down! The biting wind blew mercilessly! Walking and shivering on the vast, trackless plains with no houses or roads, when the sun set, we would gather snow to build a circular wall, huddle together inside like a flock of ducks, occasionally swapping places—those in the middle moving to the outside, those outside moving to the middle—and embracing each other to pass the night. When hungry, we took out millet flour (*sorokpun*, 粟粉) from our knapsacks. When putting the dry powder into our mouths without even water, if the wind blew suddenly, our throats would seize up, and tears would well in our eyes. Eating a handful of snow would clear the throat, but the body would shiver even more. When encountering such times, I wanted to curse the whole world anew, and my feelings towards XX grew even deeper.

In 1922, when I was wounded in the XX Battle and hospitalized at XXXXX Hospital, lying alone on the sickbed thinking about this and that, all worldly affairs seemed sorrowful. "Ah, why did I survive after being hit by that one shot, what hope could there be?" I sighed and shed tears. But then, "Why die without seeing the end after striving and fighting until now? I will achieve my goal, return to my longed-for, beloved hometown, hold hands with my affectionate siblings, and experience an ideal new life!" Changing my thoughts＜97＞ like this, I pleaded with the doctor to heal my illness quickly.

It was a day when the breeze, warm like a maiden's embrace, gently brushed past my ears, and the sunlight was bright, making my limbs feel languid. Sitting on the grassy field where new blades poked up, pondering something in my head and sighing, I felt intensely envious watching the strollers, dressed neatly in spring clothes, some in couples, some with friends, coming and going in search of spring scenery. And so, I spontaneously recited the old poem, "Are there no flowers or grass in the barbarian land? / Though spring comes, it doesn't feel like spring," and felt, "The red flowers and green leaves blooming beautifully to show their nature in the warm spring breeze, and the moon shining brightly in the high, clear autumn sky—what comfort can they be to me, homeless and hungry! They are only sources of heartache, agony, and pain!" Then I thought, "Ah, just like the phrase in the *Summary of Historical Materialism* (惟物史觀 要領記), 'Thought arises from matter,' feelings like these must be the sprout (芽) of revolutionary thought. And I am surely not the only homeless and hungry person; in the current irrational and highly contradictory society, regardless of nationality or race, there must be many homeless and hungry people. And before the united power of this homeless and hungry proletarian mass (無産群衆), the possibility of achieving XX is certain!" When I thought this, all sorrow vanished, and I felt the energy to smile broadly.

Ah! But ah, but in our society where there is still nothing, like a beggar tearing his own sack, people quarrel over things like bubbles on water—honor, rights, what have you—causing society to descend into chaos (混沌), and the sprout of hope is shrouded in dense fog. Ah! God of nature! God of fate! What will become of this homeless, hungry young body?
Night of March 28, 1924 (End)＜98＞

**Summary**

This memoir by Pak Pom, written in 1924, recounts his harrowing escape from Korea across the Yalu River into Manchuria in 1919. He describes the extreme hardships of exile life – severe cold, hunger, homelessness, and the constant threat from authorities (implied Japanese). He details his participation in conflicts (likely related to the Korean independence movement, mentioning the "Kando Subjugation" of 1920 and being wounded in a battle in 1922), his despair, and his evolving thoughts towards revolution, influenced by Marxist ideas (Historical Materialism, proletarian unity) as a potential path to liberation and a new society. However, he ends on a pessimistic note, lamenting the internal divisions within the Korean community that hinder progress. For historians, the article offers a raw, personal perspective on the experiences of Korean exiles in Manchuria during the early independence movement, showcasing the brutal conditions, the psychological toll, the influence of emerging ideologies like socialism/communism on nationalist activists, and the internal conflicts that plagued the movement abroad. The references to specific events (though censored) and figures like Ch'oe Nam-sŏn provide valuable context.
