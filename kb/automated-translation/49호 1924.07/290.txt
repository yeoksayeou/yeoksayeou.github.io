Magazine Title: 개벽 제49호
Publication Date: 1924년 07월 01일
Article Title: 버림바든 者(전1막), 懸賞入選文藝 각본 3등 (The Forsaken One (Act 1), 3rd Place Winner in the Prize Literary Contest for Scripts)
Author: 匿名生 (Anonymous Student)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

The Forsaken One
						Anonymous Student
Characters.
Kang P'ilchun.     A 24-year-old young man. Law school graduate, currently a painter.
P'ilchun's Father.  Pastor of a Presbyterian church in the city.
P'ilchun's Older Brother. Returned home due to illness while studying at a seminary in Japan.
Kang P'irun.      P'ilchun's younger brother. A second-year student at a professional school run by a city missionary; a leader of the student strike.
Pak.          P'ilchun's friend, an executive member of the OO Socialist secret society.
Deacon Ch'oe.      Around 40 years old.
Kim.          P'irun's friend. A poor student. Worker.
Yŏngsun.        P'ilchun's lover.
Two Policemen.

Time.   Spring 1924. Around 10 AM on a certain fine Saturday.

Stage.   The interior of the reception room in P'ilchun's house, built last autumn in a semi-Western style next to the back hill of Chukch'ŏm-jŏng. On the left side of the stage, there is a door leading outside, and on the right side, a door leads to the studio. Above both doors hangs a large oil painting without a frame. In the center of the front wall, a large, wide observation window leading to a veranda is wide open, allowing the warm spring breeze to occasionally flow into the room. On either side of it are windows with curtains half drawn back, the glass panes closed. Inside the observation window stands a large easel, upon which rests a canvas, apparently depicting the outdoor scene visible through the window, still unfinished. In front of it sits a chair facing the canvas, and on a small table standing to its right, a palette, oil pot, paints, brushes, etc., are scattered about. In front of the left door, a large round table is placed, with several folded newspapers laid upon it, and 4-5 chairs surround the table. This reception room also serves as a study, as next to the right door, a tall bookshelf <153> is packed with colorful books. Potted plants are appropriately placed in various corners of the room. A hat hangs on the left wall. (A bell rings, and after the curtain rises, the stage is empty and the surroundings quiet for one minute. Shortly after, the protagonist P'ilchun slowly enters from the front observation window, wearing painting clothes, his bare head bowed listlessly. He collapses into a chair placed next to the large table on the left and pulls out a pocket Bible from his painting clothes pocket, slamming it onto the table with a thud.) Then he bows his head onto the table, and after a while, he lets out a lo-o-ong sigh and lifts his head.
P'ilchun. Ah-... The world is full of foolish people. Why do they love binding themselves with their own hands so much? Since humans began crawling on the earth until today, all they've done is nothing more than twist a great rope to bind themselves... Even so, they should just bind and be bound among themselves. Why do they clamor that I, who do not wish to bind myself, am a wicked man and a sinner?... (Nods his head lightly, expressing some affirmation, and rises) That's right. I must fight. (Walking towards the easel, in a trembling, tragic voice) Ah—I must fight God. (Stamps his foot with a thud. As P'ilchun faces the easel, adjusts the canvas placed upon it, holds a palette and three brushes in his left hand, and grasps a large brush in his right hand, briefly stepping back to observe the canvas, the sound of a child's willow flute announcing spring flows in through the observation window. P'ilchun's eyes gleam; he puts down everything he was holding onto the table and goes to the observation window, leaning towards the direction of the flute sound. Brief silence. Only the flute sound continues, long and drawn out. P'ilchun's face suddenly shows a glimmer as if recognizing someone.)
P'ilchun. Where have you been already? P'irun. (The way his head gradually turns from left to right reflects the steps of someone walking in from outside)
P'irun. (Still offstage) Already? Where did you go, Brother? I went out looking for you, couldn't find you, and just went to Kwansu-dong and came back. (P'irun's figure is seen slowly walking in through the left window)
P'ilchun. Did you think I'd run off somewhere? Did you eat breakfast?
P'irun. Yes. (Appears fully in front of the observation window. He wears a professional school uniform, hat on, but the school badge is removed. Facing his brother, he leans against the opposite windowsill) I went into Chongno and bought some *hottŏk* (a type of Korean pancake). But have you eaten anything, Brother?
P'ilchun. Me?... Say, where is that flute sound coming from? It scrapes at one's dried-up insides...
P'irun. It's from the white poplar grove over there. It's especially warm today.
P'ilchun. (Straightening up, with a tragic face) It's a world where even innocent children sing of sorrow. <154>
P'irun. (Turns his face to look at his brother) Brother, why have you become so overly sensitive? What's the big deal... about that incident... Don't worry about anything. It's like the saying, 'If a person with healthy eyes goes among three one-eyed people, they call him handicapped.' Those fellows call you a sinner because you're not like them. But if you deliberately plucked out one healthy eye to become one-eyed just to avoid being called handicapped by them, that would be rather foolish.
P'ilchun. That's right. There's no need to deliberately close open eyes. Come in. (Comes to the large table and sits perched on a chair. P'irun follows behind, sits opposite his brother, and continues the conversation)
P'irun. By the way, Brother. I hear those fellows are going to excommunicate you. Calling you a sinner... What are they babbling about, telling you to mourn and repent, or wear sackcloth and ashes... Only fellows destined to be pastors for generations have gathered.
P'ilchun. There you go again with that talk. Don't you think about Father? Father is also a pastor. And aren't you and I the sons of a pastor?
P'irun. (With a bitter smile) Brother, precisely because I am the son of a pastor, I feel that much more animosity towards pastors.
P'ilchun. That is indeed a strange relationship. For the son of a pastor to harbor such animosity towards pastors.
P'irun. More than that, the fact that you, the son of a pastor, are about to be excommunicated by your own pastor father is an even stranger contrast.
P'ilchun. (Sighing deeply) But it is neither the pastor's son's fault nor the pastor's fault. It is because a great, unknown flaw lies hidden within the concept of the moral standard that all humanity has defended and passed down to this day, and within so-called faith, which is merely a variation of it... That is... people, especially so-called believers, observe all objects through the lens of faith... Therefore, what is reflected in their eyes is not the true form of things, but merely withered illusions. Yet they regard this as the truth, and if anyone denies it, they immediately denounce them <155> as sinners and wicked people deserving punishment before God. This is what's pitiful.
P'irun. The group that defends that distorted morality calls you a sinner. Those fellows try to measure even us with that distorted ruler. So, inevitably, nothing but a student strike could arise. You approve of our student strike, don't you, Brother?
P'ilchun. You've already brought up your own issue somehow. Well, looking at it from a certain perspective, I think it was an unavoidable situation. But it probably would have been better to study. How much great...
P'irun. Then what about you, Brother—uh—(Bends to one side, pulls out a watch, and looks at it) Around now, there must be a commotion near Pagoda Park. Radical leaflets must be plastered on every telephone pole.
P'ilchun. (Extremely surprised) What?... What are you talking about?
P'irun. (Innocently) And also... numerous workers are staging a demonstration or something...
P'ilchun. What are you talking about now?
P'irun. What do you mean? You probably know better than I do. I heard that you are the leader.
P'ilchun. I'm involved?... Where did you hear that?... Ah—in this world, it seems a word spoken under the mat truly travels a thousand leagues. So where did you hear it?
P'irun. What, I just went to Sangil's house, and everyone knew.
P'ilchun. I wasn't exactly the main leader, but I did attend the last meeting and express some opinions. However, my intention was not to stage a demonstration or distribute radical leaflets. I intended to make the public understand through moderate means. But they insisted on carrying out secret radical actions according to their own will until the end.
P'irun. So what does it matter? People already see you as the prime culprit...
P'ilchun. (As if annoyed by the sound) Never mind that, more importantly... (Sighing deeply, with an expression of deep thought) More importantly... I <156> am someone who might be excommunicated tomorrow. Starting tomorrow, you might also be too ashamed to go to church.
P'irun. Why? What's wrong with a person loving another person? What sin is a sin deserving excommunication? Excommunication itself isn't terrifying, but the talk about sin is...
P'ilchun. But for me, it is an extremely painful matter... They try to measure me with the fixed ruler of good and evil they hold, so it turns out that way.
P'irun. So the problem is because the lady is a stage actress?
P'ilchun. (With a bitter smile) That could be one factor. But more than that, not getting parental permission, not consulting the church's opinion, and above all, not performing the formal wedding ceremony, that... secret... that—well—that will be the great sin. (Raises his voice a notch) It will *appear* to be a great sin.
P'irun. That is...? No matter how much I think about it, I simply cannot understand why people today attach such great significance to the so-called marriage ceremony. In my view, if a man and a woman understand and love each other, it seems the marriage is already established from the moment they begin to love. Wouldn't it be right to consider the two individuals already a married couple? Why is there a need to absolutely perform that ceremony? Since men and women existed long before the institution of the marriage ceremony... Like Jeanette's words in John Quinn's "The Last Trump," people "bore children for thousands of years before the institution of marriage arose, and even if the institution of marriage were shattered now, they would continue to bear children for thousands, tens of thousands of years afterward." Why must only the union of a man and woman who have gone through the marriage institution be considered legitimate?
P'ilchun. (Smiling weakly) You're going to be quite a playwright. When you're studying at school, you should devote all your energy to your studies. Why do you keep chattering about 'Quinn says this' and 'Shakespeare says that'? That's why subversive thoughts against the school authorities naturally arise. Hahaha. Anyway, your argument is good. <157>
P'irun. If that's the case, then I'll have to stage a strike against you too, Brother. Hahahaha. (Brief silence)
P'ilchun. By the way, haven't you missed Father lately? It's been several days since you went on strike and were kicked out of the house.
P'irun. Exactly— (Counting on his fingers) It seems to be eight days. Missing him... Well, if I miss him that much, I'll just go to church on Sunday.
P'ilchun. By the way, P'irun. When you went into Chongno earlier—
P'irun. (As if guessing what he's about to say) Yes—you mean about the lady. I went. I boldly went to the actresses' dormitory and asked for her. I searched for a while, but there was no answer, so I thought she must still be asleep, as actors often are, and was about to leave when some woman inside shrieked in a terribly sharp voice, "Yŏngsun isn't in this troupe anymore! She said she's leaving because the actor's life is dirty!" Hearing her scream like that made me a bit angry, but I forced myself to remain calm and was about to leave when several women inside burst out laughing noisily. Where did the lady go?
P'ilchun. (Nods his head lightly back and forth as if understanding something) So she finally did it. She finally left. She cut ties. (Rises from the chair, unbuttons the front of his painting clothes, takes out a letter in an envelope from inside, and gives it to P'irun. P'irun takes it and reads the contents. P'ilchun stands up, folds his arms behind his back, bows his head in thought, and paces the room) That's right. Everyone, without exception, must leave their current place.
P'irun. (Lifts his head while reading the letter) Ah, then Brother. The lady has left the theater troupe. (The face of P'ilchun's friend, Pak, appears through the left window. P'ilchun quickly takes the letter back and puts it in his breast pocket. Both brothers welcome Pak warmly. Pak enters from the veranda)
P'ilchun. Come in. Why is your face always filled with anger whenever I see it? As if you're about to fight someone. (P'irun gets up and places a chair towards Pak)
Pak. I intend to fight everything except myself. Hahahaha. But you always have a crying face, like a 망부석 (widow waiting stone) standing on a parting hill. <158>
P'irun. Since both my brother and you, sir, are like that, my face probably looks the most cheerful. Hahahaha. (Pak takes off his hat)
Pak. (Turning to look at P'irun) Is that P'irun? Hmm—P'irun's face is always manly. (Sits perched on the chair and immediately pulls out a cigarette and lights it. Takes a drag and, while walking around the room, turns to look at P'ilchun, who is teasing him) By the way, my friend. I just came from seeing that almost everyone has gathered at Pagoda Park, and the demonstration procession should be heading towards Chongno right about now... But, my friend, the big problem is that the authorities seem to have caught wind that this demonstration didn't arise purely from the self-awareness of the labor union, but that some socialist group is lurking behind it with some other objective. Not only that, but it seems they've investigated the list of related individuals as meticulously as counting sesame seeds.
P'ilchun. With someone like you around, how could it not be discovered? Who else is here now? Just you and me?
P'irun. Isn't there one more detective here? Hahahaha. (Sits perched on the chair)
Pak. You mean P'irun? P'irun himself already claims to be a revolutionary.
P'ilchun. I hate hearing that. I have no idea what you guys mean by 'revolutionary'. All you do day and night is clamor about destruction and overthrow, trying to break everything you encounter in this vast, empty world without even knowing why or what you're trying to break. If you keep doing that, you'll die from exhaustion.
Pak. Revolution isn't just about destruction. We don't consider destruction our sole motto.
P'ilchun. Hmm—there must be construction too. But your construction is nothing more than construction for the sake of destruction. It's merely like building siege cannons to destroy the castle walls over there. We might need to build siege cannons aimed at destruction, but first, we must build shelters to ensure the safety of our lives. We might need to destroy the enemy's capital with siege cannons, but first, we must clearly determine the location to build our capital and charge towards that location. Just bombarding indiscriminately on an endless battlefield without any target <159> will soon lead to running out of ammunition and being surrounded by the enemy's artillery. (P'irun stares blankly at the faces of Pak and his brother in turn, as if lost in the conversation progressing between them. Pak clearly shows displeasure at P'ilchun's words)
Pak. Your words have some merit, my friend. But we are destroying the enemy's things. Since all of the enemy's things threaten our safety, shouldn't we destroy them?
P'ilchun. It's not that they aren't the enemy's swords, and they do threaten our safety, but can't we use the enemy's swords ourselves instead of breaking them? If we destroy bridges to halt the enemy's advance, won't we also be unable to cross that river?
Pak. You always tend to adopt such idealistic arguments suited for peaceful times. Are you implying labor-capital compromise?
P'ilchun. That's not it.
Pak. If not that, then what? Shouldn't there be some thorough, eternally unchanging principle? And accordingly, shouldn't there be an inflexible, concrete plan to carry things out?
P'ilchun. (Raises his hand and points at Pak) There. That's exactly it. (Walks from the distance to the table and sits opposite Pak) Your mistake lies precisely there, my friend. For some reason, humans always try to erect an idol called 'principle'. Just like you said now, they strive to erect an idol called an 'eternally unchanging, thorough principle'. And they consider those who make this idol look nice and large and carry it on their backs valuable people, while deeming those without the idol worthless. But carrying this idol is not such an easy task. My saying this doesn't mean principles themselves are unnecessary. But many people don't possess them for their own sake; rather, they consider it natural to offer even their lives for them. But if you think about it, principles should exist for people, not people <160> for principles. Modern people laugh at the ancient Egyptians who threw their beloved children into the mouths of the Baal idol they built with their own hands, but people today are throwing themselves even more readily into the mouths of invisible idols.
Pak. Then what are you suggesting, my friend? Forget principles, forget everything, and live like a cloud floating in the blue sky—is that the wisest way?
P'ilchun. No. I am by no means saying that the principle itself is wrong. I am saying the person who holds it is wrong. I am saying, even if you hold it, don't hold it as an idol.
Pak. Then what do you think is the right way?
P'ilchun. Each person must recognize the absolute value in themselves. Above all, they must be faithful to their own interests. Pursuing one's own interests through the best means is precisely the will of the Great Subject governing the universe... Don't all beings arrayed in the universe absolutely pursue their own interests? Because each pursues its own interests completely, harmony arises, and evolution proceeds. Among them, this thing called 'human' perversely babbles about sacrificing lives for something, or how something is a sin for some reason, needlessly bringing down a dismal fate upon its own head with its own hands. Ah, if only unnecessary sacrifice were removed from human society, and the sentence of baseless sin were lifted, how much happier life would become! How quickly society would progress! (Suddenly realizing his words are leaning too much towards unconventional theory, he lowers his voice and puts on a weak smile) So, whatever principle you hold, whatever means you use, whatever you rush towards, if *not* doing it is more beneficial, then resolutely *not* doing it is the wise thing to do.
P'irun. (Seeing Pak's somewhat displeased expression, he defends his brother and brings up a new topic to Pak) Brother has been quite agitated for several days, and especially last night, he didn't sleep a wink all night, so he's become very sensitive. <161> (P'ilchun makes no attempt at clarification, rests his elbows on the table, supports his head with his hands, deep in thought) ... By the way, you said the demonstration procession is walking from Pagoda Park to Yongsan?
Pak. No, not quite. Twenty-five representatives went to Yongsan first to negotiate directly with the factory owners' side, and the remaining workers were to demonstrate in the city to declare their demands to the general public.
P'irun. What method will be used for the city propaganda?
Pak. They'll distribute flyers, march while singing labor songs, and give propaganda speeches at key locations.
P'irun. Will it proceed well? Will the authorities allow such a large crowd to roam the city demonstrating so freely?
Pak. We'll do what we can. If they insist on dispersing us...
P'irun. Oh, by the way, you said the authorities seem to have already detected the details?
Pak. It seems that way. But strangely enough, despite P'irun's brother here having different opinions from us, he seems to have been identified as the main instigator. (P'ilchun sighs deeply, as if waking from a train of thought. Pak turns to P'ilchun) Oh! By the way, my friend. How is your issue progressing these days? Rumor has it that your father is quite... (P'ilchun remains deep in silence)
P'irun. Nothing much... nothing major is happening. It's just foolish people chattering among themselves.
Pak. (Shifts in his seat, facing P'ilchun) No, but what are the general details of the situation? You remain too silent. How does the church side seem to view you?
P'ilchun. (Leans back heavily in the chair, folds his arms, and sighs deeply) A sinner, of course.
Pak. Can't believers love women?
P'irun. Then should believers become monks and have no descendants? <162>
Pak. (Still facing P'ilchun) Then why do they intend to treat you as a sinner?
P'ilchun. They look through the lens called creed, so my love must appear distorted to them.
Pak. Then you have a wife... no, you aren't married yet.
P'ilchun. Hmm- I'm not married, but according to them, my love for Yŏngsun involves impure acts...
Pak. Then a secret affair... (Brief silence)
P'ilchun. Hmm- It's not that there isn't a secret affair, but whether they know that much or not is hard to say.
Pak. Then Yŏngsun...
P'irun. (Seeing Pak hesitate, says somewhat nervously) She's an actress. (Brief silence. Tension)
P'ilchun. (Opens his eyes and straightens his back) So Yŏngsun...
Pak. (Interrupting P'ilchun) ...isn't a virgin, is she? Hahahaha (The atmosphere relaxes again) Hmm? A maiden who...
P'ilchun. Of course, she's not a maiden. She is my wife.
Pak. (Looking somewhat bewildered) No- I mean, before she met you.
P'ilchun. Before she met me?... (Sighs deeply, showing reminiscent emotion) Ah- She came out of her mother's womb and gave the key of her virginity first to me.
Pak. What? A virgin? An actress- a female actress remained a virgin?
P'irun. Why? Is there a law that actresses cannot remain virgins?
Pak. (Shakes his head slyly at P'ilchun) Wha-at, her hymen probably fled long ago.
P'ilchun. (Frowning, with a scornful snort, in a barely audible voice) Hymen...
Pak. (Interrupting P'ilchun again) ...fled, I say. In that case, I too have some advice for you. Of course, I'm not speaking from the church's standpoint, which considers you a sinner and seeks to punish you, <163> but out of concern for you—well—because I feel sorry for you.
P'ilchun. Sorry for me?
Pak. Hmm- I'm saying this because you might not taste the supreme beauty of youth. The beauty of chastity. I fear you might not taste the supreme beauty of a virgin's chastity. Aren't you merely embracing the corpse of violated chastity?
P'ilchun. (With a look of anger) Violated chastity? How do you know so well? Whose chastity is a dead corpse? No- what standard do you use for virginity? How do you measure the purity of chastity?
Pak. Isn't that self-evident as an axiom, even without me saying it? Adding other sophistries to it is too much...
P'ilchun. Then you mean you consider the loss of a woman's hymen as the death of chastity? Isn't that too cruel? If viewing it that way were rational, there would be too many unjustly non-virgins in the world. People aren't immune to illness... and there's no law saying it can't be lost due to illness... I absolutely refuse to see it that way.
Pak. That's sophistry. Why bring up cases of abnormality caused by illness?
P'ilchun. Al-right then, let's consider something more. Suppose there is a woman here who has become a prostitute, swayed by her circumstances and fate. Even so, based on this fact alone, I would never judge her chastity. How many unexpected tragedies have occurred in the world because of people like you who judge the sanctity of chastity by a woman's hymen? Even if a woman is physically a prostitute, if her love is completely preserved, she will always be a virgin... In this imperfect social system, there are bound to be devils seeking to violate a woman's body, but in this sense, <164> I am convinced that Yŏngsun and I are in a relationship of virgin love between a bachelor and a maiden.
Pak. But you say this without understanding the essence of womanhood. When we picture a woman in our minds, how can we sketch her outline without including her body? Chastity, too, cannot be interpreted with such romantic theories as you suggest.
P'ilchun. You see people only as lumps of flesh. (Before he finishes speaking, a knocking sound is heard at the left door. P'ilchun frowns, glances at the door briefly, then stands up, goes to the easel, sits down, picks up the palette, and starts painting)
P'irun. (Also frowning, towards the door) Come in. (The door opens, and Deacon Ch'oe, still wearing a drab winter workman's overcoat, holding a briefcase in his left hand and rolled-up leaflets in his right, enters) Oh, Deacon, you've come.
Deacon. (Nods politely to Pak, sits down, and turns to P'ilchun) How have you been lately? Where is your older brother?
P'irun. Yes... yes- you mean my older brother? My brother- (P'ilchun stands up, palette and brush in hand, and slowly approaches Deacon Ch'oe) Yes- he's over there.
Deacon. (Seeing P'ilchun) Mr. P'ilchun, did you sleep well?
P'ilchun. Yes-. Did you sleep well?
Deacon. Yes-. ... Last night... did you... receive the inspiration of the Holy Spirit? (P'irun turns his face away and smiles silently in derision.)
P'ilchun. ... Yes- well... what... I am still receiving it now.
Pak. (With a mocking expression) He received it well until now. I also helped him quite a bit.
Deacon. Yes- Did you pray? We are all just sinners. Everyone, without exception, is a sinner before God. When faced with such circumstances, the best thing is simply to constantly repent before God and pray to receive forgiveness for sins. (P'irun and Pak each turn their faces away and mock him. P'ilchun turns back, scratches his head with the hand holding the brush, returns to the easel, and resumes painting.)
Pak. (Also in a mocking tone) That's right. If you take away sin from humans, there's nothing left. <165>
Deacon. (Always looking only at P'ilchun) We humans are weak and cannot overcome the flesh. We inevitably need the power of the Holy Spirit to escape the devil's grasp. Anyone born human feels appetite when seeing food, greed when seeing money, the desire to kill when seeing an enemy, bears false witness to hide their sins... and feels lust like fire when seeing a woman. Therefore, facing this situation now, one must absolutely 'pray without ceasing, stay awake.' In such circumstances, without prayer, one cannot rise again. (P'ilchun consistently just moves his brush. P'irun purses his lips disdainfully. Pak occasionally scoffs, "Haha, is that so?", opens his mouth wide, glances at the Deacon, and nods his head.)
P'ilchun. (Annoyed, slams down the palette and brush, pulls himself together as if regaining a new spirit, opens his eyes wide, stands up, and looks at the Deacon) Ah! Why have you come, Deacon Ch'oe! (In a somewhat cold tone) Do you have some business? (P'ilchun and Pak each turn their faces away and chuckle) If you have business, please state it quickly. I also have things to attend to.
Deacon. (Somewhat flustered by the unexpected coldness, stammers)... Uh... there... (Looking around at everyone) Let's pray a little. Let's offer a prayer to God. (As he finishes speaking, the Deacon immediately bows his head. P'ilchun frowns but calmly bows his head. P'irun and Pak conspicuously stand up and stomp around the room. Suddenly, a banging sound is heard outside, and without asking for the master, the left door swings open, and Kim, wearing a tattered student uniform with the badge torn off and a student cap askew, rushes in. He holds a cherry wood stick in his left hand, his face flushed. Pak and P'irun are simultaneously startled to see Kim)
P'irun. Hey! What's going on?
Pak. Hmm- Why are you in such a hurry?
Kim. (Barely calming his panting breath) Things have turned ugly. (Turns his face, sees the Deacon but shows no expression, then turns his head again to look at P'ilchun, who is standing quietly with his head bowed) Now! Sir, Mr. P'ilchun, please flee quickly.
Pak. (Suddenly) Why! Huh? (P'irun and Pak's eyes widen. The room becomes chaotic. P'ilchun quietly raises his head. The Deacon remains <166> with his head on the table)
Kim. It's not 'why', they're arresting people left and right. (The Deacon finally raises his head)
P'ilchun. (In a calm tone) Arresting...
Kim. Yes, it's strange. It's completely different from before. Maybe it's because of that incident that happened in Tokyo recently, I don't know, but anyway, even if it's just a mayfly, anyone who goes out on the street and sings a labor song is arrested wholesale.
Pak. So you couldn't give the street speeches?
Kim. Street speeches? We probably couldn't even give indoor speeches. Just as we were leaving Pagoda Park, before we even reached the front of the Youth Association Hall, a troop of mounted police cut off the procession. (Pak and P'irun show visible signs of anger) So 'Mr. Mun' went up to one of the policemen and seemed to ask something, but what do you know? They immediately beat him indiscriminately, tied him up like a corpse bundle, and took him away.
Pak. (Clenching his fist) What? (P'irun's face turns red, and he jumps up. The Deacon watches this impassively)
Kim. (Also getting worked up) So then, our entire group, screaming, scattered flyers and sang labor songs, breaking through the mounted police line, but what do you know? Next, who knows where so many plainclothes detectives in bright clothes came from? They were just hitting and arresting people here and there, it was chaos.
P'ilchun. So what happened to the twenty-five representatives?
Kim. Yes, you mean the ones who went to Yongsan. Those people already left by streetcar, so they must have all arrived.
P'ilchun. Trouble will start there. Only the excessively radical ones went, so they might have used some threatening tactics.
Pak. Then are all the institutions of this society arranged solely for the benefit of the bourgeoisie? <167>
P'ilchun. What, there's no need to bring up that dusty issue again now.
P'irun. By the way, how did you manage to escape?
Kim. I can get out somehow, but anyway, now- Sirs, this is no time to be dawdling here like this. Over there- Mr. Lee hinted. (Turns to P'ilchun) He said things look difficult for you, sir. If arrested this time, you're likely to be imprisoned. And Mr. Pak, they're heading straight to search your house right now.

Deacon. (Without looking at P'irun, picks up his hat and briefcase from the table) Young people these days are too radical. (P'irun glares fixedly at the back of the Deacon's head. (Takes a step towards the door, then turns back again) Last night, Mr. P'ilchun was a bit excessive, which led to the issue of excommunication, but if you just repent...
P'irun. (Interrupting the Deacon as if to quickly send him off, bows low) Goodbye.
P'ilchun. (Also following P'irun, bows) Goodbye. (The Deacon, about to say something more, exits through the left door) Huh! How amusing. One comes telling me to flee because I'll be arrested, another tells me to repent and confess <168> to receive forgiveness for sins... (Before he finishes speaking, voices are heard outside)

P'ilchun. Father, you've come. Brother... have you been to the hospital? (The Father and Brother say nothing and take chairs around the large table. Father. (After a pause, in a heavy voice) Did you repent of your sins from last night? (P'ilchun silently stares at the canvas.)... Did you realize and repent of the sin of insulting the church like that? The church session is where the servants chosen by God gather to resolve God's work. Your insulting the church is insulting God and God's will. The words, 'Whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven,' were not spoken only to Peter but are words granted by God to His church even now. Then, isn't the church the place in the world that distinguishes between sin and righteousness, with the power to loose and bind? Then shouldn't a church member rightly face the church with a reverent and obedient heart? Moreover, last night, the church didn't call you to condemn you, but merely to rebuke your sin so that you might repent and receive forgiveness before God. Why did you act so violently and speak blasphemous words? Have you realized your sin? Father. Do you not know how great the sin of blaspheming the church is? Have you not read the words, 'Whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit <169> will never be forgiven'? Brother. (In a calm tone) Yes, that is so. From the perspective of Father, who is a pastor, it may appear that way. However, even if he blasphemed the Holy Spirit, it surely wasn't intentional. Brother. (In a slightly piercing tone, tapping the table lightly) Like the church of Laodicea, why are you neither hot nor cold? Have you not read the words, 'I will spit out the lukewarm'?

P'ilchun. Yes. But Father, we must reconsider the very concept of chastity you speak of. Of course, selling love for money—though one cannot really do that—but suppose one did, that would be a sin. That would relate to chastity. But judging the purity of chastity based on the preservation of a part of the body, as you say, Father, is wrong. We cannot know how many tragedies have occurred in the world due to this mistaken notion of chastity. We cannot know how many men are tormented because of this. Right here, too, hasn't an unseen tragedy occurred? Look. P'ilchun. God's existence? I believe in God's existence more certainly than my own existence. (Raises his voice further) Because life means a continuous chain of exceptions arising ceaselessly! Yes, that's right. Because there is also freedom there. My God is a God with 'exceptions'.

No, if the younger brother aligns himself with another woman and doesn't take the childless brother's widow, wouldn't he rather be called a sinner? So look. What is considered a filthy sin here is considered a natural duty not far away, and an act of sin spat upon here becomes, in some countries, a sin *not* to perform. How does loving her become a wrongdoing against God? (Shakes his body pointlessly, as if unable to unify his thoughts due to excessive excitement)

**Summary**

"The Forsaken One" depicts the intense conflict faced by Kang P'ilchun, a young artist from a devout Christian family in 1924 Korea. He is condemned by his pastor father, seminarian brother, and church for his relationship with Yŏngsun, an actress deemed impure, and for his modern, individualistic views challenging traditional religious dogma, particularly regarding sin, chastity, and the nature of God. Simultaneously, P'ilchun is implicated as a leader in socialist-influenced labor strikes and demonstrations, leading to his arrest. The play climaxes with P'ilchun being forsaken by his family and arrested, finding a tragic kinship with Yŏngsun, who has also been ostracized, as they both become "forsaken ones."

Historically, the play is significant for capturing the intellectual and social turmoil of 1920s colonial Korea. It vividly portrays the clash between Western Christian conservatism and emerging modern, potentially socialist, ideologies among the youth. Historians might find interest in its direct engagement with themes of individual freedom versus institutional authority (family, church, colonial police), its critique of traditional morality (especially concerning women's chastity), and its reflection of labor movements and socialist activities during the period, offering a window into the complex social dynamics and ideological struggles under Japanese rule.

時代.   1924의 陽春. 엇던 해 잘난 土曜의 오전 10시경.

무대.   昨秋에 竹添町 뒤ㅅ산 엽헤 半洋式으로 건립한 弼俊家의 응접실 내부. 무대 左便에는 밧그로 통하는 또아가 잇고 右便에는 화실로 통하는 또아가 잇다. 그리고 두 또아 우에는 額緣을 대이지 안은 큼직한 유화가 한 개식 달녀 잇다. 正面 중앙에는 露台로 통하는 넓고 큰 展望 윈도가 활짝 녈녀 잇서서 간간이 밧그로부터 땃뜻한 春風이 실내로 흘너 들어온다. 그 양쪽에는 카―텐을 半쯤 양쪽으로 잣킨 윈도―가 유리창을 닷친 채 잇다. 전망창 안으로 큼직한 畵架가 세여 잇고 그 위에 아마도 그 창을 통하여 뵈이 는 外景을 그리든 것인 듯한 캔바쓰가 아직 畢하지 안은 채로 뇌여 잇다. 그 압헤는 캔 바쓰를 향해서 의자 하나가 뇌여 잇고 그 右便쪽에 서 잇는 족음아한 테불 위에는 팔넷트, 油壺, 繪具, 畵筆 등이 되는대로 벌려 뇌엿다. 무대의 左便 또아 압흐로 큰 원형 테불 하나가 뇌여 잇고 그 우에는 신문 멧 장이 접친대로 언첫고 테불 주위에는 4,5脚의 의자가 뇌여 잇다. 이 응접실은 서재로 겸용함인지 右便 또아 녑흐로는 놉직한 書棚에＜153＞울긋불긋한 책이 잠뿍 들어 잇다. 그리고 실내의 구석구석에는 화분이 알맛게 둘 너 뇌엿다. 좌편 벽에 모자가 걸녀 잇다. (뻴이 울고 개막 후 1분간 무대는 공허하고 주 위는 沈靜. 잠간만에 주인공 弼俊이 몸에 畵服을 닙고 버슨 머리를 힘업시 숙으린 채로 정면 전망창으로부터 서서히 등장해서 左便 큰 테불 녑헤 뇌인 의자에 쓸어 안즈면서 畵服 주머니에서 懷中聖書 한 권을 끄집어내서 테불 위에 쾅 소래 나도록 집어침) 그리 고는 머리를 테불에 숙으려 대엿다가 한참만에 기―ㄴ 한숨을 쉬며 머리를 듬.
弼俊. 아-이-... 세상에는 미런한 놈들만 뫼앗다. 웨 제 손으로 저 묵기를 그대지도 조와한단  말이야. 사람이란 것이 따우에서 아믈거리기를 시작해서 오늘날까지 햇다는 것은 전혀 저희 자신을 묵글 큰 결관바를 꼰데서 지니지 못한다... 그럿트래도 저희들끼리나 목고 묵기우고 할 것이지 웨 나로 나를 묵지 안으려는 나까지 악한 놈이요 죄인이라고 떠들어 댄단 말이야... (머리를 가배압게 흔들어 무슨 긍정의 의사를 표하면서 니러남) 그럿타. 싸화야 하겟다. (畵架를 향해서 거러가면서 떨니는 비장한 음성으로) 아― 하나님과 싸화야 하겟구나. (발을 콩 하고 구른다. 弼俊이 畵架를 향해서 그 우에 뇌인 캔바쓰를 바로 잡어놋코 왼손에 팔넷트와 畵筆 세 개를 끼여들고 바른손에 큰 畵筆 하나를 쥐면서 잠간 뒤로 물너서서 캔바쓰를 엿불 때에 봄소식을 전하는 어린아이의 버드나무 피리소리가 전망창을 통하여 흘너들어온다. 弼俊이는 눈에 광채가 나며 손에 들엇든 것은 전부 테불 위에 나려 놋코 전망창 엽헤 가서 피리 소래 나는 편을 향해서 기대선다. 잠간 침묵. 피리 소리만 길게 길게 들님. 弼俊의 얼골에 홀연 누구를 발견한 듯한 빗치 뵈인다.)
弼俊. 너 발서 어듸 갓다오느냐? 필운아. (밧게서 걸어 들어오는 사람의 步調가 반사되는 것  처럼 머리를 좌편에서부터 우편으로 점점 돌님)
弼雲. (아직 무대에 나타나지 안코) 벌섭닛가? 형님은 어듸 갓섯습닛가. 나는 형님을 차저   나갓다가 맛나지 못하고 그냥 관수동에 들어갓다 왓지요. (좌편 윈도―를 통해서 弼雲이 천천이 걸어 들어오는 모양이 뵈인다)
弼俊. 마득해서 어듸 다라낫스리라구. 그래 아침밥 먹엇던?
弼雲. 네. (전망창 압헤 전신을 나타냄. 어느 전문학교 제복에 모자는 썻스나 帽標는 떼엿다.  형과 상대해서 마즌 편 윈도―씰에 기대서며)
저는 종노에 들어가서 호떡을 사 먹엇습니다마는 형님 무얼 좀 잡수섯습닛가.
弼俊. 나 말이냐... 참 저 피리 소리가 어듸서 나드냐. 사람의 빼빼 마른 간장을 훌터내는...
弼雲. 저 편 백양나무 숩히얘요. 오늘은 별로두 따뜻하닛가요.
弼俊. (허리를 펴서 바로 서면서 悲惕한 얼골로) 철 업는 어린아이들까지 슬픔을 노래하는   세상이다. ＜154＞
弼雲. (얼골을 돌녀 형의 낫츨 처다 보면서) 형님은 웨 그러케 신경이 과민하게 되엿서요.   무얼요, 그까짓 사건을... 아모 념려 마르서요. 외눈깔 셋 뫼인데 성한 눈 가진 사람이 가면 병신이란단 셈으로 그 년석들은 형님을 저이들과 갓지 안타고 죄인이라고 합지요. 그러나 그럿타고 성한 눈을 일부러 한 쪽을 빼여 버리고 외눈깔이 되여서 그 놈들한테 병신이란 말을 안드르려고 한다면 그것은 도로혀 어리석은 일이닛가요.
弼俊. 그럿타. 뜬 눈을 일부러 감을 필요는 업다. 들어오나라. (큰 탁자 압헤 와서 의자에 걸  처 안는다. 弼雲도 뒤를 따라 들어와서 형과 상대해서 안즈면서 니약이를 계속한다)
弼雲. 그런데 형님. 그 년석들이 형님을 출교한댑지요. 형님을 죄인이라고... 무얼 애통하고   회개하라느니 재빗삽살이를 하라느니 떠들어 대고... 대대로 목사질이나 해먹을 년석들만 뫼엿서.
弼俊. 또 그런 소리 하는구나. 아버지를 생각지 안느냐. 아버지도 목사이시다. 그리고 너와   나는 목사의 아들이 아니냐.
弼雲. (苦笑하면서) 형님 저는 목사의 아들인 까닭으로 그만큼 더 목사에게 대해서 반감을 품게 됩니다.
弼俊. 그것은 참 이상한 관게이다. 목사의 아들이 목사에게 향해서 그러케 반감을 가지게 된  것은.
弼雲. 그것보다도 목사의 아들인 형님이 자긔의 부친 목사에게 출교를 당하게 된다는 것이  더욱 이상한 콘트라스트올시다.
弼俊. (길흔 한숨을 쉬며) 그러나 그것은 목사의 아들이 잘못한 것도 아니오 목사가 잘못한  것도 아니다. 젼인류가 오늘날까지 옹호해 가지고 나려온 도덕 표준의 관념과 또 그것의 변형에서 지나지 못하는 소위 신앙이란 것에 아모도 알지 못하는 큰 결함이 숨겨잇 는 까닭이다. 마... 말할 것 가트면 사람이란 것은 더구나 소위 신자들이란 것은 눈에 요 렌쓰를 쓰고 모든 대상을 관찰하는 것이닛가... 그러닛가 그들의 눈에 빗최는 것은 사물의 본형이 아니오 모다 말너빠진 가상에서 지나지 못하는 것이야. 그러나 그네는 이것을 진상으로 녁여서 누구든지 이것은 부인하는 자만 잇다면 즉시 그를 ＜155＞ 죄인이요 악한 자라고 한우님 압헤 형벌바들 자라고 떠들어대닛가 이것이 딱하단 말이다.
弼雲. 그 이즈러진 도덕을 옹호하는 무리가 형님을 죄인이랍지요. 그 년석들은 우리들까지   그 이즈러진 척도로 재이려고 해요. 그러닛가 급기야에 동맹휴학밧게 니러날 것이 잇습니가. 형님은 저희가 동맹휴학한 것을 시인합지요.
弼俊. 너는 발서 어늬결에 네 문제를 끄집어 냇구나. 글세 그것도 어늬 방면으로 보아서는   피치 못할 사정이라고 생각한다. 그러나 그것보다는 공부하는 것이 더 조핫슬 듯 하다. 시간에 얼마나 큰...
弼雲. 그럼 형님은 엇더케 되여서 저―긔―(허리를 한 쪽으로 꿉히고 시계를 끄집어 내서   보면서) 지금 찜해서는 빠꼬다근처에서 야단이 니러낫슬 것이올시다. 각 전신주에는 과격 문서가 부텃슬 것이고요.
弼俊. (非常히 놀내며) 무어?... 그건 무슨 소리냐?
弼雲. (숭물스럽게) 그리구요. 숫한 직공들이 시위운동인가를...
弼俊. 그건 또 무슨 소리냐?
弼雲. 무얼요. 형님이 저보다도 더 잘 아실걸. 형님이 주모자라는 말을 들엇는데요.
弼俊. 내가 련루자?... 어듸서 들엇느냐... 아― 세상이란 곳에서는 참으로 자리밋헤서 한 말  이 천리를 가는 모양일다. 그래 너는 어듸서 들엇느냐.
弼雲. 무얼 지금 상일이 집에를 가닛가 다―들 알든데요.
弼俊. 별노 주모자라고까지는 되지 안는다마는 요전번 회에 참예해서 얼마 의견을 말한 일  은 잇지. 그러나 내 뜻은 시위운동을 하고 과격문서를 배포할려고는 하지 안엇다. 온건한 수단으로 민중을 리해하게 할녀고 햇지. 그러나 그네는 끗까지 자긔네의 뜻대로 비밀히 과격행동을 할려고 햇지.
弼雲. 그러니 무얼합닛가. 사람들이 발서 다―들 형님을 제일 괴수로 보는데...
弼俊. (그 소리가 싯그럽다는 듯이) 그건 그러구 그것보담도... (길흔 한숨을 쉬며 무슨 沈思  의 안색으로) 그것보담도... 나는 ＜156＞ 엇지되면 래일 출교당할 사람이다. 너도 래일부터는 붓그러워서 례배당에 가지 못하게 될는지도 모르겟다.
弼雲. 왜요. 사람이 사람을 사랑하는데 무엇이 잘못 되엿드랍닛가. 무슨 죄가 출교식힐 죄라  는가요. 무얼 출교당하는 것이 끔찍한 일은 아니올시다마는 죄가 잇다니 말이얘요.
弼俊. 그러나 내게는 지극히 쓰린 일이다... 그네는 자긔네가 쥐고 잇는 선악에 대한 고정한  척도로 나를 재여보랴닛가 그럿케 되지.

弼俊. (苦笑하면서) 그것도 한 조건은 될 것이다. 그러나 그것보다도 부모의 허락을 밧지 안  코 또 그 교회의 의견도 들어보지 안코 또 무엇보다도 정식의 혼례를 집행하지 안코 그... 비밀히... 그― 마― 그것이 큰 죄가 될 것이다. (소래를 더 한칭 놉혀서) 큰 죄가 되여뵈일 것이다.

弼雲. (히스데리 的으로) 여보, 여보 여보... (二 巡査 弼雲을 돌녀다 봄. 同時에 左便 또아가 열니면서 英淳이 登場. 容貌美麗 검은 치마 힌 저고리를 입고 그대지 華麗하게는 입지 안엇스나 발목까지 올녀 묵근 編上靴 위로부터 무릅 밋까지 낫타난 洋襪은 分明히 高價의 것이엇다. 弼俊, 弼雲, 英淳 三人은 一時에 非常히 놀낸다)
英淳. (暫間 意外의 驚愕으로 뒤로 물너섯다가 홱 달녀드러 結縛된 弼俊에 팔을 붓잡으면서 驚愕과 悲哀가 사뭇친 語調로) 아- 필준씨 이것이 웬 일임닛가. (二 巡査 當惑한 빗으로 이 모양을 물끄럼이 본다. 英淳은 愛人을 안 내여 노으려는 듯이 붓잡고 무릅을 꿀면서 울음 셕긴 목소래로) 네? 이거 웬 일이서요...
弼俊. (悽絶한 顔色으로 英淳이를 나려다 보면서 떨니는 音聲을 억지로 참으면서)  아- 영순씨... 영순씨 너무 마음을 상하시게 해서 무어라고 햇스면 조흘지 모르겟습니다. 그러나 영순씨 아모 걱정을 마르십시요. 오늘 시위 운동때문예 잠간 불너가는 터이니... 그러나 영순씨는...
英淳. 네 저는 지금 바로 니약이 엿줄랴고 나온 길이얘요. 어적게 나왓습니다. 나왓다느니보다는 쫏겨낫습니다... (말이 맛추지 못하고 얼골을 숙으리며 그윽히 늣겨 운다.)
弼俊. 아- 잘 되엿습니다. 영순씨도 쫏겨 나시고 저도 쫏겨 낫스닛가... 네- 버림을 바든 자의  무리는 새로운 락원을 차즙시다. (忽然 갓가이서 喪輿 소리 들님) 아- 상여 소리가 들닙니다. 인생을 조상하는 애처로운 상여 소리가 들닙니다. 엇지면 또 새 나라를 차저가는 깃붐의 노랜지도 모릅니다. (乙 巡査 얼골을 찡그리며 捕繩을 잡아 다린다. 弼俊 한 거름 압흐로 내여 듸듸며 자- 그러면 영순씨 안녕히 게십시요... 아니 곳 다녀올 터닛가요. (弼俊 또 한 거름 또아를 향한다. 英淳은 弼俊을 떠러지며 뒤로 비틀비틀 쓰러질련다.  弼雲이 뒤에서 支持한다. 英淳, 늣겨 운다. 弼俊, 다시 돌아서서 英淳을 본다. 얼골에 말 못할 絶痛한 氣色이 뵈인다. 때맛츰 下幕) ＜178＞
