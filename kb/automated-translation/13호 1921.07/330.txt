**Body Text Translation:**
Setting Sun, Prize Essay Publication, Selected Short Prose, 3rd Place
						Chŏngju County, No Munhŭi

The rain has cleared. From the western sky where pieces of cloud drift, a clear, faint breeze blows. The summer sun, almost resting on the western peak, sheds a light crimson hue, beautifully illuminating the mountains and fields. In the grove at sunset, where the green branches seem oiled, only the clear song of the oriole echoes through the garden. <17>

Beneath Ch'ŏndae Mountain (天台山), the two or three houses of the fishing village are already submerged in ash-grey evening mist, seeming to await the fishing boats returning from afar on the evening tide. Is the cry of the seagull soaring high in the mid-sky a call for its mate? An old fisherman, enjoying the same leisure as heaven and earth, still lingers by the green reeds of the stream bank, while cowherds, leaning on their cows, exchange unknown sorrowful songs (愁心歌). Leisurely scenery, leisurely sounds—amidst this, the only one seen bustling about is the farmer shouldering a spade (鍤).

I have just returned from fishing and sit alone in the shade of the pine garden (松庭). The scenery before my eyes is endlessly clear and beautiful, seeming to dance with the full joy of burgeoning midsummer. Worldly thoughts that cluttered my narrow chest now melt away like spring snow, and suddenly my silent heart-zither (心琴) seems to play a fine melody of its own accord. My heart, growing clearer and newer, melts and melts, trying to unite with nature, which grows more beautiful the more I look. Flowing from my lips is a long poem by Yi Ch'ŏksŏn (李謫仙) [Li Bai]; in my eyes is the painted scroll (畵幅) of creation (造化), and in my ears, the music of nature (天然). While I had forgotten myself and was intoxicated by this scene for a time, my young sibling suddenly returned from school and wandered in the front garden, caressing three or four peonies (芍藥) drinking the evening dew. Drunk on the newly bursting buds and flowing fragrance (芳香), the way they occasionally press their lips to the petals is like a single painting brushed by a world-class master (天下一手 名畵) with unrestrained genius (天才爛漫). Ah, the heavenly innocence (天眞) filling that young heart is truly like a rosebud about to bloom. Suddenly,

"Sister, look at that! A rainbow. One end rests on the peak of Hwanggyŏng Mountain (黃鏡山), and the other end is over the stream, just like a crescent moon... Ah, it's so lovely! Pretty fairies (仙女) must be bathing (沐浴) in that stream, right? Coming down from heaven riding that rainbow... It would be nice to go see. Sister?" a clear and rather fast voice is heard. Then, they run towards where I am. Smiling, I looked at the southeastern sky. Indeed, a double rainbow of red and blue! I grasped my sibling's hand tightly and said,

"What, fairies! You're repeating grandmother's old tales, aren't you?..." I stopped speaking and just smiled. Seeing my sibling's intensely curious expression, I couldn't help but tell the story and gave a detailed explanation about rainbows. The way they nodded their hatted head, looking delighted (滋味로워하는), became more and more apparent. For a while, we held hands and strolled through the flower garden.

The evening mist gradually deepens. The oriole's song flows again. The rainbow has suddenly hidden itself within the rose-colored clouds.

Ah, be intoxicated, be intoxicated, be intoxicated! By the wonder and mystery... of all <18> nature possesses! Nature where only joy and freedom dance!! ......(End)..... <19>

**Summary**

This lyrical essay, awarded third place in a writing contest, describes the author's experience appreciating the beauty of nature after a rain shower at sunset in a rural setting. The author observes the scenery, listens to the sounds of nature and village life, and feels a deep sense of peace and connection, momentarily forgetting worldly concerns. This feeling is shared and amplified by the innocent wonder of a younger sibling who marvels at a rainbow, prompting the author to reflect on the pure joy found in nature. The piece ends with an ecstatic call to be intoxicated by the mystery and freedom of the natural world. From a historical perspective, this piece from 1921, published in the influential magazine *Kaebyŏk*, is interesting for its apparent apolitical focus on aesthetics and emotion during a turbulent period in Korea under Japanese rule. It might reflect a form of escapism or a deliberate turn towards nature and inner feeling as a response to the colonial situation, a trend potentially valued by the literary establishment of the time as indicated by the prize.
