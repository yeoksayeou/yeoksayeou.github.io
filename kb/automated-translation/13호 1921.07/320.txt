Publication Date: 1921년 07월 01일
Article Title: 希望論, 懸賞文發表 論文 選外 (Discourse on Hope, Submitted for Essay Contest - Not Selected)
Author: 京城 桂洞 鄭紀源 (Chŏng Ki-wŏn from Kye-dong, Kyŏngsŏng [Seoul])
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

Discourse on Hope, Submitted for Essay Contest - Not Selected
						Chŏng Ki-wŏn from Kye-dong, Kyŏngsŏng

Within the universe, there exists a kind of mysterious vital energy (精氣) that operates amidst the profound and obscure. Its form is exceedingly indistinct (渾渾), such that though one sees, there is no shape; though one hears, there is no sound; though one smells, there is no odor; though one lifts, there is no weight. With our physical eyes, we cannot possibly imagine or depict its actions and form. However, when its scope is large, it envelops heaven and earth, encompassing all existence; when small, even the single flight or crawl of a larva or tiny winged creature does not occur without relying upon it. Generally, among all things in the universe that inhale air and breathe space, there is none that does not possess this.

Ah! How valiant is this thing! How great is this thing! If dispersed and expanded, it fills the universe, inspiring the masses. If gathered and contracted, it does not fill the grasp, its traces concealed. However, because the way of its operation is extremely profound, its appearance and disappearance are自在 (free), its expansion and contraction inconstant (無常). It advances and retreats according to the times, comes and goes in response to opportunity. Therefore, regardless of who it is, one who skillfully manages and uses this can make the declining prosper, the severed continue, the weak strong, and the ruined rise. Ah! How mysterious is this thing! How great is this thing!!

Heaven and earth are established by this, the multitude of beings develops by this, the stars and constellations revolve by this, the four seasons begin and end by this, wind and rain gather and scatter by this, nations rise and fall by this, peoples flourish and decline by this, society advances and retreats by this, human culture waxes and wanes by this. Ah! You are truly the warp and woof (經緯) of heaven and earth, the master of the three luminaries (三光; sun, moon, stars), the supreme commander (總司令官) of all existence in the universe! Ah! How grand!! Ah! How mysterious!! <9> What is this thing? It is called Hope. Generally, living beings all survive by possessing hope. Then, what is hope? It refers to the psychological function wherein living beings postulate in the future the things they wish to do and become, and plan (企圖) to reach that goal. Regardless of who one is, everyone hopes that their future situation and circumstances will be better than the present; there surely is no one who wishes to become worse off than they are now. The poor will hope to become rich (가멸옵게); the lowly will hope to become high (놉히); the ignorant will hope to become wise; those afflicted by illness, disaster, or other misfortune will hope to escape these unfavorable demons and live a pleasant life; not only do they hope, but they truly believe (밋나니라). If each person knew for certain that their future situation would be worse than the present, I believe (밋노라) there would be many who would not continue this heartless life in this sea of suffering. For that reason, those with abundant wealth hope to become richer, those with high official rank and nobility hope to rise higher, those advanced in age hope to live even one day longer, and those with outstanding scholarship hope to discover even one more principle. Ah! Truly, hope is limitless (際限이 無할 것이다).

Behold! Even a single stalk of flowering grass growing before the courtyard—its withering, flourishing, blooming, and falling (凋翠開落) are not merely natural. Even a single snail (蝸牛) crawling on the ridge of a rice paddy—its movements and pauses are decidedly not thoughtless. How much less could humanity, which governs the transformations of the universe, lack hope? From this point, I shall state (陳述) the relationship between human life and hope, and ask you to see what kind of influence it has on our lives.

Generally, since humanity's reason, knowledge, and skills make them the hegemon (覇王) of the living world, it goes without saying that their hopes and demands also surpass those of other beings. Therefore, since the origin of humanity, the development of society and the progress of civilization have all been conceived, nurtured, and grown (孕育滋長) within this single line of hope's light, reaching the present day. In the past, countless sentient beings over millions of years lived and died within this one line; the present humanity before our eyes also rushes about (奔走) within this one line; and in the future, countless sentient beings over millions of years will also live and die within this one line. Seen from this perspective, hope is truly the life of human existence, the sustenance (糧食) of humanity.

Just as where there is a flower, there is fruit, and where there is a root, there are leaves, where there is hope, reality is realized (實現되나니). Therefore, the world was created through the hope of the Supreme Being, nations are established through the hope of the populace, descendants are produced (産出) through the hope of ancestors, civilization is born (誕生) through the hope of barbarians, innovation arises from the hope of the stubborn, and strength is obtained through the hope of the weak and remnant. Ah! Great is hope! The farmer's thousands of barns and chests are the courageous effort of a single plowshare; the fisherman's five lakes and three rivers are the success of a single fishing net; the hundred-year endeavor of human life is the result of a single hope. Looking afar, even the sun, moon, and stars in the blue sky revolve through hope; looking near, even a single blade of grass or insect before the steps <10> grows through hope. Therefore, I propose (提言하노라)! I declare (宣言하노라)! We "can live without having bread (麵麭) or beef, but we cannot live without holding (不持) hope." Where there is no hope, there is no thought; where there is no thought, there will be no progress. In a society without progress, what shall we seek, what shall we hope for? Therefore, in a society where hope is scarce, thought is scarce, and where thought is scarce, great happiness cannot be enjoyed. The faces of a people who harbor sound thoughts display sound vitality, while weak spirits linger in the minds of a nation carrying corrupt desires. Therefore, one can infer (推測하기 能하고) the advance or retreat of a society from the thoughts of its people, and one can determine (斷定할 수 有하나니) the flourishing or decline of a people from the hopes of individuals. In essence, it is no exaggeration to say that the prosperity, decline, expansion, and contraction of a nation or society depend on (如何에 在하다) the thoughts and hopes of the individuals forming that nation or society.

One who possesses this, no matter how tattered (襤褸) their clothes or difficult their life, satisfaction appears in their breast, and warmth radiates (爛漫하다) from their countenance. However, one who lacks (乏) this, no matter how abundant their wealth or comfortable their dwelling, their heart is filled with pain and struggles (戰鬪) with anguish. Therefore, pain flees (敗走) before hope, and anguish is conquered (征服된다) behind hope. Ah! Where there is hope, what is there to worry about, what is there to lament? Generally, future happiness lies not in immediate success or failure, but in the presence or absence of hope. Therefore, though the paulownia grove (桐藪) of the Koryŏ Dynasty (麗朝) was more precarious than the abalone (鮑魚) of the Silla King (羅王), because hope did not fall (不墜), there was no disaster of perilous ruin. Though Goujian's (句踐) Mount Kuaiji (稽山) was more miserable and cruel (慘酷) than Fuchai's (夫差) Yongdong (甬東), because hope was not lacking (不乏), there was no worry of destruction. Though the Jewish people (猶太民族) lived scattered and separated (支離析居) in all directions, becoming pitiful people (哀民) of a lost nation, because their firm ideal did not cease, do we not occasionally see extraordinary news published in the newspapers even today, thousands of years later?

People live by their own sense of worth, not by the worth of others; people live by their own hope, not by the hope of others. Anyone who wishes to pioneer (開拓) the happiness of a lifetime and manage a beautiful life must first pour their full strength into their own hope, protect (擁護) it well, and implement it. If one has hope but cannot implement it, they will not avoid (不免) the lament of dragon-slaying (屠龍之歎; possessing a useless skill). Therefore, if we are to properly fulfill our inherent hope and arrive at the final other shore (彼岸), we must, to whatever extent (어대까지던지), hold fast to our will (굿세게) and develop our bodies (新軆) sturdily (튼튼히). No matter how strong the will, if the body is not healthy, it will leave (遺) not a few (不少) hindrances and obstacles (障碍) to implementation and achievement.

Generally, the individual body (個軆), while being a single molecule organizing the universe, is also a member of human society, and while being a single citizen <11> forming the nation, is also a member of various small societies. Thus, as a member of each group (團軆), if one intends to strive for that group or society, the body must first be healthy.

If the body is not healthy, in the eyes of such a person, the whole world is pessimistic, and all things are cold-hearted. What contribution (貢獻) could such a person give (與) to the nation or society? If the body is weak (萎弱) and requires the protection and support (扶腋) of others, no matter how lofty an ideal or transcendent a talent one possesses, one cannot possibly manifest and implement it in reality. Due to this, that society or group will invite decline and ruin (衰廢), while progress and development will be difficult to hope for. Therefore, since ancient times, figures who concealed great ideals and contributed to the evolution of the universe and the development of society were, to whatever extent, healthy in body.

Among hopes, there are proper and improper ones, appropriate (適當) and inappropriate ones. The proper one welcomes glory (榮華), but the improper one invites shame (恥辱). If used in an appropriate place, merit is achieved, endeavors prosper, the family flourishes, and society thrives. But if used in an inappropriate place, it conversely disgraces the self, ruins the name, destroys the family, and causes the nation to decline and fall into ruin. Therefore, in employing and expressing it, one must certainly comply with (順應) the surrounding circumstances and the general trend of the world, ensuring there is no disaster of going against the current and acting perversely (逆流倒行). To reach the distant, one must certainly begin from the near; to climb (陟) the high, one must certainly proceed from the low; to aspire to the difficult, one must certainly start from the easy. If one is hazy (朦朧) with philosophical abstraction and cannot conform (順合) to practical realities, there will be no gain (所得) whatsoever, but conversely, only infinite agony will be left behind. Therefore, if a child is to eat an adult's meal and do adult work, they must certainly accumulate the corresponding age, strength, and experience. If barbarians are to enjoy the happiness and rights of civilized people, they must certainly possess the corresponding effort, morality, ideal, and will. If one idly (徒然히) indulges (耽惑) in superficial vanity (皮相的 虛榮) and is ignorant (暗昧) of the fundamental path forward, that society or group will overturn its carriage and break its ruts, unable to avoid the regret of biting one's navel (噬臍之悔; repentance too late).

The Nature of Hope
People's hopes are not identical. Just as individuals' faces are not the same, individuals' hopes are also each different (相異). Therefore, even with the closeness (親) of parent and child, the friendship (友) of siblings, the love (愛) of husband and wife, or the trust (信) of friends, what each individual desires in their heart will all be different. How could it only be so between individuals? Advancing further, even among groups, societies, nations, or peoples, it is also like this. Therefore, civilized people have the hopes of civilized people, and barbarians have the hopes of barbarians; aristocrats have the hopes of aristocrats, and commoners <12> have the hopes of commoners; scientists have the hopes of scientists, and religious figures have the hopes of religious figures. The hopes of aristocrats will not be the same as the hopes of commoners; the hopes of religious figures will differ from the hopes of politicians. Presidents have the hopes of presidents, and vice presidents have the hopes of vice presidents; capitalists have the hopes of capitalists, and laborers have the hopes of laborers; youths have the hopes of youths, and the elderly have the hopes of the elderly; men have the hopes of men, and women have the hopes of women. The hopes of capitalists differ from the hopes of laborers, and the hopes of youths are dissimilar to the hopes of the elderly. Raising the perspective (眼度) higher to discuss the peoples of the world: the British (英國人) have the hopes of the British, the Americans (米國人) have the hopes of the Americans, the Germans (獨逸人) have the hopes of the Germans, the Russians (露國人) have the hopes of the Russians, and Koreans (朝鮮人) have the hopes of Koreans. It is the fixed principle of nature that the inclinations (趨向) of Americans and British clash (衝突), and the interests (意趣) of Germans and Russians contradict (牴牾) each other. Therefore, individuals have their inherent thoughts, and nations have their inherent characters. Behold! Is it not the hope of Americans to make industry magnificent (壯麗), expand commercial power (商權), and surpass (凌駕) the six continents? Is it not the hope of the British to discover unseen islands (島嶼), conquer (征服) undeveloped peoples, and command the world with the Anglo-Saxon (앵글로쌕손) people? Is it not the hope of the Russians to advocate (鼓吹) socialist thought and whip (鞭撻) all nations on earth? Then, having reached this point, I ask! I ask! I ask about the hope of us Koreans. What is our hope? Does it exist? Does it not? Ah! Where there is land, people, language, history, customs, script, and thought, how could there not be hope to guide the path forward for human life? Reaching this point, my chest trembles with fear, and I am unaware (不覺) of tears pouring down into my collar (襟中). I briefly ask my young brothers politely (공손히): What is your hope?! Is holding hands and accompanying (握手同伴) pretty women your hope? Is wearing splendid Western clothes, gold-rimmed glasses, gaiters (기트구쓰), and dragging a high-collared (하꾸라이) short cane (短杖) your hope? Is adorning your stylish appearance (맵시) with gold watches and jeweled rings, spending your life in a carriage by day (晝) and a restaurant (料理집) by night (夜) your hope? Is drinking alcohol, playing Go (바독), composing poetry, singing songs, and appreciating nature in the spring breezes and autumn rains your hope? I am not slandering this, nor am I spitting and cursing (唾罵) it. I can believe (미들 수 잇다) that the entirety of your hope is not exposed (露出) solely within these things.

Ah! The green waters of the Han River (漢江) flow abundantly, the blue pines of Namsan (南山) are dense and lush, but on the high ridge of Pugaksan (北岳山), only white clouds come and go. Ah! With whom should I speak (語할지거나) of our history and our future path?!

Here, I intend to discuss the living conditions of Koreans and awaken (喚起) the self-awareness of our youth. Looking at the state of affairs in our society today, <13> the structure of residences (居宅) consists of shacks like crab shells, with rafters (석가래) sparsely (엉성하게) hung on the four corners. The complexions of the populace show malnutrition, revealing a sickly pallor (菜色). Looking up at the mountains, only rocks tower (嵬嵬) over a thousand *li* of red earth. Looking out at the fields, agriculture is rudimentary, and more than half (太半) of the plains and wilderness (曠野) are desolate (荒廢). Even the cultivated fields have poorly maintained furrows and plots (畎畝), so when heavy rain passes, covering sand (覆沙) forms mounds. Though mineral ores are abundant, because the mining industry is undeveloped, profits are monopolized (壟斷) by foreigners. Because the weaving industry (織業) is not promoted (振興), the materials for the clothing of our people are entirely dependent on (懸) the factories of other countries. Because education is not widespread, one-fifth of the population cannot even properly write their own three-character names. Ah! How sad (슯흐다)! How can we preserve the dignity and status of our people like this?

Think! Think! Think of that Three Kingdoms Period (三國時代)! Thinking of the lives our ancestors lived and examining (삷혀보니) the present situation, I again cannot stop (禁치 못하노라) the thoughtless tears. Among our ancestors, there were genius creators, inventors, great politicians, and skillful and special (巧特) diplomats.

Think! Think! The mountains and rivers of that Three Kingdoms Period are just today's mountains and rivers; the scenery of the Three Kingdoms Period is just today's scenery; the people of the Three Kingdoms Period are just today's people. Then, the outstanding character and transcendent originality of our kin must also be inherited and flowing latently in our blood vessels. Why, then, are the living conditions and degree of civilization between past and present so disparate? This is nothing else but that the degree of hope is different. If we possessed the aspirations, intelligence, and courage of our ancestors, we could all become the Koguryŏ's (高句麗) Kae So-mun (*髥崢嶸) with his magnificent beard, or Taebong's King Kung Ye (弓裔) (泰封王) with his single piercing eye (獨眼炯炯), or Master Ch'oe Ch'i-wŏn (崔致遠) who made the price of paper in Luoyang (洛陽) dear, or Silla's (新羅) Great Master Sol Kŏ (率居), who displayed natural genius and made unique art shine (빗나이던). Once hope arrives (一到), what undertaking can it not achieve (何事를 不成가)?

I ask: Among the gentlemen of purpose (有志紳士) in our society, how many great educators are there, zealous in education, who will cast (鑄造) the figures of the future? How many great writers (操觚家) are there, with superior knowledge and lofty ideals, capable of enlightening (啓發) the wisdom of the entire nation? Are there great philosophers? Great literary figures? Great philanthropists? Great adventurers? Great politicians? When I think of this, I just gaze longingly (長望) at the thoughtless white clouds floating away in the western sky. However, I absolutely do not despair (悲觀하지 아니하노라)! I do not lose hope (落望하지 아니하노라)! I do not grieve (슯허하지 아니하노라)! I am optimistic! I dance! I laugh! I sing! Because industry is undeveloped, I hope for industry to be promoted; because education is not widespread, I hope for education to be disseminated; because science is not advanced, I fervently hope for science to progress; because great men and patriots (偉人烈士) have not appeared, <14> I fervently hope for great men and patriots to be born as soon as possible. Ah! In a society where there is hope, what should we worry about? Our society is truly a thicket (叢藪) of hope, a stage for undercurrents (暗潮). On the ruined castles and defeated ramparts (殘城敗壘), the spring grass is already thick; on the overgrown terraces (蕪臺), plum blossoms are just blooming (梅花가 正發). I greatly leap with courage (大勇躍)! I greatly rejoice (大歡喜)! I greatly praise (大讚美)! I dance greatly (大舞蹈)!

Sitting beside the cold corpse of a 70- or 80-year-old, whose breath has stopped and cold air whistles from their nostrils, no matter how much one presses cheek to cheek and wails all day, they will never open their eyes again. No matter how well artificial respiration is performed, there is absolutely no chance of revival. However, our society today is not an old person of 70 or 80 with white hair and lost teeth (皓首落齒), but an innocent child of 5 or 6. It is not an old bee shivering in the frost and cold of the 9th or 10th month, but a beautiful butterfly (美蝶) seeking (探問) flower fragrance in the warm spring of the 2nd or 3rd month. Ah! The grand stage to test our sturdy arms is not far off! In this vast world (快濶), shedding the nine layers of cloud skin (九層雲皮), let us leap forward courageously with lively steps (活步), seek knowledge widely throughout the world, visit (周訪) learning from ancient and modern times everywhere, and earnestly pray to enjoy uniform (均一) happiness together with all the peoples of the world.

Generally, pain is the compassionate mother (慈母) of hope, and struggle is the embryo (胚胎) of success. Where there is no necessity, how can invention arise? In a society without pain, how can hope come? Therefore, intimidation (威嚇), oppression (壓迫), and pain are the headquarters (大本營) that nurture hope. Thus, if we make castle walls (城郭) with struggle and oppression, build ramparts (墻垣) with sincerity, diligence (誠勤), and courage, and then, pouring all our strength, advance bravely (勇往邁進), all things in the universe will surely submit (屈伏) before it. Behold! Though the steps (步武) of that earthworm (蚯蚓) are slow and dull (遲鈍), if it does not rest, it can cross even Mount Tai. Though dust is minute (纖微), if gathered day by day and accumulated month by month, there will come a time when it can contend in weight with the Nine Tripods (九鼎). How much more so for our human society, full of change and flux? We should only worry (患) about lacking diligence, and not calculate (計) the coming and going (去來) of happiness; we should only reproach (責) ourselves for lacking courage, and not ask about the success or failure of endeavors.

However, human society is not always enveloped in happiness and joy. Often, a cruel thing that runs counter to (背馳) hope appears, attempting infinite obstacles to our forward progress in life. What is this? Despair is precisely it. Though there are many unhappy things in our lives, there is no unhappier thing than despair. Therefore, when hope departs (謝去) and despair confronts (當前), ten thousand pounds (磅) of gold are not sufficient (足) to bring joy to that body, nor can brocade clothes, jade food, pleasant sounds, and beautiful sights sufficiently comfort (慰藉) that mind and body. Thus, when T'anhyŏn pass (炭峴) lost its strategic advantage (失險) and the old capital was overthrown (顚覆), even the beloved concubine (愛姬) of Paekche's (百濟) royal palace, who knew no sorrow (憂愁) under the king's favor (恩寵), wept her fragrant soul away on Nakhwaam Rock (落花岩). When Kung Ye (弓裔) committed atrocities freely (虐を肆) and Kyŏn Hwŏn followed, rising up (繼起), and there was utterly no plan (百爾無策) for the recovery of the homeland, even the noble sons (貴公子) of Silla (新羅), who stood solemnly in attendance (儼然 侍立) to the left and right of the Dragon Pavilion (龍樓), believing future wealth and honor to be their sole possession (惟我の所有), blessed their own monk's hair (僧髮を自祝) in Mount Chirisan (智離山). Ah! How sad (슯흐다), Despair! How pitiful, Despair! In the camp at Gaixia (垓下), the Songs of Chu (楚歌) arose from all four sides, <15> under the dark moon (黑月) at Yinling (陰陵), the dapple-grey horse (騅馬) would not advance (不進). Though he was the Hegemon-King of Western Chu (西楚覇王) who had encompassed (囊括) the nine provinces through eight years of wind and dust (風塵), could he heed the loyal advice of the Wu River (吳江) Pavilion Chief (亭長)? On the desolate lone island of Saint Helena (센트헤레나), his person was imprisoned. Where did the footsteps (縱跡) of the hero who once rampaged through Europe on a single horse fly off to? Only evil fog and black miasma (惡霧黑瘴) accompanied the white clouds, coming and going in mid-air. Though he was Napoleon (나폴레온), could he help but sing sad songs and sigh, looking up (仰歎) at the fate of Heaven? Ah!! How dreadful is Despair! You are truly the commander-in-chief (總大將) of human demons (惡魔). Generally, such great men and patriots (偉人 烈士) are figures who appear intermittently (間出) in ancient and modern humanity. How can their outstanding talent (才幹) and lofty aspirations (迢遞 心志) be compared to ours in terms of years and months? Yet, the end of heroes often leaves such tragic scenes. This is nothing else but that hope was exhausted. Ah! If it is thus even for great men and patriots, how much more so (况) for ordinary people (凡人) like us?

Ah! We should think! Those sun, moon, and stars hanging high in the vast and boundless (渺茫無窮) blue sky have remained unchanged through myriad ages. But our human life, merely a few tens of years (寒暑) after its form appears, when hair turns white (晧白) and muscles weaken, hastens its journey towards heaven. Ah! How sad (슯흐다)! Our life is truly a grain of sand from the Ganges (恒河 片沙), a single millet seed in the blue ocean (蒼海 一粟). Therefore, the wise and accomplished of ancient and modern times transcend (踰越) the individual to reach society, transcend the present time to reach eternity, transcend the physical body to reach the soul (靈魂), do not become slaves (奴隷) of entertainment in the world of song and dance, but see through (看破) the truth of the universe and uniquely possess infinite pleasure. If the highest hope of humanity stopped at that distant (邈邈) six feet (6尺; the grave), Jesus (耶穌) would surely not have sacrificed his body and suffered judgment from God. If the most beautiful hope stopped at this brief instant (刹那), Shakyamuni (釋迦) would surely not have abandoned the exquisite (玲瓏) palace, beautiful gardens, beloved father and wife, jeweled crown and embroidered clothes (繡衣), and headed for the Snowy Mountains (雪山). Seen from this perspective, how could the righteous men and patriots of old, who sacrificed their lives for their country and died for righteousness (義に殉), not know the preciousness of their bodies and lives? It is because they realized the existence of a greater satisfaction and hope beyond that. Ah! Hope! You are the gospel (福音) of society, the living spring (活泉) of the people.

Though it has no weight when lifted, its power can contend (拮抗) with the Nine Tripods. Though it has no form when seen, its authority can control (籠絡) the three armies. Therefore, if one tries to mow down and cut off (芟夷斬絶) this with injustice and violence, even if thrown into the ten-thousand-fathom seabed, even if boiled (烹) in a thousand-fathom cauldron (鑊鼎), even if tempted with a thousand chariots and ten thousand measures (千駟萬斛), the hope of an individual cannot be usurped (剝奪 不能). Even if the sky is swept clean (掃滌) with flying wheels and flying ships (飛輪飛船), cities are attacked and captured (攻拔) with a rain of cannonballs and frost of blades (雨砲霜刃), and people are massacred (屠戮) with sharp swords and long halberds (利釰長戟), the hope of a people cannot be conquered. This is nothing else but that because its established essence has no form, it cannot be cut off; because it has no traces (踪跡) or odor (臭氣), it cannot be chased, removed, or defended against (追逐除防 不能). When it finds the right time, it blossoms and spreads its fragrance, fostering the culture of society. When it encounters misfortune and the path ahead is blocked, <16> it suddenly contracts, solidifying (盤固) its foundation and expanding its branches and leaves amidst hardship and pain, establishing (扶植) its power and recovering its vitality amidst formlessness, soundlessness, colorlessness, and odorlessness, observing the state of the world. Then, one morning, when the wind comes and the waves rise, with the force of the Yellow River flowing a thousand *li* in one go, it envelops heaven and earth, ruling all things. Ah! Who can spy out (窺察) its mysterious unfolding?

Observing the history of East and West, ancient and modern, the rise and fall of nations are like the rotation (自轉) of the earth, and the changes in society are like the circulation (循環) of a water wheel (水輪). Those who act, are diligent, and strive (務) flourish and rise; those who abandon, are lazy (怠), and are arrogant (驕) decline (廢) and perish. We should rightly observe this and exert ourselves (勉勵).

Alas (噫)! The surface of the great ocean is calm, but what stirs up (興湧) the waves is the wind; the minds of the people are bright, but what guides the path forward is solely hope. Where there is hope, reality manifests (發顯); where there is reality, results are born. Therefore, if an individual cherishes and skillfully utilizes this, the foolish become wise, the poor become rich, and the destitute succeed. If a people cultivates and nurtures (培養滋長) this, promoting and expanding (振興擴充) it, the weak become strong, the ruined survive, the perished rise, and the severed continue (續). Ah! Hope is truly the driving force (原動力) governing a society or nation. How could it only be so in human society? From the stars hung high (高縣) in that blue sky to a single blade of grass, a single flower, a single insect, a single bird growing before the steps (階前), they revolve by this, are moved by this, fly and run by this. Therefore, I say: Hope is the Supreme Commander of all things in the universe. Ah! My hopeful (多望) young compatriots!!
(Evening of June 5, 1921)

Critique: Repetition of similar words and lack of variation in explanation seem to be flaws, but the effort expended seems worthy of praise. <17>

**Summary**

This essay by Chŏng Ki-wŏn is a passionate discourse on the power and necessity of hope. He defines hope as the vital, invisible energy driving all existence, from the cosmos to the smallest insect, and especially human life, societal progress, and national destiny. Chŏng argues that hope allows individuals and nations to overcome adversity, poverty, weakness, and despair, citing historical examples like Goujian and the Jewish people. He laments the perceived lack of grand hope and ambition among contemporary Koreans, contrasting their situation with the achievements of their ancestors during the Three Kingdoms period, and urges the youth, in particular, to cultivate strong bodies and lofty, well-directed hopes – focused on education, industry, and national revitalization – rather than superficial pursuits. The essay concludes by reaffirming hope as the unconquerable, essential force for overcoming present difficulties and achieving a brighter future. From a historical perspective, the essay is deeply revealing of the Korean intellectual mood in the early years of Japanese colonial rule (published 1921). It reflects a strong sense of national crisis, critiques contemporary Korean society's perceived stagnation and lack of ambition, yet simultaneously offers a powerful message of resilience and future-oriented optimism, emphasizing self-strengthening and internal development (education, industry, character) as the path forward, even under oppressive circumstances. The call for grand, collective hope implicitly challenges the despair potentially induced by colonization.

噫라. 大洋의 表面은 平穩한데 波濤를 興湧하는 者는 風이요 民衆의 心理는 光明한데 前路를 引導하는 者는 오즉 希望이다. 希望이 有한 곳에 事實이 發顯되고 事實이 有한 곳에 結果가 出生하나니 故로 個人으로 此를 愛護하야 善히 活用하면 愚한 者 智하고 貧한 者 富하고 窮한 者 達하며 民族으로 此를 培養滋長하야 振興擴充하면 弱한 者 强하고 廢한者 存하고 亡한 者 興하고 絶한 者 續할지니 아-希望은 實로 一社會나 國家를 支配하는 原動力이로다. 어찌 人類社會에만 然하리요. 彼 蒼空에 高縣한 星辰으로부터 階前에 生長하는 一草一花와 一蟲一鳥일지라도 此로써 運轉하며 此로써 發動하며 此로써 飛走하나니 故로 나는 曰 希望은 宇宙萬物의 總司令官이라 하노라. 아-多望한 우리 靑年同胞들!!
(1921, 6月 5日 夕)
