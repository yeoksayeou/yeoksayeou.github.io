이런 거룩한 이도 잇도다 (There Are Such Holy People Too)

古代 希臘 「스파타」의 隆盛이 專혀 王族 「리커꺼쓰」의 法則勵行에 基함인 것은 一般이 知하는 바이라. 그런데 傳에 依하면 「리커꺼쓰」는 方場實行코저 하는 그 法令을 頒布하며 國人을 召하야 說明하되 此法이 자못 苛嚴하야 行키 難할지나 余가 自今 諸國을 巡歷하야 諸國의 法制를 視察한 後 歸國하야 곳 改正할 것이니 어려운대로 余의 歸國하는 때까지만 此를 遵守勿犯하라 하고 그는 디듸어 身을 隱하야 다시 出現치 아니하얏다. 그의 생각에는 그 法則을 實行하면 國家는 반듯이 强盛할 터인데 或 行하기도 前에 人民이 反對할가 慮하야 此策을 用하고 自己는 혼자 山中에 入하야 餓死하얏다 한다. 果然 어떠케 거룩한 일인가.＜36＞

It is generally known that the prosperity of ancient Greek (希臘) Sŭp'at'a (Sparta) was entirely based on the diligent implementation of laws by the royal Lycurgus (리커꺼쓰). According to legend, when Lycurgus promulgated the laws he was about to put into practice, he summoned the people of the nation and explained, "Though this law is quite severe and may be difficult to follow, I shall now travel through various countries to observe their legal systems, and upon returning to the country, I will immediately revise it. Therefore, difficult as it may be, just observe and do not violate this law only until the time of my return." Then, he finally hid himself and never appeared again. His thinking was that if the law was implemented, the nation would surely become strong, but worrying that the people might oppose it even before it was put into practice, he employed this plan, entered the mountains alone by himself, and is said to have starved to death. Indeed, what a holy deed this is. <36>

**Summary**

This article recounts the legendary story of Lycurgus, the lawgiver of ancient Sparta. It describes how Lycurgus introduced strict laws, convinced the Spartans to follow them until his return from a supposed journey to study other legal systems, and then deliberately disappeared and starved himself to death to ensure the laws remained permanently in effect for Sparta's future strength. The author concludes by praising Lycurgus's self-sacrifice as a "holy deed." From a historical perspective, the publication of this story in *Kaebyŏk* during the colonial period is notable. It highlights themes of national strengthening through law and extreme self-sacrifice for the state, potentially serving as an indirect commentary or source of inspiration related to Korean circumstances under Japanese rule, using a classical, non-Korean example likely permissible by censors.
