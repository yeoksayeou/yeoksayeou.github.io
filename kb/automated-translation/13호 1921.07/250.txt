破睡漫草 (Idle Jottings to Break Slumber)
						梁白華(拔) (Yang Paekhwa (Pal))
一. 金雲翹傳 (The Tale of Kieu)

Washing the Swallow Tower (燕子樓) in wind and rain, there is no way to mourn the old story of Kuan P'an-p'an (關盻盻); locking the Pear Blossom Courtyard (梨花院) in spring grass, where today can one find the visage of Li Shishi (李師師)? Nevertheless, at the ferry crossing of the Qinhuai River (秦淮), new willows are like mist, a gentle breeze blows through the screen shaped like the character 丁, a lovely throat trills like jade turning, and the bright moon shines on the balustrade shaped like the character 亞, making dance shadows sway. Truly, it is a place of luxury! "South of the river, flowers bloom, the water flows endlessly; arriving at Qinhuai, people forget all sorrow. Regardless of the thousands of miles of misty homeland, in the fifth watch, a singing throat warbles in an embrace." (From the legend of *The Peach Blossom Fan* (桃花扇傳奇)). Alas! Is the land of Qinhuai truly the home of Mochou (莫愁, No Sorrow)? Yet, how can it be helped that it ultimately becomes the gathering place for all the faithless beauties under heaven? Do not judge so harshly. The creatures stirring within the Spring-Moving Railing (移春檻) were not originally born as demons, and those who sell their beauty and charm—this was not originally their true intention. Fate and circumstance force them to throw themselves into the sea of song and music. I reject those who treat the land of Mochou as an impure, defiled place. However, witnessing the circumstances of those who sell beauty, I cannot help but shed a tear.

The tale *Kŭm Un Kyo Chŏn* (金雲翹傳, *The Tale of Kieu*) is the work of Ch'ŏngsim Chaein (靑心才人). Among Chinese novels, it cannot be called much of a masterpiece. However, its depiction of a pure and innocent young woman who, dragged down by surrounding circumstances, finally falls into a world of pleasure quarters (烟花風月地), tastes widespread suffering, and endures various hardships, is certainly not without merit. Only, the ending, which patches up the flawed heaven of sentiment to achieve a grand, celebratory reunion, is a common device of such novels. For readers whose eyes have scanned modern novels and whose hearts cannot be consoled unless by something tragic and profound, there will likely be considerable dissatisfaction. No, creating an ending that should not exist is something the disciples of good literature cannot deeply sympathize with, but when it comes to the skill in narrating the twists and turns of her experiences and the heartlessness of worldly obligations, leading step by step towards a great rupture, there is also something quite remarkable. In China, where the beauty of prose is valued and the plot structure is disregarded, it possesses something rather outstanding. Considering the complexity of *Honglou Meng* (紅樓夢, *Dream of the Red Chamber*), the descent into obscenity of *Jin Ping Mei* (金甁梅, *The Plum in the Golden Vase*), I would rather choose the short and coherently flowing style of *Kŭm Un Kyo Chŏn*. For scholars wishing to appreciate Chinese novels beyond *Shui Hu Zhuan* (水滸傳, *Water Margin*) and *Xi You Ji* (西遊記, *Journey to the West*), I wish to recommend it. Works like *P'yŏngsan Nengyŏn* (平山冷燕), merely filled with poetic verses, and *Okkyori* (玉嬌梨), which is mediocre and common, <122> are originally not worth considering. How much less *Wǔsè Shí* (五色石, *Five-Colored Stone*) or *Jingu Qiguan* (今古奇觀, *Strange Tales Old and New*)? Calling miscellaneous secret affairs alluring and *Feiyan Waizhuan* (飛燕外傳, *Unofficial Biography of Flying Swallow*) wondrously unique—I cannot help but lament this for the sake of Chinese literature.

The single character '情' (chŏng, feeling/passion) is the warp of this book, and the single character '苦' (ko, suffering) is its weft. However, that feeling necessarily arises dependent on circumstance (境), and suffering necessarily arises dependent on encounters (遇), is stated in the commentary signed 'Kim Sŏngt'an' (金聖歎, Jin Shengtan) at the beginning of the first chapter.

Introducing Liu Tamsŏn (一劉淡仙) to serve as an introduction, and from within hazy forms and faint shadows, hinting at the lifelong sentimental suffering (情苦) that Ch'wich'yo (翠翹) will face, bringing it out eight or ninefold—this is the plot structure (脚色) of the first chapter. Observe how it is told.

Wang Ch'wich'yo (王翠翹) is a beauty. Skilled in the *hogeum* (胡琴, a stringed instrument) and clever in poetry and prose, she is the protagonist of *Kŭm Un Kyo Chŏn*. One day during the Qingming Festival (淸明), she takes her younger brother and sister out for a spring outing (踏靑). Walking on and on, she sees a solitary grave mound. Her sister does not know whose it is. Her brother tells her it is the grave of the famous courtesan (名妓) Liu Tamsŏn (劉淡仙); in life, she was the wife of ten thousand men, and in death, she is a ghost without a husband. Ch'wich'yo's long sigh is truly no accident. She then inscribes a poem, saying:
"Where now are beauty and fragrance? / Paying respects, my heart aches! / The cold moon chills the mandarin duck quilt, / Hidden dust seals the mirror stand. / Though jade is buried in yellow earth, / Her name is not yet buried by white clouds. / Though there is wine abundant as the Min River (澠), / No one offers a single cup."

Ch'wich'yo! Standing there for a long while, unable to leave, suddenly a strange gust of wind blows, whirling and scattering around Ch'wich'yo's body. This indicates that the connection (因緣) between Tamsŏn and Ch'wich'yo is not shallow, letting the reader know the origin of the karmic debt (孽債). Its conception is similar to the 108 demon stars escaping from the Demon-Subduing Hall (伏魔殿). Inserting karmic causality (因果主義) as a guiding principle is not uncommon in Chinese novels; even works like *Yinsư Chüeh-hou Ch'an* (淫書覺後禪) have imitated this. Eastern novelists, adhering firmly to this principle, have introduced unnecessary precedents into Korean literature, and its influence has long been transmitted to our literary world. However, karmic causality is excellent for so-called didactic novels (勸懲的 小說); it can make women and unlearned folk believe it as truth, and for those who call instruction the primary meaning of the novel, it is naturally a golden rule. Yet, when even obscene books incorporate this to conceal their ugliness, is it not also a pitiable matter for the karmic novel?

In a state of bewildered abstraction, the sound of hooves clatters, and a handsome young man arrives on horseback. This is a scholar from the same village, surname Kim (金), given name Chung (重), a classmate of Ch'wich'yo's younger brother Wang Kwan (王觀). A talented man and a beautiful woman (佳人才子) meet, thus creating the prelude (楔子) for the later sections.

After returning home, Ch'wich'yo speaks to her younger sister Ch'wiun (翠雲), saying, "Generally, a woman's body, if valued, is like Mount Tai (泰山); if taken lightly, it is like a goose feather (鴻毛). A blue fly on white jade affects one's entire life; how can one not be cautious?" <123> Perhaps this could serve as a motto for all women under heaven.

The second chapter is also foreshadowing (伏線) for the later sections; it too is about karmic causality (因果說). Karmic causality reaches its peak here. Ch'wich'yo's entire life's destiny is seemingly foretold here. Ch'wich'yo meets a beautiful woman in a dream. Lightly adorned in plain clothes, with an apricot face and peach cheeks, graceful and elegant (娉娉婷婷), she looks just like an immortal maiden. She says, "Today at the Heartbreak Assembly (斷腸會), I heard your great talent. The host of the Heartbreak Assembly was overjoyed, knowing you would become a member of the assembly, and sent these ten Heartbreak themes (斷腸題目) to you, requesting poems. Please compose them quickly." The themes were: Lamenting Much Talent (惜多才), Lamenting Ill Fate (惜薄命), Grieving Parted Ways (悲岐路), Remembering Old Friends (憶故人), Thinking of the Beloved Slave (念奴嬌), Mourning Youth (哀靑春), Sighing over Misfortune (嗟蹇遇), Suffering Dispersion (苦零落), Crying for Lost Love (哭相思). Ch'wich'yo takes up a red-trimmed brush (剪紅之筆) and composes poems for all. The beautiful woman praises them highly and prepares to leave. Ch'wich'yo asks the day of their reunion. She replies, "We shall meet on the Qiantang River (錢塘江)." The wind chimes jingle (錚錚), the Nanke dream (南柯夢) breaks; Ch'wich'yo is still in bed. The moon is bright as day; it is exactly the third watch. Alas! Was it a dream? It serves as embellishment (襯染) for Ch'wich'yo's entire life, making Ch'wich'yo resign herself to karma (因果) and submit to the play of fate (運命). How could this be a favored technique for a novelist seeking to reveal life's subtleties and pluck the heartstrings of human emotion? However, since ancient times, not a few have relied on dreams to subtly reveal future paths; that writers of Eastern novels and plays often use this technique needs no further mention today.

That young man on horseback has now become a captive of love (戀). Finally, devising various plans, he finds a garden behind the Wang family residence, lives there, and seeks to communicate his feelings. Patience bears fruit; after living there for over a month, one day he sees a single gold hairpin (一股金釵) hanging on a Bitao tree (碧桃樹) atop an artificial hill (假山). Alas! This is the Magpie Bridge (烏鵲橋) of love. From then on, Kim Chung (金重) finally manages to convey his feelings to Ch'wich'yo. The process in between need not be detailed here. There is no particularly ingenious plot twist descending from heaven, nor is there value in exaggerating the author's skill, but love was achieved. However, that achievement was like the morning dew, like a spring dream; heartless and unfulfilled, it was always a matter of heartbreak and sorrow (斷腸傷心).

The third chapter is a scene of these two being overjoyed (歡天喜地). Finding a small opening in a secluded corner of the garden, they create a tunnel path, and the two meet, pouring out their hearts (心肝). Truly, they are those who bore a hole (穴을 鑽하야) to meet secretly. How different is this from the illicit unions (野合) of unscrupulous people in the marketplace? If so, this book would ultimately have little value, and it would be difficult to elicit sympathetic tears from the reader by creating so many tragic tales in the later sections. The author thus devises another life-saving technique (活手段); let us not call this something that should not exist. Because of this, all the events in the later sections come alive; the reader weeps for her, and I too sigh for her. Listen to Ch'wich'yo rejecting Kim Chung's desire for intimacy (同衾), lamenting Ch'oe Ing (崔鶯, Cui Yingying), and explaining her own heartfelt convictions. <124> "I wish, my lord, that you pledge for life, and I, your servant, will guard my chastity (正戒). Let us both play the pipes (吹簫) and compose melodies, gaze at the moon and link verses (玩月聯詩), exhausting the sentiments of a talented man and beautiful woman (才子佳人), and not fall into the wicked ways of adulterous women and treacherous men (淫婦奸夫). Then we two shall become paragons of eternal moral teaching and refinement (名敎風流), pillars of refinement. Would this not be a commendable and exemplary event?" Thus, Ch'wich'yo was able to preserve her maidenly purity (處女의 純潔); her insight surpasses the common crowd. Take a look at the so-called pledge (盟誓) they made after forming their pact.

The pledge reads: "Like-minded persons Kim Chung and Wang Ch'wich'yo, born on such-and-such year, month, day, and hour, respectfully offer a stick of heart's incense (心香), a cup of water and sweet wine (水醴), pledging our oath to the spirits of High Heaven and Deep Earth. We have humbly heard that husband and wife value righteousness (義), and righteousness lies in remaining unchanged for life; young women possess much feeling (情), and feeling is rooted in being faithful through life and death. Previously, Ch'yo wished for a home, Chung wished for a household. Admiring talent and beauty has already deeply bound our hearts together. Today, Chung worries about the beginning, Ch'yo worries about the end. Pouring out our innermost thoughts (瀝胆傾心), we dare to speak our oath for the future. From this oath onward, the man promises no change even through nine deaths (九死無變), the woman vows single-minded fidelity (一節終身) for life. Even if external forces bring unforeseen circumstances, the heaven within our hearts is absolutely decided. Should we violate this pledge, may spirits and heaven bear witness."

Drunk with wine, the night deepens. Responding to Kim Saeng's (金生, Scholar Kim's) request, Ch'wich'yo plays the *hogeum*. With each pluck (撥一撥), it is suddenly slow like a gentle breeze (踈風), then suddenly rapid like a sudden shower (驟雨), seeming to resent, weep, and plead. After making Kim Saeng almost faint with ecstasy (狂喜欲絶), she gracefully and charmingly (嫣然嫵然히) presents the *hogeum* to Kim Saeng. At this moment, what was Ch'wich'yo's demeanor like? Her starry eyes were hazy (朦朧), red lotus blossoms flushed her cheeks (紅蕖映瞼), like mist enveloping peonies (芍藥) and rain moistening peach blossoms (桃花). Kim Saeng, unable to restrain his feelings (情思) and finding the fire of desire (慾火) hard to quell, finally grabs Ch'wich'yo and embraces her. Ch'wich'yo resolutely rejects him, saying, "My lord, are you possessed (着魔) again?" mocking him once. She continues, "A woman guarding her body is like guarding a vase (甁). If the vase breaks once, it cannot be made whole again. If a woman is stained once, how can she regain purity?" As she eloquently refutes him, even one who is not Kim Saeng would feel chilled to the bone (氷冷). Ch'wich'yo, guarding her maidenly purity and having the spirit (氣槪) to dare not yield her body even to her lover before the proper candlelit ceremony (華燭之式), is truly a rare gem (稀世之珍). That she values her body and guards her virtue (節) thus, signifies that Ch'wich'yo's intentions are pure and untainted, completely different in kind from those who bore holes (穴을 鑽하는 者). Schiller said, "Love is the most precious phenomenon in the world." Who would equate pure love with frivolous, fickle carnal passion (肉情)?

Maidenly purity is the utmost beauty in the world. It's not that flowers aren't gorgeous, nor that the moon isn't beautiful. But they cannot compare to maidenly purity. Between the covering heaven and the supporting earth, all beauty is gathered, all essence extracted, to create maidenly purity; it is perhaps like the brilliance of precious jade (寶玉). Ch'wich'yo understood its value. Holding onto it, she ultimately aspired to become a model (龜鑑) for all women under heaven. However, fate was different from what she thought. It drove her, trapped her in a ditch (溝壑), and sought to turn her flesh and bones together into mud (泥). The author shows her firmly guarding her maidenly <125> purity and then contrasts it with the scenes of sorrowful winds and tragic rains (悲風慘雨) in the later sections. At this point, the reader cannot help but shed a tear of sympathy (同情之淚) for the pitiable Wang Ch'wich'yo.

When joy reaches its peak, sorrow multiplies (歡樂極而哀情多). How can the morning of smiling flowers and singing birds turn into an evening of black winds and white rain? The fourth chapter is entirely written with a brush of sorrow and regret (傷心痛恨). The talented man and beautiful woman finally bid each other farewell, Ch'wich'yo's hopes all scatter like clouds, creating the starting point for the profound and tragic (深刻 慘憺) later sections. Kim Saeng rushes to Liaoyang (遼陽) for his uncle's funeral. The Wang family encounters disaster due to involvement with an uncle-in-law's family, suddenly arising, and Ch'wich'yo's father and brother both face cruel punishment (慘刑). Alas! What is needed here is three hundred taels of silver (3百의 銀子); without it, the father and brother cannot escape their fate. Ch'wich'yo, originally possessing a man's spirit (男子의 氣象), now cannot help but cast aside the maidenly purity she revered and guarded; her lifelong aspirations must ultimately be sacrificed for her family and father. She finally tells her parents of this intention. There are many tragic words here. However, this is a common scene under heaven, a standard technique (常套手段) of historical storytellers (稗史家) since ancient times. Ch'wich'yo tells her younger sister Ch'wiun (翠雲) about Kim Saeng and later begs her sister to convey her feelings to Kim Saeng in her stead; looking up and down, she cannot overcome her emotions (感慨). In her letter to Kim Chung, she says:

"Ch'wich'yo, ill-fated (薄命), disaster arose within my own walls (蕭墻). Instead of pleading innocence for my father like Tiying (緹縈), I can only follow the example of Li Ji (李寄) selling herself to save my family from hardship. Selling myself will surely be a disgrace (辱), shame (愧), and regret (恨) to you, my lord. Recalling my refusal under the lamplight (燈下投梭之拒), it ultimately becomes proof of my heartlessness (薄情). Thinking of my lord's feelings, how could there not be resentment? Thinking of my own heartlessness, I cannot continue this path. I humbly offer my younger sister to serve you (下陳). If my lord, by chance, does not reject her and condescends to fulfill this, perhaps my feelings of longing (思情) can be somewhat repaid. Setting out on a journey to the ends of the earth (天涯海角) any day now, the oath under the moon (月下之盟) has already become a vain thought (妄思). One *hogeum*, one set of mournful tunes (怨曲), one sealed packet of lingering fragrance (遺香)—if someday, with my sister, you burn incense, tune the instrument, sing mournful songs, and melodies drift, and when the fragrant smoke winds (香烟繚繞) and a desolate wind sighs (凄風淅瀝), know that my soul (魂) is present. If you would fortunately offer a cup of tea (杯茗) to my wronged soul nearby, the favor received would already be great. This farewell between life and death is all I can manage here. Words are short, feelings long (言短情長), unable to express fully. I only hope you strive to eat well (努力加餐) and fortunately do not think of me. To my lord Kim, a thousand leagues away, presented under oath, your sister who received your humiliating affection, Wang Ch'wich'yo, bows respectfully (歛衽而拜)."

The matchmaker (媒婆) arrives. She reports that a guest from Linqing (臨淸) seeks a beautiful concubine (美妾). Ch'wich'yo, weeping, agrees. Then appears the guest in cloud-patterned cap and splendid attire (雲巾華服). Responding to a request, Ch'wich'yo inscribes a line on a gold fan (金扇) and also plays the *hogeum*. What tune is it? *Hongyan Yuan* (紅顔怨, Lament of a Beautiful Face). Sorrowful, mournful, and poignant (哀怨凄楚), all who hear weep. <126>
"That person said, 'Truly exquisite skill. How much bride-price (財禮) is required?' The matchmaker said, 'She needs to save her father; nothing less than five hundred taels (五百兩) will do.' That person said, 'The bride-price is too much. Make it three hundred taels.' Ch'wich'yo said, 'If selling myself cannot accomplish the task, what use is selling?' That person said, 'Then make it four hundred taels.' Ch'wich'yo said, 'It must be five hundred taels.' That person added another fifty taels."

The flower-like, jade-like young woman Wang Ch'wich'yo has now become merchandise (賣物). The pitiable Wang Ch'wich'yo, who firmly guarded her maidenly purity and refused her body even to her lover, has become an item for discounted sale (歇價放賣物). From 300 taels to 400 taels, from 400 taels to 450 taels. Maidenly purity, at this point, is like a ball of mud (一丸泥). Alas! Poverty (貧)! From ancient times to the present, it makes loyal men weep and virtuous women cry; their integrity (節操) and virtue (德義) are trampled by the rich (富). Under the half-dimmed lonely lamp, whose tear-filled eyes are hazy? Is it only Wang Ch'wich'yo? Go look into the inner workings of society. Are not those narrow lanes and pleasure quarters (狹斜烟花) all streets of bound souls and broken hearts (鎖魂斷腸)?

That evening, Ch'wich'yo sees Kim Saeng in a dream. Kim Saeng throws down 300 taels to rescue Ch'wich'yo, chooses an auspicious day (黃道吉日), and holds a wedding banquet. The sounds of pipes and drums (笙鼓聲) are loud, the two enter the bridal chamber (洞房). Suddenly, a crowd of that guest from Linqing's men bursts into the bridal chamber and carries Ch'wich'yo away. Ch'wich'yo, supported on horseback, rides swiftly, intending to wait for Kim Saeng on the way. Just as she tries to dismount, she looks—what is this? It is not flat ground, but an extremely large fiery pit (火坑)! Fierce flames (烈焰) soar, radiating light, and fiery energy (火氣) billows up ten thousand fathoms high. A fireball the size of a bushel basket (斗大) fiercely strikes Ch'wich'yo's face. "Ah! I'm burning to death!" she cries out, startled awake. It was a strange dream (怪夢). Ch'wich'yo takes up her brush, trims the lampwick (把筆挑燈), and composes eight verses on awakening from the startling dream (驚夢覺八咏). Having finished, she cannot return to sleep, only weeps. It is unknown what state she will create next. The chapter ends with the standard phrase, "To know what happens next, read the next chapter's explanation" (再看下回分解).

The fifth and sixth chapters are the so-called "scenes of sorrow and lament" (愁嘆場). The parents cannot bear their child becoming another's concubine; Ch'wich'yo intends to substitute herself for the family's hardship. The filial piety and parental love (親子之恩愛) are fully depicted, revealing life's tragedies (人生의 悲劇) such as the father's reluctance to affix his seal (花押) to the marriage contract (立婚書), leaving nothing hidden (餘蘊). However, this is largely similar to the writing methods commonly used by Eastern and Western historical storytellers since ancient times, so there is no special need to recount the gist here. The so-called *Commentary outside Shengtan* (聖歎外書) says: "In the world, what most disturbs a person's heart is feeling (情). And among feelings disturbing the heart, what is most difficult to arrange (佈擺) is deep feeling (至情). Even a single strand is already hard to bear; how much more so when Kim Saeng's affection (恩情), parental filial piety (父母之孝情), sibling affection (兄弟姐妹의 手足情), the bitter feeling (苦情) of selling oneself to follow an unworthy person (匪人), the feeling of parting (別情) from home and world, and the worldly feeling (世情) of using bribes to resolve grievances all crowd together at once, stirring Ch'wich'yo's small heart (寸心)? How could one like Ch'wich'yo easily manage (發付) this?" So it is. Ch'wich'yo, amidst the confusion of a hundred feelings clashing, <127> is about to encounter the world's utmost sorrow (人世의 悲絶). The society of that time takes the pitiable Ch'wich'yo, makes her suffer all the torments of the world, shatters her maidenly purity, entrusts her to a pack of jackals and wolves (豺狼之羣), casts her into the mud (泥塗之裡), and intends to transform her into a shameful demon (媿魔) of the pleasure quarters (烟花場裡). Alas! Society today is like the past, and the East is like the West. Yet people forget society's culpability and point fingers at society's victims, heaping merciless abuse and cold ridicule. Even if I am not You Gou (유고, reference unclear, possibly a figure known for lamenting injustice), I too wish to cry out and proclaim society's crime!

Alas! Oh, vast blue Heaven (蒼蒼者天乎)! How heartless is Heaven? It intends to make a virtuous woman wallow and suffer (駝酪 苦惱) in the mire (淤泥). Flowers barely begin to bloom when a mad wind tears them into fine shreds (細紛紛); the moon barely begins to become full when floating clouds cover it, creating pitch darkness (黑闇闇). Reaching the seventh chapter, the reader cannot help but close the book and sigh deeply (喟然長嘆). In the quiet, Ch'wich'yo thinks of the past, imagines the future; recalling the scene of the pledge made the other day, the scene of selling herself today, and the scene of longing (相思) in days to come all at once, her small heart nearly bursts (裂破). To whom can she confide her sorrow (憂愁)? Longing is known only to the one who longs (相思를 只相知). "Old Heaven ignores people's憔悴 (haggardness), tears add to the Yellow River's nine bends overflowing, resentment presses down Mount Hua's three peaks" (淚添九曲黃河溢하고 恨壓三峰華嶽低로다). "Late arriving at the west tower, leaning to watch, that setting sun, ancient road, decaying willows, long embankment." (晩到西樓倚看하니 那夕陽 古道와 衰柳長堤로다.) (From *Xixiang Ji* (西廂記), *The Romance of the Western Chamber*). Ch'wich'yo's feelings surpass even the sorrow of Cui Yingying (崔鶯鶯) back then. Finally, she composes a letter in blood (血로써 一封을 裁할세). When her younger sister awakens startled and they talk, the pitiable girl's heart is thrown into turmoil, and she faints to the ground. "My feeling is wide as the sea, my love is deep as the sea," says the protagonist of one of Shakespeare's plays upon meeting his lover. Ch'wich'yo did not even experience one night of joy. Yet, in entrusting a fragment of her feelings, her heart cannot forget Kim Saeng even in dreams. But worldly obligations (浮世의 義理) force this pitiable child to turn her love towards another. The pain pierces her bones (痛恨이 徹骨), and thinking of it, she enters a state of unconsciousness (人事不省). Parents and siblings rush to save her. Ch'wich'yo revives and finally recounts the story of Kim Saeng. Her parents and those listening weep unbearable tears once more. The next day, that guest arrives seeking to take her by sedan chair (轎). Ch'wich'yo comes weeping; what did she say?

"Kim Saeng, Kim Saeng, your wife separates from you today. In this life, we cannot be joined like intertwined branches (偕連理), I wish to continue our bond in the next life. I, Wang Ch'wich'yo, am truly ill-fated (好薄命)! Unable to enjoy a refined and handsome husband (風流佳婿), I must instead marry a dissolute man (狂且). Pitiable, a delicate flower (一朶嬌花) carelessly stuck in mire (淤泥之上). Heaven, since you did not grant me a good fate, why did you let me meet a talented man? Having met a talented man, why could this talented bond not be formed?"

Sorrowful and mournful (悲悲切切), weeping and wailing (哭哭啼啼). But what can be done? Pitiful! A peerless beauty (絶代의 佳人) is paired with this beastly creature (馬牛蠢物).

What kind of person is this beastly creature? His surname is Ma (馬), indulging in debauchery (放蕩三昧), he reached a state of destitution (紙衣之境涯) and ended up destitute (落魄) in Linzi (臨淄). He formed an ill-matched couple (얼려맛는 夫婦) with the wicked madam Ma Xiu (惡婆馬秀) <128> and came here to buy a person. Pitiful! The virtuous woman Wang Ch'wich'yo has fallen into his grasp (掌中). Creation (造花) is exquisite, deceiving so skillfully! What a hateful matter! Ch'wich'yo arrives at his house, sees it quickly with perceptive eyes (慧眼), and cannot overcome her suspicion (疑訝). That night, sitting alone, unable to suppress her pain and anger (痛憤), she finally resolves to kill herself (自殺). Danger! Her hand reaches for the dagger (刺刀) on the table. It is a split second (一刹那間)! She will preserve her chastity (操) and end her life here. Will she end her life? Ch'wich'yo's story would end here too, but suddenly Ma Kui (馬龜) bursts into the room. Alas! I cannot bear to write this; the author too sighed. Pitiful! A delicate, fragrant, tender bud (嬌香嫩澁) is suddenly struck by jealous wind and rain (狂風妬雨). How could wandering bees and frivolous butterflies (遊蜂浪蝶) know how to cherish jade and pity fragrance (惜玉憐香)? That night, Ch'wich'yo composes nine verses of the song "Nai Jian Kuang Qie" (乃見狂且, Then I Saw the Mad Fellow) in indignation (憤慨). On the day of farewell, Ch'wich'yo speaks her mind. However, since her decision is made, she consoles her parents and siblings. The father, blinded by tears, parts ways; though his heart is full, Ch'wich'yo sets out on a journey of ten thousand li (萬里).

Fate and circumstance do not allow Ch'wich'yo to preserve her maidenly purity, causing her to fall into the pleasure quarters (烟花狹斜之地). The author depicts Ch'wich'yo's fall with a refined brush. The commentary called *Shengtan Waishu* (金聖歎之外書) says: "A woman's body has no master; suffering and joy depend on others." How could I not feel pain, anger, and emotion for women?

Fate mercilessly drives Ch'wich'yo, seeking to turn her towards tragic and profound circumstances (悲慘 深刻한 境域). The cart arrives at a certain place in Linzi (臨淄). She sees only a stout, tall madam (婆) aged about 40 or more. Seeing Ch'wich'yo, she calls out, "Ch'wich'yo dear (翠翹兒), get out of the cart." Following her, they arrive at an obscene shrine (淫祠) worshipped by brothels (娼家). Ch'wich'yo, ignorant of the world, how could she understand this? Doing as told, she bows respectfully (敬拜). The madam chants prayers for thousands of customers to come (千客萬來). "Show her to her father." Who is this father? It is that Ma Kui (OOO馬龜, original text seems to omit the name here, assumed 馬龜 based on context), whom Ch'wich'yo believed to be her good husband and married. Calling her husband 'father'? What is this? Ch'wich'yo is startled (驚呀). The madam is angry. Glaring at Ma Kui, she curses him thoroughly as a wicked beast (業畜). The remaining anger leaps out, grabbing Ch'wich'yo's hair, beating her relentlessly (打擲三昧). Ch'wich'yo realizes it is a brothel (娼家), loses consciousness (前後不覺), grabs a razor (剃刀), and stabs her own throat (喉). Alas! The pure and innocent maiden has been deceived and fallen into a den of demons (魔窟). Thinking about it, Ch'wich'yo, having such spirit (氣性), could not endure it. The wicked madam is also troubled. Promising to marry her off elsewhere after her wound heals (傷療), she has her stay upstairs in the Ningbi Pavilion (凝碧樓). One disaster seems averted, but calamities follow closely (接踵), about to fall upon the pitiable girl's shoulders.

While composing poems in the Ningbi Pavilion, she suddenly hears someone in the next pavilion reciting:
"Outside the pavilion, whose young girl with black hair is that? / Her long chant's sound is separated by green peach blossoms. / Sorrow invades the brush tip, low, seeming to choke back sobs, / Resentment towards the wind cries out bitterly. / From afar, her fragrance reaches, annoying the butterfly powder, / Subtly conveying hidden feelings, delighting the window gauze. <129> / You must pity my abundant talent, / I pity you, still an unbroken melon (未破瓜, i.e., a virgin)."

There is a scholar in the next pavilion, wearing a fluttering scarf and fine clothes (飄巾華服). Although this scholar's recitation may not be high art (陽春白雪), it still belongs to the lineage of poetry and books (詩書一脉). Could this perhaps be a convenient way for me to escape this fiery pit (火坑)? Though intelligent (慧), she is a woman; seeming meticulous yet largely careless (大粗), her single-minded desire to escape the demonic realm (魔界) instead pushes her further into suffering (苦境). Does she know this or not? Ch'wich'yo's karmic debt (*債) is deep and heavy. This is the plot structure (脚色) of the eighth chapter.

He bows once, she curtsies deeply (一萬福). Heart communicates with heart (以心傳心); Ch'wich'yo becomes acquainted with the scholar from the next pavilion, named Ch'o Kyŏng (楚卿). Ch'wich'yo finally pens a letter, entrusts it to a mute girl (啞兒) to deliver. The next day, a letter arrives. Opening it, only the two characters "昔越" (sŏk wŏl) are written. She understands it means to climb over the wall (越墻) and come see her at the *xu* hour (戌時, 7-9 PM) on the 21st day. Indeed, he comes. They talk, and she obtains his promise to help her escape the fiery pit. However, she who once refused her body even to her lover, suddenly upon a chance encounter (*하야 - unclear character, likely related to meeting/encountering), without time to consider the consequences (前後之分別), lightly gives her affection (情) to him. She who once broke her principles (操) for her father now defiles her womanly virtue (女德) for herself. But it is all circumstance (境遇) that makes it so. What can be done? Now her pitiable heart (胸裡) is desperate (切) to escape the world of suffering (苦界), without deep thought (深慮) or far-sighted observation (遠察), she falls for Ch'o Kyŏng's ploy (口計)—"of the thirty-six stratagems, running away is best" (三十六計 走爲 上策)—and one deep night, climbs out the window (越窓) to flee. However, she ultimately fails to escape (逃了치 못하고), is suddenly captured, and the beautiful woman, stripped naked (赤裸裸), is beaten continuously for two or three *nyŏn* (年 - likely a measure of time/intensity, not literal years). One lash of the whip, one turn (打一鞭, 轉一轉), reaching the extreme of misery. The ninth chapter narrates this miserable scene, showing that this calamity, this disaster, is Ch'wich'yo's own fault (罪).

In the pleasure quarters (烟花場裡), there are naturally precautions (備虞). Ch'wich'yo, near death (幾死), suddenly introduces a prostitute named Ma Kyo (馬嬌) to rescue Ch'wich'yo from her suffering. The next day, Ch'o Kyŏng arrives; he is originally a ruined man from the marketplace (市井之破落戶). Having trifled with (*弄) Ch'wich'yo, he shifts all blame for the escape attempt onto her, deceives Ma Kyo (馬嬌を瞞着), and presents himself as a good person (好兒). When Ch'wich'yo angrily confronts him about his crime (罪), he brandishes his fists (拳子), strikes Ch'wich'yo's face, and tries to demonstrate his own innocence (潔白). Suddenly, a scene of carnage (修羅場) unfolds. As people try to seize Ch'o Kyŏng, the hawk-dog (鷹犬, i.e., henchman) of the pimp (忘八烏龜) has no choice but to escape. Ma Ma (馬媽, Madam Ma) visits Ch'wich'yo and asks whether she should sell her elsewhere or have her follow her instead. Ch'wich'yo, with no way to escape (遁去無由), finally comes to the conclusion, "following the old is better than the new" (伴新不如舊新). Thereupon, Ma Ma slowly teaches her the secret arts (秘術) of the pleasure quarters. This part contains exquisite writing (妙文) and also exquisite techniques (妙術). In the daily system (日用的 制度), there are seven methods (七法): One is Crying (哭), Two is Cutting (剪), Three is Stabbing (刺), Four is Burning (燒), Five is Marrying (嫁), Six is Running (走), Seven is Dying (死). These all require the skills (工夫) of lowly brothel women (娼家賤婦) and are nothing more than techniques (術法) for deceiving customers (客). How many bearded men (有髥兒) get caught by these secret arts and cannot escape their grasp (手裏)! Pitiful (可憐) and lamentable (可憫)! **Summary**

This article by Yang Paekhwa is a literary review of the first ten chapters of the Chinese novel *Kŭm Un Kyo Chŏn* (金雲翹傳, known famously as the Vietnamese epic *Truyện Kiều*). Yang praises the novel's depiction of the protagonist Wang Ch'wich'yo's tragic descent from a virtuous maiden into the life of a prostitute due to fate and circumstance, highlighting themes of female purity, suffering, karmic destiny (因果), and societal injustice. While criticizing the eventual happy ending as a common trope and the reliance on karmic causality, Yang appreciates the narrative skill in portraying Ch'wich'yo's emotional turmoil and compares the novel favorably to other Chinese works for its focused narrative, recommending it despite its flaws.

From a historical perspective, the article reveals the significant influence of Chinese literature and narrative conventions (like karmic causality and didacticism) on Korean literary tastes and criticism in the early colonial period. It also reflects contemporary social concerns regarding female virtue, the plight of women forced into prostitution (blaming society and fate rather than the individual), and the perceived moral failings within traditional narratives, offering a glimpse into the intellectual and cultural landscape of 1920s Korea.
