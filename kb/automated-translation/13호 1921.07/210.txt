허수아비의 한물이를

그들은 人形을 썻스나
고흔衣服을 입엇스나
男女老少를 勿論하고
뼈와살과피를 가추운
튼튼한肉身을 가젓스나
不完全한 智情意를 가젓스나
人間의 참靈魂을 갓지못한

아!나는본다!
허수아비의 한물이를

그들은 日常行爲는 勿論
生物의 最大事實인
「生」과「死」의 意識도업는
마치 틀과 가튼
허수아비의 물이다!＜120＞

Ah! I See
						Kim Sŏksong

Ah! I see!
A crowd of scarecrows

Though they wear the form of humans (人形),
Though they wear fine clothes (衣服),
Regardless of man or woman, old or young,
Though they possess bones and flesh and blood,
Possess sturdy bodies (肉身),
Though they possess imperfect intellect, emotion, and will (智情意),
They cannot possess the true soul (靈魂) of a human.

Ah! I see!
A crowd of scarecrows

They, needless to say about their daily actions (日常行爲),
Even lack awareness (意識) of the greatest facts (最大事實) of living beings:
"Life" (生) and "Death" (死).
Just like machines,
They are a crowd of scarecrows! <120>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏksong describes the speaker's vision of people as "scarecrows." Despite having human bodies, clothes, and basic mental faculties, these figures lack a "true soul" and operate mechanically, unaware even of the fundamental concepts of life and death. From a colonial history perspective, the poem is a poignant critique of the spiritual and psychological state of Koreans under Japanese rule. The "scarecrows" likely symbolize individuals who have lost their agency, national consciousness, or humanity under oppression, perhaps collaborators or those passively resigned, functioning without genuine life or spirit. Historians might find it valuable as an expression of existential despair and dehumanization felt during the colonial period, reflecting a loss of identity and autonomy.
