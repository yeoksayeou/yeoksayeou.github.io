Cry of the Proletariat
						Kim Sŏksong

I am a Proletariat!
One who possesses nothing.

However, I—
Gold, Land, Housing,
Status, Honor, Comfort,
Communism,
Socialism,
Democracy,
Ah! I do not desire them!

Love, Family,
Society, Nation,
Anything of the Present,
Ah! I curse them!

And only
A rational life of the Future
Ah! I demand it!

Thus, I—
Even if all the women of the world
Were entrusted to my body,
Even if all the wealth of the world
Were given into my hands...
Ah! I would abandon it!

I am a Proletariat!
One who possesses nothing.

However, I only—
The asset called "Human"
A "Human" in the true sense...
Demand! Cry out! <119>

And next—
Let the "Rights" of a "Human"
Be in my hands, so that
I myself can recognize
And manage myself (as a human)... <120>

**Summary**

This poem is a passionate declaration by a self-proclaimed "proletariat" who rejects all forms of material wealth, social status, existing societal structures (family, society, nation), and even prominent political ideologies like communism, socialism, and democracy. The speaker curses the present and demands only a "rational life of the Future." Ultimately, the core demand is for the recognition of fundamental humanity ("Human" as an asset) and the possession of human rights, allowing for self-recognition and self-determination. From a historical perspective, the poem is significant as an early expression of radical social critique and burgeoning class consciousness in colonial Korea, published in the influential journal *Kaebyŏk*. It reflects disillusionment not just with colonial rule but with existing social norms and even the proposed solutions of the time, focusing instead on a fundamental, almost existential demand for human dignity and agency, which historians might find indicative of anarchist or humanist influences alongside socialist ones during this period.
