**Body Text Translation:**
My New Movement Standing in the Transitional Period
						Chŏng Ch'angsŏn

Reconstruction—the word "reconstruction" (改造) has become a common phrase shouted by people all over the world. When we say reconstruction, its meaning is not very difficult to interpret, nor does it require deep thought. However, there are two aspects to interpreting this word. One is internal reconstruction (內的 改造), and the other is external reconstruction (外的 改造); these two can be interpreted. External reconstruction contacts our eyes and ears, and we can clearly perceive its growth. But internal reconstruction—its growth and evolution are difficult for our eyes and ears to discern. Thus, external reconstruction holds the external meaning that actively moves all things in heaven and earth and the entire universe's material categories. Internal reconstruction, behind the material movement that actively moves and shifts this entire universe and all things in heaven and earth, becomes the driving force of its ebb and flow, its ups and downs. Thus, its activation proceeds in an orderly manner. It seems to us that external reconstruction proceeds first. However, external reconstruction signifies the current state (現勢), while internal reconstruction signifies the potential force (潛勢). Speaking of the order of their progression, the potential movement comes first, and then the movement of the current state arises. Thus, the current state is analogous to the physical body, and the potential force is like our spirit. It goes without saying that the body cannot act without the spirit, and only after the spirit's action is initiated can the body's movement be produced. Thus, all growth and decay, evolution and revolution throughout the universe occur only after there has been a potential movement, which then manifests as a movement of the current state. Therefore, although nothing may seem visible, its power can lift heaven and earth; when its energy, filling the universe, is unleashed, no great force can restrain it. This is because that movement is the inherent life of the entire universe. However, in the wake of its progression, a tragic phenomenon unfolds. At the very site of its progression, there is infinite sorrow, infinite agony, infinite hesitation,<55> and infinite conflict. When imperfection advances towards perfection, when incompleteness advances towards completeness, when lack of fulfillment advances towards fulfillment, when ugliness advances towards beauty, when instability advances towards stability, when injustice advances towards justice, when chaos advances towards tranquility, along that path, perfection must first destroy imperfection, completeness must destroy incompleteness, fulfillment must destroy lack of fulfillment, beauty must destroy ugliness, stability must destroy instability, justice must destroy injustice, and tranquility must destroy chaos.

Thus, for imperfection to advance to perfection, incompleteness to completeness, lack of fulfillment to fulfillment, ugliness to beauty, instability to stability, injustice to justice, and chaos to tranquility is not particularly difficult, nor is it bad, nor is it contrary to right principles. However, for the phenomenon of imperfection to advance to perfection, the past imperfect self must perish. For the phenomenon of incompleteness to achieve the phenomenon of completeness, the past phenomenon of incompleteness must perish. For lack of fulfillment to advance to fulfillment, ugliness to beauty, instability to stability, and injustice to justice, all past firmly held beliefs must be destroyed, past accumulated merits must perish, and all past maintained phenomena must be destroyed. When that happens, externally, great mountains crumble, rivers and seas overflow, vast fields become desolate, and all things are wiped out. Internally, when not even a single remnant entity remains, the sounds of pain, groans, and laments reaching our ears and eyes is a clear principle and a fixed law. Therefore, in the wake of reconstruction, there is sorrow, resentment, pain, and agony.

The present time is such a period of reconstruction. Thus, throughout the whole world, piercing through all directions, high and low, amidst unheard cries of desperation, groans, pain, and resentment, mountains are shaken, seas boil, and all fields are moved. Like a burning fire, like boiling water, the tumultuous thoughts find it difficult to seek solace in anything. Then, what solace can be quickly given? No, if solace is given, will they immediately find complete recovery as if having taken a quick cure-all powder? No. The growth and decay of young grass sprouts emerging in the spring breeze, however small, cannot be prevented; falling leaves in the autumn wind cannot be defended against by any chemical action; the branches of myriad trees growing lushly in summer have no recourse before being broken; and who can stop the withering under snow in winter? Likewise, the agony, pain, sorrow, resentment, and discontent faced in the present era, though like the small force of spring sprouts, have behind them a potential force greater than Mount T'aesan and an energy broader than rivers and seas backing their driving power. Therefore, its activation is difficult to prevent with any great force,<56> and there is absolutely no way to offer solace. However, I have one regret in this era.

What is this? It is not merely discontent with it, nor viewing it with resentment. It is simply that I deeply feel and lament the worthlessness of human history where our ideals and the world do not align—in other words, where philosophy is philosophy, philosophers are philosophers, and the world is the world, each following its own instincts and pursuing its own preferences, advancing randomly and chaotically. To put it another way, I deeply feel the lack of unity between the instinctual life of humans on the natural path traversed by inevitable forces—that is, the physical life—and the spiritual, that is, the ideal. Simply put, the philosopher merely sings the praises of their own ideal life alone, while the world, on its own, stirs up contradiction, reaction, instability, and discontent. The surplus energy and demands of those indulging in inevitable necessity ultimately lead to disregarding morality, disregarding ethics, deceiving conscience, and slighting righteousness, giving rise to evils. Setting aside fellow countrymen and clansmen, taken to the extreme, even the affection for parents, siblings, and close relatives becomes superficial and masked. That our five-thousand-year history has come to be recorded under such names is due to nothing else but this: when our inevitable instincts are activated, regardless of shared ethnicity or blood, we engage in life-and-death struggles at will. For the sake of acquiring authority, we abandon the level plane of morality, ethics, justice, and compassion. This inevitability, lacking even the slightest restraint, ultimately causes all sorts of ugliness to arise.

Ah! Then why were the philosophers silent, asleep? Did their efforts not even have half an influence on our human world? No. As mentioned earlier, it is because philosophers were philosophers, truth was truth, and the world was the world, each proceeding independently. Let's end this with the single phrase "the worthlessness of our history." And another point is a passive fatalism, in other words, recording T'olsŭt'oi's (Tolstoy's) non-resistance doctrine as a kind of "fatalism" (宿命論), and calling this the fatalistic confession I received at the end of my regret. This confession is not intuitive but abstract. To say it is abstract does not mean this fatalistic principle itself is abstract. It is just that the example I intend to record is merely abstract and historical. Let me speak using ancient Greece as an example. Recording the period before the era of studying the physical world in ancient Greece and surveying the history up to the period before Christ, the thoughts of those people, whose civilization was undeveloped and superstition was inherent, were satisfied entirely with myths, creation stories, and the like. As civilization developed, reaching around 600 BC, T'allesŭ (Thales) first saying "all things are made of water" might seem simple, but it shows how much civilization had developed by that time. Later, reaching the so-called Enlightenment era, looking at the Sop'isŭt'ŭ (Sophists)<57> who studied human affairs, one can clearly see the development of thought and civilization of that era. The public sentiment of that time was entirely skeptical and destructive. Those who did not value the relics of the past could not establish a definite central ideal and suffered in their wandering. Ultimately, figures like the Sop'isŭt'ŭ (Sophists), representing the popular thought of their time, represented that thought most starkly. Later, the thought that disregarded past relics gradually faded, and public sentiment once again tended towards seeking and demanding new principles in ethics, justice, morality, and religion. Sok'ŭrat'esŭ (Socrates) emerged, representing the trend of thought of that era, and advocated the ideas of that time most starkly. At the end of that era's intellectual trend, public sentiment became rigid again. The world-weariness and pessimism of schools like K'inik'ŭ (Cynic), Megara (Megari), and K'irene (Cyrenaic) should not be overlooked either; they most starkly voiced the internal stirrings of the public sentiment of that era. Later again, the pessimistic, world-suppressing tendency faded, and public sentiment once again demanded definite principles and existence in religion, ethics, morality, and philosophy. P'ŭrat'on (Plato) and Arisŭt'ot'ellesŭ (Aristotle) emerged, most starkly manifesting the public sentiment of that era and responding to its demands. Later, as public sentiment gradually declined and culture regressed, the culture of the ancient capital At'ene (亞典), destroyed by the conquest of Alleksandŭ (Alexander) the Great, was scattered in all directions. Public sentiment again turned to pessimism, world-weariness, and fatalism. At that time, disciples of schools like Sŭt'oa (Stoa) and Ep'ik'urosŭ (Epicurus) emerged, representing that public sentiment and the intellectual trend of the era, shouting the words "follow nature." Observing this, one can clearly understand the thought of that era. Later, entering a period of skepticism again was also a similar fact. Then, transitioning to the age of religion, the New Platonist school and the New Pythagorean (新「삐타콜라쓰」) school emerged, representing the public sentiment seeking to escape the fetters of the body and pursue an otherworldly spiritual life, voicing the fervent thoughts of their inner hearts on their behalf. From then until today, observing the trends of public sentiment and intellectual currents that have progressed along the natural path of rise and fall, listing them one by one would require a separate history of philosophy to be satisfactory. However, briefly taking prominent examples from among them: the cause of the dispersal of Greek culture and the world-weariness and pessimism of the Sŭt'oa (Stoa) and Ep'ik'urosŭ (Epicurus) schools becoming their guiding principles might seem coincidental if examined casually. But if one carefully considers the cause, Alleksandŭ (Alexander) the Great's conquest greatly influenced the lives of the Greek people at the time. That nation lost its political independence, the essence of national life was utterly cast to the ground, and their precious land became<58> a battlefield for foreigners. As their peace, morality, and ethics gradually disappeared, and public sentiment lost other courage, the people lacked the leisure to hope for other external things. Individuals merely sought to find a place of peace and security within their own hearts. They went so far as to say, "Philosophy is merely the study of methods to guide the individual towards a place of peace and security, free from the external world." Seeing this, we can easily understand how our life, circumstances, and situation exert influence on the development of thought. The changes in public sentiment of an era are not solely driven by the calls of philosophers. Rather, philosophers are the pioneers, discoverers, and representatives who stand at the forefront of the era's intellectual trends and public sentiments. It wouldn't be sophistry to say that their ideals did not primarily drive the public sentiment. Furthermore, looking at the recent intellectual world in modern times, one can naturally understand this by observing Ruso's (Rousseau's) most significant assertion, naturalism; Cheimsŭ's (James') P'ŭragŭmat'ijŭm (Pragmatism) philosophy; and the thoughts of recent revolutionaries like the materialist K'al Marŭk'ŭsŭ (Karl Marx) and K'ŭrop'ot'ŭk'in (Kropotkin). At first glance, it might seem that after the revolutionary Ruso (Rousseau), who broke down past intellectualism and advocated emotionalism and voluntarism, our intellectual world gradually inclined towards emotionalism/voluntarism and humanism, leading to today's materialistic tendency. However, this too suggests that the driving cause of the public sentiment and intellectual trend of the era when Ruso (Rousseau) made his call was the preceding trend of the era and the shift in public sentiment, which Ruso (Rousseau) himself was the first to voice. While it cannot be denied that the various facts emerging in modern times—the gradual decline of morality, the fickleness of public sentiment, and all the ugliness produced in religion, ethics, justice, and conscience—are influenced by the ideals of us philosophers, the fundamental motive of those ideals ultimately cannot be denied as a reflection of the circumstances and times. In other words, they were philosophers situated in the intellectual trend of the era, philosophers situated in the public sentiment, representatives who powerfully articulated the phenomena of human life passing through the natural path; it cannot be said that the philosopher's ideal governed the public sentiment, drove the intellectual trend, and controlled the entirety of life. That I am saying such things now, adopting a kind of skeptical attitude and expressing a fatalistic tone, cannot but be called a reflection of the intellectual trend and public sentiment of the era. The clamor that the history since the dawn of humankind is all a history of ugliness, or the dismissal of the past as contradiction and defect, is the main current of this era; skeptical thought is also the main current of this era. If our disregard for the past today and our doubting of all past achievements<59> stems from recognizing the imperfection and injustice of the results of all past endeavors, then it becomes even clearer that our current skeptical tendency and fatalistic attitude are all a necessary part of nature's phenomena. This is precisely because we perceive that everything is nature. I intend to stand firm in a fatalistic attitude as much as possible. I will not strive to resist the necessary movement of the great universe as much as possible. Even the idea of the new movement I intend to record below is entirely a reflection of this thought, it would not be an overstatement to say. The fundamental basis of my thought is first fatalistic. "There is nothing that can be done about you flowing naturally." However, this fatalistic attitude is by no means passive. It is like the meaning of "expansion" (擴充) mentioned by Mencius. With a fatalistic attitude, I intend to stop courting disgrace with metaphysical problems. Thus, I will give up on questions like: Is there existence or non-existence? Life or death? Subjectivity or objectivity? And I intend to turn my gaze towards the historical aspect, or towards intellectual trends and public sentiment. This is the feeling of my first thought. -There is nothing that can be done about you flowing inevitably.-

Are we people who currently have the leisure to fuss over metaphysical problems anew? No, we are not. Unlike the ancient people who had the good fortune to discover the "Idea" (이데아) and "Eudaimonia" (오이따모니아) of Sok'ŭrat'esŭ (Socrates), P'ŭrat'on (Plato), and Arisŭt'ot'ellesŭ (Aristotle), whose ideals were entirely focused on nature, investigating principles and discovering reason, modern people must pay the greatest attention to reality above all else. In other words, rather than chattering about ideals or intellectualism, we must study the pressing problem of livelihood, its fundamental motive—social problems, and the ways of navigating the world. Simply put, shouldn't we first resolve this problem where, despite having fields full of grain, one cannot eat a bowl of rice, and despite mountains full of gold, one has not a single copper coin, leading to empty resentment and discontent?

The era is clearly a transitional period (過渡期). It is clearly a period of reconstruction (改造期). It is clearly a period of doubt (懷疑期). It is clearly a period of destruction (破壞機). Therefore, it is clearly an era of pain (苦痛時代). It is clearly an era of agony (煩惱時代). The problem of livelihood clamored about by billions, and all other instabilities and discontents, are being shouted about everywhere, from East to West, South to North. However, they have not yet found a complete direction. In a dark cave, they shout until their throats are hoarse, searching for something new. They search intently with wide-open eyes, trying to see something, trying to examine something—it is precisely that new<60> thing. However, it is not that the new thing has not yet come before our eyes, nor is there any reason it shouldn't exist, but the reason they have not yet discovered it is simply because there is an easy method, but that method has not yet been realized. No, it is not that it cannot be realized, but truly that it is not being realized. This is because the method is simple. However, in some aspects, it is also extremely difficult. At first hearing, it seems easy to understand and easy to do, but in the wake of its realization, as mentioned before, there is sorrow, weeping, resentment, pain, and agony. And when it is realized, only when there is a great power and authority that cannot be bought even with the omnipotence of gold can one pass through the agony to joy, through pain to pleasure, through resentment to happiness, through weeping to song and dance. This too is nature following the natural path of the great nature that passes through beginninglessness—its power resides within our hearts, giving us the great mission to carry it out. This mission is precisely the mission of modern people, what modern people must strive for, what modern people must achieve victory in by exerting all their courage. If you have a sword in your hand, cut down the life of the evil spirit blocking the path you intend to take. If you have an axe (斧斤) in your hand, you must cut the iron wire fence blocking that path and advance. Even if you die within that iron wire fence, pass that axe down to your descendants. Your descendants must also cut that iron wire fence with that axe. Only then will your descendants for ten thousand generations attain peaceful song and dance. There is no need for hesitation, no need for wandering, no need for fear, and no need for doubt. There is no other way but to pour your heart's blood and advance.

We are now elements crying out amidst the clamor for reconstruction and the skeptical tendency. Behind the meaning of reconstruction lies some imperfection and lack of fulfillment, and behind the meaning of doubt lies some reactive psychology, containing the meaning of having seen, albeit uncertainly, a small ray of light, some new dawn that is its object. The intellectual trend and public sentiment of the present era are most fitting for this. We have discovered that all past forms and relics—religion, ethics, morality, philosophy—are contradictions, superficialities, externalisms, fantasies, and mere theories. Thus, without clearly seeing something better than this, we intend to proceed towards that utopia. And so, in our minds, everyone dismisses the past relics of morality, ethics, religion, and philosophy as worthless, and in the formless space behind them, has destroyed them all without exception. Everything!!! Religion, ethics, morality,<61> philosophy—all destroyed without exception, they have embarked on a new movement. Taking the first step, they have advanced onto the battlefield of destiny (性的 戰爭場 - *likely a typo or specific term, context suggests a crucial struggle/destiny*). I will not strive to stop this destructive tendency. This is because it is a natural phenomenon, and thus there is no reason it can be suppressed, nor is there a need to suppress it. I intend to stand aside temporarily from its path and just watch the flowing current. No, I intend to feed it an accelerating agent (快流劑). This is to see the eternal peace and eternal comfort that will return at its conclusion—no, not eternal. Eternal peace and eternal comfort are paradises that can only be seen in the metaphysical, otherworldly realms spoken of by Mr. "P'u" (「푸」氏 - *unclear reference, possibly Plato or another philosopher*) or Mr. "A" (「아」氏 - *unclear reference, possibly Aristotle*). Life is like waves. There is change. There is reversal. When there is peace, it will be extinction and silence. However, this is to face a new countenance, moving from this discontent, instability, and contradiction towards completeness, albeit imperfect completeness; towards fulfillment, albeit lack of fulfillment; towards justice, albeit lack of establishment. Thus, at the end of their efforts, I hope there will be peace and comfort. That is the world of joy. That is the new world. That is the new universe. It is the utopia we desire. To occupy that world, there must be a new compass. This is precisely the construction of a new philosophy. I hope for the construction of this new universe and demand the establishment of a new philosophy. The present is a transitional period, reconstructing from an era of contradiction to an era of justice. What I strive for facing this transitional period is merely an accelerating agent (快流劑). This accelerating agent ultimately signifies proceeding towards the utopia on the other shore without hesitation. Therefore, modern people must concentrate the energy mentioned in the previous chapter, first destroy all the inherent conventional forms, morality, religion, ethics, philosophy, and all assets within themselves, then destroy all formalistic, superficial relics in the family, then burn all conventional historical records widely in the world, and after destroying all forms from the large parts of the world down to the singular self, they must construct a new world, new philosophy, new universe, new morality, new ethics, new religion. O powerful one, do not spare your strength. O you with a mouth, do not seal your mouth. O you with ears, do not block your ears. The construction of the new universe and the establishment of the new philosophy—the author intends to write about this when an opportunity arises later. What I intend to write under this current title is, primarily, the meaning of constructing the new philosophy and new universe from a subjective standpoint. At first glance, it may seem like the destructive means employed by the rebellious factions (亂黨) that arose in Rŏsia (露國), where class divisions are extreme and material inequality is clamored about, but it differs from that, which is biased towards materialism, nor is it an externally manifested material destruction. The habits (習慣) held within each of our hearts<62> must first be destroyed, and next, advancing into the world, spiritual destruction must occur. I intend to pour my heart's blood and strive here. I intend to initiate a new movement. Such thoughts are vibrant in my mind. This is the essential point of my "My New Movement Standing in the Transitional Period." This movement not only signifies destruction but also demands reconstruction from destruction. I offer this writing finally to the constructor (建設者) and the workers (役事者).

Ah! Constructor!
Ah! Workers!
May your names shine!
Go forth—into that dust and wind—
Your path is wide and great.
I ask one thing of you.
The honor and value of the world, the true meaning of our humanity,
Furthermore, our new history,
All of this
Is in your hands!

From Miwa (三輪), Tokyo (東京)

**Summary**

The author, Chŏng Ch'angsŏn, argues that the world is in a "transitional period" requiring "reconstruction." He distinguishes between internal (spiritual/potential) and external (material/manifest) reconstruction, asserting the former drives the latter. This process necessitates the painful destruction of old, imperfect forms (beliefs, institutions, traditions) to allow for the emergence of the new and better. Chŏng laments the disconnect between ideals (philosophy) and reality, seeing history as shaped more by societal conditions and base human instincts than by philosophical guidance. He adopts a "fatalistic" acceptance of this inevitable, natural flow of change but insists on actively accelerating it (like a "kwaeryuje" or accelerating agent). The "new movement" he proposes involves radical internal destruction—discarding all inherited forms, morals, religions, and philosophies within oneself—followed by external societal transformation, ultimately leading to the construction of a new world, philosophy, and morality. From a Korean colonial history perspective, this 1921 essay is significant. Written shortly after the March 1st Movement, its themes of radical destruction of the past and construction of a "new world" reflect the intense desire for fundamental change among Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule. The call to discard tradition and even "historical records," while framed universally, resonates with contemporary debates about overcoming perceived stagnation and forging a modern Korean identity separate from both a flawed past and colonial imposition. The somewhat contradictory blend of fatalism and activism might reflect the strategic necessity of focusing on internal/spiritual transformation when external political action was severely restricted.

改造-改造라는 말은 왼 世界 사람의 한가지 부르짓는 熟語가 되엇다. 改造라 하면 그 意味를 解釋하기에 그다지 어려웁지도 아니하고 깁히 생각할 必要도 업다. 그러나 이 말을 解釋함에는 두 가지 方面이 잇다. 하나는 內的 改造, 하나는 外的 改造, 이 두가지를 解釋할 수 잇스이 外的 改造는 우리의 耳目에 接觸하야 그의 成長을 能히 判見할 수 잇다. 그러나 內的 改造, 이것은 吾人의 耳目으로 能히 그의 成長 進化를 識覺키 難한 것이다. 그리하야 外的 改造, 그는 能히 天地萬物과 宇宙 全體의 物的 萬類를 能動하는 外的 意味를 自持하얏스며 內的 改造, 그는 이 宇宙 全體와 天地 萬物을 能動能移하는 物的 運動의 裏面에서 그의 消長翻覆의 原動力이 되겟다. 그리하야 그의 發動은 秩序가 整然하게 進行되는 것이다. 우리가 보기에는 外的 改造 그것이 먼저 進行되는 듯하다. 그러나 外的 改造는 現勢를 意味함이요 內的 改造는 潛勢를 意味함이다. 그의 進行되는 順序를 말하면 먼저 潛勢的 運動이 잇고 後에 現勢的 運動이 일어나는 것이다. 그리하야 現勢는 比喩하면 肉體와 갓고 潛勢는 우리의 精神과 갓다. 精神이 업시 肉體가 動作치 못할 것은 말할 必要도 업고 勿論 精神의 作用이 發動한 然後에야 비롯오 肉體의 動作을 일으킬 수 잇는 것이다. 그리하야 왼 宇宙 가온대 生長消滅과 進化翻覆이 모도다 潛勢的 運動이 잇슨 然後에 現勢的 運動을 發하는 것이다. 그럼으로 보기에 아모 것도 보이지는 아니하나마 그의 勢力은 能히 天地를 個擧할 수 잇스며 宇宙의 充滿한 그의 氣力을 發하는 때에는 如何한 巨力으로도 能히 禦制할 수 업는 것이다. 이는 곳 그 運動이 왼 宇宙 全體의 內在的 生命임으로써일다. 그러나 그의 進行하는 跡後에는 慘憺한 現象을 이루는 것이다. 그의 進行되는 當場에는 無限한 悲哀가 잇고 煩惱가 잇고 無限한 躊躇가 잇고＜55＞無限한 爭意가 잇는 것이다. 不完全이 完全에 進할 때 不圓滿이 圓滿에 進할 때 不充實이 充實에 進할 때 醜惡이 優美에 進할 때 不安正이 安正에 進할 때 不公平이 公平에 進할 때 亂散에서 靜逸에 進하는 그의 途程에는 完全이 먼저 不完全을 破壞하여야 할 것이요, 圓滿이 不圓滿을 破壞하여야 할 것이요, 充實이 不充實을 破壞하여야 할 것이요 優美가 醜惡을 破壞하여야 할 것이요 安正이 不安正을 破壞하여야 할 것이요 公平이 不公平을 破壞하여야 할 것이요 靜逸이 亂散을 破壞하여야 할 것이다.

懷疑的 思想도 이 時代의 主潮일다. 우리가 오늘날 過去를 無視하고 過去의 萬般成業을 懷疑하는 것이＜59＞ 곳 過去의 모든 事業의 結果가 不完全함과 不公平임을 覺함으로써이라 하면 더욱이 方今 吾人의 懷疑的 傾向과 宿命的 態度가 모도다 自然의 必然的 一局部 現像이라 함을 더욱 明白히 辨明할 수 잇다. 이는 곳 모든 것이 自然임을 識覺함으로써 이다. 나는 어대까지던지 宿命的 態度에 停立하려 한다. 나는 어대까지던지 저 大宇宙의 必然的 動作을 抵抗하랴고 努力하지 아니하겟다. 내가 至今 以下에 記錄하야 보랴는 新運動의 思想까지도 全혀 이 思想의 餘影이라 하여도 過言은 아니요 나의 思想의 根本的 基礎는 먼저 宿命的이다.「自然的으로 흐르는 너를 어찌할 道里가 업다」그러나 이 宿命的 態度는 決코 消極的은 아니다. 孟子의 말하던 擴充이라는 意味와 갓다. 宿命的 態度를 가지고 形而上 問題를 가지고는 그만 辱을 보려고 한다. 그리하야 有이냐? 無이냐? 生이냐? 死이냐? 主觀이냐? 客觀이냐? 하는 問題는 그만 斷念하겟다. 그리고 나의 眼目은 한 번 歷史的 方面으로 또는 思潮와 人心에 傾注하여 보랴고 한다. 이것이 곳 나의 첫생각의 느낌이다. -必然的으로 흐르는 너를 어찌할 道理가 업다.-
우리가 지금 새삼스럽게 形而上 問題를 떠드를 餘暇가 잇는 사람이냐 하면 그러치 아니하다. 古代에는 吾人의 理想이 全혀 自然에 着眼하야 理致를 캐이고 理智를 發見하던「소크라테쓰」「뿌라톤」「아리스토텔쓰」의 「이데아(Idea)」「오이따모니아(Eudamonia)」를 發現할 조흔 八字를 타지 못한 現代人은 가장 크게 注目할 것이 무엇보다도 現實일다. 다시 말하면 理想的이니 主知的이니 떠드는 이보다도 우리에게 迫頭한 生活問題 그의 根本的 動機인 社會問題 處世間法을 硏究하여야 하겟다. 쉽게 말하면 우리는 萬野에 그득한 穀食을 두고도 한 그릇 밥을 먹지 못하고 萬山에 그득 싸힌 黃金을 두고도 저에게는 白銅錢 한푼도 업슴으로 空然한 怨望과 不平이 잇는 이 問題를 먼저 解決하여야 될 것이 아니랴?
時代는 確實히 過渡期임이 分明하다. 改造期임이 分明하다. 懷疑期임이 分明하다. 破壞機임이 分明하다. 그럼으로 苦痛時代임이 分明하다. 煩惱時代임이 分明하다. 萬億萬이 떠드는 生活問題, 其他 모든 不安定과 不平은 東에서 西에 南에서 北에 떠드지 안는 곳이 업시 떠들게 되엇스나 그러나 그네는 아즉까지 完全한 方向을 求하지 못하얏다. 暗黑한 窮窟 속에서 목이 쉬일만콤 새(新) 무엇을 부르지즈며 찻는다. 눈이 뚤허지도록 무엇을 차저 보랴하며 무엇을 삷혀 보랴는 것은 곳 새(新)로운＜60＞ 것 그것이다. 그러나 아즉것 새로운 그것이 우리의 눈압헤 오지 아니함도 아니요 잇지 아니할 理致도 업스나 그네는 아즉것 發現치 못함은 다만 簡易한 한가지 방침이 잇슬 뿐이나 그 方針을 아즉것 實現치 못한 까닭이다. 아니 實現치 못함이 아니라 實로 實現치 아니함이다. 이는 그 方法이 簡易한 所以일다. 그러나 그는 어는 方面으로 至極히 어려웁기도 하다. 얼른 듯기에는 알기도 쉽고 하기도 容易할 것 갓지마는 그 實現하는 跡後에는 먼저 말한 바와 가티 悲哀·慘泣·怨恨·苦痛·煩惱가 잇스며 그의 實現되는 때에는 黃金萬能力으로도 이즐 수 업는 巨力과 威氣가 잇서야 비롯오 그 煩惱를 지나 歡喜에, 苦痛을 지나 快樂에, 怨恨을 지나 幸福에, 慘泣을 지나 歌舞에, 나아갈 수 잇스니 이도 또한 無始에 通過하는 大自然에 自然的 通路를 追하는 自然-그의 勢力이니 그의 勢力은 곳 吾人의 內心에 在하야 吾人으로써 實行하라는 큰 使命을 나려준 것일다. 이 使命은 곳 現代人의 使命이요 現代人의 努力할 바이요 現代人의 모든 勇氣를 다하야 勝捷을 어더야 할 바이다. 너의 손에 칼이 잇거던 가랴는 너의 길 압헤 惡鬼의 生命을 斷하며 너의 손에 斧斤이 잇거던 그 進路에 막힌 鐵條網을 끈코 나아가야 할 것이다. 네가 萬若 그 鐵條網 안에서 죽더라도 그 斧斤은 너의 子孫에게 傳하여라. 그 斧斤으로 너의 子孫이 또 그 鐵條網을 끈허야만 하겟다. 그리하여야 너의 子孫萬代가 平和의 歌舞를 어들 것임으로써 일다. 족음도 躊躇할 것도 아니요 彷徨할 것도 아니요 恐怖할 것도 아니요 또 疑心할 것도 아니다. 다만 너의 心血을 注하야 進할 밧게 別道理가 업다.
우리는 方今 改造를 떠들며 懷疑的 傾向 속에서 외우치는 分子일다. 改造라는 意味의 裏面에는 무슨 不完全이 잇고 不充實이 잇스며 懷疑라는 意味의 裏面에는 무슨 反動的 心理를 가지고 그의 對象되는 무슨 新曙光을 確實치 못하나마 족으마한 한줄기 빗을 본 까닭인 意味가 包含하얏다. 方今 時代 思潮와 人心傾向은 가장 此에 適切하다. 우리는 過去의 모든 形式과 모든 遺物인 宗敎·倫理·道德·哲學 모든 것이 矛盾이요 皮相이요 外的이요 空想이요 理論 뿐임을 발견하얏슴으로 이보다 나은 무엇을 確實히 보지는 못하고도 그 理想鄕을 行하려한다. 그리하야 우리의 腦裏에는 누구던지 過去의 遺物인 道德·倫理·宗敎·哲學을 無價値에 돌리고 無形한 그 裏面에는 하나업시 破壞하야 버렷다. 무엇 무엇할 것 업시 다!!! 宗敎도 倫理도 道德도＜61＞ 哲學도 하나 업시 打破하고 그네는 一新運動에 着手케 되엇다. 第一步를 進하야 性的 戰爭場으로 나아가게 되엇다. 나는 이 打破的 傾向을 막으려고 努力치 아니하겟다. 이는 곳 自然的 現象임으로 抑制할 수도 업고 抑制할 必要도 업는 理由임으로써 일다. 나는 그의 가는 進路에서 暫間 外對的 態度를 가지고 흐르는 그 물결을 그저 두고 보랸다. 아니 흐르는 그에게 快流劑를 먹이려 한다. 그의 終局에 돌아오는 永遠의 平和와 永遠의 安樂을 보기 위하야 아니 永遠은 아닐다. 永遠의 平和와 永遠의 安樂은 다만「푸」氏나 「아」氏의 말하던 形而上的 超世間的 世界에서 밧게는 볼 수 업는 極樂이다. 人生은 물결과 갓다. 變化가 잇다. 翻覆이 잇다. 平和가 잇는 때에는 滅亡이요 沈默일 것이다. 그러나 이러한 不平과 不安定과 矛盾에서 不完全이나마 完全에, 不充實이나마 充實에, 不確立이나마 正義에, 新面目을 對하기 爲함으로써 이다. 그리하야 그네의 努力 끗에는 平和가 잇고 安樂이 잇기를 希望한다. 그는 樂의 世界다. 그는 新世界다. 그는 新宇宙이다. 우리가 바라는 理想鄕이다. 그 世界를 차지랴면 新指南針이 잇서야 할 것이다. 이는 곳 新哲學의 建設이다. 나는 이 新宇宙의 建設을 希望하며 新哲學의 建立을 要求한다. 方今은 矛盾時代에서 正義時代에 改造하는 過渡期일다. 나는 이 過渡期에 臨하야 努力하려는 바는 곳 快流劑일 뿐일다. 이 快流劑는 畢竟코 吾人에게 彼岸의 理想鄕을 나아가는대 躊躇함이 업슴을 意味함이니 現代人은 前章에 말함가튼 氣力을 集中하야 먼저 自身에 在한 因襲的 모든 形式과 道德·宗敎·倫理·哲學, 모든 資産을 打破하고 後에 家庭에 在한 모든 形式的 皮相的인 遺物을 打破하고 널리 世界에 모든 因襲的 歷史記錄을 불사르고 世界의 큰 部分으로부터 自我라는 單稱에 이르기까지의 모든 形式을 다 打破한 然後에 新世界·新哲學·新宇宙·攀°道德·新倫理·新宗敎를 建設하여야 할 것이니 힘잇는 者여, 힘을 아낄 것도 아니요. 입잇는 者여, 입을 봉할 것도 아니요. 귀잇는 者여, 귀를 막을 것도 아니다. 그 新宇宙의 建設과 新哲學의 建立은 作者-後日에 期會를 어더 쓰려하거니와 方今 이 題目 下에 쓰랴는 것은 第一次的으로 新哲學, 新宇宙 建設의 意義를 主觀的 立脚地에서 쓰랴는 것이다. 쉽게 보기에는 階級이 極甚하고 物質의 不公平을 떠드는 露國에서 일어난 亂黨의 이른바 打破한 手段가트나 物質에 偏重한 그와 다르며 外的으로 나타난 物的 打破도 아니라. 吾人의 內心에 各持한＜62＞ 習慣이 먼저 第一次的으로 打破되고 다음에 世界에 進하야 精神的으로 打破되어야 할 것이. 나는 이곳에 心血을 傾注하야 努力하랴 한다. 新運動을 發코저 한다. 이와 가튼 思想이 나의 腦裏에 潑剌하고 잇다. 이것이 곳 나의「過渡期에 立한 余의 新運動」이라는 要節이다. 이 運動은 破壞를 意味할 뿐만 아니라 破壞에서 다시 建設을 要求한다. 나는 끄트로 建設者와 役事者에세 이 글을 올린다.
