讀者 交情欄 (Readers' Friendship Column)
讀者通信 (Reader Correspondence)
From Taiwan, addressed to the various gentlemen of the Kaebyŏk Company. Dear Brother Kaebyŏk, sir. Although I first shook hands with you upon your birth, having drifted overseas and suffering hardship in life, not only being unable to distinguish eyes from nose (眼鼻莫開) but also enduring various external sufferings and numerous dissatisfactions, it is only today that my heartfelt admiration (心悅誠服) for you begins. Climbing the altar, dancing with hands and feet (手舞足蹈), and praising with mouth and pen (口歌筆讚) feels exceedingly late, and I cannot help but reproach myself. Ah, Brother Kaebyŏk, the world is one sea, and Kaebyŏk is one warship. In such a perilous (艱險) and cursed (咀呪) sea of the world (世海), having been safely born (順産), as soon as you began emitting the steam of hope with the purpose of bringing light to the sun and moon of the dark Land of Hibiscus (槿域) and rescuing the society of the corrupt descendants of Tan'gun (檀孫), seeing today when the shore of satisfaction is not far off, the various gentlemen of the editorial department, serving as captains, have overcome myriad difficulties through their struggling lives and sacrificial spirit. They have pointed the compass needle (羅盤 指針) towards the foreheads (頂門) of stragglers (落伍者) and opened the path for a fulfilling friendship column. Ah, how brilliant! This has placed the laurel wreath (月桂冠) upon our sirs' heads. The readers filling the seats, my brothers, with pure sincerity and fervent praise (讚辭), loudly chant "Manse!" and advance the steps (步武) of civilization onto the path of survival (活路) of current society, aiming to fill the prosperous Mugunghwa with the fruits of Kaebyŏk. Ah, how exhilarating! I hope and celebrate that this will place the championship flag (優勝旗) in the hands of us readers. Ah, Brother Kaebyŏk, as a final word, I pray for your eternal life (萬壽無疆) along with the white mountains and green waters. Concurrently, regarding the kind instructions (下敎) from the many loving sirs, I wish to repay (報答) even one ten-thousandth, but I only lament that my pen, lacking cultivation (素養), is too dull (鈍) and clumsy (拙).
Sirs, I hope you will correct and edit (訂正添削) this. Though childish (幼穉), I intend to continue writing articles about the situation of our compatriots, the customs of Taiwan, and matters concerning the indigenous people (蕃人). Wishing the various sirs good health and clear writing, I shall end here.
...(Night of 1921, Feb 3rd, from Taiwan, 16-year-old Pak Yunwŏn)...
To Kim Ki-o of Ŏnyang Sŏbu-ri! Regarding your inquiry (問議), the manuscript paper of the Kaebyŏk Company costs 80 chŏn for one hundred sheets, and postage is 2 chŏn. The procedure is handled (取扱) by the Kaebyŏk Company's agency department (代理部). ...(By the columnist)...
To Pyŏkp'asaeng of Kangnŭng! Your submitted writing was well received. Your intention is truly appreciated and welcomed, but worldly affairs do not always go according to one's wishes. Although there may be some who succeed through diligent self-study (苦學), it is difficult to assert that everyone can <114> achieve it. It seems better not to act rashly (輕擧) just yet (卒地), but to slowly (徐徐) research and decide your course of action (去就).
...(Reporter's reply)...
To Sŏrwŏlsaeng of North Kando! What you said is reasonable (當然), but it is somewhat difficult (難處) to publish limitless writings in the limited Readers' Friendship Column. Not only that, but writings scribbled randomly (亂筆雜書) on scraps of paper (片紙), not even on manuscript paper, are often very difficult to read. Such items are often unavoidably discarded (沒書). In the future, please be mindful to use manuscript paper and write in clear block script (楷書) so it is easy to recognize. Another point to note is that if the main text is too long, that also becomes a reason why it cannot be published in the Friendship Column. This is because it is difficult to use this narrow Friendship Column for the writings of just one or two people.
...(Reporter's reply)...
To Ilmunsaeng of Ŭiju! As yet, there is no translation (飜譯) of Rousseau's "Social Contract" (民約說) in our Chosŏn. I also do not clearly remember which bookstore (書店) in Japan published it as a separate volume (單行本). I seem to recall reading Rousseau's "Social Contract" once in a supplement (增刊號) to the Japanese magazine Taiyō, in a booklet (冊子) titled Meiji Meicho Shu (Collection of Famous Meiji Works). Besides that, there are many other books in Japan suitable for studying Rousseau.

文林 (Literary Forest)

Reading the Full Account of This Magazine's Writing Incident (筆禍) - Changnyŏn Paek Tŏkkyo
The peninsular land (半島江山), asleep for decades in entrenched customs (固襲), voiced a great demand for new civilization, and the peninsular youth, long thirsty, had a fervent desire (仰朢) for new knowledge. At such a time, when we heard the first cry (呱呱聲) of our Mr. Kaebyŏk's birth with the inaugural issue last June, who could suppress feelings of joyous excitement (欣喜雀躍)? That no particular incidents occurred as the issues accumulated thereafter must be the result of the strenuous efforts (奮鬪努力) of the various gentlemen in the editorial office. However, unexpectedly, concerning the article titled "Demands Regarding Youth Groups in Various Regions" in the December 1st issue last year, No. 6, the Tongdaemun Police Station summoned Mr. Yi Tu-sŏng, the publisher of our Kaebyŏk magazine, and based on Article OO of the Newspaper Law (新聞紙法), citing the publication (揭載) of articles beyond academics and arts, delivered a summary judgment (卽決言渡) imposing a fine (罰金) of fifty yen. Unsatisfied (不服), he requested (請求) a formal trial (正式裁判), and it is common knowledge (共知), having been reported piece by piece (縷縷) in the newspapers, that the public trial was scheduled to open at the Kyŏngsŏng District Court around mid-February. How anxious and frustrated must we readers be, who love Mr. Kaebyŏk just like the chief editor (主幹兄님) and other gentlemen sweating in the editorial office for Mr. Kaebyŏk?
While public opinion (與論) regarding our Mr. Kaebyŏk became the topic of conversation without pause in cities and villages alike, Messrs. Chang Su, Pak Sŭng-bin, Kim Ch'an-yŏng, Yi Ki-ch'an, and Yi Sŭng-u—stalwarts (重鎭) in the legal world (辯護士界) who have always devoted themselves to defending (擁護) human rights (人權) and freedom of speech (言論)—have recently expressed particularly great sympathy (同情) for our Mr. Kaebyŏk. Regardless of the eventual outcome, the fact that they have first taken charge (引受) of the entire case brings us readers, along with the gentlemen of the editorial department, to express our gratitude (感謝) to these individuals who show infinite sympathy for our beloved Mr. Kaebyŏk. At the same time, as readers, we <114> eagerly await and hope, eyes wide open, for the final outcome concerning Mr. Kaebyŏk. May you, Mr. Kaebyŏk, who bears a great mission (使命), advance further (更進) from now on. With a complete ideal, noble aspiration (抱負), and solid strength (實力), propagate (宣傳) new civilization and new knowledge on the peninsula, awaken (覺醒) our compatriots slumbering in a drunken dream (醉夢), and moreover, become a guide (引導者) for the path ahead like a lamp (燈燭) in the dark crossroads (昏衢). Finally, we hope that Mr. Kaebyŏk, with an eternal and infinite lifespan (永遠無窮 壽命), will never leave our side.

Song of the Unfortunate - Kyŏngsŏng Paek Su-saeng
Lips, oh, my unfortunate lips!
The sweet nectar to be sucked by a lover,
The warm blood that made the honey,
The fine color that adorned the blood,
The fragrance soaked in honey,
No. Flower petals hoping to taste spring,
Withered, flown, faded, cooled, scattered.
Ah, ah,
No longer a bud (蓓蕾) about to bloom,
But trampled and rotting under a shepherd boy's (牧童) foot,
A two-leaf seedling in natural compost (自然堆肥).

Rushing like a gale to the temple of the universe,
To offer life's report and prayer to the deity—
Not the floral wreath to be offered on the holy altar (聖壇),
Intended to sing of the cooperation of red souls—
Those who would mock (嘲殺) powdered aesthetes (唯美主義者)—
Rusty aristocrats (貴族主義者)—
But a desolate wind that curses life,
A multi-angled wind cave (多角風穴) where it blows aimlessly and pours down.

That old trembling tone (顫音) that made the lover weep,
Using the horizon (地平線) beyond as a mirror,
Looking down vacantly at the black mica (黑雲母) colored sky,
No, I don't know. Perhaps some frame of the universe,
It might exist as if non-existent,
And never, never again
Will it flow out more.
Made to cry with pleading words—
Made to laugh with an agitated heart—
Startled by fickle changes,
Dreams of smile, tear-drop, terror are plastered over,
Binding the erasure of the lips—.

Oh—lips, lips, my lips,
Beauty, Sincerity, Passion
Have frozen in the frost falling out of season,
Changed——.

Pupils, oh—my unfortunate pupils!
Let's say everyone saw the title,
But the content, which no one but the lover could *read*,
That *secret pattern*, the light,
Where did you discard it?
Is it a bottomless pit (無底坑) never to be found?
Or is it the bottom of the Yellow River (黃河) from which it can still be retrieved?
Ah, ah,
You are already not a star on Earth, but the objective lens (對物窓) of a camera obscura (暗箱).
A mindless beggar
Who only knows how to receive and not how to give.

Even the most cunning, azure image,
The wall containing the mystery (秘奧)
That gently transformed (褪化) it into goodness, has crumbled.
The darkness filling the inside of the wall,
Riding the vibration of opportunity,
Billows and overflows.
Upon those dark waves,
The outline of an unknown world
Forms a mirage (蜃氣樓).

A sacred image (聖像) like the setting sun,
A mouth that smiled only at the corners while holding it in,
Eyes misted with something steaming,<115>
The fence where the lover's face was drawn with deep * patterns and blue colors (丹靑),
Falls over and is washed away by the waves.
What magic (魔術) is this?
That it becomes clearer the more it's washed,
That it opens its lips again to boast white teeth,
That it fixes its pupils straight ahead like a living face,
Image, lover, woman, witch!
Is this the art of transformation (化身術) preparing to chew and melt me?
Or is it the error of my pupils that faithfully retreated?

Oh—Pupil, Pupil, my Pupil!
Radiance, Sanctity, Faithfulness (信實),
Fading away in the ceaselessly pounding waves
Worn away——.

The unfortunate one
Knows only this song to sing.
But the song flies away
And carelessly strikes anywhere,
—On stone—on trees—on rose petals—on 말꼿 lips—.

But, but,
All you things that hate to listen,
Drink the brand-new dawn wine,
With the dissonant (燥音) musician (樂師),
Laugh until your mouths split,
And raise your glasses together (合杯).
The unfortunate one,
Hearing the clinking sound
Of their glasses touching,
Can suddenly transform into a fortunate one.

My Thoughts (懷抱) to Brother N in Manchuria - Cho O Hagin
Pitiless time (光陰) is swift like an arrow, flowing like water; already 4 months have passed (亙) since you and I parted ways (作別). When Brother N went to Manchuria, late flowers (晩花) lingered (殘存) as if holding back (抑留) the spring season. After Brother N left, green shade (綠陰) thickened the trees (濃樹), fragrant grasses (芳草) covered the ground, boasting the Tano festival (端陽佳節). Then, changing once more, it created a world of intense heat (炎蒸) like fanning a fire in a red furnace (紅爐), tormenting us humans. Its emissary, the three hottest days of summer (三伏), passed, and the longed-for beginning of autumn (立秋), which should refresh our spirits, also went. Now, winter has come with clear snow (晴雪) filling heaven and earth (滿乾坤). The longing (相思) between us brothers, separated (分在) by two thousand li, grows ever more earnest (懇切). Brother, relying on the lonely land (孤域), looks at the white clouds day after day and remembers (憶) his younger brother; the younger brother, in a distant place, gazes at the bright moon night after night and thinks of (思) his elder brother. However, when the white clouds thicken and the bright moon sets, longing turns into solitary grief (獨悲), and the feeling cannot be stopped.
After the 16th day of the seventh lunar month passed, returning geese flew from the southern sky. Brother N's fine letter (錦字) flew to my window. Washing my hands and reading it respectfully (盥水擎讀), my mind felt refreshed and joyful (欣喜) as if meeting Brother N's esteemed presence (淸儀) from two thousand li away. And I understand and remember well Brother N's instructions (下敎).
You wrote that since going to Manchuria 4 months ago, you lost the friend called 'Joy' and couldn't stop sighing (歎息); indeed, you must have been too lonely (孤寂). However, I think this way. Saying this might perhaps clash (抵觸) with Brother N's sensitive feelings, for which I feel apologetic (未安), but there are many friends in nature. Elks and deer (麋鹿) are friends, grass and trees are friends, mountains and water are friends, wind and moon are friends, joy is a friend, and consequently, sorrow (悲哀) is also a friend, and hardship (苦) is also a friend. Therefore, I reckon (思料) that Brother N, among many friends, has only lost the friend named 'Joy'. Even if one only has the friend named 'Hardship', isn't that better than having no friend from nature at all? An old saying goes, "When hardship ends (苦盡), sweetness comes (甘來)." The hardship that is now Brother N's friend will depart like the cruel official (酷吏) Sambok (三伏) that tormented our bodies, and the lost joy will come like the autumn wind that refreshes (爽快) our spirits. It is true. To alert (警) the deaf ears of the twenty million brothers and sisters of our peninsula<116> and open their blind eyes (盲目), if there are those who harm our compatriots, we should punish and expel them (誅之斥之) with justice and humanity; if there are those who protect our compatriots, we should call and awaken them (喚之醒之) with evening bells and morning gongs (暮鐸晨鍾). Reporting political changes (政變) overseas instantly by telegraph (隨時電聞) and recording the people's conditions (民情) in the regions daily without omission (無漏日載), becoming an advisor (顧問) to politicians (政客), a model (師表) for society, good news (福音) for the good people (良民), and the King of Hell (閻王) for corrupt officials (汚吏). With ropes (繩) twisting on paper (轉輾) and spirits (鬼神) appearing and disappearing under the pen (筆下出沒), standing solemnly (儼然) as the shield (干城) of the peninsula, transcendently (超然) relying on the unique thunder and wind (獨步 風雷) of Manchuria, great and mighty (偉且大), divine and sagely (神且聖), the Manchuria Daily News (滿洲日報君), having suddenly fallen ill (得病) and groaned (呻吟) in bed for nearly 4 months, with medicine (藥石) proving ineffective (奏効치 못하야), day after day we couldn't hear good news. As seeing its face became rare (踈), we suspected (疑) it might just make a single cry and stop, like the crane crossing east of the river (江東過鶴 一鳴遂止格).
Brother N and I, especially, worried incessantly (疑懼不巳) that the myriad eyes of our compatriots would become blind again and their myriad ears deaf again, praying to heaven and earth (天祝地願) for the paper's swift recovery (速療) and long life (壽). As the old teaching says, "Utmost sincerity moves heaven" (至誠感天). Whether our compatriots' sincerity reached heaven, or whether heaven did not abandon (棄) our compatriots, as the new cool air (新凉) entered the fields and villages (郊墟), the paper's illness recovered (回春). Within about ten days, with a glorious face (榮光) and bright and upright character (光明且正直 品格), it is said to be running (走行) to the doors of our compatriots, celebrating its recovery (快蘇) and announcing (宣傳) that with its republication (*), it will pursue its long-accumulated (陳積) objectives with doubled effort (倍前的). If true, it is needless to say how joyful (欣喜) our compatriots will be, and Brother N, truly, must be dancing with joy as if drunk or mad. When I saw the quatrain in Brother N's instruction mentioning the republication of the Manchuria Daily News, more than anything else, I could not suppress my heart's unique joy and pride (獨喜自負). We must not forget how powerful a golden saying the old teaching "Hardship ends, sweetness comes" (苦盡甘來) is. Brother N, wasn't it because the Manchuria Daily News befriended illness and hardship for 4 months that you could not befriend joy? And isn't it leaving hardship behind as it recovers (快蘇)? From now on, Brother N stands on a living stage (活舞臺) and will enjoy eternal and infinite joy (樂). Brother N, hardship is truly the seed of joy. I envy (歆羡) the hardship that Brother N befriended for 4 months and wish to befriend it once, and also wish to ascend the stage where Brother N stands today...
Brother N, please take good care of your esteemed self (貴體 保重) so that you may warmly grasp my wrist once again. At the same time, I pray that the Manchuria Daily News, by preserving its post-illness strength like mountains and seas, may live forever (萬壽無疆). (End)

**Summary**

This section of Kaebyŏk features reader correspondence ("讀者 交情欄") and literary contributions ("文林"). Readers express admiration for the magazine, inquire about practical matters (manuscript paper), seek advice (career choices), and offer feedback (submission guidelines). The "文林" section includes an article detailing the magazine's legal troubles (a fine under the Newspaper Law for an article on youth groups), expressing support from readers and lawyers. It also contains an emotional poem titled "Song of the Unfortunate," a personal letter discussing life in Manchuria and the hoped-for revival of the "Manchuria Daily News," and several short thematic poems about Kŭmgangsan and "newness" in the era. For historians, this text offers valuable insights into the intellectual climate of 1920s colonial Korea: the readers' engagement with nationalist-leaning publications like Kaebyŏk, the constraints imposed by Japanese censorship (the "writing incident"), the activities and concerns of Koreans abroad (Taiwan, Manchuria, North Kando), the dissemination of Western ideas (Rousseau), and the blend of personal reflection, social commentary, and literary expression characteristic of the period's magazines. The discussion around the Manchuria Daily News's potential revival is particularly interesting regarding diaspora media and nationalist sentiment outside the peninsula.
