Reporting on the Current Situation of Our Brethren Studying Abroad in Tokyo, Japan
						Pak Ch'unp'a

It has not been long since I came to Tokyo. The rice I ate in my homeland (本國) still seems undigested, and the clothes I wore in my homeland have only just begun to show their fine wear. Having crossed East so recently, I am quite clumsy in all aspects. I do not know well where East or West is, nor whether the water flows North or South. If I want to go somewhere, I cannot because I do not know the street names (町名), and if I want to find someone, I fret because I cannot find the house number (番地). With such clumsy eyes, in the position of a country bumpkin, is it not too presumptuous for me to dare report on the current situation of our brethren in Tokyo? No, isn't it quite an outrageous act? Setting aside my own unease, I feel I might receive scolding from the many brethren in Tokyo and might cause dissatisfaction among our compatriots (同胞) back home. However, thinking deeply, it seems there might be those among you back home who wish to know our current situation in Tokyo, and I also have a desire to inform you, and furthermore, there was also a request from Mr. Kaebyŏk (開闢 君). Therefore, despite my clumsy eyes, and after asking and listening to brethren who have been in Tokyo for many years and possess much experience and insight (經歷과 識見), although it may be unsatisfactory, I present this to you back home.

As written in the title, it is only about the current situation (現況). If possible, it would be good to speak of the past history as well, but I lack the ability (實力) to do so, and also, because Hakchigwang (學之光) already introduced it in previous years, I fear the offense of repetition, so I will omit it and speak only of the current situation.

Just as I longed for it when I was back home, I believe there must be such curiosity (궁금症) among you back home.

Tokyo? How many of our youth are studying abroad (留學) in Tokyo? <81> Also, from which province and which region are the most numerous? How many are from Hamgyŏng Province, and how many are from Kyŏngsang Province? Also, what schools are they attending (就學), and what subjects are their intended fields of study (學科)? Also, what are their characters (人物), and what are their thoughts (思想)? Also, what is their living situation (生活 狀態), and what about their fellowship (親睦 與否)? How much are the monthly school expenses (一個月 學費), and how much money is required until graduation (卒業期)? How many are working students (苦學生), and how many are students receiving financial support (給費) from home? If only I knew a little...

This seems to be the general curiosity you all have. Now, if I were to roughly answer what you wish to know, our brethren studying abroad in Tokyo are generally as follows:

Kyŏnggi Province    127 people  Ch'ungch'ŏng North & South Provinces  64

Chŏlla North & South Provinces  197    Kyŏngsang North & South Provinces  199

Hwanghae Province    17     P'yŏngan North & South Provinces  86

Kangwŏn Province     7     Hamgyŏng North & South Provinces  100

North Kando (北間島)     1     Unknown    104

Total   902

This is the general total number of our brethren in Tokyo. However, this survey list was investigated by the Student Association (學友會) at the end of January. It is said to be incomplete, of course. It is said that there are over 100 people not included in this survey list, and also that the number of newly arrived (新渡) students is not small. If surveyed meticulously, it is said that the number would surely reach 1,100-1,200 people. Compared to the end of the year before last and the beginning of last year, the number is said to have increased by more than half.

From this, we can well understand how high the passion for knowledge (知識熱) among our Chosŏn youth is, how much the passion for education (敎育熱) among our Chosŏn parents and elders has expanded, and also how promising the future (前途) of our Chosŏn is. This reporter is also overcome with joy (喜悅), and I know that the many parents and elders back home will also rejoice. Now, if I show the breakdown by the schools they attend, it is as follows:

Imperial University (帝國大學)   9   Waseda University (早稻田大學) 47

Meiji University (明治大學)  77   Keio University (慶應大學)   4

University of Commerce (商業大學)   9   Chuo University (中央大學)   42

Nihon University (日本大學)  37   Toyo University (東洋大學)   15

Hosei University (法政大學)   2   Senshu University (專修大學)   4

Sophia University (上智大學)   2   Rikkyo University (立敎大學)   2

Agricultural University (農業大學)   1   Soto University (曹洞大學)   1

Higher Technical School (高等工業)   3   Higher Normal School (高等師範)   3

Higher Sericulture School (高等蠶業)   1   Medical Specialty School (醫學專門)   1

Dental Specialty School (齒科專門)   3   Art School (美術學校)   4<82>

Music School (音樂學校)   2   Aoyama High School (靑山高校)   3

Meiji High School (明治高校)   3   Veterinary Specialty School (獸醫專門)   1

Physics School (物理學校)   1   Pharmacy School (藥學校)     1

Horticulture School (園藝學校)   1   Aviation School (飛行學校)   2

Colonial Trade School (植民貿易)   3   Various Middle Schools (각 中等學校)  379

Unknown (未詳)    238  Total (總計)    902

The above is a rough breakdown of the schools attended. Among them, those listed as Unknown and the newly arrived students, due to language issues, attend regular schools, foreign language schools, preparatory academies (普及 學院), and other language specialty schools in large numbers. Also, those who are young or wish to follow a practical path attend middle schools in large numbers. If I inquired more and spent more time, it would be good to mention their fields of study as well, but due to some lack of personal time, I can only leave it for a later occasion. However, asking those who know the situation of school attendance well, they say that specialized courses (專門科) are generally numerous. Among the specialized courses, law (法學), politics (政治), and economics (經濟) are said to be numerous. Fields like history (史學), literature (文學), or mathematics (數學) are said to be not as many. This is usually due to reasons of tuition cost (學費), required years (年限), and also ability (實力). Being constrained by tuition, years, and ability, it is said that there is a lack of people in the appropriate main courses (本科). Because it is easy to enter, the years are fewer, and the required ability is somewhat lower, enrollment in specialized courses becomes common. This cannot but be called a somewhat regrettable point. Furthermore, as you can see from the total table above, seeing the scarcity of people in schools for commerce (商業), industry (工業), or agriculture (農業), which should become the economic backbone (中軸) of Chosŏn, causes hidden concern. Also, that there are few who pay attention (留意) to central fields like fine arts (美術), music (音樂), or horticulture (園藝) – this can also be called a regrettable point. Setting that aside for the most part, looking at the aspect of thought (思想) among our brethren in Tokyo, there are many points to celebrate (贊賀), and I believe you all back home will be very pleased.

Individually, everyone subscribes to meritocracy (實力主義) and the emphasis on strength (强力主義). No matter how the world clamors, they say, "For me, only real ability matters," and "Even to go one step, which is like ten leagues, it cannot be done without strength (强力)." Therefore, they study much, research much, and also see, compose, and write much, and if their heads are not bulging [with knowledge], they always harbor dissatisfaction. Also, they train and test themselves mentally and physically, cultivating strength (强力) in both spirit and body. Although this reporter's days [here] are few and knowledge meager, what I see and hear is certainly so. Since they are like this individually, they are also like this as individuals within the group and as a collective. If someone says, "Let's gather," they gather all at once; if someone says, "Let's disperse," they disperse all at once. If something goes against their grain (脾胃), they raise their fists and strike it down, and if it is for righteousness (義), they do not avoid life or death. However, acting according to circumstances (見機而作), they absolutely do not attempt things that cannot be reasonably carried out. Thus, they reliably express national (民族的) ability <83> and thoroughly guarantee national strength (强力). They receive much sympathy from people inside and outside [Japan] and do not transgress from the standpoint of being members of the Chosŏn nation (朝鮮 民族). However, among us in Tokyo, there is a sense that we can be divided into two factions based on thought (思想). That is, it seems one group centers on the Chosŏn problem, and the other centers on world problems. In other words, one is the national movement (民族 運動), and the other is the popular movement (民衆 運動). Therefore, there are those who receive the assessment of being nationalists (國家主義者), and many who receive the assessment of being socialists (社會主義者). Thoughts are generally like that, and the living situation is mostly precarious (荀且). Since the economic world back home is so depressed, the lives of the students studying abroad in Tokyo, who are under the support of home, are naturally precarious; that is a fact. Among the thousand-odd brethren, it seems that working students (苦學生) constitute one-third to half of the number. Indeed, the living situation of these working student brethren has many points deserving sympathy and many points deserving praise (稱賀). Disregarding face (體面) and hardship (苦生), it is routine for them to sell their labor, regardless of cloudy or clear days. Those who labor during the day (晝間 勞働者) study at night school (夜學), and those who labor at night (夜間 勞働者) study during the day (晝學). Their labor includes medicine peddling (藥行商), newspaper delivery (新聞 配達), pulling rickshaws (人力車), and being factory workers (會社의 職工). From morning till evening, carrying a medicine bag and facing the people of a foreign city whose sentiments they do not know, what embarrassing situations must they endure? Striving all day long barely covers the day's food expenses (食價). Taking off their student uniforms, putting on deliveryman's (配達夫) clothes, wearing a bell, tucking newspapers under their arm, and heading to the gates of this house and that, what must their feelings be? Those pulling rickshaws would be the same, but the factory workers are said to have even more grievances (不平). Entering their world, where ethnic discrimination (民族別) is practiced, bearing the mark of a foreign country, how much wages (賃金) would they be given, and how much sympathy would they receive? Deeply feeling the words "Chōsenjin" (「죠센진」 - Korean), even if they do all the assigned work, it amounts to around 10 yen (圓) per month. With this, they have to buy food, buy clothes, pay monthly tuition (月謝金), buy books, pay streetcar fare (電車費), and live in all other aspects, so what difficulties must they face? The circumstances of living collectively, 3-4 or 5-6 people in a 6-tatami or 4-tatami room, cooking for themselves (自炊 生活), walking 10 ri (里) back and forth morning and evening, getting through the seasons with one set of clothes, being expelled from school (黜學) for not being able to pay tuition – in all aspects, there is much suffering (苦痛).

However, they have not the slightest aversion (厭症) or discouragement (縮氣). Their demeanor of being calm and composed (泰然自若), as if all is as they wish (萬事 如意), is clearly visible. Everything is proactive (積極). Labor is proactive, study is proactive, accordingly the body is proactive, and the spirit is proactive. If you seek character (人物), seek it in the world of working students; if you seek people of real ability (實力家), I say look also in the world of working students. They often say these words:

"If one lacks ambition (뜻), so be it; if one lacks sincerity (정성), so be it; if one intends not to do it, <84> so be it. But having already ambition, having sincerity, and intending to do it, what could be difficult (難事)? Labor – what is so difficult about it? As long as the strength of arms and legs is sturdy. Lack of money (無錢) – why is there no money? The world is full of money. Saying one cannot study because there are no books (冊) is also a lie. There are libraries (圖書館) on every street (街路街路), and every library is full of all kinds of books (萬般書), telling you to read as you please. Time (時間) – saying one cannot study because there is no time is also a lie. Wake up early in the morning and sleep late at night, that's enough. Sundays (日曜日) are sufficient, and holidays (祝祭日) are sufficient. Face (體面) – what is face in the position of a working student? Occupation (職業) is sacred. Earning one's own living is the fundamental duty (本分) of human life. Never mind rickshaws or newspaper delivery, I would even pull a dung cart (똥 구루마). Since we have already left our beloved parents and siblings, departed from our deeply connected homeland (故國), and set our minds on learning in a foreign land thousands of miles away (異域萬里), as long as we are not disabled or mentally deficient (病身 天痴), if we didn't intend to do it, so be it, but if we intend to do it, what could be difficult? Let's do whatever it takes. Let's do it persistently. If we do, the day of success (成功) will naturally come."

This is generally how stout men (丈夫) set their minds on learning. This is generally how promising young men (有爲의 男兒) are. They recite again and again the phrase, "A man sets his ambition and leaves his hometown; if his learning is not accomplished, he vows not to return." (男兒立志出鄕關, 學若不成誓不還) The current situation of the working students is generally like this. Other than that, the circumstances of those living ordinarily do not offer much to report. Some live in inns (旅館 生活), some eat at cafeterias (食堂 生活), some cook for themselves (自炊 生活), Sunday strolls (日曜의 散步), library visits (圖書館 去來), visiting friends (親舊 訪問), participating in meetings (會의 參與), watching plays at theaters (劇場의 觀劇), etc. – they pass the time busily (奔走) in this way and that. And fellowship (親睦) goes without saying. Being of the same blood and same clan (同血同族) with deep ties and great connections, it's natural even back home, so how much more so overseas? That needs no repetition. Tokyo is a place with high humidity (濕氣), so if one is even slightly negligent about hygiene (衛生), illness comes easily. Therefore, they bathe (沐浴) every other day, and bedding, clothes, etc., should be exposed to sunlight (日光) from time to time. Also, when a guest visits (客이 訪來), tea (茶) is offered as a matter of course, but if it is mealtime, eating together is the common custom (普通 風) among Tokyo students.

One final word I offer is for brethren back home who might intend to come to Tokyo: ordinarily, 60 yen (圓) is sufficient for one month's expenses (1個月 學費). Food costs (食價) are usually 25-26 yen, room rent (房稅) is 5-6 yen, school fees (學費) are 5-6 yen, so regular expenses amount to about 40 yen (假量). Other expenses like transportation fare (車費), social expenses (交際費), and book costs (書冊비) can be covered with 20 yen. However, 50 yen is also enough, and even with 40 yen, one can manage quite well. If room rent is separate and one only eats at cafeterias, 40 yen is also sufficient. If you come without such a budget (豫算), you will be engaged in labor, that is, a pure working student. However, as mentioned above regarding the life of working students, I believe that a person of mediocre character (如干 人物) cannot even conceive of undertaking such hardship. Talk is easy, but is reality like that? In any case, if many could come, it would be fortunate for Chosŏn. Although incomplete, this is all I have seen so far. Might this be of some reference (參與)? I deeply wish for the great fortune (多幸) of the brethren back home and hope that any brethren worthy of studying will quickly cross the Hyŏnhae Sea (玄海). Is this not the place called the center of East Asian civilization (東洋 文明)?

**Summary**

Writing in March 1921, Pak Ch'unp'a describes the situation of Korean students in Tokyo. He humbly admits his recent arrival but relays information gathered from others. He provides statistics showing over 900 students (likely over 1100), a significant increase, mainly from southern provinces, attending various universities and specialty schools, with a concentration in law, politics, and economics, but fewer in practical fields like commerce, industry, agriculture, or arts. He praises the students' determination and focus on gaining "real strength" (實力), noting a division between nationalist and socialist leanings. A large portion (1/3 to 1/2) are working students (苦學生) enduring harsh conditions—manual labor, poor living arrangements, discrimination—yet maintaining high morale and a proactive spirit, driven by ambition and patriotism. The author concludes by advising prospective students on monthly expenses (around 60 yen, minimum 40-50) and encouraging more to come study in Tokyo, the "center of East Asian civilization."

This article is valuable for historians as it provides a contemporary snapshot of the burgeoning Korean student community in Tokyo during a critical period of Japanese colonial rule. It reveals the strong desire for modern education among Koreans, the specific fields they pursued (and neglected), the economic hardships faced, the resilience and nationalist/ideological currents within the student body, and practical details about student life, including the stark reality for working students and the financial threshold required for study abroad. The statistics on regional origins and school enrollment offer concrete data on this important demographic.
