Play Script: The Eve
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl

A Room in a Hotel
Decorated in an old-fashioned style popular in the 18th century. Gold leaf is peeling, and murals are damaged, giving off an air of desolation.
The stage shows the room obliquely. From the center to the left is a large glass window. Faded dark red curtains hang there. To the left and right of the window, high up, are small gable-shaped windows, one each. Beneath them, vases are filled with flowers. And also a jet-black wooden cabinet with heavy carvings.
Towards the front left, a small Western-style door connects to the hallway. Above the central pillar hangs a large, old-fashioned clock.
To the right, obliquely inward, is a separate room like an alcove. Dark green curtains hang halfway down. In its center is a pure white bedroom. Above the bed is a small high window. Outside the glass window is a balcony.
Appropriate places in the room hold large and small tables, a large armchair, and 3 or 4 other chairs. Leather bags, luggage, and other travel gear and bundles are scattered about messily.
Dawn. The dawn following the first scene.
(At the curtain's rise, it is still dark. The room has the feel of being by the coast, with the sound of the tide heard nearby. The sound of a fierce wind. Inside the room, a lamp covered with a red shade barely scatters light.)
In the inner bedroom, Elena is dozing in the armchair, overcome with exhaustion.
The stage is quiet. The wall clock loudly ticks away, urging time onward, punctuated by the sound of the whirlwind.

Insarov. (Moaning in the bedroom) Ugh. Ugh. Ouch. Uuuugh. Elena! Rench'ich'i! (Elena, dozing lightly, does not wake.) Who's there? Who is it? (He sits bolt upright, then stumbles out, staggering. Still in his nightclothes, he holds a pistol in his hand.) Who's there? (He shouts in a hoarse voice, gripping the pistol as he walks towards the doorway. His expression shows the obsessive idea of someone awakened from a terrible nightmare. At the doorway, he fumbles, eyes wide open, staring blankly.)

Elena. (Wakes at the sound of the door clicking, turns, and sees her husband standing there) Ah, why are you standing there like that! (She rushes over.) <147>

Insarov. (As if barely regaining his senses. Quietly) Ah, Elena… Hasn’t Rench'ich'i come yet?

Elena. No. Why, is something wrong?

Insarov. Then was it a dream? (Looking outside) Ah, it’s not daybreak yet. Seeing how dark it is outside… (Stands blankly)

Elena. You'll catch a cold standing there like that.—Come, let's go to the bed.

Insarov. Somehow, I don’t feel like I can sleep. I don't know if Rench'ich'i will come either. I'm going to get up.

Elena. Then keep yourself warm. Yes. (Drapes his overcoat over his back and helps him into the armchair.) The dawn wind is cold.

Insarov. Ah, Elena. Somehow, I feel chills…

Elena. See? What dream did you have?

Insarov. It was a terrible dream. I suddenly opened my eyes while sleeping, and the space before that door was brightly lit, and outside the sun was shining intensely. In reality, I wasn't awake, it must have been a dream… Rench'ich'i was standing there, with Elena. And he kept beckoning me to come. Startled, just as I was about to go, he transformed into that rough-looking fellow who came looking for me yesterday, the one I said was a detective. That's why I was just about to drop the pistol.

Elena. You dream such things because you are thinking too much about Rench'ich'i. But was the man who came yesterday really a detective?

Insarov. A detective, certainly. A fellow hired by the Turkish (土耳其) government to trail us political offenders… Ah, if we linger here like this, who knows what misfortune we might encounter from such a man. Considering this and that, Rench'ich'i must come quickly…

Elena. He will come now that day is breaking. Don’t be so agitated… Ah, the newspaper came last night. And also a letter came from Mother.

Insarov. A letter came? What did she say?

Elena. Many things were written, but she said she is bored and misses me unbearably. She asked me to come visit Moscow (모스코) even if just for a day.

Insarov. She would say that. Truly, I have no words for my mother-in-law. But my father-in-law…

Elena. He just wanders around 놀라만(?). They say whenever he comes home, he makes life miserable for Mother. And <148> Mr. Bersenev (벨세넵) and Shubin (슈빈) are also gone somewhere. The whole house must feel empty, like a house after a big shamanistic ritual… But aside from that, there's something funny. Secretary K'ŭllat'ŭwisŭk'ii (클나트위스키이), you know, the one Father was making a fuss about making his son-in-law… has apparently married Shoya (쇼오야) who was at the house.

Insarov. Shoya… with that German (獨逸) brat… That’s funny.

Elena. A perfect match made in hell, aren't they… But my heart aches for Mother. Maybe that's why I see her in my dreams every night.

Insarov. We have sinned too much against your mother, who is all alone. Just for that, can we avoid punishment? Elena, our work is eight or nine-tenths done, yet being stuck here like this, don't you think it's heavenly punishment (天罰)?

Elena. There you go again with such useless words. Everything is fate (運命). We two came together like this in love. Whether long or short, we have lived in happiness (幸福). Why would that be a wicked thing? There’s nothing for which we should receive punishment for that. Does God begrudge humans the happiness He gives?

Insarov. But Elena, while we were finding happiness like this, didn't we have to grieve for the great misfortune of many people?

Elena. Mother, Father, Bersenev… and Shubin, also Secretary K'ŭllat'ŭwisŭk'ii… Ah, it's like a dream. We didn't have the time to think about their happiness.

Insarov. In any case, we must be prepared for our happiness not to last that long.

Elena. I am prepared for that. If you die, I will die with you. But even if we die, I don’t want to die in a murky, dark room like this.

Insarov. That's why I want to go back to our homeland as soon as possible. I want to step on the soil of our homeland at war just once before I die.

(Silence. Outside, the wind sounds extremely fierce. Occasionally, a steam whistle is heard.)

Elena. The wind is blowing fiercely again. (Saying this, she lifts the curtain and looks outside. The dim dawn light (曙光) enters through the window.) Ah, what a terrible storm (暴風). The sea is churning with white waves. <149>

Insarov. Elena, isn't Rench'ich'i coming?

Elena. He'll be here soon. But how can the ship depart with such a storm blowing?

Insarov. Is the wind that severe?

Elena. Yes, the ships on the sea are tossed about like leaves, rising and sinking, being winnowed. And the steamship is sounding its emergency whistle like that.

Insarov. Ah. Ah.

Elena. Look at me. Look at me. Don't lose heart (落心) like that; calm (鎭靜) your mind and wait for fortune to turn.

Insarov. Am I supposed to calm my mind? Am I supposed to calm my mind and wait? Our comrades (同志) in the homeland are urgently awaiting my return. If I don't go back, they can do nothing. They have no choice but to despair and scatter. Even if I could endure that, if our work, which has just begun to sprout like this, is trampled and destroyed before it can even bloom, who will be there to continue (繼續) this task? The Bulgarian people will perish forever under Turkey's harsh oppression (虐待).

Elena. Why are you doing this again? Getting so agitated (興奮) is bad for your health. You're sweating like this (wiping sweat from his forehead), and you have a fever (熱) again. You get a fever from agitating your mind unnecessarily. Please go to bed for a bit. I'll call a doctor.

Insarov. There's no need to call a doctor. If I just lie here like this for a bit, I'll get better soon. After breakfast, let's go for a walk together. There's nothing to worry about.

Elena. Yes, let's do that. Then I'll make you comfortable, so please relax your mind and sleep a little. Yes. Let's do that. (Moves her chair behind the armchair and places her arm under her husband's head as if cradling it.) Now, sleep a little.

Insarov. Mmm, that's very comfortable. Wake me when Rench'ich'i comes. If he has the ship ready, we must go soon, even in a moderate wind. We need to pack up various things too.

Elena. The luggage can be ready in no time, so don't worry at all. What's there so much to pack?

Insarov. Anyway, everything must be tied up (묵거). (Closes his eyes.)

Elena. (Touching her husband's forehead and hair) You've become so thin (수척하셧다). Ah, the wind is blowing so hard. How can the ship go in this wind… <150> You're so emaciated, just skin and bones; it's strange you're still alive. From last night until this morning, you keep saying my illness is heavenly punishment… but why is our happiness a bad thing before God…

Insarov. (As if talking in his sleep) Rench'ich'i. Hasn't Rench'ich'i come?

Elena. No, he hasn't come yet.

Insarov. Ah. Ah.

Elena. (Touches her husband's forehead and sheds tears endlessly. Then, as if unable to bear it, covers her eyes with a handkerchief and slumps over the chair. Her spirit seems subdued (깔안진).) (A long silence follows.)

Insarov. (Hoarse voice (嗄聲)) Elena. Elena. (Calls out, trying repeatedly to sit up. His eyes and mouth open wide, gasping for breath.)

Elena. (Startled) Eh! (Leans in to look at her husband's face.)

Insarov. (Hands frantically (慌忙히) grasp Elena's hand) Elena. Elena, hasn't Rench'ich'i come… But now I… now I…

Elena. Why, what's wrong? What is it?

Insarov. It's all over. It's all just this. I'm dying. I'm dying.

Elena. Why die? Get a hold of yourself…

Insarov. Elena (points weakly with his hand) please open that window over there, maybe the wind blowing from there is from our homeland. Let me feel that wind at least.

Elena. No, you mustn't. (Pulls his hand down) Feeling the wind will harm your body.

Insarov. Then turn my head a little, towards the homeland.

Elena. (Tearfully turns her husband's head slightly towards the window.)

(A short silence...)

Insarov. I can't live anyway. I'm dead now. I know it. Elena. When I die, please bury this body at least in the soil of my homeland. When Rench'ich'i comes, please entrust him with this… Elena, it was indeed a short happiness. (His head slumps; he dies (絶命).)

Elena. (In a fading voice) Why are you like this, why are you like this? Pull yourself together. (Shakes her husband's body) Ahh. Ahh. (Screams in panic (荒急히).)

(At this very moment, the boatman Rench'ich'i enters through the door like an apparition (幻影).)

Elena. Ah, Rench'ich'i?

Rench'ich'i. Madam. (Silently takes her hand. A short silence.)

Elena. Insarov waited for you so much until just now.

Rench'ich'i. (Wordlessly goes to Insarov's side) Amen. (Makes the sign of the cross repeatedly on his chest.) This is a heartbreaking thing, Madam.

Elena. Rench'ich'i, please take me with this corpse (屍體) across the sea to that shore. Yes? You can do that, right?

Rench'ich'i. Well. It's quite difficult, but there's nothing that can't be done by trying. If we have to fight tooth and nail with the police officers… But even if we manage to transport Insarov's body by force and bury him in his homeland, I wonder if I can bring you back here alone. <151>

Elena. It doesn’t matter if my body is not sent back.

Rench'ich'i. Yes… Then what do you intend to do?

Elena. It doesn't matter to me at all. Wherever, however it happens. Whether I go south or north. I don't know either… Anyway, just take me with this corpse. Just take me there.

Rench'ich'i. Understood. Then I will make arrangements (措處) accordingly. I will go out now to prepare the boat and come back. Until then, don't let anyone know. Not even the owner of this hotel that he has passed away. I will be back. (Exits.)

Elena. Please come back. (Watches Rench'ich'i leave and stands there like a fossil (化石). Her eyes are full of tears, and the expression on her face is immobile like a sculpture. A rather long silence.) I was alone after all. (At this moment, the morning sun shines through the high window of the bedroom, brightly illuminating Insarov's dead face. Elena sees his face, clutches the chair, and collapses.) I was alone after all…
(The fierce sound of seagulls' wings flapping. The cry of seabirds.)
(In the distance, the sound of a steamship's emergency whistle.)
Curtain

The play script "The Eve," which has been continuously serialized since the founding of this magazine, has now finally completed its entire run. Among all the magazines published in our Chosŏn, the fact that our "Kaebyŏk" alone has serialized a play is a unique feature among various magazines, and we pride ourselves on it as a point of honor. However, in a place like our Chosŏn, which lacks sophistication (素養) in theater (演劇), the editors also question what kind of interest (趣味) the readers had and how they appreciated (玩賞) this script. If we intend to guide the culture of these 20 million compatriots quickly, we believe it will be difficult without borrowing the power of the art form called theater. Such an explanation, even if this editor doesn't laboriously lay out the argument, will likely be naturally clarified by the passage of time before long, provided our level of knowledge continues to advance like this. Also, when appreciating a script, it is necessary to know that there are scripts containing two types of content. One is a script that evokes the stage (舞臺)—a script read while imagining the stage conditions and actors' lines (科白)—and the other is a script that connects through writing (文章)—a script written solely to be interesting (滋味) to read. Speaking of their value (價値), of course, the script that evokes the stage is the true script. However, for those without knowledge and experience of the stage, a script evoking the stage is not only somewhat uninteresting but also may seem to have gaps in textual connection when read. On the other hand, a script not intended for the stage not only has little value as a script but is also like a novel. That is, it is a novelistic script, not a theatrical script. A truly masterful script has diminished power away from the stage, and the stage has no life (生命) apart from the script. For Chosŏn readers who have yet to see a stage performance, this might sound vague (漠然). For the culture of the 20 million populace, we must strive for the realization (實現) of a stage in our Chosŏn as quickly as possible, even by a day. When will such a time come? Starting next issue, we intend to serialize the tragedy "Hamlet" (하무레트), originally by the world literary giant (文豪) "Shakespeare" (쉑스피아). We sincerely hope (懇切히 바랍니다) you will read it carefully to understand its meaning. <152>

**Summary**

The provided text is the final scene (Act 5, Scene 2) of the play "The Eve" (隔夜) by Hyŏn Ch'ŏl, serialized in the Korean magazine "Kaebyŏk" (開闢) in 1921. The scene depicts the final moments of Insarov, a Bulgarian revolutionary exile, tended by his wife Elena in a desolate hotel room. Despite Elena's care and anxious waiting for the boatman Rench'ich'i to arrange passage back to their homeland, Insarov dies, lamenting their short happiness and unfinished national cause. Rench'ich'i arrives just after his death, agreeing to transport the body home, while Elena resolves to accompany him, uncertain of her own future. The play concludes with Elena's despairing realization of her solitude. An editorial note follows, celebrating the completion of the serialization as a unique contribution to Korean letters, emphasizing the importance of theater for cultural development in colonized Chosŏn, distinguishing between literary and stage-worthy scripts, and announcing the upcoming serialization of Shakespeare's "Hamlet."

From a historical perspective, the text is revealing. The editorial explicitly links theater (演劇) to national cultural upliftment ("2千萬 民衆의 文化"), reflecting a common nationalist aspiration during the colonial period to foster modern cultural forms. The distinction made between purely literary scripts and those intended for the stage highlights the nascent state of modern theater in Korea and the editors' desire to promote 'true' theatrical works. The announcement of translating and serializing "Hamlet" immediately after a Korean play signifies a move towards engaging with Western literary canons, perhaps seen as essential for modernization, while also implicitly acknowledging the difficulties in sustaining original Korean dramatic writing or finding suitable works.

本誌 創刊 以後로 繼續 連載하여 오던 脚本 「隔夜」도 인제야 겨우 全篇을 다 마첫습니다. 우리 朝鮮에서 發行하는 모든 雜誌 中에 惟獨히 우리 「開闢」이 戱曲을 連載하여 온 것은 여러 雜誌 中에서 特色이요. 또한 자랑꺼리로 自信하는 바이올시다. 그러나 우리 朝鮮과 가티 演劇에 素養이 업는 곳에서는 讀者 諸君도 어떠한 趣味를 가지고 어떠케 이 脚本을 玩賞하엿는지는 記者도 疑問이올시다. 萬一 우리가 이 2千萬 同胞에게 速成으로 文化를 指導하랴고 하면 반듯이 演劇이라는 藝術의 힘을 빌지 아니하고는 거북할 줄 압니다. 이러한 說明은 이 記者가 區區히 論緖를 열지 아니하여도 우리의 知識 程度가 이대로 進步만 되면 멀지 아니하여 時日이 自然 辯明할 줄이 듭니다. 그리고 脚本을 感想할 때에 두 가지 內容을 包含한 脚本이 잇는 것은 알아 두는 것이 必要합니다. 한 가지는 舞臺를 聯想하는 脚本...舞臺의 形便과 俳優의 科白을 聯想하고 읽는 脚本...과 또 한 가지는 文章을 聯絡하는 脚本...읽기에만 滋味스럽게 著作한 脚本입니다. 그 價値를 말하면 물론 舞臺를 聯想하는 脚本이 참 脚本입니다. 그러나 舞臺를 聯想하는 脚本은 舞臺의 知識과 舞臺의 經驗이 업는 이는 좀 滋味업슬 뿐만 아니라 딸아서 읽기가 文句上 聯絡이 빈 구석이 잇는 것 갓기도 합니다. 그러치마는 舞臺를 聯想치 아니한 脚本은 脚本的 價値도 적을 뿐만 아니라 小說과 가튼 脚本입니다. 卽 小說的 脚本이지 舞臺的 脚本은 아닙니다. 眞正한 傑作의 脚本은 舞臺를 떠나서 그 힘이 적고 舞臺는 脚本을 떠나서 그 生命이 업습니다. 아즉도 舞臺를 구경하지 못한 朝鮮 讀者는 이 말이 漠然할 줄 압니다. 우리는 2千萬 民衆의 文化를 爲하야 하루라도 急히 우리 朝鮮에서 舞臺가 實現되도록 努力하지 아니하면 아니될 줄 압니다. 언제나 그럴 時節이 올른지요. 次號부터는 世界的 文豪 「쉑스피아」 原作 悲劇 「하무레트」를 連載하랴고 합니다. 아모쪼록 그 뜻을 알도록 잘 읽어 주시기를 懇切히 바랍니다.＜152＞
