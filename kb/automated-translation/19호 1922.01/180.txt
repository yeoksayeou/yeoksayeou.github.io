Around the World, Over Mountains and Across Waters
						Bachelor of Letters No Chŏngil
It has already been half a year since Mr. No, having studied in America, trained in England, toured various nations like France, Italy, and Switzerland, and made a pilgrimage to the Holy Land of Jerusalem, returned to his home country. Now, amid many emotions and reflections, he takes up his pen to vividly recount the events, sights, sounds, and thoughts of his 10-year journey through winds and rains, mountains and waters. Especially when describing his life at Oxford, his observations of post-war Europe, and his pilgrimage to the Holy Land, he pours his unique, overflowing passion, evoking *particular brilliance*. Readers, following along, will reach those scenes and find themselves deeply moved and their hearts inexplicably broadened. (By a Reporter)

Section 2: The Dawn of Approaching Spring
Under the blue sky and white sun, this body, caught in a gentle breeze, stood tall on the path of universal evolution. Did I know or not know the true meaning of individuality, that I must carve out my own destiny as a single being? Looking around myself—before, behind, left, and right—whether considering the grace of convention or the virtue of tradition, I was not a child of fortune possessing even one of these. I was a child of strange fate who had to stand independently, walk alone, like my own shadow.

By saying that I possessed none of the grace of convention or virtue of tradition, <120> if you criticize it as an exaggeration, then lend your ear with sympathy and listen to my words! I am a rustic from the Northwest. Having finished reading the Four Books and about to enter the Three Classics, my head spun from wearing the Confucian scholar's hat required by the *chaejang* (齋長) or *chang'ŭi* (掌議) of the village school, and I was sickened by the *nŭngch'ambong* (陵參奉) or *ŭmyangnip* (陰陽笠) ceremonial hats. Spending my days idly, flowing into complacency, living drunk and dying in dreams in a world of flowers and willows, I considered myself a prodigy in the singing houses, but in the public eye of society, I was seen as a dissolute person causing harm and faced rejection.

A person without learning is a priceless treasure of no value. When religious propaganda and secret societies were all astir, seemingly imbued with heroic spirit and hastening a change in the situation, after running east and west for over a year, like the morning sun of approaching spring, a new prospect opened in my heart. I realized that regarding my own destiny, the evolution through effort was a better law than revolution. Changing my path, altering my attitude towards affairs, I became absorbed in finding the way of struggle, the opportunity for effort. I resolved not to be a poison to communal life in society and to perfectly develop my individuality. I decided to disregard everything else. "Honorable patriot," "Virtuous teacher"—I disregarded them all. I only earnestly sought the chance to strive for the development of individuality.

On days when the weather was clear and bright, a thousand thoughts and myriad moods would rise and fall in my chest, making it impossible to sit properly in my gloomy room, so I wandered by the wharf and seashore. As if drunk, as if sober, I gazed at the shadows of ships departing for various lands, the smoke spewing from the steamships. When I contemplated the vast ocean, the white sails gradually falling below the horizon, the direction of the fading smoke, myriad thoughts like cloud peaks drove my whole being to drift to the ends of the world. Alas—at that time, what must my eyes of envy have truly been like?
Climbing Wŏnsan (圓山) at midnight, facing Pibaltto (飛鉢島) in broad daylight, I looked up to the sky <121> and cried out aloud. "Give me the chance to struggle! If not, give me death!"

As the power of thought increased, vague fantasies gradually faded away, and as practical problems pressed closer, the yearning for studying abroad grew stronger. When the darkness of the spring embankment called forth the cuckoo heralding spring's approach, and the golden oriole among the slender willows flew in with its call to friends, was it Heaven's help? Or human effort? The first act of the stage of struggle was about to open.

Chapter 2: The First Act on the Stage of Struggle

Section 1: Three Eventful Years in Edo
The adventurous journey of studying abroad, crossing mountains and seas, required considerable preparation involving truly a thousand hardships and efforts, but a deeper pain lay on the eve of leaving the country. Taking leave of my parents under their roof for what felt like the last time in this transient world, parting from my wife and children when the path back, paired with the spring sun, seemed uncertain—the thoughts of loving parents and missing my wife are the common feelings of a son, just as the adventure of studying abroad seems the prerogative of a man. The desires of youth, the burning feelings of a son, emotions of joy and sorrow were felt thousandfold, tenfold thousandfold, and I wept. Were the thousand, ten thousand lines of sorrowful tears transformed into the fervent passion for success, like a forest burning on high peaks and deep valleys? Amidst clouds and fog, when a white dragon from the East Sea and a black dragon from the South Sea flew in to contest black and white, a fierce wind and driving rain swept in, the sky-rending sound of thunder seemed to roar at the foolish folk of the old village, the heaven-rolling wind and rain seemed to sweep away the accumulated evil customs of generations. Alas, tragic—is this the fate of human life? As I lamented thus (Eight lines omitted here - original text)

Setting out to study in Edo (江戶), first crossing the blue waves of the Hyŏn Strait (玄海), the generous form of Chŏryŏngdo (絶影島) appeared faintly. A few hours later, the scenery of my home country sank far below the horizon. In that moment, floating on the sea, looking around me, all was vast emptiness; not a single thing shielded me, only golden cloud peaks and sparkling silver waves.
Suddenly, I was thrown into the chaos and novel environment of a different world on the opposite shore (Moji - 門司).
The surrounding scenery of green mountains and clear waters added new color to the overland journey, accompanied by a sense of curious amusement. The succession of landscapes, the connection of things and people, did not let me pass a moment idly, perhaps somewhat lessening the sorrow of parting and the thoughts of home that crowded my heart? However, longing for home and thinking of parents are the constant feelings of a traveler; the chain of affection follows everywhere. The interconnected chain seemed like an electric wire of emotion, the bright full moon in mid-sky like a large mirror seen together! Alas! It is a fact. A fact of the measured world. Between me, the traveler, and the beloved hometown, oceans lay across, and islands were scattered. <122> Looking back, one chapter of world history seemed to be closing, and another seemed about to unfold. In that moment, I unconsciously sank deep into the vortex of contemplation. Adding thought upon thought about my own self, Ch'ŏnggu (靑邱 - Korea), which had vanished from my field of vision! Is it not the place of activity for those who strive to do their best for us? Is it not the soil of the hometown where the elders (僉彦) await my return in glory, with closed eyes? Before I return, will there be no changes? And in my own self, will there be any changes? Or none? If changes occur, into what? If transitions happen, to where? Ah! Ah! Who knows! Swept by a gloomy wind and miserable rain, a feeling of fear arose. And thinking of the studies I aimed for, I also felt the remoteness of the path. As feelings of anticipation and fear simultaneously came and went in my heart, I promptly inscribed a kind of vow in the depths of my sincere heart: "I will accept without reservation the opportunity for struggle given by God and strive."
Stepping onto the platform at Shinbashi (新橋) Station and looking around for direction, a peculiar middle-aged man wearing an Inverness coat and wooden clogs appeared and requested to greet me. "Are you not Mr. So-and-so?" I did not hesitate. Following his question, I stated my intention to go to Aoyama (靑山) OO! He humbly said, "Is this your first time? I will take the lead and guide you."
The first night spent in Edo was truly unforgettable! The wedding night in the East (東方花燭夜) was an old event from about 11 years ago whose meaning I didn't grasp. It didn't even enter the world of imagination; the solitary lamp by the traveler's lodging was my sole companion. When the bright moon rose in the eastern sky, streamed through the glass window, and came to rest on my cold pillow, the feeling of an actor standing behind the curtain about to open, the protagonist on the stage of struggle, pressed upon me. The anguish of homesickness and longing for parents was somehow buried in the past, and I became a guest in another world, amidst the light of knowledge and the grace of culture. Ah! Ah! A single boat leaf on the floating world! No. I was a single boat leaf on the sea of learning (學海)!
Comparing the path of organized education to my own background, the discouraging aspects were not just one or two. It was impossible to pursue specialized knowledge for qualification without completing the curriculum established for secondary education, so there was first a need to graduate from middle school. Thinking about studying for 5 years in middle school and another 5 years in a specialized school, the path forward seemed茫然 (uncertain), and feelings of melancholy increased. Rather than worrying that the path was uncertain, it seemed better to prioritize what needed to be decided first regarding studies and postpone what could be decided later. So, I decided first to graduate from middle school. My plan was to strive as much as possible to complete the 5-year middle school course in 3 years, meaning I could achieve my coming-of-age and return home sooner. I adopted a one-year accelerated plan to study the subjects equivalent to the 3rd year of middle school. <123>
At the famous yet unreliable OO School, I attended three sessions: morning, afternoon, and night. For physical health, I had no time to think about special rest or exercise, but purely for economic reasons and the need for exercise, I undertook the daily commute from the Aoyama (靑山) OO dormitory, through the Aoyama Parade Ground, to the OO School in the Kanda district (神田區).
Alas. Though I had my intended purpose and commuted the long distance cheerfully, without feeling it was far, out of interest and high spirits, what crime had the pitiful Tailing Man (尾行氏) committed? For the first few weeks, I regarded his hurried shuffling in high wooden clogs not as suffering but as a kind of curiosity! Sometimes, even when it rained buckets, his hovering at the gate holding an umbrella seemed truly pitiful. Thinking only of myself, I said, "Look, because it's raining, I won't be going out anywhere today, so don't worry and go back. How can you stand there getting soaked?" Though I spoke sincerely for the Tailing Man's sake, whether he felt his duty was heavy or didn't believe my words, he just said thank you, "It doesn't matter. Thank you for your concern," and remained standing, getting wet. Ah! Ah! Is it for the country? Or for rice cakes (떡 - i.e., livelihood)? Whether the former or the latter, he is a person who takes his duty seriously, however small. When my thoughts reached this point, I received a great lesson. I could understand the principle by which a nation prospers, precisely because its citizens each respect their duties like him and adhere to them without the slightest sign of discontent. From then on, that Tailing Man no longer seemed a strange object but a gentleman. Ah! Ha! There is a good plan! Let's invite him inside my room to escape that harsh rain. At the same time, I'll practice the language I'm studying. With this intention, I invited the Tailing Man, "Hey, it's raining, please come into my room." "No, sir. How could I do that? It doesn't matter. Please don't worry." He naturally refused. "Ah! I want to practice speaking, so please come in for a bit." Only when I said this did he show a willingness to enter. From that time, I gained a good friend for language practice. Whether I did well or poorly during my three-plus years studying in Edo, my proficiency in the language was entirely thanks to the Tailing Man. OO (likely referring to a government agency) was paying several tens of yen each month, effectively hiring a language teacher for me. Isn't turning misfortune into good fortune the rule for conducting affairs? Truly, it is the rule for managing the world.

Continuing the struggle in this way for exactly one year, when spring came, I put on the regular uniform of the middle school, and the feeling of a child thrust into a new environment arose. From then on, I had to handle everything in daily life with the attitude of a child. Playing soldier on the sports field, playing the child in the classroom—I continued this for two years. I may not have tasted any particularly refreshing flavor, but I seem to have fully <124> experienced what middle school student life is like. To recount one by one the bitter experiences of dormitory life would become a grand tale of a thousand chapters and myriad pages, but out of necessity in my thoughts, I leave it as homework.
Wearing the round school cap (環形帽) signified I was a middle school student, but my age and feelings were quite different, like comparing clouds and mud. Consequently, psychologically, dissatisfaction with the situation was natural, and sometimes tragedy was piled upon calamity. The poignant experiences gained from this are things I cannot forget even when my hair turns white. Being inherently rich in emotion, according to the height of my mood, I sometimes felt the darkness of gloomy winds and miserable rain, and other times delighted in a brightness superior even to the innocent and brilliant singing, dancing, laughing, and chatting. At such times, the chugging sound of the Shibuya train also spurred the traveler's constant feeling—the desire to return home. And the sound of the *samisen* (三絃琴 - Japanese three-stringed lute) and *biwa* (琵琶 - Japanese lute) music, echoing through the sporadically standing green bamboo and old pines, brought tears of homesickness. However, the bell of the Kannon Temple (觀音寺), sounding near though far at times, big sounds and small sounds echoing in the sea of my heart, harmonizing and fusing with my sentiments, like divine inspiration from heaven, often caused me to sit quietly in stillness. Far away, towards the church standing prominently on the small hill of my hometown—the place where I found my path, the place I longed for like Mecca—opening my turbulent heart and silently examining its depths, it felt as though I were conversing with a dear friend, hearing words of advice and consolation. As I felt passion welling up in my chest to strive and advance bravely, the pain of adversity and the sorrow of ill fortune seemed swept away like the morning star before the rising sun in the eastern sky. The prospect blooming in my thoughts added new color to every effort, every action.
From the golden sands and silver waves of Kamakura (鎌倉), from the wondrous lake and magnificent falls of Nikkō (日光), looking back at Edo, the world of dust full of sin and sorrow, I sometimes asked the great questions of life and tried to answer them. Renounce the world or be absorbed by it? Neither renunciation nor absorption. From sorrowful Edo, I praised the noble object of golden sands and silver waves even more deeply. From sinful Tokyo (東京), I passionately loved the sublime, superb view of the wondrous lake and magnificent falls. There, I came to realize that a person possessing a solemn, noble character must surely have a mission and responsibility towards the common people of this dusty world. Alas, Edo was a great metropolis of the East where at least 2.4 million common souls bustled, but when I tried to think of it meaningfully as an environment for cultivation, it turned into a wilderness. The 2-million-plus beings, perhaps living creatures, perhaps mad things, I thought of them like the golden sands of Kamakura, like the autumn leaves of Nikkō. The old and new currents of thought in the great city, I thought of them like the silver waves of Kamakura, like the silver foam of the Kegon Falls (華嚴瀧). Just as I felt such desolation, I also felt joy in my heart. The old proverb that all human affairs are like the old man Sai's horse (塞翁之馬 - meaning fortune is unpredictable) might also speak the truth—it's worth asking. <125>

**Summary**

This travelogue excerpt by No Chŏngil recounts the beginning of his ten-year journey abroad, starting with his decision to leave Korea for modern education. He describes his emotional departure, his arrival in Japan (Edo/Tokyo), and the initial struggles of adapting and pursuing accelerated studies. He reflects on the challenges, including surveillance by Japanese authorities (the "Tailing Man"), the loneliness of student life, and the contrast between his traditional background and the modern world he seeks to enter, while finding moments of inspiration in nature and his determination. From a colonial history perspective, the text offers insight into the motivations of Korean intellectuals seeking modern knowledge abroad during Japanese rule, the hurdles they faced (including surveillance), the blend of personal ambition and implicit national sentiment, and the psychological adjustments required when moving between traditional Korean society and modernizing East Asia/the West. Historians might find the detailed personal account of educational pursuit, cultural encounters, and the lived experience of a Korean student under colonial constraints particularly valuable.

그와 가티 奮鬪를 繼續하기 正히 一年되는 春에 中學校의 正服을 着하니 新環境에 投出된 幼年의 感이 生하엿다. 日常生活의 一切를 自此로 幼年의 態度로 取扱하여야 하게 되엇다. 運動場에서는 兵丁 노릇, 講室에서는 아히노름 하기를 2年 동안이나 繼續하엿다. 別로 시원한 滋味야 보앗스랴마는 中等學生의 生活이 어떤 것인지는 充分히 ＜124＞ 맛본 貌樣이다. 寄宿生活의 辛酸한 經驗을 一一이 말하자면 千篇萬頁의 大物語가 되리라 마는 생각하는 中 必要上 宿題로 둔다.
環形帽를 戴하니 中等學生을 標榜하엿스나 年期와 心情은 頗히 雲泥의 觀이 잇섯다. 딸아서 心理上으로 境遇에 對한 不愉快는 勿論, 엇던 때는 悲劇 우에 다시 慘劇을 加하야 이에서 어든 痛切한 經驗은 白首가 되기까지 忘却不能한 것도 잇섯다. 本是 感情이 富한 自己는 情調의 高를 應하야 隨時로 陰風悽雨의 暗黑도 感하엿스며 天眞이 爛漫한 歌舞談笑보다도 優勝한 光明도 樂하엿다. 그러한 때에 澁谷汽車의 噴汽振聲은 旅客의 常情인 還國心도 促起 하여주며 틈틈이 聳立한 蒼竹과 老彬을 通하야 響來하는 三絃琴의 琵琶曲은 望鄕의 淚를 뿌리게 하엿다. 그러치마는 때로서 멀어도 갓갑게 울려 들니는 觀音寺鐘은 큰 소리 적은 소리, 心海에 反響하야 나의 情思와 調和 融合하야 天來의 神感인 듯 나로 하여금 寂然히 靜座케 한 時도 적지 안핫다. 멀니 故鄕 小丘 우에 우둑 선 敎堂 내가 갈 길을 차즌 곳 멕가와 가티 思慕하는 곳을 向하야 紛紛한 心襟을 披開하고 默默히 胸底를 按察할 때 情友와 相待하야 忠告와 慰勞의 말이 들니는 듯 奮鬪勇進하겟다는 熱情이 가슴에서 湧出함을 覺할새 逆境의 痛과 惡運의 悲는 東天旭日에 曉星과 如히 一掃한 듯이 스러저 가고 念頭에 피어 오는 展望은 一擧一動의 努力에 新色彩를 加하여 주엇다.
鎌倉의 金沙銀波 日光의 奇湖華瀧에서 罪가 深하고 悲哀가 만흔 塵世의 江戶를 回顧할 때 人生의 大問題를 뭇기도 하엿고 解答도 하려 하엿다. 遁世냐 沒世냐? 遁世도 아니다. 沒世도 아니다. 悲哀 만흔 江戶에서 金沙銀波의 高潔한 對象을 一層 深切히 讚美하엿다. 罪가 깁흔 東京에서 奇湖華瀧의 崇高한 絶勝을 열렬히 戀愛하엿다. 거긔에 비롯오 崇嚴高潔한 品性을 가진 人物이면 塵世蒼生에게 向하얀 使命과 責任이 반듯이 잇슬 것이라고 自覺하게 되엇다. 嗚呼 江戶는 적더래도 二白四十萬의 蒼生의 雜踏하는 東洋의 大都會이엇다마는 意味잇게 修養의 環境으로 생각하려 할 떼에 曠野로 밧구웟다. 二白 餘萬의 生物인 듯 狂物인 듯한 蒼生을 鎌倉의 金沙가티 日光의 紅葉가티 大都會의 新舊 思潮를 鎌倉의 銀波가티 華嚴瀧의 銀泡가티 생각하엿다. 그와가티 寂寞을 感한 것 가티 心情의 愉快도 覺하엿다. 人生 萬事가 塞翁의 馬란 古諺이 眞理를 말하엿는지도 亦是 무러 볼 만하다.＜125＞
