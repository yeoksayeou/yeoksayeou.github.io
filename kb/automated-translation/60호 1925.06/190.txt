필자	김동인
기사형태	소설 (Fiction)

Country Mr. Hwang
						Kim Tongin

The X Village (X촌) where Mr. Hwang lived was a small mountain village, 570 *ri* from the nearest city, over 300 *ri* from the railway line, and over 150 *ri* from the National Road (國道). Mr. Hwang, now in his 40s, had only ever seen military police, regular police, and surveyors as 'Western-suited men,' which shows just how remote X Village was. Also, within a radius of about 10 *ri*, everyone was practically related and visited each other's houses at night, indicating how sparsely populated the village was. It was such an isolated place that even if a tiger came down from the mountain and mauled someone, the incident wouldn't even appear in the newspapers. It was a village where people bragged about having seen a 10-won banknote, a place that knew nothing of the 'threat of livelihood.'

In a word, the village was simple, honest, generous, and peaceful—a place where one could say the people lived a life akin to primitive man.

Into this X Village, an unexpected event recently occurred.
A so-called 'city person,' a man in Western clothes, suddenly appeared in X Village. This man took lodging at Mr. Hwang's house.

All the villagers flocked to Mr. Hwang's house. They examined the city person's strange clothes, shoes, and hat (shamelessly), touching them, and chattering away, criticizing him this way and that right next to him, as if assuming he couldn't understand Korean.

Mr. Hwang, looking proud, served the guest (who had happened to turn up at his house) foul-smelling tobacco and freshly steamed <10> corn. He excitedly explained to the gathered villagers the circumstances of the city person's arrival at his house.

Several days passed.
According to what the city person explained to the gathered villagers, he had come this far yearning for the "scent of the soil."
—You all are always breathing in the scent of the soil—that fragrant scent of the soil—which is why you are so robust. Ah, the fragrance of the soil. Look here. Go to the city. Aigoo, the smell of people, the sickening smell of gasoline (깨슬린), the smell of sewers (하수도ㅅ 내음새), and on top of that, automobiles, horse carriages, streetcars (뎐차), rickshaws (일력거), flashing here and there—truly, living in the city makes one long for the scent of the soil. The happiness you enjoy, always breathing in the fragrance of the soil rising vigorously on spring days when the earth stretches and yawns, is truly a happiness unattainable for city dwellers. After yearning for years, I finally couldn't stand it anymore and came here. I ran away from that dirty and troublesome city to come here. From now on, I am also your companion...
Thus spoke the city person.

Mr. Hwang understood three things from the words of this city person (let us call him Mr. Z).
Automobiles and rickshaws. Once, when Mr. Hwang had walked 150 *ri* to the National Road for some business (it was night then), he had seen a monster approaching from the distance with two glaring red eyes, making strange noises. The astute Mr. Hwang had, of course, guessed it was something people rode in. But after returning to X Village, it became rumored as a monster. Word spread of a monster that farted puff-puff (방긔를 폴삭폴삭 뀌며), shook the ground as it ran, and was ridden by rich people.
He saw a rickshaw the next day.
And both things (thinking about them after hearing Mr. Z's words) were surely fearsome objects that threatened human life. <11>
Another thing was that the smell of people was sickening. Indeed, Mr. Hwang knew that even in X Village, when dozens of people gathered for a feast, a sickening, foul smell would fill the room. Therefore, in the city, where presumably hundreds of thousands (though he wasn't exactly sure how many hundreds make a hundred thousand without an abacus) of people swarmed like maggots, there must be a considerable sickening smell.

Beyond that, there wasn't a single word Mr. Hwang didn't understand. Though Mr. Z said he longed for the scent of the soil, the scent of the soil was also quite foul. In spring, the scent of the soil (as it hadn't been long since fertilizing) was even more foul.
Streetcars (뎐차), sewers (하수도), gasoline (깨슬린)—Mr. Hwang couldn't even imagine what these things were.
However, Mr. Hwang believed Mr. Z's words. Since he only knew the countryside, and Mr. Z had seen both the countryside and the city, it was natural that Mr. Z's words were correct. No matter how foul the scent of the soil might be, Mr. Hwang believed it was better than the smell of the city.
—Lying sprawled on the road all day long in the countryside, is there any worry of being hit by a car? Is there any worry of being chased away by a policeman? It's truly free...
That too was true and reasonable. Mr. Hwang considered himself fortunate to have been born in such a countryside.

But three or four months later, Mr. Z, after spewing all sorts of curses about the countryside, went back up to the city. Mr. Z declared that he hadn't known, but the scent of the soil was extremely sickening. He said that in the city, just by clicking an abacus for half a day, he used to earn 5,000 *nyang* (냥) a month, whereas here, he had to sweat profusely and injure his hands working just to barely make 5,000 *nyang* a year, so the countryside was no place for a talented person to live. He said that having to walk everywhere, whether 10 *ri* or 100 *ri*, meant the countryside was only fit for cows and horses. He said there were no *kisaeng* (기생), so gentlemen couldn't live there. He said there were no books to read, so scholars couldn't live there. He said there was no Western food (양료리), so nobles couldn't live there.
Hearing this, Mr. Hwang, for three days after Mr. Z left, stared blankly with hollow eyes and didn't even eat. <12>

Mr. Z's words were also all true. The house of Ch'oe P'unghŏn, which he used to visit like a neighbor's, was indeed far when he thought about it. 15 *ri*! It wasn't excessive for Mr. Z to have shuddered at that.
It was also true, as he had heard in old tales, that there were no such things as *kisaeng*.
The only interesting book within a 30-*ri* radius centered on X Village was a single copy of *Imjillok* (임진록) (and even that lacked the beginning and end).
Moreover, Mr. Hwang, renowned in the vicinity for his skill with the abacus—Mr. Hwang, who according to Mr. Z could earn 5,000 *nyang* a month in the city—here endured countless hardships, his hands calloused, sweating, only to harvest crops worth around 6,000 *nyang* a year. On top of that, eating potatoes... handling manure...

Two months passed.
By then, it had already been a month since Mr. Hwang had sold all his leftover food, his house, and his furniture, and come to the city.
Mr. Hwang scraped together 6,000 *nyang* by selling everything he owned. Out of that, 600 *nyang* went for house rent. Two thousand *nyang* was spent in a month of sightseeing and eating.
The city nights in summer were truly beautiful. The lights, the people, the smells, the buildings, the sounds—everything intoxicated Mr. Hwang. He even bought and ate ice cream for seven and a half *nyang*. Again—the sounds, lights, people, smells—the more he saw, the more the city nights intoxicated him. Ice cream, shaved ice (빙수), display windows (딘렬장), night markets (야시)—ah, how Mr. Hwang must have pitied Ch'oe P'unghŏn and Kim Pyŏljang who couldn't see such things.
He also visited the zoo. He occasionally rode the streetcar. The taste of a drink at a standing bar (선술집) wasn't bad either, and the taste of wheat noodles (밀국수) sold on the street was also unforgettable for Mr. Hwang.
He was a bit disappointed that he couldn't meet Mr. Z, whom he thought he would surely run into once he came to the city, but that didn't inconvenience <13> Mr. Hwang.
Ah, the city, the city! Indeed, the countryside was no place for a human being to live.

Four months had passed since Mr. Hwang came to the city. Now his funds were gone.
"From now on..."
Mr. Hwang straightened his clothes and went out to the main street, seeking out a large store (상뎜). He explained that he was good with the abacus and asked to be hired. But unexpectedly, Mr. Hwang was rejected right away. Mr. Hwang went looking for another shop. But there too, he was rejected.
When he returned home in the evening, Mr. Hwang's face was pale as a corpse.
How could this be? The job he thought would be accepted immediately—after visiting over 30 shops today, he hadn't received even a hint of acceptance anywhere, only chased away like a beggar. What was he to do now?
The next day passed the same way. Three days, four days—Mr. Hwang had not a penny left, yet nowhere appeared willing to hire him, not even for 500 *nyang* a month, let alone the thousand *nyang*.
Starve? Mr. Hwang was now inevitably facing starvation. Starvation, something he had never experienced, nor even heard of, was something Mr. Hwang was about to taste.
But how could a person starve? Mr. Hwang was someone who fully knew the generosity of people. Although he had never done such an embarrassing thing before, Mr. Hwang knew that even if he went to Q Village, 20 *ri* away from X Village, to borrow a *mal* of rice, they would lend it. There couldn't possibly be such a heartless bastard who wouldn't give a *mal* of rice to a starving person.
Mr. Hwang went to the house next door. He explained his situation, saying he lived next door and things were such-and-such, and was about to ask for some help, when the people in that house, perhaps already sensing his intention, <14>
"We're on the verge of starving ourselves,"
they said, and went back to their work.
Mr. Hwang thought that was understandable too. In fact, that household also seemed to be living hand-to-mouth.
Mr. Hwang then went to a large tile-roofed house one house over. As he stepped into the inner gate courtyard, a young man (perhaps the owner) sitting on the main wooden porch (대청) brushing his teeth shouted, "Who goes there?"
"Yes? Uh... well..."
Mr. Hwang just turned and left.
Mr. Hwang finally understood the city. He also understood the psychology of Mr. Z who had fled the city. But, the psychology of Mr. Z returning to the city again? That too, if one chewed over what Mr. Z said when he returned to the city, could be understood. The city belongs to the city people, and the countryside belongs to the country people. Destiny (天分)! Destiny! Destiny! Mr. Hwang, who didn't know his destiny and had invaded another's domain, had failed like this. Mr. Hwang now barely realized his own sphere (령분). And he realized that people should just do their own work.
At dawn the next day, with the rising sun at his back, clutching his hungry stomach, Mr. Hwang trudged along the K National Road (K國道), which passed 150 *ri* outside X Village. <15>

**Summary**

Mr. Hwang, a naive country farmer, becomes enchanted with the idea of the city after hearing embellished accounts from a disillusioned city visitor, Mr. Z. Selling everything, Hwang moves to the city seeking fortune and excitement, initially finding it intoxicating. However, his romantic illusions shatter as he fails to find work despite his abacus skills, runs out of money, faces urban indifference, and confronts starvation. Ultimately, he realizes the city is not for him and returns to his village, understanding the concept of one's predetermined 'place' or destiny (天分/령분). The story starkly contrasts the idealized versus harsh realities of urban life for a rural migrant in early 20th-century Korea. For historians, it vividly portrays the naive aspirations and subsequent disillusionment of rural Koreans drawn to rapidly modernizing cities, the perceived social stratification (city vs. country, skills vs. opportunities), and the potentially fatalistic worldview shaped by limited social mobility during the colonial period.

그러나 서너달 뒤에. 그 Z씨는 시골에 대하여 온갓 욕설을 다하고, 다시, 도회로 올라갓다. Z씨는, 몰랏거니와 흙내음새는, 매우 역하다 하엿다. 도회에서는, 하로ㅅ동안에 한나절식만 수판을 똑딱거리면, 매달 5000량식 드러오던 자긔가, 여긔서는, 땀을 벌벌 흘리며 손을 상하며 일을 하여야 1년에 오천냥 드러오기가 힘드니, 시골이란 재간잇는 사람은 못살 곳이라 하엿다. 10리나 백리라도, 거러서 밧게는 다닐 도리가 업스니, 시골은 소 말이나 살 곳이라 하엿다. 기생도 업스니 점잔은 사람은 못살 곳이라 하엿다. 닑을 책도 업스니 학자는 못살 곳이라 하엿다. 양료리가 업스니 귀인은 못살 곳이라 하엿다.
이 말을 듯고, 황서방은, Z씨가 간 다음 사흘동안을, 눈이 퀭하니, 밥도 안먹고 잇섯다.＜12＞
Z씨의 말은 모도 다 또한 정말이다. 아직것 겻집가치 다니던 최풍헌의 집이, 생각하여 보면 참 멀엇다. 15 里! Z씨가 진저리를 친 것도, 너무 과한 일은 아닐 것이다.
녯말로 드른 바, 기생이라는 것이 업는 것도 또한 사실이엇섯다.
재미잇는 책이라고는 임진록 한 권이(그것도 서두와 꼬리는 업는 것) X촌을 중심으로 삼은 30 里이내의 다만 하나의 책이엇섯다.
더구나 그 근처 일대에, 수판 잘 노키로 일홈난 황서방이, -도회에서는(Z씨의 말에 의지하건대) 매달 오천냥의 수입은 될 황서방이, 손에 구든 살이 백이며 땀을 흘리며, 천신만고하여 일년에 거두는 추수가, 륙쳔량 내외-엇섯다. 게다가, 감자를 먹고... 거름을 쥐믈고...
두달이 지낫다.
그때는, 황서방은 자긔의 먹다 남은 것이며 집이며 세간사리를 모도 파러가지고 도회로 온지 벌서 한달이나 된 때엇섯다.
황서방은, 자긔의 것을 모도 파러서 륙천냥이라는 돈을 긁엇다. 그 가운데서 집세로 륙백냥이 나갓다. 한달 동안 구경하며 먹어가는데 이천냥이 나갓다.
녀름 밤의 도회는 과연 아름다웟다. 불, 사람, 내음새, 집, 소리, 모든 것은, 황서방을 취케 하엿다. 닐곱냥 반을 주고, 아이스크림도 사먹어 보앗다. 또한- 소리, 불, 사람, 내음새, 보면 볼사록, 도회의 밤은, 사람을 취케 하엿다. 아이스크림, 빙수, 딘렬장, 야시 아아, 황서방은 얼마나, 이런 것을 못보는 최풍헌이며 김별장을 가련히 생각하엿스랴.
동물원도 보앗다. 뎐차도 간간 타보앗다. 선술집의 한잔의 맛도, 괜찬은 것이고, 길에서 파는 밀국수의 맛도 또한 황서방에게는 닛지 못할 것이엇섯다.
도회로 오기만 하면, 맛나질 줄 아럿든 Z씨를 못맛난 것을, 좀 섭섭하지만, 그것도 황서방에게는, 불편되는＜13＞ 일은 업섯다.
아아, 도회, 도회, 과연 시골은 사람으로는 못살 곳이엇섯다.
황서방이 도회로 온지 넉달이 되엿다. 인젠 미천도 업서젓다.
「이제부터」
황서방은 의관을 정히 하고 큰거리로 나가서 엇던 큰 상뎜을 차저갓다. 그러고, 자긔는 수판을 잘 노흐는데, 써달라고 부탁을 드렷다. 그러나 의외로 황서방은 첫마듸로 거절당하엿다. 황서방은 다른 집으로 차저갓다. 그러나 거긔서도 또한 거절당햇다.
저녁때, 집에 도라올 때는 황서방의 얼굴은 송장과 가치 퍼러케 되엿다.
이런 일이 어듸잇나. 첫마듸로 승락할 줄 아럿든 일이, 오늘철로 30여 집을 다녀스나 한 곳에도 승락 비슷한 것도 못밧고 거라지나 온 것 가치 쪼껴 나와스니, 인전 엇전단 말인가.
이튼 날의 경과도 역시 가텃다. 사흘, 나흘, 황서방의 미천은 한푼도 업서젓는데 매달 천냥은 커녕, 오백냥으로 고용하려는 데도 나타나지 아넛다.
굶어? 황서방은, 인전 할 수 업시 굶게 되엿다. 아직 당하여 보기는커녕, 말도 못드럿든 「굶는다」는 것을, 황서방은 맛보게 되엿다.
그런덜 사람이 굶기야 하랴. 황서방은, 사람의 후한 인심을 충분히 아는 사람이엇섯다. 아직것 그런 창피스런 일은 하여본 적은 업지만, X촌에서 20 里를 떠러저잇는 Q촌에 쌀 한말 어드러 갈지라도 꾸어주는 것을 황서방은 안다. 사람이 굶는다는데 쌀 한말 안줄, 그런 야속한 환양놈은 업슬 것이엇섯다.
황서방은 겻집에 갓다. 그러고, 자긔는, 이 겻집에 사는 사람인데, 여사여사 하다고 사연을 한 뒤에, 좀 조력을 하여달란 니약이를 이제 끄집어내려는데, 그 집에서는, 벌서 눈치를 채엿는지＜14＞
「우리두 굶을 지경이오」
하고는, 제 일만 보기 시작하엿다.
황서방은, 그것도 그럴 일이라 생각하엿다. 사실, 그 집도 막버리하는 집이엇섯다.
황서방은, 다시 한 집 건너잇는 큰 기와집으로 차저 갓다. 그가, 즁대문 안에 드러설 때에, 대청에 걸처 안저서 양치를 하고잇던 젊은 사람(주인인지)이, 웬 사람이냐고 꽥 소리를 질럿다.
「녜? 저... 뭐... 」
황서방은 다시 나오고 마럿다.
황서방은 마츰내 도회라는 것을 아럿다. 도회에서 다라낫던 Z씨의 심리도 아럿다. 그러나, Z씨가 다시 도회로 도라온 그 심리는? 그것도, Z씨가, 도회로 도라올 때에 한 말을 씹어 보면, 알 것이 엇섯다. 도회는 도회ㅅ 사람의 것이고, 시골은 시골 사람의 것이다. 天分! 天分! 天分을 모르고, 남의 분령에 침입하엿든 황서방은, 이러케 실패하엿다. 황서방은, 인제 겨우 자긔의 령분을 깨다럿다. 그러고, 사람은, 저 할 일만 제가 할 것임을 깨다럿다.
이튼날 새벽, 황서방은 떠오르는 해를 등으로 밧고, 주린 배를 웅켜쥐이고, X촌에서 150 里 밧글 통과하는 K國道를 더벅더벅 거럿다.＜15＞
