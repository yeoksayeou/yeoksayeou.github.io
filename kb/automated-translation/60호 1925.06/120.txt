Two Nights at Ilsin Inn

It was April 10th. Around 3 PM, I got off at Chaeryŏng Station (載寧驛), and a certain boy kindly recommended, "Shall I take you into town by car?" Having a trunk and all, I readily agreed and got into the automobile. And so, I arrived in town. Chaeryŏng Town (載寧邑), which I had briefly seen once four years ago while passing through, had a familiar outline, but everything seemed changed. I knew no one, and naturally, there was no inn I had stayed at before. I hesitated for a moment, wondering what to do. The boy who guided me to the car from the station took his 20 *chŏn* and acted as if he didn't know me, without even a polite farewell. I asked a passing boy, "Hey, could you point me to an inn? A quiet one, please." The boy replied, "An inn?" and, walking past brusquely, said, "Go up that way, and behind the two-story house <87> there's an inn." Very unfriendly. "Wow! The local sentiment here is quite stiff," I thought, picking up my luggage and shuffling along as the boy directed, entering the back alley of the two-story house. Peeking here and there, I finally spotted a sign reading "Ilsin Yŏgwan" (日新旅舘). "Ah well, let's just go anywhere. It's only for a day or two..." I entered the gate briskly, cleared my throat with an "Ahem," and asked, "Hello, is the owner in?" An elderly woman came out and said, "Yes, what is it?" "Do you have a vacant room?" I asked. "Yes, come right this way," she replied, pointing to a room right by the gate. The room was shabby but spacious, and the house, though large, was quite quiet.
What was my first discovery within just ten minutes in the gate room of Ilsin Inn? Don't be surprised. It was the whispering voices of the opposite sex from the next room. "Well! Look at this. Is it fortune or misfortune?" A kind of curiosity arose, along with a kind of suspicion. Listening intently like a detective (偵探), I determined they were female, clearly *kisaeng*. Not just one, but two. It seemed they were just waking up from a nap after whatever they did last night. As the door opened, footsteps sounded. Peeking through a crack, I saw a beauty in plain clothes, around twenty years old.
"I'll investigate the beauty later, let's take care of business first," I thought and immediately went out. Returning around six in the evening, the room was already bustling with men and women mingling, talking back and forth. "Right! These fellows must be the dissolute types (浮浪派) of Chaeryŏng?" I wanted to get a look at their faces, but they wouldn't come out of the room. "Just because they're playing with *kisaeng*, are they all dissolute types? Perhaps I've been in such situations myself," I thought, granting an unasked pardon, and went out again.
I must have returned around eleven at night. It seemed a few more fellows had gathered than before. "Play!" "Why are you like that?" "You wench!" "With me..." and all sorts of strange and nauseating words could be heard. "I need to get some sleep... but how can I sleep with this racket?" I muttered to myself, tossing and turning, just coughing lightly. Even after one o'clock, then two, they didn't leave. Three, four o'clock, they stayed up all night. As one fellow left, another entered; the sound of the gate opening and closing back and forth made sleep impossible beforehand. Extremely irritated, I said this: "Humans are dogs (犬). Men are male dogs, and women are bitches." Truly, I mean, what more is there to it than male dogs drooling behind bitches?
The following night too, I spent the night sleeplessly in exactly the same manner. By the way, these *kisaeng* were supposedly Orhyon (芺蓉) and Myŏngju (明珠) from P'yŏngyang (平壤). Perhaps I heard wrong. <88>

One Afternoon at the Hot Springs

It was Sinch'ŏn Hot Springs (信川溫泉), famous in Hwanghae Province (黃海道). Hearing that it would be a lasting regret if one came to Sinch'ŏn (信川) and didn't see the hot springs, I intentionally went for a look despite being busy. I was luxuriously chauffeured right in by automobile. The Chosŏn Railway Hotel, the Japanese inns, the Koreans' temporary lodgings (假家) - everything seemed quite well-established. There seemed to be more pleasure-seekers than patients. Peeking into this house and that, inside and out, I saw beauties applying makeup, beauties waking up drowsily, beauties sitting pensively as if waiting for someone—at a glance, more than could be counted on one hand.
Paying ten *chŏn* each, I entered a Japanese bathhouse (the guide led us there). Fat guys, skinny guys, old men, young men, lying horizontally and vertically, sprawled out, getting in, getting out—truly equality, freedom, and if you call it natural, it was nature itself. Life in the bath was indeed without discrimination or class. One, two, three, four, ten, a hundred; guys counting numbers, guys singing the sorrowful "Kanda Noja" song—it was quite noisy. This must be the traditional custom of the bathing troops from Sŏgwan (West Korea, likely P'yŏngan Province).
The bath water was carbonic acid quality, so there was no sulfur smell. The water was hot and also abundant. It is said to be especially effective for skin diseases and gastrointestinal disorders. Many people drink the water too. However, being my first time, I took one sip and spat it right back out. Having no leisure time and no friends with me, what else could I do after bathing? I turned back rather uninterestingly.

An Hour Inside the Police Station

Those who don't know might think I was detained or something. But it wasn't detention. Shall we call it the conduct of a so-called reporter?
Claiming to gather news directly from the police affairs section, liberally wasting my name cards, I intentionally went to visit, but the Station Chief was busy, and I ended up waiting for a whole hour. However, the miscellaneous things I saw and heard in this one hour alone were enough to make my pockets burst, so I couldn't even take them all out, so to speak.
A police station is, for some reason, a terrifying place even for the innocent. No matter how clever one may be, upon encountering a police station, one would hesitate. Indeed, it's a dreadful place. Truly, seeing those swords, those binding ropes, those holding cells, those caps, those uniforms, one's heart would feel heavy.
This day was market day in Sinch'ŏn, and it was noon, the busiest time inside the station. Even a visitor from a thousand *ri* away, let alone waiting an hour, wouldn't they wait a whole day? Moreover, would those gentlemen consider a mere Korean reporter that important? Anyway, just gathering miscellaneous bits <89> was a profit.
I was a country bumpkin, but everyone there seemed to be a country bumpkin. One bumpkin in Western clothes was bowing before the station chief for a doctor's license application. A village woman was begging tearfully for news of her husband, arrested for setting up a stall improperly in the marketplace. A man with a topknot, on an errand for some township head (面長), presented a pair of live pheasants to the Station Chief. A Japanese man and woman were bowing deeply, applying for an inn license. A young policeman stood crestfallen before the police inspector (警部) for a translation error. A certain criminal, whether going to Haeju (海州) or P'yŏngyang (平壤), left sighing so loudly the police station seemed about to burst, bound with ropes, while a Russian White Army straggler with pale eyes and a prominent nose bowed, trying to sell woolen fabric.
Wow! This was the world. The world where people live. An hour inside the police station? It's tremendous!
By the way, they say there are many intellectual crimes like theft and fraud here.

The Wealthy Families of Anak

In Anak (安岳), there's nothing much to talk about besides the stories of the rich families. Like saying there's soy sauce in a dugout hut, they may look shabby on the outside, but inside, they are substantial. Their outward appearance (꼬락산이) might suggest even a beggar starved for three days wouldn't enter, but if you actually go in, you can see the rich master sitting reflectively on a matted seat behind a folding screen. They seem to have a specialty: they first assess your appearance and demeanor, then check their gold chests again.
Unless you address them with extremely polite words like "Your Excellency" (영감) or "Master" (주인님) and show utmost sincerity, they won't even glance your way. And unless you rather assertively state your title, like "I am a reporter" or "I am a detective," they'll think, "What's all this?" and act utterly arrogant. This isn't unique to the rich of Anak, or rather, the moneylenders of Anak. It's a universal truism that coolness and arrogance are weapons of the exploiter. Since some friends might get angry, let's drop the talk of the rich!

What Was Gained Atop Wŏlp'a Pavilion

Wŏlp'a Pavilion (月波樓), the famous site of Hwangju (黃州)! I had longed to see it for a long time. Every time the Kyŏngŭi Line train passed Hwangju, I would look far out the window, shifting restlessly in my seat. However, I never had the chance to ascend it. Then, the spring of the Eulchuk year (乙丑 春 - 1925) showed me special favor, allowing me to climb Wŏlp'a Pavilion on a morning when flowers bloomed and butterflies flew. It felt not like "Heaven and Earth intentionally created man," but rather "Man intentionally roams Heaven and Earth."
Wŏlp'a Pavilion, longed for over and over! Now facing it, what <90> was gained? The flowing waters of the Chŏkpyŏk River (赤壁江) are the same in ancient and modern times; the sun rising over Tŏgwŏl Mountain (德月山) still shines with the same light. I ask, have human affairs followed suit?
Mulberry fields turn into blue seas, and blue seas into mulberry fields; how can one speak of human affairs? But being a person with worldly feelings, a sorrowful sentiment goes out to the people of old and new.
"Kites fly, fish leap" describes the scenery of Tŏgwŏl Mountain and Chŏkpyŏk River. But I am not in a state to welcome it. The fine smoke over Yongjup'yŏng (龍宙坪) and the returning clouds of Chŏngbang Mountain (正方山) tease the spring visitor. But I lack the leisure to appreciate them. Why has Hwangju town become so dilapidated? Whose houses are those large ones? Whose orchard is that? Whose train is that? When asking these questions, Wŏlp'a Pavilion seems pitiful anew. I seem even more pitiful. All my companions seem that way too.
Ah—what was gained at Wŏlp'a Pavilion? Only heartache. Let it be. Let's not pretend to be a patriot (志士然).

Pongsan's Hundred-Ri Torchlight

It was the afternoon of April 20th. As the train bound for Sinmak passed Simch'on (沈村), it was around 8 o'clock. A young man on the train suddenly caused a commotion, saying, "Ah- look at that! It's quite spectacular!" "What is it?" The passengers all raised their eyes at once and looked out the west-side window. Wow—truly—it was spectacular indeed. Was it a ten-*ri* torchlight? A hundred-*ri* torchlight? Like the night view of Seoul's Namdaemun Street extended several tens of times, a growing line of torchlight stretched for tens of *ri*. All the passengers crowded to the right side, nearly tipping the train over. Passing Kyedong until reaching Sariwŏn (沙里院), the torchlight remained visible. Whether it was goblin mischief or a general mobilization of the fire god, it was truly a dreamlike reality. When I went out into Sariwŏn city and looked, it was still the same. It was a great spectacle I saw for the first time.
But what could this torchlight be? It's the annual crab catching on the Chaeryŏng River (載寧江), which local people view as commonplace. They say that if you go out to the riverbank at night with torches, all the crabs swarm out in droves. Then, you just scoop them up into baskets or sacks. Every year around this time, men and women, young and old, from the vicinity reportedly mobilize en masse to catch crabs.

Night Journey to Chŏngbang Mountain Fortress

It has been over 10 days since I came to Hwanghae Province, and I have already visited five counties, but I haven't seen any famous mountains or great rivers yet. Trying to tour 11 counties in a planned 20 days requires more than just a deer's pace; it's nowhere near enough. So, when I was in Chaeryŏng, though I keenly thought of it, I couldn't visit Changsu Mountain (長壽山), the Kŭmgang of Hwanghae, <91> and when I was in Sinch'ŏn, I just passed by Kuwŏl Mountain (九月山), a famous mountain of the Western Provinces (西道). Could this even be called an exploration of Hwanghae Province? I felt deeply ashamed. No matter how busy, having come to Pongsan (鳳山), I resolved to visit at least Chŏngbang Mountain Fortress (正方山城); this was both a personal desire and prompted by others. Since only nighttime was available, the motion proposed among several friends was to go at night and return in the morning.
Fortunately, the proposal passed without incident and was put into action immediately. Through the introduction of Mr. Kim Mun-hwan (金文煥) and the kindness of the Haesŏ Automobile Division (海西自働車部), a car materialized. Whether by chance or like-minded spirits converging, when I looked in the car, there were Kim Mun-hwan, Kim Chin-sŏn (金振璿), Kwak Pyŏng-gyu (郭柄奎), Kim Pong-sŏ (金鳳瑞), Ch'oe Yong-hwan (崔龍煥), and Sil Pok-sun (失福順), totaling 7 people. Issuing an emergency order in the middle of the night to assault a mountain fortress by car felt like there was some band of thieves in the mountains. Passing through the streets felt somewhat suspicious. Moreover, having a member of the opposite sex in the party seemed somewhat inappropriate. However, we consoled ourselves that this trip was also a responsible undertaking.
The car reached the outside of the fortress gate just as the lights of the mountain homes were flickering, about to go out. The innocent village elder does not discriminate between day and night guests.
Getting out of the car, what's this? We forgot something. When leaving, we had firmly planned, "It's a mountain trip, and at night too, shouldn't we have a bottle of wine?" but completely forgot. What could be done? Now we truly had to sing, "Excuse me, where is the tavern?" (借問酒家何處在). Since there's an Apricot Blossom Village (杏花村), wouldn't there be a tavern? So we really sang out, "Excuse me, where is the tavern?" But the mountain elder shook his head, saying there was no tavern (撓頭無酒家), what could we do? Smacking our lips in disappointment, we crossed the stone bridge, passed through the South Gate street, and sought out an inn (客主집). As is the nature of the business, the owner woke rubbing his eyes from a sweet dream to greet us.
The mountain stream was deep in the night, and the traveler's sorrow was equally deep. With no moon and no wine, never mind the opposite sex, what were the thoughts of the six stalwarts? Utterly without recourse, we could only rely on the owner's goodwill.
The owner, experienced in dealing with guests, offered even before being asked again, to go fetch wine from a tavern 10 *ri* away. From then on, we only sang, "Why is the wine taking so long to arrive?" (沽酒來何遲). Sŏngbul Temple (成佛寺) was only a few *chŏngbo* (町步 - unit of area) away, but the night was deep and moonless, so we couldn't go. Although the fragrance of flowers seeped through the door cracks, the bees and butterflies were asleep, so what could be done?
Around two o'clock, the wine cups were finally poured, and now it was truly a case of "not knowing the east had already whitened" (不知東方之旣白).
In the early morning, after seeing Sŏngbul Temple (one of the 30 head temples) and Chŏngbang Mineral Spring (正方藥水) (famous but unimpressive), I casually drank two bowls of mountain vegetable soup, strolled slowly through Apricot Blossom Village, and set off on the return path.

The Youth Corps by Sŏhŭng Stream <92>

"Having no friends and no place to visit, let's take a walk by Sŏhŭng Stream (瑞興川邊)," I thought and set out. The sound of washerwomen's (漂母) paddles and children's flutes sounded beautifully and affectionately. Feeling a sense of loneliness on one hand, yet sensing peace on the other, just then, through the weeping willows, pairs of young soldiers (少年軍) appeared in smart attire—ah, how delightful! The Youth Corps of Sŏhŭng (瑞興)! It felt like meeting a younger brother or an old friend; I was overjoyed to the point of suppression. Grabbing their wrists, I exchanged warm greetings and asked about their situation. They said they were organized last year and had 80 members. They mentioned they couldn't stay long as they were on their way to field practice (方場演習). "Ah, grow well!" was all I could say as a blessing.

Bitter Feelings When the Welcome Rain Comes

It's the planting season, so one drop is worth a thousand gold pieces. But why did the welcome rain (甘雨) in Suan town feel so heart-wrenching? Anyway, if I say "Bitter feelings when the welcome rain comes" (甘雨來時心情苦), it should serve as material about how Suan was for a visitor to Suan.

The Great Ignorance of the Suan Policeman

Not long after reporting my arrival as a guest, the inn servant came and said, "Sir guest, the policeman wants you to come because he asks what a reporter (記者) does." Finding it absurd, I chuckled. "Ah well, he's a Suan policeman, perhaps it's not surprising," I thought, and told the servant, "Tell him a reporter is a reporter," and waited for the next development. A little later, he came again and said, "He wants you to explain again in detail. He says he doesn't understand." "Hey boy, did the policeman come to your place? If he did, send him to me. I'll explain directly. How would you understand about a reporter, which even the policeman doesn't know? Even if you understood, you might forget..." After sending him off, soon the policeman really came. As he peeked into the room, an advertisement flyer for Kaebyŏk Publishing Company (開闢社) lying there caught his eye. "Ah—you're from Kaebyŏk Publishing Company. I see—" he said, and ran off without even coming in when invited. It was appreciated that he pretended to know Kaebyŏk-sa even if he didn't know what a reporter was. This much gives a good idea of the policemen in the countryside, and consequently, the people there seem pitiful.

Yi Wan-yong's Land Here Too

On the road from Suan to Koksan (谷山), I ordered lunch at a tavern (酒幕) in Yŏun-ni village (如雲理), Ch'ŏngok-myŏn township (川谷面), and asked a village elder about local customs. At the end of our conversation, I happened to hear him say, "There's a lot of Yi Wan-yong's (李完用) land here too." Asking for details, I learned that about 10 years ago, together with Chŏng Kwanch'o (鄭觀朝) of P'yŏngyang (平壤), they bought land paying 3 or 4 *wŏn* per *ilgyŏng* (一日耕 - area plowable in one day) and leased it out as tenant farms for a rent (租) of 6 or 7 *tu* (斗 - unit of volume) per *ilgyŏng*. The current price is about 20 *wŏn* per <93> *ilgyŏng*. "Isn't that a huge profit?" he said. The 84-year-old elder, Mr. Han In-sŏng (韓仁聖), lamented the state of the world, and the mourner, Mr. Yi Yong-jin (李溶鎭), cursed Yi Wan-yong.

Unceasing Tears in the Automobile

As the automobile passed the Miru field (野) of Koksan, a beauty in plain mourning clothes came out from a thatched hut and stopped the car. "Eek! Sharing the car with a beauty!" I thought, and indeed felt pleased. But what's this? As soon as the car started, the beauty cried out loudly, "Oh Mother! When will I see you again?" and wept profusely. Crossing mountains and rivers, still tears. These must be the tears of a new bride returning from a visit to her parents' home. After traveling some distance, a woman by the roadside raised her hand and stopped the car. She boarded, holding a 7-year-old girl and carrying a 3-year-old child on her back. The women exchanged pleasantries. "These children have no father," she said, also in tears. After traveling a bit further, a man holding a crying child got on. The child kept crying, and the man just sighed. "Why is that child crying so much?" I asked. The man, also choked up, replied, "Because he's hungry." "Does he have no mother?" I asked. "She came to her parents' home a month ago and died," he said, tears welling up. All pitiful lives. Was it the fate of the day, or how could it be that only widows, widowers, and orphans gathered like this? Misery loves company, so they comforted each other well, but will Korea's situation lead them to happiness? My eyes, too, welled up with tears.

A Bundle of Peculiar Customs Near Hanp'o

Near Hanp'o (汗浦), that is, in the P'yŏngsan (平山) area, besides the usual annual events like Tano and Ch'usŏk, the mountain ritual (山祭) on September 9th is also prevalent, incurring great expense (空費) for each village. The ancestral rites (時祭) in early October are also widespread; the trade of *suŏ* fish (秀魚 - Odontobutis obscura) alone at Hanp'o market reportedly amounts to over 10,000 *wŏn*. Perhaps the ancestors' bones and the mountain spirit's mouths get fatter, but isn't it a sin against hungry parents and scantily clad descendants? Is this peculiar custom (怪風) only in the P'yŏngsan area? Aren't there similar strange customs everywhere in Korea?

My Regret Before Kŭmch'ŏn Station

This is the moment when, even if only in name, the journey across Hwanghae Province ends. Just buy the ticket and sit on the train, and it will take me all the way to Seoul. As I went to buy the ticket, a crowd of students gathered around the ticket window (出札口), making it difficult to buy one easily. Looking closely, they were village students who had come to town for sightseeing and were now watching even the ticket selling at the nearby station. Time was short, and they wouldn't move aside. Without thinking, the words slipped out, "Move aside! These village students, honestly!" I snapped. The students, as if greatly <94> embarrassed, looked closely at my face and slowly moved aside. As these village students stared intently at my face while moving away, this thought suddenly occurred to me:
"Oops! A mistake. What kind of harsh words were those to our innocent village children! Oops, my bad," I thought. As I regretted this, it felt as if their mouths were retorting, "How much have you shed your country bumpkin ways?" Thoughts of my younger siblings back in the countryside came flooding back. I thought of all the children of Korea. I could picture them standing blankly, staring when a train, an automobile, or even a Westerner or Japanese person passed by. "How much must they want to see and learn, to act like that?" I felt, and my regret became immense.

Five Markets and Six Stations

I saw five markets: Sinch'ŏn, Anak, Hwangju, Pongsan, and Sŏhŭng. Yellow millet market, northern rice market—that's all there was. Should I call it a taffy (엿) market? Or a rubber shoe market? Rubber shoes and taffy were quite common, but it was mainly a yellow millet (黃米) market. They say Hwanghae Province is a grain region, but where has it all been taken and offered up? This is the current state (現像) of Korea. To understand Korea, one must simply tour the markets in various places. What was piled up at the six stations I saw? Again, yellow millet was common, followed by charcoal (木炭). That's what I saw. You probably know that yellow millet is Manchurian millet (滿洲粟).

Three Great Boasts and Two Great Shames of Hwanghae Province

The boasts are: Transportation, Rice Grain, Hot Springs. The shames are: the large amount of land owned by outsiders (外人) and the lack of suitable private educational institutions (民間敎育機關) (middle school level and above). <95>

**Summary**

This 1925 travelogue by Pak Tal-sŏng recounts his journey through Hwanghae Province, detailing observations from various towns and locations like Chaeryŏng, Sinch'ŏn Hot Springs, Anak, Hwangju's Wŏlp'a Pavilion, Pongsan, Sŏhŭng, Suan, Koksan, Hanp'o, and Kŭmch'ŏn. He describes his experiences in inns, bathhouses, and a police station, encounters with locals (including *kisaeng*, officials, peasants, students, and fellow travelers), reflects on the changing landscape, local customs (like crab fishing and rituals), and economic conditions (markets dominated by millet, land ownership issues involving Yi Wan-yong). A sense of melancholy and critique pervades the narrative, touching upon social interactions, colonial realities, poverty, and the author's personal reflections and regrets. For historians, the article offers valuable glimpses into colonial Korean society: the palpable Japanese presence and administration (police interactions, Japanese businesses), socio-economic stratification (wealthy landowners vs. struggling peasants), the impact of figures like Yi Wan-yong on land distribution, the state of infrastructure (trains, automobiles), local customs under pressure or scrutiny, the emergence of new religious movements like Poch'ŏn'gyo, and the underlying nationalist sentiment expressed through the author's poignant observations and critiques of Korea's situation.

日新舘의 二夜間

4월 10일이엿다. 오후 3시쯤하야 載寧驛에 내리니엇던 소년이 「자동차로 읍내에 드러가시지요」하고 친절히 勸한다. 도랑크도 잇고 하야 단바람 그러라고 승낙을 하고 자동차에 올나안졋다. 及 기타 읍내에 왓것다. 4년 전 일시 지내든 길에 잠간 본 載寧邑은 윤곽은 어럼픗하나 모든 것이 변한 듯십다. 아는 사람도 업고 단녀본 여관도 毋論 업섯다. 엇더케 해야 조흘지 한참 머믓거렷다.  정거장에서 자동차 안내하든 소년은 돈 20 錢 따먹고는 본체만체 잘가라는 인사도 업다. 지내가는 엇던 소년에게 「여보, 여관 한 곳 지시해주구려. 좀 조용한 집으로요」 하고 請하닛가 소년은 「여관요」 하고는 쓱 지내가면서 「저 위로 올나가다가 二층집 뒷골목에＜87＞ 여관이 잇슴닌다」 한다. 아조 不親切하다. 「야! 이곳 인심이 꽤 뻣뻣하구나-」하면서 行具를 들고 짓축짓축 그 소년의 말대로 이층집 뒷골목으로 드러가 섯다. 이리기웃 저리기웃 하노라니 맛츰 日新旅舘이란 門牌가 보인다. 「에라 아무데나 들자. 하로안이면 잇틀인데...」하고 대문간에 쓱 드러서 기침을 애햄하고 「여봅시요 주인 게심닛가」하닛가 엇던 老婆한 분이 나오면서 「왜 그러시우」한다. 「븬방이 잇슴닛가」하닛가 「네 이리 드러오서요」하며 바로 문깐방을 지시한다. 방은 醜하나 널기는 하고 집은 큼줏하나 퍽 조용하다.
日新舘문깐방의 불과 십분에 첫 발견이 무엇이엿슬가? 놀내지마라. 이웃방에서 속살거리는 이성 목소리엿다. 「야! 이것봐라 행이냐 불행이냐?」 일종 호기심도 나고 일종 혐의증도 생긴다. 偵探모양으로 귀를 기우려  가허 여성임을 알고보니 그는 분명한 기생이엿다. 하나도 안이요 둘이엿다. 밤에는 무엇을 하엿는지 엿태것 낫잠을 자고 이러나는 모양이엿다. 문여는 소리나자, 발자국소리 난다. 문틈으로 내다보니 이십 내외의 素服美人이다.
「美人 探偵은 장차하고 볼일부터 보자」하고 곳 외출이 되얏다. 저녁 여섯시쯤해서 도라와보니 그방은 발서 남성 여성이 어우러저서 주거니밧거니 야단법석이다 「올타! 요놈들은 載寧의 浮浪派?」하고 그놈들의 觀相을 보려닛가 방속에서 나와야 말이지. 「기생하고 논다고 다-浮浪派랴 혹! 나도 그런 경우가 잇지」하고 청하지 안는 容恕를 주고 또 나와 버렷다.
밤 열한시쯤해서 드러갓겟다. 악가보다 멧놈 더 무러드린 모양이다. 「노아요」 「왜 그리서요」 「요년아」 「나하고...」등 別別 駭怪한 말이 구역이 나도록 들닌다. 「좀...자야하겟는데 이놈의 성화에 잠을 잘수잇나-」하고 혼자 불둑거리며 누엇다 이러낫다 잔 기침만 하것다. 새로 한시가 지나 두시가 되야도 안이 간다. 세시 네시 밤을 그대로 뱔킨다. 한놈이 나가면 한놈은 드러오고 들낙날낙 대문여는 소리에 爲先잠을 일을 수 업섯다. 하도 골이 나서 이런말을 햇다. 「사람놈들은 개(犬)야 산애놈들은 숫캐고 게집년들은 암캐다」라고. 실로 말이지 암캐뒤에서 침흘니는 숫캐에서 더 지냄이 무엇이냐 말이다.
그 잇튼날밤도 꼭 고 모양으로 밤을 샌다. 그런데 이 寄生은 平壤의 芺蓉과 明珠라나. 잘못드럿는지는 모르나-. ＜88＞

溫泉의 엇던날 午后

黃海道에 有名字한 信川溫泉이엿다. 信川에 왓다가 溫泉求景을 못하고 가면 두고두고 알는다는 말에 밥브지만 일브러 求景을 가섯다. 호화롭게 바로 自働車로 드려 모섯다. 朝鐵흐텔이며 日本人들의 旅舘이며 朝鮮人들의 假家며 모든 것이 그럴 듯이 되야잇다. 病客보다 遊興客이 더 만흐렷다. 안이나 밧기나 이집 저집 기웃거려보니 丹粧하는 美人 부시시 이러나는 美人 누구를 기다리는 듯 悄然히 안즌 美人 엇핏 보아도 다섯손가락은 셈이 부족하다.
十錢式을 주고 엇던 日本人 浴湯(引導者가 그리로)에 드러서니 살찐놈, 패란놈, 늙은이, 젊은이, 가로 세로 눗고, 잣바지고, 들고, 나고, 正말 平等이요 自由이요 自然스럽다면 自然그대로이다. 湯中生活이야말로 無差別 無階級이엿다. 하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, 열이요, 百이요, 數字세는놈, 「간다노자」愁心歌하는 놈, 식끄럽기도 여간 안이다. 이것이 西關沐浴軍들의 傳來之風이것다.
湯은 炭酸質이라 䟽黃냄새가 업다. 물이 덥고 또한 多量이다. 皮膚病, 胃腸病에 特效가 잇다고 한다. 그 물을 먹는 사람도 만타. 그러나 初對인 나는 한 목음 물엇다가 도로 뱃고 마럿다. 餘裕가 업고 知友조차 업스니 하고 난들 별수잇스랴 멋업시도 슬그머니 도라섯다.

警察署 內의 一時間

몰으는 이는 拘禁이나 당햇든줄 알녓다. 그러나 拘禁은 안이다. 所謂 記者의 行色이랄가.
바로 경찰계 소식을  압신다고 名啣張이나 톡톡히 허비하면서 일부러 차자간 것이 署長님 多事에 그만 1시간이나 기다렷다는 말이다. 그런데 이 1시간에 보고드른 잡동산이만 해도 주머니가 터질가 봐서 못다네엇다는 말이다.
경찰서란 그 곳은 죄업는 사람도 엇잿든 무시무시하것다. 아무리 大똑똑이라도 경찰서만 딱 닥치면 머믓머믓하겟다. 과연 怪惡한 곳이지. 안인게 안이라 그 칼, 그 縳繩, 그 유치장, 그 모자, 그 복장을 볼 때는 심정이 산 듯 하겟다.
이 날은 信川의 장날이요. 暑內의 가장 일밧븐 正午이니 千里遠客인들 1시간 안이라 1일인들 안이 기다리랴. 더구나 그 양반들이 일개 朝鮮人 기자를 그닥 대수롭게 할가 보냐. 엇잿든 잡동산이 엇은 것 뿐은＜89＞ 이익이다.
나도 村뚝이엿지만 모다가 村뚝이것다. 엇든 양복쟁이 村뚝이는 의사 면허 請으로 서장 前앞 굽실거리고 엇던 村婦는 場판에서 假家를 잘못 버렷다가 잡혀드러온 남편 소식을 알켜달나고 哀乞伏乞하고 엇던 상투쟁이는 엇던 面長의 심부럼으로 生雉 1쌍을 署長에게 갓다드리며 엇던 日男日女는 여관업 請으로 꼬박꼬박 절을 하고 잇스며 엇던 젊은 巡査는 번역 잘못으로 警部나으리 압헤 氣着을 하고 셧고 엇던 죄인은 海州로 가는지 平壤으로 가는지 縳繩을 지고 경찰서가 터지도록 한숨을 쉬며 나가는데 눈 희고 코 웃둑한 露國白軍 찌트럭이는 양복감 삽시라고 굽실거린다.
야! 이것이 세계엿다. 사람사는 세계엿다. 경찰서내의 1시간? 굉장하구나!
그런데 이 곳에는 절도, 사기 等 知識犯이 만타고.

安岳의 富豪家

安岳에서는 富者ㅅ집 니약이를 빼고는 할 니약이가 업것다. 움집에 간장잇다고 것츠로 보면 허수룩해도 안으로 보면 씨가 박엿것다. 것 꼬락산이 보아서는 사흘 굶은 거지도 안이 드러갈 듯하나 정말 드러가면 병풍친 돗자리에 되새리고 안즌 부자님을 뵈일 수 잇다. 그들은 專門이 잇는지라 발서 觀相察色부터 하고 다시 금궤단속부터 하단다.
「영감」하든지 「주인님」하든지 엇잿든 극키 공손한 말로 성의를 다하지 안으면 눈거러보지도 안코 「나는 기자요」 「나는 형사요」하고 職名을 좀 까다랍게 붓치지 안으면 또한 「저게 다 무언고?」하고 아조 거만을 피이것다. 이것은 安岳富者 안이 安岳의 고리대금업자만 그런 것은 안이다. 냉정과 거만은 착취자의 一武器라는 천하의 定評이 잇다. 골내는 친구 잇스니 富者打鈴은 집어 치우자!

月波樓上 何所得

黃州名所 月波樓! 내 이를 동경한지 오래엿다. 京義車가 黃州를 지낼 때마다 車窓밧그로  멀니 건너 버러보며 엉딍이를 번번히 들먹거리엿다. 그러나 終是 올나설 기회가 업더니 乙丑 春 3월이 별로 나에게 호의를 던져 꼿피고 나븨나는 아젹에 月波樓에 올으게 하니 乾坤이 有意生男兒의 반대로 男兒有意遊乾坤이라는 感이 생긴다.
두고두고 동경하든 月波樓! 이제 대하고 보니 소득이＜90＞ 무엇이냐? 赤壁江 흐르난 물은 古今이 일반이요. 德月山 올으는 해는 의연히 한빗친데 뭇노니 人事도 따라갓흐냐 말이다.
桑田도 碧海되고 碧海도 桑田되나니 人事를 말하랴만은 世情을 가진 이 몸인지라 慽慽한 감회가 古人 今人에게 가는도다.

鳳山의 百里炬火

4월 20일 오후엿다. 新幕行 기차가 沈村을 지내니 때는 8시 경이다. 차중 엇던 청년이 갑작이 수선을 떨며 「아- 저것 구경하시오. 굉장도 함니다」 한다. 「무어냐」 고 車中客은 일시에 눈을 드러 西便 창 밧글 내다 보니 야- 참- 굉장도 하다. 十里炬火냐? 百里炬火냐? 서울 南大門通의 야경을 멧 십배 연장하야 옴겨온 듯 점점 한 炬火가 멧 십리에 죽-뻣쳣다. 車中滿客은 모다 右便으로 쏠니니 차가 뒤집필 지경이다. 桂東을 지나 沙里院오기까지 그 炬火는 그대로 보인다. 독갑이 작란인지 火神의 총출동인지 참말 꿈갓튼 사실이다. 沙里院시내에 와서 내미러 보니 역시 一樣이다. 처음 보는 大奇觀이다.
그런데 이것이 무슨 炬火일가? 이 지방 사람은 尋常觀하는 연례로 하는 載寧江의 갈게(蟹) 잡이다. 밤에 횃불을 들고 강변에 나서면 모든 게 색기가 떼를 지어 기여나온다 한다. 그러면 구럭이나 자루에 주섬주섬 집어 담을 뿐이라 한다. 해마다 이 때면 부근의 남녀노소가 총출동을 하야 갈게잡이를 한다고.

夜行正方山城

山家의 點火가 欲滅未滅할 때에 자동차가 城門밧게 다엇다. 無垢한 村翁은 畫夜客을 가리지 안는다.
