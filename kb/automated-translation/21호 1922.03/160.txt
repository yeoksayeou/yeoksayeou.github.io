Publication Date: 1922년 03월 01일
Article Title: 숨쉬이는 木乃伊 外 (Breathing Mummy and Others)
Author: 金石松
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Breathing Mummy and Others
						Kim Soksong

Breathing Mummy

Oh! I see it!
The breathing mummy.

Dressed in clothes called "Modernity" (現代)
Anointed with medicine called "System" (制度)
Placed in a coffin called "Life" (生活)
I see the mummy.

And I,
Myself, already
A breathing mummy—
Ah! I mourn!

Girl Who Sells Laughter

On a rotting face
Powder applied thick and musty,
Like inside a zoo cage (動物園)
In a brothel (娼樓), sitting out,
The girl who sells laughter.
Do not despair.
Though all the people of the world
Slander you,
They secretly
Envy you.
You desire only money,
But they, more than you,
Have complex desires—
Prostitutes (娼婦) of all shapes and colors.

Frightful Night

Hey, friend!
What time is it now?
Isn't the time when the rooster crows
Drawing very, very near?<16>
Like the eyes of a fox,
The starlight,
When it vanishes into that frightful darkness—
Startled by suspicious tracks,
Barking itself into a frenzy,
That relentless dog's barking,
When it can no longer be heard—
Ah! How much time is left?
Hey, friend!

Theft, Robbery, Fraud, Gambling...
All the traditional crimes,
And Adultery, Keeping Concubines, Divorce,
Poverty, Wealth, Waste...
All the sins of the future
Shamelessly dance.
This frightful night's curtain,
Ah! How long it is!

Drunkards, Hypocrites, Madmen, Geniuses...
Their groaning sounds,
This frightful symphony,
How long will it continue!
Ah! Hey, friend!
What time is it now?
Isn't the time when the rooster crows
Drawing very, very near?

Drunkard

Drunkard: "Boy! Boy!
I don't want Soju.
Take away the 'whiskey' too...
Something stronger... stronger!
Bring me 'Society' (社會) wine.
Quickly, 'Communism' (共産) wine... bring it!
I am a 'Citizen of the World' (世界人)!
Hey you, boy! Boy!"
Boy: "Those sons of bitches
Who claim to live by
Freedom (自由) and Equality (平等) —
Look at the state they're in!"<17>

Children's Song

Hey there, comrade, listen!
Lately, whenever I pass through the streets,
Those shaggy-haired children, gathered in groups,
Singing that song merrily—
Comrade, did you hear it or not?
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo
How much must one strike for it to awaken?

Look at the state of those corpse-like kids!
Did a dog run off with their family's ancestral tablets?
A wooden shoe floating by the stream's edge (개ㅅ가에),
They pick it up, calling it a proper ancestral tablet,
Wash it clean, and bow to it, they say.
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo
How much must one strike for it to awaken?

Look at the state of those corpse-like kids!
Just because he ate scraps under a stepfather's watch,
The younger brother hates his full brother.
The place where he badmouths his own brother
Is, paradoxically, the stepfather's steward's office, they say.
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo
How much must one strike for it to awaken?

Look at the state of those corpse-like kids!
They don't lament being worse off than others,
But just because others are better off than them,
They get angry and jump about for whatever reason,
Then drag others down with them into the ditch.
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo
How much must one strike for it to awaken?

Look at the state of those corpse-like kids!
Their business cards list this and that,
At first glance, they seem respectable enough,
But the more titles they have,
The more graft (뒤딱지 먹틔) they seem to engage in.
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo
How much must one strike for it to awaken?

Look at the state of those corpse-like kids!
They act like they know everything all the time,
But inside, there's only a bundle of loofah.
Picking up bits from magazine pages and newspaper scraps,
They carelessly chatter (지절대어) half-learned fancy words.<18>
Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo<18>
<16-18>

**Summary**

This collection of five poems by Kim Soksong, published in the March 1922 issue of *Kaebyŏk*, paints a bleak and critical picture of Korean society under Japanese colonial rule. Using metaphors like the "Breathing Mummy" trapped by modernity and systems, the poems express deep disillusionment and a sense of lifelessness pervading society. They critique various social ills: the hypocrisy surrounding prostitution, the moral decay and despair symbolized by the "Frightful Night," the empty posturing of a drunkard demanding radical "Communism wine," and the ignorance, internal conflict, jealousy, corruption, and superficiality satirized in the verses of the "Children's Song" set to the tune of Arirang. The poems collectively lament the state of the nation and its people, questioning when they will "awaken." From a historical perspective, these poems are significant for their raw portrayal of despair and social critique just three years after the March 1st Movement. Historians might find the direct engagement with social types (intellectuals, the corrupt, those envious of others), the use of potent metaphors for colonial modernity, and the explicit mention of "Communism" as an alternative ideology particularly revealing about the intellectual and social ferment of early 1920s Korea.
