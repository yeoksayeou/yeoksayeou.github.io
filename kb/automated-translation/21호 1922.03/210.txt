발행일 1922년 03월 01일
기사제목 除夜=(第二回前號續)= (New Year's Eve = Continued from the Previous Issue, Part 2 =)
필자 廉想涉
기사형태 소설 (Novel)

New Year's Eve =
						Yŏm Sangsŏp
3
The strong consciousness, "I am already not a virgin," reigned over the young heart of around 20, not yet hardened. It is no different from how a religious believer's breaking of commandments is by no means easy, but a single misstep, reacting paradoxically, cannot be satisfied unless the poisonous cup of corruption is drained to the last drop. The pulsation and thirst of young blood, having once tasted the sweet dew of sexuality, devoured the will for restraint. Thus, by the time I realized I had become audacious enough to surprise even myself, it was already too late. Moreover, looking around at the rich and splendid surroundings, there might be the anguish of intoxication, but how could the balance of the mind possibly be maintained?

Indeed, the six years of life in Tokyo were a splendid stage, different even from what I had experienced at home. The crowd of young men of all shapes and colors gathering before me appeared more brilliant and satisfying than a lady standing before a display case of jewelers. Among them were musicians. There were poets. There were painters. There were novelists. Law students, medical students, independence activists, social activists, church staff, theology students... etc., from various fields. Though still raw eggs, they were nevertheless a group, each somewhat known, or rather, recognized figures in Chosŏn society. There were handsome men and ugly men; there were those posing as gentlemen or scholars, and those who hadn't shed their rough student ways. There were overly sensitive, clever individuals, and dullards. There were sons of wealthy families, and poor scholars. However, whatever kind of man, there was none without his unique characteristics. There was no man who did not arouse at least some curiosity. In a word, they were like rings poured into a jewel box by the handfuls.<47> There were rubies, and sapphires; there was pure gold, and platinum. There were diamonds, and agate. There were plain ones and colored ones... If I wanted to wear one, I could wear any; if I didn't want to wear one, I could wear none at all. I could wear ten rings on ten fingers if I wished, or wear just a single one on one finger. Sometimes, to see the luster and luxurious quality, I could try them on one by one, alternating. However, I adopted just one method: to wear one on the left and one on the right, of different types, as a pair. But for those who considered it fortunate just to be worn on my hand, which held the rich musk of the flesh, there was no reason to complain that there were two, one on the left and one on the right. To them, I was absolute. My will was the highest authority. In this way, the palace of the goddess of love, surrounded by numerous attendants, only grew wider, deeper, and more brilliant the further one stepped inside... The path ahead for corruption was a wide, smooth road.

"But why call it unrestrained, unreflective, and immoral? For what reason do you humble yourself and call it corruption? Wasn't it done after realizing everything and being fully conscious?"

You might retort like this. Indeed, at that time, it was done with awareness and consciousness. Not even once did I ever regret it. Moreover, the undigested, bean-curd-like knowledge gained from some reading rather provided a shield to justify my own scandalous conduct.—
"Regret is foolish. Isn't the so-called moral repentance, which supposedly motivates change, just naming a motion of non-confidence in oneself? But ultimately, it proves nothing but one's own ignorance, discourages the precious pride called self-confidence, and fosters the vice of self-contempt, offering no benefit whatsoever. Rather, it merely provides the motive to repeat another mistake or sin that will be regretted. Saying 'I must never touch it again,' 'I must not even think about it,' after regretting and agonizing over the pangs of conscience, what remains? Doesn't time take away all heat? When that heat cools, doesn't an even more intense curiosity surge up?... Self-improvement? That is not the mission of regret. The desire to draw oneself truly towards greater good, greater beauty, greater truth, cannot be attempted without the <48> power of an impulse originating from an entirely different source. If a hundred regrets are worth less than a single stirring of reason, tiny as a needlepoint, where is the moral theory that self-improvement comes from regret? To me, regret is not worth even the value of a cold, bitter kiss from droopy, blue lips."

This was the weapon my meager knowledge had prepared to suppress my conscience. My life of faith, too, lacked even the strength of a fly caught in an autumn wind to provoke a single moment of reflection in me. Going to church, reading the Bible, and singing hymns was not the real me. Generally, habituation implies senselessness, and my faith was likely just that. Occasionally crying out to God, calling "Lord, Lord," was not prompted by regret, but either by a desire to lean on something due to extreme loneliness, or because I felt a kind of anxiety and fear when thinking about my future path.

"Do you call me a devil? So what? Isn't the awareness of being a devil itself the awareness of being strong? Nothing enriches the content of life like the joy of victory."—This was my rebuttal to moral self-examination. Truly, morality held no authority over me. The boiling desire to absolutely fulfill my own life meant that trampling and sacrificing everything was not regrettable; this was the supreme conviction guiding my life autonomously.

"We live. Therefore, we love life passionately. We have a duty to love it passionately. Therefore, any means taken to satisfy the love of life is permissible...
The shackle called morality, hiding under the fine clothes of the beauty of chastity or the sublimity of fidelity, dictates that a lifetime must be devoted to slavish service to just one man, reigning over delicate women. Moreover, it is lopsidedly harsh only on women. But even if it were equally demanded of men, it would only pass muster among the blindly obedient who are satisfied with foolish honesty that is actually false. To emotionally agile and intellectually sharp men and women, it is the tyranny of a despot demanding the sacrifice of a beautiful entire life without compensation. If asked to sit looking at the same face for just 24 hours, wouldn't claiming not to get a headache <49> be the height of stupidity? To demand sitting and looking until black hair turns white like onion roots—how much better is that than a death sentence? That love must not vanish, that aversion must not arise, is the decree of the intellect called morality, definitely not the holistic desire of the central life force. When one feels the sorrow of satiety towards one love and moves on to another, what immoral flaw is there, and what rupture occurs in communal human life? Forget everything and just love life."

A frighteningly dogmatic prejudice. But I tried to support and affirm it relentlessly, without time to debate its rights and wrongs, because it was convenient for self-justification.
Not only that, but whenever I saw newspapers, magazines, or so-called lecture meetings discussing chastity, the misconduct of female students, or the disruption of public morals—while conveniently placing their own far greater ugliness high up on a shelf—and clamoring noisily with immature logic, I could not suppress the rebellious scorn and anger rising in my throat.

"Chastity (貞操)? What does that mean? Perhaps your pockets are empty these days? Surely you were treated poorly at a kisaeng house? You couldn't even hook one scatterbrained girl willing to pay divorce expenses? Humph, Chastity? What does your own dung smell like? If you're confident there isn't a speck of dust on your eyebrows, then babble all you want. Are you that greedy for so-called female chastity? Is it because there are many unchaste women in Chosŏn society that there are so many club-wielding thugs (亂棒軍)!
But what is chastity? Is it the satisfaction of desire that men demand from women on the condition of guaranteeing livelihood? Or is it the desire of so-called cultured individuals to satisfy a refined taste? If monopoly is so desired, wouldn't it be simpler and less worrying about thieves to make a female Buddha statue with the money poured into one woman's mouth and sit looking at it, or buy a flowerpot or two? Such strange tastes abound.
Then, is it a form of transaction that women pay with rouge and powder money? As a business, isn't it too impersonal, too ugly? Then is it also a kind of <50> peculiar taste to relish the praise and pride of being a virtuous keeper of fidelity oneself? It is an ignorant act that abuses humanity too much.
But if a woman says, 'Men demand female chastity with material goods, so I will use chastity as liquid capital to trade for pleasure,' or 'The exchange value of material goods and chastity is disparate; when men lack a concept of chastity, I will purchase pleasure with the surplus value to achieve balance,' what would men answer? If it's the same commercial principle, there should be no reason why it applies to one case and is unreasonable in another.
Moreover, who says chastity is not kept? While the intimate relationship with A continues, one will be a chaste mistress to A; while the marital relationship with B persists, just being a virtuous wife to B is enough, isn't it? Continuing a marital relationship with A while feeling no affection whatsoever towards A is, rather, adultery. In that situation, even if A still has affection for me, that's a separate issue. Perhaps gratitude may be expressed, but I have no need or obligation to maintain the state of union. To speak more strictly, if one party's desire to love is intense, regardless of the other party's attitude, they might keep chastity themselves. But that only means fulfilling an obligation to oneself or satisfying the demand of morality. However, that is voluntary. It cannot be sought from the slave morality of coercion. Anyway, chastity is not a commodity. Nor is it a taste. It is a virtue, the expression of individuality left to free will.

"Christ's saying that he who looks at a woman with lust has already committed adultery is the ultimate truth. By the highest moral standard, it is not a matter of the flesh, but a matter of the spirit. Seen from the perspective of love itself, spirit and flesh are equals, but from the standpoint of chastity, the flesh is likely secondary or an attendant condition. If so, who can say they have not committed adultery? Isn't it ultimately a case of fifty steps versus a hundred? Then what should be done about this issue? A system or morality that exists but cannot be practiced is worse than useless. It is certainly poisonous. But it's not that Christ's teaching isn't true. Then how can it be reconciled with practice?
It's easy. Christ only offered negative teachings. But negativism (勿爲主義) is not applicable in the modern era. <51> In 'do not do,' there are only retreat and failure, but if one says 'Do it! Do this!' and designates the best and possible option, there will be no harm. Therefore, when seeing a woman and feeling lust—though 'lust' (色情) sounds a bit ugly, it probably means 'love-feeling' (戀情)—that is, when a love-feeling arises, if both parties agree, isn't it permissible to affirm it according to the desire?
Only in actively pushing forward is there the true expression of life. Christ gave good teachings. But because the conclusion was wrong, it couldn't be perfect."

No matter how eloquently I arrange fine words, justification is ultimately just justification, and what remains is only the solemn fact. It has less value than a tower built of ashes (재). Indeed, such justifications are merely crutches given to those wandering halfway up the stairs. However, I cannot say that these thoughts of mine were entirely wrong. But doesn't the wide gap between my thoughts and actions prove my corruption more than anything? In theory, one can say anything. I said, "Chastity is not a commodity. Nor is it a taste. It is the free expression of love arising from sublime, pure, and refined emotions." But wasn't I engrossed only in satisfying beastly desires (獸慾)? Not carnal desire, but beastly desire. While loudly proclaiming, "Who doesn't keep chastity? With A, to A; with B, to B," didn't I wear rings on both hands? Prostitutes are nothing different. They, at least, sell their bodies to eat, leaving room for sympathy. But knowingly indulging in addiction makes one a devil worse than a prostitute, forever unable to be saved. What cannot be taught, cannot be beaten, cannot be helped even by one's own hand, is the parasitic insect (蠹蟲) on humanity that knowingly hastens down the path of self-destruction.

Ah! I brazenly insisted that chastity is not a commodity. But I sold it. It was a splendid commodity. Setting aside the means of livelihood, I even tried to obtain funds for studying by this means. When I think back to my time at the girls' school, how I disliked even talking with retired kisaengs or someone's mistress who enrolled, and how my peers and I sneered, saying,
"Studying by selling her body, the price of that education isn't just one or two suits of clothes," I want to crawl into a rat hole. <52>

Selling the body might perhaps be forgiven. But what about selling the spirit as well? Sins committed out of ignorance might be sympathized with, and one might see the light on the day of repentance. But sins committed knowingly—can they only open the gates of hell? Forgiving the loss of chastity due to the torment of virginity or the pressure of sexual desire might be possible if one chooses to. Forgiving prostitute-like immoral behavior due to innate bad nature and lewd temperament might also be possible if one chooses to. But when it comes to calculating profit and loss, eyeing a man's wealth, and seducing him—isn't that the desperate end, unworthy even of being counted among human beings?

My relationships with Mr. P and Mr. E were like that. Especially my feelings towards Mr. E—I cannot help but blush just recalling them myself. If by chance you have the opportunity to meet them in the future, please convey a final word from me.
Tell them, "The travel pass for Berlin (伯林行 旅行券) became extinct in Chosŏn, so I went to the hell called America."

4
I first met Mr. E around the end of March, just a few days after I returned to the country. That day, sponsored by the X-Association (X-會), a women's lecture meeting was held at the Youth Association Hall (靑年會舘), specifically centering on me, newly returned to Chosŏn.
My topic was "The Struggle of the Sexes."
"Today's men will achieve true harmony between the sexes only after experiencing an era of struggle between them. While this is urgent for women, of course, it is also a necessary process of sexual training for men. However, complete equality between men and women presupposes equal duties and rights in both spiritual and material aspects. Among those discussing women's issues, even women themselves often focus solely on the spiritual side, neglecting the material issue that forms a crucial part of the fundamental solution. This is a severe self-contradiction. While women themselves seek liberation from today's slavish conditions, relying on men also for material benefits is the folly of entrusting the key to the enemy and then lamenting not being released. From this, the emergence of the issue of women's occupations <53> is an inevitable conclusion. Simultaneously, we encounter the significant problems of defining the boundaries of women's duties towards the family and how women should seek employment, but this cannot be discussed in a single day. Moreover, issues like the degree of service women should render to both family and society cause headaches for knowledgeable people. Ultimately, however, the urgent issue boils down to women's education. Women's independent living, and consequently issues like occupation, also have education as their prerequisite. Therefore, we self-aware comrades must first fundamentally study this education issue, while simultaneously feeling the duty and responsibility to take the lead in the struggle between the sexes. Truly, we pioneering women must declare war on men, while concurrently pouring all our efforts into training reserve troops, and also devising all sorts of plans for self-sufficient provisions...
Until we make our own meals with our own hands, we will never have the chance to escape our slave-like existence... He who drags a ladder seeing the moon is a drunkard. The wise will build a telescope. Only thirst will seek out the one standing under a fruit tree with mouth agape. Before appealing to men, let us first consult our own two arms..."

I rambled on like this for 40 or 50 minutes. You must be beyond wry smiles and into anger. But the mouth moves as it pleases, while the feet cannot lift even a foot off the ground. This is the entirety of modern women. Not just women, but perhaps the men who have supposedly emerged into society are no different.
Anyway, that day I even played the piano. I, who failed the entrance exam for the Tokyo Music School four years ago, played the piano. Even though I had practiced occasionally on my own in the school dormitory, it was certain to be worse than when I prepared for the entrance exam, but since they put it on the program without my consent and insisted I must play, I played after failing to refuse. But don't be surprised. I received such thunderous applause that I got two encores. At first, I thought I was being made fun of, but looking at the emcee's expression, it didn't seem so. Ah, this is why a young woman's vanity grows without limit, isn't it? Ultimately, however, the applause was likely not for my hands, but perhaps for my face, or respect for the abstract noun "Woman." In fact, while it was called a women's lecture meeting, the audience was overwhelmingly male, making a men's lecture look pale in comparison. <54> In other words, Chosŏn society is a bizarre, unprecedented society where men must do women's work and women must do men's work. In essence, it's because Chosŏn people are a race with only eyes and mouths, but no ears. Yet, almost all lecture meetings eventually turn into audition rooms for the Music School—what kind of phenomenon is this? Ah, we were born into such a society. Today's society too, perhaps, is merely an enlargement of our own household. It is truly difficult.

Anyway, the gathering concluded amidst great success, and while the audience dispersed, we briefly went into the reception room. There were 5 or 6 unfamiliar young men. We exchanged initial greetings. After everyone had been introduced, it was P's turn. "This gentleman... perhaps you already know each other through correspondence, but this is Mr. E, who manages ㅇㅇ," he introduced.
Tall and slender, with a longish face and lips pursed tightly as if looking down on people slightly—I instantly sized him up as not a man to be judged superficially.

There are two types of men I love. Either men who are weak-willed and subserviently follow women (like Mr. P, so to speak), or, the exact opposite, men who act somewhat arrogant and cold, as if women of average caliber are beneath their notice. Mr. E, so to speak, was one of those men. Towards men like Mr. P, I feel a kind of pity. Sometimes I even feel a mother's affection for her child. In other words, I can bestow the benevolent grace of a strong person's余裕 (leisurely) warmth towards the weak. But in cases like Mr. E, it's entirely different. The positions are reversed. I become the weak one myself, seeking compassion and protection. Sometimes I imagine the warm, sweet taste of a young chick under the hen's wing. There is an ineffable pleasure in begging for love as the weaker party. But there, the necessity of combative tension, vigilance, and effort exists.

Anyway, upon seeing Mr. E once, I already took a liking to him. No matter how much renown I had in the women's circles, receiving the adoration of the miscellaneous crowd, seeing him treat me with an attitude of indifference, as if he were different from worldly young men, <55> he seemed unlikely to fall into my hands easily. Yet, the more I thought it wouldn't be easy, the more a groundless resentment arose, and feeling insulted, anger flared up. The thought of somehow drawing him into my hands, no matter what, rarely left my mind for several days afterward. But on the other hand, I also thought, "Seeing E show such an unexpectedly indifferent attitude, perhaps it might be surprisingly easy. If so, E too is just a trivial man no better than P."... I was this kind of strange woman. I don't engage in love; I compete. In other words, I love the disposition (性僻), I love victory, I love love itself. Even with the most insignificant man, if there's a competitor, I will fight for him. But in cases like Mr. E, I fight against E's arrogance. And even if I achieve victory, a brief taste is enough. It's not out of disillusionment, but continuing beyond that feels like a kind of shame, and frankly, it's mundane. First, I pursue from this side to gain victory, and finally, abandoning him—therein lies the victory. In other words, it wasn't because the man was outstanding that I became infatuated, but rather to demonstrate that my will is absolute at all times. Therefore, wanting to taste a love more precious than death is nothing more than fantasy.

Two days after the lecture meeting, Mr. P headed back to Tokyo. P had dropped everything—his graduation thesis he was writing, various errands—and returned to the country because I had dragged him to Hagwan (Shimonoseki) saying we should sightsee together on the way, and then he came all the way to Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) since he was already there.

Mr. E was also at the train station. After sending P off on the 7:20 PM train, that night I went to Mr. E's house outside Sŏdaemun Gate and stayed together playing until nearly midnight. It was, so to speak, a situation where I sent one man out the west window and opened the front room door for another man. At first, Mr. E treated me respectfully as P's lover. I too talked mundanely about worldly affairs, dissatisfaction with Chosŏn society, etc., and then made an agreement to come learn English every day at a set time, before returning home.

Mr. E's study was a small Western-style room built separately in a corner of the rear garden, quite detached from the main inner house (안채). From the next day, <56> every day after finishing school, my hurried footsteps found their way to hide in that study. As is common with newly acquainted people, friendships often deepen, sometimes even with exaggeration, but ours was even more special. Perhaps the resonance of dissatisfaction and rebellious spirit towards society deepened our relationship at an accelerated pace. English study became just words; our time flew by as social critique turned into complaints about each other's circumstances and dreaming of future hopes. He shared various things: the worries that plague almost every young Chosŏn man—divorce issues, parents' lack of understanding—his plans to study in Germany soon, the back-and-forth regarding the travel permit application. I, for my part, also talked about my family situation, my failure to get into the women's university literature department, and that my relationship with P wasn't very deep, and besides, he was married, so nothing could be done. However, I didn't mention a word about the marriage proposal (婚談) that was my biggest headache at the time.

One day, I dropped by as usual, and found a note saying to wait a moment; Mr. E was out somewhere. As I sat alone leaning against the sofa, looking out the glass window at the lawn where green shoots were sprouting, suddenly a phrase from "Faust" popped into my head, and an urge surged to write something for Mr. E, so I picked up a pen on E's manuscript paper placed on the table.

"The restless spring has come again
Outside the fence, green pastures spread
Cattle and horses, crazed by will-o'-the-wisps,
On the dry sandy field
Circle round and round, round and round,
The cold stone fragments, unetchable save by God,
How long will they keep biting! <57>
How long will they rifle the bottomless thatched hut!
Like the goshawk (海東靑) pecking out its own life
How long will they feed on sorrow!"

I had drafted it and was about to revise it when Mr. E, who had entered unnoticed, came up silently behind me as I sat at the table, looked over my shoulder, and stood there.
"What's that?" Startled, I covered it with both hands, trying not to let him take it, but he grabbed my wrists, pushed them aside, and snatched it away. At that moment, for the first time, our warm flesh met.

Mr. E read it once, folded it neatly into his pocket, then came and stood beside me as I stood quietly by the glass window looking outside. Rumor had it that Mr. E was quite experienced, but at that moment, Mr. E stood silently trembling, unable even to look me in the face, like a boy experiencing first love... About two hours later, my face flushed as if drunk, I cooled it in the evening breeze and returned home a little early that day.

...Thinking back, his surrender so easily was truly unexpected. However, I couldn't be satisfied merely with conquering my opponent. I firmly resolved not to miss this opportunity. It wasn't because I had yielded my body. Then Mr. E's wealth? It wasn't that I didn't covet it, but that wasn't it either. It wasn't that Mr. E's learning, reputation, or appearance didn't appeal to me. It wasn't that I didn't pray for his divorce to succeed. But what absolutely could not be missed was the plan to study in Germany (獨逸留學). Even if it couldn't be Germany, just going abroad (洋行) would have been enough. Studying was one thing, but having only been to Japan, my vanity could absolutely not be satisfied.

However, though I met him daily, I showed no sign of this, but my fantasies grew day by day, and my impatient heart <58> became uncontrollable. Yet, thinking I shouldn't rush things, I finally decided to reveal the marriage issue first. I discussed how, due to my father's lack of understanding, it was almost forcibly decided, adding embellishments, true and untrue, as if it were urgent. Of course, it succeeded as expected. The relatively staid Mr. E seemed genuinely sympathetic and began to hurry things along. It was then that I first hinted specifically at my intention to study in Germany. Mr. E wasn't particularly opposed to this. However, in this society that already loved gossip, if the two of us suddenly went off together to study in Germany, it wouldn't look good for either of us publicly. So, his opinion was that we should first settle the divorce issue, get married, and then depart around this autumn, which would make things proceed smoothly. In truth, without doing so, there was no way to get the financial guarantee for the travel permit, our families wouldn't allow it, and there were numerous obstacles like P's headache-inducing misunderstanding and public opinion. Moreover, it seemed the marriage talks with you were proceeding secretly. Therefore, I generally had no choice but to agree.

However, the first obstacle was that E's father, who had passed his 60th birthday (還進甲), was raising hell, saying he wouldn't permit it unless he severed ties with his son, as long as my pupils were watching. Meanwhile, because Mr. X got involved in the so-called marriage talks, things progressed rapidly, with demands for a formal meeting (相面), setting a date (擇日), and daily commotion in the house.

But at this point, if I had taken a firm stance and insisted on going to Germany no matter what, perhaps Mr. E might have consented. However, one thing needed to be resolved.—This is the essence of the love-supremacy view (戀愛至上觀). It is the resolution to sacrifice everything for love without regret. But I loved Mr. E's father's document chest, loved Mr. E's trivial social reputation, dreamed of the empty title of "Mrs. E," and was carried away by the vanity of going abroad. For me at that time, willing to satisfy myself even with the shameful conduct of a prostitute for the sake of honor, it was absolutely impossible to bear the disgraceful name of "someone's mistress" and follow a young man.

Beyond that, however, we were both helpless. Mr. E, too, seemed at a loss, like a dazed person, merely saying,
"Well..., somehow, if the engagement is broken, or at least postponed, something might work out..." <59> But the situation then was such that postponement, let alone breaking the engagement, seemed unlikely to happen easily.

After much thought, I considered telling my father the whole story, thinking my greedy father might find it appealing, but the timing was late, and not only was there no way he would let me wait for a married man to get divorced, but it might even spoil the chance for postponement, so I dared not open my mouth. Then, I considered writing a letter directly to you, explaining the harms of customary marriage and rejecting it, saying I absolutely couldn't marry right now, as perhaps the quickest way. But firstly, your character and level of education were questionable, and consequently, if my father found out through Mr. X's ears, the matter would become neither porridge nor rice, so that too was impossible.

Here, I made up my mind. And so, thinking it advantageous for both sides to first withdraw from the educational world, I submitted my resignation from the school I had attended for just over a month, then met briefly with Mr. E and secluded myself at home.
My father, who had been nagging me like wildfire to settle down, was naturally pleased, but even my mother, who had originally shown some sympathy for my views, now seemed relieved, as if a burden had been lifted, and only tried her best not to displease me. My mother brought the photograph several times, which I used to toss onto the floor without even glancing at it, but this time she brought it again confidently, saying,
"He looks gentle, doesn't he? He doesn't drink or smoke and attends church regularly." I silently took it and placed it on the document box (文匣). Seeing this, my mother picked it up again, saying, "Now that you've decided, why be shy?" and unfolded the cover (것張) to place it in my hand.
"...A woman's face, isn't it? A household needs someone like this to manage it well."
"You'll have parents-in-law, so you'll have a proper life with them! As for provisions, that much should be sufficient anyway, and leaving Seoul is sad, but if you go as the Taegu branch manager, you'll only manage the household for a short while, and then they say they'll let you study more as you wish..."
My mother said this comfortingly. Indeed, I thought my mother's physiognomy reading wasn't wrong. <60> But simultaneously, Mr. E's face flickered before my eyes.
"Study in Germany? Customary marriage? Can such a thing happen! Meeting a man I've never seen or heard of now, and going off to a corner like Taegu—firstly, what will people in Seoul say... It's impossible. Even if I die, it's impossible."

Thinking this, I absentmindedly looked down at the photograph dropped on the desk (冊床), and my mother said,
"If you want to meet him, I can call him here even tomorrow, but..." looking up at my face. But I refused point-blank. Since it was decided according to my parents' wishes, wasn't it enough if it suited their intentions? I answered nonchalantly, then requested that the wedding ceremony at least be delayed until summer, after I recovered from my illness. I knew, of course, this request wouldn't be granted. Based on what I had occasionally overheard since the marriage talks began, your plan, it seems, was to get promoted to branch manager, hold the wedding on the way, have a grand reception (披露宴) in Kyŏngsŏng for both occasions, take up the post temporarily, and then use a business trip to Tokyo as an opportunity for a honeymoon. Added to that, since a buyer (作者) for the house you intended to sell had appeared, you seemed particularly anxious to settle everything within a month. But I was infuriated and couldn't bear that such invaluable reasons—trivial matters of profit/loss or vanity like transfers, house sales, convenience of receptions and honeymoons—were not only swaying the so-called major life event of marriage but also determining the fate of a woman. It was then that I first realized the sorrow of being born a woman.

"Nervous breakdown (神經衰弱)? Girls get that from studying too much. Once you marry, that kind of illness cures itself."
This was my father's response to my request to postpone the wedding. First, it's father's word; second, it's father's word; a child who doesn't listen to her father's word is not my daughter, he tried to coerce me with tyranny. But would he kick me out for disobedience? Not even that. The surveillance seemed to be intensifying.

And so, the date-selection slip (擇日單子) was to be sent shortly. Fortunately, the date fell on July 2nd, so there was still a month left, but if I hesitated and delayed further, I might reach a point of no return, and I couldn't stay attached to the household buzzing <61> with excitement. And so, now I made my final decision once more.
May 29th was a Sunday. Saying I was going to the chapel with Chŏngŭi, who had come from the dormitory the day before, I left a little early. In my pocket was one month's salary, 60 Wŏn.
After crossing Mo Bridge (毛橋) and walking a short distance, I told Chŏngŭi I needed to stop by K's house and that she should go on ahead. I separated from her and headed towards K's house. But in reality, I turned in the opposite direction from K's house, came out past the Commercial Bank, and jumped onto a streetcar bound for Yongsan. However, fearing I'd be late, I got off midway at Namdaemun Station... Hurriedly, somehow, I managed to hide myself on the Pusan-bound express train. I couldn't meet Mr. E, but I was sure he would come down if I just sent a telegram from Pusan; we had already agreed on this the day I submitted my resignation a week earlier, and I had also sent him a letter on Saturday, so I wasn't worried.

Somehow arriving safely at Tongnae Station, I immediately telegraphed Mr. E upon arrival to say I had settled in safely, then went straight to the hot springs (溫泉) and checked into the Sanhaegwan (山海舘). The maids, recognizing a guest who had visited a couple of months prior, were pleased but also seemed somewhat suspicious, seeing me without luggage and without the man who had accompanied me then.
"That room from last time is vacant, shall we take you there?"
The maid leading the way tried to guide me to the third floor, but I declined and took a small room on the second floor. When I came with Mr. P, we had claimed to be siblings and taken two adjacent rooms, behaving quite cautiously, but it was unlikely the savvy maids hadn't noticed, and since Mr. E would be coming this time too, staying in that room felt a bit uncomfortable, so I did that.

Mr. E, arriving one train later than promised, had departed the following night and thus arrived the morning after next. However, the news Mr. E brought was utterly beyond imagination. Deep inside Mr. E's large new briefcase lay a travel permit (旅行免許狀). From the time I fled to Pusan, it felt like acting out a silent film or a popular novel, making me feel desolate and also amused, but now that things turned out like this, I was not only disheartened but couldn't help feeling a kind of humor. <62>
The travel permit had arrived 4 or 5 days ago, but rather than informing me, he wanted to meet calmly and discuss things, which is why he hadn't notified me in the meantime—that was Mr. E's explanation. Judging by Mr. E's inner thoughts, it seemed he didn't have the passion to keep me with him at all costs and even send me to study in Germany, but anyway, it appeared he hadn't mentioned the travel permit approval and let my flight to Pusan happen, wanting to meet at least once more before departing and have a pleasant time. However, my head was tangled like messy hemp, leaving no room for even one interesting conversation. Pleading to follow him somehow was futile. Mr. E also looked resigned, but naturally said he would leave soon. There were ship schedules to consider, and not much preparation needed, so there was no need to linger pointlessly and witness a headache-inducing scene. Thinking about it, that made sense, but in the circumstances at that time, I couldn't retract the step I had already taken. With the wedding date approaching, going back home with my head bowed was absolutely impossible.

And so, after whispering together for almost two days and nights, engaging in what you could truly call a huddle (鳩首相議), we finally agreed that the only option left was for me to cross over to Japan for now, hide for a while, assess the situation, and then make plans later to depart separately.
Thus, I decided to go to Pyŏlbu (Beppu?) first and wait for Mr. E to make preparations and come, intending to leave that night. Worried someone from home might pursue me, I hurried. However, while packing my belongings, it suddenly occurred to me that I needed a travel permit to cross the Hyŏnhaet'an (Genkai Sea). It was a case of falling flat on my face and breaking my nose; now I could neither retreat nor advance, truly a rat caught in a jar. Mr. E just sat there, chuckling. But things having come to this, we decided there was no choice but to return to Seoul separately for now, obtain a travel permit, and then set out again. So we stayed one more day.

The next morning, Mr. E left for Kyŏngsŏng. After seeing him off, I lay bundled in blankets all day long in the second-floor inn room, then, like an ox heading into its coffin, I had no choice but to follow him back up. When boarding the train, my heart wasn't exactly calm, but it seemed a search warrant hadn't been issued yet. I stayed up all night in the train car, and just as the sun was rising, <63> the train arrived at Yongsan Station. I had bought a ticket all the way to Namdaemun, but unsure what might happen, I hesitated for a moment, then made up my mind and jumped off. Seeing the red band on the cap of the uniformed policeman standing right by the ticket gate, my heart sank unexpectedly, but managing to pass through the gate nonetheless, I let out a silent sigh—whew—and without looking back, immediately tried to jump into a rickshaw. But from behind,
"Hey there, just a moment!" At that single phrase, my legs froze as if stuck fast.
"Ah! 'Just a moment...'"
To my ears, it was the sound of a soul-summoning call (招魂) at dawn. It wasn't just a moment; it was eternity.
...When I returned home, accompanied by my father who had come to the Yongsan police station, it was nearly noon. <64>

**Summary**

This installment of Yŏm Sangsŏp's novel "New Year's Eve" features the female narrator reflecting on her past relationships and justifying her perceived "fallen" state. After a liberated life in Tokyo exploring relationships with various men, she returns to Korea. She critiques societal hypocrisy regarding female chastity (貞操) and traditional morality, arguing for female autonomy and intellectualizing her choices, even as she admits to manipulative behavior, using relationships for personal gain like funding her studies or escaping an arranged marriage. She describes her entanglement with two men, P and E, ultimately attempting to flee with E to Germany to avoid marriage, only to be thwarted by practicalities and eventually caught by her father after returning from a failed escape attempt to Pusan and Tongnae. From a historical perspective, the text offers a fascinating glimpse into the mindset of a "New Woman" in 1920s colonial Korea, grappling with traditional expectations (arranged marriage, chastity) versus modern desires (love, education abroad, personal freedom). The explicit discussion of sexuality, female agency, critique of patriarchal norms, and the yearning for escape (specifically to Europe, bypassing Japan) within the colonial context are particularly revealing for historians studying gender, modernity, and social change during this period.

「나는 벌서 處女가 아니다.」라는, 굿센 意識은, 아즉 굿지 안은, 20前後의 어린 마음에 君臨합니다. 그것은 마치 宗敎信者의 破戒라는 것이, 決코 容易하지 안흐나, 單 한번의 失足이, 反動的으로 墮落의 毒杯를, 最後의 一滴까지 말니지 안흐면 滿足할 수 업는 것과 다를게 업습니다. 性的 甘露에 한번 입을 대인 젊은 피의 躍動과 饑渴은, 節制의 意志를 呑噬하얏습니다. 그리하야 내가 自己도 놀랄만치 大膽하야진 것을 깨다른 때는, 벌서 時機가 느젓습니다. 더구나 濃厚하고 華美한 四圍를 돌녀다 볼 제, 陶醉의 懊惱는 잇슬지 모르나, 마음의 平衡이 維持될 理가 잇섯겟습니까.
果然 6年間의 東京生活은 家庭에서 經驗한 것과도 또 다른 華麗한 舞臺이엇습니다. 나의 압헤 모여드는 形形色色의 靑年의 한 떼는, 寶玉商陳列箱압헤 선 婦人보다도, 나에게는 더 燦爛하고 滿足히 보엿습니다. 그들 中에는 音樂家도 잇섯습니다. 詩人도 잇섯습니다. 畵家도 잇섯습니다. 小說家도 잇섯습니다. 法律學生, 醫學生, 獨立運動者, 社會運動者, 敎會의 職員, 神學生... 等, 各方面에. 아즉까지 안흔 生닭얄이지만 그래도 朝鮮社會에서는, 제가끔 족음 或은, 知名의 士라는 總中이엇습니다. 美男子도 잇거니와 醜男子도 잇고 紳士나 學者然하는 者도 잇거니와 粗暴한 學生틔를 벗지 못한 者도 잇섯습니다. 神經過敏한 怜悧한 者도 잇고 鈍物도 잇습니다. 豊饒한 집 子弟도 잇고 貧窮한 書生도 잇습니다. 그러나 어떠한 男子던지, 各其 特色이 업는 것이 업섯습니다. 多少라도 好奇心을 주지 안는 男子가 업섯습니다. 一言으로 蔽하면, 寶玉箱에 한줌＜47＞ 두 줌들이 털여 둔 指環가튼 것이엇습니다. 루비도 잇거니와, 사파야도 잇고, 純金도 잇거니와, 프라치나도 잇습니다. 다이야도 잇거니와, 瑪瑙도 잇습니다. 민패도 잇고 색인 것도 잇습니다... 끼고 십흐면 아모 것이던지 낄 수 잇고, 끼기 실흐면 하나도 안 낄 수가 잇습니다. 열 손가락에 열 半指를 끼랴도 낄 수 잇고 한 손가락에 單 한 個만 끼일 수도 잇습니다. 或時 光澤과 奢侈한 品을 보랴고, 한個式 次例次例로 번갈아 끼어 볼 수도 잇습니다. 그러나 나는, 꼭 한가지 手段을 取하얏습니다. 左右便에 한個式 類 다른 것으로 對로하야 끼우기로. 그러나 芳醇한 肉의 麝香을 쥐은 나의 손에 끼워지는 것만 多幸으로 아는 그들에게는 左右에 두 個가 잇다고 不平을 품을 理가 업섯습니다. 그들에게 對한 나는 絶對이엇습니다. 나의 意思는 最高權威이엇습니다. 이 가티하야 許多한 臣僚에 擁圍된 愛의 女神의 宮殿은, 步一步 들어 갈스록 넓고 깁고 燦爛하얏슬뿐 이엇습니다...墮落의 前程은 坦坦大路이엇습니다.
「그러나 어찌하야 無節制라 하고 無反省이라 하며 沒道義라 하느냐. 무슨 까닭에 自卑하야 墮落이라 하느냐. 모든 것을 覺悟하고 充分히 意識한 뒤에 한 일이 아니냐?」
이가티 反問하실지도 모릅니다. 果然 그 當時에는, 覺悟하고 意識하고 한 일이엇습니다. 決코 單 한번이라도 後悔한 일은 업섯습니다. 더구나 若干의 讀書로부터 어든, 消化도 잘 되지 안는 비지가튼 知識은, 돌이어 自己의 醜行을 辯明할 방패를 쥐여 주엇섯습니다.-
「後悔라는 것은 어리석은 짓이다. 自己不信任의 彈劾案에 命名한 것이 所謂 道德的 改悛의 動機를 준다는 고마운 後悔가 아니냐. 그러나 그것은, 結局 自己의 無智를 証左하고, 自信이라는 貴엽은 프라이드를 阻喪케 하고, 自己侮蔑의 惡德을 涵養하는 結果밧게, 아모 有益도 업는 것이다. 돌이어 또 한번 後悔할 失策이나 罪惡을 거듭할 動機를 줄 따름이다. 다시는 손을 대여서는 아니 되겟다. 念頭에도 두어서는 아니 되겟다고, 後悔하고 良心의 苛責에 苦悶한 後에 남는 것은 무엇이냐? 時間은 모든 熱을 빼아서 가지 안는가. 그 熱이 식으면 그 後에는 더 한層 激甚한 好奇心이 치밀어 오지 안는가...自己 改善? 그것은 後悔의 使命은 아니다. 自己를 眞情으로 보담 더 善에 보담 더 美에 보담 더 眞에 끌랴는 慾求는, 全然히 다른 源泉을 가진 衝動의＜48＞ 힘이 아니고는 期圖할 수 업는 것이다. 百番의 後悔가 바눌끗만한 理智의 單 한번의 蠢動보다도 못하거던, 後悔로 自己改善이 되리라는 道德論이 어대잇느냐. 나에게 對한 後悔는 늘어진 푸른 입슐의 차듸차고 쓴 키쓰의 價値도 업다.」
이것이 나의 少量의 知識이, 良心을 壓伏하기 爲하야, 豫備하야 준 武器이엿나이다. 나의 信仰生活이란 것도, 나에게 單 한번의 反省을 일으키기에는, 秋風맛난 파리의 힘만도 못하얏습니다. 敎會에 가고, 聖經을 읽고 讚頌歌를 부르는 것은 眞正한 내가 아니엿습니다. 大槪 慣性이라는 것은, 無感覺을 意味하는 것입니다 마는 나의 信仰도 亦是 그러하얏슬 따름이엿습니다. 間或 한우님을 부르짓고, 主여主여 하얏다 하는 것도, 그것은 後悔가 시킨 것이 아니라, 넘우 孤獨하야 무엇에던지 依支하랴는 慾求로거나, 또는 自己의 前程을 생각하고, 一種의 不安과 恐怖를 感하기 때문이엇나이다.
「惡魔라 하는가? 그러니 어쪄란 말이냐? 自己가 惡魔라는 意識이야말로, 自己가 强하다는 意識이 아닌가. 勝利의 歡喜처럼 生活內容을 豊富케 하는 것은 업다.」-이것이, 道德的 自己省察에 對한 反駁文이엇나이다. 實로 나에게 對하야 道德이란 아모 權威도 업섯습니다. 自己의 生을, 絶對로 充足시키랴는 끌는 慾求압헤는, 모든 것을 蹂躪하고 犧牲하야도, 아깝지 안타는 것이, 나의 生活을 自律하야 가는 데에 最高信念이엇나이다.
「우리는 生活한다. 함으로 生活을 熱愛하다. 熱愛할 義務가 잇다. 함으로 生活의 愛를 滿足시키기 爲하야 取하는 바 一切의 手段은 可치 안흔 것이 업다...
所謂 道德이란 桎梏는, 한 男子에게만 一生涯를 奴隷的 奉仕에 바처야만 한다는 條文을, 貞操의 美니, 情操의 崇高니 하는 等 美衣에 숨겨가지고, 纖弱한 女性에게 君臨한다. 더구나 跛行的으로, 女子에게만 嚴酷하다. 그러나 設使 男子에게도, 同一히 要求한다 할지라도, 그것은 愚直하나 其實 虛僞에 滿足하는 盲從의 徒에게나 通用될 것이다. 感情이 敏活하고 理智가 明哲한 男女에게는, 아름답을 全生涯를, 代償업는 犧牲에 供獻하라는 暴君의 끼로친이다. 가튼 얼골을, 24時間만 치어다 보고 안젓스라 하야도, 頭痛이＜49＞ 아니 난다 하는 것이. 돌이어 愚鈍의 極致일 것인데, 一生涯를 검은머리가 파뿌리가 되도록 보고 안젓스라는 것은, 死刑宣告보다 낫다면 얼마나 나흘가. 愛가 消滅되어서는 아니 된다. 厭症이 나서도 아니 된다는 것은, 道德이라는 理智의 法令이요, 決코 中心生命의 全我的 慾求는 아니다. 한 戀愛에 對하야 飽滿의 悲哀를 感할 때 다른 戀愛에 옴겨 간다하기로, 거긔에 무슨 不道德的 缺陷이 잇고, 人類共同生活에 무슨 破裂이 생기겟느냐 모든 것을 이저버리고 오즉 生을 사랑할 뿐이다.」
무섭은 獨斷的 偏見이올시다. 그러나 나는 是非를 論察할 餘暇도 업시 어대까지 든지 이것을, 支持하고 肯定하랴 하얏습니다. 自己를 辯護하기에는 便利하기 때문이올시다.
그뿐만 아니라, 新聞雜誌나 所謂 講演會라는 대에서, 貞操를 論하느니, 女學生의 不品行이니 風敎紊亂이니 하며 自己네들의 한 層 더한 醜惡은 선반에 놉즉히 언저두고, 되지 안흔 幼穉한 論法으로, 喧喧囂囂히 떠드는 것을 볼 때마다, 목에서 치바치는 反抗的 冷罵와 憤怒를 禁치 못하얏습니다.
「貞操? 그것은 무엇을 意味하느냐? 아마, 요사이 너희들의 주먼이가 말랏는게로구나. 정녕 妓生집에서 푸대접이나 바닷지? 離婚費를 내여주겟다는, 얼빠진 게집애도 하나 걸니지 못한 게로구나? 흥, 貞操? 네 똥에서는 무슨 냄새가 나든? 네 눈섭에는, 몬지 하나도 아니 부텃다는 自信이 잇거던, 마음대로 떠들념으나. 그러케도 所謂 女子의 貞操가 貪이 나느냐? 朝鮮社會에는 不貞한 女子가 만하서, 亂棒軍이 만흔게로구나!
그러나 貞操란 무엇이냐? 男子가 女子에게 生活保障을 條件으로 하고 强要하는 所謂 慾의 滿足이냐. 그러치 안으면, 所謂 敎養잇다는 者가, 高尙한 趣味性을 滿足시키랴는 慾求냐? 그처럼 獨占이 所願이거던 한 女性의 입에 퍼붓는 돈으로, 女佛像이나 만들어 노코 치어다 보고 안젓거나, 花盆個나 사다가 노핫스면 簡單하고 盜賊마즐 念慮도 업지 안흐냐. 奇妙한 趣味도 만타.
그러면, 女子가 판과粉갑으로 支拂하는 去來上 一形式이냐? 장사처 노코는 넘우도 非人格的이요, 넘우도 醜惡하지 안흐냐. 그러면 自己가, 貞淑한 守節家라는 讚辭와 프라이드를 스스로 享樂하랴는 亦是 一種의＜50＞ 奇妙한 趣味인가. 넘우도 人間性을 虐待하는 無智한 行爲다.
그러나 女子가, 『男子는 物質로, 女子의 貞操를 要求하니, 나는 貞操를 流動資本으로 삼아 快樂을 貿易하겟다.』거나, 또는 『物質과 貞操의 交換價値는 懸殊하니, 男子에게 貞操觀念이 업슬 때에, 나는 그 剩餘價値로 快樂을 購入하야 平衡을 어드랴 한다.』고 하면, 男子는 무엇이라고 對答하랴는가. 同一한 商業上 原則일진대 一에는 適應하고, 一에는 不合理할 까닭이 업슬 것이다.
더구나 누가 貞操를 지키지 안는다 하는가. A와의 情交가 繼續할 때에는, A에게 對하야 貞操잇는 情婦가 될 것이요, B와 夫婦關係가 持續할 동안은, 또한 B에게 對하야 貞淑한 妻만되면 고만이 아니냐. A에게 對하야 벌서 何等의 愛着을 感치 안흐면서, A와 夫婦關係를 持續하는 것이야말로, 돌이어 姦淫이다. 그 境遇에, A에게는, 아즉 나에게 對한 愛情이 잇다 하드래도, 그것은 別問題다. 或 謝意는 表할지 모르나, 結合狀態를 持續할 必要와 義務는 나에게 업다. 一層 嚴密히 말하자면, 一方이 사랑하랴는 慾求가 强烈할 지경이면, 그 相對者의 態度如何에 不拘하고, 自己만은 貞操를 지킬지 모른다. 하지만 그것은 自己自身에 對한 義務거나, 道德性의 要求에 對하야 滿足을 준다는 意味밧게 아니 된다. 그러나 그것은 自發的이다. 强制바든 奴隷道德에서는 求할 수 업는 것이다. 하여간 貞操는 商品은 아니다. 趣味도 아니다. 自由意思에 一任할 個性의 發露인 美德이다.
「女人을 보고 色情을 懷하는 者는 벌서 姦淫한 者라한 크리스트의 말이 至言이다. 最高의 道德的 標準에서는, 肉의 問題가 아니라, 精神의 問題이다. 戀愛自軆로 보면, 靈이나 肉이나 同格이지만, 貞操라는 見地로서는, 肉은 第二義 或은 附帶條件일 것이다. 하고 보면 姦淫을 하지 안핫다 할 者가 누구냐? 結局은 50步 100步가 아닌가. 그러면 이 問題를 어떠케 할 가. 잇서도 行치 못할 制度나 道德은, 업는 이만 갓지 못하다. 確實히 害毒이다. 그러나 크리스트의 敎訓이 眞理가 아닌 것은 아니다. 그러면 어떠케 實行과 符合시킬 수가 잇슬가.
容易한 일이다. 크리스트는 消極的 敎訓밧게 베플지 안핫다. 그러나 勿爲主義는 現代에 通用될 것은 못된다.＜51＞ 하지 말라는 데에는 退嬰과 失敗밧게 업지만, 하야라! 이것을 하야라!고, 最善하고 可能한 者를 指定하야 주면 아모 弊端이 업슬 것이다. 함으로 女人을 보고, 色情-이라면, 좀 醜惡하게 들니지만, 戀情이라는 意味일 것이다.-卽 戀情이 일어날 때에, 雙方이 合意만 하면, 慾求대로 肯定함이 可하지 안흐냐.
積極的으로 突進하는 거긔에만, 眞正한 生命의 發露가 잇는 것이다. 크리스트는 조흔 敎訓을 베프럿다. 그러나 그 結論이 틀리기 때문에 完璧은 못 되엇다.」
이가티 아모리 巧言美句를 늘어 노흘지라도, 辯明은 結局 辯明이요, 남는 것은 儼然한 事實뿐이외다. 재(灰)로 싸흔 塔만한 價値도 업습니다. 果然 이러한 辯明은 層階의 中턱에서 彷徨하는 者에게 주는 집팽이 밧게 못됩니다. 그러나 나의 이러한 思想이, 그 全部가 틀렷다고는 못하겟지요. 하지만, 正當한 主張일지라도 나의 思想과 行爲사이에는 懸隔한 距離가 잇섯다는 것이, 무엇보다 墮落을 証明하지 안습니까. 理論으로는 아모 말이라도 할 수 잇습니다. 「貞操는 商品이 아니다. 趣味도 아니다. 崇高하고 純一淨化한 感情에서 나오는 愛의 自由롭은 表現이라」고 하얏습니다. 그러나 나는 獸慾을 滿足시키기에만 汲汲지 안핫습니까. 肉慾이 아니라 獸慾이올시다. 「貞操를 누가 아니 지킨다느냐? E氏도 망단하야 하는 모양이나 勿論 速히 떠난다 합니다. 船便關係도 잇고 準備라야 별로 업는데, 空然히 머뭇거리면서, 머리쌀 압흔 꼴만 보고 잇슬 必要가 업다고 합니다. 생각하면 그도 그러하지만, 그때의 事情으로 말슴하면 已爲 내듸딘 발을 돌니킬 수도 업는 것이요, 婚期는 臨迫하야 오는데 다시 집으로 머리를 숙이고 드러 간다는 것은, 到底히 아니 될 일이엿나이다.
그리하야, 거의 二晝夜나 둘이 숙은거리며, 그야말로 鳩首相議한 結果, 結局 나는 日本으로나 건너가서 당분간 숨엇다가, 形便을 보아 가지고 追後로 따로히 떠나갈 計劃을 세우는 수 밧게 업다고 意見이 一致되엇습니다.
그러고 보면, 나는 爲先 別府로 가서 E氏가 準備하야 가지고, 오기를 기다릴 作定으로, 그날밤에 오르랴 하얏습니다. 집안에서 누가 쫏지나 안을가 하는 念慮가 잇기 때문에 急히 서두른 것입니다. 그러나 行具를 꾸리다가 문득 생각하니 亦是 旅行券이 잇서야 玄海灘을 넘어슬 수가 잇습니다. 잡바저도 코가 깨진다는 格으로, 인제는 옴칠 수도 업고 내칠 수도 업는, 그야말로 독안에 든 쥐올시다. E氏도 깔깔 웃고 안젓슬 뿐입니다. 그러나 이러케 된 以上에는, 따로따로 떠러저서, 一旦 서울로 回程을 하얏다가, 旅行券을 엇어 가지고 다시 나스는 수 밧게 업다고 決心하고, 그날은 또 하로 묵게 되엇습니다.
그 翌朝에 E氏는 上京하얏습니다.
