**Body Text Translation:**
Farmers of Chosŏn, Unite! Intelligentsia, Reflect! Propertied Class, Confess Your Sins!
						Yi Sŏnghwan

In last month's issue of this magazine, I first proposed that we liberate the farmers first. So, how shall we liberate them? Now, I change the topic and proceed with a concrete discussion. As previously stated, farmers should occupy a proper position and be granted proper treatment, yet until today they have wandered in adversity and endured contradictions. This is truly the path that has led our farming villages to decline and the sign that is causing our Chosŏn to perish. Therefore, sitting here today, if we wish to make the nation of Chosŏn prosper and restore Chosŏn's farming villages, we must first find the root of the evils that created the current situation and eradicate it (拔本); we must chase the cause of the harmful effects that produced this shameful spectacle and block the source (塞源). Thinking about it, the root and the cause lie nowhere else. It is precisely the fact that the farmers, the pillars of the nation and the protagonists of the farming villages, lost their freedom from the beginning and fell into all sorts of defects that is the root cause of the decline and the reason for ruin. Therefore, while we cannot overlook from a humanitarian perspective the fact that the farming villages themselves are not receiving human treatment, moreover, for the sake of restoring our farming villages and for the glory of our national destiny, we must liberate the farmers' current situation from adversity into a world of freedom, and we must grant them all the rights that were usurped. To do so, the facts that need liberation and the conditions that need granting must be clarified. At the very least, they must be liberated from all current unfair and irrational facts, and rational and equal rights must be granted. Thus, although it surely will not end with just one or two conditions, what I demand here is primarily to strive for the implementation of the following five points first. However, if I were to elaborate on each of these five conditions, each item would also become a lengthy article in itself. But since the pages of this magazine are limited, in this main text, I will only touch upon the general outline. First,

Grant human rights to farmers. In recent days, in our society too, the so-called "human rights protection" issue seems to be rising daily,<53> stimulating and cultivating public indignation among people incidentally. The talk of "human rights violations" regarding the Niigata Prefecture Incident (新潟縣事件), the Kohŭng Incident (高興事件), or the issue of local police brutality against the people seems to have recently spurred deep concern among intellectuals and aroused public opinion, and journalists in the press world (操弧界) report this as good material, while speakers on the lecture circuit preach it as a novel topic. I ask you, gentlemen journalists, gentlemen speakers! If your eye for selecting material is at the level of social sentiment, if your net for capturing discussion points is at the focal point of social sentiment, then you would not view the farmer abuse problem, upon which the life and death of 14 million farmers depend, as less important than the Niigata Prefecture massacre incident, nor would you think the farmer annihilation problem, upon which the survival of 2.7 million households depends, as less important than the Kohŭng shooting incident. However, I have seldom heard public opinion boiling over the "human rights issue" upon seeing the lives of 14 million farmers fallen into a tragically extreme abyss, nor have I often witnessed public attention being drawn with indignation to the "humanitarian issue" upon seeing the livelihood of 2.7 million households hanging on a heart-wrenchingly harsh cliff. Of course, the motives of those incidents and the nature of this fact differ in appearance. To discuss them comparatively side-by-side is quite awkward and likely involves many stretches. However, returning to sit with the essence of the human rights issue and the humanitarian issue, how could their significance not only be the same? One cannot help but realize that the difference between the triviality of the former and the gravity of the latter is truly as vast as heaven and earth. That was the misery of individuals; this is the misery of 14 million people.

Despite this, even journalists active within the actual institutions of public opinion rarely consider farmers as their subject. How is this not news indicating the disregard for the farmers' dignity? Even speakers on the podium who claim to beautify society with a spirit of universal love fail to take the side of the farmers. How is this not a failure to care for the farmers' human rights? Alas! Farmers are relentlessly robbed, trampled, and oppressed under the power of those with money and education, yet they have no means to convey their sorrowful circumstances and resentful situations, no place to appeal. This cannot continue. The more this contradiction persists, the more our society deteriorates to that extent, and our nation falls further into ruin. Therefore, disregarding the human rights of farmers is a matter of shame for our entire nation. Accordingly, the humanitarian issue in our society must begin with respecting the dignity of farmers. I implore you, writers! I hope,<54> speakers! And I earnestly appeal to every person in general society. "Expand the beautiful public indignation you expressed for the misery of that one person a bit further, and under the banner of humanitarianism, pay a higher price in sacrifice and hold genuine concern for this 'human rights violation' issue of the farmers, who are practically the entirety of our nation." ... And thus, quickly find and restore to the farmers the heaven-sent human rights of equality for all. This is the crucial path to restoring the lives of our Chosŏn people. Second,

Grant land to farmers. There are no farmers apart from the land, and no land apart from the farmers. When speaking of cultivated land, one cannot help but think of the cultivator, and when speaking of the cultivator, one cannot help but think of the cultivated land. Accordingly, land only gains value when it awaits farmers who have a deep affinity with it and possess the will and ability to cultivate it directly. Farmers only earn the title of "farmer" when they possess enough land to produce the materials to sustain and guarantee their lives. Therefore, it is a natural fact that land itself should always belong to those who cultivate it, and it is also a solemn fact that cultivators make the land their lifeblood. If cultivated land is taken away from the farmer, it is the same as taking away that person's life. If the farmer's freedom regarding the land is restricted, it is the same as trampling upon that person's existence. Is that all? If the farmer's attachment to the land diminishes even by a hair's breadth, that land will immediately become desolate, and eventually, the production of all raw materials essential for human survival will cease. Consequently, society will become tastelessly dry and wither without vitality. From this perspective, land—past, present, and into the eternal future—should belong to the cultivator who has attachment and affinity for the land and possesses the will and ability to cultivate it directly. Yet, what is the current situation of Chosŏn farmers? Chosŏn farmers possess the qualifications of cultivators. They have attachment and affinity for the land, as well as the will and ability to cultivate. However, they lack the corresponding cultivated land. Almost everything is under the control of the exploiting class (불쪼아계급 - likely meaning 'those who squeeze/exploit'), and society is plundering what our farmers own. And beyond that, it is not even granting what should be granted. Thus, the land, which is the farmers' lifeline, only serves to satisfy the animalistic desires (獸慾) of a portion of landlords who merely eat, merely dress, and merely play without cultivating, and it is merely being driven into the giant hands of various Japanese entities (companies and individuals) who seem poised to expel the entire Chosŏn people from the land of Chosŏn <55> and take their place. Ah—what a terrifying thing this is! Farmers who work hard cultivating have no land, while conversely, those who do not know how to plow or produce, and furthermore, do not touch a single clod of earth with their fingertips, possess land. They have freedom. They have authority. In such a state, it is utterly impossible to expect social stability or maintain national unity. Let alone that, even the self-governance within a single village cannot be guaranteed, so how could one dream of social peace? Therefore, I cry out. "To build a truly peaceful society and firmly unite a sound nation, one must first allow the individuality of each person forming that society and nation to flourish. For individuality to flourish, each person must be given their heaven-sent occupation to build a secure foundation. That is, in our Chosŏn, grant farmers enough cultivated land for each individual to eat, dress, and live, thereby ensuring the stability of their livelihoods. Without doing so, the beautiful phrase 'social peace' will not escape being a kind of delusion, and the meaningful encouragement 'national happiness' will also be nothing more than mere talk. Everything in our society hinges on whether land is given to farmers or not. Quickly grant the freedom of cultivable land to the pitiful farmers who are about to become wanderers because they have none. This must be done from a humanitarian perspective, and also for the peace of society and the prosperity of the nation." However, given that the concentration of land ownership has reached its extreme as it is today, if land is to be granted to farmers, what method should be used? In my opinion, there are only two approaches. One is an active land policy, and the other is a passive land policy. Within the active land policy, there are again two arguments: the direct import of the Labor-Farmer Russian style (勞農露西亞式 直輸入論) and Land Nationalization (土地國有論). Within the passive land policy, there are also two theories: Landlord Denial (地主否認論) and Owner-Farmer Creation (自作農創定說). However, I do not intend to judge whether the former is feasible and the latter is not, nor do I intend to debate the pros and cons of their ideals. Regardless of the method, as long as land is given to the farmers, that is sufficient. But here, one must consider the possibility or impossibility of implementation. In the current situation where Chosŏn society is dominated by imperialistic, and moreover, capitalistic politics, it is difficult to guarantee the feasibility of implementing the former, active measures. (Though it may seem my adherence to ideals is too weak,<56> I wish to avoid unnecessary conflicts as much as possible.) Therefore, regrettably, adopting the latter, passive policy seems to be one way. That is, while preventing landlords from accumulating any more land than they currently possess, landlord-owned land should be liberated and granted to small farmers, utilizing a method of installment repayment (年賦償却) over 20 or perhaps 30 years. (Naturally, starting with the Oriental Development Company [東拓]). Thus, by eliminating large landlords and tenant farmers, and ensuring the gap between rich and poor is not severe, all farmers should be allowed to enjoy shared prosperity. Only then will the flower of peace bloom in our Chosŏn society, and the smile of happiness appear for our Chosŏn nation. Give it! Give it quickly! Give the fields to those who cultivate the fields!! Third,

Grant economic rights to farmers. Our farmers are producers. The raw materials for delicious foodstuffs and light, warm clothing are produced by farmers. Besides these, the raw materials for everything else are produced by farmers.
Our farmers are taxpayers! Land tax, land tax surcharges, village fees, school fees, and other construction, repair, and maintenance costs are borne by farmers. Corvée labor for the establishment, repair, weeding, and tree-planting along first, second, and third-class roads; construction of government offices; bridge building; water management; afforestation of state-owned forests—all such labor is done by farmers. Thus, farmers sustain society. Is that right? Farmers support officials, landlords, religious figures, educators, merchants, industrialists, and intellectuals. Furthermore, they even support Japanese people and Chinese people. By producing, supplying, paying taxes, and performing corvée labor, they strive to fill the bellies and fatten the bodies of others. Haven't they fulfilled all the duties required for the state and society? No. How could it end with just duties? They are dignified sacrificers and contributors. If so, isn't it inevitable that corresponding rights should exist on the other side? Even a foot of coarse cloth or a cup of weak wine cannot be exchanged without payment. How much more so when they have exerted themselves to fulfill duties, shedding precious blood and expending heavy flesh? How could there not be rights equivalent to that compensation? Yet, do Chosŏn farmers have rights? No, they do not. Especially from the standpoint of producers, they lack the right to economic independence. Listen to the desperate cries of sorrow from the 14 million farmers. Their unanimous lament is, "Trying to make a living farming like this, we'll starve to death!" The harvest yields, the crystallization of their efforts gained while breaking their backs <57> and sweating profusely, working tirelessly (營營役役)—once these are plundered from one side and seized from the other, not only is a family's income insufficient for its livelihood, but there is no way to save elderly parents groaning on their sickbeds, nor the strength to support young wives and children suffering from hunger. Moreover, the torment of the debt demon that follows a hand-to-mouth existence grows more severe day by day, leading them to wish that their families would perish quickly, if only to escape the momentary suffering... This can be said to be the plea welling up from their naive circumstances. "Ah—it's painful! How can I escape this crisis? How can I distance myself from this anguish?" they fretfully jump about and fussily thrash (藻搔히 덤빈다). Yet, the more anxious they become, the deeper they sink into the abyss of suffering, and the more they fuss, the more violently the volcano of anguish erupts. Consequently, the weakest people in the world are the farmers, and the most powerless people in the world are also none other than the farmers. As this happens, the rice produced by squeezing out precious blood and sweat must be collectively offered up to city dwellers and landlords, leaving the farmers themselves in a situation where even barley porridge, bean rice, and plain boiled water are often unavailable. And the silk fabrics woven after staying up nights chopping mulberry leaves and reeling silk threads must also be offered for the bodily luxury of city dwellers and the landlord-capitalist class, leaving the farmers themselves unable to afford even dirt-stained coarse cotton clothes. The position of the dignified producer is buried in such contradictions; what rights could possibly exist there? Since there are no rights, there is neither the individual human taste for life nor the enjoyment of family life. Ah—pitiful is the farmers' situation! They produce, yet they cannot achieve economic independence. And when selling these products, the agricultural goods harvested through the farmers' diligent toil (營營刻苦), to the cities or distributing them to landlords, do they gain freedom regarding the price and rate determination? Is fairness ensured? Not so. Although agricultural products are clearly the farmers' property, being the crystallization of their hard labor, when determining the price and deciding the distribution, they are silently dominated by the merchant class and helplessly swayed by landlords. This is just the lack of freedom when farmers supply goods; what about when they demand goods? They are also totally controlled by them. From necessary agricultural tools to daily necessities, be it a needle, thread, or anything else, is there anything for which the farmer class <58> determines the price and obtains it? They merely submit to the prices set freely by the others' will. Because of this, the price of agricultural products is the cheapest compared to anything else. Even when prices rise, the market rate for farm goods is customarily the last to go up compared to anything else, and when prices fall, their market rate is also customarily the first to go down compared to anything else. Thus, those who absorb the sweet essence (甘味眞汁) in the middle are always the merchants and industrialists, the propertied, and the intellectuals. And those left licking the dregs (糟粕) are this unfortunate farmer class. No, these pitiful producers. Gentlemen—this cannot stand. Such an unfair system cannot be tolerated even for a moment. If the world could remain peacefully governed even while tolerating it, that would be fine; if everyone could prosper together, it would also not be an issue. But if that is not the case, and the impact of such social contradictions and defects robs the majority of producers of their right to self-reliance and hastens their path to ruin, alas, what state will society reach? It is a clear certainty that the nation of Chosŏn and the people of Chosŏn will perish together. What else could this be but a major issue that cannot be overlooked! Therefore, I cry out, "Grant economic rights to farmers!" Today's era recognizes the "right to the full product of labor" (勞働全收權). And this ideal is being wildly welcomed by people in the West and the East. If, amidst this, only the producers of Chosŏn lag behind this ideal and cannot possess economic rights, this is not only an unbearable absurdity but also a sin unforgivable by universal human justice. Seen from this point too, economic rights must be quickly granted to Chosŏn farmers. To achieve this, the abolition of conventional systems and the establishment of rational systems are necessary. At the very least, the equal rights common to all people must be granted to farmers. Thus, the intentions they express and the assertions they advocate must be treated as sacred, recognizing their right to economic self-reliance and improving their lives. Fourth,

Grant political rights to farmers. Nervous politicians (政客), do not misunderstand. When I say grant political rights to farmers, you might worry, thinking, "Aren't they going to start another Manse demonstration or something?" However, the political rights mentioned here do not mean making farmers politicians, political activists, or influential participants in Japanese politics. At the very least, if politics is an institution organized by the will of the people, and if politics refers to the policies and measures for improving and governing well the entire life of the people, then its every aspect,<59> its every advance and retreat, will affect and spread to the lives of the general populace. Thus, the lives of our farmers and politics have a close relationship and significant correlation. Therefore, shouldn't farmers possess the freedom to interfere in, discuss, criticize, supervise, and reprimand (鞭撻) politics, not just in the sense of managing their own livelihoods? So, do Chosŏn farmers have such rights? No, they do not.

After the end of the Great European War, times changed, systems renewed, and the notion that "only the wealthy are wise, only the wealthy are intelligent, only the wealthy are strong, only the wealthy hold power, and the long-standing grasp of political power by money-is-everything continued, leading to the extreme development of capitalism, and politics protecting capitalism dominated the entire society"—that has been overturned. The emerging trend of the era opposing this ultimately aims not only to deny that only the wealthy class should monopolize power but also to overturn the traditional grasp on power. This is the inclination of current public sentiment and, accordingly, the best truth, needing no further explanation (贅言). The burgeoning power of labor parties heard everywhere, the movement for universal suffrage, the prevalence of parliamentary equality—all convey news from that front. While others are like this, do our Chosŏn farmers have such rights? No. Ordinary farmers possess neither this right nor that right, but conversely, landlords and capitalists, intoxicated and deluded by capitalism, organize so-called Landlord Associations (地主會). Colluding (互相氣脈을 통하야) with officials living purposelessly (醉生夢死) under autocratic rule and the so-called Agricultural Associations (農會), they fervently engage in political interference, discussion, criticism, supervision, and reprimand to satisfy their selfish desires and beastly hearts (獸心). But what do the Landlord Associations and Agricultural Associations advocate? What political interference do they engage in? They say, "Movements like the tenant farmer movement in Chinju (晋州) are a kind of reckless action; allowing new ideals into the current Chosŏn farming villages is unacceptable. The infiltration or propagation of labor ideology and economic consciousness into farming villages must be prevented. The ideological enlightenment of tenant farmers for tenant movements must be absolutely opposed," they resolve. Consequently, politicians who are allies of landlords and wealthy farmers eventually nod in agreement (唯唯諾諾) and keep step with them. Accordingly, local <60> county magistrates vigorously explain that the landlords' claims are correct, and police chiefs summon and interrogate tenant farmers, claiming they are wrong. Ah—is this not a cursed thing, is this not a frightening thing? The Landlord Associations that obstruct the ideological enlightenment of the vast populace of over ten million, and the politicians who cooperate with them; the propertied class (有産者) that opposes the development of consciousness for living as human beings, and the government officials (官吏輩) who approve of it—these are truly apostles of dangerous thought who seek to abuse farmers forever through ignorance and exploit them like slaves without conditions. Such tyranny and such autocracy only worsen society and offer no benefit. We cannot but rebuke you (汝等) from a position of fairness. Since we all share common responsibility and common duty for the nation and society, how can the propertied class alone monopolize the benefits of political life? Considering even a single village (洞里), if village affairs are not decided by the will of the farmers who are its residents, the orderliness of those affairs cannot be expected. Similarly, for a township (面), a county (郡), a province (道), and all affairs of Chosŏn, policies and measures must be taken representing the will of the majority populace. Yet, looking at the current state of Chosŏn today, what kind of people are the village and hamlet chiefs (洞里區長)? And what kind of people are the township heads (面長)? Furthermore, look at education policy! What kind of people are the school council members (學校 評議員)? They are all influential and wealthy individuals caught in the top meshes of the net called the wealth survey (資本調査) classification. Thus, all policies are grasped solely in the hands of the propertied class, and the propertyless, poor farmers are manipulated by them in various ways. They possess money, power, status, and rights, but the general farmers lack these. Consequently, the degree of their arrogance reaches the ceiling, and the attitude of their haughtiness fills all directions. The fact that they misuse the old master-servant style dictum "The people can be made to follow, but not made to understand" (民可使由之 不可使知之) and blindly treat farmers with contempt and look down upon them is witnessed everywhere in Chosŏn society today. Look—can we live like this? Gentlemen? This cannot stand. The longer this situation continues—be it one day or one year—the more only unfortunate examples will spread throughout our society, and only dangerous factions will be conceived. Therefore, at this juncture, for the sake of justice of equality for all, for the sake of the truth of common happiness, grant political rights to the general farmers. Thus, politics is not the private property of any one individual, but the shared foundation of life for the general populace. Therefore, the general populace must bear common responsibility and duty, and decisions regarding benefits and losses must be based on the calculations of the general populace's will. To achieve this, first give this farmer class, the majority of the populace, the opportunity for political life. Let's implement all state affairs (萬機) according to the people's will! As the first step towards this, recognize Farmers' Associations (農民會) with an adversarial character to the Landlord Associations, give them opportunities for political life such as interference, discussion, criticism, supervision, guidance, and reprimand, and ensure that the people's will advocated therein becomes active as politics. Otherwise,<61> there is no hope for the farmers' status to ever improve, and the soundness of society can never be achieved. Fifth,

Grant cultural facilities to farmers. Regardless of the society or nation, the point at which that group flourishes or perishes depends on the fulfillment of the individuals forming that group. And that fulfillment must again be universal and pervasive (滲透的). For example, if there is a human body here, it is understandable even if we analyze that person physiologically. That is, to know if that person is healthy or disabled, one must first look into the four limbs, five senses, five viscera, and six bowels that form that person. When each of these parts is complete without the slightest defect, that individual is called healthy. If not, even if other parts are all perfect, the presence of even one small deficient part means that the individual cannot escape a pathological condition. Similarly, the more such defective parts there are, and the fewer complete parts there are, the more critical the individual's life becomes. All collective life in our human society is also no different from an organism; this principle cannot be ignored, nor can this law be violated. Thinking from this perspective, the entity called Chosŏn, the entity called the Chosŏn nation, just as an organic individual is formed with five viscera, six bowels, four limbs, and five senses, geographically has mountains, water, cities, and farming villages. There are also parks and cultivated lands. Looking at the larger picture, there are farmers, merchants, industrialists, officials, and laborers. Thus, although the fields of occupation in which the constituent members of the group are active may differ, and although the locations where they reside may have distinctions between city and village, whether the group is sound or not depends on the soundness of each individual living in Chosŏn. Therefore, whether Chosŏn becomes a new-era Chosŏn or not, whether Chosŏn becomes a cultural Chosŏn or not, all depends on whether each individual forming the entity called Chosŏn becomes a new-era person or not, and whether each individual leads a cultural life or not. That is, whether the benefits of culture, the conveniences of civilization, and other things necessary for human existence and useful for social progress are equally distributed (均霑) between cities and farming villages determines whether the nation's survival and the nation's rise or fall become heavier or lighter in value, and whether their color becomes richer or paler. Therefore, no matter how splendid Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), P'yŏngyang, Taegu, or Pusan may be, no matter how magnificent they are, having exhausted the extremes of beauty and culture,<62> on the day the farming villages of 28,014 hamlets (洞里) perish, Chosŏn society will consequently be destroyed.

This is something we are currently suspecting right before our eyes. Look! Cities are equipped with water supply, telegraph, telephone, parks, theaters, schools, kindergartens, public halls, churches, libraries, amusement facilities, hospitals, railways, trams, automobiles, and other sanitary facilities and transportation/communication infrastructure. The brilliance of electric lights and the roar of gasoline engines truly boast of cultural life. However, this is far removed from the farming villages, and for farmers, all such facilities are merely pies in the sky (畵中의 餠). This cannot stand. How is this different from painting one's face while the internal organs are rotting away? It is undeniably pathological. Farmers too have a desire for entertainment, a passion for education; they know sanitation is good, and they know convenient transportation is good. Consciousness as human beings is fully developed. As fellow humans, how can farmers live only in a world of darkness? Under the meaningful ideal of "Civilization Advancing Together" (倂進文明), we must quickly grant cultural facilities to farmers. (Regarding the construction of rural culture, I will present it in a separate article in the next or subsequent issue, so I conclude this point here.)

Essential Points of Liberation and Concluding Remarks

Even a single fishing net has a lead line ("벨"이 - likely '벼리', the main rope), and even one piece of clothing has a collar ("깃"). How much more so for the national and ethnic issue of the farmer liberation movement? How can success be expected without essential points? Merely inciting reckless conflict cannot achieve success, nor will the problem be solved by meekly begging. It is also not something that can be done by just a few concerned individuals, nor can success be expected solely from the simple farmers themselves. The entire society must collectively adopt organized plans and practical methods. That is, farmer liberation requires essential points of liberation. For a caged bird, opening the cage once settles the matter; for cattle or sheep confined in a pen, opening the gate once also settles the matter. But the liberation of our farmer class is not so simple. The path to liberation is strewn horizontally and vertically with thorns and axes (荊棘과, 斧鉞). Therefore, until the light floods the farming villages, we must pass through hundreds of hardships and tribulations. But what do the essential points of farmer liberation signify? Do they signify the great fire of revolution as occurred in France (佛蘭西) or was carried out in Russia (露西亞)? No. Even if such a sudden great change could be brought about, that is a separate issue. Sitting in the Chosŏn of today, to liberate the Chosŏn farmers of today, I primarily await the implementation of the following three points. Listing them in order:

1. Farmers Must Unite
Our farmers are weak. As individuals, they are too powerless to act. In this cold society that ignores the cries of individuals, especially the cries of the weak,<63> no matter how much the passion for public justice boils over. Therefore, the only path for us, the weak, is unity. In this situation where the farmers' status cannot be raised without confronting the strong, we need the "strength" to contend with that of the strong (强*). For that, there is nothing other than unity. The fact that the working class gained its current position by confronting the capitalist class was achieved through the "strength" of unity, even if their individual power was weak. However, unity for the sake of exercising violent force cannot but be rejected. Our farmers must, by all means, organize a rational union of us producers, following the great path of freedom of thought and public interest of society, loving truth and praising public justice, in order to strive for liberation. They must confront the tyrannical powerful and cry out to society for the improvement of their status.

2. The Penal Duty (罪役) of the Propertied Class
The propertied class, especially landlords, are offenders against general working farmers, guilty of acts unforgivable by universal human justice. Know that the fertile fields, grand houses, and fine clothes and food you currently possess are all proceeds of crime against the farmers. Therefore, as your penal duty (罪役): First, dispose of land using the convenient method of low-priced annual installment sales. Second, build public halls in each township (面) to train and cultivate the farmers' political life and social knowledge. Third, establish scholarship societies (育英會) and donate funds. Provide these funds for the school expenses of children from poor farmer families who have talent but lack money, thereby nurturing individuals useful to society. Fourth, manage producers' cooperatives, consumers' cooperatives, etc. Fifth, reduce tenant rents. At the very least, these five conditions of penal duty must be willingly undertaken. What is mentioned above mainly concerns the material aspect; besides that, mentally, the past violent and shameful behaviors must be abandoned.

3. Reflection of the Intelligentsia (有識階級)
The intelligentsia (有識階級) must also repent for their past indifference towards the general farmers. They must realize how crucial the farmer liberation issue is for Chosŏn society and the Chosŏn nation, reflect that their complacency until today was a mistake, shed tears of sympathy for the farmers' sorrow, and strive in the liberation movement. The conditions for farmer liberation I demanded above, the achievement of farmer unity, and the penal duty of landlords—all these must be advocated and driven forward in practice by the intelligentsia through public opinion.

Farmers of Chosŏn, unite! Propertied class of Chosŏn, understand! Intellectuals (識者) of Chosŏn, reflect! And thus, create a world of public justice! Here, for the sake of human love, I offer both sacrifice and fervent love with both hands and strive for the liberation of the impoverished farmers. (End) <64>

**Summary**

This impassioned 1923 editorial by Yi Sŏnghwan argues that the liberation of Chosŏn's farmers is paramount for the nation's survival and prosperity. He details the farmers' plight—lacking human rights, land, economic autonomy, political voice, and cultural access—despite being the producers and backbone of society. Yi calls for five key grants: human rights, land (suggesting gradual reform over revolution), economic rights (fair prices, right to the product of labor), political rights (participation, formation of farmers' associations), and cultural facilities. He urges farmers to unite, demands the propertied class (especially landlords) atone for their "crimes" through specific actions like land redistribution and funding social programs, and calls upon the intelligentsia to repent their indifference and lead the liberation movement. The article provides a stark portrayal of class conflict and social injustice in colonial Korea, advocating for systemic change grounded in humanitarianism and national well-being. Historians would find the specific demands (land reform methods, types of organizations), the direct address and condemnation of different social classes (farmers, landlords, intelligentsia), and the framing of the issue within both humanitarian and nationalistic contexts particularly insightful for understanding social movements, class consciousness, and intellectual discourse in the 1920s.

나는 本誌 前月號에 잇서 먼저 농민부터 해방하자는 제의를 하엿다. 그러면 엇더케 해방할가, 이제 題를 改하야 구체적 논의를 進하리라. 전술한 바와 가티 농민은 상당한 지위를 차지하고 상당한 대우를 부여하여야 可하거늘 至于今逆境에서 방황하며 모순에서 堪忍하야 왓다. 이것이 실로 우리 농촌을 쇠퇴한 前程이며 우리 조선으로 하여금 멸망케 하는 조짐이다. 그러기에 금일에 안자서 조선나라를 융성하게 하며 조선농촌을 회복하고자 할진대 먼저 현상을 致成한 그 폐해의 근본을 차저 拔本하지 아니면 안되겟스며, 추태를 연출한 그 해독의 원인을 쪼차 塞源하지 안코는 안되겟다. 이애 생각하야 보건대 그 근본과, 원인은 다른데 잇지 아니하다. 나라의 동량이며, 농촌의 주인공인 농민이 당초부터 자유를 이러바리고 모든 결함 속에 빠저 잇게 한 그것이 곳 쇠퇴케 한 근본이오, 멸망케 하는 원인이다. 그럼으로 우리는 농촌 자신이 인류의 대우를 못 밧고 잇는 것을 人道上으로 觀過할 수 업는 동시에 더욱 우리 농촌의 회복을 위하야 민족운명의 영광을 위하야써 농민의 현상을 역경에서 자유로운 세상으로 해방하여야 되겟스며 박탈하얏든 모든 권리를 그네들에게 부여하여야 되겟다. 그러자면 해방하여야 될 사실과, 부여하여야 될 조건이 천명하여야만 될 것이다. 적어도 현재의 모든 불공평한 것과, 불합리한 사실로부터 해방하야 합리적이오, 평등적의 권리를 부여하지 아니면 안되갯다. 그리하야 그것이 결코 一二의 조건에 끄치지 아니하겟스나 그러나 내가 이에서 요구하는 바는 爲先 左記의 다섯 가지부터 그 실행을 期하고자 함이다. 그런데 이 다섯가지 조건에 대하야 일일히 演義하고자 하면 그 各條가 亦 각편의 녊�문이 안될 수업다. 그러나 본지의 지면은 한정이 잇슴으로 본론에서는 그 境槪만 따서 말하고자 한다. 첫재에
농민에게 인권을 부여하라. 근일에 이르러 우리 사회에도 소위 「인권옹호」이라는 문제가 날로＜53＞ 놉하가면서 擦過적으로 세인의 公憤心을 자극하며, 배양하는 듯하다. 新潟縣事件이라던지, 高興事件이라던지, 또는 지방경관의 인민구타문제 등에 대한 「인권유린」의 소리는 近近 識者의 深憂를 촉진하며 사회의 여론을 환기하야 操弧界의 기자들은 이것을 好個의 재료로서 보도하고 잇스며, 강론계의 연사들은 그것을 奇拔의 문제로서 講道하고 잇다. 뭇노라 기자 제군이여, 연사 제군이여 제군의 材料選擇眼이 사회 인심의 水準線에 잇다 하면, 제군의 論意捕促網이 사회 인심의 歸趨點에 잇다 하면, 1,400만 농민의 殺活이 달리운 농민학대문제는 新潟縣虐死事件만 못하게 보지 아니할 것이오, 270만호의 존망이 달리운 농민멸망문제는 高興銃殺事件만 못하게 생각치 아니할 것이다. 그러나 나는 아즉 1,400만 농민의 생명이 悲極慘絶한 深淵에 빠진 것을 보고 「인권문제」로서 사회적 여론을 沸騰하난 것을 耳聞한 일이 적으며, 270만호의 生道가 酸鼻酷烈한 절벽에 달리웟스되 그것을 보고 「人道問題」로서 公憤的 注目을 촉진하는 것을 목도한 바 또한 드므도다. 彼事件의 동기와, 此사실의 성질이 피차 間樣色을 달리할 것은 물론이다. 이것을 相對同列하야 立論함은 자못 불편어 심하며, 무리가 多하다고는 하리라. 그러나 한번 돌아가서 인권문제과, 인도문제의 자체에 안자서 생각할 때에 어찌 그 意義만 동일할 뿐이랴. 彼輕此重의 差는 실로 天壤의 別이 生함을 깨닷지 아니할 수 업다. 彼는 일개인 일개인의 비참이어니와, 이는 1,400만인의 비참이다.
이러하거늘 실지로 사회적 여론기관 하에서 활동하는 기자들도 농민을 對手로 생각함이 적다. 이 어찌 농민의 인격을 무시하는 개중의 소식이 아니며, 汎愛的 정신으로 사회를 미화한다는 강단상의 연사들도 농민의 편은 되지를 못한다. 이 어찌 농민의 인권은 돌보아주지 아님이 아니리오. 오호라! 농민들을 돈이 잇고, 글이 잇는 그네들의 권세 하에서 여지 업시 빼앗기며, 또 밟피우며, 눌이우되 그 설은 정황과, 원통한 사정을 고할 바 편이 업고, 訴할 바 곳이 업다. 이리고는 안된다. 이 모순은 계속되면 될스록 그 계속한 이만치 우리 사회는 加一度로 악화되며, 우리 민족은 層一層으로 滅落된다. 그럼으로 농민의 인권을 무시함은 우리민족 전체의 치욕문제이다. 따라 우리 사회의 인도문제는 농민의 인격존중으로부터 비롯할 것이다. 囑하노라, 글쟁이들이여. 望하노라＜54＞, 말쟁이들이여. 또 일반사회의 사람사람에게 향하야 열망한다. 「저 一人의 비참을 위하야 發하든 그 아름다운 公憤心을 좀더 확장하야 인도적 기치 하에서 우리 민족의 전반이나 다름이 업는 이 농민의 「인권유린」 문제에 대하야 보담 더의 高價잇는 희생을 拂하며, 진실스러운 우려를 가지라」고... 그리하야 속히 농민의게 만인평등의 천부적 인권을 차자주라. 이것이 우리 조선사람의 생명을 회복하는 중대한 途程이니라. 둘재는
농민에게 토지를 부여하라. 토지를 떠나서 농민이 업고, 농민을 떠나서 토지가 업다. 경작지를 말할 때에 경작자를, 경작자를 말할 때에 경작지를 연상하지 아니할 수 업다. 따라 토지는 그와 친분을 절실히 하고, 직접으로 경작할 의지와, 능력을 구비한 농민을 기다리여서 처음으로 토지의 가치가 生하는 법이오, 농민은 자기의 생명의 유지보장할 재료를 생산함에 족할 만한 토지를 가지고야 비롯오 농민이라는 칭호를 엇는 것이다. 그럼으로 토지 그것은 언제까지든지 경작하는 자의 소유가 될 것이 당연한 사실이오, 경작자는 토지로써 생명을 삼음이 역시 엄숙한 사실이다. 若是 농민으로부터 경작토지를 탈취한다 하면 그는 그 사람으로부터 생명을 박탈함과 한가지오, 또 농민의 토지에 대한 자유를 구속한다 하면 그는 그 사람의 존재를 유린함과 맛찬가지다. 그 뿐이랴. 농민으로부터 毫末만치라도 토지와의 애착심이 감소된다 하면, 그 토지는 당장에 황폐되야 드듸여 인류생존상에 필요불가결할 원료의 모든 생산은 그만 두절될 것이니 이에 사회는 맛업시 乾燥하며, 潤업시 쇠잔할 것이다. 이 점으로써 토지는 과거나, 현재나 또 永劫의 장래에 이르기까지 토지에 대한 애착심과, 친분을 가지고, 직접 경작할 의지와, 능력을 구비한 경작자에게 소유되여야 될 일이다. 그런데 우리 조선농민의 현상은 어떠한가? 解放의 要領과 끗말

一帶의 魚網에도 「벨」이가 잇고, 一件의 의복에도 「깃」이 잇다. 하물며 국가문제며 민족문제인, 농민해방운동에 대하야 그 요령이 업시 어찌 그 성공을 기할 수 잇스리오, 턱업시 충돌이나 야기하는 것을 성공을 奏할 수도 업스며, 柔順하게 애걸이나, 하는 것으로 문제가 해결될 것도 아니다. 또 일부의 우려자들 뿐으로도 될 일이 아니며, 단순한 농민들만으로도 되기를 期할 수 업다. 全 사회가 合擧하야 반듯이 조직적 계획과, 실현적 방법을 취하여야 되겟다. 즉 농민해방에는 해방의 요령이 필요하다. 저 籠 중에 가두엇든 飛鳥 가트면 開籠一度에 事ㅡ畢할 것이오, 목장 속에 갓치웟든 牛羊 가트면 開門一次에 事亦畢할 것이지마는 우리 농민계급의 해방은 그러케 단순하지 아니하다. 해방의 前路에는 荊棘과, 斧鉞이 종횡하야 잇다. 그럼으로 농촌에 광명이 漲溢할 때까지는 幾百回의 천신만고를 통과하지 안코는 안되겟다. 그런데 농민해방의 요령은 그 무엇을 의미함인가? 佛蘭西에서 하든 바와, 露西亞에서 행하든 혁명의 巨火를 의미함인가? 아니다. 그러한 돌연적 大變을 致할 수 잇다 하드라도 그것은 別問題로 한다. 나는 현재의 조선에 안저서 현재의 조선농민을 해방함에는 爲先 左記의 세 가지 실행에 기다리고자 한다. 이제 차례로 摘擧하면,

1. 農民은 團結할 일
우리 농민은 약하다. 개인개인으로는 일하기에 넘우나 무능하다. 개인으로의 절규, 더욱 약자로서의＜63＞ 절규는 암만 公義의 열이 끌어넘처도 그것을 돌아보지 아니하는 냉정한 사회이다. 이에 우리 약자로서의 취할 바 방도는 오직 단결이 잇슬 뿐이다. 강자에게 대항하지 아니하고는 농민의 지위를 인상할 수 업는 이 자리에서 强*의 그것에 대항하야 어들 만한 「힘」이 잇서야 되겟다. 거긔에는 단결을 두고는 다시 아무 것도 업다. 저 노동자계급이 자본계급과 대항하야 금일의 지위를 획득한 것은 비록 약한 힘이라도 多數히 단결한 「힘」으로써 그러하엿다. 그럿타고 폭력주의를 발휘하기 위하야의 단결은 부인치 아닐 수 업다. 우리 농민은 어데까지든지 사상의 자유와, 사회공익의 大道에 쪼차 진리를 愛하며, 公道를 讚하는 해방을 期하기 위하야 우리 생산자들로 합리적 단결을 조직하야 橫暴한 有力者에게 대항하며, 사회를 향하야 지위의 향상을 절규하여야 되겟다.

2. 有産者의 罪役
有産者는 더욱 지주는 一般力農民에케 대하야 人間公道上에 容許할 수 업는 罪責者이다. 현재 제군이 가지고 잇는 沃土良田이며, 高臺廣室이며, 錦衣玉食이 모도 다 농민에게 대하야는 범죄소득인 줄 아러라. 그리하야 그 罪役으로는 第一에 토지를 불하하되, 低價年賦賣却의 편법으로 할 일이오, 第二에는 各面에 公會堂을 건축하야 농민의 정치생활과, 사회지식을 鍜鍊, 涵養케 할 일이오, 第三에는 育英會를 설치하야 기금을 기부하라. 그리하야 소농민의 자제로서 천재가 잇스되 금전이 업서서 취학 못하는 자의 학비에 제공하야 사회유용의 인물을 양성할 일이오, 第四는 利用組合消費組合 등을 경영할 일이오, 第五는 소작료를 경감할 일이다. 적어도. 이 五個 조건에 대한 罪役은 호의로 담당하여야 되겟다. 우에 말한 것은 주로 물질적 방면이어니와, 그 밧게 정신적으로 종래의 폭행 추태는 포기하지 아니면 안되겟다.

3. 有産階級의 反省
有産階級이 역시 일반농민에게 대하야는 종래의 냉정하얏든 일을 悔過하지 아니면 안되겟스며 또한 농민해방문제가 조선사회나, 조선민족에게 취하야 얼마나 중대한 문제인 줄을 깨달아서 금일까지에 恬然한 것이 착오이엿던 것을 반성하야 농민의 이 비애에 동정의 눈물을 뿌리면셔 해방운동에 분투하지 아니면 안되겟다. 내가 우에서 요구한 농민해방의 조건이라든지 농민의 단결의 성취며, 지주의 罪役과 가튼 것은 모다 有識階級에서 그 여론을 唱導하며, 실행 鞭撻하여야 되겟다.

조선의 농민이여 단결하라! 조선의 有産者여 양해하라. 조선의 識者여 반성하라. 그리하야 公義的 世界를 창조하라. 나는 이에 인류애를 위하야 희생과 열애를 쌍수로 捧하고 궁핍한 농민의 해방을 期하고자 한다. (完)＜64＞
