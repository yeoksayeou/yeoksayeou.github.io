기사제목	修業料低減決議에 對한 批判 (Critique on the Resolution to Reduce Tuition Fees)
필자	金松隱
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Argumentative Essay)

修業料低減決議에 對한 批判
						金松隱

Recently, several leading associations in the city joined together to discuss the reduction of tuition fees for each middle school and resolved to negotiate with the authorities of each school to implement this.

Under our current economic circumstances, comparing living expenses with the tuition fees of each middle school easily reveals that the latter are exceptionally high. Furthermore, the mental anguish of students who must squeeze out these high tuition fees from their meager living expenses, and the living difficulties of the parents who indirectly bear this burden, are readily apparent.

Moreover, following the burgeoning zeal for learning in recent years, the number of non-productive students has drastically increased, and as the number of students grows, this poor economic sphere will only exacerbate the situation. As this economic sphere becomes poorer, providing the education that accompanies the zeal for learning will become impossible. Therefore, finding some way to adjust these high tuition fees with living expenses to encourage and promote the zeal for learning, which is a vital artery for our society's development, is one of the most pressing issues at present. Thus, it is a natural and unavoidable fact that the tuition fee issue is being discussed in this context, and while we sincerely welcome its purpose, we also feel the need for deep, reflective inquiry into its methods.

It is a clear fact that high tuition fees have a great impact on the studies of the students who directly bear the burden and on the lives of the parents who indirectly bear it. When the tuition collection period approaches, students not only fail to sincerely engage in classroom lectures but also in reviewing at home, wasting time in futile distress and worry. That is, students feel greater fear and pain towards the smaller amount and less stringent collection of tuition fees compared to the larger amount and harsher demands of boarding house fees. This is because being suspended from school until payment is made for failing to pay on time is met with greater shame and trepidation than being denied meals or expelled from a boarding house. Therefore, when we realize that the composed demeanor and lifeless conversations of general students during the tuition collection period are not the result of deep inquiry born from calm reflection on their studies, but merely the result of fantasies arising from mental suffering, we can gauge how <31> greatly tuition fees affect students' studies.

Furthermore, when parents in the countryside receive demands and pleas for school expenses from their children, out of parental affection and hope for their children's future, they disregard their own living situations, sometimes selling rice at low prices or borrowing money at high interest from here and there. Thus, aside from the mental anguish and suffering, debts accumulate year after year, and poverty gradually increases its force following the daily decline in grain prices. As the scholar's expenditures become increasingly difficult, we must first adjust living expenses and tuition fees to rescue students from mental suffering and parents from impoverished living.

Then, what measures should be taken to implement this? If we inquire into this, the final points of convergence are inevitably two strategies: one, reducing tuition fees to adjust them to living expenses, and two, increasing living expenses to adjust them to tuition fees. The result of the discussion by the aforementioned eight associations was also to choose the former of these two as the best measure and implement it. However, according to our observation, the latter seems to be the prerequisite issue. Not only that, but when we discover that implementing the former is impossible without awaiting the resolution of the latter, we cannot help but raise questions about a resolution that ignores the resolution of the latter, or else imagines that implementing the former will somehow resolve the latter as well. Here, I intend to state my humble opinion and critique that resolution.

However, before turning my pen to the main issue, I wish to say a word about the essential value of the subjectivity involved in judging matters. That is, no matter how objectively and rationally a truth or conclusion is reached, when criticized from another subjective viewpoint, biases stemming from self-interest and errors and contradictions arising from one's environment are discovered. This means that however impartially and thoroughly a person judges matters, as long as the preconception of defending one's own interests and position remains latent, that conclusion is also merely like a lyric poem. Therefore, the Indian "Truth-Hold" (眞理把持), which promises to grant freedom, equality, and liberation to a world full of injustice, oppression, and evil, is considered by the British merely a flawed theory that ignores the societal principle of the strong preying on the weak. Similarly, our promotion of domestic products, a measure to maintain even our impoverished livelihood, is seen by foreigners as an empty theory ignoring economic division of labor. Just as a temperature of 10 degrees below zero Celsius feels extremely cold to a poor person but comfortably warm to a natural ice merchant, it stems from the biases and errors of judging based on the preconception of defending one's own interests and position.

Since I am also one of the school authorities, I cannot <32> assert that there will be no bias or error aimed at maintaining my interests and position when critiquing this issue. However, that my critique is not entirely like this is something I firmly believe without doubt when I reflect upon my critique again and again, convinced that it is not merely an arbitrary assertion based on temporary emotion seeking my own benefit.

Now, to briefly state the content of the resolution— "Contrary to the gradual impoverishment of our current economy, the tuition fees of various middle schools are disproportionately high. Also, in the current situation where prices are falling daily and attempts are being made to adjust prices, it is not impossible to economize on school expenses, which are non-productive costs. Therefore, school authorities should also follow the general trend, reduce school expenses, allocate the reduced amount to tuition income, and thereby lower tuition fees. As for the implementation method, fees of 3 won or more should be reduced by 1 won, and fees of 2 won or more by 50 chŏn." In short, its points can be simply classified as: One, tuition fees are high relative to living expenses; Two, prices have fallen; Three, expenses should be reduced in line with the general trend; and Four, the implementation method is to lower them by 1 won or 50 chŏn.

One, it is true that tuition fees are high relative to living expenses. However, when we speak of living expenses here, we do not mean those of the ordinary poor, but rather those of the parents who pay the tuition fees. That is, the parents of students in reasonably substantial middle schools—not new types of schools or cram schools (강습소) largely attended by self-supporting students—are members of the middle or upper property-owning class. As long as the strict rule exists that only one or two hundred students are selected from several thousand applicants each year, and the ability to pay school expenses is considered a primary entrance requirement, admission is utterly impossible for those not from the property-owning class. And even if they manage to enter by some means, they likely cannot continue for more than a few months. The rightness or wrongness of this is far removed from the main subject of discussion on this issue, so it will be omitted in this essay, but in any case, it is a clear fact that current middle schools are the exclusive domain of the property-owning class.

Therefore, according to our investigation, current middle school parents are those requiring living expenses of 70 won or more in the cities and a harvest of 70 sŏk (석, unit of grain volume) or more in rural areas. Those living below this level dare not even think of sending their children to middle school. The reason for this is not that tuition and boarding fees are excessive, but that their living expenses are excessively meager. Thus, when we speak of high rates here, we refer to these two groups as the standard.

However, even though they are middle class or above, because their basic standard of living is low, the fact that tuition constitutes more than one-tenth of their expenses cannot be compared to foreigners for whom it is less than one-thirtieth or one-fortieth. Among them, urban dwellers with easier access to finance do not suffer greatly, but for rural people who rely on the sale of rice from inconveniently located areas as their source of funds, it goes <33> without saying how high the current tuition fees are. This is precisely why grain prices are unreasonably falling. Some months ago, intending to discuss this issue, I investigated the details of one student's school funding situation. The student's parent is a farmer in a village, with an annual harvest of about 80 sŏk. However, they barely manage to cover the living expenses for their family of eight (excluding the student) and the student's school expenses. Let's glance at their income and expenditure:

That is, tuition is one-tenth of the family's living expenses and roughly equivalent to the clothing costs for the eight family members. Thus, how high this rate is cannot be overstated.

However, what we must inevitably mention is the fact that tuition is not the only thing with a high rate. Compared to foreigners, taxes are unreasonably high relative to the standard of living, of course. Additionally, non-profit items like newspapers, magazines, and books are extraordinarily expensive compared to living costs. Setting aside the annually increasing foreign currency inflow, there is nothing that isn't high-rate, including communication costs, train and steamship fares, and freight charges. Furthermore, it is an unavoidable fact that the craft goods faintly produced in recent years, due to incomplete machinery and unskilled techniques, are inferior in appearance and substance to those made by foreign hands, yet are excessively high-priced compared to living expenses.

Two, it is also a fact that prices have fallen. However, when we speak of prices here, we do not mean the price of foreign currency, but rather the price of grain, our sole product. That is, due to the aftermath of the Great War, the amount of rice exported increased drastically, and consequently, finance became fluid, expanding the purchasing power of the general populace. Now that the war has ended, the amount of rice exported is gradually decreasing, and the once vibrant <34> economic sphere is stagnant and depressed. In contrast, the once-expanded desire for purchasing knows no restraint. Also, handicraft industries, which provided some assistance to living expenses before the Great War, have vanished, and the import of foreign goods increases daily. This is the current situation. Therefore, it is a natural trend that the price of grain, whose export volume is decreasing, falls, while the price of foreign goods, whose imports are drastically increasing, remains unchanged.

The fall in grain prices is a clear fact. Comparing the grain price of February last year with today's shows about a 30% drop. That is, a farmer who had living expenses equivalent to a 70 sŏk harvest last year will live on a 49 sŏk harvest this year. One with a harvest around 20 sŏk will subsist for a year on 14 sŏk. If such a farmer has debts of 20-30 won, they will live on around 10 sŏk after deducting 3-4 sŏk. Following the hardship of their lives, debts will gradually increase, and in contrast, with no prospect of grain prices rising, eventually there will be no other way but to sell their land. Furthermore, the miserable state of lower-class farmers due to the fall in grain prices needs no further repetition from us.

However, if our daily necessities were produced in our factories, they would fall at the same rate along with the fall in grain prices. But under the current circumstances, almost everything is nothing but foreign goods. Therefore, unless the prices in the countries of origin producing these goods fall, they will not fall accordingly, no matter how much grain prices drop. Also, the price of foreign goods will never decrease unless the wages of the laborers who determine it and the living expenses of the general populace decrease. Of course, it is true that recently, foreign countries are also advocating for reduced living expenses and discussing price adjustments. Particularly in Japan, which supplies a large amount of our daily goods, price adjustment is being vigorously debated, and politicians are also striving towards this. Detailed statistics reportedly show a 10-20% decrease compared to before. Accordingly, prices in Chosŏn (조선 - Korea) have also fallen somewhat, and among them, some items have dropped by more than 20%, but such items have little significant relation to our lives. The prices of truly indispensable, important goods remain unchanged.

Furthermore, there is even concern that the prices of goods necessary for school management will rise further. The price of timber from the Forestry Bureau (營林廠) remains the same; coal imported from Manchuria (滿洲) is gradually rising; the wages of Chinese stonemasons (中國人石手) and Japanese carpenters (日人木手) have not dropped one bit; let alone a decrease, the prices of science experiment equipment, chemical reagents, botanical and zoological specimens, physiological models, wall charts (괘도), chalk, Western paper (洋紙), etc., have instead surged by 20-30% compared to before. What can be done? Therefore, when we discuss falling prices, we should not make judgments based on mere hearsay <35>; we must not forget that only the price of rice, from which we obtain the financial resources to purchase our general daily goods, has fallen.

Three, can school expenses be reduced? No, under the current circumstances, far from being reduced, they must gradually increase. Of course, after the Great War, various nations planned fiscal consolidation, economized on consumption, adjusted prices, carried out major reductions starting from state expenditures, and banks, shops, and companies drastically cut operating costs. This is a fact, and its implementation is the first step towards economic stability. However, there is a fact that must not be forgotten here: even if expenses are reduced, not all detailed items are necessarily reduced. Even in expenditures where military expansion costs are cut, social work management costs increase. Even if miscellaneous expenses and travel allowances are economized, advertising costs for products gradually increase. Therefore, it is natural for us too to economize on general expenses, and we should widely publicize this and thoroughly implement it. However, the fact that it is impossible to reduce even school expenses is proven by reality.

If school authorities, fully aware of the general poverty caused by the economic stagnation and fall in grain prices, had the ability to maintain the schools even after reducing current expenses, would they harbor the malicious intent to extract excessive expenses from this difficult situation? But facts cannot be concealed by rhetoric and emotion. Are school authorities truly capable of reducing current expenses but deliberately not doing so? What about the fact that 70-80 students are crammed into classrooms less than 20 p'yŏng (평, unit of area), bustling about? How long must we continue the contradiction where someone specializing in medicine teaches Chinese classics, someone who studied commerce teaches physiology, someone graduated from theology teaches mathematics, and someone majoring in agriculture lectures on history and geography? Can it be considered complete instruction when, lacking experimental equipment, teachers use finger gestures, causing distress for students who cannot understand and taxing the brains of teachers trying to make them understand?

We must fully develop the innate talents and skills of middle school students. At a time when we lament the lack of personnel, no matter what, if we aim for the development of our society, shouldn't we provide appropriate training? Then, let me ask: among the current middle schools, how many meet this objective? Narrowing the scope, how many schools provide qualitative instruction comparable to Japanese middle schools? Despite spending the same amount of time with the same textbooks, why is there a world of difference between the two? Is it because our middle school students' talents are dull and their intellects inferior? No. They are neither inferior nor dull-witted. It is simply that they are unable to receive the education that would bring out their innate talents and skills. This inability is due to incomplete facilities and teachers, <36> and the primary reason for this incompleteness stems from a lack of funds. Yet, it is a fact that Japanese middle schools, which are more substantial in content and facilities than ours, are increasing their current funding to gradually pursue practical completeness. Therefore, our middle schools, which cannot even dare to compare, should henceforth focus on practical training, and to do so, funding must inevitably be increased.

Furthermore, with the promulgation of the new education decree, higher common schools (고등보통학교) are treated equally with Japanese middle schools. This will, of course, provide great convenience for students planning to study in Japan, and it signifies how current middle schools have strived for substantial content improvement over the years. However, when we realize that equal treatment and equal qualification are merely slogans in regulations, while in terms of actual substance, the two are far apart, shouldn't we be further spurred on, recognizing that our time to strive has come? Let me ask again: have our middle schools achieved substantial content improvement such that their graduates can enjoy the same qualifications as Japanese middle school graduates? How many schools have facilities complete enough that reducing current funding would have no particular impact on school management?

This is what I lament endlessly. Although we may be poor, if we hope for the development of this society, even if not realized all at once, we must, as quickly as possible, upgrade the facilities and content of current middle schools so that there is no qualitative difference from those of Japanese middle schools. Exercise grounds and equipment to train students' bodies are necessary, of course. While constructing spacious classrooms immediately might be difficult, classrooms sufficient to avoid hardship in studies must exist. Physics and chemistry experiment equipment to foster scientific interest and verify questions and theories are indispensable. At a time when teachers with sufficient research in their subjects are needed, teachers who view middle school teaching merely as a business must be eliminated.

Therefore, there is no room for further debate that the current funding of middle schools is extremely small. Also, even if large financial resources cannot be obtained at once, funding must be increased by whatever means possible, and school authorities are also agonizing over this, arranging fundraising drives. Moreover, the inability to accommodate more than one-twentieth or one-thirtieth of the applicants each year, despite their having sufficient funds and skills, preventing them from obtaining the opportunity to study, is not due to the ignorance or heartlessness of school authorities, but simply due to the lack of school funds. Therefore, discussing the reduction of funding at the present time is nothing but foolish talk.

Four, there is no need for further investigation into the issue of reducing fees by 1 won or 50 chŏn, because I have already <37> explained in the preceding section that reducing expenses is absolutely impossible. Therefore, discussing the 1 won, 50 chŏn issue further might seem logically contradictory. However, to make my critique more thorough, I wish to assume a reduction of 1 won or 50 chŏn and discuss what the result would be. First, we must examine the financial situation of current middle schools. Here is a summary of the annual income and expenditure of six of the most prominent private middle schools:

Since this statistic is for the current year's calculation, if each school recruits an average of 180 students next year, the number of classes will increase by 18. Even if we assume an increase of 2 teachers per class, that would be 36 teachers. Calculating their salaries at an average of 130 won, the total required would be over 56,000 won. Since the current classrooms have no space left with this year's students, besides the graduating class classrooms, 2 more classrooms would need to be built. Setting aside this major issue, what about the shortage in teacher salaries? In any case, recruiting fewer than 180 students would go against the general trend. Consequently, establishing 3 new classes would require an increase of about 9,700 won per school. <38> Even after subtracting the tuition income from the new students, an increase of over 4,000 won would inevitably be necessary.

However, adding insult to injury (雪上加霜), if student tuition fees are reduced by 1 won each, cutting about 42,600 won annually, about 7,000 won per school would be reduced from current expenses. Thus, next year, a total deficit of about 11,000 won per school would somehow have to be covered by increased funds. But do current middle schools have financial resources capable of easily disbursing such large sums? Schools relying on land as their financial source face reduced current income due to falling grain prices, and church schools maintained by subsidies from foreign mission funds show a trend of gradual reduction in those subsidies.

Furthermore, starting next year, current higher common schools will be upgraded according to the new education decree, raising the issue of teacher qualifications. The small number of qualified teachers will demand high salaries, causing school expenses to face increasing deficits. Therefore, unless all current higher common schools are changed into cram schools (강습소) or miscellaneous schools (잡종학교), if proper middle school education is to be provided, current funding must be increased as rapidly as possible.

Of course, if middle school teachers, saying their salaries are high compared to the living expenses of the general populace, were to teach only for their actual living costs, school expenses would see a large reduction. But such a miraculous, decisive action is unlikely to happen suddenly. Therefore, isn't discussing tuition reduction, without first enriching our living expenses, nothing but an empty theory?

Above, I seem to have repeatedly critiqued the resolution. For us, who still lack statisticians, discussing such issues is extremely difficult, so I cannot assert that there are no errors above. However, what I tirelessly emphasize is that the passive measure of reducing tuition fees to adjust to living expenses is impossible under the current circumstances. Of course, I too know full well that increasing living expenses under the current economic hardship will not be easy. However, one cannot put the cart before the horse (本末을 前倒하다). A reduction of 1 won or 50 chŏn, not based on an increase in living expenses, is merely a stopgap measure like 'peeing on one's frozen feet' (凍足放尿之策). Therefore, we must resolve the fundamental rural problems and strive to raise grain prices to increase the income of general farmers. This then leads to the conclusion of rural relief measures. This is something I intend to discuss further later under a different title. Other topics, such as the zeal for learning and economic issues, or cram schools and self-supporting students, will also be deferred to a later opportunity, and I will now lay down my pen on this small, clumsy essay. **Summary**

The author, Kim Songŭn, argues against a proposal by civic groups to lower middle school tuition fees in 1923 Korea. While acknowledging the heavy burden of high tuition on students and families struggling with poverty (exacerbated by falling grain prices), he contends that lowering fees is impractical and counterproductive. He states that schools are already severely underfunded, lacking basic facilities and qualified teachers compared to Japanese counterparts, and actually require *increased* funding to improve educational quality, especially with new educational standards being implemented. He believes the root problem is the low standard of living and depressed agricultural economy; therefore, the priority should be improving the general economic situation (increasing living standards and grain prices) so families *can* afford tuition, rather than lowering fees, which he dismisses as an empty gesture or a harmful stopgap measure ('peeing on frozen feet') that ignores the need for better school resources. From a historical perspective, this article vividly illustrates the economic hardships faced by Koreans under colonial rule, the perceived inadequacy of Korean educational institutions compared to Japanese ones, the social stratification determining access to education (limited largely to the propertied class), and the complex debates surrounding practical solutions to societal problems within the constraints of the colonial economy. The detailed budget breakdown (though the figures are missing) and the discussion of grain prices versus imported goods prices offer valuable insights into the colonial economic structure and its impact on daily life and education.
