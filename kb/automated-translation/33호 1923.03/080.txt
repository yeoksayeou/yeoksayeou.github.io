Publication Date: 1923년 03월 01일
Article Title: 朝鮮敎育界와 敎員資格問題 (The Korean Education World and the Teacher Qualification Problem)
Author: 朴達成
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial)

朝鮮敎育界와 敎員資格問題
						朴達成
As a third party who is neither an educator nor a student, an outsider, I feel it is perhaps too presumptuous to dare speak about educational problems. Just as the reader may look upon this with skeptical eyes, I myself confess that I lack confidence. However, in present-day Korea (朝鮮), this problem is more significant than any other, and especially since March marks the end of the academic term and this issue [the teacher qualification problem] will be implemented from April, I cannot refrain from discussing this matter from various perspectives, however much I wish to remain silent. Thus, the topic was raised at the editorial meeting, and I volunteered to write about it.

Having decided to do so, I could not avoid sufficient consideration of the field, nor could I fail to listen to the opinions of those directly involved in educational administration (敎育當路者). Therefore, I spent several days visiting several high school principals (高等學校長), who are directly involved, to hear all their opinions. So, please understand that what is written (書) here is not the author's arbitrary judgment (自意自斷) but, in fact, the opinions of those in charge of education.

The teacher qualification issue to be implemented from this April! To what extent will its influence reach in the Korean education world? Will Japanese teachers gain the upper hand (勢), or will Korean teachers gain the upper hand? Or, in this turmoil, will Koreans completely decline, and Japanese gain complete dominance (勝勢)? Consequently, what will be the position of educators, and how will the sentiments (感念) of students be aroused (振作)? And how much will the economic issues (經濟問題) of the education world be affected? There is no lack of such doubts (疑念). It is from this perspective that we decided to discuss this further. However, some held the opinion that silence might still be preferable. In response to this,

Principal A stated:
"This is indeed a major issue. One cannot<22> possibly speak on it with just casual consideration or opinion. I too have pondered it in various ways, but I haven't yet arrived at a sufficient answer. In the first place, even before the teacher qualification issue arose, the number (其數) of regular teachers (普通敎員) was insufficient. Furthermore, if qualifications are assessed and only qualified individuals are appointed to the position (其任), then there will truly be a shortage of such people (其人). In any case, one cannot speak lightly (輕輕) without sufficient investigation and thorough observation (周到한 관찰)." Thus, he trailed off, postponing further comment (後期). Also,

Principal B stated:
"This is indeed a significant issue, certainly a significant issue. However, looking at it from another perspective, it seems there might not be such a great impact this year. Newly established schools like Chinju High Common School (晉州高普) might face considerable difficulty obtaining qualified teachers, but currently, within the various high schools in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), there seems unlikely to be major changes. In assessing teacher qualifications, graduates from higher normal schools (高等師範) are naturally included (母論), and additionally, graduates from applied chemistry departments (應用化學科), ceramics departments (窯業科), physiology specialists (生理科專門), or those from certain private universities (私立大學) who have received (受한) teacher certification (敎員檢定) are all considered qualified. Also, anyone who has taught a subject full-time (專擔) at one school for over five years is recognized as a qualified teacher regardless of the subject, so there might not be significant changes. However, since there is originally a lack of personnel in Korea's education field, the shortage of people is a fact. But, since there should be several individuals in each school who have taught full-time (專任敎授) for over five years, perhaps it might be manageable. Even in our school, there are about 10 people who could be recognized as qualified. Including Japanese, there are 6 graduates from higher normal schools, one graduate from a higher technical school (高工), and 3 or 4 experienced individuals. In any case, for any school to be deemed adequate by the authorities (當局), more than two-thirds of the teachers must be qualified, so it is indeed a difficult matter (難事).
Are you asking about the influence of the Japanese? That too probably won't be a major trend. Originally, Japan itself currently lacks over 2,000 qualified teachers, so they cannot flood into Korea like that. But it is an issue. The fundamental policy (方針) of the Japanese Ministry of Education (文部省) is this: Young teachers (靑年敎員) with appropriate qualifications, such as graduates from higher normal schools or specialized schools (專門出身) in Japan, are absolutely not sent abroad (外地). That is, they are sent to their place of origin (出身地), the respective prefecture (該府該縣), or designated locations elsewhere, ensuring they teach only within mainland Japan (內地). The reason (理由) for this is the realization (覺破) that newly graduated young educators, with their vibrant spirit (意氣 潑潑), strong drive for educational activities (活動力), and often self-sacrificing attitude (犧牲的 氣分) towards their work, are the sole means (唯一의 方便) to replenish (補) the nation's vitality (國民의 元氣).<23> Furthermore, since they are recent graduates, the aim is to pay them low salaries (俸給) and make them work hard. The maximum salary (多捧) for a first-time teacher in Japan is 100 yen. However, the teachers sent to foreign territories (外地) like Korea or Taiwan (臺灣) are those who have been used up in Japan, selected from those nearing pension (恩給). Consequently, due to their experience (履歷上) and status (待遇上), they must be given high salaries (高捧). It becomes impossible not to pay at least 200 yen, up to 300 or 400 yen. Therefore, economic difficulties in the Korean education world will become even more severe (尤甚). However, we have no grounds whatsoever to criticize (非難) the authorities or harbor resentment (憎視) towards the Japanese. In other words, elevating Korea's various high schools to be equivalent (同等) to Japan's middle schools (中學校) was something we demanded (要求), and once the schools were upgraded (昇格), it is a fact (事實) that they should be provided with appropriate facilities (施設) and qualified teachers for proper education. It goes without saying (毋論) that we should proactively select (擇할) qualified individuals even before the authorities mention teacher qualifications. In any case, the number of Japanese will likely increase somewhat. Consequently, the economy related to the education sector will become somewhat of an issue. However, I am confident that education will become comparatively more complete (完全)." Thus, he stated that there wouldn't be major upheavals and that this development is natural (當來之事) given the current circumstances. It is indeed a fact. There is no room for further argument (更論할 餘地). We can only lament (恨) our lack (乏) of qualified personnel and sigh (嘆) over our lack of money. Regarding this, Teacher C from a certain school said:
"Since the schools have been upgraded, the issue of teacher qualifications is a natural consequence of the situation (事勢當然). There's nothing to be done. From now on, schools have no choice but (밧게) to train teachers themselves. They must select the very best among their graduates, have them complete (맛추게) higher normal school or some specialized training (特殊한 專門), and then employ them to teach at that school. There is no other way (他道)."
Regarding this, it seems everyone would agree (萬人同口). The author also has no different opinion (別見). As Teacher C said, there is no other way but for schools to voluntarily train their own teachers. If they cannot do so, then indeed, they will have no choice but to leave it entirely (一任) to the Japanese.

Since the topic has come up (말이 낫스니), let me say that we indeed have few qualified teachers. I am not saying this with any envious attitude (羡望的 態度) as if higher normal schools are so lofty (놉다), but it is said that there are barely (겨오) ten or so Korean graduates from higher normal schools. And while there may be tens or hundreds (幾十人 幾百人) of graduates from various universities and specialized schools (大學 專門), most are said to have only the title (名稱) and lack real ability (實力). Even if they lack ability, it's said they are often just selling their positions for a living (賣職的 糊口的), and lack the suitability (適任) to genuinely manifest the spirit of education based on its principles (敎育의 原理에 立하야 眞正하게).<24> This is the reality (事實) of present-day (現下) Korea. How many (만슴닛가) Koreans study abroad each year in Japan, or Europe and America (歐美), and China (中國)? Every year, 700-800 to over 1,000 people cross over (渡하지만은) to Japan alone, but what they learn, whether from universities or specialized schools, is usually law (法律) or politics (政治). And this is not obtained through diligent effort (努力) over a considerable period (相當한 年限), but often just by spending several hundred or thousand won (멧 百 멧 千원) to buy a graduation certificate (卒業證). There is nothing to look askance at (무엇이 눈거러 볼 것이 업슴니다).
Speaking about this while discussing teacher qualifications sounds like a refutation asking why they couldn't obtain teacher qualifications, but in any case, it often contrasts greatly with actual ability. Since this is a separate issue, let's leave it for now. As we are discussing the Korean education world and the problem of education, we have no choice but (밧게) to reflect deeply (深思) on it.
The problem of education is said to be among the major issues (大問題 中) in Japan as well presently. The fundamental direction (根本方針) of current education greatly conflicts (大히 配置되는 점이 多함니다) with what we (吾人) desire (要求하난 바). What is the purpose (目的) of education? Is it not to make 'people' (『사람』)? Isn't education received (밧는 것이 안임닛가) to fulfill the role of a 'person'? A person is not merely a person by birth, not merely by body (肉體), not merely by knowledge (知識); are they not a complete person only after both body and spirit, internal and external aspects (精神 內外 兩面), are fully developed (十分히 發達)? Therefore, isn't the purpose of providing (施하는) education and the requirement (要件) for receiving education to enable one to acquire the ability to live well during their life (生活) in the world (世間)? Isn't the initial purpose of education to live like a human being with the ability to live well, regardless of the method (方法), path, or work one chooses? It is not intended for individuals to be sold here and there like commodities (商品 模樣), to be well-utilized or not depending on the highness or lowness of their price (價格의 高下), is it (안닌 것 안임닛가)? But what is modern (現代) education like?
Aren't schools today turned into commodity factories (商品製造所), educators into technicians (技師) or craftsmen (工匠), and students (被敎育者) considering themselves commodities (商品으로 自處하게 된 것이)? Thus, isn't modern education focused on: for schools, how to make their company's goods (會社物品) sell well? For educators, how to teach so students will be advantageous in the path (途) of employment (需用)? For students, how to learn so they can be sold (팔녀 갈고) at a high price (高價)?<25>
Schools are definitely not (決코) commodity factories. They are 'people' factories (『사람』 製造所). Educators are definitely not commodity manufacturers. They are 'people' makers (『사람』製作者). Students are definitely not beings to be sold around (팔녀단니는 者) like commodities. The goal is to learn about being a 'person' (『사람』을 배우고), acquire (엇어서) personhood, and live like a person. Living like a person means living while fully expressing all the ideals one desires as a person. What are the ideals (以上) of a person? It is to achieve a life where both spirit and body (精神, 肉體) are satisfied. There are various occupations here. There are occupations like farming (農), industry (工), or commerce (商), and there might also be occupations like religion, academics, or arts. Occupation is a matter of freedom (自由). Also, there is no high or low, noble or base. And everything is sacred and free.
Educators! We (吾人) definitely do not demand educators who act like commodity manufacturers. Students! We definitely do not demand those who go around being sold like commodities. Yet, why do current educators and students try to engage in commercial (商事的) behavior? Why do educators fear (恐懼) that the person they educated might not sell well, strive to make them sell well, and ultimately, if they cannot be sold, lament (騷首長嘆), "Ah, my reputation would improve if they were sold, why aren't they selling?" Why do those who received education desperately desire (極力으로 渴望) to be sold, strive and arrange (周旋) for it, and if ultimately unsuccessful, despair, saying, "Agh, I graduated in vain (虛卒業햇다). I just wasted my money (公演한 돈만 업시 햇다)," and fall into the pit (窟) of despair (落心千萬) and self-destruction (自沒)? Or else, complain, "Agh, this damn world doesn't recognize people! I'm a graduate of such-and-such school!" twisting their fist in frustration (不平의 주먹을 뷔틀다가), eventually ending up wandering aimlessly (浮浪의 方) or taking the path of self-destruction (自暴의 길)? Was that the original purpose (本來 目的) of wanting to receive education?
No. Definitely not. If one has learned about being a person, that is enough. It doesn't matter if one is sold or not. Things for a person to do exist in the home (家庭), in the farming village (農村), in the city (都會), in the mountains (山 中), and on the sea (海上). They exist everywhere under heaven and earth (天地到處에 다ㅡ잇슴니다). And weaving mats (집석이 삼는 것) is work, weaving straw bags (가마니 짜는 것) is work, wielding a hammer (맛치를 드는 것) is work, and carrying an A-frame carrier (지게지는 것) is work too. Work does not exist only in armchairs (安樂椅子), nor only under large signboards (大看板下). That's right. Definitely right. And human life (生活) is not found only in tall buildings and mansions (高樓巨閣), not only in Western suits and overcoats (洋服外套), and not only in rich delicacies (膏粱珍味). It exists in thatched huts (草幕), in simple cloth clothes (布衣), and in bowls of soybean dregs (비지사발). Only when the body is healthy and the spirit is content (快) is life fulfilling (圓滿). That does not happen solely through eating well and dressing well. No matter how one lives, if one learned well during their studies and lives like a person, even in simple clothes and rough food (布衣粗食), body and soul (身神) will both be complete (俱足).
Educators, students! Transcend (超越하시오) commodity-like education. Return (도라가시요) to person-centered education. Speaking now of the teacher qualification issue, we neither desire (원처 안코) higher normal schools nor envy (부럽지 안슴니다) universities or specialized schools. We welcome (還迎) any educator who possesses person-centered ability and practices person-centered education. And please move beyond (넘어서시요) the standpoint of knowledge-sellers and employers (賣識者雇用者의 입장) to the domain (境域) of those with duties and responsibilities (義務者責任者). Education is the great headquarters (大本營) for making people, aiming for mutual human existence based on human values (人類相互가 人類 本位로 살게 하자는).<26>

**Summary**

This 1923 editorial by Pak Talsŏng discusses the impending implementation of a new teacher qualification system in colonial Korea. The author expresses concern about the shortage of qualified Korean teachers and the potential dominance of Japanese educators, who might be older, less dynamic, and more expensive due to colonial posting policies. While acknowledging the need for qualified teachers following school upgrades, Pak criticizes the broader trend in modern education, both in Korea and Japan, which he sees as treating education as a factory producing commodities (students) for the job market, rather than fostering well-rounded individuals ("사람") capable of living meaningful lives regardless of occupation or social status. He urges a return to "person-centered" education. For historians, this article offers insight into the anxieties surrounding colonial education policies, the perceived disadvantages faced by Koreans in the professional sphere, the economic pressures within the colonial education system, and a contemporary Korean intellectual's critique of the utilitarian, market-driven approach to education versus a more humanistic ideal, all framed within the context of Japanese colonial rule.
