The Murder of Ch'oe Ch'angho and the Kando Compatriot Residents' General Meeting
						By One Reporter

On February 12th at 3 PM in Yongjŏng City, Kando, upon hearing the dreadful and atrocious news that our compatriot Ch'oe Ch'angho (25) was senselessly shot and killed by a Chinese soldier, we gnashed our teeth and trembled with rage. "Is the right to life of Koreans trampled upon by anyone like this? Without any reason?" we cried, clenching our righteous fists. However, still unable to be certain, while awaiting further information, the horrific report was indeed confirmed as a lengthy, five-page telegram arrived from our Kando compatriots. "Regarding the incident where our compatriot Ch'oe Ch'angho, aged 25, was shot and killed by a Chinese soldier without any reason, a citizens' rally was held and resolved the following:
1. To protest to the Chinese authorities.
1. That our 300,000 compatriots shall initiate a movement to renounce [Japanese] nationality, citing Japan's inadequate protection.
1. To hold a Kando Residents' General Meeting on the 27th of this month.
1. That all shops shall close for five days."
This was the gist of the telegram.

So, is it true that our compatriot Ch'oe Ch'angho was senselessly shot and killed by a Chinese soldier? It is true. Ch'oe Ch'angho was brutally murdered by the merciless poison bullet of that lawless Chinese soldier. In the marketplace, in broad daylight, he was senselessly struck by the poison bullet.
Compatriots, rise up! Compatriots, respond! Our brethren everywhere are suffering such violations of their right to exist. Lamenting is futile; weeping is useless. Let us rise up and resist; let us respond and bring this to a conclusion. There is no room for further tolerance.
300,000 Kando brethren, having already risen in righteous indignation and declared your resolution, fight on steadfastly to the end and achieve your initial aims. How can the compatriots in the homeland (內地同胞), who also have blood and tears, remain silent?
Compatriots, we have no one to rely on, no place to rely on. We have no other way but to unite among ourselves and live for ourselves. Therefore, let us forge a national unity. Let us thus mutually protect and safeguard our right to exist as a nation.<86> Wherever we go, there are only fellow countrymen. There is nothing else. Compatriots, let us unite! As the proverb says, "Fixing the stable after losing the horse," speaking like this after sacrificing one brother to them seems rather shameless. However, as "The overturned front cart is a warning to the rear cart," we cannot but initiate such a movement for our future survival.
Ah, Ch'oe Ch'angho, who senselessly departed this world under the poison bullet of a foreign people in a foreign land (異域)! How much did you struggle for survival during these twenty-five years of wind and rain, and what deep resentment did you harbor as you died in such tragic grief? Even if you had died fighting them with a cause worth risking your life for, our righteous indignation would swell, how much more so now... Ah, words and writing utterly fail. However,
Mr. Ch'oe, be at ease. Your brethren—300,000—have risen for you, proclaiming their lawlessness to the world and raising the banner of revenge against them. Your brethren in the homeland will also surely have some plan for you. Console yourself.
Mr. Ch'oe was from Yongsŏng-myŏn, Kyŏngsŏng-gun, Hamgyŏng Pukto, and resided in Puam-p'yŏng, Yonggang-dong, Yŏngil-hyŏn. The 12th, the day he was killed, was market day in Yongjŏng. The Chinese sentry, surnamed Liu (劉某), who shot him, was immediately arrested and detained at the lock-up of the Yongjŏng City Commerce Bureau (龍井市 商埠局). Mr. Ch'oe's body was handed over to his family, and on the 18th at 10 AM, a solemn funeral was held in the Ch'ŏndogyo District (天道故區) of Yongjŏng City according to the church's rites. Funeral committee chairman Mr. Yi Tŏkki and all committee members, citizens' rally chairman Mr. Yi Hongje, the entire staff and student body of Tonghŭng Middle School, and tens of thousands of other attendees from inside and outside bid Mr. Ch'oe a most tragic final farewell (慰送). Furthermore, the citizens' rally presented the following three demands to the Chinese authorities:
1. That the perpetrator be punished for premeditated murder.
1. That the Battalion Commander (營長) be dismissed, as the supervising officer's oversight was irresponsible.
1. Given that there was also an incident the year before last where a Chinese policeman shot and killed a young Korean man without cause, Chinese officials should not be recklessly entrusted with weapons.
Besides this, having declared the renunciation of [Japanese] nationality to the Japanese authorities, various committee members are reportedly traveling to different regions to investigate the situation of the compatriots one by one; the Kando compatriots are said to be greatly busying (奔忙) themselves with a self-reliance movement (自活運動).<87>

**Summary**

This 1923 news report from *Kaebyŏk* magazine details the killing of a young Korean man, Ch'oe Ch'angho, by a Chinese soldier in Yongjŏng, Kando (Jiandao). The article expresses intense outrage and grief, reporting on the immediate response from the local Korean community: protests, demands for justice from Chinese authorities (punishment of the soldier, dismissal of his commander, restrictions on weapons for Chinese police), a five-day business strike, and plans for a large residents' meeting. Significantly, the community also resolved to renounce their Japanese nationality due to perceived inadequate protection from Japan, signaling a desire for self-determination. The article emphasizes the vulnerability of Koreans in diaspora, urges national unity for self-protection, and describes the large, emotional funeral held for Ch'oe. For historians, this article is valuable as it vividly captures the precarious situation of the Korean minority in Manchuria in the early 1920s, caught between Chinese authorities and Japanese colonial power. It highlights the community's organized resistance, disillusionment with Japanese colonial subjecthood when abroad, and the burgeoning sense of Korean nationalism and desire for self-reliance in the face of external threats.
