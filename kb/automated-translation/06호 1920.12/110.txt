江原道 奧地의 野話 (A Folk Tale from the Backcountry of Kangwŏn Province)

This story is quite amusing, but if misunderstood, it might incur the antipathy of the brethren from that region. However, it is actually nothing more than a kind of joke (笑話), and since it is a saying prevalent in that area, I present it here. It's not a long story, but a very simple one. Namely, it's the saying that "In the backcountry of Kangwŏn Province (江原道 奧地), one must have three sons (千息 3兄弟) for just one to become the parents' share (*디)." Why is that? Because one is taken by the tiger (범(虎)) (as it's mountainous terrain with many tigers), and another is taken by the monk (중(僧)) (as Kŭmgangsan (金剛山), which could be called the stronghold (本據地) of Chosŏn (朝鮮) monks, is located there. Among the mountains there, the four great temples (4大寺刹) such as Changan (長安), P'yohun (表訓), Yujŏm (惀岾), and [missing name] all possess enormous [missing text] and monks [missing text] and their influence is grand; the temples [...] the local populace [百姓] [...]) leaving the remaining son to [...] This [...]. However, this is an old saying (녯말). Nowadays (只今), there is likely no such *reason* [missing text] for it. <59>

**Summary**

This short piece recounts a folk saying from the remote areas of Kangwŏn Province in 1920. The saying claims that parents need three sons because one will be taken by tigers (due to the mountainous terrain) and another by Buddhist monks (due to the strong presence and influence of major temples like those in Kŭmgangsan), leaving only one for the parents. The author presents it as an amusing anecdote but notes it's an old saying, implying it might not reflect contemporary reality. For historians, this snippet offers insight into local beliefs and perceived realities of early colonial Korea, reflecting the dangers of wildlife and the significant socio-economic presence and influence (perhaps perceived negatively as 'taking' sons) of large Buddhist institutions like those in Kŭmgangsan in rural areas.
