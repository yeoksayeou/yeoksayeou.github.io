Research on Innaech'ŏn
						Yare
(This article is the conclusion of the discussion on religion from the previous issue)

At first glance, it seems that God viewed from a pantheistic perspective (汎神論觀) might lack the material to draw human admiration (感心). However, the feeling of gratitude in humans varies depending on each individual's subjectivity (主觀). In my view, isn't the very fact that one came into this world as a human being the first thing to be grateful for? Of course, the feeling of gratitude might vary somewhat depending on subjectivity, but regarding our birth, various causes have intricately combined over tens of thousands of years, finally resulting in the emergence of the individual self called "I" into this world. When I think about the reason why my individual self became this self, it would not be an overstatement to say that the total power of the entire universe converged and crystallized. Thinking this way, my body is not something to be treated carelessly or lightly. Also, when I consider the time I navigate (遊弋) within this world, it is within 50 or 60 years, a short life that definitely does not exceed 100 years. Comparing this to the infinite, boundless time of the universe, how can this short span of 50 or 60 years not be considered incredibly precious to me? Indeed, when I think about the fact that this body, barely formed by the power of billions of years, is permitted to exist in this world for merely 50 or 60 years, the preciousness of this body becomes even clearer. Thinking from another perspective, if a body created by the vast power of billions of years is given only a lifespan of less than 100 years, one might feel resentment at one end, but thinking only in that direction leads endlessly further. Why? If one lives 100 years, one will think of 200 years; if one lives 200 years, <45> one will think of a thousand or ten thousand years, eventually reaching the state of eternal life, let's assume. (Though we cannot know at all without experiencing it) At that time, various inconveniences would likely arise. Although saying "It would be good just to live long, even with inconveniences" is a separate issue, since we do not yet know the taste of immortality, if we consider our actions of living with death as the subject, we cannot help but find it strange. The saying "天上天下唯我獨尊" (In heaven and earth, I alone am the honored one) is not originally a new theory, but if we take the self as the master, everything in the world becomes one's own possession. For example, when going to a park and seeing cherry blossoms or animals and plants, if we ask for whom these were created, it seems unavoidable that everything was created for me. The only reason they weren't created in my home is due to the various efforts and procedures involved, so it can be said that the world was entrusted (分付) with creating them. If I were to move this park to my home, starting tomorrow, I would need to pay land rent, hire servants (小僮), and supervise it; therefore, thinking that it was entrusted to the world to avoid such troubles (苟且), it cannot be denied that this park was indeed created for me alone. Viewing the world under this concept, there is nothing in the world that is not my possession. The world is indeed my possession, but entrusted to others for convenience. If viewed this way, there is nothing more thankful in the world. This cannot but be called the work of the wondrous God. However, after thinking this way, how can it be related to God? If the wondrous is truly wondrous, how can it be regarded as God? God, that is, the God of the pantheistic view, sees everything, this and that without distinction, as God. Therefore, the God of the pantheistic view refers to what is ultimately called Reality (究竟實在). Since Reality points to the total existence of the universe, pantheism will conclude that every object in the world is God. Then, since Reality includes even what we recognize as unjust, as one aspect of Reality, things like the constantly changing, indeterminate time must also be Reality. Since Reality refers to the entirety of the universe as it is, from our perspective, time constantly flows and is indeterminate, but from the perspective of the totality of Reality itself, the constant flow of time is ultimately not constant flow but fixed Reality. Why? I am one who will die within 100 years, but after me, people like me will continue, so time, viewed from the perspective of successive individuals, flows ceaselessly. From the perspective of the totality of Reality, that is, the totality of billions upon billions of years of people, it is fixed and non-flowing. Place (處所) too, if viewed individually, there might be distance between here and there, but viewed from the total substance of the world, there is neither far nor near. Therefore, <46> the passage of time and the change of place are meaningless from the perspective of absolute Reality. That is, from the perspective of Reality, there is neither time nor place. How would things be if we contemplate all matters with the concept of Reality without time and place? Because time flows long, this self grows from small to large, and having grown large, must eventually die. However, suppose there was a person born 30 years ago who died at the age of 30. That person would certainly not be in this world in the 31st year, but that is because the time before and after 30 years is different, so they disappeared from this world. If we look at the time before today, that is, 30 years ago, that person certainly existed. Ultimately, the constant flow of time is like the page numbers of a book; the fact written on this page is absent on the next page. However, if we open the previous page, that fact will still be written there. The flow of time is also like this; a person who died at 30 will, of course, not be there after 30, but if we return to before 30, they still existed as a fact. Therefore, from the perspective of the total Reality, things do not perish—they are eternal and imperishable. Place is also like this; the place I call 'here' now and the place called 'there' have some distance between them, but viewed from the perspective of Reality, 'here' and 'there' are identical. Therefore, wherever I may be, ultimately—the concept that religious figures speak of, being together with God, I think is not the slightest bit different. Whether one goes to Heaven or Hell, both are Reality, both are 'here' and 'there'. Therefore, from the perspective of Reality, the thing called place is the same 'here' everywhere. Ultimately—the place where I exist is the place where God exists, and what I think of as 'now' is a point in the endless time engraved on Reality, without beginning or end. Therefore, what I say now and what I say later are the same Reality. People of the world say that once time has passed, the fact disappears; this is very far from the concept of Reality. The concept of Reality is difficult to clarify, but if one contemplates the change of time well—and considers time as non-existent, and thinks about the human mind, what would happen? No matter how one thinks, a person's death is one fact, the passing of people of old is one fact. Thus, however we may have used this time called our fifty years of life, that fact does not change. I wasted fifty years of time with no use whatsoever. How could this one fact disappear? Of course, time will pass. Time passes, but the fact does not disappear. If the universe is considered as something where time flows and place changes, then the concepts of past events and future events, here and there, would differ. But if the universe is contemplated as true Reality without time and place, then past events, future events, here, and there all bear the same value, and thus are equally facts of facts. It is as if we write our biography on the surface of flowing white paper called time. <47> When the writing on the white paper is finished, we die and go forward, so it is not visible to our eyes, but it remains as it is on the preceding white paper. If looked at this way, all things and events in the world can be called God. Even within that thought, there might be aspects that must be contemplated with a true mind (眞面目). Contemplating this with a true mind, and furthermore, as mentioned before, no matter how much we think, we first have thoughts about things that resemble ourselves (彷彿한 者). Therefore, starting from thinking about what resembles us—ultimately, thinking that one's birth into this world is God's blessing (惠澤) might also be acceptable. Here, saying God's blessing attributes personality to God, but if it took hundreds of thousands of years to create one of us, there would be no other word than blessing.

Thinking thus, my so-called final religion is this: It is a religion that recognizes the absolute, so-called Reality, as God. Merely recognizing Reality as God might result in somewhat less admiration, so to clarify this, it should be explained with some meaning using the words we employ to clarify our personality. Such a God would be the true God, and believing in it would be the true religion. That is, what is the religion of the future? It is the theory of pantheism, the absolute or Reality; yet Reality alone is not sufficient. That Reality must be imagined with some meaning that can enter our imagination—that is God, and believing in it is the true religion of the future."

The above exposition by Mr. Hŭgam (黑岩氏), in its broad outline, is the religion of Innaech'ŏn. No, from beginning to end, it carries the meaning of Innaech'ŏn. Although he may not be a professional religious figure, what he believes possesses the religious meaning of Innaech'ŏn.

To clarify the doctrine of Innaech'ŏn concerning the final religion, if we reiterate Mr. Hŭgam's constantly mentioned concept of Reality, Reality is precisely the reality of the entire universe (without beginning or end). Therefore, from the perspective of Reality, the past is also not past, and the future is also not future; the past exists eternally in its place, and the future exists eternally in its place. Eternal Reality, that is, without beginning or end, is entirely God. This is precisely the pantheistic concept. Mr. Hŭgam's assertion that such Reality is God aligns perfectly (如合符節) with the God of Innaech'ŏn. However, in his view that Reality alone cannot become a religious God and thus it is good to encompass some meaning within it, Mr. Hŭgam remains entirely a critical philosopher and lacks a creative religious consciousness. Why? Because he ultimately makes no assertion of what that meaning should be. According to our (Innaech'ŏn's) thinking, if Reality itself is meaningless and it is good to encompass some meaning within it, then the most appropriate religious concept is to view our spirituality (靈性), which is a part of our Reality, as God, and to view that spirituality itself as the real expression of the great cosmic Reality. Thus, why wouldn't starting the worship of God with one's own spirituality be the final religion? <48>

**Summary**

This article continues a discourse on the Ch'ŏndogyo principle of 'Innaech'ŏn' (Man is Heaven), interpreting it through a pantheistic framework centered on the concept of 'Siltchae' (Reality/Existence), ideas the author associates with a thinker named Hŭgam-ssi (likely Kuroiwa Ruikō). The author posits that existence itself is a profound gift stemming from the entirety of cosmic history, making the individual and the universe intrinsically valuable and interconnected – effectively, the universe exists for the individual. This universal, timeless, and placeless 'Reality' is identified with the pantheistic God. While appreciating Hŭgam's framework, the author criticizes him for not defining the specific 'meaning' that should imbue this Reality for religious purposes. The author concludes by asserting that the Innaech'ŏn perspective finds this meaning in human spirituality (靈性), suggesting that recognizing and venerating one's own spirit as the manifestation of universal Reality is the foundation of the true, final religion.

*Historical Significance:* For historians, this piece illustrates the intellectual syncretism in early colonial Korea, merging the indigenous Ch'ŏndogyo belief in human divinity (Innaech'ŏn) with Western pantheism and philosophical concepts of 'Reality,' likely filtered through Japanese intellectual currents (via Hŭgam/Kuroiwa). It reflects Korean intellectuals' attempts to articulate a modern national and spiritual identity by reinterpreting traditional thought alongside global philosophical trends under the constraints and influences of Japanese colonial rule. The emphasis on inherent human value and spirituality within Innaech'ŏn could also be seen as a subtle assertion of national dignity.
