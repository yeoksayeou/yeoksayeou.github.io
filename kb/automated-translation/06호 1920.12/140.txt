The Principle of a Play's Structure
What was briefly explained in the fifth issue concerned the points of difference in composition between the novel and the play, but now let's speak generally about the principle of the structure upon which a play is organized. For any play to arise, there must invariably be some kind of conflict as its cause. To give an example, conflicts between individuals, conflicts between emotions, conflicts between interests, etc.—due to the cause of such conflicts, a single piece (of work) comes into being. Thus, from the origin of this conflict, as the event gradually becomes misunderstood and reaches a certain state, the resolution of the conflict is settled; this is the principle of a play's structure. However, the path from this occurrence to the resolution must be constructed in a way that provides flavor (滋味) to the viewer under certain conditions. In short, the arrangement of a play must necessarily be made (作) to clearly reveal the progression of this conflict. As such, the journey from the beginning of the event to the end is not made of just one part; naturally,

All the Constituent Parts of a Play's Structure
will exist. In other words, there will be a plot line (劇線), akin to a single thread, from the emergence of a certain conflict to its resolution, and it goes without saying that this plot line will also have threads of different colors. That is to say, there will be a part explaining the initial event where the conflict arises. Next, there will be a part where the colors gradually intensify, and the progression of the event reaches an extreme. And then, there must also be a part where the event that reached the extreme gradually resolves and finally returns to a normal state; this is the part that can be called the final conclusion (團結) where the event is summarized. As the parts of the plot line differ in this way, it is certain that there will naturally be several distinct stages. Therefore,

The Form of a Play Has Five Stages and Variations in Length
The plot line from beginning to end is generally <69> established in 5 parts, hence the form of the play is also commonly established in 5 acts (段). However, these 5 acts are not necessarily divided regularly into 5 parts. In other words, it's not like Act 1 is 50 pages, Act 2 is 50 pages, Act 3 is 50 pages, arranged strictly according to a rule of 50 pages each. Act 1 might be a bit shorter, and Act 2 might be a bit longer. In short, if the form of these 5 acts is present within one play, it can be said that the play has no defects. Furthermore, calculating by the number of acts (幕), suppose there is a continuous play of 7 acts. It's certainly possible that the first act corresponds to the 1st stage, the next act corresponds to the 2nd stage, the following two acts correspond to the 3rd and 4th stages respectively, and the final act fits the 5th stage. In essence, regardless of the number of acts, whether long or short, the form established by the 5 stages of a play must invariably be present.
However, considering that a play typically arises from some sort of complication (葛藤), that is, conflict, there must inevitably be some underlying cause before that conflict emerges. Therefore, when we write (書) a play, before writing the 1st stage, there must necessarily be an explanation of the part that becomes the cause of the conflict. For this reason, combining the part explaining the cause of the play's conflict with the formal structure of the play, we can say the whole is established in 6 parts. If we represent this graphically, let's say 'ga' is the 1st stage, 'na' is the 2nd stage, 'da' is the 3rd stage, 'ra' is the 4th stage, 'ma' is the 5th stage, and 'ba' is the 6th stage. The Play's Prologue (序幕)
This is what it is called; in English, it is called Exposition. Generally, those who watch a play first want to know what characters appear in the entire play, what relationships exist between the characters, and what event's conflict occurs. Explaining this and making it clearly understood is the major task of the prologue. Therefore, this part is an important gateway that those intending to write a play must never treat carelessly. If the method of arrangement is uneven or messy, it will have a significantly detrimental effect on the entire play, so those studying <70> dramatic literature should pay close attention. Now, there are various means and methods used for this prologue's explanation. When we watch Japanese theatre (日本 演劇), often a light yellow curtain (淺黃幕) is drawn, and in front of it, several characters, not closely related to the play's protagonist, appear and explain the occurrence of the event through their dialogue; this is also one type of this method. In any case, the major conditions for the prologue are: first, it must be described clearly; second, it must be written concisely without being lengthy; third, it must be woven very deeply with the 2nd stage. It should not seem like something deliberately attached separately. That is, it must be joined most naturally. That's enough about the prologue, and next, let's talk a bit about

The Play's Second Stage
As mentioned before, the former is called the prologue, and this 2nd stage is called the first act; the English are said to call it introduction. It is in this stage that the conflict of the event begins to arise. Thus, there isn't any separate division between the already mentioned prologue and the 2nd stage we are about to discuss; they must mutually fuse (融合) so that the seam connecting them continues naturally without gaps. In other words, showing the path where conflict begins to arise and enters a state of activity, based on the characters and events explained in the prologue, is the task of this stage. Enough of that, next

The Play's Third Stage
Let's talk about this. Listen closely. This 3rd stage is the section (叚) that describes the place where the conflict that arose in the 1st act gradually intensifies (濃厚) and becomes even more difficult and complex (複襍); in English, it is called Rising action. Describing the path by which the conflict arising in the 1st act gradually becomes complex, in a way that seems most plausible to the viewer, is the important task of this stage. Therefore, the cause leading the conflict to its peak (絶頂) must be depicted most vividly. To give an example, if the conflict arose between characters, the characters' personalities must be described in this stage so they can be understood. Also, if the conflict originated solely from the mental action of one character, the path of how that character's mental state changes from what state to what state must be clearly depicted. Therefore, in this act, describing new characters with minimal relevance or trivial events should not be done. Why? Because there is a concern of unnecessarily leading the path of the conflict's progression into obscurity. Let's stop chattering about the play's 3rd stage <71> and now, let's talk a bit about

The Play's Fourth Stage
As mentioned before, if any event or the force of any character cannot coexist, conflict must inevitably arise between them. If these two things could coexist, then a play would ultimately be difficult to establish. Therefore, the result of the conflict between the two will lose balance, and superiority/inferiority will emerge. That is, the event will undergo a transition (轉換). Depicting such a gateway is the task of the 4th stage. This stage is called Climax in English, and also Turning Point, they say. This stage can be considered the peak (絶頂) of the conflict for the entire play; it is the most intense focal point of the conflict that determines which of the two will win or lose. The indispensable nature of this stage is to make the characters, events, and circumstances that arose from the previous stage reach this peak most naturally. It is to make the audience understand the ascent to the peak most logically and rationally. Therefore, it is a part that especially requires the skill (手腕) of the playwright. Also, for tragedy, it is the scene where sorrow (愁嘆) is most profound. Let's leave the 4th stage of the play at this and, changing the line again,

The Play's Fifth Stage
What kind of thing is this? In all human affairs, regardless of the situation or fact, when the extreme is reached, there is invariably a reversal (逆轉). That is, the play's event, having become entangled and reached its peak, passes the peak and then gradually unravels, approaching resolution. Describing such a situation is the task of the 5th stage, and it is called Fall in English. The nature of this stage is the place where judgment is made between tragedy and comedy—whether the play will end happily or sadly. If the play is likely a comedy, in this stage, all obstacles (障害) and various misunderstandings are naturally removed, and everything proceeds smoothly for the protagonist. If the play is likely to become a tragedy, conversely, the path where disaster (災殃) and sorrow (悲愁) were intensifying is gradually removed, and simultaneously, as a result, only the tragic form naturally sharpens its teeth (其牙を研磨), and the protagonist falls further into dire straits (窮境). In any case, whether it's called tragedy or comedy, describing the path where the cause of the conflict, having reached its peak as mentioned in the previous stage, newly arises in some direction * is undertaken in this stage. Also, from the perspective of the reader or viewer, up until the 4th stage of the play they have been watching, the viewer's <73> psychological state has been watching with a kind of anxious notion about how the play will develop. Upon reaching this point, that point of doubt (疑點) begins to be removed, and with a bright and upright heart, full-hearted enthusiasm and sympathy are expressed towards the protagonist within the play. This gateway also demands extraordinary effort and skill (手腕) from the author. What is the reason? Because by the time the audience has watched the play up to this point, they have already gauged more than half of the content's circumstances, and thus they come to anticipate what the result will be. Therefore, the author cannot help but pull the audience, situated at this position, to the very end without letting their interest cease or weariness (厭症) arise. The author's greatest pains are taken in this stage due to this reason. Truly, unless the author has real ability, it is difficult to sustain the reader or viewer without causing weariness, even if this stage is drawn out long. Looking at plays of old, the tendency to often draw out the climax of the 4th stage and shorten from the 5th stage onwards exists entirely for this reason. If this stage fails, the ending becomes utterly worthless; conversely, if this stage succeeds, the play will be endlessly interesting until the end. Often, authors, in order to make this stage flavorful, insert new facts or narrate humorous (滑稽) events to make the viewers laugh. Using such devices obstructs the progression of the event nearing its resolution, causing the viewer to feel anxious and thus arousing curiosity (好奇心) with suspicion about whether the anticipated ending will be reached. In comedy, circumstances obstructing the protagonist's advance towards happiness are described, and in tragedy, the protagonist is made to seemingly gain a lease on life from nine deaths, making the already determined fate appear temporarily recovered (回復). This stage, containing such difficult problems, can be called a touchstone (試金石) testing the superiority or inferiority of the author's skill (技倆). Writing this much, separately,

The Play's Sixth Stage
Let's record a little about this. The 6th stage of the play is the very final stage; it is the concluding part that satisfies the path the viewer has been awaiting. This is called Catastrophe in English. However, some authors, especially among modern writers, sometimes use a so-called conclusionless conclusion (A Conclusion in which nothing is conclusion). A conclusionless conclusion means that the play announces its end without a conclusion that can be called a conclusion. Among modern writers, there were many cases of using this method, particularly among writers of extreme realism (極端寫實主義). <73> Their view is that plays or novels must, of course, be truthful to life. Well then, life never has anything like a conclusion. Unbroken (連綿不盡) events mutually connect, becoming causes and effects, and as long as life itself does not conclude, events will not conclude; events ceaselessly generate events. Because a play faithfully depicts such a life, there is no reason for a conclusion to exist in one part of its arrangement, they say, and concluding arbitrarily at a point where a conclusion cannot be determined is the truthful play and true literature. Under such a view, the conclusionless conclusion is used.
This argument is certainly not without some reason, nor does it deviate from logic. However, I wonder if it isn't just looking vertically at one aspect. Originally, life indeed has neither beginning nor end. But it is an undeniable fact that within the continuous stream of boundless life, upon reaching certain junctures, a single mass is formed having both a beginning and an end, connected part by part. It is a fact that from within this infinite life, a small scale, that is, a part that can be called a microcosm of life, is extracted (捉取). Humans think about the problem of life and come to realize, "Life is like this," which also means that it is possible to grasp a finite small scale sufficient to survey this infinite life. Extracting this small-scale life, this part of life that can be seen as a microcosm of the larger life, is precisely art (藝術) and literature. Therefore, it goes without saying that in literature, that is, in a play, there must also be a part that can be seen as the result of one segment. **Summary**

This article by Hyojongsaeng (曉鍾生) in the December 1920 issue of *Kaebyŏk* discusses the fundamental structure of a play. It argues that plays arise from conflict and outlines a six-part structure for dramatic progression: 1. Prologue/Exposition (introducing characters, setting, conflict basis), 2. Introduction (the conflict begins), 3. Rising Action (conflict intensifies and complicates), 4. Climax/Turning Point (peak of conflict, turning point), 5. Falling Action (conflict unravels towards resolution, determining comedy/tragedy), and 6. Catastrophe/Conclusion (the final resolution). The author emphasizes the importance of natural transitions and skillful writing, especially in the later stages, to maintain audience interest. It also briefly discusses and critiques the modern realist trend of the "conclusionless conclusion," arguing for the validity of conventional endings as reflecting graspable segments of life's continuum. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it shows Korean intellectuals in 1920 actively engaging with and adapting Western literary theory (explicitly using English terms like Exposition, Climax, Catastrophe) to analyze and define dramatic structure for a Korean audience. This reflects the broader effort during the colonial period to modernize Korean arts and letters, incorporating international ideas while debating their application within their own cultural and philosophical context, even touching on realist aesthetics versus traditional narrative closure.
