발행일	1920년 12월 01일
기사제목	上海로부터 金陵까지 (From Shanghai to Jinling (Nanjing))
필자	江南賣畵廊
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

上海로부터 金陵까지
						江南賣畵廊

I am a mere scholar, a rootless wanderer across ten thousand li, a nameless man of wind and moon (風月漢), a companionless picture seller (賣畵郞 - Maehwarang). Ah—lonely and pitiful. With scrolls tucked beside me and a camera (寫眞機) slung over my shoulder, by day I weave poems amidst the green willows, and by night, riding the clear tranquility of water and moon is also sufficient enjoyment. I alone savor true freedom without the slightest constraint, and I alone breathe the vital air without the slightest hesitation. I know not what it is, but it's a charming life amidst blooming mornings and moonlit evenings; I know not what it is, but it's an endless paradise of spring mountains and autumn waters.
"Wandering through antiquity, I ask, but there's no one; Everywhere, the scenery is all mine.
Dotting mountains and rivers flow before my eyes; White clouds stretch ten thousand li, a gourd of wine."
Though I may be excessively eccentric, my truly unrestrained state of life, though excessively vast, my utterly unhesitating living paradise's scenery—not with splendid prose, nor with exquisite colors, but just roughly and plumply, whether possible or not, just sincerely as it is, just factually as it is, without the slightest hesitation, without the slightest reservation, awake or asleep, asleep or awake, only dreaming of a life of happiness—for our fathers, mothers, brothers, and sisters, truly with aching bones and trembling heart, I cannot but cry out, cannot but introduce it.
However, the only regret is that due to the avoidance of all sorts of phrases, there is a considerable sense of inconvenience, and accordingly, in the content of the article, there is no small lamentation of insufficiency.

From Shanghai to Hangzhou

The time was precisely mid-autumn, the 8th month. Drifting along in a single-leaf boat, I became a traveler of rivers and lakes. "Red polygonum flowers bloom, autumn is half gone; The azure sky like water, the moon hangs in the middle." In this fine elegance, how much pleasure is there? In this vast universe, can it be said there is no kindred spirit (知音)?
Ah—Shanghai, farewell.
I am just leaving.
At every famous spot in Jiangnan,
The clear wind and bright moon are my companions.
Singing thus, I cross the Baidu Bridge over the Huangpu River. The black bronze statue and the white jade pagoda, half-hidden amidst the dense yellow leaves and crimson maples, subtly show affection. The boat has already rounded Yangshupu and reached Chongming Island.
"Appearing and disappearing, fish and dragons dance; Vast islands flow.
Within my chest, limitless sorrow; One stretch of blue floats to the edge of the sky." <117>
At sunset that day, they said we had arrived at the Qian Tang River. Disembarking swiftly at the wharf (碼頭) and settling into an inn (旅館), I joyfully strolled out. Gazing at Wulin Mountain, I passed through Yongjin Gate. Ah—this is West Lake (西湖).
"Ten li of lotus flowers, five li of mist; Three sides mountain rock, one side city wall." Truly wonderful, West Lake!!
Leifeng Pagoda guards souls of a thousand years; Echoes in the empty valley speak of the homeland's sorrow. The strong green wine of Xinghua Village is where Li Bai (李太白) drank; The autumn point of Huxin Pavilion is where Su Dongpo (蘇東坡) played. The Three Pools Mirroring the Moon at Xiaoyingzhou, the Eight-Gate Stone Bridge of the Inner and Outer Lakes—truly wonderful, West Lake!! Suddenly, the white sun completely set beyond Mogan Mountain. Soon, dusk deeply enveloped the tomb of Qiu Jin (秋女俠 - Female Knight of Autumn), and all sorts of scenery emerged. All sorts of emotions poured out.
Rustling, rustling over the paulownia leaves, gently, gently through the creeping fig vines, secretly tugging at my sleeve, the affectionate and fresh autumn wind whispers, "Look at me."
The fair cheek of the white day trembles, pearl-like tears well up in the slender eyebrows, seeming about to speak but not speaking, biting the hem of her skirt with her lips, as if resenting someone—the heart-wrenching and pitiful sliver of moon!
Amidst this, the night deepened to the third watch (三更). Leaning on the railing (欄干), I held onto old feelings. The scenery of West Lake is fine, and the history (歷史) of West Lake is worth hearing.
The name West Lake is sometimes called Mingwang Lake or Qiantang Lake. Naturally, since it is located west of Hangzhou city, it came to be commonly called West Lake anyway. Speaking of Hangzhou, it is separated only by a river, upstream and downstream, from Shaoxing Prefecture, the former capital (都邑) of King Gou Jian of Yue (越王句踐). Thus, the traces of Gou Jian's constant wandering and extravagant drinking seem still hidden beneath the water. Looking at this West Lake, one ancient story immediately comes to mind; it seems to be a tale from the time of the Jin invasion during the late Song dynasty. What is it? It is the story of the oil seller (賣油郞).
『At that time, in Hangzhou city, there was a famous courtesan (妓生) named Yup'o. She had been engaged (約婚) since childhood. However, during the turmoil (亂離), she lost her parents (父母) and was without relatives (親戚). Somehow, her pure body of noble birth (金枝玉葉) unfortunately fell into the life of a prostitute (路柳墻花). She was very intelligent (聰明) and exceedingly talented (天才). She was well-versed in poetry and painting (詩畵) and skilled in song and dance (歌舞). Consequently, in those days, wealthy families (富豪家) and powerful families (權勢家) were all intoxicated by her beauty, desperately vying for her. However, she guarded her chastity (貞操) fiercely, determined to die rather than yield, and constantly prayed to heaven, earth, sun, and moon (天地日月) to find her husband. Meanwhile, in Hangzhou city, there was an oil seller (賣油郞). He had come to Hangzhou following his father (父親). His father had sold him as an adopted son (養子) to the owner of an oil shop (油房). Thus, he obtained the fine name of Oil Shop Young Master (公子). However, his fate (運數) was too rough (崎嶇); soon he was separated even from his adoptive father <118> and incurred the dislike of his adoptive mother, suffering harsh false accusations (累名) and being utterly cast out of the house. Ah—he was pitiful. Finally, helpless, with a tightly bound heart filled with indescribable, endless sorrow, he resolved to become a ghost in the water forever... Holding the setting sun (夕陽), silently flowing tears of blood just dripped onto the shores of West Lake.
Ah—I, I, I now—
Where, where, where do I go?
The myriad tangled grievances (怨), the sorrow (恨) passed down through millennia,
To whom, to whom, to whom shall I speak—
At that moment, an old man (老人), unaware of the deepening dusk, was out strolling by West Lake, enjoying the water. "Ah—careful!" he cried, rushing over in an instant and seizing him firmly. And so, he entrusted his connection (因緣) to this life once more to the old man's single word of comfort (慰安). From then on, carrying oil on his shoulders, he became an oil peddler, traversing Hangzhou city from dawn till evening, from evening till nightfall, tirelessly turning back and forth. One day, carrying his oil, he turned the corner by the General's residence (將軍府) and arrived at a certain flower tower with bead curtains (珠簾) hanging low. Lifting his head, the fleeting glimpse of a shadow suddenly became a flashing "moving picture" (活動寫眞) in his mind. He thought to himself, what joy do I attach to living in this world? Is it fame and honor (功名)? Is it wealth and nobility (富貴)? Neither is it. Only—only love—my beloved full of that love—her voice softer than silk floss, her face whiter than snowflakes, her slender waist, her graceful neck. "Ah!" To pull her close fiercely, raise both hands high, suckle her bulging breasts, and fall asleep forever in a sweet dream... Thinking thus, at the end of his thoughts came resolution. One morning, gathering all the money from selling oil, he went to a clothes rental shop, put on decent clothes, and walked just like a young nobleman to seek the beauty. When he arrived, the beauty he longed to see had been called away to someone's banquet and was not there, but an unpleasant old woman put on a wry face and came out chattering, "Please come in," leading him in. Since it wasn't a house he frequented, his steps became somewhat hesitant, and even his voice trembled. "Uh—uh—that—that Miss Yup'o, where did she go—?" he asked. The old woman, as if having found a big rice cake to extort, said, "Ah—come this way. This room is Yup'o's room. Please go in there. She will be back soon," while pouring tea on one hand and flattering him outrageously on the other. In the meantime, the sun had already set, and the moon had risen. Still, Yup'o did not return. The oil seller, utterly anxious to the point of dying, saw only the shadow cast by a magnificent embroidered screen (錦繡屛風) he had never seen before. After a while, there was a sound at the door. A strong fragrance wafted in, his heart fluttered. Having drunk so much alcohol, she stumbled into the room with her skirt <119> hiked up halfway, then collapsed onto the bed just like that. Snoring, she just kept sleeping. Beside her, the oil seller sat as if painted, for some reason. Tossing and turning violently, rolling his head this way and that, she suddenly began to vomit—wak—wak. The whole room filled with the smell of alcohol. Without a word, the oil seller picked up a cloth, wiped away all the vomit, and gently covered her with the blanket; his affection was truly pitiable. Somehow, the beauty now opened her eyes. Why was this man, a stranger she had never seen before, doing all this troublesome work for her? She was surprised and grateful. Tender and devoted, endless tears of sympathy (同情) welled up uncontrollably. Ah—what is this?』 Deeply pondering such events, and also considering how I came to acquire my current name of Maehwarang (Picture Seller), indeed, I find myself in circumstances (境遇) that are not merely accidental. Ah—for you, my feelings are limitless. "On the heart, no hook hangs matters of sorrow; In the eyes, there is a measure for human feelings." Reciting this, I sat serenely and then, overcome by my own emotions, fell asleep.

The next day, I had only heard about the Qiantang tidal bore (錢塘觀潮), but now I finally witnessed the famous spectacle (奇觀), one of the world's four great tides. The number of people who came to see the water was immense; the roar of "Ah—amazing!" was deafening. Among them, Westerners and Japanese were all busy taking photographs (寫眞). Regarding this tide, they say the Mid-Autumn Festival (中秋佳節) each year is the most interesting. Thus, "Jinling in the third month, Qiantang in the eighth month" is a common saying among Chinese people. At this, I thought, when will all these people become spectators of the Manp'oktong Falls (萬瀑洞) at our Kŭmgangsan (金剛山)? Just then, a Chinese student standing next to me said, "Excuse me," and requested to greet me. So I offered my hand and expressed my pleasure.

(He) I greatly admire the people of your esteemed country. Also, regarding your country's situation, although only seen through newspapers, I feel considerable sympathy. As I am currently a student, political discourse (政治上 言論) is outside my scope (範圍), so I wish to speak only about education (敎育). How many universities (大學) does your country have?
(I) There is only one school officially named a university, but besides that, there are several specialized schools (專門學校). In terms of the educational situation, well—it cannot be compared to Tokyo or Shanghai. Consequently, the enthusiasm (熱) for studying abroad (留學) has swelled (澎漲) considerably. However, recently, something called Chosŏn University (朝鮮大學) was said to be established, but I haven't heard any good news yet about how it's progressing.
(He) Of course, you must have your insights (洞察), elder brother (兄長), but regardless of the era (時代) or circumstances (境遇), when it comes to the issue of education, it is the fundamental spiritual point (精神點) of our human lives (人生).
(I) Eh—isn't that indeed so? Moreover, we have a 4,000-year <120> history (歷史) and a population (民衆) of 20 million. Looking just at this, we have inherent customs and habits (風俗習慣) and inherent culture and arts (文化藝術).
(He) By the way, isn't it a fact (事實) that your country was once subordinate (屬) to our humble country (弊國), and we maintained brotherly friendship (兄弟의 友誼)? Why have things become so unrecognizable now? Truly, the changes of time (時運變遷) are immeasurable.
(I) No, while it's certainly a fine thing to say we maintained brotherly friendship intimately, the term 'subordinate' (屬) merely referred to the personal diplomatic relations (室家的 交際) between the one called the Son of Heaven (天子) in your country and the one called the king in our humble country at that time. Why bring that up now? Hasn't your country become a republic (共和), erasing all old appearances (舊觀) with a single stroke of the brush, and already passed 9 years (星霜) since setting the founding era (開國 紀元) from the first year of the Republic (民國 元年)? That writing 'subordinate' back then seems to be an excessive ink mark (濫墨) by the historian (史氏) of your country's imperial house (皇家).
(He) Well, let's just say that's how it was; it wasn't said with any particular meaning, so please forgive me (容恕).
(I) By the way, elder brother, which school do you attend?
(He) Eh—I attend Zhijiang University (之江大學).
Saying this, he took my hand, stood up, and said, "Come—let's go to my school and have a look around." So I followed him into Zhijiang University. School was already out. The sound of ball kicking echoed 'thump thump' on the sports field (運動場), and at the school gate, commuting students (通學生) were bustling about noisily, heading home. We went into the principal's office (校長室), greeted the principal, and exchanged friendly "How do you dos" with all the teachers (先生). Then, guided by the student, we entered the reception room (應接室). Soon, all the students lined up, and from first to last, they came forward one by one to shake hands. Among them, one Mr. Pang spoke words of greeting on behalf of all the students. And that night, I stayed (寄宿) at this school. After eating dinner (晩飯), we strolled around the school's rear garden (後園), and then I was guided to Lecture Hall No. 1 (講堂). Entering the lecture hall, all the students were gathered; it seemed they had assembled due to some announcement (通知). The principal and all the teachers were present, and outside the door stood a student (生徒) who looked like either a gatekeeper (守門) or a monitor (司察). Originally, Zhijiang University was a branch (支會) of the student self-government association (學生自治會), so he seemed to be a policeman (巡査) of the student self-government system (自治制). Last spring, within Hangzhou Provincial First Middle School, a self-government association meeting for middle school students from both Zhejiang and Jiangsu provinces was held; this truly seemed an over-exuberance of the democratic trend (風潮).
Around 8 PM, Mr. Pang declared the meeting open (開會) and, like delivering an opening address (式辭), said, "Currently, a Korean brother has graced (光臨) our school. <121> Historically, geographically, and in every aspect, we cannot help but think of our situation with the nation of Korea as one of sharing the same illness and pitying each other, sharing the same boat to cross the river together (同病相憐 同舟共濟). However, we were always exceedingly curious, not knowing how that country's situation was or how its compatriots' (同胞) lives were. Fortunately, his arrival here on this occasion for sightseeing at the lake is truly an opportunity (機會) for us that cannot be denied." He then requested me to ascend the platform (壇). At this moment, I was so dumbfounded I didn't know what to do; I couldn't just say I wouldn't do it, nor could I say I would. Having never once opened my mouth [to speak publicly] even in my own country, this was truly making me shit my pants [idiom: extremely nervous]. However, since the situation (事勢) had become thus unavoidable (迫不得已), it was truly a pitiful state. Ah well, let me just ascend the platform and see. As I strode onto the platform, the applause from the entire hall (滿場의 拍手) felt like either a thunderbolt (霹靂) or a threat (威脅) to my weak heart. My insides were trembling, yet my shoulders squared up quite properly. At that time, I stated that since I couldn't speak well, I would write down on the blackboard (黑板) what I couldn't articulate.
"I am merely a student, possessing the same heart and seeking the same hope as all of you. That something piled up in my chest is just running wild, and on top of that, my voice trembles.
Alas! We bear at least the responsibility (責任) of being a part of the world. Without divisions (區分) between nations (國家), without discrimination (差別) between peoples (民族), let us find comfort (安樂) by exchanging intentions (意思) and find happiness (幸福) by sharing common principles (主義), making mutual intimacy (親睦) and mutual support (扶持) our absolute duty (絶對的 義務). Thus, we must strike down with our own hands all the incomplete (不徹底) and unnatural (不自然) evils (罪惡) and contradictions (矛盾) in the whole world (天下). Consequently, anyone whose principles align with ours, we must shake hands with them; anyone whose ideals (理想) match ours, we must cultivate friendship (親交) with them—isn't this already something you all sympathize (同感) with? Expressing such sympathetic feelings for this person now also lies herein. Then, speaking of 'our country' or 'your country,' what need is there to draw any special boundary (境界)? What point is there in further lengthy, superfluous talk (贅說) about things you already know? I cannot now verbally express the circumstances of my hometown (故鄉) one by one, so if you wish to hear even my distress (苦衷), I will leave behind a draft manuscript (稿草) where I have recorded everything about this distress, somewhat in written form (文體上 筆記). That seems most convenient. Rather than me speaking lengthy, inept words, and rather than you striving effortfully to listen, you can look at it again and again over time... (Note: Draft manuscript omitted.)"
With this, I descended the platform (下壇). Then, a female student (女學生) played the piano (피아노), truly letting flow some melody (曲調) that agitated (散亂) my mind (心事). <122> No matter how much she played a song of encounter (相逢歌), to my ears it sounded like a song of longing for home (望鄕歌); no matter how much she played a song of friendship (交友歌), to my ears it sounded like a song of parting (離別歌). As nine o'clock struck 'dong dong', the meeting closed (閉會).

The next day, after eating breakfast early, as I bade farewell (作別) to all the fellow students (學友諸君) and teachers (先生 一同), pointing to the acacia (아까시아) tree planted in the courtyard, I recited: "When the moon is out, please look at the tree in the courtyard; Earnestly, without words, this heart knows." (有月請看庭上樹, 殷勤無語此心知). Stepping briskly out of the school gate and seeing a large old tree standing tall by the roadside, I recited: "The old tree vows to support the great mansion; Still residing naturally in the posture of a sleeping dragon." (古木誓將大廈支, 依然自居臥龍姿). I wrote these two lines separately on the back of my name card (名片), sending my regards (送情) and expressing my thanks (謝過) to Mr. P. Then, returning straight to the inn, I collected my luggage (行李), hailed a rickshaw (人力車), and went out to the train station (停車場).

From Hangzhou to Suzhou

On the 18th at noon, I boarded the train at Zhakou Station (閘口站), the terminus of the Shanghai-Hangzhou Railway (滬杭 鐵路), and arrived in Jiaxing (嘉興) around 4 PM that day. Hiring a sedan chair (步轎), I entered Xiuzhou Middle School (秀州中學). The acting principal, Mr. Shen (沈先生), was an acquaintance (情知) with whom I had visited back and forth while in Shanghai. That night, staying together with him in the dormitory (寄宿舍), we talked at length about something until twelve o'clock. He said something like this to me:
"Last winter, a person from your country came to this school. He had a topknot (상투) sticking out, and his wide-sleeved clothes (衣服) looked somewhat dirty. Moreover, his top-knotted hair smelled strongly of sweat (땀내), layered with dust (몬지)—truly very uncomfortable to look at. So, among your countrymen, how many still haven't cut their hair? Seeing compatriots in our region who still keep their Manchu queues, I always speak of the ruined race (亡國種族) of India. What was so precious to the Indians that they still keep their hair that way? I used to say this was one cause of their ruin (亡國 原因), and seeing that Korean person's topknot, it seemed almost like an Indian's. Wouldn't it be better to get rid of it? That person said he used the surname Shen (沈姓) and came here searching for his ancestors (祖先), telling me his story. Apparently, all the Sims (沈哥) in your country came from Jiaxing. So, partly because we might be of the same clan (同族), and partly because he was from your country, I felt some sympathy. He was very proficient (爛熟) in Classical Chinese (漢文). He said he believed in Confucianism (儒敎) and wore a white hat (白笠), explaining it was because of national mourning (國喪). Now, that person went to Haining (峽右 - Jiaxing से पश्चिम दक्षिण में करीब 200 ली दूर एक रेलवे स्टेशन स्थान) as a secretary (書記) for the student association, immediately cut his hair, and changed his clothes. Now he speaks some Chinese too. He said his hometown (生地) was Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道). Mr. Chang (張君 - likely referring to the author), do you know him?"
Hearing this, I thought for a while: that fellow who advertised (宣傳) his own country's undesirable customs (風俗) <123> himself is truly too absurd. I have seen with my own eyes how foreigners entering our country introduce all sorts of dirty and disgusting bad customs (惡風俗) to their own countries through photographs (寫眞) and porcelain ware (瓷器 나라 - lit. porcelain country, possibly referring to souvenirs). Now this person, finding photographs insufficient, has even exhibited the real thing (實物 出品)! At this, all sorts of thoughts connect. Years ago, I heard such a story. Someone compiled Korea's bad customs into a booklet (冊子) and propagated (宣傳) it abroad. Those caught in the hypnosis (催眠) of that propaganda thought, "Ah—Korea is truly inhabited only by strange things. Maybe it's like the Land of Giants (大人國) or the Land of Lilliputians (小人國). I wish I could see it—Of course, they wouldn't be beasts, would they? They wouldn't bite or kick, right? When can I see one and make a specimen (標本) for 20th-century博物學 (natural history/museology) knowledge?" And so, a missionary (宣敎師) who first came to Sŏnch'ŏn (宣川) reportedly even said such things while preaching (講道). Ah—what on earth is this? Of course, it's not that our compatriots lack undesirable customs, but if one compiles (綜合) them all one by one into a booklet, it could indeed seem that way. A needle-like word can become like a boulder, and a millet-grain-sized matter can become like a chestnut. Ah—blaming the blind man for the ditch (proverb: blaming others for one's own faults)—isn't everything one's own fault? This must surely be an iron whip (鐵鞭) or a wooden bell (木鐸 - warning) for our compatriots. After talking for a while, I looked at Mr. Shen and said weakly, as if resigned, "Well—the night has deepened, let's get some sleep." The next day, I took a boat, passed through Huzhou (湖州), saw Taihu Lake (太湖), and slept on the boat for a total of two nights, mooring at Wujiang (吳江) on the morning of the 21st. From there, the water was shallow, so the boat couldn't move on its own; people tied ropes to the end of the mast, slung the ropes over their shoulders, and chanting "Eo giyacha," pulled the boat upstream. This is truly the boat-trackers' song (欸乃聲) on land. Around 1 PM that day, we arrived outside Changmen Gate (閶門) in Suzhou (蘇州). Ah—Suzhou is the ancient capital (古都) of King Fuchai of Wu (吳王夫差). The city walls (城郭) are still imposing (宏壯), and the forests (樹林) are densely green (蒼鬱). "Outside Gusu city, Hanshan Temple; Midnight bell sound reaches the traveler's boat." (姑蘇城外寒山寺, 夜半鐘聲到客船) This truly is the ancient poem (古詩) about Suzhou I had always thought of and wanted to see. Donkeys (나귀) are common in Suzhou. Just going out on the street brings a clamor of requests to ride a donkey. Thinking, why not ride what they offer, I hopped onto a donkey's back and sought out Sutai Guan (蘇臺館 - Sutai Inn). Settling into official room (官房) No. 5, I rode the same donkey back out to Hanshan Temple (寒山寺). Ah—it's completely unimpressive. A monk selling rubbings of inscriptions (碑文) came out and greeted me warmly, but it was entirely different from the scenery (景致) I had imagined and longed for day and night. Moss (石苔) on the steps (堦下) was blackish; dust clung thickly to the ancient bell (古鍾). After drinking a cup of tea (茶), I immediately came back out. Riding the donkey straight back, I returned to the inn. After eating dinner and sitting out on the porch, around 7 PM, a troupe of courtesans (妓生隊) rushed in. "Ah—this is quite something!" Counting the number arriving, there were perhaps about 70 <124> or 80. Asking an attendant (下人) why they come to the inn like this, the attendant replied, "Speaking of our Suzhou, isn't it the foremost city of pleasure (色鄕) in China? That's why those women come to the inns at night to be overnight companions for guests." I casually asked, "Then do they receive money?" "Eh—of course!" "How much?" "Four silver dollars (四圓大洋) for one night." "Wow, that's really expensive. A fellow like me couldn't even dream of it." As I sat there, one of the passing women came and sat next to me, saying something I couldn't understand at all. Anyway, a woman's voice is certainly pleasant to hear. Of course, the meaning of her words seemed to be a proposition to spend the night together. When I held out my hand, she rushed closer, making quite a fuss. Uh—truly, is she a lotus-picking girl (採蓮女) of Jiangnan? Or a mulberry-picking girl (採桑女) under the moon? In writing, they read as so pretty and affectionate, but seeing them finally in person, no thoughts stirred, and instead, I felt nauseated. Unpleasant to look at, unable to understand their speech, and on top of that, their coquettish behavior—truly indescribable. Among them, those with distinct features and some romantic appeal didn't rush in so fussily but maintained a dignified attitude as they walked by, which was somewhat better to see, but the rest were all nothing. So I asked the attendant if this was all Suzhou had to offer in terms of looks. The attendant replied that among courtesans, there are various types (種類): yuanji (原妓), changsan (長三), yao'er (么二), yeji (野妓), and these were the yao'er type.

Only at 11 PM did they all retreat. Only then did I finally fall asleep.
The next day, I sought out Soochow University (東吳大學). My friend, Dai Enxi (戴恩錫君) from Hangzhou, took my hand and led me into his room. That afternoon, Dai Jun and I went sightseeing at Tiger Hill (虎邱山). Wei Lizhou (魏荔洲君), Zhang Fengqi (張逢騏君), and about ten others from Taowu Middle School (桃塢中學) formed a group (作隊), rowed a boat, and went gazing at Wangsutai (望蘇臺). The stone pagoda (石塔) stood tall, and ancient tombs (古塚) dotted the landscape—ah, this place is called Huqiu (Tiger Hill). The Kangxi Emperor's (康熙帝) plaque (扁額) and Yan Zhenqing's (顔眞卿) calligraphy (題字) still looked new; only counting fingers while facing the niche (坎中連 - obscure phrase, possibly referring to a posture or location?) containing a golden Buddha was pleasing to see. Wei Jun gave me a poem:
"Gija's (箕子) legacy, ancient country's custom; Distant guest from the East visits this place.
Sleeping tiger, dormant lion, when will they awaken? Singing of wheat and millet, sorrow stretches long." (箕子遺風古國古, 遠客東來此地游. 臥虎睡獅醒何日, 興歌麥黍恨悠悠)
I followed suit and responded in the same rhyme:
"All under Heaven are brothers; Why mind traveling the same path? Where face and heart are known; Mountains are high, waters flow long." (天下皆兄弟, 何妨同道游. 知面知心處, 山高水自悠.)
Carrying the setting sun, we returned to the guesthouse (客館), parting ways with Dai, Wei, and the group en route (途中). The next morning, packing my luggage (行李), I called a donkey, rode north towards Changbei (常北), boarded a wooden boat (木船), crossed the Yangtze River (楊子江), and left Tongzhou (通州) behind.

From Suzhou to Jinling (Nanjing)

Around 3 PM on the 24th, we reached Tiansheng Port (天生港). Disembarking swiftly at the wharf (碼頭), I boarded an automobile (自動車) and entered Tongzhou city. Tongzhou is the place of residence (居住處) of the renowned poet (in Classical Chinese) from our country, Elder Kim Ch'anggang (金滄江翁). Elder Ch'anggang's move (移寓) to Tongzhou came about through the general arrangement (周旋) of Zhang Jizhi (張季直 - Zhang Jian), who is the king of Tongzhou and a famous figure (名士) in China's business world (實業界). So, I sought out Elder Ch'anggang, and after exchanging greetings (寒暄), I asked about his affairs (所事). The Elder, stroking his white beard, wearing a worn-out gat (冠) on his small topknot, his thoroughly aged and withered body speaking with a strained voice, <125> said, "What would I be doing now? Just this bent body, still managing to run the Hanmorim Seoguk (翰墨林書局 - Han Mo Lin Bookstore). I have authored (著述) a few things." When it comes to others' writings, I have a disposition to follow them anywhere to read them and then not refrain from critique. One book authored by the Elder, "A Brief History of Korean Dynasties" (朝鮮歷代小史), sets the beginning era (紀元) with Gija (箕子) and dismisses the Tan'gun myth (檀君神話) entirely as absurd talk (荒唐). So I asked, "Why has it become like this?" The Elder replied, "Originally, speaking of Chosŏn (朝鮮), there is no value (價値) in discussing anything before Gija. Only from Gija's time did something called history begin." Therefore, he said, setting the origin with Gija, it is an unavoidable fact that it became a fiefdom (封地) of the Central Plain (中原 - China). Hearing these words, I was so dumbfounded that I simply responded to the Elder with one phrase, "Sir (령감), you are a Chinese sir. The Korean soul (朝鮮的 魂) seems to have completely disappeared." Without asking anything further, I just vaguely bade farewell, returned to the inn, spent the night there, and the next morning, went out to Tiansheng Port, boarded a boat bound for Hankou, went as far as Zhenjiang (鎭江), transferred to a small steamship (小輪船) in Zhenjiang, and arrived in Yangzhou (陽州) on the morning of the 27th. Passing Sīguān outside the South Gate (南門外絲關), I settled my lodging (寄宿所) at Quan'an Inn (全安棧) in the city center. Guided by an inn attendant (客棧 下人), I went sightseeing at Jinshan (金山 - Gold Mountain).
Hearing the words of old, "Riding a crane down to Yangzhou" (乘鶴下楊州), I had imagined Yangzhou to be quite amazing. Now that I've come, there are no notable friends to meet and no remarkable scenery to see. Among them, the only place barely worth attaching a rhyme character (韻字) to is the place called Jinshan. The historical sites (古蹟) visible here and there piqued my interest (興味) somewhat. Generally, what are called famous sights (名勝) or landscapes (風景) in the land of China are mostly just empty positions (虛位) left behind by the brush tips (붓끗알) of poets (詩人) or rhapsody writers (賦客), with nothing substantial.
Returning straight from there, I boarded a boat at 9 PM and headed down directly to Jinling (金陵 - Nanjing). At dawn on the 28th, the boat docked at Xiaguan Wharf (下關 埠頭). Boarding a train entering the city, seated inside, the railway track burst through behind Fengyi Gate (鳳儀門), and beside it, reeds (蘆花) waved vigorously. Getting off briskly at Zhongzheng Street (中正街), I entered Jinling University (金陵大學 - University of Nanking). My beloved friends P., B., K., H., and others met me inside the school gate, warmly grasping my wrists. Speaking of Nanjing (南京), our international students (留學生) number over 30, among whom 7 or 8 are female students (女學生).
That afternoon, after eating lunch, we went sightseeing at the Ming Tombs (明陵). On the slope of Zhongshan (鍾山 - Purple Mountain), with magnificent architecture (建築), all the techniques (技術) of the era were displayed. I stood tall atop this Zhongshan summit (頂上). All four directions (四眠) were visible. Behind, the Yangtze River (長江) flowed, and before, the plains (平野) stretched wide. Truly, this is the essence (本色) of the capital of the Six Dynasties (六朝王都). "On Phoenix Terrace, phoenixes roamed" (鳳凰臺上 鳳凰游)—now even its trace (瘍迹) is gone. "Two waters divide White Egret Isle" (二水中分白鷺洲)—it's just a small riverside pavilion (江亭). The flower boats (花舫) of the Qinhuai River (秦准) drift here and there following the waves. The bustle (雜遝) of the South Gate (南門) flashes like a picture. The clear water color of Mochou Lake (莫愁湖), the lingering mist of Yuhua Mountain (雨花山), the cannon emplacements (砲臺裝置) of Shizi Mountain (獅子山 - Lion Mountain), the imperial traces (帝王踪跡) of Jiming Temple (鷄鳴寺)—gazing intently until my eyes blurred, I pulled a fountain pen (萬年筆) from my pocket (포케트), opened my notebook (手帖), and unaware of the setting sun, pondered something deeply.
In the high sky, on the low earth, what fills them?
On the southern mountain, fallen leaves rustle 'ususū'; In the northern sky, returning geese cry 'jjururuk'.
Among them, even louder, my whistling sound 'ho-hyo-'
Ah! This is the Sound of Autumn (秋聲)! No, the Sound of Autumn in Jiangnan!!<126>

**Summary**

The author, a self-proclaimed wandering Korean scholar and painter ("江南賣畵廊" - Art Seller of Jiangnan), recounts his travels through China in late 1920, starting from Shanghai, moving through Hangzhou and Suzhou, and ending in Nanjing (Jinling). He describes scenic spots like West Lake, the Qiantang River tide, and sites in Suzhou and Nanjing, often weaving in classical Chinese poetry and historical anecdotes, such as the tale of the oil seller and the courtesan at West Lake. He details interactions with Chinese people, notably a lengthy discussion with students at Zhijiang University in Hangzhou about education, Sino-Korean relations, and national identity, and a disheartening encounter in Tongzhou with an elderly Korean scholar (Kim Ch'anggang) whose historical perspective the author finds overly Sinocentric. The journey mixes appreciation for Chinese landscapes and history with reflections on Korean identity, customs (like the criticized topknot), and the need for education and national spirit.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its portrayal of a Korean intellectual navigating China during the colonial period. The candid discussions with Chinese students reveal mutual curiosity but also underlying tensions regarding historical narratives (the 'subordinate' status debate). The criticism of the Korean scholar Kim Ch'anggang highlights internal debates among Koreans about history and identity. Furthermore, the encounter regarding the topknot and perceived 'backward' Korean customs, contrasted with the author's desire to promote Korea (like Kŭmgangsan), reflects the complex self-consciousness and external perceptions Koreans faced during this era. The mention of student activism (自治會) in China also provides context to the period's intellectual and political climate.

24日 午後 3時頃이라. 天生港을 다달앗다. 碼頭에 썩 내려서 自動車에 몸을 태워 通州城을 들어갓다. 通州에는 우리나라의 詩(漢詩)客으로 有名한 金滄江翁의 居住處라. 滄江翁이 通州로 移寓하게 된 것은 張季直의 一般 周旋으로 되엇는데 季直은 通州의 王이며 中國 實業界의 名士이다. 그래 滄江翁을 차저가서 寒暄을 畢한 뒤에 翁의 所事를 무른 즉 翁은 힌 수염을 슬슬 쓸면서 족으마한 상투 우에다 떨어진 冠을 쓰고 아조 다 늙엇고 삭은 몸이 힘드는 목소리로＜125＞ 「나는 지금 무엇을 하겟노. 그저 곱으라진 몸이 그러나마 아즉껏 翰墨林書局이라고 經營하여 가는데 내가 좀 著述한 것이 몃 가지 잇소.」한다. 나는 남의 著述이라 하면 어대까지던지 따라가서라도 보고는 곳 批判을 마지안는 性味가 잇다. 翁의 所著인 朝鮮歷代小史란 一書는 紀元을 箕子로 잡고 檀君神話는 아조 荒唐한 말로 비처버럿다. 그래 나는 뭇기를 웨 이러케 되엇습니까. 한 즉 翁은 말하되 原來 朝鮮이라함은 箕子 以前을 따저 말할 價値가 업는 것이오 오즉 箕子 때 와서야 비롯오 歷史라는 것이 생기엿다한다. 그래서  箕子를 紀元으로 잡는 同時에 不可不 中原의 封地가 되지 아니치 못할 것은 事實이라고 말한다. 나는 이 말을 듯고 어찌나 어이업섯던지 그저 한 마디로써 翁을 對하야 령감은 中國 령감이올시다. 다시 朝鮮的 魂이라고는 아조 업서젓습니다 그려.」하고 무엇무엇 무를 것 업시 그저 허수하게 作別을 하고 旅館으로 돌아와 그 날 一宿을 지나 그 이튼날 아참에 天生港을 나가 漢口가는 배를 타고 鎭江까지 가서 鎭江서 다시 小輪船을 換乘하고 陽州까지 가기는 27日 아츰이다. 南門外絲關을 지나 城中 全安棧에 寄宿所를 定하다. 客棧 下人의 引導를 입어 金山 구경을 간다.
옛 사람의 말을 듯고 乘鶴下楊州라 하야 매우 楊州를 놀랍게 알앗더니 이제사 와서 보니 알만한 친구도 업고 볼만한 경치도 업다. 그 中에 고작 韻字나 부틸만한 곳은 金山이라는 곳이다. 間間이 보히는 古蹟은 얼마쯤 나의 興味를 도두도다. 대개 中國 땅의 무슨 名勝이니 무슨 風景이니 하는 것은 모다 詩人이 아니면 賦客의 붓끗알에 아모 것도 업는 虛位를 남겨둔 것 뿐이다.
그 길로 돌아와서 밤 9時에 배를 타고 곳 金陵으로 내려간다. 28日 새벽에 下關 埠頭에 배를 대엇다. 城內로 들어가난 火車에 들어 안처 鳳儀門 뒤으로 툭 터저 들어가는 鐵道길 엽헤는 蘆花가 펄펄 난다. 中正街에 썩 내려서 金陵大學을 들어갓다. 나의 사랑하는 친구 P.B.K.H.等 諸君이 나를 校門안으로 마자 손목을 담뿍 잡는다. 南京으로 말하면 우리의 留學生이 30餘名에 達한다. 그 中 女學生이 7,8人이 되구요.
이 날 午後에 점심을 먹고 明陵구경을 가게 되엿다. 鍾山 허리에 宏壯한 建築으로 當代의 技術이란 技術은 모다 나타내엿다. 나는 이 鍾山 頂上에 웃둑 올나섯다. 四眠이 다 보인다. 뒤으로 長江이 흐르고 압흐로 平野가 질펀하다. 참말 六朝王都의 本色이 이러하다. 鳳凰臺上 鳳凰游는 지금에 그 瘍迹도 업고 二水中分白鷺洲는 족으마한 江亭이다. 秦准의 花舫은 물결을 좃차 이리저리,. 南門의 雜遝은 그림가티 얼른 얼른 莫愁湖의 맑은 물빗 雨花山의 느진 안개 獅子山 砲臺裝置 鷄鳴寺 帝王踪跡 한참 눈이 멀거니 바라보다가 「포케트」 속으로 萬年筆을 뽑아들고 手帖을 펼치며서 해 넘어가는 줄 모르고 무엇을 그리 생각한다.
