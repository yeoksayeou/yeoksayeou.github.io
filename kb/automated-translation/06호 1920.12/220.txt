讀者交情欄 (Readers' Exchange Column)

On this occasion of Chungyang Festival (重陽), I sincerely wish for the well-being (淸健) of all members of your esteemed company. Furthermore, I imagine the hardships you must endure in shouldering the heavy and great responsibilities in our difficult times and in maintaining the progress of the company's affairs. It is unbearable not to feel sympathy for all of you, who are outstanding and never yield despite a hundred setbacks (百折不撓). With sincere devotion stirring in my humble heart, and disregarding the shame of my foolish and clumsy learning (蠢蠢拙學), I dare to compose a few words and hereby present the manuscript on a separate sheet. I sincerely hope that the respected teachers will kindly correct any poorly written parts (杜撰之處) and insert it at the end of the column. It would be a great fortune, indeed a great fortune.
(Sanch'ŏng-gun 崔麟澤 Ch'oe In-t'aek)

I cry, "Ten thousand years for your esteemed company!" I say this not just because I am a devoted reader of Kaebyŏk, but among the dozens of magazines currently published in our Chosŏn, no matter how I search, there is none as complete in content or appearance as Kaebyŏk. If anyone knows of one, please say so! I am very pleased to be a devoted reader of such a magazine. I earnestly pray that you will ensure eternal blissful fortune (福榮) for our Chosŏn people.
(P'yŏngyang-bu 金潤華 Kim Yun-hwa)

To the respected teachers of the editorial department and all our fellow readers, are you all well? Time truly flows like a stream (如流), and the eventful and sorrowful year of Kyŏngsin (庚申年) is already passing, with not much time left before we greet the New Year. Let us strive even harder to greet a meaningful New Year. Once time passes, it never returns. If we do not do today what we must do today, we lose a day's work for our entire lives (平生). Let us strive hard by all means.
(Sunch'ŏn-gun 李聖在 Yi Sŏng-jae)

Everyone, fellow readers! Isn't it truly appreciated? I believe that the respected teachers of our Kaebyŏk magazine's editorial department have specially spared valuable space to create the Readers' Exchange Column particularly for those among the readers like us students. Among the devoted readers, especially we who are students, I think it is extremely good to utilize this Exchange Column for literature, opinions, or even our own works, apart from our school subjects (學科). Why don't you all make use of such a good platform (機關)? Let's utilize it much and read much. Especially our fellow student readers...
(Kyŏngsŏng 一學生 A Student) <105>

To all the respected teachers of the editorial department, are there truly so many new poets (新詩人) in what is called the new literary world (新文壇) of our Chosŏn? It's truly something I cannot understand. Perhaps those people think that if they just write something secretly and make it short, it's poetry. We students, lacking much knowledge (學識), might not know, but perhaps even all the respected teachers wouldn't know? It's truly laughable (可笑). On the other hand, why are there so few creative works (創作) of fiction (小說)? Is it perhaps because, besides the content needing to be meticulous (緻密), it's easy for others to understand, so they fear their own flaws (欠) will be readily exposed? And among the various magazines, it's truly impossible to know what qualifies as a novel. Among them, I believe that a creative work like "Hŭisaenghwa" (犧牲花 - Sacrificial Flower) in our beloved Kaebyŏk is a great harvest (收穫) among recent creations. Don't you, fellow readers, think so too? If not, please explain why not...
(Kyŏngsŏng 文藝 조하하는 學生 A Student Who Loves Literature)

Gentlemen and brothers (諸子諸兄)! The article in the November issue by Chŏng Tong-yun of Posŏng High Normal School (普成 高等普通學校), urging sympathy for poor students (苦學生), is truly plausible. You all must have seen that article, but you worthless rich people of the world! How is it that just because you were lucky to meet wealthy parents (父兄), you live in fine clothes and good food (好衣好食), burying yourselves headlong in taverns and brothels (酒肆靑樓), spending money like water (用錢如水), and losing your minds over a woman's words, ready to sell even your family estate (位士), yet you have not a shred (一分) of sympathy for the poor students studying by the light of fireflies and snow (螢案雪窓), trembling uncontrollably from hunger and cold? It's truly deplorable (可痛). Think about it. What are you? Didn't the ancient sages (聖賢) say, "To eat one's fill and dress warmly, living idly without instruction (飽食暖衣하야 逸居而無敎), is indeed close to being a beast (卽近於禽獸)"? Look at yourselves. What difference is there between you and beasts? Deplorable beings!
(Kyŏngsŏng 一苦學生友 A Friend of a Poor Student)

Among fellow readers, especially our young readers! Isn't there such a song (唱歌)? "Youth ages easily, and learning is hard to achieve (少年은 易老하고 學難成하니)". Don't just know it as a mere song and sing it; reflect deeply on its meaning. Its true meaning is profound. Our youth, our time to study, our time to learn – there isn't that much of it in our entire lives (人生一代). If that time passes in a blink, it is difficult to get back. Therefore, we must pull ourselves together (精神차려서) and not lose that precious time (時期). Our stubborn parents (父兄), unaware of the changes in the ways of the world (世態) and human feelings (人情), are immersed only in old customs (慣習) and try to control their own children (子弟), adhering only to the ways of the past (黙守). However, we should not just passively accept (甘受) what these elders say, but should think for ourselves, consider (叅酌) the elders' words and our own thoughts, and determine our path forward. There is a Western maxim (西洋格言) that an old man's words are without benefit, and also in our East (東洋), there's a saying that when one gets old, one becomes like a child. Elders think their children are their property or playthings to be controlled at will, but that is definitely not right. Even if one may have created their son's physical body (肉體), the soul (靈魂) belongs to the son, not to oneself. Therefore, trying to possess everything (統이 所有) seems unreasonable (非理) no matter how one thinks about it. Thus, for us who are the children of elders, it seems best to compromise (折衷) between the opinions of our parents and our own opinions, and advance forward harmoniously and cooperatively (合意的으로 調和的으로 前進).
(Wŏnsan 李學經 Yi Hak-kyŏng)

I am, in common parlance (俗談), a country bumpkin (시골무지렁이). Since my family's circumstances (家勢) were never affluent, I am not in a position (處地) like our young Kaebyŏk readers to go study abroad or in places like Kyŏngsŏng. However, just because I cannot afford to study, does it mean that as a human being, I lack the desire for knowledge (知識慾)? To the best of my ability, I obtain and buy things like lecture notes (講義錄) and magazines (雜誌). Recently, our Chosŏn magazines seem to have developed significantly compared to before, but one thing I find regrettable (遺憾) is that when looking at the contents of certain magazines among them, there are many so-called essays (論文) or scholarly articles (學術) <106> that one cannot distinguish (分揀) whether they are middle school compositions (作文) or actual essays. One might say this is because the level of knowledge in our Chosŏn is still somewhat immature (幼稚), but it would be good if those in charge of editing (編輯當務者) gave it some thought. I am not advertising myself as a devoted reader, but I declare (斷言) that among them, there is none as perfectly complete as Kaebyŏk. Whether it's mine or someone else's, what is good must be called good. Don't you all think so too? If you think otherwise, please teach me what is not so in next month's issue. For the sake of studying, let's engage in some debate (論爭) and research (硏究). To pass the time (消日) when bored.
(Haman 朴在範 Pak Chae-pŏm)

Regarding the Lifespan (壽命) of Kaebyŏk
Sanch'ŏng-gun 崔鹿澤 Ch'oe Nok-t'aek

At the very beginning of the newly established Readers' Exchange Column, I venture to pose the self-contradictory title "The Lifespan of Kaebyŏk," soliciting the perusal of the officials (當務者) of our Kaebyŏk Company and our fellow readers, hoping it might offer the slightest help (秋毫의 助) to their reflections.
A certain Western scholar (西洋의 某學者) said, "One can skip a day's meal, but one cannot forgo a day's newspaper," making it almost a common saying (俗語), such is the passion for knowledge that loves newspapers. If the advanced people of the West, hundreds of steps ahead of Chosŏn's level, are like this, then for us, it can be said that our urgent task (急務) in the present age is to necessarily read daily newspapers and magazines, even if it means possibly skipping up to ten days' meals. Then, how many people are currently reading newspapers and magazines in our Chosŏn? To know that, we must first know how many newspaper and magazine organizations (新聞雜誌機關) have been established (旣說).
Since the so-called Kabo Reforms (甲午維新), the newspaper and magazine organizations, presenting a childish spectacle (兒戲的 現狀) of being established yesterday and abolished today, founded in the morning and dispersed by evening, were subjected to absolute restrictions (絶對制限) on publication after the Japan-Korea Annexation (日韓合倂), and speech (言論), the constituent element of publication, also had absolute restrictions. Therefore, the silent (潛潛) and desolate (寂寞) Chosŏn publishing world and our sparse (寥寥) and unheard-of world of speech – truly, there was no one besides ourselves to sympathize. It has been like this for ten years. Since last year, our society, having been specially granted (特蒙) a partial allowance (一部 許與) of freedom of publication and speech, heard the sound of opening the long-sealed doors of the publishing world spread widely across the Peninsula (半島). Following this, like the responsive calls of frogs after the spring awakening (驚蟄), speech, filled with joy and delight, focused on publication, and all its activities concentrated there. Therefore, some say that the development of the publishing world is precisely criticism (非難); saying the development of the publishing world is criticism means that the activity of that society's speech world is vigorous (敏活). If there is a society whose activity of speech is dull, that society can be called a sick society (病的 社會). Then, that society needs the vigorous activity of speech. However, our past Chosŏn society was sick. Ah, receiving the heaven-sent restorative agent (蘇生劑) for that sick society, our Chosŏn society rejoices together and delights together.
Human psychological functions (心理的 作用) consist of four states: joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure (喜怒哀樂 四性). These functions are naturally restricted (天然的으로 制限) in their direct relationship with internal physical movements (內部 肉體的 動作). Therefore, when the four states of joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure act beyond their restricted scope, trouble (故障) inevitably arises in the internal body. Similarly, in our society, if joy and delight reach an excessive degree, it is difficult to avoid some kind of trouble arising, which is regrettable (遺憾) for us. <107>
Therefore, publications, the element of our social evolution (社會進化), and speech, the element of the publishing world – if the joy and delight of loving the society, aiming for the stage of these two, reach an excessive degree at the outset or during the process, trouble will arise in its course, which I believe you all commonly acknowledge (共認). The indefinite suspension (無期停刋) or temporary suspension (一時停刋) of certain newspapers, suffering the halt of publication rights, how much of a hindrance (故障) it must be to our social evolution, and how much of a setback (退步) it has become. However, although it was neither their intention (故意) nor anticipation (豫期), it only results in loss for the replenishment (塡補) of our society. Isn't it because they suffered blows from others that such losses were incurred? The reason for receiving those blows, regrettably, seems to be a lack of caution (注意). That caution, in any case, under restrictions (制限下), means nothing but self-reflection (省身). When sick (病), taking bitter medicine (쓴 藥) is not because the medicine is bitter, but because my illness is bitter. Since our Chosŏn publishing and speech world is still sick, taking bitter medicine for our illness, trembling with fear (戰戰兢兢), is the best policy (良策) for now. Our society is dreaming (夢中); to awaken from the dream, ringing bells and beating drums (鳴鍾擊鼓) are necessary. However, if one tries to ring the bell and beat the drum excessively, the bell and drum might surely break, or those nearby who have already awakened from the dream might find it unpleasant to hear and perhaps snatch away the drumstick and bell mallet. Who knows if that could happen? Alas (噫噫)! Our Kaebyŏk, which serves as the bell and drum to awaken our dream, if you are to arouse (喚起 - *note: original has a non-standard character, likely meaning 喚起*) us who are in a dream, please cherish (惜), love, and value the drumstick and bell mallet, and arouse us. I pray with a heart full of sincere blood (滿腔의 血誠) that you grant us its song with an eternal lifespan (永遠한 壽命) and place it in my hands with infinite lightness (無窮한 輕).

Autumn's Blunder (秋의 失態)
Ch'angnyŏng 朴炳燁 Pak Pyŏng-yŏp

Is it because time passes quickly (光陰이 易逝)? The calendar (曆書) already reports the 9th month of Kyŏngsin (庚申). Ah, Autumn! You are the one bearing the special name 'autumn' among the four seasons (四季) of the year. The abundant crops (豊滿한 穀物) in the fields high and low, far and wide (上下 廣野) – this sudden thought arises in me who praises your fine achievements (好蹟). Just looking at your achievements naturally makes one feel full. From the sacred great labor (神聖한 大勞働) of our farming brothers (農家兄弟) who endured toil (勤勞) since spring and summer (春夏), your honor (名譽) too has occupied the highest position (無上한 位). Human effort (人爲) is human effort, but the assistance (助力) of the Creator (造化翁) must also have played a part. This is just my observation (視査) of our local area through my narrow view (狹眼). This is a biased view (偏見). Not only do I lack the insight (識見) to discuss Eastern and Western foreign countries (東西外國), but what could I have seen of the various regions (諸方面) of the small domain (小局) of the Peninsula? Nevertheless, I wish to say a word now. Generally, autumn is not something separate. It is precisely the harvest season (收穫期) for countless grains (無量穀類), gradually brought about by tilling fields (田을 耕하며), sowing seeds (種子를 播하는), and all other kinds of effort (萬般의 努力), awaiting (俟) favorable winds and timely rain (順風調雨) from the Creator. Truly, autumn is a favorable time (好期) when indispensable (不可缺) food ingredients (食品原料) for humans (人類) and other animals (動物) are produced (産出), and accordingly, its merit (功) is great and wide. However, in certain regions (某某 地方) within our Peninsula, due to the recent hateful fierce wind and rain (惡風雨), the damage (被損) to all sorts of crops (凡百穀類) awaiting the autumn harvest (秋收), causing tragic harm (慘害) to our good compatriots (善良한 同胞) – how could it be small? What reason (理) is there to give death (死) to compatriots who diligently labored day and night (晝宵), pouring in money and effort (金力)? And the flourishing crops (油然穀物), waiting for the right time to be shared (共) as human food (食料), what evil (何惡) could they possess? Due to this, autumn's blunder (失態), humanity's misfortune (遭難) – it is a dreadful and regrettable (可恐可恨) matter. Anyway, this year, the situation is helpless (勢無奈). Ah, Autumn! Do not be discouraged (屈) in the slightest (少毫) by this year's failure. Keep moving forward (자꾸자꾸 前進), strive diligently (奮勵), properly advise (諫) the Creator, and display your strength (腕力) from next autumn (明秋) through a thousand autumns, ten thousand autumns (千秋萬秋), making it known to the world (世界), and do not cause hardship (困) in our future path (來程). We will brave ten thousand difficulties (萬難) and labor well.

Read Kaebyŏk! (開闢을 購讀하라)
Iksan 林丙熙 Im Pyŏng-hŭi

How slow, how slow it has been (遲哉遲哉)! Today, the initial path (初路程) of a new global enlightenment (世界的 新開闢) has arrived. As heaven and earth (天地) are remade, and the world (世界) is remade, Kaebyŏk-kun (開闢君 - Mr. Kaebyŏk/Respected Kaebyŏk), which guides the development (開發) of the motive force (動機) for remaking the spirit (精神) and remaking matter (物質), how can it be compared to telegrams and telegraphs (電報電信)? Who today would not celebrate and congratulate (贊祝) the new enlightenment (新開闢) of our 20th-century Chosŏn? Currently, even in the major metropolitan areas (大都會地), the sight (觀) of a portion of the student world (學生界) that has absorbed the new air (新空氣) devotedly reading (愛讀) Kaebyŏk is not absent, which is truly congratulatory (誠可致賀). However, today in the villages and hamlets (鄕里閭閻), the thought of purchasing and reading (購讀) Kaebyŏk is entirely absent (全無), such that even in a large village (大村) of considerable size (幾許), there is barely one person. This cannot be called the new person (新人物) of today's dawning era. Regardless of social class (士農工商을 勿論하고), one must devotedly read Kaebyŏk, which reforms the corruption (腐敗) of yesterday (昔日), seek the bright candle (明燭) of a guide (導師) in the perilous road's dark night (險路의 暗夜), and the responsibility (責任) of remaking the world in today's dawning new era (新時代) must deeply settle (深着) on individual shoulders (個人的 肩上). Only then, under the new epoch (新紀元下) today, 5,000 years later, can we recognize (自認) the dawn (曙光) of international peace (國際的 平和). How could this not be the fundamental duty (本分) of humanity? Alas (鳴呼)! Some people (或者) say they are not without the thought of subscribing to Kaebyŏk, but due to financial reasons (金錢關係), they cannot fulfill their intention (本意를 不遂). Could this statement possibly be true? That same person, when faced with wine and women (酒色), squanders (浪擲) several Won (數圓) of money like straw and dirt (如草芥), yet thinks of it as before. Can this truly be called a matter of financial relations? No (否)! It is merely due to a lack (索乏) of desire to subscribe. It is lamentable and deplorable (可惜可歎)! Here, I wish to offer one word (一言) to the stubborn people (頑固人) of our Chosŏn. Village scholars (村夫子), stubborn people! Living in the villages (村間), scattering sacred scriptures and wise sayings (聖經賢傳 헛터놋코), saying "Confucius says (孔子曰), Mencius says (孟子曰)" – what reasoning (理致) is there in that? Discard (消却) such corrupt thoughts (腐敗한 思想) at once (一時) and observe (觀察) the current situation (形便) as the spring wind (春風) turns. What kind of era (時代) is it now? It is the beginning (初) of a new era remaking the universe (宇宙). Youths (靑年) who will make Chosŏn prosper (繁榮)! By all means (부대부대), devotedly read Kaebyŏk and urgently (急急히) welcome (歡迎) and celebrate (慶賀) the new era of Kaebyŏk.

Hangul Song (Alphabet Song - 가나다운자가)

Yongch'ŏn 崔宗範 Ch'oe Chong-pŏm

Dearest youths, let's step by step advance,
Braving hardship and lament, work piled like clouds.
With earnest hearts, regardless of you or me,
Old and young, men and women, all compatriots, let's enjoy new happiness.
This fleeing time has no way to go slow,
Polish well morality, let's go out to the wide world.
When advancing, blowing trumpets, as if fluttering lightly,
Laboring and striving, whoever it may be, struggling hard.
In every village, every town, the spirit that fills the mind,
Fear not adventure and hardship, achieve whatever it may be.
I wish, our youth, strive hard and exert yourselves greatly,
Let's by no means fall behind the treasure-like civilized world.
Regardless of scholar, farmer, artisan, merchant, loving one another,
Discarding all trivial matters, let's save tens of millions of people.
In this beautiful world, our unerring peninsula,
Our land of embroidered rivers and mountains is something to boast of to the five continents.
Without boasting, strive hard, everyone doing their part,
Achieve good deeds, and enjoy day and night merriment.
Advancing step by step, the path is the same from beginning to end,
Strive harder in the first steps forward, awaken to the sound of the autumn wind.
Don't rely on childish strength, build the foundation and strive,
Tread firmly on rabbit hills and turtle mountains (토긔산긔 - possibly symbolic or regional term), advance resolutely.
Find the blessings God granted us,
Living splendidly, looking towards the future world.
Waves overturn, spreading is the new trend,
Don't harbor violent hearts, model after the blue sky.
With compassion like Carnegie's (카네기), singing Columbus's (커럼버쓰) song,
Compatriots sleeping and snoring, wake up with a bang! (쿠쿵쾅쾅) <109>

**Summary**

This collection from the "Readers' Exchange Column" of *Kaebyŏk* magazine (Dec 1920) presents letters and short pieces reflecting the concerns and aspirations of Koreans during the early colonial period. Readers praise *Kaebyŏk* as the best contemporary magazine, discussing the importance of reading, education, and intellectual development for national progress. Themes include social commentary on the plight of poor students versus the indifference of the wealthy, critiques of the contemporary literary scene (favoring novels over perceived simplistic poetry), generational conflict between youth seeking change and tradition-bound elders, and reflections on the restrictive publishing environment under Japanese rule. Two essays contemplate the magazine's "lifespan" under censorship, urging caution, and lament crop failures due to bad weather, while another piece urges widespread readership of *Kaebyŏk* for national enlightenment. The final piece is an acrostic Hangul song encouraging youthful striving and national pride. From a historical perspective, these texts vividly illustrate the burgeoning intellectual ferment, social consciousness, and nationalist sentiment among literate Koreans shortly after the 1919 March First Movement. They reveal anxieties about cultural development, social inequality, generational divides, and the challenges of navigating colonial censorship while striving for progress and self-awakening. Historians can gain insight into the role of print media like *Kaebyŏk* in fostering a modern national identity and public discourse under constrained circumstances.

讀者交情欄
際玆重陽하야 貴社僉位의 淸健하심을 心祝하오며 더욱 我難局에 重且大의 任을 担하시고 社務進行維持에 對하신 苦楚는 仰想不無矣. 百折不撓의 卓越하신 僉位의 同情에 難堪이올시다. 寸心에 뛰노는 血誠을 가지고 以蠢蠢拙學으로 不顧自無之愧하고 敢搆數語하야 玆以別紙原稿를 仰呈하오니 幸須 諸先生은 杜撰之處를 如除訂正하시와 欄末에 揷載하야주심을 千萬幸甚幸甚.
(山淸郡 崔麟澤)
貴社의 萬萬歲를 부름니다. 本人도 開闢의 一愛讀者라고 그리하는 말은 아니나 우리 朝鮮에서 現今 發行되는 幾十種의 雜誌 中에 內容이나 外形이 開闢가티 完備한 것은 아모리 헤알여보아도 업습니다. 누구던저 잇거던 잇다고 하시요? 本人은 이러한 雜誌에 한 愛讀者인 것을 퍽 질거합니다. 아모쪼록 우리 朝鮮民族에게 永遠히 幸福스러운 福榮이 잇도록 하여 주심을 간절이 비나이다.
(平壤府 金潤華)
編輯部 여러 先生님과 우리 여러 讀者諸氏여 萬福하십니까. 歲月이 참 如流하여 多情多恨한 庚申年도 이미 다 보내고 新年을 마질 때가 얼마나 남지 아니하엿습니다. 우리는 一層 더 奮發하여 意味잇는 新年을 맛도록 합시다. 歲月은 한번가면 다시 돌아오지 아니하나니 우리의 오늘 할 일은 오늘 하지 아니하면 平生에 하로일은 밋지고 들어가는 것이 올시다. 아모쪼록 힘써 합시다.
(順天郡 李聖在)
여러분 讀者諸氏여 참 고맙지 안습니까? 우리 開闢 雜誌 編輯部 諸先生님들이 特히 貴重한 紙面을 비여서 讀者交情欄을 만든 것은 讀者 中에도 特別이 우리 學生 가튼 이를 爲하야 만들어 주신 줄 압니다. 愛讀者 中 特히 學生된 우리들은 學校 學科 以外에 文藝라던지 意見이라던지 或은 自己의 作品을 이 交情欄을 利用하는 것이 至極히 조흔 줄 생각합니다. 웨 여러분은 이런 조흔 機關을 應用하지 아니합니까. 만히 利用하고 만히 보도록 합시다. 우리 愛讀者 中 特히 學生諸君이여...
(京城 一學生)＜105＞
編輯部 모든 先生님 우리 朝鮮 所謂 新文壇이라고 하는데 新詩人이 그처럼 만습니까 참 알수 업는 일이올시다. 아마 그 사람들은 詩라고 하는 것을 남 모르게 쓰기만 하고 짤막짤막하기만하면 詩인줄 아는 것이지요. 우리가튼 學生은 學識이 만히 업스니 或 모를는지도 모르거니와 아마 모든 先生님이 보셔도 모르겟지요? 참 可笑로운 일이올시다. 그 대신에 小說 創作은 어찌 그리 적음니까. 이것은 아마 內容이 緻密할 뿐 아니라. 남이 알아보기가 쉬우니까 自己의 欠이 곳 들어날가보아 그리하는 것이겟지요. 그리고 各 雜誌 中에도 小說이라고 하는 것은 참 무엇인지 알 수 업소. 그中에도 우리가 愛讀하는 開闢에 犠牲花 가튼 創作品은 近來 創作 中에는 큰 收穫인 줄 밋습니다. 讀者 여러분 中에는 그러케 생각을 하지 아니하십니까. 아니거던 아니라고 說明좀 해주시오...
(京城 文藝 조하하는 學生)
