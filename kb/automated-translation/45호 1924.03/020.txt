An Overview of Lao Tzu's Thought and its Trends
						Kim Chŏng-sŏl (金鼎卨)

The reason for arbitrarily changing the set topic "A Critique of Lao-Zhuang Thought" is due to the intention of limiting the scope of the issue. Taking up Lao-Zhuang as the problem would become more verbose than dealing with Lao Tzu alone, and furthermore, if one intends to critique it, there seems to be no ingenious plan to handle it simply, so it is my humble calculation to evade the responsibility for the critique. However, if I vaguely say "Lao Tzu's thought," doubts might arise about the content, so I add a word. The scope of the humble treatise I am dealing with this time should be seen in the narrowest sense – it does not delve into issues concerning Lao Tzu's scholarship and literature but merely briefly explains the gist of Lao Tzu's thought. Even that could not be done relatively clearly, so I have selected only the most crucial parts among them and tried to explain them. Consequently, it has become a sort of incomplete form, but there were unavoidable conditions. Firstly, the discussion regarding the method seems too long, and next, the part concerning the *trends (潮流) seems too short, which might appear comical, but in reality, I even thought of just writing a little about the method and stopping there – since I have already given up the critique and cannot introduce the whole picture, I thought it might be better to at least talk about the method for studying it. Moreover, in my humble opinion, not only do I believe in the need to emphasize method in studying scholarship or thought普段から, but recently, seeing Eastern and Western scholars who claim to study Lao Tzu (not just Lao Tzu, but Eastern studies in general) manipulate bizarre theories that would have made Lao Tzu die of shock if he were alive, I saw that, more than anything, the viewpoint, that is, the methodological *footing (立脚地), was wrong. I thought that revising the method would not be a futile effort (徒勞) if we are to organize it and guide future studies correctly. And my humble treatise mostly briefly presents scattered personal views held普段から, lacking a consistent thread (一貫畢竟 맥락). Please understand and read it as such. Also, the part about the trends became too short due to page limitations. And the diagrammatic chart (圖示表) was drawn temporarily, so errors are unavoidable. I intend to write in more detail next time and atone for my previous errors (前愆).

2, Lao Tzu's Way (道)
3, Trends of Lao Tzu's Thought

1. Method for Researching Lao Tzu's Thought
To cross the sea, one cannot leave behind ships and rafts (船筏), and to travel a long road, one cannot ignore carts and horses (車馬). If one tries to cross the sea using carts and horses <4> one will eventually not escape drowning, and if one traverses land routes using ships and rafts, one will eventually only meet the misfortune of capsizing (顚沛). Therefore, although the trap and snare (荃蹄) are not the fish and rabbit (魚兔), to get the fish and rabbit, one must first select the trap and snare; and although thought is not method, to know thought, one must first examine the method. If one interprets 甲's thought using an anti-甲 method, not only will one fail to understand 甲's thought, but even the anti-甲 method itself will be rendered meaningless. Conversely, just because an anti-甲 method is unsuitable for interpreting 甲's thought does not mean it is meaningless for interpreting anti-甲's thought. Looking at it this way, it wasn't that the anti-甲 method was wrong, nor that 甲's thought was false, but only due to the error in application, the method failed to function as a method, and the thought failed to be thought-like. Therefore, the method for thought is like ships and rafts for rivers and seas, carts and horses for land routes, and traps and snares for fish and rabbits; without method, there is no way to handle thought, of course, and even if there is a method (假使方法), if the way of using it is mistaken, it is worse than having none at all. However, saying "none at all" is in the context of not making thought an issue; as long as it is made an issue, it must necessarily exist, and as long as it exists, it must be used properly. ** Among those who have studied Lao Tzu's thought in ancient and modern times (今古), not everyone was wrong, but many failed to achieve a correct understanding (正解), and the reason for their errors mostly lies in the misuse of the trap and snare (筌蹄之錯用), and this misuse of the trap and snare seems most prevalent in contemporary evaluations. However, whether I have correctly seen others' errors is also questionable, and even if I have seen them correctly, merely seeing others' errors correctly does not easily become proof that I am not in error. Thus, I am not asserting that my view alone is the sole method for studying Lao Tzu's thought, but merely stating my intention of being unable to concur (參同) with those mistaken methods. And indeed, my conviction was to discuss a method that, at least for studying Lao Tzu's thought, would likely not be wrong. Now, the main material for studying Lao Tzu's thought is probably only the *Tao Te Ching* (道德經). (Other writings like the *Qingjing Jing* (淸淨經), *Huangting Jing* (黃庭經), *Ganying Pian* (感應篇) exist, but they are all forgeries (僞詐)). However, regarding the *Tao Te Ching* as well, there are discussions. Misinterpreting the phrase 『Lao [Tzu] and Peng [Zu] transmitted but did not create (老彭述而不作)』 and the text about non-action and naturalness (無爲自然), some say 『The *Tao Te Ching* is probably not Lao Tzu's original creation but merely a compilation of previous sayings (前言)』, which sounds plausible but should be known to be actually wrong. Why? Even if, as argued (或論), Lao Tzu's book was a compilation and not an original creation, looking at the consistent thread (脉絡) throughout the entire work (通篇), it perfectly forms the view of one school (一家之見) in itself. How could one <5> construct a system (體系) by merely gathering scattered legends, even if hundreds of piles (億百堆) were collected? To doubt the origin of Lao Tzu's book based on the phrase "transmitted but did not create" requires, first of all, learning how to read properly. And to suspiciously question (疑訝) Lao Tzu's authorship based on manipulating (詭弄) the phrases "non-action" and "naturalness" – this might serve as humor mocking (嘲諷) Lao Tzu, but it is clearly not the attitude of a rigorous textual critic (嚴正な考證家), because it must be a misinterpretation (錯解) of the subtle meaning (妙諦) of "non-action" and "naturalness". (*Regarding doubts about Lao Tzu's book*) Also, there are those who point out phrases found in Lao Tzu's book that exist in other texts (他書) and raise unnecessarily troublesome arguments, but while this serves as evidence (證跡) that Lao Tzu employed (襲用) previous sayings, how can the employment of phrases be a reason for the employment of the entire thought? This too is merely trivial chatter flowing from a childish view (穉見) that engages in sensationalism (好事立異) without conducting faithful research. Since this is not a textual critique of the *Tao Te Ching* (道德經辨), I cannot say everything that needs to be said, but I must premise that the *Tao Te Ching* is the sole classic (唯一典籍) discussing Lao Tzu's thought – because without correctly possessing the genuine source material (資料典籍) for studying Lao Tzu's thought, it would be like carving empty space (虛空을 雕刻). Now, regarding the misuse of methods by ancient and modern people in Lao Tzu research, there is no time (餘遑) to enumerate them one by one, so I will leave it aside for a moment. I believe that the most appropriate way is simply to savor (吟嘗) Lao Tzu himself and explain his thought. If I say this, perhaps skeptical arguments (懷疑觀的或論) like "That is also one school's view, this is also one school's view" might arise – generally, there are two ways (兩途) to study the thought of predecessors: either descriptive (祖述的) or critical (비평적). The former requires modeling (模則) the predecessor's own method, while the latter must be analyzed purely objectively (純客觀的)? This too is merely seeing one side of the shield (盾). Even if one analyzes purely objectively, one does not wield the scalpel (刀刃) of analysis recklessly; as long as one is analyzing the object of analysis, one cannot ignore (旡視) the nature of the object itself. If so, before criticizing it purely objectively, one must first clearly understand (瞭解) the nature of the object, and only then can one derive objective criticism in the true sense. Objective criticism that ignores (无視) this stage will produce nothing but delirious praise (譫語的稱송) or blind censure (맹목적譏刺). Therefore, to critique Lao Tzu, one must know Lao Tzu before critiquing; to know Lao Tzu, one cannot use an anti-Lao Tzu method; and to correctly understand (正解) Lao Tzu, one is permitted no other path (他途) than relying on Lao Tzu's own method. However, one point needs to be elaborated: the method of the person themselves who creates (創出) a thought and the method of studying that thought by modeling (模則) the thinker's own method are not necessarily identical. To see this with a concrete example (一個の實例), it can be particularly observed in religion (一個の思想 - a thought) and the philosophy of religion <6> (그 思想의 이론 - the theory of that thought). For instance, if a religious figure attained (体得햇다면) a certain religious mystery, internally or externally, consciously or unconsciously, there must have been some kind of practice method (수행방법). When a philosopher of religion critiques the rightness or wrongness (迷正) of that thought, the critic themself must also have a sympathetic savoring (共鳴的吟嘗) of the activity of that religious psychology, at least to the extent of being able to critique its rightness or wrongness. Sympathetic savoring does not necessarily mean having the identical sentiment (同一心情) as the person who created the thought, nor does it mean that only after sympathizing and becoming a follower (敎徒) can one possibly understand the thought. It just means that savoring that sentiment as a method is an important condition for studying that sentiment. Now, Lao Tzu's thought is indeed religious. At the same time, it is not non-philosophical either. Even if philosophical, it is religious philosophy, not scientific philosophy. No, originally it did not start from a scientific requirement, i.e., the intent to search for purely intellectual truth, but from an ethical requirement, i.e., embodying (體得) the ultimate meaning (究竟義) of practice, and the result was also in clarifying (闡明) the practical ultimate meaning. The practical truth thus embodied and clarified was, so to speak, religious. Therefore, in studying his thought, comprehending (領會) the meaning he clarified and concurrently savoring (吟嘗) the sentiment he embodied is the sole true transmission (唯一眞傳). If one truly comprehends the meaning and savors the sentiment, even if not Lao Tzu himself or his follower, one should be able to understand what kind of thought it is, and at the same time, grasp the foundation for true objective criticism. To do so, one must first savor the essential points of the method (法要) indicated by Lao Tzu himself:
It is said - Those of old who were skilled practitioners (士) were subtle, mysterious, profound, and penetrating (微妙玄通); their depths (源) could not be fathomed. Because they could not be fathomed, one strains to describe their appearance (强爲之容). Cautious (豫兮), like crossing a deep [river] in winter; vigilant (猶兮), like fearing neighbors on all sides; reserved (儼若客), like a guest; yielding (渙若氷將釋), like ice about to melt; simple (敦兮其若樸), like uncarved wood; open (曠兮其若谷), like a valley; murky (渾兮其若濁), like muddy water... Those who preserve this Way (道) do not desire fullness (不欲盈)... Also said - Attain utmost emptiness, hold fast to stillness (致虛極守靜篤) - Returning to the root is called stillness (歸根曰靜); stillness is called returning to destiny (靜曰復命); returning to destiny is called constancy (復命曰常); knowing constancy is called enlightenment (知常曰明)... Also said - Those who know do not speak; those who speak do not know (知者不言, 言者不知). Block the openings (塞其兌), close the doors (閉其門), blunt the sharpness (挫其銳), untangle the knots (解其紛).... Also said - Act without acting (爲旡爲), manage affairs without managing (事旡事), taste without tasting (味旡味).... Also said - My words are very easy to understand, very easy to practice (吾言甚易知, 甚易行); [yet] none in the world can understand, none can practice (天下莫能知, 莫能行). Words have a source (言有宗), affairs have a master (事有君). Only because of non-knowing (夫惟旡知)... Also said - Carrying the spiritual and sentient souls, embracing the One (載營魄抱一), can one avoid separation (能旡離)? Concentrating the vital breath (氣) to achieve softness (專氣致柔), can one be like an infant (能嬰兒)? Cleansing the mysterious mirror (滌除玄覽), can one be without flaws (能旡疪)?... Opening and closing the gates of Heaven (天門開闔), can one be receptive (能爲雌)? Understanding penetratingly in all directions (明白四達), can one be without knowing (能旡知)?... Also said - To know that one does not know is best (知不知上); not to know that one knows is a sickness (不知知病)... Also said - Trustworthy words are not beautiful; beautiful words are not trustworthy (信言不美, 美言不信). The good are not argumentative; the argumentative are not good (善者不辨, 辨者不善). The wise are not learned; the learned are not wise (知者不博, 博者不知). The sage does not hoard (聖人不積)... Also said - (Words told to Confucius according to *Shiji* [史記])... A gentleman of great virtue (君子盛德) has an appearance like a fool (容貌如愚)... [End of] brief selection.
How could the sky be anything other than one? Yet, the reflection of the sky in rivers and seas (江海), ponds (池塘), <7> puddles (蹄涔) appears differently in each. Similarly, truth is not inherently diverse, but it cannot lack distinct characteristics depending on its object. Therefore, although the Way (道) is One (一), "the benevolent see it and call it benevolence, the wise see it and call it wisdom (仁者見之爲之仁智者見之爲之智)," meaning that even for truth, the method must differ according to the respective issue. ...Lao Tzu's Way is more practical truth (實踐的眞理) than scientific truth, and even among practical truths, it lies more in clarifying the profound principle that transcends words and thoughts (離言絶慮之玄理) than in establishing the rules and standards (規矩繩墨) of rituals and laws (禮儀法度). Therefore, in clarifying the profound principle that transcends words and thoughts, one cannot misuse the scientific method of verbal explanation and discrimination (言詮識別), nor can one *add (添加) the legalistic categories (禮法的範疇) of rules and standards. To clarify the profound principle that transcends words and thoughts, one can only rely on the profound and silent inner contemplation (玄黙な內照) that transcends words and thoughts. Since it was attained (證得) from the beginning through profound and silent inner contemplation that transcends words and thoughts, the message (消息) of the scenery (地風光) that one cannot bear to speak of (不忍說道) should not tolerate even one word, and holding even one thing is wrong. How much more so is trying to grasp this through the fine work (細工) of arrangement and deliberation (安排擬議) anything other than the absurdity (환희) of trying to envelop (包羅) the vast emptiness (太空) with your family's broken net (君家의 破網)? Ha ha! Did the pale-faced old man (蒼顔老夫) of Quren County in Chu (楚苦縣) not once preach five thousand words (五千言)? But how could the meaning beyond the five thousand words (五千言言外의 趣) be captured by Cangjie's (蒼頡) bird-track-imitating shapes (鳥跡模形)? Those five thousand words are truly the single word (一言) of the superior person (上士); that single word is truly the wordlessness (旡言) of the Great Way (大道); and the wordless Great Way is capable of being the true source (眞源) of words (有言). If it accommodates one word, how could it be limited to only five thousand words? It can accommodate ten thousand words, a hundred million words, indeed, countless words (旡量數言). Therefore, to comprehend the subtle meaning (妙諦) of countless words, grasping the essential pivot (樞要) of the five thousand words is acceptable. To penetrate (照破) the profound opening (玄竅) of the one word, entering into realization (證入) in the actual state (實地) of wordlessness is acceptable. To grasp the essential pivot of the five thousand words, and to enter into realization in the actual state of wordlessness, carefully reading (정독) the five thousand characters (五千文) of the *Tao Te Ching* is acceptable, chanting (念誦) it is acceptable, pursuing its meaning (追窮) is acceptable. Through profound and silent diligent practice (淵深玄黙한 정진), if one attains (軆達) the realm beyond the five thousand characters (五千文文外之地), then Boyang, the old fellow's (伯陽老漢), horizontal and vertical talks (橫說竪說) will all just be one's own commentary (自家의 註脚). Now, the tone of my words seems to have momentarily drifted into excessiveness (矯激), but faced with the current malpractice (時弊) of forced interpretation (牽文曲釋) without examining the trap and snare (筌蹄), perhaps I cannot help but speak this way. Furthermore, to critique Lao Tzu, one must fairly observe Lao Tzu's own standpoint (立脚地); to fairly observe that standpoint, one must meticulously savor (愼密하게 吟嘗) the meaning and method of the thought itself; and to savor that, I believe the best method (최상방법) is, as far as possible, to personally realize (軆認) Lao Tzu's own mysterious Way (道妙)... Only then will the right to critique Lao Tzu's thought from any viewpoint be established.

2. Lao Tzu's Way (道)
The Way (道) that can be spoken of (道可道) is not the constant Way (非常道); the name that can be named (名可名) is not the constant name (非常名). Namelessness (旡名) is the beginning of Heaven and Earth (天地之始); naming (有名) is the mother of all things (萬物之母). Therefore, constantly without desire (常旡欲), one observes its mystery (觀其妙); constantly with desire (常有欲), one observes its manifestations (觀其徼). <8> These two (此兩者) emerge together but have different names (同出而異名); together they are called profound (同謂之玄). Profound and again profound (玄之又玄), the gateway to all wonders (衆妙之門). All things arise from it yet are not possessed (萬物作焉而不有) [by it]; it gives life but does not own (生而不有); it acts but does not rely (爲而不恃); it accomplishes merit but does not dwell on it (功成而弗居). Precisely because it does not dwell (夫惟弗居), therefore it does not depart (是以弗去). The Way is empty (道冲), yet using it, it seems inexhaustible (用之若不盈). Deep (淵兮)! Like the ancestor of all things (如萬物之宗)... Soften its light (和其光), identify with its dust (同其塵). Deep (湛兮)! As if it constantly exists (似若存). I do not know whose child it is (吾不知誰之子); it seems to precede the Emperor (象帝之先). The Valley Spirit never dies (谷神不死); it is called the Mysterious Female (是謂玄牝). The gate of the Mysterious Female (玄牝之門) is called the root of Heaven and Earth (是謂天地根). Continuously (綿綿), as if existing (若存); use it, it is never exhausted (用之不勤)... The reason Heaven and Earth can last long (天地所以能長且久者) is because they do not live for themselves (以其不自生)... Thirty spokes share one hub (三十輻共一穀); it is the space within (當其旡) that allows the cart's use (有車之用). Clay is molded into a vessel (埏埴以爲器); it is the space within (當其旡) that allows the vessel's use (有器之用),... Therefore, having provides utility (有之以爲利), non-having provides function (旡之以爲用). Looked at but not seen (視之不見), named the elusive (名曰夷); listened to but not heard (聽之不聞), named the faint (名曰希); grasped at but not held (搏之不得), named the subtle (名曰微). These cannot be fully fathomed (不可致詰故), thus they blend into One (混而爲一). This is called the form of the formless (是爲旡象之象). Meeting it, one does not see its head (迎之不見其首); following it, one does not see its back (隨之不見其後). Holding the Way of antiquity (執古之道) to manage the present existence (以御今之有). By managing the present existence (以御今之有), one can know the ancient beginning (能知古始). This is called the guideline of the Way (是爲道紀). The capacity of profound virtue (孔德之容) follows only the Way (惟道是從). The Way as a thing (道之爲物) is only fleeting, only elusive (惟恍惟惚). Elusive and fleeting (惚兮恍兮), within it there is form (其中有象); fleeting and elusive (恍兮惚兮), within it there is substance (其中有物). Deep and obscure (窈兮冥兮), within it there is essence (其中有精); this essence is very real (其精甚眞), within it there is faithfulness (其中有信). From ancient times to the present (自古及今), its name has not departed (其名不去)... There was something undifferentiatedly formed (有物混成), born before Heaven and Earth (先天地生). Silent and vast (寂兮寥兮), standing alone and unchanging (獨立而不改), circulating everywhere without tiring (周行而不殆). It can be the mother of the world (可以爲天下母). I do not know its name (吾不知其名); I style it the Way (字之曰道). Forced to give it a name, I call it Great (强爲之名曰大),... Heaven models itself on the Way (天法道); the Way models itself on naturalness (道法自然). The Way constantly does nothing (道常旡爲), yet there is nothing it does not do (而旡不爲)... Heaven attained the One to become clear (天得一以淸); Earth attained the One to become tranquil (地得一以寧); spirits attained the One to become numinous (神得一以靈); valleys attained the One to become full (谷得一以盈); all things attained the One to live (萬物得一以生); rulers attained the One to govern the world correctly (候王得一以爲天下正). Their attainment is the One (其致之一也)... The Way produces One (道生一); One produces Two (一生二); Two produces Three (二生三); Three produces all things (三生萬物)... The Way gives them life; Virtue (德) nurtures them (道生之德畜之)... The Way is the sanctuary (奧) of all things (道者萬物之奧), the treasure of good people (善人之所寶), the refuge of the not-good (不善之所保)... I have three treasures (我有三寶) which I hold and cherish (持而保之): first is compassion (一曰慈), second is frugality (二曰儉), third is not daring to be foremost in the world (三曰不敢爲天下先). Because of compassion, one can be courageous (慈故能勇); because of frugality, one can be generous (儉故能廣); because of not daring to be foremost, one can become a vessel of leadership (不敢爲天下先故能成器長)...
Lao Tzu's thought can be glimpsed in his five-thousand-word *Tao Te Ching*, and the gist (要旨) of those five thousand words lies in the single character 'Do' (道). All five thousand words from beginning to end are merely descriptions (形容) of the single character 'Do'. However, although the description of that single character 'Do' amounts to five thousand words, its ultimate destination (歸趣) finally lies in 'Mu' (旡 - non-being/nothingness), 'Muwi' (旡爲 - non-action), 'Muwi i mu bulwi' (旡爲而旡不爲 - non-action yet nothing is undone), 'Mother of All Things' (萬物母), 'Beginning of Heaven and Earth' (天地之始), 'Form of the Formless' (旡象之象), 'Emptiness' (虛), 'Not Dwelling [on merit]' (弗居), 'Valley Spirit' (谷神), 'Purity and Stillness' (淸靜), 'Naturalness' (自然)... It lies in 'Mu' and 'Naturalness'. But what kind of 'Mu' is Lao Tzu's so-called 'Mu'? There are various kinds of 'Mu'… There is a negative 'Mu' (否定的 旡) that negates some kind of 'Yu' (有 - being/having)… Absolute non-existence (絶旡). Also, even if affirming the existence of some 'Yu', there is a 'Mu' in the sense of negating its value (價値性)… 'Mu' of no value (旡價値의 旡). Also, while affirming both some existence (存在性) and value (價値性), there is a 'Mu' in the sense of negating relative value (相對的價値性)... 'Mu' as a principle/form (理象으로서의 旡). Let's say the first is physical (廣義 - broad sense) 'Mu', the second is negative 'Mu' in thought (思想의 消極的 旡), and the third is positive 'Mu' in thought (思想의 積極的 旡). The first is used in our daily language when negating the existence of some event, object, or principle (理趣), meaning simply that it is not there. The meaning of the world and life… thoughts that are pessimistic about the existence of the universe and human effort, seeing only the dark side (暗黑面) of self-abandonment (自棄) <9> and destruction (破滅), such as world-weariness and pessimism (厭世悲觀), nihilism and disillusionment (虛旡幻滅主義), or even if not going to such extremes entirely, it is generally used when negating the value of things. The third is the meaning of negating the relative existence (相對的 存在性) while affirming the existence of some 'Yu'... relatively 'Mu', only absolutely 'Yu'. That is, if truth is non-dual (不二), it affirms the existence of truth while negating its multiplicity (雜多性). If viewed only from the aspect of negation, truth is not yet 'Yu'. However, while rejecting multiplicity by saying 'Truth is non-dual', the affirmation of the absoluteness (絕對性) of truth is included. Therefore, it refers to 'Mu' in the sense of negating the false appearance (假相) in order, not to negate the existence of some thing, but rather to describe its true reality (眞相)... A kind of 'Yu' described as 'Mu'. Furthermore, not only does it not negate its existence, but it further affirms its value, yet negates relative values that are temporarily present and suddenly absent (暫有忽旡). It is 'Mu' in the sense of negating all pseudo (似而非) and illusory values (假相的 價値) in order to affirm the value of the absolute, non-dual, eternal, ever-existing (絕對 不二 永恒 常存)... A kind of 'Yu-ka-chi' (有價値 - valuable thing) described as 'Mu-ka-chi' (旡價値 - valueless). Seen this way, the third 'Mu' cannot but be called 'Mu' that is not 'Mu', i.e., 'Mu' as Absolute Reality (絶對實有). The greatest representative thought asserting 'Mu' in this sense would be Buddhism (佛敎), and within Buddhism, Mahayana (大乘敎) (though Hinayana's [小乘] Nirvana [涅槃], in a sense, should not be viewed entirely negatively, it might not be impossible to treat it under the second meaning). And within Mahayana, the Prajnaparamita school (般若門) would be even clearer... The perspective (眼目) of the Prajnaparamita school is 'smashing forms to reveal emptiness' (破相顯空). The emptiness (空) advocated by the Prajnaparamita school is not stubborn emptiness (頑空), unrecordable emptiness (旡記空), or emptiness of sinking and stagnation (沉空滯寂之空), but emptiness in the sense of the ultimate true reality (究竟的眞相) of all phenomena (萬法), i.e., 'True Emptiness' (眞空). This True Emptiness affirms both its realizability as an ideal (理想) and its absoluteness as value. There are people in the world who misunderstand the Buddhist teaching of emptiness (佛家의 空法) and judge it as a self-destructive view (自滅觀) of negative anti-value, but that is wrong. Even if one could argue that True Emptiness is merely a meaningless concept (旡意義한 관념), one should critique it only after correctly understanding the meaning of True Emptiness itself (眞空自軆). Now, Lao Tzu's 'Mu' is also the 'Mu' of the third meaning. It does not mean that things and principles/trends (理勢) do not exist in heaven, earth, and among humans, nor does it mean that although these things and principles/trends exist, they are all worthless. It only means that the origin (本源) of all beings (諸有) is 'Mu', the deep source (淵泉) of all principles (萬理) is 'Mu', the essential subtlety (要妙) of affairs and trends (事勢) is 'Mu', and the true secret (眞訣) of conducting oneself in the world (處世) is 'Mu'. 'The nameless beginning' (旡名之始) and 'the formless shape' (旡狀之狀) describe the first aspect; 'the image of the imageless' (旡象之象) and 'prior to the Lord on High' (象帝之先) describe the second aspect; 'using non-being for function' (旡之以爲用) and 'acting without acting, yet nothing is undone' (旡爲而 旡不爲) describe the third aspect; and 'precisely because one does not dwell [on merit]' (夫惟弗居) and 'sharp but not cutting' (劌而不\*耀 - \*likely 劌而不\*傷 'sharp but not injuring' or similar meaning) describe the fourth aspect. Some people misinterpret Lao Tzu's 'Being is born from Non-being' (有生於旡), saying that 'Yu' being born from 'Mu' is an inverted view (倒見), and also that distinguishing between 'Yu' and 'Mu' shows lack of attainment (未達). However, that itself is truly an inverted view. That Lao Tzu's 'Mu' is 'Mu' in the sense of 'uncallable name, unseeable form' (不可名相 <10>, 不可形容), i.e., the third meaning described above, is something stated so clearly (審言) that it would be a miracle for anyone who has read the *Tao Te Ching* not to know. And the saying that seeing 'Yu' and 'Mu' as two shows lack of attainment is itself truly 'lack of attainment'. Lao Tzu's 'Mu' should by no means be considered 'Mu' in the sense of temporal development—developing from 'Mu' to 'Yu'. It does not mean that 'Mu' develops into 'Yu', and once 'Yu' comes into being, 'Mu' disappears. It means that the produced 'Yu', i.e., Heaven, Earth, and all things (天地萬物), are transformed (化成) from the producing 'Yu', i.e., 'Mu'. Thus, 'Yu' truly cannot exist apart from (離) 'Mu'. Therefore, 'Yu' and 'Mu' are mutually inseparable (相即不離), and at the same time, the former is the phenomenal aspect (現象的一面) of the latter, and the latter is the essential aspect (本體的一面) of the former. And 'elusive and fleeting, deep and obscure, profound and again profound' (怳忽窈冥玄之又玄) are all descriptions of this 'Mu'; 'Valley Spirit' (谷神) and 'Mysterious Female' (玄牝) are all different names (異名) for this 'Mu'. 'Valley' (谷) has the meaning of emptiness (虛), and 'Spirit' (神) has the meaning of numinous subtlety (靈妙). 'Mu' is truly elusive, faint, and subtle (夷希微), unable to be seen by the eye, heard by the ear, or touched by the hand. However, it is not that it does not exist at all; it exists elusively, fleetingly, deeply, obscurely, numinously, and clearly (怳忽窈冥靈靈昭昭). This is also called 'the form of the formless' (旡象之象). The 'form of the formless' is the substance (本體) of all formed things (有象). 'Mysterious Female' (玄牝) is also identical in substance (同實) to the 'Valley Spirit'. 'Mysterious' (玄) is a description of the unfathomable (不可致詰) nature of being 'Yu' yet not 'Yu', being 'Mu' yet not 'Mu'. 'Female' (牝) is the 'pin' of 'pin-mo' (牝牡 - female and male), i.e., female. However, it is not female in the sense of a wife (妻性), nor female in the sense of a daughter (女息), but female in the sense of 'the feminine' itself (女性인 여성). That is, it corresponds (相同) to the phrase 'Mother of All Things' (萬物之母), also describing the generative power (能産) of 'Mu'. Now, this 'Mu', existing in the universe, becomes the substance of the transformation of all beings (萬有化成); existing in humans, it becomes the subtle method (妙法) for cultivating the mind and rectifying the self (修心正己), and the ultimate way (至道) for regulating the family and governing the state (齊家治國). Practicing this 'Mu' is 'Muwi' (旡爲 - non-action). 'Muwi' is not 'Buwi' (不爲 - not doing) which opposes doing, but the practice of 'Muwi', i.e., the 'Muwi' of 'Mu bulwi' (旡不爲 - nothing is undone). Practicing the 'Muwi' of 'Mu bulwi' is practicing through the Way (道). Ignorant of this subtle point (這間消息), many groups (徒輩) in ancient and modern times have misunderstood Lao Tzu's 'Muwi', deceiving themselves and others (自誑誑人), and there has been no lack of unjust attacks (不當한 공격). Then, what is it like to practice the 'Muwi' of 'Mu bulwi'? According to Lao Tzu's view, 'Wi' (爲 - action) has the meaning of leftover food and burdensome actions (餘食贅行), sharpness (銳), cutting (劌), falsehood and pretense (情僞), deceptive appearances (貌詐), deliberate action (作爲), self-attachment (我執), arrangement and deliberation (安排擬議), cleverness and deliberate contrivance (巧黠刻意), polishing conduct (繕行), forming intentions and dispositions (成心作性), engaging and struggling (與接爲構), daily mental strife (日以心鬪), contention (紛競), etc. 'Muwi' (旡爲), negatively, means stopping (止之) such 'Wi'; positively, it means attaining utmost emptiness (致虛極), holding fast to deep stillness (守靜篤), observing the mystery (觀其妙), being continuous like existence (綿綿若存), being empty yet inexhaustible in use (冲而用之若不盈), not forgetting yet not forcing growth (勿忘勿助長), releasing without dissipation (放而不肆), holding without stagnation (執而不滯), stillness, stability, and holding the center (靜定守中). All these clarify the subtleties of employing the mind (用心の機微) and the secret principles of nurturing nature (養性の秘旨).
Not acting (不爲) is 'Wi' (爲), acting 'Muwi' (爲旡爲). Practicing 'Muwi' is not opposing 'Wi' to practice 'Buwi' (不爲), but refraining from 'Wi' and practicing 'Muwi'. 'Wi' (in the sense described above) is the trigger (機) of misfortune and evil (禍惡); 'Muwi' is the subtlety (妙) of superior virtue (上德). 'Wi' is the error arising from 'Yu' (有); 'Muwi' is precisely the function (作用) of 'Mu' (旡). Just as Lao Tzu's 'Mu' is not a death-like, negative 'Mu', but 'Mu' as a kind of absolute principle/form (絕對 理象) <11>, 'Muwi' is by no means idleness and dissipation (逸遊浪散), indulgence and license (放縱姿肆), or destroying the body and extinguishing knowledge (灰身滅知). Rather, without the reality (實) of diligent effort (精進力行), closing off heterodoxy and preserving sincerity (閉邪存誠), simplicity, tranquility, harmony, and purity (冲淡和粹), it is not practicing 'Muwi', but rather not practicing 'Muwi' and practicing 'Wi'. Therefore, the likes of the so-called unrestrained scholars of the Jin Dynasty (晉代狂士) are actually, from Lao Tzu's original intent (本旨), even further removed (肖遠) than Han Yu, Duke of Wen (文公韓愈), who vehemently attacked Lao Tzu. And the claim that the so-called destructive nihilism (파괴적 虛旡主義) of recent days is identical (相同) to Lao Tzu's principle of emptiness and non-action (虛旡旡爲之旨) is even more bizarrely unfounded (詭誕旡稽) talk than the story that Bi Zhuo of the Ministry of Personnel (吏部畢卓) was Lao Tzu's successor disciple (傳鉢門人). Furthermore, Lao Tzu sometimes described 'Muwi' with other words like 'self-transformation' (自化) and 'naturalness' (自然). The misunderstanding of his so-called terms 'self-transformation' and 'naturalness' is exactly like the misunderstanding of 'Mu' and 'Muwi', distorting the original intent. 'Muwi' is the subtlety of the function of 'Mu'; 'self-transformation' and 'naturalness' can be alternative names for 'Muwi', or refer to the virtuous influence (德化) of 'Muwi', or the actual traces (實跡) of 'Muwi'. Therefore, if one comprehends the essential subtlety (要妙) of 'Mu' and 'Muwi', one can readily grasp (旁通) the subtle meaning (微旨) of 'self-transformation' and 'naturalness'. However, some people seem to interpret Lao Tzu's 'naturalness' as a principle of licentious behavior (放肆無行), or in the sense of the natural world of natural science (自然界 自然科學的 自然), or in the sense of innate endowment or instinct (生得, 本能). Thus, there are crooked scholars (曲學) who pull out unrelated famous names (人名), using colloquialisms (상말로), and make claims of similarity or resemblance, unable to stop at mere words and even writing them down with a brush. Contrary to the first interpretation, Lao Tzu's naturalness is rather ascetic (禁慾的); contrary to the second interpretation, Lao Tzu's naturalness is rather mystical (神祕的); contrary to the third interpretation, Lao Tzu's naturalness is rather idealistic (理想的). In short, Lao Tzu the person (爲人) was religious; his religion was philosophical; his philosophy was mystical. Therefore, his thought, i.e., the Way (道), started from mystical belief (神祕的 信念), clarified the ultimate meaning of life, i.e., practical truth (實踐的 眞理), and realized (體覺) the secrets of the universe (宇宙の秘奧). Just as Lao Tzu the person was more of a sage (聖者的) than a scholar (學者的), Lao Tzu's thought is more ethical (倫理的 - broad sense) than academic (學術的). Therefore, his views on religion or morality all expound the essentials of practice (實踐の要旨)—indicating the practical subtle methods (實際的 妙法) for cultivating mind and nature (心性 修養), not analytical explanations (分析的 說明) of mind and nature themselves. His cosmology (宇宙觀) and social view (社會觀) also lament their fate and secrets (運命と秘密を詠歎), or state ideals for their practical handling (實際的 處理), rather than investigating the structure of the world itself (世界自體の組織) or the principles of society itself (社會自體の原理). This is generally true for ancient philosophers and their scholarship, and furthermore, Eastern philosophy is all strongly colored this way, so why single out Lao Tzu? However, putting aside his lighter and shorter aspects and taking up his heavier and longer aspects, viewing him this way is most fitting (切當). In any case, Lao Tzu was a philosophical sage (哲人的 聖者) who, realizing himself (自證) in the subtle realm (妙境) of tranquility and non-action (恬靜旡爲), profound silence and deep mystery (玄黙淵深), constantly enjoyed (常樂) the eternal silent light (永恒の寂光). And his statecraft (經綸) also consisted solely in revealing (開示) to humanity the <12> subtle truth (妙諦) he himself had embodied (軆得)—his thought was his own inner life (內的 生活), his writings were simple descriptions (簡朴な 記述) of that thought—those descriptions are solely the five thousand words of the *Tao Te Ching*.

3. Trends of Lao Tzu's Thought
To put it in one word (一言蔽之曰): it can be said that almost everyone in Eastern thought after Lao Tzu was, directly or indirectly, entirely influenced by him. Therefore, even among those who opposed him, there are clear traces (影跡) suggesting either overt opposition but covert admiration (陽排陰崇), or receiving the implicit suggestion of reaction (反動の暗示). Looking at it this way, thoroughly tracing (溯究) its trends is by no means a small task (젹은 일이 아니다). Therefore, below, I will briefly draw a diagrammatic chart (圖學表) of the trends of Lao Tzu's thought, and that is all for now (아즉 둘 밧에 업다 - lit. "there are still only two," perhaps meaning only two tasks remain: draw chart and stop?).
However, broadly dividing, we can list three schools: the Orthodox Transmission (正傳), Unorthodox Schools (旁門 - likely meant by 㥬門), and Eclectic (折衷). Accordingly, one should be able to gauge (斟酌) the merits and demerits (良否), benefits and harms (利弊), contributions and faults (功罪), and influence (勢力) of Lao Tzu's thought. But among these, one should carefully consider (詳考) the points where Lao Tzu was misunderstood, causing harmful effects (弊毒), and the points where Lao Tzu was supplemented and developed (補充하야 발전). In any case, Lao Tzu is a great philosopher (大哲人) of the East. As much as his thought gave great inspiration (偉大한 感化), downsides (弊端) were also not absent on the other hand. However, since this is not limited to Lao Tzu, there is no need for tedious discussion (煩論). Simply, anyone interested in scholarship (學問에 留意하는 사람) should know a little about it. Not only that, but I think we modern people (現代人) need to study it as material for reflecting (反省) on our own contemporary reality (時代的 現實). However, it is right to recognize that the most refined point (最も精到な곳) of Lao Tzu lies in the subtle principle (妙理) of penetrating the subtleties of employing the mind (用心の機微) and thus grasping the handle (權柄) of fortune and misfortune (禍福) oneself (自掌). (End) <13>

**Summary**

This 1924 essay by Kim Chŏng-sŏl provides an overview of Lao Tzu's thought, focusing on methodology, the concept of the 'Do' (Way), and its influence. Kim argues against purely objective or critical approaches derived from other frameworks, insisting that understanding Lao Tzu requires sympathetically engaging with his own methods and perspective, particularly the concepts of 'Mu' (non-being/nothingness) and 'Muwi' (non-action), as detailed in the *Tao Te Ching*. He clarifies that Lao Tzu's 'Mu' signifies Absolute Reality, not nihilism, and 'Muwi' represents profound, natural action aligned with the Do, not passivity or licentiousness. He briefly outlines the vast influence of Lao Tzu on subsequent Eastern thought, categorizing followers into orthodox, unorthodox, and eclectic schools, acknowledging both positive developments and harmful misunderstandings.

From a colonial history perspective, the essay is interesting as it shows a Korean intellectual in 1924 deeply engaging with classical Chinese philosophy (Lao Tzu) as a living tradition relevant to contemporary understanding and self-cultivation. Kim's emphasis on finding the "correct method" (方法) to understand Lao Tzu, his critique of superficial or distorting interpretations (including those by "Eastern and Western scholars"), and his positioning of Lao Tzu as a "great philosopher of the East" reflect an effort to assert intellectual agency and continuity with a pre-colonial heritage amidst Japanese rule. His argument for understanding Lao Tzu on his own terms, emphasizing inner realization (體認) over purely external critique, could subtly parallel a desire for authentic self-understanding in a colonized context, resisting imposed frameworks.

업고 손으로 만질 수 업다. 그러나 아조 업는 것이아니라 怳忽窈冥靈靈昭昭하게 존재한다. 이것을 또 旡象之象이라 하나니 旡象之象은 모든 有象의 본체며 玄牝은 또 谷神과 同實이니 玄으 有이면서도 有아닌 有, 旡이면서도 旡아닌 旡의 不可致詰할 형용이고 牝은 牝牡의 牝이니 즉 여성이다. 그러나 여성 중에도 妻性인 女性이 아니고 女息인 여성이 아니라 오직 여성인 여성의 義니 즉 萬物之母란 말과 相同하야 亦是 旡의 能産을 형언한 것이다. 그런대 이 旡가 우주에 在하얀 萬有化成의 본체가 되고 인간에 在하얀 修心正己의 妙法이 되며, 齊家治國의 至道가 된다는 것인늌� 이 旡를 위하는 것이 旡爲다. 旡爲는 위함에 반대되는 不爲가 아니라 旡爲를 위하는 것 즉 旡不爲의 旡爲다. 旡不爲의 旡爲를 위함은 곳 道로서 위함이니 這間消息을 모르고 老子의 旡爲를 오해하야 今古에 自誑誑人한 徒輩도 不少하고 不當한 공격도 업지 안헛다. 그러면 旡不爲의 旡爲를 위함은 如何한 것인가? 老子의 소견에 從하면 爲는 餘食贅行, 銳, 劌, 情僞, 貌詐, 作爲, 我執, 按排擬議, 巧黠刻意繕行, 成心作性, 與接爲構 日以心鬪, 紛競等의 義오. 旡爲는 소극적으로는 如斯한 爲를 止之할지며 적극적으로는 致虛極, 守靜篤, 觀其妙, 綿綿若存, 冲而用之若不盈, 勿忘勿助長, 放而不肆 執而不滯, 靜定守中의 義니 擧皆用心의 機微와 養性의 秘旨를 闡發한 것이다.
不爲爲 爲旡爲져 旡爲를 위함은 爲에 反하야 不爲하는 것이 아니라 爲를 不爲하고 旡爲를 爲함이니 爲 (如上의 義로)는 禍惡의 機며 旡爲는 上德의 妙라. 爲는 有에서 生하는 과오, 旡爲는 곳 旡의 作用이니 효자의 旡가 死滅的 부정적 旡가 아니라 일종의 절대 理象으로서의＜11＞ 旡인 것과 갓치 旡爲가 결코 逸遊*散, 放縱姿肆, 혹은 灰身滅知의 義가 아니라 도로혀 精進力行, 閉邪存誠, 冲淡和粹의 實이 업시는 旡爲을 위함이 아니오 旡爲를 不爲하고 爲를 위함이라 할지니 彼所謂 晉代狂士의 類는 실로 老子의 本旨에서는 도로혀 老子를 極排한 文公韓愈보담도 肖遠하다하겟고 近日 所謂 파괴적 虛旡主義와 老子의 虛旡旡爲의 旨와 相同하다는 것은 吏部畢卓이가 老子의 傳鉢門人이란 소리보담도 더한층 詭誕旡稽한 수작이다, 그리고 老子는 旡爲를 또다른 말 五自化, 自然이란 말로 형용한 대가 잇다. 그 소위 自化, 자연이란 말도 旡, 旡爲에 대한 오해와 꼭 가티 그 本旨를 그릇되게 한 것이다. 旡爲는 旡의 작용의 妙, 自化, 자연은 旡爲의 별칭이기도 하며, 혹은 旡爲의 德化, 혹은 旡爲의 實跡을 이르는 것것이다. 그럼으로 旡와 旡爲의 要妙를 領會하면 自化와 자연의 微旨를 곳 旁通할 수 잇는 것이다. 그런대 或人은 老子의 자연을 放肆無行의 宗旨로도 해석하고 또는 자연계 자연과학적 자연이란 의미로도 해석하고, 또는 生得, 本能이란 의미로도 해석하는 모양이다. 그레서 상말로 얼토당토 안흔 人名을 끄어내서 相同하단니, 類似하단니 하는 주장을 입으로 못다해서 붓으로 까지 하는 曲學들이 잇다. 第一說의 반대로 老子의 자연은 찰하리 금욕적이며 第二說의 반대로 老子의 자연은 찰하리 신비적이며 第三說의 반대로 老子의 自 然은 찰하리 이상적이다. 요컨대 老子의 爲人은 종교적이며, 그 종교는 철학적이며, 그 철학은 신비적이다. 그럼으로 그 사상 즉 道는 신비적 신념으로 붓터 출발하야 인생의 究竟義 즉 실천적 진리를 闡發하며 우주의 秘奧를 置覺한 것이다. 老子의 爲人이 학자적임보다는 聖者的인 것과 가티 老子의 사상은 학술적임보다 논리적 (廣義)이라 그런고로 그의 宗敎觀 혹 道德觀은 모도다 실천의 要旨를 說道한 것이니-心性 修養의 실제적 妙法을 지시한 것이오, 心性 자체의 分析的 설명이 아니며 그의 宇宙觀 及 社會觀도 역시 그 운명과 秘密을 詠歎하며, 혹 그 실제적 처리에 대한 이상을 진술한 것이오. 세계자체의 조직과 사회자체의 원리를 討究한 것은 아닌 편이다. 그것이야 고대 철학자 及 그 학술이 擧皆그러한 것이고, 더구나 동양 철학은 모도다 그 색채가 농후한 것이닛가, 奚獨老子만 그러한 것이 아니다. 그러나 그 輕하고 短한 곳을 두고, 그 重하고 長한 곳을 들자면 이가티 보는 편이 切當하다. 엇잿든지 老子는 恬靜旡爲하고 玄黙淵深한 妙境에 自證하야 永恒의 寂光을 常樂하든 哲人的 聖者엿다. 그리고 그 經綸도 自家의 軆得한＜12＞ 妙諦를 인간에 開示하는대 잇슬 뿐이엿다-그 사상은 自家의 內的 生-活 그 著術은 그 사상의 簡朴한 記述-그 記述은 오직 道德經 五千言.

3. 老子思想의 潮流
一言蔽之曰 老子 以後의 동양사상은 직접 간접으로 그 영향을 전연히 밧지 안흔 이가 업다고 까지 말할 수 잇다. 그럼으로 그를 반대하는 이 중에도 혹은 陽排陰崇을 햇든지, 혹은 그 반동의 암시를 어든 影跡이 역력하다. 그리고 본즉 상세히 그 조류를 溯究하랴면 도저히 젹은 일이 아니다. 그레서 이하에 老子思想潮流의 圖學表를 잠간 그려 보고 아즉 둘 밧에 업다.
그런대 大別하야 正傳, 㥬門, 折衷三派를 들 수 잇다. 좃차서 老子思想의 良否와 利弊와 功罪와 세력을 斟酌할 수 잇슬 터이다. 그러나 그 중에 老子를 오해하야 弊毒을 끼친 점과, 老子를 補充하야 발전한 점을 詳考할 것이다. 엇잿든지 老子는 동방의 大哲人이다. 그의 사상의 위대한 感化를 준 것 만치 그 반면에 폐단도 업지안타. 그러나 그것은 老子에 한한 일이 아닌 이상에 煩論할 필요가 업고 다만 학문에 留意하는 사람은 누구든지 좀 알어둘만 하겟고 뿐아니라 우리 현대인은 자기의 시대적 현실을 반성해 볼 자료로 보아서 연구해 볼 필요가 업지 안흘 줄 생각한다. 그러나 老子의 가장 精到한 곳은 用心의 機微를 觀破하야 禍福의 權柄을 自掌한 妙理에 잇는 것을 보아주는 것이 바른 일이다.
