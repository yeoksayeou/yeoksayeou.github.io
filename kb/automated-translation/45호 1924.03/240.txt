Article Title: 小曲 (A Little Song)

Article Type: 시 (Poem)

小曲
						岸曙
The peony flower has no scent,
The peony flower has no scent,
The rose bush has thorns;
The flower is too beautiful,
The thorns are too sorrowful.

Pure dewdrops,
岸曙

Pure dewdrops
Form on the bright red flower bud,
And descend upon the bright red flower petals; <126>
Ah, are these tears mourning death?
Ah, is this joy celebrating creation?

If the stream with rippling wavelets
岸曙

If the stream with rippling wavelets is called joy,
What shall sorrow be called? Is it not the great sea,
Ceaselessly leaping, high then low?
Though the stream sings and flows day and night,
Yet where is its end?
Ah, it is the wide and deep great sea—
-1924. 1. 15. <127>
<126-127>

**Summary**

This poem, "Sogok" (A Little Song) by An Sŏ, uses natural imagery to explore contrasts and existential questions. It juxtaposes the scentless beauty of the peony with the thorny rose, reflecting on the coexistence of beauty and pain. Dewdrops are presented ambiguously as tears of mourning or symbols of joyful creation. The poem contrasts the seemingly joyful, ever-flowing stream with the vast, deep, and perhaps sorrowful sea, questioning the ultimate destination or meaning. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in *Kaebyŏk* magazine in 1924, might reflect the complex emotions of the colonial era – a mix of beauty, sorrow, life, and uncertainty felt under Japanese rule, expressed through lyrical contemplation of nature. The themes of confinement (stream) versus vastness (sea) could subtly mirror Korea's situation.
