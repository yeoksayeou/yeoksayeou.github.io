Red Dragonflies, Big Dipper Stars
						Yi Ŭn-sang

On a summer day, under the blazing sun, on a desolate mountain slope, in the furrows of a pepper field
When swarms of red dragonflies were drifting about—
Ah! Mother,
Mother, you were then in that field's furrow, alone—in solitude
Weeding. Yes—then, I, strangely—
With a dubious face, sweat dripping busily,
Stepped on the handle of the hoe held in Mother's hand
And grabbing and shaking the headscarf (手巾) on Mother's head—
I asked like this
—Mother, those dragonflies
Why are they so red?—
At that time, oh Mother!
You, gently—pushing away my hand
That was shaking your headscarf-covered head,
Answered like this
—Hm? Those are
Because they ate peppers
That they became so red—
Ah! Mother,
My Mother!
That question and answer from when I was a child of seven was a great omen for me. Ah! Omen. Omen!
Ah! My truthful, sincere omen.
.......
—Omen—
This young body, suffering like a summer day
On these days of languor, burning like the blazing sunlight
My past, solitary like a desolate mountain slope—
"Upon the hazy slumber (줌터) of twenty-one years
A heart red like peppers. Red Love. Red Passion. <128>
... All this red innocence,
Protecting, and protecting again—
With life still beating
I intended to offer it as a living sacrifice (祭祀)—
Ah! Mother,
Mother!
While wrapped within
Mother's womb (腹) for ten months
Deeply, deeply, deeply
The red heart received and stored within!
When—where—to whom
It was all stolen away
Only now—now—as the bell of the Cathedral (聖堂) rings
Have I come to know.
—Outside my heart's barren field (荒地)
Having eaten the red heart
Swarms of reddened dragonflies
Are flying about—
Ah! What shall I do?
Mother!
Mother!
From within Mother's breast (乳房)
And upon your lap
And under your hand and pupils (瞳子)
While being carefully nurtured (잘 아나면서) for over ten years
Deeply, deeply, and again deeply
That red Love learned and gained!
When—where—to whom
It was all stolen away
Only now—now—as the bell of the Cathedral (聖堂) rings
Have I come to know.
—Outside my heart's barren field (荒地)
Having eaten the red Love
Swarms of reddened dragonflies
Are flying about—
Ah! What shall I do?
Mother!
Mother!
Amidst Mother's tears of blood
Leaving Mother—leaving that land—
Ah—when I was leaving five or six years ago
For the last time— <129>
<128-129>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Ŭn-sang reflects on a lost innocence and passion, symbolized by the "red heart," "red love," and "red passion." The poet contrasts a childhood memory of asking his mother why dragonflies were red (she jokingly replied they ate red peppers) with his present state of disillusionment and emptiness ("barren field of my heart"). He realizes, belatedly, that his youthful fervor, nurtured by his mother, has been stolen or consumed, much like the dragonflies metaphorically ate the peppers (or his heart/love). The poem expresses a deep sense of loss and despair. For historians, the poem, written in 1924, offers insight into the personal expression of disillusionment during the Japanese colonial period. The themes of lost passion, the departure from "that land" five or six years prior (placing it around 1918-1919, near the March 1st Movement), and the overall melancholic tone might reflect the suppressed atmosphere and the personal cost of the era on sensitive individuals, even if not explicitly political.
