기사제목	朝鮮 생각을 차즐대 (The Time to Find Chosŏn Thought)
필자	權悳奎
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Essay)

朝鮮 생각을 차즐대
						權悳奎

1. Our Religious Position

The honor of a nation manifests in two ways: one political, the other cultural. The most glorious nation possesses honor in both ways, and even possessing only one of the two is not insignificant. Since it is impossible to encompass both, the cultural position is considered rather better than the political one. Of course, it is better. Even if a great empire was established for a time and intimidated its four neighbors with military might, what spectacle would remain when it collapses? Even if it seemed unnecessary and like mere play at the time, when times change and affairs pass (時移事往), and everything else disappears, only cultural relics preserve their life eternally, not only allowing the owners to speak of and boast about them but also causing outsiders to praise them endlessly. Heaven does not grant both simultaneously to one group, so all nations of the world possess one thing each to make noise about and boast of. Yet, Heaven must have had a private affection (私) for us, or perhaps we are fortunately a blessed nation. We possess both prides that others do not have. In ancient times, how much power did our people wield militarily, and how much fear did they strike into the East, West, and especially China (支那)? ** Oh, that the people of the Great Country (大國) were made to take the "Song of Not Dying Needlessly in Liaodong" (無向遼東浪死歌) as their motto (座右銘), and feared, "If they don't come to attack this year, they will raise troops in August next year." Oh, how frightening. They trembled at "Koryŏ is coming, Koryŏ is coming!" This is extraneous to the current issue, so I won't elaborate (長提), but returning to the cultural aspect, what is our position? This is what we boast of and what earns us respect from others. Having written this much, some readers might think, "Ah, humans have a characteristic of boasting about themselves, and historians <34> or writers are even more prone to it," and thus harbor suspicions. However, I intend to speak demonstratively with examples. First, let's talk about our religious position from the religious perspective. The evolution representing God initially involved worshiping idols, then worshiping natural objects like mountains or stars (星辰). As wisdom slightly increased, unemotional objects (非情的 物) like idols and natural objects were abandoned, and sentient beings (有情物) like bears or crocodiles (鰐魚) were worshiped. Then, sorcerer-like humans were called gods, advocating the theory of God and man being the same form (神人同形說). As human intelligence greatly developed (人智가 大開), religion also reached evolution, stating that God is formless and ideal. This is exemplified by Buddhism calling True Thusness (眞如) God, and Christianity calling the Supreme Being (上帝) God. Furthermore, the number of gods also evolves. In ancient times (太古), when people worshiped beasts, insects, fish, and various natural objects in a very primitive era, polytheism was always practiced. As society progressed and formless, ideal gods came to be worshiped, this became so-called monotheism. Religions practiced in civilized countries today all believe in a single God. There are two types of monotheistic religion: one is unitary monotheism (單一神敎), which recognizes only one omniscient and omnipotent God. The other is composite monotheism (複一神敎), where numerous gods are attached below a single supreme God; for example, Christianity's Trinity (三位一軆說) belongs to this category. So, what about our religion? Already 4,000 years ago, the theory of Heaven, God, and Man as a Trinity (天神人 三位一軆說) was advocated. The representative God recognized by the Divine Teaching (神敎) was Hwanung (桓雄). As this God spread his sacred thought, those who heard it gathered like a marketplace (저자), hence the name Sin Sissi (神市氏). How far did the influence of this Divine Teaching, this religion, reach? The custom of erecting Sodo (蘇塗) in Southern Han (南韓) might have migrated to India (印度) and become the Buddhist "stupa" (소트파). In China (支那), the worship of gods like our Sŏndo Mother Goddess (仙桃神) was significant. The influence our Divine Teaching exerted on Japan (日東) is too extensive to recount. Leaving aside examples like a branch of the Divine Teaching spreading to Yan and Qi (燕齊) under the name Sŏn'gyo (仙敎), causing not just practitioners of esoteric arts (方士) but also so-called princes and emperors (王公帝子) to frantically seek the Three Divine Mountains (三神山) in Haedong (海東) where the elixir of immortality (불노초) supposedly existed; it is well known that the Dongyi (東夷), specifically the people of Haeju (海州) today, named Soryŏn and Taeryŏn (小連大連), became the spiritual masters (心師) for the Chinese who still believed in natural objects like Mount Tai (泰山), i.e., Mount Niqiu (尼丘山). Furthermore, Hyŏnjang (玄奘) and K'yugi (窺基) were incomparably jealous of Monk Wŏnch'ŭk (圓測스님), and figures like Hyŏnsu (賢首) plagiarized the thoughts of Wŏnhyo (元曉), or as he put it, Monk Hyo (曉公). The profound wisdom (理慧) that led even monks from India (天竺僧人) to revere monks from Haedong (海東僧侶) as extraordinary beings (異人) was deeply rooted in the profound principles (奧旨) of the Great Ancestral Teaching (大倧), further enriching the body. As it is said, <35>

"The nation possesses a profound and subtle way (玄玅之道), which embraces the Three Teachings (三敎) and transforms all living beings (接化群生)." Compared to this, even if our ancient sages manifested differently through Taoism (道), Confucianism (儒), or Buddhism (佛), in reality, they were all like myriad flowers blooming uniquely under the nourishment of a single rain (一雨所潤에 萬奔殊芳). This cannot be denied.

2. Why the Principles of the Ancestral Teaching (倧理) Were Buried

Where there is substance, a shadow follows. If the truth of the Great Ancestral Teaching (大倧) was so profound and subtle (玄奧) and influenced others, one might ask why even its shadow cannot be glimpsed. I answer that although there are various specific reasons, it mainly stems from four causes: first, the frequent rise and fall (興替) of dynasties preventing historical records (史書) from being transmitted; second, the transgression of Sinophiles (慕華者); third, the fault of the followers of the Ancestral Teaching (倧敎徒); and fourth, the excessive breadth (過汎) of the doctrines of the Great Ancestral Teaching (大倧敎理). Because historical records were not transmitted, the path to trace the influence of the Ancestral Teaching nurtured in our spirit and its subsequent transmission is obscured. The temporary stratagems of Kim Ch'unch'u (金春秋) and Kim Yusin (金庾信), who brought in neighboring troops (隣兵) to satisfy their own ambitions, consequently fostered a habit of worshiping foreigners (外人崇拜癖) among the later Silla people (羅人). Ch'oe Ch'iwŏn (崔致遠) made his mark, and Kim Pusik (金富軾) compiled the so-called *Samguk Sagi* (三國史記), but (though named *History of the Three Kingdoms*) excluded [native elements] with his brush. Thereafter, this lineage produced sons, and more sons, and unfortunately, did not die out without heirs (無后) even after several generations of great-grandsons and great-great-grandsons, ultimately burying Chosŏn culture forever. If only they had changed their minds, the pagodas, steles, tombs (塔碑墳墓), hermitages, and temples (庵院) they could have smashed and dug up, the architecture, books, and paintings (建築書畵) in foreign lands they could have chased down and destroyed if their footsteps had reached them, and the books recently excavated from foreign lands—fortunately or unfortunately—allow a shadow of our culture to remain. Thinking about it, if even that tiny bit, like a single hair from nine oxen (九牛の一毛), had completely disappeared and become invisible, we might have all degenerated into dogs or pigs, and our minds would have been at ease. But resentfully, that remnant persists, causing us to compare ourselves to others, saying we are better or worse, lived well before or poorly now, this way or that way, what to do, what to do, talking about self-awareness, nation, cultural movements, any movement, and further, about human life, religion, or philosophy, leaving no time to avoid all sorts of sorrows and worries about others. To reiterate and add one more point, even if we, like some Confucian scholars (儒學者), <36> mastered the *Spring and Autumn Annals* (春秋) and the *Gangmok* (綱目), and recited the Four Books and Five Classics (四書五經) and the Hundred Schools of Thought (諸子百家) fluently like flowing water or pounding grain, what pride would that be for us? Ultimately, even if we tilled works like the *Jinsilu* (近思綠) like ploughing a field or weeding a paddy and perfected the philosophy of the Zhu Xi school (朱子學派), what pride could that possibly become? Even if various Complete Works (全書) and Great Compendiums (大全) piled up like mountains, they do not grant great value to our existence. To put it more plainly, they have little connection to the true life and original thought of the Chosŏn people, and therefore, they cannot influence others as something belonging to Chosŏn.
While it is quite difficult for people to break habits, they also have a characteristic of pursuing novelty and liking the strange. When the Elephant Teaching (象敎 - Buddhism) first entered, the groups performing sky-worship ceremonies like Yonggo (迎鼓祭天) in the Paedal culture resisted and reviled it to the utmost (排之毁之가 無所不至). Thinking that perhaps this land where the Mugunghwa (無窮花 - Rose of Sharon) blooms was, after all, not a land with affinity (有綠國土), those tasked with transmitting the Dharma (傳法) felt they had no choice but to turn their backs and retreat. At that moment, the blood shed by Pak Yŏmch'ok (朴厭觸) was such an extraordinary event that, although the footsteps of Buddhists were placed only outside the cave dwellings (窟室) of the Mo clan (毛體), people began, one by one, two by two, to lend an ear to the subtle truths (妙諦) of Buddha (佛陀). Eventually, upon hearing and seeing, they realized that in the aspect of not committing any evil and performing only good (as stated in the preface to the Hwarang Nallang Stele - 鸞郞碑序), it unexpectedly coincided with the Great Ancestral Teaching (大倧) they had traditionally followed (行吟). Thereafter, not only did they not draw much distinction between the Ancestral Teaching and Buddhism (倧佛), but the number of those believing in and praising Buddha grew daily. As years and months passed and centuries went by, great scholars (碩學) and virtuous monks (高德) emerged, raising high the compassionate lamp (慈燈) from the West (西域). Fearing its light might not illuminate universally (普照), they sang songs like "Returning a thousand li to ask the temple keeper, how many springs have the green mountains passed alone? If I encounter the difficult-to-practice Dharma in the final age, I too, like you, will not spare my body" (千里歸來問舍人, 靑山獨在幾經春. 若逢末世難行法, 我亦如君不惜身 - Taegak Kuksa's poem at Yŏmch'ok Sa'in Shrine - 大覺國師의 厭觸舍人廟詩). Even so, the followers of the Ancestral religion merely sat respectfully without interfering (恒恭坐而不相犯), praised without slandering (相譽而不相毁), did not speak falsely (不妄言), and smiled gently (**然笑) (as mentioned in the *Shenyi Jing* - 神異經). Turning now to the Confucian side, Confucian scholars (儒學者), then as now, rich in exclusivity (排他性) and lacking in tolerance (包容力), abandoned their own land and national essence (國粹). Every cell constituting their bodies lived confined within Confucian scriptures (儒經) and Chinese characters (漢文字). Even in Silla (新羅), they trampled upon Silla's own national essence, one of Chosŏn's national essences. Seeing this, Queen Chinsŏng (眞聖女王), unable to bear it, ordered Kakan Wihong (角干魏弘) and the monk Taegu (大矩和尙) to preserve Silla's national essence by compiling poems and songs reflecting traditional thoughts and feelings under the book title *Samdaemok* (三代目) and initiated a movement for the revival of national learning (國學復興). At this, Confucian scholars, acting typically, rose up in the capital and countryside (京鄕), even spreading vulgar accusations on the streets, such as alleging scandalous conduct (醜行) between the Queen and Wihong, obstructing the movement <37> in every way. This movement failed midway. Although this could be seen as a clash between the Ancestral Teaching and Confucianism (倧儒), essentially a religious war between religion and Confucianism, the followers of the Ancestral religion still practiced non-action (無爲而處) and taught without words (不言而敎) (Preface to Nallang Stele - 鸞郞碑序), taking these as precepts (戒喩) and paying no mind to such matters. Meanwhile, Confucianism strove diligently in secret (暗裏에 奮勵), achieving an extraordinary degree of revitalization (振興). Yet, An Yu (安裕) composed a poem to encourage later scholars (後學): "Incense lamps everywhere pray to Buddha, pipes and flutes in every house serve spirits. Only at the few gates of the Master's temple, spring grass fills the courtyard, desolate and silent" (香燈處處皆祈佛, 簫管家家盡事神. 惟有數問夫子廟, 滿庭春草寂無人). Thus, there were considerable hidden struggles (暗鬪) and more than a few clashes between the Ancestral religion and Buddhism or Confucianism, but the followers of the Ancestral religion always remained serene (晏如), as if asking, "Who would dare touch me?" However, this serene behavior of the Ancestral religion's followers cannot be separated from its principles (倧理). The principles of our True Ancestral Teaching (眞倧) are as vast, deep, broad, and profound (宏深廣博) as the heavens and the sea. They are beyond the measure of our physical eyes (肉眼). To evaluate this teaching as such-and-such is like a blind man describing an elephant (장님이 코ㅅ길이 구경한 것 갓하야). The earlier statement "embraces the Three Teachings and transforms all living beings" (包含三敎하야 接化群生) is, in reality, likely a plausible explanation derived from glimpsing just one corner of the religion. Therefore, no people are as rich in assimilative power (同化力) and yet as difficult to assimilate as the Chosŏn people. If, 5,000 years ago during the Sinsi (神市) era, people already gathered like a marketplace to listen to what was called the thought of a holy person, one can sufficiently gauge (斟酌) the cultural level of the people at that time. In any case, our Great Ancestral Teaching's doctrine (大倧敎理) encompasses everything intimately, from the inner self of mind and body to the distant tips of trees and crevices in rocks. In other words, any corner one takes is not inappropriate. Thus, Buddhist thought, Confucian thought, and the thought of any other teaching are all enveloped herein, and if reasonably applied, none fails to accord with principle (理). Therefore, Buddhist thought already existed within us, and Confucian thought was already equipped within us. Consequently, when Buddhism arrived, it was readily accepted by us, and when Confucianism arrived, it was also readily accepted by us. Thus, even if it is said that Buddhism flourished in Chosŏn, it is an expression (發露) of our thought, and even if it is said that Confucianism flourished, it too is an expression of our thought. However, while this is a source of pride on one hand, on the other hand, it becomes a flaw where everything self-proclaimed withers, failing to protect itself, thereby diluting Chosŏn's unique local flavor and national color (민족적 색채).
I intended to write here about the negative influence Chosŏn had on the thoughts of foreigners, but I stop due to lack of time.

3. The Time to Find Chosŏn Thought <38>

For us, the old has already crumbled, and the new is yet to be established. Looked at from any angle, the influence between the Chosŏn people and the world is certainly not very great. Moreover, the Chosŏn people themselves have no path to follow, making Chosŏn society truly not an ordered society progressing systematically, but rather what can be called a chaotic mess (난장판), a wandering scene. Consequently, the 5,000-year history is not much to be proud of, and the embroidered rivers and mountains (錦繡江山) are not particularly beautiful. However, do not frown upon history. Do not spit upon the landscape. The present is already worse than the past, and the future is also attached to doubt. To resolve this doubt well and for the Chosŏn people to gain the value of existence, it means occupying a glorious position in world history. The path for the Chosŏn people to gain this glorious ground (榮地) is solely and irreplaceablely the path of culture, the path of culture that can be called the Chosŏn people's own. World civilization is not yet complete, and where is the logic (理) that Chosŏn people cannot become cultured people? Especially for the Chosŏn people, who once possessed civilization? Our cultural movement is solely about finding and establishing the old culture, merely adding decoration (加飾) with ashes. Thus, it is just about expanding this. In other words, it is about the Chosŏn people's thoughts, emotions, things that connect with actual society (實사회); it is not an imitative civilization (模倣的 문명) detached from true Chosŏn. Everyone, please do not rebuke me excessively. What form does the clamor about cultural movements or whatever take now? Those who have been to Tokyo (東京) come back to Chosŏn and do things exactly as people there do them, that is, as suits the people there. Those who studied in the West also, in the same manner, do things exactly as Westerners do them among themselves, do they not? I say this is no different from how our ancestors (先人) learned Buddhism and mentally lived in India (印度), or studied Confucianism and served as vassals in China (支那邊). Those reading this passage might become greatly angered, saying where is the logic that Chosŏn people would do such a thing, and moreover, things like philosophy, religion, and literature are never studied representing only one region. We only strive for the highest good (至善) representing our human life to unite with the universe. This argument, they might say, is a biased view looking only at one side. Of course, not everything is like that, and it would be fortunate if it weren't. Striving for the highest good to unite with the universe is sufficient. However, Western philosophy does not necessarily suit the East, nor does French or Russian literature necessarily suit Chosŏn. If so, how can philosophy or literature that doesn't suit Chosŏn discuss union with the universe? People must start from their own foundation (土臺) to interpret everything <39> and strive to unite with the universe. The issue was already about Chosŏn's culture, Chosŏn thought, and we have strayed too far. Alas, where can Chosŏn thought be found, and how can it be established? It must be found in Chosŏn and established to suit the real life (實生活) of the Chosŏn people. It is not something to be found under the blackboard (칠판미테서). It is not something to be found in Chinese classics (漢籍). It is not in the insights (소견알) of students returned from Tokyo. Nor is it in the ideals (이상속) of students returned from the West. Blackboards and returned students are where one learns the methods of finding it, the forms for organizing it; it is certainly not about transplanting Western literature or Japanese ethics as they are. Also, the place to find it is, of course, Chosŏn, but if Chosŏn, is it the north or the south? Is it among the elderly or the young? Our true history is not just the 5,000-year history itself, but the high and low mountains, the clear and muddy seas. Our philosophy or literature does not reside with the young people now wearing Western clothes and speaking of science, but rather with the old elders (늘근 父老), the weak women and children (약한 婦幼), and the ignorant laborers (무식한 노늏자). Those dappled morning clouds hanging on the peaks (메뿌리) of Paektu (白頭) and Kŭmgang (金剛), how much aesthetic sense of light (빗의 美感) have they given us over 5,000 years? The rolling evening waves (저녁물ㅅ결) of the blue sea and the Archipelago (蒼海多島海), how much aesthetic sense of sound (소리의 美感) have they given us? The legends passed down by old grandfathers and grandmothers (늘근 한아비 한어미) for 5,000 years are surely not meaningless. How could the playful sounds (작난 짓거리는 소리) of children running about be so unfounded (無根底)? Could the incessant curses (욕설) of laborers perhaps be the moral echo (도덕적 반향) of our "Land of Gentlemen" (君子國)? Putting ashes in the manure pit (밋걸음) and charcoal in the kitchen (부억), how much scientific knowledge (과학적 지식) is that? Ah, those who wish to know Chosŏn, those who wish to study Chosŏn! Go to Kŭmgang Mountain (金剛). Go to the Archipelago (多島海). Go wherever the relics of our ancestors (선인의 유적) exist. Go wherever our ancestors were active (선인의 활동한 지역), whether inside or outside the country. If you listen attentively there, you will surely hear some sound. If you roll your eyes, something will surely appear. If you think, some thought (생작) will surely arise. Ask the elderly. Play with the children. Learn from the laborers. They are our great teachers. Then, when what you have heard, seen, and learned eventually reaches the tip of your brush (붓끗), it will become poetry, painting, music, literature, and philosophy. The one to open this treasure trove (寶庫), accumulated over 5,000 years, is none other than the Chosŏn person wearing white clothes (힌옷), you and me. It is the destiny (天命) of the Chosŏn person to do so, and the Chosŏn person has the ability (능력) to do so. With the joy of anticipating that the culture conceived by these Chosŏn people will startle the eyes and ears of the world (世人의 이목을 驚動), I excerpt this writing and conclude with this one phrase: "A house not built on its own foundation (제터) will eventually be torn down." <40>

**Summary**

Kwŏn Tŏkkyu argues that true national honor lies more in culture than politics, and that Korea historically possessed both. He laments the suppression and neglect of indigenous Korean thought and religion (referred to as 大倧, Taejong or 神敎, Singyo), attributing this loss to dynastic changes, Sinophilia (慕華者), the passivity of its followers, and the doctrine's excessive breadth which allowed easy assimilation of foreign ideas like Buddhism and Confucianism. Kwŏn calls for a cultural revival, urging Koreans to reject mere imitation of foreign (Japanese, Western, Chinese) models and instead rediscover authentic "Chosŏn thought" by engaging directly with Korea's nature (mountains, seas), folklore (legends from elders), daily life (children's play, laborers' words), and historical sites. This rediscovery, he believes, is the key to establishing Korea's value and future cultural contribution to the world. For historians, the article is significant as a powerful expression of cultural nationalism during the Japanese colonial period (1924). It reflects the intense intellectual effort to define and reclaim a distinct Korean identity against colonial assimilation pressures, emphasizing indigenous traditions and criticizing both historical Sinocentrism and contemporary imitation of Japan and the West.

1. 우리의 宗敎上 地位
민족의 영예는 두 길로 들어나나니 하나는 정치상으로요 하나는 문화상으로라 그 민족이 이 길로 다 영예를 가진 이가 가장 영화롭고 두 가지 중에 하나만 가지드라도 괜치 아니한데 이미 둘을 다 아우르지 못하는 이상에는 정치상으로보다 문하상의 지위가 오히려 낫다하노라. 물론 나흔 것이라 비록 한때의 대제국을 세우고 武로 四隣을 울럿다 하드라도 그것이 슬어지는 때에 다시 무엇이 가관이리요. 비록 그 때에는 필요하지 안코 한 작난갓다 하드라도 또한 時移事往하야 모든 것이 업서질지라도 오즉 문화상의 유물은 영원히 목숨을 보전하야 님자되는 사람만이 닐러 자랑할 뿐아니라 손되는 남들까지 기리어 마지안는 바이라. 한울은 한 군대에 둘을 幷與치 아니하야서 세계의 모든 민족이 다 한 가지씩을 가지어 떠들고 자랑하나 한울도 우리에게는 私가 잇든지 우리는 幸으로 福이 만흔 민족이라. 남의 가지지 못한 두 가지 자랑을 아울러 가젓도다. 고대에 잇서서 우리 결에가 武로 얼마나한 세력을 부리어 동서 더욱 支那에 얼마나 공포를 주엇는가. ** 구럭이 大國 사람으로 하야금 無向遼東浪死歌로 座右銘을 삼으며 금년 若不來攻進하면 明年 8월 就興兵이라. 아이고 무서워. 高麗來高麗來에 비에비하게 하엿도다. 이는 이 문제에 외되는 것이라. 長提하지 안커니와 다시 문화상으로 우리는 그 지위가 어떠한가. 이 우리가 자랑하는 바며 남에게 울얼음을 밧는 바로다. 이만큼 쓰고 보면 보는 이야 응 사람이란 저를 자랑하는 특성이 잇는 대에다가 더구나 역사를＜34＞ 말하는 이나 붓을 드는 사람이란 그 중에 더한 것이라 하야 돌우여 의심하는 이가 잇스리라. 하나 내 예를 들어 증명적으로 말하려 하노라. 爲先하나 종교방면으로  우리 종교의 지위를 말하야 보자. 神을 표창하는 진화가 최초에는 우상을 숭배하다가 山 혹 星辰들의 자연물을 예배하다가 족음 지혜가 늘므로부터 우상, 자연물과 가튼 非情的 物을 버리고 熊이나 鰐魚 따위의 有情物을 숭배하다가 妖術者가튼 사람을 神이라 하야 神人同形說을 주창하다가 人智가 大開함에 딸아 종교도 진화에 達하야 神은 무형이요 이상적 것이라 하기에 이르니 곳 佛敎가 眞如를 神이라 하고 基督敎가 上帝를 神이라 함이 이 따위라. 다시 神의 數도 진화하나니. 太古에 잇서서는 인민이 금수, 虫魚와 밋 種種의 自然物을 숭배함과 가튼 극히 유치시대에는 늘 다신교를 奉하든 것이 사회가 진보하야 이상적 무형의 神을 拜하게 되니 이것이 소위 유일신교라. 이제 문명 각국에 行하는 종교는 다 유일의 神을 信奉치 아님이 업고 또 一神的 종교에 二種이 잇스니 하나는 유일의 전지전능한 神만을 認하는 단일신교라는 것이요. 하나는 유일 최고의 神의 알에 幾多의 神이 附屬하얏다는 複一神敎라는 것이니 例하면 基督敎의 三位一軆說과 가튼 것이 이 종류에 속하는 것이라 그러하면 우리 종교는 어떠한가. 지금으로부터 4000년 압서에 벌서 天神人 三位一軆說을 주창하얏고 神敎의 대표적 神으로 認知는 환웅(桓雄)이라는 神이 그의 거룩한 생각을 베풀어 들릴 새 이를 듯는 자-저자와 가틈으로 神市氏라는 니름을 가젓스며 이 神敎 곳 종교의 영향이 어대에까지 밋첫느냐 하면 南韓의 蘇塗를 세우는 풍속이 印度에 옴기어 佛敎의 「소트파」라는 것이 되지나 아니하얏슬는지 支那에서는 우리 仙桃神가튼 神을 奉祀함이 대단하얏스며 日東에 우리 神敎의 준 영향은 이루 들어 말할 겨를이 업는 바요. 神敎의 一派가 仙敎란  명칭으로 燕齊에 전하야 方士 이외의 소위 王公帝子까지 海東에 三神山이 잇고 그 우에 불노초가 잇다하야 쓸어저미치든 例는 그만두고라도 東夷 곳 지금의 海州人이라는 小連大連이 아즉도 泰山 곳 尼丘山가튼 자연물을 밋는 支那人들의 心師가 된 것은 물론이며 玄奘, 窺基는 圓測스님을 못결어 새암이 無雙하얏고 賢首 따위가 元曉 곳 저의 말로 曉公의 생각을 盜賊하얏스며 天竺僧人이 海東僧侶를 異人으로 숭배하도록 理慧가 透徹함은 다 大倧의 奧旨에 뿌리가 심기어 더욱 몸이 윤택한 바니 이른바＜35＞

國有玄玅之道하니 包含三敎하야 接化群生이라 한대에 비기어 우리의 先哲이 혹 道로든지 儒로든지 또는 佛로든지 그 들어남이 비록 다르다 하야도 실상은 다 一雨所潤에 萬奔殊芳함이라 아니치 못할 이로다.

2. 倧理의 파무친 까닭
실체가 잇는 바에 그림자가 따르는 것이라. 이와 가티 大倧의 진리가 玄奧하고 남에게 영향을 주엇다하면 어찌하야 그 그림자도 접해 볼 수가 업느냐 질문하는 이가 잇스리라. 나는 이에 대답하기를 여긔에는 별다른 여러 가지 까닭이 만흘지나 대개 네가지 까닭에 원인하얏다 하노니 첫재는 朝代가 자주 興替하야 史書가 傳치 못함이요. 둘재는 慕華者의 罪過 셋재는 倧敎徒들의 죄며 넷재는 大倧敎理의 過汎함이라. 史書가 傳치 못하매 倧敎가 우리 정신에 함양된 영향과 밋 그 後傳의 여하를 차저볼 길이 아득한데 隣兵을 끌어들여 自家의 욕심을 채운 金春秋, 金庾信의 일시적 수단이 이내 後來 羅人의 外人崇拜癖을 일우어 崔致遠이 걸음을 하고 金富軾이 집대성을 하야 소위 三國史記 編纂에 (니름이 三國史記이지만) 붓을 除外하얏더라. 然後에는 이 계통으로 아들이 나고 또 아들이 나고 曾孫 高孫 몃대손에 불행히 無后하지도 아니하야서 그만 영영으로 朝鮮 문화를 가다위치고 말엇는데 그들이 생각만 돌앗드면 다 깨두들이고 파내버렷섯슬 塔碑墳墓, 庵院따위와 그네들이 발ㅅ길만 미쳣드면 쪼차가아 업쌋섯슬 外地에 잇는 建築書畵와 또 挽近에 外地에서 발굴되는 서적이 잇서 불행인지 幸인지 우리의 문화의 그림자를 끼치엇도다. 어찌 생각하면 九牛의 一毛가튼 그것조차 아주 업서져 안보엿드면 우리가 다 퇴화하야 개가 되든지 괴가 되든지 속이나 편하얏섯슬 것을 怨讎스럽게 그 남아부터 잇서서 제가 남보다 나흐니 못하니 하며 전에 잘 살앗느니 이제 잘못 사느니 하며 이러니 저러니 어쩌니 어쩌니 하야 자각이니 민족이니 문화운동이니 무슨 운동이니 하며 더하면 인생이니 종교닊� 철학이니 하야 이런 것 저런 일제 슬흠, 남의 걱정하기에 겨를이 업는 바로다. 되풀어기로 또 한가지 부텨 말할 것은 우리가 설혹 일부 儒學者모양으로＜36＞ 春秋, 綱目을 꾀어뚤코 四書五經, 諸子百家를 물흐르듯 박밀 듯 외운다 하드라도 우리에게 무슨 자랑이 되며 필경 近思綠 따위를 밧갈 듯, 논닉이듯 하야 朱子學派의 철학을 완성하얏다 하드라도 그것이 무슨 자랑이 되잘 것 업스며 무슨 全書, 무슨 大全이 山덤이처럼 싸힌다 하드라도 우리 존재의 큰 가치를 주는 것 아니로다. 더 淺近히 말하면 그것이 조선사람의 참 생활과 본래 사상에 관계가 그리 업는 바며 조선의 것으로 남에게 영향을 주지 못하는 까닭이로다.
