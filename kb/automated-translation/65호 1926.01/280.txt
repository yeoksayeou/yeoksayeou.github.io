Magazine Title: 개벽 제65호
Publication Date: 1926년 01월 01일
Article Title: 暴君 (Tyrant)
Author: 崔曙海
Article Type: 소설 (Novel)

暴君
						崔曙海
1
After beating his wife thoroughly using the excuse that the floor was cold, Ch'unsam got completely drunk on alcohol before noon. Staggering about, he stood in front of someone else's gate and urinated noisily, earning a sharp scolding from the old woman of that house, yet he still grinned foolishly and walked down the alley. Whether he was walking or dancing, like a boneless animal, he staggered this way and that, stumbled here and there, then entered a small side gate and flung open the door to the *chŏngji* (kitchen room).
"Auntie! Give me a drink!"
He collapsed in the lower part of the *chŏngji* without taking off his shoes. The elderly woman who was tending the fire in the *padang* (kitchen; in the north, the kitchen and inner room often connected without a wall. *Padang* is the kitchen area, and *chŏngji* is the room adjacent to the kitchen)
"Where did you get so plastered! Tsk, you dog butcher!"
muttered under her breath, clicking her tongue.
"Ah ha, so you won't give me alcohol!"
Ch'unsam, who had collapsed wheezing in the lower part of the room like someone shot, suddenly sat up straight. Supporting himself with both arms, he swayed unsteadily as if about to fall over any second.<76>
"Aigoo, you're drunk! Sir, go home and sleep! Then I'll make some soup for you!"
The old woman forced a smile, but the wrinkles on her forehead remained creased.
"Eh—wha-wha-what did you say?"
He looked up at the old woman with bleary eyes that seemed ready to spill alcohol.
"S-s-so you won't give me al-alcohol? Do I not have money? Have I ever failed to pay my liquor bill? What's the matter? Here, he-he-here's money! Money..."
Saying this, he fumbled inside his outer coat and put his hand into his vest pocket. The owner, the old woman, who had been watching this spectacle with an expression of disbelief, chuckled and came towards the drunkard.
"Sir, surely you know my heart? Would I ever do such a thing? Money? How could you possibly not have money, sir? I'd give it to you even if you didn't have money, so why wouldn't I give it to you when you say you have it? You're drunk right now, so drink it later!"
And the old woman retied Ch'unsam's loosened coat ties.
"Do I look like someone who'd skip out on the bill? Heh tsk heh heh heh."
He shrugged his shoulders and shook his head, snorting with laughter.
"Who said you'd skip out? And even if you did, so what? Would I file a complaint just because you skipped out on me a little? Haha, please just go now!"
The old woman hastily tried to help Ch'unsam up by putting an arm around his waist.
"What is this? Le-le-let go! Who said I liked old women? Hmph!"
Ch'unsam twisted away and shoved the old woman hard with both hands. Seeing her pushed back awkwardly, he said,
"Hahaha, so you won't give me alcohol? I'll just have one glass! Just one!"
And he shuffled his bottom over and sat down by the hearth (*puttomak*). His frozen shoes, now in the warm room, began to thaw, smearing muddy water onto the floor like a painting.<77>
"So you'll really just have one glass and then go?"
The old woman looked at Ch'unsam with eyes that seemed to ask, "Are you truly going to stop after just one?" Ch'unsam grinned and slurred—
"Of course, I'll go! Just give me one glass!"
The owner sighed heavily and went to the upper part of the room. From a small jar (*tanji*) placed on the *kongsan* (a three-tiered shelf against the wall in the upper part of the *chŏngji*; the bottom tier, *kongsan*, held large, heavy items like rice jars; the middle tier, *chowang*, held lighter items like bowls; and the top tier, *tŏktae*, held dining tables), she ladled about half a bowl of alcohol and casually thrust it towards him. Ch'unsam took it. He swayed and stared at the alcohol bowl for a moment.
"Heh heh, while giving me this alcohol, you must be cursing me inwardly, right?"
He tilted his head further and further back and gulped it down like a cow drinking water, glug glug glug. The owner glared at the Adam's apple bobbing frantically below his rising chin and clicked her tongue.
"Ugh, waek!"
Just as Ch'unsam removed the alcohol bowl from his lips, he shrugged his shoulders, jerked his neck, and bent his head forward. A cloudy, foul liquid gushed from his nose and mouth like a waterfall.
"Eek, you dog butcher! Eek, you filthy pig!"
The owner jumped up and glared at Ch'unsam.
"What did you say? What?"
Ch'unsam wiped his mouth with the sleeve of his coat and glared back at the old woman. It looked like a frosty command was about to issue forth. The old woman, trembling, retorted,
"Well, if you're not a dog butcher, what are you?"<78>
She shot back fiercely.
"What? Dog butcher? You old hag of low birth!"
Ch'unsam snatched the alcohol bowl in front of him and raised it high above his head. The old woman retreated to the upper part of the room.
"Fine! That bastard's gone mad! He drank alcohol, or did he drink shit water!"
Before her words had even finished,
"Ugh! You damned bitch!"
Ch'unsam's roar echoed as the bowl in his hand flew like an arrow towards the *chowang*. Crash, tinkle, clatter—where the bowl landed, the neatly stacked dishes shattered into pieces and rolled off. The jars on the *kongsan* were also hit by the falling dishes and cracked loudly. The owner trembled violently and ground her teeth.
"You bastard, why are you breaking things? Huh? Kill me! Kill me! Eat me up too!"
The owner screamed and lunged at him. Ch'unsam's clothes were torn. His cheek was scratched by the old woman's fingernails and started bleeding.
"You crazy bastard! Why would you break the dishes this old woman saved up bit by bit? What did I do wrong? Give me my liquor money! I called you ‘Sir, Sir,’ thinking you were at least respectable enough to set bones! You bastard, you owe me over sixty *yang* for liquor... and still, I treated you like family. I've endured this heart-bursting anger, bottling it up."
The old woman attacked him furiously, as if fighting for her life. Ch'unsam grabbed the old woman's hair.
"Aigogo! This bastard is killing me!"
Ch'unsam's strong foot struck the old woman's side.
The *padang* door opened. The *chŏngji* door also opened. People gathered.
"What's going on here?"<79>
One person rushed forward and grabbed Ch'unsam's hand.
"You bastard, let go of her!"
From the crowd that had gathered, someone shouted and dashed forward, slapping Ch'unsam hard across the ear. Ch'unsam collapsed.
"Hey, you bastard! You son of a whore! Grabbing the hair of someone like your own mother!"
The old woman, teeth clenched tightly, eyes blazing, lunged at Ch'unsam again.
"Mother, please stop!"
"Auntie, stop it! Eek, you crazy bastard!"
From front and back, people tried to restrain the old woman while kicking and cursing Ch'unsam.
"Aigoo! My heart is bursting!"
The old woman, choked with tears and unable to cry properly, pounded her chest, and then her cries gradually grew louder.
"Smiling while seeing those unpleasant sights, I saved those things... *sob* *sob*... childless, finding my joy in them! Ooong *sob sob*! Ooong!"
The old woman abruptly stopped crying, gathered her hair, and tied it up.
"Hmph, just you wait, you bastard! I'll go to your house and pull out the pillar roots!"
she yelled, rushing out the door. A fierce glint shone in her eyes. Her face was livid, alternating between dark and blue.
The crowd gathered at the doorway stepped back.

2
The chilly winter day was already nearing evening. The fact that her husband, who left at dawn, hadn't returned made her heart pound anxiously. He was a man who usually found fault whenever he came back from outside, and today, having left without breakfast after his dawn tantrum,<80> he would surely either be drinking somewhere or come bursting through the gate shouting aggressively—thinking this, Hakpŏm's mother's heart beat like that of a patient entering the operating room (手術室), unable to calm down.
In over twenty years of marriage, she had never seen a clear sky. Her father had consented to her marriage to Ch'unsam because he was supposedly of noble birth (*yangban*), intelligent, and well-off. And so, Hakpŏm's mother married at fifteen. Her mother had lamented,
"She's still such a child, where are you sending her!"
but her father had insisted,
"Child? A girl of fifteen could have borne children already!"
At that time, her father received five hundred *nyang* as dowry money.
Four years after she married, her mother-in-law passed away, and that autumn, her own father passed away. Then, five years after her mother-in-law died, her father-in-law passed away. At that time, Hakpŏm was four years old.
From the day his father died, Ch'unsam completely shut the door on his past responsibilities. Thereafter, his pursuits were alcohol, women, card games (*kolp'ae*), gambling (*t'ujŏn*), and fighting. Eventually, he even earned the nickname "liquor fiend." What little farmland they had was gone in no time, and even the deed to the house ended up in someone else's hands. Furthermore, whatever Hakpŏm's mother managed to save bit by bit by raising chickens and pigs or doing needlework for hire, he snatched away for liquor money or gambling debts. When even that wasn't enough, he would pick fights over trivial matters, beat his wife, smash the furniture, and cause mayhem. Finally, he even sold off his wife's family home, forcing his mother-in-law to come live with her daughter, enduring all sorts of abuse and contempt from her son-in-law until she passed away last winter. As she died, she held Hakpŏm's and her daughter's wrists, weeping bitterly, and died with her eyes open.
"Hakpŏm's mother! A person's life (一生) is bitter. Hakpŏm's father will surely regret his ways someday, so you must not cause trouble,<81> endure patiently, and be obedient. If you keep a good heart, you will be rewarded. Raising Hakpŏm well, isn't that a blessing in itself..."
"Huu! Anyway, your father was wicked. Aigoo, how pitiful is our Hakpŏm's mother!"
she said, and silent tears fell drop by drop from her gradually fading eyes. At that time, Hakpŏm was eight years old.
After her mother died, Hakpŏm's mother felt even more lonely. The more severe her husband's tyranny became, the more obedient she was. She washed his clothes every other day, and tried hard to ensure the dinner table never lacked side dishes. She pinned her single thread of hope on Hakpŏm. Sometimes she secretly wished she could just die, but then she thought, would she bring shame upon the husband she had served patiently until now? When she thought of the growing Hakpŏm wandering the streets helpless and alone, the thread of life pulled her more strongly than the thread of death. She secretly prayed to endure any hardship, educate Hakpŏm, see him married, and then die.
This morning too, after her husband left, she calmed her pounding heart, swept the front and back yards clean, prepared breakfast, set aside a separate meal for her husband, offered food at her mother's spirit altar (*yŏngjwa*), fed Hakpŏm, and sent him to school. Then she spent the entire day doing laundry smoothing (*tadŭmi*) and needlework. Even the sound of footsteps outside made her heart pound, thinking her husband was returning, and the sound of a child crying anywhere made her run out, wondering if Hakpŏm was crying.
As she was gathering her work materials to prepare dinner, a sound of "Waa—" accompanied by hurried footsteps was heard, and the *chŏngji* door burst open. Hakpŏm's mother jumped up startled, as if cold water had been thrown on her back, crying "Hŭk! Ek!". Rushing through the door was an old woman with her hair completely disheveled. Having experienced such things more than once, Hakpŏm's mother's heart sank when she saw the old woman. It felt as if ice had suddenly filled her veins. Her cheeks turned pale, and her large, round eyes lost their strength.<82>
"Oh my? Eh! What brings you here like this? My husband must have caused some trouble again!"
Hakpŏm's mother grabbed the old woman's wrist. The old woman immediately turned towards the *chowang* (kitchen shelf) area, saying,
"That bastard! Hmph, do you think I'll just let your husband's house be? I'll pull out the very handle of this bastard's house cauldron! Let go! Let go of me!"
She screamed at the top of her lungs. Hakpŏm's mother forced a pained smile and wrapped her arms around the old woman's waist.
"Mother! Please calm down. Listen to me! Yes... My husband must have had some drinks and disrespected you!"
"Hey, you bitch! Let go of me! Your husband destroyed my household goods (*kajŏng torip*). I'll rip the lid off your house's cauldron!"
Household goods destroyed! Hearing that all her furniture had been smashed made Hakpŏm's mother's heart drop with a thud. One pulling forward, the other pulling back—force met force, and Hakpŏm's mother and the old woman wrestled, rolling on the floor.
People gathered.
"Let go, you bitch!"
The old woman pulled Hakpŏm's mother's hair.
"Mother! Please, for my sake, stop! I will repay everything!"
Hakpŏm's mother's voice was weak and sorrowful, like the plea of the powerless crushed under great authority.
People, both men and women, gathered and stopped the fight.
"Aigoo! What a wicked man! Because he met a bad husband (*sŭna*), even this Buddha-like wife (*eminyŏ*) can't live in peace!"
"The wife (*emine*) is truly unmatched, like Hakpŏm's mother! How can that man prosper after treating such a wife so poorly?"
Thanks to the intervention of many people, the old woman sat down hesitantly. Hakpŏm's mother sat before the old woman, who was pounding the ground and wailing,<83> and said,
"I will repay everything! I have a pig I'm raising, and also some woven hemp cloth (*poe cchan sam*), I'll sell those and repay you, Mother, please, for my sake, calm down!"
The old woman left. The gathered people also left. Hakpŏm, who had been weeping, lay asleep near the hearth (*kamamok*). The house felt desolate, like a house in mourning. Hakpŏm's mother sat with her knees drawn up, staring blankly at the window. Everything felt like a dream. But it wasn't a dream. The image of herself, like a flower wilting from frost, floated before her eyes. She felt utterly lonely and desolate. Sorrow welled up inside her. She longed for her deceased mother. She silently let hot tears fall onto Hakpŏm's cheek. Her husband felt exceedingly hateful and resentful. The thought of having to sell even the pig she had raised through cold and heat for her mother's memorial rite made her heart feel like it was crumbling. Then, she wiped her tears and tried not to think about anything. Resenting her husband and shedding tears felt like some ominous sign, so she stopped herself.
Hakpŏm's mother cried again during the evening meal offering. Kneeling before her mother's spirit altar, she sobbed out her accumulated sorrow as if confiding her grievances. The hot tears streaming down seemed to thoroughly wash away the dark shadow enveloping her body. Her heart felt reassured and relieved, as if embraced by her mother's warm bosom and hearing her gentle words.
"Mommy (*chema*)! What's wrong? Do you miss Grandma (*oek'ŭnamae*)? Mm..."
Hakpŏm, who had been eating, came and stood beside his mother. She quickly wiped her tears. She didn't want young Hakpŏm to see her crying.
"Mm... I'm crying because I miss Grandma. Don't you miss Grandma too? *Sniff*."
"I miss Grandma too! Ha—"<84>
Looking up and looking down, warm smiles bloomed in the eyes of the mother and son. Hakpŏm's mother, moved by an impulse she couldn't explain, hugged Hakpŏm tightly. Their warm cheeks rubbed together.
The fading twilight crept into the room. Silence enveloped the surroundings.

3
It was past the second watch of the night.
Ch'unsam still hadn't returned. Hakpŏm's mother, taking Hakpŏm with her, had searched all the houses he might have gone to, but he wasn't there. Worried he might have gotten drunk and collapsed on the street, she searched rough alleys and quiet alleys, but still couldn't find him.
With no other choice, she returned home, put Hakpŏm to bed, sat down by the lamp, and began her needlework.
The night grew deeper. All around was quiet. The hissing oil lamp occasionally dropped sparks, making popping sounds and flickering. Each time, Hakpŏm's mother paused her sewing and trimmed the wick with a metal poker. A chilling coldness seeped through her thin clothes into her skin. She tucked Hakpŏm's blanket tighter around him and sighed deeply.
*Rustle, scratch, scratch*
At the sound, she glanced towards the window and strained her ears. Her heart thumped and trembled violently.
*Rustle rustle, scratch scratch*
It was the sound of rats scurrying in the back room. She seemed relieved, finally, and turned her eyes back to her work. But her heart still trembled. Even the sound of the wind outside sounded like shuffling footsteps, making her heart pound and tightening her chest. Her stomach also ached dully, perhaps because the fetus (胎兒) had been startled by the evening's commotion, and she couldn't focus on her work. She clutched her stomach and looked at the lamp. The lamp flame seemed to multiply into two, three, four, and finally countless fire-drops<85> floated around, scattering and disappearing, disappearing and scattering. Large and small, red and blue, those fire-drops looked like her husband's drunken eyes. She shut her eyes tightly, trying not to see them. A large shadow stood behind her, silently grasping her neck. She opened her eyes wide and turned her head. There was nothing there. Shivering, she looked around. From the dark corners, something seemed to be staring at her with wide, unblinking eyes. She steeled her heart. She tried to forget everything. She started sewing again. But the more she tried not to think, the more the eyes hidden in the corners seemed to watch her, and the shadow seemed to stand behind her, making it impossible not to turn her head. Yet, when she kept her head straight, it felt like it was still there, making it difficult to stay still and difficult to turn. She opened the door to the middle room. Her mother's spirit altar was in that room. Leaving it open made her feel as if her mother was watching over her, warming her heart slightly.
*Creak*—the sound of the brushwood gate opening was heard. Following it was the sound of someone clearing their throat, wrenching phlegm from deep within, and clumsy footsteps. Her heart thumped and pounded. She quickly stood up and went outside.
The cold air enveloped her body. She shivered violently. The sky was dark blue, dotted with stars. The snow piled on the neighbors' roofs and under the fences looked like piles of laundry in the darkness. Staggering unsteadily into the yard was clearly Ch'unsam's shadow. The woman stood silently at the bottom of the stone steps. The man, stumbling in, stopped abruptly and swayed, looking at the woman.
"Ugh! So, the master of the house (*sŭna*) doesn't even get breakfast, huh? Only the womenfolk (*emine nyŏndŏl*) eat! Heh heh, the house is—ugh, Hek tui!"
The woman caught the arm of the man who looked about to collapse.
"Hurry (*nalnae*) inside! Go in and lie down!"
"What! So you won't give me food? Hek tui! Just stuffing yourselves till your bellies burst... Ugh... I come home this late, and you don't even look for me? Hmph!"<86>
The man shook off the woman's hand. The woman stumbled backward as if about to fall, barely regained her balance, and pulled the man up onto the wooden porch floor.
"Aigoo, look at this! Hmph... Earlier, Hakpŏm and I looked everywhere for you, but you weren't there!"
Her low voice was gentle.
"What did you say? So it would be good if the wife went around looking for her husband! It would be great gossip that so-and-so's wife wanders around looking for her husband! Hmph!"
Ch'unsam sat down in the lower part of the *chŏngji*. The woman took off his shoes and outer coat. His hat was gone somewhere, his head bare and disheveled. Ch'unsam's body swayed back and forth like a scholar reciting Mencius (孟子).
"So, aren't you giving me food?"
"I'm preparing it now!"
The rice bowl that had been on the hearth (*puttumak*), the stew that had been on the brazier—she set the table like this.
Ch'unsam poked aggressively at the rice with his chopsticks.
"Are you feeding me this leftover mixed-grain rice now?"
he demanded, glaring at the woman. The woman, sitting subserviently at the head of the table, replied,
"Aigoo! No, it's what we were eating. A little bit of millet set aside got mixed in!"
Her voice was meek. She paid close attention to her husband's every move.
"By the way, why are the side dishes like this!"
"I didn't have money today, so I couldn't buy meat!"
"What happened to the dried pollack (*myŏngt'ae*) hanging outside!"
The man's eyes widened. The woman paused for a moment, then said,
"That's for Mother's memorial rite!"<87>
The words barely escaped her lips.
"What did you say? Memorial rite or dwarf rite, what the hell is all that?"
The woman sniffled. *Sob sob*, she choked back tears. She covered her eyes with her skirt hem.
"You damned bitch, why do you cry at the drop of a hat? What kind of bad luck are you inviting?"
With the words came a crash—the dining table was slammed down on the woman's head. The woman shot up.
"That damn ghost gate (*sangmun*, slang for memorial altar) or dog's leg, I should smash it!"
The man rushed into the room and started smashing the spirit altar with loud bangs. Ah—can Mother not rest in peace even after death? Thinking this, the woman's heart felt like it was melting away piece by piece. She had nothing left to fear. She started towards the room. Hakpŏm followed, crying.
"If you're going to kill someone, kill me!"
The woman stood before the man, spreading her hands to shield the spirit altar. The man grabbed the woman's arms, threw her onto the floor, stomped on her, then dragged her out to the brightly lit *chŏngji*. Hakpŏm cried loudly, stamping his feet.
Neighbors rushed over in a crowd.
"This man is drunk again!"
One person grabbed Ch'unsam around the waist. Another pulled the woman's hair from Ch'unsam's grasp, shouting,
"Hey man, stop it!"
"Leave her alone, I'm going to kill this bitch!"
Ch'unsam's powerful fist landed on the woman's chest through the restraining arms of the people.
"Aigogo! Ooong *sob*!"
"Is this man crazy?"<88>
"Aigoo, how horrible!"
The neighboring women trembled.
Pulled away by several people, Hakpŏm's mother barely managed to escape. Ch'unsam sat down, panting heavily. The people who had intervened stood silently, looking at each other, then at Ch'unsam and Hakpŏm's mother. Hakpŏm stood beside his father, still crying loudly.
"Hey, you brat! Shut up!"
Struck by Ch'unsam's fist like a scarecrow's, Hakpŏm cried out,
"Aigogok, Mo-mmy-"
screaming as if his breath was cut off.
Hakpŏm's mother, who had reached the back door trying to escape, suddenly turned back, looked at her husband and snapped,
"You're mad. What crime has the child committed?"
Her eyes were bloodshot.
"You bitch!"
Ch'unsam roared like thunder and jumped up. In his hands were the pounding stones (*pangch'it-tol*) that had been lying in the corner behind him.
"Get out! Get out through the back door!"
"Grab those stones!"
Before the shouts of the crowd ended, with a grunt of "Ung!", the pounding stone flew towards the back door.<89>
"Aigo... uung..."
With a bang—as the back door flew open, Hakpŏm's mother collapsed. Everyone cried out,
"Aaak!"
"Aigoo!"
"Aigoo, oh dear!"
The shouts seemed to shake the house. Everyone trembled violently. Ch'unsam, hit by someone, collapsed with a bleeding nose.
Everyone rushed towards the back.
Like a waterlogged corpse laid on a straw mat (*sojilma*), Hakpŏm's mother lay with her lower back caught on the threshold. The pounding stone had crushed her lower back and buttocks. Reddish blood flowed steadily from her trouser leg. She had also suffered a miscarriage (落產) when she fell.
Several people lifted Hakpŏm's mother by her head and legs. Her broken back dragged on the ground. An old woman supported her lower back.
"Hakpŏm-ah! Ik-ing, Aigoo, Hakpŏm's father! Kkeok!... Eh!"
Laid in the room, Hakpŏm's mother faintly called out in a whisper and quietly passed away.
"Ooong Mommy (*chema*)! Aigoo my mommy! Ooong!"
Hakpŏm clung to his mother's neck, weeping bitterly. The child's bone-chilling, heart-wrenching cries moistened everyone's eyes.
"Aha... May you at least go to a better place in death!"
Some woman murmured with a sigh.

4
"Yes... Please, just let me see her once..."
Sober now, perhaps, Ch'unsam's voice was clear. The front of his clothes was red from the nosebleed.
"You bastard, stop your whining! Get walking!"
The policeman holding the restraining rope (*p'osŭng*) glared fiercely.
"It was just her time to die.... Officer! Just once, let me see Hakpŏm's mother's face just once... Uuuuk"<90>
He choked out the words, trying to push through the gathered crowd towards the upper part of the room. The policeman slapped Ch'unsam's cheek so hard it seemed to flash, shouting,
"You brat, still don't fear the law? Get walking! No more talk!"
and dragged him outside.
"Ooong... ong *sob*... Even if you kill me... Aigoo... Hakpŏm's mother, just once... just let me see... see..."
He sobbed, choked with tears.
The crowd, enveloped in a faint melancholy (哀愁) and fear (恐怖), was silent as if water had been sprinkled over them.
"Haha, serves you right! You wretched Ch'unsam!"
With this desolate cry, the sound of sharp clapping came from the yard, and bursting through the *padang* door was the old woman from the tavern.
"Haha, you bastard Ch'unsam! Stabbing this old woman's heart with nails... killing your saint-like wife... do you think you'll prosper?... May lightning strike you! Lightning..."
The old woman's eyes blazed.
"Shh—The constable is here!"
Someone cautioned the old woman.
"Yes... Officer... Aigoo..."
Tears fell drop by drop from Ch'unsam's eyes.
"Get walking!"
"*Baka yarō!* (Idiot!)"
"Eku!"
Kicked by the Japanese policeman standing nearby, Ch'unsam was dragged out and tumbled onto the cold ground below the steps.
"Ooong... *sob sob*... Mo... Mo-mmy—Aigo my mommy (*chema*)! Uung!"
Hakpŏm remained in the upper room, rubbing his face against his mother's cheek, wailing sorrowfully.
The crowd, gripped by an unknown fear, wiped away their tears.<91>

**Summary**

This harrowing short story depicts the final day in the life of Hakpŏm's mother, the long-suffering wife of the violent alcoholic Ch'unsam. After Ch'unsam gets drunk, assaults a tavern owner, and destroys her property, he returns home late at night. He picks a fight with his wife over trivial matters like the food, beats her, smashes her deceased mother's memorial altar, and in a final act of brutality witnessed by neighbors, kills her by throwing heavy laundry pounding stones at her as she tries to escape. The police arrive and arrest the weeping Ch'unsam, while their young son Hakpŏm grieves over his mother's body. The story starkly portrays the devastating effects of alcoholism, domestic violence, and poverty on a family in colonial Korea, emphasizing the utter helplessness and tragic fate of the wife. For historians, the story offers a visceral glimpse into the social realities of the period, particularly the extreme vulnerability of women within the patriarchal family structure, the normalization of violence fueled by alcohol, the grinding poverty, and the blend of traditional customs (like memorial rites) with brutal modernity (the presence of police). The use of older Korean spellings and dialect terms also provides linguistic data.

1
구들이 차다는 트집으로 안해를 실컨 때리고 나선 춘삼이는 낫전에 술이 흙가티 취하엿다. 흥글 멍글하고 남의 집 대문압혜 서서 오줌을 쒜쒜 쏫다가 그 집 늙은 부인한테 욕을 톡톡히 먹엇건만 그래도 빙글빙글 웃고 골목길을 걸엇다. 길을 것는지, 춤을 추는지, 뼈가 빠진 동물가티 이리 흥글 저리 멍글, 이리 비틀 저리 주춤 내려오다가 조고마한 쪽대문에 들어서서 정지(부억방) 문을 펄적 열엇다.
「아즈망이! 술 한잔 주오?」
그는 신 신은채 정지 아래목에 씰어젓다. 바당(부억, 북도는 부억과 안방새이에 벽 업시 한데 통하엿다. 바당이란 것은 부억이고 정지는 부억에 잇는 안방이다)에서 불을 때이든 늙수구러한 부인은
「어듸서 저리 처질넛누! 엑 개장시」
하고 입속으로 뇌이면서 헤를 툭 채엇다.
「아 하 그래 술을 안준단 말이오!」
총마진 사람가티 아래목에 씨러저서 씨근덕 씨근덕하든 춘삼이는 벌걱 이러나 안젓다. 두 팔로 압흘 버티고 안진 그는 금시 씰어질 듯이 흥떵 멍떵 한다.＜76＞
「에구 취햇구 나! 생원이 집에가서 자구오오! 그러문 내 국을 끌여두오리!」
억지로 웃음을 뵈는 노파의 이마ㅅ살은 펴지지 못하엿다.
「에- 무무 무시기라오?」
그는 술이 줄줄 흐를듯이 거불거불한 눈으로 노파를 치어다 보앗다.
「그그 그래 수술을 안준단 말이오? 내게 돈이 업나? 내가 술갑슬 잘가먹엇나? 어쨋단 말이우? 자 여긔 여여여기 돈! 돈이...」
하면서 그는 두루막 압섭을 헤치고 좃기 홋주머니엔 손을 넛는다. 어이업다는 눈으로 물끄럼이 그 꼴을 보든 주인 노파는 허허 웃스면서 주정ㅅ군 압흐로 오더니
「생원이사 내 속을 뻔히 알지? 내 어듸 그럽데? 돈이? 생원의게 손이 엇지 업겟소? 돈이 업서두 줄처진데, 돈이 잇다는데 주기 실여서 안주겟소? 시방 취햇스니 이따가 잡수!」
하고 로파는 푸러진 춘삼의 옷고롬을 바로 매주엇다.
「내가 술갑을 잘가먹을 것 갓소? 에튀 흐흐흐.」
그는 억개를 읏슥하고 머리를 흔들흔들하면서 코 우슴을 첫다.
「글세 뉘가 잘각먹는담메? 또 잘니면 어때서? 내 그만 꺼 생원의게 잘렷다구 송사를 하겟슴메? 하하 어서 좀 가자오!」
노파는 얼넝얼넝하면서 춘삼의 허리를 안아 일으켯다.
「이게 무슨 짓이오? 이이이것 놋소! 뉘가 늙은거 조타구하오? 흥」
춘삼이는 몸을 틀면서 노파를 두 손으로 콱 밀첫다. 그는 머슥히 밀려서잇는 노파를 보면서
「하하하 그래 술 안주겟소? 한 잔만 딱 먹겟소!」
하면서 궁덩이를 질질 끌고 붓도막에 들안젓다. 얼엇든 신발이 뜻뜻한 방안에 들어오니 녹어서 흙물이 번지르르 자리에 그림을 그혓다.＜77＞
「그래 꼭 한잔만 죽게 먹구 가겟소?」
노파는 「네 참말로 한 잔만 먹고 그만 둘테냐?」하는 눈추리로 춘삼을 보앗다. 춘삼이는 빙긋 웃으면서 혀구분 소리로―
「가구말구 한 잔만 주우!」
주인노파는 한숨을 휴 쉬이고 웃간으로 가드니 공상(정지 웃목에 벽을 의지하야 삼층으로 시렁을 매는데, 맨 밋층은 공상이라하야 쌀독가티 크고 묵어운 것을 노코, 가운데 층은 조왕이라하야 사발, 공기가티 거벼운 것을 언고, 마지막 층은 덕대라 하야 밥상을 언는다)에 노힌 조그마한 단지(항아리)에서 술을 대졉에 반만침 떠다가 푸접업시 쓱 내밀엇다. 춘삼이는 바덧다. 그는 흥글흥글하고 술 대졉을 한참 보드니
「흐흐 이 술을 주면서 속으로야 욕을 좀 하리?」
하고 목을 점점 뒤로 잿기면서 소 물켜듯, 꿀걱꿀걱 마신다. 주인노파는 점점 들리는 턱아래 분주히 오르내리는 목뼈를 흘겨보면서 혜를 툭 채엇다.
「으윽 왝!」
춘삼은 입에서 술 대졉을 떼두마듯하야 억게를 읏슥하고 목아지를 찔눅하면서 머리를 압흐로 숙엿다. 코와 입으로 시티한 걸듸건 물이 폭포가티 쏘다졋다.
「엑 개장시야! 엑 추졉아!」
주인노파는 벌꺽 일어서면서 춘삼이를 흘겨보앗다.
「무시게 어찌구 어째?」
춘삼이는 두루막 소매로 입을 씨스면서 노파를 노려보앗다. 담박 서리가튼 호령이나 내릴것 갓다. 노파는 몸을 벌벌 떨면서
「그러문 개장시 아니고 무시기야?」＜78＞
악스럽게 한 마듸 쏘앗다.
「무어 개장시라니? 이 쌍놈으 노친 가트니.」
춘삼이는 압헤 노흔 술대졉을 머리우에 번적 집어들엇다. 노파는 웃간으로 피해서면서
「조타! 그 색기 미첫는 게다! 술으 먹엇지. 똥물으 먹엇는 갭네!」
하는 소리가 떠러지두 마두하야
「으응! 이놈으 년 가트니」
하는 춘삼의 울엉찬 소리와 가티 그 손예 잡혓든 대졉은 쏜살가티 조왕에 던저젓다. 짝근, 쩨 그르륵― 대졉이 떠러지는 고데 보기조케 싸하 노핫든 그릇들은 산산히 부서지고 들들 굴러 떠러젓다. 공상에 노핫든 독들도 떠러지는 그릇에 부다처서 탁 깨젓다. 주인노파는 몸을 부르르 떨고 니를 빡갈엇다.
「이놈아 기장은 왜치니? 응 죽여라! 죽여라! 나까지 잡아 먹어라!」
주인노파는 악을 쓰고 덤벼들엇다. 춘삼의 의복은 찟겻다. 그의 뺨은 노파의 손톱에 글켜서 피가 흘럿다.
「이 미친놈아! 늙은년이 푼푼이 모아서 일워 노흔 그릇을 무슨턱으로 부시단말이냐? 내게 무슨 죄냐? 내 술갑을 내라! 생원님, 생원님하니 침때나 놋는 체 한다구! 이놈아 내 술갑슬 육십여 양이나 지구두... 그래도 나는 혼연이 가티 지냇다. 이 가슴이 터지는 것두 꾹꾹 참아왓다.」
노파는 죽을뚱 살뚱 몰으로 덤빈다. 춘삼이는 노파의 머리채를 휘여잡엇다.
「애고고! 이놈이 사람을 죽이는구나!」
춘삼의 억세인 발은 노파의 허리에 다엇다.
바당문은 열엿다. 정지문도 열엿다. 사람들은 모혀 들엇다.
「이게 어찐일이오?」＜79＞
한사람이 우우 달려들어서 춘삼의 손을 잡엇다.
「이놈아 이것을 못놀테냐?」
오그그 모혀든 속에서 한사람이 소리를 치면서 내달드니 춘삼의 귓벽을 철석 갈겻다. 춘삼이는 씰어젓다.
「야 이놈아 이 호로색기야! 네 에미가튼 사람의 머리를 끌어!」
노파는 앙드그륵 악물고 두 눈에 불이 휑해서 춘삼에게 달려들엇다.
「어마니 그만 참소!」
「아즈머니 그만 두시우! 엑 미친놈!」
압뒤예서는 일변 노파를 말니고 일번 춘삼을 차고 욕한다.
「에구! 가슴이 터저라!」
노파는 목이 메여 울지 못하고 가삼을 쾅쾅 치드니 차츰 울음소리가 커젓다.
「그 아니꼬은 꼴을 웃고 보면서 모아노흔 것을...흙! 흙!... 자식두 업는 것이 그것으로 락을 삼든 것을! 어엉 흙흙! 어엉!」
노파는 울음을 뚝 끈치고 머리를 트러 언드니
「응 이놈 보자! 네 놈의 집을 가서 기둥뿌리들 빼오겟다」
하고 문으로 내달엇다. 그 두 눈에는 굿세인 광채가 서리엇다. 낫비츤 검으락 푸르락하엿다.
문압헤 모아섯든 군중을 뒷거름을 첫다.

2
으스스한 겨울날은 어느새 저녁때가 갓가웟다. 새벽 나간 사내가 들오지 안는 것이 퍽 마음이 컹겻다. 보통 때에도 나갓다 들오면 트집을 툭툭 부리는 사람이 오늘은 새벽트집을 쓰고 아침도＜80＞ 먹지 안코 나갓스니 반드시 어듸가서 술을 먹거나, 그러치 안으면 대문어구에서 부터 부푸른 소리를 치고 들어 올 것이다― 이러케 생각하는 학범어미의 가삼은 수술실(手術室)로 들어가는 병자의 가삼가티 두군두군하야 진졍할 수 업다.
시집사리 이십여년에 맑은 하늘이라고 보지 못하엿다. 근본이 양반이오, 사람이 똑똑하고, 돈냥도 넉넉하다하야 아버지가 춘삼에게 허락한 것이다. 그리하야 학범어미는 열다섯에 시집을 왓다. 어머니는
「아직 나가 어린것을 어듸로 보내겟소!」
하고 애석해 하는 것을 아버지가
「나가 어리긴? 게집이 나가 열다섯이면 자식을 나헛겟는데!」
이러케 욱엿다. 그때 아버지는 딸 혼수전으로 오백냥을 바덧다.
그가 시집와서 사년만에 시어머니 돌아가시고, 그해 가을에 친정 아버지가 돌아가섯다. 그리고 시어머니 돌아가신지 오년만에 시아버지가 돌아가섯다. 그때 학범의 나히 네살이엇다.
춘삼이는 아버지가 돌아가신 날부터 전ㅅ방문을 다다 채여버렷다. 그 뒤로 그의 업은 술, 게집, 골패, 투전, 싸홈이엇다. 나종은 술게걸이라는 별명까지 바덧다. 밧고랑이나 잇든 것은 어느 틈에 다 날가 버리고 집 문권까지 남의 손에 가버렷다. 그리구는 학범어미가 닭도 치고, 도야지도 길으로, 삭바누질도 하야 푼푼히 모흔 것까지 술갑, 투전채로 쪽쪽 훌텃다. 그것두 부족하야 생트집을 툭툭 부리고 녀편네를 때린다, 세간을 모은다, 야단을 첫다. 나종은 처가집까지 팔아 업시여서 친정어머니는 딸을 따라와서 가티 일스면서 사위의 가진 학대와 괄시를 밧다가 작년겨을에 돌아가섯다. 그는 죽을 때에 학범이와 딸의 손목을 잡고 설ㅅ게 설ㅅ게 울다가 눈 못 갑고 죽엇다.
「학범엄마! 사람의 한뉘(一生)라는게 쓸이니라. 학범아바지가 후회할 날이 잇겟스니 너는 일걸골을＜81＞  내지 말고 공내를 하고 순죵해라. 마음을 잘 쓰면 다 그 갑은 밧너니라. 학범이 잘자라도 그게 복 밧는 게 아니냐...」
「휴유! 어쨋든 네 아비가 못된 것이너니라. 에구 참 불상도하지 우리 학범 어미는!」
하고 점점 틀려가는 눈에서 소리업는 눈물이 방울방울 흘럿다. 때는 학범의 나이 여덜이엇다.
어머니 도라가신 뒤로 학범어미는 더욱 고적하엿다. 그는 사내의 횡포가 심하면 심할수록 종순하엿다. 의복은 이튼 건너 사흘 건너 빨앗고, 밥상에는 반찬이 떠러지지 안토록 애썻다. 그는 한 줄의 희망을 학범에게 부텻다. 어떤 때는 슬그머니 죽어버리고도 십헛스나 이때까지 참아오면서 모시든 사내에게 드러운 허물이나 가지 안을가? 나날이 커가는 학범이가 의지가지 업시 길거리에 혜매일 것을 생각하는 때면, 삶의 줄이 죽음의 줄보다 더 굿세게 그를 끌엇다. 그는 어떤 고생이든지 참어가면서라도 학범이를 공부식히고 장가들인 뒤에 죽기를 은근히 빌엇다.
이날 아츰에도 사내가 나간 뒤에 그는 울렁거리는 가삼을 진정해 가면서 압 뒤 뜰을 말끔 쓸어 노코 아츰을 지허서 사내상은 따로 채려 놋코, 어머니 령좌(靈座)에 상식하고, 학범이도 먹여서 학교에 보내엇다. 그리고 다드미, 바느질로 진종일을 보내엇다. 박게서 발자최만 들려도 사내가 오는 듯해서 가삼이 두군두군하고, 어듸서 어린애 울음소리만 들려도 학범이가 울지안는가하야 뛰어나가 보앗다.

「녜... 제발 한번만 보게...」
술이 깨엇는지 춘삼의 소리는 똑똑하다. 기의 옷 압흔 코피가 흘러서 벍엇타.
「이놈이 웬 잔소리야 어서 걸어!」
포승을 잡은 순사는 눈을 딱 부릅떳다.
「그저 제가 죽을 때라 그랫스니....나리! 한번만 학범어미의 나를 한번만 보게...으으윽」＜90＞
그는 목 메인 소리를 하면서 모혀 선 사람을 밀치고 웃목으로 가려고 한다. 순사는 춘삼의 뺨을 불이 번적나게 갈기면서
「이 자식이 그래두 법 무서운 줄 몰으나? 어서 걸어! 잡말 말고」
하고 박그로 내끌엇다.
「어엉...엉 흙...죽여 주드라도...에구....학범어미를 한번...한번만 보...보...」
그는 꺽꺽 목메여 운다.
엷은 애수(哀愁)와 공포(恐怖)에 쌔인 군중은 물을 뿌려논 듯이 고요하다.
「하하 잘됏구나! 이몹슬 춘삼아!」
하는 처량한 부르지즘과 가티 짯짯 손벽소리가 가뜰에서 나드니 바당문으로 툭 퉤들오는 것은 술집노파다.
「하하 네 이놈 춘삼아! 이 늙은 가삼에 못을 밧고...성인가튼 네게집을 잡아 먹구두...네 무슨 잘 되겟니?...벼락을 마즈리라! 벼락을...」
노파의 두 눈에는 불이 환하다.
「쉬-순검이 왓소!」
누군지 노파에게 주의를 식혓다.
「네...나리...에구...」
춘삼이는 눈물을 방울방울 떠러트렷다.
「어서 걸어!」
「빠가 야로!」
「에쿠!」
겨테 섯든 일본순사의 구두발에 채여서 끌려나가든 춘삼이는 축대아래 찬 땅에 걱구러젓다.
「어엉...흙흙...제...제-마-에이고 내제마(엄마)! 으응!」
