Author: Pak Tol-i (acting as spokesperson)
Article Type: 소식 (News/Report)

A Plea to You Humans After Five Years of Life Behind Bars

A First Public Greeting
To Koreans, Japanese, Chinese, Westerners, without distinction, I greet you all. Five long years have already passed since I came to Chosŏn. I wanted to offer greetings and lament my situation to humans earlier, but I never had the chance. In the meantime, I assume you know my face behind these iron bars, each and every one of you. So, are you all well?

I hear this year is called Pyŏnginnyŏn? You human scoundrels say that years with the character 'In' (寅), like Pyŏnginnyŏn, are the Year of the Tiger? Anyway, thank you. The Year of the Tiger! The Year of the Tiger! Men and women, old and young, all praise us together, so I am thankful regardless. As the opportunity is right, I face one of you human scoundrels, Pak Tol-i-gun, to offer greetings and訴 complain about my entire past life to all humans. Don't be scared, don't run away, please just listen. However, I do not seek sympathy.

I am from Southern Asia. It has been too long since I left my hometown,<98> so I don't remember the exact name of my birthplace. It seems it was somewhere between southern China and northern India. The name humans usually call me is Ch'ikpŏm (칡범 - Striped Tiger), and I was cruelly separated from my husband when captured by humans back then; I have no news of my children either, forever unknown. My age is now perhaps thirteen or fourteen, and my daily life, as you know, is this life behind iron bars. Ah, it's suffocating! When I think of the tens of thousands of my kin coming and going freely in the great T'aesan mountains, even though I bear the manly nickname San'gun (山君 - Mountain Lord), my heart feels heavy! Will there ever be a day of freedom for me?!

Moreover, they say "Chosŏn is the land of tigers," right? They say there are many of my kin in Chosŏn? They say they are on Paektu-san, on Kŭmgang-san, and even on Inwang-san? They say there's no place in Chosŏn without tigers? They even say the topography itself is shaped like a tiger? Anyway, I am glad. You Chosŏn people, I am glad to see you too. I am especially gladder than seeing the Japanese, who they say don't even have the shadow of a tiger. Now, please listen to my tale of woe.

Our Nickname is King of the Mountains
In physique, strength, skill, weaponry, majesty, and fierceness, there is none who can match us. There is Mr. Raion (Lion), but don't even the human scoundrels, who fancy themselves the lords of all creation, call us the King of the Mountains? Don't they call us San'gun, the chief of all beasts? Don't they refer to a brave general as a tiger-like commander, a loud voice as a tiger's roar, speed as being like a flying tiger (飛虎), and represent fear by saying "The tiger is coming!" or "Met a tiger!"? (When children fret, don't they always say "The tiger is coming!"? Though these days, it seems policemen are quite scary too...). Is that all? Isn't a well-shaped mountain called a White Tiger form (白虎形), a handsome face called a tiger face, fine whiskers called tiger whiskers (虎鬚), and even severe and frightening diseases called cholera (虎列刺)? Just as there was a fake Li Kui (假李逵) in the time of Li Kui (李逵) (from Suhoji - 水滸誌), isn't there even the saying, "In a cave without tigers, the wildcat acts like a tiger (無虎洞中狸作虎)"? What about our imposing presence?

When we roar once, T'aesan roars with us; when we get angry once, all beasts tremble; when we run once, the wind chases us with a whoosh; and when we decide to eat once, don't deer, rabbits, and pigs offer their entire bodies? Would magpies chirp in unison at a mere stretch, or the forest stir slightly at a mere yawn? How about that? Our imposing presence!

We tread upon T'aesan, eat nothing but meat, and wear<99> five-colored fur coats that even human kings cannot wear! How about that? Also, we have value even in death. Tiger skin is a treasure praised by all, currently worth ten thousand nyang; tiger bones are famed medicine for human ailments; and tiger claws are commonly used for human ornaments, are they not? How about that? Are we truly not the King of the Mountains? Who could possibly challenge us? Even with such an imposing presence, due to sheer bad luck, I fell into the poisonous hands of humans and continue this suffering! I think of that human named Napoleon on the island of Saint Helena (센트헤레니島), and also that human Kaiser (가이젤) in the Dutch Castle (和蘭城 - likely referring to Huis Doorn, Netherlands), and the prisoners in Sŏdaemun Prison, Kongdŏk-ri, and all other prisons. Is this a world where one cannot live without such things? You human scoundrels!

Childhood and Adulthood
As I said earlier, I am from Southern Asia. Whether it was India or China, I don't know, but my birthplace was a large cave deep in the mountains south of the K'ollun-san (崑崙山). I was born 13 or 14 years ago as the eldest daughter of my mother, along with three younger siblings. For the first month or two, I stayed in the cave, eating my fill of tender and delicious meat like baby deer, piglets, and baby rabbits that my mother and father brought. I grew up healthily without illness, playing inside and outside the cave with my siblings. Sometimes, when I sat on a rock, magpies would come and chatter; sometimes, when I went near the stream, mice and rabbits would all flee at once; and sometimes, when hunters saw us, they would collapse saying how cute we were, and my mother, hiding behind a rock, would laugh "hee hee" with joy. (Hunters also cherish baby tigers and don't catch them. The love for one's child is the same for humans and beasts; if you cherish the young, even a tiger smiles.)

After a month or two, awl-like teeth grew, claws like hooks appeared, my waist became slender, and my leg strength became sturdy. I started taking walks to the nearby front and back mountains, racing, wrestling, and hunting.
My mother followed behind, guarding and training me. Occasionally, when I managed to catch a deer or rabbit by myself, my siblings would rush over wanting to share, and my mother would praise me for being capable. Growing rapidly, before even five or six months passed, I had already become a fine adult. From then on, hunting was my specialty. Human scoundrels use tools like guns and nets even to catch a single rabbit, but we, without even a single knife, caught deer, stags, and even flying birds with just our teeth and claws.

"Fierce tiger emerges from the forest! (猛虎出林!)" "Fierce tiger emerges from the forest! (猛虎出林!)" Even humans<100> greatly admired our imposing presence, but truly, in our prime, who could stand against us? Perhaps Mr. Raion, but otherwise, all were our soldiers, subordinates, food supply, and playthings. If I wanted to eat pork, I ate pork; if I wanted deer, I ate deer; if I preferred something tender, I caught a rabbit; if I liked something savory, I caught a musk deer; if I wanted to eat human flesh, I pestered my parents until they finally caught some for me. Truly, I am one who has eaten the soft testicles of a young man and the plump buttocks of a maiden.

The more I think about it, the more I miss those times and can't bear it. That time when I ran freely over this mountain and that; that time when I ate this and that as I pleased; that time when nothing hindered me in any direction; that time when I feared nothing and dreaded nothing. That time when, after devouring a whole musk deer, I stretched under a wild vine. Especially that time when I met my first husband under a rock. Ah, the more I think, the more I long for it! Where have all those companions from that time gone? Where is my husband, and how is he faring?

The Path to Capture by Humans
Just as human scoundrels who are too complacent or too greedy often face failure, I, too, relying solely on my pride, acted recklessly and got caught in a human net.

It was precisely during the spring season when flowers bloomed and birds sang. Having been somewhat hungry during the winter, I decided to eat a deer that had fed on fresh grass. Leaving Indian territory, I entered the vicinity of China and was searching here and there when I spotted two deer, about the size of calves, grazing by a stream (溪邊). "Alright!" I thought, and quietly crept closer, settling down by their necks. As one unsuspectingly came up, I struck fiercely – where could it possibly escape? In a single breath, *ping*, it fell down. I mounted it, and without even plucking the fur, from head to heel, in one go, crunch crunch, I gulped it down until my belly felt like bursting. Just like a "tiger's feast," I indeed devoured it grandly.

But what's this? Fatigue set in, my limbs felt sluggish, and I couldn't move an inch! Having eaten well, I decided to sleep soundly for a while. Lying down in the thicket, I stretched out all four limbs, tossing and turning this way and that for half a day, oblivious to anyone dragging me away, even having the first terrifying dream of my life while sleeping deeply. In my sleep, I heard an owl hooting somewhere, thought "Oops, overslept!" and groggily woke up. Lo and behold, the moon was already shining brightly on the tree branches! Letting out a big stretch,<102> I tried to stand up and walk, but my throat felt scratchy, and I instantly became thirsty, feeling like I could drink the entire ocean! "Ah well, let's drink some water first!" I thought, and slowly made my way down to the stream. (Human scoundrels drink cold water after consuming alcohol, but we tigers customarily drink water after eating meat.) As I went down, I lamented the strangeness of my dream, thinking, "How strange my dream was. Me, caught in a net and struggling." Wondering where to drink, here or there, I gradually walked right into a net set by some human scoundrels. After drinking my fill of water for a while, I turned to leave, but something felt snagged on my feet. "What's this?" I wondered, shaking it off vigorously, but oh no! Big trouble! A thick hemp rope net was wrapped all around my body. I was so furious! Realizing "I'm caught!", I roared loud enough to shake T'aesan and, summoning all my courage, leaped once with all my might. The humans already knew and came running from here and there with spears and sticks. I wanted to crush their heads, crunch crunch, right away, but the net was so strong I couldn't budge. After struggling desperately and leaping about ten times, I was utterly exhausted, my strength completely gone. Nothing could be done. I had no choice but to let the scoundrels do as they pleased...

Thus, unfortunately, I was captured by the scoundrels. I became a body confined by iron bars. Leaving the green mountains and blue waters, the kingdom of freedom, I entered an iron cage the size of a measuring box (斗). I became an object of spectacle for human scoundrels. Receiving meager food from their hands twice a day, I barely sustain my life. Ah, my cruel fate! How could my dream have been so accurate?! Yesterday's King of the Mountains is today's prisoner behind bars? Alas! How tragic!

From South Asia to Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul)
While my captured self was finished, the human scoundrels who caught me were overjoyed, jumping here and there, drinking, dancing, causing quite a commotion. Spectators, men and women, old and young, formed long queues, and my ears hurt from the talk of money, a thousand nyang, ten thousand nyang. Eventually, I became a body to be sold. Whether they received 1,000 won or 1,500 won, anyway, I was sold and brought to Sanghae (上海). Falling into the rough hands of the Chinese scoundrels, I passed each day in agony. Soon after, I was sold to Japanese scoundrels and left Sanghae for Changgi (長崎). Boarding a large steamship, I crossed the vast ocean. Thoughts of home, parents, and siblings were intense, but what could I do? In the pitch-dark lowest deck storage (最下層 置庫), like a coal bunker, confined within an iron cage nonetheless, I came to Changgi, lamenting my fate alone. I began to admire the human scoundrels. How could they traverse such a great sea so freely? They were truly masters of ingenuity (智巧).

After staying in Changgi for perhaps two months, serving as a spectacle for human scoundrels, somehow I was brought to a place called Hakan (下關). Wondering where I would be sold off to next, it turned out, indeed, that I was sold for a current price of 1,000 won to the Ch'anggyŏngwŏn (昌慶苑) of the Yi Royal Household Office (李王職) in Sŏul (서울), Chosŏn.

Sŏul of Chosŏn! Capital of the Tiger Country! Ch'anggyŏngwŏn of the Yi Royal Family! Compared to prison life, they said it was a better place, so I eagerly awaited the day of arrival. It was a warm spring five years ago. Loaded onto a large ship called the Kan-Pu Ferry (關釜聯絡船), I crossed the Hyŏnhae Sea (玄海 - Genkai Sea). I arrived at Pusan (釜山). For the first time, I saw people in white clothes (白衣人). "Ah! This must be Chosŏn," I thought, feeling secretly glad. Boys and girls of the White-Clad Country shouted "Tiger! Tiger!" and welcomed me so intensely, I was overjoyed in their embrace. This time, I rode a train up to Sŏul. Once again, I admired the human scoundrels. What kind of contraption is a train? We might be able to travel a thousand ri alone, but carrying such huge objects, we wouldn't dare move an inch. What kind of pocket do humans have to be so free on land and sea like this?

Around seven in the evening, I arrived at Namdaemun Station. Quite a few people came out to welcome the arrival of the zoo tiger. There were people taking photos, taking notes, spectating – it was quite a commotion for a while. Loaded onto a large cart, I passed Namdaemun, went through Chongno (鐘路), past Chongno 4-ch'ŏngmok, past the Government-General (總督府) hospital, and entered the place called * Ch'anggyŏngwŏn. Upon entering, signifying 'The Mountain King has arrived,' I let out a roar, "Eo-heung!" Both Pugak (北岳) and Namsan (南山) responded in unison, the area inside and outside the gate trembled, and all sorts of birds and beasts seemed to shiver with fear, but among them, Mr. Raion responded with a single roar.

Thus, west of where Mr. Lion resides, right next to where my bear nephews are, within that triple-layered iron cage, I found my place. One or two keepers occasionally come to look after me. It seems like only yesterday, but already five springs and autumns, five cycles of stars and frost, have passed.

For food, around nine in the morning, I eat one rabbit raw (without plucking the fur, head and heels included (only the dung removed), I gulp it down whole. Still, like a toad eating a fly). Around four in the afternoon, I eat 4-5 kŭn (斤 - catty, approx. 2.4-3kg) of beef. I sleep whenever I please, exercise once a day in a separate exercise area, and defecate and urinate whenever I please. When giving food or water, or cleaning dung, an old man invariably comes in holding a long iron rod, opens the cage door, moves<103> me to one side or the other before entering. He dare not come near me. Even if he did approach, I wouldn't do anything, but humans dare not come close. I live in incomparable loneliness. Having come to a foreign land tens of thousands of ri away, living this lonely life behind bars, without a husband, children, or friends, spending my days alone under a windy window and cold lamp, roaring and sleeping alone – how could I not be lonely? Mr. Lion lives across the way, but since we are both prisoners, we cannot even meet once. My cousin, the leopard, is nearby, but I still cannot exchange a single word. Monkeys and foxes, my nephews, are neighbors, but we have never once gathered in one room. I am so lonely that sometimes I wish I could bite my tongue and commit suicide – but life is so precious that I dare not, and so I continue living like this until today.

Enemies, could there be enemies more wicked than human scoundrels? Yet, sometimes, I shed tears of gratitude for their kindness. When I am sick and cannot eat anything, lying down, the scoundrels become anxious, mixing medicine with meat, begging me to eat, lighting a stove to make the surroundings warm, sometimes giving me milk, sincerely praying for my speedy recovery by any means necessary. Whether it's for their own sake or for my life, there are many times I feel impressed.

Winter like now is truly desolate beyond compare. Besides the keepers or country bumpkins, I rarely see people. I wish spring would come soon. Listening to the whispers of talented men and beautiful women amidst the thick scent of flowers makes me excited enough to forget the free mountain kingdom. Especially when I see lovely young beauties with rosy cheeks like spring peaches, or female students with plump buttocks and hair flowing softly like willow branches, I recall my maiden days, and tears unknowingly trickle down.

Summer is not bad either, but autumn with its colorful leaves or chrysanthemums is better. However, it's not as good as springtime when the cherry blossoms bloom. Seeing young couples walking arm in arm amidst the yellow chrysanthemums and autumn maples, I seem to faintly hear the whistling sounds of tiger pairs amidst the green mountains and blue waters. When white snow flutters down like now, seeing the fat dog in the front yard run freely, I think even more of my siblings from the old days. Ah, how can I not be sad and lonely! It's frustratingly suffocating!

The Faces of Humans I Have Seen
Human scoundrels seem ridiculous, yet in reality, they are frightening creatures.<104> Their frail five-foot bodies, trembling like chicks, look utterly lacking in dignity or power, but what kind of strength do they possess? Capturing fierce beasts like us and putting us on display? No matter how I think about it, they are baffling scoundrels.

Their eyes are no better than ours, their teeth no better than ours, their claws no better than ours, not even a single whisker is better than ours; what do they have that makes them act like the lords of all creation? The Creator doesn't speak, so I cannot know, but it remains a question.

Now, look at the faces of these human scoundrels. Long ones, wide ones, round ones, flat ones – thousands, tens of thousands, each different. Crying faces, laughing faces, frowning faces, despairing faces – their expressions are also all different. Those wearing hats, gat (갓), official caps (감투), towels – head coverings are all different. Western clothes, Korean clothes (鮮服), Japanese clothes (日服), Chinese clothes (中服), black clothes, white clothes, red clothes, blue clothes – clothes are also all different. Leather shoes, straw sandals (집석이), rubber shoes (고무신), cloth shoes (되신), Japanese sandals (왜신), slippers (실발) – footwear is also different. If their external appearances are so diverse, their inner natures must also be equally diverse, right?

But here's the thing. You human scoundrels, whose insides and outsides are both so varied, what unity or solidarity could you possibly have that you constantly babble about 'one mind' or 'unity'?

Aren't you the ones where one person says tigers are good and praises tigers, while another says lions are good and praises lions? Isn't it you where one person says foxes are good and follows the fox's tail, while another says monkeys are good and stares at the monkey's backside? While one side praises the white crane, isn't there another scoundrel shouting that the mandarin duck is the best? Truly, each person is different (各人各色). Did you ever have unity or solidarity?

I don't know the inner workings of humans in detail, but you thieves! Are the clothes you wear, the food you eat, things you obtained by your own hands? Especially all you humans who pretend to be strong, rich, and powerful. Can you not confess the truth even before a tiger? First, didn't you rummage through others' pockets right before my eyes? Didn't you promise to commit theft right under my ears? Didn't you snatch at a beauty's skirt and slyly step on a female student's foot? Human scoundrels, you cannot deceive my lantern-like eyes. You absolutely cannot deceive them. I know everything, piece by piece. I can clearly see the poisonous fangs of the thieving cat beating, beating within your chests.

Are you teasing me or playing tricks? Why throw stones, why poke with sticks? If you don't want to eat it, give it to the beggar.<105> Why throw me snacks that are unsuitable for me? Do you think I'll respond if you do that? Why whistle at me inappropriately? Do I look so easygoing? Although I live a dog's life as a prisoner behind iron bars, I look down on human scoundrels. One swipe and they wouldn't even recover their bones...

How can I possibly describe all the faces of deceitful humans, thieving humans, humans of all shapes and colors! Why should I add the lament of human scoundrels to my own tale of woe? But I just said a word in passing.

Alas! Who Knows What the Future Holds?
Will I end my entire life living as a dog-prisoner in the zoo of the Yi Royal Family in Sŏul, Chosŏn? Will there ever be a chance, perhaps through a world-shattering event (天崩地陷), a great upheaval, to roam freely once again in the paradise of freedom? Sadly, I cannot foresee the future at all! Unless a great fire breaks out in the zoo, or a great revolution occurs in the country, causing a massive destruction with crashes and bangs, ah, my fate seems sealed from here on out! If the life of the King of the Mountains is like this, what more needs to be said about all the weak and inferior beings in the world! As a fellow widow understands a fellow widow, I send a stream of tears to all the prisoners of the world.

**Summary**

This article, written from the perspective of a female Bengal tiger captured and displayed in Seoul's Changgyeongwon zoo in 1926, is a lament about its lost freedom and dignity. The tiger recounts its majestic past as the "King of the Mountains," its capture, transport through Shanghai and Japan to colonial Korea, and its current miserable, lonely existence. It contrasts its former power with its present captivity, observes the diverse and often hypocritical nature of the humans who view it, and despairs about its future, finding solidarity only with other prisoners worldwide. From a historical perspective, the article is highly interesting as an allegory for Korea under Japanese rule. The tiger's loss of freedom, confinement in a former royal palace (downgraded by the colonizers), critique of its captors (implicitly including the Japanese who brought it there), and longing for its 'homeland' mirror sentiments of colonial subjugation and resistance. The explicit mention of different nationalities (Korean, Japanese, Chinese, Western) and the reference to Changgyeongwon itself, a site laden with colonial symbolism, provide valuable insight into the social and political atmosphere of 1920s colonial Seoul.
