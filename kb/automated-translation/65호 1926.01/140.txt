Article Title: 우리는 종놈이다., 新丙寅生 (We Are Slaves., Born in the New Byeongin Year)
Author: 新丙寅生 (Sin Pyŏnginsŏng - likely a pseudonym)
Article Type: 회고·수기 (Memoir/Recollection)

We Are Slaves., Born in the New Byeongin Year
Interesting Facts from Amidst the Tonghak Peasant Rebellion (東學黨亂)
A Story of Slaves Kicking Their Master's XX
It would be good if a creative work were written centered on this issue. But unfortunately, the author is not a creative writer. It would also be good to write a memoir centered on this issue. But the author was not an experimenter of that time. It would be even better to write down the entire facts of the Tonghak Rebellion centered on this issue. But since it is said that the February issue of "Kaebyŏk" (開闢) will be a Tonghak Peasant Revolution issue, this too must still be heard from outside. This time, I have no choice but to borrow the mouth of one of the experimenters of that time, reveal a part of the story from that time, and share a corner of old tales (古談) with tens of thousands of readers on the first day of the new year. Moreover, for us standing on the left and right of the movement line, what could be more interesting than tracing the distinct vestiges of precious blood (寶血) left behind by this class war, especially the moment when the master-slave, *sangban*-commoner relationship (主奴常班), which our ancestors meaningfully left as the first step, was overturned!
Truly speaking, not just Koreans, not just Easterners, but even Westerners cannot deny the Tonghak Peasant Rebellion in Korea 33 years ago! Let alone denial, who would not affirm that it was Korea's first class war, that it was Korea's first revolutionary uprising! Isn't their slogan at that time, "Reform the government and liberate the people," still fresh in our ears and eyes? There is no need to repeat known facts. Anyway, it is about that time.

Now, to the story. In the land of Hongju (洪州) (now Hongsŏng) in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province (忠淸道), there lived a prominent *yangban* family with the surname Kim (金姓) in a place called Kalmi (葛山) <86>, managing a large estate (圃中) and living renownedly for generations. *Yangban*, rich, many descendants, holding high office – truly a family of the greatest influence in the recent Yi Dynasty (李朝), living envied by none, not even imperial relatives or royal clans (皇門 王族). Anyway, it is said that among those who frequented notable places, whether in the capital or the provinces, there was no one who did not know "Kalmi Kim-ga" (갈미 金哥), so one can imagine their power. As they were thus wealthy, prominent, and blessed with many descendants, the male and female slaves (男女婢僕), inner and outer servants' quarters (內行廊 外行廊), tenant farmers (舍音 小作人), etc. – indeed, the lower-class people (下人) attached to them alone numbered over several thousand. From the inner and outer quarters, shouts of "Come here! Go there!" rained down like hail, and from inside and outside the courtyard, responses of "You are called! I am present!" followed one after another.
The Great Lord (大監) spits phlegm haughtily, the young master (서방님) recites the Book of Odes (詩經), the madam (마님) nags, the young lady (앗씨) puts on airs, the donkey brays "hee-haw," the horse neighs "neigh-neigh," the dog yaps "woof-woof," the slaves (奴僕) bustle in and out, the threshing workers (打作 바리) come and go, and the new sedan chair bearers (新轎 기미) have no rest – could there be such a great household of fortune (大運家)!
How can the true scene of an old *yangban* household be depicted as it was! It would be better to read an old novel.

Among the hundreds of slaves (奴僕) of this Kim family, there were a strong man named Mun Ch'ŏnjo (文天釗) and a valiant fellow named Yi Sŭngbŏm (李承範). Both men possessed strength surpassing others and extraordinary will. Though they were slaves in another's house, they always harbored great ambitions, watched for some opportunity, and lived in constant discontent (不平 不平).
How can all the accumulated discontent and suffering of slaves in past *yangban* houses be fully revealed!
Generations of servitude in another's house! The grandfather died in it, the father died in it, and if the son is not offered to it, he cannot survive. Is this damned slavery something a human should endure? Entrusting life and death (生死 與奪) to the master (上典), entrusting suffering and pleasure (苦之樂之) to the master bastard (주인놈), working until bones break and flesh wears thin, squeezing out blood and sweat to offer it all to the master, and then living day by day among the leftover scraps and discarded crumbs, treated the same as cattle, horses, dogs, and pigs (牛馬狗豚), amidst averted eyes, curses, kicks, and beatings!
How can the plight of a slave in an old *yangban* house be fully depicted? <87>
Ch'ŏnjo and Sŭngbŏm were such slaves. They were slaves like this, who generation after generation entrusted their lives, property, joys, and sorrows to the hands of their master, spending day after day in suffering (苦痛 苦痛). They too were human; how could they lack human consciousness! How could they not perceive the suffering and discontent! They found discontent in unbearable suffering, found anguish (煩悶) in discontent, found doubt (懷疑) in anguish, discovered "He too is human, I too am human" (彼亦人我亦人) in doubt, and from the fact that "We are the same humans" (彼我 同一人), they discovered the flaws of wealth and poverty (貧富), the flaws of the *sangban*-commoner system (常班), the flaws of the master-slave system (主奴) – all the flaws of the social system. From this, a spirit of resistance arose, a spirit of challenge arose, and destruction, reform – all the sparks were set to explode. Ch'ŏnjo and Sŭngbŏm conspired (陰謀) in secret (秘密裏). They formed a pact (結託).
Even when going to the mountains to gather firewood, they vented their discontent, saying, "Hey, are we just going to end up doing this bastard's work day and night?" Even when going to the fields to plow, they spoke of their suppressed anger (仰鬱), saying, "Hey, are we just going to shed blood and sweat like this day and night?" Even while carrying heavy loads, they would say, "Agh! My shoulders. Agh! My back. When will I ever be free from this servitude?" Even while running errands in the middle of the night, they would think, "Isn't there somewhere to escape? Can't I survive if I run away? Can't I live without being a slave?" Seeing his wife, her hands cracked and chapped, washing the master's laundry in the ice until sunset while heavily pregnant, they harbored unbearable resentment (鬱忿). When they saw their shaggy-haired son, dressed in rags showing patches of skin, sitting and slurping down scraps (턱찍기) that even a pig would refuse, their chests felt torn apart, and anger (憤怒) exploded. Moreover, seeing the masters, dressed in silk clothes and *tanggeon* hats (錦衣宕巾), sitting on folding screens and mats (屛風 돗자리), eating rich delicacies (膏粱眞味), yet still finding it insufficient (猶爲不足), taking wild ginseng and deer antlers (山蔘鹿茸) alternately as pills and decoctions, and constantly harassing the slaves with loud coughs and nagging, their insides boiled over, making life unbearable.
"Hey, Sŭngbŏm? I just can't stand it anymore. We need to make some kind of move (擧指). That bastard is human, and aren't we human too? As fellow humans, that bastard sits comfortably, throws tantrums, eats well, dresses well, and fools around with women as he pleases—. We work our fingers to the bone day and night, yet we can't eat well, can't dress well, and are mistreated... What kind of situation is this? Is this the Way of Heaven (天道) and human affairs (人事)? This won't do. Let's make a move (擧措). Let's escape this den of slaves (奴僕의 窟). Let's break out of this iron cage (鐵網). Aren't we human too? For our lives, for the lives of our ancestors and descendants (祖與孫), let's escape this den of savage beasts and poisonous snakes (猛獸毒蛇의 窟). Hey, Sŭngbŏm, what do you think?" <88>
"I feel the same—. It's my long-held wish (固 所願). If you say lead, I'll lead; if you say follow, I'll follow. Life and death are the same here or there (於此於彼 一般), there's not half a reason to hesitate... But hey, Ch'ŏnjo? Don't we need a plan (方略)? By what method should we do it?"
"...There's a good way. I heard from Mr. Pak (朴 某) over the pass the other day that there's a splendid Way to save the world and comfort the people (濟世安民之道) called Tonghak (東學). They say if you believe in it, commoners (상놈) become *yangban*, the poor become rich, illnesses are cured without medicine, water comes out of gun barrels, and all sorts of miracles (造化) happen. Let's believe in it. I heard that in our Hongju, there are already tens of thousands of Tonghak followers (東學군). They say there are already hundreds of thousands of Tonghak followers throughout Chosŏn (朝鮮), regardless of Chŏlla (全羅), Kyŏngsang (慶尙), or the Northwestern provinces (西北道). They say they are waiting for an opportunity to do something good. Reforming the government, expelling the Westerners and Japanese (斥洋 斥倭), liberating the people, eliminating distinctions between rich and poor, high and low (貧富貴賤) – anyway, they say there are all sorts of plans. Let's believe in it. Let's persuade our other comrades and believe in it... But for now, it must be absolutely secret."
"I also heard roughly about it from Mr. Son (孫 某) across the river. Like you said, I felt the same. Then let's believe in it together. Let's keep it absolutely secret for now. They say the government considers Tonghak believers (東學쟁이) the same as traitors (逆賊). First of all, we must absolutely deceive the master (主人)."
"Yes— That's right! Then let's find Pak or Son someday and enter the Way (入道)!"
Thus, they secretly promised to become Tonghak followers. They firmly promised to become Tonghak followers along with many other slaves and make some kind of move.

Now, to the story. The Way commonly called "Tonghak," which originated from a single individual, Ch'oe Suun (崔水雲) (real name Cheu 濟愚) in Yongt'aek (龍澤), Kyŏngju (慶州) on April 5th, Kyŏngsin year (庚申 - likely refers to 1860), whether because Tonghak itself appealed to the ways of the world and the hearts of the people, or because the surrounding environment (四圍環境) at that time also allowed for the emergence of Tonghak, somehow its influence gradually spread throughout every nook and cranny (坊坊曲曲) of all the provinces of Chosŏn. Among them, Chŏlla and Ch'ungch'ŏng were particularly affected.
At this time in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, Tonghak groups (東學黨) were forming secret societies (秘密結社) here and there under names ending in -*p'o* (-包), such as Tŏkp'o (德包), Yep'o (禮包), Ch'ŏngp'o (淸包), Ch'unguip'o (忠義包), Sanch'ŏnp'o (山川包), Hongp'o (洪包), etc. Mun Ch'ŏnjo and Yi Sŭngbŏm happened to join the Ch'ŏngp'o, centered around Son Ch'ŏnmin (孫天民).
As soon as Ch'ŏnjo and Sŭngbŏm joined Tonghak, they received equal treatment according to the main principles of the Way. Based on the idea of "Serving people like Heaven" (事人如天), regardless of high or low status (上下尊卑), rich or poor, noble or base (貧富貴賤), man or woman, old or young (男女老少), as long as one was human <89>, all were treated equally (皆 一般): sitting together (坐則同席), eating at the same table (食則同卓), bowing upon meeting (逢則相拜), speaking respectfully to each other (語則相敬). Even seeing a woman weaving cloth (織布婦), they called her "Heavenly Lord weaving cloth" (天主織布), and seeing an infant's anger (乳兒의 怒), they spoke of "Heavenly Lord— being angry" (天主- 怒하섯다). Mun and Yi were immensely impressed by this. They sincerely admired it as the Great Way of 50,000 years (五萬年之大道) that would truly "Spread virtue throughout the world and widely save the masses" (布德天下廣濟蒼生).
Above all, having bonded as the poor and the lowly, that is, the lowest class (最下級), based on the principle of like seeking like (同氣相求) and shared suffering evoking sympathy (同病相憐), they entrusted their lives and deaths, joys and sorrows (生死苦樂) to each other.
Then the time came. It was February of the Kabo year (甲午年 - 1894). In Kobu (古阜), Chŏlla Province, Chŏn Pongjun (全琫準) raised the flag of revolution with tens of thousands of Tonghak followers. Prior to this, the Sabal T'ongmun (沙鉢通文 - Bowl Circular, a secret order 秘令) had already circulated among Tonghak followers throughout Chosŏn (全鮮). "When one group moves, myriad groups follow" (一派纔動萬派隨); "When one place raises fire, ten thousand people rise" (一處擧火萬人起). The Tonghak Army (東學軍) began raising righteous flags (義旗) here and there.
How could the Tonghak Army of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province not move! How could Mun Ch'ŏnjo and Yi Sŭngbŏm of Hongju not rise! The sword sharpened for 10 years (十年 磨劒) surely awaited its time; how could they not move!
The time had come. Urgent reports flew like hail: Kobu fallen, Chŏnju (全州) occupied, Kongju (公州) in danger, Hongju also raising troops (擧兵). Alarming news kept arriving: Government troops (官軍) routed everywhere (到處 大狼敗), Tonghak Army marching en masse towards Hanyang (大擧衝漢陽). The time has come! (時機來矣) No time for delay! (暫不 遲滯) Let's rise! Let's move— Ch'ŏnjo also took up his staff (杖) and rose, Sŭngbŏm also brandished his fist (拳) and moved. Following the wind and transforming (從風而化), several hundred slaves rose up at once with a roar (으악-).
Like the scene of the secret royal inspector's arrival (御使 出道幕) in the Tale of Ch'unhyang (春香傳), the "Kalmi Kim-ga" household (門中) was thrown into a great revolution.
"Gate's open, wind's closing it!" "Pull the horse's neck, block the back gate!" "The slave gives orders!" "The *yangban* is present!" "I'll wear the lord's hat!" "Put on these drawers inside out!" – It was utter chaos (野壇 法席).
Ch'ŏnjo stood firmly in the main hall (大廳) and issued commands like autumn frost (秋霜): "You bastards, don't move a muscle, listen to me! We are the slaves you have bossed around, exploited, abused, and despised for generations. Did you know that Heaven's fate (天運) cycles, and adversity turns to prosperity (否往泰來)? Did you know that commoners become *yangban* and the poor become rich? You ignorant humans! If you want to live, prostrate yourselves on the ground below and drink pure water (淸水). If you want to die, let's fight a great battle (大決戰) with us!" he roared, shaking the mountains (山岳). Some knelt on straw mats, chanting "Serve the Heavenly Lord" (侍天主); some crouched, trembling uncontrollably, drinking pure water; some climbed over walls to escape; some hid under blankets, crying "Heaven! Heaven!" (天兮 天兮); some wept, <90> some collapsed, some fled – it was truly a state of war (戰時 狀態). It must have been a great revolutionary uprising (一大 革命亂). Bang, bang— the sound of gunshots; crash, crunch— the sound of houses collapsing; amidst thick dust and smoke (塵煙), filled with screams of agony (悲鳴).
"This pillar too, we erected it! Let's pull it out! This wall too, we built it! Let's tear it down! These roof tiles too, we laid them! Throw them away! That one was particularly tyrannical (暴虐), trample him to death! That one doesn't need killing, strip him naked, tie him to a jujube tree, and castrate him (불알을 까자).—" etc. It was truly gruesome beyond compare (悽慘 無雙).
The fireball of discontent that had accumulated for hundreds of years burst forth like this. The revenge battle (復讐戰) that hundreds of slaves had planned and planned began like this. The Kim clan (金門), which had styled itself the King of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province for generations, met its end like this at the hands of its slaves.
Some died, some were castrated, some fled, some became beggars, some were crippled; the large house was left with only its foundation, the small houses collapsed; the entire estate (圃中) was left with the feeling of "Whose estate this was is unknown" (是不知誰家圃中).
The success or failure, gain or loss (成敗利鈍) that followed need not be discussed here. How refreshing it is— isn't it an interesting issue? Let's not think of it like an old novel. Let's chew on it and swallow it.— (End) <91>

**Summary**

This 1926 article recounts a dramatic episode from the 1894 Tonghak Peasant Rebellion in Hongju, Ch'ungch'ŏng Province. It focuses on two slaves, Mun Ch'ŏnjo and Yi Sŭngbŏm, who served a powerful *yangban* family surnamed Kim. Driven by generations of suffering and inspired by the egalitarian ideals of the Tonghak movement ("Serve people like Heaven"), they secretly join the rebellion. When the uprising reaches their area, they lead fellow slaves in overthrowing their masters, enacting brutal revenge, destroying the estate, and symbolically inverting the rigid social hierarchy. The author frames this event explicitly as Korea's "first class war" and celebrates the slaves' violent liberation as a refreshing and significant moment. For historians, the article is interesting for its explicit framing of the Tonghak Rebellion through a class warfare lens in 1926, reflecting contemporary socialist or nationalist interpretations of the past. Its graphic depiction of slave retribution provides a visceral perspective on the deep-seated resentments within the traditional Korean social structure.

却說. 忠淸道 洪州(今 洪城) 땅. 갈미(葛山)라는＜86＞ 곳에 金姓 가진 조선 屈指의 양반님네가 크다랏케 圃中을 티고 累代 有名字 하게 사러 왓다. 양반이요 부자요 자손 만코 벼슬 잘 하고- 실상 皇門 王族 부럽지 안케 지내는 李朝 최근의 가장 得勢의 문중이다. 엇잿든 여간 出入 낫치나 한다는 자치고는 京鄕을 통하야 「갈미 金哥」라면 모르는 자가 업섯다 하니 그들의 세력을 가히 알 것 아니냐?. 이와 가티 富貴 多子孫한지라 따라서 男女婢僕이며 內行廊 外行廊이며 舍音 小作人 등 실로 그들에게 附從하는 下人들만도 수천 이상에 달햇섯다. 內方 外舍의서 「이리 오너라 저리 가거라」의 호령이 비ㅅ발 치듯 하고 庭內 庭外에서 『불느신다. 대령 햇슴니다』의 隨應이 꼬리를 맛무럿섯다.
大監은 가래침을 곤두고 서방님은 詩經을 외우고 마님은 잔소리 하고 앗씨는 모양을 내고 당나귀는 앙앙 거리고 말은 홍홍거리고 게산이는 께욱 께욱 하고 奴僕은 들낙 날낙하고 打作 바리 오락가락 하고 新轎 기미는 쉴 새가 업스니 이런 大運家도 또 잇스랴!.

과거 兩班家의 奴僕들의 쌋고 싸은 不平과 고통을 어이 그대로 다- 드러낼 수 잇스랴!.
대대로 두고 남의 집 종살이! 한아비도 그로써 죽고 아비도 그로써 죽고 자식도 그에게 밧치지 안으면 못 백일 이 놈의 종살이를 사람으로야 할 일이냐?. 生死 與奪을 上典에게 맛기고 苦之樂之를 주인놈에게 맛기고 뼈가 빠지도록 살이 어이도록 피와 땀을 짜내서 上典에게 몽탕 밧치고 남은 찍걱이 버리는 부스럭지로써 그나마 牛馬狗豚과 同列하야 눈 흘기고 욕하고 발길질 하고 때리는 중에서 그 날 그 날을 지냄이랴!.

산에 나무 하러 갓다가도 「여보게 우리는 밤낫 저놈의 일만 해주다 말겟나」 하고 불평을 토해섯고 들에 밧 갈너 갓다가도 「여보게 우리는 밤낫 이처럼 피땀만 흘니고 말겟나」 하고 仰鬱을 말하고 묵어운 짐을 지고 오다가도 「아구! 억개야. 아구! 허리야. 이놈의 노릇을 언제나 면하노」 하기도 하며 밤 중에 심부림을 가다가도 「도망할 곳이나 업나 도망가면 못 사나. 종 노릇 안 하고는 못 사나!」 하기도 하며 손등이 톡톡 터진 안해가 만삭 된 몸으로 어름 속에 해가 지도록 上典의 빨내 하는 것을 보고도 그들은 견댈 수 업는 鬱忿을 품엇고 더벙머리 아들 애가 살이 비죽비죽 하는 누덕이를 두루고 안저 도야지도 참아 못 먹을 턱찍기를 훌적 훌적 마시는 것을 볼 때에 가슴이 찌져지는 듯 분노가 爆破 되얏섯다. 더군다나 錦衣宕巾 下에 屛風 돗자리 우에서 膏粱眞味를 먹으면서도 猶爲不足하야 山蔘鹿茸을 丸으로 탕으로 번가라 대며 큰거침 잔소리로 奴僕들만 뒤복가 대는데는 졋먹은 밸이 북 밧쳐 못 살 지경이엿다.
『 여보게 承範이?. 암만해도 참을 수 업네. 별반 擧指를 내야 겟네. 저 놈도 사람이요 우리도 사람 안인가. 맛찬가지 사람으로 저 놈은 가만히 안저 발버등이질 하면서 잘 먹고 잘 입고 또 게집질 마음대로 하고-. 우리는 밤낫 뼈가 빠지도록 애써 일 하고도 못 먹고 못 입고, 또 학대 밧고... 이것이 무슨 일이냐? 이것이 天道요 人事란 말이냐? 안되엿네. 무슨 擧措를 내세. 이 奴僕의 窟을 버서나세. 이 철망을 뛰여 나세. 우리도 사람 안인가?. 우리의 생명 우리의 祖與孫의 생명을 위해서 이 놈의 猛獸毒蛇의 窟을 버서나세. 여보게 承範이 엇던가?.」＜88＞
『동감일세-. 固 소원일세. 압흘 설라면 압흘 서고 뒤를 서라면 뒤를 섬세. 一生一死는 於此於彼 一般인데 주저할 것은 半分도 업네... 그러나 여보게 天釗? 무슨 방침이 잇서야 되지 안나? 엇더한 방법에 의해서 할고?』
『... 조흔 도리가 잇네. 내 日前고개 넘에 朴 某에게 드르닛가 東學이란 훌륭한 濟世安民之道가 잇다네. 그것을 밋으면 상놈이 양반되고 가난쟁이가 부자 되고 藥 안이 써도 병 잘 낫고 총구멍에서 물이 나오고 별별 造化가 다 만타데. 우리 그것을 밋세. 드르닛가 우리 洪州에도 발서 東學군이 幾 만 명이나 된다데』 全羅 慶尙 西北道 할 것 업시 왼 조선에 東學軍이 발서 幾 백 만명 이나 된다데. 잇다가 機會를 보아 무슨 존 일을 한다데. 정부를 개혁하느니 斥洋 斥倭를 하느니 인민을 해방하느니 빈부 귀천이 업서 지느니 엇잿든 별별 계획이 다 만테. 우리 그것을 밋세. 우리 여러 동무도 권유해 가지고 그것을 밋세... 그러나 아직은 절대 비밀일세』
『나도 江 건너 孫某에게 대강 드럿네. 자네 말과 갓티 나도 동감이엿네. 그럼 우리 가티 밋어 보세. 아직은 절대 비밀에 부티세. 東學쟁이는 나라에서 逆賊과 가티 녁인다데. 爲先 主人 부터 絶對 속켜야 되네』

이와 가티 그들은 東學군 되기를 비밀히 약속 햇다. 만흔 奴僕들과 가티 東學군이 되야 무슨 擧措를하기로 단단히 약속해섯다.

却說. 庚申 4월 5일 慶州 龍澤 崔水雲 (名濟愚) 一個人에서 비롯한 通稱 「東學」이란 그 道는 東學자체가 世道人心에 영합되야 그랫는지 그 때의 四圍環境이 또한 東學의 出世를 容認함이엿는지 如何間 其勢가 浸浸然 朝鮮 全道 坊坊曲曲에 전파되얏다. 그 중에도 全羅 忠淸이 더 심해섯다.
이 때 忠淸道에는 德包, 禮包, 淸包, 忠義包, 山川包, 洪包 등-包라는 명칭하에 東學黨이 이 곳 저 곳에서 비밀 결사를하고 잇섯든 터인데 맛츰 文天釗
李承範은 孫天民을 중심한 淸包에 가담하게 되얏다.
天釗와 承範은 東學에 들든 즉시로 그 道의 主旨에 의하야 평등대우를 바앗섯다. 事人如天이라하야 上下尊卑 貧富貴賤 男女老少 할 것 업시 사람이면＜89＞ 皆 一般으로 坐則同席 食則同卓 逢則相拜 語則相敬 심지어 織布婦를 보고 天主織布라하며 乳兒의 怒를 보고 天主- 怒하섯다 하는 말에 文과 李는 무수히 감탄햇다. 과연 『布德天下廣濟蒼生』 할 五萬年之大道라고 절절히 탄복 햇다.
무엇보다 貧者 賤子 즉 最下級으로써 結托한데서 同氣相求 同病相憐의 원리에서 生死苦樂을 其托하게 되얏다.
그러자 때는 왓다. 甲午年 2월이다. 全羅道 古阜에서 全琫準이 수 만 동학군과 가티 혁명의 기를 들다. 前期하야 全鮮 東學군에게는 발서 沙鉢通文(秘令)이 돌앗섯다. 一派纔動萬派隨로 一處擧火萬人起로 東學軍은 此地 彼地에서 義旗를 들게 되얏다.
