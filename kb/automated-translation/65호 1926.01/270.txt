1
The scorching sun beats down. The intense heat of the sun feels like a rain of fire pouring down. It aims directly at all things on earth, firing its rays, just like a trained soldier aiming a gun at an enemy soldier to kill them in one shot.
The grains of sand on the road simmer and bubble. Tree leaves wither, and blades of grass burn up at the edges of the fields. The great earth (大地) boils like a cauldron, and all creation sits within it, swallowing the fiery steam and also breathing out fiery steam.
A dog (犬) pants incessantly, its long tongue hanging out. The way it pants, its tongue like fire hanging out long, suggests it feels the agony of its five organs burning up dryly from an unquenchable fire it has swallowed; its eyes are shut tight, and it lies sprawled with its four legs stretched out. The water collected in a puddle boils like hot soup, and a frog that jumps in with a splash! flips over, its two legs stretched straight out. Then, as its four limbs tremble violently, it rights itself again and flounders about as if saying,
“Oh my! What is this?”
with its large, bulging eyes opened even wider. But what evokes an even more profound interest in the strong (强者) might be the school of minnows gathered in the paddy drain. – The water is barely there, on the verge of drying up completely soon. The cruel heat haze (陽炎) sucks away their water of life (生命水) moment by moment. The school of minnows wriggles within it – Ah! Is it their struggle to live, feeling the ultimate terror (恐怖) of death? Their tails clash, and some leap out of the water. Those that do die instantly, a pitiful sight. Those who rush to live first – who are anxious only for their own survival – die first... <59> This gives many lessons to the weak. Yet, the cruel smiling sunbeams look down upon them as if feeling happiness.
However, they resolve upon a solemn death and fiercely resist (反抗) in the final drop (一適). They too – those tiny creatures – seem to know that dying while fighting against the strong is a magnificent (壯快) death. They too seem to know that meeting a gratifying end while resisting the strong is an eternal honor. Otherwise, why wouldn't they calmly await death?
The midday heat is indeed severe. Especially being a continental climate, it is even fiercer.
All living things feel the suffering from the heat (暑苦) and struggle against the drought, continuing the fight for life until the last moment. Shall we say they are like the masses groaning under tyranny? Shall we say they are like prisoners bound in iron chains? Even when the wind blows, it only carries dust like fire, adding more hot air that suffocates with every gust. Thus, fanning oneself is like fueling a fire. Unless one is a fool, no one would fan themselves at such a time.
Yet, as if all the moisture sucked from the earth has been injected (注射) into the flesh of humans, sweat pours profusely only from the bodies of this person and that person. Still, should humans be considered happy because sweat is also water? If so, then those who shed the most of this sweat-water and tears would be the farmers planting rice seedlings in the fields right now. It would be the laborers working in the city factories.
Their sunburnt bodies are so dark it's impossible to tell if they are of the yellow race or the black race. Just as the gold fever (黃金熱) turns the hearts of the white race black as ink – this solar heat also changes the color of others' skin like this.
Clad in whitish drawers, the exposed skin above and below appears even darker, like paulownia wood, but when the skin hidden under the clothes is occasionally revealed, it’s of such a different color that one could hardly believe it belongs to the same person. With their sunburnt, reddish-black backs bent in a line, they are now busily planting rice seedlings. As they plant one seedling, two seedlings, the color of the paddy field turns freshly green, and from their mouths flows the drawn-out sound of a farmers' work song (상사듸 소리). However, it gives the sad feeling that they are trying to forget their suffering. As they do so, beads of sweat spring up on their backs, and the sun relentlessly pours down its rays (光線). Their sweat also seems dark.
<60>

However, the owner of this paddy field leans against the north window in the thick shade of a tree, gazing at the white clouds billowing up, while listening to the tune flowing between the measures of this leisurely farmer's song. Yet, complaining of the heat, he fans himself continuously, his eyes fluttering shut and then blinking open like a bedside lamp about to go out...
Perhaps because it's the end of many dry days, even as evening approaches, far from cooling down, the expectation of a refreshing breeze is pitifully disappointed; instead, the same stuffy, hot air blows in gusts, making one feel suffocated as if wrapped in unseen smoke.
Mosquitoes are likely to be plentiful near the edges on such evenings. Originally, the bug called mosquito is born in hot weather, so it's stranger to find it odd that they are more active on hot evenings, but people who usually only think of themselves wonder,
“Ah! Why are there so many mosquitoes this evening?”
as if something unusual has happened. Whatever the case, indeed, during the summer season, even the tiny mosquito torments people quite a bit. The sight of someone sitting angrily after being bitten by a midge is comical, but that's how it is; humans don't seem to be such spiritual creatures after all. Yet, ask people! Don't they boast that humans are the lords of all creation (萬物之靈長)? Especially those called civilized people...
The moon has not yet risen, and the twilight dusk gathers hazily from beyond the grove. Already, the outlines (輪廓) of the distant mountains are faint, and everything seems to hide in the white light, reflecting within the curtain of darkness. What appear like scattered clumps of rock revealed in the mountain valley are the sparsely located village houses. With mountains surrounding it on the left and right, and fields and paddies in front, one can guess this is a farming village (農村) even on such a dark night, though one can also tell from the occasional pungent smell of manure carried on the breeze that hits the nose—
Having struggled with the heat all day and worn out by hard work, once they finish their evening meal, their limbs are so tired that the only thing that comes freely is sleep. But perhaps the devil envies (猜忌) even this, as mosquitoes attack, waking them from their sweet slumber. The god of life (生命의 神) may have prepared this night for resting from the day's fatigue (疲勞), but what can be done when their fate does not permit even that <62> much? Ah! Pitiful farmers!
In this house and that, they have lit smudge pots in the yards, and clad in rags, they lie collapsed here and there. In the dimness, the intermittent sparkling, like fireflies, is from cigarettes. Occasionally, a flare-up comes from the smudge pot. For some reason, it quickly dies down, and then grey smoke rises again like a cloud. Around it, whitish figures huddle, sitting and lying, smoking cigarettes, and murmuring something with heavy mouths. In between, there are yawns, coughs, spits, and bursts of mindless laughter that sound like a donkey braying. Above them, the night sky looks down, blinking its star-studded eyes as if drowsy, as if dimly hearing their stories...
They chatter in their slow drawl, sigh intermittently, and then laugh hollowly again. Their voices are loud but lack strength in the end. It's the voice of those who eat coarse barley rice. It's just like how they might look plump, but in reality, their skin is puffy and sallow from malnutrition! Their voices too are tinged with yellow puffiness. Yet the loafers (不汗黨) – those who live well without sweating – say laborers are strong... Your Lordships! Please stop saying such things.
In the inner courtyard, women gather, chattering about something and giggling. There, one hears the vibrant voices of youth. It is the voice of young women burning with passion, yearning for human happiness. But their fate is already decided... They were simply to follow the life their grandmothers and grandfathers, and also their mothers and fathers, lived – the path they took... Drawing water, doing laundry, pounding barley and rice, ironing clothes, sewing, cooking, and then bearing children, weeding fields, planting rice seedlings – if they disliked any of this, the command (命令) of their fate was: die! ...
Looking around in all directions, there was nothing but the same tedious things seen every day. The sky was the sky always seen, the mountains the mountains always seen! The fields were the fields always seen. Among them, many had lived rooted like stakes in one place for forty or fifty years. No, they were born here for several generations, lived here, and died here. The furthest they ever traveled was, at most, a trip to the market. After going to the market and seeing goods produced by the city, they would chatter excitedly as if it were something marvelous. <62>
Tŏksam, with his stubby legs and dirt-filled eyes, twitching his comical mouth as he spoke, asked Malburi sitting next to him,
"Hey! Have you ever ridden a train?"
"Not yet. What's it like?" Malburi asks back, sounding curious.
"What's it like? It’s amazing! The mountains spin around, and the fields gallop!"
"Hey! The train runs, right? Why would the mountains run?" Malburi retorted, sounding unconvinced.
"Hahaha! That's because you haven't ridden it yet. When you're inside the train, the mountains run!" At these words, Malburi looked up with a half-doubting expression, then said,
"I haven't ridden anything like that in my life!" and laughed with his mouth wide open, as if in despair. Tŏksam, who had been dozing off, suddenly grinned as if remembering something,
"But there are two things you'll definitely ride in your life," he said, looking meaningfully at Malburi again.
"What are the two things?"
"Well! When you get married, you'll ride the palanquin, and then you'll ride your wife's belly..." Hahaha, the sound of that mindless, donkey-like laughter erupted from all around. Makdong, sitting nearby, impudently interjected,
"But what if he doesn't get married?"
"He'll still ride one thing for sure. When he dies, even if he rides a bier (들꺼치), they surely won't tell the corpse to walk to the grave, right? Hahaha."
Malburi, who had been expectantly waiting to hear what it was, looked utterly disappointed.
"Hey, you!" he exclaimed, slapping Tŏksam on the back. He was quite annoyed now.
"Hahaha," the sound of laughter came again like a sudden shower. Hahaha, and the sound of sighing could also be heard. That was Tŏkpoi, who always wore a gloomy expression. They then gathered around Yŏngsam, who had apparently returned from Seoul last time, and started asking about it. <63>
"So, what's Seoul like, anyway?" asked the bushy-bearded Chŏng Ch'ŏmji, smiling broadly as he shifted the topic (話題).
"How can I describe it in just a few words!" Yŏngsam began, then continued as if boasting about his solo trip to Seoul,
"You folks have at least heard of Namdaemun, right?" he asked, and when someone chimed in, "You mean Namdaemun Gate—", another burst of laughter erupted.
"Yes, that Namdaemun! It's truly well-built. Our countrymen also had great skill in the old days!"
"So where has all that skill gone now?" asked Malburi, whose anger had vanished sometime between then and now, suddenly interjecting.
"It's worn out!"
"Worn out by what?"
"By nobles (량반), liquor, and women (게집)! Hahaha."
"Indeed! That might be true!" Chŏng Ch'ŏmji nodded, sighing lightly. Yŏngsam shifted the topic again.
"Ah- didn't I go to the Hyŏmnyulsa (Theater) (協律社) the evening I arrived! Truly! There were many flower-like kisaeng too. Have you folks seen kisaeng?"
Sŏngch'il moved closer eagerly, swallowing hard,
"Of course! Haven't you seen them? Aren't there some at the Ori House in town?"
"Hahaha. Not a 'house of women' but an 'Ori House' (duck house)! Truly! They all looked like ducks! Diving headfirst into money—."
"Are those kisaeng? They're prostitutes (갈보). Real kisaeng don't come down to a countryside (시굴) like this," Tŏmsam interjected again.
"So didn't you want to sleep with one for a night?"
"What, looking like me... how could I... I'd probably just have nightmares. Hahaha."
Sŏngch'un, with his gourd-like belly sticking out, teased again like this.
"Actually, I wouldn't dare try, I'd be scared, but they were pretty, though. Hahaha." <64>
"Still a male, after all... hehehe."
Yŏngsam continued speaking.
"But the way people talk in Seoul is really nice! Especially the women's voices, I was completely charmed."
"So what's the women's way of speaking like?"
"Ah! Our country women say, 'Iraessu! Chŏraessu!' (Did this! Did that!), isn't it rough? But Seoul women say, 'Ah- wae iraeyo? Annyŏnghasipsiyo! Ŏttŏpsiyo!' (Ah- why are you doing this? How do you do! How is it!) - I can't even imitate it well - but it's truly like an oriole's song. Even if their faces look like tracks left by straw sandals on muddy ground after rain, their voices are like skylarks singing in the spring sky. Such a woman would be a perfect match for Sogyŏng when he gets married. Hahaha."
"Wow, that guy went to Seoul and his speaking skills improved. He's become sharp, hasn't he?"
"Hehehe."
"Your laugh is still country bumpkin style. I got made fun of for laughing like that when I went to Seoul this time," Yŏngsam criticized Sŏngch'il's laughter again.
"Then how should I laugh?" Sŏngch'il asked back, looking embarrassed.
"Laugh like 'Hŏhŏhŏ' or 'Hahaha,' I'm telling you."
"Well, it's fine to laugh however you want."
"No, did someone really laugh like 'Hyahyahya'?"
"Of course, they did. Wasn't it just the other day I went to the market? As I happened to pass by the prostitute's house, they were playing some music, 'ttongttangdongttang,' singing a song, and then laughing 'Hya! Hya! Hya!', just like a fox laughing while eating liver."
"True! That's right, I heard it too!" Chŏng Ch'ŏmji agreed, laughing along. Someone farted loudly, causing another burst of laughter.
Before long, the night deepened. Now the quiet night drew its curtain of darkness, silently keeping its silence, with only the occasional sound <65> of the prolonged, intermittent, melancholic stories of sleepless old folks being heard. Their characteristic tone (語調) - toothless, weak, mournful, and listless - sounded tinged with grey, like their white hair. Recalling the empty past (追憶) and thinking of the sorrowful death hastening their remaining days (餘日), only sighs flowed endlessly from their mouths. At this deathly quiet dead of night (夜半), their gloomy voices sounded as if the white bones in the ancient tombs (古塚) buried in Pukmangsan mountain were rising one by one, sitting up stiffly, and lamenting their gloomy, stuffy discomfort, the stench of death, and the maggots gnawing on their bones – complaining of that terrifying life in the tomb (塚中生活), an eternal suffering.
However, as the cool moon rose in the eastern sky, the great earth (大地), embraced once again by the moonlight, seemed to welcome it! Seemed to laugh! In an instant, it became a different world, enough to make one doubt if paradise and heaven were not right here. The heat had retreated before one knew it, and a refreshing coolness (淸凉味) entered through all the pores of the body, making one feel a invigorating sensation like drinking mint sherbet. All things—enveloped in that clear, bright moonlight, holding a tender affection, now seemed newly revived with life, smiling with happiness, appearing hazy as if intoxicated in their sweet dreams. The gentle breeze, occasionally like the rustle of a silk skirt, was perhaps a sigh of happiness, saying, "Now we live!"
The stars, which until moments ago seemed to boast of their own brilliance, twinkling brightly, were now overshadowed by the moonlight, hiding their presence as if trying to conceal their embarrassment. But their occasional blinking, peering out, trying to reveal their faint shadows suggests they are somehow jealous of the moon. But the stars thought this way: —We always possess the same light consistently. We exist during the day, at night, whether the moon is present or the sun is present—we are always here, but easily misled (迷惑) people think we disappear when the sun rises and hide when the moon ascends. So they praise us only on nights without the sun or moon. And they think we are as tiny as their own eyes and say we blink, but in reality, we are larger than the moon, larger than the sun, and we are countless...
When a gentle breeze (微風) passes lightly, the smell of dry earth momentarily rises, but within it, the dew-mixed scent of grass (草香) fills the air, refreshing the nose. As one thinks this, the sweat that clung stickily to the back has mostly dried before one realizes. Perhaps even the sleeping people <66> sense (意識) this in their dreams? Their breathing sounds soft—longly exhaled.

Chŏng To-ryong (鄭道龍), returning from an outing to the village, now pushed open the bush-clover gate of his own house and entered the inner yard. The smudge pot, as if tired of repeating its task of producing a single stream of grey smoke, was lazily exhaling the thin smoke. Strand by strand, it was blown gently by the wind, swirling up into the air before vanishing again and again.
On the courtyard, a straw mat was spread, and upon it, three family members lay asleep side-by-side under a thin blanket. The smudge pot, as if solely responsible for guarding the secrets of this quiet night, flickered, sputtered, and flared up intermittently. But as the surroundings were all wandering in dreamland, perhaps drowsiness invaded it too? It exhaled smoke like a yawning breath, becoming thinner and thinner like thread...
The moon's shadow, filtering through the tree branches, swayed gently, one branch's shadow faintly covering the face of a sleeping person. Far off, the willow grove lining the riverbank clumped together indistinctly, like a splash of ink wash (水墨), while the moonlight, as if trying to peek into the mysteries within, cast a bluish light like celadon glaze upon it. And through the wide gap opening towards this side, the whole river scenery (一面江色) appeared bright and clear; the gentle scenery under the silvery waves and hazy moon evoked a streak of feeling in someone's heart. Ah - on this night, at this time, who is thinking what? The sound of a dog barking somewhere, k'ŏng-k'ŏng, broke the stillness (靜寂) of midnight (夜半), faded away far through the air, and then silence returned— only the sound of sleeping people breathing, saek-saek! And occasionally the sound of a mosquito— aeng! —passing thinly by the ear! Like the sound of a devil's penitent cry (懺悔) heard from afar in hell!...
Chŏng To-ryong was now sitting absently, gazing at the river before him. What was he thinking? Or what had moved him? For a while, he sat motionless like a dummy, staring blankly! at the river, then suddenly let out a sigh, huuu-. He lit a cigarette.
Perhaps the cigarette had already burned down without him noticing; as the sound of the pipe stem boiling went kkororok, he took a final long drag and tapped out the pipe <67>. The remaining tobacco fell below the stepping stone, still emitting thin smoke as if determined to burn itself out completely. As it finally flickered and the smoke puffed up and vanished, he seemed to snap back to his senses, startled, and turned his head this way.
Right beside him lay his wife. Next to her lay their daughter, and then their son, sleeping in order. The moment he saw this! All the thoughts he had been having until now vanished somewhere, and only tender feelings for the sleeping figures instantly filled his heart. A smile surfaced on his lips, and with eyes full of love, he looked alternately at the sleeping faces of his son, daughter, and wife. Then he gently touched their cheeks one by one, and patted their bottoms in turn. He kissed them in turn...
Though his wife was aging, seeing her lying like this enveloped in the soft moonlight, her body freely stretched out asleep, she looked unusually beautiful, as if she hadn't perhaps returned to her maiden days. And he felt as though he too had returned to his youth. Finally, he XXXXX his wife's lips. Suddenly! XXXXXXX.
His wife was now thirty-five. He met her 18 or 19 years ago. Whether she was a pure maiden then or not? He didn't know for sure, but since he didn't know his own origins well either, there was no need to ask such things. His father was said to have been a steward (청직이) at some house in Seoul, and had relations with the daughter of some butcher (백정), resulting in his birth; others said she was a shaman (무당), so there was no room to discriminate against his wife's status or speak of chastity (貞操). Whether he himself crawled out of a dog hole or was picked up under a bridge, whatever the case, the fact that he too had life, he sometimes felt happy about, and couldn't help but be grateful.
At that time, he was a rootless, solitary individual wandering here and there. He worked day labor jobs here and there, just getting by day to day, wondering where he could settle down as a servant. How he ended up rolling into the village where his current wife lived – moreover, getting hired at the very house she was in – seems, even thinking back now, not entirely coincidental. Who would have dreamt that this would lead to their marriage? He still considered it marvelous.
At that time, he was in his prime, so naturally, he would glance at her sideways, stealing looks. Oh! With that girl...... <68> he swallowed his longing more than just once or twice. But there were many others who swallowed their longing even more than he did and had been there longer, yet she somehow ended up connected to him, he still couldn't help but smile sweetly at this final victory.
His wife had been the young lady's personal maid (조선비). The lady always used to tell her,
"The household will choose well and arrange your marriage, so be aware of that," she had instructed. But when rumors of her relationship with him suddenly spread, the lady calmly called her in, sat her down, and questioned her about the facts. Upon hearing her honest confession, she was utterly astonished— as if seeing a salt bug—.
"No matter how lowly a common girl (상년) you are, how could you give your body away so carelessly? A common girl is truly incorrigible! I didn't know you were like that!" she clicked her tongue disapprovingly, scolding her until her eyes felt like popping out. At that time, his wife, feeling resentful and ashamed, shed tears like a spring and boldly replied,
"No matter how well you look out for my affairs, miss, how can you choose someone who pleases my heart! A common girl's affairs, I must see clearly with my own eyes and choose with my own hands, shouldn't I?" and again, in a passionate voice,
"I am not ashamed of it. What is shameful about choosing the person I will live with, with my own hands? Perhaps it's because I'm a common girl, I don't know— but Miss, you followed the noblemen's (양반님) etiquette and proper procedures when you married into this house, didn't you? Then why, Miss, are you dissatisfied with your husband? Why is such a marriage not harmonious? Why, Miss, do you shed tears and only sigh? First, your husband is young, isn't he! He's less mature than you, isn't he? Leaving other things aside, when you are in your prime, Miss, your husband is young, and when your husband is in his prime, won't you, Miss, already be aging like a wilting flower? Do you like that, Miss? Why is a marriage conducted according to propriety so unhappy? I would rather endure the criticism of many people than live my whole life in unhappiness. I'd rather hang myself and die than do that! That's why I... Miss! Please forgive me for that! Being a common girl, I can't help it!" After pouring out her resentful words for a while, the lady, perhaps struck by some thought, sat silently without saying anything. Eventually, her lips quivered, and then she suddenly grasped her hand and sobbed, choked with emotion. And so, they sat facing each other and cried their hearts out. That night, his wife called him to a secluded place and told him everything <69> "I was so embarrassed, I cried so much!" she said. "Why did you cry? Let others say what they will, as long as we did what we had to do, that's enough," he replied. "Yes- that's right! Being afraid of others' slander and not doing what one truly wants to do is foolish, isn't it!" she said, seeming to gain new courage, and tightly squeezed his wrist...
Whatever the case, the master of the house found it amusingly infuriating how this relationship of ours was having a detrimental effect on the morals (風紀上) of the noble household members.
"You vile pair, get out of here this instant!" he bellowed, driving us out. We happily shed the yoke of servitude. A young man and woman are like a pair of shoes. No matter how fine a single shoe is, it's nothing. So, one should choose and wear the shoes that fit one's own feet. But that lady was like pairing large straw sandals with wooden clogs. Isn't she now making her son wear large straw sandals and her daughter-in-law wear small shoes, so the son shuffles along clumsily while the daughter-in-law struggles to wear shoes that don't fit? Thinking this, he had chuckled to himself as they left. Thanks to that, he married well, was freed from servitude, and came to live in this settlers' village.
"She must have liked me back then too-" Chŏng To-ryong smiled faintly again.
—Whenever their eyes met, her face would flush slightly, but whenever there was a quiet moment, she enjoyed talking with him. Sometimes, when he told funny stories, she would reveal her pearly white teeth and laugh charmingly; seeing her like that was so endearing, he wanted to see that expression again and again. So, whenever he heard funny stories, he would carefully memorize them to tell her, enjoying watching her laugh.
However, since funny stories always ran out eventually, he ended up buying stories from the funny, bushy-bearded Song Ch'ŏmji who lived up the street. That fellow had become fond of the taste of recently introduced cigarettes, so they exchanged one story for one cigarette. Thus, he bought cigarettes and traded them one by one for stories. After that, even that source dried up, so one day, while trying hard to think of a funny story and failing, he tricked her like this once. First, he bragged that this story was the funniest and most amusing one yet, then suddenly slapped his knees, tuk-tak tuk-tak, and perfectly mimicked a rooster crowing, kkok! kiyo- kol- <70>. The act was so absurd that she found it incredibly funny and burst out laughing, clutching her stomach, "Oh, my sides- my sides!" Indeed, it was the hardest she had ever laughed. So he said, "See! Isn't this story the funniest one?" She then gave him a sidelong, resentful glance as if to say,
"Let me see!" - her gaze turned away.
"Look at you, tricking people like that!"
In other words, it seems they were doing what modern, enlightened young people call—dating, he thought, looking back at his past youth and savoring the sweet dreams of that time once more.
His wife, perhaps dreaming she was eating candy, felt her lips tickle and woke with a start, her eyes snapping open. Recognizing who it was, she simply grinned...
The moon was still bright, filtering through the tree branches, shining softly. The way its light touched her face intermittently must have been due to the branches swaying in the gentle breeze.
The son and daughter were now sleeping, snoring softly (콜콜). The couple's quiet murmuring broke the stillness of this moment. The night grew deeper...

Like prison guards changing shifts, the painful night changed into another painful day. The black night yielding to the white day (힌낫) was like a guard in black clothes leaving and a guard in white clothes arriving. At night, mercilessly bitten by mosquitoes, bedbugs, and fleas, and during the day, thoroughly tormented (복개여서) by heat and labor (勞役), their pitiful lives had no time or place for rest.
It was now dawn. The grey light of dawn gradually thinned, revealing the forms of people sprawled here and there like boulders scattered in a mountain valley. They looked like melons in a patch exposed by a gust of wind, clinging to withered stems, life out of season, rolling about haphazardly. Venomous insects (毒虫), crazed for the taste of human blood, went into a frenzy (熱狂) to fill their bellies one last time this night. Mosquitoes whined, ing- ing-, flying around and biting, while insidious bedbugs clung flat to the sleeping mats, sucking the very lifeblood from people's backs. <71> Then fleas crawled stealthily, biting and scattering blood-filled droppings everywhere. Mosquitoes attacked (攻擊) loudly like barbarians, only to be chased away, but bedbugs, like civilization, insidiously - skillfully - concealed their existence (存在) while sucking human blood. Then what could the flea be compared to? Its restless energy never stayed in one place, stinging sharply like the point of a needle. That creature thrives in dampness; since dogs sleep mostly on the ground, it breeds prolifically on dogs. While these creatures bit mercilessly inside and out*, the sleeping people, unconsciously convulsing (痙攣) as their muscles twitched (꼼작꼼작), were feeling the pain even in their dreams. The damp chill of early dawn made sound sleep impossible anyway. They would dream of falling into a pool of water, wake with a start, and find themselves soaked through, sticky and damp from the heavy dew. So they got up abruptly, slapping their buttocks, tuk-tuk, and scratching their heads, kŭkchŭk-kŭkchŭk.
Then they yawned until their mouths stretched wide, lit cigarettes, and sluggishly began to tackle their work.
Chŏng To-ryong also got up now, slapped his buttocks tuk-tuk as usual, scratched his head kŭkchŭk-kŭkchŭk, and yawned until his jaw clicked. His recently cut hair stood up bristly, like a chestnut burr. His eyes, the first to become active, instinctively looked ahead. The eastern sky was reddish, signaling the imminent sunrise, while the women, awake, had already lit fires in the kitchen (부억). The smoke mingled with the morning mist, entwined in the willow grove at the village entrance, one strand drifting lazily halfway up the back mountain.
In front, a clear river flowed serenely, piercing (뚤코) through the green banks. The fresh morning air, soft as a young girl's breath, gently vibrated (震動), making the dew-soaked tree leaves sway gently (하느작하느작). Before long, the sun rose redly, its rays, like golden threads (金線), shooting out like arrows through the gaps. On one side, the barley ripening yellow-green (부-여케) in the green fields rippled like waves. A dog barked a few times, k'ŏng-k'ŏng-k'ŏng! A pre-dawn rooster crowed a long, drawn-out kkok—kiyo—. Swallows diligently brought back insects, and sparrows chirped idly on the fence. One by one, human voices emerged as they each took hold (붓잡엇다) of their respective tasks. This was the leisurely summer morning in the farming village.
Chŏng To-ryong woke his son and daughter and first lit a cigarette. The taste of a pre-meal cigarette was truly famous. He thought that this cigarette was much better than a dull woman (게집). <72> But it was becoming a world where even cigarettes had to be rationed. As if intoxicated by the cigarette's taste, he puffed continuously, spitting out the accumulated saliva, t'ak! He picked up a broom and first swept (씨럿다) the inner and outer yards. His wife bustled about in the kitchen preparing breakfast, moving back and forth between the lower and upper rooms. The daughter helped with kitchen chores and had now gone to the well to draw water. Soon, the daughter returned, carrying a pail full of water, spilling drops, wiping the dripping water from the pail with her hand.
"Father! Time for breakfast!" At her call, the four family members finally gathered in the room. The daughter had just gone to fetch fresh scorched-rice water (숭늉). The father and son shared a small table, while the mother and daughter placed their bowls on the floor before them and ate the meal of Manchurian millet mixed with a tiny amount, like chaff in white rice, of actual rice.
"Ah! Let's buy another table. Isn't it inconvenient to eat hunched over with the bowls on the floor? Maybe that's why your back is bent," he said, looking at his wife and laughing.
"Hoho! Surely not? ... Where would we get the money for a table? There are more urgent things we can't afford."
"What, do we buy things only when we have plenty of money? We just have to force ourselves to buy it!"
The wife smiled faintly again and picked up kimchi with her hand to eat.
"Mother! We're fine without a table... When we scoop rice, we bend our backs, but when we put it in our mouths, we straighten up again. Unlike Oppa, who struggles to pick up food because he can't even use chopsticks (저깔질) properly, eats the rice first, and then eats the salty side dish later, eating with our hands like this doesn't leave a chopstick smell and tastes better, right?"
Everyone laughed at Kŭmsun's words. "She has truly graduated from poverty," Chŏng To-ryong laughed heartily.
"Still, you can't use chopsticks (저깔질) as well as I can, can you? Because you don't know how to use chopsticks, you'll probably never be able to be a guest at someone else's house your whole life?" Kŭmsŏk teased Kŭmsun again like this.
Since the house faced east, the morning sun shone brightly, making it blindingly difficult to bear. But the room was hot. These days, rooms that should be cool were hot as fire, while in winter, when they should be truly warm, the floor was cold as ice. Being cold when it's cold and hot when it's hot might align with nature, but for the fragile human life, there was nothing more unsuitable (不適). Because it was suffering. Amidst this suffering, <73> they finished their rough morning meal. Sweating profusely, they barely managed to eat.
After breakfast, he asked his son to do something and went out to the fields. It was farming work, cultivating a few paddies belonging to someone else. Behind him, the three family members sat down; Kŭmsun and her mother took up sewing materials (반우질거리), while Kŭmsŏk lay sprawled beside them, playfully watching his younger sister's hand holding the needle. The thick shade of the tree coolly covered the ground surface (地面), and its green leaves fanned again in the gentle breeze. They had spread a straw mat under the shade in front of the yard. Lying down like this after a meal, feeling the cool refreshment, brought an indescribably pleasant feeling. Kŭmsŏk expressed this sweet sensation with a smile, lying quietly now.
"Oppa! Why are you smiling? Shall I make you a trinket pouch (괴불)? With this?"
Kŭmsun showed a piece of silk scrap as if it were treasure, smiling brightly as she looked up at him. Kŭmsŏk smiled faintly and continued to look back at his sister. Kŭmsun wore a pink top and an indigo skirt; her glossy hair framed a slightly slender face that was beautiful (아름다웟을다). She already had the air of a young woman, her breasts (젓가슴) were developing plumply (도도록한게), giving her the appearance of a desirable young maiden. Her faintly visible arms had a violet sheen, hidden yet present. Her clear, distinct eyes seemed not to look casually but as if trying to discern something. So Kŭmsŏk suddenly thought, "You've grown up already too!... "
"The trinket pouch, make one for your son after you get married later! Forget about things like that and just lie down like this for a bit! Isn't it pleasant?"
"No! No! I don't want to! Oppa too... Who?... Lazybones!"
Kŭmsun, flushing crimson as if overcome with embarrassment and not knowing what to do, whined childishly, waving her elbows.
Meanwhile, the mother, smiling faintly for some reason, remained silent for a moment before saying, "They say if you lie down right after eating, you'll become a cow when you die. Get up quickly and go see."
She seemed to be reminding him of the task his father had asked him earlier. <74>
"I wish I could become a cow! Cows are lazy, so they can play around as much as possible!"
"So you like lazy cows?"
Kŭmsun cried out in a sharp voice.
"Of course, I do! I'm going to become a cow when I die."
"Ah! What's so good about being a cow? Really! Oppa, you too-."
"You simpleton, don't you get it? No matter how diligent you are, if it's all for nothing in the end, isn't it better to be as lazy as possible to save some effort? Diligence is suffering (苦痛). Is there such a fool who diligently buys suffering?"
"Hohoho! But if you're lazy, you'll be even poorer-."
"What—poorer! How much poorer can we get than this? We've already hit rock bottom poverty, haven't we? Even if our bottoms (방둥이) wear out from sitting—."
The mother and Okjin burst into laughter simultaneously, hoho.
"So I—" Kŭmsŏk started speaking again. He spoke smoothly, looking nonchalant.

**Summary**

This excerpt from Yi Ki-yŏng's novel "Farmer Chŏng To-ryong" vividly portrays the harsh realities of peasant life in 1920s rural Korea under the oppressive summer heat. It contrasts the grueling labor of tenant farmers, like the titular Chŏng To-ryong, working under the scorching sun and plagued by insects, with the idle comfort of a landowner. The narrative delves into the farmers' simple evening conversations, revealing their limited worldview, curiosity about modernity (trains, Seoul), and cynical humor born from hardship. A flashback explores To-ryong's past, his unconventional marriage to a former maid, defying social norms, and their escape from servitude. The story concludes with a family scene highlighting generational perspectives on poverty, labor, and fate.

From a colonial history perspective, the story offers insights into the deep poverty and grueling conditions faced by tenant farmers, a common reality exacerbated by colonial economic policies. The stark class difference between the laboring peasants and the lounging landowner reflects the social stratification. The characters' limited exposure to the outside world (market trips, Seoul) and fascination with simple modern marvels like trains underscore the isolation of rural communities. The discussion about traditional versus chosen marriage, and the critique of unproductive "yangban" culture, hints at simmering social tensions and perhaps nascent challenges to established hierarchies, even within the confines of rural life.

죄수(罪囚)를 감시하는 간수가 교대하는 것 가티 괴로운 밤은 다시 괴로운 낫으로 밧괴엿다. 검은 밤이 가면 힌낫이 오는 것은 검은 옷 입은 간수가 가고 힌 옷 입은 간수가 오는 것 갓다. 밤에는 모기, 빈대, 벼룩에게 사정업시 뜻기고 낫에는 더위와 로역(勞役)에 알뜰이 복개여서 그들의 애닯은 생명은 잠시도 안식할 때와 곳이 업다.
지금은 새벽녁이다. 새벽의 회색빗이 차침 차침 얄버지며 산고랑에 굴너잇는 바위돌갓치 여긔저긔 한전하는 사람들의 꼴아구니가 드러난다. 그것은 마치 건들바람에 열닌 원두가티 때안닌 생명을 시드른 꼭찌에 매달고 아모러케나 굴너잇는 그것과 갓다. 사람의 피맛에 환장한 독충(毒虫)들은 이 밤의 마지막 배를 불니랴고 열광(熱狂)한다. 모기는 잉- 잉-하고 다니며 쑈고 음흉한 빈대는 자리위에 탁 부터서 사람의 등꼴을 막 빠러 먹는다.＜71＞ 그러면 벼룩은 살살 기여단이며 뜨더 먹고는 함부로 피똥을 깔닌다. 모기는 야만인가티 함성을 지르고 대드러 공격(攻擊)을 하다가 쪼껴 가지마는 빈대는 문명인가티 음흉하게- 교묘하게- 자긔의 존재(存在)를 감추고 사람의 피를 빠러먹는다. 그러면 벼룩은 누구와 갓다할는지? 고놈의 팔팔한 긔운이 잠시도 한 곳에 부터 잇지 안코 반울 끗가티 딱끔하게 쏜다. 그 놈은 습긔에서 생기는 놈이라. 개는 땅에서 만히 자는 가닭에 그 놈은 개에게서 만히 생긴다. 이놈들이 사정업시 안팟에서 물지마* 잠자는 사람들은 무의식뎍으로 경련(痙攣)하드시 근육을 꼼작꼼작하는 것은 꿈속에서도 고통을 늣기는 것이다. 새벽무릅의 축축한 긔운은 아모려도 단잠을 이루지 못하게 한다. 그들은 물구덩이에서 빠진 꿈을 꾸다가 깜작 놀내 깨보면 찬이슬이 함씬 내려서 왼 몸이 축축하고 끈적끈적 하엿다. 그래 그들은 벌덕벌덕 니러나서 궁둥이를 툭툭털고 머리를 극죽극죽 하엿다.
그리고 합품을 입이 찌여지게하고 그 다음에는 담배를 부처물고 꿈지럭꿈지럭 일거리 붓들기 시작하엿다.
정도룡도 지금 니러나서 전례대로 궁둥이를 툭툭털고 머리를 극즉극즉하고 하품을 입에서 딱소리가 나게 하엿다. 막 깍근 머리는 더부룩하게 마치 밤송이가티 털이 니러섯다. 무엇보다 먼저 활동하는 눈은 본능뎍으로 눈 압흘 바라보앗다. 태양은 미구에 떠오르랴는 긔별을 보내는 드시 동천이 붉으스레한데 녀자들은 자고 깨서 위선 부억에다 불을 싸노앗다. 그 연긔가 아츰 안개와 어우러저서 동구압버들 숩헤 얼키엿는데, 그 한 가닥이 뒤ㅅ산중턱에 넛이즛 걸니엿다.
압흐로는 맑은 강이 푸른 언덕을 뚤코 그윽히 흘는다. 신선한 아츰 공긔가 소녀의 입김과 가티 부드러히 진동(震動)하야 이슬에 저진 나무닙을 하느작하느작 나뷔낀다. 어느 틈에 태양은 빨끈 떠올나와서 그 사이로 금실(金線) 갓흔 광선이 활살 가티 내뻐친다. 일면으로 푸른들에 부―여케 패나는 보리이삭은 굼실굼실 물결을 치고잇다. 개가 두어마듸 컹컹컹! 짓고 식전 닭이 유장한 목청으로 꼭―기요― 한 마듸를 느러지게 우는데 제비는 부지런이 버레를 무러드리고 참새는 한가히 울타리에서 짹짹어린다. 하나식 둘식 사람의 목소리가 들네며 그들은 제각기 할 일을 붓잡엇다. 이것이 농촌의 유한한 녀름 아츰이다.
뎡도룡은 아들과 딸을 깨우고 위선 담배 한 대를 피여 물엇다. 식전 담배맛이란 참으로 유명하엿다. 덤덤한 게집보다＜72＞ 이 때의 담배 한 대가 훨신 낫다고 그는 생각하엿다. 그런데 담배도 주려야 할 세상이다. 그는 그 담배맛에 취한드시 연신 빨며 울어난 침을 탁!배탓다. 그는 비짜루를 들어서 위선 안팟 마당을 씨럿다. 마누라는 아츰을 짓느라고 부억에서 달각달각하며 아래 윗방으로 들낙날낙한다. 딸은 부억일을 거드러 주다가 지금은 샘으로 물질너갓다. 미구에 딸이 물 한 동의를 찰늠찰늠 이고 방울방울 흘너내리는 물방울을 손으로 씨스며 도라오자
「아버지! 진지잡수서요!」하는 소리에 네 식구는 비로소 방으로 모혀드럿다. 딸은 지금 숭늉물을 새로 길너 간 것이엿다. 만주 좁살에 쌀이라고는 백미에 뉘가티 약간 석긴 밥을 부자는 겸상하고 모녀는 그 압헤 내려노코 먹는다.
「아! 상 하나를 더 삽시다. 편편치 안케 땅에다 노코 꾸부려서 먹기가 거북지 안소? 아마 당신의 허리가 구부러 진 것이 그 까닭이 안인가 몰나」 하고 그는 안해를 처다보며 우섯다.
「호호! 설마? ... 상 살돈이 어듸잇소? 그보다도 더 급한 것도 못하는대.」
「무엇은 살 돈이 넉넉해서 사겟소. 억지로 살내야 되는게지!」
안해는 다시 해죽이 웃고 손으로 김치를 집어다 먹는다.
「어머니! 우리는 상이 업서도 괜찬지요... 밥을 뜨러 갈 때에는 허리를 굽히지마는 입에 늘 때에는 다시 허리를 펴니까요. 옵바처럼 저러케 저깔질도 할 줄 모르는 것을 집으랴고 애쓰는 동안에 밥은 발서 삼키고 짠 반찬만 나종에 먹느니보다 이러케 손으로 집어먹으면 저깔내도 안나고 더 맛잇지요.」
「금순이의 이 말에 그들은 모다 우섯다. 가난에는 참 잘 졸업하엿구나 하고 뎡도룡은 허허 우섯다.」
「그래도 너는 나만치도 저깔질을 할 줄 모르지안늬? 너는 저깔질 할 줄 몰나서 남의 집손 노릇은 평생에 못해볼나?」 금석이는 금순이를 또 이러케 빈정거렷다.
동향집이라 아츰해가 빨끈 비처서 눈이 부시여 견딀수 업다. 그런대 방은 뜨겁다. 요새는 서늘해야 할 방이 불 가티 뜨겁고 짜장 더워야 할 겨울에는 방바닥이 어름판 가티 차다. 찰때 차고 더울때 더운것이 자연에 마질는지는 모르지마는 약한 인간생활에는 이것보다 부뎍(不適)한 일은 업다. 그것은 고통인 까닭이다. 이 고통 중에서＜73＞ 그들은 거치른 아츰을 치럿다. 땀을 뻘뻘흘니고 밥을 간신히 먹엇다.
아츰후에 그는 무엇인지 아들에게 부탁하고 일터로 나갓다. 그것은 몃마지기 안되는 남의 논을 부치는 농사이엿다. 그 뒤에는 세 식구가 금순이는 모친과 함께 반우질거리를 들고 안젓고 금석이는 그 엽헤 벌덕 드러누어서 깝작깝작 재미잇게 놀니는 녀동생의 반울 쥔 손을 드려다 보고 잇다. 두터운 나무그늘은 서늘하게 디면(地面)을 덥고 그 푸른 닙은 산들바람에 다시 부채질을 한다. 뜰 압 그늘미테다 밀방석을 편 까닭이다. 이러케 식후에 서늘한 맛을 늣기며 드러누엇는 것은 무엇이라 말 할 수 업는 상쾌한 마음을 늣기게 한다. 금셕이는 이 달콤한 맛을 미소로 표시하며 지금 가만이 누어 잇다.
「옵바! 웨 우서? 내- 괴불하나 해주까? 이걸로?」
하고 금순이는 비단쪼각을 무슨 보물인드키 살작 보이며 방긋 웃고 처다본다. 금석이는 빙그레 웃고 여전히 누이를 마주 처다 본다. 금순이는 분홍적삼에 도구물치마를 입엇는데 윤이 흐르는 머리에 좀 갸름한 얼골이 아름다웟을다. 발서 처녀태가 나서 젓가슴이 도도록한게 탐스러운 숫색시 꼴이 낫다. 얼비치는 팔둑은 보라빗으로 윤은연히 그리고 숨어 잇다. 또렷또렷한 눈매는 심상히 보지안코 무엇을 캐랴는 것 갓다. 그래 금석이는 이러케 곽 생각하엿다. 「너도 발서 다 컷고나!. ...」
「괴불은 이 다음에 시집가서 네 아들이나 나커던 해주랴무니! 그런것은 고만 두고 이러케 좀! 드러누어 보렴! 얼마나 유쾌한가?」
「아니! 아니! 나는 실혀! 옵바두... 누가?... 게으름장이!」
하고 금순이는 붓그럼에 타올나 엇절줄을 모르겟는드시 얼골이 다홍빗이 되며 어리광하듯 우는 소리를 한다. 두 팔꿈치를 내저으며.
그런데 모친은 무슨 의미인지 빙그레 웃고 잠자코 잇다가 「밥 벅고 바로 누면 죽어서 소가 된단다. 어서 니러나 나가 보아라」
하엿다. 아마 앗가 부친에게 부탁 밧든 것을 주의식히는 모양이다.＜74＞
「소나 되면 조켓소! 소는 게으르니까 할 수 잇는대로 놀거던!」
「그래 게으른 소가 조아?」
하고 금순이가 날카라운 목소리로 부르지젓다.
「그럼 조치안쿠! 나는 죽어서 소가 될난다.」
아! 소가 무에 조아요? 내 참! 옵바두-.
「이 숙맥아 그걸 모르늬? 암만 부지런해도 장- 제턱일바에는 할 수 잇는대로 게으른 것이 한 쪽 손*는 덜지 안느냐 말이다? 부지런한 것은 고통(苦痛)이거던. 부지런이 고통을 사는 그런 천치가 잇나」
「호호호! 그러타고 게으르면 더 가난하지무엘-.」
「머― 더 가난해! 이보담 더 가난할 게 잇서야지? 아주 가난이 밋바닥이 드러낫는데두? 응둥이가 찌여질내두 방둥이가 걸니도록-.」
모친과 옥진이는 일시에 호호 웃는다.
「그래 나는―」하고 금석이는 다시 말을 시작한다. 그는 순색으로 느물느물 말을 한다.
