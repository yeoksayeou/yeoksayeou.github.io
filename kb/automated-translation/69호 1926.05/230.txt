The Heart of One Going North -Three Years Ago-
						Yi Ho

Rolling upon Yŏngsakka hill
The fallen leaves are ever newer

In the heart of the winter mountains
A single tree looks even lonelier
It is the first winter

Since when
Has it thirsted for the spring breeze?
Having passed countless seasons
Leaving Chosŏn behind in winter
Leaving Chosŏn behind
Northbound! Intending to go north

As the last of this road
Those who departed the way station (逆旅)
Countless
White-clad people

This wind, this cold
Even more, battling the night gloom
Are they all in good health?

Amnok River, Amnok River!
You damn Amnok River!

If there is the Sinnamaryŏng plain
Pointlessly wide
Why here, right here, Chosŏn’s neck <112>
Do you stand, cutting it off?

Paektu Mountain, Paektu Mountain!! You damn Paektu Mountain!
The Earth’s back isn’t that narrow
I heard
Why here, right here, Chosŏn’s shoulders
Do you stand, pressing down?

Motherless Chosŏn
Has wept with a choked throat for a long time
Blocking the feet of those coming to herald the morning
Blocking the feet of those coming to herald even the spring not yet visited
Like the dark waters of the East Sea
Forever making Chosŏn a dark night

From the suffocation of this night
From the iron bars of this land
The feet of the Chosŏn fellow trying to escape
Are bound fast
Ah! Paektu Mountain and Amnok River!

Unlike Chosŏn, full of fiddly ridges, it's a different world

If there are mountains
They are low, wild hills
Beyond the fields stretching long, long
Dimly placed
Like distant mountains in an ink wash painting (墨畵)

At that house standing alone in the wide field
A dog comes out seeing the train and barks
A northern dog (胡개) comes out and barks

From the long past journey (逆旅)
The scent of this land, forgotten
I think I’ll try smelling it

Getting off the train at Ŭiju <113>
Buying tobacco grown in this land
The taste of the homeland, long forgotten, I think I’ll try it

Mountains continue, fields continue
The train continues along the railway
Time weaves the hours
And evening has arrived

The smoke from a distant village house, too
Gently falls asleep in the far sky
And the people of that house will now
Rest quietly in night's sleep

The fields sleep, the mountains sleep
The wind sleeps, everything in such sleep
In that sleep, only I, like the train
Cannot stop moving

Sleep! Darkness! Sleep! Darkness!

Ah! Why am I alone like this, sleepless?
As if finding tomorrow morning tedious (支離하다)
I await it eagerly, await it eagerly
Ah! Sleep! Darkness! Tomorrow morning!

For a young master from the Land of Courtesy (禮儀之國)

As if the mouth wasn't enough
Clinging tightly to my neck

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Ho captures the turbulent emotions of a Korean leaving colonized Chosŏn ("Three Years Ago," suggesting 1923) for the north, likely Manchuria or Siberia. The speaker expresses deep resentment towards the geographical features of the Amnok River and Paektu Mountain, personifying them as oppressors confining Chosŏn. The journey evokes feelings of displacement, a longing for the lost homeland contrasted with the vast, different landscape encountered. The poem ends with a jarring scene in a Russian bar, highlighting cultural disorientation and perhaps a desperate search for connection or escape, even through means conflicting with traditional Korean values ("Land of Courtesy"). For historians, the poem vividly illustrates the despair and motivations for leaving colonial Korea, the sense of geographical confinement imposed by natural borders perceived as collaborators with oppression, and the complex, perhaps ambivalent, encounter with foreign lands and cultures (like Russia) as potential, albeit confusing, alternatives or sites of exile. The mix of Korean resentment, nostalgia, and the bewildering foreign encounter provides insight into the psychological landscape of Koreans seeking refuge or a new path during this period.
