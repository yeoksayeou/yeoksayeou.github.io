The Pain of Russia's Period of Disillusionment Appearing in Chekhov's Plays, "Olga," If Only We Knew, If Only We Knew! Final Act of "Three Sisters"
						By Pak Yŏnghŭi

1
More dreadful than the colorless darkness that has lost its light (光明) must be the darkness of life that has lost the direction of its thought. More unbearable than the devil's outcry (號呌) cursing life must be the pain of life that torments in silence. When society decays in darkness and life weakens (衰弱) amidst agony, on a windswept mountain in a wide wilderness (荒野), there will be those who deliberately abandon great mansions (巨閣) and families, unknowingly soaked in tears, cursing life and crying out to heaven. There will also be those who cast aside ten thousand volumes (萬卷) of precious books (寶書) like dust and trash (塵芥), look upon the many hopes of the future with the contempt (輕蔑) shown to wild grass (野草), and sacrifice their precious lives to the vastness (遼遼) of past and present (古今), to profound philosophy (深遠한 哲理). Taking his beloved wife and children (妻子) to the rough plains, he suffers from hunger and shivers in the cold, but who knows him? Who will give him a job and give him food?
Ah! If you ask why it is so painful for people to live, I have nothing more to say than, "Because humans are the product (産物) of thought and life."
Indeed, humans are governed by life and, at the same time (同時), governed by thought. The land of asceticism (禁慾) is conquered (征服) by the tyrant (暴君) of the flesh, romanticism gets buried in naturalism (自然主義), the mouth that called for philosophy now praises labor (勞働), and the mouth that sang hymns (聖歌) could also be refreshed by a lover's (戀人) kiss.
Thus, literature also follows its environment (環境)—its own thought or social thought (社會思想). What shows us this change, its thought, its life, and its literary arts most clearly is everything before the Russian revolution.<55>
From the 18th century, how closely Russian life and literary arts showed us the new kingdom of "Life and Thought" in human existence—we are reminded of this again now. More than half (過半) of all Russian literary arts were Russia's confession (告白) and a great admonition (訓戒) from the reality (實際) of life. Compared to the gorgeousness (華美) of French literary arts, Russian literary arts were gloomy (沈鬱). If French literary arts were "thought," Russian literary arts were so closely related to life that they could be called "life" itself. If the former (前者) is the "truth" (真理) of the floating world (浮世), the latter (後者) could be called the cry for "life's revolution" (生活의 革命) and "life's reform" (生活의 改組). Turgenev spoke of this, Chekhov cried out for this, Dostoevsky declared this, and Tolstoy yearned for this. Russian writers were Russia's most faithful (忠實) friends.
Consequently, Russia's dark age continued for a long time, disregarding (不顧) it. Also, many writers clearly perceived (明察) this. For example, Turgenev's "A Sportsman's Sketches" depicted the conditions of Russia at that time (當代) well.—

When asking a peasant (農夫), "Why don't you seek freedom (自由) from your master (主人)?"
The answer that came from their mouths was, "Why should I get freedom? I know my master and pay my rent (賃稅) well, you see?" That's how dark it was. Everything stagnated (沈滯), everything was unfree (不自由), everything resulted in failure (不成功). All literature also had no value (價値), and everything became futile like smoke (煙氣). Turgenev's so-called nihilism (虛無主義) also originated from this. Father and son parted ways (相別) for the sake of ideology, love was abandoned due to the poverty (困窮) of life, tears of loneliness (孤寂) were shed on the cold plains, and we could see Raskolnikov's blood-curdling declaration in "Crime and Punishment" and his final fate (末路). Young men (青年) engaged in revolution, and the love affairs of fragile young women were a single entity (團合物) combining "lover and revolution." In any case, their world was a dark age. However, fundamentally (根本的) urging (催促) the dawn light (曙光) of a new life along with yearning according to the times were the plays (戱曲) of Chekhov, which I intend to discuss here.

2
The time when Chekhov first began writing (創作) was when he was 19 years old—(1879). He spent the happiest period of his life during the most unfortunate (不幸) era of his fatherland (祖國). After the tragic (悲愴) death of Alexander II and the accession (卽位) of Alexander III,<56> there was no prospect or leeway for achieving any positive (積極的) activity or splendid (華麗) hope. The fervent plans of the youth who clamored, "Become friends of the peasants," were shattered (粉碎) without any hope, and only the faint groans of the victims (犧牲者) could be heard from the depths of fortresses (要塞地) or within the vast plains (雲原?) of Siberia. It was at such a time that Chekhov began to write. Thus, if the period from 1881 to 1904 is called the Pobedonostsev era politically (政治上), in the social and literary arts field (文藝方面), it is called the Chekhov era. The society of that time, as a reaction (反動) to the heroic excitement of the 1870s, saw a trend (風潮) of coldness (冷淡) permeate society, morality (道德), and other areas, so wherever one looked, desolation (寂寥) and fatigue overflowed like the twilight (黃昏) of an autumn sky. Exhaustion and impoverishment (疲弊困憊), indecisiveness (優柔不斷), and weak will (薄志弱行) were pervasive. The cultured (敎養) society yearned (渴望) for political freedom but failed to achieve it, merely spending the years in inaction (無爲), while the intellectual class (知識階級), due to the failure (不成功) of everything, fell into strong liquor (毒酒) and disappointment (失望). Therefore, the classes on the opposite side criticized (非難) and insulted (凌辱) the intellectual class. Chekhov himself based his art on the foundation that "in our Russia, everything happens unintentionally (不知意?)." As an artist of innate talent (天稟), he depicted the truth (眞) of reality (現實) through his entire works (全作品) and described (描寫) the life of humans (人間) and the life of nature (自然). However, he did not view them with the eye of impartiality (一視同仁) like the French humanists (印度主義者). He simply showed us frankly (率直) the tragedy (悲劇) of life existing in reality with a truthful (眞實) heart. He was originally (本來) feminine (女性的), gentle (柔軟), soft (軟), and most like a lyric poet (抒情詩人), but throughout his life (一生), he possessed only sorrow (悲哀) and trembling (戰慄) without song. Looking at the play "Three Sisters," one can taste his soft (柔軟) mood (情調) and sorrowful tears. The sorrow he depicts was neither the grand tragedy (悲壯劇) found in classical literature (古典文學) nor the tearful stage of romantics or sentimentalists. It was merely a stage overflowing with the agony (苦悶), melancholy (憂鬱), and sorrow (哀傷) of ordinary (普通), mundane (平凡) daily life (日常生活). However, even in darkness, Chekhov always offers something, like the faint hope (希望) and light (光明) thrown like a maiden's (處女) intermittent (間歇) smile.

3
Before discussing his plays, what he dealt with were mostly the conflict (葛藤) between the educated and the ignorant (無識者), the pain of existence and life, the yearning for life, sorrow, desire... these things. Moreover, the element (要素) of pain and sorrow was abundant (豊富). The sorrow was not so much tearful (有泣的) as it was "tears of the heart," and the pain was not so much a burning flame as it was a "heart-breaking, secret, deep suffering." First, I intend to begin with the resentment stemming from the gap between the educated and the ignorant.<57>
"Uncle Vanya (叔父완야)"
Vanya's (완야) house takes in his former brother-in-law, Alexander Serebryakov (알넥산더 세레부라코푸), a retired professor (退職敎授). The professor, along with his young wife from his remarriage (再婚), comes to stay (留) at Vanya's house. However, since he came to this house, all the happiness (幸福) in this house gradually (漸漸) disappears (消失). One day, he had the following conversation (談話) with Doctor Astrov (아스튜로푸).

Astrov—Were you sleeping?
Voynitsky (뽀잇스키)—Yes, I slept quite a lot. [Yawning] Since that professor and his wife came here, our life has jumped off track. I sleep at random times, eat many different kinds of food at lunch, and drink alcohol too. It's not good for health. Sonia (쏘니아) (this girl is the daughter of the professor's first wife, meaning Vanya's niece) and I have never been lazy, but these days, only Sonia works, and I just drink and eat. It's not right.
In these words, one can see how much Voynitsky, who used to work daily with hope for life, has fallen (墮落) due to the professor's influence (影響). One can also glimpse the laziness (怠惰) of the educated people in Russia at that time. Therefore, Voynitsky, following that lifestyle himself, could not suppress the unspeakable feeling of hatred in his heart. Furthermore, Voynitsky's mother (母親)—

Marina (마리나)—[Shaking her head] The house has become so chaotic. Since the professor gets up around twelve, the samovar (茶釜) is kept boiling until afternoon. And all the food has to wait until then too. Before they came, we used to have lunch at one, like other people. But now we eat around six. And the professor sits up all night writing or reading books, then suddenly, around two in the morning, he rings the bell. What could it be? It means bring tea. So, they wake up the servant, make him build a fire, and boil tea. It's really irregular!
Philosophy or literature, and all other forms of learning (學問), had no relation (關係) whatsoever to the life (生) of the Russian people (民族) at that time. For those endlessly suffering in life, for those agonizing in thought, learning offered no comfort (慰安), and at the same time (同時),<58> the feelings of respect (尊敬) and affection (愛慕) had long since disappeared. The educated were merely treated as troublesome things. There was learning, but it could not comfort them; there was art, but it did not resonate in their hearts. Therefore, Turgenev also spoke abundantly of this in "Fathers and Sons." The protagonist, Bazarov (빠사로푸), was a student of medicine and natural science, but when we see him declare, "Before we read books, we must learn the alphabet. We haven't even reached the letter A yet," we can understand how powerless their learning was at the time, and we can also perceive how intense the suffering of their lives was. Upon hearing music coming from the second floor, Bazarov asked his friend Arkady (아카듸)—
"Who is the musician?" Bazarov asked, surprised.
"It's my father," Arkady replied.
"What? Your father plays the cello?"
"Yes."
"At what age?"
"Forty-four."
Bazarov laughed. Arkady asked—
"Why are you laughing?"
"My apologies, but the thought of your father, a man of forty-four, head of the household, a prominent figure in this town, having to play the cello (樂器名)?"
This is similar to the insult (侮辱) directed at the professor mentioned above.
『Uncle Vanya (반야 叔父)』 (Note: Title repeats, likely a formatting artifact in original)
Astrov—Any news?
Voynitsky—None. Everything has failed. It's because I've become lazy. I do nothing but chirp like an old crow. My mother, on the one hand, chatters about women's liberation all day, and on the other, only reads books. Trying to find the dawn light (曙光) of a new life.
Astrov—And the professor?
Voynitsky—The professor sits in his study (書齋) from morning till evening, diligently, frowning, without rest,<59> writing and writing, hoping for praise (稱頌) in the future or present (abbreviated)...
For 25 years, he has spent all his time thinking about other people's ideas regarding things like naturalism or realism (實際主義), all sorts of foolish things. For 25 years, he has only written and read things that wise people have known for a long time and dull people find uninteresting (無趣味).
When Voynitsky's mother, a proponent of women's liberation, received a newly published booklet (新著小冊子) from Pol Alexeyevich one day and praised it as interesting—
Voynitsky—There's nothing to praise. Mother, just have some tea.
Voynitskaya—You don't want to hear what I'm trying to say. My apologies. But over the past year, I've changed so much I can hardly recognize myself. You too must become a reliable person and possess a brilliant personality (光輝잇는 人格).
Voynitsky—Yes, I possess a brilliant personality. It just doesn't shine on others. [Pause] I possess a brilliant personality. Mother, you can't say anything more. I am forty-six years old. Until last year, I strove. Now, in a scholarly style (學者風), you seem to be clouding my eyes regarding the truth (眞理) of life—.
Just like Bazarov in Turgenev's "Fathers and Sons" painfully declared, "What is all this art and poetry?", personality and learning were useless to Voynitsky. His statement, "I possess a brilliant personality. But it doesn't shine on others," might be the reason for criticizing the educated at that time. We can ironically infer that all scholars lacked the influence and ability to give anything to others. Not only this, but the sorrow of life found in Byron's "Manfred" also seems similar. Faust, who studied all disciplines, also fell into the pit of despair. Going a step further, Chekhov's work is not only Russian national literature but also a work that need not be ashamed internationally. Voynitsky's words, "clouding my eyes regarding the truth of life in a scholarly style," seem mundane but were also an endlessly profound declaration. All truths, besides existing as truths, also clothed the ordinary life, which could be lived without demanding truth, in more difficult problems, leading it astray. Also, the scholars themselves despaired and cried out—<60>
Serebryakov—My whole life (全生) has been wasted studying. I was always in libraries and lecture halls. Therefore, I received respect and praise from my colleagues. Now, I've fallen into this wilderness where I have to listen to the useless talk (談話) of foolish people from morning till evening. I want to live. I want my success and fame to move the world. But I've been driven here!
This was the thought of the intellectuals who disregarded all aspects of reality and held expectations. Furthermore, when Voynitsky and the professor talk about the land (土地)—
Serebryakov—How could I know about such things? I'm not a practical (實用的) person, so I don't know. (Omitted)
Voynitsky—We always thought of you as a superman (超人). But now, as your dignity falls before my eyes, I only see you as you are. You know nothing, yet you write about art! Those writings (著書) I loved are now worthless. You're a prankster (惡戱者)!
This alone is enough to understand the nature of the conflict between the educated and the ignorant in Russia at that time. Not only that, but it also insulted the seemingly stagnant Russia. They insulted and lamented their own homeland.
Sonia—Aren't you dissatisfied (不滿足) with this kind of life?
Astrov—As for life, I like this life. But as long as my life continues, I dislike and despise being in this small Russian country village. There's absolutely nothing worthwhile. If you ride a horse at night through a dark forest and see the small light shining ahead, have you noticed how tired the branches hitting your face and the darkness make you? As you see, I don't work in the countryside like others do. Fate pursues me relentlessly. Sometimes, when I suffer unbearably, I see no light. I have no hope, and I hate seeing people. I haven't loved anyone for a long time.
Sonia—You haven't loved anyone?
Astrov—Not a single person. I only miss you for the sake of the old wet nurse (乳母) and the past (過去). The peasants (農夫) are all the same, and they live dull lives in filth.<61> So, one cannot live together with educated people. Anyone gets tired of them eventually.
The misunderstanding of the intellectuals and the lack of understanding (無理解) of the ignorant tormented them together. Mechanov's revolutionary declaration in "Virgin Soil (處女地)" could be said, on the one hand, to reveal the ignorance of the intellectuals towards the ignorant. When Mechanov gave pamphlets to peasants on the road, the peasants asked, "Are you a Christian?" showing how little they understood the cries of "To freedom! To revolution!" More than anything, their education (敎育) was lacking. But their time was pressing. Thus, what arose between them was this kind of resentment. They fell into the pit of despair because of this. They lived aimlessly day by day (醉生夢死), cursing life without purpose or desire, spending joyless days.

Sonia—Uncle, Uncle, the doctor drinks alcohol! Good people live together, right? The doctor drinks because it's a good thing for him, but Uncle, why do you do as he does? At your age, it looks very alarming.
Voynitsky—Age doesn't matter. When there's nothing in real life, one must create illusions. That's better than having nothing at all.

In Russia, there were many books and many philosophers, but they could not point the way for them. Was the only city of refuge (逃避城) the poisonous liquor that made one forget everything when drunk, and the despair that endlessly dropped upon reflection? Educated or ignorant, they all got drunk on vodka (酒名), and once sober, their only occupation was mutual recrimination. Was there no other path in the vast land of Russia? The pain of life was this terrifying, and the undercurrent (暗流) of thought was this atrocious (凶暴). There was no one to hear the cries, and no one to guide (引導) the wanderers. Before the story of this "Uncle Vanya" ends, let's examine the play "Ivanov (이바노푸)" again!
Ivanov was a young man full of hope. But he did not have the environment to sustain it. He suffered a double agony from heavy debts and his wife's tuberculosis. Like the dual psychology in Shakespeare's "Hamlet (함렛트)," Ivanov himself suffered what could be called a second "Hamlet."<62> It was the expression of suffering that reached the utmost peak of the reality Chekhov saw. If "Hamlet" was secondary, Ivanov would be primary. The suffering arising from reality would be a suffering without cooling, hotly contacting era after era deep within the heart. Suffering can be classified as realistic and perpetual. If the former is negative, the latter would be positive. In other words, unlike small realistic sufferings satisfied by small realistic comforts, a person with great realistic suffering and great sorrow will not be intoxicated by small satisfactions but will have a profound depth of composure and melancholy like a pine forest in winter. Within that depth lies "eternity." Therefore, Hamlet's love affair did not hinder his great success, and the pleasure of Faust's magic could not destroy him. In Ivanov lies Chekhov's eternal suffering. He led from a mundane beginning to a pure eternity.
After the creditor's husband, Lebedev (리베데이에푸?), came and demanded payment (支拂催促) and left—left alone—
Ivanov— I am worthless, pitiful, and useless. People like Paul, who share sorrow and suffering, would love and respect me. Oh God. How much I hate myself, my voice, my hands, my thoughts, my clothes, even my footsteps. How ridiculous and how offensive! Just a year ago, I was healthy (健康), strong, full of energy and enthusiasm. I worked with these hands, and my voice made foolish people weep. I cried when sad, and felt anger when seeing injustice. I realized the burning heat (灼熱) of inspiration (靈感), knew beauty, and knew the familiar quiet night's romance that made my soul dream, sitting at my desk from morning till night. At that time, I truly saw my bright future, just as a child sees it in its mother's eyes. But now, oh—it's dreadful. I am tired and useless. I have wasted my many days in idleness. I cannot control my head or my feet. My property is gone, and my forests have all perished under the axe. [Crying] My indolent country looks up at me like an orphan complaining. I have no expectations and no particular sorrow. My whole spirit (全靈) trembles thinking of<63> each new day. (Omitted - original text) I don't know. I don't know. The easiest way is to put a gun to my head.
Much despair and anguish (煩憫) cried out just like a common bankrupt, like someone whose character is bankrupt (心格破産者?). However, starting from this mundane daily life and reaching the profound, forest-like thought was Chekhov's ideal. Although 『Uncle Vanya』 and 『Ivanov』 belong to the same category, one depicts suffering arising from and affecting the educated, while the other shows suffering received directly from life. Here, I will compare the final acts (終幕) of both.
『Uncle Vanya (叔父반야)』
Voynitsky's resentment towards the professor gradually intensified. Both the doctor and Voynitsky were captivated by the beauty of the professor's second wife. Sonia loved the doctor secretly. But upon seeing the professor's second wife, he lost his heart. Thus, between Act 2 and Act 3, there was poignant love. But in the end (結局), Voynitsky's hatred (憎惡) towards the professor grew stronger. And so, in the final act, this happened.
Marina—Bang! What was that sound?
Professor Serebryakov (세립라고푸 교수)—(Staggering in fearfully) Catch him! Catch him! Your uncle has gone mad. Helena and Voynitsky are seen struggling at the doorway.
Helena—(Trying to snatch the pistol from him) Give it here, give it here, please!
Voynitsky—Let go! Helena! Let go! (He breaks free, rushes in, and looks for the professor) Where is he? Where? (Points the gun at the professor. Pause) Missed! Missed again! (Violently) You beast, you beast, you wicked man!
He always held the resentment, "I became like this because of you." The relationship with the educated class at that time was like this. Looking again at "Ivanov," in the final act, the creditor's daughter, "Sasha (싸사?)," due to her love, proposed marriage to Ivanov. (Of course, this was after his sick wife had died.) But the man whose heart was bankrupt did not feel happy about it. His suffering had reached a point where it was the most incurable, perpetual (永久性) suffering. Ivanov spoke of his philosophy and life and refused the marriage. But in the end,<64> the wedding day arrived, preparations for the ceremony were complete, and many people urged (勸告) him.
Ivanov—Old friends, listen to me. I will not try to explain myself to you. I will not say whether I am honest (正直) or dishonest (不正直), healthy or mad (狂人). You wouldn't understand anyway. I had a youth. At that time, I was faithful (忠實) and knowledgeable (知識). I loved, hated, and believed unlike others. —Omitted (original text)— I drank, worked, and made myself struggle (奮鬪). My energy knows all this infinity (無限). —Abbreviated (original text)— I am a broken man. I have aged 30 years. I have submitted to old age. With a heavy head, a lazy heart, boredom, and lack of courage (無勇氣), without faith (信仰), without love, without purpose (目的) in life, I wander like a shadow among other people. I don't know why I live, nor what I should do. Love is foolishness to me. It's caressing a lie. —Abbreviated (original text)—
As Sasha tries to forcibly take Ivanov to the wedding venue—
Ivanov—Where are you trying to go? Wait just a moment. Soon you will know the result (結果) of everything. My youth awakens me again. The Ivanov of old is here once more! (Pulls out a repeating pistol (連發短銃))
Sasha—(Screaming) Ah, I know what he's going to do! Ah! Nikolas. Please...! —Abbreviated (original text)— (Screaming) Nikolas. Please catch him!
Let go! (Pulls out the gun and commits suicide)

4
When the heavy rain (暴雨) of melancholy passes, the gentle breeze (微風) of sorrow relentlessly shakes the human heart; when the sun of agony sets, the heart wanders aimlessly in the moonlight (月光) of heartbreak (上心). Ivanov's fire-like agony and Voynitsky's flame-like mockery, like the changeable mercy of an angry mother, revealed a soft (柔軟) and sorrowful mood (情調) that caressed the pitiful heart with tears and sighed with sighs, which is the easiest thing to taste throughout Chekhov's art, existing inside (裏面) the agony and mockery. Like the chaos (混沌) of darkness, along with childlike confusion and agitation (驚動), the faint light of hope, like a maiden's (處女) yearning, twinkles like the light of stars (星辰) in the dark sky,<65> which is Chekhov's characteristic (特色). Looking through his plays, the fall caused by great suffering does not completely sink into the depths (深底). A small hope and the dawn light (曙光) of some distant expectation are always flickering. This could be said to be Chekhov's talented work speaking of Russia's destiny. Just as "Three Sisters (三人姊妹)" speaks of the sorrow of life, "The Cherry Orchard (櫻園)" speaks of the bankruptcy of life, and "The Seagull (海鷗)" speaks of yearning, "The Cherry Orchard" hinted at the dawn light of new life (新生), "Three Sisters" yearned for truth, and "The Seagull" declared the reality (質際?) of life. The inner smile of a deeply faithful lady (淑女) and the melancholy of a world-weary wanderer (飄流兒) can often be seen in these three plays.
"Three Sisters (三人姊妹)"
Chekhov's representative sorrow is here. It speaks of the sorrow of life occurring in the family of three sisters and one brother who left their hometown Moscow (모스코). The eldest, Olga (올가), worked at a school, and Irina (이리나) at the post office (電信局). As their daily lives gradually declined, their sorrow began to increase. On Irina's birthday (命名日), they gathered in the desolate room, sometimes reminiscing (憶念) about happy past days and resenting their current misfortune.

Olga—Don't whistle, Masha (마사)! How can you do such a thing? [Pause] Every day from morning till evening at school, teaching, my head always hurts. I have the strange feeling that I'm already like an old woman. Truly, during the four years I've worked here, it feels like my strength and my youth are gradually wearing away day by day.... Abbreviated—(original text)
Irina—Let's go back to Moscow. Sell the house, leave everything here, and go to Moscow. How much a life without attachment, a life without comfort, torments one's heart and soul! In "Uncle Vanya," Marina says, "Back then, you were pretty and young. But now, you're not pretty and look quite old. And you drink—"
"Astrov (아스튜로푸)"—Abbreviated (original text)—And anyway, existence is<66> tedious. Meaningless, corrupted. This life passes heavily," he cried out. It's an awareness of life that everyone experiences, but the suffering appearing in Chekhov's plays was, like the suffering of the French decadents, a great suffering concerning human existence. In mundane outcries, there was always a great implication. "Three Sisters" shows a richer poetic mood compared to the others.

Irina—I am tired. I really hate going to the telegraph office (電信局), I hate it.
Masha—You are getting weaker. [Pause, whistles] And you look young, like a child's face.... Abbreviated—(original text)
Irina—Abbreviated (original text)—What I want, what I try to have, what is definitely lacking here is, labor without poetry, labor without ideals... Abbreviated—(original text)

Irina's sorrow was everyone's sorrow. Compared to the fact that nothing could offer comfort in their lives at that time, they also fantasized (空想) about that much comfort. "Labor without poetry, labor without ideals" was, in other words, a tasteless life like unconscious slave labor. The temperament (心質) of women, easily weakened, and the curiosity (好奇心) of women, easily prone to yearning, could be called a mutation (變體?) of life at that time. **Summary**

Pak Yŏnghŭi analyzes several of Chekhov's major plays ("Uncle Vanya," "Ivanov," "Three Sisters," "The Seagull," "The Cherry Orchard") as reflections of the profound disillusionment, suffering (苦痛), and stagnation (沈滯) characterizing pre-revolutionary Russia. The article highlights the conflict between ineffective intellectuals and the suffering populace, the pervasive sense of despair leading to inaction or self-destruction, and the decay reaching from society into the family unit. However, Pak emphasizes that Chekhov doesn't leave his characters in utter despair, finding in the plays' conclusions, particularly Olga's speech in "Three Sisters" and Anya's in "The Cherry Orchard," a glimmer of hope (希望) and a call to find meaning and build a new future through perseverance and work (勞働). From a colonial Korean history perspective, the article is significant as it uses the critique of Tsarist Russian society—its intellectual impotence, societal despair, and search for direction—as a likely allegorical commentary on the state of Korean society under Japanese rule. The focus on suffering, disillusionment, and the final, tentative calls for work and hope would have resonated with Korean intellectuals grappling with their own constrained reality.

「무엇 때문에 내가 자유를 엇어요? 나는 내 주인을 알고 또 나의 집세도 잘 내는데요, 네?」하는 대답이 그들의 입에서 나올이 만츰 암흑하엿든 것이다. 모든 것이 침체되고 모든 것이 不自由로, 모든 것이 不成功에 일으럿섯다. 모든 문학도 아모 가치가 업섯고 모든 것은 「煙氣」와 가티 허무하고 말엇다. 투게네푸의 소위 허무주의도 또한 이에서 비롯한 것이다. 主義를 위하야 父子가 相別하고 생활의 곤궁으로 사랑을 떠나 추은 벌판에서 孤寂의 눈물을 뿌리엿고, 「罪와 罰」에 나타나는 래스콜니코푸의 피떠는 선언과 그의 末路도 볼 수 잇엇다. 졂은이는 혁명에 나섯고 여엽분 처녀들의 연애는 「애인과 혁명」을 한가지한 한 團合物이엿엇다. 엇지하엇든 그들의 세계는 암흑시대엿섯다. 그러나 때를 따라 憧憬과 한가지 신생활의 曙光을 가장 근본적으로 催促하든 것은 이에 말하려는 쳬호푸의 戱曲인 것이다.

쳬호푸가 처음으로 창작하든 때는 19歲이엿다-(1879년) 그는 인생의 가장 행복스런 시대를, 조국의 가장 불행시대에 더 지내가지 안엇다. 알넥산더 2세의 悲愴한 죽엄과 한가지 알넥산더 3세가 즉위한 후로,＜56＞ 적극적 활동과 화려한 희망은 조금도 성취할 餘望도 업고, 여유도 업섯다. 『농민의 벗이 되여라』고 떠들든 청년의 열렬한 계획은 조금도 희망업시 粉碎되고 말엇고 다만 희생자의 그윽한 신음소리가 要塞地 깁흔 곳에서, 혹은 세비리아 넓은 雲原속에서 들녀 올 뿐이엿든 때이엿다. 이러한 때에 체호푸는 창작하기 시작하엿다. 그리하야 이 1881년으로부터 1904년까지를 정치상 뽀베도노즈쓰에푸 시대라 하면 사회문예방면으로는 쳬호푸의 시대라고 한다. 당시대의 사회는 1870년의 헤토익 흥분시대의 반동으로 사회, 도덕, 기타 방면에는 冷淡의 풍조가 처들어와서 어느 곳을 보든지 가을 한울 황혼과 가티 寂寥와 피로가 넘처서 흘넛다. 疲弊困憊, 優柔不斷, 薄志弱行이 가득이 찻엇다. 교양잇는 사회는 정치적 자유를 갈망하다가 일우지 못하고 다만 無爲로 해를 보냇으며 지식계급은 모든 것이 不成功을 원인으로 하야 毒酒와 실망에 빠지엿다. 그런 고로 그 반면의 계급에서는 지식계급을 비난하며 凌辱하엿다. 쳬호푸 자신도 「우리 露西亞에서는 만사가 不知意」라는 기초가 그의 예술이엿다. 그는 天稟의 예술가로 全작품을 통해서 현실의 眞을 그리엇으며 인간의 生과 자연의 生을 묘사하엿다. 그러나 佛蘭西의 인도주의자와 가티 一視同仁의 눈으로 보지 안엇다. 그는 다만 진실한 마음으로 현실에 잇는 인생의 비극을 솔직하게 우리에게 보이여 주엇다. 그는 본래 여성적으로 부드럽고 軟하고 가장 서정시인과 갓헛으나, 일생  노래도 업시 비애와 戰慄을 가질 뿐이엇다. 희곡 「三人姊妹」를 보면 그의 柔軟한 情調와 비애로운 눈물을 맛볼 수 잇다. 그가 그리는 비애는 고전문학에 잇든 悲壯劇도 안이엿고 로맨틕이나 센틔멘탤이스트의 눈물의 무대도 안이엿엇다. 다만 보통 평범한 일상생활의 고민과 우울과 애상이 흘너넘치는 무대엿다. 그러나 암흑에서도 늘 쳬호푸는 미약한 희망과 광명이 처녀의 간얼핀 웃음과 가티, 던저주는 무엇이 잇다.
