발행일	1924년 02월 01일
기사제목	조선아 (O Chosŏn!)
필자	長白山人
기사형태	시 (Poem)

Chosŏn!
						Changbaeksanin

Chosŏn!
You who give me such sorrow,
Give me worry,
Give me longing when I leave,
Give me gladness when I see you again,
Deeply fated Chosŏn!

For you, how many times
Have I wept, have I ground my teeth?
How many times have I kicked you away, saying I hate you?
How many times have I shaken my head, saying I wouldn't look back…
And then again,
Ah, yet again,
Saying "Oh, my Chosŏn!" have I embraced you tightly?

Ah—Chosŏn!
Why could you not grow large like others?<110>
Could not grow strong?
Why could you not be wise like others?
How did you become a laughingstock for others?
Ah, how much must I have cursed you?
Must have cursed having come from your womb?

But ah, my Chosŏn!
I love you!
These young eyes have opened today
And have seen the light of the treasure
Hidden deep, deep within your breast.
Ah, I have seen that light!

Ah, you were never foolish,
Never worthless.
The Mugunghwa that will shine for a thousand years!<111>
To become that single flower,
During five thousand long, long years,
Waiting for the spring breeze of that day,
Ah, who could have known?
How could even I have known?
The expression in your eyes, pretending to be foolish,
Ah, who could claim to have known?

Look here!
Children of Chosŏn!
Sons and daughters, cease your weeping eyes!
Even the white-haired children, old from weeping, leap for joy!
Those whose hearts boiled at others' ridicule, leap up from your graves and watch with joy!
Shout out and boast to those who laughed at you—
"Behold! My Chosŏn is not small!"

Ah, my Chosŏn!<112>
<110-112>

**Summary**

This poem by "Changbaeksanin" (likely a pseudonym invoking Mt. Paektu) expresses a complex, passionate love for Korea (Chosŏn). The speaker laments Korea's perceived weakness and suffering ("laughingstock"), admitting past frustration and even curses. However, this turns into a powerful affirmation of love, declaring the discovery of Korea's hidden inner strength and potential, symbolized by the Mugunghwa (Rose of Sharon) flower enduring through millennia. The poem concludes with an impassioned call for Koreans of all ages to stop despairing, feel pride, and proclaim Korea's inherent greatness. For historians, the poem is a poignant example of nationalist sentiment and cultural resilience during the Japanese colonial period. It reflects the deep emotional turmoil—shame, anger, love, hope—felt by Koreans grappling with their nation's subjugation while searching for and asserting intrinsic national value and a brighter future.

조선아!
그러케도 내게 슬픔을 주고
근심을 주고
떠나면 그리움을 주고
다시 볼때에 반가움을 주는

너를 위하야 내가
멧번이나 울엇던고, 니를 갈앗던고
멧번이나 밉다고 발ㅅ길로 찻스며
멧번이나 안돌아 본다고 고개를 흔들엇던고…
그리고는 또다시
아아 또다시
오 「내 조선아!」하고 얼사 껴 안앗던고?

아-조선아!
왜 너는 남과가티 크지를 못하엿더냐＜110＞
굿세지를 못하엿더냐
웨 남과가티 슬긔롭지를 못하엿더냐
엇지하야 남의 우슴ㅅ거리가 되엇더냐
아아 얼마나 내가 너를 져주하엿스랴
네 배에서 나온 것을 져주하엿스랴

그러나 아아 내 조선아!
나는 너를 사랑하노라!
이 어린 눈이 오늘에야 띄어
네 가슴속에 깁히깁히 감초인
보물의 빗을 보앗노라
아아 그 빗을 보앗노라

아아 너는 결코 못난이가 아니러니라
갑업는 이가 아니러니라
쳔년에 빗날 무궁화!＜111＞
그 한송아리를 되오랴
오쳔년 기난긴 세월에
그 날의 봄ㅅ바람을 기다릴 때에
아아 그 뉘라 알앗스리
낸들 어이 알앗스리
못난체 하는 네 눈의 표정을
아아 그 뉘라 알앗다더냐

이바라!
조선의 아기들아!
아들들아 딸들아, 울던 눈물을 거둘지어다
울기에 늙은 머리힌 아기들도 깃버 뛸지어다
남들의 비우슴에 애끗던 이들아, 무덤ㅅ속에서 뛰어 닐어나 깃븐 모양으로 볼지어다.
너의들을 웃던 자에게 소리쳐 자랑할 지어다-
「보라 나의 조선은 적은 것이 아니러니라」고!

아아 내 조선아!＜112＞
