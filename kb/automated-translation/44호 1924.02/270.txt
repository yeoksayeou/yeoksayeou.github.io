발행일	1924년 02월 01일
기사제목	거츠른 터 (A Ruined Site)
필자	김동인
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Short Story)

거츠른 터
						김동인
Thus, the death of Yŏngae, which caused such a stir at one time, is now no longer spoken of by anyone. When the corpse of Yŏngae, who had hanged herself, was first discovered, there was quite a commotion among us.
“She followed her husband.”
To us, who considered it almost dishonorable to devote love to only one person (whether man or woman), Yŏngae's death was truly unexpected, and it would not be an exaggeration to call it a miracle.
“Yŏngae died.”
“She followed her husband.”
“They will live happily ever after in the next world.”
Among us, various mocking comments arose.
But truly, did Yŏngae commit suicide because she could not bear missing her husband? Of course, considering the place of her death was in some village in Kyŏngju, the house where her husband grew up, amidst a completely ruined site, and that she hanged herself with her deceased husband’s belt, which she had never taken off her waist even for a moment during that time, interpreting it this way might seem natural. Furthermore, seeing how Yŏngae adored her deceased husband, we had no choice but to interpret it this way. Not only that, but the day Yŏngae died was exactly four years to the day after her husband’s death, so if we had interpreted it differently, that would have been rather strange.
“She followed her husband.”
We believed this without a doubt.<186>
However, according to a note I recently discovered, which could well be called Yŏngae’s last testament, there was a tragedy behind her death. Of course, that tragedy also arose from her excessive devotion to her husband, but it was truly a cruel tragedy.

What was the immense veil that covered the death of the pitiful Yŏngae, a flower that bloomed in the shade? Here, I intend to reveal all of Yŏngae’s final writings and uncover all the secrets of her death, which once startled us.

Yŏngae’s Writing
I married him four years ago, the year I turned sixteen. Having no parents, siblings, or relatives, I still managed to graduate from girls’ high school thanks to some kind person. And just when I was hesitating, planning to apply for a position as a female clerk at some company to make a living, I ended up marrying him through Mr. K’s arrangement. Lacking any particularly dear relatives, I had no lingering attachments—moreover, it was a time when I faced the great, difficult problem of self-support. Without a second thought, I married him.
I had seen many men called “teacher.” I had also seen several men called “friend’s older brother.” Looking back at the hazy memories of my childhood years, the man called “father” still remains in a corner of my mind. But a man called “husband”? With such curiosity filling the inexperienced young head of a sixteen-year-old fresh out of girls’ high school, I got married. However, it was nothing special. He was like a father, an older brother, or perhaps just a very close friend, that was all.
He was twenty-four years old then, in his prime. He was said to be the son of a former *yangban* from somewhere in Kyŏngju; he had no parents or relatives, only a half-brother working as a clerk at some company in Pusan (with whom he didn’t even exchange letters).<187> He was, like me, a lonely person. The only difference from me was that he possessed abundant wealth.
I had known him for several years. When I was attending school, whenever we held concerts or lectures, I would go to the K Printing Company (K印刷會社) to print programs or admission tickets. There, sitting in the plate-making room with his sleeves rolled up, always scratching something with a knife, was Technician S, who was him. Therefore, when Mr. K told me that S had considerable wealth, I was surprised.
“He will become my husband.”
Before marrying him, I sometimes thought about this, but it seemed rather laughable. The gap between the Technician, hunched over and scratching zinc scraps in the plate-making room, and the name “Yŏngae’s husband” was too wide. Furthermore, when the descriptor “rich man” was added, it seemed like an unbelievably strange thing. However, once I actually got married, it turned out to be nothing. Surrounded by several friends at the wedding hall, I went to his house, where a few friends had already arrived first and were waiting (in a house without its master). After glancing around at them once with a smile, he leaned close to my ear and whispered like this—
“I’ll be in that room—if anyone asks, just say you don’t know.”
Surprised by his attitude, treating me like a wife of ten years already, I stared at him with wide eyes, and while I was doing so, he strode off heavily to the other side.
I realized a sense of pleasure and displeasure. A light pleasure towards him for entrusting me with the rights of a housewife already, and a displeasure bordering on dislike for his attitude, which seemed completely oblivious to the (dreamlike, joyous) concept of a honeymoon.
The days passed.
The considerable shyness I felt at first gradually disappeared. Simultaneously, I grew closer to him.<188> I respected him like a father and loved him like an older brother. His every action was, (abstractly) to me, the right thing. Every word he spoke was admirable.
Even to me, who was so infatuated, he had two habits I couldn't think well of.
First was his endless drinking.
What class of people his friends were, I couldn't understand at first. It was a hot evening not long after we got married. While I was preparing dinner in the kitchen with the maid, I heard the sound of the sliding door in his living room rattling open. Thinking he had already returned, I quickly went to that room, saying, “It’s hot, isn’t it?” as I entered.
“Yes, it’s hot. Has Mr. S not returned yet?”
The person who barged into someone else’s house without permission was a friend of S whom I had briefly seen at the wedding hall. That person plopped down on a chair in the room where even the master wasn't present.
“He came earlier, but went out again.”
Though I answered, I felt shy and embarrassed, and ran out of the room. At night, when I told S this story, he just smiled.
Since his friends were mostly like this person, they had no reservations about anything. They would visit even in the middle of the night and start drinking. What began in the dining room gradually moved into the living room, where they would chat noisily until two or three in the morning usually, and it wasn't infrequent for them to stay up all night.
Another thing was that his life was too irregular. He continued to go to the company as usual, even from the day after the wedding. However, for him, who sometimes slept until noon, one P.M., or even as late as four P.M., the days he went to work and the days he didn’t were almost the same. There were even days when he didn’t get out of bed at all. His irregularity was truly thorough.<189>
It was a Sunday. Around noon, several of his friends came to visit, but having stayed up all night the previous night, he was still sleeping. I went up to the second-floor bedroom, shook him awake, and informed him that his friends had arrived. He blinked his eyes open but turned over and fell asleep again. Standing there waiting helplessly for a moment, I woke him again—
“Guests have arrived.”
“Hm? Who?”
“The usual ones who always come.”
“Tell them to sit and wait, uh—bring out some drinks too. Eh, I’m sleepy.”
That was all; he closed his eyes again. Going downstairs, after serving drinks to the guests, I continued the sewing I had been doing earlier. Thus absorbed in sewing, I sat for about an hour, then went back to the dining room to check. He still hadn't come down, and only the guests were drinking amongst themselves. I quietly went up to the bedroom, opened the door, and looked inside; he wasn't in the bedroom, having gotten up sometime earlier. Thinking he might be in the bathroom, I went there and knocked, but he wasn’t there either. Finding it strange, I searched room after room, but he wasn't in any of them. Finally, I went to his laboratory, wondering if he might be there. When I knocked, after a moment, there was a clicking sound of a lock opening, and only his head poked out the door.
“What is it?”
He looked at me with sharp eyes.
“Guests have arrived.”
When I replied thus, he stared blankly at my face for a moment, then said,
“Well, I know too.”
And closed the door again, locking it.<190>
I jumped back startled, as if I had committed a grave sin. He was someone who absolutely forbade anyone from coming near while he was in his laboratory. Since coming to him, I had always observed this. But that day, reassured that he couldn't possibly be in this room, I had done such a clumsy thing and angered him. To me, nothing was as frightening as him being angry. He was someone who didn't get angry over minor matters.
I dragged my feet listlessly down the connecting bridge. At that moment, one of his friends sought me out—
“Where is Mr. S?”
“He seems to be still sleeping.”
“He’s not in the bedroom.”
“Is that so?”
That was all I said, and returned to my room.
Only after about half an hour did he finally come down and sit among his friends. They were already quite drunk and initially didn't seem to notice his arrival. A little later, the loud voice of a guest named Mr. T was heard—
“Hey—S, when did you arrive?”
“What? I’m in my own house, where would I arrive from?”
“I mean—where—have you been—until now?”
“I was at home.”
“Which room?”
“In the laboratory.”
“Guests are here, and you don’t come down, just minding your own business, where is there such rudeness?”
“It’s probably better than the rudeness of drinking and carrying on in a room without the host…”
“Rudeness? Hahahaha, have a drink.”<191>
Why was it that he, who lived such an irregular life, almost qualifying as a “delinquent youth” (不良靑年), and sometimes disregarded even his friends, was respected by them, and looked down upon even by the company president, yet was cherished by the company, and after his death, all who knew him mourned his passing?

There was no specific time when he entered the laboratory. It wasn't unheard of for him to jump out of bed and go in. Even while dressing to go to work, if something occurred to him, he would dash right in. And while he was inside, whether it was dinnertime or a close friend arrived, he wouldn't know, nor allow himself to be informed.
However, I didn't know (until he was gone) what he was doing. I only vaguely guessed from the eulogy read by his friend Y at his funeral…
Not long after I came to him, one day during dinner, I asked him what it was. At that time, he stared blankly at me and said this—
“I don’t interfere with your sewing or household chores. When you are cooking or sewing, if I stood watching and interfering, wouldn’t you find it annoying? Just walk your own path. Someday, there will be a time when you understand…”

One moonlit night, on the porch, he lit a cigarette and recounted various interesting experiences. He wasn't one to talk much about his own experiences, but once started on some occasion, there was no end to it. At the same time, he was someone who got extremely annoyed if asked questions while telling his story, even if they were very simple (answerable with a single word). Although I knew this habit well, when his story touched upon his research, I inadvertently asked again what his research was about. This time too, he threw down his cigarette and went back into the room.<192>
Around the end of the twelfth lunar month, nearing the year's end, as we were eating dinner, and afterwards in the living room, he was reading the newspaper and I was knitting his overshirt, a guest arrived. This guest was a friend from his hometown village, visiting for the first time since our marriage. Not knowing how to offer special greetings, they began their conversation like this from the start—
“What brings you up here?”
“What for? I heard you got married, so I came to congratulate you.”
“It’s been over half a year since the wedding, what congratulations are there now?”
“Anyway, congratulations.”
The guest said this and laughed.
“Did you bring a present?”
“Instead of a present, I’ll stay at your house for three or four days.”
“At our house?”
“Is it bothersome?”
“It’s troublesome.”
“Anyway, I’ll impose. By the way, how is that work you were doing coming along?”
Their conversation, which started as a joke, reached his research. I pricked up my ears.
“The work? Hmm, I succeeded once,—but I threw it away.”
“Why?”
“Because it still didn’t turn out as I wished… Compared to the current method, time-wise, it can be reduced to 1/20th—or as you’d say, 1/12th of an hour, but the cost would be about the same as the current one…”
“That’s great! Congratulations again. No matter the cost, if time is saved, doesn't that ultimately amount to reducing the cost by 1/12th<193> too?”
“Don’t joke. Rather, time is the secondary issue; first, the cost must be reduced.”
“What does that matter…”
“What does that matter? The current world is one where money can buy time.”
“You’re the one joking.”
“Joking? In fact, I too sell 240 hours a month to K Printing Company for 100 won. That’s about 40 *jŏn* per hour…”
“Isn’t that—in other words, borrowing?”
“Borrowing? From whom? If I give K Printing 100 won, will they give me back 240 hours of my lifespan?”
“Eh, annoying, it’s pawning, pawning it is!”
“Pawning? Since you’re a habitual offender (常習者), is pawning all you know?”
Hearing this, the guest glanced at me.
“Well, when I was in Tokyo before, I did that, but…”
“Don’t you still? Last year when you came up, Meiji-chō…”
“Annoying!”
The guest laughed loudly and stood up abruptly.
“Sit down, sit down! Anyway, I’m a mammonist (拜金宗), so to satisfy someone like me, both cost and time must be reduced.”
“Is it regression?”
“Against what?”
The guest lit a cigarette without answering.<194>
“Do you know this story? Long ago, there were two enemies. Then—wait, a trial… hold on, I forgot it all. Anyway, to put it simply, for some reason—they had a trial, and in the verdict… A’s request would be granted, and B would be granted double A’s request, it was said. At that time, if A had requested tens or hundreds of millions of won for himself, wouldn't that have been great? But, thinking about it, his enemy B would get double; if he requested ten million won, B would get twenty million. So, finally, A asked to have one of his eyeballs plucked out. Thus, A became one-eyed, and B became blind. Even if they are in the same situation, or even a worse one, people in better situations are relentlessly dragged down to the same level—it’s envy, envy.”
“Envy, everyone has it, don’t they?”
“That’s right, someone like your country wife is the ultimate authority on that, right?”
“There he goes again. Anyway, you talk like that because you’re in a comfortable position now; try being propertyless (無産者).”
“If I were, I wouldn’t hesitate for a second; I’d jump straight to communism.”
“Are you an egoist?”
“Of course, I’m an egoist. More like, (speaking in Japanese) a ごつがふ主義者 (gotsugō shugisha - opportunist/convenience-ist), perhaps. Labor problems and whatnot, isn’t it all gotsugō shugi? Why don't labor problems arise in America, the world's foremost labor nation? It ends with the single phrase that the country treats its workers well. Look at you too; why did you come to me instead of staying at an inn?”
“Hahaha. Gotsugō shugi. If I stay at your house, I don’t incur inn expenses…”
“Also…”<195>
“Also?”
“Well, also, there’s good liquor…”
“That’s right. You have better liquor than any restaurant (レストラン).”
“See? Isn’t that also thanks to money? Isn’t it immensely pleasant to use one’s own wealth whenever one wants, for whatever one wants? Do you know?”
The guest, perhaps finding it annoying, dropped the subject—
“I lose, I lose, I concede…”
“Concede? Then, you mean you weren’t defeated?”
“Lost, lost… after hearing the sound of liquor, nothing else enters my ears. Bring it out.”
“There’s none. I’ll only bring it out if you completely surrender.”
“Looks like you don’t have any good liquor.”
“Fool.”
“Well then, bring it out.”
“仕樣の無い男た (Shiyō no nai otoko da - What a helpless man). Anyway, money is…”
“Annoying, I told you I lost.”
They finally went to the dining room.
After taking a bath, I went back to the living room, picked up my needle and thread, and was about to start working when suddenly, from the dining room, I heard my name being called.
“Yŏngae? Yŏngae? It’s a good name.”
The guest said this.
“It’s not just a good name. イヤシクモ (Iyashikumo - At the very least/If nothing else) isn’t she S’s wife?”<196>
“What does Miss Yŏngae think about the work you do?”
“Think… She doesn’t even know what I’m doing.”
“That doesn’t make sense. Now…”
“Because I don’t tell her…”
“Why is that?”
“Why? Because even if I told her, it would be useless…”
“You’re too cruel.”
“Cruel? Wouldn’t it be crueler to tell her?”
“Why?”
“Do you know well the heart of a woman?”
“I know, don’t I?”
“You know? Listen carefully. The animal called woman, you see, can hate or resent others without the slightest prick of conscience.”
“But?”
“But, as Natsume also said, there is no time when they feel more vexed than when their lack of learning, or rather their ignorance, is discovered by others.”
“So!”
“So!”
He said only this, then abruptly stopped talking and seemed to pick up his cup. However, even after waiting a bit, no further words came. Thinking he realized I was here, I was about to quietly descend and leave when the guest’s words followed—<197>
“So?”
“So… So, you see… wait, what was I talking about?”
“Hm? You were, no, you were talking about why you didn’t tell Miss Yŏngae about the work you are doing.”
“Ah! So, wait, it’s not ‘so,’ but rather, do you know women well?”
“I know, don’t I? Wait, we talked about this earlier.”
“Did we? Then, women hating others or… I think we talked about this too.”
“We did.”
“Yes, I remember. Anyway, with the knowledge of someone who just graduated from Girls’ High School, no matter how clearly I explain the work I’m doing, won’t she fail to grasp more than half of it?”
“Hmm.”
“I would rather endure her resentment than make her feel ashamed.”
Ah, why didn’t I run to him then, throw myself into his arms? And why didn’t I say this—
“Even if you don’t tell me, I will never resent or hate you. Why? Because I realize it’s beyond my authority as a housewife. And even if you told me and I couldn’t understand, I would never think it shameful. I know well that you are far superior to me.”

Nine months of married life with him. During that time, his every action flickers before my eyes; even his smallest gestures remain as precious, tearful lumps that can never return. But what’s the use of recording it all? It amounts to nothing more than resentment and reproach.<198>
After the long winter passed, spring arrived, when all things seemed to revive with warm affection. Simultaneously, the whole world was threatened by the fearsome fire of a contagious disease.
“These days, I want to rest comfortably for a few days. But I can’t just lie down without being sick… I wish I could catch this disease that everyone else is getting.”
He said things like this a couple of times.
One day, as he was about to go to the company, he suddenly sat down heavily.
“From my nose, no, rather, from my whole head, the smell of cigarettes is coming out, I can’t stand it.”
He said this, shaking his head as if disgusted. He skipped work that day.
The next day, around six in the morning, he abruptly got out of bed and went out in his pajamas. I asked if he was going to the bathroom, but seeing him shake his head, I thought he must have suddenly remembered something and gone to the laboratory. I also got up, went downstairs, woke the maid (though it was a bit early), and started preparing breakfast. After about 30 minutes, suddenly, the bell rang sharply. Looking, I saw it was ringing from the laboratory. He, who disliked others coming to the laboratory, had never rung the bell from there. So, thinking perhaps his clothes had brushed against it, I waited, but the bell rang again.
“Jjireuk, jjireuk, jjireuk.”
It was a strange sound, as if a two or three-year-old child was pressing it. With a strange, fearful premonition, I ran up to the laboratory in one stride and tried to open the door, but it was locked from the inside as usual. And from inside, I could hear sounds like his groans.
“What’s wrong? Please open the door.”
“Hospital, doctor! Quick, doctor!”
His voice was thin, like a mosquito’s buzz.<199>
“Well, what’s wrong?”
“Call a doctor.”
He finally got angry. I ran downstairs, told the maid to fetch Dr. H, and then went back to the laboratory. He told me to break the door down. So, picking up a nearby folding chair, I struck the panel five or six times, and the panel fell out. Reaching my hand through the gap, I opened the door and entered. He was curled up tightly under the bell, and the windows, despite the cold, were all wide open.
“Why, what’s wrong?”
“To the—bed—room.”
I carried him on my shoulders to the bedroom and laid him down. Once in the bedroom, he seemed to fall soundly asleep.
A little later, Dr. H arrived. Dr. H examined the sleeping man for a while, poking here and there, then frowned and raised his head.
“Are there any arsenic compounds (砒化物) or cyanide compounds (靑化物) in Mr. S’s laboratory?”
“Well, I don’t know.”
I replied, thinking the names sounded familiar from chemistry class in my school days.
“It seems like poisoning (中毒).”
“Poisoning? Uh, from what…”
“Well, it seems like poison gas (毒깨쓰) poisoning.”
“Poison gas (독와사)?”
As I looked at Dr. H in surprise, he, who had been asleep, stirred slightly. His eyes fluttered, as if trying hard to wake up, to regain consciousness.<200>
“How are you?”
Dr. H asked him.
“Camphor (칼플).”
He barely managed to mumble this. Dr. H prepared an injection and administered it. He trembled violently in his arms and legs, then opened his eyes weakly.
“Another—shot.”
He mumbled and closed his eyes again.
After receiving another injection, he weakly opened his eyes again, smiled faintly at Dr. H, then turned his head towards me—
“Please go to the laboratory. That shelf, the second shelf from the bottom, at the south end.”
Barely managing to say this much, he closed his eyes again. However, his words continued, like a mosquito’s buzz—
“The bottle labeled Potassium—Potassium Thiocyanate (硫-硫靑酸 포태슘), please…”
Having come this far, he seemed to lose strength and stopped speaking. I ran to the laboratory, brought the bottle, and shook him. He opened his eyes wide, looked alternately at my face and the medicine bottle, then asked—
“Is the, stopper inserted?”
“No. It was lying down, so I inserted it.”
As I replied thus, he closed his eyes again and remained still for a while, then suddenly opened his eyes wide again. His eyes were full of vitality.
“Dr. H. It’s hydrogen cyanide (靑化水素) poisoning.”
“Hydrogen cyanide? Are you sure it’s that?”
“How about an ether (에-텔) injection?”<201>
“If it’s cyanide (靑酸) poisoning,—wait, I’ll call the pharmacy and come back.”
Dr. H ran downstairs, made a phone call, and returned.
The circumstances under which he inhaled the hydrogen cyanide were as follows. Waking early, he had a sudden idea and rushed into the laboratory. However, because the smell of cigarettes was too strong from his nose—no, his whole head—he started the experiment while boiling water to humidify the dry room. Thus engrossed in the experiment for a while, he felt a little dizzy. Simultaneously, a terrifying thought entered his mind, and gathering all his nerves into his nose (numbed by the strange smell similar to cigarettes), he tried to smell. His nose detected a slight scent of hydrogen cyanide. He hurriedly ran and threw open the windows, but it was already too late. He collapsed right there. However, somehow, he managed to crawl to the bell and, with his fingertips barely reaching where his arm couldn't, pressed the bell.
“The potassium thiocyanate must have decomposed due to moisture, right?”
As Dr. H said this, he began to tremble violently.
“Injection. Quick, ether (애-텔)!”
Dr. H hurried downstairs again to fetch the ether. Unable to bear it, I followed Dr. H downstairs, held onto him, and asked about his condition. He stood blankly for a moment before answering—
“To speak frankly, he is in critical condition. Eight or nine times out of ten…” I don’t know what happened after that. When I regained consciousness, dripping sweat from my head and chest, I was lying on the chaise lounge in the living room, and the maid was stroking my head, trembling. I abruptly sat up, pushed away the maid’s restraining hands, and ran back to his sickroom. He had already received the injection and fallen peacefully asleep.
Of course, I knew his condition was critical. But I hadn't imagined that the shadow of death had drawn so close to him, my husband, who was loved and respected by his many friends.<202>
His friends also came, summoned by telephone. It was truly a spectacle to see them—who seemed utterly ignorant of all etiquette or morality, who only knew how to drink and then charge recklessly without regard for others—gathered around a friend’s sickbed with pious faces, like elders kneeling before a sacred altar.
Around eleven o’clock, he woke up. But by this time, perhaps having resigned himself to everything, his expression was relatively calm.
“Haven’t K or Y arrived?”
These were the first words he asked upon waking.
“They are here.”
Mr. K came forward, took his hand, and said this. He glanced at Mr. K out of the corner of his eye and smiled faintly.
“Are you the only one who came?”
“Everyone—came.”
“Everyone, come closer! Let me see your faces one last time.”
“You rascal!”
Before his words finished, Mr. K shouted like thunder.
“Why such nonsense! You rascal.—If you die, if you die, we’ll—we’ll be desolate.”
Mr. K’s eyes were also filled with tears. Unable to bear it, I ran into the adjacent room.
However, not long after, I was called back into that room.
“Hey, S. Do you have any last words?”
“None.”<203>
“Like a tombstone inscription…”
“None. Just carve my name, and below it…”
He said only this much and stopped. His eyes were fixed straight at the ceiling. However, he was definitely not looking at the ceiling.
Five or six minutes passed. In the dim room, where all the curtains had been drawn according to his instructions, only the electric light shone brightly.
“Wh-whoo, wh-whoo.”
Finally, the person who broke the silence was him. Mr. K immediately asked—
“What?”
“Wh-whoo, wh-whoo.”
His left hand, lying outside the blanket, trembled terribly, as if beckoning someone. I ran over, pushed Mr. K aside, and placed my hand in his. He weakly squeezed my right hand, then began struggling to pull his right hand out from under the blanket. When I pulled it out for him, he fumbled around and finally found my hand held in his left, and wrote some characters on its palm.—Water.
“Water?”
I barely managed to make a sound. He pulled my hand impatiently, as if annoyed, and wrote again. —Light.
His face indicated agreement. However, unsure whether he meant to turn off the light or something else, I hesitated. Then, Dr. H approached,
“You mean you can’t see the light?”
He said, touching his forehead. His face indicated agreement. Dr. H put his mouth to my ear and<204> whispered thus—
“It is the end (臨終). Shall we try a camphor (캄플) shot?”
Ah, but did I truly have the courage to answer then?

Two days later, his body, after a ceremony held in the house garden, was taken to the public cemetery. At that ceremony, through Mr. Y’s eulogy, I learned for the first time what he had been doing. Mr. Y’s eulogy (吊文) was as follows:

S—
That you are dead is truly hard for us to believe. Even though your corpse lies solemnly before our eyes, we still find it difficult to believe you are dead. You, dead?
But the reality is perhaps inescapable. From among our few friends, you have suddenly vanished. Some parts of society that saw you as a delinquent youth (不良靑年) might even rejoice at your death. Or, society that only saw the irregular side of your life might even call your death the self-destruction of a degenerate. But you must not forget that all of society that knew you mourns your death as a misfortune for Chosŏn.
You entered XX Middle School at the age of twelve, didn’t you? That was when I first met you. When I look back at that time, I always see the boy genius standing at the blackboard with chalk, solving mathematics problems.
After that, crossing over to Tokyo, when I entered the Literature Department of OO School, you entered the Science Department, and we drifted apart somewhat. Your irregular life had already begun then. Ignoring your studies, you went to moving pictures to glimpse Western customs, and at night, you wandered around drinking… We watched with surprised eyes as you, who were like that, graduated without incident.<205>
And then, when you returned to Korea and settled into a salaried position at K Printing Company, we were surprised once again. We never imagined that someone like you would become a company employee, obligated to follow fixed rules. However, not long after, when we learned that your becoming a technician at K Printing Company was something you had planned beforehand, how great do you think our surprise was? We never thought that someone like you possessed plans or goals.
You saw the imperfection of current printing technology. While a typewriter could print a page (without typos) in a few seconds, you saw that current printing methods, including proofreading, took several days just to finish the typesetting. Here, your plan emerged, didn't it? Utilizing the principles of zinc relief plates (亞鉛凸板) and the typewriter, you aimed to create a special typewriter to type with special ink onto a zinc plate coated with bichromated colloid (重크롬酸膠), expose it to light, then etch it with nitric acid (硝酸) to create something like the current printing “stereotype plate” (紙型), and you succeeded. However, although it was faster than current printing methods in terms of time, the cost per page was by no means less than the current method. This did not suit you, a monetary hedonist (金錢享樂者). So? You then tried to apply cardboard (마분지). Not being a scientist, I don’t know the exact details, but everything proceeded as planned.
“If I go into the laboratory just one more time, it will be finished, but for a sluggish person like me, going in once is like a major task. It’s all resolved in my head…”
That was what you said five days ago. However, you, who “went in one more time,” met death inside there.
S—
How frustrating. But the great power called Fate (宿命) is undeniable. You, who were laughing and cavorting with us just 4 or 5 days ago, have already disappeared somewhere.
“One of us dies.”<206>
This was almost unimaginable. We, eternally ourselves, arrogant yet without pretense, living irregular lives yet treading a path of genuineness (眞純)—was it not rather laughable that one of us should die?
But, S—
You simply died. You vanished from among us. We might gather again tonight, drink, and raise hell. But you, who enjoyed and laughed with us, where will you be and what will you be doing then? S! Come! Come once more, eat, laugh, and enjoy with us. Show us once more that face of yours, large like a cow’s.
Ah, but what use is saying such things? It all amounts to nothing but empty words.
If humans possess an “undying spirit” (주지안는 넉), and if there is a day in the afterlife when we can meet you, then, then, let’s roughhouse to our heart’s content, to the fullest. Let’s eat with loud faces as if ignoring the existence of others, get drunk and brawl, and eat again.
April 17, 19XX
Before the corpse of S

After the funeral ended, for me, the whole world felt as if it had vanished before my eyes. Doing today what I did yesterday, and having to do the same things again tomorrow—life was an indescribably pitiful thing. Living for 50 or 100 years this monotonous, unchanging “life” was truly something that made me shudder just thinking about it.

Not long after the funeral, one day, unable to fall asleep no matter how hard I tried, I got out of bed,<207> sat down at the dressing table, and stared at my gaunt face reflected in the mirror, lost in various thoughts, oblivious to the passing time. Suddenly, his face, looking at me from behind, was reflected in the mirror. Goosebumps prickled all over my body, and when I turned around, nobody was there. But when I looked back at the mirror, he was still there, looking over my shoulder at me.
Finally, I realized it was his portrait hanging on the wall. The next day, that picture was moved down to the basement. At the same time, all his daily necessities (what remained after his friends took some as mementos) were also moved to the basement.
However, the feeling of suffocation and loneliness was unbearable. The loneliness that attacked my heart in the dead of night was perhaps tolerable, but the thoughts of him that arose whenever I ate or read the newspaper were unbearable. Letters that occasionally arrived, unaware of his absence, were all burned.
Finally, I decided to invite two poor friends from my old school days to stay at the house. Thus, Yujin and O came to live at my house.

As the days passed, loneliness and sorrow gradually faded from my heart. I thought of the two friends as my own older sisters. Having lost him, whom I had thought of as a father and an older brother, I gained sisters in their place.
Thus, summer and autumn passed, winter went by, and when spring arrived again, the memory of S had almost vanished from my mind. Laughter always filled the house. In the household of three women—a maiden and two young wives—there were no restraints or worries. As long as money was paid, fire came automatically, water came automatically; it was a world where one could travel while sitting still. The abundant wealth he left allowed us to live without any worries. The person named S was forgotten by the world one year after his death, forgotten by his friends, and erased<208> even from my mind, his former wife. When I visited his grave, I would think of his ill-fated and pitiful life and shed a few tears, but on the way back, I would buy snacks to enjoy happily with my friends at home.

Spring passed and came again. Another year of a life that could almost be called indulgent (放縱) passed. That spring, when the cherry blossoms at the botanical garden bloomed and fell, O got married. Since O was like an older sister who had lived with me at home, I prepared everything for her so she wouldn't be embarrassed before others.
Her wedding ceremony was held at the Youth Hall (청년회관). The wedding ceremony ended without incident, a reception was held at the Myŏngwŏlgwan Branch (明月舘 支店), and in the evening, Yujin and I returned home together. However, suddenly, an endless loneliness arose in my heart, which was unbearable.
Lying in bed that night, for some reason, I couldn't fall asleep. The clock struck half-past two, then three.
But I couldn't close my eyes. Yujin, sleeping in the next room, also seemed unable to sleep, as only the sound of her coughing echoed occasionally. I guessed the feelings of the maiden Yujin, left alone among the three women who had lived together in the house.
Finally, I got dressed and went to her room; she was still dressed, not having taken off her clothes. Though we met, we sat there stiffly, having nothing to say to each other. However, a little later, Yujin broke the silence.—
“O must be sound asleep now, right?”
“She must be oblivious to the world.”
Though I replied thus, remembering myself on my first night, unable to sleep a wink due to excitement, I collapsed right there. Tears flowed endlessly from my eyes.
After crying for a while, I sat up and looked; Yujin, perhaps having cried thinking of her own lonely situation, had red, swollen eyes.<209>
The next day, his photos and portraits, which had been stored in the basement, were all decorated again in my bedroom. That night, looking at the portrait through the mirror, he seemed to be standing right behind me, looking at me. Reflected in the mirror, his portrait was alive. I felt his breath on my back. Reflected in the mirror, he even blinked his eyes.
For the first time, I realized my affection for him as a man.
From then on, I began to spend most of my time in front of the mirror. O, thinking I had suddenly started to doll myself up, sometimes laughed strangely.
The longing for the absent him grew hotter in my heart with each passing day. He, whom I had forgotten as a father or older brother, was resurrected in my heart as a husband, as a man. I felt resentful and deeply pained that I hadn't offered him warm affection as a wife while he was alive.
Man—there was no word more beautiful or longed-for. But I had lost him just before I came to know him as a man. “Childlike.” This word, common with “innocent,” is considered good, but because of it, I lost him before I could offer him love as his wife.
His past every action, once forgotten, was resurrected in my mind.
He once said something like this: If you write the name of a country in Chinese characters, the shape of the characters represents the national character (if such a word exists). The character 英 (Yŏng - England) is beautiful and dignified; the character 獨逸 (Togil - Germany) looks blunt and diligent; the character 露 (Ro - Russia) or 俄 (A - Russia) is broad and large; the character 日本 (Ilbon - Japan) is narrow and nimble; the character 米 (Mi - America) is coarse and large – in the same sense, the characters 新羅 (Silla) represent greatness, a giant-like quality that "doesn't even glance at small things," and simultaneously vastness and breadth. However, if the characters "新羅" could be personified, they would be none other than S himself. Even in his most capricious or violent moments, his personality manifested within them.<210>
I gradually became melancholic. And I preferred night over day. The reflection of his portrait in the mirror at night seemed exactly like a living person, looking back at me over my shoulder. When I stared intently, lost in thought, he sometimes even smiled.
Born in Kyŏngju, he had lived in Seoul since the age of twelve. However, he still retained a strong Kyŏngsang-do accent. To me, thinking of and longing for the absent him, even that accent, which previously seemed rustic, was resurrected beautifully and affectionately in my mind. Occasionally, when I heard the strong accent on the street, I would turn around unexpectedly.
Yujin finally understood my inner feelings too. She tried her best to cheer me up constantly. However, all of it seemed merely annoying and tiresome to me.
Is this what it means to "awaken to sexuality" (性에 눈뜬다)? In the dead of night, sitting facing the mirror, waiting for a smile from his reflected face, the great waves surging within my heart were unbearable.
Why was he gone? He, who loved me dearly as a wife, was gone before receiving love from me as a wife. He would never know the warmth of family love, would he? Research? Whether successful or not, it didn't matter. I wanted, just once more, with a true heart, to bury my head in his broad chest.

One day, while rummaging through a closet, I found his old pajama belt, tied around a bundle of old newspapers. I snatched it untied and tried tying it around my waist. Simultaneously, I thought of the S I had heard about, the child with his hair braided down his back.
Born in Kyŏngju, he simultaneously disliked Kyŏngju as his hometown and admired it. He spoke of Kyŏngju as possessing a wide, ineffable, leisurely, and pleasant ancient atmosphere, like a spring day's nap, preserved intact. How many times had I heard this from him?<211>
“It’s indescribable. Still, if forced to put it into words, perhaps it's like a kite soaring in the high sky on a spring afternoon—a place with that kind of feeling? A pleasant, *nonbiri* (ノンビリ - carefree, relaxed) feeling overflows, spills, flows—maybe described like that.”

He always talked about Kyŏngju like this. And he said that if Kyŏngju hadn't been his hometown, he would have lived there long ago—he cherished Kyŏngju that much.
Finally, I decided to visit Kyŏngju, his hometown, where his old house still remained. By then, summer and autumn had passed, and winter was near. That strong Kyŏngsang-do accent—I heard it from the moment I got off the train in Taegu, from the inn servant boy, and from the rickshaw puller.
I took a car. Around two in the afternoon, the car left for Kyŏngju. When Kyŏngju was about halfway, indeed, the Kyŏngju atmosphere he had described overflowed abundantly. In the distance, vast fields and paddies stretched between the mountains on the opposite and both sides, with villages dotted here and there—an extremely ordinary scene, yet the arrangement, combined with the large mountain-like tumuli such as Ponghwangdae, truly offered the leisure of a spring nap and the pleasure of stretching. The car running on the road between them belonged to the present, but it made the rider's heart feel like walking along an ancient, leisurely path, leaning on a staff. Though Silla had fallen, its atmosphere and grandeur still remained intact in Kyŏngju. But what made my heart leap even more was that this scenery was, in other words, nothing less than the symbol of his personality. If he could be transformed into scenery, it would be the scenery around Kyŏngju; if the scenery around Kyŏngju could be personified, it would be none other than the personality of S himself. My heart leaped as if I had met him here.
That night, I stayed at an inn, and the next day, during breakfast, I asked the elderly proprietress for the house of S *Ch'amp'an* (a high government rank). The house was easy to find. It was a large tiled house located just across a large square paddy field from the inn. The walls were crumbled here and there, and the main house roof tiles were peeled off in places, with moss and weeds growing thickly on top.<212>
I immediately changed my clothes and went to the house. In the gatehouse (haengnang) lived the couple of an old servant from the previous generation who had guarded the masterless house for over ten years. The old woman's surprise when she learned I was the daughter-in-law of this house was almost comically large. But her sorrow upon learning of his death was difficult for me to watch.
A little later, I looked around the house with the old woman. The *sarangchae* (men's quarters) walls had all crumbled, and the building leaned; the former state of the large, luxurious house could not be discerned. The inner gate area and the *anchae* (women's quarters) were the same. We went around to the back garden too. The pavilion collapsed over the pond, the flowerbed overgrown with weeds—everything was in ruins. All the effort of commanding dozens of male and female slaves who held the power of life and death, trying to live a thick life of daytime naps and nighttime song and dance instead—all that effort now lay hidden beneath only the vainly flourishing grass.
However, what finally caught my eye was a small room, seemingly plastered anew with mud even last year, remaining amidst this ruined and crumbling house. I asked the old woman what that room was.
When the S family moved up to Seoul, they entrusted the house to their most faithful servant, leaving behind several useless miscellaneous items. These were mostly S's clothes that had become too small for him. The faithful and kind servant couple gathered them all in one room and awaited the return of the master, whose timing was unknown.
I got the key to that room from the old woman, and barely managed to open the lock, rusted for over ten years, to look inside. The inside was entirely covered in blue-green mold, and in a corner stood a moldy chest of drawers.
Inside it were his childhood clothes.
Unwilling to take them out in front of others, I merely asked the old woman to sweep the room clean, then returned to the inn for lunch.<213>
The jacket and trousers stained with hair oil, the tiny socks—at night, alone, quietly opening his chest, my heart felt ready to burst with joy. Could such a person have had such a young and lovely time? Oblivious to the passing night, I rummaged through them under the dim lamplight.

Finally, I decided to spend the winter in Kyŏngju. I renovated the room completely, and a week later, left the inn and moved into the ruined house where he grew up.

The long winter, filled with tearful joy, passed. I spent every day accompanied by his childhood clothes. Looking outside, there was a new flavor; looking inside, there was also a new flavor; looking outside again, there was yet another new flavor. During the winter, those clothes became blackened by the touch of my hands.
Thus, after spending the winter, when the thick snow began to melt, I had grown so attached to the land that I couldn't leave Kyŏngju. Yujin, who had been keeping the house alone and lonely, was finally brought down. I also renovated the house a bit. That spring, several rooms within the completely ruined house were nevertheless furnished for people to live in. Going to the back garden felt like being in the mountains, overgrown with pathless weeds, but in the inner courtyard, a small flowerbed was built, rotten pillars were painted, his photos brought down from Seoul were decorated in the rooms, making it adequate for the life of two women.
As spring passed, with horses farting as they walked the paths, countless frogs, appearing from nowhere, croaked across the wide fields.
Summer passed too. Autumn, pious as a nun and full of longing as a maiden, was also spent with a true heart. Thus, almost a full year had passed since I came to Kyŏngju last year, when, by some chance, I remembered that his half-brother (庶弟) might still be alive and asked the old woman about him.<214>
The old woman knew well. H (the half-brother's name) was born two months later than the deceased S, stayed in Kyŏngju until age six, then was kicked out of the S family due to some minor sentiment and moved to Pusan. According to news reaching H's maternal relatives still remaining in Kyŏngju, H was now grown up (of course he would be grown up), had married, and was currently earning a living working at △△ Company. However, one point in the old woman's words was hard to dismiss: H looked so much like the deceased S that they could be mistaken for the same person.
“A person who could be mistaken for the deceased S.”
I decided to go to Pusan immediately.
Yujin did not try to stop me. The old woman merely told me to return quickly.

When I finally found his house after asking at △△ Company in Pusan, the short autumn day was almost over.
Similar, identical—could there be another person so alike? Hearing the old woman's words, I expected similarity, but I never thought they would be this identical. I even wondered if I wasn't seeing the deceased S.
The couple showed me great sympathy. Since marrying the deceased S, I had become numb to the conceptual thought that "kindness" was a gift anyone would offer me, unable to appreciate any kindness. Yet, I felt truly sorry and grateful for the kindness of this couple.
Having come planning to stay for three or four days, I couldn't resist their kind urging to stay (and another reason might have been seeing H, whose movements and entire demeanor were exactly the same as the deceased S), saying "tomorrow, tomorrow," I continued staying.
The relationship between the couple was truly good. Rather than husband and wife, it seemed more fitting to call them close friends; there was no restraint or reservation between them. Seeing this, my young heart trembled strangely each time. In my heart, which had passed three years like a nun's, the concept of 'man' was keenly impressed.<215> Thin tears always welled up in my eyes.
Even in the Southern Country (南國), heavy snow, mixed with much moisture, fell like cotton puffs. But I remained in Pusan. They now treated me as one of their own family.
Winter, filled with envy, tears, and longing, passed again. Thus, one day in March, when cats seek warm sunny spots, I suddenly remembered that in five or six days, it would be the fourth anniversary of his death.
On the first and second anniversaries, I visited his grave, but thought of it as nothing more than a walk. On the third anniversary, engrossed in the Kyŏngju life, which could be called ecstatic (恐悅), I couldn't go. If people have spirits, how frustrated must he have been? I absolutely had to go.
Thinking this, my heart felt unable to delay even for a moment. Lying in bed at night, thoughts of the deceased S, the Seoul house neglected for a year and a half, and the pinks (石竹) planted around his grave came to mind, so I finally got up again to write a letter to Yujin.
Going to H's study to get letter paper, I found the study empty; perhaps he was already asleep or hadn't returned yet. I entered and took letter paper from his desk, then wrote to Yujin. Apologizing for the lack of letters, I wrote that I would go to Seoul in two or three days, would send a telegram then, and asked her to meet me in Taegu so we could go together. After finishing the letter, I opened his desk again to find an envelope.
But then, what caught my eye before the envelope was a photograph (샤진). It was a half-length portrait of H. No, rather, it was a photograph that could rightly be called a picture of the deceased S. His mouth, seeming to ask if there was anyone else in the world but him; the bridge of his nose, straight as an arrowhead; his spirited eyes—where was the difference from the deceased S? No matter how much H resembled him, this was rather a photograph of the deceased S.
My heart pounded strangely. The hand holding the photograph trembled, and tears flowed from my eyes.
“Darling!”<216>
Finally, unable to help myself, I clutched the photograph tightly to my chest and collapsed onto the desk.
However, in that instant, I startled abruptly. Someone had lightly placed a hand on my shoulder. Goosebumps prickled all over my body as I turned around; there, standing behind me, was H, his face flushed red from drink.
“To that extent…”
The next day, utterly dispirited, I returned to Kyŏngju. He, whom I had longed for and adored for these past two years, seemed to have fled far away from me.
The devil's temptation. Was that truly the devil's temptation? Even if it were the devil, where could there be such a cruel devil again?
I spent three days in a daze. Though snow still remained on the high mountains, the buzzing croaks of frogs echoed in my ears. Sometimes H's inciting, fiery eyes would appear, then change again into H's (or the deceased S's) childlike laughter. Amidst this, one thought never left my mind: everything was ruined.
However, I finally made up my mind. There could be no greater punishment than death. I resolved to atone to him through death.
Now, while writing this, Yujin sleeps soundly in the next room, oblivious. Even at the moment my life passes, she will sleep on, knowing nothing. And when she wakes in the morning and looks for me, I will be hanging cold under the large acorn tree in the back garden, overgrown with weeds that obscure the path.
Even facing death, I do not fear it in the slightest. Rather, if it can serve as some atonement towards the deceased S, I will gladly take that path. Furthermore, even if it fails to console his spirit, having no other path to take, I have no choice but to go that way. Then, quickly, hurry, before the day breaks, let me achieve my purpose. -The End-<217>

**Summary**

This short story by Kim Tongin, presented as the final writings of a young widow named Yŏngae, recounts her complex relationship with her late husband, S, and the events leading to her suicide. Initially married young, Yŏngae revered her unconventional, intellectual, and wealthy husband like a father figure. After his accidental death from poisoning during a scientific experiment (mistakenly thought by society to be suicide out of grief), Yŏngae initially felt numb but later developed an intense, posthumous romantic and physical longing for him, realizing she never truly loved him as a wife while he lived. This longing drives her to his desolate hometown, Kyŏngju, where she immerses herself in his memory. The climax occurs when she meets his half-brother, H, who bears an uncanny resemblance to S. An ambiguous, emotionally charged moment with H shatters her idealized memory, filling her with guilt and despair, leading her to believe she has betrayed S and must atone through death, planned for the fourth anniversary of S's passing.

From a historical perspective, the story offers glimpses into Korean society in the 1920s under Japanese rule. It portrays the lifestyles of the new, wealthy, educated urban class, influenced by Western and Japanese ideas (modern science, individualism, critiques of traditional marriage, casual use of Japanese terms). S represents a modern intellectual grappling with innovation (his printing technology research) and existential thoughts, contrasting with traditional values. Yŏngae's psychological journey reflects changing perceptions of marriage and female identity, moving from duty to personal desire and despair. The backdrop of Kyŏngju, symbolizing ancient Silla's grandeur juxtaposed with modern decay, adds a layer of nationalistic sentiment and reflection on cultural identity during a period of foreign domination. Historians might find the depiction of social interactions, attitudes towards wealth and modernity, gender roles, and the psychological impact of rapid change valuable.

장례를 끗내인 뒤의 나의게는, 모든 세상이, 늘 아페서 꺼뎌 업서진 것 갓햇다, 어제 한 일을, 오늘 또 거퍼하고, 래일도 또한 그런일을 하여야하는 인생이란, 엇더타 형용할 수 업는 가련한 것이엇섯다. 단됴하고 아모 변화가 업는 「인생」이란 것을, 50년이나 백년을 사러 간다는 것은, 참으로 생각만 하여도 진저리 나는 일이엇섯다.

장례 지난 뒤 얼마 되지 아니하여, 엇던 날, 아모리 잠을 자려하여도 조름이 안옴으로, 침대에서 나러서,＜207＞ 화장대 아페가 거러 안저서, 거울에 비추인 내 여윈 얼굴을 드려다 보며 이런 생각 뎌런 생각 시간가는 줄을 모를 때에, 문득, 내 뒤에서 나를, 드려다 보는, 그의 얼굴이, 거울 속에 비추엿다, 나는, 왼 몸에 소름이 쪽 기치며, 도라 보면, 거기는, 아모도 업섯다. 그러나, 다시 거울을 볼 때에, 그 속에는, 여전히 내 억개를 넘어 나믈 드려다 보는 그가 잇섯다.
마츰내, 그것은, 담벽에 걸린 그의 초상인 것을 아럿다. 이튼날, 그 그림은, 지하실로 나려다 두엇다. 동시에, 그의 모든 일용품은, (그의 벗들이 긔념으로 가저 가고 남은 것)전부, 디하실로 갓다 두엇다.
그러나, 갑갑하고, 외로운 것은, 엇지할 수가 업섯다. 고요한 밤즁에, 마음을 습격하는 외로움은, 오히려 참을 수가 잇서스나, 음식 먹을 때, 신문 볼 때마다 생각나는 그의 생각은, 참을 수가 업섯다, 간간 아직 그의 업슴을 모르고 오는 편지는, 모도 불로 태워버리고 하엿다.
마츰내, 나는, 이전 학교시대에, 가티 공부하던 벗 가운데서, 가난한 사람 둘을, 집에 쳥하여 두기로 하엿다, 이리하여, 유진이와 O 두 사람이, 우리 집에 와 잇게 되엿다.

날이 지나자, 외로움과 슬픔도, 차차 마음 속에서 업서젓다. 나는 두 벗을, 친 언니와 가티 생각하엿다. 아부지와 가티 생각하고, 옵바와 가티 생각하던 그를 일허 버린 내게는, 그 대신, 언니가 생겻다.
이리하여, 녀름과 가을이 가고, 겨울이 지나서 다시 봄이 니른 때는, 내 머리에서는, S라는 긔억이 거반 업서졋다. 집안에서는, 늘 우슴 소리가 낫다. 처녀와 새악시의 세 여편네 살림에는, 아모 구속이나 마음 둘 일이 업섯다. 돈만 내이면, 불이 저절로 오고 물이 저절로 오고, 안저서 려행을 하는 세샹인지라, 그의 남긴 풍부한 재산은, 우리로 하여금, 아모 걱정 업시 사러 가게 하엿다. S라 하는 사람은, 죽은 뒤 1년에는, 세샹의게 니저 버리우고, 벗들의게 니저 버리우고, 그의 안해이던＜208＞ 내 머리에서도, 지워뎌 버렷다, 그의 무덤에 가서는, 그의 박명하고 애처러운 일생을 생각하고, 몃 방울의 눈물을 흘리지만, 도라 오는 길에는, 집에서 벗들과 즐거히 먹을 과자를 사오고 하엿다.

봄은, 가고 다시 왓다. 거반 放縱이라 하여도 올흔 살림의 1년은 또 지낫다. 그 봄 식물원에 사구라 퓌엇 다질 때, 하는, 시집을 가게 되엿다, 나는, 집에 가티잇던, 언니와 가티 생각하던 O인지라, 남한테 붓그럽지 안토록 모든 것을 쥰비하여 주엇다.
그의 혼례식은 청년회관에서 드럿다, 혼례식도 무사히 끗나고, 明月舘 支店에서 피로연을 하고, 저녁때, 유진이와 둘이서, 집에 도라 왓다, 그러나, 갑자이, 마음속에 니러 나는, 끗 업는 외로움은, 참을 수가 업섯다.
그날 밤 자리에 누은 나는 왜인지, 잠을 들수가 업섯다, 시게는, 두시 반을 치고 세 시를 첫다,
그러나, 눈은, 부칠 수가 업섯다, 겻방에서 자는 유진이도, 잠을 못드는 모양인지, 기침ㅅ소리만, 간간 울리웟다. 한 집에 가티 잇던 세 녀편네ㅅ가운데 혼자 남은 쳐녀 유진이의 마음을, 나는, 짐작하엿다.
마츰내, 옷을 닙고, 그 방을 차저 가며, 그는, 아직 옷도 벗지 안코, 잇섯다. 둘이서는, 만나기는 만낫지만, 서로 할 말이 업서서, 우둑허니 안저 잇섯다. 그러나, 좀 뒤에 유진이가 침목을 깨트렷다.-
「O는, 한창, 잠드러술 테지오?」
「세샹을 모르겟지요」
나는, 이러케 대답은 햇스나, 흥분으로 말미여 한잠도 못 이룬 첫날밤의 내가 생각나서, 그만, 그 자리에 쓰러졋다. 눈에서는, 하염 업시 눈물이 흘럿다.
얼마 울다가, 니러나서 보니, 유진이도, 제 쓸쓸한 처디를 생각하고 우럿는지, 눈이 벍어케 부어 잇섯다.＜209＞
이튼날, 지하실에 갓다 두엇든, 그의 사진이며 초상은, 모도 다시 내 침실에 장식되엿다. 그날밤, 거울로서, 그 초상을 보매, 그는, 곳 내 뒤에 서서, 나를 드려다 보는 것 갓햇다. 거울속에 비추인, 그의 초상은 사러잇섯다. 그의 숨은, 나는 내 등에서 깨다럿다. 거울 속에 비추인 그는, 눈까지 깜박어렷다.
나는, 처음으로, 사내로서의 그의게 대한 애정을, 깨다럿다.
그때부터, 나는, 시간의 대개를, 거울 압페서 보내게 되엿다. O는, 내가 갑작이 몸치장을 시작한 줄 알고, 이샹한 우슴을 우슨 때도 잇섯다.
업슨 그의게 대한 그리움은, 내 마음 속에 날이 가자 더 뜨겁게 되어 갓다. 아부지로서, 내지는 옵바로서 니저 버리웟든 그는, 그 지아버니로, 사내로, 내 마음 속에 부활하엿다. 그와 함께, 사러 이슬 때에 안해로서의 따뜨한 정을 바처 보지 못한 내가, 분하고 또 절통하엿다.
사내-이런 아름답고 그리운 말이, 다시 업섯다. 그러나 나도, 사내로서의 그를 알기 전에, 그만 그를 일허버렷다. 「어린애 갓다.」 이 말은, 邪氣 업단 말과 공통하여 됴타 하되, 나는 그 때문에, 그 지아버니로 그의게 사랑을 바처 보기 전에, 그를 일허버렷다.
한번 이저 버려가든, 그의 이전의 一擧一動은, 모도, 내 머리 속에 부활하엿다.
그는 이젼에, 이런 말을 한 적이 잇섯다. 한자로 엇던 나라의 國名을 쓰면, 그 글자의 모양이, 그 나라의 國性(이라는 말이 잇다하면)을 나타내인다. 英이라는 자는, 아름답고 점잔은 모양을 한 글자이고, 獨逸이란 글자는, 모양으로 보아서 뚝하고 勤勉하게 생겻고, 露(혹은 俄)란 자는 넙적하고 크고, 日本이란 자는, 좁고 깜즉하고, 米란 글자는, 성글고 크고, -그와 가튼 뜻으로, 新羅란 글자는, 위대하고 「작은 것은 눈떠보지도 안는」 거인적이며, 동시에 크고 넓음을 나타내인다고. 그러나, 만약, 「新羅」란 자를 인격화할 수가 잇스면, 그것은, S그의 인격에 다름 업섯다. 그의, 가장 별을 부리고, 또는 가장 광폭하게 구는-그 순간에도, 그의 인격은 그 속에 나타나이섯다.＜210＞
나는, 차차 우울하어젓다, 그리고, 낫보다는, 밤을, 깃버하엿다. 거울에 비추이는 그의 초상은, 밤에는 꼭 사른사람과 가티 내 억개를 넘어서 나를 마조 보고 하엿다. 한참 정신 업시 드려다 볼 때는 그는 웃는 때까지 잇섯다.
慶州 태생인 그는, 열 두살부터 서울살림을 하엿다. 하지만, 아직 남어 잇는 강렬한 경상도 사투리가 잇섯다. 업슨 그를 생각하고 그리는 내게는, 그, 젼에는 천스럽게 보이던 사투리까지도 아름답고 정답게 머리 속에 부활하여, 간혹 거리에서, 강렬한 그 사투리의 말소리가 들닐 때는, 뜻하지 안코 도라다 보고 하엿다.
유진이도, 마츰내, 내 속을 아럿다. 그는, 만날 나를 즐겁게 하려고 온갓 애를 썻다. 그러나, 그것이 모도 내게는 귀찬코 식그럽게만 보엿다.
「性에 눈뜬다」는 것은, 과연 이런 것인지. 고요한 밤중, 거울과 마조 안저, 그 속에 비추인 그의 얼굴에서 우슴을 기다리고 잇는 때 가튼 때, 만음 속에 뛰노는 커다란 물결은, 참을 수가 업섯다.
그는, 왜 업섯나. 나를 안해로서 극진히 사랑하던 그는 내게서는 안해로서의 사랑을 바더 보기 전에 업섯다. 그는, 땃뜨한 가정의 사랑이란 것은, 영구히 모를 테지. 연구? 그런 것은 성공하엿던 못하엿던, 관치 안타. 나는, 다시 한번 참 마음으로, 그의 넓은 가슴에, 내 머리를 무더 보고 십다.

엇던 날, 골방을 뒤질 때에, 그 속에, 낡은 신문지 뭉치를 묵근, 그의 낡은 자리옷의 허리띄를 보앗다, 나는 빼앗듯 그것을 푸러서, 내 허리띄에 매여 보앗다. 동시에, 그의게 드른바, 머리를 뒤로 따은 어린애의 S를 생각하엿다.
慶州 태생인 그는, 고향으로서 경주를 실허 하면서도, 동시에 경주를 찬미하는 사람이엇섯다. 넓고 말 못하고, 봄날 낫잠과 가티 한가스럽고 유쾌한, 太古쩍 기분을 그대로 남겨가지고 잇는 경주를, 나는, 그의게 몃번 드럿는지 모르겟다.＜211＞
「형용할 순 업지요. 그래두, 억지루 말하자면, 봄날 오후, 노픈 한울에 솔개 떠다니는 것 가튼-그런 기분이 잇는 곳이라구나 할가? 기지게 할 때와 가튼 유쾌하구 ノンビリ한 기분이 넘치구 쏘더지구 흐르구, -이러케나 형용할가.」

그는, 경주에 대하여 이러케 늘 니야기하엿다. 그러고, 경주가 만약 자긔의 고향만 아니엇더면 벌서, 경주서 산-지 오랫겟다고, 그만큼 경주를 질겨하는 사람이엇섯다.
나는, 마츰내, 그의 고향이고, 아직 그의 낡은 집이 남어잇다는 경주를 가보기도 하엿다. 그때는, 녀름과 가을도 다자고, 겨울이 갓가운 때이엇섯다. 그, 강렬한 경상도ㅅ사투리, 대구서 긔차를 나릴때부터, 려관사환애의게서, 또는, 인력거군의게서, 나는, 그것을 드럿다.
자동차를 탓다. 오후 두시쯤, 자동차는, 경주를 향하여 떠낫다. 이리하여 경주가 그 반갓가워 슬때는 과연, 그의게서 드른바, 慶州기분은 풍부히 넘처잇섯다. 멀리 보이는 마즌편과 자우편의 뫼ㅅ새에는, 넓은 논과 바치 널려잇고, 곳곳이 마을이 감뭇감뭇잇는 극히 평범한 경치이지만, 그 배치된 모양과, 또는 커다란 뫼와가튼 鳳凰臺들을 합한 그 경치는, 과연 봄날 낫잠과 가튼 한가함과 기지게와 가튼 유쾌함을, 볼 수가 잇섯다. 그 새의 길을 닷는 자동차는 지금ㅅ것이지만, 그것을 탄 사람의 마음은 녯적의 한가한 길을 지팽이를 집고, 것는 마음이 되게한다. 신라는 망하엿다 할지라도 그 기분과 위대함은, 아직 그냥 경주에 남어잇섯다. 그러나, 그 보다는 더 내마음을 뛰놀게 한 것은 그 경치는, 밧구어 말하자면, 그의 인격의 씸블에 다름업섯다. 그를 만약 경치화할 수가 잇다하면, 그것은 경주근방의 경치가 될 것이며, 경주근방의 경치를 인격화할 수가 잇다하면, 그것은, S 그의 인격에 다름업섯다. 나의 마음은 마치, 그를 여기서 만난 것과 가티 뛰놀엇다.
그날밤, 려관에서 묵고, 이튼날, 조반먹을 때 주인로파를 차저서, S 叅判의 집을 무러보앗다. 집은, 알기가 쉬웟다. 그 려관에서, 꼭 네모난 커다란 논 하나를 근너서 잇는 큰 기와집이엇섯다. 담장은 어기뎌기 문허지고, 웅집도, 곤대곤대 기와가 볏겨저 잇스며 이끼와 잡초가 그 우에 무성하여잇섯다.＜212＞
나는, 곳 옷을 가러닙고, 그 집으로 차저갓다. 그집 행량에는, 10여년을 주인업는 집을 지켜온 先代의 늙은 종의 부처가 잇섯다, 내가 이집 며누리인줄을 아른 때의 그 한멈의 놀람은 오히려 우섭게 보이도록 컷다. 그러나 그의 죽음을 아른 때의 슬퍼하는 모양은, 나로서는 보기 어려운 일이 엇섯다.
좀 뒤에, 한멈과 가티 집을 둘러보앗다, 사랑채는 모도, 담벽이 떠러지고, 집이 기우러뎌서, 그 크고 사치하든 집의 舊態는 어더볼 수가 업엇다. 중대문 안과 안대ㅅ문 안도, 그와 가탯다, 우리는, 뒤뜰도 도라가 보앗다, 못우에 쓰러진 정자, 잡초가 무성한 화단, 모든 것은, 거츠려 잇섯다, 生殺與奪權까지 가지고 잇는 사내종 게집종의 수 십명을, 마음대로 부리고, 나제는 낫잠이오, 밤에는 歌舞로, 젊은 일생을 그대신 굵게나 사러보려고 하던, 그 모든 로력은 지금은 다만, 헛되이 무성한 풀아레 감초여 이슬 뿐이엇섯다.
