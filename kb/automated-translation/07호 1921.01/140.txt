社會諷刺 銀파리
						목성 記
Fly: “Your Excellency!? On this New Year’s Day…”
Excellency: “Ugh, this little pest is quite bold. How is a white fly still alive in this winter…” *smack* hits his forehead!!
Fly: “Ouch! I already landed over here, why did you hit your forehead on the first day of the year? I just wanted to offer New Year’s greetings, and look at you, trying to maintain your dignity but making a fool of yourself. It’s fortunate no one else was here. Don’t worry about me, just keep looking at what you were looking at. Ah, you're looking at New Year’s cards (年賀狀)... Eh? There are quite a lot. I thought there wouldn't be many for you.”
Excellency: “Why, you little brat, why would you think there wouldn't be many? Even so, there are over two hundred here.”
Fly: “That’s still quite a few. Over two hundred… But among them, how many New Year's cards were truly written with sincerity? What? New Year's cards are all the same! Well, yes, that's true, but most of the New Year's cards sent to Your Excellency are from banks (銀行) or Jingogae shops (진고개 商店) and the like, a commercial strategy at the start of the New Year hoping to get more money from you than last year. It's infuriating, isn't it just an advertisement asking you to buy lots of goods?”
Excellency: “Why, are they only from shops?”
Fly: “There are many from other places too… The National Association (國民協會), the Shaman Guild (무당組合), and what else? Ah, the Kisaeng Guild (妓生組合), truly splendid. The big seals of such organizations stamped so boldly<52> have arrived… And next, ah, these are all from those who managed to get a petty county clerk position, or failing that, attached themselves to the military police (憲兵) riffraff, leveraging the influence of Your Excellency's name, thinking of the favors received. However, even those weren’t written sincerely. They were likely sent grudgingly, thinking, ‘Ah, just write one for that old moneybag too, as if tossing it into a dung heap. Then the white-haired Excellency will stroke his chin and be pleased. At least we did this much in his name…’ Isn't that despicable?”
Excellency: “That can't be true. You're just making things up.”
Fly: “Can’t be true, you say? Didn’t Your Excellency just call your scribe and say, ‘Hey, it doesn't really matter whether we send them or not, but it's customary at the New Year, so don't forget to send New Year's cards to a few places.’? Doing it just because it’s customary for the New Year, whether you like it or not! What kind of hollow, deceitful act is that? Living life泰然히 (calmly) putting on airs of dignity while doing such things, how ridiculous this human world is. In the human world, aren’t those skilled at putting up a facade considered accomplished? Isn't a person who succeeds in social circles someone who lies well, pretends to like people they dislike, badmouths them behind their backs, yet meets them again and flatters them with lies? Anyway, the better someone is at lying, hiding their true feelings, and flattering here and there, the more they are called a socialite (交際家). Isn't your human world one where the person skilled at deceiving others this way and that, cleverly lying just enough not to get caught, becomes the victor? Aren't those hailed as great politicians, great diplomats, or great tycoons invariably the ones who are best at lying and have the greatest merit from deceiving the most people? Anyone who wants to live a materially human life, that is, become rich and powerful, just needs to close their eyes, deceive and steal with all their might, tell plenty of lies, ignoring parents, siblings, relatives, and compatriots, and they will undoubtedly succeed as a capitalist or socialite. Isn't that so? Normally, the law should punish those who lie so much and deceive people well, but your world doesn't work like that. Instead, those who don't know how to lie, don't know how to deceive and steal from others, and only know how to earn their living<53> through their own effort are gradually pushed aside, oppressed, and robbed, as if they have no right to live in this world, left trembling in poverty and cold. Conversely, those who lie and deceive others well become successful figures or capitalists, living in glory. This seems to be a special feature of the human world. Living in such a world, Your Excellency must be quite fortunate, right?”
Excellency: “I don’t know what you’re talking about. If someone commits illegal acts like deceiving or stealing from others, there are laws. Would they be left alone?”
Fly: “Laws!? The laws you use in your world now! Are they truly absolute and just? A society with an unfair system, where those who have risen to success and prominence through all kinds of injustice and deceitful means join hands only with their own kind—politics that protect only the capitalist class. In such a world, how can one speak of the absolute justice of the law or the sacred impartiality of its authority? The current law punishes criminals like murderers and robbers. However, skillfully avoiding the net, slipping through the loose meshes of the law, committing crimes against humanity that one wouldn't dare commit, yet still enjoying the world's happiness alone—that seems to be the reason why the animal called human possesses precious 'wisdom,' and simultaneously the point that makes humans the most spiritual (最靈) among all creatures. Extremely spiritual indeed. Committing sins yet not getting caught by the law, and receiving many blessings...”
Excellency: “Where are there people who sin and receive blessings? And who says people accumulate wealth through fraudulent acquisition (詐欺取財)? They become rich (致富) by working diligently.”
Fly: “Are the means by which capitalists increase their wealth so legitimate? High-interest loans (高利貸金), buying dilapidated houses to rent out rooms (삭을세) to the poor and extracting exorbitant rent money (貰金)—those are perhaps lesser evils. Let's look at Your Excellency's mine, for instance. Isn't it true? Those miners who dig into the ore source, needing lamps even during the day, what pitiful poor people they are. They work in those mine tunnels to feed their old parents and young wives and children. Isn't it common for dozens of miners to be buried and die in those tunnels when they collapse, their bodies never found? Do you give adequate wages (雇金) to them who risk their lives working like that? Moreover, don't their pitiful old parents and young children reach the point of starvation<54> due to the miners' deaths? They disregard such dangerous work and devote their lives and efforts, yet their only reward is abuse, and who takes the profit gained from their efforts? Isn't Your Excellency sacrificing countless lives for a few pennies in wages for your own profit? Sacrificing the lives of the poor, squeezing their full strength like extracting oil, and pocketing the profits alone. What greater plunder (掠奪), fraudulent acquisition (取財), or embezzlement (橫領) could there be? There's something even more astounding. Here, pitiful laborers sweat and exert all their strength to build a magnificent hospital. But a few days later, a laborer who built that very house falls seriously ill and is on the verge of death. Yet, he cannot be admitted to the hospital he built with all his might. Where else could such an unfair society exist?”
Excellency: “Is there any reason a hospital wouldn't accept a laborer? Even a laborer, if they pay the admission fee, why wouldn't they be admitted?”
Fly: “That sounds plausible. But has this society given that laborer enough means to afford hospitalization when sick? Did the person who refuses admission to a patient near death due to lack of fees—the owner of the business, the one whose important building, their money-making house, was built by that laborer—give adequate compensation? Forcing much work for little pay (雇金), feigning ignorance—such ruthless fraud (詐欺), embezzlement (橫領). And then, turning a blind eye to a patient who could easily be saved by medicine, letting them die—such inhumanity, no, no, murder, a murderer! Yet that person isn't charged with murder and remains a happy person. And consider how beneficially that unreasonable profit, that wealth acquired through the sacrifice of numerous poor people, is used for society. Have you ever donated to any school? Have you ever donated to any youth association? Let alone donations (寄附), have you ever even thought about society? Think about how many poor people could be saved with the amount Your Excellency spends on wasteful (浪費) ephemeral amusement expenses (遊興費) that bring no benefit whatsoever to human society. Or consider how Your Excellency's personal amusement expenses are derived from the sacrifice of how many poor people. Do you not know that the cosmetics of Your Excellency's wife or the new student mistress contain the sorrowful tears of the pitiable laborers of the propertyless class? Are you unaware that the sound of the piano, bought out of vanity (虛榮) even though no one knows how to play it, and thumped upon, is mingled with the cries of resentment (怨聲) from the poor who suffer persecution by the ruthless propertied class and weep in poverty?<55>?”
Excellency: “Why do you say I've never made a donation? Just this summer, I donated one hundred won for flood relief (水害 救濟)...”
Fly: “Quite generous. Anyway, you donated one hundred won. But at that time, wasn't the solicitor for the donation from a newspaper company? If it hadn't been for the newspaper article saying 'Viscount So-and-so, one hundred won,' you might not have donated even that, even if it were ten thousand won. It wasn't sympathy given out of sincerity, but an advertisement for yourself using the newspaper, one hundred won offered for the false honor of being called a philanthropist. Quite generous indeed. When Im Sŏnggu (林聖九), the leader of the Reform Troupe (革新團長), gave food and clothes to a group of beggars and orphans somewhere in the southern provinces (南鮮 某某處), I thought it was a laudable act. But later, I saw photos of it all plastered with large letters on theater advertisements posted on every street corner. I realized then that he didn't give food and clothing out of sympathy for the beggars, but as an advertising tactic to gain popularity for himself and general approval for the Reform Troupe. What a wicked fellow, I thought. Isn't Your Excellency's one hundred won donation the same kind of thing? Using relief for the poor to seek personal philanthropic fame. Anyway, the poor who received a share of that one hundred won might be grateful. But isn't it too much to repeatedly sacrifice the poor—first sacrificed for Your Excellency to become a capitalist and obtain a powerful title (爵位), and then sacrificed again through false charity to spread your fame? Just moments ago, right at the gate of the mansion of Your Excellency, who doesn't spare a hundred won donation, a pitiful blind man carrying his motherless young daughter on his back begged for a mouthful of rice. He couldn't even get that and was chased away trembling. What happened? Perhaps because dressing such an unknown beggar wouldn't get the five characters 'Viscount So-and-so' (아무 子爵) into the newspaper, it was charity without effect, so you just chased him away?”
Excellency: “I didn’t see that. If I had seen him, would I have sent him away empty-handed?”
Fly: “Haha—truly, in a world where one must lie well to succeed, you have achieved great success as a capitalist and even an aristocrat (貴族) with power. You evade quite skillfully. Well, since you say you didn't see it, let's leave it at that. But if Your Excellency were truly full of genuine charity, truly sympathetic towards the genuinely suffering populace, then how is it that, while seeing with your own eyes this desolate scene where people cannot afford their daily meals due to soaring prices (物價騰貴), struggling desperately to survive, compounded by a financial panic (財政恐慌) causing even middle-class people<56> to face repeated bankruptcies (破産), commit suicide, or flee, Your Excellency pats your full stomach and plans to invite socialites (社會名士) or whatever—masters of lies (搏殺?)—for a New Year's banquet (新年宴會) or something tomorrow? What's the reason for that? What lie will you use to evade this?”
Excellency: “Heh heh—that's going too far. It's my money. Can't I even hold a New Year's banquet once a year? No matter how much of a philanthropist one is, social obligations are separate. How can one always weep just like the pitiful people?”
Fly: “Still claiming 'my money, my own'? What do you mean, Your Excellency's money? Is it Your Excellency's money just because it's in Your Excellency's safe, no matter how it got there? Is it money for Your Excellency alone to enjoy, even if everyone else in the world starves and freezes to death? Was this Earth, this universe, made solely for Your Excellency to live in? Defying the Creator's will that all 1.6 billion people should live well together on this small piece of land, greedily seizing vast lands alone, scraping together and stealing others' possessions—is this the kind of world where Your Excellency alone is meant to live well and enjoy life?”
Excellency: “Who said I alone want to live well in a world meant for everyone to live together? Each person works diligently; those who earn a lot spend well and live well, and those who don't, don't spend well and don't live well...”
Fly: “Then why, I ask, are the miners, who work with all their might, risking their very lives to earn well, given not even one-thousandth or one-ten-thousandth of the profit, while Your Excellency, sitting comfortably, takes thousands or tens of thousands of times more and monopolizes it? What is that if not embezzlement (橫領)? What is that if not plunder (掠奪)? Is it still Your Excellency's money? If you say such a thing one more time, I will call you a robber (强盜).”
Excellency: “What?? What? Robber? You little brat, speaking so recklessly without restraint! Go away, you rascal, before you get swatted... Of all things to say on the first day of the year, you brat, calling me a robber! Why, did anyone tell those people not to become capitalists?”
Fly: “Aigoo, why are you shouting so loudly? Muttering 'first day of the year, first day of the year'... Is that all? Among capitalists, your family's wealth is particularly egregious, isn't it, having been ruthlessly and cruelly amassed by your father when he wielded great power, seizing people, beating them, and stealing from them? Ugh, disgusting! Ruffian (부란당), butcher of men (사람 백장), executioner (막난이)...” <57>
Excellency: “Hey—confound you, brat!” *smashes a vase with his fist.*
Fly: “Hahaa, my apologies. I've already flown up here, you see. Why did you break the poor vase? How ominous on the first day of the New Year... It seems your luck for this year might not be good after all. Please be careful. Even in summer, wear a thick scarf. I am truly worried. I'm here, right here, above your head. No matter how hard you look, your eyes can't reach this high. Well then—I shall go inside now. I must pay my respects (歲拜) to the Madam. Well—I'm going in. I think I might have peed on your head when you smashed the vase. Please forgive me...”
*Goes around through the back corridor of the Sarangchae (men's quarters), passes two doors, enters the Western-style room behind the inner quarters' main hall (內舍大廳), and goes into the inner main hall (안大廳마루).*
Fly: “Madam, I trust you passed the New Year (過歲) peacefully…”
Madam: “I don't want to hear it. Go away, you're annoying. What damn New Year is this? For a life like mine…”
Fly: “Oh my, why are you so displeased from the dawn of the first day? Did some brat throw stones at the house again today? Ah, those rascals are too much. They should have at least stopped on the first day…”
Madam: “Who would throw stones this early in the morning? What do you mean, 'why'? It's because he went out to celebrate the New Year, forgetting the ancestral rites (祖上 차례)... Who else but the Excellency did it?”
Fly: “Hahaa, His Excellency went to the young lady student's house to celebrate the New Year and came back at dawn, did he? Hahaa, sleeping alone in an empty room (獨宿空房)! Ahem—no, not at all. You're certainly not jealous like some lower-class woman just because you slept alone and cold last night, are you? You're upset out of concern for the ancestors, right? A perfectly reasonable sentiment, Madam. Having inherited such wealth, yet being obsessed with a concubine (妾) and forgetting the ancestors—perhaps it's because he holds a noble title? What could be more inauspicious for such a family? If things continue, why not stage a protest demonstration (示威運動) like last time? Eh? Don't have lye water (간수물)? If not, drink some soy sauce water (간장물) and try throwing a fit, pretending to die. Didn't you achieve partial success last time? Since it's already the first day of the year and the ancestors have been forgotten in this house, what does it matter if there's a bit of commotion<58>?”
Madam: “Really, I should just die. I can't live like this. Making me suffer like this from the very first day of the year, how can I endure this role?”
Fly: “Hush—a dignified noble lady (貴夫人) should refrain from curses (辱說). It's shameful. You speak of really dying? Surely, that's just delirious talk (妄佞).”
Madam: “So, am I supposed to live watching him, in my old age, dote on that girl student or whatever, young enough to be his granddaughter, whom he took as a concubine (妾)? There should be limits even to concubines. How many has it been, for how many decades? He's kept kisaeng concubines since we were married, so what's there to say? Have I truly lived my life all this time?”
Fly: “Why not? You were the wife of a rich magnate, a powerful man, an aristocrat (貴族) among the yangban. Dozens of servants below your station. A car waiting as soon as you step out the middle gate. What better fate could there be? And even in today's Maeil Shinmun New Year's issue, there was an article titled 'The Harmonious Household of Viscount O, where Spring Breezes Blow Year-Round!!' with a picture of this house... Haven't you seen it?”
Madam: “Spring breezes year-round (四時春風)?! Just pray violent storms don't blow day and night! Which rascal wrote such nonsense? That fellow probably came to the Sarangchae and got a hefty sum for New Year's food money (歲饌代錢). If they asked me to write for the newspaper, I could fill hundreds of pages. Day and night, he's stuck clinging to a concubine young enough to be his granddaughter. The first one to arrive with devoted New Year greetings (歲拜) is the Kisaeng Guild's clerk. Yet, the moment he steps out the main gate, he acts like the most dignified man, tsk tsk, that arrogance...”
Fly: “That seems plausible. Until now, I had only seen it as quite enviable. Gathering all the world's money only to waste (虛費) it like that, empty inside yet needing to maintain an air of dignity must be agonizing. Is the wife truly a wife? Is the husband truly a husband? Regardless of how lonely the wife feels, the husband is immersed with his concubine. Regardless of whether the husband comes home late from somewhere, the wife snores from early evening. Is there the joy of husband and wife sharing morning and evening meals together? Is there the affection of sharing life's worries? Bickering… Truly, the most tasteless life in the world seems to be this one… Ah, look at that thatched house (草家집) at the corner of the cabbage patch over there. See how delightfully they live. The husband is an artist; he's even traveled abroad and is quite a man of character. The wife graduated from the university department<59> of some school in Seoul and works in education. They live only with his mother, and you can't imagine how enjoyable it is. The wife serves her husband sincerely; the husband loves his wife dearly. And both husband and wife agree that a thatched house has more poetic charm (詩趣) than a tile-roofed house. So, because the city center is crowded and the air is dirty, they deliberately chose that thatched house. Around dawn or dusk, the sound of singing like a nightingale seems to come from the heavens. Wondering where it comes from, you see the teacher wife, wearing a white top (힌저구리) and black skirt (검은 치마), carrying water in a bucket along the field furrow, singing hymns (唱歌), right? The husband usually comes back from somewhere carrying fish or meat himself. Do they have servants? What servants? Sometimes you see the young couple both working in the kitchen! Isn't that delightful! And at night, the wife reads the novel the husband wrote to her mother-in-law. Then the old woman laughs and says, 'This story is just like our own household affairs made into a book. If this is published, people we know will laugh,' and they all laugh together. On holidays, the young couple walks side by side for a stroll. Isn't it delightful! Since they have no wealth (財産), they live month to month on their income. Do they hear unpleasant things from others because they have money? Are they fearful because of money? Truly, going to that house feels like real human living, and even we flies are envious. Truly, I envy the life lived affectionately without money more than this household, which has lots of money but lacks a real family life…”
Madam: “If only I could live like that for just one day before I die, that would be good. Is this a human way of living? It's just enduring because I can't die. What good is it to just have so much money? I'm a slave to money.”
Fly: “What's this now? So soon? You need to suffer quite a bit more because of money. You need to understand the taste of money better. How was the money in this house gathered? Wasn't it through countless unspeakable acts, practically like robbery? Do you think you can live comfortably and peacefully with that money? Would there be no Heavenly Principle (天理)? What are you saying? Didn't you also marry into this house out of greed for money? Now you must thoroughly learn the taste of money. Married off for money! A body trotting around, sold for money! Ugh—disgusting! Amidst that filth, you still complain that your husband only cares for his concubine? What about your chastity (貞操)? So, so… disgusting! Selling your body (賣身)! Prostitution (賣淫)! And still a noble lady (貴夫人)!”
Madam: “Ugh, this blasted fly! Why must you land right under my nose when I try to catch you? Hey, you blasted fly!” *hits under her nose.*
Fly: “Even if you hit under your nose, only you feel the pain; I'm already gone. Wouldn't it have been nice if what touched your nose wasn't my foot but the lips of that lecherous monk (못이저 하는 중놈)? Ehh, still a noble lady!”

**Summary**

This satirical piece features a silver fly confronting a wealthy nobleman (Taegam) on New Year's Day. The fly critiques the hypocrisy behind New Year's greetings, exposes the Taegam's wealth as built on exploiting miners and laborers, condemns the hollowness of his charitable donations made for public image, and mocks the injustice of a society where the deceitful rich thrive while the honest poor suffer. The fly then moves to the Taegam's wife (Manim), revealing her deep unhappiness due to her husband's blatant infidelity with numerous concubines, particularly a young student. The fly accuses the Manim of marrying for money and being complicit in the family's corrupt wealth, labeling her life a form of prostitution despite her noble status. The story uses the fly as an uninhibited voice of social critique, exposing the moral decay, exploitation, and personal misery hidden beneath the opulent facade of the colonial elite. Historians might find the detailed critique of capitalist exploitation (mining, construction, rent), the commentary on social customs (New Year cards, charity for reputation), the specific mention of contemporary figures/groups (Im Sŏnggu, Hyŏksindan), and the stark portrayal of class division and gender dynamics within the elite colonial Korean household particularly interesting.
