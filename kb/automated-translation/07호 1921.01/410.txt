大寒之後 (After the Great Cold)

If they do not fly, we may not know, but if they fly, they are bound to pierce the heavens; if they do not cry, we may not know, but if they cry, they are bound to startle people. Our Korean people, possessing such a sacred nature, have in recent years achieved astonishing, leaping progress that everyone cannot help but be amazed by. At the same time, the brethren's passion for knowledge—namely, the zeal for schooling, reading, and attending lectures—has indeed greatly increased. While each person has a new awakening, the collective power of assemblies and associations has gradually solidified and further expanded. As reference material for everyone regarding this phenomenon, the current population figures, newspapers, magazines, and a list of organizations within Korea (only those surveyed by this company) are presented as follows.

Current Population Figures for Korea
Koreans: Over 16,890,000
Japanese: Over 346,400
Other Foreigners: Over 26,300

According to the results of the Temporary Household Survey conducted on October 1st last year, Kyŏngsin (庚申, 1920), announced by the Government-General, the total number of households was 3,297,126, and the population was 17,264,119. Among these, Koreans numbered 3,196,551 households with 8,695,630 males and 8,159,659 females, totaling 16,891,289 people. Japanese numbered 93,488 households with 346,496 people. Other foreigners numbered 7,087 households with 26,334 people (excluding prison inmates). Looking at the increase over the recent three years, the population increased by 88,035 in Taishō 7 (1918), 92,877 in Taishō 8 (1919), and 114,210 in Taishō 9 (1920). **Summary**

This article from the January 1921 issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine highlights the perceived surge in Korean national consciousness and activity following the "Great Cold" (likely referring to the period around the March 1st Movement). It cites increased enthusiasm for education, reading, and association-building as evidence of "leapfrogging progress." The article provides 1920 census data detailing the population of Koreans, Japanese, and other foreigners in Korea, notes population growth trends, and ranks the major cities (Kyŏngsŏng, Pusan, P'yŏngyang). It also mentions the existence of newspapers and magazines before providing an extensive list of local youth associations (Ch'ŏngnyŏnhoe) across various provinces, affiliated with the Korean Youth Association Federation, as of August 1920.

For historians, the article is valuable for its contemporary perspective on the post-March 1st Movement atmosphere, framing it as a period of national awakening and progress despite colonial rule. The detailed 1920 population statistics offer demographic insights. Most significantly, the extensive list of youth associations across the peninsula underscores the rapid proliferation and importance of these organizations in fostering Korean nationalism, social activities, and potentially resistance efforts during the early 1920s, often under the guise of self-improvement or cultural activities. The sheer number and geographical spread of these groups listed are particularly noteworthy.
