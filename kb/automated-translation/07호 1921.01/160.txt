새날과 새사람의 인사
The Morning Star (啓明星) shone brightly (煒煌), then on the Eastern Mountain, the Rising Sun (旭日) beamed. All phenomena (萬象) in the world raised their hands in unison, shouting "Long Live!" (萬歲) for the New Year's Day (元旦). At this time, that Rising Sun looked down upon the multitudes (萬羣衆) and said,

"Greetings in the new year, elder brothers (兄님), are you well? I am the same 'light' (빗). A light filled with power and strength (勢에 力에 充滿한 빗). However, yesterday evening, like the Gyeongsin Year (庚申年), I thought I might leave entirely and not return. But because your thoughts, elder brothers, were especially earnest (懇切), I have come again like this. If you do not abandon me, I wish to share sorrows and joys (憂戚을 共分) together with you. But, as I am a body that is constantly (恒時) active, I cannot stagnate (停滯) this year either. When it is time to go, I shall surely go."

A voice shouted out from among the Brothers (兄弟中),
Brothers (兄弟들아)! This year, excellent for work, will pass quickly... <69>

**Summary**

This allegorical piece, likely an editorial for the New Year (1921, following the Gyeongsin Year 1920 mentioned), personifies the "Rising Sun" (the new year) addressing the Korean people ("Brothers"). The Sun acknowledges its return, prompted by the people's earnest desire, offers companionship through hardship, but emphasizes its transient nature and power ("light"), urging diligence as time is fleeting. From a historical perspective, the piece reflects the atmosphere of anticipation and the call for national effort during the early colonial period, using symbolic language (light, new day, brothers working) common in nationalist discourse of the era, published in a magazine known for its nationalist leanings. Historians might find the blend of hope, urgency, and nationalist undertones (addressing the populace as 'brothers', the call to action) significant for understanding Korean intellectual responses in the early 1920s.
