**Body Text Translation:**
Play Script: On the Eve (Act IV)
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl

The outskirts of Moscow. Insarop's lodging house.
The backyard of the inner house.
On the right side, a crudely built two-story house made of logs is visible, showing part of the building and the entrance. The entrance is slightly higher than the ground and accessed via low wooden steps. Light shines dimly from the glass window on the second floor.
Towards the front right, there is a small shed-like structure. The door is off its hinges, and something like straw matting peeks out from inside.
Other than these two structures, the area is flat ground. At the edge of the flat ground stand trees that look Russian, such as poplar, white birch, and fir.
On the right side, amidst a dense cluster of trees, a troika is tied up; its destination lamp casts a red beam. (This troika is rigged to move through the trees later.)
Behind the woods, there is a winding, sloping road, and in front of it, the ground appears to slope downwards. Through the trees, the distant view reveals a vast, desolate plain. The buildings and trees in the distance are all covered in white snow.
It is not snowing, but the wind seems strong.
Sunset time. It is not yet dark, but the snow reflects light, covering everything in white brightness.
About a week has passed since Act III. <181>

Someone has swept the snow in the yard in front of the shed and built a long bonfire (長斫불). An old man, Uwal, wrapped in a thick blanket (毯褥), is lying stretched out on the straw matting protruding from the shed, warming himself by the bonfire. Syubin, with both hands in his overcoat pockets, paces back and forth.
Near the shed, two or three bundles, including travel trunks and blankets, are placed. The innkeeper and a coachman move back and forth between the troika, carrying luggage. Both the coachman and the innkeeper seem drunk, humming some song nasally.

Uwal: Quite late, isn't it? What time is it now?

Syubin: It's about 6 o'clock, give or take. They said they'd pass Smolensk before dark, but at this rate, it'll probably be midnight.

Uwal: Today's scene of sorrow (愁嘆場) went on too long.

Syubin: If they just kept crying, they'd never leave. They've been crying for a week since then, but today, as the young couple (內外) are actually leaving together, the sadness must have surged even more. It's strange there are still tears left. Poor auntie's eyes are so swollen you can't tell her eyes from her nose.

Uwal: It's the bond between mother and daughter, a lifelong parting (生離別). She should cry as much as she wants.

Syubin: But looking at the situation now, I worry about what will happen after they leave. Will she die from the illness of weeping? Her daughter made an absurd marriage. Whatever about that, just the fact that she's going to a distant and unknown foreign country is something she can't resign herself to, no matter how she tries. It's a reckless thing.

Uwal: It's different from being the father.

Syubin: The father just throws tantrums for no reason. Why else would he? He had secretly decided on that fellow, the secretary (書記官) or whiskey or whatever, as his son-in-law, and got angry because Erena kicked that idea neatly away. So he raised hell with the whole family, threatening to report it to the Archpriest (大僧正) or petition the Prime Minister (總理大臣). In that chaos, I almost got beaten up too. In the end, nothing happened, but he just makes noise like a tiger stung by nettles; inside, surprisingly, there's nothing. He's probably at his usual club now, staring at other women's faces, drooling over dances, and playing cards. Occasionally, he might make small talk (閑談), saying something like, 'My girl read a bit more than others and ended up having a free marriage with some young fellow, a university student or something. What a ruined brat's business—' and drinking tea. <182>

Uwal: (Mysteriously wiggling his fingers) Even so, being someone's father, it's hard to just do that. There are other people's eyes to consider.

Syubin: You sound quite like an old man, sir. Perhaps, whatever happened, no matter how angry uncle gets or how much auntie cries, or whatever insults the world throws, Erena is not the kind of girl to turn tail hearing such things. She won't even listen to that kind of talk. (Watching the coachman and innkeeper diligently loading the luggage and sending them off) Now, we're sending Erena off like this for good. But where is she going—? Thinking about it is dreadful. It feels like wandering day and night without direction in a vast, empty desert plain. Before my eyes, I see various images of that girl's departing figure, leaving the house she lived in, heading into the dark, snowy night, perhaps minus thirty degrees Celsius. But she made such a decision with a woman's heart. I feel like I understand that heart well. The falling snow is cold, the night is dark; if she feared such things and stayed cooped up at home, what enjoyment would there be? What power is there in this vast country called Russia to hold onto just one girl like her? There's only the vast plain, yawning with monotony and boredom. Her friends are mostly the likes of Secretary Klnatwiski, Mr. Pelsenep, Uncle Uwal, and the chattering Syubin. What is there to attach her heart to?

Uwal: There might be nothing to attach her heart to.

Syubin: So, abandoning that worthless country and those worthless friends, drifting away under a distant foreign sky—her only companion, the man she trusts as her husband... that rascal Kyŏngch'il, I can't stand the anger. Well, but what can be done? It's Insarop whom she trusts as her husband, and he's spitting blood. Poor thing, probably won't live long. It seems like the faces of his parents appear before him as he goes to bid farewell, parents he won't live much longer than. Right now, that young man's face can be described just like Brutus in the camp at Philippi, haunted by Caesar's ghost (幽靈).— By the way, <183> uncle, do you know Brutus?

Uwal: How would I know? Must be a person, I suppose.

Syubin: Indeed, he is a person. That man also had a handsome face, but a terribly unhealthy one.

Uwal: In battle, everyone's the same.

Syubin: In battle, everyone's the same. Heh, uncle? You're saying quite interesting things today. But if one wants to live long without going to battle, one can't last long with such a face. Since Erena is already married to him, she must want him to live a bit longer to see interesting things together.

Uwal: Wanting to live, wanting to live. That's all talk for young people. Talk for young, energetic people. Living, dying, war, peace, victory, defeat, love, freedom, nation. - People have all sorts of concerns.

Syubin: That's right. People have all sorts of concerns. Erena wanted to know all those different things too, which is why she left this country to go out into the wide world. But couldn't her new life have been pioneered without holding onto a foreign ghost (幽靈) like that Bulgarian? Indeed, Insarop is a fine man. At least, he's not one of us chatterers. Not like us, who spend our days in useless debates about self-completion or life's revolution, saying 'this is my most urgent thought,' or 'this is the emotion that delights my quiet growth,' looking at our own faces in the mirror, feeling self-important or pessimistic, being failed Hamlets who only talk about themselves being themselves. That man calmly calculates his own worth and proceeds straight ahead without deviation. It's truly enviable. If there were even one person like him among our kind, perhaps Erena wouldn't have had to swim out into the vast sea like a fish caught in a net.— What do you think, uncle? Will there ever be a time when a man capable of holding onto Erena emerges among us in this country?

Uwal: (Yawning) There will be such a time. Certainly. (Twirls his finger.)

Syubin: Feeling sleepy already? Ah, the way you lie there, wriggling so lengthily, is exactly the image of Russia itself. Is it the sleep before waking, or the sleep before falling deep? Laziness or effort? Wrapped in mist, in that state between waking and sleeping, what can emerge? <184>

Uwal: How can anyone know but God? (Yawning.)
(Pelsenep emerges hurriedly from the woods.)

Syubin: Ah, Pelsenep, good timing. (Extends his hand.)

Pelsenep: (Panting, grasps his friend's hand) Is there still time? Is there still time left? I'm glad I wasn't late.

Syubin: Now it's really farewell.

Pelsenep: It seems so. (Pause)

Syubin: When are you planning to leave for Heidelberg?

Pelsenep: I plan to leave within the year.

Syubin: We're dwindling, aren't we? I should hurry to Rome too.— Hey, did you hear Insarop is going to war?

Pelsenep: Who knows if he can go with that body. More than half of his lungs are rotten. Only his ideals (空想) keep him alive. Perhaps it will take a while for those ideals to be realized.

Syubin: They're not just ideals. That man is alive because of Erena's intense passion (熱氣). To him, Erena is like the sunlight before sunset. Pelsenep, you too are ultimately a failure in love. There was a time I envied you as a victor, but now we are both losers. Let us losers shake hands and wish our pitiable victor the longest possible happiness. (They shake hands.)

Erena's voice: (Heard from the inner house.) Is it alright now? Slowly. Slowly. (Insarop, his entire body wrapped in a thick fur coat, only his pale face visible. His eyes look sharp and composed; though signs of illness are evident, his steps are firm, and his speech and actions have an even more poignant air. Erena follows behind him, almost embracing him. In her travel coat, muffler, and headscarf, her white face stands out like a sculpture. Her eyes seem swollen from crying, but a cheerful expression shows in her speech and movements. The innkeeper, his wife, daughter, etc., follow behind carrying hand luggage (手荷物), umbrellas (洋傘), etc.)
(Everyone gathers in the yard, centered around the couple, shaking hands.)

Erena: We've kept you waiting too long. (She looks around clearly, then sees Pelsenep) Ah, Mr. Pelsenep, welcome. (Approaches affectionately, as if about to embrace his neck) I thought I wouldn't see you again.

Pelsenep: (Silently kisses Erena's hand.) <185>

Insarop: (As if about to embrace Pelsenep) Pelsenep, I've troubled you so much. I was delirious with fever and could hardly think of anything.

Erena: If Mr. Pelsenep hadn't nursed you, you wouldn't have survived.

Pelsenep: Erena, your stubbornness is astounding. To say he would have died if I weren't there, sounds like a child's talk.—

Erena: (Holding his hand) Forgive me. Forgive me.— I was too stubborn with you, right to the end— I truly feel I owe you apologies for more than one or two things.

Pelsenep: (As if biting his lip) No— What is this—
(A silence, as if unable to speak further.)

Syubin: (Lightly breaking the silence) Well, that the three of us have gathered here together at the very end, I feel an uncontainable gratitude, thinking it must be due to no small fate from a previous life. Everything is destiny and fate. Following fate's command, we intend to laugh off all the past as past. First, we wish happiness for the new life of Mr. Insarop and Ms. Erena. Next, we wish much happiness for Mr. Pelsenep's new life as a scholar. Finally, we would like to raise a toast for the new life of the humble Mr. Syubin as an artist, but unfortunately, we haven't uncorked the champagne bottle (甁). Let's raise our empty cups, congratulate the health of our couple, Insarop and Erena, and also call out 'Manse!' (萬世 - Long Live!) for the hopeful future of the Bulgarian people. O God, please grant much happiness to the new life of our dear friends.
(Sings) The road is long, go with God—
(All raise their imaginary cups in a toast. Laughter.)

Insarop: Then, everyone, we bid farewell here. (Makes the sign of the cross on his chest) Beloved land of Russia, beloved Russian friends, may you all be well.

Erena: (Looking at Syubin) Paul (Pauwel), we fought quite a lot too. Please forgive me.— (To Pelsenep) Andrei Petrovich (Andrei Perotwich), truly, to you—
(Handshakes. Kisses. Embraces.)

Pelsenep: Please write as soon as you arrive. <186>

Erena: Where will we arrive? Even I don't know my own path.— You're going to Heidelberg, right? I will definitely write.

Pelsenep: Syubin is going to Rome too.

Erena: Really, now.— Paul (Pauel), please create wonderful works.— (To Old Man Uwal) Uncle, uncle (Embraces him affectionately) Mother, please, please take care of her.

Coachman: (Loudly) Everything's ready. Please get in.

Insarop: Well then, everyone, farewell.

Erena: Everyone, farewell.—

All: Farewell. Please travel safely.
(Kisses. Handshakes. Embraces.)
(Insarop disappears into the woods. Erena prepares to follow.)
(Sound of bells heard from afar. Gradually getting closer, then the sound of a sleigh is heard from the woods.)

Voice: (Heard from the woods.) Wait there. Stay still, stay still.

All: (Startled, they freeze. Erena hesitates before getting into the troika.)
(Nikolai Stahov (Nyagolai Sŭdamhoup) arrives, panting, his coat covered in snow.)

Sŭdamhoup: Ah, just in time. Erena! Hey!
(Calls out as he approaches the troika.)

Erena: Father! (Calls out and collapses into the snow.)

Sŭdamhoup: (Helping his daughter up) Come, Erena, Father's final blessing. (Takes a small, previously embroidered cross from his pocket and pins it to the edge of his daughter's collar.)

Erena: Father! (With a crying voice, falls at her father's knees and kisses his hand.)

Sŭdamhoup: (Loudly) Champagne! Champagne!
(The Coachman brings a champagne bottle (甁) and three cups from the woods.)

Sŭdamhoup: That, give it here. (Snatches the champagne bottle and is about to give a cup to Erena.)

Erena: (Stands up quickly) Father, wait a moment.
(Goes into the woods and leads Insarop out by the hand.)

Erena: (Still holding one of Insarop's hands, presents him before her father, then takes the cup from her father's hand) Father, this hand— <187>

Sŭdamhoup: (Seems to hesitate slightly, then quickly changes his mind) Uh-huh. (Shakes hands forcefully.) Take care of yourself too.

Erena: (With a grateful expression) Father. Father!—
Then, Father! (Offers the cup.)

Sŭdamhoup: Uh-huh. (Pours the champagne. His hand trembles violently, and champagne spills onto the snow. Sŭdamhoup's eyes are filled with tears of emotion. Erena, Insarop, Sŭdamhoup, all drink with trembling hands.) God's blessing— Come, you two have a glass too. (Passes cups to Syubin, Pelsenep. All drink.)
(Sound of the Coachman urging the horses.)

Erena: Then Father, farewell.

Insarop: Farewell.

Sŭdamhoup: Please, take care of yourselves—
(Receives the kisses of the young couple, shedding tears.)

Erena: Then everyone, farewell.

All: Go safely.
(Erena and Insarop disappear into the woods, only the sound of the coachman cracking the whip remains.)

Sŭdamhoup: (As if suddenly remembering, starts to follow) Hey, write! Don't forget! (Voice choked with emotion.)

Erena: (Her white face peeking out from the woods) Yes, Father. Please go inside. Tell Mother too.— Mr. Andrei, Mr. Paul, everyone, please go inside.—
(The troika moves at the crack of the whip. The sounds of 'Go safely' fade, and the loud jingling of bells also grows distant.)

Sŭdamhoup: (Stands watching distractedly as they leave. When they are out of sight) Again, Erena! Erena! (Puts both hands on his head, about to collapse.)
(Faint sound of bells.)
—(Curtain)— <188>

**Summary**

This excerpt depicts the fourth act of Hyŏn Ch'ŏl's play, likely an adaptation of Turgenev's "On the Eve." Set in snowy Russia, the scene portrays the emotional farewell of Erena and her ailing Bulgarian husband, Insarop, as they depart for an uncertain future, possibly involving Bulgarian independence struggles. Friends like the cynical Syubin and the loyal Pelsenep, along with the initially reluctant but ultimately blessing father Sŭdamhoup, bid them goodbye. Syubin's monologue critiques the perceived stagnation and inaction within Russia, lamenting the lack of strong individuals capable of inspiring devotion like Insarop does for Erena. For historians of colonial Korea, this play, published in the nationalist magazine *Gaebyeok*, is interesting. Syubin's critique of Russia could easily be interpreted by Korean readers as an allegory for their own situation under Japanese rule—a sense of national helplessness, the departure of intellectuals or activists seeking freedom or purpose elsewhere, and a yearning for decisive figures to lead the nation. The theme of leaving a stagnant homeland for a dangerous but meaningful cause abroad would resonate strongly.

우왈. 母女의 情理로 平生의 生離別이니까. 울고 십흔 대로 울어야지.

(馬夫가 樹林 속에서 삼판의 甁과 곱부 세 개를 가지고 온다.)
스담호웁. 그것, 이리내어.(삼판의 甁을 빼앗는 것 가티 밧더니 컵을 에레나에게 주랴고 한다.)
에레나. (急히 일어서더니) 아버지 잠간 기다리셔요.
(森林 中으로 가더니 인사롭의 손을 끄을고 온다)
에레나. (인사롭의 한 짝 손을 잡은 채로 아버저 압헤다 내어밀더니, 아버지의 손에 쥔 컵을 밧고) 아버지 이 손을—＜187＞
스담호웁. (족음 躊躇하는 것 갓더니 急히 생각을 돌린 것 가티) 으응 (힘을 들여서 握手한다.) 자네도 아모쪼록 몸 성히.
에레나. (感謝한 表情으로) 아버지. 아버지!—
그러면 아버지! (컵을 낸다.)
스담호웁. 으응. (이라고 하며 삼판을 붓는다. 손이 벌벌 떨리며 삼판이 눈우에 떨어진다. 스담호웁의 눈에는 感激한 눈물이 가득 차잇다. 에레나, 인사롭, 스담호웁, 모도 손을 떨며서 마신다.) 한우님의 祝福을— 자아 자네들도 한 잔씩 먹게. (슈빈, 벨세넵에게 컵을 돌린다. 一同이 먹는다.)
(馬車夫말을 꾸젓는 소리)
에레나. 그러면 아버지, 안녕이 계십시오.
인사롭. 안녕이 계십시요.
스담호웁. 아모쪼록 몸이나 성케들—
눈물을 흘리며서 젊은 內外의 接吻을 밧는다.)
에레나. 그러면 여러분들 안녕이 계셔요.
一同. 안녕이 가시요.
(에레나와 인사롭은 森林 속으로 업서지고 오즉 車夫의 말을 후려갈기는 소리뿐)
스담호웁. (갑작이 이진 것 가티 조차가더니) 이에, 편지해라. 잇지말고. (목이 메이는 소리로)
에레나. (森林 中에서 힌 얼굴을 내밀고) 예에 아버지. 어서 들어가셔요. 어머니에게도 말슴해 주셔요.— 안트레이氏 빠우엘氏 여러분 어서 들어가셔요.—
(말채쭉 소리에 트로이카가 움작인다. 안녕이 가시라는 소리가 차차 멀어지며 甚히 나던 방울 소리도 차차 멀어진다.)
스담호웁. (喪心을 하고 精神업시 가는 것을 보고 섯다. 가는 것이 보이지 아니하게 되니까) 다시 에레나! 에레나! (하며 머리에 두 손을 언고 넘어지랴고 한다.)
(그윽한 방울소리)
