잡지명	개벽 제14호
발행일	1921년 08월 01일
기사제목	소설 표본실의 청개고리 (Novel: The Frog in the Specimen Room)
필자	廉想涉
기사형태	소설 (Novel)

소설 표본실의 청개고리
						廉想涉

The stagnation of my mood (氣分) and the endlessly drawn-out ennui of life dragged my unwilling feet all the way to Namp'o.
After returning home, 7 or 8 months (朔間) of irregular living had not only battered my entire body like a sponge (海綿) but had also consumed (蠶食) my very soul (魂魄). My body was so fatigued (疲勞) that wherever I touched, it seemed to emit the toxic stench of alcohol and nicotine. Moreover, after passing the peak summer of June and July, as the time came to wear layered clothes and the season (節期) changed rapidly (急變), it became difficult to pull myself together. Even a neighborhood (洞里) stroll (散步) made me break out in cold sweats, and if I tried to chat (이악이) with a friend (親故), I'd be looking for a wooden pillow (목침) after just two or three words.

Despite this, my fiercely agitated (昻奮) nerves (神經) remained wide awake even in bed. After tossing and turning until the second or third cockcrow, only managing to close my eyes when the east grew bright, more than a week passed during which I couldn't bear to turn off the light and lie down.

Among all this, what adhered (膠着) to my mind, harshly assaulting (掩襲) my nerves whenever I lay down with the lights off or sat quietly (從容히), was the image (形狀) of a dissected frog pinned (핀) by its four limbs (四肢), spread-eagled on a dissection board (七星板). —When I was in the 2nd year of middle school, in the natural history laboratory (博物實驗室), the thick-bearded (鬚髥) teacher dissected a green frog. After pulling out the steaming internal organs (五臟) one by one and arranging them in an alcohol jar (酒精甁) like laying a sleeping baby down, he looked around at the students (生徒) gathered (擁圍) around as if he had made a great discovery (大發見) <118>
"Look, everyone, see how it's still alive." And as he poked here and there with the sharp tip of a needle, the frog, deprived of its organs, would twitch and struggle (苦悶) convulsively, pinned by its limbs.

The impression (印象) from 8 years ago came back to me vividly these days, and no matter how hard I tried to forget (이저버리랴고) it, I couldn't... The bright blue scalpel («메쓰»), the tiny, pulsating heart (心臟) and lungs (弊 - archaic for 肺) the size of chicken droppings, the needle tip, the small shiver (戰慄)... As these came to mind one after another, the hair on my head stood on end, and I felt as if cold water (冷水) was being poured (끼언지는) over my entire body. It felt like the intense reflected light (反射光) from the scalpel, held up under the south-facing (南向) glass window (琉璃窓), was piercing my eyes (眼孔), making my tightly closed eyelids sting even as I lay in the dark room (房). But whenever that happened, I couldn't stand the worry (操心) about the razor blade (面刀칼) kept in the desk drawer (冊床 舌盒) beside my head.

The night before I went to Namp'o, this symptom (症) was even worse (甚하얏다). — The electric light (電燈불) hanging high in the room, only about a room-and-a-half (間半통박게) wide, was too bright, so I turned it off. Although I wasn't entirely free from the concern (念慮) that I might be troubled by hallucinations (歡迎) again, feeling disturbed (心事), I threw off my upper garment (우통), shot up, twisted the switch («스위취»), and lay back down. However, as the "jjaeng-ung" sound faded through the crack in the door (門틈), what assaulted (掩襲) my mind again was the thick-bearded man's scalpel, the razor in the drawer. Scalpel—razor, razor—scalpel... The harder I tried to forget, the more stubbornly they refused to leave, chasing each other endlessly in my head. I felt my hand constantly wanting to reach for the drawer; I couldn't bear it. The strange demonic power (怪異한 魔力) grew stronger the more I tried to suppress (抑制) it. Hearing the sound of the drawer sliding (스르르) open, I started (소스라처) and opened my eyes, only to see the window (窓) behind the closed outer shutter (덧門) appearing blurry (부여케); the room was still sunk in darkness (暗黑) and silence (沈寂). An immense fear (非常한 恐怖) overwhelmed (壓到) my whole body, making me unable to lift even a fingertip, yet the strange allure (異常한 魅力) and temptation (誘惑) reached their peak (絶頂).

"Have I gone mad?... No—is this a sign (徵兆) that I'm going mad?" I screamed inside my head.

Like someone drunk on sleep, I kicked off the blanket (입울) violently (와락), got up, and put my hand on the drawer. However, just as the thought "What if I touch it..." flashed through my mind like lightning (電雷), I snapped back to my senses as if waking from a deep dream. About to turn on the light, I fumbled for the matchbox (成陽桶) instead.—I struck one match, picked up the candle (洋燭) placed on the windowsill, lit it, and then opened the drawer. After rummaging (부스럭부스럭) through the contents—manuscripts (原稿) unused for months, letters (片紙), medicine boxes (藥匣), all jumbled like a wastebasket (休紙桶)—<119> I grabbed the smooth handle, pulled out the razor without looking at it, and threw it out the window into the yard (뜰). But still, I couldn't sleep. My pulse (脈) weakened completely, and cold sweat beaded on my forehead. Lying still like a corpse (屍體), like a patient after agony, with my limbs limp (늘이털여노코), I thought—

"Somehow, I must escape this room." I glanced around the room as if utterly disgusted. The thought of traveling somewhere (어대던지 旅行) had been planned (計劃) for months, but even the idea of visiting Sinheung Temple (新興寺), where I had vacationed once in the summer, remained just talk, never realized (實現).

"I have to go somewhere. To the ends of the earth (世界의 끗). To infinity (無限). Forever (永遠히). As far as my feet can carry me... A deserted island (無人島)—the desolate plains (荒凉한 벌판) of Siberia (西伯利亞)! The South Seas (南洋) where fat sizzles from the body!... Ah—ah."

I pictured the lush forests (鬱鬱한 森林) seen in postcards, naked natives (裸體의 蠻人) sitting under coconut trees (椰子樹), and shrugged my shoulders with a sense of exhilaration (痛快한). I want to ride forever on a full-speed («푸-ㄹ, 스피-드») train, sweating profusely, breathing hard, strong breaths without even a single minute's stop (單 1分의 停車).—This was what I craved (渴求) more than anything... If only there wasn't the worry (念慮) of getting dizzy (眩氣), I might have cheered for an airplane! Airplane!

2
The main reason I hadn't been able to leave the house for several months was financial constraints (金錢의 拘碍), but in truth, even just stepping outside the main gate (大門 밧게) wasn't easy.

The next day (翌日), when H came and urged me to accompany (同行) him on a visit (訪問) to P'yŏngyang, saying he was definitely leaving today, I was drawn by the desire (慾求) to turn my back on the tiresome bustle (雜沓) of Kyŏngsŏng and find a different atmosphere, and by the curiosity (好奇心) of getting to ride a train regardless of the duration (長短을 不拘하고). "Yes, let's go, let's go," I readily agreed (同意), covering up my hesitation (덥허노코). But when the time actually came to leave, I couldn't tell if I wanted to go or if it would be better to stay; unsure of my own feelings (心中) or the reason why (細音), I somehow ended up being dragged by H to Namdaemun Station (南大門驛). The train (列車) hadn't arrived (到着) yet, but passengers (乘客) were entering (入場). I hastily bought a ticket (票) and followed the urging (催促) H. I felt rather fortunate (大幸) <120> that the force (勢力) of time (時間) was decisively (獨斷) dragging everything along under its irresistible power (不可抗力), regardless of preference (好不好) or consent (肯不肯), and thinking "whatever happens, happens," I stood in the queue unraveling one by one. However, seeing H arguing (詰難) at the ticket gate (改札口) because he didn't have a quarantine certificate (檢疫證明書), I stepped out of line from the train and said again that I wouldn't go.

Annoyed (心事가 난), H snapped, "Do as you please," pushed me away, and headed alone towards the makeshift injection room (出張注射室), but then turned back and dragged me along.

When the hysterical (히스테리크한) nurse (看護婦) lunged at me with the injection needle (注射침), I pulled down the shirt («샤쓰») I had half-rolled up and turned to face her. Forced by the nurse's scolding (핀잔) and H's urging (催促), I shut my eyes tightly, took the shot, and then rushed onto the platform (플래트포-ㅁ) and boarded the train. Even sitting on the train, I felt pointless regret (空然히 後悔), but amidst the vortex (渦中) of stimulation (刺戟) that made all my senses (官能) leap at once—the strong (强烈한) whiskey («휘스키-»), the intense (激甚한) shaking (動搖) and jolting (反撥) of my whole body, the deafening clang (轟轟한 軋響), the speed (速力) breaking through the darkness (暗黑), the dull ache (沈痛) in my injected shoulder (肩)—I forgot everything, and by dawn, my mind had calmed enough that I slept soundly (쿨쿨). Thanks to that (德澤으로), neither the scalpel flashed nor the razor jumped out that night.

Just as dawn was breaking, we got off at P'yŏngyang Station. Since the train to Namp'o wouldn't leave for another 20 or 30 minutes, we washed our faces (洗手) in the washroom (洗面所) and came out to the waiting room (待合室). I sat down at the end of the sofa («쏘파»), my puffy red eyes cast down, then abruptly stood up.
"I'm going to wander around here for a bit..." I tossed out the words and looked squarely at H.
"Go alone, meet Mr. C, and if you come back here together today, I'll see you then. Otherwise, I'll just wander around alone and take the night train back." Without waiting for H's reply, I turned and walked out.

"Huh? What's wrong? Why are you like this... That crazy fit must be starting again," H said, pulling on the raincoat («레인코-트») he had taken off and following me, grabbing my arm (붓든다).
"...I don't want to see people... Honestly, I don't have much to talk about with Mr. C anyway..." I said in a pleading (哀願하듯이), weak tone (口調), then added, like talking to myself, emphasizing each word,
"I want to flow away forever. Towards the endless..." and abruptly walked off. H, resigned, picked up the trunk («투렁크») from the table (테-불) and followed. <121>

We found our way to the banks of the Taedong River (大同江) and walked silently along the dimly lit path towards Pubyŏk Pavilion (浮碧樓) as dawn was breaking. After circling around and coming back down, we had breakfast on Chongno Street and then headed back towards Pubyŏk Pavilion. The shops, having opened for the day (開市), sprinkled water in front (門前), and people sitting reading newspapers looked cool (淸凉하고) and happy (幸福스럽게). The morning sun, which had been licking the horizon beyond Nŭngna Island (綾羅島) with scarlet flames (朱紅의 火焰) when we came down earlier, had now climbed above the chimney (烟桶) of the waterworks (水源地) and shone dazzlingly on our cheeks as we walked beneath the trees planted along the riverbank (川邊植木).

People standing on rocks holding toothbrushes (이ㅅ솔), dipping towels (手巾) in the water to wash (洗漱), occasionally came into view (間或 눈에 띄엇다). I stopped and watched them absently (無心히), then felt the urge to dip my own hands in the refreshing water, so I picked a shallow spot (야튼 곳) and scrambled down to the water's edge.

H, who had followed (쪼차) me down and sat dipping his hands alongside mine, asked,
"X, shall we take the afternoon train?..."
"Whatever happens..." My head somewhat calmer (安靜), I felt my clenched heart soften (和解). Looking out at the water surface flowing silently and coolly in the morning sun, I replied thus and murmured inwardly, "Water is great."

Just then, we heard footsteps approaching from the embankment behind us and glanced back casually.
Picking his way over the dry spots, his long hair (長髮), tumbling down to his back, flew wildly behind his pale face with deeply sunken eyes each time he hopped. The figure approaching looked suspiciously like an artist (美術家) I had seen near Tokyo. Whether out of arrogance (驕慢心), implying "Your existence doesn't even register in my consciousness (意識)," or perhaps an attitude of indifference (無關心) to avoid (避하랴는) the gazes (視線) focused (集注) on him (一身), this owner (所有者) of strange (奇怪한) hair held a short bamboo stick (대ㅅ개비) in his right hand (右手), swinging it back and forth (前後로). Keeping his eyes fixed only on his feet (발끗), he passed behind me, went about a room's width upstream, and settled down. He too plunged his hands into the water with a splash, rinsed his mouth once, then abruptly stood up and strode <122> towards Taedong Gate (大同門). His long hair, without a hat (帽子), and the hem of his rolled-up, stained, patched durumagi (박이두루막이 자락) fluttered rhythmically (풀풀풀풀), keeping time (長短을 마처서) with the bamboo twig (柯枝) deftly (巧妙히) held between the fingers of his right hand (오른便 손가락).

"Quite the worker today."
"Haha! He must have an appointment (約條) for breakfast (朝飯) somewhere." Turning to look at the long-haired man, we exchanged嘲笑 (ridiculing laughter) and climbed back up the embankment, wiping our hands.

"True happiness lies in that kind of life..." I muttered to myself.

We reached the base of the embankment (축대) in front of Pubyŏk Pavilion, enveloped in weeds (雜草) turning yellow. The pavilion, empty enough to be called a small Kyonghoe-ru, was filled with pale autumn sunlight carried in by the light morning breeze. Slightly tired, we sat on a bench inside the pavilion, looked up at the various hanging plaques (懸板), and then I pointed to a note (札) attached next to a plaque on the left.
"Are people like that? Look at that." I began.
"If the instinctive desire (本能的 慾求) is to make one's existence known to even one more person, that's one thing, but to see that they couldn't be satisfied (滿足) without doing even that... it's truly pitiful (불상해)..."

"Setting that aside (姑捨하고), who on earth were those names, Lee So-and-so, Kim So-and-so, carved distinctly (歷歷히) on that cliff (絶壁) meant for?... And they could still eat after doing that; it must have been a good world..." Finishing my words, an inexplicable anger surged within me. I abruptly stood up, leaned against the fortress wall, and stared down below.

"That's what they call 'leaving a fragrant name for a hundred generations (遺芳百世)', isn't it?" H also stood up.
"Seeing you standing there looking down like that... the only thing missing is Ippolita (입포리다)..." [Likely a reference to D'Annunzio's "Triumph of Death"]
"Am I Giorgio (쫄찌오)?" I forced a bitter smile (苦笑). [Likely Giorgio Aurispa, the protagonist of "Triumph of Death"]
"You must at least feel Giorgio's anguish (苦痛)."
"Well... that's common (一般) to everyone (누구나) among modern people (現代人)."<123>

We fell silent (입을 담을고) for a moment (暫間), then headed towards Ŭlmil Terrace (乙密臺).
The imposing pavilion (巍巍히 臺閣) looked close enough to hit with one's nose if one fell (업드러지면 코달 듯), but the steep incline (急한 傾斜) was not so easy. Panting heavily (噓唏斷心), we barely made it up. However, when the first thing that caught my eye on the terrace was a bench advertising (廣告) some tailor shop (吳服店), something that hadn't seemed out of place even inside Pubyŏk Pavilion now struck me as unpleasant (不快한). Squinting (눈을 찝흐리고), I stared at it for a moment, then turned away without setting foot on the terrace proper, heading down towards the shaded western (西便) side below the fortress wall (城 미트). Shielded by the high wall, this side was still covered in dew drops (구슬 이슬) clinging only to the yellowing tips (꼿만 노릇노릇하게 된) of the grass blades (잔듸닙헤); wherever I stepped, the dusty (몬지안진) tips of my shoes shone blackly (깜아케 반짝어리어다). Leaning my back against the wall, I gazed listlessly (脈업시) at the vast plain (曠野) spread out (展開되) before me, then my legs gave way, and I plopped down (털석 주저안젓다). As the biting cold wind, like that radiating from a gloomy, cold room (陰酸한 冷房) in deep winter (嚴冬), touched my slackened muscles (筋肉), I felt my mind clear as if waking from anesthesia (痲醉). But while sitting with my legs stretched out, leaning against the wall, scattering dewdrops with both hands, drowsiness overcame me again. My limbs grew heavy, so I pulled a newspaper (新聞紙) from my pocket (포켓트), spread it out, and lay down.

...When H shook me awake two or three times, some 30 or 40 minutes had passed.
Startled, I sat bolt upright. H was idly tapping pebbles here and there with the tip of his cane, messing around (作亂), then burst into loud laughter.
"Ah-, do you know where you are, sleeping like this? And what's with the sleep-talking (잠고대)!... Why is your face all contorted (뒤틀렷서) like that?"

I stared blankly (멀거-니) at H's sleepy face, then murmured, "Ugh!..." about to say something, but was cut off by a yawn (하품에 맥히어). I stood up, thrust both hands into my trouser pockets (포켓트에 찌르고), and paced back and forth (이리저리 건니럿다). Noticing the round, dew-soaked patch (똥그라케 이슬에 저즌 것) where I had been sitting behind H, I was surprised but didn't respond (對句도 아니하고). Feeling a slight chill (좀 선선한 症), I moved into the sunlit area (陽地) and pulled H along, saying, "Let's go."

"Why, what's wrong? What did you dream?" H asked, following me.
"...A dream of death!... If only I had died completely (永永), ...it would have been good..." Gazing blankly ahead (압흘 멀건히 내다보며) without seeing anything <124>, I recalled the beginning and end (始終) of the dream step by step, then tossed out these words and took out a cigarette (卷煙) to smoke.

"Suicide (自殺)?" H looked at me, smiling.
"...At the hands of a beautiful woman (美人)... Do you think a guy like me has the courage (勇氣) to commit suicide? Ah-ha."
"By whom? If it were a beautiful woman, I wouldn't mind dying once or twice... Hehehe."
"Really... Anyway, if only one could gain the experience (經驗) of death without any pain (苦痛), without fear (恐怖), and still remain alive as before (如前히), it would be exhilarating (痛快해) even ten or twenty times... The pleasure (快感) when the throat is choked daily! It's something unattainable through any other stimulation (刺戟)."

Unable to bear the indescribable feeling of decay (썩어가는 듯한 心思) within me, I fell silent. Walking slowly back down the path we had come up, annoyed by H's questioning, I told him about the dream as we passed in front of the Chŏn'gwon Teahouse (前卷茶屋).
—I'm not sure what it was about, ... maybe because the rice cake I steamed didn't cook properly? ...Anyway, holding a hand covered (뒤발을 한) in white powder (힌가루), I hesitated (어정버정하다가) at the edge of the wooden floor (마루 끗), then, as if it were time to die, I tied the towel (手巾) I held around my neck. Lying down straight (반듯이) on the narrow wooden veranda (퇴마루) in front of the room with its tightly shut shutters (덧門을 첩첩히 다든 방 압), a bone-thin white hand (어떤 바짝 말라서 뼈만 남은 힌 손(白手)) reached over stealthily (슬금어니) from the head of my pallet and slowly tightened (슬금슬금 졸라단이엇다) the two ends of the towel around my neck from both sides (左右로). At that moment, as if this was a fate (約條) I had to naturally (當然히) meet (當할), I lay calmly (從容히) with my eyes closed, feeling a certain satisfaction (滿足) and peace of mind (安心). Then—the extraordinary stimulation (異常한 刺戟) as my neck gradually (次次) tightened was a pleasure (快感) I had experienced for the first time since birth (落地 以後). But why did I have to die so soon?... Am I really dead? Doubts (疑問) arose, and I twisted my body and snapped my eyes open (번쩍 떠 보앗다).—
"When I woke up startled, seeing you standing there smiling (빙그레-)—I thought you were the devil (惡魔)!... H, your smile always seems mocking (嘲笑), but just then, I was truly angry (火가 나서)..."

Actually (實上), what annoyed me most when I woke up earlier was not the bad dream itself, but H standing there smiling mockingly. <125>
"...But no matter how much I think about it, the strange (희한한) thing is, I had my eyes closed from the beginning, yet how did I know the owner of that 'hand' was female (女性)? Even I can't figure it out..."

After finishing the story, I felt an inexplicable melancholy (憂愁) welling up (치미는 것 가타야서) in my chest. I ran down to the riverside where we had sat coming up and washed my face (洗手).

3
When we arrived (到着) in Namp'o, it was already well past 2 in the afternoon (午後). Y, who had been out (出入하얏던), had just come back (方今 들어와서), thrown off his clothes, and was sitting facing A, talking about someone he had just visited (尋訪). Seeing us, he jumped up as if surprised and welcomed us in. Y, who greeted us, seemed somewhat restless (坐不安席의 態度), for some reason (웬 細音인지). "Is P doing well? Weren't you planning to come up today or tomorrow... If you had called (電話) from P'yŏngyang, I would have gone to P'yŏngyang... You must be tired (困할테지)? Lunch (點心)?" He fired off questions (質問) in no particular order (順序업는), without giving us a chance to answer. I responded briefly (簡單簡單히 應對) and lay down, saying I was sleepy (졸립다고). Y, while seemingly thinking about something else, tried to liven up the mood (座中의 興을 도으랴 애를 쓰듯이), glancing from person to person, his lips twitching (입을 쭝긋쭝긋). Looking over at me, he said,
"...What's wrong? Where has all the vigor (元氣) of our time (當代) gone?... What about the usual gourd bottle (例의 瓢蕈)? Haha."
"Well..., I'm getting a bit tired (厭症) of that too, now..." I managed a weak smile.
"I did bring it this far, though!" Still lying down, I grabbed the overcoat (外套) I had taken off, pulled the whiskey («휘스키-») bottle (甁), half-finished on the train, from the pocket (주머니), and held it out. Everyone laughed (하하하 우스면서) and looked down at me lying silently (잠자코).

"But this is serious. You've felt the impermanence of all things (諸行無常을 感하얏다)... Does this mean the cancellation (取消-ㄴ가) of 'No Gourd Bottle, No Life'?" Y laughed, feelingly (흘흘 느끼며), perhaps exaggerating (多少 誇張한 듯이) a bit.
"So, what is this 'gourd bottle' (瓢蕈)?" A, not understanding the context (영문), asked Y, then turned his head towards me.
"Hehehe, to put it simply (쉽게 說明하면), first (爲先), it's X himself (自身), and at the same time, it's X's liquor bottle (술甁), symbolizing (씸볼한) X's view of life (人生觀)..." <126>
"Huh? X's view of life... and at the same time, X himself... what? Gee, can a guy like me understand that?" A counted on his fingers, then just laughed.
"No, it's not like that. Recently (日前), in Seoul (暑鬱?), I went to a shop (商店) and saw a glass sake bottle (琉璃 正宗甁) shaped like a gourd (瓢蕈 貌樣). I liked it, so I bought it, and now everyone teases me. Hahaha." I explained it this way and laughed it off.

"But should we take this liquor to the Teacher and listen to his lecture?" Y held up the bottle (甁), looked at the characters written on the label (레텔에 씨인 글字), and laughed.
"So there's a gourd bottle in Namp'o too..." H asked, laughing.
"Yes! But the glass of the bottle (甁 琉璃) is a bit cloudy (흐려(曇))... Frosted glass (擦硝子)—like surigarasu («스리가라쓰» - Japanese), perhaps?" Everyone roared with laughter (와하하하하며). I lay down with my eyes closed, listening to the conversation, but sleep wouldn't come, so I sat up again.
"Mr. A must be part of the Gourd Bottle Party (瓢蕈黨) too, right?" I lifted the whiskey bottle, poured a glass, offered it to him, and received a return cup (返杯를 바닷다).

"So, how is the gourd bottle here?" H seemed to be looking at me, but I was drinking my glass and didn't see.
"...He doesn't particularly carry a gourd bottle around, but there's this great architect (大建築) who built a 3-story house for 3 yen and 50 sen..." Y replied to H.
"For 3 yen 50 sen? Hahaha, he must be crazy?" H laughed.
"Well, maybe he is crazy... But I think he has monopolized the highest happiness (最高 幸福) in life..." Y answered seriously (眞面目).

While Y and H were talking, I exchanged a few questions and answers with A about the magazine world, but then joined their conversation when H called me over to listen.
"It's not about liquor, but there's a great philosopher (大哲人) who built a 3-story house for 3 yen and 50 sen..." Y explained again, then noticing the bottle was empty, said, "The liquor's gone. Shall I buy some whiskey?" and called a servant to order it.
"He's right. Whether a philosopher turned into a mole (땅두더지로 換腸을 하엿거나), or a great man (偉人) fell from heaven (한울서 떨어젓거나), whether he built a 30-story house for 3 yen or even 3 sen, who knows!... At least in my eyes, I don't see any book of philosophy (哲學書) greater than the gourd bottle..." I said with a cold smile (冷笑) and turned again to A.
"But why do you drink?"
"There's no logic (論理). Just to get drunk (醉하랴고)."
"Exactly... You couldn't attach yourself to anything. You couldn't find satisfaction (滿足) in anything. In the end (結局), there was nothing but alcohol (알코-ㄹ) <127> outside. It's painful (悲痛하고) and tragic (悲慘), but you found solace (慰安)... It's definitely not happiness (幸福). But if there were something that could provide not just solace but happiness, an effect (效果) greater than alcohol, without borrowing the power of alcohol—what would you choose? Hahaha..."
"An effect greater than alcohol?... Madness (狂)? Or conviction (信念)?—There can be nothing else but these two... But as long as the five senses (五官) are clear (明確),... eh-, as long as there's nothing but fatigue (疲勞), ennui (倦怠), disappointment (失望)... —though if one is fated (宿命) to end one's life as a madman, there's nothing to be done—isn't it unavoidable?" As the liquor (酒氣) took effect, I became more and more agitated (興奮) and, unconsciously (不知不識間에), finished my sentence with emphasis (악쎈트) on each word in a distinct oratorical tone (演說 語調).
"Anyway (何如間), let's drink first (爲先). Mr. A—here." I passed (傳하얏다) the cup to A.

"But X! When I heard the Teacher's sermon (說敎), flavored (加味한) with Tolstoyism (톨스토이슴) and Wilsonism (윌손이슴), I felt envious." Y, short of breath (숨에 弱한), his face already red, turned to A seeking agreement.
"He wasn't feeling well today (神氣가 不便하야)—his voice was hoarse from lecturing daily (連日 講演), so he said he couldn't talk. It was suffocating (氣가 막혀서)... hahaha." A brought up the story of when he and Y visited the 3-story house.
"Even without hearing it, he's probably just spouting stuff about world peace or love for humanity..." I said, moving to the upper part of the room (웃묵) and lying down.

In the lower part of the room (알엣묵), the conversation about the owner of the 3-story house, centered around Y, went on endlessly (끗이 아니낫다). Occasionally (가다 가다), bursts of laughter (와-하고 터져나오는 웃음소리) would startle me out of the light sleep I was drifting into (소르르 오던 잠이 깨고 깨고 하다가), until finally (終乃) I lost sleep altogether and started listening intently (귀를 기울이게 되엇다). When Y, lifting both feet and spinning around on his bottom (엉덩이로 이리저리 맴을 돌면서), imitated (흉내를 내는) the 3-story house owner boasting that entry and exit (出入) were completely free (自由自在) even though his house had no door, everyone laughed, making me open my eyes and sit up too.

Slightly drunk (若干 醉氣가 올은), wanting some fresh air, and anyway (於此彼에) planning (作定) to go to P'yŏngyang to spend the night, I felt like visiting the vicinity of the 3-story house (3層집 집아리) on the way to the station (停車場).
"Shall we go take a look?" I asked Y.
"Well, isn't it a bit late?" Y replied, pulling out his watch.
"It's not quite five yet... But he probably won't lecture! As you can see, he's got construction work (役事) spread out (버려 노코) and his throat is hoarse from lecturing every day..." he said, mimicking (임내를 내이면서) the man, and laughed (하하하 우섯다).
The four young men, somewhat fatigued (若干 疲勞를 感한 듯이) from several hours of boisterous talk and laughter (哄笑喧談), all fell silent and walked sluggishly (느럭느럭) out onto the main street, which was suddenly becoming bustling (繁雜하야 오는) in the evening sun (夕陽판에). <128>

**Summary**

The narrator, suffering from severe ennui, nervous exhaustion, and disturbing recurring imagery of a dissected frog, travels from Kyŏngsŏng to Namp'o via P'yŏngyang with his friend H, seeking escape. His psychological distress, marked by insomnia and suicidal ideation (symbolized by a razor), persists despite the journey and temporary relief from alcohol and the train ride. In P'yŏngyang, they visit historical sites like Pubyŏk Pavilion and Ŭlmil Terrace, where the narrator reflects on existential angst and the absurdity of seeking fame. In Namp'o, they meet friends Y and A, discussing a local eccentric who built a 3-story house for ¥3.50 and embodies a different philosophy of life, contrasting sharply with the narrator's alcohol-fueled despair and intellectual disillusionment, even as he dismisses concepts like Tolstoyism and Wilsonism mentioned by his friends. The story ends with the group heading out, slightly drunk, to see this eccentric's house.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the psychological landscape of Korean intellectuals during the early colonial period (1921). The narrator's profound sense of alienation, fatigue, and morbid fascination can be interpreted as reflecting the broader societal malaise and cultural displacement under Japanese rule. The mention of required quarantine injections, train travel between major cities, the presence of Japanese cultural elements (like frosted glass terminology), and intellectual discussions involving Western philosophies (Tolstoyism, Wilsonism) alongside traditional landmarks paints a picture of a society navigating modernity and colonial imposition. The contrast between the narrator's intellectual suffering and the reported simple "happiness" of the eccentric builder might suggest a critique of intellectualism detached from lived reality or a search for alternative ways of being within the constrained colonial context.

소설 표본실의 청개고리
						廉想涉
묵업은 氣分의 沈滯와 限업시 늘어진 生의 倦怠는 나가지 안는 나의 발길을 南浦까지 끌어 왓다.
歸省한 後, 7, 8個 朔間의 不規則한 生活은 나의 全身을 海綿가티 짓두들겨 노핫슬 뿐 아니라 나의 魂魄까지 蠶食하얏다. 나의 몸을 어대를 두드리던지 「알코」과 「니코진」의 毒臭를 내뿜지 안는 곳이 업슬만치 疲勞하얏다. 더구나 6, 7月 盛夏를 지내고 겹옷 입을 때가 되어서는 節期가 急變하야갈스록 몸을 추스리기가 겨워서 洞里 散步에도 식은 땀을 술술 흘리고 親故와 이악이를 하랴면 두 세 마듸째부터는 목침을 차잣다.
그러면서도 무섭게 昻奮한 神經만은 잠자리에서도 눈을 뜨고 잇섯다. 두 홰 세 홰 울 때까지 업치락 뒤치락하다가 東이 번히 트는 것을 보고 겨우 눈을 부치는 것이 1週間이나 넘은 뒤에는 불을 끄고 들어 눕지를 못하얏다.
그 中에도 나의 머리에 膠着하야 불을 끄고 누엇슬 때나 從容히 안젓슬 때마다 苛酷히 나의 神經을 掩襲하야 오는 것은 解剖된 개고리가 四肢에 핀을 박고 七星板 우에 잣바진 形狀이다.-내가 中學校 2年 時代에 博物實驗室에서 鬚髥 텁석부리 先生이, 靑개고리를 解剖하야가지고 더운 김이 모락모락 나는 五臟을 次例次例로 끌어 내서 자는 아기 누이듯이 酒精甁에 채운 後에 大發見이나 한 듯이 擁圍하고 서서 잇는 生徒들을 둘러다 보며＜118＞
「자-여러분, 이래도 아즉 살아잇는 것을 보시오.」 하고 뾰죽한 바늘 끗으로 여긔저긔를 꾹꾹 찔르는대로 五臟을 빼앗긴 개고리는 잰저리를 치며 四肢에 못박힌 채 발딱발딱 苦悶하는 貌樣이엇다.
8年이나 된 그 印象이 요사이 새삼스럽게 생각이 나서 아모리 이저버리랴고 애를 써도 아니되엇다...샛파한 「메쓰」, 닭이똥만한 옴을옴을하는 心臟과 弊, 바늘끗, 조고만 戰慄...次例次例로 생각날 때마다 머리 끗이 쭈뻣쭈뻣하고 全身에 冷水를 끼언지는 것 가타엿다. 南向한 琉璃窓 밋에서 번쩍 쳐드은 「메쓰」의 强烈한 反射光이 眼孔을 찌르는 것 가타야 컴컴한 房 속에 들어 누웟서도 꼭 감은 눈섭 밋이 부시엿다. 그러나 그럴 때마다 머리맛에 노힌 冊床 舌盒 속에 느허둔 面刀칼이 操心이 되어서 못견디엇다.
내가 南浦에 가던 前夜에는 그 症이 더욱 甚하얏다. -間半통박게 아니되는 房에 놉히 매달은 電燈불이 부시어서 꺼버리면 또 다시 歡迎에 괴롭지나 안흘가 하는 念慮가 업지 안핫스나 心事가 나서 우통을 벗은 채로 벌떡 일어나서 「스위취」를 비틀고 누엇다. 그러나 「쨍웅」하는 소리가 門틈으로 스러저 나가자 또 머리를 掩襲하야 오는 것은 鬚髥 텁석부리의 「메쓰」, 舌盒 속의 面刀다. 「메쓰」-面刀, 面刀-「메쓰」..., 이즈랴면 이즈랴 할스록 끈적끈적하게도 떠러지지 안코 어느 때까지 꼬리를 물고 머리 속에서 돌아단이엇다. 今時로 손이 舌盒으로 갈 듯 갈 듯 하야 참을 수가 업섯다. 怪異한 魔力은 抑制하랴면 할스록 漸漸 더하야왓다. 스르르 舌盒이 열리는 소리가 나서 소스라처 눈을 뜨면 덧門 안다든 窓이 부여케 보일 뿐이요 房 속은 如前히 暗黑에 沈寂하얏다. 非常한 恐怖가 全身에 壓到하야 손끗 하나 까딱어릴 수 업스면서도 異常한 魅力과 誘惑은 絶頂에 達하얏다.
「내가 미첫나?...아니ㅡ 미츠랴는 徵兆인가.」 혼자 머리 속에 부르즈젓다.
나는 잠에 醉한 놈 모양으로 입울을 와락 차던지고 일어나서 舌盒에 손을 대엇다. 그러나, 「그래도 손을 대엇다가...」하는 생각이 電雷와 가티 머리에 번쩍할 際, 기픈 꿈에서 깨인 것 가티 精神이 반짝 나서 電燈을 켜랴다가 成陽桶을 더듬어 차젓다.-한 개피를 드윽켜 들고 窓틀 우에 언저둔 洋燭을 집어 내려서 부처논 後 舌盒을 열엇다. 쓰다가 몃 달 동안이나 굴려 둔 原稿, 片紙, 藥匣들이 休紙桶가티 우글우글한 속을 부스럭부스럭＜119＞ 하다가 미끈하고 잡히는 자루에 집어 너흔 面刀를 外面을 하고 끄어내서 창밧그로 뜰에 내던젓다. 그러나 亦是 잠은 못들엇다. 脈이 확 풀리고 이마에는 식은 땀이 비저 나왓다. 屍體가튼 몸을 苦悶 뒤의 病人처럼, 四肢를 축 늘이털여노코 가만히 누어 생각하얏다.-
「如何間 이 房을 免하여야 하겟다.」 지긋지긋한 듯이 房 안을 휘익 돌아다보앗다. 어대던지 旅行을 하랴는 생각은 벌서 數朔 前부터의 計劃이엇지만 여름에 한 번 놀아본, 新興寺에도 간다는 말 뿐이요 이때것 實現은 못되엇다.
「어대던지 가야하겟다. 世界의 끗까지. 無限에. 永遠히. 발끗 자라는 대까지...無人島-西伯利亞의 荒凉한 벌판! 몸에서 기름이 부지직 부지직 타는 南洋!...아-아.」
나는 그림葉書에서 본 鬱鬱한 森林, 椰子樹 밋에 안즌 裸體의 蠻人을 생각하고 痛快한 듯이 어깨를 으쓱하얏다. 單 1分의 停車도 아니하고 땀을 벌벌 흘리며 힘잇는 굿센 숨을 헐덕헐덕 쉬이는 「푸-ㄹ, 스피-드」의 汽車로 永遠히 달리고 십다.-이것이 나의 무엇보다도 渴求하는 바이엇다... 만일 타면, 眩氣가 나리라는 念慮만 업섯스면 飛行機! 飛行機! 하며, 혼자 조하하햣슬지도 몰랏다.

내 數朔間이나 집을 못떠나고 들어 안젓는 것은 金錢의 拘碍가 第一 原因이엇지마는 事實 大門 밧게 나서랴도 좀처럼 하야서는 쉽지 안핫다.
그 翌日, H가 와서 오늘은 꼭 떠날 터이니 同行을 하자고 平壤 訪問을 勸할 때에는 지긋지긋한 京城의 雜沓을 등지고 다른 氣分을 어드랴는 慾求와 長短을 不拘하고 하여간 汽車를 타게 된 好奇心에 껄리어서, 「응, 가지 가지.」하며 덥허노코 同意는 하얏스나 인제 正말 떠날 때가 되어서는 떠나고 십흔지 고만 두어야 조흘지 自己의 心中을 몰라서 어떠케 된 細音 모르고 H에게 껄려 南大門驛까지 何如間 나왓다. 列車는 아즉 到着치 안핫스나 乘客은 入場하는 中이엇다. 나도 急히 票를 사 가지고 催促하는 H를 따라섯다. 時間이라는 勢力이 好不好 肯不肯을 不問하고 모든 것을 不可抗力 下에서 獨斷하야 껄고 가게 된 것을 나는 오히려 大幸이 알고＜120＞ 되어가는 대로라고 생각하며 하나式 풀려나가는 行列 뒤에 섯섯다. 그러나 檢疫證明書가 업다고 改札口에서 H와 詰難이 되는 것을 보고 나는 列車에서 벗어나서 또 다시 아니가겟다고 하얏다.
心事가 난 H는 마음대로 하라고 뿌리치며 혼자 出張注射室로 向하다가 돌쳐와서 가티 껄고 들어갓다.
히스테리크한 看護婦가 注射침을 들고 덤벼들 際, 나는 반쯤 거더 올렷던 「샤쓰」를 내리우며 돌아서 마조섯다. 看護婦의 핀잔과 H의 催促에 마지못하야 눈을 딱 감고 한 대 마즌 後 惶惶히 플래트포-ㅁ으로 들어가서 車에 올랏다. 車에 올라 안저서도 空然히 後悔를 하고 안젓섯스나 强烈한 「휘스키-」의 힘과 激甚한 全身의 動搖 反撥, 轟轟한 軋響, 暗黑을 突破하는 速力, 注射 마즌 肩의 沈痛... 모든 官能을 一時에 踊躍케 하는 刺戟의 渦中에서 모든 것을 잇고 새벽에는 쿨쿨 자리만치 마음이 가라안젓다. 德澤으로 오날 밤에는 「메쓰」도 번쩍어리지 안코 面刀도 뛰어 나오지 안핫다.
東이 틀락말락 하야서 우리들은 平壤驛에 나렷다. 南浦行은 아즉 2, 30分이나 잇는 故로 우리들은 洗面所에서 洗手를 하고 待合室로 나왓다. 나는 부석부석한 붉은 눈을 내리깔고 「쏘파」끗에 안젓다가 벌떡 일어나며
「난, 예서 좀 돌아단일테니...」내던지듯이 한 마디를 불숙 하고 H를 마조 쳐다보다가,
「혼자 가서, C君 맛나보고 오늘이라도 가티 이리 나오면 맛나보고, 그러치 안흐면 혼자 돌아단이다가 밤車로 갈테야.」 하며 H의 對答도 듯지 안코 돌아서 나왓다.
「응? 뭐야? 그 왜 그래... 또 미친症이 난게로군.」 하며 H는 벗어 들엇던 레인코-트를 뒤집어 쓰면서 조차 나와 붓든다.
「...사람이 보기 실혀서...事實, C君과 맛나기로 別로 이악이할 것도 업고...哀願하듯이 힘업는 口調로 하다가,
「永遠히 흘러가고 십다. 끗업는대로...」혼자말처럼, 한 마듸 한 마듸 힘을 주어 말을 맷고 훌적 나와 버렷다. H도 하는 수 업시 테-불에 노핫던 「투렁크」를 들고 딸아 나왓다.＜121＞
우리 兩人은 大同江가로 길을 차저 나와서, 浮碧樓로 훤히 東이 틀가말가 한 컴컴한 길을 소리업시 거럿다. 一周하고 나려 오다가 鍾路에서 朝飯을 사 먹고 또 다시 浮碧樓로 向하얏다. 開市를 하고 門前에 물을 뿌린 뒤에 新聞을 펴들고 안젓는 것은 淸凉하고 幸福스럽게 보엿다. 아까 나려올 際는 綾羅島 저 便 地平線에서 朱紅의 火焰을 뿜으며 날름날름하던 아츰 해가 벌서 水源地 烟桶 우에 올라서, 川邊植木 밋으로 거러가는 우리의 겻뺨을 눈이 부시게 내리쪼엿다.
이ㅅ솔을 물고 바위 우에 섯는 사람, 手巾을 물에 잠그고 洗漱하는 사람들도 間或 눈에 띄엇다. 나는 발을 멈추고 無心히 나려다 보다가, 自己도 산뜻한 물에 손을 잠가 보고 십흔 생각이 나서 야튼 곳을 골라서 물가로 뛰여 나려갓다.
쪼차 나려와시 가티 손을 잠그고 안젓던 H는,
「X君, 午後車로 가지?...」

이때에 마츰, 뒤동뚝에서 누군지 이리로 漸漸 가까히 나려오는 발자최를 듯고 우리는 무심히 흘긋 돌아다 보앗다.
마른 곳을 골라 드듸느라고, 이리저리 뛸 때마다, 등에까지 철철 내리덥흔 長髮을 눈이 옴폭 패인 하얀 얼굴 뒤에서 펄석펄석 날리우면서, 압흐로 가까히 오는 形狀은 東京 近處에서 보던 美術家가 아닌가 疑心하얏다. 이 奇怪한 머리의 所有者는 너이들의 存在는 나의 意識에 오르지도 안는다는 驕慢心으로인지 或은 一身에 集注하는 모든 視線을 避하랴는 無關心의 態度로인지는 모르겟스나 何如間 右手에는 짤막한 대ㅅ개비(竹竿)를 前後로 흔들면서, 발끗만 나려다보며 내 등 뒤를 지나, 한 間 통쯤 上流로 올라가 자리를 잡고 안젓다. 彼도 우리와 가티 손을 물에 성큼 너코 불적불적 소리를 내더니 양추를 한 번 하고 벌덕 일어나서 大同門을 向하야＜122＞ 성큼성큼 거러간다. 帽子도 아니 쓴 長髮과, 돌돌 말린 때무든 박이두루막이 자락은 오른便 손가락에 끼우고 巧妙히 들리는 대 柯枝와 長短을 마처서 풀풀풀풀 날리엇다.
오늘은 꽤 일꾼.」
「핫하! 朝飯이나 約條하야둔 대가 잇느게지.」 하며 長髮客을 돌아서 보다가 서로 嘲笑하는 소리를 뒤에 두고 우리는 손을 씻으면서 동뚝으로, 올라왓다.
