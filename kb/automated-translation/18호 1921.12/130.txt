Magazine Title: 개벽 제18호
Publication Date: 1921년 12월 01일
Article Title: 對話, 苦學의 路 (Dialogue, The Path of Studying While Working)
Author: 春坡 (Ch'unp'a)
Article Type: 세태비평 (Social Commentary)

Dialogue, The Path of Studying While Working
						Ch'unp'a
Characters: Students C and K.
Place: Boarding House in Kyŏngun-dong.
Time: Night of November 20th.

C. Ah- Mr. K. Are you just coming now? The night has gotten quite deep. It must be past midnight, right? So, how many boxes did you sell?
K. How many boxes – what are you talking about? I just suffered terribly and came back empty-handed. I can't even do this *kohak* (studying while working). It can't be helped. Starting tomorrow...
C. Why do you say that? You couldn't sell even one box?
K. What's the point of selling just one or two boxes of that stuff? I suffer as I suffer, receive contempt as I receive contempt... Ah – how can this world be so cold?
C. Did you only just realize this world is cold? Why talk of suffering when you're in the position of a *kohaksaeng* (student working their way through school)? Contempt! Even if we receive contempt upon contempt, we have no choice but to strive and fight against the world. When we left home and headed here, didn't we anticipate this kind of future outcome...? So, what kind of contempt did you receive? Tell me about it. Let me hear it too for reference.
K. Hey. Why on earth are there so many *kohaksaeng*? Go to Chongno, there are *kohaksaeng*; go to a secluded alley, there are *kohaksaeng*. In the detached servants' quarters (사랑, sarang) of salt merchants' houses, there are *kohaksaeng*; in the taverns, there are *kohaksaeng*. Everything visible seems to be a *kohaksaeng*. We are one of the *kohaksaeng* too... Since there are so many *kohaksaeng*, how can we not receive contempt? This person is told to buy medicine, that person is told to buy medicine. They see the same person again and are pestered again, they see the person who gave before and are asked for more again, so truthfully, it must be annoying and probably hateful. Unless someone comes out with a pocketful of money specifically to buy medicine from *kohaksaeng*, how can they buy from every student they meet? Thinking about it from their perspective, it seems likely. But hey. Where else could there be such a place of resentment and frustration? To be called a thief... Ah – it truly is heartbreaking.<91>
C. Called a thief?
K. Look here. I wandered around the park and Chongno all night, suffering miserably, and couldn't sell even one box of medicine. So, having no choice, as I had to prepare for morning, ignoring my dignity and feeling ashamed, I went into some tavern serving both inside and outside customers behind Chongno. At a glance, it seemed every room was full of drinkers. So I gently opened a room door and said, "Excuse me. Please buy just one box of medicine. I am a *kohaksaeng*." I spoke deferentially, as if standing right before a high official. Then, a fellow who was sitting there embracing either a mistress (蝎蜅) or a young woman suddenly yelled like thunder, "Close the door! What medicine are you talking about while people are drinking? Not buying! What kind of *kohak* is this?" and made a scene. So, something surged up in my chest, and without saying anything more, I slammed the room door shut and turned to leave. Then that fellow said, "They pretend to be so-called *kohaksaeng* and go around intending to steal things like shoes," and shouted, "Go see if he didn't take the shoes!" calling for the owner's girl. I was so dumbfounded I just laughed "Heh heh" and left, but really, where else could the world be this desolate? That guy, who throws bundles of silver and paper money into a woman's bosom and drinks and eats until his belly bursts, adding the insult of 'thief' to someone who goes through ascetic practices morning and evening, painstakingly earning one penny, two pennies to supplement tuition fees... Ah – truly...
C. You really are still naive. Is this the first time you've heard yourself called a thief? I've lost count of how many times. If your spirits get crushed by words like that, how can you manage *kohak*? Of course, it's frustrating... But as long as you're not a thief in your conscience, that's all that matters. When we sling our medicine boxes (藥加房) over our shoulders and step outside the door, didn't we anticipate encountering many such situations? Encountering all sorts of strange situations. Situations unbearable for a human being to receive... But hey. Being called a thief, know that it is a great lesson for us. Humans invariably live by instinct and impulse. Every time we have such an impulse, we can take one step further forward. As you said, we learn how cold this world is, we learn that there are many such sordid things in this world, and we can also harbor dissatisfaction and come to struggle. Look at Han Sin (韓信) who begged for food from the washerwoman (漂母). Also look at Mr. Harding (하-딩), who became President from being a printing worker. Although our circumstances today are like this and our lives are like this, I believe it is definitely not misfortune.<92> The taste of being in adversity is the best thing for a person. Though the present moment seems wretched, how wonderful will that time be when we reach our destination and look back on the past?
K. Well, yes, that's true. But how does the heart feel at the moment one endures it? Studying, whatever, I just want to throw it all away and live by my fists... Anyway, how did it go for you? You said you were visiting Mr. P?
C. I was also unsuccessful. A guy like us can't even get a visit. Whether it works out or not, one has to at least meet him. I go ten, twenty times, but I can never meet him. Whether he's there and avoiding me, or not there and they won't let me in.
K. Really? Mr. P wasn't there?
C. It seems he was there. Judging by the servants' (下人) behavior, as I entered the gatehouse and asked, "Is the teacher in?" some woman, maybe the housekeeper, came out and asked, "Who is it?" I said I was a student from OO School and came to see the teacher. Without asking further questions, she went straight inside, and after a while came out saying, "He's not in." What could I do? I couldn't search like a detective. I had no choice but to turn back. Like a defeated rooster (숫닭), shoulders slumped, I walked for a while, then Mr. K who works at OO Company came to mind. I heard he is very close to people like us and has a lot of sympathy. So I turned back and went to the inn (旅館) where he was staying. When going, my heart was quite determined, thinking I would definitely get at least a few Won from him, but when I reached the inn gate, my courage just shrank. I hesitated for a long time, wondering whether to go in or not, but didn't have the courage to turn back again, so I just sighed "Hoo-" and turned away. Walking for a while, I thought again, 'Where could there be such a pathetic loser (拙者) like me? Coming all the way to the inn gate and turning back without even looking for him. Where could there be such a pathetic person? And you call this *kohak*? And you fight against this world with this?' 'Agh, let's try going one more time,' I thought and turned back again. This time, I hardened my resolve and strode straight into the gatehouse without hesitation. My face flushed, and I felt hot-headed (上氣). Fearing I'd make a pathetic scene again if I hesitated, I quickly called out, "Is anyone there?" Then, a girl about ten years old came out. So I asked if Mr. K was in, and she said yes. Hearing he was in made me feel even more timid (拙氣). So I quickly adjusted my clothes (알에 위 衣服), took off my hat,<93> entered the gatehouse and asked, "Is Mr. K in?" Just then, he came straight out of the restroom, saying, "Eh, who is it? I am the person called K," and stared at me intently. I felt startled as if by something, my heart pounded, my face flushed hot, and no words came out. Head bowed low, I hesitated for a long while, then mustered all my final courage and managed to utter one sentence with difficulty, "I am a *kohaksaeng*. I came to see you for a moment, Teacher." At that, his expression changed slightly, and in a very respectful tone, he said, "Yes- Is that so. Please come in anyway," and invited me into his room. So I followed him in and knelt in a corner. He carefully observed my face, asked thoroughly about my residence, name, age, school, and how I managed to study. I told him my situation truthfully – about selling medicine, receiving assistance of a few Won each from Mr. So-and-so, and mentioned the tuition fee problem, asking him for 2 Won per month (每朔). He thought about it this way and that for a long time, then saying he too was in a poor situation, though his heart wanted to sympathize fully, due to material difficulties, 2 Won per month was utterly impossible (到底 不能), and he only had 1 Won left over, gave me 1 Won, and told me to explain my situation to other teachers. So, feeling grateful even for that (感之德之), I couldn't say anything more and quickly left the gate with a word of "Thank you." How much sweat was flowing from my forehead and limbs. Ah – truly – I can't do this.
K. Still, you were successful. Getting even 1 Won. I only got 10 chŏn.
C. Hey. Let's completely overhaul our *kohak* policy. Just getting a few pennies of assistance (補助) here and there or selling a few boxes of medicine won't do at all. We just face wretched and shameful situations. With so many *kohaksaeng*, how can they always provide assistance, how can they always buy medicine? Instead, it just creates ill feelings (惡感情) in society.
K. How do you propose we change our policy? Sell steamed buns (饅頭)? Sell newspapers? Even if we change, isn't it just the same situation?
C. No. That's not what I mean by changing. I mean going to some company and working as a boy (뽀이), or going to some factory and working as a laborer (職工), or if not that, selling roasted chestnuts (군밤) or carrying a water A-frame (물지개), doing actual physical labor (肉體的 勞働) and relying entirely on our own strength (自力).<94>
K. Man, aren't medicine peddling and newspaper delivery physical labor? Who does it lying down?
C. Gosh, man, you're dense. Are you still saying that after talking about suffering all night for just 10 chŏn? And about there being too many medicine peddlers, being called a thief. It's because there's no decent income (收入) and what we do is too wretched (苟苟).
K. Then what should we do to get a decent income and not be wretched? Laborer? Boy? Is there any factory or company in our society that can accommodate (收容) us? No matter how I think about it.
C. Hey. There's a way. Let's sell roasted chestnuts. Someone told me they earn over 10 Won a day. After coming back from school during the day, start around 4 PM, roast and sell all night, we should earn at least three or four Won. It seems we could get a net profit (純利益) of at least about (假量) 1 Won. If the location is good...
K. How can two people live on 1 Won?
C. I mean a minimum of 1 Won, how could it only be 1 Won? Hey, no more words, let's try it. If not that, let's do the water carrying business. Delivering water to 30 households (戶) should be enough. We can get food in return.
K. That's even more shameful. How can we go from house to house begging for food? I'd rather quit studying and go back to the countryside...
C. Man. Still living too comfortably (호강). It seems better than asking this person and that person for a penny or two. And if that's really too wretched, I suggested selling roasted chestnuts.
K. Well. Let me think about it.
C. Okay – the night has gotten very deep. Let's sleep.
K. I can't sleep either. What's the point of sleeping this rotten sleep (썩어진 잠)? Ah – the moon is so bright. That moon must be shining on the window of my house too. Why is the wind blowing so fiercely? Tomorrow morning, the frost will probably be heavy. Today's newspaper said the Yalu River (鴨綠江) froze five chi thick. The days are gradually getting colder, what should we do about this? Do we have winter clothes? Do we have bedding? Do we have a mattress (褥)? All we have is one set of worn-out summer Western clothes... Why is there no news at all from my home? I've sent letters (片紙) several times, but there's not a single reply. It would be nice if they sent just one set of clothes... By the way, is there firewood to cook breakfast tomorrow morning? We're probably out of rice too.
C. Hey man. Don't worry. Would a disabled person live, would a beggar live? With strong bodies, surely we won't freeze or starve to death? No matter how desolate the world is. Let's clench our two fists<95> and shed blood and sweat. Let's see who wins, us or the world. Don't worry about the morning, I've prepared everything. The night is deep, let's sleep now.

C. Hey Mr. K, wake up. Let's go to Ch'wiun-jŏng. It's already struck seven o'clock. Come on – get up quickly. Huh? Mr. K.
K. Huh, already seven? It's late. We need to make breakfast, eat, and go to school. Won't we be late if we go to Ch'wiun-jŏng?
C. No. It doesn't matter if we're late. Because today is Sunday.
K. Oh! Right, that's true. Today is a day off. We rested yesterday too, so it's two days off in a row. Then let's go.
C. Let's each take a book. Find a quiet place, sit down, and study for a couple of hours before coming back. Once we come down, we probably won't have time to study with this and that.
K. Okay. But I need to go somewhere around nine o'clock.
C. Where?
K. To Mr. N's place in Chŏksŏn-dong.
C. Why to Mr. N's?
K. He said he'd lend me a set of Western clothes. Seeing how pitiful my appearance is... He told me to come much earlier, but I felt too sorry and didn't go... Now that things have come to this, what choice is there? I have no choice but to take and wear whatever I can get.
C. Then go see him. It's a kind deed, no matter what. He's also in the position of *kohak*... Anyway, hey, mend your Western clothes a bit. The flesh of your buttocks is showing as big as a pindaettŏk (mung bean pancake). If you go out like that, you'll be greatly embarrassed.
K. It's not my buttocks. What does it matter if I expose my genitals? Because I'm a have-not.
C. Still, it shows laziness to others. Come on – quickly mend it and let's go. The needle is here.
K. I guess I have no choice but to just stitch it up roughly. It would be nice if I had something to patch it with.
C. It doesn't matter if you just pull it together and stitch it... Here – tear my handkerchief, patch it, and sew it.
K. I can never do needlework in my life. As soon as I hold a needle, I start sweating, aigoo, it's slippery...
C. Give it here, I'll mend it for you.
K. Okay – just put in a few rough stitches somehow. By the way, about Mr. N. It seems hard to find someone as respectable as him.
C. Ah – undoubtedly difficult. Truly, he is a model figure in our *kohaksaeng* world. While working as a laborer (職工) in someone else's company, he studies diligently,<96> and has a kind heart too, truly – it's not easy—. We've probably received his help several times too, right?
K. Absolutely. Probably five or six times. Five *doe* of rice once, 1 Won three times, and also three packs of herbal medicine when you had a cold, and now he's even lending Western clothes...
C. Hey. While his kind intentions are certainly appreciated, he is *kohak*, we are *kohak*, he is a man, we are men too, so isn't it too pathetic a thing (못생긴 즛) to just receive help from him? From now on, let's also strive hard and help him even one bit (1分), but let's try not to receive his help. Because it shows we are too incompetent.
K. That's true too. But among ourselves, under genuine goodwill, we should help each other and love each other, so we have no choice but to help each other mutually. If he starves, we starve too; if we eat, he eats too. We have no choice but to help each other like this. The practice of communism (共産主義) is truly in our *kohaksaeng* world. Well, last month, didn't we seven or eight people evenly distribute the 10 Won that came to Mr. N?
C. Yes, that's the only way. There's no other choice. Okay – it's all mended. Hurry up, put it on, and let's go for a walk.
K. Hey, instead of that, let's quickly boil some food and eat before going. No need to come back to cook and eat and then go out again...
C. Well, that seems reasonable too. Okay – then go fetch water. I'll wash the rice.
K. But the inner clothes are in the bucket (빠깨트), what should I do? I guess I have no choice but to fetch water with the washbasin (洗手대야).
C. Man. How can you fetch water with a washbasin? Transfer the inner clothes to the washbasin and quickly fetch water with the bucket.
K. What should we have for side dishes (饌)? The radish is all gone too.
C. Here. Go buy about 5 *chŏn* worth of tofu. Let's fry it in the pot (남비). Just dissolve some salt in water.
K. Hey C君. Sun'gil's mother from the back house says she'll fetch a pail of water for us. How kind.
C. Truly – kind. She is quite a good person.
K. Ah – truly kind. When Sun'gil was sick with indigestion (食滯) before, I gave him a packet of Yŏngsimhwan (靈心丸), and she considers that a kindness (恩德) and talks about it whenever we meet. And yesterday morning, she came and said she would take our inner clothes and wash them herself.
C. Look here. As the proverb says, "A white fly must go for a black fly to come" (힌파리가 가야 검은 파리가 온다고). A packet of medicine turns out to be a great deed (功), doesn't it?
K. Indeed, there's no one who feels bad about giving. The world is clearly one of mutual aid (相扶相助), but...
C. The world is indeed Kropotkin's (구로뽀도킨) world. Just because there are a few powerful people unnecessarily... Ah- Sun'gil's mother, thank you for your trouble. Where could there be such kindness? You must be busy at your home (宅) too. Ah – thank you. ..(End)...<97>

**Summary**

This article presents a dialogue between two impoverished students, C and K, in 1921 Seoul, detailing their daily struggles to survive while pursuing education (*kohak*). They discuss the hardships of peddling medicine, facing constant rejection and humiliation, including being accused of theft. They contemplate alternative, potentially more reliable but equally challenging, forms of labor like selling roasted chestnuts or carrying water. Despite their desperate circumstances and the cold indifference of society, they exhibit resilience, debate strategies for survival, find solace in small successes like receiving a single Won or borrowing clothes, and recognize acts of kindness from neighbors and fellow students, hinting at a fragile system of mutual support within their community.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a vivid portrayal of the socio-economic hardships faced by students in colonial Korea. Historians might find valuable the specific details about the types of low-paying jobs available (*kohak*), the social dynamics between students and the public (including potential benefactors and ordinary citizens), the prevalence and perception of *kohaksaeng*, the internal debate about self-reliance versus seeking aid, and the nascent sense of solidarity and communal sharing ("communism") among struggling students. It provides a ground-level view of urban poverty and the challenges of pursuing education under colonial rule.

잡지명	개벽 제18호

필자	春坡
기사형태	세태비평

對話, 苦學의 路
						春坡
人物. 學生C와 K.
場所. 慶雲洞 合宿所.
때. 11月 20日夜.

C.   아- K君. 지금 오나. 밤이 매우 깁헛는데. 아마 子正이 되엇슬 걸? 그래 몃 匣이나 팔앗섯나?
K.   몃 갑이다 - 무언가. 죽도록 고생만 하고 빈 손으로 돌아왓네. 苦學도 못하겟데. 할 수 업시. 來日부터는...
C.   웨 그러나. 그래 한 匣도 못 팔앗서?
K.   그까짓 것. 한 匣 두 匣 팔면 무엇하나. 고생은 고생대로 하고 賤待는 賤待대로 밧고... 아 - 어쩌면 이 世上이 이다지도 冷冷한가.
C.   이 世上이 冷冷한 줄 이제야 알앗섯나. 苦學하는 處地에 고생 말은 웨하나. 賤待! 賤待아니야 又賤待를 바들지라도 期於코 奮鬪하야 世上과 더불어 싸울 밧게 업네. 우리가 집을 떠나 이 곳을 向할 때 毋論 이러한 來頭를 豫想한 것이 아닌가... 그래 賤待는 무슨 賤待를 바닷나? 말 좀 하게. 나도 參考삼아 좀 들어두세.
