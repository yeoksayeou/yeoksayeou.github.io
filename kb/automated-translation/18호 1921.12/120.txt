Article Title	社會諷刺 銀파리 (Social Satire: Silver Fly)
Author	목성 記
Article Type	세태비평 (Critique of the Times)

社會諷刺 銀파리
						목성 記
Although it is now late November, in the magazine world, it is already the year-end issue. If it were an old gentleman with a long bamboo pipe, it would be the time to stroke his white beard, saying, "Alas, this year too has already drawn to a close. How swift is this year's departure!"

Come what may, this year, full of talk and events, has already passed. Sitting down at the end of the year and looking back on the path trodden, don't you all realize that despite all your clamor and commotion, you have achieved nothing but repeating the same futile path?... How about it? In your slight desire to live better, in your slight desire to gain weight, how much did you deceive others this one year? How many lies did you skillfully tell?

The first birthday (生日) of the "Silver Fly" (銀파리) arrives with the New Year. How about letting the Silver Fly rest from the New Year? To live a bit better from the New Year, to try living without falsehood, won't you completely shake off the remaining falsehoods at this end of the year, as the last act of this year? Thus, shall we not bury our past forever with the setting sun and greet the joyful New Year only with good and true hearts?

Now then - let's finally uncover and eliminate a few bad things. <84>

Leaving aside the old man with gaiters on his ankles and a *kat* (갓) over his *t'anggon* (宕巾), even a fine gentleman dressed in a new-style Western suit, upon arriving at the gate of a friend's or acquaintance's house, will feign dignity and shout in a stiff voice,
"Come hither!" (이리 오너라아!)

This might be less so at the gate of a grand house seemingly full of servants (下人) and maids (婢僕), but even at a desperately poor house – a thatched cottage (草家집) with only two rooms, where only a couple renting monthly lives – knowing full well there are no servants to call and no such young children, he still shouts in an arrogant voice,
"Come hither!"

What is this if not utter hypocrisy (虛僞)? This kind of hypocrisy is absolutely absent among the ignorant laborers. It is a hypocritical act monopolized by the so-called educated, the so-called dignified class. The spectacle of claiming dignity while loudly calling "Come hither!" with no one to call is laughable, but the truly funny part comes next.

Here, at the gate of a small thatched house, stands a clean-looking, modern gentleman (書房님) in a Western suit, appearing to be around 30 years old, awkwardly forcing his voice to be deep and shouting, "Come hither!" Inside, a young wife of about twenty-seven or eight, who was alone, puts down her needlework, comes down to the yard, and reaches the middle gate. Then, loudly enough for the visitor outside to hear, she says,
"Ask where he has come from." (어대서 오셧나 여쭈어 보아라) She commands someone who isn't there. "Where he has come from" is fine, but adding "ask" (여쭈어 보라) is sheer nonsense. Still, the gentleman, as before,
"Ask if the master is in." (主人어른 계시냐고 여쭈어 봐라) tosses back the empty words. While uttering these nonsensical empty words themselves, neither feels the slightest bit awkward nor hears anything unusual, putting on a perfectly straight face. The wife peeps at the gentleman through a crack in the middle gate, while the gentleman, unaware that the wife is watching, stares only at the tips of his shoes, exchanging questions and answers through the void.

Tell him he is not in. (안 계시다고 여쭈어라.) Ask where he went. (어대가 섯느냐고 여쭈어 봐라.) Tell him I don't know. (모른다고 여쭈어라.) Ask what time he went out. (어느 때 나가섯느냐고 여쭈어 봐라.) Tell him he went out after breakfast. (아츰 잡숫고 나가섯다고 여쭈어라.) Ask about what time he will return. (어느 때쯤 들어오시느냐고 여쭈어 봐라.) Tell him I don't know. (몰르겟다고 여쭈어라.) Then ask her to tell him that Kim So-and-so stopped by. (그럼 알에 대 金아모가 다녀 갓다고 여쭈어 달나고 여쭈어라.) Tell him I will. (그러겟습니다고 여쭈어라.) Thus, they compete in saying "ask/tell him/her" (여쭈어라), exchanging nonsensical empty words for quite a while. Then, thinking they have maintained utmost dignity, the gentleman clears his throat with a "harrumph" and turns to leave. What a ridiculous act this is. <85>

The bad habit of the so-called *yangban* (兩班) who excessively work people even for unnecessary things – however, that involves ordering existing servants, so it can be forgiven to some extent. But for people who have neither servants nor children to stand face-to-face, setting up a non-existent person in the middle, pretending someone is there, and repeatedly uttering "ask/tell him/her" (여쭈어라) is, no matter how you think about it, a bad deed. There is absolutely no need to perform such useless and harmful acts. Whether one knows or not, even if a woman shouldn't meet face-to-face with another man, even if hiding behind the middle gate, it would suffice to clearly say "He is not in" loudly enough for the person outside to hear.

Recently, even when students or new gentlemen visit friends and directly call out "Mr. So-and-so," the wife inside runs out to the middle gate and adds "ask/tell him/her" (여쭈어라), saying "Tell him he's not in." When the visitor calls directly by name, "Mr. So-and-so," I want to see the brazen face of the person who, standing just a short distance away, adds the sound "ask/tell him/her" (여쭈이라) alone.

These things called 'humans,' full of cunning and cleverness, lay out such empty acts and useless waste, puff out their chests calling it dignity or whatnot. In that state, they brazenly chatter about "not taking even an inch of time lightly" (寸陰을 不可輕이니) or "an hour being worth a thousand pieces of gold" (一刻이 重千金이니).

In general, shouting "Come hither!" (이리 오너라아-) is bizarre. It's unfriendly, arrogant, lazy, and uneconomical. Doesn't everyone have a clear surname and given name? Whether it's Mr. So-and-so or Comrade So-and-so, whatever is fine, wouldn't it be good just to call the person concerned? If it's a house with a separate men's quarter (*sarang*), that's unnecessary too... What need is there to shout loudly, "Come hither!"? Pretending non-existent servants exist, feigning dignity with empty words during busy times – all life is mostly pretense.

Ultimately, people's lives (生) too are mostly just empty shells pretending to be meaningful lives.

Among the words people use daily, multiple times a day, there is another laughable thing. It's a contradiction, truly laughable, yet people use it daily without noticing or paying attention. It is this:
Regarding grains (穀物), selling (賣) and buying (買) are reversed. Since when this usage started, or when the words got switched, people just continue to say they "sold" (팔아온다) rice when they actually paid money and received rice. Furthermore, looking at whether everyone does this, it seems not; only people in the Kyŏnggi (京畿), Ch'ungch'ŏng (忠淸), and Chŏlla (全羅) regions seem to use words this way. <86>

If people were clever, wouldn't this have been corrected long ago, and the terms for buying and selling unified?

Though it's people's business and I don't need to know the details, if I were to relate what I've heard:
In the initial era of barter (物物交換), people in the countryside often took rice from their homes to go out and acquire other goods. That is, when they went out with grain, they would have said they were going "to buy." Now, money has emerged and become the standard medium for buying and selling, but in the countryside, money was scarce while rice or grain was available at home, so they might have said they were "buying" money with grain. Thus, based on the grain standard, buying became called selling, and selling became called buying, and this usage persists to this day – this explanation sounds plausible. However, even if that explanation is correct, now that money has become the standard for all economic activity, this usage should naturally be corrected.

However, among the other stories I've heard, the most laughable and pathetic one is this:
Among the stiff-necked *yangban* (兩班) families in Kyŏnggi or Ch'ungch'ŏng provinces, especially the poor ones – when a guest was sitting in the room, the *yangban*, conscious of his status, couldn't possibly admit to buying (買食) one or two measures of rice because they had no food, lest others see. So, while going to buy rice, to make it appear to the guest that he was selling rice piled up in the storehouse to get spending money, he would tell the guest within earshot that he was going "to sell" (팔아 오라) it. Thus, forever, out of shame (昌皮) for a *yangban* to buy one or two *seung* of rice, buying was called selling.

While this cannot be seen as the sole reason, look at the deceptive cunning of people pretending to have what they don't. Humans are conveniently equipped as clever beings (靈物) for pretending and fabricating.
Pretending this, pretending that – 'people' who have only grown skilled in fabricating pretenses, when will they ever manage to live a life without falsehood?

Pretending to have what one doesn't, pretending to know what one doesn't, pretending to have done what one hasn't – this is not limited to socialites, the wealthy, or all sorts of hustlers (挾雜軍). Even among the people and classes presumed to be the most truthful, most honest, and indeed ought to be, pretense (체) is piled up.
There's no need to tell many stories, but let's just tell one and conclude.

In a certain newspaper (新聞) dated November 6th, on page 3 (in a passage from a story serialized over several days <87>), something like this was written:
...The snow fell mercilessly, piling up over a foot high on the ground, creating a silver world. Moreover, the moon, without a speck of dust, contrasted with the white snow, truly radiant beyond measure. This indeed was 'moon white, snow white, heaven and earth white; mountain deep, water deep, traveler's sorrow deep' (月白雪白天地白 山深水深客愁深)...

Reading up to here, it seems heavy white snow (白雪) has piled up over a foot, and a bright moon (皎皎한 明月) illuminates heaven and earth. It appears to be a situation perfectly suited for quoting the line "Moon white, snow white, heaven and earth white; mountain deep, night deep (山深夜深) or water deep (水深) or traveler's sorrow deep" (客愁深). However, reading the next part:
Midnight arrived, all sounds were silent (萬賴俱寂), and all around was quiet, but miserably, large snowflakes (함박눈) were pouring down.

Seeing this, the bright moon (明月) that was just described as 'moon white' (月白) suddenly disappears, and abruptly, large snowflakes pour down. However, let's generously assume the weather at that time was fickle, causing the moon to hide suddenly and snow to pour down, and read the next part:
At this time, the groom, this person, leaping into the fire(? 열화에 뛰어 - likely figurative 'impetuously'), disregarding everything entirely, that night, crunching through the deep snow (깊은 눈을 사박사박 밟아가며), shielding himself from the pouring snow with an umbrella, passed the mountain bend, accompanied by his own shadow reflected on the snow...

Reaching this point, one cannot figure out what is going on. Suddenly the moon is bright, then large snowflakes pour down like crazy, and then a shadow is cast. Did the sky have two moons? Did the moon and snow perform magic? The writer is one thing, but the readers who are made to read this are in a pitiful situation.
This is not due to haste, nor is it a misprint (誤植) or anything. It's completely detached from actual facts, knowing nothing but fabrication. To insert "Moon white, snow white, heaven and earth white" (月白雪白天地白), snow piled up and the moon rose. To write beautiful prose (美文), large snowflakes softly poured down. To write about being accompanied by a lonely shadow, he wrote just that. He just forced it all with pretense (체). Pretending the moon shone, pretending snow poured down – trying to embellish everything, he ended up creating this incomprehensible patchwork. Since it doesn't matter anyway, can't people live a bit according to the facts, according to reality, a bit truthfully, without lies?

Since the topic of newspapers (新聞) has come up, let me just add a few more words and finish.
Do they think the reporter's (記者) job is only to exaggerate reports of facts, making mountains out of molehills (針小棒大)? Or are they so busy that they have a template book specifying which phrases to use for which type of incident, a standard used by all newspapers? <88> Even if a pockmarked woman dies, as long as the body is female, they'll definitively report it as "Beauty's Suicide" (美人自殺) or "Appearance of a Beauty" (美人出現). It's just laughable – there's a story: when someone asked how an ugly woman (醜女) could become a beauty, someone replied, "That's easy. Just drown yourself in the water right now. Instantly, all newspapers will announce in huge print 'Young Beauty's Plunge' (妙齡美人의 投身), and the whole world will buzz about the beauty..." Isn't there such a story? Suppose there was a lecture meeting (講演會). Even if only one or two hundred people attended that day, making the venue feel desolate, and the speaker's careless remarks provoked antipathy from the audience, the newspaper report will invariably state: "Before the scheduled time, the venue was filled to capacity with no room to stand (立錐의 餘地가 업시 滿員). Speaker Mr. So-and-so delivered a fervent speech (熱辯) on the topic of OO, deeply impressing the packed audience, and the meeting closed amidst applause (拍手聲)." It's always like this. Perhaps they print many copies of such sentences and just fill in the date, place, speaker, and topic ad hoc. Furthermore, if the event was sponsored (後援) by their own company, it gets grandiose. In a newspaper that publishes only 4 pages in total per day, they shamelessly fill more than half a page, over six or seven columns, with it. Even then, they omit everything they want to omit, rehash only the points worth boasting about, and tediously write more than half a page. What benefit does this bring to the reader, the newspaper itself, or anyone? Ultimately, doesn't it only diminish its own credibility (信用) and authority (權威)? To give one example, last summer, when there were traveling lecture groups (巡廻講演團) or theater troupes (劇壇), newspapers praised them, regardless of their actual reputation (實際 評判), gathering all favorable phrases to report, exaggerating to the extreme. It was fine to trumpet them as being so good to local readers who hadn't seen them, but when those troupes came to their own region, people found them vastly different from what the newspaper had reported. In fact, because the newspaper's exaggerated report had raised their expectations (豫期) so high, their disappointment upon seeing the reality was even greater. People should think a bit about how beneficial or harmful exaggerated reporting is in various ways. How about it? From the New Year, aren't there any thoughts of eliminating the saying, "Weather forecasts and newspaper articles are officially sanctioned lies"?

Another thing is deliberately not writing a single word in one's own newspaper about events hosted or sponsored by other companies, no matter how great an impact the event has on real society or how closely related it is. Fortunately, if one subscribes to several different newspapers, one might find out, but those who read only one newspaper remain completely unaware of any new phenomena or major events in society if their newspaper doesn't report them. <89> Isn't this the greatest shame of a newspaper? Just be faithful to reporting the facts (事實 報道). Don't exaggerate excessively just because your company hosted or sponsored it, and don't keep silent excessively just because another company hosted or sponsored it. Just strive for truthful reporting according to the facts. There lies authority (權威), and there lies credibility (信用). On this point too, I hope much attention will be paid starting from the New Year.
One more thing to mention finally is the practice of avoiding reporting facts or distorting reports because they conflict with personal principles (主義) or intentions, and the practice of arbitrarily controlling fact reporting based on personal feelings (個人의 感情). It is wrong to carelessly criticize or avoid all reporting concerning groups related to different factions or religious sects just because their principles differ from one's own faction. But the most pathetic thing is wielding a reporter's name card (記者 名啣) as a shield and committing misdeeds worse than those of bad detectives. Although this is a somewhat old story, years ago, a certain Mr. Kim, a reporter for a newspaper's society desk (社會部 記者) (though not anymore), got heavily drunk with four or five friends. He then led these four or five drunken companions and tried to force entry into a certain performance venue without tickets. When stopped by the staff there, he reportedly shouted loudly, "You punk, do you know whose favor allows you to make a living? Don't you know that if I just wield my pen, you bastards won't be able to do business?" While it's hardly worth mentioning, I think it's something to be extremely cautious about on the path forward.

From New Year's Day until now, I have said many harsh words for a long time. I have hurled many insults and much slander. I have been tailed by detectives as a subversive (不逞者), and I have tailed others as a detective myself. Although it was rambling nonsense without any structure, if there are those who have understood the intended meaning, my purpose will be fully realized there, which would be fortunate.
Ah, amidst thorny words and troubles, this year too draws to a close. Ahead of us, on our path of awakening and progress, the New Year approaches, bringing abundant joy along with a sacred new light.
I believe that in the celebratory New Year, people's lives will also find renewal, and with this, the Silver Fly bids its final farewell.
Now then – let us send off this year with complete understanding and reflection, and together greet the New Year, where wishes will come true (所願成就), with clean and joyful hearts... (End) <90>

**Summary**

This 1921 article by "Moksŏng" satirizes the pervasive hypocrisy and empty formalism in Korean society. The author criticizes customs like shouting "Come hither!" at houses with no servants, the overly indirect "ask/tell him/her" speech patterns, and the peculiar regional linguistic habit of calling the purchase of grain "selling." The critique extends sharply to contemporary journalism, condemning exaggeration (like calling any deceased woman a "beauty" or inflating event success), biased reporting that ignores rivals' events or distorts facts based on ideology or personal feelings, and the arrogance of some reporters abusing their position. The author urges readers to abandon these falsehoods and embrace sincerity and truthfulness in the coming New Year. From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its pointed critique of both lingering traditional practices deemed inefficient or hypocritical (yangban formalities, indirect speech) and the perceived failings of a modern institution (the press) during the Japanese colonial period. It reveals contemporary anxieties about social change, authenticity, and the gap between appearances and reality in early modern Korea.

때로는 只今이 11月 下旬이지마는 雜誌로는 벌서 歲暮, 年末號이라 長竹문 老先生가트면 『鳴呼 今年이 亦已云暮로다. 歲君此別이 何其 怱怱고 하며 힌수염을 다시 만즐때이다』
이러니저러니 하고 말만코 일만턴 올해도 이미 다 - 갓다. 歲미테 닥드려 안저 지나온 길을 돌아다 보면 당신네 모든 사람들이 그러케 떠들고 날뛰고 하엿서도 亦是 똑가튼 헛길을 되풀이 기한 外에 아모 것도 업섯는 것을 깨닷지 못하겟는가... 어떠한가 족음 自己가 잘 살고저 족음 自己가 살지고저 웬 1年에 얼마나 남을 속엿는가 얼마나 거짓말을 만히 용하게 하엿는가.
『銀파리』의 돌 (生日) 과 한끠 새해가 닥드려 온다. 어떤가 새해부터는 銀파리가 쉬도록 하여주지 안켓는가. 새해부터는 좀 잘 살기 爲하야 虛僞업시 살아보기 爲하야 올해 마즈막으로 이 歲末에 남아지 거짓을 아조 털어버리지 아니하려는가. 그리하야 우리 지나간 過去는 저므는 해와 한끠 永久히 파무더 버리고 오즉 착하고 참된 마음으로 깃거운 새해를 맛지 아느려는가.
자아 - 마즈막으로 몃가지 낫븐 것을 들쳐내어 업새보자.＜84＞

발목에 행전을 치고 宕巾우에 갓 쓴 老人은 그만두고라도 훌륭한 新式 洋服쟁이 紳士도 親舊나 아는 이 집 大門압헤 가서는 점챤흔체 - 하는 뚝뚝한 소리로
『이리 오너라아!』
소리를 치겟다. 그나마 크나큰 집 下人 婢僕이나 만하 보이는 집 門압헤서 가트면 덜하거니와 貧寒貧寒한 집 - 房 둘 밧게 업는 草家집 거는 房에 朔月貰든 夫婦 단 두 食口밧게안 잇는 집에 가서 高慢하게
『이리 오너라아』하고 부를 下人도 업고 그런 어린 아이도 업는 줄 뻔히 알면서도 依然히 傲慢한 소리로
『이리 오너라아』소리를 치겟다.
그짓이 大端한 虛僞 아니고 무엇이냐. 이러한 虛僞 읫 짓에 限하얀 無識한 勞働者 축에는 絶對로 업는 일이요. 所謂 有識한 所謂 점쟌흔 축에서 마타노코 하는 虛僞 읫 짓이다. 가장 점쟌타는 꼴이 부를 사람도 업시 大聲으로 이리 오너라아 하는 꼴도 웃읍지마는 정말 웃으운 꼴은 고 다음에 잇다.

針小棒大로 事實의 報道를 誇張하는 것만이 記者의 일로 아는지 그러치 아니하면 하도 바빠서 어떤 事件에는 어떤 文句, 어떤 事件에는 어떤 文句를 쓰는 法이라고 版백이 冊을 맨들어 두고 各 新聞共用으로 쓰는 세음인지＜88＞ 곰보가 죽어도 몸동이만 女子면 定해노코 美人自殺이니 美人出現이니 하겟다. 오즉 해- 웃은 이악이지만 - 어떤 醜女가 어떠케 하면 美人이 되겟느냐고 무르니까 어느 者의 그 對答이 그것 쉽지요. 물에 빠져 只今이라도 自殺을 하시요. 금시에 各 新聞에 크나큰 活字로 妙齡美人의 投身이라고 나서 世上이 모다 美人美人하고 떠들터이니... 하더란 이약이가 잇지 안흐냐. 어대서 講演會가 잇섯다. 그날 그 講演會場에는 사람이 不過 一二白名이어서 쓸쓸하기 짝이 업섯고 演士의 不注意한 言辭가 聽衆의 反感을 삿서도 新聞의 報道는 定刻前에 場所는 立錐의 餘地가 업시 滿員 되엇고 演士 某氏는 OO란 題로 熱辯을 吐하야 滿場 聽衆에 만흔 늣김을 주고 拍手聲 中에 閉會하엿다 더라. 依例 이러켓다. 아마 그네는 이런 文句를 만히 印刷하야 두엇다가 臨時로 한 장式 끄내서 日時 場所와 演士 演題만 써 넛는 것 갓다. 게다가 萬一 어떠한 主催가 自己社의 後援이거나하면 宏壯하겟다. 一日發行이 全部 4頁밧게 안되는 新聞에 體面업시 六七段의 半頁以上을 그걸로 채우겟다. 그나마 빼고 십은 것은 모다빼이고 자랑거리만 추려서 되씹고 되씹고 하야 半頁以上을 지리하게 써 노호니 그것이 일그것에나 讀者에게나 新聞自體에나 무슨 利益이 잇느냐 決局 自家의 信用과 權威만 업서질 밧게 무슨 益이 잇느냐. 한 例를 들면 지난 녀름에 巡廻講演團이나 劇壇이 잇슬 적에 그것을 稱揚하야 實際 評判은 如何하엿던지 그것 을 報道하기에 조타는 文句는 모다 주서 노하 極力 誇張하야 떠들어 노핫다. 實際를 보지 못한 地方 讀者에게 그러케 조토록 떠들기까지는 조핫스나 그 巡廻團이 自己地方에 왓슬 때에 보니까 新聞에서 보던바와 아조 千萬 틀렷다. 오히려 그 新聞의 誇張한 報道로 그의 豫期가 커던이만큼 實際를 보고 失望이 더하얏다. 誇張한 報道가 가지가지로 얼마나 有益한가 有害한가 좀 생각할 일이다. 어떤가 새해부터는 『天氣豫報와 新聞記事는 公認하는 거짓말』이란 말을 업새버릴 생각은 업는가.

또 한가지 남의 社에서 主催하거나 後援하는 일은 그 일이 實社會에 아모리 큰 影響을 준다던가 아모리 關係가 密接한 일이라도 自己 新聞에 一字一句도 쓰지 안는 것이다. 多幸히 一人이 여러 가지 新聞을 購讀하면 알지마는 그러치 안코 新聞 한 가지뿐 만을 보는 이는 그 新聞에 報道가 업슴으로 이 社會에 어떠한 新現象이 잇섯거나 어떠한 큰 일이 잇섯거나 도모지 아지 못하고＜89＞ 지난다. 이 新聞의 가장 부끄릴 배 아니냐. 오즉 事實 報道에 忠實하라. 自己社 主催나 後援이라고 넘우 誇張하지 말고 남의 社 主催나 後援이라고 넘우 입 담을지 말고 오즉 事實대로 眞實한 報道에 힘쓰라. 거긔에 權威가 잇고 저긔에 信用이 잇지 아니하냐. 이 點에서도 새해부터는 만흔 注意를 要하기 바란다.
또 한 가지 最後로 말할 것은 個人의 主義나 個人의 意思와 어그러지는 일이라고 事實의 報道를 避하거나 또는 그 報道를 굽으리는 일과 個人의 感情으로 事實의 報道를 任意로 左右하는 일일다. 主義를 달리하는 會派에 關係하거나 信敎의 갈래가 다른 派에 關係잇는 이로서 自己會派위 主義와 다르다고 輕率히 批判을 下한다던지 一切 報道를 避한다던지 하는 것도 올치 아니한 일이거니와 第一 寒心한 일로는 記者 名啣을 防牌삼아 내흔들고 낫븐 刑事以上의 行悖를 하는 것일다. 事實은 좀 오래 된 일이거니와 年前에 어느 新聞社會部 記者(只今은 아니나) 어떤 某氏는 親舊 4, 5人으로 大飮亂醉하야 가지고 그 醉客 四五人을 다리고 어느 演藝場에 入場券도 업시 無斷히 突入하다가 그곳 事務員에게 拒絶을 當하고 그 時에 大聲叱呼하는 말 『이놈아 너의가 누구의 德으로 벌어 먹고 사는 줄 아느냐. 내가 붓 하나만 놀리면 이놈들 너의는 營業을 못하게 되는 줄 모르느냐』하얏다 한다. 足히 들어 말할 것도 못되나 압흐로 나아가는 길에 極히 注意할 일인가 한다.

지나간 正月 初하루부터 이때까지 두고 오래 險한 말을 만히 하엿다. 辱說도 만히 하고 險談도 만히 하엿다. 不逞者라고 尾行에게 쫏기기도 하고 尾行으로 남을 따르기도 하엿다. 아모 종작업시 橫說竪說한 것이나마 그 뜻잇는 바를 헤아려 준 이가 잇스면 本意는 거긔에 다 할 것이니 多幸하다.
아아 험투성이 말성쏙에 이 해가 또 저물고 깨어 나아가는 우리의 압길에 거룩한 새 빗과 한깨 깃거움 만흘 새해가 압흐로 가까워 온다.
慶賀로울 새해에는 사람들의 生活도 새로움이 잇슬 것을 밋고 銀파리도 이것으로 마즈막 인사를 드릴 난다.
자아 一切의 理解와 一切의 反省으로 이 해를 보내고 깨끗한 마음과 깃거운 마음으로 所願成就할 새해를 가티 마지하다... (끗)＜90＞
