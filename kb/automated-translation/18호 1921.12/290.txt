小說 七面鳥
						金明淳 女史
一
Dear Miss Nina Schultz,
While you, Teacher, must be suffering from seasickness in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean on your way back to your homeland, I have already returned to my homeland and am praying for your well-being from an ondol room in some inn in Kyŏngsŏng, my second home.
When I was with you in K Prefecture, you and the people in the church used to say that Japanese clothes suited me, that I looked like a Japanese woman. Now that I am here wearing Korean clothes, they say these also suit me. However, I wish to quickly follow you to Germany, wear German clothes, and strive day and night to learn the passionate efforts of the defeated. Then, when the dim evening comes, how delightful and happy it would be to stroll along the banks of the Rhine with you, Teacher, just like we did in K Prefecture. When such a time comes, I will not feel tired even without napping, nor will I cry.
Teacher.
Returning to Kyŏngsŏng, I noticed that the white clothes of the passing people seemed dirtier than before. And I look up, greedily admiring the sweet, jade-green, clear sky. During that time, perhaps by the grace of Heaven, I found a somewhat calm state of mind. And many comrades came to console me. However, I am already preemptively afraid, worrying if we will scatter apart after a while.
If you ask the reason (綠故), it is none other than that male-female social interaction is still rare in Korea, and even when it happens, there is a tendency not to clearly show it outwardly. Consequently, it seems that almost no one carries a mirror in their heart to clearly reflect on their own actions.<144>
Teacher, I, surrounded by such people, am truly the least refined example among them. However, having neglected social skills until now, even if I make some mistakes, I intend to strive to learn social etiquette and build a peaceful environment around me, as long as I do not commit sinful failures.
Teacher, truly, there has never been a time when I lamented my lack of strength as much as in recent days. As you said, if I had spent the years I studied in Japan studying in Germany instead, not only would I not need to mention the aforementioned troubles now, but my strength would likely be more than enough to control myself.
Teacher, this is the story of the experiences of a young woman attempting to enter the social world and her failures. Please criticize how bitter an experience and how great a mistake it was, and if appropriate, please discipline me with a whip.
It was January 7th of this year, an unusually mild day for winter. I abruptly boarded a train in N County. That was my first departure towards K Prefecture. When the horn blew, my younger brother, who studies at N County Medical College and had come to see me off, placed a cross ring on the middle finger of my left hand, and then got off the slowly starting train and waved his faded hat.
Although I boarded the train heading for K Prefecture, upon arriving, my purse was too light to stay at an inn, and if I went to find a comrade, it would be dark night, and I wouldn't know the way well. Thus, my worries were not small. Suddenly, I remembered hearing that a famous Christian, Mr. D, resided at the S Dormitory in Paekch'on Village, K Prefecture. At that time, I reluctantly decided to seek out Mr. D, ask for forgiveness for my impudence, and request directions. Then, deliberately putting on an expression as if I had no worries, I gazed at the scenery passing by the train window.
All the scenes unfolding anew moment by moment before my eyes looked desolate because it was winter; snow remained unmelted on the mountain peaks and in the valleys, and the occasionally visible radish fields were withered by the cold. However, to my eyes, thinking of the Korean winter, I couldn't help but feel the joy of seeing green tree leaves in winter. *Passing the shabby catfish-colored (?) place*, crossing the bridge over the stream, passing by the cliff face, when the mandarin orange groves came into view, the countless orange fruits shook my feelings of longing and gladness limitlessly, and I sang the song of the train without pause.<145>
Occasionally, when my eyes grew tired, I would survey the disagreeable appearance of passengers smoking tobacco in the third-class carriage, the state of those dozing uncomfortably without support, and the children, women, and elderly peeling mandarin oranges. Then, noticing a young man around 30 across from me seemingly sketching me, I turned my face back to look out the window.
Teacher, after passing several stations from N County, at one station, a village elder close to 60 and a young man of 24 or 25 entered the train one after the other. Glancing around, they looked for seats. The old man settled down in front of me, and the young man sat with his back to the old man, but the two turned their heads towards each other and conversed animatedly. After a while, the old man turned his head and asked me,
"Young lady, where are you going?"
From his appearance, the old man seemed to possess considerable wealth and know kindness. At that moment, thinking perhaps I was in luck, I said,
"I'm going to Paekch'on Village in K Prefecture. But is there an S Dormitory there?"
The old man tilted his head, seeming unsure, when
"I live in K Prefecture. S Dormitory, is that a Christian place?" the young man spoke up from behind the old man.
"Yes," I replied, and the young man, without hesitation, addressing the old man who was just looking at his face, intending to answer my question, said,
"Uncle, isn't there a large Western-style house in front of your house?"
The old man, with his frost-touched hair, nodded slowly and answered uncertainly,
"Could it be the one in front of our house?"
The young man, with a smile in his rough-looking eyes, said,
"Yes, that's the S Dormitory she's looking for. But since our party won't be going all the way to K Prefecture but getting off at the next stop, shall I draw you a map?" showing kindness. I was very pleased and said,
"Thank you, that would be very helpful." The young man quickly sketched on my small notebook. He drew the stop in front of Kumano Shrine, the alley in front of it, the path going into it, then drew a side alley, a telephone pole, a shop, various landmarks, and explained clearly that the path goes deep in the opposite direction of a ㄱ shape, just as he drew. Listening to his explanation, I felt I could easily find it once I reached the front of Kumano Shrine, and even felt a sense of excitement.
After giving me directions, when the train stopped, they tore out the page with the map from the notebook and gave it to me. The old man <146> said,
"Young lady, seeing you travel alone, be careful. Wherever you go, there might be troublemakers..." and the young man added, "Well then, be careful, and if you don't know, ask at the police box (派出所)." Saying "Sayonara" and "Odaijini," these young and old friends, whom I didn't even know and hadn't exchanged names with, quickly got off at their stop.
Teacher,
When I got off at K Prefecture Station, it was almost dark night. The January cold felt like it was truly piercing my skin. Shivering all over, I saved money on a rickshaw and boarded a tram, asking the conductor,
"Excuse the trouble, but please tell me when to get off at the Kumano Shrine stop," I requested.
Following the conductor's directions, changing trams here and there in a flurry, I finally got off in front of Kumano Shrine. I held the piece of paper with the map in my hand, but still feeling uneasy, I went to the rickshaw stand and asked. The rickshaw pullers said,
"It's a very secluded place, you should take a ride," showing displeasure. Still, not losing the persistence to find it myself, I plunged into the side alley next to it, asking everyone I met for directions.
As I navigated through this alley and that side alley, the sound of clear flowing water from all sides felt poignant when there were people around, and eerie when there were none.
I managed to find the S Dormitory with difficulty, but when I asked for Mr. D, a tall young man with refined features called out Mr. D's name in a deep voice and went inside. Then, a woman about 40, resembling a swaying lump of meat, came out smiling strangely and said,
"He just stepped out. If you tell me your reason for coming, I'll let him know when he returns."
At that moment, I truly felt what it means for one's heart to burn.
"Yes, I came from N County, and I don't know the way or the exact house number of my comrade. I came to ask for help finding her house." Since he wasn't here, what should I do? I stood there blankly.
"Well, that's unfortunate, isn't it," she said and went back inside. After a while, a short young man with a pale face and a somewhat gentle appearance, seemingly a fellow countryman, came out and asked in Korean,
"How did you come to look for Mr. D?" At that moment, I instantly regained vitality and<147>
asked, "Where is the house of Ms. M, who attends K Girls' School here?" The gentle young man, still in a warm voice, said,
"So you came to ask for directions, I see."
"I also just came to see Mr. D, but he's not here. However, I know Ms. M's house well, so I'll accompany you there," he said and stepped out the door. Feeling like I had found a way to survive, I followed him out.

二
As it happened, the residence of the young man who guided me was in front of Ms. M's, and his name was Mr. Y.
When we arrived at Ms. M's house, Mr. M (Ms. M's husband) was not there, apparently out celebrating a baseball victory his school team had won, roaming the streets. Only Ms. M and Ms. Y seemed to have just finished dinner.
We greeted each other warmly and sat down. Then Mr. M came in, drunk and unable to quite collect himself, but still managed to offer greetings.
That night, I slept at Ms. Y's house, a couple of doors down, and in the morning, returned to Ms. M's place, ate breakfast, and was sitting there when Mr. D, dressed in Japanese clothes, arrived with Mr. H, who had come from Tokyo.
Teacher, my mistakes began from here. After exchanging greetings and talking for a bit, Mr. H (I call him Teacher out of immense respect, and because he will soon receive the respectful title of Teacher from the entire peninsula, so I use it in advance) turned to me and asked,
"Sunil-ssi, have you come to K Prefecture for good?"
At that time, I was whispering with Ms. M, smiling. We were talking about Mr. D's haori sleeve being long and frayed, showing the white cotton padding, and saying I should mend it or point it out to him. So, smiling brightly, I answered,
"Yes, I won't go back to Tokyo again." Mr. D followed up on that, asking,
"You said you were coming in March, but you came now?" Still smiling, I replied,
"I intended to stay in N County a bit longer, but figured that if a girl stays with her younger brother, she tends to suffer losses. Also, I need to prepare for school admission, so I came early."<148> A little later, Mr. H asked, somewhat disconnectedly,
"Why didn't you play the piano?"
At that time, I hadn't played the piano for Christmas, although asked to, nor for the year-end party, but since Mr. H was already away from Tokyo then, I thought he couldn't possibly know, so I asked, somewhat bluntly,
"When?"
Perhaps Mr. H had been busy during Christmas and hadn't heard my piano playing.
"At Christmas. I even sent you a letter..." As soon as I heard that, I lowered my head, then, for some reason, abruptly raised my head and retorted sharply,
"How many times do I have to play until it's enough?" Mr. H just lowered his head, his face flushed. For a long moment, nobody spoke. However, after a little while, Mr. H asked again,
"Which school are you entering?" So I replied,
"TS School."
"Which department?" he asked, but I mumbled evasively and sat there listlessly. Mr. H then asked again,
"Are you entering the Philosophy department?"
After that, the male students gathered separately and talked amongst themselves. Mr. Y, who had guided me the previous night, and Mr. M's cousin led the way out. As Mr. D and Mr. H were about to follow, I again impulsively blurted out, "Why are you leaving? Are you leaving just because there are only women here?" and "Mr. M, Ms. Y, let's us women just go to our place," blocking their way and stepping outside first. From behind, someone's voice called me back into the room, but I didn't go back in and stood in the yard. Mr. D and Mr. H came out slowly behind me. I sidled off to a corner of the yard and stood there. Mr. D asked,
"Are you going to stay at Mr. Y's place?" I replied without concern,
"Yes, Mr. Y said he's moving into the dormitory soon." Then Mr. H said, "Well then, goodbye," and walked out the main gate. Hearing that Mr. H was returning to Tokyo that evening, I thought inwardly, "Oh, there's no one as foolish as me. How could I have spoken and acted so rudely to a Teacher I usually respect? If I were a man, I would constantly follow a teacher like Mr. H, who appears so distinct and maintains a scholarly attitude, without letting him go. Unfortunately, I am a woman...<149> But didn't I fail to even greet Mr. H properly when he greeted me earlier? How could I have done that? Ah, how frustrating. I've made another unintentional mistake. If I were a man, I would go to the station to see him off... But will anyone go?... If someone goes, should I follow!? Even if I follow... in my state, how could I offer a proper farewell? I'll probably just mumble again... But if someone goes, I should definitely follow... Thinking anxiously,
I went out with Ms. M and Ms. Y for sightseeing. So, after visiting Higashi Honganji Temple, on the way back, Ms. Y mentioned needing to send a telegram to her family home but lacked small change. So I opened my purse and gave her some... After going into the post office and coming out, she went into a confectionery shop, perhaps changed the money, and quickly tried to return 40 sen to me. I said,
"You can give it back when we get home." But Ms. Y, acting as if she had borrowed money she shouldn't have, urged me somewhat demandingly,
"Just take it now."
Returning along that path, I asked Ms. M,
"Aren't you going to the station to see Mr. H off?" Ms. M replied, seemingly displeased,
"Why should I go see him off at the station just because he dropped by?" I felt a sentimental shame at that moment. Ms. M and Ms. Y seemed to talk together all day and perhaps went out again. That day, I stayed quietly in Ms. Y's empty room, contemplating my plans for Kyoto. In the evening, on my way to Ms. M's place, I ran into the young Mr. Y who had guided me the night before. I bowed to the young man and asked, pointing to his companion, "Who is this?" I mean, I asked him who his companion was. He replied,
"This is my older brother." At that moment, suppressing a slight feeling of embarrassment, I said,
"I thought it was Mr. H. How much you resemble him. By the way, Mr. H is leaving today, isn't he?"
"Yes, he's leaving shortly," replied young Mr. Y, appearing somewhat hesitant. Still, I lingered, unable to think of suggesting he should finish his greetings and leave promptly. Young Mr. Y, unable to wait any longer, took a step <150> forward and turned his body towards the front. Only then did I say,
"Excuse me."
"Not at all. Well, I'll see you again," he replied, accepting my apology as I entered Ms. M's gate.
That evening, I couldn't sleep. I felt like a large earthworm wriggling slowly, then being stepped on by something's foot, squirming and struggling, experiencing this upon myself, tormented.
The next day, waking up and looking in the mirror, my unadorned face showed even more shadows. That morning, as I washed my face (漱洗), I thought,
"My self, how much have you cried? How much have you learned? How much have you striven and fought? How many wounds have you received? When your body stands completely naked, who would dare call you dirty?" Arousing feelings of self-love, I washed my face with hot tears mixed in. Returning to the room, I applied powder. Ms. Y, beside me, remarked, "You use a lot of powder," then poured powder water onto her own palms and rubbed it vigorously onto her long face.
Suppressing my discomfort, I said something to Ms. M and Ms. Y. Then Ms. Y again blurted out disconnectedly, in informal speech,
"You have no shame-" Although I thought Ms. Y was a pitiable, ignorant woman, I couldn't help but resent her words. So, determined not to speak, I clamped my mouth shut, but felt my displeasure growing steadily.
After an unpleasant breakfast, I went alone to find TS School. At the Imadegawa stop, looking up at the old trees in the Gosho (Imperial Palace grounds) that seemed poised to pierce the heavens, admiring their grandeur, and gazing at the equally imposing architecture and grounds of TS School, I still had to ask a passing female student for directions to enter. Entering the office, a pale-faced female clerk with round eyes, over 20, and a fussy-looking male clerk were sitting there. Seeing me enter bowing, the female clerk asked,
"Who are you looking for?" There, I said, "I came to get the school regulations. I'm from Korea and intend to enter the Home Economics Department of the specialized division of this school this coming March. I came to ask what level of entrance examination you administer." The clerks replied, "Please wait a moment," looked at each other and said,
"We have to ask the Principal, we wouldn't know, right?"<151>
"That's right, the new regulations haven't been announced yet." As they were talking, a lady who looked about 50 and somewhat stern entered the office. The young clerks said to her,
"Yamashita-san, this person is from Korea. She says she's entering our school from April and came to ask about the regulations. We should ask the Principal, shouldn't we?" they asked.
Just then, the bell rang, and a short, intelligent-looking elderly gentleman entered the office. Yamashita-san immediately said,
"Principal, this is a Korean female student who came to ask about the school regulations. How should we instruct her?"
"Haha, is that so? She doesn't look Korean at all. Please ask her to come closer to me."
At these words, the clerks who had been staring blankly at me started a lively discussion among themselves about the topic of me not looking Korean.
The Principal questioned me kindly.
"When did you arrive in K Prefecture?"
"I arrived the day before yesterday."
"Did you finish girls' school in Korea?"
"I finished in Tokyo."
"Then you don't need to take the entrance exam... you can enter the specialized division. If you like, you can complete the admission procedures starting tomorrow and audit classes for now."
"Yes, I believe that would be more beneficial than idling away three months," I replied. At this, the Principal thought for a moment, then asked,
"Who sends your tuition fees?"
I hesitated slightly, then
"Yes," I replied, thinking there was no need to mention the fact that I had been working my way through school, and pursed my lips. The Principal, perhaps not noticing my expression, introduced me to the Western teachers entering the door, saying in English that I didn't look Korean. The Western teachers also patted me on the back and treated me like a student of the school, even though I hadn't completed the admission procedures yet.
The next day (翌日), I took the procedures form, and thereafter became a student of TS School, attending daily. However, I had not yet completed the official procedures.

三
Although I had completed the provisional admission procedures and was attending school without having secured a guarantor, what tormented me more than not being able to pay the monthly tuition (月謝),<152> more than not having secured a guarantor, more than my family not sending money, was my rude words and actions towards Mr. H back then. As my distress was severe, I said to Ms. M,
"Sister, I'm suffering. What should I do? My malicious remark back then to Mr. H, 'How many times do I have to play until it's enough?' no matter how much I think about it, it was a mistake. What should I do? Should I write a letter of apology (謝罪の片紙)?" Ms. M smiled brightly and said,
"Oh, you worry about everything. How can your nervous exhaustion (神經衰弱) ever get better like this?"
"But I usually respect Mr. H, that's why. For me, who intends to enter society hereafter, wouldn't this be a huge mistake? Rather than suffering like this, I'd rather just apologize quickly. Then, even if he doesn't reply, I'll have done my part, and I'll feel relieved," I said. And I sent the letter:
"Mr. H,
I trust you have returned to Tokyo by yesterday or today and are continuing your daily research.
When you were leaving for Tokyo, Teacher, I failed to bid you farewell properly, and also,
when you asked me, 'Why didn't you play the piano?' what rude words did I utter? That was indeed something I often wanted to say to the Korean students studying in Tokyo. There are many in Tokyo who have studied piano longer than I, receive tuition freely, yet ignore me when they see me. Why is it that whenever an occasion arises, they insist I play the piano? It was truly unpleasant, and feeling resentful,
I committed such rudeness towards you, Teacher. I sincerely ask for your forgiveness again and again... Respectfully yours,
January 14th
Submitted before Mr. H."
After that, Ms. Y moved into the dormitory, and Ms. M went to Tokyo. For me, the issues of tuition fees and whom to ask to be my guarantor became major worries, and I began to shed tears. ...(To be continued)...

**Summary**

This excerpt from Kim Myŏngsun's novel "Turkey" depicts the experiences of a young Korean woman, likely the narrator herself reflecting on past events, as she navigates social interactions and academic pursuits in Japan (K Prefecture, likely Kyoto or Kobe, and Tokyo) in the early 1920s. The narrator recounts her arrival in K Prefecture, her awkward and regrettable interactions with respected male figures like Mr. H, her difficulties finding lodging and support, and her enrollment process at TS School. The story highlights her feelings of inadequacy, social anxiety, financial struggles, and profound regret over perceived social blunders, particularly towards a respected elder figure, leading her to write an apology letter. The narrative ends with her facing renewed anxieties about tuition and finding a guarantor after her female companions depart.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the lives of Korean students in Japan during the colonial period. It portrays the challenges of navigating unfamiliar social norms, particularly complex male-female interactions, which were stricter in Korea. The narrator's financial precarity (worrying about inn costs, tuition, needing a guarantor, mentioning past struggles with self-support) reflects the economic difficulties faced by many Korean students abroad. Her sensitivity about being asked to perform (play piano) and her comparison with other Korean students suggest internal community dynamics and perhaps class tensions. The mention of Christian networks (Mr. D, S Dormitory) indicates the role religion played in providing support systems for Koreans in Japan. Her self-consciousness about her behavior and intense desire for respectability reveal the pressures felt by educated Korean women striving for acceptance and success in a male-dominated, colonial context. The casual mention of Japanese locations (Kumano Shrine, Higashi Honganji, Imadegawa, Gosho) and interactions with Japanese individuals (clerks, principal, Yamashita-san) provides glimpses into the everyday reality of Koreans living within the Japanese empire.

小說 七面鳥
						金明淳 女史
一
니-나 슐츠先生
先生께서 只今 이때는 故國에 돌아가시느라고 太西洋가운대서 배멀미로 呻吟하실 터인데 저는 그동안에 벌서부터 故國에 돌아와서 第2 故鄕인 京城의 어떤 旅舘 온돌房안에서 당신의 安候하심을 비나이다.
先生을 모시고 K府에 잇슬때는 그가티 日服이 어울린다고 당신과 밋 敎會안 사람들이 日女갓다고 말슴을 하시더니 이곳에 와서 朝鮮옷을 입으매 또한 어울린다고 말합니다. 그러나 어서 速히 당신을 딸아가서 獨逸의 옷을 입고 그 熱烈한 敗者의 努力을 배우느라고 晝夜汲汲하다가. 어스름 저녁때가 되면 마치 K府에 잇슬 때와 가티 先生님을 모시고 라인河畔을 散步하면 얼마나 愉快한 일이며 얼마나 幸福된 일이리까. 저는 그런 때가 오면 낫잠을 자지안하도 疲勞도 깨닷지 안흘 것이오 울지도 안흘 것입니다.
先生이어.
京城에 돌아오매 예적보다 通行人民의 힌옷에 때가 들 무덧슴을 보앗나이다. 그리고 甘美로운 碧玉의 淨朗한 空中을 貪하듯이 치어다 보나이다. 그러는 동안에 天佑神助하심인지 저는 적이 穩靜한 氣分을 차젓나이다. 그리고 동모들은 만히 차저와서 저를 慰勞하여 주옵니다. 그러나 저는 미리 怯을 집어먹고 얼마 後에는 散散히 헤질 것이 아닌가하고 두려워 하나이다.
그 綠故를 무르시면 다름이 아니오며 아즉 朝鮮안에는 男女交際가 드믄 일 가트며 또 하더래도 分明히 外面에 나타내지 안는 傾向이 잇고 딸아서 누구나 자못 自己의 行動을 맑게 비추어 볼 거울을 가슴에 품지안핫는가 함이 올시다.＜144＞
先生이시어 그런 이들 가운대 싸힌 저야말로 그中 洗練치 못한 標本이올시다. 그러하나 저는 于今 社交術을 等閑히 여긴 탓으로 얼마큼 失策은 하더래도 罪를 짓는 失敗를 하지 안키까지는 社交法을 배울겸 저의 平和한 周圍를 지을兼 努力하려 하나이다.
先生이시어 저는 참으로 近日가티 힘이 不足한 것을 울어본 때는 업습니다. 당신의 말슴과 가티 제가 日本에서 배운 年數로 獨逸가서 배웟슬진대 只今쯤은 前記한 煩惱를 말슴하올 必要는 업슬뿐만 아니라 저의 힘은 自己自身을 支配하고도 넉넉히 남아질 것 가틉니다.
先生이시어 이것은 社交界에 나서랴는 한 處女가 失敗한 所經歷을 말슴함이오니 얼마나 쓴 經驗인가 또 얼마나 큰 失策인가 批評하여 주시고 맛당하시면 채찍도 내리어 주소서.
今年 1月 7日 겨을날치고는 甚히 눅을어운 날이엇습니다. 저는 飄然히 N郡에서 기차를 탓습니다. 그것이 最初에 K府를 向하고 出發함이엇습니다. 呼角소리가 날 때 餞別나왓던 N郡醫大에서 工夫하는 男동생이 제 왼손 가운대 손가락에 十字의 指環을 끼어주고 徐徐히 가기 始作하는 汽車에서 나려서 褪色한 帽子를 둘럿습니다.
K府를 向하고 汽車를 타기는 탓스나 K府에 이르러서는 旅館에 들려면 돈주머니가 가볍고 동모를 차저가려면 어두운 밤일 것이라 길을 잘 모르겟는 故로 근심이 적지 안흔데 문득 생각난 것이 K府白井村에 S寄宿舍에 有名한 基督敎人 D氏가 계시다던 일이엇습니다. 그때 저는 不得已 D氏를 차저가서 唐突한 罪를 容恕하라고 길을 좀 가르쳐 달라리라고 決心하엿습니다. 그리고 거리끼음이 업는 듯한 表情을 구태어 지으며 車窓밧게 지나치고 지나오는 風景을 바라보앗습니다.
눈압헤 一瞬一瞬에 새롭게 展開되는 모든 景色은 冬節인 故로 山峰오리와 山골작이에 눈이 녹지 안코 間或보이는 蕪靑밧들도 추위에 시들려서 쓸쓸히 보엿습니다. 그러나 朝鮮의 冬節을 생각하는 제눈에는 겨을에 靑靑한 나무닙들을 바라보는 愉快한 心地를 어찌할 수 업섯습니다. *님의 色色은 초라한 메기*을 지나 내물우의 다리를 지나 絶壁압흘 지나서 蜜甘밧이 보일 때 朱黃의 無數한 열매들은 그리움과 반가움의 情懷를 限量업시 흔들어서 져는 쉬지안코 汽車의 노래를 불럿습니다.＜145＞
間或 눈이 疲困하면 三等車室안에 煙草 피우는 客의 밉살스런 貌樣과 몸을 依支할대도 업는대서 괴롭게 조는 狀態와 蜜甘껍덱이 벗기는 어린아이들과 女人들과 老人들을 두루 삷혀보다가 저편에서 30가까운 靑年이 저를 寫生하는듯 하던 故로 얼굴을 다시 돌리고는 窓박글 보앗습니다.
先生이시어 N郡에서 몃 停車場을 지나느라니까 한 停車場에서 60가까운 村늙은이와 24,5歲의 靑年이 前後하야 車안으로 들어오며 휘휘 눈을 굴려서 안즐 자리를 찻다가 老人은 제압헤와서 陣을 치고 靑年은 老人과 등을 지고 안젓게 되엇스나 두사람은 고개를 마조 돌려서 議論답게 이악이를 합디다. 한참잇다가 老人이 고개를 돌리며 저에게
『젊은이 어대까지 가십니까』하고 무럿습니다.
보매 老人은 相當한 財産을 가지고 또 人情을 아는 이 갓습니다. 그때 저는 幸혀나 하는 생각으로
『K府 白井村까지 갑니다. 그러나 거긔 S寄宿舍라고 잇는지오』하엿습니다.
老人이 머리를 기울여 털엿는데
『저 K府에 삽니다. S寄宿舍라니 基督敎의 것입니까』하고 老人뒤넘어서 靑年이 말하엿습니다.
『녜,』하엿더니 그 靑年은 서슴지안코 自己의 얼굴만 바라보고 나의 무름을 解答하랴는 늙은이에게
『아저씨 아저씨宅 압헤 큰 洋屋이 잇지오?』
老人은 서리마즌 머리를 끄덱끄덱하며
『그 우리 집 압헤 그것일가?』하고 不分明하게 對答하엿습니다.
靑年은 어글어글한 눈에 우슴을 띠이고,
『녜 그것이 저이가 차즈시는 S寄宿舍야요. 그러치만 우리 一行은 K府까지 가지안코 이다음 停車場에서 내릴 터이니깐 길을 그려 들일가요?』하고 親切을 보엿습니다. 저는 아주 깃겁게,
『그러케 해주시면 고맙겟습니다.』 하엿*니 靑年은 족으만 저의 手帖에 쓱쓱 그립디다. 熊野神社압 停留場을 그리고 그 압골목을 그리고 그 길로 들어가서 사이ㅅ골목을 그려 電柱를 그려 商店을 그려 여러 가지로 目標를 그리더니 갈 길은 ㄱ字 反對로 그윽히 들어가서라고 그린대로 分明히 說明하여 줍디다. 저는 그 說明을 듯고 熊野神社압만 가면 能히 차저 갈것 갓기도 하며 興味까지 깨달앗습니다.
그들은 길을 가르쳐 주고나서 車가 進行을 멈추엇슬 때 길을 그린 手帖章을 찌저주고는 老人이＜146＞
『젊으이 보아하니 혼자길 가튼데 精神차리시오. 어대 가던지 作亂軍들이 잇슬 터이니...』하고 靑年은 『그럼 精神차러서 모르시면 派出所에서 무러가십쇼』하고 『사요나라』『오다이지니』하면서 젊고 늙은 알지도 못하는 벗들은 通姓名도 하지안코 그들이 나릴 停車場에서 총총히 나려 갓습니다.
先生이시어
그때 제가 K府驛에서 나렷슬 때는 거진다 어두은 밤이엇습니다. 1月의 寒氣는 제 살을 그야말로 찌르는 것 갓습디다. 저는 으슬으슬 떨리는 全身으로 人力車 탈 돈을 아끼노라고 電車우에 올라 안즈면서 車掌더러
『受苦롭겟지만 나를 熊野神社압 停留場에서 나리도록 가리켜 주오』하고 付托하엿습니다,
車掌이 가리키는 대로 여긔저긔서 허둥지둥하며 電車를 바꾸어 타고는 及其也에 熊野神社압헤서 나리어서 길을 그린 조히조각을 손에 내들기는 들엇섯스나 그래도 安心치 못하겟는 故로 人力車 병문에 가서 무럿습니다. 人力車軍들은
『아주 외딴 곳이니 타고 가셔야 합니다』하고 不快한 눈치를 보엿습니다. 그래도 저는 혼자 차저가 보일 根氣를 일치안코 디립다 그 엽골목으로 들어가면서 맛나는 사람마다 보고 길을 무럿습니다.
이 골목 저 골목 사이ㅅ골목을 뚤러 나가는데 四面에서 맑게 흐르는 물소리가 人跡이 잇스면 애닯음을 시켜주는 것 갓기도 하고 人跡이 끈허지면 무시무시하기도 합디다.
艱幸히 S寄宿舍를 찻기는 차젓스나 D氏를 차즈니까 키 훨신 크고 眉目이 淸秀한 靑年이 굵은 목소리로 D氏를 부르며 들어가더니 고기ㅅ덩어리가 흐늘흐늘하는 40된 女人이 나와서 異常하게 웃스며
『只今막 外出하섯습니다. 어떠케 오섯는지 事故를 말하시면 잇다 들어오시건 말슴하지오』합디다.
저는 참 그때 가슴속이 타는 듯한  생각이 이런 것인가 하엿습니다.
『녜 저 N郡에서 왓는데 길을 모르고 집 番地도 仔細치 안흔 제 동모의 집을 차저 줍시사고 왓섯습니다』 마츰 안계시다니 어찌할가하고 우둑허니 섯섯습니다.
『그럼 안되엇습니다 그려』하고 들어가더니 한참 잇다가, 키작고 얼굴히고 어댄지 溫柔스러워 보이는 同國人가튼 靑年이 나오더니 朝鮮말로
『어떠케 되서서 D氏를 차저 오섯습니까』하고 무럿습니다. 저는 그때 今時로 活氣를 내이고＜147＞
『저 여긔 K女學校에 단이는 M氏宅이 어댑니까』하고 무럿습니다. 溫柔스러운 靑年은 如前히 溫慕한 音聲으로
『그럼 길을 무르러 오섯습니다 그려』하고
『저도 마츰 D君을 보러 왓섯는데 업습니다. 그러나 저도 M女史계신 宅을 잘 아니 同行하여 들이지오』하고 문을 나섯습니다. 저는 살길을 차즌 듯이 뒤쪼차 나갓습니다.

二
마츰 길을 引導한 靑年의 居宅은 M女史의 압히엇고 그는 Y氏라고 합디다.
M女史宅에 차저가매 그날은 M女史의 良君의 學校에서 野球에 勝利를 엇고 길목마다 돌아단이노라고 안계시고 M女史와 Y女史만 저녁을 막 먹고 난 뒤 갓습디다.
서로 반갑게 이악이를 하고 안젓더니 M氏가 술이 취해서 精神을 차리지 못하시면서도 들어 오서서 인사를 하십디다.
그날 밤에 저는 그 엽헤 두어 집 걸러잇는 Y女史宅에 가서 자고 아츰에는 돌우 M氏계신 處所로 와서 朝飯을 먹고 안젓는데 그 前夜에 차저갓던 D氏가 日服을 입고 東京서 오신 H氏와 가티 오섯습디다.
先生이시어 여긔서부터 저의 失策은 始作하엿나이다. 인사를 마츠고 나서 이악이를 하다가 H先生이 (제가 그더러 先生이람은 限업시 그를 尊敬함에서 또 그가 멀지 안하서 半島全體에게 先生이라는 尊稱을 바들 터인 故로 미리 尊稱하여 둠이올시다)
『順一氏 아주 K府에 오섯습니까?』하고 저에게 向하여 무러주섯습니다.
저는 그때 M女史와 소근소근하다가 웃스며 D氏의 하오리소매가 기다라케 따저서 힌솜이 비죽이 보이는 것을 보고 꿰매어 들이거나 注意를 하여 들이거나 해야겟다고 이악이를 하던 故로 싱글싱글 웃스며
『녜, 인제 다시 東京 안가겟습니다』고 對答하엿습니다. D氏는 그 말뒤를 이어
『3月에 오신다더니 只今 오섯습니까?』하고 무르셧습니다. 그때도 亦是 웃스며
『N郡에 좀 오래 잇스려고 하엿더니 아모래도 男同生하고 가티 잇스면 女子는 損을 하겟는 故로 學校에 들려면 準備도 해야 하겟고해서 벌서 왓서요』햿습니다.＜148＞ 족음잇다가 H氏가 좀 동떨어진 말슴으로
『웨 피아노 안치섯서요』하섯습니다.
그때 저는 크리쓰마쓰에는 피아노를 치고 忘年會에는 치라는 것을 안첫지만 H氏는 그때 이미 東京에 안계실 때인 故로 아실 일이 업슬 터이라 생각하면서
『언제요』하고 좀 唐突한 音聲으로 무럿습니다.
H先生은 크리쓰마쓰에 奔走하셔서 제 피아노를 듯지 못하섯던지
『크리쓰마쓰에요, 片紙까지 하여 들엇더니..』하시는 것을 듯자마자 저는 고개를 숙이엇다가, 어찐 綠故인지 急作히 고개를 들고
『몃번이나 처야 다처요?』하고 發射的 날카러운 音聲을 吐햇습니다. H先生은 고만 머리를 숙이고 낫을 붉히섯습니다. 한참이나 아모도 이악이하는 이가 업섯습니다. 그러나 H先生은 또 족음잇다가
『어떤 學校에 드셔요』무르시는 故로 저는
『TS校에요』하고
『무슨 科에요?』하시는 것을 우물쭈물해 버리고 무료하게 안젓섯더니 H先生은
『哲學科에 드십니까』하고 再次 무르섯습니다.
