Hamlet
						Shakespeare (Original Author), Hyŏn Ch'ŏl (Translator/Adapter)
Summary of Previous Acts

Act 1
Scene 1
On the high platform before Elsinore Castle, the soldier Francisco is standing guard. It was already the middle of the night. At that time, Captain Bernardo came for the changing of the guard. Not only that, but the young scholar Horatio and also the officer Marcellus came to stand guard together. This was because, for the past few days, a ghost had been appearing near the castle, and there were rumors it looked like the late King Hamlet; therefore, the scholar Horatio came to test whether this was true or false. Indeed, just as said, when it became the stroke of one, the spirit appeared, and its attire and appearance were exactly like the late King's. Horatio tried to speak to this spirit, but it disappeared without saying anything. Thus, while they were discussing the matter of the spirit and the recent situation in Denmark, the spirit appeared again. However, they could not speak with the spirit and only saw it disappear without a word.

Scene 2
The setting was the Great Hall within Elsinore Castle. With the sound of trumpets, King Claudius entered first, followed by Queen Gertrude, Prince Hamlet, Lord Chamberlain Polonius, his son Laertes, courtiers Voltimand and Cornelius, and many other nobles and attendants. The King addressed his relatives and vassals, speaking of the death of his brother the King, how due to the state of the nation he had no choice but to assume the throne, and also how, due to circumstances, he married his sister-in-law and former Queen, Gertrude. Thus, displaying abundant signs of joy in various ways, he stated that the reason for gathering the nobles here today was concerning Fortinbras, nephew of the King of Norway <154>, who, despite the fact that his kingdom's lands were offered to Denmark through a written contract between the late King Hamlet and his own uncle, was taking advantage of his uncle's illness and old age to harbor hostile intentions towards Denmark. Having inquired into this, he decided to send messengers to the King of Norway and dispatched the courtiers Voltimand and Cornelius. After that, he asked the Lord Chamberlain's son, Laertes, to state his wish, saying there was an inseparable bond between his uncle (Polonius) and this throne, and granted Laertes' wish to return to France. Then, the King also addressed Hamlet with various words of admonition, requesting that since his father the King had passed away, he should serve him (Claudius) as he would his own father. Previously, Hamlet had been studying at Wittenberg University (*Note: Original says 빅*빠아크대학 'Pik*ppaak'ŭ Taehak'*) when he heard the tragic news of his father and returned home. Although he should naturally have returned to the university, the newly crowned uncle King and his mother the Queen prevented him from going, and Hamlet too, harboring suspicions about his father's death, decided not to go. Thus, the more Hamlet doubted the circumstances of his father's death, the more his unsettled mental state produced wavering words. Then, the King exited with the Queen and all the attendants, leaving only Hamlet alone in the hall. Hamlet, alone, delivered a soliloquy filled with doubt about the cause of his father's death and resentment that, less than a day after the funeral rites for his father were completed, his mother married his uncle, an act against humanity. At that moment, Horatio, Marcellus, and Bernardo entered and told him about seeing the ghost with their own eyes, reporting that its appearance was no different from the deceased late King. Hearing this, Hamlet, though half-believing and half-doubting, became even more suspicious and resolved to go and see for himself that very night, whatever the circumstances.

Scene 3
The setting was a room in the house of Lord Chamberlain Polonius. His son Laertes and his younger sister Ophelia entered, performing a farewell scene as he was set to return to France, having obtained the King's permission in Scene 2. He gave his sister admonitions, telling her of the dangers of love and warning her not to engage carelessly in love with Hamlet. During this, their father Polonius entered, urged him to hurry, saying the preparations for departure were complete and people were waiting at the pier, sent his son off, and then also admonished his daughter, instructing her that a love affair between Hamlet and her was inappropriate.

Scene 4
The setting was the same as Act 1, Scene 1. Hamlet entered, followed by Horatio and Marcellus. First, there were words about the bitterly cold night, and then dramatic dialogue criticizing the excessive drinking in the palace as a bad habit. Just then, the spirit appeared. Hamlet spoke dramatically, expressing his heartfelt wish to the spirit and asking it to state the reason for its appearance. However, the spirit said nothing, only beckoning Hamlet with its hand. Hamlet intended to follow this spirit. The two men with him said it was dangerous and told him not to go, but Hamlet ignored them and followed.

Scene 5
The setting was another part of the same high platform. Hamlet and the spirit spoke alone together. The spirit revealed that it was Hamlet's father and recounted in detail the circumstances of how his own brother, the current King, conspired with the Queen to poison him, and the details of his suffering fiery torments in purgatory (地獄 - Chiyok, lit. hell). It entrusted Hamlet with seeking revenge, yet <155> instructed him not to lay hands on his mother but to let her die from the torment of her conscience. Then the spirit disappeared. Meanwhile, Horatio and Marcellus, worried that something might have happened to Hamlet after he followed the spirit, had been searching for him and met Hamlet at this time, asking what the spirit had said. However, Hamlet found it difficult to speak freely and, after receiving an oath from the two men that they would keep it secret, told them the gist of it and made them swear an oath to assist in his revenge. This concludes Act 1 up to Scene 5.

Act 2
Scene 1
The setting was, like Act 1, Scene 3, a room in Polonius's house. Polonius gave money and a letter to his servant Reynaldo, telling him to take these to his son Laertes in France. He also instructed him, before meeting Laertes, to inquire into his son's conduct and gave him various instructions on how to make these inquiries. After sending Reynaldo off, his daughter Ophelia entered with a frightened face and told her father how Prince Hamlet had gone mad and come to her, acting madly. Her father, suspecting this might be madness caused by love, worried in various ways. Then, deciding he could not fail to report these facts to the King, the scene closed.

Act 2
Scene 2
A Room in the Castle
With the sound of trumpets, the King, Queen, Courtiers Rosencrantz, Guildenstern, and several Attendants enter.
King: Ah, welcome, Rosencrantz, Guildenstern! Besides having wanted to see you for some time, there is also a matter I wish to entrust to you. You may have heard the rumors, but recently Hamlet has become mad, so much so that in his appearance and inner thoughts, not a trace of his former self can be seen. What else besides his father's passing could have caused him to lose his reason like this, I cannot even dream. This is the matter I entrust to you. Since you grew up together from childhood, you likely know his heart well. Stay here for some time, keep him close company, soothe him with comforting words, and if you can discover what troubles him, which I do not yet know, there might be a way to cure him. Please find out, if you have the chance, the cause of his distress.
Queen: Oh, gentlemen, seeing how Hamlet speaks of you from time to time without forgetting, it seems there are no two people in this world whose hearts match the Prince's as yours do. If you stay in the palace for a long time <156> and accomplish what we desire, treating him with courtesy and sincerity, this visit will surely earn you a reward commensurate with the King's gratitude.
Ros.: Your Majesties, reigning over us subjects with supreme authority, could rightly issue stern commands according to your will, but to entrust us with this task is overwhelmingly gracious.
Guil.: Though we are unworthy, we two subjects accept the charge and will devote our humble selves and exert all our efforts so that there will be no fault in carrying out Your Majesty's heavenly will.
King: I express my gratitude to you, sirs. Rosencrantz, Guildenstern.
Queen: Kind words. Guildenstern, Rosencrantz. Then, please go see the endlessly changed Prince even now. Well then— someone escort these gentlemen to Hamlet.
Guil.: O God, grant that our presence and loyal service may be pleasing to His Highness the Prince and become an aid in his recovery.
Queen: Ah, God grant it.
Rosencrantz, Guildenstern exit, followed by several attendants.
Polonius enters.
Pol.: I report to Your Majesty. The ambassadors have returned from Norway bearing good news.
King: Sir, you are always an old man who brings good news.
Pol.: Are these my words? Though I tremble, I swear by the gods, my devotion to the gods, my loyalty offered to Your Majesty, and the faithfulness with which I keep my office are no different from how I guard my soul. For that reason, I believe I have discovered it... Otherwise, this brain of mine would no longer be able to sniff out the scent of stratagems as it does now... No, I have surely discovered the reason for Prince Hamlet's madness.
King: Ah! Tell me quickly. I have long waited to hear it.
Pol.: First, please grant an audience to the ambassadors. What I intend to report is like the dessert fruit that comes after a great feast.
King: Then you arrange all the procedures and summon the ambassadors here. <157>
Polonius exits.
[King to Queen] It seems he knows all the causes of the Prince's madness.
Queen: Perhaps. I am doubtful. Even if it's the old man's word, surely his father's death or perhaps our hasty marriage is likely the main reason.
King: Hmm! We must look into whether it is so or not.
Polonius enters, bringing the two men, Voltimand and Cornelius.
Have you returned safely, sirs? Voltimand, what then was the reply from the King of Norway?
Vol.: A courteous reply. Upon our first audience, he immediately sent orders to halt his nephew's military preparations. Thinking it was only intended for conquering Poland, after various inquiries, he realized it involved hostile intent towards Your Majesty, was greatly surprised, lamented that his old age and illness prevented him freedom of action, and immediately dispatched messengers to arrest Prince Fortinbras. The Prince obeyed the command, received the King's reprimand, and consequently swore before his uncle that he would never again take up arms against Your Majesty. The old King, delighted by this, granted him an annual sum of 3,000 crowns and gave permission to use the previously raised troops for the conquest of Poland. His request regarding this is detailed in this document (presents a document), asking that Your Majesty grant permission for peaceful passage through your territory for this plan, ensuring safety along the way, as written in the state letter.
King: That is most agreeable to my wishes. When I have time, after reading it carefully, I will consider it deeply and give my reply. You two may withdraw and rest. You have completed an important task successfully. When evening comes, let us drink together. I rejoice in your safe return.
Voltimand and Cornelius withdraw.
Pol.: This great matter has also been concluded successfully. Now, Your Majesty, Your Highness. To discuss what the authority of a monarch is, what the duty of a subject is, or why day is day and night is night, or time is time, is merely <158> to waste day, night, and time in vain. Therefore, since brevity is the soul of wit, and tediousness the limbs and outward flourishes, I will report this matter very briefly. His Highness the Prince's madness is indeed madness, I must report. Because, attempting to define madness beforehand is ultimately an act akin to madness itself. But let that be...
Queen: Rather than adorning your words with color, the necessary facts.
Pol.: Your Highness the Queen, why would your humble servant speak falsehoods and empty words? It is true that the Prince is mad. Truly a pitiful matter. That it is pitiful is true. No, dull embellishment. Let's leave that aside, setting aside adornment now. First, assuming he is mad, what remains for us is to discover the cause of that effect... or rather, perhaps it is more fitting to say the cause of that defect. If you command me to explain why, such a defective result surely could not arise without a cause. This is what remains to be reported (奏). The remaining condition is thus. Please give your particular wise consideration... I have a daughter... This was when she was under my care... With a dutiful heart, she... please listen. She showed this letter to me. I pray you, perceive it clearly.
(Takes out a love letter and reads.)
‘To the celestial, and my soul’s idol, the most beautified Ophelia,’... This is ill-phrased. Turbid. A turbid phrase. ‘Beautified’ is a turbid word. But listen to what follows. It is thus.
(Reads again.)
‘In her excellent white bosom, these, etcetera etcetera’
Queen: Is that from Hamlet to Ophelia?
Pol.: Your Majesty, just a moment. I will report exactly as it is. <159>
(Reads again.)
‘Doubt thou the stars are fire;
Doubt that the sun doth move;
Doubt truth to be a liar;
But never doubt I love.
O dear Ophelia, I am ill at these numbers; I have not art to reckon my groans; but that I love thee best, O most best, believe it. Adieu.’
‘Thine evermore, most dear lady, whilst this machine is to him, HAMLET.’ This letter, my daughter, according to my command, showed to me. Not only that, but I know everything about what words were spoken, at what time, and in what place.
King: And Ophelia's actions in response to that?
Pol.: What kind of person do you perceive me to be?
King: As a man faithful and honourable.
Pol.: I wish to be so. But how do you perceive it? ...When I saw the wings of this fierce love... This I should have reported beforehand... Especially since I saw such behavior before hearing it from my daughter... How might Your Majesty or Her Highness the Queen perceive it? At that time, if I had been like a desk drawer or an account book, had merely blinked my eyes shut and watched silently... what would Your Majesties think? No, I took action immediately. I told my daughter these words: ‘Lord Hamlet is a prince, out of thy star; This must not be.’ And I instructed her to lock herself from his resort, admit no messengers, receive no tokens. As a result, my daughter followed my words and kept them faithfully. Consequently, the Prince, being repulsed... to put it simply... fell into a sadness, then followed fasting, then sleeplessness, then weakness, thereupon also heartbreak, and by this declension, into the madness wherein now he raves, and all we mourn for. <160>
King: (Looking at the Queen) Do you think so too?
Queen: It truly seems possible.
Pol.: If there has ever been even one time when something I proactively reported as ‘thus’ later turned out not to be so, please let me hear of it.
King: I cannot recall such an instance.
Pol.: (Pointing to his head and shoulders) If I am mistaken, take this from this. If there is but a way to find it, I will find where truth is hid, though it were hid indeed within the centre of the earth, and show it to you.
King: In that case, we must somehow test the truth of it.
Pol.: As you know, His Highness sometimes walks for hours together here in the lobby.
Queen: Indeed, he does walk so.
Pol.: At such a time, let us send my daughter before the Prince. Then Your Majesty and I shall hide behind the arras and observe their encounter. If he does not love her and his mind has not become disordered on account of her, then I will resign from this office and go to the countryside to become a farmer.
King: Let us indeed try the test.
Queen: But look, see where the poor wretch comes reading sadly.
Pol.: Let us withdraw. Let us question him presently.
King, Queen, Attendants, etc., exit.
Ah, forgive me. Greetings, Prince Hamlet.

**Summary**

This article from the December 1921 issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine presents a Korean translation and adaptation of Shakespeare's *Hamlet* by Hyŏn Ch'ŏl. It begins with a detailed summary of Act 1, covering the appearance of the Ghost, Claudius's ascension and marriage, Hamlet's grief and suspicion, Laertes' departure, Polonius's warnings to Ophelia, Hamlet's encounter with the Ghost, and the Ghost's revelation of murder and demand for revenge. The article then translates the beginning of Act 2, Scene 2, where Claudius and Gertrude welcome Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, asking them to discover the cause of Hamlet's madness. Polonius enters, announces the return of ambassadors from Norway with news about Fortinbras, and then claims to have found the true cause of Hamlet's madness – his rejected love for Ophelia – offering her letters as proof and proposing a plan to eavesdrop on Hamlet and Ophelia. The translation cuts off as Polonius prepares to greet the approaching Hamlet.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is significant as an early example of introducing canonical Western literature, specifically Shakespearean drama, to a Korean audience through translation. It demonstrates the efforts to engage with global culture and adapt the Korean language (using mixed Hanja-Hangul script and contemporary, albeit older, orthography) for modern literary forms during a period of Japanese rule. The choice of *Hamlet*, with its themes of usurpation, revenge, madness, and political intrigue, might have held subtle resonances, though presented here primarily as a literary work. Historians might find interest in the translation choices, the specific orthography and vocabulary reflecting the language of the 1920s, and the role of magazines like *Kaebyŏk* in cultural transmission during the colonial era.

前幕槪要
第1幕
第1場
엘시노아 宮城高臺前에서 兵卒 후란시스코오가 守直을 하고 잇다. 이때는 이미 밤중이엇다. 그때에 隊長 빠아나아가 들어와 交代할 때이엇다. 그뿐만 아니라 젊은 學者 호레에시오와 또 將校인 마아제라스가 한 가지 守直하게 되엇다. 그것은 이 數日內에 宮城近處에서 鬼神이 나타나는데 그것이 先王 하믈레트와 갓다는 말이엇섯는 故로 그것이 참말인지 거진 말인지 試驗하기 爲하야 學者인 호레엣시오가 온 것이다. 果然 말과 가티 새로한 點이 되자 亡靈이 나타낫는데 그 服裝과 外貌가 先王과 꼭 가탓다. 호레에시오는 이 亡靈에게 말을 하랴고 하여도 아무 말이 업시 그냥 사라지고 말앗다. 그리하여 亡靈에 對한 일과 덴마아크의 近况을 이악이하고 잇는 中 또 亡靈이 나타낫다. 그러나 亡靈과 말은 못하고 그냥 사라지고
