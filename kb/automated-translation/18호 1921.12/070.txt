잡지명	개벽 제18호
발행일	1921년 12월 01일
기사제목	慶州行 (Journey to Kyŏngju)
필자	權悳奎
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

慶州行
						權悳奎

Even while traveling along a road, when one encounters a village nestled beneath a large mountain, where the so-called Blue Dragon and White Tiger formations are distinct, with a fitting Ansan (guardian hill) in front, a few ancient, rounded zelkova trees standing sparsely, and tile-roofed houses resembling whale backs scattered about, one naturally bows one's head, feels a certain reverence, and an undeniable curiosity arises to know whose efforts adorned this village. Conversely, when encountering a place on a flattened ridge or a sunken hollow, encircled by flimsy poplar switches, with a few new houses roofed with corrugated iron that look as if they might blow away in a whirlwind, one feels, for some reason, itchy, detestable, insignificant, and lacking substance, leading to a sense of disdain. I believe everyone shares this same feeling. Holding such thoughts, I now head down to Kyŏngju, the ancient capital of a thousand years. I don't know how long I've been on the train. Amidst the commotion of people vying for and snatching things, I glanced around swiftly and secured a spot amidst the fruits. There were persimmons, praised in a seven-character quatrain for being stolen, and chestnuts, subject to children's riddles: "Inside the rough is the smooth, inside the smooth is the fuzzy, inside the fuzzy is the crunchy." What a beginning! Ah, how delightful! The place named Kwach'ŏn (果川) originally got its name from its abundance of fruit (果實). A companion who joined me here claims Yangju chestnuts are highly praised as a local specialty, but historically, Kwach'ŏn is actually the true fruit region, especially known for its chestnuts down through the ages. That's why during the Koguryŏ era, the village name was Yulmoktongsahŭl (栗木冬斯肹). This name originated because chestnut trees (栗木) grew abundantly there, and mistletoe (冬斯肹) thrived on these trees. Thus, *Yulmok* means chestnut tree, and *Tongsahŭl* means mistletoe. Generally, our ancient place names or personal names were not created using Chinese characters as they are now, but were purely Korean words. Ch'ŏngp'ung (淸風) being called Saryŏl'i (沙熱伊) means *Saryŏl'i* is the word for "cool." Yŏng'am (靈岩) being called Talnae (達奈) or Wŏlch'ul (月出) means *Wŏlch'ul* is the translation of *Talnae*. Kosŏng (高城) being called Talhol (達忽) is because *Tal* meant "high" in ancient language. Chŏngmokchin (赤木鎭), a subordinate county of Hoeyang (淮陽), was called Sapekŭnŭl (沙非斤乙), which means "newly brightened." It is said that the Silla general Chukchuk (竹竹) symbolized "not withering in the cold season," but might this not also simply mean "growing straight and tall (주욱 죽 자라는)" without hindrance? Even the great sage Wŏnhyo (元曉), his name *Wŏnhyo* was said to be *Sitan* (始旦) in the vernacular, meaning "first morning/dawn." Oh dear, when will I ever finish talking about such things? Anyhow, passing through the place notorious for its ill-tempered people, South Suwŏn<60>, and then P'yŏngt'aek, known for the saying, "Will P'yŏngt'aek crumble or Asan collapse?" regarding its supposedly effective hand-to-hand fighting. P'yŏngt'aek is nearby, but where might Asan be? Asan, famous not just for fighting, but Asan, which enshrines the sacred remains of our great hero, Yi Ch'ungmu (李忠武)! Ah, Asan! Asan! May you be eternally peaceful. Passing Ch'ŏn'an of the "Ch'ŏn'an Samgŏri weeping willows" song, and through Sojŏngni of "Catching a horse at Kimje Station," the train slowed down and arrived at the place announced as "Choch'iwŏn! Choch'iwŏn!" Legend has it that Choch'iwŏn was a market established by the Silla scholar Ch'oe Ch'iwŏn (崔致遠), and the name changed due to a phonetic shift from Ch'oe Ch'iwŏn. There's no need to argue whether this is specifically true, but just arriving here makes one feel closer to the capital Kyŏngju and entering the Silla era of old. Passing through Hanbat (Taejŏn), whose name evokes thoughts of the Samhan (Three Han) confederacies, and leaving behind Yŏngdong, the supposed seedbed of the Yangban class, we reached somewhere around Hwanggan. There was a village with a pine grove behind the houses, bamboo clumps outside the fences, a stream flowing before the gates, and across the stream, in a fairly wide field, rice ripening beautifully, resembling a battle formation of gold. Ah, what a place to live! It looks good, but perhaps some young master from a centuries-old lineage, deeply immersed in idle dreams, lies there. Passing through Ch'up'ungnyŏng Pass, a tunnel of superstition where it's said trains struggle and pant as if unable to proceed, one passes a bend, and there is a stream; cross the stream, and there is a field; beyond the field, a mountain; beyond the mountain, a river. If the train goes this way, the stream follows that way; if the train goes that way, the stream turns this way, like children playing hide-and-seek, darting ahead, seeming to say, "Catch me if you can!" Passing Kimch'ŏn, famous for its fine *Kwahaju* summer wine, and counting one, two stations, we arrived in Taegu as the sun was setting.

Changing to the Central Light Railway, how different it was, completely distinct! The mountains and waters of the Korean Peninsula are monotonously similar everywhere, hardly worth mentioning, but from here on, the people, language, and customs belong to an incomparably different world. Let's take one example first. As we passed a railway crossing (후미기리) near a station with the strangely melancholic name Panyawŏl (Half-Moon Night), I suddenly looked out. Standing there, holding a signal flag, was a woman who looked frighteningly old, perhaps around 40. I found myself wanting to ponder her life story out of idle curiosity. Perhaps she was born into a poor family. And likely married into another poor family. Then, her fate probably took an even more rugged turn, perhaps losing her husband. Thus, like a gourd cut from its vine, she might have worked as a hired hand, carried loads beyond her strength as an itinerant peddler through various towns and villages, and perhaps drifted from one market to another. Then, as her physical strength waned,<61> she settled down to manage a meager household. Just then, a railway line was built in front of her house, and a crossing appeared right at her gate. The company requested her services, and she, finding it not inconvenient, agreed and became a signal keeper as things turned out. The shabby thatched hut (초갸집) beside the railway tracks visible now is her home. Though it appears small, low, and dirty from the outside, its interior is likely kept meticulously clean, almost like a monk's dwelling, with everything orderly and systematically arranged. One can further imagine the luxuries she experienced in her life, piece by piece. A year after her birth, at her first birthday celebration (돌짱이), she received fine clothes and good food once. Next, although perhaps not by her own will, on the day she entered the so-called hundred-year bond (百年佳約), entrusting her life to another, she experienced luxury in clothes and food once more. After that, there were likely no further indulgences destined for her share. Furthermore, discussing her character, it's a common saying that widows are invariably shrewd. Born with a strong disposition and imbued with the tenacious spirit of the Yŏngnam region, she probably couldn't be turned back even if pulled by the strength of ten thousand horses. Thus, enduring countless hardships (九死十生), she never relied on others but earned her living solely by the sweat of her brow. Looking at her now, holding that flag, doesn't it seem so? I wanted to compare her again with the women of the city. Ugh, how hateful! City women – with oiled hair, powdered faces, hands like silk ribbons gripping the dangling ties of their skirts, walking mincingly on their boat-shaped sock tips – let's leave aside that sort. Even those women with braided hair coiled like cow dung, wearing short skirts, clattering out in high-heeled shoes to call for freedom and liberation, then coming home and flicking water from their fingertips – they too are not truly genuine. They say liberation has come, but what is freedom for? Perhaps because I am ill-informed, or perhaps because they do not seek freedom and liberation scientifically, I don't know, but I simply explain freedom as that which allows one to sustain an independent life. Yet that signalwoman, she is truly liberated, independent, free, and equal – not just in thought, not just in words, but in practice and reality. She never called for liberation, yet is liberated; she never asserted independence, freedom, or equality, yet possesses them, embodying a silent philosophy of emptiness. If she were to offer an explanation, it might be this: "You men, you of a different race, try as you might to confine me, I am absolutely liberated! Try as you might to ensnare me, I am absolutely independent! When were freedom and equality ever an issue for me? Young women who seek liberation and equality from men, everything lies within me; why seek it from others?"<62> Another thing that particularly catches the eye at every station is the sight of top-knotted men with unkempt hair and young bachelors wearing pointed traditional hats (곳갈 手巾), carrying either seed trays or round baskets filled with tobacco, fruits, or sweets, calling out, "Buy cigarettes, buy sweets!" and if displeased, remarking, "Why are you acting like this?" This truly makes one feel like they are on a journey to see the old capital. The day gradually darkens. It must be around the time when "cattle and sheep return to the village lanes." Near places where bearded elders clear their throats "ahem, ahem" because they are slow, children invariably run up. By ancient trees shrouded in smoke, the flapping wings of birds seem utterly leisurely. The sight of male and female farmers returning from the autumn harvest, some empty-handed, some carrying tools, some singing folk songs (미나리 - 山有花), some clicking their tongues to drive cattle "tzut-tzut," would be a splendid painting if it were one, but since it is not a painting but a real scene, it appears all the more evocative.

Passing near Hayang, the sun finally settled into its resting place beneath the western mountains. Though riding in a train, my body, having rushed all day, could not overcome fatigue, and I briefly rested in another world. Then, passengers started bustling about, and the station attendant called out, "Yŏngch'ŏn! Yŏngch'ŏn!" Ah, so this Yŏngch'ŏn is the birthplace of the loyal Koryŏ minister Chŏng P'oŭn (鄭圃隱). A venerable land that produced a venerable man! There is no need to reintroduce him, but in his scholarship, his arguments, whether horizontal or vertical, were invariably reasonable; in poetry and rhyme, he possessed all aspects – in short, a perfectly well-rounded Yangban. I recall a verse he composed around this time in September at Myŏngwŏllu Pavilion:
"A governor of refined taste enjoys his 2000-sŏk stipend; / Encountering old friends calls for 300 cups."
I wanted to recite the Master's poem and compose one myself. But no poem came, only thoughts of the Master, leading to this impromptu verse:
Where might I find Master Chŏng's birthplace?
It's dark and unseen, asking is useless.
If only it were nearby...
Never mind the autumn moon, a bright moon shines clearly in the blue sky, decorating the cool evening heavens. The train rumbles and puffs along, reciting the verse "Front mountain suddenly becomes rear mountain." We pass Ahwa (阿火). The person beside me asks about the unusual place name. I idly tell him it's a sign that Kyŏngju is near. He persistently asks further, "Kyŏngju is certainly near, but what kind of sign?" I explain that this is an ancient word, isn't *Hwa* (火) fire (불)? He gets angry, says "Bah!" and stops talking, probably thinking it was purely a joke, assuming everyone knows *Hwa* means fire. So, I began explaining again. *Hwa* is indeed *pul* (fire), and *pul* could also mean *pŏl* (field/plain), and the word *pŏl* was widely used<63> in ancient times as part of village or country names. First, the *pŏl* (伐) in Silla's initial name, Sŏrabŏl (徐羅伐), is this word. This *pŏl* was written in various ways like *pul*, *pal*, etc., but whether the sound was *pŏl*, *pul*, or *pal* cannot be definitively determined. It is a fact that it was used in various forms like *pul*, *pŏl*, *pal*, etc. The reason just this one word for "country" or "plain" became so chaotic is that writings transcribed directly in the Korean language using a Korean script disappeared, and transcriptions were made using Chinese characters (Hanmun). Furthermore, these Hanmun transcriptions were not done by one person but by many different hands. If only Koreans had done it, there might have been some consistency, but non-Koreans, especially Chinese people—who have an ingrained second nature to transcribe foreign words as incorrectly as possible—their transcriptions are what we see today, leading to such fragmentation. Thus, both Chinese and Koreans, because they wrote in Hanmun, sometimes transcribed *pul* by sound as 不 or 弗, or by meaning as 火. They also transcribed it as 孛, 渤, or 發. The transcription 伐 was most common, with countless examples like Eumjilbal (音汁伐), Sabal (沙伐), Kibal (伎伐), Sobal (蘇伐), Pisabal (比斯伐), etc. Examples using *pul* include Talbul (達弗), Kunghwa (弓火), Kulhwa (屈火), Kajuhwa (加主火), Nosahwa (奴斯火), Ponch'wihwa (本推火), Talguhwa (達句火), Pijahwa (比自火), etc. If we trace this back further, names like Puri (夫里), Piri (卑離), Puyŏ (扶餘), Paedal (倍達) are all of the same kind. Examples using *pal* include Yungbal (戎發), Yŏndabol (延陀勃), etc. Besides transcribing with various different characters based on sound or meaning, even when transcribing with a single sound, country names were matched with characters appropriate for "country," water names with characters appropriate for "water." The *Han* of Samhan (三韓) used 韓; the river name in Han territory used 漢 (Han River); the sea name used 瀚 (瀚海, referring to the East/South Sea). Similarly, the characters 發/勃, when corresponding to a water name, were written as 渤, as in the Balhae Sea (渤海), meaning the sea of the *bal* (country). Before the explanation could finish, we arrived at Kyŏngju Station. Getting off the train, there were few people to greet us, and even the station seemed desolate. Only the bright moon, full of feeling, shone brilliantly (皎皎), like "Snow and moon retain the colors of the previous dynasty," but with the "Snow" part removed.

Upon entering Kyŏngju, the first thing that strikes one as enormous is something scattered here and there like giant steamed buns (饅頭), almost as large, with slight exaggeration, as Namsan Mountain in Hanyang (Seoul). Anyone seeing them for the first time would never think they are tombs. They look entirely like natural hillocks. It seems utterly impossible for human hands to have created such things. Humans can conceive and create many things, but can they truly create new mounds of earth? Yet, the people of Silla created mounds of earth. I climbed Ponghwangdae (Phoenix Terrace). That "terrace," which is also a hill, is presumably someone's royal tomb (陵墓), though whose it is remains unknown. There is a fanciful story associated with it.<64>
It's unknown if it was "In the beginning was the Word" time, but very long ago, a phoenix (鳳凰) descended and played here. The spot where the phoenix played is Ponghwangdae. It's said that phoenixes appear when the world is at peace, but perhaps because the world is currently in turmoil, the phoenix is unseen, and only the terrace remains desolate, with pointless crows cawing "caw, caw" in its place. Sometime later, a geomancer climbed this terrace and declared, "Phoenixes are oviparous. It won't do to have the place where the phoenix plays but no eggs." So, he gathered countless mounds, which are the hillocks now visible on all four sides centering on this terrace. Such geomancers' fabrications are hardly worth mentioning, but I feel like sending all those who fervently believe in geomancy (堪輿術) to Kyŏngju. They would claim that the site for a tomb (陰宅) requires a certain configuration of incoming "dragon veins" (來龍), orientation (向), and must be a specific "auspicious spot" (穴) where wind is contained (藏風). Do these royal mounds, then, meet all those conditions? That's unknown. If Kyŏngju as a whole is assumed to meet all those conditions, well then, look at the locations of these tombs. Weren't they placed indiscriminately in field corners, paddy edges, dry spots, and marshy areas? Seeing the ancient tombs of Kyŏngju, any so-called geomancer would surely beat a hasty retreat (36計). If they were still to brazenly insist otherwise, they could only argue, as mentioned before, that Kyŏngju as a whole meets all conditions. Then I too have something to say. In that case, the Liaodong plains wouldn't be unsuitable either, nor would the Sahara Desert, the largest in the world. Should we then discuss the entire Earth? If the Earth itself is the terrain, where would its "dragon veins" come from? This is just childish banter. Consequently, the claims that Kyŏngju's topography is weak to the north, leading women to wear northern-style topknots, and that even today prostitutes tie their sashes at the back, and that tail-less animals are called *Tonggyŏng* (東京 - Eastern Capital, an old name for Kyŏngju), originating from this – these are most likely tales spun by later geomancers, not from the Silla period. The people of Kyŏngju call these ancient tombs *tongme* (독메), and twin tombs *hyŏngje tongme* (兄弟 독메 - brother mounds). Hearing this term *me* (뫼 - mountain/mound), the statement that they are as large as Namsan seems less of an exaggeration. In any case, if about 30 people stand on the east side of a mound, they cannot be seen from the west, and if they stand on the west, they cannot be seen from the east. This gives an idea of how truly large these tombs are. Regardless, these ancient tombs constitute more than half of the form of the ancient capital Kyŏngju. Both in their form and in the relics hidden within their earthen bellies, they truly make people gasp in amazement. The antiquities recently discovered amidst much excitement came from these tombs, and half of the countless artifacts in the Kyŏngju Historic Preservation Society (慶州 古蹟 保存會) came from inside these tombs. Based on their form,<65> these tombs are classified into two types. Internally, there are those with stone chambers (石槨) and those filled with piled stones (積石). Externally, there are those with surrounding screen stones (屛風石) and other stone figures, and those without. Those without are pre-unification, and those with are post-unification. The tortoise pedestal (龜趺) and dragon capstone (螭首) of King Muyŏl's tombstone (武烈王陵碑), now considered one of Kyŏngju's Four Treasures, originated right here at the tomb of King T'aejong (太宗), who unified the Three Kingdoms. This is also why the tomb of Kim Yang (金陽), located in front of King T'aejong Muyŏl's tomb, is sometimes mistakenly identified as the tomb of Kim Kakkan (金角干 - Kim Yusin) located on the slope of Songhwa Mountain. There would be no reason to place Kim Yang's tomb below King Muyŏl's steps; even if it were, the time gap between King Muyŏl and Kim Yang is over a hundred years, so there would be no reason for its stone features to be identical to King Muyŏl's tomb, except for a single stele. The Kim Kakkan tomb site, said to be Kim Yusin's (金庾信) tomb, is equipped with screen stones and stone figures, clearly indicating it's a tomb from long after unification. Since Kim Yang also held the rank of Kakkan (角干), his tomb might well have been called Kakkan's tomb. As Kim Yusin became the representative Silla Kakkan, later generations, confused by the title "Kakkan," might have arbitrarily concluded it was Kim Yusin's tomb. Whether the Kim Kakkan tomb is someone else's or not, the so-called Kim Yang tomb is definitely not Kim Yang's but clearly belongs to the same era as King Muyŏl's tomb. Passing Anapchi Pond (雁鴨池) and seeing the Stone Ice House (石氷庫) at Panwŏlsŏng Fortress (半月城), we came back to view Ch'ŏmsŏngdae Observatory (瞻星臺). Anapchi Pond was created not long after Silla achieved unification, when King Munmu (文武王), successor to King Muyŏl, inherited the great task and was in the midst of celebrating. He built islands in the pond, constructed rainbow-like stone bridges resembling the vast sky, built stone mountains on all four sides to resemble the Twelve Peaks of Wu Gorge (巫山), planted rare flowers and wondrous plants, raised exotic birds and beasts, and built Imhaejŏn Hall (臨海殿) on the west side. With implements suitable for changing seasons – flower mornings, moonlit nights, peak summer, deep winter – whenever the royal carriage appeared, mountains and rivers would respectfully pay homage. One can close one's eyes and instantly visualize the court officials bowing and moving with deference. The stone troughs (石槽) and stone basins (石桶) remaining at the palace site today witnessed that scene. Nearby, stretching east and west like hooks, lies the crooked Panwŏlsŏng Fortress. This fortress resembles a half-moon whether viewed segment by segment or as a whole, which is quite intriguing. The half-moon symbolizes the anticipation of fullness (圓滿), further teaching the profundity of its conception. Below this lies the Royal Palace site (王宮터). Scattered foundation stones (柱礎), though silent, clearly narrate a thousand years of history. Not only these foundation stones, but every roof tile fragment and stone piece littering the Kyŏngju plain is significant; none are ordinary. Some are carved with lotus flowers (蓮꼿), cloud patterns (雲紋), or chrysanthemums (菊花); every piece has been touched by human hands. It seems even stones had their moment of glory.<66> How sorrowful it must be for them now, reduced to latrine stones or damming streams. On the slope of Wŏlsŏng, facing south, is the Sŏkpinggo (Stone Ice House). The construction date of this ice house is unknown, but the *Samguk Yusa* (三國遺事) states that during King Yuri's reign (儒理王), plows (犁耟) and ice storage (藏氷庫) were first made, and the *Samguk Sagi* (三國史記) records that in the 6th year of King Chijŭng (智證王), officials were first ordered to store ice. Thus, we know ice storage has ancient origins. The lintel stone (楣石) at the entrance bears an inscription indicating reconstruction in the 17th year of King Yŏngjo (英宗) of the Yi Dynasty (李朝). There are traces suggesting it initially had stone doors (石扉). The interior width is about 20 *ch'ŏk* (feet), and the height is four or five *kil* (arm spans). The ceiling is rounded, constructed in a ribbed style (肋式). Opening the *Guide to Kyŏngju Historic Sites* (慶州 古蹟案內), it states that this ice house used approximately 1000 stones, its ribbed structure is rare, and such stone architecture is precious in the East. I once again contemplated the luxuries of Silla. They must have cut ice from the Munch'ŏn River (蚊川江) in winter, stored it in this ice house, and then, during the sweltering heat of midsummer (三伏中), cooled the boiling palace halls, transforming them into veritable Crystal Palaces (水晶宮). When feeling stifled and wanting to shout, they would have downed a bowl of ice water, exclaiming, "Ah, refreshing!" and enjoyed themselves. Ch'ŏmsŏngdae stands north of Wŏlsŏng, beside the road from the town to Sillim Forest (始林). It is the remnant of Silla dynasty's astronomical observatory, the largest astronomical platform in the East, a venerable structure. It is said to have been built during the reign of Queen Sŏndŏk (善德女王), the 27th monarch of Silla. Made of granite, it rises 30 *ch'ŏk* high in a cylindrical shape. At the very top, a double square frame (井桁) is placed, and a square window on the central south face serves as an entrance/exit. The interior of the observatory was built solid without stairs for ascending or descending. Since it was a place for urgent time observation, it's unlikely they would have used time-consuming ladders; some opine that perhaps a lifting device (昇降機) was used. That might be plausible. A thousand years ago, with the skill to smelt glass (琉璃), weave five-colored wool (五色 毛織), achieve such architectural and sculptural feats, and create extraordinary objects like the Manbulsan (萬佛山 - Ten Thousand Buddha Mountain, a miniature landscape), creating something like a lifting device might not have been so strange. Perhaps even things like the duckweed (浮萍) of Anapchi Pond, one of Silla's Eight Wonders (新羅 8怪), were not actual duckweed but something made to float like lotus leaves, capable of supporting a person without sinking. Silla's divine object, the Golden Ruler (金尺), perhaps wasn't a real gold ruler that healed the sick when measured and revived the dead when measured. I suspect there might have been a skilled physician named Kŭmch'ŏk (金尺 - Golden Ruler) who, when treating the sick, healed them, and when treating the dying, revived them – perhaps Silla's Pien Ch'ueh (扁鵲 - legendary Chinese physician). Furthermore, if Kŭmch'ŏk wasn't a person's name, in our language (我語), someone specializing in or expert at something is called a *-jangi* (장이). *-Jangi* was translated into Chinese characters as *ch'ŏk* (尺 - ruler). For example, a singer (소리장이) was called *kach'ŏk* (歌尺), an archer (활량) *kungch'ŏk* (弓尺), a fisherman (漁夫) *ŏch'ŏk* (漁尺 or 魚尺), a cook<67> (밥 짓는 사람) *kalja* (칼자 - 刀尺) or *toja ach'i* (刀자 아치). If so, *ch'ŏk* would mean *-jangi*, and *Kŭm* (金) could be the surname, or else it refers to a *ch'imjangi* (침장이 - acupuncturist) who used gold needles (金針). Passing Wŏlsŏng and heading towards P'osŏkjŏng Pavilion site, looking towards Nangsan Mountain (狼山), I thought of a certain master who lived at the foot of Nangsan. Amidst the bustling vitality, noise, filth, and chaos of Silla's peak era, this master created a separate world of tranquility and cleanliness. On New Year's Eve, while neighbors made festive sounds, pounded rice cakes (떡), celebrated, laughed, and enjoyed themselves, the master's world became even more distinct next to this clamor. This rare master, lest he remain entirely unknown to the world, was approached by his wife, who said, "My dear, the neighbors are pounding rice cakes, but we have no food. How shall we celebrate the New Year?" The master calmly rose, took his *kŏmun'go* (검은고 - traditional stringed instrument), tuned the strings "dong-dang-jing," and plucked "dang-dung-ttak," mimicking the sound of pounding rice cakes to console his wife. This tune became the *Taeak* (碓樂 - Pounding Mortar Music) in the Silla musical repertoire (樂府). Because this master lived wearing ragged clothes with more patches than whole cloth, people called him Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng (百結先生 - Master of a Hundred Patches). Alas, Silla, once so robust (우악우악하든), has now ended up emulating this master.

To the south, below Namsan Mountain, just before P'osŏkjŏng Pavilion, lies the site of Ch'angnimsa Temple (昌林寺). Kim Saeng (金生), the Silla master calligrapher, is said to have written the inscription for this temple's stele, but not only the man but also the inscription itself has vanished. The Historic Preservation Society displays Kim Saeng's writing from the Paegwŏlt'ap stele (白月塔碑文) with grand explanations, but claiming his calligraphy can only be seen here is a bit extreme. Surrounded by a square wall, inside which stand ancient trees, beneath the trees lies a stone channel (石渠), roundish-oblong like a whole abalone (全鰒). This is the site of the *Yusanggoksu* (流觴曲水 - floating wine cups on winding stream) of P'osŏkjŏng Pavilion, where the final act of Silla played out. Could they have imagined it? King Kyŏng'ae (景哀王), the noble sovereign of ten thousand chariots, fell helplessly under the savage sword of Kyŏn Hwŏn (甄萱). Queen Kyŏng'ae, the mother of the nation, suffered the most shameful humiliation in a nightmare, while terrifying flames reached the heavens. The scene of civil and military officials and three thousand court ladies being trampled, stabbed, rushing about in panic, and screaming is vividly imaginable. I stood there stiffly, like someone who had lost their mind. Ah, how transient! What more can be said? A companion, a man of excessive sentiment, poured wine from a red bottle and passed around cups. Though not exactly *yusang* (流觴 - floating cups), we drank, accepting it as such. This reminded me of a friend with the initial 'H' (何字號) who, upon my departure, pressed a coin (O) into my hand, saying he would give me a hundred (百O) if ---. I smiled inwardly, raising a mental toast to him. Then I murmured a verse:
Amidst the revelry at P'osŏkjŏng, why the fiery glow?
Did the lofty sovereign of ten thousand chariots fall by the sword's edge?<68>
Only the tree leaves trembling in the west wind seem like the old scene.

On the way back, on the south bank of the Munch'ŏn River, southeast of Onŭng (Five Tombs), we looked for Al'yŏngjŏng Well (閼英井). In a corner of rustling bamboo grove, there was a wooden marker (木標) reading "Al'yŏngjŏng." In front of it was an earthen mound completely unlike a well, and atop it lay a large slab of granite, so imposing it felt suffocating just to look at. Ah, who could have imagined this was Al'yŏngjŏng? Truly, utterly unexpected! Of course, mulberry fields can turn into blue seas (桑田碧海), but for the birthplace of a nation's queen mother (國母) to change, could it change this drastically? I wish to ask the people of Kyŏngju a question. Do you not take pride in Kyŏngju and, by extension, in the civilization of Chosŏn? If so, even if you cannot erect a memorial pavilion (紀念閣) at the queen mother's birthplace, wouldn't it be not so difficult, with just a little sincerity, to set up a proper marker of wood or stone? Yet, to leave it neglected, such that even the flimsy wooden stick marked with the three characters "Al'yŏngjŏng," which wouldn't have been visible without the Historic Preservation Society, is barely there – how can one not feel disheartened? Furthermore, the people of Kyŏngju are quite arrogant (驕慢). Throughout history, citizens of old, great nations are typically so, and indeed, the people of Kyŏngju possess the haughtiness from the time they unified the Three Kingdoms. People of Kyŏngju! You who boast of history, why do you not reflect on history? Didn't the historians explain the circumstances of Silla's unification of the Three Han, saying Paekche fell due to arrogance (驕慢), while Silla rose due to diligence (勤勉)? Need I explain further what becomes of arrogance? Reflect on your direct ancestors! Think of the people 2000 years ago, clutching their heads, closing their eyes, pondering how to make Silla an artistic nation, how to make it richer and stronger to earn the name P'ung'guk (豐國 - Abundant Nation). Do not boast of the vanished Silla; love Kyŏngju, which bears the imprint of Silla's thought. Develop Kyŏngju! In my opinion, Kyŏngju has little prospect for revival (振興). What can Kyŏngju, situated in a remote corner with arrogant people, develop with? People of Kyŏngju, rise up (奮起)! Even if Kyŏngju lacks geographical advantages for development, if you utilize sea and land transport well and sincerely foster commerce and industry, I believe there's no reason it cannot succeed. Isn't the fallen Silla a mirror for this? This well is also known as Ariyŏngjŏng (娥利英井) or Kyejŏng (鷄井 - Chicken Well), named because auspicious signs involving a rooster and dragon (鷄龍) appeared at Queen Al'yŏng's birth. The world-famous name Kyerim (鷄林 - Chicken Forest) actually originates from this. One theory claims that during King T'arhae's reign (脫解王), when Kim Alji (金閼智) was found, a rooster crowed in Sillim Forest, so Sillim was renamed Kyerim.<69> However, I doubt this. The palace where King T'arhae resided is the current site of Ch'angnimsa Temple, while Sillim is north of Wŏlsŏng Fortress. The distance between them - rooster crows and dog barks (鷄鳴狗吠) could not possibly reach each other. How could the king alone have heard the rooster's crow? It seems quite possible that Sillim was actually this pine forest of Onŭng (Five Tombs). Then, the distance is such that rooster calls could be heard, and even the name Sillim (始林 - Beginning Forest) seems fitting. This is just a suspicion. Since our group was large, various opinions emerged. Some questioned if this was really a well. Even if it were a well, could a person emerge from a well? Regarding the legend of Onŭng too, despite the tombstone clearly indicating they are the tombs of the Founder (始祖), Queen Al'yŏng (閼英后), King Namhae (南解), King Yuri (儒理), and King P'asa (婆娑) – five individuals – people talk nonsense, ignoring this and repeating the story that when they tried to gather and bury the founder's body, which scattered seven days after his ascension to heaven, a snake monster (蛇妖) prevented it. They don't consider the reason why such legends were created and only get caught up in their own cleverness. But there's no reason to blame them. If anyone is to be blamed, it is the historians of Koryŏ. The Koryŏ historians, though Korean by birth, had minds transformed by Tang China. They disregarded Korea's lineage, ignored the Korean national character, ignored Korean legends, and ignored Korean civilization, writing history in such a way as to make Korea a barbarian vassal of Tang. Unfortunately, this has shaped the mindset of people today – it is all the fault of Koryŏ historians. Yet, this cannot solely be blamed on Koryŏ historians either. Going further back, someone like Ch'oe Ch'iwŏn was a scholar of late Silla. A renowned scholar who left his homeland at the tender age of 12 to study abroad and returned. Even if he was too young when he left to know his own country's affairs well, if he had possessed even a modicum of sense, shouldn't he have taken some opportunity to record and transmit the circumstances of the land where he grew and would be buried? Instead, my words become mere grumbling; rather than writing and transmitting, it would have been better if he had, as he himself implied, wandered through mountains and waters like a madman. What he did write, licking the heels of Tang Chinese, striving to become a dog barking for its master (吠主犬), the traces of his earnest efforts are pitiful for him and deplorable for Silla. Truly, Ch'oe Ch'iwŏn should rightly be called the progenitor of 500 years of Sinophiles (慕華家). Sending children abroad to study who cannot distinguish between 'us' and 'them' is a dreadful thing. Perhaps when a country is about to fall, only scholars destined for ruin are born. Someone picked up a broken piece of something, stroked the mottled yellow-white patterns weathered by wind, and remarked, "Is this Hsiaohsiang mottled bamboo (瀟湘斑竹)? Did the tears of Ehuang and Nüying (娥皇女英 - legendary wives of Emperor Shun) splash this far?" Hearing about the tears of Ehuang and Nüying,<70> I thought of how those tears turned to blood, which led to other associations. The azalea (杜鵑花) is red with the bloody tears of the Shu Emperor's spirit (蜀帝靈魂); the Hsiaohsiang bamboo is mottled with the bloody tears of Ehuang and Nüying. Even in our Korean folk tales, the red spots on sorghum stalks (수수대) are said to be from the blood of a tick's (튘랑이) backside. But I wasn't thinking of these things, but rather that the spots on the reeds growing in the inlet (浦間) were red from the blood of Pak Chesang (朴堤上). When Pak Chesang was burned to death (燒殺) on Mokto Island (木島), his feet flayed, a bleeding mass of flesh horrifying to behold, he trudged steadfastly over the reed stubble, thinking "Even if I become a chicken or a dog...", guarding his loyalty, then climbed onto the sizzling hot iron plate – the scene is dreadful even to imagine. Five *ri* east of the town, we paid respects to the nine-story pagoda of Punhwangsa Temple (芬皇寺). The pagoda was built by shaping Andesite rock like bricks (煉瓦) into a square, nine-story structure. At some point, three stories collapsed. Later, temple monks attempted restoration but mishandled it, causing another three stories to collapse, leaving only three. However, during repairs undertaken by the Government-General (總督府) in Taishō 4 (1915), a stone casket (石函) was discovered inside, containing comma-shaped jade beads (句玉), glass beads (琉璃), gold fittings (金具), bells (鈴子), and numerous ornaments. Among them, a Koryŏ-minted *Sungnyŏng T'ongbo* coin (崇寧通寶) was found, providing evidence of repairs during the Koryŏ period. Perhaps this corresponds to the generation when the monks undertook repairs. Even just these three remaining stories have a grandeur that evokes the feeling of looking up at Kŭnjŏngjŏn Hall (勤政殿) in Kyŏngbok Palace. How could the conception be so grand and the construction so robust? I extrapolated from these three stories to imagine the full nine stories. Built by Silla's vibrant spirit aiming to unify the world and receive tribute from neighboring countries – perhaps designating the first story for country X, the second for country Y, the third for country Z, to display Great Power ideology (大國主義) to its people – it must have been imposing indeed. The nine-story pagoda, in its original form, would have soared above Kyŏngju's four mountains, piercing the heavens and looking down upon the world, truly representing the ideal of a great nation. However, the repairs done smoothed the top of the pagoda in an indescribably poor manner. Previously, the traces of collapse on the top clearly suggested it had been nine stories, but now it looks like a child's topknot, completely stunted (응태부리). At Pulguksa Station (佛國寺 驛), less than ten *ri* away, on the southern foot of T'ohamsan Mountain (吐含山), lies the somewhat dilapidated and shabby temple that is Pulguksa (佛國寺). Dr. Sekino's (關野 博士) explanation identified Pulguksa's Tabot'ap Pagoda (多寶塔), along with Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵), as unparalleled treasures of the world. According to him, architecture and sculpture similar to Pulguksa's Tabot'ap or Sŏkkuram do not exist in India or Tang China; even if they did, theirs would not be as exquisite. Moreover, their design (意匠) was solely conceived by Koreans. Even if<71> similar structures predating these existed in India or China, these were made by Korean hands, making them treasures of Korea as well as treasures of the world. He further elaborated, repeatedly stating that the Tabot'ap Pagoda is exceptionally beautiful in form, its ingenious design (奇想) intricately executed, displaying exquisite craftsmanship (精巧한 手工) as if granite were freely carved like wood. Facing Tabot'ap is Sŏkkat'ap Pagoda (釋迦塔), commonly known as the Shadowless Pagoda (無影塔). Though simpler in technique, its grand scale and balanced proportions (權衡) give it a remarkably elegant and light character. Pointing to the ruined foundation pillars (基柱) of Pŏmyŏngnu Pavilion (泛影樓), he noted they form a single-faced cross shape, tapering beautifully from bottom to top (下濶上窄) with fine curves – truly a stroke of genius from heaven (天來の奇想), divine craftsmanship (入神の 手工). The spiritual skill (靈腕) of the artisans of that time is truly astonishing. Then, pointing to the stone lantern (石燈) in front of the main hall (法堂), he said that although it might not look impressive, creating something so light and beautiful is actually very difficult, and in Japan, the only similar example is at Taimadera Temple (當麻寺) in Nara (奈良). Lacking any knowledge of engineering (工學), we had nothing to debate, but listening to the Doctor's enthusiastic explanation, our shoulders squared with pride. Another famous feature of Pulguksa is the stone bridges (石橋) located east and west of the main stairs (階段). The eastern upper bridge is Ch'ŏng'un (靑雲 - Blue Cloud), the lower is Paeg'un (白雲 - White Cloud). The upper part of Paeg'un Bridge is supported by a barrel vault (筒狀穹窿), while Ch'ŏng'un Bridge is supported by arch-shaped stones (編拱狀石材). Stone railings (石欄) are installed on both sides of the stairs. The structure of the western bridges, Yŏnhwa (蓮花 - Lotus Flower) and Ch'ilbo (七寶 - Seven Treasures), is generally similar to the former. Here, I had a thought. Yŏnhwa and Ch'ilbo are clearly Buddhist terms, but what do Ch'ŏng'un and Paeg'un signify? Ultimately, just as the nine-story pagoda of Punhwangsa politically symbolized unification, these temple bridges must symbolize religious integration (統合). Otherwise, there would be no reason to adopt Ch'ŏng'un, a term associated with Confucianism (儒敎), and Paeg'un, associated with Taoism/Sŏnism (仙敎). Taking the path behind the temple, crossing T'ohamsan Mountain, descending a bit, then turning north and proceeding a short distance, one finds a stone structure resembling the bald head of a monk. This is the renowned Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵). Carved into a break on the eastern slope of T'ohamsan, the grotto is constructed with granite. Though the site doesn't feel extremely high, it is still loftily situated like a fish pond (魚池間), overlooking smaller mountains in front and the distant East Sea (東海), making its location itself quite impressive. At the grotto entrance (入口), the left and right walls feature bas-relief carvings (薄肉刻) of the Four Heavenly Kings (四天王) and Guardian Kings (仁王像). In the center of the grotto, atop a stone lotus pedestal (石蓮臺), sits a large seated Buddha statue (丈六佛坐像). On the rear center wall is a standing eleven-faced Avalokiteśvara (11面觀音立像) in high relief (陽刻), flanked by five Arhat statues (羅漢像) and two Bodhisattva statues (菩薩像) on each side. Further up on the surrounding wall, five niches (佛龕) are carved on each side, housing seated figures: four Bodhisattvas on the left, and two Bodhisattvas, Kṣitigarbha Bodhisattva (地藏菩薩), and Vimalakīrti (維摩居士) on the right. The ceiling is dome-shaped (穹窿形).<72> The precision of its construction (搆築), the ingenuity of its design (意匠), and the skillfulness of its technique (手法) exhibit qualities of elegance and refinement (優雅精麗), truly representing the golden age of Silla art. Thus, the Japanese scholar Mr. Torii (鳥居) remarked: "Looking at the sculptures, one doesn't feel solemnity and awe, but rather affection and warmth. In a word, quite feminine. If masculine, then an affectionate, emotional masculinity." Recently, as the grotto suffered severe deterioration, repairs were also carried out in Taishō 4 (1915). However, "repair" didn't mean restoring the original form; the entrance ceiling was removed, making it resemble a round stone Buddhist shrine, losing the feeling of a "grotto" (窟) entirely. Now it looks somewhat like a kiln placed there, or perhaps like Bodhidharma's cranial protuberance (肉髻). Furthermore, rainwater leakage is worse than before the repairs, causing the sculptures to be coated white as if powdered, completely losing their ancient patina. Even now, cement plastering continues. In my opinion, rather than just plastering with cement, it would be better to fundamentally restore it to its original form. Repairs that lose the original form are alterations, not repairs, and defeat the purpose of preserving historic sites. The engineer (技師) supervising the repair work offered us a sort of encouragement, saying, "As preservers of historic sites, we are grateful to all of you who cherish your own heritage and come to see it," and proceeded to give unsolicited explanations. He claimed this Sŏkkuram Grotto could not possibly have been achieved without a geometric conception (幾何學的 搆想) in its construction; its location was carefully chosen so that at sunrise or moonrise, the light shines directly into the grotto, creating a truly magnificent spectacle (壯觀). He mentioned that Koreans believe this grotto was built facing the East Sea with the intention of conquering Japan, but he disagreed, suggesting its location signifies worship of the sun and moon (日月崇拜) or protection for sailing ships (航海船). He pointed out that the shoes (신) of one of the Four Heavenly Kings resemble Korean straw sandals (짚신) at the back and Japanese *waraji* (わらじ - straw sandals) at the front, concluding with great self-satisfaction that this symbolized Japanese-Korean harmony (日鮮 融和) even back then. I have a different argument regarding this. It requires no explanation that cultural systems flowed from the continent to the island nation (Japan) in ancient times. This influence extended to footwear as well; one can find ample evidence in Yi Hyŏn-sŏk's (李玄錫) *Yujaejip* (游齋集) that Japanese straw sandal and wooden clog systems were introduced from the continent. Furthermore, considering the motives behind the construction of the Nine-Story Pagoda and the Pulguksa stone bridges, the weakness of his explanation regarding Sŏkkuram's origins can be found. Therefore, when discussing the reason for building Sŏkkuram, I believe the engineer's intended biased explanation must also be included. While it's true that natural objects like the sun and moon were worshipped in ancient times,<73> isn't it also true that the Silla people who built the Nine-Story Pagoda and Pulguksa Temple infused Sŏkkuram with their own ambitious thoughts? Anyone wishing to know the past, especially the culture of the Three Kingdoms, should go and see Kyŏngju. Upon visiting, certain thoughts are bound to arise. I have read historical accounts that Koguryŏ's royal palace had a Crystal Castle (水晶城) one square *ri* in size. However, this cannot be verified today. I previously visited Puyŏ, the capital of Paekche. But it pales in comparison to Kyŏngju. Only here does one grasp the grand and far-reaching vision of antiquity. Look at the Nine-Story Pagoda. Look at Pulguksa, at Sŏkkuram. Then you understand the mind of the Silla people. How could they have been so grand, solemn, and meticulous? Compared to the Silla people who built mounds like mountains, the Chosŏn people of today, building tombs the size of small measuring boxes (되빡), seem entirely different, almost historically unrelated. Seeing the relics of Silla, pondering the thoughts of Silla, I desperately long to enter the Silla era of old. A people's thought varies with geography; perhaps the Silla people learned from the beauty of Kŭmgangsan Mountain (金剛山) and the vastness of the blue sea (滄海). Today's Chosŏn people see the same mountains and sea, yet... However, the cause of Silla's decline might also lie herein. At its peak, having annexed Paekche and trampled Koguryŏ, when national power and culture reached their zenith, complacency (自滿) and luxury (奢侈) followed alongside. Instead of building fortresses (城寨), they erected temples and pagodas (寺塔); instead of fostering the Hwarang (花郞 - elite warrior youth), they elevated nihilistic monks (虛無僧). The blue sea, the vast ocean (瀚海), where maritime skills (航海術) were practiced, turned into routes for transporting exotic birds and beasts from India and Persia (波斯). Kŭmgangsan, where artistic beauty was learned, transformed into a playground for idlers and profligates (閒人 蕩子). The dividing line between Silla's rise and fall was surely drawn here.

At the police station, I saw artifacts recently excavated. The most important items were not shown for some reason, and only a few were publicly displayed – and only for one day. There were many jewels (寶玉類) and pure metal implements and ornaments, but the most precious among them were said to be glass (琉璃) and crystal (水晶). A single crystal bead was valued at over 10,000 won, indicating its immense worth. Furthermore, the smelted glass was not only rare for that time, but it wasn't just plain glass; glass smelted around a core of earthenware material (砂器質) was truly astonishing. Another marvel was discovering undecayed fabric (옷감) pressed under the buckle (띄돈) of a gold belt (金帶). This fabric, resembling coarse barley grains, was similar to a summer Western-style suit made of hemp fiber (麻絲織). Gold armlets (金腕環) worn on the wrists and anklets (脚環) worn on the ankles were also found. Putting together this fabric and the armlets/anklets, some speculate whether people back then perhaps wore clothing similar to modern<74> Western attire. Or, considering the astonishing arts like the technology (工學) for smelting glass and porcelain (磁器), and architecture/sculpture, perhaps the modern Western civilization originated from places like Silla in the East, went dormant, and is now re-emerging? Who knows. Considering the historical records that merchants from Western countries like Arabia (西剌比亞) entered Silla and forgot to return, and the relationship between Koguryŏ and Central Asia, what might one conclude? Just as the Arabian merchants supposedly forgot to return, anyone seeing Kyŏngju would surely find it hard to leave.

As a final sightseeing stop in Kyŏngju, we rang the Pongdŏksa Temple Bell (奉德寺 鍾). This bell was cast during the reigns of King Kyŏngdŏk (景德) and his son King Hyegong (惠恭) in honor of their father/grandfather, King Sŏngdŏk (聖德). It required 120,000 *kŭn* (斤 - catties) of bronze (黃銅). It is a purely Korean-style bell, with gold and silver well blended, featuring a hook (旗指) at the top for hanging – something irreplaceable. When struck once, it resonates "weeeeng," and just as the sound seems about to fade, it continues again, "weeeeng," prolonging the sound wave, silently transmitting the lingering echoes of the old kingdom. Ah, what a fine bell! Even a slight flick with a fingertip produces a humming "wing-wing" that continues for several minutes. The Silla people considered the Nine-Story Pagoda, the colossal Buddha statue (丈六佛), and the jade belt (玉帶) as their Three Wonders (三奇). But people today consider Sŏkkuram Grotto, Tabot'ap Pagoda, the tortoise pedestal (龜趺) of King Muyŏl's stele, and this bell as the Four Treasures (四寶). These are not just Kyŏngju's Four Treasures; they are treasures that vividly illustrate our past civilization. If even these did not exist, what could we boast of or find solace in?

Having sent the rest of the group off a day earlier, I stayed one more night in Kyŏngju with a friend named O (吳), visiting a few remaining sights. The previous two nights, the room had been cramped, causing real hardship. We slept pressed against each other, quite chaotically. This day, we found a slightly cleaner inn (旅館) and rested comfortably, just the two of us. That night, this song came to mind: "Under the bright moon of Tonggyŏng (東京 - Kyŏngju), playing till dawn, I return to my bed to find, alas, four legs!" This is a line from the *Ch'ŏyongga* (處容歌 - Song of Ch'ŏyong). Coincidentally, the situation seemed fitting. It's said Shin Ch'ŏng-ch'ŏn (申靑川) and Ch'oe Tu-gi (崔杜機) were like husband and wife from a previous life in their friendship; perhaps O and I also share such a karmic bond (宿緣). The next morning, after an early breakfast, we walked north through the city streets for a while. The road leading north from the town towards P'ohang (浦項) is said to be the original Silla road. Years ago, when constructing the new road, they uncovered the embankments (右築) on both sides of the old Silla road while paving, allowing repairs<75> without much extra effort. It makes sense; why would the road have been narrow back then? Transportation corresponds directly to civilization. When the time came, we hired a car (自働車) and drove off, extending this road backward in our minds, talking about Yŏngji Pond (影池) and Kwaerŭng Tomb (掛陵), pointing towards Kyŏngju's famous eastern pass - Ch'isulryŏng (鵄述嶺) - as we sped towards Ulsan (蔚山). Though moving forward, my thoughts lingered, reluctant to leave, turning my head back repeatedly as if leaving my childhood hometown.

Preface (弁言)
This account (記) was written when the wind blew crisp and cool, and the leaves turned yellowish. That it is being printed now, when all things begin to hibernate and snow flutters down, is due to space constraints in the publication and other circumstances. In any case, there is undeniably a sense of being out of season. Speaking of lateness, talking about events of a thousand years ago now is already untimely. Perhaps reading this piece at this time might offer the solace of mentally switching between chilly winter and sweltering summer as appropriate. <76>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Kwŏn Tŏk-kyu recounts his journey to Kyŏngju, the ancient capital of Silla, in late 1921. He contrasts the decaying grandeur of Silla's past, embodied in its massive tombs, temples (Pulguksa, Punhwangsa), Sŏkkuram Grotto, and other relics, with the perceived decline and lack of spirit in contemporary Korea. The author reflects on Silla's history, achievements in art and architecture, societal aspects (including commentary on women's roles and character), and its eventual downfall due to arrogance and luxury. He critiques later historians for Sinocentrism and urges contemporary Koreans, especially the people of Kyŏngju, to learn from Silla's diligence, shed their arrogance, and strive for national revival. The piece mixes detailed descriptions of historical sites with personal reflections, historical anecdotes, linguistic discussions, and social commentary.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is revealing for several reasons. It showcases a strong sense of Korean nationalism and pride in the pre-colonial Silla heritage, using it as a benchmark against which the present (under Japanese rule) is judged and found wanting. The author's critique of perceived Korean arrogance and inertia can be seen as an internalized call for self-strengthening in the face of colonial domination. The interaction with the Japanese engineer at Sŏkkuram, including the engineer's interpretation favoring "Japanese-Korean harmony" and the author's silent rebuttal referencing historical flows from the continent, subtly reflects the power dynamics and competing historical narratives of the colonial period. Historians might find the blend of cultural pride, social criticism, and nascent nationalist consciousness, articulated just after the 1919 March First Movement, particularly interesting.

하야 支하얏스며 階段 兩房에 石欄을 設한 것이니 西의 蓮花, 七寶上下橋도 그 結構는 大槪 前者와 갓다. 나는 여긔서 이런 생각을 하얏다. 蓮華, 七寶는 勿論 佛敎의 文字어니와 靑雲 白雲은 무엇을 意味함인고. 이는 畢竟 먼저 芬皇寺 九層塔이 政治的으로 統一을 意味한 것가티 이 절 石橋는 宗敎上으로 統合을 意味함이라 한다. 그러치 아니하면 儒敎에 當한 文字 靑雲과 仙敎에 當한 文字 白雲을 取하얏슬理가 업는 것이다. 寺後로 路를 取하야 吐含山을 넘어 좀 나려가다가 北으로 찍겨 얼마 아니 들어가면 머리 빡빡 깍근 중대가리 가튼 돌집이 잇다. 이것이 그리 有名한 石窟庵이라. 吐含山 東麓 끈허진 곳을 파고 花崗石으로 窟을 짜아 노흔 것이니 터가 그리 登高한 줄은 모르나 또한 魚池間히 놉흐며 압흐로 잔 山을 깔고 멀리는 東海를 바라 그 位置부터가 그럴듯하다. 窟에 入口, 左右의 壁面에는 四天王과 仁王像을 薄肉刻으로 새기고 窟의 中央의 石蓮臺上에 丈六佛坐像을 모시고 後面의 中央에는 11面觀音立像을 陽刻하고 左右로 各 五軀의 羅漢像과 二軀의 菩薩像을 作하고 周壁上에 更히 左右 各 5處의 佛龕을 穿하고 그 안에 左方에 四菩薩, 右方에는 二菩薩과 地藏菩薩, 維摩居士의 坐像을 安하얏고 天井은 穹窿形으로＜72＞ 하얏는데 그 搆築의 精함과 意匠의 妙함과 手法의 巧함이 優雅精麗한 特質을 發揮하야 實로 新羅 藝術 黃金時代의 代表的 遺物이라 할지로다. 그리하야 日東의 學者 鳥居씨가 이러케 말하얏다. 어 彫像을 보면 그리 莊重하고 崇嚴한 생각이 나지 아니하고 사랑흡고 情답은 생각이 난다. 말하면 아주 女性이라 만일 男性이라 하면  愛的情的 男性이라고 하얏다. 이 窟이 近日에 오아 荒壞가 甚함으로 大正 4年에 이도 또한 修理를 加하얏는데 修理라 하면 原形그대로 하는 것이 아니라 入口의 天井을 헐어 업시하야 동글한 石造佛龕과 가티 되어 窟이라는 생각이 도모지 붓지 아니한다. 그리하야 어찌하면 가마갓다 노흔 것 갓기도 하며 또한 達摩의 肉髻갓기도 하다. 게다가 修理 前 보다 雨水가 滲漏하야 彫像이 하야케 粉을 발라서 아주 녯빗이란 족음도 업다. 只今도 세면트 칠을 자꾸 한다. 내 생각 가타서는 세면트칠만 하지 말고 根本的으로 原形대로 修補하는 것이 조흘 듯하다. 原形을 일흔 修補는 改造요 修補가 아니며 古蹟 保存의 本意를 일흔 것이라한다. 修理 工事에 監役하는 技師가 우리에게 一種의 勸勉을 주는데 古蹟을 保存하는 우리로는 自己의 古蹟을 아끼어 구경오는 여러분에게 感謝를 하노라 하며 잇대어 시기지 아니하는 說明을 한다. 이 石窟庵은 搆築이 幾何學的 搆想이 아니고는 到底히 이에 이를 수가 업스며 位置를 잘 가리어 아츰해 도들 적이나 저녁달 뜰 적이면 그 光線이 발우 窟안에 비추어 참으로 壯觀이라 하며 朝鮮 사람들은 이 窟을 東海上에 싸흔 것은 日本을 征服하랴는 意味로 하얏다 하나 나는 그가티 생각지 아니하나니 이 窟 자리를 여긔에 잡음은 日月을 崇拜하는 意味나 또는 航海船을 保護하는 뜻으로 한 것이라 하며 四天王 中 하나의 신은 신발이 뒤는 朝鮮 집신 꾸미는 듯 하고 압흔 日本 와라지 꾸미듯 하얏스니 이것으로 보면 벌서 그때에 日鮮 融和가 意味되엇다고 매우 自得하야 한다. 나는 이에 딴 議論이 잇다. 古代에 잇서 文物制度가 大陸으로부터 島國에 輸入된 것은 說明 할 것이 업거니와 이 신발에도 또한 그 影 響이 미쳣나니 그는 李玄錫의 游齋集을 보면 넉넉이 日本의 집신과 나막신 制度가 大陸으로부터 들어간 實徵을 어들 수 잇스며 그리고 이 石窟庵 싸혼 緣起도 9層塔에와 佛國寺石橋와에 비추어 그 說明의 弱함을 차즐 수 잇다. 그러하면 나는 石窟庵싸흔 緣起를 말할 때에 그 技師의 빼려는 說明까지 너허야 可하다 한다. 勿論 古代에 잇서 日月가튼 自然物을 崇拜함도 事實이어니와＜73＞ 九層塔을 쌋는 新羅 사람, 佛國寺를 짓는 新羅 사람이 石窟庵을 지을 때에 또한 엉큼한 생각이 그 속에 들엇슴 것도 事實이 아니랴. 누구든지 녜적을 알려는 이는, 더욱 三國의 文化를 알려는 이는 慶州를 가아 보아라. 가보면 무슨 생각이 꼭 닐어 날 것이니, 句麗의 王宮에는 方一里의 水晶城이 잇섯다는 歷史를 보앗다. 그러나 이는 只今에 상고할 수 업는 것이요 나는 먼저 百濟의 서울 扶餘를 보앗다. 그러하나 그것은 慶州에 비기어 遜色이 잇다. 여긔에 오아야 古代의 雄遠한 생각을 안다. 九層塔을 보아라. 佛國寺, 石窟庵을 보아라. 그러면 新羅 사람의 생각을 다 안다. 어찌면 그러케 雄大하고 壯重하고 緻密하얏든고 나는 只今에 되빡만콤씩 한 무덤을 쌋는 朝鮮 사람과 山덤이가티 쌋는 新羅 사람 사람과는 아주 딴판이어서 도모지 歷史上 關係가 업는 듯 하다. 나는 新羅의 遺物을 보고 新羅의 생각을 斟酌하고 新羅의 녯적으로 들어가고 십어 못 견디겟다. 國民의 思想은 地理를 딸아서 다르거니와 新羅 사람은 아마도 金剛山의 아름다움과 滄海의 넓음을 배워서 그러한 듯 하다. 只今의 朝鮮 사람도 그 山과 바다를 한 모양 보건마는, 그러나 新羅의 衰亡한 原因도 이에 잇다 할지니 한참 적 百濟를 倂하고 句麗를 蹂躪하야 國勢 文物이 絶頂에 達하 얏슬 때에 벌서 한 엽흐로 自滿과 奢侈가 딸아서 城寨를 쌋는 代身에 寺塔을 세우고 花郞을 뽀는 代身에 虛無僧을 놉히어 航海術을 練習하든 滄海, 瀚海가 印度 波斯의 珍禽奇獸를 나르는 航路로 變하고 藝術美를 배우든 金剛山이 閒人 蕩子의 花遊하는 場所로 化하얏다. 定코 新羅 興亡의 境界線은 이에 난후인 것이다.
警察署에서 近日에 파아나인 古物을 보앗다. 썩 重要한 것은 무슨 關係로 뵈지 아니하고 약간의 것만 ― 그것도 하루밧게는 公開하지 안핫다. 寶玉類와 純金屬의 器具와 裝飾品도 만커니와 그 中에 第一 珍貴한 것은 琉璃와 水晶이라 한다. 水晶 구슬 한 個에 10,000여원 價値를 가진다 하니 얼마나 高貴한 것임을 斟酌하려니와 더욱 琉璃를 고은 것은 그때에 안저서 稀罕한 것일뿐더러 琉璃라 하야도 그냥 琉璃만 고은 것이 아니라 속에 砂器質을 싸서 고은 것은 참으로 놀라운 것이며 또하나 神奇한 것은 金帶의 띄돈에 눌리어 썩지 아니한 옷감을 볼 수 잇슴이라. 이 옷감은 굵은 벼가튼 것이 麻絲織의 녀름 洋服차 비슷한 것이다. 손목에 두르는 金腕環 발목에 두르는 脚環이 나왓다. 그리하야 이 옷감과 腕環따위를 모아서 밀우어 생각하면 그때의 或時나 只今＜74＞ 洋服 비슷한 옷을 닙지 아니하얏는가. 또는 琉璃, 磁器를 고는 工學과 建築彫刻 等 놀라운 藝術을 合하야 보면 只今 西洋의 文明이 東洋의 新羅 가튼 대로부터 들어가앗다가 다시 再演되어 나오는 것이나 아닌가 하는 생각을 가지는 이가 잇다. 그것도 몰라 西剌比亞 等 西國의 商人들이 新羅에 들어가아 돌아가기를 이저버렷다는 歷史와 高句麗와 中央아세아와의 關係를 밀우어 생각하면 어떠할는지. 西剌比亞 사람이 들어가기를 이젓다는것가티 아무튼지 慶州를 보는 이는 참아 돌아가기가 실흘 것이다.
慶州의 맨 나종 구경으로 奉德寺 鍾을 울렷다. 이 鍾은 景德, 惠恭父子 兩代가 그 考祖되는 聖德님검을 爲하야 鑄成한 것이니 所入 黃銅이 12萬斤이라. 金銀을 잘 調合하고 首部에 旗指를 노흔 純朝鮮式 鍾으로 다시 어들 수 업는 것이다. 한번 울리매 위잉하고 울다가 그 소리가 끈허질만하야서 다시 위잉하고 音波를 繼續하야 故國의 남아지 소리를 잠잠이 傳한다. 아아 조흔 鍾이로다. 손끗으로 족음만 튀기어도 윙윙하고 數十分씩을 繼續한다. 新羅 사람은 九層塔, 丈六佛, 玉帶로써 三奇라 하얏거니와 只今 사람은 石窟庵, 多寶塔, 武烈陵碑의 龜趺와 이 鍾을 아울러 四寶라 한다. 이것이 慶州의 四寶만 될 뿐 아니라 우리 過去의 文明을 歷歷히 說明하는 보배가 이것이라. 만일에 이것조차 업섯든들 우리가 무엇으로 자랑하고 慰安을 어덧슬고.
一行은 하루 먼조 보내고 나는 吳라는 벗과 慶州에서 몃 군대 남아지 구경을 하고 하루 밤을 더 쉬엇다. 먼저 이틀은 방이 좁아서 果然 苦生하얏다. 서로 비고 서로 깔고 아주 야단으로 지내엇다. 이 날은 좀 精한 旅館을 어더서 단둘이 便히 쉬게 되엇다. 이 밤에 이런 놀애가 생각 나앗다. 「東京 밝은달에 새도록 노닐다가 들어내 자리를 보니 가라리 네히로새라」이것은 處容歌의 一節인데 偶然하게 境遇가 들어 마젓다. 申靑川과 崔杜機가 前生 夫妻로 友好하야 지냇다드니 나와 吳가 또한 그런 宿緣이 잇는지도 모르겟다. 밝는 날 일즉 밥을 먹고 市街를 北으로 通하야 한참 건닐엇다. 邑에서 北으로 浦項에 닷는 길은 新羅적 길 그대로라 한다. 年前에 新作路를 낼 때에 길을 깔다가 新羅 舊路의 兩便 右築이 들어나아 別로 힘들이지＜75＞ 아니하고 修理를 加하얏다 한다. 그러켓지 그때에 길이 좁앗슬 理가 잇나. 交通은 文明의 正比例하는데, 時間이 되어 自働車를 잡아타고 이 길을 뒤로 延長시기며 影池, 掛陵 이악이를 하며 慶州 東의 有名한 - 鵄述嶺을 가르치면서 蔚山으로 달아간다. 가기는 가면서도 생각은 참아 떨어지지 아니하야 마아치 生長한 故鄕을 떠나 듯 連해 고개를 돌이키엇다.
