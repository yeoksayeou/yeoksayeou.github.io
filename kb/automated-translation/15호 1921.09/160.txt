慰問의 歸路
						步月

머리의 힌 나비 한 마리,
病든 꼿을 慰問하고 돌아올 때,
西山에 걸린 無情한 놀 빗은,
病든 꼿의 입슐과 갓다.

道路에 선 포푸라의 그림자는,
자최를 딸아 길어지는 것이,
돌아가는 거름을 막는 것 갓고,
病든 꼿이 딸으는 것 갓다.

理想이 잇서도 實行을 못하고,
입이 잇서도 말을 못함은,
道德에 눌림이냐 習慣에 끌림이냐,
헛된 煩悶이 일로 蜃氣樓를 지을 뿐일가.

淸風을 取함이 罪가 아니고,
明月을 봄이 盜物이 아니면,
조흔 香氣를 마심이 무엇이 罪며,
고운 빗을 봄이 무엇이 盜物일가.

妹弟라 할가 사랑이라 할가,
妹弟라는 許諾도 업고,
사랑이란 對答도 업섯스니,
오즉 病든 꼿이라 하여둘 밧게.

異性을 차즘은 肉慾이 아니다,
너의 얼굴을 가린 머리를 쓰다듬어,
어여뿐 瞳子를 나에게 보이고,
연연한 목소리로 나의 鼓膜을 울려다고.

너의 눈에는 薔薇꼿이 피엇스나,
夫婦란 名稱이 이를 꺽거버리고,
너의 입슐에는 붉은 불꼿이 일어나나,
子女라는 氷塊가 이를 시킨다.

나는 안다 너의 가슴 속을,
新舊의 衝突이 至極한 줄을,
갈가 말가 하는 그 발자최가,
畢竟에 가는 길은 무덤의 속인 줄.＜138＞

愛友
사랑하는 벗아 사랑하는 親舊야,
너의 어대가 압하서 病院에 잇나,
압흔 곳은 어떠하며,
退院할 時期는 언제인가.

蒸氣가티 찌는 여름 日氣에,
시원한 바람 한 點 업는 房속에서,
呻吟하는 소리 입으로 샐 때에,
얼마나 나의 가슴을 놀라게 하엿는지 너는 아느냐.

뭇노라 사랑하는 나의 친구야,
막스 우에다 麻醉藥을 뿌릴 때에,
너의 靈魂은 어대로 갓더냐,
白玉가튼 살에 피나는 줄도 모르고.

어서 나서라 하루가 急히,
나는 너의 理想의 實行을 구경하리라,
이것을 보아 나는 作定하겟다.
친구로 繼續하던지 나의 愛人으로 하던지.

올마가는 香氣
(아아사아·시몬쓰作)... (譯)...

너의 머리털을 내게 시처어(振)다고
그리면 나는, 그 振動과 둘이 입마추고 난 길을 往來하는 熹微한 香氣를 맛기가 조흐니까,

밤은 이 戀愛의 無價値한 때를 주엇스나, 벌서 새벽이 急한 거름으로 곳장곳장 들어와서 그래서 너의 놀랜 얼굴을 드려다 보고 홀리어 잇다.

『갑니다』가 우리들 두 사람 사이에 들어와서 너는 幽靈가티 사라지고 말앗다.
아아 그래 空虛한 場所에는 오히려 너의 머리의 香氣가 남아 잇다.＜139＞
＜138-139＞

The Path Home from a Consoling Visit
						Powŏl

A single white butterfly, starved for rare fragrance,
Returning after consoling a sick flower,
The heartless sunset glow hanging on the Western Mountain,
Is like the lips of the sick flower.

The shadow of the poplar standing on the road,
Lengthening as it follows the footsteps,
Seems to block the returning steps,
Seems like the sick flower is following.

Having ideals yet unable to act,
Having a mouth yet unable to speak,
Is it pressure from morality (道德) or being dragged by custom (習慣)?
Will futile anguish (煩悶) merely build a mirage (蜃氣樓) from this?

If taking in the clear wind is not a sin,
And seeing the bright moon is not theft,
Then why is breathing a fine fragrance a sin?
Why is seeing a lovely color theft?

Should I call it sibling affection (妹弟) or love?
There was no permission for sibling affection,
And no answer of love,
So I can only call it a sick flower.

Seeking the opposite sex is not carnal desire (肉慾),
Stroke the hair covering your face,
Show your pretty pupils (瞳子) to me,
And make my eardrums (鼓膜) ring with your soft voice.

Though rose flowers bloomed in your eyes,
The title of husband and wife (夫婦) breaks them off,
Though red flames rise on your lips,
The block of ice called children (子女) cools them down.

I know what's inside your heart,
That the conflict (衝突) between old and new is extreme,
Those footsteps, hesitating whether to go or stay,
The path they ultimately take is into the grave. <138>

Beloved Friend
My loving companion, my loving friend (親舊),
Where does it hurt that you are in the hospital (病院)?
How is the painful place,
And when is the time for your discharge (退院)?

In the steamy summer weather,
In a room without a single point of cool breeze,
When the sound of groaning (呻吟) leaks from your mouth,
Do you know how much it startled my heart?

I ask, my loving friend,
When they sprayed anesthetic (麻醉藥) on the mask (막스),
Where did your soul (靈魂) go?
Unaware of the bleeding on your jade-like skin.

Get well soon, every day is urgent,
I shall watch the realization of your ideals,
Seeing this, I will decide,
Whether to continue as a friend or make you my lover (愛人).

Rising Fragrance
(By Arthur Symons)... (Translation)...

Shake (振) your hair towards me,
Then I like to smell the faint fragrance (熹微한 香氣) that comes and goes on the path where its vibration and I kissed,

Night gave this priceless time of love (戀愛), but already dawn, with urgent steps, comes straight in and, seeing your startled face, is mesmerized.

"I'm going" (갑니다) came between the two of us, and you vanished like a ghost (幽靈).
Ah, yes, in the empty place (空虛한 場所), the fragrance of your hair still remains. <139>
<138-139>

**Summary**

The article presents three poems from the September 1921 issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine. The first, "The Path Home from a Consoling Visit" by Powŏl, expresses repressed desire and frustration, likely towards a married woman ("sick flower"), constrained by societal norms ("morality," "custom," "husband and wife," "children") and the conflict between old and new values. The second, "Beloved Friend," also possibly by Powŏl (though unattributed), is addressed to a friend undergoing surgery, expressing deep concern and a hint of romantic ambiguity about their future relationship. The third is a translation of a sensual Arthur Symons poem, "Rising Fragrance," focusing on the lingering scent of a lover after a fleeting encounter.

From a historical perspective, Powŏl's first poem is particularly revealing. It captures the personal turmoil and societal constraints felt during a period of rapid change and modernization under colonial rule. The explicit mention of the conflict between "new and old" (新舊의 衝突) reflects the tensions of the era, where traditional Confucian values clashed with emerging modern ideas about love, relationships, and individual desire, often leading to personal suffering and repressed feelings, symbolized by the "sick flower" and the path leading "into the grave." The poem's melancholic tone and sense of entrapment could resonate with the broader national feeling under Japanese occupation. The second poem's contemplation of turning friendship into love also hints at evolving relationship dynamics.
