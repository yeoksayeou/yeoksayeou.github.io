The Terror of Recognition
						Kim Sŏk-song

Now the candlelight is almost gone,
The night is deep, and everything sleeps,
Only the tick-tock sound of the clock,
And the sound of the neighbor woman's fulling block can be heard.

At this time, in this place, I recognize that I exist.

But eventually the candlelight will go out, the sound of the fulling block will cease,
And the tick-tocking sound of the clock will also cease.

Even until then, I shall recognize myself.

Ah, but, but eventually,
That (such) recognition of mine will also disappear,
I clearly recognize this, from now,

Ah, the nothingness (虛無) of all things!
Ah, the terror of recognition, (1,15) <29>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏk-song expresses a profound sense of existential dread stemming from self-awareness. The speaker, isolated in the late night, becomes acutely aware of their own existence ("I recognize that I exist"). However, this recognition is immediately followed by the terrifying realization that this very consciousness, along with all external perceptions (candlelight, sounds), is transient and will ultimately cease, leading to "the nothingness of all things." For historians, the poem reflects the intellectual climate of 1920s colonial Korea, possibly mirroring the anxieties, nihilistic tendencies, or philosophical questioning among intellectuals facing the suppression of national identity and the uncertainties of the era. The stark confrontation with mortality and the potential meaninglessness of existence provides insight into the psychological landscape under colonial rule.

認識의 恐怖
						金石松
인제는 초ㅅ불도 다 되얏습니다,
밤은 깁헛고 모든 것은 잡니다,
時計의 재각재각하는 소리와,
이웃집 아낙네의 다듬이 소리만 들님니다.

나는 이때 이곳에 내가 잇슴을 認識합니다.

그러나 마츰내 초ㅅ불은 꺼질 것이오 다듬이 소리도 긋칠 것이오,
재각재각하는 時計 소리도 긋칠 것이외다.

나는 그때까지라도 나를 認識할 것이외다.

아, 그러나 그러나 마츰내,
(이와 가튼) 나의 認識도 업서질 것을,
나는 분명히 認識합니다, 只今부터,

아, 모든 것의 虛無!
아, 認識의 恐怖, (1,15) ＜29＞
