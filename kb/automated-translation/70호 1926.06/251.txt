餘滴
◯
After finishing the editing, I listen, and the mournful sound of the bird of restoration (復國새) is heard from all directions.

Time truly flies. Over thirty days have already passed since Emperor Yunghŭi (隆熙皇帝) passed away (昇遐), and the date of the State Funeral (國葬) is merely ten days away. How deeply sorrowful must our Chosŏn (朝鮮) people, who have always had much bitterness and pain, feel?

◯
However, we cannot resolve all the grievances and dissatisfactions of the entire nation merely with tears of grief. We must take another step forward from the top of a hundred-foot pole (百尺竿頭), engage in a movement for rebirth (更生), and seek a way to turn tears into laughter (化淚爲笑).

However, it is a great regret among regrets that, due to many restrictions in the environment (環境), we could not express [missing] thoroughly according to our original intention (本意). We hope (■■■한다) that our readers (讀者諸位) will especially understand (諒解) this point.

**Summary**

This editorial postscript from the June 1926 issue of Kaebyŏk magazine reflects on the recent death of Emperor Sunjong (Yunghŭi Hwangje) and the national grief. It calls for moving beyond sorrow towards national rebirth ("another step forward from the top of a hundred-foot pole"). The editors announce a special issue focusing on 500 years of Chosŏn history to aid this process but lament that "environmental constraints" (likely censorship under Japanese rule) prevent a thorough treatment. They also apologize for postponing two articles due to space limits and promote the upcoming sixth-anniversary special issue. For historians, the piece is interesting for its veiled nationalist sentiment ("bird of restoration"), its attempt to foster national consciousness through history despite censorship, and the explicit mention of external pressures limiting their editorial freedom.
