Does Spring Come Even to the Stolen Fields?
						By Yi Sanghwa

Now it is another's land—does spring come even to the stolen fields?

I receive the sun's rays upon my whole body
And walk as if in a dream along the paddy ridge road
Toward where the blue sky and green fields meet.

O sky and field, lips sealed,
It feels as though I haven't come here alone.
Did you pull me? Did someone call? It's frustrating, please speak.

The wind whispers in my ear,
"Don't stop even for a step," shaking the hem of my clothes,
And the lark, like a young lady beyond the fence, smiles gladly from behind a cloud.

O barley field, thankfully well-grown,
With the gentle rain that fell past midnight last night <9>
You have washed your hair like threefold silk; my head too feels light.

Let's go, lightly, even if alone.
The good ditch embracing the dry paddy
Sings a song soothing a nursing babe, dancing along with its shoulders alone.

Butterfly, swallow, don't flutter about so.
I must greet the cockscombs and wildflowers too.
I miss them all, those fields weeded by someone with castor oil in their hair.

Place a hoe in my hand.
This soft earth, like a plump breast,
I want to tread it till my ankles ache, and shed good sweat too.

Like a child come out to the riverside,
My soul, running endlessly without knowing time,
What are you seeking? Where are you going? It's laughable, please answer.

My whole body steeped in the scent of green,
Between mingled blue laughter and blue sorrow,
I walk the day limpingly. Perhaps the spring spirit has possessed me.
But now—the fields are stolen, and spring too will be stolen <10>

A Morning After Rain

The rain that poured all night has now stopped,
And the eastern sky is finally turning reddish.
Over this land, quiet as if waiting,
The sun rises gently.

This dazzling land,
This beautiful land,
This world is so bright and clean
I feel hesitant even to step forward.

The sun seems to have given its milk to all things.
Look, friend!
Doesn't everything before and behind us
Grasp and suck on the strands of sunlight?

Could there be such joy again?
Could there be such a good thing again?
This land seems like a bundle of love.
Ah, our lives today seem blessed too. <11>

Moonlit Night - Metropolis

Above the dust-covered roofs,
The moon lifts its head and stands.

The poplars on the street, their thick leaves parted, flutter in the gentle breeze,
Like a person startled, dropping the money held in their hand,
They look up at the sky, scattering silver pieces, trembling.

Thin clouds, like raw cotton,
Drift towards the moon, towards the moon,
Making the eyes dizzy, as if standing upon the sea.

Do people not know they are clothed entirely in moonlight?
In twos and threes, they pair up, chattering casually.
No, when they laugh, there is moonlight in their mouths; it must be the moon.

Ah, let's turn off the lamps, at least for tonight!
Like a country maiden, in the corner of the room, under the eaves,
Look at the lamp, blushing as it sees the moon.

Even on the glass window of the back alley toilet,
The moon descends and dreams. <12>

**Summary**

The document presents three poems by Yi Sanghwa published in 1926. The first and most famous, "Does Spring Come Even to the Stolen Fields?", expresses deep sorrow and nationalistic longing for Korea under Japanese rule. Despite the beauty of the spring landscape, the poet feels the pain of occupation, questioning if true spring can exist in a "stolen" land, ultimately fearing that spring itself will be taken away. The second poem, "A Morning After Rain," offers a contrasting mood of hope and renewal, celebrating the beauty and life-giving power of nature after hardship. The final poem, "Moonlit Night - Metropolis," depicts an urban scene under the moon, observing the alienation of city dwellers from nature and suggesting a preference for natural light over artificial lamps. For historians, these poems vividly capture the complex emotions of Koreans during the colonial period: the profound sense of loss and suppressed resistance ("Stolen Fields"), moments of finding solace and hope in nature ("Morning After Rain"), and reflections on modernity and urban life under external control ("Moonlit Night"). The use of nature imagery intertwined with political sentiment in "Stolen Fields" is particularly significant.

나는 온 몸에 해살을 밧고

바람은 내 귀에 속삭이며
한자욱도 섯지마라 옷자락을 흔들고

살찐 젓가슴과 가튼 부드러운 이 흙을

나는 온 몸에 풋내를 띄고
푸른 웃슴 푸른 설음이 어우러진 사이로
