MALHEUR
						P'alp'ong

February X, 1925 — I was forced to become the young father of a barely one-day-old baby girl named Palgasung'i. That is to say, my partner had finally given birth to the child.

I cannot now recall how I spent the week after she came into the world. Friends I was always close with came to visit. Some teased me, laughing. There were also respected friends who offered sincere advice. My heart was in turmoil from this embarrassing experience, the first of its kind on this journey of life.

For a year, I had been living independently in my own house. It was the base for the two of us. Of course, since it was a rented house, our living situation goes without saying. And now, one more family member had been added to this house. However, contrary to expectations, the birth of this new life began to trouble us considerably.

One day, the day after just one week had passed, almost all the visitors who came to see me, namely A, P, R, and others, gathered in the cramped room across the hall of my house, when someone, I don't recall who, asked me:
"So, have you chosen a name for the baby?"
"Not yet."
I answered thus. The thought that I had already fathered a child and now had to name her made me hesitate, and besides, I thought it right to entrust this task to the child's grandfather.
"Let's pick a name." <23>
Said R, who was in his thirties.
"What's the point? Let's just leave it."
I replied unenthusiastically.
"Oh, you can't do that. A name is essential."
"Yes, a name must be chosen."
"Hey, you people saying 'a name must be chosen'—are you saying not to choose one?"
"No, I mean, choose one—"
They each added a word. The friend who liked joking suggested naming her "Kaettong'i" (Dog Dung) or "Twitkan" (Outhouse), and eventually, all sorts of ridiculous suggestions came out.
"Well then, what should we name her—?"
Following their lead, I laughed awkwardly and vapidly, then finally, as if seizing upon my own thought, I spoke thus. Then A suggested:
"If we're doing it anyway, why not choose a Western name? Huh? How about that?"
"That's great!"
"Let's do that."
"Like, say, Nestor, or Bakunin, like that…"
"Why are you only listing anarchist names?"
"No, if it were a girl, naming would be really easy, huh? For example, Eden, or Katerina, or Sonya… aren't they all easy-to-say names?"
"Why are you only mentioning Russian names?"
Unable to bear my friends' frivolous chatter any longer, I interjected like this. Then the friend who first suggested a Western name replied:
"Don't such names feel quite familiar to us? I think Anna Karenina sounds the best—"
Not particularly wanting to oppose this, I said,
"Well, that's true—"
And shut my mouth. And so, within my heart,不知不覺中 (at some point), an impulse began to arise, wanting to choose a name myself, whatever it might be. What should I name her? What should I name her? Let's choose a simple name that's easy to call, concise, and sounds good—I finally thought like this. <24>
"Well, a good name doesn't come to mind suddenly, and choosing one deliberately isn't fun either, so how about we close our eyes like fortune-telling, pick a letter blindly from a book, and make that the name?"
I finally said this and asked for the opinions of the friends in the room.
"That's not bad either."
"If we're doing it, let's just do it, what's there to ask?"
My two friends, R and P, answered thus. I picked up a horizontally written book lying nearby on the desk. It happened to be the original text of Saint-Pierre's pastoral romance novel, *Paul et Virginie*.
"Okay, I'll close my eyes, open this, and pick any letter—"
In that moment, with a somewhat hesitant heart, I held the book and muttered this.
"It'll be a failure if a strange, hard-to-read word comes up—"
P voiced the thought that was in my mind at that moment.
So, echoing his words, I smiled faintly, closed my eyes, opened the book to a random place, fumbled across the page with my middle finger, randomly pressed down on one spot, and then opened my eyes.
"What letter is it?"
"What does it say?"
P and A, sitting nearby, asked this before I even had a chance to look, craning their necks to see the tip of my finger pointing at the book on the desk.

malheur

"Malheur... Malheur......"
P muttered under his breath.
"This truly means 'Misfortune'!"
My lips cried out. This cry was something I had truly never anticipated, not even in my dreams. When had I ever anticipated "Misfortune"? Who could have guessed, even one minute before, that on this unexpected day, sitting with friends and engaging in idle talk, I would accidentally choose the name "Malheur" for my first child? I think no experience of our worthless, idle talk and playful jokes causing me unpleasant feelings was ever as severe as this time! I declare, I am not superstitious. But <25> who, indeed, faced with this unfortunate incident, would feel pleasant? On the spot, I regretted my own carelessness and, conversely, also mocked my own timidity.

× × × ×

This is the beginning of the story I intend to write now. After this unexpected and unpleasant event, whenever I had leisure time, I developed a habit of thinking absentmindedly, turning over and over the word "Malheur" that I had picked with my eyes closed. Malheur... Malheur... No matter how many times I mulled it over, its meaning was nothing other than "Misfortune." Should I interpret it as my misfortune, or my child's misfortune—? Asking myself questions like this, I would again realize the foolishness of it and try, more than once or twice, to banish such thoughts from my mind.

One day, about six or seven months later, during a quiet time, I was unexpectedly caught by this thought again, and before my awareness could seal my lips, I turned to my wife and said,
"But why did I happen to pick that word 'Misfortune' back then? No matter how much I think about it, I find it unbearably strange."
—Of course, my child's name was not "Malheur," but a fine name written in Hanja characters chosen by her grandfather from an Okp'yŏn (Hanja character dictionary). However, my wife replied,
"Oh! What unpleasant things you say. What use was such an act that you can't forget it now?"
She made a displeased face. I regretted having let those words escape my lips again.

The child was sturdy. Our only worry was the rash (胎熱 - t'aeyŏl) on her face due to congenital heat, which made her unattractive. So we tried all sorts of medicines. After applying medicine, her face would become unsightly like a grotesque rock in a flowerbed, but when a layer peeled off, new, tender, beautiful skin would appear. However, before long, one or two bumps of the rash would continue to emerge, pushing through that fine skin.

"They say the congenital heat won't disappear until she starts walking... It looks awful, what should we do......"
When my wife said this, looking at my face, I <26> replied,
"Well, that much shouldn't be a worry. Once she passes her first birthday, it will likely vanish as if washed away."
And I didn't trouble my mind over it.

Thus, regardless of our worries and caution, the child grew bigger day by day. She fell three times from the pram (랑코), which was set up over two feet high, yet slept peacefully that night and didn't get sick. After seven months, two pearly white teeth emerged, top and bottom, and she chewed things with them. At eight months, she crawled around the room by herself, pulled herself up holding onto the legs of the wardrobe, opened the doors left and right, and pulled out clothes, throwing them onto the floor. She even walked from one corner to the other holding onto the wardrobe. At such times, my wife would rush over, afraid she'd fall, pick her up, and sit her on her lap. And if you called out "Jjam (peek-a-boo?)", the child would open and close her fern-like fingers for a long time. When my wife patted the child's mouth with her palm, she would make cute sounds like "Awawawawawa... awawawawa..."
"○○-ah—"
"Ne—" (Yes)
"Say Appa (Daddy)—"
"........."
"Say Ŏmma (Mommy)—"
"Ŏmma."
My wife, trying to teach her words like this, upon hearing "Ŏmma," would lightly pick up the child, sit her on her lap, and kiss her soft, cotton-like rosy cheeks, losing track of time like the second hand of a clock. Watching such scenes, I would bury myself in the bliss of a smile, like a man of forty or fifty, forgetting my own age in an unknown joy.

× × × ×

However, economically, we were in the utmost dire straits. I had been laid up for over a month due to the summer heat, taking herbal medicine. And because the place where I worked was in financial difficulty, I hadn't touched a 'salary' for three consecutive months. The rent was three months overdue, the firewood bill two months overdue, and every three days, someone would come to the gate and shout orders like forceful raindrops. But there was no source of money. Yet, this was something we could endure; <27> the most critical problem, bringing tens of times more pain than that, was the question: What will we eat? What will we burn (가)? What will we make food with?—We had nothing.

We pulled out an empty fish crate (케 ) sent from the countryside at the end of last year from the shed, broke it up to make fire, and the few books rolling around in the corner of the room, without even covers, flowed out to the second-hand book dealers in Chin'go-gae in exchange for two doe (unit of volume) of rice in a paper bag, which we used to cook rice. Among our possessions, the only things of meager value were already in the pawnshop.

One morning, before I had fully woken from a heavy sleep, I dimly perceived the sound of a woman's weeping, not loud but just sniffing, coming from somewhere within the room where I lay. Forcing open my sleep-stuck eyes, I looked around my pillow. The day outside seemed bright, but the room was still dim with the morning air, barely allowing shapes to be seen. But then, I clearly saw my wife sniffing as she adjusted the mosquito net (모긔장). I realized the person crying was my wife.
"Why are you crying?"
Seeing this unexpected event, I couldn't help but ask. But my wife didn't answer easily.
"No, why are you crying? From early morning. Huh? Answer me—are you mute?"
I shouted unpleasantly.
"That damn shopkeeper old man (가가영감), how dare he look down on people—has our grocery bill been overdue five months or six? Before, did we ever let even a month pass? We paid diligently—. But unlike other times, the master of the house is sick in bed, hasn't received his salary for three months, so this is the first time it's piled up like this, I told him every time I went to get side dishes—that damn old man…………"
My wife's voice was choked with tears.
"So, he was dunning you for the bill again?"
I asked, sitting up in bed.
"It would be good if it were just the bill! Twice a day,"
"When will the money come?" the blunt old man used to ask, but today he pretended not to see me at all. I went out to get seaweed to make seaweed soup <28> and asked for just ten chŏn worth of firewood, but even after calling out five or six times 'give me some firewood,' he pretended not to hear. The neighbor's wife, who also buys on credit, came later than me, and he went up to her asking, 'What have you come to get?' while pretending not to hear my words. It's truly infuriating! After asking several times for about twenty minutes, I couldn't stand it anymore and just came back…………"
My wife wiped her tears with the ribbon of her blouse (옷고름).
"And you're crying because of that?"
I felt somewhat angry, yet also somewhat amused. At that moment, my wife, who had been wiping her tears, bit the blouse ribbon, then looked up at my words, glanced at me with her swollen, red eyes, and perhaps seeing the shadow of a smile playing around my lips, she lowered her head deeply as if embarrassed. Her appearance seemed pitiful to me. But I intentionally
"Well then, why didn't you shout, 'Why are you pretending not to hear after I've asked so many times?' grab the stuff, and come back? Why stand there blankly like a fool and just come back?"
And opened my eyes wide.
"Even when I go to buy things with cash, if I say I want to choose this or that, he shouts 'What are you choosing from such things?' and snatches the items from another woman's hand, that old man. How could I possibly do that without causing a huge scene?"
"Then if you couldn't buy it, that's that. What use is crying about it?"
I said again in a softer voice.
"Thinking about it, isn't it sad! That I have no money—even though it's credit, I'll eventually pay it back—to be treated so contemptuously even when buying things with my own money, it's infuriating and unjust. Moreover, that damn old man, even though you're sick like this, he hasn't once come to inquire after your health… No matter how much the world revolves around money, where is such humanity? Thinking about the humiliation I suffered from that bastard today…………"
My wife, speaking thus, became sad again and finally let a tear fall. Knowing there was no need to speak further on this matter, I said nothing.

After a while, my wife got up and went to the kitchen, then hearing the baby cry from hunger, she came back in, wiping her wet <29> hands on her skirt hem, and while breastfeeding, she sighed as if talking to herself:
"Money! Money… What is money………… Truly, if only we could just pay off the debts of those devilish creditors, and somehow manage to eat and dress without being hounded by the debt collectors, that would be good……"
"Hmph! Fine words! Even if you pay your whole life, you can never pay off all the debts…………"
I chimed in, also as if talking to myself, and watched my wife breastfeeding.
"What debts are so large that they can't be paid off in a lifetime?"
My wife asked, seemingly surprised.
"How, and with what, can they be paid?"
"No, the current debt, wouldn't about two hundred Won clear it all up?"
"Yes—"
"Then let's pay it all off when the salary comes, grit our teeth, and not borrow from others.—Even then, you can't pay it off in a lifetime?"
"While paying off debts, you incur more debts!"
"Why—?"
"Because the world is made that way—"
"You too—"
"Ha! Isn't it maddening? We are—the rich are the exact opposite of us—we are lives born into this world to incur debt, and leave this world unable to repay it!"
"It's fate!"
My wife said this one word as soon as my words ended.
Picking up on her words, I said, almost sighing:
"Right! That is the fate of the proletariat."

January X, 1926 — The child started to ail, suffering from both a cold and indigestion combined. In terms of years, the child had been in this world for two years, but in terms of days, she had not yet reached a full eleven months. I was healthy, and although my income was not as constrained as last year, it was, however, inconsistent.

The child had already begun to stand up from a sitting position on the floor without holding onto anything and take about ten steps. <30> When my wife and I sat side by side, she would crawl around playing, then stand upright on the floor, raise both hands slightly, and shout "Ttae... ttae... ttae... ttae..." as if performing the greatest trick in the world, looking at her mother and me as if asking us to watch, then collapse as if exhausted. She developed a habit of answering "Ne—" (Yes) whenever she heard a street vendor shouting their wares. She learned to distinguish the situations for using the five words: "Appa" (Daddy), "Ŏmma" (Mommy), "Ajjae" (Uncle?), "Innae" (Give me?), "Ŏtta" (Where?). When the dining table was brought in—we had hired a cook since winter—she would rush over first, rummage through the rice bowl with her hand, grab about a fistful of rice, and stuff it into her mouth. My wife initially tried to stop this, but since the child didn't drink milk well and attacked the rice greedily like someone possessed whenever she saw it, eventually, my wife had no choice but to place a separate bowl of rice mixed with soup broth beside the table and feed her that. She also developed the habit of opening the main room door by herself and going back and forth to the room across the hall.

And the habit of hating to leave the spot in front of the window grew stronger, so even if the weather was cold, on sunny and warm days, we would bundle her up warmly, carry her on our back, and stroll outside. And so, finally, this child began to ail with a cold and indigestion.

At first, we got Western medicine from a known hospital, fed it to her, kept her from catching drafts, stopped her from sneaking bites of plain rice—internally not too worried—and passed two or three days. However, after a few days, we discovered that her entire body had broken out in tiny red spots like millet grains.

"Honey! What is this… This child is definitely not like this just from a cold or indigestion. Let's quickly call a doctor." My wife's eyes widened as she spoke in panic.
"Okay—right. I'll go call one right away."
Feeling a bit scared myself, I immediately jumped up, ran to the streetcar track, boarded a streetcar right away, and headed towards the hospital I knew well from before.
"Well, I'll be right there, so you go ahead."
Hearing these words behind me, I left the hospital and returned home.
—What if it's measles (紅疫)? We worried like this, then heard the voice from outside saying, "The doctor has arrived." <31> We ran out, opened the door, and welcomed him.
"—How is she? What illness is it? Is it serious?"
I looked at K's face, then at the child's face, and asked this.
"Hmm, as of yet, I can't say for sure what it is, but there's certainly a strong suspicion. But it's not serious—medicine should clear it up soon."
"Scarlet fever (猩紅熱)—?"
"Yes—"
"This is serious."
"What are you worried about? Nothing to worry about."
"Really?"
When I asked this, he replied with a nod.

Thus, my wife and I watched over the child's illness with somewhat relieved hearts. However, at night, I was too tired to even open my eyes once and continued to sleep soundly.

After a few days, the millet-like spots had all died down, leaving bluish marks, and the areas where they had erupted seemed to be peeling like shed skin. But at the same time, on the left cheek, where a scab from a boil caused by the earlier congenital heat had stubbornly remained through several occurrences, a red boil started again, and my wife discovered a large lump (멍을) under the left jaw and told me. But I told my wife not to worry about such things.

However, the boil on the face and the lump under the jaw worsened while I wasn't paying attention. So finally, we called Dr. K again, showed him, and started applying medicine.
"The eruption on the face is nothing to worry about, but the lump under the jaw, if it doesn't resolve on its own, will eventually require surgery, so we must make it subside from the beginning."
The doctor said this while applying a black plaster to the child's jaw.

However, even after several days, the lump did not subside but grew increasingly hard, a higher fever developed than when she first fell ill, and she continued to have green bowel movements. Therefore, thinking that since people in the old days also raised children, it would be best to consult a traditional Korean medicine doctor (漢方醫), I called the most famous one in the vicinity of my neighborhood, explained the entire course of the illness from the beginning, <32> and then asked, "By the way, the doctor trained in modern medicine initially diagnosed scarlet fever based on the pulse (執脈) and prescribed medicine. What do you think, Teacher?"
The doctor looked at the child's skin with its bluish marks for a while and said,
"Good heavens—. What scarlet fever? This is simply due to indigestion. When children have indigestion, they often run a high fever… When the fever is high, it's common for rashes like this to appear and subside. Anyway, try the pills I give you for a few days. The stools shouldn't be green like this either."
After saying this, he left. We found his words plausible and followed his instructions. However, although the green stools became less severe afterwards, the lump under the jaw reddened as if it was increasingly filled with pus (골음), and tormented by this, the fever only rose higher and did not subside. K, who had been seeing her from the beginning, saw this situation and said that the green stools and poor appetite were all due to the fever, and the cause of the fever was the lump. Therefore, the lump needed to be resolved before it festered, but since it wasn't resolving even with the plaster meant to reduce it, he instructed me to try a poultice (濕布). However, even after applying poultices for two days, it only got worse. So, I went back to the traditional doctor I had seen later and told him about this. He said that the lump already looked like it contained pus when he saw it last time, so it couldn't be cooled down now. Applying things like poultices would only prevent the pus from draining quickly, needlessly prolonging the condition and harming the child. He advised leaving it alone and preparing for surgery. He said that surgery would allow it to heal within ten days and leave no scar. I relayed this to K, and K also agreed with the traditional doctor's opinion, so we left it alone.

Then indeed, one night—two days after we stopped applying the poultice—my wife shook me awake to show me that the lump on the child had burst open on its own and pus was flowing out. As it was just before dawn, I quickly dressed and went out to fetch K.

K was an internal medicine doctor. However, as it was not yet light and I didn't even have a penny for a rickshaw fare, I had no means to call a surgeon skilled in external medicine, so I called K. But K didn't seem particularly inept. He rushed in and immediately began scraping out the impure matter from the affected area with instruments sterilized in boiling water. A spoon-like instrument (긔이개) went in about one inch <33> and five fen (approx. 4.5 cm), drawing a circle centered on the wound opening, scraping out the filth within. In essence, this was major surgery. But the child kept her mouth tightly shut and endured it like a sensible child.—

Thus, after the surgery ended and a gauze dressing (흐름-제) was packed in, K promised to come himself every day to change the dressing (繡帶交換) and left. From that day, the child's fever subsided slightly, and she seemed less uncomfortable. However, even after a week, far from the wound healing (合瘡), there was no sign of new flesh growing. Then, perhaps due to another digestive upset, she started passing stool mixed with undigested milk. I tried everything possible, praying fervently for her swift recovery.

However, one day by chance—about twenty days after she first began to ail—a hard spot appeared under the skin in front of her left ear, like a red dot had been marked there. The next day, similar spots appeared on her forehead and in front of her right earlobe (귀ㅅ바귀). K, who came daily to change the dressing, saw them and told me to apply the same black plaster used when the lump first appeared. But it had no effect (效驗). The bright red spots grew larger and spread here and there.

The child didn't sleep at night either. My wife stayed up all night with the child. Thus, on the third day, when I asked K with a worried look, he said,
"Well, it's not critically dangerous, but pyogenic bacteria (化膿菌) have entered and are traveling along the lymph nodes (淋巴線), causing suppuration (化濃)………. However, erysipelas (丹毒) looks exactly like this, so if it's not lymph node suppuration but erysipelas, it could be considered dangerous. But right now, I can't say it's erysipelas. Still, hospitalization would be good, so let's arrange for admission."
"Then shall we hospitalize her?"
"Yes, especially if it's erysipelas, there's an urgent need for hospitalization. And it's safer—."
After he left, I finally began to doubt his knowledge and decided to hospitalize her. I went to the company (社) and pleaded for an advance of thirty or forty Won. But ultimately, the money couldn't be arranged. And since it happened to be a Saturday, after 5 PM, hospitalization wouldn't be possible until Monday, skipping Sunday. Thus, unable to get the money, I couldn't hospitalize her and came home empty-handed. <34>

When I got home, I saw the child was already more than half gone. My wife sat with the child on her lap, looked at me, and said tearfully,
"What should we do?"
Indeed, the child was in a comatose state. Her head lolled back every twenty seconds on average, drool dribbled from her mouth, and her eyes, held in her mother's arms, lacked sight. Seeing this, my eyes instantly widened.
"Honey, since when has she been like this?"
Seeing my panicked state, my wife suddenly felt the danger more acutely and blamed me for returning late.
"How could you go out for money, knowing full well there was no one else at home, and stay out so late? I wanted to go to the hospital myself, but I couldn't leave her with the cook (어멈), I almost died from anxiety. She's been like this ever since you left earlier!"
"No matter what, this can't go on!"
"Go call a skilled doctor!"
"There's a surgeon, one of the top few in Seoul, whose clinic is closest to here……… Shall I call him?"
"Call anyone, quickly, let's show her to someone."
After this exchange, I immediately went outside, borrowed a phone in the neighborhood, and called that surgeon—P.

P arrived. By then, the child had sunk deeper into a coma, almost letting go of the thread of life. P looked briefly, then stood up and said urgently:
"Things have become very critical. This child's illness is called erysipelas (丹毒). Once erysipelas invades the head and reaches the meninges (腦膜), there's no turning back. Look, isn't this baby in a coma now? It might have already progressed to meningitis. However, as of now—there's still a sliver of hope. But I cannot say for sure. Right now, there is only one course of action for this child. That is a serum injection (血淸注射), which neutralizes the erysipelas toxin. There is no other medicine besides that. If only I had seen her this morning, there might have been a chance.—-What will you do? Will you try the serum injection?" <35>
His words were like a final verdict.
"Of course, we must try it! Even if there's no effect, we must try everything possible—."
"Yes, I understand. Say no more. Since time is critical, I will go immediately, order the serum injection, and bring it back. Perhaps it might not be in time (마니아와나이 - maniawanai) by then—but let's hope it works!"
He picked up his hat and was already opening the door. I followed him out and asked him to tell me honestly if there was hope or not.
"Well... I cannot make absolute statements about human life. But your child's condition is hopeless in eight or nine cases out of ten……… Please understand."
He answered thus.
"Heavens—."
"But it will take about an hour for me to get the injection and return. In the meantime, go to a traditional pharmacy (漢方藥房) and get a cardiotonic (强心劑) to give her."
He said this and got into the rickshaw. I immediately ran to the traditional pharmacy, explained the situation, and asked for a cardiotonic. However, the pharmacist said that using a cardiotonic—like musk (사향)—for erysipelas would cause it to flare up (화띄지니), so it was absolutely contraindicated, but asked if I would take Uhwang Ch'ŏngsimhwan (牛黃淸心丸), offering advice along with one pill and two fun (approx. 0.75g) of bezoar (牛黃) to mix with milk and apply to the erysipelas-affected skin. I took them, ran straight home, mixed one-third of the Ch'ŏngsimhwan with liquid and administered it, and applied the mixed bezoar. After a while, P returned with the serum. He said giving the Ch'ŏngsimhwan was a good thing. Then he administered the serum injection into the child's leg. He also took out a black plaster, told me to apply it externally, said he expected a detailed report on her condition the next morning, and promised to come himself the next day for another injection before leaving. The child did not drool after the injection and fell into a quiet sleep.

× ×

The child received injections of 20 grams of serum once a day for a week. The erysipelas continued to progress under the skin as before.
"This is because we started treatment too late. The root of the illness is too deep, so it doesn't get better easily."
Dr. P, the surgeon, said this when he came on the seventh day, <36> and that day he gave two injections, one in the morning and one in the afternoon. However, after the afternoon injection, the child lost even more energy and fell into a coma. That day happened to be the last day of the twelfth lunar month (섣달 그믐날).

Unable to sleep well, I woke up in the morning to hear the faint sound of phlegm rattling in the child's throat (쪽구녕). My wife soaked a towel in hot water and placed it on the child's chest. Despite it being New Year's Day (설날), I went out and requested Dr. P to come immediately.

P came, looked, and just smacked his lips, seeming troubled.
"There's nothing more to be done. We can only give her cardiotonics and wait. Since she had two injections yesterday, we can't give another one now. Too many serum injections can cause serum sickness (血淸毒). Perhaps that's what's happening now. But if we stop the injections, the toxicity will disappear."
He spoke hesitantly like this and left.

"If only we had hospitalized her earlier, we wouldn't be seeing this sight ().........!"
"Who didn't hospitalize her because they didn't want to? Both P and the traditional doctor said moving her at this time would expose her to drafts and ruin everything, so we couldn't."
"What should we do? They say Y is famous as a pediatrics specialist (小兒科專門), right? Let's call him, at least so we have no regrets."
"Alright."
My wife and I had this conversation, and I immediately went out and requested Y. Y came right away.
"Hmm... hmm..."
Y fretted first before speaking, then finally gave a camphor (캄플) injection. About ten minutes later, he gave another camphor injection. But the child did not fall into a peaceful sleep, merely remaining in a coma.

Y eventually said this:
"This illness is serum sickness (血淸病). I don't know what the initial illness was, but now it can only be diagnosed as collapse (허탈증) due to serum sickness.—Serum contains serum toxin. To remove that toxin, it must always be used together with a calcium (카르시윰) injection. There are two types of serum poisoning: one manifests externally, and the other goes internally like this. When internal collapse occurs like this, it's difficult. And the reason her belly is swollen like this now is because the spleen (지레) is enlarged due to the fever. But <37> this is actually not the major problem. The urgent task is to counteract (陸하는) the serum toxin, but I didn't bring that medicine, so I'll go prepare it immediately and come back. Keep her resting quietly."
And then he left immediately. Outside the gate, he delivered the true report (實告) that it was hopeless in nine cases out of ten. We didn't know what to do.

About two hours later, Y returned again. However, just as Y opened the door and stepped into the room, the child suddenly stared blankly (눈을 모로 고) and seemed to gasp for breath. And her belly began to swell up more and more. Y rushed in frantically, administered an enema (滯腹注射 - likely rectal injection), and turned the child's body sideways. My wife, sitting by her head, held the forehead of the child whose eyes were wide open (눈을 흡는). Her breathing trembled (숨을 니고), and her face flushed red from lack of oxygen. Y, realizing the enema (灌腸) had no effect (效力), laid her body straight again and inserted an injection needle into her left chest. But the needle popped out! The injection medicine spilled. Another needle was prepared in the blink of an eye and sent fluid into the flesh. At that moment, before the needle was pulled (기) from the flesh, the rattling phlegm (튼 가레) that had been cruelly in the child's throat stopped (허저버리고), her breathing (呼吸) ceased, and her pulse (脉膞) stopped forever.

"Aigo, what do we do! Aigo, what do we do!"
My wife burst into tears upon seeing this sight.
"Fool (바가)! Go to the other room! Quickly!"
I shouted and chased (찻다) the crying wife out. Y tried artificial respiration (人工呼吸) with all his might. But alas! The magic to revive the dead had not yet been invented.

Y, having exhausted all efforts within the reach of his knowledge, closed his eyes and seemed to pray silently for a moment, then said,
"Now, nothing more can be done. The baby is forever…………. But please do not grieve too excessively."
And thus, he simply settled (닥거버리엇다) all accounts. I, who had been holding my breath until then, tense and watching only Y's face, finally let out a long sigh upon hearing these words. The sound of weeping arose from my wife and sister-in-law who had gone to the other room.

And so, the child, on February 8, 1926—the first day of the Lunar New Year, at 5:30 PM—fell asleep forever in the warm hands of her parents.

And yet, truthfully, I still don't know for sure whether your death has made both you and me "unfortunate" (不幸), or if, conversely (도로혀), this is actually not "unfortunate" for you and me. -May 17th- <39>

**Summary**

This poignant memoir by P'alp'ong (Kim Ki-jin) recounts the brief life and tragic death of his infant daughter in 1925-1926 Seoul. He details the initial joy overshadowed by poverty, a disturbing incident where friends jokingly suggest names and randomly select the French word "Malheur" (misfortune) from a book, and the child's eventual illness. The narrative chronicles the desperate, confusing, and ultimately futile medical treatments involving multiple doctors (both Western and traditional), misdiagnoses (indigestion, suspected scarlet fever, erysipelas), conflicting advice, serum sickness from treatment, and the child's death on Lunar New Year's Day. The author reflects on his profound grief, his own subsequent illness, and the lingering question of whether the randomly chosen word "Malheur" was a cruel prophecy or merely a coincidence, questioning the nature of their shared "misfortune."

From a historical perspective, the article vividly portrays the harsh realities of life for intellectuals/proletarians under Japanese colonial rule: extreme poverty making basic necessities and medical care difficult to afford, the tension and overlap between Western (신학문) and traditional Korean medicine (한방의), and the limitations of medical knowledge and practice at the time (misdiagnosis, serum sickness). The author's explicit mention of "proletariat fate" connects personal tragedy to broader socio-economic suffering. The casual inclusion of Japanese terms like 「마니아와나이」 (maniawanai - won't make it in time) also reflects the linguistic environment of the era.

一九二六年一月―日 어린 것은 감긔와체긔를 겁하야가지고 신음신음알키시작하얏다. 어린 것은 해ㅅ수로치면 이 世上에 나온지 二年이되고 날수로 치면 滿十一個月이다되지 못하엿든이다. 나는 健康하얏고, 收入은 昨年과 가티 그다지 窘塞하지는안타할것이나 그러나 말하자면 不正確한편이엇다
어린 것은 벌서부터 방바닥에 안젓다가 아모것도 붓들지안코 일어서서는 댓발자욱식거름을 것기시작＜30＞하얏다. 나와 안해가 나란히 안젓슬이면 긔어다니면서놀다가 방바닥에 웃둑서가지고는 두손을 족음들고서 이 世上에는업는크나큰 재주나부리는듯이『………………………………』라고소리를 질르면서 저의 어머와 나다려보아달나는 듯이 우리들을 바라보다가는 긔운이진듯이주저안고하엿다. 길가로물건장사가 물건을사라고 외우고다니는 소리만들으면『네―』하고 대답하는 버릇이생기엇다. 『압바』『엄마』『아재』『인내』『엇다』이다섯가지의 말의 쓰는 경우를 분간할줄을 알앗다. 밥상이들어오면 먼저 달녀들어서―겨울부터우리는밥짓는 사람을 두고 잇섯다―밥그릇을 손으로 뒤저어가지고 한주먹이나 될 만큼 밤을움켜다가는 입에다처늣는 버릇지 생기엇다. 그리하야 안해는 이것을 처음에는 못하게하다가 아이가 젓을 잘먹지아니하고 밥만보면 긔같이 들닌사람모야으로 달겨드니 나종에는 하는수업시 밥상엽흐로 국말국에 말은 밥한그릇을 로 올녀노코서 그것을 먹이게되얏던 것이다. 안방문을 저혼자열고 건너방으로 건너다니는 버릇도생기엇다.
그리고하루왼종일 들창압흘나기실혀 하는 버릇이 점점잘아서 일긔는 비록치웁드라도 햇볏이나 한 날이면 튼튼하게 둘너씨워가지고 업고서 밧갓흐로 그닐게하기도하얏다. 그리하야서 드듸어 이어린 것은 감긔와 체긔로 신음신음 알키시작한것이엇다.
처음에는 양약을 아는 병원에서 갓다가 먹이고 바람만쏘이지 못하게하고 강밥만 훔처먹지못하게하면서―내심으로는 그다지념려도 하지안코―二三日을지내엇다. 그러나 며칠만에 아이의 왼몸에는 좁쌀가티 긋긋한 것이 하나가득히 도든 것을 發見하얏다.
『여보! 이게 웬일이요……이애가 필연코 감긔나 체긔로이런것이아니요. 의사를 리 다려다봅시다.』안해는 눈을 둥그러케 고서 황겁하게 말하얏다.
『응-글세. 내 곳 불러가지고오지.』
나도 족음겁이나서 그 자리에서 벌덕니러나 한다름에 전차길지 나와서는 곳전차를타고 前부터 親하게 아는 病院을 向하얏다.
『그럼 지금곳 것이니 먼저가라구』
나는 이 말을 뒤으로 들으면서 病院에서 나와집으로 돌아왓다.
-紅疫이면어케하나 우리들은 이러케 서로 걱정하다가 밧겻해서『의사오섯습니다』하는 소리를 듯＜31＞고 어나가서 문을 열고 마지드렷다.
『―어? 무슨 病이야? 대단하지는 안하?』
나는 K의 얼굴을 들여다보며 어린것의 얼굴을 들여다보며 이러케 물엇다.
『응 아직가태서는 確實히 그것이라고는 말할수업는데 대개 그 疑心은 充分히잇서. 그러나 대단하지는 안쿠―樂쓰면곳나겟구먼.』
『猩紅熱―?』
『응―』
『이거큰일낫군.』
『무얼……. 念慮할것업서.』
『졍말?』
내가 이러케 물으니 그는 고개를 덕어리는 것으로 대답하얏다.
그러하야 나와 나의 안해는 얼마 마음을 노코 아이의 病을 보앗다. 그러나 밤에는 나는 困해서 눈한번 보지 못하고잠자기틀게 속하얏다.
며칠만에 좁쌀가티도 탓든것은모다 죽어버리고 푸릇푸릇한 흔적과 함가지로 도닷든자리는 허물어벗는것가탯다. 그러나 그와 同時에 前부터 胎熱로 發하야써 여러차례를 두고부스럼 지가안든 왼편볼에가서 다시 아캐 부스럼이시작되고 왼편턱미트로는 커드란멍을이서케 된 것을 안해는 發見하고나에게 일러주엇다. 그러나나는 그진것이야 怯할것업다고 안해에게 일럿다.
그러나 얼굴의 부스럼과 알에 턱의 멍울은 내가 怯하시안는 사이에 점점더하얏다. 그래서 마츰내 다시 K의사를 다려다가 보이고 약을발르기시작하얏다.
『얼굴에 돗는 것은 걱정할 것이업스되 알게 턱의 멍울은 저것이저대로 삭어업서지지 안흐면 필경수술을 하게 되는고로 애당초에 삭어업서지게하지안흐면 안되는 것이야.』
의사는 어린것의 택에다 새카만 고약을 발러주면서 이러케 말하얏다.
그러나 며칠동안이 지나도록 멍울은 가라안지안코 점점더 하야지며 처음에 알키시작하든보다도 더신열이 생기고는 大便을 푸르게 보기를 게속하는 것이엇다 그래서 도모지 예전사람들도 어린애를 길럿스니 漢方醫를 한번다려다려다가 보이든 것이 조켓다고 생각하고서 나살든동리의 근방에서는 第一有名하다는 의사를 청하야 다보이고서는 처음에 알키시작하든 부터 그동안 지내온 經過이약이를 자세히 한 後에＜32＞ 그런데 신학문으로 배운의사는 처음에 猩紅熱이라고 執脈하고서 藥을 썻습니다. 그런데 先生은 어케 생각하십니?』
하고 물어보앗다. 의사는 한참동안 푸른자죽이난 어린애의 피부를 들여다보고는
『원千萬―.猩紅熱이무어요. 單純히 滯해서 이러케되엇소. 滯하면 흔히애들이란 熱이 大段한데…………熱이만흐면 이러케 도덧다가 가라안는일이만소하여간 내가주는 丸藥을며칠만써보시우. 두 이러케 靑便은 누지안흐리다.』
하고말한뒤에 돌아가버렷다. 우리들은 그말이 그럴듯하야서 그대로하얏다. 그러나 靑便은 그後로는 그리甚하지는안햇스나 알에턱의 멍울이 점점흘음드는 것가티 개지고 그걸로 困하야서 身熱은 놉하지 기만하고 가라안지안한다. 처음부터보아 오든 K는 이모양을보고 大便을 그러케보는것이며 젓을 잘안먹는것이며 모다 原因이 身熱에잇고 그 身熱의 原因은 멍울에 잇스니 그멍울이 곰기前에 삭혀야할텐데 그러케 삭을고약을써도 삭지안흐니 濕布를해보라고 나에게 알으켜주엇다. 그러나 二日동안이나 濕布를하얏는데도 그것은 점점더 할이엇다. 그래서 나중에 보인 漢方醫에게 가서도 그이악이를 하얏더니 멍울은 自己가 지난번에 보앗슬부터 벌서골음이든 모양이엇스니 지금 그것은 식히어 버릴수는 업고 濕布가튼것을하면 골물 것을 리골머터지지도 못하게하고 空然히 오래기만하야서 어린애에게 만害로웁게될터이니 그대로 버리어두엇다가 手術을하게하라고말하는 것이엇다. 手術을하면 不過열흘안에 合倂되고 흉도나지안흘것이라고 말하는 것이엇다. 나는 이말을 K에게 傳하니 K도 漢方醫의 말에 讚成함으로 그대로 내버려두엇섯다.
그러자 果然 어느날밤에 濕布를안하기 始作한지 이틀되는날에―안해는 자는 나를 흔들어 워가지고 어린것의 그멍울이 체절로터저 서골음이 흘르는 것을 가르켜주엇다. 는 마침 새벽녘이엇슴으로 나는 곳옷을 닙고 K를 더리러나아갓다.
K는 內科醫이엇다. 그러나 아직날이 밝기도 前이고 그리고는 人力車갑한푼줄돈도업는 나는 다른 外科로 能熱한 醫士를 불를 힘이 업섯슴으로 K를 請한것이엇다 그러나 K도 그다지 서틀러보이지는 안핫다. 그는 달려들면서 곳 熱湯으로 消毒한 器具를 가지고 腫處의 不潔物을 글거내기 시작하얏다. 긔이개가 튼숫갈이 約深一寸＜33＞五分이나 들어가서 그것이 瘡口를 中心으로 圓을 그리면서 그안의 그 不潔物을 글거내엇다말히자면 이것은 大手術이엇다. 그러나 어린 것은 입을 담을고 철난아이모양으로 참고잇섯다.―
그리하야 手術이 나고『흐름-제』를박어노은뒤에 K는 每日自己가와서 繡帶交換을하여주기로 約束하고돌아갓다. 그날부터어린 것은 족음熱이나리고 몸이들 不便한모양이엇다. 그러니 一週日이지나도록 合瘡은커녕 새살이나오는 兆朕도업섯다. 그리다가는다시 消火器에 탈이낫는지 젓을먹은채로 大便에석거누기를 시작하얏다. 나는 별별짓을 다하야가면서 病이속히낫키를 힘쓰며 祈願하얏다.
그러나 偶然히하루는―그러니 맨처음에 신음신음알키 始作한로부터 約二十日이된 어느날에 왼편귀압흐로 붉은 점을 어 노은듯하게 속으로 단단한 점하나가 생기드니 그것이 그 이튼날에는 이마우에와 올흔편귀ㅅ바귀압헤가서 생기엇다. 每日繡帶交換을하러오는 K는 그것을보고 처음에 멍을 설에발러주든고약을발르라고말하얏다. 그러나 그것은 아모 效驗도업섯다. 새밝안점은 점점커지면서 여긔저긔로 번긔엇다.
어린 것은 밤에도 잠을자지안햇다. 안해는 어린 것을 더리고 밤을 하새웟다. 이리하야 사흘되든 날에 K는 내가 근심스러운 듯이 물으니
『글세그리 大段이 危態로운 것은 아닌대 化濃菌이 들어가서 淋巴線을 차다니며 化濃되는것이니……….그런데 丹毒이라는 것이 이모양과가트니 萬若에 淋巴線의 化濃되는것이아니고 丹毒이랄것가트면 危態하다고도할수잇지. 그러나 지금이것을 丹毒이라고는 볼수업구먼. 그러치만 入院하는것이조흐니 入院을하도록하지.』
『그럼入院을식힐가?』
『그래丹毒이며는 더구나 時』밧비 入院할 必要가 잇스니. 그리구 安全하고―.』
나는 이사람이 돌아간뒤에 그제서야 이사람의 知識을 信用하기가어려운것가태서 入院을식힐 豫定으로 社에들어가서 돈을 三四十圓만 變通하야 달나고 졸럿다. 그러나 돈은 마츰내되지 못하얏다. 그리고 그날은 마츰 土曜日이엇슴으로 午后다섯時만지나면 月曜日하루는 건너서 月曜日밧게는 入院할 수가 업섯다. 그리하야 及其也 돈이 못되야서 入院을 식히지는 못하게 되얏기에 그대로 집으로 나왓다.＜34＞
집에 나와서보니 벌서 어린 것은 半以上이나글럿다. 안해는 무릅우에어린 것을 안고안저서 나를 바라보며
『이걸 어케 하면 조하요?』
하고 울 듯이 말하는 것이엇다. 사실 어린 것은 昏睡狀態이엇다. 그개를 平均二十秒마다한번식 벅벅하며 입으로는 침을흘니고 눈에는 視力이업시 저의어머니의 팔에안키어잇섯다. 그 모양을 보든 나의 눈은 금시에 둥그래젓다.
『여보 이게 언제부터이러우?』
내가당황해하는 눈치를 보고 안해는 별안간 危險을 더 늣기엇는지 내가 늣게 돌아온 것을 책망하얏다.
『엇저면 돈맨들러나가서 집안에 아모도 업는줄 번히 알면서 이러케 늣게잇다온다말이요. 내가 病院에를 나가볼래야 밥짓는 어멈에게 맛기어노코 나갈수는 업고 가슴이타서 죽을번하야소. 전신 당신이나간뒤부터 이모양이라오』
『이거 암만해도 이러다가는 안되겟군!』
『용한의원좀 불러와요!』
『外科로는 서울서 몃재안가는 사람이 여긔서 제일갓가운곳에 한사람이 잇는데…………그사람을 불를가?』
『누구든지 좀 리불러다 보입시다.』
우리들은 이러케 이약이하고서 나는 곳 밧그로나와 동내에서 전화를 빌어가지고 그 外科醫―P를 불럿다.
P는왓다. 그에는 어린 것은 점점더 昏睡狀態에 저서 거진거 生命의 줄을 노을듯하얏다. P는 잠깐동안보고나서는 곳니러서면서 急急한 말씨로 이러케 말하는 것이엇다.
『일이 매우 急하게 되엿슴니다. 이아이 病은 丹毒이라는 것인데요 丹毒이머리만 侵犯해서 腦膜지만 犯하고 볼지경이면 돌닌수업슴니다. 보십시오 이 아기는 지금 昏睡狀態에 잇지안슴니? 벌서 腦膜炎지 갓는지도 몰르겟슴니다. 그러나 아직 가태서는―하는 疑心도업지안흔 것은 안이올시다. 그러치만 斷言은 하지 못합니다. 그런데 이 아이에게는 지금 한가지 밧게쓸 方針이업슴니다. 그럿은 血淸注射인대요 丹毒을 中和식히는것입니다. 그럿밧게는 藥이라고는 업슴니다. 오늘 아침만해서 제가 보앗서도 道理가잇섯슬것입니다.―-어케하시겟슴니? 血淸注射를써보시겟슴니?』＜35＞
그의 말은 最后의 宣告나 나름입섯다.

일이 急하니 이길로 곳가서 血淸注射를 註文하야가지고 오겟슴니다. 或그지「마니아와나이」할는지몰르되―하나 될수잇겟지요!』

『千萬에―-.』
「그런대 내가 注射藥을 가지고 오쟈면 한 時間은 걸닐모양이니 그안에라도 漢方藥房에가서 强心劑를 사다가 먹이십시오」
그는 이러케 말하고 人力車우에 올라타버렷다. 나는 그길로 다름질하야 漢藥房에가서 그이약이를하고 强心劑를 달나하얏다. 그러나 醫師는 丹毒에 强心劑를 쓴다면―사향가튼것인데―사향을쓰면 丹毒이화띄지니 더구나 못쓰는것이나 牛黃淸心丸을 가저가랴하면서 忠告와 함가지로 牛黃淸心丸을한개와 그리고는 젓에개어서 丹毒이도든 皮膚에 발르라고 牛黃두 分重을 내주는 것이엇다. 나는 그것을 바더가지고 곳달음질하야서 집에 돌아와 淸心丸의 三分之一을 개어먹이고 牛黃은 개어발르고 하얏다. 얼마잇다가 P가 血淸을 가지고 돌아왓다. 그는 淸心丸을 먹인 것을 잘햇다고 말하엿다. 그리고 血淸注射를 어린것의 다리에다 노핫다. 그리고 새카만고약을 내노코 그것은 거죽으로 발러주라고 마라고서 來日아침에 動靜을자세히 긔별하기를 바란다하고 勿論來日도 自己가와서 注射를 맛고 나서 비롯오침을 흘니지안코 고요히 잠들엇다.

어린 것은 하루에 한자레식 血淸二十그람식의 注射를 맛기를 一週日동안이나 하얏다. 丹毒은 如前히 皮膚속으로 進行하기를 繼續하얏다
『本來에 늣게서들르기 문에 이럿슴니다. 病根이 너무 묵어워서 이러케 쉬우베 낫지안슴니다.』
P外科醫는 一週日되는날에와서는 이러케 말하고서＜36＞ 그날은 午前午后로두차례의 注射를주엇다. 그러나 午后에 注射를 마진뒤로부터 어린 것은 元氣가더업서지고 昏睡狀態에 져버리엇다. 그날이 마침 陰曆으로 섣달금음날이엇다.
잠도 잘자지 못하고 아침에 일어나서보니 어린것의 쪽구녕속에서는 가래는 소리가 그윽히 들니는 것이엇다. 안해는 더운물에 수건을 적시어가지고 어린애가슴을 덥허주엇다. 그리고나는 설날임에도 고사하고 P醫師를 곳와달나고 가서 請하얏다.
P는와서보고 難處한 듯이 입맛만 다시드니
『道理가업는데요. 强心劑를먹이고 기달려볼수밧게는 업소이다. 注射는 어제두대나노앗스니 지금 놀수는 업슴니다. 血淸注射를 만히노으면 血淸毒이생기니요. 지금도 그런지 몰느겟슴니다 그러치만 注射를 으면으면 中毒은 업서집니다.』
이모양으로 우물물말하고가버리엇다.
『진작 病院에 入院식히엇드면 이런 을 보지안핫지요………!』
『누가 入院식히기 실혀셔 入院식히지안핫나. P도 그리거니와 漢方醫도 이런 움즉이기만하면 바람이나서 일이다 틀닌다고하야서 못식히엇지.』
『어케해요. Y가 小兒科專門으로 有名하다지 아하요? 그 어른이나 일는불러다 보입시다 願이나 업게.』
『그래』

『흐흥………흐흥………』
Y는 말하기前에 먼저 근심하고서 급긔야『캄플』注射를 한 대노앗다. 約十分지난뒤에 한변『캄플』注射를 노앗다. 그러나 어린 것은 고요히 잠도들지못하고 다만 昏睡狀態에 잇섯다.
Y는 結局이러케 말하얏다.
『이 病은 血淸病입니다. 처음에는 무슨 病이엇는지 물르지만 지금은 血淸病의 허탈症으로 診斷할수밧게업슴니다.―血淸에는 血淸毒이잇서요. 그 毒을 除하기 爲하야서 반듯이 「카르시윰」注射와 함씀니다. 그런데 血淸中毒에도 두가지가 잇서 하나는 거죽으로 나타나는 것과하나는 이러케 속으로 들어가는것인데 이러케 속으로 헐탈症이 생겨노코 보면 어렵슴니다. 그리구 지금 이배가 이러케 불른 것은 熱로 因하야서 지레가 큰 닭입니다. 그러나 이＜37＞것은 오히려 대단하지안흔 問題요 리 할 것은 血淸毒을 陸하는 것인데 지금 그 藥을 안가져왓스니 곳가서 準備해가지고오리다. 조용히 잠들도록하고 게심시오』
그리고서 그는 곳 돌아가버렷다. 門밧게서 그는 十中九지는 絶望이라는 實告를 나렷다. 우리들은 어케할바를 몰랏다.
約두 時間後에 Y는 다시왓다. 그러나 Y가 房문을 열고 들어서자마자 어린 것은 별안간에 눈을 모으로 고 숨을 모으는것가탯다. 그리고 그배는 점점점점 불러을느기시작하얏다. Y는 荒唐히 달려들어서 滯腹注射를하고 어린애의 몸을 모로누이엇다. 머리맛해안젓든안해는 눈을 흡는 어린것의 이마를 집고잇섯다. 그숨을 니고 얼굴은 上氣가되야 붉엇다 Y는 灌腸한 效力이업슴을 달은 듯이 다시 몸을 반듯하게 누이고서 왼편가슴우에 注射針을박엇다. 그러나 注射針은탁튀엇 注射藥은 쏘다지고 말앗다. 다시 한개의 注射針이 눈작하는 동안에 準備되어 가지고 살속으로 물을 들여보냇다. 그럴 살에서 注射針을 기前에 그 殘忍하게 어린것의 목구녕에 튼가레는 허저버리고 呼吸은 업서지고 脉膞은 永遠히 쉬어버렷다.
