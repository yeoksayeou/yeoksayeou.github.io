발행일	1926년 06월 01일
기사제목	나와 花咲丸 (Me and the Hanasaki Maru)
필자	崔承一
기사형태	회고·수기 (Reminiscence/Memoir)

나와 花咲丸
崔承一
○
-It was already five or six years ago.
Though my memory is quite hazy (朦朧), I intend to write it down just as it appears like a motion picture projected through mist.
Speaking of crossing the Amnok River (鴨綠江) on the Pongch'ŏn-bound train (奉天行汽車), it's true that I tasted an indescribable romantic feeling back then. Crossing the river at night, looking out at dawn onto what they call Chinese land (中國 ), occasionally seeing the hoe-tips (호미) of people in white clothes, but mostly seeing figures dressed in bluish winter clothes tilling the furrows in the fields. Past Ahyŏn (阿峴), seeing vegetable patches and earthen huts similar to the houses of the Qing people (淸人) nearby, and beneath the drooping weeping willows (垂楊버들), tall donkeys breathing in the morning mist—thinking about it now, it's like a cheap painting from a paper store (紙廛) pasted on the wall.
It was probably around ten in the morning when I arrived at Pongch'ŏn Station (奉天驛). Just like at Namdaemun Station (南大門停車場) a while before, amid the jostling hotel guides, I dazedly had my luggage snatched away. Then, I took a rickshaw (腕馬車) to the Kyŏngsŏng Inn (京城旅館), had what they called breakfast, and then, in the same rickshaw, took a spin around the city. The bustling area was only slightly better than Seoul's Sup'yojŏng Qing Market (水標町淸市). There too, the so-called new downtown area, perhaps thanks to the invasion of capitalist countries, had quite a few buildings of considerable scale.
-After a whirlwind tour of the East Tomb (東陵), ancestral shrines (室寺廟), and the West Pagoda (西塔) like a blind man viewing colorful patterns (丹靑), I went back to the station and asked about the Peiping-bound train (北京行汽車). However, it was right when Chang Tso-lin (張作霖) and Wu P'ei-fu (吳偑孚) were first starting their war, so nobody knew when the trains would resume service. Having no choice, I boarded the train bound for Taeryŏn (大連行), and went to Taeryŏn. There, there wasn't even a shadow of a Korean inn (朝鮮사람의 旅館). I couldn't go to a Qing person's house because I didn't know the language. So, inevitably, I went to a Japanese inn<40> and stayed for two days—waiting to take the steamship to Ch'ŏnjin (天津行汽船). During that time, not only did I have no friends, but I also had no desire to associate with Japanese people. So I wandered alone, rambling through every nook and cranny (坊坊谷谷) of Taeryŏn's streets. Sometimes, I went to places like the Electric Park (電氣公園) and took naps on benches (취우). Even thinking about it now, I can truly feel the taste of wandering (放浪). Another amusing thing was that since I didn't know the language, I couldn't ask the price when buying things. Also, since I didn't know how to count the change, even when buying a pack of cigarettes, I'd pull out a one-yen coin (一圓리) and take whatever change they gave back. This way, I could savor the sentiment (情趣) of wandering in a foreign land (異國放浪). I was satisfied with that anyway.
Two days later, I boarded the steamship bound for Ch'ŏnjin. The name of this ship was Hanasaki Maru (花咲丸), a vessel of about three thousand tons, owned by NYK Line (日郵), sailing from Nagasaki (長崎), calling at Pusan (釜山), Mokp'o (木浦), Kunsan (群山), Inch'ŏn (仁川), with Ch'ŏnjin as its final destination. Because of this route, most passengers disembarked midway, leaving only five or six passengers bound for Ch'ŏnjin. The third-class cabin (三等室) was right below the main deck. After going down below the dark deck and unpacking my luggage, we departed from the Taeryŏn pier (埠頭). When I went up on deck and looked back at Taeryŏn again, the city of Taeryŏn, built by the Great Russian Empire (大路帝國)! Clean, yet somehow melancholic construction—the roads. Now I recall seeing those gloomy, continental-style buildings with tatami mats laid on the wooden floors and doorplates with four or five characters written on them. But the thought that the Great Japanese Empire (大日本帝國) had snatched Taeryŏn and Yŏsun (旅順) to overwhelm the hegemony of the Great Russian Empire in the East—what concern was that of mine (於我에 何所關이리오)?—brought a strange sense of calm. To tell the truth, during my two-day stay in Taeryŏn, I visited Yŏsun for a day. The Memorial Tower (表忠塔) there—the smell of the skeletons (骸骨) of tens of thousands sacrificed for the military's foolishness (ミリタリ의 「가라리」)—how it struck my nose! Although I wasn't the same as I am now, my hair stood on end (毛骨이 웃슥하여젓스며), and I felt acutely the foolishness (馬鹿라시사 - bakarashisa) of the masses squirming beneath so-called reality.
Let's set aside the superficial tour (走馬看山) and get back to the story of the Hanasaki Maru—.
I boarded the ship after breakfast, so we had lunch on board. If there had been many passengers, everyone would have been served separately, but since there were only five or six of us, we gathered in one place—on the wooden floor (마루청)—and ate together like a family (家族的). The "family" consisted of two Japanese men, two Japanese women, and myself—five people. The rice <41> was served by the women. While eating, I glanced casually at the women. One looked close to fifty, and the other seemed about thirty-five or six. Perhaps if she had been a younger lady, my heart might have felt some instinctual stirring, but since they were originally old things, I didn't even spare them a second glance. After finishing the meal, while drinking tea, conversation started. The two Japanese men were both traveling for business purposes. The fifty-ish old woman (老婆) was the wife of a watchmaker in Ch'ŏnjin, returning after visiting her hometown (Nagasaki). The middle-aged woman (三十中女) seemed to be following her, making her first trip to Qing China (淸國行). When they asked me where I was going, I replied, "Peiping (北京) or Nanking (南京), wherever I end up," which made them burst out laughing. When asked about my hometown, I said, "Chosŏn (朝鮮)!", which startled them anew.
"Why don't you come visit my room?" - "Let's go," was the brief exchange, and we went to their room around noon. The room was shaped like a square box (廂子). They lay down on either side, and I sat in the middle. They lay there smoking cigarettes, and the conversation began.
"Chosŏn!" - "That's a place dear to my heart," came a deeply moved line, punctuated by sighs, from the lips of the middle-aged woman. "Why is that?" I asked. "It's where I grew up. I went there when I was fifteen and stayed until I was thirty. There isn't a port in Chosŏn I don't know."
"Is that so?" was my reply. "Chosŏn is a very nice place." "Well..." "Why do you sound so uninterested?" "Hmph, it might be nice for *you people* to live there." "What?" she exclaimed, startled. I didn't want to answer. "You're joking a lot—" "Hmph, I joke because there's nothing else to do." But she didn't seem to grasp the true meaning of my words. Let it go.
She brought out good Luzon cigars (呂宋烟). She offered tasty sweets (菓子). She kept urging me to smoke and eat. I smoked and ate my fill.
Conversation with the middle-aged woman: "What are you going to do in Qing China?" "Nothing in particular. Just wander around aimlessly." - "The life of a wanderer (放浪者), isn't it nice? We are now embarking on a path of wandering." "Then that's completely different from what you said earlier!" - I thought to myself. "Are you a student (學生)?" "No." - "Then <42> a literary man (文士)?" "No." - "Then a patriot (憂國의 志士)?" "No. Then a wanderer?" - "Call me whatever you like." - "You're quite an interesting gentleman (先生)."
"Tell us a story, young wanderer!" "Haha!" Laughter erupted alternately from the mouths of the old woman and the middle-aged woman. "At the border between Chosŏn and China, that Amnok River (朝鮮と支那との境にあの鴨綠江)..." Amidst the noisy chatter sounding like gibberish (랴랴하는 난장판), feeling as if I were sinking into melancholy and stagnation, I rambled on about this and that. For a while, the two women, who had been lying down looking up blankly, focused their gazes together and eyed me strangely. Somehow, I felt a bit uncomfortable, but thinking it probably wasn't a conspiracy (陰謀), I remained seated. "H-san (H상), have you seen Katyusha (카주샤)?" - "I've seen it." - "Katyusha is a truly remarkable woman, isn't she?" - "Well..." - "She's a rebel against humanity (人間의 反逆者)." - "Well..." - "Some call her a fallen woman, but I see her as a thorough rebel against humanity. Look at how she kicks Nekhlyudov (네퓨류돔) away. It's truly refreshing—out there on the Siberian plains—." "That part was good. Why, have you had an experience like Katyusha's?" - "Hmph, me, Katyusha! Even without that, there are many women like Katyusha in Japan."
The sound of the rudder cutting through the waves, the engine noise, the women's laughter, my half-hearted babbling—this ship is now heading towards Ch'ŏnjin. If it were a faster ship, it would take a day and a half, but this one is said to take two days.
"H-san, you are very reserved (얌전하구료)." I thought to myself, "So what if I am?" - "You said you were going to Peiping or Nanking, right? Couldn't you take me with you? If it were with a gentleman like you, I wouldn't hesitate even to die. I'll cook for you and clean the house." - "Thank you, but you're too old, it won't work." - "Haha." All three of us laughed. Hearing such words, even a person as placid as the mid-section of a Buddha statue wouldn't take it lightly. I first looked at her face. Her face was roundish, her complexion fair, her height average (말만하고), and her body plump (포동포동한). There were some freckles (죽은) on her face. Her eyes and mouth were decent, but her nose was a bit flat, which was a flaw if anything.
Outside, the sun seemed warm, but inside the dim room, it felt so stuffy that I rushed up onto the deck. The ship was now crossing the Yellow Sea (黃海). It seemed to be racing at full speed <43> over the not-so-clear sea. Nothing was visible on any side, only the sea waves. Occasionally, white cranes circled the ship. My clothes fluttered in the sea breeze. My hair was blown all together. "A mute journey (벙어리 旅行)"—not knowing the language, destination undecided—a journey to an unknown place. I chuckled to myself. "H-san, what are you thinking about so deeply?" The fifty-ish old woman, having come up unnoticed beside me, tugged at the hem of my Western suit (洋服). "H-san"—"Yes?"—"Onizuka-san (鬼塜상) says she's fallen for you." —"Hey, what's this?"—while thinking inwardly, "Onizuka! What an ominous name (鬼塜 - demon mound)! Maybe it really is a grave for demons." I muttered this to myself. "That woman is truly ill-fated (薄命한 女子). She lost her parents (父母) young, crossed over to Chosŏn, got a husband (男便), but the husband was a terrible man who abandoned her and ran away. She's been a bundle of hardship (苦生덩어리) since childhood. To put it simply, there hasn't been a single warm day in her entire life. She's a very lonely person (寂寞한사람). That's why, being a friend from the same hometown, I'm taking her to my home in Ch'ŏnjin. Please sympathize with her. Please love her. Please, H-san." —"Why aren't you saying anything?"—"Anyway, hearing that there's a woman who's fallen for me, that alone is quite a pleasant thing."
Thus, after that day and time passed, I deliberately avoided them as much as possible. The reason was none other than that, no matter how I looked at it, it seemed suspicious (殊常하기). Moreover, at that time, I had about a hundred yen (百圓) in my possession. If things went wrong—"perhaps"—my plans for Qing China, Japan, Peiping, and Nanking would all be ruined. That was why.
One night passed on the ship.
The next day—around noon, I went up onto the deck to go to the W.C. As I opened the door, I discovered a woman in the adjacent washroom (化粧室)—standing with her upper body bare, reflected in a large mirror on the right wall, smearing powder (분) thick up to her neck. It was Onizuka (鬼塜). Just as I was about to leave after urinating, "H-san, H-san—" she called out in a sharp voice, turning her head abruptly. "What is it?" I asked, turning back. "This—water faucet (물고등) won't turn, could you turn it for me? Hot water comes out, but cold water doesn't." —"This is just unnecessary teasing (히야시 - hiyakashi)."—Although I felt some displeasure inwardly, I had no choice but to go and turn it for her. "Stay here and come out with me, let's go to my room<44>," she said. —"Putting white powder (粉) all over that old dream ()." —"This one really looks like a ghost (귀신)." —You are quite pitiful too. Me, running away in anger, and you, abandoned and chased around—we share the same fate (八字). —"But why can't you just stay quiet? Why do you want to swindle my money?" Feeling increasingly uncomfortable like this, I said, "Come later. I have something to do in my room." Then she replied, "Oh my, what work could you possibly have inside this ship? A diary (日記)?" —"That's right," I said and left.
I returned to my room and lay down quietly, lost in some kind of daydream (空想), when the two of them, the fifty-ish old woman and Onizuka, came looking for me as if by agreement. They pulled me up from the bed (寢臺) and took me to their room. Again, they lay down side by side, and Onizuka suddenly cried out, "Ooh, my stomach (배)." Startled, the old woman said, "H-san, Onizuka-san's stomach hurts, could you please rub it for her? If H-san's hand touches it, she'll surely get better." "Ah, I'm really caught now," I muttered to myself. "There's no way out of this." As I hesitated, wondering what to do, she said, "I—" "I'm sleepy, I should take a nap," turning over. At that moment, Onizuka pleaded, "H-san, please rub my stomach. Come over here. Please!" —"Oh, my stomach!" —"Quite the scheme ()." As I thought this and stood up to leave, she grabbed my clothes hem, saying, "Oh my, your face is flushed (上氣). Come here and listen to what I have to say." She pulled so hard that I inadvertently stumbled beside her—
"Look here. Until now, at the age of thirty, I have never felt the emotion of true love (戀愛). But since yesterday, my feelings (感情) have become very strange. Also, although I lived in Chosŏn for fifteen years, and received passionate love from Chinese men (支那人), I have never received or given love to a Korean person (朝鮮사람)." —"So what?" I finally said. "I love you." —What kind of emergency love is this? If you want money, just say you want money. How pitiful you are! You too are human, yet you wander around unable to act like one! Is this your fate? My fate? Whose fault (罪) is it?— Thinking this inwardly, I felt a strange sensation (觸感). My hand was already inside her blouse, on her chest— "When we disembark, go to the place heading towards Ch'ŏnjin. Stay there for just a day. Let me show you around Ch'ŏnjin <45> with her, follow you to the station (停車場), either board the railway (鐵道) or just follow you altogether, okay? Yes?" —"Let's do that," I replied. But I thought, "Hmph, you're gradually using all the tricks (手段) you have. But perhaps—that might be the appropriate path for you to take. That's how you survive! Your existence (生命의 存在) will continue in this world until the day you die. Go ahead—do as you please in Qing China. Money earned through dirty deeds by dirty men—what harm is there in taking it back through dirty deeds? You are right to do so. Go on, do it. But leave me out of it." —Returning to my room, I lay down quietly on my bed and muttered like this.
The ship arrived in Ch'ŏnjin the morning of the next day. It started getting hot from outside the port (港口), and the people on the pier (埠頭) were already wearing summer clothes (녀름옷). Tens of thousands of laborers (苦力) swarmed like maggots (구덱기)—like beasts—and beyond the dusty pier, magnificent buildings (宏壯한 建物) stood in a long line. "Hmph, it's a death trap (독안)! Man-eating." Thinking this, I picked up my handbag, left the ship, and descended to land (陸地). "Hey, come to my place too! Hey!" —She grabbed the hem of my Western suit, and a strange—how should I put it? Begging-like (哀乞하는듯한)—a kind of abnormal expression (一種變態的表情) appeared on her face. "Alright," I said, but for some reason, my shoulders shuddered (억개가 웃슥하엿다). And goosebumps ran down my spine (소름이 첫다).
Since my luggage (行李) was simple, right after passing inspection by the British customs officials (英國稅關官吏), I grabbed one of the rickshaws thrusting towards me and was about to leave. She was currently having her luggage inspected, so she couldn't follow (차 올수도업고) and looked utterly dismayed. Turning my head sharply from the rickshaw, I saw her crestfallen face, those inscrutable, enigmatic eyes staring blankly at me—that gaze! Like a starving bitch seeing a piece of raw meat, wanting to eat it but unable to, watching intently only to lose it—a venomous gaze (毒한 視線)! It must be the same as a starving person seeing food but being unable to eat.
This was neither sad nor happy, nor astonishing—just, setting aside my own worries about not getting meals, it was the most unpleasant (렴칙한) and amusing incident in my nearly fifty years of various experiences. For some time afterwards, whenever I remembered that gaze—saw it before my eyes—I invariably shuddered (몸서리를 첫섯다). Riding the rickshaw, I went to the station and boarded the Nanking-bound train (南京行汽車). --The End--<46>

**Summary**

The author recounts a journey from Korea to China via Manchuria around 1920-1921. He travels by train to Pongch'ŏn (Fengtian) and then Taeryŏn (Dalian), finding train travel further into China blocked by warlord conflict. From Taeryŏn, he takes a Japanese ship, the Hanasaki Maru, towards Ch'ŏnjin (Tianjin). On the sparsely populated ship, he encounters two Japanese women, one middle-aged ("Onizuka") who claims a past connection to Korea and becomes overly familiar, eventually propositioning him and suggesting he take her along. Disturbed and suspecting she wants his money, the author coldly rebuffs her advances upon arrival in Ch'ŏnjin, leaving her behind as he departs for Nanking.

From a historical perspective, the article offers glimpses into the atmosphere of Northeast Asia in the early 1920s: the Japanese presence in Manchuria (Taeryŏn, Yŏsun), the disruption caused by Chinese warlords (Chang Tso-lin vs. Wu P'ei-fu), the casual movement of Japanese civilians across the region, and the narrator's own sense of alienation as a Korean traveler navigating Japanese-controlled spaces and interacting with Japanese individuals. The encounter with the Japanese woman, "Onizuka," particularly her complex mix of familiarity, desperation, and manipulation, alongside the narrator's cynical distrust, provides a micro-level interaction reflecting broader colonial power dynamics and prejudices, making it potentially interesting for historians studying social interactions and identities during this period.

나는 내방으로와서 감안히 드러누어서 무슨 空想인지 하고 잇슬에 둘이 約束이나 한 듯이 五十老婆와 「鬼塜」이 차자왓다 寢臺에잇는 놈을 붓잠아니리키여가지고 자긔들 방으로 가서는 한 역시 나란히누으면서 『이이고 배야.』하고 「鬼塜」이잇다가 아닌소리를 지른다 거맛긔처서 五十老婆는『H상 鬼塜상이 배가 압흐다니 좀문질러주세요 H상의 손이다으면 나을터이니.』『얘 이건 정말걸렷구나.』이러케 중얼거렷다 『이건 가자는 수도업고 엇재하면서 머믓머믓하고 안젓스려니 나-』 『졸니여 낫잠이나 자야하겟다.』하면서 도라드러눕는다 그를 맛처서 『H상 내배좀문질러주세요 이리드러오세요 예!.』-『아 이배야!.』-『가 무던한걸.』나는 이러케 생각하면서 가것다고 니러섯스나 『아이고 얼골엔 上氣가 되엿네 이리오서서 내말슴을 좀 드르세요 하면서 옷자락을 잡아다리며 엇지나 몹시 잡아다리엿든지 無心中에 나는 그의 엽헤서 씨그려젓다-
이거보세요 나는 여태것나히 三十이되도록 참 戀愛이라는 感情을 늣긴적이 업섯헤요 그러나 나는 어제부터 퍽 感情이 이상스러워젓세요 나는 한 朝鮮에 十五年이나잇섯지마는 支那人에게는 렬열한 사랑을 바다보앗지마는 朝鮮사람의 서랑을 밧거나 하거나는 업섯세요.』-『그러니 엇재?.』하고 비로소 나는 말하엿다 『나는 당신을 사랑하여요』-사랑이 무슨 기급을 할 사랑이야 돈이 먹고 십거든 거저 먹고십다고 그러지 참 불상하구나 너도 사람이것마는! 사람의 노릇을 못하고도라다니는고나 이것이다-네팔자냐 내팔자냐 누구의 罪냐?.』-이러케 속으로 생각하면서 이상한 觸感이잇스니 나의 손은 발서 그의 가슴속에 가드러가잇다-『배에서 나리거든 天津나가는 곳으로 가세요 에 가서 이를만묵어가세요 에 저이하고 天津구경식혀도＜45＞리고 停車場지 라가서 鐵道를타든지 아조 라가바리던지할게요 녜.』-『그럽시다.』이러케 나는 대답해두엇다 그러나 『흥 점점 네가 가진 手段이라는 다-쓰는고나 그러나 그것이-너로써는 맛당히 取할길일일는지도모르는 것이다 그래야 너는 먹고살지! 네의 生命의 存在가 이 세상에 죽는날지 繼續될터이니 자-淸國가서 마음대로하여라 더러운짓을하여서 벌어온더러운 놈들의 돈 더러온짓을하여서 도로 아서 오기로 무슨 흉되느냐 너는 맛당하다 해라 해. 그러나 나한아는 여노아라.』-나는 내 방으로 도라와서 감안히 자리에 누엇 이러케 중얼대이엿다.
배가 天津에 그이튼날아참에 닷는데 港口밧게서부터 더워지더니 埠頭의 人間들은 발서 녀름옷을 입고잇다 멧萬으 苦力이 구덱기-즘승과 가티 움울거리고 믄지가 야케 니러나는 埠頭저에는 宏壯한 建物이  느러서잇다 『흥 독안이로고나 둑안이! 사람잡아먹는.』이러케 나는 손가방을 들고 배를 나 陸地로 나려갈『에 나한테도 가세요 에.』-나의 洋服자락을 잡아다리면서 이상한-무엇이라고할 哀乞하는듯한-一種變態的表情이 그의 얼골에 나타나잇다『그럽시다.』하면서 웬일인지나는억개가 웃슥하엿다 그러고 소름이 첫다.
나는 行李가 簡單한지라바로 英國稅關官吏에서 檢査를맛친후 드리덤뵈는 人力車에 아모것이나한아를 잡아타고가려할 그는 方今行李의 檢査를 맛고잇는지라 차 올수도업고 기가 막히는 모양이엿다 人力車에서휙 고개를 도리케 돌려다보니 맥이 풀린얼골에 그아지 못할수수기의 두눈은나를 멀건히 바라다보고잇다-그 視線! 주린암캐가 밝안고기덩어리를 보고 먹으려하다가 먹지를 못하고 노레보다가 놋치여보내이면서 바라다보는듯한 毒한 視線! 주린 사람이 밥을 보면서도 먹지못하는 것과 맛찬가지일 것이다.
이것은 슯은 일도아니고 깃븐일도안이고 기맥힌 일도아닌-다만 지금 나의 밥못어더먹는걱정을 여놋코는 나의 여러것걱근 半五十歲의 一生涯中에는 가장 렴칙한우수운일이엿다 나는 그 後에 얼마를 두고도 그-視線이 생각되여 눈압해 보일 마다 반다시 몸서리를 첫섯다 人力車를 탄나는 停車場으로 가서 南京行汽車를 탓다  --＜46＞
