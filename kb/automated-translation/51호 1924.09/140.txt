**Body Text Translation:**
What one hears is different: The Gap Between Rich and Poor in the Western Province (西道), the Rampancy of Capitalist Cliques (資本閥), the Shamelessness of the Landlord Class (地主級)!

Some people might abruptly say that in the P'yŏngan Province region, the degree of wealth and poverty is generally balanced, differing from the Southern Korea (南鮮) region. Therefore, they might claim there is no room for landlord-tenant problems to arise, and consequently, no reason for social movements based on class consciousness to emerge. Regarding this, there is no need for us to engage in purely abstract talk; let's just look at the facts.

First, let's examine the degree of wealth and poverty through the possession of land, which is the main source of wealth in this province.

Table 2: Landlords and Tenant Farmers' Tenancy

That is, looking at the first table above, as of Taishō 11 (1922), out of the total land area of 394,584 *chŏngbo* (町步), the tenant-farmed area was 190,199 *chŏngbo*. In other words, the land possessed by the landlord side occupied nearly two-thirds of the total land. Among this, concerning paddy fields (畓), which yield high harvests, the landlords' ownership reached over three-fourths of all paddy fields. Furthermore, looking at the increase and decrease of owner-farmed and tenant-farmed land between Taishō 2 (1913) and Taishō 11 (1922) by area, although both owner-farmed and tenant-farmed areas increased, the fact that tenant-farmed paddy and dry fields did not decrease while owner-farmed paddy and dry fields increased ultimately means that the increase in owner-farmed land was a natural increase due to the development of new arable land (耕地), and certainly not a promotion of tenant land to owner-farmed status. Moreover, the fact that tenant-farmed paddy and dry fields increased by over 24,000 *chŏngbo* compared to Taishō 2 (1913) explains that landlord land annexation (the rich getting richer) increased by that much during that period.

Looking again at the second data set, as of Taishō 11 (1922), among the total 164,333 farming households in South P'yŏngan Province (平南道) (the total number of households in the province is over 212,500), there were 11,002 landlord households, 37,419 owner-farmer households, 65,089 owner-farmer-tenant households, and 50,824 tenant households. Comparing this again to the situation 10 years earlier in Taishō 2 (1913), owner-farmer households decreased by about a thousand, and owner-farmer-tenant households decreased by about 9,600. The only increases were in tenant households by over 76,000 [Translator note: This number seems extremely high compared to the others and the 1922 total, potentially a typo in the original text, but reported as written] and landlord households by about 5,300. While many twists and turns are surely involved in this, making it difficult to make a blanket judgment, looking at the general trend, it is a fact that landlords are increasing while owner-farmer and owner-farmer-tenant households are slightly decreasing. Furthermore, it can be clearly perceived (看取) that the currently existing 65,000-plus owner-farmer-tenant households are unable to advance to owner-farmer status and will inevitably fall into the tenant farmer category year after year (連해 連해). Also, although until now there has been an appearance (觀) of a middle class forming somewhat as some owner-farmers rose to landlord status, as transportation in this province develops further and the activities of large asset holders become more agile (敏活), according to the principle of "big fish eats middle fish" (大魚中魚食의 理法), even today's small middle class faces the prospect of falling into ruin. Ultimately, there is a likelihood (嫌疑) that the farmland of this province will also end up under the management of a few large landlords. Especially in areas with open fields (田野) and fertile soil (地味) like P'yŏngwŏn, Anju, Kangsŏ, Yonggang, Taedong, Sunch'ŏn, and Chunghwa, the law of the rich getting richer and the poor getting poorer will operate even more closely.

The above concerns land, but looking at commerce, industry, and mining, as is generally known, P'yŏngyang has water, coal (炭), the raw material for fire, abundant other industrial raw materials, and convenient transportation. Propaganda is rife that it is the most suitable place to become Korea's (朝鮮) industrial center (like Ōsaka in Japan (日本)), and consequently, the hands of foreign (外人) capitalists have entered here more than anywhere else in Korea. Especially, the mining rights (채굴권) for the limitless<116> anthracite coal (무연탄) produced throughout the South P'yŏngan area have all gone to the Japanese side. In terms of enterprise, South P'yŏngan has already become occupied by large capitalists. Looking at this comprehensively:

Besides this, in this province, there are over 937,000 *chŏngbo* of forest land (林野) (as of the end of Taishō 11). Excluding the 242,000-plus *chŏngbo* of private forests (사유림) and the 309,000 *chŏngbo* of forests requiring preservation (要存林), the remaining 385,000-plus *chŏngbo* of forests not requiring preservation (不要存林) will, in large part (其 大部), fall into the hands of the propertied class (유산급) in some form or another. Besides that, there must be a considerable amount of state-owned pending land (國有未懸地), which will naturally also become the property of those with means. Furthermore, things like the production of saplings (苗木) and silkworm eggs (蠶種), and the joint purchase of silk cocoons (蠶繭), are all manipulated under the plans of large capitalists. How is it that today's world situation is so much a world for the haves, and exists only for the benefit of the haves? One feels this even more keenly while traveling through the regions. <122>

**Summary**

This article from 1924 refutes the notion that P'yŏngan Province (West Province) in colonial Korea enjoyed relative economic equality compared to the south. Citing statistics from 1913 (Taishō 2) to 1922 (Taishō 11), it demonstrates a growing disparity: landlord land ownership increased significantly (especially valuable paddy fields), while owner-farmers decreased and owner-tenant farmers were increasingly likely to become purely tenant farmers. The piece highlights the influx of capital, particularly Japanese, into industries like mining (anthracite coal rights monopolized by Japanese) and anticipates the further concentration of wealth and land in the hands of a few large landlords and capitalists, squeezing out the middle class. It concludes by lamenting that the system seems designed solely for the benefit of the wealthy. For historians, the article provides valuable contemporary data and perspective on land tenure changes, the economic impact of Japanese colonialism in a specific region often perceived differently, the rise of Japanese capital dominance in key industries like mining, and the social stratification occurring despite narratives of regional egalitarianism. The specific statistics on land ownership shifts and the mention of Japanese control over resources like coal are particularly revealing.
