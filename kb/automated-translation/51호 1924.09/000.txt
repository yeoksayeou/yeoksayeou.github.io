Magazine Title: 개벽 제51호
Publication Date: 1924년 09월 01일
Article Title: 이번은 咸南踏査 (This Time, a Hamnam Field Survey)
Author: 開闢社 (Kaebyŏksa/Gaebyeok Publishing Company)
Article Type: 사고·편집후기 (Notice/Editorial Postscript)

This time, we announce a Hamnam Field Survey (咸南踏査), but there is more than one thing we are apprehensive (恐懼) about. At this time, when all of Chosŏn (朝鮮) is in turmoil, struggling for survival amidst unexpected natural disasters (天災) like floods (水災) and droughts (旱災), in what state of mind could we dare to seek you out? Considering this, we would have liked to suspend this for some time, but because this task is also important (重要) and there is more work ahead that presses upon us, although it feels like a perilous (胃險) act, we have unavoidably decided to proceed one step further from the top of a hundred-foot pole (百尺竿頭 進一步) and undertake the Hamnam Field Survey. We hope that the brothers and sisters (兄弟姊妹) of Hamnam will not just add untimely blame (非時의 責) but, whether in hardship or joy, as this is our work that cannot be neglected, please do not spare your support according to your circumstances (形便대로) and as much as your strength allows (힘밋는대로). Messrs. Pak Tal-sŏng (朴達成) and Yi Ŭl (李乙) of our company (本社) have now departed for your region (貴地). They will conduct the survey over two months, September and October, and the results will be published (發表) in the November issue.
開闢社 (Kaebyŏksa) <1>

**Summary**

This editorial postscript announces Gaebyeok magazine's plan to conduct a field survey in Hamgyŏngnam-do (咸南, Hamnam) despite the widespread suffering in Korea (朝鮮, Chosŏn) due to recent floods and droughts. Acknowledging the difficult circumstances, the editors stress the importance of the survey and appeal to the people of Hamnam for support. Two researchers, Pak Tal-sŏng (朴達成) and Yi Ŭl (李乙), have already departed for the two-month survey (September-October), with findings scheduled for publication in the November issue. For historians, this piece highlights the resilience and dedication of Korean intellectuals and journalists under colonial rule, attempting to document regional conditions and continue their work despite severe natural disasters and likely socio-political constraints. It also reflects the magazine's commitment to engaging directly with different regions of Korea and underscores the difficult environment in which such cultural and investigative activities took place.
