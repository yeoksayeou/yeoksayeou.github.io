Article Title: 湖西의 一日, 湖西銀行의 新築落成宴을 機로 하야 (A Day in Hoso, On the Occasion of the Hoso Bank's New Building Completion Banquet)
Author: 靑友生 (Ch'ŏng'usaeng)
Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

A Day in Hoso, On the Occasion of the Hoso Bank's New Building Completion Banquet
						Ch'ŏng'usaeng

Much Gratitude to the Kyŏngnam Railway
The 12th of last month was the day the completion ceremony for the new Hoso Bank building was held. Due to an unexpected company order, I was able to accept the Bank's invitation and found myself indebted to the Kyŏngbu Line express train departing at 7:05 AM that day. It was morning, the light rain from the previous night having just cleared. The invitees, presuming that the hosts for this day must surely have unfolded their worried brows, were indeed filled with the joy of jumping sparrows. Consequently, it was truly no small comfort to find myself heading in the same direction with gentlemen, more than a third of the passengers on the same train that day, with whom I had long enjoyed a close acquaintance. The train's movement, which makes punctuality its lifeblood, took less than three hours before Ch'ŏnan Station served as the transfer point, and soon the passengers bound for the Yesan area transferred to the Kyŏngnam Line departing at 10:15 AM. Although I had heard long ago that this Kyŏngnam Railway had been laid, I had always regretted not having the opportunity for a closer look, so wasn't this fortunate encounter truly the right time, like a "wind sent by the times"? Moreover, whether voluntary or passive – there was no need to investigate that far – the fact that they somehow managed to operate a special train newly arrived after the railway's opening, providing every convenience, could be said to be the commercial morality of the Kyŏngnam Railway Company. At the same time, Mr. Sŏng Wŏn'gyŏng, the Ch'ŏnan Branch Manager of Hoso Bank, and others (though one might say it's natural as it's their own bank's affair) moved through the various class compartments, exchanging glances, and during the not-so-short journey of 25 *ri* from Ch'ŏnan Station to Yesan Station, treated the arriving guests with the utmost courtesy and attentiveness, which was exceedingly gracious and made one feel rather indebted. Ah, human society is something one can only be optimistic about today! Where does a true man's refined taste not go?

The Hoso Bank Banquet, Replete with Southeastern Beauty
We arrived in Yesan that day at 11:50 AM. As our party disembarked from the train, the Hoso Bank employees, already thoroughly prepared for the reception, stood deployed in groups of threes and fives on both sides of the station front, amiably welcoming us, clad in frock coats (후로코-트) that billowed like cloud waves and flashing flower badges (花章) that seemed to boast of glory. Immediately, forming a long, snake-like procession with dozens of automobiles arranged in order, we dashed spiritedly towards the jade-jar-shaped Yesan town center. Arriving safely at our destination without incident, we briefly recovered from travel fatigue (路憊) on the second floor of the Bank. About an hour later,<84> as one o'clock PM announced the appointed time for the opening, the crowd filling the upper floor descended *en masse* to the ceremonial hall. After everyone settled into their respective seats, following the opening greetings and ceremonial address in order, heartfelt congratulatory speeches were delivered by the Governor of South Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, various banks, companies, and other dignitaries. Once these concluded, amidst the clinking of goblets and tallies (觥籌交錯), toasts were raised, and drinks began to be exchanged. Furthermore, the clear songs and wonderful dances (淸歌妙舞) of the red skirt troupe (紅裙隊) completely transformed the atmosphere both inside and outside the hall, causing everyone, without exception, to break through the boundaries of human suffering. Opening their hearts (胸襟을 披瀝하야) with pure innocence and without a single flaw, amidst a thoroughly harmonious atmosphere (和氣靄靄), they reveled in tipsy delight until around 6:00 PM. What still remains in memory is that among the honored guests who ascended the celebratory platform that day, a certain bank president (某行頭取) read his congratulatory address in a graceful and solemn (雍容鄭重) tone, notably using pure Korean. Whether this gentleman lacked proficiency (素養) in Japanese is unknown, but in any case, while one couldn't help but feel a slight hidden sentiment, as if boasting of the unique characteristic that "my bank is independently managed solely by our own countrymen," another figure (某某氏), seemingly ignoring the majority of Korean personages present and without any hesitation, looked up two or three times as if monopolizing something solely with fluent Japanese, gave an impression, no matter how one looked at it, of still being drawn by a second nature (第2天性). However, perhaps this too was due to some unavoidable reason; rethinking it that way might be all there is to it. Ah, the grand occasion of today! This place where true peace is tasted!!
The newly built Hoso Bank

The Promising Kyŏngnam Line Area
My journey is simple. It's true that I, like others, had thoughts of raising my voice at the superb scenery (絶佳한 風光) in various places,<85> and it's also a fact's fact that I intended to observe the local conditions and customs (物態風俗). However, this trip was quite different from such journeys, being one where the future felt short. Consequently, everywhere I passed, only the shadows of mountains and water reflected in the train window, flickering along with the color of tree leaves fluttering in the frost-laden wind, brushed past my eyes in a cursory glance (走馬看山的). Even so, although it wasn't a planned fact, I fortunately gained considerable knowledge through the pointers and verbal explanations of guides. The vicinity centered around the Onyang Hot Springs (溫陽溫泉) region is said to be almost entirely a hot spring zone, with numerous sources of newly gushing hot springs emerging here and there. These springs are reportedly very promising, capable of yielding over 4 *tu* (斗 - a unit of volume) of hot water even before digging about 3 *ch'ŏk* (尺 - a unit of length) deep. Currently, the Kyŏngnam Railway Company (京南鐵道會社), having discovered this, is investing significant funds to diligently investigate and test drill. Upon achieving good results, Managing Director Tachikawa (立川專務) declared, they will resolutely transfer the rights to private entrepreneurs to make a great contribution to regional development. Speaking of the topography of this region, it is generally a gentle slope (緩傾斜地); while there are minor differences here and there, regarding the soil quality in particular, aside from some sandy soil, it is entirely loam, making it a heaven-sent treasury (天藏의 寶庫) suitable not only for rice cultivation but also for cotton farming, sericulture, and fruit cultivation. Also, seeing directly with my own eyes that the land along both sides of the route is all cultivated using rainwater, and that there are patches of desolate land covered in lush weeds everywhere, I asked if perhaps there were absolutely no people striving for irrigation (灌漑) and land reclamation projects in this region. It is said that there are still no Water Utilization Association facilities here, and consequently, the influence of the Tongch'ŏk Company (東拓會礿) also seems not to have reached powerfully yet. However, the foresight (先見의 明) regarding industrial development on one hand has already succeeded in opening the Kyŏngnam Line without pause. From now on, it is an unavoidable fact that the focal point of capitalists' struggle for rights will concentrate on this region. Ah, one must live. One must eat. Who is the one infringing upon the right to live? Who is the one whose rights are being infringed upon??

Hoso Bank, A Bank Befitting the Name
Come to think of it, work, no matter what it is, must progress from one to two to three; knowledge must arise from it, and experience must be gained from it to be useful. This bank was originally established through the efforts of the current Managing Director Sŏng Nak'hŏn (成樂憲) and other local dignitaries from the entire Hoso region as a joint-stock company. It was first established on May 1st, Taishō 2 (1913), with a small capital of only 300,000 won. As its performance gradually improved, it increased its capital to 1,000,000 won on June 16th, Taishō 8 (1919). Foreseeing the necessity of further expanding the scope of economic development,<86> the Bank again merged with the Kyŏngnam Kwang'ŏp Joint-Stock Company (京南廣業株式會社), which had an equivalent capital of 1,000,000 won. At the same time, by persuading many leading capitalists from Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul) to join, they pushed for a major innovation. As a result, looking at the current business overview, the paid-in capital is 725,000 won, the reserve fund is 83,000 won, deposits amount to 800,000 won, and loans amount to 1,540,000 won. It is a fact that an annual dividend rate of 9% is easily achievable. Speaking of the number of employees, including the head office and branches, there are nearly 50 people. Branches have already been established in four locations: Kyŏnggi Province's Ansŏng (安城), and South Ch'ungch'ŏng Province's Ch'ŏnan (天安), Hongsŏng (洪城), and Kwangch'ŏn (廣川). It is said they have established plans to gradually expand the distribution area year by year. Indeed. This bank is truly a respectable bank. It is no exaggeration to say that throughout all of Chosŏn, among regional banks, this is undeniably a great bank to which others cannot but bow their heads (俛首). Ah, a bank that is truly a bank! A gift of love from the compatriots of the Kyŏngnam region!!

Hoso Bank Creates New Momentum for Economic Activity
In my opinion, I couldn't help but wonder if the bank's location, far from Sillyewŏn (新禮院) and tucked away in the corner of Yesan town, might pose a drawback to its development. However, seeing new buildings for government offices, schools, companies, and other public institutions rising prominently here and there near the bank, it seems that, let alone the future, even under the current conditions, this location was perhaps unavoidable. Traditionally, the Yesan region is said to have received the most supplies of goods by water and land from Inch'ŏn Port (仁川港). Now that the Kyŏngnam Railway connects with the Manchurian Railway Line (滿鐵線) at Ch'ŏnan and has opened as far as Sillyewŏn, commerce with Inch'ŏn Port has naturally become smoother. Moreover, once the already planned Kyŏngnam Line is extended further south to Kunsan (羣山), this place is expected to become the central collection point for the Kyŏngnam Line area. However (saying this might seem a bit extreme), the land in the Yangho (兩湖 - Chŏlla and Ch'ungch'ŏng provinces) region, singled out (一指를 屈하는) for having many vagrants (浮浪輩) absorbed in temporary speculative businesses like stock trading or spot transactions, tempted by vanity, is recklessly pawned and sold (濫典濫賣) month after month, year after year. If not for the Shokusan Ginko (殖銀) or the Tongch'ŏk (東拓), what little remains falls into the nets of loan sharks (高利鬼). Although there are some good elements among them, they are like drowning with eyes wide open, eating mustard while crying. Ah, be not surprised! The rich storehouses of various parts of South Chosŏn (南鮮), which produce the main crop of rice from thousands of *ri* of fertile fields, capable of raising the nation's food problem, have almost entirely fallen into others' hands. In essence, is it that Heaven's fortune circulates, and there is nothing that does not recover? Hoso Bank, whose nerves are keen in observing this, is currently striving for land improvement, increased rice production, and the recovery of other rights, while simultaneously having many local people as shareholders. This truly compels me, as a Korean, to express my heartfelt congratulations.<87> Ah, the Yangho region where power is concentrated! Hoso Bank, with ample prospects for victory in the economic war.

Hoso Bank is a Special Bank for Koreans
People must stand on their own. As long as one has strength, one should always strive for self-reliance. Politics self-reliant, economy self-reliant – whatever can be done, should be done as much as possible; this is a natural public principle (天演公例). This Hoso Bank seems to be a spiritual crystallization of the intent to make the economic policy of Koreans completely self-reliant in its organizational structure. It has maintained a pure integrity (貞操), like a noble scholar who wouldn't even warm himself by a side fire even if freezing to death ten times, without the weakness of receiving government subsidies or interference from foreigners. Furthermore, considering that everyone from shareholders to bank employees consists entirely of pure Koreans, these one or two points alone suggest that there might be something uniquely significant for the pioneering of the Korean people's destiny. In this sense, the Bank's consistent promotion of its fortune will not only be a great record for this bank itself but is also expected to make a huge contribution to the history of Korean economic development for the eternal future. Ah, gentlemen of the Bank! Remain ever sound and be capable (堪能하라) of the responsibility of being a special bank for Koreans.<88>

**Summary**

This 1922 travelogue by "Ch'ŏng'usaeng" recounts a journey to Yesan for the opening ceremony of the newly built Hoso Bank. The author expresses gratitude for the special train arranged by the Kyŏngnam Railway and describes the lavish banquet attended by dignitaries, noting with approval a speech given in Korean but mild disapproval of another given fluently in Japanese. The author reflects on the economic potential of the Kyŏngnam Line region, particularly its suitability for agriculture and the discovery of hot springs, while lamenting the lack of irrigation and the encroachment of Japanese capital (mentioning the Tongch'ŏk and Shokusan Ginko). The article strongly praises the Hoso Bank as a major, purely Korean-funded and staffed institution, seeing it as a symbol of Korean economic self-reliance and a crucial entity for reclaiming economic power and developing the region, particularly the agricultural wealth of the "Yangho" provinces, from foreign control.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its explicit articulation of Korean economic nationalism in 1922. It highlights the pride in a Korean-run enterprise (Hoso Bank) succeeding without Japanese capital or government interference, framing it as vital for resisting colonial economic exploitation. The mention of specific entities like the Kyŏngnam Railway, the Eastern Development Company (Tongch'ŏk), and the Industrial Bank of Japan (Shokusan Ginko), alongside observations about land ownership and development struggles, provides a contemporary snapshot of the colonial economic landscape and Korean responses to it. The contrasting attitudes towards the use of Korean versus Japanese language at the ceremony also reflect the complex linguistic and cultural dynamics of the period.

謝感이 만흔 京南鐵道
