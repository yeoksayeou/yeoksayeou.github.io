**Nine Short Pieces**
						Kim Sŏksong

**Old Man Raking Fallen Leaves**

The late autumn sunset
Casts feeble rays
Upon the frost-touched head of the old man.
With a rake held in frozen hands,
Raking the fallen leaves,
Oh, bent-backed grandfather!
Even for that clinging life of yours,
The day of falling leaves is near.

**Autumn of Pain**

The autumn morning sun shines warmly
Before my window, the chrysanthemums smile.
As the waterlessly drying tree leaves
Settle desolately in the yard,<37>
My heart withers and dries.
Ah, autumn! The autumn of maturity
Is the greatest enemy to my spirit, which withers without growing!
It is a messenger from the underworld!

**The Chrysanthemum's Loyal Advice**

The chrysanthemum, always like a virtuous lady,
Without coquetry, in a plain voice, says:
Do not cry, do not be pained,
Living in this world is all about the heart.
If you smile, the world smiles too,
If you are angered, the world is angered too.
Look at me, who belatedly, belatedly, on a frosty morning,
Have put on the smile of spring.
Ah! I cried. I cried even more.
And I kissed the chrysanthemum blossom.

**Unknowable Symbol**

Doing work I hate all day long,
Only after the streetlights are lit,
Gazing at the dilapidated old fortress,<38>
Hurriedly seeking what I call home,
My sick wife is sobbing, sniffling,
My two-year-old child shouts "Daddy!"
Happily runs out, grabs me,
And chatters, urging me to come up quickly.

Ah! Life presses down on my neck!
Ah! Wife whose health is ruined!
Ah! Little one crying in loneliness!
Ah! What symbol could this be!

**First Cold**

The north wind blows fiercely.
The trees, stripped of their clothes,
Their skeletal appearance
Is endlessly wretched.
I, trembling in the cold,
Whenever I see the trembling branches,
See the faces of my brethren
Crying from cold and hunger.

**Our Village**<39>

In a corner of the city,
Beneath the crumbling fortress wall,
Where bent-backed old trees
Stand sparsely,
A rented room or two
Is where I sleep.

A neighborhood where, once the sun sets, it's like hell,
In the pitch-black alleys,
The footsteps of hungry ghosts
March silently.
God's illegitimate children,
A village where only the poor live.
Ah! Stepping into
This village,
Whether morning or evening,
A crowd of hungry ghosts,
Only skin and bones remaining,
I always see.

**Proletarian of the Heart**

An empty stomach is filled when fed.<40>
A cold body is warmed when clothed.
The proletarians of the world today
Seem content if only they can eat and be clothed.

But I am a proletarian of the heart!
Even more pitiful than they.

What is food for a hungry heart?
What are clothes for a cold heart?

**Oh, I am Sick!**

Oh, I am sick!
To cure the pain,
As soon as I wake, I look for medicine.
When the suffering is severe,
I even take stimulating narcotics,
Knowing they are harmful.

Oh, I am sick!
An incurable patient, like one with consumption.
But I firmly believe there is a medicine
That can cure this pain.<41>
My yearning, longing, faith
Will obtain the elixir of "Truth."

Oh, I am sick!
Clutching my burning chest,
I cry out the medicine's name, "Truth."
And my frail feet
Wander in search of "Truth,"
Day and night, in all my time.

**Love and Hate**

If on the lute of my heart
There are strings of love and hate,
Striking the string of Love,
I will praise my life.
Striking the string of Hate,
I will only curse my life.
If there is another melody besides these,
It must be a lie
Or an echo of the previous tune.<42>

**Summary**

This collection of nine short poems by Kim Sŏksong paints a bleak picture of life marked by decay, poverty, suffering, and existential angst. The poems move from observing external decline (an old man, autumn) to internal despair, the burdens of family life in poverty ("Unknowable Symbol," "Our Village"), a comparison between material and spiritual destitution ("Proletarian of the Heart"), and a desperate, illness-fueled search for "Truth" as the only possible cure. The final poem simplifies existence into a stark oscillation between loving/praising life and hating/cursing it. From a colonial history perspective, the poems starkly reflect the widespread poverty, despair, and sense of entrapment felt by many Koreans under Japanese rule. The intense personal suffering and the desperate yearning for an abstract "Truth" could be interpreted by historians as reflecting a search for meaning, solace, or even national identity/liberation in a suppressed society.

菊花의 忠言
언제보아도 숙녀가튼 국화가

알 수 업는 象徵

반기어 뛰어나와 나를 잡고
어서 올나오라고 지절대인다.

아! 생활에 덜미를 눌닌다!
아! 건강을 파괴한 안해!
아! 孤寂에 우는 어린 것!
아! 이것이 무슨 象徵일가!

첫치위
북풍이 드세게 분다.
옷을 벗은 나무들의
뼈만 남은 꼬라지가
한업시 처량하다.
치위에 떠는 나는
떠는 가지를 볼 때마다
치위와 주림에 우는
형제의 얼굴을 본다.
