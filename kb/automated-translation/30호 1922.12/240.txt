Street of Old Days
						Yohan

Little complexities, little worries, little happiness,
The water seller at dawn, the drunkard at sunset,
Smoke crawling on the ground on a gloomy day,
Water seeping into the inn's kitchen...
Oh, street!
With uneven limbs,
Like a first-born precious daughter breastfeeding a house, you managed,
My street.

On rainy season days, preparing muddy ground for wooden clogs,
And in winter, on your frozen slope,
Children's ice sliding, a grandmother's lament,
The attire of men and women going for New Year's visits
Fluttering heavily in the cold wind,
Street,
Your cheek was reddish in the morning sun. <32>

Rained on, trampled on, windblown,
The bumpy, hardened ground
Where the sincere cart clattered along,
When a child selling distant glances fell over a stone root,
You couldn't bear it and laughed continuously.

On a night brightened by the frozen moonlight,
The long cry of the nighttime taffy seller,
Beneath the dim room lamp, the hurried beat of the smoothing block,
How much must it have resonated with your loneliness and cold.
And, even when spring came and the haze shimmered on distant mountains,
Even when the white shoes of women going flower-viewing were leisurely,
You, in the dusty corners, guarding faint shadows,
Restlessly wandered here and there.

Sometimes you sighed,
Passing through moss-covered drains,
Crossing bridges carved from bent pine trees,
And crossing the ferry on the early morning tide,
Pretending not to hear the sound of laundry,
Street, you <33>
Kissed the stream
And humbly joined hands with the village path.

On long, long summer days,
Diligently visiting alley houses,
Comforting the long sighs of widows preserving their chastity,
Drinking the tears of heartbroken daughters-in-law.
When did you ever
Say you disliked it, said it was dirty, said you couldn't bear it?
The peace within the dream surrounding you,
The laziness passed down through generations,
You may have looked upon it with pity,
But you did not scold.
With astonishing patience,
You waited steadfastly
For them to find their own happiness.
Back then, I knew your demeanor, your gaze,
The warmth of your heart,
Oh, street,
I knew, I heard, I touched. <34>

And yet, and yet,
Today you do not recognize me,
And I, too, meet you for the first time.
To your left and right, shabby shops
Stand far back, only glancing sideways.
Whatever tragedy occurs upon you now,
You intend to pretend you know nothing at all.
Street, street,
You have changed so,
You have changed so.

Home

Home, my cradle, my paradise,
Just as the spot kissed remains
On the breast of a lover parted by death,
From your moss-covered roof to the collapsed corner,
My childhood footprints are engraved.

There, my dawn dreams faded through the paper-covered window,
And the warm lower part of the floor on winter days
Was all the more welcome because of the wind flapping at the door. <35>
Also, the leaning (?) fence, the gleaming rice chest decorations,
Faces scattered by the arrival of "time."

Even as the busy market days came and went,
This neighborhood, which I thought would never change,
Since the fortress gate was torn down and a new road was made,
The bustle of the inner city rushed out like a flood tide,
Ah, even the memory of you has been swept from the earth.

However, even now, those dark corners,
Even now, the vexing garden on autumn evenings,
All the memories trembling in my nerves, home, my cradle, my paradise,
Even now, even now, I see them all, I embrace them all. <36>
<32-36>

**Summary**

The work consists of two poems, "Street of Old Days" and "Home," both by the author Yohan. The first poem personifies a street from the past, recalling its intimate connection with the daily lives, joys, and sorrows of its inhabitants – water sellers, drunkards, children playing, widows sighing. The street is depicted as a patient, enduring entity that nurtured its community. However, the poem concludes with lament, stating the street has changed drastically, becoming cold and indifferent, unrecognizable to the narrator. The second poem, "Home," expresses similar nostalgia for a childhood home, recalling specific sensory details and feelings associated with it. It notes how modernization, symbolized by the tearing down of a fortress gate and the building of a new road, has irrevocably altered the neighborhood and seemingly erased the past. Both poems express a deep sense of loss and alienation due to rapid change, contrasting a fondly remembered, organic past with a colder, unfamiliar present. For historians, the poems offer a personal, emotional perspective on the physical and social transformations occurring in Korea during the early colonial period, specifically the disruption of traditional life and landscapes (e.g., tearing down city gates, building new roads) driven by modernization efforts under colonial rule, and the resulting sense of displacement and loss felt by individuals.
