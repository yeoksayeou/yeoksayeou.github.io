**Body Text Translation:**
Fighting Through Storms and Torrential Rain, Near Death, to Land in Osaka
						Aviator An Ch'angnam

It is a fact known throughout the land, through reports in newspapers both in the homeland [Korea] and Japan, that among the many civilian aviators of Japan, Mr. An Ch'angnam, the sole Korean aviator, participated in the prize mail flight between Tokyo and Osaka hosted by Japan's Imperial Aviation Association and achieved success with extraordinary results.

Mr. An, who had applied for last June's prize flight and the Kanazawa-Hiroshima mail flight in the autumn of the same year but could not participate due to lacking his own aircraft, also applied in June for this year's flight announced in late spring. However, still lacking an aircraft, he was again facing the prospect of non-participation. While striving intensely, he learned of a Shima-style aircraft owned by the Army Aviation School in Tokorozawa. This particular Shima-type plane had malfunctioned every time it took off during initial test flights, leading to the tragic deaths of 3 or 4 pilots, after which it was discarded in a warehouse like scrap. Mr. An arranged to borrow this plane and managed to participate with great difficulty. However, as it was a terrifying decommissioned plane where every pilot had met a tragic end, anxiety and worry never left him. Adding frost to snow (making matters worse), unfortunately, the weather on November 6th, the day Mr. An departed, was treacherous with dark clouds and severe storms. Despite the dangerous conditions where even Japanese pilots who departed the same day with high-horsepower, high-speed aircraft had to make emergency landings midway, Mr. An pushed onward.

However, disaster indeed struck over the waters of Ise Bay when the engine malfunctioned. On top of that, assaulted by storms and torrential rain, misfortune struck over the overlapping mountain ranges of the Ise and Ōmi regions. The aircraft began to fall <74> upside down, and Mr. An's body and plane were destined to be smashed to pieces in some nameless mountain valley. Yet, our Mr. An managed to land safely in Osaka without the aircraft touching the ground even slightly [implying a controlled landing, not a crash]. Truly, this was a miracle and a new record in the Japanese aviation world. It was a great feat that could not have been achieved by anyone but Mr. An. Consequently, the Japanese public is now shooting arrows of criticism, asking, "Since a civilian succeeded with an aircraft the Army Aviation School couldn't use, was the army's failure due to the aircraft being incomplete or due to unskilled piloting?"

How gratifying! Possessing such an exceptionally skilled, genius aviator, we sincerely hope that through our own strength, we can provide Mr. An with his own aircraft so that his genius can be fully displayed without regret, and his research and endeavors can be brought to completion. - What is published here is Mr. An's account of his recent adventure, written in a popular and plain style, avoiding technical jargon, and sent to this magazine. (By a reporter)

The first announcement of the Mail Flight hosted by the Japan Imperial Aviation Association this time was in early May. When I first encountered the announcement, rather than feeling happy, my heart sank.

This was because I had the bitter experience of applying with the ambition of not losing to others in the two flights last year, only to suffer the shame towards others of being unable to participate because I didn't have an aircraft. It was because I felt the sorrow of fearing failure and disappointment more than the joy of leaping hope.

However, I still couldn't bear to just sit still, so I went ahead and submitted the application. Then, I spent the entire summer trying hard to borrow or find an aircraft but couldn't. Just as I was getting extremely anxious, thinking, "I guess I won't be able to participate this time either and will just be embarrassed," in early October, the Army Aviation Bureau agreed to lend me a Nakajima-type aircraft from the Army Aviation School (one that had bad test results and hadn't been used at all, left abandoned for a long time). Only then did I feel relieved.

Typically, other people's aircraft were Kato-types with 360 horsepower and a speed of 120 miles per hour, or Takashima-types with 270 horsepower and 140 miles per hour. This Nakajima-type I was to fly had only 150 horsepower and a speed <75> of just 80 miles per hour, making it almost incomparable. Not only that, while other planes' propellers usually rotated 1,350 times per minute, this one only turned 1,200 times per minute, meaning it couldn't even reach 80 miles per hour properly nor take off well. However, I had confidence in myself and asserted to the many people who tried to dissuade me, saying, "It's dangerous to fly such a long distance with this," that "As long as the engine holds up without malfunctioning, the flight is no problem."

Now, the rules for this flight were not a direct round trip between Tokyo and Osaka. We had to pass through the airspace of three intermediate points: Mishima, Toyohashi, and Yōkaichi. At each of these three locations, judges would be stationed at the training grounds, watching to see if we passed through the airspace above. Mishima requires crossing Mount Hakone, a famously treacherous mountain in Japan. Toyohashi requires crossing Suruga Bay in Shizuoka Prefecture to reach the coast of Aichi Prefecture. Yōkaichi requires crossing Atsumi Bay from Toyohashi, then crossing the sea of Ise Bay, and finally crossing the perilous region between Mie and Shiga Prefectures where countless mountain ranges like Mount Kinkō(?), Mount Kamagatake, Mount Noboriyama(?), and others are lined up. We had to safely cross these three points, cross the end of Lake Biwa, and head to Osaka. Landing midway meant disqualification. Merely making the round trip directly without stopping at the three intermediate cities barely qualified as passing. Making the round trip while stopping at all three places was required. The judging criteria were: 1. Stopping at the designated three locations. 2. Round-trip time. 3. Weather conditions. 4. Landing. For criteria 3, the more severe the weather on the flight day, the more points were awarded. For criteria 4, pilots often become fatigued during long-distance, long-duration flights, making landings less smooth and calm; thus, the manner of piloting during landing was observed.

Once these regulations were announced, the participating aviators were busy traveling by train to the three designated cities to familiarize themselves with the flight path direction and the appearance of the cities. I, however, did not do that. I stayed in my room and familiarized myself using only maps. This was also a reckless act, but somehow I felt confident that I wouldn't fail even with just this preparation. I drew the route on the map with a red line, calculated all the times, wrote them down, and made it clear at a glance.

Then, all participants drew lots (抽籤) for the flight schedule, and I was scheduled to depart on November 5th.

Finally, after completing all preparations, the awaited 5th arrived. My heart pounded strangely. <76>

At that time, the return telegram from the Chosŏn Ilbo Company regarding the homeland visit flight had not arrived by this day. Since I needed to finalize that decision before going to Osaka, I waited anxiously until departure time on this day, but there was no news, making me very agitated. Just then, the airplane also developed a malfunction. So, for various reasons (於此於彼), I had no choice but to postpone the flight by one day, deciding to depart on the 6th, while waiting for the telegram and repairing the machine.

Some friends even suggested giving up, saying it was better to quit since the plane was malfunctioning even before departure, but I still resolved to depart and waited for the 6th.

The 6th arrived. To the Chosŏn Ilbo reporter who came out to the airfield, I regrettably spoke of the non-materialization of the cancellation [of the homeland flight contract] and climbed aboard the plane.

Strangely, the sky was overcast that day and didn't look like it would clear up. There was much debate about whether to depart or not. "It doesn't look like the sky will clear up..." "What does it matter? These clouds will soon lift..." Ignoring these discussions, I just took off, leaving the Yoyogi Training Ground.

This departure into dark clouds, facing a thousand-ri journey, was exactly at 0:48 PM.

Taking off into the air, even in someone else's old machine, for something I had long desired, felt incredibly refreshing and exhilarating. However, the reality was that it was such a long distance, and the thought of finding my way through the air to places I had never been, whose names I was hearing for the first time, caused considerable worry. The machine was a sick, old thing, and the day was overcast—.

Leaving Yoyogi, heading towards Mount Hakone to go to Mishima, <77> about 10 minutes into the flight, danger already approached. Pitch-black clouds stretched densely from the sky seemingly down to the ground, and the machine had to plunge right into them. Dark clouds, such dark clouds I had never seen. I couldn't even see the airplane I was sitting in; I could barely make out the control stick (操縱機) beneath my eyes. Surrounded by darkness, unable to see anything beyond one foot, I had no way of knowing which way was up or down, east or west. All I could do was rely on the clock and compass needle (時針) and push straight ahead into the pitch blackness. But this "pushing straight ahead" was extremely dangerous. Besides the lack of horsepower, the propeller's weakness made it impossible to gain much altitude, and ahead stood the 1,500-meter high Mount Hakone. Unable to see beyond one foot, I wouldn't know even if the mountain was right in front of me. It was clear that if I continued straight, I would crash the plane into the mountainside. Collision, tragic death, danger pressed in moment by moment. After thinking hard, I decided there was no choice but to turn; I had to try crossing over the pass where the mountains connected to the south. So, within the dark clouds, I adjusted my angle 2 degrees south and proceeded. But that too was just my own guess; I had no way of knowing where the mountain actually was, whether I had crossed it, or hadn't crossed it yet. With a deeply anxious and uneasy heart, I flew on for quite a while. I figured it should take 35 minutes after leaving Tokyo to cross, but even after 40 minutes, I still had no idea where I was, and my anxiety grew. Then, glancing down, just like seeing a coin-sized patch of blue sky through a gap in the clouds on an overcast day on the ground, I saw a tiny, truly coin-sized patch of blue through the clouds. "Ah! It's Lake Ashi (蘆湖)!" I cannot describe how relieved I felt. Lake Ashi is a famous scenic lake in Mount Hakone, and since the lake is beyond the mountain, I knew I had passed the high peak. Having thus barely overcome one hurdle, the clouds began to thin slightly from there, so I reached Mishima without incident. Over the Mishima Training Ground, to make sure the judges below could see clearly, I flew very low beneath the clouds, circled once, and then turned my angle 33 degrees north towards Toyohashi. About 10 minutes after leaving Mishima, I entered pitch-black clouds again, but this time, after piercing through the clouds for a short while, strangely, a gap opened between the cloud layers. The clouds formed two distinct layers, one spread out below the airplane <78> and another layer resting above the airplane as if cut cleanly by a knife. A path for the airplane opened between the upper and lower clouds, and at the end of it, a patch of blue sky shone like a ray of hope. Racing through that path between the clouds, gazing at the distant blue sky, was truly exhilarating. Racing exhilaratingly until I reached the patch of blue sky, I found the lower clouds still spread out, but the upper clouds were gone, and I could see the sun. This was the first time I saw the sun that day. The sun above reflected off the clouds spread below, sparkling brightly, just like sunlight reflecting on accumulated snow. Racing above that was also extremely pleasant. But even more pleasant was this – after racing over it for a short while, the clouds spread below began to look like blooming peony flowers (牧丹의 함박꼿), billowing up. The setting sun in the west shone upon them, making them look as dazzling as fully bloomed peonies clustered together. The refreshing feeling as I flew over them was truly beyond description. I felt that even celestial beings, if they existed, could not taste such pleasure (快味). Forgetting the anxious worry about whether I was passing over mountains or sea, as the ground below was not visible, I flew with indescribable refreshment over those peony-like clouds for quite some time. Entering Aichi Prefecture, near Toyohashi, the aircraft once again entered pitch-black clouds. However, being close to Toyohashi, I flew very low and managed to pass over the Toyohashi Training Ground. Then, from within the dark clouds, I turned my angle 38 degrees north and headed for Yōkaichi.

After heading towards Yōkaichi from Toyohashi in the dark clouds for about 15 miles, the clouds began to thin, but disaster struck. After passing all of Aichi Prefecture, while the aircraft was over the sea of Ise Bay, misfortune of misfortunes, the engine (發動機) malfunctioned.

This Mie Prefecture, Ise Province area is already a difficult place for flight navigation to get one's bearings. Years ago (年前), Army Flying Lieutenant Mizuta (水田氏), intending to go to Osaka, misjudged his direction here and ended up wandering out over the Pacific Ocean. Furthermore, numerous famous treacherous mountains stand consecutively in this Mie-Shiga vicinity, making it a very dangerous zone requiring extreme caution. Facing this dangerous zone ahead, while over the Ise Bay sea, the engine failed and I began to fall. This was truly a problem beyond what piloting skill (조종술) could handle. In despair and dejection, hoping only to avoid falling into the sea, I turned towards Nagoya City (名古屋市) to the north <79> and barely managed to drag the steadily descending aircraft along without landing. Almost reaching Nagoya, thinking, "It's already a failure, what does anything matter now?" as a last resort, while still airborne, I started fiddling recklessly with the engine to try and fix it. Like a child fiddling with a broken toy, I tinkered with it for a while, trying to fix it. By a stroke of luck (天幸), the propeller's rotational force increased again, and the aircraft gradually climbed upwards. "Oh! It's working!" I exclaimed. Just as I was about to land, I turned back, hugged the coastline, taking 30 minutes for what should have been a 10-minute stretch, found my course again, and began to push forward.

After managing to fly like that for about 20 minutes, the day grew dark, nothing was visible, and a terrifying storm blew in as if trying to block my path. Even without this storm, flight would have been impossible, but I braved it and pushed onward. Immediately following, torrential rain came down with ferocious intensity.

Storm, torrential rain, treacherous mountains in the danger zone... Ah, it felt as if heaven intended to kill me. I felt endlessly resentful and sad, and my energy seemed to plummet. Even with the finest aircraft, there was nothing one could do in this wind and rain. However, thinking this wouldn't do, I resolved to go as far as I could, mustered my strength, and applied all my skill (才操) and energy to advance. The aircraft seemed to be barely moving forward, but covering perhaps 30 miles, or maybe even less, in the time it should have taken to cover 80 miles in an hour.

In that state, continuing the veritable desperate struggle (惡戰苦鬪) against the rain and wind, I suddenly felt, amidst the storm, a distinct, strong wind hitting my left cheek in the darkness. "Ah! This is serious. I am falling along with the airplane right now," I realized.

I knew this because my body was falling sideways through the air, causing a very strong wind to strike one cheek. Ah, so I'm finally going to fall and die! That miserable thought flashed through my mind even in that moment. And I also knew that the place I was falling was definitely over the treacherous mountains like Mount Kinkō and Mount Kamagatake.

So, I resigned myself to meeting my end right there.

However, even then, because I had originally practiced advanced flying techniques (高等飛行術) (having practiced aerial acrobatics many times), I thought, "If I'm going to fall anyway, let me fall on flat ground, not in the mountains." So, <80> maneuvering the falling aircraft like performing acrobatics, I righted it, turned around, and headed back the way I came, towards the coast of Ise Bay I had passed earlier. Flying for a while, I realized that this time I had fallen not because of the engine, but pushed down by the storm and rain. As I looked for a suitable place to land, the thought occurred to me that letting only the Japanese and Taiwanese succeed while I alone failed was an unbearable shame. Thereupon, deciding that if I was to die anyway, I should push forward until the very end, I fiddled with the engine again and turned back around. The suffering I endured while struggling desperately not to let the aircraft touch the ground, even while wandering like this, was truly a miserable hardship unimaginable even in others' dreams.

With such almost reckless determination, gritting my teeth, I turned back again, but this time (having flipped over several times in the air) I couldn't get my bearings. This caused trouble, but judging by the time elapsed, I estimated my current position to be between Mie and Shiga Prefectures. From there, I simply headed west, exerting all my strength to the point of death, and pushed onward. After flying for quite a while, I glimpsed greenish-blue water and land through a break in the clouds. "This is bad," I thought, "Have I come to the northern coast of Japan?" But considering the time, it was insufficient to have reached the Sea of Japan, so I realized it must be one end of Lake Biwa (琵琶湖). Realizing I had reached Lake Biwa brought another problem: I had passed Yōkaichi without stopping there.

However, I couldn't just continue straight either, so I turned back from there, passed over Yōkaichi, turned around again, crossed Lake Biwa, and when the city of Nara glimpsed below, the rain and wind had stopped, and the clouds began to clear (볏겨지기). Having endured hardships that seem impossible in reality, falling into the jaws of death (死境) multiple times and escaping again and again, enduring unknown struggles alone, the sun had already set. When I passed Kyoto fleetingly and entered the airspace over Osaka, I looked down and saw the electric lights were already on. I entered Osaka's airspace flying very low, and immediately spotted the airfield. Others might circle several times here before landing, but I just went straight in and landed right away.

When the airplane touched the ground (地面), I felt like I had come back alive from hell and stepped onto the human world.

After landing in Osaka, I heard from the judges: "Anyone else <81> would surely have landed midway, but since Mr. An is a person who has even succeeded in night flying, we expected you to arrive even in the rain and were just preparing flares (화투불 - likely meaning landing flares/lights)." And seeing my landing, they remarked, "You land as if you've always flown at this training ground... How many times have you been here before?" When I said it was my first time, they seemed surprised.

I think to myself: if there had been even one other Korean person besides me on this flight, I probably would have landed midway and wouldn't have found the courage to undertake that reckless adventure.

Mr. Hsieh (謝君) from Taiwan (臺灣) seems like a very fortunate person. He departed from Tokyo on the same day as me but arrived safely in Osaka without enduring such hardships. This is because conditions in the air are different from what is seen on the ground and change moment by moment, so even people flying the same route on the same day can experience vastly different conditions if one departs 5 minutes before or 5 minutes after the other.

During the 4 or 5 days I was in Osaka, our compatriots residing there always came to visit, showed me great kindness (厚誼), and gave me much guidance, so I spent the time pleasantly.

I returned from Osaka to Tokyo on the 11th. The return trip was smooth, without any malfunctions or particular hardships. When departing from Osaka, Ishibashi (石橋), a famous first-class aviator, Ōba (大場), another first-class aviator, Mr. Hsieh from Taiwan, and I all took off together.

The landing order in Tokyo was: Mr. Ishibashi's plane, a French-style (佛國式) Spad, capable of 130 miles per hour, surprisingly took a slow 3 hours for some reason (왼일인지). Next was me, taking 3 hours and 25 minutes. Mr. Hsieh took 3 hours and 55 minutes. Mr. Ōba landed midway.

One minor discomfort on the return trip was needing to urinate (小便). Although I had taken care of everything, including bowel movements, before departing Osaka, perhaps due to drinking coffee (珈琲) before departure, I felt the urge to urinate as soon as I was airborne over Osaka. I endured it for over 3 hours straight until reaching Tokyo, which was the most difficult part. Normally, I could relieve myself onboard, but this time, because I was wearing multiple layers of clothing (의복), I just held it.

Finally, I offer my heartfelt thanks to everyone in the homeland (故國) who showed so much concern (勤念) for me during this flight. Night of November 18th <82>

**Summary**

This article is a first-person account by An Ch'angnam, the first Korean aviator, detailing his perilous participation in the 1922 Tokyo-Osaka prize mail flight sponsored by the Imperial Aviation Association of Japan. Flying a notoriously unreliable Nakajima-type aircraft borrowed from the Japanese army (which had killed previous pilots), An battled severe storms, fog, engine failure over Ise Bay, and disorientation over treacherous mountains. He recounts near-death experiences, including an uncontrolled descent and struggling against impossible weather, yet managed through skill and sheer determination to complete the required checkpoints and land safely in Osaka at night, a feat hailed as miraculous. The return flight was comparatively uneventful.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant as it documents a rare achievement by a Korean under Japanese colonial rule in a field dominated by the colonizers. An's success, particularly using a plane the Japanese military deemed scrap, was a source of immense national pride for Koreans and subtly challenged Japanese narratives of superiority. It highlights the obstacles faced by Koreans pursuing modern professions, their resilience, and the role of Korean media like *Kaeppyŏk* in celebrating such triumphs as acts of national assertion.

暴風 暴雨와 싸우면서 九死一生으로 大阪에 着陸하기까지
						飛行士 安昌男
日本의 帝國飛行協會 주최로 東京 大阪간의 懸賞郵便飛行이 잇섯든 바 日本의 만흔 민간 비행가의 중에 다만 한사람의 朝鮮飛行家로 安昌男氏가 이에 참가하야, 비상한 好成績으로 功을 일우엇슴은 內地와 日本의 각 신문의 보도에 의하야 천하가 周知하는 사실이다.
작년 6월의 懸賞飛行과 同年 가을의 金澤 廣島간 郵便飛行에도 가입은 하여노코도 자기소유의 비행기가 업서서 참가하지 못한 安氏는 금년 暮春에 발표된 금번 비행에도 6월에 가입하여 노핫스나 역시 비행기가 업서서 결국 또 참가하지 못하게 되는가 하야 몹시 노력하든 중에 所澤에 잇는 육군항공학교 소유 중 島式비행기가 잇는데 이 중 島式機는 처음 시험비행에 뜰 때마다 고장이 生하야 조종자가 3, 4人이나 참사한 후로 폐물과 가티 창고에 버려두엇든 것이라. 이것을 安氏가 借用하기로 되어 艱辛히 참가하게 되엇스나 원래가 조종자마다 참사한 무서운 廢機라 불안과 염려가 떠나지 안튼 터에 雪上에 加霜으로 불행히 安氏가 출발하든 11월 6일은 天氣가 험악하야 暗雲과 폭풍이 심하야 당일 함께 출발한 日人은 馬力 만코 속력 만흔 飛機로도 중도에 下陸한 위험한 氣勢이엇슴에 불구하고 安氏는 돌진하엿다.
그러나 과연 伊勢灣 바다 우에서 發動機에 고장이 生하고 그 우에 폭풍 폭우의 來襲을 바다 不幸 伊勢 近江地方의 重疊한 連山 우에서 機體는 걱구로＜74＞ 낙하하기 시작하야 安氏의 身과 비행기는 일흠도 모를 山間에서 분쇄되게 되엇다. 그러나 우리 安氏는 機體를 少毫도 땅에 다히지 아니하고 무사히 大阪에 착륙하엿다. 실로 이 한 기적이요 일본비행계의 신기록이라. 安氏 아니면 能치 못할 대모험이엇다. 인하야 日本 일반은 「육군비행학교에서 쓰지 못한 비행기로 민간에서는 이 가티 성공하엿스니 육군측에서 실패한 것은 비행기가 不完한 이유냐 조종술이 미숙한 이유냐」고 공격의 矢를 쏘으는 터이다.
통쾌! 비상한 기술 천재의 비행가를 가진 우리는 우리의 힘으로 비행기를 安氏께 소유케 하야 氏의 천재를 유감업시 발휘케 하고 氏의 연구와 사업을 완성케 하게 되도록 深切히 바란다. - 이에 揭載하는 것은 安氏가 금번의 모험담을 專門的 熟語를 피하고 통속적으로 평이하게 기록하야 본지에 보내준 것이다. (一記者)

이번 日本 帝國飛行協會에서 주최한 郵便飛行이 처음 발표되기는 지나간 5월 상순이엇습니다. 그 발표를 처음 접할 때 나는 반가운 마음보다도 돌이어 가슴이 성큼하엿습니다.
작년에 두 차례나 잇든 비행에도 남에게 지지 아니하리라는 욕심으로 參加申込은 하여 노코도 비행기가 업서서 참가하지 못하야 鬱憤한 마음과 남에게 대한 羞恥만 지은 쓰린 경험을 가진 내가 금년 이번에도 내것이라고 一機도 가지지 못하고 잇는 故로 엿습니다. 희망에 뛰는 희열보다도 落伍와 실망을 怯내는 비애를 가젓든 故로 입니다.
그러나 그래도 또 참아 그냥 잇기에 마음이 괴로와 그대로 申込은 하여 노핫습니다. 그리고 비행기를 빌어 구하기 위하야 그 녀름이 다 지나도록 힘을 써보앗스나 구하지 못하고 「이번에도 참가하지 못하고 모양만 창피하게 되는가 보다」하야 몹시 초조히 지내든 끗에 10월 초에 니르러 육군비행학교에 잇는 中島式飛行機(시험결과가 낫버서 一切 쓰지 아니하고 오래 내버려 두엇든 것)을 육군 항공국에서 빌려주게 되어 그때에야 안심되엇습니다.
보통 다른 사람들의 비행기는 加藤式이 360馬力에 1時間 120(비행)哩의 속력 또는 高島式이 270馬力에 140(비행)哩의 속력을 가젓는데 내가 탈 이 中島式은 겨우 150馬力에 80(비행)哩＜75＞ 속력밧게 갓지 못하야 거의 비교가 되지 못하엿습니다. 그 뿐 아니라 다른 機는 보통 프로페라가 1분간에 1,350회를 회전하는데 이것은 1분간 1,200회밧게 돌지를 못하야 1시간 80哩나마 잘 가지도 뜨지도 못하게 된 것이엇습니다. 그러나 나는 내 자신이 잇서서 「이것으로 그닥지 먼곳에 비행을 하는 것은 위험하다」고 말니는 여러 사람들에게 「發動機만 고장이 업시 잘 견듸어 주면 비행은 염려업다」고 단언하엿습니다.

그런데 이번 비행의 규정은 東京 大阪간의 직행왕복이 아니고 중도에 三島, 豊橋, 八日市 이 세 곳의 空中을 들러가지 안흐면 안되게 되고 그 세곳에는 모다 審査員이 練兵場에 잇서서 그 空中을 들느고 안들느는 것을 쳐다 보고 잇게 되엇습니다. 三島라는 곳은 日本서 유명한 險山 箱根山을 넘어가야 되는 곳이요 豐橋라는 곳은 靜岡縣의 駿河灣을 건너 愛知縣의 해변으로 가야되는 곳이요 八日市라는 곳은 豐橋에서 渥美灣을 건느고 또 伊勢灣의 바다를 건너서 三重縣과 滋賀縣 사이의 錦向山, 鎌箇岳, 野登山 등 기타 수업는 산맥이 느려노히 險地를 넘어야 가는 곳입니다. 이 세 곳을 무사히 넘어서 琵琶湖 끗을 건너 大阪으로 가야되는데 중간에 착륙하면 落第요 중간 三都를 들느지 아니하고 직행하야 왕복만 하면 간신히 及第인 格이요 왕복에 다- 3處를 들너와야 하되 심사표준은 1. 旣定의 三處를 들너온 것. 2. 왕복시간. 3. 日氣. 4. 착륙인데 3의 日氣는 비행 당일의 天氣가 험악하엿슬스록 多點을 치는 것이요 4의 착륙은 대개 원거리 장시간 비행에는 조종자가 피곤하야 착륙이 곱고 평온하지 못하는 것인 바 착륙시의 조종 如何를 보는 것입니다.
이상의 규정이 발표되자 참가하는 여러 비행사는 기차로 指定의 3都市를 여행하면서 비행진로의 방향과 도시의 모양을 눈닉혀 두느라고 여행에 밧분데 나는 그것을 하지 아니하고 내 방에 들어 안저서 지도로만 눈을 닉혓습니다. 이것도 무모한 짓이나 어쪈지 그것으로만도 실패업스리란 자신이 잇섯든 까닭이엇습니다. 지도 우에 朱線으로 진로를 그리고 시간까지 전부 계산하야 기입하야 일목요연하게 그리어 노핫습니다.
그리고 참가인 전부가 飛行日割의 제비를 뽑은(抽籤) 결과 나는 11월 5일에 출발하기로 되엇습니다.
이윽고 모든 준비를 마치고 기다리든 5일이 되엇습니다. 내 가슴은 이상하게 뛰놀앗습니다.＜76＞
그런때 母國訪問飛行에 관한 朝鮮日報社의 回電이 이날까지 오지 아니하여서 大阪 가기 전에 그 결정을 지어야 할 필요상 이날로 출발하기까지 苦待하여도 아모 소식이 업서서 몹시 초조해 하는데 마츰 비행기까지 고장이 생겻습니다. 그래서 於此於彼 하는 수 업시 비행을 1일 연기하야 翌 6일에 출발하기로 하고 回電을 기다리며 一面으로 기계수선을 하엿습니다.
출발도 전에 고장이 새기는 비행기이니 그만 두는 것이 조타는 友人까지 잇섯스나 그래도 출발하기로 결심하고 6일을 기다렷습니다.
6일이 되엇습니다. 비행장까지 나와준 朝鮮日報 記者께 하는 수 업시 解約의 不成立, 해약을 말하고 機上에 올나탓습니다.
이날 이상하게도 날이 흐리고 개일 것 갓지를 아니하고 출발의 可否論이 분분하엿습니다. 「날이 개일 것 갓지 안타느니」... 「무얼 이 구름은 곳 볏겨질 것이니까 관계치 안타」느니 하는 것을 나는 그냥 뜨기 시작하야  代代木練兵場을 떠낫습니다.

大阪잇는 동안의 4, 5일간은 大阪에 계신 우리 동포가 늘 오셔서 厚誼를 주시고 만히 지도해 주셔서 유쾌히 지냇습니다.
大阪서 東京으로 오기는 11일이엇는데 올 때는 아모 고장도 업고 별다른 고생도 업시 잘왓습니다. 大阪서 출발할 때 石橋라는 유명한 일등비행사 大場이라는 일등비행사와 臺灣의 謝君과 내가 가티 떠낫습니다.
東京 착륙순은 石橋氏의 機는 佛國式 스바트라는 것이데 1시간 130哩의 속력을 가진 것인데도 왼일인지 의외에 더듸게 3시간을 요하엿고 그 다음에 내가 3시간 25분 걸리엇고 謝君이 3시간 55분 걸리엇고 大場氏는 중도에서 나려 버렷습니다.
올 때에 다소 괴로웟든 것은 小便이엇습니다. 大阪 출발 전에 모든 준비와 함께 大小便까지 다본 후이엇것만 출발 전에 珈琲를 먹은 탓인지 大阪 공중에 뜨면서부터 소변이 급한 것을 그냥 3시간 이상 참고 東京까지 오느라고 제일 괴로웟섯습니다. 평상시 가트면 機上에서 볼 수 잇는 것이나 이번에는 의복을 여러 겹을 닙은 탓으로 그냥 온 것이엇습니다.

최후에 이번 비행에 나를 위하야 만히 勤念해 주신 故國 여러분께 마음과 정을 다하야 감사를 들입니다.  11월 18일 夜＜82＞
