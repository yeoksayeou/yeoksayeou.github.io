Magazine Title: 개벽 제30호
Publication Date: 1922년 12월 01일
Article Title: 現中國의 舊思想, 舊文藝의 改革으로부터 新東洋文化의 樹立에 他山의 石으로 現中國의 新文學建設運動을 이약이함. (Discussing the New Literature Construction Movement in Present-Day China as a Lesson [lit. Stone from Another Mountain] for Establishing a New East Asian Culture Stemming from the Reform of Old Thought and Old Arts in Present-Day China.)
Author: 北旅東谷
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

From the Reform of Old Thought and Old Arts in Present-Day China to the Establishment of a New East Asian Culture, Discussing the New Literature Construction Movement in Present-Day China as a Lesson (他山之石).
						Puk Yŏ-donggok (北旅東谷)
Tseng Kuo-fan's Shanghai Machinery Plant Establishment - Ch'en Tu-hsiu's Literary Revolution - Liang Ch'i-ch'ao's New Learning Society Declaration - Peking University's New Education Centered on Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei - Hu Shih's Humble Proposals for Literary Reform - Chou Tso-jen's Human Literature - Wang Shih-tung's New Literature Criticism.

1
In the October issue, I shallowly recorded some impressions I had regarding various aspects of present-day China after coming to Pukryuk (北陸 - Northern Continent/Region, likely referring to Northern China/Manchuria). After that, I received an earnest request from Brother Soch'un (小春兄) to write another article about China for this journal's New Year issue. Writing this article for this month's issue might feel somewhat repetitive, I think (思), and I also cannot be certain what great benefit frequent introductions to Chinese affairs will truly bring to our society. However, regarding this article, at this time when the New Culture Movement in both places [Korea and China] is intensifying daily, I believe (思) that reporting on it in some way will surely provide some stimulation and reference for our cultural movement in certain aspects. But how could I possibly describe it thoroughly with my shallow perspective? For now, I intend to write based on what I have observed.

2<23>

When looking at the past course and trends of the present Chinese intellectual world, what first comes to my mind is like a thousand-year-old tree that does not easily accept the axe, and even when it finally is felled, its ancient, deep-rooted spiritual essence seems unchanged. Therefore, from this, one can grasp to some extent the nature of its national character and its traditional thought. What is currently called New Culture, New Thought, seems almost like a synonym for Western culture. Its contact between Western culture and China began already from the mid-Ming Dynasty, so its history dates back over three hundred years. Later, during the Ch'ing Dynasty, relations with Westerners deepened further from the K'ang-hsi era through the Hsien-feng era. During Hung Hsiu-ch'üan's revolutionary movement, not only did many British and American people come to reside in Shanghai and other places like Kuangtung, but Hung Hsiu-ch'üan's Nanking government even dispatched an ambassador to the United States. The Ch'ing Dynasty also, conforming to the changing times thereafter, dispatched government-funded students to various European and American countries, and upon Tseng Kuo-fan's proposal, even established a machine manufacturing plant in Shanghai. Since this was already an event from over a hundred years ago, not only is the history of Western culture's import into China quite long, but one could also say that maturity in importing Western culture had already been achieved during that time. However, looking at the current state of Western culture in China, in some respects, it is no better than our society, which has a shorter history [of contact]. Given that the Chinese are not an incompetent people and possess a clear consciousness regarding human culture, the reason it has become this way must be either due to initial outright opposition or because some reason existed therein. When first encountering Western culture, they were not without some awe towards the essence of that civilization. However, originally possessing the characteristic of xenophobia and an arrogant disposition (妄大의 氣風), they absolutely rejected it, and unconditionally viewed it with jealousy, completely avoiding mutual contact. This fact is revealed much in the history of modern Westernization's eastward spread (西化東漸), so further redundant words are unnecessary. Afterwards, the threats and advice from the Powers were not just once or twice, ultimately reaching the point of forcibly annexing parts of their own territory, and internal turmoil also reached a point where awakening was truly necessary, yet there was no further caution or sense of urgency beyond the degree of its own progression. This was certainly not a good phenomenon, nor something to celebrate for the future of the Chinese people. Such threats from the Powers and its own weakness meant it would be weeded out by the times and could not avoid destruction. However, its existence also has a stronger aspect on one side; therefore, the phrase "incomprehensible" (不可思議) is often spoken by many as if it were a correct assessment regarding China. It is a fact that its own culture developed in the past to a point where it need not be ashamed before anyone.<24> Whether some incomprehensible "power" (力) lies hidden within the national psychology and ethnic essence, it seems to rather grasp the various ills at the end of its own culture solely through that. Not only that, but possessing an abundance of a unique characteristic for harmonization and dissolution (造化와 融解), they only rely on something after it aligns with their own consciousness and judgment, and then implement it. Therefore, for the Chinese, only what is Chinese can be used. Consequently, no matter how astonishing an object, if it is foreign, it does not readily contact their core being, and even if it does, it only happens after it is transformed into something Chinese. Thus, although present-day Chinese have imported Western culture and have established things currently, those are already things that have become Chinese-ified (中國化). One might wonder if this is a correct insight penetrating the true inner face of the Chinese, but when their situation is like this, while they have traditionally held an attitude of rejection and jealousy towards external Western culture, even if they were to accept it 순순히 (submissively/readily), how could they possibly understand and savor it from its roots and immediately assimilate it as if it were their own original possession?

The foundation of a nation's culture and life is built through numerous stages and refinements after the nation's history has existed for at least some time. To suddenly destroy the foundation built over thousands of years, whether good or bad, and try to adopt the culture of another house which has undergone a similar amount of experience, one naturally cannot avoid much chaos and sluggishness (疲緩). Furthermore, trying to take another's culture, fit it into one's own style, and implement it only after making it one's own (自家化) will require even more years and months, and its steps will naturally not be swift. And in its core aspect, what is called its own essence (自家의 精粹) always remains as it is, and even things made under the mode of Western culture always appear somewhat Chinese at first glance, not purely Western style. Thus, looking at present-day China from the outside, it appears entirely neither new nor old, neither East nor West. However, internally, there has been progress, and efforts have been made to make those aspects of Western culture that are fully understood into their own. In this, the internal substance (充實) of the nation might even be said to be better in some aspects than the Japanese, who merely applied a veneer on the surface to match and Westernize. Regardless of the above, if one observes the trends of modern Chinese society and its national character solely with賛意 (approving intent), the preceding judgment might indeed be close to its true meaning. Besides that, there are also many great ills (病弊). First, if we listen to what the giants of the Chinese intellectual world always passionately cry out, we might understand more clearly. Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu (陳獨秀氏), in the New Youth (新靑年) magazine, first discussed the overall reform of present-day China, then harshly criticized the skeptical and sluggish attitude of the Chinese towards New Culture, saying: The greatest malady (病毒) of the Chinese people lies solely in their adherence to the outdated old morals and old thoughts of Confucianism (孔學), the crystallization of all Chinese culture.<25> Therefore, this must first be completely overthrown, and to overthrow it, one should not hesitate even to destroy all of China's traditional old culture. He also stated that the muddled (糊糊模模) national inferiority and the fragmented (支離) and diffuse (汗漫) ethnic bad habits are the greatest flaws concerning the advancement of New Culture, and moreover, adopting the principle of harmonization (調和) and reference (參考) has become the major cause leading to the current failure. Thus, it is a fact that the maladies Mr. Ch'en criticized exist within the ideological inner face of the Chinese. Therefore, firstly, being excessively inclined towards self-centeredness (自家本位). Secondly, due to the ills of muddle-headed sluggishness (糊塗疲緩) and the attitude of harmonization and reference, it seems they have failed, if it is failure, regarding the construction of New Culture, and have not brought about any significant results so far.

3
As such, a kind of strong ideology, whether it should be called national conservatism or not, unbreakably encompassed the atmosphere of general society. Therefore, during the time when Western culture increasingly penetrated after the eastward spread of Westernization (西化東漸), although there were certainly some new figures in the intellectual world, and much effort was put into propagating new thought and new culture by these new intellectuals themselves, as well as by foreign missionaries and foreigners through various organizations and facilities, there had not been a period significant enough to mark a new era for the emergence of the new intellectual world, one might say. Also, at that time, there were no new movements noteworthy or surprising. Therefore, the era of the Chinese intellectual world did not begin then. When the revolution occurred and the Republic was established, although externally it could be called an unprecedented major transformation in Chinese history, the revolution signified resistance against the conquest by a foreign race (the Ch'ing Dynasty), riding the residual force of Hung Hsiu-ch'üan's revolutionary movement. The term Republic also arose from an unavoidable method due to the trends of the times, and it was not about transforming everything inherent to oneself and importing the true spirit of Western "democracy." Therefore, like changing the soup but not the medicine (換湯不換樂格 - idiom meaning superficial change), although it was a Republic, it was not without some colors of autocracy. Thus, society did not operate under the system and spirit of the so-called Republic with thorough determination and action, and there was also no great propaganda and guidance from the intellectual world possessing foresight regarding this. Consequently, one cannot pinpoint the proper starting point of the intellectual world here either; rather, it is said to have been later. According to my view, I would tentatively propose the time when the Great European War broke out after the 3rd year of the Republic of China. Looking at this through two reasons: (1) Internally, it was truly unexpected that the revolution succeeded so easily in such a short period after it began, but subsequently, when attempting to practice direct popular politics, the general populace had not awakened to that level.<26> Instead, things increasingly moved towards a state of chaos (混沌), leading to the rise of Yüan Shih-k'ai's (袁世凱) imperial regime and Chang Hsün's (張勳) restoration attempt, plunging all of China into great turmoil exceeding that of the revolutionary period. Consequently, all Chinese people were in a period of great suffering and doubt, and the general turmoil could absolutely not be resolved merely through politics. (2) Externally, not only did China decide to participate in the European War after it broke out, but its internal politics were also in an increasingly tumultuous state. Afterwards, as the whole world gradually fell into darkness (黑暗) and then began to respectively voice calls for reform (改造), the Chinese people could not help but awaken somewhat fundamentally. From these two reasons, amidst such circumstances at that time, knowledgeable individuals keenly felt that all reform lay not in political transformation but rather in the reform of the populace and society, namely the reform of New Thought and the construction of New Culture. At this point, various new figures who had been in the political world began to exert efforts to promote popular awakening. Also, when the so-called Peace Conference was held after the European War, the conclusion of the peace treaty was a major event, but the Powers, having fallen into the misfortune of collapsing together (共倒同亡), each tried to recover from their wounds (瘡傷) by turning towards China. Here, the Chinese people gained an opportunity for deep awakening. Thus, in Chinese society at that time, the great initiation of a genuine popular movement began to be anticipated. Suddenly, Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu (陳獨秀氏) published the New Youth (新靑年) magazine in Kuangtung as the vanguard of the New Culture Movement, and its new assertions and arguments, truly catering to the times and people's hearts, caused great waves in society. Following this, Liang Ch'i-ch'ao's (梁啓超) New Learning Society Declaration and other influential promotions of New Thought flourished daily. Among these, an even greater fact was Mr. Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei (蔡元培氏) withdrawing from the political world to assume the presidency of Peking University (北京大學), greatly expanding its affairs, gathering various new figures from the national new intellectual world, imparting New Education based on the new spirit of the times, and crying out for popular reform. A new atmosphere circulated in the gloomy society, and new strength arose in the desolate intellectual world, leading society to see great impulses and transformations. This was during the 5th, 6th, and 7th years of the Republic [1916-1918]. From this, I would say that the starting point of the Chinese New Intellectual World began during this period. Consequently, on the pages of the New Youth magazine, an influential organ of the New Culture Movement highly trusted by society, even more thoughts and opinions of various new figures were published. Other influential publications and the advocacy of influential social leaders, with the force of a torrential river (一瀉千里), accelerated the awakening of general society day by day. Moreover, the great activities of the Peking University faction were, more than anything else, the foremost spectacle (景象) of society at that time. While the general atmosphere of society was greatly tense like this, popular movements were bound to arise due to some trigger. Amidst the political situation of the time, which was descending further into turmoil daily, the outrageous behavior (跳梁) of the Anfu clique (安福派) became extreme. When the traitorous actions of Ts'ao Ju-lin (曹汝霖), Chang Tsung-hsiang (章宗祥), etc.,<27> towards Japan and the Shantung issue were exposed to the world, the "May Fourth Movement" (五四運動), purely centered around Peking University students, erupted on May 4th, 1919 (民國 8年 5월 4일). With the response of the entire society, national assemblies and other protest movements in various regions cooperated and advanced together, finally achieving victory. As the great power of the entire Chinese intellectual world was established and the awakening of the whole society received a great baptism, General Wu P'ei-fu's (吳偑孚將軍) war against the Anfu clique further spurred the determination of the populace. This can certainly be seen as a stage of great progress for the Chinese intellectual world, marking the drawing of a new era. Although its awakening might be considered late, now that it existed, it possessed capability and courage, and one could anticipate the construction of New Culture.

4
From this point, a genuine revolution in literature and thought arose throughout society, similar to the Renaissance in Europe. First was the clash and debate (辦論) between new and old thought, and second was the propagation and establishment of New Literature. Examining the trends of the intellectual world and the currents of thought among various new figures in sequence: At the primary stage (第一着), when the propagation of radical new thought and the movement for the revolution of old literature erupted, the struggle and debate between new and old first intensified. A long letter addressed to Mr. Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei (蔡元培氏) by Mr. Lin Ch'in-nan (林琴南氏), famous as a master of old literature, was published in the Kongŏn News (公言報 - Public Word News) around October 1918 (民國 7年 10월경). The general gist of its message was a strong attack, stating that they banish Confucius (孔子) and eradicate the ethical relationships (倫常), and also opposed the advocacy of Baihua (白話文 - vernacular Chinese), saying it was merely concealing one's clumsiness (其拙) with vulgar and base language (通俗野卑) because one is not proficient in classical Chinese (文言). Introducing one or two passages: "Must they overthrow Confucius and Mencius, eradicate the ethical relationships to feel gratified? Alas, because of a child's weakness and hardship, instead of seeking a good doctor, they trace the blame to his parents' hidden consumption (隱·瘵) and expel them; can the child then become plump and healthy day by day? Is there such logic?" (必覆孔孟, 剷倫常爲快, 鳴呼, 因童子之嬴困, 不求良醫, 乃追責其二親之有隱·瘵逐之, 而童子可以日就肥澤焉· 有是理耶?) And also: "If all ancient books are abolished, and vernacular language is used as literature, then the language used by cart pullers and sauce sellers in the capital all has grammar (下略-original text)? Then all the peddlers in Peking and Tientsin could be used as professors (下略-original text). It is acceptable to preserve the national essence (國粹) and explain the writing (說文), but it is unacceptable to make explaining writing the guest and Baihua the host." (若盡廢古書, 行用土語, 爲文學則, 都下引車賣醬之徒, 所操之語, 皆有文法(下略-원문)? 凡京津之稗販, 均可用爲敎授矣, (下略-원문) 蓋存國粹而說文可也以說文, 爲客, 以白話, 爲主, 不可也,-). The sincerity and severity of the rest of the phrasing make it arguably the sole representative fine writing defending old literature and old thought. Mr. Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei also responded carefully to this, clarifying point by point (逐節): "In the Republic, is it still possible to uphold the duty between ruler and minister (君臣之義), the main pillar of ethical relationships? Confucius was called the sage of his time (時之聖), so conforming to the present era would be following Confucius's intent, how could it be said to contradict it?" He also questioned whether the Baihua advocated by Messrs. Hu Shih (胡適), Ch'ien Hsüan-t'ung (錢玄洞), etc., is purely like the language used by cart pullers and sauce sellers in the capital, or whether it is truly impossible to fully express the meaning of ancient books and human consciousness using Baihua. "This is also a matter requiring deep investigation (考索), but it is certainly necessary to strive for sound reforms in all aspects, conforming to the times," he stated. Thus, after a period of debate between new and old,<28> the progress of the entire intellectual world truly began to settle onto a gradually solid foundation of the new. Following this, the revolution of old thought was a matter of course, but particularly regarding the Chinese characters (漢文字), which were most unsuitable for universal education and knowledge, there were many debates among various new figures and scholars. Among them, the most radical element, Professor Ch'ien Hsüan-t'ung (錢玄洞氏) of present-day Peking University, even published the theory of abolishing Chinese characters (漢字廢止論). Later, as an unavoidable new invention, the method of Zhuyin Zimu (注音字母 - phonetic annotation symbols) was devised. Like the English method of spelling words to form sounds, 39 special symbols (字母原字畧 - original forms omitted) were created and announced by Ministry of Education decree in November 1918 (民國 7년 11월), specifically promulgated for teaching in all provinces and districts. Afterwards, the use of new-style punctuation (標點) and symbols (符號) in writing, as a method to further clarify meaning in written language (文言), was proposed many times over several years in various local education conferences and was announced by Ministry of Education decree in 1918 (民國 7年). Its content was entirely like the punctuation and symbols used in Western languages and writing, roughly similar to the symbols used in English. However, while the Zhuyin Zimu is currently advocated much by some, it is still doubtful whether it will be commonly used even in the future, but the symbols (表號) have already become almost constantly used in both official and private writing. The above describes the period moving steadily from the phase of new-old debate into the phase of new construction. Regarding the direct literary revolution, the New Youth magazine was its precursor, publishing numerous great discussions and proposals (策案). From this, the era of the Renaissance (文藝復興) of all China began to be drawn. Around January 1917 (民國 6년 1월경), Mr. Hu Shih (胡適氏) published a groundbreaking major essay titled "Humble Proposals for Literary Reform" (文學改良蒭議) in the New Youth magazine. Following this, after Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu's (陳獨秀氏) "On Literary Revolution" (文學革命論) and Mr. Chou Tso-jen's (周作人氏) "Human Literature" (人的 文學) - these three essays were published, the entire society became as if mad, as if drunk (如狂如醉), expressing agreement with their assertions. Therefore, in Mr. Wang Shih-tung's (王世棟氏) book "New Literature Criticism" (新文學評論), it is stated: "Our New Literature Movement has already arrived at the stage of implementation (實行期) from the stage of debate (辯論期). Briefly explaining its historical progression, first, there was the declaration 'Humble Proposals for Literary Reform,' followed by 'On Literary Revolution,' after which 'Constructive Literary Revolution' (建設的 文學革命) (by Mr. Hu Shih) appeared, roughly establishing its foundation. Then came 'Human Literature,' which fully and satisfactorily expressed the intention of its construction." Now, briefly introducing the main points of the above essays allows one to roughly glimpse the content of the current intellectual world and the New Culture Movement.

5
In the reform of China's old literature, more than anything else, there was the difficult obstacle of complex Chinese characters. Therefore, anyone advocating for literary revolution first spent much thought on the characters. Next, the significant discrepancy between spoken and written language (言文이 無知不一致) was a major issue. As mentioned above, Mr. Ch'ien Hsüan-t'ung directly advocated for the abolition of Chinese characters. Afterwards, the general argument among various new figures at the time was to replace the old classical script (舊文言) with Baihua (白話),<29> meaning the "official speech" (官話) commonly used by the modern populace, which could be considered the most moderate approach. Thus, "Dead script cannot produce living literature" (死文字는 活文學을 産出치 못한다) became almost the slogan of the literary revolution. Although some publications and other books at the time used Baihua, there was no particular reaction from society. Since the New Youth magazine extensively used Baihua, praise from some and criticism from others arose frequently. After Hu Shih's "Humble Proposals for Literary Reform," directly advocating the use of Baihua, was published in the New Youth magazine, followed by the publication of Ch'en Tu-hsiu's "On Literary Revolution," also strongly emphasizing Baihua, the societal reaction vibrated greatly, and the value of Baihua literature was expressed to some extent in the world. Therefore, China's literary revolution meant the construction of Baihua literature. Although this reached its current great momentum through Hu Shih's concrete arguments, the advocacy of Baihua literature can actually be said to have existed before him. Looking at ancient times, after the Yuan and Sung dynasties, when Buddhist scriptures were translated, many passages that were absolutely impossible to interpret in classical script used recorded sayings (語錄), i.e., Baihua. Later, famous novels like *Water Margin* (水滸誌) and *Story of the Stone* (石頭記) were written in Baihua. Originally, Baihua literature occupied a position even within a part of Chinese literature (漢文學). More recently, when Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu was Dean of the Faculty of Letters at Peking University, he strenuously propagated this view. While Messrs. Ch'ien Hsüan-t'ung, Chou Tso-jen (啓孟 - Qimeng, Chou Tso-jen's courtesy name), and others also put much effort into this, vigorously promoting it, Mr. Hu brought his ideas from his studies in America (米州) and announced them immediately upon returning home, making it truly like harvesting grain that was ripe for picking. Now, introducing the essential points from his "Humble Proposals for Literary Reform":
"I think that currently, to speak of literary reform, one must necessarily start from the following eight matters: they are
1.  Must have substance (言之有物)
2.  Do not imitate the ancients (不模倣古人)
3.  Must pay attention to grammar (須講求文法)
4.  Do not groan without cause (不作無病之呻吟)
5.  Strive to eliminate clichés and formulaic language (務去爛調套語)
6.  Do not use allusions (不用典)
7.  Do not emphasize parallelism (不講對仗 - i.e., matching couplets)
8.  Do not avoid vulgar characters and vulgar words (不避俗字俗語)
These eight major points simultaneously harshly criticized the ills of old literature and signified the construction of Baihua literature. Interpreting his meaning sequentially: 1. 'Must have substance' means the great illness of traditional Chinese literature was speaking of nothing (無物), i.e., revering empty rhetoric (虛文) where there was no substance, so literature failed to be a tool for recording human facts, but rather, literature merely involved constantly repeating and rewriting the characters themselves. 2. 'Do not imitate the ancients' means that traditional<30> literature, for several thousand years, relied solely on the crude works of the ancients. Even renowned pieces and great works, if not word-for-word dregs (除睡 - likely a typo for 糟粕, dregs/chaff) of the ancients, would certainly not be accepted by the world, but would rather receive attacks and criticism. This is the greatest reason why Chinese literature failed to develop. 3. 'Must pay attention to grammar' is like saying Chinese literature completely lacks literary principles and grammar. In reality, not only is there no complete grammar yet, but it is absolutely impossible to say what could constitute a complete grammar. Therefore, looking at ancient literature, there are numerous instances where the meaning cannot be purely understood, and also instances that do not conform to grammar at all. Thus, one must necessarily rely on grammar. 4. 'Do not groan without cause' seems unimportant at first glance, but it has certainly become a great malady in national thought and national literature. Looking at so-called ancient literati or literature, it seems those literati were never without sorrow and suffering. Consequently, their writing seems merely a kind of record of impressions (感想錄) decorating and expressing that sorrow and suffering, but this was needlessly groaning without cause. 5. 'Strive to eliminate clichés and formulaic language' further painfully denounces the ills of Chinese literature. Setting aside one's own thoughts, merely taking a kind of strange cliché (爛調) and formulaic language (套語), which are the dregs (糟粕) of the ancients, to form a piece of writing was especially prevalent, making up the majority (태반) in poetry (詞調). 6. 'Do not use allusions' means that when writing, one should not necessarily extract terms or characters from ancient classics or the works of ancients. This is because when trying to build a literature for the common people (平民), if one relies only on extremely difficult and profound (至難至奧) ancient classics, it is absolutely impossible to plan for the spread of culture. 7. 'Do not emphasize parallelism' refers to the fact that since the implementation of the civil service examination (科擧), as the eight-legged essay (八股文) or *fu* poetry (賦詞) flourished, the fondness for matching couplets became a common failing (通弊) of all literati. Not only that, but they often disregarded their own meaning and were solely preoccupied with matching couplets. 8. 'Do not avoid vulgar characters and vulgar words' is the most important basis for constructing new literature. So-called old literature purely deemed common language and characters as vulgar (野卑) and did not use them, instead making it their daily task to quote increasingly obscure ancient characters (深僻한 古文字). This caused the great decline (大沈衰) of literature. The above assertions are truly earnest proposals for the reform of Chinese old literature. This interpretation is based on the original text and written briefly according to my understanding, but I think (思) there should be no significant error in the original meaning. By the way, aren't the ills identified in Mr. Hu's assertions above not only problems for Chinese literature and thought currents but also the same strategy for the reform of our Korean literature, which received the pure conquest of so-called Chinese literature (漢文學)? Furthermore, Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu's "On Literary Revolution" is more purely propaganda text than a strategic proposal for the construction of new literature. First praising the greatness of revolution, he states that the literary revolution is the first step in building a New China. Following that, his assertions are not greatly different from Mr. Hu's, heavily attacking the long-standing reverence for empty rhetoric (虛文).<31> He declared that from now on, adulatory (阿諛的), deceitful (虛僞的), exaggerated (舖張的), and aristocratic (貴族的) literature must all be abolished. Introducing a passage from his "On Literary Revolution": "The atmosphere for literary revolution has been brewing for more than a day. Its vanguard, raising the banner of righteousness first, is my friend Hu Shih. I am willing to be the enemy of all scholars nationwide, raising high the great banner of the 'Literary Revolutionary Army' to support my friend. On the banner, largely inscribed, are the three great principles of our revolutionary army: 1. Overthrow the ornate, adulatory, aristocratic literature; build plain, lyrical, national literature. 2. Overthrow the stale, exaggerated, classical literature; build fresh, sincere, realistic literature. 3. Overthrow the obscure, difficult, hermit (山林) literature; build clear, popular, social literature." (文學革命之氣運. 醯釀已非一日. 其首擧義旗之急先鋒. 則爲吾友胡適. 余甘胃全國學 究之敵. 高張「文學革命軍」 大旗. 以爲吾友之聲援. 旗上大書特書. 吾革命軍三大主義. 曰推倒彫琢的阿諛的貴族的文學. 建設平易的 抒情的國民文學. 推倒陳腐的舖張的古典文學. 建設新鮮的立誠的寫眞文學. 推倒迂晦的難澁的 山林文學. 建設明瞭的通俗的社會文學). And at the very end of the essay: "Are there those who, disregarding the praise or censure of pedants (迂儒), will openly declare war on the eighteen demons (十八妖魔)? I wish to serve as the vanguard with a 42-centimeter cannon!" (有不顧迂儒之毁譽. 明目張膽. 以與十八妖魔宣戰者乎. 子願以拖四十二生的大砲. 爲之前驅.). Looking at this, one cannot help but call his magnificent spirit (氣魂) the celebratory first shot (初頭賀砲) of the new literary construction. Besides this, Mr. Chou Tso-jen's "Human Literature" is also truly valuable work. Its main argument was that past literature, in a word, was all non-human literature (非人的 文學), so now, all of it should be abolished and human literature should be constructed. Following this, Mr. Hu Shih's subsequently published "Constructive Literary Revolution" even more concretely and nakedly (赤裸裸하게) expressed the plan for China's new literature construction. (Below translation reference)

6
As the movement for new literature construction progressed with rapid downward momentum (急轉直下), the person holding the reins (牛耳 - lit. bull's ear, symbol of leadership) in the literary world must undeniably be regarded as Mr. Hu Shih (胡適氏). Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu (陳獨秀氏) also holds immortal merit regarding this, but he originally did not possess the abundant knowledge like Mr. Hu, and also had the true colors of a revolutionary. Therefore, while any assertion he made might be radical and brisk, when it came to discussing strategically with thorough insight, he naturally had to yield to Mr. Hu. Moreover, Mr. Hu purely focused on and strived for the literary revolution, while Mr. Ch'en was originally a socialist, arguably the leader among present-day Chinese socialists. Therefore, regarding literature, Mr. Hu can certainly be said to be its central figure. Besides them, among the Peking University faction, there is truly no lack of famous and unnamed great scholars and great writers. The boundless hope for the future certainly allows one to anticipate the rebirth of Chinese culture. Looking at the thoughts of various new figures and the common current of thought (思潮) in society as a whole, it seems they already understand the new, and believe that they must necessarily destroy all the old and construct all the new, meaning the import of Western culture. Thus, among the countless factions like the American-style "democracy" thought faction, the socialist faction, the anarchist faction, and other ideological factions of various colors, the "democracy" and "socialism" factions are the most numerous.<32> The Peking University faction can generally be said to be "democratic," while Mr. Ch'en Tu-hsiu's faction is the socialist faction, purely the Labor-Peasant faction (勞農派). The inner workings of the current Chinese cultural movement are roughly as described above. However, as briefly described in the introductory remarks at the beginning of this essay regarding the unique, incomprehensible national common nature and thinking of the Chinese people, no matter what, anything foreign can absolutely not penetrate the minds of the people as it is, nor can it possibly be implemented. Therefore, looking first at Mr. Hu Shih's inner assertions, they are actually about the revival of China's original literature, definitely not the embodiment (本體化) of Western culture. In the introduction (導言) to his work "Outline of the History of Chinese Philosophy" (中國哲學史大綱), he first declares almost the revival of Chinese philosophy, stating that the daily approach of Eastern and Western cultures certainly signifies the future great achievement of Chinese philosophy. Besides that, even regarding socialism or anarchism, they only strive to make them into Chinese-style versions. For example, even in Peking, the Anarchist League (無政府主義者 同盟), viewed by the world with fear (蛇蝎視), is present (들네여 잇고 - archaic form of 늘여 있고/널려 있고, 'spread out/present'), and its propaganda materials are distributed daily, yet the current Peking government, which still has many autocratic colors, does not obstruct it in the least. However, not only does no danger arise, but the nation's unique inherent power prevents hasty, reckless actions (輕輕히 妄動하지 아니하고) based on firm self-awareness, no matter what happens before their eyes. They only intend to move after gradual fusion (融合) into their own ways. Therefore, summarizing the current various intellectual circles and the general atmosphere of society in China simply, the sole trend seems to be "constructing a new culture revived from the culture of Old China." And thus, they intend to reach the establishment of a New East Asian Culture. (End) <33>

**Summary**

This 1922 essay by Puk Yŏ-donggok analyzes the ongoing New Culture Movement in China, focusing on the literary revolution led by figures like Hu Shih and Ch'en Tu-hsiu. The author discusses China's long but complex history of engaging with Western culture, highlighting a perceived national characteristic of resisting direct adoption and instead "sinicizing" foreign influences. The essay traces the intellectual awakening spurred by internal turmoil (post-Republic chaos) and external pressures (WWI, Peace Conference), culminating in the May Fourth Movement. It details the debates between traditionalists (like Lin Ch'in-nan) and reformers, particularly the push for Baihua (vernacular) literature championed by Hu Shih's "Eight Points" and Ch'en Tu-hsiu's revolutionary calls, aiming to replace classical Chinese. The author concludes that despite adopting Western forms like democracy and socialism, the underlying trend is towards reviving and renewing Chinese culture, ultimately aiming to establish a "New East Asian Culture," presenting China's experience as a lesson ("stone from another mountain") for Korea. For historians studying Korean colonial history, the article is interesting because it shows a Korean intellectual analyzing China's modernization struggles as a parallel or model for Korea's own situation under Japanese rule. The framing of China's path as a potential "stone from another mountain" reveals the search for alternative modernization paths distinct from Japan's, and the discussion of cultural identity preservation amidst foreign influence resonated deeply with Korean intellectuals facing forced assimilation policies.

如斯히 이상의 國民的 保守라 할는지 모르는 一種 强靭의 思想이 顚樸不破하게 일반 사회의 공기를 包括하야 잇섯슴으로 西化東漸 後의 面洋의 문화가 日로 侵入되는 때에 물론 일부의 思想界의 新人들도 업지 아니하얏스며 新思想과 新文化의 선전도 직접 그 사상계의 新人들이나 又는 外國宣敎師 及 外國人들의 만흔 노력으로 各般의 機關 及 施設이 만엇스나 이에서 新思想界 出現의 新紀元을 그릴만한 시기가 업서왓다 하여도 可하며 또한 그때에는 주목하고 놀낼만한 新運動이 업섯다. 그럼으로 中國思想界의 紀元은 그 때에 엇지 안엇다. 혁명이 일어나고 民國이 성립되어 외면으로는 中國有史後의 民族的의 空前의 一大 變換이라 하겟스나 혁명이라 함은 洪秀全 革命運動의 餘威로 異族(淸朝)的 征服의 반항을 의미함이엇스며 民國이라 함도 時代的 思潮에 如何히 할 수 업는 不得已의 방법에 出함이엇고 족음이라도 自家의 固有하던 一切를 전환하야 西洋의 「데모크래시」적 眞精神을 옴겨옴은 아니엇다. 그럼으로 換湯不換樂格으로 民國이지마는 幾分專制의 色彩도 업슴도 아니엇섯다. 그리하야 무슨 철저한 각오와 행동에서 사회는 所謂 民國이라는 그 제도 그 정신 하에서 運轉되지 못하고 또한 此에 대하야 先見이 잇는 思想界의 큰 宣傳과 指導가 업섯다. 딸하 이에서도 그 思想界의 정당한 출발점을 잡을 수 업스며 오히려 그 뒤에 잇섯다고 하다. 나의 보는 바에 의하면 中華民國 3년 이후의 歐洲大戰이 爆發되든 당시라고 爲先 假說的으로 말하야두려 하나니 此를 두갈내의 이유로써 보면 (1)은 내부적으로 혁명이 일어난 후 그러케 短少한 時日에 容易히 성공됨은 실로 의외의 事이려니와 그 후 직접 民衆的 政治를 行하늠� 함에 일반의 국민은 그 정도에서는＜26＞ 覺醒되지 못하얏는지라 돌이어 日로 混沌의 상태로 만들어가게 되고 이에 袁世凱의 帝制가 起하며 張勳의 復辟이 起하야 全中國은 혁명 당시 이상의 大紛亂으로 들어가게 되매 於是乎 全中國人은 큰 고통 큰 懷疑의 期에 잇서 大勢의 紛糾는 결코 정치의 一段으로만 불가능하얏던 것. (2)는 외부적으로 歐戰이 일어나자 中國의 此에 參戰키로 되엇슬 뿐만이 아니라 당시의 內政도 더욱 紛糾의 상태에 잇섯스며 그 후 漸且로 全世界가 다 黑暗에 빠젓다가 비롯오 제가끔 부르지지는 改造의 聲에는 中國民도 근본적으로 좀 覺醒치 아니할 수 업섯던 것. 이상 두 이유에서 그 당시의 환경이 그마마한 中 有知識級의 人들은 一切의 改造는 오히려 정치의 변환에 잇다는 것보다 민중과 사회의 改造 즉 新思想의 개혁과 新文化의 건설에 잇슴을 절실히 통감하는 때에 비롯오 政治界에 잇던 여러 新人등은 다 民衆的 覺醒을 促成함에 出力케 되엇스며 又는 歐戰후 所謂 和平會가 개최되는 때에 講和條約의 締結도 大事件이엇거니와 共倒同亡의 비운에 빠진 열강들은 그의 瘡傷을 제각금 中國에 향하야 회복하려 함에 中國民은 此에서 한번 깁히 覺醒할 기회를 엇게 되엇다. 그리하야 당시 中國의 사회에서는 비롯오 진정한 民衆運動이 크게 개시케 됨을 예상하게 되다. 어느듯 陳獨秀氏가 廣東에서 新文化運動의 선봉으로 新靑年 雜誌를 간행하야 참으로 시대와 인심에 迎合되는 新主張과 新議論이 사회의 큰 波動을 일으키게 되고 從하야 梁啓超 등의 新學會宣言 及 其他 有力한 新思想의 鼓吹가 日로 奮興케 되며 그 중에 더욱 큰 사실은 蔡元培氏가 政治界로부터 脫退하야 北京大學의 교장으로 취임하야 栊�務를 크게 확장하고 全國新思想界의 各新人物 등을 網羅하야 時代的 新精神에서 新敎育을 施與하고 民衆的 改造를 절규함에 침울하던 사회에는 새 空氣가 돌며 寂寞하던 思想界에 새 세력이 생기어 사회는 무슨 큰 衝動과 變換을 보게 되엇다. 이는 民國 5,6,7 兩 3년간의 事이다. 此에서 나는 그 기간을 謂하야 中國 新思想界의 출발점이 비롯하여 젓다고 하겟다. 從하야 新文化運動의 유력한 기관으로 사회의 신임이 隆篤하던 新靑年 잡지 상에는 더욱 各 新人들의 사상과 의논이 만히 발표되어 잇고 기타 유력한 出版物 及 유력한 사회 指導者의 鼓吹는 一瀉千里의 勢로 일반 사회의 覺醒이 日로 促成되며 더욱 北大學派의 대활동은 무엇보다도 당시 사회의 第一 景象이엇다. 이가티 사회 일반의 공기가 크게 緊張한 가온대 잇서 어떠한 동기로서라도 民衆的 運動이 일어나게 되엇섯는데 日로 더욱 분규의 상태로 들어가는 당시의 政治上에는 安福派 一流의 跳梁이 극심하야 曹汝霖章宗祥등의＜27＞ 日本에 대한 賣國的 行動과 山東의 문제가 世에 폭로되매 此에서 純然히 北大學生으로 중심된 民國 8년 5월 4일이론 「五四運動이 폭발되어 전사회의 響應으로 각지의 國民大會 기타 示威의 운동이 協作幷進하야 마츰내 그의 승리로 全中國 思想界의 큰 세력의 수립되고 전사회의 覺醒이 크게 洗禮를 밧게 되자 더욱 吳偑孚將軍의 對安福派의 전쟁이 더욱 민중의 각오를 촉진하얏다. 此는 확실히 中國 思想界의 대진전의 계단으로 볼 수 잇는 때에 새 紀元이 크게 그어젓다고 한다. 그의 覺醒이 오히려 느젓다 할지나 이미 잇는 때에 능력과 용기를 가지어 잇슬 뿐만이 아니라 新文化의 건설을 기대할 수 잇섯다.

4
이로부터 전사회에 純然한 文藝 及 思想의 혁명이 起하야 마치 歐洲의 文藝復興이 잇슴과 가타엿나니 第一에 新舊思想의 충돌과 辦論이며 第二에 新文學의 선전과 수립이엇다. 次第로 그 思想界의 推移과 각 新人界의 思潮를 들어보건댄 第一着 一邊에 過激한 新思想의 선전과 舊文學革命의 운동이 爆發되는 때에 먼저 新舊의 투쟁과 辯論이 激起하야 舊文學의 大家로 유명한 林琴南氏가 蔡元培氏에게 致한 長篇의 書가 民國 7년 10월경 公言報에 발표되엇는데 그 辭意의 대개는 孔子를 擯하며 倫常을 剷한다 하야 크게 공격을 加하고 又는 白話文의 주장을 반대하야 此는 文言에 能치 못함으로 通俗野卑의 語로써 其拙을 藏함에 불과하다 하얏다. 그의 一二節을 소개하면 必覆孔孟, 剷倫常爲快, 鳴呼, 因童子之嬴困, 不求良醫, 乃追責其二親之有隱·瘵逐之, 而童子可以日就肥澤焉· 有是理耶?하고 又는 若盡廢古書, 行用土語, 爲文學則, 都下引車賣醬之徒, 所操之語, 皆有文法(下略-원문)? 凡京津之稗販, 均可用爲敎授矣, (下略-원문) 蓋存國粹而說文可也以說文, 爲客, 以白話, 爲主, 不可也,-라 하야 기타 措辭의 懇摯嚴切함은 舊文學 舊思想 옹호의 유일의 대표적 好文字라 하겟다. 蔡元培氏는 此에 대하야 또한 謹嚴히 답한 중에 逐節하야 辨明하얏는데 「民國에 잇서 오히려 倫常의 大綱인 君臣의 義를 守키 가능하며? 孔子는 時의 聖이라 한즉 此時에 在하야 시대에 順應함이엇지 孔子의 意에 悖한다 하리오 하고 현재의 胡適 錢玄洞 諸君의 주장하는 白話文이 純然히 都下의 引車賣槳의 徒의 操語하는 바와 如함인지 又는 白話文으로는 결코 古書의 義와 人間의 의식을 완전히 발표키 불가능한지? 此 亦 深히 考索할 문제이려니와 확실히  시대에 順應하야 건전한 一切의 革新을 務치 아니할 수 업다 하얏다. 이가티 일시는 新舊의＜28＞ 辯論으로 全般 思想界의 진전이 참으로 점차 튼튼한 新의 土臺上에 들어언치게 된 후로 先히 舊思想의 혁명은 물론이려니와 특히 普遍의 교육과 지식에 가장 부적당한 漢文字에 관하야 各 新人 及 學者의 辯論이 만어오다가 그 가온대 가장 극렬한 分子로서는 現 北大敎授 錢玄洞氏는 漢字廢止論까지 世에 발표케 되고 그 후 부득이한 新의 考案으로 注音字母의 법을 考究하야 英語의 綴語成音의 법과 가티 字母 39字(그 字母原字畧)를 特別形으로 제작하야 民國 7년 11월에 敎育部令으로 발표하야 各省區에 특히 傳習케 하얏스며 그 후에는 文言에 意思를 一層 明瞭히 하는 방법으로 新式의 標點과 符號를 作文에 사용하자 함이 多年間 各地 敎育會議의 만흔 提唱으로 民國 7년 敎育部令에 의하야 발표케 되엇는데 그의 내용은 전혀 西洋語學과 文字에 標點과 符號를 찍은 것과 가티 略 英語의 사용하는 表號와 一樣이엇다. 그런데 그 注音字母는 현재에 一邊에서 만이 提唱하는 터이로되 아즉은 將來로도 공통히 사용될는지 의문이나 그 表號에 대하야는 벌서 公私文字間에 恒用이 되다십히 되엇다. 이상은 新舊의 辦論期로부터 着着히 新의 建設期에 入케 되어 直接 文學의 혁명으로는 新靑年의 잡지가 그의 先驅로 위대한 논의와 策案이 다수히 발표되엇는데 이에서 비롯오 全中國의 文藝復興의 紀元을 긋게 되엇나니 民國 6년 1월경에 胡適氏가 「文學改良蒭議」라 하는 破天荒의 大論文을 新靑年 誌上에 발표케 되고 從하야 陳獨秀氏의 文學革命論과 周作人氏의 「人的 文學」이상의 三論文이 世에 발표된 후로 全社會는 如狂如醉하야 그읊� 주장에 다 贊意를 表케 된다. 그럼으로 王世棟氏의 新文學評論이라는 著書 중에 「우리의 新文學運動은 이미 辯論期로부터 實行期에 도착되엇나니 그 經過의 역사를 暫說하면 第一次 宣言으로 「文學改良蒭議」가 有하고 從하야 文學革命論이 잇슨 후 「建設的 文學革命」(胡適氏 作)이 出來하야 그의 기초가 대략 奠定된 후 「人的 文學」이 잇서 그의 건설의 意思를 充分圓滿히 발표」하얏다 한다. 그런데 이상 各 論文중의 主意를 暫히 소개하면 現 思想界 及 新文化運動의 내용 如何를 略히 窺知할 수 잇도다.

5
中國 舊文學의 革新에는 무엇보다도 복잡한 漢文字의 難關이 잇서기 때문에 누구나 문학의 혁명을 주장하는 이는 爲先 文字에 대하야 만흔 思考를 費하얏고 그 次에는 言文이 無知不一致한 것이 重大의 문제이엇슴으로 이상에 述한 바와 如히 錢玄洞氏는 직접 漢文字의 폐지를 주장하기도 하얏거니와 그 후에는 가장 원만하다고 할 만한 白話＜29＞ 즉 현대 민중의 일반 通用하는 「官話」로써 그 舊文言을 代하자 함이 당시 各 新人界의 일반 主論이엇다. 그리하야 「死文字는 活文學」을 産出치 못한다 함이 文學革命의 標語가 되다십히 되고 당시의 出刊物 其他 著書 중에 혹 白話를 사용한 者이 잇섯스나 사회의 별 反響이 업고 新靑年雜誌가 다수히 白話文을 사용한 以來로 일부의 贊揚과 일부의 非難이 서로 만히 일어나오다가 胡適氏의 「文學改良蒭議」가 직접 白話文 사용을 주장하야 「新靑年」 誌上에 발표되고 이여 陳獨秀씨의 文學革命論이 또한 白話文을 高調로 하야 登載된 후에는 사회의 反響이 크게 振動되어 白話文學의 가치가 世에 어느 정도까지 표현되엇다. 그러면 中國의 文學革命이라 함은 곳 白話文學의 건설을 의미함이엇나니 此가 胡適氏의 具體論에 이르러 現下의 이마마한 대세력을 이루엇스나 白話文學의 倡導는 실로 그 이전에 잇섯다 할 수 잇나니 古代로 보면 元宋 이후 佛書가 飜譯되는 때에 文言으로는 도저히 해석할 수 업는 句節로 만히 語錄 즉 白話를 사용하얏고 그 후로 水滸誌 石頭記의 유명한 소설이다. 白話로 著作되어 원래로 白話文學이 漢文學의 일부에서도 한 위치를 점령하야 보던 바 더욱 근래로는 陳獨秀氏가 北大文科長으로 잇슬 때에 그 주장을 힘써 선전하얏고 錢玄洞 啓孟 諸氏도 만이 此에 主力하야 極力 선전하는 때에 胡氏는 米州의 學窓으로부터 그의 考案을 가저오다가 歸國하는 때에 곳 발표케 됨에 참으로 성숙되랴는 곡물의 추수와 일반인 셈이엇다. 今에 그 「文學改良篘議」로부터 그 骨子되는 바를 소개하면
「나는 생각컨대 현재에 문학의 개량을 言하려하면 반듯이 下列의 八事로부터 入手하여야 할지니 곳 그는
1. 須言之有物
2. 不模倣古人
3. 須講求文法
4. 不作無病之呻吟
5. 務去爛調套語
6. 不用典
7. 不講對仗(즉 對마추는 것)
8. 不避俗字俗語
이상 八大의 주장이 곳 一邊에 舊文學의 병폐를 통박하고 白話文學의 건설을 의미함이엇섯는데 차제로 그의 意思를 해석하면 1. 須言之有物이라 함은 中國 舊來 文學의 大病은 오즉 無物을 말하는 것 즉 사실이 업는데 虛文을 숭상하야 문학이라 함은 인간의 사실을 기록하는 工具가 되지 못하고 돌이어 문학은 곳 文字 그것만 항상 重記複書함에 불과하얏던 것이요. 2. 不模倣古人이라 함은 舊來의＜30＞문학은 幾千年 동한을 오즉 古人의 粗率한 저작에만 憑據하야 비록 어떠한 名篇과 巨作이라도 字字句句가 古人의 除睡가 아니면 반듯이 世에 需用되지 못할 뿐만이 아니라 돌이어 공격과 비난을 밧게 되엇나니 此는 中國文學의 발전되지 못한 최대의 원인인 것. 3. 須講求文法하면 中國文學을 純然히 文理와 文法이 업다 함과 如하나 사실 上完整한 문법도 아즉 업슬 뿐이 아니라 도저히 어떠한 것으로써 완전한 문법이 된다 할 수 업슴으로 古來의 문학을 보면 純然히 그 意思를 解得치 못할 것도 다수이거니와 전혀 문법에 不符되는 것도 잇슨즉 반듯이 문법에 의거치 아니할 수 업는 것. 4. 不作無病之呻吟이라 함은 一見에 별로히 주요치 안흔 것 가트나 확실히 國民的 思想과 國民的 文學에 큰 病毒이 되어잇섯나니 所謂 古來의 文人이나 문학을 보면 그 文人은 愁病과 苦惱를 아니밧는 때가 업는 것 가트며 딸하 그 문장은 그 愁病과 苦惱를 장식하고 寫出한 一種 感想錄에 불과한 것 가튼데 此는 공연히 無病을 신음함이엇던 것. 5. 務去爛調套語라 함은 더욱 中國文學의 병폐를 痛詆하는 바가 될지니 自我의 의사는 別外에 두고 오즉 古人의 糟粕인 일종 기경한 爛調와 套語를 가저다가 작문의 일편을 형성함이 더욱 詞調에는 그 태반이엊�다. 6. 不用典이라 함은 文字를 記述함에는 반듯이 古典 혹은 古人의 作物에서 術語 혹은 文字를 抽來하지 마자 함이니 이는 평민의 문학을 건설하랴 하는 때에 一種 至難至奧의 古典만 의거한다 하면 도저히 문화의 보급을 圖할 수 업는 것. 7. 不講對仗이라 함은 科擧가 施行된 以來로 八股文 혹 賦詞가 盛行함에 딸하 對마추기를 조하함은 일반 문인의 通弊일 뿐이 아니라 구태여 自我의 의사는 무시하고서라도 문득 對마추기에만 급급하얏던 것. 8. 不避俗字俗語라 함은 新文學建設에 가장 중요한 배니 소위 古文學이라하는 것은 純然히 통속의 語言 及 文字는 野卑라 하야 사용치 아니하고 日로 深僻한 古文字 引用으로써 만일을 삼기때문에 문학의 大沈衰를 致케 하얏나니 이상의 주장이 참으로 中國 舊文學 改良에 대한 절실의 책안이라 하겟스며 이의 해석은 원문에 의하야 간단히 나의 意思대로 썻스나 그 原意에 별로히 誤錯될 것이 업슬줄 思한다. 그런데 이상 胡氏의 주장이 中國文學 及 思潮에만 병폐가 되어잇슬 뿐만이 아니라 所謂 漢文學의 純然한 정복을 바든 우리 朝鮮文學의 改良에도 동일한 策略이 안될는지? 그리고 從하야 陳獨秀氏의 文學革命論은 新文學建設에 대하야 策論的으로 建議함보다 완전히 선전의 문자인데 먼저 혁명의 위대를 贊言한 후에 문학의 혁명은 곳 新中國建設의 第一步라 하고 그 다음에 胡氏의 주장과 大差가 업는데 從來로 虛文을＜31＞ 만히 숭상하야 왓슴을 大히 공격하고 지금으로부터는 阿諛的, 虛僞的, 舖張的, 貴族的, 문학은 전부 폐지치 아니하면 안되겟다 하얏다. 그의 文學革命論으로부터 一節을 소개하면 文學革命之氣運. 醯釀已非一日. 其首擧義旗之急先鋒. 則爲吾友胡適. 余甘胃全國學 究之敵. 高張「文學革命軍」 大旗. 以爲吾友之聲援. 旗上大書特書. 吾革命軍三大主義. 曰推倒彫琢的阿諛的貴族的文學. 建設平易的 抒情的國民文學. 推倒陳腐的舖張的古典文學. 建設新鮮的立誠的寫眞文學. 推倒迂晦的難澁的 山林文學. 建設明瞭的通俗的社會文學」이라하고 該論 最末에 「有不顧迂儒之毁譽. 明目張膽. 以與十八妖魔宣戰者乎. 子願以拖四十二生的大砲. 爲之前驅.」라 하얏다. 이로써 보면 그 장쾌한 氣魂은 참으로 新文學革設의 初頭賀砲라 하지 아니할 수 업스며 其 外에 周作人氏의 「人的 文學」도 참으로 가치잇는 著作인데 그의 主論에는 과거의 문학은 一言으로 蔽하면 다 非人的 文學인즉 今에 一切를 다 폐지하고 곳 人的 文學을 건설하자 함이엇고 其 後 從하야 또 발표된 胡適氏의 建設的 文學革命論이 더욱 구체적으로 中國의 新文學建設의 方策을 赤裸裸하게 表明하얏다. 如斯히 新文學建設의 운동이 急轉直下的으로 진보되는 때에 그 문학계의 牛耳를 執한 이는 如何턴지 胡適氏로써 推치 아니할 수 업다. 陳獨秀氏도 此에 대한 不朽의 功勞를 가진이나 원래에 胡氏와 如한 풍부한 지식을 가지지 못하얏스며 又는 혁명가의 본색을 가젓슴으로 어떠한 주장이던지 급격하고 쾌활은 할지언정 주도한 견식으로써 策案的으로 논의함에는 자연 胡氏에게 양보치 아니할 수 업슬 뿐만 아니라 胡氏는 또한 純然히 문학의 혁명을 주안으로하고 노력하며 陳氏는 원래의 社會主義者로 現中國社會主義者 間의 首領이라 할만하다. 그럼으로 문학에 대하야는 胡氏가 물론 그 중추가 된다 할 수 잇스며 기타 北大學派 중에 有名無名의 大學者 大文人이 실로 그 人이 乏치 안는지라. 압흐로의 무한한 희망은 확실히 中國文化의 新生을 可히 기대할 수 잇스며 各 新人들의 사상과 전반 사회의 공통한 思潮로써 보드래도 이미 新에 이해되어 반듯이 一切 舊의 파괴와 一切 新의 건설 즉 西洋文化의 수입이 아니면 안되겟다는 것이다. 그리하야 米國式의 「데모크래시」思想派, 社會主義派 又는 無政府主義派, 기타 色別의 思想派가 無數한 중 「데모크래시」 「쏘이알니슴」 이상 兩派가＜32＞ 최다수인데 北大學派는 대개 「데모크래시」的이라 할 수 잇스며 陳獨秀氏 一派는 社會主義派로서 純然히 勞農派이다. 現 中國의 문화운동의 내면은 略 以上과 如한데 本論 初頭에 叙論的으로 中國人의 특유한 그 不可思議의 國民的 共通의 性質 及 생각을 약술한 바와 如히 무엇무엇하야도 一切 外來의 것은 결코 그대로는 그 국민의 腦底에 칩입도 못할 뿐만이 아니라 도저히 실행도 못된다. 그럼으로 爲先 胡適氏의 내면적 주장을 보면 곳 中國原有文學의 復活이지 결코 西洋文化의 本體化가 아니며 該氏의 著 「中國哲學史大綱」導言에 보면 首先 中國哲學의 곳 復活을 宣言하다 십히 하고 現今 東西洋 文化의 日日의 접근은 확실히 中國哲學의 이후 大成을 의미함이라 하얏섯다. 그 外에 社會主義니 無政府主義니 하야도 곳 中國式的 그것으로 만들랴 함에 힘쓸 뿐이나니 爲先 北京에도 세계가 蛇蝎視하는 無政府主義者 同盟이 들네여 잇고 그의 宣傳文字가 날로 配布되어 잇스되 오히려 專制의 색채가 만흔 現 北京政府라도 족음도 阻止하지 안는다. 그러나 무슨 위험도 안생길 뿐더러 民族的 一種 特有의 그 무슨 힘은 眼前에 무엇무엇 할지라도 自我의 구든 의식에서 輕輕히 妄動하지 아니하고 오즉 自家의 것으로 점차 融合된 후에야 비롯오 움직이려 한다. 그런데 現 中國의 各 思想界나 일반 사회의 공기를 간단히 말하야 보면 「舊中國의 文化로부터 復興된 新의 文化를 建設하랴 함이」 惟一의 趨向갓다. 그리하야 新東洋文化의 수립에 이르려 한다.(完)＜33＞
