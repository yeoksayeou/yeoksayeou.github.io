天玄地黃 (Heaven is Black, Earth is Yellow)

The Chosŏn Association (朝鮮協會), headed by the Japanese Itabashi Kikumatsu (板橋菊松), published the first issue of its organizational magazine on October 15th last. In it, they excerpted the words and writings of several individuals, including Pak Yŏnghyo (朴泳孝) (an advisor to the association) and Yu Sŏngjun (兪星濬), claiming they represented the views of the Korean side regarding Japan's economy in Korea. However, their words truly offer a kind of spectacle. Pak stated that while Japanese-Korean harmony (內鮮融和) is indeed a splendid thing that everyone earnestly hopes to realize, the discriminatory treatment of Koreans by Japanese, stemming from their lack of sincerity, frequently prevents its realization. He then gave a practical example: "At the time of the Japan-Korea annexation, I was one of those honored with a noble title (榮爵) for being a wise man (賢良) who supported (翼贊) the new government. However, although I am a Marquis (侯爵), I was treated coldly, worse than a Japanese official of *sonin* rank (奏任官), simply because I was a *Korean* Marquis—an experience I cannot forget even now. It happened during the state funeral (御大喪) of Emperor Meiji (明治天皇). I went to Tokyo (東京) to attend (參列) the funeral ceremony, and when I saw the seating arrangement (席次) published in the official gazette, my seat was placed after the Japanese nobility (華族) and below the *sonin* officials. Feeling extremely displeased (不快), I immediately confronted a secretary (書記官) of the Imperial Household Ministry (宮內省), saying that discriminating between Japanese and Korean nobility (貴族) in this way goes against the true spirit (本意) of Japanese-Korean harmony. If there was no sincerity to correct this, I would not wear court dress (大禮服) or anything, but stand by the roadside (路傍) like the common people in ordinary formal wear (通商 禮服). Tell this intention to the Minister (大臣). Only then was I able to get the seating order changed (변경) and share a seat with the Japanese Marquises. This is a prime example (好適例) of Japanese officials (役人) discriminating against Koreans." Yu stated, "After going to the house of a certain high-ranking Japanese official (日本人 高官) residing in Korea and hearing the child (小兒) relaying the message at the entrance hall (현관) say, 'A *Yobo* (ヨボ) has come, has come,' I never visited them again." Do the words of Messrs. Pak and Yu mean that if Korean gentlemen (紳士) were simply treated the same as Japanese ones, the problem would be solved (至矣益)? Of course, we infer (抽像) that this was the work (作事) of the Chosŏn Association reporters themselves, who selectively excerpted just one remark from various conversations with the two gentlemen and noisily propagated (喧傳) it as the representative opinion of Koreans. However, aren't their words exceedingly petty (苟苟)? And what need is there for them to readily (動輒) announce (發表) their personal opinions to various Japanese individuals? Couldn't such requests (請) be refused from time to time? Not only these two gentlemen, but all prominent figures (名士) and gentlemen (紳士) of the class who publicly or privately present themselves as representatives of the Korean masses (多衆) particularly need to practice self-reflection (自省) and self-restraint (自愼).<116>

**Summary**

This article reports on comments made by prominent Korean figures, Pak Yŏnghyo and Yu Sŏngjun, published by the Japanese-led Chosŏn Association. Pak recounts being seated below lower-ranking Japanese officials at Emperor Meiji's funeral despite being a Marquis, solely because he was Korean. Yu shares an experience of being referred to by the derogatory term "Yobo" at a Japanese official's house. The article criticizes these figures, suggesting their complaints focus narrowly on personal slights against the elite rather than broader issues. It also implicitly critiques the Chosŏn Association for selectively publicizing these remarks to perhaps portray Korean grievances as superficial. Historically, this piece is interesting as it reveals the personal experiences of discrimination faced even by high-status Korean collaborators under Japanese rule. It also shows a contemporary Korean critique (from the magazine *Kaebyŏk*) of both the collaborators' perceived limitations and the Japanese propaganda efforts surrounding the idea of "harmony" (Naiseon Yūwa).
