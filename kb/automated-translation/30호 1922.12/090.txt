기사제목	內外로 觀한 彼의 二大勢力-恩平에서 東拓農場을 보고 (Observing Their Two Great Powers, Internally and Externally - Seeing the Oriental Development Company Farm in Ŭnp'yŏng)
필자	朴達成
기사형태	세태비평 (Social Commentary)

內外로 觀한 彼의 二大勢力-恩平에서 東拓農場을 보고
						朴達成
In the previous issue of this magazine, while discussing the path for Koreans to live, the reporter (I) spoke of the determination needed for Koreans to martyr themselves for Korean land as the first strategy <63>, and further stated that reviving agriculture is the best policy above all else for the Korean path to life. As a countermeasure to this, I glared endlessly at the Oriental Development Company (Tōyō Takushoku Kaisha, hereafter ODC) and Japanese immigrants, ultimately leaving the matter to the self-determination of the Korean people. However, thinking about it once more, rather than sitting outside the situation and clamoring ten or a hundred times with pen and tongue, it seemed better to go out even one step into the actual place and see the true state of affairs firsthand. Thus, I visited the Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn area in Koyang-gun, known as the ODC's dominant territory near Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul).

It was noon on November 18th. I happened to be accompanied by Comrade Hyŏn P'a. We took the tram without incident as far as Ŭiju-t'ong, and from there, we walked. My head became muddled, and my mood soured. Am I going to see people living well, or living poorly? Will anyone welcome me, or not? In any case, there would surely be many heartbreaking sights. With these thoughts, I passed Ŭiju-t'ong and reached the front of Independence Gate. From here too, there was nothing but heartbreak. Ah, Independence Gate! The name of the gate itself should be welcome, but even that is heartbreaking! Ah, that slaughterhouse! That too is heartbreaking! Ah, those prisoners! That too is heartbreaking! Is this the so-called Mohwagwan Hill? Mohwa? Mohwa (Admiring China)? The very word is nauseating. This whole place is nothing but heartbreak. Bah, let's just name it Heartbreak Village (傷心村).

With such thoughts, feeling heartbroken again and again, I crossed the hill, passed Hongjewŏn, and went over Nokpŏni Pass. I asked a passerby where the Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn office was, and they said it was a little further on, a large tile-roofed house on the left side of the road at the Ŭngam-ni tavern.

Going a bit further, it was indeed as described. The sign "Koyang-gun Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn Office" caught my eye. Entering the front hall, I cleared my throat loudly and stated my purpose. A lean young man gestured, indicating which door to enter. As I entered the room, I presented my business card to a young clerk seated at a central chair. He looked us over intently with a rather arrogant attitude, then took out a business card from his pouch that read "Koyang-gun Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn Clerk, Hyŏn Su-yŏng." He then asked why we had come. When I roughly explained our purpose, he whispered something to a pockmarked clerk in his late 30s or early 40s who was sitting separately and writing something, passing my card to him.

The pockmarked clerk barely glanced at my card, gave our party a sideways glance, and then, without a word, just flipped noisily through the pages of a ledger. There was no word of greeting, no offer to sit down; he acted completely cold and standoffish. Our party was extremely <64> angered by this. Even during office hours, how could they be so cold to visitors who had come all this way? We had guessed that the so-called township clerks, who bow and scrape to officials but act stiffly towards villagers, were inherently like this, but their lack of common sense and social grace evoked forgiveness more than rebuke. I also thought, if I had come wearing Western clothes, perhaps sporting glasses, and maybe twirling a cane, those fellows would surely have bowed and scraped as usual. But perhaps this is an unfair criticism. It might be that we were rude for intruding on their work hours.

We stood awkwardly for quite a while, waiting for the clerks' command to sit. After some time, the pockmarked clerk finally said, "Sit there," pointing to a small round stool with his finger. Gratefully, we perched on the edge of the stool and began asking questions. When asked who the township head (Myŏnjang) was, he said it was Mr. Chang Sŏk-chun, but he was out. When asked his (the pockmarked clerk's) name, he said it was Kim T'ae-yŏng, appearing quite displeased. He answered reluctantly, as if forced. When asked how much land the Oriental Development Company occupied in Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn, he ordered the youngest clerk at the other side, using informal language, to bring the land survey register or perhaps the land ledger.

Ah, do not be surprised. The ODC's land holdings in Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn primarily consist of: Dry fields (田), 141 chŏngbo (町) 318 p'yŏng (坪); Paddy fields (畓), 333 chŏngbo 635 p'yŏng; Building sites (垈), 0 chŏngbo 394 p'yŏng. In total, over 475 chŏngbo. In terms of land value (as of April 1, 1922, Taishō 11), Dry fields: 13,847.4 wŏn; Paddy fields: 74,795.99 wŏn; Building sites: 63.65 wŏn. The total value is 88,706 wŏn and 68 chŏn.

For just one township, Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn, they hold over 470 chŏngbo with a land value between 80,000 and 90,000 wŏn. What must the state of that township be? Looking again at the total land area of the township: Dry fields, 1,586 chŏngbo 546 p'yŏng; Paddy fields, 894 chŏngbo 915 p'yŏng; Building sites, 133 chŏngbo 312 p'yŏng. The total is over 2,640 chŏngbo. So, how does this compare to the ODC? Hasn't the ODC occupied more than one-fifth (1/5) of the total area? It is said that the ODC took only the best fertile land, the very core, while the remaining marginal land in mountainous areas belongs to Koreans. If we exclude government land, public land, or land owned by other foreigners besides the ODC, it seems that Koreans actually own less than half. Looking again at the total land value for the township: Dry fields, 72,935.70 wŏn; Paddy fields, 183,161.27 wŏn; Building sites, 20,747.10 wŏn. Total: 276,844 wŏn and 7 chŏn. <65>

Comparing this to the ODC's land value, the ODC clearly occupies one-third (1/3). While it holds one-fifth of the land area, its land value corresponds to one-third, showing how valuable the ODC's land is. Meanwhile, regarding the residents of Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn, there are 1,374 Korean households and 28 Japanese households. However, it is said that in terms of livelihood and influence, the extremely small number of Japanese people can easily dominate and look down upon the vast majority of Koreans.

Our brethren, who have handed over their own land and now eke out a precarious existence under them, crying out for a way to survive – rather than feeling pity, I could not suppress a feeling of abhorrence.

Having obtained this general information from Clerk Kim, I set off to visit the farming village called Yŏkch'on-ni, said to be where the most ODC immigrant farmers live.

Leaving the township office gate, it was past four in the afternoon. Smoke from evening cooking rose gently from the farmhouses. Husbands who had gone into the city (Kyŏngsŏng) to sell firewood hurried their cows, returning quickly. However, Yŏkch'on-ni was said to be five ri (Korean ri) away from the township office. Though the road seemed long as dusk approached, I decided not to miss the opportunity and headed towards Yŏkch'on-ni. Turning around a small mountain corner, I reached Taejo-ri, the village next to Yŏkch'on-ni, and saw one or two Japanese farmhouses. Korean farmhouses looked desolate, like bankrupt homes, but the Japanese farmhouses seemed distinctly prosperous, with two or three mountain-like stacks of harvested grain in their yards. My disposition soured, and anger flared up. At a tavern in Taejo-ri, five or six village elders were drinking. From outside the shop door, I asked an old man where Yŏkch'on-ni was, and he said it was right next door. Then I asked if he knew Mr. Kim Hyŏn-su, who used to be an ODC farm supervisor (Nonggam) (I knew Mr. Kim through an introduction from the township office). He introduced a middle-aged man of about 50 standing next to him, saying, "This is Mr. Kim Hyŏn-su. Where are you from? What brings you here? Why are you looking for Mr. Kim?" So, I greeted them and explained my purpose. They seemed half-believing, half-doubting, neither cold nor welcoming, just circling around and observing us. However, it was fortunate timing. I met the village head (Kujang) and also two or three farmers. Plying them with drinks and asking persistent questions, they spoke frankly about the general situation. Mr. Kim suggested we go to his house's sarang (outer reception room) to talk. Expressing my thanks, we crossed a small hill, passed through Yŏkch'on-ni village, and arrived at Mr. Kim's <66> house. (The old name for Yŏkch'on-ni is Yŏngsŏ-ri. The meaning is that when King Injo was contending for political power with his elder brother Prince Kwanghae, he plotted all his plans in Yŏkch'on-ni. Then, welcoming the troops of Yi Sŏ, the Governor (Pusa) of Changdan at the time, he returned to Kyŏngsŏng to restore the government. It was named Yŏngsŏ-ri, meaning "Welcoming Sŏ," and there is still a commemorative pavilion on the small western hill of the village today. [Omitted from original text]) The number of households in Yŏkch'on-ni is over 70, with 8 Japanese farming households. The land in Yŏkch'on-ni is fertile, originally mostly consisting of post-station land (驛屯土), but it was absorbed by the ODC eight or nine years ago. The living conditions of the Korean farmers are such that they all rely on the ODC immigrants as landlords, receiving only small leftovers. Most are laborers or tenant farmers for the immigrants. This means the immigrants are tenants of the ODC, and the Korean farmers are tenants of the immigrants, paying double tenancy rent, essentially double taxes. Even this requires having credit with them and being very resourceful to barely obtain a few majigi (a unit of land). For one majigi of 150 p'yŏng, the rent (toji) is 1 sŏk 3 tu of grain. Only in a good harvest year does the tenant farmer get 3/10ths; in a bad year, everything goes to the ODC. This year too, everything went to the ODC, and the Korean farmers labored in vain with no gain. They lamented how they would survive, sounding like they were pleading or appealing; their situation was truly pitiful.

They said they had no idea what kind of contract existed between the Japanese immigrants and the ODC, or how profits and losses were calculated. The immigrants arrive and immediately say, "We have a 25-year contract with the ODC and have obtained land plot number X, so clear out," and seize the tenancy rights. Then, the Koreans, even if they are in the middle of plowing, have no choice but to give it up without resistance. Then, by begging and pleading with those immigrants, only those destined by fate (lit., 'born with the fate to pass the state examination') manage to get a small corner plot to farm. It is said that almost all of the 70-odd farming households are in this situation. Ah, what a miserable plight!

In Yŏkch'on-ni alone, the ODC land amounts to over 20 sŏk-jigi (a measure of land based on potential yield), entirely under the control of the immigrants. Furthermore, the land owned by the Japanese farm supervisor for the ODC, Kuchi'i Unpachi (久池井運八), is said to occupy almost the entire village. [Omitted from original text] On the way back, I visited the Kuchi'i house, and indeed, it had the feel of the village king's residence. Behind it was an orchard, in front a vegetable garden, and further in front, the farm fields; the signs of wealth were evident. Kuchi'i was an old man nearing 70, wearing work clothes and weeding a ditch in the orchard with a hoe. Apologizing for the intrusion, I stated my purpose and asked about the flavor of farm life. Shaking his white beard, he replied with a very composed expression, "Naichi yori ii zo" (内地ヨリイイ子 - It's better than the mainland [Japan]), implicitly boasting about their success. It was suffocating. When asked when he came, he said 8 years ago. When asked how many family members he had, he said 7. His children commute to school, <67> but since there are no public or private schools in Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn, they attend a school in Sindo-myŏn. When asked about relations with Koreans, he said they were of course good, while lamenting his own ignorance of the Korean language. He spoke as if he could assimilate everyone if only he knew Korean. [Omitted from original text]

The sun had set, and night was falling; the return journey was urgent. There was no time for further conversation (酬酌). Clenching my fists, I felt I had to run to get home before midnight. Thus, with a heart pounding as if struck by something, I left Yŏkch'on-ni. Let me add one more thing as I finish speaking.

Seeing Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn and Yŏkch'on-ni village, one can well understand the power of the ODC in Korea and the suffering Koreans endure because of it. Although uncertain, is there any township among the 12 townships of Koyang that is not in the same situation as Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn? If one were to examine every province, every county, every township, one would likely find the situation mostly similar.

Brethren, what then should we do? Should we just stare blankly and wither away? Can we not devise some clever plan? Let us put our minds together, combine our strength, and seek the best path.

In any case, let us not disperse. Whether we become their servants, get beaten, or endure insults, let us bear it grimly and look to the future. Even if we starve to death, let it be here; even if we freeze to death, let it be here. Let us just firmly believe this place is ours and endure steadfastly. Once we disperse, it will never be ours again. And although I have cursed the ODC, upon reflection, it seems foolish. In this world, there is no powerful entity that restrains its own power and hesitates for the sake of others. If one has strength, one uses it, and even conjures strength one doesn't have, to advance and take, to conquer and seize. If we were in their shoes, what would we, as the ODC, do?

No matter how much we attack or plead with the ODC not to act this way, is it likely that their mindset will change to, "Alright, alright. We'll pack everything up and leave. You live well among yourselves"? – Those ruthless people! – How much have we cursed, provoked, <68> and pleaded with them over these past few years? Yet, they pay no heed. They become even more malicious. Their land holdings multiply year by year, and the number of immigrants does not decrease year by year. Looking at their annual immigrant numbers:
In Taishō 6 (1917): 689 households; Taishō 7 (1918): 691 households; Taishō 8 (1919): 650 households; Taishō 9 (1920): 679 households; Taishō 10 (1921): 676 households. Every year, 600 to 700 immigrant households move into Korea. At a rate of 700 households per year, in 10 years that's 7,000 households, and in 100 years, it will reach 70,000 households. A hundred years may seem distant, but while we hesitate, it will become difficult to find any trace of Koreans in the Korean interior. Ah, how frightening and staggering!

Even though they are immigrants, they live quite well. Every year, they earn profits beyond their living expenses, planning for 100 years later, or sending remittances back to mainland Japan. For example, a survey conducted somewhere in Taishō 10 (1921) on typical farm households found that after deducting all annual expenses, the net surplus (實殘金) for a top-tier farmer was 764 wŏn, for a mid-tier farmer 511 wŏn, and even for a low-tier farmer 123 wŏn. Look! What is the reality of their livelihood and power? Koreans, whether low-tier or mid-tier farmers, cannot avoid annual debts exceeding 100 wŏn, while even their low-tier farmers save over 100 wŏn, it is said.

There is no other way. Much talk is unnecessary. The more one speaks of sorrow, the more tears flow. Without further words, there is still enough room on this land for our farmers to survive. Whether facing hardship or the brink of death, let us clench our fists tightly and become loyal people who martyr themselves for the land (殉土地的 忠民) until the very end. Let us create the most solemn reality that gladdens my heart and startles the eyes of others. And let us be diligent and frugal. Let us practice thrift. Diligence and thrift can preserve life. Let us not even dream of leaving for Kando (Jiandao) or the Northern provinces. The place one goes is a place of death; there is nowhere but one's homeland. If the path for Koreans to live does not exist, then so be it; but if it exists, where else could it be but our native soil (故土)? This is my principle, a thousand times, ten thousand times over. Brethren, do you share this feeling?

Seeing the Western Learning Academy (Yŏnhŭi)
It would have been better not to bring up the issue of foreign powers' influence in Korea. If only I had shut my eyes tight and feigned ignorance, ignorance itself might have been a comfort. But now that I know, my heart just keeps aching. But what can be done? Having learned of it, I will disclose one more thing. <69>

On the one hand, the policy of land annexation (土地兼倂策) is as described above. On the other hand, the policy of spiritual assimilation (精神化策) lies frighteningly hidden – no, it is manifest. The various institutions established by those Westerners (彼- 西人) in Korea – ah, how astonishing!

Religion knows no borders, so no matter how powerful Christianity becomes in Korea, it is utterly impossible to criticize Christianity itself. Education also need not distinguish borders. Whether Westerners teach Easterners or Easterners teach Westerners, education itself should be appreciated, if anything; it cannot be rejected.

However, since I was already speaking of the power of foreigners, I am appending my account of visiting Yŏnhŭi Professional School (延禧專門學校) for reference.

It was noon on November 21st. At Namdaemun Station, I boarded a local train bound for P'yŏngyang. In the blink of an eye, we passed Ahyŏn Pass and Sinch'on Pass, arriving right at Sinch'on Station. I got off. Heading towards Yŏnhŭi School, I reached the front of the pine forest near Yŏngbin Nŭng (Royal Tomb of Royal Noble Consort Yŏng). I had these thoughts:

Westerners, generally speaking, seem to have great future prospects in any field. And their workmanship appears solid and substantial. Wherever they go, they seem to preemptively occupy secluded, high, flat places with good scenery. The power of money is part of it, but anyway, they are people with much foresight (遠慮)... This place too was a desolate mountain village, but in no time, it has become a splendid, ideal Western village. Those Western-style buildings rising prominently here and there among the pine trees on the mountainside and below – oh, how enviable!

Anyone seeing this place would call it an ideal Western village. Whether Yŏnhŭi Professional School exists or not is not easily known unless one is already aware. From outside the village entrance, many school buildings are visible, but except during commuting hours, students are not seen, so a first-time visitor might not even sense the presence of a school. They would just say, "A Western village!"

Entering the village, on both sides among the pine trees, woodcutters gathering fallen leaves were chattering away. In the front garden of the Yŏngbin Nŭng shrine, two or three young children seemed to be playing shrine ritual, cutting radishes. Students in square caps appeared one by one outside the village entrance. Gradually walking, I arrived in front of the school building gate. Whether it was dismissal time or lunchtime, students were coming and going in front of and behind the school. In the two large school buildings to the northeast, currently under construction, <70> Chinese and Korean carpenters and plasterers were working noisily. Outside the main entrance of the western school building, two or three people, perhaps teachers or students, stood reading books. Overwhelmed by the imposing appearance of the school building and intimidated (拙縮) by the presence of the square-capped students, I approached the main gate with clumsy movements, sweating profusely. I asked someone for the office, and they countered by asking whom I was looking for. When I mentioned Professor K, he guided me to the faculty room (事勝室).

The intended Professor K had already left. I then asked for Professor N, but he too had already gone home. A clumsy fellow's face knows no shame, it seems. Sweat poured down. I hadn't brought a handkerchief, so dealing with the sweat was difficult. Teachers in Western suits or Korean clothes watched me from various desks. I presented my business card to a teacher in a Western suit and stated my purpose. With a rather unfriendly attitude, he said, "If you came to look around, then look around," and introduced Mr. No Chun-t'ak, speaking indistinctly, mentioning he was an administrative staff member or perhaps the acting school supervisor (校監代理). However, Mr. No was also out for lunch and not in the room. I ended up sitting and waiting for quite some time.

The teachers seemed to be grading a student's composition, then the conversation shifted to modern poetry and classical Chinese poetry. Another teacher mentioned that among contemporary Korean women's poetry or novels, Kim Myŏng-sun's work seemed the best, and wouldn't it be nice to know what kind of person she was. An elderly teacher, seemingly a classical Chinese instructor in Korean clothes with graying hair, suggested placing an advertisement, sparking a round of laughter. Just then, someone entered from outside and said, "Please look around," leading this reporter out the door. Outside the office, Mr. No Chun-t'ak, tall and slender, neatly dressed in a Western suit, approached with a very friendly demeanor and a slight smile, offering greetings.

On the lower floor, I saw 7 or 8 small lecture rooms, each accommodating 7-8 people. Going upstairs, I saw the library, assembly hall, cloakroom (衣室), mimeograph room (謄寫室), and theology room (神學室). Then, going down to the basement, I saw the steam engine room (蒸氣機關室). Exiting through the west door, I saw the dormitory.

This dormitory was reportedly completed last autumn. Currently, there are 30 resident students, with another 50 or so residing in the old dormitory over the hill. It's purely Western-style, impressively equipped, and very clean. It has three floors: lower, middle, and upper. On the lower floor, two students reside per room. The middle floor has several connected rooms capable of housing 10 to 20 students, with beds lined up on both sides. Immediately behind the beds, attached to the wall, are narrow rooms called study rooms (工夫室). Clothing storage, bookshelves, desks, and chairs are provided for each student <71> in each space (supplied by the school). Each floor also has toilets and washrooms. It's quite well set up.

Residency is voluntary; any student can apply, but the housemaster decides, assigning rooms starting with upperclassmen. Upon entering the dormitory, students are issued desks, chairs, and other equipment. Bedding is self-provided, and food expenses are also self-borne, amounting to about 11-12 wŏn per month. Meals are communal, with the basement of the new classroom building temporarily used as a dining hall. Several cooks and 4-5 servants (使喚) are employed full-time for morning and evening meals.

I looked at the two large new classroom buildings to the northeast, but they were not yet fully completed, so it was unclear how they would be used. According to Mr. No, the largest building to the north is planned exclusively for the Literature and Commerce departments, while the large eastern building will be dedicated to Mathematics and Science. The existing old building's lower floor will become offices, and the upper floor will be exclusively for Theology. Furthermore, there are plans to construct another large building in front, which will be dedicated to an assembly hall and library. He also mentioned, though it sounded like a dream, that there are budget plans to build an astronomical observatory on the western mountain, and Mr. No smiled, full of hope. Additionally, there is a lotus pond about one chŏng (町) in front of the school building, which they plan to develop into a swimming area. A reservoir has been temporarily created behind the school building for winter skating. It's truly magnificent. Future facilities aside, even currently, it is the foremost institution of higher learning (大學府) in Korea. Moreover, all the prominent houses scattered across the front and back of the mountain are said to be residences for the teachers. Even assistants, clerks (給仕), and servants have designated housing. The brick Western-style houses and Korean-style tile-roofed houses lined up at the foot of the hill behind Sinch'on are residences for teachers and other school-related personnel.

They say money is omnipotent; it seems anything is possible with gold. Despite being amidst mountain village forests, they have created a large park-like setting, with newly built roads crisscrossing and automobiles honking.

This school, it is said, could be considered a split from Sungsil College (崇實大學校) in P'yŏngyang. That is, the Northern Methodist Faction (北監理派) and the Northern Presbyterian Faction (北長老派) jointly managed Sungsil in P'yŏngyang. The issue arose that while a complete university should be established in Korea, P'yŏngyang was not the center of Korea but a provincial city. Thus, discussions began among school officials about relocating to Kyŏngsŏng. After several debates, the relocation issue was formally proposed to the missionary society. The missionary society also agreed it was feasible but couldn't make a final decision freely. They reported the situation and details to the home church <72> in America. The American home church then approved the Kyŏngsŏng relocation and delegated the decision on all matters to certain influential individuals residing in Korea. At that time, Mr. Paek A-dŏk (Ppeik'ŏ - Becker/白雅悳), who was the president (校監) of Sungsil and advocated for the Kyŏngsŏng relocation, decisively resolved to move. Along with several colleagues and about 70 students, they separated from Sungsil and relocated to Kyŏngsŏng. [Conflict between the two factions omitted - original text] Thus, initially lacking a school building, classes began on the lower floor of the Kyŏngsŏng Central YMCA building. Subsequently, they moved back and forth between Paejae School and Hyŏpsŏng School. Around Taishō 5 (1916), through the dedicated efforts of the late Reverend Won Tu-u (Underwood/元杜宇牧師) and the painstaking work of Mr. Paek A-dŏk, the current school site was secured. Consequently, the Northern Methodist and Presbyterian factions exerted their full efforts, and the Southern Methodist (南監理) and Canadian Church (加奈陀敎會) missions also joined forces. Now, it is managed jointly by four church missions under a foundational juridical person (재단법인). The Northern Methodist and Northern Presbyterian churches provide 20,000 wŏn annually, while the Southern Methodist and Canadian Church missions provide about 10,000 wŏn annually. Additionally, substantial amounts are received under the name of donations (寄附金) in response to the school's requests.

The total area of the school grounds is said to be over 250,000 p'yŏng, and the total expenditure to date (至于今) is over 600,000 wŏn. How vast! My mouth fell open, and I could only stick out my tongue in amazement.

Feeling this has become too lengthy, I intend to stop here. Regardless of the hidden motives (내막) of the Westerners, one cannot help but be grateful for their sacrifice of such immense sums of money and effort for the education of Koreans. However, the suspicion that there must be ulterior motives (必有異心) in their hidden intentions ultimately remains unresolved.

There is no such thing as unpaid work in the world. What will they demand from us? With what shall we recompense (報酬) them? This truly seems more significant than the ODC problem. Since we are hungry, we are grateful for being fed, but the requital (報復) is indeed beyond calculation (計-窮).

Of course, their purpose is missionary work, so they might say believing in Christianity is the recompense. But this is not it. Isn't just believing simple enough? It's likely they won't be satisfied unless, behind that belief, our spirit and even our bodies become Westernized.

This is the state of foreign influence in Korea. Materially and spiritually, it permeates every nook and cranny (方方谷谷), meticulously (삿사치). Ah, my brethren, withered inside and out! What path lies ahead for survival (生道)? <73>

**Summary**

The author, Pak Tal-sŏng, recounts his visits to Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn near Seoul to observe the Oriental Development Company's (ODC) impact and to Yŏnhŭi Professional School (later Yonsei University). In Ŭnp'yŏng, he finds the ODC and Japanese settlers owning a disproportionately large share of the most fertile land (over 1/5th by area, 1/3rd by value), while Korean farmers are reduced to impoverished tenants or laborers, often paying double rent. He contrasts the prosperity of Japanese settlers with the misery of Koreans and interviews locals who describe their displacement and exploitation. He then visits the impressive, Western-funded Yŏnhŭi campus, marveling at its facilities but expressing deep unease about the underlying motives of Western missionaries and the potential for spiritual assimilation, viewing it as another form of foreign encroachment alongside the ODC's material exploitation. The article serves as a lament over the loss of land and autonomy, urging Koreans to endure, remain on their ancestral land despite hardships, and practice diligence and frugality as the only path to survival.

**Historical Significance:** This article provides a valuable ground-level perspective on Japanese colonial land acquisition through the ODC and its devastating impact on Korean farmers in the early 1920s. The detailed statistics on land ownership and value in Ŭnp'yŏng-myŏn, the descriptions of tenancy conditions (double tenancy), and the stark comparison between Japanese immigrant prosperity and Korean farmer poverty offer concrete evidence of the colonial economic structure. Furthermore, the author's parallel concern about Western missionary influence (specifically education at Yŏnhŭi) as a form of "spiritual assimilation" reveals contemporary anxieties about foreign cultural power alongside economic and political domination, a perspective historians might find particularly interesting.

기사형태	세태비평

內外로 觀한 彼의 二大勢力-恩平에서 東拓農場을 보고
						朴達成
記者는 本誌 前號에서 朝鮮人의 生道를 論하다가 그-第一策으로 朝鮮人은 朝鮮土地에 殉하자는 결심 如何를＜63＞ 말하얏고 다시 朝鮮人의 生道는 무엇보다 農業復興이 그-上策이라하야 그에 대한 對方으로 東拓會社 又는 日本人 移民에게 눈을 限업시 흘기다가 결국 朝鮮人의 自決에 任한 事-有합니다 마는 이제 또 한번 생각하니 境遇밧게 안저서 筆舌로 열번 백번 떠드는 것보다 차라리 一步라도 實地에 나아가 사실의 진상을 그대로 보는 것만 갓지 못하야 이에 京城 부근에 대한 東拓의  優勢地라는 高陽郡 恩平面 지방을 訪하얏습니다.

11월 18일 正午이엇습니다. 마츰 同志 玄波君과 同伴이 되엇습니다. 義州通까지는 別數업시 電車를 타고 다음부터는 徒步이엇습니다. 나의 머리는 뒤숭숭해지며 기분이 됴치 못해집니다. 잘사는 꼴을 보러가는가 못사는 꼴을 보러가는가 누가 반겨나 할넌가 말는가. 어쨋든 傷心거리가 만흐리라. 이러한 생각을 하면서 義州通을 지나  獨立門압흘 當하얏습니다. 여긔서부터도 傷心거리 뿐이외다. 아- 獨立門! 門名이야 반갑지 안흐랴마는 그것도 傷心이로구나! 아- 저 屠獸場! 그것도 傷心! 아- 저 罪人들! 그것도 傷心! 이것이 所謂 慕華舘재란 말가. 慕華? 慕華? 말부터 口逆이 난다. 도대체 이곳이란 傷心거리 뿐이다. 에라 傷心村이라고 이름지어 주자.
나는 이런 생각으로써 傷心 又 傷心하면서 舘재를 넘어 弘濟院을 지나 碌磻이고개를 넘어섯습니다. 어떤 행인에게 恩平面事務所가 어대냐 무르니 조금 더 가서 鷹岩里 酒幕 路左便 큰 盖瓦집이라 합니다.
조금 더 가서 보니 과연이라. 高陽郡 恩平面事務所라는 牌가 눈에 띄웁니다. 현관을 들어서서 크게 기침하고 來意를 표하니 어떤 갈늠한 청년이 손짓을 하며 어느 문으로 들어오라는 뜻을 표합니다. 문안을 드러서며 중앙 倚子에 안즌 젊은 書記에게 名啣을 通하니 그는 자못 건방진 태도로 우리 일행을 熟視하더니 簇只주머니에서 「高陽郡 恩平面書記 玄秀榮」이라 쓴 名啣 한장을 줍니다. 그리고 어째 왓느냐고 뭇습니다. 그래 온 뜻을 대강 말하니까 그는 저 便짝에 따로 안저 무엇을 기록하는 40內外의 얼금얼금한 書記님께 나의 명함을 전하면서 무어라 수근수근 합듸다.
그는 나의 명함을 보는 듯 마는 듯하더니 우리 일행을 겻눈으로 슬적 한번보고는 아모 말업시 冊張만 벌컥벌컥 뒤지며 人事하잔 말도 안즈란 말도 도무지 업고 아조 冷然히 뚝쟁이 行勢를 합니다. 우리 일행은 여긔서 골이＜64＞ 萬동의나 낫습니다. 아무리 公務時間인들 모처럼 온 손에게 이러케도 냉정할가하고. 官吏에게 굽실굽실하고 村民에게 뻣뻣이 구는 所謂 面書記 나부랑이란 버릇이 본래 그런줄 짐작은 하지만 어쩃든 그들의 沒常識 沒交際的이야말로 叱罵하기보다 容恕가 먼저 나옵듸다. 나는 이런 생각도 하얏습니다. 내가 만약 양복나치나 하고 眼鏡깨나 쓰고 막대기 깨나 내여두루며 왓스면 저 者들이 依例 굽실거리렷다 하고. 그러나 이는 非分之責이겟지요. 남의 公務時間을 犯한 우리가 돌이어 失禮라 합니다.
우리는 한참이나 서서 엄을엄을 하며 그 書記님들의 안즈란 令만 기다렷습니다. 한참만에야 그 얼금뱅이 書記님은 「그리 안즈오」하며 조그마한 동근 의자를 손가락질해 가리칩니다. 그래도 고맙다고 의자 꼭댁이에 엉덩이를 부치고 안저 이것 저것 뭇기를 시작하얏습니다. 面長이 누구냐 하니까 張錫準氏인데 出他하얏다 하매 당신(얼금뱅)姓名은 무어냐 하니까 金泰榮이라 하면서 별로 주짜를 빼입듸다. 하기실흔 대답을 억지로 간신히 합듸다. 恩平面에 대한 東洋拓殖會社所占地가 얼마냐 무르니까 그는 저 便짝 第一 어린 書記를 半말로 命하야 地積調査簿인지 土地臺帳인지를 가저옵듸다.
아- 놀라지 마시요. 恩平面에 대한 東拓所占地가 第一 骨子로 田, 一四一町 三一八. 畓 三三三町 六三五. 垈 0町 三九四. 합하야 475町步餘이외다. 地價로 말하면 (大正 11년 4월 1일 현재) 田 一三八四七, 四. 畓, 七四七九五, 九九. 垈, 六三, 六五. 합하야 88,706원 68전이외다.
恩平一面에 대하야 470여 정보를 가지고 8만 내지 9만원의 地價를 占하얏나니 그面의 꼴악산이가 어찌되겟습니까.  다시  一面의 總地積을 보니 田, 一五八六町 五四六. 畓, 八九四町 九一五. 垈, 一三三町 三一二. 합하야 2,640 町餘步이외다. 그러면 東拓과 비교가 어찌 됩니까. 無慮-東拓에서 5分 1이상을 占하지 안핫습니까. 良田沃土純骨子로만 東拓이 가지고 기타 山間薄土 부스럭지만 朝鮮人의 것이라 합니다. 東拓외에 官有地 又는 公志 又는 外國人의 것을 除하면 실상은 朝鮮人의 것이 半部도 못할듯 합니다. 다시 一面 總地價로써 보면 田, 七二,九三五,七0. 畓, 一八三,一六一,二七. 垈, 二0,七四七,一0 계 276,844원 7전 ＜65＞
이상과 가트니 此를 東拓地價에 비교하면 東拓이 3分 1은 확실히 占하얏습니다. 地積으로도 5分 1에 해당하나 地價로는 3分 1에 當하니 東拓의 地가 얼마나 骨子임을 알 것입니다. 然한데 恩平 一面의 주민으로 말하면 朝鮮人은 1,374戶요. 日本人이 28戶인데 생활상으로나 세력상으로나 극소수의 日本人이 多大數의 朝鮮人을 能히 지배하고 能히 侮蔑한다 합니다.
자기의 토지를 그대로 집어 바치고 그알에서 苟苟然 생활의 末由를 呼泣하는 우리 형제야말로 가련타하기보다 증오의 念을 不禁하얏습니다.
나는 金書記에게 이런 것을 대강 어더 가지고 東拓移民이 第一 말히 산다는 驛村里라는 농촌을 訪하얏습니다.

선생님들은 어떤 학생의 작문을 평하는 듯하더니 말은 演하야 現代詩 漢詩의 이악이도 잇고 또 어떤 선생은 현대 朝鮮女子의 詩나 소설 중에서는 金明淳의 것이 제일인 듯한데 그가 如何한 인물인지 알앗스면 조켓다는 이악이도 잇는데 朝鮮服에 半白이 된 한문선생 가튼 老先生님은 광고를 내여 보라하며 一場 우슴판을 맨듭듸다. 그리자 어떤이가 밧그로 들어오며 구경하시지요 하며 기자를 문밧그로 인도합니다. 사무실 밧글 나서니 盧俊鐸氏가 후리후리한 키에 말숙하게 양복을 거느리고 자못 친절한 태도로 半笑를 含하면서 인사를 청합니다.
下層에서 작으막 작으막 7, 8人식 수용하는 7, 8개 강당을 보고 上層으로 올라가 도서실, 집회실, 衣室, 謄寫室, 神學室 등을 보고 디시 지하층으로 가서 蒸氣機關室을 보고 서편문으로 出하야 기숙사를 보앗습니다.
이 기숙사는 작년 秋에 落成이 되엇다 합니다. 현재 30명의 기숙생이 잇고 기타 50餘 名의 기숙생은 산넘어 舊宿舍에 잇다 합니다. 순 서양식이라 설비도 壯하거니와 퍽 깨끗합니다. 상중하 3층으로 되엇는데 하층에는 一方에 2人식 기숙하게 되고 중층은 數間을 통하야 10人 내지 20人도 寄宿할만하게 맨들엇는데 침대가 좌우로 죽 노히고 침대 바로 뒤에는 벽을 聯하야 狹室이 잇는데 工夫室이라 합니다. 衣裝, 책장, 책상, 의자가 每名＜71＞ 每方에 구비되어 잇습니다(학교로부터 제공). 그리고 층마다 便所, 洗面所가 잇습니다. 그럴 듯하게 되엇습니다.
寄宿은 지원에 의하야 학생이면 누구든지 許하는데 사감이 監定하야 상급생부터 차례로 지정한다 하며 入舍 즉시로 책상, 의자 등 용구를 出給하며 침구는 自擔하며 食資도 自擔인데 매월 11, 2원 가량이라 합니다. 식사는 공동인데 신축교실 지하실을 임시 식당으로 쓰며 숙수 數名 使喚 4, 5명을 두어 每朝每夕을 專任하얏다 합니다.
新築 東北 2大교실을 보앗스나 아즉 완비가 못되어 어떠케 어떠케 쓰이게 될는지 모르겟습니다. 盧氏의 말을 듯건대 북편 최대 校舍는 文科 商科에 전용할 예정이며 東便 大校舍는 數理科 專用으로 由來 舊校舍는 하층은 통하야 사무실이 되고 상층은 神學 專用이 되리라 합니다. 그리고 압흐로 一大 교실을 또 건축할 예정인데 集會室, 圖書室 專用이 되리라 합니다. 그리고 꿈 가튼 말이나마 西便 山上에 천문대를 건설할 예산이라고 하며 氏는 희망에 넘치는 말로 웃습니다. 그리고 校舍前 一町許에 蓮池가 잇는데 그는 水泳地로 作築하리라 합니다. 임시로 교사뒤에 冬期 「스켓」운동으로 저수지를 作하엿다 합니다. 참 굉장합니다. 장래 설비는 고사하고 현재로만도 朝鮮 一의 大學府입니다. 그리고 山 前後 일원에 웃둑웃둑이 놉흔 집이 다- 선생의 주택이라 합니다. 助手, 給仕, 使喚까지도 다- 주택을 정하얏다 합니다. 新村後麓에 병열한 煉瓦洋屋 또 朝鮮式 瓦家가 즉 선생 이하의 학교관계자들의 주택이라 합니다.
黃金萬能이라드니 金이면 다- 되나 보외다. 山村樹林 중임을 불구하고 一大公園格을 作하얏는데 신작로가 縱橫하얏고 자동차가 뿡뿡거립니다.
이 학교는 본래- 平壤 崇實大學校에서 분리되엇다 해도 과언이 아니라 합니다. 즉 北監理派와 北長老派의 합동으로 平壤 崇實을 경영하야 오든 바 문제되기를 朝鮮에 완전한 대학을 두긴 두되 平壤은 朝鮮에 중앙이 아니요 또 지방이라 하야 京城으로 이전하자는 의논이 학교관계자간에 起하야 수차 문제가 되다가 결국 선교사회에 該移轉問題를 提하얏든 바 선교회에서도 可하다는 의논이 有하얏스나 自由處決을 못짓고 사실 顚末을 들어 米國＜72＞ 本敎會에 訴하얏다 합니다. 이에 米國 본교회에서는 京城 이전을 찬성하야 每事善否를 朝鮮 在留 某某 유력자에게 전하얏다 합니다. 이에 당시- 崇大 校監으로서 京城 이전을 主唱하든 白雅悳(뻬커)氏는 단연히 이전을 決하고 자기 同志 幾人과 또는 학생 70여 명과 단속하야 崇大에서 분리하야 京城으로 이전하얏다 합니다. (此間 兩派 분쟁은 略-원문) 이리하야 초에는 교사가 無하매 京城中央基督敎靑年會舘 하층에서 개학이 되고 다음 培材學校 또는 協成學校로 轉去轉來를 하다가 大正 5년경에 故 元杜宇牧師의 盡力과 白雅悳씨의 苦心으로 現 校舍地로 확정이 되엇다 합니다. 그리하야 北監理長老派가 전력을 하고 南監理 加奈陀敎會도 合力을 하야 지금은 4敎會의 합동경영인데 재단법인 하에서 北監, 北長 兩敎會에서 연 2만원, 南監, 加奈陀敎會에서 年 만원 가량을 제공하며 기타- 寄附金名 下에서 학교의 請求에 응하는 금액이 多大하다 합니다.
校地의 총평수는 25만여 평이라 하며 至于今 총경비가 60여 만원이라 합니다. 얼마나 광장합니까. 나는 입을 딱벌이고 혀만 내여 둘넛습니다.

넘우 지리한 듯 하야 그만 두려합니다. 서양인들의 내막 如何는 莫論하고 朝鮮人 교육을 위하야 그처럼 多大의 金錢 又는 노력을 희생함은 감사하다 아니할 수 업습니다. 그러나 그들의 내막에는 必有異心이란 疑訝는 終是 풀리지 안습니다.
세상에는 보수업는 일이 업습니다. 그들은 우리에게 무엇을 요구할는지 우리는 그들에게 무엇으로 報酬를 할는지 이야말로 東拓問題보다도 더- 중대해 보입니다. 배가 곱흐니까 어더 멱기는 감사하나 그 報復이야말로 計-窮합니다.
그들은 毋論- 宣敎의 목적이니까 기독교를 밋는 것으로 보수라 하겟지요. 그러나 이것은 아닙니다. 밋는 것 뿐으로야 여간 조습니까. 다- 밋는 그 裏面에 우리의 정신 우리 육체까지도 西人化가 아니되면 그들이 만족타 아니할 것이외다.
