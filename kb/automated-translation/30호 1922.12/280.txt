Author: 쉑스피아(원작), 玄哲(譯述) (Shakespeare (Original), Hyŏn Ch'ŏl (Translation/Adaptation))

하믈레트
						쉑스피아(원작), 玄哲(譯述)
Act 5
Scene 2
(A Hall in the Castle)
(Hamlet and Horatio enter.)

Ham. Enough of that matter. But this is something else, you remember all the circumstances, don't you?

Hore. Remember, you say?

Ham. No, when there was distress in my heart, making it difficult to sleep no matter what, and I lamented that my situation was no better than a rioter groaning in iron chains, without thinking strangely of the future, something came to mind... Ah, this must be called blind merit... When a deeply considered plan fails, sometimes an act done without any discretion can instead achieve great success. Though the first stroke may be made by man, the finishing touch is done by heaven.

Hore. That is not an illogical statement, sir.

Ham. Quietly rising from my cabin, throwing a sailor's cloak over my shoulders, I groped in the darkness, and luckily, the bundle I sought came into my hand as desired. Returning immediately to my room, thinking of this body, without time for ceremony and etiquette, I boldly opened the official letter they were carrying, and truly<57> Horatio, a shocking royal plot!...
Whether for Denmark or for England, oh! It declared that leaving me alive was an extremely dangerous matter, and upon seeing this letter, without a moment's hesitation, without even allowing time to sharpen the axe (斧), my head should be struck off immediately—a stern command fabricated with various excuses.

Hore. Could that be possible?

Ham. The letter is here. Read it slowly later. But shall I tell you what I did next?

Hore. Please do.

Ham. So, surrounded by such villains, in a moment of absolute crisis... before even the prelude was written in my brain, the action play had already begun. ... I sat down right away and devised a new state letter. The handwriting was perfect. Once, like the politicians of this country, I looked down on good handwriting and even tried to forget the script I had learned, but this time it served as a loyal servant. Now, do you want to know what I wrote?

Hore. I would like to know that.

Ham. An earnest request from the King to the King of England... Essentially, that England is a loyal and undivided vassal state, that the trust between the two countries flourishes like an evergreen tree, that the god of peace always wears a crown of wheat and mediates the goodwill between the nations... listing such burdens like tired horses, and after reviewing the letter, promptly, without a fraction of hesitation, absolutely forbidding confession, it ordered that the two bearers of this letter be executed on the spot.

Hore. And what about the state seal?

Ham. That too was heaven's help. In the hidden pouch (隱囊) I have carried all my life, I had hidden my father King's seal (御印), which was a model of the Danish state seal. Thus, I folded the order I wrote according to form, signed it, affixed the seal, and safely placed it back where the former one was. Who would know it had been swapped? And so, the next day was the sea battle. You already know the details of what happened after that.

Hore. So Guildenstern (킬덴스다안) and Rosencrantz (로오쩬크란쯔) ruined themselves like that.

Ham. Indeed, since they sought such consequences themselves, their destruction was self-inflicted (自得自取). When brave warriors clash fiercely, sparking<58> wildly, it is always dangerous for petty, mediocre men to participate.

Hore. Truly an unspeakable act by the king!

Ham. No, under these circumstances, isn't it only right?... He who murdered our father King, defiled our mother Queen, blocked my path to the throne, and what was left that he tried to take my life using such wicked means... isn't executing such a man commanded by heaven? Isn't letting such a human pest (蝥賊) live to prolong his harm a great sin deserving of hell?

Hore. Won't England soon notify the king of the details (顚末) of this incident?

Ham. No, they will find out soon enough. But until then, it's my affair. A person's life is gone in less time than it takes to say "one." But that aside, Horatio. I regret forgetting myself and behaving rudely towards Laertes (레에야아치이쓰). Comparing him to myself, I want to understand his feelings. Tell him I wish to reconcile. But overcome by his overly passionate grief, unexpected anger flared up.

Hore. Shh! Be quiet. It seems someone is coming.
(Enter Osric (오스리크), a courtier.)

Os. I rejoice that Your Highness has returned safely (還啓).

Ham. Well, thank you for the kind words. (Aside to Horatio) Do you know this water-seller fellow?

Hore. (Aside to Hamlet) No, I do not know him.

Ham. (Aside to Horatio) Then you are truly fortunate. To know him is a vice. He possesses good land, and much of it. Even if a beast were king of beasts, its trough could be laid out on the king's table. Though he is a foolish bird (등신새), as I said, he possesses much mud (land).

Os. Your Highness, if you are at leisure now, I wish to convey His Majesty the King's command (吩付).

Ham. I shall respectfully listen. But use your hat properly. It is for wearing on the head.

Os. I am grateful (惶感) for the command, but it is extremely hot.

Ham. No, that cannot be. It is very cold; the north wind is blowing.

Os. Truly, it is indeed cold.

Ham. But it is very sultry, so for someone of my constitution, it must be hot.<59>

Os. Truly, that is also true. It is extremely sultry... I don't know what to say... However, Your Highness, the disposition His Majesty has made is to inform you that he has placed a huge wager on Your Highness. That matter is...

Ham. No, what you just said...
(Hamlet gestures for Osric to put on his hat.)

Os. No, really. Really, it is my preference. However, Your Highness, Sir Laertes (레에야치이스씨), who recently returned to the country, is truly a flawless gentleman, possessing many outstanding strengths. His manner of reception is elegant (嫻雅), his bearing and appearance (動靜風釆) are splendid, and indeed, to evaluate him properly, he might be called a living quick reference table (早見表) of etiquette or a catalog of protocol (儀節). Suppose one wishes to be a gentleman; he possesses all the virtues one could desire.

Ham. Speaking critically, there is no regret. Furthermore, if one were to analyze his strengths and talents one by one, they are too numerous, and memory would face embarrassment. No matter how one tries to follow, his boat's pace is too swift, and one would eventually fall behind. No, truly, I believe him to be a man of great capacity. His natural gifts are limitlessly precious, so if one tries to compare and evaluate him against things, the only thing equal is his own reflection in a mirror. How much more so, then, is trying to imitate him merely chasing his shadow.

Os. Your Highness's critique is thorough and appropriate.

Ham. So, what is the reason? No, why praise such an outstanding gentleman with worthless words (蕪辭)?

Os. Pardon?

Hore. Perhaps he might understand if spoken with a different mouth. It seems understandable.

Ham. What is the reason for speaking of the rumors about him?

Os. Rumors about Laertes, you mean?

Hore. (Aside to Hamlet) His purse is already empty. He has used up all his golden words.

Ham. (To Osric) About the rumors concerning him.

Os. As you have known for some time...

Ham. Mm. I wish I could become like that. Truly, even if I did, I wouldn't call it such an honor. However,<60> as you know from before, regarding Sir Laertes's excellent strengths...

Ham. I don't insist on knowing. It is unpleasant to think of competing with him in skill in my heart (心中). But to know others well is to know oneself.

Os. What I wish to speak of is his weaponry; according to public assessment, his skill in this technique is said to be unparalleled in the world.

Ham. What weapon?

Os. Rapier (細刀) and dagger (短劍).

Ham. Those are two among weapons. So...

Os. Now, His Majesty has wagered six Barbary horses (빠아빠리스말) on him, and against that, what he has put up as security (볼모), as I have heard, are six pairs of French-made rapiers and daggers, along with accessories called ornamental belts (飾帶) and sword hooks (劒鉤). Among these, three hangers (釣懸機) are of exquisite workmanship, perfectly harmonizing with the sword hilts, representing the pinnacle of elegance and refined craftsmanship.

Ham. Hangers (釣懸機), you say?

Hore. (Aside to Hamlet) It seems you'll need a commentary to understand the details.

Os. Hangers (釣懸機) refer to the hooks for hanging the sword.

Ham. Such words might be fitting if one carried a cannon at one's waist, but until now, calling them sword hooks would have been fine. So, against six Barbary horses, six pairs of French swords and their accessories, plus hangers of utmost elegance. This is surely a Denmark (丁抹) versus France wager. Why is this matter referred to as "putting up security" (볼모한다) as you say?

Os. His Majesty the King believes that if Your Highness and he contest twelve bouts, he will not win more than three additional times, so he has wagered nine against twelve. If Your Highness gives consent to try, he says a match before the King (御前 試合) will be held promptly.

Ham. What if we refuse to stand against each other?

Os. What I meant was not standing against each other. I was speaking of a contest of martial arts.

Ham. I might as well walk around this room. If that is agreeable, since it is also my time for recreation, it would be best to bring the dueling swords. If he has no objection<61> and the King's intention does not change. If possible, I might even win. If I bungle it, I will only expose my shame.

Os. Shall I report back (復命) thus?

Ham. Ah. Embellish (潤色) as you please.

Os. For a long time, I offer my humble loyalty to Your Highness.

Ham. Excessive, excessive. ...
(Osric exits.)
It is natural for him to recommend himself. Besides him, there is no one to speak up for the fellow.

Hore. That kite-like (솔개미) fellow flies away well, though he hasn't even shed his downy feathers yet.

Ham. He is the type to bow to the nipple before sucking the milk. Thus, those fellows are altogether like fledgling birds flattering a fickle (淆薄) world, barely catching the tune of the times, conforming only superficially with clever words and pleasing expressions. Like foam (泡沫), with pseudo-knowledge (似而非學問), they merely drift along various public opinions stirred up by fanning. But just try blowing on it once, why, it will disappear like a soap bubble.
(Enter a Gentleman.)

Gent. Your Highness, regarding the matter His Majesty recommended through Osric earlier, that person returned and reported (復命) that Your Highness awaits in the great hall. His Majesty wishes to ask if you have any objection to meeting with Laertes, or if you wish to postpone it.

Ham. My mind does not change. As the King wishes, if circumstances on that side are favorable, this side is ready anytime. Now is fine, and any time is fine. If my situation remains unchanged.

Gent. Both Their Majesties and the entire party are gathered now.

Ham. Excellent.

Gent. Her Majesty the Queen wishes that Your Highness, when you meet, please greet Sir Laertes with a peaceful heart.

Ham. I shall respectfully heed the instruction.
(Gentleman exits.)

Hore. This contest does not seem likely to end well.

Ham. No, I do not think so. Since he went to France, I too have practiced ceaselessly. Only the odds are against me, but I might win... However,<62> somehow this heart feels unbearably troubled. Do you not feel that way? But such things are of no consequence.

Hore. Is that wise?

Ham. It is groundless. Perhaps women might feel apprehensive.

Hore. If you do not feel comfortable, please stop. Have Their Majesties halt the proceedings, and I will inform them of the troublesome circumstances.

Ham. No, no. There is nothing to worry about in omens. Even the catching of a single sparrow requires special help from heaven. If it comes not now, it will come later; if it comes not now, it will come eventually. Whatever it is, determination is everything. If the world one leaves behind has no value, what regret is there in dying early? Just let it be. (Enter King, Queen, Laertes, Lords, Osric, and Attendants with foils.)

King. Come, Hamlet, come and take this hand.
(The King makes Hamlet and Laertes clasp hands).

Ham. Forgive me. I have done you wrong. But if you are a gentleman, please forgive me. Everyone here knows, and you too must have heard the rumors. I have suffered entirely from madness. All my actions that offended your feelings, harmed your honor, and provoked your anger were all due to madness. Did Hamlet do wrong to Laertes? No, he did not. If Hamlet, bereft of his true self, wronged Laertes when he was not himself, that is by no means Hamlet's action. If it comes to that, Hamlet is also one who suffers its harm. That madness is pitiable Hamlet's enemy. Having sworn thus without malice in the presence of all these people, I hope you will, with a generous heart, forgive my past transgression as an arrow shot over the roof that unexpectedly injured a kinsman.

Laer. With those words, the burning emotional desire for revenge up to now has been resolved. However, regarding my honor, until shown a precedent by respected elders' reconciliation ensuring no shame comes to me, I will not yield a single step.<63>
Until that time, I will accept the friendship you speak of just as you have said.

Ham. I do not doubt that either. Then let us open our hearts and compete like brothers. Bring the foils for the match. Come, here.

Laer. Come, give me one too.

Ham. Laertes, what I give you is not a dueling foil but something like silver foil. Compared to my poor skill, your skill will stand out and glitter like a star in the dark night.

Laer. Do not speak such jests, sir.

Ham. No, I swear it is true.

King. Osric, give the foils to the two men. Hamlet, do you know the wager?

Ham. I know it well. Your Majesty has bet on the weaker side, haven't you?

King. I do not concern myself with such things. I know both your skills well. But knowing Laertes is proficient, I have arranged the odds.

Laer. This is too heavy. Show me another.

Ham. This one pleases me. Are the swords of the same length?

Os. Yes, they are identical.
(They prepare to fence.)

King. Place the wine bottles on that table. If Hamlet scores the first, second, or even the third hit against his opponent, let the cannons fire a salute from all the ramparts. The king will raise a cup to toast Hamlet's future success. And into that cup, I will throw a pearl better in quality than those set in the crowns of four generations of Danish kings. Come, give me the cups. And let the bells and drums ring out, conveying to the trumpeters, "The King now drinks to Hamlet," let the trumpeters inform the cannoneers outside, let the cannons fire, heard in heaven, and let heaven relay it to the earth. Come, it has begun. Hey, judges, pay attention.

Ham. Come on.

Laer. Come, begin.
(They fight.)

Ham. One hit.

Laer. What?

Ham. Judgment.

Os. A hit. A very palpable hit. <64>

Laer. Alright. Again.

King. Wait. Pour the wine. Hamlet, this pearl is yours. Come, I drink this toast thus.
(Trumpets sound, and cannons fire within. The King drops the pearl into the cup.)
Give this cup to Hamlet.

Ham. Let's finish this bout first. Still over there. Come on.
(They fight fiercely.)
Another hit.

Laer. A miss. A touch, a touch, I confess't.

King. Hamlet wins.

Queen. He's fat and scant of breath. Here, Hamlet, take my napkin, rub thy brows. I shall drink to your fortune.

Ham. Mother, thank you.

King. Gertrude, do not drink.

Queen. No, I will drink. Forgive me.

King. (Aside) It is the poisoned cup. It is too late now.

Ham. I do not feel like drinking yet, Mother. I'll drink soon.

Queen. Come here. Let me wipe your face.

Laer. Your Majesty, I shall now display my skill.

King. I do not think so.

Laer. (Aside) But somehow, my conscience does not permit it.

Ham. Come on. The third time. You are not really trying, are you? Attack with all your strength. Are you mocking me?

Laer. If you say so, let's have at it.
(They fight.)

Os. Still nothing.

Laer. Ah!
(Laertes wounds Hamlet. In a fierce scuffle, they exchange rapiers. Hamlet wounds Laertes.)

King. Stop them! Separate them. They are both enraged.

Ham. No, no. Once more. Come on.
(At this moment, the Queen, poisoned, falls.)

Os. What has happened to the Queen Mother? Ah!

Hore. Both are wounded. How fares Your Highness?

Ham. How is my Mother Queen?

King. She fainted seeing the blood of you two.

Queen. No, no, the drink, the drink! Oh, Hamlet, it's because of the drink, that drink! I have been poisoned.
(The Queen dies.) <65>

Ham. Ah, treachery! Hey, hey, lock the doors. There is a traitor. Find him quickly.

Laer. He is here. Your Highness Hamlet, now your life too is over. No miraculous medicine in the world can cure you. You will not sustain your life for even half an hour more. The vessel of this wicked plot is the sword you hold in your hand, its tip coated with poison. The evil design has turned back upon myself, look. I too am now unable to rise again. The Mother Queen has also been poisoned. She can no longer speak. The King... the King is indeed the culprit.

Ham. The point sharpened well for stabbing and coated with poison. Right then, poison, do your work!
(He aims at the King, rushes forward, and stabs him in the chest.)

All. Treason! Treason!

King. Oh, help me, everyone. The wound is not yet deep.

Ham. You incestuous, murderous, damned Dane... Drink this poison! Is this the pearl? Follow my Mother Queen in death.
(The King dies.)

Laer. That is just treatment. It's the poison he himself mixed. Your Highness Hamlet, do not resent me either.
(Laertes dies.)

Ham. May heaven forgive your sins too. I follow after. Horatio, this is it now. Poor Mother Queen, so now. All you people, pale and trembling at this tragic spectacle, all you who seem like silent actors or spectators... if this cruel messenger of death would loosen the snare just a little longer... Ah, I have much to say. But I cannot say anything. Horatio, this is it for me. You remain behind and tell my rightful reasons to those who do not know my intent.

Hore. No, I cannot follow that command. Though born a Dane, my spirit is no less than that of the ancient Romans. Fortunately, there is still some poison left here...

Ham. If you are a man, give it here, let it go. This I shall drink... Oh, oh... Horatio, without knowing these detailed reasons, what infamy might remain after my death? If you have true loyalty towards me, distance yourself from happiness for a while, prolong your troubled life in this transient world,<66> and tell my story.
(Sound of army marching far off, cannons fire within.)
That valiant sound.

Os. Young Fortinbras (포오틴풀라스) is returning victorious from Poland. Also, ambassadors are arriving from England, so welcome salutes are being fired.

Ham. Oh, Horatio, death is near. The potent poison has already paralyzed my spirit. I cannot endure even long enough to hear the report from the envoy from England. But I declare by testament that Fortinbras be designated the successor to the throne. Convey my final words to him. And explain the full details (顚末) of how this incident occurred. Oh, the rest is silence.
(Hamlet dies.)

Hore. Now cracks a noble heart. Good night, sweet prince. May flights of angels sing thee to thy heavenly rest! Why does that drum come hither?
(March within. Enter Fortinbras, the English Ambassadors, and others.)

For. Where is this sight?

Hore. What would you see? If you would see the wretched, the astonishing, you need seek no further.

For. This heap of bodies calls for great destruction. O proud death, what feast are you preparing in your eternal kitchen, that you have so bloodily struck down so many princes at one shot?

Amb. This dismal sight! Then the report I bring from England is already too late. Though I came to report that the two, Rosencrantz (로오쩬크란쯔) and Guildenstern (킬덴스다안), have been executed (誅), thus completing the command consistently from beginning to end, the ears that should hear are already deaf. From where shall I receive thanks?

Hore. Even if the King's life remained, you could not receive thanks. The order to kill those two was by no means the King's command. Whatever the case, at this time when such a great tragedy has occurred, with the King from the Polish battlefield, you gentlemen from England, gathered thus at court, by command, let these bodies be raised high upon a platform. And allow me, for a time, to tell the unknowing world the detailed story of how this came to be. Then you too shall surely know. Acts of adultery, cruelty, inhumanity, followed by sudden judgment, unexpected murder, unavoidable stratagems born of desperation, and at the end, this great catastrophe, where one's own plans went awry, bringing retribution upon the planner's head. I can tell the whole story (前後事然) from beginning to end.

For. Let us hear it quickly. Let all the nobles of the kingdom be summoned.<67> As for myself, though this is a time wrapped in sorrow, I embrace (抱迎) my fortune.
Having no small connection with this country, I shall take this opportunity and strive to achieve my aims.

Hore. There is something to be said regarding that. There is a testament from that gentleman's mouth which is likely to draw much consent. But please allow the execution of what I have just spoken of. Since public sentiment is turbulent today, otherwise, further misfortune arising from conspiracy and misunderstanding might perhaps occur.

For. Let Prince Hamlet's remains be carried onto the platform by four captains with full military honors. Had the opportunity been right, this man would also have been a splendid wise ruler. Announce the military music and fire the ceremonial cannons to proclaim his passing (薨去) to all directions. Bear away the remains. Such a sight is fitting on a battlefield, but here it is too tragic. Go now and order the soldiers to fire a funeral volley.
(Funeral March. All exit, carrying the bodies. Cannon sounds from within.)
= End of Hamlet =

To the Esteemed Readers from Hyŏn Ch'ŏl
The flowing time stops for no one, and now another year, the year of Imsul (1922), has completely passed. When reflecting (回顧) on the past and speculating (推想) on the future, who could be satisfied with their current small state? At this time last year, sending off this day, the new plans for the New Year were surely not just this meager, but looking back after time has passed, it is again just so and nothing more; can this be suppressed by human strength (人力)? We can only strive for humanity, setting aside calculations, as our duty (本務) as people. It has already been two years since starting this Hamlet, and making the readers waste tedious time for so long fills me with no small amount of apology. With Hyŏn Ch'ŏl's shallow and meager talent (淺薄菲才), among the various plays (戱曲) translated, I have not seen its equal in difficulty (難涉). This is because the play Hamlet itself is a world-renowned masterpiece (世界的 名篇), where not a single character or phrase can be treated lightly (泛然히), and another reason is that adjusting the dual psychology (二重心理) of the protagonist Hamlet in terms of stage mood (舞臺的 氣分) or breathing (呼吸上) was extremely challenging. Indeed, for certain passages, there were not a few instances where a whole day was spent. However, looking at the result, it cannot be said to be that satisfactory. If, fortunately, a genius of drama (戱曲的 天才) emerges and again perfects the truth, goodness, and beauty (善眞美) of this translation, it would be an honor for our literary world (文壇). Hoping for the birth of such a genius, I express my gratitude (謝意) to all of you who have read this devotedly for such a long time.
(November 25th) <68>

**Summary**

This text is Hyŏn Ch'ŏl's 1922 Korean translation of Act 5, Scene 2 of Shakespeare's *Hamlet*, published in the magazine *Kaebyŏk*. It covers the final, tragic scene including Osric's invitation to the duel, Hamlet's apologies to Laertes, the rigged fencing match where Hamlet, Laertes, the Queen, and the King are all poisoned or killed, and the arrival of Fortinbras who takes control of Denmark as Hamlet dies naming him successor. The translator includes an afterword reflecting on the difficulty of translating such a complex masterpiece, apologizing for the time taken, and expressing hope for future, better translations by Korean literary talents. From a historical perspective, this translation is significant as an early example of Western canonical literature being introduced to a Korean audience during the Japanese colonial period, offering insights into cultural transmission and the development of modern Korean literary language and translation practices in the 1920s. The translator's note reveals the challenges faced and the aspirations for Korean literary development even under colonial rule.

하믈. 오오 호레에시오 죽음은 임박하엿다. 激烈한 毒氣는 벌서 정신을 痲痺해 버렷다. 英國에서 온 節使의 보고를 들을 겨을까지도 견대지 못하겟다. 그러나 나는 遺言으로써 포오틴풀라스로 王嗣를 定해라. 그에게 최후의 말을 전해다고. 그리고 이 사건이 생긴 顚末을 자세히 가르처주게. 오오 그 뒤는 침묵뿐이다.
(하믈레트 氣絶한다.)
호레. 고귀한 그 마음도 이제는 부서젓다. 그러면 穩全히 잘가십시요. 사랑하는 皇子. 천사의 노래로 보내서 천국의 안식을 어드시도록. 웨 저 북소리가 여긔에 오나.
(안에서 行進曲. 포오틴풀라스, 英國節使 기타 多數人이 들어온다.)
포오. 어대잇나.
호레. 무엇을 보시랴고 하십니까. 慘憺한 물건, 놀날만한 그것을 보실야면 차지실것도 업습니다.
포오. 大破壞를 부르지지는 屍體의 산. 아아 자랑할만한 죽음의 신아. 네가 永劫의 廚房에서 어떠한 饗應을 하랴고 준비를 하느냐. 이와가티 다수의 貴人들을 한 彈子로 無慘하게 쏘와 죽인 것은?
節使. 慘憺한 이 광경. 그러면 내가 英國으로부터 가저온 보고도 때가 이미 느젓다. 로오쩬크란쯔 킬덴스다안의 두사람을 비여(誅)서  始終이 如一하게 大命을 마친 事由를 申達하랴고 하여도 이미 들을 귀가 먹엇다. 어대로써 감사하다는 말을 들을가.
호레. 국왕의 목숨이 남아 잇슬지라도 그 감사하다는 말을 들을 수가 업겟지. 두 사람을 죽이라는 命令은 결코 국왕의 명령이 아니니까. 그것은 어떠케 되엇던지. 이러한 대참사가 일어난 이때에 君王은 폴난드의 戰場에서 兩鄕은 英吉利에서 이와 가티 朝廷에 모임을 기회로 하여 명령으로써 이 시체를 놉히 壇上에 모시고. 그래서 나로 하여곰 얼마동안 아모 일도 모르는 世人에게 이러케된 자세한 사연을 말하게 하여 주십시오. 그러케 되는 당신네들도 아시게될 터이지요. 奸淫, 殘虐, 非道의 행동, 딸하서 돌연한 裁斷, 생각지 못한 殺人, 하는 수 업는 苦肉의 計略, 거긔에서 끗가는 이러한 大慘事, 자기의 계획이 齟齬를 생기게하여 계획한 者의 머리우에 應報가 왓다. 自初至終의 前後事然을 말슴할 수가 잇습니다.
포오. 속히 그것을 듯도록 합시다. 국내움˜ 모든 貴人을 불러＜67＞ 모으게 하지. 나의 자신에 대해서는 愁傷에 싸힌 이때이지마는 나의 운명을 안아 맛지 (抱迎).
이 나라와 적지 안니한 緣故도 잇는 까닭에 이를 기회로 하여 내 뜻을 달하도록 旋力하지.
호레. 거긔에 대해서는 말슴할 일이 잇습니다. 즉 다수의 동의를 끄을만한 그 양반의 입에서 온 유언이 잇습니다. 그러나 지금 내가 말슴한 것을 실행하도록 하여 주십시요. 인심이 흉흉한 오늘이니까. 그러치 아니하면 음모와 오해라고하는 이우에 더한 불행이 일어나지도 아니할는지 모릅니다.
포오. 하믈레트전하의 遺骸는 武將의 禮를 다해서 四人의 遂長으로 하여곰 壇上에 올려 모시게 하지. 기회만 잘탓스면 이 어른도 또한 훌륭한 英主되실 어른이다. 軍樂을 알외고 禮砲를 노하서 薨去하신 것을 사방에 고하게 하지. 遺骸를 모셔라. 이러한 광경이 戰場에서는 相當하지마는 여긔에는 넘우도 참혹하다. 자아 가서 병사들에게 弔砲를 노케하여라.
(葬送의 曲, 시체메고 모도들 나간다. 딸하서 안으로 大砲의 소리).
= 하믈레트 全篇完了 =

玄哲이 愛讀者 諸位에게
흐르는 세월 멈춤이 업서 이제 또한 壬戌의 1년도 다 가고 말앗다. 과거를 回顧하고 미래를 推想할 때에 누가 자기의 요만한 현상에 만족하리가 잇스리요. 작년에 오늘 이날을 보낼 이때에는 新年의 새로운 계획이 어찌 요뿐이리요마는 지나고 보면 또한 그러하고 그뿐인 것을 人力으로써 抑制하리요. 우리는 오즉 사람된 本務로써 打算을 멀리하고 인류를 위하야 노력할 뿐인가 한다. 이 하믈레트를 시작한지 이미 해가 지나기를 둘이나 하여 오래동안 지리한 시간을 독자에게 낭비케 한것은 자못 미안한 생각이 업지 아니하나. 玄哲의 淺薄菲才로써는 여러 가지 戱曲을 번역하는 중에 이와 가티 難涉한 것은 그 雙을 보지 못하엿스니 그것은 하믈레트라는 희곡의 자체가 世界的 名篇으로 一字一句를 泛然히 할 수 업는 그것과 또 한가지는 하믈레트 주인공의 二重心理가 舞臺的 氣分이나 呼吸上으로 調節을 마추기에 가장 힘이 들엇스니 실로 어떠한 구절에 이르러서는 하로 동안을 허비한 일이 적지 아니한 것도 잇섯다. 그러나 그 결과로 보아서는 그처럼 양호하다고 할 수가 업다. 다행이 戱曲的 天才가 나서 다시 이 번역의 善眞美를 다하엿스면 이곳 우리文壇의 한 名譽라고 하겟다. 나는 이러한 천재나기를 바라고 長久한 時日동안 애독하여 주신 諸位에게 謝意를 表하는 것이다.
