기사형태	시 (Poem)

Let Us Plant the Rose of Sharon (無窮花)
						Han Yong-un (韓龍雲)

Moon, O moon, bright moon, moon that shone on the old country, moon that crosses the iron bars to shine upon my heart, let us cut down the laurel tree (桂樹) and plant the Rose of Sharon (無窮花).

Moon, O moon, bright moon, moon reflected in my beloved’s mirror, moon that crosses the iron bars to be held in my embrace, when there is waning, let us help each other with love.

Moon, O moon, bright moon, moon shining boundlessly, moon that crosses the iron bars to pierce my soul (넉), let us cross over the cloud pass (嶺) and follow your light.<12>

**Summary**

This poem, written by Han Yong-un while imprisoned, uses the moon as a central motif to express longing for his nation and beloved, and a desire for national restoration symbolized by planting the Mugunghwa (Rose of Sharon), Korea's national flower, in place of the laurel tree (often associated with the moon in East Asian folklore, but here perhaps implying something foreign or needing replacement). The poem conveys deep patriotism, hope despite confinement, and a yearning for freedom under the oppressive colonial rule. Historians might find this poem particularly interesting as a poignant example of resistance literature from the colonial period, reflecting the nationalist sentiment and the use of symbolic language (Mugunghwa) to express forbidden political aspirations within the confines of prison and censorship.
