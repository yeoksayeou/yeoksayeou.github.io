발행일	1926년 03월 01일
기사제목	原始的悒鬱 - 一九二二 -, - 漁村哀景 - (Primitive Melancholy - 1922 -, - Sad Scene of a Fishing Village -)
필자	李相和
기사형태	시 (Poem)

The emerald plank of the sea, harboring a wandering nature, was silently prostrated
Like seeing a forest on a mountain top in late summer - it is a drowsing face.
This annoyingly drawn-out spring day - oh, how white the afternoon sky is!
Occasionally, the breath of a child sucking its mother's nipple drifts by.
Above the slanted hill, by the fences of the couple of houses squatting there,
Seashells stuck here and there in the hung nets are teeth smiling from afar.
On the sandy road lying prostrate before the village, there is no one.
Tired from last night's fishing - they must be drinking the sweet wine of a nap. <113>

Only two or three young wives, wearing red skirts with baskets at their waists,
Gather seaweed like the sea's dream, crossing from stepping stone to stepping stone.
To the soft and sad cry of the cuckoo, seemingly heard while half-asleep,
An old Sapsari dog stretches its neck, watches, then closes its eyes again and dozes.
In my heart, only the mind crossing beyond the horizon with the flock of seagulls,
And the soul-lost gaze—a cloud of thoughts like water, neither seeing nor trying to see anything, piles up.
-From 『The Gift of Escape/Seclusion (遁竄의 선물)』-<114>
<113-114>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Sanghwa paints a melancholic and languid picture of a Korean fishing village in spring. It contrasts the sleepy, seemingly peaceful external scene – the drowsing sea, napping villagers, women gathering seaweed, a dozing dog – with the speaker's internal state of "primitive melancholy," detachment, and a yearning to escape beyond the horizon. The imagery evokes a sense of stillness and quiet sadness. For historians, the poem offers a glimpse into the atmosphere of rural Korea during the colonial period. The depiction of ennui and the speaker's "soul-lost gaze" amidst the mundane village life could reflect broader sentiments of stagnation or quiet despair experienced under Japanese rule, providing insight into the psychological landscape of the time beyond overt political statements.
