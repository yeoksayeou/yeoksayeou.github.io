기사형태	문예기타 (Literary - Other)

A New Fairy Tale - Why? (新童話)
						Myuhreun (Original Author), Pak Yŏnghŭi (Translator)
Once upon a time, in a small village, there was a poor house. In that house lived a child who had no mother and no father. That child was the only young child in that house. All the other people were gloomy, disheartened, hunched-over old folks. Those old people liked sitting quietly in sunny spots, and when the child played pranks and bumped into them or made too much noise, those old people would get angry.
This was a sad life for the child, P’oul. He never heard a kind word, nor did he receive love from anyone. Also, when he was unhappy, there was no one to stroke his hair, nor anyone to comfort him.
Instead, P’oul only heard scolding every day and was even beaten from time to time.
P’oul's strange nature angered his supervisor in the poor house. It was because P’oul always liked to ask, "Why?" and wanted to know the reason for everything. <40>
"You must never ask why," said the sturdy mistress who managed the poor house, her face angry.
"Everything is as it is, and that is how it should be," the old woman added again.
"But why don't I have parents like the village kids?" insisted young P’oul.
"Because your parents died, that's why."
"Why did they die?"
"Because God (한우님) wished it."
"Why did God wish it to be so?"
"Be quiet, you useless child! Stop asking such unanswerable questions," the plump old woman’s face turned red with anger. This was because she didn't know what to answer to P’oul's question, and because ignorant people, when forced to say "I don't know," have nothing else but anger.
But no one could silence young P’oul. Looking up at the old woman's red, angry face, P’oul asked again.
"Why do you get angry so easily?"
"Smack" - P’oul got slapped on the cheek. He began to cry. And ran away. Even as he ran, P’oul asked—.
"Why did you hit me?"
P’oul came to the chicken yard. There stood a large hen with feathers of various colors. The hen clucked loudly and walked proudly, saying
"I laid an egg! I laid an egg!" Then, the chickens from all around also clucked together—
"I laid an egg! I laid an egg!" <41>
The rooster got angry. It was because the hen was proudly doing something he couldn't do. So the rooster scoffed—
"I am a rooster. Aren't you hens?"
Meri, the poor house's young blonde servant girl, came out and carefully gathered the eggs into her skirt and took them into the house.
"Where are you taking all those eggs?" P’oul asked the speckled hen.
"Taking them to the market (市)."
"Who eats those eggs there?"
"Rich people! People with lots of money!" the hen said boastfully, as if what she said was glorious for her.
"Why haven't I ever had even one egg? I'm always hungry, just like you seem to be," P’oul complained.
"Because you are a poor beggar with nothing," the hen said, majestically spreading her wings, lifting her curved beak, and looking down at P’oul.
"Then why did I become a poor beggar with nothing?"
At that, the hen, just like the old woman before, got angry and furious.
"Go away! Your questions just make me tired."
P’oul, disappointed, quietly withdrew. The garden gate was open, so he went out onto the main road and wandered aimlessly until he came to a cowshed. The cowshed belonged to a rich farmer.
Cows of white, red, and yellow colors stood looking at him with large, gentle eyes. P’oul was very hungry. So P’oul went to the cow that looked the kindest and begged.— <42>
"Cow, can you give me some of your milk?"
"I wouldn't dare do such a thing," the cow replied, "Our milk belongs to the rich farmer."
Young P’oul was surprised to hear the cow's words. Then he counted the cows in the shed. One, two, three... twelve. After twelve, he didn't know how to count further. There were that many cows.
P’oul, raised in the poor house, had only learned meekness and obedience. Nothing else.
Twelve cows, P’oul said thoughtfully.
"Can that rich farmer really drink the milk of twelve cows all by himself?"
"Oh, no," the kind cow informed P’oul. "The farmer takes the milk to the market and sells it."
P’oul thought of what the speckled hen had said earlier. And asked the cow again.
"Then can poor children drink that milk?"
"Truly, P’oul!" the cow sighed, "You are truly dull and inexperienced! They use the milk to make delicious cream, cakes, and pudding, and sell them to the rich."
"Why don't they sell to the poor? Don't poor people like delicious cakes?"
"Don't ask me so many things," the cow replied. "I am just a dumb cow. So I don't know what words would be good to answer you with. Not only that, but it's time for the master to come, so it would be best for you to leave now. If the master catches you, you will be beaten badly."
P’oul patted the shiny hide of the cow and soon went on his way again. He walked and walked until he reached a large barley field (麥田) where the wind was passing through. The barley field was like <43> golden waves moving quietly. A quiet and sad sound reached P’oul's ears. P’oul knew what that sound was.
It was the sound of, "Soon, the barley reapers will come here with sickles (鎌), swish-swish, and cut us down, swish-swish. And people will make white bread from us and bake it and eat it, swish-swish."
"Who eats that white bread?" asked P’oul, who had never tasted a piece of bread in his life.
"The rich, the people with lots of money eat it!" the barley replied, keeping rhythm with the wind.
"Ah, the rich again!" P’oul exclaimed in surprise. "Does everything in this world belong only to the rich?"
"Everything, everything," it murmured.
"Why?"
That question seemed very amusing and also laughable to the barley (麥).
"Oh, how foolish you are!"
In any case, the barley (麥) also couldn't answer P’oul's question. P’oul almost started to cry.
He stamped his foot angrily on the ground and shouted loudly—
"I have searched for someone to answer my question. But is there really no one to answer my question?"
Just then, a hedgehog slowly crawling across the road said—
"The wisest creature I know lives in the oak forest; it is the owl."
"Why didn't you go ask him?"
"Can you tell me that?"
The hedgehog, as if unable to wait until P’oul finished speaking, retracted its neck and bristled its spines until its body looked like a spiked ball. <44>
"I dislike associating with humans," he said in a tone as solemn as his spines.
"Humans are foolish. You should go to the owl. But do not anger him. If you do, he will pluck out your eyes before you."
Night came, sending dark shadows to cover all the land. The forest grew dark. Therefore, P’oul felt a little anxious, but this mysterious forest was still more pleasant than his frightening poor house. So he walked further.
The further P’oul went, the denser and more lush the trees became. Soon, there was no more path to follow. But P’oul proceeded over the soft carpet (毛氈) covered in green moss. The fragrance of the forest was refreshing. Under the tall trees were delicious strawberry plants, so P’oul picked and ate them as he went.
Finally, he came to the base of a large oak tree and saw an owl sitting on a branch. The owl was wearing glasses on its eyes and studying a green book held in its feet.
P’oul stopped beneath the tree and called out.
"Hello! Mr. Owl! Mr. Owl!"
But the owl was engrossed in his studies and didn't hear him call. Only after P’oul called repeatedly several times did he finally look down.
The owl screeched in an angry voice, glaring at P’oul with round, fierce eyes.
"Hmm, what is it? How dare you disturb my studies?" the owl asked.
"Forgive me, Mr. Owl!" P’oul pleaded, saying—
"The hedgehog told me to come to you. He said that among all he knows, you are the wisest creature. Surely you can answer my question."
"What concern is the hedgehog's opinion to me? So, what answer do you want me to give?" <45> the owl grumbled.
"How can I waste my precious time on such matters with a foolish child like you? You must know well that I see only at night and cannot see during the day, and summer nights are too short for my studies, don't you? I too think about many problems. One in particular has tormented me for many years. It has made me old. Yet, science (科學) in this world has not been able to help interpret that problem," the owl sighed deeply, his face turning sad.
"Then what is the problem you are trying to solve?" P’oul asked the owl worriedly.
"Perhaps you can solve it, you hopeless person?" the owl sneered.
"If only this problem could be solved, all other problems in the world could also be solved. Why are all people so foolish?"
the owl said.
"Are all people truly so foolish?" P’oul asked in surprise.
"Yes. If you don't even know that, why are you bothering me? Don't you realize how foolish you are?"
"I don't know," P’oul replied, blushing. "Mr. Owl! You must know that I live in a poor house. And in that house, there are only old people. They are all inherently wise people."
"Ha, ha, ha," the owl laughed. The sound echoed most frighteningly in the dark forest. "Ha, ha, ha, you are truly an excellent example of humanity's foolishness. Are all the wise people really in that poor house? Alright, let's assume you are right. Who do you like best in that poor house?"
"I like Meri."
"Who is Meri?" <46>
"She's the servant girl."
"What does that girl do?"
"She works all day long. She gets up at five in the morning and goes to bed the latest at night."
"Then she must have earned a lot of money, wears beautiful clothes, and eats good food, surely?"
"No, that's not right. She's as poor as a beggar. Her clothes are patched all over, and she only eats what others leave behind."
"Hmm, well then, why does she work so hard if she gets nothing?"
Young P’oul thought for a moment.
"I don't know."
"But I know. It's because she is very foolish. Meri must know well that there are fashionable ladies (貴婦人) who live luxuriously in fine clothes, eating expensive food, without lifting a finger to work. Meri must have asked herself, 'How is it that I, who work all day, have nothing, while those who do nothing have everything?'"
"I don't think she thought that."
"Then, your Meri is a foolish girl. Who do you think is wise up till now?"
"Old Yagop."
"Who is this old Yagop?"
"He is an old laborer. He is eighty years old. That old man worked until he was seventy. Now <47> he can't work at all.
And the old man's feet and hands are crippled with rheumatism."
"He worked for sixty years only for others. That's a long time. I would think old Yagop would be treated like a prince (王子), and everyone would be very concerned with attending to him? Doesn't he rest his tired body on a wonderfully soft bed, eat specially good food every day, and live happily?"
"Oh, no sir. When he grumbles that the cotton padding (면보) is too hard to eat, the old mistress always curses him. And when the old man asks for some tobacco, the mistress gets angry and screams that the old man is senile."
"Then, if old Yagop knew he wouldn't be able to live well when he got old, why did he work until seventy?"
"I don't know."
"It's because he is foolish. That old man must also know well, just like Meri, that there are young gentlemen living like kings without doing any work, right?"
"Yes," P’oul said sadly. "But Mr. Owl, I have something to ask you.
Why are there rich people in this world?"
"Truly, after our conversation, you must answer that yourself. Foolish one. It is because the poor people are foolish."
"But why are they so foolish?" <48>
But now the owl, just like the mistress and the speckled hen before, also got angry.
"Didn't I tell you I've been thinking about this problem for many years, you foolish human! Come back to me eighty years from now. Perhaps then I might be able to answer."
"But why?"
"I don't want to hear it. Be silent!" the owl commanded young P’oul.
"You have already stolen my valuable time. Go see the cuckoo!"
"Where does the cuckoo live?" asked the frightened young P’oul.
But the owl had already adjusted his glasses and was engrossed in looking at the green book, giving no answer.
"Oh, what should I do!" young P’oul thought sadly. "Now I have to go to the cuckoo. But I need to know where it lives. Does the cuckoo know more than the owl? But I am already tired. My feet hurt," he sat down under a young, slender cherry tree on the soft green moss. After a while, his heart felt very heavy. He thought about why he was abandoned by everyone. And suddenly, he wept bitterly. Then he realized he heard a serene, thin voice coming from somewhere high up. The voice sounded like the ringing of pure silver (純銀) bells.
"Child, why are you crying so?" the bell-like voice rang out.
P’oul looked up and saw a strange little creature he had never seen before in his life. A spirit sat on the branch of the cherry tree. The spirit had golden hair that flowed down to its toes, and its small face was pale like moonlight. And its large eyes shone green like cherry leaves. It flew lightly down from the tree towards P’oul and landed on P’oul's shoulder. It felt like a light leaf touching him. And with its small white hand, it stroked P’oul's face. P’oul's heart grew warm. How wonderful it felt when the soft hand touched him! He stopped his tears and looked up at the small creature. And finally— <49>
"Who are you?" he asked.
"I am the spirit of the cherry tree (樹精)," the creature replied.
"All day long I must sit in the tree. But when night comes, I become free. Therefore, I walk upon the earth and play pranks with my sister tree spirits (樹精). But why are you so sad?"
P’oul told the spirit his misfortunes, and finally said—
"I always must ask why (why?). That question burns inside my heart. It torments me. So I would be happy if I could receive an answer. But that question lies like a great wall (墻) between me and other people who don't ask it. And that makes me lonely."
The little tree spirit (樹精) smiled, and its lovely face became even more beautiful than before.
"P’oul, you are mistaken," it said in a gentle voice. "You are not alone. Hundreds and thousands of people ask the same question. And they grieve and suffer. Put your ear to the ground and tell me what you hear."
P’oul did as instructed. At first, P’oul only clearly heard sighs and whispers. Then he heard sounds of weeping and shouting in distress, and finally, he heard voices.
"Mother, I'm hungry. Why is there nothing to eat?" was the voice of a crying child.
"I'm suffocating to death in the hot city. Why can't I go to the countryside for the summer <50> like the rich children?" mumbled the voice of a child.
"I worked all day long. But why are my wages (賃金) so low that I cannot survive?" came a woman's sobbing voice.
"Why do the lazy have everything, and the laborers have nothing?" said a man's voice, seemingly threatening.
And then all the voices rose at once, shouting, murmuring, sobbing, threatening—
"Why? Why?"
P’oul stood up and looked up at the tree spirit (樹精). And P’oul asked the spirit, who sat very calmly near him.
"Who are these people I hear?"
"They are your people," the little spirit replied. "They are your family. You have heard all the voices of the world. You will hear the question asked angrily, worriedly, threateningly from the mouths of all people. Every day, new voices join the chorus (合唱). And the voices of a thousand people will turn into the voices of a million, ten million people. Then the end will come for the pitiful poor, and the last day will come for the lazy parasites (寄生者)."
"When will that happen?" P’oul asked eagerly.
"That I cannot say. I only know this: Every time I put my ear to the ground and discover new voices joining, I know that the final day is not far off."
"Then can't we make that day come sooner?"
"Of course. There are still many, many people who don't yet <51> know how good it could be for others and what state their own lives are in. They work like beasts, but they cannot ask why their sincere labor receives famine (饑饉) wages (賃金). These poor and ignorant people must discover the truth. But that is by no means an easy task. Because the poor work during the day, they have no time to think about it, and also because they are too tired from that work, they cannot think. Meanwhile, the rich do everything in their power to prevent questions from arising in the workers' minds. That is why those who ask 'Why?' among the laborers are punished. P’oul, you must have already known this from your own experience?"
"Then should I continue to ask?" P’oul asked.
"Do so, P’oul! Do not ask the rich. They will not answer you. But if they do answer you, they would say, 'This world is such a bad place for the poor. It is because we rich are greedy, selfish, and base.' Because people do not like to speak ill of themselves. But go to the poor people and ask them— 'Why do you work so hard and eat dry bread, while other lazy rich people eat good bread? Why are the rich children healthy and rosy-cheeked, while your children are thin, pale, and sickly? Why do the lazy wrongdoers (不正者), when they grow old, rest luxuriously and comfortably from their lazy lives, while you work your whole lives in poor houses?' Ask the poor people, who have for so long fallen under this unjust system like a hammer (맛치), until they break it down. Will you ask them?" <52>
"Yes," replied P’oul, his eyes shining brightly.
The little spirit kissed P’oul's forehead and said earnestly. "Your life will be difficult (골란할). The rich, who fear losing what they have stolen, will punish (罰) you. They will cut off your questions before they leave your throat and throw you into a prison where no one can hear your voice. But you must not lose your courage. That small question will not vanish into thin air. You must speak before the thousands who have been mute until today. And so you will find comrades, find friends, and you will not be a lonely soul."
The little spirit smiled, nodded its head, flew lightly away, and settled on the cherry tree branch.
"Are you leaving already?" asked little P’oul worriedly.
"You go back home. But whenever you return to me, I will always comfort you and help you."
"Wait just a moment," P’oul pleaded.
"The owl said he would answer my question in eighty years, eighty years from now. That's a long time. Is the owl right?"
"That depends on what your people do," the silver-bell-voiced spirit replied kindly.
"Perhaps it will take eighty years until you all become wise. Or perhaps, if you and your comrades do not cease asking, it might be resolved in fifty years. The great day of freedom might come in twenty years. Yes. Perhaps it might even be tomorrow."
The little tree spirit (樹精) disappeared into the forest. But all the trees rustled refreshingly and joyfully, calling to P’oul—
"Tomorrow! Tomorrow!"
-(End)- <53>

**Summary**

This translated fairy tale tells the story of P'oul, a poor orphan boy who constantly questions the injustices and inequalities around him by asking "Why?". He questions why he has no parents, why the poor work hard but remain poor while the rich enjoy luxury without labor, and why this social order exists. After being rebuffed or deemed foolish by adults, animals, and even a wise owl, he encounters a benevolent tree spirit. The spirit reveals that many others share his questions and suffer similarly. It encourages P'oul to continue asking "Why?" among the poor, fostering collective awareness to challenge the unjust system and hasten a future of liberation, possibly even "tomorrow."

From a colonial history perspective, this story, published in *Kaebyŏk* magazine in 1926, is highly significant. *Kaebyŏk* was known for its nationalist and sometimes socialist leanings. The tale, though seemingly simple, carries a strong allegorical message criticizing class exploitation and promoting consciousness-raising among the oppressed masses (the poor, the laborers like Meri and Yagop). The constant questioning of "Why?" reflects a burgeoning critical awareness challenging established hierarchies – potentially including the colonial structure itself, albeit indirectly through the lens of class struggle. The translator, Pak Yŏnghŭi, was a prominent figure in the Korean proletarian literature movement (KAPF). Historians would find this piece valuable as an example of how socialist or critical social ideas were disseminated through accessible forms like fairy tales during the Japanese colonial period, aiming to awaken and mobilize the populace against perceived injustices. The final, hopeful note of "Tomorrow!" suggests an imminent desire for radical social change.

새로운 童話 - 웨? (新童話)
						뮤흐른(原作_), 朴英熙(譯)
옛날에 적은 마을에 잇는 한 가난한 집이 잇섯다. 그집에는 어머니도 업고 아버지도 업는 어린아해가 잇섯다. 그 아해는 그집에서는 다만 한아인 어린 아해이엿다. 다른 모든 사람들은 침울하고 마음풀어진 꾸부렁이 늙어뱅이들이엿다. 그 늙은이들은 양지짝에 고요히 안기를 조화하며, 그 아해가 작란을 치다가 그들에게 부다치든지 혹은 너무 떠들 때에는 그 늙은이들은 노하엿섯다.
이러한 것이 어린애 포울에게는 슬픈 생활이엿다. 그는 친절한 소리도 듯지 못하고 또한 아모에게서든지 사랑도 밧지 못하엿다. 또한 그가 불행할 때에 그를 어루만저주는 이도 업섯고, 또는 그를 위로하여주는 이도 아모도 업섯다.
그러는 대신에 포울은 날마다 꾸중만 듯고 또한 매까지 때때 마젓섯다.
포울의 이상스러운 성질이, 그 가난한 집에 잇는 그의 감독인을 노하게 하엿다. 그것은 어느 때든지 포울은 「웨?」하고 뭇기를 조화하며, 모든 것에 까닭을 알고 십허 하엿든 것이다.＜40＞
「어느 때든지 너는 그 까닭을 물어서는 안이 된다」하고 그 가난한 집을 다사리는 건장한 주부가 노한 빗츠로 말하엿다.
「모든 것은 잇는 그대로 잇는 것이며, 그것이 또한 올흔 것이란다」하고 로파는 또 다시 말하엿다.
「그러치만 나는 웨 동내애들처럼 부모가 업슬까요?」하고 어린 포울은 억제를 썻다.
「너의 부모는 죽엇스니깐 그럿치」
「웨 그들이 죽엇슬까요?」
「한우님이 그것을 원하시엿든 까닭에」
「웨 한우님이 그러케 되기를 원하엿슬까요?」
