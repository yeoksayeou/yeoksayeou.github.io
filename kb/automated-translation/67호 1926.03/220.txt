Magazine Title 개벽 第67호
Publication Date 1926년 03월 01일
Article Title 싸움(戱曲) (Fight (Play))
Author 金永八 (Kim Yŏngp'al)
Article Type 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Fight (Play)
						Kim Yŏngp'al
One Act
Characters   Kyŏngae, her Mother, Chŏngae (her friend), Haksu (her husband)
Time   These days
Place   The back room of a small tavern
Stage     A south-facing wooden floor (two *kan* wide). To the left, it connects to the inner room; to the right, it connects to the tavern floor.
The furnishings are not plentiful, but various things are lined up messily; two tables are set out. When the curtain rises, Kyŏngae and Chŏngae look at each other with uneasy expressions.
Chŏngae. I thought it must be true— How could—
Kyŏngae. ...
Chŏngae. So you just let it be after suffering such an insult?
Kyŏngae. Whether it was an insult or what, I was so bewildered, I really couldn't figure out what had happened. It felt like a dream, yet also like reality— How could I go outside, so ashamed—. If the teachers saw that newspaper article, what would they think of me—. The more I think about it, the more choked up I get, I could just die.
Chŏngae. Exactly, where are such wicked people? Perhaps someone holds a grudge against you. That's why they'd publish such a false article in the newspaper to insult you, right? Otherwise, how could such a thing happen?
Kyŏngae. (Sighs) I'm so choked up! I could just die.
Chŏngae. When I saw that newspaper, I first thought maybe it was someone with the same name and found it rather amusing, but as I read on, the name was exactly yours and the details were similar <25> though the part about selling alcohol seemed like a lie, but your picture was printed clearly, so I thought maybe you really did end up selling alcohol—. If that were the case—
Kyŏngae. Think about it. No matter how desperate I became, would I sell alcohol?
Chŏngae. That's what I'm saying. How could they write such lies in the newspaper—
Kyŏngae. Exactly. Anyone who sees the newspaper would think it's true. Who would think it's a lie?
Chŏngae. Lately, all sorts of things are happening. What kind of woman sells alcohol, anyway? They say they're utterly wicked— laughing scornfully while cheating people out of their money.
Kyŏngae. Chŏngae. When you saw that newspaper, didn't you think I was a wicked woman?
Chŏngae. What— How could I call you a wicked woman without even knowing if it's true or not?
Kyŏngae. If I were selling alcohol, would you abandon me, calling me a wicked woman?
Chŏngae. Well, what then? I'm still a student, so in my position as a student, even if you finished high school, imagine associating with you while you're selling alcohol. Would society forgive that—. And my reputation too...
Kyŏngae. (Extremely agitated) Then people who don't know my situation will see the newspaper article and not think of me as a decent person, right?
Chŏngae. How would I know that—
Kyŏngae. Do all people trust newspapers like that?
Chŏngae. Of course, they trust them—. But why haven't you issued a retraction yet—. What did your husband say?
Kyŏngae. Don't even mention it, it makes me upset. While I was fuming and making a scene, he just laughed "껄껄" and said, "What if you were selling it?" I'm so upset, I could just die.
Chŏngae. What? What if you were selling it? Why is he like that? When his wife is being insulted like this. He just laughs and lets it go? How can you trust him as a husband then?
Kyŏngae. What can I do—. Anyway, I'm too ashamed to live. How can I hold my head up and go outside? The newspaper loudly declared, even with a picture, that I'm doing the liquor business, which everyone looks down on and despises. From now on, no one will see me as the clean person I was, right? Because of that wretched <26> newspaper, my reputation is forever ruined, turned into a dirty woman. Just hearing the name 'liquor seller' is disgusting! A liquor seller is everyone's servant, they say. (Cries) I don't know how I ended up like this. Oh, Chŏngae, you'll understand me, won't you? Right?
Chŏngae. (With an uneasy expression) Don't cry. Did you do anything wrong? The newspaper company published the article incorrectly. And no matter if such an article came out, as long as you didn't sell alcohol, that's all that matters, isn't it?
Kyŏngae. Even so, how many out of a hundred people would know that? Everyone will just call me a bad woman. Who's going to blame the newspaper company? Anyway, this year is cursed. Just die, die—. But I don't understand the mindset of not even issuing a retraction. If I only knew who wrote it, I'd writhe and die right in front of him. That damned bastard!
Chŏngae. That's also a business, so they probably sold out for a few pennies, making something untrue seem factual. And what then? They call newspapers a mirror? Does the backside look like the face—. And they ruin someone's image—. Hey, but how did they get that photo out?
Kyŏngae. The photo, you mean. It's truly unbelievable if I tell you (Thinks about something) The photo, right?
Chŏngae. Yes, the photo—
Kyŏngae. That photo. The one in the newspaper—
Chŏngae. Yes— (Impatiently)
Kyŏngae. One day, a child from the company brought a letter from my husband. I looked at it, and some newspaper company said they wanted to feature us in a 'Home Introduction' section and asked for photos. So I sent my photo and his photo, and then I saw the evening newspaper, and there it was— they wrote the article like that and published the photos. I was so stunned, I asked him about it, and he immediately said, "Why did they write the article like this?" Then he said he'd meet them and talk, but later said he couldn't ask, then said he'd speak well of it, seeming completely untroubled, thinking it mundane. Even when I cried and screamed about how outrageous it was, he just laughed and let it go—
Chŏngae. Then you know the person who wrote the article too?
Kyŏngae. Yes, he's a friend—
Chŏngae. A friend? What? He lets his friend's wife be insulted like that? What kind of friend is that? <27> If they were closer, wouldn't he accuse her of prostitution?
Kyŏngae. This time, I'm enduring it solely because of my husband. Otherwise, I'd set fire to that damned newspaper office right away. Because women are weak. They think we'll just stay quiet no matter what. But even a worm squirms when stepped on; if they keep looking down on the weak like this, they'll get hurt badly. Anyway, it's a damned society—
Chŏngae. How could they do that? The newspaper company itself, if they realized such an article was wrong, should have immediately issued a retraction to inform the general readers, and they definitely should have come to apologize to you. If they just play dumb, it means they want to bury the untruth by making it seem factual. That's how they sell newspapers—
Kyŏngae. You can't blame the newspaper company or anything. It's just about survival (목구녕이 부두텽이지 - idiom: 'the throat is 포도청 (police bureau)' meaning survival is paramount). Because my mother does this wretched business to eat, people look down on us. When will she ever stop this lowly business? When will we escape this business where we aren't treated like human beings even though we are, and live like others? The world's sentiment is such that they try to push down a falling tree even further, so let them do as they please—
Chŏngae. Saying 'let them do as they please' is foolish. Even for you, just staying put is not the answer. Your husband is your husband, and you are you. You have your own personality, and your husband has different opinions, doesn't he? So how can you just overlook this? You still have the strength to push forward, right? You shouldn't rot away in a place like this forever; if you shoulder the backdrop called 'society' and start working, the connections will be significant.
Kyŏngae. Exactly, that's why my heart isn't right. I always listen to my husband, and sometimes it might not be like that. But if I think more about my future, tears well up even when I'm just sitting still, my hands lose all strength, and I can't grasp anything. I've done things I shouldn't have in this world, that wretched thing (Cries)
Chŏngae. (Hesitantly) Still, not knowing that, at school...
Kyŏngae. Yes, what about at school?
Chŏngae. The teacher, you know...
Kyŏngae. Yes, what about the teacher? <28>
Chŏngae. Said something quite meaningful...
Kyŏngae. Uh-huh— What did she say?
Chŏngae. Let's drop it.
Kyŏngae. Why are you being like this? Please tell me.
Chŏngae. One day in the classroom, she said, "After graduating from our school, students either become concubines or sell alcohol. I wonder if the students saw that newspaper article"— Ah— she said it so nonchalantly.
Kyŏngae. Did she mention any names?
Chŏngae. Even if she didn't, wouldn't we know...
Kyŏngae. Still.
Chŏngae. It's like covering your eyes and saying 'aah-oong' (눈가리구 아웅하는 것이지 - idiom: obvious self-deception), what else?
Kyŏngae. Chŏngae, when you go to school tomorrow, please explain things clearly for me, okay? If only they hadn't said I sell alcohol, I wouldn't care what else they said I did. But why did they have to say I sell alcohol, making people curse me? What should I do? I think I'm going to die—
Chŏngae. Hey, don't talk like that. Tell your husband to issue a retraction quickly—. How can he just stand by after seeing that—. Oh, he works at a newspaper company too, right?
Kyŏngae. Yes.
Chŏngae. What does he do at the newspaper company?
Kyŏngae. (Hesitantly) A worker (직공)—
Chŏngae. (Looking again) Oh, a worker?— His salary—
Kyŏngae. I don't know how much his salary is.
Chŏngae. Isn't it hard to make a living?
Kyŏngae. If it's just us, we manage somehow—
Chŏngae. (Smiling) He loves you very much, right?
Kyŏngae. (Smiles faintly)
Chŏngae. Speaking of it again, do you know how much I trembled when I saw that newspaper? There was a huge commotion about it at my house.
Kyŏngae. About me, you mean.
Chŏngae. Of course (Pause) I'm leaving now. I'll come again— Okay.
(Exits)
Kyŏngae. (Watching her leave) Worrying about all sorts of things. - Everyone seems to look down on me, so what's the point of living— (Cries)
(Her mother opens the door from the tavern)
Mother. Are you crying again?
Kyŏngae. (Cries harder at her mother's words) <29>
Mother. Hey. Kyŏngae. Kyŏngae (Shakes her)
Kyŏngae. (Crying) Yes?
Mother. Why are you crying? Huh?
Kyŏngae. The tears just come. I don't know why.
Mother. What's wrong? Didn't your friend who was just here say something?
Kyŏngae. No— (Lifts her head)
Mother. Then—
Kyŏngae. (Forcing a smile) Really, the tears just came out by themselves.
Mother. What's wrong? I was sitting in that room listening, and that girl who came earlier was rambling on about this and that. That's why you cried, right?
Kyŏngae. Yes—
Mother. You're ashamed in front of your friends too, aren't you? That kind of damned thing— Eh.
Kyŏngae. My face burns so much I can't speak. Mother, what should I do—
Mother. It's fate, so what can we do— When this mother raised you, I hoped to raise you well and find you a good husband, but it's all ruined. Heaven hates me and hates you too, so what can we do? Some things in the world can't be done by human effort. When you cry and carry on like this, how can my heart be at peace? As you know, it's been three days since I've eaten properly. (Wipes tears) Raised preciously without a father, finished school, and now running a household, but this talk about selling alcohol, making a fuss about educating female students—it feels like driving a nail into a mother's heart. And especially since you keep crying and making a fuss, what can I do? Don't say another word, when your husband comes, talk to him calmly about it, okay? Kyŏngae.
Kyŏngae. Mother, please don't be too upset, okay?
Mother. No— What does it matter if I'm upset? I should have died long ago.
Kyŏngae. Mother, please stop talking like that.
Mother. Alright, I want to stop, but the words just come out.
Kyŏngae. Mother?
Mother. Yes.
Kyŏngae. Will you listen to me?
Mother. What is it?
Kyŏngae. Whatever it is—
Mother. Go on.
Kyŏngae. Mother, should people receive human treatment or not? <30>
Mother. What kind of talk is that?
Kyŏngae. Mother, I wish we could become human too.
Mother. Human? Are we not human?
Kyŏngae. We're humans who aren't human, not human humans.
Mother. What does that mean? It's frustrating.
Kyŏngae. Mother, let's stop the liquor business. Okay?
Mother. Stop the liquor business?
Kyŏngae. Yes—
Mother. (Sighing) Do you think anyone wants to be a liquor seller? Do you think I do this business because I want to be the servant of many men? Even you say this to me. Receiving ridicule from countless men, being looked down upon by countless men, hearing all sorts of disgusting things—do you think I endure this for fun? A dozen times a day, I want to throw out the liquor bowls; even when my insides churn, I endure it like holding back urine because my life depends on it. You have your husband, so you eat what he brings, but your young, deaf little brother knows nothing, don't I have to feed him? When your father passed away and your older brother died, we borrowed money for them, don't we have to pay back that debt? Should we just take others' things like thieves—. I have it worse than you. Who would enjoy doing this? This business that makes us the laughingstock of the world—. Just thinking about it gives me chills. Disgusting—. (Sighs)
Kyŏngae. Mother, I know that, I know it, but more than that, I'm angry that we aren't treated like human beings.
Mother. Of course, it's infuriating. But what can we do when it's infuriating? It can't be helped. Let those who won't see us as human do as they please. Among them, those with upright hearts will see us as human.
Kyŏngae. Mother, please do that. And let's just make porridge if we have porridge ingredients from what he earns, or rice if we have rice—. Mother, please do that, okay?
Mother. Be quiet. Let's ask him in detail when he comes. What insults will we face next, what strange things will we hear? Why did he give out the photo— If only the photo hadn't been published, it would be better. What a scatterbrain, a scatterbrain.
Kyŏngae. Mother, please don't speak so unpleasantly when he can hear, okay Mother.
Mother. This is the first time I see you taking his side like this. But today, I need to make some noise. Where is there such a man who disgraces <31> his wife like that? Where—.
(Brief silence— Kyŏngae's husband enters)
Kyŏngae. You're here now?
Haksu. Yes—
Kyŏngae. Why are you late?
Haksu. A bit late— But why are your eyes like that, have you been crying?
Kyŏngae. ...
Mother. (Annoyed) Do you think there's a day lately when she doesn't cry?
Haksu. (Looking across at his wife) Why are you crying like that, huh?
Kyŏngae. Crying about what...
Mother. Why is she crying? Don't you have any thoughts?
Haksu. (Calmly) What thoughts—
Mother. What do you mean, what thoughts? Ah— So you see that a young, vibrant woman is said to be selling alcohol, with her picture in the newspaper, and you have no thoughts at all?
Haksu. What thought is there— Why? Are you saying that's bad?
Mother. So, you think it's good? That she's selling alcohol.
Haksu. It's not particularly good, but it's not particularly bad either. (To his wife) Is that why you cried?
Kyŏngae. (Angrily) Well, how can I live when others make me so ashamed?
Haksu. Ashamed of others?
Mother. Of course, isn't that so?
Haksu. What is there to be ashamed of before others?
Kyŏngae. Look, you always scold me, asking what there is to be ashamed of, but really, it's not something to scold me about. Because of that fact, I can't hold my head up and go outside freely, so how can I not be angry?
Haksu. Who told you not to go outside?
Kyŏngae. Nobody said that.
Haksu. Then why?
Mother. (As if stunned) Then why? Is this man even making sense? Is he talking about the neighborhood, or what? I can't understand at all. So, with what face can she face people—. Is that all? All her friends will point fingers, so with what face can she walk down the street with her head held high! Huh! When I think about it, just— eh. (Sighs and slams the door shut angrily, exiting) <32>
Haksu. (Smiling) Is that so? Can't face people, and friends will point fingers? (Bows his head)
Kyŏngae. (Wiping tears) You insulted me, didn't you?
Haksu. Me?
Kyŏngae. Yes, you— You must have said such things. And gave them the photo— I'm furious. Why did you give me such a dirty name—. Liquor seller—. And made it so that teachers and dear friends can't even face me. And yet, you don't show the slightest bit of remorse, instead, you treat me with such satisfaction, with cold ridicule, don't you? And you still say you love your wife?
Haksu. So you resent me for that?
Kyŏngae. Yes, I can't help but resent you. You don't respect your wife's personality at all. You think, 'She's my wife, so I can do whatever I want. All rights belong to me.' And you still say you love me?
Haksu. What did I do that was like that?
Kyŏngae. Then, is that your way of loving me?
Haksu. That's right. That has become the proof that I love you, and also that you truly love me. It might be a fact worth crying over for one side, but for me, there couldn't be greater satisfaction. Still, you don't understand?
Kyŏngae. How long will you keep mocking me like this? What satisfaction do you get? Are you really satisfied that your wife is being insulted?
Haksu. Do you consider such a thing an insult? That's a misunderstanding. I don't misunderstand at all. I consider it something to be proud of for our family. You don't know that.
Kyŏngae. (Voice trembling, unable to bear it) I don't know, I don't know! What is there to be proud of? What, huh— Is it such a pride for our family that I suffer all sorts of insults from countless men, endure all kinds of unpleasant things, and sell alcohol? When I think about losing my human value, becoming the plaything of many people, being cursed and pointed at by friends, I feel choked up <33> and my blood boils, yet you consider it an honor?
Haksu. (Somewhat excited) You still have that bourgeois feeling. Whose wife are you? A worker's wife—. And a Korean. A Korean who receives xx and xx and ridicule. You are like that, and so am I. Not just us two, but all the people of Korea are like that—. In that situation, who has the courage to curse? Who is there to point fingers? When the entire landmass called Korea is receiving xx and xx and xx, wouldn't we Koreans receive it too? Are you more upset about that? Is an individual's disgrace more infuriating than the insult to all Koreans?
Kyŏngae. Just talk about individuals. Korea exists because I exist. When I'm hungry, Korea doesn't give me food, does it?
Haksu. That's right. Korea doesn't give food. Therefore, don't we always curse Korea and secretly shed tears? Whose fault is that? It's the fault of people. Don't you know? With such power that oppresses the weak, don't the rich oppress the poor— the ruling xx oppress the ruled xx, capitalists oppress the xxx— without mercy, with xx and xx and xx? What do you think of that? How do you see the masses groaning beneath it?
Kyŏngae. I don't know much about such things.
Haksu. You are a pitiful human being. No, more pitiful than you, I think, is the school that claims to have taught you. The school taught you falsehoods, nurtured vanity, and made you dream of a dirty paradise. If people in the world spit at a beggar, you are the type of person who would also spit at that beggar. Right?
Kyŏngae. (Excited) What are you talking about? In what way am I vain, false, or dreaming of paradise? Don't look down on people so cheaply! Look at the kind of person I associate with, like you, and then speak. I don't dream of vanity.
Haksu. No, that's a lie—
Kyŏngae. (Crying loudly) What's a lie? Tell me!
Haksu. (Calmly) You know better than I can say.
Kyŏngae. I don't know! I don't know!
Haksu. Don't know? Really don't know?
Kyŏngae. Truly, I don't know. <34>
Haksu. Then I'll tell you—. What does your mother do?
Kyŏngae. Are you asking because you don't know?
Haksu. I know, but I want to hear it from you once more.
Kyŏngae. She sells alcohol, what else?
Haksu. That's right. I was worried you might lie, but thank you for telling the truth. Then, what kind of person am I?
Kyŏngae. You're a worker, what else?
Haksu. Correct. Isn't there another occupation?
Kyŏngae. You come in the evening and sell alcohol, what else is there?
Haksu. What do you do?
Kyŏngae. ...(No answer)
Haksu. What do you do?
Kyŏngae. Me? I do housework—
Haksu. What kind of work do you do? Cooking? Sewing? What kind of work? Don't you know that your elderly mother gets up at four in the morning, suffers abuse from countless men, hears things she should and shouldn't hear, endures insults, and spends her days like that?
Kyŏngae. (Bows her head)
Haksu. As for me, your husband, it's the same. After having my blood sucked by the capitalist all day, I come home and see your elderly mother flushed from dealing with drunk people. More than anything, human compassion troubles me first, which is why I end up sitting in the tavern. When I sit there, my hot, boiling blood turns cold as ice. But through this, I merely weep inwardly with the many people who speak of the miserable human life. Why don't you people understand that? But for you to say you don't know is a fatal blow to me. It feels like a great humiliation. And yet, you, whom I sadly know as a woman who desires the bourgeois life, yearns for a piano, enjoys luxury—instead of guiding such women onto a good path, you cry because you receive insults from them, keeping it bottled up in your heart? You are a so-called modern new woman who knows letters. You should be ashamed of yourself. What is shameful, what is insulting? If my friends found out you were crying about this, it would be no small insult to me as your husband—. Understand?
Kyŏngae. ...(No answer) <35>
Haksu. Modern women think their job is just to wear high-heeled shoes, hike up their skirts to their calves, wear long *chŏgori* jackets, put on glasses, carry a book bag, and nod their heads. And playing the piano or reading sweet romance novels is not what being a new woman is about. To become a truly new woman-like new woman, one must shed all masks completely. Including misplaced dignity. Do you understand?
Kyŏngae. (Lifts her head slightly)
Haksu. Does what I'm saying sound like mere chatter to your ears? Did you think everything I've said so far was just idle talk?
Kyŏngae. (Face flushing) Forgive me.
Haksu. What is there to forgive?
Kyŏngae. Forgive what I did. I try to endure everything, but it just doesn't work out that way, what can I do? When you're not here, Mother complains at me. Why wouldn't I get upset? Is that all...
Haksu. What else?
Kyŏngae. Today, I experienced something truly bizarre.
Haksu. What...?
Kyŏngae. Someone came all the way from Inch'ŏn. To see me (As if stunned) And then they just wouldn't leave, clinging, saying they came looking for me holding my photo to their chest, saying they brought fifty won in monthly savings to have some fun, and talking all sorts of nonsense.
Haksu. So what did you do?
Kyŏngae. What could I do? Mother raised a huge fuss, calling him crazy, calling him sick, and after making a scene for a while, she sent him away.
Haksu. A pitiful human being. That's what you call a pitiful human being. No different at all from those who curse you and point fingers. (Changes tone) Humans are dirty. Why are humans so hideous? Ah! I want to cry... And I want to leave Korea (Bows his head)
Kyŏngae. Hey. Why are you being like this? Huh?
Haksu. Kyŏngae...
Kyŏngae. Yes.
Haksu. You suffered a great insult because of the newspaper, right?
Kyŏngae. ...
Haksu. Speak. Huh? Even while sucking your parents' blood, you grew up preciously, and this is the first time you've suffered such an insult, right? <36>
Kyŏngae. Yes—
Haksu. Isn't it infuriating?
Kyŏngae. It's embarrassing—
Haksu. You love your husband, right?
Kyŏngae. Yes.
Haksu. Do you know how your husband or mother feel, sitting in the tavern, holding liquor bowls and pouring drinks? Isn't that an insult?
Kyŏngae. Still, men and women are different, aren't they?
Haksu. Ten years ago, twenty years ago, perhaps they were different. But today, that's probably not the case anymore? When women wore *changot* overcoats and skirts, perhaps they were different. The era when women knew nothing beyond the gate and lived only on what their husbands brought home is gone. The people aren't the people of that time either. They are new women who talk a good game about this and that. It's a time when freedom is loudly proclaimed. It's probably not a time to distinguish between men and women either. In times like these, distinguishing between sexes makes one even weaker.
Kyŏngae. Even if you call me weak, I can't help it. No matter what, I don't have the courage to sell alcohol. I don't want to do it either.
Haksu. (Laughs) Ha-ha- And you don't think anything of your husband or mother selling it?
Kyŏngae. That's why I told Mother today that we should stop this damned business. If we stop, it won't be an issue anymore, right? I'm truly too ashamed to live like this.
(Pause)
Haksu. (Head bowed, then looks up) Kyŏngae! (Loudly)
Kyŏngae. Yes?
Haksu. I am going to start a revolution!
Kyŏngae. A revolution? What revolution—
Haksu. (Excited) My revolution against you!
Kyŏngae. (Puzzled) What is it? What do you mean, your revolution against me?
Haksu. You don't understand?
Kyŏngae. No...
Haksu. For me to kill my individuality and trample my love while loving you is something my conscience cannot bear, and it's also foolish. A marriage shouldn't be momentary. If it's not eternal— But I realize now that it cannot be eternal. No, it's too regrettable to linger here even a little longer, <37> so I'm leaving. A worker needs a woman fit to be a worker's wife. A wife like you, with the body of a worker's wife but the heart of a bourgeois, always dreaming of glory in her heart— I cannot trust and live with such a wife. (Stands up abruptly, puts on his coat)
(Pause. Kyŏngae's crying is heard)
Mother. (Entering) What's all this commotion? Huh?
Kyŏngae. (Crying) Mother, he says he's leaving.
Mother. What? What's wrong? Leaving? Where— Leaving our house, you mean. Do as you please—. Everyone leave. I'll guard this house alone. You leave too.
Kyŏngae. (Shocked) Mother, what are you saying?
Mother. Why? I'll live like this with my deaf son. You two leave.
Kyŏngae. I can't leave you, Mother. Not ever.
Mother. What will you do when I die—. I could die tonight.
Kyŏngae. What? Why are you saying such frightening things, Mother—. (Cries)
Mother. I am not human. Until I die, I won't escape being the servant of countless men. So wouldn't it be right to just die?
(Hey, selling liquor—. Heard from outside)
Mother. No liquor—
(No liquor? Must be a *yangban* liquor seller then—. Also heard from outside)
Mother. Aigoo, that awful noise—.
-Pause-
Haksu. (Dressed, coming out) Goodbye to you both—.
Kyŏngae. (Grabbing Haksu's clothes) Where are you going?
Haksu. I'm going without a destination.
Kyŏngae. Who are you going with?
Haksu. I'm going alone—.
Kyŏngae. What about your wife?
Haksu. I don't have a wife—.
Kyŏngae. You don't?
Haksu. No—.
Kyŏngae. Then what happens to Kyŏngae?
Haksu. If thoughts and principles don't align, no one can <38> stay together. However, if, in the future, that person named Kyŏngae abandons her aristocratic nature and traditional customs, I will gladly welcome her.
Mother. What is all that talk? (To Kyŏngae) Hey, what does that mean—. Huh?

**Summary**

The play "Fight" by Kim Yŏngp'al depicts the intense emotional and ideological conflict within a Korean family in 1926. Kyŏngae is shamed by a false newspaper report claiming she sells alcohol, an occupation held by her mother out of necessity. While her friend reflects societal judgment, her husband Haksu, a worker, dismisses her shame as bourgeois sensitivity. He argues her personal humiliation pales compared to the collective suffering of Koreans under colonial rule and class exploitation, criticizing her education for instilling false values. The conflict escalates until Haksu declares he is leaving Kyŏngae for a "revolution" against her mindset and for the broader struggle, seeking a wife aligned with working-class consciousness. In the final moment, after Haksu leaves, Kyŏngae responds forcefully to a customer demanding liquor, suggesting a potential shift or acceptance of her reality.

Historically, the play is revealing for its raw depiction of social stratification and nascent class consciousness in colonial Korea. The tension between Kyŏngae's concern for individual reputation (influenced by modern education and bourgeois values) and Haksu's focus on collective, systemic oppression (proletarian and anti-colonial sentiment) vividly illustrates the ideological currents of the time. Haksu's critique of education fostering "vanity" and detachment from the people's reality, and his framing of the family's struggles within the larger context of colonial exploitation and capitalism ("blood sucked by the capitalist," "Korea doesn't give food"), would be of great interest to historians studying social dynamics, intellectual trends, and resistance narratives during the Japanese colonial period. The censorship marks ('xx') also hint at the political sensitivities of directly naming oppressors or certain concepts.
