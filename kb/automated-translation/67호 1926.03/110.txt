Article Title:	退步乎? 進步乎? 平安道地方의 一部人心 (Regression? Progress? The Sentiments of Some People in P'yŏngan Province)

Regression? Progress? The Sentiments of Some People in P'yŏngan Province
						Kijŏn

Brother Ch'unp'a. How is the editing for the March issue of *Kaebyŏk* coming along? I have carefully read every letter you sent from Sŏnch'ŏn and Kusŏng, but unfortunately, I still haven't been able to write the one essay and one travelogue you requested.

Brother Ch'unp'a. As you well know, I am more cautious than anyone else. Even when I was usually at the company writing, it was my habit to mull over a single issue for at least a week. How much more difficult it is to attempt writing while traveling to various places and meeting all sorts of people? My heart is willing every day, but what can I do when it's practically impossible? As they say, "Any road leads to Seoul," so I will simply try my best, in one way or another, for our company's work and for the principles and assertions our company presents. Please do not reproach me excessively.

Brother Ch'unp'a. Today is February 16th. It has already been over 30 days since I left the head office. I have visited P'yŏngyang, Anju, Pakch'ŏn, Chŏngju, Sŏnch'ŏn, Kusŏng, Sakchu, and other places, and arrived here in Kwaksan just the other day. Brother Yare is staying here for the local lecture meeting, so I was happy to meet him. I plan to rest here for a few days, partly to take a break from my rather disorganized itinerary, attend the lecture meeting, and then visit Sŏnch'ŏn, Kusŏng, Ch'ŏlsan, Yongch'ŏn, and Ŭiju again before returning to the company around the beginning of March.

Brother Ch'unp'a. On this trip, I have met more types of people than ever before. Landlords, merchants, officials, teachers, pastors, elders, doctors, lawyers, and others—anyone who is considered somebody in their respective places, in other words, <81> the ruling class of current society—I have met them all. And I didn't just meet them for idle talk; I met them concerning what is considered the most practical matter in this world: money. It was truly fascinating. It felt like glimpsing the inner workings of this world for the first time, and I felt I saw deep into the hearts and minds of the so-called propertied, the so-called powerful, the so-called influential (up to the present day). Thus, I realized anew on this trip that one only truly understands the world by confronting it practically, and especially by grappling with practical matters of profit and loss.

Brother Ch'unp'a. If I say this, with your impatient nature, you'll likely demand an immediate explanation, asking, "Well, what is it that you realized? Spit it out!" However, this is a secret not to be leaked (玄機勿漏) and cannot be spoken of on this page. I will defer everything until we meet face-to-face upon my return. For now, I will jot down a few miscellaneous stories I've heard, seen, and experienced for you to read when you're bored. How about that?

Prison Entrance Job Examination

This is something I heard in P'yŏngyang. When I visited P'yŏngyang, a comrade living near the prison outside the West Gate invited me for dinner. According to what this comrade said at the time:
Recently, a strange saying has become popular among the people living near the prison. Whenever they see prisoners awaiting trial being transferred from the police station through the prosecutor's office to this prison, everyone says, "Those people are coming to take the test again. I wonder if they'll pass..." and so on.
In other words, they are saying that those people, having no other choice, are coming through the police station and the prosecutor's office to take the prison job entrance examination, and wondering if they will fortunately pass or not. When you think about it, how frightening and horrifying a saying it is. Are not the propertyless, who have been endlessly exploited by the wealthy and powerful gentlemen, now finally turning towards the prison to start a job-seeking campaign?

The Sentiments of Some People in P'yŏngan Province Reverted to Around 1916-1917

There was an incident where a clerk named Yang working at the Anju Court allegedly engaged in illegal acquisition during the auction of some real estate. This eventually became an issue in the Anju media circles, and the Kwansŏ Reporters' Group investigated the <82> circumstances surrounding the facts. What was shocking among the findings was that he frequented restaurants almost every other day, and his monthly entertainment expenses always exceeded his entire monthly salary. So, what skill did he possess to spend more than his entire salary each month? Needless to say, it must be because the local people who associated with him and catered to him supported him financially. More than simply supporting him, it can be seen that they drew him out and instigated such entertainment. This is just one example involving an official at a judicial office in the Anju region, but why limit it to Anju, and why only judicial officials? Towards the border regions, especially concerning police officials, there will undoubtedly be even more severe cases.

Brother Ch'unp'a. That's why, recently, the arrogant and threatening attitude of officials towards the people, and the sycophantic and submissive behavior of the people towards officials, are truly unbearable to witness. Because the region is near the border and considering the respective levels of the people involved, one might say it's unavoidable to some extent, but I truly did not expect things to deteriorate to such a wretched state. It feels exactly like a regression to the time around 1916-1917 (Taishō 5-6). If you go into the remote mountain villages, even a town center might be just a small village of 300-400 households, but among the entire population of those 300-400 households, you can hardly find a single person with a sound mind. Crushed and trampled by official power and ignorance, they are merely hurtling down the path of daily degradation like slaves, like machines. If we can overlook this, what can we not overlook?

San, Chusa, Na-ri

Wherever you go, whoever you meet, people say "San," "Chusa," and "Na-ri." What's particularly strange is the use of two or three honorifics together, like "So-and-so Chusa-na-ri," "So-and-so Constable-na-ri," or "Section Chief Na-ri." Honestly, "Chusa" is "Chusa," and "Section Chief" is "Section Chief"; what need is there to add "Na-ri" or anything else? Whether it's the "Na-ri" gentlemen who receive such respect and act self-important, or the common folk who feel secure by offering such flattery, they are all in the same boat, so there's no need to argue about who is right or wrong. But isn't it too vulgar for a person's life to fall into such an atmosphere?
And why is Japanese so prevalent in the border regions? Even sitting inside a car, it's Japanese; go to a noodle shop, it's Japanese. It seems they think some great disaster will happen <83> if they don't speak Japanese right away, or that some great advantage will come if they only speak Japanese. Their swaggering while chattering amongst themselves in broken Japanese is truly unbearable to watch.

There's Also This Rumor

Brother Ch'unp'a. Coming here, one hears all sorts of strange news. The current Director of Internal Affairs in the Government-General, Ikuta (生田), is known for his skill in dealing ambiguously between officials and the public. While he was the Governor of North P'yŏngan Province, the methods and efforts he used to placate the so-called influential figures of North P'yŏngan were truly beyond our imagination. He often used small concessions to appease the straightforward and simple-minded patriot gentlemen, even going so far as to allegedly urge Mr. X of Chŏngju and Mr. X of Sŏnch'ŏn to accept appointments as Provincial Councilors (道評議員). Although one cannot determine the truth based solely on hearsay, just hearing the rumor itself, how detestable and alarming it is!

Where is the Spirit of Those Days Now?

Brother Ch'unp'a. As you might recall, during the past Yungheui era (1907-1910), the Northwest was the most enlightened region in all of Korea. And within the Northwest, wasn't P'yŏngan Province, centered around P'yŏngyang's Taesŏng School, the most outstanding not just in literary development, but also in terms of thought and culture? Yet, looking at it today, this region seems the most backward and listless in all of Korea. Is this also some law of cause and effect, of ebb and flow, operating in the interim?
Indeed. It seems there must be some cause and effect at play here. Those people who were prominent figures back then, or the types who followed them, seem to have somehow lost their vibrant spirit and passionate emotions of those days. Under banners like "Cultural Movement" or "Cultivating Real Strength," which are as vague as the character '曰' on deerskin (鹿皮에 曰字가튼 標榜), they seem to be comfortably resting, acting like accomplished elders who have achieved success and retired (功成身退). While not all of them are like this, the majority seem to believe that their assertions have been somewhat realized just because quite a few schools have been established in Korea since the March First Movement (己未年), and talk of Japan-Korea assimilation (日鮮同化) has shifted to Japan-Korea harmony (日鮮融和). They seem to think that Koreans can now get by if they just focus on industrial education and build up wealth. As a result, they unconsciously come to affirm the current society and system, so that the intellectuals of yesterday become the obstinate figures of today, and the patriots of yesterday become the poseurs of today. <84>

Ignorance is the Disease, Knowledge is the Disease; Property and Status are the Disease

What is the main cause of all this? It's all due to ignorance. Firstly, it stems from not knowing how the upper structure of the Japanese Empire itself has transformed between 10 years ago and 10 years later. Consequently, it stems from not knowing how Japan's own policies towards its colonies have changed between 10 years ago and 10 years later. They fail to understand that the shift from the arbitrary, high-handed, bureaucratic, militaristic, feudalistic politics of Tera'uchi (寺內) to the conciliatory, social policy-oriented, merchant-style (町人式) politics of Saitō (齋藤) was caused less by the Korean Manse Movement of the March First year (己未年) and more by the extraordinary changes in Japanese policy resulting from the rapid development of Japanese capitalism after the Great European War. In other words, until the Great European War, the central power in Japanese politics lay with bureaucrats and militarists, but after the war, the central power rapidly shifted to the capitalist cliques (資本閥). Simultaneously, they failed to recognize the historical turning point where the old-style, gentry-like, feudalistic policies inevitably had to change to new, merchant-style, capitalist policies. Lacking this recognition, their minds fail to grasp how Japan's previous policies for ruling Korea differ from today's, and consequently, they are unable to discern what path Koreans should have taken in the past versus what path they should take now. This is the disease born from this inability to observe.
In short, it's all due to ignorance. Alternatively, it's because the little knowledge they acquired in the past actually hinders their ability to learn what needs to be known today. Furthermore, the small amount of property, fame, or status gained in the meantime causes them to stagnate, become resigned, and degenerate. I don't know about other places, but coming into the Western Provinces (Sŏdo), the group that most typically possesses and clings to this ignorance, this degeneration, this disease, are those holding the central power in the Christian Church. That is, those who have already secured their own houses and land, gained some renown, and have now become elders, deacons, youth association presidents, or pastors, wielding considerable influence within their church and even in the local society at large.
Brother Pak, isn't the way of this society truly strange? It was true for Korea as a whole, but especially here in our Western Provinces (Sŏdo), <85> wasn't the contribution of the Christian Church—or rather, the many seniors who emerged through the church—in enlightening us and showing us the path of national progress indeed great? However, as mentioned above, coming to today, 10 years later, they have somehow ended up embracing ignorance and baseness (卑㥘), attempting to go against the tide of the times. How regrettable and worrisome this is! For those already hardened and sunken in thought, age, and spirit, it's too late to say anything. But one can only hope that the many progressive youths within this church will quickly perceive clearly the turning wheels of this society and this era, and rise up resolutely with new observations and new principles.
Brother Pak, one encouraging phenomenon I've seen since entering North P'yŏngan Province north of Chŏngju is the flourishing of peasant night schools (農民夜學) everywhere, and seeing the new seeds of the peasant movement germinating (肧胎) there. This trend (風力) is particularly strong in places like Chŏngju and Kwaksan, as well as Ŭiju. Overjoyed, I visited several rural night schools here in Kwaksan, and I cannot describe how happy I was. Generally, there's a regrettable lack of sufficient or adequate leaders, but it's undeniably a welcome phenomenon for now.
Isn't it immensely fortunate for our future that the consciousness of the peasants—who lack food and clothing and only suffer abuse and exploitation—is being properly awakened?
Brother Pak, looking at the local news section of a newspaper today, it seems a Proletariat Comrades Association (푸로同志會) has been formed even within the old castle walls of Ŭiju. If the new uprising (舊起) of the border youth in Sinŭiju and Ŭiju becomes the vanguard bringing new consciousness and new vitality to our compatriots in this region, how wonderful would that be!
Brother Pak, what started as a few lines in lieu of a letter has become somewhat lengthy. But now, truly, my seat is becoming hurried (i.e., I must go). I want to write more but cannot. Let's talk freely when we meet in about ten days.
Evening of February 17th <86>

**Summary**

The author, Kijŏn, writes to his colleague Ch'unp'a about his observations during a trip through P'yŏngan Province in early 1926. He expresses deep concern over what he perceives as a societal regression, noting increased subservience among the populace towards Japanese officials, reminiscent of the pre-1919 era. Kijŏn laments the loss of revolutionary spirit among the older generation of activists, particularly Christian leaders, whom he criticizes for becoming complacent, ignorant of the changing nature of Japanese colonial policy (shifting from overt force to cultural/economic co-option), and focused on personal status and wealth. He contrasts this perceived decline with hopeful signs like the rise of peasant night schools and socialist groups, suggesting these represent a potential new wave of consciousness and resistance. From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for its contemporary critique of the accommodationist tendencies within certain segments of the Korean elite under Japanese rule, its analysis of the shift in colonial governance tactics post-1919, and its documentation of the nascent peasant and socialist movements in a specific region, offering insights into the complex social dynamics and evolving resistance strategies during the colonial period.

無識이 病, 有識이 病이요 有産有地位가 病이라

이것이 大軆 무슨 탓이냐하면 모다 - 들 無識한 탓임니다. 第一로 日本帝國自軆의 上部搆造가 最近 10年前과 10年 後에 잇서 엇더케 變轉되엿는가를 알지 못하는 그 탓이며 따라서 日本自軆植民地에 對한 政策이 10年 前과 10年 後에 잇서 엇더케 變幻되는 것을 알지 못하는 탓에 생기는 일임니다. 武斷的이오 高壓的이던 官僚式이오 軍閥式이오 封建式이던 寺內의 政治가 가고 懷柔的이오 社會政策的이오 町人式인 齋藤의 政治가 代한 것은 己未年의 朝鮮人萬歲運動에 依하얏다 함보다도 歐洲大戰後 日本資本主義의 急遽發展에 依한 日本政策의 非常的變動에 말미암인 것을 모르는 것임니다. 다시 말하면 歐洲大戰까지 日本政治의 中心勢力은 官僚와 軍閥에 잇섯스나 大戰以後부터의 中心勢力은 急遽하게도 資本閥에게로 移轉되는 同時에 兩班式 封建式의 舊式政策은 스스로 町人式 資本家式의 新政策으로 變하지 안을 수 업섯는 歷史的變機를 認識하지 못하엿스며 이것을 認識하지 못하는 그 머리는 스스로 日本帝國의 在來의 朝鮮統治策과 今日의 朝鮮統治策이 如何히 變動된 것을 意識하지 못하며 따라서 在來의 朝鮮사람으로서의 取할 길은 이러하엿스나 이제부터 朝鮮사람의 取할 길 나아갈 길은 엇더엇더 하다는 것을 觀察하지 못함에서 생긴 病이라고 할 수 밧게 업슴니다.

朴兄 平安北道의 定州以北에 드러와 한가지 깃분 現象을 보는 것은 各處에 農民夜學이 盛行하며 거긔에 農民運動의 새 씨가 肧胎하고 잇슴을 보는 그것임니다. 定州 郭山을 爲始하야 義州 等地에 이 風力이 大端함니다. 너무 반가운 김에 나도 이곳 郭山의 農村夜學을 몃곳 求景한 일이 잇섯는데 엇지 깃벗넌지 모름니다. 大槪로 指導者가 不足하고 또 不及되여서 恨되는 點이 업지 안으나 爲先 깃분 現像이라고 아니할 수 업슴니다.
農民 못먹고 못입고 오직 虐待와 搾取만을 當하는 그들의 意識이 바로 박히게 된다함은 우리의 압길을 爲하야 얼마나 多幸한 일임니까.
朴兄 오늘 엇던 新間의 地方欄을 보니 이 地方의 舊義州城內에도 푸로同志會가 생겻다 함니다. 新義州 義州의 國境靑年들의 새로운 舊起 이것이 이 地方同胞에게 새로운 意識 새로운 元氣를 주는 先驅가 된다하면 얼마나 조흔 일이겟소.
朴兄 편지 삼아 몃줄을 적으려 한 것이 조곰 길어짐니다. 그러나 이제는 正말 座席이 奔走해 짐니다. 더 쓰고 십허도 쓸 수가 업슴니다. 限十餘日後에 시원히 對해서 니야기 합시다.
2月 17日 夕＜86＞
