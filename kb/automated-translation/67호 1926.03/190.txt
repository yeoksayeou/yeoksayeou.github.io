기사제목	短詩六篇 (Six Short Poems)
필자	巴人
기사형태	시 (Poetry)

短詩六篇
						巴人

石手匠 (Stonemason)

All day chipping, how much do you chip away?
Amidst the tapping sound, only the eyebrows turn white.
Still, from dawn, the stonemason sits, carving the names of the dead.
How many tens of thousands of gravestones have you made, and how many thousands of epitaphs have you crafted?
Indeed, how many stonemasons like you are there in this world!

밤불 (Night Lamp)

Flickering on and off, a pit-dwelling on the hillside swaying in the night lamplight.<116>
The sparkle of tears from the wife crying, missing her husband sent away due to a tenant farmer dispute.
Are hundreds of Lenins coming and going within that lamp?

光州旅途中- (On the Road to Kwangju—)

Train arrival! Midday, two, three…
Old man, embrace your senses and jump into the coffin!
Youth, bury chunks of ice in your heart and bind your limbs to stakes!
Sprinkle ashes in the alleys and dress the sky in mourning clothes.
What, everyone dislikes it? That’s why you all become corpses before you die.

가마 (Cauldron)

The cauldron taken by the tax collector, the cauldron where tofu was made every dawn.
The cauldron that looked as blessed as my wife’s breast.
Where has it gone now, to which blacksmith’s shop? In dreams, I search for tofu and cry.

景福宮 (Kyŏngbok Palace)

On the faded *tanch’ŏng* paint, a single crow calls forth the old days.
Still, it is spring, and vines embrace the beams of Kyŏnghoeru Pavilion.

祭文 (Memorial Address)

Please accept this gift, offered with devotion.<117>
The gift of tens of thousands of beggars, asleep with bent backs on the stone steps.
It is better than the pig for the mountain sacrifice, struggling to live on the brushwood pile.
Please accept it quickly, this gift of Civilization!<118>
<116-118>

**Summary**

This collection of six short poems by "P'ain" depicts vignettes of suffering, loss, and decay in colonial Korea. The poems touch upon the toil of laborers (stonemason), the hardship of tenant farmers and their families (night lamp), societal despair and apathy (on the road to Kwangju), poverty and dispossession (cauldron), the faded glory of the past contrasted with nature's endurance (Kyŏngbok Palace), and a bitter critique of modern "civilization" that ignores the plight of the destitute (memorial address). Historians might find the poems valuable for their raw portrayal of the social conditions and disillusionment under Japanese rule, the subtle hints of resistance or revolutionary thought (Lenin reference), and the commentary on the decline of traditional symbols alongside imposed modernity. The publication date (March 1st) adds another layer of potential political undertone.
