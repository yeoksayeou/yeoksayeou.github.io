April's Past and Present

1st Dong-A Ilbo founded (Last year, Kyŏngsin year [1920])

2nd French politician "Mirabeau" passed away (1791)

4th Great Master Sŏsan, Im Hyujŏng, entered nirvana at Myohyangsan (Kapchin year during King Sŏnjo's reign [1604])

5th Ch'ŏndogyo Founder Ch'oe Cheu established the Way through Great Enlightenment (Kyŏngsin year during King Ch'ŏljong's reign [1860])

6th American forefather "Washington" was chosen as the first President (1789)

8th Shakyamuni Buddha was born in Central India (2947 years ago)
Same day Ch'ŏndogyo's Third Leader Son Pyŏnghŭi was born (Sinyu year during King Ch'ŏljong's reign [1861])

9th English man of letters "Bacon" passed away (1626)

10th Japan's Katsura Tarō (Prime Minister at the time), Komura Jutarō (Foreign Minister at the time), together with Itō Hirobumi (Korean Resident-General at the time), first plotted the annexation of Korea (Meiji 42 [1909])

11th Confucius passed away (41st year of King Jing of Zhou [479 BCE])

14th The remains of Mr. Kim Okkyun, assassinated in Shanghai - arrived at Yanghwajin (Kap'o year during King Kojong's reign [1894])

15th US President "Lincoln" assassinated (1865)

19th English poet "Byron" passed away (1824)

23rd English playwright "Shakespeare" born (1616)

27th Greek sage "Socrates" killed by poison (Four hundred years before the Common Era [c. 400 BCE])
Same day American telegraph inventor "Morse" born (1791) <61>

**Summary**

This article from the April 1921 issue of *Gaebyeok* lists significant historical events associated with the month of April. It covers a wide range, including the births and deaths of major religious figures (Buddha, Ch'oe Cheu, Son Pyŏnghŭi, Sŏsan Taesa, Confucius, Socrates), political leaders (Mirabeau, Washington, Lincoln), cultural icons (Bacon, Byron, Shakespeare), inventors (Morse), and key events in Korean history (Dong-A Ilbo founding, Kim Okkyun's remains returning, the plotting of Korea's annexation by Japanese officials). From a colonial history perspective, the juxtaposition of Korean national figures (Ch'oe Cheu, Son Pyŏnghŭi, Sŏsan Taesa, Kim Okkyun) and events (Dong-A Ilbo) with the stark mention of Japanese officials (Itō, Katsura, Komura) conspiring for Korea's annexation is particularly revealing. It subtly places Korean historical significance alongside world history while pointedly reminding readers of the recent colonial subjugation orchestrated by named Japanese individuals.
