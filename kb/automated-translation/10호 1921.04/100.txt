기사제목	暫間·鐵拳에 녹은 슬엇다 (Rust Has Settled on the Fleeting Iron Fist)
필자	鄭昌先
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir/Recollection)

暫間·鐵拳에 녹은 슬엇다
						鄭昌先
I confess. I am a dull and slow underachiever. However, I also confess and declare that I am a naive, old-fashioned new child with one thing firmly embedded in my brain: a sense of responsibility that cannot hide even a bit when fulfilling the duties and roles assigned to me, and a conscience that cannot yield even an inch or a fraction when it comes to the concept of duty. Thus, last month, I received a manuscript request from my dear elder brother Pak Ch’unp’a (朴春坡). At that time, hearing his suggestion that it would be good to write about my current situation of studying while working hard, I forced myself to write a manuscript titled "My New Movement Standing at a Transitional Period." This time again, I intended to continue it, but my body is currently lying down due to a foot injury, and it has already been 6 or 7 days. Therefore, continuing that is rather difficult. So, this time, I intend to record the hardships of my half-year journey to Japan (東渡半個星霜) and my current situation, offering a few words, though insufficient and perhaps rambling, to our like-minded youth, our deeply sympathetic companions. This is the first piece of writing I offer, recording my living conditions and all the impressions received psychologically, to my brothers and sisters in our homeland, whether known or unknown, related or unrelated.

As I lie alone now under the cold lamp by the snowy window, thinking of the past and reflecting on the present, I have infinite feelings and sorrow. Ah! I am lonely (孤獨). Ah! I recall my hardships from years ago. Whether it was walking 5,000 *li* across Siberia (西比亞 半萬里), or traversing the vast deserts (沙漠萬里) of South and North Manchuria (南北滿洲) among different people, languages, regions, and lands, I was always alone. Whether crossing mountains and waters as the cold moon set behind the western hills, or dragging a broken short staff while stroking my hungry stomach in a foreign land devoid of people and smoke on a thousand-*li* journey, or entering unattended houses without reason to ask shamelessly for food and lodging, or wandering here and there with sleeves dangling in the evening sunset smoke, wearing only thin clothes and without a place to stay, or opening my mouth trying to say "rice" (밥) while drawing a picture of the word when facing foreigners<65> whose language I couldn't speak and whose minds I didn't know, or suffering empty-handed (赤手空拳) in a land ten thousand *li* overseas without food or clothes, or barely sustaining this tenacious life that couldn't die amidst persecution (逼害) and oppression (壓迫), ah! I! I! I cannot count how many times I tore at my young chest, how many times I ripped it open. Whether sleeping or lying down, I am always lonely (孤寂). I am always sighing. I was always pessimistic and resentful. My heart, not yet fully grown, felt hot as if filled with burning embers, and like a lone sail tossed on the great waves of the vast sea, my churning heart cannot fathom how many times it felt this body could not die. However, I always had a lover I cherished. A lover I couldn't bear to be without even for a moment. I cannot forget that lover's face even in dreams. When I press my forehead against their warm chest, all the trampling and turmoil of my entire life melts away, and instead, with a smile, I feel like dancing, singing, stamping my feet, and shouting alone with joy. This is not a material desire like glory (榮華), nor is it a craving for other honors. It was simply a lover called the purely spiritual life, a determination to live according to the genuine truth (眞理). I was resolved not to live if it wasn't the truth. The ultimate desire to embrace even death if the truth were death—this impulse became the lifelong phrase that would define my entire existence. Thus, all my actions involved enduring pains others did not, receiving stimuli others did not, feeling worries others did not, and experiencing sorrows others did not.

Ah! Now, whether I look up at the sky or down at the earth, or survey the world in all four directions, I am always lonely (孤寂) and desolate. I have no comrades who know me, no close friends who understand me. I have no parents, brothers, or sisters. The world is wide, but I have no home; people are many, but I am alone.
Ah! My life! Ah! My fate! Ah! My resentment. Half a life of many fortunes and many hardships—I have no friend with whom to discuss it, no sibling with whom to read facing each other across a table. Truly, I cannot speak of the resentment that reaches the ninth heaven. However, the lover I mentioned earlier—that is my ideal close friend, my companion in loneliness (孤寂). And so, I follow that lover, searching endlessly through the vast heavens and earth, covering a thousand or ten thousand *li*. That is why last autumn, I fearlessly crossed the rough waves of the Hyŏnhae Sea (玄海灘 洪濤) and am now trying anew to decipher the heavens and earth, seeking my beloved lover—Truth.

When crossing the Hyŏnhae Sea (玄海灘), aboard the deck of the Iki Maru (壹岐丸) floating off Japan (扶桑), I made a vow in my heart. "Ah! You must become the cleanest and most perfect elite soldier (精兵). Become a true elite soldier who knows their duty (職分) and upholds their responsibility.<66> The world you are going to is your adversary, the object of battle. Become an elite soldier who conquers that world. Record that stepping even one pace back from the picket line (步哨線) is a failure greater than death. Greater than death is the suicide of conscience. Your conscience is like a burning fire, like boiling water. What you have determined with your conscience, do not abandon it even unto death. Where you are going, there will be wind and rain, pain, and sorrow. But vow that the determination overflowing in your chest today will remain until you achieve final victory in that eternal battlefield." Taking a deep breath, I cast my eyes upon the vast, boundless sea and looked out at the waves once more. Though the great ship sailed smoothly over a surface as flat as a polished rock without undulations, seeing it rise upon thousand-fathom waves and fall below hundred-fathom troughs made me feel that while it is easy to objectively view the path of our lives from a fixed goal, the actual practice involves such extreme bends and depths. Ah! The path I must tread—living in Tokyo (東京) for 7 or 8 years is easy to think about, easy to talk about, but I know there will be infinite pain and sorrow in the actual practice. This is the experience I gained from my walking journey through Siberia (西比利亞 徒步旅行) before. The ship, moving heedlessly over the chance waters, soon reached the Shimonoseki (下關) pier (埠頭). I boarded the train bound for Tokyo. After spending one night, I arrived at the longed-for Tokyo Station. But! But! My young self, empty-handed (赤手空拳), had nowhere to go, nowhere to be taken in. However, aided by the warm help of fellow traveler Mr. Mun (文君), I, who had come heading for Tokyo, passed through Tokyo and ended up lodging in a corner of a poor villager's house. But the date for entering school was only 2 or 3 days away. Thus, with the 13 *won* I had in hand—thinking I should at least enroll first and face the physical hardships with my body—I enrolled the next day in the first year of the Philosophy Department (哲學科) at Toyo University (東洋大學). However, from the next day, food was a worry, clothing was a worry. The immediate need was a tram ticket (電車券). What tram? Walking would suffice. When I had no tram fare, I began walking the 10-*li* distance. Enrolling in school without a single *chŏn* left, without even preparing a penny for food, was, even to my own mind, a childish act. It's not that I didn't know what hardship was, what eating was, or how difficult it was to rely on others for food, like some naive gesture. But my thought at that time was the determination to sacrifice anything for study (工夫)—so, from the day after enrollment, hunger wasn't that difficult, and walking 10 *li* wasn't that<67> painful. Someone else's food? I'd eat anyone's food, work, and that would be enough. If I worked, anything! If I could just eat, any work! If I just ate, I was determined only to study. However, worldly affairs, as I felt while gazing at the waves of the Hyŏnhae Sea, are not as smooth as ideals. No matter how much passion one has, it is an immutable fact that one cannot get by without eating, without clothing! Was it not a fact that I couldn't just eat the food earned by Mr. Mun's sweat and simply attend school? In just one month, I thought I had no choice but to leave Mr. Mun's kindness and inevitably earn my own keep through self-reliance (自手自足). Thus, I went to take up employment at a lonely dairy farm (牧場) tending cows in the woods of a remote village. At that time, I abandoned school and everything else; the biggest issue was the problem of livelihood, the most difficult issue was the problem of utilizing time. From childhood until today, I am someone whose youth has turned white over the grave problem of livelihood. Someone whose entire spirit has been burnt out. Someone accustomed to using non-existent time to read at night or dawn. Thus, my life until today had been one of constraint, one without enjoyment. Therefore, my thinking at that time leaned towards extreme materialism (唯物主義的 傾向): philosophy, literature, art, science—all learning has no place to exist apart from human life, and human life has no value as "human life" apart from freedom. I would not cultivate truth for truth's sake, learn art for art's sake, or learn scholarship for scholarship's sake; rather, I would learn philosophy for life, art for life, scholarship for life, but I would not live a valueless, unenjoyable, unfree life like in the past, losing freedom and the true value of life. I decided it would be better to quit school studies altogether and, within a free, independent, self-reliant (獨立, 自存的) life, record what I actually gained, making it my self-aware life, and record what I actually obtained, whether right or wrong, and sing my own song. This was because philosophy and all learning cannot exist apart from reality. And at that time, my respected fellow student, Brother Ch'ugang (秋江兄), said he would return home due to illness. But I absolutely opposed it inwardly. Saying I would not return home even if I died, I deeply contemplated the relationship between this life of mine and my spiritual life, trying to derive some complete critique from it. My thinking at that time was excessively materialistic (唯物的), heavily biased towards material tendencies—a natural result considering my past life and current straits. Thirsting for freedom for a long time, having my life restricted, focusing heavily on the problem of livelihood,<68> the problem of independence and self-reliance (獨立 自存 問題)—it's fine even if I don't study. What is study (工夫)? Isn't it the name for learning so that human life can become human life? If philosophy, religion, science, and all learning must be learned even if humans lose freedom and their innate self-reliant nature, then those theories are undoubtedly theories of the dead. If one first records and practices what one has learned and obtained in reality from a position of independence and self-reliance, that itself is a philosophical theory, a philosophical discourse. The incomplete, inexperienced argument that truth and learning exist elsewhere apart from reality is mere theory, not reality; it's empty fantasy, not fact. My most ardent, active philosophical view was the decision to go out into real society from today, record what I actually obtained, and practice what I actually learned, quitting school and studies and taking up real work. This was the sharp record obtained during two months of life in Tokyo. And so, when I went straight to that dairy shop, I went to take the job without saying anything to Mr. Mun, with whom I lodged, or to Mr. Chŏng (鄭君), whom I loved like a younger brother. Thus, for a day or two, I put in coal (石炭) and washed milk cans (牛乳통). There was absolutely no spare time there. Waking up at three in the morning, the work didn't allow me to straighten my back until nine at night. At that time, though young, I had been babbling about philosophy and logic for 6 or 7 years already; although I hadn't cultivated much, I acted dignified. When called "Ōi Ōno-kun" (「오이 大野君」), I would politely answer "Hai" (「하이」). This was because when I first entered the dairy farm, they told me to choose a Japanese name, so I asked to be called by the alias Ōno Kōzō (大野耕藏). However, I don't feel the slightest bit displeased. The owner's clerk is 21 years old. Two years younger than me. In terms of learning, though I haven't learned much, he's in a position where he should come to learn from me at my desk. Yet I call him "Danna-san" (「단나상」 - Master/Boss). He calls me "Ōno-kun" (「大野君」). Still, I don't feel the slightest aversion. And sometimes, when my back aches and my arms feel like falling off, the thought of quitting arises. But then, all the ideals I had thought of would end in bankruptcy. Therefore, to control that feeling, I write on the wall with white chalk: "Your flesh is the greatest devil that obliterates your spirit." Recording this, as I spend day after day, sleeping or waking, my thoughts, besides the problem of livelihood, are preoccupied with thinking about the lover mentioned in the previous chapter—Truth. One day, during a 20-minute break after breakfast, I went out into the fields and began reading a book. Thus, unaware of the passing time, my body resting languidly on the grass, I was engrossed in reading, apparently unaware that a swarm of flies (蠅) had gathered on my leg<69> and feasted. As I started to get up at the owner's call, I finally shooed the flies away. Flies that couldn't detach their mouths embedded in my flesh left my two legs "pitch black" (시컴언). Perhaps their bites became poisonous, or it developed into beriberi (脚氣). What would become of me, a novice trainee, being sick? From now on, I couldn't move even an inch, and I lost my appetite too. When the sun set in the evening, the dinner bell rang. Unable to go for dinner, and unable to eat even if I went, I tearfully lament the indifference of the setting sun. When the plaintive cries of the calves (犢聲) at the dairy farm pierce my eardrums, I always think of myself. If you cry, your mother is nearby, and there is someone to give you food. But it's useless for me to cry, useless to scream. Here, in this hellish place, without parents, brothers, or close friends, even if my body falls gravely ill, there are no close friends to look after me. What has happened? Is this what life is? Is this what being in a foreign land is? Is this what loneliness (孤寂) is? I sent a letter each to my close friends Mr. Mun and Mr. Chŏng. Upon receiving the letters, both replied asking me to visit as soon as I could walk. Thus, deciding to change jobs somehow, I waited for 4 or 5 days and managed to move my body to the tram stop, coming to my current address in Minami-Senju, Miwa (南十住三輪). All my close friends sympathized enough to shed tears of sorrow. They asked me to cook rice for the 6 or 7 people living together self-sufficiently. So, from that day, I changed occupations and started cooking rice. While continuing this job for about a month, the beriberi (脚氣) completely healed, and I studied German. But naturally, time was insufficient. Eventually, a newspaper shop needed a delivery person (配達夫). Thus, from that day, I held two jobs concurrently. Waking up at four in the morning, I cooked breakfast, boiled soup at 12 o'clock, and went out to deliver newspapers (新聞 配達) at three. At first, I felt an unfounded embarrassment, thinking others might be watching me. Delivering newspapers while running around without a hat, I kept my head bowed low. I delivered the newspapers respectfully. I even heard comments from newspaper readers that I was polite. After doing this for 1 or 2 months, the cooking became communal, and I only did the newspaper delivery. When we see newspaper delivery boys (新聞 配達夫) in our country, wearing bells and jangling along, wiggling their bottoms, we look down on them as quite vulgar (野卑). What enjoyment could there be in that, making them wiggle their bottoms as they go? Such low-class types (下等輩) aren't even human! But now that I've delivered newspapers for two months, I've become an even lower-class type; I want to wear a bell too, and when I carry the newspapers, I want to wiggle my bottom too. Look at this leaping figure! I must have truly become a low-class type. But I don't know myself. Since I do it with gusto (滋味), I run better and dance better than others, even joking around (弄談) quite a bit with the women of the poor households. This is what they call<70> "occupational adaptation" (職業化). They say I've now become an incorrigibly vulgar, low-class type. Now, when I put on the deliveryman's clothes and carry the newspapers, I don't feel awkward at all. I'm completely a newspaper guy (新聞쟁이). My speech, gait, and all actions have become those of a newspaper deliveryman (新聞配達夫) through and through, nothing more could be added. It's dangerous. The future of our youth—the number of newspaper deliverymen in Tokyo must count 30 or 40. Who among them was a newspaper guy from the start? Anyone would have felt embarrassed and awkward at first. But now, if they earn just 20 *won* a month, there are many cases where they spend it uselessly and that's that. I too now seem to have become a misguided artisan who jokes with the women of the poor households. Many students from our Chosŏn (條線) studying abroad here delivered newspapers. But among them, many drank alcohol. So I've seen them ruin their futures. Seeing this, I hesitated somewhat and was alarmed. But now, whether the world and I, and I and the world, have harmonized, I cannot know, but I continue on my path as before, joke with the women as before, and when I carry the newspapers, I wiggle my bottom with gusto. However, when I combined newspaper delivery and cooking, I had absolutely no spare time. But the determination I spoke of earlier has still not faded: Study (工夫)—it's fine even without that damn thing. Now that I have made my life free and independent, what other truth could there be? My extreme, passive thought that I could live my whole life with this assertion without dissatisfaction—even thinking about it now, I am definitely not ashamed. But life is life. Since life is life, it is a fact that one must live like a human being. If one is human, not just human but having become human, one must possess the entirety of human instinct before being called human. We are those who seek to become true humans (眞人), possessing the entirety of human instinct, seeking the definition and proper path of life.
So, is such a life truly a complete life possessing the principles of our entire instinctive nature? This is the primary question. "Human life" does not mean only me, "Chŏng Ch'angsŏn" (鄭昌先). It broadly signifies the 1.7 billion people spread across the world. Is it not a fact that complete truth called "human life" cannot be sought limited only to me, a newspaper deliveryman in a corner of Tokyo? Unless it is a universal principle residing throughout the entire universe and human life—human life and the universe—the universe as a whole, it definitely cannot be called complete truth. At this point, I inevitably had to destroy the entirety of the faith I had firmly believed in the past and the stage of my self-reliance, and take up the history of philosophy and ethics again. Thus, I came to advocate for the necessity of school again. And so, I was able to re-enroll at Toyo University (東洋大學), paying the tuition (月謝) with what I had earned from two months of labor,<71> and continue my schooling. However, on the other hand, I also have a tendency to want to assert the former position. Having established the assertion that I will not return to my homeland as an unfinished product before completing my studies properly, it is not limited to this year or next year, nor the year after next or the one after that. Looking at it for at least 10 years, I think I will have to live in Tokyo for a long time.

Naturally, it is easy to speak with an open mouth. Studying while working hard (苦學)? Anyone can do what others do; anyone would do it if it were that hard! But I want to say that not a single true "hardship student" (苦學生) has emerged. I want to ask anyone who studies under hardship: how painful is your life? There would likely be no one who could give a cheerful answer. I want to give a word of gold (金言) to our hardship students. Once, a letter came to me from our homeland. "The north wind is fierce, and the snow is over a foot deep; how do you manage the newspaper delivery?" Reading those words, the words of that dear elder brother are indeed very affectionate. They are comforting words. But I hate hearing the words "studying under hardship" (苦學). Delivering newspapers is not that difficult. Studying is not difficult either. What is truly difficult is lacking the will to do it. The term "hardship student" (苦學生) sounds very awkward to my ears. I am a student studying abroad (留學生). Just a student (書生). What? I want to speak based on the fundamental principle of human life.
When life originally emerges as a three-foot mass of blood (三尺의 血塊) into a place of light, it is a fundamental universal rule (根本的 通則) of life that this three-foot mass of blood must labor to a certain extent, relying on the mission of its soul (靈魂). If the body (肉體) signifies the truth that one must labor to a certain degree, and the soul is meant to govern it, then what is so difficult or impossible about this body exerting its instinctive actions, supported by the soul's mission? Life originally entails eating and clothing oneself only after this amount of labor (勞作). We should not imitate the words of those with dead ideas, who think that studying can only be done while eating idly, words originating from those steeped in the past stubborn, conventional life of the fourth class (第4階級), the propertyless class (無産階級), who suffered the destruction of even our instincts and innate freedom (天賦의 自由) for 5,000 years in the ruins of so-called capitalism (資本主義), the so-called propertied class (有産階級), putting in twice the effort for half the reward. We are the newest people (新人). We must recreate the world with our own hands. We are responsible for destroying the ethics, philosophy, religion, and morality of the past and constructing a renewed universe (一新 宇宙). We must break free from the habit, derived from the rotten words (腐言) of the past capitalist propertied classes, that study should be limited to idle eaters (遊食者). I intend to live by the spirit.<72> If anyone calls me a "hardship student" (苦學生), they are not my comrade. They do not know my ideals. If anyone calls me a "hardship student," I will not hesitate to engage in a round of decisive combat (決鬪) with them. I am a thoroughly new-type student studying abroad (新人的 留學生). A new-type student (新人的 書生). I am a person who intends to sing aloud and proclaim the newest ideals of a new person, based on the fundamental principles of life. However, if people insist on calling me a hardship student, then I hope there will be many new-type hardship students like me. I intend to gather those hardship students and form a New Man Association (新人會). And gathering them, I will establish the Tonmin Academy (鈍民學院 - Academy of the Dull People). I call those who destroy all the legacies left behind by our forefathers and ancestors who stained our long history, and who are the newest people—thoroughly new-type people—"Tonmin" (鈍民 - Dull People). I intend to gather those Tonmin and construct a new universe. This is a gathering of new people. This is my new "Academy" (아카데미). The source of this "Academy" will become, at the very least, a new compass for our human race. Anyone who wants to become a new-type hardship student in the aforementioned sense must, by all means, come east (渡東 - come to Japan). However, I do not know how much brilliance the world of thought among our peninsula's youth has taken on during this time, but those who come henceforth must first have this kind of thorough awakening (覺悟).

Ah—Rust has now settled on the iron fist (鐵拳) battling that world. During that time, I covered a delivery area of 10 *li* and delivered over 400 papers. Then, 7 or 8 days ago, I pierced my foot with a nail and cannot walk. Entrusting the delivery in that distant place over 10 *li* away to my beloved Mr. Chŏng, I lie on my sickbed, waiting for his return, with cold rice set aside—me, waiting expectantly now or later—me, unable to suppress the gloomy thoughts day or night in this small four-and-a-half *tatami* room—if our comrades were to see me, they surely would not spare a tear of sympathy. It is not a serious injury (重傷). But the date of complete recovery is uncertain. Rust has settled on the sword of the warrior battling 1.7 billion adversaries, and rust has settled on the iron fist (鐵拳) that shatters the unyielding hardships of worldly affairs. But I will not rest from the eternal battle with you. I record that the world is a battle. Though the roar of cannons is not heard—though weapons are not brandished—mountains tremble and seas boil. This is our battle against that demon with sincerity and fervent aspiration. Fight more—fight more! On the path we tread, there are mountains and seas. But for you, advancing like a burning flame, like boiling waves, like the water flower of an eternally unceasing spiritual spring, the sea—the mountains—fire—water—

**Summary**

Chŏng Ch'angsŏn recounts his arduous past journeying through Siberia and Manchuria alone, and his subsequent struggles as a poor Korean student in Tokyo in 1920-1921. He details his poverty, taking menial jobs like farmhand and newspaper delivery, his philosophical grappling with the meaning of life, truth, suffering, and the value of study versus practical existence and freedom. Despite intense loneliness and hardship, including a foot injury, he maintains a fierce determination, rejects being labeled a mere "hardship student," and calls for a revolutionary "new man" (新人) among Korean youth to reject past constraints and forge a new world based on spiritual strength and self-reliance, even proposing a "Tonmin Academy" (Academy of the Dull People) for this purpose. From a historical perspective, the article vividly portrays the harsh realities faced by Korean students in Japan during the colonial period – the poverty, discrimination (implied in needing a Japanese name, facing condescension), the difficult labor required for survival, and the psychological toll of isolation. It also reveals the intense intellectual ferment and nationalist (though perhaps leaning towards anarchist or socialist thought, given the critique of capitalism and emphasis on radical renewal) consciousness among some Korean youth seeking self-improvement and societal transformation despite colonial oppression. Chŏng's rejection of pity and his call for self-reliant "new people" reflect a strong spirit of resistance and a desire to redefine Korean identity and destiny.

						鄭昌先
나는 自白한다. 나는 鈍하고도 不敏한 低能兒일다. 그러나 나는 나에게 當한 責任과 職分을 履行함에는 족음도 숨기지 못하는 責任觀念- 職分觀念은 一寸一分도 辭讓치 못하는 良心- 이것 하나는 튼튼하게 腦 속에 서려 잇슴을 自白하며 揚言하려 하는 天眞인 古人的 新兒일다. 그리하야 나는 지난달 朴春坡 愛兄에게 原稿付托을 바덧다. 그런데 其時에 밧기는 나의 苦學하는 現象을 쓰면 조켓다는 말슴을 듯고는 억지로 「過渡期에 立한 余의 新運動」이라는 原稿를 썻다. 今番에도 또다시 그를 繼續코저 하엿스나 方今 나의 몸은 足部의 傷으로 因하야 누워잇는지 발서 6, 7日이나 된다. 그리하야 그것을 繼續하기는 좀 不容易하다. 그래서 今番에는 나의 東渡半個星霜의 風塵과 方今에 現象을 記錄하야 우리네- 뜻잇는 靑年- 마음깁흔 同情者에게 不足하나마 잔소리이나마 두어말슴 들여 보려한다. 이것은 우리 本國에 계신 有面無面 或은 親不親의 兄弟姉妹에게 나의 生活狀態와 心理上으로 바든 모든 感想을 記錄하야 처음으로 올이는 첫 글월일다.
나는 方今 雪窓寒燈 알에서 나 호을로 누워잇서 過去를 생각하고 現在를 그리는 때 無限한 感懷가 잇고 悲哀가 잇다. 아! 나는 孤獨이다. 아! 나는 나의 年前風霜을 連想한다. 西比亞 半萬里를 徒步하던 때나 南北滿洲 沙漠萬里에 異人, 異語, 異域, 異邦을 橫行할 때나 늘- 나 호을로 西山에 寒月이 넘어가는 때 山을 넘고 물을 건너던 때이나 千里遠程에 無人絶烟한 外邦을 나호올로 줄인 배를 어루만지며 부러진 短杖을 끄던 때이나 차즐이 업는 人家에 일업시 들어가 턱업시 밥을 부치고 잠자던 때나 單衣寒身이 宿所를 未定하고 해저믄 夕照暮烟에 게으른 소매를 저으며 이곳 저곳 彷徨하던 때이나 語不通心不知의＜65＞外人을 對하야 밥-소리를 하느라고 입만 벌리고 밥이란 말을 그림 그리던 때이나 萬里海外에서 赤手空拳으로 一身이 밥업고 옷업서 苦生하던 때이나 逼害와 壓迫 속에서 죽지 못하는 모진 목숨을 겨우 부지하는 때에 아! 나는! 나는! 어린 가슴을 몃 번이나 짓찌코 몃 번이나 헤친지 그 數를 헤일 수 업다. 잘 때나 누울 때나 나는 늘 孤寂이다. 나는 늘- 한숨이다. 나는 늘- 悲觀이고 怨恨이엇다. 크지도 채못한 이 어린 가슴에 뜨거운 잉글억을 담아 노혼 듯이 확근거리고 滄海大濤에 孤帆이 搖動하던지 울렁거리던 나에 가슴은 몃번이나 이 一身이 죽지 못함을 느끼엇는지 헤아릴 수 업다. 그러나 나는 늘- 사랑하는 愛人이 잇섯다. 一時도 보지 못하면 참지 못할 만한 愛人이 잇섯다. 그 愛人의 容貌는 꿈에도 잇지 못한다. 그의 따뜻한 가슴에 이마를 대일 때에는 나의 全生活의 蹂躝과 動搖가 모도다 슬어지고 오히려 微笑를 띠이고 내 호을로 질기어 춤추며 노래 부르고 발구르며 외우치려도 한다. 이는 곳 榮華라는 物的 欲望도 아니요 또 다른 名譽의 欲求도 아니다. 이는 다만 眞正한 眞理 眞理대로 살아보겟다는 純全한 精神的 生命이라는 愛人이엇다. 나는 眞理가 아니면 사지 아니할 決心이엇다. 眞理가 萬若 死라 하면 死까지 取하리라는 絶頂의 欲求, 이것의 發動이 나의 一生을 記錄할 平生句가 되엇다. 그리하야 나는 모든 動作이 남 아니하는 苦痛, 남 아니밧는 刺戟, 남 아니하는 걱정, 남 아니하는 悲哀를 感하던 나이다.

아! 나의 一生! 아! 나의 運命, 아! 나의 怨恨 多幸多苦의 半生 누구와 議論할 친구가 업고 床을 마조하고 讀書할 同氣가 업는 나는 참으로 九天에 사모친 怨을 말할 수 업다. 그러나 나는 먼저 말하던 愛人-그것이 나의 理想的 親友요, 孤寂의 벗이다. 그리하야 나는 그 愛人을 딸아 千里나 萬里나 限업시 넓은 天地에 차저 단이는 나이다. 그래서 昨秋에 나는 玄海灘 洪濤를 거침업시 건너와 所謂 나의 사랑하는 愛人-眞理-그것을 차겟다고 天地玄黃 풀이를 새로 하고 잇다.

나는 이 主張으로 一平生을 살아도 不滿足이 업겟다 한 나의 極端의 消極的 생각은 지금 생각하야도 決斷코 부끄럽지는 안타. 그러나 人生은 人生이다. 人生은 人生이니까 人生가티 살아야 할 것은 事實이다. 人生이라면 人生이 아니라 人生 된 바에는 人生이라는 本能의 全體를 具有한 然後에야 비롯오 人生이라 할 것이다. 우리는 人生이란 本能의 全體를 차지며 人生의 定理와 그의 定路를 求하는 眞人이 되려는 우리이다.
그러면 이러한 生活이 果然 우리의 本能的 全體의 原理를 具有한 圓滿의 生活인가. 이것이 第一次 問題이다. 人生이라면 나 「鄭昌先」만을 意味한 것은 아니다. 널리 世界에 廣在한 17億을 意味함이다. 東京 一隅에 新聞配達夫인 나에게만 限하야 人生이란 圓滿한 眞理를 求할 수 업는 것은 事實이 아니랴. 널리 宇宙와 人生-人生과 宇宙-宇宙 全體에 寓在한 普遍的 原理가 아니면 到底히 圓滿한 眞理라고 말할 수는 업다. 이 때에 나는 不可不 過去에 確信하던 信仰의 全體와 自恃의 舞臺를 破壞하고 다시 哲學史와 倫理史를 들게 되엇다. 그리하야 나는 다시 學校 必要論을 일으키게 되엇다. 그리하야 東洋大學에 다시 復校하기는 2個月 동안 勞力한 結果에 벌어＜71＞ 노흔 것으로 月謝를 들이고 다시 學校를 繼續하게 되엇다. 그러나 나는 또 한편으로 前者를 主張하려 하는 傾向도 잇다. 내가 말하기를 나는 圓滿한 學業을 닥기 前에는 未成品으로 本國에 돌아가지 아니하리라 主張을 세운 바에 今年이나 來年을 限할 것도 아니고 明年, 來明年을 限할 것도 아니다. 적어도 10年을 바라보면 東京에 오랜 生活을 하여야 하겟다 한다.
自然이 뚤린 입으로 말하기는 쉬웁다. 苦學? 누구던지 하는 것을 못하여 누구던지 그만큼 힘들면 하지! 그러나 나는 참 苦學生은 하나도 나지 아니 하엿다고 하고 십다. 누구던지 苦學하는 그에게 뭇고저 한다. 그대의 그 生涯가 얼마나 괴로운가 하면 快한 對答을 할 이는 업겟다. 나는 우리의 苦學生에게 한마디 金言을 주려 한다. 어느 때 우리 本國에서 나에게 便紙 한 장이 왓다. 「北風이 猛烈하고 積雪이 尺餘에 新聞配達을 어찌 하나」 하엿다. 그 말을 보면 그 愛兄의 말이 퍽으나 정다운 말이다. 慰安을 주는 말이다. 그러나 나는 苦學한다는 말을 들으면 듯기가 실타. 新聞配達이 그다지 어려웁지도 아니하다. 工夫하기가 어려운 것도 아니다. 참으로 할 생각 업는 것이 어려운 것이다. 나는 苦學生 소리가 듯기에 매우 어색하게 들린다. 나는 留學生이다. 그저 書生이다. 워? 나는 人生이란 根本的 原理를 들어 말하고자 한다.
人生은 本來 三尺의 血塊와 光明한 곳을 탈 때에 그 3尺의 血塊가 그 靈魂의 使命을 依支하야 어느 정도만큼 勞力하여야 할 것은 人生의 根本的 通則이다. 그러면 肉體는 어느 程度만큼 勞作하라는 眞理를 意味함이요. 靈魂은 그를 支配하라 함이면 이 肉體가 靈魂의 使命을 듸디어 本能的 動作을 發揮함이 그다지 어려울 것이 무엇이며 하지 못할 것이 무엇이냐. 人生은 本來 이마만한 勞作이 잇고라야 밥을 먹게 되고 옷을 입게 되는 것이다. 近來의 所謂 資本主義, 所謂 有産階級으로부터 우리의 本能과 天賦의 自由까지 滅亡시킨 그 廢墟에서 5千年 동안 自由의 拘束을 밧던 第4階級인 無産階級 倍나 더한 勞力을 들이어 배나 적은 報酬를 밧던 過去의 固陋한 因襲的 生活에 저저 온 그네의 입에서 말하던 工夫는 거저 먹고라야 만 하게 되는 줄 아는 죽은 思想을 가진 이들의 말을 模倣하야서는 아니 될 것이다. 우리는 가장 新人이다. 우리의 손으로 世界를 다시 創造하여야 할 것이다. 우리는 過去의 倫理와 哲學과 宗敎와 道德을 破壞하고 一新 宇宙를 建設할 責任者이다. 過去의 資本主義的 有産階級들의 腐言으로 工夫는 遊食者에게 限하여야 하게 되는 習慣을 脫하여야 한다. 나는 精神으로 사르려＜72＞ 한다. 나에게 萬若 苦學生이라 하면 그이는 나의 同志가 아니다. 그이는 나의 理想을 모르는 이다. 萬若 나에게 苦學生이라 하면 나는 그이와 一陣의 決鬪를 아끼지 아니하겟다. 나는 徹底한 新人的 留學生이다. 新人的 書生이다. 나는 根本的 人生의 原理의 立脚地에서 가장 새로운 新人的 新理想을 謳歌하며 외우치랴는 사람임으로써 일다. 그러나 하여간 나를 苦學生이라 하면 나와 가튼 新人的 苦學生이 만히 잇기를 希望한다. 그 苦學生을 모아 新人會를 모으려 한다. 그리하야 그네를 모아 鈍民學院을 建設하겟다. 過去의 우리네 父兄과 元祖가 오랜 歷史를 더럽히며 나하논 모든 遺物을 破壞하고 가장 새로운 新人-徹底한 新人的인 그네를 鈍民이라 한다. 그 鈍民을 會合하야 新宇宙를 建設하려 한다. 이것은 新人의 會合이다. 이것은 나의 새 「아카데미」(Academy)이다. 이 「아카데미」의 淵源은 적어도 우리 人類의 새 指南針이 되겟다. 누구던지 前者를 意味한 新人的 苦學生이 되랴면 萬을 限코 渡東하여야 한다. 그러나 나는 우리 半島靑年界의 思想界가 그동안 얼마만한 光彩를 띠인지 아지 못하거니와 今後에 오는 이는 먼저 이러한 徹底한 覺悟가 잇서야 한다.
아-나는 方今 저-世界와 戰鬪하는 鐵拳에 녹은 슬엇다. 나는 그 동안 10里나 되는 配達域을 가서 4百餘張을 配達하엿다. 그리다 7,8日 前에 발에 못을 찔리어서 步行을 엇지 못하야 10里가 넘는 먼 곳에 나의 사랑하는 鄭君을 대신 付托하고 床에 누워서 돌아올 때를 기다리노라고 식은 밥을 노코-이때나 저때나 바라는 나-四疊 다다미 작은 방 속에서 낫이나 밤이나 鬱鬱한 心思를 抑制치 못하는 나-우리네 同志가 萬若 目見하면 同情의 一淚를 아끼지도 아니하리라. 重傷은 아니다. 그러나 어느 때 完治될는지 期日이 茫然하다. 17億을 對敵하는 勇士의 劒에 녹은 슬고 鐵壁가튼 世事의 不如意를 破壞하는 鐵拳에 녹은 슬엇다. 그러나 나는 너와 永遠한 戰鬪를 休息치 아니하리라. 世上은 戰鬪임을 記錄한다. 砲聲은 들리지 아니하야도-干戈는 나적 어리지 아니하여도 산을 울리고 바다를 끌힌다. 이는 곳 우리의 精誠과 熱烈한 向上心으로 저-惡魔와 戰鬪합이다. 더 싸워라-더 우리의 가는 길은 山도 잇고 바다도 잇다. 그러나 타는 불꼿가티 끌는 물결가티 永遠히 休息치 아니하는 靈泉의 물꼿가티 나아가는 너에게는 바다도-山도-불도-물도-
