Foreword: Only Contribute to Society

Are you (君) wealthy? We know that your treasury (庫) must be full of gold (金) and your granary (倉) must be full of grain (穀). However, you too are but one person with a body like ours. ...Being one such person, your meals likely do not exceed three times a day, and your clothing likely does not exceed three layers at once. Alas! O wealthy sirs! What do you intend to do with that remaining wealth?

Are you powerful? We know that at your seat, the words of visitors are whispered low, and before your carriage, the backs of myriad people bow deeply. However, you too are but one person with feelings like ours. ...Being one such person, your power cannot satisfy feelings beyond the joy, anger, sorrow, pleasure, love, hate, and desire (喜怒哀樂愛惡欲) that reside within yourself. Alas! O powerful sirs! What do you intend to do with that remaining power?

Are you a scholar of the Way (道學者)? We know that your wisdom (慧知) can deduce the true nature (實相) of all things (萬有), and your principles (志操) can tread upon the weak waves (弱波) of the East Sea (東海). However, if that which you boast of is only that, if it is only filling your own desire for knowledge and emotional refinement, then how is it different from a criminal driven by their own desires? Alas! O scholars of the Way! What do you intend to do with that scholarship?

O gentlemen of the mature generation (老成階級), who already see death and shadow cast upon the edge (*端)! What will you do with your abundant experience and the aspirations you wish to leave behind? O young gentlemen (靑年諸君), setting fire (*부티는) in your bosoms (*품)! With whom will you share that energy and passion? Is this all? The vital essence of aspiring patriots, heroes, men of action, and spirited youths overflowing in the capital and the countryside, and the strategies of advisors, tacticians, opportunists, and masters of intrigue – where shall these too be tested?

To cover it in one word: you must only contribute (奉貢) to society. Only contribute to society. You must contribute to this society where the traces of our ancestors' lives over the past half-myriad years are intertwined as they are, and where the framework (張木) for our descendants' lives for the endless future will be laid down. Sadly, although the flowers (꼿) blooming this spring are the very same flowers as last year and the year before, today's complexion is suddenly not yesterday's. We, whether wealthy, scholars of the Way, or anyone else, all become a handful (함좀) of earth, gone, unable to return, merely becoming the laughter (뭇음) of the spring grass that greens year after year. Yet, this society to which we belong alone remains steadfast (依旦) through millennia, explaining our entire existence down through the ages. It is through society that we first complete our lives – no, attain eternal life. Ah, if we can become part of the group that contributes (*치는) some pure assets (淨資) for such a society, how joyful and glorious that would be!

Alas! Before us, long ago, Kong Chungni (孔仲尼), who was 'never tired of learning, never weary of teaching (學不厭 敎不倦),' acted as a dog of a mourning house (*家之狗). Ch'ulchangipsang (出將入相; Going out as a general, coming in as a minister) Chegal Ryang (諸葛亮) 'bent his body and exhausted his energy, stopping only after death (鞠躬盡瘁死而後已).' Closer to our time, numerous predecessors (先單) presented themselves barefoot (跣足呈身), each doing their utmost (各其 最善). Could we today dare to live alone (호올로), wishing only for comfort (便安고저)? Is it possible (잇슬가 보냐)? We must only offer our entire possessions all together (다가티) for the development and fulfillment of our society. That is all. That is all.
Brothers! Do not the times especially demand this of us? <2>

**Summary**

This editorial passionately exhorts readers from all walks of life—the wealthy, the powerful, the learned, the old, and the young—to dedicate their resources, talents, and energies entirely to society. It argues that individual lives and achievements are transient, like fleeting flowers, ultimately returning to dust. However, society endures through ages, representing the cumulative legacy of ancestors and the foundation for descendants. Contributing to society is presented as the only path to achieving a form of eternal life and meaning beyond individual mortality. The author invokes historical figures like Confucius (Kong Chungni) and Zhuge Liang (Chegal Ryang) as exemplars of selfless dedication to the greater good, urging contemporaries to follow their path rather than seeking personal comfort or glory.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is significant. Written in 1921, shortly after the March 1st Movement, its fervent call for societal contribution reflects a strong nationalist sentiment focused on collective strengthening and development during a period of subjugation. The appeal to various classes (wealthy, powerful, scholars, old, young) suggests an attempt to foster unity and mobilize diverse segments of Korean society towards a common purpose, likely related to national survival and progress under colonial rule. The emphasis on shared heritage ("Past Half-Myriad Years") and future generations ("Future Unending Years") underscores a desire to maintain national identity and continuity despite external pressures. Historians might find the article's blend of traditional moral exhortation (citing Confucian figures) with a modernizing emphasis on societal development a noteworthy aspect of early Korean nationalist discourse.
