交情欄 (Friendship Column)
In this issue, the Readers' Friendship Column is simply called the Friendship Column. Previously, we filled five pages, but due to circumstances, we are only doing two pages. It is regrettable that we cannot include all the writings from our readers.

Early Spring Suburbs by Iksan Kim Ik-pae

The spring sky is spotless like a mirror, and the distant mountains are profoundly dark. The warm, clear air just sends a thrill through me, while a single skylark, soaring fifty *gil* high, sings nature's spring song, and from somewhere, faintly heard, is the single note of a cuckoo's call. Ah—Nature, Nature, Great Nature! Ah—Spring, spring, the whole world is spring! Unable to contain the spring excitement, leaning on my bamboo staff, I leisurely set foot upon the ridge of the Eastern Hill. Far off to the northwest, stretched out long, the Paengma River is blue as ever like the sky, where white sails, scattered here and there, appear even smaller than ducks. Imch'ŏn Mountain, its head reaching into the clouds, drowsy with spring fatigue, sometimes high, sometimes low, intertwined like this, dotted about, faint as painted eyebrows (*眉) and dark as kingfisher-blue (*翠). The fields, still retaining the color of winter, seem plumply fleshed out and quietly asleep, with only bony fuzz (보부라) standing here and there, and through them, blue smoke gathering (*운타서) and rising softly, wriggles this way and that, drawing white letters. The village's Sinp'yŏng Embankment (新坪堤) is full to the brim with water, shimmering, shimmering, gold glinting upon it, while white herons landing and taking off make one suspect they are fallen blossoms soaked with tears in the late spring of the third lunar month. Two or three weeping willows, their buds bursting bit by bit, filled with vitality, hang over the ferry crossing, gazing at their reflections in the water, singing alone and dancing alone; the blades of grass rustling, the new sprouts crawling slowly—these too are a part of spring. Breaking the silence, a train draws a long line from south to north, puffing chugga-chugga (파푸파푸), exhaling bead-like black smoke plumes as it runs away. A passenger, arm leaning on the window, drowsy, holding a pencil and notebook, writes endlessly—I ask, but cannot know. Is it poetry, song, or sketching? Whatever meets the eye inspires poetic thoughts and picturesque charm (*趣). It's as if a watercolor painting is spread before my eyes; resting my chin on my hand, sitting absently, my insides are completely filled with the spirit of spring. Unconsciously escaping my lips is a clumsy song, a verse of "Natural Beauty."

One.
Warm and affectionate lover, my dear!
When your fresh, clear, beautiful song,
riding a blue chariot (車), holding a blue whip,
trickling down from the sky—
creeping up from the earth—
quietly conveys its sound waves,
driving out all the cold and cruel chill,
and on that site (基), after painting Eden's East Garden,
You call out! With an affectionate voice,
us, hiding beneath the wind.

Two.
Ah—my dear, I am glad to see you!
When your beautiful form, once, flashes,
appears (現) upon the stage,
the sinister air turns into an angel's heart,
the lonely world into a young girl's affectionate smile,
transforming, changing!
And also,
the skylark singing fifty *gil* high,
nature's divine comedy,
through the old blades of grass, stealthily,
the pace of the fresh green creeping out—
Ah—all these things are
a sincere feast of nature
spread out for you, my dear.

Three.
Ah—affectionate lover, my dear!
I, I
will fall soundly asleep, embraced tightly—
in your warm bosom.
No, I intend to declare a final moment
that cannot be forgotten again. (End) <103>

**Summary**

The article begins with an editorial note explaining the reduction in size of the "Friendship Column" section. The main content is a descriptive prose piece by Kim Ik-pae from Iksan, vividly portraying the scenery of early spring in the countryside near the Paengma River. It details the sights and sounds—sky, mountains, river, birds, fields, smoke, willows, a train—evoking a strong sense of seasonal beauty and poetic inspiration. This is followed by a three-stanza poem titled "Natural Beauty," personifying spring as a beloved figure bringing life and joy, replacing winter's harshness. For historians, the piece offers a glimpse into the aesthetic sensibilities and literary expression of educated Koreans during the early colonial period (1921). The blend of traditional nature appreciation with modern elements like the train, the somewhat romanticized yet melancholic tone of the poem, and the very existence of such a literary column in a prominent magazine like *Kaebyŏk* reflect the cultural landscape under Japanese rule.
