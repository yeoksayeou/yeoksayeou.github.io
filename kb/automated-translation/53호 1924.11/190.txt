Article Title: 아 지금은 새벽 네시 (Ah, Now it is Four in the Morning)
Author: 金石松
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Ah, Now it is Four in the Morning
						Kim Sŏk-song

Like the face of Lord Alkonan, the waning moon,
Shines silently upon the crumbled ruins of an old fortress wall,
Like the breath of Hanu-nim, the dawn wind,
Quietly dances on the branches of an ancient tree.

Ah, now it is four in the morning!

My parents and siblings are all asleep,
My friends, my enemies,
The young, the old, men, women,
All people of the same form as me,
Whether on silken bedding or on straw mats,
Each dreams their sweet and bitter dreams.

Ah, now it is four in the morning!

Young men and women, whose wedding ceremony will be held <127>
As soon as the day breaks, break their virginity in dreams,
And the song of youth overflows in their sleep-talking.

People whose pulse has already stopped,
Their lifeless bodies, which will hasten the path to the North Mang Mountains as soon as the day breaks,
Cause the last grief for those with blood ties.

Ah, now it is four in the morning!

As the breath of countless lives is heard,
Terrifyingly laborious like a tsunami,
The last breath of the night is heard.

Ah, now it is four in the morning!

The cock of the future declares the new day,
And from somewhere, the crying sound of a newborn baby is heard.

Ah, new day! New person!,
The dance floor of new life is about to open,
Ah, the solemn four in the morning!. (From a notebook three years ago) <128>
<127-128>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sŏk-song captures the transitional moment of 4 AM, suspended between night and day, sleep and waking, death and birth. It contrasts the quiet stillness of the pre-dawn landscape with the inner lives of sleeping people—dreams of upcoming weddings juxtaposed with impending funerals. The poem builds anticipation for the "new day," heralded by the rooster's crow and a baby's cry, symbolizing hope and the cyclical nature of life and death. From a historical perspective, written in 1924 during the Japanese colonial period, the poem's intense focus on the arrival of a "new day," "new person," and "new life" after a difficult night could resonate with the collective yearning for national renewal and liberation. The imagery of waiting in darkness and the powerful, almost violent, "last breath of the night" before the dawn might reflect the suppressed hopes and underlying tensions of the era.

아 지금은 새벽 네시
						金石松
알코난 님의 얼골가튼 下弦달은,
흔허진 녯 城터에 말업지 빗최이고,
한우님의 숨결가튼 새벽바람은,
해묵은 나무가지에 고요히 춤을 출제.

아, 지금은 새벽 네시!

나의 父母 兄弟는 모다 자고
나의 親舊, 나의 원수,
靑年, 老年, 男子, 女子,
나와 꼴이 가튼 모든 사람은,
비단이불에나, 거직자리에나,
제각각 달고 쓴 꿈을 꿀제

아, 지금은 새벽 네시!

날이 밝기만 하면 新婚의 禮式이 擧行될,＜127＞
절믄 男女는 꿈속에 童貞을 깨틀고,
靑春의 노래가 잠고대에 넘처들닐제.

날이 밝기만 하면 北邙의 길을 재촉할,
임의 血脉의 活動이 쉬인 사람의 등신은
血祿잇는 사람의 마즈막 애를 끈흘떼.

아, 지금은 새벽 네시!

無數한 生命의 숨소리가 들닐제,
海嘯와가티 무섭게 힘적은,
밤의 마즈막 숨소리가 들닌다.

아, 지금은 새벽 네시!

장래의 닭은 새날을 宣言하고,
어대선지 갓난이의 우름소리가 들닌다.

아, 새날! 새 사람!,
새 生命의 춤터가 열니랴하는,
아, 거륵한 새벽 네시!. (三年前空冊에서)＜128＞
