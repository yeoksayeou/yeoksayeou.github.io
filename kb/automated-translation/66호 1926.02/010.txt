기사형태	문예기타 (Miscellaneous Literary Work)

文明化한 野蠻人
						李敦化

A certain rich father asks his son who has become an ideologue (主義者):
"Hey—what do you lack that makes you run around doing such things? Do you lack clothes? I don't understand those poor folks who have no food or clothing; perhaps it's due to their circumstances or disposition that they act that way, I wouldn't know. But someone like you, who has abundant food and clothing, and also, looking at your position or knowledge, could make a decent living in any government office or society," why on earth must you insist on being called an ideologue, going in and out of the police station, and doing things that could land you in prison?
The son's reply:
"I became an ideologue for the sake of justice."

Both the father's question and the son's answer seem plausible and have their reasons. The father's words, though spoken unconsciously, certainly contain class consciousness. The implication is that poor people lacking food might become ideologues, but those with abundant food and clothing have no need to. The son's words imply that no matter how abundant one's food and clothing, one cannot lack a conscience, and since conscience transcends wealth and poverty, anyone, rich or poor, can become an ideologue for the sake of justice. Thus, within this exchange, one can see that it encompasses both class-conscious movements and movements for justice.

Originally—what distinction exists between class consciousness and righteous conscience? Class consciousness is an awareness directed towards a certain object, seeking equality concerning that object. Therefore, it is true that justice is inherently included within class consciousness. However, <2> pure class consciousness cannot exist without class confrontation. Furthermore, it doesn't become class consciousness merely because classes confront each other; class consciousness is first established when one becomes aware of belonging to a certain class, from which arises a sense of injustice and grievance, leading to a consciousness of resistance seeking to resolve that grievance. The sense of justice also arises presupposing a consciousness of resistance, but justice, simply put, can be seen as an consciousness that transcends class, seeking to correct or revolutionize the societal defects underfoot out of a purely human conscience. Therefore, class consciousness is narrow in its very nature; conversely, it contains an inherent necessity and, at the same time, it is broad in its ability to draw the masses belonging to the same class onto a single line of resistance. Thus, righteous conscience is universal in its very nature; conversely, it requires at least the awakening of individuality and attracts people of character, hence it cannot set a standard from the few for the many.

Generally, in any innovative movement, there is none without the seed of class consciousness, and at the same time, movements of conscience always run parallel. These two types of consciousness are necessarily inseparable and can also be seen as natural expressions. Thus, looking at any innovative movement, there is a sense that the movement of conscience acts as the vanguard, while the movement of class consciousness serves as the reserve force and the ultimately victorious force; this can be seen as a necessity of circumstances. For example, speaking of the past slave liberation movement, although the movement itself directly concerned the slaves, its initial phase did not arise from the slaves' own awakening. Rather, it was due to the outcry of numerous advocates for justice who were not slaves that the slaves' own awakening eventually occurred, and thus its final victory was achieved through slave class consciousness. The liberation of serfs or the liberation of women all followed such a sequence.

In recent years in our Chosŏn society, the terms "professional ○○○" and "exemplary ○○○" are often heard. These usually come from the mouths of opponents, but their meaning, for instance, is that the labor movement is not directly carried out by laborers, or the women's movement is carried out by men who are not women. Thus, they sneer, implying it's still premature for direct involvement, or that it's merely professional or exemplary behavior. However, this fails to understand the principle that all innovative movements, in their initial stages, begin as movements of sentiment, progressing into movements of righteous conscience. Thus, in Chosŏn especially, the movement for justice is still needed more than anything. Although all the reasons cannot be stated, in some sense, <3> consistently pursuing justice from beginning to end and strengthening all liberation through it might be appropriate for the environment and circumstances of Chosŏn.

In the movement for justice, discontent (不平) is invariably paired. Discontent is not always paired with ideology (主義), but justice necessarily includes discontent. The reason is that the movement for justice always arises by targeting societal defects.

Ouyangzi (歐陽子) once said, "When things lack balance (平), they cry out (鳴)." The thunder of summer, the wind of winter, the waves of the sea, the waterfalls of the mountains—all these arise from the discontent of things. Thus, the beauty of nature seems to arise entirely from the discontent of things. The spirit of nature seems to give things the instinct of discontent to reveal their own inherent beauty. Humans are also a part of nature. Therefore, human nature cannot be without discontent. Human nature certainly contains discontent. In a sense, humans can be said to be animals born in discontent and dying in discontent. The less discontent one has, the weaker their humanity; the greater the discontent, the stronger their humanity can be proven. The reason is that the discontent of human nature is twofold. One is the innate sorrow welling up from human instinct, like that of nature, and the other is the acquired discontent stemming from the social and worldly suffering unique to humans. This twofold discontent inherently adds humaneness (人間味) to humans as humans. The source of humaneness lies here. Humans find improvement and progress by harmonizing this twofold discontent well. The humaneness arising from natural discontent finds expression in the forms of art or religion, while the humaneness arising from social discontent is expressed through the psychology of revolutionaries and the activities of innovators. From this perspective, for those who undertake the movement for justice, everything is discontent, and thus they popularize and socialize that discontent.

In the eyes of the discontented, the society reflected is entirely transcendent. They always possess a discerning eye that rises above and looks down upon society. The eyes of the discontented and the satisfied are always diametrically opposed.
In a society where the discontented and the satisfied coexist, the satisfied are always the ruling class or <4> enjoy special or inevitable fortune under the protection of the ruling class. The discontented aim to destroy this or become the target of their aversion. Therefore, the satisfied universally become maintainers of the status quo, conservatives clinging to old ways, harmonizers, collaborators, moderates, and the corrupt and obstinate.

The satisfied lack holistic humanity. They view humans mechanically, not organically. Thus, they fall into extremely base individualism. They possess a psychology no different from the actions of wild beasts seeking satisfaction for themselves alone, like the solitary pleasure of a heavy drinker.

The satisfied have no tomorrow. They are always focused on the present moment (刹那主義). Entangled in their environment and circumstances, they seek pleasure in assimilating and harmonizing with the environment. Therefore, among the satisfied, there are no patriots, no lovers of the people, no one with the grand vision to broadly save the masses. In other words, the satisfied are like maggots playing in dung, dissolving their entire lives within the environment of the dung bucket.

The satisfied lack great desires and great aspirations. Thus, they lack fervent emotion. There might be the satisfaction of one cup, another cup, and yet another cup, but there is no passion or emotion seeking the great satisfaction of transcending the single cup to embrace the vastness of nature (一斗大自然). In other words, the satisfied are conformists clinging to old ways. They are sufferers of paralyzed nerves who hate danger, hate progress, hate shouting loudly; they are intoxicated by the present phenomenon; they are weaklings who discuss their view of life in the embrace of women and children.

Thus, the satisfied possess a kind of pseudo-wisdom. They possess all sorts of similar cunning skills: craftiness, neutrality, formalism, and falsehood. Playing with these skills, they cleverly supplement their satisfaction with the current state. In seeking momentary satisfaction through temporary cunning that deceives the masses and society, their psychology always harbors a small, anxious fear. They fear lest some unexpected change should arise from their present satisfaction, or lest misfortune should befall them with others or peers eyeing their position, or lest the established earthen wall should collapse due to unexpected wind and rain. They spend their lives in constant trepidation and meticulous caution, fearing great courage and angered by small slights, nurturing a cowardly nature. <5> From the perspective of Daoist scholars (道學者), this appears as the conduct of gentlemen and scholars (君子와 士人); in the eyes of those in power, they are treated as moderate citizens and trustworthy commoners. Thus, they are a worthless crowd who, with falsehood and pretense, lead society into a state of inaction and incompetence.
This is the psychology of the satisfied. The discontented possess a psychology that is the exact opposite of the satisfied. They lack conformity, pretense, and all cowardice.

The emergence of social discontent in society is not due to the discontent of the discontented themselves, but because society itself indirectly causes feelings of discontent. Just as illness arises in the body because the body itself fundamentally has some defect that causes illness, social discontent arises because society itself has contained elements of discontent from some point in time. Discontent is suffering. Just as physical discontent causes physical suffering, social discontent causes social suffering. Suffering is the declaration of treatment for discontent. If a sick body cannot feel pain, it is truly a soulless corpse. Therefore, social discontent is a declaration demanding the treatment of social suffering. Social discontent, on one hand, indicates the pathological phenomena of the society at hand, and on the other hand, it is a valuable action possessing infinite responsibility and ceaseless effort towards society.

Life scientists say that the general law of life is defense against attack. Thus, this defense manifests as continuous counter-attack, that is, defensive attack. This was the primitive instinct of life derived from the opposition movement or irritability of the living structure. Without this instinct, the existence of life has no value. Thus, the more this instinct develops and improves, the stronger the meaning of survival becomes. Human life can also be seen as the development of this law. The only difference is that human life, having consciously changed, has refined, beautified, or purified this law compared to other forms of life. From this perspective, the definition of all morality can be seen as the beautification and refinement of life's self-defense. From this perspective, the actions of the discontented are actions affirming the self-responsibility of life's morality and heightened activity. That is, the actions of justice. The distinction between the satisfied and the discontented is thus. The satisfied are like a paralyzed part of the body, inferior beings unable to feel any pain, while the discontented are living blood with highly active nerves. The discontented are a new breed with a life pulse and vitality. They are the vanguard crying out for the creation <6> of a new society.

The satisfied come in many varieties. Needless to say, there are the special classes enjoying privileges in that country, the wealthy capitalists, the bureaucratic class with power, and so on, but besides them, they can be classified into various other types. The middle class is one segment; the powerless and inactive optimists of "contentment with few desires" (小欲知足); other artists, writers, salarymen, religious pastors and priests, the "peace of mind and acceptance of destiny" (安心立命) faction of Daoist scholars—there is no class or society where the satisfied are not mixed in. This is why society does not easily achieve innovation, and the movement for justice faces such difficulties. We refer to this phenomenon as a civilized barbarian society, and we call such people civilized barbarians.

The distinction between civilized people and barbarians should not be seen merely by the difference between a sophisticated (燥爛한 - likely meant refined/developed?) life and a crude (野卑한) life; nor should it be distinguished solely by the possession of refined morality versus violent nature; nor should it be distinguished solely by the perfection or imperfection of state and social organization. Beyond these, there is one fundamental condition that differentiates them. What is it? It can be seen as the life of creation versus the life of inherited custom (遺習). Civilized people live a life of creation, whereas barbarians only live a life of inherited custom. Barbarians receive unearned enjoyment from natural resources without any creation, whereas civilized people possess the ability to create and invent from them. From this perspective, one can say those are barbarians, and these are civilized people. If we judge the difference between civilization and barbarism based on this criterion, we can discover numerous civilized barbarians even among our civilized races. We can distinguish between civilized people and barbarians among the same British and American people, among the same East Asians, and among the same Koreans. If we distinguish civilized people and barbarians by the presence or absence of creative ability according to the logic above, we can find many barbarians even among the so-called civilized races. That is, anyone who is satisfied with unearned enjoyment from the relics of their ancestors without any labor or creation can be called a civilized barbarian. Their civilized life comes from the inheritance passed down by ancestors or from their own fortunate circumstances; in the sense that they are living the life of parasites, they are called civilized barbarians.

Generally, in the phases of each era, there are three special categories: one is historical relics (歷史的遺物), one is the special problems of the present (現下의 特殊問題), and one is the buds of the future (將來의 萠芽). Historical relics, needless to say, refer to all <7> the legacies already created by ancestors of past generations; these rely heavily on inherited systems, inherited customs and habits, inherited religious morals, and legal support like the inheritance rights of the private property system, sinking deep roots into the psychology of modern people. The special problems of the present refer to all the transitional latent and manifest forces that inevitably arise due to the relationships of the current social phase, the so-called world trend (世界大勢); these rely on current public opinion, sometimes appearing as a driving force, sometimes as a reactionary force, just as waves arise from the contact of wind and water. Here, relying on all transitional impulses like generational conflict, disputes between old and new, destructive confrontations, they target the psychology of modern people propagandistically or stimulatingly, aiming at the masses, and are movements that stir up the current situation under an atmosphere of fluctuation. The buds of the future are the outcries of all innovators who have discovered a new world by correcting or firmly grasping the special problems of the present, already establishing firm ideals for the future. In times seeking change, these three phases always exist, and thus, these temporal phases themselves divide historical figures into two paths. Thus, those who rely on historical relics to live a life of unearned enjoyment from nature and uncompensated reward for effort—these were the civilized barbarians. If primitive barbarians are considered naturalized, these can be called civilized barbarians in the sense that they merely enjoy the relics of their ancestors without compensation. They are barbarians carrying historical libraries on their backs; barbarians possessing traditional authority. As mentioned above, civilized barbarians are 끼여있다 (stuck/wedged in) every society today. Those like the aristocratic privileged class who obtained their status through ancestral merit claimed as state service, or the wealthy class enjoying uncompensated benefits towards society under the guise of interest manipulation from inherited wealth or established property, or under the title of landownership—these go without saying. Furthermore, in the aspects of religion and morality, pastors, priests, etc., who deceive the masses with mythical superstitions, and the crowd of Daoist scholars who deceive the masses appearing as gentlemen with corrupt moral concepts and inherited teachings—all these civilized barbarians—need no second mention. Interpreted more broadly, even military personnel, government clerks (史屬), etc., who engage in unproductive consumption under state authority, must largely be included in this group.

Civilized barbarians are thus widespread in scope, and moreover, they hold worldly power. This is the inversion of values (價値의 顚倒); the omen of the world's ruin (世上의 亡兆). It is evidence that civilization is not progressing smoothly but deviating onto side roads. The reason for denying modern civilization lies in this point. The civilization that must advance from now on lies in correcting this. Thus, those who realize this are the new civilized people in contrast to the old civilized people; those who do not realize this are barbarians in contrast to the new civilized people. <8>

**Summary**

This article by Yi Tonhwa critiques complacency within society, defining "civilized barbarians" as those who benefit from the structures and legacies of civilization (wealth, status, inherited knowledge) without contributing through creation or labor. He contrasts these "satisfied" individuals—characterized by conformity, lack of vision, and attachment to the status quo—with the "discontented," driven by a sense of justice and a desire for social progress. Yi argues that true progress stems from righteous discontent and innovation, not passive inheritance or maintenance of existing systems. He suggests that movements driven by conscience often precede broader class-based movements and emphasizes the particular need for justice-driven activism in contemporary Chosŏn.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is significant for its sharp social critique veiled in philosophical discourse. Written in 1926, Yi's condemnation of the "satisfied" elite enjoying unearned privilege can be read as an indirect criticism of collaborators, the established yangban remnants, or even the colonial system itself that fostered such parasitic elements. His emphasis on "righteous discontent" and the need for justice movements resonates with the nationalist and social reformist sentiments prevalent among Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule. Historians might find the concept of "civilized barbarians" a useful lens for analyzing class dynamics and intellectual currents during this period, reflecting anxieties about modernity, social stagnation, and the meaning of progress in a colonized nation.
