잡지명	개벽 제66호
발행일	1926년 02월 01일
기사제목	R군에게 (To R-kun)
필자	抱石
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

To R-kun
						P'osŏk
Letter 1
It's already been two weeks since I saw you. How have you been? Have you been well and writing much? In the meantime, I've moved from my previous cell to one at the northernmost end.

The cell I moved to isn't particularly better than the last one, but maybe because it's a corner room, unlike before when even on long summer days I couldn't see a single speck of sunlight, since coming here, around the time the setting sun hangs on the back mountain peak, for about 10 minutes, a ray of sunlight, about the size of a large bowl, barely manages to shine onto the floor through the corner of the window. Ten minutes of sunlight! This bowl-sized patch of sunlight! Hey, my friend, this is something people living in the world of light might find hard to imagine. Before I go out to the exercise yard once a day, this is the first time I've quietly sat in my room and received sunlight on my body. How wondrous it must feel in my heart. Perhaps the word "moved" is more fitting than "wondrous."

Look here. One of the prisoners who was in the same room, he follows the sunlight and tries to touch it with his finger...
From this, I gathered that he, like me, had spent many months only in darkness... Are you seeing my family members lately? My mother came for a visit before you came the other day<2>, as I told you. My wife, who usually comes once a week without fail, happened to come on the very day I moved to this room. According to her, the family is always fine, but whether that's true or not, I doubt she tells me the truth. Whether the family is doing well or not, it's no use for me to know, and I don't try to find out, but still, worries suddenly arise from time to time.

You, too, who live alone, sometimes starving, sometimes eating, must be anxious about my family. It's all so pointless (ツマランコトダ)... The other day, I told my mother and my wife, please, whether you starve or eat, just go down to the countryside, don't think of me, just don't let me see you, I urged them so much, but they don't listen.

As things stand now, I expect my sentence won't be more than 10 years, but it will likely be six or seven years at most, or four or five years of imprisonment at least. So what's the point of them staying in Seoul just to see my face occasionally? Please, even if they have to beg, I wish they would go down to the countryside so I don't have to see their plight before me. Also. The other day during exercise time, I was quietly looking out the window from my room, and I happened to see C-kun, who is imprisoned with us, quickly pass by. Of course, you probably visit him occasionally, but seeing his shadow flash by made my heart jump and tears almost came to my eyes. At that moment, I realized how weak my nerves had become. I know he's about five or six cells down from me, but seeing him like this was the first time in three or four months, besides seeing R-kun like this last month. And, that C-kun looked terribly pale. Besides him, R H-kun, M-kun, and the other comrades, I don't know where they all are. Of course, they must all be in the same prison, but... well... let's leave it at that.

Letter 2
I received your reply letter. I also received and read the book 000 you sent in (差入). However, I can't find much interest in these kinds of books, so I'm spending time either in quiet contemplation (黙想) or just sitting idly. This contemplation, too, wasn't easy at first because my mind was restless.<3>

To list the main reasons, hunger tormented me the most. Once I forgot about the hunger, the next thing was the impulse related to sex (性). After overcoming the urge of sexual desire, came the impulse related to passion (情慾) (this passion wasn't directed towards my current wife, but towards some unknown person of the opposite sex (異性)). Then came thoughts regretting past mistakes, worries about home, and besides these, frustratingly, the stuffiness arising physiologically (生理上) and mentally. These things made it unbearable, but as time passed, I've calmed down considerably. As time passed, my intestines must have hardened, as worries about hunger have now almost completely disappeared. Thus, I can now manage contemplation quite well. However, occasionally, the explosive symptoms are truly unbearably painful.

Then, my mother, who was staying at someone else's house, felt unwell and came home, apparently. Thinking about it, it seems the elderly woman must have fallen ill from excessive hardship and worry while working as a housemaid, and has come home to lie down.

Hey, my friend! Even if our parents, wives, and children fall into the most miserable circumstances in ordinary times, could we only look after them? But think about it. The mindset of people who live on, forcibly enduring while seeing such pitiful sights before their eyes...
My mother seems incredibly pitiful to my heart. And my young sister too... Actually, the thought of my young sister being pitiful is even stronger. The little one suffers from hunger, and although she's of school age (學齡), she can't even attend school...

Hey, I've never once had a visit with my younger sister until now, but as soon as you see this letter, please send my sister in right away. I know doing this is extremely cruel (殘酷) to the child, but I want to endure that dreadful sight and see her...
The other day, my wife told me that the day after she came for a visit, she would start working as a factory girl at some rubber factory, earning about ten won a month. So, from now on, she wouldn't be able to visit as often as before. Also, some movie company (映畵會社) asked her to become a movie actress, saying the income (收入)<4> would be substantial, but she refused, saying she wouldn't do such a thing. Although her looks aren't particularly striking, perhaps because she's still young, such temptations seem to come her way.

And after suffering that terrible severe illness (重症) recently, she still hasn't fully recovered her health, yet she's going to work in such a factory. I don't know what will become of her. I try not to have these worries and trivial thoughts, but they keep coming up on their own.
To put it bluntly, the biggest burden in my heart right now is these people (my family). I would truly be relieved if I saw them all perish (沒死) at once. It's unbearable to have my nerves constantly frayed like this indefinitely....
You know I'm not just a person swayed by sentimentality. You once said,
"A fierce person like you is too weak when it comes to your wife..."
As they say, even a tiger-like man falls for a woman. It's not strange that you'd say something like this, but it's not like that for me. If you knew the inside story of us, the so-called couple... Because I haven't told you the story of our relationship until now, it's easy for you to find my actions unsatisfactory. Since I don't know when I'll get out into the world, while I'm on the subject of my wife, I thought I'd tell just you the story of our relationship and what I've been through these past few years.

It was when I was serving as a church elder (kwŏnsa) and teaching at the elementary school attached to the church in XX-kun town. It was right after the March First Movement (긔미운동), and even humble private schools were flooded with male and female students. We needed more male teachers and many female teachers, so we requested them from Seoul. A female teacher arrived, along with another person named H, and one more new male teacher. The once desolate school suddenly became tense with the increase in male and female teachers, a new atmosphere, a certain scent of sex (性) circulated, and even the pastor-<5>—the school principal—who hadn't been particularly concerned before, perhaps fearing moral disorder between the male and female teachers, frequently cautioned the teachers and showed an unusually stern attitude towards me compared to before.
However, regarding that female teacher named Maria Song, far from sensing any scent of sex, I privately thought, "Would anyone even fall in love with such a woman?"
But her habit of crouching silently in a corner even in bustling places, her perpetually carefree demeanor, the lonely shade that flickered even when she smiled reluctantly, or the traces of abuse and fear commonly seen in servants or pre-marital daughters-in-law (밋며누리) visible in her face and eyes – judging from all these, I could tell her personality and behavior were shaped by unfortunate circumstances. (I later learned she was an orphan (狐兒), raised in an orphanage, and educated thanks to the church). So, I merely thought, "She's a pitiful person." Not only that, but even if I deliberately ignored her, the way that pitiful person maintained silence about everything, without making a fuss—that endless silence—after observing it for several months, it seemed to leave a distinct impression on my mind.

At that time, there was a young man named H, a fellow teacher, who had a strong interest in literature, devoured things like English literature, had a rather sentimental way of speaking and acting, and occasionally showed a very sympathetic attitude towards Maria. Whether Maria, starved for affection, was drawn to this sentimental H, I don't know, but they often sat together alone, exchanging stories. The cunning and quick-witted pastor noticed this and seemed to be carefully watching the two of them.
One time, after school, the pastor, who was the principal, called the three of us—me, H, and Maria—and stood us up. With a grim face, he said,
"This school is different from other schools; it's a sacred church school. It's a serious matter if shameful things happen between male and female teachers here.<6> The behavior of H and Maria is absolutely unforgivable. Not only must they leave this school, but they should also be excommunicated. And you (pointing at me), holding the position of head teacher, can you be without responsibility?"
At this, Maria, in her usual crouching posture, sat in a corner, her face deathly pale, trembling uncontrollably. H, with a flushed face and tears welling in his eyes, said,
"Pastor, there was absolutely nothing like that between Maria and me. It's true I visited Maria last night, but I swear to God, nothing like that happened."
Following this, Maria also barely opened her trembling lips,
"I swear to God, nothing like that happened," and then collapsed, seemingly fainting (昏絶), sobbing.
Seeing the behavior of these two, I guessed it was a false accusation and said,
"Pastor! Just looking at their behavior, it seems like a false accusation."
The pastor, glaring at Maria like a weasel eyeing a chicken, retorted,
"False accusation?! No way. They must be excommunicated."
Suddenly, I remembered hearing a rumor recently that the pastor, who had become a widower seven or eight months ago, had taken an interest in Maria since her arrival two months prior, and had secretly proposed marriage not long ago, but for some reason, Maria had refused. Just then, Maria, who had seemed half-dead, sat up and looked at the pastor with slightly venomous eyes,
"That's you, Pastor, falsely accusing people." The pastor jumped up with a dreadful expression,
"How dare you! Accusing?!" he shouted.
At this point, convinced the pastor was acting out of jealousy, I felt a surge of anger and said,
"Excuse me, Pastor. I suspect these people are being falsely accused, and besides, if a man and woman genuinely love each other,<7> what's wrong with that?"
"Genuine? Fornication between men and women is one of the Ten Commandments, meaning it goes against God's will first and foremost."
"I believe genuine love, apart from adultery, is by no means against God's will. If love and adultery are recorded with the same meaning in the Bible, then the Bible needs to be torn up and fixed."
At these words, the pastor stared as if dumbfounded, then said,
"Such people are worse than Pharisees. There's no choice but to excommunicate them all."
"No! Excommunicate? You, Pastor, have committed sins greater than ours."
"What did you say?... Just try getting excommunicated!"
Just then, a servant boy came in and announced that the county magistrate had arrived. The pastor hurriedly rushed outside to greet this guest.
Right then and there, I resigned from my positions as church elder and school teacher, renounced my faith (背敎), and went straight to stay at the house of C-kun, who is now imprisoned like me, in the neighboring village.
Later, thanks to the persuasion of the evangelist and other staff, the matter of the two's excommunication was somehow buried. H left for another place after a while. Much later, one night, Maria came looking for me, sat there without saying much of anything, and then returned. A couple of days after that, I received a letter from Maria. The letter contained words implying love for me. However, I still hadn't felt anything particularly compelling about her. Yet, being starved for sex (性), the impulse to accept her love arose slightly, but then the thought "For an unremarkable woman..." came, and instead, an unpleasant feeling surfaced.
After that, letters came again and again, but I didn't reply. Finally, perhaps intending to make a final declaration, she wrote that if I didn't accept her love, she would even die. At this point, it's true my heart was moved to some<8> extent. I considered replying with comforting words, but somehow a strange feeling arose, and I decided against it.
A week later, on Sunday evening, another letter arrived. Opening it, I found shocking words written inside. It was written like a last will and testament (遺言書), ending with, "I am now heading to the land of death."
The moment I read this passage, the thought "What's all this about dying after exchanging just a few letters without any deep connection? She's narrow-minded and petty," flashed through my mind, but then I muttered to myself, "Is she really dying?" Instantly alarmed, I rushed outside towards the church. Near the main gate of the church, several people were gathered, whispering amongst themselves. Avoiding them, I tried to take another path when a former fellow teacher ran up beside me.
"Hey, Mr. XXX, long time no see. By the way, Maria Song tried to commit suicide by taking poison but was discovered. She's in the hospital now. It's just my guess, but I think it's probably because of Mr. XXX (meaning me)."
My heart sank.
"What about her condition? Is she alive?"
"She didn't die, but we don't know yet what will happen. It actually happened this morning—"
Without another word, I ran to the hospital. Ignoring the male and female church members gathered there, I rushed straight to a spot not far from where Maria lay and stood watching. Maria, lying unconscious, her face turned sideways, had a pale complexion like faded paper. Just then, a doctor passed by.
"Will this patient die?"
"Well, she was in critical condition, but we'll have to wait and see."
I stepped back a few paces and stood there, blankly watching.
"A pitiful life like that dying because of me!" The thought suddenly struck me, and I immediately wanted to run over and grasp the patient's wrist,<9> but I forcefully restrained myself. After standing there for a while,
The patient's lips started trembling convulsively (發作的), her head swayed slightly, she let out a long, seemingly suffocated sigh, and turned her head the other way. Following her sigh, everyone else seemed to let out their breath in relief, sighing all at once. Although I knew the patient's sigh couldn't possibly have any conscious meaning, something inexplicably stirred deep within my chest, overwhelming me.
A little later, the doctor came out, performed a brief examination on the patient, and declared that there was definitely no need to worry. The people who had gathered started leaving one by one, two by two. Seeing no point in staying awkwardly until the patient regained consciousness, I left too. I felt a heavy lump lodged in my chest, constantly churning.
That night, I went back again and saw that Maria had fully regained consciousness. Disregarding the people surrounding her, I rushed over and took Maria's hand. Maria's eyes, which usually seemed clouded by a mist of suspicion, now appeared darker. The way she looked at me, as if piercing through my heart, held a complex expression—was it a look of curse (咀呪), of plea (哀願), or of question (疑問)? Various complex emotions radiated towards my eyes. I looked into her eyes with a feeling that seemed to well up naturally from the marriage vow (結婚意識) itself— "I will pity and love you." And so, the gazes of our two pairs of eyes met and lingered in the short space between us for quite a while, as if a rainbow had formed. In a trembling voice, I said,
"Maria-ssi, I'm sorry. Please trust my heart." She turned her averted head back towards me, looked at me intently for a moment with questioning eyes, as if asking "Is it really true?", then closed her eyes again, turned her head back the other way, and long tear tracks streamed down her cheek, glistening in the lamplight. I squeezed her hand firmly once more and let go.....
After that, she was discharged from the hospital, faced excommunication, and ended up coming to me for good.<10>
Thus, burdened with this unwelcome new love, I found Korea tiresome and just went over to Tōkyō.

Life in Tōkyō was uneventful. It just marked a major turning point (轉換) in my life. That is to say, a turning point in my ideological life (思想生活). It was a time when social thought was rising like a tide throughout Japan, and I too, through intellectual understanding and life experience, felt new ideas stirring my blood. I gathered like-minded comrades and embarked on fervent propaganda activities.

Hey, my friend. There is surely no time more filled with emotion (感激) and passion (情熱) than when a person finds the truth (眞理) of a new life and embarks on the path of new creation (創造).
The trust and love between comrades, the emotional surge when joining a meeting—it felt like there was nothing more vital in human society. It seemed that even the filth of the most terrifying social evils would burn up and melt away before this fervent flame.
Perhaps this could be called the naive passion of the Tōkyō era (憧憬時代). But when moving from this mood-driven movement (氣分運動) to actual practice (實際運動), disillusionment struck, even with the comrades I had trusted so much. When everyone's flaws were exposed, their weak wills and impure ambitions became visible, I couldn't help but despise them. Even amidst this, C-kun, who is now imprisoned with me, was perhaps an exception, always moving forward sincerely and steadfastly. Apart from him, there was hardly anyone trustworthy. Maybe it was my rashness, but from then on, I came to doubt and hate all human beings. The "nihilistic" (니히리스틕) and "terroristic" (테르리스틔) tendencies in my thinking also began around that time. I felt the urge to kill and destroy whatever I encountered. Not only that, but I also came to hate myself. I finally discovered that I too had weaknesses and impurities like others... This whole universe became something to glare at. This was a very dangerous place. One could easily become warped (히네꾸레루... 비트러)<11>... twist. And once that happens, it's easy to fall irrevocably onto the path of degradation. However, I tried not to deceive myself, fought sincerely against myself, and tried to hold onto myself.
Amidst this, my so-called wife, from what I heard, pretended to understand this new love, but in reality, she was nothing more than an ignorant simpleton. How could she possibly understand someone like me? Consequently, even she became hateful to me.
Living hand-to-mouth, earning one day's wages (품파리) to eat for that day, how could it be otherwise? Sometimes, out of anger, on the way home after receiving my wages at sunset, I'd end up going straight to a bar/pachinko parlor (빠ㅅ짐), drinking and carousing with fellow laborers. Coming home late at night, I'd find my wife asleep, curled up in a corner of the room, not even having managed to cook dinner. Seeing this, a wave of pity would wash over me, and I'd rush over, hug her, press my cheek against hers, and kiss her. This kind of trivial drama occurred routinely every few days. Then, intending to take direct action, I joined the XXXXX group (XXXXX단체) to do something, but ended up in Tōkyō prison for a year.
Some time after I entered prison, my wife, unable to live alone in Tōkyō, returned to Korea.
After returning to Korea, she wrote to me at least two or three times a month. The letters mostly described her sorrowful situation, her worries about me, or how much she missed me. My lonely heart, yearning for human connection anew, found that with each arriving letter, with the passage of time, my lingering feelings for her and my longing to see her grew stronger, almost like being caught in a new love affair (戀情). Thus, I eagerly longed for the day I could get out and see her.
Can a person's feelings change so much depending on the situation?!

Upon release from Tōkyō prison, I rushed back to my hometown. Wouldn't I go to see my mother, who was staying at my maternal grandparents' house? My wife, having no place to settle her heart, had been shuttling between Seoul and the countryside. She had stayed here for a few months recently but then left for somewhere unknown, they said. But then, didn't I hear astonishing news? To borrow my mother's words:<12>
"She's pregnant (태중), almost eight or nine months along. Her belly is swollen, yet I don't know where she's wandering off to like that." When I heard this, I couldn't believe it no matter what.
"Pregnant? No. It must be some other illness."
"No. She's definitely pregnant. I suspected it initially and asked her, but maybe because it's her first child and she felt shy, she tried to deceive me. But can an old woman not recognize another woman's condition? Counting the months since she returned from Tōkyō, it matches up. So, although I worry about our situation, on the other hand, I also felt happy."
Hearing this, I couldn't help but feel suspicious. Suddenly realizing the anger welling up (상렬), a headache instantly started. Feelings of jealousy and turmoil exploded uncontrollably. My mother counted the months since her return from Tōkyō, but by my calculation, it had already been a year and four months since we, the couple, had lived together. Moreover, the fact that there hadn't been a single letter for the past few months made me highly suspicious. So I asked again,
"So, she didn't say where she was going?"
"Well, she said she was going to XX the other day, but since there's been no news even though it's not far, she must not be there."
Ignoring my mother's attempts to dissuade me, I immediately set off, went to T station (T역), and tried to find out where she was, but all I could learn was that she had gone somewhere a month ago and couldn't be found since.
So, heading towards Seoul from there, I pondered deeply in the train car.
"Did she really commit such an act because she couldn't bear sexual loneliness (性的孤獨)? Then why did she claim to love me so passionately? Even if it was a few months ago... could that really happen?...
Judging from her usual behavior, she seemed quite determined up to a point, so even if there was temptation... But women are weak, especially someone like her who isn't exactly steadfast..."
"Then where could she be now?... How did she fall into such temptation, and now, feeling regret,<13> or perhaps feeling too ashamed to face me, or rather, unable to bear the great fear of what lies ahead, maybe..."
Thinking this far, a different suspicion suddenly struck me, and a terrifying thought arose.
"Could she have secretly gone somewhere and committed suicide?... Ah! Really?"
As this thought occurred, picturing her final departing figure in my mind, a feeling of pity now surged stronger than anything else.
"Anyway, I need to go to Seoul and find out," I thought, and came to Seoul.
In Seoul, I searched high and low, but there was absolutely no trace of her. It seemed there was no other way to find out within Korea. Thus, the more I thought about it, the more my heart felt like mourning her dead soul, and sorrow arose whenever I thought of her.
I spent about ten days like this. One day, I was sitting alone in my room, suddenly thinking pitiful thoughts about her and feeling very unwell, when someone came asking for me. I peeked my head out the door, and look! My wife was standing outside the door.
The moment I saw her, hatred flared up as if I could devour her on the spot, and my eyes staring at her must have shown this murderous intent. I pretended not to see her, abruptly turned back into the room, and sat down. She stumbled in after me, collapsed, grabbed my knees, and started weeping. I repeatedly pushed away the hands touching my body, saying coldly,
"Hey—hey—why are you touching my body?"
Even when pushed away harshly, she clung, sobbed, and her hands reached out again, so I finally kicked her away.
Huddled in a corner of the room, she sobbed uncontrollably, choking out between long wails, "I... I was already planning to die... but... just once... just wanted to see you once..."
"What's there to see?"<14>
"Instead of... dying by my own hand... dying by your... hand is my wish."
"Me kill you?! Stain my hands with dirty blood?... Ugh!..."
I shouted and rushed outside. The street seemed pitch black. I went straight to Pagoda Park (빠고다 공원), sat slumped on a bench, just staring at the ground, and remained like that for who knows how long.
Imagining her dying again, I thought,
"What? She deserves to die."
And though I made up my mind to cut her off, picturing her dying form, a lingering feeling would softly arise again. Then it would flip back to hateful emotions, and then again change to pity. This cycle (反復) of emotions flickered relentlessly. Finally,
"Still, she shouldn't die!" I thought, standing up abruptly, only then realizing it had already gotten dark. I ran straight back to where I was staying.
Just as I feared! When I returned home, she was gone. In the middle of the room floor lay a single letter, the envelope addressed in pencil. I quickly tore it open; inside, also written faintly in pencil, were the words:
"I ask nothing from you. Only that after you hear I am dead, if you can then find it in your heart to forgive me, I will have no regrets even after death. This unsightly body will not appear before you a second time!" it said.
Just now, as I returned down this alley, hadn't I seen a female student flash past under the streetlamp at the other end of the alley? Wondering if it might have been her, I went outside and asked the landlord's child. They said she hadn't left long ago. So I immediately ran down that alleyway.
This was truly fortunate! At the far end of that alley, like someone who had lost their mind or was drunk, stumbling along, barely able to walk properly, was indeed her. Perhaps her mind was in extreme turmoil (混亂), or maybe it was because she was heavily pregnant<15> and her body was heavy. I ran up and grabbed her firmly.
"Hey! Let's go. Come with me. I'll forgive all—all your sins..."
I covered her up and dragged her back to my place.
She continued to weep and wail, saying no matter what, she couldn't possibly hope to live, crying endlessly for some time.
I kept soothing her, saying I would gladly forgive her completely. I too became unnecessarily sentimental and we ended up clinging to each other and crying for a while. And so, the situation calmed down, but.
Looking back, how could a person endure that? Truly, for some time after this, it was the period of the most extreme mental anguish I've ever experienced in my life.
When I looked at her lying next to me with her swollen belly, hateful feelings would surge again, the urge to stab her to death with a knife would well up fiercely, and I'd kick her away and rush outside for a while. But then, when pitiful thoughts started, I couldn't stop myself from rushing back in, hugging her, pressing my cheek against hers, repeating the drama, even more intense than the ones in Tōkyō, several times a day. Comparing my home life then, it was like standing on a high, sharp peak; this side was shadow, that side was sun, and with one misstep, it felt like falling thousands of feet.
Eventually, I tried to kill off those sharp nerves. No, I decided to see how deep emotions could go, to reach the very end. When that terrifying, conflicting emotion surged, I deliberately forced myself to sit still and confront this dreadful reality of life, this hateful, dirty animal (referring to his wife), staring intently (凝視) and enduring.
I won't speak further here about the changes my emotions underwent beyond this point. I'll leave that to your imagination. After that, my wife fortunately gave birth to a boy safely. And since then, my fluctuating emotions from before have vanished somewhere. All that remains is the memory of seemingly teetering atop some intricate spire (尖塔) in a dream.<16> And then, I committed the act that led me here, as you know. Anyway, this has been the past course of our marriage. My story has become too long-winded. I'll stop here.

The prisoner who was with me moved to another cell the other day. The sunlight that used to shine into my room is gradually diminishing. Soon, that too will probably disappear!

Letter 3
The other day, you came with my mother for a visit. But your time ran out, and you had to leave without seeing me. Perhaps because it was the last time she would see me, my mother's demeanor then! After that, I went back to my cell and sat there all day feeling unwell. Anyway, it's a relief that she's gone far away and won't appear before my eyes again.
My wife hasn't visited or even sent a letter for three weeks now. Maybe she can't come because she's busy, but it's strange that she doesn't even write. Perhaps her feelings towards me have changed during this time. If they have, what does it matter...
Hey, my friend. Other people's hearts, especially a woman's heart, and furthermore, what the world calls truth—I don't know how far to trust them and how far not to. Willpower! Of course, everyone absolutely needs willpower. But weak men and women simply cannot possess it. As for that willpower, it comes from and grows within sincerity (純實). In other words, I believe sincerity gives birth to willpower, and willpower, in turn, gives birth to firm conviction (信念). It's not about believing in the abstract concept of 'humanity' separate from everything else; it's about believing in 'myself'. In other words, a sincere self (自我), possessing strong willpower, fights against everything, and in the process, a great conviction takes hold.<17>
To put this simply based on my experience: I, who used to stumble along with weak steps, struggling even to hold onto my own conscience, have fought for a long time, and as a result, my own bones are becoming stronger. This time, because of that incident, I was dragged into the police station and endured horrific torture (惡形) and torment. Even facing death, I didn't want to deceive myself.
Hey, think about it. That place where they toy with you like a cat with a mouse hole—just thinking about it makes my skin crawl! If a person wants to speak of truth or conviction, they must crawl out of the pit of death, covered in blood, before they can speak. I don't believe one can find the path and conviction regarding life (生) through mere desk-bound fantasies or knowledge acquired while lounging comfortably in the sun.
Anyway, I think my mind is very strong now. As things stand, I believe I have no fear whatsoever, no matter what mental or physical suffering comes my way in the future. I don't feel like writing more, so I'll stop. Seeing the weather turning quite cool in the mornings and evenings, it seems autumn is setting in.

Letter 4
The other day, after the verdict (判決) was finalized, I saw you standing with others beside the courthouse gate on my way out. That was already a month ago. I saw the letter you sent during that time, but somehow, I really haven't felt like picking up a pen lately. It's truly been a long time. I didn't feel like writing this letter either and hesitated for several days, but decided to write just one more word.
It's about something else. My wife has finally left. After hearing this news, although not like before, I couldn't overcome my resentment for several days. Now, it doesn't matter. I just hope she goes and lives well. Please know that I mean this sincerely. Her brief letter to me reads as follows:
"I am going back to H. I can never forget what you did, even in death, and I cannot repay your kindness even with death,<18> but I also cannot abandon H, so I have no choice but to go. Please think of me as a dead woman and forget me," it said.
She went to H. Do you know H? He's the man she briefly had her first love with, and as I mentioned in the previous letter, the man she apparently lived with for about a month while I was in Tōkyō prison. Perhaps the lingering attachment to a first love is quite strong. Going to H might be better for her than staying with me. In a way, she left quite cleanly.
They're all gone... I am now laying out all the events of the past days before my eyes once more and staring blankly. My mind feels hollowed out. There is nothing holding me back. I am only grasping the solid strength of rebirth (新生). This is because I know there is nothing greater than truth (眞實)—living truthfully without deceiving oneself—and I have the confidence (自信) to carry that out to the very end. If I manage to complete these 5 years and go out into the world, I feel I will emerge with greater strength than before. I know you too will be glad that I have grown this much in a short time. Wishing well for the woman who finally left, I put down my pen.
-End-<19>

Furthermore, it reflects the intellectual currents of the time, showing the appeal and subsequent disillusionment with political activism (likely socialist or nationalist) and the turn towards more radical, individualistic, or even nihilistic philosophies among some intellectuals facing colonial oppression and personal hardship. The frank discussion of emotional turmoil, suicidal ideation, and relationship dynamics provides valuable social history insights.
