**발행일:** 1926년 02월 01일
**기사제목:** 農夫 鄭道龍(其2) (Farmer Jeong Do-ryong (Part 2))
**필자:** 李箕永
**기사형태:** 소설 (Novel)

**Farmer Jeong Do-ryong**
**Yi Ki-yŏng**

"There are many times I greatly envy the fate of that cow lying peacefully by the stream bank in the grassy field, chewing its cud after eating green grass. If you work diligently, they just make you work more. That's why the cow is as lazy as possible. Besides, if a cow is diligent, wouldn't people just eat it? If it's diligent, it will grow old quickly and enter the slaughterhouse even sooner."

"Really! Why do they slaughter and eat cows that are so useful to people?"
Kŭmsuni asked, looking intently at her mother. Her mother smiled faintly.
"Because they are useful to people, they slaughter and eat them," she said. Although she answered this way, she herself didn't know what she meant.

"Similarly, the poor are the cows of the rich."
Kŭmsŏk chimed in right after his mother finished speaking.
"What good is it to struggle day and night over things that don't amount to anything, like you do? I wonder how much more beneficial it would be to take a nap during the time spent doing such useless things."
Kŭmsŏk looked at Kŭmsuni again with a smile.
"Oh! Brother... stop it! Even without that, Koreans are known for being lazy."
Her expression turned sullen, but then, as if another thought occurred to her, her face soon brightened with a cheerful smile.<40>
"Brother, you're just like the lazy man in the old story!"
Immediately following this, she exclaimed in a voice mixed with bright laughter.
"What story?"
Kŭmsŏk prompted her.
"Then I'll tell you the story!"
Before even starting the story, Kŭmsuni couldn't hold back her laughter, giggling, "Hee hee."
"Hey, Brother! Ho... Long ago, there was a man, and he was so lazy, perhaps just like you, Brother!"
And she laughed even more.
"So? Tell the story before you laugh!"
"-Okay- So!...(She covered her mouth with her hand) So one day, he was worrying that he had no rice, and a neighbor who couldn't bear to watch said, 'Then come get a sack of rice from our house.' That man was startled and exclaimed in alarm, 'Ah! Who would carry it, and who would thresh it to eat?'"
Having barely finished the story, Kŭmsuni collapsed, clutching her stomach as if dying of laughter. Kŭmsŏk, who was blankly watching Kŭmsuni's shoulders shaking, still smiled faintly and replied thus:
"Isn't that man's fate rather good? Don't you know that all the rich people now are like that man? Hey - there are many who wouldn't lift a broom, let alone thresh a sack of rice. The lazier they are, the richer they become. Why? Because the lazier they are, the more the labor of workers like us increases. Can people like us, the poor, afford to be lazy? If we rest even for a day, there's no food in our mouths tomorrow. Therefore, our family must also study laziness. - You learn from me, and Mother learns from Father.-"
The mother and Kŭmsuni laughed. Kŭmsŏk, seemingly indifferent, lazily raised his heavy backside. He shouldered his A-frame carrier and went out to the fields.<41>
Kŭmsŏk was now eighteen, and Kŭmsuni was sixteen this year. But their mother seemed determined not to give them another sibling, as there was absolutely no sign of one. So Kŭmsuni would sometimes whine like this:
"Mother! Why don't you have a baby?"
Then the mother, perhaps having nothing to say, would just smile faintly.
"Just two more, no more! One boy, one girl. So I can be an older sister too. I hate always being bossed around by Brother-. No, have two girls! So Brother can see how we three sisters boss him around!"
Once, when Kŭmsuni said this, her mother looked at her as if dumbfounded and laughed.
"Does this child think babies are made like simple millet cakes!"
At that time, Kŭmsŏk, with a meaningful smile, boasted as if it were truly so:
"Even if ten brats like you came at me, never mind three, could you take me on?"
So Kŭmsuni pestered again:
"Let's see! Is that so? Mother, hurry up and have them!"
She acted childishly, making her mother laugh again. It was already a long time ago - an old story, but...

"My dear wife! Let's just have two."
Jeong Do-ryong had said this to his wife one night before falling asleep.
"Let's have just one son and one daughter. What's the use of having many? If we can't raise them well and teach them well - if they're not just eating whatever they can find like piglets - shouldn't we aim for quality over quantity?"
At that time, the wife turned pale and said,
"Who can force that! We have them as they come, raise them as we can!"
"Still, you seem to have quite a desire for children!"
"Well, if we don't have children, what joy is there in living?"<42>
"That's the problem, talking like that from the start. Even if we can't force it, if we don't just covet many but resolve to have even just one excellent - good child - and put our heart into it, we might be able to have such a child. Don't you know the old saying, 'Sincerity moves heaven'? Moreover, in our poor circumstances, having many children only brings suffering to everyone, which is a sin, a wrongdoing.-"
"What if I keep getting pregnant?"
The wife glanced at him then and smiled brightly.
"We'll induce an abortion!"
"Oh my! What a dreadful thing to say!"
The wife's eyes widened in a shocked expression. Indeed, when she thought back to the pains of childbirth, she couldn't help but think it would be nice if she didn't have any more. However, like some women who, despite swearing after a difficult first birth that they'd be a dog's daughter if they had another child, get pregnant again within a year, go through the ordeal, and then say they could die for love of that child, when she heard Kŭmsuni whining for a younger sibling, the thought of having just one more... stealthily arose in a corner of her mind. So, when she thought, "Am I already past childbearing age?", a mix of relief and regret washed over her, and she once blurted out resentfully to her husband,
"Your wish has come true!"
But the husband, eyes wide with bewilderment, asked,
"Huh? What wish?"
making her laugh despite herself. Even now, thinking of such things, she looked resentfully at her husband, then, as if suddenly remembering, said,
"Oh! Yongsoe's family sold their third daughter again for 300 nyang!"
She relayed the story she had heard from Ch'uni's mother who had visited earlier.
"Well done! Speaking of selling daughters, wouldn't it be fine if they diligently produced them every month like rabbits in the moon!"<43>
"Don't talk such nonsense!"
"What's nonsensical about it! And since their daughter-in-law is pregnant again, tell them to pray and offer sacrifices, begging for a daughter this time too. Then, if they sell their granddaughter for about 500 nyang, they'll have a solid bit of capital!"
Jeong Do-ryong exclaimed bluntly.
"Marrying off a son before he's even grown, making him breed like a boar, and selling the offspring as they come - that's truly a magic money pot (화수분). If they're lucky enough to only have daughters - but the Goddess of Childbirth (삼신할머니) is mischievous and sometimes creates boys. Yet Yongsoe's family is so blessed they had three daughters in a row and sold them for two hundred, three hundred, four hundred nyang. Well, it's not just Yongsoe's family. Even in so-called yangban households, they do similar things. How convenient this world is! No matter what evil deeds one commits, one can skillfully conceal them with beautiful names-"
And he snorted. He would get angry like this whenever he saw injustice, whether it concerned others or himself.

One day, as Jeong Do-ryong was passing Yongsoe's house, Yongsoe was mercilessly whipping his fourth daughter. The child looked no older than four or five. Seeing this, Jeong Do-ryong suddenly rushed over, slapped Yongsoe across the face, and snatched the switch from his hand. Yongsoe, utterly stunned, just mumbled and stared blankly like an idiot.
"Why are you hitting a small child? Isn't that child your family's magic money pot? Beating her, leaving her naked and hungry when she's young, then selling her off when she turns ten - are you even a human being, you scum?"
As Jeong Do-ryong glared fiercely, Yongsoe stood with his mouth agape, not knowing what to do. Jeong Do-ryong continued.
"You pathetic fool! There is nothing more despicable in the world than abusing those weaker than oneself. You grovel before those stronger than you, so what right do you have to look down on and beat anyone? None. It is an act that affirms (肯定) the violent. - Whether it's a yangban looking down on a commoner, an official abusing the people, a man mistreating a woman, or a parent mistreating a child,<44> isn't it all the same?"
He paused briefly, then glared at Yongsoe again.
"Humans are dull creatures; unless they experience it themselves, they don't understand others' affairs. Look at the educated yangban who supposedly learned the ethics of Confucius; through writing alone, there's no good deed they don't know, yet among them, I've seen many who commit acts worse than ignorant folk like us. They don't know the suffering of us farmers. It's like the rich not knowing the plight of the poor; even if the neighbor's family starves for days or freezes to death, they just watch indifferently, thinking only of their full stomachs as fortunate. Those fools suffer from amnesia, forgetting what happened just moments ago, thinking only of what's before their eyes now. Therefore, to make them practice the knowledge they've learned, they must first taste suffering. If possible, I'd grab every last one of them, shove them into that field ridge over there, hand each a hoe and shovel, and beat them with a whip like driving oxen to make them work. Then I'd ask them, 'How does it taste?' Otherwise, all their talk of Jesus, God, Amitabha Buddha, Confucius, Mencius, heroes, and great men is useless. No, when did their power ever save humanity?"
He addressed Yongsoe as if he were one of them. But Yongsoe still offered no reply.
"Because she's my child, I can do as I please? You might think that, but where does a parent get the right to beat a child? Humans were given limbs to work, not to beat others indiscriminately. Parents, the knowledgeable, everyone is human, so there shouldn't be much difference between one's own child and another's. Isn't it tyrannical to force others to conform only to one's own will? No matter how much you beat a pockmarked face because you hate it, there's no way it will suddenly become smooth! From today, you should learn from the beasts!"
"What? From the beasts?..."
Yongsoe asked, his face reddening, looking anxious.
"Yes! Learn from the beasts! Don't swallows raise their young in your house? Learn from the mother swallow - rather than the words of Confucius<45> or anyone else."
Yongsoe only understood this as an insult. Though furious inside, he endured it and listened.

After Yongsoe was disgraced like this, the villagers feared Jeong Do-ryong even more. However, they had respected and revered him even before that. His strong physique and righteous spirit naturally inspired trust in anyone. Even those who disliked him inwardly admired his actions. Taking advantage of his fitting name, everyone called him Jeong Do-ryong (鄭道令) of Kyeryongsan Mountain.
This respect for him extended even to the yangban village across the way - even the renowned Kim Chusa - could not treat him lightly. Thus, the people of this village, who used to live like mice unable to assert themselves in a house with a cat, gained considerable strength through him. Consequently, everyone in the village, adults and children alike, truly trusted him like the legendary Jeong Do-ryong, and obeyed whatever he said. Naturally, all matters big and small in this village were resolved through his planning and direction. Among those who loved him most were the young children and women. This was because he denounced the tyranny of ignorant men and parents. Just as he had recently disciplined Yongsoe-

4.

Despite all this, the lives of the people in this village were truly pitiful. Yongsoe at least sold his daughters, but there were sparse homes with large families, including elderly parents and young children, where food ran out and they went hungry. Moreover, it was now the difficult barley hump (보리 고개), the hardest time for farming households. Rice seedlings needed planting, but the barley wasn't ripe yet, so many families harvested unripe green barley, squeezed out the milky water, and made gruel. It's said that no one is more pitiful than a slave in this world, but a freeman without food or clothing is even more pitiful than a slave. No, the current propertyless people have become masterless slaves without food or clothing.<46>
Neighbor Ch'uni's grandmother, her body swaying precariously like a tree root in the wind, barely supported herself with a cane, standing vacant, staring at the barley field. It was as if she were saying,
"Barley! Ripen quickly. You must know that our house has run out of food! It's better to appeal to you than to plead with heartless people for high-interest grain loans. You are the one who will save my family's lives! Barley! Ripen quickly. I don't want to talk to people anymore."
Was she truly uttering these words? Her sunken mouth moved ceaselessly. And the barley, as if understanding her words, bowed its head sorrowfully. The rustling of its leaves and stalks in the wind seemed to mourn the pitiful fate of this old woman. Jeong Do-ryong, standing in front of his own house, gazed blankly at her, as if trying to decipher the meaning of the old woman's posture. Unconsciously, a heavy sigh escaped his lips...
Ch'uni's family had recently been getting by making gruel with the measure of millet given by Jeong Do-ryong's family. She, her widowed daughter-in-law, and her young grandson Ch'uni barely survived by farming a few *majigi* of rice paddy.

The civilized people who frequently visit this country, or those who come to this land and boast about how well they live, are said to mock their miserable lives. "Is this how people live?" they ask. Oh, civilized people! Your knowledge is indeed superior to theirs, that's a fact. You know how to live in luxury with that knowledge. But do you know where your happiness comes from?
You yourselves often say that Rome (羅馬) was not built in a day. Wasn't its golden age built on a century of sacrifice, the blood and sweat of slaves? That's right! Your civilization is entirely a mushroom sprouted from the skeletons of countless slaves. You are ghosts who live by eating these mushrooms. With mouths that suck our blood, you wear red smiles like vegetables - what terrifying demons (아귀) you are! Truly, the human demons are you - you...
If hypocrisy were removed from you, nothing would remain. Hypocrisy is the god (神) of life for you.
Therefore, you<47> worship the god of hypocrisy. The god of hypocrisy conquers this world disguised as justice (正義). Your morals, laws, politics, etiquette - they are all full of hypocrisy. Listen to a story like this - they say a certain person treated a goblin (독가비) well, and the goblin gave him a cap. So, wearing the goblin cap, he went around in broad daylight stealing rice and cloth from shops, yet the owners had no idea they were robbed. Similarly, you wear a goblin cap and run rampant throughout the world... wearing a goblin cap made of gold.-

"Those bastards say that ignorant people like us need to be governed. That the gentleman must rule the petty man, and in return, the petty man must feed the gentleman? - Those bastards have the gall to say such shameless things. If you're going to steal, can't you at least be honest about it!"
Jeong Do-ryong muttered this to himself.
"No, historically, what significant event has ever originated from us? We are content as long as we can sustain our lives through our labor. The biggest incidents for us might be a drunken brawl, a couple's quarrel, or perhaps some wicked fellow trying to rape another man's wife. But we can judge such matters well ourselves. All the storms and commotions in this world are orchestrated by those bastards themselves, yet ah! They dare tell us that unruly people must be governed?"
"Law, or whatever it is, we ignorant people don't know about such things. But for farmers like us who earn our own living, what use is it all? We are managing our own affairs just fine right now. Why are those bastards so meddlesome, constantly interfering in our peaceful village, telling us to do this or that? And then they empty our pockets. Like dirty thieves-
"Where on earth do we have money? Shouldn't they ask the rich for such things? Those bastards impose all sorts of unreasonable corvee labor, levy various contributions (추념) - next, are they planning to tax us for sleeping with our spouses? - After<48> we go out for corvee labor and diligently build a new road, they drive by in their cars, kicking up dust. Is that their thanks for building the road?"
He snorted again. He had snorted dismissively like this quite often before. It was several years ago, on the evening Kŭmsŏk came home from common school for the last time. After the meal, Jeong Do-ryong was puffing away on his pipe when he casually asked his son,
"What did the teacher teach you today?"
At that time, Kŭmsŏk, after listing various subjects, said,
"Today, the teacher lectured us that people must maintain good hygiene - eat at regular times, sleep at regular times, and exercise regularly - and eat as much meat and eggs as possible, because that makes the body strong-"
Hearing this, he suddenly shouted, slamming his pipe against the ashtray.
"What did he say? What? And you just listened quietly? Why didn't you ask who doesn't know how to eat such things? Telling us, who can't even get enough gruel, what to do? Ask him what we're supposed to do about getting hungrier when we exercise! That's something only those with full bellies can do! Do teachers say such thankful things because we pay the exorbitant school fees even while starving? How many rich kids are there for him to talk like that? There's a limit to sucking up to the fat cats! Is that really the kind of thing a teacher says? You foolish sons of bean-brains! Hey! Kŭmsŏk! Starting tomorrow, you quit that damned school! It's better not to hear such nonsense, like sleep-talking in broad daylight. And besides, do they think you can live just by learning Japanese or how to drive oxen?"
He was furious to the tips of his hair. So Kŭmsŏk quit school the very next day.

Not long after that. Kŭmsuni went to the newly established church (례배당) because a seemingly worthwhile *kobo* (likely a preacher or missionary figure) had come. She said the pastor's words were incredibly good and asked to attend regularly, which he allowed. But one day, Kŭmsuni<49> also ended up like Kŭmsŏk. It happened one Sunday.
"So, what did the pastor say?"
Jeong Do-ryong asked his daughter what she heard at the church service that day. Kŭmsuni cleverly relayed what she had heard.
"Well! He said that our lives are all due to God's (한우님) grace - and that everyone, big or small, is a sinner who has committed sins, so we must just be thankful in all circumstances to receive salvation-"
"What? Be thankful in all circumstances?"
"Yes, just accept whatever happens thankfully! Accept whatever is given!"
At these words, Jeong Do-ryong snorted again.
"Hmph! What do we have to be thankful for in all circumstances? Telling us, who are hungry, naked, and laboring constantly, what to be thankful for? Right! Our poverty like this is a sin - the sin of being poor? So accept whatever is given? Any kind of abuse! Humiliation! Isn't that fellow even worse than the teacher who told us to eat lots of meat and eggs! Why didn't you just slap him across the face until sparks flew from his eyes! Let's see how thankful he looks then!"
"Sure, there might be some people in this world who should be thankful in all circumstances. Those rich folks with lots of money or powerful yangban for whom everything goes their way - guys like Kim Chusa across the river, who does all sorts of crazy things with his money - they might accept what's given and say thank you. But telling people like us to be thankful for what? Those bastards say such things because they get paid a salary (월급) to live, but what reason do the poor have to be thankful upon hearing that? We have no use for such a God. Such a God is a devil to us! You are not to go to that bastard's church ever again!"
And he forbade Kŭmsuni from going as well. He had been terribly angry that time too.
When such thoughts arose, he felt like taking a torch, going around, and setting fire to everything - the church, the school, the rich man's house, whatever. He cried out that all those things were sources of resentment (怨府) that only made life miserable for innocent and meek people like them.<50>
Whenever these thoughts came to him, his fists clenched unconsciously. And he glared fiercely, grinding his teeth.

5.

But then, an unexpectedly major incident occurred in the village.
This village was originally inhabited only by poor commoners, so they were all tenant farmers (소작농민), or some couldn't even manage that and barely survived by making and selling straw sandals or firewood. The largest farm was about ten *majigi* of paddy field, enough to feed one workhorse, which was considered an upper-middle farm and relatively well-off in this village. Most of the nearby fields were state land (경답), but some belonged to Kim Chusa from the village across the way. So there were quite a few people who farmed his land, but though they were called tenants, he worked them harder than his own servants. It might be understandable if he gave them a lot of land, but at best he gave them five *majigi* of paddy, or maybe two or three *majigi* of barren land, and then subjected them to corvee labor (부역) throughout the seasons, worse than any government office. In the summer, he would invariably call them for one round each of planting and weeding his own fields; during the July woodcutting season, he made them cut wood for a day's wage, then made them work another day tying and carrying down the wood; during harvesting, threshing - anyway, whenever there was work, he exploited them. And he made the tenant pay the taxes, and took shares of grain (배짐) and straw (마?), scraping away everything so thoroughly it was disgusting. Still, because their livelihood depended on it, those without land had no choice but to endure the contempt, saying "Yes, yes," and obeying. If they lost even that, they couldn't even borrow a single sack of high-interest grain. Ch'uni's family farmed five *majigi* of his land; the daughter-in-law and young Ch'uni barely managed to survive by farming it. But now, just as it was time to plant the rice seedlings, a rumor spread that the land was suddenly being taken away.
It turned out that a Japanese man lived in the village across from Kim Chusa's. This man, on the one hand, ran a candy business, cheating little children out of their snot-nosed money, and on the other hand, partnered (合資) with Kim Chusa in high-interest money lending. This Japanese man had apparently acquired the land, and that morning he had come and told them not to plant the seedlings. So Ch'uni's grandmother, alarmed, ran straight to Kim Chusa's<51> house to ask, and indeed, it was true.
Kim Chusa put on his official hat - he was currently a Provincial Councilor (도평의원), but he had many other titles besides this: former head of the Financial Cooperative (금융조합장), former member of the Common School's Education Committee (보통학교 학무위원), former County Advisor (군참사), regular member of the Red Cross Society (적십자사정사원), vice-chairman of the Landowners' Association (지주회 부회장) - (when he eventually died, writing his funeral banner would be difficult with so many titles) - and in a dignified voice, explained the reason for taking back the land like this:
"You've farmed it well for several years now, so it's time to stop - others should have a chance to farm it too-"
At that, the old woman, in a trembling voice, pleaded,
"Oh, Sir! What are we to do if you take the land away now?
My family will all starve to death!"
She begged and pleaded, but Kim Chusa just bellowed that he didn't care about such things - wasn't it his land to take back whenever he pleased?
Still, Ch'uni's grandmother pleaded tearfully for half a day, begging him to let them farm for just one more year, but he was utterly adamant. Realizing there was no changing his mind, Ch'uni's grandmother started to leave, but on the steps leading down from his house, she did a headstand and died right there on the spot. She was eighty-five years old and had barely managed to crawl there leaning on her cane.
However, Kim Chusa, not batting an eye, ordered his servant to drag the corpse outside the gate. He then sent word to Ch'uni's house to come collect the body and simultaneously called the village headman (구장) to report it to the police station (경찰서). Since Kim Chusa happened to be drinking with the Japanese man at the time, that man naturally served as a solid witness.
Ch'uni's mother and son, who had been waiting, hoping for some solution, were struck dumb by this completely unexpected news and rushed over, stumbling and falling. They now lay collapsed beside the body, weeping bitterly.

Meanwhile, Jeong Do-ryong, who was planting seedlings in his own field that day, heard the news and came running back at once. Many villagers had already gathered, cursing Kim Chusa's cruelty. Among them, the hot-blooded Wŏndŭgi rolled up his sleeves, declaring he would go right then and<52> smash the bastard, but the cowards just hesitated, watching cautiously from the sidelines. Especially those who farmed Kim Chusa's land said nothing, already trying to sneak away.
"Huh... well... I was plowing the field, I should probably go check..."
As Yongsoe mumbled, scratching his head and turning away, several others tried to follow him, saying, "Me too, me too," but Jeong Do-ryong suddenly roared like thunder.
"A major crisis has hit the village, yet you only look after your own affairs - you bastards are just like Kim Chusa!"
At this, a dog yelped in terror and ran off. The men stopped awkwardly in their tracks. At this moment, fire flashed in Jeong Do-ryong's eyes. He sent the children out to gather everyone working in the fields. Hearing his summons, they all came running back. Especially after hearing what someone like Yongsoe had said.
Jeong Do-ryong instructed each of them, assigning tasks, and then led the remaining men carrying the bier quickly towards Kim Chusa's village. The tragic corpse of the old woman lay under the zelkova tree at the village entrance, with Ch'uni's mother and son collapsed beside it, looking more unconscious than weeping. When they lifted the straw mat covering her, they saw the old woman's neck was broken and bent backward. On her gaunt, bony face, her sunken mouth held her tongue bitten out, dripping bright red blood. For some reason, only one eye was wide open, making her look even more terrifying. Her flesh was already decaying, the stench hitting them sharply, and blowflies buzzed loudly, swarming away only to return immediately.
Jeong Do-ryong personally lifted the head end of the corpse first to place it on the bier. Then the pallbearers took hold, and he guided Ch'uni's mother and son to follow behind.
Meanwhile, the person sent to town (읍내) to buy burial clothes (상포) and the one sent to get the burial permit (매장허가) returned. Even before the bier arrived, the police chief (경부) and a doctor had come from the police station, examined (금사) the body (relying mostly on the witness's account), determined it was indeed suicide, and left.
As soon as the burial clothes arrived, the village women gathered and quickly finished sewing the shroud, so it was ready not long after<53> the bier came. During this time, the pallbearers were given a bowl of rice wine each and allowed to smoke a pipe. Jeong Do-ryong hurried them along, and after he and Wŏndŭgi quickly prepared the body for burial (염), the funeral was held that very evening. Although many contributions came from within the village, money (돈량) and rice (쌀말) also came from the yangban village across the way, enough to prepare rice wine and food (점심) for the pallbearers and offerings (祭需) like dried pollack slices.
As the early summer sun dipped low towards the western mountains (너웃 너웃), an unprecedented funeral dirge rose high in the quiet mountain village. Beneath the fluttering funeral banner (구름재일), the mournful song "O-ho! O-ho" of the pallbearers drifted out, followed by a long procession of mourners (희장군), men and women, old and young. Except for the very old and newlywed brides, everyone from the village joined the procession. Between the lead singer's chant and the sound of the funeral bell (요령소리), the cries of Ch'uni's mother and son ceaselessly tore at their hearts. As the bier left the village entrance, the old folks remaining at home watched blankly from afar, murmuring futile sighs,
"Somehow, she died lucky..."
When Sŏnguk's grandmother's funeral procession had passed that spring, they had watched the same way. Seeing the old starve and their children and grandchildren struggle in poverty, even barley scraps and millet gruel wouldn't go down easily. The thought of dying soon to escape this sight made them endlessly envy the fate of those who died first. Young brides clung to the brushwood gate frames, watching with wonder (驚異) in their tear-streaked eyes. As if asking, what is this thing called death?...
Soon the sun set, and a faint crescent moon hung in the western sky. Time resolves all things. And so, in the Sanmallangi communal cemetery on the mountain pass, a new grave mound appeared where none had been before. The cool moonlight shone upon it, giving it a kiss as if blessing its repose. Thus, Ch'uni's grandmother found eternal rest... in a land without money, without taxes, without rich and poor, a land where one can live without eating... But the demons (아귀), begrudging her even a scrap of rag (襤褸) or a bowl of thin gruel, had chased her out of this world - how happy and pleased must they be?<54>

6.

After that, Jeong Do-ryong's furrowed brow never smoothed out. His somber countenance seemed to hold some terrifying resolve. Indeed, the very next day, he did something that astonished everyone in the village. He asked the steward (마름) of the land for permission regarding his own paddy field, which he had left half-planted the day before, received it, and declared he was giving it to Ch'uni's family. Then, urging everyone to finish planting the seedlings for them that day, the villagers all pitched in and finished it in half a day. When Ch'uni's mother heard this, she was startled and tried hard to refuse, but he told her not to worry and absolutely refused to listen, proceeding as planned. After that, he went straight to Kim Chusa's house. Kim Chusa happened to be alone in the master's room (사랑방).
"Ah! Do-ryong, what brings you here?"
Kim Chusa, who had been lying on the elevated floor (평상), sat up abruptly. He was already implicitly startled by this unexpected visit, but when his eyes met Jeong Do-ryong's fiercely shining gaze, he felt an inexplicable fear. His eyes were menacing, like those of an enraged tiger.
"Yes! I came to ask for some paddy land!"
Do-ryong always spoke bluntly like this, without hesitation, in front of anyone.
"Paddy land? Why, don't you have land? And is it right to ask for land now?"
Kim Chusa looked up with a faint, incredulous smile.
"Well, there are also those who take land away now!"
At this, his smile vanished completely. Left speechless, he looked flustered, glancing around aimlessly.
"We gave our paddy land to Ch'uni's family, so I don't have even one *majigi* of land!"
Hearing this, Kim Chusa was doubly shocked. He couldn't bring himself to ask why he foolishly gave away his own land and now came looking for more.<55> Indeed, in such a situation, even the most wicked person would likely find such words hard to utter-
"Where would there be any land! Except for what your family farms..."
He said this evasively, looking embarrassed, stealing a glance at the other man's expression, then let out a pointless yawn. He quickly lit a cigarette and smoked it.
"Then please give me that! What kind of twisted mind takes the rice cake from his own family's mouth to give to a thief?"
"A thief? Who?... You should farm that yourself!"
Kim Chusa tried to change the subject, but this retort was clearly lodged in his chest.
"So you can't give it?"
"It's difficult this year!"
Before the words were even fully out, Jeong Do-ryong stood up abruptly and walked out without looking back. This made Kim Chusa gasp once more. He stared blankly at his departing figure for a long moment. Jeong Do-ryong went straight home.

Meanwhile, his wife, though not daring to openly criticize her husband's actions, felt utterly drained since hearing the news. How could they survive with young children if he completely gave away their means of farming? Work felt difficult to grasp. However, Kŭmsŏk, with an air of utter indifference, wore the same faint smile as always and was talking about something with Kŭmsuni.
The mother found this irritating.
"Do you know where Father went?"
"Don't know! To Kim Chusa's house?"
Kŭmsuni asked, her eyes wide, looking towards her brother.
"He definitely went to ask for land. If he doesn't give it, Father will kill that bastard. Truly, it's a sin to let a snake that eats swallow chicks<56> live."
Kŭmsuni's eyes shone even wider.
"If Father doesn't kill him, I will. If I strike his neck with that sickle (pointing to the sickle leaning against the wall by the upper part of the room), his head will surely drop off with a thud. And he will definitely twitch and thrash. Don't believe me? Listen! Once, when I was cutting thick grass, whish-whish-. I swiped with the sickle at a viper that was raising its head. And whish- it went. Then the thing writhed! Writhed! Even then, I thought. If only I could cut off the heads of all the wicked bastards in this world like this... and... watch their bloody heads hopping around like frogs - and hasn't just such a bastard deserving death appeared?"
Before these words were finished, Jeong Do-ryong returned. Kŭmsŏk abruptly stopped talking and observed his father's expression. He looked indeed even more somber and distressed. He said nothing. He glanced meaningfully at his family members from the corner of his eye from time to time.
Still in silence, they finished dinner. After sitting for a while, he quietly got up and went outside. He still said nothing. But just then, Kŭmsŏk called out to his departing back.
"Instead of that little knife, take that sickle!"
Suddenly, Jeong Do-ryong whipped his head around. He stared blankly at his son for a long moment, then turned again and walked out. A moment later, the mother followed him out. A look of alarm also crossed Kŭmsuni's eyes. But Kŭmsŏk, seemingly unconcerned, still wore his faint smile.
"Hey! Where are you going? Stay and talk with me!"
He stopped Kŭmsuni, who was about to go outside.<57>
"Are you that afraid of dying? You kill bedbugs just fine, don't you? A bastard like Kim Chusa is a bedbug that sucks human blood. What's scary about killing a bedbug?"
Kŭmsuni, as if taking this in, fixed her startled eyes on Kŭmsŏk's face.
"Humans are inherently born with sin, you know. By what right do we work a cow to exhaustion and then slaughter and eat it? Just for that sin alone - if someone like God truly exists and commands, 'Offer your life now!' you should obey immediately, saying 'Yes-'. But above that, bastards who commit even greater sins must be killed without forgiveness. And those who know this but don't kill such bastards are also sinners. - Even among sins, there are unforgivable sins! Like the sin of someone like Kim Chusa-"
Kŭmsŏk rambled on like this.

Kŭmsuni's eyes, which until moments ago had shone with a startled expression, gradually began to gleam with a smile concealing some intense, deeply moved emotion.
"You haven't heard the story about people being executed in prison, have you? A while ago, I said I wanted to try killing someone, but similarly, the thought occurred to me that I'd like to try being killed by someone. This is a story I heard from someone, but I wanted to die like that too!"
"How?"
Kŭmsuni finally uttered a single word, rolling off her red, moist lips. Kŭmsŏk recounted this terrifying tale quite calmly.
"-Many people stand around in a circle, and they bring the person to be executed before the execution platform!"<58>
He gave a slight, meaningful smile.
"Then a monk comes out and chants prayers for passage to paradise, and just as the executioner is about to pull the rope, the mechanical blade (키로친 - guillotine) comes rattling down from above. Flash! goes the blade, and drip! drip! falls the severed head, eyes blinking, flying up into the air! - But these pathetic fools usually turn pale as corpses even before they die and tremble uncontrollably. If it were me, I'd be smiling calmly at that moment. So as my neck flies up, like the evening sun disappearing into the red sunset glow, as my smile gradually fades, at that moment I would shout like this: - Satisfying! - Satisfy... ing... ha... da! - Before I could even finish the 'da,' my smile and life would vanish at once, and after that, it would become pitch-black night.-"
Suddenly, Kŭmsuni burst into peals of bright laughter with her ringing voice. Just as this laughter subsided,
"Kŭmsŏk! Kŭmsŏk!"
Their mother rushed in, panting heavily.
"Hey! Kŭmsŏk! Kŭmsuni! I went towards the main village (큰말) to see where your father went, and someone was coming from over there, acting strangely! I looked closely, and it was Sundŭgi's father! The one who works at Kim Chusa's house.- He said, 'Master Kŭmsŏk's father! You're requested to come over for a moment from your house!' So I asked if he hadn't gone there already, and he said no, he hadn't met him! But I faintly overheard a voice like your father's coming from Ch'uni's house behind me. So I rushed over, and sure enough, he was there, and now they've gone together to Kim Chusa's house..."
She barely finished speaking and caught her breath. Kŭmsŏk listened blankly for a moment, then
"It's all ruined!"
He cried out as if despairing of something.
"Now, Mother, you don't need to worry, it turned out well. It seems that bastard's life is fated to be a bit longer!"<59>
The mother didn't understand the meaning of her son's words, but somehow felt there was something in them that allowed her to breathe easier. She sighed and looked again at her children's expressions.
"Kim Chusa must be planning to give him the land-. The bastard must have gotten scared. Well, he has reason to be scared, but - if he's giving the land, there's no need to kill him. Ch'uni's family is better off instead. Killing him wouldn't bring Ch'uni's grandmother back to life! But if he pulls this kind of stunt again in the future, he'll definitely end up dead at Father's hands! I'll surely kill a few bastards myself! And you seem capable of earning your keep too. Anyway, it turned out well!"
Kŭmsŏk looked sidelong at Kŭmsuni with his perpetually smiling eyes, but Kŭmsuni, her eyes smiling like the morning sunlight sparkling on snow on a clear winter day, said nothing and suddenly fumbled in her sewing box (괴침이), pulling out a gleaming, sharp dagger (短刀) and tossing it down in front of Kŭmsŏk.
"Ah! So you were planning to kill Kim Chusa too? Just as I thought! You can earn your keep!"
Kŭmsŏk exclaimed inadvertently, his eyes widening. At this, the mother stared blankly at Kŭmsuni for a moment, then
"My! What fearsome seeds!"
She muttered as if talking to herself, like someone who had lost their senses. An image flashed before her eyes: Kŭmsuni, gripping that knife, rushing fiercely towards Kim Chusa's neck... A shiver ran down her spine...
The mother rushed forward and hugged Kŭmsuni tightly. Then, incomprehensible tearswelling up like a spring, she choked out,
"Kŭmsuni! Kŭmsuni!"
...But Kŭmsŏk was still smiling faintly when Jeong Do-ryong returned. He opened his eyes wide and looked back and forth between his family members. His eyes fell again on the dagger, then looked at Kŭmsuni...
-End-<60>

**Summary**

This chapter of "Farmer Jeong Do-ryong" depicts the grinding poverty and systemic injustice faced by Korean tenant farmers in the 1920s. The story centers on Jeong Do-ryong's family and their neighbors, particularly the tragic death of Ch'uni's elderly grandmother, driven to suicide after the landlord, Kim Chusa (a wealthy, influential figure collaborating with Japanese interests), abruptly repossesses her family's small plot of land. The incident galvanizes Jeong Do-ryong, who gives his own land to the bereaved family and confronts Kim Chusa. While direct violence is averted when Kim Chusa seemingly relents out of fear, the narrative reveals the deep-seated resentment and radicalization simmering within the community, especially in Jeong Do-ryong's children, Kŭmsŏk and Kŭmsuni, who contemplate and prepare for violent retribution against their oppressors.

For historians, this text offers a raw, visceral portrayal of rural life under colonial rule, highlighting the exploitative relationship between Korean landlords (often part of the collaborating elite, holding positions in colonial structures) and impoverished tenant farmers. It vividly illustrates the precariousness of life, the critique of institutions like schools and churches seen as irrelevant or tools of the powerful, and the emergence of radical thought, including the justification of violence, among the oppressed peasantry as a response to profound social injustice and desperation. The story provides insight into class consciousness, anti-colonial sentiment (implied through the Japanese character and Kim Chusa's collaboration), and the psychological impact of systemic oppression.

그런데 이 동리에는 뜻밧게 큰일이 생기엿다.
그것은 이 동리는 원래 가난한 상사람만 사는 터임으로 그들은 모다 소작농민이거나 그나마도 전장참례를 못하고 집신장사 나무장사로 근근히 사는 집도 잇다. 만니 짓는 대야 논섬 지기로서 도지 소나 한바리 먹이는 집니 그중 상농가이요 또한 넝넉하다는 집이엿다. 이 압전장은 거지반 경답이지마는 건너말 김주사집 땅도 더러 잇섯다. 그래 그 집 논을 부치는 사람도 더러 잇는데 말이 작인이지 이건 제 집 하인보다도 심하게 부려 먹는다. 그것도 논이나 만히 주고 그란다면 모르지마는 잘해야 논 댓마지기나 그러치 안으면 두 세마지기의 박토를 주고서는 사시로 부역을 식히는 일이 여간 관청보다 심하다. 여름이면 의례히 자긔집 모심고 논매는데 한 차례식 불너다 식히고 칠월나무 빌 때에는 하루삭 나무를 비게하고 그 나무를 묵거 내린 때 또 하루 식히고 베 빌때 마당질할 때-엇더턴지 일이 잇슬 때마다 부려먹는다. 그리고 구실은 작인더러 치르라하고 배짐이니 마*이니 도모지 다랍게도 알뜰이 할퀘 가는데 그래도 목숨이 보두청이라고 땅이 업는 그들은 엇잘 수 업시 그 천대를 바더가며 녜! 녜!하고 복종을 한다. 그나마 떠러지는 날에는 장리 벼 한섬도 융통을 못하는 까닭이다. 춘? 너도 그 집 논 닷 마지기를 부치는데 고부가 어린 춘이를 다리고 그것을 지여서 간신히 호구를 하는 터이다. 그런데 지금 모을 심을 임시에 별안간 그 논을 뗀다는 소문이 낫다.

뎡도룡은 그들을 일일히 지취하야 일치를 순서를 분배한 후 남어지 사람들은 상여를 메고 일선 김주사 사는 동리로 급히갓다. 참혹한 로파의 송장은 동구박 느트나무 밋헤 노혓는데 그 엽헤는 춘이 모자가 업드려서 우는지 깜을 첫는지 모르게 느려젓다. 섬거적을 떠들고 보니 로파는 목이 부러저서 뒤로 제처젓다. 앙상한 뼈만 남은 얼골에 옴으러진 입으로 혀을 빼무럿는데 거긔에는 새빨간 피가 흘넛다. 웬일인지 눈은 한눈만 흡뜬 것이 더욱 무섭게 뵈이엿다. 벌서 살은 썩어서 시취가 탁탁 터지며 쉬파리가 왱-하고 떼로 날느다가 다시 대든다.
뎡도룡은 자긔 손으로 먼저 시체의 머리 편을 들어서 상여 위에 언께 하엿다. 이에 상두군은 대드러서 상여를 메고 그는 다시 춘이 모자를 안동하야 그 뒤를 따러 갓다.
그 동안에 읍내로 상포밧구러 간 사람과 매장허가(埋葬許可)를 맛흐러 간 사람도 도라왓다. 경찰서에서는 발서 상여가 오기 전에 경부와 의사가 나와서 시체를 금사해 보고 (무엇보다 증인의 말을 듯고)사실 자살인저 하고 내려갓다.
상포가 드러 오는 대로 동리 녀자들은 일제히 모히여서 수의를 급히 마른 까닭에 상여가 온 뒤에 얼마 안 잇다가＜53＞ 다 되엿다 하엿다. 그 동안에는 상두군은 술을 한사발식 먹고 담배를 한 대식 피우게 하엿다. 그래 뎡도룡을 급히 서드러서 원득이와 가티 염을 한 후에 그날 저녁 때에 바로 장사를 지내게 되엿다. 동리 안에서 부조가 드러온 것은 만치마는 건너말 양반촌에서도 돈량 쌀말이 드러와서 상두군의 술밥(점심)과 조각포를 차려 노을 제수(祭需)까지도 마련할 수 잇섯다.
초여름 해가 너웃 너웃 서천에 기울 무렵에 적막하든 산촌에는 난듸 업는 상여 소리가 놉피낫다. 구름재일(약장)이 펄넝펄넝하는 상여 밋흐로는 「오-호! 오-호」하는 상두군의 처량한 노래가 떠나오는데 그 뒤로는 남녀노소의 희장군이 죽-느러섯다. 동리 사람으로는 극로인과 새각시를 빼노코는 모다 회장군으로 행렬을 지엿다. 선소리와 요령소리 사이로 춘이 모자의 곡성이 쉴새업시 그들의 창자를 끈코 나왓다. 상여가 동구 밧그로 나갈 때 집에 남어 잇는 로인들은 시름업시 멀니 바라다보며
「엇더턴지 팔자 조케 잘 죽엇다..」
하고 그들의 속절업는 탄식을 발하엿다. 올 봄에 성욱이 조모의 상여가 나갈 때에도 그들은 그러케 바라다 보앗다.-늙어 굼주리고 아들 손자가 가난에 허덕어리는 꼴을 보면 그들은 보리 꽁댕이와 조죽이나마 그게 잘 너머 가지를 안엇다. 어서 죽어서 이 꼴을 보지 말고 십흔 생각은 이러케 먼저 죽는 이의 팔자를 못내 부러워하도록 하엿다. 어린 각시들은 싸리문구틀에 부터서서 그의 가슴치레한 눈에 경이(驚異)를 띄우고 내다본다. 마치 죽는게란 무엇인고? 하는 드시...
어느듯 해가 너머가고 어슴푸레한 초생달이 서쪽 한울에 걸녀잇다. 시간은 모든 일을 해결하는 것이다. 그래 산말낭이 공동묘지에는 전에 업던 새 무덤 하나이 생기엿다. 그 위에 서 늘한 달빗이 그의 안식을 축복하는 드시 키-쓰를 주엇다. 그리하야 춘이 할머이는 돈업는 나라 세금업는 나라 부자와 가난이 업는 나라 밥 안 먹어도 사는 나라로-영원히 영원히 안식을 으더갓다...그러나 아귀는 그를 한쪼각 남누(襤褸)이나 한 그릇 조죽을 주기가 앗가워서 그를 이 세상에서 쪼처낸 까닭으로 얼마나 깁부고 조아 할는가?＜54＞
