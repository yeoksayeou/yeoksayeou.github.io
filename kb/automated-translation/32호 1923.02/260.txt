Sanbo (Stroll)
						Yohan
Ah, you know there must be a time when people are better than they are now. If you ask me, I would answer thus: We must use our senses. Into the wind, over the earth, into the air, and even further than that. (P'ul, P'oro)

P'ulbat (Grass Field)
Lying in a grass field on a spring day,
Closing my eyes and listening quietly,
From somewhere, subtle music,
Not just one, but many,
The sound of tens of thousands of breaths,
Ringing even when I cover my ears,
The sound of celestial maidens singing in chorus,
Rises from all directions,<33>
And vibrates my nerves.

It is the mysterious melody
Of countless lives counting time in the black soil.
Now, the "power" within that quietness—
I hear it, I smell it, I touch it.
The sun and the air, with that "power,"
Bathe my soul. (Sanbo 2)

Kaŭl metkyŏt (Autumn Mountain Edge)
Rain came, the river water is reddish,
And the collapsed mountain path, in the sunset,
When its bare leg rests,
My weary dream also rests.

Bells in the millet field are clamorous,
Disordered flocks of birds cross the field,
When the distant sound of cowbells is heard on the wind,
My heart quietly closes its eyes.<34>

The scent of pine resin in the pine grove is deep,
And in the thicket, overgrown and ripening,
When the earth and "autumn" are fragrant,
My senses open their mouths like fish.

But only when evening comes stealthily
And wraps all the clamor in its cloak,
Do my five senses pierce the secret
And discern a more wondrous sound. (Sanbo 3)<35>
<33-35>

**Summary**

These three short poems by "Yohan" explore heightened sensory experiences within nature. The first piece acts as an introduction, urging the use of senses. "P'ulbat" describes lying in a spring field, perceiving the subtle sounds and "power" of life in the soil, sun, and air. "Kaŭl metkyŏt" shifts to autumn, depicting the sounds and smells of a mountain edge at sunset—millet field bells, birds, cowbells, pine resin, ripening plants—culminating in a deeper perception of hidden sounds as evening falls. From a colonial history perspective, the intense focus on sensory engagement with the natural world, almost to the exclusion of human society, might be seen as a form of quiet introspection or escape, seeking meaning and connection in nature rather than in the potentially fraught social or political landscape of 1920s Korea. Historians might find the emphasis on individual sensory experience and the almost mystical connection to nature noteworthy in the context of intellectual currents during the period.

산보
						요한
아아, 너는 사람이 시방보다 더 잘날 때가 잇슬 줄 알지. 만일 내게 무르면 나는 이러케 대답하랸다. 우리의 감각을 내써야 한다. 바람 속으로, 땅 우으로, 공중으로, 또 그보다도 더 멀리. (풀, 포르)

풀밧
봄날 풀밧헤 누어서
눈감고 조용히 들으면
어대선가 미묘한 음악이,
하나도 아니오 여럿이,
수천 수만의 숨소리가,
귀를 막어도 울려오는,
선녀의 합창하는 소리가,
사면으로 닐어나서,＜33＞
내 신경을 진동합니다.

그것은 무수한 생명이,
검은 흙속에서 때를 헤는
신비의 곡조입니다.
시방 그 조용한 속에 잇는 「힘」을
나는 들읍니다, 마틉니다, 만집니다.
태양과 공긔가 그 「힘」으로
내령혼을 멱감깁니다. (산보2)

가을 멧겻
비와서 강물은 벍어코
문허진 산길이 석양에
그 버슨 다리를 쉬일제
내 곤한 꿈도 쉬입니다.

조밧헤 방울이 요란하고
어지러운 새떼는 들을 건너며
바람결에 소울음 멀리 들릴제
내 마음은 가만히 눈감읍니다.＜34＞

솔밧헤 송진 냄새 그윽하고
욱어저 닉어가는 풀숩에서
흙과 「가을」이 향긔로울 때
내 감각은 물고기가티 입벌입니다.

그러나 저녁이 몰래와서
모든 요란을 그 옷자락에 쌀때라야
나의 오관은 비밀을 뚤러보고
더 오묘한 소리를 알아냅니다.(산보3)＜35＞
＜33-35＞
