Wang Zhaojun
Translated by Yang Paekhwa

Characters
Emperor Xiaoyuan (孝元帝) (7th Emperor of the Former Han)
Wang Zhaojun (王昭君) (Palace Lady)
Mao Yanshou (毛延壽) (Palace Painter)
Liu Bai (劉白) (Same as above)
Envoy of Huhanxie Chanyu (呼韓邪單于) (Xiongnu)
Civil and Military Officials, Attendants, Eunuchs, Palace Ladies, Young Servants, etc.
Era
Former Han, First Year of Jingning, Autumn (前漢 竟寧元年秋)

Act 2, Scene 2
A part of the palace interior. A door downstage. An ornate paneled wall upstage. The front faces a distant great hall, with a corridor in front. It connects to various halls. The floor has patterned mats. Center stage front, Emperor Xiaoyuan sits on the jade throne. On tables to the left and right of the throne, fruits and sweets are arranged attractively.

Emperor: (Receiving paintings one by one from an attendant and looking at them) They are all more or less the same. There isn't a single one that truly stands out. Since we haven't changed them for 20 years, I thought there might be some beauties among the towns and villages, but it's completely different from what I expected. Our China seems vast, yet it is small. <67>
Attendant 1: We are truly apprehensive that there is no beauty who meets Your Majesty's eye.
Emperor: You sent people everywhere throughout the land of China, didn't you?
Attendant 2: Needless to say, the major cities were searched, and we had them search thoroughly even in remote mountain regions without open transportation and sparsely populated ports and harbors.
Emperor: (Looks through the paintings in order) Hmm. Even so, the results are too poor. Hearing that beauties were being selected, some might have fled and hidden. Among those, there might have been unexpectedly outstanding ones.
Attendant 1: With all due respect, please do not worry about that point. The envoys sent to the provinces this time consulted with the local magistrates (守令), summoned every single maiden aged 17 to 20 without distinction of beauty or ugliness, and first had them pass before the envoys' eyes. It is said that some among them fled and hid themselves, but regarding those, the various magistrates divided the work, searched for them with utmost effort, selected the prominent ones, and made examples by severely punishing those who were not. Therefore, I believe not a single fish escaped the net.
Emperor: That might be so. But then, my expectations were certainly wrong. Perhaps our China has had a poor harvest of beauties in recent years. (Finishes looking at the paintings.)
Attendant 2: Mao Yanshou, how many more sheets are left?
Yanshou: There should still be about ten sheets left. Perhaps among them, there might be a nation-toppling beauty (傾國之姿色) who meets Your Majesty's esteemed intentions.
Emperor: No. There's no need to look further. Even if there were, they would be largely the same as the ten or so I've seen so far. Expecting anything more than that will only lead to disappointment, so I no longer believe it.
(Two eunuchs enter from downstage, holding ten paintings. They kneel, bow, and place them on the table.)
Yanshou: (To the two eunuchs) Is this all now?
(The eunuchs kowtow and exit.) <68>
Emperor: (Frowning, looking somewhat tired, and as if annoyed, he looks through them more quickly than before. He glances at the very last one and is about to toss it aside, but stops, looks again, and stares at it for a while. As he looks, his eyes light up, his mood brightens, and gradually, a look of joy appears on his face.)
Emperor: (Looking at the painting) What is this girl's name?
Attendant 1: (Checking the name register) Her name is Zhaojun, Wang Qiang (昭君王嬙), Your Majesty.
Emperor: How old is she? Where was she born?
Attendant 1: She was born in Jingmen Zhou (荊門州) and is seventeen years old.
Emperor: (Thinks for a moment) The mountains and waters of Shu (蜀) are clear and bright. (Smiles.)
Attendant 2: Does she meet Your Majesty's approval?
Emperor: One cannot say she was created by mere mortals. She is a woman born of nature's vital spirit. The two moles on her forehead and near her eye are flaws, if one must call them that, but before this refined appearance and sublime character, they are flaws unworthy of discussion. I have finally obtained a treasure of this world. Obtaining an exquisite item beyond expectation is something I find greatly satisfying. Shouldn't it be like this? Hahaha. (Looks very pleased.)
Attendant 1: If she meets Your Majesty's approval, we pray you grant her an audience at once.
Emperor: (Staring intently at the painting) Moreover, compared to the others, the woman depicted in this painting looks almost alive. Her spirit has transferred onto this silk. It must surely be Yanshou's skill, but when painting such beauty, the artist himself must have been entranced, forgetting everything else. The pinnacle of human beauty and the brush that praises that beauty adhere closely to the human heart. I believe this painting was created without the slightest gap. A truly beautiful woman. An unparalleled beauty. Even the Fairy of the Luo River (洛浦之仙子) of old could not compare to her.
Attendant 2: In that case, how about summoning her here immediately?
Emperor: Yes, let's see her. Prepare her at once.
(An attendant starts to leave.)
Yanshou: (Solemnly) Your Majesty, I beseech you to temporarily halt the summons for Wang Zhaojun. <69>
(The two attendants look at each other in surprise.)
Emperor: (Startled) Why? Why do you say not to summon her?
Yanshou: That woman bears an unusually ominous sign (惡相).
Emperor: An unusually ominous sign?
Yanshou: The two moles on her forehead and near her eye, Your Majesty.
Emperor: Moles? Why are moles an ominous sign? What kind of ominous sign?
Yanshou: Yes. In terms of appearance, they are not much of a flaw, but according to physiognomy books (相書), those two moles are an unlucky sign said to kill living children. Great misfortune will befall the man who loves this woman, and in severe cases, it is said to lead to the ending of his life—a very ominous sign.
Emperor: Hmm, you say in severe cases it leads to ending one's life?
Yanshou: It is not the slightest bit incorrect. As proof, your humble servant knows many examples.
Emperor: This is the first I've heard of it. It wouldn't be a lie, would it?
Yanshou: Thinking only of upholding Your Majesty's sacred person (玉體), I have spoken frankly. How could I dare to lie? I am utterly terrified.
Emperor: Hmm. So those two moles bring such ominous misfortune?
Yanshou: To put it bluntly, I believe she belongs to the class of femme fatales (妖婦) or poisonous women (毒婦).
Emperor: (Disappointed and crestfallen) Just when I thought I had found a peerless beauty, she possesses an unavoidable, ominous sign. (Pause) When I sought to find beauties, Chamberlain Hou Ying (中郞侯應) vehemently advised against it, saying such actions were the root cause of disordering the nation and tormenting the people. Perhaps this poor outcome is due to Hou Ying's resentment. But why do those moles manifest such misfortune? For what reason do they end a man's life? (Yanshou subtly signals an attendant with his eyes.) <70>
Attendant 1: With all due respect, it is said that Heaven determines the good and evil of a person's heart, the fate of their entire life, the number of wives, children, and relatives, the suitability of their profession, and all other human affairs by drawing lines on their face and hands. Mao Yanshou is said to be very knowledgeable in physiognomy, so I believe it is right to assume his words are not far from the truth.
Attendant 2: With utmost trepidation, for the sake of Your Majesty's sacred person, and furthermore for the sake of the country and the people, we beseech you to abandon the matter of summoning that Wang Zhaojun.
Emperor: Can moles, barely the size of pinpricks, truly become the misfortune of this great China?
Yanshou: An ant hole can collapse a dike (堤防), plunging hundreds of thousands of lives into the water. Misfortune often lurks where one cannot know.
Emperor: (Sighs deeply.) It cannot be helped. This interior is still dark. (All are silent. As the Emperor is about to slump and rise, an attendant comes up from downstage.)
Attendant: (Kneels respectfully.) Your Majesty, an envoy from the Xiongnu (匃奴) Huhanxie Chanyu (呼韓邪單于) has just arrived.
Emperor: (Startled for a moment.) An envoy from the Chanyu?
Attendant: He brings 100 catties of gold and 10 pairs of white jade discs as tribute (獻上品) and requests an audience.
Emperor: He has come from afar. And no small amount of tribute (貢物). I should summon him and hear what he has to say. Dispense with the usual ceremonies and quickly bring him here.
(Attendants 1 and 2 clear away the paintings. The Chanyu's envoys follow an attendant, come up from downstage, and kowtow to the Emperor.)
Emperor: You are the Chanyu's envoys? You have endured hardship coming from afar. And the immense tribute is commendable.
Envoy 1: Seeing your magnificent celestial countenance fills us with joy. And we are overwhelmed with gratitude that you graciously accept our humble offerings.
Emperor: Is the Chanyu well?
Envoy 2: He has long wished to pay his respects to Your Majesty, but has been occupied with military preparations on the border, causing much offense. <71>
Emperor: The Chanyu understands propriety and also strives for the welfare of his people; he is China's unique border guardian general. Thanks to him, I too spend peaceful days. Tell him well when you return that he should stay healthy and not neglect military preparations.
Envoy 1: We are moved beyond words.
Emperor: China will also do anything for the Chanyu.
Envoy 2: We are deeply humbled. It is not in response to those words that we speak, but in truth, the Chanyu has one request of Your Majesty, and we have come as special envoys for this purpose.
Emperor: The Chanyu has a request of me? What is it?
Envoy 1: It seems presumptuous, and we are extremely apprehensive, but the Chanyu stated that China and we share common interests and have a relationship where we cannot be separated. China's prosperity is indeed our prosperity, and our strength or weakness might perhaps have some effect on a part of China. Therefore, to eternally solidify the peace between our two sides, with utmost humility, forming a marriage alliance (姻戚關係) with China has been the Chanyu's long-held wish.
Envoy 2: Regarding that, what we desire is nothing else. Truly, we are incomparably apprehensive, but if Your Majesty would grant one of the Imperial Princesses (皇女殿下) as a consort (妃) to the Chanyu, needless to say the Chanyu, but even down to the common man and rustic (匹夫野人), would weep with gratitude for Your Majesty's boundless grace, like Heaven's, and would not spare themselves, shattering bone and grinding flesh, to display imperial prestige (皇城). We are extremely apprehensive to speak thus, but we beseech Your Majesty to perceive the Chanyu's intentions (下燭) and show your vast benevolence (弘恩).
Envoy 1: There have been precedents of China's imperial household sending consorts before. Furthermore, if an Imperial Princess is not possible, we hope you might send one person from among the many palace ladies as a substitute for an Imperial Princess. However, this is not the Chanyu's word, but a matter conveyed secretly by us two envoys, begging Your Majesty not to trouble your sacred thoughts, so please understand this point, and we only wish that you dispose of this matter in a way that allows us envoys to show our faces.
Emperor: (Thinks for a while. Quietly) I have understood the Chanyu's request and your intentions. However, I cannot give an immediate answer to this matter. I will reply after quickly discussing it with my officials. You must be exhausted from your long journey, so rest comfortably today. <72>
Envoy 1: Yes, Your Majesty. We are deeply humbled. We only hope that the Chanyu's wish will be fulfilled, and that our mission as envoys will also conclude successfully. We prostrate ourselves and ask for your careful consideration.
Emperor: Rest assured. I will arrange things well. Rest comfortably in a separate chamber.
(The envoys kowtow to the Emperor and exit, following an attendant.)
Emperor: (As if to himself) I cannot send an Imperial Princess. (Turns to the attendants) Send one of the thousand-odd palace ladies who entered the palace this time as a substitute. It's pitiful to designate one, so first investigate if there are any volunteers (自願者). There might be one unusual woman among them. (Stands up) My feelings and the Chanyu's thoughts are the same. Without something beautiful to console one during the leisure time from busy state affairs, one truly cannot perform duties well. To blindly say that seeking beauty is wrong is the nonsense of a stubborn, dying old man. But that I, disappointed in selecting a beauty, must now select one for another... it's truly a laughable matter. The Chanyu, talking about marriage alliances and whatnot, is no simpleton either.

Act 2 [Should be Act 3 based on structure, but original says Act 2]
A part of the main hall's front is visible diagonally upstage right. Left and right are covered with old trees. In front of the stairs, there is a stone-paved path, one bay wide, covered with mats. In the distance, large and small palace buildings are visible. There is still time before dawn. Stars twinkle in the blue sky, and the entire area is soaked in the damp air of the dark dawn. It is quiet for a while.
(After some time, the lingering sound of a bell (not a half-bell, but a small bell with a metallic tone signaling the hour) seems to drift from inside. Two eunuchs enter, place the jade throne, light braziers at two locations left and right, go out in front of the stairs, light incense, and leave. White incense smoke rises. Following eunuchs from left and right, 30-40 civil and military officials, dressed like embroidered stars (綺*), enter and line up neatly left and right of the stairs. After a short while, the call "His Majesty arrives!" is heard twice from inside. On the second call, the officials all kneel. As the third "His Majesty arrives!" sounds closer, the officials perform the kowtow ritual. Led by eunuchs, the Emperor appears from the hall, followed by 14-15 attendants, eunuchs, palace ladies, and the two envoys of the Chanyu, and proceeds to the jade throne at the front, above the stairs. As he proceeds, his retinue kowtows, then lines up to the Emperor's left, right, and behind. The civil and military officials quietly raise their heads. Two attendants appear from downstage, clear the path, advance, and kowtow before the stairs. Following them, Zhaojun in white, flanked by four ** palace ladies also in white, holding crimson gauze lanterns high, supporting Zhaojun, walk slowly in. About one bay behind them, two palace ladies and two eunuchs follow. All kowtow. The civil and military officials bow again. Zhaojun and the four ** palace ladies advance further and kowtow again.) <73>
Attendant: (Leading) Your servant, Wang Zhaojun.
Emperor: (Extremely gently) Ah. You are Wang Zhaojun? It is highly commendable that you have volunteered this time to go to the Chanyu. Your loyalty (精忠), sacrificing yourself for China, puts even the civil and military officials lined up here to shame. (Pause) Lift your face.
(Zhaojun slightly raises her head. The officials also raise their heads.)
Emperor: The lands beyond the frontier (塞外) are thousands of miles away. The path is layered with perilous mountains and deep valleys, an even more difficult place for a woman's body. Moreover, it is said to be a very cold place with much snow and harsh winds. Once you go, you likely cannot return. Take good care of yourself and serve the Chanyu well. All clothing for the journey and also a famed horse with an embroidered saddle shall be specially bestowed (下賜) by me. (A long pause.) Stop bowing and rise.
(Zhaojun, supported by the palace ladies, slowly rises and stands. Incense smoke fills the area, further enhancing Zhaojun's beauty. The Emperor stares straight at her for a long time, then suddenly his expression changes, startled (imperceptibly to others). Yanshou, standing mixed in the last row of officials, glancing sideways at the scene, is also startled (also unnoticed by others), his face turning ashen. The Emperor silently continues to gaze at Zhaojun's face for a while, scanning her up and down, appearing mesmerized by her beauty. (Somehow, people feel a sense of dread.) Suddenly, as if struck by a thought, he looks around towards the officials, seeming to examine something. His gaze meets Mao Yanshou's sharply. The Emperor glares, and Yanshou lowers his head as if hiding his face. (This too, no one notices.) A eunuch adds incense. White smoke suddenly billows up. The Emperor tries hard to suppress his rising anger but cannot, remaining silently agitated (남모르게 은근히 悶悶하디). After a long while, he finally manages to speak, but what he says is barely audible.)
Emperor: (As if trying not to show his inner state.) How old are you? How old? (Speaks quickly.)
Attendant: (Whispers something to the Emperor.)
Emperor: (Nods and whispers something back to the Attendant. The Attendant replies.)
(The Attendant says something to the Chanyu's envoys. The two envoys kowtow on the spot. The Emperor glances towards them, then looks at Zhaojun and nods repeatedly. He mutters something under his breath again, seemingly unable to remain seated, and suddenly stands up. The retinue is somewhat startled. An attendant hurriedly shouts, "Enter the chambers! Enter the chambers!" (入御, 入御). While everyone prostrates themselves, the Emperor leads the way inside. (All look flustered.) A little later, Zhaojun's party, led by eunuchs, also enters. Immediately after, the civil and military officials exit to the right, all showing worried expressions. Yanshou and Liu Bai remain behind.)
Liu Bai: (Fearfully, slightly flustered, in a low but forceful voice) Teacher, what is it? <74>
Yanshou: (Completely deflated) It's all over now.
Liu Bai: What's over?
Yanshou: Didn't you realize?
Liu Bai: Well, it did seem a bit strange.
Yanshou: What should have been on Zhaojun's face wasn't there.
Liu Bai: What?
Yanshou: The moles. The moles weren't there.
Liu Bai: But didn't you arrange with the eunuch to draw moles on her face?
Yanshou: I did, but those crucial moles weren't there.
Liu Bai: What could have happened?
Yanshou: What's the point of digging into that now?
Liu Bai: Perhaps you were cheated because you didn't give the eunuch enough money, or else Zhaojun resisted and prevented the moles from being drawn. It must be one or the other.
Yanshou: No, the eunuch was thoroughly bribed. Zhaojun must have resisted.
Liu Bai: She certainly got her revenge (안갑흠) for that. She's quite a determined woman.
Yanshou: When I caught a glimpse of Zhaojun entering, I felt like cold water had been poured over my entire body. Even so, I was worried in my heart about the slight chance His Majesty might just glance over it... When His Majesty looked startled and his expression changed, I felt a sharp pang, as if my heart was stabbed with a sharp knife. Liu Bai, my life ends today, at this moment. (Shivers.)
Liu Bai: But teacher, you have His Majesty's deep trust. Didn't you say it wouldn't matter what you did? <75>
Yanshou: It's no use. The circumstances are different this time, aren't they? Isn't there some way to survive?
Liu Bai: Teacher, a person's heart cannot be controlled just by money. (Not sarcastic, genuinely.)
Yanshou: Never mind such talk now. Liu Bai, please, quickly think of a way to survive. Thinking of death makes my heart pound like this (clutches his chest with both hands). I want to live longer and enjoy luxury. Oh, Liu Bai, Liu Bai, I feel like I'm suffocating, I feel dizzy. (Sways as if about to collapse.)
Liu Bai: (Supporting him.) Teacher, pull yourself together! Nothing has been decided yet, why are you losing control like this? Teacher, you were boasting so boldly the other day, why are you like this now? Get a grip!
Yanshou: It's easy for you to speak so calmly, but I... put yourself in my shoes. Are you acting like this as my so-called disciple? After I looked after you for decades.
Liu Bai: Isn't that why I'm saying this? Losing a painter's conscience for such petty money...
Yanshou: Oh, so now you blame me, talking about a painter's conscience and whatnot. I hate you. Now I hate you.
(Two attendants rush in from the right and grab Yanshou by both arms.)
Attendant 1: His Majesty summons you.
Attendant 2: He grew tired of waiting long ago.
Yanshou: (As if breathless) Save me! Let me go! Let me go! Just for a moment! In this situation, life is more important than money. I'll give you all my gold, silver, and treasures, just let me go!
Attendant 1: Who asked for gold and silver? Stop that nonsense and come quickly.
Attendant 2: If you want to talk like that, do it before His Majesty. You disloyal wretch!
Yanshou: (Being dragged away by the attendants) Liu Bai, you're guilty too! You too... come... I won't die alone! (Screams as he is dragged away.)
Liu Bai: (Watching him go.) Perhaps that's his deserved fate. (Muttering to himself, lost in thought.)
(Gradually, the stars fade, and dawn breaks. The bell signaling the time sounds again.) <76>

Act 3 [Should be Act 4 based on logic, but original says Act 3]
A portion of the inner palace with closed doors occupies two-thirds of the stage right. Downstage and other empty spaces have scattered trees. It is around 10 PM. The sky is dark, and the surroundings are dim. Inside the palace, lamplight glows brightly, and the sound of a Pipa (琵琶) drifts out. For a while, only the Pipa music.
(The Emperor, smiling faintly, appears alone from downstage. Following the Pipa sound, he approaches, listens for a while. Then, smiling, he ascends the stairs and gently opens the door. Lamplight falls onto the courtyard. Zhaojun, who was playing the Pipa, hurriedly tries to kowtow.)
Emperor: Stay as you are. (Helps Zhaojun up.) It's a pity I interrupted you. (Sits down.) The farewell banquets continued day and night, and since tonight is the very last one, I too drank more than usual earlier. Perhaps because of that, I couldn't sleep even when I tried. To clear my head from the wine in the cool night air, I came down to the courtyard and wandered about, when suddenly I heard Pipa music I hadn't heard before. Thinking, 'Ah, there is someone with refined taste (韻致),' I followed the sound, and it was your chamber. So I listened for a moment, and the sound carried such a plaintive, sorrowful tune (悲調) that it made one unable to bear it. Your feelings, about leaving China tomorrow for the distant frontier, are pulsatingly expressed in the melody. Surely you were lost in listening too. I inadvertently did something regrettable.
Zhaojun: My lowly music reached the celestial ears; I have committed a crime punishable by death. Your servant also drank a little too much of the wine she cannot handle at the banquet earlier, and feeling relaxed, I took out the Pipa I learned as a child for the last time in this world and was playing alone. I am apprehensive for causing a disturbance at night.
(The moon rises.)
Emperor: Such sounds do not come unless played with a skilled hand and focused heart. Even an indifferent person's heart is moved by a sorrowful tune (哀調). Play one more piece (曲調).
Zhaojun: It is most improper for me to refuse like this, but please forgive me for that. <77>
Emperor: You mean you cannot play more?
Zhaojun: Yes. With utmost humility, my state of effortless concentration has been broken, and extraneous thoughts have intervened. Even if I tried to play, I fear it would not turn out the same.
Emperor: Hmm, you are right about that. All art forms fail to reach their mystical peak if the unity of the heart is lost due to extraneous thoughts. I made an unreasonable request.
Zhaojun: I am deeply humbled.
Emperor: (Looking around.) Oh, the moon must have risen. You too will likely not see the capital's moon again. As a last farewell, wouldn't you like to go down to the courtyard and stroll about? It's sad.
(Stands up and helps Zhaojun down the stairs.)
Emperor: The moon is lovely. The spring moon is warm, but the autumn moon is said to be full of sorrow. From now on, whenever I see a moon like tonight's, I will think of you in the far, distant frontier, and alone, I will recall the sound of the Pipa. No, I will never be able to forget about you. (Strolls slowly in the courtyard.) The world is full of those who boast of their talents and beauty, dreaming of wealth and glory, yet you, possessing talent and beauty surpassing ten thousand others, instead try to hide it. You are truly a pure woman. Among the thousands of palace ladies, you are the only one. (Walks behind the palace building. Only voices are heard.)... Hey, look here. Won't you give up going to the Chanyu and stay here in this palace? I desire only you, more than thousands of palace ladies.
(The voices gradually fade until they are completely inaudible. The stage is empty for a moment. After some time, voices are heard again.)
Zhaojun: To serve by Your Majesty's side would be, for my humble self, the greatest blessing imaginable. However, I have resolved to do my utmost for the country as far as possible, and furthermore, having met face-to-face with the Chanyu's envoys, I believe I cannot possibly back out now.
(They emerge from behind the palace building.)
Emperor: Yes, at this point, it cannot be helped. Even so, regarding your decision to go to the Xiongnu (匃奴), I believe there must be some deeper reason beyond just doing it for the country, but still... <78>
Zhaojun: From the moment I left my hometown, I became estranged from the world. I consider myself as non-existent. I do not wish to constantly see a world where people greedily pursue glory (榮華) and wealth (富貴).
Emperor: Why did you become estranged from the world again?
Emperor: You miss your parents and hometown dearly, that's why.
(Zhaojun does not answer.)
Emperor: (Sighs briefly.) It's not strange. It is all my fault. My sin. But speaking of the loneliness (孤寂) at that time, I couldn't help but act as I did. (They go and sit on a bench in the courtyard.) The loyal minister Hou Ying risked death, tearfully pleading (泣諫) with me to stop selecting beauties, but I absolutely could not listen. Following the late Emperor's instructions, I had not replaced the palace ladies for 20 years, so everyone around me was an old woman around 40. I thought it was the crime of the people to keep me amidst such a bleak landscape (殺風景). To be honest, I couldn't bear the loneliness. I was at the point of thinking death was preferable to living such a lonely life. That's why I sent people to the provinces to select palace ladies. If you consider my feelings at that time, perhaps my actions cannot be called a crime that tormented the people. (Pause.) No, what's past cannot be helped. I spoke unnecessarily of this to you. Now, I am only ashamed of my lack of insight that led to having to send a commendable girl like you far away. It's unfortunate for both you and me.
(An attendant approaches from downstage, seemingly searching. Seeing the two, he hesitates briefly, then resolves himself and kowtows before the Emperor.)
Emperor: (Glancing briefly) What is it?
Attendant: It is reported that Mao Yanshou's execution was carried out without incident just now.
Emperor: It was carried out without incident, you say?
Attendant: Yes. However, at the very end, Yanshou asked for a message to be conveyed to Your Majesty, uttering audacious words.
Emperor: What did he say? <79>
Attendant: It is too disrespectful...
Emperor: It's fine. Tell me what he said.
Attendant: Yanshou said that His Majesty, the Sage Emperor, ending the life of a meritorious official of several decades for the sake of a mere woman, is an omen of the Han dynasty's decline (漢家衰微之兆朕)...
Emperor: Be silent! Do you know of any crime greater than a subject deceiving his sovereign? I condemned him to death out of hatred for that crime. I swear to Heaven and Earth, it was a just decision.
Attendant: Yes... And another thing. He said, 'Until now, I believed any person's heart could be freely controlled with the power of money (金力), but today, that belief of mine has been shattered. Your Majesty also believes that with power (權力), all worldly affairs proceed according to your will, but a time will come when that belief too will be shattered...'
Emperor: What! Although power rests with me, I absolutely do not wield it knowingly as my own. It is rather as if the people entrusted power to me. Can the entrusted person act freely? What does he mean, 'belief will be shattered'?
Attendant: No, Your Majesty. Those are not my words, but Mao Yanshou's...
Emperor: He is a hateful fellow to the very end.
Attendant: And one more thing...
Emperor: Enough, I don't want to hear it! I have no ears to listen to the malicious words (惡談) of a condemned criminal.
Attendant: No, Your Majesty. This is not part of his last words (遺言).
Emperor: What is it then?
Attendant: When Yanshou's residence was searched, countless gold, silver, and treasures (金銀財寶) were discovered. The searchers returned astonished, saying even a prime minister could not easily amass such wealth.
Emperor: That would be so. Such things are disgusting even to look at. Bury them together with Yanshou's corpse. <80>
Attendant: Yes! (Startled.) Bury them together, Your Majesty?
Emperor: That gold and silver does not belong in this world.
Attendant: That may be so, but such vast treasures...
Emperor: It is none of your concern. Go tell the eunuch to bring wine.
(The attendant exits reluctantly.)
Emperor: (Standing up, helping Zhaojun back towards the palace interior, as if to himself.) That fellow Mao Yanshou is a traitor (不忠漢) for the ages. Even though I looked after him for a long time, he betrayed me. Even executing him does not appease my anger. (To Zhaojun.) Losing you is also because of that scoundrel. It would have been better if I had personally beheaded him and hung his head for a month. Even then, my anger would not subside; that's how much I hate him. (Enters the palace and sits down.)
(Eunuchs bring wine and quickly exit. Zhaojun pours into two cups.)
Zhaojun: (As if comforting him) Your Majesty, this is now the final farewell.
Emperor: (Suddenly cheering up slightly.) Ah, the final parting drink.
Zhaojun: Your servant will also drink without refusal. (Raises her cup.)
Emperor: (Lifting his cup.) Yes, do so. Refusal is unacceptable. Come, let's toast.
(They drink a toast - 乾盃.)
Emperor: If this were a pleasant banquet on a long night, how delightful it would be. But thinking of it as the parting drink of this life, the wine has no taste, and my head just feels empty. Truly, how lonely. (Pours for himself and drinks two or three cups consecutively.) Even though Mao Yanshou is dead, my misfortune remains misfortune. When will this resentment ever fade? (Becomes crestfallen.) Speaking of hate, though it seems pointless, the Chanyu's actions are also limitlessly hateful. If the Chanyu hadn't sent envoys, even if you were buried in the rear palace for a time, there would have been a chance to discover you eventually... (Drinks wine.)
Zhaojun: It is all fate, Your Majesty. <81>
Emperor: Fate may be fate, but this is something I cannot simply dismiss and resign myself to as mere fate; I have an attachment. The Chanyu is a fellow who becomes an enemy if we don't make him an ally. He was originally an enemy, so we made him an ally. Even if it is fate, having to helplessly give up you, the most beautiful woman in the palace, to that enemy Chanyu is not only unbearable suffering for me but also a supreme humiliation (屈辱). Why must the ruler of China meekly accept this humiliation? Is there perhaps some sin I committed that requires me to meekly accept this humiliating fate? (Gradually gets drunk.)
Zhaojun: Your Majesty, your servant is also a subject of China. As one of Your Majesty's children (赤子), how could your servant gladly leave China and bid farewell to Your Majesty's side? Being compelled to go to a man for whom I feel no love must be the unavoidable circumstance dictated by fate.
Emperor: (Keeps tilting his cup.)
Zhaojun: Your servant is still just a woman. Leaving country, home, parents, and also love and affection, the pain in my heart as I go to the frontier is more unspeakable than Your Majesty's.
Emperor: (Drinks wine.)
Zhaojun: Offering my pure woman's body without love, merely into the embrace of the barbarian Xiongnu... (Covers her eyes.)
Emperor: Yes. Having to place you, pure and lovely as a flower, into the arms of that hairy, demon-like, bear-like Chanyu... (Stands up and quickly tilts his cup.) Ahh, I cannot sit still! I cannot bear it! My head feels like it's boiling with blood and about to burst! Wine, wine! (Bangs the empty cup on the table. Zhaojun pours wine. The Emperor drains it in one gulp.) What humiliation is this for the ruler of China! Is my power, as Mao Yanshou said, unable to hold onto a single woman?... Very well! I will not send you to the Xiongnu! Let the world go however it may! As long as I have you, that's enough! For you alone, no sacrifice is too great! What are the Xiongnu? What is the Chanyu? What is a promise? How great is my power? I will use military force if necessary to keep you! Now it's war! War! Zhaojun, for you, <82> I don't regret risking the nation! Quickly relay the order to prepare for battle! Hey! Are there no eunuchs? Is Hou Ying not here?
Zhaojun: (Soothingly.) Your Majesty, Your Majesty!
(Two eunuchs rush in.)
Emperor: Hey! It's war! As a pretext for war, throw out the Chanyu's two envoys! And quickly tell the ministers, it's war! Hurry, hurry!
(The two eunuchs also try to calm the Emperor. While they do so, the Emperor keeps shouting "War! War!" They gently help him lie down to sleep, and the two eunuchs exit. (Pause))
(A little later, the Emperor suddenly sits bolt upright and again shouts "War! War!", Zhaojun holds him back.)
Emperor: (Suddenly coming to his senses, looks into her face.) Oh, you are Zhaojun?
Zhaojun: Please sleep a little longer.
Emperor: Yes. (Stares at Zhaojun for a while, then, as if struck by a thought, quietly gets off the bed and firmly takes Zhaojun's hand. Zhaojun starts in surprise and tries to pull away.) Revenge (復讐)! Revenge against the Chanyu! (Zhaojun is startled.) This way, it will instead be an insult to the Chanyu.
(Zhaojun, unable to bear it, covers her face. The Emperor gently puts his arm around her shoulder. The sound of insects chirping is heard.)
-(End)- <83>

**Summary**

This excerpt from the play "Wang Zhaojun" depicts Emperor Xiaoyuan of Han China selecting palace ladies. Disappointed initially, he is struck by Wang Zhaojun's portrait but is dissuaded by the painter Mao Yanshou, who falsely claims she has ominous moles. When Xiongnu envoys request a princess for marriage alliance, Zhaojun volunteers to go instead. Upon finally meeting her before her departure, the Emperor realizes Yanshou lied (the moles are gone or were never there). Furious, he orders Yanshou's execution but feels powerless as Zhaojun must still leave due to the diplomatic promise. He laments losing her beauty and purity to the "barbarian" Chanyu, gets drunk, declares war in frustration, and ultimately decides to take Zhaojun for himself before she leaves as an act of "revenge" against the Chanyu.

From a colonial Korean history perspective, the translation and publication of this Chinese historical play in 1925 are interesting. It shows a continued engagement with classical East Asian culture and history despite Japanese rule. The themes of political maneuvering (Yanshou's deceit), the conflict between personal desire and state duty (Emperor), and sacrifice for the nation (Zhaojun) might have resonated with Korean intellectuals contemplating their own situation, albeit indirectly. The portrayal of the Xiongnu and the dynamics of tributary relations/marriage alliances could also offer a lens through which contemporary power imbalances were viewed or discussed symbolically.

문을 다친 內殿의 일부가, 우편의 3分의 2를 점하얏다. 알에 편 기타 빈곳은 여긔저긔 나무가 섯고 밤 10시경이다. 한울은 어둑하고 四面도 어둠침침하다. 殿內는 등불 그림자가 훤하고 琵琶의 소리가 안에서 새어나온다. 한동안은 琵琶소리.
(皇帝가 微*을 띄고 단지 혼자 알에쪽에서 나타나서 琵琶의 소리를 딸하 갓가이 가서 한참 듯고 잇다. 그리드니 미소하며 층계를 올라가 가만히 문을 연다. 등불 그림자가 뜰에 떨어진다. 琵琶를 타든 昭君이 慌忙히 跪拜하랴 한다.)
皇帝. 그냥 잇거라. (昭君을 붓들어 닐으키며) 고만 破興을 시키어 안됏다. (걸어 안는다.) 連日連夜 계속한 送別宴도 아주 오늘밤이 마즈막이기로 朕도 앗가는 술을 전보다 과히 먹엇다. 그 까닭인지 모르겟다만, 자랴고 해도 잠이 안 와서, 서늘한 밤바람에 취한 술을 깨랴고 뜰로 나려와 이리저리 근일랴니까, 문득 듯지 못하든 琵琶소리가 나기에, 응, 韻致잇는 애도 잇구나 하고, 그 소리를 딸하오니 네 처소야. 그래서 暫間 들으니 그 소리가 처량하게 悲調를 띄어 사람으로 하야금 견딀 수가 업게 해. 내일은 中國을 떠나 멀리 塞外로 가랴하는 너의 心緖가 脈脈히 音調에 나타난단 말이다. 필시 너도 정신업시 뜻고 잇섯지. 자칫하야 고만 가엽슨 일을 하얏다.
昭君. 더러운 소리가 天聽에 들리어 죽을 죄를 젓습니다. 臣妾도 앗가 잔치에 못먹는 술을 족음 만히 먹사와 마음이 풀어지옵기, 이 세상의 마즈막으로 어렷슬 때에 배운 琵琶를 끄내어, 혼자 고르고 잇섯사옵니다. 밤에 擾亂히 하와 惶悚하옵니다.
(달이 띠 오른다.)
皇帝. 嫺熟한 솜씨로 마음 들여 타지 아니하면, 그런 소리는 안 나는 것이다. 무심한 사람도 哀調에 마음이 動하는구나. 한 曲調, 더 타거라.
昭君. 이러케 拒逆하옵는게 惶悚하옵니다마는 그것은 용서하야 주옵소서.＜77＞
皇帝. 더 못타겟단 말이냐.
昭君. 네. 惶悚하오나 무심한 地境이 깨어지고 잡념이 석기어 타드래도 그러케 안될가 하옵니다.
皇帝. 응 그건 네 말이 올타. 모든 藝術이란 것은, 잡념이 석기어 마음의 통일을 일우지 못하면, 神秘한 極致에 니르지 못하는 것이다. 억지 청을 하얏구나.
昭君. 惶悚하옵니다.
皇帝. (돌아보며.) 어, 달이 떳나보다. 너도 서울 달은 인젠, 보지를 못할라. 마즈막으로 뜰에 내려가 이러저리, 건일지 안흐랴느냐. 섭섭하니.
(닐어나서 昭君을 부축하야 층계를 내려간다.)
皇帝. 달 조타. 봄 달은 따뜻하지만, 가을달은 근심이 만탄다. 朕은 인제부터 오늘밤 가튼 달을 볼 때마다, 멀고 먼 塞外에 잇는 너를 생각하고 琵琶ㅅ소리를 혼자, 追想하겟구나. 아니,  너의 일은 永却으로 잇지 못할게다. (뜰로 가만히 逍遙한다.) 세상에는 제 才貌를 자랑하며 부귀영화를 꿈꾸는 것들만 만흔데, 너는 萬人에 뛰어나는 才色을 가지고서도 돌이어 그것을 숨기랴하니, 참으로 깨끗한 계집애다. 數千名이나 되는 宮女 중에 단지 너 하나로구나. (殿閣뒤로 들어간다. 말소리만 들리인다.)... 이애 이봐라. 單于에게 가는 것을 고만두고 이 宮中에 잇서주지 안흐랴느냐. 朕은 數千의 宮女보다 단지 너 하나를 바란다.
(소리가 차차 들리지 아니하야, 나종에는 아조 아니 들린다. 잠시는 무대가 비엇다. 얼마잇드니 또 들리인다.)
昭君. 陛下의 겨틀 모시옵는 것은 臣妾의 몸되와서는 이 우에 다시 업는 弘福이오나 臣妾도 일단, 될 수 잇는대까지는 나라를 위하야 盡心하기로 결심하얏삽고, 또는 單于의 使者와도 맛 對面을 하고 낫사온즉, 지금 다시 고만둘 수도 업슬가 하옵니다.
(殿閣뒤에서 나아온다.)
皇帝. 응, 지금되어서는 하는 수 업겟지. 그건 그리타하드래도 네가 匃奴에게로 가랴고 決心한데 대해서는, 나라를 위하야 그리는 것보다도 그 외에 더 무슨 깁흔 사정이 잇는 줄 아는데, 그러나.＜78＞
昭君. 臣妾은 고향을 떠날 때부터 세상과 남이 되엇습니다. 臣妾의 자신을 업는 것으로 생각을 하고 잇사옵니다. 榮華이니, 富貴이니 하고 눈을 홉드고 덤비는 세상을 노상 보고십지는 안사옵니다.
皇帝. 웨 또 세상과 남이 되엇서?
皇帝. 부모와 家鄕을 떠나 생각이 간절해 그리지.
(昭君이 대답을 아니한다.)
皇帝. (暫間 한숨을 쉬며) 怪異치 안타. 모도가 나의 잘못이다. 나의 죄다. 그러나 그때의 孤寂한 것으로 말을 하면 나는 그러케 안 할 수 업섯다. (둘이 뜰의 의자에 가서 걸어 안는다.) 忠臣 侯應은 죽음을 무릅쓰고 朕에게 佳人골르는 것을 중지하도록 泣諫을 하얏지만, 그것은 도저히 들을 수가 업섯다. 先皇의 遺訓을 지키어 나는 20년 동안이나 宮女를 갈아들이지 아니하얏드니 나의 주위에 잇는 것은 모도가 40前後의 노파뿐이라. 그러한 殺風景 가운대다 나를 잇게하는 것은 國民의 罪惡이라고, 나는 생각하얏다. 實狀말이지 나는 孤寂해 견딀 수가 업섯다. 이러케 孤寂한 생활을 하라면은 차라리 죽은 것이 낫다고 생각할 쯤이엇다. 그래서 나는 지방에 사람을 보내어 宮女를 고르게 한 것인데, 당시 나의 심정을 생각해줄 것 가트면, 나의 한 일이 그리 국민을 괴롭게 한 罪惡이라고 안 할 수 업는 듯하다. (동안 뜸.) 아니, 지낸 일은 어찌하는 수 업다. 이런 말을 네게 공연히 하얏구나. 지금 되어서는 너 가튼 기특한 애를 멀리 보내지 안 하면 안되게 된 나의 不明을 부끄러할 뿐이다. 네게 대해서나 내게 대해서나 모도 조치못한 일이다.
(侍臣이 알엣 편에서 차저단이는 모양으로 와서 두 사람을 보고 暫間 주저하드니, 결심을 하고 皇帝,압헤 跪拜한다.)
皇帝. (얼핏보고) 무슨 일이냐.
侍臣. 지금, 毛延壽의 사형을 무난히 집행하얏다 하옵니다.
皇帝. 무사히 하얏단 말이냐.
侍臣. 네의. 그러하온데 延壽는 마즈막 판에 陛下께 알외여 달라하고, 무엄한 말을 하왓다 하옵니다.
皇帝. 무에라고 말하얏서.＜79＞
侍臣. 넘우 惶悚하와...
皇帝. 괜찬타. 무에라구 말하얏는지, 말해라.
侍臣. 延壽가 하옵기를, 聖上陛下가, 한 兒女子를 위하야 數十年來의 功臣의 命을 끈는 것은, 漢家衰微의 兆朕...
皇帝. 잠자코 잇거라. 신하로 임군을 속이는 것만큼, 큰 죄가 또 잇는 줄 아느냐. 朕은 그 죄를 미워하야 死罪에 처한 것이다. 天地에 盟誓코 公明한 처치다.
侍臣. 네의... 그리고 또 하나. 저는 이때까지 金力이면 어떠한 사람의 마음이든지 자유로 되는 줄만 알앗드니 오늘날, 그 자신이 개어젓다. 陛下께서도 어느 권력이면, 세상만사가 다 당신의 뜻대로 되는 줄 밋고 계시지만, 그 자신도 한번은 깨어질 시기가 올 것이니까...
皇帝. 뭐얏. 권력은 朕에게 잇서도, 朕은 그것을 절대로 朕의 것으르 알고 내두루지는 아니한다. 말하면 국민이 朕에게 권력을 맛긴 것이나 다름업는 것이다. 마튼 사람이 자유로 할 수 잇슬가. 자신이 깨어진다는 것은 뭐냐.
侍臣. 아니옵니다. 그것은 小臣이 하옵는 말슴이 아니오라, 毛延壽가...
皇帝. 어대까지든지 미운 놈이다.
侍臣. 그리하옵고 또 하나...
皇帝. 에라 듯기 실타. 死刑罪囚의 惡談을 들을 귀는 안가젓다.
侍臣. 아니옵니다. 이것은 遺言이 아니옵니다.
皇帝. 뭐냐.
侍臣. 延壽의 家宅을 수색하왓삽드니, 無數한 金銀財寶가 나타낫다 하옵니다. 宰相이라도 그만한 金銀을 모으기는 容易한 일이 아니라고, 수색하라 갓든 자들이 놀라 가지고 왓다 하옵니다.
皇帝. 그러할 것이다. 그런 것은 보기도 더러우니 延壽의 송장과 가티 무더버려라.＜80＞
侍臣. 녯! (놀란다.) 함께 뭇사옵니까.
皇帝. 이 세상의 보내는 金銀이 아니다.
侍臣. 그럽기는 하오나 그 만흔 財寶를...
皇帝. 너의 알 배 아니다. 너는 나가 술을 가저오도록 內監에게 전해라.
(侍臣이 하는 수 업시 나아간다.)
皇帝. (닐어서서, 昭君을 부축하야 앗가 殿中으로 들어가면서, 혼자말가티.) 毛延壽란 놈은 千古의 不忠漢이다. 오랫동안 돌보아주엇는데두, 나를 배반하얏구나. 사형에 처하야도 내 분은 풀리지가 아니하는구나. (昭君에게.) 너를 닐케된 것도 아 그놈 때문이다. 내가 친히 그놈의 목을 베어 한 달을 매어 달어,노핫드면 조흘 걸 그랫다. 그래도 내 분이 안 풀리도록 난 그놈이 미웁다. (殿內에 들어가 걸어 안는다.)
(內監들이 술을 가저오고 곳 나아간다. 昭君이 두 잔에다가 딸흔다.)
昭君. (위로하는 듯이) 陛下, 인제 마즈막 下直이옵니다.
皇帝. (별안간 풀치며.) 어, 마즈막 이별주다.
昭君. 臣妾도 사양안코 먹겟습니다. (잔을 든다.)
皇帝. (盞을 들고.) 응 그래라. 사양은 못 쓴다. 자 건배하자.
(둘이 乾盃한다.)
皇帝. 이것이 조흔 長夜의 宴이엇드면 얼마나 질거웟겟느냐만, 이 生의 離別酒로 생각하니, 술이 맛지를 아니하고 머리만 휑하구나. 참 무척, 외로웁구나. (손수 딸하, 두서너 잔을 계속하야 마신다.) 毛延壽는 죽엿서도 나의 불행은 依然한 불행이로구나. 이 恨이 언제야 가시랴. (풀이 죽는다.) 미운 것으로 말을 하면, 쓸대업는 말 갓지마는, 單于의 소위도 미웁기가 限量업다. 單于가 使者만 보내지 안햇드면, 설사 한 때는 네가 後宮에 파무텨 잇섯슬지라도 한번은 발견할 기회가 잇섯슬 터이니... (술을 마신다.)
昭君. 모도 다 운명이옵니다.＜81＞
皇帝. 운명은 운명이겟지만, 이것은 그저 덥허노코 운명이라고만 하고 단념할 수가 업는 집착이 내겐 잇다. 單于는 이 편을 맨들지 아니하면 敵國이 되는 놈이다. 원래가 敵國이니까 이 편으로 맨들어 논 것이다. 그 敵國 單于에게 이모리 운명이라 할지라도, 宮中에 제일가는 어엽븐 너를, 고만 안내노치 못하게 된 것은, 나에게는 견딀 수 업는 고통일 뿐 아니라, 絶大한 굴욕이다. 中國의 인군으로 잇는 者가 달게 이 굴욕밧지 안이하면 안 되는 것은 무슨 일이란 말이냐. 혹 그러면 내가 이 屈辱的 運命을 달게 밧지 안흐면 안될 죄라도 잇단 말이냐.(차차 취한다.)
昭君. 陛下, 臣妾도 中國의 백성이옵니다. 陛下의 赤子의 한 사람되는 臣妾으로, 어찌 질기여 中國을 떠나 陛下의 겨틀, 下直하겟습니까. 사랑도 업는 사람에게 가지 안흘 수 업게 되옵는 것은 운명이 시키는 할 수 업는 사정인가 하옵니다.
皇帝. (자꾸 잔을 기울인다.)
昭君. 臣妾도 아즉 계집애옵니다. 나라와 집과 부모와 그리고 사랑과 恩情, 모든 것을 버리고 塞外로 가는 心中에는 陛下보담 더 말못할 고통이 잇사옵니다.
皇帝. (술을 마신다.)
昭君. 깨끗한 계집아이 몸을 사랑도 업시, 다만 우익한 匃奴의 가슴에 바치는 것은... (눈을 갈이운다.)
皇帝. 응. 꼿과 가티 깨끗하고 어여븐 너를, 저 털투성이한, 鬼神갓고 곰과 가튼 單于의 팔에 다가 안기게 하지안흐면 안되다니... (닐어나서 급히 잔을 기울이며) 아아 나는, 안젓슬 수가 업다. 견딀 수가 업다. 내 머리는 피가 끌허 금방 터질 것 갓다. 술, 술. (빈잔으로 床 우를 두드린다. 昭君이 술을 딸흔다. 皇帝가 한숨에 들이킨다.) 中國의 인군으로 이게 무슨 굴욕이냐. 나의 권력이 毛延壽의 말과 가티, 한 兒女子를 붓들 수가 업단 말이냐... 오냐, 난 너를 匃奴에겐 보내지 안흘테다. 천하는 아모러케든지 태라. 난 너 하나만 잇스면  고만이다. 너 하나를 위해서는 아모 것을 희생하야도 앗갑지 안타. 匃奴는 다 무에냐. 單于는 다 뭐냐. 言約은 다 뭐냐. 내 권력이 얼마나 하냐. 난 兵力을 써서라도 너 하나를 붓잡겟다. 인제는 싸움이다. 전쟁이다. 昭君아, 난＜82＞ 너를 위해서는 나라를 기울여도 앗갑지 안타. 얼른 出戰의 준비를 하라고 전해라. 이봐라. 內監 업느냐. 候應은 업느냐.
昭君. (달래드키.) 陛下, 陛下.
(內監 둘이 급히 온다.)
皇帝. 이애 出戰이다. 出戰하는 고사로 單于의 使者둘을 내어벼라. 그리고 빨리 出戰이라고 大臣에게 전해라. 얼른, 얼른.
(內監 둘도 帝를 달래인다. 그리하는 동안에도 皇帝는 出戰出戰하고 부르짓는다. 가만히 잠을 들려노코 두 內監이 나아간다. (동안 뜸)
족음 잇드니 皇帝가 벌덕 몸을 닐으키어 또 「出戰出戰」하며 부르짓는 것을 昭君이 붓잡는다.)
皇帝. (믄득 정신이 나서 얼굴을 들여다 보고.) 어, 너 昭君이냐.
昭君. 더 족음만 주므십시요.
皇帝. 응. (한참 昭君을 바라보고 잇드니, 무슨 생각을 하얏는지 가만히 침대에서 나려와서 昭君의 손을 떡 잡는다. 昭君이 깜작 놀나 피하랴 한다.) 복수다. 單于에게 복수다. (昭君이 놀랜다.) 이래야 돌이어 單于를 욕뵈는 것이 되는 것이다.
(昭君이 견딀 수 업서 얼굴을 가리운다. 皇帝가 가만히 그 엇개를 얼싸안는다. 버레소리가 들리인다.)
