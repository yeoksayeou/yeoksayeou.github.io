信仰
						素月
Close your eyes and think silently
For the life weeping under the heavy burden,
To add oceanic rest,
Surely a helping hand with strength
Waits for you.

But does the road end and the day grow dark?
Oh, pitiful life!
The bell sound shakes the boat rope,
When the ill-fated dirge slants in,
You will bow your head and sigh.

But kneel down quietly
And pray, strongly, reverently.
Within your heart,
The beautiful God who protects you,
Look up high and worship Him.

Though the yoke is painful and the burden heavy,
The gate held by two legs will open soon.
The flickering lamp held in your bosom <34>
With the oil of gentle wisdom,
Fill it and fill it again.

Then, on the spring hill of life,
The high branch grateful for existence,
On the forgotten peak of truth, leaves will bloom,
And the fine grass burning with faith
Will wrap your naked soul.

저녁때

Cattle, horses, and people return home, and on the寂寂 (lonely) empty stone
The tree frog's cry croaks out.
The blue sky lowers further, on the distant mountain slope's dark place
Tall, towering trees stand here and there, even the birds have roosted.

In the vast field, as I watch,
Staring vacantly at the water's reflection,
Head bowed, standing alone as if nailed down,
Do you let out a long sigh? Why so much!

Completely forgetting what has come, here together in the deepening night
When the body feels light with thought and the mind soars higher,
Suddenly, from the nearby reeds,
Starlight surges up. <35>

**Summary**

The first poem, "Faith" (信仰), encourages perseverance through hardship by finding solace and strength in quiet contemplation and devout faith in a protective God. It suggests that inner spiritual readiness ("filling the lamp with wisdom's oil") will lead to renewal and comfort for the soul. The second poem, "Evening Time" (저녁때), paints a tranquil yet melancholic scene of dusk, focusing on a solitary figure contemplating by the water, ending with a sudden spark of hope or realization symbolized by starlight. From a historical perspective, these poems by Sowŏl, a major figure in modern Korean poetry, are interesting for their focus on inner spirituality and resilience amidst suffering, themes that could resonate with the experiences of Koreans under Japanese colonial rule (though not explicitly political). The blend of personal introspection, nature imagery, and spiritual longing offers insight into the sentiments and coping mechanisms prevalent during that era.
