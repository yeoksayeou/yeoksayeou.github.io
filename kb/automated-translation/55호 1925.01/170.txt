Magazine Title: 개벽 제55호
Publication Date: 1925년 01월 01일
Article Title: 牛頌 (Ode to the Cow)
Author: 春園
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

牛頌
						春園
When heaven and earth first opened,
And all creation was brought forth,
Everything was different.

As their appearances differed,
How could their natures not differ?
As their natures differed,
Did they not each have different talents?
And as their talents differed,
Their virtues also were diverse.

The flea, being small,
Has the talent of jumping high;
The elephant, its body large,
Its steps are slow.

Does the tiger have wings?
Does the donkey have horns?
One bites,
One kicks;
Their biting and kicking differ.

The mosquito is a creature of the night,
Its sound naturally loud;
The firefly is mute,
So it carries a lamp on its back.
The grub and the earthworm
Have neither eyes nor nose,
But their talents for crawling and digging,
Seeing their forms, are remarkable.

The cat is elegant,
But sly and arrogant;
The dog, though shaggy,
Knows kindness and is not haughty;
The rooster is all bluster,
The hen, a shrew.<106>

The elegant crane,
Its legs are black, making it common;
The sweet-voiced oriole,
They say, has not a single home.

The fox is king of cunning,
The bear is king of foolishness.
Though the crow's appearance is ugly,
They say it has filial piety (反哺之孝);
Though the spider is repulsive,
Its parental love (愛子之情) is profound.

Alas, the myriad creatures of heaven and earth,
How can one discuss them all?
The large ones, the small ones,
The long ones and the short ones,
Those that fly and those that crawl,
Those that swim in the water,
Those that burrow in the earth,
The elegant ones, the detestable ones,
The slithering snake-like ones,
The flitting wagtail,
The chattering baby sparrow,
The swift flea,
The dignified lion,
The pretty squirrel,
The diligent mouse,
The skillful wren,
The heroic stag beetle,
The long-living ibis,
The spirited wild goose –
The countless myriad things of heaven and earth,
How could one name them all?
Though all talents exist,
The virtuous are rare.

In the mountains, too, squabbling,
In the fields, too, bickering,
Biting and kicking,
Robbing and scratching,<107>
Everywhere there is trouble,
Everywhere there is fighting.

Heaven and earth being this way, what is to be done?
Being so savage, what is to be done?
Everyone just schemes,
Only tries to deceive,
Only tries to devour,
Only tries to posture and look after their own interests.
Ah, heaven and earth being this way, what is to be done?

When creating all forms,
The gentle form was forgotten.
When creating all talents,
The talent of service was forgotten.
When creating all virtues,
The virtue of compassion was forgotten.
So He created them –
Perhaps the Kirin and the deer.
Though the Kirin and deer are gentle,
They are showy and arrogant,
Lazy and useless.

The sheep is kind,
But weak, so what use is it?
The goat is strong, they say,
But ill-tempered and useless.

Possessing strength and might,
Yet kind and dignified,
Modest and humble,
Without greed and diligent,
Sacrificing its body for others –
Ah, your name is Cow!
Ah, your name is Cow!

From this, I wonder if heaven and earth will learn compassion and become peaceful.<108>

**Summary**

This poem by Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu) contrasts the diverse traits of various animals, highlighting their flaws – cunning, arrogance, laziness, aggression – and laments the resulting strife and lack of virtue in the world. It culminates in praising the cow as the embodiment of ideal qualities: strength combined with gentleness, dignity with humility, diligence, selflessness, and service to others. The poet suggests the world could learn compassion from the cow and achieve peace. From a historical perspective, the poem, published in the nationalist magazine *Kaeppyŏk* during the Japanese colonial period, could be interpreted as an allegorical critique of human society, potentially reflecting on the state of colonized Korea. The emphasis on virtues like diligence, sacrifice, and humility, personified by the cow, might resonate with nationalist ideals of perseverance or, conversely, could be seen as echoing certain values promoted by the colonial regime, making its historical interpretation complex and interesting for historians studying colonial-era discourse and literature.
