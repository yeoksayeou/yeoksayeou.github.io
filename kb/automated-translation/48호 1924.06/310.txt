Sigh of the White Hands
						P'al P'ong

Sigh of the White Hands

Sitting slumped on a cafe chair,
Showing off their white, white arms,
Shouting "V Narod!"—
The Russian youth of sixty years ago are before my eyes...

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
Your hands are far too white!

Showing off their white, white arms,
Saying with their mouths, "V Narod!"...
The vain sigh of the Russian youth
Of sixty years ago is with us—

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
Your hands are far too white!

You are the "White Hands"—<136>
To the peasants you wish to go to,
You haven't the slightest, slightest bit
Of what could be called "Taste" (味覺).

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
Your hands are far too white!

Ah, the old days of sixty years ago,
The "Sigh of the White Hands" of the Russian youth
Was a sigh made with all their might, all their might,
Wishing to go down after killing their "Taste."

Ah! Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
Your hands are far too white!

Granite

I am watching—
On the pages of history, the shadow
Of the people, like granite.

Always, above the heads of the people,<137>
Towers of various colors stood,
And they judged the people.

I know—
That when the lives of the people are shaken,
Pitifully, the towers crumble.

More than politicians, more than poets,
The people, keeping firmly silent, like granite—
Might they not be the more admirable ones?

Oh, on the pages of history, the shadow of the people,
Like granite, the final judge,
I am now, placing it before my eyes and thinking.<138>
<136-138>

**Summary**

The first poem, "Sigh of the White Hands," critiques contemporary Korean intellectuals (dubbed "Cafe Chair Revolutionists") for their detachment and inaction. Comparing them unfavorably to the Russian Narodniks of the 1860s, the poet laments that these intellectuals merely posture with revolutionary slogans like "V Narod!" while their "white hands" signify their lack of connection to the working masses or peasants, lacking the genuine "Taste" or understanding needed to engage with them. The second poem, "Granite," contrasts the transience of political and intellectual elites ("towers") with the enduring, silent strength of the common people ("granite"). The people are portrayed as the ultimate foundation and judge of history. From a colonial history perspective, these poems are interesting as they reflect the internal debates and self-criticism among Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule regarding the effective means of social change or national liberation. P'al P'ong (Kim Ki-jin), associated with the proletarian literature movement, emphasizes the perceived gap between intellectuals and the masses, urging for genuine connection and action, a theme prevalent in leftist circles of the time grappling with how to mobilize the populace.

白手의 嘆息
						팔봉
白手의 嘆息
카페 의자에 걸터 안저서
희고 힌 팔을 뽑내여가며
우·나로-드! 라고 떠들고 잇는
六十年前의 露西亞 청년이 눈 압헤 잇다...

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
너희들의 손이 너머도 희고나!

희고 흰 팔을 뽑내여가며
입으로 말하기는 『우·나로-드!』...
六十年前의 露西亞 청년의
헛되인 탄식이 우리에게 잇다-

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
너희들의 손이 너머도 희고나!

너희들은 『白手』-＜136＞
가고자 하는 농민들에게는
되지도 못한 『味覺』이라고는
조곰도, 조곰도 업다는 말이다.

Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
너희들의 손이 너머도 희고나!

아아 六十年前의 녯날,
露西亞 청년의 『白手의 嘆息』은
味覺을 죽이고서 네려가서고자 하든
全力을 다 하든 全力을 다 하든 嘆息이엿다.

Ah! Cafe Chair Revolutionist,
너희들의 손이 너머도 희여!

花崗石

나는 보고잇다-
歷史의 페-지에 낫하나 잇는
花罔石과 갓흔 人民의 그림자를,

언제든지 인민의 대가리 우에는＜137＞
別別色色의 塔이 서 가지고
그것들이 인민을 審判하고 잇섯다.

나는 알고 잇다-
인민의 생활이 뒤흔들닐 때에는
애처로웁게도 塔은 부서진다는 것을.

政治家보다도 詩人보다도
꾹 담을고 잇는, 花罔石과 갓흔
인민이야말로 더 훌늉한 편이 아닐넌지-

오오 歷史의 페지에 낫하나 잇는, 花罔石과 갓흔
인민의 그림자를, 최후의 심판자를,
나는 지금, 눈 압헤 놋코 생각하고 잇다.＜138＞
