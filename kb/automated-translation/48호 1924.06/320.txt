필자	憑虛
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Short Story)

운수 조흔 날
						憑虛

It was a day when the sullenly overcast sky seemed to promise snow, but instead of snow, a half-frozen rain drizzled down steadily.
This very day was, for Kim Ch'ŏmji, who worked as a rickshaw puller inside Tongso Mun (East Small Gate), a lucky day that hadn't come around in a long while. Starting with taking the lady from the house opposite his (who insisted she was going *into* the gate, though technically her house was already inside) to the streetcar line, he loitered around the stop, hoping for another fare, casting almost begging glances at each person getting off. Finally, he managed to take a man in Western clothes, seemingly a teacher, to Tonggwang Hakkyo (Tonggwang School).

First fare 30 chŏn, second fare 50 chŏn—not bad at all for the early morning. Truly, fortune was smiling; Kim Ch'ŏmji, who hadn't seen the color of money for nearly ten days, was so happy he almost shed tears when the three or five 10-chŏn nickel coins clinked into his palm. Moreover, he couldn't know how useful this 80 chŏn would be to him on this day, at this time. Not only could he wet his parched throat with a glass of moju, but more importantly, he could buy a bowl of sŏllŏngt'ang (ox bone soup) for his sick wife.

His wife had been coughing violently for over a month now. Since they were barely eating even millet gruel, they had, of course, never managed even a single dose of medicine. It wasn't that he absolutely couldn't afford it if he tried, but he faithfully adhered to his own creed that if you give medicine to this fellow called illness, it gets spoiled and keeps coming back. Consequently, she had never seen a doctor, <139> so he didn't know what illness she had, but seeing that she was bedridden and couldn't even get up sideways, let alone straight, it seemed serious indeed. Her illness had worsened like this ten days ago after she got indigestion from eating millet. At that time too, Kim Ch'ŏmji had earned some money after a long while and bought a toe (unit of measure) of millet and a 10-chŏn bundle of firewood. According to Kim Ch'ŏmji, that damned wench had recklessly boiled it in the pot. Impatient and with the fire not hot enough, that damned wench, forgetting the spoon and just grabbing it with her hands, had stuffed it down as if someone would snatch it away, her cheeks bulging like fists, until that evening she started complaining of chest tightness and stomach pain, rolling her eyes back in a fit. At that time, Kim Ch'ŏmji, furious, yelled, "Agh, that damned wench! Cursed with bad luck! Sick from not eating, sick from eating! What am I supposed to do? Why can't you keep your eyes straight!" and slapped his ailing wife's cheek once. The rolled-back eyes straightened somewhat, but tears welled up. Kim Ch'ŏmji's eyes seemed to burn hotly too.

Even so, the patient hadn't lost her appetite. For three days, she had been pestering her husband, saying she wanted to drink sŏllŏngt'ang broth.
"That damned wench! A wench who can't even handle millet wants sŏllŏngt'ang? To eat again and have another fit!" he had scolded, yet his heart wasn't easy about not being able to buy it for her.

Now he could buy the sŏllŏngt'ang. He could also buy porridge for Kaettongi (three years old), who whined from hunger beside his sick mother. – Holding the 80 chŏn, Kim Ch'ŏmji's heart felt relieved.

However, his luck didn't end there. It happened as he was wiping the nape of his neck, where sweat and rainwater mixed and flowed, with his muffler, which had become like an oil pouch, and turning away from the school gate. From behind, a voice called, "Rickshaw!" Kim Ch'ŏmji could tell at a glance that the person calling him was a student from that school. The student abruptly asked,
"How much to Namdaemun Station?" Likely, he was someone staying in the school dormitory, intending to <140> return home using the winter vacation. He must have decided to leave today, but with the rain and his luggage, he hadn't known what to do until he spotted Kim Ch'ŏmji and rushed out. Otherwise, why would he chase after Kim Ch'ŏmji, shoes not even properly on, dragging them along, getting soaked in the rain, even if it was just a school uniform?

"To Namdaemun Station, you say?" Kim Ch'ŏmji hesitated for a moment. Was it because he disliked the idea of trudging through the mud to such a distant place in this rain without even a rain cover? Was he satisfied with just the first and second fares? No. Definitely not. Strangely, faced with this run of luck that seemed to bite at its own tail, he felt a little afraid. Also, his wife's plea as he left home weighed on his mind.—When the lady from the house opposite had called for him, the patient, her face nothing but bone, her uniquely large and sunken eyes, like the only living things left, filled with a pleading look, had mumbled like a mosquito's buzz, her breath rattling,
"Don't go out today. Please, for goodness sake, stay home. When I'm this sick..." At that time, Kim Ch'ŏmji, acting nonchalant, had said,
"Sick, damn it all! What utter nonsense. You think someone will feed us if we just sit holding hands?" and as he abruptly tried to leave, the patient stretched out her arm as if to grab him,
"I said don't go... Then come back early," her choked voice followed him.—
The moment he heard the request to go to the station, the convulsively trembling hands, the unusually large eyes, the tearful face of his wife flickered before Kim Ch'ŏmji's eyes.
"Well? How much to Namdaemun Station?" the student asked impatiently, looking at the rickshaw puller's face, muttering to himself as if, "The Inch'ŏn train is at eleven, and the next one is at two, I think."
"One won and fifty chŏn, please." The words tumbled out of Kim Ch'ŏmji's mouth before he knew it. Even as he said it, he was startled by the enormous sum. How long had it been since he had quoted such an amount at once! Just like that, the desire to earn that money overwhelmed his concern for the sick woman. Surely nothing would happen within the day, he thought. Whatever might happen, he couldn't let go of this piece of luck, which was more than double the combined luck of the first and second fares. <141>
"One won and fifty chŏn is a bit too much," the student said, tilting his head.
"No, sir. By distance, it's over ten ri from here. And on a muddy day like this, you should pay a little extra," replied the puller, his face, beaming with a grin, overflowing with unconcealable joy.
"Alright then, I'll pay what you ask, just go quickly." The generous young customer left these words and hurried off to get dressed and gather his luggage.

Once he set off with the student, Kim Ch'ŏmji's legs felt strangely light. It was less like running and more like flying. The wheels also turned so quickly, less like rolling and more like skates gliding over ice. The frozen ground, wet with rain, was slippery too.

After a while, the puller's legs grew heavy. It was because he was nearing his own house. A fresh wave of anxiety pressed down on his chest. "Don't go out today. When I'm this sick..." The words echoed in his ears. And the patient's sunken eyes seemed to glare at him resentfully. Then, he thought he heard the sound of Kaettongi crying, "Wah, wah." He thought he heard the sound of gasping breath too...
"Hey! What's wrong? We'll miss the train!" The passenger's impatient shout barely reached his ears. Startled, Kim Ch'ŏmji realized he was standing stock-still in the middle of the road, holding the rickshaw shafts.
"Yes, yes," Kim Ch'ŏmji started running again. As the house gradually receded, Kim Ch'ŏmji's steps began to regain their vigor. It was as if only by moving his legs briskly could he forget all the worries and anxieties constantly rising in his head.

Having pulled the rickshaw to the station and actually holding the astonishing sum of 1 won and 50 chŏn in his hand, he didn't think about the ten-ri journey through the muddy rain; he just felt grateful, as if he had found it. He was happy, like someone who had suddenly become rich. He bowed several times to the young customer, who was barely older than his own child, respectfully bidding him farewell, "Have a safe trip, sir." <142>

However, the thought of returning with an empty rickshaw rattling along in this rain felt unreal. As the sweat from his labor cooled, and a chill began to seep from his drenched clothes into his starved gut, he keenly felt how uncomfortable and painful the 1 won and 50 chŏn was. As he left the station, his steps lacked all strength. His whole body hunched over, and he felt as if he might collapse right there and be unable to get up.

"Damn it all! Returning with an empty rickshaw rattling in this rain? Curses! Why does this damned rain slap me right in the face!" He cursed furiously, grumbling as if in defiance of someone. Just then, a new light dawned in his mind: the thought, "Instead of just going back like this, if I circle around here waiting for the next train, maybe I'll get another fare." Since his luck was strangely good today, who could guarantee such good fortune wouldn't strike again? He gained enough confidence to bet that luck, wagging its tail, was surely waiting for him. However, fearing the established station rickshaw pullers, he couldn't stand right in front of the station. So, as he had done many times before, he parked his rickshaw a little away from the streetcar stop right in front of the station, in the space between the pedestrian path and the tracks, and decided to wander around nearby, observing the situation.

After some time, the train arrived. Dozens of passengers poured out towards the stop. Among them, Kim Ch'ŏmji's eyes, searching for a fare, spotted the figure of a woman wearing Western hair, high-heeled shoes, and even a mantle (mando), looking like either a retired kisaeng or a dissolute female student. He sidled up to the woman.
"Miss, wouldn't you like a rickshaw?"
The woman, student or whatever she was, put on airs for a moment, lips pressed tightly together, not even glancing at Kim Ch'ŏmji. Kim Ch'ŏmji, like a beggar pleading, kept observing her expression.
"Miss, I'll take you cheaper than the station fellows. Where is your home?" he asked ingratiatingly, reaching <143> for the Japanese-style wicker basket she was holding.
"Why are you bothering me like this?" she snapped sharply and turned away abruptly. Kim Ch'ŏmji stepped back, surprised.

The streetcar arrived. Kim Ch'ŏmji glared resentfully at the departing streetcar. But his premonition was not wrong. As the streetcar, packed tightly with people, began to move, there was one passenger left behind. Seeing the huge bag he was carrying, it seemed the conductor had likely pushed him off the crowded car because his luggage was too big. Kim Ch'ŏmji dashed over.
"Would you like a rickshaw, sir?" After haggling over the price for a while, they agreed on 60 chŏn to take him to Insadong.

As the rickshaw grew heavy, his body strangely lightened. Then, when the rickshaw became light again, his body grew heavy once more, but this time his heart grew anxious too. Scenes from home kept flickering before his eyes, leaving no room to hope for more luck. He had no choice but to keep scolding his legs, which felt like tree stumps and not his own, and run frantically. His gait was so hurried that passersby might worry, "How can that rickshaw puller, drunk like that, manage in this mud?" The overcast, rainy sky was already dim, seeming close to dusk. Only when he reached the front of Ch'anggyŏngwŏn (Ch'anggyŏng Garden/Palace) did he catch his breath, which had reached his chin, and slow his pace. Step by step, as he got closer to home, his heart strangely calmed. However, this calm did not come from relief, but from the fear that the moment was imminent when he would undoubtedly discover the terrible misfortune that had befallen him. He struggled to prolong, even by a little, the time before encountering misfortune. He wanted to hold onto the joy of having earned a near-miraculous amount for as long as possible. He looked around, scanning his surroundings. His appearance was like someone pleading, "My own legs are carrying me towards my home—towards misfortune—and I can't stop them by my own strength, so won't somebody please grab me, please save me?"

Just then, his friend Ch'isam emerged from a roadside tavern. His plump face, ruddy <144> like rouge had been applied, covered with a thick black beard across the chin and cheeks, stood in bizarre contrast to Kim Ch'ŏmji's appearance—a yellowish face, thin and gaunt, furrowed here and there, with a beard only under the chin, looking like an upside-down pine needle cluster.
"Hey, Kim Ch'ŏmji! Looks like you're coming back from inside the gates. Must've earned a lot of money, eh? Let me buy you a drink, quick!" the fat man called out as soon as he saw the skinny one. His voice, unlike his build, was soft and amiable. Kim Ch'ŏmji was incredibly glad to meet this friend. He even felt grateful, as if Ch'isam were a savior who had rescued him.
"Looks like you've already had a drink. Seems you had good luck today too," Kim Ch'ŏmji said, his face brightening into a smile.
"Bah! Do I need good luck to drink? But hey, why are you soaked like a drowned rat? Come on in here and dry off."

The tavern was warm and bustling. Steam billowed up each time the lid was lifted from the pot simmering ch'uhot'ang (loach soup); on the grill sizzled skewered eel, pork, liver, kidney, dried pollack, and pindaettŏk (mung bean pancakes)... the table laden haphazardly with these side dishes. Kim Ch'ŏmji suddenly felt an unbearable craving in his gut. If he could have his way, he wouldn't have felt satisfied even if he had devoured every single edible thing there. But being hungry, he first ordered two large pindaettŏk and a bowl of ch'uhot'ang. Tasting food, his starved intestines felt even emptier, constantly demanding more, more. In an instant, he gulped down the bowl of soup with tofu and loach as if it were water. As he received his third bowl, two full cups of heated makgeolli (rice wine) arrived. Drinking with Ch'isam, his utterly empty stomach tingled as the alcohol spread, and his face flushed warm. He downed another full cup right away. Kim Ch'ŏmji's eyes were already starting to glaze over. Munching on two rice cakes from the grill, his cheeks bulging, he ordered two more full cups.

Ch'isam looked at Kim Ch'ŏmji with surprise. <145>
"Hey, ordering more? We've already had four cups each! That's 40 chŏn!" he cautioned.
"Bah, you brat! Is 40 chŏn such a big deal? I earned a ton of money today! Really, I had great luck today!"
"So how much did you earn?"
"I earned 30 won! Thirty won! Why the hell aren't you pouring the damned drink... It's fine, it's fine! I can drink as much as I want. I earned a mountain of money today!"
"Hey, this guy's drunk. Let's stop."
"You brat, you think I'd get drunk on that? Come on, drink more!" the drunkard grabbed Ch'isam's ear and yelled. Then he lunged at the server pouring the drinks, a boy of about fifteen or sixteen with a shaved head.
"You damned brat! Why aren't you pouring the drink?" he scolded. The boy chuckled and glanced at Ch'isam, signaling as if asking for guidance. When the drunkard noticed this signal, he flew into a rage.
"Call your mothers, you damned bastards! You think I don't have money?" he exclaimed, fumbling at his waist, then pulled out a one-won note and slammed it down in front of the boy. As he did so, a few nickel coins jingled and fell from his clothes.
"Hey, you dropped money! Why are you throwing money around?" Ch'isam said, picking up the coins. Kim Ch'ŏmji, even in his drunken state, tried to locate the money, widening his eyes to look down at the ground, but then, as if suddenly disgusted by his own actions, he jerked his head up and shouted even more angrily,
"Look! Look, you dirty bastards! Do I not have money? You bastards deserve to have your leg bones broken!" He took the money Ch'isam handed him. "This cursed money! This money deserves death!" he yelled, throwing it down forcefully. The money hit the wall, fell back into the cauldron used for heating drinks, and rang out with a 'clang,' as if receiving its just punishment.

Two more full cups were drained before they could even be properly poured. Kim Ch'ŏmji sucked the alcohol clinging to his lips and beard, then stroked his pine-needle beard with great satisfaction. <146>
"Pour more! Pour more!" he yelled.

After drinking another cup, Kim Ch'ŏmji slapped Ch'isam on the shoulder and suddenly burst out laughing. His laughter was so loud that everyone in the tavern turned to look at Kim Ch'ŏmji. The laughing man laughed even harder.
"Hey, Ch'isam! Want to hear a funny story? Didn't I take a fare to the station today?"
"And?"
"Well, it felt wrong to just come back empty, right? So I was hanging around the streetcar stop, trying to figure out how to get another fare. Just then, there was this lady, or maybe a student—who can tell a madam from a young miss these days?—wearing a mantle, standing in the rain. I sidled up close and said, 'Miss, would you like a rickshaw?' and reached for her handbag, but she slapped my hand away, turned around sharply, and said, 'Why are you bothering me like this!' Her voice was really like a nightingale's! Haha!" Kim Ch'ŏmji skillfully imitated a voice truly like a nightingale's. Everyone burst out laughing.
"That damned stingy bitch! Who was doing anything to her? 'Why are you bothering me like this!' Oh my, what a priceless voice! Haha!"
The laughter grew louder. But before the laughter could die down, Kim Ch'ŏmji started sobbing.
Ch'isam stared blankly at the drunkard.
"Just laughing your head off, and now crying? What's this about?"
Kim Ch'ŏmji kept sniffling.
"My wife is dead."
"What? Your wife died? When?"
"You fool, when do you think? Today!"
"Get outta here, you crazy fool. Stop lying." <147>
"Why would I lie? She really died, really... Leaving my wife's corpse stretched out at home while I drink alcohol... I'm a wretch who deserves to die. Deserves to die!" Kim Ch'ŏmji cried, sobbing loudly.
Ch'isam, his good mood somewhat dampened, said, "Is this guy telling the truth or lying? Well then, let's go home, let's go!" and grabbed the crying man's arm to pull him along.
Shaking off Ch'isam's hand, Kim Ch'ŏmji grinned through his tear-filled eyes.
"Who died?" he said triumphantly. "Why would she die? She's alive and kicking! That damned wench is killing her food [idiom: wasting food by not eating]. You fell for it! You fell for it!" he clapped his hands like a child and laughed.
"Is this guy really crazy? I heard your wife was sick..." Ch'isam, feeling a certain unease himself, urged Kim Ch'ŏmji again to go home.
"She's not dead! I told you she's not dead!" Kim Ch'ŏmji yelled furiously, with conviction, but his voice held a tone of someone struggling to believe she wasn't dead. They insisted on finishing a full won's worth, drinking one more full cup each before leaving. The miserable rain still drizzled down steadily.

Kim Ch'ŏmji, though drunk, bought the sŏllŏngt'ang and reached home. Home, of course, meant a rented place, and not even the whole house, but a single servant's room (haengnangbang) separate from the main house, for which he paid 1 won a month plus fetching water. If Kim Ch'ŏmji hadn't been tipsy, his legs would have trembled the moment he stepped inside the gate, faced with the dreadful silence dominating the place—a silence like the sea after a storm has passed. He couldn't hear the sound of coughing. He couldn't even hear the rattling sound of breathing. Only breaking this tomb-like silence—or rather, deepening the silence further and making it more ominous—was a faint sucking sound, "ppak-ppak"—the sound of a baby nursing. If someone with keen hearing were present, they might guess from the "ppak-ppak" sound, which was only sucking, without the "kkul-ttŏk kkul-ttŏk" sound of swallowing milk, that the baby was sucking on an empty breast. <148>

Perhaps Kim Ch'ŏmji also sensed this ominous silence. Otherwise, it was suspicious that, upon entering the gate, he uncharacteristically yelled, "That damned wench! Your husband comes home and you don't even come out to see? That damned wench!" This shouting was surely a bluff, an attempt to chase away the dreadful premonition creeping over him.

In any case, Kim Ch'ŏmji flung open the door to the room. A nauseating stench—the smell of dust rising from under the worn-out straw mat, the smell of feces and urine from unwashed diapers, the smell of clothes ingrained with various kinds of grime, mixed with the smell of the sick person's sweat—assaulted Kim Ch'ŏmji's dulled nose.
Stepping into the room, before he even had a chance to set the sŏllŏngt'ang down in a corner, the drunkard shouted at the top of his lungs.
"That damned wench! Lying around day and night is the best thing, huh? Can't even get up when your husband comes home!" Along with the words, he kicked hard at the legs of the person lying down. But what his foot struck felt not like human flesh, but like a log. At that moment, the "ppak-ppak" sound turned into a whimper, "eung-a." Kaettongi, letting go of the breast he had been sucking, cried. But even his crying involved scrunching up his whole face to make a crying expression; the "eung-a" sound seemed to come not from his mouth, but from deep within his stomach. It seemed his throat was hoarse from crying, and he lacked even the energy to cry anymore.
Seeing that kicking had no effect, the husband rushed to the wife's head, grabbed the patient's hair, truly like a magpie's nest, and shook it.
"Hey wench, speak! Speak! Is your mouth stuck shut, you damned wench!"
"..."
"Huh? Look at this, no response at all."
"..."
"Hey wench, are you saying you're dead? Why aren't you saying anything?"
"..."
"Huh? Still no answer. Maybe she really is dead." <149>
Then, as soon as he recognized the upturned eyes of the lying person, the whites covering the irises,
"These eyes! These eyes! Why can't you look at me straight, why are you looking only at the ceiling? Huh?" His voice choked at the end of his words. Then, tears like chicken droppings fell from the living man's eyes, spotting the stiff face of the dead person. Suddenly, Kim Ch'ŏmji, as if possessed, rubbed his own face against the dead person's face, muttering.
"I brought sŏllŏngt'ang, why can't you eat it? Why can't you eat it... Strangely, my luck was so good today..."
(End)

**Summary**

Kim Ch'ŏmji, a poor rickshaw puller in colonial Seoul, experiences an unusually lucky day, earning a significant amount of money after a long stretch of poverty. He plans to buy sŏllŏngt'ang (ox bone soup) for his severely ill wife, who had begged him not to go out that day due to a premonition. Torn between hope and anxiety, he earns even more money on a long fare to the train station. Instead of going home, he lingers, gets another fare, and then meets a friend, Ch'isam. He gets drunk, celebrating his luck but oscillating wildly between boasting, laughing, and suddenly crying out that his wife is dead, only to deny it immediately after. Finally returning home with the soup, he finds his wife dead, with their baby still trying to nurse from her cold breast. The story ends with his anguished cries, lamenting the cruel irony of his "lucky day." From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the extreme poverty and harsh living conditions of the lower classes in 1920s Seoul under Japanese rule. It highlights the struggle for survival, the rudimentary understanding of illness, fatalism, and the complex emotions (including anger and despair directed at loved ones) born from relentless hardship. The language itself, with its older Korean forms and specific vocabulary related to daily life (foods, currency, transportation), is a valuable linguistic snapshot of the era.

새침하게 흐린 품이 눈이 올 듯하더니 눈은 아니오고 얼다가 만 비가 추적추적 나리는 날이엇다.
이 날이야말로 동소문 안에서 인력거군 노릇을 하는 김 첨지에게는 오래간만에도 닥친 운수 조흔 날이엇다. 문 안에(거긔도 문 밧근 아니지만) 들어간답시는 압집 마마님을 전차길까지 모서다 들인 것을 비롯으로 행여나 손님이 잇슬가 하고 정류장에서 어정어정하며 나리는 사람 하나 하나에게 거의 비는 듯한 눈결을 보내고 잇다가 마츰내 교원인 듯한 양복장이를 동광학교(東光學校)까지 태여다 주기도 되엿다.
천 번에 30전, 둘재 번에 50전- 아츰 댓바람에 그리 흉치 안흔 일이엇다. 그야말로 재수가 옴부터서 근 열흘동안 돈 구경도 못한 김 첨지는 10전 짜리 백동화 서 푼, 또는 다섯 푼이 찰각하고 손바닥에 떨어질 제 거의 눈물을 흘릴 만큼 깃벗섯다. 더구나 이날 이때에 이 80전이란 돈이 그에게 얼마나 유용한지 몰랏다. 컬컬한 목에 모주 한잔도 적실 수 잇거니와 그보담도 알는 안해에게 설렁탕 한 그릇도 사다줄 수 잇슴이다.
그의 안해가 기침으로 쿨룩거리기는 벌서 달포가 넘엇다. 조팜도 굼기를 먹다십히 하는 형편이니 물론 약 한 첩 써본 일이 업다. 구태여 쓰랴면 못 쓸 바도 아니로되 그는 병이란 놈에게 약을 주어 보내면 재미를 부려서 자꾸 온다는 자긔의 신조(信條)에 어대까지 충실하얏다. 딸하서 의사에게＜139＞ 보인 적이 업스니 무슨 병인지는 알 수 업스되 반드시 누어 가지고 닐어나기는 새로도 모로도 못 눕는 걸 보면 중증은 중증인 듯. 병이 이대도록 심해지기는 열흘 전에 조팝을 먹고 체한 때문이다. 그 때도 김 첨지가 오래간만에 돈을 엇어서 좁쌀 한 되와 10전 짜리 나무 한 단을 사다 주엇더니, 김 첨지의 말에 의지하면 그 오라질 년이 천방지축으로 냄비에 대고 끌이엇다. 마음은 급하고 불길은 달지 안하 채 익지도 안흔 것을 그 오라질 년이 숫가락은 고만 두고 손으로 움켜서 두 뺨에 주먹덩이 가튼 혹이 불거지도록 누가 빼앗슬 듯이 처박질드니만 그날 저녁부터 가슴이 땡긴다 배가 궹긴다고 눈을 홉뜨고 질알병을 하얏다. 그 때 김 첨지는 열화와 가티 성을 내며 『에이 오라질 년 조랑복은 할 수가 업서 못 먹어 병, 먹어서 병! 어쩌란 말이야. 웨 눈을 바루 뜨지 못해!』하고 김 첨지는 알는 이의 뺨을 한번 훌여 갈겻다. 홉뜬 눈은 족음 바루어젓건만 이슬이 매치엇다. 김 첨지의 눈시울도 뜨근 뜨근한 듯 하얏다.
이 환자가 그러고도 먹는 데는 물리지 안핫다. 사흘 전부터 설렁탕 국물이 마시고 십다고 남편을 졸랏다.
『이런 오라질 년! 조팝도 못 먹는 년이 설렁탕은. 또 처먹고 질알을 하게』라고 야단을 처보앗건만 못 사주는 마음이 싀언치는 안핫다.
인제 설렁탕을 사줄 수도 잇다. 알는 어미 겨테서 배꼽하 보채는 개똥이(세 살 먹이)에게 죽을 사줄 수도 잇다. - 80전을 손에 쥔 김 첨지의 마음은 푼푼하얏다.
그러나 그의 행운은 그걸로 근치지 안핫다. 땀과 빗물이 석거 흘으는 목덜미를 기름 주머니 다 된 외목수건으로 딱그며 그 학교 문을 돌아나올 때이엇다. 뒤에서 『인력거!』하고 부르는 소리가 난다. 자긔를 불러 멈춘 사람이 그 학교 학생인 줄 김 첨지는 한 번 보고 짐작할 수 잇섯다. 그 학생은 닷자곳자로.
『남대문 정거장까지 얼마요』라고 물엇다. 아마도 그 학교 긔숙사에 잇는 이로 동긔방학을 리용하야＜140＞ 귀향하랴 함이리라. 오늘 가기로 작정은 하얏건만 비는 오고 짐은 잇고해서 어찌할 줄 모르다가 마츰 김 첨지를 보고 뛰어 나왓슴이리라. 그러치 안흐면 웨 구두를 채 신지도 못해서 질질 끄을고 비록 고구라 양복일 망정 노박이로 비를 마즈며 김 첨지를 뒤조차 나왓스랴.
『남대문 정거장까지 말슴입니까』하고 김 첨지는 잠간 주저하얏다. 그는 이 우중에 우장도 업시 그 먼 곳을 철벅어리고 가기가 실혓슴일가? 처음 것 둘재 것으로 고만 만족하얏슴일가? 아니다. 결코 아니다. 이상하게도 꼬리를 맛물고 덤비는 이 행운 압헤 족음 겁이 낫슴이다. 그러고 집을 나올제 안해의 부탁이 마음이 케이엇다.- 압집 마마한테서 불러려 왓슬 제 병인은 그 뼈만 남은 얼굴에 유일의 생물가튼, 유달리 크고 움푹한 눈에 애걸하는 빗을 띄우며.
『오늘은 나가지 말아요. 제발 덕분에 집에 부터 잇서요. 내가 이러케 압흔데..』라고 모긔소리가티 중얼거리고 숨을 걸으렁 걸으렁 하얏다. 그 때에 김 첨지는 대사롭지 안흔 듯이,
『압다 젠장마질 년. 별 빌어먹을 소리를 다 하네. 맛붓들고 안젓스면 누가 먹여 살릴 줄 알아』하고 훌적 뛰어 나오랴니까 환자는 붓잡을 드키 팔을 내저으며,
『나가지 말라도 그래. 그러면 일즉이 들어 와요』하고 목 메인 소리가 뒤를 딸핫다.-
정거장까지 가잔 말을 들은 순간에 경련뎍으로 떠는 손 유달리 큼직한 눈 울 듯한 안해의 얼굴이 김 쳠지의 눈 압헤 어른어른 하얏다.
『그래 남대문 정거장까지 얼마란 말이요』하고 학생은 초조하듯이 인력거군의 얼굴을 바라보며 혼자말 가티 『인천 차가 열 한 점에 잇고 그 다음에는 새로 두 점이든가』라고 중얼거린다.
『1원 50전만 줍지요』 이 말이 저도 모를 사이에 불숙 김 쳠지의 입에서 떨어젓다. 제 입으로 불으고도 스스로 그 엄청난 돈 액수에 놀래엇다. 한꺼번에 이런 금액을 불러라도 본 지가 그 얼마 만인가! 그러자 그 돈 벌 욕긔가 병자에 대한 념려를 살우고 말앗다. 혈마 오늘 내로 어떠랴 십헛다. 무슨 일이 잇드래도 재일 재이의 행운을 깝친 것보탐도 오히려 꼽절이 만흔 이 행운을 노칠 수 업다 하얏다.＜141＞
『1원 50전은 넘우 과한대』 이런 말을 하며 학생은 고개를 기웃하얏다.
『아니올시다. 리수로 치면 여긔서 거긔가 시오리가 넘담니다. 또 이런 진날은 좀 더 주서야지요』 하고 빙글빙글 웃는 차부의 얼굴에는 숨길 수 업는 깃븜이 넘처 흘럿다.
『그러면 달라는 대로 줄 터이니 빨리 가요』 관대한 어린 손님은 이런 말을 남기고 총총히 옷도 입고 짐도 챙기려 제 갈대로 갓다.
그 학생을 태우고 나선 김 쳠지의 다리는 이상하게 겁분하얏다. 다름질을 한다는 이보다 거의 나는 듯 하얏다. 바퀴도 어떠케 속히 도는지 구은다는 이보다 마치 어름을 지처나가는 스케트 모양으로 미끌어저 가는 듯 하얏다. 얼은 땅에 비가 나려 밋그럽기도 하얏지만.
이윽고 끄는 이의 다리는 무거워젓다. 자긔 집 갓가이 다달은 까닭이다. 새삼스럽은 념려가 그의 가슴을 눌럿다. 『오늘은 나가지 말아요. 내가 이러케 압흔데』 이런 말이 잉잉 그의 귀에 울렷다. 그러고 병자의 움쑥 들어간 눈이 원망하는 듯이 자긔를 노리는 듯 하얏다. 그러자 엉엉하고 우는 개똥의 곡성을 들은 듯 십다. 딸국 딸국하고 숨 모으는 소리도 나는 듯 십다...
『웨 이러우. 긔차 노치겟구먼』 하고 탄 이의 초조한 부르지짐이 간신이 그의 귀에 들어왓다. 언뜻 께달으니 김 쳠지는 인력거 채를 쥔 채 길 한복판에 엉거주츰 멈처잇지 안흔가.
『예 예』 하고 김 쳠지는 또 다시 달음질 하얏다. 집이 차차 멀어 갈스록 김 쳠지의 거름에는 다시금 신이 나기 시작하얏다. 다리를 재게 놀려야만 쉴 새 업시 자긔의 머리에 떠오르는 모든 근심과 걱정을 니질 듯이.
정거장까지 끄어다 두고 그 깜작 놀랜 1원 50전을 정말 제 손에 쥠에 제 말 맛다나 십리나 되는 길을 비를 마저가며 질퍽거리고 온 생각은 아니하고 그저나 어든 듯이 고마웟다. 졸부나 된 듯이 깃벗다. 제 자식빨밧게 안되는 어린 손님에게 몃번 허리를 굽히며 『안녕히 단여옵시요』 라고 각듯이 재우첫다.＜142＞
그러나 뷘 인력거를 털털거리며 이 우중에 돌아갈 일이 꿈밧기엇다. 로동으로 하야 흘은 땀이 씩어지자 굼주린 창자에서 물 흘으는 옷에서 어슬어슬 한긔가 쏫아나게 비롯하매 1원 50전이란 돈이 얼마나 괴치 안코 괴로운 것인 줄 절절히 늣기엇다. 정거장을 떠나가는 그의 발길은 힘 하나 업섯다. 왼 몸이 옹송그러지며 당장 그 자리에 업허저 못 닐어날 것 가타얏다.
『젠장마질 것. 이 비를 마지며 뷘 인력거를 털털거리고 돌아를 간담. 이런 빌어먹을. 제 할미를 부를 비가 웨 남의 샹판을 딱딱 딱려!』 그는 몹시 화증을 내며 누구에게 반항이나 하는 듯이 개걸거렷다. 그럴 지음에 그의 머리엔 또 새로운 광명이 비쳣나니 그것은 『이러구 갈 게 아니라 이 근처를 빙빙 돌며 차 오기를 기다리면 또 손님을 태우게 될는지도 몰라』 란 생각이엇다. 오늘은 운수가 괴상하게도 조흐니까 그런 요행이 또 한 번 업스리라고 누가 보증하랴. 꼬리를 굴리는 행운이 꼭 자긔를 기다리고 잇다고 내기를 해도 조흘 만한 미듬을 엇기 되엇다. 그러타고 정거장 인력거군의 등살이 무서우니 정거장 압헤 섯슬 수는 업섯다. 그래 그는 이전에도 여러 번 해 본 일이라 바루 정거장 압 전차 정류장에서 족음 떨어지게 사람 단이는 길과 전차길 틈에 인력거를 세워 노코 찬 자긔는 그 근처를 빙빙 돌며 형세를 관망하기로 하얏다.
얼마 만에 긔차는 왓다. 수십명이나 되는 손이 정류장으로 쏘다저 나왓다. 그 중에서 손님을 물색하는 김 쳠지의 눈엔 양머리에 뒤측 놉흔 구두를 신고 만도까지 둘은 기생 퇴물인 듯 난봉 녀학생인 듯한 녀편네의 모양이 띄이엇다. 그는 실근실근 그 녀자의 겨트로 다가들엇다.
『아씨 인력거 아니 타시랍시요』
그 녀학생인지 만지가 한참은 매우 탯갈을 빼며 입슐을 꼭 담은 채 김 쳠지를 거들떠보지도 안핫다. 김 쳠지는 구걸하는 거지나 무엇가티 연해 연방 그의 긔색을 삷히며
『아씨 정거장 애들보담 아주 싸게 모서다 들이겟습니다. 댁이 어대신가요』 하고 축은 축은하게도 그＜143＞ 녀자의 들고 잇는 일본식 버들고리짝에 제 손을 대엇다.
『웨 이래 남 귀치안케』 소리를 벽역가티 질르고는 획 돌아선다. 김 쳠지는 어랍시요 하고 물러섯다.

『이 눈갈! 이 눈갈! 웨 나를 바루 보지 못하고 청정만 보느냐 응』하는 말 끗헨 목이 메이엇다. 그러자 산 사람의 눈에서 떨어진 닭의 뚱가튼 눈물이 죽은 이의 뻣뻣한 얼굴을 어룽어룽 적시인다. 문득 김 쳠지는 미친 듯이 제 얼굴을 죽은 이의 얼굴에 한태 부비대며 중얼거렷다.
『설령탕을 사다 노핫는데 웨 먹지를 못하늬, 웨 먹지를 못하늬... 괴상하게도 오늘은 운수가 조트니만...
