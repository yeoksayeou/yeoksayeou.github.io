Well-named Puch'ŏn County
						One Special Correspondent

Is it Puch'ŏn (富川) because there are many treasures (寶物), or Puch'ŏn because there is much money (錢)? Having been a visitor to Puch'ŏn once, I (the reporter) find that wherever I go, I inevitably look for strengths and discover weaknesses, or look for weaknesses and discover strengths. Thus, the feeling of mingled joy and sorrow deepened, making me think this must be a special kind of place. Also, it seems too hasty for anyone, anytime, to first point out others' flaws, but as this is an impressionistic essay, what can I do but draw it frankly without any pretense?

There is an old story that in the past, the people of Wei boasted of their treasures, and the people of Qi boasted of their talented people, each highlighting their strengths. However, in this region today, in an area spanning over 34 square ri, apart from 60,000 sŏk of rice, 20,000 sŏk of soybeans, 40,000 sŏk of barley, and fruits from the Tongsang Farm managed by Japanese, there seems to be little else worth mentioning from an industrial perspective. And after glancing at Munhak Mountain, said to have been the capital site of King Piryu of Koguryŏ, Nŭnghŏ Pavilion, which old tales say is a 700-year-old historic site, and Kyeyang Mountain Fortress, where the Japanese general Konishi [Yukanga] held out to the death—sites tracing the county's administrative changes from Koguryŏ's Chubut'o-gun, through Silla's Changje, Koryŏ's Suju, Kyeyang, and Pup'yŏng, to the present Puch'ŏn—there seem to be no more historical sites to speak of. Even the imposing presence of Taebu Garrison, once a breeding ground for swift horses worth a thousand gold pieces, and Yŏngjong Garrison, where the Garrison Commander cum Coastal Defense General and Naval Commander cum Royal Guard Oeyŏng Coastal Defense General once held sway, now drift meaninglessly like the tortoise-shaped island with the ebb and flow of the tide, dreaming of past glory in the mud. Is this all? Among the nearly 70,000 residents, after noting the few figures who appear slightly in historical records—such as the loyalty and righteousness unto death (殺身成仁) of Yi Ye-su, Yang Chi, and Yi Che-ga during the Imjin War, the filial piety of Yi Tong-jin and Yi Ŭng-sin, and the chastity of Songbi Yŏngnyŏl—there seem to be no other figures, past or present, rich or noble, worthy of being local historical material. It appears they have only been the ruled class and have fallen behind in promoting modern culture. Seeing this, one wonders if the saying "darkness under the lamp" refers to this region within the capital area.

However, are there only such shortcomings? It is true that the high and dry southern zone, unsuitable for farming, and especially the coastal isthmuses, present some difficulties in transportation and administration. But on the other hand, there is the vast Pup'yŏng Plain with its large farms in the northeastern zone, the Kyŏngin Railway line passing through the center, and the good port of Inch'ŏn facilitating internal and external traffic. All these not only make this region a strategic point for naval and land battles but, moreover, rely on the efforts of fishermen active in the sea surface spanning over 122 ri,<122> dotted with nearly 300 large and small islands like stars. The annual yield of over 62,000 kŭn of fish, the livestock throughout the county averaging one head per two ch'ŏk [unit of land area?], with over 5,140 cattle, over 4,360 pigs, and no fewer than 53,000 chickens annually—these practical benefits from animal husbandry, progressing rapidly with the best results in the province, how much richer will they make the homes of the local residents? And consequently, won't this become a point of pride for the people of Puch'ŏn!

Pumin-hoe and Sinnong-dan

Just looking at the line from Yi Kyu-bo's poem, "Though the people are simple and affairs uncomplicated, which is commendable, the barren land is unbearable to see," one can sufficiently grasp what kind of place Puch'ŏn is. Perhaps because of this, although the general economic power of the residents seems quite inferior compared to other counties, the crime rate is not that high, and there seem to be no disputes between officials and the populace. Should this be called the extremity of weakness? Or perhaps natural selection specific to the region?

Currently, the county authorities are improving the land, aiming for the people of this region to engage in industry and prosper like others. Whether there is a realistic prospect of achieving results in practical utilization and public welfare (利用厚生) remains unknown, but in any case, under government guidance, the establishment of the so-called Industrial Promotion Committee system and the emergence of new organizations with fine names like Pumin-hoe (富民會 - Association for Enriching the People) and Sinnong-dan (新農團 - New Farmers' Group) can indeed be called special administration for this county. First, regardless of the scale of the work, and whether initiated by the government or spontaneously by the people, if only the land—surrounded by water on three sides, remote, desolate, low-lying, and damp—can be miraculously transformed into a source of mountain delicacies and sea treasures, bringing welfare to the general populace, what hesitation or scruples should there be? However, given the fundamentally inverted nature of administration where the guest takes precedence over the host, will these groups, the Sinnong-dan and the Pumin-hoe, maintain their consistency in name and reality throughout their future path? Let's see in the next installment! For now, the implementation points of the Industrial Promotion Committee and the objectives and implementation points of the two aforementioned organizations are said to be as follows.

I. Implementation Points of the Industrial Promotion Committee
A. Ensure that each farming household plants at least 100 mulberry saplings around their house.
B. Implement the installation of rectangular seedbeds (苗代) and ensure thorough weeding (稗拔).
C. Ensure that each farming household raises at least 1 pig and 3 chickens.
D. Thoroughly encourage afforestation and eradicate pine caterpillars (松虫).

II. Pumin-hoe Projects
A. Objectives: Harmony and cooperation in the community, promotion of good customs and morals, cultivation of a thrifty and productive populace, and demonstration of results in general local improvement.
B. Implementation Points:
1. Abolish between-meal snacks during the farming season and limit meals to three per day.
2. Train sericulture apprentices.<123>
3. Establish communal mulberry fields.
4. Make efforts in tree planting.
5. Plant lotus roots in ponds and marshes.
6. Encourage night schools for both sexes.
7. Ensure strict adherence to government orders.
8. Encourage straw handicrafts as a side business during the agricultural off-season. However, construct several dugout shelters (穴屋) on the day of 立冬 (onset of winter).
9. Reduce extravagant expenses for coming-of-age ceremonies, weddings, funerals, and ancestral rites.
10. Abolish superstitious practices.

III. Sinnong-dan Projects
A. Objective: To thoroughly implement recommended practices in each myeon and ri based on the goals of agricultural improvement and promotion of side businesses.
B. Implementation Points:
1. Elimination of red rice (赤米). 2. Installation of rectangular seedbeds. 3. Production of compost (堆肥). 4. Implementation of weeding. 5. Drying and preparation of unhulled rice (籾). 6. Promotion of sericulture. 7. Promotion of animal husbandry. 8. Forest cultivation (養林). 9. Industrial lectures (産業講話). 10. Thrift and savings. 11. Awarding of excellence flags to outstanding groups (優良團).

Industrial Son? County Magistrate Kang!

In Kangwŏn and South Hamgyŏng provinces, if you ask who the practical governor of Chosŏn is, people invariably point to Mr. Yi Kyu-wan. That gentleman, whose monuments praising his good governance (善政碑) stood thickly in the alleys of Yangju-mok (though its name can't even be asked after its merger due to years) receiving unusual praise, has now become the administrator of Puch'ŏn, still embodying his administrative skills for industrial nation-building; this doesn't seem particularly new. Since his appointment, he has unusually organized new institutions like the Pumin-hoe and Sinnong-dan, crying out for rural improvement. Is this the manifestation of his sincerity, a customary skill from his time as the second magistrate of Yangju-mok? Or is it practical action devoid of empty talk? Wherever I (the reporter) face him, my inability to offer Sunu Kon's prayer is truly due to the impartial limitations of page space, what can be done... And among his characteristic popular lectures: "In my view, whether Japanese or Korean, they are the same human race, yet even the lowest class Japanese eat stone-filtered rice, while the number of Koreans, even those dressed in splendid Western or Japanese clothes, who cannot even eat bad rice, is increasing. What kind of situation is this?" Also, "The education of the people is like the cultivation of land. The increase of cultivated land in the country is because there are many educated people. Conversely, the abundance of uncultivated land is because there are many unenlightened people. Therefore, I (Mr. Kang) educate the people to cultivate the uncultivated land, and cultivate the uncultivated land to educate the unenlightened people." Furthermore, "To achieve results in the policy of increasing rice production, one must strictly prohibit the use of the traditional Korean rice-washing basin (이남박) as the vanguard for eliminating inferior rice." That he delivers these lengthy, eloquent speeches everywhere, proclaiming them as the new education for the industrial development of Koreans, appropriate for the current times and conditions, breathing fire whenever he meets people... no matter how I think about it, it seems to suggest... no, reveal... something slightly different from the mediocre official who bows his head for a mere measure of grain... Ah, when will the day of erecting monuments and inscriptions (勒碑刻銘) come in this county?

Foreign Influence Distributed Within the County

The population of surplus production [referring to Japanese settlers] overflows in this county too, with 297 households and 1,062 individuals. The cultivated land they already occupy exceeds 1,069 chō (町步) of paddy fields, 91 chō of dry fields, 54<124> chō of housing sites, and 490 chō of ponds, marshes, miscellaneous lands, and others. Among the 14 individuals occupying more than 1 chō of fruit orchards, 12 are them [Japanese], and their annual income is said to be no less than 230,000 yen. Even within the beneficiary area of the Pup'yŏng Irrigation Association (富平水利組合), the land occupied by residents of Puch'ŏn [presumably Japanese] amounts to over 600 chō. The profit from fishing, an annual 50,000 yen solely for them from the sea surrounding three sides, also seems significant. Additionally, among the 126 Chinese households with 680 individuals, nearly 100 are vegetable merchants whose annual income of 50,000 yen cannot be lightly dismissed. Not only this, but the force of the laborers, numbering about 70 at the Chu** fields [Hanja unclear, likely 朱安 Chuam?] and over 1,200 at the Pup'yŏng Irrigation Association—what is the power of their movement? Ah, how sorrowful! Wherever one goes, relentlessly pushed by the Japanese (쪽발), or sporadically attacked by the ignorantly charging barbarians [likely referring to Chinese], unable to survive, attacked from front and rear (腹背受敵) like adding insult to injury—what will become of the conscripted laborers (役軍) of Paedal [Korea] in the future?

Unstoppable Public Resentment

Puch'ŏn is Pinch'ŏn (貧川 - Poor Stream/County). Looking across the nearly 13,000 households, it is said there isn't a single wealthy person with assets exceeding 1,200 sŏk. On top of that, residents near Pup'yŏng, frightened by the Land Expropriation Ordinance, were forced to sell off their fertile lands, carefully prepared even in small corners, to the Irrigation Association at giveaway prices (歇價放賣). We don't know how much the benefits of irrigation will return to us tomorrow, but how can we bear the immediate heartache? And the residents of the islands had the rights to the salt production (鹽業), which had been their livelihood passed down through generations, suddenly transferred to the Government-General Monopoly Bureau (總督府 專賣局). The remaining scraps are constantly overwhelmed by the unlimited import of Chinese salt (胡鹽), seemingly robbing them even of their means of subsistence (糊口). Furthermore, although there are said to be six ordinary schools (普通學校) throughout the 15 myeon of the county, including the one currently under construction in Kyeyang, looking at the total number of children in the county, only about 3,000 are enrolled. The remaining 7,000 or so wander aimlessly in darkness (昏衢) without any place to learn—truly pitiful children. Despite this, on the opposite side, there is the Sosa Public Common School (素砂公立尋常小學校), accommodating merely 50 students after meticulously collecting fees as soon as their heads dry [i.e., from a young age], and additionally, the Namch'on Public Common School (南村公立尋常小學校), established with substantial subsidies for fewer than 8 or 9 children, shouting out quite vigorously. Humanity is the same, yet actions differ. While claiming impartiality (一視同仁), one side is a source of laughter, yet why is the other side a world of tears? Is it because they don't know that the so-called cultural rule (文化政治) is already understood to be anti-Korean good governance (排鮮의 善政)? <125>

**Summary**

This 1924 article by a special correspondent paints a critical picture of Puch'ŏn County. While acknowledging its agricultural potential (rice, beans, barley, livestock) and strategic location (Kyŏngin Railway, Inch'ŏn port), the author laments its lack of significant historical sites or prominent figures, portraying it as underdeveloped ("darkness under the lamp"). The correspondent highlights new government-led initiatives like the Pumin-hoe and Sinnong-dan, spearheaded by County Magistrate Kang, aimed at agricultural improvement and moral cultivation, questioning their ultimate effectiveness and sincerity. The article strongly criticizes the growing economic dominance of Japanese settlers in land ownership, orchards, irrigation projects, and fishing, alongside Chinese merchants, expressing sorrow over the displacement and hardship faced by Koreans ("attacked from front and rear"). Public resentment is fueled by forced land sales, loss of salt production rights, and stark disparities in educational opportunities between Korean children (most unenrolled) and the well-funded schools for Japanese children, exposing the hypocrisy of the colonial "cultural rule."

For historians, this article offers a valuable ground-level perspective on rural colonial Korea in the 1920s. It details specific economic conditions, colonial development projects (like the Pup'yŏng Irrigation Association), local administrative efforts (Pumin-hoe, Sinnong-dan under Magistrate Kang), the tangible impact of Japanese economic penetration on land and resources, and the deep-seated Korean resentment towards discriminatory colonial policies, particularly in education and resource control (salt monopoly, land expropriation). The critique of Magistrate Kang's "industrial" focus alongside the stark reality of Korean impoverishment provides insight into the complexities and contradictions of colonial development rhetoric versus lived experience.

일홈 조흔 富川郡
						一特派員
무이 만어 富川이냐? 돈이 만어 富川이냐? 一次 富川의 客이 되야본 나(기자)는 지나는 곳이면 반다시 長處를 찻다가 短處를 발견. 短處를 찻다가 長處도 발견. 이러케 悲喜交至의 늣김이 한 겹 더하야 짐은 지방 중이 지방이 안일가 하얏다. 그리고 언제나 누구나 할 것 업시 먼저 남의 결함부터 說破하는 것은 너무나 輕燥한 듯도 하다 만은 隨感隨筆이라 아모 假借업는 솔직 그대로 그리는 데야 엇지 하랴.
往昔에 魏人은 보물 자랑, 齊人은 인물 자랑으로 각자의 長處를 들어 내엇다는 古談도 잇다 만은 지금에 이 지방하야서는 34方里가 넘는 地面에 6만石의 米作, 2만 石의 大豆, 4만石의 麥作과 日人이 경영하는 東山農園産의 果物을 除하고 나면 産業觀으로는 이밧게 별로 이 數에 칠 것이 업서 뵈인다. 그리고 高句麗의 主夫吐郡 시대로부터 新羅의 長堤, 高麗의 樹州, 桂陽, 富平, 지금의 富川 이러케 郡治를 변혁하는 동안에 高句麗王 沸流의 定都地이엇다는 文鶴山, 7백년 古蹟이라고 古老相傳하는 凌虛臺, 倭將 小西의 死守하든 桂陽山城를 一瞥하고 나면 다시는 古蹟 거리도 업서 뵈이고 千金駿驄의 飼養所이든 大阜鎭과 鎭撫營兼海防將, 水軍僉節制使兼親軍沁營外使海防將의 威風이 肅肅하든 永宗鎭의 龜伏形도 이제는 아모 용기 업시 自去自來하는 潮水에 나붓기어 昔日의 번영을 淤泥 중에서 꿈꾸일 뿐이다. 엇지 이 뿐이랴. 7만에 近한 주민 중에는 史面에 조금 나타나 보인 壬亂時 人 李禮壽, 梁誌, 李悌可의 殺身成仁의 忠義와 李東振 李應新의 孝行 及 松婢永烈의 貞烈 등 數三을 摘記하고 나면 다시는 古今을 통하야 富로나 貴로나 할 것 업시 그나마 鄕土史料거리가 될 만한 인물이 업시 被支配階級뿐으로서 현대 문화 촉진의 落伍가 되고 말은 모양이라 한다. 이로써 보면 아마도 燈下不明이라 함이 近畿 중 이 지방을 두고 일카름이 안인가 한다.
그러나 엇지 이따위의 短處뿐이랴. 農作에 不適한 南部地帶의 高燥는 물론 海島地峽에 限하야 교통상 기타 행정상에 다소 불편이 잇는 것도 사실이다 만은 그 반면에는 東北地帶에 廣濶한 富平平野의 大農場, 중앙을 관통하는 京仁鐵道, 內外交通의 便을 주는 仁川의 良港 이것이 모다 이 지방에 水戰陸戰의 要衝이 될 뿐이 아니라 더구나 122浬나＜122＞ 넘는 海面에 星羅棊布한 大小 3백에 近한 群島에 의지하야 활동하는 水産者의 노력으로 收益되는 年 62,000여斤의 魚物과 全郡을 통하야 2尺 1頭에 當하는 年 5,140여頭의 畜牛, 年 4,360여頭의 養豚, 年 53,000首에 不下하는 養鷄 이러케 一道 중 가장 우월한 성적으로 長足進步하는 畜産의 實益은 이것이야말로 이 지방 주민의 집을 얼마나 富潤케 하며 따라서 이것으로 하야 富川人의 자랑거리가 될 것이 안일가 한다!

富民會와 新農團
李奎報 詩에 「人淳事簡雖堪喜地瘠地殘不忍看」이라고 한 이 一節만 보아도 富川이란 엇더한 지방임을 넉넉이 알 수가 잇다. 그리하야 그러함인지 當地로서는 居民의 일반 경제력은 他郡에 비하야 아조 低劣하야 보이면서도 범죄건수도 그리 만치 안코 따라서 官民間에도 아모 係爭이 업시 지나는 것을 보면 이것을 가르쳐 곳 弱者의 極이라 할가? 아니 지방적 자연汰라 할가?
現下 郡當局은 토지를 개량하야 이 지방 백성도 殖産興業 남과 가티 살어보자. 과연 利用厚生의 실적을 들어 내일 만한 成算이 잇는지는 모르겟다 마는 엇잿던지 관청 지도 下에서 所謂 産業獎勵委員制의 신설이며 富民會와 新農團이란 일홈 조흔 新團軆가 생기어 낫슴은 그야말로 이 고을의 특수행정이라고 할만하다. 먼저 일의 大小를 말할 것 업시 또한 官家의 挑發이거나 民間의 自發임을 不拘하고 三面皆水幽荒卑濕之地로 하야금 飜然山珍海錯化하야 일반민중에게 福利만 된다 하면 무엇을 주저하며 忌憚하랴만은 원악 주객전도의 施政이라 新農이면 新農대로 富民이면 富民대로의 영원한 압길에 始終一貫 名實이 俱全하야질는지? 第看下回! 이제 産業獎勵委員의 실행사항과 以上 兩箇 단체의 目的 及 實行事項은 이럿타 한다.
