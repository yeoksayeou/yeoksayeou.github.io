Magazine Title: 개벽 제48호
Publication Date: 1924년 06월 01일
Article Title: 醜로 본 京城·美로 본 京城 (Kyŏngsŏng Seen as Ugly, Kyŏngsŏng Seen as Beautiful)
Author: 네눈이
Article Type: 세태비평 (Critique of the Times/Social Commentary)

Kyŏngsŏng Seen as Ugly, Kyŏngsŏng Seen as Beautiful
						Nenuni

Since there's nothing to eat, let's have a feast of curses instead. But you deluded human fellows, know that I'm not deliberately finding fault just to be cursed. In truth, no matter how favorably I try to look at your place called Kyŏngsŏng, I see nothing noteworthy but ugliness and things to dislike. That's all I see. There's nothing to do but curse it all. It might be unpleasant to hear, but what can be done when it's the truth? Even so, you deluded fellows, don't beat me to death out of baseless ill-feeling. Now, let me ask you one thing first.

I hear that the capital of Britain is like an old venerable man. I hear the capital of Germany is like a man in his prime. And I hear the capital of France is like a beautiful lady. But what is your capital of Chosŏn like?

Why can't you answer? If you're struggling for an answer, I'll give it, don't be angry. Kyŏngsŏng is like a bushy-haired servant boy. What do you think? Of Nenuni's observation? If it's uncomfortable to say it's exactly right, at least say it's similar.

Setting aside the Japanese fellows' streets in Namch'on for later, let's first look at the Chosŏn fellows' streets in Pukch'on. First, let's go out onto Chongno Avenue. Ah, how truly ugly it is!

Is it a costume parade? Is it the chaos of strange forms (獨脚의 作亂)?
Tsk, Nenuni's eyes are nowhere near enough. Not four hundred eyes, but a fellow with at least four thousand eyes might be able to see it all. Ah, truly regrettable. Could the central grand avenue of the 500-year capital of a 5,000-year historical nation be this ugly? <116> Since they are fellows whose civilization has fallen behind, one might say it's no wonder, but still, how can it be *this* ugly? Some fellow might pick up a stone and throw it, saying, "What's ugly? You damned Nenuni," but what can I do when my eyes cross and my mouth twists [at the sight]? The ugliness is certainly not the road. From Sŏdaemun Avenue to Tongdaemun Avenue—a straight, wide road—it truly seems it wouldn't compare unfavorably to any city. The crux of the ugliness is the human fellows. It's the things these human fellows do. Now, let's see. The myriad forms (千態萬象) of the human fellows—how ugly they are. Is it a costume parade or goblin mischief (獨脚이 作亂)? The lack of uniformity in houses, the lack of uniformity in clothes, the lack of uniformity in head and foot wear, the lack of uniformity in walking, the lack of uniformity in language, the lack of uniformity in social interaction—indeed, what is it if not wildly varied (形形色色)? In your society today, where class distinctions between rich and poor persist and differences in power remain, one cannot recklessly criticize the surface of Kyŏngsŏng, but still, can it really be that ugly? Setting aside the ugliness of the myriad housing types like brick houses, tile-roofed houses, tin-roofed houses, thatched houses, etc., there are fellows in Western clothes, fellows in Japanese clothes, fellows in Korean clothes. Among Korean clothes, there are fellows in white, black, grey, yellow, red, pink, jade green, green, purple, *maroon*, brown, etc.—all 7 colors multiplied to 14 colors, all sorts of peculiar individual (獨脚) clothes come rushing out. Fellows wearing hats, fellows wearing *kat* (traditional horsehair hats), fellows wearing towels, fellows wearing official hats (*kamtu*), bare-headed top-knotted men, women with braided hair hanging down, women with chignons, women with hair up, women with hair down, etc.—the head styles are also myriad layers. Fellows wearing leather shoes, fellows wearing *geta*, Korean leather shoes (朝鮮皮鞋), Korean straw shoes (朝鮮芍�鞋), Korean hemp sandals (朝鮮메투리), also rubber shoes, wooden clogs (*namaksin*), etc.—even footwear alone is different for each person. Fellows going forward, fellows going sideways, fellows stooping, fellows strutting, fellows sitting down, fellows crawling, fellows yawning (하피염) as they go, fellows smoking tobacco, fellows spitting, even fellows urinating as they go—where can this ugliness be hidden? And Japanese language, Western languages, Chinese language, Korean language—among Korean, the dialects of the three southern provinces (三南), the northwest (西北), Kyŏngsŏng—all sorts of dialects burst forth. Looking just at the greetings of people meeting: fellows taking off hats, fellows shaking hands, fellows just nodding their heads, fellows bowing deeply, fellows just touching their hats, fellows just smirking, fellows just glancing sideways—wow, truly ten thousand people, ten thousand manners (萬人萬態)! Is it a costume parade? Is it the play of goblins (獨脚)? And why are there so many disabled people (病身)? Blind people, one-eyed people, deaf-mutes, harelips, hunchbacks, cripples unable to stand, people with lumps, one-armed people, lame people—yah, it really is a disgustingly disabled nation (病身國)! Truly pitiful. That must be why there are so many beggars. <117>

Of course, since it's a city, there are foreign fellows, Seoul fellows, country fellows, and also wealthy folks showing off their appearance, and poor folks dressed however they can manage, so in a way, it might not seem particularly strange. However, could it really be such a case of ten thousand people, ten thousand forms? Look at the Japanese fellows, look at the Chinese fellows, look at the Western fellows. Do they wear such motley clothes (形形色色) as the Chosŏn fellows? Are their head styles and heel styles so non-uniform? Are their ways of speaking and walking so varied like ten thousand people, ten thousand forms?
Seeing this, I can well gauge your level. I can well gauge the level of the Chosŏn fellows. If even something like clothing cannot be unified, what's the point of asking about the realm of thought?

Human fellows, let me show you a model, take a look at rat society. Are they not unified from head to tail tip in pure grey? When they move, their bodies touch lightly, and when they stand, don't they stand nimbly, as if dancing? They aren't sluggish like human fellows, yawning as they come and go. Human fellows, you know this is absolutely not a lie, right?
Ah, I hate to look long at the ugly sight of human fellows on Chongno Avenue. Let's duck into an alley.

Alley behind the Servants' Quarters (行廊 뒷골목)
This is the back alley behind the servants' quarters. Fat fellows go sideways, tall fellows stoop. The greasy female servant (행랑게집), the *kisaeng*'s father (기생아비) with a shiny, greasy face, the painted-face whore (갈보년), the coughing opium addict (아편쟁이), the water-carrier, the taffy seller, the cabbage seller, the water dropwort (메나리) seller—all these tangle together like scum in the July monsoon rains. Eugh, how filthy! It seems the children of human fellows can't even defecate or urinate in a designated place. On this narrow, narrow path, all that's seen and stepped on is nothing but feces. What are they, if not worse than dogs? Dogs, at least, don't defecate or urinate on the street. Eek, what's this rice now? You fellows who deserve to starve to death! Why did you throw away so much rice? You men and women whom heaven should strike with lightning! Why throw precious rice onto the street? This must surely be the doing of shamanistic fortune-teller (무당 복술) types. Tch, the things these damned men and women do are endless.
Eek, what thunderbolt is this now? Aiyee, disgusting, it's a urine downpour (오줌벼락)! You dog-like men and women! Where are you throwing your urine? Opening the small window and pouring it onto the street—you filthy women! Are you human? Or dogs? I nearly met disaster from that urine downpour. <118> Bah, there's nothing for it but to go straight home. I must go home before getting hit by another thunderbolt. Even sitting at home, I can tell you all about the ugliness of you fellows. Having spent at least 10 or 20 years in your place called Seoul, I've seen all the filth and ugliness there is to see. Don't feel relieved just because I'm going home. You filthy Seoul fellows... Right, I'm home. Listen up, as I reveal the ugliness of Seoul.

Seoul's Ten Great Uglinesses (十大醜)
Just now I nearly got hit by a urine downpour, but the filthiest thing among you fellows' dirty habits is carelessly discarding feces and urine. Feces on the street might be the act of children who don't know better, so perhaps it's no wonder. But no, that too is the responsibility of the adults, so the cap of 'ugliness' must be worn by the adults. Setting that aside, keeping chamber pots with urine and feces in the room, emptying them while sleeping, sitting and emptying them "jjeoreureu," eating and emptying them "sururu"—men, women, children alike taking turns, not even disposing of it in the latrine or urine jar, but opening a small window and pouring the filthy stuff out "jjureureu" in full view of everyone (萬人所視)—where else are there such barbaric bastards? That's why the rooms are made into breathing chambers of ammonia, and the streets are made into filthy muddy ditches (泥溝), right? And then they do laundry and wash vegetables to eat in that, right? Eugh, disgusting! Where else is there such behavior, worse than dogs? The merchants on the streets are likewise. They put out a few scraps of leftover goods someone else didn't sell, sit there yawning, eyeing the pockets of passersby, and then, as if it's part of their job, they routinely pick up a canned goods tin (간즈메통) or perhaps a tin washbasin, squat down, and urinating is their specialty. After urinating, they keep it beside them and, waiting for a moment when the police aren't passing, discard it onto the street in full view of everyone. Ah, filthy bastards—this is the ugliness that represents the ugliness of Seoul fellows.
Second is, as mentioned before, throwing food waste onto the street.
Third is the sight of the *kisaeng*'s father (기생아비) or the pimp husband (蝪甫서방), living off selling the genitals of his daughter or wife, with that greasy sheen on his face. Fourth is the bedbug blood, cobwebs, and dust piles in the servants' quarters (행낭방), and the lazy appearance of the mothers and fathers stirring within. Fifth is, come summer, the roadside sleeping and eating (路傍寢食) of the poor laborers and servants' quarters people, especially the women lying haphazardly horizontally and vertically with their thighs exposed and chests <119> bared, snoring loudly, completely unaware if a venomous snake is crawling into their lower parts or an earthworm is entering their throat.
Sixth are the proprietors and servants, and also the flies, of red bean porridge shops, beef soup (설넝탕) shops, standing bars (선술집), and taverns serving men and women (내외주점).
Seventh are those male bastards who, amidst the dancing shaman women (무당 년들) at the Association for Worshipping Spirits (崇神人組合) or whatever it's called, squat like old male dogs (雄犬), secretly staring at something and swallowing their saliva.
Eighth is women plastering too much powder on their homely faces. Ninth is the garish display of those coffin-like things (獨脚이 잡은 것 가튼 것) at the so-called funeral parlors (葬儀社) on Chongno street, left wide open. And finally, the most disgusting thing is the sight of those fellows with so-called money, power, or face, who keep many wives and concubines, keep many female attendants (줄행랑), keep many maids (食母) and seamstresses (針母)—lying comfortably amidst them, rolling around like Western pigs (洋猪), only burping up rotten egg smells—that sight seems the filthiest among the filth. Also disgusting is the sight of the so-called genealogists (譜學者類) rummaging through rotten bones. Seeing doorplates on every alley for some Clan Genealogy Office (氏譜所) or Clan Association (氏大同) makes me want to vomit (生嘔逆). Besides these, there's no limit if I were to list more. Since it's fundamentally an underdeveloped place, roads, hygiene, and all other customs and manners remain as they are, so everything has yet to escape its ugly state.

The Beauty of Scenery, the Beauty of Students
Then, is there only ugliness in Kyŏngsŏng? Is there not even a little bit of beauty? There isn't. Beauty doesn't exist. If forced to say there is, there are only two kinds. Only two kinds.
One is the beauty of the mountains and water (山水). The other is the beauty of the students. Among student beauty, the beauty of female students, especially, covers up all the ugliness of Kyŏngsŏng and is the beauty that represents all the beauty of Kyŏngsŏng.
The beauty of scenery—some fellows might criticize Kyŏngsŏng for being mountainous or narrow. It's not that it isn't true. But just because it's mountainous and narrow, could there be no beauty? Beauty exists everywhere. Beauty exists in Kyŏngsŏng too. And there's much beauty. It can be discovered on Mt. Samgak (三角山), Mt. Pugak (北岳山), Mt. Nam (南山), and the West Mountain (西山). It can be discovered in the forests of Ch'ŏngnyangni (淸凉里), the green waves of the Han River (漢江), <120> the orchards of Cham Island (잠島), the fishing grounds of Map'o (麻布), the water and rocks of Samch'ŏng (三淸), the pines and cypresses of Changch'ung (獎忠)—beauty can be discovered countless times in all those places.
Truly, the mountains and rivers of Kyŏngsŏng have escaped ugliness. Because the geology is white sand and blue stone, everywhere is clear and clean like a mirror. It's regrettable that there's no artificial enhancement, but naturally, it possesses unparalleled beauty. Under the name Nenuni, I haven't studied literature like human fellows, so I can't write poetry, but anyway, beauty is everywhere.
Setting that aside, the beauty of the students—this is truly a living beauty (活美), a dynamic beauty (動美) beyond the beauty of scenery. It is the beauty of life (生), the beauty of eternity (永遠).
When the morning sun rises red in the eastern sky, the beauty of the tens of thousands of youthful male and female students with ruddy faces, vigorously striding back and forth on the avenues of the capital (長安), carrying their book bags and wearing school uniforms—this truly is the beauty that represents all of Kyŏngsŏng's beauty, nay, human beauty.
I won't go into detail. If I describe the beauty of male and female students in detail, I might instead be criticized as the filthy Nenuni, the messy Nenuni. It's better to leave the beauty as it is and just catch its scent. There's no need to expose it.
Is there only these two kinds of beauty in Kyŏngsŏng? There aren't any more. No matter how hard I look, there are none. Sadly, none. If I were forced to mention one more—perhaps the rear view (뒷 태도) of Kyŏngsŏng women? That might seem plausible, but there's no need to specifically mention it. If I were to dig into the Japanese fellows' side in Namch'on, I could find both ugliness and beauty, but I don't want to uncover that. Somehow, I dislike doing so. Perhaps this is also due to a lack of connection. I'll leave that to a Japanese Nenuni.
Now—look at Kyŏngsŏng's ugly and beautiful sides as seen by Nenuni, but don't beat me to death. I reveal this much out of concern and affection for you. -The end- <121>

**Summary**

The author, "Nenuni," delivers a scathing critique of Kyŏngsŏng (colonial Seoul) in 1924, deeming it overwhelmingly ugly. The ugliness stems from the perceived lack of unity and modernity among its Korean inhabitants (specifically in Pukch'on): chaotic variety in dress, housing, behavior, dialects, and public hygiene (especially urination/defecation habits and littering). The author contrasts this unfavorably with idealized foreign capitals and even rats, listing "Ten Great Uglinesses" including poverty, laziness, exploitation (pimps), poor sanitation, unpleasant street scenes, and the arrogance of the elite. The only redeeming beauties found are the natural scenery (mountains, river) and the "living beauty" of the uniformed students, particularly female students, symbolizing hope. The author deliberately avoids discussing Namch'on, the Japanese district.

For historians, this article is highly revealing of Korean self-perception and social conditions under Japanese rule. The intense self-criticism reflects anxieties about perceived backwardness and a longing for modernity and national unity, projected onto the lack of uniformity in everyday life. The stark contrast drawn between the "ugly" Korean Pukch'on and the implicitly (or explicitly avoided) "orderly" Japanese Namch'on highlights colonial power dynamics and internalized hierarchies. The praise for students as the sole human "beauty" underscores the era's hope invested in the educated youth for national revitalization. The visceral descriptions of sanitation issues offer insight into urban public health challenges of the time.

醜로 본 京城·美로 본 京城
						네눈이
먹을 것이 업스니 욕이라도 한바탕 또 먹어보자. 그러나 昧惑한 사람 놈들아 욕먹기 위하야 일부러 生트집만 잡는 것은 안인 줄은 알아다고. 사실상 京城이란 너의 곳은 아무리 눈을 곱게 뜨고 보아도 추잡한 것 미운 것밧게는 이러타 할 무엇이 안이 보이드란 말이다. 都트러 욕밧게 할 것이 업드란 말이다. 듯기에 좀-거북하리라 만은 사실인 바에야 엇지 하늬?. 그럿타고 요-昧惑한 놈들아 空然한 惡感으로 打撲殺은 말어다고. 자-爲先 한 마듸 무러보자.
드르닛가 英國 서울은 老成人 갓다드라. 獨逸 서울은 壯年 갓다드라. 또 佛蘭西 서울은 美婦人 갓다드라. 그런데 너의 朝鮮 서울은 무엇 가트냐?.
왜 대답을 못 하느냐?. 대답이 窮하면 내가 할게 怒여워 말아라. 京城은 더벙머리 머슴군 갓드란 말이다. 엇더냐? 네눈이의 관찰이-. 꼭 그럿타고 하기가 거북하면 비슷하다고 라도 해라.
