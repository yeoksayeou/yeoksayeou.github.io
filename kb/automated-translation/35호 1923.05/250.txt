荒野의 나그네 (Traveler in the Wilderness)
						閔牛步 (Min Ubo)

It was already four years ago. Together with Mr. An Hongsŏk, I had newly crossed over to Tokyo and rented a house in the village of Yoyogi. Having just recovered from a serious illness, rest was more urgent for me than study, and An Hongsŏk also had a disposition that most enjoyed spending time in quiet nature, immersed in contemplation, so we first sought a tranquil place and went outside the city.

This place was truly tranquil. In front, across the rice paddies and stream, stood an old temple and a shrine on a small hill, and within the shrine grounds, thousand-year-old evergreen trees grew thickly. Behind, over the roof of the owner's house, a sprawling bamboo grove was visible, and to one side, a small lotus pond could be seen between camellias and persimmon trees. At night, besides the flickering lamplight from a solitary house across the paddies, no other dwellings were visible. When the blue stars twinkled and the dense forest shade pressed down before the window, nothing could be heard but the hoot... hoot... of an owl crying its lonely call. Even when returning exhausted from the complex crowds of the city, just a five-minute walk after leaving the tram would immediately refresh the spirit, and feelings of fatigue were often completely forgotten. The cool, gentle air, filled with indescribable fragrances of various grasses, calmed one's nerves, and the boundless expanse of the blue sky seemed to guide one's soul to an eternal, divine realm.

Being a thirty-minute walk from the tram stop, transportation was somewhat inconvenient, but for the two of us, with no school requiring daily attendance and no particularly busy affairs, there was no lack. Thus, we thought we wouldn't leave this place unless some special circumstance arose, and often spent days just sitting facing each other, letting time pass. However, even when sitting facing each other, we rarely spoke, and often, each would just open a book and focus on his own tasks. Some days passed entirely without words, and even if some academic debate started between us, that debate <96> usually concluded within a few remarks. Neither of us had any thought of being mischievous or looking down on the other; rather than the obstinacy of wanting to win, humility, seeking to understand the other's words well, came first. We preferred the wisdom of fairly understanding another's words to stubbornly insisting on something clearly wrong and giving ill feelings to our only friend. Though not quite a deserted island in a lonely sea, in a place where just the two of us sat together, there was no vanity or bias. Empty honors gained through strategy or lost through slander were irrelevant, and the need to dispute interests was even less so.

It was early summer. Waking before the valley mist lifted, opening the windows, and sweeping the room clean, we each took our washbasins to the well, overflowing with jade-like water. After breathing deeply the fresh air of the early morning and washing our faces in the bracingly cold water, our bodies filled with a fresh spirit and peaceful energy. At such times, we often passed the time without words. We disliked disturbing that serene atmosphere with useless talk. Internally, both felt satisfaction and joy, and thoughts of gratitude and reverence dominated everything.

Every morning, Mr. An would meticulously tidy his desk and room, neatly brush his clothes and hat, comb his long hair back smoothly, take out his razor, and shave his face until not a single downy hair remained. Then, he would take off the glasses he wore, carefully wipe them with a white handkerchief, put them back on, readjust his coat strings (옷-드리), and sit upright at his desk (글床). When he had thus finished grooming himself, the morning sun, rising vigorously over the woods, brightly illuminated his clean form. Meditating for a while in such surroundings was his happiest time, a moment when he felt a kind of dharma joy.

Once, while Mr. An was grooming himself like this, I made this idle remark:
"What's the point of putting on such airs in this temple-like place?"
"Hmph! Even when I'm alone, I intend to keep my body clean. Like a lily in the mountains, even if there's no one to see it, it's a duty towards God (神)." Mr. An replied thus. Indeed, he always kept himself tidy. <97> More than a duty towards God, it seemed necessary for calming his own nerves. Possessing sensitive nerves, even a speck of dust made him frown, and a single clattering sound could prevent him from sleeping soundly. Therefore, after clearing the breakfast table and neatly folding the newspaper he had been reading and placing it in its spot, there were many times we sat in silence, listening only to the sound of each other turning pages or moving a steel pen. To an outsider, we might have looked like people who had quarreled, or as if there was no enjoyment in being together. Once, a friend who had previously lived with Mr. An visited when An was out and said this:
"Are you managing to get along with Mr. An?"
"Why?"
"Why? Your temperament isn't like that; how can you stay with such a fussy person?"
"No. It doesn't bother me at all. Actually, Mr. An said something similar... that he finds it quite tolerable being with me. Mr. An probably couldn't last even a week with many people like you."
I replied this with a smile.
"Don't say that. We stayed together in the sarang room of my house for a couple of months before, and I nearly died of discomfort. At that time, his circumstances were such that he couldn't go anywhere else, so he stayed with us reluctantly, but he truly suffered a lot. It might have been better if it was just me, but when the old gentlemen would make useless small talk and puff away on strong tobacco, filling the room with smoke, you could hear Mr. An clearing his throat sharply. Sometimes, he would come in late at night, throw open the front and back doors, startling everyone sitting there."
"Hmph. With his temperament, that's quite likely."
"But if you stay with Mr. An for a long time, you might end up taking after his temperament too. Be careful about that."
"Surely not."
"What do you mean, 'surely not'? Frankly, even if I stayed with Mr. An, there's no reason I would be assimilated by him, but a gentle <98> person like you, constantly seeing his ways, might inevitably end up assimilated."
"Heh heh. Don't worry."
Although we had such conversations, I liked Mr. An. I cherished his deeply genuine nature and his pure heart, untainted by the world. Objectively, he was such a bland companion, but he was the sole friend and sole support who comforted our mutually indescribably lonely hearts. As exceptions, sometimes the two of us, equally lacking eloquence, would sit facing each other, exchanging disjointed words, a phrase from you, a phrase from me, for hours. Or, when some daily necessity was bought, he would explain why it suited his taste and seek my agreement. My taste being similar, I would agree with him, and often shared in his childlike joy. Thus, our life together continued peacefully, without any storms, though lacking any particular flavor.

It was a day when the brilliant sunlight of early summer cast bright light on the treetops and the fields (野面), and the surface of the lotus pond reflected like glass. Mr. An, having finished his post-lunch walk, returned, sat down at his desk, gazed at the distant mountains, yawned listlessly, and said,
"Ah, perhaps I'll lie down and read a book," then went into the next room. He took out a mattress, spread it neatly in the middle of the room, and lay down straight, using the dark blue tree shade as a blanket.
I continued scribbling some manuscript as before. After a while, I looked over and saw that he had placed a thick poetry collection, bound in purple silk with gold lettering, by his head, and like a child who has finished nursing, had quickly fallen asleep. Worried he might catch a cold, I considered covering him with a blanket, but seeing his peacefully sleeping face, I hesitated, fearing I might disturb his tranquil dream, gazed at his sleeping face for a while, and decided against it.

After some time, he woke up. Smacking his lips, he sat up drowsily and looked around hazily near his head.
"Hey, why are you sleeping so much during the day?" When I addressed him like this, his eyes, still blurry and not fully awake, stared aimlessly <99> without responding to my words.
"Hmm. The dream was strange! Hmm. So strange. I wonder if anything's wrong at home. I hope Mother hasn't fallen ill." He lamented thus.
Seeing Mr. An, who normally never spoke of superstitions, suddenly startled by a fleeting dream (南柯一夢) and lamenting like this, I initially found it amusing. Smiling faintly, I asked in a teasing tone, "So, what kind of prophetic dream did you have?" But he still didn't answer and just said, "Truly, the dream was strange. Something must be wrong at home!" Seeing Mr. An genuinely worried, I felt like comforting him instead. "What could possibly be wrong! If you're really worried, why don't you write a letter home or send a telegram?" "Should I write a letter? No, that seems ridiculous too..."
After sitting and thinking like this for a while, Mr. An turned to me again and told me about the dream.
"As I was lying here, just drifting off to sleep, Mother appeared in my dream. Looking exactly as she does in life, she came beside me, looking very worried, silently gazed at me, and shed tears drop by drop... Though I've been an unfilial son, wandering around in foreign lands like this, nothing like this has ever happened. This time, there must surely be some incident."
"If you're that anxious, don't torment yourself needlessly, just send a telegram home."
"Hmph. A telegram! Who would I send a telegram to? Having never sent even a single letter in my life." As he replied this, a shadow of dark clouds began to drift across Mr. An's face. His expression grew increasingly pained, as if enduring unbearable agony. He shook his head powerlessly.
"I have nowhere to send a telegram!" This reply from Mr. An sounded infinitely desolate. That short phrase was filled with despairing sorrow. Recalling that due to family discord, he was in a state of severed ties with his own home, hearing these words now, regardless of who was at fault according to old morals or what the relationship and causes of the family discord were, <100> I couldn't help but sympathize with him. I couldn't help but pity his current situation. The love of parents, such pure love, unconditional, uncalculating love, selfless, passionate love. I couldn't help but pity his current situation, unable to taste that love. Moreover, considering that under normal circumstances, as an only son, he should have been showered with the love of both parents, cherished like gold or jade, yet lived so solitarily, one could only call it truly pitiful. At the same time, I thought it wasn't strange that he had written the despairing phrase
"Solitude is your destiny!!" in his manuscript collection. My heart filled with sympathy, yet I didn't know what words of comfort to offer.
"So, since you left, have you had any communication with home?"
"None. Absolutely none. When I think of my father, I have no desire to write a letter. You probably don't know my father's temperament, but how can a person be so malicious, how can his brain be so hardened like lead?"
At this point, I habitually wanted to offer this advice: "My friend, that's a bit harsh. Even between close friends, it's a bit uncomfortable for me to hear that." But then I reconsidered. "Under any circumstances, we have no reason not to call wrong 'wrong' and right 'right'. Leaving behind all conventional notions and discovering new truths is our mission, and true human life can only be found there." Thinking this, I waited for Mr. An's next words.
"This narrow-minded and malicious temperament of mine, it all comes from heredity. My grandfather was this malicious, my father is too, and ultimately, I was born with this malicious nature as well... That's why I don't intend to marry. I'll live my whole life as a bachelor. What I resent to the bone is inheriting that malicious bloodline; how can I pass that malicious nature onto the next generation? Such a malicious bloodline must be cut off!"
While I remained silent, unsure what would be a good response, Mr. An continued.
"But there's no one as pitiful as my mother! Truly pitiful! Coming to our poor house at seventeen, tormented by my grandfather's perverse nature, then abused by my father, and after my stepmother came, <101> I was the only family member she could rely on, yet even I wander around like this, causing her anxiety. If only my temperament were a bit more yielding, no matter how discontented I might be, thinking of my mother, I would gulp it down and endure, staying at home. But since my nature is inherently malicious, I can't overcome my own temperament, what can I do? When I see something that goes against my principles, I feel like I'll just commit suicide, what can I do..."
Extremely agitated, he seemed ready to burst with hatred, pacing back and forth, then...
"...Truly, there's no one as pitiful as my mother!" With that single phrase, the sympathy and sorrow for his mother seemed to soften his temperament somewhat, calming him down a bit. However, not a single tear was visible in his eyes. Had the flames of hatred scorched away the tears of remorse and sympathy, sprinkling salt in his eyes instead? As his agitation subsided slightly, he seemed very irritated, blinking his eyes rapidly. Then, he sat silently for a while, gazing at the distant mountains.
Presently, he adjusted his clothes and went out somewhere.

The next evening, the two of us began talking again. We sat across a table with a bright 30-candlepower electric lamp hanging between us.
"Well, I feel I understand your psychology quite well, but there's just one thing I can't comprehend." I brought up this premise first, wanting to understand the cause of the discord in Mr. An's family. He gave a sharp, cynical chuckle.
"Suddenly, what's this about?"
"No, I mean, even if there are worldly troubles between father and son, how can things go so wrong?"
Feeling uneasy about stirring up his calmed state of mind again, I nevertheless eventually asked this question. <102>
"Hmph. People with easy fates all talk like that. Someone like you, who grew up in a peaceful family, might indeed say that!" Mr. An's reply was thus.
"What on earth is the reason?"
"To put it simply, it's the clash of new and old ideas that almost no family these days can avoid. But if it were like other families, perhaps some peace or compromise could be reached, and things would pass without incident. However, my father and I both have stubborn and narrow-minded temperaments, so it ends up like this."
"Then who is at fault?"
"It's my father's fault!"
He said this without hesitation, with a completely decisive attitude.
"Didn't you just say it was because both have equally narrow temperaments?"
"No. What I mean is, if it were someone else, they might be able to compromise, leaving the bent as bent. Originally, in terms of reason, I believe my thinking is correct." His brow seemed to show a spirit that could not yield even slightly.
"Perhaps so. You believe your thinking is correct, hence the conflict, but your respected father probably insists his thinking is correct too."
"Yes, that might be so, but listen to me, my friend. First of all, the primary mistake is that my father doesn't see his child as a person, but as some kind of object. Though he may have given birth to me, once I was born with a separate human form, how can he possess me privately? Where is the right for one person to privately own another? Can a young person be sacrificed for an old one? Shouldn't a new life be gently protected as a new life, allowed to develop according to its own individuality as far as possible? Yet, because I don't move my body as he pleases or speak as he pleases, he immediately treats me like someone deserving death, considers me an enemy; is that bearable? Thinking about what I went through during that time, it's truly suffocating."
"Treating a child as private property or a plaything... is that unique to your respected father?" <103>
"Perhaps not, but other fathers and brothers... they might have some forgiveness, and firstly, wouldn't such things usually manifest wrapped in the gentle covering of love? But my father, that elder, is relentless. Hardened like lead by old morals and old ethics, without a shred of forgiveness or an iota of flexibility, he insists his thoughts are right in every single matter and expects those below him to obey unquestionably, regardless of right or wrong. Even for something clearly wrong, if I dare to respond with just one phrase like 'That's not quite right,' thunder strikes immediately. Truly suffocating." As he spoke, his face didn't show such agitated color, but in his eyes, the flame of unyielding hatred seemed to flare up anew.
"Could a parent's heart be like that? We... my father is strict, but sometimes I can discuss even trivial matters with him argumentatively... Could it be?" I could only say this much.
"That's why my family, the Ans, are nothing but an incorrigible, wicked breed. It's because of our narrow temperaments, what else could it be? If one had a reasonable amount of leeway and a reasonably intelligent brain, would things be so stifling? Of course, your respected father must be like that. You inherited that bloodline too, so your temperament is gentle like that. Even I feel somewhat influenced while staying with you."
"If we stay together for some time, we might approach each other, whether for good or ill." I replied thus, feeling fortunate for my relatively smooth character.
"After I finished school, right from the next day, he started making a fuss, telling me to go somewhere and earn a living... His son graduated from high school, turned 20, shouldn't he be thinking of supporting his aging parents by doing some job somewhere, what's the point of studying more, he'd scold. If the family situation was truly impossible, or if the parents were really too old to manage the household, I might have considered it too. But if I take a salaried job now, will that contribute to the family finances? Was I made to be a breadwinner? We are destined by nature to play with books or wield a pen. If he respected a person's individuality and considered qualifications even a little, wouldn't he naturally leave me alone, saying 'You should study a bit longer'?" Mr. An <104> said this and looked at me intently, as if seeking my agreement.
"But is it easy for stubborn elders to do that?" I gave this vague answer and waited for his next words.
"Firstly, when I heard those words, what turned my stomach was this: if you bear a child, you should know the duty not only of raising but also of educating them, yet he prides himself just on getting me through that precious higher common school. Secondly, he brings up aging parents; at 50, what's so old about that? It's truly the prime time to work. Does he have the energy to keep a concubine and engage in beastly pleasures but not the vitality to go out into society and work? And being someone's parent, if he had even a little thought, could he really demand that I abandon my vast future prospects and devote myself entirely to him—his not-so-great self?"
"But that's the ideology passed down until now. Isn't it perhaps excusable that elderly people who received only that kind of education think that way? While we criticize their stubbornness, shouldn't we also try to understand and forgive them from our side?"
"We should understand them to some extent. But isn't understanding different from blind obedience? That they think that way is unavoidable, but can we tolerate them using those wrong thoughts to oppress us? Does understanding them mean blindly following them?... Let that be, I stopped talking midway just now, but initially, I had no choice but to obey the command and taught Japanese for a bit at some private school. But could a person with a conscience do that job? Firstly, it was disloyal to myself, and secondly, I felt sorry for the students, so I quit after just two months."
"That must have been when I was still attending high school."
"Right. You're two years behind us, so you were still in school. After that, while idling around at home, how could anyone endure it? At that time, my father must have been frustrated too, so he said if that's the case, I should just go back to school, <105> and when designating a school, he tells me to attend law school. Hey, man, is law something we should study? How could that dry, tasteless subject, with its arbitrarily made articles, constantly racking one's brain over interpreting those articles, ever enter our heads? But since I was so frustrated, I decided to attend even that."
"So, how many years did you attend?"
"I attended up to the second year, but no matter how I thought about it, it wasn't right. So I dropped out again."
"Then your transformation into a fervent Christian must have happened after that?"
"Right!"
At this moment, his eyes showed a new radiance, exhibiting a peaceful tension as if longing for a distant, divine kingdom. And he put away the English book he had kept open before him until now, truly settling in to talk.
"At that time, while taking the first semester exams in my second year of law school, I quietly thought about it, and no matter what, it all felt like a false life. I quit the exam midway and ran out, but now, I really hated being at home, had nowhere else to go, and had no choice but to climb Inwangsan every day. At first, I climbed out of frustration and having nowhere to go, but once I went up and was embraced by the bosom of Great Nature, I realized there couldn't be a better paradise in the world. In the vast silence of the blue sky surrounding me, infinite mystery seemed contained; the clouds drifting here and there changed into a thousand forms, seeming to possess life; and turning my head to look down at my feet, all sorts of vegetation were sprouting tender new shoots, growing day by day, and ants crawling among them went back and forth as they pleased. Sitting quietly and observing, I realized that among all things in the universe (森羅萬象), nothing lacked life, nothing was not beautiful, nothing was disharmonious, nothing was not free and joyful. From then on, thinking 'I never knew such a paradise and such lessons existed in the world,' I started climbing every day after breakfast, regardless of wind or rain. Climbing like that daily, sitting quietly and observing, the more I looked, the more I thought, the more beautiful and novel all things seemed!" <106>
At this point, Mr. An's face became peaceful, as if he had completely forgotten his family matters, and a smile played on his lips. Then, seeming frustrated at not knowing how to explain his complex feelings, he spread his hands, pressed down as if grasping something, moved his lips silently, and then continued speaking.
"So, some days, shielding myself from the rain with an umbrella, I'd crouch in the grassy field and watch raindrops clinging to the tips of grass blades. Other days, I'd watch over humble, delicate plants blooming with tiny flowers amidst the weeds. And still other days, making friends with the bustling ant colonies, working back and forth, I'd sit quietly and contemplate. I realized that none of the myriad things in this world exist vaguely, none are meaningless existences. Furthermore, I realized these things were created under some well-harmonized and unified plan, definitely not arising randomly. After observing everything like this, the more closely I looked, the more astonishing; the more broadly I surveyed, the more magnificent; the more deeply I thought, the more mysterious it became. As it began to feel this way, all worldly affairs became trivial, and nothing arose in me but the desire to enter the bosom of Great Nature and offer prayers before God... Even now, I firmly believe in the existence of God."
I saw the light of conviction flash in Mr. An's face. However, I could not entirely agree with his thoughts.
"That's a kind of self-hypnosis!" Hearing this, Mr. An seemed provoked.
"Self-hypnosis! That's a thought typical of you people. A very shallow statement. If a person truly thinks sincerely even once, the existence of God or God's providence is something absolutely undeniable."
"No. Firstly, that 'thinking sincerely' itself is an empty temptation. To take a common example we often experience, even if you stare at the character 天 (sky) until your eyes hurt, if you start thinking about it under certain circumstances, it might look like a character, but anyway, when we think intently about something, we are instead captivated and bewildered by that thing itself; in other words, we create a kind of idol with our own mind."
"So, you deny the existence of God?" <107>
"No. I don't firmly deny it either, but any religion or concept of God seems like a soul that finds no place in this world, like an ostrich hunted by a hunter, just covering its eyes."
"No, how can that be? If there were no God in this world, it would be the same as losing the sun. How can it make sense that this beautiful, magnificent, holy universe has no master?"
"There is only love... Anyway, let's leave that as a topic for later and continue the story."
Mr. An, looking somewhat disappointed by my statement that I didn't affirm God's existence, hesitated for a moment, but ultimately added one more word about the existence of God.
"No matter how you look at it, you can't ultimately explain things by denying the existence of God!" After saying this, he continued his story.
"After I became convinced of God's existence, I felt the need for religion, so from then on, whenever I climbed Inwangsan, I started bringing the Bible and a hymnbook. Sitting in the quiet shade of a tree on the mountain and reading the Bible, feelings of gratitude naturally arose, and my mind became peaceful; it seemed there could be no better life than that. Reading the Bible like that, I then felt the desire to go to church. So I started attending OO Church, but after attending there for a while and gradually learning the inner workings of the church, I felt like I wasn't entering a sacred church but a den of sin!"
"Why is that?"
"Most of the believers are superstitious people, and the young men and women who seem relatively pure mostly attend it as a kind of meeting place. On top of that, the clergy who are supposed to guide them are mostly hypocrites. Because they are clergy, they secretly do things they wouldn't do openly if they weren't, so most of them have gloomy personalities rarely seen outside the church. Also, having such natures, they try to deceive others, so they act deceitfully. And the jealousy among them! It was unbearable to watch." <108>
"Could it be that severe? Perhaps because you are a churchgoer, you expect too much from them; still, isn't it a fact that churchgoers are somewhat better?"
"Hmph. You probably don't know well." He said this, shaking his head side to side.
"Let that be too. So, you ran away from the church as well?" I asked this, smiling faintly.
"Of course. After seeing such things, I stopped going to church and just climbed the mountain alone every day to pray. While doing so, I became very angry about the corruption of religion, and also felt the need to study religion thoroughly. So this time, I packed my books and rushed off to Japan."
"Haha. So that was the prelude to your love life?"
Mr. An gave a wry smile at this remark. Then he continued.
"So I came to Japan intending to study, but first, did I have the tuition fees? Nevertheless, I had already come this far, and at that time, I felt I couldn't live without studying religion. So, I first enrolled in OO School and went around visiting this person and that, talking about tuition fees. Then someone introduced me to Baron Hasegawa's house, telling me to at least find a place to stay for the time being."
"Alright. The story is gradually getting interesting. So?"
Mr. An seemed slightly embarrassed to tell the story and chuckled softly.
"So I visited Baron Hasegawa, and the next day I moved my luggage. It was summer then too. Even though it was only around the middle of June, the weather was quite hot. I remember sweating profusely that day while bringing in my luggage and organizing my books."
"So, is the house large?"
"Quite large. The garden is large too, but since I went as a student-in-residence (書生), my place was a 4.5-tatami mat room next to the entrance hall (玄關). <109> From the entrance hall, there was a corridor; turning right led to the master's living quarters, and going straight led to the inner rooms (안房)."
"So what does a student-in-residence do?"
"The work of a student-in-residence involves dealing with visitors... but that was difficult at first. When a guest arrives, the rule is to go out to the entrance hall, kneel down, and respond. But at first, I kept forgetting to kneel and would stand stiffly while interacting. I made all sorts of mistakes. We really went through unnecessary hardships. I also ran errands for the master, and opening the outer shutters before dawn was my job, but heck, the house was so large that even that took quite some time."
"Meals?"
"Meals were mostly eaten together with the master, the same food... The first day I arrived, while I was sweating buckets hauling my luggage, a pretty young lady, about 20 years old, came out from inside and kindly involved herself, asking about this and that."
"So it was love at first sight?"
"Heh heh. What about my appearance then could possibly inspire love? Wearing worn-out black cotton clothes shapelessly, it was really beyond words. So after tidying up the luggage, since I had sweated so much, I asked where the bath was, planning to go wash up. Then the same young lady smiled brightly again and carefully showed me the way." I looked at Mr. An, who was telling the story so earnestly.
"Heh heh!" I laughed, and Mr. An also smiled broadly.
"Come on. Don't laugh, just listen to the story. It's really interesting."
"Who said I'm not listening? It was just so interesting that I laughed a little."
Mr. An, hearing the teasing, seemed pleased rather than annoyed, smiling broadly as he continued.
"So, inwardly thinking 'How kind,' I had no way of knowing who she was. What could I say? Just thinking that, I went to the bathhouse and came back. Later, I heard that she was the master Baron's granddaughter." <110>
"And of course, she must be beautiful?"
"Truly beautiful!" Mr. An exclaimed with genuine admiration. "Her face is pretty, her words are pretty, even her voice and actions, how could they be so lovely? Among the women I've seen so far, I consider her the finest."
"Well, in your eyes, she would naturally seem that way."
"No. There's a saying, 'My eyes are dim to beauty (一色),' but this woman is truly beautiful inside and out."
Mr. An folded his arms, leaned back slightly in his chair, and looked up at the electric lamp as if trying to clearly recall the beauty in his memory.
"Alright. Let's grant she's an absolute beauty according to your assertion, and continue the story."
"The next day, while I was reading a book alone, the young lady came out casually, stood outside the door, and said,
'Mr. An, have you finished organizing your things now?'
'I don't have many things. The books are mostly organized.'
'May I have a look around your room?'
'Please do.'
'Oh my. The room is so bright. Wow. What are all these books?'
'Just some unremarkable books.'
'If you have any good books, please lend me some.'
'I don't have any particularly good books, but feel free to take anything you'd like to read.'
'Thank you... I'll come by often from now on and bother you. Please forgive me?'
That look she gave me then is still vivid in my eyes. How should I describe it? Her eyes, sparkling with curiosity, seemed to hold a charm capable of melting a person's entire heart (一寸肝臟). At that moment, my mind went blank, and my whole body felt like it was trembling from within." <111>
"Hey. This is really intense."
"Truly strange. I don't know how it happened either. Perhaps when our gazes met, sparks flew. Haha."
Mr. An, realizing he had become too enthusiastic, seemed a bit embarrassed and laughed once before continuing.
"So, did you sleep that night?"
"I slept, but honestly, before falling asleep, all I could see was that young lady. The next day, when I returned from school, she came out again after a little while, looked into the room, and said,
'Mr. An. What is that painting? It's really nice!'
At that time, I had bought and hung Millet's 'The Angelus.' Of course, she couldn't possibly not know the painting, but she acted like this.
'That's a famous painting called "The Angelus" by the French painter Millet (미레).'
'Oh. Really. Ah, they are praying now.'
'Yes. It depicts a young couple who went out to work in the fields, now hearing the evening prayer bell ringing in the distance, standing bathed in the setting sun, offering prayers. Isn't it lovely!'
'Yes, indeed. Hearing the explanation, it gradually becomes more profound.'
'What other paintings does he have?'
'There are many, but among them, 'The Gleaners' (拾穗) or 'The Shepherdess' are considered world masterpieces.'
'Do you have those paintings?'
'I don't have large ones, but I have them as picture postcards.'
'Could you show them to me?'
'We talked like this for about 30 minutes, and then she left.'" <112>
"That's truly 'gradually entering the Parnassus (漸入佳境).'"
"The next day, she came out around the same time again, struck up a conversation by asking to borrow a novel, talked for a while, took a book, and went inside. The day after that, whether she had finished the book or not, she came again, saying she had finished it and asking to exchange it for another. In this way, after she had borrowed and read almost all the books I had, she bought me a volume of Nikka Kyōnosuke's (日夏耿之介) poetry collection as a 'book fee (冊貰).'"
"Nikka Kyōnosuke! Then that must be the book you had open when you were sleeping yesterday?"
"Mm." Mr. An nodded.
"I see. That's it. So it's a book with a history. No wonder the book looked beautiful."
"From then on, she came to my room every day to ask questions. At that time, we had so much to talk about; whenever we sat facing each other, conversation flowed like a spring. Truly, love is a strange thing. How could a reticent person like me have so much to talk about? But when we sat together, we never lacked things to say. It was sheer eloquence. Of course, we must have chattered a lot of worthless things too, but at that time, between us, whatever we said was poetry, it was music, it was art; what more is there to say? Truly, love is a bizarre thing."
"So, even though you spent time together like that, didn't people in the house say anything?"
"That's why, even when she came to my room, she mostly stood outside the room, talking across the window frame. Not only that, but in such a large household, the servants (下人輩) are the most fearsome supervisors. But this young lady, having a friendly disposition, had won the hearts of the servants, so they seemed to turn a blind eye often."
"I suppose so."
"Also, that year in August, the master's family went to Kamakura for the summer holidays. I had no intention of leaving the house, but since a young aunt of hers, close to my age, came to stay and they decided to go together, I had no choice but to follow. But then, did she come back the very next day? Using some excuse, having plotted somehow, she couldn't come alone, so she dragged her aunt along and came back. That alone <113> should tell you the extent of things."
"So, what happened next?"
"That time was the most joyful period of my past life. While the two of us were carrying on like that, for some reason, the old mistress (老마님) of the house, that hag (망귀쟝이), started grumbling. Because of me, the old master had several quarrels (內–싸움) with his wife. The master, Baron, being a kindly old man (好好爺), would always argue whenever he saw the hag nagging, asking why she was bothering the student-in-residence. But once things became like that, could I stay attached to that house? I ran away again."
"So, after leaving that house, did the romance end too?"
"Why, even after that, we met occasionally and exchanged letters, but the surrounding circumstances didn't permit it, so it was unavoidable. In my manuscript collection—isn't there a title 'What did the butterfly say to the flower?' That was a letter I wrote to her."
"So, you ended things after a battle of love letters (情札戰)?"
"Is there a way to resolve it? Firstly, either get married, or else run away together, holding hands. But regarding a formal marriage, she's Japanese, I'm Korean; she's nobility (華族), I'm a commoner (平民); she's rich, I'm poor. Given the current situation, it's absolutely impossible. And running away together, firstly, the young lady would need courage..." and he shook his head.
"Have you communicated recently?"
"No! Now we're completely cut off. Truly, nothing tastes as sweet as love, but in return, nothing leaves a more bitter aftertaste than love... But anyway, there hasn't been a woman as well-rounded as her. After that love failed, I have no thought of getting close to women again..."
"So the real reason for your bachelorism lies there. But seeing your frequent night outings lately, one can't be sure." <114>
Although I made this vague joke, he looked displeased, glancing around the room here and there.
"Agh. It's already midnight. Let's stop talking and sleep." Saying this, he stood up and went to get the bedding.
"So, because of that affair, your religious studies also flew away?"
"No, it wasn't because of that affair, but when I entered OO Academy, the internal corruption there was just the same. So I quit. And instead, I audited lectures in the literature department at the university."
"Speak honestly. You placed a woman on God's throne and worshipped her. That's why you converted from religion, which studies God's love, to literature, which studies human love. Confess right here, right now. Otherwise, you won't get a glimpse of Heaven when you die."
"Hmph. As if I'd go to Heaven anyway!"
Mr. An replied thus, grinding his teeth, as he spread out the bedding.

A few days later, a somewhat unusual visitor came to our house. She was a tall woman with round eyes. She looked perhaps 42 or 43, but her face, which seemed prematurely aged, looking closer to 50, was powdered white. Her stout body was draped in silk clothes, albeit not particularly fine ones.
As soon as the woman saw Mr. An, she let out a hearty laugh, approached, and standing below the porch, bowed very politely.
"Mr. An, good morning. I apologize for being so rude last time, but why haven't I been able to see you these days?"
Chattering away like this, she approached, but seeing Mr. An's cold demeanor, she hesitated slightly, then bowed deeply again. <115>
"Please come visit again," she said, turning to leave, but then, as if suddenly remembering something, turned back once more and this time spoke to me.
"Please come visit."
My eyes widened at her flamboyant behavior.
"Hey, Mr. An. Who is that?"
"Oh. The landlady's daughter."
Hearing that, I realized her face resembled the bent-backed old landlady who lived as the mistress of the back room after handing over housekeeping to her daughter-in-law. And considering our house was right next to the owner's house, it wasn't strange for her to drop by briefly to greet while visiting her parents' home. However, her attire and bearing seemed unlike a country woman's, no matter how I looked at it.
After that, the woman came by occasionally, offered simple greetings, and left, but she didn't show any particular signs, and thereafter I didn't pay much attention.
Amidst this, Mr. An decided to return to Korea. The reason was to arrange for tuition fees. One day, Mr. An returned from somewhere and suddenly announced,
"I need to go back to our country for a bit."
"Why?"
"I need to go make some money."
"You. How much money did you bring when you came?"
"I brought about 200 Won, but after paying off debts, where is it now?"
I didn't ask many more questions.
"So, when do you plan to leave?" <116>
"I'll leave on this afternoon's train."
"So suddenly?"
"Well, that's how we operate, is there any other way?"
Saying this, he gathered a few toiletries into his handbag.
"Well then, take care. I'll be going."
"Then travel safely. I'll skip seeing you off at the station."
"What's the point of coming to the station? Well then."
"Well then!"
Thus, we exchanged simple greetings and parted ways.

However, even when the end of the month arrived, Mr. An did not return. There was also no letter besides a single postcard saying he had arrived safely. At the end of the month, when paying the landlord for food and rent, I set aside Mr. An's share, paid mine, and told the landlord that Mr. An would be back soon, asking them to wait until then for his payment. The landlord readily agreed, trusting completely.
However, even when half of the next month passed, there was no news from Mr. An. Until then, the landlady (主婦) hadn't said anything, but the old landlady (主人 老婆) came by occasionally.
"Oh, when is Mr. An coming back? Where is his home?" she would ask anxiously.
But I always dismissed it as the needless worry of an old woman.

One evening, the stars were dense in the sky, and a cool breeze blew through the hemp cloth screen (베폭의). I was reading a book alone when I noticed the lights were brightly lit on the landlord's porch, and the daughter (處女) was playing the mandolin (맨도링) at the edge of the porch. The high sky, the cool wind, the beauty under the lamp—these harmonized with the lively sound of the mandolin, evoking the atmosphere of a southern country, immediately bringing to mind gondola rides on the canals of Venice.
Drawn by the sound of the mandolin, I closed my book and stepped down into the yard. At that moment, I saw the young woman's mother <117> leaning against a pillar, sitting at the edge of the landlord's porch. I slowly approached.
"It's quite lively here!"
"Oh, please come here. You must be bored being alone these days?"
"It's the same even when Mr. An is here, but yes, it's a bit boring."
"Come over here. Have a cup of bitter tea (쓴 챠나마) and enjoy the cool breeze."
"Thank you."
Expressing my thanks, I went and sat down next to the mistress (主婆).
"Why hasn't Mr. An come back?"
"Who knows. He'll probably come soon."
"They say Mr. An's family is rich?"
"I don't know the details either, but they are well-off."
"As you might know, Mr. An left with some debt this time."
"Yes? Debt? How much?"
"I don't know exactly, but they say it's several hundred won."
"What kind of debt could it be?"
"So you really don't know. You know my sister-in-law (시뉘) who comes here sometimes? He owes her money."
"Even so, would he owe several hundred Won?"
"Ah, in that demimonde (花柳界), what's several hundred won?"
Only then did it feel like my mind cleared, and I understood the whole situation. Mr. An's frequent night outings, the landlady's sister-in-law's effusiveness, Mr. An's excessive attention to his appearance, the sudden reason for his return to Korea, and even his remark, "As if I'd go to Heaven anyway"—I realized there was a reason behind all of it. <118>
"Ah. Is that so? I had no idea."
"Still, Mr. An is originally such a gentle person; even if he goes out to play sometimes, he never stays overnight."
She said this, sounding somewhat like praise.
"Well, I suppose so."
As I replied this, complex thoughts swirled in my head. The thought that it was unexpected, the thought that it was a pity for such a sincere person, and while sympathizing that his downfall couldn't be blamed, I also felt resentment that he had deceived even me. However, I thought that deceiving me was perhaps the initial step of his downfall, stemming from his still predominant pure heart. On the other hand, I also wanted to believe that his nature was such that he couldn't resist immediate, momentary emotions, so perhaps he dallied a bit, but wasn't truly corrupt. Yet, the unexpectedness remained unexpected. Inwardly, I was quite shocked.
"He's not exactly a young student, but if his family found out, would they readily pay back such money?"
Here, I felt the need to employ some strategy. So I pretended to be alarmed.
"Absolutely not. His father is so strict. If he ever caught wind of such a thing, forget paying it back, there would be huge trouble."
"That's likely. Mr. An will probably pay it back someday, but that kind of money is always like that. When do you ever get it back promptly? It just drags on and on."
The mistress's words seemed less worried about losing the money to Mr. An and more, deep down, almost hoping he wouldn't pay it back. The mistress continued.
"That's why, even if one runs such a business, it's only natural to try to keep student gentlemen from getting too involved."
Now she began to more openly reveal her poor relationship (誼合지 못한 것) with her sister-in-law. Only then did I understand the reason why the old mistress of the back room had been eager to know Mr. An's whereabouts. <119>
"But if Mr. An comes, he'll pay it all back. Well, sleep peacefully."
I bid them farewell and returned to my room.

Even when the red lotus flowers in the pond had all withered and the autumn wind began to blow colder, there was ultimately no news from Mr. An. Consequently, that autumn, I too left that house and moved near the Edogawa River (江戶川).
The following spring, as cherry blossoms fluttered wildly before the window where I sat, the boarding house maid opened the door, knelt down, and announced,
"A guest has arrived."
"Oh, really. Tell them I'm coming out now."
After relaying the message, I followed her out and saw, unexpectedly, that Mr. An had come to visit.
"Hey, man! How long has it been?"
"It has been a while." However, he replied thus with an expressionless face, as if unable to forget the past.
"Anyway, come in."
Mr. An's face looked somewhat憔悴 (haggard). Traces of hardship endured on the road of life, unseen before, were now clearly visible. I felt no need to ask about the intervening events, nor did I really want to ask. Besides, even without asking, I could roughly guess.
"So, how did you come this time?"
"How? Is there ever any other way for us?"
"So, when did you arrive, and where are you staying now?"
"I arrived yesterday and spent last night at a friend's place."
"What are your plans now?"
"For now, I'll stay in the countryside for a while." <120>
"Countryside?"
"I'll stay with Mr. Im (林氏) and gradually figure out what to do."
"With Mr. Im?"
I replied weakly, looking at Mr. An's face. His complexion had become somewhat dull and dark, and faint lines had appeared on his once smooth forehead and around his mouth. Not only that, but his skin overall seemed somewhat slackened. Mr. An, as if avoiding my gaze, looked around the room.
"By the way, what happened to all my luggage?"
"Oh. When I left Yoyogi, I got rid of some things that could be exchanged for money, but the manuscripts and other reference materials are all with me."
"Alright, please hold onto them for a bit longer. Whenever I settle down somewhere, I'll come get them."

About three weeks later, Mr. An visited again. He mentioned that through Mr. Im's introduction, he had become the secretary of some music association and had returned to Tokyo. He said he had decided to board near Ueno Park for the time being, asked me to send his luggage during the day, and mentioned that the work wasn't particularly busy, so he could study in his spare time. After sharing such news, he hurried off.
A few days later, I sent his luggage, and visited him once after that.
"So, how do you like it?"
"It seems alright. The people I mostly interact with are musicians, and artists are indeed different. Firstly, their touch (觸感) is gentle."
He also mentioned going to a café with some musicians a few days prior and how the Japanese people there simply assumed he was Japanese, smiling as if satisfied for the moment.
Then, a short while later, he came to see me and said he would be returning to Korea briefly with a certain musician. <121>
"How many days will it be?"
"A round trip should take about two weeks!"
"Then travel well. This time it's an official expense trip (官費 旅行?), so it should be fine."
"Looks like it." He gave a forced smile, turned, adjusted his hat, and walked away briskly.

However, after that, even after several weeks passed, there was no news. I sent postcards to his boarding house, but received no reply.
Around that time, he sent a letter from Kyŏngsŏng (京城). He wrote that upon returning to Korea this time, an opportunity arose, and he had joined a certain newspaper company. He asked, though it was difficult, if I could go to his boarding house, organize his belongings, pay the owner for the food expenses he owed, sell the items there to cover the debt, retrieve the books, keep them, and either bring them when he next came out or handle them somehow, but emphatically requested that I keep the manuscript collection safe. He added that he would write to the owner directly.

It was a day when the afternoon sun beat down fiercely, and the sand, washed clean by rain, sparkled dazzlingly. I was walking slowly westward up the alley of Naejadong (內資洞), holding an umbrella, when I unexpectedly met Mr. An. He was wearing a black Western suit and a black hat, walking down slowly like someone drunk with sleep.
"Hey! How long has it been?"
"Hey! It really has been a long time! When did you arrive?"
"I returned to Korea yesterday. I was meaning to meet you anyway. I have some luggage to give you too."
"Oh. Did you bring the manuscript?"
"I brought it."
"Alright, I'll come get it tomorrow. Please wait. Also, I've taken charge of the literary section at OO Newspaper, and we're starting a literary supplement from this Sunday, so please write something for it too. Please, do write."
"Do I have time for that these days? Gradually, when I have time, I'll try to scribble something."
"No. You absolutely must write!"
"Alright, let's meet tomorrow!"
"See you tomorrow!"
The next day, he came to visit. He spoke as if satisfied with his current mission.
"My literary section, one way or another, I intend to make it substantial," he said confidently.
I knew that making a newspaper's literary section truly good was unexpectedly difficult, but thinking it was a suitable path for Mr. An, I said,
"Anyway, do your best. I'll contribute manuscripts whenever I have time."
After talking like this, we parted ways, and without any opportunity to sit down and talk leisurely again, we became distant once more.

That winter, while reading the Tong-a Ilbo (東亞日報), I unexpectedly discovered an article marked with condolences (吊表 질은 記事), stating "Mr. An Hongsŏk Passes Away." I read the article several times but couldn't believe it at all. At least to myself, Mr. An was still alive as before. Even after returning to Korea later, the news that Mr. An had departed on the road of no return felt like a lie. Thus, the thought of visiting his grave didn't even occur to me. In my memory, Mr. An is still alive as before. But where is Mr. An's soul now?
Ah, his tormented life!
1923, 1, 23 <123>

**Summary**

This memoir by Min Ubo recounts his friendship with An Hongsŏk, focusing on their time living together in a quiet Tokyo suburb around 1919-1920. Min describes An's sensitive, meticulous, yet troubled personality, stemming largely from a bitter conflict with his traditionalist father. The narrative follows An's search for meaning through nature, a brief and disillusioned engagement with Christianity, and an intense but ultimately failed romance with the granddaughter of a Japanese baron. An falls into debt, possibly related to the demimonde, returns to Korea, briefly finds purpose working for a newspaper's literary section in Kyŏngsŏng, but dies young shortly thereafter, leaving the author to mourn his friend's "tormented life."

For historians, the article offers a poignant glimpse into the personal struggles of Korean intellectuals during the colonial period. An Hongsŏk's experiences highlight the generational clashes between traditional Confucian values and emerging modern sensibilities, the search for spiritual and personal identity amidst societal upheaval, the financial precarity faced by students/intellectuals abroad, and the complexities of Korean-Japanese relationships on a personal level. An's trajectory from intellectual pursuits and spiritual searching to disillusionment and an early death reflects the challenging realities and existential anxieties faced by many young Koreans of that era.

荒野의 나그네
						閔牛步
벌서 4年 前의 일이다. 나는 安鴻錫君과 가티 새로 東京을 건너와서 代代木마을에 집을 빌어 가지고 잇섯다. 重病을 칠으고 난 나는 工夫보다 休養이 急하엿스며 安鴻錫 亦是도 조용한 自然에 싸혀서 瞑想으로 時間 보내기를 第一 조하하는 性味엿슴으로 爲先 閒靜한 곳을 차저 市外로 나간 것이다.
여긔는 졍말 閑靜한 곳이엇다. 압흐로는 논과 내똘을 隔하야 족으마한 언덕 우에 古刹과 神社가 잇스며 그 神社 境內에는 常綠樹의 千年古木이 욱어저 잇다. 또 뒤로는 主人 집의 집웅 넘어로 너슬너슬한 대수풀이 보이며 한便에는 山동백과 감나무 사이로 족으마한 蓮못이 보인다. 밤이 되면 논를 건너 외딴집에서 반작이는 燈불 밧게 다시 보이는 人家도 업스며 푸른 별이 반작이고 우즁츙한 숩풀 그늘이 窓 압흘 덥허 눌으는 때이면 흑... 흑... 외마듸 소리로 우는 올뺌이 소리밧게 들리는 것도 업다. 或時 市內에 들어가 複雜한 人海 中에서 지쳐 나오는 때에도 電車를 뒤 두고 5分間만 거르면 곳 精神이 爽快하여지며 疲困한 생각도 아조 이져버리는 일이 만엇다. 形容할 수 업는 온갓 草香를 먹음은 시원하고 보드러운 空氣는 사람의 神經을 가라 안치며 限업시 넓은 蒼空의 둘네는 사람의 靈魂을 悠久한 神國으로 引導하는 것 가탓다.
電車 턱에서 30分이나 거러 가는 곳인 즉 交通은 좀 不便하나 每日 出席할 學校도 업고 別로히 밧분 일도 업는 우리 두 사람에게는 아모 不足이 업섯다. 그래서 우리는 다른 特別한 事情이 생기기 前에는 이곳을 떠나지 안켓다 생각하고 날마다 마조 부터 안져서 歲月을 보내는 일이 만엇다. 그러나 마조 부터 안졋다 하여도 두 사람 사이에는 별로 말하는 일도 업스며 各其 冊을 펴노코 안져서 저 할 일만 하는 때가 만타. 或 엇더한 날은 온終日 말 업시 지나기도 하며 間或 두 사람 사이에 무슨 學問上 論難이 始作될지라도 그 論難은＜96＞ 恒常 몃 마듸 안에 落着되는 일이 만엇다. 두 사람에게는 결코 짓굿게 굴겟다는 생각이라던지 넘겨다보는 마음이 업섯스며 서로 익여 보겟다는 勝癖보다는 엇더케 하면 남의 말을 잘 이해할가 하는 謙遜이 압흘 섯다. 번연히 틀린 것을 부득 고집 세워 가지고 唯一한 벗에게 惡感을 주는 이 보다는 남의 말을 公平하게 잘 이해하는 聰明을 스사로 깃버하고자 한 것이다. 孤海絶島는 안이라 할지라도 단둘이 마조 안즌 곳에서는 아모 虛榮도 업고 偏邪도 업는 것이다. 策畧으로 엇고 誣陷에 일는 空虛한 名譽도 相關이 업스며 利害를 다틀 必要는 더군다나 업는 것이다.
때는 初여름이엿다. 골안개가 것치기 前에 일어나 窓門을 열어졔치고 房안을 깨끗이 쓸어낸 뒤에는 各其 洗手대야를 들고 玉水가 철철 넘치는 우물가으로 나갓다. 일흔 아참의 新鮮한 空氣를 실컨 마시고 선득선득한 찬물에 洗手를 한 뒤에는 온몸에 淸新한 精神과 平和스로운 元氣가 가득 찻다. 이러한 때일사록 우리 두 사람은 서로 말 업시 지나는 일이 만엇다. 쓸대업는 말을 하여서 그 靜穩한 空氣를 攪亂하기 실흔 것이엇다. 두 사람은 內的으로 滿足괴 喜悅을 늣겻스며 感謝와 敬虔한 생각이 全部를 支配하고 잇는 것이다.
安君은 아참마다 冊床과 房안을 精하게 치우고 衣服과 帽子를 깨끗이 솔진한 후 기다라케 길은 머리를 곱게 비서 뒤로 넘기고 面刀칼을 끄내여 솜털 하나 업도록 얼골을 밀엇다. 그리고 난 뒤에는 쓰고 잇던 眼境을 버서서 흰 手巾으로 찬찬히 닥거 쓰고 옷-드리를 다시 한번 매만진 뒤에 글床 우에 가 端正히 걸어 안졋다. 이와 가티 단장을 마친 때에는 수풀 위로 불끈 솟는 아침 볏이 그의 깨끗한 몸을 明朗하게 비추어 주엇다. 이러한 環境 속에서 暫時동안 명상을 하는 것은 그의 第一 幸福스러운 時間이엇스며 一種의 法悅을 늣기는 때엿다.
安君이 이와 가티 단쟝을 할 때에 한번 나는 이런 실업슨 말을 한 일이 잇섯다.
「이 절間에서 모양은 그러케 내 무엇하나」
「흥! 아모리 혼자 잇슬 때라도 내 몸은 깨끗이 가질 터이야. 山間의 百合花가티 비록 보아줄 사람은 업슬지라도 神끠 對한 義務로라도」 安君은 이와 가티 對答을 하엿다. 果然 그는 언제던지 몸을 端正히 가졋다. ＜97＞ 神끠 對한 義務보다도 自己 神經을 安穩히 하기 爲하야 不可不 그리할 必要가 잇는 것이다. 銳敏한 神經을 가진 그는 틔검불 하나에도 눈살이 찌부러지고 대그락 소리 한번에도 安眠을 못하엿다. 그럼으로 아침床을 치우고 보던 新聞을 착착 졉어 놋는 자리에 노은 뒤에는 彼此에 冊張 넘기는 소리와 鐵筆 놀니는 소리를 마조 들어가면서 寂寂하게 안진 때도 만엇섯다. 엽헤서 보기에는 무슨 말닷틈한 사람 갓기도 하고 가치 잇는 滋味라고는 아모 것도 업서 보엿슬 것이다. 한번은 己往에 安君과 同居하여 본 親舊가 차저와서 安君이 업슬 때에 이러한 말을 하엿다.
「자네 安君과 가치 지날만 하던가?」
「웨-」
「웨라니. 자네 性味는 그럿챤은데 그 혹혹 부는 사람과 가티 잇슬 수 잇던가?」
「안이. 나는 아무러치도 안은데. 하기는 安君도 이런 말을 하데... 자네와는 꽤 가티 잇슬만 하다고. 아마 安君이 자네가티 數多한 사람과는 단 1週日을 가티 못 지날 터이지」
나는 우스면서 이와 가티 對答을 하엿다.
「말 말게. 己往 우리 집 사랑에서 두어달 동안이나 가티 잇서 보앗는데 내가 未安하여 죽을 번 하엿네. 그때 그 사람 形便이 다른 데로는 갈 데가 업섯스닛가 울며 계자 먹기로 가티 잇섯지만은 참 고생을 만히 하엿지. 나하고 잇스면 그래도 오히려 낫겟지마는 이 늙은 양반들이 쓸데업는 잔소리나 하고 毒한 담배를 퍽퍽 피워서 房안에 煙氣가 가득할 때에는 安君의 입에서 안까님 쓰는 소리가 꽁꽁 들렷섯네. 엇더한 때에는 밤 中에 들어와서 압뒤 門을 열어 적혀 가지고 座中 사람의 눈이 둥그러케 한 일도 잇섯는대」
「흥. 그 사람 性味에 그러키도 쉽지」
그러치만 자네가 萬一 安君과 오래 잇고 보면 자네까지도 安君의 性味를 딸으고 말 것일세. 그것은 注意하게」
「설마」
「설마가 무엇인가. 안할 말로 나는 安君과 가티 잇서도 그 사람에게 同化될 理는 업지마는 자네가티 부드러운＜98＞ 사람은 늘 그 사람의 사폐를 보다가서 畢竟 그 사람과 同化될 念慮가 不無하지」
「흥흥. 念慮말게」
이러한 問答을 한 일도 잇섯지마는 나는 安君을 조하하얏다. 그의 골수로 냐가는 참된 性質과 世上에 물들지 안이한 純潔한 맘을 깃버한 것이다. 客觀的으로는 그처럼 無味한 동모이지마는 彼此에 形容할 수 업시 쓸쓸한 맘을 慰勞하는 唯一의 親舊이며 唯一의 依支엿다. 或 例外로는 똑가티 口辯 업는 두 사람이 마조 向하고 안저서 너 한마듸 나 한마듸 메떨어진 말을 交換하다가 몃時間式이 지나는 일도 잇스며 或 日用品이라도 무슨 물건을 사온 때에는 이것이 엇지하야 自己 趣味에 맛는다는 것을 說明하면서 나의 同意를 求하엿다. 趣味가 近似한 나는 그에 찬성하고 그의 어린아이가티  깃버하는 모양을 가티 깃버하는 때도 만엇다. 그래서 우리들의 共同生活은 아모 別 滋味도 업는 代身에 아모 風波도 업시 平穩하게 繼續되엿다.

初여름의 繁華한 日光은 수풀 끗과 들낫(野面)에 밝은 빗을 던지고 蓮못의 水面은 류리가티 反射되는 날이엇다. 安君은 點心뒤의 散步를 맛치고 들어와서 冊床을 對하여 안졋다가 먼 山을 바라보고 긔운없시 하품을 하더니
「에. 누어서 冊을 볼가부다」 하면서 엽헷 房으로 나갓다 요를 끄내여 房 한가운대에 반듯이 펴고 暗靑色을 띄운 나무 그늘로 이불 삼아 반듯이 누어 잇섯다.
나는 如前이 무엇인지 原稿를 끄적이다가 얼마 만에 돌어본즉 그는 머리 맛헤 紫芝色 비단 衣에 金字가 번적이는 도톰한 詩集 한 卷을 펴 노코 졋꼭지 노흔 아이와 가티 어느덧 잠이 들엇섯다. 或 感氣나 들지 안을가 하야 毛布를 덥허 줄가 하다가 그 고이자는 얼골을 보고는 돌이여 穩籍한 꿈울 깨울가 하야 그의 잠든 얼골을 한참 들여다보고 그만 두엇다.
이윽고 그는 일어낫다. 입맛을 쩍쩍 다시며 부시시 일어나서 머리 맛흘 휘휘 둘러 보앗다.
「이 사람 무슨 낫잠을 그러케 자나?」 이와 가티 말을 부친 즉 그는 미쳐 잠이 들 깨여 朦朧한 눈을 定處업시＜99＞ 바라보면서 내 말에는 對答도 안이하고
「응. 꿈자리도 異常스럽다! 응. 異常도 하군. 집에 무슨 일이나 업나. 어머니께서 病患이나 나시지 안엇나」 이와 가티 괴탄 괴탄하엿다.
나는 平日에 迷信이라고는 말하지 안턴 安君이 별안간 南柯一夢에 놀나서 이처럼 괴탄거리는 것을 보고 첨에는 우숩게 생각을 하엿다. 싱글신글 우스면서 嘲弄하는 語調로 「그래. 무슨 靈夢을 꾸엇단 말인가?」 이와 가티 물엇다. 그러나 그는 亦是 對答치 안이하고 「참 꿈자리도 異常한 걸. 畢竟 집에 무슨 일이 잇는 것이야!」 安君이 眞情으로 걱정하는 모양을 보고 나는 돌이여 慰勞할 생각이 나서 「일은 무슨 일이 잇단 말인가! 졍 걱정이 되거든 집에 片紙를 하던지 電報를 노와 보게 그려」 「片紙를 해 볼가? 안이 그러기도 우숩고..」
이 모양으로 한참 생각을 하고 안졋던 安君은 다시 나를 向하야 꿈이약이를 하엿다.
「내 이러케 누어 잇노라닛가 겨우 잠이 들락말락한 때에 어머니께서 現夢을 하섯서. 宛然히 生時 모양으로 내 엽헤 오서서 퍽 愁心스러운 模樣으로 아무 말업시 나를 바라보시며 눈물을 뚝뚝 지우시겟지... 내 不孝子 노릇을 하고 客地로 이러케 돌아 단여야 한번도 그런 일은 업섯는데 이번에는 畢竟 무슨 事故가 잇는 것이야」
「그러케 궁금하거든 空然히 애 쓰지 말고 집에 電報를 처보게 그려」
「흥. 電報! 電報를 누구에게 친단 말인가. 平生 片紙 한 張을 안타가」 이와 가티 對答을 하면서 安君의 얼골에 한 조각 검은 구름이 떠돌기 始作하엿다. 그의 表情은 漸漸 참지 못할 압흠을 참는 것처럼 안탁가워졋다. 그는 고개를 힘 업시 흔들면서
「나는 電報를 칠 대도 업서!」 安君의 이 對答은 限업시 凄凉하게 들렷다. 그 짤은 말에는 絶望的 悲哀가 가득 찻섯다. 나는 그가 家庭의 不和로 하여서 自己 집과는 絶交狀態에 잇는 것을 생각하고 只今 이 말을 듯는 때에 그 家庭의 不和가 舊道德에 비취여 누구에게 罪가 잇스며 엇더한 關係와 原因이 잇는 것을 不問하고＜100＞ 그에게 同情할 수 밧게 업섯다. 그의 現狀을 가엽게 녁일 수 밧게 업섯다. 父母의 사랑가티 純眞한 사랑 無條件 無打算의 사랑 沒我的의 뜨거운 사랑. 사랑을 맛보지 못하는 그의 現狀을 가엽시 녁이지 안이할 수 업섯다. 더구나 平常한 境遇갓고 보면 兩親의 사랑을 한 몸에 모아 가지고 金이야 玉이야 떠바치고 잇슬 獨子의 몸으로 그와 가티 孤獨히 지내는 것을 생각하면 참 가엽다고 할 수 밧게 업섯다. 同時에 그가
「孤獨은 너의 運命이다!!」 하는 絶望的 文句를 原稿集 가운대에 적어 노은 것도 怪異치 안이한 일이라고 생각하엿다. 나는 同情하는 맘이 가슴에 그득하여 가지고도 무엇이라고 慰勞할 말을 아지 못하야
「그래. 자네 나온 뒤로 집에는 通信을 한 일이 업섯나?」
「업지. 도모지 업지. 우리 아버지 생각을 하면 片紙를 할 생각이 업네. 자네는 우리 아버지 性味를 모를 것일세 마는 사람이 엇져면 그러케 惡毒하고 엇져면 腦가 그러케 납덩이가티 구더지나?」
이때에 나는 習慣的으로 이러한 忠告를 하고자 하엿다. 「여보게. 그것은 자네 말이 좀 甚하네. 아모리 情다운 親舊間이라도 그 말을 듯기는 내가 좀 未安한데」 그러나 나는 다시 돌녀 생각을 하엿다. 「우리는 엇더한 境遇에던지 그른 것은 그르다 올은 것은 올타고 말도 못할 理由가 업다. 모든 因襲의 觀念을 떠나서 새로운 眞理를 發見하는 것이 우리 使命이기도 하고 人間의 참 生活도 그곳에서만 發見할 수 잇는 것이다」 이와 가티 생각을 하고 安君의 다음 말을 기다렷다.

「千萬에. 그이 아버님이 엇더케 嚴重한데. 萬一 그런 눈치를 알면 물어주기는 姑舍하고 큰 일 나지요」
「그러실 터이지요. 安상이 언제던지 물어야 주시겟지마는 그런 돈이란 것이 恒常 그러치. 언제 또박또박 바더 보겟슴닛가. 다 그렁저렁하는 것이지요」
主婆의 말에는 安君에게 돈을 띄울가 걱정한다는 것보다 속으로는 도리여 띄우기를 죠이는 것가티 보엿다. 主婆는 또 말을 이어서
「그러기에 비록 그런 營業을 할지라도 學生 兩班가튼 이에게는 아모조록 발이 잣지 안토록 하는 것이 當然한 일이여요」
인제는 그 媤뉘와 誼合지 못한 것을 차차 露骨的으로 나타 내엿다. 나는 그제야 뒷방 마누라가 安君의 去就를 알고자 하던 理由도 깨달엇다.＜119＞
「그러치만 安君이 오면 다 갑겟지요. 자아 便安히 주무세요」
나는 그네를 作別하고 내 房으로 돌어 왓다.

蓮塘의 紅蓮花가 다 슬어지고 秋風이 차차 불어올 때가 되여도 安君은 畢竟 消息이 업섯다. 그러자 그 해 가을에는 나도 그 집을 떠나서 江戶川 近處로 올머 갓다.
이듬해 봄이 되야 櫻花가 펄펄 날리는 窓門 압헤 안졋노란즉 下宿 下女는 門을 열고 꿀어 안지며
「손님이 오섯슴니다」
「응 그래. 지금 나감니다고」
이와 가티 전갈을 하고 뒤를 딸하 나아가 본즉 그는 意外에 安君이 차저 왓섯다.
「이 사람아. 얼마만인가」
「그러케 되엿네」 그러나 그는 過去의 일을 못 다 이저 바린 것가티 아모 表情도 업는 얼골로 이와 가티 對答하엿다.
「대관졀 들어가세」
安君의 얼골은 좀 憔悴하엿섯다. 前에까지도 보이지 안턴 世路의 艱辛을 격근 痕迹이 만히 보엿다. 나는 그 동안의 經過를 물을 必要도 업섯스며 참아 뭇기도 실엇다. 또 뭇지 안어도 大綱 짐작을 할 수 잇섯다.
「그래 이番에는 엇더케 왓나」
「엇데케라니. 우리가 언제는 別수 잇나!」
「그래. 언졔 왓스며 只今 어대 잇나?」
「오기는 어제 와서 간밤에는 엇던 親舊에게서 자고 왓네」
「인졔 압흐로는 엇더케 할 豫定인가?」
「爲先 얼마동안 시골 가 잇겟네」＜120＞
「시골이라니?」
「林氏에게 가 잇다가 차차 處身할 道理를 생각하겟네」
「林氏게?」
나는 이와 가티 힘 업는 對答을 하며 安君의 얼골을 바라보고 잇섯다. 그의 살빗은 좀 빗 업시 검어졋스며 팽팽하던 그의 이마와 입귀에는 若干 줄음이 잡혓섯다. 그뿐 안이라 皮膚는 全軆가 좀 눅어진 것 가텃섯다. 安君은 나의 視線을 避하는 것처럼 房안을 휘휘 둘너보면서
「그런데 내 짐은 다 엇더케나 되엇나?」
「응. 代代木을 떠날 때에 돈으로 밧굴 수 잇는 것은 덜어 업새고 原稿라던지 其他 參考될 것은 다 내게 잇네」
「그럼 아직 좀 맛허 두게. 언졔던지 내가 자리를 잡어 안지면 차저 가지」

그후 한 3週日만에 安君은 또 차저 왓섯다. 그래서 自己는 林氏의 紹介로 엇던 音樂協會의 書記가 되여서 東京을 다시 왓단 말과 아직 上野公園 近處에 下宿하기로 하엿스니 日間 짐을 보내 달난 말과 그 事務는 別로 밧부지 안이 하닛가 틈틈이 工夫를 할 수 잇단 말과 그러한 이약이를 하고 怱怱히 가버렷다.
몃칠 뒤에 나는 짐을 부처주고 그 뒤에 한번 차저갓섯다.
「그래. 滋味가 엇던가?」
「關係챤은 모양일세. 大蓋 接觸하는 사람들이 音樂家뿐이닛가 그래도 藝術家란 다르데. 첫재 觸感이 부드러워」
그리고 數日 前에 엇던 音樂家들과 가티 카풰-를 갓더란 말과 日本사람들이 自己를 日本 사람으로만 알더란 말을 하면서 爲先 滿足히 녁이는 것 가티 우서 보엿다.
그러자 얼마 뒤에 그는 나를 차저와서 日間 엇던 音樂家를 딸어서 暫間 歸國하겟다는 말을 하엿다.＜121＞
「몃칠이나 되겟나?」
「往復 한 2週日이면 단여 오겟지!」
「그러면 잘 단여오게. 이番에는 官(?)費 旅行이닛가 괜챤켓네 그려」
「그럼 모양일세」 그는 내키지 안는 우슴처럼 픽 웃고 돌어서서 帽子를 바로 잡으며 뚜벅뚜벅 걸어나갓다.
그러나 그 뒤에는 몃 週日이 지나도 아모 消息이 업섯다. 그의 下宿으로 葉書들 하여보아도 答狀이 업섯다.
그런 졈에 그는 京城에서 片紙를 하고 自己는 이번에 歸國하엿다가 마침 계데가 잇서서 엇던 신문사에 入社하게 되엿스니 어렵지마는 自己 下宿에 차저가서 짐을 좀 整理하여주고 主人집에는 食價를 진 것이 잇스니 거긔 잇는 物件은 팔어서 빗을 갑허주고 冊卷은 차저다 두엇다가 요담에 나올 길에 갓다주던지 엇더케 處置하여주되 原稿集은 꼭 잘 간수하여 달나는 사연이엇다. 主人에게는 自己가 直接 片紙를 하겟노라는 말까지 附記하엿섯다.

午后의 불볏이 나리 쪼여서 비에 씻긴 모래는 눈이 부시게 반작이는 날이엇다. 나는 雨傘을 밧고 타박타박 걸어서 內資洞 골목을 西로 올너 가다가 意外에 安君을 만낫다. 그는 검은 洋服 검은 帽子를 쓰고 졸음에 醉한 사람가티 따분한 모양으로 타박타박 걸어 나려왓다.
「야아! 이게 얼마만인가?」
「야아! 참 오래간만일세! 자네 언제 왓나?」
「나는 어졔 歸國하엿네. 그럿챤어도 자네를 좀 만나야 하겟더니. 자네게 傳할 짐도 잇고」
「응. 참 原稿를 가지고 왓나?」
「가지고 왓지」
「그럼 來日 내 차지라 가지. 기다려주게. 그리고 나는 OO新聞의 文藝部 일을 맛허 가지고 이번 週日부터 文藝 附錄을 내게 되엿스니 자네도 原稿 좀 써 주게. 부대 써 주게」
「요새야 그럴 틈이 잇나. 차차 틈이 나면 나도 무엇이고 좀 그적여보지」
「안이. 꼭 써야 되네!」
「그럼 來日 만나세!」
「來日 만나세!」
이튼날 그는 차저왓다. 그래서 現在의 自己 使命을 滿足히 녁이는 것가티 말하며
「내 文藝欄 하나는 엇더케 하던지 좀 內容이 잇도록 만들어 보겟네」 이와 가티 자신잇는 말을 하엿다.
나는 新聞의 文藝欄이 뜻밧게 잘하기 어려운 것을 알엇스나 아무러턴지 安君에게는 適當한 길인 것을 생각하고
「아무러던지 努力하여 보게. 나도 틈 잇는대로는 原稿를 提供함세」
이와 가티 이약이하고 헤여진 뒤로는 別로 종용히 안저 말할 계뎨도 업시 다시 멀리 헤여지고 말엇섯다.

그해 겨울에 나는 東亞日報를 보다가 意外에 安鴻錫氏 長逝라 하고 吊表 질은 記事를 發見하엿다. 나는 그 記事를 몃번 다시 보고도 도모지 미덤직하지를 안엇다. 적어도 내 自身에게는 安君이 如前히 살어 잇섯다. 그 後에 歸國하엿슬 때에도 安君이 不歸의 길을 떠낫다는 것은 거짓말 가탓다. 그래서 그 墓地를 차저 갈 생각도 나지 안엇다. 나의 記憶에는 安君이 如前히 살어 잇다. 그러나 安君의 靈魂은 只今 어대 잇는가.
아아. 憔燥한 그의 一生이여!
1923, 1, 23 ＜123＞
＜96-123＞
