발행일	1923년 05월 01일
기사제목	어즈러움 (Disorder/Confusion)
필자	검시어딤
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

어즈러움
						검시어딤
Yohan Kun,
When winter appears before us as spring passes and we await the rich summer, we cannot help but be timid. There can be no one as unfortunate as one driven out from a love as rich as bibimbap into a world of loneliness. I think so. He is a person who has tasted extreme timidity, loneliness, and sadness. In the same sense, the misfortune and sadness experienced by a hedonist who enjoyed money to the end when driven out of the kingdom called property is also not small. When he thinks of the warm and light clothes, the delicious good food and good tobacco, the countless things he wants to buy, and compares the years remaining until he grows old and dies, he cannot help but laugh scornfully at himself for lacking the courage to commit suicide.

In this sense, I cannot help but say that I am one of those unfortunate people. Though it wasn't much, the ample fortune I had in my life dwindled away completely during three years of endless dissipation. Large plots of land were sold and became small plots, small plots were sold and went away due to debt, and thus, finally, even the most cherished residence has now passed into someone else's hands. That huge residence, occupying over 400 p'yŏng in the only residential area within P'yŏngyang Castle - the house my father built, where I grew up, got married, and gained my precious son and daughter - has also permanently left my hands under the fine excuse that "there is no need to use such a large house unnecessarily." I moved belongings for six days, and on the final moving day, I left that house with a heart aching as if a hole had been pierced through it. The day after, on my way to the hospital, I unintentionally went back into the house and found neighborhood children clamoring in the room that was formerly my study, where even my wife couldn't enter carelessly before, trying to get scraps of pictures.<144> That three dogs guarded the empty house for four days while starving can also be called a tragedy. Even if one sells the house where one grew up at a good opportunity, it's sorrowful; having sold mine out of necessity, my heart hasn't healed even now, a month after moving.

My mind changed somewhat from the time I planned to sell the house four or five months ago. Until then, I didn't regard people as people, was strong-willed, arrogant, and strong in ego, but gradually I came to keep "others" in view. Now, after three years, I wear Korean clothes, but this too is due to a strange fear. One day, as I was going somewhere, several students coming towards me snickered looking at me. After that, when passing somewhere, I heard the words "What a dandy." From then on, when I saw people laughing on the street, it felt like they were laughing at me. When I saw young people, especially students (who like to tease others), I would avoid them even if it meant taking a detour. I, who used to walk main streets, started taking small alleys, and finally concluded that Western clothes were bad and started wearing Korean clothes. However, the laughing people on the street gradually increase rather than disappear. People are frightening.

Where has the 'I' who strode down Chongno wearing a Turkish fez gone, and where has the 'I' who traversed the main road holding a folding fan gone? Now, I can only find the 'I' who tries to avoid people as much as possible. When someone looked at me intently, I used to scoff, saying "What. Mugwort never recognizes hemp," but now I reminisce about that 'I' as if reading a novel from a past century.
Police officers (they can't distinguish if we are plants or minerals) are frightening. Although their eyes, which tend to view anyone as a criminal, are unpleasant, when passing by a police substation, I feel afraid that they will shout at me unless I strive to show the mark of an upright person. I barely peek through a crack in the door at the policeman who came to give a typhus injection. Along with that, needles and knives are frightening, main streets are frightening, small alleys are frightening, clear days are frightening, cloudy days are frightening; in short, everything except myself is frightening.

My trembling heart gradually makes me fall into melancholy. There are times I think of friends, and many times I secretly want to curse this world<145> where there is no lover to console me.
During that time, though I don't know when it fermented, one thought grew in my mind. It goes like this:
"How (or 'when' is also fine) can we live a life without dissatisfaction!"

One night, I thought. I am weak. And unfortunate. I want to grasp fearsome authority. Not just in this world, but through the past, present, and future, I want to grasp the right to rule this universe. The power over people's life and death is the least part; I want to grasp great power extending even to the weather and the movement of stars. There would be no greater thrill than this. With that, I too would be satisfied!
However, would I truly be satisfied with that? I thought about it again. Lacking experience, I cannot answer immediately, but considering that Zeus in Greek mythology was not satisfied, and Jehovah in the Old Testament was often angry, it seems it is still far from "satisfaction."

Then, I want to have dozens of lovers (hundreds are fine too). In this world, is there anyone else who consoles the heart like a lover? A friend dies. "There's nothing one can do for those who are gone. From now on, please consider me your friend,"
she says.
Property disappears.
"Money? You can just earn it again. People are more precious than money,"
she places her hand on my shoulder.
All worries and anxieties melt away like spring snow. Ah, where else could there be a life more joyful and beautiful than this? Satisfaction, "a satisfied life," was there.
However, the clever me found emperors in history who still felt lacking even with hundreds or thousands of beautiful concubines, and also discovered the proverb:
"Among women, there are two lovable ones: one who is already dead, and one who is currently being sought."
Upon discovering this, I had no choice but to think again.

Money, money, it's money. 3 billion is not enough. If I had 10 billion, with 10 billion I could enjoy all sorts of luxury. With money, one can buy power. Money is the most precious bridge for matchmaking in love. If only I had that, I thought.<146> But soon I denied it. Can money really stop the mischievous students laughing on the street? Their laughter is a weapon that kills me.
Everything was wrong. If power cannot buy satisfaction, love cannot chase away insufficiency, and money cannot obtain satisfaction either, are we, like children chasing a rainbow, destined only to search and cry out until we cease? This is too cruel. How can there be any worth in being "human"? I feel like I'm going mad.

At this moment, Yohan Kun...
I was startled.
Madman. A madman, that's it.
Last year, no, was it the year before last? - I got on a train to go somewhere in winter. It was very cold outside that day, but due to the steam heating, the inside of the train car was rather warm. As I rested my eyes, idly looking at the fields covered in snow and whitened, my cheek felt itchy. I looked, and the person across from me, who had been lying down sleeping earlier, had suddenly woken up and was staring at my cheek. Had I been a handsome man, I would have thought he was captivated by me; he looked at me with such rapt eyes, as if dreaming. The large eyes within the white metal-rimmed glasses shone like those of a seventeen or eighteen-year-old girl, looking at me. He didn't even blink (though some time had passed) as he looked at me, so I even considered this: he was a famous phrenologist looking at the development of my skull (though I don't know about it myself)!
After a bit more time passed, he was still just looking at my face, so I felt embarrassed and shifted my head slightly. But what was this? I expected his eyes to follow my head, but instead, they kept staring at the spot where my head had been, as if completely denying my existence. Feeling dissatisfied and disgruntled, I picked up a newspaper and read something uninteresting for a while. When I looked at him again, he asked me where I was going, as if seeing me for the first time. I answered him, and after looking at me with dreamy eyes for a long while, he suddenly asked if I didn't want to become rich. To write his words exactly, it was like this:
「アンタア金持に成り度くないか子?」 (Don't you wanna become rich, sonny?)
I answered that I wanted to.<147> Hearing this answer, his face filled with joy as if he had achieved his purpose, and he said the following: The world is now changing from iron and wood to rubber and celluloid. If one starts a celluloid factory now before others, one will become very rich before long. If I wanted to see how old rags turn into celluloid, I should come to his place. He had secured 80,000 p'yŏng (坪) in Hachioji (八王子) and built a large celluloid factory. Also, the world is now gradually changing from getting all light, power, and heat from coal and oil to electricity. If one builds a large power plant now and buys up the rights related to electricity at very cheap prices, one will see great profit. He had a power plant of so many tens of thousands of kilowatts at a certain place, etc., etc.

I listened carefully to his words, inwardly mocking my dull eyes that had mistaken his platinum watch chain and glasses for white metal. After explaining torrentially, he seemed tired and closed his eyes. I looked at the newspaper I had already finished reading for a bit, then looked at him again; he was fast asleep, as peacefully as a child.
At that time, the person sitting next to the factory owner (as he called himself) smirked and asked me if I had enjoyed listening. I replied that I had listened not just with enjoyment but with a feeling close to reverence. He laughed and said this...
"This man (the one calling himself a factory owner) is a megalomaniac (誇大忘想狂), and I am currently protecting him on the way back to his home country."
The incident ends here.

Yohan Kun.
That's it. A megalomaniac. Only that person knows satisfaction in any dissatisfying matter and knows no discontent no matter what situation he faces. The person who takes a slap on the cheek as a massage is only him, the person who regards coarse food as the finest restaurant cuisine in Paris is only him, and the person who truly believes in imaginary wealth and power is none other than him. Living in this world full of only discontent and dissatisfaction, the only person who knows not a shred of suffering, and moreover, can deny it, is the megalomaniac. While Zeus suffered because of love, Jehovah grew angry at the crowds betraying him, and Bonaparte trembled because of Wellington, he remained serene with dreamy eyes. The only one who squarely faced and appreciated the magnificent spectacle of Noah's Flood and the eruption of Vesuvius (떼니쓰) was that megalomaniac.

Yohan Kun,
What do you think I did after that? First,<148> I opened an encyclopedia. Then I opened a book on psychiatry. But while there were explanations and treatment methods for megalomania, there was no method for *becoming* a megalomaniac.
After that, by asking doctors and synthesizing information from here and there, I reached the conclusion that if one indulges excessively in shameless fantasies, one eventually becomes a megalomaniac. Since then, every evening, as soon as the electric lights come on, I turn them off, lie down, and spend my time in fantasy.

I have a fortune of 10 billion. Oops, before that, there must be an origin story for the 10 billion yen. On my way back from studying abroad somewhere, I am shipwrecked (like Robinson) and drift ashore on some deserted island. There, I build a simple house bungalow-style (벙갈로-式), make all sorts of tools simply in the Mission style (밋슌式), and take up residence. By chance, I discover a diamond mine (金剛石鑛) (with diamonds the size of fists) on the island, and also find gold dust (沙金) and a platinum mine (풀라틔나鑛). Using the power of the waves crashing on the shore cliff, I build a mill to pound rice (the design for this is still in my desk drawer), get hydroelectric power from a waterfall on the north side of the island, and after living like this for several years, some large company from America (米國) (England (英國) is fine too) discovers the island and buys the full rights to it for 10 billion! Roughly like this, after acquiring 10 billion!

Yohan Kun. I thought about how to spend that money. For me, who had never spent even 10,000 yen at once, 10 billion was certainly not a small amount. However (human capacity is frightening), after thinking about it for two months, now 10 billion seems rather small. And (indeed, human capacity is a frightening thing) now, not just occasionally - but for six hours at a time (though at first I always thought with scorn) - my mind has progressed (or regressed) to the point where I feel that 10 billion yen should rightfully come to me. When I occasionally meet poor people on the street, the thought arises to tell them to wait just a few days. If there's something I want to buy (using my remaining credit), I borrow money and buy it impulsively. And I think, "I'll be rich soon anyway!" The 10 billion has become a firmly embedded conviction in my heart, and I normally believe in it unconditionally. If someone says this is a lie, that is their freedom, but I can swear on my honor (if even a half-madman has honor) that it is true. Also, if there is someone who sympathizes with me for having become half-mad, that too is their freedom, but I rather sympathize with them and, at the same time, hope to become<149> fully mad as soon as possible.

The money our deceased father left us increased for my older brother, stayed the same for my younger brother, and disappeared for me; yet I, who lost it, am the one who enjoyed money the most. Confucius said to enjoy clean poverty (淸貧), and Jesus said the poor are blessed, but I, a worshipper of money (拜金宗), am a person who cannot even enjoy squalid poverty (濁貧). However, if, as a result of misusing money, I could become a megalomaniac, I would not only accept it willingly (甘受) but raise both hands and shout "Manse!" If I could sweep away all hardships and dissatisfaction and obtain peace like an opium dream, I would sacrifice everything to attain it. They say this is the only way to obtain perfect "satisfaction."

Megalomania.
If, just by fantasizing like this, I can indeed become a megalomaniac, then let me quickly enter that state and look upon those tormented by money or love with dreamy eyes. And also, let me become a person who rejoices in all things and is satisfied with everything.<150>

**Summary**

The author, writing under the pseudonym Kŏmsiŏdim, laments his fall from wealth due to dissipation, leading to the loss of his ancestral home in P'yŏngyang. This loss triggers profound social anxiety, fear (of people, police, even everyday things), and a deep sense of dissatisfaction with life. He contemplates various paths to satisfaction—power, love, wealth—but dismisses them as ultimately insufficient, citing historical and proverbial examples. He then recounts encountering a Japanese megalomaniac on a train, who, despite his delusion, appeared perfectly content. Inspired, the author decides that megalomania is the only true path to escaping dissatisfaction and begins actively cultivating grandiose fantasies of immense wealth, hoping to induce this state in himself and achieve blissful detachment from worldly suffering.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a poignant glimpse into the psychological turmoil of a member of the Korean elite class struggling with economic decline and social alienation during the Japanese colonial period (1923). The author's anxieties—fear of students' mockery, intense unease around Japanese police (巡査), the switch from Western clothes (potentially associated with modernity or Japanese influence) back to Korean clothes—reflect the pressures and humiliations of colonial subjugation and rapid social change. His ultimate desire to escape reality through self-induced madness (megalomania) speaks volumes about the perceived lack of agency and the depth of despair felt by some Koreans under colonial rule, making unattainable fantasy the only perceived route to "satisfaction."

						검시어딤
요한君

요한君. 나는 그 돈을 쓸 것을 생각하엿슴니다. 單 萬圓을 한꺼번에 써본 적이 업는 나에게는 百億은 결코 적은 돈이 아니엇슴니다. 그러나 (사람의 힘은 무서운 것이다) 두 달 동안을 생각함에 인제는 百億이 오히려 적은 듯한 생각이 잇슴니다. 그러고(역시 사람의 힘은 무서운 물건이라) 인제는 때때로-가 아니라 일시간에 여섯 시간은(처음에는 항상 비우스면서 생각하엿지만) 百億이라는 돈이 맛당이 내게 올 것가티 생각되게까지 나의 머리는 進步(或은 退步)하엿슴니다. 길에서 때때로 가난한 사람을 만나면 며칠만 기다리라는 마음이 생김니다. 사고 십흔 물건이 잇스면(아직 남아잇는 信用으로) 돈을 꾸어서라도 덜컥 삽니다. 그러고 「좀 잇스면 富者가 될테데!」 라는 생각을 함니다. 百億은 나의 마음에 단단이 박인 信念으로서 나는 通常時에는 無條件으로 自信함니다. 이것은 거짓말이라는 사람이 잇스면 그것은 그의 自由이지만 나는 나의 名譽(半狂人에게라도 名譽가 잇다 하면)를 두고 맹서라도 할 수 잇슴니다. 또 半狂人이 된 나를 同情하여 주는 사람이 잇다 하면 그것도 그의 自由이지만 나는 오히려 그를 同情하며 동시에 하로밧비＜149＞ 全狂人이 되기를 바람니다.
업슨 아버지가 우리에게 남겨준 金錢이 兄에게는 늘고 아우에게서는 그냥 잇고 내게서는 업서젓스되 업시한 나는 가장 金錢을 享樂한 사람이외다. 孔子는 淸貧을 즐기라 하고 예수는 가난한 者에게 福이 잇다 하되 拜金宗인 나는 濁貧도 질길 수 업는 사람이외다. 그러나 萬若 돈을 濫用한 탓으로 誇大忘想狂이 될 수만 잇거던 나는 이를 甘受할뿐만 아니라 두 손을 들고 萬歲를 부르겟슴니다. 온갓 苦勞와 不滿을 一掃하고 아편의 꿈과 가튼 安樂을 어들 수만 잇거던 나는 온갓 것을 犧牲하여라도 이를 取하겟슴니다. 圓滿한 「滿足」을 엇는 길은 이것밧게 업다 함니다.

誇大妄想狂.
만약 이만 空想으로 能히 誇大忘想狂이 될 수만 잇거던 하로밧비 나로 하여 그 境地에 드러 돈이나 사랑으로 말미암아 애 타는 사람들을 꿈꾸는 듯한 눈으로 들여다보게 하라. 그러고 또 나로 하여금 온갓 일에 깃버하고 온갓 것에 滿足하는 사람으로 되게 하라.＜150＞ ＜144-150＞
