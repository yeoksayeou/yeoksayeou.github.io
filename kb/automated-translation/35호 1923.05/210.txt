발행일	1923년 05월 01일
기사제목	在朝鮮英美人의 經營하는 學校의 內幕을 보고, 羊頭를 掛하고 狗肉을 賣하는 彼等의 詐僞 (Seeing the Inside Story of Schools Run by Americans and British in Korea, Their Fraud of Hanging a Sheep's Head and Selling Dog Meat)
필자	姜濟東
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Opinion Piece)

Seeing the Inside Story of Schools Run by Americans and British in Korea, Their Fraud of Hanging a Sheep's Head and Selling Dog Meat
						Kang Che-dong (姜濟東)

That the nomad horse neighs towards the north wind and the Yue bird nests on the southern branch speaks of the longing for home even in beasts. For us, who have left our affectionate homeland and suffer hardship and toil in a foreign land thousands of miles away, when is the feeling of longing for our fatherland not intense? Always facing the western sky, I wished to inquire, whether in sorrow or joy, about news from Kyerim (Korea) and the situation of Korean scholars. As long as physical suffering and spiritual dissatisfaction have not yet left our surroundings, it is all just sorrow and resentment. Those brilliant rare flowers and jeweled grasses may be materials for joy to free peoples, but to us, they have become tools of heartbreak; that bright autumn moon may be an object of paradise for the rich and noble, but for us, it has become a medium for stirring emotions. When seeing the vigorous spirit of others, when seeing the facilities of their advanced civilization, when seeing their affluent lives, and then turning back to observe our own situation, how can we not feel our hair stand on end and restrain the tears flowing down our cheeks? We stand in the same circumstances and wander on the same path of fate. Therefore, the resentful blood pulsing in your hearts is also the blood pulsing in my heart, and those hot tears flowing from your eyes are also the tears falling from my eyes. Due to economic oppression, the common people find it difficult to secure their livelihoods; due to political constraints, politicians are unable to display their statecraft; due to religious superstition, believers have lost their vitality; and the insufficiency of educational institutions and the imperfection of teaching methods make the future of male and female students even darker. Ah—in such circumstances, where is the light to break through that darkness, and in such a situation, when will the day come to close that tragic curtain? Flowers bloom and fall, the moon wanes and stars move—such is the cycle of Heaven's Way; prosperity and decline, existence and extinction, bitterness ends and sweetness comes—such is the constant pattern of human affairs. The splendor of Babylon <78> and the prosperity of Rome have flowed away with the past like water flowing and clouds in the sky, while India's non-resistance movement and the unification movement of black people are the dawn breaking to open a path for humanity. It is an inevitable and certain fact that for us too, no, for the Eastern barbarians (東方 夷人) too, the gate of hope will open and the stage of freedom will unfold in the not-too-distant future. However, in this current state of extreme sorrow and grief, where can we appeal, where can we petition? Among the many great problems in Korea today, the one that demands the first blow of the iron fist is the so-called charitable educational enterprise of those Americans and British, whose inside and outside do not match, whose interior and exterior contradict each other. Their so-called actions, wearing a mask of falsehood while pretending sincerity, hanging a sheep's head and selling dog meat, are truly detestable. If even a little of the spirit of Moses who cried out at the foot of Mount Sinai, the teachings of John who preached beyond the Jordan River, or the last will of Christ entrusted on the cross remained in them, would they, under the guise of education, annihilate the spirit of youth and poison the thoughts of students? If our young men and women were nurtured by our institutions and educated by our strength, such a separate problem would not exist. Viewed superficially, this may sound shameless. However, since the education funds they claim to manage come not from their private pockets (私橐) but from the donations of charitable and passionate individuals committed to public good and virtue, then as long as the facts contradict the purpose and the so-called actions contradict the spirit, how can one not raise the pen of public criticism to denounce their wrongdoings and condemn their crimes? Until recently, I was one who blindly tolerated their so-called actions and subserviently praised their enterprise. But now, I not only reject it but also harbor considerable resentment. The reasons are not limited to one or two. First, I point to the coordinated school strikes at Sungsil (崇實) in P'yŏngyang, Yŏngsaeng (永生) in Hamhŭng, Paejae (培材) in Kyŏngsŏng, Kyŏngsin (儆新) in Kyŏngsŏng, and the coordinated withdrawal incident at Chŏngsin (貞信) in Kyŏngsŏng. Growing up under the traditional constraints and customary oppression of Eastern morality, why would they act like this, how could things have reached such a state? Towards those suffering oppression among them, I express my warm sympathy with quietly flowing tears. However, in other general schools, one rarely sees issues like strikes or school closures arise. But that such unfortunate incidents repeatedly break out year after year in Christian schools, which claim to be civilized, no, philanthropic—where does the fault lie, and who should be held accountable? The students? The school authorities? By fair judgment, I say the fault lies with the school authorities, and the accountability should also fall upon the school authorities. Now, speaking of the reasons for the coordinated school strikes, the students' demands are mainly focused on the selection of teachers, sufficiency of facilities, and expansion of freedom. According to these demands, does it not reveal the fact that, firstly, the qualifications of the teachers are inadequate; secondly, the school facilities are incomplete; and thirdly, the students' freedom has been arbitrarily restricted? If the school authorities had sincerity, the proper attitude of an educator would be to rouse themselves further with tears of national sympathy <79> and grant these demands as much as possible. Instead, they resort to unreasonable and unjust means like expulsion or indefinite suspension, pronouncing what amounts to a death sentence upon students, dropping it on the heads of young men and women with distant futures. Is this the true meaning of education? The spirit of education? Since the purpose of education lies in cultivating and nurturing the foolish into the wise, the ignorant into the knowledgeable, how is it not rash to ignore the reasonable demands of students and resort to the most extreme measures? I deem those school authorities who handed down such unjust punishments as unqualified and judge them as lacking common sense, demanding their advisory resignation. I also wish to teach them the meaning of life. That is, life is the accumulation of time; time is life, and life is precisely time. If students who are expelled or indefinitely suspended waste their time, precious as gold, idling away their days because of this, how is that different from taking their lives? How great is the impact that spreads through chain reactions to the nation and society, and how can it not affect the speed of reaching the shore of freedom we hope for with great unity? Although they cry out in hunger and cold and groan under bondage, they are my brothers and sisters indispensable for the construction of the future great stage, my sisters and siblings who must be present in the rebuilding of the new society. I revere them and sympathize with them. School authorities! If you make no improvements to your traditional educational policies and continue in your old ways, procrastinating and temporizing (因循姑息), then your institutions are not places for nurturing talent but training grounds for slaves, slaughterhouses of the spirit, and gallows for thought. I wish to give them these names.

Next, I wish to raise an example of what I experienced and submit it for broad public commentary. This also concerns a certain girls' school in Kyŏngsŏng run by Christians. Last summer, upon returning to Korea, on my way to Japan, I requested a brief meeting with my sister-in-law who attends that school. However, on the grounds that the five-minute meeting time had passed, I was ultimately unable to express a warm farewell greeting to my dear sister-in-law. At that time, my heart was filled with unspeakable dissatisfaction. Not only that, but it is said that outside of designated meeting days, even parents and siblings visiting from thousands of miles away are absolutely forbidden to meet the students. Observed from the standpoint of human feeling or morality, how can this be considered acceptable? Furthermore, it is said that students are forbidden from going outside the school gates more than once a month, even if there are urgent circumstances. Is this a school or a prison? A detention center for criminal defendants? Even in a prison, special visits are permitted under unavoidable circumstances. Under what reasons and under what circumstances do they adopt rules more severe than the prison system, which could be called a human hell, in a sacred school? However, I saw yellow-haired white (黃毛白) Western men and women (洋男洋女) coming and going freely as they pleased. At that time, many thoughts arose in me. I pondered alone why they are lenient towards them and harsh towards us. But I could never find the true reason. <80> In any case, such educational methods belong to the past era that blocked interaction between men and women. In today's world, facing the trend of global transformation, it conflicts with the times and contradicts the current of thought. However, I have not seen such deformed regulations in general schools run by society. For convenience, they permit visits from relatives at any time, and even boarding students (舍宿生) are allowed to go out two or three times a week. Yet, this has not become the target (正鵠) of social criticism. From what I have seen, students attending such [general] schools exhibit a gentle attitude and honest conduct, and their clothing and manners all prize frugality. However, among students attending religiously run schools—though not all—some wear dazzling gold rings (金指環), lustrous crystal glasses (水晶鏡), high-heeled shoes (구쥬), and embroidered scarves (목도리). Truly, even in the expanse of Tokyo (東京), one would rarely see such things unless going to red-light districts like Yoshiwara or Sunosaki (吉原洲畸). Ah, how much effort must have gone into achieving such makeup? How affluent must the economic level of Koreans be, and how awakened must the Korean zeitgeist be, to provide excessive cosmetic expenses not just for men, but moreover for women? Ah, where does the money for these items come from? I do not wish to say more. It's not that I oppose women's makeup, but all things must be appropriate to one's status and conform to standards to avoid damaging one's dignity. Observing the current economic situation in our Korea, at this critical moment when a great number of the propertyless are about to fall into destitution, how can one have the leisure (何暇) to be preoccupied with makeup and not engage in mutual assistance out of sympathy for fellow countrymen (동족상련)? Furthermore, I wish to add a word about the inspection of student mail (信書). Mail is the expression of thoughts between parties in distant places through written form. Unless possessing the special authority of the police or prison, how can one arbitrarily open (開坼) another's mail and violate the secrecy of correspondence? Even if there is concern that inappropriate content might hinder student guidance (훈육상), this would apply only to a very small minority. To infringe upon the freedom of the majority due to a few delinquent students is extreme foolishness (우둔). How can such a person be qualified as an educator? Resigning 책임지고 (인책사직) is appropriate. That letters exchanged with home fail to reach the recipient and are lost midway is also due to this reason. Even if they are delivered, they arrive after tens of days or months. How many students groan in distress, uncertain about news from home under the cold lamp by the guest window (客窓寒燈)? How many students worry anxiously, not knowing the well-being of their parents amidst the wind and frost of a foreign land? Ultimately, many students, overwhelmed by intense homesickness, drop out of school and return home. Behold! The banner of women's liberation (女子鮮放) flies across the six continents, and the bell of freedom and equality rings loudly among the four neighbors. Let new education be implemented (施) with a new spirit appropriate to the times and conforming to the current of thought. <81>

**Summary**

Kang Che-dong harshly criticizes American and British missionary schools in Korea, accusing them of hypocrisy ("hanging a sheep's head and selling dog meat") and harming students' spirits under the guise of education. He cites student strikes at several named schools (Sungsil, Yŏngsaeng, Paejae, Kyŏngsin, Chŏngsin) as evidence of poor teaching, inadequate facilities, and suppression of freedom by unqualified school authorities. He recounts a personal experience of being denied a meeting with his sister-in-law at a girls' school due to rigid rules, comparing the school to a prison, contrasting it with the leniency shown to Westerners and the relative freedom in non-missionary schools. He also criticizes the perceived extravagance of some students in missionary schools and condemns the practice of inspecting student mail as a violation of privacy and rights, arguing these practices stifle students and ultimately harm the nation. The article reflects a strong nationalist sentiment and resentment towards foreign influence perceived as detrimental and hypocritical during the colonial period. For historians, it offers a valuable Korean perspective on the complex role of Western missionary education, highlighting tensions between foreign educators and Korean students/intellectuals, criticisms of perceived cultural insensitivity or authoritarianism within these institutions, and the linking of educational issues to broader nationalist concerns about Korean identity and future.
