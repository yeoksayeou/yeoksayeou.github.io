잡지명	개벽 제62호
발행일	1925년 08월 01일
기사제목	또다시 맛날 때까지 (Until We Meet Again)
필자	孔濯
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

또다시 맛날 때까지
						孔濯
O-hyŏng-
The night is already deep. But sleep simply does not come. As I am in the midst of crossing the Red Sea, the heat is indeed intense. Now and then, the thought of plunging headfirst into the deep blue sea arises. That's how much the climate of the Red Sea torments my nerves. They say that mad people often appear when crossing the Red Sea. Though I surely won't go mad, the extent of my stifling feeling is considerable. I had always known wind to be cool, but the wind here is hot. I console myself by thinking of the plight of one who walks through the scorching Sahara Desert under the descending white heat.
Amidst this, I think of you, hyŏng. More than anything, it's the worry about whether any changes might have occurred in your personal affairs lately. If only I hadn't heard the word "suicide" from your lips shortly before I left, would my thoughts of you in this sweltering heat be so distressing?
It would have been nice to place myself on the banks of the Seine River on a moonlit evening and think of my comrades, but in this heat, my anxious heart is quite lacking in composure.

Was it some day last January? After one o'clock past midnight, you came looking for my house. As I had been reading a book unusually late that day, when I heard your voice outside the door, didn't I go out to greet you with a suddenly startled heart?
Coming into the room, your pale and anxious face, illuminated by the dim lamplight, truly made a large "?" mark appear for me.
At that time, grasping your wrist, and surprised once again by how cold your hand was <101>
"Why does your face look like that?"
At this, like someone who had committed some crime, you blinked your eyes two or three times and said,
"Let's talk slowly. But I'm sorry, could you give me some liquor?"
Asking for liquor at someone else's house in the middle of the night might sound strange to others, but in truth, between us, it is something commonly heard. That's how pure and unceremoniously frank ("숭허물" 업는 것) our friendship was.
So, waking my wife who was sleeping soundly, oblivious to the world, in the next room, having her warm the liquor and prepare side dishes, I came back into the room, poured the first cup for you, and asked,
"What on earth is the matter?"
At this time, you silently received the liquor and drank, let out a long sigh, looked towards the desk, and then asked,
"Have you been studying until now?"
In your face as you asked this, there seemed to be a slight look of contempt towards me. Though I realized it sometime later, on this day, you seemed to resent me on one hand, and envy me greatly on the other.
Having just returned from hovering on the line between "Death or Life," how much must you have resented me, who was reading the works of Nietzsche, which extol the affirmation of Life and praise Power? And seeing my attitude of "everything will work out if you just study" without a word, how envious must I have looked to you, who was trying to evade anguish and negate Life? At the same time, how ashamed your heart must have been?

After hearing my reply, "Yes," you stared blankly at the lamplight. And it seemed you had forgotten your own existence in some deep thought. I, too, sat silently. After a while, taking out a handkerchief and blowing your nose, you gave me the liquor cup and asked,
"Hyŏng, what do you think of this world? Do you think living has value? Or do you think it doesn't?..."
I sat멍! (mŏng!) - blankly for a while at this sudden and difficult question. After some time, I said,
"Of course, there must be people for whom the world is interesting and people for whom it is not. No matter how socially dead a place like Chosŏn one might be born into, for someone who passes the time writing novels <102> or having love affairs, seeking southern countries in winter and finding fine mountains and streams in summer, even a society like Chosŏn might be interesting. However, for people like us who consider love affairs less important than lice (though perhaps it's a reaction because there's no one asking us for a love affair even if we wanted one), and also cannot write novels like, 'I love you forever. You are a musician, I am a writer, in a garden where roses bloom, you become a celestial maiden and sing, I become a prince and recite poetry, let's live happily ever after...' For people like us, I don't know if the world is interesting..."
You wrinkled your brow slightly as if displeased and said,
"No. Of course, interest may differ depending on the nation or individual, as you said, but I mean the value of human existence. Why do people live? Where is the duty to live? In other words, the reason why people live."
I felt quite frustrated for having misinterpreted your question and talking滔滔히 (t'odot'ohi -滔滔히 - volubly) about the "Theory of Taste" (滋味論) until now. Resolving to answer correctly this time, after thinking deeply for a moment, I said in a grave tone,
"Duty to live? Whether there is a duty or not, I don't know such philosophical problems well. But if you ask me 'Why do you live?' I live believing that the day will come when, sooner or later, a storm will pour down, thunder will roar, lightning will startle the heavens and earth, thunderbolts will strike down and destroy houses, white waves will cry in the sea, beasts will roar in the mountain valleys - a day when power will be exerted freely (to achieve something), and I also live because I want to see 'that day.' I live with this hope. Also, I live because there is a duty to create such a 'day.'
And an old widow lives in the house in front of ours. She supposedly has a married daughter. If you ask that widow, 'Why do you live?' she reportedly answers, 'I live because I want to see my grandchild.' The hopes and meanings of living must all be different. Accordingly, the value of life cannot be absolute."
"Then what about a person who can find no hope or meaning at all? Wouldn't life have no value for that person?"
"Well, of course, that would be the case. But where <103> would you find a person without hope? And even if there were no hope, perhaps they live because living is an instinct... My explanation is gradually becoming muddled... Anyway, I know that determining the value of life is extremely difficult."
Now slightly drunk, you finally began to speak of the reason for visiting so late at night. Taking out a cigarette (卷煙) and smoking it languidly, your words began to flow:
"For some time now, I've been agonizing terribly over the problem of life, and I've finally reached this conclusion. - At the very least, we live in this world because it has some value worth living for, right? Individually, perhaps there's the hope of gaining fame, or anthropologically, perhaps there's the ideal of building a heaven on earth; anyway, I believe we live because there is desire or hope in some form. Then, what about a person who lacks these things?"
At this point, I thought to myself, "That sounds plausible..." Your words grew more heated, and after taking a drag from your cigarette, you continued:
"For someone who recognizes some value in this human society, fame appears as fame, and hope also arises. But for me, who recognizes no value in this human society, fame has no meaning whatsoever. In other words, it is emptiness. Therefore, not only does social activity seem like a valueless endeavor, but there is also no need to engage in such social activity..."
Somewhat surprised by these words, I asked,
"In a word, you're saying the world is futile, is that right?"
"Yes. You could say it's close to that... And also, looking anthropologically, I think heaven will never eternally come to this earth. As long as each of us individuals cannot become Shakyamuni (釋迦) or Jesus (耶穌), heaven will never exist. And I cannot believe that today's wicked and viper-like human nature will improve and progress to possess the qualities of heavenly beings. Individually, I have no desire or hope, and socially, this wicked society will continue forever.
Then, if I have no active desire for life <104> and merely live passively (instinctively), then I have no longing, no joy, only suffering.
For one who climbs a mountain wanting to see the cloud-wreathed peak (山峰), shortness of breath or sore legs are not so painful. And even if it's a little painful, once they reach the intended peak, they forget all the pain.
But for one who climbs not out of any desire or hope to see the mountain peak, but because they cannot help but climb due to some instinct, there is only suffering. Sitting atop the cloud-wreathed peak is also suffering for them. From head to toe, there is only suffering.
Then, rather than becoming an eternal slave or continuing an eternally miserable life, wouldn't it be happier for them to cut that life short? Wouldn't it be happier to abandon this life that only brings suffering and instead choose the world of Death, which knows no suffering?... After thinking, I finally reached this conclusion..."
Hearing these words, I thought, "The logic indeed sounds plausible..." You lit another cigarette (卷煙), paused as if reminiscing about something, and then started speaking again:
"For several days, the thought of 'suicide' had been extremely exciting my mind, simultaneously making me envision places like Pakyŏn Falls (朴淵瀑布) or Manwŏltae (滿月臺). A short while ago, as I lay down with the covers spread, the deep blue water of Pakyŏn and the dense pine forest of Manwŏltae began to appear at the head of my bed.
Beside me, my wife sleeps with our young nursing child. I closed my eyes and thought about this and that. After a while, I opened my eyes. The electric light still proclaimed the silence of the night. Beside me, my wife and the nursing child slept soundly, oblivious to the world, just as before.
Suddenly, the sight of my wife and child sleeping began to seem terribly pitiful to me. - She suffers raising the child, the child grows up to suffer, why live in the first place? -
As I was thinking like this for a while, suddenly, without realizing it, a surge of anger welled up in my chest.
'Get rid of them all... disappear myself too... ah, whatever...' As soon as this thought occurred, I happened to look at the desk <105> by my head. I discovered there was a razor (면도) on the desk. It was precisely that moment. A strange force clouded my reason, and at the same time, I got up. And very swiftly, I grabbed that razor... It was that very instant. 'Aehang, aehang,' the child began to cry. The mother woke up too...
I immediately ran out the door. When I got outside, cold sweat drenched my entire body. Straight away, I went out to Sŏnjukkyo Bridge (善竹橋). There, I caught my breath for a while, washed my face, and then came straight here to you, K-hyŏng..."
Having finished speaking, your whole body trembled. My fists and armpits were soaked with sweat. Anxiously, I asked,
"What happened to the razor?"
"I threw it into the stream at Sŏnjukkyo Bridge."
Only then did I let out a sigh of relief, and my heart began to ease somewhat.

It must be so. - You, hyŏng, born into a wealthy and peaceful family, who had never tasted any hardship or sorrow as a human being - You, hyŏng, who had just finished W University, returned wrapped in boiling hope and sweet dreams, planning your future path - You, hyŏng, with a family (家內), money, health, and knowledge - happier and more promising than anyone - Such a hyŏng, due to an accidental cause (not your fault or your esteemed father's (춘부장), but the carelessness of a relative), receives a declaration of bankruptcy and is suddenly subjected to execution. The family disperses (離散). When things became like this, how painful and thorn-pricked your young spirit (靈), dreaming sweet dreams in a spring garden fluttering with roses, must have felt?
You must have felt like you were experiencing firsthand the thunderbolt from a clear blue sky you had only heard about. Still, since you had youth, health, and skills, didn't you look for employment afterwards? You searched here and there for several months, but who would give a job to you after bankruptcy? Before the bankruptcy, even idle people came offering jobs, making a fuss...
Friends who until yesterday said, "I don't know if it was you or me," and "Let's die together if we die, live together if we live," turned their faces away overnight and pretended not to see you. <106> When you worked eagerly for the youth association, society treated you as a model youth, a useful friend, but today, their attitude colder than marble—
When you, who until yesterday strode confidently down the main street (大道), came to seek only back alleys today, how your fists must have trembled! Unable to find a job despite searching, how could you support your elderly parents, and how would you feed your young wife and children in the future? Having become like this, how could you not think of avoiding that bitter anguish?
The faces of people who seemed affectionate until yesterday now appear like vipers, and how could you not want to curse (呪咀) the society where you couldn't get a job even with skills?
Oh, my beloved friend!

That night, you drank, cried, talked about the problem of life, and spent the entire long winter night awake with me, didn't you? And when I said, "Schopenhauer was a more terrible pessimist philosopher than anyone, but though he lived a single life, he didn't commit suicide," you replied then,
"I also heard he was a terrible pessimist, but if the world seemed so hateful to him, why didn't he commit suicide?"
And so, we took out his writings and biography from the bookshelf, and in the deep, quiet night, under the dim lamplight, we read his philosophy, didn't we? And we discovered the reason why he didn't commit suicide.

- What liberates us is only the annihilation of the will to live as a whole. Eliminating any specific phenomenon has no effect on the essence of the will, and moreover, the death of the individual cannot affect any change in the manifestation of the will as a whole...
What perishes through suicide is not life itself, but its temporal phenomenon; not the species, but only the individual. This can be seen from the subtle conviction (隱微의 碓信) that the will to live among us inevitably manifests everywhere, irrespective of the death of the suicidal individual, and continues to be active in countless other living beings... <107>
It is human nature to desire life, but under the unbearable pressure of suffering, even that (will to live) can be destroyed. Just as a thick board requires a weight of a thousand pounds (千封度) to break, while a thin board might break under the weight of even one pound (一封度). There are only complex differences according to the proportion of motive and sensitivity... Etc. etc.
- From Schopenhauer -
We realized, didn't we, that his path to liberation meant that unless this blind will to live (Wille zum Leben) - the cosmic will - devoid of any ideal or joy, this dark and gloomy thing, is uprooted and eliminated, we humans cannot be saved (how pessimistic and negative his philosophy is), and also, while saying that individual suicide offers no salvation and is thus useless, he mumbled something he himself didn't quite know, A or B, suggesting that one could commit suicide depending on motive or sensitivity? And finally, I said,
"It's useless. Are you going to commit suicide or not based on the words of a philosopher spouting things he doesn't even understand? Just as Kant supposedly said when dying, 'I wish I had smoked more tobacco,' perhaps Schopenhauer couldn't bring himself to die because he longed for the cool taste of beer..."

C-hyŏng-
As I said back then, your thought of committing "suicide" is an avoidance of anguish. It's an escape from reality, which demands courage and effort. When I asked then, "Did that thought arise after that (after the bankruptcy)!?", didn't you reply "Yes," somewhat ashamed? How did such a thorough theory of suicide suddenly emerge when you hadn't even dreamed of it before the bankruptcy?
Among suicides, there might be some worthy of respect. I express respect for the suicides of several patriots (志士) during the XXXX period. This is because the noblest love, patriotism (國家愛), was manifested there.
But yours is indeed base. Isn't it the kind that would hide its tail if only money reappeared? Such suicide, I'm sorry to say, is the filthiest <108> and most foul-smelling thing for a human being.
No matter what, no matter how many theories you lay out, there is nothing but the avoidance of reality.

C-hyŏng, trying to avoid the reality of anguish-
I trust you won't dare say, like some fallen person, "Whatever I do is none of your business! Telling me to have courage, not to avoid anguish, acting like you know everything, you worthless..." This is advice from a close comrade and, at the same time, a duty as someone intending to live in this world.
C-hyŏng, who intended to commit suicide-
Please, root out the thought of suicide. Also, merely getting rid of the thought is not the best policy. Please also get rid of the attitude of "living because I have no choice," "living because I can't die." This is a notion easy to fall into for you, hyŏng, facing difficult circumstances in an already difficult society.
C-hyŏng-
This is somewhat off-topic, but it's such an astonishing matter that I wanted to tell you about it. In the last Chosŏn Mundan (朝鮮文壇), was it the April issue?, seeing these words in XX-kun's "Muffler's Mask (Incidental Thoughts) (목도리의 복면(偶感))", I was truly surprised.
- I received a request from Pang-kun (方군) to write about recent life, or if I disliked writing that, then incidental thoughts. Life certainly has a broad scope, but since I am living because I cannot die (죽지 못해 사는 나), there is no life worth living, and consequently, no record of life... Etc. etc. -
- From "Muffler's Mask" -
As far as I know, XX-kun is considered one of Chosŏn's first-rate men of letters. (Though I don't acknowledge it, since they say so). Isn't it surprising that a man of letters brazenly spouts such words, and the editor also presents these words to the world saying, "These are the words of a first-rate literary master"? - Omitted Text - Original -
Why didn't I learn to fly an airplane? I want to go to Chosŏn right now and deal with these "bastards living because they can't die"... Chosŏn students are quick to strike or beat up teachers, making it a daily routine like drinking water or eating lunch, complaining that the math teacher is bad or the English teacher is bad, yet how can they quietly listen to the words of these literary bastards who teach wrong ideas? <109> It's truly a miracle. - Omitted Text - Original -
How thankful it must be for those "living because they cannot die," and would it be a happy thing for our society too?

C-hyŏng-
People seem to change indeed. You, hyŏng, who seemed like you'd stick out your belly and live even if the East Sea (東海) dried up and Mount Paektu (白頭山) wore away, came to think of suicide - what a changeable world it is! Is it just you? I am also changing considerably. At one time, believing in the absoluteness of truth, I talked about the "faith of life" and whatnot, even shouting to comrades, "That we won't perish is absolute. There's nothing to doubt. We just need to go the way we must go." But thinking back now, I wonder, "How could I have been so foolish?"
How lovely people seemed, especially when viewing the Sŏkkuram Grotto (石窟庵) in Kyŏngju (慶州), I felt like hugging everyone I met, they seemed so dear - but why have they become so intensely hateful?
Until last year, I wanted to grab hold and hang on even if my flesh rotted, but now the thought "Wretched race (흉한 종족), let them be..." even arises. When people change, do they change this much?
Perhaps if I had left after seeing the Youth Hall (靑年會舘) construction, I might hate them a little less. People worked so hard and put in so much effort for nearly a year, yet the pig-like masses (민중), who act as if they didn't see, kicking away Jinju (진주차버리는?), are hateful.
Even if the masses are hateful, is there reason to hate one's grandfather or father? If you go and pester them just ten times, they still bring out money. When did we ever teach them ABCD, teach them history, teach them geography? And the farmers, merchants, and artisans are not hateful either.
If you build a hall, they come alone and make noise. The bastards who have studied a bit, who wear nice white shoes in summer, are hateful. The ones who, even when someone is sweating and going around collecting money, think "What's it got to do with me?" - these types who go boating (물노리) on the day of the Youth Association General Meeting (總會日) - how incredibly hateful they are. What's the use of listing all the hateful things?
The intellectual class (지식계급) of Chosŏn, numbering less than several tens of thousands (몃 萬), who carry the fate of the nation on their shoulders (억개) and charge about arbitrarily, acting high and mighty, <110> is hateful. These bastards who haven't even looked into a single volume of our history books, nor intend to, who talk about love affairs and the self, and go to wedding halls (拜禮堂) to look at female students - they are hateful to the point of a splitting headache.
Even if I tore open their bellies, pulled out their hearts, and soaked the entire world with their dark red blood, my heart wouldn't feel refreshed. If I stuffed them all into one sack (I'm one of them too), tied it up tightly, and floated it off into the Arctic Ocean (北氷洋), would my heart feel a bit refreshed?
Hating like this is dangerous for me while on this voyage, so I'll stop. Since I strike at the end of affection, perhaps I hate because I love!?

Ah, my missed (그립은) C-hyŏng-
While writing this, dawn has already broken, and the first light has turned the sea blood-red. A flock of unfamiliar seagulls cries "Kkeok-kkeok-" while circling the ship, flying here and there. The ship continues, as ever, without rest, heading towards its destination.
Ah, C-hyŏng!
I pray that when I return five years later and embrace your chest,
You will say thus: "Ah, friend! Welcome back. During this time, I fought with myself, fought with society, fought with everything, and now my hands and chest are this strong, and my will has become this firm. Ah, friend! Welcome back. I have waited for you for a long time already; now, let us join our hearts and combine our strength to become resolute warriors on the noble front line of life!"
Until we meet again!
Oh, my beloved friend!
May 20th, while crossing the Red Sea (赤海) <111>
<101-111>

**Summary**

This article is a poignant letter written by Kong T'ak (孔濯) to his friend, identified only as "C-hyŏng," while Kong is traveling across the Red Sea. Kong expresses deep concern after learning C contemplated suicide, triggered by sudden bankruptcy and subsequent social isolation. He recounts a late-night conversation where C detailed his despair, arguing life lacked inherent value or hope, especially after losing his status and facing societal indifference. Kong tries to counter C's pessimism, recalling their discussion of Schopenhauer and arguing against suicide as mere escapism. Kong then vents his own frustrations and disillusionment with Korean society, particularly the apathy and perceived failings of the intellectual class, though he concludes this bitterness stems from a place of love and hope for change. He ends with a fervent wish that upon his return in five years, C will have overcome his despair and be ready to fight for a meaningful life together.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its raw portrayal of intellectual anguish and existential despair among educated Koreans during the colonial period. C's situation reflects the precariousness of social standing and the harsh realities faced even by the privileged under changing economic conditions and societal pressures. Kong's critique of the Korean intellectual class, their perceived detachment ('white shoes in summer'), lack of historical consciousness, and the 'living because I cannot die' attitude referenced from *Chosŏn Mundan*, offers a window into internal criticisms and frustrations within the nationalist or intellectual movements. The discussion comparing personal suffering to philosophical pessimism (Schopenhauer) and the ultimate call for resilience and striving for a future purpose highlight the complex psychological and ideological struggles of the time.

						孔濯
O형-
밤은 임이 깁헛슴니다. 그러나 잠은 도모지 오지를 안슴니다. 赤海를 지나는 중에 잇슴으로, 그 더운 맛이란 과연함 즉 함니다. 가다오다, 시퍼런 바다로 펑덩 빠지고 십흔 생각이 남니다. 그만치 적해의 기후는 나의 신경을 괴롭게 함니다. 恒用 적해를 지날 때면, 밋친 사람이 조히 생긴다고 함니다. 내야 설마 미치지야 안켓지만, 가삼 막히는 정도가 무던함니다. 바람이란 시원한 것으로 아라 왓는데 여긔의 바람은 더옵슴니다. 내려 쬬히는 白熱下에 타는 듯한 사하라 사막을 거러갈 자의 처지를 생각하고, 스스로 위로를 함니다.
나는 이러한 중에도 형을 생각함니다. 그는 무엇보다도, 그간 형의 신변에 무슨 변동이나 생기지 안엇슬가 하는 렴려올시다. 그도 떠나기 얼마 전에 형의 입으로부터 「자살」이란 말을 안 들엇스면 갓득이나 더운 중에 형을 생각하는 마음이 요다지 안타갑을 것이야 잇겟슴니가?
달밝은 저녁 「세이누」 河畔에 몸을 두어 동모를 생각해도 조흘 것이엿마는, 이 더운 중에 이다지 초조한 나의 마음도 무던히 여유스럽지 못함니다.

지난 1월 어느 날이엿든가, 밤새로 한시가 지나서 형은 나의 집을 차저 왓지요. 별나케 그날은 늣도록 책을 보고 잇섯든 나는, 문밧게서 형의 목소리가 들릴 때에, 나는 갑작이 놀낸 마음으로 형을 마지라 나가지 안엇슴니가.
방에 들어와, 희미한 람포ㅅ불에 빗초이는 형의 그 창백하고도 불안스러운 얼골은 과연 나로 하야곰 「?」표를 크게 나게 햇슴니다.
나는 그 때 형의 손목을 쥐이며, 동시에 형의 손이 몹시 찬데 다시 한번 놀내면서＜101＞
『얼골빗이 웨 그럿슴니가?』
형은 이 때 무슨 죄나 범한 사람처럼, 눈을 두 서너번 껌벅껌벅하더니
『천천히 니야기하지요. 그런데 미안하지만 술좀 주구료.』
우밤중에 남의 집에 가서 술 달나는 것이 다른 사람에겐 이상하게 들일지 모르나, 其實 우리사이에는 예사로 들리는 것이잇슴니다. 그만치 우리 벗사이는 純實하고 「숭허물」업는 것이엿슴니다.
해서 엽 방에서 세상모르고 자는 안해를 깨워 가지고, 술을 데며 안주를 갓추게 하야 가지고, 방에 다시 들어와서, 나는 첫잔을 형에게 딸아 주며
『대관절 무슨 일임니까?』
형은 이 때 잠잠히 술을 바다 먹고, 한숨을 한번 길게 쉬며, 책상 편을 바라보더니
『입대 공부하셧서요?』
이가티 뭇는 형의 얼골에는 다소 나를 경멸하는 빗이 보헛슴니다. 얼마 후에 내가 깨다른 바이나 이 날의 형은 나를 한 편으로 미워하며, 다른 한 편으로 퍽 부러워햇든 것임니다.
방금 「死냐 生이냐」하는 선에서 방황타가 도라 온 형이, 生의 긍정을 고조하며, 力을 찬미하는 「니잇췌」의 저서를 보고 잇는 내가 오작이나 미웟겟슴니가? 그리고 소리업시, 「공부하면 다- 된다」하는 나의 태도를 볼 때에, 고뇌를 회피하려 하며, 生을 부정하려 하는 형은 오작이나 내가 부럽게 보혓겟슴니가? 동시에 형의 마음은 그 얼마나 붓그러웟겟슴니까?

『네-』라는 나의 대답을 들은 후, 형은 하염업시 람포ㅅ불만 바라보고 잇섯슴니다. 그리고 엇더한 깁흔 생각에 자기존재를 니즌 것 가탓슴니다. 나역 잠잠히 안젓슴니다. 얼마후에 수건을 끄내여 코를 푼 후, 형은 나에게 술잔을 주며
『형은 이 세상을 엇더케 생각하심니까? 산다는 것이 가치가 잇는 줄로 생각하심니까? 업는 줄로 생각하심니까?...』
나는 이 돌발적이요 어려운 질문에 한참동안 멍!하니 안젓섯슴니다. 얼마 후에 나는
『물론 세상이 자미잇는 사람도 잇고 자미업는 사람도 잇겟지요. 아모리 사회적으로는 죽은 조선가튼데 태여낫슬지라도 심심 消日로 소설이나 쓰고＜102＞ 연애나 하면서 겨울이면 南國을 차저 가고 녀름이면 조흔 산천을 차저 가는 그런 사람에게는 조선가튼 사회도 자미날 것임니다. 그러나 우리가티 연애를 밋씻개만도 못 녁이고 (사실 연애를 하고 시퍼도 연애하자는 사람이 업서서 반동적으로 이러케 되는지는 모름니다마는) 또한 『나는 당신을 영원히 사랑함니다. 당신은 음악가 나는 문학가 장미꼿피 는 동산에서 당신은 천녀가 되여 노래부르고 나는 왕자가 되여 시 읇흐며 영영 즐겁게 지내자고요...』라고- 이가튼 소설을 쓸 줄 모르는 우리가튼 사람에게는 세상이 자미난 줄 모르겟슴니다...』
형은 불쾌한 듯이 이마ㅅ살을 좀 찡그리며
『아니요. 자미야 물론 형이 말한 거와 가티 민족이나 개인에 따라서 달으겟지만 인간존재의 가치말이올시다. 사람이 웨 사느냐? 살 의무가 어데 잇느냐? 다시 말하면 사람이 사는 까닭 말이 올시다.』
나는 형의 질문을 잘못 해석하고 아즉까지 「滋味論」에 대해서 滔滔히 말한 것이 퍽 안타까웟슴니다. 이제는 바로 대답하리라 하야 한참 숙고를 한 후 나는 장중한 口調로
『살 의무? 의무가 잇는지 업는지 그런 철학적 문제는 잘 모르겟슴니다. 그러나 나다려 「웨 사느냐?」 말하라면 「조만간 폭풍우 내리 퍼부으며 우뢰소리에 번개ㅅ불 천지를 놀내게 하며 벼락이 떠러저 막 가옥을 부시며 바다에 白波울며 산곡에 맹수 포효하며- 제맘대로 힘을 발휘하는 (엇던 것을 니루기 위하야) 그날이 올 것을 밋고 또한 「그날」이 보고 시퍼서 삼니다. 나는 이런 희망으로 삼니다. 또한 이런 「날」을 만들기 위한 의무가 잇서서 삼니다.
그리고 우리 압집에 늙은 과부가 한 분 삼니다. 시집간 딸이 한아 잇대요. 그 과부에게 『당신은 웨 사시오?』라고 물으면 그는 『외손보고 시퍼서 살지요.』라고 대답한담니다. 사는 희망과 뜻이 다- 다를 것임니다. 따라 생의 가치도 절대적이 못될 것임니다.』
『그러면 아모 희망도 뜻도 발견할 수 업는 사람은 엇지함니까? 그 사람에게는 생의 가치가 업슬 것이 안임니까?』
『그야 물론 그럴 것임니다. 그러나 희망업는 사람이＜103＞ 어데 잇겟슴니까. 또 설사 희망은 업다 하드라도 산다는 것은 본능이니까 사는 모양이지요. 해석이 차차 흐리뭉덩해짐니다마는... 하여간 인생의 가치를 단정하는 일이란 극히 어려운줄 암니다.』
술이 얼근해진 형은 이 때에 비로소 느즌 밤중에 차저오게 된 이유를 말하게 됏슴니다. 卷煙을 끄내여 한 대 느러지게 흡연을 한 후 말ㅅ줄이 풀리기 시작하여
『요 얼마 전브터 인생문제에 대해서 몹시 번민을 해오든 터인데 나는 필경 이런 결론을 엇게 됏슴니다.- 적어도 우리가 이 세상에 산다는 것은 살만한 가치가 잇기 때문이겟지요. 개인적으로는 혹 공명을 엇겟다는 희망이 잇다든지 인류적으로는 혹은 지상에 천국을 건설키 위한 이상이 잇다든지 하여간 엇더한 형식에서든지 욕망이라든지 희망이 잇기 따문에 사는 것인 줄 암니다. 그러면 만약 이런 것이 업는 사람은 엇지 하겟느냐?』
나는 이 때 속으로 「그럴 듯하거든...」이라고 생각햇슴니다. 형은 말에 熱이 한층 더 올나서 담배를 한 목음 빤 후 말을 다시 계속하야
『이 인간사회에 다소의 가치를 인정하는 사람에게는 공명도 공명답게 보히는 동시에 희망도 나겟스나 이 인간사회에 아모 가치를 인식치 못하는 나에게 잇서서는 공명이라는 것이 아모 의미가 업슴니다. 다시 말하면 공허외다. 함으로 사회적 활동이 아모 가치업는 일로 보힐 뿐만 아니라, 그런 사회적 활동을 할 필요도 업슴니다...』
이 말에 다소 놀낸 나는
『한마듸로 말하면 세상이 허무하다는 말슴임니다 그려?』
『네. 그것에 갓갑다고 말할 수 잇겟지요... 그리고 또 인류적으로 보아서 천국이 지상에 영원히 오지 못하리라고 나는 생각함니다. 우리 개인 개인이 다- 釋迦나 耶穌가 될 수 업는 이상에는 천국은 영원히 업슬 것임니다. 그리고 오늘날의 이다지 사악하고 독사가튼 인간성이 향상되고 진보되여 천국인의 소질을 갓게 되리라고 나는 밋지 못함니다. 개인적으로는 욕망과 희망이 업고 사회적으로는 이 사악한 사회는 영원히 계속할 것임니다.
그러면 능동적으로 나에게 생의 욕구가 업고＜104＞ 다만 피동적(본능적)으로 산다하면 나에게는 동경이 업는 동시에 깃븜이 업고 오즉 괴로움뿐이 잇슬 따름이외다.
白雲낀 山峰이 보고 시퍼서 산을 올너가는 자에게 잇서서는 숨찬 것이라든지 다리 아픈 것이 그다지 괴롭지 안슴니다. 또한 여간 좀 괴롭드라도 목적한 山峯에 다다르면 아픈 것은 다- 니저바림니다.
그러나 산봉이 보고 십다는 아모 욕망과 희망업시 엇더한 본능 때문에 등산을 하지 아니치 못하게 되는 자에게 잇서서는 고통 뿐이외다. 白雲낀 산봉에 올라가 안는 것도 그에게는 고통이외다. 머리 끗부터 발툽 끗까지 고통 뿐이외다.
그러면 영원한 노예가 된다든지 영원한 비참한 생을 계속하느니 보다는 차라리 그 생을 끈허바림이 그에게 도로혀 행복될 것이 아니냐? 고통만을 당할 이 생을 버리고 차라리 고통을 모르는 死의 세계를 취함이 행복될 것이 아니냐?... 나는 생각한 결과 필경 이가튼 결론을 어든 것임니다...』
나는 이 말을 듯고 「이론은 과연 그럴 듯 하다마는...」 햇슴니다. 형은 卷煙을 가라붓처 대이드니 한참 무엇을 회고하는 듯 하다가 다시 말을 끄내여
『수일전브터 「자살」이란 생각이 극도로 나의 머리를 흥분케하는 동시에 朴淵瀑布라든지 滿月臺 가튼 곳을 연상케 됏섯는데 앗가 이불을 펴고 누어서 또 朴淵의 시퍼런 물과 滿月臺의 욱어진 송림이 머리ㅅ맛혜 나타나기 시작햇슴니다.
나의 엽헤서는 안해가 어린 젓먹이를 데불고 잠니다. 나는 눈을 감고 이것저것을 생각햇슴니다. 얼마 후에 나는 눈을 떳슴니다. 전등ㅅ불은 여전히 밤의 적막을 고함니다. 엽헤서 안해와 젓먹이는 전과 가티 세상모르고 잠니다.
나는 갑작이 안해와 젓먹이 자는 광경이 퍽 불샹해지기 시작햇슴니다.- 저것은 어린 것을 길느노라 고생이요 어린 것은 고생하라 자라며, 당초에 웨 사느냐?-
한참ㅅ동안 이런 생각을 하고 잇스려니까 나도 모르게 갑작이 엇던 화ㅅ김이 가삼에 복밧처 올라옴니다.
「저것들을 다- 업새고... 나도 업서지고...에라...』 이런 생각이 나자마자 나는 우연히 머리ㅅ맛＜105＞ 책상을 보앗슴니다. 책상우에는 면도가 잇슴을 나는 발견하게 됏슴니다. 바로 그 순간이엿슴니다. 이상한 엇더한 힘이 나의 이성을 흐리게 하는 동시에 나는 그만 닐어낫슴니다. 그리하야 아조 민활하게 그 면도를 잡앗슴니다... 바로 그 찰나엿슴니다. 「애행애행」 어린 것은 울기 시작햇슴니다. 어미도 따라 깻슴니다...
나는 그만 문밧그로 뛰어나왓슴니다. 나오니까 식은 땀이 온 몸을 젹셧슴니다. 그 길로 나는 善竹橋를 나갓슴니다. 그리하야 거긔서 한참ㅅ동안 숨을 돌리며 얼골을 싯치며 하야 가지고 지금 막 K형에게로 온 것임니다...』
말을 맛친 형은 온 몸을 벌벌 떨엇슴니다. 나는 두 주먹과 겨드랑이에 땀이 펑하게 저젓슴니다. 나는 안타갑은 생각으로
『그 면도는 엇잿슴니가?』
『善竹橋 溪川에 더지고 왓서요?』
