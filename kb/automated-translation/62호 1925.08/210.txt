녀름과 人間의 裏面
						春坡
서울 놈들은 모다 귀신=시골이 그립다=모기빈대에 쫏겨나는 人間들=夜市의 이꼴저꼴=月夜의 漢江行=病院 內에서 五時間=噫! 所謂 有志·所謂 體面!
(Seoul folks are all ghosts=I miss the countryside=Humans driven out by mosquitoes and bedbugs=This and that scene of the Night Market=A Han River trip on a moonlit night=Five hours inside the Hospital=Alas! So-called Men of Influence, So-called Dignity!)

Seoul folks are all ghosts
Seoul folks are all like ghosts. Those sprawled high up in lofty mansions are like ghosts, those crawling in and out of single-room huts are like ghosts, the Western-suited high-collar types are like ghosts, the women whose skirts are like dragonfly wings and who strut about like Jinam hens are like ghosts, and whether their nerves are twisted or their eyes are rolled back, even the beggars in Seoul are like ghosts. That aside, even the young eagles, young magpies, young sparrows, and young rats are all like ghosts. Everything in Seoul seems ghostly. How on earth so many people on so many days eat, dress, and bustle about like that—no matter how I think about it, they are ghostly folks. Especially the women of Seoul seem like the chief ghosts among ghosts. The young magpies and young sparrows are no ordinary ghosts either.
Look now. Don't look with the eyes of a Seoul person, but look with the eyes of a country person, especially with the eyes of an old peasant from a farming village. Aren't they all ghosts? Won't you instantly stick out your tongue and say, "Hey, look at these fellows! Outrageous rascals, they've all become ghosts!"?
This one is an aristocrat, so he still rubs his belly with what he exploited beforehand; this one is an official, so he lives decently receiving a salary and bonuses; this one is a bank employee, so let's assume he lives that way; this one is an educator, so he won't starve; this one is a company employee, this one runs an inn, this one is a factory worker, this one is a driver, this one is a rickshaw puller, this one is a porter, this one sells liquor, this one is a shrimp vendor, this one is a plasterer, this one is a farmhand – these folks, even if just for a moment, still have an income, so they manage to live somehow<93> (though they are still ghosts), but what about those folks who do nothing else, those idlers loafing around? What do they eat to survive? No matter what, they are ghostly folks.
Country folks, men and women, young and old, shed sweat and blood 360 days a year without a single day's rest, yet they gasp for breath, unable to get over Barley Pass, collapse unable to cross the 7 Palaces, and ultimately produce hundreds and thousands of starvation victims. But Seoul folks, by what calculation, manage to send their children to school, let their wives tap their feet in the inner rooms, and even keep extra people like maids, seamstresses (針母), and servants (행랑), and still live comfortably? How is this calculation possible? And they live in fine clothes and good food. Look at them walking on the streets; they are all sleek and well-dressed.
Now, if this isn't ghostly, what is? In some household, they say they barely earn 25 won a month working in a factory, yet a family of five lives decently in a house, eating meals, wearing clothes – if this isn't ghostly, what is? When they go out, they are all dressed neatly. Yet, I haven't heard that fellow was caught for theft or robbery.
This is an income, even if only 25 won. But there are countless thousands with not even half a penny of income, yet they are neither robbers nor thieves, nor do they starve; they manage to live. Now, if this isn't ghostly, what is?
Anyway, Seoul folks are ghosts. Nobles, commoners, rich, poor, men, women, employed, unemployed – they are all ghosts. Never mind the humans, even the young magpies and sparrows are ghosts. What do they eat to live? But look into their underside (裏面). How dirty, fishy-smelling, tenacious, and wriggling it is. The sight of chicks fighting over a pile of dung is there, and the sight of puppies growling at each other over a dog bone is there.
There's a saying, "If only 30 households gather, they can live by tearing at each other." Seoul has at least 50,000 households, so it's ample for living by tearing at each other. As long as one has teeth and claws, one probably won't die.
There's also a saying, "To live in Seoul, you need the heart of a thief." Indeed, where is there anyone living in Seoul who doesn't have the heart of a thief? Only those caught red-handed for theft or robbery are considered thieves?
Living such a life without doing commensurate work, however it was obtained—whether by deceiving, threatening, tricking, or coercing others—whatever the method, it is clearly thievery. Being good at thievery also means being a ghost.
The country folks, the producers, should know that the Seoul folks<94>, no, the city dwellers, are ghosts. And don't just call them ghosts; think about some countermeasures against these ghosts. You probably won't be able to just stand by. Considered another way, perhaps the country folks who aren't ghosts are the handicapped ones.

I miss the countryside
I miss the countryside. I miss the countryside with its bright mountains and beautiful waters (山明水麗). I want to see the ash-gray color of the grain on the upper and lower plains. I want to see father carrying a spade and mother holding a hoe. I want to see older brother cutting fodder and younger brother feeding the cow. I want to see auntie carrying a lunch basket on her head, and sister-in-law carrying a gourd dipper of clear stream water (淸溪水), trotting (種種 거름치는) along the paddy ridge path. I want to see the calf running freely along the 10-ri long embankment (長堤). I want to see the puppy squatting under the mulberry tree, yapping "kong-kong." I want to see the young rooster chick chirping "pi-pi" under the pumpkin vines, and the pig squealing "kkol-kkul" in its pen. I want to hear the "ttumbuk-ttumbuk" sound of the corncrake in the front paddy, and see the white egret (白鷺) flying gracefully over the back hills. Above all, I want to eat the sweet potatoes (甘藷) like horse testicles, roasted by grandmother herself, and the wonderfully fragrant salted water dropwort picked by my young sister herself from the paddy ditch.
Ah—what is there not to miss? Thinking of the charming true scenes of the countryside, my mind and body leap with joy, and I don't know what to do. Ah—how deeply I long for it. Why am I in this dusty capital (紅塵萬丈의 京城), foolishly fighting with mosquitoes and bedbugs?
I miss the farming village. But what can I do? It's not that the beauty of nature isn't good, nor that the farming companions aren't welcome, but what am I to do with this weak, flighty (浮虛) body, sickened by the city, sickened by the unpleasant customs of the times? Who cannot praise the farming village and sing of nature? What does it matter if I miss the countryside? Ah—as my thoughts reach this point, I feel a sense of loss, as if I've lost something!
Can these hands skillfully grasp a hoe handle? Can these hands skillfully handle dung? Can these hands skillfully grasp thorny bushes? It's definitely a problem. Also, can these feet tread through muddy paddies? Can these feet walk on pebble-strewn slopes (짝애 돌석긴 자개등)? Can these feet wander through mountains bristling with tree stumps? It's definitely a problem. Also, can this back bear an A-frame carrier, can these shoulders carry tools slung over them? It's definitely a problem. That aside, can this mouth eat coarse millet rice (맨조밥)? Can this mouth eat rough bean rice (강낭밥)? It's definitely a problem. They say, "There's no one who won't fit if you force them," so when push comes to shove, nothing is impossible, but still, it's very difficult to assert definitively. This must be the <95> reason why modern youth talk about "Returning to the Farm (農村歸)" but fail to execute it (following 11 characters illegible). It must certainly be so. Is this all there is to it!
That's the thought while sitting in the city; what about the thought when actually in the farming village, which is quite the opposite? The psychology of yearning for the city while sitting in the farming village must be considerable. I deeply sympathize with the anguish of rural youth; moreover, what can a city-sick person do? After just two days in the farming village, one would likely shout, "Ah, it's so lonely! Damn it all! Let's go back!" and throw down the hoe handle. No matter how I think about it, it seems plausible.
Ah—I miss the farming village. But what can I do? My heart just feels heavy!

Humans driven out by bedbugs
Humans call themselves the lords of creation. It sounds like the arrogant talk of a madman. Indeed. They are driven out by tiny bedbugs and, unable to defeat minuscule mosquitoes, slap their own cheeks. Isn't it truly laughable? Of course, this isn't the entire tragedy of human life, but to call oneself the Lord (長) of all things and then surrender one's lair (巢穴) to bedbugs and flee—isn't it ridiculous the more you think about it?
The bedbugs occupied my lair ten days ago. Without a single gunshot heard, without a single sword flashed, they just stealthily, without notice (but after fighting me), occupied it.
I tried to resist them with a hundred stratagems (百計). Sticking out my ring finger (to squash them?), I fought them all night long. Not just me, but my wife joined forces to resist. The child, unable to endure their fierce attack (猛襲), whimpered and cried. We stayed up all night without a wink of sleep, fighting hard until our eyes were stiff. When one finger wasn't enough, we tried defending with two fingers. Still no good, so now we brought in a chamber pot and tried sweeping them into it with a broom. Still no use. Now we tried attacking with fire (火攻). Still no use. Now we tried using poison (毒藥). Still no use. Truly, they are a fierce and tenacious strong army. How were so many suicide squads organized? It vaguely reminds me of the scene when the Japanese captured Port Arthur (旅順陷落).
The rascals hide during the day. Around 8 or 9 PM, the sentries deploy. Around midnight, they launch a general offensive. Rascals crossing the threshold, emerging from closets, crawling on walls, dropping from the ceiling—truly, the whole place was filled with the bedbug army (滿山遍野가 都是빈대군이엿다). "Stick out your finger!" "Turn on the light!" "Bring the chamber pot!" "Sprinkle the poison!" Amidst all the commotion, these rascals are foolish yet vicious, small yet fast, appearing sporadically but numerous, and furthermore, disregarding life and death (生死를 不關), they attack from all sides, shouting, "Die! For the sake of eating,<96> dying is fine!" (前者仆後者* - as the front ones fall, the ones behind follow). What could we do? Eventually, saying, "We can't help it. Human strength cannot prevail. Let's go out. Let's just set fire to it and leave," we led the child out onto the wooden porch (maru). After several days of war, the room was entirely blood-colored, and the smell of poison stung the nose in every corner.
How could the porch belong to the defeated? We were humans beginning to lose, and the bedbugs were beginning to advance. Riding the momentum of victory, they captured even the porch. Finally, loitering in the yard, looking up at the vast sky, lamenting, "Ah, pitiful humans! Humans driven out by bedbugs!" we began living outdoors (露天生活).
How pitiful! The driven-out humans pitifully weep again under the fierce attack (猛襲) of mosquitoes (蚊)! Unable to sleep a wink, they slap their own shins with their own hands, strike their own cheeks with their own hands! Eventually, some swell up all over, and some fall ill with malaria (瘧疾) and groan! Ah, pitiful! These are the so-called lords of creation, humans. Poor humans. Never mind the whole of Korea (朝鮮), even here in Seoul, how many pitiful, poor humans are driven out like this by mosquitoes and bedbugs?

This and that scene of the Night Market
This was the human world. Humans fighting for life (生). Humans who cannot live otherwise. Humans who must necessarily do this to obtain life. Just as bedbugs or mosquitoes come out and fight to the death when the time comes, it seems humans too have a time to fight for life.
The sun has set. The electric lights are on. It's around 7 PM. From here and there, human creatures intending to compete for life emerge. They swarm onto the south side street of Chongno (鐘路) like a swarm of bees. Ice water (永水) sellers, fruit sellers, vegetable sellers, candy sellers, fan sellers, beaded curtain (珠簾) sellers, sock sellers, shoe sellers, soap sellers, cloth sellers, clothing sellers—all sorts of miscellaneous goods sellers come out. To be honest, it seems everything is there except virgin's testicles and cat's horns. Human life seems truly complex. It seems one cannot live without those things.
They start shouting. "Cheap! Cheap! Three for 10 jeon! If you don't buy now, you can't buy!" "Thirsty folks, come on in! One cup for 1 jeon!" "Good stuff! Good stuff! Cheap is good! Big or small, 10 jeon each!" ...and so on, they clamor loudly like toads (엉머구리) croaking during the July rainy season. The one whose eyes bulge to get even one more penny, the one who haggles desperately to save even one penny, the one who wants the goods but whose pockets are empty (空) and just swallows saliva, the one whose animal heart (獸心) flares up and gets caught stealing someone else's<97> goods and gets slapped, all sorts of folks abound. There's the prostitute who eyes a man and wags her tail, the fellow following a woman like a dog in heat, drooling, and the country bumpkin grabbing someone's coattails and stumbling around. Truly, it's a spectacular world (可觀의 世界). This is a scene from human life. Seeing this, the heart of the Western-suited gentleman, pretending to be influential (有志) and respectable (點잖은), folding his arms behind his back and looking smug (홍글홍글), is even more disgusting to behold. The black spots and red spots (of corruption) seem more numerous than the night market crowd. If you were to expose that fellow's inner workings, it would surely be more complex than the night market.
Ah—this is the human world. The one carrying a candy tray (엿목판) is human, and the one selling cloth (布木) is human. The one advertising medicine is human, and the one selling ice water (永水) is human. The one who pays 5 jeon for a bowl of cheap noodles (되국수), wipes his mouth, and turns away is human, and the one who pays 4 or 5 won for a mosquito net and carries it away is human. That's right. Humans are all the same. Though their occupations (業) and amount of money may differ, humans are one. Because of money, humans laugh and cry. The clamor like croaking toads in the night market is the sound of laughing and crying because of money. If a Saint (聖者) or a Great Transcendent Being (大超越者) looked down from above, it would be quite a laughable matter. But even they could not deny it.

Moonlit Night on the Han River (漢江)
The moon is the same moon. But the maiden's moon is different from the widow's moon. The maiden sees it and sings; the widow sees it and sighs. The bachelor's moon is also different from the poet's moon. The bachelor sees it and is indifferent; the poet sees it and is full of feeling (有心). So it seems human emotions differ respectively. Likewise, the river is also the same, but humans attach this color and that color to it. There are those who weep looking at the river, those who laugh looking at the river, those who feel uplifted looking at the river, and those who shrink back looking at the river. Extreme ones become wildly arrogant and jump about, while others, in utter despair, drown themselves.
I am neither a maiden nor a widow, neither a bachelor nor capable of being a poet. Therefore, I know neither how to sigh nor how to sing upon seeing the moon. Also, I know neither how to laugh nor how to weep upon seeing the river. In short, I am an unfeeling animal. However, depending on the circumstances, I do laugh and weep sometimes.
Because of this, the moonlit night (月夜) is not much of an issue for me, nor is the Han River (漢江) much of an issue. Perhaps because of that, though there have already been dozens of moonlit nights this year, I haven't once wept or laughed, and though the Han River this year has already received and sent tens of thousands of humans to the sea, I have<98> never had any connection with it.
Why engage in tedious talk, but because there are so many who laugh and cry seeing the moon, and so many who die and live seeing the river, I wonder if all ordinary humans are like that, or if only transcendent humans become so. Out of this thought, I speak a word of my feelings (所懷) and pass on.
Indeed. The moonlit night on the Han River is good, truly good. I haven't seen the Dongting Lake Moonlit Night (洞庭夜月) or the Pipa Moonlit Night (琵琶夜月), but I have seen the Taedong River Moonlit Night (大同夜月), the Red Cliff Moonlit Night (赤壁夜月), the Kŭmgang Mountain Moonlit Night (金剛夜月), and other moonlit nights throughout Korea. Of course, I've seen the Han River Moonlit Night hundreds of times.
The Han River Moonlit Night is the most bustling. This is because it has the backdrop of a great metropolis of 300,000 people. The Han River Moonlit Night is highly emotive (多感的). This is because it combines 500 years of wind and rain (風雨) with the present day. The Han River Moonlit Night is most affectionate (多情的). This is because day and night, it endlessly receives the sorrows (恨) of talented men and beautiful women (才子佳人). However, it is chilly.
There's a killing aura (殺氣). The prank of some sons of bitches shouting "Chotto omachi" (チョットオマッチ - Wait a moment) actually provokes the naturally serene young men and women.
But what great matter is that? Those who will die, die; those who will live, live; those who will sing, sing; those who will dance, dance. Just as the Han River flows long and tranquilly (天然自若하게 유유히) through myriad ages (億萬古), people too freely indulge in their own inclinations. What son of a bitch would tell a dying person not to die, and what bastard (후레자식) would tell a living person to die? Only freedom and self-determination exist.
The Han River Moonlit Night is truly fine. Better than the Taedong River Moonlit Night, better than the Amnok River Moonlit Night (鴨綠夜月). It doesn't have the feeling of facing a beautiful woman like the Taedong River Moonlit Night. It doesn't have the feeling of facing a strong man like the Amnok River Moonlit Night. But the Han River Moonlit Night offers much comfort from various aspects, like a mother's embrace. Looking up, it's as if the Milky Way (銀河) has fallen; looking down, it's as if white cloth (白布) has been spread. The smoke from the Ttukseom (뚝섬) water purification plant billows like a dragon ascending in the proverb. The lights on the slope of Sangdusan (象頭山 - Elephant Head Mountain) evoke the image of the lights resembling a Dokkaebi's fire (獨脚이 불). The sound of the train at Noryangjin (鷺梁津) roars like heavenly thunder (天雷) arriving. The villa of some fellow on the rocky peak (石頂) of Old Yongsan (舊龍山) looks quite like the abode of immortals (神仙). The sparse green pines (靑松) on Wa'usan (臥牛山 - Lying Cow Mountain), like the teeth of a comb (梳), seem distant yet near, appearing sparse (성글성글). Moreover, Pamsŏm (밤섬 - Chestnut Island, 栗島) is like a wine cup, and Yanghwado (楊花渡) is like a playground, while upstream and downstream, silver scales and jade rulers (銀鱗玉尺 - fish) leap vigorously. How wonderful it is! How fine must be the protagonist who appreciates this! Indeed. It is a fine Han River. A Han River Moonlit Night full of affection, emotion, and bustle.

Five Hours in the Hospital
Illness, that demon, is a monstrous, evil, unfathomable,** and indescribable (怪惡罔測**不可言) poison that doesn't understand human circumstances even one bit. It doesn't recognize the old, doesn't recognize the young,<99> doesn't recognize children. It doesn't know the rich, doesn't know the poor. It invades the cheeks of a beauty too precious to look at, and shamelessly (렴치도 좃케) attacks the buttocks of a child too precious to touch. To the one running with good energy, out of some malice, it attacks immediately, and to the one sleeping from exhaustion, out of some wicked mischief (惡戲), it disturbs them on the spot. If you eat a lot, it causes trouble saying you ate; if you don't eat, it causes worry saying you didn't eat. Illness is truly a monstrous, evil, unfathomable poison.
That rascal attacked an 8-year-old girl, attending school diligently, without a shred of consideration. Her ears throbbed, her head felt like bursting, she cried frantically—how could even iron or stone remain unmoved? We inevitably sought medical examination. Eventually, she had to be hospitalized and undergo surgery. Thus, as one of the guardians, I started visiting the hospital.
Five hours briefly seen in the hospital! It's not the human world. Crying sounds, groaning sounds, calls for mother, calls for the doctor—room after room, nothing but cries of suffering (呌苦聲). Patients with ear trouble, eye trouble, nose trouble, throat trouble, lung disease, stomach disease, limb diseases, skin diseases, reproductive organ diseases—countless kinds of patients filled the spacious hospital, its many sickrooms packed tightly.
It seems humans are born to suffer illness. If you remove the lesson (課) of illness from humanity, perhaps more than half of it becomes empty (空). It seems humans and illness have a predestined agreement (宿約) to accompany each other from birth. Not just inpatients, but countless millions—it's like that. Which person is free from that poison called illness? Examining my own body, seemingly sturdy and disease-free as a guardian of a sick child, I find more than one or two illnesses.
Perhaps illness is also one of the possessions worth having as a human. There seems to be nothing more to understand than that.

Alas! So-called Men of Influence (有志), So-called Dignity (體面)!
It's ridiculous. It's like dog's balls (a nonsensical situation). Who told me to act like a man of influence (有志) to cause this worry? Who told me to maintain dignity (體面) to cause this worry? Nobody asked me to, and I don't want to do it myself, so what is this? Why pretend to be influential when I'm not, why pretend to maintain dignity when I'm not? Truly—a laughable thing.
Summer has come. It's unbearably hot. If I had my way, I'd like to walk around stark naked. I wish I could go naked even in a crowd of ten thousand people (萬人叢). But I dare not. I went out onto the street without a hat, felt embarrassed, went back inside, put it on, and went out again. At night, I visited a neighbor's house wearing only a short jacket (동저고리). It was cool and simple, but not as comfortable as wearing a durumagi (두루막이 - traditional outer coat). In my heart, it feels like it would be most comfortable, but because of dignity (體面), I absolutely cannot do it. Looking at the summer clothes of the Chinese, even a cotton durumagi (洋木두루막) seems wearable for a Korean, but because of dignity, one cannot help but wear a ramie durumagi (모시두루막). For the Japanese, going barefoot is commonplace (例常事), but a Korean cannot take even one step barefoot. Now—could there be another such pitiful prisoner (拘囚者) of dignity? **Summary**

The author, Ch'unp'a, critiques the state of Seoul in the summer of 1925, portraying its inhabitants as "ghosts" living inexplicably well compared to struggling rural folk, sustained by unseen, likely exploitative or morally dubious means. He contrasts this with an idealized, nostalgic longing for the simple, virtuous life of the countryside, while acknowledging his own urbanized weakness prevents a return. The piece vividly describes the misery of summer heat compounded by pests like bedbugs and mosquitoes (driving people from their homes), the chaotic energy and desperation of night markets, and offers reflections on the Han River's moonlit beauty and the pervasive nature of illness observed during a hospital visit. The author concludes by mocking the social constraints of "dignity" (체면) and "influence" (유지) that prevent even simple comforts like dressing lightly in the heat. From a historical perspective, the article offers a stark social commentary on the growing urban-rural divide in colonial Korea, the perceived moral decay and economic parasitism of the city (Seoul), and the alienation felt by urban intellectuals disconnected from rural roots. Historians might find the raw critique of Seoul society, the detailed descriptions of everyday hardships (poverty, pests, illness), and the complex feelings about modernity versus tradition particularly insightful regarding the social and psychological landscape of 1920s Korea under Japanese rule.

녀름과 人間의 裏面
						春坡
서울 놈들은 모다 귀신=시골이 그립다=모기빈대에 쫏겨나는 人間들=夜市의 이꼴저꼴=月夜의 漢江行=病院 內에서 五時間=噫! 所謂 有志·所謂 體面!

서울 놈들은 모다 귀신
서울 놈들은 모다 귀신갓다. 高臺廣室에 놉히 잣빠저 잇는 놈도 귀신갓고 一間草屋에 기여들고 기여나는 놈도 귀신갓고 양복쟁이 하이카라 놈들도 귀신갓고 짬자리(蜻蜓) 짓가튼 치마에 진암닭 거름하는 년들도 귀신갓고 신경이 밧귀엿는지 눈깔이 뒤집혓는지 모르나 서울에 잇는 거지놈들까지 귀신갓다. 그는 고사하고 독수리색기 까치새기 참새색기 쥐색기까지 모다 귀신갓다. 서울 것들은 통트러 귀신가티 보인다. 하구 만흔 그 날에 하구 만흔 그 놈들이 무엇을 해서 그와 가티 먹고 그와 가티 입고 그와 가티 나와 덤비는지 아무리 생각해도 귀신가튼 놈들이다. 더구나 서울녀편네들이란 귀신 중에도 웃귀신갓다. 까치색기 참새색기도 여간한 귀신은 아니다.
자- 보라. 서울 놈의 눈으로 보지 말고 시골놈의 눈으로 더구나 농촌의 노농의 눈으로 보아 보라. 모다 귀신이 안인가를-. 단바람 『야- 요놈들 봐라. 맹랑한 놈들 귀신 다- 되얏구나!』하고 혀(舌)를 빼물지 안나-.
이놈은 귀족이닛가 미리 만히 착취해 둔 것으로 아직 배퉁이를 쓸고 잇지만, 이놈은 관리이닛가 월급 타고 상여금 타서 제법 살아가지만, 이놈은 은행원이닛가 그대로 산다치고, 이놈은 교육가이닛가 굼지는 안을테고, 이놈은 회사원, 이놈은 여관업, 이놈은 직공, 이놈은 운전수, 이놈은 인력거군, 이놈은 지겟군, 이놈은 술장사, 이놈은 蝎甫장사, 이놈은 미장이, 이놈은 머슴, - 이런 놈들은 다만 한 분이라도 그래도 수입이 잇스닛가 그렁저렁 산다＜93＞ 하지만 (그래도 귀신은 귀신) 그밧게 아무것도 하지 안는 놈들, 펀둥펀둥 노는 놈들, 그 놈들은 무엇을 먹고 살아 가는지? 아무리해도 귀신가튼 놈들이다.
시골 놈들은 남녀노소가 1년 열두달 3백예순날에 하루도 쉴새 업시 피땀을 흘려도 麥嶺을 못 넘겨 헐덕어리고 7궁을 못 넘어 주저안고 급기야 수백수천의 餓死者를 내이는데 서울 놈들은 엇던 셈인지 자녀란 제법 학교에 보내고 게집이란 안방에서 발장단치게 하고 식모니 針母니 행랑이니 덧부티 인간을 加外로 두고도 그래도 능히 살아가니 웬 셈일가. 살으되 호의호식이것다. 길거리에 나와 다니는 것을 보면 모다 말숙말숙 빼엿것다.
자- 이것이 귀신안이고 무엇이냐. 엇던 놈의 집에서는 공장에 다니며 한 달에 25원을 간신히 버러 온다는데 다섯 식구가 제법 집쓰고 밥먹고 옷입고 살아가니 이게 귀신안이고 무어냐. 옷을 입으되 모다 말숙하게 빼고 나선다. 그래도 그놈이 竊盜나 强盜罪로 걸렷다는 말은 못 드럿다.
이것은 25원일 망정 수입은 수입이다. 一分半厘 수입업는 놈이 不知 幾 1,000명인데 역시 강도절도도 안이요 아사도 안이고 능히 살아온다. 자- 요것이 귀신아니고 무어냐?
엇잿든 서울 놈들은 귀신이다. 양반 상놈 부자 가난뱅이 산애 녀편네 有職者 無職者 간에 귀신이다. 사람놈들은 말도 말고 까치색기 참새색기도 귀신이다. 무엇들을 먹고 사는지? 그러나 그 이면을 드러가 보아라. 얼마나 더럽고 비린내 나고 악착하고, 중글중글한가를-. 똥거위를 相奪하는 병아리 색기들 꼴이 그 곳에 잇고 개뼉다귀를 노코 서로 으르렁거리는 강아지꼴들이 그 곳에 잇다.
「30호만 모야 부트면 서로 뜨더 먹고 산다」는 말이 잇것다. 서울이 적으나마 5만호 이상이 잇스니 서로 뜨더 먹고 살기에는 넉넉하다. 이빨과 손톱만 잇스면 죽지는 안을 것이다.
「서울 살려면 도적놈의 심장을 가저야 된다」는 말도 잇다. 안인게 아니라 서울 사는 놈치고 도적놈의 심장 안가진 놈이 어대 잇스리. 窃强盜의 현행범만 엇지 도적이랴.
그만한 일을 하지 안코 그만한 살림을 하는 것은 엇던 방법으로 취햇든지 도적질은 분명한 도적질일 것이다. 남을 속엿든지 위협햇든지 남을 꾀엿든지 업엇든지 무슨 방법이엿든지 그는 도적놈이다. 도적질 잘하는 것도 亦 귀신이란 말이다.
시골 놈들이 생산자들이 서울놈은＜94＞ 안이 도회처에 잇는 놈들은 귀신인 줄을 알어다고 그리고 귀신이란 말만 하지말고 그 귀신들에 대한 무슨 대책을 생각해 보라. 아마도 가만 잇지는 못하리라. 한편 생각하면 귀신 안인 시골 놈들이 병신이니라.

시골이 그립다
시골이 그립다. 山明水麗한 시골이 그립다. 상하평의 재(灰)빗 가튼 곡식빗이 보고 십다. 가래 멘 아버지와 호미 들은 어머니가 보고 십다. 꼴 비는 형님과 소맥이는 동생이 보고 십다. 밥광주리 인 아즈머니도 보고 십고 淸溪水 한 박아지 떠들고 논드렁길로 種種 거름치는 제수님도 보고 십다. 10리 長堤에 마음대로 뛰는 송아지도 보고 십다. 뽕나무 아레 쭈그리고 안저 콩콩짓는 강아지도 보고 십다. 호박넝쿨 밋헤서 「피피」하는 軟鷄숫병아리도 보고 십고 우리에서 「꼴꿀」하는 도야지도 보고 십다. 「뚬북뚬북」하는 압논의 뚬북이 소리도 듯고 십고 훨훨 나는 뒤ㅅ들의 白鷺도 보고 십다. 그 중에도 한머니가 손수 구으신 말불알 가튼 甘藷가 먹고 십고 어린 누나가 손수 논도랑에서 뜨더온 냄새 훌늉한 미나리 짠지가 먹고 십다.
아- 무엇이 그립지 안으랴. 시골의 아기자기한 진경을 생각하매 심신이 勇躍하야 그 엇질 바를 모르겟다. 아- 몹시도 그리워라. 내 어이하야 紅塵萬丈의 京城에서 되지 못하게 모기와 빈대와 싸우는고.
농촌이 그립다. 그러나 어이할고. 자연의 미가 좃치 안은 바 안이요. 농우들이 반갑지 안은 바 아니지만 도회에 병드른 안이꼬운 時俗에 병드른 이 유약하고 浮虛한 이 몸으로 어이하면 좃탄 말이냐. 농촌을 찬미하고 자연을 노래하기야 누가 못 하랴. 시골이 그리우면 엇엇탄 말이냐.  아- 생각이 이에 밋츠매 무엇을 일흔 듯 섭섭도 하구나!
이 손으로 능히 호미자루를 잡을가? 이 손으로 능히 똥을 주물넉 거릴가. 이 손으로 능히 가시덤불을 휘여 잡을가? 암만해도 문제다. 또는 이 발로 능히 진흙논을 밟을가? 이 발로 능히 짝애 돌석긴 자개등을 밟을가? 이 발로 능히 나무그루 뽀죽뽀죽한 산을 헤매일가? 암만해도 문제다. 또는 이 등에다 능히 지게를 지고 이 억개에다 능히 연장을 둘너 메일가? 암만해도 문제다. 그는 고사하고 이 입으로 능히 고실고실한 맨조밥을 먹으며 이 입으로 능히 거츨거츨한 강낭밥을 먹을 수 잇슬가? 암만해도 문제다. 「달고 치면 안 맛는 놈 업다」고 급기야 당하고 보면 못할 것은 업지만 그래도 단언하기는 매우 어렵다. 요것이 현대청년이 「農村歸」을 말하면서 실행못하는 (이하 11자 판독불능)＜95＞ 이유것다. 분명히 그럿컷다. 어대 이 뿐이랴!
도회에 안저서의 생각이 그럿치 정말 농촌에 가서의 생각은 또한 정반대인데야 엇지하랴. 농촌에 안저서 도회를 동경하는 심리인들 여간하랴. 농촌청년의 번민을 그윽히 짐작하나니 况都會病者로서 어이 할게냐. 농촌의 불과 2일에 「에이 고적이야! 경칠 것들. 우 가자!」하고 호미ㅅ자루를 집어 던질 것이다. 아무리 생각해도 그럴 듯히만 생각키운다.
