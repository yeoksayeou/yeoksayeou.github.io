Journey to Southern Manchuria
						Yi Tonhwa

From Hŭnggyŏng to Wangch'ŏngmun

During my four-day stay in Hŭnggyŏng, there were three lectures and three welcoming banquets hosted by the Hŭnggyŏng compatriots. Within Hŭnggyŏng county itself, there aren't many of our compatriots, only about 90 households, but in the surrounding villages, several thousand households are attached. Accordingly, there are two rice mills run by our compatriots, as well as shops and restaurants that have emerged. Regarding religion, there is Christianity, and a Ch'ŏndogyo center (종리원) has recently been established and shows great promise for development. Though I received much gratitude, including invitations from the Hŭnggyŏng citizens and the Hŭnggyŏng Youth Association, due to various circumstances, I cannot list names individually. However, just by saying "Southern Manchurian compatriots," I received a welcome that will be an eternally unforgettable commemoration. This sentiment applies to all of Southern Manchuria. It was the same in Wangch'ŏngmun, the same in Samwŏnp'o, and Fushun and Pongch'ŏn were all alike. The reason my brethren welcomed me so is not for any other reason. It is to express the bond of compatriots, one who deliberately sought them out from the homeland (內地). It is not a welcome for me, but a welcome for the compatriots in the homeland. It stems from the sacred emotion of loving the motherland. It is not the sentiment of meeting an old acquaintance in a faraway land thousands of *ri* away, but rather born from the sentiment of longing for the motherland in a foreign country tens of thousands of *ri* away. Truly, this welcome contained various deeply moving meanings. Although most of the compatriots were farmers who had entered seeking sustenance, once they went to a foreign land, the feeling of "my country" developed exceptionally strongly. Furthermore, there were not a few brethren who crossed the Amnok River far away, embracing the eternal land with purpose, and within their speech and songs, most harbor the spirit of Yan and Zhao's righteous indignation (燕趙慷慨), like "The wind whistles mournfully, the Yi River is cold" (風蕭蕭兮易水寒). This describes the sentiment of the welcome extended to me by the entire Manchurian compatriot community.<89>

I entered Wangch'ŏngmun. I arrived at dusk on June 1st. About a hundred young children wearing Chinese student uniforms came out to meet me as far as ten *ri* away. Greeted by these younger siblings speaking Korean in Chinese clothes, an indescribable sorrow welled up within me, and without a word, I entered the Korean village of Wangch'ŏngmun. Wangch'ŏngmun is a place with the longest history of compatriot migration. It is said that 20 years ago from now, a brother named Chŏn Ch'inhwa (田秦化) first came here, bought land, and started making paddy fields (水田), which became the beginning (嚆矢) of Southern Manchuria's paddy field history. In this Korean village, there is much privately owned land by Koreans, and thus they have formed a proper Korean settlement, living with the scent of Korean customs in Korean-style houses, differing little from the Korean homeland (內地). The next day, a lecture was held, hosted by the local Korean school, Tongmyŏng School. There were even compatriots who deliberately came from 10 or 20 *ri* away just to hear the lecture.

From Wangch'ŏngmun to Samwŏnp'o

From here, I gradually entered the hinterland. As I actually proceeded, the thing requiring the utmost caution was the rumor of bandits (馬賊, majŏk). As mentioned before, I heard a rumor on the day I entered Hŭnggyŏng that bandits had captured a wealthy Chinese man, and on the day I entered Wangch'ŏngmun, I heard bandits had captured a student from a wealthy family there. Generally, the bandits in Manchuria are quite audacious fellows. They brazenly wear government troop uniforms and enter towns, and if they encounter a wealthy person they had previously scouted, they pull out a six-shooter (六穴炮) and abduct them through threats. Once captured by bandits, one must inevitably pay money. If the money is not sent by the deadline, they first cut off an ear and send it to the family, and if it's still not paid, they next cut off an arm or leg and send it, they say. It is an extremely atrocious punishment. However, one comforting fact is that bandits inflict no harm upon Koreans. This isn't because they view Koreans kindly, but because they know Koreans have no money. Being penniless is fortunate when it comes to bandits. What Koreans fear is not bandits, but government troops (관군), it is said. These characters called government troops are mostly not proper soldiers drafted from the law-abiding populace, but rather captured bandits, so these types, regardless of the amount of money, will inflict harm if they find any excuse, calling it "tturitan" (뚤이탄). Asking about the bandit rumors at every step, I departed from Wangch'ŏngmun. A day's walk from Wangch'ŏngmun, I entered a deep mountain valley. Since it was much more convenient to sleep <90> at the house of a Korean compatriot, I asked farmers along the way and sought out a Korean farmhouse. It was a desolate farming village of two or three houses, with rice paddies in front and mountains behind. Thanks to the efforts of my traveling companions, we found the village head (百家長), bought a yellow hen (黃鷄), made side dishes of *moktuchae* (木頭菜, likely a type of wild vegetable) and wild vegetable soup, and ate deliciously. Amidst the sound of frogs croaking, I found a night's rest. Asking about the host's situation, the daughter of a man from Ŭiju, P'yŏngan Pukto, was married to the son of a man from Yŏngch'ŏn, Kyŏngsang Pukto, and they were living cozily with the elderly mother-in-law as the head of the household. Three days after leaving Wangch'ŏngmun, I reached Samwŏnp'o.

I have arrived at Samwŏnp'o. This place belongs to Yuhahyŏn county. When I hear the name Samwŏnp'o, a strange sentiment and a tense feeling arise. This is for no other reason than that the Southern Manchurian self-governance headquarters is located somewhere in that vicinity. I will refer to this using the nickname "society" (사회). I repeat what I wrote last month. There is only one society. Whereas until last year various groups were in contention, now they have completely unified, becoming a fully unified organization. Having come this far, how could I not see the Southern Manchurian unified organization, the society? With this firm resolve, I sought by all means to meet them. It was the day after arriving in Samwŏnp'o. I sought out a certain small village. There was a small mountain in the middle, and surrounding the mountain, Korean houses stood neatly here and there, two or three, five or six in rows; amidst them, the society's institution was established. Since it is as I described last month, there is no need for repetition, but I will write down just one concrete fact.
Originally, regarding the Southern Manchurian self-governance system, until before the March First Movement, life was proceeding interestingly. However, when the March First Movement erupted, a Great Subjugation occurred. Following that, swindlers, gamblers, opium dealers, and similar riffraff who had been scattered throughout the Korean homeland or various cities in Southern Manchuria took the opportunity and poured in. Thus, considerable harm was inflicted upon the pure farmers. Consequently, negative reports about Southern Manchuria spread within the Korean homeland. The phrase "compatriots fighting amongst themselves" (同胞相爭) also arose from this, and various sayings like "people with money cannot live there," "officials cannot be accommodated," all emerged accordingly. Therefore, the Southern Manchurian self-governance system exerted immense effort to eradicate them. Now, everything has become safe, the residents have formed a self-governing society, and the society is diligently focusing its efforts on the advancement and development of the residents, it is said.
One of the economic policies implemented by the society for the residents, the communal farming system (공농제, kongnongje), is as follows.
The communal farming system, similar to the well-field system (정전법, chŏngjŏnpŏp) of the ancient Zhou dynasty (周나라), requires a village of 100 households, or several tens of households combined, to manage one day's ploughing worth of communal land (공전, kongjŏn). The grain from the communal land cultivated jointly by these several tens of households is then taken charge of by the society for handling, it is said. Its management <91> policy is for the society to use it as a public financial institution for the farmers. Generally, the most suffocating issue for farmers living in Manchuria is China's high-interest loan business. No, the high-interest grain lenders. If they lend about one *tu* (두, a unit of volume) to a farmer during the lean spring season, they demand four or five times that amount of grain back in the autumn, it is said. Therefore, to prevent this harm, the society first established the communal farming system last year, and in its first year, it achieved considerable effect, yielding a profit of over 10,000 *wŏn*, they say.
Next is the Household Chicken System (戶鷄制, Hogyeje). The Household Chicken System requires each household to contribute one chicken (鷄), and the money is collected over several years to create a shareholding system for the permanent purchase (永買) of Chinese-owned land, planning for the permanent settlement (永居) of Koreans.
This is the work of the Livelihood Department (生計部). Next, there are the affairs of the Administrative Department (행정부), the Transportation Department (교통부), the Education Department (학무부), the Foreign Affairs Department (외교부), and many other noteworthy matters, but due to circumstances, these are omitted.
Among social movements, the youth movement is also noteworthy. Firstly, the Hanjok Labor Party (한족노동당) was established, with over 400 promoters, currently about 1,500 members, and its purpose is stated as "to enlighten the laboring masses and strive for a new life."
Next is the Tamŭl Tang Youth Association (담을 당청년회) in Samwŏnp'o. It too operates in parallel with the Hanjok Labor Party, pursuing the same aims and objectives with vigorous activity.
In a word, Samwŏnp'o can be called the place holding the central power of Southern Manchurian Koreans. From what I have seen, there are several points that inspire confidence in its bright future prospects, such as, as mentioned above, the fact that the residents trust the society and the society skillfully guides the residents, or that the leaders within the society possess an air of magnanimity and maturity (寬厚長者), or that the entire resident population is honest and simple (純厚質朴), completely lacking urban deceitfulness. All these truly seem to show the vigor of a new beginning.
From the day I arrived in Samwŏnp'o, there was a large athletic meet there, and several thousand Korean and Chinese students concluded the meet in a harmonious atmosphere (和氣融融). That I also had the opportunity to give a brief greeting lecture utilizing the athletic meet is something I can never forget.
The earnest farewell from the society elders when I left Samwŏnp'o, and the various requests entrusted to me by different organizations over three days, are still unforgettable, and only silent tears (暗淚) well up in my eyes. Two days from Samwŏnp'o, I arrived at a large market town in Haeryonghyŏn called Puksŏngsanja, where a lecture was held hosted by the local Christian church. Four days from Puksŏngsanja, I came out to Kae'wŏn station, and upon arriving in Pongch'ŏn, I received much gratitude and welcome from the elders at the Pongch'ŏn Youth Association and the Chosŏn Ilbo and Kaebyŏk branch offices. There is nothing more I can say in reply, but merely offer my greetings first through this brief paper, and I intend to report on the situation in Southern Manchuria to our compatriots as much as possible when the opportunity arises later. <92>

**Summary**

Yi Tonhwa recounts his journey through Southern Manchuria, starting from Hŭnggyŏng, passing through Wangch'ŏngmun, and reaching Samwŏnp'o before heading towards Pongch'ŏn. He describes the warm, emotional welcome received from Korean compatriots, driven by their longing for the homeland and nationalist sentiment. He notes the establishment of Korean communities with farms, businesses, and schools, particularly highlighting Wangch'ŏngmun's long history and Samwŏnp'o's role as a center for a unified Korean self-governing body ("society"). He details the dangers of bandits (majŏk) and corrupt government troops (kwan'gun), while also describing the "society's" efforts to protect farmers from predatory lending via communal farming (kongnongje) and land acquisition plans (hogyeje). The piece reflects the strong sense of identity and community organization among Korean migrants in Manchuria during this period. Historians might find the detailed descriptions of the self-governing body's structure (mentioning specific departments) and economic policies (kongnongje, hogyeje), the expressed nationalist sentiments tied to leaving Korea, and the distinction drawn between the threat of bandits versus government troops particularly valuable for understanding the Korean diaspora's life and political consciousness under colonial rule.

三源浦를 다 왓습니다. 이 곳은 柳河縣 소속이외다. 三源浦라는 말을 드를 때에 이상한 감상과 긴장한 기분이 돔니다. 그것은 다른 연고가 안이라 南滿洲 자치본부가 그 곳 어느 부근에 잇는 연고입니다. 이것을 「사회」라고 별명을 지어 적으려 합니다. 먼저 달에 쓴말을 다시 거둡하게 됨니다. 사회는 다만 하나입니다. 작년까지도 여러 단체가 논위여 잇든 것을 지금와서는 아조 통일이 되야 가지고 완전한 통일기관이 된 것입니다. 일부러 이 곳까지 왓다가 南滿洲 통일기관인 사회구경을 못해서야 될 수가 잇나 하는 구둔 결심을 가지고 아모조록 그들을 맛나보려 한 것이외다. 三源浦에 도달하든 이튼날입니다. 엇든 조고만 한 촌락을 차자 갓슴니다. 가운데 조고마한 산이 잇고 산을 둘너 朝鮮가옥이 보기에도 정적하게 하나 둘 兩三 五六이 나라니 하야 잇는 그 가운데 사회기관이 백혀 잇슴니다. 먼젓달에 말한 바와 가트니까 다시 중첩할 필요가 업고 다만 구체적 사실 한 가지를 젹어 보겟슴니다.
