無産者의 詩
						曉路 譯

㝢話
에드윈씨 司作

A spotted cow calmly
Sniffs at “Yesterday’s fertilizer,”
Startled by the sound of “The Permanence of all things,”
It flicks its tail, reflects quietly,
Turns its head and stares blankly at the mountain.

Meanwhile, from somewhere,
A flock of sparrows flies down
And argues noisily,
Chirping while selecting tasty morsels.

“It’s true, it’s true! It’s true!”
The goose (鵞鳥) honks,
Waddling along the dirty warehouse floor,
Stumbling as it goes.
(Translator's Note-) “Yesterday’s fertilizer” is a translation of ‘The dung of yesterday,’ mocking the staleness of capitalism. “Yesterday” refers to old ideas, and “fertilizer” should be translated as dung, meaning the dirty and unjust things arising from untrue ideas. “The Permanence of all things” is a translation of ‘Permanency of all things,’ metaphorically meaning stubborn ideas, humorously satirizing the permanence of spiritual – or intellectual aspects – philosophy. Also, in the third stanza, the goose going to the dirty warehouse floor shouting “It’s true, it’s true!” should be imagined as going for food like the sparrows. It means that truth cannot be separated from the act of living.

We Have a Job to Do
By Henry George Weiss

Even if hell is our room and stone our bed, we cannot rest. We are the vanguard of the host.
We cannot rest. <117>
Let us swear to paint the U.S.A. red.”
The naked will be clothed, the hungry will eat.
Sweep away the parasites, cast off the rich.
Like us – you hear that sound now –
That sound says,
The laborer shall wear a crown.
Ah! You grind your teeth! It’s no use frowning.
It’s no use if your armies oppress us.
It’s no use if your laws curse us.
Behold! For us, the vanguard of labor,
Will tear down your system and set your slaves free.
To hell with you and all your power! We cannot rest.

We surely have a job to do.—
Tired children call us to work.
Babies who haven’t seen the sun plead.—
We promised them what they ask for.
We have a job to do— to strip off the mask
Of your cursed democracy,
And drive you out from the comfort
Where you gorge on stolen glory.
Day and night, we labor without rest—
Foolish you, finding pleasure in laughing at, insulting, and mocking us!
This day is your day, ruling us with the whip!
But go wear your purple robes and look for gods (신).
Tomorrow, we will sweep you from this earth.

(Translator's Note- U.S.A. refers to America (米國). The phrase “Wear purple robes and look for gods” means since there will be nothing to do when you are driven out, just wear fancy clothes and indulge in splendid fantasies. The word “you” refers to the tyrannical capitalist class. Painting the U.S.A. red means the author, being American, intends to make America communist (赤化).)

Looking at the Waning Moon
By Walter Bynner

The small and pitiful old moon <118>
Goes high in the sky,
And soon shrinks again.
After a moment,
Like people who grow weak and cannot become new again,
Like you...
But no.
I would rather be human and go truly!
Old moon!

To a Man Sleeping in the Subway Car
By Heskel Beck

Man, dirty to look at,
You, painful to behold,
Are sleeping in this train
That shakes your dirty body
Through the city’s Black Veins (黑胍).
Your sleep is a heavy stupor.
Your sleep is a death known while alive.
Your sleep is the sleep of the masses.

Man, you dirty one,
Are sleeping here where the eyes of everyone
In this crowded train violate you.
Everyone’s lips are drawing words mocking you
On the waste paper of their hidden hearts.
You must know the pitiful smile
Twitching at the corners of their mouths.

Man, you dirty one,
So deeply asleep,
Without the slightest guard in your heart.
Your face, more than an ox’s face,
Cries out the “Silence of the Clod” (土塊의 沈黙).
In your eyes and mouth remains a heavy “Stupidity” (愚鈍)
That only speechless beasts (즘생) know. <119>

Man, you dirty one.
You, so deeply asleep,
Are sleeping the sleep of the masses.
I shall shake your weak shoulders
With strong hands of rebellion.
I will wake you.

And cry out—

Can’t you hear the soft, lovely voice
Of one whose lips are pressed to the earth above this dark underground?
You will hear it.

And cry out again—

Can’t you hear the footsteps of the swift wind
Running down the sun-drenched road above your head?
You will follow it.

And cry out again—

Good god! Man! Wake up.
You are dirty.
So deeply asleep.
Sleeping the sleep of the masses.

(Translator's Note- The phrase “Your face, more than an ox’s face, cries out the Silence of the Clod” could not be idiomatically translated in short form, so I had no choice but to translate it literally. That is, it describes the worker’s face. A silent clod looks like an ox’s face. But the worker’s face is even more unpleasant to look at, like a silent clod. The description “stupidity that only speechless beasts know” is used for a stronger, more impressionistic depiction of stupidity, meaning “only speechless beasts know.” It implies they seem that stupid. This stupidity, referring to the unawakened, sleeping worker, is on one hand satirical, and on the other hand, has a stimulating meaning that must be considered. The term Black Veins (黑胍) refers to the dark underground passage. I intend to correct any errors in this translation later when I see the opportunity.
- End - <120>

**Summary**

This article presents Korean translations by Hyoro (曉路) of four poems by Western authors (Edwin Markham, Henry George Weiss, Walter Bynner, Heskel Beck), all themed around proletarian consciousness and critique of the existing social order. The poems use allegory (Markham), direct revolutionary calls (Weiss), introspective comparison (Bynner), and evocative imagery of the downtrodden masses (Beck) to convey messages against capitalism, complacency, and oppression, urging awakening and action. From a historical perspective, the publication of these translations in *Kaebyŏk* (개벽), a prominent Korean intellectual magazine during the colonial period, is significant. It reflects the influx and reception of socialist and anarchist ideas among Korean intellectuals in the 1920s, offering a translated medium to express dissent and critique contemporary social conditions, albeit indirectly through foreign works, under Japanese censorship. The translator's notes explicitly link the poems' themes to anti-capitalism and revolution (e.g., "painting the U.S.A. red," "awakening the masses"), showing a clear intent to apply these ideas to the readers' context.
