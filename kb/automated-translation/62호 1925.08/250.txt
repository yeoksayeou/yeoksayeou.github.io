To Me - Give Me the Paradise of Reflection-
						Cho Myŏnghŭi

Give me freedom.
I will just become livestock.
And so, I will stumble and struggle upon this wide earth.

Give me food.
I will just become a pig.
And so, I will plunge headfirst and wail beneath this sunlight.
-1925, 1, 7 -

Family of Three

Young Daughter: "Father, today at school, some well-dressed child called me a beggar because I wore a torn skirt and tore my clothes<115> more. I'm never going back to school wearing these clothes."
Father: "Be quiet. Listen to the sound of those wild geese. It's deep autumn!"
Wife: "Hunger (口腹) is the enemy! You must have told lies again and borrowed rice to make dinner. Committing sins in your heart..."
Husband: "My dear. Who knows if there will be a time when our skulls roll down into the rapids where the grandchild of that wild goose's grandchild sits.
And after that, what will become of those skulls?
And where will those geese go, and what will become of them?...
I am a pitiful person, but you are also a pitiful person.
But just know that what we say is not meaningless talk."<116>
<115-116>

**Summary**

The text consists of a short, despairing poem and a short dialogue depicting a poverty-stricken family. The poem expresses a yearning for freedom or basic sustenance, even at the cost of humanity, wishing to become livestock or a pig. The dialogue highlights the family's destitution – the daughter shamed at school for her torn clothes, the wife lamenting the necessity of deceit to obtain food. The father responds with existential musings, contemplating the insignificance and transience of human life against the backdrop of nature (wild geese), suggesting a shared suffering but hinting that their words, born from hardship, hold some meaning. From a historical perspective, the piece reflects the deep poverty and sense of hopelessness prevalent in 1920s colonial Korea. Historians might find the raw depiction of economic hardship, the resulting psychological burden (shame, deceit), and the turn towards existential resignation or dark desires interesting as reflections of the social conditions and emotional responses under Japanese rule.

세식구
어린 딸. 「아버지, 오날 학교에서 엇던 옷 잘입은 아이가 날더러 떠러진 치마 입엇다고 거지라고 욕을 하며 옷을＜115＞ 찌저 노켓지. 나는 이 옷을 입고 다시는 학교에 안 갈터이야.」
아버지. 「가만 잇거라. 저 기럭이 소리난다. 깁흔 가을이로구나!」
안해. 「口腹이 원수라! 또 거짓말을 하고 쌀을 꾸어다가 저녁을 하엿구려. 마음에 죄를 지여가며...」
남편. 「여보. 저 기럭이의 손자의 손자가 안진 여울에 우리의 해골이 굴너 내려갈 때가 잇슬지를 누가 안단 말이요.
그러고 그 뒤에, 그 해골이 엇지나 될가?
또 그 기럭이는 어대로 가 엇지나 되고?...
나도 딱한 사람이요 마는, 그대도 딱한 사람이요.
그러나 우리의 한 말이 시럽슨 말이 아닌 줄만 알어두오.」＜116＞
