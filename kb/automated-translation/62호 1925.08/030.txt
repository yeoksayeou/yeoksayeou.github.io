Magazine Title: 개벽 제62호
Publication Date: 1925년 08월 01일
Article Title: 밧게 잇는 이 생각, 異域風霜에 氣軆安寧하신가 (Thoughts of those outside, are you well amidst the hardships (異域風霜) of foreign lands?)
Author: 崔麟 (Ch'oe Rin), 金東成 (Kim Tong-sŏng), 金璟載 (Kim Kyŏng-jae), 金一善 (Kim Il-sŏn), 洪憙 (Hong Hŭi), 權東鎭 (Kwŏn Tong-jin), 趙奎洙 (Cho Kyu-su)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Roundtable Discussion)

Thoughts of those outside, are you well amidst the hardships (異域風霜) of foreign lands?

At what time would we not think of them? But in these lonely and painful times, the thoughts become even more earnest. Are they healthy? What work are they doing? What do their faces look like, what are their personalities, what are their principles? Having lived apart, inside and outside the country, for over ten years (十有星霜), the feelings of longing (思慕) have truly deepened. The desire to learn news of these teachers, together with our readers, through any opportunity, has also been long-standing. Shall we call it chance, or perhaps arrangement? Thus, though imperfectly, we have come to introduce glimpses (片影) of these teachers through their friends. Of course, it cannot be a complete introduction. Furthermore, it is not a critique of their character. It is merely recounting the sentiments stirred by thinking of them at this time (through their friends). We did not necessarily intend to limit this to only a few individuals, but regrettably, we were unable to find friends of these teachers residing within the country in time, and thus introduce the following few teachers. While asking for broad understanding, we sincerely wish (誠祝) for the teachers' health and ceaseless struggle (不斷의 奮鬪).

The Dignified Gentleman (凜凜丈夫), Mr. Yi Tong-hwi (李東輝氏)

Ch'oe Rin
Speaking of my friends who have gone abroad, there are, of course, many. Mr. An Ch'ang-ho (安昌浩氏), Mr. Yu Tong-yŏl (柳東說氏), Mr. No Paek-nin (盧伯麟氏), and various others are there. Though we parted ways over ten years (十餘星霜) ago, I occasionally heard of their well-being through messengers (人便) or letters, but recently, I have no way of knowing how they are faring. Speaking of which, I received a letter from Mr. Yi Tong-hwi (李東輝氏) last spring.<10> (From Vladivostok [海蔘戚]) There was nothing else, just a simple letter inquiring after my well-being, but at the end, a phrase like this was written: "Having drifted abroad for over ten years (十餘星霜), I have achieved nothing of what I set out to do (所事), and only see my white hair becoming sparse (白髮이 零星); it is truly embarrassing." Reading this, I couldn't suppress the strange emotions rising in my heart.
As for Mr. Yi Tong-hwi, as you know, he was originally from Tanch'ŏn (端川) and seems to have lived a difficult life in his youth. I don't clearly remember the events in between. Even when he came up to Kyŏngsŏng (京城), enlisted in the army, and lived as a soldier, I hadn't met him. When he became a Ch'amnyŏng (參領, Major) and his name was renowned (赫赫) in the Korean military circles, I was studying abroad in Japan and came to know what kind of person he was through hearsay. He also said he knew of me without meeting me. For the 3-4 years from that time until my return to Korea, we only associated spiritually (靈的으로); we had never met face-to-face. People today might feel differently, but in our relationship back then, righteousness (義理) was highly valued. Regardless of whether we had met, once we knew indirectly what kind of person he was, we spiritually admired (思慕) and respected each other, and our trust was considerable. I met him face-to-face when I graduated and returned to Korea (Third year of Yung Hŭi [隆熙3年]). He sought me out immediately. As if we were old acquaintances (一面如舊), we instantly poured out our hearts (肝膽을 토하고) and pledged our futures together.
After that, he was exiled to Muŭi Island (舞衣島, near Kanghwa [江華]) on suspicion of violating the Security Law (保安法). After some time, he was released from Muŭi Island, returned to Kyŏngsŏng, and then left for overseas about 10 years ago. The day before he left, we met at a certain theater and shared our feelings about parting. At that time, he asked me to accompany him. However, my circumstances at the time did not allow me to go with him. We bid our final farewell, promising to meet again later. That was already over ten years ago (十餘星霜), and like his sentiment, we have both come to feel the white hairs <11> growing.
He truly has the air of a dignified gentleman (늠름한 丈夫之風). Both his physique and face are those of a magnificent man. He is of medium height, his face is longish, and his eyes and ears are very clear and handsome (淸秀). As for his personality, he is extremely passionate. He is like a ball of fire. However, he also possesses a kind of religious compassion (宗敎家的 慈悲之風). I don't know about recently, but back then, he was a devout Christian (耶蘇篤信者). Perhaps because of that, he sometimes referred to the world as the "floating world" (浮世).
When a friend of mine, Hyŏn Tanch'ŏn (玄端川, Sŭng-gyu [承奎]), passed away, I attended his funeral procession (葬列). When the memorial service (慰靈式) was held outside Tongdaemun (동대문), I wept (哭) before his spirit (靈下). Seeing this, Mr. Yi told me, "What's the point? It's just the floating world (浮世)..." and cast a kind of cold indifference (冷突) towards the deceased person and those mourning him.
Among the friends I know, he is truly a fine man (好個男兒) with the most gentlemanly spirit (丈夫之風) in both physique and personality. When I see the phrase about drifting overseas for over ten years (海外표류 十餘星霜), achieving nothing (事無所成), and only having sparse white hair (只以白髮零星), I think of the past when I associated with him, and I yearn for the future even more. His age should be fifty-six or fifty-seven now, shouldn't it? (Talk)

Korea's Pioneer - Dr. Sŏ Chae-p'il (徐載弼博士)

Kim Tong-sŏng
My first time meeting the Doctor was years ago during the Washington Conference (華盛頓 會議). How remarkably outstanding (奇傑), energetic, and well-rounded a person he was! His radiant face, resonant voice, towering height—everything about him showed the dignity of a great man. Moreover, his fluent eloquence (活辯), pouring out like water, and his outstanding opinions certainly gave the impression that he was unique in his time (當世一人).
I don't know the details, but as a young man of only 19 (年纔19), the Doctor, along with Mr. Kim Ok-kyun (金玉均先生), plotted the Kapsin Coup (甲申의 정변). When things didn't go as planned (事不如意), he escaped his homeland barefoot in the snowy winds of October of the Kapsin year (甲申 10月), passed through Japan, and entered America, where he studied medicine.<12> Being a genius (天才) and exceptionally outstanding (特越), the teacher had profound knowledge (造詣) in medicine, soon becoming a medical professor at George Washington University, and his fame spread widely (藉藉) in Washington society.
At that time, the Korean royal court heard this news and invited the teacher to serve as a government advisor (顧問) for a fixed term of 15 years. Since the teacher had already become a naturalized American citizen (入籍) by then, Korea took the form of such an invitation. That was right after the Ŭlmi Reforms (乙未更張) took place in Korea, I believe.
It's something we all know; the Ŭlmi Reforms of that time collapsed within less than a month due to the King's flight to the Russian legation (俄館播遷). A distorted cabinet (變態의 내각) led by Kim Pyŏng-si (金炳始) was formed, the winds of enlightenment (開化) since the Kapsin year were once again cut off (頓絶), and both high and low clung to the old delusions (舊日의 迷夢). What must have been the teacher's feelings witnessing this state of affairs? From this, he initiated a major popular political party called the Independence Club (獨立協會), greatly stirring up the spirit of independence and reform. First, he advised the government of the time to renovate the Yŏngŭnmun Gate (迎恩門) at Mohwagwan village (慕華館村) into the Independence Gate (獨立門), and rename Mohwagwan (慕華館) as the Independence Hall (獨立館). He then held large-scale speeches and discussions regarding independence and reform there. This was the first popular movement and political speechifying seen in our Korea. Was that all? At the teacher's initiative, the Independence Club's organ, the "Tongnip Sinmun" (獨立新聞), was published, greatly awakening public opinion. As he raised the momentum for reform in various ways, the King at the time and the stubbornly ignorant faction (頑冥者流) ostracized the teacher. Having brought him for a 15-year term, they merely paid his salary for 15 years in advance and expelled him to America after only 3 years. This was the teacher's second exile (第2次 망명).
Finding himself in such an unavoidable situation, the teacher immersed himself solely in business (實業) in America, quietly preparing for the future's changing fortunes (明日의 風雲). When the recent national movement arose, he immediately devoted his entire effort (全心力) and all his assets (全資産), doing his utmost until today.
According to his letters, he plans to organize a large trading company (一大 무역회사) with Dr. Chŏng Han-gyŏng (鄭翰景), Yu Il-han (柳一漢), and Yi Hŭi-gyŏng (李僖敬) to conduct trade between the East and America. Can't we see from this point that he is both an idealist (理想家) and a pragmatist (實際家) to the core?<13>
As I see it, the teacher is the only senior figure (先輩) currently alive in Korea, and an outstanding figure (奇傑한 인물) full of vigor (精力), never forgetting to strive and work hard (奮鬪努力). Among the senior figures currently overseas, there are some elders who attract criticism (是非) for one reason or another, but I have never heard anyone criticize the teacher. Whenever the teacher meets our people, he always says, "Let's not fight. Let's work."
Anyone can say such words, but coming from the teacher, born out of his own experiences, listeners cannot help but bow their heads respectfully.
I will conclude my words by introducing a brief anecdote (一話) about the teacher.
It was when he arrived in San Francisco (桑港), America, after fleeing during the Kapsin Coup. How could he, knowing not a single word of English, manage in a foreign land tens of thousands of miles away (萬里異國)? Somehow, he ended up asking for work at the house of an American who made and sold chairs. Seeing the teacher's appearance, the owner gestured with his hands, saying, "You don't even know the ABCs, how can you work in my house?" The teacher cheerfully raised both hands, indicating, "With these hands existing (具存), can I not do labor?" Impressed by this silent response, the owner immediately trusted the teacher, and after some time, even gave his daughter (女息) to be the teacher's wife. What a truly straightforward (선뜻선뜻한) personality!
As for his family, they suffered extermination (滅族) from the royal family (王家) long ago, leaving no survivors. He only has daughters born to his American wife (米女). The eldest daughter is married, and he is now said to be spending his days striving (奮鬪) with his second daughter.
A teacher one always wants to meet, a teacher always revered (敬仰). May you always be well (隨時萬康)! (Talk) (The Doctor's age is 60; he is from Kyŏngsŏng [京城人])<14>

The General of Ten Thousand Troops (萬軍之將) - Mr. Yu Tong-yŏl (柳東說氏)

Kim Kyŏng-jae
Mr. Yu was born in Pakch'ŏn, North P'yŏngan Province (平北博川). His sobriquet (號) is Ch'un'gyo (春郊). Possessing a tall frame exceeding six feet (六尺), his appearance and bearing are imposing (늠름), giving the impression at first glance of a general capable of commanding ten thousand troops (萬軍之將). His nature is extremely gentle, humble (溫柔謙遜), and particularly trustworthy and sincere (信義가 敦篤), leading many to place their hopes and expectations in him. He graduated early from the Japanese military academy (士官學校) and held the rank of Army Major (陸軍參領) during the Korean Empire period. After the Russo-Japanese War (日露戰後), he is said to have shown the spirit of a valiant general (勇將) in Chŏngju, North P'yŏngan (平北定州). Later, after the Japan-Korea Annexation (日韓合倂), he, along with his close friend Mr. Yi Kap (李甲氏), became an exile (亡命의 客) far away in Beijing (花京?). Spending fruitless years with many resentful tears and sorrowful feelings, he had a good spouse (好配) to console his distressed heart (心懷)—his wife, Madam Yu Yŏng-suk (劉英淑女史), who for over ten years until now has not shied away from sharing hardships and joys (同苦同樂), enduring wind-swept meals and dew-soaked nights (風餐露宿). There are many anecdotes (一話) about this, but due to space limitations, it is deeply regrettable (遺憾千萬) that I cannot introduce them all one by one. A period of 15 or 16 years (10有5,6年) is easy to say, but if a human life span is 60 years, that's a quarter of it—how can one call it short? Wandering (漂浪) for such a long time through Southern and Northern Manchuria (南北滿洲), Mongolia (蒙古), and Russian territories (俄領 等地), firmly pledging his heart (盟約) deepened by days and months, in that boundless land where the wind is cold and snowdrifts pile like mountains (積雪이 강산가튼), seeking the final resolution not with pen and mouth, but solely through direct action (直接의 동작)—how great must his suffering (苦痛) be? Ah! I cannot write more about it here. We don't even have that much freedom today.
His greatest strength (長技) lies solely in his loyalty and honesty (忠直). Meeting countless political figures in the world, he knows neither how to deceive others nor how to flatter them. Accordingly, his daily attitude displays this loyalty and honesty just as it is. After 1919 (大正8年), when the [Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea] (OOOOOO) was established in Shanghai (上海) and he was elected Chief of Staff (參謀總長), he believed that the final resolution of our problem did not lie in such formal actions (形式的動作) and resigned from the post (任). He then established his base of activity along the banks of the Heilongjiang and Songhua rivers (黑龍松花之沿岸), experiencing all sorts of hardships (苦難) while unyieldingly persisting (百折不屈). Recently, he is said to have become a leading figure (主腦人物) in the [Righteous Government] (OO府). Since ancient times, it's said that great men are fond of women (偉人은 好色). Perhaps for that reason, it is said that he and his wife, Madam Yu Yŏng-suk, cannot bear to be apart, no matter the danger (危地) or hardship (艱難). Unlike politicians (政客) or strategists (策士) who only clamor with pen and mouth, his life as a soldier prioritizes direct action.<15> Needless to say, there were times when he faced indescribable dangerous situations (危境), and indeed, times when he stood at a critical juncture (百尺竿頭), at a crossroads (岐路) between life and death (生이냐 死이냐). Yet, he invariably accompanies his beloved wife, Madam Yu Yŏng-suk. When could he ever have had joy (喜) or pleasure (樂)? But as his beloved's warm hand never leaves his side, thawing his ice-cold body, there must surely be comfort amidst the suffering, it is said.
Now, finally, I wish to introduce one anecdote about him, sufficient to understand his feelings and magnanimity (度量), and then lay down my pen (擱筆).
He was always in northern China, on one hand observing the situation (形勢如何) in Russia (露西亞), and on the other, maintaining connections (氣脈을 통하고) with Sun Yat-sen's faction (孫文一派) in the south (he has now passed away). Due to such connections, one time when he was sharing drinks (酒席) with several Chinese colleagues, the Chinese individuals variously argued. Some said the Russian Communist Party (露西亞의 공산당) represented truth and was the only path to human salvation, while many praised America's Wilsonian justice and humanity (正義人道), speaking of America's goodwill (好意). At that time, Mr. Yu remained composed (泰然), kept silent (緘口), and uttered not a single word (一言). When his Chinese comrades asked about his views (意向의 如何), he replied that more benevolent (人心이 후하고) and kind-hearted (착한) than America or Russia, the only such country was Korea (朝鮮). Everyone burst into loud laughter (大笑) and asked for the reason. He replied, "No matter how good America is, there's no instance of it helping others while sacrificing its own country. Russia is also like that. But Korea, about 10 years ago, when Japan came and demanded the handover of Korea (朝鮮의 讓渡), responded to Japan's demand and handed it over without objection (異議업시)." Upon hearing this, the Chinese present were uniformly furious (大怒), saying "A ruined country's people (亡國人) have the disposition (根性) of a ruined country!" and the quiet gathering suddenly became tumultuous (喧優).
So why did Mr. Yu say such nonsensical (주착업는) things? Because he had his own valid reason (相當한 이유). Having associated with Chinese people for a long time, he understood their feelings and character well. He intended to provoke their anger (憤怒), and thereby achieve something (어든바이 잇섯든 연고) from that gathering.
Due to limited space (制限잇는 紙面), I cannot write at length, but I only pray that the teacher remains healthy. <16>

Dr. Yi Sŭng-man (李承晩博士) is Entirely Passion (渾身都是熱)

Kim Il-sŏn
Coming to the YMCA building via Chongno (鐘路) like this makes thoughts of Dr. Yi (李博士) particularly poignant.
Truly selfless (公번 띄고 私心업고), his whole being (渾身) is entirely a lump of passion (熱情 떵어리), Dr. Yi! He is indeed a fervent patriot and martyr (愛國愛民의 열렬한 志士요 烈士).
Having grown up in Haeju (海州), he likely came to Kyŏngsŏng following his father around the age of 16 or 17. Originally proficient in Classical Chinese (漢文), he entered Paejae Haktang (培材學堂) to study new learning centered on English. Around the Chŏngyu year (丁酉年頃, 1897), when Dr. Sŏ Chae-p'il (徐載弼博士) launched the Tongnip Sinmun (獨立新聞), he joined the newspaper company (社) as an editorial writer (論說記者), striving (盡力) to promote the ideals of independence.
Speaking of the Independence Club (獨立協會) and the Manmin Kongdonghoe (萬民共同會), they were, without need for further mention, the only popular political parties that first arose in modern Korea. How much they strove to purge (蕭淸) the royal court and palace (宮中府中) and reform civil society (民間社會) at that time is something that needs no redundant explanation now. Dr. Yi, merely 22 or 23 years old then (年少), was early involved with the Independence Club and led the Manmin Kongdonghoe, engaging in direct confrontation (正面格鬪) with the Peddlers' Guild army (褓負商軍) of the so-called Hwanguk Hyŏphoe (皇國協會). (Events of the Musul-Kihae years [戊戌己亥年間, 1898-1899]). I vividly witnessed (實見) a scene at that time: when the P'yŏngnyangja army (平凉子軍, peddlers [褓負商]), numbering in the thousands including soldiers and rioters (兵丁, 亂民), each carrying weapons (凶器), surged from outside Saemun Gate (새문 밧그), past Chŏng-dong (貞洞), towards Taehanmun Gate (大漢門), the doctor, all alone (홀홀단신), confronted those fellows, risking life and death (死生을 직결). Ah, how can I describe his dramatic, active scene (劇的 活藥場面)? Anyway, the doctor was that fervent (熱烈) and daring (大膽). Eventually, when the reactionary faction (反動派) gained victory (勝), the Association side suffered defeat (敗), and the doctor himself became one of the imprisoned (拘囚), unable to overcome his boiling indignation (비분), he secretly contacted compatriots overseas (在外同胞), shot the prison guard (獄吏) with a revolver (六穴砲), and attempted to escape prison (脫獄). However, he was recaptured, sentenced to life imprisonment (終身懲役), and languished (呻吟) for about 7 years in the Chongno prison (鐘路拘置監).<17> Later, perhaps in the Ŭlsa year (乙巳年, 1905), when the clouds of conflict between Japan and Russia (日露) gathered (巤雲이 닐던때), he was released from prison through various means and went to America. In America, he immediately began studying, obtaining degrees (學位) in literature, philosophy, and theology. Later, after the Kyŏngmu year (庚戊, 1910), he returned to Korea, briefly served as the academic dean (學館監) at the Chongno YMCA (鐘路青年會), and then went back to America with Bishop Harris (하리스 감독), who was attending the Methodist Church conference (監理敎 年會). At that Methodist conference session, he attacked Bishop Harris's pro-Japanese sycophancy (親日에 대한 추태) and strongly argued on behalf of Korea. Due to various circumstances related to that, he was unable to return to Korea again and continues to work for his distant homeland from the American continent (米洲地方) to this day. (I will not speak further of subsequent events.)
The doctor is 51 or 52 years old this year, and according to reports, his hair is already streaked with white (白髮이 星星). Perhaps 30 years of struggle (苦鬪) have aged the doctor prematurely (早老).
There are no other family members in the doctor's household; there was only a young son, but unfortunately, even he met misfortune (不幸) in America. Now, only the doctor himself rushes about (奔走) in foreign lands overseas.
I don't know about now, but the doctor's thinking (思想) back then was very progressive (急進的) for Korea at the time. From the time of the Independence Club, he considered a republican form of government (共和政體). There was an incident <18> like this: When he returned to Korea after going to America and gave his first speech at the YMCA hall, he mentioned three refreshing things, which became quite controversial (問題) for a while. What were the three refreshing things? First, the disappearance of the king (님금업서진 것); second, the disappearance of the yangban (양반 업서진 것); third, the disappearance of the topknot (상투 업서진 것).
Quietly bowing my head, I see the doctor's full image (全容): his medium height, stout body, worried-looking face, and tightly clenched lips. The thoroughly public-spirited (公번되고) and passionate doctor (박사 一身이 都是熱 - the doctor's whole being is passion) – how deeply I miss him. (Talk)

Dr. Kim Kyu-sik (金奎植博士), Proficient in Languages (語學에 能한)

Hong Hŭi
Mr. Kim Kyu-sik (金奎植氏)? You mean Dr. Kim (金博士) currently in Shanghai, right? I don't know if he might have changed it now, but his previous sobriquet (別號) was Manho (晩湖), wasn't it? When speaking of Manho, there might be someone more suitable than me. Who could that be? I don't know. Perhaps if you search among those related to the former Yŏmjŏng-dong church (염정동 교당) or the former Kyŏngsin School (경신학교), you might find a suitable person. If you insist I speak, albeit inadequately, because you absolutely lack the time to search as you wish, I won't refuse outright. My relationship (交情) with Manho isn't so deep as to make speaking difficult, nor is it so non-existent (交分이 아주 업서서) that I don't know the person at all. So, in a way, perhaps I am suitable to speak. When people mention Kim Kyu-sik, anyone familiar with the name praises (稱道) his excellent English. Just as only a sage knows a sage (聖人이라야 聖人을 안다니), we common folk just offer praise, but even others who speak English well admire his good pronunciation (口音). Generally, Manho's talent (才操) for languages is beyond others' reach. He apparently learned a little French (法語) while attending school in America. He once said that with just a little effort, listening to lectures would be manageable. Since he must have been pushed considerably (적지아니 닥달이 되얏슬터이니까) when he went to Paris (巴里) years ago, his French must be quite good. As for Chinese (漢語), when we were together in Shanghai, he already spoke it quite well, so he must be fluent <19> now. During a brief stay at a Western company in Zhangjiakou (張家口), he acquired a foundation (素養) in Russian (露語), and he speaks Japanese (日語) well without having formally learned it. When Manho teaches languages to others, he seems to judge by his own standards and gets angry easily (화를 곳잘 내는 모양입듸다). I myself witnessed (目睹) a friend, now a teacher at some commercial high school (商普敎師), bearing the brunt of Manho's temper while trying to learn some English in his spare time.
His impatience, to the point of anger, seemed unreasonable even from an observer's perspective, and of course, the friend on the receiving end must have felt wronged (억울). The reason Manho gets impatient to the point where the learner feels wronged is because he himself is a genius (天才) in languages. What about his eloquence (言辯)? Since I've only heard him speak (演說) a few times, it's difficult to say definitively (遽然히), but it would be hard to call it powerful oratory (雄辯). Because his mind (頭腦) is sharp (明晳), the logic (條理) of his arguments (辯說) is coherent (整然), leaving almost no room for leaks (물부어 샐틈이 업습듸다). Since he must have gained considerable polish (練達) after entering the public stage, I wonder how he is now compared to before. Generally speaking, his speaking skills (말재조) might have improved, but would his voice have gotten louder? For him to become an orator (雄辯家), he needs to shout by a large body of water like Demosthenes (데모스데네스) to strengthen his voice. He himself often spoke of his small vocal volume (音量 적은 것) as a matter of regret (恨事). What kind of person (人物) is he really? That cannot be answered as simply as his talent for languages or eloquence. Tell me roughly. Speaking roughly is even harder. Like someone who doesn't know the sea answering "It's similar to a boundless lake" when asked what the sea is like – even if you press me to speak vaguely, saying "boundless lake" might allow one to guess (推測) the sea's vastness, but can one imagine the sea's spirit (氣勢) with just that? Moreover, can one grasp (領會) the sea's essence (精神)? Even if there are things that cannot be imagined or grasped, please tell me. This is sheer stubbornness. Well then, what kind of person should I say he is? If we compare him to the qualifications of past monarchs (君主), perhaps he would be capable enough to be a wise ruler maintaining the state (守城하는 명군), but lack what it takes to be a heroic founder of a dynasty (創業하는 영주)? If we view this society as a large company (一大 會社) and discuss his qualifications as a member, perhaps he is more suited to be a secretary (書記) than a managing owner (經營主)? This is even more absurd <20> than pointing to the sea with a boundless lake. It must be called reckless talk (妄言). If reckless talk is a sin (罪), then the one who forced me to speak must share the blame. Let's stop the reckless and playful talk (妄言 戱言). I hear that Manho, busy with public affairs (公事), developed a strange illness and grew a lump (혹), which our people overseas supposedly named the "independence lump" (독립혹). I have some acquaintance (交情) with the handsome-featured (容貌 端麗) Manho, but I have yet to meet (面分이 업습니다) the lump-bearing Dr. Kim (혹부리 金博士). How different today's Dr. Kim is from the Manho of former days – the desire to meet him often becomes intense.

The Free-Spirited and Unconventional (倜儻不覊) Mr. No Paek-nin (廬伯麟氏)

Kwŏn Tong-jin
Mr. No Paek-nin (廬伯麟氏)? Yes, I know him well. We spent time together in Japan, and after coming to Korea, we were friends for many years. It's already been over ten years (十餘星霜) since we parted, but I've often heard news indirectly. Recently, there's news that he is ill; I wonder if it's serious.
Anyway, he is an extraordinary individual, different from ordinary people. He is a truly unrestrained and unfettered (不拘不束) cheerful fellow (快丈夫). He laughs easily, travels frequently, eats well, is righteous (義氣), has a large build (氣骨이 壯大), and enjoys playing pranks (作亂). In some ways, he has the air of a rōnin (浪人風), a fine, upstanding man (好好 丈夫).
He has a tremendous spirit of consuming whatever comes his way, regardless of whether it's his or someone else's. Even with alcohol, he casually drinks 3 or 4 dozen bottles of beer at once. He especially loves to eat, whether it's Western, Japanese, or Korean cuisine. Whenever he meets anyone, regardless of whether they have money, he gladly goes along and enjoys a hearty meal with pleasure. He often goes alone too. There were many times he ate without money, confidently lying down afterwards, promising to pay later (後期를 두고), and then getting up. However, his spirit and resourcefulness (手段) were so good that he never once got into trouble (封卞).<21>
Truly, his physique is excellent. Tall, with a large face, a rather short neck, small eyes, and a sharp brow line (알에 위가 뚝꺽은 듯 한데), he possesses the strength of a Hercules (壯士). That seems to be why he eats so well.
He's originally from Changyŏn, Hwanghae Province (黃海道 長淵), right? During the time when Ch'unch'ŏn (春泉, Pak Yŏng-hyo [朴泳孝]) was Minister of Home Affairs (內部大臣), he was one of the first 12 government-sponsored students (官費留學生) sent to Tokyo (東京), where he entered Keiō Gijuku (慶應義塾) founded by Fukuzawa Yukichi (福澤諭吉). Later, aspiring to a military career (軍界), he, along with about 10 others including Yun Ch'i-sŏng (尹致晟 等), entered Seijō Gakkō (星成學校), a preparatory school for military officers managed by Utsunomiya Tarō (宇都宮次郞). At this time, my elder brother (Kwŏn Yŏng-jin [權濚鎭]) and I were living in exile (亡命) in Tokyo. It was then that I first met Mr. No. After school (下學), he would often come to our house to hang out. Even then, Mr. No loved to eat and play pranks. And among all the students, he acted as the ringleader (主謀將), bustling about here and there. I often saw him visiting brothels and taverns (靑樓酒肆), enjoying himself boisterously (浩蕩).
Afterwards, he graduated from the military academy (士官學校) with excellent grades and, upon returning to Korea, immediately became an instructor (教官) at the Military Academy (武官學校, at the end of present-day Wŏn-dong [苑洞]).
Originally skilled in military science (軍寧學), proficient in classical Chinese (漢學), and possessing a dignified build (氣骨이 凜凜), upon becoming an instructor, he quickly gained trust and respect (信望), rising to the rank of Chŏngnyŏng (正領, Colonel) within just a few years (幾年) and eventually becoming the commandant (校長) of the Military Academy. His fame became quite renowned (赫赫).
Later, as national affairs deteriorated (國事가 日非) and the Military Academy was abolished, he threw away his military position (軍職) and, together with Mr. Yun Ch'i-sŏng (尹致晟氏), established a leather company (皮革會社). On the side, he opened a Western shoe store (洋靴店) and engaged in business (實業生活) for 4-5 years, but somehow ended up losing 40,000-50,000 won. Afterwards, his livelihood became impoverished (疲弊), he had nothing to do, and national affairs only bred discontent (不平)—so he just gave it all up and skipped across overseas.<22>
Anyway, he is magnanimous and unrestrained (豪濶). He often shows an attitude of looking down his nose (鼻穾) at the world. He often carries himself as if the whole world is beneath his shoulders. That's why if someone says something, he quickly scoffs (픽 웃고) and shows a contemptuous attitude (輕蔑하는 態). And he has the spirit of "Forget it all. I can do it alone." – a spirit of acting independently (獨狌獨來). That's why, even recently, he held the position of Prime Minister (國務總理) for 3-4 years, enduring it alone. "Forget it all. I'll endure it by myself" – he is a fearless and bold (無怯大膽), unrestrained and unfettered (不拘不束) cheerful man (快男兒).
He originally lacks a sense of family obligation (家族 觀念). After his wife passed away (喪妻), he did not remarry (取妻). He often said things like letting his son do as he pleased, his daughter as she pleased, and himself as he pleased, each going their own way. He also really liked bicycles (自轉車). Every day, he'd get on his bicycle and ride around everywhere, east, west, south, north. Anyway, he's a lively creature (活物). He never stays still, not even for a moment. That's why he would sometimes look for me, and seeing me sitting inside my room, he'd say, "Hey, let's go out. What's the point of being in a room?" and grab my wrist, pulling me along many times. His age is uncertain (未群). He must be nearly 60.

The Scion of a Noble Family (名門馨兒) - Mr. Yi Si-yŏng (李始榮氏)

Cho Kyu-su
With the hearty spirit (豪氣) emerging from Sŏngnam (城南) rice wine (濁酒), perhaps feeling some stifling melancholy (鬱悶) in the steamy heat (薰蒸), wanting to breathe the cool air of the Han River Railway Bridge (漢江 鐵橋), I jump onto the crowded streetcar, rush past Yongsan Station (龍山驛) as if driven by a strong wind (長風), alight briskly (섭벅), grasp my three-foot cane (三尺短杖) askew, stroll leisurely (어정어정) across the rainbow-like (長虹) railway bridge, and wander up and down the taverns (酒幕) of Noryangjin (鷺梁津). The old string and wind instruments (管絃) of Yongsan P'ungch'ŏng Pavilion (龍驤風翥亭) seem asleep in a spring dream (春夢), while beneath the new signboard of Yonggŭm Pavilion (湧金樓), only the sound of the long drum (長鼓) enhances all cherished sentiments. The drizzling rain (구진 비) has been swept away by the gently blowing west wind (西風), and peeking through the edge of clouds gathered over Kwangnŭng Mountain (廣陵山) is a single bright moon (一輪明月). Reflected again, submerged in the river water, its white light creates a White Jade Heaven (白玉天) above and a White Jade Heaven below. In the distance, standing tall and indistinctly white or bluish along the bank, <23> are those reeds (갈때폭) – are they merely soaked in fine rain? Are they dancing in the white dew? This is the night scenery described as "The person I long for is right there in the middle of the water" (所懷伊人, 宛在水中央). Pausing my steps, tapping the liquor table with ivory chopsticks (象牙箸), I lend my ear to the lofty discourse (高談峻論) gushing like a spring – this too, a person with a mind to listen (有心人) will surely hear with intent (有心聽).

(A) Huh... Amidst the fervent anti-foreign movement (排外運動) in China (中國), I wonder if our compatriots (同胞) in Shanghai (上海), caught in the vortex of war (戰亂渦), are safe...
(B) With infrequent communication (通信), how troubled they must be.
(A) A teacher who loved me dearly comes especially to mind.
(B) Who might that be?
(A) He is Mr. Yi Si-yŏng (李始榮氏), known as Sŏngje-sŏnsaeng (省齊先生).
(B) Ah! A teacher I too have heard much about. What is he like?
(A) What do you mean, what is he like?
(B) Where is the teacher from (어대 어른)?
(A) That teacher is a descendant (後孫) of Paeksa Yi Hang-bok (白沙 李恒福) 선생, a meritorious subject (功臣) of the Imjin War restoration (壬辰中興), whose achievements (勳業) still benefit us today. He was born the fifth son (第5子) of the loyal subject (忠臣) Yi Yu-sŭng (李裕承), who served through the three reigns (三朝) of King Hŏnjong (憲宗), King Ch'ŏljong (哲宗), and King Kojong (高宗). Born in the 5th year of King Kojong's reign (戊辰, 1868) at the old family house (舊第) in Chŏ-dong (苧洞), southern Hansŏng (漢城), not only was his inherited nature (遺傳性) from his ancestors extraordinary, but his innate intelligence (聰明) was also uncommon. His renowned talent (才名) for literary composition and calligraphy (文興筆의 風就) was said to be among the five geniuses (五才子) of that time. As the son-in-law (婿郞) of the highly reputed Minister Kim Hong-jip (金弘集), he also received much deep affection.
(B) Did the teacher ever serve in officialdom (官界)?
(A) Certainly. Just past his coming-of-age (弱冠), he passed the special civil service examination (別試及第), entering the literary ranks (文科). Until the Kim Hong-jip cabinet collapsed in the winter of Ŭlmi (乙未冬, 1895), he never left positions of integrity and prestige (淸官美職), having already reached the senior second rank (堂上2品) and served as Left and Right Royal Secretary (左右承旨) in the Sŭngjŏngwŏn (承政院) even before the reforms (更張).
(B) Then what did he do after Ŭlmi?<24>
(A) After Ŭlmi, suspected due to his father-in-law's involvement (嫌疑), he did not enter officialdom. However, he widely associated with various friends among literary figures and patriots (文人志士), investigating principles of past and present affairs (古今의 事理), discussing old and new scholarship (新舊의 學園), and building up considerable aspirations (抱負). Such aspects today likely stem from the self-cultivation (修養) of that time. Then, following the outbreak of the Russo-Japanese War (日露戰役) and the transformation (一變) of Korea's political situation (政局), he re-entered officialdom (官界), holding important diplomatic posts (外交의 要路) like Director of Foreign Affairs (交涉局長), Director of Civil Affairs (民事局長), and judge (判事) at the Hansŏng District Court (漢城裁判所). But in that political world, deteriorating day by day (日非), how tightly could concepts of glory or disgrace (榮辱) have held him?
(B) When did he go abroad (外地)?
(A) Upon the annexation in the Kyŏngsul year (庚戌, 1910), he sold his residence (自擇) in Chŏ-dong (棗洞), now Hwanggŭm-jŏng 1-chōme (黃金町 一丁目), and moved (移徒) to Segeomjeong (洗釰亭) outside Ch'angŭi Gate (彰義門). We thought the teacher was perhaps bidding farewell (下直) to the dusty world (紅塵) and intending to become a master of springs and stones (泉石의 주인). But that winter, in the blink of an eye (瞥眼間), it was rumored throughout the world (滿世人) that all five brothers of the teacher had vanished (踪跡이 妙然) from the peninsula. Only the following spring did news arrive that they had gone to a place called Hanihe (哈泥河) in Tonghua County (通化縣), Shengjing Province (盛京省), Manchuria (滿洲), newly settled there (奠接), established the Sinhŭng School (新興學校) to guide young people, and organized a group called Kyŏnghaksa (耕學社) to research, with several comrades (同志), parallel strategies for education and industry (敎育과 産業의 並進策).
(B) Then when did the teacher go to Shanghai?
(A) Just as a starving tethered horse (籠裡의 飢廌) feels the spirit to soar to the heavens (衝天할 意氣) merely hearing the sound of wind and flags (風幾), when the revolutionary storms (革命 風雲) arose in China and the republican government (共和政府) was newly established in Nanjing (哀頂城?), and then the Great War in Europe (歐洲의 大戰) broke out, making the whole world boil like a cauldron (油鍋), he wondered if some good countermeasure (善後策) could be found. He rushed back and forth (奔走往來) between Beijing (北京) and Fengtian (奉天, Mukden), experiencing all manner of hardships (風霜). Following the spread (瀰漫) of the March First Movement (三一運動) across the eight provinces (八域) of the peninsula in the Kimi year (己未, 1919), numerous comrades established the Provisional Government (臨時 政府) in Shanghai. Called upon for the crucial mission (重大한 使命) of Minister of Finance (財政總長), he decided he could not evade the duty (義務上에 사피할 수 없다). Resolutely (奮然) he went, and with utmost sincerity (寨寨한 誠心), braving a thousand hardships and ten thousand difficulties (千辛萬苦), he has pressed forward, passing these seven long, fragmented years (7年의 支離한 星霜) as if they were a single day.<25>
(B) What is the teacher's personality (性格) like?
(C) To others, he doesn't seem to have a distinct personality (別性格). However, perhaps admiring Master Ori (梧里先生, Yi Wŏn-ik), in his small frame (體樣), when he sits upright and dignified (丌然 端坐), he can remain silent all day (從日). Yet, if he has a conviction (主張), he remains unmoved (搖動이 업스나) even if urged by ten thousand people. But because he is thorough in reasoning (事理에 綜詳), there is not the slightest flaw (弊端) of stubborn inflexibility (固執不通). His integrity (志操), like ice and jade (氷璧), perhaps makes his principles and assertions (主義와 主張) known (其知) to all people today.
(B) Is there anything worth hearing about the teacher's usual life?
(A) Nothing particular, but after reading many old books (古書), he also perused Buddhist and Daoist scriptures (仙佛等書). Especially in Daoist learning (仙學), namely what is called Daoism (道敎) in China, he is said to have glimpsed its profundities (蘊奧). However, he seems to disapprove (反치 안는 것 갓습듸다) of the bizarre methods (汚怪한 方述) like the elixir refinement (鍊丹) practiced by those pointed out as Daoist masters (道士) in the world.
(B) What is his demeanor (風度) when dealing with people?
(A) Being solely elegant and refined (端雅), he seems to lack a distinctive style (別風致) in dealing with people and things (對人 接物). However, anyone who encounters his warm, respectful, kind, and understanding virtue (溫恭慈諒한 그 德氣) even once will, unknowingly (不知不識), find it difficult to forget the affectionate (藹然히 사랑하든) impression.
(B) Is there any recent news?
(A) I haven't heard details since the anti-foreign movement (排外運動) turmoil (騷然) in China, but there should be no particular incident (別故). Recently, he seems to be paying close attention (留意) to industrial matters (産業方面), but that requires funds (資金), doesn't it?
(B) How are the teacher's descendants (後進)?
(A) The eldest son, Kyu-pong (圭鳳), who graduated first from Posŏng Middle School (普成中學校) 16 years ago, is now a mature man of 40. Being exceptionally intelligent (聰明이 過人), he became overly anxious (焦燥) about worldly affairs (世事), weakening his nerves (神經) and impairing his health. Recently, however, he has recovered somewhat and greatly assists (襄助) his father's labors. After him, there are said to be two children not yet adults.
(B) Overall, he sounds like a fine teacher.
Having heard all this back and forth, the river moon (江月) risen to mid-heaven (中天) already attests to the fifth watch of the night (五更). Let's head back before the streetcars stop running. Quick, give me a drink too! <26>

**Summary**

This article from the August 1925 issue of *Kaebyŏk* magazine compiles reminiscences from friends about prominent Korean figures living in exile due to Japanese colonial rule. Contributors like Ch'oe Rin, Kim Tong-sŏng, and others share personal anecdotes and impressions of Yi Tong-hwi, Sŏ Chae-p'il, Yu Tong-yŏl, Yi Sŭng-man, Kim Kyu-sik, No Paek-nin, and Yi Si-yŏng. The pieces express deep longing and concern for these individuals, highlighting their personalities, past activities in Korea, reasons for exile, ongoing struggles abroad (often in China, Russia, or the US), and unwavering commitment to the Korean independence cause, despite hardships and advancing age. Historically, the article is valuable for its contemporary, personal perspectives on key leaders of the independence movement during a period of intense struggle. It offers glimpses into their characters, interpersonal relationships, ideological leanings (like Yi Sŭng-man's early republicanism or Yi Si-yŏng's connection to Daoism), the challenges of exile life, and the network of activists spread across different regions, providing insights often missing from purely political accounts.

기사형태	대담·좌담

밧게 잇는 이 생각, 異域風霜에 氣軆安寧하신가
어느 때에 잇서 그들이 생각키우지 안으랴마는 외롭고 괴로운 이때에 잇서 더욱 간절히 생각키워진다. 건강들 하신가. 하시는 일은 무언가 그들의 얼굴모습들은 엇더시며 성격들은 엇더시며 주의들은 엇더신가. 內外分居十有星霜에 실로 思慕의 정이 깁헛섯다. 기회를 어더 선생들의 소식을 독자와 가티 알고저 함도 오랫섯다. 우연이라 할가 作會라 할가. 이에 불만하나마 선생들의 친구를 통하야 선생들의 片影이나마 소개하게 되얏다. 母論 완전한 소개가 못된다. 더구나 인격평론은 안이다. 다만 이때에 생각키우는 감회뿐을(친구를 통하야)이약이 한데 불과하다. 반듯이 멧분에뿐 국한하려는 것이 안이엿스나 국내에 게신 선생들의 친구를 밋쳐 찻지못한데서 유감이나마 이하의 멧분 선생을 소개하게 되얏다. 널리 아양을 비는 동시에 선생들의 건강과 不斷의 奮鬪를 誠祝한다.

凜凜丈夫의 李東輝氏

崔麟
해외에 나아가 잇는 나의 친구로 말슴하면 母論 여러분 됨니다. 安昌浩씨라든지 柳東說씨라든지 盧伯麟씨라든지 기타 여러분 게심니다. 헤여진지 각각 十餘星霜에 人便 혹은 서신으로 안부는 간혹 드러스나 近日은 엇더케들 지내시는지 알 수가 업슴니다. 무르니 말슴이지 李東輝씨로부터는 작년 봄에 편지가 왓슴니다.＜10＞(海蔘戚로서)다른 말은 업고 간단한 문안편지인데 그끗 구절에 이런 말이 씨여 잇습듸다. 「해외에 표류한지 十餘星霜에 所事란 한아도 이룬바 업고 다만 백발이 零星함을 볼뿐이니 붓끄러울 뿐임니다」라구요. 이것을 볼때에 나의 마음에도 이상한 감회가 닐어남을 금치 못햇슴니다.
