A Record of the Great Flood Turmoil That Shook Heaven and Earth, Report and Miscellaneous Observations
						Pak Talsŏng

The Greatest Turmoil of Recent Times
The world's playboys (nanbong nomdŭl) would call this one of the 'three great spectacles of the world.' Namely, watching water, watching fire, watching fights – these are they. Borrowing the words of those playboys, shall I call this the first spectacle of my lifetime? Borrowing the words of some compassionate person, shall I say it was unbearable to witness with one's own eyes? In any case, this great flood is the first spectacle in this reporter's 30 years, and the world calls it an unprecedented great catastrophe in several hundred years, while some even say it is the first such event since the creation (kaebyŏk). But then,

Flood and Chosŏn, What Karmic Grudge is This?
Humans conquer nature. Cunning human bastards relentlessly conquer nature. However, humans fear nature. Incompetent human bastards cannot even twitch under the power of nature. They just cry out about irresistible force. But
What is this? What karmic grudge (業怨) exists between the flood and Chosŏn? Nature seems to particularly threaten Chosŏn people more, and Chosŏn people seem to particularly suffer the ravages of nature more. Seriously, year after year, with water disasters and drought disasters, why are nature's wicked jokes (惡戱) so severe towards Chosŏn people? If a Water God exists, the entire Chosŏn populace tearing into that bastard's flesh wouldn't be satisfying enough. (If a Fire God exists...) But what can be done against the power of nature? Did the Japanese bastards want to suffer the earthquake disaster? Did the Russian bastards want to suffer the famine? Did the American bastards want to suffer the wind disaster? Under the power of nature<73>, it seems that this bastard and that bastard, without exception, cannot yet avoid being ravaged.

The past goes without saying. This year, this month, this day's great flood along the Han River! (Also the great flood along the Naktong River!) This is truly an unprecedented great tragedy. Also, we can call it a great calamity that we shall forever remember with gnashing teeth. Who could have dreamt that hundreds of thousands of innocent souls would be trampled so relentlessly in a single day and night?

The flood of July 12th was, for Kyŏngsŏng, mere chicken feed (鳥足之血), not worth mentioning. (Although the damage at that time was also unparalleled in recent years.) This time, namely the great flood near Kyŏngsŏng along the Han River on July 17th, 18th, and 19th, is said to be unseen even by 100-year-old elders. There is no need for further tedious explanation.

Mountain Collapses and Rivers Overturn on July 17th
Only three days had barely passed since the great calamity of the Han River rising 34 ch'ŏk and 2 ch'on on July 12th. Just as attempts were being made to somehow mend the harsh wounds (瘡痍) (and how can one speak of the flood tragedy along the Naktong River during that time?), the heartless torrential rain, with what intentions, began its assault again on the 16th, raining all day and pouring violently throughout the night. It poured down relentlessly and frighteningly, like water emptying from a bottle. The citizens of the capital (長安) were gripped by great fear, saying, "It's another disaster!" Especially the residents of the Han River area (Ttuksŏm, East and West Ich'ondong, Yongsan, Map'o, Tongmak, Sŏgang, Yanghwado, Yŏngdŭngp'o, etc.), who had suffered the ravages of those dreadful floods year after year, could only tremble, thinking, "Agh! We're dead again!" Although there was much hope of "maybe," the dreadful torrential rain unfortunately continued to pour down. Once again, it turned the land into a sea of mud (泥海化).

Around 10 AM on the 17th, the warning for the Han River rising to 29 ch'ŏk finally fell upon the Han River area and the capital's citizens. The playboys shrugged their shoulders, saying another water spectacle had begun. The people of the Han River area were absorbed in trying to survive. (Having learned from the previous incident.) The torrential rain worsened further, pouring down torrentially as if it would overturn heaven and earth at any moment. Soon, the warning for the Han River rising to 36 ch'ŏk inevitably fell again. Newspaper extras with special large type (特號活字) repeatedly startled the capital like hailstones. Except for the children of the wealthy residing in high buildings and grand pavilions (高樓巨閣) far from mountains and water<74> (those bastards probably couldn't smooth their brows thinking about damage to fields and reduced harvests?), the city, inside and out, turned into a scene of great chaos.

The warnings came again: Water rising minute by minute! Ttuksŏm in danger! Ich'ondong completely submerged! Map'o area turned into a sea of mud! "Let's go! Let's go see anyway," the capital's citizens pushed towards the Han River pedestrian bridge like a tide. (Presumably, the Wangsimni and Map'o directions were the same.) Shall I call myself one of the spectators? Anyway, this reporter, unable to sit still, became one who braved the wind and rain.

A great calamity! A great water-nation calamity! Kyŏngjŏn (Seoul Electric) dispatched all its streetcars towards the Sinyongsan area. Were there tens of thousands? Or hundreds of thousands? Several hundred streetcars were in utter chaos, packed beyond capacity. The streetcars could no longer reach the terminus, stopping before the railroad hospital. All the refugees were floundering in a Red Sea. The military, police, firefighters, various newspaper companies, various youth groups, and boy scouts had already mobilized fully, striving to rescue the refugees. The water was already said to be at 38 ch'ŏk. East and West Ich'ondong were entirely engulfed in raging waves and furious billows.

On both sides of the pedestrian bridge, the household goods (家藏什物) of the refugees lay scattered wretchedly. The screams of cattle, horses, chickens, and pigs were especially pitiful. The cries of fellow countrymen appealing to heaven and weeping on the ground were unbearable to hear (耳不敢聞). Amidst the turbulent, raging waters, whole houses floated by, furniture floated by, lumber and charcoal briquettes, and all sorts of human goods floated by. It was unbearable to watch. Fortunately, at this time, I did not see any humans drifting. (However, I cannot speak solely based on a glance.)

Yonggŭmnu Pavilion on the far side of the pedestrian bridge was packed with refugees. The passenger cars at Yongsan Station were also said to be occupied by refugees. The torrential rain continued unabated. The river water seemed to have already reached 39 ch'ŏk. In East and West Ich'ondong, only the roofs of the tallest houses were visible.

Anyway, human bastards, while pretending to be compassionate, are heartless, and while pretending to care about appearances, are shameless. Witnessing this great water calamity with people weeping, crying, collapsing, and falling, yet remaining calm and composed (恬然自若) - rather, seemingly excited that a great spectacle has occurred, most people seem to be milling about. Seeing lost items, there are more women saying "Look at that! Come here! Oh, how funny!" than those saying "Oh! What to do about that!" Seeing the ragged clothes<75> of the flood victims, there are more bastards saying "Did they live wearing that!" than those saying "Oh! How pitiful!" They are this shameless. However, the prostitutes and vagrants who were good at "taking a car to the Han River" - perhaps having learned from being watched - were absent during this chaotic disaster. But the bitches and bastards of the wealthy class, who only acted as parasites in the corners of their rooms, seemed to lack experience in avoiding public eyes at such times. Whether their legs were broken or they were showing off their cars, they pompously honked their cars with immense arrogance (豪氣萬丈). It was regrettable at that moment not to have noted down the license plate numbers of those bastards.

Shall I call it fierce and venomous (獰毒)? Even amidst such raging waters, there are humans who refuse to come out, clinging to their household goods and wailing. There are humans trying to die together with their livestock, clinging to them when their own lives are at stake. What is commendable among them are the rescue teams who rescue others regardless of self, risking their lives. A certain strong man rowing a boat alone in the raging waters uses all his might. Unfortunately, one boat carrying household goods is said to have collided with the railway bridge and its occupants became resentful spirits in the water. If this was the scene on the railway bridge at one moment, how can one possibly recount all the tragedies that arose one after another along the river day and night!

The rain continues to pour. It's around 6 PM. The embankment at Sinyongsan has become dangerous. It seems likely to overflow if the water rises by about 1 more ch'ŏk. Railroad workers, firefighters, and soldiers are concentrating more on the embankment than on rescuing refugees. But how can humans contend with the power of nature? As soon as 7 PM struck, the Kyŏngmach'i embankment collapsed. Needless to say, the railroad bureau was flooded,<76> and the entire Sinyongsan and Kuryongsan areas became buried in muddy water. Truly, an even greater calamity had occurred.

Those unable to spend the night amidst the chaos were all heading home. A glance at the capital revealed it too was a water kingdom (水國). Kwanggyot'ong street was wide enough for steamships to navigate, and the Insadong and Nagwŏndong areas were also swirling with muddy water. In some houses, the red waves lapped at the verandas; in others, they reached the floors. Although efforts seemed focused on drainage and pumping, what could be done against the surging flood? Old houses collapsed, temporary structures crumpled, and household goods floated away. The sound of walls collapsing with a rumble, the sound of rain pouring down incessantly, the clanging sound of newspaper extras being distributed! A great turmoil.

After pouring violently all day, it continued to pour throughout the night. Heaven and earth seemed to meet, mountains and rivers seemed overturned, and there appeared to be no way to survive. The precarious electric company in Map'o finally succumbed to the muddy waters. The hydroelectric power from Kŭmgangsan also failed simultaneously. The gas supply also exploded. In the midst of this flood filling the heavens (洪水滔天), the city additionally plunged into a world of darkness; what more needs to be said about the scene in the capital? Is it life or death? News from the riverside became even more anxiously awaited.

July 18th, Just Like Revolutionary Chaos
It's late on the 18th. The continuously sounding sirens from the railroad bureau thoroughly startled the 300,000 citizens to the extreme.
Shall we call it a great revolutionary chaos? Or primordial chaos? Or a general strike? All the institutions of civilization made by the hands of modern humans ceased functioning at once. Trains, telegraph, telephone, streetcars, electric lights, printing presses – all institutions of civilization fell silent simultaneously.
Water level 42 ch'ŏk! Sinyongsan and Kuryongsan embankments breached! Sinyongsan and Kuryongsan in danger! Han River railway bridge collapsed! Traffic to Sinyongsan and Kuryongsan cut off! Ttuksŏm completely destroyed! Waterworks embankment also breached! 7,000 compatriots' life or death uncertain! Full military mobilization! Suicide squads organized! Train lines cut off everywhere! Truly, even among calamities, there has likely never been one like this. Newspaper extras fell like hail, sirens wailed continuously, all transportation was severed, and the raging waters rushed directly towards the capital minute by minute, while the entire Han River area, including Ttuksŏm, Ich'ondong,<77> and Map'o, was swallowed by the sea of mud.

Starting with the Chosŏn Famine Relief Society (朝鮮饑饉救濟會), various newspaper and magazine companies, various youth groups, various ideological groups, women's groups, and youth corps all commenced full mobilization, pouring desperate efforts into relief, but transportation was cut off, and the raging waves and furious billows shook the mountains and rivers, so what could be done? Human power could do nothing.
While the overflowing flood swept the land, how heartbreaking and ghastly it was to see the continuous stream of drifting corpses and houses, but what could be done? No matter how pitifully people cried "Save me!", what could be done? What could even the suicide squads do but die together with them? Fortunately, however, the compatriots of Ttuksŏm were barely evacuated towards Wangsimni, those of East and West Ich'ondong were evacuated towards the far side of the Han River or the Namdaemun direction, and the compatriots of the Map'o area were evacuated towards Kongdŏkri. The fate of the compatriots in Yŏngdŭngp'o and Yanghwado remained unknown.

Gratitude for Brotherly Love
At this critical juncture of life and death, the government authorities couldn't even dispatch a single steamship and just stood trembling, so what more could be trusted? Although the military and police made some effort, we cannot be particularly grateful for that.
Humanitarian love, brotherly love, mutual aid in times of hardship – such words need no further mention at a time like this. Anyway, during this calamity, along with the affected compatriots, I express gratitude to the Chosŏn Famine Relief Society. I express appreciation to the three newspapers: Tong-a, Chosŏn, and Sidae. I express gratitude to the various youth groups and youth corps. I experienced brotherly love and humanitarian love in reality. I was deeply moved, to the point of tears. The rescue efforts over consecutive days and nights by the second and third suicide squads in the Ttuksŏm area, and in Wangsimni, Namdaemun, Yonggŭmnu, Tongmak, Map'o, and Sinsuch'ŏlli areas, though insufficient, could not but make one grateful to fellow countrymen. Even if considered a duty, the gratitude was real.

The Torrential Rain Bows Its Head
From the afternoon of the 18th, the force of the rain finally seemed to abate. However, the water level was still far from receding. The capital joined forces and began relief efforts for the flood victims. Individuals and groups alike were compelled to contribute something, whether physical labor or money. Donations were collected. Relief teams went out. Carrying donation bags (同情袋) and pulling carts, they started visiting homes and soliciting donations on the streets. But what was this about? The police interfered even here.<78>

The 19th somehow dawned brighter. From the early morning of the 19th, the water began to recede. Statistics on human and animal casualties, damage to land and houses, and other general damages are still unknown. Since heaven and earth were turned upside down and shaken, what more needs to be said about the damage?

Stories Heard from Various Sources
Robbery and Theft Even Amidst This Chaos
According to people along the river, even amidst this calamity, there were robberies and thefts. One shouldn't speak of common thieves lacking awareness of propriety, but even amidst this great calamity of floods filling the sky (洪水瀰天), there are said to be robbers and thieves. While picking up and hoarding (拾取隱장) lost houses and items is common practice for riverside dwellers, it's said that there were quite a few instances of specifically targeting disaster-stricken villages to steal or rob the victims' houses or belongings. Relief workers from the Kyojehoe (敎濟會 - possibly Chosŏn Kigŭn Kujehoe?) reported seeing four culprits arrested red-handed.

Shameless Bastards Offer Not a Penny of Sympathy
According to a member of a certain Women's Youth Association donation collection team, when they placed donation bags or carts on the street, explained the tragic situation of the affected brethren to passersby, and asked for any amount of donation, the well-dressed, shameless bastards offered not a single penny (一分) of sympathy. They would reportedly make clever excuses like "I'll give later" or "I'll give on my way back" and slip away. Instead, rickshaw pullers and day laborers were said to be more generous. Japanese people also didn't just pass by without giving. The Japanese residential areas were apparently better. Ah, you shameless bastards! Are you still dreaming only of your own pleasure even at a time like this?

Though Some Died, Many Had Good Fortune
According to riverside people, though some died, many also had good fortune. Many are said to have become rich by collecting lost items. One fellow is said to have mobilized his entire family, collecting day and night, piling up enough to live on for more than three to five years.
Since there are even robbers, what more needs to be said?

"Trying to Ruin Me Alone"
A certain rich fellow in Sŏgang reportedly uttered these malicious words during the downpour. Thinking about his fields collapsing and his goods stacked (績置物) on the riverbank being washed away, he supposedly resented Heaven and cursed (賁) the Earth, saying, "It's trying to ruin me alone." He said this right while witnessing human bodies and houses drifting by. Isn't this fellow capable of robbery too?

Allied Tears of Three Nations
This is from Mr. Chang (張某), the chief of Sinsuch'ŏlli district. He said that Japanese and Chinese people lived next door to him. Unfortunately, the houses of people from all three nations were flooded simultaneously, and while their survival was uncertain, a Japanese person who had luckily escaped met the district chief in a corner amidst the muddy water, grabbed him tightly, and wept profusely. While he himself felt stifled (抑塞) and wept along, a Chinese person suddenly appeared, grabbed him tightly, and also wept profusely. This is the so-called Allied Tears of Three Nations (三國同盟痛哭).<79>

A Friend Who Came 300 Li Riding a Roof
This is from a member of the relief team dispatched to the Ttuksŏm area. He said he met a friend who survived after drifting 300 li (approx. 120 km) riding on a roof amidst the isolated floodwaters. Although the number of friends who drifted downstream from upper regions riding on roofs is unknown, wasn't this friend's survival truly fate (天命)? Drifting down from the distant Kangwŏndo province, he apparently survived near Ttuksŏm by grabbing onto other driftwood. The fate of the 20-odd people who were sitting together on the roof is unknown.

"Why Are You Wearing Scratchy Clothes?"
One friend among the relief workers was wearing a Western suit. While carrying a child from a rescue boat and bringing him ashore, even in this situation, the young person reportedly complained, "Why are you wearing such scratchy clothes?" One should be mindful of clothing even at times like this.

A Boy Who Cried Seeing Another Boy
This is from a member of the Youth Corps visiting affected compatriots. He said he went looking for young affected friends to give them at least a piece of bread. When he saw them, unable to eat or dress properly, trembling in their parents' arms, tears suddenly streamed down his face, and he turned away and wept for a while.
As they say, "A child's heart" (家子의 心 - likely a saying, perhaps similar to 'from the mouths of babes'), the pure heart of a child can be seen at times like these.

Unbearable Sight of Cattle, Horses, and Livestock
In this situation where people are dying and houses are floating away, what leisure (何暇) is there to discuss cattle, horses, and livestock? But witnessing it, it was gruesome and unbearable to watch. Fellows that died with their heads thrown back towards the sky, refusing to drink water; fellows that died with broken noses; fellows lying dead with bloated bellies – it was unbearable to see with one's own eyes. Among them, seeing the corpses of a person and an ox who died together, with the person holding onto the ox's nose ring, how tragic is that?

Drinking Water Calamity
As the waterworks embankment collapsed, the waterworks restricted supply, but even that was insufficient, so they stopped the supply and resorted to distributing small amounts door-to-door via water tankers (配水機). The capital faced another drinking water calamity. There was chaos even in front of Unhyŏn'gung Palace due to drinking water. Thirsty crowds, numbering several dozen, formed groups, carried water buckets on A-frames, and gathered in front of the Unhyŏn'gung gate. Police and attendants joined forces to shut the main gate. The thirsty crowd tried swearing and pleading. The police ultimately refused to listen and just shut the gate and turned away. It's a cruel world.<80>

**Summary**

This harrowing first-person account by Pak Talsŏng describes the devastating Great Flood of July 1925 in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) and its surrounding areas along the Han River. The author details the unprecedented scale of the disaster, the terrifying rise of the floodwaters, the destruction of homes and infrastructure (including power, gas, and transportation), and the immense suffering of the displaced population. Pak vividly portrays scenes of chaos, desperation, and death, while also observing human behavior – criticizing the callousness of the wealthy and indifferent spectators, yet praising the heroic efforts of volunteer rescue teams (particularly Korean civilian groups like the Chosŏn Famine Relief Society and youth organizations) and lamenting the inadequate response of the authorities (implicitly, the Japanese colonial government). The piece concludes with anecdotal observations highlighting theft amidst the chaos, acts of kindness, the suffering of animals, and the subsequent drinking water crisis.

For historians of colonial Korea, this article offers a valuable ground-level perspective on a major natural disaster and its social impact. It starkly reveals the vulnerability of the population, the perceived inadequacy and indifference of the colonial administration contrasted with the mobilization of Korean civil society and nascent nationalist sentiment expressed through "brotherly love" (동포애). The author's sharp social commentary on class divides and his explicit criticism of certain groups provide insight into social tensions and consciousness during the period.

近代初有의 大變亂
世上八亂捧 놈들은 『세상에 3대 구경』이라 하것다. 曰 물구경, 曰 불구경 曰 싸움구경- 이것 이것다. 난봉 놈들의 말을 비러 평생에 첫 구경이라 할가. 엇던 慈悲家의 말을 비러 눈으로 참아 볼 수 업섯다 할가. 여하간 今香의 대홍수는 기자 30평생에 첫 구경이요 세상이 말하기를 수 백년 來 미증유의 대변괴라 하며 혹자는 말하기를 개벽 후 初有의 事라고까지 한다. 그런데

洪水와 朝鮮은 抑何業怨?

沿江사람의 말을 드르면 이 난리 중에도 강도도 잇고 竊盜도 잇섯다 한다. 盜賤놈보고 人事不祥을 말할 것이 업지만 이 洪水瀰天의 대난리 중에도 竊强盜가 잇다 한다. 流失家屋流失物品을 拾取隱장함은 강변사람의 例事이지만 罹災洞里를 專往하야 이재민의 가옥 又는 물품을 竊取 又는 强取함이 여간 만치 안엇다 한다. 현행범 4인을 逮捕해 오는 것을 敎濟會救護班이 보앗다 한다.

빤빤한 놈은 無一分 同情
엇던 女子靑年會 동정금 募集班員의 말을 드르면 同情袋 혹은 동정 수레를 거리에 놋코 來人去客에게 이재형제의 참상을 니약이하고 다소 불구하고 동정하라 하면 양복개나 입은 빤 빤한 놈은 一分의 동정이 업드라 한다. 잇다가 한다든지 갓다오는 길에 한다든지 교모히 핑게를 대고 살작 다라나드라 한다. 도리여 人力車軍 막버리ㅅ軍의 동정이 만트라고 한다. 일본사람도 거저 가지는 안트라 한다. 일본인촌이 오히려 낫드라 한다. 아- 빤빤한 자식들아. 이런 때에도 독향락만 꿈꾸느냐?

죽은 놈은 죽엇서도 數생긴 놈도 만타
江岸사람의 말을 드르면 죽은 놈은 죽엇스되 數생긴 놈도 만타 한다. 流失物拾得으로 부자된 놈도 만타 한다. 엇던 놈은 전가족 총출동으로 連日連夜拾得해 싸 노흔 것이 3년 이상 5년 이상을 먹고도 남으리라 한다.
강도도 잇는데 더 말할 것은 업다.

나 하나만 못 살게 한다고
西江 엇던 부자놈은 폭우 중에 이런 惡言을 하드란다. 전답이 문어지고 江岸績置物이 유실될 것을 생각하야 「나 하나만 못 살게 한다.」고 天을 怨하고 地를 賁하드란다. 人軆와 가옥이 표류됨을 보는 당장에서 이런 말을 하드란다. 이야말로 강도질도 할 놈이 안이냐?

三國人同盟痛哭
新水鐵里 區長張某의 말이다. 자기집 이웃에는 日本人도 살고 中國人도 살엇는데 불행하게도 三國人의 가옥이 일시에 침수가 되야 서로 생사를 未判하든 중인데 다행으로 피신이 된 日本人은 區長 자기를 탁류 중 한 모퉁이에서 맛나 꽉 붓잡고 통곡을 하드란다. 자기도 抑塞하야 가티 우는 중인데 中國人이 툭 튀여들며 꽉 붓잡고 또 통곡을 하드란다. 此 소위 三國同盟痛哭.＜79＞

집웅 타고 3百里 온 친구
*島 방면에 出張햇든 구호반의 말이다. 獨流 중에서 집웅을 타고 3백리나 와서 살어낫다는 친구를 맛내엿다 한다. 상류로서 집웅타고 표류된 친구가 그 수가 얼마인지를 알수 업지마는 이 친구야말로 天命이 안이냐. 저멀리 江原道 땅에서 떠내려 오다가 다른 流木을 붓잡고서 뚝섬 근처에서 살어낫다 한다. 집웅에 同坐하얏든 20여명은 生死未知라 한다.

깔깔한 옷은 왜 입엇소
구호반 중의 한 친구는 양복을 입엇드란다. 救助船 중의 엇던 아동을 업어 下陸을 시키는데 이 중에서도 어린 사람은 「깔깔한 옷은 왜 입으섯소.」하고 不便을 말하드란다. 이런 때에는 옷도 주의할 것이다.

少年을 보고 울은 少年

目不忍見의 牛馬六畜
사람이 죽고 집이 떠내려 가고 하는 이 판에 牛馬六畜을 何暇에 논하랴만은 당하고 보니 끔죽해 못 보겟드란다. 물을 안 먹겟다고 대가리를 한울로 곳추고 죽어 넘어진 놈 코가 깨져서 죽은 놈 배가 퉁퉁부어 넘어진 놈 눈으로 참아 못 보겟드라 한다. 그 중에도 소곱비를 축켜 들고 同死한 人與牛의 사체를 보앗다 하니 얼마나 참혹하냐.
