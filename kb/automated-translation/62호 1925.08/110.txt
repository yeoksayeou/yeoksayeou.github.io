For the Students During Summer Vacation
						Kyŏnjidongin

Now is the peak sweltering time when the scorching sun pours down. The sun, like a tyrant, presses down upon the rivers, mountains, and villages with its scorching heat, much like that which threatens the lives of the Chosŏn people. Bare mountains, arid wildernesses, and desolate farmhouses are engulfed in the searing wind, and the large and small streets make the white-clad people weary as they walk on the sand-scorched ground. Whether looking at the mountains, the villages, or the people, everything is gasping in fatigue and ennui. Amidst this fiery summer of harsh heat, which seems to speak of the miserable lives of the Chosŏn people collapsed in the pit of poverty, I find various feelings stirring anew as I send these few lines of text to you students, scattered in your hometowns for the summer vacation.

Student comrades! As you return to your hometowns, how are you spending this summer vacation, and how do you intend to spend the time until the autumn term begins? Regarding this, of course, those of you immersed in specialized schools will diligently pursue your intended training. And for you middle school students, I know that the admonitions of your educators, who sent you off to your hometowns at the start of the vacation, sufficiently indicated how you should spend the summer break. Thus, some among you will be diligent in reading textbooks or related reference materials; some will relish the joy of travel, crossing mountains, fording waters, and traversing fields; and some, I know, will hum sweet love poems and, following the shade of the zelkova tree, repeat day after day the blissful forgetting of return (興日忘歸) with the sound of the flute. However, what I want to say to you now is not to tell you to read, nor to recommend travel. And furthermore, it is certainly not to talk about playing the flute or loitering around the town. What I want to tell you first is that this summer vacation is a time for you to "think." <57>

When it comes to speaking what we believe, how can we choose the time and place? And as youths of Chosŏn, when is it not a time to think? Yet, for you who have left the school grounds and have leisure time, I believe this is the best time to think. And seeking this time when students should think, the things we wish to speak of are not limited to just one or two.

Yes! Summer is truly a time for thinking.

Then, student comrades! What do you intend to think about this summer? Are you thinking about entrance exams for higher schools? Are you thinking about the problem of finding employment after graduation? Or, from a national perspective, are you thinking for the sake of the nation's hundred-year plan? Or, from the standpoint of the proletarian movement, are you thinking about the liberation of the poor and lowly? Or are you thinking about your love problems, which people treat superficially and negligently with their mouths or pens, but which are in reality a serious issue for each individual? Since none of these are not pressing issues for you, what you think about will also differ according to your individual circumstances, but let us now, together with you, knock on the first door of what we should think about by taking up the recent general issue of student discipline.

That students frequent kisaeng houses, that students get dead drunk and fight, or that a certain specialized school student had an indecent affair (醜行) with a female student from XX school—newspaper reporters express their indignation, loudly spreading rumors, and the police act with unwelcome zeal, claiming to regulate student morals. No matter how much the newspapers clamor, the morality possessed by reporters can hardly correct student discipline. No matter how much the police bustle about, police orders cannot purge student discipline. That is futile interference. Rather than being quick to assign blame for the consequences, I think one should first direct responsibility and apply the axe (斧鉞) towards the causes that give rise to such facts.

Students drink alcohol! Students frequent kisaeng houses or brothels! It is regrettable. Students fight and tease female students! That too is an unseemly matter. The duty of promoting virtue and reproving vice regarding such matters is most suited to your ethics teacher (修身先生), so I leave it <58> to him. Here, I wish to speak not merely as one who engages in preaching morality to you, but as a person who understands your circumstances. However, I do not wish to deliberately turn a blind eye to the fact that you have these circumstances. If, unable to suppress feelings of melancholy (鬱寂) in a lonely inn room, you lift a cup of alcohol, that need not be such a bad thing. And being full of youthful vigor (血氣方强), it cannot be said that fights never happen occasionally. On moonlit nights and flower-viewing mornings (花朝月夕), it cannot be guaranteed that a lonely traveler's heart (客懷), stirred, will not feel the blood boil in the chest following rosy lips (粉紅唇). Isn't this also something that, at times, can only be seen as unavoidable? It is best if there are no troublesome issues whatsoever, but how can one expect perfection or simplicity when many people gather? Therefore, regarding the issue of discipline, we do not wish to place the blame solely on the students involved. We believe that addressing the crux of the matter involves seeking the removal of all surrounding deficiencies, temptations, and the forces that support their existence. Not only that, but regarding discipline, we think we should take a step further from mere interventionism of restraint and punishment (抱束制裁), opening the way for students to self-reflect and self-regulate (自省自律), and that you students yourselves should also pioneer the path of self-reflection and self-regulation.

However, the major problem with discipline does not lie where the ethics teachers (修身先生) worry. The recent issue of discipline in the student world does not hinge on violating passages from the Analects, ethics textbooks (修身書), or collections of maxims. The major problem is that students' moods and emotions are leaning towards decadent pleasure-seeking. Whatever fuss the moralists make about discipline issues, it doesn't matter; that is not the big problem. How can we find a way to live without perishing? Faced with this critical question, the current situation in Chosŏn exhibits the characteristics of a transitional period where old forces and new forces are replacing each other. Will we collapse together with the old forces that inherently contain the seeds of decay (崩壞性), or passively suffer annihilation at the hands of external powers (外園의 他力)? Or will the people of Chosŏn, pulsating with the same lifeblood as the world's newly rising forces, become participants in advancing the axis of history (歷史의 機軸)? Our future path will be determined by which one we choose. What are the so-called old forces? They refer to all relics of feudalism and all culture of the plutocracy-centered system (金閥本位制度). The new forces represent all the emerging momentum centered on the poor (가난뱅이 本位). Thus, the general characteristic of the old forces lies in escapism and decadent tendencies, while the characteristic of the new forces lies in advancement, struggle, and emergence. However, for a society to advance towards a better one, <59> the rise of the emerging class of that time determines its progress or regression. And among those emerging forces, the youth become the very core. Then, do you student comrades, who will become dependable young workers as soon as you leave the school gates, truly possess all the elements to connect your lifeblood with the world's newly rising forces?

Even visiting your classrooms, one cannot see such spirit. Even listening at your speech and debate forums, one cannot discover such spirit. Contrary to that, isn't the learning you pursue often skewed towards the act of acquiring a diploma, and isn't your ideal founded upon individualistic pleasure-seeking? Why should I care about others' misfortune? As long as I study well and succeed myself, that's enough—aren't you filling your heads with the old literal meaning of "establishing oneself and making a name" (立身揚名)? Though the times are such that quickly obtaining a diploma to use as a guide on the path of establishing oneself and making a name is what you might wish to do, comrades! While you are completing middle school or specialized courses to learn the methods of achieving success, have you ever thought, even in your dreams, that countless promising young boys and girls who could graduate from elementary school are collapsing into illiteracy right under the feet where you stand holding your diplomas? Do you know that there are numerous children whose elementary or middle school education is rotting away amidst your family's owned fields and among those cutting fodder, just to provide you with a three or four-year opportunity to study? But perhaps you just dismiss the unequal opportunities as fated? Truly, the philosophy possessed by today's students is rooted in the logic of commercial free competition. If students are awakening ideologically, it is an awakening to bourgeois ideology, plutocracy-centered thought (金閥本位思想). In Chosŏn, where social evolution has been abnormal, because capitalist thought and proletarian class thought arise internally and enter from outside before all feudal relics are abolished, and not far apart in time, today's youth or students mistakenly confuse awakening to bourgeois thought with awakening to the thought of the newly emerging class (신흥계급 사상), or come to believe it is the truest and newest thing that cannot be changed. In a sense, considering the level of popular wisdom (民智), the student class, which forms the main force of the intellectual class, diligently learns and firmly holds onto the old ideas of the old forces, believing the phenomenon of class antagonism to be rational. That too is understandable. In today's Chosŏn circumstances, let alone specialized schools, even attending middle school is difficult unless one is a child or relative of a class with economic privilege. <60> The household that sends the monthly tuition for one person finds it difficult unless they possess wealth (富名). Therefore, it is easy for the thoughts students acquire to often lean towards ideas of wealth and status. Accordingly, haven't they fallen into ease and affluence, eventually pursuing decadent pleasure? Someone might just give up like this: Today's Chosŏn students are all children of the propertied class, so they are not worth pinning hopes on—. However, compared to others, students have fewer constraints in life, are simple and highly impressionable, and furthermore, act with passion. Therefore, they must gaze more intently at the reality of class confrontation and liberate themselves from the decadent pleasure and decadent mood of the crumbling old forces. Thus, at a time when the working class is not yet awakened, farmers are too ignorant, and the intellectual class has not yet formed a core social force, dragging on its days with doubt, convention, stopgap measures (姑息), and secret strife, it is one of the urgent demands of the environment that you students raise your voices and gather strength for the people's life movement. Regarding the current situation in Chosŏn, which places many expectations on you, I wish to add a few more thoughts.

Student comrades! Chosŏn is now mired in the pit of ignorance. The chains of poverty that bind every person clang at every link. This is like lying dead in a grave. Crumbling huts, gaunt figures (形容焦悴) suffering from malnutrition, crowds of unemployed people with slumped shoulders wandering the streets, crowds who die, entire families wiped out after boiling and eating pufferfish roe found while rummaging through Japanese people's trash cans—everything is locked behind the iron gate of death. The tragic harm (慘害) of large and small famines that arise unfailingly every year joins forces with man-made famine to scoop up groups of poor peasants, tens or hundreds of households at a time, and cast them off as fertilizer on the fields of Manchuria (滿洲). Furthermore, how could the untold tragedies of disasters occurring in various places each year possibly be described in words (形言)? Besides this, who can possibly count the tenant farmers (小作人) driven out as the poisonous fangs (毒牙) of the Oriental Development Company (東拓) and Japanese and Korean large landowners sink their roots into every fertile field?

That is not all. Not just in the student world as mentioned earlier, but also socially, the trend of public sentiment flowing towards decadent pleasure is quite prominent. Even if not decadent pleasure, various forms of amusement flourish in various places. The custom of observing holidays is much more prevalent than in previous years. Everywhere, if it's not a wrestling match (씨름판), it's a mountain mask play (산두탈 노름). Advertisements recruiting sightseers to scenic spots (승지유람객) are featured prominently in newspapers <61>, and then various sports competitions eagerly pop up here and there, vying to be hosted.

Everything is like a household that is currently falling apart. It is a household declining without a sound. They are people dying without a voice. They are pitiful and pathetic small-minded XX people (소국민적 XX백성) who, unaware that the thorn embedded in their heart is rotting their own body, worry only about the splinter under their fingernail and snarl at each other over immediate small gains (木前 小利).

Student comrades! Observing this reality, how do you intend to use yourselves? "Ah well! What can I do? I'll just take care of myself and manage my own future..."—throwing yourselves into despair, resignation, and selfish self-preservation (利己的 護身術), will you, after finishing your studies, those with property, just stay at home acting as landlords and creditors, merely guarding (守直) the inheritance from your fathers and grandfathers? Or will you become elementary or middle school teachers receiving the respected title of "teacher" from people, or become bank or company employees or government officials, working like machines during the day and chasing after friends at night, sharing drinks amidst conversation, passing the days without worrying about food and clothing? Or do you intend to use yourselves in some useful way for the present and future of Chosŏn? If you intend to use yourselves for the present and future of Chosŏn, I believe you must have a deep awakening regarding that "Chosŏn" which comes to your mind.

Student comrades! Today within Chosŏn, there exist two spheres of life, and at the same time, two kinds of people. One is the sphere of life of the leisured class (有閑階級) who own land, have others work it by renting it out, lend money, and live affluently. The other is the sphere of life of the impoverished class (貧窮階級) who wander in a state of barbarism, ending and beginning the year with labor. The former are merchants, aristocrats, yangban, landlords, capitalists, government officials, and other defenders of capitalists; the latter are laborers, farmers, low-paid salary earners, etc. Thus, looking at the population ratio of Chosŏn, the latter constitute 80% of the total population, while the former amount to no more than 20%. While these asset-less people (無資리) comprising 80% bear the burden of supporting the entire society, the mere 20% hold all the power of control and have become profit-takers (取利者). Comrades, what we must realize from this is that within the all-encompassing Chosŏn we have seen until now, two Chosŏns lie before our eyes: the Chosŏn of the poor and the Chosŏn of the well-off.

Then, student comrades! For whose Chosŏn will you work? Will you become a brick (煉瓦) <62> to build the Chosŏn of the poor? Or will you work to support the current Chosŏn centered on the well-off?

Student comrades! As mentioned at the beginning (扉頭), summer is a time for thinking. During this summer, think deeply about for whose Chosŏn you will work!

If you work for the Chosŏn of the poor, XXXXX will watch you closely. XX will open its doors and await you. Persecution and poverty will follow behind you. However, if you work to uphold the current Chosŏn of the well-off, you will not receive the designation "XXXX." You will receive the protection and trust of the police. You will have many conveniences for exploiting the poor. The path to building a comfortable life will also open up.

Then, student comrades! Beneath this summer day that makes one think, choose one of the two. <63>

**Summary**

The author, Kyŏnjidongin, addresses Korean students during their summer vacation in 1925, urging them to use this time for deep reflection rather than solely focusing on studies, travel, or leisure. He critiques the superficial concerns about student discipline (drinking, fighting, affairs) often highlighted by newspapers and police, arguing the real issue is a deeper drift towards decadent, individualistic, pleasure-seeking attitudes inherited from feudal and capitalist influences (the "old forces"). He contrasts this with the "new forces" representing the poor majority (80% of the population: laborers, farmers) and implores students, despite many coming from privileged backgrounds, to awaken ideologically, reject bourgeois values, and dedicate themselves to building a new Chosŏn for the impoverished masses, even though this path promises hardship and persecution, rather than upholding the existing exploitative system that benefits the wealthy few (20%).

From a historical perspective, this article is significant for its stark portrayal of class consciousness and social critique within colonial Korea during the mid-1920s, a period often associated with the rise of socialist and nationalist thought. The explicit framing of "two Chosŏns"—one of the rich exploiters and one of the poor masses—and the direct challenge to students to choose which side they will serve, highlights the growing ideological divisions and the urgent call for social transformation felt by intellectuals engaging with the plight of the colonized population. The mention of specific issues like tenant farmer displacement, poverty-driven deaths, the Oriental Development Company (東拓), and the comparison between bourgeois thought and emerging proletarian ideas provides valuable insight into the socio-economic realities and intellectual currents of the time.

夏休中의 學生諸君을 爲하야
						堅志洞人
지금은 불볏이 쏘다저 나리는 한창 뜨거운 때다. 暴君과 가튼 태양은 마치 朝鮮사람의 생활을 위협하는 그것과 흡사히 강과 산과 마을을 불볏으로써 나리 눌느고 잇다. 벌거버슨 산이며, 건조한 야원이며, 황폐한 농가들은 苦熱한 바람속에 휩싸여 잇스며, 크고 적은 길거리들은 모래가 타는 땅바닥으로 困憊한 백의인들을 걸니고 잇다. 산을 바라보나 마을을 바라보나 사람을 바라보나 모든 것이 피곤과 倦厭에 헐덕어리고 잇다. 빈궁의 구렁속에 업드러저 잇는 朝鮮사람의 도탄생활을 말하는 듯한 이 火夏苦炎에, 서서, 하기 휴가를 당하야 향촌으로 뿔뿔이 헤여저 잇는 학생 제군에게 이제 몃 줄의 글빨을 보내게 되매 여러 가지 늣김이 다시금 새로워짐을 까닷게 된다.
학생제군! 제군은 향촌에 도라온 몸으로써 이 하기 휴가를 어떠케 지내고 잇스며 또 추기 개학까지에 그 동안을 어떠케 지내려 하는가. 그것에 관하야는 물론 전문학교에 潛心하고 잇는 제군은 그 뜻간 바에 수련을 부지런이 할 것이오. 또 중학생 제군에 잇서서는 방학 당시에 제군을 고향으로 헤처 보내는 제군의 교육자의 訓話가 족히 제군으로 하여금 하기 휴가를 지낼 방법을 충분히 지시하엿슬 줄 안다. 그러하야 제군 중에 누구는 교과서와 또는 그에 대한 참고서를 보기에 부지런할 것이오, 누구는 산을 넘고 물을 건느며 벌판을 거르면서 여행의 깃붐을 누릴 것이오, 또 누구는 달큼한 연애시나 읇조리며 정자나무 그늘을 따러서 풀늇소리로 興日忘歸를 날마닥 되프리 할 줄 안다. 그러나 내가 이제 제군에게 말하려 하는 것은 제군에게 독서를 말하려 하는 것도 안이오 제군에게 여행을 권고하려 하는 것도 안이오. 그러고 더구나 풀늇이나 물고 邑洞內로 쏘다니는 것을 말하려는 것도 또 물론 안이다. 내가 제군에게 먼저 말하려는 것은 이 하기 휴가는 제군으로 하여금 『생각하게 하는＜57＞시절』이란 것이다.
우리의 밋는 바를 말하기에 당하야 어찌 때와 장소를 가릴 수 잇스며, 또는 朝鮮 청년이 된 이로서 어느 때가 생각 안이할 때랴마는, 학창을 떠나 나와서 한가한 틈을 갓고 잇는 제군에게는 이 때가 가장 생각하기 조흔 때인 줄 미드며 또 학생으로 하여금 생각하게 하는 이 때에 구하야 우리의 말하고 십흔 것도 다만 한 둘에 그칠 것이 안이다.
