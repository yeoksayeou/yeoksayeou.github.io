Magazine Title: 개벽 제62호
Publication Date: 1925년 08월 01일 (August 1, 1925)
Article Title: 두 젊은 사람(選外) (Two Young Men (Runner-up Selection))
Author: 崔文 (Ch'oe Mun)
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Two Young Men
						Ch'oe Mun

!What if we fail again this time? That would be terrible.
-Well, anyway, this is the last time.
Sitting in a shabby car speeding like skin peeling off over the rough, bumpy surface of a newly-made country road paved with crushed stone, they thought about their future prospects as they each smoked a cigarette. This was their conversation as the car passed a cliff edge around a mountain corner. The first question was Sangjik's, and the reply was Mansu's.

-But don't worry. It's bound to work out. Even if it doesn't, we must go with the confidence that it absolutely will.
-Well, yes, that's true, but...
Amidst the occasional honk-honk of the car horn and the whiff of gasoline fumes, feeling their bottoms bounce up and down, their conversation continued intermittently, haltingly, one phrase at a time.

It was the very height of the sweltering *sambok* (the three hottest days of summer). The boiling sun shone down on all the mountains and plains. Wide, expansive plains, mountains both high <14> and low—except for the patches of red earth showing through, everything was green, but under the intense *sambok* sun, unable to withstand the heat, everything seemed to have lost its vigor. Especially the wheat fields, corn fields, bean fields, and rice paddies—the leaves drooped limply, as if suffering, heat-stricken, or scowling. Occasionally, as the car passed along the new road lined with acacia trees, a long-stretching branch would sometimes enter the car and brush Sangjik's shoulder. Each time this happened, Sangjik remembered riding the streetcar in Seoul, heading towards Namdaemun Street or the Ch'ŏngnyangni area. For some reason, he was overcome by unexpected anguish. -Things just don't seem to be working out properly- As such thoughts frequently arose, he became a person floundering in melancholy and despair, pointlessly blaming the heat even more, and more than once letting out long, sigh-like breaths, "Hoo—."

They were currently on their way from H County, the capital (首府) of H Province, to visit Mansu's wealthy friend in Chukch'on (竹村) village, t Township, y County. In the car, besides the driver up front—a Japanese man—there was one man wearing a *kat* (traditional hat) and another wearing a worn Panama hat in front of Sangjik and Mansu. Altogether, there were five passengers. The car, sometimes fast, sometimes slow, would make loud "trut-trut-trut" sounds climbing mountain slopes and speed down like an arrow descending. It crossed bridges, passed through villages, and sometimes traveled through the middle of vast, empty plains stretching on both sides. Among the passengers, however, only Sangjik and Mansu were talking. Sangjik mostly talked about Seoul affairs and city life, while Mansu spoke of the lives of rural farmers. (Mansu was from H County.) But sitting in the fast-moving car, preoccupied with the passing scenery, and amidst all the various noises from the rattling old vehicle, their voices were hard to hear unless they deliberately raised them; their mood was that unsettled.

Occasionally, when the car passed by a stream, they could see village children—naked, dark-skinned kids—who had been bathing, swimming, and catching water striders, forget their play and stand blankly, staring fixedly. Entering a village, they could also see young children who had been playing naked by the roadside tavern drop everything and run <15> into their houses. What struck them most amidst all this was the poverty of everything. The fields were not well-tended (整齊), the mountains, aside from occasional pine trees, were bare and scarred by landslides. Even when a generous feeling and the unique flavor of the local landscape momentarily governed Sangjik's emotions, the feeling of passing through utterly miserable ruins (廢墟) would suddenly flash through his mind like lightning.

But thinking again, it was, in any case, a pleasant prospect. A worthwhile endeavor. To brave this scorching heat, to try and plant something, to start some movement even on these ruins or this decadent, decaying land—leaving behind his studies and his hometown city to wander these mountain valleys, trying to create some work—when he thought of his own courage, and when he thought of himself to some extent as a leader (指導者然), priding himself (自負), an unknown amount of fresh courage welled up in his chest. So, as the car passed a bend in the road, Sangjik saw a white heron that had been sitting by a paddy dike ditch startled into flight. Smiling faintly, he draped his arm over Mansu's shoulder. Mansu said nothing, just sat puffing hard on his cigarette, his body occasionally swaying.

-Where is this place we're supposed to get off and walk to? Is it still far?
-Mm. Not much further now.
-But do we absolutely have to bring that person along? What kind of person is he, anyway, the one we're going to fetch?
-A moralist (道學家). A teacher (先生). Around here, they call him a thinker (思想家). We... we suffered together back during the Manse Movement troubles.
-Mansu, if he's a village moralist, isn't it already obvious? Aren't their so-called ideas completely different from ours?
-No. It's not like that. He may be, so to speak, an old-school thinker, but he's also been influenced by new ideas and is quite progressive. And listen, the person we're going to meet now is absolutely an old-era figure. But this man we're bringing along <16> is nominally a worshipper of new thought. Even though he doesn't know anything. So the idea is to have him act as an intermediary (中間協助) to make things work. Especially since the other party reveres him as a moralist. In other words, we're using him, haha.
-Ah-ha.
Sangjik said nothing more.
Just then, as the car rounded a mountain corner, Mansu shouted.
-Hey, driver! Stop for a moment, please. There's a passenger I need to bring along.
The car stopped immediately. Mansu quickly jumped out. Sangjik got out too.
-Hey, please wait about 15 minutes. We'll bring him right out.
-You have to be quick. There's a scheduled arrival time at the destination.
The driver got out, lit a cigarette, and said this as he watched them go.
-Don't worry.
Mansu and Sangjik climbed up the slight incline.

-Is it far?
-No, right here.
They crossed the ridge and entered a grove of pagoda trees. From the trees, the leisurely chirping sound of cicadas, *seu-reu-ram seu-reu-ram*, could be heard.
-It's quite tranquil.
Walking along the ridge of a vegetable patch, Sangjik noticed a small, roughly 4-5 *kan* (unit of space between columns) thatched house directly opposite, with a wide yard in front. To the side of the gate, facing the yard, was a *sarang* (outer room), bustling with children.
-Ah! It's a village school (글방)! <17>
Following Mansu, a kind of curiosity newly arose in Sangjik.

-My friend, what brings you here?
A middle-aged man emerged from the *sarangbang* (room in the sarangchae) and greeted Mansu very politely. Behind him, children dressed in white trousers, red jackets stained with ink, holding writing brushes horizontally in their mouths, watched these two guests with curious eyes. Although their Western suits were worn out, the fact that they wore suits, glasses, and carried walking sticks made it hardly surprising that they were met with such curious stares upon entering such surroundings (境界). However, the master, the teacher, was certainly not like that. Even seeing Sangjik for the first time, he gave only a brief glance and did not look again.

-Please come up. It's so hot.
-Yes.
Mansu stepped up onto the *maru* (wooden floor). It was an earthen-floored veranda (*t'omaru*). At a glance, it seemed to be a room of about two *kan*, with a *t'omaru* placed in front, covered with a mat. Mansu went up, and Sangjik followed. The three of them ended up sitting in a triangle.

-So, Sibong (a polite term for a friend's father, likely used respectfully here for the teacher), have you been well?
-Yes.
Mansu replied politely.
-So where have you been all this time? P'yŏngyang, they said. I heard the rumor.
-Yes. I was in P'yŏngyang for a bit, and I was in Seoul for a long time.
-Ah. So what did you do while traveling around? Did you have much fun?
-What fun could there be? Just so-so. By the way, Sir— <18>
Mansu gestured towards Sangjik and said to him:
-Please offer your greetings.
At that moment, his and Sangjik's gazes met again.
-I am Yi Sangjik.
Sangjik shifted his somewhat awkward posture as he spoke.
-Yes. I am Pak Chin-O.
The last character of his name was unclear and trailed off.
-He is with me at the Chŏnjin-sa (前進社 - Advance Company) magazine company in Seoul.
Mansu added this introduction.
-Oh?
He looked at Sangjik once more, then turned his head back to Mansu.
-So, what brings you here in this heat?
-Well... Mr. Pak, could you please come with me to see Kim Chinsa in Chukch'on?
-I can go. But why?
-It's just that... I need to ask for your assistance with something, Teacher.
-What assistance? From someone like me?
He smiled modestly. At first glance, he indeed looked like a gentle village scholar. Wearing a *kwangdangp'o* (a type of fine hemp cloth) jacket and cotton trousers, carrying a tobacco pouch, and holding a long pipe, he sat huddled. His face was dark but longish, with large eyes, and he didn't look menacing from any angle; calling him an old-era scholar seemed appropriate. Moreover, wearing a *mang건* (headband) and a *kwan* (traditional hat) covered in white dust made him seem somewhat contemptible in Sangjik's eyes, but his subtle demeanor suggested he was someone respected by everyone in the countryside as a moralist. <19>

-Mr. Pak, it's nothing else, but our magazine company has decided to hold a summer lecture series (夏期講座) from early August to late August this lunar year, inviting a Doctor of Literature and a social scientist (社會科學者)—(he used this term to simplify)—from Japan.
As Mansu's explanatory tone emerged, Mr. Pak showed a very pleased expression.
-And so?
-So, first we'll hold it in Seoul for about a week, and then tour the provinces, visiting the capital city of each province. We currently don't know anything about what literature is, or how the social system (社會制度) works.
-That's right. Literature, ideology... we know nothing about what they are.
He emphasized the word "literature." (From this, we can see his attitude of wanting to appear, above all, as a man of letters.)
-But the cost is about a thousand won. (At this, Mr. Pak looked slightly startled.) We've managed to raise 500 won, and I decided I would somehow arrange for the other 500 won, so I plan to go to Kim Chinsa and try to borrow 500 won. Teacher, please go and speak well on our behalf.
-Well, I suppose I can go, but will it be that easy? He's utterly stubborn.
At that moment, Mansu glanced at Sangjik and smiled. Sangjik also found it slightly amusing inwardly.
-It's not asking for a donation outright. I will write a promissory note, and Teacher, you could perhaps act as guarantor. As soon as the lecture series is over, I'll collect the fees (청징 - likely means collect tuition/fees or contributions).
-Well...
-It might be a bit difficult, but please come with us. <20>
This time, Sangjik spoke somewhat politely.
-Yes, I suppose I'll go, but...
He hesitated slightly. Sensing this, Mansu said:
-Yes, Teacher, let's go. Please get up right away. The car is waiting down on the road below.
-Ah, a car?
-Yes.
-Then that won't do. We can't delay long, so you go ahead. I'll follow right behind.
-On foot?
-Well, how far is it? About 30 *ri*! It's just past one o'clock now, so even if I have lunch and walk slowly, I'll make it easily. Anyway, we won't be able to return today, right?
-That seems likely.
-Then what's the problem... You go first. Take the car, since it's on a schedule.
At this, Mansu stood up. Sangjik also stood up. Mr. Pak followed them out, saying:
-Anyway, you're undertaking a good project.
-Not at all. By the way, Teacher, when did you grow your hair out like that?
-I started growing it right after I got out.
-Since you cut it once, why not just leave it short?
-What's the use of cutting it in the countryside? Besides, it's hard to get it cut; I have to go 20 *ri* to do it.
Mansu laughed heartily, then said:
-You absolutely must come. You must feel a sense of responsibility (責任感).
-Of course, I will. Don't worry. <21>
They parted ways at the entrance to his house.

Back in the car, jolting along again, Sangjik and Mansu sat silently, their bodies swaying.
-Ah! How pathetic. I wonder if the *sensei* (teacher) will even come!
After a while, Sangjik exclaimed this, almost shouting, looking at the distant road visible past the driver, and let out a long sigh.
-He'll probably come. If only for his own reputation. Humph, still, he's considered the most knowledgeable and respected person around here. If someone like him speaks, they might believe it somewhat. Do you think they'd even listen to us?

Before long, the car arrived at the J Township stop, which was only 10 *ri* away, with the loud noise of the propeller (likely referring to the engine fan or just general engine noise) and the horn. Sangjik and Mansu got out. Now, they had to walk 20 *ri* on foot to reach Kim Chinsa's house. It was getting close to lunchtime. They looked for any tavern to get some food. But they were told there was only cold rice and dog meat soup (*kaejangguk*). At the mention of dog meat soup, Sangjik somehow lost his appetite for lunch entirely. Seeing Sangjik sitting there asking for dry, crumbly bean cakes (*k'ongttŏk*), Mansu said:
-City folk are different. If you want meat in a village like this, what is there besides dog meat? You might get some beef on market day.
Mansu got a bowl of the soup and ate it with relish, then stood up. Sangjik ate a couple of pieces of the bean cake and also stood up.

From now on, they trudged along the country mountain path. Sometimes along paddy dikes, sometimes along field ridges, sometimes up mountain slopes—Sangjik, who had always walked on the dusty, hard, dirty streets of the city, felt his emotions purified beyond words, his chest filled with some kind of simple, pure air as he walked through the soft, clean, wide-open fields. Although it was daytime, the surroundings were quiet. Rather, they were desolate. On the slope across the way, <22> a yellow cow stood chewing something nonchalantly, flicking its tail—it looked incredibly peaceful. How often had the footsteps of city dwellers trodden this land? A random question arose in his mind.

-Is that Kim Chinsa fellow very old?
Sangjik brought this up to make conversation, as it was getting boring.
-He must be nearly 70.
-So, you're close friends with his son?
-That's right.
-How old is the son?
-Around 40, I think. He was born late in his father's life.
-Did he get any education?
-Studied Chinese classics in the countryside.
-Then how did he get involved in the Manse Movement?
-He was very passionate back then. One could say he just acted out of passion, following the times.
-How many years were you together?
-He was there for two years, I was there for three.
-What does he do now?
-Probably farming. I haven't seen him either for about 2 or 3 years. I originally got to know him because my father and Kim Chinsa were close friends, and somehow we ended up working together back then. Still, at that time, they were considered thinkers in the countryside.
-Hmm.
Sangjik found himself unknowingly smiling inwardly. But he quickly thought, "Whatever happens, let's wait and see." <23>

Thus, they sometimes sat in the shade of a roadside zelkova tree, smoked cigarettes, sat silently, then got up and walked again. At other times, thinking about the vague battlefield that lay ahead, they slowly walked up the mountain slope.
One man (Sangjik) was a delicate idealist (理想家) raised in the city—but a young man with keen intellect (理智) and strong emotions! The other (Mansu) grew up in the countryside, wandered here and there, trying this and that—he was somewhat duller in perception and his emotions weren't particularly strong, but he was a tenacious young man. (However, neither had ever succeeded in anything.)

Talking, relaxing, and walking like this, they reached the entrance of the village called Chukch'on (竹村) in T Township as the sun began to set. Behind the village, indeed, lay a mountain covered with relatively abundant bamboo, and though the view in front wasn't wide open, a fairly spacious plain stretched out, with a small stream flowing through it. Along the stream bank stood poplar trees, planted sometime long ago; cicadas chirped leisurely from the tall, straight poplars, their sound rising into the evening sky where the sunset glow was beginning to form. Sparrows chirped noisily too. The loud call of a cuckoo echoed from the mountain. Occasionally, farmers carrying loads of fodder on their A-frames (*chige*) walked along the paths from here and there, heading into the village. When they passed Sangjik and Mansu, they seemed to look at the two strangers with curious eyes before walking on. Somehow, everything seemed static (靜的).

-That's the house over there.
Following Mansu's pointing finger, Sangjik looked and saw about 4 or 5 thatched houses. Among them stood a rather grand tile-roofed (瓦) house, which at first glance appeared to be a mix of tile and thatch construction.

The house's watchdog barked fiercely, rushing at the unfamiliar guests. Seeing this, a boy who seemed to be a servant (머슴) ran inside <24> and soon, a man appearing to be the young master of the house came out. Mansu greeted him warmly, almost grabbing his hand. At first sight, Sangjik was disappointed. The man was completely different from the figure he had expected, thinking "Surely he won't be like *that*." He looked like someone who would fit well as a steward (*ch'ŏngjigi*) in a rich country household. He wore Korean socks (*posŏn*) and straw sandals (*chipsin*), Andong hemp trousers (*Andongp'o koŭi*), with a worn-out belt (*hŏritti*) dangling loosely, the trousers slung low on his hips. Consequently, his protruding belly—like a frog's belly—was plump and greasy. His jacket (*chŏksam*) also seemed to be Andong hemp, but it was worn so tightly—though wearing tight clothes was fashionable in the countryside—that his bulging muscles seemed about to burst out, the fabric stretched taut all around. His face was large and dark, with small eyes and an unpleasant-looking mouth. His hair was curly and grown long, tied up in a sort of pine-needle topknot shape, without a *mang건* but wearing a *kwan*.

-Ah—what brings you here?
He seemed to be showing his utmost pleasure, but his face, besides being fleshy, wore that expressionless demeanor typically seen only in the Korean countryside as he greeted Mansu. Sangjik stood right behind Mansu.

-It's been a long time. Have you been well?
This was Mansu's greeting and question combined.
-What fun would I have in the countryside? But I hear you've been having quite a lot of fun. It's been about 2 or 3 years since we last met, hasn't it?
-That's right—
-Well—please come up over there. Gosh, it's so hot. We're in trouble this year with the drought.
-Yes, it is rather dry.
As their conversation continued like this, they followed his lead. Suddenly, Sangjik thought, "Ah—is this the *sarang*?" Looking around, past the main gate and turning right towards a small hill, there was a room similar to the schoolhouse they had seen at Mr. Pak's place, also with a *t'omaru* in front. Anyway, since the master was leading them there, they had no choice <25> but to follow. However, Mansu also seemed to sense some displeasure. Regardless, they ended up sitting on the *t'omaru*. Then Mansu introduced Sangjik to the young master.

-This is Mr. Yi... who graduated from university in Japan and is with our Chŏnjin-sa magazine in Seoul.
After Mansu's introduction, they bowed their heads to each other.
-I am Yi Sangjik.
-Yes. I am Kim Tŏksang.
Once the introductions were over, Sangjik thought to himself, "This guy!" and looked him over again. The sight of that stout figure sitting huddled, smoking a short pipe (*kombangdae*), looked for all the world like someone living comfortably in the countryside, eating well, sitting idly without trouble, occasionally yelling at the servants. As this thought grew, Sangjik felt increasingly displeased. He didn't want to associate with him at all. Sangjik sat quietly, looking out at the scene before him, leaving the conversation to Mansu.

The first thing that caught his eye was a flower bed (花壇), albeit meager! A few pomegranate (石榴) trees, and beneath them, crape myrtle (百日紅), plus a few other nameless flowering plants. Having wilted under the sun all day, they now seemed to barely regain their strength, their branches swaying in the occasional evening breeze. To the left stood a long warehouse (倉庫), perhaps five or six *kan* long, its door firmly secured with a large padlock. In the wide front yard, barley was spread out, and a couple of servants were gathering it back up as evening approached. Occasionally, the pungent smell from a nearby pigsty, and the ammonia stench from a urine pot placed to the right of the room, drifted on the wind to Sangjik's nose. "If they grant our request, the rice bales piled in that warehouse will surely diminish! Is it possible?" As this thought wandered through Sangjik's mind, he smiled inexplicably.

-Is your esteemed father (椿府丈 - *ch'unbujang*) at home?
-Uh-huh, in the inner *sarang*. <26>
-We came to see your esteemed father.
-Huh?
He looked again, this time at Sangjik, with a curious expression.
-By the way, Tŏksang, why aren't you using the main outer *sarang*?
-Oh, it's closed off. Been closed since last year. Family circumstances aren't what they used to be.
-What nonsense are you talking about? In a household like yours.
Mansu let out his usual hearty laugh.
-And when did you grow your hair like that? You hadn't grown it when we met three years ago.
-I started growing it after I met you then. What's the use of cutting it in the countryside?
-Does having a topknot give you some special advantage?
Mansu chuckled again, slapping Tŏksang's knee.
-No, it's not that, but for people like us...
After a brief pause in the conversation, he stood up.
-Mansu, just sit tight for a moment. I need to go inside briefly.
He went inside. Sangjik now took a look around the room. It was rather dim inside. In the upper part of the room (*unmok*), an old man, likely a passing guest (過客), lay sleeping soundly on his side. A little later, another young man entered the room, looked outside, sat down, glanced back and forth between Mansu and Sangjik, and after a short while, introduced himself.
-I am a maternal cousin to the young master here.
He initiated the greeting. Mansu and Sangjik each stated their names and sat quietly, both smoking cigarettes.

The sun was setting in the west. Casting its final yellow light across the fields, it left red clouds in the sky <27> and quietly faded. From somewhere, the sound of a calf lowing could be heard. Evening smoke definitely drifted from the village. A flock of chickens emerging from the field ridges clucked noisily, gathering near the coop attached to the warehouse, about to go inside, bidding a final farewell with even louder calls and fluttering wings. Sitting on the *t'omaru*, Sangjik found the atmosphere of the place unpleasant, but the scenery was extremely natural. Although it was evening, it was still very hot, so Sangjik and Mansu sat fanning themselves. A little later, the young master came out, preceded by an elderly man wearing a *kwan*, walking slowly and with dignity. Sangjik immediately thought, "This must be the real master."

"Oh—it's been a long time, my friend!"
Before even stepping onto the *t'omaru*, he called out upon seeing Mansu. Mansu extinguished his cigarette and stood up. The person sitting inside the room also stood up. Sangjik stood up too. When the old man came up and sat facing Mansu, Mansu bowed deeply.
-Ah, Sibong (again, used respectfully for Mansu's father's friend), are you well?
-Yes.
Mansu replied respectfully.
-Sit down.
At his word, Mansu sat down again. Sangjik also sat. His son, Tŏksang, went to the far end of the *t'omaru* and sat huddled.
-So, you've been well? Why haven't you visited even once?
-Naturally, I've been busy.
-I suppose so. You're always a busy person.
Then his gaze shifted to Sangjik, and Mansu said:
-Please offer your greetings. <28>
Sangjik had seen Mansu bow, but he himself felt reluctant to do so. Moreover, seeing Mansu bow somehow made Sangjik feel more unpleasant than anything else. So, he didn't bow but simply greeted him as he had greeted Mr. Pak earlier. Seeing Mansu bow, Sangjik felt instantly, instinctively, as if he were being subjugated. The old man didn't give his own name but asked various things: Was his home in Seoul? Was Seoul his original hometown? Had Seoul changed much? And so on. Finally, he said:
-Well, sitting here like this, I just read the newspaper and hear news of the world.
He then asked about various things: Was the rumor of war between Japan and America (日米戰爭) true? He heard socialists had appeared recently; what kind of ideology was that? He rambled on, adding his own opinions. As dusk settled and it became quite dark, the dinner table was brought out. Just as they were about to receive their dinner, and the old man stood up to go inside, Mr. Pak appeared in the yard, holding a walking stick, and slowly came up to the *t'omaru*. Everyone stood up. Mansu and Sangjik said:
-You've only just arrived?
The host, the old man, asked:
-Mr. Pak, what brings you here?
Tŏksang bowed respectfully.
-Teacher, you've come?
-Well—Mansu came and asked me to come along, so here I am.
He said this, taking off his outer coat. The host, already standing, said:
-Anyway, let's go to the inner *sarang*.
They disappeared around the corner of the main gate. Despite everything, the person who felt somewhat relieved was Sangjik. For some reason, his arrival made it seem like the task might succeed, and it felt as if their reinforcements had arrived. <29>

A smudge pot (*mogibul*) had been lit in the yard, and the fragrant smell of burning tree bark and still-damp grass enveloped the house. The lights weren't turned on brightly because mosquitoes would gather, but since there was moonlight, the scenery was even better. After finishing dinner, Mansu and Sangjik went down into the yard and strolled around, asking the young master about the farming situation in the area and the living conditions of the farmers.
-For some reason I don't know, the world just keeps getting harder.
This was Tŏksang's remark. Mansu replied, somewhat jokingly:
-How would you know? The son of a rich family, living comfortably, eating and playing.
It was night, so his expression wasn't visible, but he didn't seem particularly angry.
-How would *you* know? I know better. Someone who just wanders around the city all the time.
He actually retorted against Mansu. Mansu just laughed. At that moment:
-Have you boys eaten dinner? There weren't many side dishes... What is there in this remote countryside?
The old man came out, speaking in a loud, dignified voice, his hands clasped behind his back. Occasionally, he rubbed the belly peeking out from his loosened trouser waistband. And so, they gathered around the smudge pot, the old man sitting perched on the *t'omaru*, Sangjik and Mansu squatting in front of him, scratching the ground with matchsticks. Tŏksang was off to the side, instructing two men who looked like servants about getting up early tomorrow to go to the fields, sorting out the next day's work. The cousin who had complained of a stomach ache was inside the room; it wasn't clear if he was lying down or sitting.

-Hey, you boys. Tell me some stories about the world. Is there any interesting news?
The old man asked again, tapping out his pipe and relighting it. Mr. Pak came out later and stood nearby, pacing back and forth. <30>
-Is there really any interesting news? It's just the usual.
This was Mansu's reply. Sangjik sat quietly, saying nothing.
-Ah—right, I wanted to ask you something. Looking at the newspaper lately, it seems youth associations (青年會) are popping up everywhere, here and there. What exactly is a youth association?
-Yes, many are forming. A youth association is a gathering of young people. But there are various kinds. First, there are some that are just recreational groups (遊戱機關), where they gather to chat and play around. There are also purely educational institutions (敎養機關) that train the knowledge of modern youth.
-So, are these associations doing well?
-Of course! If the work of us young people doesn't succeed, what will?
-Heh heh.
He laughed weakly.
-Hey, you know, one formed in this township too. Some kind of youth association or friendship association (親睦會)... But among them are students who've been to Seoul, and all they do day and night is play ball. What kind of behavior is that? I saw them playing around when I went to the market a few days ago.
-Yes. As I mentioned before, there are associations like that, but there are also excellent ones.
-Well, I suppose so.
After a while, puffing hard on his pipe again, he asked:
-By the way. We touched on this briefly earlier, but what are this communism and socialism all about? Gosh, I read about them in the newspaper every day but still don't get it. It seems to be getting more intense lately. What exactly is the ideology trying to achieve? You would know, wouldn't you? <31>
-Yes, well, I don't know much either, but (at this point, Mansu nudged Sangjik hard from behind) I suppose it's an ideology about everyone living well together.
-How do they live well?
He asked pressingly, as if curious and frustrated. At this, Mansu looked at Sangjik.
-Sangjik, why don't you explain it to him?
At this, Sangjik lifted his head and slowly opened his mouth. Mr. Pak also came closer, standing straight, and for a moment, the surroundings in the dark night grew tense.
-Simply put, it's like this. Suppose there's one *mal* (unit of volume) of rice. If ten people share it equally, one *doe* (1/10th of a *mal*) each, everyone would be full. But one person eats five *doe*, and the remaining nine people have to share the leftover five *doe*. Therefore, they go hungry. So, one side becomes unfairly happy, while the other side becomes unfairly unhappy.
Sangjik stopped speaking abruptly. Although he felt slightly dissatisfied with his own explanation, he left it at that and said no more.
-Hm, hm! If the world became like that, it would be quite interesting!
This was Mr. Pak's raspy voice.
-Still, the world needs wealth and poverty, high and low status; that's what makes life worth living.
The old man, with his dignified manner, forcefully tried to overcome the young man's theory with his own assertion. Annoyed by this, Sangjik wanted to say more, but Mansu kept nudging him, and also considering their later goal, he decided to stop.

The country night deepened quietly. Though the distant horizon wasn't visible, even the shadows of the poplar trees in the nearby field furrows were pitch black. Only looking up, the bamboo-covered mountain behind the house crouched silently like some demon god (魔神), sleeping under the starry, dimly moonlit sky. Looking up at the sky, suddenly a shooting star fell. Turning his head, letting his gaze wander aimlessly, he saw fireflies (*pandwi* - 반딧불) flitting here <32> and there in the paddy dikes. The croaking of frogs was clamorous. The buzzing of mole crickets hummed. The chirping of crickets sounded lovely. The whine of mosquitoes, initially just buzzing, eventually landed on his cheek, buzzed near his ear, and flew away. The old man occasionally slapped his thigh, probably bitten by a mosquito. Fanning vigorously with a fan, they were enveloped in the deep night, sometimes talking about themselves, sometimes about world affairs, chattering away. Suddenly, flames flared up from the smudge pot, and they would extinguish it with a stick before returning to their places.

-Sleep well now. It's late. I went bathing in the stream today, so I'm tired.
The old man tapped his knee with the hand holding the pipe and slowly stood up. "Let's have the two of you sleep together. Mr. Pak, you sleep with me," he said, and Mr. Pak followed him inside, glancing back at Mansu and Sangjik. With the old man and the difficult guest gone, the place suddenly became noisy, turning into the young people's world. Several villagers also gathered around. The cousin who had complained of a stomach ache got up from the room and came out to smoke. Because the village, long dreary and desolate, had encountered two unfamiliar guests from Seoul, they sat in a circle (at this point, one of them got up, went somewhere, and brought back a mat, spreading it in the yard). Mostly, they kept asking Sangjik to talk. Perhaps because he looked fair-skinned and sat quietly without saying much, they found him very strange?
-Let's have that gentleman tell us a story.
This seemed to come from more than one person.
-You came back from Japan, right? How was it? Tell us about it.
This was from the young master, the stout Tŏksang. He hadn't spoken much for a while, but perhaps feeling bored and restless himself, he seemed to be urging Sangjik.
-What special stories are there about Japan? It's just... so-so. <33>
-It's much bigger and nicer than Seoul there, right?
came a question from one of the farmers. However, their questions generally stemmed from a kind of vanity, admiring and envying civilization because they were in an unenlightened place. Therefore, Sangjik hesitated for a moment, wondering if it would be right to explain and describe modern civilization to them with words of praise. If he were to curse modern civilization as awful right then and there, they would surely find it strange. On the other hand, if he praised it unconditionally, not only would their hearts become numb, but his own conscience would trouble him. Therefore, he tried to avoid the topic if possible, wanting instead to absorb their inner lives, their spirit... or soul (魂). But beside him sat Tŏksang, the only son of the landowner. Looking around, they would surely avoid such topics, and considering tomorrow's business, he couldn't talk about such things anyway.

So, Sangjik gave them a kind of sketch based only on outward appearances: Tokyo has many more streetcars and automobiles than Seoul, everything is on a large scale, and there are countless six or seven-story buildings. As he sat describing this, a farmer asked:
-Is it true you can earn a lot of money if you go to Tokyo? I heard people from the South (南道) go there and earn quite well.
-Even if you go to Tokyo, it's still like that. You receive more abuse (虐待) and only suffer terribly.
Sangjik spoke with a slightly emotional tone, trying to make an impression on them as much as possible.
-Hey, if you want to go to Tokyo, you have to cut your topknot.
-No, I heard it doesn't matter if you cut it or not. Hey, if it comes to that, I'll cut it, what's the big deal, man!
They also stood up. - As the night deepened - Some stretched, some yawned, and each bid farewell to Sangjik and Mansu before heading home.

Mansu and Sangjik went into the room. Tŏksang spread a mat on the *t'omaru* and slept there. Soon, Tŏksang's snoring <34> sound, *deu-reu-reong deu-reu-reong*, could be heard. For some reason, Sangjik couldn't sleep.
-Hey, Mansu, do you think Mr. Pak is talking to him right about now?
-Well, maybe he is.
Mansu's sleepy reply reached his ears faintly. From the upper part of the room, the cousin and the guest were also snoring. A little later, Sangjik tried nudging Mansu to say something, but there was no response; Mansu just turned over. Sangjik lifted his head and looked out the window. The moon was just bright. But the room was pitch black. The oily smell from the wooden pillow hit his nose pungently. Bedbugs must be biting. Fleas must be jumping. It itched. Mosquitoes whined. Amidst all this, the face of the host, the old man, reappeared in Sangjik's mind. Those gloomy eyes, the protruding mouth, the chapped lips—he was deliberately putting on airs of dignity, but otherwise seemed like nothing. Since he was the type to close off the outer *sarang* because he disliked guests, no matter how Sangjik thought about it, failure seemed likely. Suddenly, the old man's fierce eyes seemed to glare upwards - 'You scoundrel! Giving money to the likes of you...' it seemed to say. Sangjik shut his eyes tight. The ceiling and the room were pitch black.
-Ah—darkness (暗黑)! It is darkness!
He cried out silently, and his head slipped from the wooden pillow onto the floor.

The next morning, Sangjik and Mansu woke early, washed their faces at the well in the yard, and went for a walk in the mountains before breakfast. It was still early morning, and the morning sun lay quietly and silently spread over the wide fields, but the grass leaves on the mountain path were damp with dew. They climbed the mountain, treading gently on the dew-kissed grass, and went under the shade of a pagoda tree on the hill behind the house. Looking down at the wide fields below, they smiled at each other for no reason. Their feelings were generous. <35>

-Well—how will things turn out today? No matter what, I feel like he won't listen.
Sangjik glanced sideways at Mansu.
-Anyway, let's try our best. If he doesn't listen, well, screw it, we'll manage somehow.
Mansu paused slightly, clenched his fist, and punched the pagoda tree in front of him. The pagoda tree made a slight sound and fell silent.
-What good would even saying a few words do?
However, the hopes of the two men were not yet completely dashed. So, after roughly discussing how to approach the situation and what attitude to adopt, they stood silently again. A woman carrying water on her head was climbing up the mountain from below. She wore a coarse hemp skirt that rustled (*pŏsŏk-pŏsŏk*) and a hemp jacket. Since both hands were holding the water jar, her chest (胸部) and breasts (乳房) were slightly exposed. Wiping away water droplets falling from the jar with one hand, she walked slowly up. When her gaze met Sangjik's, she seemed to feel slightly embarrassed. Becoming more careful and demure, she reached Sangjik. Standing by the path, Sangjik quickly stepped aside. Her face grew redder as she passed by. Sangjik turned his head to watch her retreating figure. She was the first woman he had encountered since arriving in this village. He watched her for a long time. A woman carrying a water jar! Walking a mountain path in the morning! As these thoughts occurred to him coincidentally, the woman, like her shadow cast by the ***** light (likely 'morning sun' implied, text corrupted or missing), slowly disappeared into the upper village.

-Breakfast is ready.
Tŏksang came up the path. Mansu and Sangjik immediately followed him down. They had eaten dinner on the *t'omaru* the previous evening, but today they were led into the inner *sarang* (*ansarang*). The host, the old man, and Mr. Pak were also there. After exchanging morning greetings, the breakfast table was brought in shortly. The first thing that caught the eye was chicken soup.
-Thank you for treating us so well. <36>
As Mansu said this, the host replied:
-My friend, what kind of treatment is this? Is killing one chicken considered treatment? Come on, eat up.
They all had breakfast together.

After breakfast, the old man sat in the lower, warmer part of the room (*araenmok*), Mr. Pak sat beside him, and Mansu and Sangjik sat side-by-side in the upper, cooler part (*unmok*). And so, they sat silently for a while, without a word, as if a big fight was about to break out; perhaps blood was churning in each of their chests. After some time passed like this:
-Mansu, I heard from Mr. Pak just now...
His voice trailed off hesitantly. Sensing this, Mansu quickly said:
-Yes, the reason we came is none other than this. We came to ask you to arrange 500 won for us.
At the mention of 500 won, the old man let out a hollow laugh, "Heo-heo."
-My friend, where would I get such a large sum of money?
-So, if you say you don't have the cash right now, we're asking you to help arrange it. I will repay it. You surely heard the details from Mr. Pak, but please lend it to us out of consideration for our project.
-Of course, the project itself is admirable.
-Yes, then there should be no problem. If you just write a promissory note, tomorrow is market day, so we can arrange it at the market below; it will be done instantly.
As the conversation progressed, the atmosphere in the room was not one of mutual compromise or safety; instead, it felt strangely very precarious. First, Sangjik's heart pounded. The old man's face, directly visible, flushed red and blue as he sat stroking his beard aimlessly. Mr. Pak sat quietly, just observing the situation.
-Why don't you speak to your elders and have them arrange it?
-Yes, I spoke to my father and arranged for 500 won. (This was actually a lie.) <37>
-I don't have it.
His words were spoken with absolute finality. The accent at the end of the sentence (語尾) carried the tone used when refusing something awkwardly. Mansu stared intently. Then he turned to look at Sangjik. Sangjik thought inwardly, "Now is the time."

-Sir! (로인장! - *Roinjang!* Old Sir!)
When he called out, the host looked at Sangjik with surprise and shock. He then forced a friendly expression.
-Let me ask you one simple thing. You must have heard the general idea from Mr. Pak, but our magazine company (they had intentionally decided not to mention its content) plans to utilize this summer (夏期) to invite a great literary figure and a social scientist from Japan to hold a summer lecture series. It's not just a lecture meeting, but a lecture series (*kangjwa*), so it will involve recruiting students. Currently, young people generally lack critical thinking skills regarding society and haven't had a chance to thoroughly hear others' opinions, so we want to bring them together to learn about the ideological struggles we face and various complex issues concerning reality. (At this point, the old man started to say something but stopped as Sangjik's speech continued firmly.) So, the expenses are about ₩1,000, and as mentioned earlier, Mansu has arranged ₩500, and we decided to borrow the rest. Also, regarding the project, even considering the lecturers (講師), they are the most influential thinkers in Japan, so many auditors will gather, and we are sure there will be no financial loss. So, if there's no loss, isn't that enough? The debt will certainly be repaid as well... Therefore, if you could lend it to us for a short time, thinking of it as a donation to us young people's project, we will repay it all soon. If you wish to see the budget proposal (豫算案), we have brought it here. Also, if you wish to oversee future matters, we will go together with anyone you choose, Sir, even a relative of yours.
At this point, the old man quickly waved his hand dismissively. <38>
-I understand everything, young man. But in this remote countryside, it's impossible for me, with my means, to arrange 500 won. Anyway, I'm someone who knows nothing about such projects, so let's stop talking about it now. Besides, if there's a bad harvest due to the drought this year, it will be a disaster.
He cut off the conversation abruptly. Mansu moved closer.
-Look here! Why is the door to your warehouse locked? It's full of rice inside. Can't you sell some of that...? (At this, the old man leaned back.) Look inside that cabinet over there. Is there no money? Since you were close to my father, and yet also unrelated, I can say such things, but living in this huge house, living well all by yourself...
He stood up abruptly.
-Mark my words. There will surely come a day when I visit again.
Mansu turned to look at Sangjik. Sangjik also stood up. And so, within less than five minutes, they bid farewell and left the house. Tŏksang, looking somewhat apologetic, escorted them to the village entrance. Mr. Pak seemed to remain at the house, as he didn't follow them...

Like soldiers defeated in battle, their steps dragged heavily as the two figures returned towards their lodging in the town center (邑內) of H County, H Province. Only the farmers working in the fields watched them go, oblivious.
The lecture series was thus completely shattered. It's now been two years (잇해ㅅ재) since this event, but news of them has vanished from the world. According to rumors, Mansu went to Japan, while Sangjik is still said to be just—in Seoul—
-The End-<39>

**Summary**

Two young intellectuals from Seoul, Sangjik and Mansu, travel to the countryside during a sweltering summer in 1925 to raise 500 won for a "Summer Lecture Series" featuring prominent Japanese thinkers. They first attempt to recruit a respected local scholar, Mr. Pak, to mediate with their target donor, the wealthy and conservative elder Kim Chinsa. Despite Mr. Pak's lukewarm participation and eventual non-appearance at the crucial meeting, the young men confront the old man and his equally unimpressive son, Tŏksang. Their passionate plea for funds, framed as an educational project for youth, is flatly rejected by the elder, who cites poverty and mocks their socialist-leaning explanation of inequality. Defeated and disillusioned, they leave empty-handed, their project seemingly doomed.

For historians, the story offers a vivid snapshot of the cultural and ideological clashes in 1920s colonial Korea: the gap between urban intellectuals promoting new ideas and conservative rural elites clinging to tradition and wealth. It highlights the practical difficulties faced by nascent social and educational movements in securing funding, the subtle but pervasive presence of Japanese colonial influence (a Japanese driver, lecturers from Japan, discussion of Japan-US tensions), and provides stark descriptions of rural poverty, social customs (like bowing), and the palpable sense of stagnation and despair among the youth.
