Radio
There's an old saying that even a beautiful song sounds like chopping wood if you hear it all night long, but the "radio" that sweeps through the streets of Kyŏngsŏng city day and night these days always has the same sound, the same person. This brings a monotony and weariness like walking through a desert. The hourglass drum and drum, the kŏmun'go and kayagŭm, the piano and violin, historical tales (yadam) and humorous chats (mandam), it's always the same thing whenever you listen—the "radio" that was novel at first has now become uninteresting.

×

I fear that the sound of those songs, sung by performers(? ■生), with the unhealthy atmosphere and decadent lyrics of drinking houses (yojŏng)—like "Aera, nora, nae monnokkenne" or "Nanbongga" or whatever else is sung while drunk—comes into homes every night, causing considerable spiritual harm to children's education. Putting a "radio" in the home should be for the sake of the home, providing refined taste and being beneficial for children's education. But broadcasting this noisy drum sound every night, these nation-ruining lyrics, the pleasures of delinquents, even into sacred homes, is something to think deeply about. Isn't there a need to cultivate the sound spirit of the general populace, even more than business concerns?
I believe there is a need to create better lyrics and select the program list more carefully.

×

Whether historical talks are done in the style of historical tales (yadam) or humorous chats (mandam), the historical facts cannot be ignored. It is very welcome that the Broadcasting Bureau has recently been broadcasting Korean history stories (Chosŏn sahwa) sequentially, sometimes as yadam, sometimes as mandam. However, whether the speaker occasionally becomes senile, or perhaps the machinery malfunctions and the words are heard incorrectly, that I cannot know, but there are often people who tell nonsensical stories. If it were someone advertising medicine on the street, it might not matter if they shouted, "Ikeu! Pastor Yu (Yu Moksa) is coming out! Pastor Yu is coming out! Don't think Pastor Yu is coming out of some church, a willow-wood snake (Yumoksa) is coming out..." But if one is telling historical stories, at the very least, there should be no errors regarding the people, the era, or the facts. Yet, some people occasionally speak of Silla-era stories as if they were from Koryŏ, and some mix up stories about the Kim clan with those of the Pak clan. Since it's being done anyway, it seems it would be good for the Broadcasting Bureau to select speakers who speak a bit more responsibly, and for the speakers themselves to select their material more responsibly and tell their stories after consulting history.

×

And especially regarding the Home Lectures, which are only held during the daytime, one more word I'd like to add is that although this subject provides practical knowledge very important for our homes, unless one leads a special life using daytime electricity or meters, there is absolutely no way to listen to it. Of course, the Broadcasting Bureau itself likely has its own difficulties, but if they truly want to make this subject most effective, even if there are some circumstances regarding time allocation, couldn't they show the sincerity to resolutely arrange it so that the general public can listen evenly and feel gratitude for its benefits? This is a common complaint among "radio" fans.
×<94>

**Summary**

The article critiques the state of radio broadcasting in 1934 Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). The author finds the programming monotonous and repetitive. Specific criticisms include the broadcasting of popular songs with "decadent" lyrics deemed harmful to children's education, historical inaccuracies in history talk programs, and the inconvenient daytime scheduling of useful "Home Lectures," making them inaccessible to most households. The author calls for more responsible content selection, better lyrics, fact-checking in historical programs, and rescheduling educational content for wider accessibility. From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it reveals early audience reception to radio in colonial Korea, highlighting social anxieties about cultural influence (perceived vulgarity of popular songs), the desire for accurate historical narratives (even via popular media like radio), and practical concerns about access to new technology and its educational potential within the constraints of colonial infrastructure (daytime electricity).

아름다운 노래 소리도 밤새도록 드르면 장작 패는 소리 같다는 옛말이 있으나 近間 京城 市街를 밤이나 낮이나 휩쓰러 버리는 「라듸오」는 어느때 드러도 똑같은 소리 똑같은 사람, 이것은 沙漠을 거러가는 듯한 單調와 倦怠를 가저오는 것이다. 長鼓와 북 거문고와 가야금, 피아노와 바이오린, 野談과 漫談, 어느 때 들어도 꼭 같은 그것─ 처음에 新奇하든 「라듸오」가 인제는 무심하게 되였다.
×
「에아라, 노아라 내 못놓겟네」니, 「난봉가」니 무엇이니 하는 술 醉해서 부르는 料亭의 不健實한 氣分과 頹廢한 歌詞를, ■生의 입으로 부르는 그 노래 소리가, 家庭에까지 每日 밤 드러 와서 子女敎育에 不少한 精神 上 害毒을 받을까보아 두렵다 家庭에 「라듸오」를 놓는 것은 家庭을 위해서 高尙한 趣味와 子女敎育 上 有한 것이 있어야 겟는데 밤마다 씻그럽게 하는 이 장구 소리, 亡國的 歌詞, 不良者의 享樂을, 神聖한 家庭에까지 傳播하는 것은 깁히 생각할 것이다. 營業 그것보다도 一般 民衆의 健全한 精神을 涵養할 必要가 잇지 않은가?
좀더 좋은 歌詞를 新製하며 曲目을 選擇할 必要가 잇다고 본다.
