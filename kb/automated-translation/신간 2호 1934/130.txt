Magazine Title: 개벽
Publication Date: 1934-12-01
Article Title: 柳絮狂風에 춤추는 大洞江의 惡夢, - 三年前朝中人事變의 回顧 (The Nightmare of the Taedong River Dancing in the Willow-Catkin Mad Wind - A Recollection of the Korean-Chinese Incident Three Years Ago)
Author: 琴童 (Kŭmdong)
Article Type: 회고·수기 (Memoir/Recollection)

It has already been three years since the clash between Koreans and Chinese in P'yŏngyang.
At the time, because diplomatic issues between Japan and China were very delicate, newspapers could not provide detailed reports, and because the incident occurred in the middle of the night, few people besides the rioters and curious onlookers witnessed the full sequence of events firsthand, so the truth could not be properly conveyed.
In attempting to write about this tragedy by recalling memories from that time, I ask for the reader's understanding regarding the many points that must inevitably be omitted due to the nature of the incident.

That day was a Wednesday. Around nine o'clock at night...
As I lay reading a book while waiting for the children who had gone to the church for the Wednesday service, I heard the shouts of several hundred people roaring "Waa! Waa!" from the main street yonder. At first, I heard it without much concern. In the early evenings of early summer, it's common for people to gather and make noise happily, so I thought it was just something like that and paid little attention.
However, when the shouting didn't subside and gradually grew louder, I suddenly remembered hearing a detective (刑事) worry earlier that day that there might be an attack on Chinese people sometime today. If this shouting was indeed the sound of an attack on Chinese homes, I became worried about the path my children would take on their way back from the church. So, wearing only my underwear under a simple outer garment, I went out onto the main street to find out the cause of the commotion.
Indeed, it was an attack on Chinese people. A crowd of about a hundred people was surging in front of a Chinese barber shop not far from my house. I ran over there <6> to see.
Two policemen stood with their backs to the barber shop, facing the crowd. The hundred or so people were just continuously shouting towards the barber shop. Inside the shop, the electric lights were bright, but the owner and barbers were gone, and a large mirror had been struck by a stone and shattered like a rainbow pattern.
But that was all. They were just shouting "Waa- Waa-," and it didn't seem like any other trouble would arise. Especially since policemen were already guarding it...
Suddenly overcome by curiosity, I stealthily made my way down towards Pŏpsu-mŏri, the area where the major Chinese merchants were concentrated. The night street. Apart from the shouting still coming from behind, it was the peaceful night street of a city. Along the way, there were two or three Chinese shops – a confectionery, a grocery store – all had their shutters tightly closed, and outside, 2 or 3 policemen each stood guard.
I reached Pŏpsu-mŏri. Unlike the places I had come from, the atmosphere seemed somewhat menacing, with excited crowds clustered here and there, but policemen were interspersed among them, making it seem unlikely that anything further would happen.
After observing for a while, I returned home. It was past ten o'clock. But the children who had gone to the church had not yet returned. What could be wrong?
Should I go out again? Should I wait at home? While hesitating, it passed ten-thirty. From the main street yonder, even louder shouts than before could be heard continuously.
My worry for the children gradually increasing, this time I put on all my clothes and went out again, both to find the children and to see what was happening on the street. The first thing that caught my eye as I stepped onto the main street was the movement of the crowd in front of the barber shop I had seen earlier. Before, the crowd had just surrounded it and shouted, but this time, some chaotic action of coming and going had begun.
Running there at full speed, I saw that the policemen who had been guarding it seemed unable to control the situation and had retreated, while the crowd was in the process of destroying the barber shop with stones and clubs.
I lost any leisure to watch that. My heart grew cold with anxiety about the safety of the children who hadn't returned from church. After searching there, wondering if the children were caught in this throng, I ran along the route from the church to home, carefully scanning the crowds of onlookers on both sides.
Reaching the church and seeing the house already dark with the lights out, I returned home again, but the children were still not back. At this point, utterly frantic, I left the house to search for the children.
It was just past eleven o'clock, the time when the attack on Chinese people began in earnest throughout P'yŏngyang city.
The spectacle on the main street of Nammun that night was <7> a strange sight that I could never forget in my entire life. It was a bizarre scene, unbelievable to have occurred in a place supposedly under the rule of law.
The Nammun-daemun area, where the major Chinese merchants were concentrated, was completely filled with people. Above the people's heads, torn pieces of silk and bolts of cloth flew endlessly. Excited crowds had climbed onto the upper floors of every Chinese trading house, throwing bolts of silk and cloth down onto the street below (packed with people). Numerous bolts of cloth flew through the air, and beneath them, the excited crowd writhed and clamored.
When I arrived there, the torn silk and cloth had just begun to thinly cover the street floor. This layer on the street grew thicker moment by moment. After 30 minutes, it was already thick enough to cover one's ankles. After about an hour, it lay thick enough to easily cover one's knees.
The sharp sound of tearing cloth, the roar of the crowd – it was an astonishing scene of carnage (修羅場). The considerable distance from the government office front to Pŏpsu-mŏri was thickly covered with torn silk and cloth, making it impossible to walk freely.
Pushed and shoved by the people, I found myself in the very center of this pandemonium. All around, the excited crowd continuously screamed "Wanpaoshan (萬寶山)!" "Chinese bastards (되놈)!" busy catching the silk and cloth bolts flying down from the second floor of the trading houses and tearing them apart.
Watching the endless stream of cloth coming down, wondering how much inventory there was, I stood there blankly.
"You rip it too!"
"Son of a X bitch!"
These crowds, who until yesterday – no, even just this afternoon – had exchanged jokes with these Chinese people without any malice, were now incited by a few agitators, doing such unexpected and violent things. I shuddered anew at the terrifying power of "crowd psychology."
What about the lives of the Chinese owners of these trading houses, who had painstakingly built up these roots over several decades? Had they escaped beforehand and avoided harm? Or had they perhaps met misfortune inside these trading houses at the hands of the excited crowd?
"Hey!"
Someone forcefully struck my shoulder, startling me. I turned to see a person, their head wrapped tightly in torn silk, looking at me with accusing eyes.
"Why are you just watching without tearing anything?"
It was an order for me to tear the silk too.
I obeyed him without a word. I couldn't help but pick up one end of a torn serge cloth at my feet and pretend to tear it again. <8>
I saw people, whom I had considered to be of a considerable intellectual class, excitedly directing the crowds. All sorts of rumors (데마) flew around.
- In the Chinese bathhouse in Chŏnju-kol, there happened to be 7 or 8 Korean bathers. When this commotion started, the Chinese owner of the bathhouse rushed into the bathing area with a knife and killed all the naked bathers...
- At the restaurant Tonghwawŏn (東華園), there were several patrons enjoying themselves. When the commotion began, the Chinese staff rushed into the private rooms with knives and killed all the customers and female entertainers (妓生)...
- At a certain trading house (某商館), there were several Korean employees (雇人), who were all brutally murdered...
Exchanging such seemingly plausible stories, the excited crowd busied themselves tearing cloth.
"Mr. Kim!"
Looking, it was an acquaintance.
"Why are you so worked up?"
He was speaking, seeing me pretending to tear the serge.
I gave a wry smile. Then I dropped the piece of serge I was holding at one end.
Stepping away from there, trying to push my way out of the crowd, I hadn't gone more than a few steps when I witnessed a peculiar tragicomedy.
I don't know where he burst out from, but a single Chinese man rushed into the midst of the tens of thousands of excited people. Fortunately, there happened to be a policeman right next to where he emerged. As the crowd surged towards the Chinese man with a roar, the policeman tried to restrain them and protect him. At that moment, the Chinese man, having picked up a stone from somewhere (the whole area nearby was thickly covered knee-deep with silk, cloth, etc.), was looking around, sizing things up.
Undoubtedly, he had lost his reason due to extreme fear. The action of this Chinese man, intending to confront tens of thousands of people with a single stone, could not be seen as the act of a sane person.
"There's a Chinese bastard!"
"Kill him!"
Several policemen rushed through the crowd trying to close in. They took the stone from the bewildered Chinese man who was hesitating, threw it away, and led him away under protection.
From beneath the thickly piled cloth, the body of a Chinese man (said to have revived later) was discovered, loaded onto a cart, and taken to the police station.
Leaving this trading house street in front of the government office, I headed out towards Taedongmun street, intending to see the Chinese restaurant district.
Going from Taedongmun Gate (大同門) along Kangant'ong towards the front of Yuch'un'gwan (又春舘), I saw that Yuch'un'gwan <9> also had its doors completely smashed in. The empty building had only its electric lights on, with no sign of human presence. A little way ahead, the body of a Chinese laborer (seemingly a rickshaw puller) lay on the main street, unguarded and unwatched, startling me.
A few houses further up, across from Changch'un'gwan (長春舘), four or five onlookers were gathered, so I approached and saw two Chinese bodies piled up. Hongsŭngnu (鴻昇樓) also had its doors smashed, and there was a body in front of that building too (as I recall).
In front of the Taedong Automobile Department (大同自動車部), a Chinese man, his back broken backwards into an L-shape, was not yet dead but emitting death rattles (斷末魔). Beside him lay another Chinese body, and a policeman was arguing about whether to drag this body into the garage or not. However, since the violent mob had already passed through here, it was relatively quiet.
Having seen the restaurant district, I went back towards the government office front from before. Reaching the Sinch'ang-ri intersection, I heard the roar of tens of thousands from the direction of the government office, sounding like mountains collapsing. As I ran towards it, the wave of people surging in front of the government office began to push towards me. Simultaneously, mounted policemen (騎馬巡査) finally appeared here and there. The crowd was being driven this way by the mounted police.
Regarding tonight's sudden incident, the police authorities had finally decided on a course of action: to disperse this crowd using armed police.
Among the retreating crowd, another new rumor was spreading: that Chinese people were hiding in the dark alleys with knives, indiscriminately killing passersby.
Even I, who had been wandering around watching the excessively bizarre scenes as if in a dream, felt a chill run down my spine upon hearing this rumor. By now, the children who went to the church had returned home. I immediately ran into the police substation and asked if any Koreans – especially children – had been killed or injured in the chaos. Even if they hadn't met a terrible end at the hands of the Chinese, had anyone been trampled and hurt in the turmoil? The answer was that, as far as was known, not a single Korean had suffered even minor injuries, and the stories of the bathhouse massacre, the restaurant massacre, and so on were all just rumors.
Returning home, I found that the children and the person who had gone out to look for them had all returned, and the house was abuzz with talk about the events witnessed tonight. The children talked about following a branch of the crowd to the train station and seeing Chinese people gathering there with their belongings packed. They spoke of how the policemen were guarding them, preventing anyone from laying a hand on them <10>. They proudly showed two cans of "Anju pickled radish(? 安住のみ取紛)" they claimed to have picked up while a Chinese grocery store was being destroyed, adding that some people were carrying away goods in large bundles.

Being someone who cannot sleep even after slightly exciting events, I couldn't fall asleep this night even when my family, exhausted, went to bed. Around five in the morning, curious about further developments, I slipped out of the house again.
The tragic scene exposed under broad daylight!
This main road of P'yŏngyang, from Pŏmsu-mŏri to the front of the government office, completely cordoned off by police and firefighters prohibiting passage, was buried knee-deep in silk and cloth of all colors. Various colored silks were also draped over the electric tram lines, street lights, telephone wires, and power lines, resembling untimely full-dress ship decorations (滿艦飾). Armed policemen stood guard here and there, and firefighters were busy clearing the textiles covering the road. Onlookers had already gathered in a circle outside the police cordon. After watching this for a while from behind the onlookers, I pushed through the crowd, then through the police line in front of them, and stepped onto the scene of the storm's aftermath. The policeman let me pass without a word, perhaps mistaking me for a newspaper reporter or someone from the prosecutor's office.
Once inside, feeling the stares of the imposing policemen on my back, I went from house to house, peering into the Chinese shops that had been, truly, fundamentally destroyed last night, treading the path of the "silk road" – something humanity had never seen or heard of since the dawn of the world.
Passing the entrance to Sach'ŏn-kol (삿젼ㅅ골), I noticed the unusually grim expression of an armed policeman standing in front of a house there. Leaving the main street, I entered the alley. Without hesitation, I stepped through the gate of the house the policeman was guarding. Upon entering, I saw several newspaper reporters I knew already inside.
On the house's narrow wooden porch (툇마루), lay the body of a Chinese woman, face down. In the storage shed (광), Chinese people lay face down. Thinking they were also corpses, I went closer and saw faint breathing; they weren't quite dead yet. (Though I haven't been able to confirm this point even now) Judging by their build, they seemed to be youths of 17 or 18. And next to them—I still don't know if it was an infant's corpse (嬰兒屍) or a celluloid doll (セルロイド人形). If it was an infant, it couldn't have been more than 3 or 4 months old. Its pinkish hue made it seem possibly like a doll, but the fact that even the private parts (it was female) of the naked object were clearly sculpted made it hard to believe it was a doll. I bent over for a moment and peered at it. Curious to determine its true nature, I wanted to touch it with my finger, but fearing the unpleasant memory that would linger on my finger <11> if it were an infant's corpse, I couldn't bring myself to touch it and just kept looking down.
"Shhh ya. Shhh ya."
Suddenly, this frightened sound came from behind. Turning around, I saw an old Korean man—no, an old Chinese man dressed in Korean clothes—calling out to the dying youths. 柳絮狂風에 춤추는 大洞江의 惡夢, - 三年前朝中人事變의 回顧
						琴童
평양에서의 朝中人 충돌사건이 잇은지도 벌서 3개년이 지냇다.
當時는 日中間의 외교문제가 매우 뗄리케이트하기 때문에 신문에도 詳報는 니르지 못하고 사건이 夜中에 생기기 때문에 직접 폭동군과 好事客 이외에는 상세히 자초지종으로 본 사람이 드물어서 이 진상에 바로 전해지지를 못하엿다.
이 참극을 당시의 기억을 더듬으면서 써 보려함에 임하여 독자에게 양해을 구할 것은 사건이 사건이니만치 부득이 생략치 안흘 수 없는 곳이 만흔 점이다.」

그 날은 수요일이엇다. 밤 아홉시쯤...
수요예배에 교회당에 간 아이들을 기다리며 누워서 책을 읽고 잇노라는데 저편 행길에서 「와!」 「와!」하는 수백명 군중의 포함성이 들렷다. 처음에는 무심히 들엇다. 첫녀름 초저녁에는, 흔히 사람들이 모혀서 좋다고 떠드는 일이 잇스므로 그 일종이거니 하고 무심히 여겨 들엇다.
그러나 그 포함성이 줄지를 안코 차차 더 커갈 때에 문득 생각난 것은 아까 낮에 어떤 刑事에게서 혹은 오늘쯤 中國人습격이 잇슬지로 모르겟다는 걱정을 들은 일이엇다. 만약 지금의 이 포함성이 中人의 집을 습격하는 소리라면 예배당에서 돌아올 아이들의 길이 근심스러웟다. 그래서 푸댄님에 속옷바람으로 포함성의 진상을 알아보려 행길로 나섯다.
과연 중국인 습격이엇다. 우리 집에서 그다지 떨어지지 안흔 곳에 잇든 중국인 리발소앞에 백여명의 군중의 물결이 흐느적거리는 것이엇다. 나는 그리로 달려가＜6＞ 보앗다.
순사 두명이 그 리발소를 등지고, 군중과 마주 서잇다. 백여명 군중은 리발소를 향하여 연방 고함들만 지르고 잇다. 리발소 안에는 전등은 밝아잇스나 주인이며 리발사들은 업고 커다란 체경이 하나는 돌에 마저서 홍채 모양으로 깨여젓다.
그러나 그 뿐이엇다. 와- 와- 고함들만 지즈지 다른 변괴는 생길 듯 싶지 안헛다. 더구나 경관이 벌서 보호하는 이상에야...
거기서 문득 호기심을 이르켜 가지고, 中國巨商들의 집합지인 법수머리로 슬금슬금 나려가 보앗다. 밤의 거리. 뒤에서 여전히 나는 포함성밖에는, 평온한 도회의 밤의 거리엇다. 가는 길에도, 과자장사라 식료품점이라 中國人의 상점이 두세개 잇는데 모도 덧문까지 굳게 닷고 박게는 2, 3명씩의 순사가 경게하고 잇섯다.
법수머리까지 갓다. 거기는 아직것 오든 곳과 달라서 흥분된 듯한 군중들이 여기저기 뭉켜서서 공기가 좀 험악해 보이나, 역시 그 틈틈마다 순사들이 지켜서, 무슨 일이 더는 버러 질듯하지 안헛다.
거기서 좀 살펴보다가 나는 그냥 집으로 돌아왓다. 시간은 열시가 지낫다. 그런데 예배당에 갓든 아이들은 아직 돌아오지 안엇다. 웬일인가.
이것을 또 나가보아야 하나. 집에서 기다려야 하나, 망서리는 동안 열시 반도 지낫다. 저편 행길에서는 또 아까보다 더 큰 포함성이 연하여 들린다.
아이들에게 대한 걱정이 차차 더하여진 나는 이번은 옷을 모도 주서입고 아이들을 차질겸 또 길로 니갓다. 행길에 나서면서 첫 번으로 눈에 띠인 것은, 아까 본 리발소앞에 잇는 군중들의 동요엇다. 아까는 둘러서서 고함만 지르는 군중이건만, 이번은 왓다갓다 무슨 어즈러운 행동이 시작되엇다.
속력을 다하여 거기까지 달려가 보니, 지키고 잇든 순사들도 할 수가 업는지 뒤로 물러서고, 군중들은 돌과 몽치로서 리발소를 파괴하는 중이엇다.
나는 그것을 구경할 마음의 여유를 일헛다. 예배당에서 아직 안돌아오는 아이들의 安否 때문에 가슴이 서늘케된 나는, 거기서 左往右往 아이들이 이 틈에 잇지 안흔가 하고 차저본 뒤에, 예배당에서 집에까지의 通路로 좌우편의 구경꾼들 틈을 주이해 살피면서 달려갓다.
예배당까지 가서 벌서 컹컴하게 불끈 집을 보고, 도로 집으로 도라와 보매 아이들은 아직도 안돌아왓다. 여기서, 집안에서는 통떨처나서 아이들을 차즈려고 집을 떠낫다.
때는 바야흐로 열한시가 좀 지난 때, 평양 시내에서는 중국인 습격이 본격적으로 벌려 진 때엿다.
남문통 일대의 그 밤의 광경은 나의 일생을 통하여＜7＞ 이즐 수 없는 진기한 광경이엇다. 法治地에서 생긴 일이라고는 도저히 믿을 수 업는 기괴한 광경이엇다.
中國人巨商들이 집합지인 南大門 일대는, 사람으로 꼭 메워젓다. 사람들의 머리 위로는 오리가리 찢어진 비단이며 포목필들이 수 없시 날아다닌다. 中國商館마다 다락에도 흥분된 군중들이 가득이 올라가 잇으며 거기서는 아래(사람으로 메워진) 행길을 향하여 비단이며 포목필을 나려던진다. 여러 필이 공중을 날아 다니는 포목필들, 그 아래서 흐느적어리며 야단들 하는 흥분된 군중.
내가 그곳으로 달려간 때는, 겨우 그 찢어진 비단이며 포목들이 길바닥을 엷게 깔은 때엇다. 그 길바닥의 포목들은 刻刻으로 두꺼워 갓다. 30분 뒤에는 그것이 벌서 발목을 감초리만치 두꺼워젓다. 한시간쯤 뒤에는 무릅을 넉넉히 감촌만치 놓아젓다.
포목을 찢는 날카로운 소리, 군중들의 아우성ㅅ소리 그것은 놀라운 수라장이엇다. 관앞에서법수머리까지의 짧지 안흔 거리는 찢기운 비단과 포목으로 두껍게 깔려서, 거름을 자유로히 옴길 수가 없도록 되엿다.
나는 사람들에게 밀리우고 또 밀리워서 어느덧 이 수라장의 복판 가운데 들어와서 잇게가 되엿다. 전후좌우에는 흥분된 군중들이 「만보산」 「되놈」ㅅ소리를 연하여 부르찢으며, 商館2층에서 날아오는 비단 포목들을 받어서 찢기에 분주하엿다.

시외까지?
곳곳이 널려잇든 호떡가가까지?＜12＞
습격이 잇스리라고 경찰에서 알려 준 것은 巨商이며 요리집에 끈첫지, 경찰에서도 그런 구멍가가까지 습격할리라고는 뜻도 안하엿든 모양이다. 그런지라 巨商이며 요리집들에서는 미리 피신을 하여서, 인명의 손상이 적엇다. 거기도 中國人의 특성이 나타나서 피신을 함에 臨하여서도 전부가 避하지를 안코, 金庫지기 한 두 명씩은 남겨 두엇다. 商街에서 밋처뛰지 못하고 참살을 당한 사람들은 모도 이 下級雇人들이엇다. 아까 살전골에서 본 그 참극은 그 집이 염ㅅ집이기 때문에, 避身內指를 엇지 못한 때문이엇다.
그러면, 巨商축에 들지 못한 平壤市內의 수백 軒의 중국인들은 모도 미처 피신치 못하여 참화를 보앗슬 것이다.
이 명랑한 햇빗아래, 平壤市內 각 곳에 널려잇슬 中國人들의 慘死體를 생각할 때에 이것이 꼭 꿈과 같엇다.
시내 각곳에 널려 잇는 호떡장사 목공소 小菓子商 이발소, 돗자리장사, 시외에 나가면서는, 日工노동자의 집들, 야채장사들, 가마공장, 唐麵工場, 건축업자들-이들의 우에 모도 다 참화가 미첫다.
나는 집으로 향하엿다. 몹시 시장을 느끼기 때문에 집에 돌아가서 조반이라도 먹고 어젯밤의 참변이 어떤 정도로 어듸까지나 미첫나 죄 돌아보기 위하여...
거리에 벌서 우글거리는 사람의 무리-이 평화로운 얼굴로서 자긔의 가가를 열고 잇는 이 무리의 중에는 어젯밤에 살기가 등등하여 뭉치를 들고 구석구석 中國人의 집들을 골라 내여서 살육을 감행하든 무리도 석기어 잇지 않을가. -(끗)-＜13＞
