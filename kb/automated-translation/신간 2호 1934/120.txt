A 500-Year History of Korean Factional Strife
						Hong Mokch'un (洪木春)

The Prelude to Factional Strife (黨禍) and the Gyeyu Incident (癸酉事變) of King Tanjong's Reign

King T'aejo of Koryŏ was truly a rare sage king, not only in the history of our Eastern Country (東國史) but certainly in any country of the world. His brilliance in founding the nation, the grand scale and courage of his domestic and foreign strategies, and his plans and institutions are truly admirable. However, what is particularly regrettable is that his excessive faith in Buddhism and emphasis on monks resulted not only in the emergence of numerous eminent monks and virtuous scholars, creating a splendid golden age of Buddhist culture (梵敎文化), but also, conversely, allowed wicked monks and evil priests like Myoch'ŏng (妙淸) before and Pyŏnjo (遍照) after to muddy later politics and bring ruin to the state. Furthermore, as a reaction, the study of Song Neo-Confucianism (宋儒性理) was vigorously imported, initiating the source of the Yi Dynasty's (李朝) reverence for literature (右文) in governance. So-called scholars (士子), using civil service examinations (科宦) and moral philosophy (道學) as weapons, fought for power (權) in the court and reputation (名) in the countryside (野), opening the door to factional strife and national ruin (黨禍亡國), which is truly lamentable. At the end of Koryŏ, as the dynasty's fortune declined and the authority and virtue (威德) of Joseon's King T'aejo grew daily, drawing allegiance from both within and without, the Confucian scholars (儒者) of that time split into two factions. Figures like Chŏng Mong-ju (鄭夢周), Yi Sung-in (李崇仁), Yi Saek (李穡), Wŏn Ch'ŏn-sŏk (元天錫), and Kil Chae (吉再) followed the model of Bo Yi and Shu Qi's (夷齊) integrity (氣節), finding shame in serving (事) two dynasties. Some died as loyal martyrs (危忠) for their country (殉國), while others passed down their unsullied names (淸名) by living in seclusion and refusing office (隱遯不仕). On the other hand, figures like Chŏng To-jŏn (鄭道傳), Cho In-ok (趙仁沃), Yun So-jong (尹紹宗), and Kwŏn Kŭn (權近) abandoned their old lord and devoted their loyalty (貢忠) to the new dynasty, striving to establish careers (事業) and achieve fame (功名). Among these realists (現實派), Chŏng To-jŏn, in particular, possessed outstanding talent and learning, but also had an extremely strong desire for power and position (貪權樂勢)<1> and a tendency to envy the talented and capable (猜賢嫉能); he was an embodiment of avarice. He harmed the families of U Hyŏn-bo (禹玄寶) and Ch'a Wŏn-bu (車原頫), harbored ill will towards Yi Saek (李穡) and his son, fabricating various calamities (構禍) against them, and used forged royal orders to unjustly kill (矯旨枉殺) Yi Sung-in, among other insidious (陰毒) tactics. Eventually, while attempting to eliminate (剪除) Prince Chŏngan (靖安君) (later King T'aejong), his evil fortune (惡運) ran out (己盡), and he was instead executed (被誅) by Prince Chŏngan. Chŏng To-jŏn's execution was not merely the tragic end (末路) of a power-hungry official (權臣) who readily attacked and killed (食攻嗜殺); it also left the stain (汚點) of fratricidal conflict (骨肉相殘) on the first page of the Yi Dynasty's royal court history (王宮吏). He was a traitor (反逆) to the Wang clan (of Koryŏ) and simultaneously a rebellious subject (亂賊) with disloyal (不順) intentions towards the Yi clan. Furthermore, his mother was a low-born servant (賤婢) from U Hyŏn-bo's household who became Chŏng's concubine (妾) and gave birth to To-jŏn. His cruel persecution (肆毒構禍) of his former master U's family caused widespread outrage and indignation (駭憤). In the early years of King T'aejong's reign, the King adopted the suggestion of the Right Deputy Royal Secretary (右副代言) Sŏ Sŏn (徐選) to prohibit illegitimate offspring (庶孼) from holding official positions or prestigious posts (正職淸宦). Thus, the origin (動機) of the 'discrimination against illegitimate offspring' (鏑庶 - should be 嫡庶 distinction, but the text uses 鏑庶) problem was entirely driven by the temporary emotional outburst (興奮) of hatred towards Chŏng. To ostracize (錮廢) tens of thousands of illegitimate sons for generations because of one Chŏng To-jŏn was, of course, an excessively reckless and unreasonable (孟浪無倫) wicked policy and misgovernment (妖案失政). However, considering how rampant (猖狂) his accumulated wickedness (稔惡) and unrestrained cruelty (肆凶) were, causing the harm (害) of one person to affect (及) thousands, it is truly chilling.

The two factions of Confucian scholars—the loyalist faction tracing its roots to Chŏng Mong-ju (鄭夢周) and the realist faction following the stream of Chŏng To-jŏn (鄭道傳)—came under the influence of King Sejong the Great (世宗大王), the Yi Dynasty's fourth monarch and an unparalleled sage king (不世出의 성군). Tempered (陶甄) by the benevolent, wise, and brilliant (寬仁英明) King Sejong, those who advanced (進) to serve in the court (廊廟) and those who retreated (退) to cultivate themselves in seclusion (林泉에 藏修) walked different paths but respected each other's reputation and conduct (名行), associated through morality and righteousness (道義), and were free from envy, resentment, and discord (猜怨反目), exhibiting harmony and cooperation (同寅協恭). This era lasted for over 30 years, during which the Yi Dynasty's benevolent rule (治化) reached its peak (高潮), and the world sang praises (謳歌) of peace. Then, in the 59th year since the founding, Gyeong-O (1450), King Sejong departed from his subjects. King Munjong (文宗), who succeeded him (嗣位), passed away after only two years. King Tanjong (端宗), then merely 12 years old (中齡), inherited the royal lineage (王統) and became the sixth monarch (君主). The King's uncle, Grand Prince Suyang (首陽大君), possessed outstanding spirit and ambition (氣宇英邁) from a young age, filled with boundless aspiration (野心), appearing to everyone as no ordinary figure content to remain obscure (池中物). Yi Suk-pŏn (李叔蕃), an old retainer (老臣) who was a close friend of T'aejong from his princely days (潛邸舊友) and had rendered extraordinary service (功) in assisting the founding (佐命), pointed to Suyang and prophesied, "This child's eyes (兩眸) strongly resemble (酷肖) his grandfather's (乃祖). To satisfy his great ambition (大慾), he will show little mercy (寡恩) to his own flesh and blood." On top of this, having witnessed firsthand the real-life lesson (實物敎育) of Emperor Ch'eng-tsu (成祖) (the Yongle Emperor - 永樂皇帝) of the Ming (明) dynasty, who was not content with his position as a feudal prince (藩王) and coveted (覬覦) his elder nephew's imperial throne (帝位), ultimately succeeding in usurping (簒奪) it, Suyang diligently cultivated his strength and sharpened his resolve (養精蓄銳), bitterly waiting (苦竢) day after day for an opportunity. However, during the reigns of King Sejong and King Munjong, even the ambitious Suyang, suppressed by the authority and virtue (威德) of his father (父君) and elder brother (兄君), dared not pursue his ulterior motives (異圖). Once the era of King Tanjong began, although the young king was inherently composed, intelligent, and exceptionally bright (天質이 비록 穩明英特하다), he was merely a weak child monarch (幼弱한 童君) of 12. With elder statesmen (元老) like Hwang Hŭi (黃喜), Hŏ Cho (許稠), Yi Wŏn (李原), and Maeng Sa-sŏng (孟思誠) having already passed away (凋洛), the court (朝廷) lacked pillar ministers (柱石之臣) of high prestige and authority (威望). Only Kim Chong-sŏ (金宗瑞), a figure of outstanding intelligence and courage (智勇無雙), held office (當局), having received King Munjong's deathbed instructions (顧命). Yet, it was already clear that he could not support the great edifice (大厦) single-handedly (隻手). Prince Suyang rejoiced at the arrival of this opportune moment (好機到來) and gathered unemployed warriors (浪人)<2> and strongmen (장사), plotting day and night.

Kwŏn Nam (權擥), the grandson of Kwŏn Kŭn (權近), had once placed first in the civil service examination and was renowned for his talent and reputation (才名). Possessing great wisdom (智慮) and sharp insight (烱眼), he did not overlook the ambitious Suyang. He sought him out and attempted a great gamble to seize control of the state (乾坤一擲). From the end of King Munjong's reign, Kwŏn frequented Suyang's residence, becoming his chief strategist (謀主) and initiating all plans. Suyang listened intently, forgetting food and sleep when meeting him. Suyang's palace attendants, noting that the Grand Prince would not order his meal tray whenever Kwŏn arrived, complained, "The guest who lets the soup get cold (寒羹郞) is here again," but Suyang's desire for the throne grew hotter in inverse proportion to the cooling soup. Kwŏn had a childhood friend (竹馬莫逆) named Han Myŏng-hoe (韓明澮). He had been born prematurely when his mother accidentally miscarried and died after only seven months (七朔) in the womb; his maternal grandmother took pity and raised the incompletely formed blood clot (一血塊). Even an eight-month baby (八朔) is nowadays called 'Pasak'i' (바삭이) as a substitute for 'fool' (馬鹿), but Han Myŏng-hoe, not even a 'Pasak'i' at seven months, was physically deformed: blind in one eye (隻眼이 瞽), short-statured, and misshapen (短軀怪形), resembling something between a ghost and a human (似鬼似人). However, his mind was extremely cool and meticulous (冷精緻密), hiding resourceful and unpredictable strategies (神出鬼沒하는 智謀) within, and he was bold and audacious (豪爽大膽). Far from being a fool, he considered himself an extraordinary talent and a statesman (奇傑과 國士). Yet, the world did not recognize his talent (才智), and he lacked opportunities for advancement (出世). Despite being a descendant of a prestigious noble family (名門巨閥), he languished in poverty in the alleys (閭巷에 落魄) as a commoner in his forties (四十布衣) until finally being employed in a minor post as a guard at Kyŏngdŏk Palace (敬德宮守直官). One day, Kwŏn Nam strongly recommended Han Myŏng-hoe's exceptional talent (奇才) to Suyang. Taking him along under the cover of night (夜陰) to Suyang's residence, Suyang recognized him at first sight as a lifelong confidant (平生知己), called him "My Zhang Zifang (吾의 張子房)," and treated him with the courtesy due to a military strategist (軍師格). From then on, Suyang's supporters (羽翼) and key aides (股肱) increased daily. Among court officials, almost everyone belonging to the materialistic faction (物慾派) turned their backs on the palace housing the young king and made their way to Suyang's residence. A group seeking fame (功名) and priding themselves on their talent and wisdom (才智), including Chŏng In-ji (鄭麟趾), Chŏng Ch'ang-son (鄭昌孫), Sin Suk-chu (申叔舟), Ch'oe Hang (崔恒), Ku Ch'i-gwan (具致寬), Sŏ Kŏ-jŏng (徐居正), Kim Kuk-kwang (金國光), and Yi Kye-jŏn (李季甸), communicated their intentions overtly and covertly, joining forces before and after. As for military figures (動士), the 'Five Tigers and Eight Bears' (五虎, 八熊) like Hong Yun-sŏng (洪允成), Yang Chŏng (陽汀), Yu Su (柳洙), and Pong Sŏk-chu (奉石柱) gathered, and men of great civil and military talent (文武偉材), each capable of facing a thousand (一驕當千), flocked to him like clouds (그름가티 歸從). At that time, Suyang's influence was truly like a dragon rising to bring rain or a tiger roaring to stir the wind (龍興致雨 虎嘯風冽). No matter how world-surpassing Kim Chong-sŏ's intelligence and courage (蓋世의 智勇) were, the figures on the young king's side were already no match, either in number or quality (質). The talented individuals nurtured during King Sejong the Great's 30-year reign were thus divided: a small number of loyalists (義理派) dedicated their loyalty (節) to the young king, while the majority, the pragmatists (事功派), rushed to join Suyang (馳叅), opening the prelude (序幕) to factional warfare (黨戰).

In the 10th month of the first year of King Tanjong's reign, Gyeyu (癸酉) (482 years ago from now), Grand Prince Suyang, accompanied by warriors (武士) Yang Chŏng (陽汀), Yu Su (柳洙), and palace slave Pak Ye (朴藝), exited Tonŭi Gate (敦義門) and visited Left State Councilor (左議政) Kim Chong-sŏ at his home. Earlier that morning, Suyang had sent his confidant (心腹) Hong Yun-sŏng to spy on Chong-sŏ's movements (動靜). It seemed Chong-sŏ was unaware of how critical the situation had become; he received Hong Yun-sŏng calmly (坦然히 引見) without any security precautions (經費). To test Yun-sŏng's strength (勇力), Chong-sŏ had him break three strong bows (强弓三張) in succession, then praised him as a strong man (壯士)<3>, and had his concubine (侍妾) serve him wine (酌酒賜飮). If Chief State Councilor Kim (金政丞), known to the world as the "Great Tiger" (大虎), was truly unaware of Suyang's faction's conspiracy (陰謀), then that Great Tiger would be unexpectedly careless and incompetent (疎虞無能), like a tiger merely drawn on paper (紙上의 書虎), and consequently, later generations would doubt the judgment (知鑑) of Kings Sejong and Munjong. However, how could a great figure (傑物) like Kim Chong-sŏ be unaware of such machinations (事機) and circumstances (形勢)? He was fully aware (十二分으로 알고 잇섯스나), but given Suyang's status and position (처지), it was an extremely difficult (重難) situation. Before obtaining concrete evidence (確證), he hesitated to strike first (下手), fearing failure might further precipitate the crisis (禍機), or worse, saddle the young king with the infamy (惡名) of harming a close relative (尊屬至親). Moreover, Suyang's faction permeated (遍滿) the court, preventing him from acting rashly (輕遽히 着手). Besides this, his self-confidence (自身이 强한) likely led to the delusion (錯覺) born of bravado (豪膽), thinking, "As long as I am here, how would Suyang dare to incite (煽動) trouble?" Thus, while he spent his days unprepared (아무 준비 업시), Suyang's side had completed all their plans ('푸로그람' - program) and the time was ripe (機運이 익은) to seize the initiative (先發制勝). Based on Hong Yun-sŏng's report of Chong-sŏ's lack of preparation, Suyang decided to execute his extraordinary plan with lightning speed (石火電光的). Chong-sŏ was both surprised and suspicious at the sudden visit (突如來訪) of a prince, a prominent figure, without prior notice, and hesitated to meet him. However, seeing that the prince's entourage was extremely modest (簡率), numbering only two or three attendants, he felt somewhat reassured, thinking his own son Sŭng-gyu (承珪), whose divine courage (神勇) could supposedly match dozens of such men, was sufficient protection. He welcomed Suyang into the main hall (堂), where they exchanged greetings (叙禮) and engaged in idle talk (閒談). Suyang repeatedly signaled (目하야) his warrior to strike down Chief State Councilor Kim with a single blow of the mallet (一椎下), but Sŭng-gyu, standing beside his father (父側에 侍立), never left his side for a moment. Furthermore, court etiquette (體禮) naturally forbade an unnamed warrior from entering the hall (堂에 오르지 못하는) where a prince and a high minister were seated together. If the warrior he had brought were to enter the hall, it was clear Suyang himself would first be harmed by Sŭng-gyu. Therefore, although he feigned composure (自若한 것을 裝하나), inwardly he was extremely anxious (內心은 심히 초조). Then, devising a plan (一計를 思하야), he bid the Chief State Councilor farewell and descended from the hall. When the Chief State Councilor followed him down to the courtyard (庭) to bow in farewell (拜揖), Suyang stopped again in the courtyard, admiring the moonlight (月色을 賞하며) and exchanging a few more words (두어 마듸) of casual conversation. At this moment, Suyang looked at the Chief State Councilor and, as if suddenly remembering something, said, "On the way here, the wind blew off the horn of my official hat (紗帽뿔). I cannot leave wearing a hat missing a horn (一角이 업는 帽紗). If the Chief State Councilor has a spare, please lend me one." The Chief State Councilor turned to his son and asked him to fetch a hat horn. Sŭng-gyu, sensing some ill omen (不吉을 예감) in that instant and realizing with lightning clarity that leaving the spot was extremely dangerous, still could not disobey (違拒) his father's command in front of the noble guest. He forced his reluctant feet inward, moving quickly. By the time he returned with the hat horn (紗帽角), his father, the Chief State Councilor, had already been struck by the warrior's iron mallet (鐵椎), shed blood, and fallen to the ground. Stunned and bewildered (顚倒錯愕), he tried to shield his father's body (父屍) with his own and fight back bravely (奮勇), but being unarmed (手에 寸鐵이 업는), he could do nothing (奈何치 못하야) with his bare fists (空拳) and ultimately succumbed (殉死) to the merciless iron mace (無情한 鐵如意), dying beside his father. However, the resolute and brave (剛勇) Chief State Councilor Kim revived (蘇甦) later that night and attempted to go to the palace despite his injuries (帶傷詣闕), but the city gates were firmly shut (堅閉), leaving him helpless. The next dawn (翌曉), he was caught by warriors sent by Suyang under the pretext of checking on him (看審次), dragged out, and beheaded at the waist (引出腰斬)<4>, enacting a tragedy. Having thus successfully completed the first step of his goal by ambushing (不意에 습격) Kim Chong-sŏ and his son, Grand Prince Suyang entered the city without difficulty thanks to the gate commander (守門將) Hong Tal-son (洪達孫), who was in league with him (예약하얏든). He immediately rushed (馳往) to the residence of Yŏngyangwi (寧陽尉) Chŏng Chong (鄭悰) in Kyodong (校洞), as Yŏngyangwi was King Tanjong's only brother-in-law (親姊夫), and King Tanjong, having moved there for refuge (避接次로 移御), was conducting state affairs (聽政) from that location.

Several hours later, alerted by an urgent secret report (緊急秘密한 啓達) from Royal Secretary (承旨) Ch'oe Hang (崔恒), who was on duty at the Royal Secretariat (分政院에 入直), the young sovereign emerged from his bedchamber to grant an audience (賜謁) to Grand Prince Suyang. The Grand Prince declared, "Grand Prince Anp'yŏng, Yong (安平大君 瑢) (King Sejong's third son, also the young king's uncle), harbors rebellious intentions (逆心). Taking advantage of Your Majesty's youth (幼沖), he plots to endanger the Royal Ancestral Shrine (宗社). Chief State Councilor (領議政) Hwangbo In (皇甫仁), Left State Councilor (左議政) Kim Chong-sŏ (金宗瑞), Right Vice-Councilor (右贊成) Yi Yang (李穰), Minister of Personnel (吏曹判書) Min Sin (閔伸), Minister of War (兵曹判書) Cho Kŭk-kwan (趙克寬), Governor of P'yŏngan Province (平安監司) Cho Su-ryang (趙遂良), and Commander of Hamgil Province (咸鏡道都節制使) Yi Ching-ok (李澄玉) are his key followers (心腹). Coordinating internally and externally (內外和應), the danger (禍機) was imminent (甚急). Therefore, I (臣), leading strong men (壯士), first struck down (擊殺) Chong-sŏ. Acting without reporting (不啓擅行) is my great crime (大罪), but it was an unavoidable measure taken for the sake of the Royal Ancestral Shrine and Your Majesty's sacred person (聖躬). I humbly beseech (伏乞) Your Majesty (殿下) to arrest the rebel faction (逆黨) and restore stability (再安) to the Royal Ancestral Shrine." Even the young king suspected Suyang's accusation (告變) was clearly a blatant fabrication, but Tanjong's position (처지) and circumstances (형세) at that moment left him no choice but to cling to Suyang, pleading, "Second Uncle, please save me," trembling and weeping (戰憟涕泣), and entrusting (委任) him with full authority (全權) over handling the incident (事件 處置). That very night, orders (命牌) were issued summoning all officials (百官) for an urgent audience (急遽入謁). Warriors formed a triple cordon (三重門) around the entrance. As officials arrived, they had to dismiss their attendants (騶從) at the first gate. Upon entering the second gate, they announced their rank and name. Han Myŏng-hoe sat there holding the life-and-death register (生殺簿), comparing names against his list (名簿). Anyone whose name was on the death list (死簿) was immediately sent to the afterlife (鬼籍에 轉入) under the executioners' white blades (白刀) and iron mallets (鐵椎). Only those whose names were on the life list (生簿) could pass the third gate, approach Grand Prince Suyang seated before the throne (御前), bow their heads in submission (叩頭致恭), and inevitably praise (讚美) his great achievement (大勳) in suppressing the rebellion (逆難平定). Beginning with Anp'yŏng and his son, dozens of high officials and elders met disaster (被禍), and the number of their relatives and associates (黨類) killed (誅滅) was uncountable. A royal decree (敎書) was issued, recording the 'merit' (?) of Pacifying the Difficulty (靖難) and bestowing praise (褒美) comparing Suyang to the Duke of Zhou (周公). Grand Prince Suyang became Chief State Councilor concurrently holding the posts of Minister of Personnel and War (領議政 兼判吏, 兵曹事), and Commander-in-Chief of Internal and External Armies (內外兵馬都統使), seizing the power (威權) to decide all military and state affairs (軍國大事). Chŏng In-ji (鄭麟趾) and Han Hwak (韓確) became Left and Right State Councilors, and their partisan allies (一味徒黨) took control (掌握) of the government. The first stage (第一期 公作) of the plot to usurp the throne (簒位) was thus completed without shortcomings (遺憾 업시 竣成). At Yŏngyang Palace (寧陽宮), the mournful cries of ghosts echoed (鬼哭啾啾), and bright red blood flowed freely (碧血淋漓) from the wrongful deaths (寃死) of the loyalist ministers (義理派 公卿). The first act of the factional struggle displayed such a tragic and horrific (悲絶慘絶) scene. <5>

**Summary**

This article, part two of a series on Joseon factionalism, traces its roots to the Goryeo-Joseon transition, contrasting loyalists like Chŏng Mong-ju with pragmatic collaborators like Chŏng To-jŏn, whose actions led to lasting discrimination against illegitimate offspring. After noting the stability under King Sejong, the text provides a detailed, dramatic account of the 1453 coup (Gyeyu Jeongnan). It focuses on Prince Suyang's ambition, the planning by Kwŏn Nam and the shrewd Han Myŏng-hoe, the brutal assassination of the chief loyalist minister Kim Chong-sŏ, and Suyang's subsequent seizure of power from the young King Tanjong under the guise of suppressing a fabricated rebellion, marking a bloody start to intensified factional strife. From a colonial history perspective, the article's lament over "factional strife leading to national ruin" (黨禍亡國) reflects a common narrative emphasized during the period, potentially used implicitly to critique Korea's past political culture or internal weaknesses. Historians might find the detailed, near-contemporary (1934) retelling of the Gyeyu coup and the framing of historical figures like Chŏng To-jŏn and Suyang interesting for understanding how historical narratives about division and power struggles were constructed and perceived during the colonial era.

朝鮮黨爭五百年史
						洪木春
黨禍의 序曲과 端宗癸酉事變
高麗太祖는 우리 東國史는 물론하고 세계 어느 나라에서도 확실히 稀有의 聖君이섯습니다. 得國創業의 光明正大한 것이라든지 內外方略의 宏遠雄邁한 계획시설이 실로 欽仰할 것이 만습니다. 그런데 獨히 유감이 되는 것은 넘우 佛法을 信嗜하고 僧徒를 중시하는 결과 幾多의 高僧碩德이 輩出하야 찬란한 梵敎文化의 황금시대도 做出하얏으나 그 반면에는 前하야 妙淸과 後하야 遍照같은 妖僧凶禿들이 後代의 정치를 濁亂하며 국가를 剝喪함에 이를 뿐 아니라 그의 反動으로 宋儒性理의 學이 盛히 輸入되여 李朝右文의 治學根源을 개시하게 되고 소위 士子가 科宦과 道學을 무기로 삼어 權을 朝에 爭하고 名을 野에 鬪하야 黨禍亡國의 端을 啓하게 된 것은 참으로 嘆惜할 일이 올시다. 麗末에 王朝의 運이 衰하고 朝鮮太祖의 威德이 날로 盛하야 中外가 歸心하게 되매 그 때 儒者는 이에 二派로 난히여 鄭夢周, 李崇仁, 李穡, 元天錫, 吉再 같은 이들은 夷齊의 氣節을 배호와 二姓에 事하기를 耻하고 혹은 危忠으로 殉國하며 혹은 隱遯不仕로 淸名을 傳하는 한편에 鄭道傳, 趙仁沃

鄭夢周를 祖로 하는 의리파와 鄭道傳의 流를 汲한 현실파들의 二派儒士들도 李朝 第四世의 不世出의 성군이시든 世宗大王의 治世에 이르러서는 寬仁英明하신 世宗의 陶甄하신 바가 되여 進하야 廊廟에 오르는 이와 退하야 林泉에 藏修하는 사람이 그들의 거러가는 길은 비록 다르나 서로서로 名行을 지려하며 道義로 交合하야 猜怨反目이 업고 同寅協恭이 잇섯습니다. 이 시대가 30여 년을 계속하야 李朝의 治化가 가장 高潮에 달하고 세상의 태평을 謳歌하더니 開國 59년 庚午에 世宗이 臣民을 버리시고 嗣位하신 文宗이 겨우 2년만에 또 세상을 떠나시여 12세의 中齡으로 게시든 端宗이 왕통을 니여 第6世의 군주가 되섯습니다. 王의 숙부되시는 首陽大君은 幼少時부터 氣宇英邁하고 만만한 야심을 가저 누구의 눈에든지 심상한 池中物이 아니엿스며 太宗의 潛邸舊友로 開國佐命에 비상한 功을 세운 李叔蕃이라는 老臣은 首陽을 가르처 『此兒의 兩眸가 乃祖에 酷肖하니 大慾을 재우기 위하야 골육에게 寡恩하리라』 예언하얏습니다. 그러는 위에 明나라 成祖(永樂皇帝)가 藩王의 位에 甘치 안코 長侄의 帝位를 覬覦하다가 畢竟 簒奪을 遂한 實物敎育을 눈으로 보게 된 뒤로 그는 養精葀„銳하야 기회 잇기를 苦竢한 것이 一日이 아니엿습니다. 그러나 世宗과 文宗의 在世時에는 아무리 야심가인 首陽일지라도 그 父君, 兄君의 威德에 눌니여 감히 異圖를 수행치 못하더니 일차 端宗時代에 入하야는 天質이 비록 穩明英特하다 하야도 겨우 12세의 幼弱한 童君이오 黃喜, 許稠, 李原, 孟思誠 등의 元老들이 임에 凋洛하야 조정에는 威望 놉흔 柱石之臣이 업고 오즉 金宗瑞라는 智勇無雙의 傑物이 잇서 文宗의 顧命을 받어 局에 當하고 잇스나 隻手로서 大厦를 支持치 못할 것도 임에 명백하얏습니다. 首陽은 이에 好機到來를 깃버하야 浪人과＜2＞ 장사를 모와드려 주야로 劃策하얏습니다.

權近의 孫子 權擥은 일즉이 문과에 장원급제하야 才名이 一世에 놉핫습니다. 智慮가 만코 烱眼을 가진 그는 野心잇는 首陽을 그대로 간과치 안코 그를 끄러내어 乾坤一擲의 大賭博을 試하얏습니다. 文宗末年부터 權은 首陽邸에 출입하야 그의 謀主가 되어 모든 설계를 시작하니 首陽은 傾心專聽하야 그를 대할 때에는 침식을 니저버리게 되엿습니다. 首陽의 궁인들은 그가 올 때마다 大君이 밥상 바드실 것을 命치 아니하심으로 『국 식히는 손님(寒羹郞)이 또 왓다』하야 怨嗟하얏지만은 首陽의 郡王慾은 국이 식는 反比例로 熱度가 날로 놉핫습니다. 權의 竹馬莫逆의 友人에 韓明澮라는 사람이 잇섯는데 그는 母胎에 안긴지 7朔時에 그 母氏가 우연 失攝하야 流産死亡한 것을 외조모가 燐恤하야 인체도 완성치 못한 一血塊를 收養하얏습니다. 八朔에 출생한 아희라도 요새 사람은 『馬鹿』의 代用으로 『바삭이』라고 부르는데 바삭이도 못되는 七朔의 韓明澮는 외모는 隻眼이 瞽하고 短軀怪形으로 似鬼似人의 醜劣한 위인이엿스나 두뇌가 극히 냉정 치밀하야 胸底에 神出鬼沒하는 智謀를 감추고 豪爽大膽하야 馬鹿은 컨녕 奇傑과 國士로 自負하얏습니다. 그러나 세상에서는 그의 才智를 아는 이가 업섯고 出世의 기회도 업서 名門巨閥의 後孫임에 不拘하고 四十布衣 閭巷에 落魄하다가 겨우 敬德宮守直官으로 微官에 채용되엿습니다. 權擥은 어느 날 首陽에게 韓明澮의 奇才를 力薦하야 夜陰을 타서 同伴叅邸하얏더니 首陽은 一見에 平生知己로 許하고 『吾의 張子房』이라 하야 軍師格으로 優禮하얏습니다. 이로부터 首陽의  羽翼과 股肱이 날로 늘고 朝官 중에는 物慾派에 속할 만한 사람은 거의 전부가 幼君 잇는 왕궁을 등지고 首陽邸로 발길을 옵기게 되엿습니다. 鄭麟趾, 鄭昌孫, 申叔舟, 崔恒
