발행일	1934년 12월 01일
기사제목	長篇 無絃琴(제2회) (Feature Length: Stringless Zither (Part 2))
필자	廉尙燮 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
기사형태	소설 (Novel)

Feature Length: Stringless Zither
						Yŏm Sangsŏp

Such turmoil once arose because of tobacco. It was early last month. Though it was a daily routine, during the thirty-minute break, after hastily gulping down a stone-cold bento lunch box, Ch'ansu was frantically making trips to the outhouse in the mere ten or fifteen minutes remaining. By chance, Ch'ansu happened to come out of the toilet just as a whirlwind was brewing.
Unaware of what was happening, Ch'ansu stepped into the office and immediately sensed the strange atmosphere. The "Donkey Teacher" (떵키-선생) was standing there, eyes glaring, ranting about something. Seeing Ch'ansu enter, he asked where he had been. Ch'ansu replied he had been relieving himself. When pressed if that was all he did, Ch'ansu confessed he had also smoked a cigarette. It wasn't out of pride in his honesty or fear of the imposing authority; rather, since the intent behind the question was obvious, he thought it would be more embarrassing to try and hide it fussily. The man asked if he had smoked alone. Since it was unlikely that dozens of people had smoked together inside the outhouse, Ch'ansu replied he had smoked alone.
"You don't know this company's rules?" <71>
It felt like a thunderbolt had struck at his feet. At that point, he had nothing more to say.
The general manager (총무), who usually went for lunch, had coincidentally not gone today and had instead gone into the toilet.
People who don't understand might criticize, wondering how someone could lack patience and crave tobacco so much to end up in such a state. However, considering that it's difficult for a smoker to endure nine hours without a cigarette, and especially in this recent weather, after scraping down cold rice, almost crunching like ice, diluted with tea water, along with bits of pickled radish or soy-braised beans, one feels a sour taste rising and the mouth feels queasy, making even non-smokers want a puff—considering this, one cannot solely blame them. Moreover, entering the outhouse, the stench assaults the nose, almost inducing dry heaves, so it's common human nature for one's hand to naturally reach into the pocket for what's there. Besides, if not for this, there is absolutely no other pleasure throughout the day, so it serves as some small comfort.
The Donkey Teacher's scolding was sharp.
"Out! Get out! We have no use for such people."
It was a known pattern: being late even once, whether a long-term employee or someone hired yesterday, resulted in the declaration "Out!" and "We have no use for such people." One word of protest, and the termination pay calculation would immediately land on the desk. Having stepped forward saying "I smoked," as if it were something well done despite the strict prohibition, getting kicked out was a certainty. Ch'ansu had plenty to argue: firstly, why wasn't a smoking room provided? Or, if smoking was utterly banned, was there absolutely no fire allowed in this building? That wasn't the case. In offices without steam (스팀) heating, stoves were lit in every room. So, even if smoking was forbidden during work hours due to hindering duties, couldn't smoking at least be allowed in these rooms during breaks? He had a multitude of arguments, but he knew well that such negotiations or petitions had been submitted two or three times since he joined, only to be rejected uselessly. Why bother arguing back and forth with words that weren't novel? He remained silent and returned to his seat to prepare to leave. <72> Marriage talks were underway, with someone willing to give their daughter away based on his fifty-won monthly salary. If he lost this job, the engagement would likely be broken, and his father's scolding was also a worry. However, he didn't suit this place anyway and hadn't intended to work here until he grew old and died. Getting kicked out felt more like a bittersweet relief. Regarding his family, his father still went out to Chongno, so while he didn't intend to rely on his elderly father, being unemployed for a month or two wouldn't immediately lead to worries about daily meals. He thought it might not be bad to take a break and slowly figure out a change of direction.
"Mister Kim."
The Donkey Teacher, who had been making donkey-braying noises for a while, trying to ascertain Ch'ansu's accomplices, suddenly called Ch'ansu.
"Yes?"
Since the other man was standing, Ch'ansu reluctantly stood up too.
"Did you really smoke alone?"
"I smoked alone."
"Then why was there so much smoke?"
"I smoked a lot."
Though he wasn't particularly close to anyone, since he was unlucky enough to be kicked out anyway, there was no point in dragging others down with him, so he answered this way.
"How many cigarettes did you smoke?"
"Two."
"Two?..." The general manager's eyes widened. "You spent a very long time pooping," he sighed. The phrasing was funny, <73> and from one corner, laughter sounded like a dung beetle crawling on a straw mat.
"How long does it take to smoke one cigarette?"
Being a businessman, he immediately started calculating; it also showed how conscious he was of time.
"About fifteen minutes."
"Fifteen minutes? Then thirty minutes for two. And the break time is thirty minutes. So did you eat your lunch in the outhouse too?"
Ch'ansu remained silent.
"You're lying. Why are you lying? We have no use for such people."
Hearing the "no use" phrase again was annoying, and at this point, he decided he wouldn't stay even if they wanted him. "Don't worry, I'm leaving," he declared defiantly and started clearing his desk drawer to leave.
Anjintari (안진달이), nicknamed "Bell" (방울이), who had rushed over and stood bowing beside the general manager as if a major incident had occurred, quickly followed him out.
"Hey, Mr. An, quickly get me my wages," Ch'ansu said, holding out his hand readily. The wages would be calculated by the day, amounting to only about a week's worth.
"Hold on, what did I tell you! I warned you this would happen someday, told you all to be careful, but you didn't listen..." Anjintari clicked his tongue disapprovingly like a dignified cripple, then added, "That aside, why are you thoughtlessly taking the fall alone?" He urged Ch'ansu to wait and went back into the office, then came out again following the general manager.
It seemed he had managed to pacify the general manager.
When Ch'ansu went back into the office, passing the general manager, his colleagues chided him, asking why he had confessed so honestly. <74> The implication that he had pretended to be particularly honest made Ch'ansu even more uncomfortable.
"When he asked if you smoked for thirty minutes without eating lunch, you should have just stuck to your story and said yes, that would have been the end of it," criticized a friend nicknamed "Squirrel" (다람쥐). Ultimately, the criticism wasn't that Ch'ansu's inconsistent story led to being further scolded as a liar by the Donkey; rather, they were reprimanding him for misspeaking in a way that revealed others had smoked with him.
"If I said I didn't eat lunch and just smoked for thirty minutes, wouldn't he immediately search my drawer and check my lunchbox?" Ch'ansu retorted defensively.
"He's so honest he'd probably poop standing up," they laughed again.
"Not 'poop standing up,' but in our situation, we should say 'smoke standing up'," someone else quipped.
They laughed humorlessly again. As they sat there, seemingly next to the outhouse, humming poop-related tunes without any intention of working, the janitor (규-지) came and called Ch'ansu out. Ch'ansu, who had been sitting with his lunchbox and hat on, said goodbye to everyone and left. Everyone just watched him go while seated, offering cursory farewells like "Leaving? Sorry to see you go. Let's meet up later." Not a single person followed him out. It wasn't that Ch'ansu was particularly disliked or that they were cold; rather, having narrowly escaped a precarious situation where everyone felt their jobs were hanging by a thread, they were simply relieved. There was no room for sympathizing with someone else's misfortune. Some might already be thinking about how to quickly maneuver a friend or relative into the newly vacant position.
The Donkey Teacher, who had called Ch'ansu in, pressed him again, "So, are you leaving?" Considering his earlier haste, the question sounded awkward, but Ch'ansu clearly answered yes. Then, the man yielded on his own. <75>
"However, I'll make a special exception just this once. You lied about smoking two cigarettes, but you are an honest person. That's why I'm forgiving you."
He lied, yet he was honest? What kind of nonsensical talk was this, Ch'ansu thought, just staring blankly at him.
"I know very well the others smoked too. But if I send them all away, our work won't get done. Then, if I don't send them away but send only you away, it would be unfair. I am not an unfair person. So, I'll forgive you just this once. If something like this happens again, you'll be sent away immediately."
So, he wasn't forgiving him for being honest, but because he didn't want to be an unfair person himself. To punish accordingly and still be fair, he'd have to sweep the office clean, which would immediately disrupt the company's work—that was the reason for the forgiveness. It was all self-serving talk derived from putting himself first, but Ch'ansu thought there was perhaps a shred of straightforwardness worth noting. Deciding it was better than being kicked out, even if he quit the next day, Ch'ansu remained silent and decided to stay put.
Returning to the office, everyone acted as if someone dead had come back to life, looking more bewildered than pleased or surprised. "What did he say? What did he say?" they clamored.
"He praised me for putting in so much effort smoking two whole cigarettes in the outhouse and said he'd raise my salary too," Ch'ansu said with a laugh.
"Ha! If I'd known that, I would've said I smoked a whole pack. Damn it! How frustrating!" they exclaimed, perhaps not genuinely, feigning frustration.
Amidst that commotion, Ch'ansu got a chance to see Munhŭi, whom he usually only glanced at and greeted, up close by the general manager's side. At that moment, Munhŭi's expression was one of subtle sympathy, as if feeling sorry for him. Like a primary school kid caught snacking or a middle schooler caught smoking by a teacher, he felt ashamed and embarrassed in front of this woman. He expected her to just dismiss him with a snort as a pathetic man, <76> but seeing her sympathetic look, though unspoken, made him feel secretly grateful. In any case, after this, Ch'ansu resolved never again to even show the smoke of a cigarette upon entering the outhouse, but rather to turn away and leave. Remembering the shame and anger, he truly wanted to quit smoking cold turkey. But Ch'ansu, who couldn't drink alcohol, felt life would be too dull then. Besides, it was sneaking the cigarette that was wrong, not smoking itself—thinking this, he couldn't quite bring himself to make that resolution.
It had already been a month since this turmoil. Thanks to Mr. Donkey's impartiality, perhaps, the fifty won salary remained intact, and the marriage proposal, which might have been jeopardized, had proceeded properly. Now, he was here alone, poring over the ledger, trying to procure the money for the ring to be sent with the *sajudanja* (사주단자, paper listing the groom's birth date and time) scheduled for the day after tomorrow.

As the office workers rushed out, the office servant (사환), no less hurried, entered with a broom and duster. Ignoring whether someone was still seated, as if it were none of his business, he first overturned the remaining embers in the stove, poured water on them raising clouds of dust, and began noisily cleaning out the ashes. Ch'ansu felt annoyed and frankly couldn't open his eyes or nose, but considering this man must also be eager for his cigarette break, he felt some sympathy. More importantly, this man arrived late, by seven in the morning at the earliest, and only left when the electric lights came on, meaning his journey home must be rushed on these cold, short days. Ch'ansu couldn't sit in the dust anyway, but feeling sorry that the cleaning might be delayed because of him, he stopped his work.
"Excuse me, Kwŏn Sŏbang (Mr. Kwŏn), I'm sorry, but could you please go to that room and tell Mr. Anjintal that I'm waiting here?"
He meant to call him politely "Mr. Anjintal" (안진달씨) but inadvertently used the nickname.
The servant went out silently and returned.
"He says to just go home today," he relayed the message. Ch'ansu felt extremely displeased. Whether the money was available or not, who did he think he was, sitting stubbornly just a few steps away <77> and telling him to go home? It was excessively negligent among colleagues, and he felt angry for having waited expectantly all this time, hoping it might work out. Brushing off the thick dust from his overcoat, he walked out alone and desolate. Glancing into the general manager's office, he saw the manager wasn't there, only Anjintari, laughing and talking with Munhŭi, who sat sideways at the typewriter. Anjintari pretended not to see him and continued talking, but Munhŭi, perhaps thinking otherwise, blushed and peeked out through the glass window. Their eyes met, but Ch'ansu quickly turned red and hurried outside. Being asked to come when they were alone together was unlikely, but letting her notice him wheedling for money was one thing; thinking about how pitiful his retreating figure must have looked after receiving the dismissive instruction to "just go home today" made his face flush. Compared to his energetic mood when arriving at the company in the morning, he felt utterly depressed.

4
The next day, upon waking, Ch'ansu's first thought was whether or not to visit Chang Munhoe's house. He wanted to go, but suspected it might have been just an idle thought at the time. He decided it was better to wait until the other party said something more before doing anything.
When he went to greet his father in his room before leaving the house,
"Will it work out today without fail?" his father asked, naggingly repeating what he had said the previous evening.
"Yes..." Ch'ansu replied simply and left. He didn't know if the money would definitely come through today, and it was only upon hearing his father's words that he remembered today was the scheduled day to send the *sajudanja* (사주단자) for Munhŭi.
Ch'ansu wished his father had taken care of it first, but when asked "How will you manage?", trusting that his Christmas bonus would come through, he had casually replied "Yes," creating this worry for himself. Another reason was perhaps not that his father was pushing it onto his son, <78> but that his father, who went out to the market (상게), had developed a taste for lending small sums of money at interest, and likely calculated that it was more profitable for Ch'ansu to borrow twenty or thirty won interest-free than for him to provide it.
During lunchtime today, Ch'ansu asked Chintari again.
"Well... I'm trying to press for it..." he replied evasively. Still, Ch'ansu made a third request, emphasizing that he absolutely needed it by the time he left today. As he was about to go to the toilet after that, Munhŭi seemed to have followed him. As they passed each other, she whispered,
"I'll wait at the Sa-dong intersection later. Please come there. Since you might not know the house..." She spoke quickly and slipped into the toilet before he could reply.
Why is she so eager? Ch'ansu wondered again, though he wasn't displeased.
Closing time arrived. Dreading another awkward delay like yesterday, he hurried up today and urged Anjintari.
"What on earth are you in such a rush for?" Anjintari asked, feigning ignorance again.
"Are you kidding me? Did you just block your ears to everything I've said until now?"
"So, are you really engaged? To whom?"
"Would you know her even if I told you?"
Ch'ansu retorted sharply once more. If his father weren't pushing him, he wouldn't be in such a rush to send the *sajudanja*, feeling rather indifferent, but since he had brought it up, he wanted to see it through somehow.
"So, a man about to get married is seen escorting another young woman around. How can one tell which one is the actual fiancée?" <79>
Chintari suddenly brought this up.
"What are you talking about?"
"You think I don't know? The tea at Mitsukoshi (미쓰고시) must have tasted exceptionally different, huh?" he sneered, his eyes narrowing maliciously.
"Oh, that. What's the big deal about having tea together by chance? And that was because she asked me to walk her to the library because the ground was slippery..."
"Hahaha... Did she ask you to hold her wrist because the ground was slippery? Or support her by the armpit? Hahaha... Quite an excuse."
Ch'ansu wanted to snap back, "Whatever others say, what business is it of yours?" or "Why do you care?" but held back, fearing the money he needed might shrink away.
"So, is that ring you're getting meant as a present (푸레센트) for that young lady?"
"Which young lady? It's for your future grandmother-in-law to wear," Ch'ansu quipped, forcing a joke he didn't feel.
In the end, the money still hadn't materialized by closing time. Inevitably, Ch'ansu fell behind again today.
"Working late again? You're too diligent, aren't you?"
His colleagues jeered again. Besides their resentment over the smoking incident, there was also general animosity towards the company. Forced to arrive by eight AM, year-round, as if bound by debt, even in the short days of midwinter, being even two or three minutes late prompted remarks like, "Drinking again yesterday? We have no use for such lazy people." Being late just two or three times meant being told to leave. Consequently, the employees made it a rule never to delay their departure time by even a minute. No matter how unfinished their work was, they would drop everything and stand up—this habit was considered their only retaliation and resistance, <80> reflecting their sharp resentment towards the company. Thus, seeing Ch'ansu recently deviate from their synchronized actions made them treat him like a traitor who had deserted their ranks. Furthermore, although it was fortunate that the person who was about to be fired immediately over the cigarette incident was instead praised as an "honest person" and allowed to stay, they felt uncomfortable with the implication that they themselves were dishonest. Added to that, the fact that he had completely stopped his outhouse visits (though perhaps he smoked at home) seemed like another step away from them, which wasn't welcome. Lately, seeing him lag behind for three or four days, working longer, struck them as odd. However, Ch'ansu sometimes ignored their jokes indifferently, feeling no need to explain he was doing it to get money.
Today, after waiting for an hour, he finally managed to get thirty won and left. However, Munhŭi had apparently said she had something to do at home and left earlier, almost with the other employees. He worried she might be waiting alone, shivering on the street, and his heart pounded anxiously.
The electric lights had been on for a long time, and the day had grown completely dark. Still, he had to go to the place she mentioned. He wished she had just gone home without waiting, and indeed, it seemed unlikely she would wait for a whole hour until dark. Nevertheless, he frantically hailed a taxi and rode to the front of Umi-gwan (우미관) Theater. As he jumped out, Munhŭi appeared from somewhere behind him.
"You're just arriving now?" she asked quietly, making her presence known.
"Have you been waiting all this time? I assumed you'd have gone home."
"Of course not... Anyway, did you get the money?"
Since she shared an office with Anjintari, she already seemed to know everything.
"Yes... barely..."
"So, did you buy the ring on your way?"
"What ring?" <81>
Ch'ansu laughed awkwardly.
"If you haven't bought it, let's go buy it together now. You said today's the day to send it, right?" Munhŭi insisted, taking the lead. Ch'ansu felt his nose tingle, realizing she even knew today was the day for the *sajudanja*.
"Let's go quickly. Let me see the ring your fiancée (선생님 아씨) will wear."
"Does my fiancée wear a special kind of ring?"
Feeling awkward and embarrassed by the nagging, Ch'ansu deflected with a joke.
"Anyway, let me buy it for you. If you couldn't get it today because of me, that wouldn't do. Besides, maybe this is some kind of fate too; let me witness the first purchase for the wedding preparations."
Though it might be idle jest, he couldn't refuse her insistence as she pressed forward, taking the lead, for reasons he couldn't fathom. Ch'ansu followed her back up towards Chongno. He had thought she was different from ordinary women, but this also seemed strange. It might signify she thought nothing of marriage, yet while appearing outwardly nonchalant, perhaps something bothered her inwardly.
"Mr. An found out we went to Mitsukoshi the other day?"
Ch'ansu brought this up as they walked, wanting to gauge her reaction.
"Why, what did he say?"
"Did you tell him?"
"Why would I tell him that? He said he saw us leaving from the post office (우칭) that day. When he asked where we went, there was no point hiding it, so I told him we went to Mitsukoshi." <82>
Ch'ansu didn't like hearing this. It wasn't a secret, but he couldn't understand the woman's motive for telling that particular person, and he disliked the thought of useless rumors spreading.
"Anyway, the world is frightening, isn't it? Mr. An must have thought the ring you're buying now was coming to me. He asked me if he should give you the thirty won. I told him it wasn't for me to say, but wouldn't it be disastrous not to give it when it's for someone's marriage? Then he asked where we were going today now that thirty won had materialized, so I told him we promised to go to the Chosŏn Theater."
"Why did you say that?" Ch'ansu asked reproachfully.
"What's wrong with teasing him a bit? He said he was going to the Chosŏn Theater too. It would be funny if he followed us and ended up on a wild goose chase, but surely he's not that foolish," she scoffed. Still, Ch'ansu couldn't fathom the woman's heart. Was she being kind to him? Was she trying to provoke and tease Anjintari because he showed suspicious signs? Or was she using Ch'ansu in a common, vulgar ploy to attract Anjintari? Since he already secretly suspected she wasn't someone to be taken lightly, he couldn't figure her out.
In any case, it seemed Anjintari was trying hard to monitor Munhŭi's every move. Hearing her words, Ch'ansu recalled Anjintari showing similar signs towards him too. It seemed the reason he had held back the money for two days, despite being pressed, was because he was displeased about the Mitsukoshi outing with Munhŭi. What a scary world, rather than a scary person, he thought.
"But Mr. An is shallow, that's why he's like that. Anyway, he's an interesting fellow."
Ch'ansu didn't like hearing her call him "interesting" either.
Munhŭi was skillful at bargaining too. Pushing Ch'ansu aside, she picked out items she liked, telling him just to pay. The clerk, assuming Munhŭi was the one buying the ring, suggested, <83> "Why don't you try it on?"
"We came with a sample, so we know. Why would I try on someone else's wedding ring?" she snapped back, making the clerk look awkward.
"Now you need to buy a gold hairpin (금비녀) too, right? And the trousseau items (뒤꼬지) have to be prepared item by item... Soon, Koreans are supposed to hit a gold bonanza. Maybe you should get married after the price of gold drops?"
Munhŭi chuckled as they left the shop. Indeed, the price of gold had more than doubled, and despite getting only a few pennies back from the thirty won, the ring was pitifully small, barely two *pun* (푼, unit of weight) with tiny adornments.
"She wouldn't expect a gold hairpin from a fifty-won groom, would she?"
Ch'ansu scoffed like this, following Munhŭi's lead down a quiet shortcut. Munhŭi probably chose the side street partly for conversation, but also perhaps fearing they might run into Anjintari or other company people if they walked shoulder-to-shoulder on the main road.
"So the groom comes with a price tag, huh? A price tag worth less than a gold hairpin..." Munhŭi laughed again.
"It's true, isn't it? When my price tag was thirty-five won, an arranged marriage was actually called off!" Ch'ansu also scoffed, seemingly filled with deep emotion.
"No way! Did that really happen? When?..." Munhŭi sounded surprised, but Ch'ansu just walked silently down the dark path.
Five years ago, when he left school and started working in the Chongno area for thirty-five won, there were various other reasons, but such an incident had indeed occurred.
"So, in five years, the groom's price has gone up by fifteen won, huh?" <84>
After pondering something for a moment, Munhŭi said this and chuckled to herself.
"At that rate, how much will you be worth by your sixtieth birthday (환갑)?"
"I'm twenty-six now, so you calculate it."
"There are still thirty-five years left... five times seven is thirty-five... what's fifteen won times seven?"
Though it was pointless banter, Munhŭi calculated earnestly.
"Why calculate that?"
"So you can get married then, when your value has gone way up! Hahaha..."
"Who would take a sixty-year-old bachelor?"
The two laughed heartily before Munhŭi changed the subject first.
"That's what I mean. Like you said, sir, if the husband has a price tag worth less than a gold hairpin, and the wife is essentially sentenced to a lifetime labor camp under a lifelong contract, why have such a marriage? It's true we're like machines when we go to companies or factories. Maybe even less than machines. But if marriage is like that too, isn't it too miserable? I'm not strictly an advocate for love matches, but that kind of marriage seems like a sin. To enter such a marriage—mechanical, business-like, calculating, convertible into monetary value—and then pass on the same miserable life to the next generation, isn't that a pitiful thing? For that reason alone, I want to call it a sin."
Munhŭi spoke earnestly.
"And that's why you say you've transcended marriage and love, Miss Munhŭi?..."
"That's not the only reason, but... Have you read Nietzsche's (니-체-) 'Thus Spoke Zarathustra' (사람과 초인, literally Man and Superman)? It's been a long time, so my memory is hazy, but there's something called the life force (生의 力), <85> you know."
"Even so, I don't want to be blindly dragged along by the life force. Love itself might be the life force acting blindly, but shouldn't there be something else?"
"Something else?..."
"Anything!..."
"Ah, you mean there should be poetry, love, and dreams?"
"And ideals for humanity and future generations..."
Munhŭi added a word in the same vein, like a literary girl.
"You're surprisingly idealistic, aren't you? You've only been out in the real world for a couple of months, so you can still say such things. And truthfully, those times are good," Ch'ansu said, sounding quite world-weary and older.
"But why have those times already passed for you, sir? Don't you consider it unfortunate that those times passed so quickly?"
"Even if it's unfortunate, what can be done? Those times simply never existed for me..."
"So you live without love, without poetry, without dreams, without hope? Talking with you, sir, I'm afraid I might somehow get infected unknowingly and lose hope myself. Please stop talking like that now."
Hearing her speak like this, she sometimes sounded like someone who had been through thick and thin, perhaps with experience in married life or failure in love. But hearing her words at times like this, she also seemed innocent, with hope, ideals, and passion flowing from her every word. Perhaps her true nature was cheerful, rational, and idealistic, possessing not only hope and enthusiasm for life but also considerable <86> ambition and passion for literature. However, if she hadn't grown up in unfortunate circumstances, she might indeed have experienced bitterness in love or marriage. But if she had such bitter experiences yet wasn't bent or broken by them, retaining her cheerful personality and not losing hope and ideals, she was an astonishing woman, he thought. In any case, in Ch'ansu's eyes, she was a woman of mystery and interest.
Munhŭi's home wasn't her parents'; she seemed to be living with her older brother. In the house with just an inner room (안방) and an outer room (건넌방), Munhŭi occupied the outer room. After showing Ch'ansu in, she went across to the inner room.
"Why is this festive house so quiet? Did many guests come?" she asked, initiating conversation.
"A whole crowd just left after a round of celebrations," replied the voice, presumably her sister-in-law.
Then, the sister-in-law and Munhŭi lowered their voices, whispering as if discussing how to treat the guest. However, the phrase "our company section chief (주임)" drifting over suggested she might be fabricating a story about the male guest being her indispensable supervisor from her "territory" (가가리), perhaps to justify properly hosting him.
Ch'ansu felt a bit awkward. Sitting in the outer room of the small house was uncomfortable enough, but Munhŭi's efforts made him feel apologetic. However, it was somewhat fortunate that the master of the house, her older brother, was absent.
"What are you looking at? There's nothing worth seeing there. Let's just talk."
As Ch'ansu sat looking through an old Western magazine on the desk, Munhŭi came back from the inner room, said this, and plopped down in the warmer part of the room (웃묵). It wasn't a high-end literary magazine but a hobby magazine full of ads for new fashion accessories for women. Still, Ch'ansu, whose language skills were barely enough to puzzle through it, envied Munhŭi for being able to read even this. Since she typed letters dictated by Westerners on a typewriter (타이푸라이터-), she naturally had the skill, but he found it newly impressive, wondering where she had learned so much. <87>
"Could you show me some books?" Ch'ansu asked, looking towards the bookshelf. The desk area and top were tidy, but there were no meticulously arranged feminine decorations. The only striking thing was a wide ceramic pot on top of a large bookshelf, filled with narcissus planted densely in water. From the moment he entered the room, the subtle fragrance, revealing the owner's mind and taste, was pleasant, and the flowers looked lush and beautiful.
"What books are there to show? The novels I bought when I was in Tokyo (동경) became burdensome, so I sold them. All that's left are some foreign books I bought greedily without even reading them, nothing useful."
However, seeing the corner packed with foreign books, he felt somewhat intimidated and envious.
"Have I properly read even one of them? It's just for show. Decoration."
"Not at all," Ch'ansu replied, pulling out and looking through various books.
"Applying powder, drawing eyebrows, combing hair, dressing up, and on top of that, earning money too – how can one manage?"
"If you put it that way, don't men drink and smoke?"
"But while men are drinking and smoking, aren't women absorbed in fantasies about how to look cute, how to gain love? Not just the time, but the mental effort involved, I mean."
"Instead of doing that, why not throw away the powder puff, cut your hair short, and study more? You said you went to Tokyo; why did you come back?"
"Because I had no money and got sick," she trailed off, then continued,
"My lungs are weak, the doctor was alarmed, so I quit and came back. After resting for about half a year... it's not my fate to live idly. Out of necessity, <88> I ended up there [the company]. But I want to go back again." Her face, suddenly bright, perhaps partly due to the warm room, had cheeks flushed as if rouged with *hohoben*i (호호베니), which was pretty, but she also looked like someone who might have such an illness.
"Oh, that's too bad. How are you now?"
"It's not serious enough to worry about. But thinking that my mother passed away from that illness, I can't help but be scared."
"Eh? Your mother too?... When?..."
"It was the third year of middle school, so I think I was fifteen," Munhŭi calculated. Ch'ansu calculated inwardly too.
"Then you finished girls' school at seventeen?" he asked, surprised.
"Yes. I studied English at home with my brother for a year, then went [to Tokyo] at eighteen and studied only English for three years. Just one more year, and I could have gotten a teacher's license..."
"Hmm! Once your health recovers, you should go back and finish."
"But my older brother is unemployed now, so I can't move an inch."
"When did you learn to type?"
"I learned it while idling at school. It was a girls' English school, and they happened to have that too. But who knew it would later become my way of earning a living?"
"Then, where is your father?"
Ch'ansu asked sympathetically, thinking of his own motherless situation.
"My father passed away too. This year marks the third anniversary."
Hearing this, it seemed plausible that she hadn't married by age twenty-one. Motherless, young, frail, allowed to study as she wished, <89> especially after getting sick—marriage concerns were likely put second by both herself and those around her. Furthermore, her brother, whatever his former profession, was unemployed, and she, the frail younger sister, apparently had to work to repay the cost of her education. It seemed there was nothing more to say.
"Instead of that kind of work, haven't you tried looking for a job at a school?"
"There's no place that would hire an unqualified person like me, and even if there were, it would probably be worse for my health. My older brother worked as an English teacher for many years. So, his idea was that since I'm weak and there's no need for me to marry early, I should become an English teacher. He guided me that way, and I intended to do so too. But thinking about it now, it doesn't suit my interests, and the thought of battling with children, inhaling chalk dust and dirt, is primarily 싫혀요 (distasteful)..."
"Why wouldn't it suit your interests?"
"It's not that I have any particular ideology or the ability to critique current education, but I feel unsuited to be an educator."
It seemed the influence of literary works read during her studies had made her abandon her aspiration to become a teacher.
"Quietly practicing conversation in a clean room and typing a few pages is enough, so that way is certainly more comfortable. Most importantly, since people don't smoke, the air is clean, which is nice," Munhŭi said with a laugh, then added,
"Ah, right, I've been thoughtless all this time. Just because I said that, you shouldn't abstain from smoking because of me," she said, crossing to the inner room to fetch an ashtray and matches. It was likely an unintentional remark, but Ch'ansu felt a bit awkward, reminded of the embarrassing incident of getting caught sneaking a smoke at the company.
"Speaking of tobacco, during that turmoil over cigarettes back then, why couldn't you all unite and resolve the issue then and there? That aside, when it was clearly something done together, seeing one person take all the responsibility, suffer humiliation, and be on the verge of being kicked out while others feigned ignorance and stayed silent <90> was so pitiful and even infuriating to watch. Compared to that, your attitude, sir, was admirable."
Munhŭi's praise sounded like she was saying, 'Because you acted that way, I acknowledge you as someone worth being friendly with.'
"Not at all," Ch'ansu said, his face flushing,
"And yet, I'm still smoking this tobacco, what can I do?" he laughed, lighting a cigarette.
"Well, why should you stop just because of that? Inside the factory might be different, but what does it matter in the office? Except for the reason that the general manager dislikes tobacco."
While they were talking like this, a food tray was brought in. He had expected maybe some celebratory rice cakes (돌떡), but perhaps it had been prepared in advance; noodles in broth (국수장국), side dishes suitable for drinking, and even alcohol were served.
"It would have been nice if Oppa (older brother) were here... though maybe it's better that he's not!"
Munhŭi brought in her own tray with just a bowl of noodles, sat down, and offered him a drink. Ch'ansu demurred but eventually drank half a cup. Being a young unmarried woman, she didn't try to pour him a second drink.
However, just as he took a couple of bites of noodles, a voice called out "Is anyone home?" (이리 오너라) from outside. The voice sounded familiar, and the two inside the room pricked up their ears, looking at each other silently for a moment. Hearing the sister-in-law, who was in the kitchen, answer and go to the middle gate, Munhŭi quickly stood up and went out too. - (Continued) - <91>

**Summary**

This installment of Yŏm Sangsŏp's novel follows Ch'ansu, a low-paid office worker in 1930s Seoul. He narrowly avoids being fired for smoking in the company toilet, an incident highlighting the strict, often arbitrary rules and the precariousness of employment under potentially Japanese management ("Ttŏngk'i-sŏnsaeng"). He struggles to borrow 30 won (needed for an engagement ring) from a passive-aggressive colleague, Anjintari. Meanwhile, his relationship with another colleague, Munhŭi, develops; she helps him buy the ring and invites him home, revealing details about her past (education in Tokyo cut short by illness and finances, deceased parents, unemployed brother) and her somewhat cynical yet idealistic views on life, love, and the constraints placed on women and workers. The chapter ends cliffhanger-style as a familiar voice calls from outside Munhŭi's house.

From a colonial history perspective, the story offers glimpses into the daily life and anxieties of urban Koreans. The rigid office environment, low wages (50 won determining marriage prospects), reliance on Japanese loan words (벤또, 스팀, 타이프라이터, 미쓰고시), and the power dynamics between managers and employees reflect the era's socio-economic conditions. Munhŭi's character, a "New Woman" educated in Tokyo but constrained by health, finances, and societal expectations (including her brother's unemployment), embodies the complex struggles and aspirations of educated women during this period. The casual discussion of marriage being transactional ("price tag") also speaks volumes about societal norms influenced by economic hardship.

「삼십분 동안 점심도 안먹고 담배만 먹엇느냐 할제도 내뻐든 길에 그러타고 해두어 버리면 고만이지.」
다람쥐라는 별명을 듯는 친구는 이런 비난까지 하얏다. 결국에 찬수의 말이 이동이저서 그짓말쟁이라고 떵키-에게 핀잔을 더마질 것이 무어냐는 말이 아니라 달은 사람들과 가티 먹은 것이 탄로되도록 말을 잘못하얏다고 책망하는 것이다.
「점심 안먹고 삼십분 동안 담배만 먹엇다면 당장 내 설합을 뒤지고 변도갑은 안뒤저보니.」하고 찬수가 이러케 변명을 하니까
「내 저러케 고지식하긴 서서 똥누겟네」하고 또들 웃는다.
「서서 똥 누겟네가 아니라 우리 처지로는 서서 담배 먹겟네라고 하게.」
또들 웃읍지안흔 웃음을 웃으며 뒤ㅅ간을 엽헤끼고 잇는지 똥똥하며 똥노래들만하고 일은 할 생각도 업시 안젓스려니까 「규-지」가 와서 찬수를 불러내간다. 변도갑을 들고 모자를 쓴채 안젓든 찬수는 여러 사람과 인사를 하고 나가버렷스나 누구나 안진채 돌려다보고
「가나? 섭섭하이. 잇다라도 맛나세.」하는 따위의 인사뿐이요. 한 사람도 따라나오는 사람조차 업섯다. 찬수가 무슨 특별히 인심을 일헛다거나 그들이 랭정한 것이라느니보다 모다들 목이 공중 걸린 듯이 간드렁 간드렁하든 아슬아슬한 판을 넘기고나니 그저 천만 다행한 생각에 남의 불행쯤야 동정할 여지가 업는 것이요. 그 중에는 자리가 한아 뷔이게 되엇스니 자긔 친지를 끄러너을 운동을 어서 해보아야 하겟다는 생각부터 벌서 하는 사람도 잇슬 것이다.
찬수를 불러드린 떵키-선생은
「그래 나갈테요?」하고 다시 한번 다진다. 아까 서둘든 것 보아서는 그 말이 쑥스럽게 들렷스나 나간다고 분명히 대답하얏더니 제 풀에 숙으러저서 ＜75＞
「그러나 이번 한번 특별 용서하우. 당신 담배 두 개 먹엇다구 그짓말 햇스나 당신 정직한 사람요 그래서 용서하는거요」
그짓말은 하얏는데 정직하다니 그 따위 신거운 말도 잇나하고 찬수는 물그럼히 치어다 보고만 섯섯다.
「달은 사람들 다- 먹은 것 잘 알우. 그런데 그 사람들도 다- 내보내면 우리 일이 안되우. 그러면 그 사람들 안내보내고 당신만 내보내면 불공평하오. 나 불공평한 사람아니요. 그러니까 이번 한번만 용서하우. 이 다음 그런 일 잇스면 곳 내보낼 것이요.」
정직해서 용서한다더니 자긔가 불공평한 사람이 되기 실혀서 안내보내는 것이요. 벌은 벌대로 씨우고 공평한 사람이 되자면 사무실을 비질을 해서 모라내야 할테니 당장 회사일이 랑패하는 리유로 용서를 하는 것이 란다. 모든 것이 제일 위하자는 리긔주의에서 나온 말이나 말딴은 고지식 한 것으로 취할 모가 업지는 안타고 생각하얏다. 하로를 잇다가 그만 두드라도 쫓겨나가느니보다는 나흔 일이라고 찬수는 잠잣고 그대로 주저 안기로 하얏다.
사무실에 도라오니 모다들 죽엇든 사람이 피어난 것처럼 반갑고 희한하다느니 보다도 어리둥절해서 「무어래? 무어래?」하고 법석이기에
「뒤ㅅ간에서 담배 두 개씩이나 피우기에 얼마나 애썻겟느냐 칭찬을 하며 월급도 올려 준다네」하고 찬수가 웃어버리니까
「허! 그럴 줄 알앗드면 난 한갑씩 먹는다고 할껄. 그 분하다. 분해!」하고 들 정말은 아니겟지만 분해들 하얏다.
찬수는 그 바람에 원광으로만 보고 인사를 하고 지나치든 문희를 총무엽해서 코가 맛닷도록 자세자세 볼 수가 잇섯지만 그때의 문희는 민망하다는 듯 동정한다는 듯한 은근한 표정이엇다. 소학교 어린애가 학교에서 군것질하다 들켯다든지 중학생이 담배먹다 선생에게 꾸지람 당하듯 이 녀자 보기에도 부끄럽고 창피할 뿐 아니라 의례 못생긴 남자라고 ＜76＞ 피- 하고 얏잡아 볼터인데 말은 안해도 그처럼 동정 잇는 눈치를 보니 은근히 고맙기도 하얏다. 그것은 하여간 이래 찬수도 다시는 뒤ㅅ간에 들어가서 담배연긔만 보여도 외면을 하고 나오기로 작정을 하얏다. 창피하고 분하든 생각을 하면 그야말로 단연 단연을 하고 십흐나 술 못먹는 찬수는 그래서는 넘우 심심할 것도 갓고 도둑담배가 잘못이라 할 뿐이지 - 하는 생각으로 거긔까지는 결심을 못하얏다.
이런 풍파를 격근지도 벌서 한달이 되고 떵키- 씨의 공평무사한 덕분이라할지 오십원이 제대로 달려잇서서 하마트면 노첫슬지도 몰을 혼담이 제대로 진행이 되엇기 때문에 래일 모래로 보낸다는 사주단자에 함께 보낼 반지 갑슬 구처하느라고 지금 이러케 장을 대고 혼자 떠러저 잇는 터이다.

사무원들이 몰려나가니까 사환도 그네들만못지 안케 불이 시각하고 비ㅅ자루와 총채를 들고 드러와서 사람이 안젓거나 말거나 나는 몰은다는 듯이 타다남은 난로불부터 뒤집어 업고 물을 들어부어서 먼지를 산떼미처럼 내어 노코는 떽으럭어려가며 재를 치고 잇다. 찬수는 쾟심한 생각도 들고 사실 눈코를 뜰 수도 업스나 이 사람인들 담배 정사가 안급하라고 돌려 생각을 하면 동정도 가거니와 그보다도 이 사람은 느저도 아츰일곱시에는 대어와서 전등불이 들어와야만 나가는 것이니 이 칩고 짧은 해에 도라갈 길이 밥불 것이다. 찬수가 먼지 속에 안젓슬 수도 업지만 자긔 때문에 소제가 느저질 것이 미안해서 하든 일을 집어 치우며
「여보 권서방 미안하지만 저 방에 가서 안달씨더라 내가 여긔서 기다리고 잇다고 말 좀 해주」
점잔케 안진달씨라고 불은다는 것이 무심코 그러케 나왓다.
사환은 잠잣고 나갓다가 드러오더니
「오늘은 그대로 가시랍니다」고 전갈을 전한다. 몹시 불쾌하얏다. 되든 안되든 제까짓게 무엇이라고 멧발자곡＜77＞ 나오면 될 일을 버틔고 안저서 그대로 가거라 마라라 하는 것도 동료 사이에 넘우 소홀하거니와 이때까지 혹시나 될까하고 뻔떡히 기다리고 잇든 것이 분하다. 먼지가 부- 여케 안즌 외투를 털어 입고 혼자 쓸쓸히 나오다가 총무실을 힐끈 드려다보니 총무는 업고 안진달이만 타이프라이터 압헤 모로 꼬으고 안즌 문희와 웃어가며 무슨 이야기를 하고 잇다가 못본체하고 이야기를 하는데 문희는 그러케 생각해 그런지 얼굴이 발개지며 류리창 밧글 살짝 내다본다. 눈은 마조첫스나 찬수는 홍너케 밧그로 나와 버렷다. 단둘이만 놀고 안젓는데 오라니 올리도 업겟지만 그녀자에게 돈달나고 졸으는 눈치를 채이게 한 것은 고사하고라도 오늘은 그대로 가라는 완만스러운 분부를 밧고서 비슬비슬 피해나오는 자긔 뒤꼴이 얼마니 거련히 보엿슬까를 생각하면 얼굴이 홧홧하다. 아츰에 회사 올 때의 활긔와 비하야 넘우나 우울하얏다.

4
이튼날 찬수는 눈을 뜨면서부터 오늘 장문회 집에를 가보나 마나?... 하는 생각부터 떠올러왓다. 가보고도 십흐나 한때 실업슨 생각으로 그런 것이나 아닌가하는 의혹도 나서 저편이 다시 무어라고 하기 전에는 그만 두는 것이 올타고도 생각하얏다.
집에서 나올제 부친방에 문안을 들어가니까
「오늘은 위불업시 되겟니?」하고 부친은 어제 저녁에 한말을 또 남으래 듯이 똥기운다.
「네-...」하고만 나왓스나 오늘 그 돈이 꼭 될지도 모르거니와 문희에게 갈 생각은 찬수가 오늘이 사주단자인가를 보내기로 예정해 노핫든 날이라는 것은 부친의 말을 듯고서야 비롯오 생각이 낫다.
찬수는 부친이 먼저 해보내 주엇스면 하는 생각도 업지 안흐나 네가 엇더케 해보겟니? 하고 물을 제 크리쓰마쓰 때 나올 것을 밋고 공연히 네- 하고 대답을 하야 노핫기 때문에 군걱정이 생긴 것이요 또 한가지는 아들에게 미루고 안하는 ＜78＞ 것이 아니라 상게에 나가는 한편에 잣단돈으로 빗노리를 하는데 맛을 들인 부친인지라 이삽십원 돈이라도 리자 업시 선대해 쓴다는 바에야 그 편이 리라고 따지고 잇는 눈치기도 하다.
찬수는 오늘도 점심시간에 진달이더러 무러보니까
「글세- 좀 쫄기는데...」하고 탐탁지 안흔 소리를 한다. 그래도 역시 오늘 사퇴할 때는 꼭 해주어야 하겟다고 세 번째 간청을 해두엇다. 그 길로 변소에를 다녀 나오랴니까 문희가 뒤를 따라왓든지 서로 마조치며 지나칠결에
「잇다 사동모통이에서 기달릴께 그리오세요. 집을 몰으실거니까...」하고 저만 속살대고 이 편 대답은 들을 새도 업시 변소로 드러가버린다.
찬수는 엇재 저러케 열심일까? 하고 또 다시 의아도 하나 깃브지 안흔 것도 아니엇다.
파사시간이 되엇다. 오늘도 뒤처지면 또 어제처럼 찍고까불 것이 실혀서 오늘은 좀 일즉이 서둘러서 안진달이를 동독을 하니까
「대관절 무엇 때문에 이러케 몸이 달아서하나?」하고 또 이런 딴청을 한다.
「누구를 놀리나? 이때까지 한 말은 코루 마탓단 말인가?」
「그래 정말 약혼이 되엇나? 누군가!」
「누구라면 알겟나?」
찬수는 또 한번 핀잔을 주엇다. 부친이 동독만 안하면 그러케 몸이 달아서 사주단자를 급히 안보내도 상관업다고 청처짐한 생각도 드나 긔위 말을 냇든 것이니 엇잿든 익퀴를 지으랴는 것이다.
「그래 장가갈 사람이 남의 집처녀를 끌고다니니 엇던 편이 정작 약혼한 사람인지 알 수 잇나」＜79＞
진달이는 별안간 이런 말을 끄낸다.
「그건 무슨 소린가?」
「난 몰으는 줄 아나? 「미쓰고시」 차맛이 행결 달을 껄?」하고 또 비꼬으며 눈을 마섭게 떠본다.
「응. 난 무어라구. 우연히 동행이 되어서 차를 먹엇기루 상관잇나. 그것두 난 도서관 가는 것을 땅이 미끄럽다고 바래다 달래서...」
「하하하... 땅이 미끄럽다고 손목을 붓드러다 달래든가? 겨드랑이를 부측해다 달라든가? 하하하... 딴은 핑계가 됏서」
남은 아모랫든지 자네가 배를 알을께 무엇인가? 한다든지 웬 아랑 곳이 잇으냐고 핀잔을 주고 십흐나 그러면 나올 돈도 옴츠라저드러갈 것 가태서 참아버렷다.
「그래 그 반지는 그 색씨한테 푸레센트(선사)할 것은 아닌가?」
「그 색씨라니? 장래 자네 한머니가 끼실거라네」
찬수는 안나오는 롱담을 하야버렷다.
결국 파사시간이 되어도 돈은 아즉 안되엇다. 하는수업시 또 오늘도 찬수는 뒤처젓다.
「오늘두 잔무 처리인가? 넘우 열심일세 그려?」

그 다음에는 싀뉘을캐가 목소리를 나추어서 숙은숙은하는 양이 무슨 대접할 공론인 모양이다. 그러나 무슨 말끄테 우리 회사 주임 - 엇저고 하는 말이 귀ㅅ결에 들리는 것은 사내 손님을 데리고 온 것을 엇지나 알까 보아서 꼭 대접해야 할 자긔 「가가리」의 주임이라고 꿈여대는 모양인 듯도 하다.
찬수는 좀 송구스러웟다. 좁은 집에 건넌방에 드러와 안젓는 것도 편치 안커니와 문희의 애쓰는 양이 미안스러웟다. 그러나 집주인인 오라비가 업서서 얼마쯤 다행은 하얏다.
「무어 보세요. 그 까짓 건 보실 거 업서요. 이야기나 하셔요」
찬수가 책상 우에서 묵은 서양잡지를 들추어 보고 안젓노라니까 문희는 안방에서 건너오며 이런 소리를 하고 웃묵으로 퍼더버리고 안는다. 고급의 문학잡지는 아니요 신류행 부인 장신구의 광고가 긋득한 취미잡지기는 하나 어학의 실력이 간신히 뜨더 볼둥말둥한 찬수로서는 이런 거라도 보는 문희가 부럽기도 하다. 서양 사람 압헤서 입으로 불으는 편지를 티이푸라이터-로 찍어 내는 터이니 물론 그만 실력이야 잇겟지마는 어듸서 그 만치나 배웟는지 새삼스럽게 신통해도 이보는 ＜87＞ 것이다.
「책이나 좀 보여주세요」하며 찬수는 책장을 바라다 보앗다. 책상 아래나 책상 우에나 깨끗이 치어는 노핫서도 녀자답게 요밀조밀하게 꿈인 것이라고는 업다. 다만 한가지 눈에 띄우는 것은 커단 책장 우에 넓적한 사긔화분에 수선화를 물에 흔근히 심어 노흔 것이다. 방에 드러스면서부터 방 임자의 마음과 취미를 알의듯이 그윽히 마치는 향긔도 조핫지만 보기에도 탐스러웟다.
「책이 뭐 잇서야죠. 동경 잇슬 때 소설권이나 사보든 것은 짐이 되기에 파라버리고 보지도 안흐면서 욕심에 사둔 원서 나부렁이 밧게 쓸만한 건 업세요」
그러나 구석이는 원서가 긋득찬 것을 보고 좀 긔가 눌리는 것도 갓고 부럽기도 하얏다.
「웬 하나나 변변히 보앗나요. 남 보임이요. 치레지요」
「천만에 말슴입니다」
찬수는 이책 저책 끄내 보며 대꾸를 한다.
「분 바르고 눈썹 그리고 머리 빗고 옷치장하고 게다가 또 돈버리까지 하자면 될 말인가요」
「그러케 말하면 남자는 술먹고 담배 먹지 안습니까」
「그러나 녀자는 남자가 술담배 먹는 동안에 엇덕하면 귀엽게 뵈일까 엇덕하면 사랑을 어들까 하는 공상에 팔려 안젓지 안습니까 시간 두시간이려니와 마음 쓰는 품이 말얘요」
「그러지 말고 분병도 집어 치고 머리도 깍가 버리고 공부를 더 하십그쇼려 동경 가섯드라는데 웨 나오셧세요?」
「돈도 업고 병도 나고 해서요」하고 말을 끈타가
「폐가 약해서 의사가 놀라기에 집어 치우고 나와서 한 반년 놀다가 어듸 한가히 놀고 먹을 팔자야죠. 허는 수 업서 ＜88＞ 그리로 드러갓는데 또 가구십허요」하고 반짝드는 얼굴은 더운 방에 드러와서 그러키도 하겟지마는 하얀 얼굴에 두뺨만 「호호베니」를 칠한 듯한 것이 고옵기도 하거니와 그런 병을 가질 듯한 사람 갓기도 하다.
「허- 안됏군요? 지금은 엇더세요?」
「그러케 염려할 정도까지는 아니얘요. 하지만 어머니께서 그 병에 도라가신 것을 생각하면 겁도 안나지 안하요」
「에- 자당께서두?... 언제요?...」
「삼년급 때니끼 열다섯 살이든가」하고 문희가 따저보는 동안에 찬수도 속으로 처보고는
「그럼 열일곱에 녀학교를 마치섯군요?」하고 놀란다.
「네. 일년은 집에서 영어를 옵바한테 배우고 열여덜에 가서 삼년 동안 영어만 배웟죠. 일년만 더하면 교사 면허는 밧는 것을...」
「흠! 근강만 회복되섯거든 다시 가서 마치고 오시죠」
「하지만 오라버니가 지금은 놀고 게시니 꼼작 수가 잇서야죠」
「타이푸라이터- 는 언제 배섯세요」
「학교에서 놀며 배워 두엇지요. 녀자영학숙인데 마츰 그런 것도 잇세요. 하지만 그것이 나종에 밥버리로 써먹을 줄 누가 알앗겟세요」
「그럼 아버님은 어듸 게서요?」
찬수는 자긔가 어머니 업는 생각을 하며 동정해서 뭇는 것이다.
「아버님두 도라가섯세요. 올에 삼년마첫지요」
그런 이야기를 듯고 생각하니 스물 한 살이 되도록 시집 안간 것이 그럴 듯 하얏다. 어머니 업고 나 어리고 몸 약하고 하니  공부한다는 대로 ＜89＞ 내버려두다가 더구나 병이 낫다 하니 당자나 엽사람이나 결혼문제는 제이로 처노흔 것일 것이다. 게다가 오라비란 사람은 무엇을 하든 사람인지 실직을 하고 몸약한 누이가 학비 어더 쓴 것 갑는 세음으로 버러대야 하는 형편인 모양이니 말할 것도 업슬 것이다.
「그런 일보다 학교에 취직을 해보시지 안쿠?」
「나가튼 무자격자를 데려갈 데도 업겟구 잇대두 건강에 더 해로울 것얘요. 오라버니가 여러해 영어 선생 노릇을 하섯죠. 그래서 오라버니 생각에도 나를 몸은 약하고 일즉 결혼할 필요도 업스니 영어 교사로나 나서보라고 그런 길로 지도로 하야 주고 나도 그럴 작정이엇스나 지금 생각하면 취미에 맛지도 안코 아이들과 악다구니를 해가며 백묵가루와 먼지를 마신다는 것이 첫재 실혀요...」
「취미에 맛지 안흐실거야 무어잇나요?」
「이러타는 제 사상이 잇다거나 지금 교육에 대한 무슨 비판할 능력이 잇는 것은 아니나 교육가로 나서기는 부적당한 것 가태요」
공부하는 동안에 읽은 문학서류에의 영향이 교원지망을 버리게한 것인 모양이다.
「종용히 깨끗한 방에서 회화나 연습을 하고 몃장 타이푸라이터- 나 처주면 그만이니 그 편이 의례 편치 안하요. 제일 담배들을 안먹으니 공긔가 깨끗하야 조트군요」하고 문희는 웃다가
「아 참 내가 이때 것 무심햇군. 그런 말씀햇다고 나 때문에 담배 굶으시면 안돼요」하고 안방으로 건너가서 재터리와 성냥을 가저온다. 별 뜻 업시 한 말이겟스나 찬수는 회사에서 도둑담배 먹다가 창피당하든 생각이 나서 좀 열적기도 하얏다.
「담배 이야기가 낫스니 말이지. 그때 담배 때문에 한풍파 낫슬제 웨 좀 일치단결해서 그 김에 문제를 해결 못해요. 그건 고사하고 번연히 가티한 일에 한 사람만 책임을 지고 인격무시를 당하면서 쫓겨 나가는 마당에 시침이 떼고 가만히 ＜90＞ 잇다는 것은 넘우 보기에 딱하고 분하기까지 햇서요. 거긔다 비하면 선생님 태도는 훌륭하섯지요」
문희가 이러케 칭찬하는 것은 그러키나 하기에 너를 친해도 조흔 사람으로 인정한다는 말가티도 들린다.
「천만에요」하고 찬수는 얼굴이 붉어지다가
「그리고도 그 담배를 또 먹으니 엇저나요」하고 웃으며 담배를 피어 물엇다.
「그럼 그러타고 안잡슬게 무업니까. 공장 속에서는 몰으지만 사무실에서 상관이 무업니까. 총무가 담배를 실혀한다는 려유밧게」
이런 이야기를 하고 잇는 동안에 음식상이 들어온다. 돌떡이나 드러 올 줄 알앗더니 마츰 준비를 해두엇는지 국수장국에 술안주까지 가초으고 술을 끼어 드려 온다.
「옵바가 게섯드면 조핫는걸... 안 계신 편이 되레 조흘지는 몰으지만!」
문희는 자긔는 따로 국수 한 그릇만 노흔 상을 들고 드러안즈며 술을 권하랴 한다. 찬수는 사양하다 못헤 한잔의 반을 마서 보앗다. 아모래도 처녀이니만치 두 번은 다시 술을 딸으랴  아니하얏다.
그러나 막 국수를 두어저ㅅ가락 먹자니까 밧게서 「이리 오너라」소리가 난다. 그 목소리가 귀에 익은 듯 헤서 방안의 두 남녀는 귀를 기우리며 잠간 마조 치어다 보고만 안젓다. 부억에 잇든 오라범댁이 대답을 하고 중문턱까지 나가는 긔척을 듯자 문희도 얼는 이러나 나갓다. - (게속) - ＜91＞
