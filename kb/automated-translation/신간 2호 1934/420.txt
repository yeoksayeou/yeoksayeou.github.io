발행일	1934년 12월 01일
기사제목	흰모래 우를 것는 處女의 마음 (The Heart of a Maiden Walking on White Sand)
필자	金海剛 (Kim Haegang)
기사형태	시 (Poem)

I
Oh, savage sea, which shattered the dreams of pebbles frozen at the port on the continent's edge,
And writhed, embracing the sun's enormous corpse!
Driving away the small offspring of thunder hiding their tails far off,
Pressing down on your breathless lungs, now you quietly draw breath!

II
The countless grains of sand, once sharpened by the waves,
Tremble their pale sinews that screamed and fought,
Clutching their broken hearts, letting out faint breaths.
Unable to digest the painful memories—.

III
Oh, stepping upon your painful memories,
Her skirt hem fluttering in the spear-like dawn wind,<20>
The heart of this maiden walking on the white sand
Sees the dawn light bursting forth beyond the horizon in the eastern sky.

IV
Endless white sand field—
My long-stretching footprints—
In each footprint, the red devotion yearning for the dawn
Is burning like embers. Burning brightly.

V
Oh, city of dawn drawing near to my feet!
Oh, joyful steps parting the wind and advancing!
My heart pounds.
My pulse throbs.

VI
The birth of a new morning that will shine beautifully—
My bosom, where pure love ripples,
Sends sunlight like flower clusters to you, my beloved ones—
Descendants of the dawn! Break down the closed windows, run out, and greet <21>

**Summary**

The poem vividly contrasts the past violence of the sea (symbolizing hardship or struggle) with the serene yet expectant present moment. A maiden walks on the white sand at dawn, her heart full of anticipation for the "birth of a new morning." Her steps leave behind "red devotion," signifying a passionate yearning for the future, represented by the approaching "city of dawn." The poem evokes themes of overcoming painful memories and embracing a bright, pure future with hope and excitement. From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, might interest historians for its powerful imagery of dawn and awakening after darkness. The "descendants of the dawn" called upon to break free could be interpreted as a metaphor for the Korean people yearning for liberation and a new national beginning, though expressed cautiously through natural symbolism.
