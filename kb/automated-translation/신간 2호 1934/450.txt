Magazine Title: 개벽 신간 제2호
Publication Date: 1934년 12월 01일
Article Title: 戱曲 찬우슴(全一幕) (Play: A Cold Smile (One Act))
Author: 金雲汀 (Kim Unjŏng)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Play: A Cold Smile
						Kim Unjŏng
-「Performance Strictly Forbidden Without Permission」-
Time, Place
Modern Day. One moonlit night in mid-autumn, a place in Kyŏngsŏng's Pukch'on.
Characters
Pak Hun, 37 years old
Sukcha, 23 years old (Alias: Mistress of the Western House, Pak's mistress)
Helen, 23 years old (Female Teacher)
Hŭngbok (Pak Hun's house male servant)
Pak Ch'ŏmji (Same household, old lower servant)
Kimnye (Same household, lower servant)
-(End of List)-
Stage
The stage occupies a vast expanse of a mountain grove in Pukch'on. In the garden of a wealthy residence, center stage, there is a hexagonal pavilion <31>. In front of it lies a wide lawn. To the left and right, utilizing the natural beauty of the mountain grove, peculiar rocks and strangely shaped stones are arranged. Among them, azaleas, lilies, dwarf pines, and flowering plants are planted appropriately. Surrounding this, the branches of ancient pines and old trees, seemingly hundreds of years old, intertwine, forming a dense forest.
Upstage, a part of the corridor leading to a Western-style building (洋舘) is visible. Downstage, far below, the imposing roof of a traditional Korean tiled house (朝鮮瓦家) can be seen. This rear garden, framed by old trees with rotting bases and crooked branches, and rugged rocks, imbued with an ancient feel, is bathed in the clear, dark blue moonlight of mid-autumn, evoking a sense of eeriness and chill, as if something is hidden.
When the stage opens, the lower servant Kimnye is cleaning chairs in the hexagonal pavilion.
Kimnye: (Stops wiping the chair) Oh, my back! A person needs a moment's rest during the day to live. Ay, my wrist is aching. Now I have to bring the flowerpots... (Stands straight, holding the rag, looks up at the moon) The moon is certainly bright. If only I could play freely for just one day when the moon is this bright, I'd have no regrets. Day and night, wiping floors, wiping hallways – I'm sick and tired of wiping. When will I ever be free of this? (Looks at the moon, sighs lightly)
(At this moment, the sound of a policeman's iron staff dragging is heard from far upstage)
Kimnye: Pak Ch'ŏmji must be making his rounds already; it must be around ten o'clock. Then I should hurry and clean up. So I don't get scolded again. Why does he want to have tea here today, bothering people like this? (The sound of the staff gets closer, and the lower servant Pak Ch'ŏmji enters)
Pak Ch'ŏmji: (Appears before the pavilion, leans on his staff, straightens his back) It's Kimnye. You're having extra trouble tonight too. It's night, so what if you don't wipe with a wet rag? Just dust it off roughly.
Kimnye: Ah, who's going to listen to the complaints later? The other day, I got scolded so much for not wiping the floor properly in the detached building (別堂). The master of the house always wears old clothes at home, so if even a speck of dust gets on his trousers, a huge thunderbolt strikes. Grandpa, you know it too, yet you say that. It's pitiful.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Is there anything to say about the master's worries? He's a nobleman who regards people as less than worms. And yet, it would be nice if he paid generously. Truly, there are no people as pitiful as the servants of this house. At breakfast and dinner, to the stale old rice, what's lacking that they add millet...
Kimnye: They have to add millet for it to increase, right?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Hey, don't talk nonsense. Do you <32> enjoy eating hardened lumps of millet rice every day? Do you think people who eat millet rice or porridge do so because they are that poor? They do such miserable things because if they don't pinch pennies like that, they'll miss meals. You really don't know the wealth of this house. Nowadays, no matter who they say is rich in Chosŏn, no one can match the wealth of this house. Just what's been passed down from the old master's time amounts to seventy thousand *sŏk* of rice.
Kimnye: Seventy thousand *sŏk*? Ah, so that's seven times ten thousand, right?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Yes, seven times ten thousand. Forget rice bales; even if you piled up seventy thousand grains of sand, it would look like a mountain. With such wealth, what's wrong with eating millet rice every day? It's truly incomprehensible.
Kimnye: Why, do you think the master or the mistress eat millet rice? The millet rice fate is only for us poor souls. That "dog" they bought recently... ah, you mean that wolf-like "dog"?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Uh-huh, the one called "Shepa-do" (세파-도 Shepherd)? Or "Seppadak" (세빠닥)? That "dog"?
Kimnye: Right, right. That "dog" doesn't eat millet rice either; they buy Western food for it every day. The only ones served millet rice are us.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: That's what I mean. Why were you and I destined to be servants in such a house? Blame the fate of being a servant; who else can we blame? (Sighs and sits down on the pavilion's veranda edge)
(At this moment, Kimnye and Pak Ch'ŏmji fall silent for a moment, enveloped in light melancholy)
(From the upstage direction (Western House), the sound of a piano and a woman's sad song are heard)
Kimnye: The Mistress of the Western House is singing that sad song again. Whenever that song plays, I can't bear it because tears come to my eyes.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: I too, when I can't sleep at night and toss and turn under the covers, hearing that sound makes me feel desolate. But is the Western House Mistress's madness the same these days?
Kimnye: Of course. It seems to be getting worse lately. Yesterday morning, when I went in, her hair was dishevelled, and she had stabbed her neck with an earpick (귀개), covering herself in blood. I was shocked and went to the inner room (안사랑) to wake the sleeping master, but without even asking how the mistress was, he threw an ashtray (재떠리) at me, shouting, "You wench, why did you wake me?" I was terrified. How can the master be so heartless? At first, he was infatuated with the Western House Mistress, spending thousands of won like water, <33> completely devoted. He even built that Western house for her when she said she didn't want to be a mistress. But less than two years (잇 해) later, he abandoned her just like that, never showing his face (발그림자). Who wouldn't go mad? Truly, there can't be another nobleman as heartless as the master of this house in the world.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Everything the master of this house does is like that. He's a nobleman incapable of the slightest bit of compassion. I've been in this house for over forty years now. But the master, having grown up spoiled as a precious child (귀동이) in luxury, knows nothing of others' circumstances. He trusts only in his wealth (철량) and regards others as worms. Is that all? He doesn't spare money on women (게집), but he trembles at spending even a penny elsewhere. That's why, in such a rich house, they cook millet rice separately for the servants at breakfast and dinner, tormenting us.
Kimnye: No matter how rich one is, money only has value if used properly. Just picking up women... no matter how rich...
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Does it look wrong to your eyes too? The business of the master of this house is nothing but choosing women's looks by day and frequenting noisy restaurants (요릿집) by night. You know it all, right? Isn't it seven households he has set up with mistresses now? But for the mistresses he picks up, he provides just one *sŏm* of rice, one bundle of firewood, and ten won for side dishes monthly from the servants' quarters (수청방), barely enough, locking them up tightly like birds in a cage. The master never sets foot there again.
Kimnye: That's what I'm saying. The women who become mistresses to the master of this house are all deceived by money. They come thinking they'll spend money freely and live luxuriously as mistresses, but it's completely different from the beginning. The Mistress of the Western House also fell for it hook, line, and sinker and became a mistress, but from the very next day, she wasn't allowed to go out freely, wasn't given much money, and he only sought other women. Why wouldn't she go mad?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: I heard the Mistress of the Western House was a singing teacher (노랫선생) who earned good money?
Kimnye: Yes, that's what they said. She earned several hundred won for singing just once at the public hall (공회당). But after entering this house, she wasn't given pocket money, kept in a dark back room all day... who wouldn't develop bottled-up anger? That's why her madness started last autumn. At first, she stayed silent, lips tightly shut for days. Then it got worse, breaking mirrors (체경), <34> throwing water bottles, causing scenes. Since some time ago, she started trying to run outside, so that very day, they called a carpenter, installed iron bars on the back room of the Western House, and threw the mistress in there like a tiger in a zoo cage.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: She has become a truly pitiful person! That's why if you blindly rush at things, blinded only by money, you get badly hurt. Just like placing tempting meat in front of a fly trap to lure flies, there are many in this world who use money as bait to entice young women. Speaking of which, it seems the Mistress of the Western House was lured by the meat and entered the fly trap. Anyway, young as she is, she's ended up in a pitiful state. (Sighs lightly, taps out his short pipe (곰방대) and sticks it in his waistband)
(From the direction of the Western House, the piano sound grows louder, becoming discordant, and the sharp woman's song sounds even shriller)
Kimnye: (Quickly covers her ears) Ay, listen to that song! (It gives me the creeps.) When she sings that high, she's not in her right mind. Shaking her wildly tangled hair, eyes sunken with only the pupils gleaming, staring straight up at the ceiling, screaming like that... she looks like a demon (아귀). It's terrifying to be near her. And down her hollowed cheeks, hot tears flow endlessly as she pounds the piano senselessly.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: When people go mad, their faces gradually become frightening. Anyway, hearing your story and the Western House Mistress's song, I suddenly feel a chill, and a sense of dread creeps over me. It feels like something is about to poke its head out from the rotten hollow of that old pine tree over there.
Kimnye: (Suddenly cries out) Aigoo, that's scary! (Rushes to Pak Ch'ŏmji's side) Don't startle me. I already feel like a ghost might come out from under that dark tree; my hair is standing on end.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Ha... (Laughs) If you rush over to me like this, can I stop the ghost from coming out? That's why women always get the nickname 'weak'. Now, shall I check behind the mountain pavilion before going down? They say there are many petty thieves around these days; maybe one has climbed over the wall?
(Pak Ch'ŏmji picks up his iron staff and stands up)
Kimnye: Aigoo, I'm scared. Let's go down together. The chairs <35> are wiped; now only the veranda floor is left. Huh, Grandpa, please? Let's go together. (Quickly wipes the veranda floor)
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Just do it roughly. You can't see well at night anyway; what's the point of wiping so hard? By the way, who is coming tonight that there's such a fuss cleaning even the pavilion?
Kimnye: Who knows? A female teacher (여선생)? Or a female student (여학생)? They say the master is currently quite smitten with a woman he's chasing, and they're having tea with her in this pavilion?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Tea? Why wouldn't they have tea in the nearby Western House with its tea things, instead of this chilly pavilion? For him to order tea prepared in such a secluded rear garden, there must be another reason.
Kimnye: What? What reason?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Ha... (Laughs) You're nineteen, yet quite naive (숫보기), aren't you? The moon is bright, the garden is vast, no one is around, the scenery is beautiful... in a place like this, especially the master, who seduces (회치다) women, bringing a lovely young woman here... what do you think he'll do? Think about it.
Kimnye: Have tea, what else? Really, Grandpa, you think of strange things. How shocking!
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Haha (Laughs) So now you understand.
Kimnye: Understand what?
Pak Ch'ŏmji: You've figured out that there might be more than just tea happening in this pavilion. So when the master and the woman arrive, serve the tea tactfully. If you approach quietly just because the master calls, you might witness a rather embarrassing scene.
Kimnye: Grandpa, you say such suggestive things. I know that much too. Don't worry.
(At this moment, footsteps are heard from downstage. Kimnye and Pak Ch'ŏmji are startled and turn their gaze in that direction)
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Ah, Grandpa, looks like the master is already coming up.
Kimnye: Huh? The master?
(Pak Hun, wearing evening attire (夜會), enters from downstage)
Pak Hun: Is everything cleaned up? So, is the tea brewed properly as I instructed earlier?
Kimnye: Yes- Hŭngbok is brewing the tea right now.
Pak Hun: What have you all been doing until now that you're only just brewing the tea? What do you people do besides stuff yourselves with food? "Coffee" tea needs to be brewed for a long time to be drinkable. Truly
hopeless lot. Hurry down and tell him; the guest will <36> arrive soon.
Kimnye: Yes! Should I prepare black tea as well?
Pak Hun: (Angrily) Did you forget again? Didn't I tell you to prepare black tea too when a female guest is coming? Even when told, you don't understand. Truly useless people. (Kimnye hesitates, then exits. As Pak Ch'ŏmji also tries to exit)
Pak Hun: (To Pak) Hey, have you finished the police rounds? They say there are many thieves these days; don't just lie around in corners. Patrol the rear garden often at night. And a guest will be arriving soon, so wait by the back gate, and when you hear a car approaching, open the back gate for them.
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Yes- You ordered never to open the back gate at night; why is the guest coming through the back gate tonight?
Pak Hun: (Raising his voice) Don't talk back. Whoever comes, what do you need to know for? Just do as you're told. (Pak Hun faces the moon and sits down on the pavilion's veranda edge, taking out a thick cigar (여송연) from a silver-decorated case and lighting it.)
Pak Ch'ŏmji: Then I must go get the back gate key.
Pak Hun: (Takes out his watch) The theatre should have ended by now? Then hurry and bring the key. (Pak Ch'ŏmji exits. Pak Hun alone on stage)
Pak Hun: (Strolls before the pavilion, bathed in moonlight) It's almost ten, she should be here soon? Women are certainly stingy creatures. Just because they paid, they think it's a loss if they don't watch until the very end. Truly, pitiful things. However, the reason I always win with women is because I skillfully exploit that stingy nature of theirs. But this time, with Ma Helen, it took quite an effort. Graduating from a Western school, she knows so-called "freedom," so it wasn't easy. But women always have little resistance to temptation. Tonight, showing her this vast and splendid garden, and then boasting about my financial power (재산실력) – capable of arranging tens of thousands, even hundreds of thousands of won with just one check (소절수) – Helen, no matter how much she seeks freedom, surely won't have the courage to refuse?
(At this moment, the servant Hŭngbok enters from downstage)
Hŭngbok: (Holding a name card, looking flustered) A guest has arrived.
Pak Hun: (Annoyed) Who is it? Who came looking for me at this hour of the night? (Snatches the name card from Hŭngbok's hand.) "Ch'oe Kwan (崔寬)"? Ah, the person from the orphanage (고아원)?
Hŭngbok: Yes, that gentleman.
Pak Hun: I told you to say I wasn't here! Why didn't you send him away and accept the card? Utterly hopeless lot. Didn't I tell you <37> to say I'm not here to unannounced guests and send them all away?
Hŭngbok: Even though I said you weren't in, he wouldn't believe me and said he absolutely must see you before leaving today. He came early this evening and sat on the veranda of the servants' quarters (수청방), waiting until now. Then he saw you returning, sir, and said he'd just see you for a moment before leaving.
Pak Hun: Just say I'm not here! What a persistent (추근추근한) fellow. Whether the orphanage children starve to death or freeze to death, what concern is it of mine? There are all sorts of shabby institutions in the neighbourhood, it's maddeningly annoying. Ugh, bothersome. Tell him I have a guest today and have no time to meet him. Besides, with the money I'd donate, I could go to a restaurant (요릿집) one more time. Hurry down and send him away; tell him I can't meet him today.
(At this moment, the sound of a car horn is heard from the rear garden. Pak Hun hastily moves upstage, Hŭngbok exits downstage. A moment later, a young woman's voice is heard from upstage)
Helen: Have you been waiting long? I intended to leave right after you left the theatre first. But the last part was so interesting, I lingered a bit and ended up watching the whole thing, so I'm late. But visiting so late at night, what will the people of this house think?
(Pak Hun and Ma Helen, dressed in light Western attire (洋裝), enter)
Pak Hun: (Leading the way for Ma Helen) Not at all, it's only just past ten, what does it matter? Besides, our house has such a large rear garden, it's far from the main residence; there's nothing to worry about.
Helen: The rear garden scenery is truly magnificent. The whole city stretches out below, and those twinkling lights are all electric lamps, aren't they? Just like stars twinkling in the sky.
Pak Hun: Please sit here. (Pak Hun invites Helen to sit in a pavilion chair. Ma Helen also sits opposite him)
Helen: This is the first time I've seen such a large and splendid garden.
Pak Hun: It might sound like I'm boasting about my own things, but you could say it's the best in Seoul. The garden plot alone is eight thousand p'yŏng, and behind this, there's a traditional Korean house of over a hundred *kan*, and over there, a large Western-style building where over a hundred people can gather for an evening party (야회). However, I worry that there's no female owner to use this splendid residence effectively—that is to say, like a proper home.
Helen: Why? Is there no mistress of the house? <38>
Pak Hun: The so-called wife is an old-fashioned woman who knows nothing. She couldn't manage a house like this, and besides, what use is entrusting it to someone who doesn't know such tastes? As the vulgar saying goes, "a p'yŏnja (horseshoe pendant) on a dog's foot."
Helen: Not at all, anyone would be greatly fortunate to spend their life luxuriously in such a splendid house.
Pak Hun: That's right. There's nothing more essential to human happiness than money. Being bitten by bedbugs in a cramped monthly rented room (삭을세방), unable to sleep soundly, and being kicked out onto the street clutching a straw mat (돗자리) – there's no room for love or peace then. The suffering caused by material lack is absolute.
Helen: It really seems so. Among my school alumnae, too, some initially made a fuss about love marriages or whatnot, but in less than two days, they ended in failure.
Pak Hun: Of course. When your stomach is growling, how can even the most passionate love be sustained (지탕할)? Failure is the natural course.
(At this moment, Hŭngbok brings a tea tray, and Kimnye brings a tray of sweets, places them on the table, and exits)
Pak Hun: (Puts down the cigar he was smoking, pulls the tea tray closer, offering) What tea would you like? The "coffee" is brewed lightly, so... it's not great, but shall we have black tea? In the West, it's considered rude to carelessly offer "coffee" to a beautiful lady, so let's have black tea. (Pours tea)
Helen: Any tea is fine. I'll drink whatever you give me.
Pak Hun: Haha! (Laughs) If you say so, I'm relieved. Since the night is a bit cool, then please have some "coffee". Please try one of these sweets too. I had these specially made at the Western bakery today because I was bringing Miss Helen. Please have one.
Helen: (Gives a light bow, picks up the teacup) They look delicious just by looking at them.
Pak Hun: Even at the Western bakery, if I ask, they make special sweets for me. Because I originally order only the best, high-quality items without sparing money. They're vastly superior to what they deliver to other houses. I pay several thousand won a year just for sweets.
Helen: (Surprised) Ah, you spend several thousand won just on sweets?
Pak Hun: Yes, probably about three thousand won a year.
Helen: People who've been to the West seem to be very extravagant with sweets.
Pak Hun: Of course, they are extravagant. The standard of living in the West and here is completely different. There's no room for comparison. <39>
Helen: But spending three thousand won just on sweets seems excessive. Three thousand won could be the annual living expenses for a modest household.
Pak Hun: That's right. Speaking of the current standard of living for Koreans, three thousand won is a large sum. There probably aren't that many people who spend three thousand won a year on living expenses.
Helen: Oh, absolutely. After working hard for a month, what I receive as a so-called salary is merely fifty won.
Pak Hun: That's why I always say this. After receiving a good education, why should you always be tied down by a paltry sum like fifty won a month and live an unfree life? Even if you save for a year, it's only six hundred won; combined for ten years, it's six thousand won. Truly a pointless endeavor. Isn't it frustrating to waste the irreplaceable life of a flower-like woman, bound by such pocket change?
Helen: Calculating by money, even working for ten years doesn't amount to much, but how can a person only follow material things? Working for my alma mater in the field of education after graduating can also be considered a meaningful life.
Pak Hun: Ha... (Scoffs) Meaning? Meaningful? That alone wouldn't be a meaningful life. One should utilize the splendid material civilization offered to us by this 20th century as much as possible and live a life appropriate to the times. That's a meaningful life. Being unable to afford even one fashionable outfit, worrying about food in a dingy ondol room, and spending one's irreplaceable youth as a woman – how tragic a spectacle that is. Airplanes that travel thousands of *ri* in an instant, cars speeding down glass-like paved roads, concerts that lead to sweet dreamscapes, splendid dance halls intoxicated with pleasure – turning one's back on the modern civilization that offers us all these delightful "chances," and languishing in a rotten old shell, clinging to delusions like tradition (傳統) or contentment (安分), and spending one's entire life – that is truly a meaningless life.
Helen: Isn't that living too much in vanity?
Pak Hun: Ha... (A stifled laugh) Vanity? That's just slander from those who lack the financial means to live a civilized life, spoken out of jealousy. In this world, there's nothing with absolute power like money. What's a doctorate degree? What's the political stage? If you just have money, you can occupy those positions too. Not only that, you can grab those people and make them do whatever you want. So today, please understand my words <40> well and grant my request. Yes? Miss Helen!
Helen: But I also have my reputation, so how could I possibly do that?
Pak Hun: As I've always said before, if you just agree to my proposal, we can certainly disguise the surface appearance somehow. My earnest desire right now is such that I wouldn't regret giving all my wealth to Miss Helen at once. If you need to spend a thousand won at once, I'll let you spend it freely; if you need to spend ten thousand won, that too can be done. What are you hesitating for? Right now in Seoul, those acting arrogant as bank executives or presidents – half of them are doing so with money from my house.
Pak Hun: What would a woman do with such a large amount of money? Just living a comfortable life is enough.
Helen: Well, achieving that comfortable life is quite a fundamental issue. To live comfortably according to modern standards, one would need at least a million won. How many people in Chosŏn possess such qualifications now? Perhaps two or three. Well, there's no need to think further. Please accept my words. If you fear public criticism, can't the surface be disguised in many ways? First, let's bring you here under the pretext of being my family's home tutor (가정교사). And if you like this house, you can use the Western house or the mountain pavilion; I'll provide whatever you wish. This splendid house is currently without a mistress. Come now, there's no need to think so much. (Takes a check (소절수) from his pocket)
Here, this isn't much, but please use it as this month's pocket money. It's a five-hundred-won check.
Helen: (Hesitates, not accepting) Why are you giving me money? How can I accept such a large sum of five hundred won without reason?
Pak Hun: Come on, don't be so formal, just take it. (Tucks the check into Helen's bodice)
Helen: (Hesitates, then accepts it) If I accept money from a man, what responsibility does that entail?
Pak Hun: What worry is that? Come closer here.
(Grabs Helen's wrist.)
Helen: (Startled, tries to pull away, but is drawn by Pak Hun's strength, her shoulder touching his chest) Aigeu, what are you doing? Let go of my arm! (Excitedly, he embraces Helen and tries hard to kiss her. Helen twists her body to avoid it. Pak Hun, roaring like a lion, forcefully kisses Helen on the lips. Helen wrenches herself free and stumbles back to her chair.) <41>
Helen: (Panting) What outrageous behavior is that? What if someone saw? Oh, I'm so embarrassed!
Pak Hun: Ha... (A satisfied laugh) What's there to be embarrassed about? You're past the age of being embarrassed. Ha... (Laughs)
(At this moment, from the direction of the Western House, the sound of the piano and the Mistress of the Western House's sorrowful song are heard)
(Lyrics = Butterflies lured by fragrance fly in / Roses have thorns, mountain chrysanthemums have spiderwebs spun. / Ah! Frightening spiderwebs / This hell that confines me.)
Helen: (Turns her ear towards the Western House) Ah, piano music from somewhere? Ah, I hear singing. What? "Butterflies lured by fragrance... roses have thorns? Mountain chrysanthemums have spiderwebs? This hell that confines me..." Where is that sound coming from? Ah- what a strange song. Who is singing at this hour of the night? This hell that confines me? (As if a chill runs through her, she looks again towards the source of the song)
Pak Hun: (Frowning as he listens) That madwoman is making noise again. Truly annoying.
Helen: Who is it?
Pak Hun: Miss Helen doesn't need to know.
Helen: The piano sound and the melody of the song suggest she's not an ordinary woman. She seems like someone who has studied a lot?
Pak Hun: Study? What study? She's a home tutor (가정교사) I brought in last year just to help the children review their lessons. She has severe "hysteria," and recently she's become half-mad, just playing the piano like that every day and singing crazy songs. Truly, I can't send her away, and she's such a bother.
Helen: A home tutor? How strange. Why did a home tutor go mad?
Pak Hun: Who knows? Let's stop this uninteresting talk and take a walk in our garden.
Helen: You asked me earlier to come as a home tutor, didn't you?
Pak Hun: Come on, why keep thinking about that madwoman's song?
(At this moment, the Mistress of the Western House plays the piano discordantly and laughs shrilly with a sharp voice)
Helen: (Shivers, looking frightened) Aigu, what a scary sound she makes. Truly a madwoman. How pitiful. How old is she? Poor thing.
Pak Hun: What does her age matter? Twenty-three or four, probably.
Helen: Oh dear, I'm twenty-three this year. (As if thinking of something, Helen stops talking and sits silently)
Pak Hun: You're worrying unnecessarily. What are you thinking about so suddenly? <42>
Helen: No. My heart just feels strange.
Pak Hun: Hmm, you worry about everything. There are all sorts of people in the world. If you take on all the world's worries, you'll waste away.
Helen: Why is it that...? That song, to me...
Pak Hun: Not at all, Miss Helen, you seem quite sensitive too. Stop thinking useless thoughts, come on, let's take that walk in our garden. (Stands up from the chair)
Helen: I'm going home. (Stands up)
Pak Hun: Leaving already? Since we finally met today, let's spend a long time playing in this quiet place.
Helen: No. It's late, I need to get home.
Pak Hun: Come on, don't act like a child, let's just take a walk. (Reaches for Helen's wrist. Helen pulls her arm away)
(At this moment, the voices of Hŭngbok, Kimnye, and Pak Ch'ŏmji are heard from the direction of the Western House) "Mistress! Where are you going carrying that? Oh oh, where did you get that revolver (육혈포)? The one kept in the inner room's bedroom, how did you get it out? This is terrible! There's a guest in the pavilion now! And where are you going? This is big, big, big trouble! Who opened the Western House door? Quickly, quickly, tell the Master! You can't go there! Goodness, why are you acting so frantically?" (This section is off-stage dialogue)
Hŭngbok: (Enters frantically from upstage) Master, Master, something terrible has happened! The Mistress of the Western House somehow escaped, and now she's taken the revolver (육혈포) from the bedroom and is coming this way! Big...

**Summary**

This one-act play, "A Cold Smile," depicts the tragic end of Pak Hun, a wealthy, cruel man in colonial Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). He keeps his mistress, Sukcha (the "Mistress of the Western House"), virtually imprisoned after initially lavishing her with gifts. Driven mad by neglect and confinement, Sukcha escapes while Pak Hun is attempting to seduce Helen, a young teacher, in his garden pavilion, boasting of his wealth and dismissing Sukcha's suffering. Sukcha finds Pak Hun's revolver, confronts him, calls him a devil, and shoots him dead before laughing coldly over his body.

The play offers a stark portrayal of social dynamics in 1930s colonial Korea. Historians might find interest in its depiction of the vast wealth gap, the commodification and brutal treatment of women (especially mistresses) by the wealthy elite, the tension between modern aspirations (represented by Helen and Western luxuries) and traditional social structures, and the psychological toll of oppression and confinement leading to madness and violence. The characterization of Pak Hun as utterly callous and obsessed with wealth reflects critiques of the newly emerged capitalist class under colonial rule.
