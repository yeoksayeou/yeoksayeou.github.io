Publication Date: 1934 December 01
Article Title: 안악의 비밀(漫談) (The Wife's Secret (A Light Chat))
Author: 李瑞求
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

The Wife's Secret
						Yi Sŏgu
1
Wife: I'm leaving!
Husband: Why are you leaving?
Wife: Because I can't bear children, that's why.
Husband: That's the first of the seven grounds for divorce, isn't it.
Wife: Since I can't even bear children, and I'm getting old, you'll end up taking a concubine. Better I leave while I'm still young.
Husband: Where will you go?
Wife: I'll go to someone's house and work as a seamstress or something.
Husband: Then why not work as a seamstress in our house?
Wife: Who would do that? It's too humiliating.
Husband: What's humiliating about it? Stay as both seamstress and wife. I have absolutely no intention of abandoning you because you can't bear children, nor of finding another woman elsewhere to bear them.
Wife: You say that now, but just wait until I get a bit older and become unpleasant to look at.
Husband: Will I remain the same then? We'll grow old together.
Wife: A seventy-year-old man taking a concubine young enough to be his granddaughter...
Husband: If you only doubt people's futures like that, how can you live in this frightening world?
Wife: That's why I've figured things out early and must quickly find a way to survive on my own.
Husband: In my heart, there's no reason you should become tiresome just because you can't bear children, and there's no reason I would get another woman because I need children. Yet you alone have decided it will happen and are trying to stir up trouble in a blameless household. Isn't this truly unfair?
Wife: Stop it. Last night, I heard you at the company asking if there wasn't "one wife who bears children well."

Aha, I get it! This must be the mischief of the playful Mr. S. Mr. S, who works in the company's sales department, is my sworn brother, closer than a real sibling. He loves practical jokes. If he comes to our house and just plausibly spins tales about me, persimmons and apples emerge from the cupboard, so it's clear he just babbled whatever came to mind and left. I chuckled softly.

Wife: Now that I'm too withered, you just laugh it off.
Husband: Hey, you fool.
Wife: You finally realized I'm a fool? <98>
Husband: Not until now.
Wife: What do you mean, 'now'?
Husband: You're a fool because you get swayed by Mr. S's instigation and waste precious persimmons and apples.
Wife: How did you know that?
Husband: Why wouldn't I know? Yesterday afternoon at the company, Mr. S was joking around and said this!

Husband: "Well, I don't have to worry about fruit for lunch during the autumn season. Why? Because whenever I'm peckish, I just go to my elder brother's house, tell the madam some nonsense as 'his secrets,' and the refrigerator and cupboard doors fly open."
Wife: Oh, how infuriating!
Husband: Tricked and infuriated.
Wife: Wouldn't you be?
Husband: What kind of absurd talk is that? You were tricked, so you should be embarrassed, and I should be the one infuriated.
Wife: I'm sorry, ma'am (addressing the housemaid).
Maid: Yes.
Wife: I told her to quickly fetch a watermelon from that shop... without knowing, I polished off the whole watermelon.
Husband: Well played.

2
Husband: Ah—where does a housewife who runs a household go out so often?
Wife: Just pretend you don't know. Why is a man so petty?
Husband: Petty? When dinnertime comes, wherever you've been, you should hurry back and think about serving the meal.
Wife: From now on, I won't do it again. Please forgive me just this once.

Waiting for his wife who didn't return, that night the husband ate dinner only at nine o'clock. The next day too, she returned late again. The husband was dumbfounded and went looking for her at all the places she might go, from outside the new gate to Hongsudong, but couldn't find her.
Damn it, she's gone wild. I'm a crazy fool for chasing after a woman who acts like this.
Scolding himself, as he was about to change streetcars at the Chongno-t'ong intersection, his wife got off the streetcar coming from Anguk-tong.
Husband: Where have you been, coming back now?
Wife: Honey, let's talk at home. Why are you shouting like this in the street?
Husband: Alright—let's see when we get home.
Wife: Okay. Kill me or spare me, do it at home. That night, the husband received this written pledge from his wife.

Pledge: Whenever I go out, I will clearly write down the departure time, return time, and destination, and I will return without fail. If I violate this pledge, I will accept any punishment received for that reason without complaint and bear it willingly.
OOO Seal <99>
Having received the pledge, the husband thought, "From now on, she won't do it again," but the next day, he waited for his wife until eight in the evening, sitting down, standing up, unable to settle, getting angry, clenching his fists, sighing, thinking.

Bah—I should just bolt the gate and not let her in.
He bolted the gate from the inside. And he even confined the maid who cooked the meals. He turned off the electric light in the room facing the main gate. It's an empty house.
Alright—let's see how things turn out. That woman must be crazy. Going out until the middle of the night.
He rested his head on a pillow and collapsed by the chest of drawers, but out of boredom, his hand reached for the small chest his wife used.
Aha! This blasted little chest, she hasn't shown me its contents for several years. Let me try opening it.
He took out a screwdriver from the sewing box drawer and, prying off the chest's fastener, opened the drawer. Inside the drawer were letters the husband had sent her from Tokyo.
"When I first welcomed you as my wife,
Then later cherished you as a lover,
Now, I intend to serve you as my benefactor."
Ah, she probably intended to present this letter if I ever treated her poorly or scorned her for not bearing children. Next was a passbook with receipts for installment payments. She had bought a wardrobe and told me she'd saved up bit by bit for it, but it seems half was paid with saved money and the other half was on a ten-month installment plan. Three won per month, and there were still three months' payments left. Next came her diary. A "Freedom Diary." She hadn't written every day, but more than two-thirds of it was filled with records in pencil or ink.
"I must have a baby. When my father-in-law's words, 'A child is taking too long,' came out, I felt as if I had committed some sin. A midwife came and said there is a efficacious spirit below Pugak-san mountain where many people pray for children, and asked if I wouldn't go once, so I followed her. A seventy-year-old man lived in a stone cave, his white hair like silver, seeming like a divine spirit. As the hermit instructed, I decided to offer prayers every evening for three weeks (samchilil) and bow one hundred times each time. However, if I tell my husband about this devotion, he will surely criticize it as superstition and spoil the effort, so whatever excuse I make, I must keep it secret for the three weeks."
I understood! I understood everything. The husband who saw this tearful record! No matter how much of a superstition it was, how could he criticize it as such?
—The desire to bear a child—
For a woman over twenty, especially one who becomes the wife of the eldest son of a family, this desire has a fervor greater than the desire to live. I quietly got up, turned the electric light back on, and unbolted the locked gate.

3 <100>

On the first holiday, all the company employees went up Paegun-dae peak on Pugak-san. The husband also joined the group, carrying the lunchbox his wife had packed. Almost at the top of Paegun-dae, the husband hurt his hand on the railing secured with iron cables, and it bled slightly.
"Ah, I shouldn't go up. She's performing devotions at home; if I get injured, it will bring misfortune."
The husband, who hadn't known superstition, muttered this to himself and gave up his vested right to conquer the superb view from Paegun-dae, which was only about three hundred meters away. On the way back, passing a small hermitage, there was a spring, and in front of it stood a large rock several yards high where people offering prayers with pure water (jeong'ansū) would test whether their wishes would be fulfilled or not by throwing small stones onto this rock. If the stone landed on top of the rock, the wish would be fulfilled; if it fell off, they would be disheartened (낙심). The husband first drew some spring water into the water bottle he had carried, picked up the prettiest small stone, closed his eyes, and prayed:
"Please grant us a child. I can't bear to see my wife so pitiful."
He threw it, and whether the mountain spirit helped or not, the stone landed miraculously on top.
"It worked! The wish is granted!"
Shouting, he enlisted the help of his companions, retrieved the stone, wrapped it in mulberry paper (조회), and came down the mountain.
Husband: Hey—the small table!
Arriving home, the husband caused a fuss.
Wife: What's the small table for?
Husband: Just bring it out quickly without arguing.
At the words "without arguing," the wife became tense. She brought out the small table.
Husband: Okay, place it facing Paegun-dae.
Wife: That's northeast, right?
Husband: Yes, exactly. Now, enshrine this stone on the table, offer this water in a clean bowl, and then you bow four times.
The wife, seeming unable to contain her joy, began to bow. From the Japanese neighbor's house across the way, who worked at the construction company, the sound of a baby crying could be heard loudly. <101>

**Summary**

This short play depicts the marital tension arising from a wife's inability to conceive in 1930s Korea. The wife, feeling immense pressure and fearing abandonment, secretly resorts to traditional prayer rituals, leading to unexplained absences and her husband's suspicion. The conflict escalates until the husband discovers her diary, revealing her desperate hope for a child and her fear of his disapproval of her "superstitious" acts. Understanding dawns, replacing his anger with empathy. He then participates in a folk ritual himself, finding a symbolic stone representing hope for conception, which he brings home for his wife to venerate, signifying a shift towards mutual understanding and shared hope.

From a historical perspective, the play vividly illustrates the profound societal expectation placed on women in colonial Korea regarding childbirth and lineage, portraying the wife's deep anxiety and desperation. It highlights the persistence of traditional beliefs and practices (seeking shamans/hermits, prayer rituals, divination with stones) alongside modernizing elements (company life, skepticism towards 'superstition'). The reference to specific locations like Anguk-tong, Chongno-t'ong, and Pugak-san grounds the domestic drama within the context of 1930s Seoul. The husband's eventual participation in the ritual, despite initial dismissal of superstition, could suggest a complex negotiation between tradition and modernity, or simply deep empathy trumping rationalism in the face of his wife's suffering.

안악의 비밀
						李瑞求
1
妻 나는 갈테야!
夫 왜 가?
妻 자식 못나니까 가지.
夫 칠거지악의 으뜸이로군.
妻 자식도 못낫는다고 다 늙기에 시앗을 보게 되느니 숫제 절머서 가지.
夫 어대로 가.
妻 뉘 집에 가서 침모노릇이라도 할테야.
夫 그럼 우리 집에서 침모노릇을 하지.
妻 아니꼬와서 누가 해.
夫 아니곱긴 무엇이 안이꼬아. 침모겸 마누라로 잇구려. 나는 결코 자식 못낫는다고 당신을 바리거나 다른 데서 자식 나흘 게집을 구할 의사는 조곰도 없으니가.
妻 지금은 저러지만 내가 좀 더 늘거서 보기 시리케되 여보.
夫 그 때 나는 그대로 잇나. 가치 늙지.
妻 칠십 로인이 손녀가튼 양첩을 둘나고.
