기사제목 新聞社長의 懺悔錄 (Confessions of a Newspaper President)
필자 申錫雨
기사형태 회고·수기 (Memoir/Recollection)

新聞社長의 懺悔錄
申錫雨

Newspaper Management Was Originally Not My Intention
My involvement in newspaper management was not my original intention. It was merely due to coincidental circumstances that I formed a temporary relationship. It wasn't because I was some ideologue or political figure, but ever since my student days, I had no desire to stay cooped up within Chosŏn, engaging in trivial pursuits. Instead, I wanted to go abroad, to a land of freedom at the very least, and exert myself to my heart's content. Thus, back in 1919, I first hurried over to Sanghae, joined hands with senior compatriots there, and devoted my full energy to the national movement. However, I was greatly disappointed there. More than anything, it was because the so-called patriots, men of ambition, and political figures gave no thought to the greater cause and principles, constantly forming factions over trivial matters, fighting amongst themselves, viewing each other with animosity and jealousy. The nominal XXXX [government-in-exile implied] had no real power, living in rented houses in a corner of someone else's leased territory, chased from here this month, moving elsewhere the next month, showing no hope for the success of any great enterprise. Therefore, I gave up on that and planned to cross over to Europe or America to engage in some activity. However, as they say, "good things face many obstacles" (好事多魔); unexpectedly, I was arrested by the consulate authorities, taken via T'ienchin, and escorted back into Chosŏn. My plans, nurtured for many years, thus returned to bubbles, and I helplessly became imprisoned (囹圄). While I was living such an unfree life, a great misfortune befell my family: my benevolent mother (慈親), who loved me most and had always worried and longed for me during my wanderings abroad (海外放浪), learned of my arrest and imprisonment (逮捕入監), became ill from shock and worry, and ultimately departed on the eternal road. This is a matter of lifelong regret (平生遺限) for me, something I cannot forget as long as I live, no matter how I try. After spending some time behind iron bars, I was released, only to find my mother had passed away like that, and my father, already white-haired and frail (白髮이 蕭然하야), greeted me with tears. My sorrowful state of mind at that time cannot be well understood by anyone who has not personally experienced such a thing. Yet, even then, I had no desire to do anything in Chosŏn and intended to harbor great ambitions (壯志) once more and go abroad. Learning of this, my father strongly forbade and dissuaded me (말류하섯다). He didn't just dissuade me; he spoke of how my benevolent mother fell ill and passed away (成病卒逝) because of me. He said if I were to leave again, he too, in his old age, might fall ill, and we might part forever without seeing each other again. He promised that if I stayed home peacefully until he passed away and undertook some business within Chosŏn, he would provide funds to the best of his ability. While the affection between father and son is naturally close for anyone, my father and I were exceptionally close; he was more of a benevolent father than a stern one, a comrade, the closest comrade among them. Given his advanced age and such earnest and solemn words, how could my heart not be moved, even though there's a saying that "he who works for the world does not care for family matters"? Thus, I had no choice but to temporarily postpone my thoughts of going abroad and stayed home, looking into various fields to see what temporary business I could try. Just then, a friend, knowing my situation, happened to mention the management difficulties of the Chosŏn Ilbo. After several negotiations between us, under certain circumstances (某某 事件下), I finally became one of the managers of that company.

Points I Regret
Although I initially intended only a temporary involvement with the company, it truly became a case of "playing pretend turns into reality" (眞所謂弄假成眞), where I couldn't quit even if I wanted to (欲罷不能格). Once involved, the surrounding circumstances increasingly made it impossible for me to withdraw from the company due to relationships. One year turned into two, and before I knew it, over ten years (10餘 星霜) had passed. My responsibility grew from partial to full responsibility. Financially, what started as an estimated ten or twenty thousand won escalated to one hundred or two hundred thousand won, and ultimately, nearly my entire family fortune was poured in and utterly squandered (蕩敗無餘), leading eventually to my resignation from the position of newspaper president. There's no time to recount all the numerous twists and turns (波瀾曲折) during this period, nor do I wish to speak of them. Although regret is useless now, I feel that if I hadn't approached taking charge so half-heartedly, as a temporary measure, but instead had stepped forward sacrificially from the beginning, with a definite policy and plan, putting everything into practice, perhaps such failure could have been avoided. From this, I learned a lesson: in worldly affairs, if you decide to do something, you must do it yourself, desperately, or not do it at all. Also, while comrades are necessary for any undertaking, you absolutely cannot trust them completely. Especially in ventures like newspapers or magazines, systems like collective ownership (同人制) or multi-headed leadership (多頭制) are common, but based on my experience, I do not consider them good systems. There seems to be a need for the dictatorship of one person who controls everything. Formally, collective or multi-headed systems <15> may seem modern and good, but in reality, disagreements and conflicts often arise due to differing principles and opinions, frequently causing unfortunate incidents and ultimately hindering the business. Furthermore, when hiring people, one should not employ so-called prominent figures (名士) or men of ambition (志士), these established individuals (旣成人物). They already have their own fixed views (成見) and separate principles or beliefs, so they rarely obey, and sometimes cause trouble (弊). Conversely, hiring emerging talents is cheaper, they work diligently and sincerely, and they obey orders exactly as given. Anyone running a newspaper or magazine should seriously consider this point.

How Much Money Was Spent?
So, you ask how much money I spent on the newspaper? I don't particularly want to elaborate on this myself, but roughly speaking, the direct and indirect expenditure amounts to about 420,000 won. If I say this, some might call me exaggerating, but since spending a lot of money only makes me look more foolish, what need is there to exaggerate? Is that all? Even during the three years I stayed at home in mourning after leaving the company, due to debts from that time, I suffered the embarrassment of payment orders and execution orders a total of 38 times. How my heart must have been wounded each time I faced such matters! The reason I strictly stayed indoors for three years, not going out, wasn't out of profound filial piety to observe a three-year mourning period. In reality, I felt that if people saw me, they would point fingers, saying, "There's the guy who ruined his family running a newspaper," so I simply shut myself away (杜門不出). Fortunately, however, due to the recent rise in land and grain prices (騰價關係), I was able to settle all debts (責務), barely lift my troubled head, and secure my livelihood to some extent, bringing considerable relief regarding my family. However, regardless of how much money I spent, since it was my money, it doesn't particularly matter. What makes me feel very sorry is that because of me, other people spent a lot of money on the newspaper. To mention the main ones: Mr. Ch'oe Sŏnik (崔善益君) spent 270,000 won, Mr. Pak Yujin (朴有鎭君) 10,000 won, Mr. An Chaehong (安在鴻君) 10,000 won, Mr. Cho Sŏlhyŏn (曹偰鉉君) 56,000 won, a Mr. Sŏ (徐某氏) from Chŏlla-do 7,000 won. Besides these, excluding contributions related to Mr. Yi Sŭngbok (李承馥君), the total comes to around 1,000,000 won (including my investment). What a huge amount of money! Fortunately, Mr. Ch'oe Sŏnik took the company building under his name and is still involved with another company, which is indeed fortunate and a source of comfort. Generally speaking, Chosŏn newspapers are like the Bulgasari (不可殺이) monster that eats iron – something no one should rashly jump into. The position of a newspaper president involves much disgrace and hardship; it is by no means an honorable position like that of newspaper presidents in foreign countries.

Greatest Difficulties and Most Gratifying Moments
The greatest difficulty during my time at the company was undoubtedly the money problem. While lack of money causes difficulties in other businesses too, newspaper work is especially critical because if the paper cannot be published on time every day, its lifeblood is cut off. Therefore, not having the paper supply ready for the day ahead <16> was the biggest issue. When the publication deadline approached and the printing plant urged for paper they didn't have, there was nothing more frustrating for the person in charge. At such times, one would resort to anything, like getting emergency funds from a widow or even the king's headband case (網巾갑), if possible. Also, when we couldn't pay the wages of the factory workers, and they caused a commotion threatening a strike, it was no small difficulty. Besides that, regarding the authorities, even slight carelessness could lead to confiscation or suspension of publication, and consequently, the nominal person in charge would be summoned, scolded, and if leniency (寬恕) was granted, forced to submit a written pledge; otherwise, face fines or imprisonment. Although I wasn't imprisoned, I experienced considerable difficulty during several instances of confiscation and suspension. Furthermore, if an article was slightly careless, lawsuits for defamation or issues related to ideology (主義思想問題) and subsequent attacks were everyday occurrences (恒茶飯). To someone unaware, the position of a newspaper company head might seem like some prestigious job or rank, but in Chosŏn, there's no legal or social protection. People who clearly commit wrongful acts themselves exploit the criminal law stating, "Regardless of the truth of the matter, defamation of another's character is punishable by fine or imprisonment (體刑)," and routinely file lawsuits. Consequently, being dragged to the police station or prosecutor's office was routine. Additionally, if editorials or articles slightly contradicted or disadvantaged the views of certain individuals or groups, they would come 찾아와서, regardless of right or wrong, first curse offensively, calling you names, and in severe cases, even resort to physical assault. At such times, it felt like the newspaper company head was the easiest target in Chosŏn. I wasn't the only one who experienced this; several figures currently in newspaper companies have all gone through this multiple times. This was also no small difficulty. As for gratifying moments, there's nothing particularly noteworthy. If I must say something, perhaps it was during the great flood of the Eulchuk year (乙丑年, 1925), when I, along with the entire staff, went out to *Ttukseom Island (*島) and risked falling into the water, saving many lives of the flood victims (罹災民). Or perhaps the time of the Mie Prefecture Incident (三重縣事件), when we published an extra (號外) before any other newspaper and dispatched special correspondents, stirring public opinion!

Where Do I Go From Here?
People often ask what I plan to do now, and some occasionally encourage me to try newspaper business or some political movement again. However, I have given up on all that. Newspaper business in Chosŏn is fundamentally not financially viable. Even now, if I had a lot of money, I wouldn't do it in Chosŏn; I'd want to do it in Tokyo or Osaka, or else in China. If I were to choose, my first choice would be Tokyo, second China, and third Chosŏn. In Japan proper or China, running a newspaper offers prospects in terms of business performance, company prestige, or treatment, but Chosŏn is still not comparable in any aspect. Regarding political movements, I tried my best in the March First Movement (己未民族運動) and the Sin'ganhoe Movement (新幹運動), but they all ended in failure. <17>-(Continued on page 23) Moreover, in the current situation, if a Chosŏn person engages in political movements or similar activities, the only likely outcomes are imprisonment or disgrace. Although I have been lying low (蟄伏) during this time, even setting aside settling past affairs, I haven't cultivated myself personally at all. Therefore, from now on, I intend to focus entirely (專혀) on my personal cultivation. Since staying here in Seoul wouldn't be conducive, I plan to go near my ancestral burial site (先塋近處), the place where my late father spent his final years (百歲之地), pass the quiet years (閑歲月), and cultivate myself. However, since I am still in the prime of my life (壯年), I will certainly not retreat permanently like some decrepit, retiring figure (老朽隱退). After adequate self-cultivation, if an opportunity arises, I still hold the ambition to stage a comeback (捲土重來). -(Responsibility for text lies with the reporter)-<23>

**Summary**

Sin Sŏgu recounts his reluctant entry into managing the Chosŏn Ilbo, driven by family circumstances after his nationalist activities in Shanghai led to imprisonment and his mother's death. He details the immense financial drain, culminating in the loss of his family fortune and subsequent personal hardship. Sin regrets his initial lack of commitment and critiques collective leadership in newspapers, advocating for strong, singular control and preferring malleable newcomers over established figures. He describes the constant struggle for funds, the perils of censorship, legal threats (defamation lawsuits), and even physical attacks faced by newspaper managers under Japanese colonial rule. Despite some gratifying moments like aiding flood victims and reporting on the Mie Prefecture Incident, he concludes that the newspaper business in Chosŏn is unsustainable and political activism futile, opting for personal cultivation in retirement while retaining the possibility of a future return.

This memoir offers valuable insights for historians into the precarious existence of Korean media during the colonial period, highlighting the severe financial instability, the internal conflicts within the nationalist movement, and the intense pressure from Japanese authorities and societal factions. Sin's disillusionment and pragmatic, somewhat cynical, views on management and personnel provide a stark perspective on the challenges faced by Korean elites attempting to operate within the constraints of colonial society.

나의 後悔 되는 점
