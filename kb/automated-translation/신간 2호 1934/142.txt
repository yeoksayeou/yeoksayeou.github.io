Magazine Title: 개벽 신간 제2호
Publication Date: 1934년 12월 01일
Article Title: 八道江山遍踏記(其三)/泗泚古都覲禮 (Record of Traversing the Rivers and Mountains of the Eight Provinces (Part 3) / Paying Respects at the Old Capital Sabi)
Author: 朴魯哲 (Pak No-ch'ŏl)
Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

**From Sangdang to Hoedŏk**

April 28th

My only luggage is a small bundle wrapped in newspaper—even that feels like a big burden to me.
Thinking it would be cumbersome on a long journey, I tried to pack lightly, including only manuscript paper, one diary notebook, and a spare pair of shoes.
After bidding farewell to my parents, wearing cotton trousers and jacket, a grey *turumagi* (overcoat), rubber shoes, and carrying an umbrella, I set off on the long road toward "Hanbat" (Taejŏn).
The weather is very refreshing, but without a tailwind, I don't find walking particularly pleasant.
It's the height of burgeoning spring. My heart is drawn to the painting-like scene of roadside willows and wall flowers, and my steps naturally begin to slow.
At the base of a wall—beneath it, on the slope of the back hill, among the sparsely scattered trees, when I see yellow butterflies flitting point by point through the fully bloomed peach blossoms, I cannot help but stop my feet intentionally.

×

Around lunchtime, I reached "Kwangdae Pawi" (Bamboo Rock, 竹岩) and entered an inn to ask for something to eat. The owner's wife brought out *injŏlmi* (次食餅 - sticky rice cakes) mixed with ground mung beans, served on a stained small table.
Being hungry, I quickly picked one up and put it in my mouth. Its taste was quite different from usual *injŏlmi*. The filling of worm-eaten (버레먹은) dates gave it an extra special flavor (別味). I think this is the first time I've eaten date-filled rice cakes (餈餌) here.

×

The sound of a child's willow-leaf whistle—the sound of a farmer driving cattle after finishing farm work—the sweet murmuring of village girls gathering mugwort—among them<24> the clear, fine tune of a shepherd's light "Arirang" melody faintly flows through the gaps, spreading across the fields—the slow, taffy-like Ch'ungch'ŏng Province "Yukchabaegi" song was even more pleasant to hear.
It's a busy time for farming households. Farmhands with topknots and young boys with braided hair are mending paddy ridges, digging irrigation channels, clearing ditches, and preparing seedbeds, creating quite a bustle—the hooting of an owl hidden beyond the mountain, the chirping of a lark flitting about the field, the croaking of frogs hidden in the paddy dikes seem to hasten the already hurried steps of the farm workers.

×

From "Kwangdae Pawi," they say Sangdang is thirty *ri* away, so I still have fifty *ri* left to reach Hanbat—walking leisurely, I only reached the old town of Hoedŏk (懷德舊邑) at dusk.
Entering the town, steles (碑碣) were lined up neatly along the roadside. Most were ancient-looking achievement steles (頌德碑) for "Provincial Inspector Generals" (都巡察使), "Inspectors" (巡察使), "County Magistrates" (縣監), "Assistant Magistrates" (判官), "Prefects" (縣令), etc.—among them, three iron steles (鐵碑) stood out, adding a special charm.
These are like the "Steles of Fond Remembrance" (去思碑) composed in Chinese books, intended to commemorate the benevolence of officials so that the people of that time would never forget their grace and their virtuous deeds would shine for eternity. Yet, after not even a few hundred years, they are so dilapidated and leaning (頹圯傾仄), the turtle bases (龜跌) have shifted, the inscriptions (?) (긔) have fallen off, and the characters (字劃) are worn away—what a pity. It might be better if these broken steles (砟碣) had never existed in the first place. They are unsightly to behold. "Only broken fragments remain (斷碣만이 存焉하였고녀)."
If only these numerous ownerless stone monuments had been preserved in pavilions (閭閣) built for each, they wouldn't appear so unsightly to later antiquarians (好古者) coming to appreciate (翫賞) them—but how could one hope for such a thing?
Weren't their descendants mostly scattered and lost due to hardship and disaster (流難散亡), impoverished, illiterate, unfortunate, and declined (不遇拓落), unable even to conceive of restoring them upon seeing this state?

×

As the day grew dark and I could go no further, I stopped at a solitary inn backed by a mountain, just short of "Hanbat," to rest.
The room was pitch black, so dark you couldn't even make out the person next to you. The walls were bare plaster without even scraps of newspaper pasted on them, and the floor was as black as a well-worn wooden floorboard.
After a short while, a farmhand brought in a large bowl filled to the brim with barley rice (꽁보리밥). Receiving the tray, I ate the entire bowl due to hunger. Yet, the lingering emptiness still didn't seem to subside.
Lying down to stretch my blistered feet among six people, old men and young farm workers packed side-by-side in one room, was incredibly uncomfortable.
The smell of urine (지린내) wafting in from the urinal pot by the door—the foul smell (고린내), sweat smell, tobacco smell, and body odor (암내) creeping from the farmhands' noses, mouths, toes, or armpits mixed together, assaulting my nose.<25>
Occasionally, when the tattered, rag-like blanket lifted, I felt suffocated and couldn't suppress the feeling of stuffiness. I felt a sense of amusement, thinking I had deliberately chosen such an inn.
But unable to sleep, memories of hardships endured in places like Jilin (吉林) suddenly resurfaced in my mind.
It was already thirty years ago. Traveling on foot from Jilin for over eight hundred *ri* across the Yanbian (延邊) region (now there's a railway, but back then it was all overlapping mountains and deep valleys), climbing over Laoyeling (老爺嶺) and Changguangcailing (長廣才嶺), known bandit lairs, I stayed in Chinese inns (胡人의 旅幕). Sharing a room with opium-smoking Chinese men (되눔), sleeping on dirt floors where dust coated the back of my hand, unable to sleep a wink due to the stench (惡臭) from the Chinese men and the smell of opium smoke, I would set off again on the long road at dawn—that journey repeatedly comes to mind.
(I will omit details of historical sites seen between Sangdang and Hoedŏk for now, intending to cover them on my return trip.)

**From Hoedŏk to Yŏnsan**

The next day, I set out at dawn towards "Hanbat." Reaching a village about five *ri* south of "Hanbat," I asked a passing shepherd boy the common name of the village (洞里의 俗名), and he said it was "Pŏdŭnae" (버드내). I repeated it once more. Pŏdŭnae—an easy name to pronounce.
I crossed the "Pŏdŭnae" (Willow Stream, 柳川) bridge, went down beneath it, washed my face and feet in the clear stream, performed a cold water rubdown, and then, basking in the rising morning sun, did some stretching exercises (遊幾體操). For a moment, I felt as if I could ascend to heaven (羽化昇天), an indescribably exhilarating feeling (暢爽한 氣趣).
Passing through the Chinjam (鎭岺) town center, a prostitute (賣咲婦) in red skirt and green top emerged from a tavern with a wig (假頭), casting alluring glances (秋波) at passersby, which felt quite vulgar (醜陋). It's rare (稀罕) to find prostitutes in such a remote mountain area.
Intending to avoid that tavern street, I rested for a while far off under an old tree, screening myself with my hat, loosening my clothes to air out the sweat, and then crossed the pass towards Tugye (豆溪).
Among the students of the Chinjam Common School (鎭岑普校生), most wore *geta* sandals ("게다짝") to school, which startled me upon first sight. Some might dismiss this as trivial, but I thought it couldn't simply be attributed to fashion (流行).

×

Reaching "P'atkŏri" (豆溪), the Kyeryongsan (鷄龍山) range surrounds it on the left and right, feeling like a green folding screen.
Tugye's old name was "Tuguma-ch'on" (豆口磨村). Seeing that there is currently a Tuma-myŏn (豆磨面), it's certain that Tugye's original name (本名) was Tuma (豆磨). Tuma is the Hanja translation of "P'atkŏri," and "P'atkŏri" is a corrupted form of "P'atkari" (팟가리).
Thus, "P'atkari" being misreported as "P'atkŏri," and Tuma<26> being called Tugye, are corrupted names (訛謬된 名稱).
"P'atkari" was originally "P'atganŭn Masŭl" (팟가는마슬 - village where beans are ground), i.e., "Tuguma-ch'on," a name passed down to later generations; its ancient name (古名) was Kwang'i Pugok (廣怡部曲).

×

I reached Yŏnsan (連山) at dusk. Yŏnsan was originally Paekche's Hwangdŭngyasan-gun (黃等野山郡). After Silla unification, it was renamed Hwangsan-gun (黃山郡). Later, in the early Koryŏ period, it was changed to Yŏnsan-gun (連山郡).
Kyeryongsan is a famous mountain that has long imparted a kind of mystical folklore (神秘說話) to the people of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province. When speaking of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, the Daoist immortal-like figure Chŏng To-ryŏng (鄭道領) emerges. Thus, Kyeryongsan and Chŏng To-ryŏng are like sisters.
Until twenty years ago, the rumor that Chŏng Ch'ŏnja (鄭天子 - Son of Heaven Chŏng) would descend upon Sindo (新都 - New Capital) at Kyeryongsan was widely spread throughout the villages. Now, however, even the shadow of that tale is scarcely found, and news is scarce.
When the "Sindo Legend" (新都說話) of Chŏng To-ryŏng was actively circulating among the rural folk, various peculiar, divine Sons of Heaven would pop up—only to disappear again.
From my childhood memories, there was a shaggy-haired old bachelor who used "Ch'ŏnjihwang" (天地黃 - Heaven, Earth, Yellow) in Hanja as his mantra (呪典). Advocating a "doctrine of silence" (不言主義), he only recited these three characters. When reaching the border, his disciples would carry him across on their backs. They also provided for his food, clothing, and lodging. It was also rumored (誣說) that if disaster suddenly struck, he could avoid misfortune (厄運) through his mysterious divine protection (神秘한 玄衛).
Another Son of Heaven was said to be skilled in遁甲 (magic arts), 縮地 (earth-shrinking), 借力 (borrowing power), etc., enabling escape from all calamities and disasters. Believing he might be the Chŏng To-ryŏng descending upon the Sindo site at Kyeryongsan, foolish commoners (愚夫) intoxicated by these groundless tales added their own exaggerations (誇誔) and spread them through the villages. Consequently, for a time, the Sindo site at Kyeryongsan saw foolish commoners from the eight provinces converging like clouds (接踵雲集), creating the atmosphere of a grand marketplace of immortals (一大仙市).
How much Kyeryongsan seduced the foolish people of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, and how much the foolish people of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province believed in Chŏng To-ryŏng—the Sindo ideology served as a mystical spokesperson (神詑的代言者) for a segment of folk belief at the time, and Chŏng To-ryŏng became the "messiah" (미새아) of the foolish folk of the Hosŏ region (湖西愚夫).
But now, Chŏng To-ryŏng's shadow has long been hidden, and news from the shaggy-haired baby Sons of Heaven who revered the Sindo ideology as their "grandfather" has also ceased—how regrettable. Perhaps the time of the Sindo legend is finally ending.

×

Kyeryongsan is located about twenty-seven *ri* north of Yŏnsan town. Yi Sŏng-gye (李成桂), around the time of his ascension, intended to establish the national capital (國都) south of Kyeryongsan (the current Sindo site). He personally visited (親巡) the site by royal carriage (車駕), determined its auspiciousness (吉), roughly planned the site (基址), and initiated construction (工役을 肇興). However, due to difficulties with water transport for grain (水運의 轉漕) and the remoteness of the royal road (輅路의 邈遠), the construction was eventually halted (罷하였다). Thus, people today call that land "Sindo" (新都). It is said<27> that ditches (溝渠) and foundation stones (礎磧) still remain thick at the site (遺址), enhancing the nostalgic feelings (懷舊之感) of travelers paying respects (覲參). (Legends related to the site are not included here.)

×

During the reigns of Silla's King Chinp'yŏng (眞平大王) and King T'aejong (太宗王, Muyŏl), Kim Kwan-hu (金官侯) and Kim Hŭm-sun (金欽純, Yu-sin's younger brother) of the Saryangbu (沙潔部) Hwarang, his son Kim Pan-gul (金盤屈) of Kupch'an rank (級飡), and later, during King Sinmun's (神文大王) reign, Pan-gul's son, the Hwarang Kim Yŏng-yun (金令胤, grandson of Haegan Sŏbalhan Kim Hŭm-ch'un, 奚干徐發翰金欽春) who held the rank of Hwangsam Sŏdang Pogi Kam (黃枔誓幢步騎監), and also during King T'aejong's reign, General Suyakhu Hwang P'um-il (將軍首若侯黃品日) and his son, the Hwarang Kwanch'ang (官昌)—all fought major battles against Paekche soldiers while crossing these Hwangsan fields.
At that time, Hwarang Kim Hŭm-ch'un, under the King's command, led soldiers with his son Pan-gul to the Hwangsan plain (黃山原) and clashed (干戈를 견울새) with the army of Paekche Generalissimo Kyebaek (階伯). Initially, the battle situation was unfavorable, and they were defeated several times by Kyebaek's forces.
And Hwang Kwanch'ang, ordered by his father P'um-il, rode his horse brandishing a spear across the Hwangsan plain. He charged into the Paekche camp, killed several men, and attempted to seize (塞拔) the flag (旗旌). He even charged at Talsol (達率) Kyebaek himself before being captured by Paekche soldiers.
Kyebaek cut off his head, tied it to his saddle, and sent it to the Silla camp. However, initially regretting the valor (義勇) of the young boy, he couldn't bring himself to harm him and sighed, saying, "Silla has many extraordinary men (奇士). Even a boy is like this, how much more so their warriors (壯士)?" and sent him back.
Hwangsan (黃山), also known as Ch'ŏnhosan (天護山), is located about five *ri* east of Yŏnsan town. When Paekche Talsol Kyebaek defended against Silla forces on this Hwangsan field, he set up three camps (三營) and fought fiercely four times, winning each battle. However, because his soldiers were few and his strength weak, he ultimately died after desperate, bloody fighting (血戰苦鬪). (It is said Koryŏ T'aejo's royal portrait hall (眞殿) was at Ch'ŏnhosan).
This Hwangsan is also where King Kyŏn Hwŏn (甄萱) of Later Paekche (後百濟王) (Yi Hwŏn, 李萱, son of Ajagae, 阿慈介, the purple-robed man from Mujin's North Village) died from an abscess (등창병). At that time, Yi Hwŏn, having surrendered to Koryŏ, followed T'aejo Wang Kŏn (王建) to punish his son Sin'gŏm (神劒) and happened to tread upon this Hwangsan plain.
As Sin'gŏm, outnumbered and outmatched (兵寡力屈), unavoidably surrendered to Wang Kŏn, Yi Hwŏn couldn't bear the vexation (煩懣) caused by his son. Eventually, suffering from a carbuncle (疽瘡) for several days, he became a lonely soul (孤魂) at Hwangsan Temple (黃山佛宇).

×

Yŏnsan has no prominent local products (名物) in modern times. If forced to name one, perhaps firewood (柴木) would qualify.
In the Hosŏ region, few places might have as much firewood as Yŏnsan. Among them, the area between Tugye and Yŏnsan seems to be number one.
In every village and valley, the piles of firewood stacked up look like outer city walls (外郭) in their massive heaps (磊砢).
The *Tongguk Yŏji Sŭngnam* (東國輿地勝覽) describes Yŏnsan with the phrase "田多自古稱多稼" (Many fields, famed for abundant harvests since ancient times).<28> Indeed, within the Naep'o (내포) and Widae (위대) areas of Hosŏ, where else besides Yŏnsan, Nonmoe (논미, Nonsan), Kanggyŏng (江景), and Puyŏ (夫餘) would one mention for paddy fields (田畓)? In a poem by Yi Mogŭn Saek (李牧隱穡) of Koryŏ:

×

*Thick mulberry shade, cool summer shadow,*
*Silkworm trays stacked high, filling the house.*
*Husband toils, wife struggles, making a living,*
*Crickets chirp again under the bright moonlit night.*

×

*Small child leads the cow out of the pen,*
*Big child herds horses, able to let them roam.*
*Village elders invite each other in turn,*
*Drunk and full, how can they refuse further hospitality?*

×

This verse vividly depicts the farming scenery (田家農形) of Hosŏ's Naep'o region—a single poetic painting.
Treading carefully on my blistered feet, I entered the land of Nonsan (論山) late in the evening—about five *ri* beyond the pass, near "Pundong" (분동) and "Saoesŏng" (사외성), I found lodging for the night in a newly built, large inn.
For an inn in a remote mountain village (두메의 炭舍), it had a relatively clean and elegant feel (靜雅菌逸). The owner's wife brought the evening meal (夕飯). Surprisingly for a mountain village, there was laver ("김") on the table, and quite a few other side dishes too. The rice was piled high like a farmhand's meal (머슴밥같이 코깔모양), and I ate every last grain. Truly a delicious meal (甘食).
The way they respectfully offered scorched-rice water (숙늉) with both hands and personally spread out the mat showed the unparalleled kindness of the owner couple.
(On my return journey later, I intended to make a point of stopping by again, but contrary to my plans, I couldn't manage it, which makes me feel rather regretful.)
The room had just been used for cooking cattle feed (쇠죽), so both the hotter (아랫목) and cooler (웃목) parts of the floor were boiling hot. It was so hot that even with the door open, sweat dripped down.
But for a traveler like me with easily blistered feet, it was a suitable room, also helping to alleviate travel fatigue (路毒). In the hottest part of the floor, farm workers, lying on newly repaired earthen sections (토막), slept soundly, snoring, seemingly without breaking a sweat in that boiling spot.
The only sounds occasionally breaking the silence were the rattling noises (원앙소리) from the owner's cattle eating fodder during the night; otherwise, nothing could be heard.
Even the mountain dogs (묏개) that had been barking at ■■■ seemed to be holding their breath, asleep.
It's a lonely night in a remote mountain hamlet where even the pine-knot torchlight (관솔불) flickers and dies out. A night desolate to the extreme—an endlessly lonely and poignant night.<29>
The silent night in the mountains—Alas (嗟呼)! Why can I not forget this night—this night I don't understand for any reason.

April 30th

Rising early at dawn, I set off towards Puyŏ (夫餘). Reaching the vicinity of Nonsan town (論山邑), I asked a passing villager (村夫) if there were any old fortresses (古城) nearby. The villager said that right here is "Saoesŏng" (사외성)—a round earthen fortress (土城) on Sŏngt'aebong mountain (성태봉뫼), but he didn't know the Hanja characters for it.
Regarding this earthen fortress, I couldn't even guess without investigating it myself, so I didn't ask further.
According to the *Tongguk Yŏji Sŭngnam*, about three *ri* north of Yŏnsan town, on rugged mountain terrain (險山), there is an old stone fortress (古城) with a circumference of 1,740 *ch'ŏk* (尺) and a height of 12 *ch'ŏk*. Inside, there is one well (우물) and a military granary (軍倉), called Puksan Fortress (北山城). It also mentions another old fortress on Tosolsan mountain (兜率山), fifteen *ri* east of the town.

○

Nonsan, Kanggyŏng (江原?), Puyŏ are famed for their wealth in rice (米), truly deserving to be called the storehouse (實庫) of the Hosŏ region. But this is not *our* wealth.
Setting aside cities, even in extremely small farming villages, our wealth cannot be found. An example of this can be seen even in Sŏngdong-myŏn, Nonsan County (論山郡城東面). (Puyŏ has many places even worse than this).
Wherever *they* live, no matter how remote (陬僻) the mountain village, if their immigrant households (移民戶數) exceed ten, there isn't a place where they don't establish a school.
In every sizable village, the prominent building on the hillside is, without question, ■■.
Generally, they mainly operate large farms (農場), individually owning tens or even hundreds of times more paddy and dry fields (田畓) than we do. As side businesses (副業), they focus on orchards (果園), vegetable fields (菜田), mulberry fields (桑園), chicken farming (養鷄), pig farming (養豚), rabbit farming (養兎), sericulture (養蠶), etc., and some are also engaged in fishing (漁業).
Among them, some even imitate our style of houses (家屋) and farm tools (農具). Some speak our Ch'ungch'ŏng dialect quite well, and occasionally, some employ our workers, carrying out cultivation (耕作), threshing (打穀), etc., exactly according to our customs.

×

The *Tongguk Yŏji Sŭngnam* states that about ten *ri* west of Ŭnjin town (恩津邑) lies Hwanghwa Mountain (皇華山). Below its foot (山麓) was a flat, wide rock (平濶하고 廣大한 岩石) called Hwanghwa Terrace (皇華臺). The 31st King Ŭija (第三十一世義慈王) [error, this likely refers to a fortress, not the king himself] had a circumference of 1,650 *ch'ŏk* and a height of 6 *ch'ŏk*, with two wells and a military granary inside.
Additionally, about twelve *ri* south of Ŭnjin town, in P'unggye-ch'on village (風界村), there is an ancient tomb (古墳) popularly known (俗稱) as the tomb of King Yi Hwŏn (李萓, Kyŏn Hwŏn) of Later Paekche.

×<30>

At "Wŏnbong-ni" (원봉리), after a light meal (요기) of rice cakes (次食餅), I continued on the road, reaching the vicinity of Nosŏng (魯城). I rested briefly to wipe off sweat and then walked towards Chŭngsan (甑山).
Nosŏng was originally Mahan (馬韓) territory. During the Paekche period, Yŏryasan-hyŏn (熱也山縣) was established there. After Silla unification, it was renamed Nosŏng and attached to T'aeju (態州). Later, during the Hanyang Dynasty (漢陽朝, Joseon), it was merged with Sŏksŏng-hyŏn (石城縣) to become Nisŏng-hyŏn (尼城縣).
Thus, Nosŏng has held the names Yŏryasan, Nosan (魯山), and Nisan (尼山).
Even during the reign of King Sŏngjong (成宗) of the Hanyang Dynasty, Nosan Fortress (魯山城) had a circumference of 1,950 *ch'ŏk* and a height of 8 *ch'ŏk*, with four wells inside.

×

From Chŭngsan Market (甑山市), passing by the outer fortress site (?) (外城址) of the Paekche capital, I arrived in Puyŏ town (夫餘邑) around sunset.
The town showed little development compared to when I saw it five years ago.
The mountains, rivers, and people remained the same—a solemnly elegant and tranquil old capital (肅雅靜逸한 古邑). The water and rocks intertwine beautifully (水石이 繪龍), imbued with the charm (景趣) of an ancient capital. Its beauty is unchanged from past to present, but seeing the half-ruined fortress walls (頹圯한 城址) and the leaning steles (傾仄된 砟碣) left and right, I cannot help but feel a sense of nostalgia (懷舊의 感).
Looking around near and far, the ruins (遺墟) of the "Old Sabi Palace" (泗泚古宮) are crowded with unsightly other houses, completely obscuring the deep blue color (深靑한 빛갈) of antiquity. The solemn face of the sovereign (엄엄한 『임』의 얼골) lies hidden, changed and changed again.
Leaving Puyŏ town, I walked alone—tubuk tubuk—five *ri* further northwest and reached the ferry landing (나루가).
Twilight had already fled, and the thin wings of dusk (薄暮의 엷은 날애) lightly covered (覆嶹) the riverside world ("가람누리")—I stood alone at the riverbank (江頭), waiting eagerly for the ferryboat.
Evening smoke faintly veiled the village houses (閭閻). The Subukchŏng pavilion (水北亭), soaring as if flying on the hill behind Chaondae (自溫臺), visible several hundred *kil* downriver, silently faded its form into the darkness.
Only the willow trees lining the banks of the Sabi River (泗泚河沿崖) like a folding screen fluttered their leaves listlessly in the west wind (西風에 飄飄히). Before I knew it, the riverside village grew dark. Only the sound of oars from the Kwiam (귀암) ferryboat faintly reached me. Gazing dimly at Chaondae, the Sabi River (泗泚河), and Kuryongp'yŏng (九龍坪), I couldn't suppress my surging emotions.
Chaondae was my childhood cradle (搖簠 - likely typo for 搖籃, cradle); the Sabi River was likewise my childhood swimming place (泳遊場).
Soon, I boarded the ferryboat. Listening to the splashing water, I once again recalled the old days.
Puyŏ is my hometown, second to none. I began breathing in this land, my body soaked in the "Sabi climate" (泗泚風土) for ■, likely ten years (十年)—just as I was beginning to mature, I suddenly left this place<31> and disappeared far into the North Country (北國). How many years has it been?
Alas (嗟呼)! After drifting restlessly (樓層漂逸?) in the North Country for ten years (於焉間十年), this is my second time visiting this land.
Attending the private village school (長亭私塾) carrying my books (挾卷受業), how many times did I circle Kuryongp'yŏng? Traveling to Kongju Upper School (公州上校) carrying my books on my back for studies (負笈修課), how many times did I cross this Sabi River (泗泚水), perhaps three or four times (三四)?
Oh (嗚呼)! I cannot stop the nostalgic feelings (感舊之懷) welling up from thirty years of wandering life (逸縱).

×

After crossing the ferry and passing through "Yŏtpawi" (엿바위) street, I unexpectedly met the disciple (子弟) of Master Kang Ho-sŏk (姜湖石先生). At his earnest request (懇悃한 請), I entered an inn to rest my weary body.
Soon after, Mr. Kang had to leave due to unavoidable circumstances (不得已한 事情), walking the night path to his home in Changjŏng-ni (長亭里). As I was watching the empty room alone, fortunately, a young scholar (工君) from the "village school" (서당글) came looking for me, relieving my loneliness for a time.

May 1st

The next day, leaving "Yŏtpawi," passing Kŭmbok-ri (錦福里) on the way to "Yŏŭl" (여을), I also briefly looked at the old house where I stayed during my childhood.
This "Yŏŭl" is the sanctuary (伽檻) where I spent eleven years of my youth.
Whenever I tour the Sabi Fortress (泗泚城), I visit this "Yŏŭl," look at the fruit trees planted in childhood, climb the hill where I used to gather reeds (빨기?), and pass the main room (사랑마루) where rush mats were woven from split reeds (왕골 까서 돗자리치든). Ten-year-old calligraphy, still deeply yellowed with age (古色深黃), remains on the wall, making my already regretful heart (恰悵튼) even more sorrowful (憫栗케).
"Yŏŭl," combining the inner and outer villages (內外里) and surroundings, is a large, wealthy village (富村大里) of nearly a thousand households. Before it spreads the vast Kuryong Plain (曠遠한 九龍平原), and beside it flows the mighty Sabi River (浩蕩한 泗泚水가 滔滔히)—it can truly be called a place producing outstanding figures (人傑之地).
I continued on to Mr. Kang's lodging (寓所) in "Yŏŭl" and waited for him. While looking around the village of "Yŏŭl," I suddenly recalled the story of the Pansan Yi clan (반산 李氏네).
It was twenty-five or six years ago. Among the Pansan Yi clan here, the family of Yŏŭl's Yi Pyŏngsa (李兵使 - Military Commander Yi), full of high spirits (豪氣滿滿), was such that even local strongmen (土豪) who flaunted their wealth and power (財福으로 恣勢를 피우든) trembled before them, it is said.
Also, when I was fifteen or sixteen. One day, I visited them and listened to their stories. One of their relatives (族戚), a bit of a rogue (난봉군), jokingly (諧謔삼어) said that previously—when a certain incident (某事件) occurred—several members of the Pansan Yi clan from the Puyŏ area were implicated, arrested by the Military Police (憲兵隊), and interrogated. A malicious military policeman tried to cut off their topknots, which they cherished like their lives. But one of them, in a voice like autumn frost (秋霜같은 목소리로), shouted…………
"You dogs—don't cut my topknot—cut my throat instead!"
He fiercely gripped his topknot and offered his neck instead. The military policemen had no choice but to refrain from cutting the topknot. This anecdote (逸話) emerged.<32>
The whale-backed tile-roofed houses (고래등 같은 기와집) in "Yŏŭl" were the sanctuaries (伽檻) where they enjoyed prosperity (一樂) for generations.
Over the long years, worn by wind and rain (風磨雨嚙), the buildings (堂宇) have largely collapsed (頹圯), and the remaining tile houses (瓦家) have mostly lost their former appearance (舊態). Who would have known that pillars would rot, main hall floorboards (大廳마루짱) would give way, and the elegant gardens (曲雅한 莊園) would turn into mulberry fields (桑田化)?

May 2nd

Before breakfast, braving the rain, I went to visit No Il-kwang Hyŏng (盧一光兄) in Hapsong-ni (合松里). Il-kwang and I were bosom friends (竹馬故友), the closest of confidants (知己中의 知己).
Originally, along with Yu Hyŏng (柳兄), we formed a trio (三友一身) and were exceptionally close. But Yu Hyŏng disappeared over ten years ago, and now there's no news of him at all.
Il-kwang and I hadn't met in eight years—pent-up feelings of longing (그리운 懷抱) seemed to burst forth all at once.
However, without even a chance to talk, Il-kwang was out (出他), leaving me alone to watch the room.
Il-kwang returned around sunset (夕陽역), and after having dinner (夕飯) together, I followed him to Songdang Academy (松堂學院) to watch the teasing of a new groom (新郞다는 求景).
The young men of the village crowded into the room (接踵入室) and were deeply engrossed in playful banter (諧謔으로서 談樂).
Then, the young men teased and jostled (달고 치고) the village groom (村新郞) for a while, and soon a drinking party (酒席) began.
Il-kwang personally poured a cup of cloudy rice wine (濁酒一杯) and offered it to me. He urged me again and again (再三勸한다). Several others also offered. I refused until the end and did not accept.
It was the first drink offered in eight years, so refusing felt perhaps too heartless (몰정스럽지만), but I unavoidably had to decline. Il-kwang, unaware that I■ now abstain from alcohol (禁酒), offered it thinking only of my former heavy drinking (大酒量).
The Hapsong-ni Academy and Youth Association (靑年會) here were established largely through Il-kwang Hyŏng's efforts, so his contribution (功獻) is undeniably great.
He is a pioneer (先覺) among Puyŏ youth and, to this day (于今겄), remains a leader who consistently fulfills his duties (責務를 如一히 밟어온이중에 우두머리다).
Listening to Il-kwang briefly talk about the historical sites (遺蹟) of Puyŏ, I planned the itinerary ("古都巡歷할 順序") for visiting the old capital starting tomorrow, and stayed one night at Songdang. …(To be continued)…<33>

**Summary**

This travelogue excerpt by Pak No-ch'ŏl details his journey in the spring of 1934 through parts of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, specifically from Sangdang (near Ch'ŏngju) via Hoedŏk (Taejŏn), Yŏnsan, and Nonsan, culminating in his arrival at Puyŏ (ancient Sabi), his childhood home. He describes the landscapes, local foods (like date-filled injŏlmi), rural life during the busy farming season, uncomfortable lodging conditions contrasting sharply with memories of hardship in Manchuria, reflections on historical sites like Paekche-era fortresses and Silla battlegrounds (Hwangsan), the fading legend of Chŏng To-ryŏng and Kyeryongsan, and poignant personal memories evoked by revisiting places from his youth after a long absence. He concludes by reuniting with an old friend in Hapsong-ni, Puyŏ, and planning further exploration of the ancient capital's ruins.

From a historical perspective, the article offers interesting glimpses into rural colonial Korea. Pak's observations on the presence and economic activities of Japanese settlers ("their" schools, large farms, adoption of Korean methods) provide direct commentary on the changing demographics and land ownership patterns. His descriptions of dilapidated historical monuments and the poverty evident even in historically significant regions like Puyŏ reflect the socio-economic conditions and perhaps a sense of cultural loss during the period. The contrast between the idealized past (Paekche glory, Hwarang valor) and the contemporary reality, including folk beliefs like the Chŏng To-ryŏng prophecy, also reveals aspects of the popular consciousness.

海幕에야 連山에 이르럿다. 連山은 본시 百濟의 黃等野山郡으로 그후 新羅一統時에는 黃山郡으로 改名하엿든바 그뒤 高麗初에 와서 連山郡으로 곳처 부르게 되엿다.
鷄龍山은 일즉 忠淸道人士에게 일종의 神秘說話를 물려준 名山으로 忠淸道를 말할 때 道骨仙風인 鄭道領이 튀여나온다. 이로서 鷄龍山과 鄭道領은 서로 姊妹格이다.
二十年前까지도 鷄龍山新都에 鄭天子가 降下하신다는 말이 閣鄕간에 盛히 流布되더니 지금와서는 그 說話의 그림자도 얻어볼수 없을만치 소식이 적조하도다.
한창 鄭道領의 『新都說話』가 鄕曲간에 꼬리를 물고 돌아단일때는 各樣色色의 神異한 天子들이 몇 명식 튀여나왔다가―드러가곤 하였다.
내어릴 때 記憶으로는 「天地黃」等의 漢字로 呪典을 삼은 어느 더벙머리 늙은 總角이 「不言主義」를 내세워 단지 외우는 것은 『天地黃』세글자뿐인데 三邊에 다다르면 그 弟子들이 대신업어 건늬고 먹고 입고 居하는것도 그 弟子들이 韓族하여주며 또 급작스리 禍亂이 일면 그 神秘한 玄衛로써 厄運를 避한다는 誣說이 떠도라 단였다한다.
또 어는 天子는 遁甲, 縮地, 借力等에 能通하여 가진 災殃과 禍亂을 逃避한다 하며 이역 鷄龍山新都地에 降下하실 鄭道領이나 않인가하여 그 誣說에 陶醉된 愚夫들은 거긔에 誇誔을 더하여 閭閣으로 도라 단이면서 宣佈한 까닥으로 一時는 鷄龍山新都遺址에 八道愚夫들이 接踵雲集하여 一大仙市를 일운감이 낫섰다.
鷄龍山은 忠淸道愚氏을 얼마나 誘惑하였으며 忠淸道愚氏은 鄭道領을 얼마나 믿어왔던고―新都思想은 당시 一部民間信仰의 한낫 神詑的代言者노릇을 하였으며 鄭道領은 湖西愚夫의 「미새아」가 되었도다.
그러나 이젠즉 鄭道領의 그림자를 감초인지 이미 오래었고 그 新都思想을 「할아비」로 떠바친더벙머리 아기 天子들도 소식이 隔絶하였으니 섭섭도다―新都說話의 時運도 마즈막 가는가뷔다.

잇흔날 『엿바위』로부터 錦福里를 지나 『여을』로 갈 때 余의 幼時에 寄寓하는 舊宅도 대강 삶여 보았다

泗泚城을 巡歷할적마다 이 『여을』을 찾어 들어 어릴 때 심은 과일나무도 구경하고 빨기 뽑든 동산도 올러보고 왕골 까서 돗자리치든 사랑마루를 지날 때 十年묵은 글씨가 古色深黃한 그대로 壁上에 남어 있어 恰悵튼 나의 마음이 더 憫栗케 되는구나.
『여을』은 內外里와 그 附近을 合하여 거의 千餘戶에 달하는 富村大里로 앞으로는 曠遠한 九龍平原이 질펀이 퍼저있고 그 옆으로는 浩蕩한 泗泚水가 滔滔히 흐르고 있다―方可謂之人傑之地라 않을 수 없다.

五月二日
식전에 비를 마즈며 合松里 盧一光兄을 찾어갓다. 一光과 나와는 竹馬故友나 다름이없는 知己中의 知己이였다.
본시 柳兄으로 더부러 三友一身으로 자별나게 지내오더니 柳兄은 踵跡을 감초인지 이미 十年이 넘은지라. 지금와서는 소식조차 드를길이 없게 되었도다.
一光과 나는 그간 맛나지 못한지 八年이라―그리운 懷抱가 일시에 솟는듯싶다.
그러나 이야기할 긔회도없이 一光은 出他하고 나만 홀로 방을 직히고 있었다.
夕陽역에야 一光이 옴으로 함께 夕飯을 치른후에나는 그를 따러 松堂學院에 가서 新郞다는 求景을 하게되었다.
이곧 마슬 靑年들이 接踵入室하야 한창 諧謔으로서 談樂에 기우러지는 판이다.
그리자 뭇청년들이 村新郞을 한참 달고 치고 하더니만 금시에 酒席이 버러진다.
一光은 친히 濁酒一杯를 따러 나에게 勸한다. 再三勸한다. 그 외에 여러분도 勸한다. 나는 끝까지 사양하여 받지 않었다.
八年만에 勸하는 술이라 사양하기론 너무 몰정스럽지만 불가불 사양하지 않을수 없었다. 一光은 내가 이■ 禁酒함을 아지못하고 前者의 大酒量만을 생각하여서 勸하는 것이다.
이곧 合松里學院과 靑年會는 一光兄의 힘으로 設立되니 만치 그 功獻이 크다 않을 수 없다.
그는 夫餘靑年의 先覺으로 于今겄 指導者의 責務를 如一히 밟어온이중에 우두머리다.
