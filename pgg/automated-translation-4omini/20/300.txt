잡지명	별건곤  
발행일	1929년 08월 01일  
기사제목	구미 漫遊하고 온 여류화가 나혜석 씨와 문답기 (Interview with Female Painter Nahyesuk Who Traveled Abroad)  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue)  

It has been quite some time since I last saw you.  

Yes, it has been about four or five years.  

I heard that you studied painting in France. When did you return?  

I returned this February. However, I did not study much but rather just traveled around.  

(At that moment, his infant daughter cried softly in the background.)  

You were quite busy it seems. When did the baby arrive?  

(Smiling broadly) She was born about a week ago. This is a precious item I brought back from Paris.  

It is quite a big treasure amongst your possessions. As an artist, especially having trained in the artistic capital of Paris, this must be a significant artistic treasure. What do you plan on naming it?  

What I named it has that meaning. Since everything in France has been built anew after the revolution, I named it "Geumgyeon" (金建).  

Have there been any particular issues regarding the baby? Is she feeding well?  

There are no major issues, but as you can see, she has a reddish face due to infantile eczema, so we have to apply ointment. She is quite fussy at night, preventing me from getting enough sleep, and I have to constantly feed her milk. (He gave her milk again and then turned on the gas stove to heat more milk.) This girl seems to be stronger than the others, being satisfied as long as she sees something good and keeps a cheerful mood.  

How many siblings does the baby have?  

This is my third child; I have two older sons. (First daughter)  

Oh dear, you must be busy with farming. With all that farming work, you haven’t been writing much in newspapers or magazines lately, have you?  

(With a tousled head, he chuckles again.) You say such things. I can’t be out and about writing if I am busy with the babies. I was never good at writing in the first place, and because of family reasons, I couldn’t go out much lately.  

When did you cut your hair short?  

I cut it when I left Europe in Harbin. Originally, I had been growing it long, but when I went abroad, it seemed convenient, and since I had started to wear Western clothes, I had to cut it to fit under hats.  

Is it easier for you with short hair now?  

It is indeed convenient. First of all, it feels wonderful to have my head cool.  

But why did you grow it long again?  

While I was in Europe, I was used to having Western-style clothing and food, but upon my return, I found it quite difficult to adapt. Especially while living in a house, I cannot easily keep my hair long and neat.  

If you move to Seoul, you will likely cut it again, right?  

There’s not much difference in Seoul either. If I travel abroad again, I will surely cut it.  

I feel bad to ask such trivial matters as your health isn’t very good; I apologize again for this. By the way, when did you travel to Europe and where all did you go?  

Oh, it’s nothing. I traveled from Seoul to here, and it wasn’t a tedious journey. I left on June 19, 1927, took the Siberian train, and went directly to Paris after passing through Beijing and Moscow. I stayed there for about seven months before heading to Geneva, Switzerland, for ten days, then returned to Paris, traveled to Switzerland and Holland, then returned to Paris where I studied French for a while and spent about a month traveling through Germany. The following year, I traveled to Italy and Spain, and finally crossed to America visiting New York, Washington, and a number of cities and rural towns until finally reaching Yokohama on February 2 of this year and coming home on the 10th. Overall, the place I spent the most time was Paris, followed by America. All the other places were just brief visits that left me feeling bewildered.  

Then, what activities did you do in Paris? You mentioned studying painting, did you study much there?  

Indeed, I simply wandered around there. I barely studied specifically. Whenever I had the time, I went to the studio of a famous painter called "Bissier" and spent a few hours studying there.  

How many works did you produce during your travels?  

As for works, I don’t have many substantial pieces; I created approximately seventy to eighty sketches during my travels. (He showed the works that were kept in a cabinet.)  

Oh my, that’s quite impressive. You might have enough for a commemorative solo exhibition. Why don’t you host one?  

I have thought about it, but right now with my family responsibilities and being tied down to this small child, I’m not in a position to entertain such an idea. Perhaps if I can manage to find time in Dongnae or at least in Busan or Seoul, I will consider it. Please support me.  

Of course, I will support you. If possible, I will bring it up with others at the company and see if we can help host the exhibition for you. Also, I’d be interested to know, what is the difference between Eastern and Western paintings?  

It is quite varied. I personally have a strong interest in painting, so I kept collecting what are deemed as famous works from various places. This collection from my travels is a significant gain for me. (He pulled out some of his collection, but the cabinet door was stuck, so we had a brief moment of delay. There are about hundreds of pieces, and some have not even arrived yet.)  

I do not understand much about paintings, but they are indeed marvelous works. If you exhibit these, it could serve as great reference for the general public.  

That’s right. In our Joseon, even if you have money, you might still not be able to buy them.  

Aside from these paintings, did you bring back any other artifacts?  

Additionally, I actively collected stamps and banknotes from each country and some phonograph records.  

Oh yes, those are very good items. If you brought back rare items from various countries, it would be wonderful to share those as well.  

If I settle in Seoul, I plan to invite everyone I know and host a Le Goar (레고르) gathering.  

What was the most enjoyable thing you experienced while traveling?  

Anything is better than what we have, but it is hard to specify one particularly enjoyable thing. However, since I am interested in painting, I found it deeply moving to see ancient artworks in Italy, like in Rome, and other historical cities. The preservation of ancient works there is particularly impressive. When I visited England and America, I was amazed to find even the most rugged mountains well-constructed with good roads, and I thought that our Geumgangsan should be designed similarly.  

Was there anything significant that you learned during your travels?  

From a study perspective, I realized that unlike others, painting is indeed very difficult, and I was further enlightened about the beauty of artworks. I hadn’t understood before how profound painting can be, just as the saying goes, "One who observes the sea finds it difficult to be water." The artworks we have seen up until now or learned about seem merely like the child's sketches, and even among those artists, it seems they must have practiced countless sketches daily. I was astonished. From that, I began to comprehend that painting is genuinely difficult yet beautiful.  

How do Easterners compare to Westerners in painting? With either the techniques or thoughts?  

In terms of talent, Easterners may not lack compared to Westerners, but when it comes to studying, their diligence and perseverance are truly unmatched. Our Eastern people tend to feel satisfied after performing well, while feeling discouraged and giving up with slight failures. However, they do not do that. Even if they do not succeed today, they keep at it tomorrow, painting again until they yield good works. Among Easterners, the Chinese perhaps are a step ahead, and our Joseon people manage okay during their studies.  

What about women?  

Women, indeed, this time I firmly believed that their power is strong and not weak. Here we assume women cannot do anything, yet there they wield significant influence in politics, economics, and all other areas. Particularly in diplomacy, their inner activities are remarkably strong. I think our Joseon women should strive for the same.  

While traveling, which place did you consider the most vibrant?  

Without a doubt, it has to be Paris. Perhaps the informality does not imply it's merely busy; everything is entirely artistic. From the palaces, city walls, urban streets, parks, and even the farms are integrated beautifully like a painting. When I'm there, I cannot bear to leave. Even now, thinking back about it puts me in a pleasant mood. Anyone with expertise should definitely visit at least once. However, to do it justice, you should spend at least three to five years there, or else it will feel like just watching moving pictures.  

The Parisians, they seem to enjoy current trends regardless of past or future, do they not?  

That’s truly the case. Parisians are indeed not conservative and are quite revolutionary. Whether it’s daily needs like food and clothing, customs, or anything else, they value the new and unique. As a result, there’s a vast array of creative contributions.  

Did you meet any Koreans while in Paris?  

I met quite a few. For instance, I had a pleasant meeting with the well-respected Choi Rin, who was in Paris. He is greatly respected back home, and he garnered substantial attention and admiration wherever he went. Probably, among our Koreans abroad recently, he has received the best treatment from foreigners. I was quite envious. Please send my regards when you see him.  

Will you continue your painting studies?  

I intend to study slowly once my children are a bit older.  

This year, how old will they be—after they grow up, you will be quite elderly!  

I am thirty-four this year, but when Westerners study, the age gap doesn’t seem to matter at all. My heart remains youthful like in my younger days.  

  
**Summary**  

This interview with Nahyesuk, a female painter, offers unique insights into her experiences in Europe during the late 1920s. She reflects on the differences in artistic practices between East and West and emphasizes the empowerment of women she observed abroad. This article not only highlights the struggles and achievements of a Korean woman artist in the colonial period but also sheds light on the shifting perspectives of gender roles and cultural influences during a time of significant change in Korea. Historians may find it particularly intriguing how Nahyesuk's observations of Western society challenged traditional views of women's roles in the Korean context.
