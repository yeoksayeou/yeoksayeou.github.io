Q&A Record (2)  

Reporter: Where does an aviator look first when going out?  
Lee: Well, like anyone else, I can't speak for all aviators, but as soon as I step out, I first look at the sky. Whether it's a day for flying or not, I always pay attention to the weather—specifically, the goodness or badness of the sunlight and the airflow— in other words, the weather. The flying activity is greatly related to the weather, so at times, I only focus on the sky and end up crashing into a telegraph pole or tripping over a stone.

<13>  

**Summary**  

In this article, the aviator Lee Jeong-hee discusses the importance of observing the weather when preparing for a flight. He emphasizes that the state of the sky and air are crucial factors that can affect flying safety. This reflection on the immediate concerns of flying during the colonial period reveals the precarious nature of aviation at the time, as well as the broader context of technological advancement amidst governmental oversight. For historians, the article might illustrate the intersections between modernity and colonial limitations in Korea during this era.

一問一答記(2)
李貞喜

記者, 飛行家는 어듸를 가면 무엇을 먼저 살펴 봅닛가.
李, 飛行家도 다른 사람은 엇전지 모르겟슴니다만은 나는 門박만 나서면 하눌을 먼저 치여다 봄니다. 비행을 하는 날이나 안이나 日氣의 조코 그른 것과 氣流의 조코 그른 것 다시 말슴하면 天氣를 잘 살핍니다. 비행이 天氣와 큰 관계가 잇스닛가요, 그래 엇던 때에는 하눌만 치여다 보고 가다가 전보대에 이마를 탁 부듸치거나 돌뿌리에 발뿌리를 채임니다.
