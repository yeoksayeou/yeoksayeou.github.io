잡지명	별건곤
발행일	1930년 10월 01일
기사제목	專制國王의 白色奴隷, 新家庭生活破綻記, 新女性들 그는 왜 시집살이를 못하나. (Despot King's White Slavery, Record of Broken New Family Life, New Women: Why Can't He Live with His Wife?)
필자	一記者
기사형태	문예기타 (Literature and Other)

While I was returning to my hometown after taking care of my sister studying in Seoul, I happened to come across an unusual scene while waiting for the express train heading to Seoul in the first and second class waiting room at Daegu Station. 

"A daughter-in-law is not really a child. Why has my life ended up like this? I don't understand. Please give me a conclusion and send me off." 

The young woman, seeming to be a daughter-in-law, was repeatedly wiping her tears with a handkerchief, fidgeting with her shoed feet, and glaring at a chubby old man appearing to be sixty (probably her husband), yelling or lamenting at him without any hesitation or regard for the surrounding people. 

The gazes and interest of the people gathered in the waiting room immediately turned towards this old man and the young woman. 

The old man, embarrassed and seemingly unable to bear it, would occasionally cough and express annoyance while pretending to look out the window. The back-and-forth between the young daughter-in-law’s unreserved lament and the old man's awkward replies kept the interest of everyone in the waiting room for quite a while. 

I imagined the circumstances and so-called fate that the young woman was experiencing and was just watching when, driven by my profession (as a writer), I felt an urge to approach and inquire about her detailed circumstances. Thankfully, however, the old man only departed on the train while the crying daughter-in-law remained behind. 

In such a situation, now that she had revealed her situation to a certain extent, I thought it would be easy to approach her. So we decided to take the night train, and after giving my sister some advice, she approached the young woman on my behalf. My sister kindly said a few words of greeting, and after chatting with her, she persuaded her to come to the second-class dining room, while I remained in the waiting room, waiting for them to return. 

What follows is the story my sister heard from her. The young woman was said to have a rather lively personality.

"Of course, it was a love marriage. He is from Milyang, and I’m from Daegu. We met while I was studying in Seoul. So, he rejected a predetermined marriage after a long time separating from his family and, amid various objections from parents, we held a simple wedding in Seoul.” 

After that, our household encountered difficulties, and my husband, being a student, we could not manage our life together, so we discussed and decided that I would stay with his family until he graduated from school and we could establish a domestic life. The husband’s family was from Milyang and was wealthy and respected. In his house, there were his parents and a respected elderly couple over seventy, which meant the household was filled with burdens. From the moment a stone fell in the yard, every single person, including grandparents and younger siblings, would know, and even the rolling of a pine cone would not occur without anyone noticing, due to the severe surveillance and intimidation in the household. 

At first, my mother-in-law seemed to love me quite a bit. 

"A student daughter-in-law is all right. Even if she is somewhat clumsy, she takes care of her parents." 

It was a common occurrence when the neighbor's grandmother came by and praised the young daughter-in-law with such remarks. 

At first, I did not know any better and endured all problems, afflictions, and annoying things without complaint, obeying unconditionally. I became foolish like a woman going to a temple, and when my sister-in-law said something, I naturally went along with it, letting myself be belittled through comparisons made with the precedents of prior participants, saying "this is this," and "that is that." 

Thus, I began to carry meals for my parents-in-law morning and evening. 

Even with servants present, only the daughter-in-law was to serve the meals; it was their enjoyment and dignity. However, this was an enormous strain on me. I would gently carry the food out, and it was only after we had all finished eating and I had managed to collect the leftover plates that I could finally take them back to the kitchen, releasing a sigh of relief with weary hands. 

I couldn’t help but resist the thought of why I had to return to that old moral bondage and live as a slave. Over time, that rebellion grew stronger inside me, manifesting itself in my actions. Soon, my parents-in-law noticed this change. 

Even though they harbored discontent and regret from the very beginning about their child’s choice, they couldn’t reject it entirely, but as I gradually began to act like a new woman, they certainly felt it. 

Conceit and disdain, secretive scrutiny started to pervade from my parents-in-law, sister-in-laws, and the servants, all peering into my personal affairs through the household lens. I was in a situation of utter isolation. 

However, I also developed a severe contempt for them. 

"How ridiculous are those people? Pathetic. Are they even human?" My heart consoled itself amidst chaos. 

I felt that nothing could frighten me as long as I was backed by the strength of my husband. 

Time passed and it was April when the household began preparations for the most significant ritual. On the day of the ritual, the daughters of the house gathered to prepare the food, making traditional rice cakes and pancakes, putting their long-practiced skills to work, while I had volunteered to help with making the pancakes. As I prepared the eggs and flour, one of the young women casually said, “Ah, you from Seoul, come show us your skills. Ho ho.” 

That remark seemed to challenge the validity of my skills. 

It provoked me, and I replied in an agitated tone, “Ah, how could I, an inexperienced person, be able to make pancakes? I’ve only seen omelets and things like that at school.” The comment received a loud laugh and caused the whole room to erupt in hysteria. 

I felt that this was a huge humiliation, and I could not contain my anger, tightly gripping the arm of the woman who laughed the most, asking in a stern voice why she was laughing at me. Obviously, I was inexperienced. Yet having encountered such a situation made me completely unable to react. 

Then a voice that had been silent suddenly said, “Oh, you turn this into a laughing matter? You said you learned to make omelets at school. Ho ho ho.” The laughter rose again. 

I could no longer endure the humiliation and furiously threw the bowl of eggs and flour down, storming back to my room in tears. 

The ignorant villagers seemed to rejoice in scandalous comments. 

That night, while the others stayed up preparing the food and cleaning up, I lay in my room with my head clouded, not even looking out. 

Then something unforeseen occurred that night. 

It was probably just past midnight. It seemed everyone had gone to bed after waking briefly until the first rooster crowed. I couldn’t sleep, thinking of various things and pretending to lie down with my eyes closed when I felt a cool breeze coming through. 

What was that? I turned to look at the door. It was cautiously opening piece by piece, very slowly. 

I steadied my pounding heart, keeping quiet and lying still to see what was going to happen. 

Finally, with boldness, the door swung open, and a strange man, somewhat ragged, stepped in. 

I couldn’t hold it any longer; I jumped up in shock, startled by the look on his face. 

He turned out to be a rascally uncle who was a close acquaintance of my husband. He must have been there as a guest earlier. 

I was in my petticoat and wrapped in a blanket, I maintained my dignity as I scolded him, 

“What do you think you are doing sneaking into my room in the middle of the night?” 

He did not answer but responded only with a sly, awkward, and inviting smile displayed across his face. 

He was rather cunning. After looking at me with passionate eyes for a while, and showing no intention of leaving, he attempted to engage in some underhanded business. 

“Oh, there’s no harm in this. I just came to comfort you, risking everything. If I must, I could do something, yet seeing the look in your eyes… Ah, it’s just the passion of youth. Everyone else in the family is asleep. No one will know.” 

Then he came closer, as if to embrace me. 

I was so disgusted by that scoundrel who was trying to take advantage of the situation and just managed to shove him out, saying with all my might, “Please, just think twice before doing something disgraceful and leave before I get embarrassed!” 

I let out a long sigh afterward. 

Is this how the so-called gentleman behaves? 

And so, I faced another sleepless night. Living in this reality was no longer tolerable. 

The outdated beliefs and customs from my in-laws completely dominated and ruled the household, as any deviation in words or actions from the established norms would be seen as significant transgression, an unforgivable sin committed by those beneath them. 

The emotions and lives of the subservient were entirely unrecognized in that despotic household, wherein individuals were forced into a state of mindless submission, existing as dutiful sons and daughters-in-law. 

I'll continue the story later. So, on the next occasion, my father-in-law called me in and delivered a long lecture on ethics and morality, declaring in a severe tone: 

“You, the insensitive girl who cannot fulfill your ancestral duties and maintain peace with relatives, cannot remain in this house. So, I will buy you a modest home. Until then, discuss it with your husband and settle it properly. 

It is imperative to maintain our family’s honor." 

What followed was almost an unbearable level of sin I had to bear on my own. 

Yet, what would debating with that ignorant person accomplish? 

In that moment, I managed to stand up and boldly speak a few sentences, immediately writing a letter to my husband who was studying in Seoul at a specialized school, expressing everything, and then I came to Daegu and handed it over to my family. 

Just how desperately I loved my husband, equating it to life itself, made my parents-in-law and sister-in-law seem trivial. 

If they bought a house, surely they would support our living expenses, and then I could manage alone until my husband graduates and returns. 

But all that was merely a deceptive show to rid themselves of me. 

Even my beloved husband, whom I regarded as my lifeline, succumbed to the burdens of his parents, threatening me that he wouldn't inherit anything if I stayed on as his wife. 

Naturally, the distance between our hearts began to grow, and even after a half year, not even a room was provided for me, let alone a house. 

Now, I was dimly disappointed in my husband. 

How could I live with a man who, drawn by his parents' wealth, was willing to cast aside his unassuming wife? 

I was considering taking them to court. 

Today, I pushed through the embarrassment and staged a little drama to make that husband of mine experience a moment of shame. 

There are many unfortunate women in the world like me. 

How bitter is this? 

**Summary**

This article portrays the struggles of a young woman who, despite marrying out of love, finds herself trapped in a repressive household where she is treated like a servant. It captures the conflict between traditional familial expectations and the desires of new women in contemporary society. The narrative illustrates how societal pressures and outdated customs stifle personal freedom and happiness, particularly for women during this period. Historians might find this article significant as it reflects the broader struggles for women’s rights and the changing social roles of women during Korea's colonial era.

專制國王의 白色奴隷, 新家庭生活破綻記, 新女性들 그는 왜 시집살이를 못하나.
						一記者
내가 서울서 공부하는 누이를 디리고 오래간만에 고향에 가다가 도라오는 길이엿다. 대구정거장 1,2등 대합실에서 서울 나가는 급행차를 기다리고 잇섯슬 때 거기서 우연히도 예사롭지안은 광경을 보게 되엿다.
「그래 메누리 자식은 자식이 안이란 말이여요. 왜 내 신세를 이러케 망처노코는 영 내몰내라는 게야요. 모르겟서요. 결말을 지여주고 가세요.」
며누리인 듯한 신녀성은 손수건으로 연방 눈물을 씨스면서 구두신은 발을 토당토당 굴너가며 엽헤 사람이 잇거나 말거나 조곰도 거리낌업시 놉흔 소리로 엽헤 망연히 안져잇는 검으테테하고 비대하게 생긴 나히 60이나 되여 보이는 로인을 (아마 시아비겟지) 노려보고 호령인지 애소인지를 하는 것이엿다. 대합실에 모여 안즌 사람들의 시선과 흥미는 삽시간에 이 늙은이와  젊은 녀자에게로 몰렷다.
로인은 장죽에서 담배를 갓가수로 빨면서 창피해 못견듸겟다는 듯이 간간히 헷기침도 해보앗다가 짜증도 내여보앗다가 가만한 말로 달내도 보앗다가 안질부질을 못하는 모양이엿다. 그들의 문답안이 그 젊은 신식 며누리의 기탄업는 하소연은 연해 대합인의 흥미를 끄을면서 한참동안이나 게속이 되엿다. 나는 속으로 그 녀자의 당한 사정과 소위 운명이라는 것을 가만히 상상해 보면서 구경만 하고 안저 잇다가 문득 직업상(긔자로써) 제륙감이 동하야 그 녀자를 붓들고 자세한 사정내용을 캐보고 십흔 생각이 낫다. 그리하야 며누리가 로인을 따라가면 엇저나 하는 불안이 적지 안엇스나 다행이 로인만 남행차로 떠나고 울든 며누리는 혼자 뒤떠러젓다.
이러 경우에 발서 자기 처지를 어느 정도까지 제입으로 중인의 압헤 발설해버린 이상 그 녀자에게 접근하기는 용이한 일이다. 우리는 밤차로 떠나기로 하고 누의에게 몃가지 주의를 식여서 그 녀자 압해 나를 대신하야 내세웟다. 누의는 상양하게 내뜻을 바다 그 녀자 압흐로 나아갓다. 인사를 하고 무에라 무에라 몃 마듸 주고 밧고 하더니 주저하는 그 녀자를 잇굴고 누이가 이층식당으로 올라가는 걸 보고 나는 대합실에 도라와서 하회를 기다리고 잇섯든 것이다. 이 다음에 적는 것은 누이가 그에게서 들은 이야기다. 그는 성격이 퍽 활발한<133> 녀자이드란다.
「물론 련애 결혼입니다. 그는 밀양 사람이고 나는 대구인대 서울서 공부하는 동안에 엇더케 알게되엿지요. 그래서 그는 자기집에서 날ㅅ자까지 밧다십히 하야 정해노은 혼처를 물리치고 여러 부형의 반대속에서 서울서 간략하게 결혼식을 하엿담니다.
그런 뒤로는 우리집이 곤란하고 남편되는 사람 역시 학생의 몸으로 두 사람의 생활이 지탕해 갈 수가 업슴으로 할 수 업시 우리 두 사람이 의론한 결과 싀집에 드러가서 그가 학교를 졸업하고 독립 생활을 하게되고 살림을 날 때까지 싀집사리를 하기로 햇든 것임니다. 남편의 집은 밀양서 부자요 양반이엿습니다. 시집에는 시부모가 잇고 싀할머니 내외분까지 7旬이 넘는 로인이 잇서 싀숙이며 올케며 하야 집안이 여간 번다치가 안엇슴니다. 마당에서 돌 한아가  떠러저도 싀할아버지 이하 싀동생 하인들까지 죄다 알게되고 바람에 솔방울 한 개 구르는 것도 집안 사람 몰래는 구르지 못하는 무서운 경게와 감시하에 하로 하로 의 서투른 살림이 반복되여 갓슴니다. 처음에는 싀어머니께서는 나를 퍽 사랑하는 것 가탓슴니다.
「학생 며누리도 괜찬트라. 양 갈보갓기는 해도 부모위하는 게 제법이거든 행실도 잇서.」
이웃집 일가 할머니가 왓슬 때 신식며누리 칭찬이라고 이런 말하는 것을 멧 번들은 일이 잇습니다.
처음에는 나도 멋도 모르고 모든 우수운 일 비위틀리는 일 아니꼬운 일 성가신 일을 꾹 참고 무조건으로 복종을 하엿습니다. 절에 간 색시처럼 바보가 되고 등신이 되여 올케가 뭐라하면 뭐라는 대로 맛동세가 선참자(先參者)의 권위를 가지고 험란을 빼물고 이것은 엇덧코 저것은 엇덧코 하면서 느러놋는 것도 달게 들엇슴니다. 그리하야 나는 맛동세가 하는 일을 마터 조석으로 싀어머니와 싀아버니 밥상을 들게 되엿슴니다.
하인이 잇서도 밥상만은 며누리 손으로 밧는 것이 그들의 락이요 체면이겟슴니다. 그러나 그것은 나에게 잇서서 여간 큰 고역이 안이엿슴니다. 밥상을 조심조심 들고 드러가서 식사를 다 마칠 때까지 그 압헤 꿀어 안젓다가 숭융을 올닌 다음에야 다시 밥상을 들고 뒷거름질 처서 공손히 들고 나와서야 간신히 피곤한 손으로 숫갈을 들고 안즈면 한숨이 저절로 나왓슴니다. 내가 무엇때문에 다시 옛날 도덕의 굴레속에 사로잡혀서 노예 생활을 해야 하는냐 하는 반항심이 날로 복바처 올라오는 것을 엇지할 수 업섯슴니다. 그러한 마음이 내 행동거지에 나타나지 안을 도리가 잇섯겟슴니까. 그럴사록 싀부모의 눈치는 달라젓슴니다. 처음부터 만흔 후회와 볼안을 가지고도 자식의 하는 일이라 전연 거절하지는 못하고 들여노앗슬 터인데 그것이 차차 신녀성 행세를 시작하니 발서 눈밧게 난 것은 사실임니다. 경멸과 증오 험점을 차저내고자 하는 악의적 감시가 싀부모 맛동세 올케녀 하인들까지 왼집안 사람의 눈을 통하야 내 신변에 흐터젓슴니다. 나는 고립무원한 처지에 빠진 셈이엿슴니다. 그러나 그럴사록 내가 그들을 경멸하는 정도도 심하여 젓슴니다.
「네까진 것들이 다 뭐냐. 시시하다. 저것들이 다 사람인가.」이러고는 혼자 어지러운 내 마음을 스스로 위로하엿슴니다. 나의게는 오직 남편이라는 천만군의 힘다운 응원이 잇섯기 때문이외다. 그 따위 멸망해 가는 인간들이야 千萬사람이 다 원수가 된대도 무서울 것이 업섯슴니다.
