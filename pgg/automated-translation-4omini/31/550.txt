(Play: Rice - One Act)  
						蔡萬植  
Characters  
Mr. Lim   A Free Laborer - Temporary Worker at XX Rice Mill  
His Wife  
Daughter   A Young Girl  
Members of the XX Rice Mill Strike Team A·B·C  

Modern Day - Autumn Evening  

Setting  
In a slum area of the city.

Stage  

On the hillside is Mr. Lim's hut, with a ragged door flung open, but the inside is dim and hard to see through. On either side of the hut, ragged remnants of the slum can be seen, and near the doorway, there is a clay pot and a makeshift iron stove clearly visible. Things prepared to start a fire are piled up on one side. In front of the hut is a narrow stretch of land that can be considered a yard, and in the background, haphazardly clustered houses and huts line the hillside. As the curtain rises, Mr. Lim's wife is shown laying out a mat near the door; she is wearing a tattered dress, and her belly is quite large and uncomfortable. She is nursing her daughter, who is nude save for a wrap around her lower body.

Daughter  (squirming) Stop it.

Wife  (focused on combing) Hold on.

Daughter  Stop it.

Wife  Hold still, you little one. Look at this.

Daughter  (quiet now for a moment.)

Wife  (gentle as she combs.)

Daughter  Mother.

Wife  Yes?

Daughter  I'm hungry.

Wife  (suddenly stopping and gazing at the distant mountains, sighing.) 

Daughter  Mother.

Wife  Yes?

Daughter  I'm hungry.

Wife  (starting to comb again) Hold on. Today, if your father brings home rice, I'll make a lot of it for you.

Daughter  Really?

Wife  (thinking) Yes.

Daughter  (swallows, and feeling reassured, settles down.) 

Wife  (quietly combing.) 

Daughter  Mother.

Wife  Yes?

Daughter  Give me my clothes.

Wife  Why?

Daughter  So I can play with the neighbors.

Wife  You haven't asked yet.

Daughter  Then give me food first -

Wife  It’s because your father needs to bring rice first.

Daughter  (stubbornly) When? I’ve not had food for four times now. - Huh -

Wife  (sighing and straightening her back, aside) Oh, I'm out of breath!  

Daughter  Huh?

Wife  I'm out of breath. With just this one child, it’s hard raising them, so why have another? If you live in comfort, go to a family without children to contribute!

Daughter  Huh?

Wife  You little one.

Daughter  Huh?

Wife  Are you hungry?

Daughter  Yes.

Wife  If your father brings home rice, I'll make lots of food for you.

Daughter  That's a lie.

Wife  (to herself) Am I lying to you!?

Mr. Lim  (enters carrying three bags of rice and two dried fish with one hand. His head and clothing dusted with flour.)  

Daughter  (seeing him first, joyfully jumps up) Father!  

Wife  (cheerfully but awkwardly stands to greet him.)  

Lim  (handing the rice bags to his wife) Here - quickly (looking at his daughter) Are you hungry? (looking at his wife) One bag has white rice, another has coarse rice, and the last is millet.

Wife  (taking the bags and hastily running to the stove to prepare rice.)  

Lim  (handing the dried fish to his daughter) Here - this is a side dish. (stroking her head) Are you hungry?

Daughter  Yes.

Lim  (sitting down on the mat with her) Go inside and bring me the pestle.

Daughter  Yes (heads into the hut.)  

Wife  But I heard the people who were let go (the strikers) come to pick a fight, did it go all right?  

Lim  No.

Daughter  (bringing the pestle) Here -

Lim  (grabs the pestle and approaches the stove where his wife is cooking, hitting the dried fish.)  

Wife  Still, I can’t feel right about it.

Lim  It’s fine. Before, those guys would come and make a fuss, and that’s why it didn’t work out. But now the police are guarding us, so none of them dare come.

Wife  But those people are risking their lives.

Lim  It’s fine, it’s fine.

Wife  Then just a little more for me. (looking around) There! Brush your hair too! (laughs.)

Lim  (smiling) Is that so? (takes out a towel from his waist and brushes it off before returning to the mat to hit the fish.)  

Wife  The wives are working too, right?  

Lim  Of course. They work more than the men do.

Wife  What are they doing?  

Lim  They pick the rice. They sift through and select, pouring the cleaned rice onto a glass panel.  

Wife  I should have gone too!

Lim  With your belly like that, what would you do?

Wife  How much do they get paid?  

Lim  Three jeon for a mal. That used to be one extra one.

Daughter  Father!

Lim  What?

Daughter  (stretching out her hand) Give me some money.

Lim  Money? Here (takes out a coin and gives her a 1 jeon piece.)  

Daughter  (taking the coin and bowing.)  

Wife  What’s a child going to do with money? Buy oil for the lamps.  

Daughter  Soil -

Lim  That’s right. There isn't any oil. (pulling out a coin worth ten jeon) Go buy some kerosene and bring it back.

Daughter  Yes (takes the money and rushes into the hut to get the lamp, then runs off.)  

Wife  But why is it like that now?  

Lim  That’s because the workers said they would not take jobs if we are to reduce the pay but are asking for a strike and what it means, that’s what they are saying. Without workers, we had to work even harder, so rather than lowering the pay, it resulted in a raise instead.

Wife  So how many mal can be picked in a day?

Lim  If you’re lucky with good rice, you might get as much as forty mal, but I, not being that skilled, can only manage about twenty mal. (thinking) So I end up with about seventy-eight jeon?  

Wife  If only that could last long enough, it would be good!  

Lim  (thinking) Ah, yes! Some rice was plentiful. In any case, the rice flows like water there, pouring down. How fortunate it is!  

Wife  If only we can have rice like that to eat!  

Strikers A·B·C  (each holding a club, slowly enter from the right.)  

Lim  (startled, looks up.)  

A  (looking back at C) Is he here?  

C  Yes.  

A  (stepping forward to Lim) Hey.  

Lim  (hesitantly) Yes?  

A  You are one of the workers at XX Rice Mill, right?  

Lim  No.  

C  (stepping forward) No, you see, I’ve followed you for a while and even came to this place to remember your house, but...  

Lim  No, really.

Daughter  (who has come back with the kerosene, sees the strikers and hides behind Mr. Lim out of fear.)  

B  (aggressively stepping forward) You son of a gun, why are you sneaking around?  

Wife  (growing worried, approaches Mr. Lim.)  

A  What in the world? Are you the one who got that flour on your head?  

Lim  (touching his head timidly) This, um, this...  

Wife  You’ve been grinding at the mill down there.  

B  Women, hold on a moment.  

Wife  (angrily) Why should I hold on? Who do you think you are telling other people what to do? What’s it to you if others work or not? I’ll be damned if I have to see this.  

A  (calmly) No, we’re not aiming to stop you from working. We initially worked at that rice mill. But the owner was reducing our pay from 10 jeon to 1 won, while the women sorting the rice were reducing from per mal to 5 jeon, so we organized the strike.

Lim  Yes, I understand.  

A  So, if we are not working now, it’s because you aren’t taking any rice out, so it can’t be helped. If you guys work on, you are forcing us into deeper trouble!

Lim  I see!  

Wife  (having finished preparing the rice, calls out) It’s time to eat. Come have some food!

Daughter  (voluntarily begins to eat.) Eat, father!  

Lim  Oh, yes, come eat.  

A  Now, look. You think you’re working at the XX Rice Mill and getting a good pay, but that won’t last long. Our strike issue will be resolved, and you’ll be kicked out immediately. If you face a situation where you can’t work, why do you bother with unimportant things? We are laborers and if we don’t support each other, we will all perish, right?  

Lim  (standing silently, with nothing to say.)  

Wife  (having placed rice on the table) It’s hungry, come!  

Daughter  (still eating alone) Father, eat your rice.

Lim  Oh, yes, eat up.  

A  Now, just think! You guys work at the XX Rice Mill, but you are only given a run-of-the-mill pay. Do you think that will last long? This is why we are on strike. If we stop you from working today, the owner will have no rice left to sell the following day.  

Lim  (nodding slowly) Yes, yes.  

A  See? So it’s not going to do you any good to work with us getting in each other’s way!  

Lim  Yes, I understand that. But I’m also hungry...

A  That’s a sad matter indeed! Nonetheless, you should look for work starting tomorrow.  

Lim  Is it just me?  

A  Of course not! We’ll find others and talk with them so they understand just like you. If they do, great, but if not, we might need to use force to make them understand the owner must not get what he seeks.  

Lim  (nodding as if to say he understands.)  

A  If we can’t even have that rice tomorrow, it would drive the owner to surrender.  

Lim  (calmly.) That makes sense. So I shall quit starting tomorrow!  

A  (eagerly grabbing Lim’s wrist) Truly thank you! So you will quit? Have you joined the union?  

Lim  What union?  

A  The Free Labor Union.  

Lim  Oh, no, I don’t know about that.  

A  Then you need to join starting tomorrow! There are many benefits involved.  

Lim  Then I will join.  

A  Great! Then we will be taking our leave. We will talk it over at the union tomorrow about our tasks ahead. So take care!  

Lim  (dazed as he watches them leave.) You all seem quite hungry, why not come have a bite of rice?  

A·B·C  (simultaneously looking back at the rice, smiling sleepily.)  

Lim  Just a bite each!  

A  No way! You should eat it. Who knows when you might eat it next time? Tonight you should eat hearty and sleep well!  

A·B·C  (exit to the right.)  

Lim  (dully watches them leave, lost in thought.)  

Wife  Hurry and eat!  

Lim  Yes (sits down on the mat.)  

Wife  (worried) Can you really say such things? How should you have answered?  

Lim  But their words seem to make sense.  

Wife  So you are going to starve tomorrow?  

Lim  Starving is what we are used to, so it’s not so frightening!  

Wife  (tearfully) But still...  

Lim  (looking thoughtfully at his wife's belly and the rice) Their words carry truth. It’s not our fault that we live this way. (thinking) I can learn more by going to the union. Yes, yes, if we eat, we must eat a lot! Let’s eat up! (smiling at his wife) Come, let’s eat! (as he lifts his spoon to serve rice, the curtain falls.)

**Summary**  

This one-act play depicts the struggles of a poor family living in a slum during the colonial period in Korea, conveying the dire situation of laborers amid labor strikes for better wages. The dialogue reveals not just the personal issues of the family—hunger and poverty—but also broader social issues of labor rights and the impacts of industrialization. This narrative reflects significant historical tensions surrounding labor movements in colonial Korea, where workers faced severe repression while striving for rights that would benefit their families and labor communities, marking a crucial aspect of the colonial experience.
