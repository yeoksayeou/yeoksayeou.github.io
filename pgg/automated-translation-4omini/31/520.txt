기사제목	南大門 (South Gate)  
필자	로아 (Roah)  
기사형태	시 (Poem)  

南大門  

Open is the southern gate, I hear its creak,  

Where has the soldier who stood guard gone?  

Riding a donkey, having traveled a thousand miles,  

When will he come to see this worn stone gate?  

Thinking back on the twenty years that have slipped away,  

The people, dressed in different clothes, have gathered,  

Yet, as they spread a net to block their path,  

All the unspoken sorrows are alike in this place.  

Kicking off the shackles while departing from Bukgando,  

Thinking of Seoul, I turn to see your face,  

The tears of this nation's people fall as they turn away,  

Now there is no way to hear news of you.  

Pitiful is the crescent moon, embracing your waist,  

As I wanderingly dream of tomorrow,  

The beggars sleep through the night,  

While the southern gate waits silently for their return.  

-1930. 8. 10-<162>  

**Summary**  

The poem "South Gate" reflects on the passage of time and the deep sorrows of the Korean people during the colonial period. It captures the emotions of separation and longing, portraying a metaphorical southern gate as a symbol of loss and unfulfilled hopes. Historians might find the depiction of collective sorrow and social conditions particularly revealing, illustrating the psychological impact of colonization on individual lives and national identity.
