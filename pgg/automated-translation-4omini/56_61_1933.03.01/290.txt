A Short Iron Pencil  

March has arrived, and the soft breath of spring has swept across the world, nearly eliminating all traces of winter. The willow trees are beginning to sprout new green buds on their branches, and the grass, which seemed dead over the winter, is starting to push through with fresh, green shoots. Thus, everything is embarking on a new beginning, in harmony with the prelude of spring.  

In the past February issue, just three days after publication, we were sold out! Due to an overwhelming number of orders, our sales department had to work overtime every day, sending replies that every order would be turned into the March issue.  

In this situation, we have reluctantly stopped the reprint, deciding to put out the March issue as originally planned. We sincerely apologize to those who have ordered late.  

The long-awaited work by the writer Wŏlmyŏng-sanjin, "The History of the Vast Sea" has received widespread acclaim after being serialized for a year. The first part was published in the February issue, but due to the writer's circumstances and other reasons, it could only be released in that form without a subsequent long piece. However, something new will be published next instead in the March issue: Sin Kyŏngch'un's "Notes of the Unwritten." It will be continued in the April issue. In May, a uniquely intriguing piece will be started in "Byeolgwon," so please look forward to it.  

Despite that the March issue couldn't fully meet the original plan, we find satisfaction that it won't fall short compared to other issues. [Smiley]  

**Summary**  

The article presents a warm welcome to the arrival of spring, highlighting the emergence of new life in nature. It notes the overwhelming popularity of the February issue, which sold out shortly after release, necessitating a halt in reprints as the March issue was prepared. The magazine discusses upcoming features, ensuring continuity of engaging content for its readers. This piece reflects the cultural dynamics of the period, showcasing both the magazine's adaptation to reader demand and the broader context of life in Korea during Japanese colonial rule, where creative expression had to navigate commercial pressures.
