필자	YYY  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

As the New Year approaches, the old year is leaving. "Ah! Time flies!" it is too clichéd a lament, but is there anything worth discussing? With no freedom in everything and society being so desolate, can we even call this a society where people live? While I pondered over the year-end scenery, the chatterbox B-kun rushed in, shaking off the snow from his shoes, creating quite a stir. “What are you drinking over here? Isn’t it fun to see...” he exclaimed, and before I could reply, B-kun abruptly changed to an unusually serious and truthful tone. “Isn’t it true that alcohol isn’t so bad? But do you think I’m just a mere drinker? But…” he waved his hand dismissively. “In drunken honesty, let me ask you: why do Koreans never finish anything they start? Just like a dragon's head and a snake's tail; they start strong but taper off! Take these overseas students who have returned; what are they doing? When they first come back, they complain about how newspapers introduce them, but after a few days, their tracks fade into obscurity. So, what distinguishes them as overseas students?” He unexpectedly brought up the topic of overseas students.

“Then,” I seized the opportunity to grab B-kun, “let’s take a moment to evaluate the characters of those who returned from abroad.” Although we couldn’t possibly analyze each one in detail, at least we could provide a general overview of the overseas returnees.

Yes, it’s not just about those people; is society as a whole not like that? At first, these students, not knowing much, only focus on their places and try a few things, but when they actually engage, they realize it’s quite different. Eventually, they become disappointed and give up on their initial ideas. Are they really to blame for this?

“Oh, this guy!” B exclaimed. “You seem quite fond of overseas students! Maybe you’re a devoted Christian and you should try studying in America through the church’s connection!” And is your house rich enough to send you for studying abroad?

“Why? Have you achieved anything by studying in America?” I asked. “Whether it’s through the church or your own funding, if you can, there isn't anywhere you can't go to study...” 

“Come on, if it’s studying in the U.S., I don’t care! If you go there and only see how well others live, talking about freedom and a paradise on earth, what good does that do?” 

If they had just enjoyed themselves a little, it would have been fine, but problematically they often congregate in foreign universities and then talk about women's issues; isn’t it all just a façade they tried to put on?

“Oh! True! But did they really need to specifically be overseas students from America? Besides, aren’t there faculty members at specialized schools? There are those who still gained reputation in this world. Take Ishihara Jun who became a professor at Tohoku University in Japan; didn’t he also get involved in a love problem with Hongo Amoriko—now that’s a scandal? It shows that similar issues exist with humanity everywhere. You must be looking at things through colored glasses if you think only American overseas students are prone to such drama.”

“In our youth community, don’t we respect people like Sin Heong-u, who is quite a prominent figure among American overseas students?” However, in reality, when they return home, it seems almost nothing happens, and news about them is hard to come by.

“Right, that’s true. But even though those students might be representing American overseas students, each deals with matters according to their individual beliefs and has their own positions; it does not mean we should negate others.”

“However, looking around, aren’t there also some doctors who set up practices? And there are a few writers as well.”

“Doctors are indeed few; but even they usually end up at church-affiliated hospitals. There are hardly any who run private practices. And let’s not get into writing; anyone can write without studying abroad!”

“Are you trying to offend those individuals?” 

“Hah! It’s just that they lack strength and capability; they’ve been bested by the odds. Regardless, if American overseas students keep going like this, what future do they have? What kind of standing do they hold? Presently, they are not doing much socially, nor are they personally active. Isn’t it reasonable to be pessimistic about the future of American overseas students?”

“However,” I countered, “what about other overseas students? Other than American students, how many German ones are there returning? Besides this?” 

German overseas students! Don’t even get me started; numerically it’s true that there aren’t many, but not one is absent. They all diligently fulfill their responsibilities. Historically, Germans returning to Korea might not have been numerous, but they’ve gained a reputation that the world knows about. For example, Ch’oe Tu-sŏn was the first to return and became an important figure in the education sector, while Li Kwan-yong has made significant contributions in journalism, ideology, and academia—not to mention Kim Jun-yŏn, who, though not making a massive impact, has always seemed quietly resolute upon returning. You can talk about Yun Chi-hyung too, who came back after earning a Western doctorate, and Lee Saeng-yong, who even brought home a Western wife and opened a clinic—is that not suggestive?

Ah, and regarding Jeong Seok-tae—his personal activities are quite remarkable. From the day he first returned, he’s established an internal medicine clinic in a bustling part of Seoul and is active in many areas. The late Jo Go-man’s energy was unmatched—who could compare to any overseas student? Or even Lee Gak-su, another doctor, who seems to have opened an impressive clinic in Susong-dong and seems to advertise vigorously; you must've seen the robust promotions recently. 

In any case, I feel quite sympathetic to the German overseas students! Their understanding of vitality shows that people should be active or they might be left lifeless. 

Yes, that’s right! A society lacking in vitality is indeed nothing to behold. But, ultimately, the study system in Germany is so advanced that obtaining doctoral degrees isn’t trivial, while most people from Korea returning here seem to have nearly all acquired doctorates. Does that indicate people of talent have exceeded expectations?”

Well, they might have achieved it if they truly desired it. PhDs are simply human endeavors, aren’t they? Just a little bit of talent and a bit of hard work in research, and one could attain a degree.

“If that’s the case, then yes, German overseas students indeed show superior performance, but surely there are others as well?” 

“Aha! There must be some from France as well. For instance, didn’t Literature Scholar Lee Jeong-seop return recently? Also, isn’t Kim Jae-in, known for writing travel narratives in the Dong-A Ilbo's supplements, returning from France?” 

“What are they up to?” 

“I’m not sure; they seem newly returned. But in France, they’ll likely engage in artistic endeavors.”

“Other than that, it seems we haven’t heard much from the supposed numerous French overseas students?”

Well? I haven’t seen many either. The situation after the war has driven many economic troubles, so it’s understandable that tuition is costly.”

“By the way, are there any individuals who returned from England?”  

“Who would have thought? Min Yeong-hwi’s son, Gyu-sik, returned after studying mining at Cambridge, but what can I say? He had been persistent, but now he’s employed at some bank and lives comfortably in a nice brick house in Bukakdong. And Park Seung-cheol, who might have studied a lot in Germany, but he returned from England nonetheless; it must feel a pity though. He teaches geography and history at a central school and holds a teaching position at a specialized school, but can’t seem to catch a break. Isn’t it pathetic? Returning as an overseas student and ending up as a middle school teacher, newspaper reporter, preacher, or translator! How disheartening!”

“Oh you! But you still must learn something! Isn’t that something to appreciate?” 

The conversation dragged on at this juncture, and I halted my words. At the very least, I’m grateful to B-kun for providing such valuable information.

**Summary**

This article reflects on the activities and achievements of returnees from studying abroad during the colonial period in Korea, juxtaposing them with their peers from different nations, notably Germany and the U.S. The dialogue exposes a cynical view of Korean students' lack of initiative upon returning home, as well as the societal pressures that may contribute to their stagnation. Historians might find it revealing how these reflections mirror broader themes of colonial identity and the challenges of modernity faced by educated Koreans in the early 20th century.
