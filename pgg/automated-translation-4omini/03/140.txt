기사제목	仁祖大王을 업고 다러나든 負商의 鼻祖 白兎山 (The Ancestor of the Burden Bearers, Baekto Mountain, Carried by King Injo)  
필자	茄子峰人  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literature, Other)  

The ancestor of the burden bearers, Baekto Mountain, who carried King Injo, is indeed a matter worth viewing at least once. This year, the Year of the Rabbit, is particularly significant for the lineage of Baekto Mountain. It has been about 303 years since the year 1622, when General Lee Jeop raised an insurrection and directly charged toward Hanseong. Upon receiving urgent news of the rebel army's direct advance toward Muwha Hall, King Injo fell into disarray, bewildered and unable to decide whether to flee east or south. Under cover of darkness, he escaped alone in fear around 3 a.m., choosing to avoid the chaos and make his way toward Gongju's Double Tree Mountain Fort.

As the king exited the southern gate and crossed the Luyang River to reach Gwachon, dawn broke, and the sounds of roosters and dogs could be heard in every direction. His feet were blistered, and he felt faint, unable to muster the strength to speak or lie down on the ground in such a pitiful state. 

"Oh, Heaven! Please look upon me. The loss of my own body is not strange, but what will become of the nation's state?" As he prayed, he struggled along, eventually sitting on a small stone by the side of the main road to rub his aching legs. 

At that moment, a burden bearer, merrily coming from behind, caught his attention. He was a robust man, about nine feet tall, with a face as flat as a rice cake, and he seemed to have great vitality. So intrigued was the king by his presence that he cautiously approached him. "Sir, where are you headed as a burden bearer? Might you stop for a moment?"

"Going to the Southern Road. What is it?" the burden bearer replied, stopping out of surprise. 

"Going to the Southern Road? That’s lucky... I too am heading that way, but may I ask you something?" The king continued, "I live in Seoul, and I have never traveled this road before. I urgently need to get to Gongju but, due to my weak condition, my legs are swollen and I cannot move more than an inch. I must go, but what can I do? If you might consider leaving your burden and carrying me on your back, I assure you I will repay you handsomely in the future. In fact, I would pay you double what your goods are worth right now." 

The burden bearer considered this for a moment and examined the king closely, realizing he was not just an ordinary person. "Is he perhaps a higher official on an inspection tour?” 

"He must be! Maybe a secret royal inspector? That sounds reasonable. Regardless, since this person is no ordinary man and he shows good intentions, I will oblige," he resolved. "You are a distinguished gentleman, and since you have reasons for your predicament, how could I refuse? What I carry is not anything special—just some pig feed—but it’s about four mal (말) worth, and I cannot simply leave it behind. However, I could let you ride on top of it."

"Sir, how much could that possibly be worth?" The king interjected, "I know it’s tough, but bear it, and I will carry everything with you if it helps you out." 

"No, that's fine. This is something I can carry three times over. Please don’t hesitate. Now let’s go," the burden bearer replied, impressively demonstrating his strength.

"By the way, what’s your name? Where are you from?" 

"I’m from Hwanghae-do, Baekto. They call me Baekto Mountain." 

"Well, I am Mr. Lee from Seoul." After a brief introduction, the king climbed onto the back of the burden bearer, who strode confidently forward.

After a while, they passed a tavern, and on the entrance of the tavern, a fire pit was set up to light the guests' tobacco.

Seeing this, Baekto Mountain immediately craved a cigarette. "Let’s have a smoke!" 

While still on the burden bearer’s back, he twisted the three-pronged tobacco pipe from his pouch and loaded it. He slathered it with his saliva and drew a deep puff at the fire pit without even putting the king down. 

As he kept puffing, the burden bearer noticed his own discomfort with the king clinging tightly to his back, making him almost choke as he breathed in the heavy smoke. 

It felt unbearable; the king thought he could hardly stand it. However, despite the situation, he felt he had to endure it in silence. After a long moment ensued, the burden bearer finally put down the pipe, and they continued on their way. The king was lost in thought during this time: "Now this is not the moment to complain, but if I regain my position in the palace, I will remember to reward this man for his kindness by granting him something special." 

Feeling grateful, he mentally resolved, "When I return, I will immediately confer unto him a handkerchief. Furthermore, I shall decree that all burden bearers should have handkerchiefs."

As they traveled past Suwon and Cheonan, they finally arrived at Gongju. Achieving this journey felt as effortless as riding a swift horse. They were about to cross the Gyeong River when they waited for the ferry man to come. The king thought about how much easier it would be to set down the burden now, but he continued to shoulder it.

Suddenly, the ferry man arrived, and they crossed the river. As they moved closer to Dangsung Mountain, a local magistrate met them to guide them back into Gongju.

Once they arrived, those nearby were first taken aback by the unusual sight but quickly guessed the stature of the king, and they offered a meal for the evening. Then they inquired about the nature of their journey, and at this point, the king could finally reveal himself.

"I am your king. I have been unjustly pursued by the treacherous Lee Jeop, and I made my way here, but through divine aid, I hope to return to my throne again. You all must help me devise a plan!" 

Upon hearing those words, the local magistrate and Baekto Mountain, along with others present, were awestruck and prostrated themselves in humble supplication. Without delay, they organized a response to gather troops for an uprising to declare war against the rebellion. 

Thus, in Gongju, the righteous army emerged, and notable figures like Jeong Chung-sin and Lee Seo championed and finally subdued the rebellion. King Injo returned to his palace and initiated new governance, beginning with summoning Baekto Mountain.

"What is your wish? Speak freely." 

Without hesitation, Baekto Mountain stepped forward and humbly stated, "What desire could someone of my lowly status have? Simply to see the king return to his palace would suffice. However, should my true wish be granted, I would be the leader of the burden bearers." 

"If that is your wish, it will surely be fulfilled.” The king granted him a long bamboo staff, a handkerchief, and a burden-bearing strap and decreed that all burden bearers could possess these three things.

From that point onward, Baekto Mountain became the undisputed leader of the burden bearers, and their influence could not be understated across all eight provinces of Korea.

If I were to express in detail about the burden bearers, I would surely need more paper. 

However, due to space limits, I can't elaborate on the legacy of Baekto Mountain, which would certainly warrant further discussion as well. 

**Summary**  

The article narrates the story of Baekto Mountain, a burden bearer, who aided King Injo during a critical time of flight from an uprising. It highlights themes of loyalty, honor, and service, depicting the evolution of Baekto Mountain into a revered figure among the burden bearers. Historians may find interest in how this tale symbolizes the resilience of common people, reflecting societal structures and values during the tumultuous period of Korea's past, particularly under colonial rule.

이리하야 使令의 길 引導로 黃昏 쯤 해서 雙樹山城으로 드러가니 성 직키는 者가 보고 처음은 疑訝하다가 亦是 非凡人物임을 짐작하고 마저드려 저녁을 供饋한 뒤에 所從來를 무르니 이때까지야 엇지 숨기고 잇스랴 그래
「朕은 너이의 님금이다. 不德하야 反賊 李适에게 쫏기여 여기까지 피해왓스나 天佑神助하야 반듯이 還宮할 날이 잇슬지니 너이는 나를 도아 모든 것을 圖謀하라.」 고 令을 내렷다. 그제야 성 직키든 別將, 使令, 白兎山 모다 惶恐無地하야 伏地再拜하고서 一刻을 遲滯치 안코 公州營에 下令하야 大小官屬을 招致하고 宮兵民兵을 徵發하야 不日征伐을 宣布하얏다. (이로부터 公州使令得勢)

이리하야 公州에서도 義兵이 이러나고 鄭忠信
李曙 等 名將이 이러나서 결국 亂을 平定하고 仁祖王이 다시 還宮하야 새로운 政事를 펼세 爲先 白兎山을 불너 「너의 소원이 뭐냐. 원대로 말하라.」 하닛가 白兎山은 서슴지 안코 御前에 나와 「賤人이 무엇이 소원이겟슴닛가. 大王께서 還宮하신 것만 下情에 깃부도소이다. 正 所願을 엿주랍시면 負商의 領首가 소원이로소이다.」 하얏다. 「소원이 그럿타니 할 수 잇느냐. 그대로 해주노라.」 하고는 니여 長竹 한 개와 手巾 한 개와 작댁이 한 개를 친히 下賜하면서 모든 負商들에도 각각 이 세 가지를 가질 것을 말하얏다.
이리하야 白兎山은 영원히 負商의 首領이 되얏섯다. 이로부터 負商의 勢는 朝鮮八道에서 忽待를 못하얏다.
附. 負商에 대한 니약이를 具體的으로 써스면 조켓다.
그러나 紙面 關係로 白兎山 니약이도 다 쓰지 못하니 엇지하랴. 負商들의 人事法도 적어 노으면 한 번 우슬만하나 그 亦 생각은 잇고 못한다.＜47＞
