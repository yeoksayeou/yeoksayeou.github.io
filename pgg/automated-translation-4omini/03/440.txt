						審査記者 金復鎭, 安碩柱, 一記者  
The signboard appraisal meeting doesn't hold any special meaning. We hope this will serve as a small reference in the commercial world for Korean people, and thus, while causing trouble for Mr. Kim and Mr. Ahn, we requested a brief evaluation. Luckily, we hope that the various persons in the commercial field will understand and read this with love.

Reporter: Let's start from Jongno 3rd Street. Although there are certainly some stores without signboards, there won’t be much that would particularly catch our eye, nor are there those that would visually bother us. Shall we begin with this store?

Suk: The placement of Baegmiya Western Goods Store is slightly southeast, with a wide intersection being quite bright, and the shop building is somewhat narrow; however, due to the effect of the signboard, that narrowness is not particularly noticeable. Considering this store's location and the road's conditions, it would be prudent for passersby to clearly see it from a distance. If it were a taller building, there wouldn't be much need to rely on the signboard, but in the case of a store like this, I see a need to attract attention with the signboard. This store's signboard indeed stands out from afar. What might be the reason for that? It seems to lie in the width of the character's fonts, showcasing a profound design. The harmony of the somewhat dark interior of the store and the prominent looks of the signboard brings about a unique aspect where the signboard is aptly separated. I believe it has considerable effect not only from a distance but also from up close with interesting fonts.

Seok: (I agree.)  

Reporter: Next, what about the signboard from the Funeral Goods Manufacturing Woodwork Shop? 

Suk: Ah, this shop has managed to convey its specialty. However, considering it as a conscious signboard was a failure, and the two strips of funeral items made from dark blue wood have created a "silent signboard" that seems unexplainable.

Seok: I also agree with that. However, such a style and color have been accustomed to our eyes for a long time, and while they might tickle the curiosity of foreign tourists, there doesn't seem to be a strong flavor suited for a funeral family. So, while it may be regarded as a sort of signboard, it suggests more the colors painted on the building rather than the true effect of a signboard, so let's just put it aside.

East Asia Football Manufacturing Company

Reporter: Ah, the East Asia Women's Association's signboard is certainly not quaint, is it? Since it is originally a women's shop, I wonder if that is how it looks to me... Haha, how does it appear?

Suk: If you evaluate everything with that perspective, its shortcomings will be hard to recognize. (Of course, only recognizing the shortcomings cannot be called a proper evaluation.) I see it this way: If they hadn’t wrapped the house with such a large signboard, the things inside would appear quite insubstantial in that extravagant building. However, besides this signboard, there are too few that show specific expressions of the items in the shop, and it seems friendlier to use whatever Korea style they can. However, the colors are too complicated, which may fail to capture the attention of passersby in a fleeting moment, leading to a rather uncomfortable impression.

Suk: Overall, it has not done well. First, to have the whole signboard taken up by a woman's image for a women's shop isn't foolish, but it would not be taken as an acceptable affair for a rather large establishment. Moreover, how could the nude images of Roman paintings relate to this store? It seems a bit absurd, though seeing copies of Roman paintings while passing by carries another meaning. Furthermore, the most awkward aspect as a signboard is that its scale (size) has adversely affected its effectiveness.

Reporter: The signboard of the Sieun Western Shoes Store is rather strange, isn’t it?

Suk: Yes—indeed it is. I can’t remember how it was before, but something like this exists. However, this is quite unsuitable as a signboard. Moreover, surprisingly, the yellow signboard of Jeil Deogwon Store has been more prominently noticed. This phenomenon is called "borrowing scenery." In signboards and architecture, one cannot underestimate the importance of borrowing scenery. If attention is paid to this point, it would be quite amusing to observe.

Suk: It might be a bit exaggerated to say it will be amusing; I once saw something similar in a Western magazine's ad, and later I believe I saw it on Honmachi Street. However, in terms of signboards (like for shops), it is much better to strive for better copy than to resort to ugly works. Ultimately, for our shop signboards, isn’t it absurd? It seems everyone should be able to guess it is a Western shoe store...

Reporter: Oh dear, that’s passing. It is not that easy to create an assessment without seeing how it might be perceived, but the Lin Hat Shop sign, where a Western lady in a "Silk Hat" and a dashing Western gentleman appear, along with the scenery behind them, is extremely emotional, don’t you think?

Suk: You are saying something ridiculous. That signboard has no connection whatsoever to the hat shop. Even if they were just selling "silk hats," wouldn’t it be utterly foolish to use such a signboard to attract customers? Does it not risk damaging their own prestige? The scenic background near Gwangmudae which isolates us from our people and our lives is so different; what relationship does that signboard have with us? (The customers here are only a part of our people.) Regardless of the meaning of the signboard, if they simply want to attract customers, wouldn’t they need other stimuli or enchantments beyond magic? I think of it this way: commerce must flatter its target. Thus, success requires encroaching upon the lives of others. Commerce plays a significant role in acquiring the territories of larger countries.

Suk: Of course, but even if the signboard might be peculiar, it does not seem to have an exceptional effect. Although traditional elements might be irrelevant, there are these peculiar examples present in Korea... After all, it’s a signboard that discreetly closes one's eyes.

Reporter: What about the signboard for Dongyang Pharmaceutical Company? That tiny sign only has the character (약) for "medicine," and while the shape is okay, aesthetically it doesn’t seem very good. I lack aesthetic sense, though.

Suk: No, aesthetics is not the problem. Overall, the sign itself doesn't need to be that beautiful. In a world where this exists, merely looking at it broadly is overstated. Putting an end to casual talk—this is among the finest we’ve seen thus far. The character "약," complemented by blue lines on the edges, has considerable impact, I believe. This is what a proper "signboard" would look like.

Suk: I generally agree.

Reporter: Now let's evaluate the bookstore signboards. Since they are all... rather similar, would it be alright to evaluate them all together? While I personally am not impressed, we have Deokheung Bookstore, Dongyang Bookstore, Bakmun Bookstore, and Yeongchang Bookstore, etc.

Suk: They are all entirely inconsequential. Even though they’re called bookstore signboards, I don’t know if they were made by a committee of book groups, but it’s evident that the characters are not striking, and the alignment of the roof and the ground is poorly noted—those going to the bookstores are undoubtedly knowledgeable, so why are such unnoticed signboards allowed? The least significant, however, is the fact that the chimney of the central heating is awkwardly standing in front of every bookstore sign.

Suk: In a moment, I mentioned that borrowing scenery should be mentioned. Borrowing scenery is using someone else's assets for your own benefit. Given that signboards carry the meaning of urban competition, those with sharp minds may succeed here. Even if bookstores need larger signboards in cursive or a more casual design, that doesn’t have to be the case.

Reporter: How about the storefronts resembling a row along Cheongnyeon Hoi-kwan?

Suk: I see that all the signboards have a poor inclination. It seems that this is perhaps the largest flaw.

Suk: The uneven signboards! One could lament that the holy exterior of the only Cheongnyeon Hoi-kwan of Christianity has been marred.

Reporter: Excuse me, but the Yuchang Commercial Company (located above Chonggak) is actually quite amusing, and if that triangular building were situated on Jongno 4th Street, it would become more famous than even American companies. Look at that signboard. From the ground up to ten thousand jang (length), it seems that this is not yet open for business. Please, let’s not be too harsh on this shop.

Suk: This shop has a unique building with its unusually high signboard; to see this shop, one would need to view it from Samgaksan. 

Reporter: Well, reporter, could you lend me a bit of your height? My stature is short, and so is yours, so how would it appear? My legs are trembling; it would be hard for me to evaluate this signboard.

Suk: To use a Western manner of expression, Japanese women's makeup must look as if they emerged from a coffin, pale and yellowed from the chin down. Similarly, the shop's signboard decorates its existence while informing consumers of the shop's presence. If, then, this unusual signboard resembles Japanese women’s makeup, people like us must also find it quite similar. Ultimately, whether in modern architecture, how a rounded signboard fits a sharp-angled building becomes crucial.

Reporter: Let’s now discuss the signboard of Heungwha Pharmaceutical. How many years has it been? I do not know if they have given this signboard a taste; however, how does it appear?

Suk: The anachronistic grand attire might be the laughing stock of the character's portrait taken as a sign. The most hideous is what stands out the most; whether it's a painting or anything else, it must not ruin the essence.

Suk: Doesn’t it resemble the "Nindan" brand? If a signboard gives a feeling of accumulating wealth, it seems almost like one should not earn anymore upon noticing it. Perhaps it is an unpleasant thought, but one should consider this regarding the overall dignity of our commerce. If only they polished their exterior, what could they achieve?

Reporter: As we pass by the Shikdohwan, let’s take a moment to observe. It boasts an award for the number one gold medal.

Suk: This kind of signboard only fits in a Shikdohwan. To draw in patrons, is it not enough to showcase foreign atmosphere? However, if they also construct the entrance suitably, the effect could be even greater. Regardless, a signboard alone cannot stand by itself. The building and the signboard must synchronize harmoniously; only then does the signboard acquire its intended power.

Suk: Indeed, among everything evaluated so far, the signboard gilded on the ground representing the golden symbol of omnipotence, along with the letters made of the like, perhaps suits the people's interest with money.

Reporter: The big corner near Gwanggyo has had several signs change and now Fukushima Store has been around for quite some time, appearing to flourish; perhaps the owner put in some pocket money or that they have a better method than the previous owner. Ah, look at those signs, how do they appear?

Suk: They are astute signs created by a sharp mind. From the start, it hides the earlier sign of Fukushima Store and reads "Original Price Offered," and since the color and lines of the signboard are acceptable, it appears like the original wouldn't be necessary. "Shik-sun" will need to develop methods to attract customers.

Reporter: What about the Baek Commerce Association's sign?

Suk: In my view, it is among the best. Such a style of sign seems to overlap several times and may don’t seem out of order.

Suk: Since we are on the topic, let’s comment on the mesh sign created from iron threads; that it only applies during summer might be chilling during a harsh winter. Witnessing what appears to be like a beautiful woman in a gauze dress is an interesting thought, yet the background made of white iron patterns painted long ago is all but terrible—much like a sketch of a grilling facility—thus...

Reporter: The Geumgang Commercial Association's sign may be the last I speak of.

Suk: This shop seems to have changed its signage multiple times but remains much the same now. Ultimately, could they develop any taste with regard to their signboard?

Suk: However, if the jumbled aesthetics yielded ugly outcomes, its result is not too good, though maintaining simple may have been preferable, especially considering that it is a significant moment for the signboard; it may have had influence.

Reporter: Ah! They have cleverly adjusted the spacing of the signboard’s lettering so that through "Yeongchang Leather" with "mark" in the center and "Leather Commerce Association" is formed. If they threw the "mark" in the middle and inserted "Han" instead, it would become "Yeongchang Leather Commerce Association," haha, but how does this sign appear?

Suk: (Suk) calls it a temporary response.

Reporter: Here we have a rather solitary pharmacy close to a cliff, and regarding the mainly seen sign hanging from the overhead, the they seem several has quite similar above ground level.

Suk: The large sign, or whichever one is made, is undoubtedly reminiscent of Gyeongdan Pharmacy, categorized as elsewhere, sort of like the Shikdohwan, but a bit more high-toned.

Suk: Well executed. It seems that the wooden joints also carry a sign's role. The unfortunate part is I cannot determine if the "Zhang Yonglak Store" signed until it raised into the Agricultural and Industrial Company feels the same way.

Reporter: Let’s go from Gwanghwamun to Jongno. For some reason, there seem to be no eye-catching signs at all.

Suk: It seems so.

Reporter: The Yeongseong Western Shoes Store strikes first as noticeable.

Suk: That's a fact. There’s something strangely appealing in the picture, so it likely is a successful tactic.

Suk: I agree.

Reporter: Here is Deogwon Store, reportedly boasting several branches and also managing East Asia Women’s Association; how does this sign appear?

Suk: That’s a question. It seems far from expectations. If the headquarters and branches intend to convey a sense of richness... why not strengthen the sign for appearances or city beautification?

Suk: Silent.

Reporter: Then how about the adjacent Han-Yeong Company sign?

Suk: Just stop here, though it could pass as better than Deogwon Store’s sign. However, attempting to display the width of the letters on both sides somewhat hinders the perception of their formal stature.

Suk: Silence.

Reporter: Coming to mind... What about the Hwasin Commerce Association?

Suk: Well, this site is located along Jongno 4th Street, making it eye-catching by nature. However, absolutely it's not a strong point.

Suk: The golden background looks quite decent. It looks like it’s readily identifiable and deserving as a sign.

Reporter: The Jongno Police Station as well as the Kim Heung-ho Trading Company and the court corner seemed to form an oddly significant connection; what are your thoughts?

Suk: That said, the Kim Heung-ho Trading Company is uniquely varied in norms, with no distinction, and the Daekuk Rubber Company shows a sort of better standing. The letterings on iron and wood stand clearly, just as they do on date lines. This style is its merit; they just need a clearer background that shines brightly. To conclude, this one shop seems the best.

Suk: I agree.

Reporter: The Han-chang Commerce Association, Lee Wan-sik Store, and the Jongseong Commerce Association generally showcase similar signboards; how might they be evaluated?

Suk: Standard signs are better than ugly signboards made through designs that degrade them; they’re simply common enough that there isn't much to praise or criticize.

Suk: The blurred lines among the Hwoomok Shop and securely maintained signboards seem maybe regulated by some custom; the producing phenomenon could be present.

Reporter: The self-made shoe store, what about that one? 

Suk: Whatever it may be, it's well constructed overall.

Suk: The lettering and its design seem to imply precision. As a women’s shoe specialty store, their sign fits minimally, yet the side sign pales by comparison.

Reporter: Next up, Joseon Women’s Craft Society! The sign must appear quite respectable, right?

Suk: It isn’t strictly necessary for a sign to be devoid of illustrations; yet the illustrations adopt a Western style, which indeed expresses what “Craft Society” indicates, and the writing matches those illustrations. It may stand out while seeming plain, though, somewhat lacking energy.

Suk: It seems compelling. The entire shop has harmonized well with the ambiance. I agree with Mr. Ahn, as a monochrome blend to this degree might carry charm.

Reporter: Let’s put a pin in this; we have to see Gyeongil Western Shoes Store too.

Suk: For a western shoes store, merely sketching an illustration seems proper. However, hanging it at a corner appears out of place.

Suk: There are no differing opinions here. 

Reporter: Is there anything more to note? How about discussing the East Asian Football Manufacturing Company?

Suk: Certainly a rare illustration in Seoul. Whether it depicts a human or something resembling a human closely, it is an ugly signboard in every sense.

Suk: Until now, despite assessing them based on aesthetics alongside ugly forms, I think something like this with its distinctive character shouldn’t be disregarded. Perhaps it may just carry its unique taste, but ultimately a signboard should aim to be conspicuous. They could have improved it further to help with sales.

Reporter: Next, let’s see the Seonsi Department Store.

Suk: Referring to an unneeded illustration could be a roadblock. The winding curves apparent also help make it a façade though in essence, it isn’t particularly good. Saying it’s ugly isn’t enough to label it as "excellent."

Reporter: The Park Deokyu Western Shoe Store, reputed to be the predecessor of the Yanghwa faction, how does its sign manifest?

Suk: If it’s also a sign boasting its trend, it shouldn’t be misleading, while also not leading with an ugly image; its middle sign could drown that identity.

Suk: I would not have grasped that either.

Reporter: The Geumhui Instrument Store...

Suk: As mentioned even before, the sign functions as an ornament. Thus, this shop needs to modernize its sign writing. The building and its illustrations...

Suk: I agree.

Reporter: The Asahi Western Clothing Store presents a Japanese taste too clearly.

Suk: Indeed, it has such aesthetic from the outset...

Suk: The clothing store’s sign appears trifling with non-finesse. Yet still, if simplicity prevails, then it is preferable…

Reporter: Moving onto the Sangtae Western Clothing Store now.  

Suk: Viewing at the end would merit a full appraisal. If the design of legs or even those of Western people weren’t depicted, it likely wouldn’t seem worth noticing.

Suk: I concur.

Reporter: Well then, shall we end this discussion? Among what we have seen, which sign was the best and which was the worst?

Suk: My thoughts put the Chorong Pharmaceutical Company or the Joseon Women’s Craft Society as among the best in Jongno.

Suk: I, too, would agree with the Joseon Women's Craft Society and Jongno’s Dongyang Western Shoes Store.

Reporter: Then out of what struck the most visually... 

Suk: Everything stands out quite well, yet it would be difficult to clarify. Still, I might add Baek Commerce Association, Yuchang Commerce Association, East Asia Manufacturing Company, or Gyeongil Shoes Store as such. 

Suk: Whatever particular standard may yield no proper judgment, I agree with your sentiments.

**Summary**

The article features a discussion on various signboards of stores in Seoul, evaluating their aesthetics and effectiveness in conveying the shops' identities. The conversation reflects the perspectives of the reporters, who analyze how these signboards engage customers, their cultural implications, and how they fit within the modernizing context of 1920s Korea under Japanese colonial rule. Sign boards are a unique blend of commerce and culture, and this reveals tensions between traditional Korean aesthetics and the adoption of more Western styles during this period, a subject that may intrigue historians examining cultural transitions.

看板品評會 별다른 뜻이 잇는 것 아님니다. 朝鮮 사람의 상업계에 조곰이라도의 참고가 될가 하야 金安 두 분을 괴로히 하야 간단한 평을 청한 것임니다. 다행히 상업계 諸氏의 理解잇는 愛讀이 잇스시기를 바람니다.

記者  여보 鍾路 3丁目으로서부터 시작하기로 합시다. 이외에도 물론 간판 다라노은 상점이 업는 바 안일 것이나 별로 눈에 띄일 만한 것도 업슬 것이며 다른 의미로 눈에 거슬녀 보히는 것도 업슬 것 아니요. 그러니 이집부터 시작할가요.

復  白木屋洋品店의 位置난 약간 東南向일뿐더러 넓은 십자로가 압흐로 비끗하게 잇고 상점건물로는 협착한 편이나 간판의 효과로 인하야 도로혀 그 협작한 푼수가 서트롭게 눈에 띄이지 안치요. 이 상점의 位置로 또는 도로의 형세로 어듸로 보던지 원거리에서 통행인의 시선에 잘 보히도록 하는 것이 得策일 것임니다.
가령 고층의 건물이라면 하필 간판의 힘을 차용할 까닭도 업슬 것이나 이와 갓흔 상점에서난 간판으로서 주목을 끌 필요가 잇다고 봄니다. 이집 간판은 원거리에서 특이하게 시선에 드러옴니다. 그러면 그 원인은 어데 잇슬가요. 그것은 字體의 廣狹으로서 심오하게 보이는데에 잇는 줄 암니다. 좀 어두운 듯한 상점 내부와 간판의 깁히 보이는 것과의 조화의 묘를 엇어 간판 제대로 分離하여 잇지 안음에 잇는 줄 암니다. 원거리 뿐만 아니라 근거리에서도 흥미 잇는 字體로 하야＜114＞ 상당한 효과가 잇다고 생각함니다.

東亞풋볼제조공사
