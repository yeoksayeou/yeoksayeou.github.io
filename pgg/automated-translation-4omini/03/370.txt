잡지명	별건곤  
발행일	1927년 01월 01일  
기사제목	月宮에 게신 아젓씨 前上書, 丁卯 첫날 아츰 動物園 鐵窓에서 白톡기 上書 (An Address to the Moon Palace by a Certain Rabbit, on the Morning of the First Day of the Year of the Rabbit from the Iron Bars of the Zoo)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Other Literature)

To the Honorable Rabbit in the Moon Palace. Are you all doing well, celebrating the first day of the New Year just like the people of this world? Enjoying drinks, eating rice cakes, wearing new clothes and shoes, playing yut and running around merrily? I imagine the Moon World is very similar to the Earth where we live, being the closest to it.

Oh dear! It wouldn’t do to neglect greeting you for the New Year on such an important occasion. I wish you peace and health, and hope your attendants are well. Is Lady Hōrō doing okay as well? And are you still grinding herbs skillfully? I am extremely curious about your situation since I have never been there.

Dear Sir, is the Moon World similar to our human world? Are there mountains, waters, and all kinds of plants and minerals? I have heard you and Lady Hōrō reside there, as well as the Great Hall, and that you are busy grinding herbs, with volcanoes and clouds also being present. It seems the Moon World is similar to our human world, as I have mentioned.

Is that so? Then I must ask, do you all enjoy and celebrate the first day of the New Year in the Moon World just like we do? Eating rice cakes and drinks, singing and dancing around joyfully? Why is it that you do not partake in the celebrations on the first day of the New Year and are just busy grinding herbs? Are you preparing rice cakes to feed children? Or perhaps grinding herbs to serve Lady Hōrō? Those who dedicate themselves to their duties like you seem to be few and far between. The human beings also say, “Even after watching every night, he is only grinding herbs!”

Dear Sir, on Earth, people regard this year as the "Year of the Rabbit!" I wonder if you think the same in the Moon World? People are talking about "Year of the Rabbit, Year of the Rabbit!" everywhere and it seems that our rabbits have some special connection with the humans. During this Year of the Rabbit, stories about rabbits constantly circulate among people. There are dozens of folk tales and songs, and they perform plays featuring rabbits and make operas celebrating them.

Dear Sir, please take a look. Children sing: “Rabbit! Rabbit! Let us gather and dance!” as the bright full moon rises in the sky, illuminating everything. As the world sleeps, in quietude, they bounce joyfully together, singing. 

“Rabbit, rabbit! 
Come together and dance! 
The round moon rises bright, 
Illuminating the world’s night, 
While everyone else falls asleep, 
Into the calmness they creep! 
Rabbit, rabbit! 
Come together and dance!”

This song is adapted from a popular tale, possibly about the Rabbit’s Sacrifice, that they sing during theater performances. Ah, Sir! Furthermore, there is a song that children sing in praise of the Moon Palace.

“Oh bright moon, shining moon, 
The moon most beloved by Li Tai-bai (Li Bai, the Tang Dynasty poet), 
In that moon, indeed, 
Amidst the cassia trees, 
I shall gather and prepare elixirs, 
Craft fine dwelling for my parents, 
To live for thousands of years, 
To live for thousands of years.”

This is a song sung repeatedly by children whenever the bright moon shines at night, reflecting how great the Moon World is that such songs spring forth from human mouths.

Dear Sir, I can hardly bear my envy of your abode. Is there no way I could ever visit? Would it be possible for you to send down a rope or bridge of clouds so I could make a trip? I find living here in the human realm intensely miserable, particularly in this brutal zoo with iron bars where I am merely an exhibit. How can I live under such circumstances? Sir, I beg you to take me along with you! Even living on the branches behind the Great Hall and eating cassia bark, I would feel no pain if I could do that, as long as I could satisfy your needs and serve Lady Hōrō.

Oh dear Sir, allow me to express some grievances lightly. It is said that once, the rats lamented their circumstances, cursing humans. The year before last, the ox lamented his fate, berating the humans. Last year, the tiger who resides in a nearby cage lamented his sorrowful environment while scolding humans. Yet, dear Sir, my circumstances are truly pitiable. I was born in the eastern hemisphere of the Earth, in a beautiful land known as Korea, particularly in the mountainous region of Gangwon-do, which is famous for its picturesque landscapes.

I was raised there for five years along with my mother and three siblings, living off acorns, chestnuts, beans, and wild herbs available that day, even when it snowed or rained. Yet, during the harsh conditions, when food was scarce and my siblings cried for it, I had to search for food among the hills, with my ears perked up, hopping over stones in the snow, digging for roots or nibbling on dry leaves, enduring various hardships until I barely made it through.

But then, unexpectedly, I fell into a trap set by the mountain folk, who caught me out of sheer misfortune. I struggled with all my might, but how could I stand against those cruel hands of humans? They skinned me, consumed my flesh, and turned my bones into trinkets, having given up the idea of killing me. Perhaps it was my fate to endure more suffering as they didn’t kill me but kept me alive and carried me to this zoo in Seoul, Changgyeongwon, where I now suffer.

Dear Sir, what use is it to complain further? Even the great mountain tiger, known as the king of the mountains, is confined behind iron bars; the lion, the lord of beasts, is in that state; even famous large creatures like elephants and clever foxes are likewise captive, as are the supposedly human-like monkeys. Reptiles, hippos, and all kinds of creatures are also trapped in these houses of glass, suffering in this iron-caged existence while rabbits like me are even more helpless.

Dear Sir, those humans are undoubtedly cruel and vicious beings! I cannot fathom how they were granted such intelligence, bodies, and abilities by the creator. Their heads, bodies, limbs, ears, eyes, noses, and mouths are neither strong nor firm like lions or tigers. How is it possible for them to possess such intelligence?

They possess a multitude of weapons, including guns, knives, spears, nets, clubs, traps, and more. How can we endure this fate? When I lived in the mountains, I was eaten by tigers, wolves, big cats, and condors. Yet I have come to this human world, where I die by bullets, spears, nets, clubs, traps, and by savagery. I lament my sad destiny!

Dear Sir, I humbly ask you to show me compassion and transport me to the Moon Palace. If that is not possible, please rain down explosives or poison gas to purge this vicious human world. Let no fierce creatures remain, whether tiger, eagle, wolf, or goat—only let the weak, gentle, and morally innocent animals remain.

Dear Sir, is there such a thing as explosives or poison gas in the Moon World? If so, do not hesitate to unleash them upon these wretched humans! If there aren’t, then please throw down a water jar or something equally destructive. Strike down the heads of those evil humans with a thunderbolt.

Dear Sir, I truly cannot endure this any longer. Please, have mercy upon me. Tears are veiling my eyes, so I shall conclude here. I shall speak of the "Rabbit's Sacrifice" or "Rabbit's Trial" at another time. (The end)

**Summary**

This article is a fictional letter written from a rabbit held captive in a zoo to a mythical figure residing in the Moon Palace, expressing its woes and longing to visit the Moon World. The rabbit observes the New Year celebrations of human beings and envies their freedom, contrasting its captivity and suffering under human oppression as a zoo animal. Historians might find the narrative reflective of the feelings of despair and helplessness experienced by individuals during the Japanese colonial period in Korea, as it hints at the struggles for freedom and identity amid oppression.
