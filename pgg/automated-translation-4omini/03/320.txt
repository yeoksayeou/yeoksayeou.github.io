기사제목	抱腹絶倒 양초귀신 (The Candle Ghost that Stomach-Laughs)  
필자	波影  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Other)

In the new year, they say good fortune comes with laughter, so I shall tell a humorous tale that might just stretch your sides with laughter. 

Once upon a time, in an innocent age, there was a man who, after visiting Seoul, came back complaining. He had seen a bright candle (蠟燭) which lit up the room, and curious about it, he bought several to bring back to his hometown, showing off to his neighbors that he had seen the marvel of Seoul. He distributed three candles each to the houses, thinking it was a token from the capital. 

The townsfolk, however, were quite confused. Since they had never seen such objects before, they didn't know what to do with them. They were hesitant to question the man who brought them, so they just wandered around, asking each other aloud if anyone understood what these long, thin things were for. 

After enough time had passed, five young gentry decided to go to the most respected teacher in the town to inquire. 

“Teacher! The man who went to Seoul just brought these back. He said they were tokens from the capital and gave three to each house, did he happen to bring any to yours?” 

“Of course we have them, we got nine!” the teacher replied.

“Ah, so you received quite the special gift, Teacher... but we really don’t know what these are or what they’re for. We came to ask.” 

“No one, not even foolish people, would be unaware of what that is. It’s for eating soup. People in Seoul eat soup with that.” 

“Really? They use this for soup? Does it taste good?” 

“Of course it tastes good... if it didn’t, do you think people in Seoul would eat it? It’s delicious and nutritious!” 

“What is it, anyway, that makes it taste that good?” 

“It’s called ‘baeg-eo’ (白魚) which is a fish that lives in water and is dried.” 

“What a strange fish. It has no eyes, and what’s this thing that’s so sharp?” 

“It’s a fish that’s originally eyeless... and that odd sharp thing?” 

“This part right here, what’s this?” 

“That is the fish's 'anus'.” 

“Oh, I see. It really is dried fish... So, really, people in Seoul eat all sorts of fish.” 

“Therefore, isn’t it that Seoul is well-favored?” 

“So how do you cook this into soup?” 

“Ah, are you really this ignorant? You boil water, slice it thin with a knife, and eat it with sauce.” 

“Does it taste good that way?” 

“Of course it does. Now, since we’re at it, come over to my house and we’ll make some for you to try.” 

The teacher, having not known from the start, wished he had spoken honestly, but when it came to the guests, he called his family to boil water and prepare a meal with the fish, soy sauce, and green onions, then brought out six bowls of soup. 

“Now, eat some. You’ll be amazed by the taste.” 

“Well, that’s the case. Just thinking about this might surprise my insides.” 

“I’m not talking. Just eat. I first tasted it in Seoul last year, and it’s my first time having it again today.” 

Then the five gentry, preparing to eat, noticed a strange oil floating on top of the soup. 

“Oh my, what's this strange oil?” 

“Ah, you fools are just babbling. That's just the goodness floating on top. Even beef soup produces its own grease.” 

Hesitant, the townsfolk forcefully swallowed the soup, enduring the unpleasant sensations in their throats. 

Finally, they couldn’t take it anymore. 

“Oh my, Seoul food feels like it's clogging my throat. I might die.” 

“Haha, how could a coarse man's throat adjust to refined food? Eating it carefully is key.” 

They could do little but keep gulping, their throats feeling tight, mouths wide open, and breath labored, they struggled while the teacher himself was more affected than anyone else. 

Just then, the person who had brought the candle came into the house. Overjoyed, they exclaimed, 

“Oh thank goodness you came! We tried the baeg-eo soup you gave us, but it clogged our throats! Is it meant to be this way?” 

The man was taken aback and widened his eyes, 

“Did you eat it? How could you eat that...?” 

Upon hearing this, the people were shocked, 

“Oh my, this is bad! We just ate something that isn’t meant to be eaten!” 

They all exclaimed in dismay. 

“Who would have imagined? Who told you to eat it?” 

“What? It was the teacher telling us to eat it!” 

The teacher's face turned beet red, and he just stared at the floor without a response.

“That’s not fish, but rather something to light fires with. Now look here, watch this…” 

The man struck a match on the wick, and the room suddenly lit up, revealing to everyone, 

“Oh dear, we just ate the light!” 

Incredibly stunned, they imagined that they now had fire in their bellies, jumping about in panic, 

“Oh dear, fire!” 

“Oh dear, what if our stomachs catch flame?” 

Amidst the confusion, the teacher, who had kept quiet, became more frightened than the rest. The more he thought, the more he panicked, suddenly yelling, “Let’s jump into the water before the flames start in our bellies!” He dashed outside, gathered everyone, ripped their clothes off, and dove into the stream, leaving only their heads above water to prevent flames from igniting their insides. 

On this bright night, a traveler passing by was frightened by the bizarre noises coming from the stream, only to see heads bobbing above the water. 

“How strange these fellows are... they’re acting like crazy lot, afraid of fire when soaking in the water!” Unable to wait to see their foolishness, the traveler moved along his way.

Summary

This humorous narrative revolves around villagers' misunderstanding of an object brought back from Seoul—candle wicks—which they initially believed were a type of dried fish to be eaten in soup. It highlights the ignorance surrounding urban objects and illustrates the deep-seated stereotypes about city folk vs. country folk during this period in Korea, emphasizing the cultural disconnection brought about by colonial influence. Historians might find interest in the portrayal of the mistrust and confusion between rural and urban identities during the colonial era, revealing insights into societal dynamics at that time.
