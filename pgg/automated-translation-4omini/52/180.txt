기사제목	學窓夜話, 脫走 東京行 (A Night Talk from the School Window, Escape to Tokyo)  
필자	態超  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)

『You must go to medical school. I will not send you anywhere else.』 

This was the command, the declaration my father issued as soon as I finished middle school. However, for some reason, going to medical school seemed like a death sentence to me. So I stubbornly resisted, saying, 

『I must go to art school. If that’s not possible, please at least send me to a technical school.』

『That’s out of the question. You have to study medicine. Art, engineering, and literature won’t provide a living.』

Looking back now, perhaps I have a broader perspective, there’s surely merit in my father’s words. However, at that time, for some reason, they struck me as if they came from a rigidly stubborn mind.

I tried many ways to persuade my father, saying my piece in a solemn tone of voice, begging and pleading. But my father remained adamant, maintaining a firm position of, 

『No.』

『Ah, dear father, do you really intend to thwart your child’s future?』

Day and night, I tossed and turned in my bed, grappling with my frustrations to the point of agony. I even attempted the now-common hunger strike strategy, which I had tried ten years prior, but it was all in vain.

『Fine, let’s see how it goes. I’ll carve my own path.』

With not a dime in my pocket, I had no choice, but within my heart, I vowed to find a way to escape, and from that moment on, I became desperate to devise a plot. Ultimately, I successfully executed the last resort, my escape to Japan, on August 18 of that year. Now, what remains is to relate the chronicle of that escape.

Whenever my father made a bank deposit, he often sent me on errands. So I drafted a plan based on this point. However, my father seemed wiser than me, as he anticipated that family circumstances would unfold this way and completely stopped sending me to the bank and even made sure to send my younger brother, always secretly, without involving me.

Consequently, my escape plan faced a temporary halt. Then one day—it could be justified to call this my historic day—my father went to the bathroom after lunch. I noticed the stamp pouch I was eyeing carelessly lying on the floor of the loving room, separated only by a wall. Once my father finished bathing, it would take at least five minutes to clean up. I had to use these five minutes to cleverly move forward with my plan. My heart was pounding so fast I trembled uncontrollably. In my ears, I could hear a buzzing sound from the anxiety.

Holding my breath, I crept like a sneaky cat to the pouch tied around his waist. Trembling like a patient suffering from a fever, I opened the pouch with my shaking hands and pressed the stamp onto the pre-prepared payment slip. Then I hidden the stamp back into the pouch and quietly exited the room. Once outside, to calm my racing heart, I went to my room and lay down for a while.

While I had stolen food from the cupboard before, this type of grand theft was something I had never attempted in my life. Therefore, the effort felt immensely more taxing than stealing dried rice. Luckily, it was my own family matter; had I committed such an act in someone else's house, I would surely have faced charges of theft and embezzlement by now.

The first phase of the stamp mission was successful, but the next stage of nabbing the bankbook was quite a challenge. I had no idea where the bankbook was.

Crossing into the bedroom, it looked like the bankbook would be kept in a hand-locked safe. I searched the drawer thoroughly, and eventually, I managed to open a padlocked one, unexpectedly delighting in finding the safe inside. However, I had no means to open it. I had to investigate my father’s waist pouch again, but now that my father had bathed, he was surely firmly maintaining the pouch. Unless I was a famous magician or a renowned hypnotist, there was no way I could snatch the key unnoticed. I considered breaking the safe open, but I had no idea how to do so. Out of options, I phoned the local locksmith saying I wanted to buy a replacement key, only to be told I had to bring the safe. There was absolutely no way I could get the safe to the locksmith; at this moment, I was at wit’s end!

Meanwhile, I sensed my mother's suspicions growing as she asked, 

『Why are you suddenly rummaging through the drawer? What are you searching for? No doubt, you've committed some wrongdoing.』

『Oh, no—there’s something strange inside that safe.』 I responded, trying to feign ignorance.

By evening, my father had gone out. I headed to the loving room and started rummaging through my father’s belongings. I inspected every nook and cranny of the box thoroughly. However, I couldn’t find the key. Eventually, my hand reached the drawer. I bent a small knife to fit the keyhole and tried to pry it open several times. Lo and behold! The door clicked open.

My heart raced!

Unexpectedly, I spotted the precious bankbook in sight. The yellow envelope revealed it was currently used. I hastily closed and locked the drawer, and swiftly exited that room.

Now everything seemed to be successful. It was long past banking hours, and my father would not be withdrawing the bankbook. Looking at the calendar, tomorrow wasn’t Sunday either. As long as I managed to get through tonight, everything should go according to plan.

I hardly managed to eat dinner (as a matter of fact, I had no appetite.) I met with close friends and went to the gathering place (聚雲亭) to discuss the plans for my trip to Japan, ask them to take care of everything, and let them know I would write back as soon as I got there, encouraging them to come to Tokyo.

Lying in bed, I couldn’t sleep. I took the bankbook out, carefully checking it to confirm it had 585 won (원), written in my father's hand.

My father’s fury would surely erupt if he discovered a dime was missing, and there would be no way he would send me money for tuition in Japan. So just to be safe, I decided to take all but one hundred won, more or less costing, pretending as though I was taking five hundred won, and then marking it 585 won. After checking that stamped paper again, I felt ready.

As dawn broke, I got ready promptly, preparing to head out. I said to my mother, 

『Don't cook rice for me. I’m off to eat birthday food.』 and dashed to 聚雲亭. The three of us exchanged one last chat, shared some laughter, and then I headed home.

When I lay down in bed, sleep was nowhere to be found. The next morning, I quickly got ready, dressed in a suit, and told my mother I was leaving without breakfast.

After gathering my things, I said goodbye to my friends by the train station. Once I reached the bus terminal, I bought three tickets for the express train to Busan. Just as I got on the train, it began to leave.

I was nervously anticipating what would happen next. The train was about to leave, and I felt a mix of excitement and fear. I had to carefully write a letter to my father which read, 

I am going to Peking – in simple terms and sealed it to be delivered by my two friends.

I handed them a ten-won bill for lunch and train ticket expenses and asked them to deliver the letter once they reached Suwon to prevent any potential trouble.

Upon arrival at Suwon station, I parted ways with them and reached Busan in that night. However, an unfortunate stroke of fate awaited me as a fugitive: due to stormy weather, the ferry boats were not departing. With no options remaining, I spent the night at a Japanese inn with two Japanese boys and a middle-aged gentleman.

The next day too, due to the storm, the ferry was delayed. I pondered whether I had bought the tickets uselessly and thought about how I might have to return to Busan. 

With a fortuitous turn, the junior Japanese boy, who happened to be in a way drawing the attention of the Busan Postmaster, mediated and persuaded him to buy me a second-class ticket. I was immensely grateful and showed my appreciation countless times as I received the ticket.

Alongside that boy and the accompanying middle-aged gentleman, I boarded the second-class cabin. The wait until the ship departed felt unbearably long, wrapped in anxiety until the moment I finally relaxed upon its departure.

About an hour after leaving Busan, I noticed a group of unsavory-looking faces searching the deck. They eyed me closely, then disappeared only to return shortly with two men who stared menacingly at me, whispering among themselves. 

『Surely, I’ve been caught. What now?』

My once calm heart began to race again, trembling in fear. The thought of my studies in Tokyo being cut short and the prospect of being treated like a criminal in front of the police filled me with a greater concern than facing the consequences of a theft charge.

Eventually, the two men approached, asking for my address, name, and reasons for traveling. I answered respectfully to the barrage of questions. It seemed they also realized their mistake and left after a good long look at me. 

「That will do; we’ll leave him be for now.」 I thought to myself as I feared my time was limited.

However, minutes turned into hours with no sign of return. 

『If they decide to arrest me, I’ll surely be trapped like a rat in a cage out here in the middle of the ocean, what should I do?』—this thought tormented me.

Lo and behold, as the ship approached Hakata, a crewmember called out, 

『Mr. Kim, could you please come over?』

I replied casually, but inwardly, I felt dread and anxiety.

Passengers were disembarking, packing their belongings, and tidying up while I hastily changed into my student uniform, removed my cap, and made my way to the dock. 

Fortunately, I hadn’t been searched for the missing item, and during questioning, if a body search occurred, there was bound to be trouble. Imagining the potential outcomes where they might inquire about the substantial 500 won in my pocket horrified me. 

In no way was I prepared to meet further questions from the police. 

So, if that moment arose, I had resolved to jump overboard, blending in with the crowd of passengers. Fortunately, the detective at the dock did not manage to identify me.

When I finally boarded the express train to Tokyo that night, I took a breath of relief. However, beyond all the hurdles faced, the ensuing feelings weighed heavily—a sense of loneliness as a traveler in an unfamiliar land, especially since it was my first time living away from home. It left me feeling heavier in my soul. -(End)- <34>

**Summary** 

This memoir recounts the author's efforts to escape from parental constraints and pursue an education abroad, ultimately detailing the journey from Korea to Japan. The tale highlights the struggles against familial obligations and the anxiety of being a fugitive in a foreign land. For historians, the text offers insight into youth experiences during the colonial period in Korea and the lengths to which individuals would go for education and autonomy, illuminating the personal dimensions of broader historical movements in the era.

『내 밥은 짓지 마시우. 생일 밥 먹으로 가우.』하고는 취운정으로 뛰어갓다. 세 친구가 다시 맛나 다시 몃 마듸 이야기를 하고는 함께 파고다 공원으로 내려 왓다. 일각이 여삼추란 이런 때 두고 한 말인지 아홉시에 문을 여는 은행시간이 엇지 그리 기던지 공원 뻰취에서 기다리랴니 마음이 여간 조마조마해서 못 견딀 지경이 아니다.
거운 아홉시가 되어서 우리는 종로 네거리로 갓다. 가방가가에서 미리 손가방 하나를 금하야 마처두고 은행으로 갓다. 문을 열려 잇섯다.
통장과 지불용지를 데미럿다. 가슴의 뚝딱어림과 얼골의 상기되는 것을 간신이 억제하고 기다렷다. 조곰 잇다가 행원이 일홈을 부르더니 시퍼런 10원, 5원 딱지를 한참 세다가 통장과 아울러 내여준다. 나는 미처 세여 볼 용기도 업섯다. 어려니 잘 세여 주엇슬까 하는 생각도 잇섯지만 다시 세느라고 그 자리에서 어물어물하고 잇슬 수가 업섯든 것이다. 최후의 관문(關門)을 무난히 통과한 나는 은행을 나서서 곳 가방을 찻고 다시 양복점에서 우와기(上衣)한 벌을 사서 입엇다. 그리고 모자점에서 캡 하나를 또 삿다. 이 따위 것은 동경 가서도 살 것이지만 만일에 수색 청원을 하는 때를 두려워하야 미리 변장을 하자는 것이엇다.
정거장으로 달려가 보니 경부선 특급이 막 떠나랴는 판이엇다. 헝겁지겁 부산행 한 장 수원행 두 장, 급행권 석 장을 사 가지고 막 떠나는 렬차에 뛰어 올르다 십히하야 탓다.
자리잡은 우리 셋은 겨우 정신을 진정시켯다. 나는 곳 편지 한 장을 아버지께 썻다.
저는 북경(北京)으로 갑니다- 하는 뜻을 간단히 써서 봉하여 가지고 두 동무에게 부탁하엿다.
『수원서 내리거든 바로 개성으로 가서 이 편지를 붓치게 그래야＜33＞ 혹시 일이 잇더라도 경부선 방면에는 손이 미치지를 안을테니-』
그러고는 10원 한 장을 끄내서 점심요기, 찻 삭 비용으로 던저 주엇다.
수원역에서 두 동무를 작별한 나는 그 날 밤 부산부두에 도착하엿다. 그러나 불상한 도망군인 나에게는 또 한가지 불의의 장애가 생겻다. 그것은 풍랑 때문에 련락선이 아츰부터 못 떠나고 잇다는 것이다. 할 수 업시 차ㅅ간에서 안, 두 일본 사람 소년과 중년신사와 함께 일본 려관으로 가서 하로밤을 숙박하엿섯다.
그 이튼날도 풍랑 때문에 배가 떠나네 못 떠나네 하엿다. 엇잿든 표가 사두리라 하고 뽀-이에게 3등선 권을 사려 보내니 어제 아츰부터 밀린 승객 때문에 표를 안 판다는 것이엇다.
『꼼작업시 부산서 붓들려 되도라 가는가보다.』하는 기맥힌 생각을 하며 두 일본사람을 따라 부두로 나갓다.
부두에는 어제 아츰부터 밀린 승객으로 삑빡 하엿섯다. 개찰구로 쪼차 가보니 과연 표를 팔지 안엇섯다.
『엇잘까?』 하고 쩔쩔매는 판에 천만 의외로 일본 그 소년을 전송나온 부산 우편국장 이라는 사람이 주선을 하야 2등 표 한 장을 사다주는 것이엇다.
고마운 생각해서는 코가 땅에 닷게 백배 사레를 해도 시원찬을 만하엿섯다.
나는 그 소년과 그에 따른 중년 신사와 함께 2등 선실에 몸을 실엇다. 배 떠나기까지가 엇지 그리 지리한 지 그때까지도 마음이 불안하다가 배가 겨우 떠나게 되자 다리를 뻣고 마음을 노앗다.
배가 부산을 떠난 지 한시간 가량되여서다. 갑판 쪽에서 한 사람의 험상구진 얼골이 창안을 엿보고 서 잇섯다. 그리고는 나를 유심이 보드니 잠시 사라젓다가 또다시 두 사람의 머리가 나를 겨누고 바라보며 숙닥숙닥 하엿다.
『기여코 잡혓구나. 이를 어쩌나.』
평온하든 나의 가슴은 또다시 혼란하기 시작하며 부들부들 떨리기까지 하엿다. 동경 류학이 깨지는 것은 고사하고 도적놈 모양으로 형사에게 묵겨갈 일을 생각하니 창피 당할 일이 더 큰 걱정이엇다.
기어코 두 사람이 드러와서 주소, 성명 무슨 목적으로 가느냐 등 뭇는대로 례사로 대답햇다. 다 듯고 난 형사는 실례햇다는 말 한 마듸 하고 다시 한번 나를 유심히 보고 가버렷다. 「이만하고 조회하려 가나부다.」 하얏드니 몃 시간이 지나도 다시 오지 안엇다. 『올치! 바다 한 가운대라 독 안에 든 쥐 모양이 되엿스니까 가만히 두엇다가 하관(下關)서 붓 잡을 모양이로구나.』 하는 생각이 낫섯다.
아니나 다를까 배가 하관에 다 을림시 하야서 뽀-이가 오드니
『긴상 조곰 잇다가 저리로 좀 오시랩니다.』
『그러지요.』
시원스럽게 대답은 햇스나 입맛이 쓰고 정신이 앗질하엿다.
하관착 선객들이 이러나서 려장을 정리하며 묵그며 하는 동안에 나는 오리에리를 버서 부치고 학생복으로 가라입엇다. 그러고 모자도 박구어 썻다.
다행히 집에서 수색 청원을 아니하엿더라도 형사에게 문초를 바들 때 몸수색이나 하는 날에는 경이다. 왜 그러냐 하면 1개 소년의 포케트 속에는 5백 원이란 돈이 잇지 안느냐 어데서 난 돈이냐 고향으로 조회해 봐야 한다 하는 대목이면 콩밥 먹을 일은 업지만 곱다라케 경성으로 도라 우편 하는 판이다.
엇잿든 다시 형사를 맛날 수는 업섯다. 그래서 만일의 경우에는 배우에서 뛰여나릴 각오까지 하면서 여러 선객들 틈에 끼어 다리를 옹그리다십히 하야 기선을 용케 나렷다. 형사가 선가(船架)에 서 잇서서 나리는 사람을 일일이 살펴보고 잇섯스나 변장한 나를 발견하지 못한 모양이엇다.
그날 밤 동경 행 특급을 집어 탓슬 때 나는 비로소 숨을 돌리컷다. 그러나 모든 난관이 끗나고 다음에 도라오는 것은 누구에게나 잇는 쓸쓸한 나그네의 심정 그것이엇다. 그러고 생전 처음으로 객지생활을 하게 된 나에게는 한층 더 마음속이 구술푼 것 가탯다. -(끗)-＜34＞
