人生의 半고개 三十四十 고개 넘든 感想 諸氏 

書劍無端四十年. 

중동학교장 崔奎東 

What is my age? In terms of Joseon, I am forty-seven years old, or rather since my birthday is December 30, it is now forty-six. 

I don't have any particular thoughts; that said, it’s not that I have none but rather that they are not particularly impressive. In the year I turned forty, reflecting on the thirty-nine years that have passed, I wonder what I have accomplished during those thirty-nine years. In this state, I feel that I have achieved nothing at all. Having this thought led me to write this piece. 

"書劍無端四十年. 絲靑雪白朝仍暮. 伶俜忍送恨多年. 花似人非歲又年." 

To put it simply, I have merely passed time passively. I haven't written (書) anything nor have I accomplished anything noteworthy, having only wasted years filled with grievances. If I were to reflect upon my life, it would lead to regrets and, as for the time wasted, there is nothing to show for it. It is a perfectly ordinary feeling, I suppose, perhaps if I had another thought or reflection from six or seven years ago, I might have shared it. 

As for my thoughts on the education sector at that time? I can't say I have no reflections at all, but due to my memory, I cannot recall it well. I wrote something like: 

"伽南乳蜜君林道. 吾們從玆四十年."

This was reminiscent of the Old Testament where Moses longed for the happiness represented by the land of milk and honey, leading the Israelite people after wandering for forty years in the desert. It seems to me there is nothing special about it.

 

不知老之將至 

동덕여고장 조동식 

As we welcome the New Year, what would our reflections be? It’s always the same phrase and thought: I’m now forty-two. 

Rather than reflections on turning forty, as a member of the educational community, I must speak about this school. Over the past fifteen to sixteen years, like a flower blooming in the crevices of rocks, the winds have blown and I feared it might fall at any time. But through the support of everyone in society and our relentless efforts, in April of Taisho 15 (1926), our school was elevated, and in September of the same year, it was established as a legal foundation, and now the total number of students across both schools has reached seven hundred. 

Rather than mulling over my reflections, I feel reassured that our work has not regressed but progressed, and that it will proceed even more thoroughly with everyone’s support. I apologize for not being able to share other good words in response to this. 

 

사십까지는 勇氣而已  

徽文학교장 이윤주  

I entered the education sector at the age of twenty-six, but my life until now has been disorganized and somewhat indulgent. Until recently, I spent my time solely at school and had little interaction with the real world, so I hardly had any feelings about what I experienced or those of turning thirty or forty. 

When crossing these peaks, rather than having reflections, I had the ambition to move forward until thirty or forty, yet at present, such ambition seems to have diminished. Recently, though, I start to think deeply about both the beginning and the end of what I wish to accomplish, otherwise I feel I am just drifting along. 

However, since I am in charge of educating young men, I am continuously aware of my responsibility toward them. I am acutely aware that society is still underdeveloped. Why? Because I carry the heavy responsibility of educating the youth, who have grand aspirations and broad visions for the future, yet I feel utterly incapable of guiding them. Even more so, I see that the general public does not criticize my inadequacy but silently allows me to remain, feeling an indescribable pressure when interacting with colleagues or students, as well as with society, filled with a sense of unease. 

Thus, I strive to advance along with the new trends, yet no matter how hard I run, I feel that like a tortoise, I am ultimately left behind. In discussing this, if I think excessively about the past, I can't help but feel I am aging toward failure. 

Consequently, I feel any thought must focus less on the past and more on the future, otherwise there will be no progress or advancement. But it appears I'm more inclined to ponder the past. This isn't unique to me but probably reflects a general truth about moving toward old age! 

As for educational policies? Nowadays, I hear remarks that a teacher must be an authoritative figure while students follow his directives without question. Back when I first took up the teaching role, if students had displeased me, I could, after all, chastise them. It was taken for granted. 

But such an approach is no longer possible today. Initially, we must express to students ideologically: "Your thoughts may differ from mine, but if my thoughts are correct, then follow accordingly." One must inform them this way and implant it into their minds, but having a solid base of educators is critical, and if the young teachers only provide direction without understanding the complete picture, it could put us at a severe disadvantage. Thus, in our school, aside from myself, there are a few older teachers over forty or fifty, while a significant number are under thirty, filled with innovation and enthusiasm to advance. 

And looking at our society, some aspects have advanced, yet we cannot be content with it; if we do not work ten or a hundred times harder than others, we could find ourselves lost. This applies not only to education but to every endeavor; my continuous thought has always been to strive for greater progress. 

 

마혼에 매지근  

益世報社 서세충  

Everyone is similar, but before thirty, one has no fear, and regardless of the difficulties or hardships one faces, there is a determined drive to charge ahead without reservation. However, once past thirty, one begins to hesitate, and courage tends to diminish. 

Before thirty, days seem long and stretched; however, from thirty to forty, they seem to rush by. 

As I reflect on this time, with the turning of thirty, my insight differed little from before. The view appeared bright and the roads opened wide, making it seem that whatever I desired would surely materialize, but looking back now makes me see the twisting paths I took without much resolve.

Confucius said, "At thirty, I stood firm, and at forty, I was no longer confused," so I thought from now on I would establish intentions and efforts to succeed in all endeavors. Yet again, I arrive at the peak of forty, and while revisiting the ten years gone by, I realize much of it was spent fruitlessly. 

In the age of clarity that is 40, it feels like my responsibilities have not been fully upheld, leading to regrets. Yet this New Year, I resolve to open my eyes wide, shaking off all futile regrets and discarding unneeded pessimism. I intend to make something meaningful of my remaining time, even if it is not monumental… 

 

감회의 감상 무량  

啓明俱樂部 임규  

Throughout my life, up until now, I have never given thought to myself. Having lived for over sixty years, I did not truly reflect on my feelings at forty or fifty, or take note of them. However, when I am crossing this peak, I feel that before venturing forth, I need to recount my path. 

I left my rural home at eighteen, and do not think I did so out of lack of food; we were doing well back home. However, there was a lingering discontent in my heart, pondering, "Why is the world this unfair?" 

The merciless exploitation of the wealthy over the poor, the harsh governance against the people—everything seemed unjust, leaving an impression on my undeveloped thoughts. "Ah, if only I could have thousands of sacks of rice, I would save those crying people stuck in harsh conditions." But it all amounted to an unrealistic dream, an idle thought. 

With resignation, I fled to Seoul without ever having a grand ambition or a good opportunity to realize that thought, suffering her burdens along the way. 

In the many years of wandering, I cannot deny the hardships faced. However, looking back on my more than sixty years passed, if asked about my purpose, I can only say, "I have endured much, but what have I accomplished?" 

Thus, even while I have failed to attain significant achievements, I remind myself of the importance of us Koreans uniting and working together toward practical progress. 

In dealing with others, I must be aware of my flaws and not envy those who have fared better than me; by supporting one another and lifting one another, we can achieve something worthwhile with our own hands. This has been my consistent thought since the year of previous hardships. 

 

嗚呼歲不我廷  

조선일보사 문일평  

Once I have crossed the peak, nothing particularly changes in one’s thoughts; however, upon reflecting on the past, until I hit thirty, I leaped into challenges with utmost confidence, much like a child. 

As I crossed thirty, I noticed the scenario changed; I began to hesitate, analyzing various matters more critically, pondering each step of the way. 

At thirty, I took it as a point to establish my purpose and ensure I can accomplish anything. As the years pass, I see the winding paths I have taken make me stall as I uncertainly crossed rocky roads. 

Confucius would say at thirty I had become grounded in my path, and as I approached forty, I would be free of doubt. Yet, each decade I reconsider the turbulent stretch of years gone by brings me both sorrow and disappointment. 

Now, as I enter this fortieth year, I sense that day to day responsibilities weigh heavily on me. Half of my life has passed in this manner, filling me with a sense of inevitable despair. However, this New Year, I intend to awaken my spirit and lift my eyes, discarding useless despair and resolving to make meaningful progress with my remaining years, however trivial they may be…" 

 

**Summary**  

This article features a series of reflections from various authors on the experiences of reaching the ages of thirty and forty, encapsulating their thoughts on life, education, and personal reflections on growth and responsibilities. Each piece conveys a mixture of regret, reflection, and a desire to strive for greater achievements despite the passage of time. Historians might find the collective sentiments particularly interesting, as they reveal the tension between personal aspirations and societal expectations, reflecting the broader context of an educated elite in colonial Korea grappling with their identity and responsibilities under Japanese rule.
