기사제목	大京城 狂舞曲 (The Great Capital's Wild Dance)  
필자	双S生 (Double S Life)  
기사형태	세태비평 (Social Criticism)  

大京城 狂舞曲  
双S生  
1. I, who have put on my hat, am standing on the street in the middle of a bustling day,  
Though my ears are deafened by the dance of the Great Capital,  
I do not feel the strain of nervous breakdown.  
Wild dance, wild dance.  

2. The lady clerk, with her hat on,  
Ignoring all the customers in the shop,  
Goes around, squeezing out the customers,  
In order to have double the tips from the young ladies.  
Wild dance, wild dance.  

3. The modern educated youth, with their hats on,  
Even if their hair is hot and tangled,  
They know all about American actresses and German beauties,  
And modern young men have memorized their addresses well.  
Wild dance, wild dance.  

"Is that okay? Who made such a song?"  
"Whoever made it has lived in the eastern regions."

Beep beep, beep beep, as the dust rises,  
Through the muddy streets, the automobile with courtesans passes by.  
Crash crash, the bicycle loses its mind,  
And the rickshaw is bumping.  
Clack clack, clack, "Oh, my dear, let me down!"  
Slippery slippery, let’s go, this is "a rickshaw."  
Ding-a-ling, suddenly a bicycle hits a child and falls down.  
Ding ding, "Oh, help me down!"  
Hurry hurry, this is "a man."  
Walking and walking, "high school student."  
Click, click, click, "the sound of modern youth's home."  
Excuse me, where do I go to get to Jongno?

Every day from morning until midnight,  
Amid this noisy life, those traffic cops,  
Who, despite the chaos, do not suffer from nervous breakdowns,  
Are indeed very healthy. If unfortunate, they are in a very sad state.  
"So even if your ears are stuffed from the wild dance of the Great Capital,  
That’s fine, but if the wild dance remains only there, it could be alright; however,  
If a hundred ghosts come out at night,  
That could be a big problem too."  
"Wait a moment; let’s go to Jongno,  
To see the wild dance of the Great Capital!"

Jongno Street

It looks bright and lonely,  
But from the inner street leading to Changdeokgung,  
The road, freshly groomed, sparkles under the sunlight,  
And it is as clean as a rural train station's courtyard.  
Along that clean road, a still-heavy girl, removed from reality,  
Not resembling a bee, sways back and forth, exhausted from her dance.  
With a jagged bob cut on one side,  
Dressed in a short jacket and red scarf,  
With white powder on her neck,  
In her "la pal pants," I think,  
"I'm dressed like a modern girl of the early century."  
"La pal pants?"  
"Look at that; it’ll surely reveal more than it conceals."  
"That’s no modern girl’s proud fashion."  
"Hmph, with that modern look,  
Where are you intending to go in such a state?"  
"To a café, to the Han River, to the theater."  
"And also?"  
"Yes, a girl’s boarding house."  

Just as the heavy girl pauses to catch her breath,  
There is a young bookkeeper at the bookstore, bustling.  
"What do you mean you don't have 2 won?"  
"I have to go tomorrow to make some money."  
"Well then, can’t you just borrow from a store with 2 won left?"  
"Will it do to just take the leftover money from others?"  
"They will pay you back tomorrow."  
"They said they would pay tomorrow once I went."  
"Have you gone and come back?"  

"Ah, it’s been a while already; they’re all coming back now."  
"Coming back from where?"  
"No, really, there were 300 girls from XX girls’ school who went on a field trip to Cheongnyangni.  
So I went to get a picture, but I can't go now because I don’t have 2 won!"  
"Are you going after any girl at someone’s house?"  
"Eh, you have a long tongue, but I can’t say anything about that."  

Amid the daily music sounds of Seoul, the sounds of musicians like these  
How many of them are packed in, sending their career tools to the barbershop or spending  
2 won following the girls from school on an excursion.

At an ice shop on the second floor of Andong Street,  
Every day from this noisy recorder,  
Trying to take a bus for the first time  
Waiting at the stop, unable to bear it, went to a police station and asked,  
"Do you not work on gold bricks heading down this way?"  
"Only the ones running all day in the evening during office hours."  
"How presumptuous for someone from the village to expect such a benefit."  
As I walked down the road, touching my back to my wig,  
I thought about what the police officer was thinking and laughed widely.  
"What is that, is it a flour sack with four legs (four-footed)?"  
"A flour sack is a masterpiece! The outfit is just like your saying,  
This is a flour sack,"  
"The virtue of Korean women is admirable; if their legs are long,  
That’s fine, but the length of the dress is a bit odd for Korean women.  
With long dresses and short jackets,  
They mistakenly believe that  
Wearing Western clothes is good just because it looks good and fails to realize it;  
With their coats trailing behind them, they are dragging."  

"Did you buy that with your own money?"  
"With borrowed money."  
"Then your parents bought it for you."  
"The husband used to buy it with his own money."  
"Then wearing that, the woman is more wasteful than her husband himself."  
"The husband’s waste does not matter; the woman wears it boldly."  

The bold girl laughed as whispers arose in the back;  
With their heads shaking, dressed in modern styles, two young men passed by.  
They seemed quite delighted to shake hands, cheerfully greeting one another,  
Then pass by nonchalantly saying, "Where to?"  
Male friends didn’t answer anything but kept walking,  
And then turning back asked again about modern gear and gestures,  
Stretching their fingers awkwardly, they laughed, acting like children,  
While one boy said, "Too frivolous."  
Another remarked, "Can I also borrow?"  
Not knowing what their fingers were pointing to, nor observing the buzzing girl running nearby,  
"Ah, she’s a flour merchant," one passed by remarked.  
What does it mean to be a flour merchant?  
If the girl runs around here in flour sacks,  
It certainly must be a merry mess.  

In Jongno Square, at the center of Seoul,  
It is livelier than a battlefield, with the wild dance's percussion resonating.  
Cars and carriages pass by,  
Amid this, bicycles and rickshaws rush by;  
The heavy carts can be seen bustling back and forth,  
While in corners lie opium dens like aged spiders.  
Next to the two arguing are boys fighting their fathers and coughing heavily in rickshaws.  
All the while, the sounds of various voices combine into the great cacophony of the wild dance,  
For those unable to maintain their wits in this city they call home,  
Are they bringing and carrying all manner of life?  
"Excuse me!" A woman’s voice interrupts my thoughts!  
Turning around, I see a dark-haired woman with sun-kissed skin selling newspapers.  
"Would you like to buy a copy of our newspaper?"  
"Is it the Dong-A Ilbo or the Jungoe Ilbo?"  
"No, it’s not a worldly paper; it’s the newspaper of our savior,  
Jesus Christ, simply noting the joyous marriage."  

During the conversation, more than four or five onlookers gathered,  
The lady with the papers also came, standing there.  
"Such good words are plentiful; you surely have some nice books;  
Please sell just a few copies."  
"Don’t say such things; you’ll be grateful to have some readers."  
"Then both of you, please sell just one copy each."  

In the commotion of onlookers reflecting their excitement, someone called,  
"Look, selling the words of Jesus for pennies to survive is that how they do it?"  
Saying that, the men, like children, started laughing.  
"It is not for selling the words of Jesus but the income is used for social work."  
"Is it to live off of running away from the countryside and wearing shoes to make a living,  
That is social work?"  
The expressions of disdain echoed through the crowd.  
"Not selling; if I don’t sell, don’t talk to me," said my sister,  
Surprisingly enthusiastic for a person who might have laughed off lesser things swiftly.  
"My dear, in such a tumultuous world,  
Shouldn’t such a woman who leaves her home and works on the street know better?"  
"They think it is splendid, but did you sell your honor by passing such judgments?"  
"When one sells their character, they surely become restless;  
People who provide their skin are not won over."  

But then one of the soldiers rises again to sell more copies:  
"But the transactions do affect some."   

They are getting along fine, selling one of their copies,  
Having served their two pennies, they felt satisfied and returned.  
While one of the soldiers took it lightly, the other points out the facts.  

The article strives to show us the chaos of Seoul in the 1920s,  
Portraying the overwhelming noise and activity of urban life,  
Where social differences and struggles are revealed through the lenses of the people,  
And how ideals clash with reality in a growing city.  
This quote serves as a rich historical element,  
Showing a picture of early Korean urbanization under colonial rule,  
Documenting the transformation and the affects on social dynamics—  
Something historians may seek from these cultural criticisms.  

**Summary**  

This article paints a vivid picture of urban life and social dynamics in Seoul during the 1920s, reflecting on the chaos and the struggles of city life. It highlights the contrasts between modernity and tradition, showcasing the behaviors and fashions of youths while commenting on poverty and the economic pressures of society. 

Historians might find the portrayal of social interactions insightful, especially how the colonial context impacts economic survival strategies and cultural shifts. The mention of specific social activities provides a lens into the daily lives of people, their behaviors and struggles in the urban environment under colonial rule.
