The Feelings of an Old Bachelor, a Heartbreaking Confession  
馬面生  
This year I am two years old, yet I am still a bachelor who is aging. The marks of a child in my belly mean, if one were to decide, that I am not merely a bachelor similar to a ginger merchant or a beggar, and it is impossible to say that I should have lived until now as a widower. Even if one denies it or ridicules me, I cannot deny that I am an incredibly old bachelor who has no one to speak with. 

However, everyone is busy making a living, fighting in this world where people are driven by their own desires. How can an old bachelor, whose heart is heavy, expect someone to understand his lonely pleas and the wishes that accompany him? On long winter nights, it is unbearable, unable to fall asleep, and it is my only solace to sigh deeply on a lonely pillow. I would rather share my soul’s grieves and plead for compassion than leave them unspoken. 

I am not a person who is deformed. I do not have a crooked nose or a disfigured face, nor am I a disabled figure with swollen legs, nor am I a ginger merchant. To speak about appearance, though I cannot be counted among handsome men, when I look at my own reflection in the mirror, I seem to be a fairly decent-looking person. I might have been indifferent to women or perhaps it is that I do not know the secret of love, or maybe my ideals regarding women are too elevated. Whatever the case may be, it is a fact that I have lived until today as a traveler in a hotel alone. 

Thus, although I have not experienced the pressure of life or any other disability, the only longing that overwhelms me with a sorrowful heart comes from women. Yet, there is no specific woman that I can refer to. I am unable to be satisfied with the lukewarm love of the same sex. I am akin to a child trembling in the cold streets when the snow falls, yearning to offer a warm coat to that child. But all the while, I am left shivering, wandering aimlessly in search of warmth. When I meet a friend whom I cherish, whether it is through drinking or otherwise, I will wait for hours, unable to sleep, sending each friend home until the early hours. After wandering the streets alone until midnight, I often return to the hotel, where with what dim light from a flickering lamp, I may find the chilly winds giving me a sense of desolation. 

I know that those who know the life I live are aware of it, and over the years, there have been those who have urged me not to feel this way, yet for some reason, their admonishments do not comfort me but rather bring forth a sense of misery that pulls me deeper into unhappiness. I laugh bitterly at the major issue of life, which is marriage. If a philosopher were to hear these thoughts, he might spit in contempt and return home, while I, with a melancholic heart, have struggled through countless attempts to calm my mind by wandering through the pines of Cheongyangri. 

At times, I have worked hard to suppress the carnal impulses within me. But if seeking the opposite sex is merely a desire driven by instinct and the pursuit of pleasure, then I have not thought that way until now. I do not seek a partner simply because I have been picky about who could be my wife. Wherever I may be, when the sight of a woman’s body comes into view, my heart feels a jolt akin to a lightning strike, and I find myself applying the principles of aesthetics to measure her against my ideals. Yet, beauty does not reach me in response. Thus, no matter what I resort to for consolation, I find myself equally unhappy. I dread the idea of marriage as being a significant bond that one must take upon as a human being, and I do not think of merely growing old in this way. 

Even as I picture the woman I long for in my mind, I yearn for a singular being in this world that I could truly love. Yet, I still do not understand what a woman truly is. Is the heart of a maiden akin to the tongue of a viper, or is it as convoluted as a hen’s intricate heart? However, I have no means to comprehend, nor does anyone come forth to enlighten me. Thus, I find myself helplessly living as an unfortunate old bachelor.

**Summary**  

The article expresses the melancholic reflections of an aging bachelor who yearns for companionship and grapples with societal expectations regarding marriage and love. He feels out of place amidst a society that prioritizes survival and material gain, feeling deep sorrow over his loneliness and lack of connection with women. The piece represents a poignant introspection into the struggles of men in early 20th-century Korea, revealing themes of isolation and emotional vulnerability that resonate with the complexities of colonial life. Historians might find the examination of personal relationships and societal pressures during this period worthy of interest, showcasing how traditional views on marriage and masculinity were being challenged amid shifting cultural landscapes.
