기사제목	新生活을 하야본 實驗 (Experiment for New Life)  
저자	朴勝彬 (Park Seung-bin)  
기사형태	설문 (Survey)  

Experiment for New Life  

Every Thursday is a vegetarian day; our home is simple and frugal.

Our household does not particularly embody what one might call a new life. As you can see, our house is built in a Western style, but in all other respects, we focus on simplicity and frugality. When we talk about the so-called modern lifestyle, it typically implies that the men and women of a household adhere to trends and fads in dress, food, shelter, and behavior. However, I fundamentally reject a lifestyle of superficial vanity that caters to contemporary fashions. Our family rules have always emphasized thrift and frugality, which is why our way of life is generally simpler than that of others.

Until now, none of my family members have worn luxurious silk clothing, and even in summer, we do not wear any fabrics that are overly luxurious or impressive. Instead, we use durable materials such as Chinese silk, which is resilient and less prone to wear. As for food, even when compared to families with similar incomes, our meals are simple. My approach is not merely about saving money; it is also about adhering to our family traditions.

You might wonder why we have built a Western-style house despite all this. Compared to the traditional houses my ancestors lived in, it seems quite extravagant, and I feel guilty about that. However, for some reason, I have always wanted to build a nice house since childhood. I always seek to admire well-constructed houses of others, and I have a longing for a better living space.

Recently, I have recognized that if we do not establish good housing systems, it will be challenging to achieve family harmony and mutual respect (for more detailed views, see the section on lifestyle improvements). When I lived in Cheongjin-dong, I resided in a traditional Korean-style house and have now built this house in what can be referred to as a Western style. This represents my ideals and reflects my philosophy.

The spaces for living—our bedroom and the children's rooms—are entirely unembellished, while the dining area and study are shared among the family. While homes in general allow such arrangements, there is little else of particular distinction. 

I have declared Thursday as a vegetarian day, which means we strictly avoid eating meat on that day. This might sound ironic, as there are many people who, having eaten meat every day, find it difficult to abstain for just one day, but my intention is to encourage families that consume meat to develop vegetarian habits and promote a frugal lifestyle.


Executing Household Accounts  
梁柱三 (Yang Joo-san)  

Well, as for my life, does it differ much from others? I wear a Western suit, live in a modest house, and sometimes eat well, with others saying I lead a so-called new life. It's difficult to speak on this topic, as a slight misstep could lead to complaints against me. 

Last summer, at our church, I was asked to speak about lifestyle improvements. I hesitated a bit, as discussing living improvements is complex. Some of the young people ridiculed my perceived efforts to improve living conditions by following the latest trends, criticizing it by saying, "I'm not fitting into these trends. 

First, eating Western boiled rice and living in Western houses and clothes is not what we should be aiming for," which I found contradictory coming from someone wearing Western clothes and eating at Western cafes during lunchtime. 

If a person's income is 100 won a month, they might say we should live a little better than our current house conditions, yet still only with that same income.

I have always tried to improve our living space as much as possible. As for our house, it doesn't fit strictly into either Western or traditional Korean styles—it represents a hybrid of both. The main room and guest room utilize traditional Korean ondol heating, while the upper floor has a Western layout. 

Given that traditional Korean kitchens are less equipped, we renovated our kitchen, avoiding public display and ensuring economic heating through a new stove, using coal as fuel instead of wood, which is far more economical than the latter. 

As for meals, traditional Korean sayings often state that one has porridge for breakfast and rice for dinner, but I have flipped this idea on its head, having porridge for breakfast, such as barley or rice porridge, with an egg and a cup of tea, and then enjoying a couple of side dishes for lunch and dinner. 

From an outsider's perspective, one might think I luxuriously indulge in Western meals daily, but when you calculate it, living in a traditional Korean way often costs more. Additionally, my family eats dinner together at a set time, enjoying meals around the same table, sharing dishes. 

Our method allows us to avoid waste, as we can naturally polish off leftovers, passing them to each other, which my older mother was initially not happy about but has since seen the benefits.

When it comes to clothing, neither children nor adults wear white clothing. Wearing white has been seen as impractical and unsightly from any angle. 

Among other practices, I keep a household ledger. Our Korean people generally do not keep track of finances as well as folks from other countries. Household expenses are often left unrecorded, and one wonders how one can manage household finance effectively without recordkeeping. 

Since I married in 1915 and started family life, I have maintained a ledger consistently for 13 years without missing a single day. I have recorded even small purchases down to the last penny. I believe that anyone who starts this practice will find it beneficial.

A Couple's Perspective  
崔承一 (Choi Seung-il)  

While one person may deny reality, living in compromise and submission, eating good rice alongside lesser quality rice and wearing shabby Western clothing, they consider the idea of a new life nothing more than an empty illusion.

Thus, at times, I feel suffocated, proclaiming, "I am now dead", brimming with helpless anger.

To briefly summarize my life—typically, I arise at eight in the morning, have breakfast at ten, and leave the house only when necessary, but recently even that has become uncomfortable. I tend to lounge across the floor, reading volumes of my printed works, pondering sleep, and at around five in the afternoon, I eat dinner, both of us strolling about the hill in Jeongdong— a contented lifestyle, one could say. 

However, living this way has been termed "new life." To me, that’s an uncomfortable and disgusting suggestion. 

Human desires are seemingly limitless. Observing how two individuals walk together on a nearby road, a friend insists, "Let’s take a photograph!" but I see the absurdity ahead of me, someone engaged in self-interest. 

I long for a life where I can work in line with my appropriate abilities, creating my own profession, leading to effective actions against the realities I face.

If I express my selfish desires, these two points are my greatest hopes, which, consequently, translate into my incessant pain. 

Reflect, if you will: when we go out around five in the evening and step outside the gate of a humble house, I can tell our fellow townspeople glance at us with curiosity mixed with envy.

However, while observing their wooden houses, traditional Japanese-style houses, and well-constructed Western homes, I am left pondering how different our lives truly are. I cannot consider such a contrast to be a new life. 

Therefore, the real question lies here. In this vast uneven existence, even if one individual experiences some comfort and advantage, navigating through compromises, one cannot define that as a new life.


Our Different Family  
秦淑鳳 (Jin Suk-bong)  

Anyone who visits our house for the first time often exclaims, "Oh! You people truly live in a thoroughly modern blending of Western and traditional Korean styles!"

In truth, if I were to recount life with my children, showcasing the delights of our living experience could resemble children bragging about toys. 

Living in a way that is slightly different from others, our brick house stands out amidst the traditional structures. 

We have a large garden, adorned with nice red bricks and a neatly rusty gate. The beauty of our modest home breaks through, like gemstones glittering around the opal, epitomizing "cultural housing and a modern lifestyle."

The building itself is no wonder of a Korean design, yet the property is set amidst spacious yards, with varying gardens of flowers and vegetable patches. 

Though people say it smells of "bad luck," I believe it is essential to employ a bit of beauty by borrowing some elements from foreign designs. 

After having all the walls decorated in pleasant light colors with streamlined patterns, the rooms feel cheerfully furnished. We have dedicated our front room exclusively for receiving guests, holding clean tablecloths of simple yet organized decor set amidst tasteful flowers and frames hanging on the wall—this seems notably different from typical homes.

However, our main room remains traditionally Korean. Even our basement is well-utilized for summer groceries, especially fruits and food preservation. 

Nonetheless, a significant downside remains my inability to create a proper bath, leading to inconveniences for the children. 

Yet, whatever it may be, my thoughts center around trying different things and improving little by little daily.

Life in Various Interests  
金珠玉 (Kim Ju-yuk)  

At Huangtohyeon, one must journey through narrow alleys to finally see our house. 

Is merely consuming meals three times a day and playing the piano every evening what constitutes real living? I don't think so.

Should I tell you about my living experiences, you would find distinctive differences compared to typical modern women. 

My household items are predominantly traditional Korean. Despite having a narrow garden, I have elegantly wrapped a round flowerbed, planting over a hundred varieties of flowers that flourish throughout.

In addition, I am raising chickens and find joy in keeping birds in cages as they sing melodiously amidst work—the pure pleasure of nurturing and harvesting brings multiple levels of happiness.

In other words, I am busy yet calm, spending peaceful days, truly a joyful state of existence. 

Nevertheless, others frequently jest, "Isn't Miss Ju-yuk a millionaire's daughter?" I merely smile at such remarks inwardly.


Playing the Role of a Famous Actor!  
宋仁媛 (Song In-won)  

The "Kwangsanghoe" sign that stands out on the street is a clear testament of my life's hustle. 

Each morning, I rise early to prepare breakfast, then rush out to buy groceries. 

Once I manage to feed my daughters, they rush off to school while I scatter about, pondering what to sell that day, organizing my purchase—what can I carry with ease and what needs my assistance in carrying back home to ensure my children make it to school on time.

When I first started, I envisioned a bigger, sturdier way of doing business, but for the first two years, I saw good profits, even hiring delivery workers.

However, things changed due to rising costs, making it impossible to keep up, and now I am forced to brainstorm various methods. 

Moreover, I opened a shaved ice shop during the summer and a sundry store during winter, keeping busy with little time to manage the house. 

But I do not manage everything alone. The sheer volume of household responsibilities is overwhelming, so my children assist with attending to various chores. 

When a child complains, "Mom, my hands are too sticky because of playing the piano," I can't help but lecture, "What nonsense! Is it appropriate for a Korean with this situation to expose hands to a grand piano?” 

Balancing this daily cycle requires effort, leading to challenges with energy and patience, yet observing my children's growth and education renews my motivation, even amid difficulties.

Ultimately, my household is a constantly shifting landscape of activities.

The Entire Family in a Guesthouse  
李鍾麟 (Lee Jong-lin)  

It has been six years since my entire family began living in a guesthouse, prompted by the housing shortages in Seoul. 

Back then, I had no place to call home, often moving two to three times a month. 

Having experienced this tumult, one can only imagine the hardships and pains that come from lacking even basic provisions. 

But you feel relieved if your home is comfortable amidst struggles; my wife, however, grew weary of our hardships, urging us to abandon our urban life and relocate to the countryside. 

Thus, I found myself resigning and leaving everything behind, albeit reluctantly, to seek out a place in the countryside instead of renting again. 

With impressions that guesthouse living is much easier, despite being more costly, we moved and have enjoyed it ever since.

After a year of staying in guesthouses, our understanding of the commotion around us has become clearer.

When calculating our monthly expenses, our family of four could barely sustain ourselves on about 100 won a month. 

If we rented a house in Seoul, it often drew unexpected guests. The situation could be a strain, with over one guest arriving daily, presenting the problem of providing meals. 

Adding to this was a myriad of taxes related to housing, making it unbearable—a myriad of endless worries pops up when looking after guests. 

Having spent around a year now in guesthouses, you can fathom the financial pressure under which we lived. 

Back when, our monthly spending would only total around 55 won for food for four people. On top of that came the taxes. With guests sporadically eating our meals, I would hardly have anything left for myself. 

It wasn't unusual to conclude each month worried about just making it through and preparing meals for the children before they went to school, which failed to instill diligence in the family. 

Due to being in a guesthouse, late at night the place tends to get rather noisy, as various customers come and go; if visitors come, they may drop by without prior notice. Thus, residing in a guesthouse has its drawbacks. 

Economically, it is quite convenient at lower costs in comparison with the prices back then; hence, I strongly encourage my fellow Koreans to consider guesthouse living as a viable option. 

A False Western Style Life Plan  
鄭一史 (Jeong Il-sa)  

When asked to share my thoughts on Western-style living, I must first clarify that some may misunderstand. 

Having lived abroad for about twenty years, one might assume I reject all that is Korean to adopt pure Western lifestyles, but that would not be the case.

Yet some perceive my adoption of elements from Western culture as a complete shift from traditional Korean living, but that's a misconception.  

About one or two years ago, a reporter visited my home with the intention of discussing my lifestyle, and when they reported it later, they misrepresented our conversation completely. 

I've come to realize that we need a reawakening with regard to this economy. 

While many claim that life feels constricting with rising costs, they often fail to notice how restaurants, markets, and other establishments prosper continuously.

More questioningly, if people resist becoming more frugal in living, perhaps driven by the desire to spend, they blindly eat out instead of preparing food at home, and partake in the negligence of quality family meals.  

This mindset breeds a culture where enjoying what could be easily brought home becomes a thoughtless habit, diluting the core value of living.

Summary  

In this article, various contributors describe their interpretations of a "new lifestyle" within the context of 1920s Korea, emphasizing simplicity, thrift, and familial bonds. Many express their struggle reconciling traditional values with modern influences and sensibilities, highlighting unique personal experiences that narrate the complexities of life during Japan's colonial rule in Korea. The perspectives reveal the broader societal shifts as the populace tried to navigate through Westernization and its impact on traditional Korean culture. Historians may find it compelling how these individuals both embraced and rejected Western elements while still trying to maintain their heritage, reflecting a broader struggle within society under colonial pressures.
