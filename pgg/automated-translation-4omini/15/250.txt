발행일	1928년 12월 01일  
기사제목	婦人記者暗行記, 村에서 逃亡온 여자로 꾸미고 女俳優募集에 試驗본 記, 女優選擇의 珍奇한 試驗 (A Record of a Female Reporter’s Covert Investigation: Disguised as a Runaway Woman from a Village and Trial for Recruiting Actresses, An Unusual Test for Selecting Actresses)  
필자	白시라 記  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)

When you see the advertisement saying, “For actress recruitment details, come talk to us,” what thoughts come to your mind? You might imagine modern girls in short skirts with flashy shoes and cold fragrance, eagerly gathering and being examined, one by one, by men who dance charmingly and embrace each other right away, with everyone thinking so. Is that really the case?

Hearing that in the Joseon theater world, a certain company is looking for male and female actors, I momentarily put away the name of journalist and disguised myself as an actress hopeful. I thought that if I could see what goes on inside, I would satisfy the curiosity of readers and perhaps gain some praise from my editor, so I braved up and undertook the bold act despite my fear.

As I arrived at the temporary office located up a narrow staircase in a little wooden two-story house in 0-dong, I found four or five neat young ladies with damp hair quietly practicing lines as if they were activity photo narrators. One said, “Oh, Mother,” and another, in a more exaggerated lamenting tone, replied, “Well, child, think a bit.” Though they looked comical at first glance, they were not the baek-bong-nyeo (断髮娘, cutting their hair) types nor was there anything about them that struck me as particularly dubious; they were just innocent young girls without any make-up. “The girls do seem quite innocent and young,” I thought, brushing my hand through my hair, feeling somewhat more at ease when I saw that their appearance was far more demure than mine.

Before the ladies did anything, a tall gentleman, apparently around the age of 25 or 26, dressed in a suit, suddenly stood up and asked, “Who are you looking for?” As I thought about how many female applicants might be coming more than for the roles themselves, I said, “No. I’ve come to apply.” He replied, “Ah, then please come this way.” I followed him down the stairs I had just climbed. Upon descending, we entered another room that resembled a printing house, where I quickly removed my shoes and stepped onto a filthy mat to reach the staircase in the corner. In that modest tatami room sat three or four men, including an elderly gentleman who seemed to be around 40 or 50 and two younger men, and they too were rehearsing and eliciting dramatic responses. As I crossed over there embarrassedly, I entered another room. 

This room had a desk where a gentleman around 30, neatly dressed in a fancy suit, was busily writing what seemed to be a script. He looked up when I entered and, upon seeing me again, asked if I was here for a distinguished visit. I thought he was inspecting my face before he uttered, “What is your name?” Since my real name was not appropriate to share, I had to contrive a stage name. I said, “My name is Kim Yeongsuk.” 

“Where are you from?” he asked. “I am from Gahoe-dong. I’m new to Seoul, so perhaps it’s 216 Gahoe-dong.” In a hurry, I provided the address of Mr. X from our office. Seeing it was an exceedingly naïve response, I tried to put on a charming front.

The more they grilled me, the more curious I became about their responses. As I answered in a somewhat vague manner, I felt the fear of having entered the offices of strangers as an investigator gradually fade. The people there were not a discourteous or unrefined sort but seemed rather polite and earnest, which somewhat soothed my heart. I wondered if anyone might discover that I was a journalist and thus decided to launch my own inquiry.

In a cheeky manner, I remarked, “Let me ask you this: surely, the most important criterion for selecting actresses is their looks, right? If that’s so, I must be a shoo-in.” “Not at all! That’s a total misconception. Your looks are indeed satisfactory; however, we recruit people regardless of that,” he replied with a smile. 

Supporters usually remain casual in such competitions, but surely no one would dare say such a thing. 

“Then I’ll try with confidence,” I responded. “One of my biggest concerns is what it’s like to live as a female actor. I came to Seoul by ignoring my parents back home, and as money is tight, I can barely scrape by due to the landlords hounding me for rent. If I were to join, would it suffice to make a living?” 

He replied that we had no fixed salaries for the actresses but might cover living expenses under special circumstances. “So being an actress is merely fulfilling the vain desires of young ladies, with no guaranteed salary. What does special circumstances mean?” was difficult to obtain in terms of clarity; whether that meant looks appealing enough to attract attention or the extraordinary skills of acting remained unclear.

Eager for more insight, I blurted out, “Which village are you from?” without hesitation he answered, “Wŏnsan (元山).” 

“Since I speak Jeolla dialect, it’s rather awkward. I’ve mentioned the name of Wŏnsan just as a passing mention.” I laughed at that thought when he asked for my address, “it would be 2X Samjaedong.” 

“My father’s name is Changja Hwan.” 

“Yes! Mr. Kim Changhwan. Mother’s name?” 

“My mother is Choi Gyeongnye.” 

“I see, and do you have younger siblings?” 

“I am the eldest; I have three younger siblings.” This was all true; the inquiries continued, with his gaze fixed on my face. 

“If the troupe goes touring to the countryside, do the male and female actors stay in the same inn?” I asked shamelessly. Knowing full well that this was rather inappropriate, I still made the inquiry. 

“Certainly! However, they do not lodge in the same room,” he said, smiling slightly. 

I could not quite grasp his tone. “Moreover, one last thing. When you return from touring, if there isn’t enough room, what do you do with actresses?” I tilted my head, waiting for a response. 

“No, we can’t afford something like that; our company strictly prohibits that,” he replied. “Other impoverished troupes might do such things, but our company does not.” 

After we finished this haphazard exchange, it was already half past two, so I thought, “What are the criteria for selecting actresses?” 

“There are about 13 criteria,” he began without hesitation; surely there were 13. I took some notes at that time. I cannot write them all down, as I didn’t have any more paper left, but the first criteria concerning first impressions indicate that if they find the expression to be attractive or satisfactory at first sight, body proportions, facial expression, educational background, and taste in leisure activities mattered, as did vocal quality, penmanship, and script reading. The most difficult of all criteria seems to be the expression test, so I foolishly asked, “So how does one take the expression test? Try testing me now.” 

“That’s not possible. When we conduct interviews, we do it while having a conversation like this,” he responded. 

I feigned surprise, “Oh really? I’ve been careless. I suppose I’ll probably be failing then.” I exaggeratedly made motions as if I were making an effort to appear disappointed. 

“Not at all,” he replied, pulling out a paper and added, “Kindly write this within two hours.” 

The examination questions were printed on half a sheet of paper. 

Trial for Membership Recruitment of the Theater Company

1. What does “I” mean? 
2. Was Ibsen truly a genius?

From the two options listed, you must select one; 

A. The dramatic value of Chunhyangjeon (春香傳).
B. The literary revival of Korea (朝鮮의 문예부흥).

This seemed like an exceedingly difficult task upon reflection. To expect a response worthy of 10 pages of written notes is no small order; only someone with considerable training could answer such adeptly. I could certainly provide a prompt response but imagined that starting to write would take me significantly longer than expected. 

I said, “I definitely cannot respond to something of such complexity here.” 

“That doesn’t matter. Out of over 130 individuals who took this trial, no one managed to answer completely,” he assured. “So put together whatever you can.” 

That was a kind remark, but I insisted, “I still need to go home and write this before I can submit it properly.” 

I was trying to distract from the conditions further when I asked a lighthearted question. 

“It’s said that actors date each other, right?” 

“Well, socially people think that,” he responded, “but in reality, actors tend not to. But if you so wish…” 

With rising apprehension, I sensed the atmosphere from this inquiry surrounding me. 

“I’m married, so I have less to fear.” 

“Oh, you are? So why are you living apart and doing this?” 

“Well, there were certain misunderstandings, and I divorced.” 

Knowing they only sought unmarried applicants, I took a chance to clarify, “So then, is it only for unmarried?” 

“That’s the case, yet we sometimes accept those who are married for varied reasons surrounding individuals.”

As I glanced at my watch, it was already 3:15 in the afternoon. Wanting to leave, I said, “Then I’ll bring this back tomorrow after I’ve filled it out.” 

And they asked me to write out an application form as well. Feeling burdened, I nonetheless felt it was disreputable either to avoid it or refuse, so I dotted down whatever I could manage to create. 

Writing my name on the pristine line, I meant to write Kim Yeongsuk, yet halfway through I recalled my family name Bai almost inscribing it, making me feel embarrassed. 

“I should be going now,” I said as I eased myself away, and he asked, “Could you not come this evening?” 

I was shaken and couldn’t even mentally recall what had been said at the registration desk before I left. 

I fretted as I received questions from men I never recognized; I understood their faces as I quickly departed with thoughts of failure as if I had stepped out of the haze of captivity. 

I gained a curious satisfaction from the first attempt I had made; no harm came from my exploration through this venture as if I had triumphed over some fear. 

On my way back to the office, I looked over my shoulder a dozen times. 

What most surprised me that day was that, contrary to perceptions about the lives led by performers, they were not filled with vulgarity but rather were genteel and reserved souls, and even the people I had communicated with were remarkably sincere and genuine, which made my trepidation dissipate.

The next day’s Inquiry

As the endeavor began, I thought I should find out whether or not I’d been accepted so I resolved to return again after lunchtime, albeit feeling uneasy. 

After climbing the wooden stairs past a room resembling a printing shop, I found there were six or seven young men already waiting; feeling nervous, I lowered my head, quickly walked over to the adjoining room. 

Today I noted that the gentleman I had spoken to earlier was seated next to a man dressed in a white durumagi (two-piece traditional clothing) who seemed to be writing something. 

I greeted him with a glance and went over to him and joined the rest in waiting.

For some time, I could see him lifting, slowly flipping, and reviewing notes I’d given earlier. 

“Indeed, you wrote it quite well! Very commendable,” he said. 

“Not at all; it’s just I was befuddled by the questions. It’s hard to grasp why they are so difficult,” I replied. 

“You certainly wrote exceptionally well; no one else is here to match that.” 

As they returned, “You wrote ‘sǒsag’ for thought; how are thought and reflection different?” 

“What is the meaning of ‘jŏuch’? ” he wondered, sometimes assuming a stern demeanor. 

“Just call it philosophical.” 

He asked, “Well then, I won’t hold back; among these questions, what’s your take on the issue of self?” 

The inquiry was indeed too probing, and the assertiveness troubled me. 

“Well, it’s quite essential; after all, I am here to ascertain whether I tend to see the world philosophically or not,” I replied to inject levity.

Having established rapport he turned to me again, “You need to come by daily; finding out about acceptance means a lot to me.” 

Sensing that I was departing, I prepared. 

He casually instructed me to bring a membership application form. 

The young man dressed in white was given this task, and the gentleman in the white durumagi went out to get what I realized was akin to an academic record. 

“Fill it out,” he instructed. 

The questions on it were similar to those from my earlier assessments: where I resided, current residence, date of birth, interests, writing skills. 

The interests I noted as ‘none’, while I listed tennis among activities, then began to nervously consider my date of birth. 

Realizing that I had written it differently on a previous application, I resorted to the uncertain estimate of “October 16.” 

Then, as I acquainted myself with the names, I found it humorous as they listed writers, “Are you familiar with Kim Myŏngsŭn?” 

“Do you know Kim Il-yŏp?” 

As I started to feel somewhat anxious about whether I had mixed the names around, they asked for anotherassociative writer, “How about Lee Guangsu?” and “Do you know Bang Jeonghwan?” 

I was positioned right beside Mr. Bang due to our shared workspace, misleading me into responding with “I’m not quite certain if he is the same,” so I was firmly caught off guard. 

“Yes, he is a fine individual with stellar contributions to the children’s movements,” I too could not help but smile. 

Expressing what I believed to be scholarly or educated banter, I inquired whether or not he was kind-hearted. 

He then stated clearly, “For you to volunteer towards art sole we wish to have you stay dedicated until you are 25; thereafter, you can choose any other paths.” 

Expressing both rectitude and overt sincerity struck me amusingly sometime later. 

“How could I misjudge such a bunch of diligent and sincere individuals when I’ve only thought this place a den of delinquents among the actors?” It gave pause for thought to researchers, “But why are all the scandalous stories revolving around actresses?” Often, decent ladies might find themselves limited to entering this circle in consideration of talent and virtue.

At the core, I wished for a respectable girl to emerge here and uplift our industry. “Please make sure you come; I greatly hope to see you this evening.” 

As the autumn rain began to patter softly against me, I rushed back home in an evening hurry. 

After a week or so had elapsed, some rumors regarding a budding actress circulated, “So she seemed talented, and yet she has since mysteriously disappeared,” leaving me curious and filled with guilt over hollow respect, ultimately necessary to convey, as I prepared to write, “Due to preparations in my household, I couldn’t come,” in a postcard.

  

**Summary**

This article presents the intriguing experiences of a female journalist who disguises herself as an aspiring actress to explore the dynamics and reality of the theater world during colonial Korea. Through her candid observations and interactions, the article unveils the societal challenges faced by female actors, the scrutiny regarding their character, and the pervasive stereotypes associating them with debauchery. Historians might find this account compelling as it reflects the complexities of gender roles and societal expectations during Japan's colonial rule over Korea, particularly in how women navigated public perception and career aspirations in the arts.

이만큼 문답을 치루고 나니 지원자도 아니면서 그럴 듯이 가장하고 남의 사무소에 密偵처럼 드러온게 겁나든 것이 차차로 사라지고 더구나 여긔 잇는 사람들이 그러케 예의를 모르는 비신사적이 아니고 생각 밧그로 조용하고 성실스러운 취급에 저윽이 마음이 노엿슴니다. 이제는 내가 雜誌婦人記者인줄을 아는 사람이 드러오면 읏저나?하는 것밧게 걱정될 것이 업서서 이번에는 『그런대요. 저는 매일 이럿케 올수도 업고 한 대 합격이 되는지 못되는지. 그것을 먼저 알아야 나오기가 편하겟슴니다.』하고 차차 退軍할 준비를 차리닛가. 『녜 그것은 안심하십시오. 되고 말고요.』하니 그만하면 합격된 세음이고나 하고 속으로 생각햇슴니다. 『가서 회원명부용지를 가저 오시요.』하고 白衣청년에게 일르닛가 명령대로 흰 두루막이 남자가 어대인지 나가서 학교로 치면 학적부용지 가튼 것을 한장 가저다가 내압헤 놋슴니다. 『거긔 적힌대로 써주시지요.』하는대 보닛가 본적 現住 생년월일 취미 작문 신장 운동 이런 란이 잇슴니다. 취미는 別無 운동은 테니스 이러케 대강대강 그나마 엉터리로 꾸며서 썻스나 생년월일을 어적게 이력서 쓴것과 동일하게 써야겟는데 무어라고 썻섯든지 생각이 나지 안어서 혼낫슴니다. 간신히 어림처서 10월16일이라 하여노코 안젓스닛가. 이번에는 『金明淳씨를 암닛가.』 『金一葉씨를 암닛가.』하고 뭇기 시작하더니 『李光洙씨를 암닛가.』 『方定煥씨라는 이를 암닛가.』고 뭇슴니다. 한 사무소에서 方선생님과 책상을 나란히 하고 잇는 사람인 줄 모르고 方선생님일을 믓는 것이 엇더케 滋味가 나던지 『녜-그 이 일홈만은 더러 드럿지만 엇던 이 인지 자세 모르겟슴니다.』하고 나서 다시 方선생님께 대한 욕이라도 나왓스면 더 자미잇겟서서 『그 이가 생각이 조흔 사람인가요.』햇슴니다. 그러닛가 『녜-퍽 조흔 이임니다. 어린이 운동에 제일 힘을 쓰고 퍽 성실한 이임니다.』함니다. 욕이나 欠談이 아니닛가 싱거워서 두어마듸 더 듯고는 그만 니러스려하닛가 각본을 조곰 닑어보고 가라고 함니다 내여주는 각본은 『五人男妹』 내갊� 연극으로 하는 것을 구경까지 한 것임니다. 아모데나 잡히는 대로 편곳이 健秀라는 오라비와 蘭秀라는 누의의 대화인대 그이는 오라비의 말을 연극하듯 변사투로 하면서 작고 날더러 누의의 말을 해보람니다. 긔왕 시작한 작란이니 이약이 꺼리삼아 해보려하엿스나 책 닑는 소리갓기만 하지 여배우투로 나가지지를 아니함니다. 떨니는 목소리로 『글세요. 글세요.』하고 여러번 해보아도 그아래말이 되지를 안음니다. 엽헤서도 갑갑하던지 『크게만 내어보서요. 커다라케요. 음량만 알면됨니다.』하것만은 그래도 이내 크게는 못나왓슴니다. 이때에 키가 퍽 크고 말숙한 양복을 닙고 아조 하이카라 머리에 실례지만 난봉가태 보이는 남자가 한분 드러오더니 한편에 안저서 밉살스럽게＜130＞ 싱글싱를 우스면서 작고 처다봄니다. 긔어코 저런 인물이 눈에 뜨이는구나 하고 속으로 생각하엿슴니다. 『그만 가겟슴니다.』하고 니러나려 하닛가 『참 어제 저녁에 도서관에 가시지 안으섯나요.』하고 믓기에 『아-니요.』하고 대답하면서 아마 앗가 그 답안을 책을 들추어 보고 썻다 햇드니 도서관에 갓섯느냐고 더듬어 보는 모양인가 보다 하엿슴니다.
『될 수만 잇스면 25살 될 때까지만 다른 마음먹지도 말고 다른데로 가지도 말고 아조 예술에 봉사뎍으로 헌신해주면 고맙겟고 그후에는 엇더케하던지 좃타!』고 이런말을 퍽 정성스럽게 은근한 부탁으로 말해줌니다. 속으로 우습기도 하지만은 그이가 넘어도 정성스럽게 구러주는 만큼 미안한 생각을 금할 수가 업섯슴니다.
『이러케 착실한 이들이 이러틋 정성스러운 태도로 노력하는 것 이것만은 밧게서는 한낫 부랑한 남녀들의 소굴인 줄 만 알고 잇스니 딱한 일이다.』고 생각되엿슴니다. 『그러나 여긔에 오는 녀배우들이 기생이거나 00부 가튼 이들이 만헛든 까닭이겟스닛가 결국 저러케 정성스러운 이들도 조흔 녀배우를 엇지 못해서 그런 딱한 소문을 듯게 되는 것이지.』생각할 때에 조흔 조촐한 녀자가 하로라도 속히 이 회에 생겨지기를 빌고 십어젓슴니다.
