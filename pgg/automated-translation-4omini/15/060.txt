Current Problems: Opinions of Notables, Proposals for Improving Daily Life  

Human beings are upwardly moving creatures. The common desire is to live better than the present. However, true upward movement means progressing one or two more steps with one's own abilities; otherwise, it is merely a reckless rush to follow external trends without genuine improvement and is, in fact, a sign of superficiality, vanity, or moral decay. Such so-called improvements will only lead to setbacks and ultimately destruction. Recently, on the surface, it appears that the lives of our Korean people have considerably improved. A person who once lived in a flat straw house now builds grand tiled homes, or Western-style or Japanese-style houses. Young men and women who have studied new methods usually wear tailored suits, and life, in general, appears more luxurious than before. Moreover, the urban extravagance of places like Seoul is increasing day by day, to the extent that some individuals possess luxurious silk garments and multiple dainty possessions that others might not even recognize. However, if we look inside, we will find that there is no increased productive capacity behind this; rather, people are simply selling off their ancestral inheritance, and living a lavish lifestyle is often unsustainable without any real foundation. 

Those indulging in immediate pleasures have no solid skills to ensure that their lifestyle can be retained over the long term. The foundations of such lives, built upon hollow extravagance, are as unstable as a sandcastle swept away by the wind; devoid of solid grounding, they will suffer a calamity whenever challenged. I might come across as overly pessimistic, but considering the current conditions of our Korean people, I believe it would be better to improve our lives not through seeking greater extravagance but by actively reducing waste, living simply and humbly, and enhancing hygiene and cleanliness in all matters.

Many people cannot sustainably have proper meals, let alone enjoy Western meals, and more than half the population walks instead of driving cars, raising doubts about whether we can maintain our current lifestyle. In conclusion, I believe that until the economic foundations of our Korean people are firmly established, we should consciously and simply live with frugality, which can nurture a refined sense of satisfaction and serve as the basis for all endeavors. Without an independent mind, conforming solely to the prevailing fashions of the age will lead us nowhere and result in failure. 

Living More Abundantly  
이상협  

Our Korean people's lives are exceedingly impoverished. The cries of “We’re starving!” resonate, while hardly anyone expresses the thought of living well. Nowadays, many people often say "save! save!" However, there are limits to frugality. If you ask someone living in a rented room to save on housing, what will they do? If a person can't even afford two bowls of rice a day, should we suggest they eat nothing and just perish? No, no. If we move forward only passively, we will fall into a more miserable state than our current lives. Regardless of the hardships we face, we must take one more progressive step, improving our lives instead of retreating or merely saving. Isn't it far happier to work hard and live abundantly? 

The key issue is how we Koreans can manage to thrive. If even in the most dire circumstances, many people are indeed productive and motivated, pursuing their desires with relentless efforts, then we could surely resolve our daily life problems. Those who dwell solely in passive sorrow will have nothing to hope for. There are instances where individuals contemplate suicide due to their hardships, but ultimately that reflects a weak will. If one has made the decision to end their life, then they have the potential to live actively; why should one worry about daily sustenance then? Currently, we lack the desire for a prosperous life; however, if all 20 million of us share a burning passion to find a pathway to a better life, it would be much easier to solve our living problems. We must actively engage in everything we do as Koreans!  

Transforming Toilets  
문학士 김법린  

Some may criticize me for saying this. Having studied in France, one might ask, isn’t toilet renovation important? However, we cannot overlook the significance of toilets. People say "bread" is what sustains life, but once you eat that bread, proper disposal of waste is also essential; this is indeed a significant issue. People and animals need to eat to survive, but generally, animals do not know how to manage their disposal while we humans must take care of it. 

Humanity being the pinnacle of creation is partly due to this ability to manage waste. While using fragrant perfumes and fragrant oils, it may stick in your mind that some of us use foul-smelling and dirty toilets, and for that, I sincerely apologize; yet, regardless of superficial cosmetics, improving toilet facilities is far more critical for hygiene and urban aesthetics. Places like Paris have a range of cultural facilities, earning it the name of the "capital of the world." Among these facilities, the infrastructure of roads and sewers is indeed remarkable. In Paris, nobody could even dream of seeing the communal toilets we have here, let alone taking notice of the foul-smelling waste removal trucks or workers lugging around waste. Our Korea's housing system is still imperfect, but we neglect our toilets. 

It seems that, in recent years, there has been some improvement compared to the past, but compared to foreign countries, we still have a long way to go. In urban areas and especially rural areas, toilet facilities remain vastly inadequate. The inadequacy of toilets not only significantly impacts hygiene but is also unpleasant in appearance. Fundamentally, we should not build flat houses but instead construct multi-story buildings, which would save space and enable us to transition from traditional ondol (온돌, an underfloor heating system) to bed-living. With that change, we could save fuel and contribute to forest protection, reducing barren mountains and droughts, which are characteristic troubles in Korea. 

While it may indeed be an economic issue that not everyone can afford to build multi-story homes, companies or investors should consider constructing multi-story buildings and allowing the population to rent them like in the West. Then, our lives would become incredibly convenient, with far fewer housing crises compared to modern-day Kyŏngsŏng (capital of Korea). 

Professionalize Everything  
불경사 주관 권상로  

Since I have always lived as a monk, I do not know much about practical aspects of life, thus I won't express any particular opinions on improvements. However, I feel that our lives are overly complicated. For example, soybean paste and kimchi should not be made household-to-household; rather, we need specialists working in these areas, allowing everyone else to purchase from them. 

If sewing and laundry were done by professionals, then everyone could place orders with them, and the professionals could sustain themselves while the consumers could focus on other jobs. Life would be tremendously simplified, and incomes could stabilize. Until now, because our way of life has been largely non-professional, every household's lives seem overwhelmingly complex, resembling small department stores. 

With such chaos, how can we maintain cleanliness at home? Every time we move to a new place, it's as challenging as relocating the capital; hence, we cannot easily shift residences. Moreover, complex lifestyles lead both men and women to futilely waste time and effort, leaving little leisure time for other activities. Currently, many in society are advocating for women's liberation, but I believe that simplifying life should precede that. 

In rural homes where the family has little money, they often store excessive amounts of food. They fill rice bins in excess, hoard firewood, and keep jars of soybean paste for 10 or even 20 years. While it may have made sense in the past due to inconvenient transportation and underdeveloped trade as a form of precaution, today, it is utterly unnecessary and economically unwise. They could instead sell those items at a reasonable price, saving the money and purchasing items when needed from specialists, allowing for a far simpler life.

Household Budgeting  
보성 고등학교장 정대현  

A nation's budget requires calculating incomes and expenditures, while an individual's budget calculates expenditures against income, following the principles of economics. However, how can we Koreans, with little to no income, really budget our detailed spending? 

Even a beggar with a single bowl of leftover rice can manage to plan daily meals; without budgeting, that single bowl might fill one’s stomach in the morning and leave them starving by evening.

Confusingly, many of us are often overwhelmed and feel more concerned with how many more days we can survive rather than thinking about how we might prosper. Therefore, budgeting is not merely about following economic principles based on assumed regular incomes but about setting budgets even assuming we will have incomes. Historically, Koreans have never engaged in budgeting, whether in public or private; they either spend what's available or live hand-to-mouth without careful planning. 

When we have some money, we tend to indulge foolishly, leading to excessive debt without planning. This issue isn’t confined only to the unemployed; often, the employed face greater issues of over-spending. When it comes to cultural or leisurely activities, when salary payments come in, the lack of budgeting leads to recklessness, resulting in various debts. The reasons behind it are many, but ultimately it stems from failing to maintain a budget-conscious lifestyle. Henceforth, a life without budgeting will no longer sustain individuals or organizations. 

If we continue to live for just today without thought of tomorrow, it’s one thing; however, if we wish to live more meaningfully, having a budget is crucial. 

Urgent Issues of Clothing and Kitchen  
중앙 보육학교장 박희도  

Based on my experiences in daily life, I plan to address two issues that can be handled with limited time, minimal financial investment, and less effort. 

The first is the issue of Korean clothing. Our clothing is inadequate in color, quality, and design. Consequently, the time taken to wear clothes often exceeds the time taken to create them. For instance, a man wearing traditional garments faces numerous difficulties, but the troubles faced by those sewing are almost indescribable. 

To improve clothing, the key issue is that we need textiles made by Koreans that meet at least minimum standards. Though today's imported materials from the West might be less than ideal, we must have locally produced textiles woven by Koreans that serve daily life comfortably. Resolving this issue requires specialized research from those in industry.

Moreover, there are certain styles of traditional Korean attire that I believe are unnecessary and should no longer exist, such as the "jeogori" (traditional Korean jacket). While this has been inherited through tradition, it has little utility today. Additionally, it must be acknowledged that Korea is historically a country that respects propriety and has cultivated customs around the respectful attire, continuing to bind people to wear formal clothes even when practical alternatives exist. 

To improve deeply-rooted social customs, it cannot be achieved through individual efforts; it requires collective actions from organizations to take the initiative and create change.

Second, the kitchen issue is of utmost importance to our lives, as it is closely tied to our survival. Yet, while we keep living and welcome guests in clean living rooms, we neglect the kitchen, which provides food for sustenance. This neglect is often overlooked in terms of essential needs but, in reality, is something we cannot afford to mismanage. 

Although improving the entire housing system is a significant matter, merely upgrading the kitchen can be done within the financial constraints of our current situation. For instance, the area supplying water and food must be clean and sanitary. It would significantly improve hygiene and even address our health issues if we used better materials in kitchens, such as synthetic resin or cement flooring.

For comprehensive improvement in the kitchen, engaging architects to create simplified plans that can be socially accepted is crucial. I hope to see more individuals advocate for this issue in the future.  

The Seven Principles of New Life  
신생 주관 유작기  

First, Let's Establish a Budget for Living Costs  
We must set a budget for our daily and monthly expenses, ensuring we act within those limits for our living, education, and savings. Only by living within our financial limits will we establish a sense of financial creditability, reducing the number of mischief-makers.  

Second, Eradicate the Habit of Extravagance  
We must eradicate wasteful expenses such as unnecessary chores, rampant use of luxury imported goods, excessive extravagance beyond reasonable limits, and unnecessary spending on alcohol (already close to 1.2 million won annually), aiming for health and prosperity for individuals, families, and society.  

Third, Maintain a Regular Lifestyle  
We should raise children in a structured manner, feeding and resting at regular times. This promotes both physical health and an organized existence. Not only children but also adults must instill a sense of time management. Returning home around midnight after a late meal can be detrimental, as it is rarely observed among the American middle class. Eventually, let's act, eat, rest, and work like them.  

Fourth, Let's Realize a "Land of Courtesy"  
We boast of being a "nation of etiquette," yet in practice, we are inundated with deficiencies in personal conduct and social morals. Those who act without respect to elders, friends, or fellow citizens are worrisome. Following the words of Confucius, "We have lost touch with our common humanity," I note that we still need lessons from Americans. Let’s embrace kindness, integrity, and courtesy.  

Fifth, Let’s Work  
Instead of simply talking, let each fulfill their duties with sincerity. Let us avoid seeking empty honors, deception, or appearing as the most capable when we engage honestly in our respective roles.  

Sixth, Let’s Love  
Engage fully in work and avoid idly spending free time; instead, let’s invest that time to build warm families. By embracing mutual love among parents, children can witness love and consequently become candidates for nurturing loving families of their own.  

Seventh, Let’s Be Devout  
The true value of humanity reveals itself in our humble demeanor; even if we have material possessions and comforts, without a devout spirit, our character faces trials. Thus, without a foundation of devotion, any effort to improve our lives becomes empty rhetoric. We must genuinely pursue improvements in our lives, thereby living with others harmoniously.  

Awareness Over Improvement  
박동완  

Recently, one could claim that our Korean lives have seen considerable improvement. Individuals once wearing topknots have shaved their heads, wearing Western suits and behaving differently, which can also be viewed as an improvement. Those once using traditional modes of transportation now drive cars instead of horse-drawn carts, and it seems luxurious to go from straw houses to Western or Japanese-style residences, with women also engaging actively in social settings. While this appears positive, the underlying reality is quite disheartening. Automobile use often correlates with debauchery where young people take women of ill-repute to entertainment venues instead of for business discussions. 

Dressing well in Western attire and taking on modern fashions does not imply that one engages in constructive work. Instead, it frequently results in shameful behavior and is not improvement. Similarly, families accumulating extravagances, in the absence of increased productivity, merely constitute a reckless waste of inheritance. Given these conditions, the question remains: how long will our lives maintain this standard? 

I believe that before seeking improvements, we Koreans must undergo an awakening concerning our actual situation. Based on our capabilities, after dismissing all extravagances, even if one dons plain clothing such as "jeogori" and surplices, we must conserve as much as possible and toil diligently until we secure stable livelihoods. Even individuals who possess money often struggle to nourish themselves; how can one be frivolous in the face of such stark reality? 

For the time being, while superficial improvements are acknowledged, I firmly believe that rigorous frugality is necessary to endure.

One Easy Practical Suggestion  
민태완  

(Partial text omitted) 

Among the many suggestions I wish to highlight, there may be some proposals that are merely optional. However, there remains one straightforward recommendation that anyone can implement if they have the willpower, guaranteeing effective outcomes.  

1. Let’s Eat Together at a Set Time  
In ordinary Korean households, there is no established mealtime, leading to different family members eating at separate intervals. First, the children going to school are fed, followed by others, making the routines chaotic. Not only does this complicate life for families but it especially burdens the women, who have to maintain these schedules. This issue, although predominantly seen among the upper class, persists throughout the urban population. I see this general bad habit across households, which can easily be resolved once families agree to implement changes, generating unexpectedly significant benefits.  

First, tackle three issues  
최상현  

(Partial text omitted)  

The first is to reform the extended family system. Initially, families were based on a father, mother, and their children; but when multiple couples reside in one household, it leads to disharmony between spouses and conflicts, resulting in a reliance culture that inhibits independent living and fosters dysfunctional families. Our Korean culture, with a tradition of large family systems, results in interrelationship-based cohabitation, which often leads to conflicts. To adequately improve family happiness and facilitate independent living, we must strive to reform this system.  

The second issue is the reform of ritual customs. That is, regarding marriage and funeral customs, we must prioritize simplicity over extravagance. The traditional wedding customs inflict immense losses and waste precious time on needless rituals. We should abolish all superficial displays and focus instead on practicality and simplicity. While this approach may draw criticism for being less lawful or disregarding tradition, in today’s complex social landscape, unnecessary ceremonies should be eliminated.  

Third, let’s promote abstinence from alcohol and tobacco. I do not need to elaborate much on this. At this time of extreme economic difficulty in Korea, any discourse on life improvements cannot exclude the necessity of rejecting these economically and health-compromising substances. Embracing these three reforms, which are deeply rooted customs in our society, will necessitate collective actions by focus groups or youth associations, contributing significantly to our pursuit of improved living conditions.  

**Summary**

The article presents various perspectives on improving the lifestyles of Koreans during the colonial period, highlighting the importance of self-sufficiency, frugality, and simplifying aspects of life rather than succumbing to the pursuit of vanity or extravagance. It emphasizes an urgent need for social change amidst the backdrop of external influence and economic hardship. Historians may find the desire for reform and the criticism of both individual and societal behaviors illuminating, offering insight into the complexities and challenges faced by Koreans under Japanese colonial rule.
