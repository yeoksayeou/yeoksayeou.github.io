기사제목	外國에 가서 생각나든 朝鮮 것-잇치지 안튼 氣候와 김치 (Thinking of Korea While Abroad - Climate and Kimchi)  
필자	金俊淵 (Kim Jun-yeon)  
기사형태	잡저 (Essay)  

Anyone realizes the true value of their own when they see something from another. When I was in my homeland, I did not truly understand how good Korea's climate was or how valuable kimchi is, but after living in several countries, namely Japan, Germany, England, and Russia, I realized how distinguished Korea's climate is, as well as how remarkable Korean kimchi is. The climate of England, enshrouded in black smoke and thick fog for all four seasons, is such that even the proud Englishmen, who would claim anything from their country is the best, confess to this issue. There is little need to elaborate on the mixed climate of Japan and the frigid cold of Russia, as they are similarly deficient. Germany’s four seasons do not have a clear delineation; in summer, for example, there can suddenly be winter-like weather. Spring and autumn are particularly indistinct. When I was in Berlin and went on a trip to the southern regions, I wore winter clothing but after spending about two weeks there, returning to Berlin, I found that spring had hardly been noticeable. Flowers were already blooming, trees were in full leaf, and citizens were wearing summer clothes; yet shortly after that, just a few weeks later, it snowed heavily before I could even see the signs of autumn. In Germany, the climate changes so frequently that the air often feels uncomfortable, and it's challenging to view the fine moon (零露). 

Seeing all this constantly brings to mind Korea's splendid climate; thus, I long for the flower-filled spring, the autumn leaves, and the bright moonlit nights during that season. Moreover, living in Europe, where meat dishes prevail, I particularly crave kimchi. The people there occasionally eat potatoes to counterbalance their meat consumption, and after a meal, they might enjoy some fruit. However, the value of having Korean kimchi after a meat meal is inherently higher in terms of taste and nourishment. Naturally, that thought crosses my mind often. 

  

**Summary**  

The article reflects on the author’s experiences abroad and highlights the stark contrasts between Korean climate and culture, particularly in relation to kimchi, compared to other countries like England, Japan, and Germany. The author expresses a longing for the distinct seasons of Korea and the cultural significance of kimchi as a staple that complements a meat-heavy diet abroad. This piece offers insight into the nostalgia of overseas Koreans during colonial times, revealing how cultural identity and traditional foods become focal points of pride and comfort in foreign lands. Historians might find it interesting as it underscores how food shapes national identity, especially for Koreans living under colonial rule.
