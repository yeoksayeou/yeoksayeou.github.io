기사제목	白頭山에서 漢挐山까지 橫으로 본 朝鮮의 美 (The Beauty of Korea Viewed from Baekdu Mountain to Hallasan)  
필자	돌이  
기사형태	잡저 (Essay)  

The phrase "some speak of the beautiful Geumgang (金剛) Mountain, but I prefer to see all 3,000 miles (里) of beauty." Nonetheless, blind admiration is not what I seek.

"Hwaerye Gangsan (華麗江山) Dong bandwidth (東半島)!" "Gimyo Gangsan (錦繡江山) of 3,000 miles!" These are slogans that praise the natural beauty of Korea, appealing to both domestic and foreign people alike. Recently someone from China expressed a desire to see Geumgang Mountain, and someone from the Ming Dynasty claimed that crossing the Yalu River was like entering a fairyland. An American remarked that the beauty of Geumgang surpasses the landscapes of Switzerland, while a particular German claimed that Korea's natural beauty is the number one in the world.

How astonishing! How did the mountains and waters of Korea become so beautiful that they became the envy of people across the world? Should we express our gratitude to Creator or to Dangun (檀君)? Regardless, is the happiness of the 20 million brothers not ensured?

Let's skip to the conclusion and proceed with evidence. Where shall we begin? Should we ascend Baekdu Mountain and gaze southward, or shall we climb Hallasan and look north? Let us begin by looking southward in order.

From Seoul to Baekdu Mountain, let’s admire the landscapes along the way. The pages are few, and the journey is long; let us henceforward take a break sometimes.

The northern peaks are unkempt like a field of wild lotus, while the southern are like silkworms of the past! The Han River shines like a thousand-year-old color, filled with the morning and evening songs of fishermen. The cool breeze at Cheongnyang-ri is vibrant throughout the four seasons, and the crystal clear stream in Uiryeongdong flows perpetually. The shape of the Cheongyang Triangle seems to embody the spirit of the men of Goryeo (高麗), while Dosan and Mangwol are like the humble workshops of the Creator. Crossing through Baeksa (白沙) and Cheongcheong (淸川) and wandering through strange peaks and bizarre rocks, I pass by Yeongju (楊洲) and Chestnut Village, and here lies Dongducheon. It is said “left green mountain, right blue water,” and indeed the left is beautiful, and the right is strange. 

Having briefly seen the small Geumgang, also known as Seongha Mountain (逍遙山), I pass by Bogong Mountain (寶盖山) and travel through the fields of Iron-joon (鐵原平野) then through Pyeonggang to rise at the Gongju Plateau at an elevation of 2,300 feet. With the scattered reed flowers, the herding horses cry long.

Arriving at the magic realm of Jeonnam, I reach the Suppa Temple (釋王寺) to feel the thrill of being in the number one spot in the world. Amidst the lush pine trees, the clear river rushes down, and the Pyeongla Suite Hotel (蓬萊 館雪峰樓) stands out in bright crimson. After drinking medicinal water a few cups and reciting a few passages from the Buddhist scriptures, I make sure I buy a pine-roofed house and after stepping one more time, passing through the Anbyeon flat lands, I look out at Geunma Peninsula, before heading to the first port on the East Coast, Wonsan! Oh! The landscapes of mountains and seas are splendidly charming!

Here, having enjoyed a bowl of Baekgaek soup and after a moment’s rest at the ten-mile bright sand beach, I make the rounds at the thriving market in Wooncheon Pavilion.

The Hamheung flat lands is where multiple mountain villages can barely distinguish east from west, and the Changchun River and the Mandong Bridge are suitably long. After taking a break at Dangryong Mountain, I look west towards the egg island, poop island, and the brother and sister islands, and the grand landscapes here truly epitomize the world’s finest sights. 

Touring the literary Pine Island and the skilled ones of Cheong Lake, I pass by the new, also fresh fish markets of Shinheung and Seongnim while keeping alongside the Namdaechon River, climbing the Hoemi Peak. How mighty and incomparable it is! After resting and taking a break, I cross through the first village of Bongsan and pass over Yeongdeok Peak, cross the Heosanjin River, travel alongside Gapsan and finally arrive at Hwaksan.

Observing the flowing Yalu (鴨綠長江), I make it to Baekdu Mountain, the mountain of magnificent shape beyond all words. 

Looking down over the sky-filled pond for an hour, I sit atop the Mount Junggak and look over 3,000 miles; stretching forth like silk, scattered like gold. To the left, green mountains; to the right, blue waters; the eagles soar in the mountains, and the fish leap in the waters. Amidst the fertile lands and good fields are the sounds of the harvest song; amongst the emerald pines and green bamboo, bright lips and rosy cheeks peek through. Truly, it is the world’s paradise, a vibrant painting. The iron wood, amidst the lush forest of Ling Mountain, is the world’s premier timber source, and the spring waters bubbling from the large pond immutably mark the flow of the East and West rivers that define the boundaries.

While observing precious animals in the dense forest, with the long wind at my back, I stride up to Haengju and pass through a hill marked by the long river, crossing the Jin Castle and smoothly passing through Prefectural class walls, arriving at Sogyeong-dong. The domain's peak is the past, beneath the ever-flowing waters of the Han, above like the heights of wealth.

In the West, looking at prevalent hills and dense forests, with changing scenery that depicts the richness of nature unfolding at every stop. Ah, the scenic spots and diversity of Korea surpassing the world is indeed sublime! 

How refreshing! How beautiful are Korea's landscapes!

**Summary**

The article vividly describes the natural beauty of Korea, extending from Baekdu Mountain to Hallasan, incorporating specific locales and their characteristics, thus creating a picturesque travelogue of the landscape. The author emphasizes the awe-inspiring qualities of these locations while expressing a sense of national pride. For historians, the piece offers a glimpse into the collective identity under colonial rule and how natural beauty served as a symbol of Korean cultural heritage, possibly serving as an act of resistance against imperial narratives.
