Dreaming of Lee Sang-jae, "Is the Teacher a Great Person?"  

"Hey there! Have you seen the biography of Lee Sang-jae?"  

"I caught a glimpse of it at a friend's house..."  

"Just from that, it seems there's not even a single worth mentioning about how he stirred things up in the world, be it with his social funeral or whatnot... Oh dear..."  

"That's the truth. What has he really done? Sure, in his later years, he seems to have been trying to win over students and getting involved with media organizations, religious groups, and ideological groups. That's why things turned out that way... But, still, there are people saying that the book contains errors."  

"So besides what’s in that book, what significant things has he achieved? My goodness, who exactly is he, and what kind of person is he? I really don't know..."  

"Is there anything else he did...? If you look at his life, it seems he was a person dodging raindrops..."  

"Shh! What business do you have criticizing others here? Just drink and go home..."  

This was the advice given by J while I was in a tavern with H at the center of a lively discussion, raising a glass repeatedly.  

"Well, that might be true!"  

"How many refills does everyone have?"  

"Yes—about eighty glasses of liquor and they’ve taken in nineteen dishes of snacks!"  

"Look at this! We are indeed fine fellows, right? Just the three of us, look at how much we've consumed! Ha—ha—ha... Hehe... hehe..."  

As we rambled on, I left that place, bidding farewell to H and J, and stumbled home past Donggwan Square, realizing it must be past midnight. The house was silent as a grave. Since there were elders at home, I cautiously took off my coat and thought I would write down some notes at the desk in the corner while I adjusted to the dim light. Suddenly, my eyes brightened as if I had wings under my arms, and I floated as if my feet were no longer touching the ground, drifting somewhere without direction.  

In the vast open fields, I soared freely, wandering along mountain paths and beaches. After wandering for a while aimlessly, I arrived at a place resembling a majestic building, exquisitely decorated in radiant colors with an ethereal charm and sounds of delights ringing in the air. Curiosity spurred me, and I quietly entered through the front gate, peeking inside.  

In the center of the hall stood an elderly man, with a crown and a flowing white beard, exuding an impressive aura and remarkable poise. Surrounding him on both sides were tall Westerners with deep-set eyes and short, yellow-faced Easterners, all wearing crosses and singing hymns with trembling voices saying, "We are praising, hallelujah!" I thought to myself, "Wow! Look at this!! Last spring I had a glimpse of hell, but seeing heaven is unexpectedly wondrous. Since I’m here, I might as well take a good look."  

Thinking that, I tiptoed further inside, and as I took in the colorful flowers and figs, I was at a loss as people seemed to be endlessly singing hymns all day long. No matter what, it didn’t seem as spectacular as the hell I had seen. When King Dorrin visited heaven last spring, he raved about how wonderful it was, which made me curious if it was really so, but seeing it with my own eyes, I couldn’t figure out what was so splendid.  

People ought to be gentle and calm, but when they’re fiercely bold or courageous, they must indeed be that way. But here in heaven, I felt as if I were surrounded by moral exemplars who had done nothing throughout their lives and were nothing but passive beings entrenched in complete non-resistance.  

Conversing with Heaven  
As someone full of worldly grime and a life laden with sins, I had all the qualifications to visit hell last spring and observe the judgment there. Now, I wondered if I had the right to visit heaven, too? I thought I should test it. Thus, I coughed once and stepped boldly forward, and suddenly the previously tranquil atmosphere of heaven began to stir, becoming lively as the deities awoke.  

"Hey—how come a filthy worldly guest has come here, polluting the sanctity of heaven!?" came a booming voice.  

As they gazed at me with wide eyes, someone rushed forward, gripping my wrist tightly.  

"Hey! What’s this? How did you come here?"  

Upon careful inspection, I recognized it was Lee Sang-jae, whom I had always admired and respected. I was somewhat surprised and excited.  

"Ah! Teacher! You’re here?"  

I was both glad and somewhat flustered. But after glancing at the teacher again, I noticed he looked significantly more haggard and burdened compared to last winter when I saw him at the OO Daily Newspaper office.  

"A person like you cannot stay in such a sacred place for long; let’s go out the side door," he said, patting my back as we headed towards the exit.  

"Look at this. It seems even in heaven, I’m a dropout!" I thought to myself, silently following him to the side room.  

"How did I end up here and unexpectedly meet you, Teacher? I would love to ask you a few things I’ve wanted to know for a long time. Would you share your thoughts?"  

The teacher rolled his big eyes and asked, "What are you planning to say? Are you going to curse?"  

"Oh—what are you talking about? When have I ever cursed?"  

"You have indeed cursed me by calling me a person who dodges raindrops..."  

"Yes, I did say that. But teacher! Were you greatly offended?"  

Just then, his stern demeanor softened as he said, "Ha ha... offended? What offense?! I’ve heard this kind of talk before... You see, nowadays with social funerals, biographies, and commemorative publications, things are in turmoil. I find it deeply unsettling and quite an undeserved honor, to be frankly honest."  

"You are inherently honest, Teacher, so I understand your thoughts, but there is no need for such humility. In the world, people call you a great man, a giant star, a majestic mountain, an elder of the people, and a model for the youth—how grand it sounds."  

"Such talk isn't fitting! What is it to be an elder? Why should I be a model? Even until the day I die, I will serve Minister Min OO with deepest respect and always bow three times, and when Lord Lee OO passed, I was the first to mourn his ancestors, so I’ve been scorned by many! Being called an elder or a model is just mismatched!"  

"Teacher! You underestimate yourself. Such a viewpoint is purely subjective. I believe the world is not devoid of someone like you who has lived a challenging life, and among the difficult lives, there is no more ordinary life like yours. I always have grievances about you."  

"You have a point! If we claim being ordinary, then we must admit it is true! I have done little of significance, yet you call me someone who dodges raindrops."  

"Indeed, think of it this way—Korea has experienced a tumultuous 60 years, haven't we? You’ve lived through it all, so you’d undoubtedly understand. The turmoil of the Imjin War, the tyrannical isolationist policies of the Daewongun, the rampant influence of the clans, the Imo Incident, the capture of the Daewongun, the Gapsin Coup, the incident in the northern territories, the Japanese invasion, the Donghak Rebellion, the Sino-Japanese War, the Eulmi Incident, the internal strife, the Russo-Japanese War, the OO Treaty (where you acted as the reference for the government), and without mentioning the OOO uprising and other issues, there was nothing but a turbulent storm and waves! Wasn't there any drop of rain seeping into your life amidst all that chaos?"  

At this, the teacher showed a somewhat pained expression and said, "I see! When I think of it that way, your words resonate. I sometimes lament my own inadequacy as well!"  

"Even in OO year, when I was indeed publicly distressed, I had lamented because they claimed I was an old relic. It was when Sun OO had already sent, and I only got discarded! That was really bothersome..."  

"Why! Teacher, are you troubled by the social funeral issue too? I hear you've been quite furious about the book that claimed you favored a life of comfort."  

"That fellow is quite bold... After living for eighty years amidst storms in this land called Korea, what mark have I left just to have him publicize it like that...? How presumptuous! Perhaps being related by blood must make him less prudent! That book, no matter how useful it is, as a societal object, if there are errors, then surely society will correct it. Is it his personal property? The people are fussing about me being a great person and a star as if I were someone of extraordinary significance!"  

"You speak humbly, Teacher. If the descendants, having a biography of their ancestor, disagree with the story, wouldn't it be unreasonable for them to remain silent?"  

"Ha! How could it be a mere matter of being called a ‘sustainer’ of the society? In my youth, I had done things far more flamboyant than that! During my wandering days, such trivial things could hardly blemish my character! This must surely be an outdated way of thinking!"  

"Even so, are they lamenting about the content being ambiguous or the discourse being unnecessary?"  

"They are rampaging without understanding my intentions. I’m seeing things more clearly nowadays. They’re promoting me all too heavily, elevating me to heights I hardly deserve! They are causing quite a stir, and I've ended up with undue praise. Furthermore, the funeral costs left (1500?), and then they send it off to my descendants, and also, various collections are giving rise to all sorts of statements. What exactly are they even thinking? Alas, our populace remains naïve!"  

"Currently, it seems astonishing that there’s no one left on Korean soil with the stature to discuss social funeral matters. However, while people on one hand idolize you passionately, on the other, they criticize you as a Jack of all trades."  

"Hey there, you young one, don’t you go around speaking carelessly!"  

"Oh! After the annexation of Korea by Japan, there were claims that you received some favors from the Japanese delegation and gave them rote responses, and during your visit to Japan, you reportedly remarked that ‘Seeing the new mother’s house reminded me of my deceased mother.’ These remarks are seen by many as jokes when they should not be perceived that way. Many claim that's a clever diplomatic maneuver, you see."  

The teacher sat quietly for a while, then suddenly slammed his fist onto the desk, exclaiming, "That’s exactly why people unjustly praise me in public! I’ve committed no sin nor done anything great; living peacefully, I’ve simply gone about my business, and now I’m thrust into chaos without my consent... Oh... That’s why you find yourself here speaking your truth in heaven! You foolish ones raise the noise, and the scoundrels end up appearing!"  

"Teacher! To call me a scoundrel is too heavy a charge. I am someone who cannot beat about the bush with compliments and approached you directly, and for that, I apologize. However, allow me one last moment to speak. People say you're just as stubborn and narrow-minded as a common old man."  

"What kind of nonsense is that? I am by no means dim, nor do I lack insight into things!"  

"Teacher! Seven or eight years ago, there was an article in the Dong-A Ilbo titled ‘To the Elders of Korea’ and one about ‘Hit the Empty Head.’ Was this not written by Kwon Deok-gye?"  

"That it was... What became of it?"  

"Back then, everyone debated and criticized you for it, right?"  

"That's true, indeed."  

"Yes—it came out. While those who understood had their say, those who didn’t found it humorous."  

"What was the content like?"  

"All encompassing and mundane only; however, if we reference one phrase, it stated, ‘Even if the elders may say something not entirely virtuous, who is the writer's child or descendant?’ This particular phrasing is akin to the standard vocabulary of ignorant elders, hence no more to say."  

"Is there anything else?"  

"There’s a line where Song Yu-am criticized the motivation behind rebuilding the temples for the Ming dynasty emperors, indicating that it was for the ambition of the vassals while urging others to hold the Qing in reverence. Was this not an instance to be wary of? How strange was it that even when exalting a foreign emperor in our homeland and carrying out rituals in his honor, you still deemed this moral?"  

The teacher shook his head vigorously, "What was that? Goodness, I can hardly bear to recall! Back then, I thought it was correct, and you telling me this makes me reflect."  

"Also, Teacher! There was a remark regarding ‘before hitting others who are dim, one must hit oneself first.’ Shouldn’t you consider that for yourself today? I believe that would serve you well."  

The teacher's eyes widened, his mouth opened in astonishment, and he burst into hearty laughter, “Ah-ha-ha! It all stems from my vacillation. And thus has brought me to this day, fretting in heaven, while I realize all my wrongs, you see, do not overly press about—how could you not share some comforting words with me?"  

"Indeed. You were mistaken, Teacher. Even if you take your leave, there won't be another figure like you left in the streets of Jongno left with nothing but the remnants of your past."  

"Goodness, is that how it has become? Oh, dear! Just to make me think—what will my fate be?"  

Indicating this with a sense of melancholy, the teacher then trembled a little with sorrow and spoke with a quivering voice. I, too, felt a pang of sorrow, and tears flowed uncontrollably from my eyes, causing me to loudly weep. 

At that moment, I felt something poke me in the back.  

"Hey! What’s wrong? Why are you crying here? Who are you? Why are you sobbing?"  

When I startled awake, it was my aunt’s voice. It turned out that I had been dreaming with my head slumped on the desk, wandering in the realms of drunken dreams.  

**Summary**  

This text illustrates a surreal and philosophical conversation between the author and the historical figure Lee Sang-jae (이상재). The narrative transitions through the author's drunken reflections, dreams, and eventual conversations about the legacy and societal roles of figures like Lee amid Korea's turbulent colonial history. Notably, it touches on the theme of self-deprecation in the face of common societal praises, leading to discussions on how historical figures should be remembered and the irony in seeking validation through societal acknowledgment. Historians might find interest in the critique of the glorification of individuals in the colonial context, as well as the reflections on the experiences and perceptions of those who navigated the complexities of their identities during this period.
