발행일	1927년 08월 17일  
기사제목	朝鮮日報女記者 崔恩喜氏와의 會談記 (Interview Record with Choi Eun-hee, a Female Reporter of the Chosun Ilbo)  
필자	靑吾生 (Cheong Wo Saeng)  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Discussion)  

Interview Record with Choi Eun-hee, a Female Reporter of the Chosun Ilbo  
Cheong Wo Saeng  
Time: July 26th, 3:30 PM  
Location: Taehwa Women's Hall, Insa-dong, Seoul  

Reporter: I heard that your health has not been good recently. How are you feeling now?  

Choi: Yes, I have somewhat recovered. However, my health is quite variable, so I can't guarantee anything.  

Reporter: A woman's heart is said to change like the clouds in the autumn sky, so it seems even health follows suit.  

Choi: My health is not like the autumn sky's clouds; it’s more like the rainy days of summer. (Her tone is quite urgent, and while she spoke, tears welled up in her eyes, and the Taeguk fan in her hand was waved energetically.)  

Reporter: Why do you have the Taeguk fan? It’s quite a sight.  

Choi: Yes, I bought it myself. I plan to gift it to someone today.  

Reporter: I understand. The front has "Minseo" written on it, and the back says "Jeong Chukye," a gift from the noble columnist, Mr. An Jae-hong. Among summer gifts, fans are the most refreshing and pleasant, so surely, if the people's customs blow along Autumn Stream, it will be even more delightful.  

Choi: (She just laughed without saying a word.)  

Reporter: Now that we’ve spent some time chit-chatting, let’s proceed to formal questions.  

Choi: Is there a separate formal set of speaking rules? Everything we discuss is newsworthy.  

Reporter: Whether I write a visit record or a Q&A session is up to me, so please just answer as best you can to my questions. I promise to treat you later.  

Choi: Well, if you write it in a Q&A format like Sin Il-sun, it might end up being too rigid, so please write it differently.  

Reporter: A Q&A will be rigid…? That's not true; it’s all about the content. Whatever the format, I’d appreciate a quick response.  

Choi: Alright, then please go ahead. My birthplace is Haeju, and I was born in Yeonbaek-gun (Wonbuk Cheon). But who knows whether I fell from the sky or arose from the mud? (As she speaks, her eyebrows dance playfully, and she sheds tears again.)  

As for my age, it’s quite extensive, about 283 months. (She took out a pencil as if elementary school students do to convert her age into months, and after some fuss, she realized—Oh, my age has already passed nearly 300 months.)  

Reporter: There’s no need to fuss about your age a little bit more. Converting it into months makes it sound larger, right? If we measure it by years, you are still younger than Xiang Yu, who commanded eight thousand troops and crossed the Yangtze River by five months.  

Choi: To draw the readers' interest, it seems permissible to express myself slightly more than simply saying I'm in my twenties, right?  

Reporter: Oh dear! It seems I’ve stumbled into a major issue! Before I dig into the interesting aspects I was curious about, you began with your own intriguing phrases, which could leave me in a deadlock, right? However, you’ve been very generous in providing materials; I truly appreciate it.  

Choi: Make sure the writing fee is substantial.  

Reporter: Of course, I will settle it for you. Now, please share some enjoyable aspects of your student days and what life was like before you became a reporter.  

Choi: Oh, let’s not bother with that. You know well enough about my life so far, and there were writings published under different names from earlier on. As for my academic years, the elementary, middle, and college experiences were all quite different, so I can’t detail each one. But I remember my elementary school years the best. In terms of elementary school, it seems I attended pretty well. I entered school in December at the age of seven and graduated at eleven. That school was founded by my father, and I attended by riding on someone else's back. During my studies, my singing and calligraphy were poor, but my mathematics and classical Chinese studies were quite commendable. I borrowed a sewing machine for sewing practice at home. Later, I transferred to Haeju Ijeong Girls' School, where I improved my studies, including handwriting. Until then, I didn’t even know how to sing, and when there were music performances, I couldn’t even make it to the last place, but I was selected as the top speaker at speech contests. I entered the Girls’ High School in Insa-dong only in the middle and graduated after just two years, so I don’t recall much. At Japan Women’s University, I studied economics quite enjoyably. However, I didn’t particularly enjoy practical logic or such writings. After that, my entire history has been as a teacher (before studying abroad in Japan), moving from Suwon Samil Girls' School to Pyongyang Jeongjin Girls' School and Anju Wijeong School, where I ended up applying chalk to my face for makeup. Then, during the 1919 movement, I underwent interrogation at the police station eight or nine times, and I did six months of pre-trial detention (after being held in Haeju for six months during the preliminary examination and receiving a two-year probation during trial). (While recalling that time, her fingers imitate a dancing gesture as she holds the Taeguk fan to fan herself.)  

Reporter: I have a general idea of your background now, but what motivated you to become a journalist, and who introduced you to the Chosun Ilbo?  

Choi: Motivation? I don't suppose there’s any particular motivation, but I have always been interested in journalism. About a year before I joined, while I was in school in Japan, I came back to Seoul during a break and happened to meet Mrs. Heo Yeong-suk, the wife of Lee Gwong-soo, who mentioned that being a reporter was a delightful existence. After that, I returned to my hometown, Haeju, and finding myself quite bored, I asked Mrs. Heo to recommend a job for me. She promptly responded that I should apply as a female reporter for the Chosun Ilbo and told me to come up to the capital. Until then, I had doubts considering the management of the newspaper company, namely Mr. Song, was not aligned with my ideas. I exchanged letters that were non-committal until Mrs. Heo wrote again saying I should come up to Seoul. It was a chance to refresh myself, so I hurried to Seoul. Upon arriving at Seoul Station, Mrs. Heo and Mr. Kim Ji-tae were there to greet me. Following them to Mrs. Heo's house, I found there was Min Tae-won, the editor-in-chief of the Asahi at that time. I got acquainted with him as well. (While in Japan, I naturally had some prior acquaintance with him). During my stay at Mrs. Heo's, I happened to undertake an unusual task. Mrs. Heo was a doctor at that time, and a wealthy person from Wonkong-dong was unable to pay the bill for a treatment that cost over 80 won. He kept delaying payments, and when I learned about this, I thought it was terrible, so I went by myself to collect the money. Out of frustration, I pleaded and persistently demanded until that miserly wealthy man finally paid up. The news of my actions must have reached the ears of the Chosun Ilbo authorities, for they saw me as a suitable candidate for a reporter and accepted me on October 5th, four years ago, based on Mrs. Heo’s recommendation. I also ended up lodging together with Mr. Min’s family until then. (Her long hair uncommonly seen on a woman glistened like pearls, and she wiped her face with a towel due to the sweat.)  

Reporter: What has been the most interesting and most troublesome experience during your time as a reporter?  

Choi: Oh, by the way, I’ve absolutely had to deal with many laborious things during the reporting process. As journalists are generally competing for articles, discovering and publishing exclusive materials that others might not know about is incomparable in its thrill. Moreover, in my case, as I compete with female reporters from other newspapers, the more outstanding my articles are, the more thrilling it feels. Introducing a figure who is known to the world is less exciting than discovering and introducing someone new from abroad or somewhere, as if I have found a new universe. Also, when I disguise myself and successfully conduct surveillance without being detected, it brings great enjoyment. For instance, about four years ago, in November, I disguised myself as a servant woman to conduct a survey. Not only did I not get caught, but seeing society from the perspective of being a servant was a completely different experience than that of a reporter, and I found it quite fascinating. While visiting locality trying to gather article materials, they often remain suspicious and treat us as if we were criminals, evading giving the truth, which is very distressing. When comparing the value of an article to the effort and time spent inquiring, nothing is more unpleasant than having a dead-end without any responses.  

Reporter: When handling articles concerning social class or articles related to organizations that have mutual relations, what attitude do you adopt?  

Choi: I generally do not deal with such topics on my appointed boards, as different sections are inherently different. Even if the social section takes up such topics, I just pretend to be unaware.  

Reporter: As a reporter, which type of job do you find to be the most worthwhile?  

Choi: A job that pays decently, along with the presence of freedom of the press, is the most worthwhile for a reporter. If you and I were not suitable for this profession, it’s hard to think what we would have done otherwise. (The flower in her laughter seemed to bloom again.)  

Reporter: Do you often go to theaters?  

Choi: I don't go often, but occasionally I do.  

Reporter: Do you prefer moving pictures or plays?  

Choi: I prefer moving pictures comparatively. As a reporter, I pay attention to article material everywhere I go, but I had a successful experience while watching a moving picture. It was just last June when, during a performance at the National Theater, you and the adherents of the Cheondo religion were being captured. I had gone to Chosen Theater and as I departed, I encountered suddenly a tumult of automobiles, sensing that a major event must be breaking out. At that moment, no other reporters were present. I decided to avoid phones and summoned a rickshaw right away, directly heading to inform Mr. Min Tae-won’s house about the situation to dispatch others to investigate. Then, I informed several reporters, making sure to be the first to publish the extras. While others were being caught swiftly caused a great sensation, at least getting out the extras first was very refreshing. My visits to theaters have brought me such rewards.  

Reporter: Among the reporters in Seoul, who do you think has the best editing skills?  

Choi: Well, I am not sure about others, but based on what I've seen in our company, Mr. Jo Kyu-su is impressive in the political department, while in the social section, it seems like Mr. Hyeon Jin-gyeon. However, for the job of a chief editor, I believe it is probably Min U-boo. (The wind suddenly blew, making her hair scatter in all directions like the clouds after a rainy season.)  

Reporter: Do you foresee continuing as a reporter indefinitely in the future?  

Choi: The profession may become dominated by issues regarding "bread" (income), so I can’t know what will happen. However, if the bread issue stays separate from my life, I would like to continue in this manner. (Seeming playful, she swayed her head and tapped her writing brush on the desk.)  

Reporter: What happened to your plans for travel abroad?  

Choi: Last year, I submitted my application for travel on August 7, and I had barely arranged my travel funds, only to learn on October 27 that it had been denied, so I withdrew. (Even while smiling, this note seemed to sadden her, and a moment of silence fell.)  

Reporter: So, what happened with that money?  

Choi: I spent it all. (Her face brightened again.)  

Reporter: Oh dear, I was a bit mistaken in my questioning; that’s unfortunate.  

Choi: …Hehe…  

Reporter: What kind of books do you enjoy reading?  

Choi: I enjoy reading books related to economics and also fitting in some leisure reading. I truly enjoy your magazine, Byeolgongon, but please don’t include that in your writings. (When saying this, the reporter felt terribly apologetic.)  

Reporter: What kind of food do you like?  

Choi: If I were to eat like Shin Il-sun and say I like “banana," it would be fitting, right? But I prefer fruits and vegetables more than meat, and I consume cold foods more than hot ones. Cold noodles, certainly, ice cream, shaved ice, and anything cold regardless of the season all suit me well.  

Reporter: Is it because you love cold foods that you enjoy them?  

Choi: No, it seems it's because I become easily overheated that I tend to prefer to cool down. (Haha…)  

During this chit-chat, time had already reached 4:20 PM. It seemed the meeting time for the Jin-yook Society was approaching, and she rose to leave.  

**Summary**  

This article presents an interview with Choi Eun-hee, a pioneering female journalist for the Chosun Ilbo in the late 1920s, detailing her life experiences, motivations, and reflections on her career. Notably, it reveals insights into the challenges faced by women in journalism during the colonial period, particularly the societal expectations and barriers they had to navigate. Choi's story provides a window into the struggles and triumphs of women in a male-dominated field, emphasizing the importance of women's voices in Korean media history.
