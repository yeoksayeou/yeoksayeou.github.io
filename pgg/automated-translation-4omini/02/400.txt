Secret Drugs

When a rural visitor has come up, there is little to see, and if you guide them to the motion picture theater, it's a problem. 

"Ha-ri-kke-hat-ji-neun mo-da-sa-i-gul-eul jab-a ta-go hue-rip-pu-ui dwi-reul..." 

Those who understand this only pound their palms, but as for the exciting narration by the benshi, it's likely that they won't understand a word, prompting a common refrain of "My head hurts. Let's go out soon," within ten minutes. 

Nonetheless, even self-proclaimed modern people from Seoul seem not to know the subtlety of the Korean language. In one train carriage, two well-dressed gentlemen sporting fashionable glasses were engaged in a conversation,

"Where did you go?"

"Oh, just a moment, there was a delicious person."

"Hmm, I wonder who you were talking about."

"They said that person speaks pretty well there."

"What did they say? Is it related to that matter?"

"They say that the issue there is done with already."

"How? What happened?"

"That event, needless to say, happened as expected; was it not the first time for that person? And there are already new matters evolving."

"Well, that's true, but still, this and that are a bit different."

"However, that person is saying that this new event should not be disclosed to anyone yet."

From this, one could surmise that the content is somewhat understood, yet also murky to some extent... Regardless, it seems that the hearts of young people nowadays are rather revealing...

  
**Summary**

This article underscores the cultural and linguistic disconnection between modernity and tradition in Korea during the colonial period. It illustrates a scene where individuals, despite being part of a modernized society, struggle to communicate effectively, reflecting a broader commentary on the state of Korean cultural identity. Historians might find the portrayal of cultural norms, hidden discussions regarding societal events, and the ambivalence surrounding modernity in Korea to be particularly revealing in understanding the psychological landscape of colonial subjects.
