Until Magazines, Newspapers, and Printed Materials Reach the Reader's Hand  

Mr. Kim, how trivial can ten coins be? There’s no need to have a headache over it. Rest assured and take your time to read it thoroughly. Nevertheless, ten coins have their value. If you, Mr. Kim, were to spend that money not on this magazine (or book), but on alcohol, cigarettes, or other entertainment, then what headache would you have? An individual who pretends to be knowledgeable and is frugal may buy a magazine for ten coins and murmur about it—what good would that be? Donating or giving it for free is not something that one would complain about repeatedly.

Mr. Kim, you seem knowledgeable, but you ought to enrich your plate before you boast about it. Even though you act like an informed person, you should still gather more knowledge.

Ultimately, do you know what the path and hardships are for all printed materials like newspapers, magazines, and books to reach your hand? Perhaps you don’t know. Generally, one only sees these magazines or newspapers in their currently existing form, without knowing the paths they traversed or how they came to be. Let me reveal it to you. Don’t be flustered.

To discuss the routes taken by printed materials until they reach the reader's hand—first, let me begin with something as trivial as a snack. It won’t be easy to get through this. Truly, there are numerous twists and turns, and untangling the complicated yarn feels just like starting from one point and ending at another—it's truly bewildering and hard to grasp the beginning of the story.

The route of magazines and books, spoken of in metaphorical terms, begins with manuscript paper, which is only one form in itself. The complexities from raw materials to the paper mills, to binding, to the journalist’s workplace, and the twists and turns throughout are hardly lesser headaches than your own. I won't go into details regarding advertising, but generally, this is how it goes.  

Considering how manuscript paper becomes what it is, how much more convoluted is the route of a magazine or book? The manuscript paper goes to the journalists or authors, from the head and thoughts, through the writing tool, to the printing house, then to typesetting, to composition, to proofreading, from lead plates or paper shapes, from printing machines, to binding, and finally to the shipping department, then wrapped and stamped with 1 or 2 penny stamps, loaded into carts, sent to the post office with a seal, transported by automobiles, trains, or even on foot, to reach various corners where readers reside—this process is truly exasperating and arduous.

Mr. Kim, the 4, 5 pages in that magazine, or one paper out of many, the articles on politics and current events, poetry, novels, reflections, travel narratives, and other assorted writings, may seem negligible to you? However, the path taken for a single article or poem to reach the reader is indeed filled with intense waves of turmoil. Do you understand? The efforts made by the author to create a single piece, the challenges faced before even entering the editorial room, are also significant.

In the early days of a month, or as the previous month draws to a close, meetings regarding the next publication’s editing are held. Based on the magazine's declared principles and the important social facts of that time, various themes are discussed to select a representative piece—either an article on current issues, or aspects of cultural literature, or even filling in the margins. After such discussions, tasks are divided among participants: one writes an article, another handles political content, another discusses social matters, etc. After agreeing on a deadline, everyone takes their designated task and dispenses.

Now, observe this. The burden faced by the author of an article is immense, and so is the hardship faced by each person responsible for their respective tasks—it can feel as though they are overwhelmed, staring blankly while considering their ideas, and sometimes meandering through nature for inspiration.

However, the ideas circulating in their heads are only about collecting materials. They think of titles and organization, how to structure and refine their ideas, and the tasks take days to weeks.

Once a rough draft is ready, they must deal with fears: Will this article benefit the readers? Will it provoke counterarguments? If misunderstood, could it harm their reputation? They go through all these fears and anxieties to revise their work multiple times before finally submitting it to the editorial room.

What about those responsible for political and economic content? They meticulously sift through newspapers, review other magazines, visit libraries, and inspect government offices while observing social changes, gathering significant materials before penning down their drafts. Assessing whether events timely or wrongly unfolded, determining the veracity and significance of events is truly difficult work.

What do those preparing social sections experience? They work harder than servants of wealthy households. They go everywhere and see everything—government offices, banks, communities, schools, private homes, parks, theaters, street corners, back alleys, bars, brothels, calamity sites, disaster areas—there is seemingly no place left unseen, no experience untouched before they can write.

After composing the drafts, they must also face various issues like deletion, confiscation, prohibition, defamation, legal challenges, and more. Even fiction or poetry undergoes these same rigorous examinations. Do you understand now?

Once the deadline for submissions is reached, what remains is to finalize the layout and arrangement of the magazine, calculating the number of pages and lines, discussing the cover and table of contents, including issue number and advertisements before finally sending it to the printing house (if following the publishing laws, after obtaining governmental review of the manuscript).

Mr. Kim, do you understand now? It's not as simple as you might think!

When it reaches the printing house, workers in the typesetting department refer to the magazine's table of contents and page numbers, piece by piece preparing a few while passing them to the typesetting section. The typesetters carry out the work according to the listed contents as per the written manuscript. Once typesetting is done, it returns to the typesetting department. Here, the manuscript is compared, and the corrected types are gathered according to the manuscript. After the layout is finished, it is sent to the proofreading room. Proofreaders align with the original text checking for errors.

Once the proofs are ready, they are sent back for typesetting adjustment. It can go through repeated preparations until no typographical errors remain, then it is sent to the lead plate department where lead plates are made (or sometimes directly fed into the machines without them). Afterward, the process of printing takes place on the printing machines. The coordinated operation of several machines can print about four or five thousand to ten thousand copies in a day or two.

Once printing ends, the copies are sent to the binding department. The pages are folded in sequential order, and the binding process begins, yielding batches of 50 or 100 copies which are sent to the distribution department. Here, they start by submitting copies to the Governor-General’s Office or the post office, preparing distribution slips for rapid delivery across the cities or provinces via postal services or railroads.

If unfortunate circumstances occur, such as a confiscation order, distribution can halt, and all copies are collected back. The police may come to seize them. Everyone faces the moment stiffened with sighs before preparing for a special or extraordinary issue.

This arduous journey filled with trials and tribulations leads to the reader's hand. Do you understand how much effort this entails?

Mr. Kim, do you see it clearly now? There’s no need for a headache anymore. What a burdensome ordeal. It’s time to stop. -The End-  

  
**Summary**  

This article elaborates on the intricate processes and significant hardships involved in getting magazines, newspapers, and other printed materials into the hands of readers, starting from manuscript creation to the final distribution. It reflects a broader commentary on labor and the societal implications of publishing during a time of Japanese colonial rule in Korea, where such challenges could imply the difficulties faced by Korean creators under oppressive systems. Historians might find the article revealing in its emphasis on community engagement through the labor of publication, suggesting a blend of resistance and adaptation amid colonial pressures.
