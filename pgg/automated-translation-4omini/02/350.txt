Radio  
Though recently introduced, the microphone used in Prime Minister MacDonald's labor speech in London, England, sent his voice through the airwaves, reaching thousands of people in Moscow two or three years ago. However, in Korea, news of this is still novel.  
Radio, the peak of modern scientific civilization—like light bulbs and microphones—provides a way to feel the world's movements with an uncanny connection. The phrase “I am hungry…,” once it enters the microphone, is transmitted as electrical signals that travel through space and can circle the earth more than seven times in one second—the sounds of world movements! The voice of a politician, the deceit of merchants! The angry shouts of workers—these can be heard, but without a 70-won receiver, they remain unheard.  
When the radio is set up on the outdoor stage and connected to the transmitter, JOAK comes forth. Sounds of geishas singing from Tokyo can be heard. Suddenly, the announcement that rice has gone from 56 cents to 57 cents per bow is relayed. The people wielding sickles claim it is the work of demons. Science is now the new god of modern civilization.  

JODK announces, “This is Seoul’s Post Office.” Immediately followed is Kim Chulwol's song from Namdo: “Oh, white gull, do not fly away…” can be heard. “Uh…” and one opens their mouth wide.  
ROS proclaims, “Workers of the world! We must naturally work only eight hours. Urban workers, first make sure you implement the eight-hour work system, and to local farmers! You are to—three parts to the landowners, six parts to the tenants—what shall be done?” Marx said that the non-propertied should unite. “It’s just as annoying as the croaking of frogs…” This refers to the words of a friend who was seated next to me. Meanwhile, in London, a child may pass the receiver to his mother while exclaiming “Huh?” At the same time, an old man in Shanghai could be heard saying "What's that?" Over in Tokyo, a young lady may question, “What is this?”  

Last spring, a newspaper reporter from America succeeded in sending a report about his exploration in the Arctic back to his newspaper in New York via radio waves. In fact, radio is conquering newspapers; this is a clear fact, no matter how many times I say it.  

But comrades without funds! Can you really spend 90 cents on a newspaper and then pay a similar amount just to listen to the radio? The radio, which operates like newspapers by day and phonographs by night, seems out of reach for us! The relationship between life and radio is still distant, it appears. Civilization—this too must belong to the wealthy. Civilization brings forth something new at a fast pace as it is sold off to the bourgeois. Thus, like a needle, consciousness touches the core of things.  

Civilization is crying out. Radio is crying out. It remains distant from us.  

Lords and ladies, with radio, Park Nokju's gayageum and vocal performance echo beautifully, creating a scene of life amidst the Southern grass. Korean melodies only become a topic of discussion over tea in wealthy homes in Tokyo. So how can we fail to hear the news that relates to our lives?  

Korean radio—civilization—it remains the possession of the conquerors. The day modern civilization falls will also be the day we emerge into a new world of growth.  

The struggle between electricity and electricity! The current struggle is a battle of science—of electricity. It is a struggle to stop the invisible waves sent across vast spaces! The invasion and defense in electricity—that is, the battle of electricity.  

The products of civilization have become mere playthings for the powerful! Indeed, we feel ashamed of science.  

Yet, I read in a wireless magazine that in a Russian household, an old man became tense as he listened to Stalin's speech about the countryside from Moskova, so enthralled that he wouldn’t even stir as his young son asked, “Father, may I listen too?” This imagery struck me profoundly.  
Lenin once said, “In the future, the world I desire is a world of electricity.”  

Sports  
It costs a large sum of one won to watch a baseball game.  

This is worth some thought. While sports undoubtedly contain elements of leisure and competition, the extent to which even health-promoting exercise is infiltrated by capitalism is debatable.  

The commercialization of sports has led to the enslavement of sportsmen—when sports venues become gambling arenas, it becomes rather disturbing. The stakes on home runs and strikes often reach thousands of yen, draining funds like a vacuum.  

In Japan, baseball alone seems to take precedence over all other sports, while here in Korea, such events are barely on the horizon. Comparatively, back in the days of the headman era, young women in the neighborhood dated army lieutenants; the current situation sees university teams flaunting their prowess. Truly, the psychology of people aiming to follow new trends, eras, and cultures is truly inscrutable.  

A famous American boxer, Semubushi, earned millions by boxing, and the fact that a Japanese tennis player, Harada, became engaged to a certain viscount’s daughter illustrates this very fact. 

The income of athletes surpasses that of university professors, and film actors even earn more than the president’s annual salary—this illustrates the latent entrenchment of capitalism within competitive sports.  

Look at the conflict over titles at boxing matches! In a recent seven-year span, Semubushi amassed 860,000 yen. However, in a humorous twist, he later lost because it was reported his wife was a top actress. Modern girls dream of dating those who embody a wild spirit, and Semubushi represents that ideal.  

During international games, millions of yen flood in from the railroads—the gross extravagance of the wealthy.  

On the other hand, we struggle in the fields. Remember that time in Gyeongseong when our military faced off with some American team in a friendly match? The stark difference between the ease of those who grew fat on rich meat and the hearty voices of those nurtured on kimchi and saltwater fish is palpable. The triumph of wealth sits intricately in this dichotomy.  

The commercialization of sports, the enslavement of sportsmen—this is the product we see in this phenomenon. Many sportsmen seem shackled to their owners, namely the capitalists behind the scenes, while they bring home trophies and accolades, ultimately thwarting their existence in this grip. (Though it may be different for university teams)  

When sporting events occur, half the newspaper headlines are filled with reports on them, making it a significant cultural phenomenon—what a perplexing matter!  

Whether it be news of breaking world records or captures of world championships, it results in the emergence of a new culture. Yet, we hope for a future when all can freely participate in sports and enjoy any activity among the public. In such times, the memories of spending one won to watch baseball will seem like a distant dream.  

The commercialization of sports and the ensuing enslavement of the athlete are hallmark issues that we can observe in this phenomenon. Will the day come for all this to change?  

Cinema  
In reality, film has conquered the novel.  

And because of this, novels tend to be intellectual and analytical, while films possess the capability to demonstrate actions that can be understood beyond mere thought through visuals.  

Economically, one can access a plethora of stories for just 30-40 cents by enjoying films and catching the essence of full expandable motions of events in minimal time.  

In Korea, the cinema produced by our hands (excluding money) has been active for less than three years, but over fifty different productions have been made. Without the funding for theaters, we see films generating decent audiences when a cheap 5-yen ticket appears. It’s heartwarming when a 5-yen ticket sells as “Ah, this is fine art!” “This is splendid!” in the alleys of modest houses. Thus, the Korean film, which consumed up to a thousand won, finally premieres at a theater like the Korean Theaters.  

And as the theatre crowd increases, the joyous atmosphere swells. The actors experience success and fame in a bustling film environment. Yet we must ponder—what have we seen in these backgrounds for ourselves?  

The few examples worth mentioning are adaptations of historical works like “Chang Hen Mong” and “Gong Jung Bird”.  

However, many directors wear shabby clothes and struggle to get proper meals before the audience. Tears, blood, and sighs collectively flow from the film; meanwhile, they, accordingly, have only ever been to make ends meet.  

But personally, it’s disheartening to see the limited originality due to appeasing the tastes of capitalist investors, leading to low-quality renditions of “Chang Hen Mong” and “Gong Jung Bird”.  

What about the viewers? Do Korean fans tend to have deeply lined pockets? A considerable time ago, there were four regular cinemas open, and when tickets were 10 dollars, those who didn’t usually see them were thrilled to come. I have paid 10 cents to watch films in my youth, but these days, it seems impossible to find a theater that offers a 10-yen ticket anywhere in Korea. However, it’s a fine development. Thus, people rather than wealth are beginning to attend the films, signaling a shift in social viewing among the common folk.  

Yet Ronnachi's weekly salary could afford at least five films in our land—what a stark contrast is painted here!  

However, there is a revolutionary product appearing—Ariang.  

Accidentally bumming around in school, one stumbles upon the spoil of reality where it continues to shine.  

The rags of life in rural Korea show up, with the protagonists dancing in traditional clothes. Despite the absence of exquisite craftsmanship, there is a sorrowful and chilling reflection at play.  

Though the film industry of Japan held decades of history, its magic cannot match Korean films’ compelling emotional resonance. When I hear of Japan’s so-called new movies, I have observed no true artistic value in them. The lack of lighting and settings is something we cannot overlook.  

In just two years within the Korean film industry, these achievements are commendable! Ultimately, this “Ariang” film stands as a giant that will burn and erase all previous works of Korean cinema.  

Though there may be no worthy representation beyond films for which we can swiftly reveal to our audience.  

Noting the cheap costs associated with films, let’s hope for longevity.  

Korean culture will gradually find its way into film. The age of cinema has begun—Ariang stands alone. And what else will our film industry show us?  

Modern civilization revolves around radio, sports, and cinema. Will these forms of civilization ever grow closer to us?—The End  

**Summary**  

This article discusses the introduction and impact of radio, sports, and cinema in Korea during the 1920s, examining how these modern forms of entertainment are both enticing and economically exploited by capitalists. The author critiques how these advancements remain largely inaccessible to the general populace, illustrating a common theme in colonial Korea where the wealthy benefit from modern innovations while the majority suffer from economic disparity. Historians might find the reflections on cultural imperialism and class struggle pertinent when analyzing the socio-economic conditions of colonized Korea.
