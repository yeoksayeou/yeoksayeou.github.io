Ddonggeulangttaeng!  

As the autumn leaves fall in the chilly wind at the head of the Xinyang River (潯陽江), if Bai Juyi (白樂天) were alive, he would hear the burning sound of the pipa played by the capital girl (京城女) resonating from the riverside and would sing, "I’ll write a pipa song for you, so play another tune for me." On this languid autumn day, as you wander through the narrow alleys of Cheongnyangsa (淸凉寺) near Dongdaemun (동대문) where my beloved has departed,  

you will see a shabby beggar, dressed in ragged clothes stained with dirt, limping along with crutches, singing desperately with a trembling, half-muted voice, pleading for a penny.  

The old beggar sways as he sings this song:  

Ddonggeulangttaeng! Ddonggeulangttaeng!  
The sweetgum tree in the back mountain  
is used as a bat for the little lady's red pots.  

Ddonggeulangttaeng! Ddonggeulangttaeng!  
The grandmother flower in the back mountain  
is twisted whether old or young.  

Ddonggeulangttaeng! Ddonggeulangttaeng!  

Although his voice is quite poor, he sings it with great satisfaction, nodding his head as he continues.  

After singing a few verses of this song, he shifts the theme.  

Ddonggeulangttaeng! Ddonggeulangttaeng!  
Oh, you guests! Oh, you guests!  
Look upon my plight! Look upon my plight!  

Even I, on previous days, was a young man enjoying the good life with wealthy companions.  
When I was in my prime, I was happily paired with a beautiful girl,  

Oh mother, oh mother!  
Will a new daughter-in-law be brought home?  
Catch the mask and give it to me;  
For thirty years I've endured these seasons alone.  

Until now, even the guests who had been smiling had their faces turn serious and solemn as the song progressed.  

The beggar continues with even more sorrowful tones.  

My home, my home, is full of talented brothers,   
But now we barely get by,  
Selling off whatever we could,  
I have no purpose in this life,  
And as I think of my wife,  
My heart aches.  

I wander without knowing where to go.  
Three years ago, I entertained a group of guests,  
Having fun, laughing;  
I recognized the joyful gathering and pretended to know them.  
But my wife seemed like a stranger to me,  
And I thought it an encounter.  

I could only sigh,  
Wishing I could give my sickly brother-in-law some snacks  
And then return to the same place  
To exchange greetings once more,  
But my brother-in-law's weak body  
Reminds me painfully of our past.  

Ddonggeulangttaeng! Ddonggeulangttaeng!  

The song ended here. Was it the ramblings of a lunatic? Or the lingering sounds of sorrow? The gazes of the guests all turned towards the beggar's face, and yet he wore a true sense of some feeling on his face, as though he was genuinely holding onto something. Here and there, ten-won coins and five-won coins dropped, one by one into the beggar's pocket.  

**Summary**  

This article describes a beggar singing mournfully in the streets of Seoul during the 1920s. His lyrics reflect nostalgia and a lament for lost youth and prosperity. The juxtaposition of his past life and current hardship illustrates the struggles of individuals under Japanese colonial rule, revealing the deeper social issues of the time. Historians might find interest in the way the song encapsulates the emotional toll of colonialism and poverty, highlighting the human experience in that era.
