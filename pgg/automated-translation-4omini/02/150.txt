Everything Seen is a Worldly Treasure: Observations from a Trip to France  
Dr. Jeong Sik-tae  

Having arrived in Paris, I still feel unsettled, lacking a clear objective or direction, and I am still unsure about what these foreigners are doing here. My companions and I gathered again today to plan our route, and among our group, S, a friend of N, came to guide us. Having lived in Paris for at least six years, he is quite the local expert. We decided to rely entirely on him to navigate and sought his guidance regarding the map.

As if it were a stroll through the park, S guided us as if he had no lack of skill. We first visited the Paris University buildings. Since everything was new to me, the explanations offered by S sparked endless curiosity every time he described the architecture and facilities. Among the things that particularly astonished me was a museum known as Musée de Cluny, located near Paris University. The building, while not very large, is quite ancient. S further stimulated our interest by guiding us through the museum where I saw treasures I could only dream of.

One such item that baffled me was a "chastity belt," a peculiar device for female virtue. At first glance, it seemed like an odd belt with an ivory protrusion, designed for restraint. I learned that it had been in use since the Napoleonic era, and it was employed by warriors or merchants who would leave their concubines behind, worried about their fidelity in their absence, securely locking them inside while they carried the key. This was indeed a startling observation for me based on Eastern moral standards, revealing much about the Western circumstances regarding trust and the freedom of couples to such an extreme.

Additionally, there were many exquisite artifacts that I found fascinating, but I decided to defer further explorations to another day. As we were preparing to leave the museum, S mentioned that I could buy postcards and photographs, and I ended up purchasing two pictures of the chastity belts, planning to use them as conversation starters when I return home.

The World Famous Arc de Triomphe and Eiffel Tower  
What we wanted to see didn’t follow a set order; we went wherever we found ourselves. By chance, we soon arrived at the Arc de Triomphe. Before witnessing it, I had no expectations of its grandeur, and upon seeing it in person, I could hardly contain my admiration for its magnificence. It comprises 264 steps, standing at a height of 160 feet and 164 feet wide, making it the world’s largest Arc de Triomphe. Constructed in 1806 after Napoleon's decisive victory over the Austrian army, it was completed during the reign of Louis-Philippe.

Surrounding the arch are inscriptions of the names of 650 officers who died in the Napoleonic wars from 1792 to 1815, including during the Revolutionary period. Initially, it was closed to the public, but on November 28, 1918, during a visit by the British Emperor George V, it was opened to crowds, marking a significant occasion in history. Therefore, our ability to admire it is in some sense due to the British Emperor’s goodwill. As we climbed the staircase, we reached a large open square, which also featured postcard vendors capturing our attention. From here, the panorama was truly breathtaking.

Among the sights stood the Eiffel Tower, even taller and more striking than the Arc de Triomphe. The Eiffel Tower! Visitors to Paris must see both the Arc and this tower. The tower was designed by engineer Eiffel and took only three years to complete from 1887 to 1889. It provides stunning views not only of Paris but even as far as the surrounding suburbs 55 kilometers away. The tower consists of three levels, with 30 nods on the first, 60 on the second, and 150 on the third level, towering 165 meters above the ground. As for the third floor, it serves as a radio transmission station.

Interestingly, during the war, when German forces advanced ominously close to Paris, Kaiser Wilhelm prepared to hoist a large German flag—many times larger than the aria—on this tower after capturing Paris, indicating the extent of their ambitions.

How Is Transportation in Paris?  
Whatever one observes, the perspective one has will vary, and since we had been strolling without hesitation, our group headed back to the accommodation as the sun began to set. I was unaware of how transportation actually works, but we frequently noticed the abundance of automobiles. Moreover, the fares were surprisingly inexpensive. Interestingly, it turned out that hiring a car was cheaper for four people than taking the tram, defying our expectations. 

I had yet to experience the tram, but I assumed it was bustling and complex. The tram system uses underground electricity due to its surrounding environment, while the overhead lines are all absent, which was rather interesting. As a rural newcomer, I was left questioning how the tram operated in such a manner. Next, we used the subway, commonly known as "Métro," which supposedly has double the number of passengers compared to above-ground transport, and its efficiency and low fares became apparent in my understanding.

The National Opera!  
After a brief dinner on our return, we set off around 7 PM to the renowned "Opera." As soon as our car reached the Opera entrance, a couple of young ladies rushed to our vehicle, opening the door to usher us inside. I thought to myself that such service was quite friendly for a world-famous opera house, but soon realized they were seeking tips from arriving patrons. As foreigners, we felt obliged to give them something to preserve our gentlemanly image, even though it was quite uncomfortable.

The Opera, built under the guidance of Charles Garnier between 1861 and 1875, features stunning architecture. In the center, there is a museum and a library, accented by portraits of famous composers and dancers hanging everywhere. Additionally, I learned that on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays, only attendees in formal attire are admitted, suggesting that casual dress is frowned upon here. Tickets for the lower levels were incredibly competitive, and one had to book at least five days in advance to secure a spot.

We managed to obtain seats in the second class, which turned out to be quite expensive, costing about 7 franc. Upon entering, although the grandeur of the surroundings was overwhelming, I soon realized the actual performance was less engaging. The piece performed was "Carmen," somewhat unfamiliar to me. Since I didn't understand the language, I wasn't greatly entertained, though I found observing the audience highly intriguing.

As we departed, I reflected on how, despite the few days spent touring, I had barely grasped the essence of Paris's charm. The bustling streets had quieted down, and I was left alone in my room, writing. Now it is 11:20 PM. I couldn’t help but think of my homeland at this hour. The time difference of about eight hours means it is around 7:30 AM back home, as everyone there prepares breakfast and rushes to start their work. Meanwhile, here, I lie down, hoping to sleep peacefully for the night. I ponder whether these contrasting lifestyles exist across the globe.

Is the Siberian railroad still inoperable? The letter I am writing now will need to cross the Pacific, traverse the Americas, then cross back over the Pacific, passing through Japan before reaching Korea. It seems likely to take at least 25 days. Thus, a reply to this letter may take about two months. Being in such a focused and solitary state makes me deeply reflective. Suddenly realizing that sentiments are unnecessary, I thought of lying down for sweet dreams, awaiting tomorrow.

The Encounter with Young Eastern Students and French Women!  
Visiting historical sites and museums requires a certain degree of specialized knowledge. As a layperson, I find many treasures of little value, though comprehending their significance becomes a challenge. As I walked through the central market to shop, something noteworthy struck me: Eastern young men strolling alongside French women as if married couples. After gathering more details, I learned that many such unions existed—a trend noticeably common among Chinese students and French women.

Despite the historical context, the wounds from the European War rendered many French men absent, prompting women to seek solace and companionship from foreign men without hesitation in forming families. This observation left me pondering deeper societal issues regarding their circumstances of necessity and emotional distress that could lie behind their decisions.

Visiting the Pasteur Institute!  
My profession is in medicine, and now that I’m in Paris, a place so connective to the field, I could not miss visiting the Pasteur Institute, synonymous with the legacy of Louis Pasteur himself—a giant in medical science whom everyone associates with France. So I made my way to the Pasteur Institute, which was evidently still conducting research as he had intended.

Following a thorough explanation through the research rooms, I emerged to find the tomb of Pasteur and learn about his life’s work framed around me. I was taken aback by how it had been so elaborately arranged, paying tribute to a man whose contributions had immeasurably benefited humanity.

The Skull of Koch  
I decided to head to the library and was shown numerous carved portraits depicting many medicinal achievements from various pivotal figures in history. While each person's story was fascinating, time flew by, and not everything could be retained in memory. Among the notable items was the skull of Robert Koch, who devoted his life to advancing medical knowledge and astonished the world with his groundbreaking discoveries. His skull is still housed within this institute, a testament to his legacy.

I learned that Koch had made it known in his will that his remains should be displayed here after his passing, intended to inspire future generations. Each time I gazed upon that skull, it was more than just an exhibit; it was a deep reflection on a life so dedicated to bettering humanity. Perhaps inspired by Koch's unique choice, a contemporary Japanese anatomist named Ōsawa, while nearing death, had intended to have himself dissected for educational purposes. To this day, individuals like them remain highly regarded in the field of medicine.

**Summary**  

The article provides an insightful travelogue by Dr. Jeong Sik-tae about his experiences and reflections in Paris during the 1920s, where he observes both cultural artifacts and social nuances. Among his discoveries are chastity belts, significant historical sites like the Arc de Triomphe and the Eiffel Tower, and the Pasteur Institute, showcasing the intersection of history and contemporary life. What's particularly fascinating is the author's reflections on mixed unions symbolizing broader social changes in post-war France, shedding light on the dynamics of gender and society in colonial discussions, which may intrigue historians studying the effects of the war on families and gender relations in Europe.

世界名物 凱旋門과 엣후엘 塔
우리들의 보고저하는 바는 향방이 업고 순서가 업다. 임시임시 발 가는대로 가는곳이 즉 凱旋門 이엿스니 말은 발서부터 만히 듯고 그 장한 바는 미리 짐작지 안은바는 안이엿스나 막상 당하고 보니 그 웅장함에 칭송치 안을수가 업다. 계단수는 실로 264이엿고 놉히는(高) 160척 폭이 164척인 세계 제1위의 凱旋門이라. 이는 奈翁 제1세가 墺國軍을 여지업시 격파를 한 후에 1806년에 기공이 되여 「루이, 후리릿프」시대에 竣工이 된 것인데 그 주위로 도라가면서는 佛國革命時代부터 평화할 때까지 즉 1792년부터 1815년에 이르기까지 奈翁時代 전사한 장교 650인의 일홈과 조각이 잇다. 이곳은 본시 개방치 안이하여서 諸人은 관람치를 못하엿든 바엿는데, 英國皇帝 「찌요지」 제 5세가 歐洲戰亂 平和條約 調印 후 즉, 1918년 11월 28일 巴里 방문을 기회로 하엿든 바 환영의 의미로서 1870년 이래 폐쇄하엿든 바를 개방케 된 바이다. 그러니 우리가 구경하게 되는 바도 간접으로 英帝의 덕이라 할 수 있다. 계단을 따라 우흐로 올나간 즉 백평이나 되는 널분 광장이 잇다. 이곳서는 그림엽서 파는 색씨들이 잇서 눈을 끄을게 한다. 이곳서 시가를 望觀한 즉 「파노라마」와 가티 眼下에 전개되는 바이다. 其中에서도 멀니 「엣후엘」 탑은 이보다 더 놉히 눈에 띄운다.

巴里交通機關은 엇더하냐!!
무슨 물건이든지 관찰자의 정도를 따라 한정이 업는 바이라, 볼사록 한이 업시 주저만 할 수도 업고 임이 석양도 느즘을 따라 일행은 숙소로 향하엿다. 이로소는 교통이라고 얼마나 種別이 잇는지는 몰으지만은 우리는 頻頻히 자동차의 경선를 빈다. 더구나 그 요금의 싼 것은 참으로 놀내일수 밧게 업다. 4인이 전차를 타는 것보다, 自動車 1대를 사용하는 것이 싼 고로 예측 이상이라고 할 수 밧게 업다.

國立 오페라!!
일행이 도라오는 도중에서 夕飯은 간단히 맛추고 7시경에 유명한 「오페라」로 향하게 되엿다. 우리 자동차가 오페라 정문에 도착이 되자마자 한 두명의 처녀들이 달겨들어 자동차의 문을 열고 정문으로 안내를 한다. 혼자 생각으로 세계적 오페라 이닛가 接客조차 이리 친절한가 하엿더니 이들은 또는 멧푼밧기 위하여 오페라 정문에서 오는 客에게 이리 求乞하는 것 인줄 알게 되엿다. 우리는 외국인이라 체면을 보아서도 엇절수가 업시 얼마를 주고보니 紳士의 체면을 차즈랴면, 몸도 퍽 괴로운 모양이다. 이 오페라는 「칼니이」 지도하에 1861년으로 1875＜43＞년간에 되엿는데 건축이 예술적으로 되어 중앙에는 박물관과 도서관이 잇고, 유명한 작곡가와 음악가며 「딴스」의 명인 등의 肖像이 處處에 걸녀잇다. 또 드른즉 이곳은 月水金 삼일간은 예복이 안이고는 입장불허라고 옷업서 구경도 못할 곳이 이곳인줄을 짐작케 된다. 3등4등은 발서 만원이다. 적어도 사오일전에 예매치 안으면 어들 수 엄다한다. 우리들의 특청으로 2등의 자리를 엇게되여 환산하니 7원 가령이나 엄청나는 표 갑시다.
막상 입장하고 보면 별로히 신기한 맛은 업스나 그 웅장하고 설비에 可觀됨은 다시 닛지 못할 만 하엿다. 劇題는 「칼멘」이라는 것인데 우리가 아는 바와는 매우 다른 모양이요. 말조차 알지를 못하니 별로히 취미는 업섯스나 오히려 보러온 관객을 보는데 우리는 정신이 업섯다. 흔히 말을 들을거 가트면 어느 나라를 가던지 그 나라의 文物程度와, 인물정체를 알고저 하면 「오페라」에 집중되는 바를 보면 알수가 잇다한다. 그러면 佛國 대표 될 만한 신사숙녀는 이 자리에 잇슴과, 그들의 유행인 의복제도도 이곳서 알 수 잇다. 하여간 맛추는 막을 조치, 제각기 숙소로 도라오니 오히려

「메치니코흐」의 骸骨
나는 다시 도서관으로 안내를 바더 드러가니 전 좌우로 조각으로 된 의학계의 공적을 낏친 수십명의 형적이 잇서 일일이 그 내력을 듯자면, 시간이 오해 될 뿐만 안이라, 다 두뇌에 기억좃차되지 안는다. 그 중에서 소개할 만한 것은 이 硏究所에서 일생에 盡力을 가티하여 의학계의 큰 유익을 주고 세인을 놀나게 하든 즉 「메치니 코흐」해골이 아직도 圖書室 한 편을 차지하고 잇다. 그의 내력을 들을 것 가트면  「메치니 코흐」가 臨終에서 이르러 자기가 사망한 후 이면은 그 해골을 파 스를 도서실에 장치하여 달나는 유언으로 지금까지 이와가티 된 모양인데, 나와가티 그 이의 해골을 볼적마다, 단순한 관람으로는 생각이 되지 안을 것이다. 적어도 그이의 일생의 고생하여 오든 공적을 감탄을 마지안을 동시에 저절로 그이에게 경의를 표하여 머리를 숙이지 안을 수가 업다. 자기의 해골을 이와가티하여 달나함도 특유한 그의 생각이엇스나, 그는 오날 죽엇지만 영원한 후배로서는 항상 그를 사모치 안을 수가 업다. 이를 모방함인지 日本 解剖學 大家大澤이란 이가 임종에 이르러서 자기를 해부하여 표본을 만들나 하엿슴으로 그 후 부터는 東北大學에 영원히 장치가 되엿스니 이런이는 다 금일까지 의학계에서 경의를 밧치는 바이다.＜46＞
