기사제목	언제든지 의심스런 景品附 大賣出 內容 이약이 (Anytime Doubtful Promotions With Prize-Linked Large Sales)  
기사형태	소식 (News)

Anytime a suspicious promotion is linked with large sales, there is a warning.

The contents are something that need to be noted as they are not simply trifling. Spring flowers and autumn's good harvest were once beyond reach, but as December approaches, every department store in Seoul begins the hustle and bustle of the so-called 'Combined Prize Promotions'. 

Each store decorates with flowers, flies scarlet banners, and puts up splendid advertisements proclaiming that they will give gifts for the season... Even in streets shadowed by gloom and the lament of poverty, it offers a glimmer of hope that appears even to us poor salarymen from time to time. However! “If you buy just one won's worth, you get a prize ticket…” The longing in the families at home becomes even more palpable as they bear the lonely lives of routine, also showing gratitude for these moments that are mere distractions for our hearts and eyes and ears.

“If you buy just one won’s worth, we will give you a prize ticket.”  

“And if you draw the lottery, if you win first place, you will receive a mother-of-pearl three-tiered cabinet. There is no question about it.”  

With banners flowing and palanquins (거) set swinging, it feels like a festive day, especially as women flock in merrily. 

“Oh, who will take that mother-of-pearl cabinet?”
“If we buy only one won's worth and receive something like that for free, it would indeed be a stroke of enormous luck...”  

During the excitement of giving and receiving, one often hears comments like: 

“Give what? They only offer a mere pencil for advertising.”  
“Of course! What kind of world are we living in where a cabinet is offered for a single won's worth of goods? They do it to attract people and just display their goods here!”  
“Oh, look! That person must be taking home the mirror stand!”  
“That mirror stand looks suspiciously cheap; it’s likely one of those that they just give away without a real prize.”  

The suspicions voiced by the women and the men alike seem to echo from one ear to another in a relay of distrustful murmurs. 

Could this be true? Or, if there are scams at play, then surely the words and actions of those women could give rise to doubts in the minds of men as well, leading to hesitation in purchasing, even if the goods are cheap. 

Ah! There is no need to find a representative to clear the matter for us; we should understand these complex situations ourselves! The gentleman gathered around, holding a brush, immediately began to talk with great intensity.

“Sometimes there are large sales using prizes, but some customers spread rumors that the prizes for the first and second places are merely lies and tricks. Is this a possibility?”  

“Absolutely not! That is just a matter of misunderstanding. There can be no such thing! When the prize tickets are prepared, whether it’s tens of thousands, we always draw them under supervision of the police, and the number of winning tickets is always made public.”  

“Could tickets for the first and second prizes or even the sixth or seventh ticket be hidden? Of course not. How can that be?”  

Then there arises questions around what the stores are doing to bear the costs of these prizes. When they sell goods, they are said to include extra costs for the prizes, suggesting that the prices are higher when they announce prizes. It raises a point that if you buy something expensive hoping for a great prize, and end up with a postcard or only a pencil, it doesn’t seem fair!  

“That might sound like anxious talk from customers who don't understand what’s going on,” he replied. “But really, you can absolutely verify this through personal purchases. It is not the first time you buy something, and how could that be possible?”  

“Furthermore, the money used to supply all those flashy prizes does indeed come at a substantial cost; while it is assumed the stores bear this, that isn't the case. Capitalists do not supply the funds directly; rather, they receive subsidies from other places.”  

“Packets of thousands of woes come from bank conventions as well. That money is utilized for both prizes and advertising. Each store buys prize tickets for funds. High-selling stores will lack tickets while smaller stores will find themselves with surplus, so they agree to buy from the auction of an average amount, paying four jeon for each prize ticket, and in this case, each store may not incur higher costs for their goods together.”  

“Thus, during these prize promotions, sales can increase threefold against other times.”

“Ah! Now I understand.” 

“Could you please share your observations regarding the promotional activity? How much more was sold?” 

“Yes, it is planned three times a year—once in spring, autumn, and once at the end of the year. The amount sold last year was as follows: In last spring, 80,000 won; in last autumn, 120,000 won; last December, 230,000 won; this spring, 180,000 won; during the summer special event, 300,000 won; in the autumn of this year, 150,000 won was sold. This means we have sold considerably more than during periods without prizes.”  

“I understand. Has there been any sentiment shared regarding these sales?”  

“Well, there’s really nothing much to note of sentiments as such. However, we must say that to force this, it feels somewhat tearful. The plight of our Korean commerce keeps dragging on. Even for small amounts of goods purchased, we look abroad, and foreign merchants are increasingly active in accumulating our Korean funds. This cannot continue; we cannot endure this.” 

“Yet we still believe that even by uniting together, we can at least strive to keep our customers and halt the ongoing deterioration of our northern commercial viability.”  

“Such acts are undeniably vital, not only for our shopkeepers but for our Korean customers to support this cause. Thus, I sincerely wish that each one of you will share and explain the matter accordingly.”  

“Indeed. We must promote these matters so that the mutual benefit is apparent to all of us and it ultimately serves a bigger purpose for us.”  

The gentleman spoke and left, noting that from December 16th, there will be a start to promotional sales with prizes.

  

**Summary**

This article presents a critique of promotional sales strategies using prizes as incentives, reflecting the broader struggles of Korean merchants under colonial rule. It captures the mixed emotions of individuals trapped in poverty who are at once drawn to these promotions for hope but are also deeply skeptical due to persistent dishonesty in marketing. Historians may find it interesting as it highlights the complex relationship between colonial economic pressures and local enterprise, showcasing how merchants tried to combat foreign exploitation while navigating the challenging market environment imposed upon them.
