기사제목	記者總出動 京城白晝暗行記 (Reporters Mobilized: A Daytime Dark Journey in the Capital)  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

The great capital is bustling. Society is stirring constantly. “Gather all the phenomena of the capital’s activity!” With the mobilization order issued, a group of reporters rushed out in five vehicles. The time was from 2 PM to 3 PM on November 15.  

Reporters: Songjak, Chunbo, Seokgye, Boyeong, Iseong  

I, having arrived at the district court, requested they give me lunch. In the past, Zhang Fei's military order was for three days, but our editorial room's order was for just one hour. And this hour was strictly limited to the period from 2 PM to 3 PM. After much hesitation, I thought, “Where should I go and what should I investigate?” I eventually decided to head to the courthouse, a place always crowded with people. Upon visiting the courthouse, I found that it was remarkably quiet for an afternoon, with hardly anyone inside. 

In the public prosecutor’s office, there were merely two Japanese men selling stamps and bread, and a moneylender who had come for a bidding application. The witnesses from a forestry lawsuit were rural folks called in. In the defense attorneys’ office, two Korean lawyers sat playing Go with their socks on, making a dull sound as they placed their stones.

Why was the courthouse so desolate today? I went to the prosecutor’s office. There, too, it didn’t seem lively. A rural woman in a gray jacket stood there sobbing, calling toward a man in a black hat with her eyes fixated on the detention room, asking, “Is that child not out yet?” 

Despite her crying and the awkwardness of intruding, I approached the woman to inquire about her situation. She seemed unaware of my position and tearfully replied, “Please, save my child. He’s a 15-year-old boy from Cheongju, and he was caught watching some kids gambling with two coins. I was hoping to see him today.” I reassured her, “I’m not from the courthouse, but don’t worry. If he hasn’t committed a crime, it won’t matter. Nevertheless, even just watching such gambling can be serious.” 

The woman then requested, “Even if you’re not from the courthouse, you can speak Japanese well. Please, just request them to give him a little something to eat.” I could feel both her innocence and my sympathy for her plight. I agreed to speak with the guard about it. After some back and forth, the policeman tersely responded that it wasn’t his concern. I then went to the Japanese officer at the detention room and conveyed the woman’s request. To my relief, he was quite gentle and agreed without much resistance. 

I returned to the woman and told her she could buy something if she had the money. She replied, “I want to buy some rice cakes for him. Just give me a bit of money,” and we moved on. In this manner, the hour slipped by swiftly. 

Having collected little in terms of story material, I departed the courthouse and noted it was precisely 3:10 PM.  

---

“Is one hour of city exploration interesting? But where to go and what to see? The station? The library? No, that won’t do. It’s not the right time. The wealthy’s home? The bank? The company? Ah, let’s check out the home of a wealthy person near Dongdaemun. Maybe this will be intriguing.” 

I mused to myself while heading toward the alley of School Cave. I debated whether to take a tram or walk to the street, finally concluding to walk down but take the tram back, allowing me to enjoy both the street experience and the comfort of the tram ride. 

I quickly encountered three notable events. A water removal truck passed by, and a rural man, drenched in mud from the freshly washed road, appeared ready to reprimand a young lady nearby. 

As I passed Donggwan, I glanced into someone’s house and noticed, “Aha! A major event!” A hen was being chased by a child into the other room, dislodging a basket on the porch, amidst great commotion. 

While continuing on, I approached the area near Dongdaemun and came across a site bustling with dozens of onlookers gathered around. “Could it be that this is where the shaman is?” I thought, amidst the excitement. 

I squeezed through the crowd and found an impressive-looking man in formal wear speaking attractively. 

He was presenting some sort of remedies, proclaiming the benefits of dung! 

“Indeed, it’s renowned for treating maladies! Truly fascinating! Come now!” He declared, calling for attention. “Human, cow, chicken, rat dung, it’s all here for your benefit! 

It seems dung can do wonders for ailments! Have you ever wondered what cow dung is used for? It can be ground into powder and used for children’s issues! 

How about dog dung? It’s perfect for wounds! Pig dung? Ideal for women’s issues! Even tiger dung promotes longevity! 

And remember, rat dung is effective for sudden childhood illnesses!” 

While he rambled on about the wonders of dung, the audience gawked in disbelief. 

With not much time left, I turned back, having observed this incredible event, and made my way to the home of a wealthy person in Changsin-dong near Dongdaemun. 

Upon arriving at the house of the wealthy family, well-known in Seoul, I could do nothing but gaze in awe from outside. I felt completely daunted and went back, leaving without stepping two feet inside.

Bookstore Tour in One Hour  

Human mouths are one, yet the sounds they produce are numerous. Often, one can hear sounds of food, clothes, and liquor among a plethora of others. Yet the most delightful sound is that of reading. It conveys the person’s thoughts and reveals their cultural levels.  

On November 15th, I took advantage of the cold winds heralding the first winter chill and aimed to gauge the current literature enthusiasm and to see what books people are reading within an hour from 2 PM to 3 PM. I began at Tapgol Park, proceeding to Anguk-dong, visiting the prominent large bookstores along the route. 

Each store appeared adequately equipped and displayed a decent atmosphere, yet I was disheartened to see hardly any customers buying books during my brief visit. 

Judging by the time from 2 to 3 PM, the workers, perhaps from morning experiences, seemed listless, counting receipts or absent-mindedly playing with abacuses, simply waiting for time to pass. 

Occasionally, a student might pop by to browse through the newest magazines but often left without making a purchase, whereas rural customers would only inquire about novels before departing without buying anything. 

Observing the bookstores, I decided to inquire about the average daily sales and what types of books were bestsellers. The answers were generally consistent: they hadn't tracked exact sales numbers but noted that popular magazines included “Children’s Magazine,” “New Youth,” “Byeolgeon-gon,” and titles like “Society,” along with novels such as “Chunhyangjeon” and “Simcheongjeon."  

Despite the vibrant sound of voices typically associated with books, listening felt almost contrary. It was more like a dreary sound compared to lively discussions, and the atmosphere suggested a dying spirit in the world of books. 

We must contemplate how to rekindle the charm of literary discourse, dear friends involved in writing, selling, and reading. Let’s endeavor to find ways to allow new and delightful sounds to emerge, both for our mouths and ears.

Exploring the Newspaper Office in One Hour  

When setting out to understand the day’s sentiments through the newspaper office, one might expect to read easy and pleasing articles without much effort while blending in among the crowd coming from the office.  

As I left the office, I worried someone might take my chosen destination first. I declared, “I’m going to the newspaper office,” establishing my territory and went north.  

“Do you recognize the letters on the Dong-A Ilbo (東亞日報社) sign?” I asked people, and though some could barely recognize it, the sign had survived the test of time and weather, bearing the history of this newspaper uniquely.  

Upon opening the door to the editorial room, it looked much the same: an endless flow of paper was filled with drafts, some waiting to be discarded or chewed up by an editor.  

The desks and floors were littered with crumpled papers, a chaotic sea of unfinished writings and drafts waiting to be completed. Each editor was working furiously, with the ticking clock suggesting it was already 2:30 PM—a crucial time for the newspaper.  

I hesitated, reading an unfinished article titled “Young Man with a Pistol Threatens Rich.” “Did anything major happen?” I inquired. They responded casually, “Yes, it happened in the countryside.” 

A nihilistic air permeated the room, with reporters busy scribbling away while grabbing materials from paper strewn about. I noticed one editor browsing a manuscript about “Women Entering Bedrooms,” while another worked on a sensational piece about a “Fire in 700 Factories.”  

“What significant fire could that be?” I asked, and among the noise, a young editor identified as Kwon Dong-ah was busy formatting articles to prepare for print.  

As I peered about, the social department head inquired how many lines I had written. “I’ve only managed about 80 lines.” 

The head retorted, “We’re 300 lines short! There’s simply no material these days.” 

“A call comes in and weighs heavily on our minds,” one editor said. “If anything major erupts, news about it would be incredibly beneficial.” 

Amid the chaos, lunch appeared, but no one seemed inclined to take a break. It was sheer dedication to work that drove them, with snacks placed atop scattered papers while writers continued typing furiously.  

As the clock approached three, the sound of machines began to fill the air from the typesetting room below. The articles—once mere drafts—were in the process of being printed, ready for release.  

“Don’t worry if article topics are lacking; we might even gather more from the street for future issues,” someone assured.  

“Ah, but time’s up!” I noted regretfully, observing how little I got to witness.

**Summary**  

This article captures a reporter's journey through the events of a single hour in Seoul, highlighting societal undercurrents and the often overlooked realities of everyday life. The reporter navigates various settings, from the courthouse and pharmacies to bustling marketplaces and ultimately, the newspaper office. This narrative reveals the struggles of ordinary citizens against the backdrop of a society in transition during the colonial period in Korea. Historians might find the reflections on public sentiment and social dynamics during the 1920s significant as they provide insight into how Korean society was coping with severe changes brought about by colonial rule.

똥벼락을 마젓소
길거리에서 한시간
『1時間 市內探訪? 이야말로 興味 잇구나! 그러나 어대로 가서 무엇을 보고 올고? 停車場? 圖書館? 안이다. 때가 不適하다. 富豪家? 銀行? 會社? 올타! 東大門 밧 林富者 집이나 차저보자. 그러나 拳統事件이 頻發하는 이 때에 그는 좀 嬚疑 적은데 爲先 市街 氣分부터 보면서 하자.』 이러게 혼자 작뎡하고 校洞 골목으로 내려 가것다. 타고갈가 거러길가 망설거리다가 에라 가기는 거러가고 오기는 타고 오리라. 街上의 기분도 보고 전차안 기분도 보고 兩全之策 이엿다. 우선 大事件 셋을 발견하얏다.
撤水車가 지나가면서 촌 양반의 새로 다음이 해임고온 두루막에 물탕을 첫스니 이런 大事件도 잇스랴. 『그거 참! 그러 참!』하고 도라서 새악씨한테 꾸중 먹을 것을 적정하는 촌 서방님 情景이 딱해 보인다. 東關압흘 지나다가 엇던 집 大門안을 드려다 보노라닛가 『아-大事件 突發!』 마루에서 밥알 주어 먹든 숫닭이 어린애 압헤 쫏기여 건너방 툇마루로 닷는 서슬에 툇마루에 노엿든 고초 광주리가 궁이 밋흐로 걱구러 백이니 이런 大事件도 또 잇스랴 마님의 통통거리는 擧動도 한참 可觀이다 군밤장사의 군밤 둥저기 업드린 것도 大事件 이려니와 배추장사의 요행 빗싸게 잘 팔앗든 것을 다시 무루게 됨도 大事件이다.
이렁저렁 가는 것이 東大門 근처 再橋를 넘어섯것다. 노접엇던 공터에 사람이 사오십명이나 들너서서 무엇을 자미잇게 구경하고 잇다. 「傳道軍 안이면 약장사 겟지?」 하고 무심히 지내려다가 하도 흥성흥성 하기에 꼴악산이를 잠간 보자하고 사람들 틈으로 억개를 비비고 드러서 보닛가 안이나 洋服에다가 하이카라 머리에다가 身數 멀정한 작자가 입담도 좃케 한바탕 떠 드러대는데 한참 可觀이엿다.
약은 무슨 약인지 모르지만 똥타령이 한바탕 쏘다저 나오는데 개똥, 쇠똥, 닭의 똥, 쥐똥, 똥이란 똥은 다 쏘다저 나온다.

아모려나 사람 만히 다니는 종로 네거리로 나가보자 무엇이 붓잡히려니 하고 막 신동 골목을 나가랴 닛가 탁 부드치는 것이 어제저녁 공주(公州)에서 가족동반으로 원산(元山)을 향한다는 I군의 손. 『앗차차 이것 탈낫구나!』 하고 생각하는 판에 엇재 황망한 꼴이 I군의 눈치에 채웟던지 저녁먹고 맛나기를 약속하야준다.
급한 숨을 휘- 내쉬고 종로시장 진장ㅅ거리 느러노은 마당을 아조 무엿이 나살듯키 휘휘 한박휘도랏다. 허- 연배치 폭이, 무ㅅ대가리, 새빨- 안고초 열매, 새-파란 미나리, 갓, 수둘수둘한 파뿌리, 올망졸망한 생강알 넛털넛털한 청각 줄거리, 동글동글한 마눌 대가리가 너의들의 겨울동안 먹을 것을 준비하라는드시 산덤이가티 쌔여 쌩쌩한 가을볏에 다 각기 제 빗을 내고잇다.
시장을 얼는보고 족금 내려가랴닛가 총출동 중의 하나인 M군이 두루막이ㅅ자락을 추근-히 끌고는 헤-하고 올나오는데 마조첫다. 눈치가 벌서 진장마당을 향하는 모양... ... .
여긔야말로 서울하고도 종로네거리, 사람이 옥신각신 길이 터지라는 듯이 야단법석이다. 그 중에도 눈에 얼는 띄우는 것은 쪼굴쪼굴하게 국인 백립, 쳐다만 보아도 찬바람이 훌훌나서 싸-늘한 맛이 마음을 제절로 차게하는데 망건(網巾)으로 동여 맨 것이 얼마간 방한(防寒)을 하는 듯한 모양, 바람에 날니는 흰 옷을 입은 상투장이 시골량반! 아모리 보아도 조선을 망처논 녯날 량반의 후손- 그래도 량반이라면 신이나서 조석죽거리는 업서도 그건 하야 한다고 아조 무슨 큰 위선이 나히는드시 의긔만장의 족보(族譜)꾼이 분명하다. 이야말로 먹고 노는자 중에도 망국적 고등부량자.
그리고 넓으나 넓은 길거리에 무엇이 무엇인지 전혀 모르고 파리한 몸 창백한 얼골에 하로 먹기에도 오히려 부족한 밥버리¥¼ 하느라고 길역사를 하는 로동자들이 흙을 파고 전선ㅅ대를 세우고 죄악돌을 깨틔리고 길을 고르고 헛눈 한 번 팔지 못하고 눈이 휘돌만큼 밧비 일을 하고 잇다. 심지어 어제ㅅ 저녁 얼은물에 빩-아 케피ㅅ빗도는 발을 담그고 하수도(下水道)를 고치는 분까지 잇다. 종로네거리는 뻘건 쇠전신ㅅ대가 새로 서기 시작한다. 꽤 놉흔 모양이다. 그 놈을 보니 이런 생각이 문득난다.
이 종로네거리 한 복판에다 한 칠팔십층 아니 될 수 잇다면 한 칠팔만 층되는 공중 누각을 지어노코 맨 꼭닥이에는 한 번 울리면 왼세계가 움직움직할 쇠북과 한 번 빗치면 지구 전체가 환-하게 밝을 등대(燈臺)를 달아노코 긔 위에 올나안저서 된놈 못된놈 쓸년 못쓸년들의 고자리 끌틋 우굴우굴하며 된진 안된짓 쓸짓 못쓸짓하는 모든 인간의 생활상을 탐사하여 보고 위선 캄캄한 밤ㅅ중 깁흔 꿈을 꾸고잇는 미련한 민중들의 머리를 쇠북 한 소리로 캣처주고 십흔 그 것이다. 그러면 이 1시간 탑방의 사회상도 그 집에 올나안저 볼 때 서울장안 안의 모든 광경이 일목요연하게 나타나련만... ... .
