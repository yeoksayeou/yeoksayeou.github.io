A Special Report on the Extreme Cruelty of Humanity: The Massacre of Forty-Eight Women, A Modern Crime Shock!!  
It was in 1908 during the turmoil of the great European war that shook the world. At that time, Germany resembled a hell on earth, an absolute slaughterhouse. All the men went to the front lines, and the remaining population was filled with hunger and complaints. The long-lasting war led them to feel immense boredom and despair, turning into beasts and violent men. Robbers and lewd-minded individuals roamed the streets and parks while revolutionary plots emerged here and there, making the security of Germany maintained merely by martial law.

Among these, the city of Berlin (백림) became an even more chaotic and terrifying hell. During this time, a string of mysterious female disappearances occurred in the city’s most vibrant areas, specifically in the vicinity of Puri-Turikh Street and Platan-Pluratz. A woman named Luze, the widow of Colonel Brantein who fought honorably in the battle, suddenly went missing after leaving her two children at home. Her six-year-old daughter, Miriya, also vanished without a trace.

However, Berlin was already a living hell, and it was common to think that they might have succumbed to the temptations of gold. Following this, another six-year-old girl from Polta Street went shopping one evening and disappeared, and a beloved shop assistant known for her beauty disappeared on her way home from the Platan Department Store. A female school teacher from Platan Street and her students went missing, along with a young nurse from Kraus Street and a typist working in a local shop, and the daughter of a wealthy family living on Peltruen Street also vanished without a trace.

Despite the continuous bizarre disappearances, the Berlin populace, who were already numb with fear, thought nothing of the young women going missing in the evenings, assuming they indulged at nightclubs. The nightclubs had emerged because public entertainment venues had been closed due to the war, allowing wealthy individuals who had profited from the war to establish these clubs away from police oversight, creating a haven for illicit pleasure. Many young women were presumed to have gone to these secretive spots, lured by the temptations of hunger and gold.

At that time, the police authorities could not give these incidents proper attention due to the revolutionary movements spreading in nearby countries and the pervasive ennui of the Berlin citizens. They viewed the mystery as a possible act of enemy espionage and clamped down on news about it in the press. Yet, daily reports flooded the police station with complaints from families, saying, "My sister hasn't returned since last night," or "My daughter has been missing for three days; please find her."

As bizarre as it was, many male detectives were off at war, leading to a higher number of female detectives investigating this case. However, unexpectedly, three detectives actively working on these cases disappeared without a trace. Initially, everyone speculated they had gone into hiding elsewhere, yet ultimately, none returned. Following this, the local police, who had not deemed the situation serious, were compelled to launch a thorough investigation. 

Restaurants, cafes, hotels, and boarding houses underwent strict searches. Authorities summoned around eighty witnesses, but none were connected to the cases. They then investigated merchants who could lure women easily, but this yielded no results. Subsequent raids on gambling dens netted around fifty arrests, yet again, none were involved.

Despite the continued investigations, the authorities could not discover any useful evidence related to the dozens of missing women, which escalated their anxiety. Typically, such incidents wouldn’t last long in ordinary circumstances; however, given the ongoing war, nothing was straightforward. 

Then one early morning, a ferryman rushed to the police station to report that a corpse had been found on the riverbank. When investigators arrived at the scene, they found the body of a woman, identified as the female detective Ki-Je, who had disappeared three weeks prior. Her gold watch was missing, but her engagement ring had also been taken, and her once beautiful body had turned into an unrecognizable corpse.

The authorities resumed their investigations urgently when they uncovered something peculiar: at a rag shop on Inpariten Street, they found twenty-six women's garments. These were later identified as belonging to the victims of the disappearances, confirming invaluable evidence. Each of the women’s family members recognized the clothes and demonstrated their grief.

Among the garments were those belonging to Colonel Brantein's widow, as well as items from missing students, but Ki-Je’s clothing still remained unfound. Blood stains were found on the skirts of the daughter of a wealthy family. Inside the pockets were banknotes, indicating that the assailant’s motivations were not monetary but of a purely heinous, deviant nature, leading authorities to see a flicker of hope amidst despair.

The inquiry took a turn when they investigated an old scavenger who had sold the clothing. They discovered he was an elderly man named Lu-Man residing on Okerstreet near the Puri-Turikh-Straße station. They later learned he had died three days prior. Reports revealed he had been a diligent stationmaster for thirty years, with no indication of a brutish criminal nature. He passed away from a heart attack while working at his station. There were no known relatives, and only a friend occasionally helped him.

Ultimately, the source of this strange issue—Lu-Man—remained a mystery. 

Meanwhile, a butcher, who had returned from America six months before, had set up a shop on Krausen Street. He was about forty, had a glaring scar on his left cheek resembling a crescent moon, and communicated little with his neighbors. However, he was always seen carefully watching his surroundings. 

While he did not have many customers during the day, on Tuesday and Friday nights, sidecars would visit his shop, leaving something behind, after which bloodied lamb meat would be found for sale. Suspicion grew that there were hidden secrets behind the sidecars’ visits and the sale of uninspected meat, fueling rumors.

Moreover, despite being single, strange sounds of women coming from his house raised eyebrows. On the following day, a female detective reported their suspicion that everything about the lamb meat concealed sinister secrets. Prompted by this information, police sprang into action and arrested the butcher, Hellman.

After several intense days of questioning and investigation, authorities released him due to insufficient evidence. There was even one man who confessed to being the perpetrator, yet he turned out to be a French spy aiming to glean information on Berlin police operations.

This brutal case remained unresolved for over half a year since its inception, and several months had passed since investigations began, still lacking progress.

Against this hellish backdrop during the late war, detective Angela was determined to uncover the truth behind her colleagues’ mysterious disappearances and seek safety for all women. Every evening, she staked out likely locations for crimes, concealing herself for three weeks. One night, hidden under the shade of trees by the riverbank where Ki-Je's body had been found, Angela saw a dark figure carrying a large bundle.

To her astonishment, it was a woman. The suspect approached the river, checked her surroundings, and then tossed the bundle into the water without looking back, only to swiftly slip away. Angela swallowed hard and followed discreetly.

The mysterious woman walked down the riverbank and eventually arrived at Hellman’s butcher shop. Upon receiving this report, authorities mobilized and began a shift in the inquiry. At the bank of the river, they discovered a bunch of women’s hair that led them to Hellman's house, where they found alarming evidence in his basement. It turned out the woman was none other than Hellman in disguise, revealing him as a violent, deviant individual.

Hellman was born into poverty in Berlin and grew up under difficult circumstances. At age fifteen, he became a servant in the house of a ship captain in America. One evening, the captain, in a fit of rage, accidentally killed his wife during an argument. In his desperate situation, he forced young Hellman to help him transport the body to the basement and, later, offered him money to flee to America.

Upon returning, the captain falsely accused Hellman of the murder, leading authorities to seek him even across the ocean. Over time, he grew resentful, and his personality worsened, descending into brutality.

Years later, he was hiding in the Mountains of Montana in America, living as a shepherd. During those years, his youth was spent indulging in the company of ewes, developing an enjoyment of killing them in accordance with his deviant sexual satisfaction. With the outbreak of the European war, he returned to Germany, finding the desperate women easy prey for his cravings. He often disguised himself as an old woman, seducing young women, drugging them, and bringing them home where he indulged in his perverse desires before killing them with a hammer, flaying their bodies, and selling their meat, discarding the rest in the river.

It is horrifying to think that he is alleged to have slaughtered a total of forty-eight women. This can be seen as a manifestation of his tragic past and the depraved environment he grew up in. This shocking and heinous crime can be called one of the prime examples of human brutality in modern criminal history.

Germany has kept these events secret during wartime to protect its honor, yet in the Berlin police museum, the tools he used to commit his crimes are displayed alongside those of the forty murders he committed. 

**Summary**

This article recounts a horrifying account of the mass disappearance and murder of forty-eight women during a tumultuous time in Berlin amidst World War I. The brutal acts, attributed to butcher Hellman, reflect a chilling intersection of societal collapse, psychological depravity, and wartime horrors. Historians may find particular significance in how such events were suppressed for wartime morale while simultaneously revealing the severe consequences of societal collapse during crises.
