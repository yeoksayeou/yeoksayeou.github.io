A Reading on Marital Life, Strategies for Renewing Love  
Kim Yeong-pal  

There is a saying in Japanese, "Noroke." This Noroke may be something that everyone is familiar with and seems somewhat trivial, yet it often leads to a smile, and in more severe cases, it can seem quite unpleasant. Thus, when my friend Byeolgeongon asks me to open up about the Noroke, I cannot refuse outright, but it makes me uneasy.

It seems that back when my wife and I married, "what is a poppy flower?" It was a time when, even when falling in love with others, we would venture into parks together without a care. If not, we would go to the theater, just like these brisk days of late. Wasn't there a time we shared sweet love letters? No, not at all. Back then, during that period of limited finances, how could we do that? Thinking back, I was quite foolish and could hardly lift my head in shame. I wonder why, but perhaps I might not have thoughts like that in the present time?

After all, seeing each other once a week and sneaking into the kitchen of my current wife (who was once my lover) while time passes feels both a shame and a beautiful memory, as I recall the times of holding her hands tightly and shivering with sighs. After marriage, whenever a friend visits and called out "Yeong-pal!" I can only furrow my brows and reply, "Not now." If that friend were to look back on those days, he might think, "What a despicable man!" Yet, in truth, I cannot deny that it was a time of unparalleled happiness. Should a friend come to my house and call out for Yeong-pal now, before he finishes calling my name, my wife would tell him, "Go outside!"

Why is that, you might ask? We have one child, then another, and now I have two brothers; I cannot even entertain guests. Moreover, as a mother of young children, I have no time to spare, so when evening falls and I return home, either my husband is present or not, but there lies my spouse asleep, clasping our children. I cannot bring myself to awaken someone so peacefully asleep, so this pattern has become somewhat habitual. I, too, am one to drink, and by ten o'clock, I must return home; however, sometimes it stretches late into the night.

Just today, I thought, "I am only going to sleep here tonight," but similar thoughts ran through my mind as I considered that soon it might take form. Recently, it feels as if my wife's trust in me has drastically diminished. She might think I'm such a person, and I might think, "How did I become like this?" Thus, the word renewal (更新策) arises, and I wonder, "My friend Byeolgeongon, how should it be done?" This is akin to the feeling of going out only to come back and strike again.

However, in my thoughts, I ponder that perhaps a person who engages in the literary field is indeed unfortunate if devoid of a spouse. How can one maintain the temper of their husband consistently? If, at any moment, my sentiments shift even slightly, wouldn't words I utter be met with disdain and be dismissed at once?

Furthermore, there is another troublesome matter. Regardless of which maiden may flirt with him and laugh charmingly, that type of attitude can ultimately create stress. This not only causes strife but leads one to view their current partner in a disrespectful light. Seeing my wife, who has already been my partner for so long, as merely the cook and caretaker, it may lead to the pursuit of new loves, expending endless efforts. This might eventually result in sudden divorce or, worse, lead to infidelity. Yet this is merely speculation. If I were to understand the reality, it would be quite serious; my position is difficult.

Should the affections between my wife and me diminish, how would one strategize a renewal? 

"I suppose a separation is in order."

"What do you mean?"

"Divorce?"

"Not at all."

"Then?"

"If such a situation arises, it's merely a temporary delusion. If my wife understands and allows a separation for three years, then from my sentiment, I would say that within two and a half years, the love that once was could regain fifty percent."

"Huh? That's quite a profound statement. Then what if she does not understand?"

"That’s a touchy subject, let me think. It would lead to a critical lifetime of disharmony."

"In that case, one must avoid such actions from the start."

"What do you mean? What a friend’s phrase; that’s only commentary on the aftermath. It’s akin to climbing a tree and subsequently shaking it. Let’s meet again. Surely you have time?"

-The End-  

**Summary**  

The article reflects on the evolution of love and marriage as experienced by Kim Yeong-pal. He discusses the disparity between the early days of romance and the realities of married life, highlighting feelings of regret and yearning for renewal in the relationship. One revealing aspect is the struggle to maintain affection and trust amidst the demands of family life, which underscores the challenges faced by couples, particularly in a colonial context where societal pressures might exacerbate personal conflicts. Historians may find the portrayal of marital struggles during the colonial period noteworthy, shedding light on the intimacies and difficulties of life under Japanese rule.
