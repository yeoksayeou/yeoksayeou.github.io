기사제목	萬人必讀할 今日의 經濟常識 第二課 今日의 問題 조선 사람은 웨 가난해지나 (Economic Knowledge Everyone Must Read: Lesson 2, Today's Question: Why Are Koreans Becoming Poor?)  
필자	徐椿  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Discussion)  

The majority of Koreans, excluding the exceptionally wealthy, are exceedingly poor. They are so poor that even if they were to try, they can't become rich due to the lack of resources. Like creatures born in rough conditions, they are just surviving with only basic necessities. They have nothing to start trading, and they are unable to cultivate crops or do anything significant. Being hemmed in, they can't venture east or west due to the encircling seas, and if they attempt to head south, they are met by many hardships, while going north results in hostility.

Where can they go? How can they live? Why, after all, are Koreans becoming poorer?

It's impossible to clarify this alone; I must revisit the Dong-A Ilbo (East Asia Daily) to share the thoughts of Mr. Seo Choon, as was promised earlier.

Reporter: Please tell us why Koreans are becoming poorer.

Seo Choon: This topic is somewhat uncomfortable.

Reporter: Particularly, please focus on the land issue as a cause of poverty.

Seo Choon: Yes... There are many potential causes, but if we were to summarize the most significant ones, they would encompass the following:

(1) Lack of capital = Because of this,
1. Whether in agriculture, business, or industry, they cannot expand significantly, hence unable to produce or sell goods efficiently.
2. They cannot engage in political dealings (this explanation cannot be elaborated further).

(2) Lack of knowledge = Among them,
1. They lack legal knowledge, making them prone to deception.
2. They lack economic knowledge, thus unaware of how the economy works.

(3) Due to the previously mentioned lack of capital and knowledge, they cannot significantly engage in industry or commerce, and in addition to the belief that "farmers are the foundation of the world," 
1. They refuse to widen their farming practices, causing their own agricultural and industrial products to be sold at low prices, while they end up buying goods at high prices.
2. They cannot process agricultural products, relocate to sell them, or time their sales well.

(4) Conservatism due to tradition = 
1. Failing to properly decorate their storefronts.
2. Neglecting to display their products well.
3. Failing to advertise effectively.

(5) Living beyond their means but using common goods = Even though in better times a suit would cost around fifty to sixty won, now they still wear suits at that price and consume liquor costing one won a glass, hence their pockets become lighter over time.

(6) Debt on land. 
1. Borrowing on their land and incurring interest.
2. Despite knowing they cannot repay, they sell it, leading to depletion of their resources while their land continues to vanish.

These six factors can be seen as causes for the increasing poverty among Koreans.

Reporter: I can only vaguely guess, but specifically regarding item (6), could you elaborate?

Seo Choon: That’s probably the case. You will find more details in the "Hyesung" issue of April.

Reporter: Now, please talk about the land issue in more detail.

Seo Choon: Yes.. The land issue naturally has many aspects, but lately the most crucial aspect is the land price issue. The land price issue causes the issues mentioned earlier about "the six reasons for Koreans' increasing poverty."

Reporter: Really? So that is a highly significant issue?

Seo Choon: It is indeed.

Reporter: Please explain it to me in an easy-to-understand manner.

Seo Choon: Suppose twenty years ago, I owned three thousand pyeong of rice paddies. If each pyeong was worth ten won, how much would my asset be?

Reporter: Three hundred won, right?

Seo Choon: Exactly! But I, like all Koreans, have no money beyond what is earned from the rice harvested.

Reporter: Just rice?

Seo Choon: Yes, I could only afford food from the rice I harvested. I had to sell rice to cover expenses for clothes and other needs.

Reporter: Understood.

Seo Choon: If I were short on money after selling rice?

Reporter: I would go into debt.

Seo Choon: Correct.

Reporter: Or sell the land.

Seo Choon: Right. Thus, to cope with my recurring shortfalls, I gradually sold off my rice paddies over the years. For instance, if I sold off a hundred pyeong per year, over twenty years, that would amount to selling two thousand pyeong. Thus, my initial three thousand pyeong would reduce to only one thousand pyeong left. Naturally, this leads to a situation of increasing poverty.

Reporter: That seems reasonable.

Seo Choon: However, this isn't just my situation; all Koreans have done the same. Who among Koreans hasn’t sold off their land during hard times?

Reporter: No one. In my family, we had to sell our assets over time as well.

Seo Choon: You see?

Reporter: But wait a moment... If you sold two thousand pyeong out of three thousand, would it not make you poorer?

Seo Choon: It would seem so.

Reporter: But that's not entirely the case. In the past, it was difficult even to have one or two won in Koreans' pockets, but now people have ten and a hundred won coins, wearing suits costing ten won, and consuming liquor, resulting in significant changes in lifestyle—don’t you think that's enormous?

Seo Choon: Yes, indeed. That's profound. Let me explain. Twenty years ago, my three thousand pyeong of land was priced at ten won per pyeong. That meant my total asset was three hundred won.

Reporter: Right.

Seo Choon: Over that time, did land prices not generally rise across Korea?

Reporter: Yes, indeed.

Seo Choon: Even though I sold off two thousand pyeong over those years, if land prices have risen, then the land I still own, even if diminished, would still appreciate in value; for example, a remaining one thousand pyeong might now be worth fifty won per pyeong, totaling five hundred won.

Reporter: Correct.

Seo Choon: Thus, my land quantity has decreased, but my net worth hasn’t decreased—it might actually have increased.

Reporter: That is certainly the case.

Seo Choon: Recently, prominent figures such as Baek Seonhaeng and Kim Seongsu becoming wealthy is also credited to the rise in land prices; it’s not primarily due to any large-scale industry or business they operated.

Reporter: Yes, I understand. Then does that mean Korean people are not completely in desperate straits?

Seo Choon: Yet another point remains. Listen carefully. Thus far, Korean land prices have been rising in comparison to Japanese prices; therefore, while they may have sold some land, their net worth in monetary terms has not sharply declined beyond expectations. 

However, last year, that is, 1929, land prices in Korea took a significant downturn owing to the global economic crisis. 

Reporter: I see... that is truly remarkable.

Seo Choon: Consequently, regarding the land I have left, it was worth five hundred won before last year but now has dropped to three hundred won. What does that mean?

Reporter: What will happen next?

Seo Choon: I’ll have to sell off more of my remaining land, and without any entry price increase or volatility, I am likely to lose even that. 

Reporter:....!

Seo Choon:....!  

**Summary**  

This article delves into the pervasive poverty among Koreans under Japanese colonial rule, identifying multiple causes such as lack of capital, knowledge, and oppressive land conditions, significantly influencing their social and economic status. The conversation vividly illustrates the struggles of Koreans to maintain their livelihoods, revealing a nuanced perspective on the economic complexities during this period. Historians may find interest in the candid acknowledgment of both individual and collective challenges faced by the Korean population and the societal implications of land ownership and poverty driven by economic policies.
