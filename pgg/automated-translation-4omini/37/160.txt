始初이야기  

『고구마』의 始初  
As the autumn progresses and the chilly winter wind begins to whip around people’s ears, the familiar sound of the chestnut street vendors in Seoul can be heard without fail, calling, “Here are roasted chestnuts! Buy some roasted chestnuts! They’re delicious! Roasted chestnuts! The water is sweet! Roasted chestnuts!” In recent times, however, there has appeared a new street vendor on a small cart with a flickering lantern, selling sweet potatoes, marked with the Japanese characters "ヤキイモ" (yakiimo) and the Chinese characters "夜氣己暮 九里 四里 十三里." This hapless voice calling, “Imo! Imo! Yakiimo!” goes around this alley and that alley. The yakiimo, being tasty and inexpensive, has gained greater popularity in Seoul than even roasted chestnuts, capturing the hearts of students as well as children and adults alike. The term "yakiimo" is indeed a Japanese word, while "夜氣己暮" is a similar-sounding representation in Chinese characters, and "九里 四里 十三里" is a Japanese expression referring to the idea that sweet potatoes (栗) taste better than chestnuts. These bits of trivia are something everyone is aware of, so I consider it redundant to elaborate here. 

However, it seems that the emergence of yakiimo vendors is a recent phenomenon, and their selling style mimics that of the Japanese. The term yakiimo is Japanese, so it is easy for the original Korean word "고구마" to be gradually forgotten, with some people mistakenly thinking it was recently imported from Japan. Yet, if we delve into history, sweet potatoes did not originally come from Japan; rather, it dates back 168 years ago during the 39th year of Yeongjo's reign (1763 C.E.), when a diplomatic envoy named Cho Dae-ran (趙曮) visited Tsushima Island and brought back seeds from a place called Soonsun. Gradually, this expanded, and it was widely cultivated in Gyeongsang and Jeolla Provinces. In Jeolla Province, particularly in the vicinity of the Dadohae Islands, sweet potatoes became a primary crop for households, serving as their staple food while also being exported to other regions. Even to this day, if you visit Mukhye-port during harvest season, you will see heaps of sweet potatoes piled at the dock, all of which have been brought in from the islands. 

Moreover, a well-known scholar and economist from the same region as Cho Dae-ran, Seo Yu-yeok (徐有榘), a friend of the Cho family, authored a book titled “The Cultivation of Sweet Potatoes” (種藷譜), teaching and promoting the planting of sweet potatoes. This made sweet potatoes widely cultivated. It is easier to farm sweet potatoes compared to grains, so for a time in Gyeonggi Province, people even neglected other crops for the cultivation of sweet potatoes alone. The Great Queen (大院君) became concerned that this would reduce the kingdom's grain imports and thus prohibited it, so the cultivation of sweet potatoes was not as widespread in northern Gyeonggi and the Gyeonghu regions, becoming confined primarily to the mountains of Jeolla and Gyeongsang Provinces. 

Regarding the origins of sweet potatoes, there are two claims according to popular lore: One is that they are referred to as "goguma" (고금마) because they are plentiful on Gogeum Island (古今島). "Gogeum" is a name of an island, and "ma" in "goguma" likely refers to "mountain yam" (山藥), a wild plant with edible roots used for medicinal purposes, denoting the sweet potato's similarity to mountain yam; thus, it can mean "the yam of Gogeum Island." Another theory suggests it comes from the Tsushima language "hinakiyo" (孝行藷), as the pronunciation is similar.  

According to Cho Dae-ran's diary, which documents the introduction of sweet potatoes, the term "sweet potato" is sometimes referred to as "hyoja ma" (孝子麻), implying that the sweet potato indeed has roots in the language of Tsushima. And the practice of selling roasted sweet potatoes began only in recent years.  

**Summary**  

This article provides a historical account of the introduction and cultivation of sweet potatoes (고구마) in Korea, emphasizing their growing popularity in Seoul, especially among students and families, and discusses their origins traced back to the late 18th century. It critiques the misunderstanding that sweet potatoes are a recent Japanese import, delving instead into their historical significance and cultivation in Korea, particularly in Jeolla Province. Historians may find interest in the way food culture reflects colonial influences, as well as in the cross-border exchanges of agricultural practices during this period.
