기사제목	秘中大秘話, 나의 첫날밤 公開狀 (Secret Stories of Secrets, My First Night Revealed)  
필자	宋金順 (Song Kim-sun)  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)  

My marriage happened so quickly that it was almost laughable to others. It was only three days after the discussion of marriage began, and I had already transformed into a husband’s wife, needing to serve my husband. It felt like a dream, or perhaps more accurately, like a dream within a dream. 

“Dear Kim-sun, when did you get married?” my friend exclaimed, eyes wide in disbelief upon hearing my news. I responded, “There was nothing I could do; that is simply how it turned out.” 

As I absentmindedly touched my first hairstyling, my friend interrupted my explanation. “You’re so carefree; it’s truly enviable,” they remarked.

“Carefree?” Their words stirred feelings within me. Was I really carefree? I found it hard to understand their interpretation of my state, especially in contrast to how I was suddenly experiencing life as a wife. So, I asked, “Am I really so carefree?” They replied, “Yes, your face looks so natural. You don’t appear to be someone who has just had a terrifying first experience.” It seemed odd to them. “Even if it is an official marriage, relinquishing one’s virginity to a man is a serious matter. Naturally, a woman loses her chastity when she marries, but to throw away something carefully preserved over many years in just one night—it’s a significant concern.”

While I watched my friend stirring tea with a spoon, sunlight streaming through the window, I reflected on their words. “Yes, that’s true…” 

Every young woman values her virginity. It signifies the preciousness of life and is deemed far too significant to be disregarded. Regardless of how passionate the romance, no woman would willingly part with it. Families and society at large preach the same message, and often girls are led to devastating outcomes for a mere loss of chastity. 

Such a treasured female pride! The struggles and temptations endured to protect a woman’s honor—what could be more emotionally weighted than the thought of losing it to a man in just a single night? For my family, the situation was particularly strict. My father, who was a scholar, held a stern view, and with my mother managing the household without a husband, her expectations were markedly high. 

From the moment my marriage was rumored, her attitude toward me grew increasingly severe. Though she had no unreasonable restrictions, she often reminded me, “You are going to live in someone else’s home, so you must take care of yourself.” Therefore, she had been careful to supervise me closely. As a result, I was rarely allowed outside alone and even when I ventured to eat with friends, my younger sibling was made to accompany me. Once, when I returned home late from a friend’s, I was met with scolding that felt like punishment. 

“Why do you think I respond like this?” My mother had such a strict view of interacting with young men that it left me feeling as though I had committed a grave sin. I could do nothing but respond, “I absolutely won't go out alone anymore.” I complied, finding comfort in the rigid, sheltered environment dictated by my mother. Living in ignorance of the world while being curated by my family made the outside world seem overwhelmingly fearsome. 

Especially regarding issues of the opposite sex, my thoughts turned increasingly peculiar. Even a fleeting glance at a male student boarding nearby stirred strange feelings within me. I often wondered, “What is happening to me?” Despite my efforts to amend these timid feelings, I achieved no success.

As I continued my life in this isolated existence, the topic of marriage suddenly apparated and within a mere three days, I was wed.

I first heard of the marriage proposal on the cold day of December 25. My mother quietly called me into the living room while I was engaged in stitching. 

“A wonderful marriage has been arranged for you, so we’re planning to send you off,” she stated, her demeanor calm as she touched my hair with affection. 

“Next year may be bad luck for you, and the other side is pressing for haste, so let’s go ahead and marry this year.” There was no room for a refusal or discussion. 

“Others are getting married!” I returned to my room, contemplating the fate that seemed dictated by some unseen force. However, instead of feeling the enchanting joy of a grand marriage, my heart was filled only with sorrow over the virginity I would be giving up that night. 

Even if my life were in peril, I resolved not to relinquish the last vestige of pride in myself—what I had protected all these years. 

“Marriage is the flower of life,” I thought while attempting to reject this burden of responsibility. Instead, I sank deeper into despair, weeping that fateful night.

You may question how I was able to feel this way, but given that I had only three days to prepare for my wedding, this made my distress even more profound. 

What weighed heavier than this sorrow was my immense anxiety about my unknown husband, whose values and views on women were still too mysterious to me. It forced me to see that night as a harrowing experience. 

“How can I endure this?” 

“What a delightful experience,” people would ask when they learned of my marriage. “What makes it so wonderful?” I would suffer silently to myself.

Carrying these burdens, I went to the wedding venue, and without incident, completed the ceremony, then we embarked on a honeymoon trip to Onyang. 

The sound of the train wheels rolling along the tracks felt like a call to bid farewell to my virginity, echoing ominously. I often turned my head to avoid the gaze of my husband, who was becoming increasingly animated.

Once we arrived at the inn, the perceptive owner seemed to instinctively know our situation and guide us to a room that was particularly quiet. Although the room was indeed clean and tranquil, I felt odd as I faced my first night. 

“Perhaps they sensed how little we knew,” my husband commented, pouring out a bottle of liquor. 

“Not for me…” I couldn’t manage to speak any further, unable to accept it. 

By nine o’clock, the vast inn fell into silence. 

“Oh, are you tired?” 

With his face flushed from a bottle of liquor, my husband continued to sit before me, gazing intently. 

“Come on, let’s get to it.” 

While such words might be commonplace for many, to me, they struck me as an overwhelming sensation akin to being pushed into a corner of my chest. 

“... ... ...” 

“If we sit like this too long, it will become unbearable,” he stated.

The sound of the doorbell echoed in the quiet room as the maid quietly laid out the bedding and left. 

“Oh, what now!” I realized the moment had arrived. 

My eyes closed, but my heart raced with emotion beyond description. 

However, my anxiety and fear didn’t remain long. I accepted my husband entirely, surrendered myself to him and as dawn broke, I felt considerably lighter, and my mind cleared somewhat.

For the first time, I grasped the essence of life. This was not about the beauty of intimacy; it made me recognize the true significance and profound responsibility of embracing one’s virginity on the wedding night. 

The sincerity in my husband’s words, “I will cherish you forever,” during that moment was something I had never fully understood before. Our hearts were bound together in that union.

I wish to share this foolish story to all those fearful of the marriage night. -The End-  

**Summary**  

The article explores the author’s psychological state as she transitions from being a young woman to a wife, reflecting on the societal expectations surrounding virginity and marriage in 1930s Korea. The complex emotions surrounding her first night married—spanning anxiety, confusion, and ultimately a realization of connection with her husband—capture the struggles faced by women in a rigid patriarchal society. Historians might find this narrative valuable as it illustrates the pressures and expectations of women during Korea's colonial period, and how traditional values intersected with modern experiences of marriage and sexuality.
