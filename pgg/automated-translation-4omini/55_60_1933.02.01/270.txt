A Romantic Memory of Joy and Sorrow - Snow! Snow! - Stories in the Snow  

Love's Snow Burial  

I am not skilled at snow, perhaps because I am dim-witted with my eyes and ears. Thus, I often speak of "stories in the snow." In any case, I have nothing particularly interesting to share. However, to avoid disappointing you, I will share just one story! It was a winter night two years ago when snowflakes fell abundantly. Santa Claus, instead of giving children gifts, was busy stealing the socks hanging from the roof; such were the difficult days of that time.

What is a bonus? The next morning, I found myself overwhelmed by poverty. My friend suggested we go for a drink, but it wasn't to the cafeteria; it was Cheongjin-dong! Cheongjin-dong! I quickly tied up the torn socks at the back and got on the rickshaw.

Upon arriving at Cheongjin-dong, I found myself at C's house. C was a courtesan with whom I had been in a relationship for about four months without any passion. It turned out C had married someone during that time and had saved up about a thousand won.

"... I will stop being a courtesan and move to the countryside. While we believed our love would last a hundred years, we never imagined it would end like this. So let's end this night with a clean break. If we prolong it, the wounds in my heart will only deepen. Please live well with your wife. I will go to the countryside, even if it's to live as a poor woman with a blind fortune-teller."

Of course, C's words were reasonable. I couldn't clap my hands in agreement, but instead, I shed tears and sympathized with her. Following her suggestion, I drank a liquor I could barely swallow until it filled my throat.

As I left C's house, the streets were thickly covered with snow. As soon as I jumped into the alley, I forgot my promises to C. Then, I buried them completely in the snow.

"Alright, C! Let's hold a snow burial for our love!"

Of course, I was drunk, so perhaps that was my reasoning, but it seemed I felt quite sentimental. Thus, I buried my love in the snow and returned home.

A Story Lost in the Snow! 

A Night in the Mountain Lodge  

This was during my boyhood.  

That winter was particularly snowy. 

The branches of the old pine trees sagged under the weight, and the houses were covered with snow as thick as a meter. The entire scene was enveloped in a northern chill, with the cold wind blowing snowflakes everywhere. 

In this situation, one morning, I insisted on following my neighbors to hunt deer, despite their warnings not to go.  

The snow was nearly knee-deep. I exerted myself to keep up with the hunters' footsteps while pushing through the snow. 

Because it had snowed so much or perhaps due to the abundance of deer, the hunters were having lively pursuits, running both horizontally and vertically to capture several large deer. Additionally, the hunters were all experienced marksmen, so the day's results were excellent, and by evening, we already had three or four deer. 

However, the hunters seemed unsatisfied with their catches and decided to gather them and tie them to the trunks of trees while heading deeper into the snowy mountain. 

As the sun began to set, casting a red hue over the white mountain tops, the view was truly magnificent. 

I was exhausted and struggled to keep up. They didn't pay much attention to the frail young boy trailing behind. As twilight approached and darkness fell, the sounds of birds began to die down, and I feared I might lose them. 

At that moment, suddenly, a deer, as large as a calf, came bounding down, and I saw it darting away at a great speed. 

"Catch it!"  

The hunters shouted in unison, racing off, and some of them fired their guns. Each gunshot echoed ominously, sending a jarring sound reverberating through the mountains. With every shot, the earlier chatter of birds was momentarily silenced as if they held their breath in fear. 

I also ran swiftly behind them but lost balance and slipped on the snowy ground, tumbling chaotically into a distant valley.  

Eventually, in a daze, I caught a glimpse of a dim light. Then I saw a look of concern from an old man and the worried face of a girl. I wondered if I was dreaming, so I painfully opened my eyes and regained my senses.

The old man kindly smiled at me and murmured something I couldn't quite hear. 

I had injured my legs. In my dazed state, I must have lost consciousness, and the hunters had brought me to a small cabin in the mountains, leaving me there while they exited to investigate. 

In that isolated mountain cabin, I spent the night, fully regaining consciousness. That girl, the old man's granddaughter, brought me cold water and kindled a fire, which left a significant impression on me. Though I was just a child, I didn't feel any romantic affection for her—as a child, it was indeed memorable. 

The next day, just as dawn broke, I glimpsed the beautiful snow fall again. From a corner of the valley, I spotted shadows approaching. Recognizing the voices of my father and uncle filled me with indescribable joy. 

I dashed out, taking a couple of steps forward, assuring them I was alright, and they reassured me as well. The girl also stepped out and smiled at me. 

My Father's Warmth  

It was a snow-laden evening of bleakness.  

"The corpse of a ghost has appeared at XXX,"  

Upon hearing this news, we hurried to XXX for a new hunting expedition. The orderly was sitting inside, and the warden hadn't yet arrived.  

The corpse was covered with a quilt and lay in the ditch of the lower rice field.  

"Who could it be?"  

"I don't know—who knows!" This was the grumbling response from the orderly. He continued on that two people seemed to have died.  

"Where is the other?"  

"Go and find out."  

One friend went down to lift the quilt.  

"Ah, a child was among them!" he exclaimed, stepping back and sticking out his tongue.  

Suddenly frightened, he retreated, shouting about something.  

"What's wrong? Did you see a ghost?"  

"Uh... No, just something astonishing."  

"What's that?"  

All those who were there joined in looking at the corpse. It was indeed trembling under the quilt.  

The orderly approached, shrugged the quilt off, and half-smiling, he remarked, "It seems like someone has survived."  

We hurried away.  

A child about four years old, cradled in his father’s arms, woke up by rubbing his eyes and looked around at us, then smiled.  

One of the hunters was overcome with emotion.  

The orderly reassured the child, and while a friend’s overwhelmed feelings were evident, he decided to step outside.  

The snow gently fell, covering the father's corpse completely.  

... ... ...  

Now, that child has grown up to be a servant in someone else's house.  

The Story of the Deep Sea Ghost  

When I was about eleven or twelve years old, in my hometown called Kkommi, I traveled to a nearby village named Aliesmal to study Chinese history.  

Because I was a child, the road was long, and being in the countryside made it quite lonely. With no companions, I felt immensely lonely during the journey.  

Moreover, at that time, there were many stories circulating about ghost-like creatures in the school and at home—talking of things like nursing a round pebble or a bunny ghost that turns into an egg, they would attach to a person and would not break even if stepped on, or rumors about ghosts of maidens flying around in skirts. All these stories stuck with me vividly. I still remember much of them and can visualize those eerie creatures today. 

When the winter snow fell for days, I was frightened by the thought of those ghost tales while trying not to go to school.  

Across the long hill, the world was covered with soft white snow, and I alone would walk, carrying my book. 

I spent my days bending over books in class, striving to outperform my peers, followed by the long journey back home on chilly nights. Without the sun setting, I felt extreme dread as the thick snow made the night seem darker, conjuring thoughts of ghosts.  

While walking home, I passed the village of Saettera. I turned my head toward that place as I was passing by and suddenly saw a ghost on the edge of the village, staring directly at me.  

It was a fearsome figure, possibly as tall as six or seven feet. It was a towering giant with an intimidating face wearing a hat and arms stretched wide. For a moment, I felt compelled to run into its arms. When I glimpsed its face, it appeared ghastly.  

I was stunned and instantly decided to retreat, panting as I hurried back home. That evening and the next morning, I couldn't shake the terrifying thought of the ghost and trembled with fear, never once sharing the story with my father, mother, or friends. 

However, the next day, instead of going to school, I went out with the servant from the house. The snow had persisted just as before, sparkling brightly in the morning sunlight, filling my heart with a bit of pleasure.  

As I approached Saettera, I found the ghost's spot had vanished. My companion watched me curiously as we approached where I thought I saw the ghost.  

Upon arrival, it became evident that the supposed ghost had indeed been a large tree stump that had been cut halfway through with many branches lying about it, all buried in the snow, giving only the top part a ghostly appearance, resembling a head with a snowy cap, and the branches resembling arms stretching out as though greeting me.  

Realizing that I had been deceived, I felt relief wash over me.  

I immediately took my servant partner and quickly ran back toward the village, recounting how I thought I saw a ghost just the night before.  

Thus, our snowy paths again turned into a collection of ghost stories.  

The Shipwreck  

It was a snowy day, the winds raging in a dark winter night.  

The wind blew fiercely, and heavy snow fell, causing the North to feel particularly desolate.  

The winds rattled the pork curtains while the sound of the waves echoed, and amid this uproar, people remained huddled under their blankets.  

"Hmph, are they trying to take more lives tonight?"  

The elderly experienced fishermen murmured, their teeth chattering.  

"Are they hoping their boat is safe without being crushed?"  

This was the captain's worry.  

"Thank goodness if the boat just stayed in port without encountering the storm, but what are we to do now?” They thought, in denial of the horrible situation that was now unfolding.  

The darkness grew thicker, and the shadows grew intense. 

"Someone went missing!"  

Voices shouted as people dashed to check on their families and companions outside.  

People hurried to the dock, their feet moving quickly. The snow continued to fall heavily, obscuring vision.  

"What do they say?"  

I approached a group of women who were crying, and I asked, "What happened?"  

They gazed at the sea, whispering their fears. 

"Ah! It was that boat, six people were on it, shortly before it sank!"  

In those fleeting moments, their distress turned into panic—a plea for prayers, for mercy, and cries for salvation: "Oh God, forgive our sins. Let us live."  

Among the passengers, one old man prayed, tears rolling down his face, while a young person shouted, seeking help, "We're drowning! Help!"  

Yet the waves roared increasingly, drowning their desperate cries.  

Then!  

Onlookers gasped, instinctively clutching their chests, eyes clenching as they braced for the worst.  

"The boat is gone! We can't see anyone!"  

People cried out in sorrow, calling out to one another. My own eyes began to swell with tears as I held the child who was trembling with fear. After crying together for a while, people began to disperse back into the darkness.  

The next day, two bodies were washed ashore, covered in a thick layer of snow that seemed endless.

The End  

**Summary**  

This article includes a mixture of personal memories woven together by the thematic motif of snow, making the narrative both nostalgic and poignant. It reflects on lost love, childhood fears, and communal fears during disasters, all under the oppression of the snow. The segments capture intimate moments set against the backdrop of Japanese colonial rule, revealing how folks grappled with their everyday struggles and emotions amid the harsh realities of their lives. Scholars examining this piece may find the interplay of personal stories and colonial experiences significant in understanding the complexities of identity and emotion during this period in Korea's history.

하나, 둘, 셋 ... 한정이 업스니 고만 둡시다. 그러나 섭섭하실테니 꼭 한가지! 재작년 겨울 그야말로 눈뭉치가 부슬부슬 나리든 날 밤입니다. 쌘타클로-스 옹이 어린애들에게 작난감을 노나 주기커녕 아랫묵에 걸려잇는 양말짝을 훔치려다니든 아조 불경기한 때엿습니다.

청진동! 가서보니 C의 집이엇습니다. C는 나와 연애라는 형식으로 사귀어 온 기생이엿습니다. 그러나 약 4개월 동안이나 피차에 아모 리유업시 못 맛나든 연인이엇습니다. 드른 즉 C는 그동안 「봉」을 하나 무러 살림을 살고 집간에다 돈 천원이나 어덧답니다.
「... 저는 인제 기생노릇도 고만두고 시골가서 살겟서요. 우리들이 맛날때 우리들의 사랑이 백년 가리라고는 피차에 생각지도 안엇든 것이 아니얘요. 허니 오늘밤 이 자리에서 인연을 깨끗이 끈읍시다. 오래 끌면 끌수록 끗판에는 내가 밧는 마음의 상처만 클 것이니까 당신은 당신 부인과 잘 사십시요. 저는 시골 가서 눈먼 상투쟁이래도 어더서 살겟서요」
당연 우 당연한 C의 말이엇습니다. 그러나 그러타! 하고 손벽칠 처지는 못되엿습니다. 나는 피눈물 뿌리는 탣를 취하고 그를 동정하엿습니다. 그리고 그의 권하는 대로 먹을 줄 모르는 술을 목구녁까지 채워 넘겻습니다.
C의 집을 나슬때 거리에는 눈이 힌무리 떡만이나 두텁게 쌔워 잇습니다. 나는 골목으로 뛰여 드러가기가 무섭게 목에까지 찬 C가 내인 마찬을 모조리 돌려버렷습니다. 그리고 나서 눈으로 깨끗이 덥헛습니다.
「그러타- C야! 우리의 사랑을 설장(雪葬) 지내자」

그래서 나는 눈 우에 사랑을 파뭇고 집으로 도라갓습니다.

山莊의 一夜

李香村

소년시대의 일이다.
그해ㅅ 겨울은 특별히 눈이 만히 왓섯다.
늙은 소나무가지가 척척 휘여저 내리고 집웅 우에는 멧자로 재리만큼 두텁게 싸혓섯다. 만목(滿目)은 세게를 이룬 북국(北國)의 찬바람이 간단업시 눈가루를 휘- 휘- 쥐여 뿌리엇다.
- 이러한 하로 아츰 나는 이웃사람들이 노루사냥을 가는데 구지 오지 말라는 것을 한사코 따라나섯다.
산에서-＜57＞
눈이 거의 가슴 밋까지 푹푹 빠지는 데를 죽을힘을 다하야 사냥꾼들의 꽁문이를 대서 갓다. 눈이 만히 와서 그런지 또는 특별히 짐승이 만흔 해엿든지 사냥꾼에게 쪼겨서 가로 뛰고 세로 달니는 큰 노루들이 그야말로 너저분하엿다. 게다가 사냥꾼들은 모두 상당한 포수들 뿐이엇음으로 그날의 성적은 매우 양호하야 일은 저녁에 벌서 큰 것 적은 것 해서 서너 놈을 쏘앗섯다.
그러나 사냥꾼의 욕심은 그것으로 만족하지 못햇든지 사냥한 것은 한테 모아서 나무 밋동에 마승으로 붓들어 매여 노코 다시 깁픈 산중으로 짐승의 발자최를 더음어 들어갓다.
해는 어느 듯 뉘엿뉘엿 서산의 놉흔 은령(銀嶺)을 기어넘고 잇섯다. 하-얀 산머리에 붉으례한 석양(夕陽)이 빗긴 경치란 참 장엄하엿다.
나는 왼 사지가 극도로 피곤하야 가까수로 그들을 따러 다니고 잇섯다. 그들은 사냥에만 눈이 어두어 긔진맥진한 어린 소년에게는 별로 주의도 하지 안엇다. 날이 저물어 황혼이 나리매 사냥꾼에 쪼낀 산새들은 높픈 나무 그들에 숨어서 겨우 속살거리고 습 사이에는 황혼과 함께 두려움이 차차 농후해저서 나는 죽을 악을 쓰며 그들에게서 떨어지지 안으려고 힘썻다. 예서 떠러젓다는 꼭 죽는 수 박게 ...
그러든 차에 저- 산골자구니를 향하야 그야말로 송아지만한 노루 한 놈이 껑충껑충 굉장히 빨은 스피-드로 달녀가는 것을 발견하엿다.
「우야- 잡어라」
사냥꾼들은 일제히 고함을 지르며 날새게 쪼차가며 어떤 사람은 탕! 탕! 총질을 하는 것이엇다. 총소리가 날적마다 한참동안 처참한 반향이 왼 산중을 뒤-흔들엇다. 그럴 때마다 겨우 속살거리든 산새들은 아주 공포에 전률하는 듯 죽은듯 고요한 침목에 잠기는 것이엇다.
나도 역시 그들을 따라 빨리 달니다가 그만 눈 우흐로 확 미끄러저 넘어진 채로 듸굴듸굴 굴너 깁흔 골속으로 떨어지고 말엇다. -

이윽고 몽롱한 의식 가운데서 나는 방 등불을 보앗다. 그 다음 웬 늙은 영감의 얼골과 근심스러운 얼골로 수집은 시선을 살짝 살짝 던지는 처녀의 얼골을 보앗다. 나는 꿈이나 아닌가 하야 눈을 똑똑히 뜨고 정신을 가다듬어 가지고 다시 멧번이나 살펴보앗다. 내가 눈을 이상하게 뜨고 놀내는 표정을 지엇슴인지 그 늙은이는 빙그레 우스며 무어라고 중얼거렷다. 무슨 말인지 분명히는 알 수 업스나 압흐지 안느냐고 뭇는 모양이엿다.
