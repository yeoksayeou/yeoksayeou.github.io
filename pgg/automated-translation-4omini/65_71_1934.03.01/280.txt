잡지명	별건곤 제71호  
발행일	1934년 03월 01일  
기사제목	約婚女의 惡魔性 (The Demon Nature of the Engaged Woman)  
필자	崔流帆 (Choi Ryuhan)  
기사형태	소설 (Novel)  

The true culprit is an unexpected character. 

Rimanse stood vacantly by the window, as if bewitched by a firefly. In his mind, the image of a straw doll stabbed with a dagger and a white wall merged with the fleeting face of Park Kyung-sook. The darkness inside his head felt heavy, and from within that darkness, strange shadows began to rise. What could it be? The vague imagery of two corpses haunted Rimanse terribly. Rimanse struggled to unveil the mystery of that image. But suddenly, the fog lifted, and his mind felt as bright as day. The riddle that had felt so clear was unraveled. 

"It's the Women’s Sanatorium," he muttered to himself, and turned to the officer saying, “Please wait until I see the inspector before I go. And tell them I have found the real murderer.” The officer, bewildered by his words which seemed nonsensical at that moment, retorted, “Yes, isn’t the culprit Kim Young-ho?” 

“No, there is someone else,” Rimanse said, his voice filled with certainty. “I’ve just come to realize that. It’s an unimaginable act of terror. I will explain it when I go later.” The bewildered officer, not fully understanding the situation, left for the police station.

Rimanse went to the station and grabbed the guard. “Were there any wooden beams loaded on the freight train bound for Kaesong at 9 AM today?” The guard looked surprised and responded after some thought, “There’s actually something.” 

“Does that freight train stop at D Station?” D Station exists in the opposite direction of S Village. 

“Yes, it stops. It will also unload a bit at D Station.” 

Once this straightforward question was answered, Rimanse called the famous Women’s Sanatorium at D Station using the payphone, inquiring about patients, and seemingly satisfied with the response, returned to the police station. 

Inspector M was in the chief's office when Rimanse burst in, startling him. “I know who the real culprit is! Kim Young-ho is innocent!” he shouted. Inspector M, taken aback by the absurdity of the claim, asked, “Then who is the true culprit?” 

“You will have to see for yourself. We'll go to D Station. The culprit is a patient at the Women’s Sanatorium.” Rimanse's demeanor became increasingly strange. 

“A sick person?” Inspector M asked. 

“Yes, that's right. A patient. She pretends to be ill, but in reality, she is a mentally unstable person. How else would she be able to carry out such a terrifying and bold act of murder?” 

“I cannot even begin to understand how this is possible...” the inspector replied, suspecting Rimanse might be losing his mind. 

“You will find out. There is no precedent for this in any police record of any country. If you continue to conduct the investigation in this manner, it would be impossible to avoid grievous mistakes. Let’s go together to the sanatorium. If you can’t trust me as an inspector, you can go as an individual. If my conjecture is incorrect, it’s only a matter of wasting two hours.” 

Inspector M could not refuse Rimanse’s request and agreed to go to the Women’s Sanatorium. 

The sanatorium was located not far from D Station, nestled beautifully in a tranquil forest among white structures resembling a painting. The director, Dr. P, was a talented physician in both medicine and literature, having published several works, and thus was well acquainted with Rimanse. 

“The patient you inquired about is named Kim Young-ja, and she is being well looked after as you mentioned,” Dr. P said. 

“When did she arrive here?” Rimanse asked. 

“She came in around 9:30 this morning.” 

“Was she admitted due to illness?” 

“It seems she is suffering from nervous exhaustion. Something must have agitated her as she became overly excited. Though not necessarily a condition requiring hospital care, as you know, this place is more of a recuperation facility, allowing patients to check in at their discretion... What did this lady do?” 

When asked, Dr. P was still unaware of anything. 

“She’s the murderer,” Rimanse replied flatly. 

“The murderer?” Dr. P repeated, astonished. 

“Yes, she is the true culprit of the murder case in S Village.” 

The director was immensely shocked and hastily called for a physician to lead Rimanse and Inspector M to Kim Young-ja's room. 

When Inspector M and Rimanse opened the door to the room, Rimanse’s heart raced sharply. Upon entering, they found Yoon Young-ae staring wide-eyed in surprise. Rimanse had met Young-ae that morning and there was no way she could forget his face. Unbeknownst to Inspector M standing behind, in that moment, Young-ae realized everything about the intruders. 

“What’s this?” Rimanse suddenly rushed toward Young-ae and snatched a little glass bottle with a blue cap from her. It seemed she was prepared with poison for any eventuality. After losing the poison, she collapsed on the floor in despair, crying heavily. 

“Inspector, you know about Yoon Young-ae who disappeared from the room this morning? She has been hiding as a patient here in this sanatorium,” Rimanse explained. 

Inspector M, now a little skeptical, looked down at the weeping woman and said, “But Yoon Young-ae never went out on the day of the crime. Furthermore, the victim, Park Kyung-sook, had no romantic rival. Kim Young-ho was completely in love with Young-ae, so what would compel her to commit a murder for her?” 

“Perhaps this woman is experiencing an acute nervous breakdown and has developed disturbing hallucinations,” Rimanse speculated. 

“Yes, that is precisely it. There is a significant error in your assumptions. The identity of the suspect and the victim possesses a grave mistake. Both the victim and the perpetrator are completely unknown to you.” Rimanse confidently made a bold claim. 

“What on earth are you saying?” Inspector M exclaimed in astonishment. 

“The victim is not Park Kyung-sook. Then who was murdered?” 

“The body was brutally torn apart by dogs before identification could occur. They dressed the mutilated corpse in Park Kyung-sook's clothing and scattered her belongings at the scene.” 

“So how do you interpret Park Kyung-sook's disappearance?” 

“Park Kyung-sook could never return home. I found out that she was a keen reader of detective novels and absolutely adored them. She has read almost all of my novels, as she mentioned.” Rimanse stated with raised voice, perhaps a bit too loud. 

“Are you not trying to malign Park Kyung-sook?” 

“Not just slandering her: she killed someone. A heinous murderer.” 

“Really?” 

“Yes, indeed. Park Kyung-sook is not the victim as you surmise; she is, in fact, the perpetrator.” 

“Who did she kill?” Inspector M asked, still confused. 

“She killed Yoon Young-ae.” 

“Are you implying she can’t be in front of us now, crying?” 

“Indeed, consider this: the woman in front of us is none other than Park Kyung-sook, wearing the guise of Yoon Young-ae. She held great affections for Kim Young-ho. Though it was a forced engagement, it was indeed Park Kyung-sook who wanted it. This woman bore a deep grudge against Yoon Young-ae, who monopolized Kim Young-ho's love and resented him for betraying her. This is an inconceivable scenario that led her to create a vicious plot: to kill her romantic rival and frame Kim Young-ho as the murderer! It is truly a perfect revenge: one kills whilst the other takes the fall as a murderer, yielding two results from one action. An intricate and sophisticated method couldn't be more fitting for a study of criminal novels,” Rimanse elaborated. 

At this point, Rimanse approached the weeping Park Kyung-sook and asked, “Kyung-sook, did you hear everything I just said? And is what's being said true? You, a detective novel enthusiast, surely know the clever trick behind this all.” Driven by despair, Park Kyung-sook quietly wept, her expression confirming Rimanse was not mistaken. 

“To think Park Kyung-sook killed Yoon Young-ae and is now posing as the deceased,” Inspector M spoke in disbelief. 

“Yes, she had to. For it to work, the identity of Yoon Young-ae needed to be completely obscured for the plan to unfold successfully. Moreover, for whoever may have understood that Yoon Young-ae had gone missing, it required her to hide herself without fail. Thus, by disguising herself as Yoon Young-ae, and dismantling her alibi, Park Kyung-sook aimed to ensure that Kim Young-ho bore the blame for murder. Such cleverness is indeed astounding,” Rimanse explained. 

“Indeed, that clears her motives for destroying Young-ae's appearance,” Inspector M noted, piecing together the story. 

“And so, she used Young-ae’s absence against her; the only obstacle had been removing herself from suspicion based on their relationship. So upon her hiding as Young-ae, all suspicions against her would effectively vanish, while at the same time framing Kim Young-ho as the murderer by lying about Young-ae's alibi. This is a brilliantly calculated revenge, all borne from deeply seated jealousy.” 

Rimanse's monologue continued, “This incident illustrates the lethal power of love. This young woman has been transformed into a demon through her anguish. This crime is beyond the realm of humanity; it is the act of a ghoul unleashed from Hell.” 

Despite being humiliated and condemned, there Park Kyung-sook lay sprawled on the floor, unmoving. 

Questioning on Points of Ambiguity 

Inspector M was impressed as he listened to Rimanse’s research, his astonishment wavering. But still there were aspects of doubt. 

“Rimanse! Then isn't it true that Kim Young-ho didn't need to lie about anything either? He insists he was at Yoon Young-ae's house until late that night. It appears Young-ae was indeed in Seoul that evening. Nonetheless, she is said to have been killed far away. Isn’t that contradictory? Even should he have used a car, wouldn't it be frightening for a young woman to brave the dark country roads? Surely her elderly father would have voiced his concerns…” 

“That’s precisely the key point where ordinary people may falter; that is why I said there is no precedent in police records for this case.” Rimanse leaned in as his explanation unfolded. 

“Indeed, it is an ingeniously sinister trick. Only a wild mind would conceive of such an extraordinary mechanism.” He continued, “Earlier, as the officer brought it to your attention, the straw doll stabbed with a dagger was the test run of a dreadful plan, examining where the body would fall when loaded on a freight train.” 

“What about the freight train?” 

“To explain simply, the perpetrator who is an enthusiastic reader of detective novels knows they should be cautious, as some residue of evidence typically remains at the scene. So, rather than risking exposure during the crime itself, she planned to set the body at a location where it would be found, steering clear of the crime scene.” 

“Excellent.” 

“That method of placement was made possible because of fortunate timing. The train passed by, situated right next to Yoon Young-ae's room and the train tracks adjoining, enabling the victim’s bodies to be tossed out as the train sped away.” 

“This required Park Kyung-sook to have frequented the site where the cargo train would stop outside of Yoon Young-ae's house when she was known to be absent.” 

“Quite right. Park Kyung-sook took advantage of the moment to deliver the ‘body’ to the freight train and predict where it should fall after leaving the station. Given the long freight train and the gradient towards the tunnel, the initial speeds would mean the staggered height of the platform would lead to delay in falling. Knowing where it falls later would have never caused any hindrance. Then, it was executed with each of Young-ho’s planned trips to Yoon Young-ae’s residence precisely when she was absent, allowing Park Kyung-sook to enter unnoticed and quietly murder her.” 

“Rimanse, are you really claiming that Park Kyung-sook successfully disguised herself to appear as Yoon Young-ae?” M asked incredulously. 

“Yes, because she had just had to appear like Young-ae, resembling that woman became necessary as well. By making her absence known to make sure her body would not be found, she meant to be utterly unidentifiable as alive. Young-ae, who assumed the role of the dead, was molded into Park Kyung-sook’s character, creating her own duplicate in form amongst the populace.” 

“Unless found, no one would ever suspect a thing.” 

“In truth, connecting everything might be as simple as realizing this elusive manipulation.” 

“Of course, if my deductions are found to have any flaws, feel free to correct me. However, I'm inclined to think there’s little room for debate.” Rimanse leaned back, confident in his assertions. 

Thus, the case of the S Village murder was resolved. 

Imaginative Crime of the Engaged Woman 

Inside the automobile headed for Seoul, Inspector M posed another question to Rimanse. 

“Still, I wonder how you discerned the whereabouts of Park Kyung-sook when she escaped aboard that train?” 

“This is because it was common knowledge that the freight train halted behind the Women’s Sanatorium at 9 AM and she hid amongst the loading materials. Since she could risk discovery while aboard the freight until D Station, she planned sneaking out beforehand. Upon securing her escape, she realized that the sanatorium was conveniently located nearby. Given that the facility provides refuge, it would serve as an excellent hideout for a fugitive.” 

“Yet we did not know any of this, did we?” 

“And there's another point of ambiguity, too. Although it is clear that Park Kyung-sook forged a letter signed by K left on her desk, the bloody cloth found in Young-ho's home was a curious piece in itself.” 

“That must have been taken when the servant left to do the laundry. She had likely taken it then.” 

“After the crime had passed, she strategically placed evidence on display before the staff.” 

“However, they once confirmed that the blood was human blood.” 

“That I cannot say for sure, yet obtaining just a single drop isn't difficult. Provided there was a syringe, they could inject it into themselves without much ado. There is potential that even now remnants might remain in Park Kyung-sook’s arm.” 

“Rimanse,” the inspector shook his head, “As for laboratories checking blood samples, I should not doubt their reports.” 

“I, too, am fully aware of that yet such actions are common in detective novels where securing evidence, though simple, proves quite elusive.” 

Inspector M, deeply impressed, nodded appreciatively more than once. 

“I deeply regret dismissing your theories as mere fantasies. For a crime like this, I would have never considered it important. Henceforth, I will be certain to be guided by your insights in actual matters.” 

Rimanse and Inspector M shared a friendly and unguarded camaraderie. 

**Summary**

This story presents a thrilling narrative involving carefully plotted murder, identity deceit, and the intricacies of love and jealousy leading to crime. It dives into the psychological motivations driving the characters, particularly focusing on the engaged woman's dramatic transformation into a perpetrator. The vivid portrayal of mind games and manipulation serves as a reminder of the societal pressures faced during the 1930-1940s, especially for women in colonial Korea. Historians may find interest in how this thematic representation reflects broader issues of female agency and the darker aspects of interpersonal relationships in colonial Korea.
