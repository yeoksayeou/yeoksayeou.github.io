Imjin War and the Japanese Army  
First Wave  
- On the fourth day of the fourth month in the twenty-fifth year of Seonjo, Japanese troops landed in Busan -  

The first deployment of the Japanese army during the Imjin War numbered approximately 300,000, with the preliminary deployment as follows.  

First Legion - Konishi Yukinaga, Munenori Yoshichi, Matsuura Takanobu and other six commanders (18,700 troops)  

Second Legion - Katō Kiyomasa, Hirado Naoshige, Sagara Yoshimura and three commanders (22,800 troops)  

Third Legion - Kuroda Nagamasa and Ōtomo Yoshitune (21,000 troops)  

Fourth Legion - Mōri Yoshinari, Shimazu Yoshihiro and other six commanders (14,000 troops)  

Fifth Legion - Fukushima Masanori, Nagamasa and other six commanders (25,000 troops)  

Sixth Legion - Kobayakawa Takakage, Mōra Hideo, Tachibana Munenari and other five commanders (15,700 troops)  

Seventh Legion - Mōri Terumoto (30,000 troops)  

Eighth Legion - Ukita Hideie (10,000 troops)  

Ninth Legion - Hashiba Hidekatsu and Hosokawa Tadaoki, two commanders at Ikki (11,500 troops)  

Second Wave  
- The second invasion, in the thirtieth year of Seonjo -  

First Legion - Second Legion, Katō Kiyomasa, Konishi Yukinaga, Munenori Yoshichi, Matsuura Takanobu, and Arima Harunobu seven commanders (10,000 troops from Katō, 14,100 from Konishi and others)  

Third Legion - Kuroda Nagamasa, Mōri Yoshinari, Sō Katsunaga, Shimazu Toyohisa and other eight commanders (10,000 troops)  

Fourth Legion - Nabeshima Naoshige, Sō Katsushige (12,000 troops)  

Fifth Legion - Shimazu Yoshihiro (10,000 troops)  

Sixth Legion - Nagamasa and other seven commanders including Tōdō Takatora and Katō Yasuaki (13,300 troops)  

Seventh Legion - Hachisuka Masanori, Ikoma Hisamasa, Waki Chūan among three commanders (11,100 troops)  

Eighth Legion - Mōri Hidemoto and Ukita Hideie (30,000 troops from Hidemoto, 10,000 troops from Hideie)  

In addition, 10,000 troops were stationed at Busan under Kobayakawa Hideaki, and 5,000 troops at Angolpo under Tachibana Munenari. Other small detachments included Takahashi Naotaka at Kadekishima with 1,000 troops, Mōra Hideo at Takeshima with 1,000 troops, Asano Yoshinaga at Nishiseungura with 3,000 troops, totaling 141,500 troops. <7>

**Summary**  

This article outlines the military organization and troop strengths of the Japanese army during the Imjin War (壬辰乱), noting the significant number of forces involved in both waves of invasion. Historians studying this period may find it intriguing how the Japanese military strategy was structured and the scale of the conflict, as it showcases the intensity of the invasions and the military capabilities of Japan during the late 16th century. The statistics provided here could enhance understanding of the scale and impact of these invasions on Korean history.
