They Saw the Japan-China Conflict: Chinese Moving Discussion Meeting  
Attendees  
Hui Ah-in (誰雅仁) Minister of Religion  
Wang Zhi-Jiang (王之江) Dumpling Shop  
Nam Seung-Pok (南承撲) Dumpling Shop  
Zhang San-De (張三德) Restaurant  
Deng Chao (鄧 超) Carpenter  
Liu Fu-Tang (劉福堂) Hotteok Shop  
A Person from the Magazine  

Record: "The weather suddenly became cold."  
Nam: "Oh, it is cold."  
Record: "With such thick clothes, it must be less cold for us Koreans!"  
Zhang: "What's that about?"  
Record: "Where is your hometown?" (to Nam)  
Nam: "Our Shandong."  
Record: "How about Liu?"  
Liu: "It's about the same."  
Hui: "Everyone here is from Shandong."  
Record: "Is business good lately?"  
Nam: "Business?"  
Record: "Are you earning money? Is it selling well?"  
Nam: "Oh! Is it selling well? Not well. It's hard."  
Nam: "How about you Liu?"  
Liu: "It's just about the same."  
Record: "What is written on the wall?"  
Hui: "Oh, that's (自暴動以來現錢交易慨不*賬=what's written on the wall) nothing at all."  
Record: "Ever since the Manchurian Incident, everything is cash-only, no more credit."  
Nam: "Haha... that's too exaggerated."  
Record: "Is it the same among Chinese people?"  
Wang: "All the same."  
Record: "From where?"  
Liu: "If that happens again, we’ll return to our hometown. Then how will we earn money?"  
Record: "Did you get into trouble then?"  
Nam: "Our hometown has some folks there."  
Record: "What are you doing, Hui? Have a drink."  
Hui: "I can't. I am a Christian."  
Record: "What do you do?"  
Hui: "I am a church minister."  
Record: "And Deng?"  
Deng: "I'm a carpenter."  
Record: "How long have you been in Korea?"  
Deng: "Three years."  
Wang: "We've been here one year."  
Record: "And Liu?"  
Liu: "It's been ten years."  
Record: "Looking quite dapper, but only earning money."  
Liu: "Haha... not a single penny earned. It's all by my bare hands."  
Record: "Zhang! They say the Yeobindwoo and Bokhaehyeon were sold to the Japanese."  
Zhang: "They say so. Only the houses were sold."  
Record: "How much were they sold for?"  
Zhang: "Bokhaehyeon was for 7,000 won, and Yeobindwoo about 130,000 won, or something like that."  
Record: "Why don’t you go back to your hometown?"  
Zhang: "Not possible with the war."  
Record: "From where?"  
Zhang: "I don't know. Just watch."  
Record: "Do you hear news from China these days?"  
Wang: "We don't know, really."  
Record: "Do you read newspapers?"  
Deng: "We don’t read."  
Hui: "The newspapers from China are confiscated by the authorities, and the newspapers here are all lies."  
Wang: "We don't read the newspapers. You have to pay, and all they tell are lies."  
Record: "Then do you know that Japan and China are fighting in Manchuria?"  
Wang: "I know."  
Record: "How do you know?"  
Nam: "Heh-hee. I don’t know that."  
Record: "What happened due to the fighting?"  
Nam: "We don’t know."  
Record: "Hui, what about you?"  
Hui: "I haven't been here long, so I don't know the news."  
Record: "How can you not know about your own country's affairs?"  
Wang: "How can we know? We don’t know."  
Zhang: "Even if we hear that we are fighting, China doesn’t think of fighting."  
Record: "Manchuria has almost been occupied by the Japanese military, what about that?"  
Nam: "Even if Manchuria is taken, that’s fine. China is still a big nation."  
Record: "Don't speak nonsense. Japan has already occupied Jinzhou (金州), right? If they keep this up, they will occupy Tianjin and Beiping tomorrow or the day after!"  
Nam: "I don’t know, I don’t know. It’s all lies!"  
Record: "There’s an independence movement in Manchuria. They talk about Mongolia and China separating for independence. What do you think about that?"  
Hui: "We don’t know. But that’s just nonsense. They can’t achieve independence."  
Liu: "This war started because of Chen You-Ren (陳友仁). That bastard!"  
Record: "From where?"  
Deng: "That guy went to Japan to perform (演劇)."  
Record: "It's true that Chen You-Ren went to Japan, but I don't think he's that kind of person."  
Deng: "Isn’t he the Minister of Foreign Affairs in the Guangdong government?"  
Deng: "We don't know about that."  
Record: "Who is the best among the Chinese?"  
Wang: "The best people are the correct Koreans and the good ones."  
Record: "Zhang Xueliang (張學良)?"  
Wang: "He is a good person."  
Zhang: "Zhang Xueliang is still young! It's unfortunate that he was driven out of Manchuria."  
Record: "Liu! Who is the best?"  
Liu: "Wu Peifu (吳佩孚) is the best."  
Record: "Wu Peifu? He doesn’t even have soldiers or money now, what!"  
Liu: "I don’t know. If Feng Yuxiang (馮玉祥) had not betrayed Wu Peifu, he wouldn’t have ended up like this."  
Record: "Wu Peifu is from Shandong, right?"  
Liu: "Hehe, all Shandong people are good."  
Record: "What about Chiang Kai-shek (張介石)?"  
Wang: "He is a good person."  
Liu: "Chiang Kai-shek has talent."  
Record: "Because he has money and soldiers?"  
Zhang: "Only with money and soldiers."  
Record: "Chiang Kai-shek has already resigned!"  
Zhang: "That’s a lie."  
Record: "From where?"  
Zhang: "Oh? He has been saying he would resign, but he hasn’t actually done it."  
Liu: "He has resigned, but just in words, not in reality."  
Record: "What about Wang Chao-Ming (汪兆銘)?"  
Nam: "He is a good person."  
Wang: "He is even a better person than Chiang Kai-shek."  
Liu: "Wang Chao-Ming worked with Sun Yat-sen (孫逸仙), but Chiang Kai-shek was under him."  
Record: "What about Wang Ting-Zheng (王廷正)?"  
Nam: "He is a good person."  
Wang: "He was chased out due to the students."  
Record: "What about Ma Zhan-Shan (馬占山)?"  
Nam: "He is a good person."  
Record: "Where is he from?"  
Nam: "I don’t know, but he is a good person."  
Liu: "He is from Heilongjiang."  
Record: "What about Yuan Jin-Hai (袁金鎧)?"  
Wang: "He is a traitorous person."  
Record: "From where?"  
Wang: "I don’t know. Wherever, he is a traitorous person."  
Record: "Are you not thinking of going to war?"  
Wang: "No, why?"  
Nam: "Even if Japan and China fight, it has nothing to do with our hometown in Shandong."  
Record: "I’m sorry. I’ve talked for a long time."  
All: "Not at all!"  

**Summary**  

This dialogue reflects the perspectives of Chinese immigrants in Korea regarding the ongoing conflicts between Japan and China during the 1930s. The discussions highlight economic difficulties, a sense of detachment from the war, and a mix of political opinions about various Chinese leaders. Historians might find the sentiments revealing as they showcase how colonial subjects navigated their identity and loyalties amidst regional tensions and illustrate the impact of events like the Manchurian Incident on local communities.
