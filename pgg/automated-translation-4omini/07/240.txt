잡지명	별건곤  
발행일	1927년 07월 01일  
기사제목	朝鮮映畵界 唯一의 花形女優 一問一答 申一仙孃과의 問答記, 履歷·出演經驗·結婚問題·希望 (The Only Star Actress in the Korean Film Industry: A Q&A with Shin Il-sun, including her Biography, Acting Experience, Marriage Matters, and Hopes)  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Discussion)  

The only star actress in the Korean film industry: A Q&A with Shin Il-sun, including her biography, acting experience, marriage matters, and hopes.

Date: June 20, Evening

Location: Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), Kwanhun-dong

Participants: Shin Il-sun and the reporter  

Reporter: “I suppose you are quite embarrassed by what I might ask, so I will aim to provide answers that would satisfy not just me, but the fans who love your films.”  

Shin: “Yes.”  

Reporter: “I will be asking questions not so much for my own sake or the magazine's sake, but for the sake of those who adore your work.”  

Shin: (just smiles)  

Reporter: “So, may I ask your actual age?”  

Shin: “I’m sixteen.”  

Reporter: “Where were you born?”  

Shin: “Seoul, in Gyunji-dong.”  

Reporter: “What did you do before you entered the theater world?”  

Shin: “I attended O Deok Girls' Normal School. I entered first grade at the age of ten and completed up to the first year of the fourth grade by the age of thirteen before I got involved in theater and eventually left school.”  

Reporter: “How did you come to join the theater?”  

Shin: “About three years ago, a troupe called the Joseon Arts Troupe was formed, wasn’t it? When Mr. Kim Mun-pil, who used to act, established it. At that time, my older brother was one of the executives in that troupe.”  

Reporter: “Yes, I see.”  

Shin: “However, during that summer, the actors and executives of the troupe reached an impasse and went on strike. Consequently, my brother, as one of the executives, arranged various temporary measures which led to me becoming a female performer.”  

Reporter: “How old were you at that time?”  

Shin: “I was thirteen. I entered the Joseon Arts Troupe at thirteen and started going on tours.”  

Reporter: “What did you do while on tour?”  

Shin: “I participated in plays but also sang solo at every place we visited, receiving applause. Even when I was in school, I was good at singing every day.”  

Reporter: “What was your first role with the arts troupe?”  

Shin: “I played the role of a raccoon in a play called ‘The Raccoon and the Tortoise.’”  

Reporter: “And then what led you to transition to the film industry?”  

Shin: “When the Joseon Arts Troupe returned from touring all over Korea, another strike arose, and my brother left to organize the Bando Arts Troupe. I was with the Bando Arts Troupe and toured the western regions of Korea. After touring, when the Bando Arts Troupe was disbanded, it transformed into the Dongban (East) Arts Troupe, which later became the Haidong (Sea East) Arts Troupe, and I thus became a member of the Haidong Arts Troupe.”  

Reporter: “There’s been quite a change, indeed.”  

Shin: “Yes, a great deal has changed. It must have been really tough for my brother.”  

Reporter: “What led to your move to the film industry?”  

Shin: “While the Haidong Arts Troupe was performing in Hamgyeong-do in Hŭnghŭng, I met some members of the Dolwolhee (土月會) troupe. Among them was Mr. Lee Kyŏng-son, who saw me and consulted with my brother about my potential. He then introduced me to the Joseon Cinema Company upon his return to Seoul, which led to my immediate participation in ‘Arirang.’”  

Reporter: “Haha! So ‘Arirang’ was your first film.”  

Shin: “(Nods, smiling gently)”  

Reporter: “Since ‘Arirang,’ how many films have you appeared in up to now?”  

Shin: “After ‘Arirang,’ there was ‘The Crown of Bonghwang,’ then ‘The Identity of the Monster,’ followed by ‘The Field Mouse,’ and I’m currently shooting ‘Goldfish.’”  

Reporter: “Having tried both theater and film, which do you find more pleasing to perform in?”  

Shin: “In terms of immediate satisfaction, theater is more fulfilling. However, with moving pictures, the first time you view the test screenings, it’s quite thrilling and enjoyable.”  

Reporter: “Which medium do you believe offers more potential for success?”  

Shin: “I would say moving pictures do.”  

Reporter: “While you’re currently acting in films, at what point does it feel best for you—when you are getting along with someone, or when you have a romantic scene?”  

Shin: “Hehe, why must I be asked that? (smiles)”  

Reporter: “Fans are naturally curious about those kinds of emotions.”  

Shin: “The best times are when I get to greet an older brother I haven't seen in a long time; it makes my heart swell with joy.”  

Reporter: “In movies, romantic scenes with love interests often occur more frequently; don’t those moments make your heart race?”  

Shin: “(Bowing her head) That seems so.”  

Reporter: “What are the hardest times you face while acting?”  

Shin: “It’s toughest when I have to put up a fight against a villain trying to overpower the heroine. For instance, in ‘Arirang,’ when Zhu In-gye’s character comes at me, I really struggle to push him away.”  

Reporter: “Are there times when you just can’t get into the role?”  

Shin: “Why wouldn't that happen? I can lose focus during a scene, especially if the weather turns cloudy. I feel the images may not come out clearly and lose the joy of acting, or if I happen to think about something else, I get agitated.”  

Reporter: “That makes sense. They say your best expressions are when you smile, but what about the hardest expressions?”  

Shin: “Surprise is hard. I need to practice a lot, looking in the mirror and recalling times I was genuinely surprised.”  

Reporter: “Among all the films you've participated in, which do you think you performed best in?”  

Shin: “I would say ‘The Crown of Bonghwang.’”  

Reporter: “But there are criticisms that ‘The Crown of Bonghwang’ is not a well-made film.”  

Shin: “Well, the film as a whole isn’t necessarily good, but judging by my own performances, it was a standout for me.”  

Reporter: “Of all the scenes you acted in, which specific moment do you think was your best expression?”  

Shin: “I don’t know if there’s a particularly great one, but in ‘The Crown of Bonghwang,’ when I was lost in daydreaming, I feel that was particularly well done. Have you seen that film?”  

Reporter: “No, I haven’t.”  

Reporter: “In your mind, what stands out as your worst performance?”  

Shin: “In ‘The Field Mouse,’ when I cried alone that was rather poor in performance.”  

Reporter: “During your various film appearances, have there been moments when you had to force feelings?”  

Shin: “Yes, I have. One time was while filming ‘The Identity of the Monster.’”  

Reporter: “Why was that the case?”  

Shin: “The co-actors weren’t in sync, and additionally, the role I played didn’t resonate with me at all. I was just struggling to fulfill the role.”  

Reporter: “What role do you believe suits you best?”  

Shin: “It may sound silly, but I feel playful, lively characters resonate with me more. Perhaps it’s because I’m still young.”  

Reporter: “Though a bit out of order, during romantic scenes, how does it feel to hold a male co-star’s neck and touch cheeks while crying?”  

Shin: “Regardless of whose cheek it is, there is no distinction. I secretly think about what it would be like if I were genuinely in love with someone during such moments, and that naturally makes my emotions raw.”  

Reporter: “Ah, so it’s not hard to express that melancholic emotion?”  

Shin: “That’s right. When I’m performing on stage, there are occasions when I truly feel so emotional I end up shedding tears.”  

Reporter: “That must be a good thing. By the way, did you recently watch any Western films that particularly impressed you?”  

Shin: “Oh, there was one about a country girl who comes to the capital to become a female performer and falls in love with the director, I think.”  

Reporter: “Is it the film ‘Through the Dark Clouds’ that was screened at Dansungsa?”  

Shin: “Yes, yes—that’s the one! It was remarkable. The story was great, and the performances were wonderful.”  

Reporter: “What kind of films do you usually prefer?”  

Shin: “I like comedies the most.”  

Reporter: “Who is your favorite foreign actor?”  

Shin: “Among women, I like the ‘Dainty Girl’ played by Corinne; among men, please don’t laugh, my favorite is the comedian Reginald Denny.”  

Reporter: “While acting in films, have you had any near accidents?”  

Shin: “Yes, there were two times when I almost died. For ‘The Identity of the Monster,’ I went all the way to the top of Mount Bukak to perform and, leaping from a high rock, I stumbled badly and ended up hitting my head, losing consciousness. I still have a scar on my forehead from that incident. And during the current filming of ‘Goldfish,’ I nearly drowned in the Han River after slipping and falling.”  

Reporter: “How did that happen? There are even ludicrous rumors that you attempted suicide by jumping in.”  

Shin: “Those rumors are just absurd fabrications. It was a romantic scene by the riverside, and while I was on a rock, I accidentally slipped and couldn’t even call out before plunging into the water. But at that moment, I thought about how I might die, and even now, I can vividly remember flailing my arms underwater.”  

Reporter: “That was truly perilous. But now let’s leave the topic of films and discuss something a bit more personal.”  

Shin: “What do you mean?”  

Reporter: “You’re still sixteen, but how do you feel about marriage?”  

Shin: “Oh!” she exclaims, bowing her head and laughing.  

Reporter: “But in all likelihood, that question must come up since it’s unavoidable. I’m sure your fans are particularly curious about this.”  

Shin: “I plan to act until I’m twenty.”  

Reporter: “And then, what happens when you turn twenty? Will you marry simply?”  

Shin: “That’s right.”  

Reporter: “If you were to marry, what kind of man do you think you would marry?”  

Shin: “Even though it might seem silly to say, I need to say something for the sake of my fans. I might marry an artist, or it could be someone entirely unrelated to the  arts.”  

Shin: “(Bowing her head more deeply) An artist.”  

Reporter: “I see.”  

Shin: “(Bowing further)”  

Reporter: “Originally, as you have some background in music, did you not think about studying music?”  

Shin: “Of course I want to study music.”  

Reporter: “Who has truly supported you and guided you throughout your journey?”  

Shin: “Only my older brother, and also Lee Kyŏng-son and Ra Un-kyu.”  

Reporter: “Do you receive many letters?”  

Shin: “Yes.”  

Reporter: “How many usually?”  

Shin: “Already, I have over three hundred letters. My older brother reads them all. I receive thank-you letters from women, while letters from men mostly contain disreputable love declarations.”  

Reporter: “Do you reply to them?”  

Shin: “What replies?”  

Reporter: “In Korea, there seems to be a perception that actresses are viewed as lowly or scandalous. How do you feel about that?”  

Shin: “That’s just because they don’t know better. As long as I don’t engage in that behavior, it suffices. That’s why I always go to the theater with my brother and don’t often go out with others.”  

Reporter: “What kind of sport do you enjoy?”  

Shin: “I like gymnastics.”  

Reporter: “Is that really suitable for a young lady?”  

Shin: “But I enjoy it.”  

Reporter: “What about reading at home? What kinds of books do you like?”  

Shin: “I read art magazines and Korean history.”  

Reporter: “As you do perform in the theater, among narrators, who do you think is the best?”  

Shin: “As of now, I think Kim Yeong-hwan is the best.”  

Reporter: “What about food? Is it three meals a day, or do you have snacks as well?”  

Shin: “Three meals.”  

Reporter: “Anything special you like?”  

Shin: “Such as banks and bananas (smiles).”  

Reporter: “Ah, I’ll let you go now since it seems I’ve kept you long enough. Is there anything more you’d like to say?”  

Shin: “I wouldn’t have much else to say. Just one thing I wish to ask everyone is to watch the films I’ve appeared in and share your evaluations either directly through letters or otherwise.”  

She seems quite timid, making it difficult to ask further questions.  

Her older brother is Shin Chang-un.  

**Summary**  

This article presents an engaging interview with Shin Il-sun, one of Korea’s leading actresses in the 1920s, touching on her background, entry into the performing arts, and her thoughts on marriage and the film industry. Her reflections reveal both personal aspirations and the struggles of being a female performer in colonial Korea, where actresses often faced societal challenges and stigma. Historians would find the insights into her life representative of the challenges faced by women in the arts during this period, offering a glimpse into cultural norms and the evolving landscape of Korean cinema.

記 『여러가지 사진을 박히는 중에는 더러 마음에 실흔 것을 억지로 억지로 하는 때가 잇겟지요』

仙 『좀 우습지만 까불까불 유쾌하게 뛰노는 인물이 제게는 마저요. 아마 나희가 어려서 그런가 바요.』
記 『좀 순서가 밧귀엿지만 련애하는 장면을 박히노라고 애인 노릇하는 남자 배우의 목을 얼사 안고 뺨과 뺨을 마조대고 눈물짓는 형용을 할 때에 당신의 마음이 엇덧슴닛가. 좃슴닛가? 그런 일을 하게 되는 것이 마음에 실혀짐닛가.』
仙 『그것이 어느 배우의 뺨이거나 그런 분간은 업서요. 속으로 내가 정말 누구와 련애를 하다가 이런 경우를 당하면 엇저나 하고 생각하게 되고 그러 하닛가 저절로 마음이 비창해저요.』
記 『그래 저래 비창한 표정이 저절로 잘 되겟네요』
仙 『그럿탐니다. 저는 무대 우에서 연극을 할 때에도 정말 설허서 눈물을 흘니고 운 때가 만하요.』
記 『그건 조흔 일이겟지요. 그런데 이 번에는 최근에 구경하신 서양사진 중에 무엇이 제일 좃튼가 말슴해 주십시오.』

記 『당신이 제일 조와하는 사진은 대개 엇던 종료의 것임닛가.』
仙 『희활극이 제일 조와요.』
記 『당신이 가장 조와하는 외국배우＜101＞는 누구임닛가.』
仙 『녀자 치고는 『사치한 계집애』하던 코-린무-어 고요. 남자는 웃지 마세요 희극배우 레지날드 떼니야요.』
記 『활동 사진을 박히다가 잘못하야 몸을 닷치는 일도 잇겟지요.』
仙 『잇고 말고요. 그 동안에도 벌서 두 번이나 죽을 번 하엿담니다. 「괴인의 정톄」라는 것을 박힐 때 북악산 도선암까지 긔여 올나가서 놉다란 바위에서 뛰여 나리다가 잘못되야 머리가 터지고 인사불성이 되엿섯슴니다. 그 때에 험집이 지금껏 이마 우에 남어잇서요. 그리고 이번 「금붕어」를 박힐 때에 한강에 나갓다가 물에 빠저서 한 동안 죽엇다가 요행으로 살아 낫지요.』
記 『엇저다가 빠젓슴닛가. 세상에는 당신이 자살하려고 일부러 빠젓다는 당치도 안흔 소문까지 잇스니요.』
仙 『그 까짓 허풍선이 소문이야 종잡을 것 잇슴닛가. 애인과 애인이 강가에서 이약이하는 장면이라나요. 강가에 바위 우에 안젓는데 발을 헛드듸여서 그만 아이그머니 소리 한 마듸 밧게 못하고 풍덩바저 드러갓슴니다. 그려 그래도 이제는 죽엇고나 하는 생각 까지는 낫서요. 그러고 지금도 물 속에서 두 팔을 휘젓든 생각 까지는 남니다. 』
記 『참말 위태한 일이외다. 그런데 인제는 사진에 관한 이약이는 그만 두고요 다른 일을 좀 거북한 것을 뭇겟슴니다. 』
仙 『무슨 말슴이서요.』
記 『아즉 열여섯살 이시라닛가 일르기도 하지만은 결혼문뎨에 관해서 엇더케 생각하는 일이 업슴닛가?』
