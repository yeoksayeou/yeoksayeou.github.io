Recording of Thoughts!! If It Had Been That Way, How Would Korea Be Now, If Daewongun Had Not Rejected Foreigners  
Chao Kyu-su  
The changes in human affairs differ across the ages, yet the seasonal phenomena are the same each year; as the seasons return, last year feels like the year before. As the green leaves begin to grow at this time, they say that the mullet fishing on the Han River is fresh and lively. Sweeping aside the myriad of dust and busyness, I squeeze out a moment of leisure, stepping briskly into the light of day as if boarding a streetcar, reaching the southwest corner of the training institute, transferring to the Southern Line, and arriving at the beautiful Jangchungdan Park after a slight rain. Then I lingered a moment, looking at the waist of Mukmaek Mountain to the east, walking step by step, passing through the dilapidated castle wall of Namsomun, and arriving at the Riverside Hotel on the Han River. With a bottle of takju (Korean alcoholic drink) in one hand and a fishing rod worth a penny in the other, dressed in a wind-blown jacket and a straw hat, I stepped onto a small boat. Watching the fishing rod whirl, it reminded me of simpler times, waiting by the riverside for a glimpse of the moon on the waters of Weishui. The silver fish responded with glimmers reflecting the sunlight as I lifted my rod and cast it gleefully into the waters. After catching a fish, I raised my takju bottle and downed a hefty gulp, pulling out the knife I had folded away and slicing my bait lazily, savoring my catch while singing a tune about the shimmering beauty of the river.

Passing the corner of the Western Ice Storage, I watched the scenery behind me fade, moving towards Noryangjin, taking another sip and thumping the boat's side, sing a song about the autumn moon and spring breezes while watching the waves break around my boat. Suddenly startled by a flock of swans, I came across a clean sandy beach laid out like a white sheet. This beach, formerly known for carrying out executions, is said to have been a place of 500 years of bloodshed during the infamous “Saenamteo” executions.

If this sandy beach had such transformations, what changes there are for the people who own this landscape. It is now the 15th year of King Gwanghae, or perhaps in the 1st year of Injo's reign around March in the year of Gyeong-hae (or 1610). The notorious gunner Yu Si-seo, suspected of treason, had been seized and found himself drinking from the waters of the Han, suddenly committing suicide by jumping into the river while lamenting. If one imagines the river of that time surging over that execution place, one might wonder about the state of the waters.

Under the moonlight of the March full moon, reflecting on how it would have been to gaze upon the beautiful Aam Pavilion, one recalls the struggle that must have been present at that time, though the body is dusted clean and feeling refreshed. It was, after all, a moment that led to the waters soaked with the blood of countless martyrs over the last 500 years. If asked about the historical figures therein, most were branded as traitors; all who were involved in the rebellion were labeled as renegades.

Fueled by anger towards the treachery, an inspirational cry broke forth, urging the people to rise from the despair they found themselves in. In frustration, their blood splattered onto that sandy beach as they faced their end in a heroic act of defiance. This incident culminated in the eddies of the Han River sweeping away heroes from throughout history.

Moreover, it was the great foreign religious conflict that arose on January 22nd of the year of the Gyeong-hae, leading to executions of Christians. The mass execution wasn’t merely a stand-alone event, as there had been previous incidents regarding Christians and other faiths throughout Korean history. The introduction of Catholicism started in the 27th year of Seonjo, in the year of Gyeong-ju (or 1853), with missionary activities coming from Jesuit priests like Nocarius and Cospeth. By the 12th year of King Jeongjo, there had been warnings regarding an alarming growth of the religion among the masses, and in the years of King Jeongjo's reign, the church's fortunes began to grow rapidly.

By the time of the incident of January Gyeong-hae, the massacre may have appeared as a frivolous method born from misunderstandings and fears about the foreign influences in a changing world. The growing tensions stemming from foreign incursions compelled the ruling class to reconsider their policies towards these religious affiliations. The Daewongun’s harsh treatment of foreign relations led to a national crisis. The convictions of many monks were regarded as threats that demanded a severe response from the Daewongun, who took action that ended in bloodshed.

On the 22nd of January, the historical massacre of Catholic Christians on Saenamteo served to underline significant issues surrounding external influence, threatening the very fabric of traditional Korean beliefs at that time. The Daewongun's actions illuminated how ignorance might perish under the guise of state policy or as a reaction to foreign encroachments.

Had the Daewongun conceded ground and embraced the growing pressure of foreign powers, might this not have led to a different outcome? By refusing to accept the liberals’ calls for modernization and rejecting foreign intervention decisively, the Daewongun ultimately blocked Korea's engagement with the modern world. The geopolitical shifts during his time indicated a lack of insight that might have otherwise opened pathways toward progress if only he had demonstrated statesmanship amidst the transitions of the era.

With a sobering hindsight of events surrounding the Gyeong-hae era, there is a reflection on whether it was possible to create a better future had the Daewongun held the world in a wider perspective. If strategic policies had been enacted to modernize and advance the nation instead of isolating from foreign diplomacy, it could have set Korea on a path of significant growth rather than entrenching it in isolation. This contemplation draws one to wonder about the alternate histories that could have unfolded had the Daewongun embraced reform instead of opting for isolation.

**Summary**

This article reflects on the consequences of the Daewongun's rejection of foreign influence, particularly concerning the massacre of Catholics in Korea in 1866. Presented through a personal narrative that contrasts Korea's rich past with its self-imposed isolation, the author argues that the Daewongun's hardline stance against foreign powers and religions led to tragic outcomes for both the Catholic community and the nation's path forward. Historians may find this articulation of events significant as it connects personal anecdotes with broader historical narratives, illustrating the complexities surrounding Korea's identity and its relationship with globalization during the late 19th century.

그리고 그 翌年 春에 露國人이 慶興府使에게 투서한 일이 잇고 또 戊辰年에 미국군함 一隻이 평안도 대동강구에 들어오는 것을 포격하야 逃歸케한 일이 잇섯드니 辛未 4월에 미국군함 5척이 江華에 襲來하야 廣城津, 德津의 상하를 포격함으로 中軍於在淵이 逆擊하다가 전사하고 米艦도 이어 捲退하니 그것도 戰勝한 듯이 당국자의 豪氣가 놉핫다. 그리하야 洋夷는 하잘것이 업서서 迎擊만 하면 敗歸하는 것으로만 안다. 그 당시 攝政의 지위에 잇서서 軍國重事乃至政令施措를 모다 摠攬하는 大院君으로서도 對外 排斥熱이 머리 털끗까지 뻗치엇다. 「洋夷侵犯, 非戰卽和, 主和賣國」이라는 12자를 特筆大書한 石碑를 전 경성의 正中央인 鐘路大街에 ＜16＞ 세우고 전 민중에게 排外的 결심을 보이니 强固한 쇄국주의가 國是로 完定되엇다.

大院君이 이러햇드면
潯陽江頭의 琵琶婦가 血色羅裙을 입든 시대의 聲價를 자랑한다 드니 이것만 해도 우리의 血色羅裙을 밉든 시대의 일이다. 「總爲浮雲能蔽日」을 늣기는 이 자리에 잇서서 그러한 허무한 斥和論의 쇄국주의를 다시 비판할 것이 엄지마는 亡洋稱牢라 할가 成服後藥方文이라 할가 그때 만능의 권력을 부리든 大院君으로서 世界大勢에 우연만한 각오가 잇서서 그러한 쇄국정책을 膠守치 말고 시대에 순응하야 『이러케 하얏드면 오늘날 이 지경은 안이 되지 안엇슬가』하는 생각은 엄지 아니하다.
