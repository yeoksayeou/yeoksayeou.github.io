발행일	1927년 07월 01일  
기사제목	編輯局員總出 내가 第一 창피하얏든 일, 記者 1人 1話- (An Embarrassing Incident, One Reporter’s Tale)  
필자	車相瓚 (Cha Sang-jam)  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)

Embarrassment! What kind of embarrassment could be worse than this? I was in middle school at the time. At that time, one of my older brothers was a writer at a certain newspaper, and another was a scholar living in the countryside. One summer, my brother from the countryside came to meet our brother in Seoul. When the three of us gathered together, how could I possibly express the joy of that reunion? After discussing a variety of topics, we decided to take a tour of the city. 

As we were walking about, we unexpectedly encountered one of our friends from our hometown. He insisted on treating my elder brother to drinks and asked us to follow him somewhere. Of course, being unfamiliar with urban life in Seoul, and though we had been living there for a long time, we still had no experience of drinking alcohol freely, and we didn’t even know what a bar or a tavern was. 

We wandered around the alleys (previously, we were in Namcheon Hoedong) until we finally arrived at a place that had a long sign that read "별별락주가" (Byeolbyeollakjuga). Since we had only seen paper lanterns at local taverns in the countryside, we thought that the taverns in Seoul would have the same kinds of lanterns and entered without any forethought, expecting drinks to be available there. 

As we opened the door, several women, dressed in skirts and with oiled hair and powdered faces, came out and said, "Please come in!" Until that moment, we thought that women in these places were just selling liquor. When we eventually entered the room, what happened next was astonishing. Women came rushing from every corner, offering drinks and chattering away as they held on to our knees, even singing and dancing. 

How could the three brothers, sitting together, possibly condone this?! Especially for me, as a student, the feelings of guilt and embarrassment were overwhelming. <138>

**Summary**

The article recounts a personal and humorous memory from the author’s youth when he and his brothers, unfamiliar with the nightlife in Seoul, mistakenly entered a bar, leading to an awkward situation filled with embarrassment for the author, especially given his status as a student. This vignette highlights the cultural contrasts between urban and rural life during the colonial era, as well as the challenges of navigating social norms and behaviors within the burgeoning modernity of Korea in the 1920s.
