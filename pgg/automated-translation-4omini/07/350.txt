When all the friends have parted, and the annoying small duties are taken care of, the school feels as quiet as a deserted house, with only the duty officers gathered to chat idly.  

甲: "So tell me, which teachers do you prefer, male or female?"  

乙: "Well, there's no doubt female teachers are more friendly and caring!"  

甲: "What about you?"  

乙: "Whoever it is, it really doesn’t matter."  

丁: "Exactly! That's not something to even ask."  

甲: "Then among the female teachers, do you prefer those who are married or those who are not?"  

乙: "Hmm... I haven't really thought about that."  

甲: "You?"  

丙: "I prefer the unmarried ones!"  

甲: "You jest!"  

丙: "Married teachers, no matter what, are preoccupied with their household issues. Unmarried ones, they can be on our side regardless of whether they are single or not, and they tend to give their affection exclusively to the students."  

乙: "But the married ones really do understand our situations well and truly care for us. Having been married, they can explain what family life is like and what men are like, helping us for our futures."  

丙: "That's true, but the married teachers also have that situation; when they are involved with students, they may care more, but if they have conflicts with their husbands, it might affect them in class, causing them to frown and feel lethargic, and at times they might even brag about their husband's career or be focused on knitting their child's socks or gloves in the office."  

丁: "That's indeed the case."  

甲: "What causes so many unmarried teachers to have hysteria?"  

丙: "That's a bit... hmmm..." *Laughter follows.*  

At that point, the conversation quiets down as they tuck away their books and quietly begin to head out through the pathway.

  

**Summary**  

This article captures a casual discussion among students about their preferences for male versus female teachers, and particularly about whether they prefer married or unmarried female teachers. It reveals insights into the students' perceptions of their teachers' personal lives and how those might impact their teaching styles and relationships with students. This conversation reflects the complex dynamics of authority and affection in education during the Japanese colonial period in Korea, highlighting the influence of gender roles and societal expectations on both teachers and students.
