잡지명	별건곤  
발행일	1927년 07월 01일  
기사제목	雜同散異 臨時大淸潔 (Miscellaneous Dispersions: Temporary Great Cleanliness)  
저자	미상  
기사형태	세태비평 (Societal Critique)  

Spring is for spring cleanliness, summer is for summer cleanliness; cleanliness is as necessary and beneficial as anything else, and there is no time as refreshing as after being clean. Therefore, to wash my face and feet every day, to clean my ears and blow my nose, to shake off dust in spring and to clean grime in autumn—all these are part of bodily cleanliness. Maintaining cleanliness in the home means cleaning up dust in three seasons and sweeping the mountains and valleys with a broom refers to clothing cleanliness. Eventually, one could argue that a person is simply born to pursue cleanliness and die clean.

However, since I am but a fellow human, I cannot neglect cleanliness. Lacking a family does not bother me, and my body is still healthy, so I can manage to tend to my own cleanliness after eating. I wonder why on earth I must trouble myself to participate in the communal duty of societal cleanliness in such a way. In doing so, I could risk making mistakes that might provoke someone’s reprimand or lead to the breaking of a broomstick. After making mistakes, I may also face the disdain of those observing me. Yet, I cannot escape responsibility. How shall I proceed? Should I use a broom? A broomstick? Water? Medicine? It's daunting to even consider doing it, and even if I do have such resources, there's the lamentation of never being able to cover every nook and cranny of society alongside the frustration of it all. It is not as simple as simply starting with a broom; I must not use a soft or plush brush, but instead utilize a stiff metal pen or a fountain pen.

But if I use such sharp implements, I may encounter trouble from those who are sensitive. What if the Cleanliness Army gets around to cleaning up everything? I shall simply close my eyes and eliminate anything and anyone that seems out of sorts.

What shall I start with? Should I address the outside first, or the inside? It would be wise to tackle the outside first. Where do I go to do this? Should I sweep horizontally or vertically? It seems prudent to start with horizontal sweeps. Well then, let’s get moving. I shall load some ink into my fountain pen. 

Ah! I've caught something. There’s someone who discarded rice and soup, along with fish heads, on Samno Street. It is very unpleasant to behold. This is evidently the foolhardy action of ignorant men and women, entrapped by the tricks of shamans in the shrine, exhibiting superstitious and absurd behavior. Given the degree of malfeasance here, I might be lenient. However, out of respect for society's dignity, I cannot forgive this. I shall eliminate the evidence and leave. If such behavior persists, I might well impose severe penalties or enact divine retribution on them.

Indeed, every grain is earned with difficulty! Each grain of sweat is worth thousands of drops, and a drop of blood is worth a thousand. To discard this treasure out in the open street, where all can see, can invite cosmic punishment. Instead of offering sacrifices to spirits, perhaps I could extend a hand to the poor or a beggar, but I insist that such negligence must not continue.

Oh! I’ve found another disgrace. This scene of men and women, at all hours, relieving themselves openly onto the street is intolerable! Is there truly no toilet in their homes, or even a simple water closet? How can they be so shameless? Such filthy behavior may be forgiven in lower-class areas, yet when it damages public health and societal standards, I cannot excuse it. I shall rid the streets of this filth and uphold some decorum.

What is this foul mess? It looks like the droppings of children — how is it that parents are absent when their little ones make such messes? These larger droppings suggest they belong to a raucous drunkard. Let us discard it, and should I see anyone repeating such vulgar acts, they will pay a fine of fifty jeon or else will feel my slap.

As I endeavor to cleanse the streets of excrement and urine, it feels noble. The Cleanliness Army shall take no prisoners; I must proceed. Together, let’s take a look at the communal toilets. How filthy they are! The habits of the males are vile, but the governance of sanitation is beyond appalling.

Insa-dong? Human traffic makes it even dirtier. The communal latrines are so disorganized that one cannot tell where the toilet is. What is happening near the Geunjeong Pavilion in the heart of the city? This state of affairs is so unsightly I cannot describe it—this is practically a pit of excrement! If the commons are in such disarray, what can one expect from other districts? I would urge the sanitation authorities to put up a notice—if such happenings continue, they shall not go unpunished.

Ah, in the glaring light of day, vendors are urinating into buckets or containers before the eyes of customers. Such lowly behavior cannot be tolerated.

What’s this? A clinic specializing in syphilis and gonorrhea? No matter how desperate the advertisements are, displaying this grotesquery where everyone can see is not appropriate. They may intend to alert everyone to stay cautious, but it’s gross, and one who sees this may find it hard to eat for days. I once heard an old man say he lost his appetite for three days after seeing it. Is this really an advertisement? 

What madness is this? A collection of disgusting taxidermy resembling infernal spirits! God forbid! Such unseemliness makes one shudder. Why not keep such items hidden until the secret reveal in a sanitary cleanup? 

What’s this? A procession of brides and grooms, so lavishly adorned in what can only be described as something pitiable, yet they parade on the streets. What a mockery! How on Earth could anyone dare sell food amidst such filth? I’d rather go hungry than choke it down, and even in the case of delectable morsels, it’s impossible to take that risk. Who could possibly endure such enduring malfunctions in preparation and memory? 

The shops at the entrance to the Umyin-gwan always seem to have Japanese and Korean noodles, but they sell them alongside cast-offs for the needy, especially toward country folk. Ice cream is about to come out. But for someone in need, this may seem more than convenient, and still the vendor stands amidst the chaos, slurping down noodles himself, which looks dreadful. 

The stalls in Dongdaemun are on a different level of filth, and it's hard to believe that people cluster there under such horrid conditions. The execution of social hygiene and human dignity must be observed. Though poverty sometimes leads one to overlook etiquette, charity should not deny human dignity.

That old lady has a message for those selling pancake skewers: Conceal those soiled containers. This food does not get washed before consumption; care must be taken. And as for the operators of taverns and rice soup eateries, be mindful not to soil chopsticks or spoons during service—this is but a single plea. 

There is not a single public washhouse in this city. I am unsure if anyone here can bear to think honestly about such matters. When one watches the scene at the riverside—whether it be the Saemangeum or Hwanggukjung streams—a suggestion of the filth washed down becomes apparent. With 300,000 worth of foul waste from urban areas, the putrid water in which one washes their clothes is absolutely intolerable (cleaning may occur in clearer water later). 

The authorities have the responsibility to provide this service; if they are unwilling, why can’t the public initiate the establishment of at least one washing facility? Now it seems best to consider a naked alliance, for wearing those tainted clothes seems too hazardous, yet one must not live without clothing! Thus, the establishment of public laundries becomes unavoidable. 

The areas from Dongdaemun to Hwanggukjung, through the Training Center and Gwanghwon, are the dirtiest. Cleanliness cannot be maintained here. 

This concerns all regions: the main streets of Hongguk, the market areas, and even the back alleys of Daegu. Daeheung’s dining options are by no means clean. All eateries, particularly taverns, exhibit this filth. They cannot be trusted with one’s health, and it’s disheartening to think how easy it is to fall ill from dining at such places. 

The unfriendliness of bank employees toward customers is unacceptable. Likewise, the lack of courtesy displayed by Korean clerks at post offices is particularly discouraging. The post office in Oehwa-dong is one of the worst offenders.

It is necessary to enforce sanitation and foster cleanliness throughout all sectors, even for the lowliest of tradesmen. The jesting behavior of street vendors and auctioneers dehumanizes customers and is wholly unacceptable.

The government must eradicate those defiling public hygiene, and those engaging in selfish self-promotion must also be dismissed from their positions. Those pretending to work for the many while only filling their own pockets should be dismissed. 

Those steamrollers of misrepresentation and braggarts must also be dealt with, alongside deceptive sellers, foolish gamblers, and anyone else who brings disrepute to our society. The grip we hold on cleanliness must be tightened lest such offenses continue unchallenged. 

Cleanliness? This cannot be accomplished all at once. No matter how well it is done, it must be maintained continually. If one cleaning spot is addressed, another foul spot emerges, and the task seems never-ending. For today, let us set aside this for the summer or autumn, let us clink our cups to celebrate our labor.  

**Summary**

The article critiques the lack of cleanliness in urban society, addressing the profound neglect of hygiene standards in public spaces. The author catalogs the various unsanitary behaviors witnessed, calling for communal accountability and action to improve societal standards. The vivid descriptions of conditions in public latrines and streets reflect the struggles facing Koreans under colonial rule, implying a connection between cleanliness, dignity, and national identity. Historians may find this piece particularly revealing as it underscores the complex interplay of personal and communal responsibility against a backdrop of colonial neglect.

봄은 봄 청결 녀름은 녀름 청결 청결처럼 필요하고 유익한 일은 업고 청결후 처럼 爽快한 때는 업다. 그래서 날마다 세수하고 발 씻고 귀 홉이고 코딱지 홉이고 몸 때 떨고 하는 것이 몸청결이요 봄에 三冬 먼지 떨고 가을에 三伏븍덕이 치우는 것이 居處淸潔이요 山谷마다 방망이질 하는 것이 의복청결이니 사람이란 것은 결국 청결하러 나서 청결하다 죽는 것이라고도 볼수 잇게 된다.

그런데 나도 인간 놈의 하나이니 청결을 안이할 수 업다. 가정은 업스니 그것은 문제가 안이오 몸은 아직 별 병은 업스니 그런대로 王눈이게 말손을 식키되소곰 왜 비누에 맛겨두고 사회에 대한 공동의무로 사회청결이나 서틀지만 좀 해보기로 한다. 하다가 잘못한다고 감독의 비ㅅ자루 끗테 꽁문이가 부러질 넌지도 모로겟고 한뒤에 잘못 햇다고 뭇는 놈의 입질 손길질이 잇슬넌지도 모르겟다. 그러나 책임상 안이 할 수는 업다. 무엇으로 할고? 걸네로 할가? 비ㅅ자루로 할가? 물로 할가? 약으로 할가? 그것이 업기도＜48＞ 하려니와 잇다 해도 사회 각방면 구석구석을 다 하기에는 不及의 嘆도 잇겟고 귀찬은 감도 잇다. 손쉽게 붓끗흐로 해볼밧게 업다. 붓끗으로 하되 늘큰늘큰한 黃毛大筆이나 보들보들한 羊毛中筆로 해서는 안되겟다. 적어도 꼭 찌르면 피가 쫄 나리 만치 꼿꼿한 鐵筆이나 만연필을 쓸밧게 업다. 그럿타-그것이 第一策이다. 그러나 함부로 꼭 찔너 놈들이 압흐다고 울며불며 告發大發을 하면 엇지할가 그러나 淸潔軍이 어대 이것 저것을 다-갈릴수야 잇스랴 눈 꽉 감고 웬만한 놈은 다 쓰러 버리기로 한다.

무엇부터 먼첨 할고? 外面을 먼첨 할가 內面을 먼첨 할가 順次上 외면을 먼처함이 조켓다. 어대로 먼첨 할고? 橫으로 갈가 縱으로 갈가. 亦 횡면부터 먼첨함이 조켓다. 그럼 떠나자. 만연필에 잉크는 잇는대로 쏘아 너어라. 자-떠난다.

익크! 하나 걸엿다. 三路街上에다가 쌀밥 메욱국 북어 대가리를 내버린 년놈이 잇다. 보기에 퍽도 불쾌하다. 이것은 분명히 崇神窟을 차저 단니며 무당 판수놈들의 꼬임에 빠저 귀신독갑이에게 홀닌 愚夫愚婦의 迷信行爲니 그놈의 정도를 보아 혹-용서도 할 수 잇지만 사회의 체면을 보아서 도저히 용서할 수가 업다. 쓰러 버리고 가노니 다시 그런 즛을 말렷다. 다시 그런 못생긴 즛을 하면＜49＞ 이 掃除軍의 혹독한 벌은 고사하고 한눌이 暗暗中에 벌을 내려 그야말로 너의 말과 가치 귀신을 내목이 떠러질 것이다.
글세 粒皆辛苦 ! 한알의 땀이 萬 방울이요 피가 천 방울이나 되는 그러한 人間血汗의 결정품인 그 至寶를 먹기도 오히려 두려운데 萬人所視의 三路街上에 내다 버리니 엇지 천벌이 업겟느냐 그 귀신독갑이를 주는 대신에 인간의 하나인 빈민이나 거지를 주렴으나 다시는 그런 즛 말렷다.
익크! 또 하나 걸엿다. 이런 오줌벼락을 마즐 년놈들 밤새도록 (혹은 白晝에도) 년놈이 (혹은 애색기까지)요강을 타고 안저 실실 내 갈닌 그 오줌을 萬人所視에 들총을 열고 행길을 향하야 쭈루루 내버리는구나 그 년놈의 집에는 변소도 업고 하다못해 水口門도 업단말이냐 엇지면 그러케 철면피들이냐 이 더럽고 몰체면한 즛을 감행하는 것은 대개 행랑사리하는 하급에 만흐닛가 혹-용서할 수도 잇지만 사회의 체면이나 공동위생을 위하야는 도저히 용서할 수가 업다. 쓰러버리고 가노니 다시는 그런 즛 말렷다.
익크! 이것은 또 웬 똥이냐 엇던 어미 아비 업는 자식들이 해 노은 즛이냐 똥뗑이가 큰 것을 보니 어린 놈의 즛은 안이다. 심술구진 층각 놈 안이면 술주정군의 즛이 분명하다. 더러우니 쓰러버리고 가자 다시 그런 즛을 하는 놈을 보면 벌금 50전은 말도 말고 당장에 귀가 달아나가게 따귀를 붓칠테다.
청결벽두에 똥 오줌을 먼춤하게 되니 신수 좀 궁하다. 청결군이 이것 저것을 가리랴 임이 그길로 나섯스니 공동변소 구경이나 잠간 하고 가자. 齊洞 네거리의 변소? 에구 더러워! 놈들의 밋구멍 버릇도 괴악하지만 府廳의 위생표방도 거즛말이다. 인사동은 엇던고? 인간의 왕래가 복잡하니만치 더 더럽다. 똥간이니 오줌간이니 분별할수 업스리만치 亂放을 해 노앗다. 그러면 대경성의 한복판 종로 비각 뒤의 변소는 어떠한가? 이런 개가튼 ‥‥ 더럽기는 둘재요 (변소가 더럽지 안으랴마은) 창피하야 말 못하겟다 이는 그저 분요부식의 陰窟밧게 안된다. 종로 네거리의 공동변소가 이러하니 다른데야 더 말할 것 무엇이랴 년놈들의 똥구멍 오줌구멍에 注意碑를 붓치거니와 소위 부청당국 또는 위생부제군에게 一矢를 放한다. 차후 다시 그러면 更不容特엿다.
아-이런 백주대낮에 店頭에 안저 장사하는 놈들도 요강 또는 양철통에 오줌을 싸서 店外 막 행인의 안전에서 쭈루루 쏘다 버리는 구나. 참 할 수 없는 위인들이다.
