잡지명	별건곤 제7호  
발행일	1927년 07월 01일  
기사제목	드면錄!! 그때에 이리햇드면 只今 朝鮮은 엇지 되엿슬가, 丙子胡亂때에 林慶業將軍을 信任하얏드면 (Record of Events!! If We Had Done This, What Would Korea Be Like Now? If General Lim Gyeongyeop Had Been Trusted During the Byeongja War)  
필자	車相瓚  
기사형태	논설 (Editorial)  

A solitary castle from ancient times, how pitiful it is that our East Land has no longevity.

At this moment, tears are unavoidable as one reflects on the past; we have not severed the old enemy's head.

These awkward lines are a poem I composed during my middle school days after visiting Namhansanseong in Guangzhou. Namhansanseong is a place that has brought eternal shame upon us Koreans. 

If there are tears, they could have been shed once, and if there is blood, it could have been spilled once. If there were iron clubs or cannons, we could have charged without leaving a trace. The so-called nation of Eastern propriety boasts of grandeur, yet in a single day, it succumbed to the invasion of northern barbarians, allowing enemy cavalry to break through the ice on the Yalu River just six days later. Our borders, which stretched a thousand li, were trampled as if they were unprotected lands, and all ranks from king to minister were thrown into confusion, abandoning the capital Hansung as if it were nothing more than a shoe. After forty days of siege, when we were beset by hunger and unable to sack a single bullet or swing a single sword, the only vigorous debates came from the likes of Yun Ji-up, Oh Dal-ji, and Hong Ik-han (the three scholars), who passionately advocated for peace. However, in the face of overwhelming circumstances, we ultimately had to endure the humiliation of signing a treaty beneath the city walls. The prince and high ministers were captured by the enemy, compelling King Injo, who had held a position of vast honor, to shed tears, captured in the lines of a poem, “Raindrops cascade at the three rivers, the sovereign's tears join the father's pain”—and so on.

At that time, Korea was indeed weak. Since the numerous wars that followed the Imjin War, our national wealth and civilian strength had been entirely washed away and had yet to recover. Nonetheless, both the royal court and the ministers continued to adhere to complacency, living day by day and singing songs about their superficial peace, while during the Imjin War, political factions would intermittently resurface. After the war concluded, the factions regained their ascendancy. Ultimately, Gwanghaegun was ousted amid this political turmoil, and with Injo's ascension, the so-called Restoration Loyalists, namely the Western faction, consolidated power while excluding anyone outside their clique. The method of employing men favored the aristocracy, propelling the harmful trend of respecting letters and scorn for arms, creating a situation where even if there were myriad talented generals and heroes across the realm, they could not wield any real power in politics or military, often reduced to disappointing circumstances or failures in the fields, within the army, or in exile.

Consider Zheng Chongxin, previously acclaimed as the top general during the Imjin War, who was demoted from vice-general during the Jeongka Invasion due to proposing some dissent regarding the court's relationship with the Qing. He was immediately lowered to the rank of a common official and excluded, while the one called Zhuge Liang at the time, Xu Sheng, fearing that his critiques of court decisions might bring calamity upon him, hid away from the public eye, not daring to show his face. The venerable general Kim Jong-seon (also known as Ahn Yeon-gong) was captured during the Imjin War without being permitted a military position, and while Lim Gyeongyeop, though he was in charge of border defenses as the governor of Uiju, was also restricted by the enmity of schemers and corrupt scholars, he maintained troops that were hardly more than 800 elderly soldiers. When he reported the approach of enemy troops, the commander, Kim Jidal, hid the information, and it was only when the enemy arrived at the capital that the news finally emerged.

How can such events occur without bringing national shame and the humiliation of our people? Moreover, our adversary, Aisin Gioro, held a newly rising power, commanding an army of a hundred thousand, and there was no one among the decaying and corrupt Korean military who could withstand their advance.

However, if we look back on our history, we see that Yang Manchun of Goguryeo valiantly defended the solitary castle of Ansi against an army of 300,000 from Tang, and even injured Emperor Taizong to the point of lamenting, “Who knew the black flower blooms as a white feather?” Eulji Mundeok slaughtered a million troops of Sui Yang Guang at the Sasu River, not allowing even a small imperial armor to escape. Not to mention, during the Goryeo period, Gang Gam-chan and Yi Sun-sin during the reign of Seonjo soundly defeated the formidable Khitan and Japanese pirates. If there had been figures of equal caliber to those mentioned during the Byeongja year, even if Korea was weak at that time, we would not have suffered such humiliation. 

Of course, it is not that there were no capable people present then. If there was someone like General Lim Gyeongyeop, who had always planned for robust military action, had received trust from King Injo, it's likely that Korea's humiliation during the Byeongja War would never have occurred. If our extraordinary troops had been stationed along the Cheongseong Ridge and the Huiri Ridge, then strategizing when to launch an assault, this would truly be a strategy of overcoming the odds; hence, we would have had no cause for concern.

At that time, Lim Gyeongyeop volunteered to exterminate the enemy on Ganghwa Island while presenting a request to the observation officer, expressing that if the court would grant him 40,000 elite troops, he could annihilate the invaders and wash away the dust by the Yalu, returning victorious. Also, just before the outbreak of the Byungja War, he foresaw the enemy's arrival and discreetly informed the court about the necessity of dispatching 20,000 troops to Hwanghae. He received the emperor's permission and boasted to the garrison commander, Yu Rim, saying, “As I now possess an army of 20,000, why should I fear the enemy's approach? Even should they enter our borders, let us not undertake light strikes, but direct our main forces at the enemy's homeland, and they will surely retreat!”

Even this plan was thwarted due to the obstruction of the courtiers.

Moreover, when the enemy invaded the country, Lim Gyeongyeop sent a letter to Master Yu Rim outlining, “These foes, known as the Manchus, can march across the land even in the millions. How much more now that they number in the tens of thousands? They can indeed fight with wisdom but struggle with strength. Now the enemy has gathered their elites and sent only the old and weak to Shenyang, thus I urge you to send 5,000 troops to boldly advance towards the enemy's land, and within a week, we shall reach Shenyang. There, the enemy will undoubtedly send word to the Han, which will reach the Han within seven days; I propose we set ambushes at critical points, thus preventing the enemy’s movements. When they realize their stronghold is at risk, they will withdraw. We can march triumphantly and return home without any loss of life.” This suggestion was also disregarded, purely because Lim Gyeongyeop was envious of Yu Rim.

It can be easily understood that General Lim Gyeongyeop possessed both the strategy and bravery necessary to repel the Manchus. Yet, when one considers the phrase “Even when a person wishes to die, elixirs are of no use,” the royal court at that time was already heading towards destruction and did not trust men of his caliber. Instead, they appointed the mediocre criminal Kim Jidal as the Grand Marshal, ultimately leading to the country's ruin and the people's humiliation, with General Lim ultimately assassinated. This is indeed a regrettable affair of a thousand years.

Now, after 292 years, to bring up this argument seems similar to looking at bones buried for hundreds of years and wondering what medicine could have prevented their demise. However, is it not possible to learn something from the stones of another mountain?

  

**Summary**

This text reflects on the tragic failures of the Joseon dynasty during the Byeongja War, particularly highlighting the potential that could have been realized under generals like Lim Gyeongyeop. The author laments the historical inadequacies of the ruling class, which led to national humiliation and defeat. The commentary offers a poignant reminder of the value of capable leadership and the deep consequences of political mismanagement, providing a critical look at Korea's colonial history that scholars might find revealing regarding patterns of neglect and failure in leadership prior to colonization.

「此賊은 소위 女眞이라. 女眞은 萬만 되여도 천하를 횡행하거든 況且 이제 數十萬이나 됨이리요. 그는 가히 智로 鬪할지인졍 力으로 勝키 難할지라. 이제 汗이 精銳를 다 다리고 오고 다만 老弱만 으로써 瀋陽을 직히고 잇슨즉 청컨대 5천의 兵을 어더서 直히 虜地로 향하면 七日之內에 가히 瀋陽에 이를 것이요, 瀋陽에서는 반드시 汗에게 馳報할터인즉 그 亦 七日之內에 汗에게 이를지라. 원컨대 兵相은 要害處에다 복병을 만히 설치하야 虜人이 이곳으로부터 저의 곳으로 가는 것을 묵인하고 저의 곳으로부터 이곳으로 오는 것은 일일히 捕捉하야 서로 통용치 못하게하면 虜가 그 巢穴이 覆沒의 患이 잇슬가 염려하야 수일이 못되여 撤兵還國할터인즉 우리는 武威를 떨치여서 그 本窟을 소란케 한 뒤에 建州衛祖大壽의 곳으로 향하얏다가 登州를 거처서 椵島를 지내 도라오면 군병이 칼날에 피칠도 안이하고 대사를 가히 이룰 것이라. 이것은 孫臏의 魏都로 직주하든 계책이니 우리의 孤軍이 비록 龐涓의 머리는 엇지 못할지라도 한국의 圍를 可히 풀것이다.」(이것도 柳琳이 林將軍을 婧忌하는 까닭에 듯지 안어서 실패하얏다.)
이 우에 말함과 갓치 당시 林慶業장군은 능히 胡賊을 격퇴할 지략과 雄圖와 武藝가 다 구비하얏다. 그러나 眞所謂 사람이 죽으랴면 불사약도 소용이 업다고 당시 君臣은 벌서 패망을 하랴고 그와가튼 인물을 신임치 안이하고 金自黙가튼 凡庸奸凶의 輩로 都元帥를 삼어 결국 國破君辱하는 비운을 당하고 임장군까지 살해하얏다. 이것이 엇지 千載痛恨한 일이 안이랴.
벌서 292년이나 지낸 오늘에 잇서 이 의론을 하는 것은 마치 수백년된 塚中枯骨을 보고 무슨 약을 먹엇스면 죽지 안엇스리라는 것과 가튼 감이 잇다. 그러나 이것이 엇지 他山의 石이 되지 안으리라고 말할수 잇스랴. 噫噫 ＜11＞
