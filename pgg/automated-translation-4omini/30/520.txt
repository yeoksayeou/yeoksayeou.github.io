기사제목	初秋와 隨筆 (Early Autumn and Essays)  
필자	尹白南 (Yun Baengnam)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)  

The autumn water is clear— 

When I had not yet awakened to the world’s affairs… Spring meant flowers blooming, summer brought catching minnows in the stream, and boasting of one’s voice from a willow branch was the joy of catching a minnow that passed by, but these memories did not return when I was still a child. However, the feeling that autumn water is clear was an unfamiliar one. The reason this feeling came about is a funny story, but my fortune-telling told me of it. In the summer of the year I turned nine, an old man came to our house to read fortunes for my family. At that time, I was curious about what fortune-telling was, standing in the corner of the room, when the old man glanced at me and said, “That child will become a great person. Yes. In the eighth month… in October… when October comes, you’ll see.” He began to mumble, drawing with a brush. As I heard the words “My fortune,” my curiosity spiked, and I approached to take a closer look. 

At the beginning, he wrote, “秋水長淸, 鱗魚自肥.” I could not interpret the meaning of “鱗魚自肥,” but I could understand the meaning of “秋水長淸.” “秋水長淸, autumn water is so clear,” I wondered deeply, could autumn water be that clear? 

Thus, long after, it wasn’t curiosity about fortune-telling that remained in my mind, but only that autumn water is clear held sway over my head.

Another time I perceived this was several years ago while wandering around 철원 (Cheorwon) and near 금화 (Geumhwa) on a high plateau. The fresh breeze caressing the backs of the bending yellow rice stalks made me recognize the trail of the wind as if it was gold, and a few larks took off with the speed of stones, darting here and there, creating a cool and mournful autumn scene. Oh, the autumn of the highlands, I muttered a few times to myself. Suddenly, the words “秋水長淸” came to mind.

I imagined the water flowing from the highlands heading in a direction. Indeed, the stream flowed. It continued further, through home channels connecting rice mills, and the shimmering water flowed over into the grains like pearls. I gazed at this flow for a while. Oh, how could the water be this clear? It was so clear that I could not directly see its existence. The thin water plants beneath the water swayed with their shapes intact, dancing as they did. They must have had sounds when they danced, but the water kept them silent in my ears. It was a faraway, splendid world. 

On the fine sands at the bottom, golden flakes of reflection sparkled in the sunlight. The water was so sharp that it seemed you could witness the frolicking minnows playing among the thin blades of grass.

Autumn water is clear! 

I felt that truth again, very keenly.

In a place called “雲水” (Unsu), while I was looking at the nearby 安東 (Andong) across the border, I entered a teahouse and, while conversing with the owner through writing, I noticed a tall person passing by outside. I gestured to imply that I was also tall and shared the meaning of being playful, when the owner took a brush and wrote, “山高水長, 水長則淸.” I read the text and, in solitude, the thought of the phrase “秋水長淸” came to my mind, bringing a bittersweet smile to my face. The owner did not comprehend the reason behind my bitter smile, but seeing it, he also smiled softly. 

Oh, the land of characters, the land of my childish friends. I regretted not having seen the autumn of China.

As the cicadas chirp, I recognize that autumn has arrived, and as this refreshing evening breeze blows, I sense summer must be coming to an end. Oh, welcome autumn, but why do you feel so sorrowful? Autumn reminds me of lost lovers’ graves; autumn always brings me nothing of sorrowful feelings.

If spring symbolizes joy and love, autumn seems to symbolize a gentle sadness.

From the glistening ground, the workers’ songs around the fields, to the clear and cool moon overhead shining brightly, if an occasion were to happen where the sound of the oaks would quietly settle without songs, what would remain there but sorrow?

In autumn nights long, I wonder if I might enter your room and wake from sleep to find you there.

Indeed, autumn is sorrowful—

If spring is a season for romance, then autumn can be said to be a time for reminiscing about past loves, immersed in nostalgic intoxication that brings tears to one’s eyes. 

I often heard the sounds of a bamboo flute or a shawm during spring’s greenery waking along the mountains and fields teeming with flowers. But those merely gave me a languid vigor and some fleeting excitement.

However, the chilly autumn breeze creeping into my arms at night… When the bright moonlight floods my yard, I often hear from afar the thin, lingering sounds of a flute. What a lucky time to be so matched with music! That thin, clear, and gentle melody stirs my peripheral nerves, causing tears to flow for reasons unknown. Some may laugh and call me sentimental, but can anyone deny that autumn is truly sorrowful? This ought to be my own conclusion.

When I was teaching in a southern village for several years, every spring was a sign that spring had returned; summer brought thoughts of a vacation soon to come. Thus, I lived an ordinary life.

While leading such a mundane life, there were times in the fall when I thought about my hometown and the deceased, leading me to sometimes shed tears in solitude.

In the “秋夕” festival in the village, the celebration might be greater than in the New Year. Crops are flourishing in the fields, and there’s little to do but wait for harvest time. How can there not be excitement during this?

It is this time that people raise the flag of jubilation, and the excitement spills over as they gather around. I, too, have participated in those festivities. 

While people play through the night with drums and gongs, I long for the bright moonlight, extinguishing the lamp and burying myself in the glow of the moon creeping into my room, solely indulging in thoughts of old loves. 

It happens involuntarily. Gradually, for me, the more autumn there is, the more it feels lacking in tenderness. 

If spring belongs to youth, autumn might be considered belonging to the aged. Autumn is philosophy. It’s bitter.

To those looking at me, I have come out into the season of autumn. Even with the arrival of spring, I find little joy, although aware of the beauty of flowers; I cannot summon the courage to dance and sing among them. 

Ah, as the life of youth evaporates from my veins, nostalgia and regret flow through my being like lukewarm, forgotten blood. 

Indeed, I am in the autumn of my life. Autumn, autumn, it is in autumn that I recall my lost youth with feelings unfathomable to bring me even the slightest scent of the remnants of lively blood. 

Come! Autumn, so that I may once again see and breathe the escaping scent of my lost youth, as I gaze at the red clusters of the blooming hibiscus flower.  

  

**Summary**

The essay reflects on the writer's deeply introspective view of autumn, intertwined with memories of lost youth and the beauty of nature. It presents a contrast between the joys of spring and the melancholic feel of autumn, correlating personal experiences with the changing seasons. Historians might find the emotional resonance linked to colonial Korean identity in this piece, revealing underlying sentiments of nostalgia and loss amidst the backdrop of a society evolving under colonial rule.
