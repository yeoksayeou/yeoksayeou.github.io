New Global Instability: Is the Second World War Approaching?  
Addressing the problem... Editor  
Trotsky expressed the prior world wars succinctly as "the expansion of the productive forces of capitalist powers colliding with the barriers of their respective borders." This expression is unarguably derived from an accurate observation of facts.  

Indeed, centered around the newly formed German Empire, achieved through the iron and blood policies of Bismarck and Wilhelm III, the recent activities of Austria, Hungary, and the decrepit Ottoman Empire, along with powers in the Balkans, Mesopotamia, Asia Minor, and the Far East, have notably placed England and France, supported by Japan, Italy, and ultimately the United States, in a situation where they cannot effectively counter these movements. This reflects Germany's and Austria-Hungary's expansionary policies, driven by their intense productive forces, seeking to dominate raw materials and commodity markets on a global scale, particularly against the backdrop of England maintaining its existing privileges or exploring new territories. This leads to the capitalist powers of the world reaching their climax of development through warfare.  

Prior to the war, capitalism among global powers was in a constant upward trajectory. As they reached a point where the continuous ascent became unsustainable, various collisions arose, resulting in inevitable consequences. What then are these consequences?  

First, they partitioned the territories of the defeated nations. Thus, the victorious powers temporarily regained vigor in their domestic markets, meeting demands through newly acquired territories or by trading over the lenient borders of the defeated nations. However, the availability of new territories or the requests of domestic markets could not be sustained indefinitely.  

At this point, they no longer had measures to absorb their productive capacities in either domestic or overseas markets. If the production capacity remains unabsorbed, it indicates that capitalism has transitioned from a phase of growth into a decline.  

Secondly, a significant outcome is that economic focus shifted from Europe—especially from Britain—to the United States leading up to and during the war. The U.S., which profited tremendously during the Great War, has positioned itself as the dominant force in global finance, dictating international economic conditions.  

Thirdly, the rise of liberation movements among oppressed ethnic groups has ignited social instability, resulting in both conscious and unconscious boycotts against the goods from the imperialist nations due to the growth of industrial capitalism within those nations themselves.  

Fourthly, the establishment of the Soviet Union marks a new chapter in human history.  

Thus, capitalism, now in decline, struggles with severe and chronic economic stagnation, resulting in various means to adapt: domestic rationalization of industries and the erecting of high tariff barriers across borders. Although these may serve as their immediate solutions, they spur social unrest, leading to a worldwide emergence of an unemployed class that may number in the tens of millions, causing intensified labor disputes in various regions.  

With the enduring economic conditions in the United States, the winds of depression are ballooning, spilling over into the global arena. Britain, while struggling to comprehend its own domestic crisis, finds itself caught in a bind, unable to escape the looming threat of unrest in Indochina and calls for independence in Egypt.  

Countless other specific facts could be mentioned, all of which would aptly describe the rapidly descending state of global capitalism. In this context, Stalin pointed out during the 16th Congress of the Russian Federation Communist Party that the powers were facing internal economic decline and sharpening clashes with colonies, prophesying that the Second World War was forthcoming.  

Of course, capitalist powers will strive to avoid war as long as they maintain their strength. Hence, they advocate for disarmament. Those who speak of world peace should recognize that disarmament is not necessarily a precursor to peace. Even should disarmament occur, with the first gunshot, artillery or warships can be produced in abundance, as evidenced by the recent World War.  

The powers are entangled in the aforementioned economic crises and social unrest, resembling a volatile explosive ready to ignite. Meanwhile, a new economic force has emerged internationally—the European Economic Union. The formation of this union, responding to British concerns, appears to be coalescing around Germany and France.  

Given that this union bears the name "Europe," it signifies opposition to the economic domination exercised by the United States (though Britain retains some influence). Global observers, particularly labor and socialist Russia, regard this union as an attempt to confront European capitalism.  

Recently, there have even been whispers of a tripartite alliance among Russia, Turkey, and Italy. If that materializes, can the world find itself divided into three major powers? Would this not present a considerable threat to capitalist powers?  

As stated earlier, these powers will indeed seek to avoid conflict at all costs. But will they succeed in this endeavor, or will they ultimately find themselves unable to escape? These are questions worthy of reflection and should not be taken lightly.  

Attempting to Avoid  
Song Jin-u  

The issue is far too extensive to condense neatly. Should hostilities arise, could it result in class warfare? However, class warfare hasn't reached the point necessary for an international political form to emerge.  

While the current situation bears some resemblance to pre-war conditions of the previous great war, there are points of divergence. Unlike prior to the last war, when belligerents were eager to go to war, the experiences and lessons learned from that war influence contemporary sentiments. 

Winning a war ultimately yields no new territories beyond what is sustainable for the victor's economy, and one could foresee changes similar to those seen in Russia thereafter. As the European Economic Union matures and positions itself against the Anglo-American blocks, conflict may indeed arise. Yet, that alone cannot clearly suggest the onset of another world war.  

Efforts to maintain peace, through the establishment of international organizations or disarmament conferences, signify attempts to deter war and sustain peace. One area of caution is China. Currently, while Chiang Kai-shek is clashing with Yan Xishan, the emergence of Communist forces gaining traction does not lead to an immediate redirection toward full communization of the entire nation. The reaction of domestic counter-revolutionary forces and the scrutiny of global powers must not be overlooked.  

At present, although China cannot claim absolute sovereignty, it remains perpetually within the sphere of economic influence of great powers. Thus, instability among powerful nations regarding China's status could conceivably serve as a catalyst for conflict. The great powers' maneuvers regarding China might very well trigger a fuse for the Second World War; however, they too will desire to avoid such scenarios.  

Thus, assessing only the present state of affairs does not allow one to categorically assert the imminent arrival of a second world war.  

Difficult to Assert  
Kim Gi-dan  

The proposition of a Second World War fails to resonate with the intensity and urgency one might expect. It may stem from a lack of focused attention on that topic.  

Europe's Economic Union is maturing, aggravating the British and American concerns while at the same time, Russia perceives this union as oppositional to their interests. Recently, reports have indicated potential alliances among Russia, Turkey, and Italy; should this become so, it could present significant issues, given that the outcomes of the previous global conflict had not reached a definitive conclusion, paving the way for a renewed round of conflict. However, I do not sense an immediate urgency in this direction.  

Many anticipate the Second World War emerging as a class struggle; however, it appears to me that this is unlikely. Should a comprehensive class war emerge internationally, Russia alone would be ill-equipped to manage it.  

Even if nations such as China or Britain attempted to form a Soviet state, they would not readily engage in a unified alliance against the surplus of capitalist powers, and they would continue to employ diplomatic strategies based on national considerations. Ultimately, the feasibility of a worldwide class war seems dubious.  

On Class Warfare  
Han Yong-un  

The devastation following the Great War has persistently lingered, naturally affecting the defeated nations to this day. Nations like Japan and the United States, which benefitted immensely from the war and have basked in what is known as "war fortune," have pursued profoundly lax political and economic policies, contributing largely to today's chronic economic depression.  

Current global capitalism, greatly influenced by economic crises, is several degrees removed from the pre-World War I crises, deserving to be noted as a shift in nature. The crises expand in scope, gravitating toward agricultural downturns. With the entry of capital from large corporations disrupting regional buffers and solidifying export channels for overproduced goods, it is evident that these cyclical phenomena cannot be short-term remedies within the developmental stages of capitalism.  

Reports indicate the proposal for a European Economic Union stemming from Brion's diplomacy; its establishment remains uncertain, yet it's undoubtedly the final gasps of faltering capitalism. The reinforced unity within European capitalist nations will only exacerbate unemployment, while simultaneously posing a direct threat and oppositional force to Soviet Russia, which cannot remain silent.  

Inevitably, this necessitates the definitive confrontation and antagonism between socialist and capitalist states. Increased unemployment will spark the flames of domestic revolution, leading to the emergence of formerly suppressed communist parties. This challenge posed by Soviet Russia will represent a turning point for collapsing capitalist states, rendering eventual global class warfare inevitable. The prospect of the Second World War holds great intrigue for us.  

Opportunities Ripen  
Choe In  

War represents an unparalleled catastrophe in human society, one that eclipses all other forms of suffering. Specifically, during the European War, human loss and impact incurred immense damages quantified in astronomical figures; direct military expenditures amounted to over three trillion won, indirect costs reached two trillion, totalling approximately six trillion won. The substantial mobilization led to nearly sixty million participants, with injuries suffered by over thirty-three million soldiers; civilian casualties surged past ten million, exceeding half the population of Korea.  

If we categorize these expenses and figures by country, we observe the following:  

Country        Britain            France               Russia             Italy           Germany  

War Costs     760 billion won   486 billion won    451 billion won   248 billion won   740 billion won  

Deaths         3 million         4.5 million        9 million       Unknown        6 million  

Country        Austria-Hungary   Ottoman Empire      America  

War Costs     412 billion won   25 billion won  

Deaths         Unknown            Unknown                300,000  

Moreover, deaths from non-combat causes, such as disease and starvation, far exceed combat-related casualties. This dispels any notion that war is anything but tremendously catastrophic.  

From both the perspective of our Cheondogyo belief in human dignity and general humanitarian principles, I hope fervently for a day without war to arrive swiftly, and for the realization of lasting world peace where all peoples can live harmoniously as one family. I deeply regret that the issue of the anticipated Second World War has even come to be discussed at all.  

However, the prevailing global currents seem unfavourable, with surface-level appearances of tranquility masking fierce preparations for conflict by the major powers, preparing for a storm that could suddenly erupt at any time.  

In general, there are multiple causes for the genesis of war; however, the principal modern cause would often arise from the contradictions inherent in imperialism and the subsequent clashes among imperialist nations. Concretely, this can be expressed through two central issues: one is population issues—effectively, food supply concerns—and the other is economic issues, namely contests over commercial goods and market control. These two focal points lead to conflicts, with nations embroiled in colonial disputes and market rivalries.  

Preventive measures against these wars consist of alliances, treaties, and discussions on disarmament, where nations seek to balance their respective powers. However, the proclaimed "balance" is far from permanent, bound inevitably to break, and once this imbalance occurs, war is typically the outcome.  

Simply put, as with physical principles where forces flow toward areas of less resistance, wars also erupt among nations when power balances are disturbed. Presently, various scholars and commentators advocate for world peace and engage in international conferences aiming for peace; yet in the practical realm, the annual population growth of over twenty-three million starkly contrasts with the static nature of suitable arable land, with only fifty-three billion land areas potentially farmable among the total one hundred thirty-two billion.  

With this limited amount of habitable land, accommodating an infinite increase in population leads inevitably to conflict and rivalry. The issue of population growth serves as a significant catalyst for future global wars. It is widely assumed that the next battlegrounds will revolve around the Pacific, rather than solely centered on the recent European conflict. Central to this will undoubtedly be issues related to China.  

While global powers publicly advocate for peace, they harbor ulterior motives, preparing for war with greedy and ruthless hearts. The recent disarmament talks held in London demonstrate this: the U.S. and Japan engaged in endless bickering over military costs, reflecting public sentiments predominantly anticipating future conflict rather than genuinely aspiring for peace.  

Today’s world resembles an active volcano; though the surface may appear calm, eruptions could occur at any moment.  

China, having briefly unified under Chiang Kai-shek in response to the southern government’s autocracy, has found itself backsliding into chaos as warlords rise to coalesce under a northern government. Recently, amid the weakened central authority, Communist forces have seized control in regions including Changsha, thrusting China again into turmoil.   

Whether Chiang Kai-shek or any other individual can stabilize this crisis remains uncertain. In my view, no current figure or system can unify China until its transportation infrastructure is completed. In any nation where civil conflicts emerge, there exists no absence of a decisive governmental response, unlike in China, where the government's ability to quickly suppress insurrection is severely hampered by transportation inefficiencies. This led to Sun Yat-sen’s pushing for railway expansion as the foremost task following his successful initial revolution—a vision reflective of substantial insight that few dared to propose then.  

Because of the inability to unify, China will continue to remain prey to great powers eyeing its economic interests. The ensuing battle for commercial and market dominance will intensify, with the fiercest feuds anticipated between Japan and the U.S. The unyielding resistance will lead either to armed conflict or further escalation of tensions and instability. While specifics of timing and outcomes remain daunting, it is clear that warfare becomes almost unavoidable under such circumstances.  

The U.S.'s Defensive Offensive  
Zhu Yo-han  

Since the First World War failed to yield decisive results, it logically follows that a Second World War may emerge. However, present-day powers, having learned from the past, are more inclined to attempt avoiding war at all costs—holding disarmament conferences in hopes of replicating the desire to prevent conflict found after the previous war.  

Nevertheless, indicators suggest that the European Economic Union will falter, as it is unlikely for Britain to yield to it. While some foresee class struggle, it’s improbable that such conflicts will emerge on an international scale. Should warfare occur, it is more likely to arise in the continental imperialist model instead. Furthermore, if the U.S. employs a more aggressive strategy, the focus would undoubtedly shift toward East Asia.  

The uncertainties of this complex situation defy simplification; hence, I remain unable to elaborate extensively.  

Centered in the Pacific  
Kim Gyeong-jae  

1  
All are familiar with the prevailing dynamics, where Britain, the U.S., and Japan dominate global affairs, and among these, it appears as if the U.S. holds sway over most of civilization today.  

2  
It is indeed true that powers aim to avoid war. Therefore, various congresses and disarmament talks have been convened multiple times. Yet war does not transpire upon desire alone. The surrounding factors, particularly economic conditions, serve as underlying sources of conflict.  

While I could provide various examples, it remains undeniable that Europe contends with many issues unsolved by peaceful means, leading one to ponder whether conflict will reemerge shortly. As discussed previously, some political figures predicted that war against Japan could arise as early as 1931. Thus, could there not be parallels to the previous European War igniting anew, possibly centered on the ongoing crisis in China?  

  

**Summary**

The historical context revolving around the predictions of impending conflict reveals the pervasive anxieties of colonial subjects such as Koreans, further underscoring the fragility of the geopolitical balance during this period.

새로운 世界的 不安, 風雲漸急 第二世界大戰爭?!
問題를 取扱하면서...편집자
트로츠키-는 前來의 세계대전을 「자본주의 열강의 팽창한 생산력과 각기 국경 장벽과의 충돌이다」는 간단한 일언으로 표현을 하엿다. 이 표현이 어데까지든지 사실을 정확하게 시찰 한데서 나온 것이라는 것은 누구나 거부하지 못할 것이다.
과연 삐스막크와 윌헬름3세의 鐵血政策으로써 연방 통일을 달성한 獨逸의 신흥 제국을 중심으로 墺地利, 匂牙利와 老大 土耳其 제국을 일단으로 한 일대 세력의 빨칸 메소포타미아 小亞細亞 極東 등 방면으로의 활약은 英佛을 중심으로 일본과 이태리 그리고 필경은 미국까지 합하야 그에 대항을 아니치 못할 사태에 빠트리고 말은 것이다. 그것은 물론 前記 地方에 정치적 군사적 세력을 잡음으로써 전 세계 적으로 원료와 상품 시장을 독점하려는 獨逸과 墺地利.匂牙利(土耳其는 消極的 의미 밧게는 가지지 못 하엿섯다)의 팽창한 생산력을 등에 질머진 침략 정책에 대하야 其餘의-특히 英國 열강이 기득권을 혹은 新地境을 개척하려는 데서  나온 것이다.＜2＞
이리하야 세계 열강의 자본주의는 大戰으로써 그 발전의 클라이막스에 다달으고 만 것이다.
大戰전의 세계 열강의 자본주의는 부단의 상승 과정에 잇섯다. 끈이지 아니하고 상승 되여 오다가 피하려나 피할 수 업는 곳에 다달음애 그와 가튼 충돌이 생긴 만큼 그들은 그 충돌에서 어든바 결과가 반듯이 잇서야 할 것이다. 그러면 그 결과는 무엇인가?
첫재로 그들은 패전 측의 영토를 분할하여 가젓다. 그리하야 그등 승전 측에서는 전쟁으로 부진 되엿든 국내 시장의 需要와 새로 어든 영토로 또는 패전국의 만만한 국경을 넘어 해외 시장의 수요를 채우느라고 일시적이나마 생산의 활기를 보지 아니 한 것도 어니엇섯다. 그러나 그들의 어든 바 새로운 영토나 국내 시장의 수요가 무제한코 계속 될 수 업는 것은 일목요연한 사실 이엇섯다.
인제야말로 국내로나 해외로나 향하야 그들의 가진 바 생산력을 소화식힐 아모런 대책도 그들에게 업시 되엿다. 생산력이 소화가 될 여지가 업다면 그것은 자본주의가 발전의 상승기로부터 一轉하야 하강기에 들어섯다는 것을 스사로 말하는 것이다.
둘재로 한가지 중대한 결과는 大戰 전까지 세계의 경제가 歐羅巴를-그 중에도 영국을-중심으로 움즉이고 잇든 것이 미국으로 옴기어 갓다는 것이다. 大戰 당시에 크나큰 利를 본 미국은 전 세계의 경제를 좌우할 만큼 국제 금융의 大王 格이 되여잇다.
셋재로는 세계 약소민족의 해방운동이 요원의 불길과 갓치 일어나는 그 사회적 불안과 아울러 그 자체 내에 공업 자본주의 발달로 인한 종주국 상품에 대하야의 의식적 혹은 무의식적 뽀이코트가 일어난 것이다.
넷채로는 人類史 上의 신기원을 지은 소비에트 露西亞의 건설이다.
이리하야 하강기에 잇는 자본주의는 심각한 만성적 경제 공항으로 생산의 부진으로 가지각색의 수단을 다 부리며 헤매이고 잇다. 국내적으로는 산업의 합리화 대외적으로는 국경  마다 관세 장벽의 高築....이것은 물론 그들의 당면의 타개책이겟스나 그것은 도리어 사회적 불안을 조장식히는데 불과한 것이니 이천만으로 헤일 수 잇는 실업군이 전 세계에 퍼지어 街頭에서 헤매인다는 것과 그로 인하야 勞資鬪爭이 각각으로 첨예화하여 간다는 것이다.
미국이 자랑하든 만년 경기도 간 곳 업고 공황의 바람이 휩쓸어 일어나며 잇고 그것이 전세계에 파급 할 것은 물론이다. 영국은 역시 국내의 공황에 염두를 못 내이면서 한편 밧삭 말은 간듸 하나를 엇지 하지 못하여 속을 조이고 잇다. 잠자든 安南이 일어나며 埃及도 독립을 요구한다.
이 외에 구체적 사실을 들자면 한이 업슬 것이다. 그러나 그것들은 전부가 急轉 直下的으로 몰락되여 가는 세계자본주의 열강의 현상이라 하기에 족하다. 이에 대하야 스탈린은 제16회  露西亞聯邦 共産黨大會에서 열강의 국내적으로는 경제적 쇠퇴를 들고 국외적으로는 식민지와의 충돌의 첨예화를 지적하야 제2의 세계대전이＜3＞ 불원함을 예언한 바가 잇다.

자본주의 열강은 물론 힘이 밋치는데 까지는 전쟁을 謀避하려고 하는 것이 사실이다. 그리하야 그들은 군비축소를 말한다. 이것을 보고 세계 평화를 말하는 사람이 잇스나 원래 군축이라는 것은 결코 오려는 평화의 전조가 아니다. 아모리 군축을 한다 하더래도 첫 번 총소리가 터지면서부터 시작하야 대포든지 군함이든지야 얼마든지 만들어 내일 수 잇는 것이니 그 실례는 전년의 世界大戰에서 우리가 보고도 남은 바이다.
열강이 上記와 가튼 경제적 공황과 사회적 불안-터지려는 폭발물을 안은 듯이 아슬아슬한 불안-에 싸여 허덕이면서 국제적으로 새로운 기운 하나가 보이는 것이 잇스니 歐洲經濟聯盟이 그것이다.
이 經濟聯盟은 영국의 쏘삭거림을 바더 이맛살을 찌푸리는 미국에게 아유구용을 하여가면서 獨佛이 중심이 되야 점차 그 정체가 형성되여 가는 기색이 보인다.
과연 이 연맹이 「歐洲」라는 두 글자를 머리에 쓰고 잇는 만큼 경제적으로써 歐羅巴에 군림하고 잇는 미국(英國도 다소의 세력은 남어 잇다.)에 대립 한 것이 소극적으로나마 아닌 것이 아니지 못할 것이다.
이 歐洲經濟聯盟에 대하야는 세계의 달은 一隅에서 크나큰 주목을 밧고 잇게 되엿스니 勞農露西亞가 그것이다. 勞農露西亞에서는 이 聯盟을 毆羅巴 資本主義 露西亞에 대항하려는 것이라고 관찰을 한다.
여긔서 자극을 바듬 인지 최근에는 露土伊 三國의 연맹설까지도 들니게 된다.

물리학상 원칙에 力이란 것은 항상 저항이 약한 곳으로＜7＞ 유입하는 것과 갓치 전쟁도 국가와 국가 간에 세력이 균형을 일는 때 생기는 것이다. 이 세상에 여러 학자와 논객들은 흔히 세계평화를 불으짓고 또 국제 간에도 소위 평화회의니 무엇이니 하는 형식적 평화를 云爲 하고 잇스나 실제로 전 세계 인구의 증식 율을 보면 매년 이천 삼백만식 증가되고 전 세계 地積은 일백 삼십이억 町步 內에 가히 경작을 할 만한 地積은 僅히 오십 삼억 町步에 불과하다. 이 유한한 地積으로 무한히 증식하는 인구를 수용하랴면 엇지 충돌이 업고 쟁탈이 엄스랴 이 인구의 증식 문제가 세계대전에 큰 원인이 될 것은 다시 설명 할 필요도 엄다. 누구나 대개 짐작하는 바와 갓치 금후의 전쟁 무대는 歐洲大戰 때와 가치 저 太西洋을 중심으로 하야 잇지 안코 우리와 가장 각가운 太平洋을 중심으로 이러날 것이다. 즉 동양문제를 중심으로 하야 이러나는 중에도 특히 中國 문제가 문제의 문제일 것 갓다. 세계 열국들은 표면상 소위 평화를 주창하지만은 그 실은 所謂 菩薩口氣에 夜叉心臟으로 공공연하게 전쟁을 준비하고 잇다. 이번에 倫敦에서 열인 所謂 군축회의의 광경을 보더라도 日米 양국이 공히 그 병량의 문제로 무한한 爭詰을 하고 그때 양국 국민간 여론을 보더라도 그 논조가 모다 전쟁이 잇을 것을 먼저 전제로 하는 것이 현저하고 근본적으로 전쟁을 업시 할 성의가 一毫도 뵈히는 것 갓지 안타. 지금의 세계는 맛치 熄火山과 갓터서 비록 표면은 아모럿치 안은 것 가트나 어느날 어느 때에 爆發이 될지 알 수 업는 것이다.

중국은 蔣介石이 남방정부의 專權을 잡으면서 일시적으로 남북을 통일하얏스나 此를 반대하는 군벌파들이 다시 跋扈하야 소위 북방정부를 설립하게 되고 또 근일에는 중앙세력의 미약한 틈을 타서 공산군이 無人의 境과 갓치 長沙 일대를 점령하게 되니 中國은 다시 亂麻의 형태에 빠지고 마럿다. 蔣介石이나 혹은 기타 엇더한 인물이 다시 출마하야 이 난국을 수습할지 알 수 업스나 내가 보기에는 中國은 여하한 인물과 여하한 제도를 쓰던지 근본적으로 교통 기관이 완성되기 전에는 도저히 통일하기 어려울 것이다. 어느 나라던지 내란이 업는 나라가 업지만은 중앙정부가 즉시에 토벌 진정하게 되는 것은 병력도 병력이지만은 第一은 교통기관이 완전한 까닭에, 가령 甲地에서 무슨 暴亂이 이러나도 정부의 명령 일하에 각지 군병이 즉시 그 暴亂地로 집중하야 초벌을 하게 되나 中國은 그와 반대로 교통기관이 아주 불완전하야 한 지방에서 엇던 인물이 근거를 잡고 난을 이르키면 장구한 시일를 가저야 토벌군이 가게 되고 그동안에 적은 방어, 기타 준비를 잘하게 되니 엇지 용이히 평정할 수가 잇스랴. 그럼으로 왕년에 孫逸仙이 제1회 혁명을 성공하얏슬 때에  무엇보다도 먼저 전국에 철도 부설을 주창한 것이다. 이것은 무엇보다도 큰 高見으로서 당시에 누구나 감히 生意도 못하던 것이다. 나는 그의 고견에 대하야 항상 感心하얏다. 中國이 이와 갓치 통일이 되지 못하는 까닭으로 열강은 漁父의 利를 어들 야욕으로 언제이나 中國의 이권을 엿보고 잇다. 무엇보다도 상공품 시장의 쟁탈전이 격렬하게 된다. 그 중에는 日本과 米國의 충돌이 제일 격렬하다. 이와 가치 互相 양보가 업는 이상에는 결국 은 兵火로 相交하는 수 밧게는 업슬 것이다. 그 시기 여하와 결과 여하는 내가 여긔에 말하기도 어렵거니와 나보다도 현명한 여러분이 잘 알고 짐작할 터이니 그 말만은＜8＞ 긋치고 만다.(文責在記者)
