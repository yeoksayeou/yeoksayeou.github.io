Pyongyang...Pyongyang Notes, The Song of the Merchant Woman of Paju  
						Kim An-so  
The sound of the bell ringing is like a melody.  
In the night of Arirang, the cold moon is full.  
Even if I cross the twelve passes of Arirang,  
This parting is a mountain I cannot traverse.

As the wind blows, is today also like this?  
In this pleasant season, all has become still.  
I pick up the brush, yet there is no appointment.  
Hearts and minds are intertwined, yet cannot be separated.

Even if I wish to float my thoughts on the river,  
The wind blows and gives no direction.  
Drifting again, I cannot find my way.  
On the bank of the river, the yellow flowers bloom today as well.

On a spring day, the flowers fall;  
In the wind, the whispers refuse to come.  
Because of you, today too, I hold a cup of dew,  
Singing Arirang alone.

On the trail where stones float with the wind,  
Holding a vignette, I sing and wave goodbye.  
Following the water, my dear travels a thousand miles.  
If only this way I could keep going like this.  
-1930, 8, 10  

On the road to Pyongyang-  
Postscript: This is a song that dreams of the vibrant world of a bright afternoon. As the full moon weeps alone, the gentle waves of the Taedong River shimmer in the rain. The merchant women sing while holding their vignettes and express the worries of their hearts that rise with their own interests. A noble gentleman might find this unpleasant and furrow his brow; however, this too reveals the psychology of the world which cannot be disentangled at the same time, and there are circumstances that may touch the hearts of travelers in the dead of night. I publish this without minding the ridicule of the prominent class; those who laugh may laugh, and those who appreciate it may sincerely applaud.  
<55>  

**Summary**

This article captures the essence of a song reflecting the everyday experiences and emotional struggles of a merchant woman in Pyongyang while evoking the beauty of the city and its rivers. It portrays a vivid image of longing and separation, indicating the complex emotional lives of individuals in colonial Korea. Historians might find it interesting that artistic expressions such as these provide insight into the deep cultural sentiments and resistance to colonial oppression during this period, as expressed through the lens of personal and communal identity.
