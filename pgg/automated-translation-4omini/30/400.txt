기사제목	紙上特別公開 暴利大取締(第一回) (Special Publication on Excessive Profits Control - First Series)  
필자	浩然堂人  
기사형태	세태비평 (Social Commentary)  

Ladies and gentlemen!

In an economic system where goods can hardly circulate and profits can hardly be realized, we must absolutely prevent the practice of profiting from sales... Well, that is... um... therefore... there’s no way to avoid the situation.

Yet, amidst this reality, there are those who grotesquely take advantage of the system, and it cannot be overlooked. So, where do we begin? 

Let’s start with something that cannot be separated from our lives: rice. This has become a target for exorbitant profits, as it is impossible to monitor effectively...

Then there’s fabric? Similarly, the rent on houses? Indeed, the house rent has become quite exorbitant. Additionally, homes nowadays are being constructed in high quantities by certain companies, which are reportedly turning dividends of 60% annually... Ah! Unbelievable!

But, let us move on and begin discussing something even more serious. 

I suddenly recall the costs of medications in hospitals. Traditionally, the practice of medicine is considered a noble pursuit, leading society to deeply respect physicians. However, while it may be inconceivable that they would take advantage, there are reasons we ought to examine this in detail.

Now, let’s begin.

Let’s assume you, dear reader, happen to suffer a sudden illness that might put your parents or siblings in jeopardy. In such a case, you would understandably summon a physician and reluctantly have to pay for a commonplace injection of camphor (캄폴). 

Afterward, regardless of its effectiveness, you need to pay for that injection. How much does it cost? It is about 1 won. For those with even a little money, 1 won is not much, but for the poor, 1 won is a considerable amount. 

Generally, what is the actual cost for that single camphor injection? You must be curious. To find out, take a look at a pricing list published by a large pharmacy. It states that "Camphor injection is 15 tubes for 1.181 won." That means it costs 18 jeon for five injections. Therefore, each injection costs approximately 3.6 jeon. Charging 1 won for an injection that costs only 3.6 jeon!

Then there’s the commonly referred to 606 injection, also known as Salvarsan. Most people may not be familiar with this. Depending on the severity of the illness, this 606 injection is priced from 5 won to 30 won at hospitals. The base cost ranges from 42 jeon to 2 won.

Suppose you happen to contract a fever while taking a nap. When the day arrives, your outfit may be thrown on and be strictly limited to the essentials as it is indeed a rare ailment. However, by simply receiving a specific injection from the hospital, you could resolve the issue. The usual fee for this injection is 1 won, but when it comes to the base cost, the pricing shows that for an injection containing 1 gram of the substance, the total cost can range from 14 jeon to 24 jeon. Yet, they charge 1 won per injection. That is hardly even 10 times markup!

Last summer, at a girl’s school, one student fainted. The school physician, being called, administered a pantopon injection, which is a painkiller and stimulant, for a fee of 5 won. Typically, this injection costs about 1 won. The cost to produce it is around 56 jeon.

There is also a robust stimulant called penicillin. This is an intravenous injection, and if administered correctly, it causes a pleasant sensation throughout the body. For those with the means, it is often given. Nevertheless, the cost of the medicine is usually between 1 and 2 won, while the base cost is between 18 to 20 jeon. 

Next, there is the anti-carkolin injection. If, unfortunately, you were to be troubled by that awful rheumatism, the physician is likely to give you this injection. The production cost is around 33 jeon, yet they charge between 1 to 2 won. Charging 2 won is quite excessive.

Should you be unfortunate enough to require a local surgery, they will administer a cocaine hydrochloride injection first— a truly remarkable medication. After its effects, you won't even feel pain when cut with a knife. Therefore, after treatment, they also charge an injection fee of 50 jeon, while the production cost is only about 6 jeon.

The aforementioned medications are but a fraction of what is available; all of this is indicative.

Now let's say you visit a hospital for constipation and buy the prescribed medicines. What are the components? 

Sodium Bicarbonate 3.0 G  
Lead Nitrate 1.0 G   
Powdered Homin 0.06 G  

This is typically divided into three packets for a daily dose. The packaging cost is usually around 30 jeon, whereas the raw materials amount to almost nothing, since sodium bicarbonate would cost around 5 jeon for 3 grams derived from 45,800 grams at 7 won. Lead nitrate costs about 1 jeon for 1 gram from a total of 5 won and finally powdered homin costs so little, it would be nearly worthless. So altogether, it equals around 1 jeon and 2 rin. Yet, the selling price is 30 jeon!

Another prescription exists for a cough remedy:  
Propo-mycine 3.0 G  
Almond Water 4.0 G  
Single Corm 5.0 G  

These are poured into a bottle and told to be taken in three batches. This packet typically costs around 50 jeon, while the total cost of components is only about 2 jeon, adding up to 30 jeon meaning it’s 15 to 40 times the production cost.

If you purchase fever remedy:  
Bira-midon 0.6  
Lactose 1.0  

They typically sell this for 30 jeon, but the total production cost would like be around 1 won, making it a 30 times markup.

If you experience severe stomach pain, a typical prescription would confuse with:  
Sodium Bicarbonate 1.5  
Lead Nitrate 0.6  
Powdered Rodogis 0.03  

It may be priced around 50 jeon, yet the actual cost is lesser than 1 jeon.

Should you injure yourself seriously and visit a hospital, they might treat you with boric acid solution, sprinkle some antiseptic powder, and cover it with a bandage for about 40 jeon, all while the total production cost is approximately only 2 jeon.

Visiting fees for home consultations are tremendously high. Those who can afford it would find little complaint over the prices, yet the impoverished, in need of urgent treatment, end up paying exorbitant amounts as well which amount to 2 or 3 won while one could make do with 60-70 jeon.

While much can be said on this topic, I will conclude for now as it might be better tackled in the next issue. Those suffering will surely feel indignant as it’s not merely the cost of the pharmaceuticals that should factor in but also the cost of the expertise rendered. However, whether those arguments will hold weight is another question.

In conclusion, one must acknowledge that a skill is not limited only to medical practice, as any professional activity resembles a skill, whether being a driver or a farmer.

  
**Summary**

This article critiques the exorbitant prices charged for medical treatments and pharmaceuticals, comparing these costs to their production prices in an effort to highlight the injustice faced by patients, especially the poor who are unable to afford necessary medical care. In the context of colonial Korea, this discussion points to broader issues of exploitation within the healthcare system, exacerbated by the socio-economic divide, making it significant for historians studying the impact of colonial rule on public health and welfare.
