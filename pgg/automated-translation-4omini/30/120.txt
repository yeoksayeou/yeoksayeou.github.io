기사제목	平壤...平壤雜記, 마닐라 舞踊 (Pyeongyang...Pyeongyang Miscellanea, Manila Dance)  
필자	시어딤  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literature Other)  

The summer of that year (the year of Gyeihai) saw little sunlight. For two or three hours, the sun might flicker, but soon dark clouds would roll in, followed by thunder and rain, and day after day, the Daedong River would rise by 56 chuk or more than 10.

Seeing the Daedonggang as the only beauty of Pyongyang and the sole lifeline of our Pyongyang was a cause for great concern. It seemed foolish for anyone not to glimpse a fishing rod cast upon the wide river, waiting for fish to approach; yet for our life, it provided the greatest comfort and joy, and was no less than a means of leisure. However, as the rainy season changed the currents and the fish scattered—despite spending an entire day waiting with bated breath over my fishing line—I could hardly think of catching even a single rotten mullet. We cursed the heavens.

Even if the currents changed, that was fine. If it rained, at least we could prepare some fish soup instead of fishing... We gazed towards the northern sky and sighed.

Then, on this day, for some reason, the thick dark clouds that had been spread out until noon began to thin and scatter towards the western sky by lunchtime. The freshly fallen sunlight flowed softly across the red waters of the Daedonggang.

Those of us gathered at the club—H. K. Seoul R. and I—looked upon the Daedonggang with seemingly dreamy faces. “What’s going on?” H asked. 

“Let’s go out,” we replied, not needing to say where we were going or what we would do. We only needed to change into our river attire and quietly prepared our mae-sang (seaweed). Then we set out towards the river.

The water from the muddy red earth of Yangdeokmaeng Mountain poured into the Yellow Sea during the rainy season, creating a very red Daedonggang. The current was also faster than usual. As we paddled against the flowing Daedonggang, we sweated profusely, competing with the rushing waters towards the fish soup maker’s home.

After an hour or so, we finally reached our destination. Yelling at the top of our lungs from the mae-sang, we called out to the soup maker. He rushed out with wide eyes, surprised to see someone cooking fish soup during this rainy season, and stared at us, bewildered.

“Hey, prepare the ingredients!” we urged him.

“Eh? -Yes, yes,” he answered in a flustered manner, then hurried back inside to prepare the cooking materials and brought out our mae-sang as well. After loading it up, we took our mae-sang and rowed back towards Banwoldo against the red water.

Typically, the area where we cooked fish soup would leave no room for a needle to fall, but at this time, there was no one else to be seen. We felt fortunate there were no other foolish minds around, despite the numerous morons in Pyongyang. 

“There are no women to take our spots.” 

We were relieved there was nothing like that, mainly due to the fact that there was no fear of losing our advantageous cooking spot. We set down our mae-sang in our usual cooking spot on the island. After laying our belongings on the hill, we drew lots to decide our roles. My lot was to catch a chicken.

The first few times I found it horrific and terrifying; I had always let someone else do the killing, yet I had honed my skills enough to catch a chicken comfortably. Additionally, once I had studied anatomy with Dr. O during our fish soup sessions, I earned the nickname “CEO of O’s Company.” I chopped down two chickens in no time. After that, with the carcasses lying still and regretting my previous actions, I looked up at the gloomy sky from time to time.

“Oh no! What a situation.” 

After a short while, when H who was cooking fired up the stove, I noticed a situation developing. I saw that the waters of the Daedonggang had risen to where we had placed our cooking pot and were about to enter the stove.

“It’s high tide. The freshwater is especially intense.” 

We had already prepared for this scenario, and after pulling up the chicken feathers by hand, we advanced to a higher position with the pot, and H offered fresh water while the other three pulled out feathers and cleaned the entrails, kneading it properly and removing the feathers in the pot. After that, H, who was stoking the fire, was busy while the other three enjoyed a brief respite.

“Fireman, you’re working hard.” 

“Hey, I’m a member of Company O!” 

“Call me the CEO, I’m not just an ordinary member.” 

After jokingly bantering with each other, we turned to find smooth flat seashells scattered about and started a competition to skim stones over the water. Splashing and laughing, the stones glided back and forth across the surface.

“Look at mine! I made it sixteen skips.” 

“I passed twenty a while ago!” 

“Stop lying; I can count yours. You can’t have done more than ten!” 

“Hahaha.”

While we playfully wasted time with bantering, we heard H’s voice again from across the water. Looking back, the river continued to rise to the level we had cooked at previously. We would not be able to row to a higher place. 

The sunset over the Daedonggang viewed from Banwoldo was beautiful. The evening glow illuminated the gentle current, while the reflection made a lovely picture against the flowing waters. The small willows on Banwoldo swayed gracefully.

Soon, the fish had vanished. We gathered again and started to subsume the cooked meals into the pot with rice while starting to drink liquor using the bones. The liquor was Pyongyang soju.

As a person who typically could not handle alcohol, I had to sip once while others enjoyed several cups. However, I found myself similarly intoxicated with my friends. After finishing our drinking toast, feeling the lightness, I found myself at a loss, my senses fading into darkness. It seemed everything around me blended into splendid confusion. It was then that everything turned dark, while within Pyongyang city, the lights twinkled.

Feeling completely intoxicated, I cannot recall, but at some point, we shifted our cooking pot yet again. Nevertheless, we merely thought that the rising tide was quite impressive.

Oh! Oh! Oh!

What sounds were those? 

All the oddest sounds landed in my ears dreamily, and ultimately, I slipped into slumber, awoken by my friends.

“The meal is ready. Come eat!”

“Meal?”

We drew around the cooking pot once more. We enjoyed the well-cooked soup together, tasting it appreciatively.

“Let’s perform the Manila Dance!”

Once we finished eating, R proposed this.

“Sure,” we all agreed without dissent.

The Manila Dance was a name we had given. After finishing the soup, we would stoke a fire and each grab a piece of firewood, dancing wildly around the flames. Our style of dance wasn't anything special. Just like the native islanders seen in travel pictures, we would circle around the fire, swaying back and forth. With our faces darkened by smoke, we resembled those islanders even more. 

We ignited the remained pieces of wood, plucked some willows, and tied them to our heads, beginning our circle dance. Burdened by the world's worries, we settled into tremendous joy, dancing. We sang too.

If the soup maker had not been agonizingly worried about us, we might have danced there for a few hours. The soup maker, growing weary and worried, eventually brought us back more mae-sang as a way of coaxing us. 

Then we informed the soup maker that the Daedonggang was currently rising drastically and was on the brink of danger.

While we were resilient and working hard, our boat drifted back downstream, and we struggled to even reach the hillside.

That evening, near the bridge, the path overflowed with Daedonggang water, which swept over the entire area and even flowed all the way to Namsi Station and the hospital nearby.

This was the first occurrence reported in the years since the Pyongyang revolution, a significant flood in the year of Gyeihai. (End)  

**Summary**  

This article captures a nostalgic summer day of river fishing and cooking fish soup in Pyongyang, interspersed with camaraderie among friends. It describes both the joy of their activities and the subsequent flooding of the Daedonggang. Historians might find interest in the depiction of everyday life in colonial Korea, shedding light on social practices and leisure activities during a time of political and social change.

우리는 異議가 엇섯다.
마닐라 舞踊이라 하는 것은, 우리가 지은 이름이엇섯다. 우리는 어죽을 다 쑤어 먹은 뒤에는 언 œ던지 아직 남아 잇는 나무깹이에 불을 질르고 제각기 나무깹이 하나씩을 둘러 메인 뒤에 그 불의 주위를 춤추며 도라 가는 것이엇섯다. 춤이라야 별다른 방식이 업섯다. 活動寫眞에서 보든 바 南洋 土人들이 추는 것과 마츤가지로 웃줄 웃줄하며 불의 주의를 도라 다니는 것 뿐이엇섯다. 우리들의 얼굴이 식검어케 탄 것은 더욱 南洋土人과 가텃다. 이 南洋人의 춤을 우리는 부러 마닐라 舞踊이라고 명명하는 것이엇섯다.
우리들은 남겨 노흔 나무ㅅ단에 불을 질르고 버드나무를 한가지씩 꺽거서 억게에 메인 뒤에 웃줄 웃줄 불의 주위를 돌기 시작하엿다. 세상의 온갓 근심을 니즌 듯이 커다란 悅樂에 잠겨서 우리는 춤을 추엇다. 그러고 노래를 불럿다.
우리의 행방을 근심하여 어죽집 주인이 부러 차저와 주지 안엇드라면 우리는 몃 시간은 거기서 춤추며 돌앗슬넌지 모를 것이 엇섯다. 어죽집 주인은 근심하다 근심하다 못하여 만츰내 우리를 차즈려 매생이를 저어 가지고 건너 온 것이엇섯다. 그러고 우리는 그 어죽 주인에게 처음으로 大同江은 지금 刻刻으로 增水가 되는 중이며, 발서 위험한 상태에 드러 갓다는 것을 아럿다.
매생이에 능한 우리가 전력을 다하여 저엇지만, 우리의 배는 고추 건너가기는커녕 대동강 아래까지 밀려나려 와서야 겨우 언덕에 대엿다.

이튼날 저녁때는 이아ㅅ다리 근처의 시가는 사람의 한 길이 넘도록 대동강물이 몰려 드럿다. 그러고 또 그 이튼날은 시가로 넘처들은 大´ž江 물이 신시가 일대를 전부 바다로 화하게 하고 그 물은 멀니 정거장 압과 자헤의원 아페까지 니르럿다.

이것이 평양 개벽 이후 처음 보는 일이라는 癸亥年의 큰물이 엇섯다. (끗)
