잡지명	별건곤 제11호  
발행일	1928년 02월 01일  
기사제목	世外世 人外人 奇人奇事錄 (Chronicles of Extraordinary People and Events in the World)  
저자	孟峴學人 (Myeonghyeon Hak-in)  
기사형태	논설 (Essay)  

Is a person to be called a remarkable person, a wandering person, an adept, or a sage? In today’s terms, is he an anarchist, a stateless individualist, or a nihilist? It is difficult to pinpoint his true identity, yet, indeed, there are two extraordinary individuals in ancient history, Xu You (許由) and Chao Fu (巢父), who lived in a world detached from the mundane. 

“On the hillside of Misusan, Xu You and Chao Fu are known... …” 

This verse I often heard growing up in the countryside, sung by young drinkers. Even in Seoul, I would see it performed in theaters and brothels by so-called famous singers. Sometimes, I indulge in humming it myself.

Who were Chao Fu and Xu You, and what were Misusan and the Yingshu River? Why have these great talents of ancient times earned such fame and remembrance, sung and lauded by so many, from courtesans to performers, even to the point of evoking overwhelming emotions? This question piques my curiosity.

Those who know, know; those who don't, don't. Regardless, I wish to discuss the substantial lives of Chao Fu and Xu You, two remarkable figures who transcended worldly pursuits. In this world, where individuals chase after fame and fortune, their exceptional stories are worth recounting.

For those who merely engage in dance to the songs sung by figures like Lee Dong-bok or Song Man-gyeong, prepare yourselves for a few missteps in my account as I anticipate the readers’ engagement.

In the era of Eastern philosophy, traversing through the realms of Huangdi, Dihuang, and Renhuang, passing through the Three Sovereigns—Taihao, Yan Emperor, Huangdi—and the Five Emperors: Xiaohou, Zhuanyou, Diku, Yao, and Shun, there emerged the still-remembered age of Yao, that esteemed sovereign.

In the words of Sima Qian in his Records of the Grand Historian, Yao was a ruler whose benevolence was as vast as heaven, wisdom as profound as the gods, virtue as radiant as the sun, and aspirations as lofty as the clouds. For fifty years, governing Pingyang, the results were pristine; doors did not close, there were no thieves, and the common people simply sang ballads in the manicured paths during tranquil nights and moonlit nights.

"When the sun rises, we work; when the sun sets, we eat; we drink from the wells we dig and eat from the fields we plow. What does the emperor's great power offer us?" 

This was the common people's song at the time. From this, one may discern Yao's character and envision his governance.

No matter how benevolent a king may be, can he truly remain a Son of Heaven for eternity? Even with exemplary governance, can he be a ruler for life? Especially in a time when the bloodline inheritance system wasn't firmly established, and Yao's son, Danju, was notably unworthy, he could not simply pass on his rule. Thus, the quest for worthy successors was paramount.

During this period, in the land known as Misusan, there lived two remarkable figures, Xu You and Chao Fu, often foraging for herbs and catching fish. Living happily and unconcernedly, at ease in the universe, they would laugh and sing with the world, leading others to call them sages or wise men. Upon hearing of these extraordinary individuals, Yao, overwhelmed with joy, went discreetly to Misusan to seek out Xu You, requesting him to take charge of the world.

“The sun and moon shine, yet the human flame never extinguishes. To rule the world is indeed challenging. Even if the timely rain falls, the waters remain as they are, and it is certainly challenging to manage a marsh." 

Upon hearing these words, Xu You was taken aback, stunned to meet someone of such stature. He smiled lightly and responded sincerely.

“If you seek to rule the world, how long have you done so? Now you ask me to take charge, is it that you want to gain fame? Fame is but a guest to reality, so are you asking me to serve as a mere guest?”

The words struck Yao deeply. With a swift rejection, Xu You turned away, washed his hands in the Yingshu River, cleansing his ears from hearing such vile words.

At that moment, Chao Fu, who had been herding cattle, approached the river and spotted Xu You washing his ears. Curiously, he inquired, “What are you doing over there?”

Since they were friends who shared no secrets, Xu You did not hold back and explained everything simply.

“I am washing my ears because I have heard such disgraceful speech.”

Upon hearing this, Chao Fu elegantly replied, “If you cleanse filthy ears from dirty sounds, the dirty water you wash with will not be fit even for a cow.”

Chao Fu leisurely walked away. After witnessing this, Xu You laughed heartily.

“Well then, you too are quite remarkable—what's filthy about the world we live in? We shall be great men, great sages wandering alone in the universe...” 

And after finishing washing, Xu You returned leisurely into the forest.

What marvelous extraordinary people they were! Even in modern times, are such individuals still among us?

To chase tigers and drive away dragons is an unparalleled art. The Oriental scholar, Seo Hwa-dam.

Seong Cheon-dong.

With the Lotus Sutra, he drove out a fierce tiger.

Mr. Seo Hwa-dam (敬德) was not just a scholar but also a master of miraculous arts, and he performed many miracles throughout his life. Though one might deem essential to mention all the miracles performed, I shall share just a few significant examples.

While teaching several disciples, an old monk came across and bowed respectfully before leaving. After the old monk’s departure, Mr. Seo casually commented to his disciples that this monk intended to visit the “Cultural Nine-Month Mountain” where a tiger would visit a certain village tonight to devour a girl. However, human fate is fixed, so there was nothing he could do to save her.

Among the disciples, one asked whether there was a way for him to save her. Mr. Seo responded, saying that there was no strategy for ordinary people to do so. The disciple implored Mr. Seo to let him try, so Mr. Seo gave him a copy of the Lotus Sutra and instructed him not to yield under any circumstances.

The disciple immediately rode a fine horse to the girl’s house, where it happened to be her wedding day. When he spoke, the homeowner initially dismissed him as a mere stranger and ignored him. However, upon repeated insistence, the owner began to take notice and asked many questions. Following Mr. Seo’s instructions, the disciple secured the bride in a secluded room, barring the doors with multiple nets.

He then calmly stayed in the adjacent room, lighting candles, and read the Lotus Sutra. Around midnight, a harsh wind blew, and the fierce growling of the tiger echoed outside.

Everyone in the house panicked, thrown into disarray. Without resting, the disciple raised his voice and continued reading the Sutra. Eventually, dawn broke, and the tiger left.

The family expressed immense gratitude, but upon his return, Mr. Seo smiled and said that although he had succeeded, the disciple had misread three lines of the Sutra, and the tiger had bitten the threshold three times. Upon checking, the disciple indeed found three tiger claw marks on the threshold.

From then on, the disciple felt profound reverence for Mr. Seo, who was deemed an extraordinary person by all.

Unconsumed and sleepless for thirty days.

Was that all? There was a monk in Geumgangsan who, once he heard of Mr. Seo’s great reputation, visited to challenge him to a competition. They agreed to a wager, to not eat or sleep.

Both men readily accepted, and for a full month, they endured without food or sleep. On the day the wager ended, the monk collapsed, suffering a nosebleed, and finally succumbed to sleep after a week.

However, Mr. Seo, even after the deadline, showed no sign of fatigue, continuing his usual routine without deviation. The monk apologized and left.

Between brothers, playing with fish and dragon.

Mr. Seo's brother also delighted in studying Confucianism alongside him, and after ten years wandering to learn strange and wondrous techniques, he returned home, eager to showcase his newly acquired skills. He poured water on a tray and lazily wrote the character for fish, and in an instant, a goldfish began to leap within the tray, eagerly springing to life.

Mr. Seo, seeing this, immediately wrote the character for dragon onto a plate and, pouring water onto it, summoned a tumultuous storm, with colorful dragons twisting and turning as they entered the room.

His brother was astonished and terrified, unsure of what to do. Mr. Seo waved his hand, and they vanished, leaving only the bewildered brother. 

Getting such minor tricks did not deserve study, he told him, deeming them as mere tricks. Thereafter, he encouraged his brother to pursue studies in deeper theological understanding.

Without time, the peach blossoms bloomed abundantly.

In his garden, there was a lone peach tree that remained barren while all others bloomed in the spring. After observing it for a while, Mr. Seo dug around its roots and covered it with soil. 

By the following night, the tree was in full bloom. His disciples were amazed and asked how he had achieved it. Mr. Seo humorously replied that no miraculous artistry had been employed; he simply allowed the tree’s energy paths to flow freely. For all things to grow well, the interaction of yin and yang must be balanced; this particular tree had not bloomed well due to excess soil piling on its roots.

Now that I removed that excess soil, the energy was released, allowing it to flourish.

In the realm of beauty where female and male coexisted.

During that time, a famed courtesan, Hwang Jin, boasted unparalleled beauty, literary talent, and musical skill, claiming to be one of the three achievements alongside Mr. Seo's teachings and the splendid waterfalls of Pakwon.

She greatly admired Mr. Seo, but due to his high ethical standards, never entertained her thoughts.

However, seeking to test Mr. Seo's virtue against another renowned monk, Wang Saku, she proposed that they live together. Without hesitation, Mr. Seo agreed promptly.

Living together for several months, Hwang Jin, an intelligent and talented woman, resorted to various means to attract Mr. Seo, yet he stood unyielding and tranquil, unaffected by her charm. Unable to approach him, she ultimately apologized.

Hwang Jin later approached Wang Saku, seeking to live with him as well, yet on the very first proposal, he refused outright. Left with no choice, Hwang Jin returned home, pondering various methods of approach. Disguised as a Buddhist monk, she sought to gain entrance, and within a few days, Wang Saku succumbed to his feelings and broke his vows. This anecdote led to the saying that “those monks are worthy of deception.”

Foreknowledge of disaster and self-assessment of death, a modern prophet, Nam Sa-gu (南師古).

The Bamboo West Zen Man.

Nam Sa-gu, from Samcheok, Gangwon Province, was known by the name Geugam. He possessed a striking appearance from a young age, bearing the grace of a celestial being. In his youth, while visiting the Buddhist temple of Beomeosa in Uijeongbu, he encountered a monk burdened with a large package. When asked if he needed help, the monk graciously accepted.

Unlike most, who would deign to mock a monk, Nam treated the monk with kindness and offered assistance, akin to Zhang Zipang honorably returning a magic shoe to Huang Shigong (黄石公). Together, they climbed the peak of Pureun Mountain to play a game of Go.

After some time spent engaging in the game, the monk suddenly shouted and vanished. Nam, unfazed, continued attentively to his Go board until, a while later, the monk reappeared with a look of fright, asking why he was not afraid.

“Was there any fear when you were present? If you are here, why would one fear?” replied Nam.

Respectfully, the monk began to teach him geomancy, astronomy, divination, and other arcane crafts. Since then, he became well-versed in the affairs of the world and could foresee events even a hundred years into the future.

One day, while traveling on a donkey, he suddenly wept and fainted. Concerned, his servant rushed to his side, inquiring after his distress. Nam replied, “Today in Japan, a man will rise to slay our Korean people by tens of thousands. I may be old and die soon; I shall not witness this calamity, but you shall.”

It later turned out that he prophesized the birth of Toyotomi Hideyoshi (豊臣秀吉).

His skills in geomancy led him to excavate his ancestors' tomb but ultimately, he was unable to find an auspicious site and lamented, stating, “Fortune relies not merely on skill.” This lamentation would be known as Nam Sa-gu’s Nine Distressing Mournings.

While in Seoul, the constellation of Tai Star signaled doom, and the official in charge, Lee Ban-sin, predicted his own death. Nam responded with a smile, saying such predictions are only valid to their respective masters.

When he returned to his hometown, it was not long before he passed away. The people revered him, stating that he was a spirit of the Tai Star, and poet Lee Dal remarked of his passing with the elegy: 

“Like the phoenix rides through the wind,  
Who else is beneath the curtain of the world,  
The thousand trees of peach blossom bloom each spring.”

His miraculous stories are numerous, but unfortunately, only a few have been passed down.

Towering and uninhibited, surpassing the mundane, the romantic poet, Lim Baek-ho (林白湖).

The Downstairs Cave Dweller.

Who does not know Lim Baek-ho, the famous gallant of Hanyang? Yet his extraordinary spirit and audacity deserve further examination. 

Lim Baek-ho was born to the famous Jin clan during the reign of King Seongjong of the Joseon dynasty. From a young age, he exhibited phenomenal talents, committing even vast texts to memory by simply reading them. As he grew, he sought learning with Seong Daegok (成大谷) and further enriched his understanding.

By the ninth year of the Cheongjeong period, he became a scholar in the civil service, advancing to official capacities, yet disdained ambition and instead chose to embark on lengthy travels across the renowned landscapes of the land.

Whenever he visited places where literary friends gathered, he exhibited the charming demeanor of a cultured poet; among gallant gentlemen, he displayed his valiant characteristics; while in a gathering of beautiful women, he unveiled a captivating grace that would outshine even Du Mu (杜牧).

Even to write a parting poem amidst parting turned into a fitting farewell could evoke reverent emotions from all while the cries of latecomers would touch every soft heart, echoing still into posterity.

He would often visit a renowned courtesan in Kaeseong, known for her beauty and poetic talent. Upon hearing of her, Lim sought opportunity to visit, sensing it unlikely he would return and, without zest, he let his wanderlust consume him. 

He half-heartedly questioned those crossing paths, inquiring after her grave, and learned it lay beside the lonely hill. Once he reached the burial ground, he composed an elegy and wept heartily while pouring a drink in offering, unable to hold back his feelings. This instance highlighted his unrestrained nature and poetic flair.

Yet, his poetry, brimming with illustration, was often criticized: “How could such whimsical poetic expressions reflect a serious poet’s demeanor?”

Even now, some people recount hymns of his poetry, celebrating his unique form of expression. Lim Baek-ho's overly expressive nature invokes feelings of sympathy among readers. 

Despite any acclaim, behavioral expectations during his lifetime were rife with pressures troubling his ambitions. Historians may find his choice to forfeit status for a life of wandering worth noting, as it reflects the struggles and societal constraints faced during the difficult colonial period.  

**Summary**

The article presents chronicles of remarkable individuals from ancient history, particularly Xu You and Chao Fu, who lived in simplicity, gathering renown for their philosophical comebacks against powerful figures like Yao. The lives and thoughts of Xu You and Chao Fu are intertwined with the themes of liberation from societal constraints and the pursuit of individual meaning, akin to the constraints of 20th-century Korean colonial experiences. Historians may find this juxtaposition of cultural embodiments in antiquity and modernity intriguing, noting how figures like Xu You and Chao Fu express humanity’s enduring quest for freedom against oppressive norms.

世外世 人外人 奇人奇事錄
千古의 二大奇人 許由와 巢父

孟峴學人

奇人이라 할가 浪人이라 할가 達人이라 할가 聖人이라 할가? 지금 말로 무정부주의자라 할가 無國家主義者라 할가 또는 개인주의자라 할가 허무주의자라 할가? 그 정체를 지적키는 갑작히 곤란하나 그러나 엇잿든 堯가 천하를 가지라 하매 픽 웃고 그 말이 더럽다하야 穎川水에 귀를 씻고 宇宙間에 獨往獨來한 二大 奇人이 잇스니 그가 즉 巢父와 許由이다.

「箕山穎水別乾坤 巢父許由노라잇고... ...」
「... ... 穎川水 도라드니 許由는 어이하야 팔을 것고 귀를 씻고 巢父는 어이하야 소곱비를 빗겻난고... ...」
이 短歌 一節은 내가 어려서 시골 잇슬 때 풋건달들의 酒興끗테서도 만히 드럿거니와 자라서 서울와서 劇場이나 靑樓에서 所謂 名唱歌客의 입에서도 갓금 듯는 노래이다. 뿐만 안이라 나도 심심하면 갓금 불느는 노래이다.
대체 巢父는 엇던 사람이고 許由는 엇던 사람이며 箕山은 엇던 산이고 穎水는 엇던 믈이기로 천고의 佳人才子가 입으로 붓으로 노래하고 떠들며 妓生앗씨 광대서방＜2＞ 外入정이 八亂捧까지 닷투어 謳歌하고 추억하게 되얏는지? 이것이 너나내나 궁금한 문뎨이다.
아는 놈은 안다 치고 몰으는 놈은 몰은다 치고 엇잿든 나는 이 巢父 許由의 이약이를 한번하고 십다. 名利를 위하야 덤븨는 이 놈의 세상에서는 이 脫俗한 一大 奇人奇事를 한번 말하고 십다.
團成社나 光武臺에서 李東伯이나 宋萬甲의 口唱에서만 억개춤을 추든 자는 이번 이 學人의 筆唱에서는 그만 엉딍춤이 발꿈치춤까지 나올 것을 미리 짐작하고 쓴다.

동양사상의 所謂 太古라는 天皇 地皇 人皇을 지나고 所謂三皇이라는 太昊 炎帝 黃帝를 지나서 所謂 五帝라는 小昊 顓頊 帝嚳 帝堯 帝舜의 시절에 밋첫섯다.
그 중 五帝의 一이요 성군의 一이요 千古의 明主라는 堯의 세상이엿다. 史記의 말과 갓치 堯란 임금은 엇잿든 其仁이 如天이고 其知가 如神이고 其德이 如日이고 其望이 如雲이엿다. 平陽에서 都하고 천하를 治한 지 50년에 治蹟이 如何히 홀늉하얏든지 집에 문을 안닷고 街里에 도적이 업섯고 일반민중은 康衢烟月에 擊壤歌만 불느것다.
「日出而作하고 日入而食하며 鑿井而飮하고 耕田而食하니 帝力이 何有於我哉리오」
이것이 그때 백성들의 불느든 노래이다. 이만하면 堯의 인격을 알 수 잇고 堯의 治蹟을 짐작할 수 잇다.
아무리 성군인들 엇지 萬年壽야 할 수 잇스며 아무리 治蹟이 양호한들 엇지 평생 天子야 될 수 잇스랴. 하물며 그때는 아직 繼繼繩繩의 血統傳位法도 업섯고 또한 堯의 子-丹朱가 몹시 不肖한지라 천하를 讓渡할내야 그럴 수도 업섯다. 그래서 천하에 널니 천하를 맛를만한 인물을 구하는 중이엿다.

이때에 맛츰 箕山이란 땅에 許由와 巢父라는 兩大 奇人이 잇서 항상 산에 가 나물 캐여 먹으며 물에 가 고기 잡아먹으며 우주간에 悠悠自適하야 천하와 더부러 웃기도 하고 천하와 더부러 노래도 하니 世人이 稱하야 그를 至人이라 하며 성인이라 하겟다. 堯가 左右에게 箕山의 二大 奇人說을 듯고 喜不自勝하야 곳 微服으로 箕山에 나아가 맛츰 許由를 보고 삼가히 천하를 讓渡하야 가로듸
「日月은 잇서도 炬火는 쉬지 안이 하니 빗노릇하기도 과연 어럽도다. 時雨는 내려도 물은 그대로 숨여 가나니 못(澤)노릇하기도 과연 어럽다. 당신이 천하를 다시리면 찬하가 잘 다사릴터인데 나는 공연히 尸位素餐格으로 잇서 미안하노라. 請컨대 천하를 맛틈이 엇덜가?」
하얏다. 이 말을 듯든 許由는 무슨 큰 괴물요물이나 만난 것처럼 흠즛 놀내이다가 다시 한번 픽 웃고 나서 쳔쳔히 대답하되
「자네가 천하를 다시리매 천하 다시린 지 임의 오래 엿는지라. 이제 나더러 천하를 맛트라 하니 나더러 名을 圖得하라＜3＞ 함이냐. 名이란 것은 實의 賓이니라, 나더러 賓이 되라 함이냐? 鷦鷯가 深林에 깃드리지만 一枝에 불과하고 偃鼠가 河水를 먹지만 滿腹에 불과하니라. 자네나 내나 천하를 이약이 하나 다-소용업는 일이니라」
이럿케 대번에 딱 거절해 버리고 堯더러는 다시 말도 거듭하지 안코 더러운 소리를 들엇다 하야 곳 穎川水로 나아가 팔을 것어붓치고 귀를 씻고 잇섯다.
이때 맛츰 許由의 유일의 志己이든 巢父란 자가 맛츰 강변에 나아가 소를 푸러 가지고 도라 오다가 穎川水를 건너다가 소에게 물을 먹이고져 할새 맛츰 보닛가 웃목에서 許由가 귀를 씻는지라 이상히 생각하고 단바람 許由더러
「자네 그곳에서 무엇하고 잇는가?」
하고 무러 보닷다. 彼此知己로서 隱諱할 것이 업는지라, 許由는 서슴지 안코 자초지종을 이약이 하고 나서
「그래 더러운 소리를 드럿는 고로 귀를 씻노라」
하얏다. 이 말을 듯든 巢父는 단바람 소를 끌고 웃목으로 올나가며
「더러운 소리를 들은 더러운 귀를 씻츤 더러운 물은 소도 안이 먹이겠다」
하고 유유히 웃목으로 올나가고 마럿다. 이 광경을 본 許由는 한번 크게 우스며

鵝城野人

누구이나 衣冠의 차림차림이가 보통인과 다르며 행동의 되임 되임이가 보통인에 버서나면 그것을 야릇하게 보며 우숩게 녀기는 가운대에 이러한 평판 저러한 평판이 모다나다가 이상스러운 사람 奇怪한 사람하는 일음을 부치어 그 당시에 그 사회에서 알게 되고 후세까지 말거리가 되는 것이지마는 그러케 異人奇人의 이상한 행동과 奇怪한 행동의 실례를 들어서 사람답게 行事답게 말하자면 또다시 천인의 하나가 듬을고 만인의 하나가 듬을고 고금을 통틀어서 몃이 못되는 것이어늘 奇人의 奇事로 처들것이 과연 잇다할가. 아는 것만코 이상야릇한 土亭李之函先生 이약이나 대강 말하고자 한다.

土亭先生이 八吏大姓의 하나로 유명한 韓山 李氏의 후예로 태어나서 稼亭(穀) 牧隱(穡) 가튼 선조를 이고 省庵(之蕃) 가튼 형님을 두고 鵝溪(山海) 가튼 족하가 잇는 바에는 그 천연적 稟賦가 남다르고 중종 12년 丁丑에 나서 선조 11년 戊寅에 별세하는 62세의 그 일생이 漢陽朝의 人物全盛을 말하든 시대인 것에 대조하면 시대적 薰陶로 말미암은 才德이 구비할 것도 짐작할 바이나 선생의 一動一靜을 낫낫치 처들어 보면 보통인의 재능이라 총명이라 德量이라 智鑑이라 하는 것에는 하나도 근사할 것이 업고 다만 입살 홰홰 두루면서 奇怪 新通 이상스럽다고 일커를 것 뿐이다. 선생이 일즉이 양친을 여의고 物外仙標 가튼 그 형님에게 길리어 非烟火的 성격을 온전히 모방한 것도 한 원인이 되겟지마는 快濶玲瓏한 거동은 그 最早시대에 나태내엇나니 장가 가서 醀禮하든 이튼날에 어대로 나아 갓다가 저물게 돌아 오는데 입엇든 도포가 업는지라. 처가사람이 보고 깜작 놀나서 물은즉 弘濟院 다리를 지나는데서 거지아이가 벌거벗고 우는 것을 그대로 둘 수 업서서 도포를 뜨더서 그 세 아해를 싸주고 왓다하는 그 기상이 자못 선선한 것은 다른 사람으로는 듬은 일이 이것이다.＜10＞
그리다가 年紀가 將盛한 뒤에는 文識이 크게 열리어 고금을 博通함은 물론이오 醫藥 卜筮 가튼 제가의 방술을 모다 꾀뚤어 천지의 運度와 산천의 氣象까지 추측함은 그 당시에 벌서 異人이란 소리를 들엇고 年祚가 오래된 오늘날에도 사주쟁이 問卜쟁이 입에 반듯이 토정비결을 처드는 것은 陰陽術書에도 一家를 완성하얏든 것은 족히 알 수 잇는 것이다.
장가든 뒤로는 그 처가(毛山守星琅이 그 聘父)에 의탁하고 잇드니 하루는 그 형님더러 하는 말이 처가에 古氣가 업는 것을 본즉 얼마 아니잇서 大禍가 잇슬리라하고 처자를 다리고 나오더니 그 翌年에 과연 全家가 覆滅되엇스니 그 소위 吉氣가 업다는 것은 무엇으로 알어본 것인지 보통인은 알 수 업는 것이다. 그리고 師友사이에 조케 노는 것도 보통인과는 다른 것이 만엇스니 일즉이 徐花潭(敬德)先生에게가 글을 배홀 적에 국달안베옷에 집신을 끌고 冊行擔을 진 그 모양이란 수통하기 짝이 업것만은 조금도 거침이 업섯스며 曺南溟(植)을 차저 가서도 노낙끈으로 얼거뜬 갓(繩笠)을 쓴 남루한 모양으로 丹碧이 淨麗하고 几席이 청결한 그 방안에 쓱 들어가서 흔연히 손을 잡고 이理致 저理致를 논란하야 열어날 밤을 지냇고 그 길로 盧玉溪(禧)를 차저갓드니 마침 근처 사람더러 꾸어 간조(粟)의 利息을 가저 오지 안는다고 꾸짓는 것을 듯고 怫然히 일어서 간 것은 아무나 하지 못할 것이다.
