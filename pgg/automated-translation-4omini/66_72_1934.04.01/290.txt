발행일	1934년 04월 01일  
기사제목	探偵小說, 누가 죽엿느냐!! (Detective Novel: Who Died!!)  
필자	崔流帆  
기사형태	소설 (Fiction)  

The sound of a gunshot echoed through the quiet house. “Krabett” startled and raised his head, removing the pipe from his mouth and jumped up suddenly. Was it a corpse?... No, that couldn't be it. This evening was quieter than usual, so he had come to the master's study to proofread his new detective novel “The Fearful Legend.” He didn't see the master yet, but it was already past 11 o'clock.  

As Krabett opened the door to the master’s room, he thought it must be that the master “Frederick Winter-” was conducting some kind of experiment with a pistol. “Winter-” was an imaginative writer who always thought of unexpected “tricks” to surprise fans of detective novels. However, thinking that way, for some reason his heart was racing. He hurriedly went down the stairs and thought that he needed to knock on the master's study to see if everything was alright.  

When he reached the “hall,” suddenly he heard a man’s voice.  

“Hey! Hey!” a desperate scream, filled with fear, “He... he is dead!”  

At that moment, Krabett was taken aback. The voice undoubtedly belonged to “John Beeman,” the actor... and it was definitely coming from the study.  

“What on earth happened?” the man said again.  

Krabett ran to cross the hall and just as he reached the study, the door was firmly shut. He hesitated for a moment and then pushed the door open.  

“Ah!”  

“Frederick Winter-” was sprawled face down on the red velvet floor. His arms were limp, and blood was streaming from his white hair in a few lines.  

Nearby, “John Beeman” was trembling on his knees, and behind him stood the master’s wife “Eva,” motionless as if she had fainted, clutching her neck with one hand and holding a pistol with the other.  

In a flash, Krabett surveyed the scene and thought, “Is this mistress dead? This mistress-” he shouted.  

But was he really dead?  

He rushed into the room and held the master’s hand, realizing there was not even a faint pulse in his wrist or heart.  

This man—no, the entire world loved and respected “Frederick Winter-” so cruelly... And especially his wife... He thought he mustn't leave his wife alone amid all this and looked toward her to speak. But at that moment, he did not notice the wicked flame of malice in Beeman's eyes. Suddenly Beeman seized the pistol from Eva's hands and swung it with all his might, striking Krabett down.  

“Get a hold of yourself—quick!”  

A voice shouted, and Krabett opened his eyes slightly. He found himself lying in Winter’s armchair, surrounded by detectives, with Officer Tate shaking his hand. However, his head throbbed with pain, and he couldn't recall what had happened.  

Then he saw the body lying on the floor. All memories surfaced.  

He jumped up and cast a glance at Eva and Beeman, who were standing by the window.  

“It's that guy. The one who hit me—is that guy!” But as he tried to speak, his voice was choked.  

“Get a hold of yourself, Krabett!” Officer Tate said calmly.  

“We know everything. We know all about how you fell over and various other things...”  

“Can I answer now?”  

Krabett was well-acquainted with Officer Tate, not just as a police officer but as a close friend of the master. In fact, most of Winter's detective stories were based on Tate’s wealth of experience.  

“I will answer whatever you ask.”  

“Well then, let me ask: why did you do such a thing?”  

“This... What thing do you mean?”  

“Why did you kill him?”  

“Me?”  

“That's right. You need to answer clearly.”  

“But why would I- I-”  

Krabett's voice faltered.  

“Those two—the Winter lady and Beeman—”  

“No more nonsense!”  

Officer Tate shouted, cutting him off more loudly than usual.  

“I want to know the reason. I’m not asking for an alibi.”  

The lady has already told me everything, so do you understand?”  

“But I couldn’t—”  

Krabett shook his head adamantly.  

“I don’t know what those two have told you, but the truth is—I heard a gunshot while reading a manuscript on the third floor and came down here, only to see the lady and Beeman standing over the body.”  

“Lies!” Beeman shouted, interrupting him loudly.  

“Officer! Don’t let this man spin these absurd tales!”  

“Wait a minute. I will take care of this…” Tate said while looking at Krabett.  

“You have two witnesses contradicting you, and you still want to lie? What use is that? Speak honestly.”  

“Lies? I’m not lying. But what did those two say to you?”  

Tate turned to the lady, who was looking like a statue in her blue outfit, and asked calmly, “Could you repeat what you just said, Mrs. Winter-?”  

“I think it will clarify what position Mr. Krabett is in.”  

The lady opened her mouth quietly.  

“I was talking with Mr. Beeman in the library. My husband has a habit of writing at night, and he was writing in his study. The gunshot was heard after 11:10. Mr. Beeman and I quickly crossed the hall to the study, and when we opened the door, there was Krabett on top of my—my husband.”  

Her voice was calm, though it trembled slightly. No one could help but admire her composed attitude.  

“But then Krabett, as soon as he saw us, tried to flee, so Mr. Beeman jumped in and there was a struggle between the two. Mr. Beeman managed to seize the pistol, and while Krabett was grappling with him…” she pointed to the severely damaged table lamp on the floor, “Mr. Beeman struck him with the pistol. That’s all. I called the police at that time.”  

“Thank you, Mrs. Winter-.”  

“Officer Tate” then looked sternly at Krabett again.  

“Officer!” Krabett shouted, pale.  

“Those two have seamlessly crafted a story against me.”  

“Is there any proof?”  

“Yes! Proof is…”  

“If there’s no proof, then it’s not useful to consider it. Since the two of them already…”  

“The thing is…” However, Krabett broke off.  

He realized it was senseless at that moment to discuss whether his innocence was clear without any definitive evidence to unmask the deceit of “those two.” He needed to discover some conclusive evidence that would undermine their statements, or he would be arrested as the murderer of Frederick Winter-. So how could he find evidence—  

In despair, he considered, “If someone else witnessed me rushing down…”  

However, it was a wishful thought. The housemaids had gone to sleep in their rooms on the fourth floor, and even if they had heard it, they wouldn’t have come down in time when the commotion had happened in the study.  

Krabett’s pale face turned crimson as he thought about it despairingly.  

“Officer, if you don’t mind, I have one thing to tell you…”  

Officer Tate sharply looked at him before nodding lightly and then ordered the officers,  

“Hey, ‘Puls,’ take this man downstairs. Watch him closely until I return.”  

Krabett rose from the armchair, feeling dizzy. The officer grabbed his arm and led him away. As they reached the door, he stumbled against the threshold, nearly falling over, but he gathered himself and staggered away. He had gone halfway down the hall when he suddenly stopped.  

“Wait—just wait a moment. I have a thought. I want to talk to the officer again. Just two or three minutes…”  

He hurried back to the entrance and called out urgently, “Officer!”  

Officer Tate turned around, having spoken to the wife.  

“What is it?” His voice was devoid of kindness.  

“No, I have to mention something crucial that I must let you know. Earlier, the lady and Mr. Beeman claimed that they rushed here the moment they heard the gunshot. Did the door to the study at that moment open or close?”  

Tate listened and glanced over to Beeman, who squinted his eyes before responding, “It was closed.”  

“Ah—closed.”  

Krabett’s eyes sparkled with hope.  

“Then the handle of this door will determine our verdict.”  

“What do you mean? What about the handle?”  

Ignoring Beeman’s words, Krabett focused on Tate,  

“Yes, officer! If the lady and Beeman indeed entered later to open this door after I was here, then the surface of the handle should have their fingerprints distinctly over mine. Conversely, it means if I opened it later than the two, my fingerprints would overlap theirs.”  

Officer Tate grimly regarded Krabett’s pale face before looking at the lady and Beeman again, analyzing their expressions.  

“It indeed is worth checking.” After a moment of silence, he finally agreed calmly, “Let’s go check the fingerprints.”  

In the library, while being watched by Officer Puls for countless minutes, Krabett suffered greatly. He sat silently, but his head became clearer, and various thoughts began to unfold one after the other, which made him anxious and tense. In fact, he was fighting for his life. Whether he would be executed or not hung in the balance.  

Eventually, Officer Tate returned. From Krabett's appearance alone, it was evident that some significant development had occurred. Tate approached him and said,  

“Krabett, you are cleared. It turns out your fingerprints were the most recent impression on the handle.”  

Krabett felt a rush of relief, though he tried to suppress it.  

“However, that doesn’t mean you’re completely off the hook.” Tate continued. “This fingerprint holds great significance in my eyes, but to jurors, it might not hold much weight. The prosecutor could skillfully dismantle that claim, saying, ‘Those two were so excited that they did not notice whether the door was fully closed or partially open when they grabbed the handle indiscriminately.’ If he says that...”  

“There’s more.”  

“But at that moment—” Suddenly his words cut off, and he looked closely at Krabett.  

“Do you know how long you have known Winter-?”  

“Six years.”  

“No, five years.”  

“Is that so? I know Winter- regarded you as a fine assistant, and I know you have been very loyal to him. So, I couldn’t understand why you would kill him. But now, from this point on, you are on our side. Let me help you. Your fingerprints have altered my perspective.”  

“…”  

“However, what is difficult about your statement is the lady's motive for murder. I cannot understand why she shot her husband.”  

“I can answer that.”  

“Really?”  

“I can provide insight, but right now, this is a matter of whether I go to the gallows or not.”  

“Yeah! So?”  

“Actually, the lady and Beeman deceived her husband…” Krabett began detailing how the lady was 30 years younger than her husband, had married for wealth, and how there was no affection in their marriage. It was during that time that Beeman had appeared, and the two fell in love. The lady had demanded a divorce from her husband, but he had refused—and developed a plan for a final meeting in the study where the argument escalated, resulting in all three losing their tempers. The stubborn and stubborn Winter- attempted to strike Beeman with the desk lamp. The lady knew that his pistol was always inside a desk drawer, and in her excitement, she pulled out the pistol…  

“But that’s totally hypothetical.” Officer Tate interrupted, raising his voice.  

“The only way to find the real perpetrator is through confession.”  

“Certainly. However, Officer,” Krabett leaned forward and grasped the officer's hand tightly.  

“I found a way to prove my innocence!”  

“This isn’t something I conceived; it belongs to Winter- himself from his last detective novel. The story features a man who suffers a stroke and can’t speak, yet witnesses a murder. However, he is unable to inform the police—though that’s not the most crucial part. It was modeled on Ronald Henderson. As you know, he has been bedridden due to a stroke.”  

“The bed is directly across from Winter’s study’s window. If he had been lying in bed at the moment of the murder, he would’ve witnessed everything, right?”  

“But he said he was asleep then.” Tate gave a skeptical response.  

“I already investigated that.”  

“Yes, I know that. But while we have the key to confirming it, can you please utilize my reasoning?”  

And then Krabett whispered something into Officer Tate’s ear.  

It was after midnight.  

The surroundings were silent as “Eva Winter-” and “Beeman” remained in the library, responding to Officer Tate’s tedious interrogation. Krabett, his face pale, awaited the forthcoming events—whether they would save his life.  

Suddenly, Officer Puls burst in, breathless.  

“Officer!”  

Eyes wide, he exclaimed.  

“What’s wrong?” Officer Tate replied impatiently.  

“A visitor has arrived! Henderson. He came urgently to see you. He has been bedridden due to a stroke and says his bed is facing the window of this study. He claims he witnessed the murder—specifically, Mrs. Winter- shooting her husband.”  

As he spoke, Mrs. Winter staggered to stand, her face blanching as she pressed her hand to her mouth, suppressing the sounds that threatened to escape.  

“Henderson saw that?” Officer Tate's frustrated voice shattered the silence.  

“Yes! He is ready to meet and will tell everything he witnessed.”  

“Hmm! No one is to leave this room until I return!”  

He ordered a policeman to stay and hurried away.  

A terrible silence filled the room.  

About ten minutes passed before Officer Tate returned, sharply calling out, “Mrs. Winter-! You deceived us!”  

The lady could not reply. Neither could Beeman. Both made faces of dismay.  

“With a witness to the crime, we must revise our earlier thoughts.”  

The lady shook her head in despair. Her hair became disheveled, and the sob that hung within her throat couldn’t escape.  

“How could this—what can I do?”  

“Eva!”  

Beeman shook her arm, gesturing urgently for calm, “No! I told you it wouldn’t do you any good.”  

Her voice trembled with despair. Then, with determination, she pointed at Officer Tate, “I wasn’t aiming for Fred! But he was about to throw the lamp at me, so I pulled out the pistol at that moment…”  

Krabett's eyes shone brightly.  

An hour passed, and only Krabett and Officer Tate remained in the study.  

“Officer! I don’t know how to express my gratitude.”  

“Not at all! This is all thanks to you. If you hadn’t noticed the handle’s fingerprints, I might have made a grave mistake.”  

“However…” Tate began.  

“Wait a moment. I must tell you something regarding that fingerprint.”  

“What? A trick?”  

“It was indeed a trick. Actually, when I heard the gunshot and started to rush in, the door was only slightly ajar. So I didn’t hold onto the handle but opened it.”  

“Then how—”  

“Wait just a moment. During the statements made by the lady and Beeman, I realized there was no way to prove my innocence. So I had to create evidence. You also remember, as I exited the study, I nearly tripped over the threshold. At that moment, my hand brushed against the handle, and my body obscured it from their sight.”  

“What do you think? The reason my fingerprint was found last on the handle is exactly this.”  

“Hmmm—so that’s how it was. But how did you come up with such a surprising trick?”  

“After five years as an assistant to a detective writer, I think it’s fair to have a few tricks up my sleeve.”

총소리가 고요한 집안에 울렷다. 『크라벳트』는 깜짝 놀래서 머리를 들고 파이푸를 입에서 빼고는 벌떡 일어섯다.
변사라도?... 아니 그럴리가 업다. 오늘 저녁은 다른날보다도 조용하야 그래 자긔는 아까부터 삼칭 자긔방에 와서 주인의 신작 탐정소설 『피무든 게명』의 교정을 보든 참이다. 시게는 안보앗지만 벌서 11시가 넘엇슬 것입니다.
『크라벳트』는 자긔 방문을 열면서 이것은 필시 주인 『푸래데릭 윈터-』가 피스톨로 어떠한 실험을 하는데 틀림이 업슬 것이다하고 생각하엿다. 『윈터-』는 독창적인 작가로 언제나 긔상천외식의 『트릭크』를 생각해 가지고는 탐정소설 팬들을 깜작 놀래키든 것이엿다.
그러나 그러케 생각하면서도 웬일인지 그의 심장은 두근두근하얏다. 그는 발빠르게 층층대를 나려가면서 우선 주인의 서재를 두드려 보아서 무사한가 어떤가를 아라보지 안으면 안된다고 생각하엿다.
그가 『홀』까지 왓슬때 뜻밧게 어느 남자의 목소리가 들렷다.
『에봐! 에봐!』하고 공포에서 나오는 필사의 소리로 『이이가... 이이가 죽엇구료!』
그때 『크라벳트』는 깜짝 놀래서 주츰햇다.
그 목소리의 주인 그것은 분명코 『쫀 삐-만』이다. 배우인.... 그리고 틀림업시 서재에서 들려오는 것이엿다.
『어쩌자고 이런 짓을-』 또 그 사나이의 소리.
『크라벳트』는 뛰여서 홀을 건너가 서재의 압흐로 오자 그 문은 꼭 닷처잇지를 안앗섯다. 잠간 주저하다가 문을 밀고 들어갓다.
『아-ㅅ!』
『푸레데릭 윈터-』는 붉은 보료바닥에가 얼골을 땅바닥에다 대고 쓸어저잇다. 두팔을 버리고 허-연 머리에는 샛밝안 피가 두서너줄 흘르고 잇다.
그엽헤 『쫀 삐만』이 무릅을 꿀코 몸을 부들부들 떨면서 안졋고 그뒤에는 주인의 안해 『에봐』가 실신한 것처럼 우드먼히 서잇다. 한손으로는 목을 꽉집고 또 한손은 피스톨을 든채 축느러트리고-
이상의 광경을 한눈으로 바라본 『크라벳트』는 심중으로
『이 예편네가 죽엿다? 이 예편네가-』하고 소리쳣다.
그러나 정말로 죽엿슬가?
그는 방으로 뛰여들어가서 『쫀 삐-만』의 엽헤가 안저서 주인의 손을 잡아보앗다. 손목에도 심장에도 조고만 고동조차 업섯다.
이이가- 아니 온 세게의 사람들이 그러케 사랑하고 존경하든 『푸레데릭 윈터-』를 이러케도 잔인하게- 더구나 그의 안해가-. 그는 부인을 남을하지 안으면 안되겟다고 그 편을 바라다보며 말하엿다. 그러나 그때 『쫀 삐-만』의 눈가운데 아지못할 잔악한 불꼿이 타는 것을 아지 못하엿다. 별안간 『삐-만』은 『에봐』의 손에서 피스톨을 빼서가지고 기운껏 『크라벳트』를 내리첫다. 『크라벳트』는 그만 정신을 일코 쓸어저 버렷다.



『정신차려- 어여!』
누군지 소리를 질르는 바람에 『크라벳트』는 실눈을 떳다. 그는 『윈터-』의 안락의자에 눕혀잇고 그의 주위에는 경관들이 둘러잇고 『테이트』경부가 자긔손을 붓잡고 흔들고 잇는 것을 알앗다. 그러나 머리가 직근직근 압하서 무슨 일이 일어낫는지 조곰도 생각이 나질 안엇다.
그러자 마루우에 넘어저잇는 사체가 보이엿다. 그와 함께 모-든 긔억이 살아일어낫다.
그는 벌떡 일어나서 마침 마진짝 들창 압헤 섯는 『에봐』와 『삐-만』에게 눈을 흘기엿다.
『저놈이다. 날 때린 놈은- 저놈이』 그러나 말이 더 나가지를 안엇다. 목소리가 탁탁 막히기만 한다.
『정신을 차려 크라벳트!』 테이트경부는 점잔은 목소리로
『우리들은 모-든 것을 다 알고 잇단말이야. 자네가 으더맛고 넘어진 것도 그리고 더 다른 것도...<58> 그래 좀 질문을 하랴고 하는데 대답을 할 수가 잇나?』
『크라벳트』는 『테이트』경부를 잘 아는 터이엿다. 단순히 경찰관으로서가 아니라 주인 『윈터-』의 친우로서-. 사실 『윈터-』의 탐정소설의 대부분은 『테이트』경부의 풍부한 경험을 재료로 한 것이엿다.
『대답하고 말고요.』
그는 기운을 주어가지고
『무슨 말슴이든지 대답하겟습니다.』
『그럼 물어보는 말이니 어째서 이런 일을 하엿단 말인가.』
『이... 이런 일이라니?』
『어째서 그를 죽엿느냐 말이야?』
『내가?』
『그러타. 똑똑이 대답을 해야해.』
『그러나 어째서 내가- 내가-』
『크라벳트』의 목소리는 맥히엿다.
『저 두사람이- 『윈터-』부인하고 『삐-만』이-』
『딴소리는 그만두고!』
경부는 평소와는 큰소리로 그의 말을 가루막엇다.
『나는 리유를 뭇는 것이야. 아리빠이(現在不在證明)를 뭇는 것이 아니란말야. 부인이 자초지종을 모다 이야기하섯스니까 알엇나?』
『그러나 내가 그럴리가- 업습니다.』
『크라벳트』는 머리를 흔들면서 부정하엿다.
『두사람이 무슨 말을 하엿는지는 모르지만 사실은- 내가 3층에서 원고를 읽을 때 총소리가 들려서 여기까지 나려와서 부인하고 삐-만이 사체엽헤 섯는 것을 보앗습니다.』
『그짓말!』 삐-만이 소리를 꽉 질으면서 말을 가루막엇다.
『경부! 이사람이 이런 헤튼 수작을 하는걸 그냥 두십니까?』
『잠간만 기다리십쇼. 조토록 처치하겟스니요...』 하면서 『크라벳트』를 바라다보고
『자네는 증인이 두사람이나 모다 자네와 반대의 진술을 하는데 그래도 거짓말을 하려고 하나? 그게 무슨 소용잇나. 어서 바른대로 말을 하게?』
『거짓말요? 나는 거짓말을 하지 안습니다. 대관절 저 두사람은 무어라고 진술을 하엿습니까? 당신한테-』
