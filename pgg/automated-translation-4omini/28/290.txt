The Effect of Psychological Treatment on Mental Patients  
                                                            Lee Yong-sik  

1. Benefits of Psychological Development  
The trends in life, which tend to flow in a passive direction, might lead those with a somewhat pessimistic worldview to regard modern culture as a target of curses. However, particularly in modern times, the increase in mental illness cases is evidenced by statistics from various fields. This so-called cultural disease is entirely due to intense stimuli from a complex environment, indicating that it is unreasonable to ignore the harmful effects of modern culture. Nevertheless, the treatment methods for these mental illnesses are also products of modern culture; thus, I believe it is necessary to point out the shortcomings of modern culture.  

It goes without saying that all branches of science have made substantial contributions to the production of modern culture. Among these, the ability to heal incurable diseases that cannot be treated by medicines through psychological treatment allows patients to overcome the pain of illness and experience the joy of life, benefiting from the significant advances in psychology, which is an indirect reward from the development of psychological therapy.  

2. The Actual Value of Medicine  
Superstitions permeate every aspect of human life, but they are particularly severe concerning medical treatment. Although it is generally accepted that activities such as the shaman's ritual, the blind reading of sutras, and praying to mountains and rivers are superstitions, it is not enough just to recognize these. In fact, even in the field of medicine, which is based on solid scientific foundations, superstitions are quite prevalent. People believe they must consult a doctor when they fall ill; doctors, on the other hand, think that they cannot treat illnesses without their powers, creating a kind of superstition around the idea of "illness equals doctor." However, this does not imply that the entirety of medicine is superstitious. There are indeed illnesses that can be treated without the help of medicine, and conversely, there are instances where diseases are untreatable solely with medicinal means.  

While drug treatments have effects for physical illnesses requiring surgery and internal or external injuries, in the realm of mental illnesses, the effectiveness of medicines is nonexistent. Furthermore, for organic diseases arising from the malfunctions of bodily organs, treatment without medicine is impossible. However, functional diseases caused by loss of activity while the bodily organ remains in a normal state cannot be addressed by medication either.  

Regardless of the type of illness, there is a widespread belief that medical examination and medication can successfully treat illnesses, but attempting to seek medication efficacy in psychological diseases is a grave misconception. In fact, it is said that 70% of the causes of illness arise psychologically, and consequently, 70% of treatment effectiveness revolves around psychological actions. Miura Moriji stated, "Healing is governed solely by the will of Heaven, and we, the doctors, are merely helpers at the periphery." Moreover, Professor Morita Seishin declared, "Effective medicines for treating illnesses include only three types: hydrochloric acid, anthine, and salicylic acid." However, even these medicines, while they may treat primary symptoms, often come with side effects, thus making their effects incomplete. Other medications for digestive issues, like gastric powder or baking soda, or various sedatives for excitement have proven effective, but three consecutive uses may lead to habit-forming behavior and sudden overdoses, which indicates a duality of benefits and harms.  

The restoration of health from illness primarily takes place through the body’s natural healing powers. Likewise, the effectiveness of medicines primarily derives from the patient's belief in the doctor's reputation, equipment availability, and changes in the environment, effectively deriving strength from suggestion. Various mental illnesses such as epilepsy, hysteria, hypochondria, and problems like wandering, theft, bed-wetting, or teeth-grinding are conditions that the medical field cannot treat at all. However, collaborations with dullards like myself have indeed led to successful psychological treatments of these difficult illnesses, as well as mental and cerebral diseases, and even digestive problems, leading to significant recovery at great cost and over an extended period for those who had seen no improvement with conventional medicine.  

To exemplify my own experiences somewhat hesitantly, I recall an incident a few years ago on a night during the Chuseok festival in Dong-cheon-dong. A 22-year-old female student attending a literacy course at a particular school came in with a deeply frowning face, obviously displaying a very unpleasant mood, and said:  
"Teacher, I came to see you."  
I responded, "Yes! Who are you and what is the matter that you wish to see me?"  
The student replied, "I am Kim OO, residing at OO street in Dong-cheon-dong, and I came to have a look at my illness."  
"Yes! You want me to take a look at your illness? Do I look like someone who can diagnose illness? But what is troubling you exactly?" I questioned, noting that her health seemed not too poor.  
She said, "Oh no! My heart is pounding like a hammer, my head is aching, cold water feels like it's being poured down my spine, and I can't move even one finger; I have no energy."  
"Then, why don’t you go to a good doctor for a consultation and get some medicine?"  
"I have been taking medicine from OO hospital for four days, but there's been no improvement, so I came to see you."  
"When did you start feeling this way?"  
"It started after I came home from school with my aunts five days ago, and crossing the Middle School Bridge, a man suddenly rushed out of the dark and scared my aunts. At that moment, I was so shocked that I've been like this ever since."  
"Oh, is that so? But tell me what kind of a man he was; have you seen him in person?" (I somewhat guessed the man was a student.)  
The student replied, "He was a male student."  
"Then, since that moment must have scared you so much, you assumed that such a scare was due to your weakness. Did you think that undernormal circumstances anyone would feel as alarmed as you did?”  
"I think anyone would be scared in that situation."  
"Of course, that would be your understanding. Since the person who scared you was a male student, you have conceived the idea that males behave impudently towards females. But in this incident, while that boy might not have acted impudently, you assumed he would, which triggered a strong latent fear in your subconscious. This fear has caused you to suffer all this time. That feeling hanging over like a shadow while your aunts were shocked is still reigning in your memory."

Do you find it strange?  
The student replied, "Now I understand." (Her face lit up with color and began to smile.)  
"How is your heart now? Is it beating heavily like before?"  
"No, nothing at all. I feel fine!" (I placed my hand on the patient's chest to check the heartbeat and confirmed it had returned to normal, and the unpleasant feeling on her face had entirely disappeared.)  
"You're entirely well now."  
"Is it alright if I never come back again?"  
"If you are well, then you have no reason to come to see me."  

Just like that, she completely recovered. This is an example of treatment through psychological therapy, specifically via psychoanalysis. To give another instance from my mentor, Professor Morita Seishin, there was a 29-year-old woman who, eight months pregnant, was unable to move due to severe pain in her left sciatic nerve; having entered a hospital for a week without any signs of improvement, she sought Morita's attention. When he made a house call, he found her lying on her right side, unable to move, not even permitting the nurses to come near her. Through questioning, it became clear that the woman had suffered a miscarriage during her previous pregnancy eight months prior, complicating her pain this time. By discovering the cause of the illness, he could discern the method of treatment. He then instructed her to show him her pain in order for him to treat it. Upon complying and turning toward where it hurt, she suddenly realized that there was no pain at all. He directed her to get out of bed, and even when she did so, she still felt nothing. When he asked her to walk around the ward, she insisted her pain was non-existent. Thus, she was discharged.  

These two cases illustrate that in the first case, "the man who scared her was a male student; her intense fear has led her to endure this burden, believing no one else could be as scared," and in the second case, "the pain she thought she felt disappeared after she followed directions." Such extraordinary recoveries may seem absurd to those not witnessing them firsthand. It is unreasonable to reject the claim that the inability to find improvement after a week of medication could vanish with a single sentence, and such miraculous effects naturally provoke skepticism among common people. Frankly speaking, my fear of this psychological treatment stems from the charlatans who exploit this phenomenon for monetary gain, leading me to refrain from praising its powers lest I degrade my credibility through such associations.  

Recently, many have denied the miraculous acts of Jesus as unscientific. However, I maintain that it is impossible to dismiss them in their entirety. While I cannot accept claims regarding events like the parting of the Red Sea or the walking of the Israelites through it as credible, I do believe that miracles related to healing can be substantiated scientifically through psychological treatment.  

3. Diseases and Psychological Actions  

When one imagines a sour taste, saliva is secreted without one’s awareness, and when one thinks of bitter substances, one often grimaces. This is a common experience for many. Additionally, there are instances where one eats something they normally cannot without noticing, or conversely, someone may enjoy a meal at the table yet suddenly reject it upon discovering an insect in their dish. In such cases, while the insect and the meal itself have no relation, the unpleasantness triggered by the sight of the bug can cause significant agitation in the stomach, leading to vomiting. 

For instance, a 35-year-old Japanese man suffered from nervous diarrhea for over six months. Every time he ate, he experienced diarrhea and could not even tolerate eating ordinary solid rice. If he was served a thin porridge, he would still get diarrhea, as he could only consume it very watered down. He sought treatment from Nakamuura Koyasu of the Japanese Medical Ichthyology Society. After a series of inquiries, Nakamuura ascertained that the patient had a particular fondness for tempura, and after delaying the treatment for a while, he presented tempura to him, which the patient joyfully scarfed down without issues. Following his meal, Nakamuura asked the patient how he felt after this meal, at which point the patient replied that he felt fine. He was then told to go home, concluding that he was all better, and correspondingly, from that point forward he could eat solid rice without experiencing diarrhea.  

This is a reliable case from my own experience as well. One of my acquaintances who was 29 had struggled with roundworms during his childhood but recently had suffered from stomach ailments for seven continuous years. He experienced constant pain and diarrhea, hunched forward like a shrimp, and had become almost lifeless, knocking on death’s door before coming to me for treatment. I employed a technique known as "Dandelion Therapy" once and then intensive psychological treatment through persuasion. Remarkably, the next day was his son’s first birthday, so the family had made a large quantity of sweet rice cakes. Despite being concerned about his condition, he insisted, "I wish to try them," to which his family argued, "You said you could not eat rice and always had pain!" But he still insisted, noting he was not worried, eventually eating a bowlful. His family felt anxious, fretting over possible consequences, but to everyone's surprise, he suffered no adverse reactions and continued to enjoy food thereafter. As a result, his chronic gastrointestinal condition remarkably healed completely.  

Every action taken by a person reflects a psychological operation. All movements arise from psychological actions stimulating muscles and skeletal structures; hence, the body functions as the servant of the mind. Therefore, the severity of the illness depends on the psychological state, and the efficacy of medicine can be judged accordingly. Even regarding the effectiveness of medicinal treatments, patients residing far from the sources of medicine may experience significantly greater healing benefits than those residing nearby. This clearly demonstrates the role of psychological actions at play. Even if a powerful medicine is ingested, without belief in its effectiveness, no matter the expense incurred, the treatment may lead to great disappointment. Furthermore, whether with food, if there exists a strong expectation that one will digest a poor diet well, digestion will likely proceed smoothly. Yet in contrast, if a person exists with continual concern over digestive troubles while eating white rice, they are highly likely to end up with digestive issues themselves—this is commonly witnessed in summer. When the weather becomes hot, even if one feels a slight discomfort after a meal, the individual will begin to worry, thinking, "Oh no, I might have caught a heat stroke," consequently leading to discomfort and resulting gas. 

However, if they can momentarily divert their thoughts toward amiable endeavors, the discomfort may be ignored and passed without pain. Therefore, when the patient's fear of illness is predominant, illness asserts control over them, but if they could distance themselves from the disease, they may indeed regain their health. This is akin to a rabid dog charging at a terrified person; should that person run away timidly, the dog finds confidence and will chase them down. However, should they embrace courage and confront the dog with a stick, the dog will retreat. 

In summary, it's a primary requirement for treatment to diminish the patient's fear of illness and stabilize their mind. Yet, too often, doctors exacerbate the situation by appearing overly worried with the patient's slightest symptom, even to the point of leaning in with their stethoscopes, creating a sense of urgency that may frighten the patient further. This is both a disappointment and a regrettable occurrence.

**Summary**  

The article discusses the importance and effectiveness of psychological treatment for mental illness, highlighting the influence of psychological factors on the onset and healing of diseases. Lee Yong-sik presents case studies that illustrate how psychological interventions can lead to remarkable recoveries where traditional medicine has failed. The assertion that a significant proportion of health issues have psychological roots challenges the contemporary medical practice of the time, particularly under colonial rule in Korea, where Western medicine was predominantly practiced. Historians may find interest in how these discussions reflect the evolving understanding of mental health in a colonial context and the struggle between traditional and modern medical practices.
