Postcard Communication

A Unique Restaurant  
When I went into Pyŏngyang-ryŏ (平壤樓) in the back alleys of Jongno, Seoul to buy a bowl of cold noodles, I noticed a large blank advertisement above the western painting framed on the wall.  

The advertisement read:  
“The amount spent by Koreans on alcohol is 58,820,062 won, and on cigarettes, it is 32.14 million won. Without 20,000 won, we cannot establish a private university.”  

Entering while intending to drink, the owner of the restaurant had hung an advertisement incompatible with the desire for profit directly overhead the heads of customers. I lowered my head with gratitude to the owner and felt a surge of emotion, realizing that Koreans, regardless of social class, are gradually awakening to the important realities we must recognize as Koreans. Oh, those virtuous names among the so-called ‘enlightened men’ who only care about their reputations—aren’t you ashamed? (From Jongno, a moved citizen)  

The Han River has Dried Up!  
Recently, the heat has become intense; if you go outside, the air feels thick and suffocating. What makes it worse than the heat is the unbearable humidity that makes it impossible to keep your eyes open. The municipal office watering the streets might be aimed at alleviating this discomfort, but whether the Han River is drying up or if there’s a shortage of sprinklers, the water is rolling off the streets and causing inconvenience. 

Those in the two- and three-story buildings, armed with electric fans and bowls of shaved ice, may be fine, but people of our walking class cannot even afford 5 jeon, making it impossible to ride the tram or the bus. Oh, if General Iŏng (영감), the director of the municipal engineering division, goes to heaven when he dies, please water the streets well! (Walking citizens of Kyŏngsŏng)  

A Series of Scandalous Events  
As you all know, throughout Korea, there are agricultural associations in each county, and while they might seem beneficial like fertilizer incentives and livestock awards, does it really make sense to mandate the forced sale of saplings as a primary business? Furthermore, completely replacing the traditional wooden pig pens with the latest hole-type manufacturing has caused pigs to die; is this really acceptable? We should just stop promoting such improvements and encouragement... (Mŏngsan, Bang Ŭng-gye)  

Your Sense of Shame is Excessive  
Ladies! Your sense of shame is excessive. When I say this, it might sound like a harsh criticism, but what’s happening in Gyeongseong with the so-called ‘women of virtue’ crying for freedom and liberation? Why can’t you ladies initiate the Women's Sports Promotion Association by yourselves, instead of having men take the initiative and pushing aside your presence? Isn't it shameful? The Women's Sports Promotion Association, if it is not merely a social organization, does not need to list the names of those so-called ‘virtuous gentlemen’ and end up with a man as the president and only a woman as the vice-president. What kind of situation is that? If I were a woman, I would feel outraged! (From Gongpyeong-dong, a male citizen)  

**Summary**  

The article discusses various social issues in Korea during Japanese colonial rule, highlighting a unique restaurant's contrasting advertisement that critiques Korean spending habits while noting their detrimental effects on education funding. It reflects on the heat and discomfort in the streets of Seoul, implies a disconnect between the elite and the common people, and criticizes the agricultural policies that force farmers into unsustainable practices. The sentiments expressed point towards growing national consciousness among Koreans, particularly through the lens of economic struggles and social expectations, shedding light on how colonial experiences shaped societal attitudes in early 20th-century Korea.

Historians might find interest in the discourse surrounding public advertisements that provoke social awareness, as well as the critiques of local governance and agricultural practices, for they reflect the complexities of identity and resistance during a period of repression.
