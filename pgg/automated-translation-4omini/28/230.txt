Modern Fortune Room

For whom is it adorned?

On a certain afternoon, before the summer heat of Almajin had begun to make sweat beads roll down, I was passing by a tobacco factory in Uiju. It was the time when the factory had just closed, and both male and female workers rushed out of the gates. Among them, the female workers were notably more numerous.

Having nothing else to do besides eating and going to the bathroom, we, the idle observers, gathered here and there, as if something noteworthy had appeared. They were mostly lovely young girls around the age of fifteen or sixteen, and some younger women were also there.

“Wow! This is truly splendid!”  
“Yeah! It's been quite a while since then!”  
“She’s a beauty, indeed! I could fall for her!”  

Such unrestrained praises spilled from the mouths of those watching. I too halted my steps and joined in the observation. Indeed! Who could claim they were first-class workers earning thirty to forty jeon? They were not much different from the upper-class girl students emerging from the school. The long hair braided and adorned with ribbons, elegantly high yellow shoes, artificial stockings, well-pressed kimonos, and the fragrance wafting from the neatly curled hair — what lovely sights… 

Ah, could these beautifully dressed girls be the laborers grappling with the dreadful labor-capital relationship? Are they indeed the children of the proletariat, whose lives depend on three meals a day? In truth, I sighed three times and lamented four times. Why bother going into details?

Factories must be the brothels of capitalists. It cannot be that way. That would be unthinkable. But then why... why do they indulge in such excessive luxuries and adornments? Alone, the price of their shoes would consume an entire month's wage. Are there not mouths waiting in their shabby homes, begging for their compassion? … And yet, I cannot pry into their actions. Perhaps they are still too young to realize. Perhaps they are simply distracted. While there is an old saying that a woman adorns herself for the satisfaction of others and a scholar dies for the recognition of others… have the luxurious trends devoid of substance seeped into the hearts of Joseon people? How lamentable!

Chick Poisoning Incident

Chick poisoning does exist. However, who could be such a demon as to catch a cute chick that flutters about, clucking and clumsily walking in the spring garden? If there are those who catch and eat them as meat, they are pitifully cruel humans. What a villainous eagle that, before showing mercy, would mercilessly strike with talons! 

Yet, just last month in Incheon, there was a horrific incident of 56 chicks being poisoned. The perpetrator, skilled with knives and adept at disemboweling on his own (shinjulguh), was a skilled Japanese man, possibly from an area nearby called Jeongji-hyo-seong (井智孝誠). This man is said to have fed poison to 56 chicks from the house of someone living in Wonsan (遠山) when they would occasionally invade his home. Truly, what a story to tell! While the mother hen could not speak to lodge a complaint, this villain is now under severe interrogation. This incident reflects the unspeakable tragedy of the past spring.

The Barbarism of White People

It goes without saying that contemporary trends absorb various rudimentary barbarisms, yet even the nature of so-called civilized people appears to be becoming increasingly barbaric. Should there be a white civilized person to contest this statement, let them come forth. A recent example proves this point. 

In the previous month, in Sherman City, Texas, a most heinous lynching incident occurred. The victim was a 41-year-old black man named Josie Hughes (쪼-지.휴-즈), accused of assaulting a white woman, faced severe scrutiny in the county courthouse. When an angry mob demanded his extradition, and it was refused, the violent crowd surged forth, overwhelming hundreds of soldiers, policemen, county officers, and firefighters, and set fire to the courthouse.

Thus, the poor black man died a tragic death by fire in the basement. Yet, the crowd of white people, having forsaken humanity under a curse of Satan, found no satisfaction with that alone. They detonated dynamite to collapse the courthouse, and after pulling the burned body out, the sight spurred brutal applause from the white women.

They then tied the corpse to the back of a car and paraded it through the streets. Eventually, they hung the body on a wooden post outside a barber shop and ignited it again, also setting fire to the victim's home. Interspersed among this carnage, the police seemed to do little in the way of restraint, even directing traffic instead of intervening—this mindset is equally unfathomable. Thus, by around two o'clock in the morning, the mob dispersed, and soldiers guarding the area opened fire, injuring six of the rioters.

The lynching of black people by white Americans is by no means a new occurrence. However, such a cruel act has never been so barbaric as this. Oh, vain and blood-thirsty white descendants, consider the tears your grandmother, Mrs. Stowe, would shed. When the time comes for the black man to reclaim his rights, there’ll be no point in regretting it. In your words, it will be too late! A cycle of barbarism is surely turning between the black and white. The unfortunate body of Hu-ju, burnt twice, has thrown a mighty bomb into the hearts of black brethren. Can we, who are so far removed, expect that mere tears of sympathy will suffice to fill our eyes?

What is this strange transformation?

In May in Shinheung, snow fluttered down, and water froze. What a strange phenomenon this is. Isn’t this only the beginning? Recently, I read in the newspapers of children killing their fathers and husbands killing their wives, breaking down even the bonds of heaven and earth. It seems that as time approaches a period of calamity, some strange signs must appear. In this regard, the tale of a hen transforming into a rooster at the end of the Han Dynasty may not indeed be out of the ordinary.

Mengchangjun’s Banquet Guests?

In Korea, the plight of the people is bankruptcy and vagrancy. The reason for this is too vast for me to elaborate now; the reality is starkly evident. In Ganggyeong, the wealthiest man Kim Cheol-su (金哲洙) has dozens of beggars gathered by his front door. Among them are obviously cripples, ragged individuals unable to cover their flesh, but even among the destitute wearing tattered clothes, the honorable guests of the past, like those of Mengchangjun, still await their meals. What does this indicate? Are we not the people cornered at the end of a hundred-foot pole? It is better to remain silent.

**Summary**

The article "Modern Fortune Room" discusses the dissonance between appearances and reality among young female workers in the tobacco industry, exploring themes such as class consciousness and the disconnect between impoverished laborers and their ornate styles. Furthermore, it addresses broader societal issues such as the disturbingly violent lynching of black Americans and emerging family disruptions in Korea, suggesting a general moral decay and barbarism that permeates both societies. Historians may find interest in how the author connects these local and global issues, illustrating the striking contrasts and moral crises within Korean colonial society.
