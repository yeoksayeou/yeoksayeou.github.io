기사제목	(創作) 歸鄕(下) (Fiction: Return Home - Part 2)  
필자	兪鎭午 (Yu Jin-u)  
기사형태	소설 (Novel)  

I climbed the stairs lightly and opened S's door. However, neither S nor Sadako were there. Perhaps Sadako had gone to someone else, but where did S go? 

Anyway, I decided to wait for S's return. To find out about Sadako's whereabouts, I needed to see S. 

On S's desk, there were reference materials for his dissertation and scattered newspapers. I picked up a manuscript that was written by him. The dissertation, titled "How will colonial and semi-colonial regions under imperialism come to be XX - particularly regarding the urgent real issues of XX," was almost finished. I reflected on S's handsome figure and his eloquent writing. The fact was that S was brilliant. His speech was free of contradictions, and once he started talking, he would endlessly delve into every subject with meticulous consideration. His dissertation was of that kind as well. 

"But," I thought while flipping through the pages of the dissertation. "Oddly enough, S is a person who has experienced a loss of power." 

Perhaps the thought was tinged with resentment regarding S's intelligent mind. There was also an unpleasant feeling about the fact that Sadako got along with S better than with anyone else. Nevertheless, S was undoubtedly that type of person. 

In the room, remnants of clothes were scattered about, which did not seem like S's usual style. After placing the manuscript back on the desk and looking around the room, I happened to find a piece of clothing carelessly thrown in the corner. It was undeniably Sadako's outer garment. It was the very one she wore the night we both left for Tokyo. My suspicions multiplied infinitely in an instant. The unpleasant feeling transformed into rage, spewing forth like red flames. 

"Where could they have gone?" 

Both S and Sadako had not returned for quite a while. Nine o'clock, ten o'clock, eleven o'clock... the time passed slowly, ceaselessly. What in the world could this be about? Of course, I felt a sense of despair when Sadako was left in Tokyo, particularly when surrounded by male friends, but transcending all that, what I firmly believed was that my male friends were not merely friends, and Sadako was not just a mere sweetheart or "mistress" of mine. What if they were to shake that conviction? 

The idea that Sadako was involved with someone, or that S might steal Sadako away from me was not such an important issue. But could such a relationship actually come to exist without undermining the bond of comradeship? The thought that S might be involved with Sadako—assuming Sadako was engaging with S—could indeed mark the beginning of her downfall, couldn't it? 

At just past twelve, I heard the creaking of the front door opening. Then, as I climbed the stairs, I heard the cheerful footsteps of two people coming up. I reflexively closed my tired eyes. 

“I don’t like Lillian’s a bit.” 

“Then Mary, Pickford is good?” 

The two opened the door, talking about the popular film stars of the time. 

“Ah!” It was Sadako’s surprised voice. 

“Isn’t this Kim-kun?” 

“Hmm?” 

I awoke as if surprised, slowly opening my eyes to look at the two. 

“What brings you here? Last Saturday, you seemed to be busy. Was there something happening?” 

In a brown new suit, Sadako stood in the middle of the room, unaware. The suit didn't seem very luxurious but looked modest, perhaps due to the fashion of the time. I spoke words contrary to my thoughts. 

“Oh, just a bit busy, but the result was worth it. Just you wait and see; soon I’ll bear fruit from the seeds I’ve sown.” 

“We are ‘seed planters,’ you know,” Sadako added, glancing at S's face as if to complain. “He’s out at M’s to watch a movie this evening. It's really fun.” 

I couldn’t contain the rising anger any longer. 

“That suit?” 

I shot a sharp gaze. Sadako seemed to have been complaining something, looking at S's face. 

“This suit? I bought it. I can’t keep wearing the same outfit from that cold season in Tokyo. Since the suit is more comfortable than a kimono, I bought it. Doesn’t it suit Sadako-chan? Huh? It really suits you.” 

Although I had further questions to ask, I restrained myself and didn’t speak. What right did I have to question her about anything? 

That night, neither Sadako nor I went to bed until S finally fell asleep. However, S seemed unable to fall asleep easily and tossed and turned. 

“Sadako-chan, do you also sleep in this room when S is all alone?” 

“Yeah, of course.” 

Sadako seemed to caress me more boldly than before. At least that’s how it felt to me. S and Sadako, also K and Sada C? P? All those unpleasant thoughts buzzing in my mind, kept me from sleeping. Although it was obvious what would happen. 

The Njeong Paper Labor Union held a public founding ceremony on February 11. Many policemen attended, and it was safely concluded under the leadership of "Asano." 

However, after that, our task became even more arduous. We worked day and night to form the craft labor unions at each factory in Njeong, with the goal of establishing the Japan-wide labor union Njeong branch. 

During that time, within the limits of my schedule, I repeatedly went back and forth to Tokyo. Yet, the more I went, the more Sadako seemed to be changing, and consequently, my worries deepened. Although I had never heard gossip about Sadako or witnessed anything, her behavior and speech slowly began to make Sadako, a simple girl, sparkle and shine as if caught up in the urban whirlwind. It would indeed be difficult for Sadako to become a good worker while living like that. The misunderstanding regarding "Asano" could very well be directed at me. 

Sadako barely paid attention to the process of forming labor unions, which I found interesting, but instead asked me to take her to places like "Ezora" or "Asagusa." If I rejected her request, she would pout. 

“How can anyone work and live like that? At least we should enjoy ourselves, right?” 

Then she would pout her lips—this had become a habit. I often yielded to her requests, and we would stroll through the brilliantly lit streets of "Ezora," which had just been illuminated by the "Pyramid." We also peeked into the theaters in "Asagusa." By then, Sadako had nearly completely turned into a girl captivated by urban glamour. Standing in front of a shop in Ezora, looking at the flashy items in the display window, she asked, 

“Hey, look over there. Isn’t that pretty?” 

“What is?” 

“I mean that.” 

What she was pointing at was a Japanese girl's flashy waist belt that was styled off its designated place. 

“That’s nothing of significance! One girl wears that waist belt, while hundreds of women are toiling to make those clothes!” 

“That may be true, but—” 

I tried several times to put an end to the increasingly uncomfortable relationship with Sadako. However, I was not yet fully aware of my desires. There was a stark difference between the Sadako who came from Chogin and the well-developed woman she had become, with her face and body resembling a twenty-three-year-old woman who I could not help but admire. 

“Sadako! Why are you becoming more and more frivolous?” 

“What? Am I doing something wrong? Oh… you’re just being a killjoy.” 

“Don’t act foolish. You really ought to go to work in a factory.” 

“Nobody’s stopping me from doing that, if only there were a position open.” 

“Anyway, stay away from S and M’s house!” At that time, I imagined both of them in the most discomforting scenarios. 

“Oh… you’re making a fuss over nothing. Do you think I’m that type of girl?” 

As February drew to a close and March came to an end, eventually in April, the tasks we had planned in Njeong were finally taking shape. Indeed, while many were dispatched from the headquarters to different places, there were no successful outcomes like that of Njeong. I received praise from the headquarters, and comrades praised my activities. However, my heart felt heavy. How would I deal with the Sadako issue? 

As April approached, with a warm breeze and flowers blooming, it hardly felt like spring was here. Days of gloom passed and returned again. 

I returned from the Njeong paper factory to Tokyo towards the end of April. I was now in a position where I could no longer get along with anyone, just like before. I had sent the previous teacher, T, to work as a delivery person at a soy sauce shop while Sadako entered the cotton mill at "Fukagawa." We started living in a small room in the back alley of "Fukagawa."

We would leave at dawn and return in the evening. I had planned for Sadako not to work nights from the start. What was more crucial was to find time to work on our own goals during the night. 

However, our living conditions were far from satisfactory. Sadako openly began to express complaints about our impoverished life. 

“How long are we going to endure this hardship?” 

“While we live, we must strive for happiness. Didn’t we understand it would be like this from the beginning?” 

“But who would know it would turn out this way? Oh, I don’t want to live like this!” 

Sadako’s lips jut out once again. 

“Sadako! So what do you propose we do? We’re engaged in this work not because it’s interesting or feels just, but because we cannot avoid it. Isn’t that so?” 

“Who doesn’t know that? It’s just so unbearably hard right now. You only bring back soy sauce bottles at night while I’m stuck gulping down cotton.” 

Her complaints, though understandable, still vexed me, and her pout was becoming habitual. Every time I entered the room, my brow would furrow deeply without fail. Yet as the night wore on, we repeatedly glimpsed the political world’s turmoil under the dim glow of the flickering lamp. 

S had recently come back. His previously published dissertation caused a huge controversy, and he brought fruits and cash to give as gifts. Yet such things often happened on nights when I wasn’t at home. During those times, when I returned late, scraps of fruit peels were carelessly strewn across the tatami mat, and Sadako was sprawled in the middle of the room, sleeping deeply like a fallen soldier. As I fell asleep, the fruit peels brushed off the sheets and wrapped in the blanket, 

“Ah! Welcome home!” she exclaimed with wide open eyes as soon as I pulled the blanket back. 

“What is this mess?” 

“S gave us fruit and waited for you, but you weren’t coming home to eat even a single piece.” 

I felt a surge of anger that almost led me to slap Sadako. But then, Sadako’s long eyelashes and her subtly red lips spoke differently than before, and within moments, I sank into deep melancholy. 

As time passed, I felt increasingly distant from Sadako. By now, I found the matter concerning her family embarrassing. Given how things had unfolded, how would I justify bringing Sadako to Tokyo? 

I had lured the simple girl, one who longed for the urban splendor, to Tokyo—what was the proof of that? 

“It was a mistake. Everything is my fault.” 

Regardless, I had no intention of giving up on Asano. Just a little longer. Let’s see how Sadako turns out. 

In early May, because the plan we had was coming to fruition, I rode the train to “Soshigaya” several times to meet comrades until late at night for discussions. And I continued doing it for a week. 

That night, I was excessively fatigued and took the last train with a red lantern back to our little burrow in "Fukagawa." In the room, the fruit peels were still strewn about, and Sadako was deeply asleep. When I saw her, something about her skin seemed unusually dry. 

“It’s a gift from the factory.” 

I embraced Sadako’s hand deeply moved. Her palm felt rough, and her nails looked unkempt. However, at the same time, I discovered something unexpected. On her left hand was a simple but bright red gemstone ring! 

I shook Sadako awake. She opened her eyes wide and was taken aback as if struck by lightning. 

“What’s wrong? Why do you look so frightened?” 

“Where did you get this ring?” 

Sadako touched the ring as if it were electric, and a look of panic suddenly spread across her face. 

“Why?”

“Who bought it for you?” 

“Don’t look at me with those scary eyes. I really don’t know.” 

Suddenly, Sadako burst out crying. No matter how much I questioned her afterward, she wouldn’t say a word. On one side of my heart, empathy surged like a storm. Yet on the other hand, a voice cried out, "This is a curse! Don’t push it away; resolve your life!” What should I do? 

“Sadako! If you feel like that, we really can’t go on like this. I believed you would become a good worker, but you’re just like those fallen proletarian women. We cannot move forward as comrades if you persist in this!” Go wherever you wish for all I care. Find a husband and live comfortably; wear rings or fancy clothing!” 

My voice rose with the tension as I spoke. Sadako, in a surprising moment, sprang up and grabbed my pants. 

“Please forgive me. I was wrong. I won't act like this again. Please don’t go. Stay with me!” 

“You’ve done nothing wrong. I cannot care if you had done something when I wasn't around or if the ring came from someone else. Regardless, you don't help my cause; you’re a hindersome to me. That's all it matters.” 

I pushed her away and rushed out of the room. I heard Sadako's wailing behind me. I was muddled and felt a burdensome weight on my heart, as if I had found solace in a cruel action after clearing my conscience. 

“But there’s no turning back! Things have gone awry.” 

Towards the end of May, I was forced to return to my homeland. The day before leaving, I visited Asano after a long while. Since then, I had my own thoughts about the incidents. Asano truly reacted with a miserable expression. 

“You’re now my little sister. Haven’t you lost all amusement by now?” 

He was still misunderstanding the situation! 

“You think that’s all I feel? That’s really pitiful!” 

He intensely scrutinized my face, and my chest tightened. 

“Still, I acted in a way that was right, according to my conscience.” 

“Conscience! Ha!” He said with a tone of bitterness. “Just think! I can understand why you would doubt Sadako now!” 

I was left perplexed. After parting ways with Sadako three days later, I headed back to our hut, but she no longer was there. 

“I heard from S that she’s been living quietly with some guy named 'Gondo' and they’re producing works like ‘Love's Cradle.’” 

According to Asano’s information, Sadako's relationship with Gondo had begun long before. I learned that the incident with the suit had been a gift from Gondo—actually given by S to expose Sadako’s secret. 

“Earlier, S had come by and said those things while you worked like a madman.” 

I felt guilt wash over me. I grasped Asano's hand. 

“Asano! I ask for your forgiveness. I had unjustly suspected S and you, as well as all our comrades.” 

“I had my suspicions about Sadako's path from the beginning. You did not do anything wrong. But consider this: What made her turn into who she became? We cannot see this merely as a trivial mistake from a young girl.” 

I returned to my hometown. In my tiny room of less than a square meter, I gazed through the prison-like bars at the blue sky of my homeland, my thoughts spiraled in anguish and resentment. Each time I did, I wondered, 

“What made her go so low?” 

The path Sadako took is the same as all daughters of the proletariat in every nation are tumbling down. - 30. 4 -  

**Summary**  

This narrative portrays the internal struggles and emotional turmoil experienced by the narrator as he navigates his relationship with Sadako against the backdrop of colonial Japan. The narrator grapples with envy, feelings of betrayal, and the harsh realities of life under colonial rule while attempting to uphold his ideals about the working class. The vivid depiction of Sadako’s transformation and the pressures exerted upon her reflects broader themes of oppression facing women and the proletariat during this period. Historians may find this story significant as it reveals personal and social tensions under Japanese colonialism, illustrating how colonial occupation exacerbated individual lives within that context.
