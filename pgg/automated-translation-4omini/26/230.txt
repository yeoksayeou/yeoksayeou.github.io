On the 1st of May, 1930, a lively discussion took place among notable figures from the Dong-A Ilbo newspaper, featuring editor-in-chief Lee Kwang-soo and president Song Jin-woo, reflecting on the challenges and the peculiarities of life as journalists in colonial Korea. The dialogue humorously explores various topics, including the readership of the Dong-A Ilbo, the nature of literary works being published, and the influence of realist narratives versus popular tastes in journalism. Amidst lighthearted banter, deeper insights emerge regarding the role of journalists in shaping public discourse during a time marked by cultural repression under colonial rule. The conversation reflects a collective concern over the shifting expectations in literature and journalism, especially regarding content that resonates with broader public sentiments. 

There are moments of honesty where the harsh realities of journalism are addressed, such as financial challenges and the nature of public readership in Korea. The discussion concludes with personal reflections by Lee, questioning the personal impact of love and relationships against the backdrop of societal expectations. This glimpse into the lives of journalists of that era highlights their struggles for creative expression while navigating the political oppression of their time.

**Summary**

This article offers a vivid portrayal of a dialogue among prominent journalists of the period, reflecting their experiences and challenges in colonial Korea. It showcases how they navigate their responsibilities towards their readership while facing the constraints imposed by colonial authority. Historians might find the duality of jest and earnestness particularly revealing, illustrating how public discourse was managed through humor, while also shedding light on the socio-political climate of Korea under Japanese rule.
