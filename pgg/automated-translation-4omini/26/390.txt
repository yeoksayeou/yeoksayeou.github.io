저자	金岸曙 (Kim An-so)  
기사형태	시 (Poem)  

My hometown is Guksan near the Yellow River.  
On the boat carrying spring songs, the waves are high.  
In the back mountain, I'm playing with the cuckoo flowers.  
What I longed for has now become a dream.  

A thousand miles seem like four or five tens in my dreams.  
The road to Pyongyang is a place of play.  
Last night I couldn’t go again,  
How can I bear this wandering heart?  

In the Yellow River's Five Li Village, my hometown stays.  
I took the path through the middle road.  
As I go, the dream path feels like Menglang.  
There’s no place to see the white sail that is vivid in my eyes.  

The setting sun also flies in the wind today.  
The azaleas sing in the spring day.  
The long road of a thousand miles leaves no place to stay.  
I wander today to my hometown as well.  

(Attached note: This is neither a folk song nor a poem, but something in between, as stated by the author.)  

In the parting I can’t manage - A song from Jiangxi -  
Han Jeong-dong  

The rooster crows at dawn,  
If you’re going, please do come  
There’s a way to go,  
But the fog is thick.  

It is hard to go as the fog gathers,  
Even across the stream over there fog covers it.  

Along the river of Dae Dong, -A song from Jiangxi-  
Han Jeong-dong  

First of all, do not stop in Pyongyang,  
With a vest or stockings,  
I will wear a headdress.  

The elegant lay of Yaryodo,  
Even the stream draws near.  
When I go back and forth,  
I have to stay a day.  

If I go home from Pyongyang,  
And if evening comes,  
In the river near Boshun,  
The bird will turn into an enemy.  

If I return, it has to be for a hundred miles,  
The bird tells me to go  
And only cries out.  

It's May,  
Park Ro-eul  

It's May, it's May,  
When flowers bloom and trees grow, it's May.  
Is the servant at someone’s house busy now?  
There’s no time for farm work under the sun.  

I’m floating about in the morning,  
And at night the dew falls quietly.  
The girl next door is in a dream or awake.  
How bright the morning sun on the bedding is.  

Picking strawberries,  
Park Ro-eul  

The strawberries ripen behind the hill,  
And I see them shining like a red dress.  

Oh, strawberry picker,  
Is it delicious?  
Is it bitter from picking?  
Or is it love that’s bitter?  

For the horses,  
Yi Eun-sang  

Every year after eating the spring grass,  
How many stallions are raised?  
As the spring breeze blows,  
The new grass is sweet to the scent.  

Galloping, the warhorses who played  
Are now sick and lie down—is it true?  
Now which horse dares  
To fight after going out?  

Raise the colts more,  
And produce warhorses.  
If they grow up and their four legs are big,  
Will the riding nets break under them?  

Let go of the body that has fattened,  
And calling to go breaks apart  
Doesn’t the cry shatter the mountains and rivers?  

**Summary**

This article features a collection of six folk songs, rich in imagery and emotions tied to the homeland. It reveals nostalgia and a longing for connection to one's roots and traditions, even amidst the struggles of daily life. The heartfelt expressions and themes of love, separation, and nature reflect the realities and sentiments of Koreans during the colonial period, representing both personal and collective experiences that might intrigue historians studying cultural resilience against the backdrop of colonial rule.
