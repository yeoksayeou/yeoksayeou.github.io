1930 Fragments of Jazz Era Cinema and Young Poets  

Under the vibrant lights of a color lamp,  

The scenery painted in blue mint,  

Blurred by the jazz dance,  

A waitress with rosy lips.  

On this side, behind the curtains,  

A young man with a red tie,  

Randomly banging the piano keys,  

On the other side, at the table,  

Two drunken XX · XX,  

All night they debate about the end.  

Oh, like a cat, blinking,  

Chewing on a yawn, in the air,  

XXX XXX, your XX sways.  

A few years ago, XXX in XX had some XX.  

Today the weight of new worries is unexplained.  

A longing filled with the scent of alcohol, love, and Korea.  

Oh, bring me a strong curry tea,  

So often I see the coronas blooming in spring,  

Your face, uplifted, is beautiful.

**Summary**  

The poem captures a vivid scene typical of the 1930s jazz culture, where a social atmosphere saturated with alcohol, music, and youthful aspirations is depicted. It merges personal introspection with collective experiences of young poets against the backdrop of colonial Korea, hinting at the social and political unrest of the time. Historians may find interest in how young intellectuals expressed their emotional turmoil and cultural identity through art during colonial rule, reflecting broader sentiments of resistance and yearning for a distinct Korean identity.
