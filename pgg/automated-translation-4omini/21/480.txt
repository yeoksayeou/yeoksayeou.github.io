기사형태	논설 (Editorial)  

Seoul is the capital of Joseon, where all the culture within and outside of its borders is concentrated. Today's Seoul is not the same as Seoul twenty years ago, and this year’s Seoul is quite different from last year’s. Likewise, next year's Seoul will be significantly different from this year's. It is interesting to understand the various statistics of Seoul, which is developing every month and year—albeit with the influence of foreign hands—and it is also worthwhile to consider what the current population of 320,000, made up of approximately 72,000 households in 101 neighborhoods and 85 districts, does, what they eat, and how they live. The total population is categorized by nationality as follows (the statistics quoted here are mostly from the end of September, Showa 3 [1928], as surveyed by the Seoul City Office, but as of the end of June, Showa 4 [1929], the population is 321,843).

Marriage, Divorce, Birth, Death  

Looking at the number of marriages, as of August 12, Showa 4 [1929], there were only 1,369 cases within the year. However, among Japanese people, those who married Joseon people—very few—were not included in this number. In contrast to the number of marriages, the divorce rate is also low, with only 18 cases recorded within the same year—this reflects the investigation by the courts—and can be said that people living in Seoul are living very peacefully. Transportation  

Next, how do these 300,000 citizens move around? Over the course of a year, an average of 1.5 million people depart from and 1.3 million people arrive at Gyeongseong Station every day. The city has approximately 120 trams with 660 conductors, and excluding government-owned and private automobiles, there are 250 operating in the city—66 are managed by Joseon people and 149 by Japanese people. Additionally, there are 70 rural shared taxis—44 managed by Joseon people and 26 by Japanese—making for a total of 70. City government-run minibuses (buses) number 40, driven by a staff of 32 drivers, with 40 conductors. Apart from these, there are 92 motorbikes, 12,832 bicycles, and 1,184 rickshaws as well as 3 horse-drawn carts and 5,411 freight carts, alongside 599 donkey and mule-drawn carts. (This data is current as of June, Showa 4 [1929].) 

These countless transportation options transport over 320,000 citizens day and night. What do these citizens do to cause such a hustle and bustle? Is everyone employed? How many are job seekers and how many have found jobs?

General Health  

First, there are 200 medical institutions in total, comprising of 4 national, 1 public, and 195 private hospitals. Physicians employed by government agencies include 43 Joseon and 74 Japanese nationals, while practicing physicians consist of 97 Joseon, 98 Japanese, and 18 foreigners. Next, one should look at the supply of water, with a total supply of 6,776,015 cubic meters, the maximum supply on any given day is 23,147 cubic meters, and the minimum is 11,961 cubic meters, with a daily supply capacity of 20,300 cubic meters. If this water supply were to be interrupted, the entire population would suffer greatly. This water supply is essential for the lives of citizens. Those without access to this facility may be vulnerable to infectious diseases due to carelessness about sanitation.

Other Matters  

Moving on to a somewhat different aspect, let's look at the facilities for dealing with waste. There are 68 public toilets, and the amount of waste collected is approximately 21,092,065 quintals and 472,324 stones of excrement; to manage this transportation, there are 137,183 laborers working daily. Keeping the city clean in such ways is vital, and however, the failure to make the community toilets clean signifies both flaws on the part of authorities and citizens. In any country, the cleanliness of public toilets can reflect the moral standards of the citizens. A person’s life may be lost due to neglect of health and sanitation.

Now, regarding burial, the city manages cemeteries for Joseon People in four locations covering 401,280 thwa and two for Japanese People covering 151,254 thwa. There are two crematoriums where, in the year of Showa 2 [1927], 3,257 corpses were cremated. 

The life of urban citizens is marked by countless hardships, and they engage in daily labor, inevitably confronting death in the end. After working hard all day, they seek solace in their homes, yet their weary nerves cannot find rest in the monotony of domestic life. 

Thus, they seek solace through fleeting pleasures. They are drawn to places where they could find respite:  

Currently, there are 2 theaters managed by Joseon people and 11 operated by Japanese.  

The dining establishments include 19 run by Joseon people, 66 by Japanese, and 15 by foreigners.  

Restaurants have 1,668 operated by Joseon people, 517 by Japanese, and 15 by foreigners.  

Furthermore, there are 371 Joseon geishas, 384 Japanese geishas, 365 Joseon prostitutes, and 391 Japanese prostitutes, along with 1 foreign sex worker, providing the citizens with commercial respite.  

Lastly, with the numbers presented, the private moneylenders are also revealing of societal conditions; at the pawn shops primarily managed by Joseon people, the goods reveal a lot about the living conditions. When you look at the loans taken from pawn shops, you can get an insight into the economic circumstances of the surrounding area.  

Here are the statistics from the Joseon-managed 151 pawn shops showing an average of 1,000 won loaned out per store monthly. This results in a total annual amount of approximately 1,200,000 won through these pawn shops. The Japanese-run pawn shops total about 80 in the area, with an average loan amount of 24,000 won per shop, equating to approximately 190,000 won in total loans for the year.  

This gives perspective on the lifestyles of both sides; while it indicates that the Japanese are somewhat better off, it does not present a complete picture of their respective life experiences. 

Now that we have explored the living conditions of Seoul, a final overview of crime statistics for the year is necessary, despite the challenges of accurately assessing all incidents within five police stations. Summarizing this is no simple task, and it is worth noting that only major crimes will be recorded here due to space concerns. 

**Summary**  

The article provides a comprehensive statistical overview of life in Seoul during the late 1920s under Japanese colonial rule, reflecting the complexities of social, economic, and cultural dynamics. It highlights the juxtaposition of wealth and poverty, as the growing city developed an elaborate educational system and diverse industries while a significant portion of the population struggled for basic subsistence. Historians would find the contrasts in living conditions between various communities, along with the socio-economic pressures faced by the Joseon population, particularly noteworthy as it underlines the impacts of imperialism on local society.

교통
