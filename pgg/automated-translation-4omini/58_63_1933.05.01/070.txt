기사제목	最近 合走男女氏 列傳 (Recent Stories of Eloping Couples)  
필자	雙S  
기사형태	잡저 (Essay)  

Recently entitled “Eloping Couples' Biography.” Perhaps if you do not know, you could still guess the meaning of the title by just looking at it. Let us start by explaining this title. 

In interpreting “jointly fleeing,” it means to come together and run away. In the countryside, when a maiden and a bachelor take a glance at each other and run away from their homes, it is said, “the boats have left.” This term “jointly fleeing” signifies that. In other words, when a certain gentleman and a certain lady are in love and, due to unfavorable circumstances around them, long for a free world and go off the grid, they are “jointly fleeing.” In Japanese, it might be called “kakeochi,” right? 

This year, in the spring breeze, we know of three pairs of such “eloping couples.” In light of that, I would like to convey their difficult circumstances and sentiments to all the readers in this “Eloping Couples' Biography.” 

A Young Lady K and Mr. S who went to Shanghai by airplane 
You may surmise something upon seeing the title. 

With an air of elegance like that of a wealthy heiress, she has caused quite a stir among young men for quite some time, this Lady K. During her time in XXX, there were many rumors about her. Some said she had gone to visit a handsome gentleman in Pyongyang, while others mentioned a possible marriage arrangement with a school professor. 

One rumor had it that some fellow saw R, who hikes up the back hills of Central School early in the morning, and wondered why he did not greet Lady K at dawn. Yet, it seems sure that Mr. R and Lady K were spotted playing around the famous Pebi-Culpujang in Jinheung. However, Lady K herself denied the engagement rumors with Mr. R. Mr. R did not provide a clear answer either. 

Then last summer, shocking news from Seokwangsa appeared, causing quite a stir in Seoul. “Lady K was seen visiting Seokwangsa with an unknown companion, who was tall and somewhat dark and indeed quite good-looking.” Someone remarked, “Does that mean he is the allegedly engaged Mr. R?” “I know Mr. R well…” was the reply. 

That young man who went on a retreat to Seokwangsa was none other than Mr. S. Mr. S, who belongs to a wealthy family in Gyeongsang Province making quite the fortune. Since then, many rumors have circulated. “Lady K is marrying Mr. S and is set to receive 500 seok of cash!” and “They will live grandly in Seoul.” Or “They will start a magnificent household in Daegu.” There were even reports in the newspapers stating “Mr. S is a married man, and after an illicit marriage with Lady K, he will send both his first wife and second wife away, thus elevating Lady K to the position of first wife.” 

Amidst such tales, one wonders which to believe; that is not for the author to determine. However, at some point in such activities, the two, K and S, boarded an airplane bound for Shanghai from Japan. 

Now in Seoul, Lady K's mother, who is said to be living in a house worth 3,000 won, is waiting for them with joy, expecting them to return rich after achieving their “studies” with ten sacks of rice and some cash from her previous endeavors. I wish for the two to return safely and without illness. 

The Heartbroken Poet Mr. R and Ms. R 
The common reason love is often a tragedy is that the two individuals in love may find themselves unable to reach the next step of marriage. For instance, if the lady desires something more, like money, and finds the gentleman unable to provide it, or if the gentleman happens to be married already. 

Is this elopement of Mr. R and Ms. R due to Mr. R being married? Mr. R is a gentle poet, currently in his 30s, caught in the trap of traditional marriage and not outside the realm of young Korean men. He has made several attempts to divorce but has not succeeded. 

“There is no need for someone who already has a wife to be dating! It was a mistake to begin with,” some might say. However, even while having a beloved wife, one may find themselves in such circumstances. Strict traditional morals which suppress feelings cannot exist in 1933.

Mr. R also had his romantic endeavors. He knew well that love is the prologue to marriage. Thus, he worked hard to obtain a divorce. But how could that come to pass? 

His parents were against it, and the lady’s side was even more adamant against it. Their relationship eventually became known in Ms. R’s family. They accepted the marriage proposal, but with conditions: 

“R, you must fully divorce your first wife before you marry my daughter. And if you cannot get divorced, you are never to have contact with Ms. R again.” 

What a frustrating situation! If he cannot divorce, he cannot be with his lover... However, the passion of youth can melt even iron. They would not allow societal constraints to extinguish their emotions. 

Thus, R and Ms. R decided, “Let us break free from this clumsy and suffocating environment and flee together!” And so, on a night in April, they boarded a train to Gyeongwon. Where were they headed? 

There might not be many places a Gyeongwon train could take... but they did go to Seokwangsa to spend a brief honey-moon-like retreat, lasting for a bit longer than mere courtship. Although, undoubtedly, it could not have ended there. What happened after that is uncertain, and I too am anxiously waiting with the readers. 

Mr. A of the Music World and Young Lady K are Heading to Beijing 
Our tenor Mr. A, renowned in music concerts in the region and on the radio, has been a source of deep pride for our people. It is known widely that he has been a professor at the XX Women’s School in the region. 

This young couple, Mr. A and Young Lady K, suddenly vanished from our sight around mid-April. Once this news was reported through the newspapers, it caused quite a shock, and for a time, the topic of young couples in Seoul inevitably centered around this couple, AK. 

Artistic types are often prone to infatuation. Both A and K are artists. 

Their mutual interest had been apparent ever since Young Lady K was a student. Many were not aware that their relationship had developed to this stage. Their love burned brightly, and congruously, they should become husband and wife. 

From Young Lady K's perspective, it would not be objectionable to pursue love. However, for Mr. A, that was a different story. He had a wife and children, a proper family. He was a husband to someone else's wife, a father to someone else's children, and generally, a respectable man cannot simply become a lover to another woman in this society. 

Especially so since Mr. A was a faculty member at a women’s school that radiated a religious atmosphere. Engaging in an illicit relationship is certainly out of the question for him. But would the two part ways, unable to disconnect? The fiery passion of youth would not allow that. 

After considering all possible options, they devised a last resort. Mr. A would take a leave from his school on the pretext of going on a retreat to Harbin, and the two would join hands and head toward Beijing, towards a land of freedom. 

But did they truly set sail for Beijing? 

Thus, this spring breeze twirled the narratives of love around this land.  

**Summary**

This article shares tales of several romantic couples during the spring of 1933, particularly those evoking traditional norms versus their desires to elope. The stories reflect on intricate dynamics, revealing both social constraints and personal aspirations during the Japanese colonial period in Korea. Historians may find the characters' struggles between established customs and their longing for personal freedom particularly interesting as it illustrates the complexities of relationships amidst societal expectations at that time.
