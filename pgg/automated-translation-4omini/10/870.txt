기사제목	咄歎보다 現場으로 먼저,  大火災 때에 新聞記者는 어대로 엇더케 活動하나 (Before Complaining, First to the Scene: How Newspaper Reporters Act During a Major Fire)  
필자	東亞日報 金枓白  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)  

The sound of the fire station's alarm bell ringing continuously like the roar of an angry bull, and the fire brigade's automobile dashing off urgently will cause everyone to run outside, of course, shouting, "Where is the fire?" In particular, newspaper reporters and police officers will rush there first.

However, unlike the police, who have the responsibility to maintain public order or prevent theft during a fire, reporters only need to reliably report to the public the fact that a fire has occurred. Therefore, if a journalist finds themselves in such a calamity, their mindset becomes very different from that of an ordinary person. When ordinary people see a fire, they all think, "Oh dear! So unfortunate! What can be done? I hope no one has died!" and lament endlessly. For me, as a journalist, there is no time to lament; I head straight for the scene to ascertain the cause of the fire. Then I inquire which house belongs to whom, check the extent of the damage, and whether the home is insured or not. I also investigate whether there are any injuries to people or livestock and finally ask about the household's recovery plans.

With no time to think about anything else, I am preoccupied with writing my report. I do not even think of offering condolences to the affected families. Just as one might feel bored watching a house burned down after going to the scene, if the fire turns out not to be large, I also feel bored when there are not enough materials for a report. This might be understood as having the mind akin to that of Nolbu, watching another's house burn while wishing for misfortune upon them. It is because there are insufficient materials for writing.

From my experience, this is how it is. -<끗>-<125>  

**Summary**  

The article discusses the mindset and duties of newspaper reporters during a major fire, emphasizing the difference in emotional responses between ordinary people and journalists. While common individuals feel sympathy and concern, reporters prioritize fact-finding to produce news reports, often neglecting personal feelings of empathy. This perspective provides insight into the professional detachment expected of journalists, even in times of disaster, reflecting the broader socio-cultural dynamics during Korea's colonial period. Historians may find the article interesting as it highlights the role of the media and the psychological impacts of colonialism on professional identities.
