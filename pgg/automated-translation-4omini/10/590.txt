If I Were a Man, If I Were a Woman!! If I Were a Man!!  
                        Zhao Won-sook  
The other day, when I was in Guga-dong, I felt that everything was constrained by the male perspective and that I could not freely act alongside men. There were certainly many moments when I thought, “If only I could be a man once…” This desire was as fervent as the thought of a lower-class person wishing to become an aristocrat or a poor person wishing to become rich. However, gradually I started to engage in society, studying what men study, participating in sports that men participate in, and engaging in various activities without being restricted. At this point, I don’t have any envious thoughts about being a man as I did before. 

However, yet the societal systems remain unchanged, and customs still persist; I still have thoughts about what it would be like if I were a man… 

1. When looking for a job, being a man grants more opportunities, but within the current system, it is difficult for women to find work anywhere. 

2. When doing certain types of work, if I were a man, I could do it more freely. However, because I am a woman, due to both physical capabilities and traditional customs, I am unable to work like a man.  

Yet, the phrase “if I were a man” is just that – a phrase based on this current system; if the system were to change completely, the distinction of gender would disappear, and only the merits and abilities of the individual would matter.

**Summary**  

This article reflects on the limitations faced by women in a patriarchal society, expressing a yearning for freedom and equality often associated with masculinity. The author, Zhao Won-sook, articulates a deep desire to experience life as a man, citing specific societal barriers that inhibit women's opportunities and liberties. This essay reveals insights into the gender dynamics of 1920s Korea, highlighting the systemic inequalities that women faced under colonial rule and the aspirations for change, which might interest historians studying gender and colonialism.
