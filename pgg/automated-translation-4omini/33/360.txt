기사제목	權悳奎氏 家庭訪問記 (Visitor's Account of the Kwon Deok-kyu Family)  
필자	昭姐 (Sister So)  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

권덕귀씨 가족 방문기  

I reached the end of Hwadong and turned into an alley twice towards the north to arrive at 7th place in the foreign section, the home of Kwon Deok-kyu. The thatched house, swaying in the autumn wind, seemed as if it would sweep away my head. As I opened the front door, it felt somewhat shabby because this house did not have the usual guest quarters one sees in Seoul. In the not very spacious courtyard, there were even a few faded crape myrtle flowers lingering sadly. A box and brass bowls were strewn about, and in the large room, a girl of about 6 or 7 years old was engrossed in sewing with the autumn rain in her grasp, while the doors all around were closed, and a single cough could not be heard from within.

"Who are you, mother?" The girl opened the door and announced my presence, and the woman, holding a piece of fabric, looked out suspiciously at the unexpected guest and hesitated several times from within.

Having explained the purpose of my visit, the woman arranged a seat on the porch and began to speak slowly.

"Our family life is too monotonous to have much to say." While passing small talk, the conversation soon turned to Mr. Kwon's daily life.

"Because he enjoys medicinal wine, his daily life is quite irregular. He is quite particular about his belongings, but his lifestyle lacks regulation."

"Mmmm, medicinal wine? We don't really buy it at home. He usually drinks it outside."

"Sometimes when he comes home late in the evening and has perhaps had too much to drink, he may skip school."

"If he comes home drunk, does that cause any change in the family?"

"No, there’s nothing unusual. He originally doesn’t speak much at home. Whether things are going well outside or family matters, he talks hardly at all. Even if he travels somewhere, he doesn’t talk about it at home. He just goes out as usual, and if he doesn’t return for a while, they probably think he is staying overnight. After that, unless he mentions it, they have no way of knowing he went on a trip."

"It seems that when his nine-year-old son died, the child was very aware; even when he was supposed to be at school, he didn’t come back. After hearing about it later, he was told that the students had all gone on a trip to Pyongyang, right? So, what was I to do? The child's illness worsened, and I had to admit him to a certain hospital. The day he died was the last day, as I sent a telegram to Pyongyang."

"He is over 40, with just one son and one daughter; the son passed away. Now it seems there is no hope left, and I hardly know what to live for anymore."

"That person centered his entire life around that child. Originally, he didn’t have a playful nature, but he really cared in his heart. On Sundays or days when he didn’t go to school, he always took care of the child, teaching him how to read and managing everything when guests came."

"These days, he seems to drink more medicinal wine and cannot stabilize himself at home."

"There used to be a time when he read tirelessly. Whenever he came home, he did nothing but read books; nowadays, he doesn’t read anymore."

"You have a wide social circle, so do many students visit?"

"Yes, many of his friends often visit. Students come at one time or another. But still, because of love and hospitality, this house doesn’t seem like other houses in the neighborhood, so usually, he is alone and doesn’t know whatever is happening."

"Throughout his life, he has hardly spoken at home, nor has he indulged in any familial joy. Is it the same for other families as well? Our home feels quite lonely. The family doesn’t seem to care or take an interest in anything."

"Does he ever voice any complaints, or does the lady ever bring up topics of conversation?"

"Sometimes it gets so frustrating that I might speak out, but he never responds. So now that has become a habit, and I don’t consider whether I should talk or not. It's just lonely. When my son was here, I used to place all my hopes on him and lived for him..."

The woman could not finish her words, and her voice trembled with emotion.

"......"

"......"

Seeing the woman's tearful face, I lost my courage to ask further questions. I changed the subject and brought up the last point.  

"Besides your salary, do you have any additional income? And does the family’s finances depend on you?"

"Other than a fixed monthly salary, sometimes there may be a little coming from my writing, but it’s not much. Just my salary. So it’s not enough. And at home, we live without knowing about money. Everything is given to us, and we just eat and wear what is provided; all the money is used by him alone. It seems he recognizes that money shouldn't be spent at home."

"They say life is the bitter sea, so it must be just suffering—it’s daunting how things turn out as they do... Even with a child like that left, he’s strong and smart, and anyone who sees him would have thought they had one child to take care of, but it turned out that way. He couldn’t even get proper medicine. In the beginning, after exercising, he injured his leg, which was the cause of the fever, and because he was suffering, his father sent him Chinese medicine, and after he went to Pyongyang, what could I do alone? I just managed the treatment poorly and ended up losing the child."

Tears welled up in her eyes once again, and her voice grew dim.

  

**Summary**

This article describes a visit to the home of Kwon Deok-kyu, revealing the family's struggles and the devastating impact of the son's illness and subsequent death on the family dynamics. The woman's poignant reflections underscore the isolation and sorrow felt in their household, exacerbated by the father's emotional detachment. From a perspective of Korean colonial history, it offers insight into the societal norms of the time regarding family roles and the impact of external pressures on personal lives, emphasizing the challenges faced by families during this historical period.

花洞 막바지를 거의 올나가서 북으로 두어 번 골목을 도라들면 외인편으로 7번지 權悳奎氏宅이 잇다. 가을 바람에 쓸쓸이 나붓기는 초가집녕이 바로 머리를 슷칠 듯 하엿다. 대문을 드러서니 어쩐지 엉성한 것은 이 집은 서울의 항용 보는 행랑방이 업는 때문이엿다. 바로 마조 바라뵈이는 넓지 안은 마당에는 서리 마저 빗 낡은 백일홍이 두어 포기 조을고 잇섯다. 궤짝이며 놋그릇이 부산이 노인 대청에는 6, 7세 된 처녀애가 힘 업는 가을 빗을 안고서 솟곱질에 몰두하여 잇고 사방의 문은 다치엿고 안에서는 기침 소리 하나 안들녓다.
『엄마 누구오섯수.』 처녀애가 문을 열고 나의 옴을 告하니 바누질감을 손에 든 부인은 意外의 客을 의심스럽게 내다보며 방안에서 몃 번이나 주저하엿다.
訪問한 뜻을 대강 드른 부인은 마루에 자리를 정하고 차근차근 말을 게속하시엿다.
『우리집 생활은 너무 單調해서 무슨 말슴을 드릴 만한 것이 업슴니다』 이말 저 말 건느다가 드듸어 權先生의 일상생활에 이르럿다.
『그분은 藥酒를 잘 잡수시는 까닭으로 매일 생활이 일정하지 못함니다. 당신의 물건 가튼 것이나 혹 보실 일에는 퍽 깐깐이 하시지만 생활은 규측잇게 못하심니다.』
『藥酒요? 안에서는 별로 사다드리지 안슴니다. 늘 밧게서 잡숫지요.』
『혹시 저녁에 늣게도 드러오시고 과히 취하신 때에는 혹시 학교를 쉬이기도 함니다.』
『취해 오시면 家庭에 무슨 變化를 이르키시는 일은 업스세요.』
『별로 주정을 한다든가 그런 일은 업슴니다. 원래 그분은 가정에서 말이 업슴니다. 밧게서 일어난 일이고 집안 일이고 도모지 말이 업서요. 어대 旅行을 하서도 집에서는 도모지 모름니다. 그냥 집에서는 평상시 가티 나가서서 몃츨이고 안드러오시면 아마 宿直인가부다 하고 생각하고 잇지요. 그 다음에 남에게 듯기나 하여야 여행을 하섯든 줄 알지요.』
『그러기에 이번에 아홉 살 먹은 아들애가 죽을 때도 애가 몹시 알는대 학교 가신 분이 잇틀이 되여도 오시지를<112> 안켓지요. 그 후 남에게 들엇드니 학생들 다리고 平壤旅行을 가섯다고 하지요? 그래 나 혼자 어떡함니까 애의 病은 점점 더하고 할 수 업시 某病院에 입원을 식혓구려. 그리고 그 잇흔날 죽어버렷지요. 그래 평양으로 전보를 첫드니 올나오섯드구만요.』
『그분이 40이 넘어 아들 하나 딸 하나 단 두흘이 든 것을 그만 그 애가 세상을 떠낫슴니다. 이 압흐로는 아모 희망도 업는 것 갓고 이 세상을 무엇을 바라고 살는지 딱함니다.』
『그 분도 모든 것을 그 애를 중심으로 하섯지요. 원래 아기자기한 性味가 못되여 거트로 살갑게는 안 그르섯스나 마음으로야 다시 업시 귀해 하섯지요. 일요일이나 학교 안 가시는 날은 늘 다리고서 글도 가르키시고 손님이 오서도 그 애 다려 모든 것을 식히고 하엿지요.』
『요새는 藥酒도 더 잡숫고 도모지 집에 安定하시지를 못하시는 모양임니다.』
『전에는 책도 무척 보섯지요. 집에 드러만 오시면 그저 책 보시는 것 외에 아모 하시는 것이 업섯는데 요새는 책도 안 보심니다.』
『交際가 넓으시니까 학생들도 만히 차저오나요.』
『네 친고분들은 만히 차저 오심니다. 학생들도 각금 놀너오지요. 그래도 모다 사랑에서 접대를 하시고 보시는 바와 가티 이 집은 딴 집가티 도라 안저스니까 일상 혼자 종용히 밧게 무슨 일이 잇는지도 모르고 잇슴니다.』
