발행일	1930년 12월 01일  
기사제목	雪夜斷想 (Snowy Night Reflections)  
필자	露兒 (Ryu-uh)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literature, Other)  

Snow falls quietly as I sit wrapped in my memories. 

As I muse over the year that has passed, I feel an inexplicable warmth beneath the wintry silence. Snowflakes gently fall, covering the ground, reminiscent of the passage of time and evoking sweet nostalgia. 

“It’s snowing,” I murmur softly. This statement is imbued with dreams and reminiscences of the past year. Each snowfall summons an inherent tenderness that pricks at my heart, a veil of soft memories bound to this crystalline blanket, not unlike the gentle and warm touch of a tender heart. 

With fluffy snow burying roads and warming the thatched cottages, it blankets hills and valleys, falling tirelessly through the night. I long to wander, losing myself in the heart's turmoil that cannot be contained. Snow embodies the lonely yet affectionate spirit of winter. 

Each flake whispers its own tale as it falls, talking to me quietly without a hint of confusion. Who could doubt the peace that a sight so clean, soft, and serene can impart upon the mind? Even if just for a moment, who could resist feeling their soul clear from burden?

Snow might be the celestial guide that takes one back to the innocence of childhood.

I grew up in the southern regions where snow was a rarity. Hence, children there cherished it profoundly. Whenever the snow fell, even the sleepiest child would awake, leaping barefoot outside. 

As a child—unaware of the snow's arrival during the night, sound asleep by the warm glow of the morning stove—my mother would wake before dawn, having already swept the pristine snow off the jars and cleared a path with a wooden shovel, calling softly, “Children, awake! It has snowed!” 

Seeing the delighted chaos of me—barely dressed and stumbling around outside—she would smile and tease, “See, wear this and run! I’ve gathered the snow into a pile. Go and create a snowman!” She’d look fondly upon us while we reveled in the joy of making a snowman and partaking in snowball fights. 

That mother is now gone. As I return from the North Myeong Mountain where I have laid her to rest, my body feels like that of a drifting reed. I do not know when I might return to my hometown, and my five siblings and I weep, each going our separate ways.

Now, having sent my children far away, my mother lies alone. Above her grave, snow shall accumulate. Yet here I am, unable to collect any of that snow! 

In her lifetime, I could not even offer her a proper fish in return for her kindness, wandering as I did in foreign lands. Each day away, she must have wept, worried about me, while I languished in thoughts about her after her passing. 

It has yet to be a year since she left us, and many times I forget her. Each time, I rebuke myself for my forgetfulness.

Is this the nature of one’s heart when becoming estranged? How cruel the nature of the human heart must be!

Recently, I have not even been able to catch a glimpse of her in dreams, perhaps because the divide between the living and the dead is too vast, or perhaps because she, in her deep love, wished for me to focus on my pursuits and not dwell on her love. It might be due to the burden of the traditional morality she upheld, living her life to its fullest while enduring the hardships of our unfortunate Korea.

When the chilly winter wind howls and the snow accumulates, I am reminded of an event from three or four years ago. The back bay of Shinhan Village in Haeseong was coated with thick frost beneath the snow. The village lamps cast long shadows against the night, tempting mysterious thoughts.

At the Starlin Club, for some reason, I became briefly close to a girl, whose youthful presence was magnetic as we walked along that frozen sea. At that time, barely understanding the world, she whispered to me, her voice a mix of Korean and broken phrases, expressing her burgeoning feelings towards me.

But despite her beauty and lively spirit, a gentle disappointment washed over me, realizing my fervor couldn’t match hers. I felt as if I had to divert her attention elsewhere to save myself from her tears, refusing to be vulnerable that night. 

“Are you going to Goryeo?” 

“Yes, I have to.” 

“Really? Then...”

“When would I not go?” 

To my surprise, she took my icy hand and, tenderly, without mentioning her own warmth, tried to melt it. A moment of bravery! In that instant, I saw the distinct passion of Northern women, where love and motives were unbound by societal expectations.

That day, I learned that she was a middle school student, not even fourteen years old. Trudging through the deepening snow, I found my way back to where we had been and earnestly pleaded with her to love me not as an object but as a friend.

Though I left that place, I wonder if she is still there, perhaps now a wife and mother. The banks of the sea where we once shed tears, surely, too, still lie beneath snow.

Truly, human connections are unpredictable, and it’s easy to distance oneself from the heart.

**Summary**

This reflective piece enmeshes memories of the past with the narrator's sentiments about snow, intertwining nostalgia with the poignant loss of the narrator's mother. It encapsulates the warmth of childhood memories against the chill of winter, revealing not just personal grief but also how winter serves as a metaphor for both innocence and the inevitable passage of time. Historians might find the mother’s unwavering support and sacrifices, alongside the sense of longing and loss, illustrative of the era's familial responsibilities and emotional depth, particularly in the context of Korea’s colonial struggles.

나는 눈이 만히 오지 안는 南道에서 자라낫다.
그래서 그곳 아희들은 눈을 뭇척 사랑햇섯다. 눈이 왓다! 하면 아모리 늣잠 잘 자는 아희라도 눈을 번쩍 뜨고 맨발로 뛰여나가는 것이엿다.
내가 어렷슬 때 밤사이에 눈이 온 줄도 몰으고 아침 짓는 불긔운에 따뜻이 여튼 잠이 들어 잇스면 그 동안에 어머니께서는 어느새 일어나서서 장독ㅅ간에 싸힌 눈을 말갓케 쓸고 다시 우리들을 위하야 손수 마당비를 들고 뒤ㅅ간에 가는 길을 내여노흐시고 나서는 「얘야 고만 자고 일어나거라. 눈이 왓다!」 하고 가만히 깨우시곤 하섯다. 그래 바지를 움켜쥐고 뛰여나와 개처름 뛰고 거덜거리는 나를 보고 방그시 우스시면서 방안에서 허리띄를 차저들고 「앳다. 이거나 매고 뛰여라. 저긔 눈을 쓸어 한데 모아두엇스니 눈사람이나 만들여무나!」 하시면서 문을 다드려고도 하지 안코 우리 형제가 눈사람을 만들고 눈작난을 하고 하는 양을 행복스럽게 내다보시는 것이엿다.

이제 아들들을 멀니 보내고 고향에 외로히 남어 잇는 어머니 무덤 우에도 눈이 싸헛슬 것이다.
그러나 나는 멀니 잇서 그 눈을 쓸어들이지도 못하는구나!
生前에 생선 한 마리 떳떳이 사서 奉養해 들이지 못하고 노 客地로 外國으로만 떠돌아 단이는 동안에 어머니로 하야곰 한시도 마음을 노흐지지 못하시고 눈물만 흘니시게 하엿고 死後에 汎然하기 또한 이러하다. 그리고 아즉 돌아가신 지 1년도 못되엿는데 어머니 생각을 아주 이저바리고 잇는 때가 만타.
그럴 때마다 나는 내 마음을 스사로 꾸지젓다.
