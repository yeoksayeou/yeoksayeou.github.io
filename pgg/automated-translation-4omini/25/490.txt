Fourth Lecture: The Reign of Empress Myeongseong and the Imo Incident  

Daewon-gun, during his ten years in power, attempted many reforms, such as breaking traditional practices, organizing systems, and filling warehouses, but his methods were too drastic, causing fear and unease among the people. Moreover, his demands for palace repairs and the abolishment of the Seowon (Confucian academies) further alienated public sentiment and incited discontent among Confucian scholars. In this context, in the third year of Gojong's reign, Daewon-gun appointed Min Jaeguk (민치록) from Iju as queen; this astute and agile-minded Minbi began to slowly establish her brothers, including Min Seongho (민승호), in positions of power, preparing to confront Daewon-gun. 

While this was happening, there were petitions for direct governance from figures like Lee Heung-ro and Choi Ik-hyeon, leading to Daewon-gun's forced retirement in Gojong's tenth year (Gyeyei). Daewon-gun's withdrawal paralleled the rise of Minbi's authority. With the birth of the prince in the eleventh year of Gojong (Gapsul) and the subsequent designation of the heir the following year, the government effectively transitioned to Minbi and her clan, establishing a Min-era dominance lasting from Gojong’s tenth to twelfth years—a full 23 years of Min clan rule. During this time, Daewon-gun was visibly on alert against Minbi, leading to turbulent political dynamics; thus, the Imo Incident in the nineteenth year of Gojong marked the beginning of disastrous events.

Before the Imo Incident, Minbi took power, completely transforming the personnel and politics of the Daewon-gun era. Prominent figures such as Park Gyu-su, Lee Jae-yeong, Min Gyu-ho, Min Dae-ho, and Min Yeong-mok were appointed. Around this time, bombs were sent to Min Seongho’s house, resulting in the annihilation of his family. This occurrence, interpreted as a plot by Daewon-gun's faction, incited extreme rage within Daewon-gun, further complicating relations. Additionally, the government at the time rejected Daewon-gun's isolationist policies, concluding a treaty with Japan in February of Gojong’s thirteenth year. This increased interactions with Japan, while the reform faction within the court grew stronger, leading to the dissolution of the previous five military units and the establishment of two new ones. Maj. Hori Moto was summoned from Japan, and a new battalion was trained in modern military techniques, provoking dissatisfaction among the traditional soldiers.

The reality of the Imo Incident is that in July (the sixth lunar month), the Office of General Affairs made a delayed payment of one month’s salary to the old-style soldiers, who hadn’t been paid for thirteen months. The distribution was unjust, resulting in soldiers inciting a rebellion and attacking treasury officials. When Minister Min Gyeom-ho demanded punishment for the rebels, approximately 5,000 soldiers simultaneously attacked houses of the Min clan and set fire to the Japanese legation. They also killed Lt. Horimoto of the training unit and, in the early hours of the next day, went to Daewon-gun for help. Min Gyeom-ho and several high officials committed suicide, while Minbi, believing the chaos might lead her downfall, sent word to Gojong to ask for Qing military assistance.

Gojong, convinced this was a critical moment, solicited aid from the Qing dynasty. At this time, the Qing general Li Hongzhang declared this was an opportune moment and dispatched 5,000 troops to enter the capital on July 11 and subsequently to Beijing on July 15. Meanwhile, Japan, concerned about this Qing intervention, sent four warships and an army detachment to Incheon, which resulted in the signing of the Jeemulpo Treaty (between Korea's Lee Yu-won and Kim Hong-jip) on the same day Daewon-gun was captured. This military incident led to the stationing of both Japanese and Qing troops in the capital, marking the onset of a phase in which Korea found itself caught amid Japanese-Chinese conflict, providing a proxy for the underlying tensions preceding the Gapsin Coup and the Sino-Japanese War.

Fifth Lecture: The Gapsin Coup and the Treaty of Tianjin  

The emergence of the Enlightenment Party was a means to promote Korea's own Meiji Restoration, drawing from Japan’s Meiji Restoration; this, along with the treaty with Japan during the Byeongja era, further bolstered their power. Reviewing recent relations with Japan, it is noted that during the last days of the Tokugawa shogunate, they initiated foreign trade with intent to convey connections to us. Moreover, emissaries were sent in the first year of Meiji to express intentions for restoring the monarchy and to seek trade relations; however, at that time, Daewon-gun maintained a policy of closed doors against foreign influence. 

After Minbi gained control, she gradually prepared for secret interactions with Japan; thus, in September of the twelfth year of Gojong, Japanese warship Un’yō attempted to anchor near Ganghwa Island but was fired upon by Gwanghwa Fortress. Following these events, the Japanese government used the attack as an outcry, sending generals Kuroda Kiyotaka and Inoue Kaoru to demand reparations and a commercial treaty. Despite opposition from some in the government, with support from figures of the Min faction like Park Gyu-su and Oh Gyeong-seok, the Korean-Japanese Treaty of Ganghwa was concluded on February 26. This treaty was the first formal opening of relations with Japan, consisting of 12 articles stipulating that Korea would be treated as an independent nation. It also determined that in 20 months the ports of Busan (opened in Gojong's 13th year), Wonsan (Gojong's 17th year), and Incheon (Gojong's 20th year) would be opened.

In accordance with this treaty, in November of Gojong's 17th year, Haneda Yoshizumi was dispatched as the appointed minister to Gyeongseong (present-day Seoul). 

From that point onward, as the protectorate over Japan was being established, the Enlightenment faction's influence progressively increased. In May of Gojong's 18th year, leading officials including Park Jeung-yang, Yu Un-joong, and Hong Yeong-sik were dispatched to observe conditions in Japan. Furthermore, in the same year, the regime suffered casualties under Lee Jae-seon’s conspiracy while military reforms were continually advanced, strengthening the Enlightenment faction. Before the Imo Incident, the conservative faction referred to as Daewon-gun’s group had been dominant. 

After the Imo Incident, the Enlightenment faction, despite initially being forced back, regained strength as Japan dispatched troops and Daewon-gun was transported to Tianjin. In August of the year of Imo, figures like Park Yeong-hyo, Kim Ok-kyun, and Seo Gwang-beom were sent as special envoys and aides to Japan. 

It must be noted that following Imo, the suppression from the Qing dynasty and the changes within Korea’s political arena from this point were critical.  

In examining Korea's relations with the Qing dynasty, it is noted that since King Injo's 15th year, following the Joseon dynasty’s tributary relationship with the Qing, it was not until the 33rd year of Gojong following the Treaty of Shimonoseki that this relationship was essentially severed, lasting a total of approximately 259 years. During this time, between approximately 1860 and 1973, the internal affairs and external pressures faced by Joseon left little room to deliberate on such matters, and diplomatic issues arose internally, complicating diplomatic maneuvers.

When a French missionary was executed in Gyeongseong in 1866 or during the 1873 Imo Incident following the "General Sherman" incident resulting from American efforts at communication, the Qing dynasty would reply that "Joseon is not a subject of China" and "while it may venerate China's legitimacy, engaging in war or peace is up to the nation." Following the Joseon dynasty’s signing with Japan, China altered its stance toward Joseon, taking advantage of the Qing Intervention during the Imo Incident, deploying troops to quell the unrest. 

At this time, Yuan Shikai was ordered to station 2,000 troops to both manage political affairs and influence the military. Though many high-ranking officials in the Joseon government were named, in reality, it was Yuan Shikai, Ma Jianzhong (the German advisor), and others who held the true authority over Korea’s politics and military powers. In an effort to counterbalance Japan, Li Hongzhang urged for increased exchanges with Europe and America leading to various treaties, such as the one with America in 1882 during the Imo Incident and subsequent treaties with England and Germany in 1883.

The rise of the Independence Party and the Daedong Party was marked by the growing influence of China, transcending the previous notion of revolutionary factions and the conservatives that had once triumphed through association with Daewon-gun. By circling back to the bodies of influence tied closely to the conservative government of the Joseon dynasty, led by the Min faction, they affiliated more with Qing influence and established the Daedong faction. Conversely, the Independence Party aimed to declare independence akin to the Meiji Restoration. Figures such as Hong Yeong-sik, Kim Ok-kyun, Park Yeong-hyo, Seo Gwang-beom, and Seo Jae-bil formed a faction though not as strong as the Daedong party, they constituted a significant force. 

The growing clash between these two factions peaked in the winter of Gojong’s 21st year (Gapsin Year) following the international incidents that occurred with France and Russia. In August of this year, news reached Gyeongseong of the Qing navy's defeat in the war against France, leading figures of the Independence Party to conspire with the Japanese minister Takezō. 

On December 4 of that year (October 17 lunar calendar), they sparked a movement aiming to counter the government, marking the genesis of the Gapsin Coup. 

The Gapsin Coup was orchestrated by Hong Yeong-sik (the son of Hong Sun-muk, then 30), Kim Ok-kyun (34), Park Yeong-hyo (24), and Seo Gwang-beom. They secretly allied with the military and planned to assassinate officials using the pretext of a banquet at the Post Office. However, their aim was limited to wounding only Min Yeong-ik. Their secondary plan sought to claim the advantage by conspiring to station the Japanese troops within the palace under royal decree. Subsequently, they assassinated nine leaders of the Daedong Party, including Min Yeong-muk and Min Dae-ho, forming a new government that consisted of Hong Yeong-sik, Kim Ok-kyun, and Park Yeong-hyo. 

However, on the third day, Yuan Shikai led troops and attacked through the east gates, while soldiers on both sides engaged in confrontation. Amidst this chaos, Kim Ok-kyun and Park Yeong-hyo desperately attempted to recover the situation but were ultimately defeated, surrendering to the Qing troops. The king retreated into the Qing legation, forming a new government with officials like Shin Sun-jak, Kim Byeong-guk, and Kim Hong-jip, resulting in Kim Ok-kyun's reform efforts only lasting about 30 days. 

Subsequently, in March of the Gap year, Kim Ok-kyun resurfaced in Shanghai after fleeing from Japan and was subsequently assassinated by a political enemy, Hong Jong-u. 

As the turmoil unfolded, the citizens of Gyeongseong targeted the Japanese legation, leading to the death of a military officer. Japan used this as justification to send Inoue Kaoru for the purpose of drafting the Hanseong Treaty, which primarily implicated financial reparations. Following this, the Japanese and Qing troops stationed in Gyeongseong reflected worsening conditions as tensions escalated.  

In early September, Itō Hirobumi was sent to Tianjin to negotiate the signing of the Treaty of Tianjin, which consisted of three articles:

1. Both nations must withdraw their troops from Korea within four months after signing.
2. Japan urged the Korean king to train troops to maintain domestic peace autonomously, while neither country shall send instructors without consent.
3. Should disturbances or significant events arise in Korea, Japan and the Qing must first notify each other, and withdraw troops when the situation calms.

Consequently, both nations withdrew their forces. For Korea, this moment was pivotal for potential independent endeavors; however, following the backlash after the Gapsin Coup, the declining global order crossed with the national issues led to a clear indication that the influence from both Japan and Qing was resuming.

Sixth Lecture: The Uprising of the Donghak Society and the Sino-Japanese War  

After Gojong’s ascension, the failed internal reforms under Daewon-gun were witnessed. Following the years leading up to Gapsin and amidst ongoing upheaval, the state faced external pressures from Qing and Russian influences while the government, led by Min's faction, became increasingly complacent toward popular discontent among common people. 

As described previously, by the late Joseon dynasty, there were initial sporadic uprisings that seemed destined to open paths to popular movements. The Donghak movement emerged during the late years of Gojong's rule; its leaders aimed for a fundamental reform addressing the socioeconomic injustices faced by common people over the last thirty years. 

In the spring of Gojong's seventh year (Imja), Donghak followers, under the leadership of Jeon Byeong-jun and Kim Gae-nam, rose in revolt, killing Governor Jo Byeong-gap of Oguhyeon and raised the banner of "Protect the people, all-round recovery" to mobilize widespread participation, leading to a surging escalation of popular movements throughout Jeolla and Gyeongsang provinces. 

In May, former Jeolla Army Commandant Hong Gye-hun was sent as the military commander to quell the uprising, deploying 800 troops toward Gusan, yet Donghak forces occupied Jeonju prompting yet another governmental response. Observing the strength of the Donghak army, Hong Gye-hun urged the deployment of Qing troops, leading to Yuan Shikai sending 1,500 Qing troops under Ye Zichao. 

The Qing army announced its troops on June 6 and on July 7, Japan, meanwhile, declared a state of military protection for its residents, resulting in their own deployment. 

Nevertheless, the Donghak uprising was ultimately quelled through troop suppression by Chinese and Japanese forces, leading to the final rout by the year’s end.

In considering the Donghak incident, it's significant to note (1) that the movement itself was a grassroots struggle grounded in opposition against both an oppressive ruling class and social injustices—it was a genuine reflection of the needs of the common populace rather than merely an internal clash within the ruling elite (2) the consequences of this uprising disturbed the foundations of the ancient Joseon society and intensified the emergence of reform governments, fostering a fresh spirit of transformation among the upper and lower classes alike (3) the Donghak uprising also served as a proximate cause triggering the onset of the Sino-Japanese War, reshaping the regional dynamics.

Seventh Lecture: Gapsin Independence and the Eulmi Incident  

Following the return of Daewon-gun post-Gapsin, the withdrawals of Qing and Japanese forces were executed, but China subsequently garrisoned troops under Yuan Shikai to observe governmental activities. The government affiliated with the Min faction expressed notable discontent toward Yuan. 

This led to a formal interaction of Korea and Russia in June of Gojong's 21st year, having negotiated the Korea-Russia Commercial Treaty alongside advances toward the Romanov ambassador. Yuan Shikai resented both the royal court and the Min faction, and in the 10th month of the Eulmi year, he exerted further control over the government’s internal and external policies. 

Gapsin reformations commenced following the Donghak uprising; in May of Gapsin’s year, the Qing and Japanese military deployments prevailed, while Japan formulated key proposals for significant government reforms, which were realized by July 3rd. Amid this context, Joseph Takasune, the government envoy, went to the Gyeongbok Palace to form a coalition with Daewon-gun and expel corrupt Min faction members to distant areas of exile. On July 26, a temporary Military Affairs Commission was established, with Kim Hong-jip as head; by the time these constitutional amendments concluded on February 17, around 208 laws had been resolved. 

Notably, the new cabinet set forth under the new laws, titled after their respective ministers, consisted of Kim Hong-jip (Prime Minister), Lee Jae-myeon (Interior), Min Yeong-dae (Home Affairs), and others leading the administration. The pursuit of reforms moved forward amid the increasing threat of the Sino-Japanese War, military alliances established by August 26 among the Japan-Korea Defense Alliance, reshaping both geopolitics and internal affairs. 

The notable divisions within the reformist government centered on these tensions were exacerbated when Daewon-gun ultimately withdrew, leading to the appointment of conservative elements who fell out of favor among those implementing proposed changes. 

However, in Gojong's 32nd year (1919), on January 7, the king revealed the sentiments surrounding state reform and independence at Taemyo (the royal ancestral temple) announcing the "Fourteen Articles of Hong."  The date also marked the shift regarding the official designation of the royal sovereign from "Gyeonggyeon" to the term "Daegunjo" and initiated endeavors to dispatch representatives abroad that demonstrated Korea's self-sovereignty. 

Ah! The realization of reform was coming to a conclusion as Park Yeong-hyo was summoned into the Interior Ministry, asserting authority within the government, thus empowering Minbi to leverage this by removing other political enemies, thereby reinstating her power. As Park began to implement changes, he expelled the military minister Zhao Xi-yuan; in response, Kim Hong-jip, another powerful official, once again resigned, sidelining strong internal opposition.

Yet again in June of that year, the Japanese minister withdrew due to complications arising from the newly appointed cabinet. In this environment, Minbi seized control of the palace, establishing a preferential officer system and began a gradual attempt to undermine Park’s influence. It was then revealed that Park Yeong-hyo was plotting his return to Japan as the program of reform in Korea lost momentum. Eventually, power returned to the Min faction, who clashed with pro-Russian ally factions. 

In October, outraged by Japan’s victory in the Sino-Japanese War, the factions ultimately staged a coup, executing the deposed Minbi. This triggered international controversy, and as Japan removed several of its officials from Hiromura Yeiichi’s replacing them in November to mourn the queen and to control popular outrage. 

Eventually, on February 11 (December 28 lunar calendar), the king fled to the Russian legation while Park Yeong-hyo was appointed interim prime minister. This led to subsequent violent conflicts where Kim Hong-jip deemed as involved in the assassination, leading to further killings with names including Yu Un-joong, Jo Byeong-gip, and Zhao Xi-yuan fleeing in panic.

The pro-Russian faction now decisively holding the reins control announced the withdrawal of the reformations implemented post-Gapsin, signifying the end of the newfound independence aspirations. 

This too brought an end to the tensions between Daewon-gun and Empress Min, with Daewon-gun passing away in Gwangwu two years later at the age of 79.  

**Summary**  

This article analyzes significant historical events surrounding the reign of Empress Myeongseong, focusing on her rise to power, the Imo Incident, the Gapsin Coup, and the Donghak Uprising, as well as Korea's fraught relationships with Japan and China during these transformative periods. It highlights the constant struggles for power and reform within the dynastic court and the broader implications for Korean independence and identity in the face of foreign interventions. Historians might find the shift in allegiances and the political realignments significant, as they illuminate the complex dynamics of nationalism, colonialism, and reform in late Joseon Korea.

第六講 東學黨의 蹶起와 日淸戰爭＜143＞

高宗登極 이후로 일시 대원군의 내정혁신의 시험이 잇고 또 甲戌이후 開化와 守舊 獨立과 事大의 정당이 잇서 갑신개혁과 가튼 정변을 니르켯스나 그것은 모다 경성의 내지지방의 宮界에 대한 일시의 파동을 니르켯슬뿐이오 일반인민의 실생활에는 별반의 反響이 업섯다. 더욱 갑신이후 개화신진의 徒가 迫害되고 淸國 乃至 露國의 세력을 배경으로한 閔族一流를 중심한 정부의 專權은 一層下層 인민의 痛苦를 더하엿다. 우에서 述한 바와가티 李朝의 말기에는 처음에는 일종의 發作的 端的의 인민운동이 닐기 시작하다가 哲宗年間에는 당시의 變運에 응하는 새로운 진리와 목적을 갓추고 나타난 동학이 생기여 多百姓의 不期而同을 엇는바 고종등극의 초에 其指導者 崔水雲을 죽이고 니여 그것의 활동을 금하엿스나 運을 응하야 나타난 그것은 인력으로 禁過할 바가 못되고 이후 30여년간의 형세는 그의 발전을 조장하야 고종 28,9년 즉 壬辰 癸巳間의 동학은 慶尙, 全羅, 忠淸, 京畿, 江原, 黃海 일대에 遍滿하야 其 潑*한 신흥세력은 당시의 양반, 土豪, 관료의 一隊威脅이 되엿다. 이에 지방지방에서는 이 신구세력이 互相對峙알륵이 되야 公州, 全州의 군중운동이 잇고니혀 경성에서는 光化門전의 수만명의 伏閤示威가 잇섯스나 오즉 世道의 爭奪이 잇고 외국병의 鼻息을 엿봄이 잇는 외에 다시 다른 것이 잇는 줄을 모르는 당시의 閔泳駿 즉 閔泳徽 일파를 중심삼은 정부는 單히 全羅監司 金文鉉, 慶尙監司 李容直에게 전령하야 慰喩解散을 명하고 후에 魚允中으로 兩湖宣撫使를 삼아 宣喩를 행하는 이외에는 하등의 考慰가 업섯다.
고종 31년 甲午(1894년) 3월(陰 2월 하순)에 全琫準, 金開南을 수령으로한 동학의 一群이 古阜郡守 趙秉甲을 죽이고 保國安民(除暴救民)의 烽火를 들자 爲先 全羅, 慶尙의 일대가 響應하야 金海府使 趙駿九는 그 지방 백성에게 境外逐出이 되고 列邑守宰가 다가티 降服혹은 희생되야 形勢大振하엿다. 정부에서는 5월에 前 全羅兵使이던 洪啓薰으로 兩湖討捕使를 삼고 兵8백을 주어 群山을 向케 하엿스나 동학의 軍이 그달 하순에 全州를 進占하매 監司金文鉉은 일즉히 다라나고 소위 관군은 전의가 업시 도처에 敗走하엿다. 이를 본 洪啓薰은 곳 정부에 上書하야 淸國의 大兵을 請할 不得已를 訴하며 정부(閔泳駿)는 곳 이를 袁世凱에게 통하고 袁世凱는 李鴻章에게 訴하야 葉志超 葉士成을 領將으로 한 1500여명의 淸兵은 6월6일 牙山에 도착하엿다. 淸國은 이와가티 조선에 출병하는 동시에 그 旨를 同月 7일 附公文으로 일본에 知照한 바 일본에서는 청국이 天津條約에 豫先知照라는 約束에 위반한 것을 責는 동시에 居留民保護의 名으로 역시 출병을 決하야 그때 귀국햇던 大島公使는 水兵 4백을 率하고 10일 경성에 歸任하고 후 13일에는 일본육군 약3000人이 入京하엿다. 이와가티 兩國兵이 출동하야 동학난은 討滅식히게 되엿스나 그 토벌이 끗나자마자 日淸의 충돌은 시작되야 8월1일로 日淸兩國은 서로를 宣戰을 布告하고 마럿다.
東學黨은 그후 洪啓薰과 西湖巡邊使 李元會의 合攻을 닙어 全州를＜144＞ 失하엿스나 다시 北接의 孫義菴軍과 합하야 公州를 占하는 등의 일승일패가 잇던 중 三國軍兵의 合攻을 입어 이해 년말까지 완전히 敗散되엿다. 이 동학난에서 생각할 것은 (1) 그 운동자체에 잇서 甲申의 개화당운동과 가티 동일한 집권계급안에서 改新派가 守舊派에 대한 政見上의 충돌이 아니라 정치와 경제의 이중압박에서 呻吟하는 常民級이 정치와 사회에 대한 근본적 개혁을 試한 민중운동인 점 (2) 그 결과는 조선재래사회의 陣壘을 흔들고 개혁정부의 출현을 促하야 상하가 한가지로 변혁의 新氣運을 呤味케한 것 (3) 日淸戰爭의 近因을 지여 東洋大勢를 변동케한 것등이겟다.
