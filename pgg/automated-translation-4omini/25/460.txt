발행일	1930년 03월 01일  
기사제목	봄마지 隨筆, 「봄」의 豫言 (Essay on Spring, "The Proclamation of Spring")  
필자	崔承一 (Ch'oe Sŭng-il)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)  

The heart of a person living with remnants of stale bread waits eagerly for spring.

The scenery of early spring in 1930. A conversation with a tax investigator from the government office adorned with a golden badge.  
"Do you still have the short-term position?"  
"How could I possibly survive, given my circumstances?"  
"Ah! I know everything. How then do you live?" <133>  
"Not yet in a place of comfort."  
"Well then, this spring you must grab a job again."  
"In doing so, while I absentmindedly gaze at my ledger, I will head out carrying a round stone. In a few days, the first notice for school tax will show a greatly increased amount. The fact that one person is unemployed will mean that less money will enter the national treasury. This is another disheartening scene of early spring in a narrow entrance. Wouldn't it be a spring that allows everyone to lead fulfilling lives and fills the national treasury, so that when the gold sanctions are lifted, we won't find ourselves trembling in a panicked state in this 1930 spring? 

However, when the warm sunlight casts over the showroom window, and the warm spring breeze enters lightly through the glass window, when the young man takes out his multicolored marketing pamphlets, he recalls last night’s dance craziness in the jazz club, shifting a cocktail from the girl's mouth to his own, and relaxing for a moment, the sight of spring shining through the taxi's window, rushing away from the city of splendid symphonies to the suburbs – are we merely spectators?

In the air of rusty bread crumbs, the wind from the unkempt house traveling over the hill is the first to greet this house. Anecdotes from a Japanese friend arriving say,  
"Your suit does not match this house at all."  
"I know very well."  
"I say that knowing it too."  
"Certainly."  
"In the same capital, it seems like there is a gap of five centuries between your life and mine."  
"Still, with this money, our hearts do not match."  
"Today the weather has suddenly become mild, it is a charming Little Spring Day. I hear a 'Ten Sisters' bird chirping from your house."  
"Yes, that's why when the spring sunlight rises, the light will first touch the windows of my house on the hill before yours."  

Silent and bitter. After a short moment, the response comes.  
"Ah, this is indeed the spring of 1930."  

"Spring of 1930, what will you bring us?"  
"Nothing so remarkable. Due to the enthusiastic activities of the citizens, we should occupy the same value of currency in the world market, wealthy people will become richer, and small and medium businesses will receive strong support, and preventive measures for the unemployed will be systematically implemented nationwide." <134>  
"And-"  
"To firm our foundation further, industries will be rationalized, and labor union laws will significantly progress, leading to the approval of legal political parties to a certain degree."  
This is just the beginning of several conditions in 1930.  
"Thus entrepreneurs will flourish, and the red book will be rampant; scenes appearing in the streets will lead to decreased value; luxury items will increase, cafes will multiply, dance halls will emerge, cars will proliferate, colorful skirts will march, and at night, beneath red and blue lights, kisses will arise in the shadow."  
Yet in the alley's taverns, such dialogues will surface.  
"The cost of goods has certainly fallen compared to last year, but we still cannot survive."  
"It must be because money has become scarce."  
"Indeed, do you know they have started exporting gold overseas?"  
"To be in austerity and frugality, what would that mean to someone who has nothing?"  
"That indeed means for those who have to spend less."  
"Then does that money come to us?"  
"Ha! Will it come to us? Wasn't it our standard? The rich getting richer is the national wealth."  

"Oh spring, where all of a sudden you seem to be stepping forth, but my life remains stuck with stale bread crumbs. My body is weary."  
"Then kindle even the embers of a broken hearth. Light the fire of your spirit."  
"Then you have indeed turned into a spring of white reaction, a gray spring?"  
"However, the spring of 1930 is just like last year’s spring. Rather, it is likely to be a more terrifying spring."  
"Indeed, let it be a warm spring filled with individuals, a spring of peace, a spring of justice. If not, light a torch. Let us take hold of that spring. Let's march forth. Opening the window from the dark corners, let us ascend toward the hill!" <135>  

**Summary**  

This essay reflects on the anticipation of spring amid economic hardship, contrasting the bustling life in the city with the quiet despair of those living on the margins. It captures the tensions between the promised prosperity and the harsh realities faced by the impoverished. These insights provide a glimpse into the sociopolitical atmosphere of colonial Korea in the 1930s, where class disparities and economic challenges were at the forefront of public sentiment. Historians may find it revealing how citizens fostered hope amidst persistent struggles, creating a profound narrative of resilience.
