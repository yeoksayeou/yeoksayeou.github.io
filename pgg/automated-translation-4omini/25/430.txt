3. 万造寺あき子  

In 1918, after experiencing two significant events in life—my father’s passing and my marriage—I returned to Tokyo in the fall. 

Having just passed my teenage years and stepped into youth, this young boy filled with dreams of art and a desire for literature sought to rediscover student life and was sorting through enrollment documents from various schools on his desk. In this process, I submitted an application to Kawanata School of Painting. 

However, it was not easy to attend school. Instead, I went to attend lectures on aesthetics by F Painter. A prominent figure in the Japanese painting world, F Painter had a few disciples to guide, sharing all the knowledge he possessed with them. I became one of those disciples. 

However, my primary concern was not about learning to paint. What I desired was a general knowledge of aesthetics and a grasp of concepts related to painting—this was my goal. Consequently, I never attempted to replicate a plaster model. 

Among F Painter's disciples was 万造寺あき子. She had large, shining eyes, plump cheeks, and quite notably, slender fingers with remarkably shiny pinkish nails. She often wore clothes and ribbons in vivid red tones. 

Yet, being inherently cold towards women, I did not harbor any particular feelings for her. To my eyes, she simply seemed like grass by the roadside.

She was a woman of sanguine temperament. She loved to engage in debates alongside scholars and chirped like a sparrow. During F Painter's lectures on aesthetics, her outrageous questions often left the disciples wide-eyed. One day, while the painter was lecturing about "simple beauty" and "compositional beauty," Aki-ko suddenly blushed and called out to the teacher. 

"Teacher, you said I'm beautiful. But even if I lack 'expression' in my face, am I still a beauty? Please evaluate." 

The painter couldn't help but chuckle at her question. 

"You're a mischievous one, be quiet." 

"Please evaluate my beauty." 

"Stay quiet!" 

"I don't want to. Please evaluate me!"  

Like a stubborn child, her eyes widened, glistening with tears. 

"Even if you had expression, you wouldn't be a beauty." 

With this blunt answer, the painter caused her to burst into laughter. 

After the lecture ended and we were all leaving, she exclaimed loudly enough for him to hear, "Hmph! Teacher said I'm not beautiful." 

As he was leaving, the painter stopped and looked back at her as she snickered. 

Until that day, I had no particular curiosity about her, but I couldn't help but reflect on that moment alone, smiling to myself.

About ten days later, filled with immense ambition to become a literary figure, I was rummaging through books at an old bookstore in Kanda one evening. 

"Hey, hey!" 

Someone called to me. I thought it was the shopkeeper calling for their husband and continued searching through the books. 

"Hey!"  

With a sudden pull on my sleeve, I turned to see Aki-ko standing there. 

"You must be Korean, right?" 

What was this girl saying? I was suddenly filled with a combative spirit and merely shrugged her off, going back to a different book. 

"Se-eh?"  

"What?"  

"Se-eh?"  

"What does that mean?"  

"Are you Korean? Se-eh?"  

"What's that?"  

"Hey! You're Korean, right?"  

"Yeah."  

"Let’s go."  

She seemed to have a conviction that I would follow her, as she turned and left the store. 

Sure enough, I put the books back on the shelf and followed her almost instinctively. 

Thus began our strange and peculiar relationship.

However, from that point on, our deteriorating relationship was oddly enchanting. 

We would often look at each other with cold animosity, yet unexpectedly charge towards each other, passionately kissing each other’s lips. When our lips finally met, it felt as if we were fiercely devouring each other like starved beings. However, this passion would yield to a displeasure between us immediately afterwards. 

"Fatty! Pig!"  

"You snob!"  

"You fool!"  

"Slob!"  

"Care for another kiss?"  

"I don’t want to! It’s disgusting!"  

"You’re the disgusting one."  

After hurling insults at each other, we would part ways, deeply discontented, without even saying goodbye. 

When I returned home after such exhausting encounters, it felt as if I were drowning in opium-like fatigue, overwhelmed by thoughts of Aki-ko, resolving that I would never meet her again. Yet, the next clear day at the painter’s house, upon seeing her again, as she walked by and softly said, "Follow me," my body instinctively followed against all reasoning. 

What followed was yet another strange and suffocating moment of passion, then a cycle of boredom and hate.

At the painter's house, she completely ignored me. 

Ah! Her loud, hysterical laughter resembled that of a turkey and surprised both F Painter and the other disciples, but she never even glanced my way. 

Occasionally, when she happened to glance in my direction, she offered a polite apology with a laugh. 

All the disciples tried to win Aki-ko's favor, and she, in return, seemed to satisfy their desires. 

"Your face is quite nice. I’d love to paint you."  

"You said you think I'm beautiful? When can I repay that compliment?"  

"Which lucky girl will get to be your wife?"  

"I’d like to visit your home once."  

Such remarks made all the disciples overly eager. When I witnessed such things and heard such words, it filled my heart with a strange pride, as if I were cherishing a prized possession all to myself. 

Then, when I met Aki-ko alone, I would tease her. 

"That XX is smitten!"  

"Smitten? I’m already doing that!"  

"Yucky! Vulgar!"  

"Ha, ah, ah, ah, ah."  

Then, again, eyes filled with hatred would meet. Afterwards we would ignite passionate kisses again. It was an exhilarating tension that suffocated me, only to be followed again by hatred, insults, and separations. Each night ended in unpleasantness. 

This was how my relationship with her unfolded for three months. 

During the peculiar sensations in the lives of students in Tokyo, February 1919 marked a particular day. After staying up all night, I said goodbye to Johan at our boarding house. Not returning to my hovel, I went to F Painter’s house. 

That day marked the second day of the model session. However, the scheduled model could not come due to a temporary issue. 

"Don't worry."  

"Hey."  

"Damn model."  

Such remarks echoed around the room. 

As things dragged on, Aki-ko rushed in, saying she would be the model, stripping off her clothes and climbing onto the model stand. Everyone was dumbfounded and unable to pick up their brushes. F Painter was usually unflappable but seemed at a loss, simply staring at the stand. 

However, knowing her character, I was not surprised at all. This was precisely what Aki-ko’s character would prompt her to do. So I turn to a friend nearby and say, "Hey, that’s a good pig. It would taste good once made into ham."  

Hearing that, my friend gave me a look as if his sacred honor had been violated, turning his head away in anger. However, the outcome of my remark was unexpectedly profound. 

While posing on the model stand, Aki-ko, glistening and bright, boldly came down and stood right before me at my table. 

"What did you think I was, a class of freaks?"  

I was left speechless at her exuberance. I couldn’t respond, so I simply gazed at her, clueless about her real intent. 

"Morning Star! Mountain Monkey!"  

"What?"  

I too was just as taken aback. 

At that moment, my wrath rendered me blind. Unable to control my impulse, I punched her bare breast and shouted all sorts of insults about how barbaric Koreans are, then grabbed my hat and stormed out of her place. 

After that, I never returned. In this way, at the beginning of March, I hastily returned home, and by the end of March, I was arrested under the pretext of violating publishing law. 

For three months in jail, my thoughts were strangely entangled, roaming around her figure. I recalled her plump body, her shining eyes, and her unique laugh. I thought of her soft breaths and the details of her fingers and nails, which haunted my dreams, awakening me to long sighs. 

Finally, on June 20th, I was released under the judgment of "six months in prison with a two-year probation." Ignoring the doctor's advice to stabilize, I left for Tokyo again in mid-July. My only resolve was to see Aki-ko one more time. However, upon my return to Tokyo, disappointment awaited me. Aki-ko had moved, and it was said she no longer visited F Painter. How would I find her in this Tokyo? 

Yet, emboldened by an unrelenting pride and self-confidence, I resolved to track her down. My mind was set on finding her. I sought sustenance, feeling an overwhelming longing for her ample physique. Thus, carrying Aki-ko's surname as my only hope, I decided to visit every post office and police station in the area. At that time, I was based in Taeryongdo, and I showed up briefly at the headquarters in Kuku-machi, spending the rest of my time visiting the various police stations. 

On the eighth day, while at Shibuya, I finally found her address. Yet, when I called, the encounter was nothing out of the ordinary. 

"You are a Korean, right? I am Japanese.”  

Only that chilling line remained. 

That night, I returned home. 

From that point on, my relationship with her was entirely severed. However, for many years after, I would occasionally dream of her luscious body, experiencing sexual excitement in my sleep. Sometimes, when I saw a woman with plump cheeks, I would remember Aki-ko and think, perhaps I would meet her again. 

Thus, seven years passed, and in the summer of 1926, my second bout of debauchery began, wasting my days with liquor and women. One night, still dazed from the previous night’s drunkenness, I had to visit a jewelry shop in the new city. After completing my errands, I headed back up when I spotted a woman with plump cheeks approaching from the right. 

"Is that Aki-ko!" 

Surprised in my semi-drunken state, I called out, and she too stood across from me. 

"Oh!" 

"Long time."  

It was indeed Aki-ko. The Japanese man accompanying her glanced at me curiously before walking away. 

"What brings you this far?"  

"My hometown's very far, Pyongyang." 

"Yes."  

"I am—"  

She was panting, already gesturing towards her companion from earlier. 

"Just traveling?"  

"Are you on your honeymoon?" 

She blushed and giggled. 

"Congratulations."  

"By the way,"  

"What?"  

"Let's hang out!"  

"Sure."  

"It’s at 柳屋ホテル; please wait for me there tonight."  

"I’m heading to 安東縣 (Andong) now, so I can’t drop by."  

I didn’t even understand what I was saying. 

"安東縣? Are you staying at the Andong Hotel?"  

"安東호텔."  

"We're also heading to Andong tonight, so please wait there. I promise I’ll come! Please!"  

"Alright."  

With that, we parted, and I couldn’t tell how I spent the rest of that day. When I regained my senses, it was already twilight, and I found myself at the 大同橋 (Daedong Bridge), lost in thought, gazing at the rushing waters of the river. 

After that, I never saw her again. Yet, her voluptuous body and peculiar laughter still occasionally take my breath away, stirring memories of excitement within me. 

She will always remain one of the women who can never be forgotten in my life.

**Summary**  

This narrative reveals the complicated emotions and interactions between the narrator and Aki-ko, set against the backdrop of colonial Korea in the early 20th century. Their tumultuous relationship alternates between passionate encounters and intense disdain, showcasing the conflicted nature of desire interwoven with societal constraints. Historians might find it interesting as it reflects the broader themes of identity, cultural clash, and gender dynamics in colonial Korea, highlighting how personal relationships were influenced by the pressures of nationality and ethnicity during this period.
