Long Story Talk, The Strongman of the Vast Sea (Episode 7)  

As Commander Pitong was placed under the command of a lower official, his appearance was quite humble, yet he could adeptly inspect the celestial phenomena.  

General Yeowonsoo, while discussing the achievements in the battle of Hamapyeong with Commander Dorawa and the soldiers, spoke to General Hongcheonbi saying, "The great victory we achieved today in the Hamapyeong fight was solely due to the power of divination; how could the military commander foresee the snow to come and prepare for it?"  

Hongcheonbi replied, "That was not the merit of the commander's capability but indeed of the man named Pitong, who claimed to be suitable to lead an army and sought the general's view. When I placed him under my command to observe his strategy, he accurately predicted today at four o'clock an immense snowfall would occur, and upon hearing this, he prepared for it, eventually leading to the success you see."  

Yeowonsoo then asked Labor Taek to inquire, "Since Cheokju is the hometown of General No, has the general heard before of a man named Pitong?"  

Labor T'aek responded, "There is a person named Pitong from the rocky area of Gu, also known as Commander of Shoes. He is said to have the ability to foresee the celestial phenomena, and is also called San-gok Cheon-un (Mountain Valley Heaven's Luck), but I do not know of his true talents or achievements."  

Yeowonsoo remarked, "Though some call him Commander of Shoes, considering today's achievements, he seems worthy of being called the Commander of Dragons."  

When Pitong was called in, he was indeed of an unimpressive demeanor and spoke with a very shabby tone, making Yeowonsoo hesitate in his heart, and he decided to merely appoint him as an aide. Pitong then said, "An aide is akin to a mere figure; how can I demonstrate my talents while being in that position?" He refused the position and prepared to leave when Hongcheonbi spoke, "One ought to employ people according to their abilities, not by virtue of their appearance or speech; though Pitong’s looks may be shabby and his words flimsy, in his heart he houses the storms and calmness. Today’s success in the Hamapyeong strategy is entirely due to his strength and cunning, so I implore you, General, to employ Pitong as a commander and test his wisdom."  

Thus Yeowonsoo, heeding this advice, appointed him as a commander, but all the generals were skeptical.  

Yeowonsoo’s accomplishments were already remarkable throughout the land, yet the traitors continued to escort the enemy general's remains.  

General Yeowonsoo captured the enemy general Wonirang and Hujang and housed them amongst the troops, beheading Wonirang and Hujang to report his victory to the Changheiji. The King of Changhae was astonished and summoned his ministers to discuss, saying, "I knew that Seobok would surely capture Yeobang, yet Seobok ended up defeated by Yeobang, losing not only his life but also his children. Surely, he will harbor resentment towards me; what misfortune might arise again? How shall we navigate this matter?"  

At this, Dojeongjak replied, "Though Yeobang has won this time, it was merely by happenstance, and how could he withstand Seobok’s elite forces? If Yeobang were to face defeat again, the repercussions on the court will be inevitable, so I suggest Your Majesty issue an edict to the people, calling for volunteers for army duties and to detain the families of the troops while disbanding the army; this would be the only means to mitigate Seobok's grievances."  

The Minister of Rites, Eunroe, remarked, "In my view, since the heroes of the rivers and lakes have thrown themselves towards Yeobang, how can we disregard their families? If we follow the advice of Dojeongjak, we will invite resentment from Yeobang. Thus, Your Majesty should personally visit Yeobang’s battlefield to commend his achievements and comfort the soldiers while simultaneously retrieving the heads of Wonirang and Hujang to send to Seobok by due courtesy."  

The King of Changhae accepted this proposal and immediately advanced towards Yeobang’s camp, personally visiting all the soldiers and subsequently recovering the heads of Dorawa and Wonirang, replacing their bodies with incense for preservation, wrapping them in silk, and assigning Eunroe as envoy to deliver them to Soebok of the western region.  

Pitong imposed strict discipline in his military, such that not even a single soldier disobeyed the military orders.  

When Pitong advanced into the military administration, among all the generals, only Noyaksoo did not obey the orders. The military was laying down commands while imposing strict prohibitions against eating meat and drinking alcohol, preventing arguments, and mandating that no one stray from their post and warning sternly that any violation would face military punishment.  

On that day, Noyaksoo, unable to resist the temptation of alcohol, headed out to a tavern for a drink. Fearing that there would be a breach of military discipline, the tavern keeper refused to serve him any liquor. Noyaksoo, binding the proprietor, hung him by the neck and took a jug to drink, consuming vigorously before returning to the camp while calculating how many drink purchases he made. After getting heavily intoxicated, the soldiers of the military administration apprehended Noyaksoo and brought him to the military authorities, where Pitong commanded, "Noyaksoo has violated five military regulations; he shall be punished per military law." At this moment, Yeowonsoo, overhearing this statement, was extraordinarily shocked and dashed forward to seize the sword of the soldier, attempting to stop Pitong.  

Pitong stated, "This sentence is not directed at you, but rather a due punishment from Heaven itself, so why do you plead?"  

Yeowonsoo responded, "I too had briefly strayed from my post thus I also violated military law, so I request you grant me the same punishment as Noyaksoo." At last, unable to proceed any further, Pitong then forgave Noyaksoo, and henceforth all the generals thereafter approached military protocol with utmost caution, fearing violations would not be tolerated.  

The traitors schemed and transported the remains of Seobok’s children, while Commander Pitong ordered his soldiers to insult the remains.  

Commander Pitong summoned Noyaksoo and Huhbudong, instructing them, "General Noyaksoo, lead several dozen elite troops southwards for ten miles, where there is a grove called Chuja; lie in ambush there and when a single funeral procession passes by, capture the people escorting it. General Huhbudong, head eastward for ten miles to a ravine known as Yeoji-dong, where two long-tailed monkeys will be perched on a gnarled tree; capture them as they are."  

Thus, the two generals heeded the orders and each moved towards their respective locations.   

Noyaksoo, lying in wait at Chuja, saw indeed at around midnight when the moonlight faintly illuminated the road, a man in a white robe escorting a funeral procession. Noyaksoo rushed forward to block the path, and the man scolded him, "Hey, how dare you, ignorant bandit, block the procession?"  

Noyaksoo replied, "We are not bandits, but rather the troops of General Pitong, just patrolling; if we see a mouse, we catch it, if we see a bird, we capture it; how would we know what a funeral procession is?"  

The man inquired, "Who is your commander?"  

Noyaksoo replied, "Do you not know General Yeowonsoo’s soldier, Pitong?"  

The man declared, "I am Yunroe, envoy of Changhae King. I carry a royal order to the Yusan kingdom, and you have no business here." He attempted to withdraw when Noyaksoo insisted, "Such words are not for you to say; speak to our commander," and seized the funeral procession.  

Yunroe then ordered the funeral procession to strike Noyaksoo, however suddenly a commanding shout erupted, and dozens of elite troops who had been lying in wait rushed forward, securing Yunroe with ropes as they seized the funeral procession back. At this moment, Huhbudong had captured two monkeys at Yeoji-dong.  

When Commander Pitong saw the coffin carried from the funeral procession, he recognized the bodies as those of Seobok’s children — Wonirang and Hujang — and seeing that incense was used to replace the bodies, he discarded the incense and exchanged them for the monkey bodies caught at Yeoji-dong, preserved them, and sent the coffin back to Changhae. This was an announcement for Seobok, containing a eulogy, which stated, "The King of Changhae Yu-pi retains the memorial service. Respectfully presenting the good fortune, the grievous misfortune struck the honorable family of the great men, Yeongnam Wonirang and Yeongae Hujang, both sacrificed for the kingdom’s duties; even the new immortal phoenix weep and the paralleled ducks shed tears. In misfortune, I have connected the rope of alliance, and upon the field, the oppositions stand firm; knowing that Yeobang would perish by the King's blade was my contemplation, yet fate turned the tables and unexpectedly, Yeobang brought the heads of our honored men, which is also surprising and lamentable. Please take care with the provided incense that remains to be escorted here."  

The soldiers conveyed this eulogy, and upon reading it, the proclamation “Seobok” altered "Seojin-in" to "Seosja" and "Wonirang" and "Hujang" were replaced with "The Long-tailed Monkey Won" and "The Small-tailed Monkey Hu", sending the modified proclamation back, apologizing, "Our ignorant troops, not understanding the royal order regarding the funeral procession, mistakenly engaged in egregious behavior; please forgive us."  

Yunroe subtly queried, "Did Yeowonsoo know of this incident?"  

They replied, "No, he did not."  

Yunroe further stated, "If this matter leaks, it could be troublesome for our national principles; the soldiers must keep quiet; I will also refrain from speaking."  

Thus he hurriedly prompted the return to Soebok’s household.  

After Seobok was defeated, his wife Wa Seokjin and children, having died and been captured, lamented night and day in sorrow. Then Yunroe finally returned with the funeral of Wonirang and Hujang, hastily opened the coffin to see the body parts, only to find the replacement body of a monkey where the remains were supposed to be.  

**Summary**  

This excerpt from the long story portrays the significant tactical developments and moral dilemmas faced by the military commanders following a recent battle. Central to the narrative are themes of loyalty, the burdens of leadership, and the fine line between duty and honor. Notably, the depiction of Pitong's character as a cunning yet disregarded leader reflects the complexities of loyalty and respect amid colonial hierarchy, which may intrigue historians examining the period's socio-political climate and military structures.
