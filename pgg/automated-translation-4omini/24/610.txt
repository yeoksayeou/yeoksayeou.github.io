The Hours of Korean Folk Songs  
						An Ja-san  
『One』  

Folk songs, commonly referred to as wind songs or rustic songs, possess a simple charm when compared to other songs, serving as the roots of music and the foundation of literature. According to ancient definitions, in Korean poetry, the verses used for singing are termed songs, while the ones not meant for singing are called ‘yals’ (謠). In the 'Er Ya' (爾雅), a 'tuge' (徒歌) refers to songs that aren’t accompanied by musical instruments. Both of these definitions seem plausible, and more aptly put, the term 'yals' signifies non-artistic folk songs that originated from pure, unadulterated poetry. This emergence, particularly lacking textual documentation, relied heavily on music to gain existence. Therefore, the birth of folk songs was a direct expression of genuine human observation and feelings, manifested through oral tradition, and the materials therein reflect a sincere portrayal of the genuine life essence.

Thus, folk songs reflect the emotional states of the people's lives, and as such, express the sentiments of the general populace without artifice.  

『Two』  

In essence, folk songs have no textual transmission from the start. Therefore, it is evident why their lyrics have not been inherited. For this reason, discussing ancient folk songs today is gravely challenging. However, upon examining the literary collections of ancient authors, one can find translations of popular songs of their time into Han poetry on several occasions. Collections like 'Tao Yin Ji,' 'Zhi Zhi Tang Ji,' 'Xiao Gao Ji,' and particularly the 'Bei Sai Za Yao' which assembles around forty folk songs from Hamgyeong-do serve as valuable references. Three years ago, I presented some findings related to this in the 18th issues of 'Arts and Literature' and have lectured on the subject multiple times since. Although I find it difficult to form a well-rounded conclusion based only on previously gathered materials, one can nonetheless obtain insights into their lineage.  

The emergence can be viewed from two aspects. One is derived from the natural sensation of life. Our sounds carry a natural rhythm and when these ideas triggered by everyday experiences are expressed through vocalization, they become songs or musical expressions. Such ideas emerge not only during labor but also in various contexts such as pastoral, maritime, agricultural, artisanal, and commercial activities where the materials of these songs are drawn.  

The Farmers' Song  
In February, the farmers cry for the cuckoo,  
The mud thaws, farmland is ready for plowing.  
The old man strong, holding the plow,  
His wife, the clothmaker, has no completed garment.  
The spring earheads are gradually turning yellow, new rice green,  
When neighbors meet, there’s no different chatter,  
The joy of harvest is both pleasing and pitiful,  
When a good year arrives, marriage is discussed.  
The customs of farming families are true,  
The poem belongs to the fields in the folds of their sleeves.

Thus, folk songs bloom vividly, manifesting diverse emotions. Within these, some songs sadly lament life’s difficulties, while others express a serene joy in natural blessings. The second aspect arises from the sentiment of social conditions. Human activities prompt reflections on changing times and communal actions leading to an influx of emotional responses, thus creating songs with intellectual factors, many of which relay factual subjects. Their nature inherently embodies both joy and sorrow, and their compositions may arise from occasional creations that gain popularity, largely due to their alignment with popular sentiments and material from the era.

Occasionally emerging folk songs may also be disseminated through the unconscious of children, and regarding these folk songs’ trends, some observe them as mystical symbols of fortune or misfortune through the delusions of the rulers. Such is the case with the children's song collection documented in the literature of section 11.  

『Three』  

However, the first aspect results in the folk songs becoming fragmented as they are transmitted and lost across time, with their artistic value being of a different kind. The second aspect refers to the songs that have been incorporated into the music realm, thus becoming artful melodies, as ancient music often derived from rural folk songs. In Silla music,

Baeksil      Gunyang-gun Music

these evolved to become palace music, originally compositions enjoyed by the local populace (refer to the Samguk Sagi, chapter 32). The songs of Goguryeo handed down to Goryeo were typical compositions by people of Yeongsan. Meanwhile, those from Baekje were rustic songs from the communities of Gwangju, and Goryeo folk songs originated from Hanseong province, including songs from Yangju and Chungju that lamented the burdens of taxation. Hence several songs, originally the work of rural people, developed into famous compositions within the music community, thus showcasing a distinct value. Therefore, ancient perceptions of folk songs fall into three categories: being viewed mystically, perceived as rustic sounds, or valued as authentic sentiments.  

『Four』  

Currently, folk songs are thriving, and the academic community is paying significant attention to them, thus many magazines display explicit folk songs, and for general entertainment at gatherings, singing these songs has become common. Observing today’s development of folk songs, three factors appear apparent. Firstly, the collective sentiment of the time is tumultuous, and high culture is not recognized. Additionally, the arts and music have traditionally been professional, rendering ordinary society mostly unversed in them. Therefore, they rely solely on the folk songs that are self-sustained within rural areas. Meanwhile, the younger generation does not encounter the arts of the previous generation; the pursuit of reform and change is evident, resulting in the proliferation of folk songs.  

Secondly, with naturalism and equality ideologies intersecting, public sentiment tends to favor directness and simplicity, regarding high cultural endeavors as counterfeit, false, or oppressive. Thus the straightforward and commonplace are appreciated, manifesting in literature and music as popular folk songs.  

Thirdly, there is a modern appreciation for folk songs stemming from sincere affection. Earlier literature was predominantly associated with classical Chinese texts, causing the Korean language to be largely marginalized, and our nation found itself with scant written art. Recent historical records found no instances of distinct Korean responses associated with Chinese writings. The ‘Goryeo History’ music volumes make it clear that texts in Korean were disparaged and considered vulgar, leading to a reduction of literary activity in Korean. For these reasons, we should hold our national literature, which embodies our essence, in high regard. This sentiment has resulted in a collective cultural awakening, thus manifesting a significant rise in the influence of folk songs.  

Thus, the protracted influence of classical Chinese texts must be addressed, while concurrently birthing a new culture that harmonizes with local customs, comprehensively revamping the societal fabric begins with a careful review of folk songs. In recent times, literary figures have taken to disregarding past literary traditions, dismissing classical literature entirely in favor of modern works, tending additionally to meld into foreign artistic forms. To fundamentally realign this, a rigorous examination of folk songs becomes utterly essential. Hence, I humbly accept the responsibility of exploring folk songs within my work. However, I aim to approach this with a cool disposition in my studies, striving to academically uncover the value of folk songs.  

『Five』  

What then is the current number of existing folk songs? If we consider the amalgamation of various colors and degrees, we find it difficult to make an accurate assessment, yet even at a great intellectual scale, it merely amounts to roughly hundreds. (The musical scores are published by Lee Sang-jun, whose contributions are considerable.)  

Nongbutaryeong 	Bangataeryeong 	Jangtaeryeong 	Hungtaeryeong  

Santaryeong 	Multaryeong 	Gaetaeryeong 	Saetaryeong  

These are certainly the well-known examples. However, previously, I would like to grasp the significance of the suffix ‘taeryeong’. Traditionally, it appears there have been few scholars discussing folk songs in depth, and precise terminology eludes our grasp. Therefore, while the term ‘taeryeong’ may seem related to the music of the ‘Gyongsan Meeting’, the evidence available is somewhat tenuous. Nonetheless, the associations with musical nomenclature can be drawn from historical accounts. If we delve into the records of ancient music as cited by Zhu Zi in the ‘Yuelu’ (朱子語類), section 93, we find references to ‘Dali’ (打令) as a term used by Tang people to categorize folk dances that entailed four actions, namely 'raise', 'shake', and 'send'.  

Following this, concerning the structure of folk songs, the rhythm employed is generally four-four time, forming eight-character phrases. This four-four time is a long-standing foundational motif of song lyrics, mimicking the natural ebb and flow of respiration, reflecting a primal simplicity inherent to folk songs.  

As for literary formats, they typically manifest in two varieties: one is extensive, while the other incorporates refrains, termed ‘korass’, creating a communal singing dynamic. These refrains often include delicate antitheses and unique metaphors, frequently utilizing exclamatory verbs, representing primitive ornamentations and showcasing natural development.  

『Six』  

From a third perspective on poetic imagery, the core of most folk songs revolves around entertainment. Hence, tales of romantic endeavors and witty banter abound; such content, at first glance, might suggest a lewd and indulgent folk culture. In reality, however, it stems from a pursuit of self-comfort and enjoyment derived from everyday living, free from material interests.  

The fourth exploration pertains to the musical expression of folk songs; how do their melodies resonate? It is generally embedded in a tone of sorrow. This is indicative of past statements relating to Buddhist influences; should we again expound upon the nuances of this sorrowful tone, we might reveal a reflection of class oppression experienced by the lower strata. Historically, court music has been characterized by its inclination towards grandeur and exaltation of the accomplishments of past kings (i.e., ‘Yongbi Oga’), emphasizing ceremonial grandeur while village songs, having endured centuries of subjugation, resonate with unexpressed reactions against this societal oppression, revealing a pronounced contrast in expression.  

This absence of patriotic themes within Korean folk songs is particularly notable. While Italian folk songs occasionally touch upon similar themes, Korean compositions are significantly less so for the reasons previously mentioned. Of course, there are other contributing factors, yet the underlying class politics are paramount. Amid the transitional cultural milieu, folk songs may also experience an infusion of new vigor, with the emergence of fresh emotional expressions giving rise to new folk songs. In this context, the editorial staff of ‘Kaepungsa’ have engaged considerable effort, recently collecting over a thousand old folk songs to contribute towards new research, and their contributions are expected to be noteworthy in the coming times.  

 

**Summary**  

This essay explores the origins, evolution, and significance of Korean folk songs, arguing that these compositions are rich in genuine human sentiment and serve as reflections of everyday life. Analyzing traditional forms and social context, the author notes that contemporary folk songs are thriving due to the people’s need for authentic expression amidst societal change. Interestingly, the article highlights how colonial influences and past literary biases against the Korean language have marginalized folk expressions, emphasizing the need for a cultural revival rooted in the people’s own traditions. Thus, historians may find significant insights into the dynamics of culture and identity during the colonial period, particularly in how folk songs serve as a mirror to the struggles and aspirations of the era.

『四』

금일에 잇서는 민요가 盛豊히 유행하며 학계에서는 그를 중요시하야 염탐하는 時色이 심하나니 고로 잡지 등에는 뻔이떠케 鄕曲의 俗歌를 게재하며 일반 연회의 여흥에는 謠曲을 唱함이 恒茶飯이라. 觀컨대 금일의 민요 발달의 源因은 三條의 눈치가 뵈는 듯하다. 一은 시대적 민심이 擾亂하야 고상한 오락을 취할 줄 모른다. 加于에 음악 문학은 自來로 전문적이든 것인바 일반 사회에서는 보통으로 알 수 업든 길이라. 고로 오직 촌락에서 자급자족하야 스스로 興行하든 歌樂을 취할 밧게 업스며 신진인사는 前日의 예술에는 聞見이 업서 巽方이 되매 더퍼노코 改舊革新의 사상이 잇는지라 그런 여러 가지 사정으로 인하야 민요가 왓작 盛行한 것이라. 二는 자연주의와 평등사상이 겻드려 수입되는 바람에 인심은 모도 그리로 趨向되야 체면과 高等의 事는 도트러 假飾 虛僞 또는 壓迫的으로 對視하는지라. 고로 順直이오 本心이오 평범한 것을 同情하매 文學 及 歌樂도 서민 공통의 謠曲을 만드려 耽翫하는 모양이라. 三은 愛本心으로 우러나와 민요를 貴愛하는 時色이 잇다. 前日의 문학이란 것은 전혀 한문을 爲主하고 정작 朝鮮語 朝鮮文으로 된 것은 自鄙自棄하야 고만 無文學의 민족이 되엿는지라. 年來 史籍을 들처볼진대 한문에 對隻하는 讐仇¿ƒ이 안이 날 수 업다. 高麗史 樂志에 보면 朝鮮語로 된 것은 鄙俚타 하야 말살한 글발이 두렷하며 成宗 實錄 19年條 李世佐의 上啓에도
御正殿臨群臣時用俚語於事體何如... 今妓工狃於積習舍正樂而好淫樂甚爲未便一應俚語請皆勿習
라 하얏스니 금일 조선문학 조선음악이 衰殘한 것은 彼 한문의 중독이라. 是以로 吾人은 我 民族의 本精神 되는 문학을 爲重함이 可하다. 이런 사상은 擧民族 일치로 되는 결과 민요의 聲勢는 크게 발흥하게 된지라.
따는 그러하다. 우리는 日久月深에 매처 잇는 漢文奴를 贖良도 할지오 新文化를 開創함에 就하야 因勢上으로 民風을 廉探하기는 歌謠를 收取함이 가장 緊할지라. 더욱 近者의 文藝家는 前日 漢學者 버릇으로 舊文學을 蔑視하고 新文만 爲主하야 덧읍시 外國 文套에 동화하야 가는 爻象이라. 이것들을 근본적으로 頓整하자면 未嘗不 민요를 詳審함이 單罷榜이라 할지라. 그래서 나도 민요를 講考하는 生意를 마지 안이하는 바라. 그러나 余는 時方 그런 感情的을 품고 말하는 것보다 거시키 冷情한 태도로써 연구를 시험하야 민요의 가치를 학술적으로 엿보고자 하는 바라.

『五』＜147＞

대관절 현행하는 민요의 수효는 얼마나 되나뇨. 各色의 雜同散異를 모도 몰면 限量을 못 차리되 大頭腦만 잡드라도 一端 百의 數로만 되지 안는다. (其 曲譜는 李尙俊 氏의 發行이 잇스매 其 李氏의 功은 多大하다 하노라.)
농부타령        방아타령        장타령        흥타령

산타령          물타령          개타령        새타령

등은 아모라도 일상 熟聞하는 게라. 그런데 于先 其 接尾 名詞인 打令 二字가 무엇인지 이것을 알고 십다. 自來 歌謠를 論講한 者이 희소하고 昭詳한 어휘가 업슴으로 其 語源과 其 出處를 알기가 索莫하다. 거시키 靈山會相曲이란 樂曲에 타령이란 樂節이 잇지마는 여긔다가 證據대기는 너무 淺近하다. 그러나 이것이 樂曲名과 관계됨은 이로써 짐작키 용이하다. 고로 次漸次漸 古人의 樂說을 뒤저보면 朱子 語類 卷九十三
唐人俗舞謂之打令其狀有四曰招曰搖曰送其一紀不得盖招則激之意搖則搖手呼喚之意送者送酒之意云云
라 하고 그 뿐 外라 經世大訓에도 亦如是 이 말이 적혀 잇스니 이 朱熹의 기록에 憑準하면 타령은 즉 唐人 民俗의 舞蹈曲名이 분명하다. 고로 타령의 유래는 필시 고려 登內에 唐의 舞樂을 만히 수입하든 次 其時에 음악과 眼同하야 其 名詞까지 수입된 듯 짐작하노라.
於其次에 민요의 형식을 말할진대 音調의 數律은 매양 四四調로서 八字를 一句로 구성한지라 이 四四調는 古來 歌詩의 元套 丹骨로 쓴 것이니 그의 그 투는 생리상 호흡의 맥박을 準的하야 自然한 强弱을 되푸리 繰返한 것이니 이것이 제일 순수하게 나온 민요의 本色이라 할지라. 文章의 體裁는 二種으로 되니 一은 長篇으로 된 것이오 他一을 後廉을 入하야 「코러쓰」를 作하니 이는 社交的 唱和로 된 것이라. 이 後廉은 미묘한 對句와 奇絶한 比喩도 잇스되 매양 感動詞를 反復的으로 用함이 통례니 이는 원시적 修飾法으로서 自然한 발달을 作한 것이러라.

『六』

第三次 詩想으로 볼진대 일반 민요의 內脈은 娛樂的으로 中心하다. 고로 남녀의 情事 戱弄의 才談 등이 車載斗量이라. 이는 얼핏 짐작하면 淫蕩放逸의 民風이 잇게시리 보인다. 그러나 其實은 딴판이니 인민은 아모 利慾 업시 자연생활의 여가를 乘하야 自樂的 安慰를 求함에서 발생한 口氣라 하노라.
第四次로 音樂的 表白 즉 민요의 聲調는 엇더하뇨. 이는 通히 悲哀의 韻을 띄여 잇는 바 그 이유는 去番에(別乾坤 前前番號) 說破한 것이라. 즉 悲哀調된 裏許는 대체 불교의 영향으로 된 것이라 하얏스되 한 번 다시 그 悲哀調의 結晶을 分釋하면 계급정치의 被壓迫되던 反影의 分子도 비벼 잇는지라. 본래 宮廷音樂은 榮慾的으로 되매 先王의 建國해 준 功德을 讚頌하고(龍飛御天歌類) 자기의 威信을 위하야 장엄함을 治裝하야 음악을 形式的 又 道德的으로 團束햇지만은 下民은 천 년간 굴복하야 向上發達을 피치 못하매 黙새긴 白活 本能的한 反感이 不言之中에 發動하야 그것이 일반 音調에 골아 나으니 이런 이유는 참말로 歇后하게 칠 것이 안이라 하는 바로다.
이 때문에 조선민요에는 愛國에 관한 語調라곤 一言半句가 업다. 伊太利 歌謠도 그러치만 조선은 上에 말한 이유로 인하야 더욱 볼 것 업다. 물론 딴 관계도 잇지만은 계급정치의 빌미가 중요한 頉이러라. 時方 新舊文化가 갈마드는 틈에 잇서 민요도 新氣運이 나스겟고 더욱 層生疊出한 감정의 폭발은 새 민요를 産出할 要代가 된지라. 玆에 開闢社 編輯子는 工力을 드려 이것을 注意할 새 벌서 천여 편의 舊民謠를 수집하야 新硏究에 滲着한다 하니 將次 그의 功獻이 적지 안이할 줄로 짐작하노라.＜148＞
