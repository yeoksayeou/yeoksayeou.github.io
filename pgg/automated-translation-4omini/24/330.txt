Seollal in Korea! The Celebrating Korea!, Proposal to Abolish the Double New Year  
Chunpa  

It has been nearly three weeks since the New Year, exactly three weeks and four days. I am still wearing the clothes I put on for the New Year, and a couple of the New Year gifts I bought are still lingering about. Yet, I cannot seem to give up the habit of sending New Year’s greetings, and though I haven’t completely transitioned yet, there are still memories and lingering scents from the New Year’s gathering that have yet to fade. The evidence of the past celebration is quite clear and undeniable, as this is not a dream but indeed a reality.  

However, despite this, it seems that not only my children and wife but also the female traders in our neighborhood are still ardently discussing and debating how to celebrate the New Year, deciding what festive attire to buy, and even commenting on the fillings of dumplings to the point of distraction. It’s puzzling to understand why this is happening. While there is clear evidence of the previous festivities, it feels as if we have to celebrate again just one short day later. Is time rushing ahead or are we caught in a dream? It is truly incomprehensible.  

Is it just my family that is caught in this dream? Looking around, my neighbors appear to be the same. Regardless of their size, whether it is next door, at the back, or the large household, the entire northern area is proceeding in this manner. Upon further inspection, it seems that all across Korea, families are in a similar state. Children, elderly, and everyone in between are busy preparing for the New Year. Some people are skipping meals to focus on the celebrations, while others seem to be weeping and consumed with joy. This is truly a peculiar sight if taken in context of Korea.  

Certainly, similar occurrences can be seen in China, Japan, or Russia, but it appears especially pronounced in Korea.  

Regardless of any concept of fortune, Korea has an abundance of festivals. There are New Year, Full Moon festivals, Hansik, Samjil, Dano, Sambok Chilseok, Chuseok, Guhil, and Dongji Day, comprising quite a few national celebrations. Among these, however, the New Year is uniquely prominent.  

A clever friend might say, “How can one contend there are many when there are only two New Years in a year?” but in Korea, there indeed seems to be many. This leads to the issue at hand.  

In addition to the solar New Year and the lunar New Year, there are Korean New Year and Japanese New Year, as well as multiple days of significance tied to various customs, such as those for commoners, aristocrats, men, women, children, and elderly, even including Kkachisul and Saengjisul.  

How numerous they are! However, this is merely a matter of nomenclature. There being so many means it is also counted as such. Clearly, the overlapping folkways create confusion, with these four distinct concepts being entangled within both the minds of the common people and in customs. However, despite the many holidays, the actual celebrations seem negligible.  

In the end, the current Korea holds no grand festival for the entire nation to revel in. On January 1, under the name of the solar New Year, the majority of the old customs are swept away, and very few of the new wave even recognize the lunar New Year. When it comes to celebrations like Dano or Chuseok, is there still room for discussion about minor festivities during the year? In conclusion, Korea lacks any substantial celebration.  

There are not yet any grand festivals for the nation to come together and celebrate. Is this a form of misfortune? It perhaps points to national impotence and inefficacy. Can there be a nation that does not celebrate even a single festival? How painful and profound such a struggle is!  

The power of habit is indeed formidable. I alone among my family of seven observe the solar New Year. After years of hearing about it for 25 years, I stand as the only one truly embracing it. How fearful and tough such a fact is! If one does not know another’s customs, one should observe their own. From the realities of my family, I can understand all of Korea. I first heard about the solar calendar 25 years ago, and used the lunar and solar calendars intermixed about 20 years ago. (My parents and relatives likely have also started this practice at that time.) However, I have only celebrated the New Year surely according to the lunar calendar for about ten years now. This too is exceptional, as my entire family is still inclined toward giving significance to the lunar New Year. Among my family of seven, I alone experiencing the transition to solar means it should reflect the general circumstances of Korea! Truly, the power of habit is fearsome! Hence, Korea remains without a grand national celebration, which could be called a transitional period.  

The overlapping celebrations between the solar and lunar cycle! For those ignorant, they may not understand, but contemplating it reveals an immense burden. Indeed, the entire nation bears a considerable loss. Setting aside the tangible economic damages and pains, intellectual hardships and precarious psychological states are present.  

For both the solar and lunar, the annual cycle does not yield even a single day for the entire nation to rejoice! Is there even such a sadness? Some may even regard the double taxation as incidental, yet just how many losses follow suit!  

Let us dismiss worries. Let us unify under one calendar, whether it be solar or lunar. Then as a whole nation, let us create a festival of immense joy. We might overcome the losses and pains of the past. Whether it is a solar or lunar celebration, where we ultimately land with these customs is merely a matter of judgment. However, these people have little pathway to that end. It is imperative to organize a movement to abolish double New Year celebrations. We must do so for the sake of our national dignity!  

  

**Summary**  

This editorial discusses the peculiar situation in Korea surrounding the celebrations of the New Year, expressing concern over the excessive number of holidays and the confusion they cause. The author critiques the habit of celebrating both solar and lunar New Years, suggesting that it leads to a lack of unified celebration for the entire nation. The article reflects the internal struggles of Koreans during a colonial period and sheds light on themes of national identity and cultural confusion, potentially interesting historians exploring the socio-cultural dynamics during this time of Japanese rule.
