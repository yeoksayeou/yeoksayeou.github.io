Tug-of-War, The Korean New Year’s Festivity  

In the warm winter of the southern country and among simple farmers, aside from the local folk song “Kwaijina Chinchinna,” the deep and savory atmosphere of tug-of-war cannot be compared. 

During long nights, when the sharp winter moon shines alone by the mirror, the young men who have caught the fish gather inside their room, and with the cheerful and earnest dialect of the Lingnam region, they gather to tell folktales that swell in their hearts, and it cannot help but to bring up grievances that cannot be suppressed at heart, and with overflowing strength in their arms, they throw aside all their worries, break open the door, and rush out into the alley, ready to test their youthful vitality in a tug-of-war. 

As the lunar December begins, groups of children start to engage in tug-of-war, reminiscent of playful sounds. Each group creates ropes from various colorful threads brought secretly from their homes, glancing at nothing but the teacher’s gaze. These ropes are made to be as thick as a strand of kelp (甘藍) or perhaps even less. They cheerfully call, “Wee-yotsawie! Wee-yotsawie!” with an innocent voice as they tug on the rope throughout the cold night. 

Even passersby comment, “Oh, the children are pulling the rope!” expressing astonishment at the fast passage of time, while the young unmarried men and farmers pause to watch, smiling slyly while also feeling a bit of envy as they secretly take part in the playful conflict. 

Thus, the rope that starts with children slowly grows larger and thicker, and eventually becomes a really large rope as the bright moon of the waning phase looms. However, the actual tug-of-war among the adult villagers only begins to take place after the lunar New Year and during the full moon festival. 

“Tonight, tug-of-war is happening at the upper village.” 

“I saw in the lower village that the children have a big rope that’s as thick as two people!” 

When the time comes, messages about the tug-of-war spread through each household, and at dawn children, each with 3-4 friends, take worn pots or pieces of wool from their homes and playfully strike them, shouting, 

“Please share some colorful threads!” 

“Please share some colorful threads!” 

As they visit each house, the welcoming young women smile cheerfully and when the children arrive, gather whatever leftover colorful threads and leftovers to hand over without hesitation. With both donated things and what has been collected, they find a wide place, like a vegetable garden or a playground, to set a fire, where farmers gather together to create threads and create the rope. 

While one neighborhood prepares the rope, another neighboring village is also secretly busy preparing their own. The size and amount of each rope is held as a secret, and only the tip-off teams sneak back and forth to gather information. But regarding the rope, since there are certain taboos, they arrange beforehand to keep track of the ropes according to size—male ropes (雄) and female ropes (雌), where women are usually avoided. 

If a woman were to step over or lead the rope, they believe that it will cause bad luck and possibly even ruin the tug-of-war. Women, generally aware of this superstition, often diligently avoid this mistake, but if it happens, they are met with ridicule such as “Hey, foolish girl!” or “What an off-target gal!” (얌치업는 계집애) responding to the insult while feeling the heat rise at the back of their heads. (If you visit the southern region to watch tug-of-war, women should pay special attention to this.) 

Over several days, colorful threads are twisted and braided together, and the base is prepared with sturdy wooden pillars that are about a foot long in size, wedged into their hair like a hairpin (this is referred to as “gotnamu”). One end of the thick rope extends far beyond the length of a person, creating scary-looking bodies (물체) that branch out like octopus legs with four or five long tails attached to it, while the male rope must stand out on the street. Simultaneously, another neighborhood quietly prepares their female rope that will not be confused with this one. 

After several days of public demonstrations, everyone gathers together to watch. It all comes down to selecting a bright night of the moon to finally begin the tug-of-war. 

On this night, without distinction to age or gender, young and old gather from all sides of the village emitting laughter. 

Beginning from just after dusk, the young children merge hand in hand, singing traditional songs like “Kwaijina Chinchinna” at the top of their lungs, joyfully skipping along and sharing their talents in various ways. 

This also becomes a sight for the village women, who bend down and giggle at their merry expressions while leaning on the walls, and the awkwardness of young men passes by with laughter. 

Meanwhile, the stronger men in charge of the action gather in the men’s rooms or pubs with jars awaiting to toast, consuming the rich traditional rice wine several times, and those who are in charge are apparently preparing for battle by collecting enough history that stands as their status.

To be the tug-of-war champion is an honor associated with being named the leader of the tug-of-war. The champion is a person who is on good terms with the farmers and who can maintain enthusiasm and interest throughout the competition. This means they are somewhat notable and can bear part of the expenses when creating the rope. As such, during the event, they hold a powerful command like a general or princely figure, respected by local farmers.

Thus, tug-of-war begins. 

Like a tiger pouncing with a single fist, strong men come out from behind, and young men and children cling to the middle and back of the rope, shouting out, and this is the most strenuous and glorious moment. 

Men on both sides grab their own side of the rope and energetically pull to try and turn the tide in their favor, naturally wrangling and pushing one another back and forth. At this moment, if a weak person is at the trailing edge of the rope, it is disheartening to even be stepped upon without uttering a sound as they are crushed underfoot, while children cling to the back, screaming as if the street will erupt. 

“If you don’t pull, we will jump!” 

“If you don’t pull, we will jump!” 

However, the male rope named gotnamu one side barely turns, with just enough force from the female rope to pass it, with their strength barely holding on. 

Thus, after studying the situation carefully, they strategize and try harder. The battle cries erupt like thunder from both sides as they pull in a grand celebration while the voices of women cheer louder reinforce the sounds of supporting chants ringing over, “Weyutsawiyi!” 

“Weyutsawiyi!” 

A wide variety of voices—deep grown men’s voices, echoing the shrill cries of children like melodies—create a single chorus, filling the air generously. When it rings as if in celebration, one cannot help but break free from any warmth and rush outside. 

For a while, the rope pulls amid the chaotic noise of the crowd, as people are yanked and flung around. The situation turns somewhat dismal during tough bouts when some men sent as spies, called “yabaukkun”, infiltrate the back ranks, trying to mislead them as the contest unfolds. 

They will slap their hands and scream, while the group members telling them not to let go steadily distract them, to seize the opportunity to call out “Stand up!” or “Leave!” to create confusion and pull their side over to victory whenever possible. 

But once discovered, they will be met with the wrath of all, catching the unfavorable ending behind the scenes, gaining the spurns that lead to a regrettable fate where they tumble down like a common pestel lying on the ground unable to keep up.

Tug-of-war is indeed a delightful affair for the spectators but becomes a severe challenge and heated struggle for those who actually take part.

Seeing their own village unable to advance or retreat, both villagers and the women gathered at the edges could no longer behave; they dropped the stones hidden in their skirts and rushed into the fray, with a desperate cry catching onto the ends of the rope, resembling the shrieking cries of the battle. 

Spectators have yet to hold back, watching closely in disbelief. 

At a time like this, even the gisaeng (기생) who usually played instruments join, throwing aside their musical tools and leaping in, harmonizing, singing, “Wee-yotsawiyi!” in an enthusiastic tune. The tug-of-war arena seemed to inhale a fresh spring breeze with their invigorated voices rising harmoniously louder. 

In addition, as the women join and participate, their exuberance becomes visible, with young girls gradually becoming more cautious, and the tenor of the tug-of-war shifts, becoming a scene increasingly detached from the innocent and genuine pleasures of farming—the activity becoming a more sordid indulgence for the upper classes and the elite as it loses its connection to the laboring farmers. 

Furthermore, when authorities intervene claiming that it hinders public order and makes the streets impure, several scenes of tug-of-war were continued to be disrupted and cut off amidst complaints from the policemen, causing this traditional pastime to decline more and more. 

On that night, upon an agreement regarding the drinks (酒), while switching sides in the tug-of-war, they pulled continuously as their strength waned and as one spirit drained after another. 

As one slips here, another there falls back, and at last, when the defeated side sits down mourning, pouring a mourning tear down the cheek. After the last team leads and pulls until all of the members have been taken, they triumphantly navigate through the flowing creek, satisfied with a winning shout, ringing heads triumphantly with cheers of victory, as “Kwai-jina Chinchinna” echoes back home. 

Upon returning to the district, the leader deeply takes a breath, as the children respond, resonating back, as the full moon of Chientao Mountain gives the last gleam shining in dawn on the wet road they walk upon; the joyous melody of Kawai-jina Chinchinna drifts beautifully through the quiet night sky, like a song of lament for the departing citizens.

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

In the sky, only a star remains!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

On the stream bank, stones also exist!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

Beneath the clouds, so many worries lie!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

Married daughters from other homes also exist!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

In my heart, worries still abound!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

So many to remember!

Kwai-jina Chinchinna! 

- This is the story about tug-of-war I saw in Gyeongju during my childhood. -  

**Summary**

The article describes the concept and cultural significance of tug-of-war during the Lunar New Year celebrations in Korea, illustrating the joyous atmosphere created by community participation and youthful exuberance. It reflects the social dynamics among individuals of different ages and genders, showcasing how traditional games served as both a form of entertainment and a means of community bonding. Historians might find the transformation of these festivities from genuine communal activities into events influenced by social stratification, where the participation of elite classes diluted their original essence, noteworthy in the context of Korean colonial history.

「우-엿사 위-!」
대통가치 굵은 어룬의 목소래 피리가치 간열푼 아희들의 고흔 목소래 목쉰 총각머슴들이며 말나빠진 하이카라 계집아희...이러한 각가지 목소래가 혼연히 뭉치여서 한번 놉헛다 한번 나젓다 멋잇게 퍼저나갈 때 멀니서 이걸 들으면 아모리 추운날이라도 이불을 박차고 뛰여나가지 안코는 못 견딈니다.
한참이나 이와가치 줄이 사람속에 파뭇처서 밀니거니 땅기거니 억개들이 들석들석하면서 위엿사 소리를 군호로 승부를 다투는데 형세가 불리할때는 양편에서 야바우꾼 이란 야마시꾼을 적군에 파견함니다. 그러면 이 야바우꾼은 가장 속어 넘어가기 쉬운 후위군에 잠입해 가지고 될수잇는데로 적군에 불리하게 誤指揮를 하는 것임니다.
그리하야 손벽을 치고 고함을 치면서 안저서 안될 때
「안저라!안저라!」하야 안즐려는 틈을 타고 또 서서는 안될 경우에 「서거라! 서거라!」하야 일어서는 허를 타서 얼마만치나 자기편에 끄을여 가게 만 만드는 고약한 야바우를 꿈미는 터임으로 발각이 되기가 무섭게 여지업는 唾罵를 밧고 장골들의 무쇠가튼 손아귀에 잡혀서＜65＞ 하늘과 땅이 팽-돌아가게 뺑뺑이를 당하고 길가에 쓸어진채 얼마마동안 일어나지 못하는게 악의업는 수수한 裁制요 툭툭 털고 일어나는 승거운 최후임니다.
원래 줄다리기란 승겁게 보는 사람에게는 이보다 더 승거운 노릇이 업는 것 갓지만 정작 줄다리기 마당에 나서든지 줄가락을 잡고 보면 이만치 열이나고 승벽이 나는 노름이란 업슴니다.

자긔동리줄이 안저도 안되고 서도 안되고 작구만 끄을여 가는걸 보고는 행길ㅅ가나 담 모퉁이에 붓터서서 구경하고 잇든 아낙네며 말가튼 처녀들까지도 참다참지 못하여 치마폭에 돌을 한아람식이나 싸안고 뛰여나와서 두둘두둘한 헹길바닥에다 배를 깔고 짜작짜작 비명을 내면서 끄을여가는 줄꽁지를 밟고 안자 줄과 한가지 날카라운 비명을 피밧는데야 엇저랴!
중대가리 총각들로 엇지 떨면서 구경만 하고 백이겟슴닛가.
더구나 이즈음에 와서는 기생들까지 줄펜이 되야 장고와 가야금을 내던지고 뛰여나와서 그 가다듬은 고흔 목으로 「우-엿싸위-야!」를 음율적으로 한바탕 뽑어노흐면 줄다리기판은 갑작이 봄바람이 불어 온 듯이 화기가 돌며 그 소래에 화답이나 하는 듯이 「위엿사」소래는 더욱 놉허짐니다. 그뿐임닛가 실바람에 잘물결치는 호를호를한 비단치마에 앗김업시 돌을싸고 줄우에 펑퍼저 안저서 嬌愁만면하야 끄을여가는 그 애처러운 자태와 어즈럽고 황망한 표정이 그때만은 遊治郞이나 농부들 가리지 안코 매달닐 때 물불을 헤아리지 안코 뛰여들어 갈만치 그들의 용기와 힘은 고조되여 가는 것임니다.

거츠러운 줄 소나무 껍질처름 거칠거칠한 손에 흙냄새나는 농부들과 향굿한 향수냄새며 비단치마 작고 얫분 하얀손 이것이 본대 얼토당토안는 엄청난 대조이지만 조곰도 어섹함이 업시 한곳에 얽어 바리는 것이 줄다리기가 가진 음석깁흔 매력이라 할넌지요. 그러나 기생들이 나옴으로부터 수집은 처녀들이 차차 자최를 감추게 되고 줄다리기도 처음 순박하고 구수한 맛이 적어지고 농민들의 손을 떠나 유한 階級들의 一種 卑劣한 유흥꺼리가 되어가는 경향을 볼 때 어릴적 목가를 부르며 꼿따고 놀든 野原을 빼앗긴 뜻한 섬섬한 눗김이 업지 안슴니다.
더구나 공안을 방해하며 도로를 불결하게 만든다는 이유로 경관들의 번적이는 장검이 한번 떨어지매 줄허리가 두동강이 나는 일이 만흐니이  가튼 향토적 노름도 점점 숙저가는 모양인가 봄니다.
그날 밤 술(酒)내기 약조를 하고 동리로 편을 갈너서 ＜66＞줄다리기를 하다가 勢를 일허 사정업시 질질 끄을여기는 도중에 멕이 풀니고 긔운이 다하야 한 사람 떨어지고 두사람 떨어지고 마지막 패군지장마자 소매에 눈물을 뿌리고 돌아을 때 허리가 상하고  발이 떨어진 形不成容의 참담한 적군의 줄을 길이 다 할때까지 끄을고 갓다가 그곳에 가로흘으는 시내물속에 처넛코 나서야 겨우 覇氣를 만족하듯이 승전의 高喊聲을 치며 개가를 울니며 돌아서서 득승한 영예를 가진 줄머리우에 줄패장을 덩그럿케 올여안처 가지고 줄머리에서 꽁지까지 떠밧처 억개우에다 매고는 웃줄웃줄 춤을 추면서 「쾌지나 칭칭나네」를 목쳥껏 부르고 돌아옴니다.

놉히올나 안즌 줄패장이 목을 가다듬어서 먹이면 줄떠멘 사람일동이 화답을 하는데 仙挑山말낭이에 반만걸닌 겨을달이 나도 아즉 여긔서 구경하고 잇다는 듯이 새벽가튼 달빗흘 흘녀 그들의 밟고 가는 압길을 밝힐 때 고요히 잠든 밤하늘에 퍼져 나가는 쾌지나칭칭의 悠長한 여음갓고 또는 그 향토를 일허바리고 떠나가는 백성들의 挽歌와도 가치 구슯피 들니기도 함니다.
