Let's know the coming day, a New Year's message to the people  
Yun Chi-ho  

Even squirrels save food for the winter, and acorns are stored away for evening meals; how much more should we, the rational beings, save for the future? Historically, Koreans have had less of a savings mentality compared to other nations. This could be attributed to our traditional systems and customs that have made it difficult or to our country's economic weakness preventing us from having the luxury to save. Yet, fundamentally, the question arises whether we inherently lack a savings mentality. For instance, one might argue that the impoverished and powerless are merely surviving under oppression and have no means to save. However, even the wealthy, upon earning money, often fail to save for business ventures, instead dissipating their funds on pleasure and gambling, a truly lamentable condition whose cause remains elusive.

On a recent trip to my hometown in Chungnam, I observed a shocking scene: the son of a local official wasted family inheritance on frivolous pursuits, eventually selling ancestral land all the way to a Korean cemetery, only to witness the official's wife dealing with the aftermath of this loss. In contrast, someone who was once scorned, now commands respect in the same locality due to their wealth. In our current system, even individuals of high character and education may find themselves powerless if they lack financial resources, becoming mere objects in a system where riches determine worth. Without money, one grows distant from their kin, carelessly voiding obligations to any group they associate with.

Previously, when Koreans lived in communities with mutual support, even someone who lost their wealth had the opportunity to regain it eventually. However, now, due to the direct relations with foreign entities, every money spent immediately flows out of the hands of Koreans. When I speak this way, some may critique me, yet I feel that in contemporary Korean society, a frugal person accumulating savings is more beneficial than a frivolous spendthrift. This is because a spendthrift harms not only themselves but also their family and society, whereas a frugal person, holding onto their savings, often has future opportunities to invest in societal goods.

Thus, I continually wish to emphasize the importance of thrift and savings. On one occasion, while accompanying Western missionaries on a local circuit, I noticed they urged their congregants, citing Scripture, that "money is given by God, and should be used for God's work," eliciting responses to sell even land to fund church building. In contrast, I argued that to truly be a child of God, one must protect their possessions well. If one loses everything, including family, in the pursuit of heaven, what good is it? If one has the means of living, one can contribute to church initiatives, but if not, the focus must be on saving.

Westerners often claim that Koreans are people who do not know what tomorrow holds, mainly suggesting that they lack the savings mentality to plan for the future. Regardless of past hardships, starting today, we must consider that tomorrow exists and pay particular attention to savings.  

Summary  

The article discusses the importance of saving and thriftiness, particularly in the context of Korean society in the 1930s. The author highlights a cultural tendency among Koreans to not prioritize saving adequately and contrasts this with the behavior of both the wealthy and the poor. A notable argument is made about individual responsibility to maintain one’s finances for the sake of both personal and societal well-being. Historians might find this piece revealing as it reflects the socio-economic mindset of Koreans during the colonial period, providing insight into their struggles against oppression and the importance of financial literacy as a form of self-empowerment.
