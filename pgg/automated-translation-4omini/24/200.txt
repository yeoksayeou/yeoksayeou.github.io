기사제목	男편의 말 中에 니치지 안는 말 (Words that did not sink in from my husband)  
저자	曹在浩 夫人 崔英姬氏 (Mrs. Choi Young-hee, wife of Jo Jai-ho)  
기사형태	설문 (Survey)  

There are a few phrases that did not sink in. The two phrases are “Let’s get a divorce” and “I won’t get divorced.” I will broadly explain the circumstances under which these two phrases came out of the mouth of a person who was supposed to be a gentleman.

After one year of marriage, he (Mr. Jo) proposed divorce on the grounds that I was uneducated and could not live with him. For a woman—especially considering my situation at that time—what a misfortune that would be! So, I came to Kyŏngsŏng and entered Taehwa Girls’ School. After that, I thought again: considering his academic knowledge and social standing, I did not have the qualifications to be his wife. If I were to remain as I was, seeing his friends, all the old-fashioned women were either getting divorced or starting new families—how unhappy Mr. Jo would be if I remained like that! However, I was unable to give my consent to that. So for two or three years, I ruminated alone, with nowhere to go and only feeling sorry for ourselves. After considering that two people suffering like this should find a resolution quickly so that at least one person might obtain happiness, I decided to return to my parents’ home and express the intention to divorce this time by saying, “I will get a divorce.” As I expected, he expressed remorse for his past actions and firmly refused to consider divorce. Although this caused the most significant turmoil in our marital life, such words stuck in my bone marrow. However, after reflecting on it, they didn’t mean much.

At the time, I suppose it might have been when we were engaged. He (Mrs. Shin) was a teacher at Gongju Yeongmyeong School when he visited my home, which was in the city, and wrote on a calendar: 

"After diligent work, nothing is difficult in this world. 

In the midst of trials, there is peace."

This saying always lingers in my mind even during the most painful times.

The most impressive story? Please consider that. I cannot particularly recall impressive stories. However, over the long years, there should be something. There are many various stories that can be remarkable and stimulating, like social issues or political matters. Yet, when asked to specifically pinpoint the single most impressive story, it is quite difficult. Even if I say there are some, it would probably be such that if I wrote it directly in a magazine, it would not pass. Therefore, let's leave that aside. Well then, I would like to share this: after he (Mr. Jeong) decided to engage in the Kuhakdang (苦學堂) project, he courageously abandoned everything and made a bold leap, and that endeavor left a lasting impression.

Among the words I have heard, there are no remarkable phrases that persist to this day. Nevertheless, I can share one something somewhat silly yet meaningful. It is not an impressive one in particular, but it’s a phrase I have been mindful of: “Let’s not just play around but be people who work.” Even if it was a shameful remark made during a quarrel, there's nothing notable about that, so I will refrain from delving further.

  
**Summary**  

This article presents a survey of women discussing memorable phrases or words from their husbands and the emotions tied to them. Each woman shares various reflections on marriage, divorce, personal growth, and mutual respect in relationships. Particularly striking is the way the women navigate societal expectations and personal convictions within a colonial context, reflecting the complexities of gender roles and familial obligations in early 20th-century Korea. Historians might find the candid insights into the struggles and aspirations of women in this era to be revealing, particularly given the societal pressures of colonial Korea.
