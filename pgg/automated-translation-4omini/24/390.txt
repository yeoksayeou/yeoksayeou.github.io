기사제목	늘어노코 추리기, 그 일과 그 時間 (The Work of Reporting: Its Tasks and Time)  
필자	申敬渟 (Shin Kyung-tang)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Miscellaneous Literature)  

The working hour, if I may speak of it, the so-called “work” that I am doing is indeed the task of “reporting.” Why is that? Because the work of a newspaper entails a lot of hustle and bustle from morning until evening, yet ultimately everything is completed in just 20 to 30 minutes after the deadline for the manuscript. To put it simply, it starts from morning until evening and then from that moment begins again until one o'clock the next morning. - The newspaper I work for is a morning publication, so the former refers to the evening publication and the latter refers to the morning publication. Starting from noon, the work day goes for about 15 hours until the next dawn. Even if the night shift and day shift were to evenly allocate this time, it would still require seven and a half hours of work.

However, it cannot be said that simply submitting the manuscript to the factory for typesetting and completely finishing the proofread page means that the newspaper has been fully made. In the production of a newspaper, the final typesetting is one of the crucial roles, even more important than collecting the original manuscripts. Why is that? Because around ten years ago, readers were able to just “read” a newspaper without having any critical judgment or discernment. At that time, newspaper editors could do as they pleased without concern. But now, the readers' standards are vastly different from those times. Moreover, it is not only in Joseon's newspapers; newspapers have disappeared as mere articles and have turned into complete commercial products. 

Hence, when editors compile newspapers daily, they have in mind the aim of creating a product that will stand out against similar products from other factories, akin to industrial workers making a commodity. The articles, even if they treat the same events, strive to present titles that are sharper than others, embedding themselves deeply in the readers' hearts, and providing freshness that captivates the readers unexpectedly. However, just like referring to a mountain (山) includes all the flora and fauna within, it cannot be said that an article or title alone constitutes a newspaper. Therefore, no matter how meticulously the editing is done, until the typesetting is completed, it cannot be said that a “newspaper” has been created. It can be likened to raw materials for a product, and typesetting resembles its processing.

Furthermore, after the newspaper has been distributed to the market (市場) and in comparison with other newspapers, to ensure that readers find that one newspaper (甲) is indeed superior to another (乙), it cannot be the case that readers must painstakingly read every word or character from beginning to end before offering their critique. First and foremost, when readers pick up the newspaper, if the layout is vibrant and engaging—making a scene simply for noise is a form of sin—then they should feel inclined to read it. Without having to even go through the content, if they grasp that this is superior, then this newspaper will have achieved its purpose like a product entering the market and receiving general acceptance, thereby having already overthrown other similar products.

Therefore, what is most crucial in making a newspaper appealing to readers lies in the layout rather than merely the title itself. Thus, for the cumulative time of seven and a half hours, collecting reports from external reporters and attaching titles serves as just the raw material of the product. The processing that allows it to compete against other items in the market occurs during the typesetting; ultimately, to put it plainly, my “work” consists of those 20 to 30 minutes spent on typesetting after the manuscript deadline.

Thus, it becomes “the work of reporting.”

Now, towards questioning what it means to submit it at the same time every day—this involves making a newspaper that contends with time, hence the typesetting process taking maybe 20 minutes or so, why the additional “up to 30 minutes” can seem like an audacious claim. The workers in the typesetting department every day, if they are familiar with each other’s methods and share a common understanding, working with set procedures means that 20 minutes would indeed be ample. However, in less ideal circumstances, 30 minutes can be a generous estimate, with times exceeding 40 minutes commonplace.

Therefore, the work at a newspaper office is an all-day endeavor, but in reality, it consists of just 20 to 30 minutes of meaningfully engaging work, thus it is called “the work of reporting.” <87> <88>  

**Summary**  

This article provides an insightful glimpse into the life of a newspaper worker in colonial Korea, emphasizing the time constraints and creative efforts involved in producing a daily publication. The author reflects on the evolution of newspaper readership and production standards, revealing that newspapers have transitioned into competitive commercial products that require intensive labor, especially in layout and presentation. Historians may find the combination of colony politics, consumerism, and the evolution of journalistic standards particularly revealing about Korean society's changing dynamics during this era.
