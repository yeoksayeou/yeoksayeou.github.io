Spring in the North  
						Kim Dong-hwan  
The edge of the Tumen River has begun to bloom softly, and the garments of the movers heading towards Russia and Northern Manchuria have started to flutter gracefully. The sight of old men and women, carrying their luggage tied with cloth and holding children, moving in a group, is surely a sign that spring has come. The movers disembarking at the riverbank seem to bring 'spring' with them from the lush green fields of the southern regions to sow it across the thousand miles of the North, and then return to Huji as if they have left spring behind.  

Indeed, at this time of spring, the river is beautiful. In this ninety springs, the dew on flowers melting in the warmth collects tears that flow into the river. When these tears wander across the icy riverbanks, they shimmer as if breaking, and the spring water of the Tumen River flows in this way. When loggers on rafts navigate the flowing water and the snowmelt, if a migrant being watched from the bridge raises their hand, that signifies the arrival of spring in the North. Although it is difficult to recognize spring in a place like the North, where not a single flower blooms and no azaleas bloom, it is only when seeing the throng of clothing moving towards Huji that one truly understands. Moreover, it seems that this year the spring austerity is severe, as migrants have begun coming even in early February, before the swallows arrive on the third day of the third month. – Watching the fluttering garments in the northern winds by the riverside, I felt a deep sadness, as if I were witnessing the loss of part of Joseon.  

When the fields are tilled, I swing a weeding sickle  
And after weeding, I go to clear the fields yet again.  

I thought I understood this, yet I am forced to go on.  

If it gets crowded in the town, one moves to stay in the countryside,  
If it gets crowded in the countryside, one moves to the mountains.  
But even if I go to the mountains, where is there to go? Only the heavens know.  

Spring in the North is a spring of tears. It is a bustling spring where unfortunate migrants, who have lived off the misery of Joseon, hurry to the Huji regions. What resemblance is there to spring days in the southern regions, where one hears the songs of the willow flute and the skylark? When the luggage arrives, it is said spring has come, and when the luggage leaves, it is said spring has gone. Yet, spring light does indeed come to the mountains and rivers of the North. Perhaps it is due to the northerly Pacific? Here, there still exists untouched virgin forests where people have yet to set foot, covering tens of thousands of hectars, millions of hectares, stretching across mountains and valleys like vast waves of the sea. On sunny days, one can hear the rumbling of bears waking from hibernation. When spring arrives, one can hear the hooting of owls in the foggy valleys deep in the mountains at dusk. Though blind in the night, the allure of spring makes the wings flutter. The sounds of the bear bells and the village elders returning with baskets of wild edible herbs flow through the air. Everything here is primitive and holds the ancient landscapes intact through millennia.  

But, the hallmark of the North lies in its woodsmen. As the sun sets, silhouettes of figures climb along the mountain ridges, and one may see a dozen figures bearing loads of firewood, while young children carry green saplings and tap them rhythmically with their sickles as they ascend remote mountain paths, creating a melancholy yet pure and beautiful melody that fades into the grasslands, as if this body, still steeped in earth, is vanishing.  

Even as the mountains are carved away and the villagers gradually depart, the late spring still allows one to see these rustic songs and shepherds among the mountain ridges, making this a truly peculiar spring of ours.  

Whether one goes to the mountains or fields, it is all spring.  
The sky sends down flowers abundantly,  
Everywhere one goes, it is nothing but spring.  

The palanquin carrying spring is probably a flower palanquin (꽃가마).  
As the road is long, do the movers not come?  
Even with the arrival, do not a single flower bloom before departing?  

**Summary**  

In "Spring in the North," Kim Dong-hwan reflects on the arrival of spring in the northern regions of Korea, portraying a landscape filled with tears of migrants who have been driven from their homes. The imagery of moving toward Huji whilst stripping the beauty of the North acts as a poignant representation of the struggles faced during the colonial period. Historians might note the contrast between the natural beauty of the landscape and the harsh realities of life for Korean people under Japanese rule, emphasizing the deep sorrow and resilience captured in the depiction of a simple seasonal change.

北國의 春
						金東煥
두만강에 어름 쪽이 우실우실 풀니더니 다리 우로 노서아와 북만주로 가는 이사꾼들 옷자락이 펄넉펄넉 날니기 시작한다. 박아지를 다람다람 달아맨 이사짐과 어린애를 안은 老嫗老爺가 떼를 지어 가는 그 꼴이 누가 봄이 아니 왓드랫느냐. 이러케 江岸 停車場에 내리는 이사군들은 「봄」을 三南 푸른 대밧 속에서 캐어다가 北國 천리에 뿌리어 노코는 저들만 도로혀 봄을 둥지고서 胡地로 가버린다.
그러타. 참말 이즈음의 봄 맛난 江流에는 落水가 진다. 九十春光에 무르녹는 화초의 이슬은 업고 쪼기고 쪼기어 長白山脈까지 넘는 어느 족속의 뿌리는 눈물에 강에는 락수가 진다. 그 눈물이 이리저리 어름강판을 헤맬 때 어름짱은 금이 슬면서 쪼각 쪼각 갈라저 드부모 가티 흐르는 것이니 豆滿江의 봄물은 이러하야 쁫는다. 營林廠筏夫가 떼목을 타고 流水와 雪解의 강수를 노저어 내릴 때 다리 우에서 그를 바라 헷손질 치는 이주민이 보이면 그때에 북쪽의 봄은 바야흐로 닉어가는 것이라. 실상 화초 한 가지 필 줄 모르고 杜鵑 한마듸 올 줄 모르는 北國에 봄 온 줄을 무엇으로 알랴만은 밤낫 胡地로 가는 이 힌 옷의 떼를 보고서 비로소 아는 것이다. 더구나 금년은 春窮이 심한가 보아서 삼월 삼진날 제비 올 철도 아득한 이월초순부터 이사꾼들이 들이밀기도 한다.- 나는 강가에서 북풍이 날리는 그네의 옷자락을 바라보다가 조선의 한 귀통이 문허저가는 듯 창연해서 눈물을 금치 못햇다.

北國의 봄은 눈물의 봄이다. 불행한 조선의 우로를 먹다가 눈바래 부는 胡地로 가는 이사꾼들을 맛고 보내기에 분주한 봄이니 어느 해가에 버들피리 불며 종달새 딸아다니는 남국 가튼 봄날을 마저보랴. 이사짐이 오니 봄이 온가하고 이사짐이 가니 봄이 간가할 뿐이다. 그러나 北國의 산천에도 춘광은 잇다. 北氷洋에 갓가운 때문인가. 여기에는 인적이 아직 드러가 못 본 大處女林이 잇스니, 몃 십 만평, 몃 백 만평, 산에서 산으로 골에서 골로 바다 물결 가티 쭉 버더진 그 밀림 속에서 해 잘난 날이면 곰이 우르렁거리는 소리가 들닌다. 양지짝에서 색기를 갓 나은 암곰이 溪流에 내려갓다가, 두던두 빨간 진달내 꼿을 보고 무서워 짓는 소리다. 그 뿐인가 解春만 되면 황혼이 되기 밧브게 안개 자욱한 山谷에서 부엉이 소리 들니나니 눈은 멀엇건만 춘광에 못 이겨 날개 펴느랴는 수작이다. 밤길을 것는 소곰실이 마발꾼의 방울소리, 또 마을 삭시들이 물이 쭉쭉 흐르는 풋나물 광주리 이고 도라오는 것, 모다 원시적이며 모다 수천 년 풍광이 그대로 남어 잇는 것 갓다.
