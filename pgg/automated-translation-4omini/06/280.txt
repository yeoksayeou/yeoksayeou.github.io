Spring's Village House  
						李秉岐  
The rooster crows and  
Scatters the feed,  
The chicks chirp,  
The mother clucks;  
In the yard, shadows  
Fill and gather softly.  

In the empty cabbage field,  
Yellow flowers bloom delightfully.  
Yellow butterflies,  
Fluttering and swirling,  
Dance from flower to flower,  
From here to there, buzzing along.  

Behind, the decaying earth  
Stings at my nose,  
Yet like the scent of ointment  
It beckons mysteriously,  
As the molasses in the field  
Jumps and rises.  

The new grass is sprouting  
On the hilltop by the rice paddies,  
The cow lies down lazily,  
The calf frolics and jumps.  
The white-haired hen  
Naps cozily under the leaves.  

Wild greens—wild greens—  
Filling our bellies—  
This spring,  
For this alone we live.  
Going out to the mountains or fields,  
Gathering wild greens freely.  

There’s little to eat,  
Even the fussing children  
Are led off to hide.  
Bending willows and catching  
Shrimps or crayfish,  
Such delightful amusing play occurs.  

**Summary**  

The poem captures the lively and refreshing essence of spring in a rural village, depicting scenes of animals, blooming flowers, and the joys of gathering wild greens. The vivid imagery evokes a sense of simplicity and abundance during this season, highlighting the connection between nature and daily life. Historians may find the portrayal of agrarian life under Japanese colonial rule revealing, as it emphasizes traditional rural practices and the cultural significance of seasonal changes amidst the socio-political backdrop of the time.
