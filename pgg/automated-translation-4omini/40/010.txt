Song of the Haenyeo, Jeju Island Folk Song  
I heard of Iheo Island, oh Iheo Island  
Iheo, Iheo, oh Iheo Island  
When I go to Iheo, tears arise  
Since it should not be said, go away  

While crying, others cry too  
On the main road, sing on your way  
When leaving it looks glorious  
But upon return, it's just a flower hat  

Even if the waves of the island cry, it’s of concern  
Even if the sea currents cry, it’s of concern  
My younger sister crosses the water in distress  
I worry about the ailment  

Worrying makes it hard to sleep  
I won’t know when the day breaks  
On a new day, my mother arrives  
On a new moon, my father comes  

The boatman’s girl is soaked  
The ducks are in the shallows  
I wait for the wild wind  

On a day when there’s rain  
The rain pours down on Jeju Yeongsan  
What sadness for the beloved  
Tears are set in the marsh  
Drifting away in sighs  

When I was thirty, when my mother was alive  
Which sea's sweet herb did I eat?  
In every storm and in every bowing  
I wander lost in these waves  
The English sea is indeed vast  
Someday all will feed off this sea  

**Summary**  

This folk song reflects the struggles and emotional landscape of the haenyeo (female divers) of Jeju Island, expressing their deep connection to the sea, the sorrow from loss and hardship, and their familial relationships. It reveals the pain and anxiety intertwined with their daily lives as they face the uncertainties of nature and life, resonating with the broader experiences of Koreans during the colonial period. Historians might find this piece valuable as it illustrates the cultural identity and resilience of the Jeju people, capturing the socio-economic challenges faced under colonial rule.
