기사형태	세태비평 (Social Critique)  

The great city of Seoul is filled with Eros, Grotesque, Terror, and Ugliness  

Terror, that frightening and astonishing terror, often takes innocent lives, but sometimes it allows cute little children to be born. Recently, there have been unfortunate incidents involving Korean and Chinese people, leading to the sacrifice of nearly a hundred lives throughout the region. In the midst of such turmoil, amidst the depths of the chaos, there was a celebration as twins were born to women who had sought refuge at the Chinese Consulate in Seoul and at a refuge for Chinese people in Pyongyang. This occurred on the evening of July 5th, around 9 PM. At the Chinese Consulate in Seoul, more than 4,000 Chinese people who had fled the region were crammed together in extreme disorder when suddenly, a woman's scream rang out from one corner. With nerves already raw, the slightest noise felt like the sound of cannons firing, causing everyone to stare in shock. What was happening? Had some vile person committed a heinous act against a woman amidst the confusion? The lively Chinese community immediately began to stir with noise. However, it turned out that a woman, Zhou Shuhang from Xintangli, gave birth to fraternal twins. Then, on the night of the 8th, in Pyongyang, another Chinese woman named Chen gave birth to another set of twins, and the joy continued to spread as other women also gave birth. While it is indeed tragic that many innocent lives were lost, the occurrence of twins being born is a rare and celebratory event.

Ugliness! The Ugliness of the Mahjong Club  

The term "Ugliness!" is not derived from the Japanese word for road, but rather it is a new term created to connect with Eros and Grotesque. This year, many Mahjong clubs have come into existence in Seoul. Titles such as "Green Growth," "Three Ordinaries," "Four Joys," "Peace," "Qingping," "Zhongfa," and "Yuanxi," number in the hundreds like mushrooms sprouting after the rainy season. However, I don't feel qualified to comment on their nature since I count myself among the patrons of these clubs. What I want to convey is that the quality of these clubs has significantly declined and they have become quite filthy. It is distressing and disgusting. I have taken up this pen to write about it.

One night, after finishing a manuscript at about 9:30 PM, I decided to visit a Mahjong club on An-guk Dong, coming from the nearby place. Initially, I had planned to simply enjoy a drink after a long time, but I ended up heading there anyway. I had not been to such establishments since I had become busy with my writing duties. Thus, my visit this time was truly long overdue.

I ran into some acquaintances there. When I arrived, there was little conversation to be had, as the host was preoccupied, catering to guests. Despite the club being lively, the proprietor was engaged in a busy situation.

After a while, once the proprietor finished with their duties, we began the game. We arranged our tiles and held onto our luck tightly. The opposing players appeared to be experienced in this location, as they approached with a sense of coolness.

Just before the dealer was about to draw, one of them hesitated and glanced at me, saying, “Are we going to play just for fun?” I answered, “Pardon?”

I quickly realized what they were implying. The dealer, who looked rather serious, insisted, “Let’s place a bet.” 

I replied, “I’m not that skilled at this.”

“They were careless—don’t think about it too much,” he said, almost as if he couldn’t resist.

“Fine, let’s do it,” I said. The betting began, and I suddenly found myself winning about 7,000 won from various tiles such as East Wind and South Wind.  

The atmosphere shifted drastically. They seemed to intend to conceal their losses. I noticed a couple of times they tried to place their bets while feigning nonchalance.

At that moment, one of the players, who seemed overly frustrated, turned around and commented, “How much is it?” The reply was, “Two.” 

“Oh! How can you lose this much?” he exclaimed.

“What can I do?” I thought to myself. 

In that moment, I couldn’t help but feel exhausted and annoyed at such disgraceful behavior from them.

The game ended, and I found myself stuck in the middle of their murmurs. “Such filthy characters,” I thought, as I prepared to leave.  

Their shouts echoed behind me as I left: “This irritating kid! We’ll never see you again!” A companion remarked, “Everyone is like that these days. What more can we expect?” 

I brushed off my discontent and returned to my residence in the upper room.

The Mahjong clubs in Seoul are the epitome of contemporary ugliest spectacles. How can the authorities leave this unchecked?  

Cafe's Huge Grotesque  

Eros... Grotesque... Terror... and Ugliness... Out of these four elements, it is oddly enough that I found myself in the realm of "Grotesque."  

Is there anything grotesque about Korea? In places where life and livelihood are like the palm of your hand, can there possibly be anything truly grotesque? Our lives seem too desperate for such pastimes!

Yet, I must admit, disregarding the potential loss of principal, someone did introduce me to something in Seoul that claims to be grotesque, though I take full responsibility for partaking in what I express here.

In the southern part of Seoul, there are two Japanese friends, XXXX and XXXX, who operate a barber shop. Both of them seem to be somewhat perplexed by the concept of "Grotesque."  

It is well known that Japanese people happily handle snakes, treating them as delicacies, and these two friends have taken to doing so in their own fashion, flaunting their live snakes that are nearly three feet long, which they cleverly conceal in their sleeves.

One day, in a cafe called XX in the Jingo-dong, amid the tranquil ambiance with blue lanterns casting a dim glow, a faintly fragrant atmosphere, and the unending chatter of conversations, these two friends strolled in, each carrying their respective drinks and a waitress.  

As they settled down at a table, one of the friends couldn't help but chuckle at their inside joke, and unbeknownst to the waitress, they discreetly pulled their snakes from their sleeves and laid them flat on the table.  

“Goodness!” This ultimate display of shock from the waitress immediately turned the place into a chaotic scene, where alcohol spilled, glasses rolled, and frantic sounds erupted throughout the establishment.  

The two friends relished the uproar, beaming with delight as they casually picked up their snakes and strolled out elegantly.  

This happened numerous times, as they tirelessly roamed and ultimately found themselves, for the following week, in solitary confinement of the police detention center, humorously offering their share to the vermin of the place.  

Eros, The Seventh Stationed Army's Beauty Troupe  

Eros! Eros has been frequently conversed about lately, becoming one of the new terminologies, yet I do not even know what Eros truly represents, simply following suit in usage.  

But now, we have a significant news piece in the Eros realm, as the stationed beauty troop from Osaka presents itself in Seoul. This beauty troop, with a capital of 2 million yen (though I can’t necessarily vouch for its accuracy), has boldly declared to capture the Eros world.

This beauty troop’s headquarters is indeed in Osaka, along with three additional branches catering the same concept. Thus, this beauty troop, the Seventh Stationed Army, has also set up in the heart of Jingo-dong in Seoul.  

When this beauty troop arrived in Korea, it did not merely bring along its local talent but also included twenty beauties from Osaka to serve in the operation. Not only are these ladies attractive, but they also possess the allure of more than mere appearance, routinely drawing in more eager patrons.  

There is an interesting detail: all of the beauties' names have been stylized after famous actresses from Japanese films, making it intriguing to consider their qualms in adopting such labels. 

However, upon stepping into the venue, one might feel that the allure of the environment falls short of the hype, as it seems slightly cramped and shallower in ambiance than one would expect.  

All in all, one can hear the bustling and affectionate atmosphere from their ongoing interactions.  

Yet, as I settled in, the seating seemed less than desirable and far from accommodating. Eventually, I had to stand up and wrap things up, feeling rather perturbed by the atmosphere.  

I thought of how difficult it would be to fully embrace such a commercialized notion of Eros, often lacking depth and essence, despite the ephemerality of its charms.  

Isn’t this a kind of bad advertisement? I can’t say I paid to endure a trap like that. But come wintertime, it may prove to be a different matter!  

Take the opportunity, and once again enjoy the fleeting splendor of Eros!  

Summary  

This article explores the contrasting themes of joy and tragedy in 1931 colonial Korea, particularly surrounding the birth of twins in times of terror, the decline of social standards in establishments like Mahjong clubs, and the emergence of Japanese beauty troops in Seoul. The article paints a vivid picture of the social issues faced by Koreans during this era, capturing a sense of nostalgia as the author reflects on these experiences. Historians may find interest in the depiction of the leisure activities and struggles within this colonial context, which would highlight the complexities of identity and cultural exchange during Japanese occupation.
