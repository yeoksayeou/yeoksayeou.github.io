New Arirang

Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo  
Arirang, let's play well together  

Where did this Arirang come from?  
It has been played with all the attention  

Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo  
Arirang, let's play well together  

Couldn't cross over there, look at the bridge  
Kyongbok Palace's beams don't even fit  

Even if the seeds are ruffled, they are quick  
Why is this monster's dandelion so riled up?  

A horned hat, a golden button, sharp pants  
It’s all because of the rice harvested  

The girls' school has no discipline   
The horned hat student is keeping watch  

With dandelions in mix-and-match dishes  
The Yalu River's iron bridge swiftly flows  

Who eats the rich rice?  
Only the barley and chaff make air go puff puff  

You shouldn’t say it a million times  
Even if I show you this way, it’s still nothing  

Everything is sold in every which way  
What’s left is a pot of beans for a princess  

**Summary**

The poem "New Arirang" presents a playful yet poignant reflection on societal issues under colonial rule in Korea during the 1930s. Through a series of vivid imagery and metaphors, the work captures the struggles of ordinary people facing hardship, the influence of the ruling class, and the cultural identity represented by Arirang, a traditional Korean folk song. Historians might find this piece particularly interesting as it encapsulates the sentiments of resistance and the longing for freedom that characterized the period of Japanese colonial rule.
