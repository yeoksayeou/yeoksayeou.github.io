Eternal Sorrow: A Tale of the 700 Righteous Men of Geumsan  
This tale was published continuously in a student magazine, but when the magazine ceased its publication, the continuation of the manuscript could not be published, so the content was revised and published continuously in this magazine. While thanking the author, I hope all readers will read it with interest. (Editor)  

People in the world say that after the fall of the Chu hegemon (楚覇王), his earnest minister met an untimely death, and along with 500 brave warriors, they gathered in a remote island in the eastern sea, setting up an altar to offer incense to their king and general. Afterwards, they collectively took their lives with swords in a splendid death that mirrored that of the 500 warriors of Tian Heng (田橫)... People also say that in order to avenge their wronged lord, they disguised themselves as beggars, fools, or madmen, deceiving their enemies into a false sense of security before parting from their families, consuming dirt while struggling, crossing the river 700 li to attack, killing ruthlessly, and avenging their lord's death in the greatest way. They were the 47 righteous men of Akō (赤穗) whose graves lie in Tokyo's Sengaku-ji (泉岳寺)...  

However, it is a sorrowful affair! It is an agonizing matter! To not know about our ancestors... As our beautiful land of 3,000 ri flows like a river through turbulent turmoil, everywhere is a mountain of blood, houses destroyed, and families scattered. In spite of this, wearing simple clothing, organizing a coalition of Confucius and Buddha, they waged a war; though it might be said there were achievements, they could not evade defeat, thus the city of Geumsan (錦山城) turned into one of grand battles. 

When the arrows were exhausted and strength dwindling, without ever receiving reinforcements, the city gates were breached, and the enemy's bullets rained down like hailstones, calling for the lives of the 700 coalition forces. They adjusted their clothing, bowed four times to the north, and after praying for the security of the nation and the people, they realized that the time was critical, that their mission was a great undertaking, and that their ultimate end was gloriously commendable. It is truly a sorrowful matter that we do not know about the 700 righteous men of Geumsan! Also, it is tragic to be unaware of where their graves might lie...  

It is indeed a sorrowful affair! If this is our decision, it must be a great one! Few are those who strive to know, and to have individuals among our kin deterring such endeavors is an unbearable matter! It is a pathetic state! It invites despair!  

1. Five-Year-Old Miraculous Sandalwood Fragrance  
Ahh! Summer heat! Under the scorching summer sun, the iron melts and precious stones burn; it is easy to imagine how life itself suffers! The farmers in the fields, sweat dripping from their brows like molten bronze, feel the heat not just as warm, but rather hot to the touch. If the sun bearing down on them is already unbearable, earth itself emits a stifling steam that makes breathing difficult. On the opposite hill, a large ox, tethered to the willow trees, shows no interest in the grazing grass, lifting its head until it seems parallel with its back, languidly drooling its long tongue and heaving dramatically as its massive body sways. 

Yet, amidst this terrifying hellish heat, there exists an indescribably refreshing spot—an earthly paradise. The silk, on the branches (地上仙) and the ether (枝上仙) exists there, singing a symphony of sounds. The cicadas, in all their varieties, contribute their melodious singing, and who could fail to envy the purity and freshness of those sounds? Thus, the ancients referred to cicadas as the insects of the celestial beings. 

In the greenery of the mountainous fields, small pavilions were erected, where several children, aged four or five, studied. Inside, some recited the poem 'Meeting Cold Food while on Horse' (馬上逢寒食, 途中屬暮春 唐音), while others loudly read 'Scriptures of the Calculation by the Crag' (經水無風也自波=聯朱詩). The sounds of their reading mingled with the clear babbling of the creek, creating a melody that transcended the sufferings of human society. I thought, those farmers struggling in the fields, sweating, truly deserved to be called the heavenly beings of the earth.  

This earthly paradise existed in the countryside of Okcheon (沃川郡) in Hoseo (湖西), around 370 years ago. The celestial beings of the earth and heaven had no knowledge of the sweetness and bitterness of the mundane world and emerged to create this natural music that flowed at that time. The main road leading from Okcheon to Yeongdong (永同) passed straight through there, and suddenly sounds of instruments and the rattle of bells could be heard along with the commotion. 

Everyone gathered to see the heralding of the lord's procession, and in the midst of the crowd, a child called out with all his strength, "Look! The lord is passing! Go see! Go see..." One boy, excitedly, left his inkstone on the ground without a second thought and dashed towards the road without looking back. 

Upon hearing these words, the other children dropped their books and brushes, rushing off in a flurry, creating chaos. Sounds of instruments reverberated along the roads. The lord, seated under a large palanquin (加碼), gazed around with delight, full of self-satisfaction and smirking at the surroundings. This was the epitome of the luxurious lifestyle of feudal officials in ancient times, exemplifying the disparity between the high-class and the masses. 

Yet once they turned up the narrow winding path of the hills, the enchanting music suddenly stopped, and a profound silence fell, as they passed by the stream flowing away from view. As the procession neared the pavilion, the lord halted, and everyone, including those of his company, turned to see what had caught his attention. 

Intrigued, he observed a boy of five or six, exquisite in appearance, earnestly reading the text 'The Eight Counties under Household Seal' (戶封八縣 家給千兵).  

The county governor was astounded! 

At that time, the county governor, commanding the authority akin to that of a local king, held the lives and rights of the populace in his hands. Thus, from the perspective of the governor, commoners were nothing more than approaches to ensure sustenance. Thousands upon thousands of peasant families were simply a resource to exploit, and peasants often resigned themselves to this reality, expecting to offer up what little was surplus to the lord. In this procession through Okcheon, while there were musicians contributing to its grandeur, there were also constraints tied to imprisonment and punishment riding alongside. The atmosphere was grave and distinctly oppressive.  

As they passed by, the people, some giving tobacco to the governor, others drunkenly acting out, some fighting without shirts on—anyone behaving disrespectfully would be captured and exposed to corporal punishment, while the imposing presence of the lord wielded a mindful authority over the general populace. Amidst this, the governor’s thoughts turned to the boy still reading quietly.  

"Why is this young lad not joining the masses here to watch such an opulent spectacle—the lord passing by? Even a clueless child would come forth to witness my grandeur, yet this youth is left just buried in his books."  

Contemplating this made him feel oddly concerned but also somewhat disheartened, thus stopping the procession to summon the boy. "Come forth, boy!"

Gazing at the boy caught under the palanquin, the governor could see that the boy was remarkably handsome. 

"Come here, what is your name?" 

"Baekcheon (白川) T'oga (趙氏)..."

"What do you read?" 

"Daebok (大福=儿名)..."

"How old are you?" 

"Five years..."

"And where do you live?" 

"There—at the X village..."

"You, boy! You must be from the city! Speaking without honorifics..." 

The governor laughed heartily at the boy’s reply and amiable demeanor. 

"But why do you not come out to see the splendid procession? It is because I—the lord—am here to bewail you’ve chosen to read alone instead of joining in on the amusements?"  

Hearing this, the boy stood up and bowed again, his sleeves lifted respectfully, responding, "My father says that if you let your mind wander while reading, it's unworthy of a person who studies. So when I read, I only focus on the book..."  

The words of a five-year-old boy flowed with such gravitas; what maturity for one so young! Anyone observing could not help but admire him for that. The county governor found himself greatly moved by Daebok's composure and what he had heard. 

He chuckled and proudly placed the boy on his knees, stroking the top of his head, "Ah, Daebok! What good does it do you when you read so diligently?"  

"My father says reading makes one a great person and enables them to serve the nation."  

"What do you suppose a great person does?"  

"Ah! What would that be? Doing great deeds!"  

"And what do you mean by great deeds?"  

"Working for the country is what great deeds are."  

"Oh my! This boy, raised and taught in such a manner, will surely become someone great in time..." 

As he mused, "Call a servant!" 

"Yes!"  

When the chief servant approached, bowing deeply, the lord asked, "Do you have gold or jewels with you?"  

"Yes—I do have several strings of gold."  

"Then bring me one string!"  

The servant fetched a string of gold. The governor, taking it, sealed it in a silk pouch, writing the name 'Hong Jong-deok (洪種德)' on it. "This is a reward from me, young Daebok. May you grow and eventually accomplish great deeds for your nation."

And after this encounter, the pompous procession continued, resuming its music and departing. This remarkable young boy, Daebok, later went by the name Chaehon (趙憲), taking the title of Jungbong (趙重峯) and ten years hence became a major general, leading a coalition against the invading Japanese army. In the end, it was the golden pound presented by the governor Hong Jong-deok that emerged in the midst of chaos, turning into a furious storm upon battle, as the path from Geumsan (錦山) caught fire in a magnificent finale—who could have predicted such an outcome at that moment?

**Summary**  

The article reflects on the forgotten heroes of Korea’s colonial history, particularly the 700 righteous men of Geumsan, who embraced their fate in a struggle against colonial powers. The poignant narrative not only illustrates the commitment of these brave individuals but also serves as a critique of social hierarchies in traditional Korean society. Historians would be interested in this portrayal of resistance, as it emphasizes a cultural legacy of valor amidst national oppression and raises questions about collective memory in the face of colonial rule.
