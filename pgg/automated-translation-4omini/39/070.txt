기사제목	白晝大京城에서 「방갓」을 쓰고 본 세상 (Seeing the World Wearing a "Banggat" in Broad Daylight in the Great Capital)  
필자	李瑞求 (Yi Su-kyu)  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)  

In 1931!  

April 9th, at 0:40 AM!  

Mother! You passed into the next world without even being able to open your eyes one last time and leave a word of farewell to your awfully beloved children. That night at Daokdong Jeongseong Hospital, I was dozing off beside your body while my younger brother snored at your feet, and my father had gone to our home in Gwanghwamun to tend to his swollen eyes. Alone, I was awake, looking after your body when suddenly I stepped out to get some fresh air, unable even to stay in bed. I heard in my dream, “Ah, my heart is finally lightening a bit,” and thought, “Oh, it seems she is about to get better now.”

The doctor continued to give her heart stimulants over ten times, thinking her heart was finally getting stronger. However, that did not coincide with her final departure. I still heard the words “I’m going to lie down a bit more comfortably,” which made me rush to your side. Just ten minutes later, I heard an unusual muffling sound from your head, extremely quietly, three or four times in continuation. At first, I did not mind, but soon it frightened me, and when I opened my eyes wide and called, “Mother!” and rushed to embrace you, I found you peacefully sleeping, surrounded by all the cherished relationships in your life, including your husband, who had longed for grandchildren.

Having become a mourned son, I now had a “banggat (방갓)” (a traditional Korean hat) and a funeral robe, as well as a headscarf, but was destined not to wander the streets wearing them. I did not think of these cumbersome cultural forms of an era gone by as filial duty to my departed mother. However, I living in Shiheung, where my father’s friends still dwell, practically had no choice but to comply with the customs, habits, and rituals in my sonly obligations. Thus, I would wear the banggat until I could return to my hometown for a while. Having inherited the foot-binding shame of rural poverty in the very center of the capital, I was now a country bellower entirely at the mercy of this kind of fate.

When I was on my way to Gyeongseong Station to take the tram, a flood of passengers prevented me from getting on.  

“Sir! Come on the next train!” 

The sleepy young conductor said to turn me away. Frustrated, I raised my banggat and opened my eyes wide, asking, “Why do you refuse me?” As I was arguing, the train departed, leaving me in anger. Without the banggat, there I would be exposed to scorn; and if I bent down too much, I could not see well enough to comfortably proceed. With no one watching where I was going and dodging traffic, I thought of my uncle who had been hit by an automobile while wearing a banggat at Hwanggeumjeong four years back and suffered from internal injuries.

I entered a bookstore to buy some monthly magazines to stave off boredom while in the countryside. Although the place was small and narrow, it was getting busier with dozens of people visiting. While entering, I thought about my wearing the banggat, and with that mindset, I crossed in as if it was an ordinary day just to browse through the magazines.

When I raised my hand to examine some issues of magazines, a Japanese gentleman dressed in a suit shot me a look, saying “Excuse me, you can’t come in!” Suddenly realizing I was still wearing the banggat drew a sense of anger within me. 

Disgruntled, I muttered, “Shut up.” I felt that a rude remark like that could only be an insult, suggesting that in Korean one could politely enter without a fuss. Thinking perhaps my Korean speech was not sharp enough, I decided silently there would be no more comments from me. 

After picking up a few magazines, I exited back onto Meiji Street. I noticed a Japanese store having a special for some rings, which lured me in. Considerably aware of how I might be undervalued there, as I moved further inside, a clerk hurriedly came and began wagging his finger while saying, “Excuse me, you can’t come in!” The most disdainful treatment against Koreans often comes in the form of clumsy Japanese accents where they speak rudely.

I lost my temper and shouted, “Fool!” 

It was a loud response directed at that little subordinate dressed just like him. I rebuked, “You, why is there someone so rude treating customers this way?” At last, it seemed the “little Japanese” had lost his confidence, and even the owner of the store came to talk to me. Presenting my ring, I expressed feelings of discontent, lamenting that it was difficult to live as a Korean gentleman in a Japanese store. 

Going out dressed in the banggat was uncomfortable, especially as I navigated through Jongno's four corners, which I used to stroll through freely. I realized my acquaintances did not recognize me, and even some famous actors I once knew just passed by. 

Those are the common critics. 

“Ah, Mr. Ri! What brings you here?”

They made remarks. But as I moved onwards, I remained resolute, not allowing any thoughts of mistrust to entertain. 

A little past evening, I was on my way back home and headed for Gwanghwamun Street to catch a tram. It’s the busiest avenue in Gyeongseong, and I witnessed the tram coming down the wide street. Unfortunately, just as I was about to cross, a banggat seller appeared gaping ahead on the path. 

I felt a sudden wave of disbelief wash over me. Was this that same fate once again stalking my banggat-worn appearance?

In this way, my first day out in Seoul wearing a banggat ended with contempt and scorn. It was a sorrowful experience. Who has reduced the sacred custom of this honorable nation to a pitiful figure? I realized the humiliation I experienced wearing the banggat was not a recent affair, as some years back, when my grandfather passed away, I too wore a traditional robe and trekked to Gyeongseong. Carrying a large burden while boarding an express train was an extreme hardship. 

When I arrived, I planned to sneak in amongst the newly disembarked individuals. I was required to buy a ticket plus squeeze within the crowd to relocate to a place. Then at the gate, I was apprehended. 

“Why is this luggage so big?” 

It was plainly an implicit satirical jab at me from that issued remark. Whether or not his derision was aimed at my local customs, I could not be angry. “What does that matter?” I asked in refinement.

The officer in charge took hold of my arm and led me out, skeptic about my presence. I was clueless about the reasons behind their practices. 

“Why are you crowding?” he had said angrily in Japanese. 

In utter frustration, I found an opportunity to challenge him about his enforcing rules on my behalf that I had not violated. He further pressed beneath summons from another, asserting, “You must apologize in front of others for disrespecting the native people.” This I truly found awkward. Some listeners were observing and acquiring a sense of deep discomfort about how lowly treated I had been. 

At last, about eight or nine years ago, while working for the Dong-A Ilbo (東亞日報), during a large fire incident when I went on to cover the scene, dressed somewhat with a sword and winter hat amongst other reporters, I fell victim to the same situation. 

Even under pushing on ahead, I became snared alone wearing Korean clothing. Hence I found myself taken in under claimed “impedance of public duties.” 

While enduring all of this over many years, certain influential friends of mine, including Ji Jang-sook and other newspaper editors had to communicate on my behalf to the police chief on the particular matter of that time, at length getting the chance to be released afterward. 

From that incident, the observations of many in attendance were more often similar to the outlooks of various Korean who also observed in despondent silence. I afterwards went to present my sentiments directly to the police chief regarding how not all individuals in Korean attire are unruly or don’t conduct in accordance with expectations, and that there lies a footing of commendable citizens in native garments whom your officers should recognize. 

I have worn Korean clothing scarcely once or twice a year throughout my span of a decade at the Gyeongseong news office, yet it felt like every single occasion brought on a likely mishap. 

**Summary**

The speaker reflects on their experiences mourning their mother while wearing traditional Korean attire, particularly the banggat, which led to humiliating encounters in 1930s Seoul. They describe the societal disdain directed towards Koreans wearing traditional garb, revealing deep-rooted issues of identity, class, and the impacts of colonial rule. Historians may find it significant that such cultural attire prompted not only social scorn but also highlighted the broader struggle of Koreans maintaining their identity under Japanese colonial rule.
