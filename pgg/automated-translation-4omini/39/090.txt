統計室  
Last year in Korea, the number of marriages and divorces:  
The total number of marriages was 199,090 and divorces were 9,077.  

From the statistics of the past five years regarding the number of marriages and divorces, relationship between spouses, and situation by region, the total number of marriages conducted last year was 199,281 (Koreans 197,563, Japanese 1,683, foreigners 135), averaging 546 cases per day, which is an increase of 5,016 cases compared to the previous year, while the number of divorces was 9,077, averaging 25 cases per day. The regions with the highest number of marriages were Gangwon, Pyongan South, Pyongan North, and Hamgyeong South, whereas the region with the least was Gyeongnam. The detailed statistics are as follows:  

Married person age distribution: Japanese men (ages 25-30: 44%), Koreans (under 20 years: 38%) = Japanese women (ages 20-25: 52%), Koreans (under 20 years: 60%)  

Average marriage age: Japanese men (29.3 years) wife (23.1 years), Koreans men (23.6 years) wife (20.3 years)  

Among the Korean married population, there were 13,059 men (total 6.6%) and 10,683 women (total 5.4%) who were below the legal marriage age.  

Divorces: 9,077 (average 2,488 per day)  

Divorce age distribution: Japanese and Korean men (ages 25-30: 34% of total Japanese, 29% of total Koreans) and women (ages 20-25: 33% of total Japanese, 31% of total Koreans).  

Status of incarcerated individuals in Korea consuming rice, as of the end of February this year:  
As of the end of February this year, the total number of individuals in each prison and related facilities was 16,719, consisting of 16,013 Koreans, 507 Japanese, and 199 foreigners. If categorized by the type of punishment, there are 14,037 convicts, 1,918 criminal defendants, and 594 detained workers.

Significant political offenders include violations of the Maintenance of Law and Order Act with 229 men and 2 women, and violations of the Security Act with 44 men, and the crime of riots with 15 men.  

Comparing this inmate population statistic to the year of the merger (Meiji 43) which had 7,021, there has been an increase of 9,698 individuals.  

Consequently, every year the number of individuals in prisons has increased leading to overcrowding, surpassing their capacity of 11,000 by 6,000. This has resulted in some cells being cramped with up to 6 individuals per square meter (as opposed to Japan's 1 person per 3 square meters and Taiwan's 1 person per 4 square meters).  

Since issues like budget constraints and insufficient prison expansion have arisen, the first plan starting from Showa 6 is to build single cells for remand prisoners within the territory of Seodaemun Prison, which is expected to offer some relief, though it is unlikely to be highly effective, thus bail will be promoted with approximately 1,000 individuals expected to be released on bail in Showa 6.  

Criminal offenses resulting in individuals detained in jail:  
In the year Showa 4, the number of criminal occurrences handled by police stations in Korea was 177,806, which is an increase of over 18,000 cases compared to the previous year, and compared to the 82,900 cases in the year Taisho 10, this is more than double.  

The nature of the crimes revealed theft as the most prevalent with 46,000 cases, followed by administrative law violations with 28,000 cases and fraud with 24,000 cases.  

The increase in theft and fraud cases is consistent with trends in other countries, but the significant increase in administrative law violators is unique to Korea. Compared to the 7,017 cases in Taisho 11, there has been a threefold increase (the increase in theft was just about 19,000 cases, which is only double).  

In Korea, due to the ongoing prevalence of police politics, competing and wrongful convictions are rampant. As of Showa 2 (and the subsequent statistics are yet to be announced), among the 32,220 individuals sent for prosecution by the police stations, only about half, approximately 16,776 individuals were formally charged, while the remaining were either indefinitely postponed, not charged, or released.  

**Summary**  

This article provides detailed statistics on marriage, divorce, and crime in colonial Korea during the early 1930s, illustrating the socio-economic dynamics of the time. Notably, the data reveals an alarming increase in crime rates and prison overcrowding, shedding light on the impact of Japanese colonial governance on Korean society and the legal system. Historians may find the stark contrast in the demographics of criminals and the legal traffic in the penal system particularly revealing, highlighting systemic issues deeply rooted in colonial rule.
