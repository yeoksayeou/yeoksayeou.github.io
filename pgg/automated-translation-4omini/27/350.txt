新綠想華  
崔泳柱  
"Mother! Mother! The bracken has come up!"  

The voice of my poor younger sister, who had been lost in the haze of time, calling last year as she eagerly went to the big mountain to gather wild herbs, still echoes in my ears. Mother replied, "Yes! How wonderful!" as she brought out the tender young bracken and mixed it with pine needles.  

Even now, everything is as green as it was then. The whole world is covered in a vibrant green. The trees, the mountains, the sky, the fields — everything is green. And this year too, our family went to the big mountain to gather wild herbs.  

As I went to pick herbs, I saw the newly sprouted bracken, and suddenly thought of my young sister who had just entered the world. I shouted, "Mother! Mother! The bracken has come up!"  

Mother, responding as if to my sister last year, was looking at me with tears in her eyes and forced a smile upon her lips.  

The clear days of new green quietly allow me to lie down on the grass and gaze at the clouds drifting in the blue sky. They remind me of the shadows of the past and the shadows that will come again, painting them in my mind.  

Once, at this time of year, I lay down under the willow tree near Monamdong Falls with my friend Sŏkch'ung and read Kikuuchi Kan's "Diary of a Nameless Writer," unable to straighten my back.  

That laughter was a mix of joyous laughter and bitter laughter. It was laughter that sprang forth from the words that accurately captured our hearts. That laughter raced in the blue sky just like today. Each of us fell into our own imaginings, breathing in the blue air and lying on the fragrant grass. Back then, even our fingers could freely touch each other.  

The hurried steps of gathering herbs took me back to Monamdong Falls. I must have wandered around the willow tree many times. Then I lay down again, listening to the sound of mosquitoes coming from the village below, quieter than the sound of "Hey! Come here!"  

**Summary**  

This article evokes a sense of nostalgia through the imagery of gathering wild herbs in the mountains and the memories associated with familial connections. The author reflects on personal and collective experiences tied to nature during a time of childhood innocence. This piece highlights the simple joys found in nature, contrasting with the turbulent socio-political climate of Korea under Japanese colonial rule. Historians may find interesting parallels between this nostalgic longing for simpler times and the broader themes of loss and longing in Korean literature during the colonial period.
