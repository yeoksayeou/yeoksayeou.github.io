New Green  

Beautiful May has returned.  

The fragrance that remains is still invigorating, while the green leaves reflect that a new month of May has arrived.  

When spring comes, the spring rains have already vanished. The sky is shrouded in thick vapor, and it does not appear clear. No matter which tree branch one examines, there is not a single tree devoid of leaves. In spring, while other trees bud and flourish, some trees still cling to the desolation of winter, which brings a sense of inadequacy to anyone observing it from a distance. However, once May arrives, this feeling disappears. The heart of a person is naturally filled with a carefree spirit, and all the trees, even those that look shriveled or pitiful, bloom with new leaves that saturate the world in green. It is only then that we can witness a great harmony.

The impulse that draws human hearts into nature must certainly be part of God's design. I have often realized that the regrets from the past are particularly strong in spring, and I have come to know that these regrettable moments often stroll beneath the new green leaves. New green is something that calms the heart and inspires contemplation; what could one compare it to in the water?

New green is a good friend. Although it is not just quaint and lovely like a humble house, its clarity and gentleness fill us with a profound sense of inspiration. Gazing at the new green, seeing the fresh sprouts like blue jewels, and watching the fledglings flutter as they learn to fly, we come to appreciate the beauty of life. Thus, how beautifully we can meander in a world of serene contemplation.

The Romans say, "Memento mori," meaning "Remember your death." It is a phrase likely meant to encourage deeper thinking in a person whose eyes do not grasp the reality and who rushes like an electric figure.

We have the ability to look upon our own lives, to recognize our happiness and unhappiness at their true value. That way, we can illuminate life and consider death brightly, as the Buddhist scriptures say.

New green is indeed a good friend. Beneath the new green leaves, we can come to experience a tranquil passion. There, we can converse with our own selves and savor the reality of our existence and our happiness.

Many indulgent poems arise from the trees, yet saints like Tagore always gaze upon the new green and recite poetry. Each time I see new green, I feel regret for not being a poet.  

**Summary**

In "New Green," Lee Tae-jun reflects on the arrival of May and the renewed vitality it brings. He meditates on how nature affects human emotions and encourages introspection, drawing parallels between the vibrancy of spring and the introspective moment for personal growth. The article reflects the deep connection between nature and emotional well-being, embodying a sense of optimism that may resonate with an audience experiencing colonial constraints. Historians might find Lee's voice an insightful expression of Korean identity during the colonial era, emphasizing the healing power of nature amid social turbulence.

新綠은 조흔친구외다。

나무 밋헤서는 淫迭한詩가 만히을퍼젓슴니다 그러나 『타코어-』가튼聖者는 늘 新綠을 바라보며 詩를 을펏슴니다。
