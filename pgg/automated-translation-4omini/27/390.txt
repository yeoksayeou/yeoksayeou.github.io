戱曲 落日 (一幕)  

This play must be written in the dialect of Jeolla-do because the location is Jeolla-do. However, the original intention of this play is not to express local color, so I will avoid the harsh and difficult dialect and use standard Korean... (Author)  

Characters  
Jambong, about 50 years old, landowner  
Sang-geun, about 30 years old, Jambong's child (but does not appear in the play)  
Sang-ho, 26 years old, Jambong’s child  
Sang-cheon, 21 years old, Jambong’s child  
Sang-hee, 18 years old, Jambong’s child (but does not appear in the play)  
Gyeong-o, 21 years old, Sang-hee’s boyfriend  
Kang saengwon, about 50 years old, guest  
Song Seondal, 45 or 46 years old, a relative of Jambong  
Yong-bok, servant  
Western gentleman, bank clerk  
Police officer Jo-sang, local policeman  
Detective A, B, C (A is Japanese)  

Location  
A rural village in Jeolla-do  

Time  
Modern, mid-September afternoon  

Stage  
A wide room with four corners and a thick wooden floor. A part of a room with a door made of paper on the left side is visible. On the right and front, all windows originally made of glass have been replaced with wooden boards, and stacks of dried rice are piled up on the stairs seen from the right back.  

In the back, there is a white ash wall in the middle, and on the wall is a piece of calligraphy by Heo Syo-chi. There are doors on both sides of the wall, and through those doors, the sunset shines in an oblique angle from the left, casting a shadow on the wooden floor. (The characters should position themselves as much as possible so that they do not obstruct the audience's view.)  

In the back, the door and the right side show a part of a wide threshing ground. The wooden floor is polished, and on the right side, it is painted with a fresh coat of paint.  

There are four small cushions made of rush mats, and in the center is a Go board, a tobacco case, stones, and a writing brush.  

There are small shelves hanging on the left, back, and right. In the front, two round wooden pillars are fixed on pumpkin-shaped bases, and the house still smells new, as if from freshly cut pine.  

As the curtain rises, Jambong (who wears a white tunic over his head and a yellow jacket with cotton socks) holds a tobacco case, leaning sideways against the back wall towards the hanging scroll. Kang saengwon (with a topknot, wearing a loose tunic and cloth shoes) sits on a cushion and also looks at the hanging scroll.  

Jambong: (nodding his head and speaking to himself) What a genius! I’m entranced!  

Kang: Yes, it’s truly a genius! (Pause) So, the King Cheoljong, while in the palace, must have painted peonies, right? When the King Cheoljong saw the work, he said, "Well done! Since there is a great fool (黃大痴) in the great world, then you must be 'Sochi'..." and hence assigned the name "Heo Sochi," you know. (Pause) What a remarkable name it is!  

Jambong: Isn’t he from Jindo?  

Kang: Yes, he was truly a man of high stature. (Pause) Still, since he gained such fame, the country conferred upon him a title.  

While Jambong and Kang are talking, Song Seondal appears from the left (wearing an old straw hat, a loose old coat, rubber shoes, and socks, with an old bamboo fan in his hand). He coughs loudly and enters the room.  

Song: Whew, it’s hot!  

Jambong: (turns around simultaneously) Oh, you’re here?  

Kang: (turns and looks) Isn’t it terribly hot?  

Song: (to Jambong) Oh, what is this? (to Kang) Whew, it’s really hot! (He takes off his hat and coat and hangs them on the pillar) The sunlight is really pouring in here! (He begins to fan himself)  

Jambong: Yes, during sunset the light gradually enters like this.  

Song: (stepping between the two and looking at the scroll) What’s this?  

Jambong: One has been sold.  

Song: Oh (stands up and goes to look at the scroll) Oh, it's Sochi’s work? (Then not quite satisfied, he begins to comment) This is wonderful! It goes very well! (To Jambong) How much did you sell it for?  

Kang: (tentatively) If I say it, I wouldn’t call it a favorable price.  

Song: What? You lad! You’re so naive! (looking at the scroll and shaking his head) I reckon it’s worth 70 or 80 won.  

Jambong: Kang, let’s exchange glances and grin.  

Song: (looking around) Why? Didn’t you get much?  

Kang: (turning around sharply) Goose! You pretend to be knowledgeable!  

Jambong: (turning to the left) I sold it for 30 won, you know. (smiling)  

Song: (feigned shock) 30 won? What a fortune!  

Kang: It’s not a fortune for sure; 30 won is a cheap price! But...  

Song: Truly! Cheap, you say? It’s far better than that!  

Kang: (chiding) Cheap is not worthy of mention here! In this poor year, isn’t 30 won hard to earn?  

Jambong: So, since in this poor year it’s worth thinking about why money is not being raised, even 3 won or less would be a steal, but isn’t Sochi a famous master after all?  

Song: A master indeed! A mere commoner from the island, but such a work should be revered throughout history!  

Jambong: In this world, it signifies the value of being recognized.  

Song: (suddenly thinking) But you know, everyone envies you so much! (Pause) Now you too.  

Jambong: (confused) What do you mean by that?  

Song: You also have such a son, don’t you?  

Jambong: Such a son?  

Song: You know? Isn’t that Sang-ho?  

Jambong: Yes, Sang-ho?  

Song: That boy went to Japan and is studying painting.  

Jambong: (laughs) Oh, you too always! (Pause) What nonsense! What a famous painter! He’s enrolled in the law department of a Japanese university! (Pause) Next spring he’ll graduate as a doctor of law and aim to be an officer by passing the local government exam, so he asked me to delay this a few days before going to study!  

Song: (genuinely surprised) Then what’s this news in today’s paper?  

Jambong: (leaning forward) News? What has come out?  

Song: In the newspaper, aren’t you the one from Gimje, Song Mun-kyu?  

Jambong: Yes.  

Song: And his second son is Song Sang-ho!  

Jambong: Yes, that’s Sang-ho.  

Song: That Song Sang-ho submitted a famous painting—it was (smirking)—what was it called? In essence, he submitted a highly esteemed painting at a national exhibition.  

Jambong: (cutting off) What exhibition? What do you mean by submission?  

Song: I don’t know the new character well, but it seems like it’s an event where many people collect paintings.  

Jambong: (frustrated) So?  

Song: That painting is selected for the special prize, and they say that Song Sang-ho is the first genius in Korea.  

Jambong: (more suspicious) Is that likely? (Looking at Kang) You check the newspaper today!  

Kang: (goes into the room)  

Jambong: Is it real?  

Song: (seriously) Come on! Why would I lie about such matters?  

Kang: (coming out with the newspaper) Here it is, here it is.  

Jambong: Is it true?  

Kang: (hands the newspaper, pointing to where the article is)  

Jambong: (takes the newspaper and reads) Yes, yes (after reading) Oh, this is something unexpected. (Turning to the back stage) Yong-bok!  

Yong-bok's voice: Yes— (enters from the right) Yes?  

Jambong: You—go to the lower house and tell our second son to come! (His expression is deeply troubled)  

Yong-bok: Yes— (exits to the left)  

While Jambong is reading the newspaper again, the sound of Sang-ho can be heard, and Kang and Song smoke quietly. Shortly after, Sang-ho and Sang-cheon appear from the left. They are both wearing white tunics. Sang-ho has long hair, a calm face, and delicate hands and feet. Sang-cheon is the opposite with a sporty hairstyle, cheerful face, and lively expression. Their faces show slight signs of argument. The brothers, standing there, do not yet appear in the great room.  

Sang-ho: (pausing and turning to Sang-cheon) No matter how you argue, it's all pointless. You throw around terms like materialism, dialectics, and class struggle, but we are above it all. If you think you can venture into the captivating art world that we embrace, you are gravely mistaken.

Sang-cheon: (mockingly) It seems theory works for you, huh?  

Sang-ho: We dismiss that notion entirely; the essence of art, the peak of artistic pursuit, holds no need for such theories if they aren’t there in your grasp.

Sang-cheon: Our theories arise from reality, though.  

Sang-ho: The reality you speak of is mere fancy?  

Sang-cheon: With Russia around, what’s fanciful about it?  

Sang-ho: In a few days, whether in a hundred or a thousand Russians, they can’t breach our true artistic experience. Such the intoxication of art! (in a flurry of sentiment) That taste to feel deeply entranced!  

Sang-cheon: Sounds an awful lot like you are just chasing after drugs of pleasure.

Jambong: (disturbed by his children’s voices) Where’s Sang-ho?  

Sang-ho: Yes— (as if puzzled, looks at Sang-cheon)  

Sang-cheon steps out to take a look at the stage and noticing Jambong’s expression perplexed, he turns slightly and gestures to Sang-ho with a look of fear before leaving to the left side. Sang-ho comes up to greet Song-seong.  

Jambong: (to Sang-ho, handing over the newspaper) Here, read this. What’s the matter?  

Sang-ho: (hesitantly takes the newspaper)  

Kang: (leans closer, pointing to the article)  

Sang-ho: (as he reads, his expression changes, becoming more complex. After finishing, he can’t lift his head and fumbles with the newspaper)  

Jambong: (watching him closely, noticing his expression change and suddenly getting angry) What’s the meaning of this? Do you understand?  

Sang-ho: (barely in a whisper) Yes.  

Jambong: Yes—what? Is it real?  

Sang-ho: (silent)  

Jambong: Hmm?  

Sang-ho: (in an even smaller voice) Yes.  

Jambong: (yelling with rage) What? (after staring at Sang-ho for a while, his frustration reaches its peak) What kind of scoundrel is this! How could this child come to think that he—this morning was attending the law department of a Japanese university! (Pause) This brat! You are deceiving me so? (Pause) Though you have deceived me, it’s clear that you indeed, despite spending seven to eight won per month for study, have been painting for five years! A painter? (anxiously) A painter from the son of Song Mun-kyu!  

Sang-ho: (silent, upset)  

Jambong: (hits the newspaper down) Ugh— (looking at Song Seondal) Oh dear, what kind of world is this? How could the child of Song Mun-kyu become a painter! (looks sternly at Sang-ho) You, leave at once! (Pause) From today, you are no child of mine! (begins to smoke wildly, banging the pipe on the cushion)  

Bank clerk: (enters from the left, dressed largely in a white linen robe and holding a black leather bag)  

Jambong: (visibly flustered, with a look of dread on his face)  

Bank clerk: (walking towards the room and takes off his hat) Is this where Mr. Song Mun-kyu lives?  

Jambong: (stammering) Yes—uh— (his face becomes pale)  

Bank clerk: Then, are you the head of the house?  

Jambong: Uh— uh— (gestures to Kang)  

Kang: (looking scared) Yes—who are you?  

Bank clerk: Yes— I have come to see the head of the household.  

Jambong: (enters the room cautiously)  

Kang: Now, who are you?  

Bank clerk: Yes— (hands out a business card) This is who I am.  

Kang: (taking the business card and finally putting himself at ease) Yes—come in. (to Jambong) Sir, come out here, uh— this is a man from the agricultural bank!  

Jambong: (coming out from the room) What do you mean? (motioning to sit on the cushion) Please come in.  

Bank clerk: (walking in and taking a seat) Is this Song Jambong? (bows)  

Jambong: (reciprocating the bow) Yes, that's me, Song Mun-kyu.  

Bank clerk: Yes, I am Cho Yong-taek from the Agricultural Bank in Gunsan. (Bows again)  

Jambong: Yes, thank you for coming in such hot weather. (Pause) I thought you were someone recruiting for my…  

Bank clerk: Haha, sorry. (Pause) However, I came to speak about a matter that isn’t bright; Mr. Jambong, regarding the borrowing of 35,000 won that was promised as confirmed.  

Jambong: (surprised) What? What is this? (Pause) I borrowed 35,000 won?  

Bank clerk: (looking confused) Yes.  

Jambong: What would I borrow that for? (looking around for agreement from others) Well, I wouldn’t have anything to spend that upon! (Pause) Not a thing! (Pause) Perhaps you confused me with someone else. (to himself) Oh, would I have spent that much?  

Bank clerk: (smiling awkwardly) But your actual name is written down, and we have a Mr. Song Sang-geun— your oldest son, right? (He opens his bag and pulls out a signed note)  

Jambong: (takes the note, looking behind him, feeling angry) This brat! Has he done such a thing?! He’s the one bringing our family down! (In a flustered state) I don’t know about this! I don’t know! I’m no longer part of this family! (Pause) You can send him to prison or make him pay, it’s up to you! (Exhales deeply).  

Sang-cheon, Sang-hee, Gyeong-o enter from the left. Sang-hee wears a white dress and sandals with a large parasol. Gyeong-o has a beige suit, spats, and white shoes. The appearance of these well-dressed individuals is clear and prominent.  

As this occurs, Song Seondal, Kang saengwon, and the bank clerk all shift aside and enter the room, while Sang-ho also greets them with a gaze before stepping inside.  

Sang-hee: (calling from the room) Father, (bowing her head)  

Jambong: (looking at Sang-hee, then at Gyeong-o, growing furious but unable to speak, just glaring)  

Gyeong-o: (standing next to Sang-hee, removing his hat and bowing)  

Jambong: (getting up hastily) You girl! Get out of my sight this instant?! (Looks at Gyeong-o) Who are you? (Pause) I cannot see you! I don’t allow you to enter my home?! Why are these couples reeling in with the so-called free marriage???  

Sang-cheon: (stepping forward, a bit spoiled) Father, please forgive us…?  

Jambong: (looking at Sang-cheon, aside) All of you—out! Out! (Pause) It is like my entire family has fallen into disgrace! Get out!  

Sang-cheon: (calmly, trying to act charming) Father, if you only let us go, wouldn’t it be better just to give us some money to spend? (Proceeds to be rather insistent) After all, aren’t you my own child? Even though I did wrong, wouldn't it be sad for me to suffer out there?

Jambong: (fully furious) Get out! You brat! (Pause) Get out! (Pause) The world is in disarray, and my family is falling apart! All of you—scram!  

A police officer and two detectives enter from the left. Sang-hee and Gyeong-o step aside to the right.  

Jambong: (making efforts to calm his anger) Jo-sang? Please come out!  

Police officer: (raising his hand in salute) Yes.  

Sang-cheon: (calling to Detective A) Long time no see.  

Detective A: Actually, I would like you to accompany me today to the main office.  

Sang-cheon: (as if understanding everything) Yes (folds his arms and thinks)  

Jambong: (noticing that a detective has come in) What is it, Jo-sang?  

Police officer: Yes, (looking troubled) it’s that Sang-cheon has been implicated in some investigations (Pause) and we must search the home as well.  

Jambong: (shocked and unable to even act surprised) What? Implicated? Searching the home? What crime does he have? (Pause) Is there a police officer?  

Police officer: (looking sheepish) Perhaps he’s involved in the ××× case.  

Detective A: (scolding the police officer) Hey! Don’t spout baseless words! Instead, quickly get to work!  

Police officer: Yes.  

Jambong: Huh? What’s that supposed to mean? Sang-cheon! The ××× case? You? (yelling)  

Sang-cheon: (turning pale, steps forward) Father! What’s going on is this, I (pointing to the sun setting slowly) do you see? (Pause) When I rise early and see the sun all day, it falls like this? Even if you are upset today or heavy-hearted at night, however, the sun sets. Father- though there is an exhausting day, can’t you see?  

Sang-cheon steps toward Detective B, who beside him, as if marked empty, looks over in shock as Detective and the police officer step forward into the great room. The curtain falls.  
(1930, February, 16)  

**Summary**  

This play, "Falling Sun" by Choi Man-jik, illustrates the tension between generational expectations and aspirations in a rural Korean household, wherein the protagonist Jambong grapples with the aspirations of his children, especially Sang-ho, who wishes to pursue art instead of following a traditional legal career. The play highlights conflicts that emerge under colonial pressures, revealing the struggles between individual desire and familial duty. Historians may find interest in how the narrative taps into socio-political realities of Korean colonial society, showcasing the shift in cultural values as younger generations sought autonomy in defining their identities.
