기사형태	소식 (News)  

A Survey of Working Women on the Battlefield of Life: Their Lives and Aspirations

Is it an extraordinary job? I don't know where to start and where to end. While many can manage in internal affairs, I am on business trips for more than twelve days out of the month. I have no place in the area that I have not been to, visiting towns, villages, and various locations. However, there are both troubling and joyful aspects to it.

If there is joy, it would be in traveling far and wide, enjoying the scenery, and walking alone for one or twenty miles, feeling at peace with the mountains and rivers. If I must say anyone can do this, it seems pretty slow to get used to. Whether it is the literary types or the like, ha-ha…

If one has a strong character and diligence, one can live comfortably without any worries, but for us women, it seems somewhat difficult. The current state of farming in our countryside? The situation is quite obvious. Where would we start talking about it? It was merely conjecture based on what we could see on the surface or hear through rumors. Coming from the city, I had no idea it was this grave. It is disheartening. I cannot say specifically what it is like, but can it even be called human life based on the visible lifestyle? So, are those people lazy? Absolutely not; they work far harder than others. It is fundamentally a problem, but if we were to think of a remedy immediately, wouldn’t it be to teach them at least the national language so that they could read a newspaper or a magazine? Whatever I might say, they don’t seem to listen, even if I try to explain in the simplest manner. 

It is not that I lack understanding, but it feels like hitting a wall and it’s frustrating. Currently, educated men and women pursue pleasure and fame in the city, but in reality, what must be done urgently is to assist the women and children in rural areas. I haven’t thought this through in detail, but in any case, it’s an urgent issue. I am speaking from what I have seen. 

There is a village nearby with about a hundred households. In that village, there is a man named 0, who came in and at first, everyone went hungry. However, he established a literacy school and reformed everything over about ten years, and now there is no family that goes hungry, nor anyone who cannot read and write. When you enter that village, the atmosphere is quite different. You do not feel like you are in a village at all. It might sound exaggerated when I say this, but it is truly an ideal village. Even the officials cannot act carelessly when they go there. 

It is quite common for women bureaucrats to feel ashamed and face difficulties. However, in every era and in every country, who has ever stepped into the light without encountering scorn? It is through suffering and struggle that one gradually accomplishes their work. One can say it is the suffering of the pioneers.

Women’s Tailor Lee Jiyong

I don’t know much and can’t speak well, so I might not be able to elaborate adequately. I started for the first time nine years ago. At that time, I opened my shop at the position of Yanghwa Store in the neighborhood of the Danish National Hospital, and I have moved to this location for the past three years.

It is a women's clothing store, and all the workers here are women as well, but it does not imply a shop aimed at women since all the customers are men.

While I do have workers, that is only because I lack hands, and I work together with them.

I don’t have much ambition. Traditionally, women have only been confined to household responsibilities, but I have come to think that it is preferable for women to engage in their own professional activities.

So, I plan to keep this up until I am too old to work.

In terms of income, it barely meets expenses, but compared to a salary job, it is significantly better. I feel pity for those who are still on salary positions as they seem to receive only around 40 or 50 won after working a month, and as soon as they receive it, it is usually all gone the very next day. However, even with this meager income, if I just hold on and persevere, then I can still end up with ten won now and then, and there is rarely a day when I do not earn some money.

As for housework, a helper takes care of the children and other chores, so it doesn’t much interfere with my work.

I learned my trade initially at other tailors and then spent a year in Osaka, Japan.

I am currently 37 years old.

Female Driver Lee Jeong-ok

People like me need to know how to speak… Is this really considered a proper occupation? Nonetheless, it is true that Koreans still hold a mix of hope and anxiety regarding women's jobs. I learned to drive a car just last year. Until then, I had worked as a midwife, but due to the time constraint and decreasing pay, it was becoming increasingly difficult as rivals also increased.

When I pass by automotive places, the sight of women driving evokes curiosity among everyone. However, in order to do this, I had to go through the automotive school, and when I saw the mechanics dressed in oily work clothes huddled together, I felt a sense of shame and couldn’t find any courage.

Then the Oriental Automotive Research Institute opened, and they said they would offer special treatment and teach women, so I entered. However, it was still not easy. After just one day, we had practical sessions together with men, struggling hard to survive and prove myself.

Do I face hardships because I am a woman? Yes, often I do. Some customers think of female drivers as a novelty and want to ride once just for the experience, but generally, they’d prefer a man. Thus, they often joke around, saying things like “She won’t be able to drive!” When they realize a woman is behind the wheel, it can be irritating.

Initially, I lost my cool and had the urge to slap back at those insulting me, but what could I do considering the nature of my job? So I simply ignore these remarks, although that, in turn, gets criticized for not responding. Sometimes I would pretend to show a good face to relieve the situation.

One of the most difficult aspects is dealing with drunk passengers who hassle us, making it impossible to find home until late into the night. They keep saying, “Let’s go here!” and “Let’s go there!” with loud laughter, providing no help at all in locating an address.

Those who insist on taking a ride tend to be drunk people. Japanese people seem to ride for business purposes, but Korean customers are mostly for pleasure. When the car stops, they usually just glance inside to see if the car is a Chrysler or a Ford, being completely unaware of the type. It’s quite disheartening.

Nonetheless, the income is rather good. On average, a car can earn about twenty won. Overall, my monthly income comes to about 1,200 or 1,300 won, but after all expenses, I might be left with around 600 won. When I am out driving, many people tend to ride because I am a woman, and the view from the driver's seat can be quite different. I often see Koreans not even flinching while I blow the horn to avoid an accident. Watching those who are slow to act, it feels like we, who must hurry, often walk ten or twenty steps more than they do, which is truly distressing.

Pharmacist Jang Geum-san

As for my feelings? It’s been a long time and I don’t have any particular feelings about it yet.

However, I have long been aware that professional women are looked down upon generally. Particularly, women who engage in practical work are viewed even more disdainfully. This issue arises from an incomplete understanding of women’s roles in society.

Yet, I believe that one has to endure initial hardships. There’s nothing to worry about—one must compensate with skill. If women are not any less capable than men and if they have economic strength, then that’s enough.

I work in a Japanese apothecary. Thus, I get to see their commercial techniques in action, adopting their strengths while discarding their weaknesses, I learn and practice.

Having been in the field for only a short while, I haven't faced the struggles of the profession extensively yet.

Factory Technician Choi Bong-sin

As for flavor? I live so busily that I hardly have time to think. The joys and sorrows of life, I believe, only arise from having leisure.

If you don’t have time to consider your life as troubled or happy, how can you gain insights?

As for my impressions, I graduated from school and came to the factory. It’s like moving from the best environment to the worst, from one extreme to the other, resulting in a shocking realization that it indeed exists.

Seeing the dreadful conditions for the first time was remarkable and unspeakable. I had never seen such horrific scenes before my student days; it was quite a revelation to see such a different reality upon entering the workplace.

What pains me most is the suffering of the young workers and their lack of understanding of my goodwill. It stems from their lack of education, growing up in tough situations which can be an emotional burden on them. If only they could appreciate someone truly working for their betterment, they'd dedicate themselves more.

Initially, I just wanted to teach them skills, and here I was honored with a role as a technical instructor. However, witnessing the reality made me realize that it involves more than technical skills. Thus, I have been considering their educational matters as well.

Seeing young people wasting their youth working with no opportunity for education is pitiful. Therefore, I strive to teach them something in my limited capacity, conducting night classes for an hour. On designated holidays, I spend time with them, telling stories. They initially couldn’t understand, but recently they seem to have improved.

As this work is the first for a woman, it paves a new way for female professionals. Accordingly, the responsibilities are heavy.

Indeed, following the paths laid down by previous generations should require less effort and suffering. Yet, our scope of activity is still limited, narrowing the future of job opportunities for women. Therefore, we must always think innovatively and broaden our horizons to find more possible avenues for women in the workforce and explore the breadth of professions available to them. 

Bank Employee Oh Ja-jun 

Being told to express my feelings abruptly, I can hardly find anything valuable or interesting to say due to my lack of education, but let me write a bit. I always hear my elder brother say that Korean women have a very weak economic outlook and that their status is greatly marginalized.

For this reason, when I meet women, I feel a twinge of regret in my heart.

When I applied to the upper school after finishing elementary school, my elder brother told me to choose a vocational school, and at this, he supported me. However, back then, entrusting women to vocational education was not regarded as proper.

Many schoolmates and teachers would suggest other schools, wondering why I didn't want to choose another, but despite that, I firmly maintained my ambition and entered a woman’s commercial school.

Nonetheless, I faced grave threats in my family finances and my health, experiencing endless hardships. Luck has it that I graduated from a women's college this spring, even if it was a short term. Upon receiving job offers afterward, I sharply realized just how bleak our society is and how narrow the path ahead will be.

I might be overstepping saying this, but I feel an even greater weight of responsibility for our women. No matter how narrow the path may be, we must fight even harder and not lose heart. 

If we just give up, our status will surely drop further. Our nation's economy is daily facing disasters and disturbances. Who will bear that burden? The educated young should be at the forefront. Even if education is scant and power is weak, if our new female professionals could put forth greater energy, it would be beneficial.

Thus, I aspire to serve with dedication to the best of my ability in my current position. Or to pave a weak path for other women. My interest in my profession still feels immature, and I am uncertain about what aspects I might find most enjoyable, although recently my earnings from pearl covering have become substantially better—money is truly vital, isn’t it? 

  

**Summary**

This article presents a candid exploration of the lives and hopes of women working in various fields during the late 1920s in Korea. Women from diverse backgrounds, such as public servants, tailors, drivers, pharmacists, factory workers, and bank employees express their struggles against the societal perceptions and hardships faced in the workforce, as well as their aspirations for a better future. Historians might find interest in the way these women navigate the sociopolitical landscapes of colonial Korea, highlighting their resilience and the gradual shifts in gender roles within a conservative society.

婦人洋服店 李慈鎔

아는 것이 업고 말도 잘할 줄을 몰나서 충분히 말슴을 하여 드릴 수가 업슬 것 갓슴니다.
맨 처음 시작하기는 아홉 해 전임니다. 그때는 우리 양복점 越便 德濟病院 엽헤 잇는 양화점 자리에서 개업을 햇는데 이 집으로 옴겨오기는 사년째 되엿슴니다.
일홈이 婦人洋服店이고 또 멧 사람 가티 잇는 직공도 다 부인이지만 뭣 부인을 상대로 한대서 婦人洋服店이라는 것은 아님니다. 고객의 전부가 모도 다 남자임니다.
