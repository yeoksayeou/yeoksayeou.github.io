笑話二題  
劍鬪  
The barber said, "Oh dear, I apologize. Sir, you've flinched three times now."  
The customer replied, "No worries. This time it doesn’t seem to hurt too much... But do lend me a blade."  
The barber asked, "What will you do with it?"  
The customer responded, "Since you're trying to cut me, I must defend myself."  

直感  
The poet asked, "Is the editor here?"  
The receptionist replied, "He is not."  
The poet then said, "In that case, throw this manuscript into the trash." <84>  

**Summary**  

This article consists of two light-hearted jokes highlighting misunderstandings and the absurdities present in everyday interactions. The first joke revolves around a barber and a customer in a comically tense situation involving a blade, while the second showcases a poet's frustration with a missing editor, leading to a dramatic dismissal of his manuscript. These jokes reflect the humorous undertones of life during colonial Korea, where societal roles often collided with personal agency, pointing to the struggles and dissatisfaction felt during that era. Historians might find the portrayal of occupational roles and responses to authority in these humoristic scenarios revealing of the broader social dynamics in the colonial period.
