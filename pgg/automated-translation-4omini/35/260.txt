羅馬法皇面會記  
						李甲秀  
When I left Germany, my long-held aspiration to travel to Italy began in mid-April 1925. Throughout thousands of years, millions of brave generals and countless soldiers faced death multiple times, overcoming the harsh cold winds and snow of the treacherous mountains, hesitating in their journey toward success. While comfortably gazing at Rome's elegant yet rugged landscapes from a brilliantly decorated glass-windowed house that enjoys the benefits of modern civilization, I arrived in Rome, the capital of Italy, where the Pope resides, after passing through numerous tunnels. Upon getting off the car and hopping onto one of Italy's famous horse-drawn carriages, I took a quick glance around the city; the imposing yet splendid modern buildings mixed with remnants of ancient structures were seen everywhere, and monks dressed in black hats and robes frequently came and went among the temples and on the streets—it was a unique religious atmosphere that one could hardly find elsewhere in the historically significant city of Rome.

After settling into my hotel, I first visited the ancient landmarks of Rome. As the sun began to set, I contemplated how this moment marked the end of my pursuits in ancient Rome, reviving memories of its past glory once more.

Next on my agenda was the Vatican, and upon hearing the hotel owner's advice that I should prepare to visit the Papal chambers, I got ready early the next morning. At precisely 7 AM, I stepped out, greeted by a clear blue sky with a few drifting white clouds. The weather was quite pleasant; however, the chilly wind brushing against my face reminded me somewhat of the aristocratic demeanor of the old Roman city. Upon taking a taxi to the Vatican, I arrived as many others were arriving by taxis, buses, and horse-drawn carriages to the Papal chambers. It is said that daily visitors to the Vatican number in the thousands.

As I entered St. Peter's Square, I was overwhelmed by its artistic construction, with the entire square paved with square white stones. In the center stood a tall monument surrounded by splendid lamp posts, and to the left, there were two fountains where waters cascaded down in harmony. From this monument, looking towards the front, I saw a grand ancient-style cathedral towering high above, with a lightning rod at its peak faintly visible within the clouds. Alongside this cathedral, hundreds of buildings constructed in a curvilinear shape framed the square, with the Pope's residence, administrative buildings, and offices for the officials lined up on either side.

Walking leisurely through this magnificent square towards the cathedral, I arrived at its entrance after climbing numerous stone steps. The cool breeze that whisked by from the dim interior sent shivers down my spine, as if divine phenomena were forcing me to submission. Upon regaining my senses, I observed that about four to five thousand congregation members were entering without difficulty into a rather large hall. The splendid artworks intricately carved on the grand columns and the beautifully painted stained-glass windows were indeed the pride of the Italian people, who possessed a talent for extraordinary artistry.

However, my primary purpose for arriving early that day at the Vatican was to meet the Pope. Following the instructions given by the guards at the cathedral's entrance, I was directed to join the queue for those wishing to have an audience with the Pope. When I arrived, I found that a significant crowd of several hundred men, women, and children had gathered. Inquiring here and there, I received either no response or vague answers in Italian, and I was unsure of the direction to take. Nevertheless, I pushed and squeezed through the chaotic throng, eventually finding three or four rows of people standing respectfully.

They appeared largely to be foreign gentlemen and ladies, and on either side of the rows stood Vatican officials preventing anyone from approaching. Among the crowd, I noticed a gentleman standing toward the back, who was from England and identified himself as a Catholic (old church) believer, indicating that he was about to have an audience with the Pope. Learning this made me eager to state that I had also come to meet the Pope, so he kindly suggested that I follow him.

After standing for some time, a chamber officer approached me and asked in English whether I had a "metal." Puzzled, I replied, questioning what a "metal" was. He informed me that those wishing to meet the Pope must wear a special "metal" on their chest. To obtain this "metal," one needed to apply several days in advance, either through the Vatican or certain places in the city. If I did not have the "metal," I was to leave and come back later. At this news, I was filled with disappointment. I then asked the English gentleman if he could help, explaining that I had traveled all the way from Korea specifically to meet the Pope, but I was denied entry due to my lack of a "metal" or invitation. Unfortunately, he firmly declined, stating that it was impossible, urging me to leave the queue.

Helpless, I departed from the line and began to contemplate my next steps. Just then, I noticed a monk-like gentleman in a black hat and robes strolling by. Realizing I might have a chance, I approached him, introduced myself as a Korean studying in Germany, and told him about my desire to meet the Pope and how I had just been rejected due to not having a "metal" or invitation. He seemed pleasantly surprised and spoke fluently in German.

I was relieved and asked him how he became so proficient in German. He explained that he had graduated from the Theology Department of the University of Berlin some ten years ago and then returned home to serve in the Vatican. Recognizing that we were alumni, we shook hands warmly and engaged in a detailed conversation. He then kindly led me to the main entrance.

As the previous line of visitors was beginning to file into the entrance, he gestured for me to follow closely behind. He spoke to the guard watching the gate in his native language, and saying goodbye, he disappeared into the building. At the gate, the guard, tall and clad in bright red military attire, with a helmet that shone, stood holding a long spear, vigilantly monitoring the crowd.

My heart raced as I passed by him, feeling daunted. I displayed a brave face and crossed the threshold. Once past the first set of stairs, I found two more guards stationed there, but I managed to get through without any issue. Upon reaching the second floor entrance, once again, there were two guards. Just as I was about to pass, one of them noticed that I did not have a "metal" prominently displayed on my chest.

As I continued forward, the sharp point of the spear seemed dangerously close. At that moment, I knew I had to find a way to get through, even though I could not discern if the guards’ gestures were invitations or rejections as they silently nodded their heads. 

I pressed on, and after ascending to the third floor, I was again met with two guarding soldiers. After walking down the hallway for a short distance, I arrived at a grand entrance where two more guards stood sentry. Inside the hall was spacious, capable of holding about one thousand people, magnificently adorned in yellow, white, gold, silver, and various colors of silk, all combined to create an opulent atmosphere. The hall buzzed with soldiers and officers hustling back and forth.

My senses overwhelmed, I felt as though I had entered a palace of the kingdom of Yama (the king of Hell). I was in a daze, caught in this inhuman environment. After waiting roughly 30 minutes, an officer emerged from one side and announced that the Pope would soon appear. He instructed everyone to stand in an orderly fashion and adopt a circle.

Time passed without any progress; my impatience grew as I realized that nearly 20 minutes had gone by. Glancing at my watch, I saw it was already past 11:30 AM. I had left the hotel at 7 AM, so four and a half hours had elapsed since then. At that moment, I felt a sense of grievance; I wondered if the Pope, being such a high-born person, was so difficult to approach, or if I was being foolish for going through all this effort to meet him. But the Pope allegedly possesses the "keys" to Heaven—perhaps that is the reason.

Not wanting to be ungrateful, I reminded myself that the Pope serves as the shepherd for countless souls referred to as "Papa" (meaning father). Historically, the Holy Roman Emperor Otto did not consider himself an emperor without the Pope's crowning in the late 10th century. Thereafter, the heroic Pope Gregory VII declared that in order to reform the world, the Pope must stand above all kings, serving instead of the heavens. The king then begged for forgiveness for his excommunication from the Pope in the winter of 1077 while bare-footed in the snow over the course of three days. In the 13th century, Pope Innocent III even exercised the right to depose and appoint kings and intervened in disputes between the kings of France and England.

In essence, the Pope had more power than the kings themselves during that era. But now, as I pondered the present Pope, Pius XI, it was almost maddening to hear the sudden clattering of shoes against the floor, startling me.

Looking up, I saw a commander entering, commanding the crowd to bow, indicating that the Pope would soon make his entrance. The crowd stood waiting as the time reached noon. Suddenly, the sound of boots was heard, as around 30 guards lined up solemnly, followed by several black-robed figures. Amid rising tension, a commanding voice called for silence and everyone to bow their heads.

Then, amidst all of this, a figure resembling Kaiser with bushy moustaches emerged surrounded by about ten guards in precise military attire, escorting the Pope. I watched closely; the Pope was neither exceedingly tall nor short, a bit rotund, yet dignified, appearing as a kind-hearted elderly man of about sixty, donned in colorful ceremonial robes. On top of his head, he wore a flat black hat, which made him appear as no ordinary individual.

Noticing me amid the assembly, he turned and smiled, showing interest in my appearance (since I was the only Asian person present). I bowed my head, and his attendant standing by acknowledged it, asking if I was from China.

"No," I responded. "I am from Korea, neighboring China."

"Are you a Catholic?" the Pope asked.

"Yes, I am!" I replied (if I had answered otherwise, I would have been expelled immediately).

"Then, in your country, are there many Catholics, and are there churches as well?"

"Yes, many exist."

"I am pleased to see someone from such a distant land," he replied, extending his hand, and I reached out to grasp his jeweled fingers.

He continued meeting each person, and after greeting us, he retreated through the entrance from which he arrived. About ten minutes later, we were called into a chamber.

Once inside, the Pope was seated on a high platform, delivering a sermon as an attendant translated in English. After the sermon, we were given a small box as a gift from the Pope containing a silver medal bearing the likeness of Pope Pius XI. Upon exiting the Vatican, I checked my watch and realized it was already 2 PM, meaning my visit had lasted around seven hours.

(This is an excerpt from my travel journal from around the world.)  
『完』  

**Summary**  

This account details the author's journey to meet the Pope in the Vatican, capturing not only the author's anticipation and the grandiosity of the surroundings but also the complex rituals and requirements for obtaining an audience with the Pope. The narrative brings to light the stringent protocols of religious authority and the author's unique position as a Korean in a predominantly Western context. Historians might find the juxtaposition of the author's efforts to meet a powerful religious figure against the backdrop of Korea's colonial status particularly compelling, reflecting the intersections of religion, culture, and colonial power dynamics during this era.
