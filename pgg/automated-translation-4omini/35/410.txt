발행일	1931년 02월 01일  
기사제목	내, 蒼白한 마음을 弔喪하노라 (I, Mourn the Pale Heart)  
필자	로아  
기사형태	시 (Poem)  

I, mourn the pale heart  

Coldly, like a corpse,  

teasing my eyes,  

the dancing waves of light flicker undisturbed.  

A star-swallowing dark gray sky...  

My hands, gripping tightly,  

tremble, hidden in the outer pocket.  

The breath of the frozen dead  

hangs, heavy like a thousand pounds!  

Remembering the formless shadow of a past echo,  

I seek a little peace,  

the sound of footsteps retracing  

the troubled path, thud, thud, thud...  

The food passed through the intestines ten hours ago.  

From head to intestines,  

fantasies like burnt straw fly.  

The scattered thoughts jump from fragment to fragment,  

oh, what a sight to behold!  

Even today,  

on that vast battlefield,  

you were a foolish soldier,  

unable to own a single iron bullet.  

Still, you abandon  

the slaughtered war dead, bleeding and worn,  

trampling the beautiful dream of flowers,  

sprinting across the desolate expanse.  

Oh, pale heart!  

Shall I go for suicide?  

Shall I go back to sleep?  

The sentimentality of the interior has no bounds.  

There was a time when one motivated by emotions  

held sway over the creative spirit.  

The old beggar taught fine arts  

to the tearful, enraptured beings.  

Resonating with the tears of maternal love  

and the charitable arts learned.  

What do you hesitate for?  

Since life drifted away from your attention,  

your heart has turned pale...  

Do not attempt to apply a dangerous paint  

with crude skill!  

Is there not a palette here?  

In the factory,  

the number of machines spins like a wheel in a vast loom...  

Do you lack the courage  
to hang this palette  
over your life and head?  

Do you lack the courage  
to ignite your pale heart  
in the iron mouth of the furnace?  

-1931.1.21-  

**Summary**  

This poem articulates a deep sense of melancholy and existential despair amid the backdrop of colonial Korea. The speaker expresses a personal grief, contrasted with the broader suffering of the soldiers in war, symbolizing a collective national trauma. The reflections on dreams, creativity, and the weight of societal expectations indicate the struggle of individuals under colonial rule. Historians might find it particularly revealing how personal anguish intertwines with collective memory in art during this period of oppression.
