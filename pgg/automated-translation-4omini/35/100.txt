기사제목	내가 본 내 얼굴 (My Face as I See It)  
기사형태	잡저 (Essay)  

I see my face as others see it. I think that it resembles a certain beauty, but I wonder if that's true. Hahaha...

My eyes are quite fierce. And my beard gently strokes my chin as I look in the mirror.

There are two ways to look at it: when viewed statically, there is great disappointment, but dynamically, there seems to be some charm. Hahaha...

One friend of mine said after trying to draw my face and failing, "Your face is too non-sculptural." I admit that. In other words, one might say it's non-masculine. My face is indeed non-masculine, yet it lacks any remarkable features.

An expert in physiognomy, Bae Sang-cheol, says I am a "handsome man according to bone structure." If his face was ugly, he wouldn’t have said such a thing.

My face is very dark and lacks flesh, and I feel that I resemble Abraham Lincoln (아부라함·링컨) when I look in the mirror—can such compliments be related to anything? Hahaha.

I’m not handsome. No matter how good-looking an old man may be, what good does it do him—especially after more than 20 years of suffering life's tribulations, resulting in such a face (stroking my face)?

Once, I went to Kaesong (開城) to have casual conversations, and an acquaintance, a detective, bowed deeply in surprise. He said he mistook my beard for Mount Fuji (富士山). Hahaha, how could that be compared to Mount Fuji?

As for what others say, I believe I am handsome. However, the rough place under my chin is... a bit...

**Summary**  

This essay reflects on the author’s self-perception, blending humor and an analysis of physical appearance through various societal lenses. The conversations with friends highlight different perspectives on beauty and masculinity, suggesting a deeper cultural commentary on standards of attractiveness during the Korean colonial period. Historians might find the exploration of appearance and identity in colonial Korea interesting, as it offers insight into the personal and social dynamics of that era, especially relating to Western ideals imposed on local perceptions.
