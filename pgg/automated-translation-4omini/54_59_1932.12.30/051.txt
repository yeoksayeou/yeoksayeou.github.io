A Letter from a Rural Farmer  

Today, the village official came to our village and gave a talk. Although we farmers may know something, he told us many stories. He repeatedly said, “Live by your own strength.” Now, there are various things in Joseon (Korea) regarding the phrase “live by your own strength,” but all of those require a capability we either cannot accomplish or which we are already fulfilling. Whenever we fail to do something, we do not carelessly throw away waste on the roadside, but rather we take care of it and throw it into the barley field. I wonder, when have rural farmers ever thrown out waste carelessly?

People in Seoul do not throw waste away on the streets out of fear of getting fined, but rather because they find it disgusting to waste even a drop.

Indeed, these days, the price of ammonia has skyrocketed, and we cannot afford to use barley manure. While ammonia itself may not be literally causing this spike, how can one expect to grow barley under such conditions?  

Recently, the rice price has sharply risen. I have heard it said that the rice price has gone up, and the rural farmers are happy about it. That may be correct. Last year, one gourd of rice was only worth five won, but this year it’s worth ten won. Thus, a one-won bill now goes into my pocket. Truly, isn’t that enough to make one drool?  

Yet, when I sell it at ten won, it must be rice that is available to sell! In the end, is there even a farmer left who possesses any rice right now? The farmers have finished harvesting in the early autumn, put their crops through winnowing, and after settling their debts, they have sold all the rice they had, even as they struggled to make ends meet. Now, they have not a single grain left.  

But what good is it for the price of rice to go up? It is not a blessing, rather it is a burden.  

As the rice price goes up, the price of food also increases. In our landlord's household, previously they would receive six goods (mal) for a gourd of rice, but this year it seems they are only receiving seven goods per gourd.  

Is there really any such law? However, the money they take must also be reasonable. Who knows what next year will bring? Who could predict the plight of the farmers?  

They say wear colored clothing. On market days, the officials of the county bring a barrel of ink and set up in the alleyways. When a merchant in white clothing comes, they splatter ink on their clothes and even stamp them. Then they send them back, telling them to wear dyed clothing. Nowadays, the markets are not bustling as they used to be.  

But while merchants are required to wear them, that isn't too bad. I went to the county office to pay the tax, and returned with my clothes soiled. Who knew about that? Even poor farmers go into the government offices wearing their patched clothing.  

When I explained that I had come to pay the tax, they said that was not acceptable either. In the end, I had to leave a piece of clothing behind and returned.  

Then, my tax payment wasn’t accepted, and the pressure to pay led to a notice of payment being sent to me. Despite the inconvenience of wearing dyed clothes, to force a person already wearing something to apply ink on top of it, what does that mean?  

Moreover, the tax payment deadline is coming up, but I still haven't been able to pay. This is all so exhausting.  

As the end of December approaches, the pain of suffering is overwhelming. Besides, the wives might be resting.  

The educated kids so fear to become like this, thus they send their children to school, hoping they won't turn out like their parents. However, my son, having spent three years in school, cannot even write his name properly. Nonetheless, when it comes to collecting money, he seems to do just fine.  

Having missed my chance to secure my house in the agricultural cooperative for three months now, the house went up for auction.  

Well, what good is it for farmers to possess a house anyway? But even hanging onto that, thinking all the while makes me feel frustrated and sad.  

Even though I have become this so-called farmer now, I didn’t always live like this before. Thanks to my parents, I attended school, read some of the books of the governor-general, and had even tasted a bit of comfort.  

After my parents passed away, I was unable to continue my education, nor was there enough food to sustain myself properly. With no other choice, I worked the fields.  

I don’t know if it’s because I have always been inadequate, but I have never taken a drink, just earnestly farming. Yet, year after year, my debts have continued to rise, and it was only a matter of time before everything my parents had left—including the rice fields—turned out to belong to someone else.  

I have spent over a decade as a tenant farmer. People living in the cities should understand the struggles of rural tenants.  

All sorts of discussions arise in newspapers and magazines about how rural areas are impoverished and in need of assistance, but those are just external arguments. It seems that no matter how much they care, the plight of the rural areas remains unchanged.  

In this dire situation, life is bearable, but feels utterly hopeless; that reflects the condition of rural farmers.  

**Summary**  

The article provides a vivid account of the hardships faced by rural farmers in Korea during the Japanese colonial period, highlighting issues like inflation, oppressive taxation, and the disparity between the livelihood of farmers and urban officials. The narrative of a farmer reflects widespread discontent and the struggle for survival amid systemic exploitation. Historians might find this piece particularly revealing of the socio-economic dynamics of the time, illustrating the increasing burden on rural communities and the failures of supposed reforms under colonial rule.

시골농군의 편지
						徐炳點
오늘 면서기한분이 우리동리에와서 열설을 하엿소.
우리가튼 농군들이 무엇을 알겟소만은 여러 가지이야기를 만히합데다.
「제힘으로 살어나라」고이말을메번이고합듸다. 이제힘으로 살어나라는 조선가운데는 여러 가지가만히 잇는데 그것이모도가 우리가 할수어엄는것이아니면 임이우리가 하고잇는 것입듸다. 뭣 오좀한번을 누어도 길가에다 함부루누지말고 보리밧헤다누라든가요 글세시골 농군들이 언제그러케 오좀을 함부루누엇나요?
서울사람은 길에다 오좀을누다가 벌금이무서워서가 아니라 오좀이 악가워서 한방울이라도 흡히한적이업소.
정말이지 요새는 암모니아갑시엄청나게 빗사저서보리ㅅ거름을 주지못하오.
빗싸진 것이 암모니아 아니겟지만 정말이러케 빗싸고야 어느놈이 보리농사를 해먹을 장수가업겟소.
◇
요새는 쌀갑시 퍽올낫소.
말하기를 쌀갑볏ㅅ갑시올나서 시골농군들이 좃켓다고 들하고.
그럴한 말이겟지요. 작년에는 벼한섬에 오원밧게ㅏ니하든 것이 금년에는 원각가이가니.
그러니 지전한장이 주머니속에 들어오고.
참 그럴하지요 침이 넘어가지요.
그러나 십원을 밧고 팔자니 벼가잇서야지요. 대관절 지금지벼를 가지고 잇는 농군이잇소?
농군들은 발서초가을에 타작을하오 타작을하여서 도조를 물고 장리ㅅ벼를 감고 그리고 걸음갑을 물고 빗을 물고하느라고 벼한섬에 육원할에 다팔어바럿소 지금은 한톨도업소.
그러나 벼ㅅ갑시올는들 무엇이고맘겟소. 고맙기는커녕 되려어럽지요.
벼ㅅ갑시 올나간 멧갑절이나 달은 물건갑시올나갓소.
광당목이 올나가고 삼갑시올나가고무리농군이 일년치면 삼백날을 먹고사는 좁쌀갑시올나갓소.
헐한 벼를 팔어바리고 빗싼좁쌀을 사먹어야하니 세상에 이런노릇도 잇소?
◇
쌀갑시빗사다고 장리ㅅ변도 올낫소.
우리 지주댁에서는 전에는 한섬에 닷말씩단리ㅅ변을 노앗는데 금년은 벼ㅅ갑시 올낫다고 일곱말씩이라나든가요. 체-세상에 이런법도 잇는지요.
그러나 위선당잡 굴머죽지아니하랴니아 그런 장리라도 어더야지요. 명년에 어케될 것을 누가아니요 배곱혼호탱이가 원님을 알어볼리업지요.
◇
색복을 입으라고하오.
장날이면 면소서긔양반들이 먹물을한통씩가지고 물목골목에 지혀서서 힌옷입은장ㅅ군이오면 옷에 먹칠도하고 도장도 어주오.
그러고는 물감옷을 입고오라고 도루차보내오. 그래서 요새는 장이잘서지를못하오.
그러나 장ㅅ군을 두루는 것은 그래도 괜찬치요.
나는 세전을 밧치러 면소에갓다가 도루겨왓소.
그런줄을 누가알엇나요. 아모리농군이라도 관가출입이길네 당목두루막이나마 입고 그리고갓지요.
그랫더니 연서긔양반이 먹통을 좍언져주면서 흰옷을 입은 사람은 어한일이 잇든지 드리지아니한다고합듸다.
세전을 밧치러왓다고햇더니 그것도 아니된다고하고.
할수업시 두르백이 한 벌만 버리고 겨왓지요.
그랫더니 세전을 아니밧친다고 독촉수수료지서 독촉장이 나왓습데다.
물드린옷을 입는것도 조키는하지요 그러나 기왕해입은것이나 해여지거든 그에 다시 물감옷을 해입으라든지 할 일이지 입은옷에다 먹칠을해노니위선당장에는 어하란말이요.
더구나 납세기일에 가지고간 세전도밧지아니하고 도루구는 기한이지 멋다고 독촉수수료를 바드러들으니 참 억울하오.
◇
섣달금음이되니 졸나는 곳도 넘무만허 살수가업소. 그런데다가 녀편네는 알어누어잇지요.
지식놈은 그래도 나가든 온투산이를 아니만들녀고 삼년학교에 보냇지만 그놈이삼년이나 학교에라고가서 배운 것이 무엇인지 제성명삼ㅅ자도 변변히쓰지못하오 그러면서 노-상 밧치는 것은 어면 그러케도만흔지.
금늉조합에 집을 잽힌 것을 석달둘지못햇더니 경매에 붓쳣다든가요.
하기야 농투산이가 제집을지녀서는 무얼하겟소만은 그나마 길일을 생각하니억울하고 섭섭하오.
