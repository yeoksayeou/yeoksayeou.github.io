내 자랑 座談會  

Chairperson: 双S  
Attendees: Tall Guy, Dwarf, Cripple, Fatty, Baldy, Grizzled Man, Curly Hair, Blind Man, Deaf Man, Mute, Stutterer, Pockmarked Man, Cleft Lip, Eunuch  

双S. Ahem! The weather is chilly like this, and it is the end of the year, so I sincerely thank all of you—especially those of you who are special individuals—for coming here. Tonight, I ask that you each share your pride. Often in our world, you are referred to simply as “disabled” and are seen as lesser than ordinary people, but you must have things you can proudly present, correct? So, without hesitation, please share those prideful things with us tonight. Now—who would like to start...?  

Deaf Man. I can’t really understand what you’re saying, so why are you all moving your mouths like that?  

Stutterer. Wh-wh-what's the d-d-deaf guy doing here?  

Blind Man. Since it’s a roundtable about pride, it seems like I should join in, but the deaf man is pretty frustrating.  

双S. Ah, everyone. I asked for you to share your pride, not to criticize others. (Explaining the intent to the Deaf Man in a loud voice).  

Deaf Man. I see, I see. You’re asking me to boast, right? What could I possibly boast about? It’s a hard truth, but there’s no treasure in being deaf. So, why don’t you all curse at me as much as you want? It seems there’s no point in cursing if it doesn’t reach my ears; for me, it won't matter! Indeed. Even if a bomb explodes here and goes off ‘bang!’, you’d all be panicking, stumbling and stepping all over each other trying to get away, but I—what would I care? So, I can just maintain my peace, free from the world’s filthy sounds and annoying noises.  

Blind Man. Ah, not hearing isn’t an issue, you know. Like me, not seeing anything is not a problem either. Just don’t see it, and it’s fine. For instance, if I were to marry, it wouldn’t matter at all if my wife were ugly to me. 

Stutterer. N-n-no way! In this world, countless people ruin themselves with l-l-looks! If you s-s-speak poorly like me...  

Cleft Lip. Ah, it’s frustrating! I can’t decipher everything you say.  

Mute. (Replying in writing to what 双S has written) I don’t hear anything in the world and don’t intervene, just watching the play unfold like a spectator; I’m the greatest here.  

Eunuch. All of that is nonsense! Yet, my pride is the best in this world.  

Pockmarked Man. You, being an eunuch, are considered human!?  

Eunuch. Certainly not! I transcend humanity and dwell beyond it, and that itself is what I pride myself on.  

双S. That's quite an interesting viewpoint. Please elaborate further on what you mean.  

Eunuch. Well, there’s nothing particularly amazing about it. It originates from the theories of Freud, the father of psychoanalysis. Don’t people say that humans are driven by food (食) and sex (色)? These two issues are fundamental. According to Freud’s theory, among these two—food and sex—the latter is considered most fundamental. All human behavior stems from sexual motivation. Therefore, social problems should ultimately be resolved starting from sexual issues. Now, you see, we eunuchs have resolved the issue of sex before posing the problem, right?  

Blind Man. I can’t understand what you’re talking about.  

Eunuch. In simpler terms, while the rest of the world worries about wives and relationships—engaging in conflicts, awkwardness, and tragedies—I don’t have to worry about any of that. Even if a woman is annoying or undesirable, it doesn’t matter to me! In fact, I’d go further and say that I could never receive the disgraceful names like “lecher” that come to people like us.  

Curly Hair. That’s all nonsense. If you’re a person, you must have the capability to be a person! Look at my hair! Isn’t it splendid? With its countless curls! Others struggle to get their hair styled, but I naturally have these beautiful curls!  

Baldy. Friend! You should read “Ten Great Virtues of Baldness,” and then talk to me! Look at my shiny forehead; use the mirror to compare it with your wavy hair, and you’ll recognize the greatness of my baldness! I could go on and on about the ten great virtues of baldness, but I digress: the presence of hair is a marker of barbarism. If you consider that, a bald person must have evolved longer, don’t you think? And you, who doesn’t need to visit a barbershop, have your hair all over the place, while I don’t even need that service. If the world becomes entirely bald, then both barbershops and mirrors would go bankrupt!  

Grizzled Man. That’s not the case. A person should always have something on their face. Without facial hair, that shouldn’t even be considered a head! To be a person, having a mustache or beard is necessary to show one’s dignity. Isn’t that right? Isn’t it universally acknowledged that all great figures have beards?  

Fatty. No, there’s only one proper use for a beard when it’s too much—serving as glue when plastering walls. Other than that, there’s nothing cleaner and more pleasant than being without facial hair. Moreover, it’s not an issue during meals or when kissing with a partner.  

Tall Guy. No, all of these statements are just excuses, not pride. Pride can only come from my great height!  

Dwarf. You look like a roadside pillar.  

Tall Guy. You little brat! Keep quiet. When I go outside, everyone looks down at me. Everyone wanders around the streets looking up at me. They can’t escape; they have to look up at me because they’re shorter than me! That really is great. I can even witness spectacle events without struggling to squeeze in. Ah, I genuinely thank the heavens for giving me such height!  

Dwarf. That kind of unrealistic and unscientific pride isn’t true pride; it’s just a joke. I have a practical pride. Since I’m short, I always take up less space; I also happen to wear well-fitting clothes. My short stature also gives me the advantage of kissing without bending over.  

Tall Guy. Wasn't there a time you kissed her at the belly button?  

Dwarf. The tall guy has to lift his girlfriend onto a height before kissing, I heard.  

Cripple. I don’t need to lift my legs. My girlfriend comes to me just as she wishes. If I must risk it, I just take a taxi—only if needed. So, nowadays I never fear getting hit by a tram or car in the cultural city.  

Skinny Guy. The world is an emergency situation. In such emergencies, being like me is the simplest option. I only have bones and skin, so if the world turns into a situation where everyone must escape and travel, then someone like me would have no trouble getting on a train or car! In Africa, no cannibal would dare eat me, right? I don't have much flesh, so my expressions are extraordinary!  

Fatty. Isn’t that a human? Others are struggling, while you’re hard-pressed.  

Skinny Guy. So you admire that? For a fat person, being huge means you represent the nourishment you didn’t excrete. Since you’re thick-bodied, even if a storm blows, you wouldn’t be blown away easily.

Pockmarked Man. If nothing else, you could sell your body for hotpot!  

双S. Doesn’t anyone else have something to say?  

Pockmarked Man. Well, as for me, they say I make a good <41> kankang. If someone needs an odd specimen at a ceremony, I can provide it! Moreover, if my partner has some dirty skin, I can help clean it off! It seems that’s why women genuinely follow after me, considering that if my skin rots, in summer, I won’t even have to worry about it becoming an issue.  

双S. Thank you very much for your lively contributions tonight! In the past, people might have faced punishment for saying what you spoke, but thankfully, the corporal punishment laws have been abolished these days. I truly regret that. —(End)— <42>  

**Summary**  

This article presents a humorous roundtable discussion among various disabled individuals who share their unconventional perspectives on pride in their unique states. Each character contributes their perspectives, revealing a sense of community among those marginalized and often stigmatized by society. Particularly interesting is how their pride derives not from conventional societal norms but from their lived realities, reflecting the challenging social landscape of colonial Korea. Historians may find the representation of disability in this context relevant to understanding differing constructions of identity and pride in an era of colonial oppression.
