발행일	1932년 12월 30일  
기사제목	淸凉線電車 身勢打鈴 (Cooling Line Tram, Body and Bell)  
필자	艾島浪人 (Aishō Rōnin)  
기사형태	세태비평 (Social Criticism)  

Ah, it’s cold again this winter. Is it colder than last year? Winter this year is certainly very chilly! And it feels like it’s even more severe for the latecomers. I believe this is the first time I’m showing such a yellowed sigh as I reminisce about things to you, but surely you can guess the general idea of my lament when you’re watching me closely? 

Even if you can guess my situation, can you truly share my feelings? Hey, are you just looking across lightly? Is the owner of my fate, the tram, ready to abandon me? My destiny is withering moment by moment. Dying and resurrecting once again is the law of the universe, but gazing quietly into the treacherous trap of death, I can only express my unbearable despair. 

You know, they’re widening the main road in Gyeonggi to fifteen widths and pavers—doesn’t that mean they’ve had their fill for decades? And in the face of the vehement opposition from my beloved residents of Cheongnyangni, it seems they’ll pay no heed and abandon me. I really have no words to say there. 

What about the company? When it comes to the tram, they’re simply profit-minded, collecting expenses while failing to earn any income. They even plan to abandon me entirely and hire a replacement for forty thousand won! 

Ha! Truly! There are some ridiculous people in this world. If they had even a bit of conscience, they wouldn’t be able to say such absurd things. 

You say I’m being loud? Listen to what I have to say; I must tell you just how I ended up in this predicament and how he’s fattening because of my and my brothers’ burdens. It’s time for me to express all the resentment lodged in my chest…

You know, I came out into this world thirty-four years ago, in the spring of 1899. Ah—how long it has been! Is it true that I have been poor for so long?

Thirty-four years is indeed a long time. In the years since then, the world has changed so much, and I miss the days gone by even more.

Let me tell you, during the glorious five hundred years of the Yi dynasty, the palace was on the verge of collapse, and dark clouds were swirling, while national affairs were full of troubles from both within and outside. 

But regardless of how the nation was collapsing, would it be this miserly and severe in today’s world? There were times when the people were prosperous, and the songs of joy filled the streets, genuinely alive when debating national affairs. 

Well—1896—American Golbu ran (骨富蘭) came to Seoul, aligning with the Korean Li Du-seung (李斗昇) and Kim Geun-bae (金根培) to form the Hanseong Electric Company with initial capital of three hundred thousand won, finally receiving permission the following year and, the subsequent year, finally establishing the tramline connecting Jongno and Seodaemun, extending it to Ddongdaemun and then to Cheongnyangni. 

When I think back to my origins, it seems like I was just a “country bumpkin”—like a a clumsy cart on the road—isn’t that so? At the time, my siblings were scarce. But even with such humble beginnings, those people treated me with such joy. 

While my attire looked rustic, people were so amazed and curious to meet me that I didn’t lack for visitors, men and women alike. 

It was so that they would routinely dress up to see me, throwing about pom-poms in attempts to catch a glimpse of my face. I thought all the beauties of the world belonged to me, as if the world existed solely for my sake. 

Well, I made my entrance with a jingle at Dongdaemun, first moving with dignity and then running as people gathered around, creating a ruckus of cheers. At first, I was proud, but in the end, just like a pesky fly, I ended up being overwhelmed. The officers would come with batons to clear a path, just to keep me moving along daintily. 

It felt like that caused a great stir throughout the city at the time!

Of course, back then, homes weren’t even as tightly packed as they are now. A few houses at each stop, the willow trees swayed gracefully at Cheonan Samgeori, as if to greet the throng that still seemed youthful. 

Nevertheless, though I faithfully traveled between Dongdaemun and Cheongnyangni, transporting people back and forth, the development of society didn’t progress as expected; it was all due to the desire of everyone to live in the outskirts.

In this endeavor to construct a significant urban area and to facilitate suburban transportation, the “Ritaewang” (李太王) directly contacted Golbu and signed a formal contract for tram operation, promising a huge amount of support—seventy-five thousand won—splitting the funding to assist the Hanseong Electric Company. 

Despite how things appear, at least I was born from the assistance of XXX (restored) and brought into this world with my proud legacy. 

<20 lines omitted>

The company easily abandoned its commitments over time; as of today, it has been twenty-five years since the establishment of the suburban lines, merely constructing the Wangsimni line without any plans to develop any more, yet reaping enormous profits yearly and generously returning dividends to shareholders—fact that indisputably proves my claims. 

Can you understand my point? If so, look into the reports from Japanese newspapers about this issue. In Tokyo, profits from tram construction reached seven percent (7%), Osaka was claimed to be at five percent (5%), and Toyama at five percent (5%), while Gyeongseong's profits were a mere fifteen percent (15). It seems clear that this circumstance is a flagrant example of capitalists’ schemes. 

However, to achieve profits, these capitalists would go so far as to engage in any cruel practices. No matter how much my beloved resident community opposes, thirty-four years of history cannot be easily removed; the company is bound to disregard all difficulties.

My siblings in Mapo, Wangsimni, and others share the same fate as mine. 

Last night, I roamed quietly along the dark Cheongnyangni, contemplating my imminent demise, while I lamented the cold and unyielding cruelty of existence. 

You may welcome the hope of the new year 1933, but for me, there is only the despair of darkness! 

So, in this still night, if you hear the despairing sound of the tram clanging from afar, please recognize this as the voice of my grief and shed even a single tear of sympathy for me. That is my only final wish.  

**Summary**

This article expresses a nostalgic lament from the perspective of a tramway system in Seoul, reflecting on its initial hopes and subsequent decline under exploitative capitalist practices. The author characterizes the progress and suffering within the socio-economic framework of colonial Korea, illustrating the tram’s significance as a metaphor for emerging industrialization and cultural life. 

Historians might find the juxtaposition of urban development against the realities of colonial oppression particularly interesting, as it reveals the economic struggles and community dynamics of Koreans during the period of Japanese rule.
