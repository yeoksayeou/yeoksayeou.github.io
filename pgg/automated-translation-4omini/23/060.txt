기사제목	各界各人 十年回顧 (Reflections on Ten Years by Various Figures)  
저자	李光洙 (Lee Kwang-soo)  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Retrospective/Self-Writing)  

Reflections on Ten Years by Various Figures  
As a writer, I must speak in the context of the literary world. Anyway, the first time I wrote and published a novel was exactly twenty-two years ago.

Ten years ago, the question arose whether one could write literary works in Joseon, to the point where calling it a literary world would not be an exaggeration to say it did not exist at all. If it had existed, it would have been one that strongly expressed a certain emotionality typical of the years leading up to the Gyeongju year (1920) - something that has been frequently seen in a column titled "Today's Yesterday" in the Dong-A Ilbo these days. At that time, if something was written, it was certainly sung aloud.

Indeed, prior to the Gyeongju year, there was a solicitation of prize-winning novels by "Seongchun". About twenty pieces were chosen, with Zhu Yo-han receiving second place and Kim Myeong-soon receiving third place. Bang Jeong-hwan was also one whose work I saw submitted at that time. 

Then, after the Gyeongju year, many newspapers and magazines emerged, and the literary world began to take shape gradually. In terms of trends, I would say there were three main veins: the nationalistic literature that descended directly from the ideological emotionality of the years prior to the Gyeongju year, the literature characterized by the "Dada" influenced by Kim Eok-kun's "The Dance of Distress," which once swept through the literary world, and the idealism of Park Wol-tan. Following idealism was today's emerging literature.

What was my first work? It was a novel titled "Gwaewon" published in the Daehan Hyunhakbo (大韓興學報) magazine published by Japanese students back then, which was twenty-two years ago as mentioned above. As for the writing style, most of what I wrote at that time was in classical style, and I began writing in colloquial style starting from "Mujeong".

"Mujeong" was serialized in the Maeil Sinbo, and the manuscript fee back then was five won a month (haha). It is amusing that now, for writing in newspapers, one is paid this amount in a single day, which means it has increased twelve-fold compared to then.

Emerging literature? Of course, I believe it will continue to develop. However, I think it's important to consider this apple (there is a piece of apple on the table). Probably, not many people would eat it as medicine. Some might eat an apple daily because it helps relieve constipation, and they may be eating it as a remedy for their condition; however, they certainly would not be doing so thinking of it as medicine. 

Literature is the same. As literature inherently exists as literature, there must be a place that is truly literary; otherwise, it won't suffice. People do not eat just for swallowing the skin (the bitter part). However, I feel that the current emerging literature is not limited to just swallowing that bitter part. 

The so-called literature that is wandering only with a sense of purpose is the childish literature of our time. While such literature can exist if it serves merely as a tool, it is historically proven that such literature does not have a long lifespan. 

Looking at Japan's emerging literary scene, the propaganda literature centered on the literary front, represented by the Seongyeo Gi-ki school, which focused solely on a sense of purpose, has declined, while the proletarian literature of the Jeonggi faction has failed to develop. Even in England, just looking at the works of Jack London nowadays indicates that the general public prefers to demand apples rather than merely consuming the bitter parts.

Thus, I think that regardless of whether someone proclaims, "I am a proletarian writer," if their work expresses the feelings of the masses well, it can be considered proletarian literature. Although Tolstoy is not a proletarian writer, many of his works qualify as genuine proletarian pieces.

Everything in the world is multi-faceted. Our general attitude towards literature is the same. That multi-facetedness becomes the basis for contradictions, or when these contradictions are observed again, there is harmony within them. Until we can discover that harmony, we cannot truly say we have observed and cannot genuinely claim anything.

That necessitates a span of life experience. Consequently, very few writers have produced major works before the age of forty or fifty among the many writers. 

As for my literary stance? As you all know, it is based on nationalist literature. However, before I read anyone else's work, I would read Kinoshita Kiyoka's "Fire Pillar" and would read Tolstoy before the Bible, gaining immense influence from him. 

I will surely read Tolstoy a lot and will continue to follow him. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Newspaper Reporter  
Go Yeong-han 

Write about Korea ten years ago and Korea today based on ten years of experience as a newspaper reporter — this request makes me feel choked and utterly unable to contain myself. Ten years may not be said to be so long, yet it can also be said to be not so short; thus the saying, "Ten years can sharpen a sword" embodies the critical significance of this period for various matters.

What was Korea like ten years ago, and what is today's Korea like? As I attempt to write this, I find it hard to express all my feelings. The world has undergone significant changes in the last decade, which we have all heard and are well aware of. Yet, when I turn that perspective to look at our declining state, who can refrain from sighing?

Looking back ten years ago, Korea was indeed not woozy as it is today. At that time, Korea appeared as if waking after a long slumber, with vigor evident across all social classes. Everyone was filled with enthusiasm and a sense of determination to see how they might achieve something resembling a desire for change. This was a period of excitement flooding even the general social fabric.

From that point onward, the fervor for education emerged, alongside youth movements, social movements, women's movements, and various other enterprises blossoming like bamboo shoots after the rain. 

I still remember the bustling scenes of students from the proletarian class eagerly seeking education during the winter nights. The spectacle of people engaged in promoting local products is still vivid in my mind. Moreover, diverse initiatives and enterprises thrived during that period, with many businesses and organizations springing up across various locations. 

The atmosphere of Korea at that time was indeed vibrant, with an emerging spirit of progress filling the air, infused with strong emotions and action.

During that time, everything I treated as a news article only reinforced my satisfaction and pleasure with my job, as at the same time, the nature of being a newspaper reporter naturally brought to me instances of joy and satisfaction.

Conversely, when I look at Korea today ten years later, it reminds me of a young person afflicted with meningitis or anemia, perhaps. It feels as if the awakening has regressed again to an original slumber. The once-vibrant energy resulting from the various movements has been replaced by malnourishment and fatigue, leading to symptoms akin to anemia.

The situations that once flourished now seem erased or have dwindled to faint traces after so many years. 

People feel exhaustion and apathy, taking the path of least resistance, and the prevalent notion of survival through self-interest has spread like an extreme form of egotism. 

It is said that the level of culture and other aspects has developed significantly compared to ten years ago. I do not deny this. However, I question whether this development is a healthy sign. 

In that decade, those with some kind of money in their pockets can now find themselves practically empty. Many who lived without significant concern then are struggling to make ends meet today, often hearing news of bankruptcies and failures from wealthy individuals and businesses. 

It seems whispers of prosperity have become mere echoes, while the painful cries of hardship prevail. Consequently, we find ourselves in an increasingly chaotic state, where no one can grasp how to navigate the upcoming trials. 

We have a culture that is supposedly better than ten years ago, yet in reality, our lives suffer compared to that time, indicating a discrepancy between culture and lived experience. 

Knowledge has increased as never seen before over the last decade, with most of this group comprised of unemployed individuals lacking proper opportunities. Yet, our society has failed to prepare enough job opportunities for them.

The lament of employment difficulties has only deepened, and the struggle of knowledge-class youth in search of jobs has intensified, often culminating in despair and relinquishing hope. This reflects a significant societal issue in today's Korea.

Ultimately, every problem present today is gravely deepened compared to the past. The earlier prosperity has given way to an arid state where excessive effort must be exerted to reestablish vitality. With concern for the future of Korea ten years down the line, the situation remains uncertain... (End)

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Doctor  
Kim Yong-seong 

This year marks exactly ten years for me. 

However, as an ordinary doctor, there’s not much to reflect upon. 

In terms of the general economic state observed through a doctor's eyes, I must say that it is indeed worse than ten years ago. 

In terms of patient types, there doesn't seem to be a significant difference, but there’s been a notable special change. 

Previously, there were no cases of beriberi (脚気) in Korea. Indeed, while there was a condition referred to as a swollen leg disease, it was not the same as beriberi. Japanese people, of course, recognized what beriberi was, but no Korean had known it before. 

However, now beriberi has become widespread in Korea. I’ve been contemplating this issue, however, I cannot clearly discern the cause. 

As for sexually transmitted diseases, has there been an increase? It would seem possible to think so, but in reality, the number has remained about the same compared to ten years ago. 

This also depends on considering the population growth. 

One strange observation regarding Koreans is that although many Japanese individuals know the disease of myelopathy (脊髄癆), I have yet to see a single Korean case in the past ten years.

This illness appears after syphilis and leads to nerve damage that causes inability to use the lower limbs and vision impairment. While it is akin to a stroke, it is not the same. 

This lack of cases among Koreans may also reflect lower instances of sexually transmitted diseases among them (which are notably higher among Japanese). Yet, we shall see how it develops over time.

As for health consciousness, certainly, with the rise in the number of educated individuals, public awareness regarding hygiene has also grown.

Ten years ago, when an infectious disease appeared, people would outright try to hide it; today, it’s rare for anyone to refuse to treat dissections.

Doctors now serve their patients, emphasize the urgency of a well-managed illness, and genuinely work toward making patients return to health. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Translator  
Lee Jong-yeop 

There is no definitive end to the subject at hand. Yet, facing the dilemma of needing to articulate, it remains complicated.

Most of the foreigners I interact with are mainly French; also, there are people from newly independent nations who have not yet established consulates in Korea, such as Poland and Yugoslavia. 

Since the end of World War One, the number of foreign visitors to Korea had diminished significantly, but this year, a considerable number have begun to arrive. Generally, these visitors are scholars or correspondents, with fewer businesspeople.

Businesspeople who do arrive only come briefly, perhaps traveling from Japan to China, stopping over to survey the situation briefly, but there hasn’t been enough investment from French entrepreneurs within Korea.

Recently, I have received many requests from major French retailers to introduce them to Korean markets; this frequent exchange only highlights the lack of viable opportunities for investment.

About ten years ago, a grocery store called Hanyang in Seoul began thriving after my introduction and established a connection with a reputable Paris shop, but the outcome was not particularly fruitful.

As for foreigners' observations of Korea, can they speak candidly about what they've seen? Typically, they will only offer praises that align with our preferences, stating that there has been development when in reality, it may not be fully merited.

I have been engaged in this work for over twenty-one years now. It seems like a decent time, right? (Haha)

My observations over this time could fill volumes, yet I cannot delve deeply enough to properly express everything, leading to frustrations. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Culinary Businessperson  
Ahn Soon-hwan 

Now, I operate various businesses and serve food, having worked in this field for several decades.

Changes over this period are notable. Not only has the taste of dishes transformed to satisfy the palate of contemporary diners, but what is more important is how dishes are presented.

Previously, fruits and assorted meats arranged on the table were typically home-cooked, served in a very simple manner. Especially in royal courts, care was taken in the arrangement, ensuring quality, with edible parts prioritized over mere presentation. 

This led to certain dishes spoiling quickly and being inedible; for instance, how would a foreigner react to such when served what was essentially spoiled?

When I was the head chef in the court, I made some improvements to the dishes, which even earned me some criticism of being a revolutionary. 

Thus, improvements began from the court level, and proper arrangements through careful selection resulted in the general public eventually adopting these practices. Hence, today’s dining establishments are significantly more developed than before.

The clientele for restaurants has changed in many respects. Among these shifts, the previous prevalence of drifters has decreased, and social habits surrounding dining have changed markedly, with more Japanese customers seeking engagement in this trend, requiring restaurants to adapt to greet them in their language and aesthetics.

In summary, everything has transformed with the emergence of new styles.

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Streetcar Conductor  
Seo Gye-won 

As for me, it has been twenty years now. 

I first became a conductor at the time when the Seoul Electric Company was still run by Westerners and was called the MiHan Electric Company. 

Following that, it transitioned to the IlHan Gas and Electric Company, later reverting back to the Seoul Electric Company.

At that time, streetcars were very much valued. The lines were all single tracks. Passengers often needed to hold umbrellas inside the streetcars upon arrival of rain (Haha). At that time, there were only a few twenty-three vehicles.

By Gyeongju year (1920), the number of streetcars increased to sixty, and now there are more than one hundred and thirty vehicles operating.

The fare system was by districts; initially, it was charged at 2 jeon 5 ri or 3 jeon, but transitioned to a fixed fare of 5 jeon.

However, the streetcars were once very much beloved, like modern automobiles, so much so that taking them was considered a feat.

In terms of salary, both then and now, it is by hourly wage. Back then, it was about 5 jeon an hour, while now the starting pay is around 9 jeon 5 ri.

The earnings from streetcar fares in the past could not exceed 2000 won daily, whereas today, even during peak seasons, it surpasses 4000 won regularly.

The flow of passengers has indeed increased significantly. Students and commuters on their way to work are riding the vehicles by the bushel.

I worked as a conductor for some time and have since become a supervisor, hence my income is much easier now.

Having worked for so long, I feel like it may soon be time to retire. However, the company has asked me to continue working, so I remain in this capacity. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Newspaper Delivery Person  
Seo Byeong-uk 

I have been delivering newspapers since the days of Maeil Sinbo. I worked there for a good while before coming to work for the Dong-A Ilbo when it first launched.

Back when I was with Maeil Sinbo, there were only eight of us scarce deliverers in all of Seoul; now I operate in an area close to seventeen times that size.

The readership has greatly increased compared to what it used to be. More people are aware of newspapers, taking an interest.

Delivering newspapers comes with many challenges. Not all readers share the same inclination; among them, there are some who create unnecessary fuss.

When news is collected, we have to evade any police stations while trying to deliver every single paper we can.

The payment method, whether ten years ago or now, is done directly in cash to the delivery person.

My wage was six jeon for each paper back then; currently, it stands at fifteen jeon, which means I need to deliver three hundred papers just to earn a monthly payment of forty-five won.

The bonuses we receive have increased by around fifteen won too. 

There were instances in the past where readers would abscond with the papers discreetly while I was distracted with the news. However, lately, those incidents have significantly decreased.

Now, I am considered a veteran, having worked in this role for a long time and currently hold a deputy supervisor position; however, I still essentially remain the same newspaper delivery person I started as. I have spent my needed days creating a career out of this.

When comparing the newspapers of yesteryears to now, the differences are profound. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Gisaeng  
Kim Hwa-jung 

I became a gisaeng (妓生) at the age of thirteen, and now I am twenty-five, making this past twelve or thirteen years of my life in this role.

After a time, I did enter into a long-term relationship for about three years; I resumed my role again seven years ago.

Why did I leave gisaeng life? It involved certain circumstances leading to that change, but certainly, the ease of life during that time was undoubtedly better. 

Ten years ago, the art and style of a gisaeng involved elements of pansori (판소리) and beating of the drum, all of which showcased lively performances. This led to some conflicts with the tea house organizations. 

At that time, gisaeng from Pyongyang alone were entire divisions, whereas gisaeng from the western provinces were treated akin to mere entertainers.

Now, it is progressively unfamiliar for gisaengs to be knowledgeable in singing traditional forms; instead, many gisaengs of the modern era perform Western-style songs. 

Labor rates for gisaeng performances have transformed as well, where previously the initial cost would be one won and further engagement priced at ninety jeon, whereas currently it starts at one won ninety-five jeon, and subsequent hours at one won thirty jeon. 

Yet, with rising costs, there remains no profit due to the significant fees taken away. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Rickshaw Puller  
Kim Jun-sam 

I’ve been working this job for around ten years now. It’s hard to believe that humans are doing the work animals once did. 

As I finally reflect on the past decade, things have certainly altered. 

In terms of income? Back then, it was indeed pleasant. The old Emperor Yo Jin (이태왕) who earlier reigned was quite generous, whereas this market-centric approach now has created a host of organized companies. Thus, I barely earn enough to sustain myself.

Riders are much less now. Back in the old days, it was far better. Even when you rode with comfort, it was not uncommon to earn sixty jeon, but now I am receiving only sixty jeon. 

Those who seek transportation or go towards Yong-san or Munbang often opt for automobiles instead, so much so that I may go entire working days without a fare.

Ten years ago, the only rickshaw service came from Hanseong. That is not the case anymore; companies have taken a stronghold here now.

Although the road conditions have improved, it has not substantially benefited us. The rise of automobiles has driven down our business.

Have I encountered difficult times? Undoubtedly. 

Imagine when you have a drunk passenger. Helping someone into the vehicle is already challenging enough; if they stumble out mid-ride, it's even worse. 

It’s frustrating, especially when passengers, once they reach their destination, often refuse to pay or leave a tip, cursing and grumbling instead.

Thinking back, I wonder how I can continue in this career for my remaining years as I approach my forties, nearing fifty, with the current hardships making it ever more difficult. 

What a surmountable time it has been — how one partnership with guests may lead to generosity over tips is all that makes sense anymore. 

A Decade of Korea from the Perspective of a Clerk  
Kim Yong-jin 

I began my life as a clerk at seventeen years old, marking around fifteen years now.

Back then (though there is none now), I worked at a grocery store known as the Second Branch of Wofuk Store in the Jongno District, earning four won a month. I left after several days because the wage was too low. The owner, however, lured me back with a raise to seven won.

At that time, seven won was less than how much one would pay for a few empty bags, yet it was quite a decent amount back then when rice cost about seven won fifty jeon per sack, making it even a better earn than fifty won today.

Thus, I worked there for about four years before moving on to the Daedong Trading Company in South Gate, which offered a salary of forty won.

The working hours at that company were shorter than at the first place, which required me to work more hours.

I had stayed there for about four years when the unfortunate chain of bankruptcy hit. The owner was quite a kind man and offered me funds to assist in my endeavor.

I had given that money to my brother-in-law so that he could start a business, though that venture ultimately failed — both due to luck.

Therefore, I saved a little more and attempted to start an antiquarian shop in Guri. However, it is now thoroughly impossible to make a living on the little that remains of that initial investment.

Previously, it was easy to utterly disappear without large investments; today, it seems even a hundred won cannot pay rent, which indicates how things have changed.

I can no longer set up a business with even the slightest bit of savings; these are greatly diminished in value now. 

The number of storefronts has greatly increased since then. 

Previously, the only Japanese shops in Jongno were the Seongyeon Welfare Hall and Changwae Seongwon, but now they are found everywhere.

People mentioned earlier are lacking in opportunities; since clerks function mechanically, they typically do not generate news.

Now that I’ve spent over ten years as a clerk, and as I look ahead, I find no elaborate future within reach—dismal indeed. 

**Summary**  

This article presents a collection of reflections from various individuals from different professions recounting their experiences over the past decade in Korea, showcasing the complexities of social, economic, and cultural changes during the colonial era. Notably, the reflections reveal a stark contrast between a once-vibrant society with aspirations towards modernization and education and the present, where they encounter disillusionment, economic decline, and cultural dilution. Historians may find the sentiments of nostalgia and struggle against colonial repression particularly revealing, illustrating the socio-economic challenges faced by Koreans in their quest for identity and autonomy during a tumultuous time.

잡지명	별건곤 제25호

기사형태	회고·수기

各界各人 十年回顧,
作家로서 본 文壇의 十年
李光洙

작가라니 작가 行勢를 햇서야 말이지요. 좌우간 내가 첫 번 소설을 써 발표한 것은 꼭 스물 두 해째 되엿슴니다.

십 년 이전에는 조선 글로 문예를 쓸 수가 잇느냐 하는 의문까지도 가지게 되엿섯스니까 문단이라고 일홈 지을 존재도 업섯다고 하여도 과언이 아니겟슴니다.
만일 잇섯다고 하면 庚戌年 이후 己未年까지에 00적 情調가 아주 농후한 時調(이것은 요지음 東亞日報에 『過去의 今日』이라는 欄에 만히 보이드군요) 唱歌 가튼 것이겟지요. 그 때에는 글로 썻스면 반드시 목으로 읍게 되엿섯스니까요.

역시 己未 이전의 일인데 『靑春』에서 懸賞小說을 모집한 일이 잇섯슴니다. 한 이십 편이나 당선이 되엿는데 朱요한 君이 그 때에 2등으로 金明淳 氏가 3등으로 뽑혓슴니다. 方定煥 君도 그 때에 투고를 한 것을 보앗슴니다.
그리하다가 己未 이후로 신문 잡지가 만히 생기고 문단도 점점 형태를 갓추게 되엿는데 경향으로 말하면 己未 이전의 00思想을 정통으로 바더 내려온 민족주의 문학과 金億君의 『懊惱의 舞蹈』 이후에 一時 문단을 風靡한 떼카다니즘의 문학과 또 한 가지 朴月灘의 理想主義의 세가지라고 보겟슴니다. 理想主義를 뒤이어 바든 것이 오늘의 新興文學이겟지요.

맨 처음 작품이요? 그것은 우에 말한 스물 두 해 전인데 그 때에 일본 유학생들이 발행하든 大韓興學報라는 잡지에 발표한 『閨怨』이라는 소설임니다.
문체로 말하면 그 때의 것이 대개 古文體엿고 내가 口文體로 쓰기는 『無情』부터-ㄴ＜52＞ 것 갓슴니다.
『無情』은 每日申報에 연재하엿는데 원고료가 한 달에 오 원이엇슴니다(허허). 그러든 것이 지금은 신문 소설을 쓰면 一日分 이 원을 주니까 열두 배나 올은 세음이지요.

新興文學이요? 물론 이 압흐로 잘 발전이 되여 나갈 줄 암니다.
그러나 여긔에 사과(맛침 책상 우에 사과 한 쪽이 잇다)  이 사과를 아마 약으로 먹는 사람은 別로히 업슬 것임니다. 혹 便秘症이 잇는 사람이 매일 사과 한 개식을 먹으면 排便이 잘 되니까 그래서 먹는 사람도 잇겟지만 그 사람 역시 달은 약을 먹는 생각으로는 결코 먹는 것이 아닐 것임니다.
문학도 그럿슴니다.
문학이 문학인 이상 문학다운 곳이 잇자면 사과와 가터야 아니하겟슴니까. 쓰듸 쓴 금게랍을 맛으로 녁여 먹을 사람은 업스니까요.
그런데 요지음의 新興文學이 단지 금게랍에 긋치지 아니하나 십슴니다. 目的意識만에 彷徨하는 소위 小兒病的 문학이 그것임니다.
그것도 단지 한 개의 도구와 가티 쓴다면 그 문학이 성립치 못할 것은 아니나 在來로 보건데 그러한 문학은 결코 수명이 길지 못함니다.
일본의 新興文壇을 보더래도 文藝戰線을 중심으로 한 靑野季吉 一派의 목적의식만을 내용으로 한 宣傳文學은 몰락이 되고 戰旗 一派의 푸로레타리아리아리슴이 새로운 발전을 하지 아니함니까? 영국의 째크·론돈의 작품이 요지음 와서 덜 일키는 것만 보아도 일반 대중이 사과를 요구하느냐 금게랍만을 먹으려 하느냐는 것을 알 수가 잇슴니다.
