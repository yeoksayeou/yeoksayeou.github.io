Horse Tales  
Horses are the essence of the Star (房星) among the 28 lodgings; in the Heavenly Branches they correspond to the Horse (午) and belong to the Fire element (火) in the Five Elements, while they are associated with the South (南) in the cardinal directions. The origin of Korean horses is unclear; however, according to 고려 李牧隱集 (Goryeo I Muk-yin's Collection), there are two types of horses: one is the local horse (鄕馬), produced in the country, and the other is the foreign horse (胡馬) that came from the north. 柳得恭 (Yoo Deuk-gong) in 京都雜記 (Kyoto Miscellaneous Notes) mentions that Korean horses exist in various forms: the red horse (紅馬) is called 절다 (Jeolda), the chestnut horse (栗色馬) is called 구랑 (Gurang), the red sandy horse (紅沙馬) is called 부로우 (Bulou), the black horse (黑馬) is called 가라 (Gara), and the yellow horse (黃馬) is called 공홀 (Gonghol), while the black mane yellow horse (黑鬃黃馬) is called 고라 (Gora). The green-faced horse (綠臉) is called 간자 (Ganja). All these terms reportedly originate from Manchu language. However, their origins are still unclear. In the Annals, the first recorded instance of horses is during the year of 赫居世 (Heokgyeose) in Silla, when Dong-wokjo presented 24 fine horses and again, in the year 50, when Dong-wokjo presented 200 fine horses. The first record in Japan details that in the 15th year of Emperor Ōjin, Baekje sent one male and one female fine horse. It is widely reported in Chinese accounts as well. In the Zhou official documents, horses larger than eight feet are called dragons (龍), those over seven feet are called riddles (騋), and those six feet tall are simply referred to as horses. In 何承天's works, a one-year-old is called a horse (馬), a two-year-old is a colt (駒), and an eight-year-old is referred to as * (the term is omitted here). In the Shiyi record, it is mentioned that Zhou Mu Wang had eight fine horses: one is called 絶地 (Zeodi), meaning it does not touch the ground; the second is called 翻羽 (Fan Yu), meaning it easily surpasses flying birds; the third is called 奔宵 (Ben Xiao), meaning it travels ten thousand miles at night; the fourth is 超影 (Chao Ying), meaning it runs faster than the sun; the fifth is 踰輝 (Yu Hui), which indicates its radiant color; the sixth is 超光 (Chao Guang), meaning it creates a shape with ten shadows; the seventh is 騰霧 (Teng Wu), meaning it rides the clouds; and the eighth is 掛翼 (Gua Yi), suggesting it has flesh wings. 崔豹's Ancient and Modern Notes states that Qin Shi Huang owned seven notable horses, named 追風 (Chui Feng), 白兎 (Bai Tu), 躡景 (Nie Jing), 追電 (Chui Dian), 飛翮 (Fei He), 銅雀 (Tong Que), and 晨鳧 (Chen Fu). As recorded in the Analects, it states that the horse is assessed by its virtue, not its strength. It is also mentioned that in the palace there were horses valued at a thousand pieces (千駟), and in the records of 魏, there were three fine female horses in the villa. The Qin Shi Huang also had sleek horses, while Xiang Yu had a black stallion (烏騅馬) and Guan Yu had a red hair horse (赤兎馬), and Liu Bei had a fine horse (的顱馬).  

Fine horses such as 駕車 (Hwa Li) are considered ancient renowned horses (古之良馬). Legendary horses such as 龍媒 (Long Mei) and 騕袞 (Tian Yu) are all renowned horses, whereas the lesser horses such as 駑駘 (Pu Dai) and 款蹄 (Kuan Ye) are all common racing horses. According to Zhou official documents, in spring, sacrifices are made to the Horse Ancestor (馬祖), which is considered the progenitor of horses, while in summer sacrifices are made to the First Breeder (先牧), the first person to domesticate them, in autumn to the Horse Society (馬社), the first to ride them, and in winter to the Horse Disaster (馬步), representing horses' calamities. 莊子 mentions the 駱馬首 as well, and 馬援 indicates how a nobleman adequately wraps his body in horsehide when burying the dead. 曹孟德 states that even an old horse lying in the stable aspires to travel a thousand miles. "A long whip that does not reach the horse's belly" is a phrase from the Zuo Zhuan, while 牛驥同皀 appears in the Han Book, where 礼记 also mentions that the 帷 is used to shield horses during their burials. The 後皇后紀 states, “the horse is like a roaming dragon.” In regard to poetry, "The bright white horse" symbolizes a satire of 宣王, and "In the field of cattle" praises 息公 for implementing agricultural practices... 

In recent years, a young man's family often used the saying "Horses must still carry out dirty work" in times of harvest recognizing how they were beneficial in pastoral care. 

As the prevalence of smallpox vaccination has increased today, the common term for smallpox, which was once quite unfamiliar to young people, has also faced misunderstanding. In times past, when smallpox was rampant, there was a superstition of driving away the "demon of epidemic" by burning a horsehair (싸리말) to chase away the spirits of sickness.  

In particular, the practice of using decorative flourishes in spring festivals and rituals such as putting out "Horse Tales" prayers (馬廐 지둥에 馬行千里路) has become commonplace. Notably, on the first day of spring, blessings are inscribed such as "Horses like roaming dragons travel a thousand miles." In these superstitions, it is proclaimed that those having the Horse Star (驛馬星) in their Four Pillars (四柱) often travel far and wide.

Among the folk customs and legendary songs concerning horses are many superstitions and proverbs perhaps the most notably is "The horse's legs bear the burden of others” (가는 말도 채를 처라). One would find interesting that similar sayings have thrived culturally leading to unique adaptations over the decades. In Jeju, it is recorded that women performing horse beauty pageants have existed since the Mongolian times, owing to the region once being a horse breeding ground. Additionally, there's a song that expresses the story of a Jeju woman conversing with her horse, intertwined with the culture and beauty of the region. 

Summary  

The article discusses the cultural significance and historical references of horses in Korea, detailing various types of horses, their historical records, and the customs associated with them. It also highlights how horses are embedded in Korean mythology and rural life, reflecting the deep-rooted connection of horses to Korean identity. The text indicates the varied uses of horses in agricultural practices as well as in traditional folklore, which illustrates the richness of Korean heritage during the colonial period.

Historians may find it interesting how the article encapsulates the intertwining of practical agricultural utility with cultural traditions, demonstrating how historical narratives around horses continue to influence Korean identity amidst Japanese colonial rule.
