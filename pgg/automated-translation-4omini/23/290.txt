Modern Fortune House  
In the United States, famous as the birthplace of new stories, another miraculous, mysterious, and religious tale has emerged this time. It is located in the "Mutton" in the state of "Massachusetts," at the "Holy Cross" cemetery. Sixty years ago, a Catholic priest named Patrick Power was buried there, and eleven years later, it was reported that numerous sick individuals have flocked to this long-deserted grave, drawn by a miracle, leading to overwhelming numbers of spectators and sick people that exceeded twenty million, creating a great chaos. This can be seen as a manifestation of the American people's increasingly cruel curiosity or perhaps as an expression of a deep-rooted religious sentiment. In any case, the more civilized the person is, the more they mock superstition while simultaneously indulging in a bizarre interest that draws them to superstition itself. How can such civilized beings dare to ridicule the primitive worship of reproduction by primitive men!

The Joseon Hat and the Fake World  
The women, who wear an old-fashioned hat with a cloth tied up in the center of their heads and back, are unaware of any other hair-clipping methods. Yet, the fact that during a single season they wear hats impressively is quite remarkable. If a woman wears a hat, it is considered extremely modern, but in reality, it has a monotonous old-fashioned model (and this could very well become the modern trend). This is what is called "Joseon Hat." However, from now on, this lack of fashion is truly unique. When one sees a woman wearing this, even if she has a very appealing face, one cannot help but feel a repulsive thought that seems unique to me. Until this point, it can be somewhat reconciled with the cold of Seoul and the notion that "Joseon women wear hats" which can have a semblance of pride, but when one observes ladies in streetcars, the affinity does not grow, but rather disdain!

In a Café  
At Café Baron during the late hours. The phonograph plays a tune reminiscent of the sounds of cattle. The waitresses are dainty. Their backs move rhythmically. The electric puppets and the familiar human forms are dancing around. “Alright, let's dance!” One young man in a neatly put-together suit stands up gently and rushes toward a waitress, wrapping his arms around her waist. “Oh! Wait, wait! I really can't dance!” the girl gasps, twisting her body as if she's about to faint! However, it seems like they are awkwardly bumping each other while trying to move in the narrow table space, resembling something pitiful. The steps are mismatched. Social dancing seems to be faltering. Two young men in suits, intoxicated with excitement, take each other's hands and dance a Charleston, moving their feet randomly, filling the room with a chaotic atmosphere. A Russian young man, upon seeing this, apparently finds it amusing and stands up suddenly, joining in. The three men dance together. Yet, the Russian’s dance is truly elegant and graceful. Though he swings his feet freely, he maintains rhythm and elegance. After a bout of chaotic dancing, the two Joseon youths proudly exclaim in Japanese: “Why don't you come here, stranger!” Unfortunately, they do not comprehend the meaning behind the Russian youth's ‘You should learn from me’. Nevertheless, they get up several times to dance gracefully, observing the musicians Hong00, Choi00, and company while strutting like performing artists, thinking them to be the highest-class gentlemen of Joseon, modern and sociable. How utterly disappointing! It is said a sparrow that walks like a stork ends up with its legs aching!

A Blind Person’s First Encounter with the World  
This is a story about a blind person living in Nebraska, America. This person, who was blind since birth, ultimately grew up and graduated from a blind school, and at the age of eighteen (equivalent to nineteen or twenty in our country), suddenly gained sight. “I was diligently listening to the radio, and suddenly, it felt like some electric light buzzed on my forehead, sweeping around the back of my head, and in that instant, I could see!” Various medical theories have been proposed regarding this miraculous event. However, we cannot detail each one here; instead, let us recount a story about this young woman, who at twenty and newly sighted, saw the world clumsily and made silly mistakes.   
She had trouble understanding distance and reached out to grab a clock hanging on the ceiling as if it were a hanging moon, mistakenly pushing a table in the center of the room toward the back wall. Other times, she questioned, “Why does the flower in the vase above the window not appear larger than the chestnut tree on the mountain when I observe them? When I look closely, which is bigger: the flower or the chestnut tree?” 

Due to such experiences, even with sight, the young woman felt more discomfort, and when going out, she deliberately closed her eyes and preferred to feel her way along crowded roads.  

“They were dressed in red and white”  
When she first saw pigs sleeping in a pen, she reportedly exclaimed a ridiculous remark. Additionally, upon seeing someone smile, she was startled by the gleaming white teeth that appeared. The trees in bloom looked like they were all waving their arms in a dance. Upon first seeing a beloved black puppy she had cherished in the dark before her blindness, she felt frightened and shocked. 

Witch's Supporters  
The fight against illiteracy! The collapse of superstitions? This is the only slogan and alarm ringing lately. Many leaders and enlightened ones have devoted their energies and made desperate efforts to achieve this movement. However, the elderly in Joseon, deeply rooted in long-standing ignorance and customs, still refuse to sanction this and dismiss it as a ridiculous act or a shameful remnant of Japanese influence, relying on the authority of their forefathers to drive it back instead. As such, it has led to clashes between the new and the old, causing emotional conflict and turmoil in families. Now, speaking of one aspect of this superstition, the role of shamans, they have long maintained control and command over the ambiguous rituals regarding the fortune and misfortune of Joseon people. In this way, the ignorant people of Joseon (particularly women) have endured. They run to shamans for any ailment: a severe illness or even mild discomfort, refusing to understand that terrifying germs are invading their bodies, instead believing it to be the agitation of wood spirits or the spirit of a deceased person. They even chase these shamans, dance frantically at their insane drumming and cymbal-crashing, and while deceivingly speaking with words of falsehood, they let themselves be robbed of their money and even their lives. It is truly pitiful and makes one want to shed tears.

However, lately, it seems such shamans and their believers have gradually begun to realize the absurdity of these beliefs. They are beginning to understand that there is medicine and doctors to treat the sick, yet the sounds of shamans performing rituals continue unabated in Seoul, in the heart of Joseon. Yet due to the shamans, a strange incident occurred. (In Hamnam, Jeongpyeong-gun) Young men who had established a night school and educated impoverished children reportedly faced a backlash from villagers when they stopped a shaman performing rites in the house of a villager and one student, "O", spoke out against the shaman’s absurdities. Enraged, some eighty elders banded together to raid the night school, beat the instructor, and expel the students. Upon hearing such a story, we can only sigh while contemplating those individuals who have turned their backs on the times, and express a degree of sympathy for their plight. When considering the fates of the children who are being influenced by their misguided beliefs under these restrictive, regressive influences and leadership, one cannot help but feel profound concern. The extent of their obstruction and hindrances to our efforts to eradicate our superstitions and usher in a new life aligned with the spirit of the times is indeed significant. Perhaps it would be best for them to either stay quiet at home or hastily abandon this filthy world, seeking a peaceful rest in the afterlife!

  

**Summary**  

This article explores various facets of society in the early 20th century, particularly focusing on the intersection of modernity and superstition in Joseon, along with the impact of evolving beliefs on individuals and communities. It highlights the ironic dynamics between educated elites' disdain for superstition and the persistent influence of folk beliefs, as seen through the eyes of a newly sighted blind woman and reactions to shamans. Historians may find this juxtaposition revealing, illustrating the tensions within Korean society during Japanese colonial rule and the struggle for modern identity amidst lingering traditional practices.
