발행일	1927년 02월 01일  
기사제목	(創作) 廢物 (Creation: Waste)  
필자	權九玄 (Kwon Gu-hyun)  
기사형태	소설 (Novel)  

It was the last night of December in 1924, a time of snow and wind on the second day of the new month.  
Having just completed the delivery of the evening newspaper despite struggling with numb legs, I shook the snow off my hat that had carelessly piled up from my head to my feet and stepped out from the corner of Jonggak.  

When I think back on it now, it was an exceedingly frightful night, but at that time, I was sweating profusely, feeling as if my burdens were piling up. Perhaps it is too hard for the readers who are far removed from this direct experience to imagine.  

If it were any other night, one might say that the evening deliveries were late, but everyone knows that tomorrow is the first day of the new year. Therefore, the number of newspaper pages today was three times more than usual, totaling twelve pages, about six sheets.  

For the newspaper company, this is no small undertaking, whether considering the need to maintain appearances or adhering to company policy and past traditions. However, given such a rigid personnel structure, producing this volume requires great effort and time, which inevitably falls on the shoulders of the workers below, namely the delivery people.  

I had a relatively small delivery area, so I ordinarily delivered about 150 newspapers, but tonight the number skyrocketed to over 450. However, given my weak constitution, it could be said that managing this is no simple task.  

Although it is Jongno Intersection, being the second lunar day of the year, there were hardly any people around, perhaps because of the fierce cold. In the shabby shops with their shoddy decorations, young clerks loitered about like cats in a cramped space, moving around sporadically underneath the dimly lit and scattered streetlights. The wide avenue, which used to be bustling, was now starkly empty, empty even of passing cars or horse-drawn carriages hoping to make their way through the howling snowstorm.  

When the clumps of snow crashed against my cheeks, the unending suffering seemed to dissipate, contrasted by the stinging cold filling my lungs and smothering everything in its vastness. If anything unusual occurred with anything other than nature’s phenomena, at that moment, I could only imagine how my heart would respond.  

I could not stand here for too long in a scene drenched in a strange mystical thrill. This was partly because the sweat was gradually cooling on my back and somehow, my hands and wrists began tingling with numbness. More importantly, however, the reason was simpler; a single five-won bill (五圓) crammed in my pocket prevented me from standing still.  

I poked my hand into my pocket to check the money again, turned my body, and headed down a side street behind Jonggak. Originally, I had planned to go to an inn for a few drinks, but with my stomach rumbling at this very moment, I thought instead that I would settle for a couple of "dasi nomi" (다시노미).  

I had a preference for a small drink. However, more than just a preference, I had little choice in my options since my salary or wage barely amounted to thirty won a month, which must sustain three people—my mother and younger sister included. It would have been understandable if I were alone, but with all our lives intertwined, this was more than enough to make life difficult.  

With that five-won worth little in my pocket, I reasoned that I could starve rather than die outright. However, it had been three days since I had even gotten to spend a dime, as this quote-unquote "free money" was just enough for a drink here or there.  

At seventeen, I was aware that my sister had been sick for years with tuberculosis and could barely find the strength to manage any medication. My mother, over sixty years old, sought odd jobs to ease her suffering from various ailments; in addition, I faced a multitude of headaches from our dire situation.  

Under such thoughts, my intention to indulge in drink disappeared. Although I was no saint, harboring feelings of sorrow and resentment filled my heart. Not to mention amidst my misery, I knew that those working in the newspaper office had no idea of our plight as they donned sharp suits and indulged in endless banquets, all while I struggled as a humble delivery person just trying to get by.  

When these thoughts crossed my mind, my own miserable living conditions saddened me, but more than that, I filled with disdain for their very actions. I wanted to clench my fists and shout out that I wanted to shake this earth and bury it all.  

After having downed a few bowls of makgeolli, I tipped over the roasted ribs sizzling on the stove and chewed heartily. More than anything else, I savored the taste of the flesh, letting the meat tear away until I was satisfied to the point of feeling content. Then, rather than overfilling my belly on the fermented rice drink, I switched to stronger liquor. The clattering of dishes filled my ears as I lost track of how many more drinks I had, until my body finally relaxed and pain began to creep in.

As I left the inn, I felt a mix of elation and liberation, and my mind softened with the thought of heading to "Shinmaji" (신마지) for a change of pace.  

The snow had mostly subsided, yet the wind howled and the ground remained white. I stuffed my hands in my pockets, taking on a cheerful stride, crunching the snow underfoot as I made my way to the tram station. Just as I nearly reached the area behind Jonggak, I noticed a dark object in the corner of my eye, some circular form collapsing in on itself as it crawled in my direction. Then something caught its attention, and it hesitated, creeping back behind a corner.  

At first, I could not tell what it was. I stopped and stood still, staring intently at it for quite a while. It seemed strange that there was no pathway leading in that direction, and the creature vanished from sight.  

After careful consideration, I concluded this was no apparition, yet I couldn’t reconcile how a person would retreat from me like that. It must have been a thief. At that thought, curiosity stirred in me, pushing me to go see.  

But I hesitated to rush forward, wondering if the thief had any weapons in hand. I grasped the wall and quietly inched my way closer. Each step I took crunched deep into the snow beneath me; I crept low as I approached until just then, the thief suddenly bolted, making a ruckus as he dashed towards the direction of a narrow alley behind the Bank of Korea.  

“Hey! Let’s see what you've got!” I shouted, instinctively clenching my fists as I readied myself.  

The little thief couldn’t have been older than twelve or thirteen, his unkempt hair sticking out haphazardly. In the dim alley, devoid of any streetlamps, I could still make out the details of his worn black student uniform, though his ankles and knees were bare beneath his shabby gray pants. His feet were half buried in the snow, revealing only the tips of worn-out socks. 

“What are you running away for?” I called out, grabbing him by the collar and commanding him sternly.  

“I’m not running away!" he replied shakily, trembling and casting me a glance filled with defiance while bracing himself for an escape.  

“Did you just run from me?”  

“Why would I run from you? You must be mistaken!”  

“Confess, are you related to that thing I see in your hand?”  

“What are you talking about?”  

Now it was my turn to shake him a little harder. I had to keep my frustration from overtaking me.  

“Just fucking tell me—what did you steal?”  

“Did you see me committing theft?”  

“If I hadn’t seen it, how could I know?”  

“Then how would I know that you don’t know?”  

“Listen here, kid. What have you got there?”  

“I’ve got nothing to do with you!”  

“Don’t play dumb with me! I’ll take you to the police if you keep this up.” And I lifted him to reveal what he was holding beneath his armpit.  

Desperately holding the object with both hands, he pleaded, “No, I won't—let me go!”  

His voice took on a panicked tone that made me wary. At that moment, I turned around as I clearly heard some footsteps behind me.

“Police!” I quickly whispered in his ear.  

The little thief froze at my words, turning to look at me with a shocked expression before swiftly attempting to break free of my grip, crying out, “Please let me go!”  

I clutched him even tighter, keeping my voice low, “You stay still—” while turning my gaze to the oncoming police.  

“Come on, what are you up to? Let’s go!” I pushed forward, keeping the struggling boy under my control, glancing back at the approaching police.  

If he were to slip from my grasp, I had no doubt, he would flee without a second thought, and at that moment, I couldn't let him take off, especially with the police so near.  

I was struggling internally; what crime had he committed? He might not yet fully understand what he had done wrong. If he had a home and parents, he wouldn’t be wandering in the cold streets tonight, he would be safe and warm, perhaps partaking in sweet rice cakes and fruits with family or playing games. Why must he roam outside in the harsh, snowy cold?  

This realization filled my heart with pity; I almost felt like a humanitarian, yet clearer thoughts reminded me that he was merely another lost soul in this world. 

I hesitated again as the police walked past without pausing.  

Finally feeling the little boy’s breath becoming steadier at my side, I released him and asked, “What are you doing? You know I wouldn’t harm you, right?”  

“…?”  

“Tell me—do you have parents? Are you on your own?”  

“I don’t have any.”  

“No siblings?”  

“Nothing at all.”  

My suspicions were confirmed.  

“Then where do you sleep, and how do you eat?”  

Although I knew it already, I continued to ask.  

“Me? I sleep at the bridge.”  

“A bridge? How do you sleep there?”  

“I built a hut inside to stay in with a few others.”  

I recalled having heard that such huts were typically home to drug addicts; as this thought crossed my mind, I pressed on, “Are there adults there?”  

“There’s no one like that.”  

“Really!?”  

Although I wasn’t fully convinced by his solemn tone, something stirred within me, prompting me to take a more probing approach.  

“Okay, then what else do you have?”  

“Just rice cake.”  

“Rice cake? Where did you get it?”  

“Right there.”  

He seemed to stumble over his words, lowering his head.  

Not willing to let the matter rest, I shifted my tone and stated, “Follow me. I’ll treat you to some soup.”  

I took my hand off him and led him back into the inn.  

Inside, the atmosphere was different, with hardly any patrons left.  

After treating him to a bowl of soup, I relished another few rounds of drinks myself. A sense of defeat settled in. I recalled that "Shinmaji" I had wanted to visit earlier.  

“As you ought to join us!” a cheerful voice echoed, raising the atmosphere before a pitcher was brought around the room.  

I watched the young boy inhale the scent of the soup voraciously, lost in his own world. The thought of his “bridge” brought forth images of sobering loneliness, akin to the figures of my own siblings now embraced by a suffocating silence.  

My heart sank as I recalled my sister now nestled safely within her quilt at home, no longer capable of bringing forth sweets due to her illnesses, as I stayed up late worrying.  

“Why must I face such difficulties? I’ve become a widower, and here I am, losing hope.”  

The thought burrowed deep within me; I was left feeling utterly defeated as I wandered back into the cold dark night.  

Between us were only the shared struggles in life, and the impermanence of fate loomed heavily. Just as quickly as we find ourselves before tragic events, most would turn to become mere shadows, lost to time and suffering without consolation.  

Under the severities of fate, indeed, there are those who must scavenge what’s left behind by time itself.  

In this world governed by money, one would dare say; in a system where even lives come cheap, will compassion ever live again?  

Perhaps, until the last breath drawn, we will remain pawns, playing beneath the grand strategies only to be sacrificed in the end.  

My mind flashed back to the warm belly of the inn's fire, only to face the chilling thought of sharing values—if I cannot express them as my own, should I let them slide away?  

Wasn’t this the very embodiment of “Waste”?  
—(written in the year 1926, on December 8th)  

**Summary**  

The story revolves around a struggling newspaper delivery man who wrestles with his life choices and moral dilemmas amid a freezing winter night. He encounters a young thief, and through their conversation, he grapples with his own empathy and the harsh social inequalities prevalent during Japanese colonial rule. Through their interaction, the narrative explores themes of poverty, vulnerability, and the human condition, especially focusing on how the less fortunate are disregarded in society. Historians may find the depiction of class struggles and human interactions under colonial pressures revealing as it highlights the social dynamics of the time.

때는 천구백이십사년이 마지막 가는, 눈 날리고 바람부는 섯달금음날 밤이엿다.
나는 열 한점이나 거진 다 되얏을 무릅에서 겨우 석간(夕刊)배달을 맛치고서 머리에서 발등까지 함부로 덥힌 눈을 모자를 버서 툭툭 털며 종각모퉁이를 나섯다.
지금 와서는 생각만 하여도 치가 떨일만치 몹시도 치운 밤이엿건마는 그때의 나는 짐이 무럭무럭날 듯한 더운 땀을 처흘 엿든 것이엿다. 두렵건대 이것의 즉접 체험자가 안인 독자(讀者)로서는 이에 대하야 좀 상상하기에 부족한 혐의가 잇슬넌지도 모르겟다.
여늬 때 가트면 아모리 석간배달이 늣다고 할 지라도 여섯점이나 혹 일곱점이면 끗이 나겟지마는 다 아는 바와 가티 내일은 새해의 첫날이다. 그럼으로 신문페-지 수는 여느때의 삼배나 늘어서 사페-지 한매 엿든 것이 오늘에 한하여서는 십이페-지, 장수로는 석장이나 된다.<134>
이것을 신문사 자체로서는 그의 체면상으로 보던지 신문정책상으로 보던지 또는 전례에 의하야서라던지 그럿케 안이 할 수도 업다고 하겟지만은 판에 박힌듯한 그 인원을 가지고서 이만한 것을 맨들어내자면 노력도 노력이거니와 시간도 안이 걸일 수가 업는 것이라. 그 사이에서 죽느니 사원이하 직공과 배달부들이다.
나는 비교적 배달구역이 좁으니만치 신문부수도 백사 오십부에 지나지 안엇다마는 오늘밤마는 이것의 세목 즉 사백오십여매다. 그러나 나가티 약한 체질로서야 견듸기 힘들지 안는다고도 할 수 업는 것이다.
종로 네거리라고는 하지마는 섯달금음날 밤이니 때가 때라 그런지 일긔가 넘우도 사나운 탓인지 사람의 그름자라고는 별로 업다. 너저분한 점두장식으로 세모대렴매를 표방하는 각 商店에는 젊은 점원들만이 쥐굴을 수직하는 고양이처름 옴초리고 조으는 듯이 안젓슬뿐이오 함부로 널녀잇는 흐릿한 전등불 아래에 열십자로 툭 터진 큼직한 거리는 비일때로 비여 잇다. 으르대는 눈보라만이 그 위를 한판 츨뿐이다.
보기만 하야도 역증이 나리만치 둔하게 꿀여 다니는 전차며 제법 낸체하고 두 눈을 부르대며 내달이는 자동차라든지 어느 요리점으로 가는지 오는지 십흔 꾀죄한 인력거가튼 것은 잇다금 오고가나 엄청나게 울부적 나려치는 이 눈보라 압헤서는 도로혀 잇느냐 업느냐가 문제되리만치 너무나 적도도 가엽시 보엿다.
배꼿가튼 눈송이가 뺨에 턱턱 붓흘때면 미상불 매차운 괴로움도 업는 것이 안이로되 응응 씽씽 쌀쌀하며 천지를 뒤집어 덥는 듯한 이 웅장한 현상을 불 때에는 두 팔을 버리고 가티 고함치며 뛰놀고도 십헛섯다.
만일에 이 자연현상 이외의 다른 사실이 내 눈 압헤 이조자 이 형세로 나타나게 된다면 그때의 이 심장은 엇더할넌지?



입으로 말하기 어려운 무한한 신비적 감흥을 이럿케 늣기면서도 이대로 길게 서 잇슬 수는 업섯다. 그것은 차차 땀이 개여짐을 따라서 발끗으로 손끗으로 등꼴까지 저릿저릿 숨여 올으는 치위를 감각하게 된 것도 한가지 리유라고 하겟지마는, 보다 더 큰 다른 한가지의 리유가 또 잇는 것이다. 그것은 압흐로 차차 설명이 되러니와 위선 간단히 말하지면 포켓트에는 오원(五圓)짜리 지페 한 장이 나를 그대로 길게 못서잇게 하는 것이엿다.
나는 포켓트에다 손을 너어 돈을 다시 만저보며 종각뒤ㅅ길로 몸을 돌여 세웟다. 그것은 위선 칩기는 고사하고 배ㅅ속이 출출한 김이라 「다ㅅ지노미」나 좀 하자는 조건이엿다나.
나는 본대 술을 조와 하엿다. 조와 한다는 이 보다도 만히<135> 마섯다. 사정이 허락하는 범위 안에서는 얼마든지 사양치안코 마시는 술이라면 더 말할 것이 잇스랴.
그러나 월급이라 할넌지, 품삭이라 할넌지, 한달에 쭉 해야 겨우 삼십원 남즛이 밧는 돈을 가지고 그나마 혼자 가트면 모르지마는 세 식구-어머니와 누이동생을 합하야-가 목숨을 이어가는 내 처지로는 이만하면 무던하다 십흐리만치 술양을 한꺼번에 채와 보기는 고사하고 단 몃잔식이라도 잇대놋코 먹을수도 업는 것이다. 운수가 조와야 한달에 두서너번 그것도 책역 보아가며 먹게 된다.
이 오원으로 말해도 그럿타. 내게 웬 눈먼 돈이 오원은 그만두고 단 오십젼인들 드러 올 리가 잇스랴. 그럿타고 제법 신문사에서 년말 수당금이나 얼마 어든 줄로 알아서도 안이 된다. 내가 C신문사의 배달부가 된 지가 벌서 해ㅅ수로는 삼년째라도 아즉까지 년말수당이라는 것은 밧아 본 적이 업다.  수당은 고사하고 월급도 제때에 잘 내지 못하는 신문사라면 그만 안이냐? 이 오원도 두달전부터 월급 속에서 밀여 오든 것을 그야말로 년말의 덕택으로 이 달 월급과 한거번에 수당대신으로 차즌 것이다.
이 돈을 마자 보탠대도 가용에 부족이 날 것은 물논이겟지만은 이것 한 장 잇서도 못 살기는 맛창가지요 업서도 맛창가지니 짐 한 장 뿌스는 셈 대리라 생각하고, 우물 쭈물하야 어머니를 속히고서 따로 제처논 것이다. 당초의 게획은 올해도 마지막 가고 하니가 남들은 요리ㅅ집에 가고 기생집에 가는 대신으로 막걸니나 한잔 톡톡이 하고 「신마ㅅ지」나 오래간만이니 한번 가 보자는 것이엿다.
그러나 돈을 주먹에 쥔 제가 사흘째나 되도록 쓰기가 앗갑다는 이보다도 쓸 용긔가 업서서 지금까지 그대로 가지고 잇섯다. 열일곱에 나는 내 누의동생은 몃해를 두고 전매국에 단이든 보수로 지금 페병에 걸여서 누은 대로 약 한첩도 변변히 못먹고 꼬치꼬치 말나 잇다. 륙십이 넘은 어머니는 남의 바누질 품팔기에 안질과 요통이 나서 신음중이다. 그외에도 이로 머리ㅅ골 글키는 궁한 잔소리를 다해서 무엇하랴?
엇잿든 이런 생각에 치눌여서 참아 손에 내들지를 못하얏다.

이럿케 생각을 하며 그네를 바라볼 때에는 도로혀나 보다 달관(達觀)한 선배의 늣김이 잇는 듯 하얏다.
나는 가슴에다 불이 나 때는 것처럼 렬이 확확 치밀며 답답증이 나서 더 잇고십지 안엇다.
나는 오십전ㅅ자리 은화한푼을 남기고는 잇는대로 그네 압헤다 털어놋코서 뚸여나왓다.
눈가루를 날리는 사나운 바람은 여전히 음산(陰酸)과 우울(憂鬱)로 가득찬 밤한울을 으르대며 나려친다.
이것은 미구에 닥처올 생명의 봄을 꿈꾸는 대지(大地)의 수란(受難)이다.-〔일천구백이십육년십이월팔일〕-<145>
