기사형태	시 (Poem)  

Sijo  
Plum blossoms return in the spring festival on the aged tree trunk; though the old branches may bloom,  
The spring snow falls thickly, causing confusion and stirring feelings.  

With snow and moon filling the courtyard, O wind, do not disturb. Though I know the sound of the dragging shoes signifies someone’s approach,  
In longing and yearning, I may perhaps wish to meet.  

Hidden away in the East Pavilion, is it a azalea or a cuckoo flower? As the universe turns white with snow, I cannot bravely  
Let you know who among us transforms into a plum blossom, while the white snow and the springtime are yet lingering.  

With snow falling in the pine forest, each branch becomes a flower. I take one to present to you, dear one,  
After you have seen it, what will happen if it melts?  

Over there, the Mount Lu (羅浮山) in snow shows a black and rugged silhouette. The old trunk stands sturdily,  
Causing buds to bloom anew. Even if the rotting fruit remains only half, what spring feelings could arise?  

Who could bend low as the snow is disturbed? If it bends, will the willows not bloom among the snow?  
Perhaps only the high and noble pines endure the cold years.  

**Summary**  

This piece includes a collection of sijo poems reflecting themes of nature, longing, and the transition from winter to spring. Each poet captures different elements of the season, from the beauty of plum blossoms to the sights of snow and the yearning for connection. For historians, this poetic portrayal reveals aspects of cultural identity during the colonial period in Korea, where traditional forms were preserved despite the pressures of modernization and colonial influence. The attachment to nature as a muse in these works could indicate resistance to the erasure of cultural heritage under Japanese rule.
