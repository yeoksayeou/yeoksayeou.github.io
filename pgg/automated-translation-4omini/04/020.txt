Breaking Tradition: A New Step Towards Creating a New Life, My New Practice  
Baek Nam-kyu et al.  

As the days go by and things become new, clinging only to the past and singing its praises is evidence of falling behind! For us, who are shackled by customs regarding ethics, morals, and lifestyle, it may not be futile to listen to your courageous and decisive practical experiences in executing new things. — A Reporter —  

Wearing socks with the trousers not tucked in  

Central High School  Baek Nam-kyu  

When you asked me about my feelings regarding this practice, I will answer in one word. This has been ten years since I returned from Tokyo. It may not be a particularly noteworthy experience. To sum up my thoughts, it is as follows.  

First, let me mention the beginnings.  
1. One day in Tokyo, I went to see the tribesmen from Taiwan and visited a military factory. As we left, I was struck by a sight that impressed me. It looked like "leading a pack of feral dogs," as the Japanese police shouted around. The first thing that caught my eye was the "tucked-in trousers" we were wearing, as they were rolled up to the ankle and snugly wrapped around the leg.  

2. After returning to our country, I have encountered various feelings. Many things have affected me, but among them...  
a. We wear wide-legged trousers, which droop and fail to present a good appearance; in other words, the lower part of our clothing appears to sag and reveals unsightly combinations (which now many middle-aged people still show).  
b. Those who wish to appear fashionable adorn their trousers with colorful socks—red or blue— fluttering about like women's clothing. In other words, they’re displaying women's beauty on a man’s leg (as rural young men often do).  

To explain how things have come to this, it might be difficult to remember when our formal attire and leg coverings began to be discarded. Since then, we have gradually regressed instead of improving.  

As I reflect on my experience over the past ten years...  
1. While I have faced ridicule from others, I have not felt particularly anxious.  
a. I am called a horse groom for not being elegant.  
b. I am considered strange.  
c. I am mistaken for a monk.  
d. To understand the reasons, look at the lower half of my attire.  

I have heard various remarks like these. However, when I go out on a rainy day, I see that my companions are quite well-dressed. Or among the travelers, my companions are also dressed well. Perhaps those who do this find it lively, simple, and convenient? Or when we see it; how do people from other parts of the world perceive it...  

To sum it up, I have not yet considered this practice to be a success but rather I am merely shouting, "If there are things we need to correct, we must do so as soon as possible." Oh— I always cherish the photo of my old principal, the late Yu Gil-soon (兪吉濬), in his rounded collar coat. Whenever I meet my close friend, Lee Beom-seong (이범승), a member of the clothing reform group, we always share such discussions.  

Aside from the "tucked trousers," there are many other matters that I have experienced regarding this.  

Wearing hats  

Ewha Womans University  Kim Hwal-lan  

Hats were originally worn by our people, and many still wear them today. The moment we abandoned the so-called new-style women’s fashion of curling their hair and dressing them up, we unconsciously discarded this practice and never looked back. Furthermore, there are few who wear hats, and wearing scarves has now become quite fashionable.  

However, it is neither uncomfortable nor unattractive, yet I do not understand why people throw them away despite their usefulness; or when it comes to keeping women's heads warm in winter, it proved to be perfect. Last winter, I thought to try wearing it after hesitating but ended up not doing so. However, starting this winter, I successfully implemented it.  

Since it's a new thing for me, I may be anxious about what others think; there might be hesitance about wearing it, but I do not feel this way at all. And while seeing people wear it, they might say it doesn't fit well with their shoes, but in my opinion, those thoughts or the worry about ridicule is not my motivation. It is simply something made by our hands, something we have worn before, and it is not only warm but also economical in terms of time and finances compared to making a hat or scarf, wearing only this one.  

It may have such advantages, but who knows if someday not only Korean women but also Western women might imitate this and make something similar?  

Using polite language with servants and younger people  

Bang Jeong-hwan  

Irrespective of whom one might encounter, I maintained the commitment not to use harsh language, and since five years ago, I resolved not to say "do" ("하라") or "do" ("해라") to anyone. I thought if I spoke "please do" ("하십시오") to those younger than me, it would misuse hierarchy and the aged convention imposed by old standards. Especially, for young people who lack knowledge or wealth to say "do" ("해라") to an elder is certainly a major fault of traditional hierarchy. One of the new rules we have to establish is to use polite language for everyone, and I have devoted myself to practice this since five years ago.  

Of course, initially, it felt quite awkward. When encountering someone I had previously said "do" to ("해라 해라"), it was not easy to switch suddenly to saying "please do" ("하시오"), and I felt remarkably strange to myself as well. However, observing others responding politely started to transform my thoughts.  

When calling out to a child or a laborer passing on the street with "hey," they didn’t recognize it was a call to them, and I frequently had to say "younger one" ("이애야") or "young man" ("여보게") until they responded. There was a time when this might have easily turned back to old habits.  

Now, even when I approach an elder looking to say “do” ("해라") or "please do" ("하십시오"), it does not come out easily. Yet I still find it difficult to say "please do” ("하오") to the young people among my own family members still growing up in our household.  

The fact is that my efforts to use polite language are harder when family members do not follow suit.  

Now we find ourselves with Kim Gi-dan who works with me has long since adapted himself to the polite usage of language. We started together, yet Kim has comfortably used polite language with young ones around his home including his child.  

The moment he said, "If you can’t use polite language with your child, whom else can you do it with?" illustrates how unfortunate those who think in such a twisted way must be.  

Execution of layered clothing and eliminating ironing  

Jeong Sin Women’s School  Kim Yeong-soon  

It has been three years since I began this practice. 

Though it may seem strange to many people to get through winter without wearing cotton clothes, their perceptions are initially rooted in an ingrained belief that without cotton clothes, one cannot survive winter.  

As for my experience, I began this practice of getting rid of cotton clothing even though initially it seemed challenging, but now it has converted into habit. Now, when I see someone in cotton clothing, it feels quite uncomfortable to me.  

In conclusion, it turns out that no matter what it is, once I resolve to stick to it, it can become achievable.  

There are many reasons to discontinue wearing cotton; from a hygienic standpoint, it indeed is quite relevant. To wear cotton at home, one should frequently replace the old cotton with new, and it usually requires the help of women in the family to reorganize it. Then, any residues from the cotton can pose health hazards, letting germs linger as one constantly handles it, thus can unknowingly carry risks to health.  

Moreover, if I wear linen clothes alongside cotton, and as they fit closely to the body, it does not appear bulky nor overly warm like cotton looms. When you consider the necessary time to make cotton clothing, anyone can imagine the effortless choice between the two—hygienically, economically, and efficiently—layered clothing proves better.  

I have also abandoned ironing completely, and nowadays, merely hearing the word "ironing" makes me indignant. We find that housewives see ironing as essential; however, I have experienced that even linen materials require less effort than expected.  

With solid fabrics, you can wash and simply air them without heavy ironing, allowing my attire to be much more presentable. It turns out that even if I abandon ironed clothing, they appear finer than those forced to be pressed and are far easier to manage for cleaning and convenience.

Since the dawn of promoting domestic product consumption, I have adhered to these practices, and I aim for persistent consistency with the abandonment of colorful clothing and ironing. Nevertheless, it seems that students still find it challenging to give up on cotton.  

As my story continues, even Western women can walk in with cotton attire in the similar surroundings; therefore, while living in western-style house structures makes it easier for those who live in Korean-style houses, we will improve our conditions only if our mindset evolves with our living practices. 

Cutting hair short  

Dongdaemun Women’s Hospital  Hyeon Deok-sin  

Short hair was once quite the trend, but nowadays it seems many are growing their hair out again. However, when I asked women from progressive backgrounds why they are growing their hair, they mentioned it is because short hair has not yet gained popularity among the masses, and to work in harmony with the general public, they should follow the crowd.  

But I got my haircut not attracted to its beauty nor for any specific trend but simply for convenience. Thus I have no intention of letting my hair grow long again. 

The ease of washing my face or combing my hair is incredibly convenient to go out as well. Moreover, it does not leave a dark mark on the back of my jacket; it looks neat, and in winter, wearing a hat is sufficient to keep warm.  

But if spring comes and the wind blows hard without wearing a hat, I might face slight discomfort, but essentially, maintaining a strict routine of hairdressing every day could consume a lot of valuable time and effort, which is less efficient economically.  

High time-savings would enable me to utilize them in more meaningful ways.  

Starting to wear hats as women, at first, was quite the laughing matter.  

Joseon Women's Academy  Shin Albet  

Wearing a hat as a woman! It might seem unusual for Korean women, and thinking back to my early experiences with hats brings a smile to my face.  

Due to my long-term education in school, I saw students making knitted hats for each other and thought, since they wear it, it should be plausible for a woman to wear something made by ourselves. So, I thought it would be better to wear one rather than in a traditional method with a funny feel.  

However, it has indeed been many years since that time. When making hats, I had zero courage to go out wearing it at first. Thus, I created a discouragement for myself by wearing it under my clothes, and eventually began to wear it publicly. 

In the beginning, no matter where I went, people would giggle and laugh at me as I passed by. Once, walking on the street, even the prisoners working outside laughed at me, displaying their contempt.  

Thus, at first, I dared not venture into the main streets with my hats but walked in shadows or covered it when meeting acquaintances; I would even duck behind others’ gates before entering.  

As time progressed over the years and it became a habit to wear it, it wasn't awkward anymore. It had become customary for women to wear hats indoors as well; however, back then, I had to remove mine upon entering other homes.  

Even back then, it was common practice to never wear them indoors, while now it is easier to don it.  

My hats are not a form of extravagance or an attempt to showcase style. I never used hair products or cosmetics, yet by wearing a hat in the winter, I require no fussing about it, as it becomes practical.  

It proves far better than wearing the inconveniences of older styles which often look tarnished; therefore, I believed this single unit would suffice for multiple occasions.  

Once it becomes more common to wear hats, it would undoubtedly herald an improvement in our day-to-day lives.  

Through my experience of wearing a hat, it seems any practice requires one to adapt to new habits. However, once one changes their habits, it becomes the norm for everyone.  

**Summary**  

This article presents various personal accounts of individuals who have adopted new practices that break away from traditional norms in Korean society during the colonial period. These practices include wearing socks with trousers not tucked in, adopting hats as part of women's attire, using polite language regardless of societal hierarchies, and discarding cumbersome practices like cotton clothing and ironing. The accounts reflect a desire for modernity and practical lifestyles while challenging traditional conventions, highlighting a broader cultural shift during a time of colonial influence and national identity formation. Historians might find this exploration of everyday practices significant as it illustrates how societal norms were contested and redefined amid the complexities of colonialism and modernization in Korea.

단임 안치고 양말 올려 신는것

中央高普 白南奎

나의 이것 실행(實行)에 대한 감상(感想)을 물으시니까 간단히 한마듸 말슴하겟슴니다. 이것이 내가 東京에서 도라온 뒤이니까 발서 십년이올시다. 별로 들으실만한 늣김이라고 할 수 업슴니다. 다못 생각나는대로 말슴하면 이러합니다.
먼저 시작한 늣김을 말하겟슴니다.
一. 東京에서 어느날이든가 대만(臺灣)에서 생번(生蕃)의 단톄가 왓다하기에 사람구경 갓슴니다. 마침 포병공창(砲兵工廠)을 구경시키고 나오는 거리에서 맛낫슴니다.
그 모양이 비젓이 「사나운 김생의 떼를 몰고 다니는 것」처럼, 日本巡査가 사이사이에서 호령하면서 끌고 감니다.
그런데 가장 먼저 내의 눈에 보기 슬케 띄이는 것은 우리가 발목에 매고 다니는 「단임」을 썩 길게 만들어서 발목에서 친친 감어 올라와서 다리 전부를 잠맷습듸다.
二. 우리나라에 도라온 뒤에 여러 가지의 늣김이 잇섯슴니다. 이것도 한두가지가 아니지마는 其 中에 하나인 것이 잇슴니다.<2>
ㄱ. 우리가 바지통을 넙게 만들어 가지고 질질 끌면서 옷주체를 못하는 모양 다시 말슴하면 옷 입은 아래도리가 말할수 업시 축처저 가지고 게우른 쌍이 들어나는것(지금만히 시골어른네가 하는 것)
ㄴ. 좀 모양을 내는 이는 「단임」에다가 호사를 내려고 무색것을 씀니다-붉으스름-파르스름-한 것이 부인네옷 고롬가티 바람에 날립니다. 말하자면 남자의 다리에다 女子의 미를 냄니다.(시골 젊으신네가 만히 하는 것)
이와가티 되여진 까닭을 말슴하고 보면 지금부터 몃해나 되엿는지는 모르지마는 우리가 외출할 때에 닙고 다니든 례복(禮服)이며 다리에 감든 행전(行纏)을 제절로 버서 버리게 된 뒤에 아모 개량(改良) 고만 고대로 퇴화(退化)가 되고 말어 버린것입니다.
이러케 하여온 십년동안의 늣김을 말슴합니다.
一. 남의 조소(嘲笑)를 만히 바닷슴니다마는, 나의 마음에는 별로 불안이 업섯슴니다.
ㄱ. 마부(馬夫)처럼 되여 점잔치 못하다고.
ㄴ. 이상(異常)하다고.
ㄷ. 증(僧侶))갓다고.
ㄹ. 그 이유를 알려면 아랫도리만 보라고.
이와가튼 여러 가지의 말슴을 들엇슴니다. 그러나 내가 비올때(雨日)나서보면 내의 동무가 꽤 만습듸다.
또는 여행하는 분 중에 나의 동무가 만습듸다. 아마 이러케 하는 것이 활발(活潑)하고, 간단하고, 편리하기에 하시는 것 안닙니까? 또는 우리가 보니까 그러치 만일 세계사람이 볼 때는 엇덜넌지오...
몰아 말슴하면 내가 이것이 잘된 것으로 아직 생각지 못함니다마는 다못 『우리가 지금 고칠 필요가 잇는 것이면 빨리 빨니 고처 나가야 하겟다는것』만을 웨치(絶呌)고저 함니다. 아-나의 다니든 옛날 中央學校長 故 兪吉濬 先生의 團領(옷깃을 동글게 만든 옷)의 사진을 늘-귀하게 두고 봅니다. 또는 나의 친고에 衣服改良派의 하나인 李範昇과 맛나면 늘-이런 말슴을 함니다.
이 「단님」 외에도 여러 가지가 잇슴니다마는 이것에 대한 늣김이라고는 이러함니다.<3>

넷 主人차진 남바위
원래부터 우리 손으로 만드는 것 남이 도리여 본바들지도 모릅니다

梨花女子專門學校 金活蘭

남바위는 원래 우리 사람이 쓰던 것이요 지금도 만히 씁니다. 소위 신식 녀자로 양머리를 틀고 쪽찌던 것을 페지하면서부터 무의식적으로 서양것이 조커니 하고 이것을 집어 내던지고 도라보지 안케 된 것이지요. 그리고는 모자를 쓰는 이도 약간 잇고 목도리를 두르는 것은 아조 류행이 된 것입니다.
그러나 그것이 불편한 것도 아니요 보기 실흔 것도 아닌데 덥허노코 집어내 버리는 것이 엇덜넌지도 모르고 또는 극히 치운때 녀자의 머리를 방한(防寒)하는데는 이것이 조켓다고 생각하야 작년 겨울부터 써보랴고 하엿스나 주저하다가 못하고 말엇다가 올겨울부터 실행해 보앗슴니다.
처음 하는 일이니까 남의 생각에는 좀 조마조마하야 붓그러우리라고  추측할넌지도 모르지만 조금도 그런 생각이 업슴니다. 그리고 이것을 쓰고 다니는 것을 보고 구두 신은 우에 모양이 어울리지 아니하다고는 할넌지 모르지만 내 생각에는 아모러한 붓그런 생각이나 또는 남이 흉보는 것을 긔어코 단행해 보겟다는 그런 용긔를 가지고 하는 것도 아닙니다. 다만 우리 손으로 만드는 것이요 그전부터 쓰던 것이요 또는 방한이 잘되고 모양이 흉업지 안코 편리할 뿐 아니라 모자 만들어 쓰고 목도리 만들어 두르는 것보다는 이것 하나만을 쓰는 것이 경제나 시간으로 보아서도 괜찬켓다고 생각한데 지나지 못함니다.
그러나 이것에 그와가튼 장점이 잇다면 장래에는 우리 朝鮮녀자들 뿐만 아니라 서양녀자들까지라도 이것을 모방하야 그와 근사한 것을 만들어 쓰게 될넌지도 누가 압니까?<4>

힘부름하는 사람과 어린 사람에게도 존대를 함니다
