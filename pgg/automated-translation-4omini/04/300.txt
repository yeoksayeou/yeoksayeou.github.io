기사제목	『엇더한 脚本을』, 農村劇의 特性을 들어 地方靑年會 兄님들께 (About What Kind of Script: A Discussion on the Characteristics of Rural Drama for the Local Youth Association Brothers)  
필자	李慶孫  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literature, Other)  

About what kind of script is what I am now writing as a letter of greeting to the brothers of the cultural department of the Local Youth Association, and I would like to discuss rural drama and its characteristics with a heartfelt mind.

Some of you may feel disappointed that my title does not take a more solemn approach like "Rural Drama Movement" or "About Rural Drama," but these days my title sounds like the appearance of a fire brigade with its whiskers trimmed.

Thus, this writing is similar to responses to your letters asking me, "Do you not have any written scripts?" and "What kind of scripts should be submitted anyway?"

"What kind of script?" Of course… Some of you might pull out Lenin's biographical book to respond, but what I want to say is different from that.

"Hatred for landlords!" 

No, that is not what I mean, so please read to the end slowly.

These days, when looking at the plays published in magazines in Seoul, or the compositions enjoyed by young people in the provinces, they all merely say, "The current Korean scripts are" - "long" and "lack flavor."

"Long"

"Lack flavor"

This might sound like the complaints of a chef complaining about something that has no taste, but it is not a trivial matter at all.

Imagine you advertise one of the scripts published in a Seoul magazine saying "This was written by a famous XX from Korea," "This is a play performed by young people from my hometown," or "This is sponsored meaningfully by the local Sankyoshibun." Then you deliver a lengthy speech interpreting the meaning of the play and then it is publicized.

"Oh, it's boring!"

"Shut up!"

"Ha ha ha ha"

"Who's the kid laughing?"

So, having successfully examined that night, the next night you might go to the theater to see a melodrama from a local troupe that has shown up in your hometown.

When you see members of the audience resembling those who have taken medicine that evening, thronging around, practicing the expression of Mother Chunhyang, you might feel a bit angry.

"My fellow townsfolk are so pitiable."

"How could our public performance that night not succeed? I know how it felt the night I first performed Ibsen's A Doll's House!"

Suddenly applause might seem like it would touch the ceiling of the theater again. On stage, there appear two foolish figures with grotesque eyebrows, doing exactly idiotic antics.

"Oh poor folks!"

You might not be able to endure it and might storm out of the theater.

However, I cannot agree with your dramatic theories. And I am not saying that melodrama is better. I am merely saying that the plays being published in the Korean magazines at that location are “lacking.” The melodramas must retain some flavor that you ought to reflect on.

"Flavor!" 

Whether it is called "taste," or "charm," or anything related to "thematic farming and agricultural humor," it matters not. In any case, Korean plays currently lack this flavor. "Why is that?" I know the reason.

Imagine two friends discussing tomorrow's weather.

"I still believe it will be sunny."

"Don’t talk nonsense; even if it rains, the weather will be harsh here; it will be the rainy season.”

Rainy season? It will be so hot that we will need umbrellas."

Works that are the first of their kind transitioning from romanticism to naturalism, or the first works transitioning from naturalism to expressionism - or even the first strokes of painters who have broken new descriptions are always "stumbling."

You might just keep shouting "Lenin" and only looking for Lenin without creating the "taste" of the work alongside the grotesque facial expressions of Lenin and making something "flavorless." Also, aiming to shout "a new era is upon us!" might resemble how Korean students listen as if the sound of school bells corresponds to the "stumbling" behavior of a miserly person.

"The miserly behavior of stumbling."

I am not attempting to delve into the subtle architecture behind the "taste" packed in the dialogue I wrote. Furthermore, I do not even know much about these kinds of vivid dramas and comedies to speak of them.

You must prepare to not let those in your hometown who do not understand "new drama," or your brothers who do not understand "Lenin," to "laugh! Why are you! Can't you see my solemn face?!"

"Use it if a bit bitter!"

"If your dialogue contains terms you don't know, come to the Cultural Department of the Youth Union tomorrow to learn and don't simply shout!”

I know well what kind of hardships you suffer. However, that is far too stiff. It is much too pitiable on the wedding stage. The groom and bride are carried on the covers of the Six Laws (六法全書). The guests must artificially mingle and feast. You might catch sight of the bride secretly smiling. You might see the groom’s hands an awkward mixture and then gently grasping the bride’s hand. You might see everyone in the hall notice that and starting to all gasp with amazement at the drunken appearance of the past with horrified faces. Do you understand that "taste"?

Do not neglect that "taste."

Do not neglect the "taste" of life.

When you write a script, you will determine your characters as "landlord," "tenant," "his daughter," "virtuous youth" – and you will lay out a backbone. Of course, that can be easy for a school moral textbook for elementary students, and you can’t know if it even might be a performance. But making it tasteless like the court secretary's shorthand record would be a disaster.

Also, even if you could create taste, do not fall back to the expressions of the old days saying there is no event that contains "taste." For example, using cues like “look over there” or expressions like “old man old man, crossing the field” to try to spark a new flavor is the most useless endeavor.

Expression and description evolve; they will never regress to the old. The thick lines and wild colors of modern futurism are distinct from the carvings of ancient barbarians from cave drawings.

Whenever I go out to the provinces, I find one or two comrades wrongly understanding and longing for what they call "classical."

Whether you make the audience laugh or cry, it is your freedom. However, we must always have “taste” that can be shared by a group or common people, connecting the old day's Korean drama "mask play" and the new Russian dramas to allow for amusement.

Because of this, Korean drama is often less than a middle school festival, and those who laugh with disbelief about that may be right. It does not matter to those who cry weeping for the absence of a film or stage that appears to be stamped out the same.

As I look at the scripts gathered at the office of the Töwoll Society, I told Park Hyung, who was present there, looking over the hundred scripts:

"Among those, the beautiful ones are "Sanseonangdang," "Getting Married," and "Monday." 

In my opinion, what you should first execute is only "imitation." That which is also not essentials should be abandoned, and research into "comedy" and "fairy tales" must be done.

Moreover, do not fear the theater or all the traditional rules and rather focus solely on the vividness of the voices as they sing while farming in the fields, noisy and unrestrained.

If the ambiance between performers and the audience is dull as a police desk’s transactional mood, then nothing can be done.

Though I may not have emitted all I wanted to say, reading a script is always worth more than a hundred theories.

You should read Kume Masao’s "Origin of Jizo Religion," as it relates to our Töwoll Society’s "Sanseonangdang.” You will find it pleasing when you read it on the thirteenth day.

  
**Summary**  

The article discusses the essence of rural drama in Korea, emphasizing the need for authentic flavor and engagement in plays rather than following outdated styles. It critiques contemporary Korean scripts for being overly long and lacking emotional depth. The author argues that successful performances must connect with the audience in a heartfelt manner, reflecting their realities and capacities for laughter and tears. This piece could be of interest to historians as it highlights the cultural context of theater during the colonial period in Korea and showcases the struggles for authentic expression in arts amidst oppressive systems.
