기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)  

Reporter’s Confession  
						Former Reporter  
Now that my body has fallen so low, everything brings tears to my eyes. However, I have almost run out of tears, and my eyes have become completely dry. No matter how much I repent and wish for forgiveness, can the sins I have committed ever be washed away? I would have been better off not learning to write and becoming a completely ignorant person, like the humble peasants who toil in the fields, preserving their innocent hearts that they can sincerely offer to the Lord, and then eventually returning to the earth as they once were. 

With my inadequate knowledge, how could I dare to present myself as a reporter? I wasn’t even able to graduate from middle school properly, and I fell prey to the addiction of fleeting literature! I longed to be called a reporter, and after many attempts and encouragements, I managed to join XX Newspaper as a social reporter over six or seven years ago. 

With nothing inside me, nothing on my back, and no money in my pocket, I became a king without a crown and a public tool for the masses. From the very first day I joined, I strutted around, full of youthful vanity, and thought, was it really so strange? But at that time, I could hardly endure the rush of excitement that came from having the reporter title bestowed upon me. I often think back to the past when scholars waved red and white cards and ran around proudly; it was an absurdity more foolish than anything else. 

Recalling those times makes me so embarrassed that I can hardly stand it. I couldn’t even master a foreign language, and though Chinese classic texts seemed unnecessary, I still couldn’t entirely abandon them. The fact that I couldn’t freely use Chinese characters meant that I couldn’t even write a simple news piece. I could not read articles written by others due to my inability to recognize common characters, and especially when it came to economic reports, I didn’t even understand basic concepts. I had no worries about writing such articles since there were economic reporters distinct from myself handling those topics, but the fact that I was unable to read my own company’s newspaper was a clear reflection of my profound ignorance. My awareness of this now is sharper than ever, making me actually quite embarrassed. 

Why didn’t I then break my writing instruments and search anew for the path of study? This realization now weighs heavily on my heart. Yet I was bound, as if to have my life tied to the whim of my adversary, unable to break free while gripped by feelings of shame even back then. Naturally, at the newspaper, I had to flatter the department heads and the editor-in-chief, and when I faced the educated class outside, my head would bow in deference. What grounds did I have to stubbornly insist on my arguments as a reporter, and how could I ever establish any authority as a reporter? Then, when I faced the ignorant masses, I thought to myself, “as a reporter…” 

Isn’t loyalty something that only pertains to saints serving two kings? Isn’t it only outstanding women who do not bow before two husbands? Even when faced with poverty, could a noble scholar’s decayed morals be simply measured by their steadfast unwillingness to be subservient, or are they merely rot without principles and aspirations? In the face of that reality, how trivial my past life was! Even if my heart is stirred to write this down, given that it is a mere confession, at least I had to put something of my wrongs down here.

After becoming a member of XX Newspaper, I’ve changed workplaces exactly five times and have worked continuously in this industry. Since there were no major political factions or backgrounds supporting the newspapers in Joseon, if such means had existed, I could understand why I would have been embraced by such organizations. Even if a newspaper had no significant backing, there would still be some minor principles differing from each other. When I worked at this paper, I could not escape its corporate ideology, and when I went to that other paper, I could not evade its ideology either. I wandered from one paper to another without any grounding like a leaf in the wind, repeatedly switching sides while living a life devoid of conscience.

When I worked at XX Newspaper, my ideological tendencies began to lean toward the side of the advocates for the wealthy, diligently attacking the rich in my articles while supporting the poor. Then a sudden upheaval arose in the company, leading me to XX Newspaper. I knew that the owners of that paper were not likely to support my views, so I prepared myself for the fact that I couldn’t maintain my previous position with my thoughts and attitude. Though the current circumstances of so-called literate people in Joseon necessitate that they bow down to material demands, society accepts, forgives, and takes into account that reality. However, my conscience pricked me deeply. I had mustered the courage a few times to stand up for myself, but on occasions when that courage failed, my heart burned with shame.

In the end, whether I discarded the honorable title of a newspaper reporter or dressed up and wandered around while still claiming to be a reporter, the two states of being hardly differed. I cannot grasp why I allowed my conscience to deceive me in those instances. Is it not better to die than to betray one’s principles? I lacked the resolution to hold onto that fundamental belief, making it impossible for me not to regret those days.

My lack of common knowledge stemmed from my insufficient learning. Acting as if I were a reporter, swaying to and fro, toasting and flattering others, and being rowdy while spending time with courtesans—I could excuse my youthful recklessness as merely being part of my folly back then, yet none of that absolves me of greater offenses. What remains a source of constant regret is that I was living in deceit. The saying goes that a thousand agreement cannot equal a single voice of protest from a single individual. Even now, newspapers proudly proclaim themselves to be a public tool and the ears and eyes of the people. If I, as a recognized member of this reputed sphere, have such authority in my hands, how could I fail to pursue fair and honest journalism and instead bow to others in the slightest matters? Where did my failures lie? Why did I not fight strongly against establishments and instead chose to live a life while selling my conscience?

Such events transpired. There arose a civil lawsuit between an author and a publisher that extended over several years, involving their wrongdoings. When I was at XX Newspaper, I wrote extensively on this without reservations. Upon moving to XX Newspaper, I discovered that the people managing it had ties with the opposing party, so when covering the trial, I was still compelled by the same mannerism with the articles I wrote for that paper, though my writing faced deletion by the editor-in-chief. 

Nonetheless, I failed to protest vigorously or maintain my stance. Such occurrences were not rare but rather a daily affair. I found myself continually concerned, fearing that my writings might benefit the news management, yet I still continued to write unflinchingly for some time. Reflecting on these circumstances, my heart mirrored that same failing. It was an obligation for a journalist to write with the utmost impartiality; I should never have polluted my conscience with misleading articles. 

Once, concerning a love affair incident involving a young man and a female student where the latter attempted suicide after being deceived by the former, I composed a serialized romance piece. At that time, a friend of mine, who was connected to the young man, asked me to soften the portrayal. I inadvertently lightened the young man’s faults and shifted some blame onto the female student. Eventually, a similar incident arose, and noticing that the girl was not unattractive, I developed some desire and immediately began writing another romance piece. The truth was that the female student bore more than her fair share of blame; however, I only targeted the young man’s transgressions while depicting the girl favorably. I truly found it corrupt to think that my pen could bring any honor or merit from such actions.

If I were to confess, my guilt knows no bounds, as this does not only pertain to my own self, while many individuals are entangled in the affairs of the newspaper. I cannot, having now left that position, disclose everything about the inner workings of that office as my conscience fails to permit it. Nevertheless, considering the need to pen down some of my past wrongdoings as a means of confession, I will conclude this section now.

Seven years in journalism! What does it amount to? Presently, I find myself in dire straits, with my wife and children starving and creditors chasing me, making me think that this is a karmic outcome. I find no way to differentiate myself from a person released from prison! 

**Summary**

The article is a confessional reflection from a former reporter who recounts the shame and regret of his experiences in journalism, highlighting his superficiality and the compromises he made in the face of moral challenges. He expresses a yearning for a simpler, more honest life and laments the loss of integrity throughout his career. His reflections shed light on the struggles of journalists in colonial Korea, revealing the inherent conflicts faced when attempting to maintain journalistic integrity amidst societal expectations and material needs, which is of great interest to historians studying the dynamics of power, media, and ethics during this period.
