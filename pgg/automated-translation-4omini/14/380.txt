理髮所投票 豫選發表

The barbershop voting results for the six cities publicized in the previous month's issue did not perform particularly well, possibly due to the vacation season. First, let us announce the numbers that have come in by July 25.

Seoul		Central (종로2) 115 votes   Dunan (종로3) 107 votes   Dongchang (안국동) 82 votes   Gwangcheon (서린동) 14 votes  
Pyongyang 	Toiyang (대동문통) 17 votes   Ilmi (설암리) 11 votes   Gicheng (磚九里) 6 votes  
Kaesong		Gaodeng (북본정) 17 votes   Godu (남문통) 37 votes   Songgak (북본정) 9 votes   Kaesong (북본정) 7 votes  
Namgung (대화정) 7 votes  
Wonsan		Rodong (석우동) 33 votes   Central (북촌동) 33 votes   Miwa (상 동) 7 votes  
Daegu		Invalid Votes 3 sheets  
Incheon		Joseon (寺町) 5 votes  

The final deadline for submissions is September 20, so please cut out the voting paper located on page 32 of this issue and cast your votes actively.  

You may also vote for any barbershop that is not yet listed among the above shops. Voting for barbershops outside of Seoul, Pyongyang, Kaesong, Wonsan, Daegu, and Incheon will be considered invalid (including those operated by Korean people).  

Prize Regulations

For the aforementioned six cities, prizes will be designated from first to seventh place (42 shops in total). Winning barbershops will be featured in an article with photographs. 

Voters will have their votes collected separately by city, and prizes will be drawn randomly: one person for first place (6 total), one person for second place (totaling 6), and ten people for third place (totaling 60). 

First place will receive one high-quality shaving blade (plus two other prizes).  
Second place will receive high-quality hair oil and dandruff treatment (and additional prizes).  
Third place will receive special prizes from our company. <163>

<162-163>  

**Summary**  

This article announces the voting results from barbershops across six cities in Korea and encourages participation through a prize incentive. The notes on the voting process highlight the limited options available for where Koreans could vote, and the invalidation of votes from certain non-selected shops reflects the colonial structure's influence over local businesses. Historians may find interest in this as a representation of consumer culture and the everyday life of Koreans under Japanese rule, as well as the role of local businesses in colonial society.
