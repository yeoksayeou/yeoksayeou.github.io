잡지명	별건곤  
발행일	1930년 11월 01일  
기사제목	스위트 호-ㅁ 李光洙氏 家庭訪問記 (Sweet Home - A Visit to the Family of Lee Kwang-soo)  
필자	金昭姐 (Kim So-ja)  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

On a clear autumn morning of the first autumn day with crisp air, the reporter set out to visit Lee Kwang-soo's house from the Changgyongwon tram stop. With the stone wall on the left, I climbed up the steep Parkseok hill. The lovely scenery of Chungsam-dong becomes visible.

Passing a few houses where cultural residences are rarely found, one comes across a red-roofed traditional Korean house built by Joseon. After going through the front gate and the middle gate, to the right are the main room, a big hall, and a guest room. Through the glass window of the hall, a piano, gramophone, and various books on literature and medicine are visible on the shelf.

As the editor explained the purpose of the visit, Lee Kwang-soo's wife, Heo Yeong-suk, who was busy making clothes for their second son’s first birthday the next day, remarked, "Our family needs to have some interesting stories to write a visit report. If there are plenty of interesting materials, I can talk more, so please feel free to ask any questions."

The dialogue then commenced in a question-answer format. 

“How is Mr. Spring Garden’s health these days?” 

“He recently caught a cold after giving a lecture at a Shigol. He is currently lying in that room. Even a person who is normally healthy gets fatigued after more than an hour of speaking, and he is not an exception. Furthermore, too much speaking can affect his lungs. So, when he left, I specially requested him to avoid lecturing for over 20 minutes, and we firmly promised each other. Yet somehow, when it came down to it, he couldn’t keep it to just 20 minutes. He ended up giving a three-hour lecture with intervals of one hour each, and he caught a cold afterwards.”

“How is his daily life?”

“Every morning, he gets up at six and writes manuscripts until about eight. During this time, everyone in the house must be very quiet and use the back door, so only the sound of my footsteps on the floor is heard. Of course, until eight, no one is allowed to open my room’s door. After eight, he starts preparing for the day. Then, since he leaves the house at eight-thirty to go to the newspaper office by nine, he returns home around six in the evening. By nine-thirty in the evening, he must go to bed. Therefore, we refuse visits from any guests after six in the evening. He works for nine hours from nine in the morning until six. If he works beyond that, it can be detrimental to his health. Initially, it was hard to refuse unexpected guests. If the children or housemaids ask him to speak, they might not know the circumstances and could feel hurt, so during such times, I come out to explain things clearly. Therefore, if anyone needs to visit, they can come to the newspaper office between nine in the morning and six in the evening. Of course, he cannot attend dinners or gatherings in the evening. If someone invites him, he may attend for a bit before six. We primarily focus on cooking at home, of course, as he has health issues. He is sensitive to food matters as he prefers vegetables over meat, so it can be tough to make delicious dishes with just vegetables. Perhaps because he stayed in Japan for a long time when he was younger, he enjoys Japanese dishes. This is why we often use fish. However, I do know how to prepare simple Japanese dishes like teriyaki. Spicy foods are absolutely out of the question at home because of his health issues. For example, with kimchi, I make sure to soak it in water to remove the spiciness. Although my wife says it’s good for health, it can smell pungent and is sometimes hard to have company, so I strictly avoid it.”

“Does the family engage in any enjoyable recreational activities together?” 

“Not anything specifically planned. However, I do have two days off a month from the newspaper, and on those days, our family of four takes the children out for fun. Last time, we went to Oryudong.”

“How do you spend your afternoons?” 

“I mostly attend to the children. I give them milk three times a day and help dress them. The older child naps for one hour while the younger for two hours. It’s ideal to have both children nap at the same time because it keeps the house quiet for over an hour. During this time, I read magazines or novels that I enjoy. When someone comes to visit during this time, it surprises me a lot. I end up feeling like I wasted my day. Regarding medical books, I cannot read during these hours.”

“Does Mr. Spring Garden interfere with your domestic matters or your work?” 

“Not at all. I don't really understand about housework either. Regarding myself, I don't have much interference. However, he doesn't like me going out much. Therefore, unless there’s something special, I don’t go out at all.”

“Do you ever argue?” 

“Of course, we do. He is quite gentle, but he does have a temper sometimes. Although he rarely loses his temper, when he does, he can be quite rough. When we argue, it’s usually about the children’s health. He thinks he does his best in raising them, and thus, he can be critical if something goes wrong. He believes he is the best at it, which can be concerning. Moreover, because I’m a doctor, I often prescribe the medicine we use. He doesn’t trust it and prefers to buy medicine from other hospitals. Then I complain about that. Recently, both children had whooping cough, and we have been arguing daily. Whooping cough means coughing for one hundred days, and I have been prescribed medication that seems ineffective and he insists on getting Chinese medicine. I thought it would be a disaster to have two children in the house, so I ran away with them. But we ended up coming back. Thus, for two months, we just argued every day. In general, I do have some interference in household matters. There are some nights I may go out. He might have some female company because he sees that I am out as well. But arguments are primarily due to the health of the children. Recently, I have also been less involved. When he went to give a lecture, I insisted he only promised to talk for twenty minutes for his health, but when he returned, he had lectured for three hours. That made me slightly upset. When he returned with a cold, I wondered if he had taken any medicine bought from Pyongyang. He was then advised to see a doctor, but he hasn’t sent anyone yet, so he is just lying down.”

This absent-minded gentleman, Lee Kwang-soo, constantly worries, resembling a child, as he narrates his experiences.

“What kind of precautions do you take with the children?” 

“First and foremost, I focus on their health. I usually do not let them eat meat indiscriminately. If I haven't allowed it, they cannot eat anything. Even when they go to the neighbor's house, they cannot eat a grain of rice without my knowledge. They would either bring it back or send someone to let me know ‘Mom said to eat this.’ Therefore, during this summer, when we went to Seongwangsa temple, where ice cream is quite a rarity, my child stood still there. The store owner’s friend bought it for the child, spending 25 jeon on it. But the child refused, saying, ‘I can't, my mom said I shouldn’t eat ice cream.’ That became quite the anecdote. As of this year, we don’t have any money. Even if we give them money, they don’t know how to use it. Since they are children, when they get money, I worry they will squander it on snacks unnecessarily.”

  
**Summary**  

This article provides a glimpse into the domestic life of Lee Kwang-soo, revealing his disciplined routines, family interactions, and his wife’s deep concern for their children’s health and the balance of work and home life amidst the societal pressures of the colonial period. Historians might find the tension between personal and public life, especially the ways in which Lee's health conditions influenced his family life, particularly significant in understanding the broader context of health and societal roles during the Japanese occupation of Korea.
