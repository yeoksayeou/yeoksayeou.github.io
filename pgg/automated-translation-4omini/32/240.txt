Record of Same-Sex Love by Female Celebrities  
Hwang Sin-deok  
Few people have not experienced same-sex love during their school days. I wouldn't say that I didn't have such experiences, but I mostly made friends along the conventional path, so I didn't have any particularly troubling incidents. Looking back, there are indeed some unfortunate moments. The first experience I remember was during my time at Sŏngŭi School, when I became very close to a classmate who had come from Taichŏn. This classmate, being an orphan, was quite unfortunate, and I guess my initial affection stemmed from pity. When there was a delicious meal at home, I would always bring that classmate from the dormitory to eat, feeling at ease only after sharing the meal, and thinking about seeing her face would make me blush each morning I went to school. Then, during the winter break, when my classmate had to go back home for two weeks, it was incredibly hard to endure being separated for that period. I don't know how I ended up crying at the station, holding the bag tightly, with passersby stopping to look. After my classmate left, I got deeply absorbed in my studies, but still ended up crying several times over her absence. This seems to be my first experience of same-sex love, I suppose. After that, I made many friends, but I never loved anyone purely as I did then.

Hŏ Yŏng-suk  
When I was around 14 or 15, I had many experiences of same-sex love while attending Ch'inmyŏng School. I think I did particularly well without falling behind! I spent a lot of time with Kim Kyŏng-hŭi, who is now the wife of a teacher at Ch'ungang High School, while she was at Paehwa Girls' High School. She was in the dormitory, and my house was right nearby. Our schools were different, so we only got to meet once a week at the chapel. Those seven days felt endlessly long. When we finally met, I was so glad that I hugged her without knowing what I was saying. And when we had to part, I always gave her at least a handkerchief to hold onto.

During the revival meetings held at Ch'ŏndong Chapel, the students from Paehwa School would go to the chapel every day. At that time, I was hiding behind my house to watch when my beloved Kim would pass by, and when she did, I would rush out the door, hoping she could slip away from the teachers' gaze. 

There was another person who was an upperclassman at the same Ch'inmyŏng School, now known as Bae Yŏng-sun, residing in Sinŭiju. I was cherished by her. She was in the dormitory while I commuted to school, and I begged my parents to let me stay in the dormitory for a while because it was too heartbreaking to be away from her. So, at home, they couldn't particularly deny my request. Whenever I got permission to stay in the dormitory, I would make excuses to explain my sore legs. Even after much pleading, the approval took about two months. Then I heard that my beloved sister was in love with someone else. I was extremely upset, and one day, when she was leaving for her hometown, I clung to her and cried, saying I would die if she didn’t refuse that man. It seems like I was quite passionate at that time. When my classmate was about to leave, I felt incredibly sad and cried a lot. Later on, as I grew up, my rationality increased, and it became harder to maintain such feelings. 

Lyu O-jun  
How ridiculous! Asking someone nearly eighty years old to talk about same-sex love? Nevertheless, it is indeed a very fascinating topic. Most female students probably have had such experiences. How could I have not? It’s been a long time, but the memories emerge vividly in my mind as if they happened yesterday. Ha ha... I was originally more rational than emotional since my early years, so during my student days, I couldn't say that I loved anyone or experienced love from others either. When I first came to Seoul to attend Chŏngsin Girls' School, at that time, being a girl of around 15 or 16, even going to Japan or the West seemed perfectly normal. However, back then, moving from P’yŏngyang to Seoul felt quite far, and coupled with the mountains and rivers looking gloomy and the strict dormitory life, I struggled to cope, missing my parents and friends from the countryside terribly. As a result, after finishing my studies every day, I could only go back to my room and cry. I even cried at the full moon nights, and just seeing flowers in the yard made me weep. One night, I had cried so much that my bedding was completely soaked, and my eyes were swollen.

It turned out that Students started calling me by various nicknames because of my tears. So much so that I cried every day that the dorm supervisor comforted me with words such as everyone goes through this for the first two months. During that time, my innocent young self believed her words blindly, waiting for those two months to pass, thinking that once they did, I wouldn’t miss home anymore. I expected enduring two months would be like a prisoner awaiting their release or an old maid waiting for her wedding day. Looking back, it seems silly. Not knowing that a month or two would pass just like that, I believed completely that it would happen as the supervisor said. The only comfort I had was counting the days until those two months ended. Eventually, those two months passed, and it was just three days before I would see my family again. Delighted, I told the supervisor that according to what she said, it had been almost two months since I arrived, and there were only three days left. Why did I still feel like crying about home? She then scolded me, saying, "You ugly girl! Why are you crying, thinking about home for two months, when you are just a countryside bumpkin studying here? If you are going to do that, just quit studying and go back home!" I had nothing to say and just cried more. I didn't eat dinner that night and cried all night.

Such rumors spread throughout the dorm, and it became known to everyone. At that time, in the room next door was P, an upperclassman, who happened to be the sister of Dr. P, the head of a clinic in Rakwon-dong. Seeing my situation, she felt such pity that she intentionally came to comfort me with various words and would invite me to her room to hang out. I was so grateful to her, having nowhere else to lean my heart. She treated me like a sister, and I felt a deep affection for her as if she were an older brother. If I was ill, she would make me herbal medicine, and if I got hungry, she'd buy me snacks or fruits, and sometimes she gave me handkerchiefs or writing supplies. And whenever I received a gift from my hometown, even if I couldn't eat it, I made sure to give something to her. This could be termed as what one might call same-sex love today. However, I didn't really love her but felt quite afraid of her. Her face and figure were not at all ugly, but for reasons beyond that, I was terrified of her hands. Whether at night or during the day, just seeing her hands made me feel sick and terrified. The thought of her hands would quake me as if a huge snake were coming to me. Even now, when I think of that classmate, the first thing that comes to my mind is those hands of hers. Those hands were scarier than the claws of bad ghosts in films. Were they my partner or a source of fear? 

Lee Dŏk-yŏ  
Ha ha ha! Are you asking about my experience with same-sex love? It was indeed quite complicated and full of issues. It was over ten years ago, and although it now feels vague, back then, my passionate love was more intense than some conventional heterosexual romances and had its fair share of dramas. Although I loved various people, the most complicated affairs occurred during my studies in Tokyo, particularly with a classmate named Yi O-k'yŏng and a Japanese female student known as Ozi. Speaking of my love for Yi O-k'yŏng could easily fill a long novel. Originally, my feelings for her began at a certain high school for girls in Kyŏngsŏng, where we shared the same dormitory and bonded over our shared thoughts, leading to a deep mutual affection. 

At that time, I loved her so much, wrapped up in feelings instead of rational thoughts. Once we became close, she devoted herself entirely to expressing her love for me. Wherever we went, we went together, and at night, we slept under the same blanket, sharing everything, both body and spirit. She even declared to her parents that she wouldn't marry any man but would live her life with me. However, after graduating, I went to study in Tokyo while she began teaching at a school in her hometown in Gyeongnam, which created a heartbreaking separation for us. We expressed deep sorrow for each other, and I rarely let myself dwell on it as much as I wanted. 

Once, while vacationing during the summer back home, I stopped by her home in Suwŏn, feeling as if I were reuniting with a dead person. Our love that summer was even more intense than during our school days; I put aside all my travel plans to stay with her and spent the summer happily at her house.

As autumn came, I returned to Tokyo, and she couldn’t bear being apart from me. She vowed to die if I didn’t take her with, and I, being drawn by my feelings and unable to refuse her, managed to arrange some funds and brought her to Tokyo with me. We lived in the same dormitory, and although everything was moving along smoothly, as our social circle expanded, so did the love triangles and jealousy among us.

In the dormitory in Tokyo, several other female students also expressed affection. H and K were particularly notable. H had become quite an expert at same-sex love and maintained a passionate love affair with P, while also developing feelings for me. K was similar and showed interest in me as well, so our dorm room became a theater for triangular or quadrangular romance. When it came to sleeping arrangements, Yi would stay close to me, watching over me as I might accidentally caress or touch someone else. P was equally wary of H, while K found herself unable to navigate the dynamics, completely unable to sleep due to stress. 

This messy situation took a toll on our studies and physical health considering all the emotional turmoil involved. Each winter spent there left my hands numb from the cold, yet Yi remained impressed and reassured by my distress due to jealousy. Unfortunately, rather than dying down, our troubles persisted, leading me to eventually move out to another boarding house. 

Yi expressed her displeasure toward me, blaming me for being unfaithful, which understandably led her to harbor intense animosity and resentment. She indulged in shouting and cursing, even going as far as displaying my picture with derogatory remarks penned on it. Occasionally she would hold a knife intending to harm herself and me both, yet I managed to remain calm, even as she eventually cooled down and sought reconciliation, asking to live together again in the same house. Looking back, those memories feel like a dream, both amusing and nostalgic. There were many more turbulent experiences afterward, but I'll stop my account here.  

**Summary**

This article reflects on the memories of same-sex love shared by women during their school days in Korea in the early 20th century. The narrators recount their friendships and emotional attachments, revealing how societal norms and biases shaped their experiences. These recollections provide insight into the complexities of female relationships during colonial rule in Korea, illustrating how these connections often blended love, competition, and emotional distress. Historians may find the expressions of longing and the challenges of same-sex love particularly interesting, as they reflect both the constraints of the era and the emotional depth of women's lives at that time.
