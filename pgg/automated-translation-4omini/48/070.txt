기사제목	소대가리 京城 싀골학생이 처음 본 서울, 在京初日記 (Seoul Observations of a Country Student)  
필자	態超  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Other)  

A country student, having just completed elementary school with the lofty ambition of enrolling in middle school, was in Seoul for about a month. In the year since arriving, I have become somewhat familiar with the customs of Seoul. It is not just because I have shed my rural roughness; it may be that the incoming country students are lacking or perhaps because now dressed in a shiny, newly acquired county-style overcoat, wearing my elementary school hat, and tugging at a pair of muddied rubber shoes along the bustling streets of Jongno, I recall my thoughts from the same time last year, feeling a mix of sentiments. 

"I remember feeling like that!" 

After a whole day of travel by train, exhausted in my lodgings, I lay down immediately and woke up early the next morning, possibly due to the unfamiliar room. With a cigarette in my mouth, lost in thoughts, I realized that it seemed the others had already awakened. The sounds of the kitchen filled my ears; the noises of washing rice, rinsing water, and utensils made it lively. 

“Oh dear, there’s no water—Mrs. get the water!” A voice from the laundry. “Mrs.”, I pondered—what could that mean? If that’s how a daughter would call her mother, isn’t “get the water” odd? And what could “water in a box” refer to? They say that people in Seoul live lavishly but keep their water in boxes with locks inside the room? Surely not! 

As I peeked through the door, I saw the laundress picking something up and taking it out with a water container. 

"Wow! People in Seoul buy their water. What a strange place! Even if I had family in Seoul, I would have gotten it wrong thinking I would eat rice!" 

When the laundress came in with the water container, I hesitated and asked, "How much is that water?" 

"Excuse me?" 

"I am asking the price of that water." 

"Yes—yes, how much do you want to buy it for? This would be about two hundred won—oh! In the countryside, we don't buy water!" 

That’s quite expensive. If my family lived near Seoul, even digging a well would make us quite wealthy. 

The owner's voice came again. "Take ten won, buy one piece of meat, and later bring back some bean sprouts and onion for a penny." The money is ten won, but what is this purchase? If one is buying meat and bean sprouts and also onion for only one won left over, calculations using addition and subtraction reveal that the meat costs five won, and the tofu two won. They even have beef, not just fish, for five won! Seoul is a remarkable place! But cows come from the countryside, and the price of meat is somewhat high, so if my whole family were to eat five won's worth of meat, we might just be smelling the scent to taste. 

Ah! What’s that shouting from outside the window? 

"Milk!" 

"Ice cream!" 

Upon waking up, I thought that something was happening, and eagerly I opened the window to see vendors passing by yelling catchy phrases, which I eventually recognized as the cries for "milk" and "upturned ice." 

In early in my stay in Seoul, I learned two new pieces of information and two characters.

After folding my blanket, I went out to the yard, splashing my face with water while warm water or a towel was not ready. 

The breakfast table was set. The white rice was not even half as good as the barley rice I had eaten before. On the side were at least ten dishes of side dishes set on the table. 

In a bowl floating with oil there was kidney bean sprout soup, a serving of meat and tofu stew, kimchi, diced radish, bean sprout mixed with dried seaweed, and a side of shrimp paste, as well as a small bowl of soy sauce—eight kinds altogether. The taste was not outstanding, being mild and somewhat hot, but it was fragrant and refreshing. A breakfast of one won and ten jun might be a pretty extravagant meal; only a person from Seoul would eat like this. 

After scraping the bottom of the bowls, I felt like, if I had been eating only this for breakfast, I could not afford to get any traditional supplements. 

The table was almost cleared away when the hostess arrived, opening the door to the living room. She smiled broadly and said, “You must be exhausted. Although the side dishes are unsatisfactory, do eat well, as a young gentleman who grew up in the countryside must find less comfort in Seoul than in his home here. When evening comes, we will grill some kimchi and bake some beef as well.” 

She started off with small talk, asking where I was from, who my parents were, which school I came from, and while answering she would interject with “Hello! I’m sorry to bring such talk on your first morning here.” 

Steering back, she said, “But it must be hard for country folks. Seoul is not a free place; in this harsh world, if you blink, everything can be lost. I hope that when you stay as a guest in my home, you understand that I mean no harm by asking you to prepare your monthly fees in advance so that I can prepare rice and side dishes.” 

With a twinkle in her eye, she seemed to be trying to get more money from me at once. 

“Oh no! Can it be?” I thought, 

I reached into my pocket to feel my money and pulled out a ten won bill, paying her immediately. 

The hostess appeared flustered but accepted it. 

“I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to ask for immediate payment.” 

Finally, she shifted topics, warming up to a different kind of discussion, and affirmed me. "You see, you appear well-mannered. I hope I’d have a son just like you." 

As she played off my naivety, she went inside. 

The more I pondered, the more unsettled I felt. Even though I had barely eaten anything, I could endure, while my thought process revolved around how it was a natural expectation to pay for meals. The hostess’s behavior, casual and amiable towards me, allowed me to recognize the cleverness and articulate nature of the people from Seoul. 

I stepped out to the main street. 

At a crosswalk, among the coming and going cars, streetcars, and pedestrians, everything sparkled like lightning, busy and bright. I later discovered that the asphalt street was quite smooth. Bending down to rub my hands on the surface, they picked up some fine dust and dirt. I thought it was somewhat dusty. The ground was shining brightly, smoothed by thousands of feet and countless vehicles. 

Across from me stood a grand department store. As I decided to take a look around, I realized the process of crossing the street was daunting, filled with streetcars, automobiles, bicycles, and more. Cautiously observing for about two to three minutes, I gathered enough courage and dashed across. After crossing, I felt a slight flush from the many eyes. 

Inside the department store, I hesitated for a long time to step in, but once inside, everything was intriguing yet overwhelming, making my head spin. The store was brimming with items that the ten won bill in my pocket felt heavy. 

Young boys dressed all fashionably in stylish Western suits were standing casually together, causing me to worry about the results of my exams. 

Ten won was the uniform price. Since I was here already, I might as well buy something. First, I sought for a face towel, a toothbrush, and a bar of soap that cost one won. 

As I took these items under my arm, it felt somewhat fulfilling to be seen shopping for basic necessities, despite not knowing how to behave properly in the store. 

The restaurant inside served food, though I had little knowledge of it but dared not enter. 

Seeing the elevator, I thought I should try it. I squeezed through the crowd to hop on but nearly shouted, "Please, take a ticket!" out of fear that I would be inspected. 

Going down, I felt dizzy as I descended down several floors, moving quickly. Standing terminably, I fell out of the elevator after reaching the street level—not realizing I had not paid. I noticed that others did not pay, either. Remarkably, if such amenities were near the lodging, visiting for leisure could be nice. 

After leaving the department store, I considered taking a streetcar on the way back. Last night, I had the chance to ride due to my friend, but today I thought to do it alone. Five cents for one section. 

I got on the streetcar and punched my ticket. After getting off at the next station, I turned back, reflecting on how terribly expensive the fare compared to the train. (In the end, I realized I endured a large loss.) 

Strolling back towards my lodging, I rummaged through my purchases brought from the department store. All I had for lunch was yet another meal heated bob s are just serving me. 

The hostess called from inside the kitchen, “Grandma, go down to the bank and have this money on hand.” 

The bank? Exchange money? I wondered what they might be fated to have returned later. The grandma was taking the meal tray. 

“Excuse me, what is a bank?” I chuckled softly. 

“They’re used for clothes, blankets, and any possessions you can pledge for cash. Later, you are repaid in small amounts and will pay interest,” she explained softly. 

“So what about exchanging?” 

“Exchanging means if after four months you don’t reclaim your item, then it belongs to the bank. Thus, you need to pay up the interest before the set time limit to maintain possession.” 

From that, it seemed convenient. There are indeed splendid systems to manage in Seoul! A bank! It’s even more suitable than both a post office and cigarette monopoly combined. 

After lunch, I took a stroll near the lodging. I caught a whiff of something troubling, guiding me away from a stall with a sign that read “Seolleongtang.” What could the famed Seolleongtang specialty involve? Outside, steam clouded and a head of a cow was boiling in a cauldron. 

Unceremoniously were the skulls of cows that had already died, upon having sacrificed themselves ripped apart. Nearby sat some well-dressed men, engaging in their soup while murmuring, “Ah, now I feel satisfied!” 

How could cow soup be savored this way? Everything in Seoul seemed wonderful, but Seolleongtang exuded a certain repugnance. 

Without meaning to, I repeated, “The cow skulls of Seoul!” 

(However, later I thoroughly retracted that statement.)  

**Summary**  

The article recounts the experiences of a country student visiting Seoul for the first time, highlighting the contrasts between rural and urban living conditions. As he navigates the bustling city, he experiences both wonder and discomfort, from learning the cost of daily items to the etiquette expected in urban settings. The student's observations reflect not only on socio-economic disparities but also on the intricacies of urban culture during a time of Japanese colonization. Historians may find this text revealing in its portrayal of early 20th-century urban life in Korea and the cultural adjustments faced by those migrating from rural areas.

고루대하에, 오고가는 자동차며 전차, 길 지나는 행인, 모든 것이 번개ㅅ불가티 눈부스고 시게 속가티 복잡하다.
아스팔트 길바닥(후에 안 일홈이지만) 허리를 굽혀 손끗으로 싹-문지러보니 손끄테는 콩가루가튼 보드라운 흙이 약간 무들뿐 길바닥은 손끗다은 자리가 가무시름하게 속살이 내다보인다. 수십만의 사람과 수만의 거마가 번거러웁게 래왕하는 바람에 길이 나서 반들반들해진 줄아럿더니 이제보니 무엇을 깐 것이 확실하다. 잔듸풀 뗏장뜨더시 슬며시 몃장 떠가지고 걸레질 고이하야 방에 까랏스면 만년장판지로 십상 조케 생겻다.
길건너 백화점이라 써부친 집이 크기도 하다. 구경을 좀 하자니까 전차 자동차 자전거등살에 길 건느기가 큰일이다. 좌우를 둘러보며 2,3분동안 겨누다가 와락 다름질 처서 길을 건넛다. 건너노코 생각하니 열적기란 짝이 업고 행인의 시선 때문에 얼골이 확 단다.
백화점이다. 드러가도 조흔지 안조흔지 한참 망설거렷다. 산가치 싸인 물건이 요지경 속 가티 조키도 하지만은 고루고루 보자니 눈이 뻑뻑해진다. 족기 안 봉창에든 10원짜리가 공연히 들먹어린다. 인물곱고 맵시조코 고은 옷입은 녀점원들 보고 천만 뜻박게 우연히 나는 생각-
「후일 학업을 다 마치고 장가들 때는 반드시 서울 색시를 어드리라」어려서 배운 하늘천따지는 니저도 이 생각만은 안니즐 것이다.
삼삼오오 고우라 양복의 중학생들 번떼 잇는 것을 보니 입학 시험에 합격여부가 공연히 마음에 걸려 풀이 죽어진다.
10전 균일 싸기도 하다. 이왕이면 나도 좀 사자. 우선, 세수수건 치분 치솔 왜 비누갑 일원 한 장으로 거실러 밧고 녀점원이 고히 싸주는 뭉텡이를 겨드랑에 끼니 구경하기에 항결 떳떳하다.
식당 압헤 느려노은 음식 먹고 십기는 하나 일홈도 모르거니와 첫재 갑슬 몰라 겁이 나서 못드러가 먹겟다.
남타는 승강기니 나도 한번 타볼까 사람들을 비집고 드러가서 「표찍으시요」소리가 날까바 미리부터 족기 봉창에 ＜19＞ 손을 너코 구녁 뚜러진 돈 한푼을 꼭 쥐엇다. 땅속으로 몃만길이나 떠러지는지 현기ㅅ증이 난다. 급행차가 시골정거장 지나드시 4층 3층 2층 그다음에 가서 승강기는 딱 슨다. 엉겁길에 튀여나와 돈도 안내엿다. 나만 공ㅅ자인가보다. 아니다. 남들도 돈을 안낸다. 하숙 근처에 이런 것이 잇다면 심심한 때 오락삼아 타볼만하다. 백화점을 나섯다. 귀로에는 전차를 타볼까. 어제밤에는 친구덕에 공차를 탓스나 오늘은 나혼자 한번 타자. 한구역에 5전. 전차에 올라 표를 찍고 다움 정류장에 가 나렷다. 타고온 길을 되도라다 보니 업허지면 코다을 데가 기차삭보다도 더 비싸다.(나종에야 큰 손해당한 것을 알엇지만) 터덜터덜 거러서 하숙으로 도라왓다. 백화점에서 사온 물건을 펴노코 뒤적뒤적 하는데 점심이 나온다. 제사집 철상이 아닐터인데 더운 물에 밥을 마라내왓다.
반찬도 아침에 먹다 남은 놈 단 한가지 비웃토막 하나가 이채를 띄우고 잇다.
안에서 주인마누라의 목소리가 들린다.
『할멈 저 웃 전당국에 가서 이것 개표해가지고 오』
전당국? 개표? 저녁에 해즐 반찬거리나 아닌가. 할멈이 밥상을 가질러 왓다.
『여보소 전당국이 머시요』씽긋 웃는 할멈 나직한 목소리로

『개표는』
『개표는 그 물건을 넉달이 지나도록 찻지 안으면 그 물건은 떠내러가거든요 뺏긴단 말이요 그러니까 리자만 우선에 몃달치 내고 그만치 연기를 하는 거라우』
딴은 그럴듯하다. 무엇이고 편리한 제도를 가진 서울이다. 전당국! 우편국 전매국보다 얼마나 그 일홈이 향그러우냐?
점심 후에 하숙 골목근처를 산보하엿다. 킈킈- 한 누린내를 조차 발길을 옴겨가보니 간판에는 「설넝탕」이라 씨워잇다. 서울 명물 설넝탕이 어떠한 것인가 하고 드려다보니 콩멍석만한 김서리는 가마속에 소대가리가 푹 소사잇다. 그 엽헤는 죽어서도 악착한 희생을 당하엿다는 드시 소 해골박아지 서너개가 간주런이 노여잇다. 그리고 또 한편에는 신사양반들이 모지라진 숫가락으로 뚝백이 바닥을 다그락 다그락 긁으면서 국물을 훌훌 마시며 하는 말슴
『어- 이제 속이 풀리는군!』
소대가리 삶은 물 먹어 저러케도 조흘가. 서울의 모든 것이 다 조흐나 설넝탕만은 악인상을 준다. 무심길에 외처지는 소리.
