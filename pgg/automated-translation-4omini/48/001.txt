Editorial - Spring Does Not Feel Like Spring  

As we pass over the boar ridge,  

March, the season of spring, is generally considered the most wonderful time of the year. With flowers blooming, birds singing, and all things awakening, spring indeed seems to be a pleasant season for human beings. However, despite the arrival of this delightful spring, there are those who cannot recognize its charm and instead spend their days in tears. Who are these people? To illustrate, I would like to introduce a line from a famous Chinese poem, the lament of a silkworm's mother: 

“Just returned from the city, tears fill my eyes. Others wear silk, but I, a silkworm's mother, have nothing.”

This reflects the plight of farmers during spring starvation, and it articulates the sorrow of those who toil yet reap little benefit. In poetic terms, it expresses that a silkworm's caretaker should be able to wear silk, but in reality, those who labored to raise silkworms find themselves unable to wear the fine clothes made from their labor. Meanwhile, the urban residents, who are oblivious to such hardships, enjoy the luxuries of silk—how tragic this contrast is! 

The expression “Spring Does Not Feel Like Spring” should not be regarded as something unique to Wang Zhaojun (王昭君) but rather applies appropriately to the farmers. Is it the absence of work or is it due to some unchangeable fate that makes spring feel unwelcoming? Although there is a spring, the conditions under which farmers live create a feeling of dread in the face of its return. This phenomenon reflects their reality during the spring famine. 

After a year of cultivating the land, half of the yield goes to landlords (地主), and the rest is burdened by debts (債主) and taxes (稅金). After settling various expenses, the farmers are left with barely enough to sustain their families. With an old wife wearing a tattered apron and children crying in hunger, the thought of tomorrow’s difficulties fills them with despair that is more bitter than any fate they could have imagined. Are these fates preordained by destiny? 

This reminds me of the lines in a poem about a forsaken child, expressing the torment of a parent faced with hunger: “To abandon this child or not, if I do not abandon this body, I shall die of hunger.” 

The cries of a cuckoo symbolize how scarce food has become, leading to the heartbreaking choice of abandoning a beloved child for survival. Just as the old adage reflects, not consuming the cuckoo brings regret, so too does scarcity highlight the harsh truth of existence: without money, there is no rice; without rice, there is hunger. This cycle of poverty forces even the most loving parents to consider the unthinkable—abandoning their children in desperation. 

Indeed, spring returns yet again and the cuckoo will sing. But how many impoverished farmers will hear that song without it bringing sorrow instead of joy? Those who are oblivious to the hardships of the farmers cannot comprehend their pain. The question remains: how can we help these suffering farmers? 

While speaking of desires and hopes, landlords fatten themselves, creditors seize resources, and the wealthy indulge in debauchery and extravagance. These injustices emerge from an economy that is far from balanced, but lamenting will achieve nothing. 

Ultimately, the most feasible solution appears to be one where farmers take initiative for their own salvation. Through sheer grit and determination, they must collectively awaken to their circumstances and strive for their own self-reliance, thereby paving the way for improvement in the future. Such a collective awakening among farmers will lead to a better outcome than any temporary assistance that may come from outside.  

**Summary**  

This article reflects on the disparity experienced by rural farmers during the spring season, traditionally associated with vibrancy and hope. While nature comes alive, many farmers struggle with severe poverty, burdened by landlords, debts, and taxes. The piece poignantly draws parallels between the beauty of spring and the hardship faced by those dependent on agriculture, emphasizing their desperate situation. Historians may find interest in the article's literary deployment of classical poetry and metaphors to articulate the emotional landscape of Korean farmers in the colonial era, shedding light on the socio-economic challenges of the time.
