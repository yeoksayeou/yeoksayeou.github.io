Spring Color Over Three Thousand Miles

The spring of the South  
By: Hak San

Spring, indeed! For the bustling, delicate, and tumultuous lives that have flourished chaotically, stories about spring often take on an especially sentimental tone. It is a dream that cannot be captured with pictures or sounds. However, if I were to forcibly describe the spring of the South, and assuming there are those who yearn for the South's spring, I would suggest the following route.

The spring of the South is filled with water; that is, the spring of the South fills the rivers and also pours into the sea. From Gongju in Chungcheongnam-do, well-known for its historical sites, starting from the state-protected pavilion exploring the legendary twin-fried color, and floating a small wooden boat on the Geumgang River, you may see. Crossing the sandy shores, washing through the trees and rocks along the banks, as you pass through Ungjin and reach Buyeo by the Baengmang River, behold the flourishing green leaves of the famous Mt. Busosan and the ancient structures of the Banwol Fortress, the red-twigged pines and the cliffs of Nakhoam, encased in beautiful waves. If one does not know how to drink or sing here, they are certainly devoid of spirit.

Passing through Yeobawi and passing through the streets of Gangjeong, gazing at the pine forests of Unsan, and going down the steep cliffs of Seocheon, we arrive at the end of the Geumgang River – Gunsan Port. However, one cannot just pass through this route, as during this brief period, only here can the songs of fishermen echo out at night, entreating them with tales of the spring and a rare catch of sweetfish. During this season, even the quiet borders of a solitary town experience a burst of vitality, with romance that evokes echoes of a pure bygone era.

Departing from Gunsan, we truly turn towards the endless sea, sailing gracefully around the bends of Mugpo Port, admiring the waves of Byeongpajeong while recalling the historic achievements of General Lee Sun-sin, the islands twisting and turning in the south sea under the soft kiss of the spring sunlit waves. If possible, I shall go to the alluring island of Jeju, where I can take in the fragrant scent of yuzu. As I return, taking in the spring colors of Tongyeong and Masan will also be a pleasant affair. However, all of this is merely a dream. It is something to be experienced just in dreams.

Spring comes every year, yet every spring transforms. The beauty of spring on this land changes annually. Each spring brings new sounds of rain and whispers of spring. 

Oh! Will spring like this again return to our southern lands?

Spring Wind in Hwaseong  
By: Choe Yong-joo

I have gathered fragments of the news of spring from Hwaseong. However, how could this be just a singular scenery of Hwaseong? It represents the general panorama and melody shared across Korea. 

When flowering in the Poru Pavilion,  
The lady rejoices in the spring breeze with her basket.  

In Suwon (Hwaseong), too, the tranquil spring is upon us. If you proceed through the North Gate to Hwahongmun, the Poru Pavilion awaits. Although it is called Poru Pavilion, it ironically never saw much action nor did it face even a single cannonball in its commendable history. Now it is crumbling, with just one corner of the abode barely remaining, the chalky stone wall having been ravaged, leaving less than half. 

When spring arrives, dense with verdant blooms, early blooms sprout before any other. Already the cabbage fields are burgeoning, adorned by the ladies in their colorful skirts as they gather the fresh spring herbs. 

Once March has arrived, young men, having graduated from school, pass through here ceremoniously, and sometimes, the sweet songs of the ladies can be heard echoing over the hills. 

As the willow tree at Yongdu Pavilion embraces the rising waters,  
The elder sister of my family carries the laundry.  

Going just over ten miles from where Poru Pavilion presently lies is the now unrecognizable Yongdu Pavilion. Although this little place is absent of its former stature, even so, spring vibrates here. Nowadays, it is called Sagu (Four Seasons) flower, but the elegance that emanates from Yongdu Pavilion adorned with willows is even more graceful.

On warm days, the young ladies from the castle hide away and gather at this creek to do their laundry. It seems as though the sound of spring songs would emerge at any moment. However, the only sound is the rhythmless beat of laundry and the soft whispers of children playing, echoing this dreamlike scene.

At the site where Yongdu Pavilion once flourished is Banghwa Suryujeong (Visiting Blossoms and Willows Pavilion). While gazing upon the fair figure that resembles a cloud, it appears as though lovely, fairy-like flowers are poised to render a performance, as if spring music would soon resound.

Indeed, the spring of Suwon cannot be substituted for any other locale.

Below the willow trees, where the small creek flows, spring has waned with its little ponds that once were teeming with stone water chestnuts, now serves as a vivid memory of young sisters scurrying to collect them, a scene that stands as one complete painting.

As the forsythia bloom over the Udeokjin Rock,  
The spring waters reveal the stone water chestnuts.  

The spring waters descending down from the Udeokjin Rock constitute yet another splendid moment. This rock is located along the trail leading to Hwahongmun, down to Gwanggyosan (光故山) hill. Below the rock flows the Mangsocheon (Forgot River), and on the banks grow rich spring grasses, brightened in the light. Here the fair maidens chase butterflies alongside the gentle flowing waters, extending gracefulness momentarily.

It appears as if one could collect dreams and moments from the shrines of Shillukbong ( 時樓峰) against the brief splendor of spring while the lily-of-the-valley flowers perch on the spring meadows. The symphony reverberates beautifully above the old Hwahongmun, and under the green willows surrounding Suryujeong. The ten-mile long pine forest, enshrines the gentle sounds of labor from the wooden plows echoing from the large and wide field outskirts and shall not soon be forgotten amidst such gentle imagery.

As spring kicks off, the youth of Suwon naturally gravitate here. This serene place offers the chance for pondering dreams freely by this rock. The magnificent landscape is a true encounter with the warm spring's essence mingled with the tender nature's affection. For Suwon's youth, Udeokjin Rock becomes a cherished spot brimming with gentle promise.

When I go out from here, there exists Dongjangdae (East Victory Pavilion), whose crooked yellow earth path lies like an unfolding piece of parchment.

Birds chirp in the breezy woods of Dongjangdae,  
They dance their way back home.  

The spacious jade-filled fields aligned in succession form the dreams of youthful nostalgia. Here, there are also sightings of cranes floating overhead, making a rare spectacle. Moreover, as the evening sun sets behind the sloping hills, the lively voices of young men returning from the mountains create a hidden ethereal ambiance.

The spring of Suwon is full of painting and poetry alike. It, with its lingering earthliness, remains even more poignant. 

As haziness hovers over the willow-lined path,  
The sounds of children playing ease our breaths.  

The spring of Suwon is a tapestry of three connected entities. One part is centered around the Hwahongmun and Udeokjin Rock, another being focused on the West Lake, and the last, the encompassing Willow and Hwasan (Flower Mountain). 

The willow trees along Burdnae stretching south serve as a significant path. This road came into existence after the tomb of the tragic royal figure, King Jeongjo, was established at Hwasan. Thus the willows were planted along this road from then on. If one passes this way during the flowering time, the distant sounds of children's laughter rise harmoniously resonating from the cheerful willows, a crucial part of Suwon's spring tale.

At this juncture, proceeding further leads to Daewanggyo (Great King Bridge).  

At the stone arch bridge, when spring waters roll through,  
Persimmons bloom in full view.  

Hwasan is home to persimmons. Many types of persimmon blossoms flourish here. Though they chant about their halcyon desires, yet this place is also revered for its sheer reverie in great admiration of the tomb of the king and his son. 

As the blossoms dwindle, the fragrant lilies spread gently over the ground auspiciously thickening this area while spring approaches its end. 

Seohogun (West Lake) remains in the foreground, but should not be neglected. However, the refreshing sights of spring remain unblemished in our eyes; this painting, encircled with spring echoes, resonates through Hwaseong, and is indeed no less than an exhilarating experience.

The Old Beongmang Mountain's Spring - Snapshots of the Paupers  
By: Hong Seok-ju

Ah! The spring has come! Who on earth can acutely perceive that it is spring, and who would dare to sing it?

Even past the ruins of the crumbling fortress, where the old tombstones prevail, the spring has indeed made its arrival. 

A new morning dawns with the crowing of the rooster, tenderly nudging awake the farmers who are like scattered seeds across the land. Old men and grandmothers seem to emerge from their humble abodes, stretching like fighting kittens waking in the sun. The leeks that have grown in the cultivated fields are more vibrant than ever, basking under the radiant April sun and dancing gently in the breeze. 

The misty mountains of the capital gradually reveal their emerald shape, and the songs of spring are flung across the air, exuberantly filling the skies over the ancient Beongmang Mountain.

The fields of earth, riddled with the stark relics of the past, tell the tales of its former inhabitants. The remnants of long-gone neighbors fade after years of hardship. Sprouting among the crimson soil are vibrant patches of grass that send gentle invitations to idle survivors of the dreary winter, exposing their dormant hopes.

Spring has come! No, winter has departed! The chilling grips of a life-threatening winter have receded. That echoes through the voices of the aged, horrifically waiting in despair, and the anguish of stale winter loses its grip. With the arrival of spring comes, however faint it may be, the aliveness of joy, lifting the spirits, even in this forgotten hamlet. 

Young mothers hastily adjust their bloated bellies, raising their children as they hasten down to the village. Are they heading there to partake in a feast at a friend's birthday celebration? Or perhaps they are off, adorned in freshly washed garments, to offer their cozy wares for sale. Upon every twist of their muddled existence, spring has arrived, injecting them with unexpected vitality.

But here, too, the burden remains. The children pick through urban refuse, sifting through coal scraps to collect leftovers, their voices cracking through the air as they bang with cymbals made of soot, both desire for recognition and rejection. Amidst this, another larger dilemma arises in their lives that they may forget.

Faced with the impending threat of eviction from their homes, the people without refuge are still in worry. Where could they find sustenance? Where could they shelter?

Just the other day, under the shadow of the city stood countless skeletons—their skeletal remains lingering from bygone springs—now faced with unwelcome demolition and alterations. The joyous rebirth is racing towards a barren fate. What lies in waiting for tomorrow? Will vibrant homes fill this hill again? Ah! Spring has indeed arrived, but this is a spring still packed with yoke of despair.  

Spring Sounds of Gando  
By: Sucheongsan-in

As the weeping willows dance at Cheonan Samgyeori,  
And as the azaleas bloom at Yeongbyeon Yaksan,  
Spring has indeed meandered into Gangwon-do, with burgeoning willows and flowers. Flowers and willows are the flags of the spring country – wherever one goes, seeing flowers and willows reveals that it is spring.

However, in Gando, the arrival of spring is known more by the birds' songs than by flowers and willows. 

The scenery of Gando is lush with mountains and deep valleys, a natural tapestry teeming with the sounds of countless birds; every bird brings its varied melodies. To describe the types of birds and their songs one might almost find yourself repeating the jingles of southern Korean minstrel songs, and while I will refrain from such detailed categorization, it suffices to note that the most distinguished is the cuckoo.

Cuckoos can be found towering on any mountain in Gando. But the ones from Samhaksan in Chuncheon and the ones from Seoraksan in Inje boast their magnificent songs as representative of their mountains.

While poets from China sing the praises of the cuckoo in the western lands, poets from Yeongwol will recite verses about the cuckoo in their beloved lands as well. My heart, too, beats for the cuckoo of Yeongwol, as it has long been esteemed. 

As the day darkened upon the ascendance of King Danjong, he said aloud on a moonlit night, where the cuckoo sang,  
“When the cuckoo sings on nights of the bright moon, do not climb the stony hill of eternal sorrow, my dear cuckoo, for how could I fret if you did not regret the past?”

Thus do we find our spring songs filled with melancholy.

This song resonates beyond the heart; for in springtime, as blossoms awaken and during moonlit nights filled with historical resonance, should you hear Yeongwol’s cuckoo sing in the depths of the lush mountain valleys it would draw tears even from the onlookers.

This type of spring sound is exclusive to Gando, where nothing else can steep in. 

The sounds of singing during the process of moving timber also should not be overlooked. Given the elevated mountains and thinner flatlands, farming methods differ greatly from those in other areas, where agriculture might thrive, those in Gando harness the rhythm of nature. 

To labor during the farming seasons requires a different touch; while in other regions, a single horse would do, in Gando they employ two; but as their work grows heavy the songs take flight – with accompaniment of the draft horses’ pounding hooves. 

Those who have traveled in Gando know this well: as the skyline conquers the heavens, wisps of white-caped men toil over fields from dawn till dusk, accompanied by their lumbering teams.

As the paddlers divide the water smoothly, they sing:  
“Ladies washing and tossing cabbage,  
To the beckoning stream, come forth and share your delights!”  

To their lyrical calls, the horses respond.

In the spring motion of the noble plains lies a nectar-rich song of nature itself, and none can feign singing it to hint at its virtues:  
“Ferrymen! Listen!”  
The rising tide of rivercraft:  
“Dear!                                  -                                a companion ferry on the way…”

The Boundaries of Spring

By: Lee Hyo

Spring at the border! Crossing the city of Andong, the mesmerizing Jin-gangsan falls asleep in the silent mist, gazing out at the looming mountains beyond; beyond this, the flowing waters of the Apnokkang (Amnok River) reveal the thawed ice and rafted wood carried by the industrious Japanese workers who from their distant haunts hum the soothing melodies of the spring—"Spring of the Apnokkang!"

Upon Jin-gangsan's peaks, it marks a forward thrill into the season, inviting travelers and visitors alike to experience its beauty.

Spring at the border! The light-bearing beauty is wafting warmly into place.

To adore spring, no doubt there are varied visions amongst people. Accompanied by lovely ladies aboard a small rickshaw, the spring breeze streams through, gazing upon the flowers dung around Jin-gangsan, is certainly a delightful existence.

Yet without such ease, there are vehicle ferries that take us along the shores; steamboats sailing to the hot springs that provide unbothered views of spring.

Even the long journey from the Shinheung Bridge brings forth leisurely sights of the picturesque landscapes branded in landscapes where the seasonal blur becomes one with every masterpiece of nature's grasp.

Ah! The arrival of spring in our borders, no matter where we go, grows so beautiful!

But should you truly desire to bask in the truest essence of the border's spring, we need to delve deeper into its discovery.

We can take the shortcut to Jinheung with a smooth passage down the road; after a hearty drive of about four hours, perhaps you might maintain a slice of experience of treasure as the bustling roads play their nature’s orchestration, singing of the bounds.

The moment the vehicle arrives at the historical gates of the old Tale We190, it will yield such tender moments akin to a remora swimming at pace in synergy with this tapestry, stretching across time. Behold! The historic city of Eui-ju awaits inside, with its breathtaking views around the old fort confines, while expecting visitors to encounter nature’s vibrant harmony!

Clambering further, ascend the hills of the Tonggunjeong Observatory and overlook the stunning vistas of colorful landscapes—the pristine waters below, embraced by emerald mountains.


Yet the adventure does not conclude here; having gazed at the beauty of nature present, the moment draws near to purchase mementos and souvenirs, a common circumstance amid explorers making their way to Jinheung. Wrapping oneself in leisure moments comes with tales of endless myths, flirting with histories along the great river.

Yonder in the flow of fate, the companions drift down alongside lush valleys, surrounded by mesmerizing nature.

Undoubtedly, myriad voices of people whisper tales from beyond centrality while revealing the echoes of life in the pursuits of independence.  

To conclude, spring’s returns mark cherished opportunities that impart countless tales unspoken, with echoes that are even vaster than mere illustration.  

Summary

This article celebrates the varied experiences of spring throughout South Korea in the 1930s, traversing landscapes filled with cultural and historical significance. It delves into the picturesque scenery from southern springs to the ancient sites of Hwaseong, each providing a unique lens into Korea’s heritage. The bittersweet joy intertwined with the daily lives of the people further illustrates the emotional landscape of colonial Korea, emphasizing how cultural traditions and natural beauty coexist amid the struggles of modernity, thus offering historians valuable insights into the time's socio-political context.

南方의 봄
曉山

봄이라고요. 쫍니고 부댓기고 시달니고 멋대로 될대로 시드러진 인생에게 봄이야기 특히 쎈치맨탈하게 되기 쉬운 봄이야기는 너무나 꿈입니다.
그림과 가티 소리와 가티 뷔여잡을 수 업는 한 꿈입니다.
그러나 억지로 남쪽의 봄을 말하자면 아니 남쪽의 봄을 찻고 십다는 이가 잇다고 가정하면 나는 이러케 길이나 가르쳐주어 볼까요.
남쪽의 봄은 물에가 차즈시요. 다시 말하면 남쪽의 봄은 강에가 찻고 바다에가 차저 보시라는 말입니다.
충남이라 공주하면 고적으로 이름엇는 쌍수정알에 거의 넘어저가는 공북루 미테서부터는 조그만 그야말로 일엽편주 나무배를 금강우에 띄울 수가 입슴니다. 양언덕 모래와 나무 익기와 바위ㅅ새이를 저어서 또 저어서 웅진을 지나 부여라 백마강에 내려가보십시오. 오복 소복하게 파란 포기를 이룬 부소산과 반월성 우의 조선송낙엽송과 낙화암 절벽미테 빗두루 선 고묵나무 가튼 파란 물결 연긔에 싸인 평제탑 숨에 무친 수북정! 여긔서 술먹을 줄 모르고 노래를 부를 줄 모른다면 그는 완전이 무신경자일 것입니다.
엿바위를 지나 강경시가를 지나채 운산의 송림을 바라보며 황등곰개를 지나 한 산의 후포 서천의 암포를 내려가면 금강의 끗나는 군산항입니다.
그러나 그 새이를 그대로 지날 수는 업슬 것이니 다른 때는 볼 수도 업든 고기 ㅅ 뼈가 총총이 드러서서 밤이나 나지나 배ㅅ 동우들의 돗치며 부르는 배ㅅ 노래는 그것이 이 금강에서 이 봄한철만 그리고도 아조 짧은 동안 뱅어(白魚)라는 고기잡이의 노래ㅅ소리랍니다. 그것으로 인하야 이 한철은 적막한 연변 한촌도 일시의 번화를 자랑하게 되면 그 속에서는 순박한 태고시대에서만 볼 수 잇든 애릇한 가지가지의 로맨쓰도 잇다금은 매처진답니다.
군산을 버서나서 그야말로 만경창파 그대로의 바다를 배질하야 묵포항의 박그로 구부러저 도라가며 벽파정 싼 물결에 충무공의 전적도 추억하려니와 섬과 섬이 이로나오고 저로 드러 가고한 다도해ㅅ 새이 따뜻한 봄볏에 반짝이는 물결을 아로 바누질하고 저로 바누질해가며 노를 저어 나가다가 할 수만 잇다면 「남해야 잘잇거라」하고 남쪽 한가에 혼자 뚝 떠러저 잇는 탐나의 녯서울 제주도 봄빗을 응당 차저가 볼일인가 합니다. 가서 향긔나는 유자 냄새를 실컷 맛고 도라올 일입니다. 도라오는 길에 통영과 마산의 해안 춘색도 보아 조흐려니와 안으로 쑥 드러와서 진주의 남강 춘색을 상주는 것도 또한 무방한 일일 것입니다. 그러나 이 모든 것은 꿈입니다. 꿈에나 할 수 잇는 구경입니다.
봄은 해마다 오는 봄이언만 오는 봄은 올적마다 변해집니다. 이 땅의 봄은 해마다 달나집니다. 빗일흔 봄 소리일혼 봄이 되어 옵니다.
오! 어느 때나 우리 남방에도 봄가튼 봄이 다시 오려는가?

華城春風曲
崔泳柱

화성(華城)의 흙냄새나는 봄소식을 주섬주섬 몃조각 얽어보앗습니다. 그러나 이 엇지 화성 하나의 정경이겟습니까. 말하자면 조선의 전원이 가진 거즈반 공통된 풍경 또 선률(旋律)이 아닐가 합니다.
포루각 집궁에가 밈둘네 피면
아씨넨 봄바람에 바구니찻소
수원(水原=華城)에도 북문(北門)안의 고요한 봄이올시다. 북문서 화홍문(華紅門)으로 성장을 세고 가노라면 포루각이 잇습니다. 이름이 포루각이지 생겨난 후 한번 제법 한 싸홈도 못격것고 대포한번 걸녀도 보지 못한 가엽슨 포루각(砲樓閣)이올시다. 지금은 허무러져서 집둥도 한 귀퉁만 간신히 남어잇고 그 둑겁든 담벽도 억척세인 선모슴돌에게 모다 뜻기어서 반도 채 못남어잇슴니다. 봄이 되면 양달진 그 곳에는 어데보다 먼저 봄풀이 돗고 조고마한 봄 꼿들이 간즈런히 핍니다. 압흐로는 과원이 연니어 잇서서 채색진 꼿옷을 닙고 뒤로는 삼밧(벌판)치라 다웅치마 닙은 아가씨네들이 때지어 봄나물을 뜻고 잇습니다. 3월이 되면 학교졸업하고 마움복개는 젊은 네들이 곳잘 이곳으로 지나다니고 간혹 섹기어서 아씨네의 부로는 고운 노래 소래도 들니웁니다.

국경의 봄!
국경의 도시 안동현을 넘어서 진강산이 고요히 아즈랑이에 잠이 들고 그를 넘어서 넘겨다보이는 온갓 산맥이 한층 더 갓갑게 보이는 때
그리고 국경을 가로흘으는 압록강에는 어느 듯 어름장도 꺼지고 천천히 떠내려가는 뗏목을 탄 일본 노동자의 입에서 쬬이나 쬬이나의 「압녹강절」이 한가롭게 들려오는 때
진강산에 꼿노리 하는 여객과 유객 그리고 유람선의 기생들의 한가롬은 가련한 노래와 함께 구경의 봄은 시작된다.
국경의 봄! 그것은 이와 가튼 화창한 그리고 엑쏘틕한 공긔를 갓고 차저드는 것이다.
이와 가튼 국경의 봄을 우리들이 완상하는데는 물논 사람따라서 여러 가지로 구경하는 것이 달너질 것이다.
기생아씨들을 동행으로 이십전 자리 인력거에 봄바람을 멍에하야 안동의 진강산의 꼿츨 차저 보는 것은 물논 조흔 노릇이고 그럿치 아니하면 긔차로 약 한시간 가는 오통배의 온천의 봄을 차저 보는 것도 해롭지 아니한 노릇일 것이다. 그리고 멀그러케 멀니 행차할 것 업시 바로 신의주 철교 밋헤서 멀니 보이는 영림창 굴*을 바라보면서 축동을 거러가보라! 거기에도 완전히 국경도시의 봄은 발견될 것이다.
봄! 봄! 국경의 봄은 어데를 가나 우리들의 눈압헤 사모차잇스리라. 하나 우리들이 만일 참말로 진정한 국경의 봄을 보랴면 우리들은 좀더 좀더 잘 볼 수 잇는 방법을 연구할 필요가 잇다.
우리들은 몬저 신의의주통로로 가서 의주행의 자동차를 잡어탄다.
그리고 우리들 동행중에는 약 네시간 가량 자동차안에서 견듸는 동안에 아-국경의 봄은 외이다지 살 풍경인고? 하고 한탄을 마지 아니할 것이다. 과연 홍진(紅塵)을 일으키우며 자동차가 막달으는 신작로에는 국경의 봄은 쓸쓸할는지 모른다.
하나 우리가 차자가는 국경의 봄은 홍진아 이러나는 신작노에 잇는 것이 아니고 우리들 글태인 자동차가 의주성내의 남문안에 스톱을 할 때에 나타나는 것이다.
보라! 거기에는 고적의 도시 의주 그리고 우리들의 귀에 익은 유명한 통군정! 거기에는 풍부한 자연미에 어우러진 무한한 춘색이 완상의 유객을 고대하지 안는가?
스틕을 의지하야 통군정에 올나서서 압뒤 풍경을 굽어보라! 압헤는 녹수 뒤에는 청산! 산수의 경치를 아울너 가진 뒤에 고적까지 겸해 볼 수 잇는 곳 그리고 글자 그대로 일목천본의 사구라 꼿치 자욱히 만발되여 잇는 이곳 여긔서 우리들은 한껏 국경의 봄을 완상할 수 잇는 것이다.
그러나 국경의 봄은 결코 여기에 끗치지 아니한다. 여긔서 꼿도 보고 물도 보며 통군정 풍경도 눈에 실어지거든 이번은 다시 신의주를 향하야 나려올 차례이다. 물론 의주서 육십리 거리나 되는 신의주에를 도보로 내려오는 수는 업고 그럿타고 하야서 다시 자동차를 리용하는 것도 무추미한 일이다.
여긔서 우리들은 생각을 돌녀 이번은 이십전 삭전을 물고 지나 인의장구선을 잡어타게 된다. 이 때에 만일 우리들이 넉넉한 처지가트면 인물조코 목성곱은 의주기생을 한 두사람 함께 싯고서 그들의 무릅을 벼개로 배우에 누으면야 오작이나 조흐련만 만일에 그까지는 못할 처지면 책가게에 가서 갑싸고 자미잇는 별건곤이라도 하나 사가지고 그것을 동무로 배에 올너보라!
흘느는 물결에 배는 저절로 쏜살가티 흘너가서 배는 어느듯 의주와 신의주의 중류에 일으게 된다. 그리고 거긔서는 다시 조흔 국경의 봄이 보여지는 것이다.
보라! 좌우에 욱어진 절벽절벽! 그리고 그 절벽에는 가지각색의 일음 모를 꼿치 장식되여 그야말로 명화의 유화(油畵)의 일폭이라 할가? 그럿치 아니하면 둘너친 꼿평풍이라 할가 그나 그뿐이랴 그 새이를 비거비래하는 일음모를 산새들의 노래소래 이야말로 별유천지 비인간이 아니고 무엇이랴!
이밧게도 우리들이 자랑할려면 얼마라도 국경의 봄의 조흔 곳을 소개할 수 잇다.
아! 국경의 봄! 우리들은 만은 말을 가지고 국경의 봄을 찬양치 아느련다. 우리들은 다만 간단히 말해둔다. 금강산의 경치를 못보앗거든 경치를 말치 말라는 말과 가티 국경의 봄을 보지 못하엿거든 감히 봄 이야기를 말나고.＜7＞
