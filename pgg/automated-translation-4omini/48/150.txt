Readers' Column, Jokes  

Peaceful Jokes  

1. It must be a girl-  
A foolish man who was being hit by his wife in the kitchen suddenly jumped out at the sound of his friend's laughter and asked,  
"Must it be a girl?"  
Friend: "Um... huh..."  

2. It's only for the one to hear-  
The husband who had gone out to sell his well-prepared silks was coming home, and seeing his wife who had just stepped out the front door as if something had happened, he asked,  
"Did you only hear about the silk at the market today?"  
Wife (frightened): "Then what about our silk?"  
Husband: "Well, we'll hear about it when we get back."  
Wife: "......."  

3. The sayings of Seoul are difficult-  
A country person who had come to Seoul for the first time did not understand the local dialect.  
Landlord: "Where have you been?"  
Villager: "I went to get some 쩍 (tteok) at Dongdaemun."  

4. Still not sorry-  
A pair of twin brothers got married on the same day. The younger brother’s wife, having done her makeup, went to the bathroom, and the older brother, mistaking her for his own wife, met her.  
Younger sister-in-law: "I’m quite lovely, aren’t I?"  
Older brother: "No, you’re not." (Meaning, it's not a man)  
Her face flushed, she hurriedly backed away and met the older brother again (thinking he was a man).  
Younger sister-in-law: "I was embarrassed in front of you just now."  
Older brother: "You're still not sorry."

Mistake  
Lee Yong-kyu  

Traffic officer: "Hey! You can’t exceed the speed limit!"  
Driver: "Oh! I made a mistake. I never dreamed there would be a police station around here!"  

Strange introduction- At the hospital ward  
Young woman: "I came to visit Byeong Joo."  
An attendant who seems to be in charge said: "Do not meet anyone other than close relatives as the doctor has instructed."  
Young woman: "I'm Byeong Joo's younger sister."  
Attendant: "Oh, is that so? Then you may see him... I am Byeong Joo's mother."  

A one-eyed man  
A one-eyed man goes to buy a children's ticket to watch a movie.  
Ticket collector: "Excuse me. This ticket is not for children. Please buy another ticket."  
One-eyed man: "Hey, please pay attention. Look at people closely before speaking. Since I have one eye, I’ll only look for half the time."  
Ticket collector: "Haha, then you need to pay double the admission. Because you see, it will take twice as long."  

Holding back tears  
Brother: "Hey- why do you have a bandage on your finger?"  
Younger brother: "I cut my hand with your knife."  
Brother: "When did you cut it?"  
Younger brother: "Uh, I cut it in the small room."  
Brother: "Wow, you are really brave. Starting tomorrow, the sun will rise in the west. You’re not even crying from cutting your hand."  
Younger brother: "Why wouldn’t I cry? I’ll cry when father and mother come back."  

What did you say?  
Yangju Seo Jeong-hyun  

A country person asked a chestnut seller in Seoul:  
Villager: "What is this?"  
Chestnut seller: "It’s 자시 (jasi)."  
The villager, not understanding, ate all the chestnuts without paying.  
Chestnut seller: "Hey, pay for the chestnuts!"  
Villager: "You said it was 'jasi', so I ate it."  

Is it dark?  
Father-in-law: "Child, go have dinner."  
The daughter-in-law who often lives with her in-laws: "It's just getting dark for dinner."  
Father-in-law: "Don't be smart with your words. Like a deer in the mountains."  
Daughter-in-law: "Before the deer gets it, it is just getting dark."  
The father-in-law got angry saying, "Where did you learn this foolishness?"  
The daughter-in-law answered while running away, "I learned from a young man at the market in Unsan."  

Didn’t pay the money  
Lee Ki-joo  

Traveler: "The rice is full of stones, where can I eat..."  
Owner: "Surely the rice can’t be that full of stones."  
Traveler: "There's a fly in the soup! Huh?"  
Owner: "Flies are food too, but they didn't pay for the fly."  

**Summary**  

This collection of humorous anecdotes highlights the daily lives and social interactions among Koreans during the colonial period, reflecting cultural nuances and humorous misunderstandings common in that society. Historians may find it particularly interesting how humor served as a social commentary amidst the constraints of colonial rule, providing insight into the coping mechanisms of ordinary people in their daily struggles.
