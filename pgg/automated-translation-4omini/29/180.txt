기사형태	문예기타 (Other Literary Forms)  

A person who came out of the coffin fifteen months after dying

If a dead person were to come back to life, who could truly believe such a statement? In old myths and stories, we often hear tales of deceased individuals coming back to life, either in reality or in an overly fanciful manner. However, modern medicine has advanced to the point where it can realize these old fables; through medications and surgeries, modern science has been able to resuscitate individuals previously thought to be dead.

Yet, the fact I am about to describe is neither a myth, nor an imaginative hallucination, nor the result of medical advancements that could bring someone back from the afterlife. The protagonist of this remarkable story is none other than Ann Carter Lee, the mother of General Robert E. Lee, during the American Civil War. This story is so dramatic that it is unprecedented in my research until now.

Indeed, Robert E. Lee, who was born fifteen months after his mother’s burial, is the subject of this tale. Mrs. Lee was dead for seven days, during which she spent three days in a coffin at the Lee family shrine in Virginia. By a chance occurrence, it was discovered that she was still alive; afterward, she fully recovered her health and lived for approximately twenty-one more years.

Originally, Mrs. Lee had a habitual tendency to faint, a condition that her doctors could not accurately diagnose, leaving them with no treatment options. Gradually, she became more feeble and, in November 1805, after ineffective treatment from four doctors, she passed away. She was placed in a simple coffin and, four days later, moved to the Lee family shrine. Family members visited daily to see her face, but no one ever realized she was still alive.

On the seventh day, while preparing her grave, the gravedigger heard the faint sound of someone calling out "I am alive." At first, he thought he was imagining it and continued with his duties. However, hearing the voice again, which clearly came from a person, made him panic, and he fled the site in shock. When he relayed this story to the family, they doubted his sanity and wondered if he were speaking the truth. Yet, the gravedigger insisted this was a real occurrence, and he sought others to return to the graveyard and continue the preparations.

Again, a faint voice calling "save me" was heard. Surprised and suspicious, they looked around, among the many coffins, and noticed that the surroundings were eerily silent, with only skeletal remains lying still. As the gravedigger continued his work, when they attempted to cover the coffin with dirt, the faint voice saying "save me, save me" clearly came from within. Terrified, the gravedigger instinctively looked down into the coffin. He saw the deceased Mrs. Lee struggling to open her eyes and move her lips. 

Although alarmed, the gravedigger, composed and methodical, opened the coffin lid. Mrs. Lee was eventually taken back to her home and, surprisingly, after some time, regained her health and became even stronger than before. Five years later, on January 19, 1807, General Robert E. Lee was born. Mrs. Lee lived twenty-one more years until she passed away in 1826.

A temporary state of death is a matter that science struggles to acknowledge. Medical professionals generally deny the existence of such a state. Mrs. Lee's case could be classified medically as a "coma." Yet, contemporary medical practitioners have now begun to accept the idea of a state referred to as "suspended animation". For instance, there is Mari Truue, a nurse at the Long Island Sanctuary. Ms. Truue died from a disease while traveling on a Pennsylvania Railroad sleeping car. The attending physician, H. Sparrow, diagnosed her as "dead for five hours" and called for a body transport. However, two hours later, rescuers from the Washington Fire Station discovered that she was still alive.

Dr. H. Sparrow vehemently asserted that she was completely dead, supporting this claim with all medical methods available for diagnosis at that time. He expressed, had the woman been alive at the moment of my diagnosis, there are other diagnostic methods not yet recognized in modern medicine.

However, general medical standards indicate that there is no state of suspended animation, claiming that such states are merely confused with deep sleep or total body rigidity. Research in British medicine suggests that sometimes tuberculosis patients may experience a deep coma due to the severity of their illness, leading to total body rigidity; consequently, Ms. Truue likely experienced a similar condition. According to Dr. H. Sparrow, who diagnosed her, she had developed muscle rigidity by that morning and died in the afternoon, only to inexplicably revive at three o'clock in the afternoon.

**Summary**

This article recounts the remarkable story of Ann Carter Lee, the mother of General Robert E. Lee, who astonishingly came back to life after being declared dead for seven days. The narrative intertwines elements of speculative medical practices and raises questions about the understanding of death. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article reflects contemporary fascination with unexpected medical phenomena, paralleling the era's interest in Western science and the complexities of life and death.
