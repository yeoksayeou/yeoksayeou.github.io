葉書通信  

In summer, the rainy season invariably comes to Korea. The saying that men must wear raincoats and rubber shoes originated from this. However, these days I often see police officers trudging along in their white shoes and white raincoats, squeezing under someone else's umbrella in the rain. It makes me feel sorry for them; it's not their fault. And what on earth are white shoes and white raincoats on a rainy day? Looking at our situation, it's inappropriate to wear white shoes and white raincoats in the summer. Have they ever thought of emulating the people of other countries? They should quickly sell those pants and invest in a pair of rubbers that don’t get wet and an umbrella, or they'll be ruined!

At the University of Germany, do they also teach sparrows?  

At our hospital in Susong-dong, every day German students gather to talk about something like scientific research or political discussions, but it turns out they are just gathering for a Mahjong club. It seems they are so bored just with men that they invited a courtesan named Lee X to join them. I cannot know whether the university in Germany teaches Mahjong, but if it does, those students are unlikely to worry about becoming Mahjong doctors this year. If that’s the case, that courtesan may not go to Germany but at least she can learn Mahjong from those German students, so she might achieve a master’s degree. This is just a notification from my first letter.

Why isn’t there a Korean language specialty established?  

At Jinsung Specialized School in the city, they have started teaching Korean language for three hours every week, and the teacher in charge is Principal Park Seung-bin. Would it not be better to thoroughly establish a Korean language specialty at that school?  

Unique Busan Restaurants  

When you get off at Busan's pier, anyone can expect to be lured into an eatery boasting cleanliness and delicious food similar to Paris. Unable to resist their incessant coaxing, the unsuspecting diners might soon find themselves surprised. Inside, a foul smell assaults the nose, and if you order noodles, they'll serve you a bowl of rice soup instead, charging you an outrageous 25 jeon for what basically amounts to rotten soybean paste soup with a lump of cold rice thrown in. It seems that the restaurant owners in Busan plan to never see those customers again after a first visit. No matter how desperate the situation may be, what compels those business owners to treat their customers this way? For the sake of their business, it would be better if they behaved a little more honestly.

A Pervasive Advertising Board  

In the mountains, fields, and rice paddies, everywhere you turn, there is an overwhelming number of advertisements that make it impossible to enjoy the natural scenery. Traveling along the railway, I couldn’t see anything but ads, no matter how much someone might claim we live in a commercial society. It seems they believe that if someone doesn’t know the product name or how to buy it, then they must be unable to purchase it. When looking at the mountains, they can only see advertising boards, leaving a feeling of despair. Looking at it, it seems that if I confront the railroad authority to remove them, it will be akin to removing a pen from a prison cell. We must travel, yet the sight of the advertisements is a pain. Thinking that these billboards are obstructing our living conditions really makes me angrier.  

Summary  

This article reflects the everyday concerns and social observations of individuals living in Korea during the colonial period, capturing their frustrations with both the weather and societal issues, notably the quality of services. It paints a picture of a society burdened by the impacts of colonial rule, as people struggle with the impracticality of imported fashions and the dishonesty of local businesses. Historians might find the commentary on local Korean identities and cultural practices particularly illuminating, as it demonstrates the tension between Western influences and traditional Korean values during the period of Japanese occupation.
