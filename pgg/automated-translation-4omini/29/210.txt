My Protest  
To the Intellectual Class  
Ahn Yeon-dong  

For many years, the terms “intellectual class” (知識階級) and “intelligentsia” have been frequently mentioned in Korea, and I have also heard them so often that my ears ache. I even learned the new term “intelligentsia.” However, the intellectual class in Korea is quite pathetic. Some individuals treated middle school students as if they were part of the intellectual class, and thus, in Korea, the intellectual class is considered precious.  

Once someone graduates from middle school, if they can just read through a daily newspaper (Osaka Mainichi Shinbun) and can somewhat speak Japanese, they consider themselves gentlemen. They then claim to be the leaders of Korea.  

Then, if these individuals could study more consciously and guide the Korean populace effectively, how wonderful that would be! Yet they act as if having merely graduated from middle school grants them extraordinary status.  

So, once they attend a specialized school or the imperial university, they become utterly arrogant.  

They hardly think about what they eat, what they wear, or what they used to study; they act as if they have only studied on the wealth their grandfathers or fathers earned and had a better fate.  

In reality, it stems from the blood and sweat of farmers and laborers, which they entirely overlook. Even the little they know, they seem to take lightly.  

After graduating from middle school, their first step is to frequent some clubs and restaurants. Then they boast about how well they live, while they had previously taught even by sharing knowledge with students below them.  

The intellectual class in Korea is merely a splendid façade.  

Look at it—the same goes for social movements and national movements. They wear suits and flaunt their honors while sneaking into restaurants, and that is the intellectual class in Korea. When a significant event occurs, they pretend to be the most loyal, pushing to the frontlines, but as soon as they see a police officer approaching, they sneak away and run off.  

Once they have gathered, if they don’t mix well with others, they immediately start to make grand arguments. How is Sun Yat-sen and what about “Lenin”, and what do they say about “Marx”? Yet no matter how they discuss, these people possess thoughts that drive them. However, the most educated are usually wealthy youth.  

They study, study, and go abroad to America, Germany, France, and England, yet they are too busy promoting matters such as divorce, culture housing, and so on.  

In fact, the intellectual class in Korea is merely a façade, akin to second-rate people.  

Indeed, the intellectual class never publicly teaches in labor night schools or farmers' night schools for free. If they taught, it would be an embarrassment for their social standing and, otherwise, a matter of honor. So how can such night schools develop well?  

Look, where are labor night schools that provide thorough education for workers in Seoul, a city that is supposedly the capital? All that exists is a middle evening night school run by others at a distant location, and a central youth association labor night school, which barely exists.  

Thus, our Korean intellectual class considers itself quite noble. Hence, the intellectual class becomes estranged from the populace. Furthermore, I must say this: there is no doubt that the intellectual class in Korea is merely a façade. They are egotists in seeking selfish interest, seekers of fame.  

But does anyone recognize this sort of thing? They indulge completely in their own desires.  

Moreover, what’s more disheartening is that the private education entrepreneurs only know how to educate the children of the middle class, neglecting the education of farmers and laborers.  

To the Newspaper Operators  
Seokchon Saeng  

I am a subscriber with a name, and it is truly a ridiculous thing for an anonymous reader like me to present a complaint. To file a complaint should be the role of some notable figure or someone with rights; if someone like me submits it, I think the newspaper authority wouldn’t listen even a little. However, it cannot be left as is. Regardless of what they might think, I solely present my honest opinion.  

A newspaper considers itself a bell of the people and we also acknowledge this. As a bell, it is indeed a fact that it should strive for our society. Nevertheless, at times, it goes to great lengths to the point of being overly annoying.  

Even if something arises in some part that isn’t widely known, they still write large headlines about some worthy matter and hand them to the general populace; I cannot fathom what thought led to that decision, but as part of the populace, I have extremely unpleasant feelings about it.  

And a newspaper's mission is to report the events of the world objectively, yet if it neglects this responsibility and gives biased reports out of personal feelings among its staff, then even if it asserts its position for justifiable reasons, it can no longer be viewed as a public instrument of the people, but merely a journal of a few staff members or a group of relatives.  

Why do they insist on presenting novels like those? Once a newspaper starts featuring such stories, it seems that they all follow the trend and start publishing them equally; perhaps it’s a sales strategy.  

Do they want readers of different backgrounds to enjoy works like Journey to the West (西遊記) that appeal to Buddhist monks but not care for something like Dream of the Red Chamber (紅樓夢)? The newspaper has a responsibility to smash superstitions, eradicate bad customs, and establish new culture, yet by serializing such novels, they continue to contribute to superstitions and ignorance among the awakened masses; what kind of mindset is that?  

The photos that are sent from across the ocean come to be distributed for the ordinary reader; those who engage in translating are so ignorant that they may think it worthwhile to act in such a manner.  

From now on, could they wait a while and release them for use only after general readership has faded?  

Moreover, as for the editorials, they are the most influential organs when discerning right and wrong in the world and also the life of the newspaper itself. It is only proper to comment on new and real problems as they arise and enlighten the general populace.  

However, lately the editorials in our civilian newspapers seem to be more about the writer’s composition practice or research presentation; why do they lack to state their ideological position? Certainly, various conditions may not permit true expression, yet it would be more effective if they pay attention beyond this. And as for the space, making it longer by 8 pages is merely giving us a chance for more senseless wasteful articles; if they are to waste funds for page extensions, could they not responsibly choose and present valuable content rather than just filling the pages with trash?  

To Male Students  
Kim O-sook  

Is there anything a female student can express as a complaint against male students? Perhaps something like, “Be quieter,” or “Hold yourself together.”  

What you mentioned seems to be merely an entreaty of this nature.  

However, isn’t this kind of begging excessive? In the past, such attitudes didn’t change; were they simply reading a sutra to a deaf pig? What good will it do to say anything?  

However, feeling greatly stuck, shall I speak again?  

Men, who are idols created out of superiority, why are you so vile and lacking in conscience?  

There is a proverb that knowing one leads to knowing ten; you are presumably promising young men, and we are young people who are prepared to join our strength and hearts together to contribute to this society, so are we not?  

Your humor has the right measure, yet why is your demeanor so rude and contemptible?  

You frankly disregard the vile things you say, showing no awareness of trespassing limits, or are you utterly devoid of senses, so much so that you cannot grasp that we feel humiliated every time we hear those words?  

When I see you fluttering carelessly like a lunatic while mindlessly mumbling foolish words, if I dare to respond to you, you promptly label me as a weak woman in that decrepit head of yours and hurl insults at me.  

So, can we ignore this groundless insult and pretend we’ve heard nothing?  

This indignity is beyond comprehension. You are indeed insulting.  

Yet how many wrongs do you men have, while you exaggerate and spread the smallest faults of women? Why, in this era of male dominance, must you so heinously act in such ways?  

Your simple amusement inflicts an ever-deepening scar on women at every level.  

You are a contradiction and shameful, oh men!  

You have taken the virtue of young women, crushed them, and yet feel no pangs of conscience!   

You tremble with sincerity, moaning, “Oh, goddess of love, without you, I am a walking corpse,” all the while parading around like a vicious viper.  

In the end, witnessing the true consequences brings forth an endless curse and you quickly jump back into the arms of other women.  

So please reflect back on your actions.  

It is true, I do not mean to generalize, and I sincerely apologize to those who do not fit this description.  

To My Mother-in-law  
Unfilial Daughter-in-law  

Dear Mother-in-law!  

1. Why do you suspect me so?  

1. You quietly eat from the servants, yet abhor any food I eat—why is that?  

1. Why do you treat me like an animal while expecting perfection?  

1. We are equally your children; why the disparity between us?  

1. What’s wrong with you treating my husband, your son, well?  

1. Why do you begrudge the harmony and joy of this marital bond?  

1. You speak ill of me, a daughter-in-law in your own home, why do you?  

1. Why do you gossip about me to the neighbors, a mere daughter-in-law?  

1. Even without needing to mention my husband, why do you pry and poke at him?  

1. When my husband shouts or despises me, dear mother-in-law, what do you find joy in,bearing that long cigarette holder around?  

To My Modern Son  
My Useless Son  

A father voicing opinions to his son? The world has indeed changed, but it has utterly fallen into darkness as well. In the past, such discussions would not have surfaced, but in this era where the young think only of themselves, if I remain stubborn, what’s the use?  

So while I let them be, I can’t help but have my heart weighed down. Nevertheless, whether I speak or not matters not; it seems useless either way. Thus, I simply must let these feelings out.  

You see, I am already 70 years old, and among my three sons, two passed away last year, leaving the youngest at twenty-seven. I have tenderly raised him like a precious gem and so he is dear to me. After finishing elementary and middle school, I lacked funds to continue his education, so I sold an old house I had, renting thatched huts for 300 won. After spending every last penny on education, he is supposed to have graduated this spring. Now, if he were to come out and support us, I had hoped that such respect would come my way.  

Yet here I am, simply filling out the void like a fool while feeling indebted for all I have done for him.  

Nevertheless, as I talk to some friends, I may vent my frustrations. 

So tell me, do you think I should rush this dialogue?   

Let time reveal everything.  

Ah, at 70 years of age, my youngest son is all that remains. All my hopes and careful efforts turn to ashes when he graduates and still I have nothing, only to become like an idle man. I cannot stand for him to merely enjoy life and not help out the family.  

On top of that, he now sits idly at home instead of seeking fortune and prosperity—that is the hard truth of where we are left.  

When he was in school, going off to Japan to marry into a wealthy family—oh, is that an intelligent match?  

Having married off a girl who had no proper education, living in fear of failure is another tragedy there. Thus it also results in my son's indifference; he willingly abandons me to fulfill their fantasies and expectations, bestowing pity upon them, believing it may bring my son fortune.  

Anyway, whether or not I allow myself to see in this life won’t matter because our families will never understand why such things are happening.  

Where is my son to be, who tiring of thoughts has lost home, wandering about in the shadows?  

Where is my son? Would he not rather have remained a student elsewhere? Does he live a carefree life?  

Even so, he will eventually come back to manage the affairs of our family, though will I see such things before I die?  

All I ask is that every Korean youth take sincere consideration of their place and engage in the truly honest struggles for their country and family.  

This is what I, a frail old man, humbly implore, hoping that each finds fulfillment, and I wish to see through the eye of history.  

**Summary**  

The article expresses deep criticism of the intellectual class in Korea during the colonial period, portraying them as disconnected and self-serving. The author laments their arrogance, lack of genuine effort to understand or guide the common people, and superficial engagement with societal issues. The piece serves as both a protest against this disconnect and a broader appeal for a restructuring of priorities that involve genuine contributions to national and societal growth. Historians might find this critique significant as it reflects the societal tensions of colonial rule, where many Koreans grappled with identity and responsibility against a backdrop of oppression.

나의 抗議
知識階級에게
亞鉛洞人

조선에도 년래로 『지식계급』(知識階級)이니, 『인테리켄챠』이니 하는 소리가 풀풀 날너단여서 나 역시도 귀가 젓도록 들엇슴니다. 또한 『인테리켄챠』라는 신술어(新術語)까지 배우고요. 그러나 조선에 지식게급이란 한심한 것이지요. 엇던 분은 년전에 중학생을 다 지식계급으로 취급하야 어느 곳에 쓰섯드군요. 이 만츰 조선에 지식게급은 귀한 것이니까요.
중학교나 졸업하고 대판매일신문(大阪每日新聞)이나 줄줄 닑을 줄 알고 일어(日語)마듸나 하면 벌서 이는 신사(紳士)외다. 그리고 조선의 지도자(指導者)라고 자처하고 나스게 됨니다.
그러면 이들이 진정한 의식(意識)으로 좀 더 공부하고 조선 민중을 지도하얏스면 그 얼마나 조흐릿가 만은 중학교 쯤 졸업한 것을 가지고 무슨 굉장한 무엇이나 한 것가티 역이지요.
그러니 전문(專門)이나 뎨대(帝大)쯤 가면 그야말노 안하무인(眼下無人)이지요.
이들이 무엇을 먹고 무엇을 입고, 무엇을 가지고 공부하얏는 지는 조금치도 생각지 안치요. 그저 할아버지나 아버지가 벌어서 노흔 재산으로 팔자 조하서 공부만 한 것가티 역이고 잇섯든지요.
실상은 농민, 로동자(農民勞働者)의 피와 땀의 결정인 것은 조금도 몰은단 말임니다. 약간 안다고 하야도 수박 것할기지요.
중학교를 졸업하고는 첫번재 가는 것이『무슨會』 『무슨會』하고 료리집 ＜76＞부터이지요. 그러고 이제는 되엿다하고 조금 물씬 물씬한 선생에게는 (작일까지 자긔를 가르첫건만)농(弄)비슷한 소리를 걸거든요.
조선에 지식게급이란 한개의 훌륭한 외입장이외다.
보십시요. 사회운동(社會運動)단테에도 그럿코 민족운동(民族運動)단테에도 그럿슴니다. 양복이나 훌륭이 입고 슬금슬금 명예(名譽)나 돗그고 료리집이나 가고요. 이것이 조선의 지식게급이지요. 그러다가도 무슨 큰 일이 나면 가장 렬심인 체 충성인체 하고 압흐로 나섯다가 어떡하다가 경찰 당국 가튼 곳에서 잡는 눈침만 보면 살살 피하야 벌서 어대로 도망가버리고 업단 말이지요. 그러고 각금가다가 모혀 안지면 그야말노 명론탁설(名論卓說)을 하지요. 손문(孫文)은 엇더코, 「레-닌」은 엇더코, 『맑스』는 엇더타고요. 그러나 이러니 저러니 하야도 이런 분들은 사상(思想)이 들엇다는 분이지요만은 제일 말재 가는 양반은 재산 잇는 집 지식청년이지요. 그들은 공부, 공부하고 미국(米國)이나 독일(獨逸) 불란서(佛蘭西) 영국(英國)등지를 단여와서는 첫재 리혼(離婚), 둘재 문화주택(文化住宅), 셋재 고등XX를 운동하기에 겨를이 업지요.
실상은 조선에 지식게급은 외입장이만 안이라. 뎨이호(第2號)인간들이지오.
그야말노 지식게급이란 그 분들이 어느 곳에 로동자 야학(勞働者夜學)이나 농민 야학(農民夜學)에 무료로 가르친단 말을 내가 듯지 못 하얏슴니다. 가르친다면 체면에 못익여서 그러치 안흐면 명예이지요. 그러니 그런 야학이 잘 발뎐할 수 잇나요.
보십시요. 조선에 수부(首府)이라는 서울에 로동자를 위하야 투철이 가르처주는 야학이 어대잇슴니까. 그것도 남의 손을 비러서 멀리 남산 밋 일인 경영에 중등야학교(中等夜學校)가 잇슬 뿐이지요. 그러고 보통 교육을 가르치는 중앙 청년회 로동야학교(中央靑年會勞働夜學校)가 잇군요. 이마만츰 우리 조선의 지식게급은 고마운 냥반이거든요. 이러니 지식게급은 민중(民衆)과 거리가 멀어지지요. 더 한말 하겟슴니다. 조선에 지식게급은 두 말할 것 업시 외입장이요. 리긔주의(利己主義)자이요, 명예탐구자(名譽探究者)이지요.
그러나 이 따위들을 누가 알아주기나 하는 줄 아십니까. 제멋에 껏덕어리고 단이지요. 더구나 가증한 것은 민간 교육 사업자들이 중산계급의 자제들만 교육 식힐 줄 알엇지 농민 로동자 교육은 여지 업시 등한하거든요.(下略)

新聞 經營者에게
石村生

나는 한 개의 名色업는 신문 구독자이다. 이와 가티 名色업는 한낫 독자로써＜77＞ 항의를 한다는 것은 참 우수운 일이다. 항의도 엇던 名士나 혹은 권리 잇는 사람이 하여야 효과가 잇지 나 가튼 無名之卒이 제출한다고 하여야 신문 당국자로서는 이 말을 개방귀 만치도 안이 역일 것이다. 그러나 그러타고 해서 그냥 둘 수는 업다. 무얼로 역이거나 그것은 그 사람들의 생각에 맛길 것이요. 나로서는 다만 솔직하게 제출하고야 말겟다.
신문이란 민중의 本鐸이라고 자처하고 우리도 또한 그러케 아는 바임니다. 本鐸인 만큼 우리 사회를 위하야 만히 애써주는 것이 사실임니다. 그러나 엇던 때에는 공연한 애를 써서 좀 꼴보기 실흔 때가 퍽 만히 잇습듸다.
일부에서 생겨나 전 사회에 알녀지지 안이 하야도 조흘 일을 가지고 크다란 활자로 제목을 부치어 일반 민중의 압헤 내 노으니 엇더한 심사로 그러는지 몰으나 민중의 한 사람인 나로서 볼때에는 대단히 아니꼬운 생각이 남니다.
그리고 신문이란 매일 세상에 생기는 일을 공정하게 보도하는 것이 그 사명이어늘 이 사명을 이저버리고 자기의 社로나 혹은 社員 間에 감정이 좃치 못한 편에서 엇더한 사실이 이러난다면 공정치 못하게 한편으로 치우친 기사를 내이니 신문 자체의 주의주장이 다른 까닭으로 그러케 한다고 하겟지 만은 이러케 하는 것은 민중의 公器라고 볼 수가 업고 다만 몃 社員이나 그 친지의 團體 혹 個人의 機關誌라고 볼 수 밧게 업게 됨니다.
