The House of Korean Theater  
Park Yeong-ho  

After the sun has set.  
After the flowers have fallen.  
The 19th century — poets of early Romanticism, like the lakeside poets who used to float fallen leaves on the flowing waters of the Kan partition and sing of high mountains — experience the passing of time after the sun has set and the flowers have fallen, with a longing that transcends life itself.  

Did it not happen the other day then?  
Isn’t it that on such an evening, even two people recite tales of woe?  
This is the reason for the long lamentation of life.  
As is often the case with artists, the profound mysteries of this twilight can often be overwhelming.  

Currently, the streets are bustling with chocolate shadows and a fainting gloom.  
It seems as if the sun, the water, the stones, and the wind have all gathered to enjoy the splendor of this twilight.  

Twilight is the pier for every source of life.  
The ships departing from this pier are grand postal vessels that can be found in any zone of life.  
A traveler yearning for home.  
A monk wishing for fortune.  
A grandmother waiting for her child.  
A young girl dreaming of her husband.  
Many such hopes are carried away by the ships that set sail from this bustling harbor — the twilight.  

Then, isn’t it rather disheartening that the ships departing from this harbor have no clear escape? Hence, they must depart.  
Yet, this pier is ruled by the sovereignty of impermanence that governs the order of this harbor.  

Who is it that governs?  
He is the one who, within this twilight, forges food, creates art, forms lovers, and even establishes honor and power. Is this not theater?  

Even a president would yield this right to the theater artists.  
They are all cosmopolitans in their own right.  
Even if they are not eager to make assertions or do not wish to send out letters, they serve as clear mirrors reflecting the world.  
Blue things glisten blue, and red things glow red, even while they put forth all their efforts to create theater, that is their pride and their honor.  

If they wish to depart, they do so. Yet, if they wish not to, they will stop. When they desire to depart, they will surely go, and should they wish not to, they will seemingly struggle to stay; this is their nature, and also their characteristic.  
But beyond this stubbornness, there is a generosity that many of them possess without concern.  
This is a beautiful virtue that only those who espouse the wandering spirit can have.  

It is inevitable that an individual carries the temperament of another.  
Mr. Ji Du-han, a notable figure in theater history, embodies laughter, while Mr. Mun Su-il in the theater market brings with him the essence of a tiger; and Mr. Seok Heung-jo of the Golden Seat embodies puppetry.  

Naturalists seem to imagine flowers blossoming, while aestheticists say that flowers smile.  
Women in India claim the inability to marry as a common hardship, while medieval ascetics exalt the single life as the pinnacle of faith.  

Thus, everyone’s temperament is different, and each carries varying degrees of honor while still holding the characteristics of each personality. However, the concerns of the Korean people, who cannot solely possess the complexities of character, cause a dilemma.  
A comedic actor must be that person; a tragic actor must also be that individual.  
Only then can they truly become masters of acting.  

Recently, there are rumors about Lee Gwang-soo of the Chosun Ilbo, claiming he occupies seven or eight seats while struggling. Yet, that matters little.  
The truth is that it should be those in the Korean performing arts who truly find themselves in a struggle.  

There is both sorrow and happiness among the Korean performers; if they can endure this with resilience, they will naturally rise to the status of acclaimed actors.  
Unbeknownst to it, they seem to be equipped to become a character corporation or character investment firm. This delineates the line that separates Korean actors from those of other countries.  

甲 "Is Gyeongseol good at acting?"  
乙 "Gyeongseol, that’s tragic acting."  
丙 "Don't you know he does well in comedies as well?"  
甲 "Either way, a master actor must shine in any role."  

The decisions made by them reverberate from the first act to the last, bustling with vibrancy.  

I have had the following thoughts. Although they may be meaningless,  
It is often said that a person famed in the world lives through a single character, suggesting that a hundred years of human life may be too short.  
Yet Korean actors carry within them the essence of a hundred people, and they must establish their names amidst the limited scope of time; this is not only a testament to their talent but poses an unreasonable burden.  

Korean actors courageously accept this struggle. However, if they succeed, they may even seize the honors that should belong to a hundred or even a thousand.  

It could be that there are only one or two who attain that scholarly status.  
Philosopher, literary doctor… what of those who have acquired expertise in songwriting or painting? Would not others claim their accomplishments? It’s hard to imagine.  
Thus, if a Nobel Prize is awarded, it would conventionally be granted to four or five individuals, and this is the fortune of humans.  
Such is the caprice of fate.  

One must also place decorations based on their profession; while the heart may ironically reflect one way, the face, back of the head, belly, buttocks, and even the soles of the feet could end up covered as well — would that not be ludicrous?  
However, without such ambition, it is also impossible to reach greatness.  

This, however, represents a significant dilemma. Herein lies the true tragedy of the Korean actors:  
To speak of character bankruptcy. What are the consequences? One should know what lies beneath that inquiry.  

There exists a secret within magazines called reads (讀物), and within theaters, a mysterious concept called ticket (興行向).  
These two are essentially similar, emphasizing trading in only monetary terms.  
Indeed, this represents the essence of character bankruptcy.  

Before the budding of profiteers in theater — before this "professionalism" dragged theater into a specific boundary — feelings were solely tied to deep emotions.  
Yet now, it is no longer so, for theater is traded at thirty or fifty percent off.  
The smooth implementation of this trade falls under the duties of the ticket office.  

For example, even if an English speaker appears in "Chunhyangjeon," or if a person with a topknot is introduced into "Prosecutor" or "Friend Flower Garden", as long as they cater to the tastes of those purchasing the theater, it does not matter.  
In essence, those who purchase theater will find themselves at the door once more.  

Here, the tradition of theater is shattered and broken.  
Where then shall we lead this shipwrecked vessel, laden with grievous injury?  
And where shall we salvage their pride and honor, knowing they must act despite any circumstances?  

The lifeblood of theater lies in its popularity, as is a classic adage.  
These days, the status of money has considerably risen.  
Why would it not?  

Indeed, it appears that even I understand the rhetoric that if things must go, at least let us gather some earnings.  
However, the general trend does not yield easily.  

Monthly meal expenses are twenty won.  
Night meal expenses are thirty jeon.  
Annual clothing, two pieces — apart from these, there seems to be no means to financially support them.  
Currently there is none, nor will it be in the future.  
They will have to return to their rightful fame.  
To return to fame implies recognizing the value of art over money.  
Herein lies true pride.  

And again, the sun sets.  
And again, the flowers fall.  
As this twilight, that lively harbor, sees ships prepare to depart.  
Already the sound of anchors is heard.  
The rowers gaze at the sky, prognosticating the night’s omens.  
Even the stones seem to weep,  
And twilight weeps once more.  

**Summary**  

This article reflects on the struggles, aspirations, and artistic identity of Korean theater artists during the era of Japanese colonization. It highlights the profound emotional and existential challenges they face, describing their roles in society as both burdens and powerful representatives of cultural pride. The mention of theater as an embodiment of personal and collective integrity suggests a nuanced view of resistance and resilience, vital for understanding the cultural landscape of colonial Korea. Historians might find the reflections on the importance of character and the moral dilemmas faced by artists particularly revealing of the sociopolitical context of the time.
