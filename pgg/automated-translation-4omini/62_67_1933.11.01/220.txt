기사제목	抱腹絶倒, 八道사투리 좌담회 (Laughing to the Belly's Fullness: Discussion on Dialects from Eight Provinces)  
필자	金玉斌 (Kim Ok-bin)  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue)  

As autumn deepens and the nights grow longer, how shall we pass these long autumn nights without boredom? Simply reading old novels may not suffice. Gathering men and women, young and old, even children, to listen to humorous old tales told in dialects from the thirteen provinces might be a way to avoid boredom. Therefore, I plan to share old sayings in the thirteen dialects. However, verbal expression carries the essence of feelings and emotions that cannot be captured fully with writing. A local accent makes even a common saying sound more amusing. It’s essential to convey those accents in writing, yet no amount of writing can truly imitate the nuances of dialects. Regardless, compelled by the magazine's request, I take up my pen. 

Incidentally, humorous tales require a sprinkling of wit, akin to seasoning good meat. Without it, one might say it would taste bland. Furthermore, our country's standard language is based on the Seoul dialect, while many regions have their own dialects. They can differ so greatly that even within a single nation, as in the case of Chinese people, communication can require interpretation to some extent. In terms of dialect use, Hamgyeong-do, Gyeongsang-do, and Jeolla-do show significant variations, followed by Pyeongan-do. Yet here, we will not specify people from any particular province but will instead engage in a discussion using letters like A, B, C, D, etc., allowing us to distinguish each speaker’s provenance.

Date: October 10  
Location: Chundangdae  
Participants: In a certain old man's living room  

A: Everyone, please enjoy your meal.  
B: How are you doing?  
C: Oh, Mr. D, you arrived rather late today.  
D: Where could I possibly be?  
E: Don't joke around. The weather has been too mild.  
B: Well, if the weather is like this, I could probably finish my meat.  
F: It indeed feels like winter. The weather has become rather strange.  
G: When did you arrive? Did you have a meal before coming over?  
H: That gentleman doesn’t know much. Let’s all sit together and enjoy. What are you doing here?  

(Laughter from everyone)

A: How about we share some old sayings?  
B: I remember something funny from my youth. Back when I lived in Kimje, I went to Seoul to buy some beans at the market, but the vendor didn’t understand what "beans" were and started crying. Later, when I pointed at the beans, he finally hugged his belly and laughed. I left without buying any beans because it was so funny. The next day, I attempted to buy beans again, thinking of how to say "beans" as "kong" and ended up causing another hilarious misunderstanding. I returned home without beans again.  

C: Let me share a story too. I once went to a friend's house in Hamgyeong-do and almost lost my life trying to figure out directions. The owner instructed his wife to call out loudly but it sounded so hilarious to me.  

Additionally, we made a toast together and celebrated, but when I ordered jjigae, they seemed to think my command was quite peculiar.  

The next day when we went to the market, the vendors were all men. The way they spoke was quite amusing. “Hey, isn’t that fellow quite comical?” “This traveler is quite entertaining,” they'd say among themselves. It was all very funny!  

E: Are those people really from our country? By the way, you’ll come to visit again soon, right?  

H: We've mostly been discussing southern dialects, but it’s all been fun, so it doesn’t matter.  

I: You know, saying "hey" [nonsense word] when a friend arrives is a playful type of greeting in our dialect. When greeting them, instead of saying "stranger," we use "hey."   

Here, a conversation has come to a pause.  

Moderator A: Now, let’s each share one famous story from our province in turn.  
B: I shall start. In Pyongyang, there’s a legendary figure known as Kim Bong-woong, who was quite impressive. One time, he went to the countryside with friends. They resided in an impressive tile-roofed house where there was a big child around fifteen years old. Everyone spoke highly of Kim Bong-woong’s wisdom, and they joked, “If you treat him to something, we can feast together.” As Kim Bong-woong was curious, he went to the house to see the child. The homeowner asked what he needed, and Kim Bong-woong said, “As your family has a child, I heard there’s a feast today!” The homeowner, rather confused, reluctantly allowed him to come in and prepare a meal.  

C: In the Yeongnam region, there’s a person named Jeong Man-se who’s known for being a very clever and vigorous man. Let me share a short story about him.  

He was heightening his reputation when the local magistrates were aware of him. One day, Jeong Man-se suggested to the magistrate, “You should really get married.” The magistrate was hesitant but said to him, “There aren't any good matches around.” Jeong Man-se replied, “There’s a widow in our town with a decent fortune and excellent looks. What about her?”  

The magistrate, who had his own reservations, finally relented after much discussion. 

Isn’t that quite an ingenious scheme?  

As night falls, all the participants begin to take their leave, wishing each other well on their journeys home until next time.  

**Summary**  

This article captures a humorous gathering among men and women across different provinces of Korea as they share comical stories in their local dialects. It reflects the cultural significance of dialects as a source of laughter and camaraderie. The mention of local customs and anecdotes provides a unique glimpse into the social dynamics of early 20th-century Korea, pointing out the regional distinctions that persisted even under colonial rule. Historians may find interest in how language and humor served as both a unifying and distinguishing element among Koreans during this period.
