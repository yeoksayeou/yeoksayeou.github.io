龍岡民謠  

Ah! Come, come to the house of the elder brother's husband.  

Sleep and stay, eat the olbaesong (송편) rice cake.  

Whether you will stay overnight or take off,  

Whether you will eat the olbaesong and then go.  

Be mindful of the wife and husband's eyes.  

  

Mama, mama, I have been scolded.  

Don't stand in the way at the Samhwa family (三和家庭).  

Water has been poured, the new plaster (새발라서) applied,  

It's a place where a bride cannot be married.  

What about the barley rice like sand?  

Can I eat just the accessories (반찬)?  

I secretly turn over the beak pillow.  

Do not speak without care!  

— Do not speak without care, it belongs to the hands of Mul-rae (물래의 손잽이) —  

  

My father has the characters, yet 

He has not sent even a single letter.  

The elder sister’s quality is also poor,  

Even a pocket of money can't be gathered.  

  

Cousin, cousin,  

How many months will it be until you marry?  

With twelve layers of skirts,  

Tears will flow, and the soup will overflow.  

The silver ring of twelve nyang,  

Melted with a runny nose.  

  

Heading to the sea, heading to the sea,  

I’ll go to Ilwolbong (日月峯) to the shore.  

The sun setting on Ilwolbong  

Will include the rear of the bronze tray.  

Even today, toward the young man,  

Who will go to the youth?  

O maiden of Amori (아모리)  

Will fetch water at Okdangchun (옥당춘).  

I will not be able to cook at Salmunpat (살문팟).  

At Apnam Mountain (압남산), with a plow,  

— The scythes —  

Scythe it there, even if half the garment  

Cannot be worn properly.  

   

On the bright moon of the third month,  

The external energy will come and go.  

The clear blue sky of the hundred days,  

I am alone crying, where will I go?  

Tonight again, it’s the night of the musics.  

The official arrives.  

My father, the late Minmun-rae (민문래),  

Why do you not return even after death?  

  

**Summary**  

The folk song "Dragon Valley Folk Songs" reflects the social life and challenges of rural Koreans in the 1930s. It portrays traditional customs, family obligations, and communal relationships while highlighting the difficulties faced by women, notably in marriage and household management during a time of colonial oppression. Historians may find the expressions of socio-cultural norms and the emotional resonance within such songs indicative of the broader struggles of Korean communities under Japanese rule.
