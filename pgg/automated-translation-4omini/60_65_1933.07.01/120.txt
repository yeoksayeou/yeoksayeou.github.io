Looking Through the Window at This Situation  

First Scene

As I passed by the stream of OO-dong, the window opened at the back alley. It was 9 PM. 9 PM on a summer night isn’t really late.

Looking down, there were various belongings cluttered in a small room, likely belonging to a man from the countryside. On the table covered with a cloth, a bowl of rice was heating up. From the hearth, the sound of boiling can be heard.

In the lower room, a somewhat irritated woman around 30 was comforting her crying baby.

“Mama, I want to eat... Huh-huh.”  

“Just wait… Your father will come soon; we need to eat together… Isn’t that right?”  

“Really… Huh-huh.”  

“We can’t do that… How could you eat alone when your father hasn’t come home yet? Huh?”  

Despite this, the baby continued to cry stubbornly.

The woman, out of desperation, fed the child. As she was doing so, perhaps feeling angry—she looked very hungry—she began to complain to herself. Ultimately, the complaints revolved around her husband coming home late.

Just then, a man, presumably her husband, dressed in a shabby coat, stumbled in. Upon seeing this, the woman’s expression changed instantly to one of annoyance.  

“Oh, just great… What on earth is in this place?”  

The man, wobbling, collapsed onto the floor of the living room.  

“Where did you go drinking again?”  

“Where? Haha?”  

“There aren’t only one or two bars in Seoul, so who knows where you went?”  

“Good job on that… I have been waiting for you, hungry, holding onto my stomach.”  

“Hmph. Who said not to eat?”  

“Don't worry. I’m not even sure anymore… Let’s see when you’ll get your salary.”  

“Why? It is my money that I earned.”  

Thus, a quarrel ensued. However, both parties seemed to be genteel, as they did not hurl insults but exchanged harsh words. The woman pushed the table, and the man made a noise as he flopped down.

The woman, striking the baby, quieted down again, laid back down, while the man fell asleep in the other room. 

This is how it ended. Perhaps by dawn, the woman may have thrown a blanket over the man.

Second Scene

In a cultural housing of XXX, a 40-year-old gentleman, who only wears a “fundoshi” (Japanese traditional loincloth), carelessly tosses his hefty body down onto a tatami mat. Honestly, his stout form resembles that of a character from *Charles* (a popular figure, likely). 

The woman, probably returning after an evening bath—women coming back from bathing often seem attractive, yet too overtly stated would cause scandals—can be seen. 

Although no words were audible, her movements were quite elegant, reminiscent of a motion picture. Hence, it is not that there isn’t anything to see.

The woman fans the man, who’s looking relaxed, with a handheld fan. 

The man gazes at her with a grin. After a moment, he pinches her by the arm with his finger.

The woman laughs softly and bows her head in modesty. It is cute even to outsiders. Especially to her husband… oh.

Yet suddenly, what is this! The woman, who has always been obedient, suddenly jerks her arm away and stands up.

Just at that moment, the lights flicker off.

“Hmph, if that’s the case…” 

Muttering this to myself, I turned to leave.

Third Scene

This is XX-dong—a place infamous for student boarding houses. 

One room immediately catches the eye, presumably that of a female student. A skirt and blouse hang on the wall, while the dressing table is more elaborately furnished than a department store's cosmetics display. 

The characters here are a young female student—perhaps 19 or 20—and a male student—around 23—who is an athletic type (from a vocational school, surely).

As I observe, the female student, who is reading a Japanese women’s magazine, is now leaning towards a male student who seems to be in her presence.

“Hello.”  

“Hello.”  

“It’s hot.”  

“Yes, it has gotten quite hot.”  

They each formally exchange pleasantries and take their seats next to each other. 

“What’s that magazine?”  

“It’s XXXX.”  

“Yes... in fact, Japanese magazines are better. I read *Chungang Gongron* (Central Discussion) diligently, but compared to that, Korean magazines are nothing.”  

“Women’s magazines are the same… I just like to look at them…”  

“Do you ever read music magazines?”  

“Yes... please play me a tune.”  

“What? Can I?”  

Amid refusal, she opens a case and takes out a violin. After applying rosin to the bow, she tunes the strings. 

Although I wouldn’t say it was good, she plays *Humoresque* with some flair. 

The female student giggles and listens closely, her cheeks flushed. 

While this is happening, the distance between the two students diminishes subtly. 

Once the performance ends, a flurry of compliments and modest refusals followed, and a silence ensued for a while. 

For about three minutes, the silence continued. It seemed the male student was gathering courage to speak and raised his head. 

“Uh… did you see the letter?”  

The female student quietly replies, keeping her head down. 

“Yes.”  

Once again, a hesitant silence lingered. The male student places his hand over the female student’s hand. 

There is no resistance—both are gentle. Their bodies draw closer together. 

And then their lips meet.

Fourth Scene

This is XX-dong, the neighborhood—everyone knows it, famous for “colorful” establishments. 

In the center of a drinking room, under insufficient lighting, several gentlemen are gathered. 

1. Mr. XXX from XXXX

2. Mr. XXX from XXXXXX

3. Mr. XXX from XX

4. Mr. XXX from XXX

They are all mildly intoxicated. Suddenly, a shrill cry, akin to tearing silk, reverberates through the air.

Upon closer inspection, one gentleman’s hand was caught unexpectedly on one of the ladies (31 hurried and there’s some kind of allegations). 

After some time, one of them embraces a lady and asks for a kiss. 

The toadstool flower also must bloom. One must not refuse the butterfly that comes.

With exaggerated gestures, they showcase a hundred and twenty percent of charm and coyness. 

If I stay longer watching those gentlemen, I might see two of them too drunk to spend the night there, but... it really doesn’t make a difference.

Fifth Scene

This is in the large house of XX-dong, the cramped alley.

A window opened, and, in the dim light of an oil lamp, a woman around 30, holding a child resembling a porcupine, is saying goodbye to a man. 

About five minutes later, her husband, dressed in a work uniform, stumbles in, already intoxicated.

“Did you drink again?” 

This was the woman’s reprimand.

“What if I did? Did you buy it?” 

This was the response from the “husband’s” audaciousness. 

“Don’t you know it’s nighttime... and the family has small children? Should they suffer?” 

“Why should I care? Let them die.”  

“Why die? Why die? What did I do wrong?” 

“Hmph, don’t you know? I know exactly what you do.” 

The woman seems dummyfounded, acting as if she’s about to cry, throwing the accusations reluctantly. 

Meanwhile, a crumpled bill drops from her clothes. The man quickly picks it up.  

“Where did this money come from?”   

“Wherever it came from…”  

“Hmph, I know.”  

Saying this, the man slyly opens the door to leave while adding,  

“I am thirsty and it seems I’ll go drink some makgeolli.”  

At this, the woman tries to hold him back, but a man’s strength is superior. While she cursed against him, he leisurely walks out, drunk.

  

**Summary**

This piece comments on the struggles of various social classes during the 1930s in Korea. Through multiple scenes, it depicts the lives of a struggling working-class family, domestic quarrels stemming from substance abuse, and the fleeting moments of intimacy among students. This vivid portrayal of everyday hardships against the backdrop of colonial life in Korea encourages readers to reflect on socio-economic disparities and the emotional toll faced in personal relationships. Historians might find the interplay between the private and public spheres in a colonial setting particularly revealing of the societal stresses of the time.

그러니까 여인은 목을 매는 듯이 매달닌다. 그러나 기운은 남자가 더 센 법이다. 등뒤에 대고 욕을 하는 것도 들은체 아니하고 그 양반은 (막걸리를 머그러) 유유히 밧그로 나가바린다. (單S)＜33＞
