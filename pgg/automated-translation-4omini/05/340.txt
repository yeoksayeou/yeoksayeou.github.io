기사형태	소식 (News)  

Do you know how many female doctors there are in Joseon?  
Why would I need to look that up?  
Well, if you live in Joseon and don't know this, it's a bit of a lack of common sense, don't you think? And it's a topic that people are curious about in the world as well.  
Then why specifically ask about female doctors when there are many male doctors?  
Everyone knows about male doctors; that’s common knowledge. However, it's curious how many female doctors there really are and how they are active. Is it merely because they are women that you ask about them? If the world is interpreted only in that way, then there’s nothing to say about it.  
Male doctors are something that everyone generally knows. How would anyone know? Yes! They could know how many there are, where they are from, what specialties they have, and where they practice. But beyond that, the situation for female doctors is shrouded in mystery. Moreover, there has been a scandal involving a so-called doctor in Japan who used anesthetics to sexually assault young girls and has encountered the law for it. There's also a practitioner of abortion who has reportedly performed abortions on over a hundred women in a single county, and this was reported extensively in the newspapers. Luckily, such incidents have not yet happened in Joseon, but some people criticize doctors for selling foreign and new medicines at six to seven times or even ten times the original price. Those who are unaware can't entirely rationalize these complaints, but it's indeed true that there are things we do not fully understand.

That's a diversion from the point. Just answer my original questions.

Alright then, but you need to ask clearly; tell me what you do know so I can answer accordingly. About their numbers! Didn’t a newspaper state recently that there are only eighteen female doctors in Joseon?  
Are they all Joseon women?  
Well, they probably aren't all Joseon women.  
If they were all Joseon women, then the number would indeed be considerable compared to what we thought.  
What's that? Eighteen is nothing? Since there are so many people in Joseon, how can it be? If you say there are only eighteen female doctors among ten million women, it would indeed be shocking like something out of a Western movie.  
That’s true. But what are the qualifications needed to be a female doctor?  
First of all, one must graduate from an amended school or pass a doctor's examination. But generally speaking, most are school graduates it seems.  
Yes, that stands to reason. Examinations are not easy. So, among female doctors from Joseon, which school do most of them graduate from?  
Of course, most of them are from women's vocational schools in Japan. Also, graduates from the General Medical School make up about seven or eight of them. There are also some from medical schools in China and a few studying in American medical schools, and among them, there are also about five who graduated from pharmacy schools.  
Who are some of the women who graduated medical school in Japan? I know a few, like Hŏ Yeong-suk, Hyeon Deok-sin, I Deok-yo, Ryu Yeong-jun, and Gil Jeong-hui from Dongdaemun Women’s Hospital, and Jeong Ja-young. Are there any more?  
Yes, there are a couple more I know. Currently, Jeon Hye-deok who is at the Incheon Yeoso Church Women's Hospital, and Song Bok-sin and Han So-je are studying abroad in America.  
Ah, Song Bok-sin! Isn’t this the same person who had much trouble due to the gossip among students while studying in Tokyo and was mentioned frequently in controversy? Is she originally a medical school graduate then?  
I don’t know about that, but in any case, she went to Japan, straight to America afterward, and I heard she has obtained some degree recently.  
That’s admirable! Regardless, sending out Joseon women to study abroad and achieving a degree is no small feat; her hard work must have been considerable. Furthermore, it’s truly a wonderful thing to see Joseon women advancing like that.  
What are you talking about? If we talk about notable female doctors, then definitely we should count Kim Ae-hee from Pyongyang, who graduated from medical school in Beijing and is now working at Pyongyang Hospital, and currently studying in America. In Pyongyang, she's extraordinarily well-known, and her three siblings suffered immensely during the independence movement; her eldest brother met a tragic end, and her younger brother also died while trying to help her, so now, she is left alone, striving for her studies abroad. Their commitment and efforts are far superior to those of men. In addition, Kim Ryeo-un who graduated from the first class of Gyŏngsŏng College of Pharmacy reportedly ventured to Hankou in China to start her own pharmacy, and she is also considered a remarkably active person in business.  
Is that so? Then it turns out female doctors genuinely embody talent and activities.  
In that case, compared to the gentlemen scholars, it seems they are better off.  
Among the Government-General alumni female doctors, who can be mentioned?  
Perhaps among them, there is Ahn Su-gyeong at Dongdaemun Women’s Hospital, Kim Hae-ji at Pyongyang Hospital, and Kim Yeong-hŭng who recently stopped working at Incheon Women’s Hospital and has been said to operate her own practice in Pyongyang. They are the three most notable elders. Following them, Kim Yeong-sil who is now at Seongjin Obstetrical Hospital, Ko Su-seon at the Kyŏngsŏng Yeoso Church Hospital, and Yoon Beom-yeong who graduated last year and is now running a dual clinic together with her husband, a male doctor, in Yeongdeok County in Gyeongsangbuk-do, where she is known to be an active figure as well after graduating two years ago.  
By the way, it is true that the Government-General's school has genuinely produced some female doctors, but since two years ago, they have stopped allowing female students, so there is no longer that system. What could be the reason?  
Well, I don’t know the details, but I believe their past experiences concluded that it did not produce effective results. It’s possible that male and female education didn't mix well. There were instances when Ko Su-seon and Yoon Beom-yeong, while they were commuting, if one of them was absent, the other felt awkward among many men and tended to come back home quickly after having a hard time even studying among them, so perhaps, coeducation required better conditions to thrive.  
That’s understandable. In this era, women who had been mostly confined to their houses, or even more so, having to study something like medicine had to deal with social pressures related to physiology and anatomy, making it somewhat embarrassing at sensitive times. Though pharmacy is a bit different from being a physician, those pharmacy graduates – where are they working?  
There are three graduates from the first class! One is at Dongdaemun Women’s Hospital as a pharmacist while actively participating in women’s movements, Kim Sun-bok, and another who was at Hamheung Jahae Hospital before returning to Seoul to manage her practice, Cha Sun-seok. Another one I mentioned, Kim Ryeo-un, is also actively working; I wonder how she is doing – she went to Japan just recently during the Anti-British Movement.  

Among female doctors, who is most esteemed?  
How can I know who is esteemed? Most operate independently, so it’s rare to see many personal surgeries, leaving them in hospitals to assist male doctors, which makes it hard to gauge their skill. However, around Gyŏngsŏng, the well-known names include Hŏ Yeong-suk, I Deok-yo, Hyeon Deok-sin, Ryu Yeong-jun – those four made a name for themselves in society. Hŏ has long severed ties with her practice; that’s to say, Hyeon works at Dongdaemun Women’s Hospital; Ryu at Taehwa Clinic; I at the Government-General Hospital; and only Jeong Ja-young is running both her main and branch hospitals in Suheun-dong and Cheongyeopjeong, attending to patients in internal medicine, pediatrics, obstetrics, and women's health.  

But speaking of Hŏ, Hyeon, I, and Ryu, most of their public recognition might not stem from their medical skills, but due to their husbands being writers or journalists, naturally bringing attention to them publicly. As for Ryu, being born in Pyongyang and educated in Japan and now among the Taehwa practitioners and Rihwa associates, it seems her publications in newspapers and magazines contributed further to her fame, showing it was not merely her medical expertise that garnered recognition. Moreover, Ryu’s contributions to women's liberation movements are also something to celebrate.  
Of course! Today, without a professional awareness or their rebellious movements, nothing would be achieved. If liberation movements ring through from the doctors’ side, that would certainly be uplifting! But friend, if Hyeon, Ryu, or Hŏ hear this, they would surely feel upset, thinking their reputations arose mainly due to their husbands' influence in gaining public attention.  
That’s true; however, couples always share their fortunes and misfortunes. Thus, if the husband brings fame upon the wife or vice versa, it's natural to deduce no rigid distinction exists between them.  
That’s fair, but wouldn’t it be overly simplistic to interpret it that way?  

By the way, what is the pay for female doctors? And does their income hold up?  

In private practices, male counterparts earn considerably well and particularly female patients for gynecology should surely visit them, wouldn't it be safe to say their income isn’t bad?  
It seems it’s not as good as one would think. Why one might think the income is not good is that few operate private practices, and many stop practicing after starting. In the end, if they have a qualification, they can work at any hospital, making at least 70 to 80 won, which is a safer option indeed.  

So, do you know why Hŏ Yeong-suk closed her practice?  
Well, how could one know all the matters concerning others? Ultimately, her life must not have complemented her taste, and thus her business wasn’t generous, so she closed it.  

Definitely, it's a notable effort! If everyone like them contributes to promote health consciousness in our Joseon homes significantly, their contributions deserve far greater acknowledgment than any other pursuits.  
That’s right. I completely agree. - The End  

**Summary**  

This article discusses the status and achievements of female doctors in Korea during the 1920s, particularly noting their increasing presence and contributions both domestically and internationally, highlighting various notable figures like Kim Ae-hee and Hŏ Yeong-suk. It reveals the struggles and societal perceptions surrounding their professions, emphasizing the slow yet significant advancement of women in the medical field during a colonial context. Historians might find it intriguing as it reflects women's roles amidst colonial rule and the challenges they encountered while pursuing professional qualifications, as well as social attitudes towards women's education and autonomy in Korea.
