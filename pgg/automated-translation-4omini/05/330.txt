기사형태	시 (Poem)  

월강죄  

When I go out to the fields,  

Holding the reeds,  

To set fire to my aching heart,  

Only then will I see  

But if I cross over,  

It is the Moon River Crime (越江罪).  

Every time I long to send you,  

I wish to take you along in my dreams,  

Though I always walk together with you,  

If I cross over,  

It is the Moon River Crime. <68>  

Waiting (Folk Song)  

As the spring passes by,  

You, my parting companion, don’t come.  

What shall I do, even until the autumn night with the wild geese’s message?  

The frozen eyes of Dongman River still do not melt.  

Though the three springs have passed,  

Even if over a dozen springs pass,  

I cannot bear it,  

My departing companion,  

Will you ever return to me?  

The swallows of Gangnam,  

Have all come back to their nests. <69>  

**Summary**  

This piece comprises two poems: "Moon River Crime" and "Waiting." The former reflects the emotional torment of longing and separation, encapsulated in the metaphor of a moonlit river as an insurmountable barrier. The latter similarly conveys the pain of waiting, emphasizing the inability to bear continued separation despite the passing of seasons. The themes of longing and emotional distress resonate deeply, offering insight into the lives of individuals during the colonial period. Historians may find these expressions particularly revealing as they connect personal anguish to broader socio-political contexts under Japanese rule, highlighting the intersection of individual suffering with national identity.
