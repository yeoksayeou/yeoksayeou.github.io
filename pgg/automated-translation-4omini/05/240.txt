기사제목	笑話二題 (Two Jokes)  
기사형태	잡저 (Essay)  

A man from Seoul, unable to catch a pheasant while hunting all day, saw a villager standing by a ditch and said, "I will give you fifty jeon if you can help me catch that chicken with your gun." He handed the fifty jeon to the villager and, after several attempts, managed to catch one chicken. 

Feeling emboldened, he said, "Sir, please help me catch one more." He gave another fifty jeon, and after catching a second chicken, he became increasingly greedy. "Sir! Please let me catch the last remaining chicken as well." The villager replied, "You can catch as many as you like, but that chicken seems to be returning to its owner who is coming home." 

A hunter named A saw fisherman B and said, "Don't you have just one eye? You surely see only half as well as we do." B replied, "No, even if I have only one eye, I can see twice as much as you." A responded, "Is that really so?" 

B then said, "If you think I see twice as well, then how many eyes do you see me with?" A replied, "You surely can't see with just one!" B responded, "But to me, your two eyes look like four! Now, let's hurry to the tavern."

**Summary**  

The article presents two comical anecdotes that illustrate human folly and misunderstandings in social interactions. The first joke revolves around a man who mistakenly assumes he can outsmart a villager in hunting, only to be reminded of the consequences when the owner's return is hinted at. The second joke showcases a clever exchange about perception and the nature of sight, revealing the humorous dynamics between the characters. This reflects the societal roles and interactions during the colonial period in Korea, highlighting themes of class difference, humor, and the simple yet profound misunderstandings that persist in everyday life. Historians may find the representation of social class and the blending of rural and urban interactions particularly interesting as it showcases the cultural nuances of the era.
