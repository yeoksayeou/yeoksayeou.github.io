잡지명	별건곤 제5호  
발행일	1927년 03월 01일  
기사제목	地下國訪問記, 所謂 富 所謂 貧者 諸君에게 (Record of Visiting the Underground Country, To the So-Called Rich and So-Called Poor)  
필자	먹적골 許生  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)  

Inside the belly, I hear the sound of gurgling. The rich man pretends not to hear, busy with his affairs, coming and going. As he is afraid of right and wrong, he cannot commit suicide, nor do anything else, nor can he complain, nor even speak out of anger. The fact that I am in such a miserable state is indeed unfortunate. 

In hard times, even following behind a chick makes no sense. Where am I to go, whether to the Underground Country or to the Sky Country? There's neither a word for 'dead' nor 'alive.' Am I playing chess with Akamatsu or enjoying the moon with Li Taibai? Am I in the Beoribong of Geumgangsan or the main peak of Hallasan? Whether I am in the East Sea or the Southern Sea? Am I in a one-room hut in Mukjeok-dong, where it's impossible to find traces at all? 

Ah! It seems to be there. Let’s head there. The Teacher spoke of wealth and honor as floating clouds and of fame and merit as worn-out shoes, and I doubt that I will find them in this world. Moreover, that place is one that the Teacher pioneered himself, where wealth could be disregarded and is one of the three sacred mountains, a land where immortals reside. It certainly seems to be there. It is likely to be among the crater where the 청학 (blue crane) cries and 백록 (white deer) jumps, somewhere in the hall of Hallasan, with its charming scenery. Let's head there. 

"Though it may be extremely rude for a being from the mundane world to visit the celestial realm, I have come a great distance for important matters, so please do not dirty your experience by despising me... I carry the burdens of my poverty, the grumbling of all my penniless comrades, and the complaints of all the rich worlds. Please tell me about your poor times and the times of your golden days. How did you gather and spend your fortune? Please provide me with specific examples to contrast the ways of the rich and poor, and teach me how to manage according to the teachings of your life." 

After a long silence, the Teacher slowly begins to speak, and indeed his heart opens up. 

"Is this indeed a mundane being? What need do we have to fear poverty? And why do we need to envy wealth? As people who come empty-handed and leave empty-handed… I, too, have lived enjoying freedom over poverty and wealth. Let me tell you about my poverty. At that time, I lived in a small house in Hyangnam-mukjeokdong, only reading books at night. My wife worked on sewing for others, barely managing to survive. She was so desperate, starving to the bone, that she begged me while crying, 'When hunger and cold are at the bone, how can you just read?' I replied lightly, 'I still haven’t finished my studies.' So, she angrily snapped at me, 'If we don’t do business or work, how could thieves not come?' and rebuked me accordingly. I thought, 'Alright, ten-year pact, yet seven years have passed.' Then I closed my book and left to the streets. 

I went to a known wealthy man, Byun, and suddenly requested ten thousand won. How must he have viewed me at that moment? Despite his esteemed guests, he immediately gave me the ten thousand won without question. With that money, I went to Ansung and traded for dates, chestnuts, pears, and dried fruits. When the fruit was suddenly in short supply nationwide, the price began to rise tenfold. I then took that money to try something bigger, setting out to Jeju-do for trading sea cucumbers. Amazingly, it again yielded tenfold profits. Then, through an introducer's recommendation, I obtained a sizable public work contract, gathering thousands of pirates from Byun-san to start cultivation. As it was new land being developed, it was astonishing how easily crops thrived there; within a few years, I harvested thousands of bags of rice. Right then, a famine broke out in Japan, leading to orders for the grains, so I sold them for over a million won. 

At that moment, I thought, 'Ah! Now I've tasted wealth. The test is over; let's return to a life of moderation.' I told my associates, 'I’m leaving now,' and threw fifty thousand into the sea, sharing the rest among the people, returning with only ten thousand with me to Gyungsil. Byun obstinately refused to take it back. Forced, I threw it to him and returned home; my wife, believing it was the day I departed, had already held a memorial service.

From here, I had dealings with Byun. Subsequently, through his introduction, a military general named Lee Hwan came to consult me on discussing the precious methods for the North invasion. Initially, I met his requests with skepticism, given the lack of sincerity among those seeking fame or power in our times. Although he pleaded, I provided several practical suggestions. Upon hearing my ideas, he lamented, admitting their substantial difficulty.

Eventually, I gathered the country's best and dressed them in Chinese clothes, sending them to Manchuria to wander among officials and citizens, probing their realities and seeking alliances with heroes, gradually assembling a grand plan to shine light on national disgrace. Upon uttering these words, the commander could only sigh and responded, 'In our land, the scholars adhere to propriety, how could they adopt Chinese dress and hair?' This too, he found difficult. Frustrated, I exclaimed, 'What does scholar mean? Fan Yuki didn't sacrifice his hair for personal grievances, and Zhao Wu-ling didn’t change his clothing for the strength of the nation. Now, to speak of national disgrace while refusing the Chinese attire, what can we accomplish?' I yelled, 'Cut off the crown prince's hair first!' and looked around, brandishing my sword. He was so mortified he fled in panic. 

After that, he continued to seek me for advice; however, none of the capable men had the will for action, consumed with either fame or riches, and this led me here, secretly lighting a fire at home, becoming unknown for three hundred years, singing with the natural world of heaven and earth.' As he continued to narrate his life, the common people could hardly even dream of such grand dialogues. 

Once again, he taught me ways to deal with all the rich and poor, revealing insights that left me breathless. 'One can be proud, yet once one has suffered hardship and accumulated wealth and gained experience, the essence of life will become evident easily. What is the essence of wealth? I do not oppose the notion of money outright; however, the idea of crying and dying over money, or dancing and laughing for it, is indeed absurd. Isn't human existence fundamentally about our basic needs? The world is inherently centered on economic issues. Still, a life spent crying over a single penny, or living and dying for money, such a miserable existence must be the evil of the overall situation. I wholeheartedly agree with the disastrous news about _____. This is likely to turn out that way, but I oppose the notion of _____. Isn’t that the cause of all the turmoil? 

I merely gathered wealth temporarily as an experiment; just the fact that I threw back a fortune after acquiring it proves that.' 

 

**Summary**

This piece reflects on the nature of wealth and poverty, offering a personal narrative from the author’s past as a contrast to contemporary social attitudes. The article poses philosophical questions about the values attached to material wealth and challenges the norms governing rich and poor individuals in society. For historians, this text highlights the socio-economic disparities in colonial Korea during the Japanese occupation and provides insight into the psychological and socio-cultural struggles, framing wealth not just in terms of money but as a complex societal construct linked to honor, identity, and personal philosophy.

지하국으로 차즐가? 천상국으로 차즐가? 죽엇단 말도 업고 살엇단 말도 업스니 어듸로 차즐가? 赤松子와 바둑을 노는지 李太白과 玩月을 하는지? 금강산 비로봉에 게신 지 한라산 제 일봉에 게신지? 동해에 노시는 지 남양에 노시는지? 一間斗屋으로 墨積洞에 그대로 안저 게신 지 그 종적을 도저히 알 수가 업다.
올치! 그 곳에 게실 듯하다. 그 곳으로 차저 보자. 선생이 본래 富貴如浮雲을 말하얏고 功名如弊履를 말하얏스닛가 이 속계에는 잇지 안을 것이다. 더구나 그 곳은 선생이 몸소 개척한 곳의 하나이요, 만금을 어덧다가 만금을 훗터버린 곳의 하나이요, 삼신산의 일이요, 진인이 상주하는 해도의 하나이니 그 곳에 분명 게실 듯하다. 즉 남해의 거도 제주의 한라산 그 중에도 청학이 울고 백녹이 뛰는 분화구 좌우안일 듯 하다. 그리로 찻기로 하자.

「속계 천인이 선경을 범함은 극키 황송하오나 긴요한 일로 천리를 불구하고 왓사오니 더럽다 마시고 밧어주십시오... 저는 제 貧寒에 대한 抑鬱과 모든 無産동무들의 苦情과 또는 모든 부자 세상의 모든 불공불평을 한 짐 걸머지고 선생님 전에 왓슴니다. 선생님의 빈곤시절을 말슴해 주십시오, 그리고 선생님의 황금대왕 시절을 말슴해 주십시오. 엇더케 모으시고 엇더케 쓰신 것을 자세히 말슴해 주십시오. 그리고 세상의 모든 빈자부자에 대한 처세법을 말슴해 주십시오. 빈자로는 엇더케 해야 될 것과 부자로는 엇더케 해야 될 것을 선생의 일생에 대조하야 구체적으로 들녀 주십시오.」
선생은 침묵 良久에 천천히 입을 열어 일장을 설파하시는데 과연 흉금이 通開해진다.
