기사제목	어려서 離婚한 夫婦가 戀愛로 다시 結婚 (Couple Divorced in Youth Remarry Through Love)  
필자	鄭寅翼  
기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Other)  

The train rumbled towards Seoul, its sound overwhelming the carriage's silence. It was well past midnight, and long after Daegu had come and gone, the tiredness of dawn began to dissipate. The boisterous young men from Gyeongsangdo gradually fell silent, one by one, nodding off into sleep. The cramped carriage was enveloped in slumber. Mun-ho also could not fight off the drowsiness and remained in his seat, dozing off. With so many people on board, it was particularly awkward for him as he sat beside a young woman. The fifth time he involuntarily nodded off, he suddenly leaned towards the young woman, who, having been startled awake by his heavy body, quickly opened her eyes. Mun-ho, realizing what had happened, felt a rush of embarrassment and apologized in a fluster, then awkwardly slipped out of the dimly lit carriage. The young woman, likely aware of how red her face had become from the surprise, hastily wiped her cheeks against the cool dawn wind outside the carriage window.

The sleep in the carriage was still deep. The four eyes of the two young people sparkled in the gloomy corner of the compartment. Thus, the two found themselves struggling against loneliness, and through that loneliness, they began to communicate.

After arriving in Seoul, they walked nervously through the bright morning streets. Surrounded by fresh air, they certainly appeared vibrant and happy to anyone who saw them. The two parted ways just outside Gwanghwamun. Mun-ho went to his lodgings in Tae-pu-dong, while the woman headed towards her home in Samcheong-dong. Thus began a series of happenings in which the young couple’s Western-style shoes could be seen at the wooden threshold of their respective homes. For a while, this created rumors that Mun-ho was in a relationship. Discussions about the woman’s potential marriage among her peers began to circulate.

After a long summer and what seemed like a late autumn, Mun-ho finally married Park Ae-kyung. It was as if the winds of fate had quietly swept in and out. The news of Mun-ho’s marriage spread when winter was approaching. Of course, Park Ae-kyung was the same young woman he had met for the first time in the carriage. However, the gods of fate were far too fickle, and there was nothing but chaos. It seemed absurd that two individuals who had separated through divorce could come together again through love and remarry. This fact, however undeniable, was still hard to accept. But it was true! Then, what could have led them to such a divorce in the first place?

Both Mun-ho and Ae-kyung belonged to prestigious families. They were treasured children who were loved dearly by their aged parents. At the tender age of twelve, both families arranged for the two children to be married, treating their union like a pair of dolls. The cry of the young groom and bride filled the two households with unparalleled joy. The bride was still so young that she only participated in the wedding ceremonies and returned to her parental home afterward. The young groom, Mun-ho, lived in Paju, while his bride, Ae-kyung, lived in Yeosan. They truly did not even know what each other looked like. Given the distance between their families, there was little opportunity for the young couple to meet or connect. For a year, they would only see each other once in a while, enjoying brief encounters like the stars on Chilsok night before parting once again.

Later, Mun-ho left this artificial life and moved to Seoul to pursue his education. A naturally studious individual, he successfully enrolled in Seoul High School after completing several tutoring sessions, graduating with high marks at the age of twenty. During this time, he read magazines and newspapers, which opened his eyes to the perceptions of the era. Eventually, he began to question whether his family background and marriage were truly fitting for the times. There were whispers of discontent regarding his arranged marriage from a partner he hardly knew, with proposals for divorce being persistently urged on him for years.

Of course, such claims did not gain traction, but they did hinder him from filing for divorce after reaching the age of marriage. After finishing high school, Mun-ho’s thirst for knowledge drove him to Japan. During his year in Japan, his father tried to invest money during the financial boom, losing all of their wealth, which eventually led to his father’s demise as well.

With no money left and now fatherless, Mun-ho faced an incredibly dark future, but he did not yield, spending seven or eight years as a working student. During this time, his home fell apart, with his mother moving to her parents, and he completely lost contact with his in-laws, resulting in a practical divorce. In Mun-ho's mind, the concept of marriage had disappeared long ago. 

After finishing school, Mun-ho returned to Korea and immersed himself in various endeavors aimed at establishing a life in his homeland. Meanwhile, after a long period of neglect, his health began to fail, which prompted his family and friends to send him to Dongnae for recuperation during the hot summer.

Grateful for their kindness, Mun-ho regained his health and one evening left Busan, heading back towards Seoul. That night happened to be the same night he would meet the ex-wife he divorced. 

Ae-kyung was seen as a mischievous girl in the eyes of the aristocratic families. If insulted, she would retaliate in kind, so much so that on multiple occasions, her parents scolded her with remarks like “the daughter of an aristocrat” and “who is she to be causing such problems?” Despite their criticisms and suppressions, Ae-kyung ignored them and spent her time joyfully playing with other neighborhood children in the hills.

As she entered adolescence, the notion of divorce from her clueless husband became ever more serious. Fiercely independent, Ae-kyung could not afford to be made to feel inferior by a husband who wanted a divorce. In the midst of this, her parents could not intervene, despite their anger, and so Ae-kyung’s family moved from Yeosan to Seoul. Around this time, Korea experienced a whirlwind of women's education reform, and Ae-kyung also began her studies at a girls' school.

Deviating from her parents’ expectations, she strove to excel academically. Each semester, she received excellent grades, improving herself steadily to avoid being overshadowed. Ae-kyung, who was quite appealing, garnered admiration and praise much like the peony blooming in spring.

As time passed, her parents passed away, leaving behind a younger sibling and a small inheritance while continuing to support their studies. This family, having lost the pillar that held it together, drifted like a lone boat in a tempest. The remaining land was feeble support for relatives, and as their financial situation worsened daily, Ae-kyung decided to take action to become the backbone of her family.

Through the introduction of her friends from school, she sought a teaching position at Jinju Normal School and even arranged to meet the school principal. However, feeling an unpleasant first impression, she abruptly rejected the job and headed back to Seoul. That very night was when she first reunited with Mun-ho.

Returning to the marriage they once severed, they found joy through love. They established their safe haven in the heart of Daegyeong City and continue to live their days filled with happiness from yesterday to today and into the future.

  

**Summary**

This article narrates the story of Mun-ho and Ae-kyung, who, despite their early divorce at the age of twelve, unexpectedly reconnect and remarry years later after their individual hardships. The piece reflects on their social standings, familial pressures, and the impact of education on their lives. It is particularly interesting as it hints at the broader social changes occurring in Korea during the colonial era, including the questioning of traditional arranged marriages and the emerging roles of women in society, demonstrating how personal relationships were transformed by socio-political influences. Historians might find it revealing as it reflects the tensions between traditional Korean values and modernity amidst the tumult of Japanese colonial rule.

어려서 離婚한 夫婦가 戀愛로 다시 結婚
						鄭寅翼
서울을 향하야 기차는 우렁차게 달렷슴니다. 車室안이 조용하야지면서부터 불까지 껌벅이엇슴니다. 밤은 子正이 훨신 넘은 뒤이며 길은 大邱를 지난지 이미 오랜 뒤이엇슴으로 새벽의 困한 긔분이 차차 지터가는 것이엇슴니다. 떠들고 수선피던 慶尙道 량반들도 하나 둘식 입을 버리고 코를 골기 비릇하엿슴니다. 좁은 車室에는 잠이 그둑히 찻슴니다. 文浩도 조름이 오는 것을 막어낼 힘이 업섯슴니다. 자리에 안진채 멧 차례 꾸벅꾸벅 조랏슴니다. 원톄 손이 만히 타서 비좁은 車間에 더구나 文浩는 젊은 女性과 자리를 가티 하게 되여 한층 불편을 늣기엇슴니다. 멧번째 不可抗力的으로 꾸벅어리던 文浩는 엽흐로 젊은 여성을 향하야 쓰러젓슴니다. 그 역시 잠에 醉하야 정신을 못 차리든 차에 무거운 文浩의 몸이 실임을 깨닷자 깜짝 놀나 눈을 떳슴니다. 文浩도 깜짝 눈을 뜨고 자지가 그에게 쓰러진 것을 깨닷자 북그러움과 미안함이 일시에 온몸을 싸고 도라 얼골이 붉어짐을 늣겻슴니다. 그리고 잘못되엿다는 인사를 한 후에 쪼끼는 톡기 모양으로 자리를 이러 어둠에 싸힌 車室 밧그로 뛰여 나갓슴니다. 그 여성도 졸다 들킨 것이 북그러웟던지 어둠에 까마케 물든 車窓을 치밀고 새벽 가볍고 싸늘한 바람에 그 얼골을 시치고 잇섯슴니다. 車室의 잠은 작고 깁허갓습니다. 두 사람의 네 개의 눈은 음침한 차실 한 구석에서 샛별과 가티 반작이엇슴니다. 이리하야 두 남녀는 외로움을 늣기고 그 외로움에서 이웃을 찻게 되여 交涉이 비릇하엿슴니다.
