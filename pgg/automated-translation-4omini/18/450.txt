기사제목	記者 大出動 1時間 探訪 大京城 白晝 暗行記 (Reporter Mass Mobilization: 1 Hour Exploration of the Great Capital, A Daylight Dark Journey)  
기사형태	세태비평 (Social Commentary)  

Reporter Mass Mobilization: 1 Hour Exploration of the Great Capital, A Daylight Dark Journey  
Unforeseen mobilization order! The essence of the order was to capture and write about a fleeting situation in the bustling city from 2:30 to 3:30. Where should I go and what kind of article should I handle? With such a restless thought, I set out with S. 

“Where are you going?” I asked S about the direction. “Well, I might go to the foreign diplomatic recruitment,” he said, presenting a perplexed demeanor. We passed the school district streets and came out onto the Jongno street. Crowds of people began to move in the spring air!! Modern girls and boys approached us from the other side. Streetcars and automobiles were rushing by. Wondering what story to cover, I split off from S and proceeded to the gambling house known as Gonggulni.

Though it appears advertised as an entertainment recreational area, in reality, it is an open gambling den. “Come in! Come in! Any entertainment you can engage in,” shouted a person looking as if he suffered from chronic bronchitis while playing the gramophone to draw in passersby. The time was 2:47! The room's entrance was packed with people. Fresh-faced youth who just arrived from the countryside, laborers, vagrants, and drifters were crowded together playing the game. They have written down the station names as Gyeongseong, Daegu, Pyongyang... and by rolling the balls toward the designated stations, they would win six times the amount they put in. Whether it was 2 or 10 jeon, they could play whatever amount they wanted.

I observed two individuals in their late teens, one a tall rural youth and the other perhaps some sort of useless consumer. They were rolling the balls, calling “Wonsan!” “Daegu!” “Pyongyang!” hoping to strike it rich by betting 10, 20, or 50 jeon. They repeatedly lost. With a forlorn expression, the unfortunate fellow became upset, remarking, “I already lost so much.” 

Well, I just witnessed him lose maybe 80 or 90 jeon within just 15 minutes. A little later, some shop employee arrived by bicycle, hesitated for a while, and then bought a ticket for 10 jeon, playing for some time but similarly lost. The youthful player continued as well, losing just as before. Meanwhile, the gramophone still played, “You know, young people, life is only once and if you die...” The music flowed fluidly through the air. 

The crowds pressed closely together as people jostled. The atmosphere became tense between the players and the proprietor. Eventually, a countryside youth who had lost all his funds exclaimed, “Please, I can’t anymore. Just give me 50 jeon.” “You can’t expect to get it. Go ahead and play again,” the proprietor responded firmly, indicating a refusal with a rather assertive attitude that suggests a long-standing operation. Clearly despondent, the young villager, now nearing defeat, turned away. 

I followed him and asked, “Where is your hometown?” He seemed surprised, “Why do you ask that?” Then when I did not answer, he replied, “It’s Seoncheon.” “What brings you here?” I questioned. “I came to take the entrance exam but ended up in this situation,” he shared, suggesting a twinge of sympathy as he spoke. “I don’t have a direct connection to your situation, but please refrain from making such choices in the future.” “I was just spectating, but I ended up being encouraged to play,” he offered in defense, continuing, “I only won 2 won the first time.” “Then that’s where you should stop,” I replied. “What’s with the greed?” He revealed a look of regret as he wandered away toward Changgyeongwon while I headed back to town, myself pondering, “If one goes about with an attitude of not working hard, how could they expect different outcomes?”

As the time slowly progressed, I realized I was producing no articles and gathering no materials. Looking at my watch, I cursed my bad luck, returning to the office when I spotted the countryside youth again at the Gonggulni gambling house, just as before.

Four Pillar Fortune Telling Group at Tapgol Park  
This was the peak hour for human interaction from 2:00 to 3:00 PM. A crowd of nearly 200 people had gathered in the park, also called Tapgol. The atmosphere in the park felt quite stagnant and chaotic, reminiscent of children playing in muddy water in May or June. Old men in hats and ragged clothes, children in colorful garments, middle school students, laborers, beggars, and street hawkers comprised the diverse groups of people lounging about or mingling as if waiting for something. 

A few were gathered in the octagonal pavilion, taking naps while others aimlessly wandered. Most congregated around fortune tellers, sitting on old benches. In the greenhouse area near the pavilion sat around 60 or 70 people. An old man with round glasses read aloud, expressing interest in “The Actual Account of the Flower Path.” Elderly men listened attentively while those unemployed gathered around. 

“It seems that Gongmyeong has outlived by fifty,” one said. “Zhang Fei also died prematurely,” said another. “Fifty isn’t too bad,” was a remark made that drifted through the crowd. Here, seemingly divorced from reality, these individuals deliberated over figures from a millennium ago, enjoying a recounting of tales from the Three Kingdoms Period. While urban life was swift, here it seemed as if another world existed. As I exited through the back gate, I noticed the three-story stone pagoda, which looked as if it was aged and crumbling. Nearby, the fortune teller was still engaged with clients. 

A fortune teller started conversing with a woman about her relationship. “I got married at 19 but soon after my wife left me—what should I do?” “Take the direction southwest,” replied the fortune teller. “Then probably towards Incheon,” speculated the crowd. “By the 4th or 5th month, my wife will return, or if not, I’ll find another.” It was indeed an enticing way of speaking.

People were around to observe these fortunes being told, collecting coins bit by bit. There were about 7 or 8 of them in the park. It seemed noteworthy that a 20-year-old country youth was involved. The sight of 40 or 50 individuals also visiting for fortune-telling was remarkable. As students walked past, one declared, “I would get married anytime,” while another elderly person remarked, “There are ways to live forever.” This highlights that here in the daytime, public fortune-telling remains prevalent as ever for poor gullible souls who seem unaware of the absurdity surrounding them.

At the main entrance, two well-dressed middle-aged women were smoking, casting suspicious glances at those entering and exiting.

As I strolled from the park, I regarded it as lacking in the facilities to truly be a relaxing spot. Perhaps its location near Jongno contributed to its inadequacy. It would have been better if it were kept up properly to offer some soothing breezes. 

Nevertheless, as I meandered within the park during that hour, I was left with a sense of weariness in a world without excitement. What remarkable events could occur in such a mundane and ordinary existence? The iron-barred gates of the park stood open, yet no one appeared to take their leave as if they, like dying animals, had no will to live! 

Strange Transactions  
At 2 o’clock! 

The streets of Jongno in Seoul were bustling with activity. Especially with spring here, the body shivers as it stirs, and people, along with their wives and husbands, young ladies and gentlemen, crowded together making it noisier than ever. 

As well, the station near the Korea Railway witnessed a throng of passengers heading in and out, crowding the ticket barriers, even without a chance to take a breath! 

Entering a bookstore, I noted that a pen was selling for 30 jeon. A thirty-jeon fountain pen! Someone appeared excitedly in a middle-aged overcoat, trying to capture the attention of potential customers by showcasing the pens laid out on a board. 

As I squeezed through the crowd at this bustling street, I noticed that just then a middle-aged man with a bowler hat paused to examine a pen. 

Suddenly, a shabby-looking youth wearing a white hat resembling a rural gentleman approached the fountain pen vendor and said, “Please give me one more pen for 10 jeon.” 

He was met with the vendor's indifference, “I cannot. I will only sell it for 2 won 50 jeon.” 

This caught my attention. I realized that this might be the street where such transactions occurred, amidst the hustle and bustle. Perhaps they were trying to avoid the attention of passersby. The shabby figure then explained, “I’m from Daejeon in Chungcheong Province, and I came to pick up my brother from Wonsan. I ran out of money and I have no option but to sell the glasses I bought for 21 won 80 jeon last fall.” 

The vendor continued ignoring him. The middle-aged gentleman began inspecting the glasses for quality, assessing them. With scrutiny, he remarked, “These appear to be real!” but still edged toward buying them.

The youth continued, “It’s genuine, I can assure you! I wish not to resort to this, but I need money for expenses!” After much deliberation, the middle-aged man conceded, “I’ll buy it for 2 won 50 jeon,” and handed over the money with hesitation. 

The youth, voice filled with awkwardness, said, “Please just give me one more ten jeon,” to which he was turned down once more. 

With that, this peculiar transaction was completed. The vendor continued to feign ignorance while they both wrapped the transaction in a sense of pity and regret. 

Time in the busy Jongno street! The crowds surged with elderly and young, city dwellers and villagers mixing together, as if amidst the flowing tide of the afternoon filled with such faltered trade transactions.

Judicial Circles within an Hour: A Millionaire's Daughter-in-Law at the Court  
At 2 PM, the mobilization order was issued, yet it would not be until 2:30 PM that I would set out. Knowing that my destination was the courthouse, I anticipated that if I weren't fast-paced, I could miss what might unfold there so I hurried with all my strength. However, the distance from the our office to the court is considerable, so regardless of how fast I might pedal, it took me 20 minutes to arrive. 

I slipped into the courtroom at 2:50 PM to find lawyers already making their exit. The bent figure of Lee Han-gil came into view, carrying a weight of experience as he hustled out behind some friends pursuing lively discussions about their cases. Meanwhile, I thought that I might have just barely missed out on key proceedings with the time slipping away. 

Having appropriately investigated the public areas of the courthouse, I sensed that the courtroom atmosphere was bleak—all sorts of activities I expected to witness were absent as only a couple of ineffectual barristers playing Go with a few prisoners occupied the space. While I thought to check the civil and criminal courts, I still found no significant cases unfolding. The court attendants merely customarily waited, quietly attending to their duties. 

I asked an attendant if there were any cases underway, and he responded, “Well, every court cannot have cases every hour or so—what could possibly be happening at a time like this?” I realized soon enough that I came too late. But rather than return home empty-handed, I directed myself around the courthouse gardens searching for anything of interest. 

Beside the detention facility, two officers were standing in the sunlight talking about the latest matters of the court. Just as I caught sight of their conversation, I felt they might just be sharing some gossip on recent cases. 

“I recall back in the past,” one officer said, “that a wealthy nobleman’s wife had shunned the court summons. She was involved in some fraud and continued to ignore the court demands, leaving one to inquire just how strong the law is versus well-placed coins.” 

This caught my attention, so I listened closer. Through this exchange, I gathered that the woman accused, known for her family wealth, had defied summons implying that money indeed carried weight within the legal system, even more than justice itself. 

It was during this court visit of 3:40 PM, that I realized I was there for more than mere observation; the conversations hinted at the long-standing issues of class and gender roles within the legal framework reflecting the struggles faced by women like the wealthy daughter-in-law referenced in their chat.

Journey to the Billiard Hall via Laborer’s Area  
As I ambled along, the Editor-in-Chief called out, “Time for lunch! Let’s have some food.” I naively thought such gestures were worthy of questioning whether he was feeling generous that day. “What’s the occasion for a treat?” I asked. 

“Oh, I’m running out of time for my exploration from 2:30 to 3:30 PM, and we should eat lunch quickly!” 

It became apparent I needed to act swiftly! Except that what was left was the idea that if only one had to eat on a meal, it wouldn’t be exceptional. The Editor had a bowl of soup while I pondered the possibility of a fuller meal. 

As I considered where to go from Tapgol Park, an outcry from C drew my attention as he seemed to be engaged in divination over in the park. 

I reached the billiard hall with intent to check the East Asian Women’s Association upstairs. 

The conversation I overheard was about how difficult it is to catch a ball given how the Koreans have changed the game. 

At 2:25 PM, I entered and quickly found a springy chair to ease into. 

Two tables visible were occupied with players taking turns. The customers were noticeably relaxed, filled with multiple opportunities for leisure. 

A sense of banal connection stirred within me while watching the players. Much to my dull surprise, an old friend K was among them, demonstrating a nonchalant attitude that mask excitement within. 

With the laughter and casual conversations unfolding, I locked back to K, who had apparently become accustomed to living life in classes of privilege. 

“Things going well?” I asked, commenting on the day’s brightness. 

“You wouldn’t believe it, been waiting for some cash to arrive, 350 won is on its way!” he gleefully divulged. “Want to join me here for drinks later?” 

“Perhaps, consider where you’ve been getting your funds from,” I reconsidered as my mind flashed to thoughts from earlier. 

Meandering with curiosity, I admired the range of players milling about. A young gentleman clad in a new, tailored suit seemed preoccupied, perhaps finding the gesture of his surroundings troubling. 

By around 2:45 PM, I considered what associations led them here given that most were likely merely killing time. 

Though many had familial ties to laborers, I often mused whether those public havens reflected back on the urban pressures consuming these youthful lives. 

As the spring afternoon progressed, I made a beeline towards the exit, spotting a mix of young and old headed toward a populated area, distinctly a farming family steering their way through the city, revealing the true spirit that composes the urban experience back home. 

At 3:35 PM, the time caught up with me, and I found myself with no income from my hour-long excursions.

Expecting Appearing Processes where I may supposedly find “diplomatic” advancement, I mustered the last of my energy to head back towards the train station, pondering if any job postings or choices awaited me before sundown trickled in. 

In an endeavor because of mere sustenance, I stepped into a dyeing company only to find that position I seemed to yearn for had been filled. 

The workers there, awash in colors, toil beneath gloom, and appeared devoid of vitality that pulsates through a vibrant community, persistently managing to only meet ends with little to spare. 

Eventually, my circumstances required that I come up with another approach; upon seeing additional postings on the window about the need for more secretarial positions, I dashed back to the office in pursuit of any opportunity left. 

The day’s awareness of economic hardship dug deeply, intrinsically bound to my own naivete, epitomizing the struggles faced by those losing their way, even as they sought fortunes hidden amidst the fabric of urbanized hope.

**Summary**

This article captures the urban realities and chaotic lives of common people in the bustling streets of Seoul in 1929, bringing to light the prevalence of gambling, labor struggles, and social encounters. It offers a revealing glimpse into the economic despair and social dynamics of the time, illustrating how deeply the issues of class and financial limitations affected even those who dared to hope for a better life. Historians may find the portrayal of social interactions and economic challenges insightful as it reflects the sentiments and realities of Koreans under colonial rule.

白晝 公行 賭博場인 공굴니는 집

孫發

뜻하지 안헛든 불시의 동원령! 두시 삼십분으로부터  세시 삼십분까지 대경성의 움즉어리는 한 국면의 片影을 잡아 쓰라는 것이 명령의 요점이다. 어듸를 가서 어떠한 기사를 취급해야 조흘까? 나는 한 불안한 생각을 가지고 S군과 가티 社門을 나섯다. 『당신은 어듸를 가시료.』 하면서 S군에게 방향을 물엇다. 『글세 나는 外交員募集 하는데나 가볼까』 하면서 역시 의아한 태도다. 校洞 어구를 지나 鐘路거리로 나섯다. 봄쳘에 움직이여 나오는 사람의 떼들!! 모던걸 모던보이들이 저쪽에서 쓱삭이며 이 편을 향해온다. 전차 자동차가 풍우가티 몰녀간다. 여긔서 무슨 기사를 어들까 하면서 S군과 宗廟압헤서 길을 나누고 좀더 내러가다가 공 굴니는집으로 나는 쑥 들어갓다.
표면 간판에는 娛樂的 遊戱場이라고 내세웟지만 그 실은 공공연한 도박장 가튼 곳이다. 「들어오시오. 들어오시오. 누구든지 할 수 잇는 오락이요 하면서 慢性肺病에 걸인놈처럼 얼골이 놀안 작자가 소리를 치는 한 편에는 축음기를 틀어 놋코 오고가는 사람을 모아 들인다. 시간은 2시 47분! 넓두 안흔 두間 店頭에는 사람의 때가 꽉 찻다. 시골서 갓올나온 샛님, 노동자 얼치기 부랑자 遊民級 한때가 서서 알을 굴닌다. 정차장 역명을 京城, 大邱, 平壤... 여덜을 써놋코 공을 굴녀서 자기가 지정한 역처로 말이 들어가면 태워논 액수에 6배를 먹는 것이다. 2전도 좃코 10전도 좃코 얼마든지 놀 수 잇는 놀음<132>(言必稱 공굴니기)이다. 멋업시 키가 커달안 18,9세의 농촌 청년과 꼴사납게 생긴 아마도 놀며 지내는 일종의 不生産消費者 가튼 두사람이 공을 굴닌다. 『元山이요.』 『大邱요.』 『平壤이요.』 하면서 일확천금의 요행을 낙그려고 10전, 20전, 50전 작고 댄다. 일헛다 먹엇다. 결국은 주인이 다 먹고 만다. ‘에횟 또 일엇써’하면서 꼴사납은 양반은 나슨다. 아마 나 보게만 15분 사이에 80전인지 90전인지 일코 돌아스는 모양이다. 조금 잇다가 어떤 상점에 점원가튼 사람이 자전차에서 나리더니 한참 주저주저 하다가 10전을 내서 표를 사가지고 꽤 한참 하다가 일헛다. 또 10전, 20전 그러다가 끗끗내 그도 일엇고 아까부터 계속하든 농촌청년도 작고 일는 모양이다. 한편에선 여전히 「노자 노자 젊어 노자 인생 한 번 죽어지면...」 하고 레코-드 소리가 매우 流暢하게 울어 나온다. 사람들은 좁은데 서서 서로 비비고 떠들며 돈 일흔 當者보다도 멧 10배 더 忿怒한 기색으로 주인을 놀이고 본다. 한참동안 돈을 일튼 농촌청년은 미천이 다 달엿는지 「요보 인젠 고만하겟소. 돈 50전만 주시요.」 「공연히 딴 말슴을 하시는구려. 어서 한번 더 대고 따시오.」 「돈 5원을 다 일헛는데 그래 50전을 안주어.」 「점심 사먹게 그럼 30전만 주시요.」 그의 눈초리에는 확실히 눈물석긴 핏발이 섯다. 「안돼요.」 하면서 강경하게 거절하는 품이 그 영업에 꽤 달년바든 태도로 나온다. 말이 오거니 가거니하는 가운데 잠시동안은 공기가 험악햇지만 그는 할수업시 낙망하고 돌아서 갓다. 나는 그 뒤를 딸아가면서 「여보 고향이 어듸시요.」 하니까 이상하다는 듯이 「그건 웨 그러우」 하다가 내가 대답을 안이하니까 「宣川 이어요.」 「무엇하러 오섯소.」 「네 입학시험 보라 처음 서울 왓다가 이럿케 되엿세요.」 하면서 맛치 동정하라는 빗치 떠돈다. 「내가 직접 관계는 업지만 그런 것을 이다음은 하지 마시요.」 「글세 나두 구경만 하는데 작구 권하니까 햇지요.」 하면서 변명 비스름이 대답을 계속해서 「첫번엔 한 2원 땃섯서요.」 한다. 「그때 고만 두지요.」 「어듸 욕심이 그래요.」 하면서 후회하는 빗을 짓고 그는 창경원 쪽으로 나는 本町으로 가면서 혼자 「철업시 노력업는 태도를 어드려면 저런 결과를 엇는다.」하고 걸어갓다.
시간은 작고 가는데 기사도 재료도 아모 것도 생기질 안는다. 시계를 발아 보면서 오죽 실패를 불으짓고 社로 오다가 아까 후회하고 돌아간 농촌청년이 또다시 공굴니는 집에 서잇는 것을 보앗다.

四柱 古談꾼 集團所 塔洞公園

鏡花
