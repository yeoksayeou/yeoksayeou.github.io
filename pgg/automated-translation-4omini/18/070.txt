Oh, Forsythia  
						麗水  
Oh, forsythia, oh forsythia  
On the hills of the spring meadow,  
Blooming in yellows, blooming in yellows,  
Oh, forsythia.  

"Winter has passed,  
Now it is spring,"  
In a soft voice, you inform us,  
Oh, forsythia.  

Although the wind is still lonely,  
When the world does not know spring has arrived,  
Only you alone, in solitude,  
Sing the spring song.  

Before a hundred flowers bloom,  
You bloom alone in solitude,  
Bringing the news of spring to the world,  
Oh, forsythia, the silent flower.  

When the midnight rain and wind, like a pack of wolves,  
Howl and darken the night sky,  
Your precious life falls unnoticed,  
Alas, there is no one to recognize you.  

However, when spring arrives, after the storm has passed,  
When the myriad flowers strive to bloom,  
The moment you fall and are trampled,  
Instead, a slender smile shall linger on you.  

Oh, forsythia, oh forsythia,  
On the hills of the spring meadow,  
Blooming in yellows, blooming in yellows,  
You, the silent flower, oh forsythia.  

**Summary**  

The poem "Oh, Forsythia" reflects on the beauty and solitude of the forsythia flower, which blooms early in spring, often before other flowers. It characterizes the forsythia as a herald of spring, faced with loneliness amid the transitional seasons. The poet highlights the ephemeral nature of its bloom and the unnoticed struggles of this flower against the harshness of wind and rain. In the context of Korean colonial history, the forsythia's early emergence could symbolize resilience amid hardship, resonating with feelings of longing for recognition and prosperity in a time of oppression. Historians might find the poem's emotional depth and connection to seasonal change intriguing as it parallels the broader societal shifts occurring during the colonial period.
