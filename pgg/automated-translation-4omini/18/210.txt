A Collection of True Stories about Revived Life from the Brink of Death: A Memoir of Surviving the Catastrophic Earthquake  
朴珍  
In the twelfth year of Taisho! Everyone will first remember that the Great Earthquake occurred in the Kanto region (Tokyo) in Japan. And when September 1st is mentioned, one must first recall that terrible day when the great capital of Japan, Imperial Tokyo, was engulfed in flames, and over 400,000 lives were mournfully lost! What is worse is that so many of those deaths were for no apparent reason.  

September 1st! The Kanto Earthquake! The battlefield of despair! How did I rescue a life from that dead zone, and why do I still recall those memories seven years later? Yet among those who survived, there are many who endured far more dire experiences than I did. It’s not that I faced greater danger than anyone else, but as the saying goes, the troubles of others seem less than my own when they're as severe as death. There were certainly many who died without理由, but I was merely facing my personal hardship.  

However, even if I attempted to write down all the circumstances and dangers I encountered back then, I would not be able to convey all of that from memory, nor could my pen express it adequately, thus I will simply jot down a brief account of my return from the dead city, Tokyo, to my hometown.  

First, I offer my silent prayer over the brutal deaths and unexpected grievances, to the souls lost without reason.  

The day before—on September 1st—it rained heavily! As dawn broke, it was bright. Ah! The dreadful September 1st has arrived! From early morning, it was strangely humid and felt as though it would roast me alive. Dark clouds were swirling, and I had a foreboding sense that something was amiss. However, it was a typical midsummer day.  

That summer, for some reason, I found myself alone in the city, longing for my beloved hometown, while everyone else returned to share love and memories of home. I was alone in a tiny shack at the outskirts, living on the gruel of morning and evening. I wouldn't dare say that it was because I had no money to return home.  

That day was September 1st! I had made a quick meal of soy sauce-infused rice and decided to head to school—using some money I had borrowed from a friend’s pocket watch three days prior, I slowly walked to school to pay my fees. However, the treasurer was absent on that fateful day, so I had to return the money.  

Without a proper meal in my stomach, I was feeling quite hungry by lunchtime. Wondering if any of my friends who returned from their homeland might visit me, I thought, perhaps I would have a chance to eat a couple of plates of Western food since I'd grown tired of eating plain rice after a long while.  

Ah, abandon that thought—let’s just chew on some cold rice left over from lunch.  

As I perspired outside, and while switching places among several spots, every time I moved, beads of sweat would gather. Carrying my parasol for shade, I boarded the provincial line from Ochanomizu Station and could only think of the cold rice waiting for me at home. It was almost the time for primary and secondary school opening ceremonies, and there was no room left in the train as it ran full to the brim.  

The train sped through Yotsuya Tunnel and emerged at Shinanomachi Station, but curiously, the noise from the wheels resonated oddly. But since the train was travelling at speed, it was expected to shake violently in stopping moments. Yet for an unusually long time, it kept emitting strange sounds and shaking.  

"Ah! This is strange."  

At that moment, someone shouted, "It's an earthquake!" just five minutes before noon.  

In every car, people began to panic, trying to break the windows to get out, but in great haste, one person fell while a girl was crying. It was chaos! The uproar was so loud that it made me feel dizzy. Although I was startled by the screams surrounding me, my composure remained intact. I didn’t rush out immediately, yet the noise sent me tumbling out of the train towards the platform and I found myself at the exit of the station.  

The heat remained oppressive. No one remained on the train as everyone hurriedly scrambled to escape. But I was wandering across the platform, dragging my parasol back and forth, entertaining myself by observing the chaos around me.  

And another earthquake jolted the ground! The earth quaked violently as if a baby in a cradle was being rocked. People jumped out when it shook and scrambled back on board in a hurried frenzy, as this continued for multiple rounds. "The tunnel's ceiling has collapsed on one side, and the slope along the line has caved in." This was the report from the colorless stationmaster.  

"Oh!" At these words, my companions’ faces turned pale.  

"Namu Amida Butsu! Namu Amida Butsu!"  

Horrified women huddled together on the ground with their faces ashen, shouting with mouths dry. A minute suddenly felt like eternity, as the ground shook violently over and over again. Each time it roared ferociously, people screamed in fear.  

The signal lights broke one after another. The stationmaster began calling everywhere over the phone, but none of the eight lines connected. Thirsty, I turned on the drinking fountain. No matter how long I turned the tap, no water came out. All around, black smoke rose in the air.  

"Fires have erupted everywhere in the city!" was shouted from every mouth.  

It was only then that I felt fear grip me.  

"There is no hope of reopening, but I ask you to proceed as is." This was the proclamation of the stationmaster. Nevertheless, I walked languidly towards the ticket gate, retreating with the hasty crowd, climbing the stairs one by one as I went.  

"Is an earthquake so frightening? There must have been some quakes before, but this one seems unprecedented." I thought about where to go to get a glimpse of it.  

As I pondered, I finished my thoughts and the fear began to sink in as I realized I had nowhere to go.  

In that moment, N's house (N氏) located in Kioicho, near the Akasaka Palace came to my mind. N was the sister-in-law of the vice principal of my school and was a sincere Christian family. Around them, all the women held an interest in me and did everything possible to help me as much as they could.  

At least it was not simply out of confusion and fear that I thought of going to N's house, rather the thought came to me in a flash like a bolt of lightning in the shadow of fear and desperation.  

As I stumbled amidst the ruins, the sky alight with flames raging toward the horizon, my heart gripped with uncertainty as I half-fled and half-ran to N's house. There was thick smoke in the air, and flames leaped wildly in the sky while fires robbed and consuming everything in their way— houses crumbled, tram depots were laid bare, and trains derailed. It was an unending realm of horror—a stage of atrocities that cannot be conveyed!  

Slipping between collapsing houses, I made it to the vicinity of N's house. N worked for the Imperial Household Ministry, so they were residing in a government residence. My thoughts towards the family whose house was connected, I rushed towards the Sōji University that was just beyond, but as I approached, a powerful quake struck, and the feeling paralyzed my heart.  

Amidst the terrifying noise of the vibrating brick buildings, I heard the scream of walls crashing just one space away, and bricks leapt in the air. Even already, the terrifying echo of that building stunned my consciousness. Suddenly realizing the importance of the role I was playing in this drama, I understood then the fearful stakes of the scene.  

That night, N’s family and I spent the night on the road, anxious and unable to enter any buildings, regardless if there were valuables inside.  

Flames soared high to the heavens, lighting the smoke-filled sky while fires raged on and on. The firefighters stood idly, holding their hoses.  

House after house was ablaze, mothers screaming for their children, their eyes burning with an unspeakable rage as they remained shaken and trembled and terrified.  

Still, the impact of September 2nd—more terrifying than the earthquake or the flames—had yet to arrive. It was dawn.  

Fires had spread through the back alleys of that neighborhood, and the situation grew increasingly grim. Since there was no great danger in the suburbs, I had to make arrangements for N's wife and children to be evacuated to my home in Okubo while keeping N’s eldest son, M, sheltered with me until fire engulfed their house.  

The heat above rose from the flames consuming the air, but as that area was relatively peaceful, there wasn’t too great a danger yet. However, places such as Honjo, Fukagawa, and Kanda struggled as fires burnt fiercely. The firefighters tried to extinguish the flames, evacuating burnt and scorched corpses lining the streets, and the flames remained virtually inescapable.  

For several hours, I roamed through the chaos, observing the devastation unfolding before my eyes. The word "atrocity" seemed insufficient to describe what lay before me.  

Roaming around, I returned to N's neighborhood around four o'clock. The climax of my experience begins here. Within two hours, N's house would become engulfed in flames. As I planned to escape to Okubo, where N's family resided, I began to tie the knots on my belongings and prepared to leave.

Many thousands fleeing to the square by the Akasaka Palace felt like they were being drawn into a pit of demonic wrath as they pushed and shoved their way through the chaos around them—forming a hellscape.  

"Are they fighting over food, incapable of sharing?" they wondered, reflecting the anxiety of starvation. It truly was chaos—murder and violence taking place simultaneously across every locality of Japan...  

As the crowd surged forward, I, like a deer stalked by wolves, fled without direction. Yet I was still oblivious to the nature of this frightful situation. Fire blazed without end! The tremors of the earth continued! Among the clamor of voices, I could scarcely hear or recognize M beside me, and he was looking back at me with a worried look. M placed a finger over his lips instructing me silently to be quiet.  

I felt my heart sink as my understanding of my predicament mirrored that of the fleeing masses.  

M and I planned to seek refuge with his relatives in Nikko, waiting for darkness to fall before escaping from this brutal horror.  

The time felt oddly stagnant amidst the chaotic frenzy surrounding us. The flamelike red of the torches looked brighter against a night of chaos. I could scarcely breathe, and neither M nor I could exchange more than a few words, holding tightly while anxiously waiting for darkness to render ourselves discreet.  

The people of that community had become well aware of my identity as a Korean from my frequent arrival at N's house, which made it increasingly risky to remain there. Realizing this, I felt an overwhelming urge to escape. But when I tried to leave, a woman appeared before me, breathless, unable to speak. It was M’s younger sister, Y, who was around eighteen at the time.  

My heart plummeted thinking of the potential danger. M felt the same shock. Y, unable to speak, was gesturing earnestly.  

After a while, as she caught her breath, she managed to warn me. "There's a lot of chaos breaking out everywhere. Don’t go to Okubo! Just stay in the house and don't move!"  

She was crying then, and it was apparent that she had hurriedly come to me, foreseeing the dangers awaiting me.  

How grateful I felt! But where was I to run? Like a lamb straying away from its flock, I was lost amidst a pack of wolves, and there was yet to be a discovery of my whereabouts.  

Ah, where could I go to be safe? Where could I hide to live? At this point, I was more afraid of people than I was of the quake or fire. I thought about using anything against whoever might come for me...  

I could feel my eyes welling with tears. Yet, it was futile, as I knew that, without the help of the crowd, death was probably upon me.  

It must have been around eight in the evening. The flames grew fiercer; cries echoed around us.  

Then, just as I was thinking of fleeing that dreadful place, I spotted R, a classmate from middle school, looking pallid and fraught, running toward me from some unknown pursuit. Seeing him was less of a relief than a surge of inner strength.  

...

Hearing the shouts from the crowd, I was startled too. The flames burned! The tremors shook! Amidst all the voices, R's expression was an enigma. I could barely articulate any words myself, wanting to call out to M but found silence enwrapping me.  

Could I remain there amid the chaos? Forced in retreat, R and I sought refuge hurriedly back to N's house, although that seemed the only remaining viable escape route.  

The wall behind us began to blaze. The embers fell on the ground. The glass of Sōji University shattered in the onslaught of flames, triggering a frightful growl echoing ominously, as the building quivered, swinging toward us.  

We both felt the indescribable adrenaline coursing through our bodies as the fear morphed into frantic energy.  

The fire! The crumbling bricks! The cracks in the walls! None of these were as alarming anymore. M seemed to be struggling and in disarray, without knowing why he would leap into such a dire situation.

"Release the XX person!" the crowd shouted toward the entrance like a wave crashing down upon us.  

"Quickly! Get out! The houses will burn down! Let’s flee toward Nikko!" M barked once again, yet the clarity escaped us amidst the turmoil surrounding us.  

Bound to my previously concealed capacity to separate myself from fear, I grew bold. I didn’t care for my life anymore; my burning excursion of courage had transcended me. I quickly gathered my satchel containing a rice bag and steeled myself to flee.  

But then, R appeared even more troubled—I looked closely, and there was something about his demeanor that spoke volumes. He had traveled from Chungcheong-do, not long before arriving in Tokyo only to now find himself trapped as a Korean in this turbulent maelstrom.  

Looking back at my thoughts, I realized that each second felt monumental around us, a period that may determine our survival.  

As we escaped into the night, I couldn’t think where we might be safe until we suddenly found ourselves near N’s house.  

Fatigue hung heavily upon us as we drew closer. Failing to make it earlier would have proven disastrous.  

At last, night enveloping the scene, I made it alongside Y once more while her mother reached for her urgently.  

Y's cries resounded as she dashed forward to grasp her mother, who emerged from inside, and as the sight of them together overwhelmed me, I slumped down, incapable of moving.  

Yet I could hardly relax, for before long, a group of ten approached, taking hold of me without choice.  

"Oh! Now this..." I thought; I was paralyzed with no hope of rebellion. I followed them quietly, alongside N’s family.  

But they didn’t acknowledge my presence as I remained dumbfounded. The mob had suspected me from Shinjuku since early on. I couldn’t summon words.  

"I plead with you! He is innocent!" N’s wife insisted firmly, alongside the elderly housekeeper and her son, pleading on my behalf.  

The mob impulse drove on, they thought. I couldn’t say a word now.  

Amidst my struggle, a commanding voice emanated, "Stop!" The voice, along with the sound of blades drawn, belonged to a familiar vegetable vendor who regularly came by our place.  

It was then I felt the chill of desperation—he knew me all too well. Seeing the military approach increased the weight of panic in the air.  

N’s struggle reached the ear of the neighborly merchant, who firmly addressed the offended crowd.  

“Everyone, stand down. This boy is with me, for heaven’s sake!”  

One by one, they withdrew, bowing to the vegetable merchant’s authority and leaving me alone.  

Next, R came back to safety as well, and in the subsequent days I took sanctuary under N’s protection, hiding until I could escape Tokyo to Gyeongseong with my police certificate, which affirmed my identity.  

Even amidst the gruesome events along the way, the thought of now penning those days feels terribly burdensome so I put down my pen. Ah! What dreadful memories. I will express gratitude to the N family, who rescued my life for all time. (The End)  

**Summary**  

This article recounts the experience of a Korean individual, Park Jin, during the catastrophic Kanto Earthquake, which devastated Tokyo and led to a horrific loss of life in 1923. The narrative is a personal memoir detailing the chaos and fear that ensued during the earthquake and the struggle for survival in the aftermath. It highlights the panic among civilians, the intensity of natural disaster, and reflections on human compassion amidst tragedy. Historians might find this document particularly revealing about the experiences of ordinary Koreans living in Japan during colonial times, showcasing both the peril faced in natural disasters and the complex interplay of identity as a Korean in a foreign land.
