Post-Graduation Issues of Young Women: Absolute Sanctions  
                吳華英  
If there is a daughter who has graduated from a girls' school, should she not be sent to a household without question? If not, if she finds a job, she must live as a single woman! However, even if a woman works, it cannot be for long. She may decide to remain single and work as a teacher or in some other field, but when she reaches around 30 years old, she will surely regret living in a life filled with vain desires that she once indulged in during her youth. However, whether she regrets it or not, a woman over 30 will find it difficult to get a suitable husband, leading her to a path of a sort of decline as she cannot simply go to another person's home without purpose. Therefore, if a woman goes along the path of being a woman in its rightful way, there is no need to say that she should definitely get married by a considerable age. 

That said, what should be done before marrying after graduating from school? It is absolutely necessary to implement strict sanctions; neglecting it would be an extremely dangerous matter. However, even if sanctions are imposed, should the daughter still refuse to marry? There is a certain clever tactic. If a daughter says, "I will not get married!" then the appropriate person should be selected through whom she can be led into romance! While it may sound silly for parents to lead their daughter into romance and select a suitable husband for her, it ultimately comes down to saying that they should choose a partner for her. Therefore, whether by any means necessary, she must be married. If left unguarded, things can become much more precarious, so one must at least use such tactics!

However, the education offered at girls' schools is beyond inadequate for future living; it teaches nothing of actual use, resulting in no benefit at all. Thus, for about a year after graduation, there must be strict supervision of her behavior at home and she should be taught essential knowledge for household living before she develops interests elsewhere, ultimately sending her off to marry as the first strategy.

This may seem like an unorthodox statement on the matter, but regardless of whether a girl graduates from school or not, a collective training program for women who are to get married should be established—a facility where general life skills are practiced for about a year, teaching how to make kimchi, pickled radishes, and sauces, alongside child-rearing and sewing. It would be beneficial that what is produced is sold for a modest profit, covering materials and expenses in the process. Conducting such a program seems necessary. 

Even if there is a home economics department at schools, they teach it in a way that draws on Western styles that are of no use to Korean people at all. Also, the Women's High School education is ultimately of no practical benefit; it only serves to create a sense of emptiness. 

One could certainly allow general life skills education to be taught, but enforced strict standards should be maintained at home, ensuring no neglect occurs. After about a year, sending her off to a suitable place for marriage would be the best course for oneself.

**Summary**  

This article discusses the consequences faced by young women after graduating from girls' schools in Korea during the 1920s, emphasizing the importance of strict home education and parental guidance to prevent regret and decline. The author advocates for a program to equip these women with practical household skills and urges parents to actively engage in their daughters' marriage arrangements. Historians may find this perspective particularly interesting as it highlights the societal expectations and limited autonomy of women in colonial Korea, reflecting the tensions between traditional values and modern influences at that time.
