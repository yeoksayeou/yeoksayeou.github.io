Song of the South - Reminiscing the South  
					Kim Seok-song  
As the second month draws near and it becomes March,  

When the swallows of the South return,  
This land too experiences spring once more.  

As March’s first day is upon us,  
Swallows gather and chirp in my heart,  
The South, no one knows where it is,  
In my heart alone I’ve drawn it, but for ten years,  
I have never been there, only the swallows remain.  

Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo,  
Arirang, go to the South quickly.  

In every household, a spring of water springs forth,  
Fruits dependably held in the hands of hard work,  
A thousand years feels like a day, it's peaceful.  

Everyone works for their own lot,  
Neighbors caring for each other,  
No snatching or fighting here.  

When the sky is blue, we go out to work,  
When together under the stars, we sing,  
In this land, I call it the South.  

Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo,  
Arirang, go to the South quickly.  

Oh, the South that I miss, can't I go?  
The three thousand li waterway is it difficult?  
It feels like my feet have become weary.  

Oh, the South that I long for, when can I go?  
Even September's ninth day comes year after year,  
But the swallows go alone.  

Oh, the South that I miss, can I cross over?  
Like a flock of swallows, let’s all gather,  
Even if I'm tired, I will go there.  

Arirang, Arirang, Arariyo,  
Arirang, go to the South quickly.  
(Arirang, alternate tune)  

**Summary**  

The poem captures a longing for the southern region of Korea, known as 江南, reflecting on the peaceful lives of its inhabitants and the beauty of spring as swallows return. The nostalgic tone expresses a deep yearning for a homeland left behind, with the imagery suggesting both a visual and emotional connection to the region. Historians may find the sentiments of displacement and nostalgia significant in understanding the wider context of Korean experiences during the colonial period, highlighting the personal impacts of separation from one’s homeland.
