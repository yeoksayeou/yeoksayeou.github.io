Impressions of Women Seen in Travel  
靑吾生  
It has already been three years since that day.  

In July of the year before last, a great flood, the largest in several hundred years, struck Seoul, causing the Han River to rise temporarily by forty-one feet. The records reported people drowning, houses collapsing, the electricity being cut off, the water supply breaking down, and train cars being unable to move, rendering the area around I-dong and Yeongdeungpo completely submerged. It was during this terrifying calamity that it occurred.  

I was on a pilgrimage to the Honam region for work, and I happened to experience the downpour at Cheiri Mountain’s Seoin Temple. As everyone knows, Cheiri Mountain is not only a famous mountain of South Korea but also Seoin Temple is one of the finest temples in South Jeolla Province, ranking alongside Hwaeom Temple and Baekjung Temple.  

The serenity of the rocks and waters was profound, and the monks were quite kind as well. Under normal circumstances, I would have stayed several days in such a place, but because of the persistent rain, travel was cut off in all directions, and I could not see any news—no letters, no newspapers—other than the daily sight of the male monks.  

Even more concerning was the rumor that the area around Gyeongseong was completely submerged; not being able to hear any news about my homeland was more than a little disconcerting. It felt akin to being trapped behind enemy lines during a war, unable to learn news from home. Even in a paradise like the Pure Land, the mind must be at ease, which I had not known, and I spiraled deeper into loneliness.

One day, I can't remember exactly when it was (probably July 19), the rain still did not let up, pouring from morning until noon without pause. I became bored and tired of the solitude, so I put down the Hwaeom Gyeong (Avatamsaka Sutra) that I had been reading and went for a walk around the temple compound. After a while, a young woman with a long braid unexpectedly appeared at the temple gate, clad in a black skirt and white blouse, both completely soaked, her hair tormentingly disheveled by rain and wind, and her bare feet muddy, with no socks or shoes, and her shins completely soaked.  

Her face seemed to be that of a six-tenths beauty, her complexion surprisingly good, as if her cheeks were adorned with fresh peach blossoms, and her eyes sparkled brightly like the stars shining in the dawn sky. She looked striking and formidable rather than merely pretty. I recognized her from somewhere, but I couldn't remember where, encountering her in this secluded mountain area—amidst the dreary rains—was infinitely comforting. When she turned to me and asked, “Is this temple Seoin Temple?” using a friendly Pyongyang dialect, my joy was amplified, akin to meeting an old friend from back home. She seemed quite pleased to assume I was from Seoul.  

Later, upon discovering her background, she was originally from Pyongyang and was teaching at a local girls' school while on vacation, having made the journey to this temple amidst the rain, as she wouldn't normally have the leisure to visit Seoin Temple. I was moved by her brave and lively spirit, silently reflecting on how, being a woman from Pyongyang, she dared to venture into the wilds during this downpour—such boldness was rare nowadays. Over the last few days of weariness from the rain, my feelings of languor disappeared completely. 

I naturally began to share stories of Cheiri Mountain, Seoin Temple, and Hwaeom Temple, providing her with accounts of the history of these places, which she seemed very grateful to receive. After we had lunch together, we decided to head from there to Gurye-eup. I took off my shoes, wrapped my clothes tightly, and then we left the temple. Luckily, the heavy rain had stopped, but the mountain road was still treacherous due to the downpour: the path was mostly destroyed, and streams were transformed into roaring waterfalls or dark pools, making it practically impossible to walk, even for men, let alone a woman.  

Yet she had no concern and walked with confidence. However, being a woman, with delicate skin, her feet were injured and blood was dripping. Seeing this caused me much sorrow, and I went to a nearby village to buy her a pair of traditional shoes. The feeling of human love is truly marvelous. Although we exchanged only a few words, the day’s connection felt several times deeper than the affections of young friends over many years.  

It took us nearly twenty kilometers from Seoin Temple to Gurye-eup, and during this time, our conversation did not cease for a moment. We discussed educational issues, political matters, women's liberation, and even romantic topics. She stated that it had been quite a while since she engaged in such discussions since moving from Pyongyang, while I too hadn't shared such dialogues since arriving at this area. Regardless, the impressions from that time were quite profound.

That night, despite her fatigue, she came with a student to the inn and stayed engaged in delightful conversation, and when I was about to depart to Suncheon in the morning, she came to send me off at the bus station. Just three days later, unfortunately, I contracted a severe case of diarrhea in Suncheon, experiencing thirty-four attacks in a single night, which led to my hospitalization at the local Mae Mountain Hospital. Finding myself in a lonely situation while hospitalized far from home, I cannot express how dismal my heart felt.  

I wrote her a letter, combining my thanks with news of my hospitalization. At the time, I assumed she would return to Pyongyang but since communications were disrupted due to the flood, I felt so lonely that I carelessly sent the letter. Furthermore, I never dreamed that she would happen across my letter while still in Gurye-eup and purposely come to visit me at the hospital on her way to Gwangju due to the halted transportation in Seoul. The gratitude and joy I felt then are truly beyond words.  

Time was limited, and I couldn't stay long, so we chatted briefly before she had to leave. After that day, our mutual plight of feeling sad outweighed the joy of any reunion. It seemed we both became caught up in our busy lives, or perhaps it was due to the indifference of the times; we fell into a state of estrangement, and there has not been a single letter exchanged since. Even now, I know not whether she continues her life in that locality or has been transferred elsewhere. Despite it being three years since then, the memory of her vividly lingers in my mind. As the autumn winds begin to stir and the yellow leaves flutter by, I can’t help but wonder if she is well, harboring any thoughts of the past, prompting me to sing to the south, "O beautiful one, where art thou?"  

**Summary**

This article narrates a poignant encounter between the author and a woman from Pyongyang amidst a severe flood while visiting a mountain temple. The interaction unfolds against the backdrop of a tumultuous natural disaster, showcasing not only the pain of separation and isolation but also a sense of human connection in adversity. The author's reflection on their discussions touches upon progressive themes such as women's liberation, illustrating the complex realities women faced during a challenging period in Korean history, specifically in the colonial context.
