A Major Event in the World, Special Edition Release, Dream Dream Dream  
						薛義植  
Perhaps because my head is not so strong, I dream a lot. And generally, they are messy dreams. Messy dreams are not worth anything significant. Furthermore, if these dreams were to appear only once or twice a year, or at least once a month, they would be dreams worth interpreting using methods such as the "T'odong Secret Method," but instead, they are jumbled dreams that blend together day and night.

If we consider the argument by the famous psychoanalyst Freud, who proclaimed that even children have "sexual desire," it follows that dream phenomena are all variations of "sex" or "sexual desire." Thus, I cannot pinpoint the basis upon which my dreams arise, but generally, they lie in a wide-open field. It is a plain littered with rocks that shoot up like stalagmites amidst the soft grass. And through the field, a train or streetcar rushes by at full speed—often carrying friends from my hometown, most of whom have already passed away. At times, I think strangely in my dreams, "That friend is already dead..." It is a dream that I cannot comprehend.

Shall I also try to delve further into my fantasies in which dreams realize something? I often indulge in daydreaming. Since "newspapers" represent the bulk of my current life, my fantasies are mostly intertwined with newspapers.

If it were said, "Today marks a significant event that will greatly delight Korean people," I would indulge in fantasies about how to create that day's newspaper. First, I would print the first special edition with just four large wooden print characters on a page and convey a single simple line, and following that, I would detail the relevant articles in the subsequent special editions. This is the nature of such daydreams that I frequently realize in dreams. A recent example of this was the execution of our newspaper's plan to print eight pages, a dream that led to the issuance of an edition similar to this kind. Perhaps this is a sort of professional psychology.  

**Summary**  

In this article, the author reflects on his vivid and disordered dreams, expressing a sense of surreal connection to his past and personal experiences through flowing thoughts about train journeys and deceased friends. He connects these dreams to his profession as a newspaper editor, imagining how he would report a significant event in Korean history. This piece provides insight into the internal struggles and cultural reflections of Koreans during the colonial period, shedding light on how the past, often marked by loss and nostalgia, intertwines with professional identity.
