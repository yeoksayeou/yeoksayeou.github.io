Women - The Path of Memory  
Kim Dong-in  

Throughout my thirty years of life, the number of women that I have encountered, whether on the street, on a tram, or in various meeting places, who vanished like a fleeting shadow, corresponding to the phrase "people by the roadside," must be countless.

Amongst these myriad women, there were some who, at one point in my life, left a lasting impression, a point of punctuation in my feelings; their number cannot exceed one hundred.

When I attempt to recollect these countless women and stir my memories, how many of them linger in my mind like a stubborn ghost? 

There are a few whom I did not pay heed to at that time, those who seemed extremely ordinary, yet even after several years, their images still linger vaguely in my mind. Among those who had escaped into obscurity, I find myself wishing to meet again those who had once brushed shoulders with me. There are even some whom I once blamed for not showing up in my life, but now bear no more than a faded impression.

In the sea of countless women, how many truly remain imbued in my being? I will take a moment to note them down.

Mary, Nakajima Yoshie, Manzoji Akiko, Kim Ok-yeop, M Senmaru, Hwang Jeong-ok, X (whose full name is unknown), Noh San-hong, Kim Baek-ok - altogether ten, among which two, Hwang Jeong-ok and Kim Baek-ok (one from Seoul, the other a gisaeng from JinNAM Po), have long since departed from this world. (Oh Lord, may the spirits of these young ones be protected.) The five among Mary, Yoshie, Akiko, M, and X are unknown to me regarding their current whereabout, while among the remaining three, Kim Ok-yeop has begun her seventh (?) household, M has become a good mother and is raising several children well, and Noh San-hong still practices her trade as a gisaeng in Seoul.

1. Mary  
It was autumn of 1915. As a second-year student at Meiji Academy junior high school, at the age of sixteen, Kim Dong-in moved into a certain private academy in Shiba Ward, Hakugan-dai Town. At that time, although I was only sixteen, I was on the smaller side, and I can hardly imagine how I was when I went to Tokyo at the age of fifteen with a half-fare ticket.

Upon getting off at Hakugan-dai Town for the Meguro line, I descended a slope leading to the Seishin Girls' School halfway down and arrived at my new boarding house.

At that time, the Hakugan-dai Town area was still new land, with houses bordering the east and north sides of my new home, but to the south, across the road, lay several thousand tsubo of wilderness, and to the west, one had to cross several hundred tsubo of wilderness to find any houses.

After having dinner with R, who had moved in with me, I declined R’s suggestion to go for a walk and research the neighborhood, and I went up alone to the second floor. Without even the inclination to study or review my textbooks, I headed west toward where my school was located.

It was a dark night with the moon waning. However, as I stepped out toward that place, there shone brightly a spot that seemed to ignore the dark night. It was a two-story house about twenty steps from where I was standing. The Japanese-style two-story house had its sliding doors removed and replaced with glass doors, painted, so to speak, with a European-style finish.

It was then that I first saw her. Inside the glass doors, (I surmised) there were about a hundred glowing lamps, and around a center table sat what seemed to be a family of five Westerners, a couple and their two sons and a daughter, laughing and engaging in conversation.

The curiosity of a teenage boy is vast. I returned to my room and took out my telescope to peer in secretly. The focus of my telescope at that time (I don't remember this clearly) was directed toward the girl inside.

Thirteen or fourteen years old. With an oval-shaped face, and as she laughed, dimples appeared on her cheeks. Though I could not see her lower body, it was clear from her gestures that she had nimble legs.

I don’t know if a poet would say that such a scene should only be seen in spring, but for a boy overwhelmed with emotion, even autumn had nothing to do with it. In that dark night, with the bright glass doors and the snug interior, that girl inside seemed like a scene from a painting. If Millet had seen it, it could have turned into a masterpiece, and if Goethe had seen it, perhaps it would have inspired a line of poetry. But for the boy Kim Dong-in, everything else was inconsequential. The single eye of that girl with one side of her long hair and a cheek, still unblemished, contrasted with her lovely youth that enveloped my soul like a lantern; she was the only uniqueness in that scene.

Two days later, after school, when I came back home barely able to wait for night to arrive, standing in my second-floor room gazing absent-mindedly down, I spotted the silhouette of the girl in a western-style dress. Jolt of surprise struck me, and I nearly knocked over the table rushing to look. Excitedly, I started looking out the crack of the door. The girl, with her hands tucked in the pockets of her skirt, kicked small stones here and there as she walked down the hill.

In spring, she would have flowers, in summer, greenery, in autumn, falling leaves, and in winter, snow - though the boy was gloomy and could not love anything else, Cupid's arrow had pierced through even into the hearts of us, like summer's warm embrace.

From then on, before bed, I began to take deep breaths and went out toward that place. I'd stand there until her house's door opened, gazing up at her second floor, and in the mornings, I’d run out there with dumbbells to send my heart’s greeting toward her before washing my face.

Our boarding house was across the road to the south, where several thousand tsubo of wilderness lay, which we (R, myself, and two others) used as a practice field for tennis.

On a certain day, having just played with the tennis ball, I hit a shot that soared right over the net. The ball went across the road and into the wilderness to bounce against her house wall, and I ran to retrieve it, my heart filled with longing. 

“Arthur,” came a motherly voice calling for her son from within the wall. At the same time, a soft answer could be heard.

I grabbed the ball and threw it back across to my side, and I hurried back home.

Ah - ter - Surely, I had not lost my mind. At that time, we were learning about King Arthur in our lessons, so the worries fell away. However, for me, thoughts of that name made me feel as if it wouldn’t be forgotten—a name of joy and profound beauty. A few of the tales of King Arthur adorned my bookshelves. There was a picture of knights from Rome that decorated my walls. In my textbooks and the pages of my notes, the letters spelling "Arthur" often marred my focus. Glancing at the book, I was often able to discover that name. "Arthur" was indeed something I could not forget back then.

On another day, as I was practicing tennis, I overheard a girl’s English words behind me. I was startled and lost focus, causing the ball to fly away. I turned around to find her standing there, hesitating where to send the ball back. Instinctively, my hand reached out, but I was so overwhelmed that it slipped from my grasp. Spotting the girl, I knew she was the one from before. She was a little girl who'd been playing nearby, but now she was brushing off dust as she walked down the steep slope.

In the spring, she would have flowers, in the summer greenery, in the autumn falling leaves, and in winter snow. Though my moody nature could not allow me to love anything else, Cupid's arrow pierced my heart. 

The next day I went back home for school, not brimming with enthusiasm but ambivalent instead, and I didn’t see that beautiful snowy home whose doors were closed. 

Days passed—no sign of her house opening, and it was unusual indeed. Moments when I would wait for a response or see her family bustle about became mere whispers. Despite whatever I would think of adjacent mysteries, circumstances turned cumbersome as new weights would then descend. While I would occasionally search my heart for her, the absence, little by little, sank into my soul.

Winter came, carrying with it my final chance of peace from these thoughts. I would study even harder, since frustration mounted as I had lost my chance at expressing that feeling I had toward her. 

With the winter break and the new year of enforced studies forcing all my local classmates into busy lives, the weight became heavier. 

In my third year, how eagerly I had awaited it! It was not solely from due diligence to my schoolwork that I had awaited. The prospect of Mary’s siblings returning filled my thoughts, however, as school stuff started back up there they weren’t in sight. However, I couldn’t shake that aching desire.

But no sooner had I settled that conviction than my textbooks filled with the remnants of past names. I molded the term "scouting of a kind" into a mark that a boy with a tender heart should feel even more deeply at his loss. 

“Love is a monster,” someone had once said. Who could have coiled that phrase? My youthful heart, ripped apart by this very monster, felt painfully clear. However, in the adult sphere, love demanded reciprocity. Such was not on my mind in those days. Having a boyish affection for her, would lead to the void being filled. If she Mary would entertain love for someone, it would ignite an unwelcome spark of feeling in my heart, but in my pure youth, thoughts of displeasure did not exist. 

As endeavors unfolded, the trappings of my ignorance bolstered me, enabling a grieving desire to watch. Subsequently, I never again saw Nor did I hear word of her.  

That autumn, I, now a third-year student, would walk very well as I crossed many an enduring path, among graced spectacles a vehicle arrived, pulling forth a Western-dressed girl. 

That moment, I thought I had misjudged as my heart faltered, but in recovering my senses I recognized as she rode past me that the young girl was none other than Mary. Though disguised in the joyful rags of youth, I could not miss.

It was bittersweet— a threshold upon which we once laughed, quietly. 

Mary - (If she is still alive, she must now be twenty-eight years old). She, of course, would never know how a young Korean boy bestowed such love, nor truly grasp the aching loss I felt upon losing her from my world. 

My youthful heart was mercilessly shattered. Yet the scarring from that wound will remain— over ten years, I crossed many women and had chances for love, but (barring one exception) I could scarcely dare look upon them without casting my soul aloft in their memories, all of this remnants lining like traces in this bleak and somber tale.

So too did I wrestle with transformations; grasping perceptions of others - brightened by remembrances of various images and scenery, hastily sown from those early years opens, forever becoming a lasting symbol throughout my life's journey.  

**Summary**  

In this poignant reflection on love and memory, Kim Dong-in recounts his youthful infatuation with a girl named Mary during his time in Tokyo, illustrating the profound effect of first love. He evokes nostalgia for a lost time, examining the feelings that come with unrequited longing and the lingering pain of formative years. Historians might find the exploration of personal emotions, especially in a colonial context, revealing of how cultural and societal pressures shaped intimate relationships during a tumultuous era in Korea's history.

メグロ가는 뎐차를 白金臺町에서 나려서, 오른편쪽으로 聖心女學院으로 가는언덕길을 한 절반이나 나려가서, 오른손짝으로 잇는 다락집이, 내가 새로잠은 하숙이엇섯다.
