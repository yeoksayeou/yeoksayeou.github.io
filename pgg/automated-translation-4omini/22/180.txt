필자	壬寅生  
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir)  

Five coins and one penny; the year-end bonus tale.   

30% is like Jesus, that is quite a shock, as it only amounts to 18%. I am at a loss for words.  

18% of 52 won means 93 won and 60 jeon, adding up to a total of 145 won and 60 jeon including this month’s salary.  

I have 10 won remaining from the month before and 15 won from the previous month, totaling a debt of 25 won. After subtracting that, I am left with merely 120 won and 60 jeon.  

Holding the envelope with 120 won and 60 jeon, I contemplate how I am going to take it down the mountain. I think about a bowl of winter soup costing 9 won and 15 jeon from the <40> soup restaurant; last month, I wasn’t able to take half of it, so I could likely manage.  

How can I endure this without screaming at the embarrassment? Handing over a 10-won bill and receiving 85 jeon in change, I hear the words, “Mr. Kim, the economy looks good.”  

The tailor from China says that. It irritates me to have to show money to that Chinese merchant, even if I feel bad doing so. Besides, my monthly installments are four months overdue, so I must hand over two months' worth, accumulating to 30 won.  

“30 won? That’s troubling.”  

“But that will take a while to come again.”  

“However, Mr. Kim... another 10 won...”  

“Shut up!”  

“Don’t be rude, Mr. Kim. When will that be?”  

“Next year-end.”  

“Don’t joke around… Next month!”  

“It’s next year!”  

“Ah, Mr. Kim, joke aside.”  

Learning from multiple past experiences with landlords, I decided not to wait and went straight to the office instead. Paying the house rent would only cover two months of the three-month total of 36 won, but since I haven't paid any for several months, I must pay the full price of 42 won and 76 jeon for rice, lest I not be able to buy on credit for the next month.  

After paying my rent and for rice, the envelope becomes quite thin.  

Let’s see how much remains. 10 won and a little bit of change!  

I cannot go on like this. I must go to the management department and plead for the allowance.  

The manager of the department, who is as slick as oil, does not listen to me.  

“Just give me 30 won.”  

“30 won? You are saying that, having over 120 won in hand, looking for more allowance?”  

“Only 10 won remains.”  

“What for?”  

“I don’t even know...”  

“Just add 30 won more.”  

“I can’t do it; if it were up to me, I could give you any amount…”  

“Please let me live!”  

“Just take 10 won.”  

“Taking 10 won is no use.”  

“The rest cannot be done.”  

“Just give me 20 won.”  

“I can’t… It’s impossible.”  

After much struggle, I managed to acquire 10 won more, making it 20 won.  

Having given 13 won to a person who came from the woods, I am left with about 7 or 8 won.  

Even now, I have so much to spend on... my child's monthly tuition is for three months, at 1 won and 50 jeon, and I promised to buy my wife a winter coat, which must be done by year-end. I also have to buy a jacket for myself, and the electric bill exceeds 2 won. Plus, there are sanitation service fees, night watch fees, and oh, I mustn't forget the year-end party fee. My shoes are worn out, and I need to buy at least one shirt. Tonight, I must at least go to the brother’s fish soup restaurant for a drink.  

Even having over 100 won would not be enough.  

Mr. P stands there, bending forward with an exhausted face.  

“Have you got any change left?”  

Mr. P pulls out his salary envelope and rubs it clean.  

I drag Mr. P to the sukiyaki restaurant. After we have some drinks, I have a 5-jeon coin and three smaller coins left.  

However, I spend the night wandering without a care.  

And finally, I return home—with five coins and one penny left.  

<40-44>  

**Summary**  

This article reflects the struggles of a worker living under financial insecurity during the colonial period in Korea, emphasizing the pressures of unpaid debts and household expenses amid meager wages. The author’s humorous yet desperate grappling with financial duties provides insight into the challenges of the lower classes during this era. Historians might find value in this account as it illustrates the everyday realities of life for Koreans under Japanese rule, highlighting the socio-economic conditions prevailing at the time.
