잡지명	별건곤 제24호  
발행일	1929년 12월 01일  
기사제목	歲暮喜悲 交響樂 (Year-End Joy and Sorrow Symphony)  
필자	豪蕩兒 (Hodang'er)  
기사형태	문예기타 (Other Literature)  

The year is ending, and it is the last month of the lunar calendar.  

It is the wage earners. And they are waiting for their bonuses and year-end rewards.  

In ten days, it will be here. How many days have they been hoping and waiting since a month or two ago? They wait eagerly for their bonuses.  

Seoul is bright. And it is bustling.  

In the automobile department, they are trying to show off with cars, working diligently, preparing for a long night. Department stores are all refreshing their display windows.  

Year-End Big Sale!! Huge discount sales!! It's madness.  

Beautiful women are preparing themselves— in restaurants and cafés, various preparations are underway—  

Oh, how terrifyingly Seoul prepares, as if it might fly away! Discounts from 10% to 70%!!! The city is bursting with excitement from the capitalists' thinly-veiled offerings! Oh! Oh! Oh!  

Bag-carrying cashiers are actively at work, taking care of the monthly collections. Mobilization! It’s a total mobilization! Their eyes are full of blood from the exertion of total mobilization.  

And thus, our human tragedy of joy and sorrow unfolds. It has unfolded each year when the year-end arrives.  

I feel like a bibimbap, a mixture of emotions— I will witness the symphony of December delights, the symphony of tears and laughter.

C— He is single.  

This spring, he graduated from university and has become one of the earnest wage earners at an M company.  

The year-end has come for him as well. He has been dreaming of bonuses for ten days now. 20%? No— that seems small! Then 30%! 30% seems possible. He has made a budget calculation in his head.  

The paycheck has arrived. He is trembling with curiosity, like a starving puppy with a dried fish head, wanting to know how much it is, anxious to see if his dreams will come true. He hurriedly rushes down to the cafeteria in the basement, secretly opens the paycheck envelope.  

Oh! He is astonished. It is far beyond what he had expected.

“Oh!” He exclaimed twice. His envelope contains even 45%—275 won. Now, he must correct his predicted expenses. He has been planning to buy an acoustic phonograph, something he had always wanted, but it was previously out of reach financially. However, now it seems possible. Anything lacking can be covered by selling his current machine. He was filled with joy.  

That afternoon, he went straight home, bonuses in hand.  

However, that evening— he felt too restricted lying in his boarding house room.  

At 8 o'clock— he was wandering amid the brilliant lights of Chingognae, lost in a sea of men and women.  

But when mesmerized by Chingognae, every time he thought, “I should stop there!” he would urge himself fervently, thinking, “Let's just enjoy the party! Let's just spend the money!” — he found himself among the crowd filled with joy from the year-end bonuses and swallowed in melancholy.  

He had decided to marry F in the upcoming spring. He thought of buying a gorgeous French-made handbag for her, imagining the lovely wallet filled with a few ten-won bills— no, perhaps they would fall short, so three bills— let's send those! This thought weighed heavily on his heart, and he suppressed a sigh. But before one minute passed, he thought, “I'll save that for tomorrow!” This would require him to completely revise his budget and inevitably sigh later on.  

Two hours passed.  

Drunk from “Gakure,” C had reached the tram stop at Saengwonri, ready to take a train when suddenly he felt like his head had exploded. His face turned to that of a failing student finding out his grades. “Men's suits have at least fifteen pockets,” he believed, and in a flash, despite all his pockets being full, the paycheck was missing. Oh, his disappointment! All his hope until now! But his reason wouldn’t escape him. On the tram, his mind was as active as a detective’s.  

“Oh— when I buy that thing, that beggar should not be this dumb!” “No, and the one who didn’t come to the party is just a blundering idiot….”  

His lips trembled, and his mind raced, his heart sank. The crowd in Chingognae seemed a bunch of thieves. Oh! Oh!  

That evening, as he returned to the boarding house and took off his clothes, the moment he opened the pocket of his coat to pull out his paycheck— oh, his heart— no, his laughter was mixed with bitterness, anguish, and sweetness…  

“However, a man's suit has at least fifteen pockets!” he cried out decidedly.  

“Here you are!”  

The usury guarantor’s association head had set out from dawn to collect his debts on year-end day.  

“Here you are!”  

A mighty, fierce voice rang through a lonely alley as he knocked on doors for the second time. And the sound of the door being slammed echoed.  

He must have felt this way. If he had called for the second time, the house inside was as silent as death, and the money lent of 60 won given last October not being repaid until today would have made any decent man angry.  

Just then, a woman’s crying voice, thinner than a spider's web, could be heard like a melody from within the house. Then a door opened with care.  

“Who is it?”  

The voice sounded as though an ornament was being polished. He looked toward where the voice came from. Oh! There was a woman, her hair down, weeping as she looked at him. He was surprised. What could the matter be?  

“Yes— I am here to meet the main tenant as promised today. Uh— that money I borrowed, 3,000 nyang, I am here to pay back.”  

His voice turned soft. This was because she was beautiful. Her fine face, clear eyes, lovely lips, delicate fingers, and graceful body— oh, she was a beauty.  

At that moment, she began to weep even more, telling him how her husband was neglecting his family, drunken and ignoring his duties, and she shared how he often returned home late at night after drinking himself into a stupor, resulting in a tragedy in their home.  

With a sorrowful voice— thus, her husband, whom he knew was a notorious drunkard in the neighborhood, had passed away since he could not bear the pain of that morning's excessive drinking.  

She spoke without foresight, even as she tried to pull herself together. The listener could not help but sympathize. He turned his head down; failing to speak, he glanced into the room.  

Through the dimly lit interior, there was a coffin. “Yes— this is a tragic situation. How will the funeral be conducted…?”  

“It is concerning. Today is year-end, tomorrow is New Year's, and on top of that, there is no money; it is all worrisome.”  

Having listened to her, he took a moment of silence, then opened his bag, drew out ten 1-won bills, and said, “I understand the situation is dire. Although it may not be much, please use this to assist in the funeral.”  

The woman was surprised, saying, “Thank you. But how can I receive this after having owed you a debt? Please, keep it.”  

“No, please take it; this is not an issue, is it?”  

She insisted again, “Please stop.”  

“Please take it.”  

Alas, there is such human kindness. However, he could not resist. The man in the coffin overheard this conversation entirely from beneath the coffin and was indignant.  

The husband, who was not supposed to be working the night shift, was irritated at his wife's reminders about their financial woes. He couldn’t tolerate it anymore. So, while holding back from wanting to leap up, he suddenly shouted, “Hey!  

Don’t give it away! Don’t give it away!”  

This is the most unbelievable story, yet it raises a significant question for us.  

“With no money, what do you propose to do? I'd rather die. Do as you please!” It was an era of naivety back then.  

The man, with a pain-stricken expression, pulled out a poison pill and said, “Hey, everyone! I knew this would happen and saved some poison! Now, it’s time for a long farewell!”  

He cried out in agony, took the pill and died, thrashing about. This was the infamous poison suicide incident that happened in Gangwon-do 18 years ago.  

The money is all gone, the New Year has come, and no one can pay. Might someone have passed away with the news of their father's death while beggars knock, it might seem like something we can brush off in jest, but how much sorrow from the year-end will these events leave for them?  

Year-End Parties! What a strange custom that has begun in recent years.  

Year-end parties provoke the weeping, plight, and sorrow of the lower-class employees, all while bringing together packages from higher-ups to amuse themselves. How painful and sad it must be to let go of 30% or 50% from their already tight family budgets for the sake of pleasing those in charge.  

When they receive presents from their spouses, what do they find? Diamonds? Gold watches? Hairpins? Or something else? They just fling things about when receiving handkerchiefs! Both the giver and the receiver break into tears! What a scenario that is!  

Long ago! Back during the Shilla dynasty.  

They would say that just to wear patched clothes, having stitched fabrics together, is how I became known as “Mr. Hundred Repairs” also suggests modesty. In this time of year, households would be bustling, making rice cakes, yet in our teacher’s home, it must have been painfully quiet.  

When it started snowing, children would tug at the teacher’s sleeves, begging for rice cakes, as they hadn’t had any that day. The teacher, pondering deeply, would step into the yard, take out a stone mortar, and start to pound out the rice cakes, singing in a mournful voice, “Eh-he-ra, thump thump the mortar!”  

Oh, how beautiful this song is! “Eh-he-ra, thump thump the mortar!”  

How the song might last long while the year-end is approaching!  

Let’s stop writing! There isn’t enough time left. The topic and my writing have strayed off to a different path.  

The sudden summons to write a succinct piece has left me scrambling to finish. Please forgive me if it’s a little disjointed.  

**Summary**  

This article captures the contrasting emotions of joy and sorrow as the year comes to an end in Seoul, illustrating the lives of wage earners who eagerly anticipate their bonuses while experiencing the weight of poverty and despair. It highlights the societal pressure of year-end celebrations, the kindness unveiled amid hardship, and the stark realities of life for many. Historians may find interest in the depiction of emotional struggles during the colonial period, revealing the interconnectedness of economic challenges and cultural customs prevalent within Korean society.
