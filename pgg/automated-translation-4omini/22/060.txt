기사제목	한글大家 金枓奉氏 訪問記, 在外名士訪問記 (A Visit to the Great Hangul Scholar Kim Hwa-bong, A Visit to a Notable Person Abroad)  
필자	李允宰  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

I arrived at the Shanghai port on August 8 at 1 PM. I took a horse-drawn carriage into the French Concession, passing through streets akin to Jongno in Seoul, and then took a turn onto Maximo Tiro Road to reach the Wonchang Company. After greeting the owner, Mr. Cho, warmly and exchanging pleasantries, we set off together to visit Mr. Kim Hwa-bong.

As we turned westward, we reached the neighborhood of Wangji Road where Mr. Kim's house is located. As I approached, he must have sensed my arrival, as he came out to warmly shake my hand in the street. His home is a two-story Western-style house where he lives with his family. Although it is to be expected, it is rather rare to find a compatriot abroad who is doing well. Especially since he is leading a simple scholarly life, fighting against poverty while nurturing love and comfort for his wife and their five-year-old daughter, he has now unexpectedly spent ten years there like a flash of lightning.

He had just returned from a trip to Changsha in Hunan province a few days prior, and he appeared quite weary as if traces of fatigue still lingered on his face. Returning inside, he politely refused me a seat while smiling and said to me, “I have heard through a grapevine and in the newspapers about your various efforts.”

“Oh no. Hearing you say that makes me feel completely ashamed as if I have done nothing.”

After this exchange of greetings, he continued, “Was your trip here particularly difficult?”

“No, it was not difficult at all. The seas were extremely calm, and the police provided close protection throughout.”

“Yes, that is as it should be. They tend to view visitors to Shanghai with some suspicion.”

“Mr. Kwon (Deok-kyu), Mr. Choi (Hyun-bae), Mr. Shin (Myeong-gyun), Mr. Jang (Ji-yeong)… They are all doing well.”

“Yes, they are all well. Furthermore, they are all working hard for the ‘Han-geul’ (한글).”

“Yes, I have heard that. It is indeed a wonderful thing.”

Just then, Mr. Cho, who had come in, mentioned he had business to attend to, so he returned home. I continued my conversation with Mr. Kim.

“It must surely have been hard for you after being abroad for so many years.”

“Not at all. The ones in the mainland are the ones suffering. Since I am living here freely, what suffering could I possibly experience?”

“What are you currently doing to pass the time?”

“I have been teaching at Inseong School, which is a Korean elementary school in Shanghai, for a few days now, albeit with the name of a principal.”

“That must be quite enjoyable. Do you have many staff members there?”

“There are about five to six male and female teachers.”

“And how many students?”

“There are about a hundred.”

“Is the school's maintenance solid?”

“What can I say? With the small strength of our expatriates, it cannot be very well-resourced.”

“I suppose our expatriates' strength is meager indeed.”

“Indeed. The fact that this school has lasted for about ten years since its establishment is solely due to the efforts of our expatriates. There are about a thousand Koreans residing in Shanghai. There are even hundreds of children. If we were to send them to Chinese elementary schools, they would end up losing all knowledge of their mother tongue and only speak Chinese. How could they then live as Koreans? For this reason, we have no choice but to put more effort into the school. But see, there is even this matter.”

He then presented a printed document from the desk. It was a statement written under the name of the school's board of directors. I took it and read it.

“Oh, so there are those opposed to the school?”

“Yes, just a few days ago, members of the Communist Party circulated a denunciation against our Inseong School. This is a statement intended to clarify that issue.”

“Oh, is that so? Why would they denounce the school? It seems very strange.”

“Well, they claim that the school’s classrooms are not to be used as their meeting place, but this assertion goes against the words written in this statement, regarding the sacred educational site not being used for general assemblies. Furthermore, it is intolerable to instill such political strife into the innocent minds of children, and if they act recklessly while receiving attention from the foreign authorities, there could be adverse consequences for the school. Thus, we did not allow them to hold their assembly. Nevertheless, their denunciation is rather lamentable.”

“If they saw this statement, they would surely understand, right? This is just one of the various difficulties we face. But how many funds do you have?”

“Do we even have any funds? All that supports us are monthly donations from the community and fees from the students. We must find a way to raise some foundational funds. So, this time when the Korean Magic Troupe came here, we organized a performance at the French Clubhouse for the benefit of Inseong School, but it ended up costing us too much, resulting in a deficit without any earnings. What they say is true: misfortune comes in pairs.”

“Then the upkeep of the school entirely depends on your efforts, Principal.”

“Oh no, I intend to resign from my position as principal. I lack the capacity, and I have other matters to attend to as well.”

“Then, the school affairs will hit a snag.”

“Not at all. If someone more capable takes over, the school will fare better. Though I may leave the position of principal, I will still try my best to assist indirectly.”

“Then what will you be managing?”

“It’s difficult for me to say just yet. Also, I hope to finish the Korean Dictionary that I have been working on for the past five to six years.”

“A dictionary? I have heard about your exhaustive efforts in compiling it. How is it progressing?”

“Would you like to see it roughly? Please come this way.”

He went upstairs to the loft. He brought out several pages filled with ‘cards’ packed among old books and showed them to me.

“Look at this; it has come this far.”

“Is the organization complete?”

“If the organization is complete, what else could I worry about? My life is uncertain, so I cannot invest too much into it. What I have done so far is only collecting vocabulary and explanations.”

“How much longer do you think it will take to complete the organization?”

“Looking forward from now, I think it could take about another year for at least a rough organization to be done. Additionally, if I supplement the missing archaic words, dialects, and new words… it could potentially become a complete dictionary.”

“If all is ready, how would you publish it?”

“Wherever I can, if I decide to publish it, I will make sure to publish it. I will decline any compensation or manuscript fees, just ensuring the book is published.”

“That is a very gracious intention.”

“I have heard that they are also compiling a dictionary back in the mainland; how is that progressing?”

“I have heard it is being done by the Kaemyung Club.”

“What is the Kaemyung Club?”

“It is a club that is straightforwardly for refinement and recreation, composed of a few in the asset class.”

“How did they come to initiate such a dictionary project?”

“It is being led by a few passionate individuals.”

“Which individuals have contributed to the writing?”

“Initially, Choi Nam-seon, Jeong In-boo, Im Kyu, Byun Young-ro, Yang Gyeon-sik, Han Jeong, and I were among the seven who participated together.”

“Oh, so you were one of them.”

“Yes, I have been participating up until now.” 

“Have all seven been continuing until now?”

“The compilation started in June of last year. A few months in, each one began to drop off, and now only Lim Gyu and Han Jeong remain.”

“If that’s the case, did you also leave?”

“Yes, I stopped just a few days ago.”

“How much time do you think it has taken thus far?”

“It has taken a considerable amount of time, but frankly, we have only been engaged in collecting vocabulary without any organization, and I have hardly touched on the matters of literary vocabulary and academic language, thus it will still take quite a while longer for a dictionary to take shape. Based on our current pace, it seems we could continue for one or two more months before stopping.”

“Ah, it is regrettable to think that our project ends up this way.”

“How do you plan to structure the grammar in the dictionary?”

“Regarding grammar, it is difficult to say simply. I have been contemplating that here for several decades... (At this point, numerous hours of dialogue transpired surrounding grammatical theory; thus, I will omit further detail here—original text) After spending two weeks in Shanghai, we exchanged many opinions regarding the Korean Dictionary issue. I sincerely wish for Mr. Kim's health and the completion of his endeavor.”  

**Summary**

The article describes a visit by the author, Yi Yoon-jae, to the notable Korean scholar Kim Hwa-bong living in Shanghai in 1929. The conversation highlights the struggles of the Korean expatriate community, particularly in maintaining cultural identity through education in the face of challenges such as funding and political opposition. Most notably, Kim discusses his role in leading a Korean elementary school and his efforts in compiling a Korean dictionary. This narrative reveals the difficulties faced by Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, emphasizing their commitment to promoting the Korean language and culture despite adverse circumstances, which is of significant interest to historians studying the Korean diaspora and colonial resistance.
