Confession of a Magazine Reporter  

A person from Ho-ran-dang  

This is less a confession of a magazine reporter and more a struggle for resolution from a young man who has fallen into a misprinted life. 

There was a time when I was to visit a girls' school.  

In terms of form, as well as in terms of the content of the articles, visiting a girls' school is a task suitable for a woman.  
Observing my ridiculous shadow nodding his head, I discovered the foolish clown within me.  
Foolish clown! Pierrot....dressed in a ridiculous manner, making others laugh while laughing at himself....Pierrot!  
Yet, inside that foolish clown lies a tear.  
In the foolish clown's ridiculous shadow, there lies even more resentful and angry tears. 

Having to do what is supposed to be done is often difficult, and not being able to do what should be done is the tragedy of a misprinted life.  
Writing what one should write while neglecting it, or writing what one cannot write, are the pains of a journalist who has strayed from his path.  
If a person cannot adhere to the aspirations with which they came into the world, what brilliance can there be in that life?  
How can one who calls out the justice that weighs on their heart and utters the arguments that weigh on their intentions be different from a prostitute singing a song with no heart and a laugh without meaning?  
O pale intellectual prostitution! I curse myself for being this way.  
I would rather be born as someone who has nothing but iron chains, or, if I cannot do that, as a loyal dog fulfilling the destiny in my birth.... or spend my life indulging as an apostle of liberalism, rather than ever having to bear the burdens of obligation, and without needing to go back on a lost path!  
But the die has already been cast.  
Fate has been decided.  
What remains is time.  
I swear—with my life—that I will not coexist with the "present."  
After settling my debts in the feeble intellectual profession, under the splendid golden backdrop, a sharp light blue glimmer—is it to be covered and drenched, and drenched anew, so that bright red blood flows, that the shining sword light may dull, and finally be overwhelmed by the nights of slaughter, as the tranquil dawn of an eerie sunrise approaches...?  

Even those who seem knowledgeable just stand with their hands clasped, waiting only for inevitability.  
Yet inevitability cannot merely advance as inevitability.  
To me—nothing comes unless there is heat. Their heat is a great lesson to us.  

To be a newspaper reporter for two years...  
During that time, my life was nothing but an unintentional carnival, drowning in alcohol and brothels, leaving no room for self-awareness and reflection.  
It would not have been surprising for someone from the fallen middle class, steeped in weak ethnic animosity and selfish liberalism, to become like this.  
While claiming to look after others, at night I live through drink and brothels and by day I sit in disgrace—not even bothering to please the likes of the leaders in society, but with a rather staunch character, I often found myself being chased, and it had become common to think that I would not lose my eggs.  
While trying to find a job, I thought of going to a newspaper office, but where would someone with just a small amount of common sense go and what would they do?  
Unable to do so, I returned to my hometown and spent three years eating my father's hard-earned food.  

That three years was the most significant and decisive period determining my entire life.  
More than anything, during that time, I read extensively.  
At first, I devoured Kropotkin and then moved to Marx. This was the time when I read the most in terms of quantity and quality while still half a life ahead of me.  
One important observation during this time was of society....particularly the objective realities of rural areas.  

I returned to Seoul and began to eat at a magazine office again. At first, I continued in ignorance of such matters and later I compared my ideology with my actions.  
Thus began my struggle.  
When comparing the benefits and harms of what I am currently doing, the latter is far greater than the former.  
Moreover, the benefits are nothing more than part of a world that is different from my own ideology—"liberalist Joseon."  
What I am currently possessing in my realm of existence is not solely in terms of labor relations. I exist within a consensual system of naming and real substance.  
Thus, it is vastly different from when a proletarian goes to XX factory to make XX, XX, and XX for themselves.  
It is completely different.  
It is something I cannot unequivocally forgive in myself.  
Even if it is a pure labor relationship, though I may be engaged in some sort of social labor (in a broad sense), my conscience would be unsettled.  

Why can’t I throw this away?  
Thinking about it, I still lack sophistication and strength.  
We must reveal our true strength through all our struggles—how can I stand away from that?  
It cannot be.  
I am currently entangled in trivial human emotions.  
As a human, I need these emotions and obligations, but because of that, I cannot throw them away and wander with tears.  
The practical XX's sophistication is entirely lacking, and my theoretical cultivation is very thin.  
It’s also true that I am not without the causes of my "now."  
Another major force is the atmospheric pressure of society that I have endured for the past thirty years.  
To break away from the environment means that all of that second nature must be completely utilized in order to escape.  
Knowing but not acting is far worse than being ignorant and not acting.  

However, my efforts do not cease.  

It is said to be the New Year; I have lived half a life, and what remains is half a life.  
I have tasted the worldly pleasures, but now it is time to sever all bonds and advance towards my convictions.  
Even if my life is extinguished now, I would not lament but rather feel little regret.  
At any time, we all either die once or live this life; I would prefer to live short and rich rather than long and thin.  

Those curious about what great vices I might have committed at the end of this so-called confession of a magazine reporter may feel disappointed.  

While it might be rather apologetic that the substance doesn't match the name, I genuinely believe I have done my duty as a magazine reporter, if not a full twelve points but at least seven or eight.  

If someone in a similar situation and state sees this, they will undoubtedly find a same self in it, I believe.  

Confession of a Newspaper Reporter  

A lone sail  

The author's regret.  
Having been a newspaper reporter for over a decade, the admonition comes down to me, much like "You shall settle your past wrongs to this day." I have committed many injustices against others, surely some vile deeds as well, and ultimately, during those ten years as the social department reporter, one might ask, "How many various 'affairs' have you been involved in?"  
"About ten or so," I would reply.  
They might remark, "That's surprisingly few."  
"In the streets, rushing around, if I were to count all the women with whom I engaged for a night, there would be at least two hundred,” then they would exclaim at once, “No way! Really, how could you have had so many?”  
So, in my life as a reporter for a decade, I have encountered people, incidents, and secrets that I have buried deep, resolving to make a clean break from them and unable to come clean, even in front of the most venerable interrogations.  

Once when I was thinking about my past, I said, “Today I went to Jeongjaok to have some diems, and I was surprised that the person who came out with their husband and two little kids was that Zhao XX I had known. I must have misjudged for I felt like a criminal trying to hide away.”  
This Zhao was, of course, that student who had been my plaything a decade ago when I was so enthusiastically wandering around the city. Now, a decade has passed, and she has married, while I remain as I was. She saw me and appeared startled, her pitiable demeanor seemed troubled, and thinking about her happiness in her family, I fled in shame.  
In a society like Joseon, where all is unprofessional, the materials for a social reporter's confession can indeed intrigue readers when it touches on women's affairs.  

“Whether the prime minister is involved or not, an article that muddles public perception with words he dare not utter, may result in a moment of glory.”  

“Or raising a fist in the director's reception room and causing a stir that could have saved a grand event.”  

“Or uncovering the secret personal affair of a baron.”  

“Or a mere misstep with my brush resulting in the bankruptcy of a major company.”  

Such matters would seem like entries worthy of columns in Tokyo or Osaka, yet in our Gyeongseong, they are quite rare, so it concludes that regarding women and such, it cannot be but secretive. The conclusion draws us to this; cannot revealing the edge of a precarious life in the high wire circus like what I did a decade ago remain without confession? The time is now. However, I remember the day the Italian aircraft was to arrive for the first time in Joseon. Lieutenant Ferrarin will land in Yeouido, and the Dong-a Ilbo (East Asia Daily) had even published an extra, performance that turned Gyeongseong upside down. In the archives on the third floor of the Dong-a Ilbo building, there likely still hangs the sign for "perpetual preservation," and the extra edition published that day is there.  

The confession begins for me at this point. Now, Manager Song Jinwoo, who was already a key figure in the Dong-a Ilbo at that time, upon reading this confession of mine might be saying, “Ah—that sly rascal that back then extravagantly made that profligate affair!”  

Therefore, if I am still at the Dong-a Ilbo, this secret confession may very well be prolonged once again. Thus, I am unpretentiously struttin’ my fate，not caring about the judgments of my peers.  

Now, how did that extra report come about?  

“A special report on Lieutenant Ferrarin’s landing in Gyeongseong!”  

Our company promptly presented a floral wreath upon his arrival.  

That special edition was issued in anticipation of the aircraft that was meant to arrive that afternoon, but it was delayed in China. All newspapers were prepared for the extra, anxiously awaiting his arrival.  

At that time, the military command from Yongsan was overseeing the whole operation (and still does most of the time). While the various newspaper offices in Joseon could not completely grasp the communication situation, they waited anxiously at the military command, hoping only to hear the official news of the aircraft.  

The word already came informing that the plane would not arrive until dinner time. Thus, at the army command office, not a single officer was present; only a night watchman was left in place.  

To issue an extra newspaper, I had to spur on that night duty officer.  

It was supposed to be issued that special edition, so I dared to be bold.  

“A car has arrived!”  

Quickly, the urgent report came to my ear like thunder.  

The plan to leave and meeting my lover was not to be.  

I found myself pondering my heavy decision, so I requested to the driver, “Please stop for a moment at the corner by Inkyungjeon in Jongno.”  

The car arrived in less than five minutes. That charming figure, unaware of what was in store, readily awaited me.  

“Is today by car?”  

Not knowing, he happily thought it was an outing.  

I became a little cunning, but the situation didn’t allow for casual conversations. So, I asked him directly,  

“Just please drive.”  

And the car past the gate of the command very safely.  

“Where is this place?”  

It was a troubling question, boldly toting a woman in the broad light of day with a newspaper car.  

“Regardless, don’t worry too much—let’s pretend to be the wife of the newspaper.”  

Finally, after passing the military command office, I was able to be presented to the officer.  Beside me was my beloved and we awaited the news anxiously together.

In the meantime, the messenger dispatched from various other newspaper offices, began loading in the telephone wires.  

“See here, if another newspaper finds out about this, then what will come of us?”  

Thoughts of secrecy flourished through the nervous strain of my heart. But as I made my way there to escape, moving across this path made me anticipate the inevitable revelation.  

A child of about four or five years old stumbled in, followed by the officer’s wife bringing dinner for her husband.  

Seeing this scene, I instantly became aware of myself and said,  

“Dear, you know how women are; let us speak gently to that lady—I'll entertain that little one."  

This shared decision led us to spending the next half hour comfortably and reassuringly with our separate but equal tasks.  

Soon enough the anticipated message rang in,  

“Wait a minute, now that the common affairs we discussed are underway, when will the news that the aircraft crossed Shiniju arrive?”  

No longer caring about discretion I wasted no time in asking the commander the simple question.  

In the middle of milestone messages ordering us to arrange ourselves properly, our little unexpected romantic excursion was successfully navigated.  

Half an hour later, we found another quiet restaurant to share dinner together, celebrating the avoidance of disaster under the cover of night.  

“We are coming out with an extra! The Dong-a Ilbo has issued the extra!”  

It didn’t take long for the Dong-a Ilbo to publish ahead of any other newspaper. While I held the extra edition, I remarked,  

“Thank you so much!”  

To which the clever companion responded with a smile,  

“If it were not for us, they would have thrown us out while their family happily finished their meal without a worry,” she teased, echoing a lighthearted sentiment.  

Yet I also thought to myself,   

“Well, in the end, even if they wanted credit for the effort, they sequenced it perfectly and played their part just right.”  

What a coincidence in this glamorous façade!  

Confession of Mahjong Maniac  

The Mahjong Maniac  

When considering the words of an opium addict, hearing that a person who passes through this life without experiencing the taste of opium has not truly lived.  

Wielding that sentiment, I shall boldly proclaim this:  

In this modern age, if one does not understand the thrill of mahjong, it might as well mean one has not truly lived a life substantial of merit.  

Just as a non-opium user would hardly know the realm of opium’s bliss, a mahjong enthusiast must be entrenched in the game to understand its depths.  

When the tiles are playing around with incredible skill matched with nerve, achieve the thrill that arises when counting out the tiles—nothing else in the world can compare.  

The satisfaction that comes from a white tile on the table or pairs of colorful combinations igniting joy is beyond description.  

Even thrilling moments waiting for 'Chi' while gathering sequences; when the right tiles fall, oh how exhilarating and vibrant it is—the two worlds, love and mahjong, are unmatched.  

For all these reasons, Mahjong draws deep from one’s core, transforming them entirely.  

Since last summer, I became acquainted with mahjong.  

At first, I knew little of its import—just playing around.  

But once I grasped its impact, it wasn't a joke; such experiences demand respect.  

My daily routine became completely consumed by mahjong.  
During that time, I could hardly feel my worries or any pain that pressed within my mind.  

While opium leads to the demise of many, I genuinely believe that mahjong is a preferable alternative; it does not trap you without reprieve, it allows retreat into freedom upon reflecting.  

Some might criticize my bold suggestion, but I insist—  

To the youth burdened with unrequited love leading to suicide—play mahjong a while.  

Surely, in a span of two or three months, the sting of heartbreak can be played through just fine.  

If not, then there lies the opium, waiting.  

I cannot stop for now; I still play mahjong as I did. Numerous times I vowed to quit, but it has proven too powerful and I might spend a whole year still engaged.  

At night, when laying in my dorm, stray thoughts about life wander aimlessly in darkness.  

Drowning any grim thoughts, I find solace jumping into the company of friends shouting "Pong! Chi! Hu!"  

The play on tiles ends up with bedtime arriving, inching past midnight before I succumb to sleep, relying on sleeping pills to drift away.  

As I observe myself, I see how I am slowly becoming a permanent fixture in this game, absurd yet entertaining.  

Confession of a Doctor  

A physician's confession  

I was requested to draft a confession.  

Yet, is there no one untroubled when called upon for confession? 

Just this spring, the school took me in and after not much longer than six months, I am summoned to write about my experience as a physician; what a heavy burden it is. 

However, I cannot reject being presented with this task, but under pressure, I only catch fragments of prior thoughts as I sweep through memories revisiting notes.  

Above all, I ponder if this profession truly suits me, as, in my mind, this position never truly fits.  
Undeniably, why did I ever pursue this line of work?  

I am unsure myself how things went so far—or if it wasn't so unwary of a journey, but after four years of effort, I received my diploma and medical license.  

It’s as if I now realize, with great shock, that regardless of how I see it, what I have come to know is limited.   

The situation today has shed a glaring light on this "noble profession"; what was once a dream career has transformed into one with glaring over-saturation.  

People would insist “Medical occupation is xxxx," merely looking back to several decades ago, with a heavy sigh, for what had been a time of fruitfulness.  

Upon graduating, expectations soar on the horizon—look at the prospect; work would be waiting. The claim bought through hopes of easy life but never ventured nor allowed into existence.  

In choosing to sanction graduation, we few are condemned to echo—that being ‘native doctors’ would be shamed with the qualification of the majority, thus leaving us behind.  

Through this notion, immediate opportunities flicker before me, yet, as we glance down this path, where might it be taking me?  

Another person would proffer a practice but anything here well...there lies the lack upon our part.  

Acceptance of helplessness due to a lack of funds could testify as a barrier to engaging in private practice; it simply does not exist.  

Even if some limited capital does exist, purely being existing leads one back down the wrong path to follow as I word through once more.  

Now since I returned to this city; too many things have stood between the hard choices in practice.  

If you ought to find a proper place, visit luxury establishments to understand; see the single in place or two appearing on the premises and re-scan the medical landscape.  

Amongst private clinics, lying in the shadows of less than reputable backgrounds, I observed how most hold degrees from prestigious universities.  

Concerning that only entreated sickly souls seek you out; in need, the great cooperation exists only among the privileged.  

I consider that side contemplating my needs; while I nearly glance through the colorful tipping point of despair gathering over distant medical reports.  

How could these archaic obstacles slow me down?  

So long as labor is strained, and emotional distancing among master and servant prevails, I could conceive within the nature of grievances solvable only through declarations of sympathy.  

For whatever the county—when people believe only the wealthy or titled amongst the clientele shall prevail, any horned tail which presented upon a teacher held above it would not stand.  

Herein lies the touch of confessions then and now.  

The passage through a district seeking out treatment for our fellow acquaintances, could indeed lend a goodness begotten by folly into kindness and return them again to a stiffened position.  

With time, your eyes may squint through seeing the physician through the other angle; it is truly disheartening.  

The privilege marked not just by risks of scarcity shall filter out to pure silence among mediocrity—while finances or the closeness set in, yet one tainted by unlikely bombardments as time continues pouring onto your path, leaving yet another soul broke or beleaguered by experience.  

For the longer we observe the fraying ties deepening—the rough patch remains intact, dark thought weighs heavy like an anchor.  

Yet once called to confess, troublesome futures seem bluntly reassembled.  
Overwhelmed by the remnants, herein exists my word; proud seem the queries to harvest.  

If the end of confession is difficult in itself—I hereby speak—tethering practice from wall to wall may not yield instant delivery at my learning must be sought furthermore than merely existing on paper.  

Only so much quiet reflected upon and shrouded hurriedly into lines fills the space, leaving breaths seldom released.   

Summary  

The text consists of several reflective essays by a magazine reporter, a newspaper reporter, a doctor, and a mahjong enthusiast, each confession exploring themes of disillusionment and struggle with identity, profession, and ethics. Particularly poignant is the lament of the reporter about the moral compromises faced in the realm of journalism under colonial rule, capturing the zeitgeist of 1930s Korea where personal aspirations clash with societal expectations. Historians might find the self-reflective nature of these confessions interesting, providing insights into the inner lives of individuals navigating the complexities of life under Japanese colonialism.

雜誌記者懺悔

浩然堂人

이것은 잡지기자의 참회라 하는 것보담도 當티 아니한 환경에 誤植된 한 젊은 콤XXXX의 과거청산의 고민이라 함이 可하겟다.

여학교방문을 가게 된 일이 잇섯다.
형식으로 보나 記事 取扱의 내용으로 보나 여학교방문이란 여자에게 맛당한 일이다.
맛당히 여자가 할 일을 사내놈이 끄덕끄덕하고 가는 나의 우숫광스러운 그림자에서 나는 어리ㅅ광대를 발견하엿다.
어리ㅅ광대! 피에로....우수꽝스럽게 차림차리를 하고 남을 웃기며 제가 웃는 어리ㅅ광대....피에로!
그러나 어리ㅅ광대의 우슴속에는 눈물이 잇나니.
어리ㅅ광대 아닌 어리ㅅ광대의 우수꽝스러운 그림자 속에서는 더욱 恨겨웁고 憤겨운 눈물이 잇나니.

맛당히 해야할 일을 제체노코 하기 실흔 일이나 하지 못할<96> 일을 하게 되는 것이 誤植된 인생의 비애요. 맛당히 써야할 글을 제체두고 쓰기 실흔 글이나 쓰지 못할 글을 쓰게 되는 것이 軌道에서 밋그러진 짜나리스트의 고통이다.
사람이 세상에 생겨나 지닌 바 抱負에 節을 직히지 못할지면 그 생명에 무슨 광채가 잇스랴.
마음에 업는 義를 부르지지며 뜻에 업는 論을 함이 엇지 마음에 업슨 노래를 부르며 뜻에 업는 우슴을 웃는 娼婦와 달음이 잇스랴.
蒼白한 인테리의 智的 賣淫이여! 나는 내 자신을 이러커 咀呪한다.
차라리 「쇠사슬 이외에는 더 이즐 것이 업는 사람」으로 태어나거나 그러치 아니하면 타고난 宿命에 自足하는 뿌르의 忠犬....자유주의의 使徒로서 일생을 맛티든지 할 것이지 누구에게 强制를 當한 배도 업고 또 그래야 할 필요도 업서스면서 왜 방향을 밧구게 되엿든가!
그러나 주사위는 이미 던젓다.
運은 결정이 되엿다.
남은 것은 시간이다.
나는 맹세한다-목숨으로써 「現在」와 양립티 아니하기를 맹세를 한다.
무기력한 인테리 業을 하로 밥비 청산하고 나서 황금의 찬란한 배경 압헤 劒光이 시퍼런 XX-그것에 부드처 버혀지고 버혀지고 수업시 버혀져 새빨간 피가 淋漓하야 그러하야 그 날카라운 劍光이 무듸여지고 필경은 불어지고 하야 殺戮의 밤이 새이는 고요한 아츰 날이 도다 오를지...

세상을 아는 듯한 사람도 손을 꼿고 서서 必然만 기다린다.
그러나 필연은 필연으로만 나아나지를 못한다.
나로토니-들을 본바든 熱이 업시는 아모 것도 오지 아니한다. 그들의 熱만은 우리에게 큰 敎訓이다.

신문기자로 二年.....
그동안의 생활은 때아닌 떼카단이씀에 술과 게집에 파뭇티어 아모런 자각과 성찰이 업섯스니 말할 것도 업다.
根據薄弱한 민족적 적개심과 야욕적 자유주의에 침체된 몰락한 중산계급의 막동아들이 如意티 못한 세상에서 그와가 된 것도 결코 무리는 아니엇슬 것이다.

빈민환자가 진찰권은 겨우 사가지고 와서 診察를 밧는다. 그런 뒤에 처치를 밧거나 처방전을 바더가지고 출납계로 가서 먼저 處置料나 藥價를 준 다음에야 약을 어더 가지고 가게 된다. 그러나 이 빈민이 처치료나 약가를 가지고 왓슬 리가 잇는가 돈이 업다고 한다. 그러면 발서 일홈 조흔 간호부의 얼골빗부터 달너지며 말버릇부터 달너진다. 바로 제 수입이나 주러지는 듯이 그러니 약을 못어더가는 가는 것은 말할 것도 업거니와 기왕 처치햇든<106> 붕대조차 끌너서 빼서 바린다. 물론 그네들도 제 마음대로 그러는건 안이다. 제 위로 의사선생님이 게시고 그 위로 감독관청이 잇스니 하는 수야 잇는가만은 처치햇든 붕대까지 끌너바리는 것은 넘어 과하지 안는가. 이것도 갓치 돈 안 가지고 온 사람이라도 의복이나 깨끗이 입고온 환자에게는 엇더한가 흔년히 내일 올 때 가지고 오라고 해 보낸다. 얼마나 기막힐 일인가 또 한 약가나 처치료에 잇서서도 보자. 아무리 기술이요 생명을 구한다고 할지라도 그런 폭리가 어데 잇슬가. 그 原料에 잇서서는 더 자세히 말치 안는다만은 기술이라고 하면 의술만이 기술일가 우리의 생명을 무엇보다도 직접 지지하고 잇는 밥을 짓는 것도 기술이 아닐가. 밥 짓는 사람의 그 기술에 대한 報酬에 비해 보아라.
나는 이런 것을 볼 때에 붓그럼을 참지 못한다 내 호을로 생각할 때도 만타. 다른 사람이나 더러 이럭헤 뭇는다면 나는 무슨 말로 대답을 할가 과연 할말이 업다. 대체 또 기술이라고 하니 얼마나 큰 기술인가. 물론 특수한 것도 만치만은 웬만한 것은 사러 잇스니 산 사람이니 나어간다. 내과에서나 외과에서 잇서서 특수한 걸 제한 외에는 이렁저렁하는 동안에 환자 스사로 나어가는 것도 적지 안타.
되지 안흔게 길어만가고 길어가니 탈선이 만타. 하여간 나로서는 이 직업이 어는 점으로 보든지 제 소질이나 성격어는 방면으로 보든지 맛지 안는 것만은 사실이다. 크게 잘못 들은 길이다. 이것은 다른 것과 달너 업는 점잔도 내야 된다. 체모도 보아야 된다. 그러자니 업는 돈이라도 엇턱헤 해서 해지지 안흔 양복도 입어야 된다.
고맙지 못한 직업이다. 그나마도 깻닥 잘못하면 면허장조차 빼앗기게 된다. 그라도 빼앗겨 바리고나면 그날부터 밥줄 곳이라고는 씨나다나 경찰서나 형무소밧게 업슬 것이다. 이 사회에서 누가 아무 재조엄는 나에게 밥을 줄 것이냐?
