Choi: Regardless, if we are looking for horror now, it wouldn't bring much to the table.  

Park: I have a particularly fascinating ghost story. It recounts something that happened in Hong-Ro Province, which was supposedly seen directly. A maiden was engaged at her cousin's maternal family home but passed away before the wedding, and her spirit supposedly came back to haunt them!  

Bang: What kind of place is that?  

Park: It's her own home... adorned in a red jeogori (jackets) and a blue chima (skirt) - just like a bride on her way to marriage. She appears and is seen by everyone, laughing heartily.  

Lee: What do you mean by "laughing"?  

Park: It means she laughed...  

Cha: I mean "rice" in the context of the story.  

Park: Oh, right! (finally) It says she bursts forth from behind the rice straw while laughing. However, only one or two people see her, and she wanders about laughing and acting all sorts of odd. Yet, her brother is genuinely frightened. So when he calls out sternly, the spirit laughs from the cedar tree behind their house.    

Bang: But wait—doesn't that reveal her entire body?  

Park: So she just plays around and acts silly, but there's no significant story or meaning behind her presence? For example, what kind of spirit is she?  

Park: There doesn't appear to be anything significant... Since this family just worried so much about the ghost, they consulted a fortune teller. Upon consultation, they were told that a spirit has taken residence in that cedar tree...

So they had cremated the tree, but the ghost still continued to laugh away as before... 

All: Ha ha...

Park: What’s stranger is that when her brother scolds loudly, she announces, "I will return to my brother's house," and disappears towards a nearby village where the sound of a palanquin had been heard carrying her away before a death notification arrived saying it had crashed, killing someone. 

Choi: Where did that person die?  

Park: Did anyone from Hong-Ro Province witness that?  

Park: Oh, no...

Cha: I have a somewhat similar story too. Near the path to the Central School in Pak-Dong, there used to be a well... Ah, it was quite a while ago when I was in technical school... That well has since been filled in... But back then, if a drunk person passed by...

Bang: You can’t trust the words of a drunken person?

Cha: No, it's not just for the drunk; several sober people encountered this as well. During the dark of night, a host would be sitting with a spread of liquor, and when they drunkenly stand up, they offer, "Why not have a drink?" Not a day later, a corpse would float up in that well...  

Bang: So, for those who have experiences, they must all have perished. But people who had witnessed it seem to narrate the details well?   

Cha: A cousin of a friend of mine who was a public servant almost drowned in that well, and I saved him. 

Lee: For real! When I was in elementary school, I saw someone drown in that very well.  

Bang: However, when it comes to ghostly tales about wells, everyone tends to have preconceptions. For instance, if two people drown in the same well, anyone passing afterwards would think that spirits might appear, becoming self-hypnotized into belief.

The people in that house, they also went “Fire!” and it caused quite a commotion.

Choi: Of course not!  

Bang: Were there children in the house?

**Summary**  

The dialogue among the participants dives into various local ghost stories, misogynistic views, and the evolving attitudes towards women and gender roles in early 20th-century Korea. The discussions reflect a blend of traditional beliefs, a satirical take on societal norms, and cultural narratives shared among peers. Historians might find the casual, comedic discourse significant, showcasing the blend of folklore, the struggles of colonial youth, and their adaptations in a rapidly changing society amidst colonial pressures.
