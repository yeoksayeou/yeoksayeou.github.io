Five Teams of Reporters Dispatch

Especially for you, Miss

A Reporter

"Who is it?"
"It’s me."
"It’s me?!"
That insolent fellow! A city officer is throttling his voice, undoubtedly feeling intimidation as air is thick with tension—no, even more grave than that.

It was the early morning of October 25, 1932, around 3 a.m. in a narrow alley of Bukchon, Soqyeok-dong, where the incident occurred… This is how it goes.

The reporter was walking alongside Miss C from Cafe XX, exchanging stories sweeter and more fragrant than a 'dobu bus' (a type of sweet dessert), heading towards Miss C's home when, unexpectedly, they ran into a police officer.

The response from the reporter appeared to fluster the officer, who in the dark night was glaring with sharp, glistening eyes.

"Where are you going?"
"I'm going to that house."
"Where is that?"
"It's at Baek XX, Soqyeok-dong."

The officer then interjected with more questions directed at Miss C.

"Where is your house?"
"It's at Baek XX, Soqyeok-dong."

Providing arbitrary addresses without hesitation, Miss C answered adorably with a bright voice.

"What’s your name?"
"My name? I’m Park Chun-gwon—this is my younger sibling."
"What’s your name?"
"It’s Jeon Yeong-sil." Oh, what an unfortunate child! The older brother's surname is Park while the younger's is Jeon.

"I’m basically her cousin."

Navigating this awkward situation to break the tension, the conversation became more strained. The officer smiled softly. The situation was turning increasingly complicated, casting a shadow over what might happen subsequently.

"No matter how you look at it, something seems suspicious! Speak honestly now," the police officer continued to throttle his voice again. It seemed as if embarrassment was inevitable. It wasn’t out of concern but rather because the poor Miss C could end up in trouble. What should she do?!

"I’m telling the truth. We have a ritual at our house today. This child came to visit my younger sibling and now it’s late. Can’t I see her home? So I’m walking her home."

"Where is your house?"
"It’s in Yeon-gyeon-dong."
"And your occupation?"
"I’m at the XX Bank’s financial department."
"Do you have a business card?"
"I don’t have one on me at this moment, but it’s true."

As the officer discerned her tentative honesty, he seemed more relaxed.

"Honestly, things have become more ominous lately, so what’s more, to be out wandering around at this hour. Especially young girls like you…"

Finally considering that his words had been kind of a warning, he spoke, "You really shouldn’t do this in future."

"It’s especially because of you, sir," the officer emphasized as if scolding a daughter, revealing he might have some intuition about Miss C's true nature—or perhaps he is simply showing her a bit of kindness.

"Go on!" With that, we walked on, aligning our steps as if we were marching students. Sweet stories began to whisper again.

"Hey! That was embarrassing! That young lady, Jeon Yeong-sil, should have just said her name was Park Yeong-sil..."

"What a frightful situation I found myself in, walking out at a time like this."

"Anyway, it doesn’t matter now." 

With that, the reporter gently pressed his lips against Miss C's forehead. After this, they reached her door.

Dear readers! What happens next may remain a mystery. That is to say, he only saw her home and nothing more. Wandering through the narrow alley of Bukchon as aimlessly as one might drop a coin, he happened to encounter Miss C again on her way back home. 

Thus concludes the reporter's investigation.

Eating Sweet Potato Songpyeon and the Great Encounter with a Female Student

B Reporter

If I embark on an investigation after midnight, I would surely lose sleep, so I decided to take a quick nap early in the evening. However, I had just fallen asleep, and suddenly, a loud noise jolted me awake. Oh no! It was already 1:10 a.m.! 

Hurriedly, I put on my pants, light coat, and hastily donned my hat, then lit a cigarette before rushing out through the front door. If there were someone at home, even if it were late, I wouldn’t hesitate to go out. However, alone, venturing far made me uneasy. 

Well then, I decided to just go to the place closest to me and proceeded through Hong Sul-hae alley, heading towards Gye-dong.

As I entered a side alley in Gye-dong, I passed a few houses and heard sounds of an argument between a man and a woman from the other alley. 

Arriving there, I saw the man, surprisingly, was a sweet potato songpyeon (sweet rice cake) vendor, and the woman seemed like a female student. The sweet potato vendor stood by the window, while the student shouted from inside.

Man: “Hurry and give it to me! I already owe 30 jeon from before, and if you don’t give it to me again, how am I supposed to do business? If you had said from the start it was on credit, I would have understood but... ”

Woman (in a Gyeongsang-do dialect): “Oh, come on! What are you talking about? I’m a regular who always comes! Who would eat all this without paying… Just leave and come back tomorrow.”

Man: “You’re saying that, huh? Yet, for me, I need to eat tomorrow morning! If you don't give my 10 jeon worth for today, how can I live? I mean, it’s not like this is my first time asking.”

The woman's tone was very displeased, and she shut the window loudly.

Man: “Even if you close the window, you should still pay! If you can’t pay, at least write me a note saying when you will...” 

The vendor kept insisting, and ultimately the female student, left with no choice, threw a piece of paper through the window while saying she would come back after a few days. The vendor grumblingly accepted it and went away with it.

At that moment, I was curious about the situation, so I followed the sweet potato vendor and asked, “Hey, why are you out in the middle of the night arguing with a girl? Who is she?”

Vendor: “I don’t really know. She’s probably a student. She used to come with some guy and paid well for her food, but she seems to be struggling money-wise lately. She ate 30 jeon worth before and now it seems she can only afford 10 jeon.”

I said, "Well then, let me take a look at that note she wrote."

Vendor: “Why do you want to see it? If you must, then look.”

I took the note, expecting it to be a letter from a dear person to her, and went to a nearby lamp post to read it. The content was as follows:

"Pay 40 jeon by the 30th."

Showa 7, October 26

Gye-dong OO 

Lee O-ja

Judging by the handwriting and content, it seemed like it must have been written by someone well beyond just a typical student or a graduate. Upon asking further, I found out that she was a third-year student at a girls' school in the city. 

For a student staying away from home, it’s not too strange to eat something like sweet potato songpyeon on credit if she’s hungry, but writing a note for 40 jeon feels slightly embarrassing. 

I continued to walk out, feeling as though I had gathered some material and my adventure was quite successful. 

While pushing ahead, I aimed towards the end of Gye-dong when suddenly, out of nowhere, a voice called "Hey, hey!" It startled me as I found the police officers.

They rushed into the street as if they were arresting someone. When I approached, I discovered that we knew each other. He said that recently due to so-called pawn shop robbery incidents, which happened late at night, it’s increasingly dangerous. Unless it’s something really urgent, he recommended I go home early tonight. 

Listening to him made sense, and I didn't feel up for further adventure. I turned around and headed back home; it was already 1:53 a.m. 

Mysterious Street Entrances

C Reporter

The sound of footsteps crunching on the sand beneath my shoes hits my ears suddenly. My feet turn towards the north at Donggwan intersection.

The drinkers at XX Roo have quieted down, and from behind empty windows, the clock chimes 3 a.m.

From midnight until 3 a.m.! - Perhaps it’s just my luck, but after three hours of wandering, not a single interesting story has materialized.

Finally grasping at a shred of hope, I entered the back alley that was infamous for its brothels in Seoul.

Even this place, usually filled with lively sounds, seemed so lonely now, even devoid of the lively sounds that used to fill these streets.

Now, it’s time to return, so I might as well make the best of it. Gathering my nerves, I anxiously made my way northward; I felt as if I looked quite pitiful under anyone's scrutiny.

Suddenly, two men in suits popped out from a corner alley and said, "Hey!”

Startled and surprised, I comprehended that they were detectives. Apparently, the atmosphere is thick with tension due to recent pawn-shop burglary incidents.

I kept walking and soon, as if to save my rationality, I stumbled upon a pair of "couples" in the streets amongst the hustling crowd. 

“Just go ahead,” one said in an alluring tone, resembling the seductive commands of women at brothels.

“Can’t! Wait here. What if someone comes?” 

“Who’s coming? No worries! Just watch!” 

Soon thereafter, in the silence, they engaged in muffled breathing and... 

I had inadvertently discovered a rather interesting scene, and I couldn't move in to disturb it. Nevertheless, they were so enraptured in their moment of passion that they remained oblivious to my presence.

Even as I silently watched, the mannerisms were most fascinating and hard to articulate—an extraordinary vision.

Suddenly, I spoke up, “My goodness—what’s going on here?”

Turning swiftly, I took off, hearing whispers and movements behind me as I departed. 

The Lonely Street

D Reporter

Once, it was said that the bell tolled at midnight. But now, even the bell does not toll.

The streets of Jongno, these lonely four corners, were dark and desolate. The streetlights were dim enough that I could flick them with a finger, creating shadows.

Inside Cafe B, I waited for minutes after midnight, alone, as I planned to explore the city after midnight, feeling rather lonely.

There were my friends, R from M Newspaper, H from C Newspaper, and tall K, sitting there. Their conversations initially revolved around the gunfire incident, but soon began to take on a stale tone.

As they left the table, my anxiety rose. They poured me tea over my indifferent expression, and I had no better anecdote to share, leaving me speechless in silence.

A waiter boy who appeared quite endearing quietly tidied up a chair. 

It was one o'clock.

Ah! It’s time for a midnight stroll through Seoul. In the heart of the city, Jongno.

As I exited Cafe B, my newspaper friends followed closely behind. They seemed reluctant to part as they thronged up to the street.

R, who lived in S-dong, was heavily drunk and shouted, “I’ll see you tomorrow!” and hopped into a rickshaw flowing through the street. Left behind, K found himself in the same situation, jumping in as well.

H and I also decided to join in the rickshaw, heading westward.

The rain seemed imminent. With an approaching storm and gathering clouds, it felt inevitable.

The dark streets of Jongno, the desolate Jongno. The lights of S Department Store barely flickered in the gloom.

Nonetheless, the rickshaw lights glimmered along the street, illuminating the path.

From that direction approached a group of young men. As I strode past them, I noticed they were the teachers from C School and the headmaster from P School.

Crossing the YMCA, I spotted some more youthful figures weaving back from Cafe R, their movements hinting at a late-night jaunt.

On the vast boulevard of Jongno, this was the only atmosphere that maintained its composure and silence, as I lingered alone amidst shadows while gazing into the intersections, feeling the piercing sweep of a police officer's sword shining brightly. 

Standing at Cafe R, I didn’t hear much of a lively voice. I had crossed the threshold towards C Theater, but even that was shrouded in darkness, their signs angled in odd positions, and no flicker of light stirred.

Before I reached the main street by the grand automobiles, I could hear faint sounds of laughter echoing from a distance. In this prominent street, the entertainers and workers ambled around aimlessly.

It’s typical to see locked windows. Ah! The notorious Choonwonok tavern, and I spotted a gray overcoat on one of the middle-aged friends coming out.

Called renowned, this place has gone through numerous changes. The light of Venus offered dwindling hope. Reports said that Cafe A had closed just three weeks after its opening. Ah, such desolation!

As I continued upward, these streets offered nothing to haunt me yet provide a moment of intrigue. Now rising through the shadows, I turned back at S Girls’ School, catching sight of a lone female student. 

What could this tall student be doing at such an hour? However, erasing my notions led me to contemplate that I was on a cursed path meant solely for exploration. 

With minimal company, I crossed into Songhyeon-dong alley. 

The night stretched long into “Manju-noho,” yet still... 

Finally reaching home, I found it was exactly 2 a.m. Wrapped in my blanket, I began to transcribe this account. 


The Beauty of Venus with Apollo and Bacchus

E Reporter

On the X day of X month, at X o'clock in the afternoon. 

Tonight, late into the evening, "What’s going on in Seoul?" I couldn’t refuse the editor’s command. 

The weather turned chilly, and I received a few coins to buy some makgeolli (rice wine) which clinked around in my pocket as I set off uncertainly, feeling miserable and uneasy. 

Though my stomach was full from dinner, I still didn't feel hungry, so I wandered purposelessly about the streets, wishing at least for some scraps to be found. The glimmer of dissatisfaction loomed as the night deepened and dawn approached. Worries about my writing tomorrow started haunting me, and I felt embarrassed for the empty coins of makgeolli lingering in my pocket. 

“Am I just a reporter?” I thought to myself resentfully while trudging along. 

But I needed to find something, anything, to put together for tomorrow’s submission to the editor. That being said, I couldn’t help but feel shy and insecure as I saw the light of a distant bar glimmering invitingly.

"Bar Vonus" (바뷔너스)—across the glass door, the sign laid with golden letters, signifying it as a place for various activities from drinking to gathering. 

So, in the spirit of curiosity and perhaps a bit shyly, I resolved to enter, clutching the 10 jeon remaining tightly in my pocket. 

Indeed, it was quite cozy. 

Immediately, I noticed the alluring beauty, the goddess Venus, sitting at the forefront of the establishment, with patrons filling up tables either alone, two together, or in groups; each quietly sipping drinks as if lost in their own world. 

In every way possible, the scene resembled a courtroom where many awaited their judgment, and there was a quiet ambiance scrubbing away the loudness typical of such places. 

Madame! Was it even in English? Being the goddess Venus here, she presides over everyone comfortably. 

Among the various patrons gathered there, one figured sorrowful individuals sharing drinks. Yet, it felt like I was merely one among the throng, waiting for justice under the protective yet uncertain gaze of Madame Venus.

Honestly, the atmosphere felt incredibly heavy. Here they were on the brink of understanding the vagueness of existence, while I, on my part, was caught with the triviality of ordering a cup of tea priced at 10 jeon.

Yet nothing was profound anymore; the presence of the collective made it unnaturally silent. 

The night wore on, the patrons becoming restless, and I sat back with my mundane cup of tea, wishing desperately for my time in this space to come again, lost within the resonance of “Madame Venus, oh!” 


**Summary**

This article presents a snapshot of a night in 1932 Seoul, featuring various reporters and their experiences as they explore the city after dark. Interactions between women and men, police scrutiny, and the struggles of urban life amid a colonized Korea reveal the complexities and uncertainties of social relations during that time. The narrative touches on themes of class, gender, and the social dynamics prevalent in modern urban settings, as well as the challenges faced by students and young women under colonial rule.

Historians might find this article interesting for its depiction of nightlife in Seoul during the Japanese colonial period, reflecting the tensions between traditional values and modern influences. It illustrates how urbanization shaped social interactions and explores the lives of everyday individuals against a backdrop of political oppression.

고구마송편 먹고 女學生의 大逢變
