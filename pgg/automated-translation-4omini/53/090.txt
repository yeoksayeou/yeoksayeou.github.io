기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue)  

Rice Price Problem: Agricultural Department Round Table Discussion  

Participants: Kim Myŏng-nyu, Pak Ch'ŏn-sŏ, Chŏng Yun-mo, I P'ung-ch'ŏn, and other 5 or 6 members from the agricultural military.  

Kim: What's up? Have you all had dinner already?  
All: Oh! Myŏng-nyu, it seems like dinner is quite rich today. This is new.  
Kim: What a lot of cigarettes you are smoking. The smell is thick in the room; could you open a window?  
Chŏng: What does that matter? To be honest, all cigarettes are harsh regardless. Even if you smoke a type like jiksa-mi (キサミ), you wouldn't know it's tough. Back in the day, before the neighborhood brand cigarette and guryŏn (a type of cigarette), smoking the leaves right after harvest was something awesome.  
Pak: Yes, it's just a name for cigarettes, isn't it? It makes smoke come out, but there's no real flavor to it.  
I: I heard that at Jeong's house, they harvested the back field today. How much? I guess about a dozen sacks?  
Chŏng: Where did you harvest it? From Holtaek (a type of harvesting method).  
All: Yes, indeed, is there still harvesting left now? Using the Holtaek method? Indeed, harvesting is crucial; it's crucial. When you're harvesting, if you meet the county office people, who could say anything about the scenery?  
Chŏng: No kidding, laying mats and doing Holtaek functions but, after all, the rice is indeed good.  
All: Oh, you’ve just returned from school in Seoul and seem all in for modern techniques, but during the old days, we used to do night harvests in groups of ten and sing as we went. Do you think we couldn’t eat rice because of stones in it?  
Kim: Right, right; saying to lay down a mat and do other chores just favors those profiteering from rice trading. Asking us to harvest Holtaek makes it harder to find anything to eat. Back then, with the mortar inside, our supplies were definitely more abundant.  
All: Myŏng-nyu, you’re saying this year's farming improvements are leading to increased poverty.  
Chŏng: Well… not entirely. Things are hard, so if we can improve outputs from one sack to two, and make better rice, then that will help.  
All: Ah! That person is stubborn, isn’t he? Those who have studied recently seem duller; did the rice prices go up more than before?  
Chŏng: That’s contingent on the market; there may be such cases. There's no law that says it should only rise.  
All: So your point makes sense then; in that way, rice prices soar according to the market. Holtaek harvested rice can’t sell much cheaper than others. Nowadays, even Holtaek harvested rice sent to the market is much less than it was during the harvest three years ago.  
Chŏng: That's an unreasonable statement. The prices were notably different back then and now. You shouldn’t compare them. But indeed, when you compare them, the stones and mixture of our rice and the clean rice and other aspects clearly show differences, don’t they?  
All: That’s just a marginal issue; whether differences occur slightly affects us as farmers. It’s a matter for rice traders and landlords, not us. We usually can manage only about two sacks of rice, and those trading at seven sacks can afford to make rice prices become big deals.  
I: Really, what do you mean discussing these things? What’s crucial at this point is how much rice you harvested from Jeong’s field yesterday? We’re dragging the conversation.  
All: This kid! You’d be fine as long as prices go up! If your rice is four sacks worth, you’re already in luck. It seems like one sack costs seven won; after working hard for a year, you'd barely get twenty-eight won. Come on pause!  
I: Don't worry too much, we just have to store and wait for the prices to rise next spring when they rise, right? We have no taxes, nothing encumbering us, and life goes on.  
All: Hey you! You’re a bit too carefree!  
I: Doing this and that means we need to know how much rice was harvested from Jeong’s place?  
Chŏng: Ah! I harvested about twelve sacks today; this year wasn’t good, but it should be able to reach thirteen to fourteen sacks after all, but that shiny fertilizer wouldn't release well.  
I: You got greedy and overdid it.  
Kim: I heard that Ch'ŏn Seo's round field also harvested today. How much did they get?  
Pak: Don’t talk so casually. Still, us tenant farmers at least get something small to be satisfied with about. However, that scoundrel of an agricultural clerk brought scales and measured, measuring down to the last detail, taking even our crappy rice. They charge us for everything measuring out with painful precision. We barely made eight sacks! Plus, the agricultural clerk argues and requests reductions until only three or four sacks remain! Plus, how can we even inspect them afterward? Still, today the agricultural clerk was coming, and he did have some meat and side dishes for dinner! What are we going to eat from our farming?  
All: Hey! It’s also somehow a fair calculation. At least we all have some harvest or others might be empty. It seems ridiculous that some farmers actually get a proper yield. By the way, Yoon, did today’s news arrive? If it did, read it!  
Chŏng: The rice price problem is mentioned in today’s news again. You all should check too.  

Due to the impact of hail and heavy rain, the harvest amounts to about 500,000 sacks which is about 16 million sacks in total, but will the rice prices still not be different?  
All: And so it goes, seems like there's been another report indicating 16.5 million sacks, that’s higher than last year’s harvest! Why did hail have to do so much damage?  
Chŏng: It seems in this region it’s not so bad, but in harder hit areas, it’s serious. I've heard some areas faced total destruction, where a single plot yields a mere one sack. This makes the loss of 50 or 60 thousand sacks across the whole of Korea look trivial, how can that not happen?  

I: Speaking of which, Jeong, wasn’t your last phrase poorly understood?  
Chŏng: Yes! It was said, “the rice price will be bad.”  
I: What does that mean?  
Chŏng: It implies that rice prices aren't expected to go up.  
I: How could this be? In years of excellent harvests, shouldn’t rice prices drop when there’s a bounty?  
Chŏng: Well, that might often hold, but the connection isn't always straightforward; fundamentally, the market pricing during the harvest is what truly matters.  

All: What will the price of this year’s crop be?  
Chŏng: That’s a little complicated to answer right now.  
Kim: Hey! This guy, academia has made him arrogant. He's just going to be looking down on the farmers like us. What value would that have?  
All: Yes, indeed! Since we’re all farmers here! Now let’s have an honest conversation!  
Chŏng: Hey, you shouldn’t tease him too much. If he flops down, it could lead to trouble!  
I: It’s not teasing, it’s just facts. There’s no one who knows these matters. When you can’t even get a newspaper, what can be done?  

Chŏng: Well, talking about Japanese agriculture, there’s a system called the standard rice price (基準米價) you know. It’s where the government legislatively sets a standard based on the market prices and the cost of farming. They research in detail and set the rice price. That standard rice price is fixed at, for instance, 20 won for a sack of rice, it may rise by a suffix when market prices rise, but it can’t go too high—like say over 24 won. Whereas if rice prices drop, they’d only allow it to go down to about 16 won.  
All: That’s an interesting model. It would be good for farmers. To everyone, rice is everything. You need rice to live, but the way prices fluctuate chaotically makes it hard to survive. If circumstances were good, it should assist the general farmer, yet when autumn arrives and it’s time to sell, prices become miserable! In the end, the riches are left behind while ordinary farmers are basically left only with brown rice.  

I: What is this law to be applied in Korea?  
All: You’re just saying this while lacking understanding. Who would apply that law in Korea?  
I: If not, it would be of no use for Korea at all.  
Chŏng: No, that’s not true. Regardless of that, Korea’s rice prices tend to follow Japanese rice prices; they’re influenced by fluctuations.  
Pak: Yes. While there might be some influence, if there was a real legal framework, it would be another situation altogether. Traders, cunning as they might be, find weak points in the market. When it comes to ordinary farming families selling their rice, we need to sell at peak times, which are hardest financially, trading when the harvest concludes. The traders make a profit by coming early, and once the poor have sold all their rice, prices rise. It’s a despicable cycle.  

All: So what’s the outlook for this year’s rice prices? Yoon!  
Chŏng: According to last news articles, Japan’s standard rice price this year was set at 20 won and 58 jeon.  
All: Why’s that so different from the previous figure of 6 won or 7 won for a sack of rice? If the price is 20 won and 58 jeon, then rice could be around 10 won?  
Pak: That's only for Japan. What fortune would come to Korea with that?  
Chŏng: Even when said, that’s based upon Japanese rice; that 20 won and 58 jeon reflects urban districts’ grain consumption standards where the prices might be around 1 won and 50 jeon cheaper. So that makes a rough market price for rural areas around 19 won.  

Kim: But that’s primarily considering Japan’s rural market, where Korea's local pricing doesn't bear out like that.  
Chŏng: Right! Whenever you mention Japanese rice prices, expect Korea’s prices to have a fixed difference of around three won. So in Korea, it makes an unrealistic presumed price for rice at about 16 won, while for barley or other grains, costs would be around 8 won.  
Pak: Hey, this guy claims 8 won is the going rate, so farmers might accept that price!  
Kim: Even at 8 won, perhaps it’s about the previous figures; the trade's benefits are popular since the farmer's metrics of market influence damage their income through added costs like weights, transport, and additional taxes, but ultimately perhaps the amount finally reflecting to be around 7 won.  

All: Right, anyway, as long as some money comes back to us, let it be that way!  
I: Yet the Japanese government is buying Korean rice, right? Does that influence prices in Korea?  
Kim: What would change? What massive benefit might come to us?  
Chŏng: The government plans to first buy 400,000 sacks of rice from Korea starting from late November to early December, isn’t that the lowest at this current price? I doubt most gain from it.  
Pak: Furthermore, it seems there’s the recent Korean rice taxation project (朝鮮米稅制案) being planned; what does that mean?  
Chŏng: Ah! That project does not bode well for us. The rice prices in Korea have been poor, meaning rice flowing into Japan would eventually influence prices. Eventually, the Japanese farmers may suffer economic strain leading to larger fallout, as drastic situations are becoming increasingly frequent today; thus, the farmers of Korea will end up being the ones who will suffer yet again.  

**Summary**  

This dialogue revolves around the prices of rice and the agricultural challenges faced by Korean farmers during the colonial period. The participants discuss the impact of harvesting methods, market inequalities, and the influence of Japanese price stabilization laws, highlighting the mistrust towards profit-driven traders and critiques of agricultural administration. The article sheds light on the plight of Korean farmers under Japanese rule, emphasizing their struggles with both foreign policies and local market conditions. Historians might find it particularly revealing for understanding economic exploitation and resistance among Korean agricultural communities during this era.
