A Strange and Rare Figure: The Giant Paektu Mountain, Rather an Expedition than a Visit  
A Reporter  
Little kids or small ones! Everyone gives an unflattering nickname to short people. If someone is just over five feet tall, with a sparse beard of a few hairs on their chin, that nickname would hardly be applicable. However, if someone stands over five feet tall, becoming a middle height from 6 to 7 and a tall person at 8, or when crossing 9, it is fragrant to say 'six feet tall.' Such a person receives nicknames like a tall pole, tall general, and so on.

It is true that height means something, but if someone is like a dry fish stick, only having height while looking wretched as they flounder about, they indeed are fitting of their nickname, uncanny. 

Among Westerners, it is not uncommon to see people over six feet tall, but in Korea, it is rarely seen. Bigger is definitely better in Korea, and if such a person were to remain here, they would deserve to be referred to as a rare talent. And if there were someone significantly taller than this, could they not even be qualified as a national treasure of Korea?

I personally went out with Mr. Kim Bugui (김부귀) to stroll about Nakwon-dong, where the unusual national treasure, Big Paektu Mountain (백두산), resides. When I knocked on the front door of the house of his acquaintance, I asked the servant, “Is there someone named Paektu Mountain here?” 

They replied, “Paektu Mountain??.... Yes, yes. Is it the tall guest?” Then they peeked through the middle door of the inner room and said, “Since I see shoes, it must be him.” I also peeked and saw, indeed, a pair of shoes large enough to have been exhibited at an exhibition.

Opening the door, I called out “Is anyone here!” but there was no answer. Calling again, only then did a deep voice respond from inside, and the door opened, revealing the face of the giant. 

“Who is it?” 

Seeing Paektu Mountain merely in spoken word until now, when actually facing him, I felt a sense of fear due to his sheer size. Taking off my hat with a polite demeanor and even a smile, I muttered, “I came to see you...” feeling a bit embarrassed. 

“I was getting up early this morning,” he replied. 

“No, I wanted to see you, not the host...” 

“Me? What is it about?” 

He spoke clumsily, much like hauling a heavy oak lumber. I recklessly went up to the raised floor and said, “Then let me excuse myself for a moment,” and stepped into the room. After sitting down on the indicated cushion, I showed him my business card. 

Mr. Paektu Mountain stared at it for some time. 

“What is the matter?” he asked, revealing a noticeable difficulty with written words. 

“I’m with a magazine. I have heard that your stature is quite remarkable, and I came to request you talk a bit about it.”

Only then did it seem that he understood my intention, he nodded and said, “Yes, if so.” His tone became more affable, and the intimidating atmosphere eased.

“What is your real name?” 

“I’m called Park Han-kyu (박한규).” 

“Where is your hometown?” 

“I’m from Cheongju (청주), Chungcheong-do.” 

“How old are you this year?” 

“I’m twenty-eight.” 

“Then how did you get the nickname Paektu Mountain?” 

“Yes! I received that name while acting as a wrestler in Japan.” 

This police-style inquiry finished straightforwardly here. Before proceeding to further questions, I would like to comment on his physique: he stands at seven feet one inch tall, weighs two hundred twenty-one pounds, has a foot one foot three inches long, and fingers five inches long. 

“Is this your first time in Seoul?” 

“I came once before, but not as tall as I am now.” 

“When did your height start to increase like this?” 

“It was fine when I was younger. But suddenly, it started to grow at the age of seventeen.” 

“How about your family?” 

“Currently, they have mostly passed, but they’re no different from ordinary people.” 

“Then have you thought about the reason why you suddenly started to grow?” 

“Certainly, I have thought, but I cannot discern why.” 

“Is there still any growth occurring?” 

“I am no longer growing.”

“Then when was the time you were the biggest?” 

“It was when I went to Japan at twenty-three years old as a wrestler, that was when I was the biggest. At that time, my weight exceeded two hundred thirty pounds, and my body was much fuller than now (showing his arms).” 

“Now, you are no longer a wrestler, correct?” 

“I had an accident wrestling and broke my bone, spending nearly two years in the hospital. The doctor said to rest, and I myself could not handle wrestling anymore.” 

“Given your size, you must be quite strong. How much can you lift?” 

“I haven’t particularly tested it. When I was thirteen, I carried about fifty pounds in one hand.” 

“What are you doing now in Tokyo?” 

“I’m working at a power company in Tamagawa (玉川).” 

“What’s your job?” 

“I’m just relaxing.” 

“How much do you spend for living expenses each month?” 

“Only for food, about five to six hundred won.” 

“Are you living alone?” 

“Yes, I am by myself.” 

“How much rice do you eat in one meal?” 

“About fourteen to sixteen bowls.” 

“What food do you like the most?” 

“Japanese fish, especially flounder, I can eat about four pounds at once.” 

“Four pounds?” I was in disbelief. 

“Do you like beef?” 

“If it’s good, then yes, but I don’t particularly like it.” 

“How about good beef?” 

“I can’t eat more than two pounds (1 관 - about 5 kilograms).” 

Eating one pound in one meal would mean three pounds across three meals! Is that not eating like a wild animal? 

“Do you drink alcohol?” 

“I drink a little.” 

“How much?” 

“A koto.” 

“A koto? That’s amazing!” Indeed, if one continuously drank, one would eat less. 

“We just do both; we eat as we drink.” 

Mr. Paektu Mountain lights a cigarette (金剛) and puffs. 

“Do you have incidents of getting into fights when you drink?” 

“No, I don’t. I neither feel like fighting nor does anyone want to fight me, so we have to get along.” 

“What about suits?” 

“I have them tailored in Kanda (神田). Even if it is not that fancy, it has to be at least ninety won.” This and all suits, socks, shirts, and shoes must all be tailored. 

“Then with such minimal income...” 

“Indeed, that’s the case.” 

“Do you have a family back in your hometown?” 

“Yes.” 

“Are you married?” 

“Yes, I have a wife, but living separately is lonely. That’s why I can’t bring her along.” 

“Is your wife of normal stature?” 

“Yes, she is of ordinary height. But for some reason, she gets sick.” 

“How old is she?” 

“She is twenty-seven this year.” 

“She was born in the Year of the Fire Horse (丙午), then... how many children do you have?” 

“I don’t have any.” 

“How is your living condition?” 

“There’s not much, but I’m not starving. I manage to not let my small savings slip away, and I have sent some home for parents' memorial services, while I live day by day.” 

“In such a lonely environment as a wanderer, surely there are times when you crave company?” 

Mr. Paektu Mountain appears to laugh without humor. 

“Well, yes. In Tokyo, if there’s money...” 

“You mean to go to a tea house?” 

“Yes.” 

“Is there anyone of your size who you can relate to?” 

“Having a large body... is that it?” 

Mr. Paektu Mountain seems somewhat embarrassed, blushing lightly and mumbling. 

“Staying here all day must get boring for you?” 

“I would if it were possible to go out. It gets tiresome…” 

“That might be true. There would be many spectators.” 

“Sometimes if I get really bored, I take a drive around in a car.” 

“Do you have enough money saved?” 

“Is there a possibility of saving?” 

“Shouldn’t you try and save as much as possible... when do you plan to marry?” 

“I think I will get married after thirty.” 

“Don’t get too enthralled in the fun for fear of coming to be considered a small person.” 

While taking a break from smoking, Mr. Paektu smiled nonchalantly (his smile seems to be the way he usually is). 

“When are you planning to head to Japan?” 

“I was planning to go already, but the host has advised me to stay for a few more days.” 

“Will you come again?” 

“I will certainly come again in two or three years. But making a trip will cost at least three hundred won, which is quite challenging.” 

Mr. Paektu seems to lack time to circle the city in a car. 

“Before you go to Tokyo, let’s meet again.” 

I left the room with this remark. In the alley, while taking a photograph, a crowd of passersby gathered and said, “Look! A tall person!” 

“Wow, amazing!” 

Those were the children. Mr. Paektu, while smoking, smiled faintly.  
(The photograph shows Mr. Paektu and me; I am five feet five inches tall, so I would be of average height.)  

**Summary**

The article introduces the extraordinary figure of Paektu Mountain, a giant wrestler from Korea who stands at an impressive seven feet one inch. It explores his life, including his upbringing, his experiences as a wrestler, and his day-to-day living in Japan. The piece captures not only the physical attributes of this captivating individual but also his struggles in maintaining a personal life far from home. This portrayal reflects themes of identity and belonging, particularly in a colonial context. Historians might find the life and perceptions of a person like Paektu Mountain particularly compelling, as it raises questions about the intersections of nationality, physicality, and societal roles during the colonial era in Korea.

『히노에우마(丙午生)이로군요... ... 소생이 몃분입니까?』
『업서요.』
『살기는 어떠하십니까?』
『별로잇는 것은 업스나 굶지 안코지냅니다. 몃푼 안되는 재산이나 맛기어 버렷습니다. 어머니 아버지 봉제사나 해달라고... ... 나는 나대로 버러먹고 사니까』
『철리타향에 그러케 외로히 잇쟈면 자연 객고가 잇슬 것 아닙니까. 그런때는 어떠케 합니까? 더러 오입하시지요? 』
백두산군 어이가 업는 모양인지 픽 웃는다.
『그야 잇지요. 동경천지 돈만 잇스면 』
『유곽에 가시겟군.』
『네.』
『당신가티 거대한 체격으로 상대자가 잇서요?』
『몸이 크다고...그것도 그런가요..』
백두산군 비밀 폭로나 된 듯이 다소 얼골을 붉히며 우슴으로 우물쭈물 말을 덥는다.
『종일 이러케 방속에만 잇기심심치안아요?』
『박게 나갈수가 잇서야지요. 괴로워서....』
『그러켓지요. 구경ㅅ군이 꾀일테니..』
『이러다가도 정 심심하면 자동차로 한박휘 돌고옵니다.』
『돈만이 버려두엇습니까?』
『돈이 모일턱이 잇나요?』
『될수 잇는대로 모아두어야지요... 새장개는 언제나 갈작정인가요?』
『30넘어서 갈까합니다.』
『어엽분 새부인 어더서 너무 재미보다가 거인이 소인되리다.』
백두산은 담배를 끄다말고 시원치안은 우슴을 웃는다.(군의 우슴이 의래 그러한모양)
『언제 일본가시겟습니까?』
『벌서 갈랴고 햇섯는데 주인이 좀더 놀다가라고해서  이삼일만 더잇겟습니다.』
『또오시겟습니까?』
『이삼년후에 또한번 오겟습니다. 그런데 한번 래왕에 적어도 이삼백원이드러서 퍽 곤란합니다.』
백두산군 자동차로 시내 일주할 시간이 가차운 모양이다.
『동경가시기전에 또한번 맛납시다.』
긔자는 이한마디를 수인사로 남기고 방을 나왓다. 문박 골목에서 긔렴사진을 박는데 행인의떼가몰려와서
『이-키 키큰사람봐!』
『어이참. 대단하다.』
야- 단들이다. 백두산군 담배를 빨면서 빙글빙글.
