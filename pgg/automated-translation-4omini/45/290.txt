잡지명	별건곤 제47호  
발행일	1932년 01월 01일  
기사제목	反作春香傳 春香이는 정말 美人이엿더냐, 薄色고개의 한 傳說 (Anti-Story of Ch'unhyang: Was Ch'unhyang Really a Beauty? A Legend of the Thin Color Hill)  
필자	風流郞  
기사형태	문예기타 (Miscellaneous Literature)  

Ch'unhyang! In Joseon, who is the most famous and who is the most well-named? No one can surpass Ch'unhyang, whose name is widely transmitted through novels, songs, or plays, and is known by both men and women, the educated and the uneducated. While some may not know the name of Yi Chungmu-gong (李忠武公), who revived the dying people of Joseon, no one will fail to know Ch'unhyang. Similarly, even those who are unaware of King Sejong the Great (世宗大王), who created the Korean script to awaken the ignorance of the people, will certainly be familiar with Ch'unhyang. The power of beauty is truly great, and the essence of art is indeed magnificent. It is, therefore, quite curious to ponder whether the Ch'unhyang described in the novel exists in real life. However, discussing whether Ch'unhyang, celebrated as a universal beauty and a noble woman by the people up to this point, was indeed beautiful is also somewhat absurd. Regardless of how things were, in this world, everyone, without exception, sees Ch'unhyang as a heavenly beauty and a timeless noble woman, just as she is depicted in the story. Yet, in the vicinity of Namwon (南原), a curious legend, contrary to this, still circulates.

According to this legend, Ch'unhyang was not originally beautiful but rather a universal ugly woman. The time aligns with the era of King Sukjong (숙종大왕) during which "Ch'unhyangjeon" (춘향전) was produced, which is undoubtedly different from the present day. It is also a fact that she was the daughter of the courtesan Wolmae (월매). However, her actual appearance was completely the opposite of that described in Ch'unhyangjeon, being exceedingly ugly. Her nose resembled that of a pig, her eyes were like a pig's, her hair was as flat as a Mongolian cap, her hands resembled those of a frog, her neck resembled that of a turtle, and her body resembled a mortar. To make matters worse, she marred her figure even further, and the image described in Park Chunjae's (朴春栽) "Gombotarang" (곰보타령) depicted an entirely hideous state. It not only resembled a dog but was so fearsome that anyone who took a glance at her might instantly contract a fever. Even if someone who was around 20 or 30 years old were to see her, it was practically impossible for anyone to look at her once, let alone think about marrying her.

However, human instincts cannot simply be denied. One day, while Ch'unhyang was washing up by the river near Gwanghanru (광한루) on the banks of the Lale River (압 으천강), she happened to see a handsome young man, Li Do-ryeong (리도령), passing by. Enchanted by his refined appearance, she lost her senses. Eventually, she fell ill, unable to conceal her feelings after having met him. The illness she suffered was one that could not be treated even with medicine, for it originated from love. Days passed, and as she continued to suffer, her worried mother, who had always been concerned about her daughter's health, finally asked Ch'unhyang the reason for her illness. In that moment, Ch'unhyang, unable to deceive her mother any longer, confessed the truth.

But given that Ch'unhyang was originally so ugly, what could anyone do? Moreover, how could she ever even dream of speaking with the son of the local magistrate? However, being a woman who had seen all kinds of things at the pleasure house, her mother suddenly thought of a plan. It was nothing other than to bribe the servant with money to entice Do-ryeong to her home. Following the plan, she invited him to Gwanghanru, using a girl named Hyangdan (향단), who had been beautifully adorned and delicately dressed, as bait. The plan was executed successfully, getting him drunk and keeping him in Hyangdan's room overnight, while Ch'unhyang snuck into that room by taking Hyangdan’s place.

In his drunken stupor that night, he would not have been able to distinguish between Hyangdan and Ch'unhyang, and intoxicated and absent-minded, he spent the night with Ch'unhyang, believing her to be Hyangdan. The next morning, Do-ryeong awoke to see Ch'unhyang next to him, and shocked, he wondered whether he was seeing a ghost or a human. The horror of waking up next to an unbelievable ugly woman left him bewildered. 

At this time, Ch'unhyang’s mother had anticipated this and was already waiting by the door. Falling to the ground, she begged for forgiveness, confessing the whole scheme to Do-ryeong, while also explaining that if he would love Ch'unhyang forever, she would express her eternal gratitude, even after death. But if he were to abandon her, her feelings would be so bitter that she would die in despair. Initially, Do-ryeong was angry and hurt about being tricked by Ch'unhyang's mother but eventually, out of pity for her, he settled upon a compromise. He would not be able to marry her but offering her a token of his affection would not be an issue.

Before long, matters began to grow serious, and Do-ryeong had to leave for Seoul, following his father. If Ch'unhyang had been a beauty, and Do-ryeong had fallen for her as the novel implies, they would have surely had a dramatic parting like the parting scene at the "Oryeongjeong" in Ch’unhyangjeon. However, their case was not as romantic; Do-ryeong departed hurriedly with no formal farewell to Ch’unhyang in the end. He left almost carelessly, while Ch’unhyang was engulfed in deep yearning for him after their brief encounter.

Even if her heart ached for him, weighed by the feeling of anticipating his return, she despaired at her fate as an ugly woman and sunk into hopelessness. Understanding that her fate prevented anyone else from loving her, Ch’unhyang resolved to take her own life out of despair when she discovered that Do-ryeong had departed on the same day. That same night, she went to the edge of a cliff and hung herself with the silk scarf she had received from him, which was a painful act of self-destructive love. 

The people around her grieved greatly over her death, and they buried her at Imsilgogae (임실고개), naming it Parksaekgogae (薄色고개), later corrected to Bakseokgogae (박석고개). 

Ch’unhyang wasn’t killed by anyone but was merely the victim of fate as she was unable to live a happy life in this world, leading her to committed suicide out of despair. However, the deep resentment of her unfulfilled love could drown oceans, filling the skies above Jiri Mountain (智異山) with the clouds of her sorrow. 

After her death, tales circulated about her, and the new magistrate of Namwon met with numerous unfortunate incidents, where his horse would refuse to ride past Parksaekje without bucking him off. 

As word of this tragedy spread, Do-ryeong, who could not remain unaffected by this legend about Ch’unhyang, grew increasingly uneasy. He vowed to resolve her injustices or meet his own demise. 

Eventually, one year, when he took the exam and passed with top honors, he expressed his desire to be appointed as governor of Namwon. 

During his assignment, he wrote a compelling account of her story, praising her as a mythical beauty contrary to what was said, glorifying what was depicted in Ch'unhyangjeon. He also planned a ceremony to honor her properly.

Approaching Parksaekgogae, he called out that he was coming to honor the memory of the beautiful Ch'unhyang, and upon his arrival, he performed the memorial rites, vowing to soothe her eternal spirit. 

This story may be questionable in its accuracy, but elders in the Namwon area still recount these stories today. 

**Summary**

The article explores the dual narratives surrounding Ch'unhyang, the iconic figure in Korean folklore. While traditionally celebrated as a paragon of beauty and virtue, the article introduces a counter-narrative wherein Ch'unhyang is portrayed as ugly and unremarkable, raising questions about societal standards of beauty and love. The story reflects the complexities of human emotions and urges readers to consider how tales evolve over time, particularly in a colonial context where folklore serves as both a reflection of cultural values and a critique of societal norms. Historians might find the juxtaposition of beauty standards and the resulting tragedies particularly relevant to understanding Korean cultural identity during colonial rule.

춘향이! 춘향이 조선에서 누가 제일 잘나고 누가 제일 이름이 만히 낫느니 하야도 이 춘향이 처름 소설로 노래로 또는 연극으로 이름이 넓이 전해 남녀로소 유식무식 할 것업시 누구나 잘 아는 인물은 업슬 것이다. 다 죽게 된 조선 사람을 다시 살게한 리충무공(李忠武公)의 이름은 몰나도 춘향의 이름은 모를 사람이 업고 조선 사람의 무식을 깨워 주기 위하야 한글을 창작한 세종대왕(世宗大王)은 몰나도 이 춘향이는 또한 모를 사람이 업슬 것이다. 미인의 힘이란 참으로 위대하고 예술의 생명이란 정말 장원한 것이다. 소설에 나온 춘향이를 실제에 잇느냐 업느냐 의문을 가지는 것도 우서운 일이요. 또 이때까지 세상 사람이 천하 미인이요 렬녀라고 떠드는 춘향이를 지금와서 그가 미인이엿느냐 아니엿드냐고 하고 론난하는 것도 역시 실업슨 일 갓다. 그러나 세상 사람들은 춘향이를 꼭 실재의 인물로 생각하고 심지어 어떤 사람은 남원(南原) 땅에 가서 고적(古蹟)조사를 하는 때에 음지(邑誌)를 가지고 춘향의 집터를 찻고 춘향의 무덤을 차진 우서운 일도 잇섯고 작년 여름에는 남원의 긔생(妓生)들이 군청에서 광한루(廣寒樓)를 수리하는 긔회를 타서 맛치 진주(晋州)의 긔생들이 의긔론개(義妓論介)의 사당을 지여 놋텃이 춘향각(春香閣)까지 지여 놋코 춘추제향을 지내기 까지 하게 된 바에야 이 춘향이를 미인이엿느냐 하는 말을 쓰는 것도 과히 실업슨 일은 안일 것 갓다. 그것이야 엇지 되엿던 아즉것 이 세상에서는 누구나 물론하고 춘향이는 그 소설에 씨워잇는 것과 맛찬가지로 천하의 미인이요 만고의 렬녀로만 안다 .그러나 남원 부근에는 그와는 정반대 되는 한 이상한 전설이 지금것 떠돌고 잇다.
