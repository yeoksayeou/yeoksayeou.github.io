잡지명	별건곤 제47호  
발행일	1932년 01월 01일  
기사제목	水利組合은 왜 破綻되나, 農村座談會 (Why Do Irrigation Associations Go Bankrupt?, Rural Roundtable)  
필자	鄭寅寬  
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue)  

Chairman: Ahem, despite the late hour, thank you all for coming in such numbers. This evening, I would like you to discuss the issues regarding the irrigation associations openly and without concealment. Recently, it seems that the irrigation associations in various regions have been quite vocal without any signs of relief, engaging in counter-movements or demanding government subsidies to the extent that it could ignite a fire at the Governor-General's office. It's a fundamental issue that there are indeed flaws present. We are also experiencing it firsthand, so it is undoubtedly necessary to review this matter thoroughly. I invite each of you to voice your opinions as you see fit. (Applause)

Lee: Chairman, there seems to be a lot of discussion about irrigation associations, and since the materials are readily available, could you please explain briefly why these irrigation associations were established in the first place?

All: Please go ahead.

Chairman: I do not possess special knowledge, but since you have requested, I will share what I know.

"Why Were Irrigation Associations Established?"  

To discuss the irrigation associations, we must first explain what agricultural improvement means, and if that involves food security, we must talk about Japan's colonial policies. However, detailing this would take quite some time, so I will keep it brief. First, Japan's industrial development required the advancement of agriculture in its colonies. Secondly, to feed the annually increasing Japanese population, it was essential that large quantities of rice were produced in Korea. Thirdly, since Korea is the raw material source, it becomes significant in constructing the driving force behind the recent industrial policy. Under these circumstances, the so-called "rice increase policy" in Korea emerged, which was initially suggested by the former Governor-General, Ha Gyeong (下岡摠監), who advocated for industrial supremacy. This rice increase policy necessitated land improvement, and the immediate prerequisite was irrigation projects, which led to the establishment of the irrigation associations. The rice increase policy came to fruition through discussions starting from the 15th year of the Taisho period, aiming to improve irrigation systems over the next twelve years and ultimately enhance 350,000 jeongbo (jourbos) of land, producing an additional 8.7 million sacks of rice. If accomplished, 300,000 sacks would suffice for consumption within Korea, while 500,000 would be exported to Japan, combining to yield a total of 10 million sacks annually, thus generating 3 billion yen for Korea, based on an estimated export price of 30 won per sack. The budget for this venture was set at 285,353,000 won, where the government promised to subsidize 65,070,000 won, and the entrepreneurs would contribute 22,206,000 won, using 1 billion 9820 million won in borrowed funds for the project. It was noted that this borrowed amount would be repaid with the funds collected from the irrigation association dues.

"What Were the Construction Costs and Debts?"  

Kim: I understand that. Does anyone know how much the construction actually cost, and what the debt is?

Kong: According to the previous year's statistics, in the 4th year of Shōwa (1930), the construction costs for 139 irrigation associations amounted to a total of 86,817,723 won, which includes installation, area development, improvement projects, and disaster recovery costs, totaling revenue losses to maintain, including the dues from the associations, which resulted in a lack of recoverable debts totaling 80,515,429 won. Thus, the costs incurred were 11,556,574 won which amounted to 56 won and 10 jeon per 300 pyeong of rice fields, with the overall costs noted as 10,412,1363 won.

Yoon: Oh dear, that means owning land has become as good as renting it.

Han: Our association is no different from that. If not, it would dissolve. The dues for each of Korea would accumulate to quite an amount. If the debts are all recovered, we'd be ruined.

Chairman: In the 4th year of Shōwa alone, it amounts to 1,347,4473 won, averaging 6 won and 54 jeon per pyeong.

Park: Wait a minute. I believe I paid around 13 won per pyeong two years ago.

Choi: How much was the highest and lowest dues across Korea?

Kim: In the 4th year of Shōwa, there were 29 districts with dues below 5 won, 16 districts above 5 won, 16 above 6 won, 28 above 7 won, and so forth, with the highest being 16 districts above 12 won.

"How Were the Irrigation Association Dues Calculated?"  

Kong: On what basis were the dues roughly calculated?

Lee: Using the average rice yield per pyeong before the irrigation association, calculated at 1 shō (石) and 7 shaku (升), after the project, it became approximately 2 shō (石) and 8 to 7 shaku (斗) thus justifying the increased yield of 1 shō, 7 shaku, 9 shaku under ideal conditions, yielding an average benefit of 17 won and 52 jeon per unit, with the calculated foundational dues amounting to 8 won, 87 jeon. However, the actual dues collected averaged 6 won, 54 jeon, thus a net profit of 2 won and 33 jeon.

Yoon: The standards for yield might be erroneous since the yield of 4 won a shō is quite low; the failure of the irrigation association seems apparent.

Han: Under the assumed benefit of 17 won and 52 jeon based on average yields, the remaining 8 won and 65 jeon needs justification.

Lee: That included cases where landowners would allow half of the yield to their tenants while covering management costs associated with the cultivation. Thus, they claim a net of 2 won and 33 jeon. It suggests a haphazard approach to these calculations.

Park: Consider that at any given place, landowners cover half the irrigation dues, which is utterly unreasonable.

All: Exactly, this is unacceptable for farmers!

"How Did the Situation Change after the Formation of Irrigation Associations?"  

Choi: Chairman, how much more rice did the crop yield post-formation of the irrigation associations? What are the implications?

Chairman: Certainly, there has been an increase. According to last year's statistics (in the 4th year of Shōwa), it indicated that in an area of about 121,331 jeongbo, before the construction work, the yield stood at 1,294,455 shō, and after, it rose to 3,826,693 shō, indicating an increase of 2,532,239 shō. Thus on average per pyeong, yield figures showed it moved from 1 shō and 7 shaku pre-construction to 3 shō, 1 to 5 shaku post-construction, which means an improvement of 2 shō and 8 shaku. Therefore, valuing rice at 11 won 11 jeon per pyeong, the gain translates to approximately 703,3293 won, while dues based on the calculated amount would indicate figures amounting to 351,6646 won, and consequently the total number of dues collected for the year was noted to be 782,1152 won, meaning a net loss of 430,4506 won.

Lee: Admittedly, the yield increase might have been influenced by irrigation projects but also the use of fertilizers. In the 4th year of Shōwa, the average across Korea indicated that fertilizers valued at 5 won and 16 jeon per pyeong were employed; thus, having sufficient fertilizer contributed to the increased yield alongside improvements in farming techniques and favorable weather conditions over the past few years.

Kim: Certainly, one cannot attribute increased yield solely to the irrigation association's efforts. However, the burden of fertilizer costs solely falls on the farmers. How can one boast about the success of the irrigation association?

Audience: Yes, increases aren't beneficial to us; it’s actually a loss.

Kong: Adding insult to injury, the fact that we’re forced to sell rice means we're not even able to feed ourselves properly despite producing more.

Chairman: It seems that Koreans' rice consumption is gradually declining. In the 8th year of Taisho, the average per capita consumption was 6 shō, 7 shaku, and from the 11th year to subsequent years, there was a decline to 4 shō, 4 shaku, 4 kö by the 4th year of Shōwa. It appears we are not consuming more rice.

"Why Do Irrigation Associations Go Bankrupt?"

Chairman: Recently, irrigation associations have been facing extreme difficulties as members are unable to pay their dues, leading to land auctions and the levying of crop seizures, with many associations going bankrupted. Now, let’s explore the internal and external causes of this issue.

All: Please do.

Lee: The severe agricultural depression, resulting from the global economic crisis, has significantly impacted the agricultural economy.

Kim: Due to excessive rice production in Japan in recent years, the rice prices have fallen, making it difficult for members to repay their debts and pay association dues.

(Middle section omitted)

Yoon: The calculation basis for the dues seems extremely flawed.

Kong: The misunderstanding of agricultural techniques and policies led to excessive construction costs.

Park: Assigning a profit-driven corporation to oversee these construction projects only exacerbated costs.

Choi: Given the significant industrial nature of the colony, inadequate government funding was provided in an effort to minimize costs.

Audience: Yes, there are several issues.

Chairman: If someone from the audience has further comments, please share.

First Audience Member: The inadequate government funding, compounded with high interest rates of 7% and lengthy payment periods, has caused significant financial strain.

Second Audience Member: Additionally, they employ unnecessary personnel, which inflates labor costs.

Third Audience Member: The governance of irrigation associations was not autonomous, with heavy bureaucratic engagement leaving small landowners voiceless, depriving farmers, the actual cultivators, of representation.

Fourth Audience Member: Given the public exposure to corruption, it is hardly possible to rectify matters.

Fifth Audience Member: The collusion of large landowners and financial interests continues to trample over smallholders and exploit every method to maximize their gains.

All: Truly, absolutely!

Chairman: It seems we have ample material to discuss, but let’s save future discussions for land tenancy issues. I hereby conclude today’s session. (Applause)

**Summary**

This dialogue highlights the struggles of irrigation associations in colonial Korea during the early 1930s, examining the underlying issues of financial mismanagement, economic exploitation, and structural inefficiencies. Participants question agricultural policies and the burden of debts, indicating that the actual benefits promised by irrigation improvements often fell short, leading to widespread bankruptcy among associations. Historians might find the discussion significant as it reflects the interconnections between Japan's colonial economic policies, local agricultural practices, and the resulting challenges faced by Korean farmers.
