기사제목	狂人의 나라 西八號室 探訪記 (A Visit to the Manic Realm: Room No. 8)  
필자	一記者 (A Reporter)  
기사형태	소식 (News)  

狂人의 나라 西八號室 探訪記, 이 세상에 누가 精神病者냐? 

Mental patients! Though there are varying degrees, the world often refers to them as "those who are insane" or "mad people."

While not conscious of it, they disregard all social norms and impulsively stage absurd tragedies and comedies, performing what ordinary people cannot achieve—genius, or perhaps even an extraordinary expression of genius. In Korea, there stands a singular collective of such madmen: the West Room No. 8 of the Kyungsung Imperial University Hospital (京城帝大附屬醫院西八號室). Fortunately, I have had the opportunity to visit this place, and thus I wish to share the experience with readers for both interest and reference.

Room No. 8! It is a quiet brick house standing with a dark grove behind it, separated from the outpatient clinic's northern embankment where "entry is prohibited."

As I pushed open the door, A—my guide—was waiting for me. A smell of some kind of food lingered in the air. I greeted him lightly and asked, "What’s with this food smell?"

"That’s because it’s lunchtime," A replied. It is unthinkable to serve food in this setting, especially to mental patients, so I ventured, "Isn’t there a dining hall over there?"

"This section is independent," A informed me.

"Yes—I thought mental patients would only be served nutrition food," I remarked.

"No, that's not true," A said with a smile. 

Leaving my hat and coat in the reception area, I followed A down a dim corridor, and nerves crept over me at the thought of encountering the mentally ill.

"How many patients are currently here?" I inquired.

"About fifty, give or take. There used to be around seventy, but they've become fewer," A answered as we approached the room.

Before the large, heavy door, peering through a narrow opening reminiscent of a prison cell, I saw what appeared to be a letterbox. As A pressed a doorbell mounted on the wall, I anxiously peered through that narrow gap. Suddenly, two eyes appeared at the window! I jumped, thinking, "Is that a mad person?" My heart raced.

With the sound of keys, the door swung open, and I stepped in behind A. Alas, my foolishness—it was not a patient at all, but the guard of that ward. My frightening thoughts began to dissipate.

"It’s time for the morning assembly," A explained. I realized the person holding a broom was actually a patient among a rather polite bunch. The patients didn't look wild at all—some even seemed more like seriously ill individuals rather than the mad. However, it was evident—even to a layman like me—that their gazes were bizarre.

Among more than ten patients, there were both Koreans and Japanese. In the center of the ward lay a wooden floor, with rooms arranged on either side. There was a couple bundled beneath blankets, one person was just waking up, and another was cleaning.

Entering a spacious tatami room, A said, "This is the recreation room. They play Go and Japanese chess here." Indeed, a Go board and a shogi board lay in the corner.

A approached a patient and, in a kindly voice, asked, "What's up with you?"

"Today, I’m going home," the patient replied with unclear diction.

"How many more days will you stay?" A continued, leaving that remark before stepping out with me from the ward. With a heavy clink, the door was locked behind us. 

A then shared with me, "Most of the patients here are diagnosed with early-onset dementia (조발성치보). While it is a mad affliction, they can be quite tame and calm. But when they have a sudden outburst, it can get quite severe. Thankfully, we currently have no patients who cause uncontrollable outbursts. In the past, there have even been incidents where orderly staff got injuries, like when someone broke a leg during an altercation."

"Did you ever have a close call?" I asked.

"Not really with the doctors. But last year, there was a case where a patient, missing his son greatly, went out to find him, encountering a child while being unable to restrain himself. They embraced, kissed, and he suddenly bit off the child’s genitals. It turned out the child had to undergo surgery and barely survived, but now only one side remains intact."

"So they didn't lose everything?" I remarked.

"No, one side is still sufficient," he assured me.

We passed through the upper floor to the hospital. A pressed the door and it swung open, revealing the first glimpse of the room.

Patients were more prominently Japanese than Korean. In one bed, a Japanese patient lay covered with a blanket, staring blankly at the ceiling while continuously chuckling.

"That’s a patient with grandiose delusions," A explained. He approached the patient. "What’s so funny?" 

The patient looked up and replied with a simple "Eh?" (네?), then continued laughing.

If the world he lived in was that endlessly amusing and joyful, I thought, even a healthy person might long to experience being a delusional person, delighting in laughter.

In another bed, another Japanese patient was singing. A pointed, "See? He sings all day, but we can’t understand what he’s singing. It’s just nonsense."

There was also an older man with the demeanor of a politician who stared at us intensely, seemingly deep in thought, as he puffed away on his pipe.

"This patient always presents himself like a statesman or politician, demanding to speak highly of officials with tremendous energy," A noted.

At this point, the other patients merely gazed blankly at nothing.

We exited this ward and continued discussing amongst ourselves.

"They seem to appear more seriously ill," I commented.

"Yes. Generally, they suffer from paralytic dementia (마비성치보). As for the early-onset dementia we've mentioned, many causes can lead up to it, but that remains vague. The paralytic dementia, however, is all due to syphilis. When the syphilis bacteria invade the brain and manifest, that's when one goes mad. Therefore, common syphilis treatments like Salvarsan 606 or arsenic compounds don't work here." 

"Is it possible to see the women's ward?" I asked.

"Sure, I can show you. We’re heading there now," A replied.

We climbed the stairs and returned carefully along a corridor towards the second floor. A approached a ward and, with a twist of a key, opened the door.

Here, two nurses were attending at a table in the center. The south side was a regular ward, while the north side was an isolation ward.

We decided to see the regular ward first, so headed to one of the doors. The men’s ward had rows of ordinary doors, but this one had thick doors with small glass peepholes.

In one room, a Japanese woman has her hair loose, wrapped in blankets and pressing her head against the open window.

In the next room, two rooms over, a Japanese woman was curled up and sleeping under blankets.

In yet another room, an older Japanese woman was sitting on a mat, humming while fiddling with a handful of charms.

"What are you doing, grandma?" A asked her.

The woman cast a sidelong glance at us and continued her activity undeterred.

"See how she works diligently to line up the charms in a row the entire day; she makes them change." 

Next was the isolation room. When A suddenly threw open the door, the stench hit us. The room was of substantial size but looked like a cemented zoo or prison from novels. In one corner was a small hole for waste—a slop bucket, filthy and used.

The patient lay bare an abdomen covered with yellow stains and squatted gracelessly.

A explained, "That hole acts as a toilet, while that bucket is their food dish. Those kept in isolation are the dirtiest and act the worst. They rip apart blankets, smear excrement on walls, and even eat it. It’s incomprehensibly filthy.”

We moved on to the next isolation room, where again the foul odor was unbearable. A patient was hiding their head in that hole, rolling over in their blanket.

After seeing us, they showed a yellowed smile.

A added, "You see, that’s a distinction; their behaviors contrast sharply with those of normal individuals."

We then entered the extensive ward across the eastern side. It was a large tatami room where around three Korean women and four Japanese women were.

The Japanese women mostly lay still, while among Korean women, one older was eating, another was wearing rough clothing, and a last woman, appearing in her forties, wore a pink silk dress.

Books piled high seemed to indicate she had recently written something on the shooting table.

Upon hearing our quiet whispers of Japanese, she suddenly shouted, "What is the matter? Speak quietly! You can't do that!"

In a moment of indignation, she laughed again and recited various phrases absurdly. 

She seemed to blend clarity with nonsense; a contrasting figure amid indistinguishable murmurs.

The older woman looked at us and asked, "What’s your name?"

Seeing her composure, she didn't seem mad at all.

"I’m Kim," I answered.

"Where’s Kim from?"

"From Gwangsan."

"What’s your mother’s surname?"

"Kim."

"Oh, you cheeky one."

Then, I remarked to A who still lingered, "Is that woman of any specific service as a woman?"

"Seems she used to be a gisaeng. It appears fights between couples are what led to her madness. Even without motivation, she comes to life at night sometimes and writes something every day." 

"How skilled is she in writing?"

"Sometimes thoroughly so, and sometimes in an incomprehensible manner. Sometimes the characters displayed are intricately unknown to us too, but she seems adequately versed in Japanese too."

"Then can she hand over what she writes to you?"

"I have a few pages. I can give them to you,” A offered, looking towards his laboratory.

He explained further, "While the mental illnesses are many, they differ in type. For instance, the patients in isolation, like those who consume their own feces, fall into a category called coprophagia, while those who suddenly become agitated and lethargic have manic-depressive illness."

Additionally, conditions termed 'epilepsy', as well as unexplainable hysteria, are all included under mental diseases. One should note that personalities deviating greatly, like a person overly obsessed with minute details and legal matters, would be termed 'peculiar.' Such individuals exist in notable numbers in society.

Thus, while we regard mental illness, we often benchmark the healthy common person (레벨) as the norm; those who exceed are genius, and those who fall below are deemed mental illness. Yet, on certain points, mentally ill individuals can exhibit behaviors that transcend the average person’s creativity. 

Consider this: how a poet might exclaim, “The heavenly bodies are merely the farts of God; the rain is his diarrhea..." Such absurdities are often beyond the comprehension of ordinary folk yet become famous among the circle of poets.

"Exactly, whether a mental patient who laughs at nonsensical ideas or a poet using absurdity to define moments—there lies a similarity."

"A further characteristic of mental illness distinguishes it from other ailments, wherein patients often remain oblivious to their own orthodoxy. They might recognize the onset of illness at first or even in improvement, albeit faintly—that recognition is termed awareness of illness (병식)."

"Does any fundamental remedy for mental illness exist?"

"No, there's no such thing. Only symptomatic treatments can be provided based on the illness type."

"What kinds of food do the patients eat?"

"Ordinary ones. However, there are cases pertaining to some patients whereby they refuse food. That’s called anorexia (거식징). Sometimes they even steal food from others and end up eating three or four times what they actually need."

"What about the sexual behavior?"

"Manic-depressive patients also display some erotic tendencies. At times, female patients might subtly grasp a doctor’s wrist. Other times, they engage in masturbation indiscriminately, regardless of presence."

"Can you take action against them?"

"Where would you even begin? It's pointless." 

As we strolled and conversed, the reception area appeared before us. I took up my hat and coat, thanking the staff.

"Thank you for your hard work," I said.

"Not at all. It would be better to spend a month observing; there are always things worth discussing."

I exited Room No. 8, gazing at the clear sky, feeling lifted. Yet considering whether I might be a lunatic or not rendered me into a riddle.

**Summary**

The article takes readers on a visit to West Room No. 8 of the Kyungsung Imperial University Hospital, uncovering the lives and daily experiences of mental patients during the colonial era in Korea. It highlights the stark contrast between societal perceptions of madness and unconventional behavior, dissecting how mental illness leads to various forms of human expression. The interview gives insights into treatment methodologies and social stigmas of the time, painting a complex picture of mental health in colonial Korea. Historians may find intriguing the way mental health conditions were understood and handled, showcasing the ethical dilemmas of treatment and the stigma surrounding mental illness in a period when psychological understanding underwent significant evolution.

정신병자 (精神病者)! 정도의 차이는 잇슬지라도 세상에서는 부르되 「실성한 사람」, 「미친사람」이라고 한다.
의식적(意識的)은 아니나마 인간사회의 모든 규약을 무시하고 발작적(發作的)으로 엉뚱한 비극과 희극을 연출하며 보통사람으로서 하지 못할 천재(天才)아니 천재를 발휘하는 그들 미친 사람의 집단지(集團地)가 잇스니 조선에서 오즉 하나 뿐인 경성제대 부속의원서팔호병실(京城帝大附屬醫院西八號室)이 그 곳이다. 다행이 긔자는 이곳을 탐방할 기희을 엇게 되여  듯고 어든 바가 잇서서 독자와 더부러 흥미와 참고를 나누고저 한다.

서팔호병실(西八號室)! 외래진요소(外來診療所)의 북쪽 뚝 떠러저서 울울한 송림을 등지고 서 잇는 호젓한 벽돌 집이다.
「開放ヲ禁ズ」하고 씨위 잇는 또어를 밀치고 드러가니 마침 긔자를 안내할 A씨가 기다리고서 잇섯다. 어데서인지 양료리 냄새가 난다. 긔자는 인사 한 번을 꿉뻑하고
『웬 양료리 냄새가 이러케 남니까』하고 무르니 A씨는
『지금이 점심때니까요.』한다. 정신병 환자에게 양료리 먹일리는 만무한 것 가태서,
『식당은 저-쪽에 잇지안어요.』
『여기만은 독립임니다.』
『네-정신 병자에겐 양료리만 먹이는 줄 알엇지요.』
『아니요』하며 A씨는 빙그레 웃는다. 응접실에다 모자와 외투를 버서 노코 A씨에 뒤를 따럿다. 어둠 침침한 복도 마루를 지나는데 정신병자와 초대면 할 것을 생각하니 엇전지 으시시해진다.
『현재에 환자가 멧명이나 됨니까』
『약 오십명 가량 됨니다. 전에는 칠십명 가량 되엿는대 만니 주럿지요.』
A씨와 더부러 이러한 문답을 하는 동안에 벌서 병실 문 압헤 이르럿다.
굿게 다친 육중한 문 드려다 보고 내다 볼 수만 잇게 된 우체통의 편지넌는 구녁 가틀 조고마한 구녁 마치 감옥에 감방문을 연상케 한다. A씨가 벽에 달린 초인종(招人鐘)을 누르는 동안에 나는 넌즈시 그 구녁으로 (밧삭 각가이는 못 가고) 드려다 보앗다. 그 때 돌연히 그 구녁에 나타나는 두 눈초리! 나는 깜짝 놀라고 마럿다.『미친사람 인가 보다』하고 속으로 우물쭈물하는데  가슴은 두방망이질을 하엿다.
열쇠 소리와 함께 문이 열이엿다. A의 뒤를 따러 드러섯다. 아-나의 어리석음이여 그 무서운 눈초리의 소유자는 그 병실의 간수인(看守人)이엿다. 무시무시한 생각은 조곰치도 업서젓다.

『멧 칠만 더 게시요. 좀 더 낫거던』

『이 병실 환자는 대개가 조발성치보(早發性痴呆)라는 병명을 부치게 됨니다.＜28＞ 역시 미친병인데 저러케 온순하다가도 발작이 이러나는 때는 야-단 이지요. 그러나 것 잡을 수 업슬 만큼야료를 부리는 환자는 지금 업습니다. 엇저다가 별안간 뒤에 와서 꽉 껴안끼도하고 이야기 끗테 별안간 따귀를 부치기도 하는데 힘이 장사지요. 전ㅅ번에는 간수하는 사람이 다리가 부러진 일 까지 잇섯스니까요.』

2층 병실에 이러럿다. 문 압히며 실내의 구죠가 먼저 병실과 가치 되어잇다.

『저것이 「과대망상광(誇大忘想狂)이라는 것입니다. 무엇을 작고 망상하고는 웃는 것입니다. 』 A씨는 다시 과대망상광을 향하야
『Xさん何がそんなに可笑いんだ』하니 환자는 고개를 드러
『え?』(네?) 한 마듸를 하고 여전이 웃고 잇다. 언제까지나 우슬 것인가.
