발행일 1932년 11월 01일
기사제목 米價問題 農廳座談會 (Rice Price Problem: Roundtable Discussion at the Agricultural Bureau)
기사형태 대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Roundtable)

Rice Price Problem: Roundtable Discussion at the Agricultural Bureau
Attendees - Kim Myŏng-yŏ, Pak Ch'un-sŏ, Chŏng Yun-mo, Yi Pong-ch'un, and five or six other farmers

Kim: Oh, these people are already here. Have you already eaten dinner?

All: Oh! Is that Myŏng-yŏ? You're late this evening. Come and sit down.

Kim: Oh, you've been smoking a lot. The smoke is thick in the room; let's open a window.

Chŏng: What's the big deal? These days, cigarettes aren't that strong anyway. You wouldn't even notice how strong kizami (刻み) is, no matter how much you smoke. Speaking of tobacco, before kizami and cigarettes came to the villages, people used to smoke strong leaf tobacco, and that was something else.

Pak: Yeah, these days, cigarettes are just called cigarettes, but are they really tobacco? You just puff on them, and only smoke comes out. There should be some kind of spicy taste.

Yi: I heard the Chŏng family harvested the five paddies in the back field today. How much did you get? Maybe a dozen bags.

Chŏng: Where did you harvest? With a huller.

All: Yeah, yeah. Do they still use hullers these days? They use threshers. Hullers are old news. If you were using a huller and met a township clerk or an agricultural association technician, who would admire you?

Chŏng: Actually, it is true that using a thresher on a mat yields cleaner rice.

All: Oh, you've been to school in Seoul and came back, so you seem to prefer the modern way, but back when the yard was well swept and the mortar was left out, and ten or so people sang songs together while hulling, did you not eat because the rice was bad, or did you not eat rice because it was full of stones?

Kim: Yeah, yeah. Telling us to use a mat and thresh is just to please the rice merchants. Even if we thresh without stones and use a thresher, we don't have more to eat than when we used to leave the mortar in the yard and hull, and it's even harder to have white rice.

All: Myŏng-yŏ is saying that the more we improve agriculture this year, the poorer we get.

Chŏng: That's not always the case. We're getting poorer, so we should try to improve things as much as possible, so that we get two bags from where we used to get one, and make cleaner rice so we can get a better price.

All: Huh! That guy is still stubborn. People who have studied are more foolish these days. Is rice more expensive these days than when we were hulling?

Chŏng: That depends on the market. There's no law that prices only go up.

All: That's where you're wrong. Rice prices go up and down according to the market. It's not like rice hulled with a huller is cheap and rice threshed with a thresher is expensive. These days, even rice hulled with a thresher is less than a third of the price it was 34 years ago when we were hulling.

Chŏng: That's an exaggeration. The market is different now than it was then. You can't compare then and now. But if you look at the prices at the same time, rice with a lot of stones and weeds is graded differently and priced differently from clean rice, isn't it?

All: That's a trivial matter. What does it matter to us if we get a slightly better or worse price because of a slight difference in grade? That's a problem for the merchants and rice sellers, the story of landlords who have a lot of rice. For those of us who barely harvest two or three bags of rice in the fall and have to buy four or five bags of rice, the price of rice should be high. What big impact would a little more or less grade have?

Yi: Goodness, you haven't even heard my question and you're already having a debate. How much did you harvest in the back field today at the Chŏng's place? If you keep talking about other things, it will be dawn soon.

All: Hey, you, it's better for us if the rice price goes up so we can get higher wages (saegyŏng, 세경). If your wages are four bags of rice, you're melting away. Rice is 7 won a bag, 7 won! You work your bones out for a year and get 28 won. Forget it.

Yi: Don't worry. We don't sell now; we keep it and sell it next spring when the price goes up. We don't have to pay taxes or be chased by debt collectors. We're always carefree.

All: Hey, this guy is really showing off.

Yi: Anyway, how many bags of rice did you get at the Chŏng's place? I want to hear that, or it will be dawn soon.

Chŏng: Ah! Twelve bags and five quarts. I thought for sure I would get thirteen bags this year, but I used too much gold fertilizer (金肥), and it rotted.

Yi: You were too greedy, so that's what happened.

Kim: I heard that Ch'un-sŏ harvested the round paddy today too. How much did you get?

Pak: Don't even talk about it. We tenants should at least be satisfied with the amount we harvest, even if it's a bit rough, but that stingy farm supervisor even brought scales and weighed everything, adding and adding and adding, even shaking the straw rice in, so I was speechless. He calculated exactly 200 kŭn (斤) per string, so even though it was dried hard, the weight was still different. I barely got eight bags, and four bags were for the supervisor. And then he took my share for the farm supervisor's color fee (農監의 色租) or whatever, and told me to pay this year's land tax 1st installment, so he took 1 bag 7 won worth of my harvest and weighed it out, so I barely had three bags left. And then there's the cost of going down to the port. Still, because the farm supervisor was coming today, I bought half a kŭn of meat for side dishes. I don't know where to farm.

All: Hey, that's a pretty fair deal. Whether it's a lot or a little, you split it evenly. At XX field or whatever, they just stand on the ridge and estimate the rent, and I guess a lot of people have wasted their farming there. Hey, Yun-mo! Did the newspaper come today? If you brought it, read the part about the rice price.

Chŏng: I was going to talk about the rice price anyway. Listen up.

Due to hail and insect damage, production decreased by 500,000 bags, amounting to only around 16 million bags. Is an increase in rice prices still unlikely?

All: We're dead. Everyone was happy about the good harvest, but look at the hungry people. The last newspaper said that 16.5 million bags would be produced, which is 600,000 bags more than last year, but now it's decreased by 500,000 bags. How much hail fell to cause that much damage? <19>

Yi: By the way, Mr. Chŏng! I don't understand the last part.

Chŏng: It says, "Rice price increase is still unlikely." (米價騰貴는 亦是 無望)

Yi: What does that mean?

Chŏng: It means there's no hope for the rice price to go up.

Yi: Why is that? Shouldn't the rice price go down if there's a good harvest and go up if there's a bad harvest?

Chŏng: It's not always like that. The harvest does have some effect, but the market price is the most important thing.

All: Then, what will the grain market price be like this year?

Chŏng: That's hard to explain quickly.

Kim: Oh, that's why you studied. What's the use of showing off because you studied and looking down on us farmers? What value is there in that?

All: That's right. That's why we have Chŏng Wŏn-mo in our village! Tell us quickly.

Chŏng: Hey, don't put me on a pedestal too much. What if I fall?

Yi: It's not putting you on a pedestal, it's the truth. Who else knows about these things? We can't even read a newspaper, so there's nothing we can do.

Chŏng: Anyway, let's talk about it. In Japan, there's something called a standard rice price (基準米價). The government sets it by law, and every year they precisely investigate all the prices and the costs that farmers spend on farming, and then they set the rice price. That's the standard rice price. So, if the standard rice price is 20 won per bag, then no matter how much the market price rises, it cannot rise more than 20% of 20 won, which is 4 won, so no more than 24 won. Conversely, even if the rice price falls, it cannot fall below 20% of the standard rice price of 20 won, which is 4 won, in other words, 16 won per bag.

All: That's a strange method. That's how it should be. That way, farmers can live. To farmers, everything is rice. You can only live if you sell rice. But with the rice price being so erratic and fluctuating wildly, how can we live? If it benefits ordinary farmers, then there's nothing to say, but every year, when we sell our rice in the fall, it's dirt cheap. Then, after we sell everything we have and only the rich people's grain remains, ordinary farmers can't even think about white rice because they're eating millet, but when we have to buy rice to eat, the rice price goes up because it's set like that, so how can we live?

Yi: But is that law being implemented in Chosŏn?

All: He's always saying such senseless things. Who would implement such a law in Chosŏn?

Yi: So, it's useless in Chosŏn?

Chŏng: No, it's not entirely useless. The Chosŏn rice price usually follows the Japanese rice price, so it's somewhat affected.

Pak: Well, even if it's a little affected, even if it's set by law, merchants will cleverly try to take advantage of other people's weaknesses and buy cheap and sell expensive, so there's nothing we can do. Isn't this the time when we sell our rice? We have to sell our rice in the late fall to pay taxes and pay off debts, so this is when we sell the most rice, and merchants take advantage of that and buy it cheap. Then, when the poor people's rice is all gone, they gradually raise the rice price. It's really annoying.

All: So, what will the rice price be like this year? Yun-mo!

Chŏng: Well, according to the newspaper, the standard rice price in Japan this year has been announced as 20 won 58 chŏn.

All: Then it's very different from what we heard. Didn't we hear that it would be 6 won or 7 won per bag? If rice is 20 won 58 chŏn per bag, then one bag of rice should be about 10 won.

Pak: That's only in Japan. What good fortune will come to Chosŏn?

Chŏng: That's the price for Japanese rice. And that 20 won 58 chŏn is based on the prices in large cities that consume a lot of rice, and if you're talking about the market price in rural areas, there are various related costs such as freight and handling, which will cost about 1 won 50 chŏn, so the market price in rural areas is only about 19 won. <20>

Kim: Even that is for rural areas in Japan. What about the market price in rural areas in Chosŏn?

Chŏng: That's how it is. The Japanese rice price and the Chosŏn rice price are always different by about 3 sŏk. So, in rural Chosŏn, one bag of rice is about 16 won, and one bag of rice is about 8 won.

Pak: Hey, if it's really 8 won, I'll sell it.

Kim: If it's 8 won, it'll be a little better, but that's just the market price. There's the freight, the bag fee, and the handling fee. And then there are the dishonest merchants who cheat the villagers who don't know how to read the scales, so in the end, it'll be about 7 won.

All: Yeah, yeah. However much comes back to us, it comes back to us, but it won't come back more.

Yi: I heard that the Japanese government is buying Chosŏn rice. Will that have some impact on the Chosŏn rice price?

Kim: What can we do? How much benefit will we get?

Chŏng: The government is buying 400,000 bags of Chosŏn rice as the first phase, and they're buying it from late November to early December, which is the cheapest time for rice. It won't have much of an impact.

Pak: By the way, I heard that there's a new Chosŏn Rice Tax Control Plan (朝鮮米稅制案) or something. What is that?

Chŏng: That's not good news for us. Because the Chosŏn rice price is low, Chosŏn rice keeps flowing into Japan, which affects the Japanese rice price, which eventually causes a crisis in Japanese rural areas. The Japanese government wants to maintain the Japanese rice price to save Japanese rural areas, so they have to control and regulate the rice coming from Taiwan and Chosŏn, which will only further sacrifice Chosŏn farmers.
