줄다리기, 朝鮮의 正月노리 (Tug-of-War, Korean New Year's Game)
박로아
문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

Tug-of-War, Korean New Year's Game
						Park Ro-ah

Without the warm winter of the South and the innocent farmers, and the folksong of "Kwajina Ching Ching Nane," it would be impossible to find the true, deep, and savory charm of tug-of-war.

On long nights, when the cold winter moon shines alone like a mirror outside, the servants who have slaughtered an ox gather in a room and, sitting there, cannot suppress the *bun (憤 - resentment/indignation) rising in their chests with just the rustic and unadorned Yeongnam dialect banter that comes out of the house and straw mat talk, nor can they handle the strength overflowing in their arms. They throw away the twisted straw rope, kick away the intertwined straw mat, burst out the door, and rush out into the alley, wanting to test their youthful vigor, whether it be among themselves or between villages. This is what tug-of-war is all about. From the time they enter the lunar December, the playful tug-of-war of the children in every alley begins. The rope is made by each of them secretly taking straw ropes from their mothers' houses, and just imitating the adults, they twist and entwine it however they can, so is it only about the size of thick seaweed (甘藿) stalks? Setting this in the middle, dividing into teams, and shouting "Wi-yotsa wiya! Wiyeotsa-wiya!" in the shrill voices of children, they often pull until late at night without even knowing it's cold.

<62>

Passing adults say, "Oh, those kids are already pulling the rope!" lamenting the speed of time, and bachelor servants or farmers stop and watch with amusement, then slyly joining the pulling side, receiving the ticklish resentment and cuteness of the children, laughing heartily, and then stepping back and slowly sneaking away.

In this way, the rope that starts with the children grows thicker and longer day by day with the bright moon of the waxing crescent, and eventually becomes a truly large rope the size of a big tree.

However, the tug-of-war of the adults properly begins after the Lunar New Year and when the full moon is bright, combining it with the Boreum (보름) festivities.

"Tonight, they're pulling the rope in the upper village."

"When I went to the lower village, the choguns (초군 - villagers who go around asking for rice) were braiding a big rope, and it was two arm spans thick, you know!"

When that time comes, rumors of tug-of-war spread from house to house like this, and during the day, young servants in groups of three or four go around with old pot lids or pieces of sheep wool, making noise as they shout in unison:

"Give us straw! Give us straw!"

"Give us straw! Give us straw!"

Then, the women and young girls greet them with happy faces and, smiling, when the children come, they gather the straw rope and the straw left in the house that they saved without using it for heating, and generously bring it out. In this way, they borrow a wide space such as a vegetable garden or an empty house site and, as their hands allow, the farmers come out in shifts, light bonfires, twist straw ropes, and make ropes.

When a rope is being made in one village like this, another village is secretly and busily starting the same work. <63> The size of the other side's rope is completely secret, and only spies secretly come and investigate, but because the rope also has sex, they agree beforehand and decide on a male rope (雄) and a female rope (雌) to make the rope. At that time, the most avoided thing is women.

If a woman unknowingly steps over the stretched-out rope, it is believed that the rope will break or lose during the tug-of-war, and something ominous will surely happen. Although women are mostly aware of this, if they unknowingly make this kind of mistake, they will be cursed with "Bangjeong majeun nyeon!" (방정마즌년! - flighty woman!) "Yamari ppajin gasina!" (얌치업는 계집애 - shameless girl!), and get a scolding that makes the back of their heads tingle. (If you happen to go to the South to watch a tug-of-war, ladies, please pay special attention.) In this way, after spending several days twisting the straw ropes, making the rope strands, and binding the trunks, the male rope has a sturdy oak tree trunk more than one fathom long stuck crosswise on its head like a hairpin (this tree is called "gotnamu" (곳나무)), and its terrifying trunk (rope body) more than one arm span thick is equipped with four or five long tails or legs that spread out like octopus tentacles, then displaying its majestic figure on the main road. Soon another village makes a female rope that is not inferior to this and brings it out.

After showing it to the general villagers for two or three days for public display and demonstration, they finally start the tug-of-war on a moonlit night.

On this night, unlike a holiday, old and young, men and women, gather in groups of three or five from this village and that village with undeniably joyful faces.

From early evening, the children form groups, holding hands, singing Poompumba, Gakseori Taryeong, Kwajina Ching Ching Nane, and whatever else they can, until their throats are hoarse, dancing excitedly and showing off all their talents by the rope.

Then, the women, thinking this is also something to see, cover their faces with their skirts and sit huddled together on the walls, their white faces like gourds in the moonlight, the village girls hiding their laughter and smiling subtly. The footsteps of the bachelor servants passing in front of them feel strangely elated.

Meanwhile, the strong men who are really going to use their strength come out to the rope body, gather in the servants' rooms or taverns, embrace liquor jars, and take turns drinking thick takju (탁주 - unfiltered rice wine) from large bowls, which is mostly a tug-of-war leader preparing for battle by gathering the strongmen and serving them makgeolli (막걸리 - Korean rice wine) bowls. This tug-of-war leader means the rope chief, who is the most friendly to the farmers in the village and has much enthusiasm and interest in the rope game. He must also have some reputation and be able to bear part of the various expenses from the time the rope is made until the tug-of-war ends. <64> Only then will he be recommended and take the honor of the tug-of-war leader. During the tug-of-war, he is at least the commander-in-chief, and like the prince of that village, he receives the farmers' worship and respect. It's something worth trying for a tug-of-war fan.

In this way, the tug-of-war begins.

Strong men who seem to be able to knock down even a tiger with a fist rush out one after another, and bachelor servants and children hang on to the rope's waist and tail, screaming and pulling the rope together. This is the most difficult and spectacular moment.

The strong men on both sides lift their own rope heads as much as possible and try to entwine them as quickly as possible to their advantage, so naturally, there is wrangling and it gets delayed. At times like these, if a weak person goes out to where the ropes are being entwined, they won't even be able to find their bones and will be flattened under the feet of the strong men without a sound, so the children hang in clusters on the rope tails and only scream as if the main road will be torn apart.

"If you don't entwine, you lose!" (An eobureo jumeon-ttwi! - 안 어불어주면-뛰-!)

"If you don't entwine, you lose!"

But only one end of the male rope's gotnamu (곳나무 - tree used in tug of war) has barely been hooked into the noose-like head of the female rope.

After carefully examining each other's situation like this, as soon as one side manages to stab one end of the gotnamu (곳나무) in after much wrestling, a thunderous sound bursts out and immediately becomes a sea of voices.

"U-yeotsa wi-!"

"U-yeotsa wi-!"

Adult voices as thick as bamboo, the fine voices of children as thin as flutes, hoarse bachelor servants, and sharp-tongued high-collar girls...these various voices blend together and spread out beautifully, rising and falling once. If you hear this from afar, you can't help but kick off the blanket and run out, no matter how cold it is.

For a long time, like this, the rope is buried in people, pushing and pulling, with shoulders rising and falling, contesting the victory with the call of wiyeotsa (위엿사) as a signal. When the situation is unfavorable, both sides dispatch a Yabau꾼 (야바우꾼) or Yamashi꾼 (야마시꾼) to the enemy. Then, this Yabau꾼 (야바우꾼) infiltrates the rearguard, which is the easiest to deceive, and gives false commands to the enemy as much as possible.

<65>

So, when sitting and cheering and shouting doesn't work, they shout, "Sit down! Sit down!" to take advantage of the gap when they try to sit down, and in cases where they shouldn't stand up, they shout, "Stand up! Stand up!" to take advantage of the gap when they stand up, trying to pull the rope to their side as much as possible. Because they dream up such nasty Yabau (야바우 - trickery), as soon as they are caught, they receive merciless abuse and are grabbed by the strong men's iron fists and spun around until the sky and earth spin, and they are left lying on the side of the road, unable to get up for a while. This is their innocent and simple punishment, and their cheerful end as they dust themselves off and get up.

Originally, tug-of-war is the most cheerful thing for people watching it enjoyably, but when you actually go out to the tug-of-war field or grab the rope, there is no game that makes you so heated and competitive.

Seeing their village's rope not sitting down or standing up, but constantly being pulled, the women and brazen girls who were standing and watching by the roadside or at the corners of the walls can't stand it any longer, so they run out with their skirts full of stones, lay their stomachs on the rough ground, and scream while pulling and sitting on the rope tail that is being pulled away. What else can they do!

How can the young bachelors with topknots just tremble and watch?

Moreover, around this time, even the kisaengs (기생 - female entertainers) become rope fans, throwing away their janggo (장고 - drum) and gayageum (가야금 - zither) and running out, using their refined and beautiful voices to sing "U-yeotsa wi-ya!" in a melodic way. Then, the tug-of-war field suddenly turns warm as if a spring breeze has blown in, and as if responding to that sound, the "wiyeotsa" sound becomes even louder. Is that all? They wrap stones in their shimmering silk skirts that ripple well in the breeze, and sit on the rope with a charmingly sorrowful face, pulling, their disoriented and flustered expressions at that moment make everyone, regardless of Yujilang (遊治郞 - playboy) or farmer, want to jump in without considering fire or water, their courage and strength are heightened.

The rough rope, the soil-smelling farmers' hands as rough as pine bark, and the fragrant perfume smell and the small, pretty white hands of silk skirts—this is an absurd contrast, but it blends together in one place without any awkwardness. Perhaps this is the deep and mysterious charm of tug-of-war. However, as kisaengs (기생) appear, the shy young girls gradually disappear, and the pure and savory taste of tug-of-war gradually diminishes, and when we see the tendency to become a kind of vile amusement for the leisure classes, we cannot help but feel a subtle regret, as if we have lost the wild field where we used to sing pastoral songs and pick flowers as children.

Moreover, because the glittering long swords of the policemen often fall on the rope and break it in two, on the grounds that it obstructs public peace and makes the roads dirty, it seems that this kind of local game is gradually fading away.

After making a bet on alcohol that night, they divided into teams by village <66> and started tug-of-war, but in the process of losing momentum and being dragged helplessly, they lost their strength and exhausted their energy, and one person fell and two people fell. Finally, when the defeated general even sprinkled tears on his sleeve and returned, they dragged the rope of the tragic enemy, whose waist was broken and feet were falling off, until the end of its length, and only after throwing it into the stream flowing across the place did they finally satisfy their conquering spirit. They shouted the victory cry and returned in triumph, putting the tug-of-war leader in the rope head on their shoulders, lifting him from the rope head to the tail and carrying him on their shoulders, dancing and singing "Kwajina Ching Ching Nane" at the top of their lungs.

The rope leader, sitting high and adjusting his throat, starts singing, and all the people carrying the rope respond. The winter moon, half-hanging on the Seondosan (仙桃山) ridge, sheds a dawn-like moonlight as if to say that it is still watching here, illuminating the path they are treading. The drawn-out reverberation of Kwajina Ching Ching (쾌지나 칭칭) spreads out into the quiet sleeping night sky, sounding like a mournful elegy of the people who have lost their hometown and are leaving.

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

There are so many stars in the sky!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

There are so many pebbles on the stream bank!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

There are so many lice in the old tattered clothes!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

There are so many words about other people's daughters-in-law!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

There are so many worries in my heart!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

Good, good, it's a little good!

Kwajina Ching Ching Nane!

-This is the story of tug-of-war I saw in Gyeongju (慶州) when I was young.- <67>

줄다리기, 朝鮮의 正月노리
						박로아
南國의 따뜻한 겨을과 순박한 농민,「쾌지나칭칭나네」의 향토적 민요를 빼고서 줄다리기의 참말 속깁고 구수한 정취는 차즐수 업슬 것임니다.
기나긴 밤 찰삭한 겨을달이 밧게서 혼자 거울가치 밝을제 소잡은 머슴들 방에 모혀 안저서 그 꺽둑꺼둑하고 꾸밈업는 嶺南사투리로 집과 색기모댁이＜62＞속에서 풀여나오는 외담괴설만으로는 가슴에 끌어 올으는 *憤을 눌을수 업고 두 팔에 넘치는 힘을 감당할수 업을세라. 꼬든 색기를 집어 던지고 얽든 멍석을 젯처노코 문을 박차고 울우우 골목으로 몰려 나와서 자긔네 끼리 동리와 동리끼리라도 조흐니 한바탕 용소슴치는 청춘의 혈기를 시험해보자는 것이 이 줄다리기일 것임니다. 동지섯달에 들어서면서부터 발서 골목골목 어린아희들의 작난가음가튼 줄다리기가 시작이 됨니다. 줄이래야 제마다 집에서 어머니 몰내 집어내온 색기로 어룬들뽄만보고 아무럿케나 제끼리 꼬고 얽고해서 만든것이니 굵은 미역(甘藿)줄기만이나 할가요. 이걸 가운데 노코 편을 갈너서 동자들의 엽분 목소래로 「위-욧사위야! 위엿사위-야!」하고 추운줄도 몰으고 밤이 눗도록 당기는 것을 흔히 볼수가 잇슴니다.
지나가든 어룬들이 「어 발서 야들이 줄을 당기는구나!」하고 세월의 빠른 것을 탄식하기도 하고 총각머슴이나 농부들은 발을 멈추고 자미잇는 듯이 구경하다가 끌여가는 편에 슬그머니 한목들어서 어린이들의 간지러운 미움과 귀여움을 밧고 허허허 웃스면서 뒷걸음질처 슬금슬금 꽁문이를 빼기도 함니다.
이리하야 어린이들의 줄에서 시작된 줄은 하로하로 살쩌가는 상현의 밝은 달과 함께 기리가 벗고 등치가 굵어지면서 나종에는 아람도리 정말 큰 줄이 되는 것임니다.
그러나 정작 어룬들의 줄다리기는 음력설을 지나고 보름달이 밝을때나 되여 보름노리를 겸해서 벌어지기를 시작함니다.
「오늘 밤에는 웃동리에서 줄을 당긴다네.」
「아래ㅅ마을에 가보니 초군들이 큰 줄을 뀌미는데 크기가 두아람이나 되드구나 야야!」
그때가 되면 이럿케 집집마다 줄다리기 소문이 퍼지고 낫에는 어린 머슴아희들이 3,4명식 짝을지어 헌 남비바닥이나 양털 쪼각 갓흔 것을 두들겨 가며
「집히나 색기나 좀주소!」
「집히나 색기나 좀주소!」
하고 소래를 맛추어 외치면서 집집마다 순회를 돔니다. 그러면 안악네나 처녀들이 반가운 얼골로 웃스면서 그 애들이 오면 줄여고 불때지 안코 남겨 두엇든 색기와 집안에 남어잇는 집흘 모아서 앗김업시 끄내줌니다. 이와가치하야 동냥을 어든것과 한동 두동 유지들이 기부해준 집흘 모아서 노는 菜圃나 뷘집터 가튼 넓은 장소를 빌어가지고 손나는 대로 농군들이 밧구차레 나와서 모닥불을 피어 노코 색기도 꼬고 줄도 드리고 함니다.
이럿케 한동리에서 줄을 꾸밀때는 반다시 다른 동리에서도 비밀리에 분주히 갓흔일을 시작하고 잇는 것임니다.＜63＞ 상대편의 줄이 얼마나 크고 적은 것은 일체 비밀이요 오직 양편 탐정군이 몰내와서 탐지해갈 뿐이지만은 줄에도 性이 잇슴으로 이것은 미리 內約을 하고 서로 숫줄(雄)암줄(雌)을 작정하야서 줄을 꾸미는데 그때 제일 기피한것은 여자임니다.
여자가 만일 몰으고 뻐처노흔 줄을 타넘으면 줄다리기할 때 줄이 끈허지거나 지거나 하여 반다시 무슨 불길한 일이 생긴다는 一種 미신을 가지고 잇서서 여자들도 대개는 이것을 알고 잇지만 혹 몰으고 이갓흔 실수를 범하게 되면 그만「방정마즌년!」「야마리빠진 가신아!」(얌치업는 계집애)하고 뒤통수가 뻐근하게 산뗌이갓흔 욕을 먹게 됨니다. (南道에 가서서 혹 줄다리기 구경을 하시드라도 부인들께서는 특별히 주의하실 일입니다.) 이리하야 몃날며칠 색기를 꼬고 줄가리를 드리고 둥치를 동이고 하야 숙줄은 길이가 한발이 넘는 단단한 참나무둥치를 비녀가치 머리에 가로꼽고 (이 나무를 「곳나무」라고 함니다.) 한아람이 훨신넘는 끔찍스런 동체와 (즐대목)문어발처럼 벌어저 나온 4,5개 길다란 꼬리랄지 발이랄지를 갓추어서 그 雄姿를 큰길우에 낫하내이면 또 다른 동리에서도 여기 밋지지 안는 암줄을 꾸며가지고 내여놋케 됨니다.

이럿케 2,3일동안 공개와 시위를 겸하야 일반동리 사람들에게 구경을 시킨다음에 다시 달밝은 밤을 택하야 비로소 줄다리기를 시작하는 것임니다.
이 날 밤에는 명절이나 답지안케 늙은이 젊은이 남녀 할것업시 무비 질거운 얼골로 이 동리 저 동리에서 삼삼오오 몰려나옴니다.
어린 아희들은 초저녁부터 떼를지어 손을 맛잡고 품품바 각설이타령 쾌지나칭칭나네 할것업시 되는데로 목이 잡히도록 부르면서 흥청덩청 춤들을 추어가며 줄겻헤서 가진 재롱을 다부리지요.
그러면 이것도 또한 구경꺼리라고 안악네들이 치마ㅅ자락을 뒤집어쓰고 담벽에서 옹기종기 모혀안저서 달빗에 박꼿가치 흰 얼골이 싀골시악씨들의 수집은 웃음을 먹음고 빙그릴 때 그 압흘 지나는 총각머슴들의 발거름이 까닭업시 웃줄거리는 것임니다.
이리는새 줄대목에 나서서 정작 힘깨나 쓸 장골들은 머슴들방이나 주점에 모혀서 술독을 끼고 둘너안자 대접으로 뻑뻑한 탁주를 차례로 들이키며 눗장을 피우는데 이것은 대개 줄패장되는 사람이 전투준비로 역사들을 모아서 막걸니 대접을 하는것임니다. 이 줄패장이란 줄두목이란 의미로 그 동리에서 가장 농민들에게 친근하고 줄경기에 대하야 만흔 열성과 취미를 가진 사람으로 다소간 명망도 잇고 줄을 꾸밀 때부터 줄다리기가 끈날때까지 여러가지 비용의 일부를 감당할수 잇서야만＜64＞ 비로소 擧薦되야 줄패장의 영예를 차지하게 되는 것인데 줄다리기하는 동안만은 적어도 총사령관이요 그 동리의 왕자와 가치 농부들의 숭앙과 존대를 밧는 것이니 줄다리기팬으로는 한번 해볼만한 노릇이라 하겟슴니다.
