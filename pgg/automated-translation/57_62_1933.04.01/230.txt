별건곤 제62호
1933년 04월 01일
(漫談) 醫師와 占쟁이 (Random Talk: Doctors and Fortune Tellers)
柳光烈
문예기타 (Miscellaneous Literature)

Some friends were sitting around on a winter day, and, for lack of anything better to do, conversation turned to the topic of whether people can ever foresee what will happen to them. From there, the discussion went to the saying that doctors can’t cure their own illnesses.

A: "It's true that doctors can’t cure their own illnesses."

B: "There are plenty of examples. Kim Yong-chae (金溶埰), who died at the age of 40, was a famous internal medicine doctor, but he died of a stomach ailment, which was his specialty."

C: "Ah, and that's not all. Dr. Jeong Seok-tae (鄭錫泰), a medical doctor in Taya-machi, suffered from appendicitis and spent months in the hospital affiliated with the medical school."

B: "Are you talking about that person? Min (閔某) from XX-dong, a specialist in venereal diseases, couldn’t cure his own venereal disease and had to close his hospital due to arthritis, wasn't it?"

A: "It’s not just doctors. Even shamans who supposedly cure illnesses with their prayers can’t cure their own family's illnesses. It's all so futile."

B: "Ha! I had a very effective shaman living next door. She could cure illnesses by performing rituals for other people, but when her three children all died, she seemed to lose her power."

C: "Recently, in Gye-dong, a shaman performed a huge three-day ritual for her family’s happiness at the beginning of the year, but a fire broke out and burned down her house. It seems the spirits were too powerful."

A: "Let’s leave the shamans aside. Even in places where they believe in God, lightning strikes. Years ago, lightning struck Jonghyeon Catholic Church (鍾峴天主敎堂) and shattered the character for ‘heaven’ (天) that was placed on the very top."

B: "That might happen because nature and God are different... But during the reign of King Seonjo (宣祖大王), there was a famous fortune teller named Hong Gye-gwan (洪繼寬). He was so good at fortune telling that he accurately predicted the death dates of others, but he didn’t know his own death date. That’s why there’s a saying, ‘Even Hong Gye didn’t know when he would die.’" <42>

B: "When Hong Gye-gwan was about to die, King Seonjo summoned him and had a mouse run across the floor. He asked Hong Gye-gwan what kind of animal and how many had passed by. Hong Gye-gwan said that four mice had passed by. So the king had him beheaded for deceiving the royal family. When they cut open the mouse's belly, they found that it was pregnant with three pups, they say."

C: "Is it just Hong Gye-gwan? According to the *Samguk Yusa* (三國遺事), there was a famous fortune teller in the Goguryeo kingdom named Chu-nam (秋南) who was also beheaded because he couldn’t identify a dog that was pregnant with seven pups."

B: "In the old days, the fortune teller Yi Sun-pung (李淳風) was ordered by the Tang Dynasty emperor to predict the fate of the nation. He foresaw that Empress Wu Zetian (武則天) would seize power and throw the Tang Dynasty into chaos, but he was so afraid that he would be punished that he was beside himself with fear. Fortune tellers can predict the misfortunes of others, but when it comes to their own necks…"

A: "Shao Kangjie (邵康節) was also famous for his fortune-telling skills. They say that Shao Kangjie went up a mountain and sat down, contemplating the fate of the universe in his unique way. He happened to notice a small mound of soil rising up from the ground at his feet. Upon closer inspection, he saw that it was a small pine seed sprouting and pushing its way out of the soil. So, Kangjie immediately calculated the *saju* (四柱) of the pine tree and divined its fate. He saw that the pine tree would grow to touch the sky and live for hundreds of years. Eventually, the tree would be cut down and used as a main pillar in the government office (官衙) that a magistrate (守令) in a certain village would build. What was even more coincidental was that, in a fortune he had previously told about his descendants, he had predicted that one of his descendants would commit murder and be put to death. The magistrate who would judge that case would be using the government office with the main pillar made from that tree, and that the magistrate was destined to be crushed to death by that pillar. So, Kangjie smiled and wrote something on a piece of paper, which he placed deep inside a box and told his descendants to pass down through the generations without opening it. After Kangjie passed away, the box was passed down for one generation, two generations, nine generations, as a document written by the great scholar. Sure enough, the ninth-generation descendant committed murder and was arrested as a murderer, about to be beheaded. <43> The official who was to judge the case dreamed that night that an old man came to him and said, 'I am Shao Kangjie, and my ninth-generation descendant is about to die because of your judgment. You must put the killer to death, but I have something that has been passed down for generations. Take a look at it before you execute him.' So, the official summoned the criminal and asked, 'Do you have anything passed down from your ninth-generation ancestor?' The criminal replied, 'Yes, I do.' So the official ordered him to bring the box. When the box was opened, there were two lines of writing on the thin paper. The officials were very puzzled and were about to go down from the veranda to the courtyard to look at the writing inside the box more closely, but as soon as they stepped down from the veranda to the courtyard, the large pillar broke and collapsed, nearly killing everyone. Despite the frightening situation, the officials opened the box and saw the writing: '救爾壓棟死活我九代孫' ['I will save you from being crushed to death by the pillar, so spare my ninth-generation descendant']. So, moved by this, the officials spared Kangjie’s ninth-generation descendant. So- Kangjie knew that a certain official would be crushed to death by a pillar hundreds of years later, but there is no mention of him knowing the date of his own death."

A: "Was Kangjie the only one at that time? When the two scholars Jeong Myeong-do (程明道) and Jeong Yi-cheon (程利川) went to play at the Yueyang Tower (岳陽樓), Jeong Myeong-do looked at the main pillar and smiled, but Jeong Yi-cheon stared at his older brother's smile for a long time and shook his head, saying, 'First, it will become timber for the Epang Palace (阿房宮), then the main pillar of the Yueyang Tower, and finally the coffin (柩) of Kublai Khan (忽必烈).' Jeong Myeong-do frowned, saying that he was divulging the secrets of heaven (天機), and said, 'Hey! Why are you so careless?'"

B: "Those aren't fortune tellers, they’re Taoist scholars."

C: "In our country's *Samguk Yusa*, it doesn't say that Queen Seondeok (善德女王) was good at fortune telling, but how could she have been so clever as to predict the day and time of her own death and even know that 천왕사 (Cheonwangsa Temple) would be built in front of her tomb hundreds of years later?"

B: "What good is it to know the future, and what does it matter if you don't? We have suffered so much in the past because of being misled by such things. It's all superstition (迷信) and nonsense. Marx said that all reasoning should start from 'self-evidence,' that is, from self-evident truths. We don't even have time to deal with the self-evident things that are clearly in front of our eyes-"

A, B, C: "You are right. Your story is just idle talk and empty words. For us new young people living in science, this kind of story is just a digestive aid after a meal." <44>
