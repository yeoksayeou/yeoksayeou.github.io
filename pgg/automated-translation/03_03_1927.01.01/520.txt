잡지명	別乾坤 제3호
발행일	1927년 01월 01일
기사제목	(創作) 콩나물 죽과 小說 (Creative Work: Bean Sprout Porridge and the Novel)
필자	崔承一
기사형태	소설 (Fiction)

崔承一 (Ch'oe Sŭng-il)

One

A few days ago, the rice and barley I sold for a few *tae* (되) disappeared, and there wasn't even half a *toe* (되) left over from last night's cooking. It was cold, and there was nothing to pawn. So, on that chilly morning, to at least warm our throats, I made bean sprout porridge. After the family had a bowl or a large cup each and I was about to clear the table, my worried mother asked,

"What are we going to eat for dinner?"

I saw her utter, perhaps a lament or a complaint, probably coming out with a burp. Unconsciously, I worried about her concern. Upon hearing this, the faces of the family members, who had been enjoying the porridge and wiping their noses while eating heartily, momentarily forgot the pleasure of eating, and suddenly, their faces were filled with dark expressions.

"What we ate is coming back up. We should digest what we ate well."

I said, throwing out the words, but not in a very unpleasant way. <132>

It wasn't often that the family of six or so—though it's an uncomfortable expression—ate as if pigs were sticking their snouts into the trough, slurping and making noise, the steam rising and the faint smell of beans stimulating their appetites, enjoying their meal while talking. But as soon as my talkative mother spoke a word while clearing the table, their spirits plummeted, and they wore expressions of concern and an inexplicable unpleasantness. My wife and stepson took out the broken pieces of the table.

But indeed, my mother's words were true—a very clear fact. Stimulated by my mother's words, I put the remaining piece of kimchi in my mouth and sat squatting on the edge of the porch, looking at the messy table after the meal. Beyond the window in front of me, I saw the rice bin on the edge of the porch—though it's called a rice bin, it's a small, broken thing that used to be a bean bin when we were well-off. It's empty. It's truly baffling. These days, if I happen to oversleep in the morning, I wake up at the sound of the bottom of the rice bin being scraped. But what good does it do to wake up? For the past year or several months, the six or seven family members—my mother, my wife, my sister, my stepson, my niece—have only looked at my face. There are times when I'm truly at a loss. After wandering around all day, I sometimes wonder if I should just steal something. Indeed, lately, when passing by houses or grand houses with imposing gates, I've thought about how I would cleverly steal something from those houses, or how I would enter those grand houses and act like a detective in a mystery novel. Carrying my heavy legs, tired body, and confused mind, I often had these thoughts almost every day when I couldn't get lunch and had to wander around until dinner time. But I've never acted on it. The world might call it a pure heart or moral conscience, but I see it as an anesthetic that paralyzes people's innate and natural instincts. What good are conscience and morality? I think that I can't do such things only because of my incompetence. I don't want to be known as someone who can't do such things because I'm weak. So, I'm afraid that I might end up in shackles, behind iron bars, or on the guillotine—it would be better to die with just skin on my face, twisted legs, and a stomach stuck to my back.

Another reason is the dirty compromise, the hope that I might do well and have some kind of happiness. <133> That hope is dragging me through this wicked, dog-like world, and that wicked hope is pulling this withered, large body around, spinning it in circles. I hear someone calling "Kim Hyŏng (김형)" from outside. I jump up and go out. It's P'yŏng (형) from T magazine.

"How's the novel going?"

He asks with a gaunt face—perhaps he had a good breakfast—wearing the same worn-out cotton overcoat I've seen him wear for three years, looking tired.

"I can't write it. I sent you a postcard last night. I really can't write it. I need to have some idea to start with."

"That's too bad."

He just clicks his tongue. We stand in silence for a while, feeling sorry for each other. We probably had a lot to say, but no words came out. But I tried to say as much as I could.

"I can't do anything these days. I'll bring it to you whenever I write something. Please publish it then."

He wasn't a very talkative friend either.

"Then next time."

"Yes."

I said, shivering, and stepped into the yard. The warmth from the bean sprout porridge had vanished, and my body trembled like an aspen leaf again. Suddenly, I muttered to myself, "Bean sprout porridge and the novel—bean sprout porridge and the novel."

As I entered the room,

"Who is it...?"

My wife asked, her face showing a hint of hope that there might be some help with dinner.

"Hey, what am I supposed to do when someone tells a dying man to write a novel?"

"Hmph."

My wife scoffed.

"Hey, it's pathetic. Asking someone who ate a bowl of bean sprout porridge without even kimchi to write a novel—hey, tell me a story or give me some material. Then I'll write it."

I was so speechless and dumbfounded that I said this jokingly, hoping to calm my mind or forget about it, engaging in this insincere banter.

"So, what did you say?"

"I said I couldn't write it. I hoped they wouldn't see it as avoidance or anything like that."

"Oh, don't do that, just write it. This morning we ate bean sprout porridge, there's nothing for dinner, <134> the whole family is worried—write even what you always say, 'For some reason, when I sit hungry, I'm ashamed to even look at my family.'"

She throws these life-giving words at me—full of passion—like the faint feeling we get when the fog clears on a hazy winter morning and the morning sun rises.

Hearing those words, I laughed inwardly. But then I felt a prick in my heart. (That's right. That's also a novel, the most vivid novel of honest life—a record, a novel. But I don't have the courage to write it. The whole family is trembling, there's nothing for dinner, and I also have no idea what to eat—can I write a novel in this situation?)

As I was leaving, not knowing where to go, wearing my worn-out overcoat,

"I guess we won't be making kimchi this year either. It's over. Since things have turned out like this, I wonder if we'll even remember it. I wish we had bought some radish seeds."

"Well, Mother, you're being too much. We can't even eat, and you're worrying about making kimchi."

For some reason, I don't know why, I suddenly got angry. So, as I stepped onto the porch, I shouted in a loud voice—loud enough for the neighbors to hear.

"Oh, where have all the days gone when we used to make ten loads of kimchi?"

I turned my back and came outside, these useless lamentations of wanting the past to be better were leaking out in the cold, dilapidated room. My mother's mind, thinking only of the days when we were well-off—not knowing what kind of environment she's in now, thinking that even if we starve to death or become more ruined, we can't rise again and live well, still having the vain, ignorant hope that the old days will return—I felt sorry for her and also somewhat annoyed, feeling suffocated and my head aching.

"Ah, this heart. Where are you going to drag this tired, incompetent lump of flesh today, my uncontrollable, suffocating heart? To the main street of Jongno (종로)—to a friend's house—to the newspaper and magazine room of the library—."

Two

I don't know whether I was carrying my wandering heart or my wandering heart was dragging me. <135> I went back home, exhausted, and my stepson was tending the fire in the kitchen.

"How are we going to cook rice? But I guess no one is destined to die,"

I said, first opening the door and sticking my head in, looking at my mother's face,

"How did you manage to cook rice?"

It wasn't that we were going to cook rice, but there was nothing more welcome or fortunate for me than that. So, the first thing I asked was how we managed to cook rice.

"I borrowed a *tae* (되) of rice from the neighbor. They somehow managed to sell three *tae* (되) of rice today. Anyway—"

"That's good."

"What about you? Did you manage to borrow any money?"

My mother, who had been wondering about me all day, asked as she saw me enter the room.

"What money? Who would give it to me? I can't even borrow it because I don't know if I can pay it back, so I just asked them for some."

My mother was startled.

"You're pathetic. How can you just ask people for something?"

"I tried saying that for the first time today. What's wrong with asking a friend or two for some?"

"Who would give it to you if you just ask?"

The conversation ended there. The report of the results of their staying home all day and my wandering outside ended simply there.

But I noticed something very strange in front of me. Someone was lying down in the warmer part of the room, covered with a blanket. Only black hair was sticking out from the dirty blanket. I instinctively thought, "Ah, it's my wife."

I didn't even know where my wife had gone, but now I realized that the person covered in the blanket was my wife. I slowly went to her head and sat down.

"Where does it hurt?"

Without a word, she turned her body and groaned like a sick person.

"She was making a fuss all morning. She said her stomach hurt so much, and she must have gotten indigestion from what she ate this morning. She was washing radishes with cold water and washing her underpants, so she must have gotten chilled."

My mother told me the outline of the story in a disjointed way.

"The bean sprout porridge gave her indigestion."

I shouted inwardly at that moment. I rubbed my cold hands together and touched my wife's forehead. My hands were cold, but she seemed to have a fever.

"It hurts a lot. Washing clothes in the cold—."

Poverty brought the illness—I knew that, but I had nothing to cure her. So, I had nothing but empty words to comfort her. My pockets were empty. I had nothing but cold, empty hands. Should I blame poverty? Poverty is the enemy. Reaching this point, I didn't even know the cause of this poverty. If I knew, what would I do? I do know. But knowing it doesn't solve the poverty we're experiencing now. I know why I'm like this, why my family is like this, and why so many people are like this. But what good is it? It's nothing. Even the truth in this world is nothing. I doubt that things are going according to the truth. Even the thought of this is nothing. Right now, my wife is sick. I'm hungry. These fragmented thoughts circled in my head, and my eyes didn't leave my wife. Suddenly, my wife jumped up and pointed to the upper part of the room. Instinctively, my hand went to the chamber pot. The chamber pot was placed in front of her. She—gagged and vomited. I patted her back. She vomited up what she had eaten in short bursts, like rainwater pouring from the eaves during the rainy season. Pieces of bean sprouts came out, some whole, some half-broken. "How can people live eating this?" I had nothing but what I was seeing and experiencing. In the end, she trembled convulsively once and wiped her mouth with the cold water that my mother brought, then collapsed back into her place.

It was dim. No, it was completely dark. I had to light the kerosene lamp. The lamp was lit, and its light dimly showed my wife lying down in front of me. Like a ghost, she lay there with her hair disheveled. "I can't watch. I can't watch." How much did I shout, but I was still holding my wife's head and sitting there.

Dinner was brought in.

The sick person was sick, but the healthy people had to eat.

The rice passed down my esophagus with difficulty.

My wife was suffering from the bean sprout porridge, but the people who ate started nodding off and then collapsed as they were. Is this how people live? I wanted to shout up at the sky. But I could only look up at the ceiling.

The night grew deeper. "Yagimo (야기모)"—"Yagimo (야기모)"—"Manjuno (만주노) hoyahoya (호야호야)" still stirred my appetite, even though I hadn't eaten a decent dinner. I lamented, "Why did I have to have a stomach and not be able to live? I wish I didn't have a stomach or internal organs and could just live by breathing air."

I also looked at the ceiling, laughed, and leaned against the wall.

Since she's sick, she needs medicine. She needs a doctor.

"Since she's vomiting so much, it would be good to give her an injection and then give her medicine as she gradually gets better."

"Or we could just buy her a package of Lyongsinhwan (령신환) to eat."

But we didn't have money to buy Lyongsinhwan (령신환). I was at a loss. It would be good to go and get the money, but there was no one to give it to me, and everyone was asleep.

"Let's bear with it. Putting salt in her mouth calmed her down a bit earlier."

I muttered to myself, leaning against the wall, and looked down at my wife's face with blurred vision as I fell asleep. My wife was in a daze—was she sleeping or not?

I ran quickly to the doctor. The doctor was there. He was my classmate from elementary school. After hearing my story, he seemed annoyed and followed me as if saying, "It's cold and bothersome." Since we were close, I didn't call a rickshaw and walked with him. When he entered the room where my wife was lying, there must have been a natural odor. He turned his head and came in, first taking out his stethoscope and listening to my wife's chest. Then he asked me about the beginning and symptoms of the illness. He gave her an injection and said,

"Her stomach is naturally sensitive, and she seems to have *kwangyŏk* (관격) due to malnutrition and weakness, and she also seems to have a slight fever (丹積)."

Then he stood up. I followed him. I was worried about what to do since I didn't have money.

"But surely, in our situation—."

I followed him and was about to get up and leave with a bottle of liquid medicine and two days' worth of powdered medicine when

"Hey, Doctor Pak (박형), I suddenly don't have any money—"

I hesitated, and he said,

"Oh, never mind—but when did your family become like this? It's okay if you don't bring it."

The hand holding the medicine bottle was relieved, and my worried mind was at ease. But for some reason, it felt like I was being insulted and looked down upon.

"Is this how people who receive charity from others feel?"

As I realized this, I felt endless displeasure with the actions of my old friend, Doctor Pak (박), who had treated me with such lack of sincerity even while helping me.

"Ungrateful bastard—."

I muttered as I walked to our alley. Since it was a dark neighborhood without a single streetlamp, I stumbled and fell, breaking the medicine bottle and spilling the medicine.

"Ah—chamber pot, chamber pot."

I shouted and shook my wife, waking her up. She was having me hand over the chamber pot. She vomited. My mother also woke up. She came and rubbed her back. Only then did I regain my senses.

"It was a dream?"

I laughed bitterly, but I didn't know how precious that dream was.

Seeing my wife vomiting and suffering.

The short dream—in the midst of suffering, the night had already dawned. The window was so bright that I looked out and saw that it had snowed for the first time this year during the night. It was piled up beautifully here and there. The window was even brighter because of the snow. I could hear a rooster crowing from somewhere in the neighborhood. I could hear the sound of shoes rolling and the sound of water buckets starting to be carried. The world was starting to move again.

"Damn, why is the night so bright?! It's sickening—."

I muttered to myself again.

After a while, the sun was visible in the clear, chilly sky. The edges of the roofs were wet, and the sunlight came and embraced them. Again—illness—doctor—Lyongsinhwan (령신환)—hunger—all these worries became a jumbled mess and crashed into my head.

"Damn, I hate the sunrise." - "End" <139>
