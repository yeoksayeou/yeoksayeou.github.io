기사형태	회고·수기 (Reminiscence/Memoir)

The Hardships of Chasing After Ch'a Ch'ŏnja
						Chŏng In-ik

I don't remember exactly whether it was late spring or early summer last year, but it was when I was at Company C. As usual, I left for outside work late in the morning and went to the C Police Station (C警察署), my usual place of contact. At that time, we used to call the editors in the newspaper company every day to maintain contact. I made a call to the editor that day as well. Then, the editor, as if he had been waiting, happily said, "Hey, I heard that Tae Siguk Ch'a Ch'ŏnja (Pot'ŏnkyo Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk) (大時國車天子(普天敎車京錫)) has come up [to Seoul]. Have you heard the news? Find out if you didn't know and come in." At that time, Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk was the focus of public criticism. Even children and errand boys knew his name and his persona when you said Ch'a Ch'ŏnja Kyŏng-sŏk, so the news of this person's arrival in Seoul was indeed the biggest "news" for the newspaper, and the fact that this person had left his den in Chŏngŭp, from which he never left easily, meant that there must be some significant content behind it. So, I immediately ran to the Police Security Section. There, they brought out the lodging books of each inn in this police station's jurisdiction, and I first rummaged through the K neighborhood J Inn (K동 J여관). However, all I got from this was the discovery that a certain Yi from a certain county in Kyŏngsangbuk-do (慶尙北道 某郡) and several others had stayed there the day before this person supposedly came up. Anyway, even if Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk was staying at this inn, it was reasonable to assume that he wouldn't be staying under his real name, so I went to J Inn right away. At that time, K reporter from Company D (D社) had already arrived before me and was questioning the owner. I thought to myself, "Damn! I can't write this alone," and reluctantly stood with him and questioned the owner, but the owner didn't say anything in detail either. While doing this, time was short and the deadline for the evening edition was approaching. In desperation, I went back to the newspaper company. And I wrote an extremely abstract article saying that there was a rumor of Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk's arrival in Seoul and reported it first in the evening edition. The D newspaper that evening also only published vague articles.

After finishing the evening edition, taking the opportunity of C Daily having a morning edition, unlike other newspapers that were competing at the time, I decided to investigate the details by any means and report it first in the morning edition. And I immediately went to J Inn again. However, I still couldn't hear a satisfactory story and only heard that two or three rustics <35> with topknots had come and stayed and then left. At that time, I intuitively thought, "That's it, thieves usually wear topknots, so maybe these guys are them," and asked about their actions while they were staying. Then the answer was that they went in and out very busily.

Hearing that, I thought on the spot, "If these guys are Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk's gang, they definitely wouldn't have walked. They're trying to avoid the eyes and ears of the world, and Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk sees himself as an emperor, so he definitely rode something." I decided to search the nearby rickshaw stations and automobile departments first. Leaving the inn, I searched all the rickshaw stations in the neighborhood, but I failed too easily. So, finally, I went to the K Automobile Department (K自動車部) across the alley from Chŏn-dong Inn. At that time, I found that a classmate named H was visiting this automobile department. So, after exchanging greetings with the owner through Mr. H's introduction, I asked if he had ever taken a handcart-pulling guest staying at Chŏn-dong Inn. At this time, his answer indeed gave me a glimmer of hope. That is, one day at dawn, there was a handcart-pulling rustic who took a car to Yŏngdŭngp'o Station. I was so happy to hear this that I shouted "That's it, it's done!" without realizing it. And then I asked about the subsequent progress. His answer was, "I waited for the Kyŏngbu Line northbound train (京釜線北行列車) at Yŏngdŭngp'o Station, picked up an extravagantly luxurious, obese old man getting off the train, and then took him all the way to Map'o outside the city." Without saying a word, I told him to quickly drive the car to the house in Map'o he had taken him to. The owner, without knowing what was going on, grabbed the "handle" of the car as instructed and sped towards Sŏngsŏ (城西). In the meantime, I was planning various tactics inside the car, but I was suddenly dizzy with the joy of getting this much of a clue and the anxiety of what if these people were not Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk's party even though I had put in so much effort.

Soon the car stopped. The place was at the foot of a hill almost all the way to the Map'o riverside (麻浦江邊). The car owner said it seemed to be a house on top of that hill, but he didn't know exactly. I rushed up that hill in one breath and peered into every house, but I couldn't find a single grand building where Tae Siguk Ch'ŏnja Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk could stay. Somewhat disheartened, I called out the children who were making a fuss in the neighborhood one by one, giving them one chŏn or two chŏn, and asked them in the most affectionate way if there was a house nearby where rustic guests had come. The children, having received money, were chattering as they pleased, trying to be quite kind and telling me everything they knew, but they couldn't give me a single satisfactory answer. Continuing to be disappointed, I had this thought again. "Let's search all the side dish stores in this neighborhood. If there is a house where Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk is staying, or if there is a house where other rustic guests are staying, they would have provided better side dishes than usual." <36> And then I went on a pilgrimage to the side dish stores that were closest to the houses on this hill and the most abundant in goods in that neighborhood. I bought useless fruits, cigarettes, and matches, and while walking around like this, I reached a certain place that seemed to be a medium-sized store, and after starting two or three questions and answers, the store owner said, "Oh, right, right, a few days ago, my in-laws from the countryside came to that not-so-big house on top of that hill and bought great side dishes, and they said they came from Chŏlla-do or something. Listening to the people who come to buy things, they seem to be important guests." I left the words "Yes, is that so?" as a greeting and went up the hill and found the house the store owner was pointing to and looked for the owner. But no one came out and it was as desolate as a house after a gut (굿) [shamanic ritual] had been performed. After repeatedly calling out "Come here" for a while, I turned around with nothing but a dry throat. At that time, I found an old man wearing a tanggeon (宕巾) and holding a long bamboo cane (長竹) on the road where I couldn't find a single adult, and I started begging him again. Then the old man pouted and pointed to the house, saying, "Oh! That house is Ch'a Ch'ŏnja's in-laws' house. Someone came early yesterday morning and left this morning! They stayed in all day yesterday and didn't go out at all until midnight, and then two cars came and went after midnight!" He answered very displeased in a rude tone and turned away. Anyway, I was as happy as if I had gone to heaven. "Okay, there's a reward for my efforts. Let's go back to the city first." I turned around and made this decision alone. "Those guys who stayed at Chŏn-dong Inn must be the advance team. It's a fact that Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk came to Seoul. There's no reason for smoke to come out of a chimney if there's no fire." Reaching the car, I asked the car owner if there was an automobile department here, and he replied, "No." On the way back to the city, I was again immersed in inquiry. "Who did that car that came last night pick up? Or where did Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk go?" After repeatedly thinking this way and that way, I came to this conclusion alone again. "The first car came to pick up Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk. The second car picked him up again and brought him back. Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk must have gone somewhere."

Arriving in the city, I started the automobile department pilgrimage again with the car owner. I went to all the large automobile departments that would be good enough for Ch'a Ch'ŏnja's use. However, there was not a single place that gave me a plausible answer. More disappointment! The time to go to the company was approaching moment by moment, and at that time, I knew about six or seven parts of this fact, but I couldn't find out the remaining three or four parts, so I was indeed burning inside with angry and impatient thoughts. Then the car stopped again in front of the O Automobile Department (O自動車部) on Chongno Street. At that time, I didn't have the courage to go down and ask because this automobile department didn't look great at first glance, and I had failed so many times. <37> So I had the car owner ask instead, and soon the car owner came out with a not insignificant look of joy, bringing a driver-like person and putting him on the driver's seat, and then drove south, south on the main road, where a faint darkness was slowly beginning. The immediate riddle was bigger to me than the big riddle of Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk. "What is this all about? Now that it's come to this, I'll just watch like a spectator." I waited patiently for the place where the car would go. But even while thinking this, I was actually holding on to a glimmer of hope. Just then, I caught a certain hint that jumped into my head like lightning. And I asked the unknown man sitting in the driver's seat as if begging. I intuitively, almost instinctively, realized that this man definitely had the key to this riddle.

The unknown man was indeed an invaluable benefactor and an irreplaceable teacher to me at this time. Afterwards, coming back to the company and grabbing a pen, I wrote an article roughly as follows, recklessly, as if a hungry man were tearing into a meal, and gave it to the editor.

Ch'a Kyŏng-sŏk came to Seoul. Due to the karmic consequences of his past sins, he was wriggling in a last-ditch effort to restore his ruined foundation. Dragging his obese body, he appeared in Seoul and, to avoid the sound of "daytime outing (晝出)**," he called a car from the O Automobile Department after midnight, when it was dark and quiet, lowered the window curtains, visited the Bureau Chief of Police, had a long, secret discussion about something, and then left. And that day at dawn, he took a southbound train from Yŏngdŭngp'o Station again to avoid the eyes and ears of the public and left.

Readers will also guess that this is of course based on what the unknown man provided. A few days later, the fellow S Daily (S日報) newspaper began to serialize an impressionistic account of meeting Ch'a Ch'ŏnja on the train leaving Yŏngdŭngp'o. In doing so, it proved that the previous C Daily (C日報) article I wrote had some value. <38>
