별건곤 제20호
1929년 04월 01일
어려서 離婚한 夫婦가 戀愛로 다시 結婚 (A Couple Divorced Young Remarry Through Love)
鄭寅翼
문예기타 (Literature, Other)

The train roared toward Seoul. The inside of the car became quiet, and even the lights flickered. It was well past midnight, and we had already passed Taegu a long time ago, so the drowsy feeling of dawn was gradually thickening. The gentry from Kyŏngsang Province, who had been chattering and fussing, began to open their mouths and snore, one by one. Sleep was heavy in the narrow car. Mun-ho couldn't resist the drowsiness either. He dozed off several times in his seat. Because the car was crowded and cramped, Mun-ho felt even more uncomfortable sharing a seat with a young woman. After involuntarily nodding off a few times, Mun-ho slumped toward the young woman next to him. She, too, was drunk with sleep and hadn't fully regained her senses when she was startled awake by Mun-ho's heavy body falling against her. Mun-ho also opened his eyes in surprise and, realizing he had fallen on her, felt shame and embarrassment wash over him, and he felt his face flush. After apologizing, he jumped up like a startled rabbit and ran out into the darkness of the car. The woman, also embarrassed to have been caught dozing, leaned against the car window, which was darkly dyed by the darkness, and cooled her face with the light, chilly dawn breeze. Sleep deepened in the car. The four eyes of the two people twinkled like morning stars in a corner of the gloomy car. In this way, the two men and women felt lonely and sought neighbors in their loneliness, and the conversation began.

Arriving in Seoul, they strolled happily through the clear morning streets. Wrapped in the fresh air, they must have looked fresh and happy to those who saw them. The two parted ways in front of Kwanghwamun. Mun-ho went to his lodging in Ch'ebu-dong, and the woman headed to her house in Samch'ŏng-dong. After that, the sounds of the two young men and women's shoes were often heard in front of Mun-ho's lodging and the woman's house. As these things happened more frequently, rumors of Mun-ho being in love grew louder. Talk of the woman's marriage rose and fell among her peers. After passing through a season from late summer to late autumn, Yi Mun-ho indeed married Pak Ae-gyŏng. As if trying to keep it secret, it quietly passed by. News of Mun-ho's marriage spread when winter was about to pass another peak. Of course, Pak Ae-gyŏng was the woman he had first met in the train car. But the fortune-telling ghost was too much of a troublemaker. It was like a "joker" ghost. This was Mun-ho and Ae-gyŏng's second marriage. They were two people who had divorced because they disliked each other. From marriage to divorce, and then by chance to love, and then back to marriage—it was too absurd a fact. Even if it is said to be an undeniable fact, it is an impossible fact to believe. But the fact is the fact! Then why did these people, who ended up marrying through love, divorce in the first place?

Mun-ho and Ae-gyŏng's families were both prestigious and wealthy. They were precious sons and daughters cherished by their elderly parents. When Mun-ho was twelve and Ae-gyŏng was twelve, the same age, the two families married off their doll-like children. The snot-nosed groom and the crying bride were the laughingstock of both families. They were treated like precious treasures as a source of amusement. The bride, still young, only performed the wedding ceremony and returned to her parents' home. The groom, Mun-ho, lived in P'aju, and the bride, Ae-gyŏng, lived in Yesan. To tell the truth, the husband did not know his wife's face, and the wife did not know her husband's face. The two in-law families were too far apart to send and bring back their precious young children frequently. Until they reached an age where they were truly husband and wife material, they met and parted once a year, like Kyŏn-u (牽牛) and Chik-nyŏ (織女) on the night of Ch'ilsŏk (七夕, the seventh night of the seventh month). Later, Mun-ho left the village school where he had been steeped in love and sought a school in Seoul. A natural talent, Mun-ho completed several training courses and was admitted to Seoul High School, graduating with honors at the age of 20. During this time, Mun-ho became acquainted with magazines and devoured newspapers, opening his eyes to the times. Thus, he first began to question whether he and his family were appropriate existences in this era. Eventually, he raised the flames of opposition to his own absurd toy-like marriage. A wife whose face he couldn't remember, a wife he had met and parted with like lightning 4 or 5 years ago, a snot-nosed playmate he had married at the age of twelve—he insisted that they should divorce first. Of course, this argument did not pass easily, but he was able to steadily prevent and obstruct the marriage registration even after the marriage age had passed. Mun-ho's thirst for knowledge, having finished school in Seoul, drove him to Japan. Mun-ho, who had been driven to Japan, lost all his family fortune when his father ventured into mining before a year had passed, and this led to his death. Mun-ho, who had lost his money and his father, felt his future was too bleak, but he did not give in and studied diligently for 7 or 8 long years through self-help. During this time, his family was scattered, his old mother was left to live with her relatives, and communication with his in-laws was cut off, effectively becoming a divorce. The idea of a wife or divorce had long disappeared from Mun-ho's mind. After finishing his studies, Mun-ho returned to Korea and immersed himself in various movements to live. In the meantime, his body, which had not been cared for for a long time, began to cause trouble, and he was filled with minor illnesses. His friends and comrades, who cherished him, sent him to Tongnae Hot Springs in the middle of summer to take care of himself. Mun-ho, who received their kindness, recovered his health magnificently by the end of the summer, and one evening, he took a train from Pusan to return to work in Seoul. This night was the night he met Ae-gyŏng, whom he had divorced.

Ae-gyŏng, in the eyes of the so-called prestigious families, was like a tomboyish girl. She was a fiery girl who repaid insult with insult and responded to biting and kicking with biting and kicking. Therefore, she received scoldings from her parents several times a day, such as, "You girl from a gentry family" and "Who did that girl take after?" However, she did not fear these scoldings and oppressions from her parents at all and spent her days running around day and night with the dogs she raised, Shindungi and Kŏmdungi, in the front and back yards that opened wide onto the mountain slopes and gardens. As she gradually reached the age of a young woman, she heard that her husband, whom she did not even know, was insisting on a divorce. Ae-gyŏng, who was naturally fiery and unwilling to lose to others, did not mind at all that her husband, whom she did not even know, wanted a divorce; rather, she wished she could get divorced. Her parents, who noticed this, scolded her fiercely, but they could not do anything about their son-in-law and daughter both being like that, so they vaguely moved from Yesan to Seoul. At the same time, swept up in the whirlwind of women's education that had blown into Korea, Ae-gyŏng also opened the iron gate of a girls' school. Ae-gyŏng, who was unwilling to lose anywhere, was determined not to be defeated by her classmates, even if she had to steal and add to her grades if she could not reach them with her own strength, and she continued to work hard on her studies. Ae-gyŏng, who received excellent grades every semester, always wore a lively air on her slightly sullen face, and like a peony in full bloom, she gathered the admiration and praise of many people. In the meantime, time passed. Her elderly parents passed away one after another, and the siblings, still young, continued their schooling with their nanny as their mother, with a small amount of inheritance. One year before Mun-ho finished his studies in Tokyo, she graduated with excellent grades. As another year passed, this family without a pillar was swept away by raging waves like a lone boat. The fields and lands that supported four or five lives were used to fill the bellies of useless relatives, and Ae-gyŏng became increasingly distressed by the difficulties of life. Ae-gyŏng bravely stood up to become the cornerstone of their lives. Thus, she was introduced by a teacher at her alma mater to become a teacher at Chinju Ordinary School, and she even went there to meet the principal. However, she disliked the unpleasant first impression and threw away the job as if it were a worn-out shoe, and the night she headed back to Seoul was the night she met Mun-ho. Ae-gyŏng was Ae-gyŏng to the end. Even if the principal's first impression was somewhat unpleasant, if she had thought of the current family situation and taken the job, the revival of her relationship with Mun-ho would not have happened. These two, who had divorced because they disliked each other, will continue to build their love nest in the heart of the capital city, smiling with happiness yesterday, today, and tomorrow as well.

어려서 離婚한 夫婦가 戀愛로 다시 結婚
						鄭寅翼
서울을 향하야 기차는 우렁차게 달렷슴니다. 車室안이 조용하야지면서부터 불까지 껌벅이엇슴니다. 밤은 子正이 훨신 넘은 뒤이며 길은 大邱를 지난지 이미 오랜 뒤이엇슴으로 새벽의 困한 긔분이 차차 지터가는 것이엇슴니다. 떠들고 수선피던 慶尙道 량반들도 하나 둘식 입을 버리고 코를 골기 비릇하엿슴니다. 좁은 車室에는 잠이 그둑히 찻슴니다. 文浩도 조름이 오는 것을 막어낼 힘이 업섯슴니다. 자리에 안진채 멧 차례 꾸벅꾸벅 조랏슴니다. 원톄 손이 만히 타서 비좁은 車間에 더구나 文浩는 젊은 女性과 자리를 가티 하게 되여 한층 불편을 늣기엇슴니다. 멧번째 不可抗力的으로 꾸벅어리던 文浩는 엽흐로 젊은 여성을 향하야 쓰러젓슴니다. 그 역시 잠에 醉하야 정신을 못 차리든 차에 무거운 文浩의 몸이 실임을 깨닷자 깜짝 놀나 눈을 떳슴니다. 文浩도 깜짝 눈을 뜨고 자지가 그에게 쓰러진 것을 깨닷자 북그러움과 미안함이 일시에 온몸을 싸고 도라 얼골이 붉어짐을 늣겻슴니다. 그리고 잘못되엿다는 인사를 한 후에 쪼끼는 톡기 모양으로 자리를 이러 어둠에 싸힌 車室 밧그로 뛰여 나갓슴니다. 그 여성도 졸다 들킨 것이 북그러웟던지 어둠에 까마케 물든 車窓을 치밀고 새벽 가볍고 싸늘한 바람에 그 얼골을 시치고 잇섯슴니다. 車室의 잠은 작고 깁허갓습니다. 두 사람의 네 개의 눈은 음침한 차실 한 구석에서 샛별과 가티 반작이엇슴니다. 이리하야 두 남녀는 외로움을 늣기고 그 외로움에서 이웃을 찻게 되여 交涉이 비릇하엿슴니다.
