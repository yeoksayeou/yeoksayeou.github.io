白晝 公行 賭博場인 공굴니는 집

Sunbal

An unexpected sudden mobilization order! The gist of the order was to capture and write about a fleeting glimpse of the bustling Gyeongseong (大京城) from 2:30 PM to 3:30 PM. Where should I go and what kind of article should I cover? With anxious thoughts, I left the company with Mr. S. "Where are you going?" I asked Mr. S about his direction. "Well, I thought I'd go where they're recruiting foreign affairs personnel (外交員募集)," he said, also with a dubious expression. Passing through Gyodong (校洞), we headed towards Jongno (鐘路) Street. Crowds of people coming out in the spring!! Modern girls (모던걸) and modern boys (모던보이) swished from the other side towards us. Trams and cars rushed by like a rainstorm. Wondering what kind of article I could get here, Mr. S and I parted ways in front of Jongmyo (宗廟), and I went down a bit further and slipped into a Gong Gulleo (공굴니) house.

The surface signboard advertised it as an entertaining game center (娛樂的 遊戱場), but in reality, it was a place like a blatant gambling den. "Come in, come in. An entertainment anyone can enjoy," a pale-faced fellow, like someone with chronic lung disease, shouted, while playing a gramophone to attract passersby. The time was 2:47! The two-room shop front, not very spacious, was packed with people. Newcomers who had just come from the countryside, laborers, bunglers, vagrants, and unemployed folks were standing and rolling the balls. Station names such as Gyeongseong, Daegu (大邱), Pyongyang (平壤)... eight in total, were written, and if the ball entered the station they had designated, they would win six times the amount they bet. Two jeon (전) was fine, ten jeon was fine, it was a game where you could play any amount <132> (always called Gong Gulleogi (공굴니기)). A clueless, tall 18 or 19-year-old rural youth and a slovenly-looking, perhaps parasitic unproductive consumer (不生産消費者) were rolling the balls. "Wonsan (元山)!" "Daegu!" "Pyongyang!" they shouted, betting 10 jeon, 20 jeon, 50 jeon, hoping for a lucky windfall. They lost, they won. In the end, the owner took it all. "Aish, lost again," said the slovenly fellow, walking away. In the 15 minutes I watched, he seemed to have lost 80 or 90 jeon. A little later, someone who looked like a store clerk got off a bicycle, hesitated for a while, then took out 10 jeon to buy a ticket, played for quite a while, and lost. Then another 10 jeon, 20 jeon, and eventually, he lost too. The rural youth, who had been playing since earlier, also seemed to be losing. On one side, the record player was still playing very smoothly, "Play, play, play while you're young, once you die..." People were standing in the cramped space, rubbing against each other, and looking at the owner with a demeanor several times more indignant than the losers. The rural youth, who had lost money for a long time, said, "Hey, I'm going to stop now. Give me 50 jeon." "Don't say unnecessary things. Bet again and win." "I lost all 5 won, so won't you give me 50 jeon?" "Give me 30 jeon to buy lunch, then." His eyes were definitely bloodshot with tears. "No," he said firmly, with an attitude that seemed well-versed in the business. As the conversation went back and forth, the atmosphere was tense for a while, but he had no choice but to give up and walk away. I followed him and asked, "Hey, where's your hometown?" He looked at me strangely and asked, "Why do you ask?" When I didn't answer, he said, "Seoncheon (宣川)." "What did you come here for?" "I came to Seoul for the first time to take the entrance exam, but it turned out like this," he said, with an expression that seemed to ask for sympathy. "I'm not directly involved, but don't do such things in the future." "Well, I was just watching, but they kept urging me, so I did it," he continued, giving a somewhat apologetic answer, "I won about 2 won at first." "You should have stopped then." "How could I with greed like that?" he said, looking regretful. He headed towards Changgyeongwon (昌慶苑), and I headed towards Honmachi (本町), thinking to myself, "If you adopt a thoughtless, effortless attitude, you get such results."

Time was passing quickly, but there were no articles or materials to be found. Looking at my watch, I was about to give up and return to the company when I saw the rural youth who had regretted and left earlier standing at the Gong Gulleo house again.

四柱 古談꾼 集團所 塔洞公園

Gyeonghwa

From 2 PM to 3 PM is when human society is at its busiest. As people scattered in various directions, I headed to Tapgol Park (塔洞公園). There, nearly two hundred people were milling about. The atmosphere inside the park was very depressed and chaotic, like maggots playing in dung water in May or June. Old men wearing hats and white clothes, children in colorful clothes, middle school students, laborers, beggars, porters, "Motji" peddlers, Western-dressed men, Japanese friends (왜 친구), and people of various classes were sitting or standing in every corner, chattering as if they had months to spare. On the stone railing of the octagonal pavilion (八角亭), there were two or three people taking naps, people sitting and chatting <133>, and people wandering around aimlessly. Most of them were gathered around fortune tellers (사주쟁이) under the old pavilion. Near the greenhouse, there were 60 or 70 people gathered. I saw an old man sitting down, wearing thick glasses askew, and reading "Hwarongdo Silgi (華容道 實記)" aloud. Old men with dim eyes were listening intently, and a group of unemployed people were sitting nearby listening as well. "Gongmyeong (孔明) barely lived past fifty, so he died very young." "Jangbi (張飛) also died an unnatural death early." "Fifty is not bad." They were talking about the stories of the Three Kingdoms period thousands of years ago, as if they were detached from reality and had forgotten about life. Although it was a high-speed urban life, this place felt like another world. As I went out the back gate, the 13-story stone pagoda had its third story broken off. A fortune teller was sitting under it. A peanut vendor was getting his fortune told. "I got married at 19, but my wife ran away after a few months. How can I find her?" "Go southwest." The people standing around said, "Then it must be in the direction of Incheon (仁川)." "Around April or May, your wife will either come back or you will marry another woman." He was just making up plausible stories with good words. A few coins, saved one by one, came out of his pocket. There were seven or eight Tang book (唐冊) readers in this small park. I saw that he was a rural youth barely over 20. He sees 40 or 50 people a day, so it's amazing. A middle school student, passing by, said, "When will I get married?" "There is also a way to achieve immortality." The city is full of superstition. The day when the practice of openly sitting in broad daylight and fleecing fools will disappear is still far away.

Near the main gate, two middle-aged women with powdered faces were smoking cigarettes, their eyes meaningfully scanning the people coming and going, which was not a good sign. A student who seemed to have just come from the countryside was looking at the Daewongaksa Stele (大圓覺寺碑), while a young man wearing a 눌러쓴 "dorijjeuchi" (도리우찌) that looked like a vagrant was glancing at her furtively. Could this be a sign of trouble? At the restaurant "Victory (勝利)," a "jojju" (죠쮸) and a servant (下人) were having a fun time bickering. A friend coming out of the bathroom said, "There are so many people. There are only two toilets, but there are six or seven people who need to poop." A porter was playing nijjaebi (니잽이) by the rotten-smelling pond. What are these idle people seeking here? Tapgol Park, which lacks any facilities for rest, entertainment, or exercise. Perhaps it is a sinner for being near Jongno. I thought it would be better to get rid of it or, if it were to be kept, to make it more like a park so that people could at least get some fresh air. Anyway, during the hour I spent wandering around the park, I was so distracted that I forgot about the world. What amazing things could happen in this ordinary world? The park gate made of iron bars was open, but were they unable to leave or too weak to leave? We are like pitiful animals confined within, dying! Even though there was a time when we shouted loudly and pushed our way out!

怪常한 取引

Pureunso

Two PM!
Jongno Street in Seoul is bustling. Moreover, because it's spring, people are itching to get out and about, so the street is even more crowded with women, old men, young ladies, and husbands. The spring breeze is blowing in Seoul <134>, Seoul Station. Gentlemen rushing in to the point where the ticket gate is about to collapse. Jongno Street is even more hectic than ever.

People going! People coming! In this whirlwind of eyeballs. In front of XX Seorim (XX 書林), under the magazine advertisement stand, a man with a low-cut hat is tirelessly hawking, with a resting voice, "30 jeon each! 30 jeon! 30 jeon for a fountain pen!" He has a book bag, and he is spreading out the fountain pens on top of it, shuffling them back and forth, waiting for someone to take a look.
The reporter, trying to find a story, weaves through the crowd and stops in front of this man. Just then, a gentleman around 45 or 6, wearing a golden suit and a cap, also stops. That's when it happens. The fountain pen seller pretends not to notice and is cleaning the fountain pens. A young man, around 30, wearing a white durumagi (두루맥이) and a cap, looking like he's from the countryside, comes to the fountain pen seller and says, "Give me just 10 jeon more. Please," and puts down a pair of glasses. The fountain pen seller doesn't even look at him and says, "No. I'll give you 2 won 50 jeon if you're selling," and continues to clean the fountain pens.
The reporter thinks, "Aha! This could be a story!" The man in the white durumagi says:
"Listen to this! I'm from Daejeon (大田) in Chungcheongdo (忠淸道), and I was going to find my brother in Wonsan (元山), but I ran out of travel money. I don't know what to do in this unfamiliar place, so I'm selling these glasses. But I'm only asking for 2 won 50 jeon! I bought these last fall for 21 won 80 jeon. The lenses are real stone. Take a look at them," he says, appealing to the gentleman in the golden suit and me, as if seeking sympathy, and holds out the glasses.
The fountain pen seller ignores him and just cleans the fountain pens. The gentleman in the golden suit takes the glasses and starts inspecting them, saying, "Stone lenses!" He puts them up to his eyes, licks the lenses, and reflects light on them. "They're stone lenses," he says and inspects them again.
The man in the white durumagi says, "Yes! Yes! That's right! I bought them at Wega (위 가가) last August for 21 won 80 jeon. The frame is nothing, it's the lenses. I ran out of travel money..."
He babbles, half begging and half explaining. The gentleman in the golden suit asks skeptically, "How do I know they're stone lenses?"
"Well, how would I know, but I paid 21 won 80 jeon for them. But this man (the fountain pen seller) is only offering 2 won 50 jeon. But what can I do? I don't know anyone here, and I ran out of travel money... if you could just give me a few more jeon, I'd sell them, even though it's unfair."
After a while, the gentleman in the golden suit says, "2 won 50 jeon! You're selling them for 2 won 50 jeon!" and gives the glasses back to the man in the white durumagi. The man in the white durumagi follows him, saying,
"Hey! Excuse me. Just add 10 jeon more."
"Forget it."
The man in the white durumagi hesitates for a while, then says,
"Okay! 2 won 50 jeon! Take it! Okay."
And so, a strange transaction is concluded. The gentleman in the golden suit hesitates and then takes out 2 won 50 jeon in cash and gives it to him. The reporter is curious. So, he asks the man who bought the glasses, "Excuse me, did you know what you were doing when you bought the glasses?"
He replies unexpectedly, "No." <135>
While they were talking, the fountain pen seller and the glasses seller disappeared. They are people who habitually do this kind of thing with 30-40 won glasses. Jongno Street in Seoul at 2 PM on March 29th! A bustling street! Old people, young people, children, city people, country people, women, men, all mixed together in a chaotic commotion, and yet, such leisurely transactions are taking place.

裁判所의 1時間 百萬長者 며누리의 法廷出入

Cheongoesaeng

The mobilization order came down at 2 PM, and it was already 2:30 PM when I finally set out. Moreover, since the area I was assigned to was the courthouse (裁判所), I anticipated that I might fail if I arrived during recess (閑廷時間), so I ran as fast as I could. However, since the company and the courthouse are quite far apart, it took me 20 minutes no matter how fast I ran. I rushed into the courthouse at 2:50 PM, and the lawyers were already scattering. Mr. Yi Han-gil (李漢吉), walking with a back bent like an old pine tree (長松), was the first to be seen. Behind Mr. Yi, a man in a white durumagi (두루맥이) was following him like a magpie (鵲) chasing a kite (鳶), shaking his hands and shouting, "So, that guy did that to me? How can a guy like that say such things..." I thought he was arguing with Mr. Yi, but upon closer listening, it seemed that someone had come as a witness and made unfavorable statements against him, which was why he was so angry. And then, Mr. Jeong Gu-yeong (鄭求瑛) in a mouse-colored Inverness coat (鼠色 임바네스) came out, and then Mr. Choe Jin (崔鎭), with snow-white hair fluttering in the air. I thought to myself, "Ah, a failure, a failure. I'm late, so it's a failure," and went straight to the courthouse side entrance. I looked into the cafeteria, but there was no one there. I looked around the bailiff's office (執達吏 事務所) and the general complaint office (一般 控所), but there was nothing of interest. I immediately went up to the second floor and went to the lawyers' office (변호사 공소). The place, which was usually bustling with friends wearing beoseon (버선, Korean socks), was deserted like a marketplace after the performers had left. Only three foreign lawyers (外入쟁이 賣法師) and a hairy policeman (털보 순사) in charge of the detention center (유치장 擔任) were sitting face to face, playing Go (바둑). Seeing the policeman in charge of the detention center relaxing, I could tell that there was nothing happening in the criminal court either. But anyway, since I was already here, I decided to check all the courtrooms and went down from the second floor again to the back garden (後庭), checking both the civil court (민사법정) and the criminal court (형사법정), but there were no cases, and only the court officers (廷丁) were there, guarding the place.

I asked the court officer if there were any courtrooms that were in session, and he replied, "It's already past 3 PM, so what cases would there be..." I had no choice but to try one last thing: I went to the vicinity of the courthouse detention center, hoping there might be something there. I saw two policemen standing side by side in a sunny spot, talking. "Aha, this is it." I slowly approached them, hoping to overhear their conversation. Standing at a corner of the detention center, as if waiting for someone, they were just talking without any signs of awareness. In their conversation, such a story briefly circulated:
(Policeman A) "Hmph, in the old days, if you were a nobleman and had a lot of money, it didn't matter what crime you committed <136>, but in today's world, that's all useless... Recently, the daughter-in-law of some aristocratic tycoon was summoned as a witness twice for her relative's fraud (詐欺取財) case, but she didn't appear, so they even arrested her and brought her to court, where they publicly humiliated her..."
(Policeman B) "What aristocratic tycoon are you talking about?"
(Policeman A) "Ah, weren't you there that day? It was about 20 years ago. Back then, Mr. O, who was shaking the entire Joseon (朝鮮) and is still the richest man in Joseon, had his daughter-in-law's nephew arrested for fraud, and his daughter-in-law was summoned as a witness. But because money was still more powerful than the law, she didn't appear after being summoned twice, and the judge had to go to her house to ask her questions. The daughter-in-law must have taken that as an example, because she didn't respond to the summons from the courthouse two or three times, so they had no choice but to arrest her and humiliate her..."
I heard that and thought, "Aha, good, let me listen more closely," but the policeman stopped talking and left... Later, I found out that their story was actually true, and the woman in question was Ms. Maeng (孟氏), the wife of a high-ranking official at a certain bank (某 은행). This courthouse exploration failed in terms of what I could see, due to the time constraints, but this is what I gathered through hearing. I'm briefly recording this to fulfill my duty (塞責). The time is 3:40 PM.

撞球場으로 勞働의 거리로

Ddodoli

"Aren't you going to eat lunch! Let's eat quickly." It was something I had never heard before, coming from the editor-in-chief! People can live a long time and still see new things! I thought, "What's gotten into the chief editor today that he's offering to treat us to lunch!" I was quite happy because I was hungry! But I was suspicious, so I asked again. "Why are you doing this? Are you going to treat us?" But alas, the world is fleeting, and I was wrong. "We will be exploring for one hour from 2:30 PM to 3:30 PM, so eat quickly within 30 minutes!"
I knew it! He wouldn't be urging us to eat if he wasn't up to something. Editor-in-chief! Ring-a-ling! One bowl of seolleongtang (설렁탕, ox bone soup)! He seemed like he might treat us to a bowl if we were lucky, but the fact that it was just one bowl made me lose all hope! The chief editor quickly ate his 10-jeon bowl as if he were hiding it and said, "So-and-so go there and so-and-so go there! It's two o'clock, so you need to leave quickly!" I became one of the so-and-sos and had to go to the billiard hall (撞球場) and the labor village (勞働村). Billiard hall? It's located after leaving Tapgol Park! There was no other way to go!
Special Envoy C was standing there watching people read fortunes and tell fortunes, as if Tapgol Park was his territory.
I left the park's main gate and went up to the upper floor of the Dong-A Women's Merchant Association (東亞 婦人商會). Ddodrak- huda-sseu! Isseu-sseu! I felt funny when I heard these sounds. The equipment was made by Westerners, but Koreans were playing on it, and they were counting in Japanese, so does that mean that Japanese is easier to count in than Korean? Is the billiard hall a cosmopolitan club (코스모 포리탄의 俱樂部)? For some reason, it felt funny and sad at the same time! <137>
"Welcome!" In the middle of huda-sseu, hufsseu, if a guest came in, the greeting from the receptionist was like a record player, with no expression or movement!
When I entered, the round clock directly in front of me said 2:25.
I immediately sat down on a springy long chair, as if I was going to stake tens of jeon. On the two tables, two or three people were holding cues (몽동이) and taking turns hitting. There weren't many customers, only eight people - excluding me - present. A couple of them were wearing winter suits like me and had black beards, looking like they were over 30. And then there were the so-called modern boys (모던 뽀이), with their hats folded roundly and their pants legs wide. Seeing them at the theater in the evening and on the streets during the day, it seemed like they didn't have any specific jobs. Of course, judging by the time, they were leisurely playing billiards at 2:30 PM, which gave a general idea. They were wearing custom-made suits and pretending to be sons of wealthy families (豪家 子弟 然), lavishly and leisurely playing in these high-class game centers (高等 遊戱場(?)), and I wondered if they knew whether their families had to worry about rice and firewood. Among them was someone named C, who was my elementary school classmate, but he was pretending not to know me. I had heard that he was going to study abroad somewhere, and I wondered if he had graduated and come back or if he was here on vacation. He was enthusiastically rolling the balls, only caring about covering himself with silk from head to toe.

One person was holding a cue and talking on the phone for a very long time. I overheard him saying:
"Did many calls come for me? They're coming at five? How much are they bringing? 350 won? Then I'll be here, so call me here..."
This gentleman was Mr. K, the owner of the 00 Dye Manufacturing Plant (00상회) downstairs, a high-class young man. I wasn't particularly close to him, but I just knew him.
K hung up the phone, discovered that I was sitting there, stretched out his hand and said, "It's been a long time! I have to come to a place like this to see you!" He greeted me in a very welcoming manner.
"Are you doing well these days! You must have been out playing a lot because of spring! It's such a nice day today, why don't you take a lady out and play instead of playing billiards?" I asked casually.
"Um! I'm waiting for someone."
"You look good today!"
"Why?"
"You're getting 350 won, so let's have a treat."
"It's money from business, but if you want to eat, let's eat!"
And then he stopped talking. On the other side of the table, modern young men were gathered, each holding a stick and taking turns hitting. Among them was the gentleman in the dark suit from earlier, but he didn't seem to know the modern group, so he sat alone on one side, and his face changed with every huda-sseu! yosseu! Perhaps he was worried about the money or didn't have enough in his pocket. Let's take a look at the modern young men! The gentleman in the dark suit was hitting while thinking about the money he was losing, but these gentlemen were just playing for fun, casually throwing away 8 jeon each time they hit. Listening to these friends, they were saying that so-and-so was supposed to come by two o'clock, but since he wasn't coming, they needed to meet that person and have a treat, so it seemed that the people who came to play billiards during the day were either waiting for time to pass or had nowhere else to go.

This gentleman in the suit - the other gentleman in the suit was playing over there - must have been very concerned about money.

Young friends have nothing to do during the day, so they are tied to the billiard hall, playing a 1-jeon game, which is worrisome. These people are not people who have become unemployed because of the job shortage, but rather people who have been faltering from the beginning. Ikke, why is that gentleman coming in so angry? Olchi! This person is the son of the principal of a private school and is probably back from studying in Japan for vacation. I don't know if he learned this high-class entertainment method when he was in Joseon, but there are very few people who have studied in Japan who can't play billiards. I don't know if that person didn't see me or was pretending not to see me, and since there was no need to acknowledge him, I also pretended not to see him and sat down!

Ddodrak! Nana-sseu! For some reason, there were only two people here. The tram was full of young ladies and masters who had come from the countryside for the first time to enter school, and it was also full of middle-aged men and women who were called by the spring breeze. The conductor was even busier because they were asking where to get off to go to certain places.
I got off at Bongnaegyo (蓬萊僑). It seemed like all the beggars who were sitting in the sun and dying of starvation and the people who had come from the countryside to do manual labor but couldn't do anything and were wandering around had gathered here.
The traffic police came and chased them away like a herd of pigs. But they had nowhere to go. It's a great contrast between the vagrants who spend their days at the billiard hall because they have nothing to do and the people who have nothing to do and spend their days soaking in the train smoke on the bridge.
On the roadside, there were piles of rubber shoes that had washed up during the flood, and they were sitting and shouting that they were cheap. Everyone seemed busy. The wheelbarrow was loaded with a mountain of luggage, and they were pulling it, sweating profusely. The money that they wasted at the billiard hall was six times the amount of money that they earned by sweating. People's lives are so stratified! There were many people wearing clothes made for the army and carrying a jiggeh (지게, Korean traditional carrying frame) on their backs, but since they didn't see anyone calling them, how would they feed their wives and children! Now that I'm starting to notice, I'll be more careful and see if I can improve their lives even a little bit. As I looked, I was able to understand the surface of their lives. As I was doing this, I arrived in front of the Bongnaejeong police substation (蓬萊町 派出所). When I quickly looked at the clock, it was already 3:35! The time was up, but I didn't have any results in the end.

白晝 公行 賭博場인 공굴니는 집

孫發

뜻하지 안헛든 불시의 동원령! 두시 삼십분으로부터  세시 삼십분까지 대경성의 움즉어리는 한 국면의 片影을 잡아 쓰라는 것이 명령의 요점이다. 어듸를 가서 어떠한 기사를 취급해야 조흘까? 나는 한 불안한 생각을 가지고 S군과 가티 社門을 나섯다. 『당신은 어듸를 가시료.』 하면서 S군에게 방향을 물엇다. 『글세 나는 外交員募集 하는데나 가볼까』 하면서 역시 의아한 태도다. 校洞 어구를 지나 鐘路거리로 나섯다. 봄쳘에 움직이여 나오는 사람의 떼들!! 모던걸 모던보이들이 저쪽에서 쓱삭이며 이 편을 향해온다. 전차 자동차가 풍우가티 몰녀간다. 여긔서 무슨 기사를 어들까 하면서 S군과 宗廟압헤서 길을 나누고 좀더 내러가다가 공 굴니는집으로 나는 쑥 들어갓다.
표면 간판에는 娛樂的 遊戱場이라고 내세웟지만 그 실은 공공연한 도박장 가튼 곳이다. 「들어오시오. 들어오시오. 누구든지 할 수 잇는 오락이요 하면서 慢性肺病에 걸인놈처럼 얼골이 놀안 작자가 소리를 치는 한 편에는 축음기를 틀어 놋코 오고가는 사람을 모아 들인다. 시간은 2시 47분! 넓두 안흔 두間 店頭에는 사람의 때가 꽉 찻다. 시골서 갓올나온 샛님, 노동자 얼치기 부랑자 遊民級 한때가 서서 알을 굴닌다. 정차장 역명을 京城, 大邱, 平壤... 여덜을 써놋코 공을 굴녀서 자기가 지정한 역처로 말이 들어가면 태워논 액수에 6배를 먹는 것이다. 2전도 좃코 10전도 좃코 얼마든지 놀 수 잇는 놀음<132>(言必稱 공굴니기)이다. 멋업시 키가 커달안 18,9세의 농촌 청년과 꼴사납게 생긴 아마도 놀며 지내는 일종의 不生産消費者 가튼 두사람이 공을 굴닌다. 『元山이요.』 『大邱요.』 『平壤이요.』 하면서 일확천금의 요행을 낙그려고 10전, 20전, 50전 작고 댄다. 일헛다 먹엇다. 결국은 주인이 다 먹고 만다. ‘에횟 또 일엇써’하면서 꼴사납은 양반은 나슨다. 아마 나 보게만 15분 사이에 80전인지 90전인지 일코 돌아스는 모양이다. 조금 잇다가 어떤 상점에 점원가튼 사람이 자전차에서 나리더니 한참 주저주저 하다가 10전을 내서 표를 사가지고 꽤 한참 하다가 일헛다. 또 10전, 20전 그러다가 끗끗내 그도 일엇고 아까부터 계속하든 농촌청년도 작고 일는 모양이다. 한편에선 여전히 「노자 노자 젊어 노자 인생 한 번 죽어지면...」 하고 레코-드 소리가 매우 流暢하게 울어 나온다. 사람들은 좁은데 서서 서로 비비고 떠들며 돈 일흔 當者보다도 멧 10배 더 忿怒한 기색으로 주인을 놀이고 본다. 한참동안 돈을 일튼 농촌청년은 미천이 다 달엿는지 「요보 인젠 고만하겟소. 돈 50전만 주시요.」 「공연히 딴 말슴을 하시는구려. 어서 한번 더 대고 따시오.」 「돈 5원을 다 일헛는데 그래 50전을 안주어.」 「점심 사먹게 그럼 30전만 주시요.」 그의 눈초리에는 확실히 눈물석긴 핏발이 섯다. 「안돼요.」 하면서 강경하게 거절하는 품이 그 영업에 꽤 달년바든 태도로 나온다. 말이 오거니 가거니하는 가운데 잠시동안은 공기가 험악햇지만 그는 할수업시 낙망하고 돌아서 갓다. 나는 그 뒤를 딸아가면서 「여보 고향이 어듸시요.」 하니까 이상하다는 듯이 「그건 웨 그러우」 하다가 내가 대답을 안이하니까 「宣川 이어요.」 「무엇하러 오섯소.」 「네 입학시험 보라 처음 서울 왓다가 이럿케 되엿세요.」 하면서 맛치 동정하라는 빗치 떠돈다. 「내가 직접 관계는 업지만 그런 것을 이다음은 하지 마시요.」 「글세 나두 구경만 하는데 작구 권하니까 햇지요.」 하면서 변명 비스름이 대답을 계속해서 「첫번엔 한 2원 땃섯서요.」 한다. 「그때 고만 두지요.」 「어듸 욕심이 그래요.」 하면서 후회하는 빗을 짓고 그는 창경원 쪽으로 나는 本町으로 가면서 혼자 「철업시 노력업는 태도를 어드려면 저런 결과를 엇는다.」하고 걸어갓다.
시간은 작고 가는데 기사도 재료도 아모 것도 생기질 안는다. 시계를 발아 보면서 오죽 실패를 불으짓고 社로 오다가 아까 후회하고 돌아간 농촌청년이 또다시 공굴니는 집에 서잇는 것을 보앗다.

정문 갓가운데는 분바른 중년게집 두엇이 담배를 픠고 안저서 출입하는 사람을 뜻잇시 흘터보는 품이 심상치 안흔 눈치엿다. 시골서 갓올나 온듯한 여학생이 大圓覺寺碑를 구경하는데 「도리우찌」를 눌러 쓴 부랑자가태 뵈는 청년이 흘금흘금 눈짓을 하는 것도 탈날 징조가 안일가. 요리점 「勝利」에 「죠쮸」와 下人은 자미잇게  갈감질을 한다. 변소갓다 나오는 친구 「웬 놈이 그리 만하 똥간은 둘인데 똥눌 놈은 댓씩 여슷씩 되니.」 죽탕물고에 썩은 냄새가 나는 연못가에는 지게꾼이 니잽이를 한다.  할 일 업는 사람들 그들은 무엇을 구해 이리로 오느냐 휴식 오락 운동 아모런 공원으로서의 설비를 갓지 못한 탑골공원. 아마도 鐘路 갓가히 잇는 죄인가 보다. 차라리 업새버리기나 하엿스면 이왕 둘 바에는 좀 더 공원다웁게 그래도 바람이라도 잘 쏘이도록 꾸며 노앗스면 하는 생각이 낫다. 엇잿든 공원을 빙빙 도는 한 시간동안 나도 세상을 이저버릴 만큼 정신이 업섯다. 平平凡凡한 세상에 무슨 신통한 일이 잇스랴. 쇠창살로 만든 공원 문은 그냥 열린 채 잇건만 나올 일이 업는가 기력이 업는가 다 죽어가는 동물들처럼 가처 잇는 불상한 우리들아! 그래도 지나간 한때는 큰소리 웨치며 밀켜나온 일이 잇섯건만!
