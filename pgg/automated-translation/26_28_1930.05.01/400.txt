발행일	1930년 05월 01일
기사제목	歸鄕(上) (Returning Home (Part 1))
필자	兪鎭午 (Yu Chin-o)
기사형태	소설 (Fiction)

Returning Home
						Yu Chin-o

1.

Six years ago.
The ground shook recklessly, and houses fell haphazardly. At night, flames piercing the sky seemed to end the world, engulfing a great city of two million people. Human life was cheaper than a penny or a rubber balloon. The gleam of steel and the holes of ominous machines. In the darkness, my mother was a lamp. Unexpected gunshots. From the wall of a certain count's mansion, a young man with a hunting rifle would look up at the window of a room on the second floor of that house, facing the street, every morning, clicking his tongue and pacing back and forth. In the room slept the son of an M aristocrat who had crossed the long sea.

The old woman, bought for a "salary" of thirty won, was the grandmother of our three lives. The old woman sold provisions and prepared side dishes for us. But our dreams were still unsettled. Sometimes, in the middle of the night, someone would shake the front door. Pushing the old woman ahead of us, trembling, we would see the ominous steel hole pointed at the center of our red chests. We begged with our hands and feet. Whatever he was thinking, the masked man threw a loud laugh and disappeared into the darkness.
We miraculously survived. But the frightened old woman abandoned us the next dawn, rejecting our pleas, throwing down even the money she had received, and left to save her own life, going somewhere. We were left again, like people who had met a shipwreck of helpless thieves, vainly hoping for a helping hand.

2.

The threat seemed to subside with time. Like the lucky people in old stories, we barely managed to get over several precarious situations and finally breathed a sigh of relief. But I finally found myself in a position where I could no longer stay in Tokyo. I was not deeply involved in the incident, but because I was not deeply involved, I was more likely to be treated unfairly. The situation became critical by the minute. The aftershocks of the earthquake were still severe, so I could not quietly return to my homeland. Helpless, one night I sought out my comrade Asano, who was still hiding somewhere in "Asakusa".

After hearing the situation, he wrote me a letter of introduction.
...My classmate Esima is going to take a rest for about a month due to illness, so please treat him well...
"Mr. Kim, when you go to the countryside, you must act as 'Esima'. You must never let them know that you are Kim."
"Esima, heh heh heh, don't worry. Thank you."
In fact, I was not only confident in my Japanese conversation skills, but my slightly stocky features made it even more likely that I could pass as "Esima."
Asano said that the people in his hometown were ordinary landowners with no special qualities, and their personalities were mostly "good farmers," so if I acted as "Esima" until the end, I would be safe.
"Okay, Esima-kun. Why don't you answer when I call you 'Esima'? You are Esima. Don't forget it even in your dreams."
"Don't worry. Mesima, Esima."
The next night, I packed a few volumes of Sōseki's complete works into a small bag, carried a gift in one hand, and left "Ueno Station." Anyone who saw my pale appearance at that time would have recognized me as someone going away for convalescence. At 10:30 PM, the train began to rush north toward Tochigi Prefecture. I quietly sighed and took out "Kodan Club". <134>

3.

A little after 3 AM, I got off the train. First of all, there was nothing I could do but go to the inn in front of the station. I dozed off for a while and woke up at eight o'clock. S Village is about ten li from here. Then there is no need to rush so much. I left the inn around ten o'clock and walked along the winding road into the mountains. The mountains on both sides were covered with fir trees so dark that they seemed to be in darkness, and the rice had already begun to turn yellow. S Village was a small village nestled on a low mountain with a small valley. It looked like a truly "peaceful farming village."
Asano's house was in a sunny spot facing away from the mountain. As one of the few remaining landowners in the village, the thatched roof was covered with plaster here and there, and the black walls of the earthen warehouse (土藏) were broken in places, revealing the mud.
As I stood there awkwardly with my bag, an old woman holding a water dipper (釣甁) at the well in the yard came out to meet me, showing a happy expression, saying,
"You came from Tokyo."
It was unexpected. <135>
"Yes, that's right. But—"
"Then you must be Esima-ssi."
I was even more surprised.
"That's right. But how did you know I was Esima?"
Before I could finish speaking, the woman led me to the back of the house, muttering to herself.
"Oh, but there must be a clean room. I cleaned up the detached room (뒤ㅅ방; hanare), but it's so dilapidated, hohohoho."
I had no choice but to follow her. Behind the house was indeed a separate building far from the main house. The tatami mats were all worn out, and the paper doors were full of holes, but it had been cleaned neatly.
I couldn't help but be grateful.
"Thank you. For doing all this for me. But how did you know I was coming?"
"Adamadayo. My son sent a letter the other night. He said you went to the same school as my son. How is my son? Thank you. My son wrote in his letter that he received a lot of favors from Esima-ssi in Tokyo. Even if you talk about talent, you need to be rich enough to pay for tuition."
Judging from her words, she was Asano's mother. But she didn't seem to know that "Asano" had already quit school last spring.
"Not at all. What power do I have?"
Asano's mother busily went to the main house (원채; omoya) and brought Asano's father. He was an old man who looked a little over fifty and seemed very stubborn. But he also repeatedly thanked me. In reality, I had never given Asano any material assistance in Tokyo. But "Asano" must have written the letter with that intention.
I presented the gift I had brought—a box of sweets. I had put thirty won in the box so he could use it for meals.

4.
From the detached room (hanare), I could see the main road leading to the station.
-Whenever something happens, people will come rushing down that road-
But first of all, I was tired. As I stretched out my legs and lay down, the weariness that had been weighing on my chest for so long relaxed, and a sweet sleep crept over me.
I don't know how long I slept, but the remaining sleep lingered faintly. I heard someone whispering outside the door. My nerves froze again.
"-I don't want to. Sister, come in with me."
"I don't want to. I'll be back later. You go in first and say hello."
"I don't want to. Come in with me."
I laughed anew at my cowardice. To be so agitated by just a little bit of conversation... <136>
After a long time of arguing outside,
"Then I'll go in with you. Go in first."
That was the voice of a girl, "sister". I jumped up. Evening sunlight was pouring through the paper door.
"Excuse me."
I heard a humble voice that sounded very rural. I told them to come in and opened the paper door. Instantly, the girl who had been standing with the boy giggled and ran towards the main house (omoya). From her appearance, she looked like a female student of about seventeen.
The boy, embarrassed by his sister, hesitated and entered the room. When I asked, he said he was Asano's younger brother, a fourteen-year-old student in the first year of middle school this year. At first, he didn't talk much, but I could tell that he had an unspeakable liking for me. While we were taking out the snacks I had brought and talking, the boy gradually became eloquent, and I realized that he had infinite goodwill towards me because I was a classmate of his respected older brother from Tokyo. As we were talking, I asked.
"What school does your sister go to?"
"It's a girls' school. She's in the fourth year."
"Where is the school?"
"It's in front of the station."
"In front of the station? It's far, so how does she go?"
"She goes by bicycle."
"Bicycle? Your sister too?"
"Of course. Why?"
"No, I was just wondering."
After eating dinner that night (with Asano's younger brother), Asano's mother came to the detached room (hanare). After hesitating for a moment, she took out the envelope of money I had put in the box of sweets from her waist and said,
"What is all this? Not at all. Especially because my husband wouldn't accept something like this. Even last night, after seeing my son's letter, he was saying to himself that he was sorry. We don't have anything to offer, but you can't do this."
I tried to give it back, but she refused stubbornly. But in the meantime, I could guess that her refusal was not from her heart, but because of her husband's orders. Waiting quietly, I followed her to the yard as she got up, and quietly handed it to her. She quietly accepted it and tucked it into her waist, saying,
"Aww, I'm sorry, I'm sorry. If my husband finds out—"
Asano's younger brother followed me so much that he didn't leave my side for a moment, calling me "uncle, uncle." I was bored too, so I told him stories about Tokyo and old stories, and asked him about his family matters and village stories. Judging from his answers (his name was "Harugi"), I could guess that his family's situation was no different from the appearance of his house. "Harugi" finally brought his blanket and decided to sleep with me.

5.
The next morning, I went out to the well to wash my face early and met Asano's sister. She was, in a word, an innocent girl. After briefly greeting me, she ran back into the house and hid. Breakfast was very early. The siblings rode their bicycles side by side and left the house. The sight of the girl riding her bicycle to school was actually funny to me, who had not seen such things often.
Compared to the scattered life in Tokyo, the first day in the countryside was too quiet. I was going to take a walk, but it was also boring to attract the attention of the villagers. I opened the spotty complete works of Sōseki and read novels that were full of nonsense all day long. I was getting a headache.
When "Harugi" came back from school in the evening, carrying his books and running cheerfully, it was like meeting a savior.
"Uncle, will you tell me another story today?"
"Of course."
"And teach me some English too. I have a temporary exam from tomorrow."
"Of course, of course."
That night, "Harugi," who had brought his English book, said,
"Uncle, teach my sister too. She has an exam too."
"Tell her to bring her book. But what do I know?"
"Will you definitely teach her?"
After getting that promise, "Harugi" opened the paper door and shouted,
"Sister, come here. Come in."
The girl seemed to be waiting outside the door. When "Harugi" called, she came into the room, blushing slightly, and sat quietly in the corner of the room. I thought it was polite to offer a greeting first. We greeted each other. The girl's name was "Sadako." My name, of course, was "Esima Gasuo."
At first, she hesitated, but as we talked, I realized that "Sadako" was also a cheerful girl. Moreover, she seemed to be one of the "literary girls" often seen in rural female students. She occasionally brought up stories of people like "Kunikida Doppo", "Sōjō Hachijū", or "Tokuda Shūsei". That night, I had no choice but to acknowledge these writers that the girl admired as she thought of them.
"Harugi," who was tired of the boring conversation, said,
"So what happened to the red ogre? Huh? Huh, uncle, did he beat that tyrannical white ogre to death? But the red ogre is still young, so he must be strong. The white ogre must have a lot of cronies." <138>
"Yes, yes." I laughed and continued the story I had started the night before. "The red ogre is still young now, but he grows three inches taller every day. ..."

6.
The cool autumn moon was bright.
The black walls of the earthen warehouse (土藏) were even darker, and the rice paddies in the village rippled yellow. "Sadako" and I sat on the porch and continued our endless conversation. It had already been a month since I came here. In the meantime, "Sadako" had come to follow me almost like her older brother. And I had come to send her a romantic, pure love. My tired nerves seemed to be recovering with the fresh energy of a boy's dreamlike longing for "Asano Sadako," the cheerful daughter of the soil.
When I talked to "Sadako," I almost forgot my class position. Talking to her, I felt as if there was something of a pure spiritual nature that transcended a person's class identity, somewhere in my heart.
"There are a lot of love triangles in Kunikida Doppo's novels. Tsuyuko's fate is so pitiful. Fumiko is so hateful."
"That's right. People who are fundamentally honest are destined to walk an unhappy path in this world."
"Is that really true?"
I could see the fire of indignation in the girl's eyes even in the moonlight. The girl sighed faintly, as if she was thinking about something. At the same time, I felt something stinging in my heart. What was I going to give the girl with such irresponsible escapist words? What was making me give such an answer? "Asano Sadako" was, at the very least, the sister of my respected comrade "Asano Takeshi."
"But," I corrected myself. "What do you think of Tsuyuko? She is definitely honest and gentle, but she is too weak to live in this world where rough waves are raging. Isn't it an outdated standard to say that weakness and gentleness are the virtues of a woman? So," I said, emphasizing my words, "isn't it Tsuyuko's own fault that a social activist like Kimura abandoned her?"
I looked into the girl's face, wondering what kind of answer would come out.
"But," Sadako replied. "Is it up to her to decide whether her heart is weak or strong?"
"There is a focus (重點) precisely on what is not up to her. In other words, isn't it because she is weak that she is not the kind of person who would pull herself out of being the daughter of a poor person and go out to save unfortunate people?" <139>
The girl closed her mouth and thought about something. Soon she opened her mouth again.
"Yes, that's right. That's true! Even a woman must have a strong heart!"
"A strong heart that is ready to resist and rise up against the outdated system if necessary!"
"Of course!"
"In Russia," I began to tell the story, "during the great turmoil of 1917, young girls of sixteen or seventeen bravely took up guns and fought for the party alongside the men. Didn't they save the party with their strength and liberate women who had been enslaved? ..."

7.
After that night, the relationship between "Sadako" and me became even closer. Gradually, I gave her basic explanations of social thought. "Sadako" listened intently. But his consciousness was still confused. She listened to my stories not because she thought it was the only way, but because she thought it was also one way. As evidence of this, "Sadako" still admired the assassinated person who had shocked the world at that time, while reciting the poems of "Sōjō Hachijū."
One night, I was suffering in a terrible dream. Someone was calling me "uncle, uncle" and shaking my body. When I opened my eyes, sweat was pouring from my forehead. "Harugi" was staring at me with wide eyes.
"Oh, you're awake now. I was so scared. I woke up in the middle of the night and saw you clenching your fists and shouting 'Inomu, Inomu.' What does 'Inomu' mean?"
Oops—I must have been talking in my sleep in Korean. But I pretended not to know and replied,
"I wonder what I was saying."
For some reason, I had bad dreams for several nights in a row. Every time, every time, "Harugi" would laugh and say the next morning,
"What does 'Inomu' mean?"
Eventually, he called me "Inomu No Ojissang". I tried not to talk in my sleep, but the more I tried, the more I talked in my sleep.
"What were you shouting in English last night?"
"English."
When I thought about it, I must have been shouting in Korean. I had gone back to my hometown and given a speech in my dream last night.
I received a letter from a classmate in Tokyo. It said that I was no longer in danger. But it also said that I had been expelled from school. Expulsion—it's not something to be afraid of. But I still felt a little sad.
"From now on, I'll be doing real work!"
But more than that, I had to return to Tokyo as soon as possible. What was I going to do hiding in the countryside any longer? <140>

8.
I didn't have much luggage. But even packing that made my heart feel heavy. I would be in Tokyo tomorrow afternoon. Once again, active work, fresh work, busy life, a life worth living, would begin.
After dinner, while I was teaching "Harugi" English, "Sadako" came. She said she had something to tell me and asked me to come out for a moment. "Sadako" had an expression on her face as if she was surprised by some unexpected news, but also as if she couldn't believe it.
We walked around outside the earthen warehouse (土藏) and talked. The moon, which was two or three days past full, was already casting a faint light on the rice paddies and mountains across the way. "Sadako" hesitated and then opened her mouth as if she had made up her mind. She even stopped walking.
"Esima-ssi. I have something to ask you. Please don't be upset."
"Not at all. Why would I be upset?"
"Well, Esima-ssi. Are you really Japanese?"
It was a shock. What was the right answer?
"Why do you ask? That is."
In that instant, I decided to tell her my true identity. What did it matter if she found out that I wasn't "Esima" now? Besides, I was very distressed that I was deceiving even this innocent girl.
"Yes, to tell you the truth, I'm not 'Esima.' I'm a Korean person named 'Kim Taek'."
"Korean person!"
The girl said to herself unconsciously.
"Yes, I'm Korean. But how did you guess that?"
"Actually, my parents have been saying for half a month now that no matter how they look at him, 'Esima'-sang doesn't seem Japanese. I argued that it wasn't true, but after you started talking in your sleep, they started to suspect even more, wondering if you might be Chinese or Korean."
"Yes, your parents were right. Since I've started talking, I'll tell you everything. I'm not someone who came to get treatment for an illness, but someone who came to escape." <141>
"To escape!"
The girl looked even more surprised.
"You're surprised, aren't you?"
She didn't answer.
That night, in the middle of the night, I heard someone calling "Esima-ssi, Esima-ssi" outside the door. I hadn't been able to sleep, so I got up right away and went outside, and it was "Sadako." Since it was a mountain village, the moon had already set.
"What's wrong?"
"Sadako" said passionately.
"Take me with you tomorrow morning. To Tokyo!"
"Eh-?"
"Take me to Tokyo!"
In that instant, I thought about everything at an incredible speed. First, taking "Sadako" would create a misunderstanding, and second, it would be impossible to leave in the morning without her parents knowing.
"I can't go this time. I'll try to come back when I have a chance."
"I'm going to follow you to Tokyo!"
"I'm Korean. What's more, I'm a fugitive. Your parents won't allow it."
"Please run away with me!"
I was moved by "Sadako's" passion.
"But life in Tokyo is scary. Our lives are about fighting against rough waves."
"I've decided everything. Run away with me!"
In that instant, I ran like a wild horse and hugged "Sadako." Her lips were hot.
"Let's leave! Get ready!" <142>

歸鄕
						兪鎭午
1.

여섯해전.
땅이 함부로 흔들니며 집이 되는대로 넘어갓다. 밤이되면 하늘을 찌르는 불꼿이 이 세상의 결말을 지을듯이 인구 이백만의 큰 도회를 뭇질넛다. 사람의 목숨이 일전자리고 고무풍선 보다도 더 헐하게 최후를 지엿다. 번적이는 쇠끗과 색감안 긔게의 구멍. 어둠에서 내 어미는 등불. 난데업는 총소리. 어느 백작의 집담 박에서는 산양총을 든 젊은 사내가 아츰마다 아츰마다 길로 향한 이집 이층의 한방ㅅ문을 치어다 보고 혀를 툭툭치며 왓다 갓다 하엿다. 방ㅅ속에는 얼골이 기다란 바다를 건너온 M귀족의 아들이 자고 잇섯다.
「월급」삼십원을 주고 사드린 노파는 우리들 세 사람의 생명의 할머니엿다. 노파는 우리를  위하야 량식을 팔어다 주고 반찬을 준비하엿다. 그러나 우리들의 꿈은 아즉도 어수선하엿다. 어느때는 한 밤중에 현관문을 흔드는 사람이 잇섯다. 노파를 압세우고 벌벌 떨며 나아간 우리들의 눈압헤는 우리의 붉은 가슴 한복판을 향한 색감안 쇠구멍이 잇섯다. 우리는 손이 발이 되도록 빌엇다. 무엇을 생각하엿든지 복면의 사내는 놉흔 우슴소리를 던지고 어둠속으로 사러저 버리엿다.
우리는 긔적(奇蹟)으로 무사하엿다. 그러나 혼이 나간 노파는 잇흔날 새벽 애원하는 우리를 떼던지고 바덧든 돈까지 내여 던지고 자긔 자신의 목슴을 구하기 위하야 어대로인지 가 버렷다. 우리는 다시 무인 절도의 파선 맛난 사람가치 헛되히 구원의 손만을 바라고 잇게 되엿다.

2.

위헉은 시간과 함께 들하엿 갓다. 우리는 녓날 이약이에 나오는 행운의 사람가치 아슬아슬한 고비를 몃번이나 넘어 겨우 가슴을 내리쓸게 되엿다. 그러나 나만은 드듸여 동경에 머물너 잇슬 수 업는 처지가 되엿다. 그 사건에는 나는 별로 깁흔관게는 업섯스나 깁흔관게가 없섯기에 도리혀 억울한＜134＞ 꼴을 보기가 쉬운 것이엿다. 사정은 시시각각으로 위급해 갓다. 아즉 지진끗흐 경게가 엄중하니 가만히 고국으로 도라갈 수도 업섯다. 하는수 업시 하로밤 나는 「아사구사」의 어느 곳에 아즉도 숨어잇는 동지「아사노」를 차젓다.
사정을 들은 그는 나에게 한장의 현지를 써 주엇다.
... 동창생 「에시마」가 신병으로 인해 약 한달둥안 정양을 하러가오니 부대 잘 대접해 주십시요. ... ...
「김군 싀골을 가면 꼭 「에시마」 노릇을 해야 되네 결단코 자네가 김가인 것을 알녀서는 안돼.」
「에시마 허허허 걱정말게. 고마워이.」
사실 나는 일본말 회화에 자신이 잇섯술뿐 아니라 약간 다다분하게 부튼 이목구비는 더욱히 「에시마」 행세를 할 가능성을 주는 것이엿다.
「아사노」의 말은 그의 시골집 사람들은 아모 특질도 업는 평범한 자작농이며 성미들도 대개는 「선량한 농민」 그대로이닛가 내가 끗까지 「에시마」행세만 하면 몸은 안전하리라 하는 것이엿다.
「자 에시마군. 웨 「에시마」라고 부르는데 대답을 안해 자네는 에시마야. 꿈에라도 니저 버리면 안돼.」
「걱정말게. 메시마 에시마.」
잇튼날 밤 나는 조그만 가방에에다가 소석(潄石)전집 몃권을 집어 넛코 한손에는 선물 줄 것을 싸들고 「우에노역」을 떠낫다. 그때의 나의 파리한 모습을 본다면 누구든지 별으로 정양가는 사람이라고 인정하지 아니 할 사람이 엇섯슬 것이다. 오후 열시반-긔차는 북으로 「도찌기」 현을 향해 내닷기 시작하엿다. 나는 가만히 가슴을 내리쓸고 「강담구락부」를 꺼내들엇다.

3.

싸늘한 가을 달이 밝엇다.

나는 엇던 대답이 나올 것인가 하며 소녀의 얼골을 들여다 보앗다.
「그럿치만요.」하고 「사다꼬」는 대답한다. 「마음이 약하고 강한 것이야 제 마음대로 되는 것인가요.」
「제 마음대로 안되는 곳에 도리혀 중점(重點)이 잇는 것임니다. 다시 말하면 마음이 약하다는 것은 그가 가난한 사람의＜139＞ 딸 스스로 몸을 빼내처 불상한 사람을 구하러 나설 사람이 아니라는 것을 말해게 되지 안음니까.」
소녀는 입을 담을고 무엇을 생각하엿다. 이윽고 다시 입을 열엇다.
「네 그래요. 사실 그래요! 녀자라도 마음을 튼튼히 가저야 될 것이예요!」
「여차직하면 날근 제도에 반항하고 니러설만한 튼튼한 마음을요!」
「물론 그래요!」
「로서아에서는」하고 나는 이약이를 시작하엿다. 「1917년의 대소란때에 열여섯 열닐곱의 어린 소녀들이 용감하게 총을 잡고 나서서 사내들과 함께 당을 위해 싸웟담니다. 그들은 그들의 힘으로 당을 구하고 속박되엿든 녀자를 해방한 것이 아님니까. ... ...」

7.
그날밤 이후 「사다꼬」와 나의 사이는 더욱 갓가워갓다. 차차로 나는 사회사상의 초보적 설명을 해 들녀주엇다. 「사다꼬」는 열심으로 귀를 기우렷다. 그러나 그의 의식은 아즉 아즉 혼돈하엿다. 나의 이약이에 귀를 기우리는 것은 그것이 오즉 하나인 길이라고 생각하기 때문이 아니라 그것도 또한 한가지 길이라고 생각하고 잇는 듯 하엿다. 그 증거로는 「사다꼬」는 아즉도 그때 세상을 놀내인 암살당한 모씨를 숭배하는 한편 「서조팔십」의 시를 애송하고 잇는 것이엿다.
어느날밤 나는 흉한 꿈 속에서 괴로워 하고 잇섯다. 누가 「아저씨 아저씨」하고 나를 불으면서 내 몸을 흔들엇다. 눈을 떠보니 이마에 진땀이 흘넛다. 「하루기」가 눈을 둥그럿케 뜨고 나를 늘여다 보고 잇섯다.
「아이구 인제 정신이 나슈. 난 혼낫서 자다가 엇제 다 깨닛가 아저씨가 왼통 주먹을 내둘으면서 무슨 「이노무 이노무」하면서 소리를 치신다우. 「이노무」가 무슨 소리유.」
앗차―나는 조선말로 잠꼬대를 한것이 아닐까. 그러나 나는 시침이를 뚝떼고 대답하엿다.
「글세 무슨 소릴 첫슬까.」
웬일인지 몃칠밤을 련해 꿈자리가 사나웟다. 그릴적마다 그릴적마다 잇튼날 아츰이면 「하루기」는 깔깔 우스면서
「대체 「이노무」가 무슨 소리유.」
하고 뭇는 것이엿다. 내종에는 나를 「이노무 노 오지쌍」이라고 불넛다. 나는 잠꼬대를 아니하랴고 애를 섯스나 그럴 수룩에 잠고대는 점점 더 심해갓다.
「엇저녁엔 또 웬 영어로 그럿케 떠드슈.」
「영어.」
하고 보면 나는 필연 조선말로 떠들어 대임에 틀님엇섯다. 나는 엇저녁 꿈에 고향에 도라가 한바탕 연설을 하엿스니.
동경 잇는 동모에게서 편지가 왓다. 나는 그만하면 몸이 위험하지는 아니하게 되엿다는 것이다. 그러나 학교에서는 제명을＜140＞ 당햇다는 것이엿다. 제명-그것이 무서울 것은 업다. 그러나 역시 무엇이 앗가운 듯한 늣김이 업지 안엇다.
「인제부터 정말 진짱 일을 하게 된다!」
그러나 그럿케 된 것 이상 하로밧비 동경으로 돌아가야 하는 것이엿다. 이 이상 싀골에 숨어서 하랴는 것이 무엇인가.

8.
짐이라야 별로 업섯다. 그러나 그나마도 주어싸놋코 나니까 마음이 가든하엿다. 내일 오후면 동경 가잇게 되는 것이다. 다시 활반한 일 싱싱한 일 분주한 생활 생활다운 생활이 시작되는 것이다.
저녁후에 「하루기」에게 영어를 가르켜 주고 잇노라닛가 「사다꼬」가 왓다. 무슨 할 말이 잇스니 잠간 나오라는 것이엿다. 「사다고」는 무슨 의외의 소식에 놀난듯한 그러나 그것을 미들 수 업다는 듯한 표정을 하고 잇섯다.
우리는 「도쏘-」(士藏)엽 밧가흐로 거니며 이약이 하엿다. 보름을 이삼일 넘은 달은 벌서 건너편 논과 산에 엷은 빗을 던지고 잇섯다. 「사다꼬」는 머믓거리다가 결심한 듯이 입을 열엇다. 그는 거니든 발까지 멈추엇다.
「에시마씨. 저 무어 하나 엿주어 볼텐데요. 마음을 상하지 마세요.」
「천만에. 웨 마음을 상하겟세요.」
「그런데요. 저 에시마씨. 당신은 정말 일본 사람이시지요.」
뜩금하엿다. 무엇이라고 대답하여야 올흘가.
「웨 그러세요. 그건.」
「글세요.」
순간에 나는 나의 본색을 말하기를 결심하엿다. 이제 내가 「에시마」 아닌 것이 알니운들 무엇이 엇더랴. 뿐 아니라 나는 순진한 소녀까지 속이지 아니치 못하는 것이 퍽이나 괴로웟다.
「녜 바로 말슴하지요. 나는 「에시마」가 아니올시다. 나는 「김택」이라는 조선 사람임니다.」
「조선 사람!」
소녀는 무의식적으로 혼자말 하엿다.
「녜 나는 조선 사람이예요. 허지만 그건 엇더케 짐작하엿소.」
「실상인즉 집이 어버이들이 벌서 반달이나 전부터 「에시마」상은 암만 보아도 일본 사람갓지 아니하다고 그래요. 나는 천만에 말이라고 욱엿지만 그 잠꼬대를 시작하신 후로는 더구나 의심을 하기 시작해 가지고 혹시 중국 사람이나 아닌가 조선 사람이나 아닌가 늘 하엿세요.」
「녜 역시 당신의 어버이들이 바로 알엇슴니다. 정말 말하기 시작하엿스니 아조 다 말하지요. 나는 병을 곳치러 온 사람이 아니라 몸을 피해 온 사람임니다.」＜141＞
「몸을 피해서!」
소녀는 더욱 놀내는 모양이엇다.
「놀나섯지요.」
그는 아모 대답이 업다.
그날밤 한밤중에 문박게서 「에시마씨 에시마씨」하는 소리가 들녓다. 잠이 오지 안하든 터이라 바로 니러나 나가 보니까 그것은 「사다꼬」엿다. 산촌이라 달은 발서 깔닥 넘어 갓다.
「웬일이시요?」
「사다꼬」는 정렬적으로 말하엿다.
「내일 아츰에 나도 데리고 가 주세요. 동경으로!」
「에-?」
「동경으로 데리고 가 주세요!」
나는 순간에 굉장한 속도로 모든 것을 생각해 보앗다. 첫재로 「사다꼬」를 데리고 간다는 것은 오해를 바들 렴녀가 잇고 둘재로 그의 부모 몰래 아츰에 떠나는 것은 불가능한 일이다.
「이번에는 못가심니다. 긔회를 보아 오도록 하십시요.」
「나도 당신을 따러 동경으로 가겟세요!」
「나는 조선 사람임니다. 더구나 쫏겨 단이는 사람임니다. 당신의 부모는 허락하지 아니 하실 것임니다.」
「나를 데리고 도망가 주세요!」
나는 「사다꼬」의 렬성에 움즈겻다.
「그럿치만 동경서의 생활은 무섭습니다. 우리들의 생활은 거츠른 물결과 싸호는 것임니다.」
「모든 것을 결심햇세요. 나와 함께 도망해 주세요!」
순간 나는 사나운 말가치 달녀들어 「사다꼬」를 안엇다. 그의 입술은 뜨거웟다.
「떠납시다! 준비를 하시요!」＜142＞
