기사제목	水利組合은 왜 破綻되나, 農村座談會 (Why are Irrigation Associations Bankrupt? - A Rural Roundtable Discussion)
필자	鄭寅寬 (Chŏng In-gwan)
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Roundtable)

Why are Irrigation Associations Bankrupt? - A Rural Roundtable Discussion
						Chŏng In-gwan
Chairman: Ahem, despite the late hour, I thank you all for coming. Tonight, I hope we can have an open discussion about the irrigation association problem. Lately, the newspapers have been filled with news about irrigation associations in various places, with farmers staging protests and demanding national subsidies, causing a stir at the Government-General (總督府). It seems like the courtyards of provincial and county offices are turning into marketplaces for petitioners. What is the fundamental flaw, and what are the actual problems we are facing? I think we need to thoroughly examine this issue. Please, everyone, share your opinions. (Applause)

Yi: Chairman, you have spoken extensively about irrigation associations and have a lot of material. Can you first explain why irrigation associations were created in the first place?

All: Yes, please tell us.

Chairman: I don't know if I have any special knowledge, but since you all ask, I will tell you what I know.

"Why Were Irrigation Associations Created?"

To talk about irrigation associations, we must first talk about what the Rice Increase Plan (산미증식) is. To talk about the food problem, we must talk about Japan's colonial policy. But to go into detail would take too long, so I will keep it brief. First, for Japan's commerce and industry to develop, it needed to develop agriculture in its colonies. Second, to feed Japan's ever-increasing population, it needed a large supply of Korean rice. Third, as Korea, the raw material base (原料地), has agriculture as its primary industry, formulating the current industrial policy serves as the main driving force (主動力). Under these reasons, the Korean Rice Increase Plan (朝鮮産米増殖策) was a policy put forward by the then-Government-General (朝鮮總督) Hasekawa, who advocated so-called industrial supremacy. With the Rice Increase Plan (산미증식책), land improvement was advocated, and to improve the land, irrigation projects were seen as a prerequisite, so it was decided to create irrigation associations. The Rice Increase Plan (산미증식책) was conceived as follows: over twelve years, starting from Taishō 15 (1926), to improve the irrigation of rice paddies (灌漑改善), convert fields into rice paddies, and cultivate barren fields, improving 350,000 chŏngbo of land to produce an additional 8,700,000 sŏk of rice. Of that increase, about 3,000,000 sŏk would be consumed in Korea, and about 5,000,000 sŏk would be sold to Japan. Combined with the 5,000,000 sŏk already being sold to Japan in Taishō 15, this would result in 10,000,000 sŏk being sold annually, bringing 300 million won to Korea each year, assuming a price of 30 won per sŏm of rice. The budget for this project was estimated at a total of 285,330,000 won, with the government subsidizing 65,070,000 won and entrepreneurs (企業者) investing 22,060,000 won. The remaining 198,200,000 won would be borrowed and used for fertilizer costs, agricultural improvement costs, land improvement costs, irrigation construction costs, and general expenses. The borrowed money would be repaid through irrigation association fees.

"How Much Were the Construction Costs and Debt?"

Kim: Very well explained. Afterwards, does anyone know how much the actual construction costs were and how much debt was incurred?

Kong: This is based on statistics from two years ago, but in 1929 (Shōwa 4), the construction costs for 139 irrigation associations, including establishment project costs, district change project costs, additional improvement project costs, disaster recovery project costs, and compensation for revenue deficits, totaled 86,810,723 won. The amount used to repay the debt with association fees was 6,295,294 won, and the outstanding debt was 80,515,429 won. Perhaps there were also administrative costs in addition to the construction costs, and the interest rate on the debt was 7.4 percent per annum, which must be quite burdensome. In any case, the total amount spent was 115,565,744 won, equivalent to 56 won and 10 chŏn per tanbo (300 p'yŏng) of rice paddy. At that time, it was 104,121,363 won, which is 50 won and 54 chŏn per tanbo.

Yun: Oh my, so it's like buying my own land back.

Han: Why isn't our association like that? It would be good if it didn't increase further. The association fees, when added up across all of Korea, must be considerable. If we have to repay all that debt, we'll melt away.

Chairman: Yes, in 1929 alone, it was 13,474,473 won, averaging 6 won and 54 chŏn per tanbo.

Pak: Wait a minute. I think I paid 13 won per tanbo in taxes two years ago.

Ch'ae: What is the highest and lowest association fee across all of Korea?

Kim: Again, in 1929, there were 29 places where it was less than 5 won, 16 places where it was 5 won or more, 16 places where it was 6 won or more, 28 places where it was 7 won or more, 24 places where it was 8 won or more, 8 places where it was 9 won or more, 8 places where it was 10 won or more, 3 places where it was 11 won or more, 2 places where it was 12 won or more, 2 places where it was 13 won or more, 2 places where it was 14 won or more, and 2 places where it was 16 won or more.

"How Were Irrigation Association Fees Calculated?"

Kong: What was the standard for calculating the association fees?

Yi: The average yield per tanbo in Korea was estimated at 1 sŏk and 7 sŭng before the irrigation project, and 2 sŏk and 8 tu and 7 sŭng after the project, with an increase of 1 sŏk and 7 tu and 9 sŭng. Assuming an average price of 9 won and 70 chŏn per sŏm of rice, the increased rice yield would generate a profit of 17 won and 52 chŏn. The basis for calculating the association fee (組合費算出基礎) was set at 8 won and 87 chŏn, but the actual average association fee collected was 6 won and 54 chŏn, resulting in a net profit of 2 won and 33 chŏn, they claimed.

Yun: The standard for the increased yield is also nonsense, and it was originally wrong to base it on the rice price during good times. Now the price of one sŏm of rice is around 4 won, so it is inevitable that the irrigation associations will fail.

Han: So, if the average price of the increased rice yield per tanbo is 17 won and 52 chŏn, and the basis for calculation is 8 won and 87 chŏn, what was the remaining 8 won and 65 chŏn used for?

Yi: It is said that in the case of landlords, half of the increased yield is given to the tenants, and the remainder is used for other expenses. In the case of owner-farmers, it is used to cover farming management expenses (榮農管理費) and other expenses. Even then, they claim there is a remaining profit of 2 won and 33 chŏn. In other words, it's just scribbling with a brush arbitrarily.

Pak: Wherever you go, the landlords burden the tenants with half of the association fees, which is completely unfair.

All: That's right, that's right. Who would pay for it? A tenant?

"What are the Advantages and Disadvantages after the Establishment of Irrigation Associations?"

Ch'ae: Chairman, how much more rice is produced after the irrigation associations were established? And what are the advantages and disadvantages?

Chairman: Of course, more is produced. According to statistics from two years ago, in 1929 (Shōwa 4), the actual cultivated area (實收面積) of 121,331 chŏngbo produced 1,294,455 sŏk before the project and 3,826,693 sŏk after the project, an increase of 2,532,239 sŏk. On average, per tanbo, the yield before the project was 1 sŏk and 7 sŭng, and after the project, it was 3 sŏk and 1 tu and 5 sŭng, an increase of 2 sŏk and 8 sŭng. So, if the price of rice at that time was calculated at 11 won and 11 chŏn per sŏm, the increased rice yield would be worth 7,033,293 won. If we follow the method of using about half of the increased amount as the basis for calculating the association fee (組合費算出基礎), we would have to pay 3,516,646 won as association fees. However, the actual association fee for the cultivated area (實收面積) in that year was 7,821,152 won, resulting in a deficit of 4,304,506 won compared to the basis for calculating the association fee. Even if we consider the entire increased amount, there is a shortage of 787,859 won, ultimately resulting in a loss.

Yi: The increased rice yield is partly due to the irrigation associations, but also because a lot of chemical fertilizers (金肥) are being used. According to the average for all of Korea in 1929 (Shōwa 4), the cost of chemical fertilizers per tanbo was 5 won and 16 chŏn, so the rice yield increased because of the heavy fertilization. Also, agricultural techniques have gradually improved, and the weather has been favorable for the past two or three years, resulting in good harvests.

Kim: That's right. We can't say that the increase is solely due to the irrigation associations. Speaking of chemical fertilizers, the cost of chemical fertilizers is purely debt. How can we boast only about the performance of the irrigation associations when that's the case?

Audience: That's right - there's no benefit even if there's an increase. It's actually a loss.

Kong: And when we sell fields to buy rice paddies, it's to eat rice. But even though more rice is produced, farmers can't even afford millet rice (좁쌀밥) from Manchuria. Isn't that pitiful?

Chairman: Well, the rice consumption of Koreans is decreasing. In 1919 (Taishō 8), it was 6 tu and 7 sŭng and 2 hap per person. In 1922 (Taishō 11), it was 6 tu and 6 sŭng and 2 hap. In 1925 (Taishō 14), it was 5 tu and 9 sŭng and 2 hap. In 1927 (Shōwa 2), it was 5 tu and 9 hap, and in 1929 (Shōwa 4), it was 4 tu and 4 sŭng and 4 hap. So, while rice production is increasing, consumption is decreasing.

"Why are Irrigation Associations Bankrupt?"

Chairman: As irrigation associations are now in dire straits, with members losing their land through auctions and facing the seizure of standing crops (立稻差押) for not being able to pay association fees, and with associations going bankrupt one after another, let's examine the causes, both internal and external (內的外的).

All: Agreed.

Yi: The global economic depression, and especially the severe agricultural depression, with plummeting rice prices and financial stagnation (金融梗塞), must be one of the major causes.

Kim: The repeated bumper crops in Japan in recent years have caused rice prices to fall, and the association members have accumulated debt and excessive association fees.

(5 lines omitted)

Yun: It's because the basis for calculating the association fees (組合費算出基礎) was inaccurate.

Kong: The construction design was done by people who didn't understand agricultural technology and industrial policy, resulting in excessively high construction costs.

Pak: The construction was carried out by companies that were profit-seeking exploitation institutions, which inevitably led to higher construction costs.

Ch'ae: Despite being a critical industrial responsibility in a colony, the national subsidy was reduced to cut costs.

Audience: A, there's also this. B, there's also that. C, there's also this. D, there's also that. E, there's also this.

Chairman: Then please speak from the audience.

A: Not only was the national subsidy small and the debt large, but the interest rate of 7.4 percent was too high, and the grace period (据置期間) and repayment period were too short.

B: Not only are there too many unnecessary people employed, but the salaries are also excessive, resulting in high labor costs (人件費).

C: The irrigation associations are not managed autonomously, but are controlled entirely by the government from beginning to end. The majority of landowners cannot participate, and even more so, tenants are not even considered. Can you ignore the farmers who are the actual cultivators of the land?

D: Judging from the frequent exposure of corruption cases, we can't even trust the entire system.

E: It's because large landowners and financial magnates collude with government authority and disregard small and medium-sized farmers to pursue their own interests by all means.

All: That's right, that's right.

Chairman: There is much more to say, but let's stop here and discuss the tenant farming problem another day. Meeting adjourned.

(Applause)
