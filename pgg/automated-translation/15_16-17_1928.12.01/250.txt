필자	白시라 記 (Paek Sira)
기사형태	회고·수기 (Memoir/Personal Account)

A Female Reporter's Undercover Story: A Record of Dressing Up as a Woman Who Fled Her Village and Taking an Actress Recruitment Exam, A Bizarre Examination for Selecting Actresses
						Paek Sira

When you see an advertisement that says, "Details on actress recruitment will be discussed later," what thoughts come to your mind? Modern girls with high-heeled shoes, wristwatches, bright blue skirts, bobbed hair, red lipstick, and love letters in their handbags come to mind first, and then modern boys in Ralph pants, smiling and taking care of the women who give off a strong scent and gather while laughing, guiding them one by one, and then the men and women quickly become close, embracing each other, and dancing the jazz wildly. That's what everyone thinks. Is that really the case?

The Sa0-hoe (士0會), whose name is well-known in the Chosŏn theater world, is recruiting male and female actors, so it's a bit embarrassing, but I hid my title as a reporter for a while and transformed into a so-called modern girl aspiring to be an actress to see the inside story, hoping to satisfy the readers' curiosity and receive praise from the editor-in-chief. While suppressing some fear in my heart, I started a daring act for my sake.

I went to the temporary office, which was on the upper floor of a small wooden two-story house in front of the Mokyoktang (沐浴湯) in 0wŏn-dong. As I climbed the narrow, weak stairs, there were four or five young women with their hair neatly braided, sitting quietly in a small room, practicing plays in the style of a silent film narrator. One said, "Oh, Mother," and another, in an even more exaggerated narrator's tone, said, "Well, child, think about it." It was funny at first glance, but they were not short-haired tomboys or mischievous girls as I had imagined, but pure young women without any vulgarity. "They are so quiet and young." I touched my hair with my hand, and because I had a chignon, I looked older than them, so I felt a little relieved and went next to the young women. Before the young women could say anything, a man in a Western suit, who looked like he was 25 or 26 years old and seemed to be an actor or staff member, suddenly stood up and said, "Who are you looking for?" Thinking that more female guests were coming to find individuals than those applying to be actresses, I said, "No, I'm here to apply." He said, "Yes, please come this way," and went back down the stairs. So, I followed him and went back down the stairs I had just come up. After going down, we entered a small house that looked like a printing shop, and there I took off my shoes, stepped on scattered papers, and climbed the ladder-like stairs in the corner. In the small tatami room, an old man who looked like he was in his 40s or 50s and two or three young men were sitting facing each other, practicing plays in the style of a narrator. They all looked at me at once. Embarrassed, I bowed my head, crossed the room horizontally, and entered the next room. Here, there was a small tatami room with a desk, and a neatly dressed man in his late 20s wearing a Gonse wool suit was busily writing something, whether it was a script or something else. He looked at me and said, "Please sit down," and then stared at my face again. I thought, "Okay, a face check first," and felt like his gaze was tickling my nose and forehead. I thought that if my face was a bit more attractive, I would have passed right away, and my face flushed a little. But since I wasn't really an applicant, why should I think that way? So, I went closer to him and sat down a bit boldly. His face was generally that of a gentle scholar, but he looked very cold, and his body and hands were very thin. Still staring at my face and clothes, he asked, "What is your name?" Oh no, I can't reveal my real name, so what should I make up? I said whatever came to mind, "Kim Yŏng-suk." "Where do you live?" "In Kahoedong, I think. I don't know Seoul very well, but I think it's Kahoedong 216." I gave the address of Mr. X, who works at our company, in a hurry. I thought my answer was too clumsy, so I tried to be as cute as possible and smiled once. He smiled back in response and asked, "Who told you about this place?" "No, I didn't hear it from anyone in particular, but I just heard it from friends. I've liked things like plays since I was young, so I came to apply." He said, "Yes, but I'm trying to find out the connection," and insisted on an answer.

<115> But I still gave the same vague answer as before. After this much questioning, the fear of pretending to be someone I wasn't and entering someone else's office like a spy gradually disappeared. Moreover, I was relieved by the fact that the people here were not rude or unsophisticated, but unexpectedly calm and sincere. Now, my only concern was, "What if someone who knows that I'm a magazine female reporter comes in?" So, this time, I decided to take the offensive. Before he could say anything, I said first, "Look here. I heard from others that the most important condition for choosing an actress is having a pretty face. If that's really the case, I'll leave." I showed signs of getting up. "No, not at all. Your face is good enough. But even if it's not that good, we still choose them." He laughed. Among the applicants who are just trying to be chosen and acting demure, no one would have said such a thing.

"Then I'll take the exam with peace of mind." After saying that, I said, "What I'm most afraid of is that actresses are considered the lowest, most vulgar, and dirtiest women in society." He said, "That's right. That's a big problem. That's why, since we first started the T'o0hoe (土0會), we've been working harder than before to choose actresses, focusing on character rather than looks. But often, only delinquent girls gather. This time, about 30 people came, but we only chose 3 or 4."

"So, the recruitment exam is already over?" He said, "Yes, it ended on the 4th." "Oh, really? I didn't know it was already over. I only found out yesterday, so please forgive me for being so late and please let me join." He said, "Yes, that doesn't matter. If the person is suitable, we'll choose them anytime," and reassured me.

But since there wasn't much time left, I became impatient and asked unrelated questions, trying to find out what I wanted to know quickly. "By the way, what I'm most concerned about is the issue of financial difficulties. Because I came to Seoul without listening to my parents in my hometown, I can't use their money, and these days, the landlord is urging me to pay for food, so I'm thinking of going to the Han River. If I join here, can I at least make a living?" I asked, trying to find out the salary or living conditions of so-called actresses. He said, "Well, we haven't set or paid any monthly salary yet, but if there are special circumstances, this association can supplement your living expenses." So, it was clear that being an actress was not only about satisfying the vanity of a naive girl, but there was no specifically defined salary. The phrase "special circumstances" seemed meaningful, and it was difficult to judge whether having an attractive face that could gain popularity or being particularly good at acting was a special circumstance.

There were so many things I wanted to ask and find out, so I was thinking for a moment while sitting down <116>. This time, he took the offensive and asked, "What village are you from?" Without hesitation, I blurted out, "Wŏnsan." The dialect I use is Chŏlla-do, but I quickly made it up and said Wŏnsan, where I have a close friend. I thought it was so awkward and funny that I smiled to myself, and he smiled back, seeing it as cuteness. "What's the address?" "Sanje-dong 2X." I even gave my friend's house number. "What's your father's name?" "Chang and Hwan." "Yes! Mr. Kim Chang-hwan. What about your mother?" "My mother is Ch'oe Kyŏng-rye." "Do you have many siblings?" "I'm the eldest daughter, and I have three younger siblings." This much I told the truth. Of course, even while asking these questions, his eyes were fixed on my face.

"By the way, when the theater company goes on a regional tour, do male and female actors stay at the same inn?" Even though it was a shady thing to ask, I asked this just to satisfy my curiosity. "Yes, of course. But they don't stay in the same room." He smiled slyly. What kind of smile was that? "I'll ask one more rude question. When the food expenses are not enough after a tour, they temporarily leave the actress at the inn, right? What happens then?" I tilted my head like I was acting cute and asked. As if it was a big deal, he said, "No, that doesn't happen. Such things never happen in our T'o0hoe (土0會). Other poor theater companies in the past did that a lot, so they think our company is like that too, but in reality, such things don't happen in our company." From this, I could guess that such things don't happen in this company, but they often happen in other theater companies.

I lifted my sleeve and looked at my watch slyly. It was already three o'clock. In order to ask a few more questions, I hurried and asked, "What are the conditions for choosing an actress?" "There are 13 conditions." His immediate answer showed that there were definitely 13 conditions. I only heard them briefly (I couldn't write them down on a handkerchief at the time), so I can't record them all now, but the first is to see if the impression is good or bad when meeting, and then character, physique, expression, appearance, education, ideology, hobbies, memory, voice, handwriting, script reading, etc. Among them, the most interesting seemed to be the expression test, so I said, "How do you do the expression test? Please test me now." It was an impertinent act for an applicant. "No, we are conducting the expression test while talking to Miss Yŏng-suk (he actually called me Miss Yŏng-suk) like this." I pretended to be surprised and said, "Oh, really? I didn't realize it until now, so I guess I'll fail." I nodded and showed a look of disappointment as if I was making some kind of expression. "No, you're doing well." Then he took out the test paper and said, "Please write this within two hours." The test questions were printed on half a sheet of paper.

<117> T'o0hoe (土0會) 0th Recruitment Exam for Members
1. What is "I"?
2. Was Ibsen really a genius?
3. Choose only one topic from the two topics below:
   A. The dramatic value of the Ch'unhyang Story (春香傳)?
   B. The literary renaissance of Chosŏn?

It seemed like an overly difficult question even with a moment's thought. It seemed like they were asking for dozens of pages of essays, and it seemed like only someone who had graduated from a university abroad could write a few lines. I could answer quickly if it was a one-word answer, but it seemed like it would take a while to start writing, whether I wrote well or not, so I knew it would be foolish to sit and write for a long time since I wasn't really an applicant. "I can't write something this difficult here," I said. "It doesn't matter. 130 men and women took this test, but no one wrote the answer correctly. So, please write it roughly." Knowing that it was very kind and grateful of him to say this, I still insisted, "I'll take it home and bring it back." And then, to quickly cover up the condition, I brought up another topic. "I heard that male and female actors date in the theater company." He laughed. "Well, that's what society thinks, but in reality, there is less dating between actors." "So, it's not completely non-existent. Where else would they go?" I pretended to be naive and said such things. "But it depends on your own mind." "To be honest, I'm married, so I'm less worried." "Yes, you're married? Then why aren't you taking care of your household and coming here?" "Yes, because our ideals didn't match, I got divorced. I heard you only choose single women here." "Well," he said, "But we also choose married women. The reason why we're a little wary of married women here is not because of any other meaning, but because married women are naturally less free, have many restrictions from all sides, and are busy, so if possible, we choose pure single women to focus only on acting."

The clock was already 15 minutes before 3 o'clock, and since I had to go to the company, I thought I should leave quickly. "Then I'll take this with me and bring it back tomorrow," I said and tried to get up. He told me to write a resume first, so I sat down reluctantly because it would be too rude to refuse. It was a funny but tiring task to make up things like this and that. Moreover, when writing my name on the top line, I almost wrote the surname "Paek (白)" instead of Kim Yŏng-suk, and I was about to correct it to Kim, which was almost embarrassing. "I'm leaving," I said and tried to get up. "Can't you come tonight?" he asked, so I said, "It's a little difficult at night." <118> He asked where my house in Kahoedong was, which made me panic. I had heard that Mr. X's house was at a certain number in Kahoedong, so I blurted it out in a hurry, but how would I know where that house was? I just used the fact that I had just come from the countryside as a shield and said, "Well, I don't know very well because it's my first time here. Maybe it's near the back gate of Chae-dong Elementary School." Then I started to run away. Then he said, "It's okay to have someone write the answers to the test questions for you." I couldn't immediately figure out what he meant by that, and I couldn't ask, so I just left the place. I felt relieved, as if I had been locked up somewhere and released. It was my first job after joining the company, and it seemed like there was no big failure in the first thing I had done in my life, so I came back with a somewhat pleasant feeling as if I had achieved some kind of victory. On the way to the company, I looked back more than ten times, wondering if anyone was following me. What surprised me on the first day of exploration was that, unlike what people thought, the inside was not chaotic or full of corrupt atmosphere, but very simple, quiet, and unexpectedly gentle people. Moreover, the person who questioned me treated me so sincerely that I felt bad that I had acted mischievously and couldn't ask all the questions I wanted to ask.

Second Day's Questions and Answers

Since I had already started, I decided to find out whether I would pass or not, so I went there again after lunch the next day with hesitant steps. As I passed the house that looked like a printing shop and climbed the ladder stairs, there were six or seven young people gathered in the first room. I was afraid that someone would recognize me, so I bowed my head and quickly crossed over to the other room. On this day, the person who had questioned me the day before was sitting facing a man wearing a white durumagi (a traditional Korean overcoat), and they seemed to be writing a script in a hurry. He glanced at me and bowed his head, but just kept writing what he was writing. After waiting for a while, he put away what he was writing and sat facing me. He made a gesture to the young man in the white durumagi sitting next to me, and he seemed to be sitting behind me and taking notes on what I was saying.

First, I handed over the test answers that I had written carelessly, even though I didn't have much knowledge. After reading them for a while, he said, "You wrote it very well. It's very excellent." I said, "No, I'm embarrassed. How can I know how difficult the questions are? I just looked through this book and that book and wrote whatever I could." He said, whether it was true or not, "No one, male or female, has written such a good answer." Then, while looking at the answers again, he asked, "You wrote 'thought (思索),' so how is it different from reflection and idea?" "What is the subject (主體)?" I said, "This is very philosophical." "What? How would someone like me know what philosophy is?" Since the question of what "I" (我) is <119> was like that, what else could the answer be but philosophical? While reading the answer to the second question, whether Ibsen was really a genius, he asked, "Whose works do you read a lot?" I said, "I don't know English well, so I only read Japanese authors a lot." I listed Natsume, Tokutomi, Takayama, Kikuchi, etc. He asked if I had read Arishima Takeo's works. And at the end of the answer to one of the questions, there was something written after the Kimi Movement (己未運動), and it was unpleasant because asking what the Kimi Movement was seemed too condescending.

"By the way, I can't come here every day like this, and I need to know if I'm accepted or not first so that it's easier for me to come." I started to prepare to retreat. "Yes, please rest assured. You'll definitely be accepted." I thought to myself that I had been accepted. "Bring the membership list form," he told the young man in white, and as ordered, the man in the white durumagi went out somewhere and brought back a form like a student record form from school and placed it in front of me. "Please write as it's written there." When I looked at it, there were sections for permanent address, current residence, date of birth, hobbies, writing, height, exercise, etc. I wrote my hobbies as "nothing special" and exercise as "tennis" roughly and falsely, but I was worried because I couldn't remember what I had written on the resume the other day, so I had to write the same date of birth. I barely guessed and wrote "October 16th" and sat down. This time, he started asking, "Do you know Kim Myŏng-sun?" "Do you know Kim Il-yŏp?" Then he asked, "Do you know Yi Kwang-su?" "Do you know someone named Pang Chŏng-hwan?" It was so interesting that he was asking about Mr. Pang, not knowing that he was sharing a desk with Mr. Pang in the same office. I said, "Yes, I've heard his name, but I don't know who he is in detail." I wished he would say something bad about Mr. Pang, so I said, "Is he a good person?" He said, "Yes, he's a very good person. He's working the hardest on the children's movement and he's a very sincere person." Since there were no insults or jokes, it was boring, so I was about to get up after hearing a few more words. He told me to read a script briefly before leaving. The script he gave me was "Five Siblings," which I had even seen performed. The part I was assigned was a conversation between Kŏn-su, the older brother, and Nan-su, the younger sister. He spoke the older brother's lines in a dramatic narrator's tone and told me to speak the younger sister's lines. Since it was a prank I had already started, I tried to do it for the sake of a story, but it only sounded like I was reading a book and didn't come across as an actress. I tried saying "Well, I don't know" several times in a trembling voice, but I couldn't get the words out. Perhaps feeling frustrated, he said, "Just say it loudly. Just say it loudly. I just need to know the volume." But still, it didn't come out loudly. At this time, a man who was very tall and neatly dressed in a Western suit, with very high-collar hair and looking like a rake, came in and sat on one side, staring and smiling unpleasantly <120>. I thought to myself that such a person would definitely catch my eye. "I'm leaving," I said and tried to get up. "By the way, didn't you go to the library last night?" he asked, so I answered, "No," thinking that he was probably trying to find out if I had gone to the library because I had said that I had looked through books to write the answers earlier.

"If possible, I would be grateful if you could devote yourself to the arts in a dedicated manner until you turn 25 without thinking about anything else or going anywhere else! And after that, it's fine no matter what you do!" He said this very sincerely and earnestly. I felt a little amused inside, but I couldn't help but feel sorry because he was being so sincere.

"These people are so sincere and hardworking, but it's a pity that the outside world only knows that it's a den of reckless men and women." I thought. "However, is it because many of the actresses who come here are kisaeng (Korean courtesans) or 00bu (likely referring to a type of entertainer)? In the end, even those sincere people can't find good actresses, so they end up hearing such unfortunate rumors." I wished that a good, chaste woman would appear in this association as soon as possible.

"I really hope you come tonight. Please come," I heard behind me, and ran back in a hurry, dodging the drizzling autumn rain.

About a week later, I heard from someone that "There was a promising woman, and we were really counting on her, but she disappeared without a word." They were very curious and waiting for me. So, I felt even more sorry and sent a postcard saying, "I couldn't go due to family circumstances."

『女優募集詳細는 來談하시요.』라는 광고가 눈에 뜨일 때 여러분은 엇던 생각이 머리에 떠돔닛가. 굽 놉흔 구두 팔둑시계 새파란 치마 단발 빨강칠한 입술 연애편지 드러잇는 손가방 그런 모던-껄들이 먼저 떠돌고 그러고 독한 향내를 피우고 우스면서 모여드는 그런 여자들을 안내하노라고 시험하노라고 하나씩 마터가지고 싱글거리는 라팔바지의 모던 뽀-이들이 떠돌고 그리고는 그 남녀들이 금방 친해 얼싸안고 쨔스의 춤을 어즈러히 추는 것이 떠돈다고 누구던지 그럽듸다. 정말 그럴까요.
朝鮮劇界에 그 일홈이 놉흔 士0會에서 남녀배우를 모집한다하니 조곰 미안한 일이지만 기자란 名啣은 잠간 감추어 두고 女優志願의 소위 모껄로 변해 가지고 그 내면을 보아오면 독자의 궁금한 마음을 푸러주고 편집장에게 층찬을 밧을가 하야 다소 마음에 겁나는 것을 참으면서 내주제엔 대담한 짓을 시작하엿슴니다.
그 임시사무소를 차저가니 0園洞 沐浴湯 압헤 잇는 족고만 목제 2층집의 웃층이라 좁다란 약한 층계를 올너가닛가 족고만 한칸방에 머리를 축축 따어느린 새악씨 4, 5인이 얌전히 안저서 活動寫眞辯士 가튼 말투로 극연습을 하고 잇습니다 하나가 『아이고 어머니』하면 또 하나가 그보다 더한 변사투로 『글세 이애야 생각을 좀 해보아라.』하면서 주거니 밧거니 하는 것이 처음 보기에 우스우나 그러나 그들은 미리 생각하든 것처럼 斷髮娘도 말광냥이도 아니고 조곰도 잡스런 틔가 업는 순진한 어린 처녀들이엿슴니다. 『저럿케 얌전하고 어린 처녀들이고나.』하고 손으로 내머리를 만즈니 내게는 트레쪽이 달려잇는 것이 그들보다 나희라도＜114＞ 만케 보여서 다소의 안심을 어더가지고 그 처녀들엽흐로 갓슴니다. 그 처녀들이 무에라 하기 전에
