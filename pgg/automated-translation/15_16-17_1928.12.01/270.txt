Modern Women, Modern Professions: New Women's New Professions
Even Disguises and Outings: A Female Reporter's Life
Ch'oe Ŭisun

S sister! Please forgive me. I'm being a bit direct.
I've just finished reading your article and have been sitting here in extreme excitement for quite some time. I haven't been able to cool down and hastily grabbed my pen.
I know that you care deeply about me and have given much thought to my life as a reporter. However, as someone who doesn't have even a small amount of experience as a reporter, you've been so boldly critical of me, who has gained such valuable experience... I have no choice but to elaborate on the life of a female reporter.

Your sentiment that "becoming idle makes one useless!"
"Because one is always in contact with numerous men in many places, it becomes difficult to correct oneself to the habits of a woman, and one cannot avoid spending time on grooming. Therefore, one's mental and physical cultivation, which is only gained through self-reflection, gradually becomes deficient." I absolutely cannot agree with these words.
You say there's plenty of time for grooming? A true reporter hardly has time to eat properly.
However, dashing about like lightning during a storm or acting calmly like dew falling in the quiet dawn, after enduring mental and physical labor, the pleasure one feels, or the mental and physical training one receives indirectly, is immense.
When walking down the street without a specific purpose, others may see one as a "loafer."
However, as a reporter, especially a female reporter, one is acutely aware of even the smallest stories that catch the eye and ear, and one immediately tries to imagine and judge the before and after of the situation. So, one's mind is constantly busy.
Depending on the situation, one might subtly try to read someone's mind, eagerly dig up someone's story, disguise oneself and peek into someone's house, follow suspicious-looking women, or dress neatly and visit respectable homes. At first glance, some may see this as mere entertainment, as you do, sister, and dismiss it as depraved. However, the more one thinks about it calmly, the more one realizes it is a dedicated life with deep meaning. In fact, how much of a hardship is it?
In any case, it's something that no one can do without a passion. It's something that only a woman with keen sensitivity, abundant skill, and great effort can continue to do.
Even while using such talents, the urgent feeling and purpose of revealing and criticizing the terribly bleak facts of reality and trying to effect reform and improvement, make one fearless of any temporary shame, no matter what means one employs.
In this way, considering the continuous exertion of a woman's true, albeit small and lengthy, effort and activity, the complex life of a female reporter, who goes, writes, and reads day and night, is so broad and busy that it seems to be the most mentally and physically distressing of all ordinary women's occupations. However, I firmly believe that the greater the hardship, the greater the reward.
You, sister, are primarily concerned and critical of my ever-changing life from a noble perspective, but I am steadily grasping the true value of my current life as a reporter to the end.
In closing, I ask for your generous understanding of my clear writing.

Message Mediator: A Newspaper Telephone Operator
Chu Kisun

Every morning, I pass the busiest intersection in Seoul, Chongno <127> and go up north to find the two-story brick building standing tall in Chŏndong. I go into a small, somewhat messy room and stand in front of the switchboard that takes up more than half the space, put the receiver to my ear, and act as a matchmaker, allowing the caller and the receiver to speak whenever the signal light flickers. That is my job and my duty.
What special impressions could I, who repeat this life every day, possibly have to offer that you've come to this cramped room to ask? Only when one is engaged in a job that one desires can one have particularly interesting and satisfying impressions. What interesting impressions could someone like me, who is tied to livelihood and forced to work, possibly have? Anyway, since you've come all this way, I will tell you what I have felt from my experience.

To others, this job may seem easy: just sitting still and quickly plugging in switches for this person and that person as the signal lights flicker. Many may think it's not that difficult, but that's a rash assumption by those who don't know the reality of this job. In any case, it's a job that seems easy but has hidden pains.
I am currently working as a private exchange operator at the Chosŏn Ilbo Company, but until a few years ago, I was at the Central Telephone Bureau, which had the most subscribers in Seoul, for some time. Because of that, I have little to no clumsiness in terms of experience or skill in exchanges, so it's safe to say that I have relatively few painful experiences in my duties.
First of all, when I was at the exchange bureau, I had to sit in front of the switchboard from morning to evening, and no matter how quickly I moved my hands, I couldn't keep up with the number of calls, so I would get scolded by customers and scolded by supervisors if I looked away even for a moment. There were many times when I felt wronged and resentful enough to clutch my chest and want to cry out loud, but since I started working at a private exchange, I have been personally pleased that I don't have that kind of severe pain. However, <128> even if the occupational pain is less severe, this is a newspaper company, so there are many cases where every minute and every second counts, and I keenly feel that the responsibility is several times greater than at the exchange bureau, so I can never find peace of mind even when I have some free time.
For example, if a breaking event occurs that warrants the publication of an extra edition, the reporter's call to report this must be connected as quickly as possible, even if it takes only a second.
However, being able to find time to read even a few lines or a few pages of a book in the midst of all this is more than anything else, a happy thing.

Why did I leave the Central Exchange Bureau? What would you do with that information? Still, if it's necessary, I'll tell you... Although it's a job I do for a living, I was so fed up with it, and since my specialty was music, I briefly went to Tokyo to study in that direction at the Toyo Music School for about a year. When that didn't work out, I returned to Korea and got married. I decided to become a working woman again and got a job at the Chosŏn Ilbo Company.

A Female Bank Clerk Wrestling with the Abacus
Kim Sŏnbu

In Korea, being a female bank clerk is still a fairly new profession. I had always admired it as a highly interesting and refined profession, and whenever I thought about career issues during my school days, I infinitely longed to become a bank clerk as my ideal job. Out of so many jobs, why a bank clerk in particular? It seemed to be the most suitable for my temperament, and I had confidence in my aptitude for it. And secondly, I admired being a school teacher. However, being a school teacher was merely a substitute to fill in if my first aspiration failed. It was not an absolute ideal or hope that would completely satisfy my mind without any gaps. Of course, being a bank clerk was a more desired target of admiration than being a teacher.

Then, in the spring of last year, upon graduating from the Second Higher Girls' School, I realized my long-cherished ideal more easily than I had imagined. Of course, the school's arrangement and help were involved, but considering the reality of Korea <129> that I was able to achieve my ideal so easily seemed like a miracle no matter how I thought about it.

Since it hasn't been even a year and a half since I got the job, I don't have much experience in the profession, so it's difficult to say anything definite about my impressions. However, if I were to force myself to say something within the limited range of my direct experience, this job is very physically comfortable because it involves only working with an abacus and ledgers, always running a pen at a desk. There is no particular pain in my duties, but this mental and emotional pain is the same as in other professions.
The heavy responsibility of being in an important position, where I am in charge of the main section and calculate the statistics of the results of the bank's operations, that is, the settlement of accounts, is one of the pains. Repeating the same monotonous work every day is the second pain. And having to be extremely cautious in every move, being a maiden among men, is the third pain.
And while these pains are latent, there are also joys. Gaining social status and working as a working woman is one of the joys. The daily balance sheet coming out well as intended is the second joy. And being trained in a regular and disciplined life is the third joy.
In any case, to sum up my impressions of my profession in one word, no matter how much greater the occupational pain is than the occupational joy, the greatest spiritual joy that makes my entire life satisfying and happy lies in realizing the ideal that I have always longed for and being able to go down that path that suits my temperament.

A Queen of a Fleeting World: A Bus Girl
Ch'oe Sukkyŏng

From 8 AM to 12 PM, from 1 PM to 5 PM, and from 6 PM to 10 PM, I stand in the bus bustling through the complicated streets of Seoul, unable to sit, and shout "All right!" and "Stop!" until my throat hurts, in a three-shift rotation of four hours each day. That is the essence of my job.
As someone who didn't even graduate from a Girls' Higher Common School like others, this might be too much of a generous and appreciated job, but in any case, it's a difficult job that doesn't bring any pleasure or comfort to both the body and the mind. Not only me, but how can anyone who is doing mechanical servitude under the absolute power of a motor powered by electricity, like a motor, expect peace of mind and body? However, when my body is too tired and my heart is hurt, I sometimes think, even knowing it's a useless fantasy, that I wish I had a more comfortable job.
To others, it's a job where you just ride around in a car, so many envy it without knowing the inside story, but in reality, it's a very tiring job, and there are times when I secretly feel sorrow more often than not.
First of all, as those of you who have ridden the bus know, it's very bumpy and shaky, unlike ordinary cars, so the physical pain is beyond words. It's only been a few months since the buses were introduced, so some of you may not know, but some of the customers deliberately tease us, and their so-called *hiyakashi* (teasing) is incredibly severe. They demand that we issue transfer tickets that don't exist, demand discounts on fixed fares, and make all sorts of demands, so there are times when we are sad and resentful enough to cry.
And the thing that hurts us the most is that people in the world who like to talk about others make all sorts of bizarre noises when they see us exchanging even a word with the driver in the car.
Well, please think about it. As people who work together in the same car every day, we are just different in terms of gender, but they are also our colleagues and friends. How can we not exchange a single word? And even if we do talk, why would it seem so strange?
Since the "bus" is still in its early stages, we don't have much *tanryŏn* (training, experience) with customers, and customers don't know our sincerity, so these kinds of impure things happen, but please understand our lives a little more. And I always feel a yearning in my heart for them to treat us a little warmer and more generously.
After saying all this, I don't know if it's an impression of my job or a complaint about my job.
Finally, you ask me to talk about joy, but it hasn't been long since I got the job, and I haven't fully settled in yet, so there's no particular joy that I can boast about, either professionally or mentally. However, if I were to force myself to find joy in my life, it would be the extraordinarily kind friendship of my fellow workers, who are in the same situation and work in the same place! There's one joy that I feel from that, and that's the joy of running home with my pay envelope, which is the reward for my efforts, in my hand every 21st of every month.

珠盤과 씨름하는 女子銀行員
金仙婦

朝鮮에서 여자은행원하면 그래도 꽤 새로운 직업이요. 상당히 흥미잇슬 고상한 직업으로 알어 온 나는 재학당시부터 직업문제를 생각할 때마다 나의 이상적 직업으로 이 은행원 되기를 무한히 동경하엿슴니다. 허로만흔 직업 중에 유독 은행원이엿스리요만은 이것이 제일 나의 심성에 적합할 것 갓햇고 자신할 만한 소질이 잇섯기 때믄이엿슴니다. 그리고 둘재로 동경한 것이 학교 교원이엿슴니다. 그러나 학교교원은 제일의 憧憬이 만일의 경우에 결렬될 때에 그를 충당식힐한앗 補選에 지나지 안엇지 나의 마음을 조곰도 빈틈업시 전적으로 만족식힐 절대한 이상적 동경이나 희망은 아니엿슴니다. 물론 은행원 그것이 교원보다도 더 지극히 바라는 동경의 관역(的)이엿든 까닭임니다.

그리다가 작년 봄에 第二高等女學校를 졸업하자 상상보다는 너무도 쉬웁게 오래동안 동경하든 이상을 실현하게 되엿슴니다. 물론 학교의 주선과 힘도 잇섯지만 朝鮮의＜129＞ 현실을 놋코 볼 때에 나의 이상대로 그럿케 손쉬웁게 된 것은 아모리 생각하여도 기적과 각햇슴니다.

아즉 취직한 지가 불과 1년 반을 더 넘지 못한지라 별로 직업상 체험이 업서서 이럿타할 만한 감상을 확정해 말슴드리기는 어려우나 직접 경험한 고 범위 안에서 억지로라도 말슴한다면 제일 이 직업은 주산과 장부만 가지고 책상 압헤서 늘 펜만 달니는 일인 까닭에 몸은 대단히 편한 직업이여서 직무상 특별한 고통은 조곰도 업스나 이 亦 마음의 고통 정신의 고통은 다른 직업이나 맛찬가지임니다.
내가 主係課에 잇는 까닭에 여럿이 分掌해 보는 일이나 行務의 결과즉 결산의 통계를 내는 중대한 자리에 잇는 이만치 그 책임의 중한 것이 그 고통의 하나이요 날마다 변화업는 단조로운 일을 되푸리 하는 것이 그 고통의 둘이요 처녀도 남자들 틈에 석겨 지내노라니 一動一靜에 극히 勤愼하여야 하는 것이 그 고통의 셋임니다.
그리고 이러한 고통이 잠재해 잇는 반면에 또한 깃븜을 주는 것은 나도 한 직업부인으로서의 사회적 지위를 어더 활동하는 것이 그 깃븜의 하나이요. 날마다 日計表가 뜻과 갓치 잘마저지는 것이 그 깃븜의 둘이요. 일정하게 규칙적 생활의 훈련을 밧어나가는 것이 그 깃븜의 셋임니다.
하여간 내 직업에 대한 감상을 한마대로 끈어 말하면 직업상 고통이 직업상 깃븜 그것보다 여하히 크다고 하드라도 내가 늘 동경하든 이상을 실현하야 내 심성에 맛는 그 길로 나갈 수 잇는 거기에 정신상 최대의 희열이 내 생활 전체를 만족식히는 행복을 만드러 주고 잇슴니다.

한 間 世上의 女王 뻐-쓰껄
崔淑敬

오전 8시부터 12시까지 오후 1시부터 5시까지 저녁 6시부터 밤 10시까지 매일 4시간식 3차레의 순번교환으로 복잡한 서울의 거리(街)를 까불거리는 뻐쓰차 속에서 안지도 못하고 슨 채로 『오라잇』 『스톱』을 목이 압흐게 부르는 것이 내 직업의 정체임니다.
남과 가티 女子高等普通學校도 졸업하지 못한 나로서는 이것도 너무 과대한 고마운 직업일는지 모로나 하여간 육체와 정신 양방면에 조곰도 유쾌와 편안을 느기지 못하는 까다러운 직업임니다. 나뿐 아니라 누구이나 전기의 위력으로 도라가는 모타와 가티 팡의 절대한 위력 밋헤서 기계적 노예질을 하는 사람으로서 심신의 安逸을 엇더케 바라겟슴닛가만은 하도 몸이 괴롭고 마음히 상할 때에는 쓸데업는＜130＞ 공상인 줄 알면선도 좀더 편한 직업을 가저보앗스면 하는 생각도 남니다.
남이 보기에는 자동차만 타고 단이는 직업이닛가 속모르고 부러워하는 이도 만흐나 실상은 몹시 피곤해지는 직업이면서 남모르게 비애를 늣기는 적이 여간 만치 안슴니다.
우선 여러분 중에 타보신 분이면 다아시는 바와 가티 보통자동차와 다르게 몹시 까불까불 흔들니는 가닭에 육체적 고통은 말도 말고라도 뻐쓰차가 생긴지가 불과 몃 달이 되지 안으닛가 모르고 그러시는 분도 게시겟스나 각금 손님 중에는 불량하신 분이 일부러 우리들을 조롱하노라고 소위 히야까시가 엇지 심한지 모름니다. 업는 乘換票를 내라는 등 일정한 賃金을 割引하자는 등 별별 성화가 다 잇서서 어느 때는 눈물이 나도록 슯흐고 원통한 경우를 당하는 때가 적지 안슴니다.
그리고 세상에 남의 말하기 조와하는 사람들은 차속에서 운전수와 말 한마대라도 하는 것을 보면 별별 괴악한 소리를 다 지여내는 것이 우리의 제일 가슴쓰린 일임니다.
글세 여러분도 생각을 좀 해보십시요. 날마다 한차속에서 갓치 從務하는 사람으로서 남녀의 구별만 달엇지 그도 동지요 갓흔 친우인 이상 엇더케 말 한마대 안하고 지낼 수가 잇스며 또 설사 말을 한다기로서니 그것이 그럿케 이상스럽게 보일 것이 이 무엇임니가?
아즉 『뻐쓰』가 생긴지 초기에 잇는 까닭에 우리도 손님과의 鍛鍊이 적고 손님도 우리의 진정을 모르시는 까닭에 뭔말이라도 그러한 불순한 일이 생기는 것이나 우리들의 생활을 좀더 이해하여 주십시요. 그리고 좀더 따듯하게 좀더 너그럽게 대해주섯스면 하는 생각이 일상 가슴에서 용소슴침니다.
여기까지 말슴하고 보닛가 직업에 대한 감상인지 직업에 대한 하소연인지 모를만치 되엿슴니다.
