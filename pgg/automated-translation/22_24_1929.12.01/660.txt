A Glimpse of Korean Poetry
						An Ch'ijae-san

(I) Beginning

To say that Korean poetry only consists of the single format of Sijo is inadequate. Even so, it is unacceptable to say that Korean literature in the thousands of years of the Orient has only that format. Likewise, to say that Korean music has no instrumental pieces and only songs is also unacceptable, as it is unlikely that music played for thousands of years only consisted of Sijo. Solving this doubt might seem like a trivial matter.

I have been struggling for years to understand this, and for the past five years, I have been drafting a history of court music. In doing so, I took the opportunity to pore over old books in the royal household and investigate major musical scores. Gradually, I was able to find over two thousand songs. By uprooting the source and delving into the depths of the poetry, I discovered that there were various formats of ancient songs. However, since the accumulation of thousands of years of decline is so vast and originally chaotic, it would be futile for my humble opinion to attempt a solitary correction. Therefore, I am communicating with Pyŏl Kŏn’gon to report this to the world, hoping that the members of the new academic community will not neglect this but will jointly strive to understand it.

(II)

With the over two thousand poems I have already investigated, I have counted the verses, traced the threads, and standardized the poetic forms, melodies, and musical measures. By categorizing their rhythmic patterns, they can be broadly divided into the following types:

Types of Poetry
1. Samdaemok Style
2. Chŏngŭp Style
3. Repetitive Verse Style
4. Kyŏnggi Style
5. Long Poem
6. Sijo

(I) According to the Samguk Sagi (History of the Three Kingdoms), the Sinra (Silla) chancellor, Wihŭng, gathered the scattered Hyangga (native songs) of the time with the monk Tae’go and created a book titled Samdaemok. <166> Although that book is now lost, there are twenty-five Hyangga extracted from the Kyunyŏjŏn in the Samguk Yusa (Memorabilia of the Three Kingdoms) and Kyobunkiyŏnt'ongch'o, which have already been explained by Mr. Ogura Shinpei and are well-known to the world. These are papers that cannot be easily dismissed. The poetic form of these twenty-five poems has a fixed structure, and this format is called Samdaemok style. It uses the rhythmic beauty of the four-four meter and writes eight-syllable counts. In large poems, a special supplementary verse is added, which is called the closing verse. The Samdaemok style was also imitated in the Koryŏ period.

◇Short Song of Yejong
"Juŭlwanho paekho simmun chech'ŏnŭl gŭpkun honjosi kŏsagi chung samjosi kyojik ma u mangmang ari rallagŭppi kagongsin yanggun najigŭnhŭnjosi chŏng"
This was found in the biography of Sin Sunggyŏm and the Sinssi Ch'uwŏnrok, and its meaning is that Yejong mourned Sin Sunggyŏm and Kim Nak, meritorious subjects who died during the reign of King T'aejo. In addition, the song "Chinsak," found in the Akhak Kwebŏm (Canon of Music), is considered to be a poem called "Chŏng Kwajŏng," written by Chŏng Sŏ of Koryŏ, and it is also a imitation of the Samdaemok style.

(II) Chŏngŭp Style. The northern literature of the Three Kingdoms period was mostly romantic. A representative poem of this is the Chŏngŭpsa (Song of Chŏngŭp), which was written by a woman from Chŏngŭp-hyŏn in Paekche, lamenting the absence of her husband who had gone out to trade. It was discovered by referring to the Akhak Kwebŏm and the Koryŏsa Akchi (History of Koryŏ Music).

(III) Repetitive Verse Style is a variation of the Chŏngŭp Style that appeared in the Koryŏ Dynasty. It does not have refrains, but only repeats important phrases with sustained intonation. Examples include Chŏng Sŏkka, Ch'oyongga, Pŏksanggok, Ŏbucha, Ponghwangŭm, Samogok, Mundŏkkok, and Mandjŏnch'un Pyŏlsa. It is similar to the repetitive verse style developed during the Tang Dynasty under Emperor Xuanzong.

(IV) Kyŏnggi Style is a type of repetitive verse style. It equalizes the number of syllables with three or four syllables, and repeats the places where the meaning is strong. It is characterized by the exemplary use of the words "Kyŏnggi" and "wi". It originated in the naturalistic wave literature of Koryŏ. An example is the Hanrim Pyŏlgok.

This style of writing continued until the reign of King Chungjong of the Chosŏn Dynasty. The songs in Koryŏ An Hwi's (Kŭn’irjip), "Kwandong Pyŏlgok," "Chukgye Pyŏlgok," Pyŏn Kyeryang's "Hwasan Pyŏlgok," Kwŏn Kŭn's "Sangdae Pyŏlgok," "Oryunnorae," "Yŏnhyŏngjegok," Chŏng Kŭgin's "Pulbunhŏngok," Kim Ku's "Hwadaen Pyŏlgok," and the songs in Chu Se-bung's "Muryŭngjapgo" are all in this system.

(V) Long Poem is an advancement of the Samdaemok style. It uses the four-four meter and creates eight-syllable rhymes. The introduction is grand, the arrangement is extravagant, and it is an unlimitedly long text. The long songs in the Samguk Sagi, such as the "Haerongjŏn" and "Sillaejŏn," O Se-jae's "Yŏktaega" in Koryŏ, and in Chosŏn, "Sangch'un’gok" in Pulbunhŏnjip, Songgang’s "Samin’gok," No Gye's "Sŏnsangt'an," and Kim Ŏiam's "Hwaryusŏ," "Ch'up'ung Kamgyŏlbok," etc., are numerous, making it impossible to list them all here.

(VI) Sijo originated in the mid-Koryŏ period and is a short poem of six lines with eight syllables. Comparing it to the Greek Rhapsody's hexameter, and translating two lines as one line of Chinese poetry, it can be compared to the three-line forms of Tang poetry such as "Kan’ŭnda" and "Hwansŏksa." It developed from around the time of King Chungjong of the Chosŏn Dynasty and became very popular. Collections of these Sijo include Chang U-byŏk's "Kajip," O Hŭi-sang's "Haeyu Kayo," Kim Cha-ik's "Chŏnggaryult'e," and An Min-yŏng's "Haedong Akchang," which are famous.

(III) Melody and Musical Measure

The value of poetry is fully realized when accompanied by musical expression. Therefore, in order to thoroughly study poetry today, it is necessary to compare the melody and musical measure. To explain this, one must understand five key elements: tone, leaf, melody, tune, and rhythm.

(I) Tone refers to the alteration of high and low notes to create music. It is based on the differences in the breadth of the five-note scale (Kung, Shang, Kak, Chi, Woo) and has two melodic methods: P'yŏngjo (level mode) and KyeMyŏnjo (changing mode).

P'yŏngjo scale: Kung ○○○ Woo ○ Chi ○ Kak ○○ Shang ○ Kung
KyeMyŏnjo scale: Kung ○ Woo ○○ Chi ○ Kak ○ Shang ○○ Kung

○一 is one step, an ordinary interval, and ○○二 is one and a half steps, a slightly wider interval. The principle of changing these intervals is a matter of music theory, so I will omit it here. It is sufficient to simply note the difference in scale between the two modes.

(II) Leaf refers to the shortness of the melody. In ancient times, the ups and downs of the song were compared to the branches and leaves of plants. Liu Nu's Sŏkmyŏng says, "The human voice is called a song, and the song is like branches, showing its quality; singing with sound has ups and downs, like grass and trees have branches and leaves." Here, one musical measure is called a leaf, and one song is divided into musical measures such as one leaf, five leaves, and six leaves.

(III) Melody refers to the entire length of the melody. When repeating the melody, it is sometimes divided into three parts: front melody, middle melody, and rear melody, as in Chinsak and Pukjŏn. In addition, when a song and a musical instrument are played together, there are times when the singing stops and only the musical instrument plays at the melody, leaf, or musical measure. This is called yŏŭm (residual sound).

(IV) Tune is the combination of middle sections of a song to create one piece. The collection of these pieces is called a t'aesu (suite). The number of t'aesu in one tune is unlimited. A recent song consists of twenty-four t'aesu, and this is called one kwŏl.

(V) Rhythm: The most important aspect of singing is the beat. It is common to tap the knees with both hands, but the original method is to use a cane to regulate the progress of the singing. The rhythm of any song is divided into three speeds: slow, medium, and fast. It starts slowly, speeds up in the middle, and rushes to a climax at the end. Please refer to my article in Buddhism No. 63.

(IV) Poetry and National Character

In conclusion, one word about the relationship of national character. In general, it is a very important matter to inquire into what kind of spirit is embodied in the image of the national mind painted in poetry.

The characteristic of the Korean people is their inherent gentleness. Compared to things, it is neither as transparent as crystal nor as opaque as a stone. It is like a precious jade, with a warm glow and a pure appearance. <169> Chinese poems have complex rhymes and rhythms, and Japanese poems have simple phrases with many sudden emotional expressions. However, Korean poetry, due to the gentleness of its inherent nature, has a natural and pure taste without extremes. The syllable count is four-four, using eight characters, which comes from the natural rhythm of breathing, the natural beat of the mouth. The content depicts an extremely optimistic temperament.

The presence of this optimistic flavor is the manifestation of a harmonious temperament like jade light, expressing the equal thought of oneness with all things. However, this innate idea of equality calls for imbalance and floats upward in order to demand absolute equality through external obstacles. Therefore, general ancient art is neither the beauty of form like the Chinese nor the beauty of color like the Japanese, but only the beauty of line. Line is a state of aiming from one point to another, neither active nor calm, but floating. Also, the tone of singing poetry is entirely accompanied by a sorrowful sound. This sad tone of singing and the floating artistic beauty are neither desolate disappointment nor chaotic disorder, but a wave state that seeks to reach the realm of thorough equality. In light of this, the progress of literature is that the construction of a new ideal world arises from the violent chaos of imbalance. Therefore, Korean literature is a movement of pure equality, and its future is vast and expansive. (The End) <170>

朝鮮歌詩의 苗昹
						安自山
(一) 發端
거시키 朝鮮歌詩의 文套는 單거리 時調만잇다하기는 斟酌에 未洽하다 그래도 數千年東方에서 이러타한 朝鮮人의 文學이 외그리 套式으로만된게라할수업고 거시키 朝鮮音樂은 樂曲이업고 모도 歌曲이라 그러면 數千年노라오든 音樂도 唯獨히 時調만불넛슬이가 萬無하다 이 疑端을 解惑키는 시럽지 魚池間치안이한 公事라할지라.
余는 이걸 透得키로 積年間汨突할새 近者五年間은 雅樂史를 起草함에 投托한바 그 要代에 乘時하야 宮家舊書를 涉獵하고 大樂曲調를 廉探하야 次漸次漸二千屢百篇의 歌詩를 査得하게된지라 이로써 拔本塞源하야 歌詩의 裏許를 講磨하니 이제사 古歌의 各色文套가잇슴을 發覺한지라 그러나 數千年落華가 원낙 深遼하고 本來왁자한게라 小拙한 所見으론 焉敢히 單獨歸正을짓기는부질업슨일이라 故로 別乾坤에 連通하야 이걸 天下에 申白하노니 幸혀 新學界의 僉位는 小歇히치말고 共同으로 解得하길 發願하노라.

(三) 旋律과 樂節
歌詩는 音樂的表白에 伴하야 其價値가 完全히 發現되는것이라 故로 今에 歌詩를 典實히 工究하자면 旋律과 樂節을 比準함이 可하니 그것을말하자면 調葉腔曲 節奏等 五條目이 大頭腦로알것이니라.
(一) 調는 音의 高低를 變更하야 作曲된것을 謂함이니 그는 五音宮商角徵羽의 音階上廣狹의 差異를 準據하야 平調界面調二旋法이잇나니
平調音階 宮○○羽○徵○角○○商○宮
界面調音階 宮○羽○○徵○角○商○○宮
○一은 一段으로서 普通間隔이오 ○○二는 一段半으로서 少廣한 間隔이라 이間隔變更되는 理致는 樂理問題라 故로 今에는 此를 略하니 아직은 二調의 音階差異만 眼標함이 足하니라.
(二) 葉은 旋律의 短小함을 謂함이니 古者에는 歌音의 上下를 草木의 柯葉에 比함이잇스니 劉怒의 釋名에 人聲日歌歌柯也所歌之言見其質也以聲吟咏有上下如草木之有柯葉也라함이 此라 그런데 一樂節을 葉이라하매 歌一篇을 一葉又五葉六葉等으로 樂節을 區分함이잇나니라.
(三) 腔 旋律의 全長을 謂함이라 腔을거푸함에는 前腔 中腔 後腔의 三同으로치기도하나니 眞勺 北殿等이 其例라 그런데 歌와 樂器를 合奏할 時에 腔이던지 葉이던지 或時樂節에 至하야 歌를 停止하고 樂器만 奏함이잇스니 이를 餘音이라하나니라.
(四) 曲은 歌의 中篇을 組合하야 一篇을 作한것이니 그 衆篇을 套數라하는지라 一曲의 套數는 限이업스니 近來歌曲이란것은 二十四套數로 成한것이 매 此를 일闕이라하나니라.
(五) 節奏 歌法에 가장 重要히치는것은 拍子라 이拍子는 兩手로 膝을 打함이 普通이나 原法은 杖䜵로써 歌聲進行을 整齊하는것이라 其節奏는 何種의 歌던지 慢中數(삭) 三機에 分하니 最初는 徐緩하다가 中間에서는조곰 速하고 最後에는 急促하야모라치는것이니 拙者의 論文佛敎六十三號를 參照하라.
(四) 歌詩와 民族性
終末에 磨勘으로 一言할것은 民族性의 係關이라 大體歌詩는 民族心像의 畵本에매처잇는 精神이 果然如何한가함을 探知코자함은 未賞不喫緊의 事라할지라.
朝鮮人의 性格은 本來溫順한것이 特徵이라 物에 比하면 水晶가티 透明치도안코 石塊가티 暗濁치도안타 卽美玉가＜169＞티 溫潤의 光과 淸淨한 相이라 漢人의 詩는 複雜한 廉과 韻이잇고 日本詩는 單調의 排句로서 突發的感情語가 多하다 그러나 朝鮮人의 歌詩는 本性의 溫潤함에 因하야 極端味가업시 自然的의 純平한 味가잇스니 音數는 四四調로서 八字를 用하매 이는 生理上呼吸의 强弱인 天然的口拍子로나온것이오 內容은 極히 樂天的情性을 描寫함이 一手니라.
그樂天的의 風味가잇슴은 卽玉光가티 圓融한 情性이 物我一體의 平等思想을 發揮함이라 그런데 그 先天的의 平等思想은 外觀的事物의 支障으로써 더욱더욱 絶對的平等을 要求키 爲하야 不平을 呼하며 浮動的으로나감이업지못하다 故로 一般古藝術은 漢人가티 形美나 日本人처럼 色美도안이라 오직 線美가잇스니 線이란것은 一點에서 他點에 向往하는 狀態라 卽 活動도안이오 沈靜도안이오 거시기 浮動的이라 한 歌詩를 唱하는 音調는 一體로 哀怨聲을 帶한지라 이 悲調의 歌聲과 浮動的 藝術美는 凄凉한 落望이안이오 無秩序한 散亂도안이라 透徹히 平等界에 到達코자하는 波動狀態라 보건대 文學의 進步는 不平의 矯激狂亂이 起하여야 新理想境을 建設하는것이니 故로 朝鮮人의 文學은 純全한 平等의 運動으로서 前途가 活大코 洋洋타하노라.(完)＜170＞
