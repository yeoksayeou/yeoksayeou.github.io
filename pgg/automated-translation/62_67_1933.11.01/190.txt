A Record of Escaping a Bandit Den
						Choe Su-bok

"Baby, why are you crying? The dog is barking in the next village. Drink your milk and don't cry, okay." No matter how much the mother coaxed her young baby not to cry, adults would make clicking noises with their tongues while spending the night in the sorghum field in June, so how could a two-year-old baby endure it? The sound of the dog barking in the next village and the baby's crying made her heart pound, and she couldn't even breathe deeply. Trembling, she felt so afraid that she couldn't even move if bandits were to come to capture them right then and there. After about 20 minutes, a light flickered about 300 paces away! (The bandit's cigarette light!)

"Oh, save a person's life." In a panic, the mother carried the baby and ran down to the lower valley. The mother had to flee because of the baby, but my father and I couldn't stay there either. It sounded like those guys were coming after hearing the crying, so now we had to employ the thirty-six stratagems. We ran eastward, stepping wherever our feet led us. We stumbled on stones, got pricked by thorns, and fell several times, but we didn't even feel the pain and just kept running. After running for a while, a voice suddenly shouted in front of us, like a thunderbolt:

"Shwei ya!" (In Korean, "Who is it?")
"Ha!"
A gun was already pointed at my chest. (Not the guys who were following us, but different bandits.) Now I couldn't run anymore, and I could only stare blankly at the sky. All four of them had long rifles (長銃), and without question, ropes (捕繩) were thrown at my father and me. And they pointed their guns, telling us to go somewhere. As we went into the terrifying darkness, sweat streamed down my hemp clothes, and my heart pounded like it was being hammered. After walking for what seemed like forever, we crossed three mountain passes (嶺) as tall as mountains, and the first rooster crowed in a distant village.

We kept going and going, through endless valleys like small intestines, and through thick forests that seemed to pierce the sky. Now dawn was breaking, and the sky was getting bright, but we couldn't tell where we were, and we couldn't distinguish east, west, south, or north. We had walked so much all night that our legs ached, and our feet were blistered, making it impossible to take even a small step. So, I sat down on the ground, saying I couldn't go anymore. The bandits couldn't help it, and they couldn't leave us behind. One of the older ones took something rustling out of his bag, dissolved a white substance in something like a syringe needle, and told us to get a shot. Reader, don't be surprised. This needle is an opium needle. Which spirit (靈) would refuse? My father also got a shot. The bandits were in a hurry, so they couldn't smoke opium, but they took raw opium as big as a red bean, put it in their mouths, and drank water. After resting for about 20 minutes, they urged us to go again. I struggled, saying I couldn't go anymore, and cried. One of them came at me and hit me mercilessly with a club. My father looked at me, and I looked at my father, and we started walking, feeling sorry for each other. After walking about 1 li (里), for some reason, my legs suddenly didn't hurt as much as they had before. My body felt light, like I could fly away. The bandits told me that this was because of the opium needle.
<42>

We kept going and going, and it was all mountains. And more mountains! Now the sun was shining brightly, and it was probably breakfast time. Sometimes we heard the sounds of unknown animals that we had never heard before, which made the mountains seem to shake, but the bandits had guns, so they weren't afraid at all. Also, there was no way that enemy soldiers would be in this deep mountain. So, the bandits relaxed and talked casually, but at the top of a mountain, one of them shouted,

"Kouling!"
The whole mountain echoed with the shout. This is a "password" (口令). The "password" is called Kouling in Chinese. The bandits also had passwords, just like the military. From this side, they replied with

"Ching!"
as the password and then went up towards that side without saying anything. When we got there, the guard looked quite like a soldier. He was carrying a rifle, had a cartridge belt full of ammunition, and even had binoculars. He also had a badge on his left arm that said "Save the Country, Love the People" (救國愛民). These were bandits who were using the so-called Volunteer Army (義勇軍), which emerged after the Manchurian Incident, as a front to commit robbery. If you look at the inside story of the Volunteer Armies that are still in various places today, they are purely bandit gangs.

The guard (the following line is unreadable) the valley again.

It was about nine in the morning. Whether it was because the opium from dawn had worn off, my legs ached again, my bound arms were numb, and I was so hungry that I fell to the ground, saying I couldn't go anymore. The bandits couldn't help it, and one of the stronger-looking ones carried me on his back.

Before long, we heard a dog barking in the distance. I suddenly thought, "Aha, so this is the bandit's lair."

Five or six houses were built comfortably under the shade of trees that were hundreds of years old. This is the bandit's main den.

When we entered, it wasn't originally a den but ordinary people's houses. They dragged us to the room where the bandit leader was, and they saluted him, making a fuss as if they had done something great. Then they told us to salute too. So, we reluctantly gave a half-hearted salute. The leader looked to be in his forties, with a dark blue face and eyes like an ox. He looked much taller than me and looked like "Mussolini" (뭇소리니) that I had seen in the newspaper. The room smelled dirty, and there were five pistols and two rifles hanging on the wall. The leader looked at us and ordered us to kneel down. Then he lay down and smoked opium, and a pretty woman who looked to be in her twenties continuously offered him opium. The bandits filled the room. Among them, there were those who cooked rice, those who fed the horses, and those who fetched water, each doing their assigned tasks. They dragged us to some dark room. I was shocked when I saw the room. The large room was filled with captured people. There were about ten of our Korean compatriots, including four people from the same village. Myeong-ho, who was twelve years old this year and went to school with me, had also been captured (about ten days <43> ago). They all looked at us with surprised eyes. We weren't allowed to speak to anyone we knew. We could only look at each other and shed tears. After a while, they brought us rice, which was a mixture of rice and beans (강낭밥사*) with only soybean paste (된장). But since we were hungry from the long journey, we ate our fill. After eating, I felt a little better. I looked around the room and saw that some people had their ears pierced with wire (쇠사실) and others had their noses pierced. This was the punishment for not paying the money on the first deadline, they said. And the groans and sighs of more than half of the sick people, along with the stench, pricked my nose. If I had to stay here for even a month, I felt like I would die on my own, even if the bandits didn't kill me. Night fell. Many thoughts shook my head, and I couldn't sleep at all.

"What happened to my mother and young baby that day? Did those vicious guys capture them? If they weren't captured, where are they now?"
I just cried, thinking about this and that. If I fell asleep even vaguely, I would only have terrible dreams of being beaten by bandits. When I shouted "Ugh!" (으악!), the bandit next to me would beat me with a club.

It was the next day. One of the bandits took my father out. My father didn't come back even by lunchtime. So, I asked one of the bandits discreetly,

"Your father has been sent home, but you will only be released if you send 200 won within 15 days."
Hearing this, I was so dumbfounded that I burst into tears. How much must my father have cried when he left me behind? The people in the room were all teary-eyed. Last year, our family had all of our food and clothes taken away in the Great Sword Society Riot (大刀會亂), and this year, we barely started farming, struggling to make ends meet with empty hands (赤手). Two hundred won? In a life where we don't even have two won, I felt dizzy just thinking about it. I don't know about the Chinese people who were captured in this den, but most of our Korean compatriots are poor people like us. Then why did these guys capture us, knowing that we are poor people? It's because during last year's Volunteer Army Riot, people who had some money had all moved to the Yeonseon area (沿線方面), regardless of whether they were Chinese or Korean. The only people left are those who have nothing to rely on, like us, so they just barely get by each day, knowing that they will inevitably suffer the bandits' wrath. Still, they do this with the excuse that they can raise at least 120 won if they just capture someone.

A day passed, then two days. As the days went by, more and more people were captured, and the groans of those who were being tortured weighed on my heart and gave me goosebumps. Then one day, one of the bandits came into our room and

"Are there any of you who can write?"
he asked. So, we all just looked at each other. Then one of our Korean compatriots

"That kid writes fast and well,"
he said, pointing at me. I didn't know what was going on, but I followed him to the leader's room. It wasn't anything special, but because they were capturing so many people these days, they had a lot of correspondence to send to various places, so they had hired someone, he said. So, I pretended to refuse but then sat down. The leader asked about my family's situation and then said that he wouldn't take any money from my family and treated me kindly. I thought to myself, <44> "This is the right thing to do (일이 바로 ****)," and breathed a sigh of relief. Then he offered me delicious food. In this bandit den, everyone from the leader down was ignorant and uneducated, so it seemed like one person couldn't possibly handle all the correspondence (一切去來). They send threatening letters (脅迫狀) to the rich people in various places, with a chicken feather (닭의 짓) stuck on the envelope. The method of sending it is as follows: If it's going east, they go east and deliver it to the first house. Then that house delivers it to the next house, and from this village to that village, they quickly run and deliver it without any delay. It is written that the chicken feather means to deliver it quickly, and anyone who delays even for a moment will be shot. Even though they write these threatening letters, if the scribe (서긔놈) makes a draft (草), they write it exactly the same, no matter how many copies there are, and only change the amount of money, so it wasn't that difficult to write. Not only that, but judging by how the incoming correspondence comes in like a flurry, it seems like this bandit leader is the commander-in-chief (총사령) and that branch troops (支隊) are scattered everywhere. Now I had at least gained some freedom inside, so I had a chance to escape, but I couldn't tell where my home was, so I couldn't escape on my own. As I was living like that for about ten days, a notice came in that the military unit that had been stationed about 60 li away had departed for a distant place, so these guys were spending their days gambling and smoking opium without a care in the world.

This was it. A golden opportunity (千載一遇의 好機會) to escape. The guys who had been engrossed in gambling for four or five nights, and had also been smoking opium, were exhausted. On this night, they put the responsibility on the four guys guarding us and all fell asleep. I pretended to be asleep and looked around. The guys who were guarding us were also nodding off and eventually fell asleep. So, I tried to get up, but for some reason, it felt like something was pressing down on my whole body, and I couldn't get up. But with the determination that I wouldn't miss this opportunity, I got up slowly, taking shallow breaths, with all my strength. The first thing I saw was a pistol next to the leader. I thought I would grab it (I had learned how to use a gun here), shoot the leader first, then shoot everyone else. I pointed the gun at the guy's face. Now, all I had to do was pull the trigger, and bang! But for some reason, my hand was shaking, and I couldn't bring myself to pull the trigger. I don't know exactly which night it was, but it was probably the 14th or 15th night. The bright moonlight was shining on these guys, and when I saw them sleeping without knowing anything in this situation where I was pointing a gun at their faces, I couldn't bring myself to do it, so I thought to myself, "Well, these are people too, so I can't."

So, I put the gun down and sneaked out of the prison cell and peeked through the door. On this night, the guards were also snoring, and when I saw the people poking each other next to them so they couldn't sleep, I knew that they weren't going to miss this opportunity to escape. So, I quietly entered the room, and six people used hand gestures and eye movements to ask me to quickly untie them. Whoever was untied would get up and untie the others. In a short time, everyone was untied. Fearing that they would follow us if this was discovered, we took all the weapons that were hanging in this room and that room, without leaving a single one, and gave seven guns to the seven people who knew how to use them. As we were about to bury the rest next to the house, we saw a shadow of a person moving quickly on the north wall. The people were <45> at a loss for what to do when they saw it. The shadow was gradually approaching. Thinking that this was definitely someone who had noticed and was coming, I slowly approached the shadow, holding a pistol, and said in a low but forceful voice,

"I'll shoot if you move!"
"... ..."
The guy couldn't say anything and just trembled. So, we caught the guy and made him our guide. (In reality, it seems like that guy came out to pee.) It was about eleven at night. The moon was shining brightly in the sky, and we could see the road well, but fearing that we would be discovered by the guard (步哨) at the top of the mountain if we went on the road, we started running aimlessly southward, where there was no road. That night was twice as hard as when we were captured, but we crossed mountains and streams in one breath until the rooster crowed, as if we were flying with the joy of escaping. Now that there was no fear of being captured by those guys, we were about to take a break when

from somewhere, we heard
"Shwei ya!"
"... ..."
"Shwei ya!"
"... ..."
"Shwei ya!"
"... ..."
Bang! Bang! Bang! (They ask three times, and if there is no answer, they shoot.) Aha, so we met again. Now that we have guns on our side too, we had no choice but to fight back desperately (應戰). Thinking that they knew we were on their side because they were shooting from the top of the mountain, I went forward, shooting. The guy had escaped somewhere, but I saw the guard from the bandit den we had escaped from in his place. We were shocked. After running all night, we had ended up back at that bandit den.

Feeling angry, we simultaneously opened fire and launched a siege. Shouting "Agh!" (악악), we burst into that den. When we went in, everyone had escaped without a single person. They had been so frantic that they had just barely escaped, leaving behind money, opium bundles, and all kinds of equipment. We picked six horses out of about 50 horses and loaded them with the buried guns and essential equipment and started going again. The faint sound of gunshots that we occasionally heard in the distance made our group walk even faster. As it got light, the guide seemed to be leading us on the right path. We couldn't see any houses all day, but as the sun was setting, we saw houses of our Korean compatriots scattered here and there. Having survived after almost dying, it felt like seeing our father and mother when we met our Korean compatriots, and we were happy and tearful at the same time.

The road ahead was still about 50 li, they said. But the group decided to reach our home that night, no matter how tired we were. As we got closer to home, the familiar road came into view, and we walked even faster once we got here. We arrived near home before the first rooster crowed, we had walked so fast. Strangely enough, we couldn't hear any dogs barking in our village. With a hurried heart, I ran into the house. Ah! What is this? My father and mother were gone, and the house was empty. The door was covered with weeds. I called out to my father and mother and cried for a long time. I didn't know where they had gone, and there was no way to ask anyone this night, so our group spent the night here and there in the empty house. The next morning, I asked a Chinese person nearby early in the morning, and he said that the whole village had fled to XX County (XX縣) (30 li from here) because they couldn't stand the bandits' harassment. Hearing that, I was relieved. And they returned all the horses and goods to me. But I refused, arguing that we should all take them together, but they didn't listen (안들은).

"You Korean people should divide it among yourselves," they said, and then they each went their own way.
That day, I found my house and burst into the room. My father and mother were crying because of me, not even knowing who was opening the door.

"Mother, I'm back."
"... ..."
They wiped away their tears and looked up, then ran towards me with a start,

"My son, what is this? Is it a dream or reality?"
they said, and just cried. My father cried too, and I cried along with them. The next day, XX County (縣) Chief gave me many awards (상이 나리엇다) as a reward (報酬) for saving so many lives and seizing weapons. Also, the bandits' horses and equipment were divided equally among our Korean compatriots who came out together.

After a few days, many more gifts came to our house, sent by the Chinese people who had survived because of me. Not only that, but they always helped us with material things, so our family, which had been so poor, is now living as well as anyone else. This is an unforgettable memory (追憶) in my life.
(August 22, 1933, Heunggyeong, Manchuria)
Written by Choe Su-bok

그 날 집을 차저 방문을 왈칵 여니 아버지, 어머니는 나 때문에 울으시면서 누가 방문을 여는지 그것도 모르고 울으시는 중이다.
「어머니 내가 왓습니다.」
「... ...」
눈물을 싯고 바라보시더니 와락 달녀드시면서
「내 아들이 이게 웬일이냐, 꿈이냐 생시냐.」
하시면서 그냥 목노아 우섯다. 아버지도 울으시고 나도 따라 울엇다. 그 이튼날 XX현(縣)  장으로부터 수다한 사람의 생명을 구하고 또는 무긔까지 압수하야 주엇다는 보수(報酬)로 만은 상이 나에게 나리엇다. 또는 도적의 말과 기구들은 갓치 나온 우리동포들에게 고로이 논아주엇다.
그 후 멧날이 지나자 또 만은 선물이 우리 집에 왓는데 그것은 나로 말미암아 살어 난 청인들로부터 보낸 것이다. 뿐만 아니라 그들은 늘 물질로서 도아주어서 그러케 가난한 우리살림이 지금은 남부럽지 안케 살어가게 되엿다. 이것은 나의 일생에 이저지지 안을 추억(追憶)이다.
(1933. 8. 22 만주흥경에서)
