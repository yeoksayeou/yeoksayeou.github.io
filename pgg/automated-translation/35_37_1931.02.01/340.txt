神秘의 나라 중국, 不老長生傳 (Mysterious China, Tales of Immortality)
愚石
잡저 (Essay)

Happy New Year in the solar calendar, and Happy Passing Year (or Celebrating the Year) in the lunar calendar. Did you have a peaceful Passing Year?

As February is the first month of the lunar calendar, I offer both New Year's greetings at once. It's customary to wish you wealth, success in all things, and a long life of thousands of years, but since you've probably already dreamed of becoming rich and achieving everything you desire, I'll wish you a long life instead. Let me delve into the old records of our neighbor, China, and share stories of people who lived long and never aged.

Regardless of the country or region, old tales from the past are always filled with mystery, but China, in particular, is a land with many unknown mysteries.

Figures like the Heavenly Emperor (天皇) and Earthly Emperor (地皇), who are said to have founded China, lived for 18,000 years. However, such extravagant tales are typical of China, a country known for its exaggeration, so they are not reliable. Myths and legends, such as immortals living for hundreds of years on Bongnae Island (蓬萊島) or female Taoists living over a hundred years on Yo Yo Chung Godo (余姚沖孤島), are inherently unbelievable. To find relatively accurate records, it's best to look to the time of the Yellow Emperor (黃帝) or, to be more precise, the Zhou Dynasty. Even these may not be trustworthy, but excessive skepticism leads nowhere. In any case, these mysterious old stories are written by fanciful Chinese people, so we can only accept them with a grain of salt. <150>

Who can say for sure that there weren't people living hundreds of years in some corner of that vast land? The phenomena of nature, which allow a single blade of grass to bloom white and red flowers and distinguish between water and fire, may occasionally grant humans miraculous lifespans, allowing them to live for hundreds of years. Considering this, their records may not be entirely far-fetched.

Let's start by looking at those who lived over a hundred years, up to 150 years old. Those under a hundred are not even worth mentioning.

The emperors from the Yellow Emperor to the Zhou Dynasty indeed lived long lives. Nüwa (女媧), who is said to have mended the hole in the sky by melting stones, was depicted as having a snake's body and an ox's head, but it would be a mistake to think of her as being like us. Shao Hao (少昊), who established the basis of the calendar, lived over 100 years; Emperor Gaoxin (帝舋) lived 105 years; and Yao, Shun, Yu, and Tang also lived well over 100 years. King Mu of Zhou (穆王) also lived 105 years.

It seems that around that time, most people lived long lives, and a hundred years was considered young. However, strangely, the story of Qin Shi Huang (始皇), who consorted with three thousand palace women and sent people to the Three Divine Mountains (三神山) to find the elixir of immortality, doesn't mention him living a long life.

His Concubine was One Hundred and Thirteen People.

Zhang Cang (張殷昌), the prime minister of the Former Han Dynasty, lived to 190 years old and had 113 concubines. He never saw a woman again after she gave birth to a child. Zhang Cang was a large, robust man with intelligence and courage. He had 28 children, and his youngest son was born when he was 81 years old—amazing! All of his children were around 20 years old. Wang Huan (王桓), a weak scholar, was Zhang Cang's subordinate. One day, he said to Zhang Cang, "My youngest child, born when I was 50, was frail from birth and died at the age of three. Children born in old age are not promising."

Hearing this, Zhang Cang laughed and said, "You don't know the joys of life yet!" Zhang Cang married an 18-year-old beauty at the age of 83 and got her pregnant. However, he soon died. He called Wang Huan and said, "I now understand your lament at 50, only at the age of 83." He stroked his half-white hair with a deflated look, and they looked at each other and laughed.

The vigor of a long-lived person is usually strong, but Zhang Cang's case, where his energy waned only after 80, is rare.

Also, Gu Sasiweon (顧思遠), a subordinate of Xu Juci (肅映), the governor of Xuzhou, married seven wives and had twelve children during his 120 years of life. Unlike Zhang Cang, Gu Sasiweon did not take concubines but changed wives through monogamy. It is rare for a Chinese person not to take concubines.

When someone asked Xu Juci the reason, he replied with embarrassment, "Don't ask me; ask my wife." He said nothing else. He later became a senior official (散騎侍郞) and was favored by the emperor, living a happy life. Considering that his wife, who was 40 years younger than him, was so virtuous, we can understand that he did not take concubines throughout his life. <151>

Liang Hang (梁項), a Taoist priest who lived to 140, stayed healthy throughout his life by eating Jeosil (楮實, paper mulberry fruits), and Zhang Wonshi (張元始), who lived to 116, had a son at the age of 97, are also worth mentioning. Although constitution plays a role, the fact that Jeosil acts as a tonic is also interesting. Liang Hang, who ate this medicine, rode horses and ran until he was 12 or 30, so he must have been very healthy. And the fact that Zhang Wonshi had a son at the age of 97 can be described as "super-high-class" compared to Zhang Cang, who lamented that his child born at 83 could not grow up. However, it was a miscarriage. So, if Wonshi had to lament, he would have said, "I only understood Wang Huan's lament at 97." Anyway, isn't it amazing?

During the Zhou Dynasty, Gawoo Jeomjol (嘉祐點卒)'s real name is unknown, so people called him Nan Noin (赧老) at that time. He only learned medicine at 60 and first saw the So Mun (素問, medical book) at 70. He always rubbed his buttocks and lamented, "If I had seen So Mun earlier, I would have practiced better health maintenance."

This Somun is the Yellow Emperor's Somun, i.e., the So Nyo Gyeong (素女經). The So Nyo Gyeong is a kind of sex education textbook in the form of questions and answers between the Yellow Emperor and So Nyo (素女), and although its content may not be scientific, every sentence is a truth gained from experience about sex and has valuable merit that makes you nod. It describes the principles of sexual health maintenance very sincerely, and it is surprisingly well-written that the close relationship between sexual desire and longevity has been studied to this extent since that era. Nan Noin read this at 70 and realized it, so he greatly exerted himself in health maintenance and lived to over 100 years.

Besides this, there are many people who lived over a hundred years. Lyeo Sang (呂尙) lived 101, Beom Jangsaeng (范長生) 130, Jang Yu (張游) 105, Ra Gyeol (羅結) 120, Cho Heon (曹憲) 100, Jang Wu (張祐) 120, Wu Baekryong (于伯龍) 120, Lee Jae Suk (李在宿) 133, and many others, but I will omit the list.

When the long-lived people were surveyed on October 23rd, 27th year of Kangxi in Qing Dynasty, there were 9996 people over 90 and 21 people over 100. The court gave those over 80 two rolls of silk, one catty of cotton, one stone of rice, and ten catties of meat, and gave double that amount to those over 90.

It is not unusual for people to live over a hundred years, but if they live over 150 years, it would be more appropriate to call them superhumans rather than people. During the Later Han Dynasty, Gwuk Gyeong (屈頸) lived to be 156 years old, outliving his sons and grandsons, entrusting his old age to his great-grandson and spending a peaceful life. He loved bird eggs (卵) so much that he ate them every morning and evening, and in his later years, even minor illnesses were cured by eating those bird eggs. I don't know what kind of eggs they were, but they seem to be a panacea for longevity.

炎帝, who ruled the world after 태호씨 (Taeho), died at the age of 161. He is said to have had a hon body, an ox head, a dragon's face, and three lips, so he must have been a very scary person. He could walk five days after birth, had teeth on the morning of the seventh day, and was capable of anything at the age of three. It seems that the semi-humans of that era not only lived long but also developed very quickly. But that can also be said to be the instinct of an animal. He reigned over the nine provinces, teaching the people how to plow and plant and how to cure diseases with medicine, guiding the ignorant people to self-reliance. His achievements during his 140-year reign are immeasurable.

薛京 (Seol Gyeong) of the Ming Dynasty lived to be 178 years old, but he was not originally strong, but he was not weak either. He had a constitution that was slender like a drooping willow branch but did not break in any strong wind. His unique health maintenance method was to drink cold water every morning. Until his later years, he never failed to drink a glass of clear cold water every morning. Even these days, there is a theory that this method of drinking a glass of cold water is good, but this health maintenance method seems to have started from that person at that time (?) Shao Gong (召公) of the Han Dynasty, Wang An (王安) of the Qin Dynasty, and Mao Baekwon (毛伯溫) of the Ming Dynasty all lived to be 180. Among them, Wang An, as a Buddhist sculptor, worked with his disciples until he was 150, but he could not hold a chisel after he was over 150.

Emperor Taeho lived for 194 years and reigned for 115 years, his head was long, his eyes were slender, his teeth were like a turtle, his lips were like a dragon, and he was 9 cheok (척, a unit of length) tall, and his hair touched the ground, so it can be said that he was different from humans. He invented writing and made musical instruments, greatly contributing to the culture of that era.

When Lee Suchung (李守忠) of the Song Dynasty went south as an envoy, crossed the sea and went to a place called Gyungju (瓊州), he met an old man with white hair on the road and asked him his age. "I am 122 years old, but there is an older person in my house, would you like to meet them? If you want to meet them, please come with me." So he followed the old man to his house, and indeed there was an older person, so when he asked him his age, he was 195 years old, his eyes were bright, and his ears were sharp, and there were no inconveniences, but one difficult thing was that he slept too much, and he wondered if there was any good medicine. This old man was Yang Songgyeong (楊宋卿), his son was Suklyeon (叔連), Suklyeon was Songhyang's (宋鄕) son at the age of 67, Suklyeon had a 91-year-old son, a 65-year-old grandson, and a 27-year-old great-grandson, and all were healthy.

At that time, Lee Suchung asked if there was a secret to immortality. <153>

"When Songhyang was 80 years old, he went up to the nearby mountain to catch wild goats, and he was thirsty, so he went to a place to drink water. A clear and unpolluted spring flowed out from a crack in the rock, and its taste was incomparably good. So, every time Songhyang went to that mountain to catch wild goats, he made it a habit to drink that water. After several months, his complexion became harmonious, and his wrinkles smoothed out. Seeing this, his descendants were very happy and kept asking him the reason. Songhyang thought about it and said that the only thing he could think of was that he drank that water every time he went to the mountain, so he told them the story. The descendants immediately went to that mountain, dug the well well, and made it even more clean and started drinking it like medicine, and the whole family lived so long." So, Lee Suchung had them guide him to the well, and indeed, a spring was gushing out like a horse. Lee Suchung immediately named the place Cheonhabullocheon (天下不老泉, Fountain of Eternal Youth). Since their longevity became known to the world, many people from far and wide went to draw water from that sacred spring, which is a plausible anecdote about longevity. If the Cheonhabullocheon is still there and you want to live a long life, save up for travel expenses and go there as soon as possible.

People who have lived long are not only in China, but also in Korea, but I will stop here.

If you want to go to Cheonhabullocheon once, save your whole life and make enough for travel expenses. It's too boring. <154>

양력 2월이면 음력 정월이니 무근인사 해ㅅ인사 한번에 드림니다. 새해인사로는 으레히 부자가 되시고 萬事가 如意하시고 천년만년 기리사시라는 것이나 여러분께서는 벌서 부자 되실 꿈은 꾸어노으섯고 만사가 여의하섯슬 것이니 오래오래 사시도록 축원을 드리며 우리 니웃나라 중국의 녯 기록을 들추어서 長生不老한 사람의 이약이나 드려볼가 함니다.

어느 나라 어느 지방을 말론하고 녯날녯날 고려적 이야기를 드르면 어느곳이나 신비스럽지 안은 곳이 업스나 그중 중국이라는 곳은 그 중에도 미지의 신비가 만히 잇는 나라임니다.
중국이라는 나라를 창설햇다는 天皇씨와 地皇씨 가튼 이들은 1만8천년이나 사라잇섯드라하지만 그 따위 허황한 소리는 역시 풍치기로 일홈 난 나라인 중국의 이야기이니 확적한 말도 안이고(?) 蓬萊島에는 수백세에 다한 신선들이 잇섯다는 것이나 余姚沖孤島에 백세가 훨신 넘은 女道士가 살아잇섯다는 것과 가튼 신화전설은 처음부터 미돌수 업는 것이거니와 비교적 정확(?)한 기록을 구하랴면 물론 黃帝 혹은 좀더 정확을 구하랴면 아마 周 이후가 오를가함니다. 이것도 미들 수 업다면 또한 미들 수 업는 것이나 그러케 못미더워하게 되면 끗이 업는 것이고 여하간 신비가 가득한 녯이야기를 풍쟁이 중국인이 기록한 것이니 그럴사하게 아라두는 수박게 업슬 것임니다.
저 廣大無邊의 땅떵어리의 어느 편 어느 구석에 수백세의 장수한 사람이 업다고 누가 단언을 할수 가 잇겟슴니까. 한오라기 풀닙에서 희고 붉은 꼿을 구별해 피게하고 물과 불을 분간하는 자연계의 현상은 혹 가다가 인간에게도 기적적 수명을 주어 수백세의 장생을 이루움니다. 이러한 것을 상상해보면 그들의 기록이 반드시 엉뚱한 것이라고도 할 수 업는 것임니다.＜150＞
그럼 우선 백세 이상으로 150세까지의 장수자를 골나 보기로 합시다. 백세 이하쯤은 문제도 안됨니다.
黃帝로부터 周에 이르기까지의 제왕들은 과연 장수를 하엿슴니다. 한울이 뚤어 젓다하야 돌을 익여서 그 구녕을 메엿다는 女媧씨 가튼 이는 蛇身牛首이엿다고하닌 요새 우리와 가튼 사람이려니 생각을 하시면 대실수임니다. 曆日의 근본을 지엇다는 少昊씨도 100여 세 帝舋은 105세 그러고 堯舜禹湯도 100세를 훨신 넘엇고 주의 穆王도 105세나 사럿드람니다.
아마 그때쯤은 대개가 장수를 하야 백세쯤은 소년대접을 바든 듯십슴니다. 그러나 이상한 것은 삼천궁녀를 거르니고 장생불사하기 이하야 三神山으로 불사약을 구하려 보냇다든 진나라 始皇의 이야기에는 오래 살엇다는 이야기를 두를 수가 엄슴니다.
