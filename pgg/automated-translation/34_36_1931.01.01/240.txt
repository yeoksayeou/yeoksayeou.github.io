문예기타 (Literature Miscellaneous)

Magazine Reporter's Confession

Horantang Person

This is less a magazine reporter's confession than the agonizing soul-searching of a young, misplaced, not-quite-right combo looking to liquidate the past.

I had to go visit a girls' school.
Formally and in terms of how the article was handled, visiting a girls' school is a job suitable for a woman.
As a man trudging along to do what a woman should be doing, I discovered a comical clown in my ridiculous shadow.
A comical clown! A Pierrot… a clown dressed ridiculously to make others laugh while laughing himself… a Pierrot!
But there are tears in the clown's laughter.
In the ridiculous shadow of a not-quite clown, there are even more resentful and angry tears.

The tragedy of a misplaced life is doing work that one shouldn't be doing instead of what one ought to be doing. The pain of a journalist who has slipped off the rails is writing things one shouldn't be writing instead of what one ought to be writing.
If a person born into this world fails to uphold the ideals they possess, what radiance can there be in their life?
What difference is there between proclaiming righteousness without sincerity and writing arguments without intention, and a prostitute singing songs without heart and laughing without meaning?
Oh, intellectual prostitution of a pallid intellectual! I curse myself this way.
I should have been born as "a person who has nothing more to lose than chains," or else a loyal dog of the bourgeoisie, content with my innate destiny… or dedicated my life as an apostle of liberalism. I wasn't forced by anyone, nor was there any need to do so, so why did I change direction?
But the die is already cast.
Fate is decided.
All that remains is time.
I swear—I swear with my life that I will not coexist with the "present."
After hurriedly liquidating my powerless intellectual profession, the glittering background of gold will see the flashing blade of XX—clashing against it, being cut and cut, being endlessly cut, until the bright red blood is dripping, until that sharp blade becomes dull and eventually breaks, until the quiet morning dawns after the night of slaughter…

<96>

Even those who seem to know the world stand with folded arms, waiting only for inevitability.
But inevitability does not advance by itself.
As I learned from Rotoni—nothing comes without fervor. Their fervor alone is a great lesson for us.

Two years as a newspaper reporter...
During that time, I was immersed in alcohol and women in an untimely decadence, without any awareness or introspection, so there's nothing to say.
It's not at all unreasonable that a scion of a fallen middle class, steeped in baseless nationalistic animosity and ambitious liberalism, became like that in a world that didn't go his way.
Pretending to help others, while living on alcohol and women at night, and scraping by during the day—but unable to even appease the superiors, with such a fiery temper, it was obvious he would be chased out.
After being kicked out of the newspaper company, he tried to find another job, but what could a person with so little common sense do anywhere?
Having no other choice, he rolled back to his hometown and sponged off his struggling father for three years.

<97>

For me, those three years were the greatest and most decisive period in determining my life's destiny.
More than anything, I did a lot of reading during that time.
At first, I was absorbed in Kropotkin, then moved on to Marx. This period was the time when I read the most, both in quantity and quality, in my entire life.
Also, I saw the objective situation of society… especially the countryside… and learned how to see it.

I jumped back to Seoul and started working for a magazine company. At first, I went along without knowing the ins and outs, but eventually, I compared my ideology with my actions. And so, my agony began.
The harm I am doing now far outweighs the benefits.
Moreover, the "benefits" are only for "a liberal Joseon," which is a different world from my ideology.
My current life is by no means a labor-management relationship. It's in a consensual system where name and reality match.
Therefore, it's different from the meaning of a proletariat going to the XX factory to make XXs XXs XXs for themselves in the future. It's very different.
It is something that I absolutely cannot forgive myself for.
If it were purely a labor-management relationship, I would have no shame in my conscience, no matter what kind of social labor (broadly speaking) I am doing now.

Why can't I break free from this?
I think I am still lacking in refinement and weak. We can only demonstrate our inherent mission and strength through all organizations. But I am now standing outside the organization.
This won't do.
I am currently bound by small human feelings. It may be because human beings need human feelings and righteousness—and so, I cannot break free from it and wander around, with tears in my eyes.
In reality, I have absolutely no refinement in XX, and my theoretical training is also very shallow. My "present" is not without a cause for wanting to shake this off after delving deeper into these things.
Another great force holding me back is the stifling atmosphere of the petit-bourgeois society that I have lived in for 30 years.
To escape from the environment, one can only do so by thoroughly washing away and discarding the accumulated second nature.
Knowing and not acting is more "evil" than not knowing and not acting.

<98>

But I am constantly striving, so please forgive me.

They say it's a new year. I've lived half my life, so the rest is the other half. I have tasted the pleasures of this floating world, so now is the time to cut all ties and advance toward my beliefs.
Even if my life disappears now, it wouldn't be too regrettable, and there's not much to be attached to.
Since life is something that must end in death someday, how much more pleasant it is to live boldly, even if briefly, than to live thinly and long.

Those who read the title "Magazine Reporter's Confession" with curiosity, wondering if I had committed some great wickedness, will be disappointed.

Like that, what does not fully live up to its name is perhaps quite regrettable as a magazine article, but in fact, I have no regrets as a magazine reporter. I think I have fulfilled that mission to at least 7 or 8 out of 12 points.

If there is even one comrade in the same situation and state as me who sees this, I believe they will surely discover and feel the same self.

Newspaper Reporter's Confession

Gopan

A reporter’s repentance.
After ten years of working as a social affairs reporter in Chosŏn, experiencing much and feeling much, it was as if I had been given a sentence:
“Clean up the wrongs you have accumulated until today.” I must have done many unjust things to others and told many lies. Eventually, a friend asked me,
“How much did you earn on the side during those ten years as a social affairs reporter?”
If I said,
“About ten-odd people,”
they would say,
“That’s surprisingly little.”
If I said,
“If you add up all the women I enjoyed on the streets, as my feet led me, enjoying the love of that day and that night, it would be at least 200,”
the person asking would immediately <99>
“Hey… a lie. Surely it wasn’t that many,”
A protest would be heard, so I decided that the best policy was to bury in secrecy the facts that I met during my ten years as a reporter and the things that happened to me that would be regrettable in the future, so I can’t possibly confess, no matter how much the prominent Kaebyoksa presses me.
Once, I told my close friend,
“Hey, you know, today I went to Ŭnjŏngjuk to have lunch, and that Cho XX from Chaedong came. She was carrying a child, and the three of them, husband and wife, were coming out of the restaurant, looking happy. For some reason, I avoided them like someone who had committed a big crime.”
Of course, that woman named Cho was a student I played with ten years ago when I was busy roaming around downtown. In the blink of an eye, ten years have passed, she has gotten married, and I have grown old. Seeing her surprised and shrinking appearance, the thought came to me that she was a virtuous wife and a good mother of a happy family, and that I couldn’t bear to cause her new pain about the sins she committed in the past, so I avoided her myself.
In a place like Chosŏn, where society is not affluent, the only material that could give readers interest in the confession of a social affairs reporter is the issue of women.
“That the Prime Minister told an unspeakable lie when changing sides, obscuring the wisdom of the readers to achieve temporary success,”
“That they clenched their fists and made a fuss in the office of the president of the bank and only managed to get 1,000 won,”
“That they found out a secret about the person of a certain count and played a strange prank,”
“That a big company went bankrupt because they made a mistake with their pen once,”
These kinds of issues are only things you can hear about in places like Tokyo or Osaka, and it’s difficult to happen in our Kyŏngsŏng, so in the end, apart from money and power, there’s nothing left but the issue of women. Since the conclusion has reached here, I can’t stand it any longer and can’t help but confess a secret about a more breathtaking act than a tightrope walk in a circus, pretending not to remember and treating it as an old story from ten years ago. I’ve forgotten when it was. But it was when the Italian plane first flew into Chosŏn, and when Lieutenant Ferrarin came to Chosŏn and landed at Yŏido, the Dong-A Ilbo issued an extra edition and turned Kyŏngsŏng upside down, so if you think carefully, the wise readers might say, “Oh, what was that then?”
In the warehouse of the Dong-A Ilbo, built of three-story brick in Kwanghwamun Street, there is still a sign that says “Permanent Preservation,” and that extra edition is still there.
The story becomes breathtaking from here. Mr. Song Chin-u, the current president, <100> was the highest-ranking executive of the Dong-A Ilbo at that time, but if he reads this article, he might say,
“Hey—that wicked guy, he was fooling around with a woman and said such a brazen thing.”
So, in the end, if I were still at the Dong-A Ilbo, the opportunity to reveal this secret to the world might have been extended again. So, it means that a life of swaggering around without being tied down might be twice as bad, but it’s masculine.
So—how was that extra edition with the following meaning issued?
Italian Lieutenant Ferrarin safely landed in Kyŏngsŏng
The company immediately donated a wreath
The plane that was supposed to arrive during the day was delayed in China, so each newspaper prepared an extra edition and eagerly waited for it to come to Kyŏngsŏng. At that time, the plane was under the jurisdiction of the Ryongsan Military Command (as it is now in most cases). The newspapers in Chosŏn, which did not have complete communication facilities in each region, could only call the adjutant’s office of the military command as if a fire had broken out, asking about the movements of the plane.
The plane, which was supposed to arrive during the day, did not arrive until evening, so there were no officers in the adjutant’s office of the military command—they had all left, and only one lieutenant on duty remained in the duty room.
The only way for the newspaper company to hear the news of the plane to publish an extra edition was to urge this lieutenant.
The plan to issue an extra edition that day was that when the Sinuiju Military Police sent a telegram to the military command duty lieutenant saying that the plane had passed Sinuiju, he would immediately calculate the time of arrival in Kyŏngsŏng and print the extra edition.
That day, I finished my outdoor work and went to the office, and I received a call from S according to precedent.
“Let’s go out to play somewhere.”
“Okay.”
“What time will you come out?”
I didn’t think the plane would be delayed that much, so
“Four o’clock,”
I replied.
“Where?”
“The corner of Ingyŏngjŏn in Chongno.”
“Then definitely.”
“Don’t worry.”
I made an appointment with my lover so readily, and I was waiting for four o’clock, but the accursed plane didn’t come, so I couldn’t go out. At that time, the editor-in-chief, Ri Sang-hyŏp, called me and said,
“From now on, go to the duty room of the military command and call the office immediately when the telegram saying it has passed Sinuiju comes.”
His command is absolute. And the fact that he chose <101> me, a young man, for this task shows that he has at least a sliver of trust in me. Anyway, I couldn’t even make a sound and said
“Yes”
as if crying. I looked at the clock and saw that it was already past four o’clock.
“The car has arrived.”
The messenger gave me a report like a thunderbolt in my ear. The decision has been made to go, and even the car has been prepared, so it’s not the time to sing love songs or indoor songs.
I had no choice but to get in the car. And I made a desperate decision.
“Driver, please stop at the corner of Ingyŏngjŏn in Chongno for a moment.”
I asked. The car stopped in front of Ingyŏngjŏn in less than five minutes. That delicate and pure woman like a cosmos had already discovered the car I was in and came running.
“It’s a car today.”
She doesn’t know what’s going on and thinks we’re just going for a “drive” together. I’ve already made up my mind, and I can’t even talk properly because the car driver is in the way, so anyway
“Get in quickly.”
That’s all I said. The car passed through the main gate of the military command safely.
“Where is this?”
It’s not strange to ask. To boldly bring a hidden lover into the military command in the middle of the day in a newspaper company car—what kind of courage is it?
“Whatever happens, just pretend to be a female reporter from the Dong-A Ilbo and do as I say.”
I finally lamented the desperate pain of an employee who received a business trip order to the military command and couldn’t meet at the appointed time, so I had no choice but to bring her here. Can there be a weak soldier under a brave general?
“Oh, that’s fun. Okay, okay, I’ll pretend to be a female reporter with a straight face.”
If it comes to this, there’s no doubt that they’re a pair of delinquent men and women. The two of them boldly go up to the second floor, greet the lieutenant on duty, and just sit there and wait for the telegram to come. While they’re doing this, calls are coming in like wildfire from each newspaper company.
“Hey, what if other newspaper reporters come running while we’re talking on the phone like that and our secret is discovered?”
I’m also worried about that. But I can’t send my lover away first, and if we go together, I’ll lose my job. If I were on good terms with the lieutenant on duty,
“As a favor to save someone, we’ll go first, so if a telegram comes from Sinuiju, please just say one word to Mr. Ri Sŏ-gu, the proxy at the Dong-A Ilbo, that the plane has passed Sinuiju,” but <102> I can’t ask for that out of shame, and I don’t think he’ll listen. S and I were saying these things and looking at the lieutenant on duty, and then the door burst open and a child of 4 or 5 years old came in front, followed by the wife of the officer on duty carrying dinner for her husband. Seeing this, my eyes widened and
“Hey, you’re a woman, so let’s get to know that wife quietly. I’ll play with that child.”
The two people’s operation plan was immediately successful, and in less than 30 minutes, S and the officer’s wife were having a very enjoyable conversation. How they managed to be so brazen, the officer took the initiative and gave a decision that was more sacred than the words of God:
“It’s a simple matter, so if a telegram comes that the plane has passed, I will definitely call the Dong-A Ilbo without falling behind anyone, so don’t worry and go back.”
We rushed out on that road, had dinner at a quiet restaurant, and were having a good time when
“Extra edition! Extra edition!”
the sound came. It was the Dong-A Ilbo extra edition. The extra edition came out the earliest of all the newspapers. I picked up the extra edition and said
“Thank you so much,”
and the sly S smiled and said
“Who knows if they did it for us or if they kicked us out and had fun with their family.” I followed and said
“Well, that’s true, but they gave us a reliable phone call.”
“They’re soldiers, how could they say one thing and do another?”
S pursed her lips again. (The End)

Mahjong Maniac's Confession

One Mahjong Maniac

Listening to opium addicts, they say that dying without experiencing the taste of opium after being born into this world is a life without any meaning.
Borrowing the psychology of this saying, I boldly want to say this—that without knowing the fun of mahjong as a modern person, it is a life without any meaning...

Just as one cannot know the true state of being intoxicated by opium unless one is an opium addict, one cannot know the true state of being engrossed in mahjong unless one is a mahjong maniac.
When playing cycle, arranging four sets of four tiles three times, and then drawing jjonjjong to make up fourteen tiles, the satisfaction of having a green "發" (phae) tile written in blue letters on a white background, or a red "中 (chung) character" <103> or two blank white tiles, or three concealed triplets, is incomparable to any fortune in this world.
Also, waiting for a side piece after setting up 8 or 9 tiles, and then drawing tsumo (自摸) when the seventh piece comes out, setting up 1 or 3 man and waiting for a middle piece, when the upper hand gives the "chi" of 2 man, wondering if anyone will "pung" (碰) it, the delicate fun of picking it up and inserting it—this is more than getting the love that one has been waiting for... more fun than anything that cannot be exchanged even with this world is overflowing.
That's how tense mahjong is and makes you forget everything else. In the past, when I was troubled, I played billiards to forget my troubles. But compared to mahjong, billiards is nothing. There is a difference between bland sake and "Baegal (백알)" that you eat in Andong County and other places.

I started learning mahjong last summer.
When I was learning, I didn't know this or that, I just learned it.
After learning it, I realized it was no small world.
When I sit in front of a mahjong table, I forget everything. It is truly a state of selflessness, a world of surrealism.

From the 136 bone-attached bamboo tile pieces, I discovered a state of selflessness last summer.
In the meantime, I devoted almost my entire life to mahjong. The price was that I could completely forget my pain—the pain of constantly thinking and worrying about a hundred things—during those few hours I sat at the mahjong table.
Opium is said to be good for people who have failed in business, people who are pessimistic about the world, and people who are struggling with heartbreak, but in my opinion, mahjong is better.
Opium ruins people forever, but mahjong doesn't. There is plenty of leeway, and you can even repent like me.

It may be something that some people will criticize, but I want to recommend this:
As a young person, especially those who have lost love and are causing suicide commotions, play mahjong for a while...
If you follow human nature, you should be able to forget most heartbreaks in 2-3 months.
If you still can't forget it, then opium would be better.

I am still playing mahjong now. I have tried to quit several times, but I still can't quit. I may spend a year on mahjong.
When I lie alone in my lodging, I have other thoughts. <104> When I am thinking, pain seeps into my body. Trying to avoid pain (depending on the type) is human nature. Unable to bear it, I run to a club or a friend's house and shout "Pung," "Chi," "Hula."
After doing two, three, or four connections, I usually return after midnight and lie down in bed.
Still unable to sleep, I take a sleeping pill and barely fall asleep.
I look at myself becoming a hopeless person and laugh sadly.

Doctor's Confession

OO Hospital Hidden Needle

I received an order to write a confession.
Who doesn't have confessions, but for me, who graduated from school this spring and has only been a little over half a year, to write a doctor's confession is too heavy a burden. But since I have agreed, I cannot not write it, and since the deadline is urgent, let me write down a few things that come to mind.
More than anything, when I think about whether this profession is right for me, I think it is absolutely not right for me, no matter how I look at it. Then I don't know why I learned this either. In the midst of not knowing, I did this and that for about 4 years and ended up graduating and holding a medical license in my hand. But even if I change direction now, I have already learned it, and this is the only talent I have. If I let go of this, I will be hungry from today, so I have no choice but to do it to fill my stomach. But if you ask if my stomach is full now, it is absolutely not full. The job called a doctor, a good (?) profession, was decades ago, but now there is an overproduction, so poor doctors like us are rarely able to have even coins in our suit pockets due to constant malnutrition. The world doesn't know the inside story and says that medicine is the best. They say that if you graduate, you will get a job easily, get a lot of money, and even if you don't get a job, you can start your own business and have enough to eat, but in reality, there are so many unspeakable circumstances. Let's say you graduate from school and get a job. It is a bitterness that all Korean people who graduate from middle school or higher experience together. The order form says, "Looking for one Japanese doctor, but limited to native Japanese." So when it comes to our turn, it is a thousand times unreasonable, and if we are lucky enough to have our turn come, it will be the leftovers they have eaten. So what is good about getting a job for us?! Also, let's look at what other people say about starting a business. If you start a business, you do start a business, and you will eat and move forward, but if poor people like us start a business, let alone the equipment, we don't even have the money to rent a house, so what's the use of talking about it? If you only have capital, what can't you do for yourself? And even if you have capital, just look at the number of hospitals in Kyŏngsŏng. Which street doesn't have a hospital sign? And even if there is one, is there only one or two? Go to big hospitals such as university hospitals or medical school hospitals. All you see are doctors, all you bump into are doctors. There are more doctors than patients. There are many well-fed people among them, but apart from the scholars who are directors or department heads or medical officers, there is no way to get a good income, and it is embarrassing to play around, so they are pitiful teachers who go and work as assistants or sub-hands. Among them, there are admirable people who are working hard, forgetting sleep and meals in order to study to become scholars, and there are not a few wealthy people who want to try more practice for another year or two and gain more confidence before starting their own business, but there are countless pitiful teachers like me who are unemployed, have nowhere to go, have no capital to start a business, don't know how to do other things, and are embarrassed to play around, so they beg and go around. Let's look at the income of those people. It varies depending on each department, but if you are an assistant, you will only get enough living expenses to barely pay off your debts. The next one, a sub-hand, who doesn't have a very prestigious name, may get 40 or 50 won, but don't be surprised, there are not a few who get 3 won, 5 won, or 7 won. Besides, how many trainees are there? If it goes like this, is there any way to avoid malnutrition? Is this all? How much will be made in Chosŏn every year after this, and how much will be pushed in from foreign countries? So, is overproduction not common everywhere?
Writing a confession has gone off the rails. In the current society, it is said that everything is for people with money, but the medical profession is said to be an art of healing, and they treat people regardless of wealth or status, but this is just talk. Isn't it something that everyone experiences? If poor people, laborers, go to a hospital and ask for a house call, will they gladly respond? Even if they don't do a house call, just go to the clinic and give medicine and treatment, and then ask for money. If they don't have 10 jeon or 20 jeon, or if they say they don't have any, look at the face of that doctor. Since the examination or treatment has already been done, they leave it as it is because they have no choice, but they take back the medicine. In private hospitals, they leave the treatment as it is, perhaps out of consideration for their reputation, perhaps out of conscience, but look at the government-run hospitals that are said to be built for the sake of public health. I am also one of the people there, but there is not a day that I don't turn my head every time I see it, and there is not a time that I don't clench my fists.
Poor patients barely buy a examination ticket and get an examination. After that, they get treatment or a prescription and go to the cashier to pay the treatment fee or medicine price first, and then get the medicine. But is it possible that this poor person has brought the treatment fee or medicine price? They say they have no money. Then, the face of the nurse with a good name changes immediately, and the way they speak changes. It's as if their own income is decreasing, so of course they can't get the medicine, and even the bandages that have already been treated are unwound and taken away. Of course, they are not doing that on their own. There is a doctor above them, and there is a supervisory office above them, so what can they do? But isn't it too much to even unwind the bandages that have been treated? What about patients who don't have money but come in clean clothes? They casually tell them to bring it tomorrow. How frustrating is it? Also, let's look at the medicine price or treatment fee. No matter how much it is a skill or saving lives, where is such a profiteering? I won't go into more detail about the raw materials, but if it's a skill, is medicine the only skill? Isn't cooking, which directly supports our lives more than anything else, also a skill? Compare it to the compensation for the skill of the person who cooks.
I can't bear the shame when I see these things. I think about it a lot on my own. If other people ask me this, what can I say? I really have nothing to say. Besides, how great is the skill? Of course, there are many special things, but most of the time, as people come and go, the patients heal themselves.
It's getting longer and longer without being right, and there are many deviations because it's getting longer. Anyway, it is a fact that this profession does not suit me in any way, whether it is my aptitude or personality. It's a big mistake to have come this way. Unlike other things, I have to maintain a certain level of decency. I also have to take care of my appearance. So I have to wear a suit that is not worn out, even if I don't have money.
It's an ungrateful profession. Even worse, if I make a mistake, I can even lose my license. If I lose my license, the only places I can get food from that day are a police station or a prison. Who in this society will give me food, who has no talent?
Should I change direction now? But the enemy's age is too high, and it is truly too late. Since there is no way to undo it, let's just make a living and see what happens in the next life. I was going to write well, but I was procrastinating, and it became urgent, so I couldn't write what I wanted to write, and it became a non sequitur and even lacked coherence. I am very sorry for wasting the valuable space of Byŏlgŏn'gŏn and the precious time and eyesight of the readers. I deeply apologize.

雜誌記者懺悔

浩然堂人

이것은 잡지기자의 참회라 하는 것보담도 當티 아니한 환경에 誤植된 한 젊은 콤XXXX의 과거청산의 고민이라 함이 可하겟다.

여학교방문을 가게 된 일이 잇섯다.
형식으로 보나 記事 取扱의 내용으로 보나 여학교방문이란 여자에게 맛당한 일이다.
맛당히 여자가 할 일을 사내놈이 끄덕끄덕하고 가는 나의 우숫광스러운 그림자에서 나는 어리ㅅ광대를 발견하엿다.
어리ㅅ광대! 피에로....우수꽝스럽게 차림차리를 하고 남을 웃기며 제가 웃는 어리ㅅ광대....피에로!
그러나 어리ㅅ광대의 우슴속에는 눈물이 잇나니.

참회를 쓴다는 게 말이 탈선이 되어바렷다. 현사회에 잇서서 무엇이고 돈 잇는 사람을 위해서 잇다고 하지만은 의업만은 소위 인술이니 무엇이니 하야 가며 빈부귀천을 가리지 안코 보아준다고 한다만은 이것은 말뿐이다 누구나 다 당해보는 일이 안인가 돈 업는 사람 노동자들이 어는 병원에 가 왕진을 청해 보아라 썩 반가히 응해주는가. 왕진은 고만두고라도 宅진이라도 가서 하고 약을 주고 處置를 하고 난 뒤에 돈을 청한다. 가지온 게 10전이나 20전 또는 업다고 해노코 그 의사의 相을 보아라. 진찰이나 처치는 旣往 한 것이니 하는 수 업스니까 그대로 두지만은 약은 도로 빼아서바린다. 개인병원에서는 체면이라고 할가. 그대로 양심이라고 할가 무엇에 거리껴서든지 처치한 것은 그대로 둔다만은 소위 국민의 보건을 위하야 세웟다는 관립병원갓흔 데를 보아라 나 역시 그곳에 잇는 한 사람이다만은 하로에도 멧번식 보며 그것을 볼때마다 머리를 도리키지 안흔때가 업스며 주먹을 쥐지 안흔때가 업다.
빈민환자가 진찰권은 겨우 사가지고 와서 診察를 밧는다. 그런 뒤에 처치를 밧거나 처방전을 바더가지고 출납계로 가서 먼저 處置料나 藥價를 준 다음에야 약을 어더 가지고 가게 된다. 그러나 이 빈민이 처치료나 약가를 가지고 왓슬 리가 잇는가 돈이 업다고 한다. 그러면 발서 일홈 조흔 간호부의 얼골빗부터 달너지며 말버릇부터 달너진다. 바로 제 수입이나 주러지는 듯이 그러니 약을 못어더가는 가는 것은 말할 것도 업거니와 기왕 처치햇든<106> 붕대조차 끌너서 빼서 바린다. 물론 그네들도 제 마음대로 그러는건 안이다. 제 위로 의사선생님이 게시고 그 위로 감독관청이 잇스니 하는 수야 잇는가만은 처치햇든 붕대까지 끌너바리는 것은 넘어 과하지 안는가. 이것도 갓치 돈 안 가지고 온 사람이라도 의복이나 깨끗이 입고온 환자에게는 엇더한가 흔년히 내일 올 때 가지고 오라고 해 보낸다. 얼마나 기막힐 일인가 또 한 약가나 처치료에 잇서서도 보자. 아무리 기술이요 생명을 구한다고 할지라도 그런 폭리가 어데 잇슬가. 그 原料에 잇서서는 더 자세히 말치 안는다만은 기술이라고 하면 의술만이 기술일가 우리의 생명을 무엇보다도 직접 지지하고 잇는 밥을 짓는 것도 기술이 아닐가. 밥 짓는 사람의 그 기술에 대한 報酬에 비해 보아라.
나는 이런 것을 볼 때에 붓그럼을 참지 못한다 내 호을로 생각할 때도 만타. 다른 사람이나 더러 이럭헤 뭇는다면 나는 무슨 말로 대답을 할가 과연 할말이 업다. 대체 또 기술이라고 하니 얼마나 큰 기술인가. 물론 특수한 것도 만치만은 웬만한 것은 사러 잇스니 산 사람이니 나어간다. 내과에서나 외과에서 잇서서 특수한 걸 제한 외에는 이렁저렁하는 동안에 환자 스사로 나어가는 것도 적지 안타.
되지 안흔게 길어만가고 길어가니 탈선이 만타. 하여간 나로서는 이 직업이 어는 점으로 보든지 제 소질이나 성격어는 방면으로 보든지 맛지 안는 것만은 사실이다. 크게 잘못 들은 길이다. 이것은 다른 것과 달너 업는 점잔도 내야 된다. 체모도 보아야 된다. 그러자니 업는 돈이라도 엇턱헤 해서 해지지 안흔 양복도 입어야 된다.
고맙지 못한 직업이다. 그나마도 깻닥 잘못하면 면허장조차 빼앗기게 된다. 그라도 빼앗겨 바리고나면 그날부터 밥줄 곳이라고는 씨나다나 경찰서나 형무소밧게 업슬 것이다. 이 사회에서 누가 아무 재조엄는 나에게 밥을 줄 것이냐?
지금부터라도 방향을 밧구워볼가 하나 원수의 나히 만어 그야말로 晩時之歎이 업지 안타. 後悔莫及이라 하는 수 잇는가. 그리저리 연명해 가며 後生이나 보자. 잘 쓰지도 안는 글에 미루어오다가 급박하게 되어 쓰고 십흔 말은 쓰지도 못하고 동문서답이 된데다가 두서조차 업시되엿다 別乾坤의 귀한 지면과 독자의 중한 시간과 시력만 소모하게 되어 미안천만이다. 깁히 사과한다.
