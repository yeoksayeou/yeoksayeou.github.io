Radio, Sports, and Cinema
						Seung-il

Radio
It may seem old news now, but the story of how, when Prime Minister MacDonald of England gave a labor speech in London, the microphone in front of him captured his voice, transmitted it on radio waves at a wavelength of 1500 or 1600 meters, and was heard by tens of thousands of people in Moscow, Russia, is already two or three years old. However, in Joseon (朝鮮), the news is still fresh.

Radio—the pinnacle of modern scientific civilization—a mysterious tale of hearing the movements of the world through something like a light bulb and a microphone. A single phrase like, "I'm hungry..." enters that microphone, which resembles the back of a sword—to explain it with expert terms, the sound mixes with the electricity in the air and travels around the world seven and a half times in a second—now, we can hear the movements of the world, the sounds of the Earth turning, the "empty words" of politicians... the deception of merchants! The sound of the bourgeoisie filling their bellies! The shouting of laborers—we could hear the cries, but we can't because we don't have a 70-won receiver.

If you set up a clothesline on the threshing floor, connect a battery, and attach a loudspeaker horn, JOAK comes on. You can hear a geisha singing from Tokyo. Suddenly, you hear, "Today, a doe of rice costs 56 jeon, up from 57 jeon." (鋤) The peasants wielding hoes say it is a mischief of spirits. It is the god of science. A new god of modern civilization. 악

JODK, "This is Seoul Communications Bureau." - Suddenly cut off - Kim Chu-wol's Namdo Short Song comes on - "White gull, don't fly so fast..." can be heard. "Huh..." someone says, mouth agape.

ROS, "Workers of the world! Let us naturally work only 8 hours. Urban workers, first, quickly enact the 8-hour workday. Rural farmers! How about this—landlords get 30 percent, tenants get 60 percent? Comrade Marx said it like this." "Workers of all countries, unite!" - Russian and English mixed together. "Sounds like frogs croaking..." This was the remark of a friend standing on the rice paddy embankment. Instead, if you sang "Let's play while we're young," in London, any child would hear it and give the receiver to their mother, saying, "What?" At the same time, an old Qing man in Shanghai would hear it and say, "What is this?" And in Tokyo? An okke-san (おっ母さん) in a tatami room would hear it and say, "What could this be..." <104>

The news that an American newspaper reporter succeeded in sending an exploration report to his newspaper in New York via shortwave radio while exploring the Arctic Ocean was already news from last spring. In fact, radio is conquering newspapers. It is a clear fact that needs no reiteration.

But poor friends! Can you afford to pay eighty or ninety jeon to read the newspaper and instead listen to the radio? Can you listen to the radio, which is a newspaper during the day and a phonograph at night? Yes, life and radio—for us, it is still far away from our lives. Like anything else, civilization—it is the property of the wealthy. Civilization constantly puts out new things. They are sold to the bourgeoisie. And so, something that came about with much difficulty loses its original purpose.

And so, civilization cries, civilization cries. It laments. Radio cries. It is far from us.

In the love of noblemen, radio is present. Pak Nok-ju's gayageum byeongchang (伽倻琴並唱) comes out, and surrounded by the rhythm rising from the knees, it vanishes into the smoke of Namcho. The music of Joseon—brought even to the drawing rooms of the wealthy in Tokyo, it merely becomes a topic to murmur about in a foreign atmosphere.

How can we not hear news that is closer to our lives?

The radio of Joseon—civilization—it is the exclusive property of the conquerors. The day the current civilization collapses will be the day we develop a new world.

The battle of electricity against electricity! The current battle is a battle of science—a battle of electricity. A battle to prevent radio waves sent from tens of thousands of feet away in invisible space! Electrical intrusion—attack and defense! This is the battle of electricity.

A product of civilization that has become a plaything for the wealthy! Truly, we have no face to show to science.

However, I saw a picture in a wireless magazine of a Russian farmhouse family listening to the radio, where the old grandfather, the head of the house, was sitting with a tense face, his bushy beard barely twitching, holding the receiver to his ear because Stalin was giving a speech about the countryside from Moscow at eight o'clock, and next to him was a young friend, presumably his son, waiting for his turn, saying, "Father, let me listen." - I once saw this picture that I liked.

Lenin said, "The world I desire in the future is the world of electricity." <105>

Sports
One won for one baseball game-.
It is a problem to think about. Of course, sports games include both entertainment and competitiveness, but even sports that are meant to strengthen the body—the fact that the "empty talk" of money worship has entered even into that—is something to think more about...

The enslavement of sportsmen to the wealthy—it is a bit uncomfortable when sports stadiums become gambling dens. It's sickening when tens of thousands of won in gambling money hang on a home run or a foul ball, and when thousands of won in hush money go into a suit pocket for declaring an obvious strike a ball.

In Japan, baseball has taken over the era of sumo wrestling—and if one can swing a bat nicely, he can buy the joy of a beautiful woman. He soon becomes the object of admiration for modern girls. Joseon hasn't had enough time to talk about it yet, but even in Japan alone, during the heyday of hair ornaments, young ladies from good families would praise Army Second Lieutenants and Navy Ensigns, but now they put the pitchers and catchers of university teams on pedestals. Truly, the psychology of people following new things, eras, and cultures is immeasurable.

The fact that the American boxer Sam Bush got a top movie actress and that Japanese tennis player Harada had marriage talks with a certain viscount's daughter clearly proves this fact.

The fact that the income of a sports coach is higher than the salary of a university lecturer, and that the income of a movie actor is higher than the president's annual salary is because the "empty talk" of money worship is latent within it.

Look, in the world championship fight between American boxer Sam Bush and Tani, Sam Bush, who had held the championship for seven years until last year, received a large sum of 860,000 won even though he lost. But there is one funny thing. The reason he lost this time is said to be because he married a top beauty actress last year. Modern girls, who admire the most wild personalities and dignity, have finally come to cherish the personality and dignity of that modern boy forever.

When a game between the Yankees and the Kakhus is held, the Ministry of Railways makes solid preparations for a fortune, and tens of thousands of won fall on the ground in a single day—truly, it is the sacred play of the people of a wealthy country.

We can see people struggling even on the ground. Once, at the Gyeongseong (京城) ground, an American team and the Joseon team played together—the energy of those who grew up eating fatty meat and bread and eggs in thick brick houses—the soft voice, like the croaking of frogs, and the energy and voice of those who grew up eating only salted fish stew and rough kimchi radish every day in flimsy tile houses or thatched houses—I saw them mingling. The victory of money—truly, it is stiff. <106>

The commercialization of sports games—the enslavement of sportsmen—it is a product. The commodification of the clash between meat chunks—this can only be seen in this phenomenon.

Many sportsmen are caught in chains and bring the banner of honor—the silver cup—to their master, the capitalist behind them. And so, their lives exist because of it. (Although school teams may have somewhat different natures)

When a sports game is held, half of the newspaper is filled with articles about it, and whether it's in the office, within the government, in society, or even in cafes, it becomes a topic of conversation—truly, the reality of sports culture—how is it calculated?

Articles about who set a new world record in what and who won the world championship are creating a new culture in this phenomenon every day.

However, the time will come when we can all freely exercise, go to a public place for three hours a day, and do whatever exercise we want with the most joyful heart (without paying), and when we can all watch and enjoy the World Championships or even greater events for free—then, everyone will think of the old days—the fact that they had to pay one won to watch a baseball game—like a dream.

The commodification of sports games—the enslavement of sportsmen—this can only be seen in this phenomenon. When will the day come when all of this becomes an old story?

Cinema
-In fact, movies have conquered novels.

Why... is that? It is because—generally, novels are intellectual and thought-provoking, while movies, with just the gaze alone, have the ability to act beyond the thinking of the mind.

Also—economically, if you spend only 30 or 40 jeon per night, you can see several novels—productions—the direct movements of events, and also, in this so-called busy world, you can see the full action of events in a short time.

-In Joseon, it has been less than three years since movies made with our own strength (excluding money) have existed, but there are already more than dozens of them. It has been a long time since the number of movies made without a studio has exceeded 10. On cloudy days, they can't even film and just look up at the sky, and when the sun flashes, a 5-jeon reflector flashes. When a 5-jeon reflector is horizontally stripped across a person's body, the sounds of "Wow, great art!" and "A hundred views!" arise from houses at the foot of the mountains - by the roadside. In this way, a Joseon movie that has consumed about 1,000 won is released at Danseongsa (團成社) - Joseon Theater. People flood in like water. <107>

And so, in our society, which lacks theater, everyone is drawn in by the atmosphere of watching a play, raising their heads and sitting. White clothes flutter on the movie screen. How heartbreaking and deeply emotional is this scene? A bare - seemingly lifeless - mountain corner appears, and here and there - young pine trees grow. But it's a shame. What did we see in that background? We only saw examples of 2 or 3 old novels adapted into scripts like "Janghanmong" (長恨夢) - "Cage Bird" (籠中鳥).

Even the director, who can't afford a single piece of clothing and wears summer clothes in the fall, while the actors barely manage to eat one decent meal and their eyes sink in, has produced it. Truly, thinking about it, the blood - tears - sighs that flowed from the film, drip, drip. But even then, they can't produce one decent thing according to their own will, and they have to cater to the tastes of the big capitalists who shell out a measly 1,000 won, so they pick up scraps that others have eaten and re-bake them - to see "Janghanmong" - "Cage Bird" again is truly pathetic.

Also - what about the circumstances of the viewers? It's hopeless to expect the pockets of Joseon fans to be full. At one point, four regular theaters were on the verge of closing down, so they had no choice but to charge 1 geum 10 jeon, and fans who had never seen them before swarmed in, saying, "Wow!" I remember paying 10 jeon to watch a movie when I was young, but charging 10 jeon at a regular theater these days is probably only done in Joseon on this earth. But anyway, it's a good thing. It's a bit disappointing that even movies, which have the closest meaning to the general public than any other art, are becoming small and distant from the general public, so we must assume that the new fans who flocked in when they charged 10 jeon are truly movie fans.

If I had only Lon Chaney's weekly salary (15,000 won), I could make at least five movies with it in our land - how can there be such a difference between heaven and earth?

But here is a groundbreaking product - that is - Arirang - Arirang - that is it.

The protagonist, who supposedly went crazy while attending school, we, who are bumping into the reality of today, don't even want to repeat why he went crazy. All - a collapsing thatched house - the signboard of the Youth Association (靑年會) is attached to the outer pillar that looks like a temporary stall. Gi - Mr. Park, a rural intelligentsia who goes to work with the hem of his durumagi fluttering in the cool breeze - a college student who studied in Seoul and returned home wearing a Western suit and a conical hat with farmers - singing and dancing "A bountiful year has come. A bountiful year has come." - This is a movie that was born in Joseon with everything in the background of Joseon. The flag of the Youth Association flutters and the procession of members is seen. How longing is this scene? <108>

Instead of flashy and elaborate scenes, the unbearable frustration of the heart and painful regret appears in this scene.

Even in terms of technique, it is not inferior to the movies of Japan, which has decades of history in film. I have seen the so-called new movies of Japan as much as anyone else, but I have never seen a movie that truly smells of Japan. Only because there is no light and no set, we have to give way there.

Anyway, I am very happy to see such a harvest in the Joseon film industry in just two years. Anyway - this - the movie Arirang burns all the past Joseon movies - this - you can't live without money, and only sighs fill the land, and something cries wildly in the sky - it's a giant statue.

Anyway, in Joseon, the only thing that can quickly show us everything is movies. 5-jeon reflectors? I hope they continue to be active.

The culture of Joseon is gradually entering into movies. Everyone is saying movies-movies. One movie, one movie - Arirang, one movie. And what will you show us after that? Joseon film industry -

Modern civilization is, no matter what, radio, sports, and cinema.
When will these civilizations become closer to us? -End- <109>

**Summary**

This article discusses the introduction and impact of radio, sports, and cinema in 1920s Joseon, then under Japanese colonial rule. It highlights the disparities in access to these modern forms of entertainment and information, with radio being primarily a luxury for the wealthy, sports becoming increasingly commercialized and exploitative, and cinema struggling to find its footing as a medium for authentic Joseon stories. The author expresses a desire for these cultural forms to become more accessible and relevant to the lives of ordinary Joseon people.

The most interesting aspect of this article is its critical perspective on the introduction of modern entertainment in a colonial context, revealing the economic and social inequalities of the time. The author's concern that these forms of entertainment are being used as tools by the colonizers or for profit, rather than for the benefit of the Korean people, is revealing of the cultural anxieties of the time.
