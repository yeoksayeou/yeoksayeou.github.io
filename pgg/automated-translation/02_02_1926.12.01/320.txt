How Magazines, Newspapers, or Printed Matter Reach the Reader's Hands
						原稿紙 (Wŏn'goji)

Nephew of Clerk Kim. What's the point of getting such a headache over a few dozen *jŏn*? Calm down and please read carefully. It should still be worth a few dozen *jŏn*. If you hadn't bought that magazine (or book) with that money, but instead bought alcohol, tobacco, or some other amusement, you probably wouldn't have suffered such a headache. For someone respectable, who also considers themselves a person of common sense, or someone who spends money quite freely, what is there to grumble so much about after buying one magazine for a few dozen *jŏn*? Even if you had given it to a beggar or simply donated it, there would be no need to nag like this two or three times.

Nephew of Clerk Kim. You act smart, nephew, but I believe you still need to experience more hardship (*lit. empty your rice bowl more*). You pretend to be a person of common sense and strut about, but you still need to broaden your knowledge and experience.

Nephew, do you have any idea about the path and the ordeal that all printed materials—newspapers, magazines, books, etc.—go through until they reach your hands, until they reach the hands of all readers? You probably don't know. You likely just think, "Oh, it's a magazine," or "Oh, it's a newspaper," assuming they were originally made this way, without knowing the detailed path they took or where they came from (所經路와 所出來). I'll tell you, so please listen. Don't just get angry for no reason.

To talk about the path printed matter takes until it reaches the reader's hands—really, one needs to have eaten a hearty lunch first. I might not be able to speak on an empty stomach. Truly, the twists and turns (波瀾曲折) pile up so much, like untangling a messy ball of yarn, that it's bewildering and vague where to find the starting point and where to establish the end; I cannot find the thread to begin the story.

Never mind magazines or books; even talking about myself, who goes by the name Manuscript Paper (原稿紙), the path is quite complex. From the raw materials, from the paper mill (製紙所), from the binding (製本), until arriving on the reporter's desk (冊床), recounting those twists and turns is truly a headache no less severe than yours, nephew. It sounds like self-promotion, so I won't go into detail, but it's generally like this. <92>

If this is what it takes for manuscript paper to become manuscript paper, what kind of ordeal must the path of a magazine or book entail? When the manuscript paper leaves the hands of the reporter or author, leaves their mind, leaves the tip of their brush (붓 끗트), and is handed over to the printing house (印刷所); then goes through typesetting (採字), composition (植字), page makeup (整版), proofreading (校正), stereotype plating (鉛版) or making paper molds (紙形), the printing press (印刷機械), and binding (製本); arrives at the distribution department (發送部), is tied up one or two volumes in wrappers (封皮), has a 1 or 2 *jŏn* stamp firmly attached, is loaded onto a cart (구루마), goes to the post office (우편국) to be firmly stamped, travels by automobile (自働車), by train (기차), carried on the backs of postmen (체부), seeking out readers in every nook and cranny (방방곡곡)—this ordeal is truly so tedious that words cannot fully describe it.

Nephew of Clerk Kim, those 4-5 pages, 7-8 pages, or perhaps the short or long essays, political commentary, poems, novels, impressions, travelogues, and all other miscellaneous writings (雜俎) published in that magazine might seem like rather insignificant, valueless things, right? However, the path that one essay or one poem takes to reach the reader is truly full of tremendous twists and turns (波瀾). Nephew, do you understand? Setting aside the author's painstaking effort (苦心苦行) to create that one piece, first listen to the story of the editorial department (編輯室).

Early in the month, or late in the previous month, they already hold a meeting about editing the next month's issue. Based on the principles and stances expressed by the magazine, based on important events in society at that time, representing the mood of the month, they thoroughly discuss everything—whether essays, political commentary, academic or literary works, other miscellaneous pieces, even the filler space (餘白欄). Then, assigning tasks—so-and-so for essays, so-and-so for the politics section, so-and-so for the society section, so-and-so for the academic section, so-and-so for the literature section, so-and-so for the hobby section—they agree on a deadline (締切) for a specific day and time, and then each person takes responsibility, tucks a roll of manuscript paper under their arm, and departs. Some rack their brains, some torment their fingertips (손끗을 잘니고), some use the tip of their tongue (혀끗 - i.e., interviewing/talking), and some run around in all directions (四面八方).

Look here! Consider the anguish of the one responsible for the essay, and the ordeal of all others responsible for their sections—they might look like someone heartbroken or like a mental patient, sitting blankly at their desk, looking up at the roof, collapsing onto the floor and looking up at the ceiling, going out into the yard <93> to stroke the flowers, climbing the back hill to roll stones, going to the riverbank to build sandcastles. They draw pictures with their toes, write characters on their palms, mutter to themselves, stand blankly looking at Namsan mountain—they perform all sorts of bizarre acts. They look like jobless mental patients. But in their heads, there is absolutely nothing but gathering material. Thinking about the title, thinking about the main theme and structure (主義綱領), thinking about classification and organization, thinking about conception and refinement—it's truly not just one or two days of anguish. When the general outline is decided, they take the manuscript paper and sit at the desk. They write one character and pay attention, write one page and read it over, sometimes tearing it up (쯔저버리고), sometimes crossing things out (黑線을 그으며), sometimes getting angry, sometimes laughing, sometimes sweating, sometimes sighing. Writing that one piece is so difficult. When one piece is finished, they might smoke a cigarette or have a drink as if relieved. But then fear arises again. Will this essay benefit the readers or not? Might there be refutations? What if it causes harm? Will there be any excessive impact on reputation or social standing? Setting those aside, might it be subject to deletion (削除) or confiscation (押收)? Such doubts and fears are considerable. So, they review it again and again, revise (添削) and revise again, and finally, with a final act of courage, submit it to the editorial department.

The anguish of those in charge of the politics or economics sections is similar. They rummage through newspapers, skim other magazines, go to the library, visit government offices (官公署), observe the ever-changing society (時變時生), and gather materials from various sources before sitting down with the manuscript paper. Judging the timeliness of issues, the truth, importance, and scale of events, criticism and debate—it is truly the most difficult of difficult tasks (難事中 難事).

And what about the ordeal of the one in charge of the society section? It's truly worse than being a servant (머슴군) for a wealthy family. There's nowhere they don't go and nothing they don't see. Government offices, banks, companies (社會 - used here likely for businesses/society orgs), schools, private homes (個人家), parks, theaters, intersections (네거리), back alleys (뒷골목), bars (술집), brothels (妓生집), gambling dens (蝎哺집 - likely 노름집, gambling house), fight scenes (싸홈터), dumping grounds/places of misery (쓰림터), fire scenes, flood scenes—there's nowhere they don't go and nothing they don't see. When enough material is gathered, they sit down with the manuscript paper. After the manuscript is finished, they too face all sorts of bizarre situations like deletion, confiscation, bans (禁止), defamation (名譽毁損), trials (裁判), imprisonment (징역). Poems, novels, and other miscellaneous writings, even filler content (餘白), all involve such anguish and ordeal. Nephew, do you understand?

Thus, when all the manuscripts arrive by the deadline, they gather together, decide on the magazine's layout (體裁) and arrangement, calculate page numbers (頁數) and line counts (行數), discuss the cover (表紙) and <94> table of contents (목차), determine the issue number (號數), margins (갓도와 餘白 - 갓도 likely refers to outer margins/gutters), and advertisements (廣告), and only then is it handed over to the printing house. (If required by the Publication Law (出版法), after submitting the manuscript for official censorship review (原稿檢閱)). Clerk Kim, your nephew. How about it? Did you realize it wasn't so easy?

When it goes to the printing house, the composition department (植字部) takes charge, considers the magazine's table of contents, page numbers, issue number, arrangement, etc., tears off the manuscripts one or two pieces at a time, and passes them to the typesetting department (採字部). The typesetting department takes these and assigns the typesetting to about ten typesetters (採字員). Following the characters in the manuscript (天字, 地字, 原稿에 잇는 글자대로 - likely meaning 'exactly as written'), according to the font size (호수), they set the type. When the typesetting is done, it comes back to the composition department. The composition department refers to the manuscript and arranges the set type according to the manuscript. They make the printing plate (造版/製版). Once the plate is made, this time it's pressed on a proof press (押機) and sent to the proofreading room (校正室). The proofreaders (校正人) compare it against the manuscript and correct errors (正誤). When the first proof (초준) is done, this time it's sent to the plate correction department (정판부). The plate correctors re-set the type according to the corrections. It's sent back to the proofreading room for a second check (再準). After undergoing third (三準) and fourth checks (四準) like this until there are no typos (誤植), it is sent to the stereotype department (鉛版部) to make stereotype plates (鉛版). (Sometimes it goes directly onto the press (機械) without stereotypes). Then it goes onto the printing press. With 4 or 5 machines operating, they can print 4,000-5,000 to over 10,000 copies in a day or two. Once printing is finished, this time it goes to the binding department (제본부). The binding department folds the pages in order (頁數 次序대로), staples them (鐵絲를 끼고), attaches the cover (표지를 듯치고), trims the edges (도련을 하야), ties them into bundles of 50 or 100 books, and sends them to the distribution department (발송부). The distribution department first submits mandatory copies (納本) to the Government-General (總督府) or the post office, then spreads out the mailing list (발송장), and quickly sends them out—whether all at once or individually, within the city or regionally, by mail or by rail. If, unfortunately, a confiscation order (압수명령) is issued during distribution, everything stops, and the entire stock, according to the number of books, is seized. Detectives (형사) from the police station (경찰서) come and carry it all away cleanly. Everyone stares blankly, just sighing, and then prepares an emergency issue (臨時號) or an extra (號外). They do double the work.

After so much difficulty and so many twists and turns, it reaches the reader's hands. How much suffering is involved?

Nephew of Clerk Kim. Did you understand? Now, please don't suffer that headache. Ah, I'm feeling faint (虛氣症). I'll stop here. -The End- <95>

**Summary**

Written from the perspective of personified manuscript paper ("Wŏn'goji"), this 1926 essay details the arduous and complex process behind publishing magazines, newspapers, and books. It addresses a complaining reader ("Kim Chusa's nephew"), explaining the journey from the raw materials of paper, through the intense creative and editorial labor (writing, researching, editing, layout), the multi-stage printing process (typesetting, proofreading, plating, printing, binding), and finally distribution, emphasizing the numerous difficulties and potential setbacks like censorship and confiscation. The aim is to instill appreciation for the effort and cost behind printed materials. For historians of colonial Korea, the article offers valuable insights into the material culture of publishing, including the specific technical steps involved in printing during the 1920s. More significantly, it explicitly mentions the Publication Law, manuscript censorship (`原稿檢閱`), confiscation (`押收`) by police (`경찰서`) acting under the Government-General (`總督府`), and the fear authors and editors felt, vividly illustrating the constraints and pressures faced by the Korean press and intellectuals under Japanese rule.

雜誌나 新聞이나 印刷物이 讀者의 손에 들기까지
						原稿紙
金主事네 족하. 돈 멧십전으로 그다지 두통 아를 것이야 무엇잇소. 안심하고 어서 차근차근히 읽어 보십시오. 그래도 돈 멧십전 가치는 잇스리이다. 족하가 만약 그 돈으로 그 雜誌를 (혹은 書冊) 사지 안코 술이나 담배나 다른 오락물을 사섯드면 그러케 두통은 알치 안엇스리이다. 점잔은 처지에 겸하야 常識家로 自處하는 이가 또는 돈푼이나 헌덕헌덕 쓰신다는 이가 돈 멧 십전으로 잡지 한 권 사시고 그러케 중얼거릴 것이야 무엇 잇겟소? 거지를 주엇거나 거저 寄附를 한 셈 처도 그러케까지 두 번 세 번 잔소리 할 것은 업스리이다.
金主事네 족하. 족하 검리한 체 하지만 아직 밥 그릇이나 더 업시해야 될줄 암니다. 常識家인 척하고 줏척대지만 그래도 聞見을 좀 더 널켜야 될 걸요.
족하 대관절, 신문이나 잡지나 서책 등 모든 인쇄물이 족하의 손에 들기까지 모든 讀者의 손에 가기까지 그 경로와 고행이 엇더 하얏는 것을 아시오? 아마 모르리이다. 그저 대체로 잡지거니 新聞이거니 본래 이러케 생긴 것 이거니 할 뿐이고 그 所經路와 所出來는 자세히 모르리이다. 알켜 들일게 드러 보서요. 空然히 골내지 마시구요.
印刷物이 讀者의 손에 들기까지의 所經路-니약이 할랴면 실로 點心 그릇이나 톡톡이 먹고 해야지. 虛氣가 들녀 말 못하리이다. 참말 波瀾曲折 重疊하야 헛트러진 실뭉치를 푸는 것처럼 어디서 始點을 잡고 어디서 終端을 삼을지 實로 깜앗코 막연하야 말의 端緖를 잡을 수 업슴니다.
雜誌나 書冊은 말말고 爲先原稿紙라는 이름을 가진 내 자신부터 말한데도 그 經路가 여간 複雜하지가 안슴니다. 原料로부터, 製紙所로부터, 製本으로부터, 기자의 冊床에 오기까지, 그 波瀾曲折을 말하면 이야말로 족하의 두통보다 못하지 안은 두통거리 임니다. 자가광고 가태서 자세는 말치 안슴니다만은 대개 이럿습니다.＜92＞
원고지가 원고지 되기 까지가 이러하고 보니 잡지나 서책의 경로야 그 苦行이 엇더 하겟슴닛가? 원고지가 기자나 著述家의 손으로부터, 머리ᄉ속으로부터, 붓 끗트로부터, 印刷所에 넘어가서, 다시 採字로부터, 植字로부터, 整版으로부터, 校正으로부터, 鉛版 或 紙形으로부터, 印刷機械로부터, 製本으로부터, 發送部에 와가지고 한 권 두권 封皮에 묵거서, 1전이나 2전표를 딱 붓치고, 구루마에 실녀서, 우편국에 가서 도장을 딱 박어 가지고, 自働車로, 기차로, 체부의 등에 업혀가지고, 방방곡곡 독자들 차저가는 이 고행이야 말로 참말 지긋지긋하야 말을 다하지 못하겟슴니다.
