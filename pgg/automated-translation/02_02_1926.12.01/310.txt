Story of Motion Pictures, They Say the Modern Age is the World of Motion Pictures, But When Did They First Arrive in Chosŏn?

If you ask who MacDonald is, only a few among the masses can answer that he is the leader of the British Labour Party. But if you ask who Mary Pickford is, everyone brightens up as if hearing the name of their own friend, saying she is a famous actress of American motion pictures. It has become a fact that people known to the world through motion pictures are more numerous than world figures in politics; this is because, without needing to laugh, motion pictures have greater intimacy with the masses.

Motion pictures here, motion pictures there; this matter involves motion pictures, that matter involves motion pictures; hygiene propaganda uses motion pictures, education propaganda uses motion pictures, politics uses motion pictures too. Eventually, in the West, governments started jumping in, using national funds to produce motion pictures informing people of the necessity of war. Even the Government-General of Chosŏn has come to travel around boasting through motion pictures about how well they govern Chosŏn. The world has truly become the world of motion pictures. This is because it has been realized that entertainment, once dismissed merely as "play" (『노리』), possesses a greater power than anything else in dominating the thoughts of the masses.

The masses of Chosŏn and Motion Pictures! Though it may seem amusing, it is not something to be dismissed lightly. For the pitiful masses of Chosŏn, who have fewer entertainments than others, who laughed while watching unfamiliar foreign pictures even though there were only a few permanent theaters (or playhouses) and little intimacy with the films – just now, a few Chosŏn films have begun to appear. At this time when this joyful phenomenon is emerging, looking back at the time when motion pictures were first screened in Chosŏn will surely not be a meaningless task.

First Film Screening at Kwangmudae

As far as I know, the first time something called motion pictures came to Chosŏn was about 20 years ago from now. At the Kwangmudae (光武臺), a wooden building inside the grounds of the Electric Company near Tongdaemun (東大門) in Seoul, entry was permitted if you brought just ten empty cigarette packs.<90> Compared to when Americans first operated a motion picture hall in China, begging spectators to enter while giving them 5 *chŏn* each, this could rather be called a boom.

Mr. Kolburan (골부란), an American who owned the Electric Company, borrowed machinery from Mr. Maren (마렌), who ran a hotel outside Sŏdaemun (西大門), and began screenings at Kwangmudae. Thinking back on those times now, there is truly a strong feeling that it was a different world.

Drawn by the sounds of the *hojeok* (traditional oboe) and *janggu* (drum), amidst the chatter of "They say pictures move!" "They say pictures move!", I went in holding ten cigarette packs (卷煙匣). On the stage, a curtain painted with the American flag and the Chosŏn flag was hung, and a thick rope was tied horizontally in front of it. First, a girl acrobat came out and walked the rope. Then, the curtain was pulled aside, and a Chosŏn woman performed several dances (like the Monk's Dance, 僧舞). After that, the lights went out, so I waited, thinking, "Eek, the motion pictures are coming out!" It remained completely dark for quite a while, and then a group of Westerners wearing pitch-black overcoats, standing stiffly, were brightly illuminated.

"They said it moves, but where is it moving?" "It just stands there day and night." Amidst this murmuring, one person pretending to know said, "No, that white stuff is fallen snow, and those are people frozen to death because it's cold."

"Oh, that's why they stand there without moving an inch..." If someone had only explained that these were magic lantern slides (幻燈), it would have been good, but without explanation, they just showed the motion [picture]—so people kept thinking everything that appeared was frozen people. After those frozen people changed several times, without any greeting, without explanation, without notice, without advertisement, the motion picture simply came on right after the magic lantern slides.

There were no subtitles. Suddenly, a Western woman was washing clothes inside a room. A puppy came in and dirtied the laundry, so the woman chased the puppy out. Then, a tall man holding a long hunting rifle came in and fired the gun. The woman ran around frantically, trying to escape. Then a whistle blew sharply, the lights came back on, and that was it. The projectionist was even a Chosŏn person, but perhaps he was fiddling with the machine, as the picture would flee into a hollow and then get swept to the far edge—it was utter chaos. That was all there was. No explanation, no introduction, no music... Thinking about it now, it must have been a segment cut from some comedy film that had become unusable and been discarded.<91>

**Summary**

This 1926 article recounts the author's memory of the first motion picture screening in Chosŏn, estimated to be around 1906 at the Kwangmudae theater in Seoul, operated by the American-owned electric company. It describes the primitive conditions: admission via cigarette packs, audience confusion between lantern slides and actual moving images, poor projection quality by a Korean technician, and the showing of a fragmented, unexplained film clip. The author contrasts this early experience with the growing pervasiveness and influence of film by 1926, noting its use in propaganda by both Western nations and the Japanese Government-General in Korea, and expresses cautious optimism about the recent emergence of domestically produced Chosŏn films. For historians, the article offers a valuable first-hand perspective on the initial reception of cinema in colonial Korea, detailing audience reactions, the technical crudeness of early exhibitions, the role of foreign entrepreneurs, and the evolving perception of film from mere novelty to a powerful medium for mass communication and colonial propaganda. It also marks a specific point in time when indigenous filmmaking was just beginning.

活動寫眞 이약이, 現代는 活働寫眞의 世上 이라는데 朝鮮에는 그것이 언제 드러왓섯나
맥드날드가 누군 줄 아느냐? 고 무르면 英國 勞動黨의 수령이라고 대답할 사람이 대중의 속에는 몃 사람이 못 되지만 메리-픽 포드가 누구냐고 무르면 누구든지 자긔 친구의 일홈이나 드른 것처럼 미국 활동사진의 名女優라고 반가워한다. 정치로 세계적 인물 보다는, 활동사진으로 세상에 일홈이 알려진 사람이 더 만케 된 것이 사실이니 웃지 안어도 活動寫眞이 더 대중과 親密性을 가진 까닭이다.
여긔서도 활동사진, 저긔서도 활동사진, 이 일도 활동사진, 저 일도 활동사진, 위생선전도 활동사진, 교육선전도 활동사진, 정치에도 활동사진, 긔어코 서양에서는 정부에서 드리덤벼 국고금으로 전쟁의 필요를 알니는 활동사진을 박혀 내기 시작하고 조선의 총독부까지 조선 통치 잘한다고 활동사진으로 자랑을 하며 도라다니게 까지 세상은 활동사진의 세상이 되고 말엇다. 『노리』라고 우습게만 녁이 든 娛樂이, 대중의 생각을 支配하는데에 아모 것보다도 큰 힘을 가진 것을 알게된 까닭이다.

朝鮮의 대중과 活動寫眞! 우스운 일 갓해도 等閑히 생각해 바릴 일은 못 된다. 남보다 더 娛樂도 가지지 못한 朝鮮의 대중, 常設館(或 演劇場)도 몃낫이 업지만 寫眞도 親密性 적은 남의 것만 가지고 우서오던 불상한 대중에게 이마 적은 朝鮮 映畵가 나타나기 시작하엿다. 이 깃븐 現象이 나타나는때, 朝鮮에 맨 처음 活動寫眞이 上映되는 때를 도라다 보는 것도 결코, 무의미한 일은 아닐 것이다.

광무대에서 처음 映畵
조선에 活動寫眞 이란 것이 처음 오기는(내가 알기까지는) 지금으로 보터 약 20년 전이엿다. 서울 東大門턱 電氣會社 터안에 잇던 목제 집 光武臺에서 담배갑(븬 것) 열장만 가지고 가＜90＞면 입장을 식혓섯스니 中國에서 米國人이 처음 活動寫眞館을 經營할 때에 구경꾼에게 돈 5錢씩 주어 가면서 입장을 애걸하엿는데 비하야 도로혀 호황이라 할 것 이엿다.
電氣會社의 임자인 米人 골부란씨가 西大門 外에서 호텔을 경영하는 마렌 씨의 긔계를 빌려서 광무대에서 映寫를 시작한 것인대, 지금 그 때의 일을 생각하면, 참으로 딴 세상일 갓흔 늣김이 만타.
호적과 장구소리에 끌려서 『사진이 나와논 다지.』 『사진이 나와논데.』 하고 떠드는 틈에 끼여 담배ᄉ갑(卷煙匣) 열장을 들고 드러가니 무대에는 미국긔와 됴선긔를 그린 휘장을 처놋코 그 휘장압헤 굵은 줄은 가로 매여 놋코 맨-먼저는 소녀광대가 나와서 줄을 타고 그 다음에 휘장을 거더 치우더니 조선 녀자가 춤(僧舞等)을 몃가지 추고 그리고 불이 꺼지는 고로 『익크 활동사진이 나온다.』 하고 기다렷더니 한참이나 캄캄한대로 잇다가 실껌언 외투를 닙은 서양사람 한떼가 웃둑웃둑 서잇는 것이 환-하게 빗취엿다.
『나와 논다더니 어데노-나.』 『밤낫 고대로 섯기만 하네 그려.』 이럿케 수군수군 하는 중에 아는 체 하는 한 분이 『아니야 저-허연 것은 눈이 와서 싸힌 것이고 추어서 어러죽은 사람들이라오.』 하엿다.
『올-치 그럿킬네 저럿케 꼼짝도 못하고 섯지...』 누구던지 이것은 환등(幻燈)이라고 설명해 주엇드면 조왓슬 것을 설명도 안하고 활동을 빗치닛가-나오는 것마다 어러죽은 사람이라고만 보고 잇섯다. 몇 번인지 그 어러죽은 사람이 밧귀여 나오고 나서 아모 인사도 업시 설명도 업시 통지도 업시 광고도 업시 그냥 환등 뒤 끗헤 활동사진이 나왔다.
자막도 업고 댓자 곳자로 서양부인 하나가 방속에서 빨내를 하는데 강아지가 드러와서 빨내를 더럽혀 놋는 고로 부인이 강아지를 내여 쫏츠닛가 엇던 키 큰 남자 하나가 길다란 산양총을 들고 드러와서 총을 노닛가 부인이 이리저리 쫏겨 다니느라고 발광을 하다가 호각 소리가 후루룩 나고 불이 다시 켜지고 그만 그 뿐이엿다. 그나마 사진기사는 조선 사람이엿는데 긔계를 들고 작란을 하는지 사진이 움´구텅이로 다라낫다가 저 끗으로 쓸려 갓다가 야단법석이엿다. 다만 그 뿐 이엿다. 설명도 업시 소개도 업시 음악도 업시... 지금 생각하면 어는 희극 사진이 못 쓰게 되야 내여 버린 것을 한 토막 끈어 가지고 나와 든 것이엿다.＜91＞
