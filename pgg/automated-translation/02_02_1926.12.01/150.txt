Everything Seen is a World Rarity: Sightseeing in Paris, France - Miscellaneous Observations and Thoughts During a Trip Abroad
By Doctor of Medicine Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae

The World's Rarest Treasure, a Lock for a Woman's Chastity
Though I have come to Paris, I have not yet settled down; my purpose and direction are still undecided, and thus I still do not know what this place called France, Paris, France, Paris is really like, nor how its people live.

Today, like yesterday, our group gathered to figure out where to go. Mr. S, a friend of Mr. N in our group, came specially to guide us. Mr. S has been in Paris for at least six years and is a Paris expert (巴里通). We decided to entrust ourselves entirely to him, asking for his guidance and simply following him. Mr. S, seemingly quite capable, led the forty or fifty of us out the door, heading first to the University of Paris (巴里大學). The architectural style and facilities were all things I was seeing for the first time, and every explanation I heard sparked endless interest. Among them, there was something even stranger. Right across from the University of Paris is a museum called the Musée de Cluny (Musee de Clung Sonbonne Pan-theon - *likely a confusion of nearby landmarks: Musée de Cluny, Sorbonne, Panthéon*). The building didn't look excessively large, but it seemed very old. Mr. S, continuously trying to pique our interest, steered us into this museum, slowly showing us around room by room; truly, the depth of interest it held cannot be recorded in full detail. Among these, thanks to Mr. S's special guidance and introduction, we saw a world treasure (天下珍物) that we could never have imagined even in our dreams. Truly, France (佛國) is a country full of strange things... There were two items displayed, so-called 'chastity belts' (貞操帶), which could be called locks for a woman's chastity. At a glance, they looked like they were made to be worn like a belt ('빤도' 혁대 - *possibly 'band' belt*), with an ivory protrusion below, and the belt was made of metal that could be locked; the construction seemed extremely simple. According to the explanation, they had been used since the old Napoleonic era <41> but fell out of use in between. It is said they were created by soldiers going off to war or merchants traveling far, who, leaving their beloved concubines behind and worrying about their chastity being lost in their absence, would put this belt on them and take the key, thus ensuring peace of mind about their chastity while far away.
This is truly something rarely seen from our Eastern moral perspective and the first time hearing of it. One wonders how much attention they paid to such matters, or how much lack of liberation, freedom, and trust existed between couples; this object vividly explains it even today.
Besides this, there were many other fascinating items, whether swans or whatnot, but deciding to see them again later, we were about to exit when Mr. S mentioned there were picture postcards for sale. I bought two postcards of the chastity belt (貞操帶 寫眞) to use as conversation material after returning home.

World Famous Landmarks: The Arc de Triomphe and the Eiffel Tower
Our sightseeing had no fixed direction or order. Wherever our feet took us led us to the Arc de Triomphe (凱旋門). Though I had heard much about it already and had anticipated its magnificence, actually facing it, I couldn't help but praise its grandeur. There were indeed 264 steps; its height (高) is 160 feet and width 164 feet, making it the world's number one triumphal arch. Napoleon (奈翁) I commissioned it in 1806 after utterly defeating the Austrian army, and it was completed during the reign of Louis Philippe (루이, 후리릿프). Around its perimeter are the names and sculptures of 650 officers who died in battle during the Napoleonic era, from the French Revolution until peacetime, specifically from 1792 to 1815. This place was originally closed and not open for public viewing, but it was opened on the occasion of the visit of the British Emperor George (찌요지) V to Paris after the signing of the European War Peace Treaty, on November 28, 1918, as a gesture of welcome, reopening what had been closed since 1870. So, our being able to see it can be indirectly attributed to the British Emperor's grace. Following the stairs up, there was a wide plaza of about 100 pyeong. Here, young ladies selling picture postcards caught the eye. Looking out at the city from here, it unfolded beneath like a panorama. Among the sights, the distant Eiffel (엣후엘) Tower stood out, even taller than this.
The Eiffel Tower!! Visitors to Paris must first see the Arc de Triomphe and this Eiffel Tower. Looking into its history, the German Eiffel was its builder, and it was completed in three years, from 1887 to 1889.
It's not just Paris's viewing spot; one can see as far as 55 ri (approx. 22 km) into the outskirts. It has three levels: the first level's height (高) is 30 kan (間 - approx. 55m), the second is 60 kan (approx. 110m), and the third is 150 kan (approx. 273m), making the height from the ground to the very top truly 165 kan (approx. 300m). That's 300 meters high, and the third level is used as a wireless telegraph station.
There's an interesting story about this tower: during the height of the war, when the German army advanced like splitting bamboo right up to the outskirts of Paris, the German Kaiser (獨逸 카이젤) had prepared a huge German flag, hundreds of pyeong in size, intending to hoist it <42> first on this tower upon the fall of Paris. This alone shows how unpredictable the German ambitions were at that time, and also how much they targeted this Eiffel Tower.

What About Paris Transportation?
Whatever one observes, the depth is limitless depending on the observer's level. We couldn't just stand around indefinitely looking; as dusk was already falling, our group headed back to our lodgings. Although I don't know how many types of transportation there are, we frequently hailed taxis (自動車). Moreover, the cheapness of the fares was truly surprising. Using one taxi was cheaper than four people taking a tram, which was beyond expectation.
I haven't had the chance to ride the tram yet, so I have no experience, but since it's such a bustling and complex place, it's novel how they utilize the space between the tram rails (레루) for the electrical power to come up from the ground, eliminating overhead electrical lines which would be obstructions. For a country person seeing this for the first time, the question arises as to where the electricity for the tram comes from. Next is the subway, also known as the 'Métro' (메드로). The convenience it offers, with twice as many passengers traveling underground as above, and its network reaching everywhere, even under the great Seine River (세이뉴 大河) that bisects Paris, is significant. There are 1st and 2nd class carriages, and trains seem to depart at least three times every two minutes. Being fast and cheap, I finally realized how convenient it is.

The National Opera!!
On the way back, the group had a simple dinner and headed to the famous Opera (오페라) around 7 o'clock. As soon as our car arrived at the Opera's main entrance, one or two young women rushed over, opened the car door, and guided us to the main gate. I thought to myself, perhaps because it's a world-class opera house, even the reception (接客) is this kind. But then I realized they were begging (求乞) for a few pennies from guests arriving at the Opera entrance. As foreigners, we felt obliged by appearances and gave them some money; it seems trying to maintain the appearance of a gentleman (紳士) can be quite burdensome. This Opera was built under the direction of Garnier (칼니이 - *likely Charles Garnier*) between 1861 and 1875<43>, architecturally artistic, with a museum and library in the center, and portraits of famous composers, musicians, and dancers (딴스) hung everywhere. I also heard that on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays, entry is prohibited without formal wear (禮服), making me realize this is a place one might not even be able to visit for lack of proper clothes. The 3rd and 4th class seats were already sold out. Apparently, you can't get them unless you book at least four or five days in advance. Through special request, we managed to get 2nd class seats, which cost around 7 won each – an exorbitant ticket price.
Once inside, there wasn't anything particularly novel, but its grandeur and well-equipped facilities were unforgettable. The play (劇題) was 'Carmen' (칼멘), but it seemed very different from what we knew. Since we couldn't understand the language either, there wasn't much enjoyment, and instead, we were engrossed in observing the audience members who had come to watch. It is often said that if you want to know the level of culture (文物程度) and the character of the people of any country, you can tell by observing those concentrated at the Opera. Therefore, the representative gentlemen and ladies of France (佛國) must be present in this venue, and one can also discern their fashionable clothing styles here. Anyway, after the final curtain fell, we each returned to our lodgings.
Thus, having spent several days sightseeing without pause, busily doing nothing (無事分主), I still haven't reached a state of being settled in Paris (巴里安着). Taking this opportunity tonight to write down a few lines about my experiences, I think about how I have come to this foreign land, fifty thousand ri away, striving to plan for the future, and I can't help but wonder how things are back home. The bustling, noisy city has already quieted down, and in the room, there is only me, writing. It is now 11:20 PM. What time is it in my home country now? The time difference between Europe and my home country is about 8 hours, so in Chosŏn (朝鮮), it must be 7:30 AM. They must be busy preparing breakfast, and everyone must be rushing to start their work (事務始作). But here, it is the time when one finally gets into bed to sleep peacefully. Although we exist together on the same Earth, how can there be such variations in life (生活)?
Since the Siberian Railway (西伯里 鐵道) is still impassable, this letter I am writing now will likely take the fastest route to reach my home country: crossing the Pacific, traversing America (米州), crossing the Pacific again, passing through Japan (日本), and finally reaching Chosŏn. It will probably take at least 25 days. Therefore, to see a reply to this letter, it will likely take about two months. In this place where even news is infrequent, I cannot help but feel lonely. Suddenly realizing that sentimental (센치메탈) fantasies are unnecessary, I lay down again, wondering if I should dream a sweet dream, waiting for tomorrow, and for a moment, I forgot everything.
*There seems to be a misplaced sentence fragment here, possibly related to the museum mentioned earlier:* "The clothes, gold and silver jewels (金銀 寶石), and gold brocades (금납능라) worn by Napoleon I (제1세) and Empress Josephine (지요세휜) during their coronation ceremony filled two rooms."

East Asian Exchange Students and Young French Women Companions!!
For observing any historical site (古蹟), at least some specialized knowledge about it is necessary. Therefore, for us laymen (門外漢), although there is much valuable material to see, it is difficult to understand the true meaning (眞情) of everything. Even if we don't understand, there isn't much we particularly feel the need to know, so we quickly grow weary of just looking for old things. As our group walked through the central streets for some time, window shopping (賣物삼아), what frequently caught our eye was the surprisingly large number of East Asian young men (東洋靑年) walking around with French women (佛國女子) like married couples. Later, hearing the details, I learned that indeed many East Asian young men have married in France. Among them, many Chinese students (中國學生) live with French women, which raises further questions. Although it happened long ago, the European War caused many French men to die in battle, reducing the number of fathers (父), leaving many women without support. Thus, they unhesitatingly commit their lives even to foreigners of completely different races. This suggests how much suffering must exist in their inner lives (生活). Seeing this for the first time, our eyes couldn't help but follow them again and again.

Visiting the Pasteur Institute!!
My profession is medicine, and my current purpose is also medicine. As a first-time visitor to such a bustling place like Paris, unable to find my bearings, now that I am here, I cannot neglect seeing something related to medicine. Pasteur (파스툴), a titan (泰斗) in the medical world and a global figure, is known to everyone. Just as some associate France with Pasteur, and Pasteur represents France, I couldn't miss visiting the place of our esteemed predecessor. Heading to the Pasteur Institute (파스툴 硏究所), it seemed his research continues just as he left it, without cease; his greatness cannot be praised enough. Following the explanations, I finished touring each research room <45> and came out to the main entrance hall again, then entered through a large door to the right. This was the tomb of Pasteur and his wife. A brief account of his life's research and explanations of several inventions he made were inscribed on the left and right walls, with the tomb in the center. Its splendid decoration astonished first-time viewers.

Metchnikoff's Skeleton
Guided next to the library, I entered to find dozens of sculptures on both sides depicting figures who made contributions to the medical field. Listening to the details of each would take too much time, and besides, I couldn't possibly remember them all. Among them, worth introducing, is the skeleton of Metchnikoff (메치니 코흐), who devoted his life's efforts at this institute, greatly benefiting the medical world and astounding the public; it still occupies a corner of the library (圖書室). According to his story, Metchnikoff, on his deathbed, requested that after his death, his skeleton be installed in Pasteur's library – his dying wish (遺言) – which is why it remains like this today. Looking at his skeleton, like I did, one cannot think of it merely as a spectacle. While admiring the achievements born from his lifelong hardships, one cannot help but bow one's head in respect. Requesting his skeleton be displayed like this was his unique idea; though he died that day, future generations will forever admire him. Perhaps imitating this, the great Japanese (日本) anatomist Ōsawa (大澤), upon his deathbed, asked that he be dissected and made into a specimen, and since then, it has been permanently installed at Tohoku University (東北大學). Such individuals receive respect from the medical world to this day.<46>

**Summary**

The author, Dr. Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae, recounts his initial experiences as a Korean visitor in Paris in 1926. Guided by an expatriate Korean, he visits landmarks like the University of Paris, the Musée de Cluny (noting a chastity belt), the Arc de Triomphe, and the Eiffel Tower. He comments on Parisian transportation (taxis, metro), attends the Opera ('Carmen'), and reflects on the cultural differences, the loneliness of being far from home (Chosŏn), and the long travel time for mail. He observes interactions between East Asian students and French women, attributing this partly to the demographic impact of WWI on France. Finally, as a medical doctor, he visits the Pasteur Institute, viewing Pasteur's tomb and the preserved skeleton of Metchnikoff, reflecting on their scientific contributions. From a historical perspective, the article is revealing for its portrayal of a Korean intellectual encountering European modernity during the colonial period. His observations on French society (chastity belts, post-WWI gender dynamics), comparisons with "Eastern morals," reflections on travel and communication limitations, and specific interest in Western scientific institutions like the Pasteur Institute offer valuable insights into the mindset and experiences of educated Koreans navigating the world under Japanese rule. Historians might find the blend of awe, cultural comparison, and personal reflection particularly interesting.

天下珍品, 女子의 貞操 잠을쇄
巴里에 오기는 왓스나 아즉도 안정치 못하야, 목적과 행방조차 질정이 업고 따라서 佛蘭西 巴里 佛蘭西 巴里하든 이곳 실정이 엇더한 곳이며 저들은 엇지들 지나는지도 나는 아즉 모른다.
오날도 어제와 가티 일행이 모여서 갈바를 도모하는중 일행중 N군의 친우 S군이 특히 우리를 위하여 안내차로 차저왓다. S군은 巴里에만 적어도 6년이나 오래잇서 巴里通이라. 우리는 전혀 同君에게만 맛기어 지도를 청하고 뒤만 따라다니기로 하엿다. S군는 못하는 솜씨가 안인듯이 사오십을 다리고 문 밧글 나서니 가는 곳은 先次로 巴里大學이엇다. 건축의 制度라든지 설비가 다 처음으로 보는바는 설명을 드를적마다 한이 업는 흥미를 갓게한다. 其中에서 더 이상한 것이 잇다. 바로 巴里大學 마츤 편으로(Musee de Clung Sonbonne Pan-theon)라는 박물관이 잇는데 건물은, 그리 과히 크도안은 모양이나 매우 오랜상십다. 世界名物 凱旋門과 엣후엘 塔
우리들의 보고저하는 바는 향방이 업고 순서가 업다. 임시임시 발 가는대로 가는곳이 즉 凱旋門 이엿스니 말은 발서부터 만히 듯고 그 장한 바는 미리 짐작지 안은바는 안이엿스나 막상 당하고 보니 그 웅장함에 칭송치 안을수가 업다. 계단수는 실로 264이엿고 놉히는(高) 160척 폭이 164척인 세계 제1위의 凱旋門이라. 이는 奈翁 제1세가 墺國軍을 여지업시 격파를 한 후에 1806년에 기공이 되여 「루이, 후리릿프」시대에 竣工이 된 것인데 그 주위로 도라가면서는 佛國革命時代부터 평화할 때까지 즉 1792년부터 1815년에 이르기까지 奈翁時代 전사한 장교 650인의 일홈과 조각이 잇다. 이곳은 본시 개방치 안이하여서 諸人은 관람치를 못하엿든 바엿는데, 英國皇帝 「찌요지」 제 5세가 歐洲戰亂 平和條約 調印 후 즉, 1918년 11월 28일 巴里 방문을 기회로 하엿든 바 환영의 의미로서 1870년 이래 폐쇄하엿든 바를 개방케 된 바이다. 그러니 우리가 구경하게 되는 바도 간접으로 英帝의 덕이라 할 수 있다. 계단을 따라 우흐로 올나간 즉 백평이나 되는 널분 광장이 잇다. 이곳서는 그림엽서 파는 색씨들이 잇서 눈을 끄을게 한다. 이곳서 시가를 望觀한 즉 「파노라마」와 가티 眼下에 전개되는 바이다. 其中에서도 멀니 「엣후엘」 탑은 이보다 더 놉히 눈에 띄운다.
「엣후엘」 탑!! 巴里 來訪者로서는 몬저 凱旋門과 이 「엣후엘」탑 두가지를 보아야 한다. 이 탑 來歷을 볼것 가트면 獨 Eiffel 이 건설자 이엇는데 1887년으로 1889년까지 3년만에 준공이 되엿다 한다.

이 탑에 대하여 滋味잇는 말이 잇스니, 한참 전쟁 당시에는 獨軍이 파죽의 세로 巴里부근까지 드러 왓슬때에 獨逸 「카이젤」은 수백평이 될만한 큰 獨逸國旗를 만드럿다가 巴里 함락을 따라 몬＜42＞저 이 탑에 달려고 준비까지 되엿섯다 한다. 이것만 보더래도 其時에 얼마나 獨人의 야심은 예측치 못할만큼 잇섯든 것을 알 것이요, 또 얼마나 이 엣후엘 탑을 목표로 노리고 잇섯든 것을 알 수 잇는 것이다.

나의 직업은 의학이요 지금으로 목적조차 의학이다. 巴里에 이와가티 처음오는 客으로서는 선후를 찻지 못하게 번화한 곳이라 이왕 나는 巴里에 왓슬것 갓흐면 의학에 대한 바도 한 번 구경치 안을 수가 업다. 의학계에 泰斗는 세계적 위인인 파스툴은 세인이 다 아는 바이다. 엇더한 이는 佛國이라면 파스툴을 聯想하고, 파스툴이라면 佛國을 대표한다는 말도 잇는것과 가티, 참으로 우리 선배인 곳을 안이 차즐수가 업자, 파스툴 硏究所로 향하니 그의 연구하든 그대로 아직도 여한이 업시 진행되는 모양이니, 그의 장함이야 다시 칭송치 안을 수가 업다. 硏究室마다 這這히 설명을 따라 구경을 다맛초＜45＞고 다시 현관으로 나오다 우측으로 큰 문을 열고 드러간다. 이곳은 파스툴 부부의 무덤이엇고 그의 일생 연구하든 간단한 내력이며 발명한 멧 가지의 설명이 좌우 편에 색이여 잇고 그 가운데로 무덤이 잇서 그 찬란히 장식함은 처음보는 자로서는 놀나게 되엿다.

「메치니코흐」의 骸骨
나는 다시 도서관으로 안내를 바더 드러가니 전 좌우로 조각으로 된 의학계의 공적을 낏친 수십명의 형적이 잇서 일일이 그 내력을 듯자면, 시간이 오해 될 뿐만 안이라, 다 두뇌에 기억좃차되지 안는다. 그 중에서 소개할 만한 것은 이 硏究所에서 일생에 盡力을 가티하여 의학계의 큰 유익을 주고 세인을 놀나게 하든 즉 「메치니 코흐」해골이 아직도 圖書室 한 편을 차지하고 잇다. 그의 내력을 들을 것 가트면  「메치니 코흐」가 臨終에서 이르러 자기가 사망한 후 이면은 그 해골을 파 스를 도서실에 장치하여 달나는 유언으로 지금까지 이와가티 된 모양인데, 나와가티 그 이의 해골을 볼적마다, 단순한 관람으로는 생각이 되지 안을 것이다. 적어도 그이의 일생의 고생하여 오든 공적을 감탄을 마지안을 동시에 저절로 그이에게 경의를 표하여 머리를 숙이지 안을 수가 업다. 자기의 해골을 이와가티하여 달나함도 특유한 그의 생각이엇스나, 그는 오날 죽엇지만 영원한 후배로서는 항상 그를 사모치 안을 수가 업다. 이를 모방함인지 日本 解剖學 大家大澤이란 이가 임종에 이르러서 자기를 해부하여 표본을 만들나 하엿슴으로 그 후 부터는 東北大學에 영원히 장치가 되엿스니 이런이는 다 금일까지 의학계에서 경의를 밧치는 바이다.＜46＞
