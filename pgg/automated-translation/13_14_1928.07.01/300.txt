잡지명: 別乾坤 第14號
발행일: 1928년 07월 01일
기사제목: 京城中等學校 校長夫人訪問記 (Gyeongseong Middle School Principals' Wives Interview)
기사형태: 대담·좌담 (Conversation/Symposium)

Gyeongseong Middle School Principals' Wives Interview
Mrs. Kim, wife of Mr. Choe Kyu-dong, Principal of Joongdong School, a harmonious housewife

If you go down a little way into the back alley of Waryong-dong, flanking the large street of Changdeokgung, you will pass a small side dish shop and find the nameplate of Choe Kyu-dong.

I visited the house a little after 4 p.m.

A woman in a light green jacket looked out from a room, paused, and then a woman who appeared to be in her forties, looking generous, sat in front of the twin windows of the main room, looking at the reporter with a very welcoming smile.

The reporter mumbled to himself, "Ah, my older sister and mother-in-law. No, the principal's wife."

He approached the front room and said, "Are you the principal's wife? Nice to meet you."

"Yes! Come on in. The baby is sick, so I have him on my lap…"

She answered in a Gyeongsang dialect, still smiling. I thought she would ask why I had come, but I was so happy at how gently and familiarly she treated me.

"Where are you sick?"

While caressing the baby,

"It's not serious, but he's a little sick."

Her maternal sincerity in cherishing and loving her son was evident throughout.

The reporter glanced at the clock hanging on the wall of the main room and asked,
<108>
"The principal hasn't come home yet, has he?"

"No. It's only half past three now. It's still a long way off. He usually comes home around six. He's always busy."

"Then dinner must be late."

"We have a big family. We don't usually eat dinner late. We have a very large family. If you include the students who are part of the family, there are eighteen of us. So we have dinner early, but I can't always make it on time for my husband."

"I see."

"On days when he comes home early from school, guests come. Even when he's in the middle of eating, if a guest comes, he'll drop his food and meet them, and only after the work is done will he eat the cold, tasteless food again. It's really difficult."

Whenever that happened, the wife seemed not only uncomfortable but also very anxious.

"When we were newlyweds, I would watch for my husband to come home and boil eggs in a pot and serve them to him when they were at their best, but these days, with so much going on and my body being sick day and night, I can't do that, so he can't eat well. It breaks my heart, but I can't help it."

The reporter, grateful for the wife's fluent story, and with the intention of carefully considering her concerns, asked,

"How distressed you must be? But doesn't the principal get angry?"

The wife seemed surprised by this question.

"No. He never says anything about anything. He doesn't show it at all. Even if something upsets him at school, he never gets angry at home. I've raised nine children, all of whom have gone to good schools, and while there have been times of hardship and worry, my husband never seems bothered. And I'm the same way. My husband and I both love our children. But every day he goes to school early and comes home late, and because of school or student matters, many guests come and he sits in his room or has dinner at gatherings, so he rarely gets to rest at home."

The wife was infinitely happy that the principal was in a high social position, but on the other hand, she seemed to feel lonely that he didn't spend much time at home.
<109>
The reporter asked another question.

"Does he drink alcohol?"

"No. Maybe a couple of glasses of beer."

"Did you tend to the flowers in the yard yourself?"

"I'm not very good at that either. My son, who is in Japan, planted them when he came back in the spring. I just give them water."

"You've been living in Seoul for over ten years, haven't you?"

"Yes, it's been seventeen years since we left our hometown of Gyeongsang Province."

"Do you ever go sightseeing with the principal?"

"We can't. He's busy day and night, and unlike before, we haven't done that in recent years. I don't even know about the famous 'Yosakura'. I'm always at home, unlike my husband. If a merchant passes by, I might go out to the gate to buy something. And while my husband goes to the hospital from time to time, I'm always unwell, so I can't go out and about or help him, which always makes me feel sorry."

I sat there for about an hour, but for a house with so many family members, it was very quiet. After seeing the principal's separate room, the reporter said goodbye, and the wife came out to the gate to see me off.

Just then, I saw her seven-year-old daughter returning from playing outside. The reporter asked again, "Is this a good neighborhood?"

"Not really, it's not a good place to raise children. But she'll be going to school next year, so it doesn't matter."

The reporter attached meaning to these words and returned, hearing the kind words, "Come again."

Mrs. Cheon, wife of Jeong Dae-hyeon, Principal of Boseong High School, an exemplary wife of an old family

All in all, a demure lady! From the way she ran the household to the way she hesitated to talk about things and was careful, she seemed to be an exemplary wife of an old family.

When the reporter arrived, she was busy making gochujang tteok (고추장떡, red pepper paste rice cakes).

"I'm from a magazine. I'd like to hear about your household. It may seem strange, but I'd like to introduce some of the things you do to other women."

"Yes, please do. But there's nothing special."

She answered so simply, as if she didn't understand, so I asked,

"You have a telephone at home, don't you?"

"Yes."

"When the principal isn't home, do you answer the phone?"

"No, the worker answers it."

"Do you do all the housework yourself?"

"I do most of it. I have a daughter-in-law and a housekeeper, but I take care of what my husband eats and wears."

"Does the principal wear suits often?"

"He's getting old, so unlike before, he's not comfortable in suits and mostly wears hanbok (한복, traditional Korean clothing)."

"Does the principal drink alcohol?"

"He drinks as a pick-me-up. He can't drink as much as he used to. And even when he does, he doesn't get angry. He's actually nicer. So he's very easy to live with."

"But since you do everything yourself, you must be very busy and tired."

"Not really. We don't have a big family. It's not that hard. We only have three children, and the oldest son is in Japan, so it's usually quiet. It's good for my husband to read books. But he often comes home late at night."

At this point, the wife looked a little lonely, then went to a corner of the yard where a rabbit cage was placed and began to give food from a full basket.

"You like rabbits."

"I don't dislike them, but my youngest daughter, who goes to art school, likes them and raises them. She had eight babies this time, but she gave them all away except for two."

Whether it was the daughter-in-law attending to her or the old housekeeper in the kitchen, everyone seemed to be proper and restrained.

"I came suddenly and caused you a lot of trouble."

"Don't mention it. Come again."
<111>

Mrs. Park Du-jin, wife of Choe Du-seon, Principal of Chungang High School, a new housewife with a combination of old and new styles

The reporter found a small new house on a high hill in Wondong. As soon as I stopped in front of the gate, I noticed the blankets and bedding spread out in the yard.

"Is this the home of the principal of Chungang School?" I asked, and the person who quickly came out of the room and stared at the reporter's face was the principal's wife. She looked to be about thirty-five or thirty-six years old, a young woman.

Wife: Who are you? Please come in and sit down.

Reporter: Thank you. I'm here to ask a favor of you. I'm here to ask you to tell me about how you manage your household.

Wife: Oh, you're welcome. I don't have any special talents, so I don't have anything interesting to say.

Reporter: Oh, don't be so modest. The way you run your household so neatly is something that modern women can learn a lot from. Please tell me about it.

Wife: Well, there are many people who want to run a good household but can't because they don't have money, but in fact, there are also many people who don't know how to run a fun household even if they have money.

Reporter: That's true.

Wife: But I'm probably one of those who can't run a good household because I don't have enough money.

Reporter: You're being modest. What you're doing now is running a household.

Wife: No. Just feeding and clothing the family every day is not running a household. It's more fun to dream of a more economically advanced household by referring to and using new inventions and things that are being invented in this era. If I had enough money to spend freely, would I be living in such a cramped way? Even now, I can't treat such a valued guest the way I'd like to. I feel like a cripple.

Reporter: Oh, you're too kind.

Wife: It's the same when teachers from school come. If they have to eat a meager meal with tasteless side dishes, I feel so ashamed. At home, my husband says, "People who work in education <112> have a hard time eating and dressing well. And people like us are all like one family, so there's no need to go out of our way to treat them," but as long as I'm running a household, I think the housewife in charge should be able to do what she wants and when she wants to, so that she can have fun and come up with new ideas. My husband only knows about school work. Of course, he doesn't have time. Naturally, that's how it is, but we only have three sons after losing our youngest daughter, so I have to do everything around the house.

Reporter: You don't have any help?

Wife: I do have someone to help with the chores, but I can't trust anyone completely. If you hire someone, you have to serve them. If you leave it to them and pretend you don't know, there's a lot of waste. Even with buying things, if you misuse a penny or two, it can create a big hole.

Reporter: Do you buy groceries with cash?

Wife: I should, but it doesn't work out that way. It seems that people who live on a salary end up using their bankbook a lot. Even if I don't use my bankbook because things are too expensive, it still turns out that way.

Reporter: And those flowers, you tend them yourself?

Wife: Of course. I dig and plant them myself. Who else would do it? The children are young and active, so they're more of a hindrance than a help with housework. Still, I can manage because the four of us often wear Western clothes. But I make the shirts myself. How much would it cost to have them made? More than anything, I'm really busy darning socks.

Reporter: Then you probably don't have much time to read novels or magazines.

Wife: You mean storybooks? I'm not good at reading in the first place. That's why I regret not studying as much as others and I feel resentful.

Reporter: You're being too modest.

The cleaner the house and the more full the crocks on the jangdokdae (장독대, platform for crocks), the more surprising it is to see such a well-established household.

Wife: Well, it seems easier to live like the Japanese, with just a few chests, but I don't think it's possible for Koreans yet. Look, soy sauce, soybean paste, and red pepper paste are essential side dishes. If a large company could make them cheaply and everyone could buy them, that might be different, but for now, it's still more satisfying to make them at home. If women start working in significant positions or working outside the home, then they'll have to buy everything, but I think it's inappropriate and uneconomical for Koreans to buy even the sauces.

So modern people need to think about it several times. You shouldn't throw everything away just because it's old or try everything just because it's new.

Reporter: You've given it a lot of thought.

What do you think about Korean clothing?

Wife: Who wouldn't like the neatness of clean white clothes, especially in the spring? But Korean clothes should be made in as many plain colors as possible. Because old-fashioned women only knew about housework, they could spend their time washing, ironing, and sewing, but modern women need to choose clothing colors and fabrics that save time. To do that, we need good fabrics made by Korean hands.

My husband mostly wears plain clothes at home.

Reporter: Then you must have some time to go sightseeing or visit other people.

Wife: But it seems that the more you do, the more housework there is. I'm busy with this and that every day. If the weather is nice, I'm polishing the floors, cleaning every corner, and airing out the bedding, so I don't have time to go out and play. And I need a place to go by myself. I only go to big or small family events.

Reporter: Do you prepare the principal's meals yourself?

Wife: Yes. My husband says there's no need to spend a lot of money on fancy side dishes, so I have to make whatever's available taste good, so I don't leave it to the servants. And he eats lunch at school and dinner elsewhere half the time. So I try to make him a good breakfast. But he always goes to bed late, so he barely wakes up in time for breakfast and goes to school without being able to enjoy his meal.

Reporter: You must be worried.

Wife: What can I do? I always live with the desire to not be a hindrance to my husband, who is dedicated to his school work, and to help him as much as I can.

The reporter listened to the humble wife's passionate and powerful story and left with a sense of awe.

Mrs. Ryu Seung-chun, wife of Lee Yun-ju, Principal of Hwimun High School, a diligent modern woman

As I entered Hwadong, I went around looking only for "a new gate like Goraedang." It was indeed a house that I could find even without knowing the address.

As I stepped inside the creaking gate, I felt a little hesitant because the house looked so splendid.

A woman who was breastfeeding a baby in front of the north window of the main hall looked at the reporter and asked, "Who are you?" The reporter hesitated even more. "She looks like the mistress of the house, but how can she be so young?" I wondered to myself, trying to guess the principal's age.

Later, I found out that the wife was young to begin with and looked even younger.

I gratefully accepted the wife's offer to come up to the porch and sat down, and we exchanged formal greetings.

Reporter: I'm sorry to bother you when you're busy.

Wife: It's okay, it's not urgent sewing. It's my husband's overcoat for the winter. I can't finish it when it gets hotter. And in the fall, the days are shorter.

Reporter: It seems that wives who run their households so neatly are all like that. Doing things little by little... It's not easy.

Wife: It's easier that way. Isn't it better for both the wearer and the maker to be diligent and not rush things?

Reporter: But is it easy for everyone to do that?

Wife: It's a little difficult. It's easy to miss the joy of having something made and wearing it when you're a housewife who spends the spring napping or a modern woman who floats on the spring breeze and wastes her time on picnics or outdoor strolls.

Reporter: Your daughters have all gone to school. Which schools do they attend?

Wife: Two of them attend Dongduk School. The third child attends the kindergarten attached to the Anguk-dong Presbyterian Church in this neighborhood. The youngest daughter is still breastfeeding and sleeping.

Reporter: Since they are still young, you must spend a lot of time taking care of them.

Wife: That's right. I don't know what I would do without the housekeeper, who is very good at everything, but it's chaotic in the morning. Dressing the children, packing their lunches... It's a lot.

Reporter: Do you have to take care of the principal much?

Wife: In reality, it's not that much, but if you say it's a lot, it's a lot. He doesn't eat well. No matter how delicious I make something, he only takes a few bites, so I worry day and night about how to get him to eat more.

Reporter: Does the principal drink a lot of alcohol?

Wife: He drinks a little too much. But even when he's upset and drinks, he never gets angry or makes a fuss. He actually cherishes the children very much.

Reporter: Is the principal staying in that back room?

Wife: Yes. Because it's uncomfortable for him to be in the main room with the children...

Reporter: When he's home, does he always study?

Wife: He used to, but these days he doesn't read books at home. It seems he does everything at school. When he comes home, he enjoys playing with the children.

Reporter: But he doesn't spend much time at home.

Wife: That's right. It's even more so since he became the principal.

Reporter: Have you been living in Seoul for a long time?

Wife: I went down to the countryside and came back during that time. So I was only renting houses even when I was in Seoul, but I thought it was uneconomical, so I built this house and moved in.

Reporter: Is your hometown in the north?

Wife: Yes, it's Pyongyang.

The wife, without being asked, had the servant bring a plate of cookies to the reporter and offered them to me. The interview continued.

Reporter: Oh, why did you bring this?

Wife: It's nothing. I'm sorry to treat a guest who came all the way here so poorly.

Reporter: By the way, excuse me, but your household seems very simple.

Wife: Well, a simple household would be the best. It's the same with everything... I hope that both men and women will improve the way they dress to save time. Western clothes would be good for children too. Our children sometimes wear Western clothes.

Reporter: Who makes those clothes?

Wife: Sometimes my husband goes to a store and buys them, but I usually make them myself as I can. We Koreans should try to make our households as simple as possible. I've even started cutting our children's hair. How much time does it take to comb the hair of so many children?

People criticize and make fun of modern women who cut their hair short, but I'm very much in favor of it. If the women in the house, including myself, could cut our hair short, I would cut mine right away... I don't have the courage to cut it first.

Reporter: But these days, some people who cut their hair short are starting to grow it out again. Maybe they can't stand being ridiculed.

Wife: If that's the case, then it makes sense. Those so-called modern women who cut their hair short just to be ridiculed or talked about were not good.

Reporter: You are truly a modern woman.

Wife: Well, I haven't studied as much as others, but I think I have new ideas.

Reporter: Do you get your daily spending money from the principal every day?

Wife: He gives me an allowance for the month, and I spend it wisely.

Reporter: Excuse my question, but which school did you attend before?

Wife: If I had to say I went to school, I would say I only studied at the attached elementary school of Jinmyeong School when I was young.

At this moment, an old fortune teller came in and begged to tell fortunes. The wife just smiled.

Reporter: Why don't you ask her something?

Wife: What does someone like that know? And what good would it do to hear it? It's more fun to accept the fate that comes without knowing it.

The wife is very generous.

Wife: I'm not impatient.

The wife's expression seemed to match her large stature.

Reporter: Goodbye. I'll visit you often.

Wife: Yes, please come by whenever you're passing by.

京城中等學校 校長夫人訪問記
中東學校長 崔奎東氏 夫人 金氏, 원만한 가정의 안주인

청덕궁압 큰길 엽흘 좌편으로 끼고 와룡동 뒷골목으로 족금 내려가면 족고만 반찬가개 하나를 지나서 바로 최규동씨 문패가 붓허 잇다.
서슴치 안코 그 집을 차저 간 때는 오후 네시가 족금 지난 때.
건는 방에서는 연두빗 조고리 입은 색시가 내다 보다가 다시 멈츳하고 잇는데 안방 쌍창 압헤는 부대해 보히는 사십여세쯤 되신 부인이 안지신채 매우 반기시는 미소로 긔자만 바라보고 게시엇다.
긔자는 『아-메 누님과 시어머님.아-니 교장 선생님 부인』
이러케 속으로 중얼거리면서 압호로 갓가히 나아가
『교장 선생님 부인이시지요. 처음 뵙슴니다.』
『네! 이리로 좀 올나오세요. 끗헤 아해가 압하서 이러케 무릅에 뉘이고 잇기 때문에-』
경상도 사투리 석긴 말슴으로 이러케 대답하시고, 역시 웃고만 게실 뿐. 응당 내가 차저 온 리유를 물으실 줄 알섯더니 뜻밧게 부드럽게 익숙하게 대해 주시는대 얼마나 맘에 반가움을 늣기 엇는지 몰낫다.
『어대를 그러케 알슴닛가』
누은 아헤를 어루만지면서
『대단치는 안음니다만은 좀 압하서요.』
어대까지든지 어머니 다웁게 아들을 액기시고 사랑하시는 진정이 마대마대 나타나 보이엿다.
긔자는 안방 벽 다지에 걸닌 시계를 치여다 보며
『선생님께서는 아즉 아니 도라 오섯슴니다 그려』<108>
『네. 지금 세시 반밧게 아니 되엿는대요. 아즉 멀엇슴니다. 늘 여섯시나 되어야 드러 오시지요. 항상 밧부세서-』
『그럼 저녁 진지가 늘 느지시겟슴니다 그려.』
『우리 집은 식구가 만허서요. 원체 저녁은 늣게 먹지 안슴니다. 우리 집 식구가 퍽 만치요. 일가 학생과 합치면 열여덟 식구나 되야요. 그러닛가 저녁은 일즉 함니다만은 선생님께는 항상 때를 마초아 해서 드릴 수가 업서요.』
『참 그러시겟슴니다.』
『학교서 일즉 도라오시는 날이면 손님이 오시지요. 한참 밥을 잡수시다가도 손님만 오시면 고만 던저 버리시고 만나 보시고 일이 끗나신 뒤에야 다-식은 것을 맛이란 업는 것을 다시 잡수시는 성질이세요. 참 어렵슴니다.』
그럴 때마다 부인께서는 맘이 불편하시다 못해서 몹시 안타까워 하시는 것 가티 뵈엿다.
『전에 신접살림 할 때에는 선생님 드러 오시는 것을 보고야 남비에다가 오로를 끄리여서 한참 맛잇슬 때쯤 퍼다가 잡수시게 햇지만요 요사이 가티 번열해지고 또 내 몸도 주야 압하서 그러케 못해드리기 때문에 갓득이나 식사를 잘못하시는 대 더 못잡수시게 하지요. 그 생각하면 늘 가삼이 저리여지지만 할 수가 업서서요.』
긔자는 거침업시 술술 이야기하시고 계신 부인에게 감사하는 맘으로 또 부인의 애닯어 하시는 점을 곰곰이 생각해 드리는 뜻으로
