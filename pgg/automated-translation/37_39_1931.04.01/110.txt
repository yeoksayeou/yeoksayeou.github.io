발행일	1931년 04월 01일
기사제목	夫婦生活讀本, 사랑의 更新策? (Marriage Life Reader, A Renewal Strategy for Love?)
필자	金永八
기사형태	잡저 (Miscellaneous Essay)

In Japanese, there's a word called "noroke." Some people might enjoy it, and others might find it tiresome, but generally, it's something to laugh off, or even something that can make you feel unpleasant. But my friend, Mr. Pyŏlgŏn'gon, is urging me to open my mouth about this "noroke," and I can't refuse him, so I have to say a few words. But, frankly, it's embarrassing for me.

Well, certainly, when I married my wife, what was Yang Guifei? Did I ever hold hands and stroll through a park when we were dating? Or, like the young women these days, did I ever go to the theater? Did I exchange love letters freely? No. There was no time as constrained as back then. Thinking about it now, it's laughable. I don't know why, but I don't think I had the toughness (떡심) I have now.

So, barely managing to meet once a week in my sweetheart's (now my wife's) kitchen, carefully measuring the time, feeling so sad that time was passing, holding my sweetheart's hands tightly and trembling, sighing – our relationship was like that. After we got married, if any friend came looking for me and said, "Yŏng-p'al, Mr.," I would furrow my brow and say, "He's not here – ta-ta!"

If any friend who came looking for me back then reads this, they'll be angry and think, "What a jerk!" But, really, I couldn't help it back then. Those were the happiest days. If a friend came to my house now and started to say "Yŏng-p'al," I would cut them off with "Go away!" before they could even finish saying "Yŏng." Why?

Because a child was born – then another – and now I have two children. I can't openly enjoy myself because of appearances, and as a mother with children, I don't have time to relax, and I'm constantly dealing with the kids. By the time evening comes, I'm worn out, and when I come home, my wife is already asleep with the kids, not caring whether her husband comes home or not. I feel bad waking her up, so I just leave her alone, and this becomes a habit. I also enjoy a drink now and then, and someone who used to come home by 10 o'clock now comes home at 11, 12, 1 AM, 2 AM, 3 AM, 4 AM. I start thinking, "Let's just sleep here for one night. I did it before, so surely it'll be alright today." Lately, my wife's trust in me has completely disappeared, and she thinks, "He's become that kind of person." And I've also resigned myself to thinking, "I've become this kind of person." Now you're talking about a renewal strategy (更新策), Mr. Pyŏlgŏn'gon. What should I do? This is like trying to get rid of a wart and ending up adding another one.

But in my opinion, the wife of someone in the literary field is probably unhappy. How can she always match her husband's mood? If she slightly offends him, he gets upset and, when he's deep in thought, if she says anything, he shouts, "Shut up!"

And there's another annoying thing. If I meet any young woman and she smiles every time I speak, I inevitably become infatuated. If this gets worse, how can I value my current wife as I did in the past? She's just the cook who I see every day, always preparing meals. And she's been my woman for so long. So, eventually, I endlessly strive to find new love. Then, eventually, there's a sudden divorce (리혼) for the wife. If not, I become irritable, or, even worse, it can lead to an affair (情死). But this is just a hypothetical situation. It would be terrible if it actually happened. My position would be difficult. If there's a complicated situation and the love between my wife and I cools, what renewal strategy (更新策) should I come up with?

"We should separate."
"Huh? How? Divorce (리혼)?"
"No."
"Then?"
"If such a thing happens, it's just a temporary delusion. If my wife is an understanding woman, then if we just separate for three years, in my feelings, after only two and a half years, 50 percent more of the previous love will return."
"Huh? That's a great idea. But what if she's not an understanding wife?"
"That's hard to say. Let's see. We'll probably be incompatible for life."
"Then you should avoid such a thing from the start."
"What? You're talking like a friend, but there are many stories of what happens after things have already turned out that way. It's like telling someone to climb a tree and then shaking it. Let's meet again. Do you think I'm free?"

- End - <15>
