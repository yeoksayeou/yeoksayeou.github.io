기사형태 문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

An Unparalleled Tale of Korean Sentiment: A Beautiful Woman in the Mountains Who Killed a Tiger and Died for Her Husband
By Yare

It was about thirty years ago. In a village called T'ongch'ŏn (通川) in Kangwŏn Province, there lived a farmer named I Min (李民). His occupation was selling salt, so he carried salt into the mountain villages to exchange it for grain in order to make a living. One day, he carried seven bags of salt on his back and went to a deep mountain village called Kujangmyŏn (舊長楊面) in Hoeyang (淮陽). One day, he had to cross a large and treacherous mountain pass called Changdaeryŏng (長大嶺), and as he reached the bottom, the sun began to set, and a dark twilight covered the mountains. The already frightening mountain pass became even more terrifying and ominous than the road to the afterlife. With fear, he couldn't take a single step, but he had no other place to go except to cross this mountain, so he had no choice but to go. With a desperate courage, he grabbed onto the mountain crevices and went about three majŏng (里正), when he found a sack lying in the middle of the road. "How could a sack be lying in such a deep mountain village?" he thought, but he didn't deeply suspect anything and carelessly picked up the sack and placed it on top of his salt load. As he reached the bottom of the pass, he unexpectedly saw a small thatched house, which he wouldn't have expected to find in such a place. He was overjoyed to see the house, as if his dead soul had come back to life. So, without delay, he went to the front of the house and called for the owner. Then, from inside the house, a young and beautiful voice said, "Who is it? What brings you here?" and a lovely woman, about thirty years old, came out. "The night has fallen in this deep mountain village, please let me stay the night," he asked, and the beautiful woman readily replied, "Of course. It would be very dangerous to go any further tonight," and kindly welcomed him in and cleaned the room. The salt merchant put down his salt load in front of the door and lay down in the room. The woman was busy preparing dinner, and she picked up the sack that was among the salt load. Suddenly, her eyes widened, and she rushed to the salt merchant and asked, "Excuse me, is this sack yours?" The salt merchant thought it was strange and truthfully replied, "No, I picked up the sack that someone dropped at the bottom of the pass." The woman heard those words, tears welling up in her eyes, and said in a flustered voice, "Oh dear, oh dear, this is terrible. My husband must have suffered a tiger attack (虎患)." She continued without taking a breath, "Earlier, around noon, my husband went to a feast in a village across the mountain to help out (扶助), carrying a mal (斗) of rice in the sack. It is clear that he was caught by a tiger at the bottom of the pass on his way back. What should we do?" And she began to sob and weep. The salt merchant was scared and felt sorry for her, not knowing what to do. The woman suddenly stopped her tears and said, "Now that things have come to this, what good is it to cry? We must find the body." And she said again in a strong voice, "Sir!" and called out, "It is your fate that you came to stay in such an unfortunate house tonight, so you must do as I say." The salt merchant was already scared, and he wondered what she was going to make him do, his heart sinking and trembling, not knowing what to do. The woman went out to the yard, lit a torch with firewood, and said, "Sir! Come quickly." The salt merchant finally managed to speak, "Where are we going in the middle of the night? I'm too scared to go," and sat down on the spot. Then the woman spoke in a venomous voice, "Then there's no choice. If we can't even find the body, I will have to kill you and then kill myself," and she went to the kitchen and came running with a kitchen knife. The salt merchant was half out of his mind, and he decided that if he was going to die anyway, he might as well go out and search. He took the torch as the woman ordered and groped his way to the place at the bottom of the pass where the sack had been. When he arrived, he saw that there was indeed bright red blood splattered there in a gruesome sight, and even the footprints of a tiger remained. Seeing this, the woman went mad and ran into the forest, tangled with vines and vines, following the trail of blood for about one or two majŏng (里正). Sure enough, under a large rock, a striped tiger was growling, holding a corpse. As soon as the woman saw the corpse, she ran like lightning to the tiger's side and put the corpse on her shoulders and ran back to the road. The tiger, losing its meal, followed behind, trying to bite the woman's skirt. The woman shouted to the salt merchant, "Will you hold the torch and come from behind, or will you carry the corpse and go ahead?" The salt merchant, completely out of his mind, quickly thought that both options were impossible, but he was too scared to carry the corpse on his back, so he had no choice but to hold the fire and go from behind. So he took the fire and looked behind him, and the tiger was following closely as if it would pounce at any moment. The salt merchant was frightened and said, "I will carry the corpse and go ahead." He exchanged the corpse and the fire and came all the way home. Arriving home, the woman locked the salt merchant in a room, put a lock on the door, and then, holding her husband's corpse, she went into a dugout (움) and made a round door by piling up pieces of wood at the entrance of the dugout. She stood inside the dugout with a dagger, waiting for the tiger to come. Before long, the tiger followed the corpse to the outside of the dugout and barely pushed its head into the dugout to bite the corpse. At that moment, the woman raised the dagger with all her might and struck the tiger's head. The amazing devotion of a virtuous woman (烈女)! She finally split the tiger's head open. Meanwhile, the tiger roared like thunder and died. After dawn, the salt merchant abandoned his salt load and left the house. The woman expressed her gratitude in many ways and gave him a root of ginseng and a bolt of cloth from the chest, saying that this was all she had in the house and begged him to take it. The salt merchant couldn't refuse and took it and left the house. Leaving, he felt as refreshed and relieved as if he had been dragged to hell and back. After walking about one or two majŏng (里正), he looked back and saw that the woman had set fire to her own house and, holding her husband's body, had entered the fire. (The End) <233>

벌서 한 30여 년 전 일임니다. 강원도 통천(通川)이라는 골에 리민(李民)이라고 하는 농부가 잇섯슴니다. 그는 직업이 소곰 장사라 소곰을 가지고 산골에 드러가 곡식으로 밧구어 곡량(穀糧)을 작만하기위하야 소곰 닐곱말을 등에 지고 회양골 구(舊) 장양면이라 하는 깁흔 산골을 차저 갓섯슴니다. 그런대 하로는 장대령(長大嶺)이라 하는 크고 험한 고개를 넘어가게 되엿는대 영 밋까지 가고난 즉 해가 점점 기우러지기를 시작하야 컴컴한 황혼이 산을 덥허옴니다. 그러지 안아도 무서운 산고개가 저승길 보다도 더 무섭고 흉하여졋슴니다. 무서운 생각으로는 한 거름도 발이 나가지 안컨만은 이 산을 넘지 안코는 또한 갈 곳도 업슴으로 불가불 안이 가는 수도 업섯슴니다. 하는 수 업시 죽을 용긔를 내여 가지고 산 틈을 잡아드러 한 삼마정쯤이나 가노란즉 길 가운대 자루 하나이 떠러저 잇섯슴니다. 『이러케 깁흔 산골에 자루가 엇지하야 떠러저 잇슬가』 생각은 하면서도 별로 깁히 의심하지도 안코 무심히 그 자루를 집어 소곰 짐에 언저 가지고 영 밋까지 가고 난 즉 뜻밧게 영 밋헤 이러한 곳에 잇슬 듯 십지도 안은 조고마한 오막사리 집 한 채가 보임니다. 그는 집을 보자 죽엇든 목숨이 다시 산 듯이 깃벗슴니다. 그래서 잡담 제지하고 그 집문 앞헤 가서 주인을 차젓슴니다. 한즉 집안에서 젊고도 고흔 목소리로 『누구심닛가. 무슨 일로 찻슴닛가』하고 뭇는 소리가 나면서 나히 한 삼십이 될락말락한 어엽분 미인이 나왓슴니다. 『네 이러케 깁흔 산골에서 날이 어두엇스니 하로밤 자고 가게 해 주십시요』하고 청하닛가 그 미인은 서슴업시 『그러시지요. 이 밤중에 더 가실랴면 대단히 위험합니다.』하고 친절히 마저 드려서 방ꊹ�지 치워주엇슴니다. 소곰 장사는 소곰짐을 문 압헤 버서 놋코 방안에 드러가 누엇스랸 즉 부인은 저녁을 차리느라고 분주히 드나들다가 소곰 짐에 끼인 자루를＜231＞ 집어들고는 별안간에 두 눈이 둥구래저서 뛰여드러와 소곰 장사를 보고 『여봅시오. 이 자루가 손님 것임닛가』하고 무럿슴니다. 소금 장사는 속으로 이상하게 생각하면서 바른 대로 『아니야요. 이 영 밋헤서 뉘가 떠러트린 자루를 주어 가지고 온 것임니다.』하엿슴니다. 부인은 그 말을 듯고 눈에서 눈물 석긴 눈물이 핑핑 돌며 황망이 하는 말이 『여봅시요 여봅시요 이것 큰일날슴니다. 우리 집 주인께서 호환(虎患)을 당하엿구려』하고 그는 숨도 쉬일 사이 업시 다시 말을 이어 『여봅시오 우리 집 주인께서 앗가 오정 때쯤 해서 저 산 넘어 동리에 잔체집이 잇는대 그 집 부조(扶助)를 하느라고 자루에 쌀 한 말을 너허가지고 가섯는대 돌아오는 길에 령 밋헤서 호랑이에게 잡힌 것이 분명함니다. 이 일을 엇저면 좃슴닛가.』하고 흑흑 늣겨 울기를 시작하얏슴니다. 소곰 장사는 무섭기도 하고 불상하기도 하야 엇지할 줄을 모르고 잇는대 부인은 다시 눈물을 뚝 끈치고 하는 말이 『이왕 일이 이 지경이 된 바에야 울면 쓸대잇는 일임닛가. 좌우간 시체나 차자 와야겟슴니다.』하고 다시 힘 잇는 말로 『손님!』하고 부러더니 『여봅시요. 당신께서 오날 저녁 이런 불행한 집에 와서 자게된 것도 역시 당신 팔자오니 불가불 나 하나라는 대로 하야 주세야 하겟슴니다.』하고 말합니다. 소곰 장사는 갓득이나 무서운 판에 또 무엇을 식히려나 하야 가슴이 덜컥 내려안자 엇지할 줄을 모르고 그저 몸만 벌벌 떨고 잇는대 부인은 마당에 나가서 싸리나무로 홰를 켜 가지고 오더늋�  『손님!
