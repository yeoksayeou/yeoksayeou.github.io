Daughter: A young girl

SETTING
Modern day. Dusk in autumn.

PLACE
A slum in a city.

STAGE

Im Sŏbang's shack, situated on a hillside, has its rush mat door open, but the inside is dark and cannot be seen. Around the shack are shacks typical of a slum, with various household items scattered here and there. Slightly pulled to the left of the rush mat door is a worn-out sheet metal stove with an earthen pot (土釜) hanging on it. Beside it are pieces of firewood and torn mats neatly prepared for starting a fire. In front of the shack is a narrow patch of land that could be called a yard, and in the background, small houses and shacks cling to the hillside. As the curtain opens, Im Sŏbang's wife (wearing a worn-out dress and heavily pregnant) sits on a straw mat in front of the rush mat door, with her daughter (naked except for a cloth covering her lower body) lying on her lap, picking lice from her hair. <164>
Daughter (wriggling): Stop it.
Wife (absorbed in picking lice): Stay still.
Daughter: Stop it.
Wife: Stay still, you little brat. Look at these lice.
Daughter: (Quiet for a while)
Wife (picking lice carefully):
Daughter: Mother.
Wife: What?
Daughter: I'm hungry.
Wife (stopping suddenly, looking at the distant mountains and sighing):
Daughter: Mother.
Wife: What?
Daughter: I'm hungry.

Daughter: Really?

Wife (sighing, straightening her back, aside): Oh, I'm out of breath!

Wife: (Out of breath) I can't even raise this one like other people's children, why did another one have to come along! Why couldn't it have happened to a family that lives in luxury and wants a child but can't have one!
Daughter: Huh?

Daughter: Huh?
Wife: Are you hungry?

Wife: Your father will buy rice soon, and we'll have plenty to eat.

Wife (happy but cautiously getting up to meet him):

Wife: Still, I'm uneasy.

Wife: They say women are working there too?

Wife: I wish I could go too!

Daughter: No-

Wife: But why do they pay more now?

So, because there aren't enough workers, they're grabbing anyone they can find and making them work, so they're paying an extra jeon because they're desperate.
Wife: So, if it's three jeon per mal, how many mal can you sort in a day?

Wife: It would be nice if you could do that for a long time!
Im (thinking): Oh! There was so much rice. Anyway, the unpolished rice was pouring out of the machine like a waterfall. It was really amazing.
Wife: I wish we could eat rice like that!

A (speaking calmly): No, we're not telling you not to earn a living, but we used to work at that rice mill. We used to work there, but the owner lowered the wages for men operating the machines from 1 won 20 jeon to 1 won, and for women sorting rice, he lowered it from 2 jeon to 1 jeon 5 rin per mal, so we went on strike, understand? (looking at Im) Do you understand?
Im: Yes, please speak.
A: So, if we don't work, wouldn't it be frustrating for them because they can't get the rice out? So, wouldn't they give us back the original wages—because they're frustrated—wouldn't they give us back the original wages?
Im: Geez! What's the big deal? If I were the owner, I'd kick you all out right away and hire other people. Wife: Okay— (going to the stove and scooping steaming, reddish rice into a bowl)
A, B, C (standing there, staring blankly):

A: No, it's not that we're telling you not to earn a living. Whether you're hungry or we're hungry, everyone gets hungry, right? But we're in a fight with that owner, and because you people are coming to work, it's like we're going to be kicked out for good, isn't it? We have dozens of people in the police station right now, suffering through a life-or-death situation, and the people who are left are barely eating one meal every two or three days, but we're still not going to work to show that we won't give in to that owner, and you people are jumping in and interfering with our work, so what's the point of us getting arrested, beaten, and starved for a month? Aren't you and I both laborers who make a living by working? So, shouldn't we laborers understand each other's situations and not tear each other apart, bickering and only looking out for ourselves, or we'll all die?
Im (standing silently, without saying anything):
Wife (having finished scooping the rice and putting it on the table): You must be hungry, come and eat.
Daughter (sitting alone and eating rice): Father, eat rice. <168>
Im: Okay, eat up.
A: Hey—look at this. You people are going to XX Rice Mill and getting paid well to work, but do you know how long that will last? Once our strike is resolved, you'll be kicked to the curb. Also, if we decide to compete with you, we can go work tomorrow, and you'll be kicked out right away. Understand? Then why aren't we going to work tomorrow, and instead carrying these clubs around, saying this and that? You must be thinking that way. Of course, I expect you to think that way. But if we laborers keep doing that, we'll all die. I won't go into detail here because you probably won't understand, but I'll just tell you the basics, so listen. Let's say you made a thousand mal of rice at XX Rice Mill today. You made that thousand mal of rice with your own hands, but you only get paid about 1 won or 80-90 jeon for your labor, but the owner makes hundreds of won in profit every day! But if we laborers don't go to work, no matter how many tens of thousands of bags of rice there are, they can't be turned into rice, as you people can see from this situation, right? Look, the laborers are the ones who actually grind the rice to make it edible, but the owner gets the profit, is that fair? The reason we laborers live in poverty isn't our fault! So, is that fair? (insisting) Is that fair? It's not fair, right? For example, if you sorted twenty mal of rice today, and someone else sorted twenty mal, but you got 60 jeon and the other person got 80 jeon, would you like that?
Im: Of course not.
A: See! If you think of that other person as the owner of XX Rice Mill, you'll understand why we went on strike to prevent them from lowering our wages.
Im (nodding): Yes, yes.
A: See? So, shouldn't you people stop interfering with our work?
Im: Yes, I understand that well. But I'm hungry, so...?
A: Yes, of course, that's tough. So, if our strike had enough money, we'd give you some money while you look for a job, but actually, we're also tightening our own belts. (unable to speak)
C (coming forward): So, we're sorry, but <169> you should look for another job starting tomorrow.
Im: Should I be the only one to do that?
A: Of course not. So, from now on, we're going to find other people and talk to them like we're talking to you now, and if they listen well like you, that's good, but if they don't, we're fighting to the death, so we'll beat them or do whatever it takes to stop that XX Rice Mill's machines from running and stop the rice from coming out.
Im (nodding as if he agrees):
A: If we do that, the owner will eventually surrender to us, right?
Im (relaxing): That's right, that's right. Then I'll quit starting tomorrow.
A (grabbing Im's wrist with a happy expression): Thank you so much! You said you do odd jobs? Then are you in the union?
Im: What union?
A: The Free Labor Union.
Im: No, I don't know anything about that.
A: Then join the union starting tomorrow. There are many good things about it.
Im: Then I'll join.
A: Okay, then we'll be going. If you ask about it at the union tomorrow, they'll tell you all about what we're going to do in the future. Okay, then goodbye.
Im (hesitating): Um, you all seem very hungry, would you like to have some barley rice?
A, B, C (looking at the rice on the table at the same time and laughing hungrily):
Im: Just one spoonful each.
A: No, you should eat. Who knows when you'll get to eat rice like that again! Eat a lot tonight.
A, B, C (exiting to the right)
Im (standing there blankly, staring after them, thinking):
Wife: Come and eat your rice.
Im: Okay (turning around and going to the straw mat).
Wife (anxiously): Why did you say you would quit when you can't?
Im: But what can I do, those people are right!
Wife: Does that mean we'll starve again tomorrow?
Im: Starving is something we do every day, so there's nothing to be afraid of.
Wife (on the verge of tears): But—
Im (standing there blankly, looking at his wife's belly and the rice, aside): Yes, those people are right. It's not our fault that we live like this. (pause) They said I'll find out more if I ask at the union, right? Yes, that's right. If we're going to eat, we should eat a lot! First, let's eat, (looking at his wife) Come on, let's eat together. (as he picks up the spoon and scoops up rice, the curtain falls.) <170>
