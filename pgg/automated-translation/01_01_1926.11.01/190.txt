Publication Date: 1926년 11월 01일
Article Title: 對話 暗室鶯歌, 『輿論』社 編輯室의 一日生活記 (Dialogue: Nightingale's Song in a Dark Room, A Record of Daily Life in the Editorial Office of the 'Yŏllon' Company)
Author: 速記者 (Shorthand Reporter)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Talk)

Dialogue: Nightingale's Song in a Dark Room, A Record of Daily Life in the 'Yŏllon' Company Editorial Office
						Shorthand Reporter
(Setting - Indoors)
(Characters - All male and female reporters)

P'yŏnggye: (Stopping his editing amidst many manuscripts, letting out a sigh) Ahh—it’s already autumn!
(Amidst silence)

Ch'unsu: (Writing a manuscript, glances towards the female reporter's side-) Ah! The sound of crickets already sounds mournful.

Hwasŏng: (With a cool attitude) Why is everyone starting to cry from the morning today? A bunch of perfectly fine widowers sitting around—.

Ch'unsu: We cry because we're widowers. If widows were sitting around crying like this, would the household function?

Hwasŏng: What are you talking about? It's a worry because they are widowers. If they were widows, what worry would there be? They just need to find a man, and that's that. It's because they are widowers who can't be dealt with—.

Ch'unsu: Well, if widowers can't be dealt with, how could widows be dealt with? There have to be widowers and widows for pairs to match (glances once towards the female reporter).

Hwasŏng: Fine, you're right. Let's drop it.

P'yŏnggye: (Chuckling) Oh my, you seem to have lost your edge today. What's wrong?

Hwasŏng: Whether Ch'unsu's logic holds or not, he always tries to win... in front of... so what's the big deal if I lose over something trivial?

Ch'uho: (Half-asleep, muttering like talking in his sleep) Heh heh! <111>
Hwasŏng is really getting it today.

Female Reporter: (Smiling) Mr. Hwasŏng, are you not feeling well somewhere today?

Hwasŏng: (Becoming quite agitated this time) No. I am always distressed. Why strive to win in idle chat just because something feels good?

Female Reporter: (With a slightly startled expression) Why do you say that? Is something particular... ?

Hwasŏng: If you must ask... it's when I think about the lives of Korean people—.

Ch'unsu: (Sarcastically) Look here, another patriot (志士) has emerged. When such a patriot engages in romance, he must do it sneakily.

P'yŏnggye: Of course. Typically, when sitting before women, they bring up irrelevant topics like 'how is society' or 'how is life'... just like a patriot... Is that how romance works out well?

Female Reporter: (Silent in thought)

Hwasŏng: (Extremely agitated) Hey, you people! Are you really insulting me like that?

Ch'unsu: (Glancing once at the female reporter) Hey man. Taking that as an insult makes you narrow-minded—. Is there a law saying patriots can't pursue romance?

Female Reporter: (Her face flushing slightly) Then what do you mean by that? If everyone who acts like a patriot in front of a woman pursues romance, do you think women are objects who know nothing and have no thoughts? Why do you speak like that?

Hwasŏng: (Calming down a bit) Yes, that's right. Those people demean women like that just for the sake of momentary advantage—.

Ch'unsu: (Seeing he is losing ground, flustered and unsure what to do, looks up again at the female reporter's face—) Why is she getting worked up? It's because of things like this that pretending to be a patriot proves effective! Look at that! You see the effect immediately!

Female Reporter: You seem overly agitated. Is there really anything to be so agitated about?

Ch'unsu: That person over there got agitated, not me. <112> Did I say anything more than that the crickets were crying at first? That person got worked up for no reason and acted like a patriot! Ma'am, please don't sympathize with people like that. It only encourages them more.

Hwasŏng: What on earth do you mean? What am I doing more of?

Ch'uho: Ah! I'm sleepy! And I'm getting hungry again. You all certainly have a lot of energy. Complaining about having no rice or the millet running out, why do you get so crazy excited when it comes to romance?

Everyone laughed.

Kaptol: Mr. Hwasŏng, telephone call for you.

Hwasŏng: Huh—for me? (Goes outside)

P'yŏnggye: Hey Ch'unsu! So you ended up losing?

Female Reporter: Oh my! You gentlemen too! What does it matter if you win or lose?

Samt'an: (Entering the room) That's it, things have fallen through again today!

P'yŏnggye: What do you mean? That deal?

Samt'an: That money deal fell through! Oh dear! We're dead again today! Is there any wood, is there any rice? Ah—ah!

Hwasŏng: (Returns and sits in his seat. Silent with a gloomy expression)

P'yŏnggye: Hwasŏng really seems down today, what's wrong?

Hwasŏng: Sigh—. Hey! Don't tease me calling me a patriot (志士) again! I heard that A-gun, who was in prison, finally died—. And N-gun is in critical condition—.

Everyone was startled.

Everyone: What? A-gun died? Huh! What a shame, a valuable person has died.

Hwasŏng: It's truly lamentable! Valuable people die off, smart people get arrested, are only the dregs of humanity left?

Ch'unsu: (As if deeply moved) Now I mean it truly. I sympathize with the patriot's words this time!

Ch'uho: Oh—I'm hungry!

Hwasŏng: Everyone's dead. Those remaining are dying of hunger—.

Ch'unsu: Didn't you still eat breakfast before coming this morning? <113>

Hwasŏng: What good does eating do?

Ch'unsu: What's the use in talking like this and making ourselves miserable? Come on! Let's just talk. (Looking at the female reporter) Isn't that right?

Female Reporter: (Forcing herself—) That's right. But somehow, lately, I don't feel like listening to even the most interesting stories. My body is terribly tired. (Yawns) Ah! I'm exhausted!

Ch'unsu: Then maybe you should try some exercise.

P'yŏnggye: Right. Since you yourself do mountain exercises—why not a little—

Ch'unsu: (His face flushing slightly) No, it's true! You feel much different if you exercise.

Hwasŏng: Ah! It's painful! I don't feel like doing anything. There needs to be something that works out!

Ch'unsu: All the more reason you must do it. You must fight—

Hwasŏng: You seem to have gotten your mind right! No, what do you mean 'all the more reason'?

Ch'unsu: Anything, really. Whether it's the XX movement, or the OO movement, or romance, whatever is necessary.

P'yŏnggye: That fellow can't seem to talk without mentioning romance.

Female Reporter: Ah! I'm tired! I should go!

Ch'unsu: Why are you leaving already? Since we're all bored, isn't it nice for us to talk like this together?

Female Reporter: I've completely lost interest in everything.

Ch'unsu: I'll tell you an interesting story!

Female Reporter: (Sits down reluctantly)

P'yŏnggye: Oh—I'm hungry—.

Hwasŏng: (Only sighs)

Samt'an: (Sits puffing cigarette smoke.)

Kaptol and Ch'uho are napping while seated.

(Silence)

Female Reporter: (Quietly whistles a tune tinged with sorrow)

Hwasŏng: Ma'am!

Female Reporter: Yes?

Hwasŏng: The world is so... ... .

Ch'unsu: ... ... so desolate... ... ?

P'yŏnggye: ... ... so desolate, in the wide field... ... ?

Samt'an: In the wide field, when wandering alone... ... ?

Hwasŏng: When wandering alone, the longed-for person's... ...

P'yŏnggye: Heh heh... that "longed-for person" inevitably comes out.

Hwasŏng: Hey, but why do you people always <114> meddle so much in others' words and turn everything into a joke?

Female Reporter: Mr. Hwasŏng! Please continue speaking.

Hwasŏng: Yes! What was I saying earlier?

Female Reporter: You said, "The world is so... ... ."

Hwasŏng: Yes, yes! That's right. When the world is so desolate—

Female Reporter: Yes—

Hwasŏng: If people understand each other's hearts—

Female Reporter: Yes—

Hwasŏng: They comfort each other—

Female Reporter: Yes—

Hwasŏng: "... ... ... ..."

Female Reporter: Yes?

Hwasŏng: Ma'am!

Female Reporter: Please speak.

Hwasŏng: I have a request.

Female Reporter: Are you speaking to me?

Hwasŏng: That is correct.

Female Reporter: "... ... ... ..."

Hwasŏng: It's nothing else, but please sing a song for me to hear. It's too lonely—.

Female Reporter: I'd have to know how, wouldn't I?

Ch'unsu: (Quickly interjecting) Well, it's not a concert hall, so it's alright even if you don't sing well—.

Female Reporter: Well...

Hwasŏng: Shh—don't be noisy. Now—

Female Reporter: (Her face flushing slightly) If you are all that bored, I suppose I can try singing once, even if it's not good.

Everyone becomes very tense.

(Quietly, the voice emerges)

"In the quiet night sky
As I wander beneath the stars
My lonely heart sways in the wind
The distant past comes before me once again."

Hwasŏng: That's truly wonderful. That indeed is a nightingale's song in a dark room (暗室鶯歌)! Please sing the rest!

Female Reporter: I can't. My body is tired.

Hwasŏng: I understand.

Female Reporter: I'll be going now. Goodbye (Starts to leave).

Ch'unsu: By the way, Ma'am, I have a complaint.

Female Reporter: Yes? <115>

Ch'unsu: It's nothing else, but yesterday when I asked, you refused, yet you agreed to Hwasŏng's request. Is it because it was a patriot's (志士) words?

Female Reporter: Haha! Please forgive me. Yesterday there was someone I didn't know present, that's why! But sir, you shouldn't misunderstand.

Ch'unsu: What does it matter if we misunderstand a little among ourselves?

(Female Reporter Exits)

Hwasŏng: Ah! Just like that, today has also passed completely. The days go by so quickly.

P'yŏnggye: Let's go, everyone! What are you all sitting around waiting for?

Hwasŏng: Spending time like this, just talking!

P'yŏnggye: A time when we don't spend it like this will come too! Oh, I'm hungry!

Samt'an: Right, let's go.

Hwasŏng: I'm going too—

Ch'unsu: Hwasŏng—don't misunderstand.

Hwasŏng: Heh heh! It's just joking among ourselves! But even if there was a misunderstanding, there probably wouldn't be such a violent fight! Heh heh! Hey Kaptol! Stop sleeping, lock the door and let's go! Hey Mr. Ch'uho, let's just go home and sleep.

Everyone Exits (End) <116>

**Summary**

This dialogue captures a day in the editorial office of the magazine company "Yŏllon Sa" in 1926. The male editors engage in banter, teasing, and philosophical musings, often revolving around romance and their perceived status as widowers or patriots ("Chisa"). The female reporter navigates their interactions, sometimes participating, sometimes pushing back against their assumptions. The mood shifts dramatically with news of financial trouble and the death of an imprisoned acquaintance ("A-gun"), revealing underlying anxieties about poverty and political oppression. The piece concludes with the female reporter singing a mournful song before leaving, and the men dispersing, weary and hungry, reflecting on the passage of time under difficult circumstances. For historians, the text offers a glimpse into the daily lives, social dynamics (including gender interactions), and palpable anxieties (economic hardship, political suppression under colonial rule) of Korean intellectuals and journalists during the 1920s. The blend of mundane conversation with sudden intrusions of grim news about imprisonment and death is particularly revealing of the era's atmosphere.
