**Body Text Translation:**
A Record of Visiting Chuwang Mountain
						Chŏng Hyŏn-mo

This Chuwangsan (周王山) is a beautiful famous mountain hidden away a good 40 *ri* from Ch'ŏngsong (靑松), a mountain town in Kyŏngbuk province. Also called "Little Kŭmgang" (小金剛), it is Kyŏngbuk's sole representative scenic spot. However, aside from its shadow being briefly cast upon the pages of some newspaper years ago, and the existence of a Chuwangsan Historic Site Preservation Society (周王山 古蹟保存會) in Ch'ŏngsong town for the sake of this famous site, that is all. It is regrettable above all that this Chuwangsan, too, having failed to meet a worthy promoter, has become one of the neglected landscapes of our homeland, unable to boast its beautiful existence widely to the world.

Fortunately, I grew up in a region near this Chuwangsan, but unfortunately, I never had the chance to see it. Whether studying abroad or wandering in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), I always yearned in my heart for its beautiful nature (自然). Only today, as I temporarily savor the rural life of my hometown (鄕里), have I finally come to see it.

Embraced by this beautiful, lovely, and great nature, I realized this happiness was not something to be rejoiced in by myself alone. Thus, though my brush may be clumsy, I presumptuously attempt to sketch even a ten-thousandth of the whole scene, hoping it might serve as a guide or route reference for the many sightseers (探勝客) who will illuminate this Chuwangsan widely and eternally.

Today, I finally set off on the path to see Chuwangsan, a journey I had wished for many years. It was around 4 p.m. on the 6th day of the 9th lunar month (음력), a cool autumn when the beautiful autumn leaves were coloring the dense forests (樹林) on the mountain slopes and in the valleys with crimson hues.

The autumn sky, without a single cloud, was round and high, and the descending sunlight (日光) gave off a feeling of heat reminiscent of the past summer. Waiting for the automobile with simple travel gear (旅具), I wandered around the entrance (洞口) of my home village (鄕第) until I gladly jumped onto the car arriving with a "ppung-ppung" sound. As I took my seat, the car, still making noise, <142> began to speed away. Soon, we passed Ch'ŏnjip'yŏng (泉旨坪). This place is one of Andong's (安東) major agricultural areas (農山地). Here and there in the wide fields, the sounds of farmers (農夫) cutting rice stalks and the sight of them hauling in the rice – all are art and poetry depicting their rural life. However, looking at them, I feel this again:

Ah, grateful farmers! Precious brethren! You are truly warriors and workers struggling for life. You work without rest even when your hands and feet are worn and you sweat blood. But this world is still a wicked world that does not know your grace (恩惠) and merits (公勞). It is a world of contradiction (矛盾). It is a world that rather despises, oppresses, deceives (欺瞞), and exploits you, the grateful and precious ones. Indeed, it is so. But if it is the principle that a beautiful world where truth (真理) triumphs emerges from this world full of deception, then I believe, whether near or far, the future (將來) will solely be the world of you, the grateful and precious ones.

Passing Ch'ŏnjip'yŏng, we headed east (東) and reached Huryŏng (後嶺) pass behind Sanha-ri (山下里). This mountain is famous in the vicinity for its ruggedness (峻險). The automobile, carrying six passengers (乘客) and also mail (郵便物), crawled up this high pass. It seemed truly strenuous. It seemed choked and out of breath (氣). The higher it climbed, the more smoke it puffed, the louder it roared, and the harder it panted. After nearly rolling backward several times, unable to make any sound, puff smoke, or pant, it barely reached the mountaintop (山上). It is such a rugged mountain.

Here, as if gaining new energy, the automobile resumed its dash. After going a while, it crossed the next pass, Tŏkch'ŏn Husan (德川後山). With the slanting evening sun (石陽) at our backs, we continued east, east. By the time we reached the Ch'ŏngsong town (邑) bus stop (停留場), the afterglow (落照) mingled with the ripe colors of the distant mountain's autumn leaves (丹楓), blurring into reddish hues and fading away. A crescent moon (半月), bent like a bow (弓), soared in the sky (天空), casting its clear, bright light onto the cool late autumn night scene (夜景) of the earth (大地). I took my luggage (旅具) and entered an inn (旅館).

After finishing supper (夕飯), I went out into the town streets (市街) and gazed at the bright moon. Ah, the moonlit night (月夜) at Chuwangsan must be endlessly wonderful. Bathing in the love of that clear and beautiful nature, receiving its baptism, gaining its embrace, ah! How much refreshing solace would this body, sick and weak from being stained by worldly dust (世塵), receive? What a lucky child (幸運兒) I would be! Thinking this, I returned to the inn. <143>

(Seventh Day, Clear)
As planned (預定), after finishing breakfast (朝飯), I set out for Chuwangsan. By chance, I met Mr. S, the Ch'ŏngsong Bureau Chief (支局長) of a certain daily newspaper (某日報), and we decided to go together (同行). Feeling lonely (孤獨) on this trip (今行), I mentioned several times how happy (幸福) I was by this unexpected companionship with Mr. S. We rented bicycles (自轉車) together and left the noisy Ch'ŏngsong town streets (市街), filled with sounds of fighting and chatter. We crossed streams, treaded narrow paths along rice paddy ridges (논둑), passed mountain (山) slopes, and wound around bends, gazing at the scenery (風景) left and right (左右). Before we knew it, we had covered the forty *ri* (四十 里). The ripe colors of the dense autumn leaves, adjusted (調節) by the bright sunlight (太陽 光線), shone reddish-yellow all around—front, back, left, and right. Before our eyes (眼前), imposing cliffs like sheer walls, large rocks, small rocks, fantastic rocks of grotesque shapes (奇岩怪石) lay scattered here and there. And with the stream flowing from Chuwanggul (周王窟) between them, ancient temples (古刹), seemingly millennia old, stood one on each densely wooded bank (樹林 兩便). The one entering on the east side is said to be Taejŏn-sa (大*寺 - *likely 大典寺, Taejŏn Temple), and the one on the north side is Paengnyŏnam (白蓮庵). This is said to be the first entrance (初入口) to Chuwangsan.

We entered Paengnyŏnam. When we sought the head monk (主持), a woman (夫人) came out and said the head monk was currently away (出他中). She called another monk (僧) who kindly (親切) led us to the reception room (應接室). First (爲先), I took off my hat (帽子), overcoat (外套), Western suit jacket (洋服 上衣), and socks (洋襪). As if in a hurry, I took a towel (手巾) and soap (石鹼) and went down to the clear, white-flowing stream in front of Paengnyŏnam. Ah, what clear stream water! I immediately washed my face (顔面) and limbs (手足), covered layer by layer with worldly dust (世塵). Then I came back in and had lunch (午飯). The food (飮食), unexpectedly (意外), was Japanese style (日本式). Rather than saying it was a blend (折衷) of Korean style (朝鮮式) and Japanese style, it seems much more appropriate (打當) to say it was entirely Japanese style. In a world where everyone learns from Japan (日本), this alone wasn't that surprising, but to think it had thoroughly reached even this deep Chuwangsan ancient temple (古刹), far removed from worldly connections (因緣)! Frowning once, I tasted the side dishes made with Japanese soy sauce (醬油) using the provided sterilized chopsticks (消毒箸). It was Japanese-style food prepared with considerable effort (苦) in learning. Having no choice, I scooped rice from the communal container with a rice paddle into a bowl. Even the dining table (飯床) was Japanese. Sitting bent over with my companion Mr. S, we each had three or four bowls (食器). This meal offered not even a hint of the flavor of our Korean temples (寺刹味). Anyway, since the purpose (目的) of this trip was to see Chuwangsan, the absence of the head monk, our guide (案內者), was regrettable (遺憾) above all. While thinking this, the head monk happened to return. Carrying a tray piled high with locally produced (當地 所産) soaked persimmons (沉柿), he came outside the window with a respectful (恭順) demeanor (態度), put his palms together (合掌), and bowed. Words of greeting (人事) followed: <144> "I apologize for the various ways I failed to host you properly (未對接) due to my absence." I greeted him gladly (引接) and invited him into the room. After exchanging greetings, as if in a hurry, I requested his guidance for touring Chuwangsan. However, as my knowledge (知識) of Chuwangsan was poor (貧弱), I first asked the head monk to roughly explain the sequence (順序) of sightseeing and the historical background (古蹟 材料) of the scenic spots (名勝). The head monk spoke with a gentle and respectful (溫恭) demeanor and humble (謙遜) tone (語調). "I do not know much, but I will tell you what I know," he said, and then continued as follows.

"The name (名稱) of Chuwangsan came about like this," he began. "During the reign of Emperor Dezong (德宗皇帝) of the Tang (唐) dynasty, Zhou Du (周鍍), the eighth-generation descendant (八代 孫) of the Jin dynasty official Zhou Yi (晋世臣周顗), resentful (憤慨) that his ancestral homeland (祖先 故國) of Jin (晋) had been destroyed (滅亡) by Tang, harbored the great ambition (大志) of restoration (回復). He raised an army (兵) of ten thousand men (萬衆), proclaimed himself the Son of Heaven of Zhou (周天子), and attacked Tang. Defeated (敗) by the Tang general Guo Ziyi (唐將郭子儀), he fled far across Liaodong (遼東) with over a thousand soldiers (千餘 兵士) to this mountain. Later, responding to Tang's demand, made through fierce negotiations (猛烈 交涉) with the king of Goguryeo (高句麗王), to capture and send this Zhou Du, the famous generals (當時 名將) Ma Ilsŏng (馬一聲), Isŏng (二聲), Samsŏng (三聲), Sasŏng (四聲), etc. (等) were dispatched. They surrounded this mountain and captured Zhou Du, the King of Zhou (周王). Since then, this mountain has been called Chuwangsan. As for things verifiable historically (史的 可考), I will explain them when we reach the sites (現場)."

Thanking him repeatedly, I stood up and read the *Chuwangsanji* (周王山誌 - Record of Chuwangsan) posted on the wall (壁上). Materials for historical consideration (史的 考察 材料) were all recorded (記載) there. "Alright, this is sufficient," I thought. Putting on the straw sandals (集身) given by the head monk (主持), Mr. S and I followed behind while the head monk led the way, now beginning the sightseeing tour. We went out the gate (文 밧글 - likely typo for 밖을, outside), crossed a small bridge (橋) amidst overgrown yellow chrysanthemums (黃菊), looked back at Changgunam (將軍岩 - General Rock) to the north (北便), and proceeded along the mountain slope path to Kiam (旗岩 - Flag Rock). This Kiam is also called Kunryangam (軍糧岩 - Army Provision Rock). This rock is an imposing cliff rising nearly a thousand *ch'ŏk* (近 千尺) high from the mountaintop (山上). It became "Kiam" because flags (旗) were planted on this rock to establish a defensive formation (敵軍 防備 陣營 整頓 意味). It also became "Kunryangam" because, as a show of force (示威運動) against the enemy (敵軍), they wrapped this rock with straw sacks (섬) and claimed it was army provisions (軍糧). Seeing the numerous scars (無數 傷處) on the rock's front face (岩 全面), I asked the head monk. He replied that they were marks left by enemy arrows (弓) shot to test if it was indeed army provisions.

I ask you, Kunryangam!
Your name (일홈) is fine, but
On your beautiful waist,
What are these scars? <145>
Had you rather been Nameless Rock (無名岩),
Leaving your beautiful body
As it was,
I would not be grieving.

Reciting this impromptu poem (興詩), I continued. From here, we descended the slope again and gradually wound deeper into the narrow path amidst tangled trees (樹林) by the clear stream (시내ㅅ가), heading towards the deep valley (深谷). We entered a narrow gorge beneath imposing, mountain-like rocks (산덤이 가튼 우렁찬 바우) that seemed to touch heads (머리). From here, a cool breeze (바람) rushed past my hands (손틍) and struck the rocks on both sides (兩便), creating an echo (反響) that sounded like "wheeeng-." Ahead, Chahasŏng (紫霞城 - Purple Mist Castle) came into view. This is a castle built connecting large rock mounds (큰 바우덤과 덤과) to conceal Ch'ŏnghaktong (靑鶴洞 - Blue Crane Grotto) on the left (左便) and Chuwangdong (周王洞 - King Zhou's Grotto) on the right (右便). We passed through the remains of the castle wall (城址 陳). This area was somewhat wider. Ch'ŏnghaktong and Chuwangdong embraced it on both sides, surrounded by Yŏnhwabong (蓮花峯 - Lotus Peak), Yŏnhwagul (蓮花窟 - Lotus Cave), and numerous other peaks (峯) and rocks (岩). A short distance further, P'yoam (瓢岩 - Gourd Rock) and Kŭpsuam (汲水岩 - Water-Drawing Rock) stood shoulder to shoulder (억개). P'yoam indeed had a large hole hollowed out like a gourd (표주박 모양), and Kŭpsuam looked exactly like it was drawing water, dripping water drop by drop (방울방울). Sitting down here, I briefly recalled the final fate (末路) of King Zhou.

Ah, thinking of his ancestors' past deeds, he cried out, "Why weep for ancestors? The mountains and rivers remain unchanged (何用先祖泣, 山河自無異)," but this was his end (末路)!

Chahasŏng built high,
Hiding a thousand soldiers (千兵),
He prepared a place to stay,
Called Ch'ŏnghak and Chuwang.
Drawing water at Kŭpsuam,
Scooping it with P'yoam,
Cooking rice at Kunryangam,
Did he stave off hunger and thirst (飢渴)?
Alas, a defeated fate (敗運),
Was it easy to reverse?
Ask not of success or failure,
Past and present are the same.
Praising his original intention (素志),
I too grieve.

I marveled thus at this place. From here, the autumn leaves (丹楓) increasingly made the sightseer stop his staff. This slope, that hillside, every corner—all were red. As Chuwangsan originally bears the name Little Kŭmgang (小金剛), especially meeting the beautiful season of yellow chrysanthemums and autumn leaves (黃菊丹楓), <146> we frequently encountered sightseers coming and going. We set off again towards Oeryongch'u (外龍湫 - Outer Dragon Falls). After passing a short slope, the valley floor was paved with flat rocks (盤石) of various shapes and colors (형형색색). We walked over Waryongam (臥龍岩 - Lying Dragon Rock), a thick and long rock, and beside it, the beautiful Ch'wisuam (醉水岩 - Intoxicated Water Rock). Seeing the Maitreya statue sitting alone hidden in a cave (窟) on the left (左便), we climbed up below Hyangdŭngbong (香燈峯 - Incense Lamp Peak) and Hakso-dae (鶴巢臺 - Crane Nest Terrace), which faced each other (左右 相對). We then passed along the steep path of Hakso-dae's rock wall (石壁), and finally, stroking the end (끗) of the rock wall with our palms (손바닥) and pushing our bellies (배) forward, we crawled up through a gap between rocks like stacks of grain (집덤 가튼 바우 틈) and arrived at Oeryongch'u. Though it was a chilly late autumn day, here sweat (땀) poured like rain (雨), and my body (身體) felt sticky. Taking off my hat and overcoat and wiping the sweat, I bent over on a flat rock (盤石) and peered into Oeryongch'u. The waters pouring down (네리쏫는 물들) from Naeryongch'u (內龍湫 - Inner Dragon Falls) upstream flowed through winding rock crevices (구불구불 바우 틈) and fell into this Oeryongch'u with sounds of "Swaa-swaa-kwang-kwang-." The height (놉히) of the waterfall (龍湫) seemed immeasurably deep, appearing to be nearly a thousand *ch'ŏk* (近 千尺). Ah, what a stunningly beautiful (奇絶) place! If this had been widely introduced (紹介) to the world earlier, how many people would have feasted their eyes on this beautiful sight? From here, we treaded an even more dangerous path towards the even more beautiful Naeryongch'u. With hat and overcoat still off, making myself much lighter, I descended the rock wall (石壁). Treading footholds sparsely carved out with a chisel (정), just enough to place a foot, I barely crawled into a rock cave (石窟) for a while, then emerged onto another steep rock wall path, finally reaching the entrance (入口) of Naeryongch'u. The guide, the head monk, suddenly spoke with a seemingly worried expression (튑�정 - likely typo for 표정, expression): "If you enter this gorge (골작구니), it is Naeryongch'u. Usually, to enter inside, one wades through the water (물) of this gorge. However, due to unprecedented rainfall (無前 雨水) this year, the sand (모래) has been washed away, making the water at the entrance passage (어구) about five *kan* (五間 - approx. 9 meters) wide and unfathomably deep (深 不測). It is difficult even for a skilled swimmer (水泳 選手)." Although I couldn't swim (水泳), my desire (行動) to see the beauty (美) of Naeryongch'u made me unconsciously forget (忘却) the danger (危險). I wanted to try entering, even if it meant taking a risk (冒險的), even risking my life (決死的). So, I said this to the head monk: "I will definitely attempt the adventure (모험). If there is danger, please rescue me." The head monk, his face even paler (失色), said, "I will describe the scenery (景致) of Naeryongch'u to you in detail (仔細) as you see it. It is truly dangerous (危險)." Hearing these words, my heart suddenly weakened (선듯 마음이 弱), and the thought of danger resurfaced. Like a madman (밋친드시), I took several (멧) steps forward and peered around, trying to glimpse as much of the inside of the gorge as possible. However, the waterfall (瀑布) and Naeryongch'u, which could only be seen after turning one more bend, <147> were naturally not visible.

Ah, am I the unfortunate one (不幸兒) unable to experience the beautiful nature of Naeryongch'u? Or is this creating a future opportunity (機會) to meet it once more? Oh! If so, I shall come again! Surrounded on all four sides (四面) by rock walls (石壁), I sat down on a flat rock (盤石) by the stream flowing below. Now, there was no other choice (他道) but to listen to the head monk's explanation.

"Naeryongch'u," he said, "has the waterfall (瀑布) pouring down behind it and is fronted by a crescent-shaped (半月形) sandy beach (白沙場). The day before King Zhou (周王) was captured, a dragon (龍) cried three times in this waterfall (龍湫)."

Though I couldn't see it from this close distance (地尺), it must indeed be beautiful nature (自然). There was a feeling here as if some mystery (神秘) was hidden. The faint "kwang-kwang" sound of the waterfall, the gentle murmur of the water flowing from Naeryongch'u, and the cool "swaa-" sound of the blowing wind—all combined and echoed (反響) off the surrounding rock walls. The resulting wave (波動) of "Swaa-wang-" filled this narrow space (空間) where only a piece of the sky was visible.

The wind grew colder. Chilly. Retracing the path we came (오든 길), we carefully passed the steep rock wall (石壁), went through the rock cave (石窟), and returned again to Oeryongch'u. Putting on the hat and overcoat I had taken off earlier (악가버서 노앗든), we left this place, went around the base (밋틀 - 밑을) of Hakso-dae, and pushed through the thicket (숩) of autumn leaves (丹楓), heading towards Chuwangam (周王庵 - King Zhou's Hermitage). Along the path between rocks (바우 틈 길), the evergreen color (四時 푸른 빗) of the boxwood trees (黃楊木) standing in clumps (폭이 폭이) was also a pleasingly unusual sight (異彩) in this world of autumn leaves. After winding around for quite some time, we arrived at Chuwangam, situated in a hollow surrounded on all four sides (四面) by mountains and rock walls (石壁). Seeing the hermitage, we crawled up (기여올나) the gully winding between the rock walls behind it. Placing hands and feet (手足) on the steep stone path (石逕 빗탈), sometimes praying, sometimes pushing our bodies (身體), we barely entered Chuwangam. This cave (窟) is a rock grotto (岩窟) large enough for about thirty or forty people (三四十名). King Zhou took refuge (避身) here. One morning (아츰), while descending to the stone basin (石函) in front of the cave (窟 壓) to wash his face (洗手), Ma Sasŏng's (馬四聲) iron hook (鐵鉤) came down from above the cave (窟 우으로), caught him, and he was taken away. From one side of the cave's brow (이마), several thin streams of water hang down (듸리우고 잇는) like threads (실가치); they look like silver threads (銀빗) or perhaps tears (눈물). And the rocks below are always damp (추근추근하게 저저잇다).

Receiving the will of ancestors (祖先 뜻),
The body that fought steadfastly (굿세게 싸우든 몸),
Has it come to this?
Leading a great army (萬衆 大軍),
Suffering defeat (敗北),
That too was fate (運).
Being captured was harsh enough, <148>
But what was the reason for handing him over?
Was that not a deep resentment (원한)?
The resentment (원한) bound for a thousand autumns (千秋),
Is the water falling (나린 물) from the cave ceiling (窟頂)
Tears (눈물), I wonder?

Reciting this extemporaneous poem (即興詩), I left Chuwanggul (周王窟) and came back down to Chuwangam (周王庵). In front (壓헤) of this hermitage stand Pihaebong (飛海峯 - Flying Sea Peak) and Oksunbong (玉荀峯 - Jade Bamboo Shoot Peak). Seen from here, the entire Chuwangsan area (全區) looks exactly as if enclosed by a stone screen (石屛). Perhaps this is the reason (까닭) why this mountain was anciently (古代) named Sŏkpyŏngsan (石屛山 - Stone Screen Mountain). The sightseeing was now finished. The head monk (主持), who had guided us briskly for over half a day (半日), let out a sigh as if having completed a major task, "Hoo~," and then said, "Indeed, this Chuwangsan is a beautiful scenic spot, but because its area (幅圓) is narrow, it is very convenient (簡便) for sightseeing. It is a beautiful place that everyone can see in a short time (時間)." He then suggested we descend the mountain and return to our lodging at Paengnyŏnam (宿所). Hearing his words (말), I followed behind him, feeling somewhat reluctant (섭섭한드시). Thus, we descended (나려섯다) to the Chuwangsan entrance (入口) where we had first climbed up (악가 올라오든). We entered Taejŏn-sa (大典寺), which we had only seen from across the stream when first arriving. This temple (寺) commemorates King Zhou's (周王) own son (親子), Chu Taejŏn (周大典). After King Zhou was captured, Taejŏn found his father's (親父) corpse (尸), buried it (安葬) on this mountain (此山), shaved his own head (自己 머리), became a monk (僧), studied Daoist practices (仙學 功夫) here (此處), and subsequently met his end (最後). Paengnyŏnam is said to be the house (집) where he studied (공부하든). The tour ended here. Following the head monk, we crossed the stream and returned (도라왓다) to our lodging at Paengnyŏnam. The evening sun (石陽) had already set (어늬듯 넘어가고), and the autumn leaves filling the mountains and streams (左右 山川) on both sides combined their color (빗 合) with the red afterglow (落照), settling themselves down (自藏) even more richly (一層 더-무르녹게). I opened the window again and stepped out into the garden (뜰). Looking afar at the entire scene (全景) of Chuwangsan that I had vividly seen.

Ah!! All beautiful rocks! Rock walls (石壁)? Flat rocks (盤石)?! Clear stream? Lovely autumn leaves (丹楓)? Ah! All nature (自然)? Within the great (大), wide (廣), and boundless (限業) loving embrace (愛 品) of you, great nature (大自然), I, having received your embrace (擁抱) all day long (終日토록), bow my head (頭) in respectful (恭順) gratitude (感謝), I thought.

Then I went into the room and faced supper (夕飯). The food was still Japanese style (日本式). The head monk's words, "It might be bland (신거우나), but please eat a lot (만히 잡수시오)," sounded truly sincere (誠意) and kind (親切) in tone (語調). After finishing supper, intoxicated (醉) by nature (自然), I lit a cigarette, having abstained all day (終日토록 굶은 담배), and climbed up to the second-floor pavilion (二層 樓上) at the entrance (入口) of Paengnyŏnam.

The crescent moon (半月), bent like a bow (弓), soared in the sky (天空). The sound (소리) of crickets (귀뚜램이) crying from all sides (四面) in the cool breeze (서늘한 바람) sounded boisterously loud. <149> Adding to that, the sound (소리) of the clear stream flowing (흘너나리는) below the pavilion (樓下) sounded even more beautiful. And the night (밤) kept getting deeper (깁허간다). Everything was asleep (萬衆이 모다 잠자고). Only the imposing rocks of Kunryangam and Changgunam, casting shadows leaning towards the north (北便), remained. At this moment, all the things I had dreamed of (꿈구든) in the world (世界) began to disappear. Not a single one remained; all vanished. There was nothing but gazing at the peaceful (平和) moon (달) shining impartially (公平) without a trace (一毫) of personal feeling (私情), listening to the sound of the stream flowing as it pleased (흘느고 십흔 대로 흘느는 시내 소리) and the sound of the crickets crying as they pleased (울고 십흔 대로 우는 귀뚜램이 소리). And so, in the embrace (品) of this peaceful (平和) and free (自由) great nature (大自然), my only thought was how to eternally prolong (永續) this moment-like extension (延長) of life (生). And all the deceitful turmoil (거짓 作亂) of fighting (相爭), deceiving, coaxing, and stealing in the secular world (世間) did not touch my mind (念頭) at all. Before I knew it (어느 듯), the night (밤) had grown much deeper (훨신 깁헛다). While lost in these fantasies (空想) for a time, the moon set, and the earth (大地) seemed to have a black curtain drawn over it (黑幕 나려친드시), changing (밧귀어젓다) into pitch-black darkness (캉캄한 漆夜). I descended (나려와) from the pavilion and returned (도라오니) to my lodging (宿所). The time (時) was already 11:30 p.m.

(Eighth Day, Clear)
Deep sleep (곤한 잠) ended. The earth (大地) was brightly (환-하게) lit. Today too, as planned (預定), in order to catch the regular (定期) departing automobile (自動車) from Ch'ŏngsong town (靑松邑), I quickly (얼푼) washed my face (세면), took my travel gear (旅具), checked my watch (時計), thanked the head monk (主持) repeatedly (멧 번이나), and boarded the automobile. In the meantime, the nature of Chuwangsan, to which I had grown attached, stood silently surrounding us, as if sad (슬허하는 情) to send me off. As a farewell (作別), I looked back (도라다보고) at Chuwangsan again and again, saying, "See you again in spring (春)! See you again in summer (夏)!" Driving (모라) the automobile, we came to Ch'ŏngsong town. Here, I parted ways (作別) with Mr. S, threw myself (身體) into the automobile urging departure, and returned (도라왓다) to my home village (鄕第) in Andong (安東). --(The End)-- <150>

**Summary**

The author, Chŏng Hyŏn-mo, recounts his long-desired trip to Chuwangsan in North Kyŏngsang Province in the autumn of 1926. He travels by car from near Andong to Ch'ŏngsong and then proceeds by bicycle to the mountain entrance. Staying at Paengnyŏnam temple, he tours the scenic spots like Kiam, Chahasŏng, Oeryongch'u, and Chuwangam with the head monk, learning the legend of the exiled Chinese prince Zhou Du. He is deeply moved by the natural beauty, especially the autumn foliage and rock formations, contrasting it with the troubles of the world, and finds profound peace in nature's embrace.

From a historical perspective, the article offers glimpses into colonial Korea: the improving but still difficult transportation (automobile struggling over passes), the explicit mention of Japanese cultural influence extending even to food service in a remote Buddhist temple, and the author's social commentary contrasting the hardship of farmers with the perceived injustice of the world. The underlying sentiment of pride in Korean natural beauty ("our homeland's landscape") juxtaposed with its lack of international recognition, and the solace found in nature away from the "deceitful turmoil" of the world, might reflect subtle nationalist feelings and escapism common during the period.

周王山探勝記
						鄭顯模
이 周王山은 경북의 山邑인 靑松에서도 훨신 40 里나 드러가 숨어잇는 아름다운 名山이다. 일명은 「小金剛」이라고 부르는 경북 대표의 유일한 名勝이다. 그러나 이것이 年前 엇던 신문의 지면에 약간 그 影子를 빗치 웟든 일과 또 이 名勝을 위하야 靑松郡 읍내에 周王山 古蹟保存會라는 것이 되여 잇슬 뿐이다. 이 周王山도 일즉이 주인을 못 맛난 우리 강산의 하나가 되여 널리 세계적으로 그 아름다운 존재를 자랑하지 못하엿슴은 무엇보담 유감이엿다.
나는 다행히 이 周王山 갓가운 지방에서 生長하엿스나 불행히 어더볼 기회가 업섯다. 해외에 在學할 때이나 京城에서 방랑할 때이나 항상 마음으로만 그 아름다운 자연을 동경하든 나는 당분간 鄕里의 전원 생활을 맛보게 되는 今日에야 비로소 와 보게 되엿다.
아름다웁고 사랑스러운 이 위대한 자연에 擁抱된 나는 이것이 나 혼자만의 환희할 행복이 아닌 것을 깨달엇다. 그리하야 비룩 졸열한 붓이나마 외람히 그 全景의 萬一이라도 그려서 널리 영원히 이 周王山을 빗낼 만흔 探勝客들의 도라볼 때의 路次나 알려줌이 될가 한다.
나는 여러 해를 두고 원하든 周王山 구경의 길을 오늘이야 떠낫다. 때마츰 아름다운 단풍이 산의 언덕과 골작구니의 우거진 樹林에 불근 물을 드리고 잇는 서늘한 가을 9월(음력) 초6일 오후 4시 경이엇다.
구름 한 점도 업는 가을 한울은 동그러케 놉흐고 내리 쪼이는 日光은 오히려 지나간 녀름을 연상하리만큼 더운 늣김을 주고 잇다. 간단한 旅具를 가지고 자동차 오기를 기다리면서 鄕第의 洞口에서 배회하든 나는 뿡뿡하는 소리로 모라오는 자동차에 반가이 뛰어올넛다. 자리에 안즈매 차는 여전히 소리를 치면서＜142＞ 다라나기를 시작한다. 어느 듯 泉旨坪을 지나게 된다. 이곳은 安東으로도 주요한 농산지의 하나이다. 널분들의 여긔 저긔서 농부들의 베비는 소리와 벼 실어드리는 광경. 모다 그들 농촌 생활을 그려내는 예술이오 詩이다. 그러나 나는 그들을 볼 때에 다시 이러한 늣김을 가지게 된다.
아! 고마운 농부들아? 귀중한 형제들이여! 그대들이야말로 生을 위하야 분투하는 용사이며 일꾼들이다. 손발이 달코 피땀이 흘너도 쉬지 안코 일하는 이들이다. 그러나 이 세상은 아직도 그대들의 은혜와 공로를 모르는 악한 세상이다. 모순의 세계이다. 고맙고 귀중한 그대들을 도리혀 천대하는 세계이오 억압하는 세계이오 欺瞞하는 세계이오 착취하는 세계이다. 과연 그럿타. 그러나 이 모든 기만이 충만한 세계로부터서 아름다운 진리의 승리한 세계가 출현되는 것이 원칙이라 하면 밋노니 갓가우나 멀거나 將來는 오즉 고마웁고 귀중한 그대들의 세계일 것이라 하엿다.
泉旨坪을 지나서 東으로 山下里의 後嶺에 다다럿다. 이 산은 이 부근에서도 유명하게 험준한 산이다. 승객 6 인을 실은 자동차는 게다가 또 우편물을 달고 이 놉흔 재를 기어오른다. 맹낭이 거북한 모양이다. 목이 메이고 기가 막키는 모양이다. 올나갈스록 갈사록 연기를 토하며 소리를 질느며 헐덕어런다. 그리다가는 아무 소리도 연기도 헐덕어리지도 못하고 도리혀 뒤거름질 할번한 때를 멧 번이나 격거서 간신이 山上에 올나섯다. 이러케도 험준한 산이다.

「內龍湫는 우으로 쏘다지는 폭포를 등지고 압흐로는 반월형의 백사장을 둘느고 잇다하며 周王이 잡히기 前日에 이 龍湫에서 龍이 세 번을 울엇다」 한다.
지척에다 두고 보지는 못하나 과연 아름다운 자연인가 보다 하엿다. 여긔는 그 무슨 신비를 감춘 듯한 늣김이 잇다. 은은히 들리는 쾅쾅하는 폭포 소리와 內龍湫에서 흘너나리는 잔잔한 물소리와 쏴-하고 부러오는 서늘한 바람 소리 모다 합하야 사면 石壁에 부드처서 쏴-왕-하는 反響의 波動은 한 쪼각 한울만 보이는 이 조분 공간을 울리고 잇다.
