Pilgrimage to Free Wedding Halls
						By Wŏtting T'ebŭl Saeng

I. Introduction

Before I begin writing this pilgrimage account of wedding halls, let me first introduce myself, the reporter writing this article. Introducing oneself is indeed terribly forward, but isn't it much smarter than being a fool who doesn't even know how to greet a person they see for the first time, even if someone else introduces them enthusiastically? If there's no one to introduce you, just sitting back hesitantly for a hundred years, or a thousand years, achieves nothing. Therefore, although it may be a bit forward, I will introduce myself. Also, there are many advantages to introducing oneself: firstly, the introduction will be done precisely, better than someone else could; secondly, one can add boasts that aren't even there; thirdly, one can hide flaws as much as possible. So, isn't introducing oneself much more beneficial than having someone else do it? That aside, let me mention the disadvantages of having someone else introduce you. Firstly, if the relationship between the introducer and the person being introduced is subtly bad, one worries about being subtly tormented even with a single word. Secondly, one worries about being introduced with falsehoods to someone you meet for the first time<77> and later becoming a laughingstock. Thirdly, when the introducer's character is not good, one worries that the other party might assume one's own character is the same as the introducer's. So, having someone else introduce you is generally not good. Therefore, I, being full of worries, intend to introduce myself without an introducer. This introduction is getting too long, and readers must be getting bored, but since the topic has come up, let's proceed step by step.

II. Introduction

I have named myself Wŏtting T'ebŭl (Wedding table). This name wasn't given by someone else, nor by my parents. I brazenly gave myself the name. If awkwardly translated into Korean, perhaps it would be *kyŏrhonsiksang* (結婚式床)? Knowing just that much should probably be correct. Those who don't know rolling words [foreign words] or perhaps elderly folks of the past might hear this and say, "Ah! With tens of thousands of Chinese characters available, why couldn't you choose a proper name? What kind of blasted nonsense is that? Bah! What sensible thing could a rascal like you possibly say? I won't listen!" But it's not like that. Even a sage follows the customs of the time, so there's no need to be overly stubborn. Furthermore, let me give an example to prove why such a name is necessary now. Do you know what the names of newspaper reporters are these days? You'll be surprised when you hear. It's nothing special! But since I started talking about it, let me continue. Please try following newspaper reporters around. Especially watch them in theaters or on streetcars. A newspaper reporter just walks right into a theater without paying. Then the ticket taker hurriedly asks, "Who are you?" and the reporter, with a strange look in their eyes, just says "Pass!" and that's it. On the streetcar too, when getting off, if the conductor says, "Show your ticket before getting off," they again just say "Pass!" and that's it. Because his name is "Pass," he can go anywhere freely. He travels well without paying money. Pass just barges into places where even paying doesn't easily get you in. Similarly, when I just walk into a wedding hall, if the gatekeeper shouts, "Who's there?" I reply, "T'ebŭl!" and I can pass safely, no matter how guarded the wedding of a beauty might be. A somewhat burly gatekeeper might needlessly bother<78> people by asking, "What T'ebŭl?" But then I say, "Wŏtting T'ebŭl!" and no matter how mischievous they are, they will adopt a serious expression, lift my body with both hands, and place me right in front of that lovely bride. Nowadays, at the weddings of young men and women, everyone uses English names. For example, Bride (新婦), Bridegroom (新郞), Veil (面紗), Wedding Ring (結婚式契約指環), Frock coat (禮服), Morning coat (禮服), some kind of Collar, Necktie... and so on. There are truly so many. Are the bride and groom's names the only Korean names there? No. Sometimes you hear bride and groom names that sound strange. Names like "Maria," "Yosŏp," "Yohan," "Meri," "Ro-sŭ," "Heren." Truly, if an old person from the past heard them, they'd laugh, saying "Ha! What kind of names are those? In our language, are they names like 'Kaettong' [Dog dung], 'Soettong' [Cow dung], 'Twitkan' [Outhouse], 'Kannan' [Newborn]? Haha!"
So, my name also has to be "Wŏtting T'ebŭl" to participate in such grand wedding ceremonies. But if I have no profession, others won't know my inner thoughts, and the police station will take note of me as unemployed. That's why I chose such a name, so please understand that much, forgive me, and listen.

III. Lineage Introduction (血統紹介)

It's not right to talk only about my family background, but matters should not be handled carelessly; everything must be done step by step. First, let me introduce my face. I am truly sorry and embarrassed to say this, but I have long been famous for my ugliness.
I have no eyes, no nose, and no face. All I have is a back (背) and four feet (足 四個). However, lacking these things makes me just as perceptive. Therefore, I see everything others see, hear everything others hear, and I am very quick-witted. Originally, I did have a neck, but among the young men who come to weddings to give congratulatory speeches, the hot-tempered ones, when excited, regardless of others, just pound on my neck with their fists. At such times, unable to bear it, I scream "Thump!" Having been hit on the neck so much, it eventually disappeared, and now only a flat back remains. Now, whether they hit me or not, only their hands will hurt; my back won't break. Oh dear! But just as there's a flyer above a crawler, there might still be someone<79> who can break my back.
Originally from a distinguished noble family, wealthy and prolific since our ancestors' time, I have many ancestors, many relatives, and many siblings. However, unfortunately, like a eunuch in human terms, my reproductive function is incomplete, so offspring like me can only be born through the hands and tools of a carpenter.
My ancestors, as you know, are likely the *soban* (소반) [small portable dining table] on which you place your rice bowls and side dishes for breakfast and dinner. If we dig deeper into the family register, perhaps it's the *kaedari soban* (개다리 소반) [dog-leg table] used when tormenting Yi Toryŏng (리도령), Ch'unhyang's (춘향) lover. And I have many cousins: *kyojasang* (교자상) [large dining table], *ch'aeksang* (책상) [desk], *samusang* (사무상) [office desk], *ŭngjŏpsilsa* (응졉실사) [reception room table], etc., there are many. Their fortunes are all good. Others might say my fortune is good, but being called around for weddings, do I get to eat the food or enjoy the charming sights? It's truly pathetic. Yet, there's my truly pitiful uncle, whose name is *Anban* (안반) [large wooden pounding board]. Originally having a wide face, ignorant country lads of the past would place rice cakes on it and slam down with a hundred-pound *ttŏkme* (떡메) [rice-cake mallet]. What do you think happened? In an instant, he just became flat. What are eyes or anything else? What is a face?
Oh no, I've chattered so much when I'm busy needing to get to the wedding hall. Don't worry. Now! Let's really get to the story.

IV
"Marriage fundamentally aims for a man and woman to unite according to the principle of yin and yang in heaven and earth (天地陰陽之理), form a family, bear sons and daughters, and achieve wealth and prosperity!" This is what the elders of the past say. "Late at night, lonely and desolate, while drawing the face of my beloved and sighing frequently, I unfold a letter which says, 'My love! My love! My dear love! Let's never forget! Ah, the world is lonely. My heart is lonesome. But if only you are beside me, I smile magnanimously. Flowers bloom in the winter garden!'" -- getting married amongst themselves like this is probably the marriage procedure of modern young men and women.
Therefore, a marriage arranged by the strict command of parents, pairing a boy and girl who have never even seen each other, where the woman couldn't even properly look up at her husband until she bore her first son – such clumsy wedding ceremonies – versus falling for someone after just one sideways glance in some corner<80> if they happen to like each other, and thereafter, whenever the two meet, they jump for joy, kissing noisily enough to make bystanders nauseous, and then, forget the first son, just three or four days after meeting, the woman allegedly jabs her finger indiscriminately at the man's face with the hand sparkling with her pretty engagement ring. Then, by the time they enter the wedding hall, the bride usually already has a child in her belly! This is the gist of free marriage.
So, you see! Since the people are different, circumstances are different, lives are different, and thoughts behind the marriage are different, it is naturally an inevitable fact that the wedding ceremonies will be different.

My first time participating in a free wedding ceremony was in the mid-spring of 1919. Thanks to my parents, I went to study in America, finished my studies, and upon returning, received a degree called W.T. so that the earnest efforts of my parents and siblings back home tens of thousands of *ri* away would not be in vain. Due to being "Wŏtting T'ebŭl," I received overwhelming favor from young unmarried men and women who admired new learning, and thus was appointed as the wedding table for new-style weddings.
I don't know the exact date, but anyway, as I mentioned earlier, it was mid-spring. Suddenly, a white cloth was spread over my back, and on top of it was placed a vase filled with various flowers. "Aha! So this is the modern Korean wedding!" I thought, tensing inwardly as I awaited the ceremony. After returning from America, I had heard about traditional Korean weddings from my cousin, the *kyojasang*, so I had some idea, but compared to what I saw in America, which was truly free and even dissolute, how could I compare it with our Chosŏn?
First, a white cloth was spread on my back, and fragrant flowers were placed on it. According to the stories heard from the *kyojasang*, in old-style weddings, not only were such fresh flowers (生花) not used, but there were many things placed on the table. Also, tables had genders, a groom's table and a bride's table. The groom's table came from the bride's house, and the bride's table from the groom's house, loaded so heavily that the *kyojasang*'s back bent like a porter's waist! Still, that fellow had good fortune; his forehead shone slick with food stains, yet whenever he saw me, he would grin and tell me all sorts of stories.
Nowadays, sometimes there are still bride and groom tables, but generally speaking, this wedding ceremony is simpler. If,<81> say, noodles or kimchi were placed on my back, and some thoughtless attendant or matchmaker spilled kimchi juice all over, oh my, how disgusting would that be?
After a short while, spectators crowded in. It was indeed a spacious church hall. In old-style weddings, even wealthy families couldn't help but have cramped houses, and in summer, it was truly difficult. According to what I heard, one could nearly die from the smell of people's sweat, and women's chatter could make one's hair fall out. Moreover, there are women who smell strongly of body odor. Why would a woman, not being a main guest, eat so much garlic? It must be thanks to having a drunkard husband. And lazy women who don't bathe might smell of rotting flesh. Anyway, they say all sorts of smells, foul and fishy, rise up.
But new-style weddings are truly nice. There are no such dirty smells. Because the place itself is spacious--. Rather, if it's past autumn, one might need to light a stove, so times have truly changed! After a little while, a middle-aged gentleman dressed in truly neat Western clothes entered. He carried a gold-rimmed Bible tucked under his arm. He seemed deeply moved, perhaps recalling his own unhappy wedding, and entered with his head slightly bowed. A little later, the sound of automobiles outside went "Vroom-vroom-" "Uwaa-" creating a ruckus. In the old days, it would be like sedan chair bearers shouting "Ekkara! Tchekkara! Make way! Hey-" causing a commotion. Automobiles are better. In the old style, dealing with sedan chair bearers, lantern bearers, rickshaw pullers, etc., caused headaches for busy people, but automobiles are good in that regard. Then, the spectators suddenly stood up, shouting, "They're coming! The bride and groom are coming!" creating an uproar. I also became tense. According to a message from my cousin, the *ch'aeksang* (desk), the groom was a graduate of B University, and the bride was also a graduate of some university. Furthermore, according to the *ch'aeksang*'s detailed investigation, the groom and bride were liberals (自由主義者). However, he was someone who broke down old customs, demolished conventions, opposed religious superstition, and cried out for the freedom of human life. The woman was also the same kind of liberal. Hadn't she once delivered a fiery speech at the Youth Association under the title "Demolishing Conventions and Liberalism"? The groom once met a pastor for evangelism and argued vehemently against it. He contended that humans should not be bound by religion and flounder in superstition.<82> He also strongly opposed the idea that God created humans. He insisted that humans came about through biological evolution (生物進化).
Anyway, they were such famous liberals.
After a little while, the sound of an organ began. At first, I found it quite strange. But watching closely, the organ music started, and then finally the bride and groom entered. The organ music continued softly, but the bride and groom never seemed to reach me. So I wondered if there was another "Wŏtting T'ebŭl" who had studied in America besides me, and they had gone there instead. I looked out, and what a ridiculous sight! It was like kindergarten (幼穉園) children mimicking a procession. I don't know if they were trying to show off their elegant steps, but matching their steps to the organ, they walked as slowly as sesame seeds rolling. Goodness, this is truly the walk of an old-fashioned bride! How could a bride walk so awkwardly! Maybe this too was an attempt to break the convention of the old-fashioned bride's walk, which was a source of deep regret, so they walked like rolling sesame seeds, albeit a bit stiflingly. That's also their freedom, right? What if they took all day to walk in? Ah! That's why I thought, they're liberals, so they must be different! What nonsense! Maybe the bride and groom sprained their ankles while joking around earlier? I thought all sorts of things to myself. Anyway, I wondered if this was how new-style weddings were, being shy right from the doorway. After fifteen or twenty-five minutes, they finally arrived in front of me. The smell of hair oil from the groom stung my nose, and the scent of powder and perfume from the bride made my chest churn. Such things don't happen now, but back then, perhaps because they took too long walking in, the pastor, unable to bear it any longer, must have had a mental lapse. He suddenly called for a "hymn," and everyone sang together. Anyway, singing together as a form of congratulation is good, but why include prayers and conduct the celebration like a worship service? Is that a new-style wedding? Then it's a new-style worship service!
But what impressed me most was this. At other times, this bride and groom would have vehemently opposed not just the church, but even the pastor's words, yet today they entered the church hall very solemnly. And when the pastor said, "God created heaven, earth, and all things, and made a man in His own image, naming him<83> Adam, and while he slept, took one of his left ribs and made a woman, whose name was Eve (?)", whereas previously they would never have quietly listened to such nonsensical talk, today they bowed their heads deeply as if the words were absolutely correct, repeatedly saying "Yes, yes!" Thus, in front of all those people, their liberalism completely collapsed. So it seems marriage is both blissful and fearsome? Those two young people sacrificed all their thoughts and principles for the thing called marriage. Perhaps they thought they could just muddle through for the moment, but what kind of thorough young person acts like a liberal when alone and like a heretic (異端者) in front of many people? If people change like that just because they get married, is it really people getting married? It seems marriage controls people, so are we returning to the era of phallic worship?
The pastor rambled on for a while with sense and nonsense, then said:
"Groom! Do you pledge to love the bride unceasingly, whether she is healthy or sick, happy or unhappy? Say 'Yes'."
"Yes!"
Then again:
"Bride! Do you pledge to love the groom unceasingly, whether he is healthy or sick, poor or rich? Say 'Yes'."
"Yes," replied the marriage-starved patients. I thought to myself--
"If it's a wedding ceremony, just have the wedding ceremony. That old man's meddling has gone too far! Whether they love each other later or not, what business is it of his! Really! He's quite talkative and meddlesome! Does he think these liberals, who aren't little children, lack the sense to figure that out, so he has to instruct them now? Perhaps that pastor was cruelly mistreated by his pockmarked wife when he was sick! Instead of talking about poverty, wealth, strength, and weakness at a wedding, it would seem more relevant to marriage to ask, 'Everyone, if this bride and groom have their first child, will it be a son or a daughter? If it's a son, which school should they enroll him in? If it's a daughter, which music school should they send her to?'"<84>
Even more ridiculous is when the groom gives the bride a ring. He doesn't just give it; he solemnly takes the bride's hand and puts the ring on her finger himself. What kind of promise is that? Their own firm pledge feels insufficient, so they give a gold ring as both a commemorative gift and a marital promise in front of many people. The promise should be sealed when the bride receives and puts on the ring herself, but the groom puts it on for her, and people nearby help hold her hand. What if later the bride breaks the contract and says, "You forced the ring on me, when did I ever put it on with my own hand?" And how can a new-style woman be so shy that she can't even lift her own hand without help from others? It may look very dignified, but in reality, it's an utterly incompetent, energy-draining wedding ceremony to watch.
They don't realize that "breaking formalism" leads to being bound by even more awkward formalism.
In old-style marriages, the bride and groom had never seen each other, so it was natural for them to be shy and careful. But in so-called new-style weddings, after seeing each other until their eyes hurt and whispering until their ears were soaked, they act even shyer than an old-style bride during the ceremony, behaving like dolls. Does this mean the new style involves more hypocrisy compared to the old style? Look! Young men and women these days loudly proclaim that new-style weddings break convention (인습타파), but what convention is actually broken?
Ultimately, the cramped house changed to a Western-style building, the *kkangkkaengi* [likely a simple traditional instrument] (though I don't know if *kkangkkaengi* were used in old weddings) was replaced by a Western organ, Korean clothes were replaced by Western suits, the *nangja jokduri* [traditional bridal headwear] was replaced by a veil (面紗), the wedding rite isn't performed with traditional rituals (禮) but with gold rings, attendants (수모) and matchmakers (중매) are replaced by bridesmaids/groomsmen (?) (들러리), pastors, and friends, the feast of colorful rice cakes and food (색떡밥소래) is replaced by meals at Myŏngwŏlgwan (명월관), Siktuwŏn (식도원), or some -ru (-루) or -sikdang (-식당) [types of restaurants], and the sedan chair (사인교) is replaced by an automobile. Everything is for the Japanese and Westerners; it's all just mimicking them. What's even funnier is when the groom wears a Western suit, but the bride still wears traditional Korean clothes. Is it like marrying a Westerner?
In the end, "breaking formalism" isn't about ideology; it's all about mimicking the formalities of foreigners, mimicking even rigid formalities, leaving no time to breathe. Look! Small formalities have turned into big ones: Western houses<85> are bigger than Korean houses, organs are bigger than *kkangkkaengi*. Western suits cost more than Korean clothes. Automobiles are bigger than sedan chairs. The ceremony involves more verbose talk than the Korean ceremony (례식). Moreover, many people who have such a wedding apparently go back to their Korean homes (their parents' homes) and have an old-style ceremony once more. It's said that the demanding bride, on the first night, despite their intimacy, acts standoffish and asks the groom to undress her! That makes sense too. If she can't act cute that day, when can she? If she can't receive love and act childish that day, when can she?
So, what's new about the new style? It's an East-West joint ceremony that incorporates every possible formality from both the West and the East. Thinking about it, people without money wouldn't even try to do it, but there's no need to waste money unnecessarily like that. Such things are just new formalities for the wealthy.
Is liberalism just the freedom to spend money? When the ceremony ends (식 끗나면), the automobile honks again. Then the bride and groom ride together. In the old days, where would they ride together? That's also a benefit of liberalism. Doesn't it look quite affectionate to others? But in reality, it's as if some dreadful martial law is imposed inside that car; they sit stiffly, feigning indifference, looking solemn like enemies. Whether the bride is secretly pinching the groom's thigh is a secret, so I don't know, but while they call it a honeymoon trip, it's actually a silent journey, a very hypocritical trip. Only when night falls, perhaps,
"Honey! How could you act so indifferent? Now I know, you're just pretending!"
"Oh, goodness! How did you manage to hold back all that whispering and chattering? Now I see, you're quite different inside and out!"
"Well, what could I do? Everyone else does it! Did you think I didn't want to talk to you? My heart was beating even faster." (Takes husband's hand and places it on her chest). "Free marriage! Free marriage! Ah! How agonizing formalities are! Can't we get married without formalities? Huh?" sighed the bride.
"Well! Haha! What's the use of talking about that?" The lights in the room went out.
This too is a joint report from my cousin *Kyojasang* and my eighth cousin, *Pamch'amsang* (밤참상) [late-night snack table]. (To be continued)<86>

**Summary**

Writing under the humorous pseudonym "Wŏtting T'ebŭl Saeng" (Wedding Table Person), the author critiques the "new-style" or "free" weddings emerging in 1920s Korea. The author, personified as a wedding table, attends such a ceremony involving two self-proclaimed "liberals." Despite their supposed rejection of tradition and superstition, the couple awkwardly mimics Western wedding customs (organ music, slow walk, English terms, rings, pastor's sermon) and submits meekly to religious elements they previously opposed. The author finds the ceremony superficial, hypocritical, costly, and merely an imitation of foreign forms rather than a true break from restrictive tradition, concluding it's an "East-West joint ceremony" primarily accessible to the wealthy.

From a historical perspective, this article offers a fascinating contemporary critique of cultural change and Westernization in colonial Korea. Historians might find interesting the detailed descriptions of both old and new wedding practices, the specific English loanwords entering the Korean vocabulary related to weddings, the tension between adopting Western "liberalism" and adhering to social expectations, and the socioeconomic commentary linking the new style to wealth and potential hypocrisy. It vividly portrays the anxieties and contradictions accompanying rapid modernization under colonial rule.

自由結婚式場巡禮記
						웻딍테블生
一, 서론
결혼식장 순례기를 쓰기 전에 먼저 이 글을 쓰는 긔자인 본인을 소개하겟슴니다. 자기가 자기를 소개하는 것은 너무도 당돌하기 짝이 업슴니다만은 다른 사람이 렬심으로 소개를 해도 처음보는 사람에게 인사도 치를 줄 모르는 못난이 보다는 얼마나 똑똑함니까? 소개해주는 사람이 업다고 백년이면 백년 천년이면 천년을 그대로 주저 물러 안저서는 그것은 아무 것도 아니올시다. 그럼으로써 본인은 좀 당돌은 하지만 본인이 소개를 하겟슴니다. 또 자기가 자기를 소개하는 데에 유익한 점이 만히 잇스니 첫재는 다른 사람보다 소개를 맛침으로 할 터 둘재는 업는 자랑도 더 보태서 할 터 셋재는 결점은 할 수 잇는 대로 감추고 소개할 터이니 다른 사람이 자기를 소개하는 것보다 자기가 자기를 소개하는 것이 그 얼마나 유익함니까? 그는 그러코 그러면 다른 사람이 자기를 소개하는데 남분뎜을 말하지 안을 수 업스니 첫재로 소개자와 소개를 밧는 사람 사이애 새이가 은근히 좃지 못하면 말 한 마듸로도 은근히 골탕을 먹을까 보아서 걱정 둘재로는 처음 맛나는＜77＞ 사람을 자기에게 거짓말로 소개하고 나종에 놀림거리를 당할가 보아서도 걱정 셋재로는 소개자의 인격이 조치 못할 때에 상대자가 자기의 인격도 소개자와 가티 알가보아서 걱정이 되니 도모지 남이 소개하는 것이 조치 못함니다. 그럼으로 걱정이 만흔 본인은 소개자 업시 그냥 소개하려 함니다. 이것 너무 서론이 길어서 독자 여러분도 지리하시겟지만 이왕 말이 나왓스니 차근차근 하여보지요.

二, 소개
저의 일홈은 웻딍테불 Wadding table 이라고 일홈을 지엿슴니다. 이것도 누가 지여준 것이라든지 혹은 부모가 지여준 것이 아니올시다. 제가 당돌하게 일홈을 지여버렷슴니다. 조선 말로 군색하게 번역을 하면 혹 결혼식상(結婚式床)이라고 할는지요? 그쯤만 알고 게시면 대개 틀림은 업슬 것입니다. 굴리는 말을 모르시는 분이나 혹은 녯날 로인들게서 들으시면 「아! 한문 자가 멧 만자인데 일홈을 뎡당하게 짓지 아니하고 원 그것이 무슨 비러먹을 소리냐? 에라! 네까진 놈이 이야기를 하면 무엇을 똑똑히 하겟니? 나는 안이 드를테다』 하시겟지만 그게 그런 것이 안님니다. 성인도 종시 속을 하랫다구 너무 고집을 쓸 것도 안이겟지요. 더욱 이제 일홈은 그러케 해야만 할 것을 증명하기 위해서 한 가지 례를 들어보지오. 요사이 신문 기자들의 성명이 무엇인 줄 아심니까? 들으시면 놀나심니다. 공연히! 그러나 한다든 말이니깐 하지오. 여러분 신문 기자의 뒤를 좀 따라 다니여 보시오. 더욱이 연극장이나 뎐차에서 보세요. 신문 기자가 연극장에를 돈도 아니내도 그냥 쑥 드러감니다. 그러면 표 밧는 사람이 황망하게 『누구심니까?』하고 무르면 기자는 이상스럽게 눈을 뜨면서 『패스!』하면 그만임니다. 뎐차에서도 그냥 나릴 때에 운전수가 『표 내고 나리시오 하면 그역 『패스!』하면 고만임니다. 그의 일홈이 『패스』기 때문에 아모대라도 함부로 단임니다. 돈 안이 내고도 잘 단임니다. 돈을 내도 잘 안이 드리는 곳에를 패스는 그냥 막 드러감니다. 저도 결혼식장에를 그냥 쑥 드러갈 대 문직이가 『누구야?』하야 저는 『테불!』하면 아모리 미인의 경계만흔 결혼식에라도 무사이 통과가 됨니다. 좀 우락부락한 문직이는 공연히 사람 을＜78＞ 시달리느라고 『무슨 테불이야?』함니다. 그러나 그때 저는 『웻딍테불!』하면 제아모리 작란군이라도 정색을 하고 두 손으로 내몸을 번적 드러다가 그 어엽분 시악시 압헤다가 노아줌니다. 요사이 젊은 청춘남녀의 결혼식에는 모도가 영어로 일홈을 부름니다. 가령 말하면 쁘라이드(新婦) 쁘라이드그룸(新郞) 베일(面紗) 웻틩으링(結婚式契約指環) 후록코트(禮服) 무슨 모닝코트(禮服) 무슨 칼라 넥타이 ... ... 등. 참말도 만치요. 그곳에 조선 일홈이라구는 신부 신랑의 일홈뿐일까? 안이예요. 어느 때 드르면 신부신랑의 일홈도 듯기에 괴상한 일홈이 잇셔요. 무슨 『마리아』 『요섭』 『요한』 『메리』 『로-스』 『헤렌』 참말로 녯날 로인이 드르시면 『야 그것이 무슨 일홈이냐 일을케면 우리말로 하면 『개똥』이 『쇠똥이』 『뒷간』 『간난이』 하는 일홈이냐? 허허!』 하고 우스시겟지요.
그러니깐 제 일홈도 『웻딍테불』이라고 해야 그 성대한 결혼식에를 참예함니다그려. 그러타고 아모 것도 직업이 업스면 남의 속은 모르고 경찰서에서는 무직업자라고 주목을 하지요. 그래서 그러한 일홈을 지엿스니 그만큼만 아시고 용서하시고 들으서야 함니다.

三, 혈통소개(血統紹介)
이것 제 집안 소개만 넘우해서 안되엿슴니다마는 매산느 경솔이 할 것이 안이라 다 차근차근히 해야 함니다. 먼저는 제 얼굴부터 소개하지오. 저는 참 말슴하기 미안하고 황송함니다마는 얼굴 못나기로는 일즉이 명성이 놉지오.
저는 눈도 업고 코도 업고 얼굴도 업슴니다. 다만 잇는 것이란 등(背)하고 발(足) 넷(四個) 밧게 업슴니다. 그러나 그러케 업는 것 만큼 그만치 으뭉함니다. 그럼으로 남 보는 것도 다 보고 남 듯는 것도 다 듯고 또 눈치가 몹시 빠름니다. 원래는 모가지도 잇기는 하엿스나 결혼식에 나와서 축사하는 청년들 중에서 성미가 팽팽한 놈이 흥분이 되면 남이야 엇지든 주먹으로 내 목을 막 따림니다. 그 때면 참다 못해서 『쿵!』하고 악을 씀니다. 하도 모가지를 어더 마저서 그만 목은 업서지고 인제는 넙죽한 등만 남엇슴니다. 인제는 따리든지 말든지 제 손만 압핫지 내 등이 깨지지는 안을 것임니다. 아이쿠! 그러나 기는 놈에 나는 놈이 잇다고 또 내 등을＜79＞ 깨트릴 분이 잇슬는지도 모름니다.
원래 명문거족으로 선조때부터 부귀하고 다남하여서 선조도 만코 일가도 만코 형제도 만슴니다. 그러나 불행이 사람으로치면 고자 모양으로 생식 기능이 불완전하기 때문에 목수의 손과 연장이 들어야 가튼 후손이 생김니다.
선조는 여러분이 아시는 바와 가티 여러분이 조석으로 밥사발과 반찬 그릇을 놋코 잡수시는 소반일 것임니다. 좀더 올러가서 족보를 캐면 춘향이의 정부 리도령을 구박할 때 쓰든 개다리 소반일는지도 몰나요. 그리고 사촌이 만히 잇스니 교자상 책상 사무상 응졉실사 등이 만히 잇슴니다. 팔자들이야 다들 좃치오. 내 팔자는 남들이야 좃타고 하지만 나는 결혼식으로 불려단인다고 음식을 먹어보나 아기자기한 광경을 구경을 하나 참 한심하지요. 그런데도 참말로 불상한 내의 삼촌이 잇스니 그의 일홈은 안반임니다. 원래 얼굴이 넙적해서 녯날의 무지한 산아희들이 그곳에다가 떡을 노코 백근이나 되는 떡메로 냅다 갈겻스니 엇더케 되엿겟서요? 일순간에 고만 넙적만해 젓서요. 눈이다 무엇이고 얼굴이 다 무엇임니까?
이것 결혼식장에 갈 시간이 밧분데 수다해서 큰일 낫슴니다. 염려마삽쇼. 자! 이야기를 참말로 함니다.

四
『대체 결혼이라자는 텬지 음양지리로 이성이 결합하야 일가를 일우워 유자생녀하고 부귀번창하는 것을 목뎍하는 것이니라!』하는 것은 녯날 로인들의 말슴이고 『깁흔 밤에 외로히 쓸쓸하게 님의 얼굴을 그리면서 한숨만 바야흐로 자조 쉬일 때에 한 쟝에 편지를 펴서 보니 하엿스되 사랑아! 사랑아! 내 사랑아! 영원히 잇지 말자! 아아 세상은 쓸쓸함니다. 내 맘은 외로워요. 그러나 당신만 엽헤 잇다면 나는 너그러운 웃음을 웃음니다. 겨울 동산에는 꼿치 핌니다!』하다가 저이끼리 결혼을 하는 것은 아마 근대 청년남녀의 결혼 수속이겟지요.
그럼으로 부모의 엄격한 명령으로써 한번 보지도 못한 산아희와 게집 아해를 짝을 지여서 그 녀자는 첫 아들을 나흘 때가지 자기 남편을 바로 치여다 보지를 못하든 그러케 서투른 결혼식과 맘에만 마지면 어느 구퉁이에서 겻눈질 한번에 홀려서 그 후에는＜80＞ 둘이 맛나기만 하면 조화서 펄펄 뛰면서 엽헤 사람이 회가 동하도록 「키쓰」를 쭉쭉거리면서 놀다가 첫 아들은 고사하고 맛난지 삼사일만 되면 녀자는 그 산아회의 얼굴에 그의 어엽분 손구락 약혼 반지가 번적이는 손구락으로 상아대질을 분별업시 한담니다. 그러다가 결혼식장에 드러올 때는 대개 신부 배 속에는 어린애가 들어잇슴니다그려! 이것은 자유결혼의 대강이올시다.
그러니 말슴이여요! 사람이 다르고 처지가 다르고 생활이 다르고 사상이 다른 혼인이니 물론 결혼식이 다를 것은 필연적 사실임니다.

제가 자유 결혼식장에 맨 처음으로 참섭하기는 그것이 일천구백십구년 중춘이엿슴니다. 그래도 부모 덕분에 미국 류학을 가서 업을 마치고 도라올 때 수만리 본국에 계신 부모 형제의 열성이 헛된 데로 가지 안케스리 W, T라는 학위를 밧고 나와서 『웻딍테불』이란 바람에 신학문 조화하시는 젊은 미혼남녀에게 넘치는 총애를 바듬으로 신식 결혼식의 결혼상으로 임명이 되엿슴니다.
어느 날인지는 확실히 모르겟스나 엇재든 악가 말슴한 것이나 가티 중춘이 엿슴니다. 별안간에 내 등 우에는 흰보를 덥허놋코 그 위에는 가진 꼿나무를 꼬진 화병이 노힘니다. 『오냐! 이것이 조선의 현대식 결혼이로구나!』 하고 속으로 긴장이 되어서 결혼식을 기다리엿슴니다. 미국서 나온 후에 대개 내 사촌인 교자상에게서 조선의 원래의 결혼식을 들엇스니깐 좀 참작해서 할 것이나 미국서 본대로한면 참말로 자유스럽기도 하고 유탕하기도 하니까 우리 조선과는 대중을 할 수가 잇서야지요.
위선 내 등우에 흰 보자가 덥히고 향기나는 꼿이 노임니다. 교자상에게 들은 이야기로 보면 옛날 구식 혼인이면 그러케 생화(生花) 가튼 것은 쓰지도 안을뿐더러 상우에 논는 것이 만핫때요. 또한 상에도 암수가 잇서서 신랑상과 신부상이 잇담니다. 그래서 신랑상은 신부 집에서 신부상은 신랑 집에서 교자상의 허리가 지게꾼의 허리가 되도록 척 휘여저버린 담니다그려! 그래도 그 놈은 팔자가 조화서 이마는 음식 때가 올나서 반질반질하면서도 나만 보면 생글거리고 가진 이야기를 다 한담니다.
