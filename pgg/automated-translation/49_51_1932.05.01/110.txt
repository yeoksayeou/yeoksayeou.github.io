Article Title: 大京城 칠낭팔낭, 속는 사람이 「바보」 (The Many Pitfalls of Seoul, Those Who Get Fooled Are "Fools")
Author: 熊超 (Ung Cho)
Article Type: 잡저 (Miscellaneous Essay)

"They say Seoul is full of traps, so when country people come to Seoul, they crawl from Wangsimni onward." I don't know when or from whose mouth this saying came, but it is certainly meant to belittle and make fools of country people.

Wangsimni, as a neighboring area of Greater Seoul (大京城), is located not far from the Han River (漢江) to the south. It is famous as a key point of land and water transportation to the famous rice-producing areas of Gwangju (廣州), Yeoju (驪州), and Icheon (利川), as well as parts of Gangwon Province, and therefore a place where goods gather. Even today, with the railway network that serves as the main artery of all of Joseon concentrated in Gyeongseong, it still forms a bustling small town. It is located just a few miles straight from Gwanghuimun (光熙門), and to go from there to the end of Wangsimni is said to be ten ri (里), but in reality, it is definitely about five ri. In the meantime, even a single-track tram passes through, and cars and carriages come and go incessantly. In any case, it would not be wrong to say that it is one of the material supply points of Gyeongseong, directly and indirectly handling rice, vegetables, firewood, and various other goods.

However, how much do people fear Seoul that they say country people crawl on all fours from Wangsimni when they come to Seoul? This saying must have been made up by Seoul people and must have come from the superiority complex of Seoul people who boast about being good at everything. It is true that Gyeongseong is the capital of Joseon and a place that embraces hundreds of thousands of people, so people are likely to be more agile and knowledgeable due to the complex living environment compared to rural people. However, I cannot condone them rushing in as if they are good at everything and using outrageously disrespectful language toward our rural people. Even now, shouldn't the one thousand several hundred million rural people rise up and unite to launch an "Anti-Seoul People" demonstration? That's a huge ambition. Enough with the clumsy tricks—Seoul people are only smart in what they know; they are also fools and blockheads in what they don't know. Pure Seoul natives, especially those who have never felt the country breeze, don't even know where rice comes from or what the plant looks like (it's true). Yet, they sit in the warmest part of the room, use chamber pots, and gobble down white rice until their stomachs burst. ... Oops, this is going to cause a big fight between the two sides, so let's stop!

Regardless of whether they are Seoul people or country people, pitfalls (낭) exist wherever there are foolish people. What is a pitfall? It's a trap. If you don't look ahead and fall in by mistake, there may be a pitfall of ten thousand feet, or a pitfall of one or two feet. Accordingly, you may offer your life to King Yama, or you may break a leg or sprain a joint. These pitfalls will be more numerous in Gyeongseong, which is a complex human community. Among these frightening pitfalls, let's take out the particularly serious ones and dig up a few relatively humorous little pitfalls.

In front of a busy street store, a crowd of people is milling about. On the threshold, a gray-bearded old man sits cross-legged, shaking a bell. The advertisement written on the shutter: 店仕舞·大競賣 (Store Closing · Grand Auction).

Surrounded by tens of thousands of people, one person stands on the platform and opens an umbrella.

"How much!"

From the crowd, one person shouts, "Fifteen jeon (錢)!" The friend standing on the platform shouts at the top of his lungs, "Fifteen jeon, fifteen jeon, fifteen jeon, fiftee-n jeon—!"

From the crowd, another person, "Twenty jeon!"

"Twenty jeon, twenty jeon, twenty jeon, twentie-n jeon—!"

"Twenty-five jeon!"

"Twenty-five jeon, twenty-five jeon, twenty-five jeon, twenty-five jeon. ..."

The amount gradually increases. They competitively raise the price. As it goes up and up, if there is no one else calling out the amount, the umbrella is sold to the person who bid the most. They keenly call out numbers, watching each other's reactions.

It went up to fifty jeon. No one bids anymore. If an umbrella is fifty jeon, it's quite cheap, so if you have money, you should bid at least one jeon more before it's auctioned off. "Fifty-one jeon," he shouts urgently, and the person who bid fifty jeon says, "Fifty-three jeon."

"Fifty-five jeon."

The person who bid fifty-three jeon probably doesn't have enough money in their pocket, so they just watch helplessly with a disappointed look.

"It's a bargain!"

It was auctioned off for fifty-five jeon. He pays the money, picks up the umbrella, and comes out of the auction house happily, but the spring rain is falling steadily, urging the flowers to bloom.

"This is just right!" He says, opening the new umbrella triumphantly, but listen to this. Black ink drips onto his brand-new, pure white cotton double overcoat—it's all ruined!

Pitfall! Pitfall! <22>

"It's cheap and good food. Let's have a drink here; there are girls too."

He implies in advance that he doesn't have much money and goes to a naeoejujujeom (內外酒酒店), also known as a saekjuga (色酒家) or a bar with women.

At thirty jeon per round of drinks, he sings merrily, flirts with the girls, and offers and receives drinks until he gets quite drunk. Not only that, but he also commits rude acts to a woman he just met, until she coughs on his lap and is exhausted by his advances.

"Let's go now," says his friend, but he reluctantly gets up and says in a slurred voice, "How much is it?"

"It's thirty-one rounds in total."

"Huh? Thirty-one rounds?" Even in his drunken state, his heart sinks. "Have you ever seen such thieving women?"

"It's nine hwan (圜) and thirty jeon."

"Why thirty-one rounds? It's eighteen rounds."

"If you think about it, you'll know. How can a business cheat a customer if the customer doesn't allow it? Don't be like that; pay up."

A really big problem has occurred. Arguments come and go, and the voices gradually become louder. The owner comes out, and passersby gather to watch. The owner still says, "No, no."

"Try what you want."

When the excitement between the customer and the owner reaches its peak, three menacing young men appear. The atmosphere in the place becomes truly ominous and murderous. At the slightest provocation, fists will fly like rain, so he laughs it off and hands over nine won (圓) and thirty jeon, but as he leaves, he hears mocking laughter from the bar.

Poor man, he lost all his pocket money; how will this country bumpkin get home? Pitfall! Pitfall!

"Sir, where are you going? You just got off the train, right?"

"Yes, that's right."

"I got off this train too. It's my first time here, so I'm unfamiliar with the area. If you're going to a hotel, can I come with you?"

"Sure."

The two people who met by chance chat about this and that as they walk side by side. The person who spoke to him unexpectedly picked up something wrapped in paper from the street. When he opened it to see what it was, it was a gold hairpin, worth at least forty or fifty won (圓). Both of their eyes widened.

"Oh, what a windfall! Since I'm the one who found it, it's my item, but since we found it together, let's share it."

The country friend's mouth dropped open at the offer to share it. However, since he was offered to share it, he couldn't refuse. It would be easy to get caught if he recklessly took it to a pawn shop, and there was no one who would suddenly buy it even at a low price—in the end, after a big concession and compromise, the friend who spoke to him received ten won (圓) and handed it over to him. Since he got a forty or fifty won (圓) gold hairpin for ten won (圓), the country friend must have been very happy, thinking he would make a profit of twenty or thirty won (圓) even if he pawned it. However, how frustrating it is—the gold hairpin was not real but a fake, gold-plated hairpin. Pitfall! Pitfall!

"Find the marked piece of paper here! Bet one won (圓) and get two won (圓), bet two won (圓) and get four won (圓), bet four won (圓) and get eight won (圓)! As much as you want—as long as you find it, you get double!"

One person sits on the ground and shouts like this, surrounded by many people. There are ten pieces of paper (?) rolled into balls the size of cherry pits. One piece of paper with a mark written on it in ink that everyone can recognize is also rolled into a ball the size of a cherry pit and mixed in. If you find this, you get double the money you bet.

"Look!" he says, showing the marked piece of paper to everyone once and then rolling it again and dropping it among the many pieces. Anyone can easily find it. One person confidently comes forward and bets one won (圓), and sure enough, it is the marked piece of paper. With people rushing in and losing and winning, one friend stands quietly and watches, and suddenly, a gambling desire arises. Thinking that it is definitely that one, he puts down one won (圓). When he opens the paper ball, it is exactly the marked one. In less than a minute, this person makes money.

"Ah—I must have seen it wrong!" he says and bets a third time. He wins. The fourth, fifth, sixth time... He bets two won (圓) and five won (圓), and in the end, he empties his salary envelope for that day. Only then does he come to his senses and get angry.

"You damn swindler, let's go to the police station!"

As a last resort, when he rushes in, the guys who were gambling with him unexpectedly attack him with their fists. In this chaos, the guy with the money runs away, and the guys who were hitting him also disappear in an instant—this is what is called the "Maruichi" gang. Don't recklessly rush in when you are blinded by money. The marked piece of paper is always playing in that guy's fingers. Pitfall! Pitfall! One of the pitfalls where it is easy to lose money and suffer humiliation is the Maruichi pitfall. <23>

논차는 바람에 시골 친구 입이 떡 버러젓다. 그러나 실제에 논차니 논을 도리가 업다. 섯불리 전당포로 가저가다가는 탈나기 쉽고 또 갑작히 헐갑시라도 살 사람이 업고―결국은 크게 양보 타협이 된 후 말부친 친구가 일금 십원을 밧고 그 사람에게 넘겻가 四五十원짜리 금비녀를 십원 내고 어덧스니 전당포에 잡히더라도 二三十원 리익은 떼노은 당상이 다 시골 친구 오즉 조와 하엿스리요. 그러나 이런 답답한 노릇이 잇소 금비녀는 진짜가 아니라 갓자 도금 비녀 낭! 낭!

「자-표한 요것을 차자 내시요. 一원 걸 구하면 二원 二원 걸고 하면 四원 四원 걸고 하면 八원! 얼마던지―차자내기만 하면 곱시요.」
땅바닥에 주저 안저 이러케 외치는 한 사람을 여러 사람들이 둘러서 잇다. 앵두씨만하게 비벼 노은 열 개(?)의 조히 쪽 거기에 누구나 다 알도록 먹으로 표한 조희 하나를 역시 앵두씨만하게 비벼서 석거 놋는다. 이것을 차저내면 거튼 돈의 곱술 낸다는 것이라.
「자-봅쇼」하고 표한 조희를 한 번 여러 사람에게 보이고 다시 비벼서 여러개 가운대 떠러트렷다. 누구나 빤히 그것을 차질 수 잇다. 한 사람이 썩 나서서 일원 걸고 집어내는데 역낙 업시 표한 조희다. 이 사람 저 사람들이 달려들어 일득일실이 잇는 판에 한 친구가 가만히 서보고 잇노라니 갑작히 도박욕이 생긴다. 꼭 저것이다 생각하고 一원을 내거럿다. 조희 환약을 펴 보니 꼭 드러마젓다. 불과 一분이 못되여 이 사람은 돈一*********
「아- 내가 잘 못 본 게로군!」하고 세 번째 거럿다. 땃다. 네 번째 다섯 번째 여섯 번째. ... 二원도 걸고 五원도 걸고 하다가 마지막에는 그날 탄 월급 봉지를 축축 터러버리고 마럿다. 그제야 정신이 나고 상열이 되어서
「이놈 야마시ㅅ군이로구나 경찰서 가자.」
최후의 발악으로써 덤벼들 때 자기와가티 돈을 걸고 하든 놈들이 뜻박게 주먹 밧길질로 덤벼든다. 이통에 돈 가진 놈 다라나고 때리든 놈들도 금시에 부지거처―이것이 소위 「마루이찌」패라는 것 돈에 회가 동하야 섯불리 덤비지 말라. 표한 조희 쪽은 언제던지 그 놈의 손고락 사이에서 노는 것이다. 낭! 낭! 돈 일코 봉변하기 쉬운 낭의 한 낭이 마루이찌 낭이다.＜23＞
