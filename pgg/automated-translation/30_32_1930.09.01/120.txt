Pyeongyang... Pyeongyang Miscellany, Manila Dance
						Sieodim

That summer (Gyohae Year) made it difficult to see the sun. The sun would shine for two or three hours, then dark clouds would gather again, followed by thunder and pouring rain. Every other day, the Taedong River would rise by 5-6 cheok or even 10 cheok...

Our hearts, seeing the Taedong River as Pyeongyang's only beauty and its very life, were far from calm. Casting a single fishing line on the wide riverbed and waiting for a fish to bite might seem foolish to some, but for us, it was the most relaxing pleasure <49> and the most comforting pastime. However, the river's color changed due to the long rains, and all the young fish scattered – even if we cast our lines and waited all day, how could we possibly catch even one rotten "galmeri" fish? We blamed the heavens.

Even if the river's color had changed, it would have been fine. But if it didn't rain, we could at least go out to make eojuk (fish porridge) (instead of fishing)... We would look at the northern sky and sigh.

Then, on this day, for some reason, the thick dark clouds that had been spread out until morning gradually began to fade toward the western sky from around noon. And a faint sunlight quietly shimmered on the murky red Taedong River.

A few of us (H. K. Seoul R. Na) who had gathered at the club gazed at the Taedong River with almost dreamy expressions. What was happening? Had the heavens forgotten us, or had our spirits moved the heavens?

Steam rose, and a hazy cloud made a rainbow over the Taedong River, swaying gently.

"Let's go out," H said, standing up.

"Let's go out," we said, rising without another word. There was no need for us to say where we were going or what we were going to do. We all changed into our river clothes (江衣) and silently went to the house where we had left our maesaengi (small boats). After dividing into two maesaengi, we set out onto the river.

The Taedong River water during the monsoon season, which poured red soil from areas like Yangdŏk and Maengsan into the Yellow Sea (Hwanghae), was very red. And the current was more than twice as fast as usual. Struggling against the rushing Taedong River, we sweated profusely, battling the fast current as we rowed upstream toward the eojuk maker's house.

After an hour or so, we finally reached our destination. And, at the top of our lungs, we called out the eojuk maker from the maesaengi. The owner came running out, eyes wide. He just stared at us blankly, wondering how many crazy friends in this vast world would come out to make eojuk during this monsoon season.

We urged the owner on.

"Hey, hurry up and get ready."

"Yes? - Yes, yes."

He replied in a flustered manner and ran into the house to prepare all the ingredients for making eojuk, even bringing out our maesaengi. After loading it, we untied the maesaengi from there and crossed the red water again towards Panwŏl Island (Panwŏldo).

Normally, Panwŏl Island would have been so crowded that there wouldn't have been even enough space to stand *** <50>, but at this time, there was no one but us. Why would there be? No matter how many fools there are in Pyeongyang, there couldn't be any lunatics like us...

"There's no need to worry about losing our spot."

We were rather pleased that no one was there. It was because there was no worry about losing the best spot for making eojuk to someone else. We brought the maesaengi to the front of the familiar spot at the tip of the island where we always made eojuk. After unloading all the luggage onto the bank, we drew lots to determine who would be responsible for each task. The lot I drew was for catching the chicken.

At first, I was always reluctant and shuddered at wringing a chicken's neck, always leaving it to someone else, but now I was skilled enough to catch them with ease. Moreover, once when I came out to make eojuk with Doctor Mo and received instruction in dissection from him, I had even earned the title of O Pyŏngsa President in our club. Two chickens were killed in an instant. Afterward, they lay flat on their backs, repeatedly looking at the sky, which seemed to regret clearing up and was about to cloud over again.

"Oh, what's this?"

After a while, H, who was in charge of tending the fire, made this noise, and I jumped up and sat up to look. I saw that the Taedong River water had risen to the spot where we had set up the cauldron and was about to enter the hearth.

"It's high tide. The freshwater is also very high."

Knowing this much, we took the opportunity to scald the chicken feathers in the boiling water. Then we moved the cauldron to a higher place. H refilled the water and boiled it again, while the remaining three people plucked the chicken feathers, cut up the chicken, and neatly cleaned out the insides and feathers before putting them in the cauldron. After that, only H, who was tending the fire, had work to do; the other three people were temporarily free.

"Fireman, you're working hard."

"Ugh, just like O Pyŏngsa member."

"At least he's the president, not a member."

After exchanging these jokes, we left the rest to H and competed to skip the flat, smooth pebbles scattered there. Charchalcharl, the stone flew over the water like a swallow, playing with the water as it flew to the other side.

"Jjo jjo jjo jjo jjo jjo, look at that. Mine went sixteen times."

"I went over twenty times a while ago."

"Oh, don't lie. You can count up to four times, but how can you count more than ten times?"

"Hahahaha"

While we were making a fuss like this for a while, we heard H's shouting again from the other side. When I looked back, the river water had risen again to the spot where we had moved the cauldron <50>. We had no choice but to move the cauldron to an even higher place.

The Taedong River at twilight, as seen from Panwŏl Island, was beautiful. The sunset was burning red on Ch'ŏngnyubyŏk (Chongryubyok) standing tall across the river, and it was reflected in the swaying Taedong River, creating a painting. The small willow trees of Panwŏl Island swayed in front of our eyes.

Soon, the fish also simmered. We gathered again, shredded the fish, put it in the cauldron with the rice, and started making porridge while also starting to drink with the bones as anju (food served with alcohol). The alcohol was Pyeongyang soju.

Originally, I wasn't good at drinking, so I drank one cup while others drank three cups. But I got drunk just like everyone else. After finishing the alcohol, I suddenly fell over, and the world became hazy. Everything in front of my eyes was dizzy and funny. By then, the world had already sunk into darkness, and the lights were twinkling in Pyeongyang.

Although I was heavily drunk, I don't remember exactly, but I have a vague memory of moving the cauldron again at some point. However, we only believed that the tide was terribly high.

Oh-la oh-la oh-la
Wagyagyagya.

I heard all sorts of sounds in my drunken state. While hearing such sounds like wriggling, I fell asleep before I knew it, and woke up when my friends woke me up.

"Hey, the porridge is done. Let's eat."

"Porridge?"

We sat around the porridge cauldron. And, enjoying the taste of the handmade porridge, we ate deliciously.

"Now. Manila Dance" <52>

When we finished eating the porridge, R made this proposal.

"Okay."

We had no objections.

Manila Dance was the name we had given it. After we finished making and eating eojuk, we would always light a fire with the remaining firewood, and each of us would carry a piece of firewood around and dance around the fire. There was no special style of dance. Just like the natives of the South Seas that we saw in the newsreels, we would wiggle and dance around the fire. Our faces, blackened by the smoke, were even more like South Sea natives. We jokingly named this South Seas dance the Manila Dance.

We lit a fire with the remaining firewood, broke off willow branches one by one, put them on our shoulders, and began to wiggle and dance around the fire. As if forgetting all the worries of the world, we were immersed in great joy as we danced. And we sang.

If the eojuk maker hadn't worried about our whereabouts and deliberately come to find us, we might have danced and circled there for hours. The eojuk maker was so worried that he finally rowed across in the maesaengi to find us. And we learned from the eojuk maker for the first time that the Taedong River was rising every moment and had already entered a dangerous state.

Although we, who were skilled with the maesaengi, rowed with all our might, our boat was pushed down the Taedong River instead of going straight across, and we barely managed to reach the bank.

The evening of the next day, the Taedong River water flooded the streets near the Ayasdari (이아ㅅ다리) to more than a person's height. And the day after that, the Taedong River water that had flooded the streets turned the entire new city area into a sea, and the water reached as far as the front of the train station and the Chahei Hospital.

This was the great flood of the Gyohae Year, the first time such a thing had been seen since the opening of Pyeongyang. (End)

「그래라」
우리는 異議가 엇섯다.
마닐라 舞踊이라 하는 것은, 우리가 지은 이름이엇섯다. 우리는 어죽을 다 쑤어 먹은 뒤에는 언 œ던지 아직 남아 잇는 나무깹이에 불을 질르고 제각기 나무깹이 하나씩을 둘러 메인 뒤에 그 불의 주위를 춤추며 도라 가는 것이엇섯다. 춤이라야 별다른 방식이 업섯다. 活動寫眞에서 보든 바 南洋 土人들이 추는 것과 마츤가지로 웃줄 웃줄하며 불의 주의를 도라 다니는 것 뿐이엇섯다. 우리들의 얼굴이 식검어케 탄 것은 더욱 南洋土人과 가텃다. 이 南洋人의 춤을 우리는 부러 마닐라 舞踊이라고 명명하는 것이엇섯다.
우리들은 남겨 노흔 나무ㅅ단에 불을 질르고 버드나무를 한가지씩 꺽거서 억게에 메인 뒤에 웃줄 웃줄 불의 주위를 돌기 시작하엿다. 세상의 온갓 근심을 니즌 듯이 커다란 悅樂에 잠겨서 우리는 춤을 추엇다. 그러고 노래를 불럿다.
우리의 행방을 근심하여 어죽집 주인이 부러 차저와 주지 안엇드라면 우리는 몃 시간은 거기서 춤추며 돌앗슬넌지 모를 것이 엇섯다. 어죽집 주인은 근심하다 근심하다 못하여 만츰내 우리를 차즈려 매생이를 저어 가지고 건너 온 것이엇섯다. 그러고 우리는 그 어죽 주인에게 처음으로 大同江은 지금 刻刻으로 增水가 되는 중이며, 발서 위험한 상태에 드러 갓다는 것을 아럿다.
매생이에 능한 우리가 전력을 다하여 저엇지만, 우리의 배는 고추 건너가기는커녕 대동강 아래까지 밀려나려 와서야 겨우 언덕에 대엿다.

이튼날 저녁때는 이아ㅅ다리 근처의 시가는 사람의 한 길이 넘도록 대동강물이 몰려 드럿다. 그러고 또 그 이튼날은 시가로 넘처들은 大´ž江 물이 신시가 일대를 전부 바다로 화하게 하고 그 물은 멀니 정거장 압과 자헤의원 아페까지 니르럿다.

이것이 평양 개벽 이후 처음 보는 일이라는 癸亥年의 큰물이 엇섯다. (끗)
