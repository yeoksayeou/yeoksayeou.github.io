기사제목	殺人的 物價暴落의 原因은 무엇인가? 萬人必讀의 當面한 經濟常識 第一課!! (What is the cause of the deadly price crash? An essential first lesson in economic common sense for everyone!!)
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Conversation · Symposium)

What is the cause of the deadly price crash? An essential first lesson in economic common sense for everyone!!
Everyone should know today's question!! Why are the prices of goods falling?

A straw bag of rice is 6 won. It's incredibly cheap. A mal of rice is 60 jeon. It's incredibly cheap. A ja of artificial silk is 10 jeon. It's incredibly cheap. A geun of beef is 35 jeon, a ja of Gwangdang cotton is 10 jeon, a durum of dried smelt is 35 jeon, a gold watch is 6 won, a mal of silkworm cocoons is 1 won 80 jeon... It's incredibly cheap. Everything is cheap. Except for a few things that haven't gone down in price, everything else has. Everything is cheap. It's so cheap, but people are too poor to buy it as they please, so the world is strange. "Even when an old copper coin (muk-eun t'ong) was 7 bu 5 rin per mal, there was not enough to sell, and a poor, flighty old woman starved to death" is a song.
Korean people aren't that flighty, but in this era when a dae of rice is only 1 won 60 jeon, they can't afford to buy it and are starving, so the world is truly chaotic.
But anyway, why are the prices of goods falling? Is it a trick of fate? Just looking at rice, it used to be 12 won per bag, sometimes even 20 won, but now it's only 6 won... It's not like rice is only half-yielding these days... The goods are as good as ever, but the price is less than half of what it used to be, so is that trickery or what! Prices are falling... So, if I'm worried about how incredibly cheap things are, some people might scold me, saying, "Hmph, worrying is a luxury! If prices are cheap, just buy a lot... Looking at it now, a mal of rice is 60 jeon, which is less than half of the previous 1 won 20 jeon, right? So you can buy two mal for the price of one before, so why are you complaining so much that you're going bald with worry!"
But wait a moment. Let's say that person who said that is a teacher at an ordinary school. The salaries of ordinary school teachers are paid with the tuition fees paid by the students of that school. And the students of ordinary schools are mostly children of farmers. But because the price of rice is so low, farmers can't pay their tuition fees regularly. So, the teachers can't receive their salaries regularly either. How is it, teacher! Can you buy rice even if you don't get paid?
That's not all. Now, let's say that person is a shop owner. Since they're a shop owner, they've been buying rice to eat. But the goods they sell have also become cheaper. Even though they're cheaper, they're selling even less. That's because farmers, laborers, and other poor people have become several times poorer than before. So, even if the price of rice is very cheap, the shop owner has to eat two meals instead of three, and if they still can't make ends meet, they have to put a piece of paper on the shop window saying, "This store is closing down and selling everything cheap," and shake the bell.
So, it seems that all Koreans are sick in their lives. Since they're sick, we need to find out how that sickness started. To find out about this sickness, the reporter went to visit Mr. Seo Chun of the Dong-A Ilbo newspaper for a day. And we had the following discussion about the sickness. -In general, why are prices falling...?

Reporter: Why are goods so cheap?
Seo: In a nutshell, the prices of goods fall and rise according to the law of supply and demand (需要供給).
Reporter: Please don't use such difficult words and explain it more easily. I don't even understand the law of supply and demand.
Seo: Then that's a problem. To explain the words one by one...
Reporter: Still, you have to make it understandable for everyone who reads it.
Seo: Then I'll try to do that as much as possible... Demand (需要) is "what is used," so it's good to think of it as "buying" goods. And supply (供給) is putting out goods, so it's good to think of it as "selling" them.
Reporter: Yes, then what is that law?
Seo: Wherever you go, this world is about buying and selling goods. If you go to a fabric store, you'll see people buying and selling Gwangdang cotton, silk, and artificial silk. If you go to a rice store, you'll see people buying and selling rice. If you go to a rubber shoe store, you'll see rubber shoes, and if you go to a bookstore, you'll see people buying and selling books.
Reporter: If you go to a general store, you'll see general goods...
Seo: That's right. But in the process of buying and selling like this, when the supply is greater than the demand, the price of goods becomes cheaper...
Reporter: Huh? Huh? When the supply, that is, the amount being sold, is greater than the demand, that is, the amount being bought?
Seo: That's right, that's right.
Reporter: The price of that good becomes cheaper.
Seo: Right... And conversely, when the demand, that is, the amount being bought, is greater than the supply, that is, the amount being sold, the price of that good becomes more expensive... So, you can think of it as goods becoming cheaper when they're plentiful and more expensive when they're rare.
Reporter: Yes, yes, I understand. So, the price of goods in Korea has fallen because the amount being sold is greater than the amount being bought. Is that right?
Seo: That's right. But there are three things to consider again.
Reporter: How so?
Seo: If the supply is greater than the demand, that is, if the amount being sold is greater than the amount being bought, the price of that good becomes cheaper, right? You understand that, right? But the idea that goods are plentiful or scarce in the world isn't just a floating statement. Saying that they are plentiful means that they are more plentiful compared to the previous amount. Saying that they are scarce also means that they are scarcer compared to the previous amount. For example, saying that the Korean population has swelled to 23 million (that it is more plentiful) means that it is more plentiful compared to the previous 20 million. Saying that there are less than three nyang in this three-nyang bag means that it is less than when the bag was full before.
Reporter: Yes, I understand.
Seo: So, it's the same with the goods being bought and sold. For example, if the Korean rice harvest was abundant last year and increased to 19 million seok, that means it was more plentiful compared to the previous harvest of 14 million seok, right?
Reporter: Yes, yes.
Seo: So, until then, Korean people used to eat and sell to Japan about 14 million seok of rice every year, but last year, the harvest was a whopping 19 million seok, an increase of 5 million seok, so the supply of rice became greater than the demand, that is, the amount of rice being sold was greater than the amount being bought, so the price of Korean rice became so cheap.
Reporter: Haha, that's true.
Seo: Wait a minute. There's more. What I said above is that the price of rice fell because the supply was greater than the demand, as the harvest increased by 5 million seok, from 14 million seok to 19 million seok. But another reason is that even if the supply, that is, the amount being sold, remains the same, if the demand, that is, the amount being bought, decreases, the supply will still be greater than the demand, right? In other words, let's say there are twenty used coats in a secondhand shop both last year and this year - the supply, that is, the amount being sold, is the same - but if the number of people who wanted to buy used coats last year was twenty, but this year it's only fifteen, then you can say that the supply is greater than the demand. So, the price of those used coats will be cheaper this year. But there's more. Let's say that both of the above two things happened. That is, what if the supply increased dramatically and the demand decreased dramatically?
Reporter: Huh?
Seo: Don't you know? Now, let's talk about the coats in the secondhand shop I mentioned earlier. If there were 20 coats last year and now there are 30?
Reporter: The supply has increased.
Seo: But if the number of people who want to buy them was 20 last year and now it's 15?
Reporter: The demand has decreased.
Seo: See? The price falls even if the supply increases and the demand stays the same, and the price falls even if the supply stays the same and the demand decreases, so if the supply increases and the demand decreases at the same time, wouldn't the price of goods fall dramatically?
Reporter: Yes, yes, I understand.
Seo: Let's look at it with Korean rice. Last year, there was a bumper crop in Joseon, and because of something called the Rice Production Increase Plan (産米增殖計劃), the rice harvest, which used to be 14 million seok, increased to 19 million seok, an increase of 5 million seok, right? So the supply increased a lot, right?
Reporter: That's right.
Seo: But who has been buying Korean rice until now?... Who was the person demanding it?... It's Japan (日本). Japan has been buying Korean rice. But last year, there was also a bumper crop in Japan. Because of the bumper crop, they didn't buy as much Korean rice as before, so in other words, the demand for Korean rice decreased, right? So wouldn't Korean rice fall even further?
Reporter: Yes, yes, I understand enough.
Seo: But I used rice as an example, but let's look at something like rubber shoes. Even if the supply of rubber shoes is the same as before, the Koreans who wear rubber shoes have become very poor for various reasons. So, there are many cases where they want to buy them but can't. That is, the demand for rubber shoes has decreased. So, the price of rubber shoes has become so cheap.
Reporter: Yes, yes, I understand. But wait a minute. Then what about this? Even if people are fighting to buy cigarettes at the tobacco store, they still charge 5 jeon per pack, and even if there isn't a single person all day, they still charge 5 jeon per pack, no matter what. Why is that?
Seo: Well, that's what's called a monopoly. Because of the government's monopoly system, they disregard the law of supply and demand and the price doesn't change. I'll tell you about this monopoly next time.
Reporter: Thank you. But rather than that, next time, please tell me "Why are Koreans so poor?"
Seo: I will.
