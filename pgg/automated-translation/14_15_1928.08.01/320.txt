If Sages Don't Stop, Great Thieves Won't Cease

"If sages don't stop, great thieves won't cease." This is a famous saying by Lao Tzu of China, meaning that the world was originally meant to be a place where everyone lived equally well, but because sages created systems and institutions, people tear each other apart over them, and eventually, great thieves arise who exploit these systems, slaughter people, and rise to the top. (I don't know if this interpretation is correct, but I want to tell a story related to my time at a certain newspaper company.) At the time, Mr. K, who later became an advocate, was in the editorial team of a certain newspaper company, and Mr. C was the chief editor in charge of controlling all the writers. Mr. K, borrowing from Lao Tzu's words, drafted a scathing editorial to be published in the newspaper's editorial section. It was even put in the editorial section, but Mr. C, upon seeing the final proof (the last proofread before printing), said, "We cannot publish this editorial as it is. First, it has a destructive meaning for traditional morality, which will cause public controversy and greatly affect the newspaper. Second, it is a nihilistic idea that does not touch on reason." He opposed its printing and distribution. Of course, it is common for opinion differences to arise between editorial team reporters at any newspaper, and they usually don't back down from their opinions. Moreover, Mr. C, the chief editor of the newspaper, and Mr. K were graduates of the same university, with similar academic abilities. Mr. K was also six years older than Mr. C. Mr. C became the chief editor due to personal connections, but Mr. K thought, "Am I really inferior to C?" This slight sense of resistance was always expressed, sometimes genuinely, sometimes sarcastically, and sometimes jokingly. However, when Mr. C dared to cut his editorial, Mr. K, who was usually resentful of being suppressed by Mr. C, became furious and refused to comply under any circumstances, leading to a heated argument between the two. Mr. C, with his small eyes blinking, said coldly, "Let's delete it. Do you agree?" Mr. K, his whole body trembling, the flesh on his cheeks twitching, and eventually tears streaming down, said, "How can you cut such a meaningful article – only someone like you would misunderstand it!" and even cried. At this time, the atmosphere between the two was truly intense. Other staff members present did not know what to say and remained silent. Then, the managing editor, Mr. R, with his eyes wide open, said, "If that's the case, why not just leave the editorial section blank and send it out?" And so, the editorial section was left blank that day. Readers probably thought that an editorial that violated public order had been cut due to interference from the Governor-General. <138>

The Great Secret Within Ten-odd Lengths, Two Thousand Miles for Ten Jeon

Now, I'll tell you about a great secret that is deeper than ten lengths! What is this secret that is deeper than ten lengths? The proverb says, "You can know the depth of ten lengths of water, but you cannot know the heart of one length of a person." So, is a person's heart deeper than ten lengths of water? Therefore, I will talk about the depth of the writer's heart. You might think that I am about to reveal some secret intention hidden deep within me, but it is actually a story about my empty stomach. Years ago, I traveled to Japan, and as a student traveler, I couldn't afford much for travel expenses. I barely managed to secure enough money to get from Tokyo to Osaka. My plan was to borrow more money from a friend in Osaka to get back to Korea. However, contrary to my expectations, I couldn't meet my friend in Osaka. I patted my pockets and found that I had ten jeon left after buying a third-class ticket and ferry fare to Pusan. So, I had no choice but to buy a ticket to Shimonoseki. A Japanese woman, whom I had met in Osaka, also happened to be traveling to Pusan. We got on the train together in Osaka in the morning. She had a young girl with her and seemed to have plenty of money. Around lunchtime, she smiled and said, "Let's go to the dining car for lunch. Come with us." I thought, "! ?" I was in trouble. I couldn't possibly have a young woman buy me a meal, and I certainly couldn't just sit there and expect her to pay. Hesitantly, I said, "Dōmo harano guai ga warui node..." (Well, my stomach isn't feeling well...). The woman said, "Oh, is that so? You should be careful..." and went to eat in the dining car. In the evening, before boarding the ship in Shimonoseki, the woman said, "Why don't you have dinner at the station restaurant before boarding? Won't you come with me?" My stomach had been growling all day, but I grimaced and said, "...My stomach still isn't feeling well..." In fact, my stomach was so empty that it was hurting, so it was true that my stomach wasn't feeling well.
I boarded the ship and spent the night clutching my empty stomach. As we approached Pusan in the morning, the sun rose over the sea, and soon a cabin boy came to serve breakfast. The woman kindly asked, "Your stomach still isn't feeling well enough to eat breakfast?" I was so hungry that I could barely speak, but thinking about the ten jeon I had left, I said, "My stomach isn't feeling well, so I can't eat breakfast. I'll just have a cup of coffee." I kept up this pretense of being Westernized. As soon as the ship docked in Pusan, I went to my friend's house and got some food. So, I traveled two thousand miles for ten jeon. Wouldn't you say this is the most painful and distressing secret I have? <139>

The Secrets of Newspaper Companies Hidden in Openness

Since newspaper companies are institutions that openly report facts to the public, one might think that there are few secrets there. However, just as the sun has sunspots and rain is hidden in white clouds, there are few places as full of secrets as newspaper companies. The business bureau has secrets about business, the editorial bureau has secrets about editing, and the president and staff have their own secrets. It is impossible to list all the numerous secrets, and even if I tried, there would be many things I would hesitate to say. So, I will mention only a few prominent ones. First, concerning the business bureau, the biggest secret is the circulation of the newspaper. For example, if 10,000 copies are sold, they will invariably exaggerate and say it's tens of thousands of copies. This is because it greatly affects advertising sales and other business matters. Asking a newspaper or magazine salesman about circulation is considered the questioner's fault. Second, <140> the secrets about advertising rates. Originally, the rate regulations are fixed, but in reality, they vary depending on regular customers and personal relationships, and the discounts are, of course, kept absolutely secret. Similarly, the deposit for branch offices is also similar. Moreover, it is a matter of course that there are particularly many secrets in the case of newspaper companies in Korea, where business is difficult like our Korean newspaper companies. In the countryside, they secretly negotiate to catch even one rich man who comes along. If a wealthy person has a dispute, they take advantage of the situation to try to get some money from that wealthy person, engaging in various shameful activities. I am too embarrassed to go into detail and will only touch on the periphery. Next, the editorial bureau has even more secrets than the business bureau. Try visiting any newspaper company. The business bureau doesn't have signs like "Non-Staff Members Prohibited" or "Restricted Access Except for Bureau Members," but the editorial bureau often does, which gives you an idea. The editorial policy of secrets varies from company to company, so it's difficult to generalize, and I won't say much about it here. However, concerning articles, what we call foreign news is actually mostly "kirinuki" (clippings) from foreign newspapers, though uninformed readers believe that telegrams and communications are sent directly from abroad. Therefore, there is a saying among newspaper reporters: "The one who does kirinuki well is a first-class reporter." However, what can we do in our situation, where we don't have the resources to send special correspondents abroad and rely on borrowed foreign newsletters to learn about foreign affairs? Nevertheless, that is also a secret. And when we host a conference, solicit entries for a contest, or raise relief funds, it is kept absolutely secret until the announcement because of competition with other companies. Even after the announcement, there are many things that are openly kept secret. For example, if a company holds a popularity poll and finds it difficult to announce the results due to circumstances, they make some excuse and quietly drop it. Regarding money and goods, they nominally and publicly state the income and expenses so that there is no difference in the balance, but who can know the detailed secrets? Also, if another newspaper or magazine attacks a certain fact, one newspaper watches for an opportunity to publish a vague article, places someone in the middle to pretend to sympathize with the attacked weaker party, and makes special demands. Years ago, when the press and youth organizations were vehemently attacking the so-called P'ot'ien Kyo, a key executive of a certain company secretly swallowed a large sum of 20,000 won, and fearing indigestion, he even disinfected it in a salt container before eating it. When it comes to secrets, these are all big secrets, but if I talk too much about them, it would seem like I am exposing other people's sins, so I will stop here and conclude. <141>

How Rooming House Secrets Unfold

Even if the rooming house doesn't have a sign, it's located in S-dong, in a very secluded and quiet house. The owner is a middle-aged widow living alone. Because the house is so quiet and doesn't attract attention, many guests come to avoid noisy places, and male and female students come to practice romance secretly.
In reality, students from the countryside who are supposedly lodging at their relatives' houses rent rooms here, telling their families that they go to their friends' houses every night to study together. Instead, they secretly meet their female student sweethearts at this place.
At the XX rooming house in K-dong, whenever a lodger comes, the students, who already like to gossip, gather and make a fuss. The reason is that whenever a letter with a flower seal comes for a pretty student, the sender's name is always written with the initials "Y.S.," so mischievous students tease her about it.
Also in K-dong, there is a rooming house where they reuse the leftover side dishes and conserve water by drawing well water instead of using tap water. Although the male students complain, it is the most prosperous rooming house in the neighborhood. The reason is that a pretty student from the XX Girls' High School in the neighborhood passes by in the morning and evening, and the male students want to see her. In H-dong, outside Seodaemun, a certain rooming house has many holes in the sliding paper door. It turns out that they house female students inside and male students outside, and when the female students come and go, the male students compete to peek through the holes.
In a lodging house/rooming house in C-dong, a certain reporter from XX Company eats meals with a female student with a traditional hairstyle who attends XX Girls' High School. They act like a married couple, always together day and night, but they are not actually married. However, they are so close that rumors are spreading that the female student is pregnant.
At a rooming house in S-dong near Mt. Pukak, only female students are housed. The landlady says that if you collect all the letters from unknown male students that arrive in a month, it would be a bundle. She laughs, unaware that the students in the neighborhood are trying to flirt with the female students by sending them "love letters."
At a rooming house in H-dong, a pretty female student often visits a male student. <142> It seems that they have agreed to call each other "oppa" (older brother) and "dongsaeng" (younger sibling), but they are not actually related. This kind of situation is common in any rooming house, so there is no need to mention it. Also, at a certain rooming house (a salt shop) in S-dong, a male student who graduated from middle school in the countryside and came to Seoul without a job is somehow close to a female student. The female student pretends to go to school, packs a lunch in her bag, and comes to the rooming house. When no one else is around, they have quiet, enjoyable conversations that last past lunchtime. At night, they whisper sweet nothings among the pine trees and rocks on the hill behind the house when the moon is bright.
A male student at a rooming house along the street in P-dong noticed a pretty student from XX Girls' School passing by every day. Coincidentally, they go to the same school, so he waited for her every morning and evening, rode the streetcar together, and eventually found out where she lived. He constantly wrote her love letters, but she ignored him. He was consumed by unrequited love. In another rooming house in the same neighborhood in P-dong, there are both male and female students. The female student started writing letters first, sending strange, bizarre writings, which annoyed the male student.
In any case, rooming houses are exclusive stages for male and female students. There may be admirable students who are solely focused on their studies, regardless of what's happening in the world, whether it's a deep winter night or a very hot summer day. However, it is unacceptable that young students who are just beginning their education engage in inappropriate romance and secretly make rooming houses thrive.
How many sins are committed in secret, and how many men and women stumble into the abyss of destruction? If they are to become buds, flowers, and fruits, the rooming house operators themselves must conduct their business ethically, instead of filling their bellies by facilitating sin.
There is an old saying: "A virtuous person chooses where to live." Has even truth changed now? Students themselves should be careful, and school authorities and parents need to monitor these issues. <143>

Secrets of Auction Houses That Deceive People with Scraps

Whether they are from the countryside or Seoul, people knowingly get deceived by items from auction houses. They don't realize they are being deceived because they buy things cheaply. In fact, the most deceptive business is the auction house business. Unlike other stores that buy goods at reasonable prices from wholesalers, the places where auction houses buy their goods are strange. The "auction house" has a contact person who is familiar with large shops or wholesale stores. These large stores buy and sell dozens of bolts of ramie or other textiles. No matter how careful they are, they often have problems with moth damage or discoloration. They buy these damaged goods, cut off the moth-eaten parts, and pre-cut the undamaged parts into jacket fabric, skirt fabric, and pants fabric to sell. They usually sell discolored items at night, using three times as much electricity as ordinary stores. Besides these, they buy goods from various other places. To mention a few, they buy from people who are forced to sell everything or from people who buy stores cheaply and sell them at half price, ending up with leftover goods that they have no choice but to sell to auction houses. They also buy from pawnshops. Even if they sell these goods about 10% cheaper than others, they still make a large profit. All items at auction houses are like this. People who get deceived without knowing these secrets are pitiful, but their methods of attracting customers and deceiving people are even stranger. If you pass by an auction house, you'll see that there are no customers inside, but they are shouting out prices like 30 jeon for items that usually cost 2 won. Even the smartest people get deceived in these situations. They think it's so cheap that they enter the auction house. Once they enter, the auctioneer mumbles, "1 won 30 jeon! 1 won 30 jeon!" The person who was shouting 30 jeon suddenly raises the price to 1 won 30 jeon. If they had shouted 1 won 30 jeon from the beginning, no customers would have come. By shouting 30 jeon, they can attract customers to come in to watch, whether they sell the item or not. As customers come in, the auction house fills up. They then display the items and call out prices. Many of the customers present will make a bid to buy the items. However, the customers who are buying are not actually customers but are collaborators of the auction house. Auction houses, whether in the countryside or Seoul, hire 5 or 6 secret customers and pay them a monthly salary. You will definitely be deceived by these secret customers. They also hire a few old men with topknots <144> and a few tailor customers dressed in Western clothes. They also have people dressed as if they are running a large business. Since these people are buying items, unsuspecting customers think that the items are good and follow suit. They also have good techniques for selling items. They offer a bar of soap or a pencil as a prize to customers who bid even 5 jeon more. The secret customers also bid at market price, a price at which they would be willing to sell the item. For example, if they are willing to sell an item for 1 won 30 jeon, they will bid 1 won 30 jeon. But they won't sell it at that price and will encourage customers to bid higher, with someone standing nearby feigning innocence. This person is also a salaried employee. How much money is lost in a year by people who are deceived and buy items like this?
What will happen to the commercial credit of Koreans if things continue like this? The people who don't know these secrets are Korean, and the people who are deceiving them are also Korean.
There are more detailed secrets, but I can't write them all down due to limited space. I will conclude here.

Secrets of Street Vendors Selling Medicine, Hanging Up a Sheep's Head and Selling Dog Meat

There wouldn't be any special secrets in businesses where they shout and urge people to buy their goods on the street, but the fact that they sell items priced at 30 jeon for 10 jeon, where they buy those items, and what their profit is – this is probably their secret. They usually buy medicine from Hanseong Pharmacy (漢城藥局) at 1-chome, Seodaemun-machi, Kyŏngsŏng (京城). They advertise that they are selling things particularly cheaply, and that's how they sell. They claim that items priced at 30 jeon are actually priced at 10 jeon but are being discounted, and they sell them to various customers. In reality, they buy them for 4 or 5 jeon and sell them for 10 jeon. They say that their average daily income from these sales is 20-30 won. They also have side businesses in addition to this business. This is a business where they don't invest capital and get to eat for free. They don't do this alone but with four or five people. One person shouts various things to attract customers. They call passersby "in-laws" for no reason and tell them to come in. After attracting customers, that person calls another person and introduces them to the customers, saying, "This person is a special representative from our company, and they will give you a good gift." Then, they quietly slip away. Since 40 or 50 customers gather, <145> various strange people gather among them. Some may be so hungry that they are eyeing the wallets of the people standing next to them. Some may be running away from someone and looking in. They gather these various customers – full, hungry, those who are looking to survive, and those who are looking for entertainment – and after receiving money for the goods, they have another side business. It would be impossible for a normal person to know that secret unless they were a medicine vendor. What do they live for? The secret of those who scramble to live, glaring at each other, is far from the law, and among them are people who are scrambling to survive...... (10 lines omitted here - original text)
They usually gather near the lumberyard in the market or in Seoul. If someone looks like they have about 70 or 80 jeon, which is barely enough to sell a load of firewood, they pounce on them and reach into their pockets to take the money. This is one of their side businesses. What do they use this great profit for? They spend it in places like the entertainment districts (遊郭) of Shinmachi in Seoul. This is their daily life. This is probably their hobby. Who doesn't know these secrets, and who wants to know them!

Two Major Secrets of Chinese Restaurants

There are several secrets in Chinese restaurants, but mainly there are two: one is about opium, and the other is about women. Chinese people, who value money even more than their lives, especially those who run restaurants, somehow deceive the police and, on the surface, sell food but secretly sell opium. They also set up secret rooms for opium use or for prostitution and attract debauched men and women. This is, of course, a business tactic to sell more food.
Large Chinese restaurants usually have side rooms and back doors, which allow people to come and go secretly and engage in all sorts of secret activities. Therefore, they do not open the back door and side rooms to people who are not regular customers. They also collude with prostitutes near the restaurant and, depending on the customer's situation, call them in. They also collude with opium dealers and, if a regular customer wants to use opium, they buy and sell opium, rent out rooms for them to use it, and charge high prices, which in turn sells more food. They also have secret codes in each restaurant. For example, if a customer comes to use opium, they shout "daikeo (大客)" (big customer), and if someone comes to sleep with a prostitute, they shout "nikeo (二客)" (second customer). In Pukchon, places like OO-ru in AnO-dong and OO-won in KwanO-dong, <146> and OO-ru branch near Chongno and OO-won in Hwanggeumjeong were famous secret restaurants. Recently, it seems that they have new codes for calling in prostitutes. If they put their hand on their head, it means a girl with a modern hairstyle, and if they put their hand behind their head, it means a girl with a chignon. However, it varies from house to house and there are secret agreements between customers, so others do not know it well. Anyway, the biggest secrets of Chinese restaurants are the secrets about opium and women.

Secrets of Russian Peddlers Who Deceive People with Waste

There probably isn't anyone who considers themself smart and has some money who hasn't bought something from a "Russian" peddler. Those smart people buy things because they think the items are good because they are products of "Russia" or because the prices are cheap. Why don't they get deceived elsewhere but get deceived in those places? What secrets are behind the backs of those "Russians"? After the Russian Revolution seven or eight years ago, people who were expelled as part of the White faction came to Korea, hoping to rely on the dying people, and first came to Hoeryŏng (會寧) on the northern end.
Somehow, the issue of prohibiting landing was resolved, and after landing, they were worried about feeding themselves. They had no choice but to become servants who cooked side dishes in bars or clerks in Japanese stores. They tried to feed themselves in various ways. Eventually, they were sold for a piece of bread even in Seoul. Even after coming to Korea like this, they couldn't speak Korean but became good at Japanese. They sold what we know as "Russian" bread, manufactured by a man named Fujiwara (藤原) in Hasegawa-machi, Kyŏngsŏng, and a man named An. At that time, it was so cheap that there probably wasn't anyone who hadn't eaten it. But it wasn't cheap. It contained about 2/10 more baking soda than Chinese bread, and hotteok (호떡) usually has a lot of sugar to make it sweet, but instead of sugar, it contained about 1/10 of rock sugar. In fact, it was cheaper to make than hotteok, and it looked big, but it contained more baking soda than hotteok, which is harmful to people. Only later did people realize that it wasn't cheap and stopped buying it, and the business failed. Because of the bread problem, they became employed by Japanese silk stores or tailor shops and started peddling damaged textiles and old Western clothes. Koreans didn't know this and blindly bought them because they were "Russian" and cheap. When they tried this for the first time, the initial results were good. Therefore, other Russians even had problems because they were selling bad goods to Koreans by deceiving them. How many Koreans are still being deceived and losing money without knowing these secrets? Why aren't we paying attention to these White Russian peddlers who openly deceive people in broad daylight? <147>

聖人不止大盜不息

『聖人이 不止면 大盜는 不息이라』 이것은 유명한 중국의 老子의 말로서 이 세상에는 원래 모다 평등으로 잘 살게 되엇는데 聖人이라는 者들이 무슨 제도 문물이니 무엇이니 하는 것을 만들어 노키 때문에 세상 사람이 그것을 가지고 서로 찟코 까부르다가 畢竟 그러한 여러 가지 제도를 이용하여 가지고 계급이 생기어 서로 싸호고 이에 따라 모든 사람을 처죽이고 그 우에 올라서는 大盜가 생긴다는 말인가 한다. (解釋이 잘못되엇는지 모르겟스나 이에 대하야 역시 某 報社에 잇든 때 일로 한가지를 이약이 하려 한다) 某 報社 당시 論說班에는 그 후에 主義者된 K氏가 잇섯고 모든 필자를 統御하기 위하야는 C氏가 主筆로 잇섯다. 그런데 K씨는 前記 老子의 말을 비러다가 辛辣한 논설 一文을 草하야 某 報 社說欄에 기재하려 한 일이 잇섯다. 그래서 社說欄에 실어까지 노앗는데 이 大張(인쇄하기 前準 마치어 인쇄한 것으로 한번 마지막 보는 假刷)을 본 C씨는 『이런 논설을 사설란에 써서 그대로 내어보내서는 안 되겟다. 첫재 고유한 도덕에 대하야 파괴적 의미가 잇스니 이는 일반에게 만흔 物議를 이르키게 하야<138> 신문사에 큰 영향이 잇슬 것이요. 둘재 이유에 닷치 안는 虛無思想이라』고 인쇄 배포를 반대하엿다. 물론 어떠한 신문사에서든지 論說班 記者 間에는 의견이 서로 달라서 論戰이 이러나면 서로 자기 의견을 굽히지 안이하려고 하는 것이 상례인데 더욱 이때 某 報社의 主筆 C씨와 記者 K씨는 가튼 大學 出身으로 서로 學識도 씨름할 만콤 莫上莫下하고 또 記者 K씨의 연령은 C씨보다도 6년 長者이라 다만 情實 관계로 C씨가 主筆은 되엇스나 K씨 생각에는 『내가 아모려면 C에게 진단 말이냐 하는 가벼운 반항심이 혹은 정말로 혹은 諷刺로 혹은 戱談으로 항상 표현되엇섯다. 그런데 이날 C씨가 감히 자기가 쓴 논설을 깍겟다고 하매 K씨는 평소부터 C씨에게 압제 밧는 것이 忿한데다가 더구나 이런 말을 듯게 되매 理論은 何如間 絶對로 못하겟다고 버틔어 兩氏 간에 激論이 이러낫섯다. C씨는 그 적은 눈을 깜작 깜작하며 『삭제합시다. 동의하시요』하고 냉정하게 말하면 K씨는 全身이 치가 떨리는 듯이 뺌따귀의 살이 痙攣的으로 부루를 떨리며 나종에는 눈물까지 더벅더벅 떠럿트리며 『그런 글의 뜻을 웨-일반이 오해하여 너 가튼 것이나 오해를 하지-깍는다는 말은 절대로 못 듯겟다』하고 울기까지 하엿다. 이 때에 兩氏 간에는 실로 風雲이 왕래하엿다. 그래서 그 자리 섯든 다른 사원들도 어떠케 말을 하여야 올흘지 몰라서 잠잣코 섯는데 이때 눈 말똥말똥 뜨고 섯든 편집국장 R씨는 『이왕이면 사설란을 깍고 白紙로 내어 보내지-하엿다. 그리하여 그 날 사설은 백지로 내보내이엇다. 독자간에는 무슨 治安妨害의 사설을 썻다가 總督府 간섭으로 깍진 줄 알 것이다.

신문사는 일반 사실을 사회 민중에게 공개 보도하는 긔관이닛가 잠간 생각하면 그곳에는 별로 비밀이 업슬 것 갓다. 그러나 태양에도 黑點이 잇고 힌 구름 속에도 소낙비가 감추어 잇다고 실제에 보면 신문사처럼 비밀 만흔 곳은 업슬 것이다. 영업국(營業局)에는 영업에 관한 비밀이 잇고 편즙국(編輯局)에는 편즙에 관한 비밀이 잇스며 사장(社長)이하 사원에게는 그에 관한 비밀이 잇슬 것이다. 그 허다한 비밀을 일일히 다 말할 수도 업고 또 말을 하랴고 하야도 역시 거리끼는 일이 만흔 즉 현저한 것으로만 몃 가지 들어 말하랴 한다. 첫재에 영업국으로 말하면 제일의 비밀이 신문의 部數이니 가령 萬部가 나간다면 의례히 외누리를 하야 몃만 部라고 한다. 그것은 廣告 모집이던지 기타 영업에 관계가 크게 잇는 까닭이니 신문이나 잡지의 영업자에게 部數를 뭇는 것은 도로혀 뭇는 사람의 잘못이라고 할 것이다. 제2에는<140> 광고 료금에 관한 비밀이니 원래 료금의 規定이야 다 일정하지만은 실제에는 단골과 친분에 따러서 다르고 또 그 割引은 물론 절대 비밀에 붓친다. 그와 한가지로 支分局의 보증금도 또한 그와 비슷하다. 더군다나 우리 조선의 신문사와 가티 영업이 곤난한 경우에 잇서서는 특히 비밀이 만흘 것은 물론이다. 시골에서 부자가 한아와도 잡아드리랴고 비밀하게 교섭을 하고 엇던 부자의 누가 시비가 난다면 그 틈을 이용하야 그 놈의 부자의 돈을 좀 맛을 보랴고 비밀이 활동하야 여러 가지의 추태(醜態)를 부린다. 그것은 도려혀 창피하야 자서히 말치 안코 변죽만 잠간 울린다. 그 다음에 편즙국으로 말하면 영업국보다 비밀이 더욱 만타. 어느 신문사던지 訪問을 하야 보라. 영업국에는 非社員禁入이니 局員이외 출입금지니 하는 패를 붓치지 안엇지 만은 편즙국에는 흔히 그런 것을 붓친 것을 보와도 짐작할 수 잇다. 편즙의 비밀 政策은 社마다 다르닛가 일률로 말하기 어려울 뿐 안이라 여긔에 굿태여 말하지도 안커니와 記事편으로만 말한다면 소의 외국 정보라 하는 것은 모르는 독자들은 외국에서 일일이 전보나 통신이 오는 줄로만 알지 만은 실제에 보면 대개가 외국 신문의 『기리누끼』다. 그리하야 신문 기자 새에는 이러한 隱話가 잇다. 기리누끼 잘하는 사람이 일류 기자라고... 그러나 자기 社에서 외국에 특파원을 보낼 힘은 업고 간단하나마 남이 발행하는 通信誌를 빌어서 외국 사정을 알게 된 우리 처지에서야 엇지하랴. 그러치만 그것도 비밀인즉 한 비밀이다. 그리고 무슨 會의 主催나 무슨 懸賞 모집을 한다던지 무슨 救濟金을 모집한다 하면 다른 社와 경쟁이 붓는 까닭에 발표하기까지는 절대 비밀인 것은 물론이고 한번 발표한 뒤에도 그 속에 公然 비밀 될 일이 만타. 한 예를 들어 말하자면 엇던 社에서 인물 투표를 하엿다가 사정에 인하야 발표키가 어렵다면 그저 무슨 핑계던지 하야 가지고는 씩색하고 말고 金品에 대하야도 名義로는 公然하게 무엇에 수입이 얼마 무엇에 지출이 얼마 하야 收支가 相差가 업시 하지만은 자세한 비밀이야 누가 알 수 잇스랴. 또 엇던 사실에 대하야 다른 신문이나 잡지에서 공격을 하면 한 신문은 그 기회를 보와 우물 주물하게 기사를 내고는 사람을 중간에 노와서 공격을 당하는 약자에게 동정을 하는 척하고 특별한 요구를 한다. 年前에 각 언론계와 청년 단체에서 소위 普天敎를 한참 공격할 판에 엇던 社의 중요 간부는 이만 원이나 되는 大金을 넘적 생키엿는데 잘못하면 消化가 잘되지 안을 갑아 소금섬 속에다 소독까지 한 뒤에 먹지 안엇는가. 비밀로 말하면 이런 것이 다 큰 비밀이지만은 너무 말을 작고 하면 남의 죄악을 폭로하는 것 가트닛가 이만하고 擱筆한다.<141>

중국인의 큰 료리집에는 대개 골방과 뒷문이 잇스니 이것이 곳 남모르게 사람이 드나들며 별별 비밀 행동을 하게 하는 것이다. 그럼으로 여간 단골로 다니는 사람이 안이면 그 뒷문과 골방은 개방을 하지 안는다. 그리고 또 료리집 부근에 잇는 밀매음녀와는 대개가 결탁을 하야 두고 필요한 경우에는 손의 형편을 보와 불너다 주고 아편도 또한 엇던 밀매자와 결탁을 하야 두고 단골로 다니는 손이 먹겟다면 아편을 사도 주고 또 방도 빌녀 주어 먹게 하고 빗싼 갑도 바드며 그 까닭으로 료리도 만히 판다. 그런데 그자들은 또 료리집마다 암호(暗號)가 잇서서 가령 아편을 먹으로 오는 손이면 다이커(大客)하고 소리를 지르고 밀매음녀를 다리고 자러 오는 사람이면 니커(二客)라고 하고 소리를 지른다. 북촌으로도 안O동 OO루 관O동 OO원<146> 가튼 데와 종로 부근의 OO루 지뎜 황금뎡의 OO원 가튼 것은 비밀 료리집으로 유명한 곳이엿다. 그런데 이 청료리집의 비밀 특히 밀매음녀를 불너 오는 것도 요새이는 또 새 암호가 생긴 모양인데 머리 우에 손을 대면 틀에 머리 여자요 뒤에 손을 대면 쪽자 튼 여자이다. 그러나 그것도 집집마다 다르고 손마다 서로 밀약이 잇스닛가 남은 잘 알지 못하는 것이다. 엇지하엿던 청료리집의 비밀은 우에 말한 아편과 여자에 관한 비밀이 가장 큰 비밀이라 하겟다.

廢物로 사람을 속히는 露西亞行商人의 秘密

돈푼이나 잇섯서 똑똑하다고 하는 사람 치고 『러시야』 행상인의 물건을 사보지 못한 사람은 업슬 것이다. 그 사람의 물건이 『러시야』의 産物이기 때문에 조와서 사느니 물건 갑이 싸서 사느니 하고 사는 그 똑똑한 친구들은 왜- 다른 곳에는 속지 안코 그러한 곳에는 속는지 그 『러시야』 사람들의 등뒤엔 엇더한 비밀이 잇는 것을 왜 모르느냐? 七八년 前 러-시야 혁명이 잇슨 후 백파 게통으로 쫏긴 사람들은 조선에 와서 다 죽어 가는 사람에게 긔대여 볼가 하고 北쪽 끗 會寧으로 먼저 오게 되엿섯다.
그 당시 떠들던 上陸 금지 문제는 엇더케 우물쭈물 해결이 되고 그 후 上陸이 되자 또 배불릴 걱정으로 문뎨가 되여 엇절 수 업시 술집 가튼 곳에서 안주 굿는 下人이 된다. 日人의 상점 가튼 데서 점원이 된다. 이럭케 저럭케 배를 불려 본 것이다. 그리던 것이 내종에는 서울까지 빵 한 조각에 팔리게 되엿다. 이러케 되는 조선에 와서도 조선말은 못해도 일본말은 잘하게 되엿다. 그리하야 서울 長谷川町 藤原이란 사람하고 安氏란 이 하고 우리가 만히 알든 『러-시야』빵을 제조해 팔게 되엿다. 그것도 그 당시는 갑이 싸서 엇더한 사람 치고 안 먹어 본 사람은 업슬 것이다. 그러나 그것은 싼 것이 안이엿다. 支那빵에 늣는 소-다보다 더 약 10분의 2 가량은 더 느은 것이고 홋떡은 설탕이나 만히 느어 단맛이나 잇는데 그것은 설탕을 늣는 것이 안이라 비일사탕이란 것은 重양으로 말하면 약 一分쯤 뿌리는 것이다. 사실은 홋떡보다 더 원가는 저근 물건이요 보기에 크게만 보엿지 滋養分엔 홋떡보다 소다를 더 뿌려서 사람에게 害物이다. 이런 것은 나종에서야 비로소 싸지 안타는 것을 알고 사지를 안어 그여이 그 영업은 하지 못하고 그만 그 者도 害를 보게 된 것이다. 이럭케 되니 또 역시 빵 문뎨로 日人의 羅紗店이나 洋服店 고용이 되어 상한 직물과 지여논 지 오란 양복을 행상을 하게 된 것이다. 조선 사람들은 그런 것을 알지도 못하고 그저 露國 것이닛가 좃코 싸다고 하야 맹목뎍으로 작고 삿것다. 이럭케 첫 시험을 해본즉 처음 成績으로는 양호하엿다. 그래 가튼 러시야 사람들은 그런 좃치 못한 물건을 사 가지고 조선 사람에게 속이여 가며 팔게 한다고 문제까지 생기엿다. 이러한 비밀을 아즉까지도 알지 못하고 속아서 損을 보는 조선인이 몃이나 되는지 알 수가 업다. 백주에 公然하게 사람을 속히는 이 露國의 백파 행상을 엇지 注意치 안이하랴. <147>
