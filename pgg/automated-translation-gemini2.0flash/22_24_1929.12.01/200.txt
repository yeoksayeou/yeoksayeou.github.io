Year-End Joy and Sorrow Symphony
						豪蕩兒 (Hotang'a)
It is Seoul. And it is the last day of the year.

It is the salaried workers. And they have received their bonus envelopes.

Not even ten days. For a month or two, they have been hoping, waiting, wondering how much it will be? What will they use it for? They have received the bonus envelopes.

Seoul is bustling. And it has become noisy.

The automobile departments are eagerly polishing the cars, hoping to make a lot of money. And preparing to stay up all night. Department stores are all busily redecorating their display windows.

Year-End Grand Sale!! Grand Sale with Prizes!! It's a big deal.

Elegant women are getting dressed up--Restaurants and cafes are making all sorts of preparations--

Ah--Seoul, a frightening preparation as if Seoul will leave.

10%! 15%! 20%! 30%! 40%! 70%!!! With the stingy gifts of capitalists, Seoul is becoming abundantly full enough to seemingly take flight! Ah! Ah! Ah! Ah! Ah!

Collectors with briefcases, monthly-wage earners, debtors are greatly active. Mobilization! It is mobilization! It is their general mobilization! With bloodshot eyes, it is a general mobilization.

Ah--and so, the comedy and tragedy of our humans is being unfolded. Yes, it is being unfolded. From the old days, whenever it becomes the end of the year, whenever it becomes the end of the year, a commotion is unfolded.

I am going to weave a symphony like bibimbap. Yes, a symphony of the last day of the year--I am going to weave a symphony where tears and laughter overflow.

C--He is a bachelor.

He is one of the very young salaried men who graduated from college this spring and works at the M Company.

The end of the year has come to him as well. And he has already been dreaming of bonuses for ten days. 20%! Ah--no, that's too little! Then 30! It has to be 30! He has been making budget tables in his head.

The pay envelope came out. He was as happy as a hungry puppy getting a dried pollock head, and unable to bear his curiosity about how much it would be and the anxious feeling that his dream might be shattered, he hurriedly went down to the basement cafeteria and secretly opened the pay envelope. <41> Ah! He was surprised. It was more money than he had expected.

"Ah!" He exclaimed twice. It was a total of 275 won, which was 45%, in the envelope. And he had to revise his expected budget. He had been wanting to buy an Orthophonic phonograph for a long time, but his financial plan at the time could not accommodate it. But now it is possible, and the shortage can be made up by selling his current machine. He was overjoyed.

That afternoon, he went straight home. With the bonus as it was.

But that evening--he was too restless to just lie down in his boarding room.

8 PM--he was wandering in the dazzling lights of Chinogae and the throngs of men and women.

However, whenever he was hypnotized by Chinogae and stopped at "just this one," he thought, "Let's just do the calculations!" "Let's just save the money!"--Among the crowd of people enjoying happiness with year-end bonuses filling Chinogae, he was enduring, swallowing his emotions close to sadness. <42>

He could not forget F, whom he had decided to marry next spring. For her, he would buy a luxurious French-made handbag (handbag), and inside the lovely coin purse, he would put a ten-won bill--no, it might not be enough, so exactly three ten-won bills--and send it! As this thought arose in his heart, he was very happy. But in less than a minute, he thought, "Let's do that tomorrow!" That was because he wanted to completely revise his budget and make it systematic so that he would not sigh later.

And two hours passed.

C, intoxicated in the "Gagutei," came to the Choson Bank streetcar stop and was about to take the streetcar when--ah--he suddenly stiffened like a firewood bundle. His face changed into a dispirited face like a failing student looking at their grade report. His belief that "a man's suit has fifteen pockets" filled his head in a flash, even including the watch pocket on his pants, but the pay envelope was gone. Ah--his disappointment! The hopes he had been riding on until now! But he did not lose his reason. Inside the streetcar, his mind was more active than a detective's.

"Oh--that's right, the guy who was standing next to me when I bought the pigeon seemed strange!" "Ah--and the guy who was sitting in front of me at the bar seemed bored..."

His lips were parched, his head was throbbing, and his heart was pounding. The crowd filling Chinogae all seemed like thieves. Ah! Ah! Ah!

That evening, when he went back to his boarding house and took off his coat, and found the pay envelope again in his inner coat pocket, his joy--no, his laughter was bitter, painful, and sweet...

"But a man's suit has at least fifteen pockets!"

He shouted resolutely.

"Come here!"

The head of the usury association went out from dawn today (the last day of the year) to collect the money he had lent.

"Come here!"

He shouted a second time in a desolate alleyway, with a sound louder and fiercer than a bull's roar. And he made a sound like kicking down the gate.

It was understandable. Even after calling twice, the house was as silent as death, and he had only received excuses and no interest on the 60 won he had lent last October, so even for the sake of the association leader's face, it was impossible not to be angry. Right then. The sound of a woman crying, thinner than a spider web, tickled his ears from inside the house. And the paper door of the main room slid open.

"Who is it?" <44>

A woman's voice like rolling jade came out. He looked towards the source of the sound. Ah! There, a woman with disheveled hair in plain clothes was wiping away tears and looking at him. He was surprised. Wondering what was going on.

"Yes--I had an appointment to meet the master of the house today. About the 3,000 nyang (兩) he borrowed back then--yes! He said he would pay it back today."

His voice became very soft. That was because the woman was beautiful. Yes--her fair face, her clear eyes, her cute lips, her smooth hands, and her slender body, her loose hair--ah--she was a beauty.

Just then. The woman began to cry again and talked about how her husband did not take care of the household and only drank, and how the family was in dire straits. In that lovely voice--Yes. That woman's husband. He was a well-known drunkard in the neighborhood. He knew that well--The woman continued, saying that yesterday, he went out all day and came back drunk after midnight, and when he was writhing in pain saying his stomach hurt, she was so disgusted that she left him alone, and when she saw him this morning, he was dead. She told the story with absolutely no lies. Anyone who was not made of wood or stone would have listened to her and agreed, and could not help but sympathize. <45> After finishing her words, she turned her head towards the lower part of the room.

He also looked into the room beyond the sliding door, and in the dim room, there was a coffin. He said, "Yes--that's a pity. Then what about the funeral..."

Woman: "That's what I'm worried about. Especially since today is the last day of the year, and tomorrow is New Year's Day, and besides, I don't have any money, so I'm worried about everything."

Hearing those words, he lowered his head for a while, then opened his bag and took out ten ten-won bills and said,

"Hearing your situation, I feel very sorry for you. It's not much, but please accept this and use it to help with the funeral expenses." The woman was surprised and said,

Woman: "Thank you. But I already owe you so much, how can I accept this? Please stop."

He: "No--that's that, and this is this, isn't it? Please accept it."

Woman: "Please stop."

He: "Please take it."

Ah--there was such sentiment in the world. However, the drunken husband, who could not pay back his debt and had planned to play a trick to avoid it, was listening to the whole story from the coffin in the lower part of the room. <46>

He couldn't stand how his wife kept telling him to refuse what he should accept. He couldn't bear it any longer. So he opened the coffin lid and said,

"Honey, honey, take it. Take it."

This is not a joke, but there are many facts like this among us.

"What can I do if I don't have money? I'll die. Do as you please!" That was in foolish times. This pitiful friend, tormented by debt, took out some Intan and said,

"Honey, I knew this would happen, so I got some poison! This is goodbye forever! Do as you please!"

He shouted painfully, ate the Intan, and then died writhing. This is the so-called Intan suicide that happened in Kangwon-do 18 years ago.

A certain friend, burdened with debt, unable to pay it back, the end of the year approaching, and unable to escape, thought and thought and spread the news to the moneylender's house that his living father had died, didn't he? We who hear it just laugh it off, but how much sadness and year-end sorrow does it leave for those who suffer?

Year-End Party! It's a strange custom that started in recent years. <47> I have many funny stories and sad stories left behind by year-end parties, but I can't write any more because I'm busy with editing. But you all have probably experienced more of the comedy and tragedy of year-end parties than I have.

Now, debtors run away. They take trains and cars to remote rural areas and to relatives' homes to hide. Creditors are also sharp-eyed in that direction and ride in the same car as the fleeing debtor and get off at the same stop and say,

"What's going on?"

and pretend to be surprised and exchange greetings.

The debtor is actually receiving an embarrassing situation where it is difficult to move forward or backward! Ah!

Is that all? When it becomes the end of the year, gifts! or New Year's gifts! or presents! make lovers laugh and cry, and wring out tears, sighs, and laughter from lower-ranking employees. In order to please the so-called executives and superiors, they close their eyes and send 30%! 50%! of their meager living expenses, and no one knows how much that sorrow and bitterness makes their families cry.

They receive presents from their lovers and wonder, what is it? Jewels? Gold rings? Or a gold watch? A hairpin? They only imagine good things, and when a handkerchief comes out, they throw it away, cry and fuss, and stomp on it. Both the lover who sent it and the lover who received it feel sorrow and cry! The story is good, but since it's a symphony, let's just move on!

Long ago! It was during the Silla Dynasty.

There was a scholar named Paekkyŏl-先生 (Paekkyŏl S先生), who was called Paekkyŏl S先生 because he wore clothes patched with hundreds of patches. Even in his poor and virtuous home, the end of the year came. When the sound of pounding white rice cakes and injeolmi rice cakes resounded from every house in 長安萬戶! (Changan Manho) (a town), only our teacher's house was desolate. The unknowing children grabbed the teacher's collar and begged for rice cakes, but there was no gruel even for this morning. Thinking and thinking, the teacher came down to the yard, took out his wife's mortar, and began to pound the pestle, singing a song in a grave voice:

"Ehe-ra, push it, it's a mortar!"

It is said that this is the current pounding song that is passed down. Ah--what a priceless song? "Ehe-ra, push it, it's a mortar!"

How deeply felt a song to sing on the last day of the year?

Let's stop writing! There's no more time to write. It's become completely different from the title. What reason could there be since I turned the pen like a flash of lightning at the sudden order to write to fill a dead article? It would be a thousand blessings if you would press down and read it. <49>
