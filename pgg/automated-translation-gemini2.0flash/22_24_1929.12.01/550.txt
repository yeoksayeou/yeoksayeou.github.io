1929년 12월 01일
女人-追憶의더듬ㅅ길- (Woman - Tracing a Path of Memories)
김동인 (Kim Tong-in)
소설 (Fiction)

Throughout my thirty years of life, I cannot count the number of women I have met fleetingly on the street, on trains and streetcars, or at gatherings, women who appeared and disappeared like "people on the street" (路傍의 사람) without leaving any impression.

Among those countless women, there must be hundreds who, for a time, punctuated my life and emotions with a comma.

When I shake that multitude of women in a sieve, how many will remain without falling through?

Among those whom I considered quite ordinary at the time, there are some whose image still lingers in my mind even after several years. Among those from whom I shied away, even avoiding sitting next to them, there are some whom I now wish to meet again. Among those whom I resented for not appearing in my dreams, there are some whose faces I have now completely forgotten.

Among them, how many women truly stick with me and refuse to fade away? I will attempt to list them one by one. Mary, Nakajima Yoshie, Manzōji Akiko, Kim Ok-yeop, M Semimaru, Hwang Gyeong-ok, X (whose name I don't even know), No San-hong, Kim Baek-ok - a total of ten people. Among them, Hwang Gyeong-ok and Kim Baek-ok (one a *gisaeng* in Seoul and the other in Jinnampo) have already passed away to the peaceful afterlife (May God protect their young souls). Mary, Yoshie, Akiko, Semimaru, and X are people whose whereabouts, let alone their well-being, I do not know. Among the remaining three, I heard that Kim Ok-yeop has started her seventh(?) married life, M is said to be a good wife in a happy family and a good mother to several children, and No San-hong is said to be still working as a well-known *gisaeng* in Seoul.

1. Mary

It was the autumn of 1915. Kim Tong-in, a sixteen-year-old second-year student at Meiji Gakuin Middle School, moved to a private tutoring house in Shirokanedaimachi, Shiba Ward. At that time, although I was sixteen, I was quite small. You can guess how small I was from the fact that I traveled to Tokyo the previous year at the age of fifteen with a half-price train ticket without any questions asked.

Getting off the Meguro streetcar at Shirokanedaimachi, I walked about halfway down the hill leading to the Sacred Heart Girls' School on the right-hand side. The two-story house on my right was my new boarding house.

At that time, the Shirokanedaimachi area was still a newly developed area. My new house was connected to other houses to the east and north, but to the south, across the street, there was an empty lot of several thousand *pyeong*, and to the west, there was a house only after crossing an empty lot of three or four hundred *pyeong*.

After having dinner with R, who was also staying at the boarding house, I declined R's suggestion to go for a walk to explore the neighborhood of the newly moved-in house and went up to the second floor alone. I opened my textbook, but since there was nothing to review, I went out to the veranda (내말니우는곳) located west across from my room.

It was a dark, moonless night. However, when I stepped out onto the veranda, a brightly lit place caught my eye as if defying the dark night. It was the second floor of a house about twenty *gan* away from where I was standing. A Japanese-style two-story house, with the *shōji* doors removed and glass doors installed instead, painted, in other words, a Japanese house with Western-style gilding.

That was when I first saw "her". Inside the glass window, a 100-candlepower electric light was brightly lit, and a Western family of five, a couple, two sons, and what seemed like a daughter, were sitting around the center table, chatting and laughing. <113>

The curiosity of a young boy is great. I went back into my room, took out a telescope, and hid myself to watch again. My telescope was directed only at the blonde girl sitting sideways (론드의 게집애게로만향하엿다).

She might have been thirteen or fourteen years old. She had an oval face, and dimples appeared on her cheeks whenever she smiled. Although her lower body was not visible, I could easily tell from her gestures that she was tapping her feet playfully.

A poet might say that such a scene is only appropriate in spring, but for a sentimental boy, autumn was fine too. A dark night, a bright window, a happy family, a blonde girl. If Millet had seen it, it would have become a painting - if Goethe had seen it, he would have recited a line of poetry, but for the boy Kim Tong-in, nothing else mattered. One side of her hair, one side of her cheek, her shoulders that had not yet filled out, and one eye from which the playful laughter of a young girl flowed were the sole existence in that magic lantern-like scene.

Two days later, I saw her face completely for the first time. After school, I returned home and waited impatiently for night to fall. As I was restlessly looking down the street from my second-floor room, the figure of a Western-dressed girl appeared at the edge of my vision. I was startled, closed the *shōji* door, ran to the desk, stood there, and then ran back to peek through the crack in the door. The girl, with her hands in her coat pockets, kicking small stones on the street, going down the hill, was none other than the girl from the magic lantern-like scene two days ago.

In spring, flowers; in summer, greenery; in autumn, fallen leaves; in winter, snow - the heart of the quiet and melancholy boy who knew nothing else to love was finally pierced by Cupid's arrow.

From then on, before going to bed at night, I would go out to the veranda (내말니우는곳) under the pretext of taking deep breaths and stand there watching her house until the *amado* (storm shutters) were closed. In the morning, as soon as I woke up, even before washing my face, I would go out there with dumbbells and offer her my morning greetings of the heart. <114>

The empty lot of several thousand *pyeong* south across the street from our boarding house was used by us (R, me, and two or three others) as a practice field for *beisupōru* (baseball).

One day, as I was passing the ball while facing away from "her" house, I missed a ball coming intensely. The ball crossed the street, crossed the empty lot, and came to rest under the fence of "her" house. I, crestfallen, with my head bowed, ran to pick up the ball.

"Arthur" That was the voice of a mother calling her child from inside the fence. At the same time, a gentle reply was heard.

I picked up the ball, threw it back with all my might, and ran back to my room.

Ah, Arthur. Of course, it was not a name to be forgotten. At that time, we were learning about the story of King Arthur, so it was a name that would not be easily forgotten. However, to me, the name seemed like one that, once forgotten, would never be remembered, so I immediately wrote the name Arthur in a memo.

The name Arthur appeared to me as a joyful and dreamlike beautiful name. Several types of King Arthur's legends adorned my bookshelf. Pictures of King Arthur's knights were displayed on the walls. The word Arthur was written on every page of my textbooks and notebooks during class. I was so engrossed in the name that I felt like I could find the word Arthur at the edge of the sky if I looked up at the sky intently. The name Arthur was indeed an indispensable nourishment for me at that time.

One day, while I was practicing baseball again, I heard a young girl's voice in English from behind. In the moment I was distracted, I missed the ball coming towards me. When I turned around, "she" was holding the ball, hesitating where to send it. I instinctively reached out my hand, but my eyes grew dim. With a small clatter (カクゴヱ), I was aware of her throwing the ball, but with my vision blurred, I couldn't see which way the ball was coming. The ball hit my face and fell. As I took off my glasses and covered my face with both hands, thick nosebleed dripped into my palms.

"Ach, gomen nasai."

Along with the sound of running footsteps, I heard a beautiful voice in my ear. I pushed away her hand for no reason and ran up to my second floor room. Tears! I had never shed so many tears before.

What kind of tears? I cannot explain it. Gratitude? Joy? Sadness? Embarrassment? It was tears that did not belong to any of those categories, and at the same time, it could be said to be a surge of emotions combining all those things. A boy has subtle emotions that adults cannot understand. Although every adult once grew up without having stepped into boyhood, the "realm of emotions" called boyhood is a season of dreams that does not allow re-entry once adulthood is reached, and at the same time, it is a splendid flower garden hidden by the veil of oblivion.

Ah, how beautiful my dreams were at that time. The future that one imagines in boyhood is completely different from that in youth. There are no practical problems such as a happy family, and furthermore, the ages of old age and middle age are completely ignored. Only the eternal spring unfolds, and the seasons of autumn and winter are not even considered to exist. At that time, how hard I studied English, neglecting other subjects. How much did the dream of crossing the Pacific Ocean with a beautiful blonde maiden (on a ship several times larger than the Koryomaru, which I took to Tokyo) captivate my heart. The sound of the rippling waves and the vast sea, the moonlit night, the sound of the engine - ah, where is there such beautiful music? A country like America, without history, never even came to my mind. How many times have I blushed imagining wandering around the outskirts of Rome or the scenic countryside of England, hand in hand with her, wearing a stylish travel outfit with a helmet and a white towel trailing behind, holding a camera in one hand and her hand in the other, exploring the ancient ruins of Egypt.

One day (I don't remember exactly, but it was either a Sunday or a holiday, anyway, a day when school was closed), as I was sitting on the threshold of the second floor, dreaming such dreams, I suddenly heard a phantom sound (which I always heard in my dreams or waking hours), this time in a human voice -

"Arthur"

I was startled and turned my head that way.

?

??

The only people passing by were "her" older brother and "her" mother. "Arthur" was the voice of the mother calling her son.

However, Arthur was, of course, a boy's name. "Arthur," which I had known only as her name, rejoicing alone, and thinking of it as the most beautiful rhythm (리즘) in the world, was the name of someone else entirely.

Just as children have their own emotions and imaginations, they also have a sense of pride that adults cannot even fathom. The anger of having my sacredness violated burned fiercely in my heart. <116>

The next day, when I went to school, all my textbooks were already newly bought the night before, and there wasn't a single old book. This was because I couldn't bear to carry textbooks with the name "Arthur," which had awakened such pride, written on every page.

After that, I inquired in various ways (but within the scope allowed by a boy's pride) and learned that her father was a mixed-race person born between a Japanese person and a Western woman, her mother was English, and her older brothers were one Arthur and one Tommy. Since the Japanese pronunciations of "Arthur" and "Tommy" (ア-サ and トンモ) sounded like *asa* (淺, shallow) and *tomo* (富, wealth), one was named Asatarō (淺太郞) in Japanese and the other was Tomojirō (富次郞). I also learned that "her" name was Mary.

After that, there is no need to mention that the name Mary was written on every page of my new textbooks in greater numbers than the previous Arthur. The spelling Mkarmy, which could not be found even by searching in any dictionary, was decorated as a flower-shaped letter on the front and back covers of the book. Various designs made by weaving together the letters M and K were all the rage. No, it wasn't just that. The four letters M, A, R, and Y not only appeared in all the words formed by those four letters but also seemed to me like meaningful letters even as individual words. When I discovered an unpleasant word while flipping through the dictionary, either in the M section, or in the A, R, or Y sections, I sacredized each letter, cursing the English language for including such unpleasant words in the sections of such sacred letters.

As winter approached, our baseball practice was suspended. Instead, my deep breathing and dumbbell exercises increased in intensity. And so, on a day close to Christmas, I went out in the morning to do dumbbell exercises, but (unlike other times when it would already be open) the *amado* of her house on the second floor was not yet open. Deep breathing, then dumbbells, then deep breathing again, repeated several times. But her firmly closed shutters did not open.

I ate breakfast hastily and came back up to check again, but the shutters were still closed. I took the school exams in a daze, not knowing how I did, and hurried back home, but the doors were still closed.

The shutters of her house did not open the next day either.

In the evening, I casually (or so I thought, but I don't know if my facial expression didn't change and my voice didn't tremble) told the boarding house owner that it was strange that the shutters of that house hadn't opened. The owner immediately replied -

"They probably went on vacation."

It was a simple conclusion. But also a plausible conclusion. Why hadn't I been able to come to such a simple conclusion?

Even when all the international students were happily celebrating the winter vacation, the New Year, and the increase in tuition fees, the lonely boy who had lost the whereabouts of his beloved played alone, separated from them, with an even gloomier face.

The third semester! How much I had been waiting for it. It wasn't because I was particularly interested in studying, but because Mary's siblings were all students, and I hoped they would return for the new semester.

However, they did not return even for the new semester. I tried to console myself by thinking they might be a little late, but when they didn't return by mid-February, I finally decided to give up.

However, although I decided to give up, I couldn't quite let go. The name Mary was written everywhere in my third-year textbooks. The single phrase "Shall I give up? (斷めやうか)" written in the Chinese textbook was enough to express the bitter heart of the boy who had lost the whereabouts of his beloved. This also explains the sudden increase in the number of "unexcused absences" of the quiet student who considered being even one hour late a shameful thing.

"Love is a monster," it has been said. I don't know who invented the phrase, but the heart of the boy who was violated by that monster was certainly bitter. Adult love has a quid pro quo, a condition of exchange, but this boy had no such thoughts. He just wanted to love, and the other person's feelings were not even considered. If Mary were to love someone else, an ugly flame of jealousy would undoubtedly have flared up in my young heart, but at that time, my pure and innocent heart had no complaints about "unrequited love." As long as I could see her sometimes and live not too far away, I had no other desires. I never even thought about talking to her or associating with her. Moreover, even if there was an opportunity to do so, my shyness would have forced me to avoid such a thing and be satisfied with just watching from afar.

I never saw her again after that. I had no way to even hear news of her.

In the autumn of that year, I was already a third-year student. As I was returning from school, a heavy book bag hanging over my shoulder, I saw a rickshaw coming from the other side (with a Western-dressed girl riding in it).

In the moment I thought I had lost my mind, my eyes went dim and I lost my sight. However, before the rickshaw came in front of me, I regained my sight and breathed a sigh of relief (rather) when I realized that the girl was not Mary. This was the last of my superficial encounters with her(?).

It was a dreamlike love.

Mary - (if she is still alive, she would be a woman of twenty-eight years old). Of course, she would never dream that some Korean boy devoted so much love to her in her childhood. But how beautiful and tender was my dream at that time. And how painful was the wound after losing that dream.

The boy's dream was shattered mercilessly. And the wound it left was deep. These past ten years, I have seen many women and had many opportunities to fall in love, but the fact that I have never looked at them with eyes that were not tinged with a playful feeling (except for one exception) is all due to the continued influence of that time. The fact that the quiet and melancholy boy put all his energy into transforming his personality into a cheerful and carefree young man was also to eliminate the chance of reliving the pain of that wound.

Thus, Mary, the owner of shining blonde hair and translucent skin, who had such a great influence on my life, is a dreamlike symbol that I will never forget in my life. (Unfinished) <119>

女人-追憶의더듬ㅅ길-
						김동인
나의 삼십년의 一생을 통하여, 길에서, 긔차뎐차에서 혹은 엇던 집회장에서,「路傍의 사람」이라는글자고대로한으로만나서한으로(엇던 인상 조차 업시) 사라저버린 女人의수효는, 萬으로서 헤이지 못할것이다.
그러한 만흔 女人가운데, 나의 생활에, 나의 감정에, 한때에 한점의 컴마를 짜어노흔 女人도, 百으로서 헤이지 못할것이다.
그 만흔 女人을 다시한번 체우에 올녀도아가지고 흔들을때에, 그냥 그래도 체에 걸니어서, 나려지지 안는 女人은, 몃명이나 되나.
당시에는, 극히 평범한 녀인이다 하여, 한눈발조차 주지 안엇든사람 가운데, 몃해를 지난 지금지도, 아련히 나의머리에서 그인상이 사러지지안는 사람도 잇다. 그의 겨테 안기지 실혀서,＜112＞ 자리를 피하든 사람 가운데도, 이제 다시한번 만나보고 십픈 사라도잇다. 당시에는, 나의 에 나타나주지 안는 그를 원망하는이 만치 그리든 녀인으로, 지금은 얼골의 인상조차니저버린사람도 잇다.
그러한 가운데서, 나의 체질에 그냥 걸니어서 떨어지지안는 女人은 과연 몃사람이나 되나. 나는 차레로 그것을 한번적어보려한다. 메리, 中島芳江, 萬造寺あき子, 金玉葉, M蟬丸, 黃璟玉, X(성명조차모른) 盧山紅, 金白玉-합하야 열사람, 그가운데 黃璟玉, 金白玉의 두사람(한사람은 서울, 한사람은 진남포 기생)은 벌서 고요한 저세상으로 길난사람(하누님이여, 어린 그들의 령을 보호하여 주시옵소서) 이오, 메리, 芳子, あき子, 蟬丸, X의 다섯사람은, 그 거처는커녕 생사초차 모르는 사람이오 남어잇는 세사람가운데, 金玉葉은, 第七回(?)의 가정생활을 시작하엿다는 소식을 드럿스며, M은 단락안 가뎡의 어진 지어미로서, 몃아이의 어머니의 노릇을 잘 한다하며, 盧山紅, 현재 서울서잘팔니는기생노릇을 그냥한다한다.
1 메리
一九一五年가을이엇다. 明治學院중학교二학년생, 열여섯살되는 소년 김동인은, 芝區白金臺町 엇던 사숙으로이사를 하엿다. 그때의 나는, 열여섯이라 하나, 몹시 작은편으로서, 그전해 열다섯살에 동경갈때에, 긔차 반액권으로 아모말업시 간것만으로도, 내가 엇지작엇는지 짐작할수가잇다.
メグロ가는 뎐차를 白金臺町에서 나려서, 오른편쪽으로 聖心女學院으로 가는언덕길을 한 절반이나 나려가서, 오른손짝으로 잇는 다락집이, 내가 새로잠은 하숙이엇섯다.
당시의 白金臺町一대는, 아직 新開地로서, 나의새집의 東쪽과 北쪽은 인가와 접속되엿지만, 南쪽으로는, 길을 건너서 몃 千평의 뷔인터가 잇고, 西쪽역시 三四百평의 뷔인터를 건너서야 집이잇섯다.
그하숙에 가티잇게된 R과 함께 저녁을먹은뒤에, 이사온집 근처의 지리를 연구키 위하야 산보를 나가자는 R의말을 거절한뒤에, 나는 혼자서 二층으로올나왓다. 교과서를 팻스나, 복습할것도 업슴으로, 나의방에서목도하나를 건너서 西쪽으로 잇는 내말니우는곳으로 나섯다.
달업는 어두운 밤이엇섯다. 그러나, 내가 그내말니는곳으로 나설때에는, 그 어두운밤을 무시하는듯이 밝게 빗나는 곳이 눈에엇다. 그것은 내가 서잇는곳과 한 二十여간 상거되는 엇던집 二층의 문이＜113＞잇섯다. 일본식이층집, 장지문을떼어버리고 그대신류리문을 달은뒤에, 페인트칠을 한, 말하자면, 洋式도금을 한일본집이엇섯다.
내가,「그」를 처음본것이 그때엿섯다. 류리창안에는(짐작컨대) 百촉되는 뎐등이 밝아잇섯고, 센터테이블을 가운데로, 夫妻두사람과 두아들과 한딸인듯한서양인의 한 가족 다섯사람이 둘너안저서, 무엇을 직거리며 웃고잇섯다.
소년시대의 호긔심은 크다. 나는 내방에 드러와서 망원경을가지고 나와서, 숨어셔서 다시보앗다. 내 망원경의 리는,(나의게는) 側面으로 안저잇는 론드의 게집애게로만향하엿다.

나는, 그뒤에 다시 그를 보지를 못하엿다. 그의소식조차 드를길이 업섯다.
그해 가을, 인저는 벌서 三학년생이로라고, 목을 넘겨서 메이든 책가방을, 외인편억개에 걸친뒤에, 학교에서 도라오든 나는, 저편에서 오는(洋裝한 少女의 탄) 인력거를 보앗다.
내가 정신을 일흐렷다 생각한 순간은 벌서 눈이아득하여 시각을 일어버렷다. 그러나, 그인력거가 내앞지 오기전에 시각을 회복한 나는, 그少女가 메리＜118＞가 안인것을 알고(오히려) 숨을 내여쉬엿다. 이것이 나와 그(?)의 표면적 사괴임의 마즉막이엿섯다.
과가튼사랑이엿섯다.

매리-.(그가 아직사러잇다 하면 벌서 스물닐여덟의 녀인이 되엿슬것이다) 그는 무론, 자긔의 어린시절에, 엇던 조선소년이 자긔에게 그다지도 사랑을 바첫다 하는것은 에도 모를일일것이다. 그라나, 나의 그때의 의, 그다지고 아름답고 애처러웟슴이어. 그리고, 그을 일어버린뒤의 상처의 아팟슴이어.
소년의 은 무참히도 여저버렷다. 그러고, 그바든 상처는 컷다. 이리 十數년, 만흔 녀인을보고, 만흔련애할 긔회를가젓섯지만, (다만 한번의 例外를 제하고는 유희긔분이 안석긴눈으로 그들을 바라보지안은 일이 업는것은, 모도가 그때의 그영향의 持續이 잇섯다. 말업고 음울하든 소년이, 죽을힘을다하여, 자긔의 성격을, 쾌활하고 ノンキ한 靑年으로 변케한것도 그때의 그상처의 아픔을 再現할 긔희를업시하기 위하여서엇섯다.

이럿틋, 나의 一生에 커다란 영향을 준, 빗나는 金발과 투명되는 피부의 소유자 메리는, 나의 생애에는 영구히 잇지못할 과가튼 심볼이다.(未完)＜119＞
