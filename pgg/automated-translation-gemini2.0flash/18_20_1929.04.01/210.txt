회고·수기 (Memoir)

Stories of Survival from the Great Kanto Earthquake: A Collection of True Stories of Lives Saved from the Brink, Recollections of Nine Deaths and One Life in the Midst of the Cataclysmic Great Earthquake
						Pak Chin

The year Taisho 12! Anyone would recall that as the year of the Great Kanto (Tokyo) Earthquake in Japan, and September 1st! Anyone would first think of that terrifying day when the magnificent imperial capital of Japan, Great Tokyo, was consumed by flames, along with the pointless deaths of over 400,000 souls! Furthermore, there must have been deaths without reason.

September 1st! Kanto Earthquake! (......27 characters deleted here - original text......) How did I manage to escape that scene of carnage! That land of death, and continue to recall those memories from time to time even after seven years have passed! However, there must be many who escaped situations tens or hundreds of times more deadly than mine. I am not saying that I faced greater danger than others, but in the proverb, "Another's illness is not as bad as my own boils," even though some died, I feel like I experienced it all alone.

However, if I were to write down all the circumstances and dangers of that time, I would not be able to fully recreate the experience, and there would not be enough brush or space to write as I wish. Therefore, I will simply jot down my journey from the land of death, Tokyo, back to my hometown, as a pastime.

First, I offer a humble bow before the innocent souls who met a tragic death and vented their unappeased grievances.

The day before - the day before September 1st - it rained heavily! The day dawned. Ah! The dreaded September 1st had arrived. From morning, it was strangely humid, and the sun blazed down fiercely. Dark clouds came and went, and there seemed to be ill omens to foresee, but <60> nevertheless, it was an ordinary late summer day.

That summer, I was feeling melancholic, while others went to their beloved hometowns to savor love, I remained alone, living in a solitary hut outside the city, eking out a living, eating gruel morning and evening. I hesitate to admit that I simply lacked the travel expenses to return home.

Today is September 1st! I prepared breakfast and ate it mixed with soy sauce and, intending to pay the school fees, I took the money I had obtained three days prior by pawning a friend's silver watch and trudged to school to find the treasurer. However, the treasurer was absent that day, so I put the money back and left.

The tasteless meal had left me feeling hungry, and my incorrigible stomach demanded lunch. Should I visit the boarding houses to see if any of my friends had returned from their hometowns? Having grown tired of soy sauce rice, should I treat myself to two or three plates of 8-sen Western-style food?

No, I should just stay put and chew on the leftover cold rice from breakfast.

Everywhere I went, the ground was so hot that beads of sweat sprang up with every step, and that seemed too difficult. Using the parasol I had brought, I sought shade at Ochanomizu Station (御茶水驛) (apologies, but I must use the place names as they are) and boarded the suburban electric railway, thinking of the cold rice waiting for me in my locked house. It was the time when elementary and middle school commencement ceremonies were ending, and the train was packed.

The train sped on, passing through the Yotsuya Tunnel and approaching the Shinanomachi Station platform when it began to shake strangely, making loud wheel noises. However, since the train was originally quite fast, it was expected to shake violently when stopping. But the strange sounds and shaking continued for a long time.

"Ah! This is strange."

In that instant! Some giddy fellow shouted in a pig-slaughtering voice,

"Earthquake!"

It was five minutes before noon. In this car and that car, people tried to be the first to escape, smashing windows and sticking their heads out in a frenzy, only to be cut and bleed! Some fell, others cried! It immediately turned into a scene of carnage. I was bewildered by the screaming. I was startled by the reckless shouting, but I did not act as hastily as those others. I did not intend to rush outside first, but in the commotion, I was pushed out onto the platform. The day was still very hot. There was no one on the train, and then they all clamored to get back on. But I leisurely dragged my parasol from one end of the platform to the other, finding amusement in watching the chaos.

Then, with a rumbling sound like the heavens were falling, the earth shook as if I were standing in a cradle rocking a baby. People jumped out when it shook, and climbed back on when it stopped, doing this several times. "The tunnel ceiling has partially collapsed, and the <61> embankments along the line have crumbled." This was the report from a pale-faced station employee.

"Ah!" The people's faces turned blue.

"Namu Amida Butsu! Namu Amida Butsu!"

The hopeless women huddled on the ground, their faces like corpses, their mouths dry, chanting. One minute felt like an eternity, and the shaking would end and then begin again. Every time it shook with a terrifying sound, people flinched.

The signal broke and bent. The station master tried to call everywhere. But none of the eight phones worked. I was thirsty and turned on the faucet at the water station. No matter how much I turned it, no water came out. Black smoke rose from all directions.

"Fires have broken out everywhere in the city!" Everyone shouted.

Only then did I feel truly afraid.

"There is no hope of reopening the line, so I apologize, but please proceed on foot."

That was the station master's announcement. Still, I leisurely walked toward the ticket gate, lagging behind the hurried people, climbing the stairs one step at a time, thinking,

"Is an earthquake such a terrifying thing? It happens occasionally, but this one is shaking quite violently. Should I go outside and take a look?"

Carefree, I was getting even hungrier.

"Oh, my goodness, look at this."

Until now, I had been ignorant, only thinking that the ground was shaking and fires were breaking out. But when I came outside, I was shocked and terrified. The tiles had fallen off every house in the city, and the walls had crumbled, leaving only the bare frame. This was among the least damaged houses. Trains had derailed, and people covered in blood were being carried to the hospital on stretchers. The number was uncountable. Cries, shouts, and screams! People running here and there, not knowing what to do, like madmen. There were no fires here yet.

After much thought, I could not linger there, and I could not go home, for who knew what had happened to my house? Only then did I feel bewildered and frightened. After much thought, I had nowhere to go.

Then it occurred to me to visit N-ssi's house in Kioicho, Kojimachi Ward (麴町區 紀尾井町) - across from the Akasaka Detached Palace. N-ssi was the family of the younger sister of Kosugi-ssi, who was the vice-principal of the Yosei School that I had attended. The entire family were devout Christians. N-ssi and his wife, indeed the women of the family, all felt favorably toward me and provided convenience to the extent they could.

I can confidently assert that I did not think of N-ssi's house simply because I was becoming frantic, frightened, and had nowhere to go.

Upon seeing and hearing all the horrifying sights—collapsed houses! People covered in blood! Screams—N-ssi's house flashed into my mind like lightning.

Reader! Please imagine in your mind this unending, slow shaking, the fires raging from all directions, <62> the ground sinking and cracking, houses collapsing, trains burning to the bare bones on the tracks, train lines twisted, explosions of gas tanks and powder magazines, houses falling down, the weeping sounds of mothers, children, siblings calling out to each other! I ask that you read this while visualizing this indescribable scene of carnage.

Barely - carefully - escaping through the gaps in the collapsed houses, I arrived near N-ssi's house. N-ssi worked for the Imperial Household Ministry and lived in the ministry's official residence. Adjacent to his house was the towering brick building of Sophia University (上智大學). No fires had broken out here yet. Just as I was about to pass in front of Sophia University, a sudden, intense tremor struck, shaking me to the core. Along with the terrifying sound of the brick building's windows rattling, there was a thunderous crash just a ken (間) or so ahead of me, and a fragment of brick flew up into the air. Already, the sight of that brick building had been ominous, making me come to my senses, and now, seeing that sound and flying brick, I let out a scream and collapsed, not knowing whether I was conscious or not, or what was happening in the world. But when I quickly got up and passed the spot where the brick had fallen, be not amazed. Half of it had flown up, and half of it was embedded in the ground.

"If I had been..." A shiver ran down my spine, and cold sweat poured out. Only then, realizing that I was playing an important role in this drama, did I feel fear with every step on this terrifying stage, whereas until then, everything had seemed strange and like something to watch.

That night, I spent the night with N-ssi's family on the roadside outside their house. Even if there was a safe inside the house, it was too dangerous to enter.

The flames soaring skyward illuminated the smoke-filled sky, and the fires continued to spread, while the firefighters stood idly by with their picks. Houses were burning, mothers were losing their children, and children were burning to death inside. There was a murderous glint in their eyes, as if they would bite someone to death. The ground still shook.

More terrifying than the fire or the earthquake, September 2nd arrived. It was dawn.

The flames were devouring the back of the neighborhood. The situation was growing increasingly dire. Since there was no great danger in the suburbs, I had no choice but to send N-ssi's wife and children to my house in Okubo (大久保), and I stayed behind with N-ssi's eldest son, M, to watch and wait until the fire reached his house. The sky was so hot from the flames that birds could not find a place to fly. However, since that area was relatively quiet, there was no great danger there yet, but Honjo (本所), Fukagawa (深川), Kanda (神田), and other areas were like battlefields, with charred bodies piled upon collapsed houses, and countless corpses, unable to withstand the heat, had jumped into the water and lost their lives, lying piled so thick that one could trip over them.

For several hours, I wandered around, out of curiosity, visiting the most severely affected areas and witnessing everything. The word "惨憺" (tragic) was insufficient to describe the scene.

I wandered around and returned to the area where N-ssi's house was located around four o'clock in the afternoon. <63>

The climax of my drama begins here. In just two or three hours, N-ssi's house would be consumed by the flames. M and I waited for the fire to reach the house. Just as we were about to discuss evacuating to Okubo, where N-ssi's parents were, and I was retying my shoelaces,

The tens of thousands of people who had evacuated to the plaza near the Akasaka Detached Palace suddenly turned into a demonic pit, surging and pushing in every direction, creating a scene of utter carnage.

"Are they fighting over food because there isn't enough to eat? Or are they robbing? Is it murder?"

It was murder. This murder was happening in various parts of Japan, and even at the same time...

The people running from the chasing crowd were like rabbits being chased by a pack of wolves with no sense of direction. However, I still did not know the reason for it all. The fires were burning everywhere! The crowd was surging! They were hungry. They had lost their children and wives. There was fire in their eyes as well. All that could be seen were the raging fires, collapsed houses, and corpses burned and crushed to death.

The rabbit being chased by the wolves was finally chased to where I was. The pack of wolves came running, each with a club, a stone, or whatever weapon they could grab. The rabbit's white fur was stained with blood. It was difficult to make out his face. But he seemed to be a laborer, and he was wearing white clothes like a tennis uniform. There was some sign that he was Korean (朝鮮) (Chosen).

The mass of clubs finally brought him down.

"Ah, Mother!" A final scream! My hair stood on end. My skin crawled. But I still did not know the reason!

I was startled by the crowd's shouts. The fire burned. The violent shaking came. I could barely hear the voices of the people next to me.

I looked at M. M also looked at me with unease. M put his finger to his lips. As if telling me not to speak.

I had already lost my original composure and had almost adopted the same psychology as them - in the opposite sense, of course - but I had no choice but to avoid that place.

I decided to go with M to where his mother was, and then to wait until dark to escape to Nikko, where M's relatives lived.

The day grew dark earlier than usual. Their red eyes were even redder in the firelight. I could barely breathe deeply, and I could not speak to M. I was just waiting for it to get dark. They were growing increasingly agitated, and their horrifying shouts were growing louder.

Because I frequently visited N-ssi's house, the people in that neighborhood knew that I was Korean. Realizing this, it was increasingly dangerous for me to be there. Just as I was about to leave that place, a woman suddenly collapsed in front of M and me, struggling to breathe, and looked up at us without being able to speak. It was Y, M's eighteen-year-old younger sister. My heart sank. M was also surprised. Y could not speak and just shook her head, denying something.

She gasped for a long time, then gathered her shallow breaths and managed to say:

"Things are terrible everywhere right now, so don't even think about coming to Okubo, and even if it's dangerous, you must go inside and stay put. I saw such horrific things on my way here."

She then burst into tears. N-ssi's wife had foreseen that I would try to evacuate there and, fearing the danger of that place as well as the danger along the way, had sent her daughter running to us. What a kind heart. But where to go? It was as if a young sheep had strayed from its flock and was mixed in with a pack of wolves, only the terrifying wolves had not yet discovered it.

Ah! Where could I go to be safe? Where could I go to survive? Now I feared people more than the violent shaking, the flames, or hunger. I, too, wanted to swing a club at random and try to be malicious, even if it was just against one person...

Fire began to burn in my eyes as well. But I was reluctant to have my life pointlessly taken by them.

It seemed to be eight o'clock in the evening. The flames were growing ever more intense. The shouting was growing ever louder.

At that moment, R-kun, a middle school classmate who had been chased from somewhere, came running with a pale, weary face, looking frantic. Seeing R-kun, whom I had thought was dead, was less a relief than a source of great strength.

They foamed at the mouth and raged like madmen. The crowd stirred at once.

Hearing this sound and mixed in with the stirring crowd, what must my face, or rather R's face, have looked like! My vocal cords could not produce a sound. I wanted to say something to M, but no words came out.

Could I have stayed there at that time? I had no choice but to slip out of the crowd and go with R to N-ssi's house to escape for the moment. That was the only option at the time.

The house next door, separated only by a wooden fence, had begun to burn. Embers were falling on the lawn. The windows of Sophia University, which could be seen looming overhead, reflected the firelight and looked like the eyes of a terrifying tiger, and every time there was a violent tremor, the sounds seemed like the roars of a ferocious lion, threatening to collapse on top of us. My back was hot. The brick building was about to collapse. R wept, clutching at the grass. I wept too. But the approaching fire would not be extinguished by the tears in our eyes, and we could not stop the collapsing brick building with our strength. The tiles on N-ssi's house were flying off. The foundation was collapsing, and the pillars were tilting. Cracks were beginning to appear even in the lawn. <65>

"No. Whether we burn to death, get crushed to death, or fall into the ground... let's get out of here. I don't want to die like this. Even if we die, let's die moving."

I grabbed R's hand and stood up abruptly. But at that time, R could not speak. I was no longer in a human state of mind.

The fire! The brick building! The cracks! The flying tiles! None of that was frightening anymore. M rushed in, frantic. For no reason, he had rushed into this dangerous place.

"Hand over the XX people!"

Just as this cry from the crowd outside the gate was growing like a tidal wave, suddenly the heavens and earth seemed to collapse. The mob passed by.

"Come out quickly! Let the house burn as much as it will, and let's go to Nikko." M said nothing else. I had long since given up on life, so I had become bold. However, I had not lost my attachment to life. I picked up a bag filled with a mal (斗) of rice and came out. But I was more worried about R-kun than about myself. After four or five years of studying in Tokyo, he still spoke Korean-accented Japanese, just as he had in Chungcheong Province - though I didn't speak Japanese much better than him.

And since middle school, he had been given the nickname "fool" by his teachers, and indeed, he was a fool. Even in the midst of this disaster, thinking ahead, he had wrapped two cotton shirts in a patchwork cloth that his mother had made for him. - He had returned to Tokyo around August 29th and, unable to find lodging, had left his belongings at N-ssi's house. -

No matter how much I tried to dissuade him, he would not listen, but instead wrapped the bundle and carried it on his back, as befit a peddler. Who would not recognize him as a Korean upon seeing that patchwork bundle? Moreover, with his speech, gait, and appearance, he looked like someone studying in a village school, and in this situation, when even fellow Koreans were in danger of being attacked if they didn't speak the Tokyo dialect, I gave up on dissuading him, fearing that he would be suspected. I reluctantly placed a blanket on top of the bundle so that his face would not be easily seen, and told him to just follow us, and with M's siblings, we began to move. -M said that his house would burn down in less than 30 minutes and that he would watch it burn and then follow us.

Sweat poured down. Hot sweat, cold sweat - with the foolish R-kun carrying the blanket-covered bundle in front, I, wearing summer Japanese clothes and a straw hat, followed a few paces behind, and Y, a few paces behind me, crossed Yotsuya Bridge, looking toward Shinjuku, anxiously and with cold sweat pouring down, carefully moving. People were evacuating in a wave, going wherever others were going, not knowing where they could survive. The intense flames scorched our backs and lit our way forward. Among the crowds who had taken out their furniture and were waiting for their houses to burn, only the crying of hungry children was pitiable. I, too, had been without food for two days, but I had no room to think about that.

They ask for your name, address, and destination. What name should I <67> give? In my confusion, I said "Nakamura". Still trying to survive - Ssi's daughter, Y, also defended me, saying that I was her brother, and sometimes she would cry, saying that her mother was about to die, so we had to hurry. But the foolish R-kun who had been walking ahead was nowhere to be seen. Since he had gone ahead, I figured that if he was caught and suffered hardship, I would surely meet him later, so I praised myself for the clever idea of covering him with the blanket. A ken is not far, but they stopped and asked questions. But if they had just asked, it would have been better. It seems that hitting you once first was some kind of tactic. In this way, barely, barely, nearly dying several times, we finally arrived at where N-ssi's wife was. It was about two o'clock at night. There were few places there where fires had burned or houses had collapsed.

Entering the house where N's wife was - the house next to the one I had been staying in - Y called out to her mother, and seeing her mother and the old woman who owned the house running out, Y fainted. I, too, exhausted, heard N-ssi's wife's voice and felt a slight sense of relief, my anxious and tense mind relaxing somewhat. I thought, "Okay, I'm alive now." And I collapsed on the spot, unable to move again. But that sense of relief was of no use. There was a rush of footsteps, and a group of about 10 people grabbed me and lifted me up.

"Ah! Now..." I did not feel fear or resist, and I was about to go with them. N-ssi's wife, of course, as well as the old woman who owned the house and her sons, grabbed the hands of the group and defended me. But they would not listen. It was a group that had suspected me since Shinjuku and had been following me. But I said nothing. N-ssi's wife and several other people defended me. N-ssi's wife defended me while weeping. But the group said they would take me with them and send me back if I was not Korean. I had no choice but to be dragged by the group, and just as I was about to take a step, suddenly, with a terrifying, gruff voice, a shout of "待て!(Mate!)" (Wait!) was heard, and a military policeman with a sword and the vegetable seller who came to sell vegetables every day appeared. Only then did I feel fear. That vegetable seller knew too well that I was Korean. Seeing that he had brought a military policeman with him, there was no way to defend myself, and it was no use. N-ssi's wife grabbed the vegetable seller's hand. But he stood sternly and turned to the group:

"You all go ahead. I know this guy well, so don't worry and go." The military policeman also seconded the statement. The group seemed to think it was plausible and left.

Why did the vegetable seller save me? The reason was that I had always been kind to him. Truly! If that vegetable seller had come just a little later... A little later, the foolish R-kun also managed to survive and arrive. For several days after that, I hid under the protection of N-ssi's wife, and on the dawn of September 13th, I obtained a certificate from the police station and returned to Gyeongseong (京城) on a free train. There are terrifying stories from the journey home as well, but I will stop here, as I am reluctant to write any more details. Ah! Those terrible memories. I offer my gratitude to the N-ssi family, who saved my life, for the rest of my life. (End) <68>

그러나 그때의 그 情況과 위험을 격근 始末을 다 쓰게 된다면 그때 그대로를  그리여 내일수도 업거니와 붓이 모자라고 쓰고 십흔대로 쓰지도 못할 것이다. 그럼으로 조곰만 간단하게 死地 東京서 고향으로 도라오기까지만 심심푸리 대신 적어나 보려한다.
먼저 무참한 죽엄과 不測의 怨을 풀은 애꾸진 生靈 압헤 虛拜를 드린다.
그 전날-9월 1일 前日-은 비가 몹시 쏘다저왓다! 날은 밝엇다. 아! 무서웁던 9월 1일은 왓다. 아츰부터 이상하게도 무덥고 볏은 태울듯이 나리쪼이엿다. 검은 구름이 오락가락하고 무슨 조치 못한 兆朕을 예측할 것도 가탓스나 <60> 그러나 평범한 느즌 녀름의 하로날 이엿다.
