記者總出動, 大京城白晝暗行記(제2회), 『一時間社會探訪』
						松雀 외
Last January 24th (Monday), from 1 PM to 2 PM. This is how Seoul was moving during that one hour!!

Passing by the Hanseong Bank, and a glimpse of money at the Hanil Bank.

Songjak

Ugh—another mobilization order has been issued. "Go anywhere you want for exactly one hour and sketch a glimpse of Seoul in motion," they say. Where should I go this time? Going to the streets won't be interesting because there are few people walking around in this cold weather. I went to the courthouse last time, so I can't repeat that. Ah, what's the point of saying more? Even if I just chase chicks because I have nothing to do, and because my pockets are empty, I'll go to a wealthy bank to smell some money. So, I left and headed to Namdaemun-tong, which could be called the center of banks and companies, passing through Jongno.

Among them, I decided to visit the Hanseong Bank (漢城銀行), a Korean-managed bank (the foreign investment is a separate matter) <26> with the largest capital (capital of three million won) and a noble atmosphere. I went straight in, and since I was someone who had nothing to do with financial transactions, I felt awkward entering the bank. Also, because of the times, I thought that if I hesitated, I might be mistaken for a suspicious person and be greatly embarrassed. However, since I had already entered, I decided to take a look around. I noticed that among the twenty-one people who came for transactions, thirteen were Chinese, which was surprising. I wondered if this bank should be called the Hanjok Bank (漢族銀行) rather than the Hanseong Bank (漢城銀行). I felt awkward just standing there, so I pretended to be making a large deposit. I took a deposit withdrawal slip and jokingly wrote "一金壹千圓也" (one thousand won), signed and stamped it, and wandered around. The ignorant guard thought I didn't know where to withdraw money and kindly showed me that the deposit counter was over there and the payment counter was over here. However, I pretended not to know and wandered around. As the saying goes, "到處에 逢故人" (Everywhere you go, you meet an acquaintance). I found some people I knew here as well. Min Dae-ho from Dongyang Seowon, Kye Yeon-jip from the Cheondogyo Church, Park Kyung-tae from Kyodong Paljinok, a messenger from Boseong Jeonmun School, and a messenger from Chungang High School all came. If I had made a mistake, I would have been caught by these people. I greeted them while observing the atmosphere inside the bank at that time. However, it was difficult to investigate the names and amounts of people withdrawing or depositing money without a reason. It was easy to be suspected if I stood too close to the payment counter and peeked at other people's money. That was not easy either. All I saw was an old villager with his hair in a topknot holding a check for one hundred won from Kim Young-wan and not knowing how to cash it, and a messenger from Chungang School depositing money from his colleagues (amount unknown). Ugh, at this rate, I would just be wasting time. So, I decided to stop skimming the surface and turned back. As I left, I muttered to myself, "This is a failure, a failure." Then I thought, "All banks are the same, so let's go to the Hanil Bank." I entered that bank again. Because the building was newly constructed, it was better than the Hanseong Bank, and the facilities were well-equipped, and there were quite a few customers. However, I still couldn't find out who was depositing how much or withdrawing how much. I caught a glimpse of Manager Lee Hang-jong's golden face through the window. That's it—I've got it. "江頭之事는 問於江頭之人" (Ask the ferryman about the affairs of the ferry), so since he is the manager of the bank, he would know the situation at any given time, and since I am friendly with him, it would be better to ask him directly. I sent my card through the clerk and requested a meeting. He told me to come in.

I went to his seat and saw Im Geun-sun, Cho Gang-ha, and Cho Nam-jik. However, no matter how many people I knew, I didn't have the courage to ask how much people were depositing or withdrawing at that time due to the bank's secrets or customer confidentiality. Also, anticipating that they wouldn't answer, I only learned that at that time (from 1 PM to 1:30 PM), there were eight depositors with more than 1,000 won, with the highest being 5,000 won (names are confidential), and six withdrawers with more than 1,000 won, with the highest being 3,000 won. In the end, the deposit amount was higher than the withdrawal amount. This is a common phenomenon on Mondays (conversely, there are more withdrawers on Saturdays). While this was happening, it was already exactly 2 PM, so I ran out. Whether this is a failure or a success, this is all I gained from this exploration.

A strange debt collection, an ogre watching a fire.

Pa-yeong

This time, I chose a familiar and convenient place and went to the printing house of Hanseong Doseo, but coincidentally, I met Mr. X (I'm hiding his name because I feel sorry) of the 00 Ilbo at the top of Andong. "I was just about to see you, so it's good to see you," he said, taking out the overdue manuscript, which was no different from a moneylender meeting a debtor who avoids him.

"I'm so sorry. I couldn't write it again. Please forgive me this time. I'll have some time in about a week, so I'll write one for you then." I begged for an extension. I was going to get a story, but I was stopped by a manuscript debt... Will I ever be in a position to beg him for a manuscript? I can't refuse outright... Being cornered on the street like this... Neither of us can say that being a reporter is an easy job. I was getting impatient. I had to get a good article in exactly one hour of exploration until 2 PM, but Mr. X wouldn't let me go easily. "Anyway, let's meet later. I'm a little busy right now." He said, "Okay. Where are you going? Let's talk while we go together," and kept urging me to write it by tonight. If I kept arguing like this, I would lose an hour on the street. I thought I should escape right away, so I said, "Yes, yes, yes, yes, I'll send it tonight," and he said, "Okay, I'll trust you. Where will you be tonight?" He still wouldn't let me go. "Yes, yes, I'll be at the office until midnight." "Then I'll go to the office tonight." I finally got away.

Feeling relieved that I was late, I hurried to Anguk-tong intersection. Near the tram stop in front of Dongduk Girls' School, a gentleman in a dark overcoat and a felt hat was arguing with a rickshaw driver. "This is a story!" I thought happily and ran over to listen. This was even more of a strange debt collection than the manuscript debt I had just been cornered by. "I have to earn money today. I don't have anything to pawn, so I can't even make a living because the rickshaw is being seized." "Who else could you deceive in this wide world? You're trying to deceive a rickshaw driver like me. You have to give me at least half of what you owe me today." The gentleman with the felt hat, who looked so gentle, seemed to be in debt for the rickshaw fare. His face was as red as gamhongro (甘紅露, soju), and he was embarrassingly saying, "Hey, what are you doing in the street? Come to my house later. Why don't you listen? Come to my house. I'll give you some money there," and trying to escape into the narrow alley next to Dongduk School. The rickshaw driver grabbed the gentleman's overcoat sleeve. "I've been to your house and been disappointed so many times that I'm tired of it." As the voice grew louder, the gentleman's gamhongro color deepened. "Why are you charging us poor people eighteen won for the rickshaw fare when we can barely make a living? For someone like you, eighteen won is less than a night's drinking money, but for us, it's a month's worth." The gentleman couldn't stand it anymore because people were gathering around. In a pitiful voice that seemed to be begging, he said, "Let's go, let's go. If you don't believe me, let's go together now. I'll give you the money at home..." He coaxed the driver like he was coaxing a woman, shaking his octagonal beard carelessly, and disappeared into the narrow alley. In general, what kind of strange debt is it that he was riding a rickshaw without money and how many times did he ride to owe eighteen won? And when he was riding, he must have acted like a bully, saying that the main street was too narrow. There are really all sorts of things happening in the world.

I entered the printing house, which was my original destination, but I couldn't get any stories. As I was about to go back, I heard a surprising sound from the main street... An extra edition? No, the sound of a fire truck! "Fire! Fire!" People crowded around the glass window in a commotion, and I squeezed in to look. Right in front of the eye-popper or nose-popper, a fire truck arrived and firefighters rushed out. Another fire engine came flying from Jongno like a bird, and people jumped out before the machine even stopped.

"A story!" I shouted and ran down, following them. It was an alley with signs for Shin-tong Company and Women's World Company. Five or six men with crowbars and shovels were busily running around in the narrow alley. It was a thatched-roof house (42 Gyeonji-dong, a student boarding house) deep in the Chinese restaurant Yeonghae-ru alley, in front of the house of the late Min Chung-jeong Gong. There was no fire or smoke, and it was completely quiet. I felt embarrassed for following them. I grabbed a neighbor and asked, "Do you see that? Isn't the top of the newly built roof on that gate burned? It's a strange fire." When I looked, it was true. Only the top of the roof tiles (the bottom was fine) was burned. Fortunately, the fire was quickly extinguished, but was it a fire that fell from the sky or a fire that started on its own? If there was a two-story house nearby, I might suspect that someone threw a cigarette butt, but there was no two-story house. If it was dawn or dusk, I might think that sparks flew from a nearby chimney, but it was late afternoon. No smoke came out, but a strange fire started on the top of the roof. "The firefighters must have looked around, but didn't they find anything?" "What could they find? There's nothing there, so the police are just wandering around the houses in front and behind, not knowing why the fire started." It was indeed strange. The young owner, who had been dragged around by the police, came and said as if he was giving a speech, "I've lived here for many years without ever hearing a harsh word from my neighbors, so I don't know why anyone would be angry with me." What's strange is that there was another fire for no reason just ten days ago. At that time, it was nine o'clock after dinner, and a fire broke out on the top of the roof on the other side. "It's a strange fire. It's not an ordinary fire." I heard the neighbors' words as meaning it was an ogre's fire. A fire for no reason in the broad daylight startles people... If it was really an ogre's fire, my article would be worth more, but I can't help but be amazed by the firefighters' quick response in rushing to the small fire that even the neighbors didn't know about. Moving Seoul! The fire department, which moves the fastest of all! It was already 10 minutes past two.

Failure at the police station and chased away to a hotteok shop

Gi-am

Would it be better to build a seven- or eight-story castle in the air? Or is it better to step on the actual ground, even if it's just one step?

I decided to make this exploration a bit more thorough! As soon as it was exactly 1 PM, I left the company gate. The terribly cold weather that had been going on for several days continued. My feet were so cold that I could barely stand it, and the pedestrians who passed by hunched over, clearly proving how "cold" it was.

Smiling Mister and I went back and forth, passing Gyodong Ordinary School, going behind Tapgol Park, passing the Gyeongseong Library branch, and going out to Jongno. But I didn't see anything that caught my eye. As soon as I got to Jongno, Pa-gun, who was going somewhere, suddenly appeared at the tram stop in front of the Youth Hall and stood there waiting for the tram. I could tell that he was going to Seodaemun or Namdaemun. He smiled at us as if he had been caught. When the three of us met, we just glanced at each other, wondering where to go. Eventually, Pa-gun went by tram, Smiling Mister went that way, and I went this way.

I thought there might be something interesting at the busy police station, so I went straight into Jongno Police Station. The security section was on the right and the police affairs section was on the left. I had heard that all sorts of miscellaneous incidents happen in the security section every day, so I opened the door and went in. Sure enough, there were about sixteen or seventeen people, including people who seemed to have been unfairly treated or people who had come to complain. Some of them were waiting impatiently, drawing 川 (cheon, river) characters on their foreheads and sitting on chairs. Others were giving advice on how to say something. But I couldn't figure out what they were talking about.

As soon as I entered, I saw an old mourner in front of the business section. He was wearing a North Pyongan direct garment (北布直領), a North Pyongan headscarf (北布巾), a white raincoat (흰남바위) on top of that, and white hemp shoes (흰밋투리). He was bowing respectfully and answering the questions of a young policeman.

"Maybe there's some rare incident happening," I thought, and I got close to listen to the conversation. It seemed that he was an old landlord from a wealthy family in Seoul. He had been charging too much for rent and had taken away the tenant's tenancy rights, so he had met with the tenant's petition. The old mourner was holding a small book bound with Korean paper (朝鮮백지) with "大正十五年度丙寅秋收記" (Taisho 15th Year Byeongin Autumn Harvest Record) written on it. Next to that, "管理李潤夏" (Manager Lee Yun-ha) was written, so Lee Yun-ha seemed to be the old landlord. After hearing the policeman's words to wait a little, he politely bowed again and sat back down. Then, a middle-aged man with a topknot, wearing a threadbare gat (宕巾, traditional Korean hat) with holes here and there, whether he was a chambong (參奉, government position) or a jusa (主事, government position), submitted a petition. The policeman asked him a few questions, and when he said he would investigate later, the man bowed deeply, his nose touching the window, and went back out. Then, a young woman in a small 쪽찌 (jjokji, 쪽 means 쪽빛 or indigo, and a jjokji could be a 쪽빛 coloured paper, or could be a hair decoration in that colour), wearing a hemp skirt (향주치마) and rubber shoes (고무신) opened the door and came in. I thought something might come of it if she came in to complain, but it was another failure. She had come to complain that she couldn't live with her husband and wanted a divorce. When the policeman told her to leave if she didn't like it, she just stood there and smiled. Next, the old mourner was called in again and asked a few questions. He was told to write down the reasons in a few lines. He bowed his head as if he was very grateful and left. But after that incident, there was no news and the people who had gathered gradually dispersed, leaving only a few. As the clock approached two o'clock and the police station was very quiet, I thought it was a failure to sit here. I came out as if I was running away from bees, but I couldn't think of where to go. Time was running out and everything had gone wrong.

"Ah, let's go to a hotteok shop. I'm hungry." I went up to Nakwon-dong and went into a hotteok shop on the street. Don't look down on a morning hotteok shop. Of course, there are many people from the lower classes, but students and gentlemen also come in. Even though this is a small and dirty shop, five people, including me, came in in about five minutes, and two people who had been sitting left. If one person eats one hotteok, it's thirty-five jeon. If they eat two, it's seventy jeon. In one hour, 4 won 20 jeon to 8 won 40 jeon of money goes from the pockets of poor Koreans to the pockets of dirty Chinese people with dark faces and dirty clothes. Calculate it by the day, by the month, and by the year. It's no wonder that a hotteok vendor from last year becomes a restaurant owner this year. You can see here how shrewd they are at making money.

It was already 2 PM. All I got from the hotteok shop was this. Fifteen jeon flowed out of my pocket.

Thirty Minutes at the Gate of Seoul, Bongnae Bridge

Chunpa

Last time, I went east and unexpectedly got a lot of that dirty dung splash, but this time, I'll go south and at least watch people at Seoul Station, or if not, I'll stand on Bongnae Bridge (蓬萊橋), the gate of Seoul, which I've been keeping an eye on, and watch the wagon chaos, I left the company gate quietly. Cheongo-hyung followed me. When I asked him where he was going, he hesitated to answer. As I was going to Sajik-tong, Jeong Jong-myeong of the Women's Needy Student Assistance Association came up, saying "何相見之晩也" (Why have we met so late?), and immediately started scolding us, saying "Byeolgeon-gon is too harsh. You pick on everything-" We laughed and said "We don't pick on much," said goodbye to him, and went to Sajik-tong intersection to wait for the tram. Xiaosan-gun and Smiling Mister suddenly hit me on the back and asked, "Where are you going?" We kept our destinations a secret and went our separate ways.

When I got on the tram, I happened to get on a new green tram. It was specially manufactured in green to run in the refreshing Cheongnyangni area during the spring and summer. It was being test-run or used to advertise "A new tram has come out." Whether it's the doors on the platforms that open and close, the lack of doors at the entrances to the tram cars, the different lights, or the slightly different seats, it looks different in every way. Maybe it was because it was a new tram, but there were so many passengers that I got my feet stepped on four or five times as I passed Seoul Station.

When I arrived at Seoul Station, it was exactly 1:15 PM! There were few passengers and no train whistles. It was very quiet. It's not busy except at 7 AM to 10 AM and 7 PM to 11 PM when express trains depart. The next train is at 2:10 PM, bound for Incheon. Is there any reason for there to be many passengers? "It's boring!" I thought and went from the third-class waiting room to the first- and second-class waiting room. There were only three Japanese people and two Koreans sitting there, just staring blankly. "Let's go to Bongnae Bridge!" I thought and left the station entrance. A police officer at the police box was calling over two people who looked like villagers and questioning them. What kind of incident is it? I stood nearby and tried to listen, but the police officer slammed the door shut and signaled with his eyes to "Go away." It didn't seem right to stand there for long, and there weren't many people, so I just turned around. Ah, the gate of Seoul! The complexity of Bongnae Bridge! It's really easy for country bumpkins to get disoriented, and even smart people have to be careful of their feet. As soon as I walked, I counted thirty-four wooden posts on the bridge. Forty-seven freight wagons passed by in five minutes. How complicated it is. Among them, coal and charcoal wagons are the most numerous, white rice and Manchurian millet wagons are the second most numerous, and tangerine wagons are the third most numerous. On the south side of the bridge are Seoul Station, the Customs Branch, and various transportation companies, and on the north side of the bridge is a coal storage facility. Domestic and foreign daily necessities all gather here and are unloaded here. I grabbed a wagon driver in front of the customs office and asked, "What is the most common cargo these days?" He said, "Manchurian millet is the most common." I was surprised. "It's common in the countryside, but is it the most common even in Seoul? Even if I look at the rice stores in the city, there doesn't seem to be that much Manchurian millet..." But he clearly said that it was the most common. The next most common are white rice and fruits, and coal and charcoal are also quite common, but they are handled by other agencies. "How much do you earn per day?" He said that there is no fixed amount. If there is a lot of cargo, they can earn two to three or four won, but if there is no cargo, they just have to play. <33>

The wagons are not individual businesses, but are owned by customs or transportation companies. On days with a lot of cargo, they load, unload, push, and pull seven or eight times, even ten times, from morning to night, which makes them tired. But they say that the greatest comfort is receiving the agency fee slip, drinking three or four cups of "dachinomi nama" (立ち飲み生, draft beer while standing) with two or three friends. That must be it. I looked at the clock and it was about ten minutes before the hour. I turned around and counted the wagons going from the south back to the city, and about nineteen passed by in three minutes. A train carrying coal came in again. Dozens or hundreds of workers, looking like ash puppies from the chimney, were shoveling and loading coal. Just then, a tram bound for Dongdaemun came. "What a complicated world!" I thought and got on the tram. I happened to be sitting next to a beautiful woman and was inexplicably happy as I entered the gate. When I got to the company, it was ten minutes past the hour. Was it better than the dung splash last time? <112>

The Story of Transforming into an Unemployed Person and Going to Seek a Job, One Hour at the Personnel Consultation Center with Bloodshot Eyes

Smiling Student

Receiving an unexpected order from the editor-in-chief, I was suddenly called out. Since this was my first time, I lacked experience and confidence. Where should I go? Where should I go to get surprisingly fresh and excellent article material? After wandering around for a while, the Personnel Consultation Center suddenly came to mind. This is because I thought that in Seoul, a metropolis of Korea with at least 300,000 citizens, there are only two job placement offices, in Yongsan and Meiji-cho, so there must be all sorts of people and all kinds of job seekers. (The time is 1 PM!)

Mr. S and I parted ways in front of Yumeikan and went through the copper street past Dongyang Takushoku and entered Meiji-cho.

Almost reaching the Gogae, I saw a dilapidated two-story brick house on the left. This house is one of the only two Personnel Consultation Centers in Seoul.

Entering the main gate, the first thing that caught my eye was a sign on the left corridor: "Male Job Seekers Entrance" and "Female Job Seekers Entrance."

Without hesitation, I opened the door with the sign "Male Job Seekers Entrance" and went in. It was 1:12 PM! Contrary to what I had seen outside, the room was very small. A desk was placed across the room, occupying about half of the office. Men who had come to seek jobs were gathered around the desk in layers. They were mostly young men between the ages of eighteen or nineteen and twenty-six or twenty-seven. Some were wearing Western suits or Japanese clothes with caps, some were wearing Korean clothes with winter hats or fedoras, some were wearing student caps without badges, and some were wearing Japanese clothes with Japanese clogs barefoot. It was like people waiting for a train at a station. All sorts of people were gathered there, whispering among themselves. People who were just coming in were busy getting job applications and writing down their personal histories. There were three Japanese people in the office, and Koreans were bent over their desks, writing something diligently. <34> In the corner of the office, there were five women between the ages of twenty-four or twenty-five and forty or more, either with their arms crossed or with their chins resting on their hands, sitting on chairs. There were few women, so they seemed to be getting special treatment.

The phone rings. Jingle! Jingle!

As the phone rang, everyone turned their ears to that side. The room became quiet for a moment. The staff member answers the phone and writes something down. It must be a phone call from a Korean store looking for people. As soon as the phone call ends, the chatter starts up again.

"朝鮮人商店ジヤダメタ。 You have to know someone to get a job!" A young man of about twenty, wearing Japanese clothes and a cap and Japanese clogs, said with a frown, mixing Japanese and Korean. "ソウデスネ! Actually, it's better to go to a Japanese store to get a job than a Korean store. Knowing someone is secondary, you have to have money," said a man of about twenty-four or twenty-five, wearing a winter hat and Korean clothes, also mixing Japanese.

"Japanese people are like that, but not all of them are like that," said someone else in perfect Japanese. Speaking Japanese seems to be a great boast here and a necessary weapon for getting a job.

Just then, a young woman who looked to be about twenty-eight or twenty-nine years old, either a wife or a woman, was sobbing and talking to a staff member in the office. I thought something had happened and squeezed through the layers of people and listened. I couldn't hear clearly because of the men's chatter, but

"How did you leave the place you were working before? I'm so speechless. Can such a thing happen in the world? I was working at that Japanese person's house for seven months and the Japanese couple praised me a lot for my work. But recently, I caught a cold in this terrible cold weather. Even though I didn't want to, I forced myself to get up and couldn't cook breakfast and dinner on time for a day or two, and I didn't clean the house properly. The Japanese couple didn't like that, and yesterday evening they picked on me for not making charcoal properly in the brazier, and the man cursed at me in all sorts of ways. Not satisfied with that, he pushed me so hard that I fell with my waist on the door sill. But I'm just a poor wretch who works as a servant in someone else's house because I don't have any money, so I just lamented my fate and didn't say anything. I just got up and was about to light the fire again, so I picked up the brazier and went to the kitchen. This time, the mistress chased me to the kitchen and said, 『빠가 단신이 안해서 좃소』and snatched the brazier away. I still apologized, saying, "I was wrong," and took out charcoal from the charcoal bag and put it in the brazier. She dumped the charcoal bag all over my head, saying, "I told you to stop, why didn't you listen?" I had to endure it because my mouth is my enemy, so I forced myself to endure my anger and resentment, and I didn't cook breakfast this morning, right? So, this morning, she said that I put too much tea in and brewed it too much, and she cursed at me again and poured the hot tea on my body. So, how can I live with that abuse and that sorrow even for a day? So, after thinking about it, I decided to leave, even if it means starving to death. But after leaving, I thought about my young children and my old mother starving at home, so I had no choice but to come back to look for a job." She wiped away her tears.

"There are terrible people in the world," I thought and clenched my fist. The staff member seemed to sympathize with the woman and asked,

"Do you have a husband?"

"If I had a husband, why would I be going through this? My husband is already dead."

"Then how old are you and how many children do you have?"

"I'm twenty-eight years old and I have two children, a ten-year-old son and an eight-year-old daughter."

"Where is your hometown and what is your name?"

"My hometown is Yongin and my name is Lim Bo-seong."

"Then that's it. There's a house looking for a maid, so I'll introduce you to them," he said and stood up to look through the job postings and wrote a letter of introduction to a person named Ono in 00-cho (I found out later).

I was silently happy that the unfortunate woman was getting a job and was about to slip through the crowd of people and come back this way when I heard a voice right next to me,

"Hey, what if we don't get a job today?"

"I don't know. If we don't get a job today, we can't go back to that house and we'll have to go down to Yangju, but I'm worried." I turned my head and saw an adult of about thirty years old and a young boy of about fifteen or sixteen years old worrying about what would happen if they didn't get a job today. I quickly grabbed the boy and kindly asked him what was going on.

"My hometown is Cheongyang, Chungcheong Province. I graduated from the local ordinary school last spring, but my family is poor, so I couldn't even think about going to a higher school. I lived with my old parents and farmed other people's land to barely make a living, but in the summer, there was a flood and all the crops I had farmed were ruined. In the fall, both of my parents died of illness, so I had no choice but to come to my sister's house in Yangju. But my sister is also struggling to make a living, so I thought about it and came to this consultation center last October. I told them my situation and asked them to introduce me to somewhere, and fortunately, they introduced me to a Japanese general store in Yeongnak-cho. But misfortune always follows me. I got sick everywhere I went. So, the owner said,

"Go home and get treatment for your illness and come back." So, I went back to Yangju and was sick until recently, but I've recovered now, so I came back to Seoul and went to Yeongnak-cho where I used to be, but that store has already hired someone else. So, what can I do? So, I'm here at this consultation center looking for a job, but I can't get one easily, so I'm worried," he said with tears in his eyes.

I felt infinitely sorry for him. When I looked at the clock, it was exactly two o'clock.

"It's time for the announcement!" People who had been standing apart were gathering towards the office. I wondered what was going on. Every day at 11 AM and 2 PM, they announce the job requests they have received by phone or letter to the general public and select and introduce people who meet the conditions requested by the people who are looking for workers.

About five minutes after the hour, a Korean staff member stood up and declared the announcement in fluent Japanese, "今カラ公開致シマス" (We will now begin the announcement). The room became as quiet as a mouse.

"Sowon Store, clerk, age 18 to 24, ordinary school graduate, must have a reliable guarantor in Seoul!" As soon as he finished speaking, four or five people raised their hands, saying "私シ" (I). The staff member looked at the people who raised their hands and asked the youngest-looking and most intelligent-looking boy about his guarantor and the school he graduated from. He seemed to be satisfied and immediately allowed the introduction and told him to come forward and write his personal history on the job application. From this, it seemed that even if you had written your job application first, you couldn't get a job easily unless you caught the staff member's eye. I found out later that he was an eighteen-year-old boy named Ma Seon who lived in Goyang-gun.

In the same way, a boy named Jeong Hak-su, also eighteen years old from Anseong, was introduced to Nagano Confectionery, and a boy named Lee Shin-seong, seventeen years old from Gwangju, was introduced as a server to the Shikishima Hall Inn. And they introduce women all the time. While I was there, three or four women were introduced as maids or nannies to Japanese people's houses.

In this way, hundreds of people gather at this Personnel Consultation Center every day, but on average, only about ten to twenty people, both men and women, get introduced.

They say that women get placed easily regardless of age, but men get placed easily if they are between the ages of seventeen or eighteen and twenty, but it is very difficult for men over the age of twenty to get a job. And now, unlike in the past, even if you are working as a servant in someone else's house, you need to have a certain level of education, such as graduating from ordinary school or completing one or two years of higher ordinary school.

And they say that the most painful thing for job seekers is that even if they meet all the other qualifications, they cannot be hired if they do not have a reliable guarantor.

So, even among those who are introduced, those with specialized skills or a certain level of education earn about forty or fifty won per month, but this is rare. Most of them are store clerks or delivery people, earning twenty-five won at most, usually twenty won or less, or fifteen won. The lowest-paid are young children around twelve or thirteen years old who go to Japanese people's houses to take care of children, earning three to four won or five to six won. On the other hand, women earn thirteen or fourteen won if they earn a lot, and eight or nine won if they earn less. However, in contrast, there are many job openings for Japanese people, but few job seekers, so if they get introduced, they earn about 50% to 70% more than Koreans.

This alone proves how miserable the lives of Koreans are compared to Japanese people.

When I looked at the clock, it was 18 minutes past two. As I was about to run out, the sight of the unfortunate boy from Yangju was so heartbreaking that I couldn't move my feet.

But I couldn't stay any longer, so I left the consultation center, leaving behind the boy who was still waiting, hoping for luck, even though the announcement time was over.

Ah, poor boy! Was he lucky enough to get a job after I left? Or was he unlucky enough to not get a job and left for Yangju on this cold day

漢城銀行을 것만할고, 韓一銀行 돈구경

松雀

익크-또 동원령이 나렷구나. 『꼭 한시간동안에 아모데나 마음대로 가서 움즉이는 京城의 片影을 그리여 오라』하니 이번은 또 어듸로 간단 말이냐. 街路로 가자니 이 치운 날에 다니는 사람도 적으니 문제가 별로 업슬 터이오 재판소는 전번에 한번 가 보왓스닛가 되푸리 할 수도 업구나-에-라 더도 말할 것 업다- 진소위 입만 구푼데 병아리 궁덩이만 쫏차 다녀도 좃타고 주머니 속도 너무 겁 뿐하니 돈 만흔 은행가튼데 가서 돈냄새라도 한번 맛터보겟다 하고 뚝-떠서 鍾路를 통과하야 은행, 회사의 집중지라 할 만한 南大門通으로 향하얏다.
그중에도 朝鮮人 경영은행으로(외국인출자가 만흔 것은 별문제)<26> 자본금이 제일만코(자본금삼백만원) 귀족 냄새나는 漢城銀行을 좀 가보자 하고 단도직입으로 문을 쑥-드러스니 한쪽으로는 금전거래에 관계도 업는 놈이 은행에 드러 서는 것이 점즉하기도 하고 또 시기가 시기인 까닭에 경계하는 이목이 번다한 판에 잘못 서슴다가는 수상지 人으로 몰리여서 대창피나 당하지 안이할가 하는 생각이 업지안타. 그러나 이왕 문에 드러선 이상에는 그 行내의 광경이나 좀 보자 하고 휘휘 둘러보니 거래관계로 온 사람은 이십일인대 其中 支那人이 십삼인이나 되는 것은 놀나운 일이다. 이 은행의 이름이 漢城銀行이지만은 漢族銀行이 안인가 하는 생각이 낫섯다. 그대로 우둑하니 잇기는 승겁기도 하야 바로 예금이나 만히 한드시 하고 예금인출령수증 한장을 뚝-뜨덧서 비위조케 一金壹千圓也라 하고 서명날인한 후에 이리저리로 도라다니니 멋도 모르는 수위는 내가 돈 찻는 곳을 알지 못하는 줄만 알고 예금계는 저곳이고 수납계는 이곳이라고 친절히 가르처 준다. 그러나 나는 시침이를 뚝-떼고 저편으로 도라다니는데 到處에 逢故人이라고 이곳에도 아는 사람이 띳이 잇는것을 발견하얏다. 東洋書院의 閔大鎬군도 오고 天道敎會의 桂淵集군도 오고 校洞八珍屋의朴敬泰군도 오고, 普成專門學校의 小使, 中央高普의 小使도 왓다. 내가 만일에 실수한 일이 잇슬것 가트면 이 사람들에게 들킬번 하얏다. 하고 한편으로 인사도 하며 한편으로 그때의 行內광경을 살피엿다. 그러나 까닭도 업시 돈찻는 사람이나 돈맥기는 사람의 성명과 금액을 조사하기는 어려웟다. 슷부르게 수납계 엽헤 잇서서 남의 돈 가저가는 것을 기웃거리는 것은 의심 밧기 쉬운 일이다. 그것도 여의할 수가 엄섯다. 다만 안것은 엇던 상투 짠 시골사람이 金永完이라 하는 이의 액면 백원금의 切手를 가지고 돈 차질줄을 알지 못하야 갈팡질팡하는 것과 中央校小使의 同校직원의 예금하는 것만 보왓다.(그것도 액수미상) 익크 이러다가는 제한잇는 시간에 시간만 공비하겟다 하고 수박 것 할는 격으로 그만 발길을 돌이엿다. 문을 나오면서 이번이야 실패다 실패다 하고 혼자 중얼대다가 다시 생각하기를 은행은 다 일반이니 어듸 韓一銀行이나 가 보자 하고 다시 그 은행으로 드러섯다. 집이 신건축인 까닭에 집도 韓銀보다 조흔 것은 물론이고 각 설비도 상당히 설비되엿스며 내객도 상당히 만타. 그러나 그것도 역시 누가 얼마 예금을 하고 얼마를 차저 가는지 알 수가 업섯다. 문틈으로 잠간 드려다 보니 지배인 李恒鍾씨의 金빗 가튼 얼골이 선 듯 뵈인다. 올타-되얏다. 江頭之事는 問於江頭之人이라고 그가 그 은행의 지배인이닛가 고때고때의 상황을 잘 알 것은 물론이고 나와 친분도 잇는 터이닛가 직접 무러보는 것이 조켓다 하고 급사에게 名刺를 통하고 면회를 청하얏다. 그는 나를 드러오라고 傳請한다.
나는 그의 잇는 자리 엽흐로 가니 동행의 任肯淳, 趙鋼夏, 趙南稷군이 또 보인다. 그러나 아모리 아는 사람이 만타하야도 行의 비밀신용 또는 고객의 비밀신용<27> 관계상 그때에 누가 예금을 얼마나 하고 인출을 얼마나 하얏느냐고 물을 용긔도 업고 또 대답도 안이 할 것을 미리 짐작한 나는 다만 그때에 (오후 한시로 同 삼십분까지)예금자는 천원이상이 팔인인대 최고가 오천원이요(성명은 역시 비밀 不啓) 인출자는 천원이상 육인인대 최고가 삼천원인바 결국 인출액보다는 예금액이 만흔것만 알엇다. 이것은 월요에 항상 잇는 현상이라한다.(그와 반대로 토요일은 인출자가 多함) 이러는 판에 시간은 벌서 정각이 되야 그만 뛰여 나왓다. 이것이 실패라 할지, 성공이라 할지 이번 탐방에 취득은 이뿐이다.

야릇한 빗졸림, 독갑이 불구경

波影

이번에도 나는 갓갑고 편한 곳을 골라서 漢城圖書의 인쇄소를 차저 가는데 공교하게도 安洞마루턱에서 00일보의 X씨(미안하야 氏名은 숨긴다) 맛낫다. 『지금 뵈우려 가는 길인데 잘 맛낫슴니다』하고 약속원고독촉을 꺼내는 것은 맛치 대금업자가 피해 다니는 빗장이를 만나서 하는투와 족금도 틀니지 안는다.
『이거 넘어 참 미안함니다. 또 못썻슴니다. 제발 이번에는 용서해 주시지요. 한 일주일 후면 틈이 나겟스니 그때에나 하나 써드리지요』 나는 애걸애걸 연기해 주기를 빌엇다. 원고거리를 엇으려 가는 길에서 원고빗에 졸리우고 섯다니... 요 다음에 내가 그에게 원고를 구걸할 때가 잇겟스닛가 딱 잘러 거절도 못하고... 길거리에서 졸니우는 꼴이야... 피차에 기자란 직업이 편편한 짓이라고는 못할 것이다. 성화낫다. 두시까지 꼭 한시간동안의 탐방에 조흔 기사를 엇어내야겟는데 X씨가 용이히 노아주지 안는다. 『좌우간 이따가 맛나십시다. 지금 좀 밧븜니다』 하닛가 『네. 어데로 가시는지 갓치 가면서 이약이 하지요』하면서 오늘 저녁으로 꼭 써 내라고 졸라대인다. 이럿케 승강을 하고 잇다가는 길에서 한시간이 다 업서지겟다 십어서 잇다가는 또 엇잿던지 당장에 도망을 해야겟다고 『네 네 네 네 저녁에 써 보내드리지요』 하닛가 『네 꼭 밋겟슴니다. 저녁에 어데 게실터임닛가』고 또 안놋는다. 『네 네 밤중까지 社에 잇겟슴니다』 『그럼 저녁에 社로 가겟슴니다』 간신히 방면을 엇엇다.
느저서 안도엿다고 급한 거름으로 安國洞 네거리로 거러 나가닛가 전차 종점 근처의 同德女學校 판장 압 큰 길에서 잘 목도리 대인 외투입은 중산모자 신사 한분과 인력거군 한분이 말다툼을 하고 잇다. 『이것도 기사거리』하고 반가히 달려가서 들어보노란즉 이거야말로<28> 앗가 내가 졸리든 원고빗보다도 더 야릇한 빗을 졸리고 잇다. 『오늘은 주서야 합니다. 전당 잡힐것이 업서서 인력거 겟도까지 잡혀먹고 버리를 못할 지경이야요.』 『이 넓은 세상에 누구를 못 속히서서 저갓흔 인력거꾼을 다 속히 심닛가. 오늘은 단 반의 반이라도 주서야 합니다』 그러케 점잔케 생긴 중산모 신사가 인력거빗을 진 모양이니 적지 아니 뻔뻔한 친구이다. 그래도 남이 붓그러운지 얼골이 甘紅露(소주)가티 밝-애저 가지고 『이 사람아. 로상에서 이게 무슨 짓인가. 잇다가 집으로 오라는데 왜 듯지 안코 이래여- 집으로 오게. 집으로 오면 더러라도 줌세』하면서 同德學校 담엽 좁은 길로 빠저 나가려고 애를 쓴다. 인력거군은 신사의 외투소매를 잡엇다. 『댁으로 갓다가 허탕을 치기도 하도 여러번이니까 인제는 넘어짓-처서 실혀요』 소리가 놉하 갈스룩 신사의 甘紅露빗이 점점 깃허진다. 『무슨 빗을 못저서 우리가티 불상한 인생을 굼겨 가면서 인력거 갑을 십팔원씩이나 지워요? 나으리 가튼 이는 십팔원이 하로 저녁술갑도 못되지만 우리가튼 인생에게는 한달량식이야요. 한달-』 사람이 작고 꼬여드닛가 甘紅露선생 참말 견딜 수 가 업던지 목소리까지 애걸하는듯 십은 불상한 소리로 『가세 가-. 못밋겟스면 지금 가티 가세. 집에 가서 돈을 줄 것이니...』하고 살살 살살 팔자 수염을 채신업시 까불면서 계집 달내듯 달내여 가지고는 좁다란 골목으로 가 버리고 말엇다. 대톄 야릇한 빗이지 무엇 때문에 돈업는 인력거를 타고 도라 다녓스며 몃번이나 탓스면 십팔원이나 되엿느냐 말이다. 그러고도 타고 다닐대에는 큰길이 오히려 좁다고 곤댓짓을 하엿겟지... 세상에는 참말로 별별 별의 별것이 다-움즉이고 잇다.

아초의 목적처인 인쇄소에는 들어 갓스나 아모 기사거리도 엇지 못하고 도로 나려오러 할 때에 큰길거리로서 들려오는 놀나운 소리... 號外? 아니다. 소방대의 자동차소리! 『익크 불낫다! 불낫다!』하고 수선히 유리창 엽흐로 몰려온 틈에 끼여 내다 보닛가 바로 고압헤 눈압히라 하야도 조코 코압히라 하여도 조흘 갓가운 압에 소방자동차가 와서 소방부가 우루루 몰려 나리고 또 종로 쪽에서도 1대의 自動자전거가 그야말로 비조가티 날러 오더니 역시 거긔에 웃둑서자 섯기보다도 사람이 먼저<29>뛰여 나린다.

무한히 동정을 하면서 시계를 처다보니 꼭 두시다.
『인제 공개할 시간이 되엿군!』하며 따로 떠러저 잇던 사람들이 제각기 사무실 쪽으로 몰려가기에 왼일인가 하엿더니 날마다 오전 열한시와 오후 두시만 되면 의레히 전화로 또는 서신으로 구인 부탁바든 것을 일반에게 공개하야 구인하는 사람편에서 요구하는 조건에 합당한 사람을 선출소개하는 법이란다.

정각보다는 한 오분이 지난 후에 과연 朝鮮人 계원 하나가 벌덕 닐어 나더니 숙련한 日本말로 『今カラ公開致シマス』하고 공개를 선언한다. 실내는 쥐 죽은 듯이 고요해진다.
『瑞源商店 점원 나희는 십팔세로 이십사세까지 보통학교 졸업정도 京城에 상당한 보증인이 잇는 사람!』하고 말이 끗나닛가
『私シ』 『私シ』하고 사오인이 각기 자기를 보내달라고 손을 든다. 계원은 손드는 사람을 이리저리 들너보더니 그 중에서 제일 나희가 어린 듯 하고 외양이 똑똑해 보이는 소년에게 보증인을 뭇고 졸업한 학교를 뭇더니 그만하면 합당하다는 듯이 즉시 소개허락을 하고 불너 듸려서 구직표에 이력을 쓰라는 모양이다. 이것을 보니 아모리 구직표를 먼저 써드렷드라도 계원의 눈에 들지 안는 사람은 얼른 구직이 안되는 듯 하다. 나종에 알고보니 그는 高陽郡에 사는 馬善이라는 십팔세된 소년이다.
이와가튼 형식으로 長野菓子店에는 安城에 고향을 둔 鄭學壽라는 역시 십팔세된 소년이 소개되고 또 敷島館이<37>라는 여관에 급사로 멀리 光州에서 올라온 李新星이라는 십칠세된 소년이 소개되엿다. 그리고 부인들은 무시로 소개하는데 내가 가 잇는 동안에도 日人의 집 여하인으로 또는 유모로 소개되여 간 분이 삼사인 이나 된다.
이러케하야 이 人事相談所에는 각처에서 모여드는 사람이 하로에도 수백명식이나 되것만 평균 소개되기는 남녀 합하야 십여명 내지 이십명 밧게는 안된다 한다.
그것도 女子이면 나희를 불문하고 씨워지지만 남자는 대개 십칠팔세로부터 이십세 이내는 만히 씨우나 이십세 이상이면 한번 구직되기가 대단히 어렵다 한다. 그리고 지금은  녜전과 달라서 남의 집 하인노릇을 하드라도 보통학교졸업 내지 고등보통학교 일이년 급수업 정도의 학식을 가진 사람을 요하게 된단다.
그리고 구직하는 사람의 제일 쓸아린 고통이 다른 것에는 죄다 합격이 된다 하드라도 보증인이 상당치 안으면 채용될 수가 엄단다.
그래 소개되여간 이 중에도 전문적 기술이 잇던지 상당한 학식만 잇스면 월 사오십원 밧기는 예사이나 이것은 퍽 드믈고 대개는 상점점원배달로 만흐면 이십오원 보통이면 이십원 이내 십오원이고 아조 적은 것은-열두서너살 된 유년으로 日人의 집에 가서 어린애 업어주는것-삼사원 내지 오육원이라 하며 부인은 만흐면 먹고도 십삼사원 적으면 팔구원이라 한다. 그러나 이와 반대로 日本人은 구인은 만흐나 구직하는 사람이 드믈어서 소개되여 가기만 하면 朝鮮人에게서보다 약 오할 내지 육칠할이나 만흔 수입을 엇게 된단다.
이것만 보아도 우리 朝鮮사람의 생활이 日本사람에 비하야 얼마나 비참한 경우에 잇다는 것을 잘 증명하고 잇다.

그러나 지체할 수도 업는 처지라 공개시간은 넘엇스나 그래도 요행을 바라고 기다리고 잇는 그 소년을 남겨노코 상담소의 문을 나섯다.
