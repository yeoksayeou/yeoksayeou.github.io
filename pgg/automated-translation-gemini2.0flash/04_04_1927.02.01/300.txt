What Kind of Script?: To the Local Youth Association Brothers, Regarding the Characteristics of Rural Drama
						李慶孫 (I Gyeongsuk)

I am writing this letter to the entertainment department brothers of the Local Youth Association, and I want to talk about rural drama and its characteristics with a friendly heart.

Some of you may be disappointed that the title of my article is not more formal, like "The Rural Drama Movement" or "Regarding Rural Drama," but lately, I somehow feel like the face of a fire brigade stroking its beard.

Therefore, this article is not a speech on "What is Drama?", "Drama and Life," or "Rural Areas and Drama," but rather similar to an answer to your frequent letters asking, "Do you have any scripts written?" or "What kind of script should we put on?"

"What kind of script? Of course..." Some of you may come forward with Lenin's Reader, but what I want to say is different from that.

"Hate the landlords!"

No. That's not what I'm saying, so please read to the end.

Looking at the plays that come out in Seoul magazines a few times a year, or the plays that young people who like literature show while touring the provinces, they all say, "The current Korean scripts are" - "long" and "tasteless," and then they leave.

"Long"

"Tasteless"

This may sound like looking for spicy, bitter, and sweet seasonings in a place where there is no food, but it is by no means a light matter.

Suppose you take a script published in a Seoul magazine and circulate advertisements saying, "This was written by the famous XX in Korea," "This is a performance by the local youth," "This is a meaningful public event sponsored by the three major newspaper bureaus here," "The proceeds from this play will be donated to XX Academy," and you explain the meaning of the play with a long speech before the curtain rises, and then you hold the public performance.

"Ah - sleepy!" <96>

"Don't make noise!"

"He he he he"

"Who's the kid laughing!"

So, let's say you get through one night without incident, and the next night you go to the theater to see a performance by a Shinpa theater troupe that came to your hometown.

You will be angry when you see the audience, who looked like people who had taken medicine the previous evening, now jumping for joy and acting like Chunhyang's mother.

"My hometown people are so pitiful."

"But that doesn't mean our public performance last night was unsuccessful. I know how Ibsen felt on the night The Doll's House was first performed!"

Suddenly, the applause seems to tear through the ceiling of the theater again. On stage, two fools with strangely drawn eyebrows come out and do utterly foolish things.

"Oh, pitiful people!"

Let's say you can't stand it and run out of the theater.

However, I cannot agree with your theory of drama. That doesn't mean that Shinpa plays are better. However, you must consider what the current Korean plays published in magazines "lack" and what Shinpa plays "have."

"Taste!"

You can call it "taste," or "truth," or "leisure," or "peasant-like themes and peasant-like humor" - no matter what you call it, it doesn't matter. Anyway, Korean plays lack it. "Why is it missing?" I know the reason.

Suppose you and a friend are sitting together and arguing about tomorrow's weather.

"I still believe the sun will be shining."

"Don't talk nonsense. Even if it rains, it won't be just for a day or two, it will be a monsoon."

"Monsoon? It's so hot that I'll need an umbrella."

Like the first work of a period transitioning from Romanticism to Naturalism, or the first work transitioning from Naturalism to Expressionism - or the first brushstrokes of painters who first realized a new depiction - there is always "exaggeration."

You keep looking for only "Lenin," "Lenin," and you end up making the "taste" of the work as "tasteless" as Lenin's ignorant face. And in order to shout "A new era has come!", there is no "exaggeration" like a country bumpkin hearing the school bell for the first time.

"Exaggeration."

I am not trying to talk about the subtle story of which school of depiction the "taste" contained in a single word in writing a play belongs to. Also, I don't know it as well as others. I am going to talk about things that are more easily visible, like tragedy, comedy, and jokes.

For your hometown people who don't know what "new drama" is, for your brothers who don't know what "Lenin" is,

"Don't laugh! Why are you laughing! Haven't you seen my sullen, solemn face?!"

"It's a little bitter, but eat it as medicine!"

"If there's a word in the dialogue you don't know, come to the Youth Association's entertainment department tomorrow and ask, and don't make a fuss!"

I know very well that you are going through such hardships. But it is too stiff a sermon. It is too pitiful a marriage certificate. The bride and groom look like they have been dragged before the Six Codes. Everyone at the wedding should be happy and joyful together. Suppose you saw the bride secretly smiling. Suppose you saw the groom's hand hesitating and then gently touching the bride's hand. Suppose you saw everyone at the wedding discovering it, rejoicing, and wearing the faces of people drunk on happy old dreams. Do you know that "taste?"

Don't miss that "taste."

When drawing "life," don't miss that "taste."

When you write a script, you will decide on the roles of "landlord," "tenant farmer," "his daughter," "pure young man" - and you will set up the plot. Of course, it should be as easy to understand as an elementary school reader, and it may even become a play. But it would be a big problem if it were as tasteless as a court clerk's shorthand record.

Also, even if you find "taste," you shouldn't try to return to old expressions because there are no "tasty" events. For example, trying to bring in descriptions like "Over there, look at that" to show a new taste with expressions like "Old man, old man, the old man next door" is the worst thing to do. Expression and description will absolutely <98> evolve, and will never go back to the old days. The thick, rough, and ignorant lines of modern futurism are different from the cave carvings of ancient barbarians.

Whenever I go to the provinces, I always find one or two friends who misunderstand and yearn for "the classics."

Whether you make the audience laugh or cry is up to you. However, we must always have a taste that makes a village or a group of people rejoice and grieve together, like the old Korean play "Talnoreum" (탈노름) and the new Russian plays of today.

Because of that, it's okay if someone laughs that Korean plays are worse than middle school fancy dress parades. It doesn't matter if someone sheds tears because the stage design or the film industry, which is showing things as if they were printed from a mold, has disappeared.

I said to Mr. Park, who was next to me, looking at the hundred or so scripts piled up in the To Wol-hoe (土月會) office.

"Among them, the pearl-like beauties are 'Sansŏ Nangdang (산서낭당)', 'Sirhŏ Jangga Deulgi (장가들기 실혀)', 'Woryoil (月曜日)'."

In my opinion, what you should do first is "imitation" only, and you should leave "facts" that are nothing, and you should do more research on "farce" and "fairy tale drama."

And don't be afraid of the theater or any of the traditional rules, but be joyful and uninhibited like the sounds of weeding in the rice paddies, and concentrate only on the effect.

If the feeling of back-and-forth between the performers and the audience is lukewarm, like a conversation in front of the police station's "reception desk," it's nothing.

I'm afraid I haven't said everything I wanted to say because I'm busy, but it seems that reading one script is always better than a hundred theories.

It would be good if you read Kume Masao's script "Jizō Kyō Yurai (地藏敎由來)" and To Wol-hoe's "Sansŏ Nangdang (산서낭당)" once. On the 13th day. <99>
