Article Title: 記者總出動 京城白晝暗行記 -- 第一回 『一時間社會探訪』 (Reporters Mobilized: Kyŏngsŏng Undercover Day Trip -- Part 1, "A One-Hour Social Exploration")
Author: 松雀, 春坡, 石溪, 波影, 磯岩
Article Type: 소식 (News/Report)

**A Record of Undercover Reporting in Broad Daylight in Great Kyŏngsŏng, All Reporters Mobilized**

Society moves. It moves moment by moment. "Capture and gather the moving phenomena of Great Kyŏngsŏng!" The mobilization order was issued! A unit of reporters scattered into five teams.
The time: November 15th, from 2 PM to 3 PM.
Reporters: Songch'ak (松雀), Ch'unp'a (春坡), Sŏkkye (石溪), P'ayŏng (波影), Isoam (磯岩)

**The Troublesome Courthouse**
*Just let me feed him lunch*

In the old days, Chang Fei's (張飛) military orders lasted three days, but our editorial office's military order is for just one hour. And even this hour was strictly limited to between 2 and 3 PM. Wondering where to go and what to investigate, I hesitated for a long while, then muttered to myself, "Alright, the courthouse is always full of stories and people, so let's go there." Like a post horse bolting from Ŭiju, I shot out of the company gate and headed straight for the courthouse without looking back. The area in front of the courthouse, always bustling with people, was bustling as usual. However, inside the courthouse proper, there were few people because it was the afternoon. In the general waiting area, there were only two Japanese men selling stamps and bread, one moneylender who came to file a foreclosure application, and some country folk summoned as witnesses for a forestry lawsuit. In the lawyers' waiting area, only two Korean legal brokers (朝鮮人賣法師) were present, having taken off their sock-like legal caps (法帽) beside them, leisurely sitting and playing Go, the clicking sounds ringing out crisply. "My, why is this courthouse so deserted today?" I thought, and went to the front of the prosecutor's office reception desk. That place didn't seem too dull. A country woman wearing a headscarf and a gray jeogori was sitting facing the sunny spot, sniffling and crying. Next to her sat two men wearing gat (traditional Korean hats). The woman occasionally glanced towards the courthouse detention center and spoke to the men in a choked voice, "Uncle, isn't he coming out yet?" Although it felt awkward to speak to someone crying, I swallowed my shame for the sake of getting material for an article and approached the woman to ask why she was crying. Perhaps thinking I was some official, she looked at me and said, "Sir- please save a person- Aren't you a gentleman (兩班) who works at this courthouse? I'm from Korangp'o (高浪浦) in Changdan (長湍). My innocent fifteen-year-old son went to play at his friend's house, and while watching other kids play hwatu (flower cards) betting two pennies, he was caught by a policeman (巡査) and brought all the way to Seoul." "My son is in that house right now. Sir- please let him go today-" she pleaded. I told the woman, "Ma'am- I'm not a courthouse employee- But don't worry. If he is innocent, he won't be implicated- However, even if he didn't do it himself, watching gambling hwatu is also a crime. Whatever happens this time, make sure he doesn't go to such places again." The woman then said to me, "Even if you're not a courthouse gentleman, you must speak Japanese well. Please see that policeman and speak to him for me. Just ask him to let me feed my son lunch." Her words seemed both naive and pitiful, so I decided to give it a try, whether it would work or not. I approached the guard policeman (巡査) and told him, "That woman is the mother of a juvenile prisoner currently in the detention center. She has traveled hundreds of *ri* to buy her child a bowl of lunch, so how about it?" The policeman replied bluntly, "They are given lunch here too, so don't worry." Thinking it would be even more embarrassing to be refused flatly after just one word, although it seemed foolish, I pleaded again. The policeman then said, "I can't authorize it; speak to the Japanese policeman (日本巡査) inside." I went to the detention center gate and explained the situation again to the Japanese policeman. This Japanese policeman was relatively mild-mannered. He agreed in one word. I went back out and told the woman, "If you have money, buy anything and give it to him." The woman looked at the man beside her and said, "Please buy some rice cakes and give them to Ch'undori." During all this, an hour had already passed. Wondering if this was all the gain for today, I left the courthouse. The time was exactly 3:10 PM. -(Songch'ak) <13>

**Got Hit by a Poop Bomb**
*One Hour on the Street*

"A one-hour city exploration? This sounds interesting! But where should I go and what should I see? The train station? The library? No, the time isn't right. A wealthy family's house? A bank? A company? Right! Let's try finding the house of the rich Mr. Im (林富者) outside the East Gate (東大門). But with pistol incidents happening frequently, that feels a bit suspicious... First, let's observe the mood of the streets." Having decided this myself, I headed down the alley in Kyo-dong (校洞). Should I ride or walk? After pondering, I decided, "Heck, I'll walk there and ride back." It was a two-birds-one-stone plan: see the atmosphere on the street and the atmosphere inside the tram. Right away, I discovered three major incidents.

A water-spraying truck passed by and splashed muddy water onto the newly tailored, gleaming white durumagi (overcoat) of a country gentleman. Could there be a bigger incident? "Goodness gracious! Goodness gracious!" The sight of the country master turning around, worrying about the scolding he'd get from his wife, looked quite pitiful. Passing by the front of Tonggwan (東關), I happened to glance inside the main gate of a house. "Ah - Major Incident Erupts!" A rooster that was pecking rice grains off the *maru* (wooden floor), chased by a child, darted towards the *toenmaru* (narrow wooden porch) of the opposite room, and in doing so, knocked over a basket of red peppers placed on the *toenmaru*, sending it tumbling under the porch floor. Could there be another incident as major as this? The agitated movements of the lady of the house were quite a spectacle. A roasted chestnut seller's basket of chestnuts overturning was also a major incident, as was a cabbage seller having to take back cabbages he had luckily sold at a good price.

Wandering along like this, I passed Chae-gyo (再橋) near the East Gate. In a previously empty lot, forty or fifty people had gathered and were watching something with great interest. "Must be the Salvation Army (傳道軍) or a medicine peddler," I thought, intending to pass by indifferently. But because it was so bustling, I decided to take a quick look at the spectacle and squeezed through the crowd, rubbing shoulders. Inside, a fellow dressed in Western clothes with a modern ("high-collar") hairstyle and a decent appearance was holding forth quite eloquently. It was quite a sight.

I don't know what kind of medicine it was, but a whole spiel about poop came pouring out – dog poop, cow poop, chicken poop, rat poop, every kind of poop imaginable burst forth.
"Even poop, famously dirty and smelly in this world, can be medicine, so how much more... Now listen up! If your son or daughter, mother or father gets sick, don't go to the hospital or the pharmacy, just listen carefully to my words. Right – poop! Poop! Poop is also used in medicine, everyone. There's a curse, 'a child who couldn't even get a gourdful of poop to eat during a three-year epidemic.' Human poop is medicine for epidemics. Cow poop? Do you know what cow poop is medicine for? Black cow poop is medicine for when a baby gets stuck during birth (they say if you burn black cow poop and apply it below the pregnant mother's white navel, the baby will smoothly turn and come out). In summer, fresh cow poop is medicine for boils. Dog poop! Do you know what dog poop is medicine for? It's medicine for blood clots from falling off a tree! Pig poop? Do you know what pig poop is medicine for? It's medicine for women's urinary tract infections! Tiger poop! Tiger poop makes children live long if they eat it. Moth poop! Moth poop is medicine for poultices. Chicken poop? Chicken poop is medicine for drawing out the root of a boil. Rat poop? Rat poop is medicine for childhood convulsions! ... Now! Ahem..."
As he rained down this barrage of poop talk, the crowd of onlookers, the riff-raff, listened intently with mouths agape, as if their illnesses were already cured. I checked the time; only 20 minutes left until the deadline. "Just got hit by a poop bomb!" I thought, turned around, and dashed towards the house of the rich Mr. Im in Ch'angsin-dong (昌信洞), outside the East Gate.

The house of Rich Man Im! Eck, don't even mention it. In the west, Yun Taegari (尹대가리); in the center, Minister Min (閔大監); in the east, Rich Man Im (林富者) – these are the three most famous grand households in Seoul. Truly more magnificent than the Epang Palace (阿房宮), one wouldn't even dare to open their mouth judging solely by the exterior appearance. One can only stand with mouth agape, thinking, "Wow – how majestic!" Just like that, because it was so imposing, I couldn't even go inside and came back. What a pointless trip, right? Reader, but next time... -(Ch'unp'a)-

**What Are They Reading?**

*A One-Hour Bookstore Pilgrimage*

Humans have one mouth, but the sounds coming from that one mouth are so many. Suddenly, sounds of food, sounds of clothes, sounds of alcohol – countless sounds can be enumerated. Among them, the most precious sound is the sound of reading. This is because based on which sounds are many or few among reading sounds, one can guess the thoughts possessed by those people and the cultural level of that place.

So, I wanted to find out how much the reading fever has actually risen in our reading world, and what kind of texts are being read the most. On November 15th, as a chilly wind made the sounds of early winter, I used the one hour promised by the company, from 2 PM, to visit the famous large bookstores (冊肆) between Tapgol Park and An-guk dong (安國洞) to hear the news from the reading world. Everywhere I went, the facilities and displays were plausible, but it was quite disappointing that I couldn't actually witness any books being sold. I don't know what it was like before I arrived or after I left, but during the time I was there, from two to three o'clock, in every bookstore, one or two clerks guarding the books sat hunched over empty ledgers (帳薄) and idle abacuses, looking utterly bored, just waiting for time to pass. Occasionally, a student might come in, browse through the table of contents of a newly released magazine, and then leave without buying anything. A country gentleman might ask the price of a novel (小說冊) and turn away. There wasn't a single person buying even one book. Just seeing this, there was no way to know the news of the reading world. <15> So, I asked the bookstore owners what the average daily sales were and which books sold the best among them. Their answers were mostly similar (大同小異). They said they hadn't done statistics on average daily sales. As for the books that sell, in Seoul, magazines like *Ŏrini* (어린이), *Sinsonyŏn* (新少年), *Pyŏlgŏn'gon* (別乾坤), *Tonggwang* (東光), and standalone books like *Sarangŭi Sŏnmul* (사랑의 선물 - Gift of Love), *Segye Ilchu Tonghwajip* (世界一週 童話集 - World Trip Fairytale Collection), and works on Socialism (社會主義) are sought after often. For mail orders from the countryside, novels like *Ch'unhyangjŏn* (春香傳) and *Simch'ŏngjŏn* (沈淸傳) are the most popular (I myself witnessed 40,000 copies of Ch'unhyangjŏn being printed in one hour last month), and for use as textbooks in private village schools (私塾), the Four Books and Three Classics (四書三經) are sold in some quantity. What sense does it make that the bookstores are so deserted, and the only reading sounds to be heard are the dying sounds of Ch'unhyang's nose, this nose, that nose (춘향코 내코 한데 대코소리 - likely referring to rote, nasal recitation), the sound of old clams giving birth to new clams (무군 조개가 햇조개를 낫다는 소리 - nonsensical phrase, perhaps meaning outdated talk), or "Confucius said..." (公子曰), "Mencius said..." (孊�子曰)? Eating expensive rice and just spouting nonsense, what will become of us in the end? Friends who make books, friends who sell books, friends who read books, can't we somehow change our thinking? I wish we could make it so that new, precious sounds come from every mouth and new, precious sounds are heard by every ear. (Sŏkkye)

**One Hour at a Newspaper Company**
*Seeing the Society of a Day While Sitting*

Where could I go to get a good article with less effort? Clutching the collar of my overcoat, slipping out of the company gate amidst the crowd, I thought,
"Right! Instead of tiring myself out walking the streets, if I go to the nearby newspaper office and watch them typesetting the newspaper, it would be comfortable and result in an article worthy of praise." Having decided this in my heart, and fearing that my destination might be snatched by the group hesitating at the company gate, wondering "Where are you going?" and pointing north or south, I preemptively declared, "I'm going to the newspaper office," setting up my defense line (防禦線), and walked north, north.

It would be worth asking, "Everyone, do you know if there are any letters left on the nameplate of the Tong-A Ilbo Company (東亞日報社)?" The nameplate, battered by long winds and frost, with only faint traces of letters remaining, seems to embody the history of the Tong-A Ilbo all by itself. Whenever I see it, I think of it as one of the treasures of this building. I wonder what they plan to do with this treasure when they move to the newly built brick building...
I opened the door to the editing room, and it was the same as always. People were sitting amidst a mess overflowing with waste paper like a trash bin, diligently writing something that could become either waste paper or a manuscript.
Not only under the desks, but even the floor where people walk was white, covered in paper scraps. Books lay on top of the scraps, and people walked on the scraps. Scraps clung to the knees of the reporters' Western suits, and the desks where articles were being written were also white with scraps. However, seeing the reporters occasionally find one or two things from that messy pile of scraps to read or translate, it seemed that not everything on the desks was just waste paper.

I looked at the clock: 2:30 PM! It was indeed a busy time for a newspaper office, so it was also the time when waste paper production was at its peak.
Though it might be intrusive, I went closer to take a look. One person, unable to even sit properly, was perched awkwardly, <16> scribbling in handwriting that was nothing but manuscript, "Pistol-Wielding Youth Threatens Wealthy Man Late at Night." "Did it happen again?" I asked. "It happened in the countryside," he replied lightly, as if it were no big deal, already writing the 7th or 8th line of the article in the meantime. Over at that desk, someone hunched over wrote, "Broke into a Woman's Bedroom," and next to him, someone else was writing the startling news, "Over 700 Factories, Large and Small, Burned Down." "Where was such a big fire?" "Kyŏng'i." "Hey- Kwŏn Tong-a!" A sharp-looking boy took 3 or 4 sheets of manuscript paper and entered the printing plant (工場). The fire article just written was first passed to the plant to be set in type (活字).

Under the pillar with the clock, the man who had been hunched over writing something all this time suddenly lifted his head and, looking at the person sitting opposite him, asked, "Hey, how many lines did you write today?" This was the head of the city desk (社會部長). "I wrote 200 lines." "I only managed 80 lines." "Trouble! Mr. O, what are you writing now?" "The Salvation Army (救世軍) fight." "And you?" "How many lines will it be?" "About 70 lines, I guess." The department head scribbled something on a piece of paper, then suddenly stood up, "Big trouble! We're short 300 lines! 300 lines." Lately, there haven't been many incidents, so it seems they're short on news material. "Someone has to produce 300 more lines." "Mr. X, please write something more." "I don't have any ideas." "Haha, big trouble," he seemed to have lost his appetite. "Why not just run a big advertisement?" I teased. The department head chuckled, then asked, "Hey, how many lines for the Entertainment section (演藝欄)?" "80 lines today." "Anything more to write?" "Nothing." "No photos either? Fill it with photos." "Didn't prepare photos either. Let's put in the Football Association announcement (蹴球社告)." "We'll put that in, but still short. Well then, you write the 'Waste Bin' column (휴지통)." "Me? I don't have anything to write." "Write anything." Just then, the telephone rang, "Trring!" "Yes- Tong-A Ilbo," someone answered hastily. At a time like this when news material is scarce, they would be grateful for a call reporting some sudden incident. That's why, even when a friend comes and pats them on the back, they can't even turn around, busy writing manuscripts, but when the phone rings, "Trring," they drop everything and lunge for it, saying, "Yes- Tong-A Ilbo here..." ... .
They couldn't get any news material from the phone call either. The entertainment reporter wrote "Waste Bin" (휴지통) on the manuscript paper and sat scratching his head, <17> wondering, "What should I write?" Over in the Economics Department (經濟部), perhaps they had much to write but couldn't finish it all, as someone was shouting into the phone with one hand while writing a manuscript with the other. "Lunch is here!" Only then were bowls of *ttŏkkuk* (rice cake soup) placed here and there on the desks, on top of the waste paper. "Heck, I'll eat and write," said one person. I watched him eat *ttŏkkuk*, perched awkwardly without fully sitting down. A moment later, I saw that he had already put down his spoon and, without even wiping his mouth, was writing a manuscript again. It was truly a busy office. The 'Waste Bin' reporter was still just scratching his head, while from one corner, the sound of reading aloud, like reciting Mencius (孟子), arose loudly. Someone was reading aloud about Chang Tso-lin (張作霖) being something and Tientsin politicians (天津政客) being something; it seemed they were proofreading foreign news dispatches (外電記事). The 'Waste Bin' reporter, still scratching his head, finally wrote a couple of lines. Wondering what it was, I looked, and it seemed to be a sarcastic remark about the Salvation Army fight. Then, thinking, "Ah, forget those pitiful people," he tore up the manuscript and threw it off the desk. Another sheet of waste paper was added.
"Should I tear into the Kyŏngsŏng Municipal Council election?" The fountain pen took a nosedive; it seemed the council candidates were intimidating.
After seeing him write the 'Waste Bin' column, I saw the department head enter the printing plant; it looked like they were arranging the printing plates (판). Knowing it would be disruptive during their busy work, I followed him in.

Don't be alarmed. Although the afternoon sun at 3 PM was shining brightly outside, here it was another world; electric lights were on. In the low, narrow old Korean building (朝鮮古屋), stacks of type plates stood densely packed; it felt like a dark basement (地下室), and indeed, if the lights went out, you probably couldn't see your own hand in front of your face.
Looking at the arranged plates, the manuscripts that were just being written in the editing room were already neatly set in type (活字), waiting for the department head's instructions on whether they would be placed at the top or bottom.
Articles like "Praise for Hometown: Famous Products of My Town" (鄕土禮讚 『내 고을 명물』) and the front page's "The Way for the Rebirth of the Korean Nation" (朝鮮民族 更生의 道), which were not daily news, sat settled in place alongside business advertisements (商界의 광고), which also seemed fitting.
"Isn't that whirring sound the rotary press (輪轉機)? Are they printing something already before the plates are even fully set?"
"The appendix pages for the children's section and the regional section are typeset and printed in advance."
"Haha - Sneaky?" "Sneaky or not, we're short 300 lines, it's a big problem. Society is quiet these days, so there's nothing to report."
Oops, 3:15 PM! I was 15 minutes past the time set by the office, so I had to go back. I left without getting to see the rotary press. By then, Tong-A delivery boys clad in *happi* coats were already gathering in twos and threes.
-(P'ayŏng)-

**A Seventy or Eighty Thousand Story Castle in the Sky**
*At the Magnificent Chongno Intersection*

The clock struck two PM, ding! A chilly wind was rolling the withered, fallen ginkgo leaves across the ground like playing marbles, but thankfully, the sunlight was warm.
Absently turning the corner at Chae-dong (齋洞) intersection and passing by An-dong Pyŏlgung (安洞別宮), I saw seven or eight carts loaded with firewood bundles of pine branches and fallen leaves, belonging to farmers from the countryside. They were standing aside by the palace wall, waiting for buyers, eyeing the passersby. <18>
In the wide plaza at the An-dong (安洞) intersection, Mr. P, who had been walking with me, parted ways. It seemed Mr. P, with his portly figure swinging his cherry-wood cane (사구라 단장), was heading into the Hwa-dong alley, probably to get unique material from the Tong-A Ilbo office.
"Anyway, let's go out to the Chongno intersection (鐘路네거리) where many people pass by. Something is bound to catch my eye," I thought. Just as I was about to enter Sindong Alley (신동골목), bump! I ran into the hand of Mr. I, who had said last evening he was heading from Kongju (公州) to Wŏnsan (元山) with his family. "Oops, this is trouble!" I thought, but perhaps my flustered look gave me away, as he agreed to meet after dinner.
Letting out a quick breath, I swiftly circled the area where the Chongno market (鐘路市場) stalls were laid out, almost as if gliding. Huh - bundles of Chinese cabbage, radish heads, bright red chili peppers (안고초 열매), fresh green water parsley (미나리) and mustard greens (갓), rough green onion roots (파뿌리), clusters of ginger roots (생강알), shaggy strands of seaweed (청각 줄거리), round garlic heads (마눌 대가리) - piled up like mountains as if urging you to prepare your winter food, each displaying its own color under the crisp autumn sun.
Quickly surveying the market, I went down a little further and bumped into Mr. M, one of the mobilized reporters, coming up with the hem of his durumagi dragging slightly, grinning "Heh-". His expression already indicated he was heading towards the market stalls... ... .
This here is indeed the Chongno intersection of Seoul, bustling and chaotic, as if the streets might burst with people. Among them, what quickly catches the eye are the country gentlemen (시골량반) wearing crumpled black *paengnip* hats (백립) – just looking at them makes you feel a cold wind, bringing a chill to the heart – their topknots tied with *manggeon* (網巾) headbands seem to provide some protection against the cold, dressed in wind-blown white clothes! No matter how you look at them, they are clearly descendants of the old *yangban* (량반) who ruined Chosŏn – yet, if called *yangban*, they puff up with pride, acting as if performing some great virtuous deed even without knowing where their next meal of gruel is coming from; clearly, they are arrogant peddlers of genealogies (族譜꾼). These are truly, among the idle eaters, the nation-ruining, high-class delinquents (高等浮浪者).
And on the wide, wide street, laborers (勞動者) – completely oblivious to what's what, with gaunt bodies and pale faces, working history on the streets just to earn enough rice (밥버리) to barely survive the day – are digging earth, erecting utility poles (電線ㅅ대), breaking paving stones (罪惡돌 - likely typo for 碎石돌, crushed stones?), repairing roads, working so busily their eyes spin, unable to even glance away for a moment. There are even those dipping their reddish-purple feet, numbed by yesterday evening's icy water, into the drains to repair the sewage system (下水道). Red steel utility poles are starting to be newly erected at the Chongno intersection. They look quite tall. Seeing them, a thought suddenly occurs.
Right in the middle of this Chongno intersection, build an aerial pavilion (空中樓閣) of seventy or eighty stories – no, if possible, seventy or eighty *thousand* stories – and at the very top, install a bell (쇠북) whose single toll would shake the entire world and a lighthouse (燈臺) whose single beam would brightly illuminate the entire globe. Sitting atop it, I would want to investigate the lives of all humans – the accomplished and the incompetent, the useful women and the useless women (쓸년 못쓸년) – teeming like maggots in an anthill (고자 리 끌틋 - possibly 'like ants dragging larvae'), doing both worthy and unworthy deeds, useful and useless things. Then, first, I would want to wake the foolish masses, deep in slumber in the dark night, from their profound dreams with a single strike of the bell. If so, the social scenes from this one-hour exploration could also be observed from that building, and all the sights within the capital city of Seoul would appear clearly at a glance... ... .
Ah! But that's a dream. A dream.
While doing this, I took out my watch; exactly one minute past three PM...
Oops, that's it. I saw many other things, but I can't write about them. Strictly for the one hour duration -(Isoam)- <19>

**Summary**

Five reporters from *Pyŏlgŏn'gon* magazine were dispatched for one hour (2-3 PM on Nov 15, 1926) to observe and report on the "moving phenomena" of Kyŏngsŏng (colonial Seoul). Songch'ak visited the courthouse, encountering a desperate mother whose son was detained for watching gambling, and navigated bureaucratic hurdles to get him lunch. Ch'unp'a wandered the streets near Tongdaemun, observing minor "incidents," listening to a medicine peddler's bizarre lecture on the medicinal uses of various animal feces, and briefly viewing the imposing mansion of a wealthy man. Sŏkkye toured bookstores around Tapgol Park and An-guk dong, finding them deserted except for clerks and browsers, noting that while popular fiction and classics sold by order, current intellectual engagement seemed low. P'ayŏng visited the chaotic editorial office of the Tong-A Ilbo, witnessing the frantic process of news gathering, writing, editing, and dealing with article shortages under deadline pressure. Isoam observed the bustling Chongno intersection, contrasting the idle "yangban" descendants with hardworking laborers, and mused about building a fantastical observation tower to survey all of society.

This article provides a valuable multi-faceted snapshot of colonial Kyŏngsŏng life through the eyes of journalists. Historians might find particular interest in the candid descriptions of the justice system's impact on ordinary people, the stark social contrasts between the poor, the wealthy elite, and traditional figures, the commentary on reading habits and the state of publishing, the detailed glimpse into the operations and pressures within a major Korean newspaper office under Japanese rule, and the reporters' blend of detached observation, social critique, and imaginative reflection.
