Notable Figures of Kyŏngsŏng, A Collection of Anecdotes
						The Observer

Mr. Ch'oe Rin tapers his Korean trousers like Western trousers, not tucking them in, while Mr. Yun Ch'iho wears his Western trousers wide like Korean trousers, tucking them in. Ms. Paek Namgyu of Tongdŏk Girls' High School wears Korean trousers without tucking them in, and on top of the trousers, she wears long socks like leggings in the old days. Is it all up to individual preference (多任意)? A good contrast among the three.

Mr. Pak Sŭngbin, the president of Posŏng College, always wears a Korean durumagi (Korean overcoat) and sits with his knees bent at home. His pen name is Hakp'am. Don't assume he's just acting like an old scholar because he always sits that way. The reason is that he usually doesn't wear Korean trousers but just a Korean durumagi over Western underwear, so he sits with his knees bent when meeting guests, fearing it might be impolite, just like Japanese women do to cover themselves. And he also always spits phlegm and swallows it back, which is either a habit or an old-fashioned health practice, which his friends find puzzling.

After Mr. Yi Sangjae, who used to wear a horsehair hat (南바구우) with a Chungshan cap and leggings (行纏), passed away, Mr. Yu Sŏngjun alone carried on the tradition, wearing leggings. Recently, Mr. Myŏng Chese of the Product Promotion Society also started wearing leggings, earning the nickname "Leggings the Third." In an era where many wear Western-style gaiters (脚絆), wearing old-fashioned Korean leggings indirectly promotes local products, but it would be even more thorough if he wore a Korean dopo (Korean overcoat) as well.

Mr. Yu Ch'int'ae of the Korean Education Association was a proponent of the "Outerwear Ruin the Nation" theory (外套亡國論). No matter how cold the winter, he never wears an overcoat and always advises his close friends to abolish outerwear. However, a gossiper says that even though he doesn't wear an overcoat, he wore an Inp'annyesŭ (입반녜스, likely a Western-style vest or waistcoat) until the New Year and frequented Song's mansion - well, let's not dwell on the past.

Mr. Ch'oe Namsŏn, also known as Yuktang, who was famous for wearing a dung-colored hat and mituri (hemp shoes), now wears a very neat fedora and economic shoes (經濟靴) instead of convenient shoes (便利靴). Is clothing also subject to changing with the times?

Mr. Chŏng Taehyŏn, the principal of Posŏng High School, smokes so much that his fingers are stained bright like a marble (瑪瑠) mouthpiece. Students lament that if they could only collect the cigarette butts he's burned through when they're having trouble with tuition, they wouldn't have to worry about high school for a semester. <74>

Mr. Ha Yunsil, a professor at the same school, enjoys eating beef honeycomb tripe (쇠천엽). Because he married the female writer Kim Illyŏp last year, his friends tease him by asking which is tastier, Kim Illyŏp or beef honeycomb tripe (金=쇠=牛, Kim sounds like the word for metal or cow). Haha...

Speaking of Posŏng High School, Mr. Cho Namhŭi, who used to be a clerk at the same school and is now working at Tongyang Academy, was so famously fat that he would be angry if he were said to be second in Seoul. Whenever he goes somewhere, everyone is speechless and just giggles. Once, a student he was teaching saw him and started giggling again, so Mr. Cho Namhŭi, who was always good at telling jokes, said, "Hey kid, why are you giggling at me?" The student replied, "Not at all, sir. How could I giggle at you? 'Pig' (픽) is 'pig' in English." Cho just went red in the face and laughed it off.

Mr. Kwŏn Tŏkkyu of Chunang High School, who has various nicknames such as "Orange Teacher" and "Single Bath Teacher," was also a famous alcoholic. No matter how much of an alcoholic someone else is, they wouldn't dare enter a bar with only 5 chŏn, but he would always go to Hwang's ch'ut'ang (추탕, loach soup) restaurant every afternoon with 5 chŏn and have a drink. The restaurant gave him the new nickname "5-Chŏn Teacher." Even in the afternoon, Morning (午前=5錢) Teacher!

Mr. An Chaehong of the Chosŏn Ilbo originally had nasal congestion (鼻塞症), so he always made a "kingking" sound before saying anything and said "uh-uh" to anyone when answering the phone. He often wrote "An-bi-mok-gae (眼鼻莫開)" to mean he was busy (literally "eyes and nose cannot open"). A wit said, "Then you should change 'An-bi-mok-gae (眼鼻莫開)' to 'An-bi-mok-gae (安鼻莫開)'!"

Mr. Song Chin-u of the Tonga Ilbo blinks his eyes a lot when dealing with people. Once, someone visited him and was about to start a confidential conversation when an employee happened to pass by. When President Song blinked his eyes, the visitor assumed that he couldn't talk about such things while someone was passing by and stopped talking. After a while, he tried to speak again, but Song blinked again, so he stopped again. After waiting for a long time, he tried to speak again, but Song blinked again, so he couldn't say anything in the end and just left after a long time. Later, he found out that Song always blinked his eyes and burst out laughing, regretting his own misunderstanding.

Mr. Kim Kit'ŏn, the head of the Ch'ŏndogyo Youth Party, always has a runny nose because there's something wrong with his nose. Once, he stayed at an inn in Onjŏng-ni, Kŭmgangsan (Mt. Kŭmgang), and Mr. K from the Wŏnsan Youth Association happened to share a room with him. Dinner was served together, but Kim Kit'ŏn had a bad cold and his nose was running more than usual, so K felt embarrassed to eat together. He secretly called the innkeeper and ordered separate meals starting in the morning. Later, he learned that it was Kim Kit'ŏn, whom he had always admired and wanted to meet, so he no longer cared about his runny nose and even thought his farts smelled sweet. He called the innkeeper again and asked to have meals only with Kim.

Mr. Yi Tonhwa is suffering from a toothache these days but can't stop drinking because he's originally an alcoholic. A phrase is offered for him: "How can I refuse tooth alcohol (齒酒)?" <75>

These days, Mr. Pak Talsŏng of Ch'unp'a is winking his right eye, perhaps in preparation for picking spring greens. For ordinary people, it would be a sty, but for Mr. Kwangmyŏn, it should be called Kwangju (광주, a large container) rather than a sty.

Mr. Pak Sajik has a strong and unyielding personality, so he is called "Mr. Sŏebur-al (쇠불알, bull testicle)" among his colleagues. Once, his colleagues were eating beef soup at a restaurant and asked if Mr. Pak was there, but they mistakenly asked, "Is there any wurang (우랑) here?" The restaurant owner, who was good at selling meat, immediately replied, "Yes, we have wurang. How much should I bring?" Everyone burst out laughing.

Mr. Yi Iksang, the author of the novel "The Dance of Demons (魎魎의 亂舞)," resigned from his position as the head of the literary department at the Tonga Ilbo and transferred to the position of acting editor-in-chief at the Mae Sinbo. People in the literary world are very curious about whether another masterpiece like "The Dance of Demons" will appear in his new publication.

It is said that Mr. Ch'oe Haksong, Chŏng Inik, and Ch'oe Tokkyŏn, also from Chungoe, also transferred to the Mae Sinbo, but only Mr. Tokkyŏn returned to his company for some reason. Tokkyŏn = Tokkyŏn (獨遣, sent alone).

Mr. Kim P'alp'ong found Kyŏngsŏng too noisy and went to Yiwŏn Chaho Port (利原遮湖港), a scenic spot on the northeast coast, and enjoyed fishermen's songs for a while. Recently, he returned to Kyŏngsŏng and took the position of the social affairs editor at the Chungoe Ilbo. Readers of the Chungoe Ilbo, don't rashly ask questions if something is incorrectly written in the social affairs section. If that giant social affairs editor just stands there, you won't even be able to speak because of the intimidation. Indeed, he is the largest among the social affairs editors of all companies.

Ms. Kim Malbong, a female reporter at the Chungoe Ilbo, lives alone in a house in Kantong (諫洞). She was seen personally buying firewood at the Songhyŏn-dong (松峴洞) charcoal yard the other day, working very hard. It's not a problem that she buys firewood herself, but if she also splits it herself, it's concerning that her delicate hands might get calluses and that the pen she uses every day might be shaken.

Ms. Ch'oe Ŭisun, a female reporter at the Tonga Ilbo, was planning to re-elect her second president (her eldest son, whom she always calls "president" out of love) for several months, but the term (任期=姙期=未滿, term=pregnancy=incomplete) expired and she unfortunately lost the election. Due to that, a revolutionary revolt broke out in her body, and she was evacuated to the hospital for a while. These days, peace has been restored, and she goes to work every day. May those who lose their way in Ŭn-nŭng (陰陵) not despair. They must turn back to Kangdong and raise their troops again.

Speaking of female reporters at the Tonga Ilbo, Ms. Yi Hyŏnhyang, who used to be a female reporter at the company, had been out of touch for a while, but these days, I heard that she is in Shanghai with her husband, Mr. An Kwangch'ŏn, and recently had a child.

One rainy day, Ms. Kim Hwallan of Ihwa College was walking along Chŏng-dong (貞洞) street with an umbrella when a student followed behind her and said, "Ah, I wish I could share that umbrella." The American-style Ms. Kim turned around briskly and said, "If that's your wish, then let's share this umbrella." The student's face turned red, and he couldn't say anything and just ran away. If you asked for an umbrella and got permission, you should have walked together. Why did you run away after making up your mind? Alas, clumsy student!

Ms. Im Sunbun of the Girls' High School, although a woman, is so tall and lively that she should naturally love tigers. However, in reality, she is completely different and especially loves dogs. She always buys a bottle of milk for her dog when she goes home. Her elderly mother scolds her for it, saying, "Oh, you're giving that precious milk to the dog? I'll eat it instead," and gulps it down. <76>

Ms. Yu Kakhyang of the Christian Women's Youth Association was walking somewhere when some male and female students behind her, thinking she was a young female student because she was small, made some insulting remarks. She turned around and said, "Hey you! Don't you know you're talking to someone who's old enough to be your grandmother?" The students were so embarrassed that they ran away with their tails between their legs.

Mr. Sin Hŭngnyang takes a walk in Sajik Park (社稷公園) every morning. Having been influenced by American customs, he used to take his unmarried daughter with him. One morning, some delinquent male students followed them and made fun of them, saying, "Some people are so lucky that they get to take a walk with their wife from the crack of dawn." He was so dumbfounded that he didn't say anything and came back home. He was greatly indignant at the students' disorderly conduct and has since taken walks alone.

There was a sad rumor that Ms. Cho Wŏnsuk, who was actively involved in the women's movement as a member of the Women's Friendship Society (女性同友會) and Kŭnhwoe (槿友會), returned to her hometown of Yangyang (襄陽) from Shanghai last winter and became a victim of disease. It turns out that she is not dead at all but is healthy and has even given birth to a jade-like child. It was her younger sister, Ms. Cho Okhwa, who died. Brothers and sisters are like one body, so the misreport is perhaps not surprising.

Ms. Sŏng Ŭigyŏng, who was famous as a jokester teacher at Sukmyŏng Girls' High School, married Mr. Kim Tongsong, the Thousand-Li Horse (千里駒), last year and has been quiet since then. At a wedding reception the other day, the two attended together (同夫人안이 同夫君을 하고 오섯는데). It seemed that she was already expecting a Second Thousand-Li Horse or even a Ten-Thousand-Li Horse (乃至 萬里駒) (腹高於南山), so we wish her success.

Ms. Sim Ŭnsuk, who upheld the tradition of bobbed hair until the end at Kŭnhwoe, has not been seen lately. I heard from her acquaintance that she is studying at Hiroshima Girls' High School in Japan.

Everyone knows that Mr. Kwang Sindeok had a beautiful daughter last year, but the house he moved to is right next to Mr. Sŭng Kyŏngae's house (wife of Hyŏn Tŭgryŏng of Ihwa School), separated by only a paper wall, so they live as closely as if they were living in the same house. Since Mr. Sŭng has a cute young son, the two of them, who are good at joking, jokingly suggest that they become in-laws. When they call each other, they don't use their names but just call each other "In-law's House." When they see the children playing together, they say, "Oh, those kids are playing well as in-laws." It's also a funny story.

Mr. Yi Il, from Tongwŏn, used to exercise on the mountain behind Ch'uiunt'ing (翠雲亭) every morning, but he hasn't been seen for a while, so his friends who used to go with him were very curious. It turned out that his beloved wife was sick in bed, so he didn't have time to exercise because he was doing housework. If he said it was because his hands were chapped from cooking, he would be upset if people said it was because he used to exercise hard.

It was said that the female aviator Yi Chŏnghŭi was going to study dance with Ishii Baku (石井漠), but she couldn't do it as she wished and stopped. After being a dancer for a short time, she has changed a lot from her former aviator <77> days. She has bobbed her hair and wears a lot of makeup, and she doesn't go out much because she's studying something at home alone.

There is Mr. Sagong Hwan at Chungdong School and Mr. Toggogo Swan at Chungang Nursery School. New students who are not familiar with surnames don't know that their surnames are compound surnames and often call Mr. Sagong "Teacher Sa" and Mr. Toggogo "Teacher Tok." It is said that many teachers often burst out laughing because of this. If it's Teacher Tok, then some new female students might think, "This school must be rich to have a teacher who only teaches one person," because in the old days, during the Sŏdang (書堂) era, wealthy families would hire a teacher to teach only one person, and that teacher was called a Toksŏnsaeng (獨先生, exclusive teacher).

Mr. Min T'aewŏn's pen name at the Chungoe Ilbo is Ubo (牛步, cow's pace), Mr. Yŏm Sangsŏp's at the Chosŏn Ilbo is Kwangbo (廣步, wide pace), and Mr. Kim Chingu's at Yadam Society (野談社) is Hakbo (鶴步, crane's pace). By chance, all three gathered at one place and were talking about their pen names. The taciturn Mr. Ubo said, "I was the first to use a pen name with the character 'bo (步)' in Chosŏn, and then many people followed suit. But I'm still the originator, aren't I?" He subtly teased Kwangbo and Hakbo. Then, someone who was good at joking said to Ubo, "Who has a longer history of having the character 'bo (步)' in their name than anyone else in Chosŏn? The kalbo (蝎步, prostitute) has a longer history, so then the kalbo is the founding ancestor." The entire room burst out laughing.

It has been a few years since it was decided that after the annexation, the term "Korean" should be changed to "Chosŏn." However, a old-fashioned Japanese person, thinking that even personal surnames should be changed, sent a letter to Han Sangnyong of the Hansŏng Bank, addressing him as "Chosŏn Sangnyong," causing everyone to burst out laughing.

It is not well known that Mr. Pak P'alyang's pen name at the Chosŏn Ilbo is Kim Yŏsu. A man of letters from the countryside, who had always loved Kim Yŏsu's poems, came to Seoul and went to see Mr. Pak P'alyang, asking, "Where is Mr. Kim Yŏsu now?" Mr. Pak P'alyang, assuming he was talking to him, said, "He's still here." If the story ended there, there would be no problem, but the country friend insisted on being introduced to Mr. Kim Yŏsu. Only then did Mr. Pak realize that the country person had misunderstood and said, "Yes, I am Kim Yŏsu." The country person's eyes widened in surprise.

Ms. Yun Kŭn of Kŭnhwa Girls' School is a teacher at that school just by looking at her name. Mr. Ch'oe Kyudong of Chungdong School is also the principal, as the character "dong (東)" suggests. If Mr. Chŏng Inbo were to become a teacher at Posŏng High School, it would be a good comparison of three.

Yi Chonggŭn's big feet, Min Tan's half-and-half hair (南北머리), Yun Taegam of Ap'anggung (阿房宮主尹大監)’s big head, Cho Namhŭi’s fatness, Yu Sŏngjun’s short stature, and Cha Manjae of the Chosŏn Infantry's tall height are all talking points in Kyŏngsŏng. If these people were to gather in one place, it would be a considerable source of cartoons.

Mr. Ma Ch'unsik, who was a reporter for the Chungoe Ilbo, happened to go to Pusan the other day and sent letters to a few friends in Seoul, saying something similar to a will that he would commit suicide. He then disappeared, and everyone is very curious about what happened to him. If he were a heavenly horse (天馬), he would have drowned himself, but since he is a human horse (人馬), he must still be alive in this world.

Since this is a collection of a hundred people's stories (百人話集), there should be a hundred people. However, there are too many pages, so let's stop here and save it for another opportunity in the future. <78>

京城名流, 人物白話集
						觀相者
崔麟씨는 조선 바지를 양복바지처럼 아래통을 좁게하고 다님을 안이치고 다니며 尹致昊씨는 양복바지를 조선바지처럼 넓게 해입고 다님을 치고 다니는데 東德女高 白南奎씨는 조선바지에 다님을 안이치고는 바지 우에다 기다란 양말을 녯날에 행전치 덧신고 다닌다. 다님=多任意라 할가 세분이 好對照.

普成專門學校長 朴勝彬씨는 집에 잇슬때에 꼭 조선 두루막이만 입고 꿀어 안는다. 그의 號가 學帆이요. 또 끌기만한다고 녯날 학자님의 風으로 알어서는 良貝다. 알고보면 그는 일상 조선바지를 입지안코 양복 속내의에다 조선 두루맥이만 입고 잇는 까닭에 손님을 대하면 행여 실례나 할가하고 맛치 일본 여자들이 압흘 가리기 위하야 꿀어 안는 것과 갓치 꿀어 안는 것이다. 그리고 그는 또 항상 가래침을 딱 뱃터서 도로 샘키는데 그것은 그의 한 습관인지 또는 舊式養生法인지 잘 알 수가  업서서 그의 친구들의 한 의문거리가 된다고.

남바구우에 中山帽 그외에 또 행전(行纏)을 치고 다니던 李商在선생이 작고한 뒤로는 兪星濬씨가 홀로 계승하야 그 행전을 치고 다니더니 근래에는 物産獎勵會
明濟世씨가 또 행전을 치고 다녀서 행전 제3세의 별명을 어덧다.
外國風의 각반(脚絆)을 만히 치는 시대에 조선 舊風의 행전을 치는 것도 간접의 物産獎勵는 되거니와 기왕이면 조선의 도포까지 입고 다니는 것이 더 철저할듯.
