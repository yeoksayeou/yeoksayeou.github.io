The Lingering Shanghai Incident

The Shanghai Incident, caused by anti-Japanese sentiment in China from afar and the publication of disrespectful articles in the Minguk Ilbo (민국일보) and the killing of Japanese monks nearby, eventually led to Chinese and Japanese forces confronting each other. Shanghai, the so-called international city that had indulged in pleasure and boasted of its splendor, was inevitably transformed into a zone of turmoil, filled with screams amidst a rain of shells, and turned into a near-devastated place or a scene of carnage. As a result, the eyes of the world were focused on this place, watching its progress with sharp nerves. For a while, contrary to the expectations of many, an unexpected surge of valor was displayed, something rarely seen in the Chinese army. Cai Tingkai (채정해), leading the elite 19th Route Army of the so-called Guangdong Army, not only employed skillful tactics and offered staunch resistance but also received reinforcements from Chiang Kai-shek's direct guard army. This led to a significant increase in the * army's forces, causing the Japanese army to face considerable difficulties. They determined that the dispatched naval and ground forces alone were insufficient to protect Japanese residents. Therefore, Japan decided to send more troops to counter this, dispatching the 11th Division, which, along with the previously dispatched 9th Division, formed the Shanghai Expeditionary Force. General Shirokawa (白川) of the army was appointed to ***. A unit of the expeditionary force finally landed near Shanghai on the last day of February, and the military headquarters landed on March 1. After several days of fierce street fighting and aerial combat, the Japanese army eventually seized the Wusong Fortress and Wusong Battery due to their relentless attacks. In particular, the fierce battles of the Japanese air force led to the simultaneous bombing of the Chinese air force base in Hangzhou, abruptly shifting the balance of power in favor of the Japanese army. The Chinese army *** retreated towards the Jiangnan area. There were various rumors during that time, suggesting that the retreat of the Chinese army was a tactical maneuver or that Cai Tingkai (채정해) of the 19th Route Army retreated out of anger because Chiang Kai-shek promised reinforcements but failed to send them. In any case, the intense fighting subsided due to the retreat of the Chinese army, and on March 3, both the Japanese and Chinese armies issued a ceasefire order, ending the ceaseless sound of gunfire. This was due to the mediation of the four major powers—Great Britain, the United States, and France—who aimed to resolve the Sino-Japanese conflict through a roundtable conference in Shanghai. The League of Nations also held daily and nightly meetings to discuss the Sino-Japanese issue, and the American political scene showed extraordinary tension, ultimately leading to the realization of a ceasefire between the two armies.

However, the roundtable conference stalled because the claims of the two countries differed, each insisting on ** mobilizing troops before attending the conference. Japan eventually stated that it would withdraw its troops independently but would freely dispatch them again if necessary. However, the confrontational situation between the two countries remains, and it is not easy to predict in which direction its progress will go.

The Shanghai Incident and the League of Nations

Regarding the Shanghai Incident, let's examine how the League of Nations, which advocates for ** peaceful means of resolving international disputes ****, is responding and the general course of events.

As the turmoil in Shanghai began to *****, all countries around the world closely monitored its progress, and when Japan and ***dong finally began, especially the United Kingdom and the United States became even more tense, carefully observing the development of the situation. ***** In America, after meeting with the US Secretary of State *****, the UK and the US formally submitted ** Japei (閘北)**, a section of the Chinese city, to Japan. The formal protest was submitted based on the report of the Shanghai Anglo-American consul that military action was taken after Shanghai's mayor Oh Chul-sung accepted Japan's demands, arguing that the Japanese Marines used the joint concession as a base of operations, risking involving the concession in the war, and that the Japanese army's unilateral action violated the joint defense regulations, issuing a warning. Japan explained the situation in response. In particular, American public opinion was so critical of Japan that some even advocated for economic sanctions, and British public opinion was also very strong, with the idea of Anglo-American cooperation to manage the situation gaining traction. Only France quietly observed the situation, but the UK and the US ordered the deployment of their Eastern fleets and ground forces to Shanghai. At the same time, the British government proposed the establishment of a neutral zone to both China and Japan and encouraged the US to make the same proposal.

Then, on February 2, an emergency meeting of the League of Nations Council was convened. The British representative read a statement from the British government regarding the Shanghai situation and the Anglo-American response to Japan. Representatives from France, Italy, and Germany also declared their support for the Anglo-American actions. The council then decided to wait for reports from the Shanghai consular corps before discussing the League's actions. At a public council meeting on the 9th, after passionate speeches from the Japanese and Chinese representatives, the British representative expressed hope for a ceasefire, and Chairman Boncour recommended establishing a neutral zone with neutral country troops stationed there. As the first part of the consular corps' report was made public, the atmosphere of the council seemed to be developing favorably for Japan. However, on the 11th, the Chinese representative, in accordance with Article 15 of the League Covenant, initiated procedures for convening an emergency general assembly. The second report from the consular corps was very unfavorable to Japan, and despite the Japanese representative's denials, the atmosphere of the council deteriorated significantly, sending a strong warning only to Japan. Regarding the Chinese representative's request for an emergency general assembly, despite Japan's objections, it was eventually decided to convene an emergency general assembly as requested by the Chinese side, in accordance with Article 15 of the League Covenant. In the meantime, the Japanese representative's presentation of a resolution for resolving the Shanghai Incident to the British and French representatives suddenly reversed the tense atmosphere of the League. At a public council meeting, a resolution was presented by the chairman to first implement a ceasefire and then hold a roundtable conference in Shanghai, which was adopted. At that time, the Chinese representative announced the conditions for a Sino-Japanese ceasefire, advocating for the simultaneous withdrawal of troops from both countries, but the Japanese side did not agree and submitted its own ceasefire conditions.

On March 3, when the emergency general assembly was to be held, the report that the two countries had ceased fire arrived just before the meeting was to begin. At the meeting, the Chinese representative made a speech rejecting Japan's newly submitted ceasefire proposal, and the Japanese representative made a speech supporting it, causing considerable turmoil. Eventually, it was decided to adopt a recommendation and resolution on the ceasefire by unanimous vote. The Shanghai *** and a report were ＜9＞ previously ordered by the League of Nations Survey Commission led by the British Lord Lytton, who is currently being dispatched, to determine its attitude after seeing the results, and decided to extend the session of the General Assembly. It is said that the members of the League of Nations Survey Commission have just arrived in Japan, where they are receiving preferential treatment from various parties, before heading to China. It remains to be seen what kind of report they will produce, and the ceasefire conditions between Japan and China still differ, remaining unresolved. It is uncertain whether the temporarily subsided atmosphere in Shanghai will become tense again or whether a peaceful resolution will be reached given the current circumstances.

Continued Disarmament Conference

The question of "What is the Disarmament Conference?" was already published in the February issue of this magazine, so readers should have a rough idea of what it is. It is also hosted by the League of Nations and has been held in Geneva, the capital of Switzerland, since February 2. It is said to be a very grand affair, with over 60 countries participating and a scheduled duration of six months. Although it is irrelevant to us, it might be good to get a general idea of what they are doing.

On the first day, they appointed three commissioners to review the credentials, decide on the agenda, and draft petitions. At the plenary session on the 5th, they elected fourteen vice-chairmen. Then, the French delegation submitted an important new proposal, which was to thoroughly reduce armaments after ensuring reliable security through the League of Nations. This proposal caused a great deal of impact from various perspectives. Subsequently, there were speeches from representatives of various countries and general proposals, but the most notable was Germany's proposal for thorough disarmament. As a defeated nation, this was to be expected. From the 8th, they entered general discussions, ending a week of speeches from representatives of various countries on the general direction of disarmament. The speeches of the representatives of the major powers unanimously called for reducing offensive weapons. It was as if they were saying, "Don't attack, just fight." Next, disarmament proposals were submitted by the delegations of various countries, and the General Committee began its deliberations, starting with Russia's (로서아) bold disarmament plan. The plan received only two votes and was defeated. This was an inevitable result given the ulterior motives of the great powers. Next, a resolution submitted by Sir Simon of the United Kingdom was adopted, and the French delegation proposed establishing a political committee in addition to the existing four committees on land, sea, air, and national defense. Despite opposition from Germany and others, it was eventually decided to adopt the proposal. There is still plenty of time left, so who knows what kind of conclusion they will reach. In any case, the restaurant owners in Geneva are said to be making a lot of money.

General Election Results

When the Kenseikai (견양) cabinet was formed, it ordered the dissolution of the parliament during its session and held another election. This was the third general election in Japan since the implementation of the universal suffrage law. Among the 715 candidates who ran for the Diet, there were 352 from the Seiyūkai (청우회), 282 from the Minseitō (민정당), 28 from two factions of the proletarian party, 11 from the Andar faction, 12 others, and 31 independents. The total number of votes cast was 9,638,763, and 2,158,900 people did not vote, abandoning their right. By party, the number of votes was 5,719,460 for the Seiyūkai, 3,385,106 for the Minseitō, and 287,417 for the proletarian party. Ultimately, 466 people were elected, including 304 from the Seiyūkai, 147 from the Minseitō, 5 from the Andar faction, 2 from the Innovation Party, 5 from the Social Democratic Party, 3 independents, and 2 from the Labor-Farmer Party. The Minseitō had held an absolute majority of seats during the Minseitō cabinet, but when the Seiyūkai cabinet was formed, this power dynamic suddenly shifted, with the Seiyūkai holding more than twice as many seats, resulting in a victory for the government party. Among the newly elected Diet members, Ozaki Yukio (尾崎行雄), who has been elected without fail since the opening of the Japanese Diet, was also elected, even though he is currently traveling abroad. Park Chun-geum (박춘금), who has been working to promote Japan-Korea harmony, was elected in Tokyo, attracting attention in the news. (March 10) ＜10＞

그리자 긴급총회가 열릴 3월3일에는 양국의 정전하엿다는 보고가 바로 총회개최되기 전 시각에 도달하얏다. 그 회의에서는 중국대표가 일본의 새로 제출한 정전안을 거절한 연설과 일본대표의 그를 주장한 연설이 잇서 파란이 적지 안튼 중 마침내 정전에 관한 권고겸의안을 채택키로 되야 만장일치로 가결하고 상해에서의 잀•전공보경과보고를 ＜9＞ 일즉이 영국 릿튼 경을 위원장으로 하야 방금 파견중에 잇는 연맹대중조사위원회에 명하야 그 결과를 보아 가지고 태도를 결정하려고 총회회긔를 연장하기로 결정하엿다 하며 대중조사위원 일행은 방금 일본에 도착하야 각 방면의 우대를 바든 후 중국으로 향하야 떠나리라 하니 그들이 과연 어떠한 보고를 할지 그가 후일의 주목처이며 그 후 일중양국의 정전조건이 서로 배차되야 아직도 미해결 그대로 잇는 중이니 일시 가라안진 상해의 공긔도 다시 험악화할지 또는 화평해결이 될지 아진 형편으로는 알 수 업는 일이다.
