잡지명	別乾坤 제48호
발행일	1932년 02월 01일
기사제목	祕密家庭探訪記, 變裝記者=냉면配達夫가 되여서 (Secret Family Visit Report, Disguised Reporter = Becoming a Cold Noodle Delivery Man)
필자	夜光生
기사형태	세태비평 (Critique of Social Trends)

I hastily threw off my Western suit, which was scattered and crumpled in the corner of the room, and put on a pair of grubby Korean trousers and a jacket (which I had brought wrapped in a book bag). I borrowed a delivery man’s gloves and pulled a wool cap low over my ears. Then I stuck a bicycle light on one finger.

“This should do it!” I said, looking at myself in a small mirror. It was quite convincing. It might be an exaggeration to say I was unrecognizable, but it was true that no one who knew me would be able to tell who I was.

“Well, how is it?” I said, pushing open the sliding door and running out. The owner of the cold noodle shop and the delivery men burst out laughing.

“It’s good, it’s good… but I don’t know why you’re making such a fuss,” the owner said, worrying that something might have happened.

“Hey – don’t worry about it. Why are you like this?” I interrupted the owner, as if annoyed. Then I added, “Give me the next delivery.” I lit a cigarette.

Before I knew it, the clock struck eleven.

The phone rang, "Ring...ring."

“Yes, yes, that’s right. Two bowls of cold noodles to 24X Gwancheol-dong. Yes, yes.”

Before the owner finished taking the order, I jumped up. While the noodles were being made, I asked one of the delivery men about the directions to Gwancheol-dong.

I turned on the bicycle light. A red cover was placed on the cold noodle bowls, then a wooden board was placed on my shoulder, carrying two bowls, a pot of broth, two sets of sterilized chopsticks, and a bowl of kimchi. It felt strangely heavy. It was hard to bear.

“Okay – put it on like this, and hold one side with your hand,” the delivery man friend said, concerned, sharing his technique. Following his instructions, I tilted my head and firmly gripped one side of the board. It felt a bit more balanced, and I didn’t think I would spill it on the way. I excitedly rang the bicycle bell once in advance and got on the bicycle, but the owner came out and warned,

“Be careful. The ground is slippery. Something terrible will happen.”

“Don’t worry,” I said, leaving the alley.

As I entered the streetcar road, the wide road and open breeze made it easier to pick up speed, even with my unfamiliar driving skills. I carefully crossed the Jongno intersection and turned behind Jonggak (鐘閣). Half-listening to the record playing from a café, I turned again and entered the narrow alley of the Pyeongyangru (平壤樓) cold noodle shop. Trying to avoid a stream, I became disoriented and nearly ran into a telephone pole. A critical moment! I quickly turned the handlebars the other way, but it was already too late. One side of the wooden board crashed into the pole, and the cold noodle bowls slid backward.

“Ah, shoot!” I exclaimed, stopping the bicycle and taking off the board. Fortunately, the cold noodle bowls didn’t break, but the kimchi bowl had overturned, and about a third of the broth had spilled. Like someone gathering scattered embers, I desperately picked up the kimchi with my gloved hands, feeling terrible for the person who was going to eat it. But since it had already happened, I couldn’t do anything about it. So, I rearranged the dishes and found 24X address.

I raised the bicycle light to check the house number. I had found the right place. Judging from the large gate with a telephone number, it seemed like a wealthy household. I creaked open the middle gate and entered the courtyard, calling out,

“Cold noodles are here.” The sliding door to the main room opened, and a woman’s voice said,

“Bring it here.” I went to the edge of the porch and set down the board. The woman, who seemed to be the mistress of the house, pulled over a small table that had been prepared in the corner of the room and began to place the cold noodle bowls on it. The reporter stealthily peered into the room. The wide two-bay room was surrounded by silk screens. There were two mother-of-pearl inlaid Korean chests (朝鮮欌) and a tall Western wardrobe next to them, along with many other scattered decorations.

“Pour it into another bowl and give me back the bowl,” the man, who looked to be in his forties and seemed to be the master of the house, said to the woman, who looked to be in her thirties. The woman replied,

“It won’t taste as good if you move it to another bowl.” Then, while pouring the broth from the pot, she exclaimed,

“Oh, there’s so little broth. How are we supposed to eat with this?”

“Customers don’t usually have a lot of broth in the winter,” I said.

“Oh, what are you talking about? I’ve never seen such a cold noodle shop. Do they think we’re eating cold noodles for the first time?...Really!”

The woman slammed the sliding door shut and muttered, “What an idiot.” Although it was my fault for spilling the broth, I felt inexplicably unpleasant.

I sat on the edge of the porch for thirty minutes, feeling incredibly cold. The cold air rose up my buttocks, and I stood up and sat down four or five times. I looked around, hoping to find some good gossip, but nothing seemed worthwhile. Finally, the sliding door opened, and she said,

“Don’t stand there. Just go. Come back tomorrow morning to pick up the bowls and get paid…” and the door slammed shut.

“What a damn awful household. Making me freeze in this cold for thirty minutes…” I restrained myself from saying it out loud, thinking, “No, I shouldn’t interfere with their business.” I picked up the wooden board and hurried back to the cold noodle shop.

The owner, who seemed worried when he saw the reporter returning, asked,

“Why were you so late? I thought something must have happened.” The reporter sheepishly replied, “Not at all! I was waiting for thirty minutes,” and told them about the incident. The owner laughed and exclaimed,

“Those things happen often. You did a great job delivering it!”

This was my second delivery. Jae-dong X address. It was farther than the first one. Carrying two bowls of dumplings, one bowl of rice cake soup, and one bowl of cold noodles, I went up the Jae-dong alley against the chilling north wind. After entering the alley across from XX School, I saw a newly built tile-roofed house. I creaked open the oiled gate and entered the middle gate. Somehow, they seemed to know it was from the cold noodle shop, and the door to the room below the courtyard suddenly opened. The first thing I noticed was several pairs of men's shoes scattered at the end of the porch.

“Are they newly married couples?” I wondered, resting the wooden board on the porch. <17> When I looked into the room, I realized I was in big trouble. I had run into some prominent people. I was seized with fear that the disguised reporter’s identity would be revealed, but surprisingly, they didn’t seem to recognize me.

“You never know when they might find out!” I thought, looking at them carefully in the dim light. They were definitely Mr. OOO from XX Company, someone from XXX Company (I don’t know the name), and Ms. OOO, who works in the National Tax Service Investigation Division. Judging from the furnishings in the room, the owner of this room was definitely Ms. OOO, who had a round, pretty face. I stood there waiting to take back the empty bowls, while a nonsensical conversation blossomed in the warm room. What were they talking about? I felt disappointed that I had to stop listening because I had another delivery to make. A man gave me a one-won bill, and I gave him 40 jeon in change. I hurried back to headquarters, where the owner and delivery men praised me even more highly.

This was my third delivery. I tossed away my half-smoked cigarette and carried two bowls of dumplings. I went up the main street of Cheongjin-dong and turned into the alley of Lawyer Lee In’s (李仁) office. Then another alley appeared. I led my bicycle through several narrow alleys leading back to the stream, finally finding the house.

25X Cheongjin-dong. Compared to the squat houses on either side, this new house stood out. Two gentlemen in suits stood in front of the gate, staring intently at the reporter.

“Who are these guys?” I muttered to myself, passing through the gate and the middle gate into the courtyard. The inner courtyard was even more impressive. It was a newly built house with glass windows in the main hall (大廳마루) and a kitchen (饌間) facing the main room (안방) porch. It seemed like they had spared no expense.

“Did the dumplings get cold?” I called out, and an old woman came out from the main room, saying, “Yes.” Then, a young woman’s voice came from the main room.

“Grandma, put them in the lower room.” The old woman hurriedly shuffled toward the lower room, almost tripping over her rubber shoes.

“Okay – put them down here. Come back tomorrow morning to get paid.”

“Okay.”

I put the bowls on the porch and was about to leave with the empty wooden board when the old woman locked the gate as soon as I stepped out of the gate. The men in suits were still standing at the gate.

As I led my bicycle out of the alley, I inadvertently looked back at the suspicious men. What a bizarre sight met my eyes! The two men in suits were rummaging through the trash can in front of the house and then leaning against the telephone pole, trying to climb over the wall. One of them had already climbed onto the wall and was pulling the other one up. In a moment, they had climbed over the wall and disappeared. My heart pounded with inexplicable anxiety. I found myself drawn back to the front of the house. Shining my lantern, I looked at the house number again. The surname on the house number was rare in Korea, and the middle character was 啓 (Gye). X Gye X! It was the name of a woman I had heard before. Suddenly, I had an idea.

“That’s right. She’s the mistress of Assemblyman X’s (XXX의원) concubine.”

After about twenty minutes, I shook the gate. There was no answer from inside. I shook it harder. Finally, I heard the sound of rubber shoes shuffling, and the old woman opened the gate. Her eyes were wide, and she was gasping for breath. She stared at me for a moment and then said,

“Oh, you damn fool! Why are you shaking the gate like that without saying anything?”

“Why? I came to pick up the bowls.”

“I thought the master (주인 나-리) was coming.”

“Why would you be so surprised if the master comes, Grandma?”

Only then did the old woman seem relieved and, perhaps hoping to get some extra money, smiled slyly and muttered,

“Why wouldn’t I be? There’s another guest here.”

“Oh! The guest who jumped over the wall? How interesting.”

“Shh – don’t say anything. I’ll bring out the bowls.”

The old woman went inside and then came back with the bowls stacked together.

“Here’s 30 jeon, and this is for cigarettes.” In my palm, she placed 30 jeon and an extra 10 jeon.

“Thank you.” Before I could finish the sentence, the gate slammed shut. I put the 40 jeon in my pocket and hesitated for a moment before pressing my ear against the window of the lower room. There was no sound. Then, after a moment, a woman’s voice burst out for the first time.

“This is too much! My heart is so anxious, I can’t stand it.”

“That delivery boy – doesn’t he have any worries? There are two of us. If necessary…”

It was a deep, rough male voice. When he stopped at "if necessary," he might have raised his fist and shown it to the woman, indicating something unpleasant.

I thought about waiting a little longer, but it was getting late for deliveries, so I gave up.

“Damn thieving bastards!” I muttered and returned to headquarters, where it was already 12:40. “I’ll do one more delivery,” I said, sitting on the edge of the porch and lighting a cigarette.

This was my fourth delivery. X address, house number X at 1-jeongmok, Seodaemun (西大門정 1정목 19XX번지의 X호). A very tricky address. Going from the streetcar line toward the broadcasting station (放送局), I turned into the alley behind the Jeil Girls' High School (제일고등녀학교). <19> It was one of the few houses there. Shining my light, I looked at the house numbers on each house and entered through an ordinary house gate. It was a squat Japanese-style house, but it seemed like Korean people lived there. Could it be a company residence for XXXX Company?

Standing in the middle of the narrow courtyard, I called out,

“Rice cake soup is here.” There was silence, then a woman’s voice came from the lower room (?), sounding urgent.

“Wait a moment.”

For some reason, that voice sounded strange to my ears. Instinctively, the hairs on my head stood on end, and my heart pounded. Then, after two or three minutes, there was whispering in the room, and the door opened. A woman of about eighteen or nineteen years old came out, wearing only her underwear and with her hair down. At that moment, I quickly glanced at everything in the room. There was someone lying down with a blanket pulled up to their chin toward the lower end of the room. I was certain it was a man. Judging from the skirt and school uniform hanging on the wall, it was clear that this woman was a student at XX Girls’ School (XX녀학교). I felt a surge of jealousy and wanted to know everything about her. I couldn’t see any signs that she was living there independently in someone else's lower room. So, was that man her lover? It might seem like they were lovers, but the reporter’s so-called sixth sense wouldn’t allow it.

So, what was it? I was sorry for the person involved, but I could only call it “student prostitution.” (學生密賣淫)

The woman (who had a slender face that wasn’t entirely unpleasant) took the small table lying on the porch back into the room and then came out with 30 jeon.

“Come back tomorrow morning to get the bowl.”

“Okay.”

I glanced at the woman’s face again and left the house. The woman followed me out, locked the wooden door (Japanese-style door), and went back inside.

As I rode my bicycle back to headquarters, I thought about everything that had happened tonight. It was exhilarating to have experienced something I had never experienced before, but the female student lingered in my mind.

“Why did that young student become like that?” I thought, and my heart ached more and more.

On the way back to the cold noodle shop, I asked one of the delivery men.

“Yes, yes, you mean that house! It’s a flour house (밀가루얘 – a term for a prostitute or a house where prostitution occurs). There isn’t a night that men don’t gather there.” The delivery men all said the same thing.

I changed back into my suit, packed my clothes, thanked the owner and the delivery men, and left the shop. The streets were already deserted. Taxis carrying revelers for 80 jeon were speeding by.

“Should I have a cup of tea?” I wondered, looking at a café on the street, but it was already closed. Still, it seemed crowded with customers inside.

“Is it already past two o’clock?” I quickened my pace toward Anguk-dong (안국동) on the main street of Gyeonji-dong (견지동), and then I realized that the cold wind was blowing hard, and my neck and left arm were stiff. <20>

祕密家庭探訪記, 變裝記者=냉면配達夫가 되여서
						夜光生
한참 분산하게 양복을 버서 버트리고 방구석에서 굴러다니는 군때무든 조선 바지저고리를 (책보에 싸가지고 온) 박구어 입엇다. 배달부의 장갑을 빌려끼고 방한모(防寒帽)를 푹 눌러 귀까지 덥허 썻다. 그리고 자전거 전등을 한 손가락에 뀌여 들엇다.
『이만하면 되겟지!』 하고 조고마한 체경 압헤서서 내가 내 꼴을 바라보니 아닌게 아니라 능청맛다. 내가 낸 줄 모르리만치 변장이 되엿다면 과장일지는 모르나 사실 나아는 남이 나의 정체를 아라낼 수 업스리만콤 된 것은 사실일 것이다.
『자-엇덧소.』 하고 미다지 문을 열치고 뛰여나오니 냉면집 주인 배달부 할 것 업시 박장대소다.
『됏는데요. 돼서... 그런데 엇저자고 이 야단이신지 알 수 업군요.』 주인양반은 무슨 일이나 나지 안엇나해서 악가부터 하는 걱정이다.
『이-글세 염려마시라는데 웨 이러십니까.』 나는 됩데 귀찬은 듯이 주인의 말을 가로막어버렷다. 그리고 다시
『요 다음 차레를 날 주십시오.』 하고는 담배 한 개를 피여무럿다.
어느 틈에 시계가 열한시를 가르친다.
전화가 떼르...릉 하고 울린다.
『네-네 그럿습니다. 관철동 24X 번지 냉면 두 그릇이요. 네네.』
전화 밧는 주인의 분부가 나리기도 전에 나는 벌덕 이러낫다. 국수 누르는 동안에 배달부 한사람에게 관철동 지리를 무러 두엇다.
자전거 등불을 밝히엇다. 냉면 그릇에 붉은 곡갈을 씨워 두 그릇 장국 한 주전자 소독저 두 개 김치 한 그릇을 실은 목판을 억개 우에 올려노으니 웬일인지 묵직한 것 갓다. 그뿐 아니라 거북해 못 견듸겟다.
『자- 이러케 올려 노으시고 이 손을랑 한 구통이를 잡으서요.』 배달부 친구가 걱정이 되는지 그의 기술(技術)을 공개해준다. 나는 그의 가르키는 대로 고개를 갸웃둥 꾸부리고 한손으로 목판 한 구통이를 든든이 쥐고 보니 다소 중심(重心)이 취해지는 것 갓고 가다가 뒤집어 업흘 것 갓지는 안타. 신ㅅ바람이 나게 자전거 종을 미리 한번 울리고 나서 자전거에 거러안는데 주인이 나서서
『조심하십시요. 땅이 밋그러운데 큰일 나시리다.』 하고 경고한다.
『염녀맙쇼.』 한마듸를 남기고 골목을 나섯다.
전차 길거리를 나스니 길이 넓고 훠-ㄴ한 바람에 익숙지 못한 운전(運轉)솜시나마 속력노키에 매우 편하다. 종로 네거리를 조심조심 건너 종각(鐘閣)뒤로 꼬부러 드럿다. 카폐에서 흘러나오는 레코-드 소리를 듯는둥 마는둥 다시 꼬부러저 평양루 냉면집 좁은 골목을 속력을 나추어 드러스는데 쓰러질듯 말듯 개천을 피하느라고 어리둥하다가 압헤슨 전주(電柱)에 코끗이 다을 지경이 되엿다. 위기일발! 핸들을 얼른 다른 쪽으로 들렷스나 벌서 느젓다. 목판 한 구통이가 전주와 덜컥 부다치며 냉면 그릇이 뒤로 밋그러젓다.
『앗 차차.』 소리를 치며 자전거를 멈추고 목판을 내려노코 보니 천만다행으로 냉면 그릇은 깟닥업고 김치 그릇이 뒤업허젓고 장국 국물이 삼분일은 업지러저 버렷다. 업허진 화로불을 두 손으로 주어 담드시 응겁질에 장갑 낀 손으로 김치를 주어 담고 생각하니 장차 먹을 사람에게 여간 미안한게 아니다. 그러나 이미 저지러 노은 일 할 수 업는지라 그대로 그릇을 정돈하야 가지고 24X 번지를 차젓다.
자전거 등을 놉히 드러 문패를 보니 올케는 차젓다. 큼직한 대문에 전화번호까지 부튼 것을 보아 돈양이나 잇는 집 갓다. 중문을 삐걱 열고 안마당으로 드러스면서
『냉면 가저왓습니다.』 하고 소리를 치니 안방문 미다지 문이 열리며 녀자의 목소리가
『이리 가저오.』 한다. 퇴ㅅ마루 각가히 가서 목판을 내려 노앗다. 주인마님인 듯한 그 녀자는 방웃목에 미리 준비하야 두엇든 소반을 끄러 당겨다 노코 손수 냉면 그릇을 주섬주섬 집어다 놋는다. 긔자는 슬금슬금 방속을 기웃거렷다. 칸사리 넓은 2간장방에 비단방ㅅ장이 쫙 둘러처 잇다. 자개박은 조선ㅅ장(欌)이 두 개 그 엽흐로 키 큰 양복장이 한 개 그 외에도 너저분하게＜16＞ 치장해 노은 것이 만타.
『다른 그릇에다 쏫구 그릇을 내주구려.』 주인인 듯한 40먹은 남자가 30되여 보이는 그 녀자에게 일르니까 그 녀자는
『딴 그릇에 옴기면 맛이 업서요.』 한다. 그러더니 주전자의 장국을 따루다 말고
『아-니 국물이 요고 뿐이야. 이걸 가지고 엇떠케 먹으란 말이야.』 하고 탁 쏜다.
『겨울에는 손님들이 국물을 만이 안 치니깝쇼.』
『아-니 그건 무슨 소리야. 별놈의 냉면집도 다 봣네. 그럼 더 치운 겨울에는 국물업시 비벼먹고 말겟네... ...우리 집에서 냉면을 처음 먹어보는 줄 아나봐 참!』
녀자는 미다지를 탁 다드며 『참 멍텅구리』 한다. 국물 업지른 잘못은 내게 잇지만 엇전지 아니꼽살스럽다.
