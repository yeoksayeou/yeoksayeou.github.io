1930년 02월 01일
설만흔 朝鮮! 설업는 朝鮮!, 二重過歲廢止策 (Korea, Full of New Years! Korea, Without New Years!, A Plan to Abolish Double New Years Celebrations)
春坡 (Chunpa)
논설 (Essay)

When I count, it has only been three weeks since we celebrated Seol (설, Lunar New Year). Exactly three weeks and four days. I'm still wearing the clothes I wore on Seol, and one or two rolls of the straw mat I bought for Seol remain. I haven't been able to give proper New Year's greetings to everyone yet, and the bump on my forehead from falling while going to pay New Year's respects hasn't completely healed. Furthermore, the fatigue from staying up all night preparing the New Year's issue still lingers on my eyebrows, and the smell of alcohol from the New Year's party remains in my nostrils. The evidence of celebrating the New Year is clear and recent. This is not a dream but a clear fact.

Despite this, my children or wife, including the wife of the oil merchant, repeatedly propose and argue for celebrating Seol again, talking about buying tangi (댕기, a traditional Korean ribbon) and changing fabrics for new clothes, even discussing the fillings for mandu (만두, dumplings), so much so that they are dozing off from lack of sleep. I cannot understand why this is happening. We clearly celebrated the New Year, but they are celebrating it again in just over ten days. Is time suffering from acute rapid progression, or are people lost in a dream? It is truly incomprehensible.

I wondered if only my family was having this dream, so I looked around the neighborhood, and it was the same everywhere. In the house in front, the house behind, the big house, the small house, all around Bukbu (北部, northern part of Seoul) and most Korean villages are the same. It seems that every household throughout Joseon (朝鮮) is like that. So, children, old people, women, and husbands are all counting down the days to Seol and focusing on preparing new clothes for Seol. Some people don't even eat and nag while dozing off, and some people cry and plead. This is a sight that can only be seen in Joseon. No, it is a sight that also exists in China, Japan, or somewhere in Russia. However, it seems to be particularly prevalent in Joseon.

Whether it is fortunate or unfortunate is a separate issue, but there are many holidays in Joseon. There is Seolmyeongjeol (설명절, Lunar New Year), Boreumyeongjeol (보름명절, the Full Moon Festival), Hansikmyeongjeol (한식명절, Cold Food Festival), Samjilmyeongjeol (삼질명절, a spring festival on the third day of the third lunar month), Danoeomyeongjeol (단오명절, Dano Festival), Sambokchilseok (삼복칠석, the hottest days of summer and the Qixi Festival), Chuseokmyeongjeol (추석명절, the Harvest Festival), Guilmyeongjeol (구일명절, a fall festival on the ninth day of the ninth lunar month), and Dongjitsnal (동짓날, Winter Solstice). Adding them all up, there are quite a few national annual events. Among them, Seolmyeongjeol is particularly abundant, so what can be done? <76>

A smart friend might say, "There aren't even two Seols in a year, so why do you say there are many?"

But there are many in Joseon, so what can be done? That's why it's a problem. Listen to what I have to say.

There is the solar New Year, the lunar New Year, Joseon New Year, Japanese New Year, and foreign holidays, as well as Chinese holidays, Japanese holidays, commoner holidays, noble holidays, mountain holidays, women's holidays, children's holidays, old people's holidays, and even magpie Seol and mouse Seol.

How many are there? But this is just the name. No, even one is too many. It is clear that the lunar calendar, the solar calendar, and the Joseon holidays are four separate things. This four-fold concept is firmly ingrained in the heads of the Baekui people (白衣人, Koreans), men, women, young, and old, and has become a chronic problem. However, because the number of holidays is so large, the reality expressed on the holidays is almost non-existent.

In conclusion! There is no great holiday that the entire nation of present-day Joseon celebrates with joy. On January 1st, because it is the solar New Year or the Japanese New Year, the majority of men and women clinging to old customs pass it by without any value, and on the first day of the lunar month, because it is the lunar New Year or the old customs New Year, even the few modern men and women do not regard it as important as the memorial day for their father-in-law. If Seol is like this, is there any room to discuss the minor holidays throughout the year, such as Dano or Chuseok? In conclusion, Joseon has no holidays. There is not a single holiday where the entire nation can proudly dance and celebrate. Is this fortunate or unfortunate? There is no greater national incompetence, powerlessness, or national sorrow than this. A nation without a single holiday? How great is this sorrow?!

Habit is truly frightening. I am the only one in my family of seven who observes the solar New Year. I have been following this path for 25 long years, and I am the only one standing on the Yang (陽, solar calendar) ground. How frightening and difficult is this reality? As the saying goes, "If you don't know the customs of others, look at your own." I can understand all of Joseon by looking at my own reality and my own family. I first heard the word "solar calendar" 25 years ago (when I was 7 or 8 years old), during the Gapjin (甲辰) and Eulsa (乙巳) years. I started using both the lunar and solar calendars 20 years ago, when I was 14 or 15 years old. (My parents, uncles, and aunts must have learned it at that time and used both calendars together.) However, I have only been celebrating Seol according to the lunar calendar for about 10 years. Even this is just me alone, and my entire family still knows the lunar calendar as if it were their own holiday. If I am the only one out of seven family members who has been doing this for 25 years, what is the point of further investigating the situation throughout Joseon? Indeed, isn't habit frightening? That's why Joseon still doesn't have a national holiday. Is this what is called a transitional period?

Celebrating both the solar and lunar New Years! Those who don't know may not understand, but thinking about it, it is truly excessive suffering. It is an excessive loss for the entire nation. Leaving aside the actual losses and suffering, <77> the mental losses and suffering are no small matter. Neither the solar calendar nor the lunar calendar is properly observed, and there is not a single day in the 365 days of the year when the entire nation can dance and celebrate. Is there any sorrow greater than this? Furthermore, some people treat this double New Year celebration like a party with incense, tea, and rice, so how much material loss is there!

Let's stop the complaining. Whether it's the solar or lunar calendar, let's return to work. Let's create a holiday where the entire nation can experience great joy. Let's compensate for the losses and suffering we have endured in the past. Solar or lunar? Where should we settle? Of course, those who simply raise their hands will be on both sides. However, we cannot trust these people who are famous for taking backward steps. Inevitably, we must organize a Double New Year Abolition Group (二重過歲撲滅團). We must do so for the sake of the nation's honor. <78>
