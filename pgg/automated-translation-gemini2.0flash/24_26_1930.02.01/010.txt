Let Us Also Know the Future, A New Year's Message to the People
						Yun Ch'i-ho

If even squirrels store food for the winter and chipmunks save some for dinner, how much more should humans, the masters of all creatures, save for the future? It would be dangerous not to. It seems that Koreans have historically had less of a saving mentality than other nations. Of course, our past system and customs shaped us that way, and the weak economic power of our people may have left us with little to save, but I cannot help but wonder if the fundamental reason is a lack of saving habits. For the propertyless and powerless, oppressed and struggling to survive each day, it may be understandable that they have no means to save. However, even the wealthy and powerful, instead of saving their money for future endeavors, often squander it on wine, women, and speculative ventures. I truly cannot understand why this is, and it is disheartening.

<2>

When I traveled to my hometown in Ch'ungnam province, I witnessed a former minister's son, indulging in debauchery, selling off the inherited fields and even the grave sites of his ancestors. I also saw the minister's daughter-in-law gathering fallen leaves from her ancestors' graves, which had been sold, and suffering humiliation at the hands of the new owner. On the other hand, I saw a former paekchŏng (백정, outcast), once despised, now treated with respect in the community because of his wealth. In the current system, without the means to survive, even the most noble and knowledgeable person cannot display their talents, becoming a so-called "hero without money or land" (無錢天地소英雄). Without money, even relatives become distant, and one may be forced to withdraw from cherished organizations due to the inability to fulfill obligations. In the past, when Koreans lived only among themselves, even if someone ruined their family fortune, there was a chance for the so-called superman (超人) to retrieve it, directly or indirectly. However, now, with extensive direct dealings with foreigners, money spent immediately goes into their hands, with no way for it to return to Koreans.

I know some may criticize me for saying this, but I believe that in today's Korean society, a miser is more beneficial to the nation and its people than a wastrel who squanders money foolishly. This is because a wastrel who ruins their family fortune harms not only themselves but also their family and society, while a miser, though not actively helpful, does not cause much harm, and the money they hoard may eventually be used for public benefit. Therefore, I always want to speak about diligence and saving whenever I have the opportunity.

Once, when I went on a local tour with Western missionaries, the Westerners would tell the congregation, "God gave you money, so you must use it for God's work, even selling your land to pay for church construction and church projects," quoting the Bible. I, on the other hand, would say, "To be eternally a child of God, you must carefully preserve your wealth. What good is it to go to heaven alone while your wife, children, and parents <3> starve to death? If you have enough to live on, then use it for church projects, but if not, save diligently." I even earned the missionaries' displeasure for this.

Indeed, that is the truth. If one spends all their wealth on the church in a moment of temporary fervor and cannot maintain their livelihood, how can they continue to be a church member for long? Westerners often say that Koreans are people who do not think of tomorrow. That is, they have no saving mentality and do not think about what they will live on tomorrow. Whatever the past may have been, from today onward, we must think about the future and pay special attention to saving.
