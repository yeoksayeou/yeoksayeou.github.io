Five Reporter Teams Mobilized
Especially to that Madam

A Reporter

"Who is it?"
"It's me."
"Me?!"
That impudent rascal! A city policeman steps forward. The air becomes tense, no, more ominous than that.
This incident occurred at 3 AM on October 25, 1932, in a narrow alley in Sogyeok-dong, Bukchon, Seoul.
While this exploration reporter was walking side-by-side with Miss C from Cafe XX, exchanging stories more fragrant and sweeter than "Do-do-bus," heading towards Miss C's house, they unexpectedly encountered a patrolling policeman.
Perhaps offended by the reporter's way of speaking, he repeats, "Me?!" In the dark night, the black-clad policeman's twinkling eyes were terribly fierce.
"Where are you going?"
"I'm going to that house."
"What's the address?"
"Sogyeok-dong 100XX."
The policeman then interrogates Miss C.
"What's your address?"
"Sogyeok-dong 100XX."
Without hesitation, Miss C replies with a clear voice, like "C'est lui."
"What's your name?"
"My name is Park Chun-gwon, and this is my younger sibling."
"What's your name?"
"Jeon Yeong-sil." Oh, what a damn woman! The older sibling's surname is Park, but the younger sibling's surname is Jeon.
"She's my maternal cousin."
Somehow, they managed to get through the awkward moment, but it was still awkward. The policeman smiles. The situation becomes more complicated and reaches its climax. Alas, what to do with this situation?
"Something's fishy! Tell me the truth." The policeman takes another step forward. It seems like they're about to be disgraced. Not that the reporter cares, but what if poor Miss C suffers some kind of misfortune! What to do!
"I'll tell you the truth. We had a memorial service at our house today. This child is my younger sibling's friend, and she came to play, but it got late, so could I send her home alone? That's why I'm walking her home."
"Where do you live?"
"Yeongeon-dong."
"What's your occupation?"
"I work in the money-handling department at XX Bank."
"Do you have a business card?"
"I don't have one on me right now, but it's true."
Confessing something that wasn't true as if it were true, the policeman seems to believe it now.
"Well, it's different sometimes, but these days, when surveillance is so strict, you shouldn't be wandering around late at night like this. Especially you, young madam..."
Feeling like they'd finally survived, the reporter was so grateful for the lecture.
"How did it end up like that? It can't always be like that."
"Especially to that madam, but if you're wandering around late like this again, I won't forgive you!"
Giving emphasis to "especially that madam," as if scolding his own daughter, it seems the policeman sensed Miss C's true nature. Or perhaps he was being generous to "especially that madam."
"Then go!" At that one word, we walked in step like marching students. Sweet stories were whispered again.
"Hey! We almost got disgraced. Damn woman, why didn't you say Park Yeong-sil instead of Jeon Yeong-sil..."
"I was so scared that I said that on the spur of the moment."
"Anyway, we survived."
"Anyway," the reporter gently kissed Miss C's forehead. What happened next? They arrived at Miss C's door.
Reader! Don't ask what happened next. <4> They just walked her home. As if picking up a fallen piece of paper money, they wandered through the narrow alleys of Bukchon and happened to meet Miss C on her way home after her time was up.
End of the reporter's exploration.

Eating Sweet Potato Songpyeon, a Female Student's Great Predicament

B Reporter

If I were to explore after midnight, I would miss my sleep, so I decided to take a nap before going out. I lay down with a wooden pillow, and the first time I fell asleep, I woke up with a startle at the sound of a clapper. Oh, no—it was already 1:10 AM. I hurriedly got up, put on my winter coat over my pants and undershirt, pressed down my worn hat, lit a cigarette, and stepped out the gate. If I had a companion, it would be nice to go exploring, no matter how late it was, but I didn't have the courage to go far alone. Oh well, let's just go to the closest place first. I went through Hong Sul-hae alley and headed towards Gye-dong (桂洞). I entered the well alley in Gye-dong, where there were many female students, and after passing six or seven houses, I heard a man and a woman arguing from the alley across the street. That's it! I wondered what was going on and walked quietly towards that place. When I got close, I saw that the man was none other than a sweet potato songpyeon seller, and the woman seemed to be a female student. The sweet potato songpyeon seller stood outside the window, and the female student stood inside, arguing with each other.
Man: "Please pay me quickly. The previous one was 30 jeon, and if today is also on credit, what capital am I supposed to use to do business? It would be good if you said it was on credit from the beginning. If you eat other people's things and then say you don't have money, what am I supposed to do?"
Woman (in a Gyeongsang dialect): "Oh, you're so stingy. I'm a regular customer, so what's with all the fuss? Who would cheat you out of such a small amount? Just go now and come back another day."
Man: "Even if it's a small amount for you, I need to receive that money to eat breakfast tomorrow. If it was just once or twice, I wouldn't be like this, but you've done this several times already. If you were just sightseeing, you would have paid that small amount already." (His voice was very unpleasant.)
Woman: "If it's just the songpyeon price, you should just say the songpyeon price. What business is it of yours whether I go sightseeing or not?"... and (the woman's voice was also loud and very unpleasant. She slammed the window shut.)
Man: "Hey, even if you close the window, pay me the money. If you can't pay the money, at least write a note saying when you'll pay me..."
The sweet potato seller kept making a fuss like this, so the woman couldn't help but write a note and throw it out the window, telling him to come back in a few days. The argument between the two ended there. Out of curiosity, I followed the sweet potato songpyeon seller and asked, "Hey, why are you fighting with a woman in the middle of the night instead of selling rice cakes? What does that woman do?"
Seller: "I don't know. She seems to be a student, but maybe she's a delinquent student. Before, she was always with a man and paid well, and sometimes she even bought Chinese food, but these days, I don't see the man, and it seems like she's running out of money. She already owes me 30 jeon from before, and today she ate 10 jeon worth without paying."
Me: "Hey, can you show me the note you received?"
Seller: "What are you going to do with it? If you want to see it, go ahead."
I received the note as happily as if I had received a letter from a loved one <5> and went under the electric light to read it. The contents were as follows:
Will pay 40 jeon on the 30th.
October 26, Showa 7 (1932)
Gye-dong OO Address
Lee O-ja
Judging by the handwriting and the content of the note, she seems to be well above a regular graduate. When I checked again, I found out that the woman is a third-year student at O-hwa Girls' School in the city. It's not strange for a student living away from home to eat songpyeon on credit if they're hungry and don't have money, but writing a note for 40 jeon is a bit embarrassing. I got the news material and left the alley as if I had achieved something. As I was about to go further in the same direction, heading towards the end of Gye-dong, I suddenly heard the sound of "Hey, hey" from inside the alley. I looked up and saw a policeman. The policeman was chasing after me as if he was going to arrest someone right away. When he came closer, I realized that we knew each other. He said that surveillance was strict at night these days due to the so-called pawnshop robbery case (典當鋪 專門 强盜事件), and it would be better to report tomorrow and go home early unless there was a very important matter. I listened to his words and thought it made sense, and I didn't have the courage to go any further. I turned back and returned home, and the time was 1:53 AM.

Strange Street Advance
C Reporter

Thud, thud, thud. The sound of sand being stepped on from under the soles of my shoes is newly and loudly heard. My steps turned north from Donggwan Intersection.
The drinkers at XX-ru are also gradually ceasing, and from within the empty gates of each house, the clock strikes three, ting, ting, ting.
From midnight to three o'clock! - Whether it's due to my unlucky fate, I've been wandering around for three hours without finding a single story worth telling.
Putting my final hope in the back alleys of Donggwan, known for having many houses selling pleasure (色酒家), in Seoul.
Since houses selling pleasure are also a part of modern society, they must be affected by the economic depression; they are very desolate.
In the past, at this time, entering this alley would have meant hearing the sound of new janggo drums and the stuttering sounds of Banga Taryeong, with groups of drunken people encountered at every turn, but now it's desolate.
Since I'm already on my way home, it's no loss to try. Gathering all my nerves in my ears (even I think my appearance looks a bit pathetic), I head north. Suddenly, two men in Western suits pop out from a complex alley and call out, "Hey!" Surprised, I realize they're detectives. It seems they're on high alert due to the pawnshop robbery case.
After sweating and walking for a while, heaven seems to be helping my efforts, as I discover a pair of "Adam and Eve" venturing onto the street. It's a dim alley.
"'Please, no.' That's not the erotic refusal of a pleasure house madam like Yang Geum-chae."
"No... Here!... Hmm."
That's Mr. Gweol's tongue-tied voice.
I stop walking, hold my breath, and gradually sketch the unfolding scene of eros in my mind.
The excited Miss Gweol and Mr. Gweol don't recognize my intrusion.
"Here!... ... ..."
"Oh! I'm going to die! What are you doing!"
"Heh! This is even better, hoho."
"What if someone comes?"
"Who's going to come! Even if they do, it's okay! Let them watch a bit, ho-hmm."
After that, silence, with the sounds of stifled breathing... ... ... ...
I've discovered an unexpectedly good spectacle, so I can't help but make an appearance. Still, not wanting to disturb their good time, I quietly approach them as soon as Mr. Gweol's final "ho-hmm" ends.
Miss Gweol screams and collapses, while Mr. Gweol's surprised face is so bizarre that even the best writer in the world would have difficulty drawing it.
Without saying a word, I smile broadly.
Still, they maintain their pose without any change.
It's as if they're saying, "Catch them and eat them."
"Ugh, damn friend, what are you doing?"
He throws out this one sentence and turns away abruptly. As he walks away with heavy steps, I finally hear the sound of whispering behind me, then it stops. <6>

Lonely Street
D Reporter

In the old days, an evening bell rang at midnight, I heard. But now, not even the evening bell rings.
Seoul's Jongno Intersection was a lonely intersection. Even the streetlights were few enough to count on one's fingers, a desolate street. It was a dark street.
I was a honest me, waiting for midnight at teahouse B. A lonely me, sitting there to see Seoul after midnight.
There were the faces of Mr. R from newspaper M, Mr. H from newspaper C, and the tall Mr. K. Their story blossomed at first, with the story of the pistol incident's protagonist, but now the story smells stale. And occasionally, there was only lukewarm laughter, "Heo-heo."
They passed me a sake cup over my empty tea cup. Like a pitiable traveler, I couldn't refuse and swallowed it.
It was my turn to bring out a story. But I didn't have one. Dawn broke at our seats. Behind me, at A-dong X Clinic, a young man and someone with money, like K or P, sat like mutes, also pouring "Masamune."
A cute errand boy dozed off on a chair. The clock struck one.
Okay! It was time. It was time to see Seoul after midnight. Moreover, the face of Jongno, right in the middle of it.
When I came out in front of teahouse B, my newspaper friends had also come out. They seemed reluctant to part ways. Among them, Mr. R, who lived in S-dong, was drunk and shouted, "See you tomorrow!" before grabbing a rickshaw flowing on the street. So, Mr. K, who was remaining, also hesitated before raising his hand to a rickshaw flowing by. And he ran west with H in two rickshaws side by side.
It seemed like it was going to rain. It was a sky that often hailed and had sudden showers, so it might be another gust of wind.
Dark Jongno, lonely Jongno, Jongno where even the lights of S Department Store were completely dark!
Still, the lights of the running taxis shone on the streetcar tracks.
A group of people swaggered down from that side. When I got closer to them, I realized that they were teachers from C School and P School.
When I passed in front of the YMCA building, I met another group of tipsy young people. The way they swaggered suggested that they had come from Cafe R. And I knew that their feet were changing direction to Cafe P. There was also the face of youth activist U.
Only these countable people passed by on the wide Jongno main street, and it was silent. When I turned back my lonely shadow and looked up at the intersection, a policeman walking in the middle of the intersection had a glittering sword hilt beyond the shadow of that young group.
It was in front of Cafe R. But even there, I didn't hear such a bustling voice. When my steps turned towards the entrance of Sa-dong, a rickshaw came and stopped in front.
I waved my hand. It wasn't my turn to ride a rickshaw today.
The lights were dark in front of C Theater as well. The clumsy pictures and writing looked very strangely sad. I thought about how those pictures had been very dazzling when I was young and smiled. <7>
A whistle sounds from the auto shop next door. I met a courtesan in a rickshaw here, whom I hadn't met on Jongno main street. That courtesan must have met and played somewhere.
The doors of the stores were also tightly locked. Eek, a middle-aged friend in a white durumagi and a gray durumagi came out of the problematic Chunwonok tavern.
The famous Mitschang-pa—the lights of Venus are lonely. Cafe A, which was above it, closed three weeks after opening. Ah, everything is desolate. I looked into the Peppi Golf Course. But even there, the doors were locked.
First of all, there's nothing more to say about the night streets of Seoul. As I came up above, it was deserted. The only character on this street is me, who came out for exploration. I was reminded that if I were to get caught, I would be taken away and abused.
Before I could reach the Anguk-dong 6-way intersection, I met a female student wearing the uniform of S Girls' School. What business did the tall female student have on the desolate night streets? But, that lonely friend, numbing my sixth sense, I think only safe return is important. But because only safe return became important, I apologize to the readers. How I wanted to make a play, but since the other reporters will all do that, I'll leave it at that and my wandering steps go along the Songhyeon-dong alley as they are.
"Manju-nohoi"—the night is very late, but still
"Manju-nohoi"
Is presented, and when I come into the room, it is exactly 2 AM. I get into bed and begin to transcribe this writing.

Apollo and Bacchus Seeking Venus

E Reporter

X Month X Day X PM.
"Tonight, late at night, go find out what Seoul is like." It's an order from the editor that I can't refuse.
Since it's cold, he gives me a few pennies for makgeolli so I can get a late-night snack, and I start out aimlessly with the money jingling in my pocket. I've never worried so much. I ate dinner already so I'm not hungry, but the way I'm wandering around aimlessly looking for material is not something I can be proud of, no matter how kindly I think of it. To others, it might look like I'm just taking a walk after dinner, or that I'm just going back and forth because I have nothing to do. I need to get at least some kind of leftover material, but it's getting late, almost midnight, and I haven't found anything. At this rate, I'm worried about the manuscript I have to submit tomorrow, and I feel sorry for the makgeolli money that's jingling forlornly in my pocket. Someone like me has a business card with "magazine reporter" printed on it... These worries and those worries, after thinking and thinking, in the end, I was worried about the editor's angry face tomorrow morning, and ultimately, I was greatly worried that I would lose my job. Oh well! Who knows what will happen tomorrow? First of all, since it's cold... I enter a tavern. Unable to bear these worries and those worries, I drink three or four cups of strong liquor, and the dizziness is pleasant. It's already past 12 o'clock. I leave the tavern, light a cigarette, and now I have no choice but to go home. If I can't submit a manuscript, I'll just have to write down the nagging of my wife, who doesn't know my feelings and scolds me for drinking late at night. I drag my feet, unable to lift them properly, and enter Sa-dong alley, looking up at the long street of Gwanhun-dong. "Bar Venus" A line of gold letters runs across the wide glass door. If there's a problem with that writing, it's there. According to my early look in the English dictionary, Bar has many interpretations in one line. Among other things, it clearly states: judge's seat (法官席), criminal defendant's seat (刑事被告席), and a place to sell alcohol (酒場). In the Venus line, it says that it is a goddess (女神) who presides over beauty and love. Anyway,
everything is ruined, so I decide to enter this place for the first and last time. I clench the 10 jeon I have left in my pocket and enter. It's arranged as one might expect in name only (名實相符). It's quite cozy (?) and very quiet. In the high counter (臺) that can be seen directly in front, Venus (女神) is seated to one side, and below her, many people each occupy a seat, either alone, in pairs, or in groups of three. Some hold empty glasses, and others sit stiffly. There are sake drinkers who sip their glasses, each with a glass in front of them. I don't know how much they've drunk, but it seems that they haven't drunk much at all. I also order a 10 jeon cup of tea, sit down, and observe the scene. Everyone gathered seems to be refined and gentle. They don't seem to be greedy about drinking, and they just stare at one place. <8> No matter how I look at it, Bar Venus seems to be a group of defendants before Venus (재판장), waiting for some kind of verdict. Everyone has a respectful demeanor, and the quiet atmosphere makes such thoughts unavoidable.
Madam! I don't know if this is Korean or English, but it's the name used to respectfully address the female judge of Venus. Among these defendants, there are school teachers, newspaper reporters, novelists, poets (詩人), theater people, film people, noble sons, vagrants (浪人), and myself - since I was there at the time - magazine reporters too. But, except for me, most of them seem to come to this Venus court every evening late into the night, looking up at the kind face of the judge, waiting for a verdict that never comes.
The judge of this place, and Venus herself, Madam Kang Yeong-ju (姜英周), is said to have experienced a special romance and lived a life of luxury before taking on the role of Venus's judge - madam. Her respectful demeanor, her treatment of guests, her way of speaking, and her very existence all seem to bring these defendants to the court and make Bar Venus what it is - a Venus-like presence. Among those who gather, there are those who clutch their unspeakable hearts and gulp down bitter coffee, occasionally calling out "Madam," and there are those who seem to be waiting for an opportunity to sit next to this madam. But in that jostling crowd, who will leave first, and who will be the lucky one to sit next to the madam and exchange warm words? In the midst of all this, the one who suffers is the errand boy and the owner who calculates on the abacus. They've been sitting with one 10 jeon cup of tea until late at night, so even if it's their job, the madam is tired of yawning. Thus, the time at Bar Venus is late, and the night is deep. Uh-hum! I'm sleepy. I've also spent my time with just one cup of tea, so if my life is good, I'll see you again. Madam Venus, adieu! <9>
