잡지명: 別乾坤 제8호
발행일: 1927년 08월 17일
기사제목: 朝鮮日報女記者 崔恩喜氏와의 會談記 (A Conversation with Ch'oe Ŭn-hŭi, a Female Reporter for the Chosŏn Ilbo)
필자: 靑吾生
기사형태: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Talk)

A Conversation with Ch'oe Ŭn-hŭi, a Female Reporter for the Chosŏn Ilbo
Ch'ŏngosaeng

Time: July 26th, 3:30 PM
Place: Kyŏngsŏng, Insadong, inside T'aehwa Women's Hall (泰和女子館)
Reporter: I heard you haven't been feeling well lately. How are you doing?

Ch'oe: Yes, I've recovered a bit. But my health changes so often that I can't rely on it.

Reporter: They say a woman's heart changes as much as the clouds in the autumn sky. Is your health like the clouds in the autumn sky too?

Ch'oe: My health is not like the clouds in the autumn sky, but like the summer rainy season. (Her tone was quite quick, but her eyes were smiling, and the t'aegŭk fan (太極扇) she held in her hand was swung around as if creating a whirlwind.)

Reporter: Why do you have a t'aegŭk fan? Like a child.

Ch'oe: No, I didn't buy it. I just received it as a gift from someone today.

Reporter: I see. It says "Minse (民世)" on the front and "Chŏng Ch'ugye (呈秋溪)" on the back, so it must be a gift from An Chae-hong (安在鴻), the noble chief writer. The most refreshing and essential gift during the summer is a fan. Among them, the people's ways (民風) blowing to Ch'ugye (秋溪) would be even more refreshing.

Ch'oe: (She only smiled without saying anything.)

Reporter: Since we're both busy, let's stop the small talk and have a formal Q&A.

Ch'oe: Is there a formal and private way to talk? This is all news material.

Reporter: That's right. Then let's talk however. Where were you born, and how old are you?

Ch'oe: Are you writing an interrogation record like a detective instead of a visit record? And why are you asking about things we already know?

Reporter: Whether I write a visit record or a Q&A record is up to me, so don't worry about it and just answer my questions well. I'll treat you to something later.

Ch'oe: Hey, are you going to write my article like Shin Il-sŏn's (申一仙) Q&A? If you do that, it might be too shallow, so please write it differently. <80>

Reporter: What's wrong with a shallow Q&A? It's about the content. Just answer quickly, no matter what the article is like.

Ch'oe: Okay, then ask away. My hometown is Haeju (海州), and I was born in Yŏnbaek County (延白郡) (formerly Paekch'ŏn (元白川)). But who knows if I fell from the sky or sprang up from the ground? (Her eyebrows danced, and she laughed again.)

And about my age, I'm quite old. 283 months in total. (She took out a pencil as if doing arithmetic for elementary school students, fussing for a while to convert her age into months, and then inadvertently sighed, "Oh dear, my age is already almost three hundred months!")

Reporter: Why sigh just because you're a little old? Calculating your age in months makes it seem older. If you count in years, you're still a strapping young woman, five months shy of the age when Xiang Yu (項羽) led eight thousand soldiers across the river and headed west.

Ch'oe: Is that how we should talk to attract the readers' interest? There's no fun in simply saying I'm in my twenties.

Reporter: This is a disaster! Before I could bring up an interesting topic, you mentioned an interesting phrase first. I might lose my scoop. But thank you for providing good material.

Ch'oe: Make sure to pay me well for the manuscript.

Reporter: Of course. And if there's anything interesting about your school days and life before becoming a reporter, please tell me.

Ch'oe: Oh, you're asking too much. You roughly know about my life, and you've written about it in the Pyŏlgŏn'gon (別乾坤) sometimes, haven't you? Speaking of my school days, the elementary, middle, and university years are all different, so I can't tell you everything, but I remember my elementary school days the most. I seem to have started elementary school quite early. I entered on December 19th of the year I turned seven and graduated at the age of 11. At that time, the school was founded by my father, and I was carried on someone's back to school. I was very bad at singing and calligraphy, but I did very well in arithmetic and Chinese classics. And I always borrowed needlework from home. Later, when I transferred to Ŭijŏng Girls' School (懿貞女學校) in Haeju (海州), my calligraphy skills improved quite a bit. Until then, I couldn't sing at all, so I couldn't even attend music concerts, but I was chosen as the best speaker for speech contests. I didn't have many memories of Chaedong Girls' High School (齋洞女子高普) in Kyŏngsŏng (京城) because I entered in the middle and graduated in just two years, but I enjoyed studying economics at Japan Women's University (日本女子大學). I didn't have any interest in books on practical logic, of course. After that, all my career was as a teacher (before studying in Japan), going around Suwŏn Samil Girls' School (水原 三一女學校), P'yŏngyang Chŏngjin Girls' School (平壤 正進女學校), and Anju Yusin School (安州 維新學校), pretending to put on makeup with chalk powder on my face. And during the Kiminyŏn (己未年) March 1st Movement era, I was indebted to the police station eight or nine times before and after, and I also spent six months in jail (I was in preliminary examination in Haeju (海州) for six months and then got a two-year suspended sentence at the trial). (At that time, she gesticulated excitedly, repeatedly clenching and unclenching her fingers and hitting the chair with the t'aegŭk fan (太極扇).)

Reporter: I roughly understand your career now, but what motivated you to become a newspaper reporter, and who introduced you to the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報)?

Ch'oe: I don't have any special motivation, but I've always been interested in being a reporter. About a year before joining the company, I came to Seoul (서울) during the summer vacation while studying in Japan (日本), and I happened to tell Hŏ Yŏng-suk (許英肅), the wife of Ch'unwŏn Yi Kwang-su (春園 李光洙), that being a reporter was an interesting life. Later, after I returned to my hometown, Haeju (海州), from Japan (日本) and was idle for a while, I asked Hŏ (許) to introduce me to a job because I was so bored. Hŏ (許) immediately replied that she had recommended me as a female reporter for the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報) and that I should go to Seoul (서울) right away. Until then, I was only half convinced, and because Song (松), the manager of that newspaper, didn't quite match my principles, I vaguely replied with a letter and stayed at home. Then Hŏ (許) sent me another letter urging me to go to Seoul (서울), so I rushed to Seoul (서울) to get some fresh air. When I got off at Kyŏngsŏng Station (京城驛), Hŏ (許) and her husband and Kim Chi-t'ae (金智泰) came to greet me. I followed them to Hŏ's (許) house, and Min T'ae-wŏn (閔泰瑗), the editor-in-chief of the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝日) at the time, was there. That's how I got to know him. (Of course, I had some acquaintance with him when I was in Japan (日本), too.) While I was at Hŏ's (許) house, I did something like this. When Hŏ (許) was a doctor at the time, a certain wealthy man in Hwanggeumchŏng (黃金町) received treatment and owed about 80 won (원), but he kept delaying payment and shamelessly demanded a reduction in the amount. Until then, I didn't have much association with men and kept silent, but I was so outraged by his behavior that I volunteered to collect the money. Because I was so angry, I went to his house and argued and badgered him so much that the miser had no choice but to pay the money.

Somehow, that rumor reached the ears of the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報) authorities, and they thought of me as the right person for the female reporter position and hired me on Hŏ's (許) recommendation. I joined the company on October 5th, four years ago, and I've been living at Min's (閔) house ever since, so it's been like that until now. (Beads of sweat like pearls were coming out of his unusually long beard (he wouldn't have a beard if he were a man, but it's relatively long for a woman), and he kept wiping it with a towel.)

Reporter: What was the most interesting and most difficult thing you experienced while working as a reporter?

Ch'oe: Oh dear, are you going to dig up everything in the end? As a reporter, isn't everything we go through the same? When each company competes for articles, finding and publishing special material that other companies don't know about is the most interesting thing. Especially for me, competing with female reporters from other companies, it's even more interesting when the article I write is better than theirs. And when introducing people, it's refreshing, like discovering a new world, to find and introduce new people from abroad or somewhere else before anyone else introduces people everyone already knows. It was also interesting to succeed in disguised investigations without being discovered by others. For example, about four years ago, around November, I did an investigation disguised as a maid, and not only was I not caught, but looking at society as a maid was completely different from seeing it as a reporter. At that time, I was very interested. And when visiting homes or organizations to get news material, they still don't understand and are suspicious of us, treating us like detectives and avoiding telling us the truth, which is very difficult. If the result doesn't match my efforts and time wasted and the value of the article, it's very unpleasant.

Reporter: What attitude do you take when handling articles about colleagues, personal matters, or organizations you are related to?

Ch'oe: I originally handle different sections, so I don't have such things at all. Even if I know that the social affairs department is handling such articles, I just pretend not to know.

Reporter: What do you think is the best job as a reporter?

Ch'oe: If they pay well and have freedom of speech, the job of a reporter would be relatively good. If you or I didn't like the job of a reporter, would we have been in it for several years? (The flower of laughter bloomed again.)

Reporter: Do you go to the theater often?

Ch'oe: I don't go often, but I go occasionally.

Reporter: Do you like movies or plays?

Ch'oe: I like movies relatively more. As a reporter, I pay attention to news material no matter where I go, but I once succeeded when I went to see a movie. It was when you guys at Kaebyŏksa (開闢社) and Ch'ŏndogyo (텬도교) people were arrested around June of last year. I was coming out of the Chosŏn Theater (朝鮮劇場) and stepped out onto Chongno (종로), when suddenly cars were speeding by, and I sensed that a big incident had happened. No other reporters from other companies were there at the time. I didn't call because I was afraid others would find out, so I took a rickshaw to Min T'ae-wŏn's (閔泰瑗) house and woke him up to go investigate at the Chongno Police Station (종로서). I notified the other reporters and was the first to release an extra edition before the other companies. Whether others were arrested and suffered, I felt very refreshed because I was the first to release the extra edition. That's all the benefit I got from going to the theater.

Reporter: Who do you think is the best editor among the newspaper reporters in Kyŏngsŏng (京城)?

Ch'oe: I don't know about other companies, but from what I've seen in our company, Cho Kyu-su (趙奎洙) is good in the political section and Hyŏn Chin-gŏn (玄鎭健) in the social affairs department. But Min U-bo (閔牛步) is probably the best as a general editor. (The wind suddenly blew, and his hair scattered in all directions like clouds after the rainy season.)

Reporter: Are you going to continue working as a reporter forever?

Ch'oe: Since jobs are also controlled by "bread," I don't know what will happen, but if the bread issue is not a problem, I think I'll continue that life as it is. (She nodded her head with interest, tapping the rhythm with the brush.)

Ch'oe: What happened to the trip abroad?

Ch'oe: I applied for a travel permit on August 7th last year and barely prepared the travel expenses, but on October 27th, I found out that it was denied and gave up. (Even she, who laughed well, lowered her head at the end of this statement and was silent for a while as if she had lost her mind.)

Reporter: Then what did you do with the money?

Ch'oe: I spent it all. (Her face regained color.)

Reporter: Oh dear, I was going to ask to borrow it, but I missed my chance.

Ch'oe: ...Heh...heh...

Reporter: What kind of books do you read?

Ch'oe: I read books on economics and also read books for pleasure from time to time. I really enjoy reading your company's Pyŏlgŏn'gon (別乾坤). But there's no need to write that. (The reporter felt very sorry for having to write everything she said.)

Reporter: What kind of food do you eat well?

Ch'oe: Should I say I eat "bananas" well like Shin Il-sŏn (申一仙)? But I like vegetables more than meat, and I eat cold things better than hot things. I eat cold noodles, of course, and ice cream, shaved ice, and other cold things well, regardless of summer or winter.

Reporter: Is it because you're so cold that you like cold things?

Ch'oe: No. I think it's because I'm so passionate that I'm trying to balance it out (Haha...).

In the meantime, it was already 4:20 PM. It seemed to be the time for the executive meeting of the Kŭnwoohoe (槿友會), so I quickly left my seat. <84>

						靑吾生
時. 7월 26일 오후 세시반
處. 京城 仁寺洞 泰和女子館內
記. 요새이 몸이 건강치 못하시다더니 좀 엇더하심닛가.
崔. 네- 좀 회복이 되엿슴니다. 만은 나의 건강은 변화가 만어서 미들 수가 업슴니다.
記. 여자의 마음은 가을 하눌의 구름과 가티 변화가 만타더니 건강까지도 가을 하눌의 구름과 갓슴닛가.
崔. 나의 건강은 가을 하눌 구름은 커녕 녀름 장마날갓소. (語調는 퍽도 급하면서도 눈에 우슴을 띄이고 손에 들엇든 太極扇은 回旋風이 날만치 휘둘녓다.)
記. 태극선은 왜 가지섯소. 아희들 모양으로.
崔. 네- 내가 산 것이 안이요. 오날 누구에게 선사바든 것임니다.
記. 네- 알겟슴니다. 전면에 「民世」라 쓰고 후면은 「呈秋溪」라 하엿스니 貴族主筆 閣下 安在鴻선생이 선사한 것이올시다. 淸風來故人이라고 녀름선사 중에는 부채가 제일 시원하고 긴한 선사지요. 그 중에도 民風이 吹秋溪하면 더욱 시원할 것임니다.
崔. (아모말 안코 우슬 뿐.)
記. 피차에 시간도 밧부니 한담은 그만두고 정식으로 문답을 합시다.
崔. 말하는데도 정식 私式이 따로 잇나요. 이것이 다 기사거리지요.
記. 그것은 그럿슴니다. 그러면 아모러케나 말슴을 함니다. 당신의 출생지는 어듸이고 년령은 얼마심닛가.
崔. 訪問記를 쓰지 안코 형사 모양으로 取調記를 쓰시랴오. 그러고 그것은 서로 아는 일을 또 무러 무엇하시랴오.
記. 방문기를 쓰든지 문답기를 쓰든지 그것은 내가 쓸 터이니 상관치 마르시고 내가 문는 대로 대답만 잘하야 주시요. 이 다음 한턱을 할 터이니.
崔. 여보- 내 기사도 申一仙 문답기 모양으로 쓰시랴오. 그러케 하면 너모 천착할 듯하니 달리 써주시요.＜80＞
記. 문답기가 천착할 것이야 무엇잇소-. 내용 문제이지. 기사야 엇지 되엿든 速이 대답이나 하야주시요.
崔. 네- 그러면 말슴하십시다. 나의 原籍地는 海州구요, 出生地는 延白郡(元白川)이람니다. 그러나 누가 아나요 하눌에서 떠러젓는지 따에서 솟삿는지. (눈섭이 팔자춤을 추면서 또 우엇다)
그리고 년령 말슴이요. 년령은 퍽 만치요. 달수로 283개월임니다. (소학교생들의 산술하듯이 연필을 끄내들고 년령을 월수로 환산하노라고 한참 수선을 피우더니 부지중에 아이구- 나의 년령은 다수로도 벌서 근 삼백달이 되엿구나 하고 적은 한심을 쉬엿다)
記. 년령이 조곰 만키로 한심쉬일 것이야 무엇잇소. 긋득이 만허가는 년령을 월수로 계산하니 더 만흔 것 갓지요. 년수로 따지면 項羽가 팔천자제를 거나리고 渡江而西하든 년령보다도 아즉 5개월이나 未滿되는 壯年인데요.
崔. 독자의 흥미를 끌드록 말을 하자닛가 그럿치요. 단순히 이십몃이라 하면 무슨 자미가 잇나요.
記. 이것- 큰일 낫구려! 내가 흥미잇는 문제를 끄러내기 전에 당신이 먼저 흥미잇는 구절을 말슴하니 잘못하다가는 초라를 떼일 모양이요 그려-. 그러나 재료를 잘 제공하시니 단단이 고맙슴니다.
崔. 원고료나 톡톡이 잘 주시요.
記. 드리고 말고요. 그러시고 당신의 재학시대와 기자되기 전까지의 생활상 滋味잇든 일이 잇스면 말슴 좀 하야주서요.
崔. 원-별말슴을 다 무러요. 나의 그동안 생활이야 대강 알으시고 또 別乾坤에도 각금 쓰시지를 안으섯나요. 나의 재학시대로 말하면 소학·중학·대학시대가 다 다르닛가 일일히 말슴할 수는 업지만은 소학시대가 제일 기억이 만슴니다. 소학교로 말하면 꽤 일즉 다닌 모양임니다. 일곱살 되는 해 12월 19일에 입학하야 가지고 11세 때에 명색졸업을 하얏슴니다. 그때 그 학교는 우리 아버지께서 설립한 학교인데 나는 남에게 업혀서 학교를 다니엿슴니다. 과정에는 唱歌·習字를 아조 잘 못하고, 산술·한문은 성적이 매우 조왓슴니다. 그리고 針工은 꼭 집에서 借作을 하얏지요. 그 뒤에 海州 懿貞女學校에 전학하야서는 습자공부도 제법 늘게 되엿슴니다. 그때까지 창가는 아주 할 줄을 몰나서 무슨 음악회가튼 것을 할 때에는 꼴찌에도 못 갓지만은 연설회에는 일등변사로 뽑피엿슴니다. 京城齋洞女子高普는 중도에 입학하야 단지 2개년간에 졸업한 까닭에 별 기억이 나지 안코 日本女子大學에서는 경제학이 퍽 자미잇게 공부하얏슴니다. 실천논리 등 書는 물론 흥미를 가지지 못하얏지요. 그 뒤 이력으로는 전부가 교원생활인데(日本유학전) 水原 三一女學校로, 平壤 正進女學校로 安州 維新學校로 도라다니며＜81＞ 분필가루로 얼골의 화장을 하다십히 하얏슴니다. 그리고 己未年 00운동 시대에는 경찰서의 변도 신세를 전후에 8·9회나 지고 철창생활도 6개월(海州에서 6개월간 예심에 잇다가 공판에서 2개년 집행유예)이나 하얏슴니다.
