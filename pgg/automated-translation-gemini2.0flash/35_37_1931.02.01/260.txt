Publication Date: 1931년 02월 01일

Article Title: 羅馬法皇面會記 (Record of an Audience with the Pope in Rome)

Author: 李甲秀 (Yi Kap-su)

Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

Record of an Audience with the Pope in Rome
						Yi Kap-su

In the spring of 1925, while staying in Germany, I finally embarked on my long-awaited journey to Italy. Having endured countless hardships, millions of brave generals and soldiers, harboring great ambitions for thousands of years, had failed to overcome the severe cold and snow of the treacherous Alps. Receiving the benefits of modern civilization, I comfortably sat in a rattling glass-windowed carriage, watching the flowing, rugged lakes and mountains, passing through numerous tunnels, and finally arrived in Rome, the capital of Italy, where the Pope resides. <104> As I stepped out of the train and onto a horse-drawn carriage, a unique feature of Italy, I took a glance at the city. The imposing and splendid modern buildings mingled with the antiquated ancient structures, the temples protruding everywhere, and the monks, dressed in black hats and black robes, frequently passing by on the streets, in carriages, trams, or cars, perfectly displayed the unique religious color that could only be seen in Rome, a religious city, regardless of ancient or modern times. After arriving at the "hotel" and settling in, I first went to see the ruins of ancient Rome. The sun was already setting in the west, and the thought that this was the last moment of the day made me recall the past of ancient Rome. I opened my eyes wide and looked at its ruins once more.

Next, I planned to visit the Vatican. Having heard from the "hotel" owner that I had to prepare early in the morning to visit the Vatican, I finished all the preparations early the next morning, and it was exactly 7 o'clock. As I went out, a few white clouds floated in the clear sky, and the weather was very clear, but the chilly wind that touched my face seemed to be the aristocratic grandeur of the ancient Roman aristocratic city, and the cruelty that had no sympathy for the martyrdom of the two saints, the apostles "Peter" and "Paul," still seemed to linger. I took a "taxi" and arrived at the Vatican, where countless "taxis," "buses," and carriages were lined up in front of the hall. They were carrying members of the Vatican (Vaticano), those who sought an audience with the Pope, or those who came to visit the Vatican. It is said that the number of visitors, both domestic and foreign, reaches several thousand every day. <105> As I entered the square in front of the hall, the wide courtyard was paved with square white stones, all of which were artistic. In the center of the square stood a tall monument, and around its base were rows of brilliant lampposts. Away from this monument, on the left, were two fountains, where water, like a waterfall flowing from the mountains, spurted into the air. Looking from the entrance of this square, a majestic ancient-style temple stood tall in the sky in front, and the lightning rod at its summit seemed to be faintly embedded in the white clouds. From the left and right wings of the temple, hundreds of bays of palace buildings surrounded the square in a semi-circular shape, and on the left, right, and rear sides of the temple were rows of palaces where the Pope resided and pavilions used by the hall's affairs and officials. I slowly walked through this hall's square, and as I approached the temple and entered the temple's main gate from the countless white stone steps, the cold wind and eerie energy that struck my body from the dimness made me feel as if the divine spirit was welcoming me. Goosebumps ran all over my body, and my chest fluttered. I gathered my wits and looked around. It was a large "hall" that could easily accommodate four or five thousand worshipers. The carvings engraved on the magnificent "cement" pillars, the paintings on the four walls or the colorful images drawn on the glass windows were the pride of the Italian people, who possessed a special talent for being immersed in artistic beauty. It was not enough to be immersed only in the viewing of the temple, but my purpose in visiting the Vatican early this morning was, first of all, to meet the Pope, and I asked the guard at the temple's main gate about the procedure. <106> The man replied in French that the people lined up over there were the ones who would have an audience with the Pope, so he told me to join the line. I was worried that I might be late, so without further questioning, I went there as quickly as possible. I found hundreds of men and women, young and old, mixed together. When I asked them, they either did not answer or gave vague answers in Italian, so I could not figure out what to do. However, I forcibly pushed through the crowd and went forward, and I discovered that about thirty or forty people were lined up in front, standing quietly. They were mostly gentlemen and ladies from foreign countries, and the Vatican staff members guarded the left and right sides of the line to prevent others from approaching. I asked a gentleman standing at the very back of the line, <107> and he said that he was a "Catholic" (old religion) believer from England and was about to enter to have an audience with the Pope. I was very happy to hear this and said that I had also come to meet the Pope. The gentleman said, "Then follow me," so I thought, "Now I can see the Pope," and I stayed very close to the gentleman in a friendly manner. After standing and waiting for a while, a staff member came to me and asked in English, "Do you have any 'metal'?" I replied, "What 'metal' are you talking about? I don't have anything." Then he said, "Then you can't. Those who want to have an audience with the Pope must wear a 'metal' of the Pope's face on their chest. To get this 'metal,' you must apply to the Vatican or somewhere in the city several days in advance. Come back after you get the 'metal.' Otherwise, you must have a letter of introduction from a pastor of the 'Catholic' church in your country." I could not help but be disappointed to hear this. At that time, I had no choice but to ask the gentleman, "I have come from the far-off country of Joseon to have an audience with the Pope, but I don't have a letter of introduction or 'metal,' so please allow me." However, the gentleman strongly refused, saying, "Absolutely not," and told me to leave the line quickly. Unable to do anything, I left the line and pushed through the crowd. As I was thinking, a gentleman wearing a black hat and black robe, like a monsignor, passed by. When I found him, I followed him and greeted him. Then I said, <108> "I am a Joseon person studying in Germany, and I came all the way to Rome to have an audience with the Pope, but I was rejected because I didn't have a 'metal' and a letter of introduction." He was very happy and started speaking German fluently. I was also happy and asked, "How do you speak German so well?" The gentleman replied that he had graduated from the theology department of the University of Berlin in Germany more than ten years ago and was now working in the Vatican. At that time, we realized that we were alumni, shook hands again in a friendly manner, and after a detailed conversation, he personally led me to the main gate. The line that had been standing earlier was just entering through the main gate. The gentleman winked at me, telling me to follow the line entering, and at the same time, he said something to the soldier guarding the main gate in his own language. The gentleman said "Addio" (a greeting meaning "see you again") to me and went inside the main gate. The soldier guarding the main gate was over six feet tall, wearing a red uniform and a shiny steel helmet. He held a spear that was several tens of feet long and watched the crowd of the line with a menacing look. It seemed that he would stab people with the spear at any moment, so it was daunting to pass him and enter the gate. I boldly passed through the main gate and took a few steps forward. At the entrance to the stairs, there were two more soldiers guarding. I passed them safely and went up to the second floor. At the entrance to the second floor, there were also two soldiers guarding. As I was about to pass, they discovered that I did not have a "metal" on my chest and pointed the sharp blades of the spears, crossed with each other, at my chest as if to stab me. At that time, I asked the soldiers to allow me to pass because I had received permission, but they shook their heads silently, wearing steel helmets, as if they did not understand or did not allow me to pass. The line that had been going together had already gone up to the third floor, and I was left alone, not knowing what to do. As I looked down at the lower floor, the gentleman who had allowed me to enter was coming up quickly, saying, "I expected this to happen, so I quickly finished my business and am coming to help you now." He said something to the soldiers who were blocking me with their spears, and the soldiers lifted their spears and cleared the way for me. At that time, I quickly went up to the third floor with the gentleman. At the entrance to the third floor, there were also two soldiers guarding on the left and right. From there, we walked down the corridor and soon reached a large gate. There were also two soldiers standing on either side of the gate, holding spears, and people were being called in one by one from inside the gate. I opened the door and entered the room. There were still soldiers standing quietly on the left and right. The room was extremely spacious, a large "hall" that could accommodate about a thousand people. The interior, decorated with yellow, white, gold, silver, and five colors of blue, red, yellow, black, and white silk, was dazzling, and red-faced soldiers and officers with spears and swords ran back and forth. I was dazed, as if I had strayed into the palace of the King of Hell. It was like a dream, and it felt like I had come to an inhuman world. After waiting in this room for about 30 minutes, an officer came out from one side of the door and said that the Pope would be coming out soon, so everyone should line up in a circle and stand at attention. Everyone in the crowd lined up at once. After that, there was no further news, and I looked at the clock and it was already half past eleven. I had left the "hotel" at 7 o'clock in the morning, so four and a half hours had passed. At that time, I felt dissatisfied in my heart. What kind of position does the Pope have that it is so difficult for people to see him? Or what was my purpose in trying so hard to meet the Pope? Even though it's a foolish question, is it because the Pope really has the "key" to go to heaven? No, I shouldn't be dissatisfied like this. The Pope of Rome is a person in a noble position, worthy of being called the head of tens of thousands of people, whom people call "Papa" (meaning father). In the late 10th century, Emperor Otto the Great of the Holy Roman Empire could not be called emperor without the Pope's coronation. Later, Pope Gregory VII, a hero, declared, "To inspire the creatures of the world and create a great golden world, the Pope must be above the kings of all nations and be the supreme leader to represent heaven." He even excommunicated King Henry IV, who was king at the time (a major religious punishment). <111> In late January of 1077, the king hurried to Canossa Castle in northern Italy in the severe winter and begged the Pope barefoot on the snow for three days before he was pardoned. This is clearly recorded in history. Also, in the 13th century, Pope Innocent III freely deposed and established the kings of one country, and not only that, but he also interfered in the disputes between the kings of France and England, excommunicating the King of England, and freely exercised power over the internal affairs and diplomacy of European countries above the kings. In other words, he had the highest authority than the kings of the time. Since the current Pope Pius XI is inheriting the power of the previous popes, how could he not be respected? As I was thinking about the history of the Pope, I was startled by the sound of footsteps. When I looked up, another officer was coming out. He also warned us that when the Pope came out, we should all bow our heads, and when the Pope passed by us, we should put our left knee on the floor and extend our right hand, and "kiss" the ring on that hand. He repeated this, telling us to keep it in mind and do it accordingly. Then the clock struck twelve. A little later, about 30 guards with spears came out and stood in a solemn line. Next, about 10 people, such as attendants or monsignors wearing black hats and black robes, came out and checked whether our standing line was in order. After about 5 minutes, a soldier came out first and shouted at the crowd to bow their heads, striking the floor with the heavy handle of his spear. <112> Then, about 10 guards officers in army uniforms with close-shaven "Kaiser"-style "mustaches" and about 10 adjutants came out, protecting the Pope. I secretly glanced at him. He was neither too tall nor too short, and his physique was slightly plump but dignified. He was an old man of about 60 years old with an extremely gentle and outstanding appearance. He wore dazzling clothes of five rich colors, and the middle of his head was shaved clean, covered with a flat black cloth. He was not an ordinary human being in the secular world. I wondered if the King of Hell had descended. As the officer had warned us, the Pope's right hand, which he gave to each of the audience members, was indeed as beautiful as jade. When he came to me, I also "kissed" the ring on his hand and looked up at the Pope. The Pope also looked at me strangely and carefully (I was the only Asian person). He said something to me. Then, one of the adjutants who did not leave the Pope's side translated in English, "Are you from China?"

(I) No. I am a "Korean" from Joseon, a neighboring country of China.

(Pope) "Korean"? Are you a "Catholic" (old religion) believer?

(I) Yes! (If I said no, I would have been immediately dismissed.)

(Pope) Then are there many "Catholic" believers and church temples in your country?

(I) Yes! There are many.

(Pope) I am very pleased to see you, who have come from a far-off country. And he gave his right hand to the person next to me. After seeing all the audience members, the Pope went back into the door he had come out of. After about 10 minutes, he told all of us audience members to enter the room where the Pope had gone in. When we entered, the Pope was sitting high on a throne on a high platform and preached to us. One of the adjutants translated this into English. After finishing the sermon, he let us see the interior of the Pope's palace. Then, he gave us a small box each, saying that it was a gift from the Pope. When I opened it, there was a silver "medal" with the face of the current Pope Pius XI engraved on it. When I left the Vatican and looked at the clock again, it was already 2 p.m., and the visit time was 7 hours in total.
(The above is excerpted from one part of the author's world tour journal)『End』 <113>

羅馬法皇面會記
						李甲秀
獨逸在留時 필자가 宿望이엿든 이태리여행의 길을 떠나게 된 그때는 서력 1925년 4월 중순이엿다. 과거 수천년을 두고 하날를 찌를뜻한 大志를 품은 수백만천의 勇將雄士와 수만수억의 병사가 九死一生의 천신만고를 당하야가면서 嚴寒風雪이 심한 險峻泰嶽를 이기지 못하야 성공의 길을 주저하든 저-놉흔 「알프쓰」지대를 현대문명의 혜택을 밧는다는 떨떨구는 琉璃窓집속에 편안히 안저 그의 유려하고도 험악한 水湖山岳을 바라보면서 수만흔 「톤넬」를 지나 於焉間법황이 살고 잇는 이태리 首府인 羅馬시에 도착되엿다.＜104＞ 차로부터 나리지 이태리의 명물인 마차에 올나 시내를 一瞥하니 엄장하고도 화려스러운 근대적으로부터 틈틈히 섯기여잇는 馥落한 고대식건물이 處處에 웃독웃둑 소사잇는 저-사원이며 街路上이나 마차전차 또는 자동차안에 黑帽黑衣로 전신을 감은 승려들이 빈번한 往來去來야말로 시대의 古今을 물론하고 종교적 도시인 라마가 안이고는 볼 수 업는 고유한 종교적색채가 완전히 나타내엿다. 「호텔」에 이르러 숙소를 정한 후 爲先 고대라마의 舊跡地를 차저 보고나니 발서 일몰서산이여서 금년금일의 「해」도 그만 요때가 최종일껀하는 생각에 고대라마의 과거가 새삼스러히 회상되여 눈을 번쩍 떠 또다시 한번 그의 고적지를 바라보왓섯다.
다음에 방문하려는 곳은 法皇廳이엿스니 이 법황청을 방문하자면 아참 일즉이 준비하여야 한다는 「호텔」주인의 말을 들은 나는 그 잇흔날 早朝에 諸般준비를 맛치고 나니 때는 正히 7시이엿다. 나서서 靑天에는 약간의 백운이 浮來浮往하며 日氣는 매우 晴快하나 얼골에 부듸치는 쌀쌀스러운 바람이야말로 往時 라마귀족적 도시이엿든 「아리스토크라뤽」한 위풍이며 使徒「페-더」와 「포-ㄹ」 兩聖의 순교적 유혈에 조금도 동정이 업섯든 酷態가 아직도 사렂지지 안이한 듯 「락시」를 타고 법황청에 다다르니 수만흔 「락시」 「뻐스」 마차 등이 櫛比하게도 廳前에 내왕하고 잇다. 그들은 이 법황청(Vaticano)의 소속원, 法皇謁見者 그럿치 안이하면 法皇廳拜觀者들을 태고 온 것이 엿스니 내외국인이 매일 배관자가＜105＞ 무려 수삼천에 달하다한다. 청전 광장에 드러서니 널고 넓은 廳庭에는 사각의 백석으로 까러노흔 것이 전부 예술적이엿고 그 광장 중앙에는 놉고놉흔 기념비가 소사잇스며 그 밋헤 주위에는 찬란스러운 전등대가 나열하야잇고 이 碑로부터 멀니 떠러지여 左便에는 二個의 분수장이 잇스니 그곳에는 산중에서 흐르는 폭포수 갓흔 물결이 공중으로 뻗치엿다. 이 광장의 입구로부터 바라보건대 정면에는 고대식건물인 장엄웅대한 사원이 놉히 중천에 소사잇고 그의 최정에 잇는 피뢰침은 백운에 박힌 듯시 희미하게도 보이엿다. 그 사원의 좌우익으로부터 이 광장을 둘너 수백여간의 殿廓을 鬱弓形으로 건축하얏고 그 사원의 좌우편후측에는 법황의 친거하는 궁전, 廳事務及廳務所屬官의 전용하는 전각이 즐비하여잇다. 이 廳전 광장을 엇청어청 緩步하야 사원에까지 다다러 수만흔 백석 층계로부터 사원정문안으로  드러서니 어듬침침한 곳으로부터 몸에 부다치는 찬바람과 恰似한 긔운은 마치 신의 영험이 나를 마지하는 듯 전신에 소름이 끼치며 가삼이 울넝거리엿다. 정신을 반짝차리어 살펴보니 四五천명의 예배자는 무난히 입장할 수 잇슬만한 큰 「호-ㄹ」인 바 그의 웅장한 「세맨트」기둥에 아루새긴 조각이라든지 사면백상 또는 유리창에 그린 오색이 영롱스러운 像畵야말로 예술적 美에 젓고저진 특수한 천재를 가진 이태리민족의 자랑꺼리가 안이고 무엇이랴! 사원의 관람에만 취할 것이 안이라 내가 금일 早朝에 법황청을 차저온 목적은 첫재 법황면회이엿다하고 사원정문에 잇는 간수원의게＜106＞ 수속을 무럿다. 그 사람은 불란서 말로 대답하기를 저곳에 列를 지여 잇는 저사람들이 법황끠 謁見할 사람들이니 그 列에 참가하라기에 나는 이것저것 더 문답할 여가도 업시 시간이나 눗지 안이하엿슬가 염려하야 가장 速步로 그곳에 가서 본 즉 수백여명의 남녀노소가 혼잡하여 잇는바 이사람 저사람의게 무러보와야 혹은 묵묵부답 혹은 요령부득의 이태리말로 답지함으로 아모 방향을 알수 업는지라 그러나 나는 그 복잡한 人山의 틈을 억지로 비기고 압흐로 나아가보니 전면에는 약 三四十명의 列을 지여 정숙히 기립하고 잇슴을 발견하엿다. 그들은 대개 외국서 온듯한 신사숙녀이엿스며 그 열의 좌우측에는 법황청속원이 擁護하야 他人은 열에 접근치 못하도록 감시하고 잇섯다. 그 열중에서 가장 후측에 기립하여잇는 신사 한분＜107＞의게 무른 즉 그는 영국으로부터 온 「카톨릭」(舊敎)신자인데 지금 법황을 謁見코자 드러가는 중이라한다.
