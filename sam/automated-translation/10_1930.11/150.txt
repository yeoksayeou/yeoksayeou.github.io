Pilgrimage to Historic Sites
Empress Dowager Cixi and Mount Wanshou
By Medical Doctor Yi Kapsu

While traveling, I entrusted my weary body to the bed and, having barely slept, was startled awake by the morning sun shining brightly through the window. Opening the window and looking outside, the sun was already high in the Eastern sky, and the clear, harmonious weather of Beiping urged us on our way to Mount Wanshou (萬壽山). After a simple breakfast, we boarded a car and headed towards Mount Wanshou, which I had always longed to see. About 20 *ri* after leaving Beiping's Xizhimen Gate, the car suddenly honked loudly with spirit. Wondering if some "accident" (事故) had occurred, I looked out in surprise, but this was the signal that we had arrived at Mount Wanshou. Unable to contain my joy, I leaped out of the car. Now then, what kind of mountain is this Mount Wanshou? It is a historic site of unparalleled beauty under heaven, holding numerous historical pasts, especially related to the Empress Dowager Cixi (西太后) of the great East Asian Qing Dynasty (淸衣國). Let us quickly go see it. The original name of this mountain was Jinshan (金山), and its old name was Wengshan (甕山). This is because in a corner rock on the south side of the mountain, there is a cave resembling an immortal's chamber (仙室). According to ancient legend, an old man discovered a stone jar (石甕) here. Its size was twice that of ordinary jars, its surface had intricate carvings of flowers and insects (華蟲彫刻文), and inside the jar were dozens of types of real objects. The old man took all the objects, leaving only the stone jar on the west side of the mountain, hence the mountain was called Wengshan. Later, in the 57th year of Qianlong (乾隆), the various streams of Yuquan (玉泉) were guided to flow into the West Lake (西湖), and the West Lake was renamed Kunming Lake (昆明湖). The mountains and waters were picturesque (山水가 罨畫하야), the scenery was superb (絶勝), and the constructions within the garden entirely imitated famous scenic spots of Jiangnan (江南). In the past, warships were floated on the lake, and naval battle exercises were reportedly held every year during the dog days (伏日). Then, during the Opium War (鴉片戰役), when the Anglo-French allied forces <45> burned the Yuanmingyuan (圓明園), this garden was also burned. It was repaired in the 14th year of Guangxu (光緖) and renamed Yiheyuan (頤和園). Later, in the 26th year [of Guangxu], British and Russian troops stationed themselves in this Yiheyuan for about a year before withdrawing after peace negotiations. The palace halls that existed then were somewhat dilapidated (狼籍), so they were rebuilt again in the 29th year [of Guangxu]. Afterwards, from the 3rd year of the Republic of China (民國), it was reportedly opened to the public as a tourist site. Arriving before the main gate of Yiheyuan, the Donggongmen (東宮門), magnificent bronze lions (銅獅), male and female, sat on marble pedestals, staring intently as they welcomed our party. Taking one step, then another, we entered the gate, and there stood the Renshoudian (仁壽殿) towering high. In ancient times, it was the Qinzhengdian (勤政殿), where Emperor Gaozong (高宗皇帝) of the Qing Dynasty (淸朝) attended to state affairs. It was burned down mid-reign and rebuilt in the 16th year of Guangxu, renamed Renshoudian. On the inner eaves (內簷) of this hall hangs Empress Dowager Cixi's own calligraphy reading "Great Perfect True Mirror Plaque" (大圓實鏡額). The brushstrokes are profound and thick (渾厚), truly worthy of being called masterful calligraphy. Between the hall steps (殿陛間), two bronze dragons and two bronze phoenixes each are displayed. From here, going to the northeast corner of the garden, there is the Xiequyuan (諧趣園). Its original name was Huishanyuan (惠山園). In the preface to the Eight Views of Huishanyuan poem composed by Gaozong, there is a line: "One pavilion, one path, is enough to harmonize interest" (一亭一徑足諧奈趣). Thus, the two characters *xiequ* (諧趣) were taken to name it Xiequ Pavilion (諧趣閣). Next is the Zhichuntang (知春堂), where Emperor Wenzong (文宗皇帝) of the Qing Dynasty stayed and reviewed troops. Next is the Zhuxinlou (矚新樓), the former site of the Longyunlou (龍雲樓). It has two stories, upper and lower. The upper story faces the mountain path outside the garden, a vast and boundless (茫茫浩然) pavilion. Descending the stairs from this pavilion leads to the north side of the garden楼's myriad bamboo grove (北修篁萬竿). A cool breeze gradually arrives, solemn and rustling (嚴嚴簫然), evoking thoughts of Weibin (渭濱) and Qi'ao (淇澳). Among the mountain rocks north of the bamboo thicket (叢篁), there is a plank bridge inscribed with the two characters "Lanzhao" (嵐沼 - Mist Marsh). Below the bridge, a small stream's gentle current flows murmuringly (潺流하며) into the pond. On a rock beside the stream, "泉流不息" (Spring flow unceasing) is carved. Among the lake rocks east of the stream, "Luoyue" (蘿月 - Vine Moon) or "Songfeng" (松風 - Pine Wind) are carved. These are said to be inscriptions by Empress Dowager Cixi. To the south of the pavilion is the Chengshuangzhai (澄爽齋), the former site of the Danbizhai (澹碧齋). Next is the Zhiyuqiao (知魚橋 - Bridge of Knowing Fish). Above it is a stone archway (石坊) carved with two couplets by Gaozong. The first says: 「月波自明金爲色」 「風瀨쟁종石有聲」 (Moon waves self-bright, gold is their color / Wind rapids contend, stones have sound). The second says: 「廻翔鳧雁心舍喜」 「新茁萍蒲意總閒」 (Circling ducks and geese, the heart finds joy / Newly sprouting duckweed and reeds, the mind is wholly at ease). To the west of the bridge is the Shuizuoting (水座亭), which was the Shuizuoting in ancient times. Facing south towards the lake is the Leshoutang (樂壽堂), where Empress Dowager Cixi resided. In front of the hall stands a huge rock inscribed with characters. On the front face (定面), it says "Qingzhiting" (靑芝亭 - Blue Fungus Pavilion). On the east face, "Yuying" (玉英 - Jade Blossom). On the west face, "Lianxiu" (蓮秀 - Lotus Grace). These are said to be inscriptions by Emperor Gaozong. It is said Gaozong discovered these during a southern inspection tour and brought them back. To the left and right of this rock, one green cypress (翠柏) is planted each. Their deep green (蒼翠) is lovely. It is said the fruits of these cypress trees were presented to Empress Dowager Cixi during her sixtieth birthday celebration, signifying the wish for longevity like the South Mountain (南山不老). Then, there is a palace hall built following the rising tiers of the mountain path; this is called Paiyuntang (排雲堂). This hall is the former site of Yanshousi Temple (延壽寺). It was rebuilt in the 18th year of Guangxu <46> and was also where Empress Dowager Cixi stayed. Its brilliant opulence (輝煌富麗) likely makes it the foremost among the garden's palace halls. The main gate of this Paiyuntang faces south, namely the Paiyunmen (排雲門). Inside this gate, the east hall is called Yuhuadian (玉華殿), and the west hall is called Yunjindian (雲錦殿). Entering through the second palace gate, inside the gate, the east side is Fanghuidian (芳輝殿), the west side is Zixiaodian (紫霄殿), and at the rear is Dehuidian (德暉殿). Going west again from this Paiyuntang, there is the Qinghuaxuan (淸華軒). This was originally the site of the Luohantang (羅漢堂 - Hall of Arhats) and was built concurrently with the Paiyundian during the reign of Emperor Guangxu. Inside the rear hall of the pavilion, there is a screen made entirely of pure marble. It is over a *zhang* in length and about 8 *ch'i* high. Carved on it are the Record of the Luohantang composed by Emperor Gaozong, a poem titled Xishishi (西師詩), and the stele inscription commemorating the Pacification of the Dzungars (平定準噶爾). It is said to be an extremely valuable artifact. Going around behind the Paiyundian, high on the mountain slope, stands the three-story Foxiangge (佛香閣 - Tower of Buddhist Incense), soaring majestically into the sky, appearing to reach a height of several *zhang*. Inside this three-story pavilion, a Buddhist statue is enshrined, its Dharma body (法身) reaching over a *zhang*. The plaque on its exit door reads "Guiding and Nurturing Righteous Nature" (導養正性). Below it are several hundred stone steps, named Chaozhendeng (朝眞燈 - Lamp for Audience with the True), reaching a height of over 10 *zhang*. This Foxiangge was built during the Ming Dynasty (明朝) at the same time as the Yuanjingsi Temple (圓靜寺). It is also commonly called Fo'erda Duo (福兒達朶). This pavilion is the former site of the Yanshou Pagoda (延壽塔), which was nine stories high but was reportedly burned down by foreigners. Between the mountain rocks to the left and right of the pavilion, there is one pavilion each: the east one is called Fuhuating (敷華亭), the west Lai Xiuting (賴秀亭). Inside both pavilions rests an extremely large clock. Below and to the east of this Foxiangge, built leaning against the layered rocks (層巖), is a pavilion. This is the Zhuanlunzang (轉輪藏 - Revolving Scripture Repository), where Buddhist statues were enshrined in ancient times. In front of this pavilion, to the left and right, there is one wooden pagoda each. This pavilion entirely imitates the Cangji Pavilion (藏繼閣) of Fayun Temple (法雲寺) in Hangzhou (杭州), but lacking the scriptures (藏繼), wooden pagodas substitute for them. In front of this pavilion stands the Stele of Mount Wanshou and Kunming Lake (萬壽山昆明湖碑). This stele is made of pure white marble, with a stone railing surrounding its base. It is over a *zhang* high and several *ch'i* wide. Westward from the Foxiangge is the Baoyunge (寶雲閣). From the windows, railings, beams, and tiles of the pavilion, down to the tabletop furnishings inside, everything is made of brass (黃銅), hence it is commonly called the Bronze Pavilion (銅閣). The inner eaves plaque reads "Treasury of Great Brilliance" (大光明藏). It is said that not a single visitor fails to be astonished and marvel (驚奇糾絶). Besides the halls, pavilions, and towers seen so far, within this Mount Wanshou, there are over 100 large and small palace halls scattered before and behind the mountain, on layered rocks and steep cliffs, high and low, or even isolated by the edges of ponds, lakes, and marshes (池湖沼邊), connected by innumerable stone and plank bridges and long corridors. These are mostly divided into the Front Mountain Road's 8 major locations (山前路 八大處) and the Back Mountain Road (山後路). These countless halls, pavilions, and towers, from the base of the pillars up to their high eaves, are all brilliantly decorated with carvings of dragons and phoenixes in the five colors: blue, red, yellow, black, and white. The roofs are entirely covered with yellow tiles (黃瓦). These rooftop yellow tiles reflect the sunlight shining from the heavens, making the entire mountain garden seem paved with gold. The sound of ringing metal and jade (鳴金鳴玉) striking against each other in the cool breeze blowing through the green leaves of the lush pines, cypresses, willows, and poplars nestled between the palace halls rings clearly (淨淨) in the ears. Amidst all this extreme, unparalleled splendor (華麗無双), one's spirit becomes dazed (精神이 휘황하야지며). It felt as if I were not in the human world, but either in the palace of the Jade Emperor (玉皇帝) in Heaven or surely in the Dragon Palace (龍宮) underwater, in a dreamlike state (비몽사몽). Our party, intoxicated by the sights, was led to a public restaurant inside one of the halls to satisfy our hunger and thirst. Afterwards, we reached the vicinity of the Jilantang (寄瀾堂) near the Shizhangting (石丈亭) within the West Lake. Here lies the Stone Boat called Qingyan Chuan (淸晏船), constructed entirely of marble. In the 29th year of Guangxu, a two-story structure in European-American style (歐美式) was added. The interior of the upper structure is entirely fitted with patterned tiles (花磚), and the windows on all four sides are structured with colored glass (玻璃), making it even more splendidly adorned. Beside this Stone Boat are countless excursion boats, each with one or two boatmen inside, clamoring in their incomprehensible native tongues to get customers onto their respective boats. This lake was historically a place where successive emperors and empresses, especially the heroine of her time, Empress Dowager Cixi, amused themselves. Even today, having lost its master, it still retains an imperial air, and the fares for the excursion boats are surprisingly expensive. However, thinking that in previous eras, one would not have dared to take a boat trip here, we did not haggle over the price and immediately boarded an excursion boat. Floating gently in the middle of the lake, we looked around in all directions. The circumference of Yiheyuan appeared to be about 10 *ri*. On the east, south, and west sides, it is surrounded by a lake with shimmering blue waves (碧波가 蕩溠). The lotus leaves on the lake surface look like a spread of blue silk brocade (綾라綢席). The weeping willows and poplars lined up along the pond edges seem to sadly droop their green branches into the lake, as if waiting for their departed imperial master to return, just like Jiang Taigong's (姜太公) fishing line appeared idle while awaiting the right time. Turning our heads northward to look up at the mountain peak, a mountain range of imposing majesty (嚴峻한 산악) soared high into the sky. Along the tiered ascent of the mountain path, halls, pavilions, and towers stood densely amidst the green shade of lush (膿鬱한) pine and cypress branches. This entire panorama was reflected far into the water below, so there was a Mount Wanshou palace complex on the water's surface and also one in the water's depths, <48> creating a grand, elegant landscape painting (山水圖). Its scenery (景槩) can truly be called the most superb under heaven. This is the historic site that is the eternal pride of the Chinese (支那) people, consequently a great legacy of Empress Dowager Cixi, and simultaneously the yolk (노르자위며 - nucleus, core) of a vast territory (縱橫 數萬里 强土) stretching tens of thousands of *ri* in length and breadth, and what else but the lifeblood (膏血) squeezed from 400 million common people (蒼生)? If Empress Dowager Cixi's spirit, even after death, has not left this Mount Wanshou palace where she spent her entire life indulging in extreme pleasure and luxury, and resides here still, would that spirit, upon seeing us, be endlessly arrogant (傲慢不己), proud that what is hers today is hers? Or would it be endlessly ashamed (慚愧不己) for having squeezed the lifeblood of millions for her own sake? This is not the place to debate good and evil. In any case, as a representative famous historic site of the great East Asian nation, a source of pride for millennia, Empress Dowager Cixi's achievement cannot be said to be small. Its grandeur, magnificence, splendor, and brilliance are said to be incomparable to the palaces of Western (泰西) countries. No matter how much praise the Palace of Versailles (베르사이 궁전) in the French (佛蘭西) kingdom or the Sanssouci Palace (상수씨 궁전) in the Prussian (普魯西) kingdom, renowned for their splendor and scale, receive in continental Europe, how could they compare to this? And even the so-called Million Room within the Vienna Palace (維也納宮殿) in the Austrian (墺) kingdom, famous for its splendor, where Empress Maria Theresa (마리아 테레지아 后) personally resided, how could it possibly match the palace chambers where Empress Dowager Cixi lived? Until seeing this Mount Wanshou, this author, having only seen the palaces of Western countries, could not avoid being a frog in a well (井底蛙).
The excursion boat, floating on the extremely calm water waves, was carried by the gently arriving cool breeze and soon reached a corner by the pond edge. Our party disembarked from the boat and walked along a clean mountain path paved artistically with yellow, black, and white sand and rocks. Passing back through the main gate of Mount Wanshou, the Donggongmen, the driver who had been waiting for us, dozing at the wheel, was startled awake and greeted us. The resounding sound of the car engine echoing back to our ears from the mountain was the sound of farewell exchanged between us and Mount Wanshou.
(Night of October 10th) <48>

Visiting Napoleon's Former Residence in Paris
By Chŏng Sŏkt'ae (鄭錫泰)

Driving the car about 4 *ri* southwest of Paris (巴里市), we arrived at a place called "Malmaison" (마루메-손). There stood a small, secluded building amidst the forest. The scale of this building cannot be considered large, but it possesses a history capable of drawing the public eye, as it was once the residence of the hero Napoleon (나파룬). First, we knocked on the main gate and stated our purpose to the guard, who kindly guided us through the entrance hall, explaining each room. Items related to Napoleon were displayed here and there.
This place, of course, allows one to witness closely where Napoleon lived his daily life. When viewing historical paintings and splendid gold artifacts, anyone could vividly recall the extent of the glorious era of that time.
Among them, what drew my attention most significantly was Napoleon's bedroom. Above the head of the bed hangs a portrait of Madame Maituisŭ (마이투이쓰부인 - likely Empress Marie Louise), who shared Napoleon's lifelong romance. Above the bed in Madame Mailuisŭ's (마이루이쓰부인) bedroom also hangs a portrait of Napoleon, seeming to speak of their romantic marriage. The bedding lies just as it was used back then. The tableware in the dining room, the chair he sat in every day, the desk – nothing fails to evoke meaning in the viewer. Napoleon seemed to have considerable taste in music, as there was a separate music room where period musical instruments (古樂器) lay as they were used then. In the room right next to it, one could see the washstand where Napoleon washed his face every morning, and next to that, the gymnasium, all visible in detail. <48>
Entering the meeting room again, there was a long table in the center, and beside it, several dozen chairs. It was here that important meetings were usually held, where plans for the so-called globe (地球儀) were devised, and where the grand dream (雄夢) of conquering the world was decided upon – all conceived in this room.
Arriving at another place, there was the study he used. Once inside this room, though there are windows facing outward, the entrance/exit is not easily found. This room's walls are lined full of books, and the doorway itself is designed to hold books, so once the door is closed, it transforms into a bookshelf, becoming unrecognizable. Another curious item is Napoleon's desk and chair, which, when not in use, become book holders, transforming into an oval shape.
Looking briefly at the books he used, they were all materials for surveying the world and researching conquest. The image of his lover, Madame Mailuisŭ, bringing warm tea into this study while her husband was engrossed in research, seemed to flash before my eyes, making it impossible to suppress the imagination.
There is also the bed where the hero lay during his final moments, and above it, many wall paintings. There is a portrait showing him formally, hand on chest, viewed from the side. Another portrait shows him unhappily with his hands clasped behind his back; this was his unique posture when he was displeased or dissatisfied, pacing the room endlessly with his hands behind his back. Although I have never seen him, seeing these accounts and the actual various items he used makes him seem strangely like a close relative to me.
In another place, there is a historical sacred painting (聖畵). In this painting, Napoleon stands beside a soundly sleeping guard, holding the gun the guard had held. This depicts a time during the Russian campaign (露國), late at night in the field, when Napoleon, sleeplessly inspecting the camp, found a guard overcome by fatigue and briefly asleep. He stood in place of this soldier, holding the infantryman's gun. Afterwards, when the soldier awoke and saw the Emperor Napoleon acting as a soldier in his stead, he was so moved that he requested execution for himself, but Napoleon graciously forgave him, earning the great gratitude (大大的 感意) of the exhausted soldiers. This was that historical painting.
Is it only things like this? Countless other historically famous items are scattered everywhere.
Finishing this viewing, we went out again into the garden. There is a place where the Napoleon couple used to stroll during their leisure time amidst the trees, and the carriage used for their wedding also exists to this day.
Ah, where does this great hero, who schemed for the world, finding the universe too small, roam now?
Who could have known that a mere islander raised on Corsica (콜시카島) would start revolutions and nation-building, dream limitlessly of world domination, and astonish the world with rough winds and harsh waves?
Yet, his final traces are too pitiful. Meeting his final fate on Saint Helena (센트헬라), attached to Africa in the Southern Hemisphere, bringing his life's curtain down – who could have ever dreamed of this?
Now this Malmaison residence, having lost its master, evokes tears from nameless visitors like myself across hundreds of long years. <49> If the hero's spirit exists and thinks upon this, his heart must ache endlessly.
The sad autumn rain falls quietly,
The traveler's heart is further distressed.
Who blamed the affairs of human life?
Did they not know life, like flint sparks, is a spring dream?
We who receive the jests of fate,
What reason is there to fight over the unknown future?

Red Russia's Tolstoy Museum
By Scholar from Red Square in the Russian Capital

Looking ahead from Kropotkin Street (쿠로포토킨 街) in Moscow (莫斯科), one can see the museum of the literary giant Tolstoy (톨스토이). The Tolstoy Museum is that white, three-story building whose front yard is shaded by old trees. I sought out this building hurriedly after unpacking my luggage in Moscow (모스코바). A notice was posted at the gate:
"Open Wednesday, Friday, Sunday from 10 AM to 3 PM. Free admission on Sundays only."
Reaching the reception desk, I asked a Russian woman, who looked about 30, in English,
"How much is the admission fee?"
"30 kopecks," she replied.
I paid the entrance fee and also bought all the available picture postcards and Tolstoy guidebooks sold nearby, then entered the main building.
Opening the door on the right and stepping inside, this was the first room. A sign read "Tolstoy's Sevastopol (센스트볼) and Kazan (가잔) Period." And a marble bust of his brother, Count Nikolai (니코라이 伯爵), enclosed in thin glass, hung on the wall. There wasn't much to see from the Kazan period, but the second room had far more exhibits. Starting with a model evoking the scene of his final moments when, despising civilization, he reached the small southern Russian (南露) village of Yasnaya Polyana (야스나야 뽀리야나) <50> and died, there was the original manuscript handwriting from when he wrote his great masterpiece "War and Peace" (戰爭과 平和). Also, the clothes, hat, and shoes he wore during his marriage in his youth were all displayed cleanly as if from yesterday. Although they are all 60-70 years old, the bride's pure white gloves and the pink ribbon and artificial flowers pinned in her hair truly steep one in the sorrow of remembrance. The next room was labeled "Second Period of Creation." There, all his collected works, photographs, handwriting, etc., were displayed even more completely.

Next was the "Religious Philosophy Period" room, covering 1890 to 1900. There, one could glimpse the face of the old seeker of truth, Tolstoy (杜翁). The short staff (短杖) and rubashka (루바시까) he wore in life were displayed, bringing to mind the image of the white-bearded old religious figure walking from village to village, leaning on his staff. And the pen, ink, manuscript paper, etc., he used when writing.
And in the fifth room, sad words were written: "Flight from Yasnaya Polyana and Death."
This is the room of tears, showing the entirety of the literary giant's final moments. Indeed, what first catches the eye is something like an iron pipe nailed to one spot on the wall. This was the pine torch (솔불) he carried in his left hand to light the way when searching through streets late at night. (Remainder omitted - original text) <53>

**Summary**

This collection of three travelogues from 1930 describes visits by Korean authors to significant historical sites: the Summer Palace in Beijing (Yi Kapsu), Napoleon's residence Malmaison near Paris (Chŏng Sŏkt'ae), and the Tolstoy Museum in Moscow (pseudonymous author). Yi Kapsu marvels at the grandeur of the Summer Palace, reflecting on Empress Dowager Cixi's legacy and comparing its splendor favorably to European palaces. Chŏng Sŏkt'ae explores Malmaison, contemplating Napoleon's rise and fall and the personal artifacts that evoke his presence. The third author visits the Tolstoy Museum, tracing the writer's life through exhibits, particularly focusing on his later years and death. From a Korean colonial history perspective, these articles are interesting as they show Korean intellectuals engaging with major world historical sites and figures during a period of limited national agency. Yi Kapsu's assertion of the Summer Palace's superiority over Western palaces can be seen as a form of cultural pride or resistance, while the reflections on fallen heroes like Napoleon or philosophical figures like Tolstoy might subtly resonate with the authors' own constrained circumstances under Japanese rule. The mere fact of these travels and published accounts indicates a layer of Korean society that maintained intellectual connections with the wider world despite colonial restrictions.

遺跡巡禮
西太后와 萬壽山
醫學搏士 李甲秀

여행 중에 피곤한 몸을 침상에 실니여 살잠을 다 자노라니 窓戶에 빗치는 朝陽에 눈이 부시여 놀라 번적 뜨며 窓을 열고 밧글 내여다 보니 발서 해는 東域 中天에 놉히 소사 잇고 晴和스러운 北平天氣는 우리의 萬壽山行을 促告하고 잇다. 조반을 간단히 畢하고 자동차에 올라 於恒心에 동경하여 오던 萬壽山으로 향하게 되엿다. 北平西直門을 나서 약 20리 되는 곳에서 별안간 자동차는 豪氣스럽게 뿡뿡 소리를 친다. 무삼 「악시덴트」(事故)나 생기엿나 하고 놀라 내여다 보니 이것이 萬壽山에 도착하엿다는 신호임으로 반가움을 이기지 못하야 자동차에서 몸을 날리여 뛰여나렷다. 자- 그려면 이 萬壽山은 어떠한 산인가? 東亞 大國인 靑衣國의 歷代 帝后 특히 西太后를 모시여 허다한 역사적 과거를 가진 天下絶勝의 古蹟地인 바 어서 구경 하여보자. 이 산의 原名은 金山이요 舊名은 甕山이라 하엿다. 이는 山의 南便 어느 모퉁이 암석에 仙室과 흡사한 一洞穴이 잇는 바 자고로 相傳하야 오는 말이 고대에 한 노인이 이곳에서 石甕 一個를 발견하엿스며 그 大小가 보통 옹기보다 배나 더 크며 그 표면에는 華蟲彫刻文이 잇고 甕 중에는 수십여종이 실물이 들어 잇섯는대 그 노인이 실물은 전부 가저가고 石甕만 산의 西便에 留置하엿슴으로 이 산을 甕山이라 칭하엿다 한다. 그 후 乾隆 57년에 이르러 玉泉의 諸流를 인도하야 西湖에 排流케 하고 西湖를 昆明湖라 改名하엿스며 산수가 罨畫하야 풍경이 絶勝하고 그 園內의 造作이 전부 江南의 명승지를 모방하엿스며 往時에는 호수에 戰船을 띄여노코 매년 伏日에는 水戰을 연습하엿다 한다. 그리고 鴉片戰役時에 英佛聯合軍이＜45＞ 巴里市外의 서남으로 약 4리 가량 자동차를 날리니 『마루메-손』이란 곳을 도착하엿다. 그곳에는 수림 속에 은윽한 적은 건물이 잇섯다. 이 건축의 규모는 그리 크다고는 볼 수가 업스나 세인의 이목을 끌만한 역사를 가지고 잇스니 이는 한때에 영웅 『나파룬』이가 거주하든 곳이다. 爲先 대문을 두다려 수위에게 訪意를 告한즉 쾌히 안내를 하여 현관을 것처 각방에 대한 설명을 하여 준다. 거기에는 『나파룬』에 관한 물품이 처처에 진열되여 잇섯다.

其中에서도 나의 주목을 끄으Š” 가장 중요한 것은 『나파룬』의 침실이다. 침대 머리 마테는 『나파룬』의 일생의 로맨쓰를 가지게 하엿든 마이투이쓰부인의 화상이 걸리여 잇스며 마이루이쓰부인침실 침대 우에는 또한 『나파룬』의 초상이 걸리여 잇슴은 그들의 로맨쓰의 결혼을 말하는 듯 하엿섯고 침구는 당시 사용튼 그냥 그대로 노여잇다. 식당에 잇는 식기이나 그가 매일가티 안저 잇든 의자 書机 등 무엇이나 보는 이로 하여 무의미하게 하지 안는다. 『나파룬』은 음악에도 상당한 취미을 가젓슴인지 따로 음악실이 잇서서 당시에 쓰든 古樂器 그대로가 노여 잇섯다. 그 바로 엽 방에는 奈翁이 매일 아츰이면 낫슬 씻든 세면대이며 그 엽흐로 체조실 등을 낫낫치 볼 수가 잇섯섯다.＜48＞
다시금 회의실로 들어를 가니 그 중앙에 긴 테볼이 잇고 그 엽흐로는 數十介의 의자가 잇서 일상 이곳에서 중요회의를 개최하여 소위 地球儀를 案謀하고 세계를 정복하려 雄夢을 決裁하든 곳이 모다 이 방에서 꾀하든 것이엿다.
또 한곳을 당도한즉 그가 쓰든 書齋室이 잇다. 이 방에 들어만 가면 외면에 향한 창만 잇지 출입구는 용이히 차질 수가 업다. 이 방은 벽에다 서적이 가득이 싸여 잇서 출입문에도 서적을 넛케 하여 들어는 갓서도 출입문만 닷치면 서적장으로서 변하여 알 수가 업게 되엿다. 또 한가지는 奈翁이 쓰든 책상과 의자가 기이한대 사용치 아늘 때에 서적 넛케만 되며 타원형으로 변하게 만드럿다.
잠시 그가 쓰든 서적을 보매 모도가 세계를 엿보아 정복을 연구하든 재료뿐이며 夫君이 열중히 연구에 몰두할 때에 애인, 마이루이쓰부인이 따뜻한 차를 만들어 가지고 이 서재실로 들어오는 형상이 눈에 빗치는 듯 상상을 금치 못하겟다.
또 한아은 영웅 최후 임종에 누엇든 침대도 잇고 그 우에는 만흔 벽화가 잇스니 正色으로 가슴에 손을 대리고 엽흐로 보이게 한 畵像도 잇스며 또 하나는 초상으로서 뒤짐을 지은 불쾌한 그림이 잇스니 이는 奈翁이 일상 유쾌치 안커나 불만이 잇슬 때에는 그이의 독특한 자태인대 뒤ㅅ짐을 지고서 방안을 수 업시 왓다갓다하는 것이다. 나는 그를 본 적은 업스나 이러한 내역과 실지로 그가 사용튼 제반 기구을 볼 때에는 그와 나와는 이상하게도 갓가운 친척갓다 하여도 보인다.
또 한곳에 역사적 聖畵가 잇는대 이 그림은 奈翁이 곤히 잠이 든 守兵 엽헤 서서 守兵이 쥐엿든 총을 들고 섯스니 이는 露國을 정벌하여 戰地에서 深夜 奈翁이 잠을 자지 안코 진 안을 순회튼 차 一守兵이 피뢰한 잠을 익이지 못하여 잠시 잠이 들엇슬 때 이 一軍兵 代身으로 其 步兵이 쥐엿든 총을 들고 잇섯든 것이엿다. 其後 보병이 잠을 깨고 보니 군왕인 奈翁이 자기 대신 軍兵이 됨을 보고 엇지 감격이 되엿든지 스사로 사형을 원하엿스나 특히 관대히 용서하여서 피뢰한 兵卒들의 大大的 感意을 삿다든 역사적 그림이엿다.
비단 이러한 것만이리요. 수 만흔 其 역사적 유명한 물품으로만 수 업시 널리여 잇다.
이러한 참관을 끗을 막고서 다시 정원으로 나와 수림사이로 奈翁부부끼리 閑暇한 틈을 엇어 산보하든 곳도 잇스며 결혼시에 쓰든 마차도 다 금일까지 존재케 하여 잇다.
아-이 우주가 좁다고 천하를 도모하든 일대 영웅 지금은 어대로 배회하는고.
當初 콜시카島에서 자라난 一島民으로서 혁명과 국가의 건설을 비롯하여 세계를 두고 雄夢을 한업시 꾸기도 하엿스며 것친 바람과 모진 파란을 世人을 놀라게 할 줄 그 뉘 알엇스랴.
그나마 마즈막 자최 너머나 가엽도다. 남반구 아프리까에 달린 것은 『센트헬라』에서 마즈막 운명으로서 일생종막을 나리게 하엿스니 이를 夢想이나 한 이는 그 뉘이엿드냐.
지금 이 『마루메-손』주택은 주인을 일코 멧백년 오랜 세월을 두고서 나가튼 무명의 訪客으로서 오직 눈물을 자으게 하니＜49＞ 영웅의 혼이 잇서 생각게 된다면 그이 가슴은 한업시 쓰라릴 것이다.

赤露 「톨스토이」 博物舘
露都에서 赤廣場學人

莫斯科의 『쿠로포토킨』街에서 압흘 내다보면 문호 『톨스토이』박물관이 보임니다. 토翁박물관은 늙은 나무가 뜰 압흘 가리운 白堊의 3層建物이 바로 그것이니 나는 『모스코바』에 旅裝을 풀어노키 밧브게 이 건물을 차젓소이다. 門前에는 貼紙가 부터 잇는데
『水, 金, 日은 오전 10시부터 오후 3시까지 개관, 단 일요일에 한하야 무료로 참관시킨다』
함이엿다. 接付口에 이르러 番을 보는 한 30먹어 보이는 露西亞여자더러 영어로
『참관료는 얼마임니까?』
한즉
『30고베이크』
라고 한다. 나는 입장료를 내여주고 또 그 겨테서 파는 繪葉書와 杜翁안내서 등을 잇는대로 다 산 뒤 본관으로 들어갓다.
右便쪽 문을 열고 안으로 발을 드려노흐니 이것이 第1室 『톨스토이의 『센스트볼 及 가잔時代』라고 한 貼紙가 부터 잇다. 그러고 그의 동생인 니코라이 伯爵의 대리석상이 엷은 유리에씨여서 壁上에 걸리여 잇다. 가잔시대에는 별로 볼 것이 없섯스나 第2室에 들어가서 출품이 훨신 만하엿다. 그가 문명을 시려하야 南露의 小村 『야스나야 뽀리야나』에 이르러＜50＞ 임종할 때 광경을 연상시키게 하는 그때 모형을 비롯하야 翁의 一代의 大作 『전쟁과 평화』를 쓰든 原稿筆蹟이 잇고 또 翁이 청년시대에 결혼 당시 쓰든 의복 모자 신발들이 모다 어제런 듯 깨끗하게 진열이 되여 잇다. 모다 6,70年前 것이라 하나 신부가 소비든 순백장갑이며 머리에 꼬잣든 분홍색 리봉과 조화 등이 실로 추억의 哀傷에 젓게 한다. 그라고 그 다음 室은 『第2期의 創作시대』라고 씨여 잇는데 거기에는 모든 着作集과 사진, 필적 등이 더욱 완전하게 노여 잇섯다.

그 다음에는 1890년으로부터 1900년에 이르는 『종교철학시대』室인 바 거기에는 늙은 求道者 杜翁의 면목을 엿불 수 잇게 생전에 입고 다니든 短杖과 루바시까들이 진열되여 힌수염이 성성한 老宗敎家가 이 마을 저 마을 집행이 집고 다니는 모양이 면전에 떠으르게 한다. 그러고 집필할 때 쓰든 펜과 잉큰 원고지 등등.
그러고 第5室에는 슬푼 문자가 씨여 잇섯스니 즉 『야스나야 뽀리야나의 遁亡과 死』라 함이다.
이것이 文豪의 임종을 전부 보여주는 눈물의 방이다. 과연 먼저 눈을 뜨이는 것은 壁上 한 곳에 鐵管가튼 것이 못박여 잇다. 이것은 어두운 밤 杜翁이 이 거리 저 거리 차저다닐 때 길을 밝히기 위하야 왼손에 들고 다니든 솔불이엿다.(하략-원문)＜53＞
