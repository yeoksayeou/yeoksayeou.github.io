Article Title: 男便 在獄·亡命 中 妻의 守節問題 (The Issue of a Wife's Chastity While Her Husband is Imprisoned or in Exile)
Author: 설문 (Survey/Symposium - Multiple Authors)
Article Type: 설문 (Survey/Symposium)

**男便 在獄·亡命 中 妻의 守節問題 (The Issue of a Wife's Chastity While Her Husband is Imprisoned or in Exile)**

**Maintain Chastity Absolutely!**
Song Pong'u (宋奉瑀)

The formation of a relationship between the two sexes occurs where love and trust unite. If there is only love and no trust, it is merely the temporal act of wandering men and women; if there is only trust and no love, it ends in mechanical movement. There is one more thing: the guarantee of living conditions. Even if the former two conditions are perfectly met, if this latter condition is absent, everything collapses. Among these three conditions, not all apply to the imprisoned person. The latter condition is far removed from the imprisoned person. Why is this so? Because they belong to Chosŏn, where the organization of that movement is not yet well-ordered.

However, the first two conditions must necessarily be united, but reality is different. Love may be more passionate than that of ordinary people, but the lack of trust brings feelings of emptiness, tastes of anguish, and the staging of tragedy. Who would not trust each other at first? But since it is a union of formless and transient affection, even if living and eating together constantly, it is difficult to guarantee no gaps, let alone for someone on the path to prison or whose exiled hiding is transient, like a person who rises in Manchuria today and shouts in Tokyo tomorrow?

For the group of travelers who cast aside self-interest and walk the bloody path wearing a crown of thorns for the sake of truth and righteousness, their place of rest and solace for their weary minds and bodies is their wife. If another person's pillow lies in that place of rest, and another person's fingerprint is imprinted on the comforting hand, what would their hearts feel?

The wife of an imprisoned person must be a comrade. She must be a woman who is a comrade with both love and trust running parallel. She must be a woman whose weight of trust is greater than that of love. A wife who is a comrade but lacks trust, on the contrary, creates many great problems. This means that problems arise as love shifts.<36>

It may not matter if one seeks a new love when the old love disappears, but the side effects arising from transferring love become a source of trouble. Taking digestive medicine for indigestion is a good thing. But one must know that the arising side effects cause more harm than the help they provide for digestion. Therefore, a trustworthy wife who is a comrade prevents shifts in position and, of course, does not cause side effects. Whether her husband is imprisoned, in hiding, or in exile, because she has made a trustworthy vow regarding her husband, she awaits their reunion without any abnormality concerning her chastity.

What I have said above is the ideal attitude for the wife to take, but physiologically, can she truly maintain strict chastity during her husband's long absence? Even if she is a wife who is a comrade with both love and trust, can she manage it? There will be those who insert such doubts.

However, I cannot affirm this objection. She must maintain it strictly, no matter what.
I assert that a wife who cannot maintain it is a courtesan-type wife, and it is because she lacks the pride and cultivation of character.

If her love for her husband cools, seeking a new love is a separate issue, regardless of whether the husband is present or absent. But if her love has not cooled, yet she cannot control her physiological impulses simply because her husband is absent and hunts for flesh (肉獵), what is she if not one of the worshippers of lumps of flesh (肉塊)? Those who consider the friction of flesh against flesh as omnipotent only destroy the light and authority of the whole personality. Such a wife is a flesh-hunting (獵肉) woman, a debauched (放淫) woman. She cannot be the wife of an exile or prisoner.

I shout loudly:
The wife of an imprisoned person must be a female comrade with trust and love, and whether her husband is absent for a short or long term, she must strictly maintain her chastity...
(If I had more time, there is much more I would write, but I regret that things do not go as intended. I will take up my pen again on this issue at a later date.)

**Ideals and Reality are Different.**
Hŏ Chŏngsuk (許貞淑)

I know this issue has already been presented to Chosŏn society as an important task for several years now. Thus, several figures who became targets of criticism have emerged, and I believe that in the future, as the number of imprisoned persons increases, the issue of chastity for women corresponding to this situation will arise complexly and diversely throughout all of Chosŏn.

Thinking about it, this is not necessarily a task confined only to Chosŏn women. I believe it is something that inevitably arises wherever government suppression of thought movements is severe, whether in Germany, America, England, France, or any other country. Therefore, I think it is right for everyone on the front lines of the movement to establish a firm view on this matter beforehand.

However, I think this way: It is a fact that the education and refinement of Chosŏn women are low compared to foreign women, and it is also a fact that they face many economic restrictions. For instance, suppose a woman has lived trusting only her husband, and one day he leaves on a path of exile, unsure if he will ever return.<37> Or, in the case where he begins a long prison life of 4 or 5, or 6 or 7 years, no matter how strong her devotion and yearning for that one person usually is, I believe that in the vast majority of cases, she will be driven by the difficulty of making a living imposed by Chosŏn's reality and find herself unable to avoid remarriage. Also, since she is the wife of a husband active on the movement front lines, the wife's age is often in her youth. Being young, there is often no way to easily block the loneliness that approaches physiologically, and I believe that cases of refusing a life of falsehood regarding instinct are also numerous in actual situations. Therefore, Chosŏn women, who have considerably less sexual and economic liberation compared to Germany or America, should perhaps have even more freedom compared to those foreign women.

Furthermore, even among wives of imprisoned persons, there must be distinctions, such as cases where the man and woman are comrades (e.g., members of the same party) and cases where they are not, merely a couple where one understands the other's ideology to some extent. In the case of comrades, this issue becomes even more complex and serious. But generally speaking, in principle, it is ideal for the wife to maintain chastity until they reunite, whenever that may be, and it would be good to generally hope for that. However, entering into the practical problems of economic relations, sexual relations, etc., I believe it will be difficult to implement ideals in the same way. If Chosŏn women too could find a means of livelihood even without a husband, the problem might be resolved differently...

**Sexual Desire Must Be Endured.**
Yi Tŏgyo (李德耀)

Before rendering a judgment on whether to maintain chastity or not regarding this issue, I would first like to speak of my experience. I, too, sent H. far away across the sea and have been living a lonely life for the third year now. Thus, sometimes this issue becomes "my own directly faced problem" awaiting my judgment.

However, because my longing for him is growing increasingly intense, I have vowed to myself that it is right to maintain chastity for his sake, and have lived this way until today. Of course, since my age is still that of a young woman, sometimes I yearn for the opposite sex unbearably (やる瀨ない - *yaru senai*, Japanese: unbearable, helpless), and especially when I fall ill and lie alone in bed, the desire for a companion of the opposite sex arises unavoidably.

Nevertheless, recalling his situation, enduring all sorts of hardships (風霜) overseas, and the affection (情誼) from when we spent time together in the past appears before my eyes, and I grit my teeth and repel all the temptations that approach me from my surroundings.

Therefore, when looking at this issue presented before us, I believe that if "love, the love of comrades," has not yet cooled, then it is naturally right for her to maintain chastity always. There are those who say, "How can one endure sexual desire? How can one endure that physiological instinct while the husband is in exile or imprisoned? Chastity is a lie. Can that even happen?" claiming inability to wait primarily because of sexual desire. But frankly, based on my experience, I have endured it for three years. If love was passionate, and if that passion never cools, sexual desire!<38>
Why couldn't one endure that trifle for 100 years! Also, in cases where it's absolutely unbearable, it might be possible to maintain the love for the former beloved while forming temporary relationships with other men from time to time.

However, thinking again, since I am a woman who has secured a way to live with the profession of a female doctor, the issue simply places importance on sexual desire. But for a woman whose means of livelihood are completely cut off because her husband went to prison or overseas, given today's social reality, her inability to maintain chastity will likely be unstoppable.

Therefore, I think that if there is love, it is right to maintain chastity always. If one has food to eat, why couldn't one endure that trifle of sexual desire?

**Act Consciously During Absence**
Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng (丁七星)

This issue has already been handed down as a major point of contention in our women's society. Hasn't the issue of comrades So-and-so already arisen? Regarding this, I hold the following judgment.

First, let's say the husband, weary from the movement, is bedridden for a long time due to illness, unable even to get up. At that time, I believe it is right for the wife to naturally provide nursing care (病救援) with all sincerity. Even if he fell ill while striving for personal glory, it would be proper human feeling (常情) to do so, let alone for a body that was busy with great work. As much as possible, she should buy medicine (藥石) to serve him, warmly boil even a spoonful of gruel for him, hoping for the day he can rise again. And by always showing a peaceful countenance (낫빗) beside him, she should ensure he does not suffer mental anguish. I believe it is right for everyone, in principle, to wish to do this.

Second, even while the husband is serving a long prison sentence of 5 or 6, or 7 or 8 years, it is right for the wife to still wait for him. Similarly, if he is in exile and absent, the woman who is his lover should sit counting the days, waiting for her beloved husband to return.

However, an ideologue (主義者) is also human, and a thinker (思想家) is also an animal. Is there a way to block the physiological suffering in a hot-blooded young body?
Everyone clearly knows it is right to maintain chastity and endure that period, but how can one truly repel the physiological desire that sweeps through a young body with the changing seasons (春秋)? Demanding unlimited endurance of sexual desire, a human instinct, seems no different from the outdated (時代遲) morality that forced chastity upon widows.

Therefore, while recognizing in my head that failing to maintain chastity is not good, when I see a wife unable to maintain chastity, I instead feel human sympathy.

So, where can we find a path to judgment on this issue? I will give an example. Let's say a certain female ideologue, whose husband and comrade entered prison, could not endure the physiological desire during his absence and ran to a second man. Even so, when her originally beloved husband finishes his sentence and is released from prison, it is naturally right that she should return to his embrace.<39> That period – that transitional act (過程的行爲) – should rightly be considered something done consciously (意識的) and passively (消極的). I believe the hint of what I want to say is sufficiently contained within this, so I will not speak further beyond that.

Also, there is a tendency to attribute the issue of maintaining or not maintaining chastity to economic relations, but I believe that rather than that having such a large effect, physiological relations are also a major part. Moreover, living in such an atmosphere, one frequently encounters many people of the opposite sex, so inevitably, instances where a woman is tempted naturally increase, and there is no way to stop this.

**Failing to Maintain Chastity is Natural**
Yu Yŏngjun (劉英俊)

I heard that some time ago, there was a wife with children who visited her husband, who was imprisoned in Sŏdaemun Prison after receiving a heavy 15-year sentence for committing a crime of shamelessness (破廉恥罪). Seeing her husband in the visiting room, she asked for a divorce, saying she could not live looking forward to him for the next 15 years, but the husband stubbornly refused. A guard nearby, finding it absurd, tried to persuade the prisoner, "Isn't it better to separate? Doesn't she have no way to eat and no way to raise the children? Can't she live unless she remarries?" But the prisoner reportedly got angry instead and replied,
"Whatever happens, I love that person, so I can't help it."

Indeed, in this case, should the wife wait for the husband sentenced to 15 years and still maintain her chastity? Similarly, for a wife whose exiled husband's return date is unknown, no matter how deep the comrade morality and loyalty, should she just sit and wait for his return until her youth turns to white hair?
Setting aside the small number of women with iron-like determination and refinement, looking at the general ordinary woman, would that be possible?

I believe that in reality, it is impossible. How can one block sexual instinct and also obtain the means (質) of life as a single person?
In a world placed in such extraordinary circumstances (非常한 처지) as our society, it is natural for the wife of an imprisoned person to fail to maintain chastity. Maintaining it should rather be considered an exceptional fact.
However, seeking another man the day after imprisonment is something that not only socialists but also ordinary humans morally cannot do.

And it is certain that love will cool without physical contact. Whether there are children or not is a fourth or fifth-level issue in that case; when love is cooling, why should one be bound (拘泥) by children? Of course, her departure will cause much pain to her beloved in prison, but continuing unwanted patient endurance (忍從) – how is that different from the feudal ideology (封建思想) that forced chastity on widows?
Therefore, I venture to say that failing to maintain chastity is natural.

**Endure for Three Years**
Kim Il'yŏp (金一葉)

For a man and woman to reach a state of mutual love, it is never solely based on shared thoughts and feelings. That there must necessarily be physical contact is a fact no one can deny.

Now, if the husband is bedridden with a long illness, or enters prison, or is in exile and absent, can the wife maintain chastity? Morality would say it is right to maintain chastity. However, a life lived without falsehood would teach that this morality already becomes an obstacle to life. Furthermore, in a society like ours where the husband's absence immediately means lack of food (糧食) and clothes (衣服), in cases accompanied by such economic threats, demanding that fidelity (節槪) for one person be maintained indefinitely would indeed be a difficult task. It would be a difficult task like forcing ritual suicide (殉死).

However, I am absolutely not saying that one should seek another man the day after the husband becomes absent. That is something one cannot bear to do as a human being. Even considering loyalty (義理)...
Therefore, while fighting against all sorts of physical hardship (肉體的苦難) and economic pressure (經濟的逼迫), I believe it is right for the beloved person to grit her teeth and maintain chastity for at least three years.

If after waiting for three years, he still has not returned, then it is likely that irresistible force (不可抗力) will follow. It would be good to consider that loyalty has been fulfilled to a certain extent.

In short, it is right to maintain chastity as much as possible, but I know that in actual fact, it is extremely difficult to do so. Therefore, I believe it would be good if enduring for three years became the principle.<41>

**Summary**

This symposium presents diverse opinions from prominent Korean figures in 1930 on whether wives of politically imprisoned or exiled husbands should remain chaste ("maintain chastity" - 守節, 수절). Song Pong'u argues for absolute fidelity based on love, trust, and shared ideology, equating infidelity with betrayal and lack of character. Hŏ Chŏngsuk acknowledges the ideal but emphasizes the harsh realities faced by Korean women: low education, economic dependence, and the physiological needs of youth, suggesting more freedom might be necessary compared to Western women. Yi Tŏgyo shares her personal experience of enduring loneliness for three years fueled by love, arguing sexual desire can be controlled if love persists and economic means exist. Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng advocates waiting but shows sympathy for failure due to unbearable physiological urges, suggesting infidelity could be a temporary, conscious act until the husband's return. Yu Yŏngjun controversially argues that failing to maintain chastity is natural and almost inevitable given the extreme circumstances, economic hardship, and biological needs, criticizing forced fidelity as feudalistic. Kim Il'yŏp proposes a compromise: enduring for three years as a demonstration of loyalty, after which succumbing to necessity might be understandable.

For historians, this article offers a fascinating window into the social pressures and evolving ethical debates within Korean society under Japanese colonial rule, particularly concerning women involved with the independence or socialist movements. It highlights the immense personal sacrifices demanded by political activism, the stark conflict between traditional Confucian ideals of female chastity and the practical/biological challenges faced by women, the nascent awareness of female sexuality and economic autonomy, and the varying attempts to reconcile ideology (comradeship, loyalty) with human needs and harsh colonial realities. The differing viewpoints reflect the complex social dynamics and ideological currents of the era.
