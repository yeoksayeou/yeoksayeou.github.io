觀菊詩筳記 (Record of a Chrysanthemum Viewing Poetry Gathering)

崔義順 (Ch'oe Ŭi-sun)

Publishing a diary is something I both know and don't know.
No one would want to publish it exactly as it is, nor could they. It felt awkward to refuse the brilliant new plan of the respected Editor of Samch'ŏlli (三千里), so I reluctantly gathered some entries to send.

Month 0, Day 0
As soon as I opened my eyes, I went down to the yard.
I quickly weeded the vegetable patches in the front, back, and side yards, but breakfast preparations were late.
After finishing the task I had been putting off for several days, the meal tasted especially sweet.
After eating, I sowed the flower seeds I hadn't managed to plant yet.
They were seeds obtained late from a faraway land, and coincidentally, they were planted by my clumsy hands, so I wait anxiously for them to sprout. Moreover, he said, "Make sure you plant them today," before leaving for the countryside. I hope the bank-colored cotyledons raise their heads neatly soon, so I can boast about my skill.

Month 0, Day 0
Every day is filled with the same routine: preparing meals, attending to the adults and children of the household, answering the door and the telephone, cleaning the house, plus laundry and sewing. Truly, a housewife's free time is precious beyond measure.
"Oro" is a faithful dog. As if watching over me sitting up late, he occasionally dashes out of his house and barks "컹컹".
I remembered the trick the puppy performed in front of the young master during the day and chuckled to myself.<76>

Month 0, Day 0
Just as I spread out Father's ramie summer trousers to make them, an order came down to take Mother to see the Myŏngch'ijwa (明智座) theater, so after a long time, I went out with her.
Before the movie started, I explained the story to her and occasionally whispered explanations of the scenes into her ear, so she watched it with great interest.
"When they show something good, it's worth showing to the young folks. That war film was even more worth watching."
I was able to hear such words from her.
On the way back, I bought clothes and socks for the child.
Then, in the evening, I went to Ch'ŏngnyongsa (淸瀧寺) temple in Sŏngbuk-tong, where Father is staying to escape the summer heat. Carrying the pots of prepared braised crucian carp, shrimp and radish salad, fried red pepper paste, and restorative soup.
The figures of the nuns following us out into the darkness to bid farewell looked exceedingly lonely.

Month 0, Day 0
Having not prepared ingredients for the morning side dishes, I thought for a moment and then searched under the pumpkin vines in the backyard. Two dew-moistened young zucchinis, five or six freshly formed green peppers, and three or four eggplants - picking these made me feel reassured.
After picking the year's persimmons and giving them as gifts to the house upstairs, the house in front, and the house with the second floor, I felt several times more cheerful than just splitting and eating them at home.
Last spring, I gave quite a few gifts of the green garlic that he likes, but today's gift felt more impressive (번치).
I sliced well-formed tomatoes, sprinkled white sugar over them, and took them over, saying, "Have a cool slice." He would invariably just say, "Hmm, I should have sent that somewhere."
They say he enjoys the pleasure of making things more than the pleasure of eating them, so sometimes I think I understand his mind, and sometimes I don't.
In the evening, I finished sewing the pajamas and put out my fine silk gauze *chŏgori* fabric to dry.
Just because I acquired a treadle sewing machine this summer, my sewing tasks only increase.

Month 0, Day 0
Today is the ninth day of the ninth lunar month.<77>
The chrysanthemums he grew himself are in full bloom. Though he didn't arrange them particularly beautifully, he cherishes chrysanthemums dearly. And whenever he grabs anyone, he boasts, "Look at this flower," "I grew this myself," "Shall I present you with one?"
But today is the day he said he would truly show off the chrysanthemums.
It's the day Father invited his literary friends to gather, compose, and recite poems about the yellow chrysanthemums. Six people including Mr. O Se-ch'ang (吳世昌).
He came home early from the company and was a great help in receiving the guests.
The menu served today was as follows:
In the reception room
(1) Black Tea
(2) Fruits Punch (푸릇스 폰지)
In the main room (*k'ŭnbang*)
(3) Western Liquor Table
Manhattan Cocktail (만한탄 칵텔) with two plates of dry snacks (each different), one plate of *kujŏlp'an* (nine-sectioned platter), one plate of peeled fruit, pickles (*changajji*: garlic (마눌), green peppers, red pepper paste (고초장), radish, bellflower root), raw pear juice, mustard, vinegar-pepper paste sauce (*ch'ogoch'ujang*)
In the inner room (*anpang*)
(4) Dinner Table (*chinjisang*)
Rice wine (*yakchu*) as an aperitif with a couple of dumplings in chicken broth; besides that, *sinsŏllo* (royal hot pot), watery kimchi (*changk'imch'i*), diced radish kimchi (*kkaktugi*), fermented crab (*kejang*), fermented oysters (*ŏriguljŏt*), seasoned raw small octopus (*ch'omuch'im*), assorted seasoned vegetables (*kaksaek much'im*) platter, raw sliced octopus (*nakchihoe*), raw beef (*yukhoe*), kidney and liver sashimi (*k'ongp'at kwa kan hoe*), assorted pan-fried delicacies (*kajin puch'im*) platter, one plate each of cooked greens (*sukch'ae*) and raw greens (*saengch'ae*), sweet rice dumplings (*kyŏngdan*), sweet rice cake (*yaksik*); besides these, brought in sequence later were pollack and red pepper paste stew (*sungŏ koch'ujang tchigae*), stir-fried chicken (*talk pokki*), steamed sea bream (*tomi tchim*), shrimp and oyster tempura (덴뿌라), baked eggs (게란 구은 것), grilled ribs (*kalbi kun gŏt*), and finally, rice served with beef bone soup (*komkuk*).
(4) Fruit
(5) Cocoa Tea
Until their departure
(6) Apple Juice
(7) Coffee Tea
Placing glass cups (유리곱뿌) before everyone, failing to refill them with cold water until the end<78> was today's masterpiece blunder. No, it was the biggest failure of the day. Just thinking about it makes my face flush (훗꾼하다).
The sight of the elderly gentlemen gathered around a piece of paper, heads together, reciting poetry, glimpsed through the glass window, remains deeply impressed in my memory. In the quiet night hours, that classical scene floats before my eyes once again.

Month 0, Day 0
Today, for the first time, we took out and ate the fermented crab (*kejang*) preserved in soy sauce in a new pot.
After the autumn re-papering of the house and the memorial rites, and after Mother left for Tanch'ŏn, I was continuously busy with kimchi-making for this house and that. Today, feeling a bit relaxed, I wanted to prepare the first-birthday clothes for my younger sister-in-law's (자근동세) son. I immediately went to the fabric store and bought fabric for a set of clothes.
Gold-patterned five-colored silk gauze trousers (*chami sa paji*), thick artificial silk deep pink *chŏgori* fabric, dark indigo silk vest (*pŏptan nam chokki*) fabric, light pink silk outer coat (*pŏptan turumagi*) fabric, and for the *magoja* (short jacket), silk grass-green body with rainbow-striped sleeves (*saekdong*), along with lining fabrics chosen to match the colors.
To start working on them quickly, I put them out to dry, making the night longer (밤을 도두아).
Though tired and sleepy,
"What about your studies?" he calls from the main room. I've been pestering him to teach me classical Chinese (pure Hanmun - specifically *Okrumong* 玉樓夢, The Jade Pavilion Dream) to gain some understanding (物理), but I keep procrastinating one way or another and get soundly scolded by the teacher (?). In truth, he also struggles when difficult characters appear, but he's always quick to reprimand.

Month 0, Day 0
I swept the first snow of the year.
I went to Hwasin department store and bought a complete set: skein-patterned padded socks (*t'arae pŏsŏn*), a dark blue sleeveless jacket (*chŏnbok*), a dark ceremonial hat (*pokkŏn*), and embroidered silk shoes (*t'aesasin*).
For the first time in my life, I prepared clothes for a young child with my own skill and effort. Roughly estimated, it all cost twenty won and sixty chŏn.
This is perhaps too extravagant for a child, but because the child's father<79> is far away in America, I put more heart into it.
Needless to say, it was to console the child's mother's heart.
Placing the child's colorful clothes in a box, along with the ramie silk *turumagi* I had made for Father during that time, my heart feels light and joyful.
Thinking of showing them to Mother, who should be returning today or tomorrow, makes me feel as excited as a child.
Picking up the newspaper that arrived tonight, advertisements for year-end sales poured out (쏘다젔다). (The author is a former Dong-a Ilbo reporter)

"Piano" and "Yut Nori"

黃信德 (Hwang Sin-dŏk)
December 9th (Friday) Cloudy
This morning, a light sleet fell, making it quite chilly.
I sent Hyŏngbin (亨彬) off to school first, then walked Chŏngbin-a (貞彬兒) to kindergarten, and went to work at the newspaper office as usual.

My life is typically monotonous, but today something unusual (?) happened. It's nothing other than the fact that I am writing this diary after 20 years, truly 20 years. Samch'ŏlli-sa really does eccentric things. Once, they asked me to write something like "Thoughts on that person far away" about a certain Mr. Someone (某氏) who was living right here in the heart of Seoul with his eyes wide open like saucers. Annoyed at having to write a manuscript anyway, I nicely refused, saying, "That person is in Seoul right now." This time, they've picked someone like me who doesn't keep a diary and asked me to send a diary, of all things. What can I do but write a diary for a few days starting today?

Why do people even keep diaries? Having heard rumors that many people got into trouble because of that document called a diary, I, as if guarding some great secret document, hastily burned the diary I had kept without missing a single day for 5 or 6 years, and resolved not to<80> write one again. It seems I'm quite the coward, even after 20 years. To be fair, the regretful thought of wishing I could at least jot things down in a diary sometimes hasn't entirely disappeared. But in any case, it turned out for the best; could I even publish the words I wanted to write? Ha! As if I have the talent.

December 10th (Saturday) Clear
This morning, under my name on the newspaper office attendance sheet, a blue stamp reading "Late" was marked. In the morning, I was fussing with the maid about dumpling soup, got startled, and made a dash for it, but even if I had blinked just once more, this would have happened. A pitiful situation.

When I came home in the evening, a blind piano had somehow entered our house and taken up residence in the spare room.
My goodness, having nowhere else to go, did it seek out this tin-roofed shack (빠락크)? Perhaps angry at having rolled in by itself, no matter how many times each family member pressed the keys, it stubbornly (무가내로) refused to make a sound. Maybe a canary that forgot its song has arrived. Still, the children rejoiced and danced, but then fell asleep sometime in between. Ah, my lovely angels, sleep well. Your sleeping faces are truly innocent.

I think I've had this cold for about twenty days. Thinking maybe I should warm myself (지저나 볼까) properly on the warm *ondol* floor early tonight, I lay down at the warmest spot (*araetmok*) from eight o'clock.

December 11th (Sunday) Clear
As soon as I opened my eyes at dawn, Chŏngbin-a asked, "Mom, you're not going to the newspaper office today, right?" "No." "It's a holiday— Then is Dad off (놀우)?" "No." "Then just because I'm on holiday? I don't like it, both of you going—" Her voice was already choked with tears. It was quite heartbreaking.

Around five in the afternoon, I was invaded by an assault troop (돌격대). Several young colleagues (족하님네) from the newspaper office, perhaps intending to tease this auntie,<81> stormed in without warning and demanded rice wine (*t'akchu*). What could I do? Guilty of living near the famous Hyŏngje ("Brother") tavern, I was completely cornered and subjected to their campaign. It had been a long time, but the *sijo* chanting and *yut nori* (윳노리) were fun. Mr. Yi Chae-nam (李載南), with his clumsy *yut* skills, happened to throw a "Mo" (the best throw) once and shouted at the top of his lungs, "People of Sinsŏl-jŏng! Everyone come! Yi Chae-nam threw a Mo!" making everyone hold their sides laughing. The neighbors probably jumped in their sleep. It reminded me of my old Tokyo days, playing the tomboy (오템바상), making enough noise to shake the second floor of that matchbox-like boarding house.
I wonder if they managed to catch the streetcar home.
I didn't lie down until after one, but sleep wouldn't come easily. We talked about various reminiscences until my husband (任) started snoring first. (The author is a Dong-a Ilbo reporter)(End)

樑上君子記 (Record of the Gentleman on the Beam)

尹聖相 (Yun Sŏng-sang)
Day X
My temperament, unable to tolerate things that are unpleasant or messy, today again sought something to rummage through and discard in closets and drawers. There wasn't particularly much to throw away, nor would discarding one or two old scraps of paper reduce our possessions.
The household of two people, a household without any unnecessary luxury or complexity, yet how can this extremely simple household not restrain and bind me? My hands and thoughts cannot remain indifferent to the dust settled on the wardrobe or the black ashtray (재터리), and doesn't this empty house hold me back when I wish to freely go somewhere? It's not just people who restrain people. While cleaning the room, wiping the wooden floor (*maru*), handling the furniture,<82> all these familiar, time-worn, insignificant things, instead, bind me. Two sets of clothes demand twice the time of one set, two rooms require double the upkeep of one, and two pieces of furniture steal more than one's share of attention and trouble.
A household where the body can freely go wherever the heart desires, a household that can be abandoned like straw for the sake of one task, a household that leaves no lingering attachment even if left empty for several days— though the thought itself is just a thought, that would be the most liberated way of life.

Day X
The room, having lost the piercing sunlight, feels gloomy. While eating breakfast, I suddenly thought of L. Perhaps because the day he died was a snowy day, he always comes to mind on cloudy, dreary winter days. The work and duties he cherished so much in life, his wife and son—are they not already walking their separate paths? An uncontrollable sentimentality brings forth, one by one throughout the day, the phantoms of many people who followed him and have already departed from me. With my sewing basket and books placed beside me, I buried my body in the warm spot (*araetmok*) and lost the day.

Day X
I placed a heater on the *maru*. Like last year's charcoal heater or the gasoline (까소령) heater, it's unsatisfactory and inadequate. I regret not having used that money instead to buy coal and stuff it into the furnace all day long.
Housekeeping (살님) is a petty affair. Among them, there's probably nothing as shabby as that of a meager (빤한), cheap salaryman. Pretending to be sophisticated (히떠운체), appreciating movies, tilting tea cups—all the while, the salary envelope in the pocket is already being calculated, and one remembers all too well that sometimes, the price for this outrageous luxury is having to contentedly eat meals lacking side dishes for several days. That's why haggling with merchants over even a penny often breaks down. It's a dirty business. I don't desire a decadent (데카단) life. I fear the pettiness that refines even the heart and thoughts.

Day X
I left the house empty for a moment, and perhaps someone jumped the wall; both back doors were wide open, and the sack and the glass bottle for gasoline (까소링) that were in the storage shed were gone. Thinking it must be the work of some young beggar coveting the bottle,<83> I went out to the trash bin, and sure enough, as if just poured out, ashes were heavily smeared (*진채), and the smell of gasoline stung my nose. More precious to us than the worn-out sack or the 5-chŏn bottle was the gasoline, which is hard to obtain even with money, but to that beggar, the bottle and sack must have been more valuable. How anxious must he have been while pouring out about a *doe* (approx. 1.8 liters) of gasoline?

Day X
As the weather turns cold, the peddlers' cries sound harsher too. Among them, the salt seller who wakes you from a sound sleep at dawn, the vegetable seller passing by at breakfast time, the fermented shrimp paste (*saeujŏt*) seller who comes occasionally—though they are faces without much fault, where do such mournful and miserable sounds come from? The more I hear them, the angrier I get, yet they are sounds I wait for when I need something. The mournfulness and misery are likely not just from the cold. (The author is a former Chosŏn Ilbo reporter)

A Life for "Husband and Child" (「夫와 子」를 爲한 살님)

許英肅 (Hŏ Yŏng-suk)
December X
Since the day Ch'unwŏn (春園) returned from his official trip, my work has significantly increased, and my mind is busy too, but the children are happy that their father has come, and I also feel the joy of the household seeming complete. The sorrow from having struggled entirely on my own—designing, building, and opening the Maternity Clinic (産院) until today—seems to surge up all at once, but realizing anew that Ch'unwŏn is still recovering from illness, I feel my shoulders grow heavy.
I immediately vacated my room for him and moved down with the young children to the room originally built as Ch'unwŏn's room. This room seems less warm (덜 더움즉하고) than my room, and also because the Maternity Clinic is nearby,<84> I was concerned the children's frequent crying would be noisy.

December X
I woke up at seven in the morning. Ch'unwŏn was already up sometime before, having swept the outer yard and the main street, and finished cleaning the room and the *maru*. As always, I washed the children's faces, fed Yŏnggŭn (榮根) breakfast and sent him to school, washed my face, ate breakfast with the children, and then began my rounds at the Maternity Clinic. Ch'unwŏn had eaten breakfast early with Yŏnggŭn, so he came down to our room, watched me and the children eat, played with the children for a while, and then began writing at nine o'clock.
Even while I am out at the clinic, my mind is not at ease because of Ch'unwŏn. I worry the children might tire him by staying too close to him, I worry if he entertains guests for too long, and I can't help but worry if he writes too much. It feels exactly like being cautious about a sore on my own body.
So today, I drew up a timetable.
From nine AM to twelve PM, writing manuscript— (During this time, even if the children say they miss their father, the door will be kept closed.)
From twelve PM to one PM is designated as lunchtime. (Lunch must be eaten outside, freely as he wishes. Not only can I not prepare it myself at home, but I also worry the side dishes might not be satisfactory.)
When he returns at one PM, visiting hours (面會時間) are until three PM—(No matter who comes, unless it's between one and three, we have no choice but to say he's not in. Otherwise, from morning till evening (*녁까지), the whole house is like one undergoing some major event due to the constant stream of guests. If this continues, it will certainly harm Ch'unwŏn's health, and he won't be able to write. It's also a significant disturbance for me.)

December X
Chŏngnan (貞蘭) suddenly developed a fever close to 40 degrees Celsius and started being ill (앓기). Examination reveals pneumonia (肺炎). My heart sinks (선듯한다). And then Ch'unwŏn caught a cold. Back and forth (들낙날낙) between the clinic and the house dozens of times all day long,<85> I was frantic attending to Ch'unwŏn and the child. Completely forgetting my own fatigue until late at night (밤느께까지), sitting there worrying about Ch'unwŏn and Chŏngnan, I thought, "Are wives (여편네) creatures born into this world for the sake of their husbands and children?"

December X
Now that Chŏngnan's fever has gone down and Ch'unwŏn's cold has recovered (낳고), my body feels light enough to ascend to the heavens, and everything seems peaceful. As soon as his cold got better (낳자), Ch'unwŏn immediately started sweeping the outer yard, the main street, cleaning the room, and the *maru*. Even when I tell him he might get sick again, he says he gets sick if he doesn't work. Anyway, during the several years (멫해) we were apart, Ch'unwŏn has changed in many ways. He constantly strives to be closer to his father's (부친님) intentions. He shows a polite attitude and prudence even in a single word towards everyone in the house, from the children down to the cook (食母) and the errand runner. While I respect him for having such a mindset, I cannot help but feel that he has somehow grown distant from me. Though he is more affectionate, kinder, more considerate, and more attentive than before, I sometimes feel empty (허젔하고) and desolate. To put it plainly, Ch'unwŏn seems to me like only a father from my natal home or perhaps a retired old gentleman (御穩居樣, O-inkyosama).
When asked, "Who is that sweeping the main street wearing a silk *magoja*?"
My voice somehow feels desolate as I reply, "That is my husband (영감)."

December X
Perhaps because the tension from nursing the sick suddenly eased, my body feels ill (앞은) again. I lay down after dinner, and when Yŏnggŭn pestered me for money, I snapped at him out of annoyance (귀찬타고). He got angry and went into his father's room, saying he would sleep there. Ch'unwŏn, after calming Yŏnggŭn down, came down to our room as he does every day and plays with the children. He sings songs with them, tells them stories, and listens to their stories. He plays with the children like this until their bedtime at eight o'clock (여듧시), then returns to his room and writes for an hour. What he writes, I don't know that either.<86>

**Summary**

These diary entries from four Korean women in 1939 offer glimpses into their varied daily lives and inner thoughts during the colonial period. Ch'oe Ŭi-sun details domestic responsibilities, gardening, social obligations like hosting her father's poetry gathering with an elaborate menu, and her efforts to learn classical Chinese from her husband. Hwang Sin-dŏk, a journalist, contrasts her monotonous routine with moments like dealing with a broken piano and hosting impromptu gatherings with colleagues. Yun Sŏng-sang reflects on the burdensome nature of possessions, the melancholy of winter reminding her of loss, the frustrations of meager finances clashing with desires for simple comforts, and a minor theft. Hŏ Yŏng-suk, a clinic director married to the famous writer Yi Kwang-su (Ch'unwŏn), chronicles the added burden and worry of his return home in poor health, her attempts to manage his schedule and protect his well-being, dealing with childhood illness, and her complex feelings about his changed, more reserved demeanor which makes him seem distant despite his kindness.

From a historical perspective, the diaries reveal the domestic sphere, social customs (gift-giving, hosting, entertainment like movies and *yut nori*), and economic realities (managing budgets, dealing with peddlers, affording items like sewing machines or children's clothes) of middle-to-upper class urban Korean women. Mentions of specific items like Japanese brand names (likely Hwasin Dept Store), Western imports (piano, cocoa, coffee, cocktails), Japanese cultural elements (*otenba-san*, *o-inkyosama*), and the constraints felt even within relatively privileged lives provide texture to the colonial experience. Hŏ Yŏng-suk's entries are particularly notable for the insight into Yi Kwang-su's life and personality during this period, reflecting his health issues and evolving mindset, which historians studying prominent figures of the era would find valuable. The blend of traditional Korean practices with modern and foreign influences is also evident.

崔義順
일기를 발표한다는 일 알듯이 모를 일입니다.
누구나 사실 그대로 발표하지도 안으려니와 발표할 수도 없을 것입니다. 억지로 유명하신 三千里主幹先生의 기발*신 풀랜을 물리치기가 미안해서 주섬주섬 모아 보내기로 합니다.

0월 0일
눈을 뜨자 마당으로 나려섰다.
앞뜰 뒷뜰 옆뜰 여러고랑 채전을 잿빠르게 솟과주었으나 조반준비는 늦었다.
몇 일채 벼르던 일을 치르고 나니 밥맛이 더욱 달었다.
식후에는 체 못 심은 화초씨를 뿌렸다.
먼 땅에서 늦둥이로 구해진 씨았인데 공교히 섰투른 내 손에 심어젔으니 싹날 때가 조심스럽게 기달려진다. 더구나 「꼭 오늘 안으로 뿌리시오.」하고 시굴로 가섰는데 어서 빨리 은행색 떡잎이나 고소란히 고개를 들어 주어야 내 솜씨 자랑도 하게 될 것이다.

0월 0일
날마다 한결같은 식사준비 집안 어른 아이들의 시중 대문과 전화시중 그러고 집안 치우는 것외에 빨래와 바누질들로 하로 행사는 가득 놓여 있다. 참으로 주부의 한가한 시간이란 귀하기 짝이 없다.
「오로」는 충실한 놈이다. 늦도록 앉은 나를 지껴나주는 드시 틈틈이 제 집에서 튀여나와서는 컹컹 짖고 있다.
낮에 도련님 앞에서 재주 피우던 팔이 생각이 나서 혼자 웃었다.<76>

0월 0일
아버지* 모시겹바지를 *으랴고 펴 놓자 어머니께 명치좌구경을 시켜드리라는 명령이 나려서 오래간만에 뫼시고 나섰다.
사진이 나오기전에 스토리를 말슴해 드리고 간간히 귀에 대고 화면설명을 해드리니까 매우 재미있게 보셨다.
「좋은 것 할 때는 젊은 애들에게 보여줄만 하고나 그 전쟁사진이 더 볼만하드라.」
이러한 말슴을 들을 수 있었다.
돌아 오는 길에 아이양복과 양말을 삿다.
그러고 저녁때 아버지 피서나가게신 성북동 청롱사에 갔다왔다. 준비해 놓았던 붕어조림 새우뭇침 복근고초장 보탕들의 항아리를 쌓들고
컴컴한데 인사차로 뒤따라 나오는 여승들의 모양이 몹시도 고적해 보였다.

0월 0일
아침 반찬감 준비를 못해놓아 생각하던 끝에 뒷마당 호박덩굴 밑을 뒤젔다. 이슬에 젖은 애호박 두 개에 갓맺은 풋고초 대여섰개하고 가지 서넜을 따드니 든든했었다.
일년감을 따서 웃집 앞집 또 이층집으로 선물을 하고나니 집에서 쪼개먹은 것 멫곱절 유쾌했다.
지난 봄에 그 분이 좋아하시는 풋마눌로도 선물들을 꽤 했지만 오늘 것은 더 번치가 났다.
잘 생긴 도마도를 점여서 하얀 설탕을 끼언저 들고가서 「시원한 것 한점 잡수시요.」하면 번번이 「그 어딜 좀 보낼 걸 그랬군.」하실 뿐-
그 분은 만드시는 재미가 잡수시는 재미보다 더 하시다니까 그 속을 알상싶으기도 하고 모를상 싶으기도 하다.
저녁 때는 파자마 짖던 것을 마치고 내 깩기저고리감을 말러 놓았다.
이 여름에 새로 작만한 발재봉틀이라고 해서 내 바누질감은 한 가지라도 많아질 뿐이다.

오늘은 음력 구월 구일이다.<77>
손수 만드신 국화는 한창이다. 그다지 이쁘게 만들어 놓지도 못하시고는 국화라면 끔직이 얼으만지신다. 그러고는 누구나 붓드시면 「이 꽃 좀 보시오.」 「이것 내가 만든거요.」 「이것 한 분 선사하릿가.」하시며 웃쭐대신다.

아츰 일곱시에 이러났다. 春園은 벌서 어느새 이러나서 밖았뜰 큰길소제를 다하고 방 소제 마루소제까지 다했다. 나는 언제나 하듯이 아이들 세수를 식히고 榮根이 밥을 먹여 학교에 보내고 세수하고 아이들과 밥을 먹은 다음 産院回診을 시작한다. 春園은 榮根이와 함께 아츰진지를 일직 지냈음으로 우리 방에 내려오서서 나와 아이들 밥 먹는 것을 보시고 한참 아이들을 대리고 노시다가 아홉시부터 글쓰기 시작하신다.
나는 산원에 나가 있으면서도 도모지 春園 까닭에 마음이 놓이지 안는다. 아이들이 아버지 곁에 너무 가서 몸살을 낼까 싶어 걱정되고 손님을 오래 대해도 걱정되고 글을 많이 써도 걱정되지 않을 수 없다. 꼭 내 몸이 어디 부스럼 난 것을 조심하는 마음이다.
그래서 오늘은 시간표를 작성해 놓았다.
아츰 아홉시부터 열두시까지 原稿쓰고- (이 시간은 아이들이 아버지가 보고싶다고 해도 문을 닫아 걸게한다.)
열두시에서부터 한시까지는 점심시간으로 정했다. (점심은 꼭 밖에 나가서 마음대로 잡수시게한다. 집에서 내가 손수해 디릴 수 없을 뿐 아니라 반찬이 맛당하지 않을까 해서 이렇게 한 것이다.)
한시에 도라오시면 세시까지 面會時間이다-(어떤 분이 오시든지 한시에서부터 세시 사이가 아니면 안게시다고 거절하는 수 밖에 없다. 그렇지 않으면 아츰부터 *녁까지 줄창 손님때문에 온 집안이 무슨 大事치르는 집 같다. 이대로 그냥 게속한다면 물론 春園의 건강에도 害되고 글도 못 쓴다. 또 내게도 적지 않게 妨害가 된다.)

12월 X일
貞蘭이가 갑작이 열이 40도 갓가히 오르며 알키 시작한다. 診察하니 肺炎이다. 가슴이 선듯한다. 그런데 또 春園이 감기가 드시였다. 産院에서 안으로 하로 종일 수십번을 <85> 들낙날낙 달녀 다니며 春園과 아이시중에 정신이 없었다. 밤느께까지 내 피곤한 것은 전연 잊고 春園과 貞蘭이 때문애 앉었으면서 여편네란 남편과 자식을 위해서 세상에 탄생한 동물인가 부다고 생각했다. (筆者는 産院院長)

12월 X일
貞蘭이도 열이 내리고 춘원의 감기가 낳고 보니 몸이 하늘에 오를 듯 가벼웁고 만사가 태평한 듯하다. 춘원은 감기가 낳자 곳 벌서 밖았 뜰과 큰 길거리 소제와 방소제 마루 소제를 하는 것이다. 또 병이 나면 어찌느냐고 일너도 일을 하지 않으면 병이 난다고 한다. 어쨋든 멫해 떠러저 있는 사이에 춘원은 전보다 달너진 데가 많다. 늘 부치님의 뜻에 갓가웁자는 마음이다. 집안에 아이로 심지어 밥짓는 食母 심부럼하는 사람에게도 공손한 태도와 말 한마디까지라도 삼가는 것이다. 이런 마음을 가진 그가 尊敬되긴 하면서도 어쩐지 내게서 멀어진 것같은 느낌을 가지지 않을 수 없다. 전보다 더 정다웁고 더 친절하고 더 생각해주고 더 살들하것만 나는 때때로 허젔하고 쓸쓸한 마음이 다 말하자면 春園은 내게 친정아버지나 또는 「御穩居樣」와 같이 밖에 생각되지 안는다.
「宅에 모번단 마고자 입고 큰 길 쓰는 이가 누구예요.」하는 무름에
「우리 영감입니다.」하고 대답하는 내 소리가 어쩐지 쓸쓸해지는 것을 느낀다.

12월 X일
병간호에 긴장이 탁 풀닌 까닭인지 내 몸이 또 앞은 것 같다. 저녁을 먹고 누었는데 榮根이가 돈을 달나고 졸느는 것을 귀찬타고 쏘아줫드니 골을 내고 아버지한태 가 잔다고 들어갔다. 春園은 榮根이를 달내여놓고 어느날이나 하듯이 우리 방에 내려와서 아이들과 논다. 아이들과 같이 노래도 부르고 아이들에게 이얘기도 들녀주고 또 아이들한대 이얘길 듯기도 한다. 아이들이 자는 시간 여듧시까지 이렇게 아이들과 놀다가 자기방에 도라가서 한 시간을 글을 쓴다는 것이다. 무슨 글을 쓰는지 나는 그것도 모른다.<86>
