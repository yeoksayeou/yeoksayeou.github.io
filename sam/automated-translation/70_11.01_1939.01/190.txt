Publication Date: 1939년 01월 01일
Article Title: 『女子의 一生』을 말하는 佳人會議 (A Meeting of Beauties Discussing "A Woman's Life")
Author: 毛允淑, 李善熙, 崔貞熙 (Mo Yun-suk, Yi Sŏn-hŭi, Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Discussion/Roundtable Talk)

**Body Text Translation:**

『女子의 一生』을 말하는 佳人會議
毛允淑, 李善熙, 崔貞熙

"Man does not make a woman's body, a hundred years of joy and sorrow depend on others," it was lamented. Was the suffering of being a woman truly so profound? Now, we intend to hear about "A Woman's Life" here from the young women of this land.
1. Impressions lingered from the "Bride's Morning"
2. Impressions from the day of becoming a "Mother"
3. Impressions of facing one's sixtieth birthday with a mother-in-law
4. Generally, what is the fate of a woman?

① Impressions of the First Morning as a Bride
Reporter - The three of you are all like flowers before the age of 30, so surely life is enjoyable even now<138>, but please tell us about the joyful words from that morning after getting married, wearing the veil, the first morning you greeted as a bride. The times you smiled to yourself thinking, "My youth has now arrived," the various stories of being intoxicated in dreams, thinking, "My life is beginning to bloom from now on..."

Mo Yun-suk - Is the day after getting married really that good? Hahaha. In what sense are you asking?

Reporter - Why wouldn't it be good? Didn't the Greek poetess Sappho dance for joy, proclaiming, "Now I have obtained the world"? Because she was a woman, the contempt she received, because she was a woman, the sorrowful grief that never left her—didn't all of that completely disappear? Like the full moon rising through the clouds, entering the groom's room filled with light, embracing many dreams and joys, wouldn't she have been immersed in a profound happiness that only a woman could possess? Young ladies like you three, fresh as blue flowers.

Mo Yun-suk - How much joy could women here possibly have had? If it were a society like Greece where culture flourished, and men displayed their respective vitality, managing the affairs of the world, such fine husbands abounding, how joyful would it have been for us women to greet the first day of marriage? A mature body would begin to blossom from this time, and a spirit and soul laden with dreams would endlessly sprout wings, flying to good worlds. But what joy could there be in the heart of a bride here who generally welcomes a poor, uneducated, and hopeless groom? These words are not about me personally, but I believe the psychology of all women here would be like that.

Reporter - Oh dear. I should have mentioned this beforehand. Regarding tonight's topic, like "A Woman's Life," I know there might be various uncomfortable points to discuss, as you all have husbands, children, and good families. Therefore, tonight's discussion doesn't have to be about yourselves. Just speak as one woman of our land, telling the psychology of others, the state of mind of other women. I'll listen to it that way, so please don't misunderstand at all and speak frankly without concealment.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - That would be better. Please don't listen as if it's our own confession, but rather as if some woman spoke thus and passed by. Speaking of the "bride's joy," the joy of a woman on the day after marriage must surely be immeasurably great for anyone. Someone was said to have obtained the world, but even if not the whole world, having completely obtained the strong man she intends to rely on for her whole life—how great must that joy be? Perhaps since being born from her parents' womb, such a great, deep, splendid, and wonderful joy will never come again.
However, for the women of this era, wasn't <139> "the morning of each and every day during the dating period" happier? Not just 'wasn't it,' but it certainly seems it would be happier. The time when one first became intoxicated by the fragrance of the opposite sex seems likely to be happier. Because the young women of today certainly try to enjoy the dating period more and embellish it more valuably.

Reporter - Then does that mean women during the dating period have already gone through all the processes of becoming a wife from being a young girl?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Even if it doesn't reach the realm of the physical, the deep emotions of being a woman are generally experienced during that time. Therefore, for a woman in the dating period, "I never realized before that the stars shone so brightly," "I never realized before that the scent of the passing spring breeze was so warm"—it seems all things in heaven and earth come to be felt anew and realized anew through the existence of that one man. Isn't that so?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - It is. Once married, won't marriage become nothing more than a procedural matter? Men and women who already know each other hold a ceremony, make it a mark of union, register as husband and wife in the family register (戶籍), and from then on, they leave behind the romanticism of fantasy and come face-to-face with realistic, all too realistic, problems.
First, they must establish a household.
Second, they must manage the household.
Third, they must bear and raise children.
Since all these solemn and unavoidable fates of a woman converge here, the bride's morning is not a morning of dreams, but a morning when all dreams are shattered. Isn't the bride's morning not a morning to enjoy youth, but a morning to bid farewell to youth? At least, the psychology of many modern women who have experienced a dating period seems to be like this.

Mo Yun-suk - Right, right. So, the "Bride's Morning," as Ms. Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi said earlier, is not "How bright the stars in the sky are!" but rather, "How dim and dark they are!" And not "How fragrant the passing spring breeze is!" but rather, "How bland it is!" they say. Hahaha.

Reporter - But the three of you have all received high-level education in this society and all had opportunities for free love, so your maidenhood steps were luxurious and free. Perhaps that's why. But isn't it still the case that the vast majority of unmarried women go to the wedding hall without having experienced dating? For them, wouldn't "that first morning of the bride" truly be mysterious and happy?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - What we are saying is not that the morning after getting married isn't happy and mysterious. However, <140> when dating precedes it, the wedding ceremony becomes merely a procedural formality—that's the point we're making.

Reporter - So, the "newlywed bride"—in other words, the woman awakened to sexuality—abandons all thoughts of being unhappy because she is a woman and truly feels happiness, right?

Mo Yun-suk - Of course, she feels it, absolutely! 100 percent, no, 120 percent she feels it. Hahaha. (All laugh)

Reporter - Yes, I understand. This makes one instance of women's "Happiness!"

② The Happiness of Becoming a Mother

Reporter - Then, next, please tell us about the happiness of becoming a mother.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - We three are still young ladies under 30, so calling us mother, mother—oh dear, how embarrassing. Please don't evaluate us as too old. Hoho.

Mo Yun-suk - I still really don't feel like a mother of a little baby, just like when I was still a girl attending girls' school (女學校).

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Even if I did, I'd want to deliberately avoid it.

Reporter - Does one's view of life change on the day one becomes a mother?

Mo Yun-suk - I keenly felt the mystery of life anew. The mood became solemn.

Reporter - Please don't just say that; describe the psychological and physiological changes, like Maupassant (모팟상).

Mo Yun-suk - Alright. When I was 5 or 6 months pregnant (懷姙), I could feel the baby's movements (胎動) in my belly. At that time, the thought occurred, "This is life playing." It felt both scary and joyful. Thinking that within my insignificant body, there was a new, single life awaiting the world, from then on, ridiculously enough, I felt a sense of responsibility as a mother. The term 'prenatal education' (胎敎), which I used to hear casually, came to mind. So, I tried my best to have joyful thoughts, sought out and looked at good pictures—like sacred paintings (聖畵)—read good texts, sang good songs, and listened to good music. Thus, I was filled with the sole wish (祈願) and hope that a bright and high-minded child (子女) would be born. My daily life...

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - It's really true. The strange thought naturally arises that a woman's calling (天職) lies in becoming a good mother. And then, one's ears perk up at the disciplinary tales (修養談) of various mothers from biographies of great people during their pregnancies (姙娠). One makes it a daily routine (日課) to try to do the same.

Reporter - And then?

Mo Yun-suk - And then, when it was time to give birth (解産), I was admitted to the maternity clinic (産院).

Reporter - During pregnancy, isn't there a hope that the baby will be a boy? Since you women have suffered much grief <141> just for being women, don't you wish that your child would not be a girl but a precious son (玉童子)?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Perhaps only women who think being born female is a sad thing feel that way—

Mo Yun-suk - I did have the thought, "Wouldn't it be wonderful if it were a boy?" It was partly my own wish, but more so, I thought my husband would be happier if it were a boy, so I prayed for it to be a boy. Moreover, since my husband is an only son, the whole family was waiting for a son to be born; what would happen if I suddenly gave birth to a girl? So, I inwardly wished* for a boy. (*Original text error: 바랬요 likely means 바랐어요)

Reporter - We men don't really know, but is the labor pain (陣痛) during childbirth extremely agonizing?

Mo Yun-suk - Some people say they had easy births, but from my experience, it was the hardest thing in the world. Lying on the operating table (手術臺), it was so painful and agonizing that everything in the room spun like a top. The electric lights, the nurse's (看護婦) face, the doctor, the midwife (産婆)—everything spun round and round, and eventually, I couldn't see anything. Truly, the culmination of pain seems to be childbirth. When the labor pains start, it feels like you're definitely going to die. It doesn't feel like you can possibly survive through that agony.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - The agony of childbirth cannot be known unless one experiences it oneself, no matter how many thousands of words are spoken. Since it's the greatest part of a woman's physiological destiny, men have no way of knowing the suffering of labor pains, no matter what.

Mo Yun-suk - Just then, I heard the sound of a baby crying, "Waaah." After a while, I came to my senses and heard that the baby was a boy, and my heart* settled completely. (*Original text: 듣* 마음이 확- 까란자요 likely means 듣고 마음이 확 가라앉아요 - "heard and my heart completely settled"). If you want to imagine the face of peaceful womanhood, picture a young woman lying down and sleeping with a gentle smile after giving birth following long hardship (辛苦), having heard the fortunate news that the child born is a boy—that would be the image of a peaceful woman.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Truly, for women like us, becoming a mother is like accomplishing a sacred task. On the day we become mothers, we attain a position worthy of reverence (敬拜) from all people. Perhaps "all people" is an exaggeration (語幣). If so, then it's the day we receive reverence from our husband, his parents and relatives, acquaintances, and all the village people. In the ancient matriarchal societies (母權中心社會), how proud and happy could we have been? But now, who considers it such a big deal? We women perform this sacred task of creating life. Men should perhaps hold the notion of "respect for women" (女人尊重), but alas... hahaha.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Even now, the South Sea Islanders (南洋土人) supposedly view childbirth with such high regard that the entire village, led by the chief (酋長), gathers to perform a religious ceremony (宗敎的 儀式) honoring the woman. They call an astronomer (天文博士), perform purification rituals (齋戎沐浴 - likely 齋戒沐浴), and make a big fuss. The young people <142> light a bonfire that night, drink, dance, and celebrate, holding a festival for the whole village or hamlet.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Just as ancient men considered passing the state examination (科擧) the joy of a lifetime, a woman's joy, or rather her achievement, could be said to lie in becoming a mother. Instead of boundless joy, on the other hand, one feels a sense of satisfaction, thinking, "I have fulfilled my duty as a woman." In this society where failure to produce an heir (無後) is considered one of the seven grounds for divorce (七去之惡), how much of a shadowed life, as if guilty of a crime, would a woman lead if she couldn't have children?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - That's true, too. And by having a child, one comes to realize a different, higher world of love that was previously unknown. It's not the love of a husband, nor is it the same as parental love; it's an unlimited, true love felt only for one's own flesh and blood (血肉). There's a saying, "Great Buddha's eye-opening" (大佛開眼); perhaps we could truly call it "Woman's eye-opening" (女人開眼)? Only upon encountering the love for one's child.

Reporter - Yes. I understand well. So, this makes the second instance of women's happiness!

③ The Joy of the Sixtieth Birthday Banquet (回甲宴) Day

Reporter - Pearl Buck's (팔, 팔크女史) O-lan (阿蘭) in "The Good Earth" (大地) depicted the fate of an Eastern (東洋的) woman relatively well. In it, after spending a miserable, obscure life, heaven grants her just one joy in the end. That is the day her precious son, whom she raised with care, finally takes a bride—that is, the day she herself, having grown old, receives the bow from her new daughter-in-law. That day is the greatest joy and happiness given to this woman worn out by a long and arduous journey (旅路). The age of becoming a mother-in-law (시어미), in other words, passing 20, 30, 40, 50, and reaching 60, becoming an old woman with white hair on the main floor (堂上 白髮老婆), commanding descendants (子孫), and receiving the sixtieth birthday banquet (回甲宴).
Like a ship that has finished a long voyage entering the harbor (港口) and finally finding peace, the image of an old figure (老姿) who has experienced all the waves of human life and finally arrived at the port of the sixtieth birthday celebration (回甲壽宴)? A state where her children take over all her worries, and she herself becomes a person with nothing to do (事無閑身), sitting and smiling peacefully. That morning of the 60th birthday, facing flower-like young ladies, the day of becoming a white-haired old woman—asking you to speak of this now must make your faces flush, but please imagine such feelings and speak, just as you skillfully handle any subject matter in your novels or poems.

Mo Yun-suk - I don't want to talk about it.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Me neither...

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Will we also age like that—hahaha (All laugh)

Reporter - Why would young ladies age? The three of you will never grow old in your lifetimes. I mean other women, like those in Egypt (에지뿌도), hahaha—

Mo Yun-suk - We'll age, of course. But will we age that much?<143> Past forty, past fifty, even sixty—oh dear, what to do.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Still 30 more years—

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - After 30 years, with a bent back and sunken cheeks—

Mo Yun-suk - Wrinkles on the forehead...

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Speech becoming rambling (橫說竪說).

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Hating to look in the mirror.

Reporter - Ah-ah-.

Mo Yun-suk - But what can we do, that's reality. It seems like only yesterday (어적겐데) we were attending girls' school, and already we're married and mothers of children.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Truly, bumping into this and that every day (날마다 이리 부닥기고 저리 부닥기고), in the end, we'll become old women (老婆).

Reporter - Losing one's youth by aging—how could that be only for women? For men too, there's nothing so regrettable and agonizing. That's why, as things are now, many young men probably think they'd rather commit suicide (自殺) before getting old. If committing suicide, then perhaps a love suicide (情死) with a loved one—this might be the thought of most young men.

Mo Yun-suk - That's right. How can one grow old? Thinking about getting old makes one want to die before waiting to become old. But perhaps for thousands of years, all women thought this way when they were young, but eventually, they aged without realizing it, grew old, and died like withered trees (枯木) falling.

Reporter - Yang Guifei (楊貴妃) and Xiang Yu's (項羽) Consort Yu (虞美人) were saddened by their aging, so they asked for mirrors, applied final makeup (丹粧), and killed themselves (自刎)—

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Most women, like rootless plants (草木), are swept away by the great waves of human life, drifting towards old age, towards death.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Yesterday's grass flows on today's waves, today's grass flows on tomorrow's waves. The water is not the same day by day, and the grass floating on the water is not the same wave by wave.

Reporter - Alright—now let's put aside all sentimentality (感傷) and become cheerful again. Please imagine and tell us about the joy of becoming a mother-in-law, the day of receiving the sixtieth birthday banquet table (回甲壽筵床).

Mo Yun-suk - Since there's no escaping white hair in human life, I suppose we must think about getting old too. Hahaha. Anyway, Ms. Buck saw it correctly. O-lan (阿爛) in "The Good Earth" depicted the life of an Eastern woman exactly. The beauty of her endurance (忍從), her effort to survive even while situated at the very bottom (맨 밋바닥), her attachment to life, wanting to live long despite a woman's life having no hope—this fate of hers, carrying a heavy load of suffering, from poverty in childhood to facing her father-in-law (시앗보기?) in adulthood, trudging wearily along a distant mountain path, is not just O-lan's. It's the fate experienced day in and day out by women in Korea (朝鮮), China (支那), or India (印度). Recently, while doing a radio broadcast (라지오 放送) about Pearl Buck's character and works, I felt an even deeper respect for the author.<144>

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - So, thinking it through thoroughly, since one will inevitably grow old (旣往), educating one's children well so they succeed (出世) and become a source of joy in old age (老後) is a woman's great instinct (본능) and also a great desire (욕심).

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - I haven't thought deeply about life after 30 yet, but anyway, it seems that once a woman passes middle age, the object of her desire (慾望) shifts. The kind of simple, single-minded desire (외골수) as in the dating period seems impossible in the future. It doesn't seem one can live solely on the affection received from the opposite sex. Eventually, a life plan (生活設計) centered around children seems likely to emerge. Thus, one sees daughters-in-law, grandchildren, and receives the sixtieth birthday banquet.

Reporter - So, that joy is?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Like the joy of being a bride or becoming a mother, it seems it would be a great joy for a woman. Isn't that right?

Mo Yun-suk - Seems like it.

Reporter - So this is the third joy in a woman's life. I understand well.

④ What is a Woman's Life?

Reporter - Do you think it's unfortunate to be born a woman?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - I've never thought it unfortunate. Rather, there are many times I feel pride (푸라우드) in being a woman.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Me too. I don't have the thought, "How much happier would I be if I were born a man?"

Reporter - Are women cherished more? In our society?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - There are numerous instances where women possess qualities that men cannot, precisely because they are women. If one has exceptional looks, some talent (才操), and corresponding refinement, women can easily gain fame. Because even women of that level are precious.

Mo Yun-suk - In other societies, I would want to lament (恨歎) keenly (痛切히) being born a woman. But here, men don't possess that many special privileges (特權) that women lack. Just because one has talent or administrative skill (經倫), does one become a great general? A great politician? In a society without politicians or generals, how great can a talented man be? These are all things to be said in societies elsewhere.

Reporter - That's one interpretation. Anyway, since the three of you haven't even felt curious about being born male, you must be content with your lot (安身立命之格).
Alright, let's move to the conclusion. In the end, a woman's life consists of becoming someone's wife, then a mother, then a mother-in-law, and eventually returning to the dust of the green mountains (靑山一杯土). As you mentioned earlier, I understand well that there are three peaks (고비) of joy in a woman's 60-year life. Then, what about sorrow <145>? What is the unavoidable sorrow of a woman?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - A woman's life is a tragedy. A woman's life is eventful and full of resentment (多端하고 多怨하지요). It's a chain (連鎖) of misfortune.

Reporter - More concretely?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - When young, love doesn't go as desired; after having children, sometimes one must send those children far away (外方); then there's poverty and illness; on top of that, the conventions (因襲) and traditions of Korean society senselessly confine and oppress women...

Mo Yun-suk - It's Bai Juyi's (白樂天) "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (長恨歌). It's "A Concubine's Unfortunate Fate" (妾薄命). It's exactly "In another life, do not be born a woman; a hundred years of joy and sorrow depend on others" (他生莫作女人身하라 百年苦樂이 依他人).

Reporter - But when Yang Guifei (楊貴妃) received Emperor Xuanzong's (唐明王) favor (寵愛), didn't all parents at that time wish to have a daughter like Yang Guifei? Didn't that lead to the famous song
"Do not value sons, value daughters" (不重生男, 重生女)
becoming popular? In Korea too, didn't mothers in P'yŏngyang (平壤), because the scenery was good and giving birth to a daughter inevitably meant giving birth to a beauty, rather wish to have daughters?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - But those are all exceptions. Just because there is one Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi (崔承喜), it doesn't mean all Korean women wish for daughters.

Mo Yun-suk - Indeed, if you look at the time of Herod (헤로데), there was an era where nations fought over a single woman, making her seem like an incredibly significant existence, but—

Reporter - Ch'unwŏn's (春園) "That Woman's Life"
Maupassant's (모-팟상) "A Woman's Life"
Yamamoto Yūzō's (山本有三) "A Woman's Life"
Please tell us your impressions after reading these three works.

Mo Yun-suk - All three attempted to depict the "Fate of Woman," but in my view, Maupassant's work lets the woman go her own way, while the author holds the pen and closely follows her heels, faithfully collecting the record. Ch'unwŏn's "That Woman's Life" seems to involve the author standing in front, guiding the woman's steps, saying "walk this way, walk that way," holding her hand (붓잡고 가며 - likely 붙잡고 가며), and ultimately leading her to a certain conclusion.

Reporter - That's understandable. Maupassant was an author of the golden age of Naturalism, while Ch'unwŏn always weaves his stories using the techniques of Idealism and Humanism he advocates.

Mo Yun-suk - Right. Although the authors' attitudes in contemplating (觀照) women differ, the depicted facts are all true. They are all correct. From the first passage to the last, the psychology and physiology of women, and their way of walking through life, are all correct.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Besides those, "Anna Karenina" (안나카레니나), "La Dame aux Camélias" (椿姬), Elena (에레나) in "On the Eve" (그 전날밤), Gide's (지이드) various heroines, Anatole France's (아나돌푸란스) heroines—they all depicted truth. They are all <146> masterpieces depicting the "fate of woman."

Reporter - What was the theme (테-마) of Yamamoto Yūzō's "A Woman's Life"?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - I read it a long time ago, so I don't remember well*, but a woman desperately longs for a lover but ultimately cannot marry him and marries another man. Much later, she coincidentally meets her former lover on a train, but even upon meeting, they don't touch upon their past love story at all, merely exchanging mundane family news like strangers on the roadside (路傍), and then go their separate ways, not knowing when they might meet again. I believe it depicted such a woman's fate. (*Original text ends with 않으* - likely 않으나)

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Speaking of women's fate, recently I saw a column in either the Yomiuri Shimbun (讀賣新聞) or the Tokyo Asahi (東京朝日) where Ms. Yamada Waka (山田ワヵ女史) was answering questions for women's consultations. It was filled with cries for help (구원을 부르짓*) from truly numerous shipwrecked (破船한) women. Just like planks of wood washed ashore at a pier (波止場), piling up disorderly (推積되드시), one woman asked what to do because she lost her chastity (貞操) and her lover ran away; another asked what to do because a man proposed a love suicide (情死); another was suffering from a love triangle (三角戀愛) and asked how to resolve it; another asked whether to pursue money or a handsome man, etc. Truly, a department store (백화점) of modern women's love and hate (愛憎) was laid out. Although they are fragments (斷片), if gathered, they become a long history of women's lives (一代史), and even left broken, they each represent an aspect of womanhood.

Reporter - In the end, what is a woman's life?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Being deceived (속히우고 - likely 속히고) by men, longing for men, hating men—it's a chain of love and hate.

Mo Yun-suk - They are nameless flowers that bloom halfway (반조고레 - likely 반쯤?) in the shadow (그늘 밑) of men and then disappear.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Whatever one may say, men are the boatmen (사공), and women are the passengers (승객) on that boat.

Reporter - Thank you for speaking at length. These are all excellent words.<147>

**Summary**

This 1939 roundtable discussion features three prominent Korean female intellectuals, Mo Yun-suk, Yi Sŏn-hŭi, and Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi, discussing the stages and nature of a woman's life in contemporary Korea. They touch upon the ambivalent feelings associated with marriage (contrasting the idealized "bride's morning" with the potential loss of freedom experienced after dating), the profound experience and societal expectations of motherhood, and the distant prospect of finding peace in old age as a respected elder receiving a 60th birthday banquet. While acknowledging moments of joy, the overall tone leans towards the tragic, emphasizing the limitations, sorrows (love, loss, societal constraints), and dependence on men (using metaphors like passengers on a boat steered by men, or flowers blooming in shadow) that define women's fate, drawing parallels with literary depictions of female suffering by authors like Maupassant, Pearl Buck, and Bai Juyi.

For historians, this article offers a valuable glimpse into the perspectives of educated Korean women during the late colonial period. It highlights the tension between traditional expectations (marriage, childbirth, filial piety) and emerging modern ideas (romantic love before marriage, individual aspirations, critique of societal constraints). The participants' references to both Eastern and Western literature, their nuanced views on gender roles within the specific context of colonial Korea (where male opportunities were also limited), and their frank discussion of female experiences like childbirth and aging provide rich material for understanding gender dynamics, cultural change, and intellectual life under Japanese rule.

『女子의 一生』을 말하는 佳人會議
毛允淑, 李善熙, 崔貞熙
「人間莫作女人身, 百年苦樂依他人」이라고 불넛스니 女人된 이의 괴롬이 그토록 深切했든가? 이제 우리는 이 땅 젊은 女性에게서 이곳 「女子의 一生」을 듯기로 하다.
1. 「新婦의 아츰」에 늦긴 感想
2. 「어머니」되든 날의 感想
3. 시어머니와 回甲 맛는 날의 感想
4. 大體로 女子의 運命이란 어떠한가

① 新婦의 첫날 아츰 感想
記者 - 세 분이 모다 30전 꽃같은 나이니 지금인들 人生이 어찌<138> 즐겁지 않으리만은 저 면사포를 쓰고 시집가든 그 이튼날 아츰 新婦로서의 첫날 아츰을 마지하든 그때의 깁부든 말슴을 좀 하여주서요. 「내 靑春」이 인제 왔구나」하고 혼자 微笑하든 일, 내 一生이 이제부터 피기 시작하는구나 하고 꿈 속에 취하엿든 그 가지가지 이야기들을...
毛允淑 - 결혼한 이튼날이 그렇게 좋은가요. 하하하. 어떤 의미로 그렇게 무르서요?
記者 - 웨 안조와요. 끼리시야의 「삽포-」란 여시인은 너무 깁버서 이제는 나는 천하를 얻엇노라 하고 춤추지 않었어요. 여자이기 까닭게 받어오든 천대와 여자이기 까닭에 떠나지 안튼 서글푼 悲哀 그 모든 것이 깡그리 사라지고 마치 구름 속에 보름달 솟듯 光明에 찬 新郞의 房으로 많은 꿈과 즐거움을 거더않고 드러갈때에 진실로 여자가 아니면 가질 수 없는 그윽한 행복에 저저질 것이 않이엇겠서요. 세 분 가튼 샛파란 꼿 閣氏가.
毛允淑 - 이 곳 녀자들에 깁붐이 있다면 얼마나 있었겠어요. 끼리시야 같이 문화가 빛나고 남성들이 제각금 활긔를 펴서 天下의 일을 경륜하는 그런 好丈夫가 찬사회라면 우리들 여성이 신혼 첫날을 마지하는게 오작이나 깁부엇겠서요. 成熟한 육신은 이 시절부터 피기 시작하겠고 꿈 실은 정신과 영혼은 끗없이 날개가 도처 좋은 세계로 나라 나라가기라도 하겠지만 一般으로 가난하고 교양이 없고 히망이 없는 신랑을 마지하는 이로의 신부의 심정이 무에 그리 깃부겠서요. 이 말은 내 개인에 대한 말슴이 안니라 우리들 이 곳 여성의 심리가 모다 그럴 줄 아러요.
記者 - 앗불사. 미처 말삼하여 둘 것을 오늘 저녁에 이약이하려는 이 여자의 일생같은 제목에 대하여서는 여러분은 다 남편있고 자녀있고 좋은 가정까지 갖이고 있으니까. 말슴하기 거북한 점도 여러 가지 있을 줄 아러요. 그러기에 오늘 저녁 이약이는 自己自身의 이약이를 안 하여주서도 조와요. 그저 우리 땅 여자의 한 사람으로 남의 心理 다른 여자의 心境을 말슴하여 주는 것으로 드를 터이니 조금도 誤解마시고 솔직하게 숨김이 없이 말슴하여 주서요.
崔貞熙 - 그러는 편이 좋겠서요. 그저 우리 자신의 고백으로 듯지 마시고 어떤 여자가 그렇게 말하고 지나드라 하는 식으로 드러주서요.
「신부의 깁붐」 말슴인데 결혼 이튼날의 여성의 깁붐이야 다 말할 수 없이 누구든지 클 것입니다. 누구는 천하를 얻덧다고 하였다고 하나 천하까지는 몰나도 재가 일생을 依托하려는 힘굿센 남성을 완전히 얻어으니 그 깁붐이 좀 크겠어요. 아마 부모의 배박게 나서 이만한 크고 깁고 화려하고 멋진 깁붐은 두 번 다시 없을 것입니다.
그러나 時代女性에게는 이러한 신부의 아츰보다.<139> 「연애시절에 잇는 날마다 날마다의 그 아츰」이 더 깁부지 않었을까요. 않었을까요가 아니라 확실히 더 깁불 것 같애요. 처음으로 異性의 香氣에 醉하든 그 시절이 더 깁불 것 같애요. 지금 시절의 처자들은 연애시대를 확실히 더 즐기려 하고 더 갑있게 꾸미려 하니까요.
記者 - 그러면 연애시절에 벌서 여성들은 한 少女로서 한 안해가 되여지는 모든 과정을 다 밟고만다는 뜻이서요?
崔貞熙 - 가령 그것이 육체의 경지에까지 이르지 안는다 할지라도 여성으로서의 깁분 감정이란 대개는 그 사이에 경과하고 말게되지요. 그러기에 연애시대의 여성은 「저 별이 저렇게 빛나는 줄을 예전엔 미처 몰났어요」 「지나가는 봄바람의 향내가 이렇게 다스할 줄은 미처 몰났어요」하고 모든 天地萬物이 그 한 분 남성의 존재를 통하여 모도 다 새로 늣겨지고 새로 깨달어지게 될 것 같애요. 요 안 그래요?
李善熙 - 그러지요. 결혼하게 되면 결혼이란 한 事務的임에 불과하게 될 것 아님니까. 彼此에 서로 다 알어진 남녀끼리 式을 하여 결합의 標를 삼고 戶籍에 夫婦라 올니고 그제부터는 空想의 로-만티시즘을 떠나 현실적인 너무나 현실적인 모든 문제에 맛부닷치게 되지요.
첫재 가정을 일어야 하지요.
