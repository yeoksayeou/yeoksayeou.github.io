Preface to the Book of Songs

Someone asked me, "For what reason were the poems created?" I answered, saying, "Man is born tranquil; this is the nature endowed by Heaven. To be moved by external things is the desire of this nature. Since there are desires, one cannot be without thoughts. Since there are thoughts, one cannot be without words. Since there are words, when words cannot fully express, what bursts forth in sighs and songs must naturally have rhythm and sound, unable to cease. This is why poetry is created." He then asked, "So, what is its didactic purpose?" I replied, "Poetry is the surplus of what the human heart feels when moved by things, expressed in words. The feelings of the heart can be deviant or proper, thus the expressions in words have right and wrong. Only when a sage is in a high position are his feelings always proper, and all his words are sufficient for instruction. If perhaps the feelings are mixed, and what is expressed cannot be chosen, then the person in a high position must reflect upon himself and thereby encourage good and punish evil; this also serves as instruction. In the past, during the flourishing era of Chou (周), from the suburban temples and court down to the village lanes, the words were pure and invariably stemmed from propriety. The sage had already harmonized them with melody and rhythm, using them among the countrymen and in the states to transform the world. As for the poems of the various states, when the Son of Heaven made his inspection tours, they were presented and examined as a basis for the rites of promotion and demotion. From the time after Kings Chao (昭) and Mu (穆), decline gradually set in, and by the time of the eastward move [of the capital], the practice was abandoned and no longer discussed. Confucius (孔子), born in that era, did not attain a position to enact policies of encouragement, punishment, promotion, and demotion. Therefore, he specifically took up the records, discussed them, removed repetitions, corrected disorder, and even excised those poems whose goodness was insufficient as a model or whose evil was insufficient as a warning, aiming for conciseness to reveal them for posterity. This was so that scholars could thereby examine the gains and losses, emulating the good and correcting the bad. Thus, although his governance could not be implemented in his time, his teachings truly spread across ten thousand generations. This is how poetry serves <266> as instruction." He asked, "Then, why are the forms of the Kuo Feng (國風), Ya (雅), and Sung (頌) so different?" I replied, "I have heard that what are called Feng in poetry mostly originated from the songs and ballads of village lanes. They are what men and women sang together, each expressing their feelings. Only the Chou Nan (周南) and Shao Nan (召南) were directly influenced by the civilizing virtue of King Wen (文王) to achieve moral perfection, and everyone was able to attain the propriety of their nature and emotions. Therefore, what they expressed in words was joyful without being licentious, and sorrowful without being injurious. Thus, these two sections alone are considered the orthodox canon of the Feng poems. From Pei Feng (邶風) downwards, the governance of the states varied, the worthiness of the people differed, and what they felt and expressed displayed an uneven mix of deviance and propriety, right and wrong. Thus, what was called the 'style of the former kings' changed. As for the Ya and Sung sections, they are all lyrics for music performed at court and in the suburban temples during the Chou dynasty. Their language is harmonious and solemn, their meanings broad and meticulous. Their authors were often disciples of the sages, inherently serving as models for ten thousand generations, immutable. Even the 'altered' Ya (變雅) poems were largely composed by worthy men and gentlemen of the time, lamenting the times and grieving over customs. The sage selected them for their loyal, compassionate hearts and their intention to promote good and suppress evil, something which later eloquent scholars could hardly replicate. This is why the Book of Songs, as a classic, encompasses human affairs below and is equipped with the principles of Heaven above, lacking no single principle." He asked, "Then how should one study it?" I replied, "Base oneself on the two Nan sections to seek its beginning, consult the various states to exhaust its variations, rectify with the Ya to broaden its scope, and harmonize with the Sung to grasp its endpoint. This is the main gist of studying poetry. Thereupon, use the chapters and verses (章句) to structure it, glosses (訓詁) to record it, recitation (諷咏) to make it flourish, and immersion (涌濡) to embody it. Examine it in the subtleties of emotion and nature, scrutinize it at the inception of words and deeds (樞機), then the way to cultivate the self, regulate the family, and bring peace to the world (脩身及家平均天下之道) will also be found herein without needing to seek elsewhere." The questioner nodded assent and withdrew. As I was compiling the 'Commentary on the Book of Songs' (詩傳) at that time, I accordingly arranged these words to preface the volume. Written by Chu Hsi (朱熹) of Hsin-an (新安) on the wu-tzu day of the tenth month, winter, in the ting-yu year of the Ch'un-hsi era (淳熙四年, 1177).

Preface to the Sijŏn (Book of Songs)

A visitor came to me and asked, "For what purpose was poetry (詩) created?" I answered, saying, "Human nature is originally tranquil. When it is moved by things, this is the desire of nature. Since desires arise, thoughts cannot but exist; since thoughts exist, words cannot but exist; since words exist, what cannot be fully expressed through words—the movements of a fleeting and aimless heart—will inevitably find expression, 맞춰, in natural sounds and rhythms, <267> unable to cease. This is the reason songs came to be composed." He then asked, "Then how does it become instruction for people?" "Poetry is the remainder of the human heart's feelings, moved by things, appearing in words. As the heart feels, there is right and wrong, so when it appears in words, it is divided into right and wrong. If a Sage (聖人) is above, there is nothing he feels that is not proper, so his words are all sufficient to serve as instruction. But if his feelings are mixed, reaching a point where discernment and selection are needed, then being the superior person, he must certainly reflect upon himself. Consequently, by encouraging the good and punishing (懲戒) the wicked, it also becomes instruction. In the past, during the flourishing times of the Chou (周) dynasty, from the capital palaces down to the rural villages (洞里), their [expressions] were pure (淳粹), all arising from propriety. The Sage himself composed sounds and rhythms for them, so the entire nation sang, and the whole world (天下) was harmonized. Furthermore, when the Son of Heaven (天子) toured the various states (列國), he would view the poetry (詩) of that state and reward or punish officials accordingly. From the time of King Chao (昭王) and King Chi (稷王, likely Mu 穆王), that system declined. When King P'ing (平王) moved the capital to Loyang (洛陽), it finally disappeared. Confucius (孔子) was born in that era, did not attain a position (位), and could not carry out governance (政事). Thus, he discussed the Poetry, eliminated repetitions, corrected disorder, discarded things that, though good, could not serve as law, and things that, though evil, were not worthy of punishment (懲戒), revised them to be concise (簡約), and revealed them for eternity, enabling learners to contemplate the gains and losses, follow the good, and correct the wrong. Therefore, although his governance might not have been implemented in his time, his teachings truly reached myriad generations. How could it not be instruction? However, why are the forms called Feng (風), Ya (雅), and Sung (頌) dissimilar?" he asked. I answered again, "I have heard that what is generally called Feng in Poetry mostly came from songs sung in the village alleys (閭巷). It is men and women singing together, each expressing their feelings (情). Only Chou Nan (周南) and Shao Nan (召南) personally received the civilizing influence (德化) of King Wen (文王), achieving virtue, so everyone's heart was proper. Looking at what appeared in their words, they were joyful but not licentious (淫), sorrowful but not harmful (傷). Thus, these two sections alone are called the 'orthodox canon' (正經) of the Feng Poetry. From Pei Feng (邶風) downwards, the governance (治亂) of each state was not the same, and the wisdom/foolishness (賢愚) of the people also differed. What they felt and expressed in song was also uneven in propriety (發르고) and deviance (姿邪), right and wrong. Thus, the 'civilizing influence of the former kings' (先王之風化) changed in this way. As for the <268> Ya and Sung sections, they are all court music (風流) from the time of the Chou dynasty. Their words are balanced yet vigorous (씩씩하고), their meaning broad yet dense (빽빽하니). The composers were generally disciples (弟子) of the Sage. They were originally models (法程) for myriad generations and cannot be changed. Even in the 'Altered Ya' (變雅), they were all composed by worthy men and gentlemen (賢人君子) worried about the times and concerned about customs. The Sage selected them for their loyalty to the country and compassionate feelings (뜯을) towards the people. This is beyond the capability of eloquent scholars (선비) of later generations. Also, being the 'Classic' (經) of Poetry, it below permeates human affairs (浹洽) and above is equipped with the principles (理致) of heaven. Not a single aspect of humanity is missing." The visitor asked about the method of learning it. I answered, "Take the two Nan sections as the foundation, exhaust the variations (變) of the various states (列國) to find their meaning (뜯을), correct with the Ya to broaden the scale (규모), and harmonize with the Sung to reach the end. This is the method. Therefore, read the chapters and verses (章句), record the glosses (訓話), recite and memorize (을프고 외여서) to practice it. If you examine the inner heart and are careful at the beginning of words and actions (言行), the way to cultivate the self, manage the household, and pacify the world (天下) will be found here." The visitor then withdrew. As I was currently compiling the Sijŏn (詩傳), I used the words I spoke in reply to the visitor as the preface. Written by Chu Hsi (朱熹) of Hsin-an (新安) on the wu-tzu day of the tenth month, winter, in the ting-yu year (丁酉) of the Ch'un-hsi (淳熙) era (fourth year, 1177). <269>

Selection of Greek Lyric Poetry

An Sŏ (岸曙)

It goes without saying how extremely difficult the transplantation (移植) of poetry is, but my attitude in translating these Greek lyric poems this time is no different from my previous assertions. In other words, I took the ideas from the original Japanese translation (和譯) of the original poems and strove to make them resemble poetry with the freest possible brushstrokes. The world seems to value direct translation (直譯) or faithful word-for-word translation (遂字譯), but this is something I absolutely cannot abide. Why? Because it does nothing but kill the poetry itself forever.
There may be those who ridicule my free style as incorrect, but my assertion will never change.
Well, leaving that aside, on this occasion, I would like to briefly relate the story of the Greek poetess Sappho (삽포). This is because considerable interest arises.
Excluding Homer (호메로스), Sappho's poetic evaluation as a woman is just as high today as it was in the past, without the slightest difference. As for why this poetess became so famous, it is said to be because her songs were elegant, sincere, possessed a feminine grace, and had a beautiful sentiment. However, it is regrettable that this poetess's poems remain only in fragments, making it difficult to glimpse their full glory (金面). It seems that Sappho's poems were mostly burned along with other books because they largely dealt with love (戀愛), so what remains today are mostly snippets discovered in various books. Thus, there is a sense of searching for the fragrance (芳香) of a past poetic season from fallen flower petals. <272>
Sappho was born around the end of the 7th century BC in the town of Eresos (에레소스邑) on the island of Lesbos (레스보스島) off the coast of Asia Minor (소아시아). In the local Aeolic (아이오리스語) dialect, her name is Psappho (프삽포). She was from the local aristocracy (貴族), became involved in a dispute that arose then with the burgeoning commercial petty bourgeoisie, and left her homeland for a while with her clan (一族同類). Later, she returned to her hometown, set up house in the capital Mytilene (미티레네), and is said to have taught young maidens the zither (거문고) and dance. Besides songs about entertaining friends and weddings (婚禮), many of her works (制作) seem to have been composed for use on such occasions.
Sappho also had friendly relations with the poet Alcaeus (알카이어스) from the same island. Her brothers held considerable status (지위) and wealth (재산) and traveled back and forth doing business. The fact that this poetess loved a maiden named Cleis (크레이스) like gold seems true, and it appears this is why she was called a homosexual (同性愛者). However, detailed biographical information (傳紀) about this poetess lacks sufficient evidence (憑證), so it is regrettable again that I cannot elaborate further.
If even a glimpse of this poetess can be caught from these few translated poems, my joy as the translator (譯者) would be great, and it would likely do no harm to the bygone poetess either.

Irish Folk Song

Translated by Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑 譯)

I met little Collen (콜넨, horse name?) in the market
"God protect thee," I whispered
He intoxicates my heart.

Faraway Irish mountain village
When my hand grows strong with the grass
My heart leaps lightly with joy.

The Irish house you always sought
Will greet you courteously with a smiling face.
Oh! How long I have missed brown Pogi (뽀기, horse name?)
Strong and cheerful Pogi
May God above the blue sky abide upon you.

She is always kind, humble, and wise
A woman of love, faithful also in work
Prayer dwells in her heart <277>

A smile lingers in her eyes
Trustworthy Irish mother, Mercury (매큐리)!

Rekindling tender memories
Beloved old Irish roads!

Though I wander far
My heart stays in the joyful little Irish home.

Binding my thoughts (想念) with mystical threads
Take me to the shores of the Irish lakes.

When hope truly finds me, my wish is
In that little house in the Irish valley
To stay with you.

Carefree and peaceful Ireland
Always welcoming and waiting for you.

Like in the old days, like a flower
I see Samnyak (샘략, horse name?) growing on the cool path. <278>

A Visitor in White Arrives

Yŏsu (麗水)

I do not wish to say anything
Beautiful words, embellished this way and that
I dislike the falsehood that follows those words
Rather, I will say nothing at all

Besides, what is there for me to say now?
You know everything better than I
And the spirits of heaven and earth (하눌땅의 신명) know everything
That is all - there is nothing more to say <281>

Fallen leaves (락엽) tumbled futilely onto the street
Just like before, the visitor in white (소복닙은 손님) - winter
Shakes the window frame (들창문) of my solitary weeping
Again tonight, I only sigh in vain.

Not on paper (조희우), but now
I am recording in my heart
A heavy silence drifts in the room
Outside on the street, a snowstorm (눈보라) is still raging. <282>

That Beloved Day

Karam (가람)

The lark's ear is tempted, the mayfly catches the eye
Digging wild onions (옹곳) in the marshy field (진펄밭), climbing the mountain (뫼) to pick bracken
Sitting upright (오또기) in the room, disliking reciting texts.

On the grassless riverbank (강변), the sun's rays are harsh

Stealing beans (콩서리) and roasting them over a bonfire (모닥불)
Pestering the old man making straw mats (밀방석) and twisting hemp ropes (신삼는)

That winter, the winter solstice month (동지 섣달), did we not know the cold?
Barefoot in the snow, racing with friends (동모)
Comparing icicles on every rice stack (볏가리). <283>

Last Night of the Month

Kim Myŏngsun (金明淳)

On the last night of the month, the stars seemed likely to fall
I gathered an armful, but this emptiness
Insects cry out.

Seeking autumn, I came to a deep valley
Blue, yellow, red abound (靑黃赤 爛漫한대), where is this place?
The sound of water is profound, knowing hidden feelings (情).

Oh, gentle stream flowing over the sandy path
I raise my voice and call your name <284>
Let us go together on the road to the sea.

At the end of the desolate street, the beloved is unlikely to come
When longing arises, the moon rises as if they have come
Perhaps the same image will appear before my eyes.

Late on an early winter night, reading difficult texts
The sound of dragging slippers (曳履聲) in the back garden sounds like theirs
But not knowing my hardship, is it the sound of falling leaves (落葉聲)?

If my wish comes true, flying freely and seeing
Beside the fortunate stove where my beloved laughs and works
As a bluebird, I would announce this cold. <285>

Plucking White Hairs

An Sŏ (岸曙)

Playing a toy trumpet and beating a drum "dong-dong"
Gathered in the yard for the children's enjoyment, making noise (홍어릴제)
The shining sun, already early autumn, is suddenly at sunset (夕陽)
Sitting on the sunlit veranda, I gaze absently
Holding a mirror, plucking white hairs alone.

An old saying goes, the young age easily
And the old depart one by one.
Rereading books read long ago (두적이며)
Trying to fill the emptiness (空虛) of words, is there even merit?
Lost in aimless thoughts (心思), just stroking white hairs.

Around twenty, like flowers, this beautiful world (世相)
Quietly sketching castles in the air (空中巨閣) in my mind,
Alone enjoying and agonizing over the philosophy (哲理) of this life, the happiness of this world
But soon growing old, lamenting,
A bowl of rice (밥) becomes precious, sons and daughters all seem beyond measure (가이없을 듯해).
Children playing, clapping hands with excitement
In tearful recollection (回想), I lonelily, lonelily
Stroke my white hairs and gaze with a sigh. <288>

**Summary**

This article from the January 1939 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents a diverse collection of poetry. It begins with a preface and translation of Chu Hsi's commentary on the Chinese *Book of Songs*, followed by translated excerpts from the *Book of Songs* itself. It then shifts to translations of Greek lyric poetry (primarily Sappho), an Irish folk song, and concludes with several original poems by contemporary Korean poets (Wŏlt'an, Yŏsu, Karam, Kim Myŏngsun, No Ch'unsŏng, An Sŏ) covering themes like nature, historical sites (Sŏkkuram), personal reflections on aging, solitude, and longing. The inclusion of classical Chinese, ancient Greek, Irish folk, and modern Korean poetry within a single issue highlights the complex cultural landscape of colonial Korea, showcasing an engagement with both Eastern traditions and Western literature, alongside contemporary Korean creative expression.

For historians, this collection is interesting as it demonstrates the literary currents and cultural consumption patterns among Korean intellectuals during the late colonial period. The juxtaposition of classical East Asian texts (Chu Hsi, *Book of Songs*) with Western classics (Sappho) and modern Korean works reflects an era grappling with tradition, modernity, and foreign influence under Japanese rule. The translation efforts themselves (from Chinese, Greek via likely Japanese, and English/Irish) and the themes of nostalgia, nature, and introspection in the original poems offer insights into the intellectual and emotional climate of the time. The inclusion of an Irish folk song might subtly resonate with themes of national identity and longing under foreign influence.

詩傳 =孔子曰, 詩三百一言以蔽之, 曰, 思無邪. - 論語-
						柳錫彬 譯
詩傳序

或有問於予曰, 詩何爲而作也. 予應, 之曰, 人生而靜, 天之性也. 感於物而動, 性之欲也. 夫旣有欲矣, 則不能無思, 旣有思矣, 則不能無言, 旣有言矣, 則言之所不能盡而發於咨嗟咏歎之餘者, 必有自然之音響節族而不能己焉. 此詩之所以作也. 曰然則其所以敎者何也. 曰, 詩者人心之感物而形於言之餘也. 心之所感有邪正, 故言之所形有是非. 惟聖人在上則其所感者無不正, 而其言皆足以爲敎. 其或感之之雜, 而所發不能焉可擇者, 則上之人必思所以自反, 而因有以勸懲之, 是亦所以爲敎也. 昔周盛時, 上自郊廟廟廷而下達於鄕黨閭巷, 其言粹然, 無不出於正者, 聖人固己協之聲律, 而用之鄕人, 用之邦國, 以化天下. 至於列國之詩, 則天子巡守必陳而觀之以行黜陟之典. 降自昭穆而後寖以陵夷, 至於東遷而遂廢不講矣. 孔子生於其時, 旣不得位無以行勸懲黜陟之政, 於是特擧其籍而討論之, 去其重複, 正其紛亂, 而其善之不足以爲戒者, 則亦刋而去之, 以從簡約示久遠. 使夫學者卽是以有以考其得失, 善者師之, 而惡者改焉. 是以其政雖不足以行於一時, 而其敎實被於萬世, 是則詩之<266> 所以爲敎者然也. 曰, 然則國風雅頌之體其不同若是何也. 曰, 吾聞之凡詩之所謂風者多出於里巷歌謠之作. 所謂男女相與詠歌各言其情者也. 唯周南親被文王之化以成德, 而人皆有以得其性情之正, 故其發於言者樂而不過於淫, 哀而不及於傷, 是以二篇獨爲風詩之正經, 自邶而下, 則其國之治亂不同, 人之賢否異, 其所感而發者有邪正是非之不齊, 而所謂先王之風者於此焉變矣, 若夫雅頌之篇, 則皆成周之世朝廷郊廟樂歌之詞, 其語和而莊, 其義寬而密其作者往往聖人之徒, 固所以爲萬世法程, 而不可易者也. 至於雅之變者亦不皆一時之人君子閔時病俗之所爲, 而聖人取之其忠厚惻怛之心, 陳善閉邪之意, 尤非後世能言之士所能反之. 此詩之爲經所以人事之於下天道備於上, 而無一理之不具也. 曰, 然則其學之也當柰何. 曰, 本之二南以求其端叅之列國以盡其變, 正之於雅以大其規, 和之於頌以要其止, 此學詩之大旨也. 於是乎章句以綱之, 訓詁以紀之, 諷咏以昌之, 涌濡以體之察之, 情性隱微之閒, 審之言行樞機之始, 則脩身及家平均天下之道其亦, 不待他, 求而得之於此矣. 問者唯唯而退, 余時方輯詩傳, 因悉次是語以冠其篇云. 淳熙四年丁酉冬十月戊子新安朱熹書.

손이 내게 와서 詩는 무엇 때문에 만드러 낸 것이뇨 하고 묻는지라. 대답하야 말하되 사람의 본성은 원래 고요한 것이라 그것이 사물에 느껴 움직이니 이는 본성에 바라는 배라. 이믜 物欲이 생기매 생각이 없을 수 없으며 이믜 생각이 있으매 말이 없을 수 없으니 이믜 말이 있은즉 말로다 할 수 없는 덧 없음과 하염없는 마음에 움직임을 자연의 소리와 가락에<267> 마처 을퍼 마지 아닐 것이니 이것이 노래를 짓게 된 까닭이라. 그러면 그것이 어떻게 시람에게 가라침이 되나뇨 하고 또 무르니 시는 사람의 마음이 사물에 느껴 말에 나타난 나마지이니 마음이 느끼매 그른 것과 발른 것이 있어 그것이 말로 나타나매 올흔 것과 올치 않은 것으로 난흐이니 聖人이 우에 게시면 느끼매 발로지 아니한 것이 없어서 그 말이 모다 가라침이 됨에 足하나 혹시 그 느낀 바이 잡스러워서 분간하야 가려내일 지지경어면 우ㅅ사람이 되야 반드시 스스로 도리키여 살필지니 말미아마 착한 것을 勸하고 악한 것을 懲戒하야 또한 가라침이 되는 것이라. 예전 周ㅅ나라 盛할 때에는 서울 대궐로부터 시골 洞里에 이르기까지 그들이 淳粹하야 모다 발른 것이여쓰니 聖人이 스스로 소리와 가락을 지어주사 왼나라이 불러 天下이 다 和하야 쓰며 또 天子이 列國에 巡狩하사 그 나라의 詩를 보시고 관리를 賞罰하시여 쓰니 昭王 稷王때부터 그 제도가 衰하더니 平王때 洛陽으로 遷都함에 이르러서는 드듸어 없어저 버려쓰니 孔子는 그 때에 나으사 位를 얻지 못하시고 政事를 행치 못하시니 이에 詩를 토론하야 거듭된 것을 없이하고 어지러운 것을 바로 자바 착한 것이라도 法으로는 삼지 못할 것과 惡한 것이라도 懲戒ㅅ거리가 못될 것 등은 버리고 고처서 簡約하게 하야 영원히 밝혀주사 배흐는 사람으로 하야곰 그 得失을 생각하야 착한 것을 조츠며 그른 것은 고치라 하서쓰니 이럼으로 그의 정치는 비록 한 때에 行치 못하였다 할지라도 그의 가라침으 참으로 만세에 미처쓰니 어찌 가라침이 안이랴. 그러나 風雅頌이라하야 그 體가 같지 아니함은 무슨 綠故뇨 하고 묻는지라. 또 대답하되 듣건대 무릇 詩에 이른바 風은 閭巷에서 만히 불러 나온 것이니 男女가 서로 그 情을  을퍼 노래한 것이라. 오직 周南召南은 文王의 德化를 친히 이버 德이 이루지저 사람마다 마음이 발라서 말에 나타난 것을 보드라도 즐기되 淫치 아니하고 슬프되 傷치 아니하야 이 두 篇은 홀로 風詩에 바른 날(正經)이라 하야스며 邶風으로부터 아래는 各其 나라에 治亂이 같지 아니하고 사람에 賢愚도 또한 달른지라 그 느껴 노래에 나타난 것도 발르고 姿邪하고 올코 그른 것이 골르지 못하야 선왕의 風化도 이러틋 변하야 진것이라. 저<268> 雅頌篇을 다 成周ㅅ나라 때 나라에 풍류이니 그 말이 고르되 씩씩하고 그 뜯이 너그럽되 빽빽하니 그것을 지은 사람도 大槪 聖人의 弟子이라. 본래 萬世에 法程으로 가히 밧고지 못할 것이며 變雅에 있어서도 모다 賢人君子 이 때를 걱정하고 풍속을 근식하야 지은 것이니 그 나라에 충성함과 백성을 불상히 역인 뜯을 성인이 취한 것이니 後世의 말하자는 선비에 능치 못할 바이라. 또한 詩에 날(經)이니 아래로는 사람일에 浹洽하고 위로는 하날 理致로 가초와 있으니 한 가지에 다 사람도 빠짐이 없난 것이라. 손이 그 배호는 방법은 무르매 대답하되 二南으로 근본을 삼고 列國에 變을 다하야 그 뜯을 차즈며 雅를 바로하야 그 규모를 넓히여 頌에 和하야 그칠지니 이것이 방법이라. 이에 章句를 일거 訓話를 적고 을프고 외여서 行할지니 마음 속을 살피고 言行의 비롯을 삼가면 몸을 닥고 집을 다사리며 天下를 고르게 하는 길리이 이에 있을지라. 손이 그만 물러가니 내 바야흐로 詩傳을 編輯하고 있는 때이라 손에게 대답한 말로 머리ㅅ말을 삼다 순희 사년 정유 동시월 무자신안 주희는 서하노라.<269>

希臘抒情詩歌抄

岸曙

詩歌의 移植이 어떻게 至難한 일인지는 새삼스러이 이야기할가 없거니와 나는 이번 希臘 抒情詩들을 옴겨 놓음에 대한 나의 태도는 종래의 주장과 조곰도 다름이 없었읍니다. 다시 말하면 和譯의 原詩에서 그 想을 따다가 가장 자유로운 筆致로 詩歌답도록 만들기에 힘을 썻을 뿐입니다. 直譯이니 충실한 遂字譯이니 하는 것을 세상에서는 존중하는 상 싶으나 이것은 나의 어디까지든지 참아할 수 없는 일이외다. 웨냐하면 그것은 永永 詩 그것을 죽여버리는 것 밧게 아니되기 때문이외다.
나의 이러한 자유로운 筆致를 아니라고 비웃는 분이 잇을 것이외다만은 나의 이 주장은 언제나 변치 않을 것이외다.
자 이것은 이러타 하고 나는 이 기회에 잠간 希臘 女詩人 삽포의 이야기까지 옴겨 보랴고 합니다. 적지 아니한 흥미가 일기 때문이외다.
호메로스를 除하고는 여성으로의 삽포의 시적 평가는 예나 지금이나 조곰도 다름이 없을 만치 놉습니다. 엇지 하야 이 女詩人이 이렇게 이름이 높았는가에 대하야는 그의 노래가 高雅하고 진실하고 女性다운 優雅에 다가 아름다운 심정을 갖었기 때문이라 함니다. 그러나 이 女詩人의 詩篇이 단편으로만 남아서 그 金面을 엿보기 어려운 것이 恨이외다. 삽포의 詩歌가 대개 戀愛에 관한 것이라는 이유로 다른 서적과 같이 焚燒되야 지금 남은 것이란 대개가 이 책 저 책에서 발견된 토막 토막에 지내지 아니하고 보니 떨어진 꽃송이에서 지내간 詩節의 芳香을 찿는 感이 없지 아니하외다.<272>
삽포는 기원전 7세기말경 소아시아 해안 「레스보스」島의 「에레소스」邑에서 낳는데 고향인 아이오리스語로는 프삽포라고 합니다. 그 토지 귀족의 출신으로 그때 釀成된 상업소시민과의 분쟁사건에 관련되야 一族同類와 함께 한동안 고국을 떠났다가 그 뒤 고향에 돌아와 수도 「미티레네」에다 집을 잡고 어린 처녀들에게 거문고와 춤을 가르첬다 합니다. 이 女詩人의 制作에는 친구와의 接待 婚禮를 노래한 이외에도 이러한 때에 사용할 목적으로 지은 것이 많은 듯 하외다.
삽포는 同島의 시인 「알카이어스」와도 친교가 있었고 이 女詩人의 형제들은 상당한 지위과 재산이 있어서 옛집을 왔다 갓다 하며 장사를 하였다 합니다. 그런데 이 女詩人 크레이스라는 처녀를 황금과 같이 사랑하였다는 것은 사실인 듯 할 뿐 아니라 이 시인을 同性愛者라고 한 것은 이 때문인 듯 하외다. 그러나 이 女詩人의 자세한 傳紀는 이러타고 憑證할 만한 것이 적어서 더 자세이 이야기 할 수 없는 것이 다시금 유감어외다.
몇 편 아니되는 이 譯詩에서나마 이 女詩人의 한모가 엿보여진다고 하면 譯者인 나로서의 깃붐도 크려니와 또한 지내간이 女詩人에게노 害로울 것은 없을 것이외다.
