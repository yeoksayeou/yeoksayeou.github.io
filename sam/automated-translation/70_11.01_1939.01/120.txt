Yulgok, Forerunner of the East: The Master's Doctrine of Rich Country, Strong Army
						By Yu Cha-hu

Throughout the five hundred years of the Yi Dynasty, considerable figures succeeded one another in each generation, but never before in history was there such an outpouring of generals and ministers of military prowess and literary accomplishment, and such pure, magnificent sages and scholars, as immediately before and after the era of King Sŏnjo. Among them, listing a few sage scholars, starting with Chŏngam Cho Kwang-jo, there were T'oegye Yi Hwang, Kubong Song Ik-p'il, Hwadam Sŏ Kyŏng-dŏk, Ugye Sŏng Hon, Hasŏ Kim In-hu, Namyŏng Cho Sik, Mojae Kim An-guk, Hwanggam Kim Kye-hwi, his son Sagye Kim Chang-saeng, and the accomplished master of both literary and martial arts, Yi Sun-sin. There were many figures who were like "blue dye emerging from indigo, surpassing indigo," but broadly speaking, Yi Sun-sin was the highest peak in martial arts, and Master Yulgok was the highest peak in literary arts. However, this comparison is made in terms of their contributions to the nation; as a sage of heavenly nature, enlightened and magnanimous, our Master Yulgok stood tall as the main peak among the群岳 (crowd of peaks). It is a famous story that the invention of the renowned Admiral Ch'ungmu's Turtle Ship was also Master Yulgok's creation. During his lifetime, the place that is now Chukdonggung Palace in Kwanhun-dong, Kyŏngsŏng, was originally Master Yulgok's residence, and there was a pond where he reportedly built model turtle ships and conducted launch tests multiple times. The Master was truly someone who, without a teacher, understood the myriad phenomena of the universe and the single principle underlying them (萬殊一理) as if born knowing (生面知之). If he had a teacher in his youth, it was only his mother, Saimdang.

Among the various scholars and worthies mentioned above, the most intensely debated topic was the Theory of Principle and Material Force (理氣之說). In addressing this, he overturned the views of the other worthies and established his own unique theory <95> for all ages. Considering how he mutually cited and cross-referenced not only the theories of Confucius, Mencius, Cheng Yi, and Zhu Xi—the sages of Confucianism—but also the Buddhist theory of the empty bottle (空甁說) and Laozi's theory of being arising from non-being (有生於無說), he must have achieved great enlightenment and sagehood in Buddhism and Daoism as well. He must have been a great sage of supreme authority who grasped the profound, mysterious, fundamental, and correct true message of the three teachings: Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism. Borrowing Master Yulgok's dialectic of "material force initiates, principle rides" (氣發理乘), later generations might say that in terms of material force (氣), Master Yulgok could be called the grand master of the Confucian tradition, confined to that one teaching. But in terms of principle (理), he was a great sage-teacher who could have founded a comprehensive religion for the universe and humanity.

However, because Chosŏn during Master Yulgok's time was primarily a Confucian state that revered literary culture, the standards and measures for achieving practical results in governing the state and saving the people lay in Confucianism, thus he emerged as a grand master of that tradition. Had he gone to the Buddhist gate, the monks would have called him a living Buddha; had he gone to Daoism, the enlightened ones would have revered him as one of the Three Pure Ones (三淸). In short, Master Yulgok was a sage of the great self of the universal heavenly way, a being who transcended teachings like Confucianism, Buddhism, or Daoism.

Master Yulgok became a sage within his early adulthood (弱冠時代), having read and understood everything (無所不讀하시고 無所不知하사) in the literary classics and scriptures. Why did he knock on the quiet Zen gate at Mahayŏn Hermitage in the Diamond Mountains? It is thought that, as a result of thoroughly understanding the teachings of Confucius and Mencius, they were insufficient for his vast sage capacity, not providing enough room. Thus, he aimed to expand and extend his own being beyond the path of Confucian learning, striving for a sagehood greater than Confucius and Mencius, that is, transformation into the eternal great self (永生大我化). If not, it wasn't so. This was the expansive practice of the saying "the sage has great desires." Master Yulgok, embracing the eternal great desire for the eternal great self in his mind and heart, surely must have moved his sacred footsteps to the Zen gate of the Diamond Mountains for this reason. And so, spending a full year absorbing the Tripitaka (三寶에 經典), making it his own (我有化하사), he achieved thorough understanding of the Buddhist path. I once pondered deeply on this point. If Chosŏn at that time had not suffered the tyranny of the Yŏnsan reign and had avoided the later wars, achieving peace for the people and security for the nation, perhaps Master Yulgok would not have returned permanently to the Confucian fold. Perhaps he might have further absorbed Daoism and manifested as the revered sage of a supreme world religion, unparalleled globally, unifying the three teachings (三合渾一)? The reason is, if Master Yulgok hadn't had such thoughts, he would likely never have gone to the Zen gate in the Diamond Mountains in the first place. And having already been revered as a living Buddha at the Zen gate, there is absolutely no chance he would have descended the mountain again. Master Yulgok was our past sacrifice. It is clear that he descended the mountain to save the suffering masses who were about to fall into misery (塗炭). This is how the name "sage-scholar and statesman" came about.

After descending the mountain, although he opposed the deviant monk Pou and the Ŏpchŏngwŏn (a government office related to Buddhism), he never advocated for the abolition of temples (寺院撤廢論) or the expulsion of virtuous monks (賢僧排斥論).<96> If he had intended to eradicate Buddhism, he would have argued for the destruction of all Buddhist temples nationwide, seeking to permanently extinguish Buddhism throughout the country.

Just as the saying goes, "Only a sage can know a sage," the great sage Master Yulgok knew well not only the sagehood of Confucius but also the sagehood of Shakyamuni. He never rejected the sage Shakyamuni. On the contrary, he adopted many good things from Buddhism. As evidence, when addressing King Sŏnjo, he frequently used four-character aphorisms (四子에 佳句). When King Sŏnjo heard this and asked, "How is it that you use four-character aphorisms so well, minister?" the Master, without the slightest hesitation, replied, "It is because, when Your Majesty's humble servant was formerly in the mountains, I read much of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra (楞嚴經)." (The Śūraṅgama Sūtra is a text for overcoming Māra, the king of demons). This conversation between ruler and minister has been passed down as a great anecdote. The Master, serving in court as a sage, spoke of having read Buddhist scriptures extensively before the king. Is this statement one that truly rejects Buddhism? On the contrary, it is surely a statement friendly to Buddhism. When he was wandering in the P'ungak (Diamond) Mountains, he met an old monk in a small hermitage. After sharing drinks (酬酌하신 후에), at the old monk's request, he appropriately used the Buddhist analogy of the empty bottle (空甁) in relation to the Confucian theory of principle and material force. In a letter sent to Sŏng Ugye (Sŏng Hon):
"That the principle of man is the principle of things means that principle is universal (理之通也). Just as the shape of square and round containers differs, the water within the containers is one; just as the size of large and small bottles differs, the emptiness within the bottles (甁中之空) is one," etc. He also sent a poem on Principle and Material Force (理氣吟詩) to Sŏng Ugye:
"From what origin does primordial energy (元氣) begin? / Formless, it exists within the formed. / Pursue the source, know the fundamental unity; / Follow the streams, see the myriad essences. / Water follows the square or round vessel; / Emptiness follows the large or small bottle (空隨大小甁). / Do not be confused by the two paths, my friend; / Silently verify nature becoming emotion (性爲情)." What does the note on "Emptiness follows the large or small bottle" mean, where it says, "The theory of the empty bottle (空甁之說) comes from the Buddhists (釋氏), but because its analogy is pertinent and sharp, it is used"? If Master Yulgok had rejected Buddhism, I believe he absolutely would have avoided such conversations with the king and analogies involving the Buddhist empty bottle theory.<97>

For a negligible later descendant like myself, born hundreds of years later, to try and fathom the mind of the utmost sage and philosopher, the Great Master, might seem excessively presumptuous and arrogant. However, what prevented the Master from becoming a great world sage and instead made him a Confucian sage of this small Eastern land of Chosŏn was that the circumstances of Chosŏn at the time forced our Master Yulgok to sacrifice his great aspirations and great path. I presumptuously assert, as a later person, that the state of mind with which the Master returned after studying the inner canon (內典) in the Diamond Mountains was not in the slightest bit different from the state of mind with which he resolutely entered the Diamond Mountains after studying the Confucian sage classics and worthy transmissions before going there. The reason is that the enlightenment of achieving sagehood in the Confucian Way and achieving Buddhahood in the Buddhist Way are fundamentally one and the same, without duality. However, when it comes to the application of these paths, it is an unavoidable reality that they have mutual strengths and weaknesses. Therefore, sequentially, the Master should perhaps have exchanged the Zen gate of the Diamond Mountains for the Daoist sacred ground (三淸道場) and proceeded to dissect and unify each teaching. But at that time, all of Chosŏn had suffered under King Yŏnsan, followed immediately by the aftermath of the Kimyo Scholars' Purge (己卯士禍) after the Jungjong Restoration. The miserable state of the nation and people was utterly unbearable. Furthermore, Chosŏn was destined to become mere flesh for the fishes due to the flames of war in the not-too-distant future. Although there was a sage like Master T'oegye, this elder exclusively favored scholarship and practiced self-preservation through wise retirement (明哲保身), even earning the nickname "advancing and retreating like a * head" (* looks like 鼠 but contextually difficult - likely means timidly or hesitantly) for not staying in office beyond 20 days. Knowing well that T'oegye would not actively step forward, Master Yulgok decided to descend the mountain for the sake of the suffering masses, having sacrificed his own great aspirations and great path. It was more than natural, the proper course of action, for him to enter the Confucian fold and become its grand master after descending the mountain. Those who followed him, carrying their book bundles along the paths (負笈間道하는 者), were countless; the most famous among them numbered 108, led by Sagye Kim Chang-saeng and Chungbong Cho Hŏn. The popular analogy comparing Master Yulgok's disciples to the 108 beads of a rosary (108염주에 應數) is also quite interesting (滋味있는). What was the news concerning his disciples like Kim Sagye, Cho Chungbong, Ch'up'o Hwang Sin, and Mukchae Yi Kwi? In this way, while nurturing such capable individuals for the nation, regardless of whether he was in or out of office, he authored works such as the Theory of the Heavenly Way and Human Way (天道人道論), the Tongho Dialogues (東湖問答), the Ten Thousand Word Memorial (萬言封事), various letters, memorials, and petitions (書箚疏啓) outlining policies for governing the state and saving the people (經國濟民), the doctrine of Rich Country, Strong Army (富國强兵說), the Essentials of Sagely Learning (聖學輯要), and the Secret Formula for Striking Down Ignorance (擊蒙要訣). He initiated and diligently promoted a movement for sage transformation uniting ruler and people (君民一體에 聖化運動), thereby sparking a great revolution in politics and academia. Let us pick up a few remaining fragments (遺殼) of the revolutionary bombshells he threw into politics and education.

The Master's Doctrine of Rich Country, Strong Army

Master Yulgok was, more than anyone, an advocate of the Rich Country, Strong Army doctrine. Not the Rich Country, Strong Army doctrine of hegemonic militarism (覇道에 軍國主義), but the Rich Country, Strong Army doctrine of kingly way militarism (王道에 軍國主義).<98>
This kingly way militarism first involves cultivating the people's minds (心田) through benevolent governance (仁政), teaching the four cardinal virtues (四維), namely propriety, righteousness, integrity, and shame (禮義廉恥), and the four harmonies (四和), namely filial piety, fraternal duty, loyalty, and trustworthiness (孝悌忠信). After sufficiently promoting what would today be called spiritual cultivation (精神作興), enabling every person to maintain a constant mind (恒心), and as the nation's virtue and righteousness (德義) become manifest, agriculture, industry, and commerce (農工商) are encouraged, animal husbandry (牧畜) is fostered, forests are propagated, fish are raised in rivers and marshes (河澤), and land and water transportation are developed. Then, every person will have a stable occupation (恒業) suited to their skills, leading to a rich country (富國). This corresponds to what Mencius called "constant mind and constant property" (恒心恒産). The Master frequently mentioned here and there that without the development of the mind-field (心田開發), i.e., spiritual cultivation, the development of the national and popular economy could absolutely not be achieved.

Furthermore, while promoting the nation, he repeatedly emphasized the doctrine of national defense (國防主義) at every opportunity. He stated that soldiers (武夫), the pillars (杆城) of the state, must be trained based on actual numbers (실수) to form a national defense force that is true in name and reality (명실상부한 국방군인). However, during King Sŏnjo's era, old and new corrupt practices piled up (架上疊架), and the persecution of the common people's livelihood was like frost added to snow (雪上加霜). Thus, signs of national poverty and territorial reduction (邦貧國削) were extremely pronounced, and the prospect of achieving a rich country and strong army was bleak. As exhaustion (疲廢) worsened daily and withering desolation (萎荒) intensified over time, Master Yulgok was deeply worried about the king and the country (憂君憂國). Therefore, in every memorial (上疏), he pleaded countless times for these harmful practices to be cut away and reformed like clearing tangled hemp with a sharp knife (快刀로 亂麻같이 刈除更張). He also elaborated on identifying and reforming abuses in detail, citing ancient examples to prove present points (引古證令), in the chapter on the art of pacifying the people (安民之術章) in the Tongho Dialogues. Extracting the main points, there were five detrimental practices preventing the Chosŏn populace from becoming prosperous and strong at that time:
1. The abuse of pursuing relatives and neighbors (一族切隣之弊).
2. The abuse of burdensome tribute offerings (進上煩重之弊).
3. The abuse of substitute tribute payments (貢物防納之弊).
4. The abuse of unequal labor service (役事不均之弊).
5. The abuse of extortion by clerks and officials (吏胥誅求之弊).
What was the abuse of pursuing relatives and neighbors? Originally, Chosŏn was a nation based on universal conscription (國民皆兵主義), where no one was exempt from paying the military cloth tax (군포). However, if even one person absconded, to collect the military tax, the authorities would encroach upon the fugitive's entire clan (一族). If the clan was wiped out, they would encroach upon the close neighbors (切隣), resulting in 1,000 households falling into ruin together (連坐相亡) due to one person's flight. How could a rich country and strong army be achieved under such circumstances? Moreover, military forces should be assigned based on the actual number of soldiers available for defense (防上其實數), but instead, they maintained inflated rosters (虛簿로 擁兵) merely to collect the military tax. This abuse of pursuing relatives and neighbors must be eliminated.<99>
The burden of tribute offerings (進上) was beyond description, and concerning tributary goods (貢物), corrupt clerks (아전) and other minor officials (吏胥) would privately prepare goods. They would reject the fine tribute goods offered by the people and force them to present the officials' own goods at a hundred times the price. This corruption must be reformed. The inequality of labor service (役事) led to wasted manpower and overflowing resentment (稱怨이 漲溢); this must be reformed and made fair.
The extortion by clerks and officials (吏胥에 誅求) was like knives and swords cutting the people's bellies and intestines (腹腸 割刀斫劍); how could the people endure? To prohibit this, first, adequate salaries (歲祿) for clerks and stipends (祿俸) for officials must be arranged. Because they lacked stipends, this practice of extortion naturally flourished. Even if Boyi (伯夷) were made a magistrate, he could not manage official life without using state resources. This must first be rectified, and the corrupt practice of extortion strictly forbidden. By eliminating these five great abuses, and with officials and people united in striving for production (生聚), i.e., agriculture, industry, and commerce, and by training soldiers based on actual numbers, a rich country and strong army will naturally come about. In the past, when King Goujian of Yue (越王句踐) was in Kuaiji (會稽) with only 5,000 soldiers, he could be considered extremely small and weak. Yet, after 10 years of nurturing the population (生聚) and 10 years of instruction (教訓), he was able to achieve a rich country and strong army and destroy his great enemy. How much more so for our magnificent nation of ten thousand chariots (堂堂萬乘)! If we strive in nurturing the population and instruction, how could we fail to achieve a rich country and strong army? Currently, the number of Capital Guards (都監捕手) in our capital, Seoul, is 5,772. However, this is insufficient for national defense in case of emergency (一朝有事時). As a minimum prerequisite, we must implement a 10-year plan to train 100,000 elite soldiers (10만精銳에 10년 養兵), organizing them with 10,000 in each of the 8 provinces (合 80,000) and 20,000 in the capital. He repeatedly advocated this (屢屢히 主强하섰읍니다) both in and out of office. This proposal by the great sage, who foresaw a thousand years ahead, failed to materialize due to opposition from ignorant and vulgar factions (野俗한 群輩) as well as figures like Chief State Councilor Sŏae Yu Sŏng-nyong. Master Yulgok always lamented, saying, "If Heaven would grant me just 10 more years, there might be some hope for the nation's governance (庶幾之望)." However, Heaven did not lend those 10 years to our Master Yulgok, revealing the misfortune (無福) of the East (東邦).<100>

**Summary**

This article by Yu Cha-hu, published in 1939, extols Yi Yulgok (1536-1584) as the preeminent sage-scholar of the Yi Dynasty and a "forerunner of the East." It highlights Yulgok's intellectual breadth, encompassing Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism, suggesting he could have founded a universal religion had Chosŏn's political crises not compelled him to focus on practical governance. The core of the article discusses Yulgok's "Rich Country, Strong Army" doctrine, emphasizing moral cultivation (benevolent rule, virtue education) as the foundation for economic prosperity (恒心恒産 - constant mind, constant property) and military strength. Yulgok's specific proposals, such as eliminating five major administrative abuses (corrupt tax collection, forced tribute, unequal labor, official extortion) and raising a 100,000-strong army, are presented as visionary but ultimately unrealized solutions to the problems plaguing late 16th-century Chosŏn, foreshadowing the Imjin War.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its invocation of a national hero and his pragmatic statecraft, particularly the "Rich Country, Strong Army" (富國强兵 - Pukuk kangbyŏng) slogan, which resonated strongly with modernization discourses in both Meiji Japan and early 20th-century Korea, albeit often with different ideological underpinnings (Yulgok's being rooted in "Kingly Way" morality). The author's lament for Yulgok's unrealized potential and Chosŏn's "misfortune" could subtly reflect contemporary feelings about Korea's historical trajectory under Japanese rule. The framing of Yulgok as potentially transcending Confucianism also hints at broader intellectual currents exploring Korea's philosophical heritage beyond the confines imposed by the late Yi Dynasty's orthodoxy.

東方의 先駈者 栗谷, 先生의 富國强兵主義
						柳子厚
李朝 오백년간에 歷代로 상당한 인물이 많이 接踵되였었지만 宣祖大王時代의 直前直後처럼 그렇게 武烈文學에 將臣과 그렇게 純眞雄偉한 聖賢學者들이 배출되기는 과거에 역사상 그 類例가 없었읍니다. 그 중에 聖賢인 학자를 맻분드러보면 靜菴 趙光祖先生을 爲首로 退溪 李滉先生이며 龜峰 宋翼弼先生이며 花潭 徐敬德先生이며 牛溪 成渾先生이며 河西 金鱗厚先生이며 南溟 曺植先生이며 慕齋 金安國先生이며 黃崗 金繼輝先生 그 아들 沙溪 金長生이며 文武兼全에 玉子 李舜臣先生 등이 배출되였었읍니다. 靑出藍而勝於藍에 인물이 많었지만 통터러 말하자면 武에 李舜臣先生 오 文에는 栗谷先生이 最高峰格이었읍니다. 그러나 이 對照도 국가에 功效論으로 하는 말이오 圓悟豁達하신 從天에 聖者로서는 우리 栗谷先生께서 群岳 가운데에 대한 主峰으로 屹立하었었읍니다. 그 유명한 忠武公의 龜船의 創案도 栗谷先生의 발명으로 先生在時에 지금 京城寬勳町 竹洞宮이 원래에 栗谷先生의 宅으로 연못이 있는데 그 연못에서 龜船의 모형선을 많들어서 進水試驗을 여러번 하섰다함은 유명한 傳話입니다. 先生은 實노 先生이 없이 宇宙萬象에 萬殊一理를 生面知之하신 분으로 다만 幼少時代에 先生이라하면 그 어머님 師任堂이 게실 뿐입니다.
上述한 당시에 여러 學者群賢 가운데에 가장 論議가 激盛하든 理氣之說를 말삼함에게서 群賢을 推倒하시고 天生自說을<95> 萬古에 수립하시되 儒敎에 聖賢이신 孔孟 程朱의 說은 勿論이오 佛氏의 空甁說이며 老子의 有生於無說을 互引叅證하신 것을 뵈오면 佛敎와 道敎에도 大悟成聖하서서 儒佛道 3敎에 玄玄妙妙하고 元元正正한 眞消息을 파악하신 最高權威의 大聖人이 아니시면 않입니다. 栗谷先生의 理氣說인 氣發理乘에 論法을 비러 後進이 말삼하여보면 栗谷先生은 氣로는 儒敎-라는 一敎에 限局된 斯文에 宗匠이시라 할 수 있지만 이로는 宇宙人類의 綜合敎를 創案하었을 大聖師이십니다.
다만 栗谷先生 當時 朝鮮이 儒敎斯文에 主國으로서 右文을 崇尙하든 시대이라 經國濟民에 實務功效를 비저내는 그 規矩準繩이 儒敎에 있음으로 斯文宗匠으로 나오신 것입니다. 佛門에 가시면 僧徒가 生佛이시라 하고 道敎에 가시면 群覺이 3淸으로 모섰을 것입니다. 요컨댄 栗谷先生은 宇宙天道大我에 聖人으로 儒敎-니 佛敎-니 道敎-니하는 敎를 초월하신 존재이였섰읍니다.
栗谷先生께서 弱冠時代 以內에 右文經傳를 無所不讀하시고 無所不知하사 成聖하신 분입니다. 무슨 이유로 金剛山摩하 衍에 靜寂한 禪門을 두다려 보었읍니까. 孔孟에 學을 無不通知하신 결과에 당신에 洪大한 聖量에 부족이 있었을망정 裕足치 못함으로 自己에 一身을 孔孟의 학문의 道以上에 擴大延長하야 孔孟以上의 聖化 즉 永生大我化를 목적하신 것이 아니면 아니라 생각되는 바 올시다. 즉 이것이 聖者는 大慾이라는 말에 擴充實行으로서 栗谷先生께서 永生大我에 永生大慾을 마음과 가삼에 품으시고 聖者에 거룩한 발자최를 金剛의 禪門에 옴기여 보신 것이 아니면 않입니다. 그리하야 3寶에 經典을 週年 이동되여 我有化하사 佛道에 通徹하섰읍니다. 나는 여기에 한번 생각을 골돌히 하여 본 일이 있었읍니다. 당시 朝鮮이 燕山朝의 虐政에 시달임이 없고 後來의 兵亂이 없이 安民保國이 될 수 있섰다하면 혹은 栗谷先生께서는 永永儒家로 도라오지 아니하시고 다시 道敎를 더 攝取하사 三合渾一에 大宗敎家로 世界無類의 最高宗敎에 權現尊聖하지 아니하섰을가 하는 바 올시다. 그 이유는 栗先生께서 그러한 사상이 없으섰다하면 當初에 金剛禪門에 가었을 리가 없고 旣往 禪門에 生佛로 追仰을 받는 분이 다시 下山하였을 리가 萬無함니다. 栗谷先生은 과거에 우리의 희생자이었읍니다. 將ㅅ찻 塗炭에 빠질 億兆蒼生을 구하시랴고 下山하신 것이 분명함니다. 聖人學者-오 政治家시라는 일홈이 이로 생기게 된 것입니다.
先生께서 下山하신 후에 妖僧普雨와 業淨院을 반대하신 일은 있으나 寺院撤廢論이나 賢僧排斥論을 주장하신 일은 없으섰읍니다.<96> 만약 佛道를 撲滅하시랴 하섰다하면 全國佛寺撤毁을 주장하사 全國에 佛敎를 永滅케하랴 하섰을 것입니다.
聖人이 能知聖人이라는 말과 같이 大聖人 栗谷先生은 孔子에 聖만 하신 것이 아니라 釋迦에 聖도 잘 아시는 분이였었읍니다. 聖人釋迦를 排斥하신 일은 없었읍니다. 도리혀 佛에서 좋은 것은 많이 採取하섰읍니다. 그 증거로는 宣祖大王을 모시고 말삼하실때에 恒常 四子에 佳句를 多用하섰읍니다. 宣祖께서 드르시고 卿은 읏지그리 4字佳句를 善用하시오 하시니 先生께서는 조금도 躕躇치 아니하시고 小臣이 昔在山中에 多讀楞嚴하온 까닭입니다 하사(楞嚴經은 解拓魔王에 文) 君臣間에 一話를 大遺傳하였으니 先生이 聖人으로 立朝하사 人君 앞에서 佛經多讀을 말삼하섰으니 이 말삼이 정당한 佛氏를 배척하신 말삼입니까. 도리혀 佛를 友하신 말삼이 아니면 아니오. 楓岳에 노실 때에 어떠한 小菴에서 老僧를 맛나 酬酌하신 후에 老僧에 請氏空甁을 儒學理氣之說에 당당히 引喩하었읍니다. 成牛溪에게 보내는 글에
人之理 卽 物之理者는 理之通也-니 方圓之器不同而 器中之水는 一也-오 大小之甁이 不同而 甁中之空은 一也云云하시고 또 理氣吟詩를 成牛溪에게 보내시니
元氣何端始
無形在有形
窮源知本合
沿派見群精
水遂方圓器
空隨大小甁

黙驗性爲情 하라하신 이 詩에 空隨大小甁의 註에 空甁之說이 出於釋氏而 其譬喩覩切故用之-라 한 것이 무었입니까. 만약 栗谷先生께서 佛氏를 배척하섰다하면 이러한 君臣遣話와 佛氏空甁說에 引喩는 절대로 回避하었을 줄노 아는 바입니다.<97>
