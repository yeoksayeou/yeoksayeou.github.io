Record of Visiting Novelists (2), Mr. Yi T'aejun Speaks of Ideals
						One Reporter

Writer and Life

The visits to novelists continue for the second time. For the second installment, we decided to seek out the writer Mr. Yi T'aejun, who receives our affection.
Hearing rumors that Mr. Yi was busy with publishing preparations after newly setting up the "Munjangsa" (文章社), I visited his office around six o'clock in the evening, guessing it would be a time when he might have ample leisure. However, Mr. Yi was not at all leisured – despite being someone whose character is formed by maintaining a quiet demeanor – he was extremely busy, unable to sit still in his chair for long due to phone calls and receiving guests. Nevertheless, I couldn't just abandon the purpose of my visit.
"I apologize for interrupting while you're busy, but please take this time to speak for me."
"Please do. I'm always busy anyway."
"I came so late trying to catch a quiet moment, but you're still busy." <222>
"It's because I usually handle Munjangsa affairs around this time. I drop by on my way back from school. Today was a day I didn't go to school, so I tried to write a bit since morning, but all day long, although I had the manuscript paper spread out, I only managed to write three lines."
"Is it a novel?"
"Yes. I've already started a short story, but I've just been carrying it in my bag every day and still haven't managed to write it. Everyone is in this situation, so can we really call it a literary profession? We should all quit other jobs quickly and just write."
"But you have to be able to make a living just by writing, right?"
"That's what I mean. If manuscript fees came pouring in so that one could live just by writing, there would be no need to take other jobs. Before many good friends who will invest capital for literature appear, it's disheartening to think that we'll just continue in this state, unable to write proper literature and only being busy."
"What is the minimum amount needed to make a living?"
"Even if they just gave 1 won per sheet of 200-character manuscript paper (原稿紙), I don't think people would bother trying to get other jobs. And if magazine or publishing companies could provide even a fixed monthly designated manuscript fee (指定稿料) of just 20 or 30 won, it seems one could somehow get by."
"Still, it seems like someone like you, Sir, could make a living from manuscript fees."
"Not at all. Writing a newspaper novel (新聞小說) pays relatively well, but can one always write those? It's only when one's turn happens to come around."

Long-form and Short-form Stories

"Still, haven't you written quite a few long-form novels, Sir?"
"I think it's about 7 or 8. *Kuwŏn ŭi Yŏsang* (久遠의 女像), *Pŏbŭn kŭrŏch'iman* (法은 그렇치만), *K'osŭmosŭ p'inŭn Chŏngwŏn* (코스모스 피는 庭園), *Chei ŭi Unmyŏng* (第二의 運命), *Pulmyŏrŭi Hamsŏng* (不滅의 喊聲), *Sŏngmo* (聖母), *Hwang Chini* (黃眞伊), *Hwagwan* (花冠). Among them, *K'osŭmosŭ p'inŭn Chŏngwŏn* was serialized in a magazine, so it might not really count as a long-form novel, but let's just keep it as one."
"Which of these works are you most confident about?"
"Well. Since I'm not yet a master, I don't have a work I'm confident about, and that question is like asking which child in the house is the best, so it's difficult to answer."
"So you mean they are all good?"
"No. The long-form novels we've written so far have been newspaper serials. Because these have to be written one installment per day and sent to the newspaper company, rather than focusing on writing a good novel, when rushed, there are many times when one just somehow cobbles together that day's portion and sends it off. Therefore, there are plenty of parts I lack confidence in, so how can I answer which one is good and which one is bad?<223> If it turns out poorly, it's by chance; if it turns out well, it's by chance – one just writes as it happens."
"Then, among your short stories, you must have many you are confident about?"
"Yes. It's not quite confidence, but unlike long-form novels, whether it turns out well or poorly, once I finish writing it, I feel like I've made something – in other words, I experience the joy of creation."
"If that's the case, shouldn't long-form novels perhaps not be written at all?"
"Why? That's not necessarily true either. If one could write a long-form novel as one pleases, finish it all, and then have it published in a newspaper or magazine, wouldn't the joy of accomplishing a great task (大事) be even greater? In any case, writing one installment each day without finishing the entire work first – that's not a complete creative attitude (創作態度). It's, so to speak, literary labor (文筆勞働). That's why, for the first 30 or 40 installments, the writer can manipulate the characters (作中人物) as they please, but after that, the writer ends up following the characters."
"Will you write long-form or short-form stories in the future?"
"I don't have any particular plans, but if time permits, I have the thought of trying to create something rather large-scale."

What Subjects to Choose

"What kind of novel do you want to write?"
"Writing novels right now is truly awkward. Unless it's a detective novel (探偵小說) or a historical novel (歷史小說), it's impossible to write. Our lives are too two-dimensional (平面). Even someone like Mr. Kawabata Yasunari (川端康成氏) said that life in mainland Japan is too non-three-dimensional (非立體的) for constructing a novel, and we have even less possibility of finding active characters (行動的)... But we can't feature characters we don't find ideal – people involved in gold mining (金鑛) or stock speculation (株式) or other speculative businesses (投機業). So, in the end, there's nothing but historical novels to write..."

Regarding the Novel *Hwang Chini*

"Is that why you wrote *Hwang Chini* (黃眞伊)?"
"That wasn't the reason I wrote it. When I left the Joongang Ilbo (中央日報) to become a guest contributor (客員), the chief editor (主筆), Mr. Yi Kwangu (李寬求氏), really liked Hwang Chini and asked me to write about her for the Joongang paper, so I did."
"Were you planning to write it beforehand?"
"Not really. The request came right after I became a guest contributor, so I had no prior preparation. I researched here and there while writing, but Hwang Chini's history wasn't clearly recorded anywhere, which was quite difficult."
"Where did you generally find references?"<224>
"I referred to some genre paintings (風俗畵) by Sin Yunbok (申潤福) at the Office of the Yi Household (李王職) and also heard things from Mr. O Sech'ang (吳世昌氏). And although I went down to Kaesŏng (開城) and saw Sŏ Hwadam's (徐花潭) Sŏsajŏng Pavilion (逝斯亭), *Hwang Chini* still had many forced parts towards the end. One-third was published in the newspaper, but then the newspaper ceased publication, so I stopped writing. Later, a publishing house said they would publish it, so I had to force the ending, which caused many issues."
"Forced parts, you say? Do you mean you fabricated events completely detached from history?"
"It's different from that. Rather, I think it's not good for a historical novel to be bound by its historical documents (文獻). As some historian in mainland Japan said, historians cannot live apart from records, but one cannot understand why creative artists insist on being captivated only by those documents... Seeing words like these, I believe that even a historical novel doesn't necessarily have to follow history strictly. If one writes exactly according to history, it's a biography (傳記), not a novel. For example, if someone writes about Yi Sunsin (李舜臣) in a way completely different from Yi Sunsin's history, and critics rise up against it, they can only be called critics who don't understand literature. Perhaps among the so-called intellectual class (知識階級) elements (分子), there seem to be those who say a historical novel is completely different from history, but I view that as a very mistaken idea. For instance, with Hwang Chini, regardless of how Hwang Chini's history actually went, isn't it enough if the author's perspective from a certain angle is accurately expressed? Even if there is an author who writes historical novels exactly according to history, it's absolutely not history exactly as it was. Currently, Hong Pyŏkch'o's (洪碧初) *Im Kkŏkjŏng* (林巨正) is said to use old customs and old language exactly as they were, but that's the customs and language from 50 years ago, that is, the customs and language Pyŏkch'o saw, not from further back. Things from several hundred or several thousand years ago cannot be known even if documents exist. It's impossible to figure out the language and customs. Unless it's a sound film (發聲映畵), how could one depict the era accurately for something like *Im Kkŏkjŏng*, which is from 400 years ago? Therefore, even when I write a historical novel, I just bring the characters to life and handle the events from the angle I see them, rather than trying to needlessly apply makeup (化粧)."

Greatest Ideal

"Please tell us your ideals, Sir."
"My ideal? It's just to be able to sit down in a nice study (書齋) and write, without being busy."
"Do you have any other thoughts besides that?"
"Of course, I do. There are tons of things I want to do, but among them, the thing I want most is what I just mentioned."
"What is your next wish?"<225>
"What are you trying to hear by asking like that? Are you trying to hear me say I want to fall in love (戀愛)? Haha."
"No. I just thought you might have many other ideals."
"Actually, since the topic of love came up, let me say this: we have thought of love too vulgarly. Many people equate the affairs seen in newspaper gossip columns (三面記事) with love (戀愛). Perhaps there are people who would curse me for discussing the issue of love like this now. But truly, I think there is nothing in the world as beautiful as love. The heart in love is a heart close to God (하느님). People in love do not need God. That's how much they are elevated, deepened, and beautified through love. If someone can engage in love that allows such elevation, deepening, and beautification, they can certainly devote their body for their country or for humanity (人類). One cannot love properly unless one is a complete (完全) human being. Should we treat with contempt this thing that is more beautiful and precious than anything else in the world? If one dedicates that passion to business or art because of love, what wonderful results that business or art would show! Western writers often wrote their works thinking of one particular person. In my view, their works are greater because of that. I wish we too could reach the point where we write with the feeling of 'dedicating' it to someone."
"Do you believe marriage and love should be separated, Sir?"
"I don't think so. If you love, you marry."/
"Then you rub out the saying that marriage (結婚) is the tomb of love."
"The saying that marriage is the tomb of love also has a point. If it's a union of undeveloped emotions, that can happen. In other words, if people love blindly and then marry, after marriage all sorts of faults become visible to each other (彼此), leading to boredom (倦怠). But if it's between fully matured (成熟) emotions, there's no way that could happen. What can be done about breakups (破綻) resulting from the union of undeveloped emotions? Even forcing them to be tied together won't help. In my opinion, I don't want to bind unions of emotions that don't match – that is, unions of non-matured emotions – with law (法律) and morality (道德). That might be a very crucial ethic (倫理) for rulers (爲政者), perhaps."
"I understand. Now, please tell us your view on life (人生觀)."
"My view on life? It's very vague."
"First, do you want to die or live?"
"I'm an optimist (樂觀主義), so I don't want to die; I want to live well. One's view on life also keeps developing (發育) as one grows, so even if I stated my view on life now, it wouldn't be a complete view on life. If you want to know my view on life, please read all (全部) the novels I write from now on."<226>

Writers Admired

"Whose novels do you like the most?"
"I like Chekhov, and I also like Dostoevsky."
"Aren't Chekhov and Dostoevsky writers with very different tendencies?"
"That's right. But both writers are good. Chekhov is skilled at description, and Dostoevsky presents stories with plot."

Reading

"What books are you reading these days?"
"I can't read much because I'm busy, but I'm reading Mr. Kawabata Yasunari's *Yukiguni* (雪國), and it's quite interesting."

Entertainment and Hobbies

"Do you watch movies (映畵) often?"
"I go often. Because it's educational. Other people think of pictures as entertainment (娛樂), but I always connect literature and movies when I watch."
"What is your entertainment?"
"I don't really have much entertainment. I don't play Janggi (將棋) or Baduk, I can't play Mahjong (마짱), maybe reading books is my entertainment? Still, I do want to live life brilliantly."
"Sir, I heard you like antiques?"
"Yes, I like them very much. Wherever there are good antique calligraphy and paintings, I want to go see them all."
"Didn't you previously take such items to Tokyo and hold an exhibition?"
"I did before, but I've stopped now."

Regarding Emerging Writers

"You've told us a lot about yourself, Sir, so now please talk about emerging writers (新進作家). Whom do you have high hopes for (囑望) among the emerging writers?"
"I think someone named Mr. Hyŏn Tŏk (玄德氏) is very talented. *Namsaengi* (남생이) and the works that came out after that all show signs of intense effort. Although he's a newly debuted writer, looking at his prose (文裝), you can tell he prepared a lot beforehand."
"Is there anyone else?"
"Mr. Kim Tongni (金東里氏), I see this person as promising too. I heard he is currently at some temple; not only does he study, but he has capability. As for someone like Mr. Chŏng Pisŏk (鄭飛石氏), his first work *Sŏnghwangdang* (城隍堂) was good, but looking at his subsequent works, I don't think he's that talented." -End-<227>

**Summary**

This 1939 interview features Korean writer Yi T'aejun discussing his work and life. He laments the financial difficulties forcing writers into other jobs and compromising literary quality, especially for serialized newspaper novels compared to short stories. He argues historical novels shouldn't be rigidly bound by historical fact but should reflect the author's perspective. Yi expresses frustration with the perceived "flatness" of contemporary Korean life as novelistic material, pushing writers towards historical or detective genres. He shares his admiration for Chekhov and Dostoevsky, his interest in film and antiques, and his views on emerging writers like Hyŏn Tŏk and Kim Tongni. Yi also speaks passionately about the ideal of pure, elevating love, contrasting it with societal trivialization and advocating for marriages based on mature emotional connection.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the challenging environment for Korean intellectuals and artists during the late colonial period. Yi's comments on the economic precarity of writers, the perceived limitations on subject matter forcing a turn to historical fiction (potentially as a safer, less politically charged genre), and the mention of Japanese cultural figures like Kawabata highlight the complex cultural and socio-political landscape. His idealistic discourse on love could also be interpreted as a yearning for authenticity and higher purpose in a constrained reality.

長篇作家 訪問記(2), 理想을 語하는 李泰俊氏
						一記者
作家와 生活

長篇作家 訪問이 두 번채 게속됩니다. 第2回로 이번엔 우리의 친애를 받는 작가 李泰俊氏를 찾기로 했읍니다.
氏가 「文章社」를 새로 꾸미고 출판준비에 奔忙하시다는 소문을 드른 나는 氏가 한껏 閑暇할 듯한 때를 살펴서 오후 여섯시 갓가히 氏의 사무실을 찾었읍니다마는 氏는 전혀 한가롭지 못하고 - 조용한 자세를 가추어야 格을 이루는 氏임에도 불구하고 - 의자에 오래 안정할 수 없이 전화에 接客에 몹시 바뻐하섰읍니다. 그러하나 찾은 뜻을 버려둘 수는 없지 않습니까.
「바쁘신데 미안하지만 이 시간은 저를 위해서 말슴해 주십시요」
「그렇게 하십시요. 늘 바쁘니까요」
「조용한 틈을 타느라고 이렇게 늦게 왔는데 그저 바쁘시군요.」 <222>
「대개 「文章社」 일은 요때에 보게되서 그래요. 학교에서 돌아오든 길에 들니게 되니까요. 오늘은 학교에 않나가는 날이 돼서 아츰부터 좀 써보려고 했는데 하로 종일 원고질 펴놓기만 했지 석줄 밖에 못 썼군요」
「소설입니까?」
