Magazine Title	삼천리 제11권 제1호
Publication Date	1939-01-01
Article Title	『戰爭文學』과 『朝鮮作家』 -戰爭과 文學과 그 作品을 말하는 座談會- ("War Literature" and "Korean Writers" - A Roundtable Discussion on War, Literature, and its Works)
Author	朴英熙, 金基鎭, 本社 金東煥 (Pak Yŏnghŭi, Kim Kijin, Company Representative Kim Tonghwan)
Article Type	대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

『War Literature』 and 『Korean Writers』 - A Roundtable Discussion on War, Literature, and its Works
						Pak Yŏnghŭi, Kim Kijin, Company Representative Kim Tonghwan

I. What is the War Literature of Mainland Japan Like?

Kim Tonghwan - Currently, the nation is at war. Also, looking at the trends in literary thought immediately following the World War, war literature customarily becomes the mainstream before and after the progression of a war. Therefore, I believe that war literature will become the center of production in our literary society (not just ours, but probably throughout all of Asia, including mainland Japan, China, and Manchukuo) from now on. Thus, as we greet this New Year, the third year of the war, let us deeply reflect on "War Literature and<206> Korean Writers." Now, when discussing war literature in Asia, "Korean War Literature" presents some awkward points, so let's exclude it from the topic. Also, Manchukuo was founded recently and hasn't even prepared the fertile ground (培土) for literature to emerge, so that too is beyond discussion. Furthermore, regarding Chinese literature, although historically there has been war literature famous for quantity and numerous in quality, such as the "Ballad of the War Carts" (兵車行), and even recently, some level of production exists through writers like Guo Moruo or works like "A Thousand Li Rout" (敗走千里), for various reasons, we shall also omit that. So, let's limit the scope of our discussion here today to "Japanese Literature."
Now, what kind of war literature, writers, and works would you say existed in the mainland in recent or modern times?

P'albong Kim Kijin - War literature in Japan mostly consisted of works produced during wartime or those created influenced by the dust of war (戰塵) afterwards. The wars Japan faced were the Russo-Japanese War of Meiji 37-38 (1904-1905), then the Manchurian Incident, followed by the Shanghai Incident, and now this China Incident. During the Russo-Japanese War, there were the famous "Human Bullets" (肉彈) by Sakurai Tadatsugu and "This One Battle" (此一戰) by Mizuno Hironori. During the Shanghai Incident, there was Naoki Sanjūgo's "Japan's Shudder" (日本의 戰慄). And with the current Incident, war works are about to pour out like a flood. Among those already emerged, the notable ones are Hino Ashihei's two-part work "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥과 兵隊) and "Earth and Soldiers" (土와 兵隊), and Ueda Hiroshi's "Yellow Dust" (黃塵).
Also, dealing with another side of war, from the Russo-Japanese War, we can mention two works handled from the Russian perspective by the Russian writer Novikov-Priboy: "Tsushima" (對馬) and "The Battle" (戰鬪). "Tsushima" and "The Battle" were serialized in *Kaizō* (改造) a few years ago and caused a great sensation in the mainland reading circles.

Kim Tonghwan - Perhaps among foreign war literature, there were works with anti-war tendencies, but limited to mainland writers, almost all war literature, from the standpoint of patriotic literature, praised the sublimity of war and the patriotic passion of brave soldiers who discard their lives and gladly die for the nation's mission. In sum, most probably took up the pen from the viewpoint that "war is the mother of cultural creation."

Pak Yŏnghŭi - That's right. They are works I read some time ago, so my memory is somewhat faint,<207> but even looking at the various works P'albong just mentioned, both Sakurai and Mizuno went to the front lines themselves, and through long struggles, they conveyed the patriotism gained through conviction to the entire nation via the form of art. In other words, they were works of war-beautifying literature written to perfectly align with the national objectives.
These two works first laid the cornerstone (礎石) of war literature in Japanese literary history. After that, there was Naoki Sanjūgo's "Japan's Shudder," but that was less war literature and more a contrived didactic novel (目的小說) aimed at redirecting the Proletarian (푸로레타리아的) literary trend, which was surging (澎湃한) in Japanese society at the time, towards nationalism; it merely borrowed one or two scenes from the Shanghai battlefield.

Kim Tonghwan - Have you recently seen "Wheat and Soldiers" or "Earth and Soldiers" published in *Kaizō*?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - I've looked through them roughly. Hino's attitude is not what we commonly call contemplative (思索的), but can be called practical literature. It's not writing about having fought, but writing while fighting. That vivid emotion directly brings something to the hearts (胸底) of the people. Recently, I hear critics calling such literature "documentary literature" (記錄的 文學). In other words, it would be reportage literature (報告文學).
However, war, specifically our country's (我國) current war, is fundamentally different in significance from the European Great War (WWI). Because it is a Holy War (聖戰) for the eternal peace of the Orient, its war consciousness should not be treated as a temporary life phenomenon. Rather, an ideology embodying the philosophized Japanese spirit (日本精神) accompanying long-term construction (長期建設) should be contained in our current war literature.
People in the past used war as material (素材) to construct a work or depicted it with a critical attitude (批判的 態度), but we, with a firm (確固한) ideal as an important element in this exploration of humanity (人間探求), can take up the pen as a single soldier. In this sense, "Earth and Soldiers" is a noteworthy and problematic work.

Kim Kijin - That's right. Generally classifying war literature, first comes the period when the fighting (싸홈이) begins, and both frontline soldiers and the home front populace (銃後國民) are all excited, praising war (驅歌하든) with simple emotion and passion without deep reflection. Second is the reportage literature (레포-타文學) period during the war's progression, focusing on describing and recording the activities on the battlefield and the calm, brave (沈勇한) actions of soldiers. And third, the stage after the war ends,<208> when all excitement and sentimental or romantic passion have faded, i.e., when calm reason is regained, writing with a quiet contemplative and introspective (內省的) attitude towards the war. Looking at Hino's two-part work now, it belongs to the first and second periods, starting with depictions of fierce battle scenes and consistently portraying soldiers' actions. In a time of ongoing war like now, this will gain the support (支持) of the reading public (讀書層). I also looked through it roughly, and I was surprised (놀났어요) by the richness of his imagination.
I heard (드른) an episode (에피소-드) about Hino. He graduated from Waseda University's Faculty of Letters (早大文科) and was an energetic person (精力家) who wrote for a coterie magazine (同人雜誌) every issue (每號) during his student days. One day (하로는), at an editorial meeting (編輯會) among the magazine members, after various talks, Hino confessed an experience. He said he once met an old man on a train during a trip to Shimonoseki (下關). The old man was carrying a small, square box (괴짝(箱子)) wrapped in a cloth (보). Whatever he kept inside, the old man guarded the box very preciously; he took it with him when going to the toilet (便所) and took it when going out to buy a bento (벤또). Hino became curious about what this old geezer had put in there to act like that. Then, he had to get off midway. When Hino got off at Nagoya (名古屋) and entered an inn, the old man followed, saying it's nice to have company and he'd stay the night too, and entered the same inn. At the inn that evening, Hino bathed (沐浴) first. When he came up (올나와서) and told the old man to go bathe, the old man hesitated greatly, then reluctantly (앗가운 듯) placed the box to one side and went to the bathhouse. Finding it so strange, Hino quickly (얼는) unwrapped the box during that time, and don't be surprised (놀나지)! Inside, wrapped in oil paper (油紙), was the corpse of a young child (어린아히). Terrified, he quickly wrapped it up as before (앗가 모양으로) and pretended not to know (모르는체). Just as he sat there, the old man came up hurriedly (황겁이) without even finishing his bath, slid open the door (미다지를), and upon entering (드러서자) and seeing Hino's face (얼골을), shouted, "You rascal (년석), you opened it! Now that you know this secret, I'll take your life!" and reportedly shot (쏘드래요) with a six-shooter (6穴砲). Startled, Hino jumped down (뛰여나려) the stairs (충충대를), missed his footing (빗구로 드듸어), fell down (잡버저), and woke up (깨어나니) to find it was all a dream. His colleagues (同僚들), who had been deceived into believing (속아드른) it was real until then, were angry but also amazed at his rich imagination. It seems it wasn't a dream but a fictional story he made up<209>. Imagination just as surprising is employed (驅使되여) in "Wheat and Soldiers." He's a naturally talented (天稟) war writer. He'll develop further. He'll probably be the one to lead (있글고) future war literature in Japan. The feeling of not fearing being conscripted and going to the battlefield under the national flag, but knowing it gladly (깁부게) - the literature that arises from there, in short (요컨대), will likely be the unified path forward for Hino and the current Japanese writers.

Kim Tonghwan - Couldn't we summarize it like this? That is - in a victorious nation (戰勝國家), the war literature that arises naturally becomes war-beautifying literature because the gains (었는 것) from the war are greater than the losses, filling the people's will with gratitude towards the war. Conversely, in a defeated nation (戰敗國), anti-war (X戰) literature arises. Therefore, as long as this atmosphere (雰圍氣) continues in mainland Japan, the spirit of war praise will be reflected (反映되어) in literary trends - consequently, writers and readers will live under this influence for some period to come.

Pak Yŏnghŭi - Can we see it that way? That might be one perspective. However, looking at the literary trends after the European Great War (WWI), it doesn't necessarily seem so. Literature during the Great War largely leaned towards criticizing the past based on post-war social thought or views on life. But the literature we are discussing now is literature emerging during the war. Therefore, I don't think it pursues only utilitarianly (功利的) advantageous results, as Mr. Kim Kijin (巴人兄 - P'ain hyŏng: Note - 巴人 Pa'in/P'ain was a pen name for Kim Kijin) suggests.
The thoughts, feelings, hopes, and lives that soldiers possess during the war appear directly in the literature. Thus, in this kind of on-site reportage literature (現地報告文學), I believe there is much for us to study as well. Also, speaking of praising war (戰爭을 謳歌云云), I think this expression is misleading (語弊). A fight for justice is fought until justice prevails. In such a war, whether one wins or loses, I believe the consciousness remains entirely unchanged (異變이 없을 줄노).

II. What are the Literary Works and Trends After the Great War?

Kim Tonghwan - Then, let's now talk about the major works and<210> writers after the European Great War (WWI). Probably the work that caused the biggest global sensation was Remarque's "All Quiet on the Western Front" (西部戰線 異狀없다), wasn't it?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - We also read it 7 or 8 years ago, and in Tokyo, starting with its performance at the Shintsukiji Theater (新築地劇場), it caused a great whirlwind among literary, theatrical, and film circles nationwide.

Kim Tonghwan - What was Remarque's level of education (敎養程度)?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - According to his biography, during the World War, he was just a rosy-cheeked youth (紅顔少年) of only nineteen, attending school when he threw away his books (책보를 집어뿌리고) and ran off (뛰여나갔대요) to the battlefield. Looking at his entire work, its special characteristic (特長갓더군요) seems to be more emotional than intellectual.
Isn't there a passage like this?
"Why does war happen?" asks a dirt-covered (흙투성이) soldier in a trench (斬壕). Another soldier, pretending to know, replies,
"Well, it happens when one country severely insults (侮辱한) another country, what else!"
"What? One country - that doesn't make sense. Which mountain (山) in Germany insulted the mountains and rivers (山川) of France? It doesn't have to be a mountain; maybe a forest (숲울) or a barley field..."
"You idiot (천치야). It means when one people insults another people."
"That makes even less sense. Then we have nothing to do here. I don't recall ever being insulted by any Frenchman."
At that, the soldier, perhaps getting very angry, punches him once and says,
"Do you think a country bumpkin (시골뚜기가) like you matters (문제된다드냐)?"
"Then I guess I can go home (도라가도)?"
He insists stubbornly, making several people (여렀은) laugh (웃섰다), ha ha ha.
Like this passage, he frankly explained and depicted war in a way anyone could easily understand.

Kim Tonghwan - How does he view the state?

Kim Kijin - In that work, he doesn't depict the state but primarily aims to portray humanity (人間性) within the battlefield scenes. He seems to focus (力點) on vividly depicting (刻明하게) human life situated amidst scattered limbs (팔악신이), etc., scattered on the battlefield.

Kim Tonghwan - And yet it's called anti-war (X戰) literature? <211>

Pak Yŏnghŭi - Well, in conclusion, despite the many calamities (慘禍), it covers them all up (덮어버리고) and simply reports (보고하였으니까), "All Quiet on the Western Front"...

Kim Tonghwan - P'albong, you brought Henri Barbusse's Clarté movement (쿠라루테운동) to Tokyo and stirred up a trend (바름을 이르킨) in the literary circles here, didn't you? What about that?

Kim Kijin - Yes, that happened. Barbusse was a French writer, and his representative work was "Under Fire" (砲火). More influential on the Zeitgeist (時代思潮) of that time than Barbusse was Romain Rolland, who wrote "Jean-Christophe" (先驅者 - Pioneers) and "Above the Battle" (戰爭의 우에서(戰ひの上にあれ)). Besides them, many writers advocating Cosmopolitanism (世界主義) and Internationalism (國際主義) emerged in places like Germany, France, and Poland.

Kim Tonghwan - What about Ibáñez?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - His known works are "The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse" (黙示錄의 四騎士) and "The Shadow of the Cathedral" (僧院의 그늘). More than that, the Expressionist movement (表現主義運動) that appeared in the German literary scene after the Great War - that swept the world (風靡하였지요) for a while. Expressionism is an activism (積極主義) that says instead of seeing all the suffering (괴롬을) arising from war as unavoidable fate, let's conquer this fate through action and realize a new world. Georg Kaiser's "The Burghers of Calais" (카이자의 카레-市民) is probably its representative work. The synopsis (梗槪) of the play is:
"The French army is defeated (敗하여) by the English army, and the citizens of Calais are surrounded (포위되었다) by the English. At this time, an envoy (使者) comes from the English army and says that if the city of Calais sends six citizens as sacrifices (犧牲者) to the English army tomorrow morning (明朝), the entire city will be rescued (救助하여), but otherwise, the entire city will be destroyed (破毁하여). Thereupon, the city council (市會) is immediately convened (소집한 즉). At the meeting (席上에서), a French officer bravely insists on dying defending the castle (城을 벼개삼고 戰死할 것을). At that moment, council member Eustache (에스타-슈), as a representative of humanism, recommends (勸告한다) accepting the English conditions. Thus, he says he will be the very first sacrifice to save (救濟키) the city. Other council members are also moved (움지기여) by his noble (高潔한) intention, and seven people declare they want to be sacrifices. However, the English only demand six people. One person is left over. Thereupon, Eustache proposes (提議하였다) that when they all meet (맛날) in the temple square (寺院廣場) the next morning (翌朝), the last one to arrive will be rejected (물니치기로). The next day (이튼날), early (일즉이) in the morning (아츰), they gather (모아들어) one by one, two by two. All have arrived, but Eustache alone finally (끗끗내) does not show up (모양을 보이지 안는다). So, as the others begin to doubt the intentions (心事를)<212> of the one who first made such a proposal, at that moment his corpse (屍體가) is carried in (운반되어 온다). Apparently, he came not as the last one, but committed suicide as the first sacrifice. Thus, the remaining six people, encouraged (激勵를 바더서) by his spirit of sacrifice, calmly (從容히) set out on the path of sacrifice. At this moment, an English envoy suddenly (突如히) appears (낱아나서) and announces (告하며) that because the Crown Prince (太子가) was born (誕生하였기에) in the camp last night (昨夜), the English King, to express congratulations (祝意), specially pardons (特赦한다고) the six sacrifices, and then leaves. And so, when the English King comes to this temple to offer (드릴) prayers (祈禱) of thanks, they intend to place (安置하려한다) Eustache's coffin (棺) high on the altar (祭壇). That is, they intend to make the victor kneel (꿀안게 하려하는 것이다) before him."
These are the kinds of works. It is truly a representative work showing the greatness of the human spirit.

Kim Tonghwan - Then what about works like Tolstoy's "War and Peace" (戰爭과 平和) from before the Great War? And in "Resurrection" (復活), isn't there a scene where Nekhlyudov (네류토프) departs for war? Works like "The Cossacks" (코삭크), Tolstoy's works have many battlefield scenes, don't they?

Kim Kijin - But Tolstoy's attitude is different (달느지요). He always wrote "Sevastopol Sketches" (쎄빠스트볼-) or "The Cossacks" from the standpoint of a humanitarian (人道主義者). He readily (즐겨) dealt with war scenes because he went to war (출정하여서) multiple times as an officer (士官으로서), perhaps during the Russo-Turkish War (露土戰爭), but we cannot acknowledge any pro-war stance (積極性) regarding war itself.

Kim Tonghwan - It's the turn to talk about the rise (發興) of Proletarian literature (푸로文學) after the Great War, but let's omit (省略키로) that.

What Should Writers Do During Wartime?

Kim Tonghwan - What actions did writers take during the World War?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - As for the situation in 1914, the first year of the war, many writers in Germany and France rose up (蹶起하여) and wrote poems and novels praising (驅歌하는) the war spirit. Isn't it recorded that in Germany alone, 1.5 million poems (首) were written in the first year? An even bigger event, most active in Germany, was when 93 famous scholars, thinkers, poets, and novelists like Hauptmann (합트맨) and Eucken (오이켄) issued a declaration signed jointly (連名自著한 선언), "Why Must Our Country Stand Up and Fight?", greatly defending (변호하지 않었어요) their own country's (自國) war spirit. Besides that, there were many debates (論爭)<213> between writers. Like the debate between Hauptmann and Romain Rolland...

Kim Kijin - The proposition (命題야) of the debates at that time could be summed up in one phrase: a fight between "Love humanity and civilization" and "Love the nation." The "Declaration of 93 German Intellectuals" (93人의 獨逸知識階級의 宣言) that Pak Yŏnghŭi just mentioned represented the latter (後者).

Kim Tonghwan - During this Sino-Japanese Incident, numerous (多數한) writers from Tokyo went out to the vicinity of the Yellow River (黃河) and Yangtze River (揚子江) and were active as war correspondent writers (從軍作家), weren't they?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - And the writers who returned (도라온) are taking the lead (압장서서), rallying (紏合하여) critics, university professors, and other intellectuals to actively engage in cultural work (文化工作). Some are traveling around (도라다니고) Korea and Manchuria, claiming to build Continental literature (大陸文學).

Kim Kijin - Looking domestically (國內的) now, this phenomenon can be pointed out: all the complicatedly diverse (複雜多端하게) ideological confrontations (對立) that were simmering (들끌튼) until before the war seem to have been resolved (解消되고) or suspended (休止되고서), and an atmosphere (空氣) of wanting to cooperate in unity (一致協力하자는) for the achievement (達成) of the nation's war objectives seems to dominate the minds (머리를) of the entire intellectual class (知性階級).

Pak Yŏnghŭi - That's right. During the European Great War too, there was a trend where, in times when the nation's fate (흥망) was at stake (달닌), people thought there was no need to quibble over minor differences (小異) and halted all political and ideological confrontations to focus solely on the external (對外) war. Now is exactly such a time when people are showing enthusiasm (熱意) to protect and support (擁護하려) the homeland through national unity.

Kim Tonghwan - In the end, won't Korean writers also merge (合流되어지지 않을가요 -> 合流되지 않을까요) into the current (潮流) as the various literatures of Asia move centered around war literature? In the future...

Kim Kijin - Among Korean writers, there are still no writers who have personally carried a gun (총메고) and gone to the battlefield or served as war correspondents. So, can they truly depict (그리여낼 수 있을가요) war literature in its real sense?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - Also, literature doesn't necessarily have to be written right when gunfire (銃火) is being exchanged (相交하는) nearby. It's common for the material to mature and ferment (묵키어 醱酵하여) over several years before becoming a work. Remarque's "All Quiet on the Western Front" was written quite some time after the Great War, and even hearing Hino's confession, he said he wanted to try writing a real work several years later.

Kim Tonghwan - That must be so. By the way, I want to ask you two:<214> Just as Hoe Wŏl (懹月), upon welcoming new, distinctive (異色) writers like Sŏhae (曙海) and Kiyŏng (箕永), strongly advocated (力說) and praised (推獎하여한) the emergence (擡頭) of "New Tendency School Literature" (新傾向派文學) to form a trend, and later, as P'albong and several others guided (引導하엿드시) Korean literature down the path of proletarian literature, now, at this time when signs (兆朕) of the rise of war literature are visible, do you two critics have any intention of making efforts to open and guide that path?

Pak Yŏnghŭi - The intention is there, but my talent is still (아즉은) insufficient. Recently, I've been too busy (奔忙해서) to even find time (틈조차) for reading and contemplation (思索할), but I intend to gradually make an effort going forward.

Kim Kijin - In the end, since various problems (諸般問題) relate to the construction of the East Asian New Cooperative Community (東亞新協同體), unless the political outline (輪廓) of that cooperative body becomes clearer, won't literary activity also be unable to become that active?

Kim Tonghwan - Then, let's stop here (이만치 하고) for now and talk again when we have another opportunity. (End)<215>

**Summary**

This 1939 roundtable discussion features Korean intellectuals Pak Yŏnghŭi, Kim Kijin, and Kim Tonghwan discussing "War Literature." They survey Japanese war literature arising from conflicts like the Russo-Japanese War and the ongoing Sino-Japanese War (e.g., works by Sakurai Tadatsugu, Hino Ashihei), characterizing it as patriotic and promoting the national spirit, contrasting it with post-WWI European works (Remarque, Barbusse, Rolland, Expressionism) which often contained anti-war sentiments or focused on humanitarianism and individual suffering. The participants note the trend in Japan for intellectuals to unite behind the national war effort. They cautiously consider the future role of Korean writers, acknowledging the lack of direct war experience among them and expressing hesitation or uncertainty about actively promoting or creating "war literature" aligned with Japan's objectives, especially pending clearer political developments regarding the "East Asian New Cooperative Community."

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing about the intellectual climate in colonial Korea during the Sino-Japanese War. It shows the awareness Korean intellectuals had of Japanese and world literary trends concerning war. More significantly, it highlights the implicit pressure and expectation for Korean writers to contribute to the Japanese war effort through literature, framed as participation in an "Asian" or "East Asian" literary movement centered on Japan's "Holy War." The participants' careful navigation of the topic—acknowledging Japanese trends while citing the lack of direct Korean involvement and the need for political clarity—hints at the complex and constrained position of Korean intellectuals under colonial rule, trying to respond to dominant narratives without direct opposition but also without full endorsement. The discussion around Hino Ashihei's popular "reportage" style reflects contemporary trends, while the debate on whether war literature should be patriotic or critical touches on universal questions relevant to their specific colonial context.

『戰爭文學』과 『朝鮮作家』 -戰爭과 文學과 그 作品을 말하는 座談會-
						朴英熙, 金基鎭, 本社 金東煥
一. 日本內地의 戰爭文學이란 어떠 어떠한 것인가
金東煥 - 지금은 국가가 전쟁중이요. 또한 세계대전 卽後의 文藝思潮의 경향을 볼지라도 戰爭進行前後에는 依例히 戰爭文學이 그 主流가 되어 잇드니만치 今後 우리들 文學社會(우리들뿐 아니라 아마 日本內地와 支那 滿洲國 등 全 亞細亞에 있어서)에 있어 制作의 중심이 이 전쟁문학이 될 줄 아러요. 그러기에 전쟁 제3년을 맛는 이 新年勞頭에 우리는 「전쟁문학과<206> 朝鮮作家」에 대하야 깊이 성찰을 보내기로 하옵시다. 이제 亞細亞에 있어서戰爭文學을 이야기하자면 우리들 「朝鮮의 戰爭文學」은 다소 거북한 점이 있어서 이것을
話題外로 하고 또 滿洲國은 建國日淺하여 아직 문학이 생길 培土조차 준비되어 있지 안으   니 그 亦 論外로 하고 또한 支那文學에 대하여도 가령 역사적으로 兵車行이라거나 하는     등의 戰爭文學이 量으로 유명한 질로 만했고 近日에 와서도 郭沫若이라거나 「敗走千里」등   의 작가를 통하야 어느 정도로 생산이 되고 있지만은 여러가지 의미에서 그 亦 省略하기    로 하고 이제 이 자리에서 이야기할 범위는 「日本文學」에 한하야 論議하기로 하옵시다.
그러면 近世나 現代에 있어 內地에 어떠한 戰爭文學과 또 작가와 작품이 있섯다고 하겟읍   니까.
八峰 金基鎭 - 日本에 있어서의 戰爭文學이란 역시 전쟁기간 중에 되었거나 戰後에 그 戰塵을 바더서 제작되어 온 것이 대부분이었지요. 日本이 當面하였든 戰爭이란 明治 37,8년의 日露戰役, 그 다음이 滿洲事變 또 계속된 上海事變 그러고는 이번의 支那事變이겠지요. 日露戰爭 때에는 저 유명한 櫻井忠溫의 「肉彈」과 水野廣德의 「此一戰」이 있었고 上海事變 때에는 直木三十五의 「日本의 戰慄」이 있었고 이번 事變에는 바야으로 전쟁작품이 홍수같이 쏘다저 나오려 하는데 이미 생긴 것중 저명한 것으로는 火野葺平의 「麥과 兵隊」 「土와 兵隊」의 2부작과 上田廣의 「黃塵」 등이 잇다할 걸요.
그리고 같은 전쟁의 一面이면서 日露戰爭 때에 그것을 露西亞側에서 取扱한 露西亞作家     뿌리뽀이의 「對馬」와 「戰鬪」의 2편을 들 수 있겠지요. 「對馬」나 「戰鬪」는 몇 해전 「改造」   에 연재되여 內地讀書界에 큰 센세슌을 이르킨 작품이었지요.
金東煥 - 아마 외국의 戰爭文學 속에는 反戰傾向的 作品이 있었겠지만 內地作家에 한해서는 모도다 戰爭文學 즉 愛國文學이란 見地에서 어느 것이나 전쟁의 崇高와 그 생명을 버리고 국가의 사명에 즐기어 죽는 勇士의 愛國的 情熱의 高揚 등 一括하야 國家魂을 讚美하여서 즉 「전쟁이란 文化創造의 어머니」란 見地에서 붓을 든 것이 대부분일걸요.
朴英熙 - 그렇치요. 녜전에 읽은 것들이 되야 다소 稀徵하지만<207> 악가 八峰君이든 諸作品을 치켜세워 볼지라도 櫻井이나 水野나 모도 다 제가 직접 戰線에 나가서 오랜 苦鬪 속에 신념으로써 었은 그 애국심을 예술의 형식을 통하여 전국민에게 高揚시킨, 다시 말하  면 국가의 목적에 완전히 합치되도록 지은 戰爭美化의 문학들이엇지요.
이 두 작품이 爲先 日本文學史上에 있어 전쟁문학의 礎石을 노았다 할 것이지요. 그 뒤     直木三十五의 「日本의 戰慄」이 있었지만 그는 전쟁문학이라기보다 그때 일본사회에 澎湃    한 푸로레타리아的 文藝思潮을 國家主義의 방향에로 전환시키기 위한 作爲的 目的小說로    써 다만 작품 중에 한두 장면을 上海戰場에서 떼여왔달 뿐이었요.
金東煥 - 최근 「改造」에 난 「麥과 兵隊」라거나 「土와 兵隊」라는 것을 보섰서요?
朴英熙 - 대강 보았는데 火野의 태도는 흔이 우리가 말하는 思索的의 것이 아니라 곧 실천의 문학이라고 할 수 있겠지요. 전쟁한 것을 쓴 것이 아니라 전쟁하면서 쓰는 작품입니다. 그 생생한 감정이 곧 국민들의 胸底에 가저다 주는 무었이 있게 되는 것이지요. 近間 評 論家들의 말을 드르면 이러한 문학을 「記錄的 文學」이라고 합데다. 말하자면 報告文學일 것입니다.
그런데 전쟁도, 현재에 있어서 我國의 전쟁은 歐洲大戰과는 그 意義가 아조 相異하여서     東洋永遠한 평화를 위한 聖戰인 까닭에 그 戰爭意識은 一時的 生活現象으로 취급될 것이    아니라, 長期建設에 따르는 日本精神의 哲學化한 한 개의 이념이 현재 우리들의 전쟁문학   에 함유해야 될 것입니다.
옛날 사람들은 전쟁을 일개의 작품을 구성하는 素材로 쓰거나 或은 批判的 態度도 그렸지   만 우리는 確固한 이상이 이 人間探求에 一重要한 요소로서 한 사람의 병사로서 붓을 들    수 있는 것이니 이러한 의미에서 「土と兵隊」는 주목되며 문제될만한 작품입니다.
金基鎭 - 그러치요. 대체로 전쟁문학을 類別하자면 첫재는 싸홈이(시작되어서 前線兵士와 銃後國民이 모도 다 흥분하여서 그저 깊은 성찰이 없이 단순한 감정과 정열로만 전쟁을 驅歌하든 그 시절에 된 문학, 둘재는 戰爭進行 중에 戰場의 活動相과 병사의 沈勇한 동작을 묘사, 기록하는 것으로서 일삼는 레포-타文學時節, 그리고 셋재 段階에 나가서 전쟁이 끗난 뒤 모든<208> 흥분과 感傷的 또는 浪漫的 정열이 사라진 뒤 즉 냉정한 이성을 도로 찾어가진 때에 고요히 전쟁을 觀照하면 內省的 態度로 쓰는 時節, 이렇게 분류할 수 있는데 이제 火野의 2부작을 보건데 그것은 제 1기와 제 2기에 속하여 壯烈한 戰鬪光景의 묘사에서 시작되여 병사의 행동을 그리는 그것으로 시종하였더군요. 이것이 지금 같은 戰爭過程의 시기에 있어선 讀書層의 支持를 받게 되겟지요. 나도 대강 보았는데 그 사람의 상상력의 풍부한데는 놀났어요.
火野에 대하야 나는 이런 에피소-드를 드른 적이 있어요. 이 사람은 早大文科出身인데 학   생시대에 同人雜誌에 每號 글을 쓰든 精力家이었다는데 하로는 그 雜誌同人끼리의 編輯會   席上에서 여러가지 이야기 끝에 火野가 자기 체험담을 고백하는데 자기가 언젠가 下關으    여행 중 차 중에서 어떤 노인을 맛났는데 그 노인이 조고마한 네모반듯한 괴짝(箱子)를 보에 싸들었드래요. 무었을 간직했는지 그 노인은 몹시 소중히 그 상자를 간직하는데 便所에 가도 들고가고 벤또 사려나가도 들고 나가고 그러드래요. 대체 이 영감쟁이가 무얼 너었기에 저러는가 하고 호기심이 나드래요. 그러다가 中途에 네리게 되었는데 火野가 名古屋에 네려 어느 여관에 들자 그 老人 亦 同行이 좋으니 저도 하로 묵어가겠다고 따라   와 그 여관에 들드래요. 여관서 저녁에 火野는 몬저 沐浴을 하고 올나와서 그 노인더러     沐浴하고 오라니까 몹시 주저하다가 그 상자를 한쪽 옆에 앗가운 듯 놓고서는 沐浴場에     가드라나. 하도 이상하여 그 동안에 얼는 그 상자를 풀어보니 놀나지 말아. 어린아히 송장   을 油紙에 싸서 넣었드래요. 그만 무서워 얼는 앗가 모양으로 싸놓고 모르는체 않고 있을   려니 그 노인이 沐浴도 채 못하고 황겁이 올너 오더니 미다지를 쓱 열고 드러서자 火野     얼골을 보고 「이 년석 너 풀어보았구나. 이 비밀을 안 이상 네 목숨을 빼앗겠다」하며 6穴   砲로 쏘드래요. 그만 놀라 충충대를 뛰여나려 오다가 발을 빗구로 드듸어 잡버저 깨어나    니 그만 그것이 꿈이드래요. 이야기를 듣고 同僚들이 지금까지 사실인 줄만 알고 속아드    른 것이 분하기는 하면서도 그 풍부한 상상력에 놀랐대요. 꿈도 아니고 또 자긔가<209> 지   어내 맨든 소설스토리었든 모양이지요. 「麥과 兵隊」속에도 여기 못지지 않는 놀라운 상상   력이 驅使되여 잇드군요. 天稟있는 戰爭作家얘요. 앞으로 더 발전할걸요. 아마 日本에 있    어서의 今後 전쟁문학을 있글고 나갈 사람이 그 사람이 될걸요. 國旗의 밑에서 제가 召集   되어 戰場에 나가는 것을 조곰도 두러워하지 않고 깁부게 아는 그 감정 - 거기서 우러나   오는 문학이 요컨대 火野以下의 現日本諸作家의 일치된 거러나갈 길일 걸요.
金東煥 - 결국 이렇게 총괄하여 말할 수 있지 않을까요. 즉 - 戰勝國家에서 발생되는 전쟁문학은 전쟁때문에 일는 것보다 었는 것이 더 많으니까 국민의 意思가 전쟁에 감사하는 念에 꽉 차지어 戰爭美化의 문학이 저절로 생기겠고 그 반대로 戰敗國에 있어선 X戰文學이 생기겠고 - 그러니까 日本內地에서는 이 雰圍氣가 계속되는 한 戰爭讚美의 정신이 文藝思潮에 반영되어 흐르리라고 - 따라서 작가와 독자도 이 영향속에서 앞으로 어느 기간까지는 살게되리라고.-
朴英熙 - 그러타고 볼 수 있을가. 그도 보는 一面이 될는지 모르지요. 그러나 歐州大戰後의 文藝思潮를 보면 반드시 그러치도 않은 듯해요. 大戰때 문학은 다 戰後에 생긴 社會思想 혹은 인생관이 과거를 비판하는데 많이 기우러젔으나 지금 우리가 말하는 문학은 전쟁 중에 생기는 문학인 까닭에 巴人兄이 말하듯이 그렇게 功利的으로 유리한 결과만을 추종하는 것은 아니라고 생각합니다.
전쟁 중에 병사가 갖이고 있는 그 생각, 그 감정, 그 희망, 그 생활이 그대로 문학에 나타   나는 것이니 이러한 現地報告文學에 있어서는 우리들로서도 연구할 것이 많은 줄로 압니    다. 또한 전쟁을 謳歌云云하니 말이지. 이것은 語弊가 있다고 봄니다. 正義의 싸홈은 正義   가 승리할 때까지 싸호는 것이니 이러한 전쟁에서는 전쟁에 勝하든지 敗하든지 그 意識은   조금도 異變이 없을 줄노 압니다.

二. 大戰以後의 文藝作品과 그 傾向은 어떠한가

金東煥 - 그러면 이제는 歐洲大戰以後의 주요한 작품과<210> 그 작가를 말슴키로 합시다. 아마 가장 세계적으로 큰 센세-슌을 이르킨 작품으론 루마루케의 「西部戰線 異狀없다」 일걸요.
朴英熙 - 우리도 7,8년 전에 읽었고 東京에서는 그것이 新築地劇場에 상연된 것을 비롯하여 전국의 문학, 연극, 영화인층에 큰 旋風을 이르켰지요.
金東煥 - 루마루케의 敎養程度는?
朴英熙 - 傳記에 쓴 것을 보니까 世界大戰 때는 겨우 열아홉살 밧게 안되는 紅顔少年으로 저도 학교에 다니다가 책보를 집어뿌리고 戰場으로 뛰여나갔대요. 그 全作品을 보면 지성적이라기보다 감성적으로 그런 것이 特長갓더군요.
그 중 이러한 대목이 있지 않어요.
「전쟁이란 웨 이러나나」하고 斬壕 속에서 흙투성이 다 된 한 병사가 무르니까 다른 병정    이 저는 아는체 하며
「그야 어떤 나라가 다른 한 나라를 몹시 侮辱한 때에 이러나지 무얼!」
「무에야 한 나라가 - 그게 모를 말이야. 大體 獨逸 나라의 어느 山이 佛蘭西의 山川을 욕   했단 말이냐. 반드시 山이 아니래도 조치 숲울이든지 보리밭이든지...」
「이 자식 참 천치야. 어떤 한 국민이 다른 국민을 모욕한 경우에 말이야」
「더욱 모를 말이 아니냐, 그럼 우리는 예서 할 일이 없지 않으냐. 나는 조곰도 佛蘭西놈한   테서 모욕밧은 일이 생각 안나」
그때 그 兵丁은 너무도 화가 나는지 한 개 쥐여박으면서
「너 같은 시골뚜기가 문제된다드냐」
「그럼 나는 집에 도라가도 좋겠구나」
하고 고집을 피우기에 이 말에 여렀은 하하하 하고 웃섰다.
이런 대목과 같이 그는 솔직히 누구나 알기 쉽게 전쟁을 설명하고 전쟁을 묘사했지요.
金東煥 - 그는 국가를 어떻게 보는가요.
金基鎭 - 그는 그 작품에서 국가를 그리지 않고 戰場의 장면에서 인간성만을 주로 그리려 하였지요. 전장에 나둥그는 개개의 팔악신이 등등 이 속에 처한 인간생활을 刻明하게 그리려는데에 力點을 둔 듯해요.
