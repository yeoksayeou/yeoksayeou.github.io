발행일	1940년 10월 01일
기사제목	風浪 (Wind and Waves)
필자	金永壽
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

風浪
						金永壽

Truthfully, having picked up the spoon with absolutely no thought, he hadn't felt much appetite since facing the dinner table, but since he'd already started, he figured he'd manage a few spoonfuls somehow. After pecking at the food a bit, he quietly pushed the table away. His wife asked,
"Why, is it because there are no side dishes?"
She looked up at him with a slightly disappointed expression, but Ilsu merely replied curtly,
"No."
<223>
"Can I have some water..."
He quickly changed the subject. So, his wife asked no more questions and swiftly carried the table outside. Still, wondering if perhaps his wife's attitude might become disrespectful or harsh once she went out onto the wooden porch, Ilsu paused the hand that was about to strike a match to light a cigarette and diligently craned his neck to look outside, but there was no sign of such behavior.
(Why has she suddenly become so docile again?)
Somehow, he couldn't fathom the reason behind his wife's currently meek attitude. Ilsu just smacked his lips a couple of times and was about to strike the match with a *deuk* sound when—
"Here, have some water. The scorched-rice water (숭늉) has completely cooled."
His wife, her expression unchanged, opened the small side window and handed him a water bowl. Indeed, the scorched-rice water was like cold water. After gulping it down coolly with loud slurps, he suddenly called out,
"Yeobo (Honey)."
In a household where only two people lived, even if one person just suddenly shouted "Yeobo," the person hearing it knew exactly who was being called; it was like a signal. Sure enough, as soon as her husband Ilsu's voice sounded, it was immediately followed by,
"What is it?"
from outside. Of course, it was his wife's voice.
However, although he had called out impulsively, Ilsu hadn't yet formed a coherent thought about how and where to begin the conversation. As he just puffed away at his cigarette,
"Why did you call?"
his wife's voice came once more.
Oops, I called too soon. I could have talked slowly later. Ilsu instantly changed his mind and couldn't help but give a lame reply,
"Oh, nothing. I just called."
Then, just as expected, his wife, who must have already come right up to the side window in the meantime,
"Ai, how silly..."
tossed out that one phrase and went back towards the other side, the sound of her rubber shoes squeaking <224> *jjaek jjaek*.
"Silly man."
Ilsu muttered this to himself unnecessarily and then just lay down right there, using his arm as a pillow.
As he relaxed his legs and arms and stretched out his back, the fatigue from the whole day spent nagging at the office (社) finally felt agonizingly noticeable.
His wife seemed busy washing dishes in the kitchen now. The clattering sound of dishes being handled could be clearly heard through the wall.
Ilsu briefly closed his eyes tightly, pretending to sleep, but the more he closed his eyes and tried to rest his head, the more the thoughts he had planned earlier that day at the office surfaced too clearly in his mind, making it unbearable.
Of course, Ilsu didn't think divorce was as simple and easy as the word suggested, nor had he ever even dreamed of it until now.
However, lacking the confidence to continue dragging out this painful life any further felt not just painful, but frightening.
(Anyway, let's just say it and see.)
Ilsu spurred himself on mentally once more and, like someone suddenly startled, bolted upright and shouted towards the outside,
"Yeobo."
He definitely shouted, but the word "Yeobo" barely left his lips.

He called, but his wife didn't answer. Thinking perhaps she had stepped outside for a moment, he waited a bit, but the clattering sound still came from the kitchen. She was there. His wife was definitely in the kitchen. Why didn't she answer while she was there? A slightly unpleasant thought welled up, and this time, mustering more strength than before, he called out loudly,
"Yeobo."
Only then did his wife's voice reply,
"Hold on. I'll be right in—"
<225>
But this was undoubtedly an utterly insolent answer. No, it wasn't an answer, it was sheer petulance. If he called "Yeobo," she should just say "Yes" or else come into the room promptly. What did she mean by "Hold on," and whose presence did she think she was in to be grumbling on and on like that? The more Ilsu thought about it, the more his wife's attitude grated on his nerves, making it unbearable to endure any longer.
The thought of flinging open the window and shouting a stern reprimand did cross his mind. But doing so would only make the household awkward and also seemed to go against the original intention for which he had planned things out and come home early from the office. So, Ilsu decided to pretend he hadn't heard her and wait patiently with a deliberately relaxed heart until his wife finished the dishes quickly and came inside.
The pillar clock hanging on the porch wall slowly struck six. It struck really slowly. Just striking six took quite a while. He didn't know when even that blasted clock had become such a lazybones.
Speaking of the clock, it was the same story. When they first bought it, Ilsu had insisted in front of his wife multiple times that they should spend a little more money and get a cuckoo clock (빽국시계) with a pleasant chime. For just 3 won 50 jeon, or whatever small amount more, they could have bought a splendid one that went *cuckoo cuckoo* every time it struck, instead of that endlessly slow and unpleasant-sounding regular pillar clock. But his wife, saying,
"Ah, where does 3 won 50 jeon come from? Isn't that money? As long as the clock keeps time, that's enough. What's the use of looking at its appearance?"
had stubbornly insisted and eventually brought home and hung up that dreadful thing. True, as his wife said, despite its looks, it had never malfunctioned once, nor had it ever been off by even 5 or 10 minutes. But since it was hung right in the middle of the main wooden floor hall, shouldn't it at least have a decent appearance and look somewhat presentable?
It wasn't just the clock. Everything was like that. Needlessly frugal, she never even considered things like taste or appearance from the start. Ilsu glanced around the room, and his gaze stopped at one spot. Smacking his lips <226> *jjep jjep* in displeasure, he even shook his head *jeolle jeolle*.
What caught Ilsu's eye just then was the black skirt his wife frequently wore whenever she went out. He didn't know its exact name. But he'd been talking about that darn black skirt for ages. Black has its seasons too, doesn't it? Yet she wraps herself in that dark skirt all year round, twelve months a year. It's mortifying enough for the wearer, but utterly embarrassing for him to look at. Ah, these days are rare times when good fabrics in seasonal colors and beautifully patterned cloths pour out abundantly. Yet, her persistence in sticking solely to that darn black color was truly an incomprehensible disposition.
It would be a different matter if he never gave her money to have clothes made. Doesn't he set aside a separate budget, 5 won or 10 won a month, whenever he feels like it, specifically telling her to get some outing clothes made? She was good at readily accepting whatever he gave her, *neopjjuk neopjjuk*, but where on earth she spent it all after receiving it, he had absolutely no clue.
Just last month, when he came home after getting his salary, he divided it up – this for that, and that for this. When there was some extra left over, he readily picked out 10 won and said,
"Use this to get yourself a skirt."
He had even urged her repeatedly. Yet, that 10 won was nowhere to be seen, and that darn black skirt was still fluttering on the wall. People unaware of this situation would surely just criticize the husband for not even buying his wife a single piece of clothing. The more he thought, not even one thing in ten met his approval.
Well, having graduated from girls' high school and even dipped her toes in a professional school, one would expect her thinking to be somewhat different here and there, know how to consider others' appearances, and understand things like her husband's tastes. But his wife, as someone might say, seemed to have attended school in name only, completely walled off from such emotions and tastes.
"Tsk!"
Ilsu clicked his tongue once in displeasure, and averting his eyes from the unsightly black skirt, he shouted, <227> this time with considerable anger welling up,
"Ah, what are you dawdling around for, being in there—"
Still, his wife in the kitchen remained composed.
"Ah, I have to finish washing what I started before I can come in, don't I? Honestly..."
His wife continued making the *deolgeureok deolgeureok* sound.

The reason husband Ilsu was urgently seeking his wife now was, of course, due to the plan he had pondered, hesitated over, and thought about this way and that at the office earlier that day, finally steeling his resolve to bring up the issue of divorce right away while it was fresh in his mind.
Naturally, the wife, busy washing dishes in the kitchen, had no way of knowing about this plan harbored in her husband's heart.
Moreover, they had gotten married right around the time the cool breeze began to blow in early autumn, so this autumn marked exactly three years. In those three years, neither the wife nor the husband Ilsu had ever once dreamed that any such wind and waves (風浪) would arise in their married life.
Just three years. During these three years of living together, what the wife, along with her husband Ilsu, had always tediously thought about was what he constantly talked about, like a habit, whenever he had a chance, even now:
"When will we ever live comfortably in our own house?"
And the wife would also chime in, often saying things like:
"If we had saved up all the rent we've paid so far, it would be tremendous—"
Whenever she said that, Ilsu would reply, as if her lament was no big deal,
"What—. It'll be soon, that kind of thing—."
But inwardly,
(Indeed, a house is urgent, when will we manage to get one—)
this was no small worry. However, just because he was worried, as a man, he couldn't possibly reveal this poverty of mind outwardly in front of his wife, a mere woman, sitting knee-to-knee with her and saying,
"We really need a house. This is a big problem."
<228>
It was absolutely impossible. So he just kept soothing his wife, who was growing anxious about the house, by saying,
"Don't worry, don't worry at all."
They had lived like this for three years already. During these three years, was Ilsu's situation so dire that he couldn't manage to acquire a comfortable house costing merely 5,000 or 6,000 won, no matter how they lived? No, that wasn't the case.
If Ilsu swallowed his pride and went to his father in the countryside to insist, it was perfectly possible to get that amount. But as a man in the family clan, he had to maintain his dignity. That's how Ilsu felt.
When Ilsu and his current wife, who had already fallen in love during their school days, went down to their respective homes – Ilsu to his, his wife to hers – to ask their parents' opinions on their marriage, the wife's parents, whatever they thought of Ilsu, readily consented. However, Ilsu's father strongly opposed it until the end.
The father's reasons for opposition seemed utterly nonsensical to Ilsu, and forgive the disrespectful thought, but they were merely outdated and childish.
"Look here, you clueless child, no matter how the world has changed, you still need to look at the lineage (뼈다귀 - bones), don't you? And is that all? That woman, having studied so much, do you think she'll come into our house and manage a household properly?"
The father persisted in opposing, citing only such reasons. Not only the father, but eventually the entire household rose up in uproar, fiercely raising the banner of opposition. But Ilsu alone tried to fend them off, acted as a shield, remained stubborn, opposed the opposition, and eventually rebelled. Their marriage was the result of this rebellion.
The father, ultimately overwhelmed by his son's rebellion, gave in helplessly, saying,
"Agh, I don't care anymore. Get married, do whatever. I just won't consider a child like you my child—"
With these harsh words, he showed no further intention of opposing his son's marriage.
So, although his father's declaration sounded somewhat <229> disheartening to Ilsu at the time, he convinced himself, 'He's just saying that because he's angry now, surely he wouldn't sever ties (어절) forever,' and returned to Seoul where the bride's family lived. They hurriedly arranged what they called a wedding, but indeed, only his mother came up from the countryside; his father did not appear.
So, in a manner of speaking, Ilsu had been estranged from his father since then. He lacked the courage – no, more than courage, the pride – to go to that father, bow his head, and brazenly ask for a house because he didn't have one, even though he was his own flesh and blood. So he had just carried on like this until now. Of course, his wife knew this well. That's why, whenever he worried about the house, his wife would adopt a notably soft tone and a thoroughly gentle expression, saying,
"Are we the only ones in Seoul without a house? There are plenty. We can manage it gradually."
She would cheerfully offer words of comfort in front of her troubled husband.
That was the kind of wife she was. So affectionate, so kind, so thoughtful. To think that such a wife had a secret— The more Ilsu traced his thoughts back up, the more hateful, cowardly, and despicable his current wife seemed, making it unbearable.
They had even firmly promised each other, when they pledged their love, to confess cleanly and settle all past secrets each person held and concealed.
At that time, Ilsu had confessed everything, starting with the story of a woman named K, with whom he had had a somewhat deep relationship in what could truly be called his first love, as well as his connections with women named Y and H. His wife, too, had clearly stated the name, Kim something-or-other (金 무어), and confessed in detail about her relationship with a man studying at some private university in Tokyo at the time – where and how they met, how the relationship progressed afterward, as if telling a story from a novel.
After that, the two had made a firm vow to completely, absolutely cleanly settle the past and build a new life together going forward.
Yet, to think that this Kim something-or-other fellow was still breathing <230> somewhere in a corner of their lives—. The more Ilsu thought about it, the dizzier he felt with anger, and his whole body trembled violently.
"Since I have this evidence, surely she won't deny it."
As Ilsu reaffirmed this in his mind two or three times, he once again gently felt the small notebook in his pocket, which he had been carrying since yesterday. Just then,
"Why are you looking for me like that? Like a child—"
his wife rushed in.
As she came in, she tried to abruptly sit on her husband's folded knees. Ilsu roughly pushed his wife's slender waist away.
"What is this? Hmph."
He let out a sharp, dismissive sound, putting force into the "hmph," and shifted away slightly.
"Ah-joo (Goodness)."
Seeing her still smiling brightly, it seemed his wife was indeed clueless about her husband's inner turmoil. As Ilsu remained sullen, chain-smoking,
"What's wrong, all of a sudden?"
his wife asked, this time looking a bit bewildered.
"All of a sudden?"
Ilsu repeated his wife's words and adjusted his seating position. Only then, noticing her husband's unusual expression, did the wife's dark eyes immediately widen with alertness.

Gazing at his wife's gradually changing expression, Ilsu once again instinctively reached for the right trouser pocket containing the notebook. And he gently patted it from the outside. As he patted the notebook like this, he pondered,
(What should I do? Should I just take this out first, without a long talk? Then she'll understand—)
However, it felt like the story would end too simply before he got to the bottom of it, and in a way, it seemed his wife might then go her own way instantly without <231> suffering too much pain or shame, so he wasn't quite inclined to do it yet.
(That's right. If I take it out now, the matter will end easily. But I might as well torment her a bit before bringing it out—)
Finally, husband Ilsu's thoughts settled on this, and the notebook remained in his right trouser pocket for the time being. But ultimately, it was all the same. Whether presented first or later, it would come out eventually. The moment this notebook was spread open between them, it was obvious that all their promises up to now, along with all the beautiful customs and trivial habits that accompanied them, would immediately be declared bankrupt. Moreover, for her, who always boasted of her own innocence and asserted that the only reason she could live cheerfully and brightly within the difficult confines of this family was solely because she trusted her husband's love—
"Here, what's all this written in this notebook?"
If he presented the notebook right before her eyes like that, she would surely, with her sensitive temperament, shriek and faint or cause some kind of commotion. It seemed certain. That's how he thought.
So, he decided to bring it out a little later anyway, and first resolved to probe his wife's feelings with a few simple words.
Therefore, slowly removing his hand from his right trouser pocket, which he had been pressing down until his palm sweated, he lit another cigarette. As if unaware of her husband's complex inner thoughts, his wife, who had been sitting blankly, shifted back slightly from her spot and asked,
"Ah, why are you being like this?"
This time, her voice was full of suspicion. And although he couldn't hear it clearly, the *jjet jjet jjet* sound indicated she had surely clicked her tongue at the end of her sentence.
"Ah, who are you clicking your tongue at?"
Ilsu shouted abruptly, glaring fiercely with narrowed eyes and clenched teeth, as if about to strike his wife's cheek, and snapped his head around.
"Aigoo, you startled me..."
His wife, genuinely startled, widened her eyes and drew back, sitting down further away. <232>
"If you don't want to be startled, behave yourself properly, shouldn't you?"
Oops, I went too far with my words. He regretted it instantly, but the words already spoken couldn't be taken back.
"Uh..."
His wife just gaped, "Uh," unable to figure out what to do for a moment. Since the words had come out like this anyway, Ilsu decided to stick with it.
"Hmph. How do you take people? Do you think I'm just some idiot wearing pants and a jacket?"
He threw out another remark. Still, his wife couldn't quickly find grounds for rebuttal and just kept saying "Uh, uh," her mouth still wide open. Seeing his wife's pitiful expression, Ilsu felt increasingly triumphant.
"If you have that much education, act like it."
He said things like this, and also,
"Who are you keeping secrets from? Do you think I have no eyes and no ears?"
He provoked his wife's anger, feeling quite energized himself.
Still, his wife stubbornly remained silent. 'Serves you right, what could you possibly have to say?' thinking this, he glanced sideways at his wife's face, and saw that tears had already welled up in her dark eyes. She must be crying. But there was no reason to be scared just because she cried. No, whether she cried or whatever, now that things had unfolded this way, there wasn't the slightest reason for him to concern himself with such things. The husband's thoughts grew increasingly harsh, and the points he had intended to make in order got jumbled up, pouring out incoherently, which he couldn't stop either.
"No need for a long talk, let's just put it bluntly and do what's best for both of us."
"..."
"I'll just consider myself fooled for about three years, that's all."
"..."
"It's fortunate I found out now. Ah, why aren't you saying anything? Have you become mute..."
Still, his wife remained stubbornly silent, lips sealed. <233> Finally, her shoulders began to heave, *deulmeok deulmeok*, and with a gasp, *heok*, she collapsed right there, burying her face and starting to cry loudly, *eong eong*. No matter how long he waited, the crying wouldn't stop easily.
After crying like this for a while, the wife slowly lifted her face and began to string together disconnected words, one or two at a time, with pauses in between.
"Is a woman just someone doomed to die? Can you say anything you want to her?"
However, such grumbling (푸념) was clearly insufficient from the start to quell the fury of the husband, currently burning with intense anger.
"Hmph, listen to the best excuse she can come up with..."
Ilsu didn't offer any further reply to those words and just listened silently. Then the wife, on her part, retorted,
"You told me to behave myself properly, so what did I do wrong? Huh? What did I do wrong?"
She threw a tantrum (포달) like this, this time even drawing her knees up and arguing vehemently. So Ilsu could no longer remain silent either.
"Hey, where did you learn these manners? Did you behave properly? Are you saying this because you behaved properly?"
As the words left his mouth, he pointlessly yelled "Huh!" loudly once.
"Heuheung..."
Then his wife collapsed again with a *heok* sound and began to cry tempestuously (앙까님). She even scratched the papered floor with her fingernails, making scraping sounds, *bagak bagak*.
Feeling that just exchanging words like this wouldn't likely bring things to a swift conclusion, Ilsu declared,
"Here, look at this. Are you still going to stubbornly deny it after seeing this?"
He quickly pulled the palm-sized notebook from his right pocket and set it down. With a *cheolkeok* sound, the notebook fell, causing his wife to momentarily stop crying, lift her face, and glance down at the floor.
"Here, who is this? Whose address is this?"
Ilsu abruptly opened it to the page he had folded beforehand and thrust it close to his wife's eyes. Only then did his wife stop crying. <234>
(Hmph, just as I thought—)
Thinking this, feeling quite triumphant, Ilsu was about to light another cigarette when his wife suddenly retorted, pale with fright,
"Hey, what's wrong with this?"
Ilsu quickly took the cigarette he had just put in his mouth back out. Turning his face sharply towards his wife, he glared with intensely fierce eyes and shouted menacingly again, just like before,
"Ah, you still don't get it?"
"Hey, what's wrong with this?"
"Why did you write down this person's address?"
"So what if I wrote it down?"
"So what?"
"What's wrong with writing it down?"
"..."
This time, it was Ilsu who was at a loss for words. Of course, it wasn't because he had nothing to say. It was because his wife's current attitude seemed utterly absurd.
After staring blankly at her husband's silent face for a while, the wife bowed her head again, burying it between her arms, and began to cry. Even so, she spoke in broken fragments.
"What's so wrong about writing an address in a notebook? Did I even write it down because I wanted to? You yourself said you wanted to go check out places like Sinŭiju (신경) or Dalian (대련), and asked if I knew anyone there, asking me to find out if there was anyone you could visit. So I barely managed to find out through connections among friends and wrote it down. Then why did you even say in the first place that you wanted to go find a job over there..."
As his wife's words continued, Ilsu indeed remembered saying something like that one night in bed, while feeling his wife's waist, which had become much thinner than before, and feeling newly ashamed of the meager salary he brought home each month:
"Damn it, I wish I could go work somewhere like Sinŭiju or Dalian too. I heard the salaries are decent there if you find a good position. Don't you know anyone over there?"
He remembered saying that.
"Alright, so she was trying to pick someone from that area <235> for my sake, and that's what it was."
Ilsu finally understood the secret behind the address written down and felt utterly foolish and silly.
His wife just cried more and more vehemently. Ilsu picked up the black-covered notebook lying open near his wife's head as if handling something dirty, pushed it aside, and quietly stood up, opened the door, and went out onto the porch.
Already, dusk was quietly settling (기뜨려) in the yard, *nüyeot nüyeot*. A large leaf, flown in from somewhere, tumbled *dinggul dinggul* across the middle of the yard after flying over the wall.
The clock slowly struck seven. It really needed to be replaced with a cuckoo clock (빽국시계), the husband thought. Then, suddenly turning towards the room where the sound of crying still hadn't ceased, he said,
"Hey, look here. Stop crying now and go get that clock replaced, won't you? Prices are lower these days, so a cuckoo clock shouldn't cost much..."
*Hwiik hwik hwik*. A leaf, blown by the wind, swept amusingly across the entire yard, which wasn't too boring to watch sitting at the edge of the porch.
(September, Year of Gyeongjin [1940]) <236>

**Summary**

Ilsu, a husband dissatisfied with his marriage and financial situation, comes home determined to confront his wife about divorce. He misinterprets an address found in her notebook as evidence of infidelity. He berates her, causing her great distress. However, his wife tearfully explains she only wrote down the address of a contact in Sinŭiju or Dalian after Ilsu himself expressed a desire to seek better employment there. Realizing his mistake and foolishness, Ilsu drops the confrontation, goes outside, and awkwardly suggests replacing their plain clock with the cuckoo clock they had previously disagreed on, leaving the underlying marital tensions unresolved.

From a historical perspective, the story offers glimpses into urban middle-class life in colonial Korea around 1940. The mention of seeking better job opportunities in Manchurian cities like Sinŭiju (on the border) or Dalian reflects the economic realities and migration patterns of the time under Japanese rule. The characters' concerns about finances, homeownership ("when will we ever live comfortably in our own house?"), and differing values regarding frugality versus aesthetics (the clock, the black skirt) provide insight into the social pressures and aspirations of Koreans navigating modernity and colonial constraints. The husband's patriarchal assumptions and the wife's eventual explanation subtly challenge traditional gender roles within the context of their modernizing lives.

하고 턱 이런 소리를 하며, 약간 섭섭한 표정까지를 지으며 쳐다보는 것이었으나, 一洙는 역시

「...」
이번에는 一洙가 말이 막혔다. 허나 할 말이 없어 그런 것은 물론 아니다. 너무도 안해의 지금 이같은 태도가 어처구니없이 생각되었던 때문이다.
말없이 덤덤이 있는 남편의 얼굴을 한동안 물끄러미 처다보더니, 안해는 다시 엎드리어 두 팔 사이에다 머리를 파묻고 울기 시작하는 것이다. 그러면서도 토막토막 끊어서 이야기를 한다.
「수첩에 주소 적어둔 게 뭐이 그렇게 잘못이람. 그것두 뭐 적어두래서 적어 뒀나. 자기가 뭐 신경이나 대련이나 그쪽으루 가보겠으니, 어디 그쪽에 아는 사람이 없느냐, 아는 사람이 있으면 가서 좀 찾아보게, 수소문해 달라기에 그것두 간신히 동무들헌테 연줄연줄로 알아서 적어둔 게지. 그럼 애초에 왜 자기가 그쪽으루 가서 취직을 해보구 싶다구 말을 했담...」
안해의 말이 계속되자, 一洙는 과연 그 언젠가 밤자릿 속에서 전보다 훨씬 여웨진 안해의 허리께를 더듬으며, 그리고 지금 자기가 월급이랍시고 매월 타오는 돈이 너무도 초라한 것임을 새삼스럽게 부끄러이 여기며
「젠-장, 나두 신경이나 대련이나 어디 그쪽으로 가서 활동 좀 해봤으면. 그 쪽은 월급생활도 잘만 붙으면 괜찮다든데. 어디 그쪽에 아는 사람 없어?」
이런 소리를 한 기억이 났다.
「올치 그래서 제딴에는 그쪽에 있는 사람을 고른답시고<235> 고른게 그게구나.」
一洙는 비로소 그 주소가 적히게 된 안해의 비밀을 알고, 그리고 또 얼마던지 덤덤해지고 승거워젔다.
안해는 점점 더 기세를 올려가면 울뿐이다. 一洙는 무슨 더러운 거나 집듯, 안해의 머리맡에 펄쳐있는 깜정 표지를 한 수첩을 집어서 저쪽으로 밀어놓고, 그리고 가만히 일어나 방문을 열고 마루로 나왔다.
벌써 뉘엿뉘엿 마당에는 어둠이 고요히 기뜨려 가고 있다. 어디서 날아왔는지 커단 나무닢 하나가 휘익 담을 넘어와 마당 복판에서 딩굴 딩굴 한다.
시계가 느리게 일곱을 친다. 참 저 시계를 빽국시계로 바꿔다 달아야 할텐데, 남편은 이런 생각을 하다가, 문득 아직도 울음소리가 끄치지 않은 방을 향해서
「이거 봐. 인제 고만 저 시계 좀 내다 바꿔오지. 요즘 물건값이 싸저서 빽국시계두 얼마 안헐텐데...」
이런 소리를 하였다. 휘익 휙 휙 바람이 부는 대로 나무닢 하나가 재미나게 왼 마당을 휩쓸고 다니는게 마루 끝에 앉아서 보기에 그리 심심치가 않었다.
(庚辰 9月)<236>
