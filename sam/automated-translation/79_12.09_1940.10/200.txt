**Body Text Translation:**
P'yŏngyang Thirty Years Ago
						Doctor of Laws, Ninagawa Arata

P'yŏngyang at That Time

In April of Meiji 37 [1904], I (余) first set foot on Korean soil. First arriving at Chinnamp'o, I saw the desolate mountains and rivers. Then, I traveled upstream on the clear, wide Taedong River by small steamship. Along both banks, I watched with interest the poor figures of white-clothed Koreans (鮮人) gazing at this novelty,<98> and arrived in P'yŏngyang, where I stayed for several days.

I had heard that P'yŏngyang was just like Kyoto. Thus, I hoped to arrive quickly, but when I actually arrived and saw it, the streets were filled with dirtily dressed Koreans (鮮人), there was no elegance (雅趣) whatsoever, nor was there any sign of prosperity. It merely presented the appearance of a half-dead society, completely different from what I had expected.

However, present-day P'yŏngyang is a splendid capital where commerce thrives. It is entirely different from the P'yŏngyang I saw before. P'yŏngyang at that time was an old, poor, small, undeveloped place. White-clothed Koreans (鮮人) carried petroleum cans on their backs, using them to draw water from the Taedong River and sell it in the city. There was no water supply system, no wells; they drew and drank natural clean water. It was close to a primitive life. Compared to the present, the difference was like that between clouds and mud (雲泥의 差). Moran Hill (牡丹臺) existed, but it was dilapidated.

North-South Imperial Tour (南北幸行)

Resident-General Itō (伊藤統監) planned an unprecedented event called the "North-South Imperial Tour" for the Korean Emperor, setting the itinerary for the imperial procession (行幸) north to Ŭiju and south to Chinhae Bay.<99> He had the so-called civil and military officials of the time follow, and the Korean Emperor undertook his first great journey. I (余), being the Director (理事) of the Palace Ministry's Treasury Department (宮內府 內藏院), was ordered to handle the payment of travel expenses during this grand tour, and so I escorted (扈從) the party.

I (余) do not know what Resident-General Itō's objective was. At train stations along the route, numerous *yangban* dressed in traditional Korean formal attire (禮裝), that is, the hereditary ruling class (司配階級者) of Korea, lined up to offer greetings (御出迎). Many Japanese people also came out to greet the procession. The Korean upper class must have realized how many Japanese had entered all of Korea. In Chinhae, many Japanese warships were anchored. The Emperor and everyone else witnessed firsthand how the cannons on the warships moved freely.

In Taegu, about 20 or 30 *yangban* in Korean attire were lined up at the station. Resident-General Itō had the Ritual Officer (禮武官) named Ko Ŭi-gyo (高義敎)—that person was an excellent gentleman of talent and wisdom—address the lined-up *yangban*, saying, "If there is anyone among you gentlemen who wishes to cut off this Itō's head, speak up." In response, all the *yangban* simply replied, "Yes," and bowed their heads in unison. I (余) saw this as Resident-General Itō's way of eliminating the *yangban*'s courage (膽力).<100>

He tested the wisdom of the late hero from the era of the old Regent Hideyoshi (太閣秀吉), but fortunately, among them, there was no one who would later fire a pistol in Harbin. It seemed such figures no longer existed among the *yangban*. However, I (余) myself always carried the resolve that should anything happen, I would throw myself completely into handling it, but nothing occurred.

At the same place, I (余), along with some subordinate officials (屬官), was carrying the entire sum of money to be paid out, so we separated from the main group and stayed at a Japanese inn. Late at night, for some reason, I felt uneasy and peeked into the next room, where a suspicious (怪常한) Japanese man was lying asleep. I shouted, asking who he was, and woke him up. This person behaved increasingly strangely, making no particular attempt to resist, offer excuses, or flee. So I (余) immediately called a police officer and handed the suspicious man over. Because I (余), along with the subordinate officials, was safeguarding several thousand *won* in cash for the lodging expenses of several hundred officials, I had to be cautious about my person and belongings, as being robbed would result in a great loss. Taegu at that time was also different from the present; it was a meager town, but compared to its size, there were quite a lot of *yangban*.

The North-South Imperial Tour was quite taxing. Unfortunately, I contracted typhus (지부쓰) and suffered from high fever daily in Kyŏngsŏng, becoming somewhat critical at one point, but I recovered soon after. The Imperial Household created and distributed commemorative insignia (徽章) for the tour. Also, silver tableware (銀器) was bestowed (下賜) upon the Palace Ministry officials. This too, today, is just an old story.<101>

**Summary**

Ninagawa Arata recounts his first visit to P'yŏngyang in 1904, contrasting its "desolate," "primitive," and "half-dead" state with its later development under Japanese rule. He then describes his participation in Emperor Sunjong's 1909 North-South Tour orchestrated by Resident-General Itō Hirobumi, handling finances. He reflects on Itō's possible motives, suggesting the tour aimed to impress upon the Korean elite the extent of Japanese presence and military power (warships in Chinhae) and to intimidate them, citing an incident in Taegu where Itō challenged the *yangban* to assassinate him, seemingly to break their spirit. Ninagawa also mentions contracting typhus during the tour. The article offers a Japanese colonial official's perspective on Korea's state before intensified colonization and justification for Japanese intervention, portraying pre-colonial P'yŏngyang negatively and highlighting Itō's calculated actions to assert dominance over the Korean elite. Historians might find the firsthand account of the 1909 tour, particularly the description of Itō's interaction with the *yangban* in Taegu and the author's interpretation of its intent (eliminating their courage), of significant interest for understanding the psychological tactics employed during the protectorate period. The casual description of Koreans (鮮人) and the justification of change also reflect colonial attitudes.

三十年前의 平壤
						法學博士 蜷川新
當時의 平壤

明治 37년 4월 余는 처음으로 朝鮮땅을 밟었다. 爲先 鎭南浦에 도착하여 荒寥한 산하를 보았다. 그리고 大同江의 맑고 넓은 강을 小증기선으로 溯行했다. 兩沿엔 이것을 진기하게 바라보는<98> 白衣의 鮮人의 가난한 자태를 흥미있게 보면서 平壤에 이르러 거기서 수일 머믈렀다.
平壤은 꼭 京都와 같다는 이얘길 들었다. 그래서 빨리 도착하기를 바랐는데 정작 도착하고 본즉 더러운 맵씨를 한 鮮人들이 도로에 꽉차고 하등의 雅趣도 없고 또 그러타고 무슨 번영도 보이지 않었다. 거저 半死의 사회와 같은 觀을 보일 뿐 전혀 예기했든 것과는 달렸다.
하나 現今의 平壤은 상업이 번성한 훌융한 도읍이다. 余가 전에 보든 平壤과는 아주 달르다. 그 당시의 平壤은 낡고 가난한 적은 미개지였다. 白衣의 鮮人은 석유통을 질머지고 그것으로 大同江水를 기려다가 시중에 팔러 단였다. 수도도 없고, 우물도 없고, 자연의 淸水를 기러서 먹었다. 원시적 생활에 갓가웠다. 現今과는 雲泥의 差가 있었다. 牡丹臺가 있긴 했으나 황폐했었다.

南北幸行

伊藤統監은 韓國황제의 「南北行幸」이라는 미증유의 일을 계획해서 北은 義州까지, 南은 鎭海灣까지 사이를 行幸의 여정으로<99> 하고, 당시의 소위 문무백관을 따르게 하여 韓國황제는 처음으로의 大여행을 하섰다. 余는 宮內府 內藏院의 理事라해서 이 大여행 중의 여비의 지불을 命해웠기 때문에 扈從했다.
伊藤統監의 목적은 뭤이였는지 余는 알지 못한다. 沿道 정거장에는 朝鮮고유의 禮裝을 입은 다수의 양반, 즉 朝鮮來의 司配계급자가 정렬하여 御出迎했다. 日本人도 많이 출영했었다. 얼마나 많은 日本人이 全朝鮮에 들어와 있다는 것을 朝鮮의 上流者는 알었으리라. 鎭海엔 日本군함이 많이 投錨해 있었다. 艦上의 대포가 자유자재로 움즉이는 모양을 皇帝를 비롯하여 죄다들 實見했었다.
大邱에선 정거장에 2, 30인의 朝鮮服의 양반이 열을 지어있었는데 伊藤統監은 高義敎이라는 禮武官(그 사람은 才智가 우수한 신사였다), 그 高씨를 시켜서 양반들이 열지어있는 데를 향하여 「諸君 중에 이 伊藤의 목을 베고싶은 사람이 있거든 말해요」한즉 여기에 대하여 全양반은 「네」라 하며 오직 머리를 일제히 숙일 뿐이었다. 伊藤統監은 이렇케 하여 양반의 담력을 없새게하는 것이라고<100> 余는 보았다.
옛날 太閣秀吉시대의 故영웅의 智를 시험해보았는데 그중 그 뒤 할빈에서 피스톨을 發하는 사람이 없은 것은 다행했다. 양반에는 그러한 인물이 벌서 없었든 듯했다. 하지만, 余 자신은 만약 무슨 일이 생긴다면 余 자신을 아주 던저, 이것을 처리하리란 결심은 항상 가지고 있었으나 아모일도 없었다.
同所에서 余는 약간의 屬官과 같이 지불할 일체의 금액을 죄다 가지고 있었슴으로 일행과 떠나서 日本人여관에 숙박했으나 밤이 느저 웬일인지 마음이 이상해서 옆방을 엿본즉 거기엔 괴상한 日本人이 누워자고 있었다. 어떤 자냐고 소리를 지르며 이르키니 이 자는 점점 더 괴상한 행동을 하면서 별로 반항하려고도 하지 않고, 변명도 하지 않고, 피하지도 않었다. 그래서 余는 곳 경찰관을 불러 그 괴상한 자를 맽겼다. 余는 數十百人의 官吏의 宿費지불 때문에 수천원의 현금을 屬官과 함께 보관하고 있었었음으로 만일 도난에 걸닌다면 큰 손해를 보게 됨으로 身上과 함께 주의하지 않으면 안되였다. 당시의 大邱도 현재와 달라서 빈약한 거리였으나 거기에 비하면 양반은 상당히 많었다.
南北行幸은 퍽 마음을 썼다. 불행히 지부쓰에 걸려 京城에서 날마다 고열을 발하게 되여 한 땐 좀 위태했으나 이내 나었다. 皇室에선 行幸기념의 휘장을 맨드러서 분배했다. 또 宮內府관리에겐 銀器를 하사했다. 이것도 오늘에 있어선 한 개의 옛 이얘기다.<101>
