Article Title: 端宗大王 莊陵 參拜記 (English: Record of a Visit to King Tanjong's Royal Tomb, Changnŭng)
Author: 金性睦
Article Type: 기행문 (English: Travelogue)

**Body Text Translation:**
There are so many mountains. Layer upon layer, the mountains get higher as one goes. Looking down from T'aesan Pass, turning bend after bend, mountains block the way. It's already been over a month since entering Kangwŏn Province. Passing through Hoengsŏng, Wŏnju, and P'yŏngch'ang to reach Yŏngwŏl, I feel suffocated every day. How I long for the vast, boundless ocean that opens the heart wide! The mountains of Kangwŏn Province, pressing down on my chest in thousands upon thousands of layers, make my body increasingly weary as I search deeper into them.

Although the majestic mountains, the scenery adorned according to the four seasons, and the serene atmosphere may hold tens of times more value than the admirable waves of the sea, when one gets lost on the rough paths of the deep, layered valleys and asks a young mountain wife for directions, it's truly upsetting when she turns away without answering. The road ahead is distant, and when dusk falls, cold sweat beads like pearls on the forehead, the mouth goes dry, and even the throat burns. Leaving this place and driving over a pass, tall, lush pine trees soared, and beneath them, grasses met the autumn, their yellow leaves fluttering in the wind. Autumn chrysanthemums filled the valley; this was the Yi Royal Household Forestry (李王職山林). To the left of the road was a large tomb (陵); this is the tomb where, some 280 years ago, the unfortunate royal spirit body (御靈體) of King Tanjong was enshrined (安莊).

I combed my hair again and straightened my clothes to pay respects at Changnŭng (莊陵). Guided by Ch'ambong (叅奉) Kim Pok-kyu (金宓圭), I performed a deep bow (重敬拜) before the Hongjŏnmun Gate (紅箭門) of the Royal Tomb's T-shaped Pavilion (御陵丁字閣). As I paid respects at the royal tomb with deep emotion, the silent blue mountains (靑山) seemed to inform me through the rising wind. Limitless longing wandered, unsure where to go, like a flock of sheep without a shepherd – where could they rest? Endless tears gathered in my eyes. Step by step, carefully, I paid homage (拜謁) at the tomb, turned around, descended, bowed in retreat (拜退) before the tomb, and went into the office (事務室). When I asked Kim Ch'ambong about the brief history (畧歷) of King Tanjong, [he explained that] King Tanjong was born (御誕生) on the 23rd day of the 7th month of the Sinyu year, the 23rd year of King Sejong's reign [1441], and ascended the throne (卽位) on the 18th day of the 5th month of the Imsin year [1452]. After reigning for three years, on the 11th day of the intercalary 6th month of the Ŭrhae year [1455], he abdicated (裨位于) to Sejo. He came to Yŏngwŏl and stayed at Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o (淸冷浦), but during the long rains (長霖), the damp peninsula (半島) became severely cold, causing much hardship. Moreover, there was nothing even to eat, so the Hojang (戶長) of Yŏngwŏl county town (寧越邑), Ŏm Hŭng-do (嚴興道), avoiding others' eyes late each night (每夜深頃), brought food to Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o. Due to the severe cold, King Tanjong inevitably <109> came to the Yŏngwŏl Guest House (寧越客舍) and then met his demise (遭變). That time was the 24th day of the 10th month, a time of accumulated snow (積雪之時) [1457]. When one could not distinguish even short distances, he suffered this misfortune. At that time (其時), Hojang Ŏm Hŭng-do carried King Tanjong's remains (遺體) on his back and came here. A mountain deer (山鹿) guided the way, allowing the enshrinement (安莊) here. His ninety-odd retainers (從者) died upholding their loyalty (節死). In the 7th year of King Sukchong's reign [1681], the tomb was restored (復陵) to its present state, I was told. Also, after the office was destroyed by fire (消化 - likely a typo/euphemism for 소화(燒火) or 소실(燒失)) in the 6th year of Shōwa [1931], the reference documents (參考書類) are now gone, they say. Leaving this place and arriving in Yŏngwŏl county town (寧越邑), on the way to find the Post Office (郵便所) first, the bicycle crank axle (自轉車クランク心棒) snapped. Although I had arrived, this happening right upon entering the town, even if not a superstition, left me feeling uneasy. So I left it at the bicycle shop (自車房) and went to the Post Office, but the letter I was waiting for hadn't arrived, so I sent a telegram (電報) to the Head Office (本社) and settled into the Yŏnghŭng Inn (寧興旅館).

Then, after visiting the County Office (郡廳), Police Station (警察署), and Township Office (面所), I had time, so I went to Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o (淸冷浦), located 10 ri to the west.

Seeking Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o
Reaching the riverside, there was an ownerless boat. Poling that boat across to Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o, the first thing that appeared amid the lush, tall pines was the Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o Boundary Marker Stele (淸冷浦禁標 碑). And going inside, it was the place where King Tanjong (端廟大王) used to reside (起寢).
There was only a stele pavilion (碑閣) erected, inscribed 'Site where King Tanjong stayed when in this district' (端廟在本府時 遺址). Sitting on a rock and looking around in all directions (四方), all that could be seen were forests (森林) covering the high riverbanks (川), and all that could be heard were bird songs.
River water (江水) surrounded it on three sides, and one side was a high mountain cliff (高山絶壁). When King Tanjong was staying here, if Ŏm Hŭng-do had not offered provisions (食糧), his suffering would surely have been greater.
Climbing up the western cliff (西便 벼랑), which seemed sheerly cut, I wrote a verse.

Oh, Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o! Did they name you Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o ('Clear and Cold Inlet') because you openly spoke, piece by piece, of our Lord's suffering?
How cold must it have been? How damp must it have been that he left this place?

Our Lord, who used to sit on the royal couch,
bidding farewell to the Palace City (宮城) with tears,
came down the rough mountain path of over four hundred ri (四百餘里)
to this island, those old days!

How heart-wrenching must have been the first night longing for home (望鄕)!
Black clouds (黑雲) gathered in the sky (天空), the sun (太陽) lost its light,
The four classes of people (四民) were weeping, loyal subjects (忠臣) died martyrs (殉死),
Who would rebuild this fatherland?

Even the bright moon (明月) in the mid-sky (半空中天) depended on one's perspective.
Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o's strangely shaped (奇形異態) rocks (岩石) rising from the water's surface (水面),
and the lovely flowers of spring blossoms and autumn maples (春花秋楓), only added to the sorrow of our lonely Lord.

Turning back from this place, I took the boat and returned. On the way back...

To Kŭmgangjŏng Pavilion
With another guest staying at the inn (旅館), we went through the back alleys of the village (洞里), visited the local Confucian school (鄕校), recalling how the youth of old (古代靑年) were educated (敎養), and climbed up to Kŭmgangjŏng Pavilion (錦江亭). Below the sheer cliff (斷崖絶壁) <110>, the clear, blue autumn river water (江水) flows. Kŭmgangjŏng Pavilion, built on the bluff rising as if carved, speaks of past history (녯 歷史). Here I composed a verse, rising from decaying fragments:
Only after waking from the dream does one know it was a dream (몽각연후방지몽 夢覺然後方知夢).
Loyal subjects and devoted martyrs remain by the Kŭmgang River (츙신열사금강유 忠臣烈士錦江留).
Worldly customs value wealth, nobility, and long life (시육부귀생활다 時俗富貴生活多).
The foremost rivers and mountains are in this land (제일강산재차향 第一江山在此鄕).
With whom can I share the scenery of Kŭmgang (금강경개수여공 錦江景槪誰與共)?
Like this lonely pavilion, an old man climbs (여차고누로상부 如此孤樓老上夫).
A piece of loyal heart is entrusted to the flowing water (일장단심긔유수 一片丹心寄流水).
Ten thousand ri of mountains and rivers - when will there be peace (말리산학안하시 萬里山河安何時)?
After writing this.

Climbing to Minch'ungsa Shrine
The door of the crumbling shrine (祠堂) was open, the floorboards were missing and scattered about, and the walls, weathered by wind and rain (風雨), were collapsed and fallen. Going inside, in the center (中央) was the spirit tablet of the Court Ladies (侍女之神位), to the left the spirit tablet of the Attendants (开ž人之神位 - likely 從人之神位), and on one side, only the altar base (床) remained from whoever's spirit tablet (神位) it was. Oh, indifferent and cold-hearted people! Protect the historic remains (遺蹟) of this place.
About one chō (町步) east of here.

Called Nakhwaam (Falling Flower Rock)
From the water's surface (水面), a sheer cliff (懸崖絶壁) tens of chang (丈) high rises steeply. On the 24th day of the 10th month, the 65th year since the founding of the Yi Dynasty [1457], when King Tanjong met his demise (遭變), about 90 court ladies (侍女) and attendants (從人), with unwavering loyalty (一片丹心) and the spirit of 'a loyal subject does not serve two kings' (忠臣은 不使二君), threw themselves into the river from this cliff (絶壁), dying for their principles (投江節死). This is a place of deep sorrow (원한). The white-robed (白衣) loyal subjects (忠臣), some 90 bundles dying for their loyalty (殉節), fell one after another onto the river (江上). Because their fall resembled falling flowers, it was named Nakhwaam (落花岩 - Falling Flower Rock). Where will these fallen flowers eventually be reborn (還生)? Do the spirits of the 90 loyal dead play upon the river (江上)? The autumn river (秋江) in the 9th month sparkles, reflecting the sun (太陽). And do the fish, perhaps consoling the departed souls, leap towards mid-heaven (中天)?
Here I tried composing a verse.
On Kŭmgangjŏng Pavilion (錦江亭), a lone traveler (孤客) rests, bowing his head in thought of the loyal subjects and devoted martyrs (충신렬사) who threw themselves into the river (投江殉死). On the clear water of the 9th month autumn river (秋江), a boatman poles his boat across, seemingly unaware of the court ladies (侍女) and attendants (從人) who fell, chattering thoughtlessly. Autumn leaves (단풍닙), touched by the autumn wind, flutter down from mid-heaven (中天). Indeed, this place called Nakhwaam (洛花岩) is truly so.
Turning back from here, I rested once more at Kŭmgangjŏng and looked at a writing composed by Master T'oehan (退漢先生任 - likely Yi Hwang, T'oegye).
Cuckoo cries tear the mountains near the capital year after year (鵑啼山裂畿窮年).
That the river shares its name with Shu waters is no coincidence (蜀水名同非偶然).
Faint light on morning eaves greets the ocean sunrise (明滅曉簷迎海旭).
Whistling winds through evening doors sweep autumn mists (飄蕭晩互掃秋烟).
Green pools stir with maples, fish swim like brocade (碧潭楓動魚遊錦).
Blue cliffs birth clouds, cranes tread on carpets (靑壁雲生鶴踏氈).
Again I promise a Taoist friend to bring an iron flute (更約道人携鐵笛).
To come and blow, shattering the old dragon's sleep (爲來吹破老龍眠).
I came to record these lines here.
Next, I sought out Changjŏl Academy (彰節書院) at the western foot (西麓) of Maebong Mountain (鷹峯山), about six chō (六町許) northwest. This is the place where the spirit tablets (神位) of loyal subjects (忠臣) from the court of the sixth king of the Yi Dynasty (李朝), King Tanjong, are enshrined. There, ten loyal subjects (10忠臣) including Sŏng Sam-mun (成三問), Pak P'aeng-nyŏn (朴彭年), Yi Kae (李塏), Yu Sŏng-wŏn (柳誠源), Ha Wi-ji (河*地 - likely 河緯地), Yu Ŭng-bu (兪應孚), Ŏm Hŭng-do (嚴興道), Kim Si-sŭp (金時習), Nam Hyo-on (南孝溫), and Pak Sim-mun (朴審問) are venerated together (列享). Next, although I saw Yŏngmojŏn Hall (永慕殿), where the portrait (御像) of King Tanjong is enshrined and venerated (奉安信仰), and Chagyuru Pavilion (子規樓), where the King would come out alone late at night (深夜) and shed tears, [I heard about] Ŏrayŏn (魚羅淵), a broad and flat pool (廣濶平舖한 淵) located about 30 ri upstream on the Kŭmgang River. There is Sŏnam Rock (仙岩) there, and in the deep water (深水中), schools of fish (魚軍) play freely. In the fine seasons (好時節) of spring blossoms and autumn maples (春花秋楓), the mountain scenery (山景) along the riverbanks (江岸) resembles a surrounding brocade screen (錦屛), with the art of Great Nature (大自然) immersed there – truly a superb view (可謂絶景). <111>

**Summary**

The author recounts a journey through the mountainous Kangwŏn Province to Yŏngwŏl, focusing on visiting sites related to the tragic King Tanjong. He describes the oppressive mountains, the solemn atmosphere of Changnŭng (Tanjong's tomb), the desolate beauty of Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o where Tanjong was exiled, and other historical spots like Kŭmgangjŏng, Minch'ungsa Shrine, Nakhwaam (where loyalists committed suicide), and Changjŏl Academy. The author expresses deep sympathy for Tanjong and admiration for the loyal subjects, reflecting on history, loyalty, and the landscape's connection to these events through prose and poetry. From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its focus on pre-colonial Korean history and figures, particularly the tragic narrative of King Tanjong and loyalty, themes that could resonate with feelings of loss and yearning for sovereignty under Japanese rule. The mention of the Yi Royal Household Forestry (李王職山林) and the destruction of records in the office (possibly related to administration under Japanese rule) are subtle markers of the colonial context, while the author's deep emotional engagement with Joseon history highlights the persistence of Korean historical consciousness during this period.

端宗大王 莊陵 參拜記
						金性睦
山도 만키도 함니다. 重重疊疊하야 갈스록 높은 산이요 泰山 嶺을 굽어 발바 구비를 돌아 갈스록 山岳이 앞을 막슴니다. 江原道에 드러선지 발서 1개餘月에 橫城, 原州, 平昌을 거처 寧越을 오기까지에 매일 답답함을 느끼게 됨니다. 胸襟이 濶然開하는 茫茫無涯한 大洋이 위그리 그리운지요? 千첩萬첩으로 가슴을 누루는 江原道山岳 차저 드러갈사록 몸이 더욱 괴롭슴니다.
山이 雄大하고 四季마다 때를 따라 꾸며지는 風景과 沈着한 맛은 흠상쓰르운 바다의 波濤보다 몃십배의 價値가 잇겟지만 疊疊幽谷 길 험한대 길이나 잘못들 때에 산골 새악씨에게 무러면 대답도 아니하고 도라서는 대는 참말노 속이 상함니다. 갈 길은 아득한대 날은 日暮하면 니마에는 식은땀이 구슬같이 허르고 입에는 침이 말나 咽喉까지 바싹바싹 타드러 가지요. 이곳을 떠나 차를 달녀 한 고개를 넘어서니 낙낙장송 큰 나무가 욱어지고 그 밋헤는 풀들이 가을을 맛나 누른 닙이 바람에 팔낭팔낭 하더이다. 가을 菊花는 골작에 가득한대 이곳이 李王職山林 이엿나이다. 길 좌편에 큰 陵이 잇스니 距今 280여년 전에 不遇에 端宗大王御靈體를 이곳에 安莊한 陵임니다.
머리를 다시 빗고 의복을 단정히 하여 莊陵을 參拜케 되엿나이다. 叅奉 金宓圭氏의 안내를 밧아 御陵丁字閣 紅箭門앞헤서 重敬拜하고 감개무량의 御陵을 叅拜함에 말업슨 靑山은 바람일켜 알외주고 太限업는 그리에는 갈길몰나 방황하니 목자업는 양의 무리 어대가서 깃더릴가? 하염업는 내눈에는 눈물이 고이더이다. 일보일보 조심하야 陵을 拜謁하고 도라서서 내려와 陵前에서 拜退하고 事務室에 더러 갓나이다. 端宗大王의 畧歷을 金叅奉에게 무럼에 端宗大王이 世宗23년 辛酉 7월 23일에 御誕生하시고 壬申 5월 18일에 卽位하섯는대 在位3년 乙亥閏 6월 11일에 裨位于 世祖하시고 寧越에 오시사 淸冷浦에 게시다가 長霖에 濕한 半島에 냉기가 심하여서 퍽이나 골난 할 뿐아니라 잡수실 것조차 업서셔 寧越邑 戶長嚴興道가 每夜深頃에 남의 눈을 피하야 잡수실 것을 淸冷浦에 갓다 올녀섯다. 냉기가 심한 관계로 不得己 宗端大王은＜109＞ 寧越客舍에 오섯다가 그만 遭變하섯다. 그 때는 積雪之時인 10月24日이다. 지척을 분별치 못할때 이런 변을 당하섯고 其時는 戶長嚴興道는 端宗大王의 遺體를 등에 업고 이곳을 오니 一匹의 山鹿이 길을 인도하야 주어서 이곳에 安莊한 바 되고 從者 90여인은 節死하엿든바 肅宗 7년에 復陵하야 현재 보는 바와 갓치 된 것이라 하더이다. 그리고 昭和 6년도에 사무실을 消化식히고 나서 지금은 참考書類가 업서젓다 함니다. 이곳을 떠나 寧越邑에 니르러 제일먼저 郵便所를 차저가든 도중에 自轉車クランク心棒가 둑부러저서 오기는 다 왓스나 邑에 드러서자 마자 이러케 되니 미신은 아니일망정 긔분이 조치 못하더이다. 그래서 自車房에 맛기고 郵便所에 가니 기달닊�든 편지가 아니와서 本社에 電報를 치고 寧興旅館에 주인을 정햇슴니다.
그리고 郡廳과 警察署와 面所에 가서 단겨 나온 후 시간이 잇기에 西으로 10里所되는 淸冷浦에로 갓슴니다.

淸冷浦를 차저
강변에 니르니 임자업는 배 한척이 잇난지라 그 배를 산대하야 淸冷浦로 건너가니 낙낙장송 욱어진 곳에 우선 나타나는 것이 淸冷浦禁標 碑이다. 그리고 드러가니 端廟大王이 起寢하시든 그곳이다.
端廟在本府時 遺址라고 쓴 碑閣을 세워둘 뿐 바위우에 안저서 四方을 살펴보니 보이는 것은 모다 놉흔 川에 욱어진 森林이요 들니는 것은 새소리 더이다.
三面으로 둘녀싸인 江水 일면은 高山絶壁이라. 이곳에 端宗大王이 게섯슬때 만약 嚴興道가 食糧을 공양치 안이 하얏든들 더욱 고생하섯슬 것이 엿슬 것임니다.
깍가 저른듯한 西便 벼랑우에 올나가 한귀의 글을 쓴나이다.
