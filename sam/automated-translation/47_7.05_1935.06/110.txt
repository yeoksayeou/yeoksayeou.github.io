A Theory on Friendship Between the Sexes: Record of a Beautiful Brother-Sister Relationship
						Na Hye-sŏk

Although unlearned, Y had not only experienced many hardships early on but also had a naturally affectionate and valiant character, understanding others' adversities well. Due to her life circumstances, she inevitably lived calculatingly, yet she couldn't help but respect the character of R, who was benevolent, virtuous, and adept at philosophical discourse. R and Y had developed affection through over 20 years of friendship; R was the one who understood Y's life struggles well, and Y was the one who pitied the adversity R was currently facing. Therefore, when their thoughts aligned and discussions matched, even a single word of help when they felt frustrated brought great comfort. When R had rented a room in a certain dormitory, Y came to visit.

"Hey Y, the dormitory students have apparently formed an alliance to not allow outsiders."
"That's not good. What should we do then? The room across from ours is vacant, why don't you come over there?"
"Should I? I'll pay for my meals from here."
"Let's try that."
I abruptly moved to her room across the way in OO-dong.

A certain handsome young man often came to visit her house. One day, he came again. Y had already introduced his character to me, and she had also spoken of me to him.

"R, come over to the main room."
"Why?"
"Just come here."
R went across.

"Please greet this gentleman." <97>
"I am S."
"I am R. Please favor me with your affection."
"I hear you are XXX. Please take care of me, just in case you don't know."
"Thank you."
"Whereabouts is your office?"
"I'm currently in the process of registering the business, so I'm still at the OO Inn."
"OO Inn? That's near my house."
"Yes, that's right. I often pass by your house."
"By the way, R, please arrange a match for me."
Y looks at R.
"Why? Don't you have a wife yet?"
"She caught the prevailing disease."
Y retorts sarcastically.
"So you got divorced, I see."
"That's right."
"Why did you do that? Is there anyone special out there? The person you have affection for is the best."
"I asked you to arrange a match, but you're saying other things."
The handsome man, appearing to be around 34 or 35, sat slightly tilted, smiling sweetly, and spoke in a tone dripping with affection.
"Perhaps your ideals for a partner are high."
"Not at all. A woman just needs to obey her husband well (男便), that's all."
R and Y couldn't help but be surprised that his answer was unexpectedly conventional. R thought,
"He doesn't seem like the type... perhaps this person has really tasted the bitterness of the world,"
and her curiosity was piqued. Y continued,
"In this world that respects individuality, where would you find a woman who obeys unconditionally? Isn't that right, R?"
"That's why I'm saying such women are scarce."
"Then it's not such a difficult problem. If you go to the countryside, there are plenty of such women. It's a bit difficult with city women."
"Actually, there needs to be such a woman in the city too. Country women are unconsciously submissive, but there needs to be a woman who consciously submits to a man. That is individuality, a person, a woman."
"Then why demand such a person only from women?"
"Men, of course, must also become people who act consciously. However, because men experience much mental turmoil trying to make money, so to speak, they must find concentration at home. The wife shouldn't express her opinions this way and that. She must show absolute obedience."
"Oh my, how frightening. Feudalistic thinking is still alive and well."
"There's no need to talk about this ideology or that ideology; how people live is the same now as it was then."
"Well, that may be so, but..."
"I'll be going now. I'm always there in the evenings, so please come visit." <98>
The cheerful man stood up and left. R became needlessly curious.

One evening, after dinner, Y said to R,
"Shall we go out somewhere?"
"Where shall we go?"
"Here, to the OO Inn."
"To whom?"
"To a certain handsome man. Don't lose your head when you see him again."
"We'll see about that."
We went to the OO Inn to find S. He happened to be eating dinner. With a pleased look, he said,
"Please come in. You've come at a good time, I was just feeling lonely. Have you had dinner?"
"Yes, we've eaten. Please go ahead and eat."
"Then please excuse me."
The dinner table was cleared. R said,
"Please introduce yourselves."
"I am K."
"I am S."
K, charmed by S's amiable responses, despite it being their first meeting, acted overly familiar and spoke tactlessly with pointless words. In any case, the atmosphere became lively, which was nice. S responded well nonetheless.

After chatting for a while, we left. A few days later, S came looking for R, visited briefly, and mentioned that he would be moving to OO-dong, Number OO the next day, opening his office there, and asked her to visit often. One day, R bought some fruit and went to visit S. The large house was quiet, and S was alone in a large room. He greeted her warmly with a handshake. With an affectionate look in his eyes, he said,
"淋しいでしよう. (You must be lonely.)"
"淋しいのが 面白い. (Being lonely is interesting.)"
"そうですかね. (Is that so?)"
"淋しいのが 樂しくなつて來た. (Being lonely has become enjoyable.)"
"偉いてとを 云つているね. (You're saying something profound.)"
"偉いでしよう. (It is profound, isn't it?)"
"女の王國は 矢張り 家庭ですな. (A woman's kingdom is, after all, the home, isn't it?)"
"それは 考へよろによりますな. (That depends on how you think about it.)"
"い* 矢張り 家庭から 離れゐと不幸ですな. (No, after all, being separated from the home is unfortunate, isn't it?)" (*Illegible character*)
"幸福かも知らないよ. (Maybe it's happiness, you don't know.)"
"それは 所謂 *せがまんですな. (That's so-called *stubbornness, isn't it?)" (*Illegible character*)
"い*喜樂です. (No, it's joy and sorrow.)" (*Illegible character*)
"ろそでしよろ. (Is that a lie?)" (*Likely うそでしょう*)
"本當です. (It's true.)"
"矢張り人間は 人間ですからな. (After all, humans are humans.)"
"たから幸福も人間が味ろてとでありますし不幸も人間が味ふものです. (Therefore, happiness is something humans taste, and unhappiness is also something humans taste.)" (*味ろてと -> 味わうことで*)
"ハヽヽヽヽ. (Ha ha ha ha ha.)" <99>
"其点考へれぱ獨身生活も丈夫であゐか疑問ですな. (Considering that point, it's doubtful whether single life is robust.)" (*丈夫であゐか -> 丈夫であるか*)
"どろです あなたわ矢張り淋しみを感じられますか. (How about you? Do you, after all, feel loneliness?)" (*どろです -> どうです*, *あなたわ -> あなたは*, *感じられゐ -> 感じられる*)
"感じられゐ云ふよりも 그냥두고 볼 수 업는 것 갓하야 엽헤서 못견대게 구는구려. (Rather than saying I feel it, it seems people around me can't just leave it alone and nag me unbearably.)" (*感じられゐ云ふ -> 感じられると言う*)
"Why? Are matchmakers coming and going?"
"They're forcing me so much, it's annoying having to answer them, I can't stand it."
"Absolutely ridiculous, leaving a handsome man like you alone."
"Professor R must be getting quite bothered too."
"Not at all, that's spoken without knowing the Chosŏn social system. A man can marry ten times and still be a new groom, but if a woman is rejected just once, she's considered used goods (흔게집). So, many people look for a new groom, but nobody looks for used goods."
"That's why being a man is better."
"I don't want to be a man."
"Why?"
"What's so good about being so cheap? There should be a sense of dignity (尊貴) like a woman has, don't you think?"
"Ha... hee..."
"Eloquent, truly eloquent."
"Should I become an XXX too?"
"Indeed, Chosŏn will eventually have female XXXs."
"Women will end up doing everything men do."
"That's the worry."
"Why?"
"Because women are running around like men, the home loses its composure."
"But that's a product of civilization."
"If each individual were self-aware, it wouldn't be like that."
"Exactly. That's why in America (亞米利加), with its short history of civilization, society, family, and individuals are all restless (ウカウカ-), but in Europe (歐羅巴), with its long history of civilization, society is tense, yet families and individuals are deeply composed. It's a matter of time."
"Indeed, someday I must leisurely listen to your impressions from your tour of Europe and America."
"I've squeezed everything out through writing and speaking, there's nothing left. And going there feels just like a dream."
The air in the room grew a bit heavy.
"The weather is warm. Let's go for a walk to Ch'ŏngnyangni."
S picked up his hat and stood up. R also stood up.

In front of Pagoda Park, they boarded a streetcar bound for Tongdaemun.
On a clear, blue, and high late spring afternoon, the air in Ch'ŏngnyangni was refreshing. Through the woods, the University Preparatory School (大學豫科) building and residences could be seen.
S, who had been silent, said,
"It would be nice to live in a place like that, with the stability of a university professor's life."
"Indeed, it would. Like leaving the mundane world (娑婆世界)."
The two walked endlessly until they reached a dead end, then crossed the adjacent mountain <100> and climbed to the peak behind the monks' quarters. There, the view opened up, and a cool breeze blew in. The two wiped the sweat from their foreheads and took deep breaths.
"Ah, it's so refreshing. When will my heart ever feel this clear?"
R said this, standing blankly and gazing at the distant mountains.
"It's truly a nice place."
"Indeed."
R holds out her hand. S grasps both her hands firmly.
"What can I do to satisfy R?"
"Not at all. Thank you. If we maintain this friendship, I'll be satisfied."
"What's difficult about that?"
"That's right."
A lonely atmosphere settled between the two.
"Shall we go down?"
"Let's go down, buy some food, and then leave."
They entered the house where elderly nuns and young novice nuns lived.
"Please prepare two dinner sets quickly. We're hungry."
"Certainly. Shall I prepare drinks as well?"
"We don't drink alcohol, so please don't bother."
"Elderly nun (老시님), how old are you now?"
As there wasn't much to say between S and R, S asked the nun.
"I'm seventy."
"You look very vigorous."
"The day the Buddha calls me is not far off."
"Not at all. You're still so vigorous."
"It's just because I force myself to move."
"By the way, nun, at what age did you become a nun?"
"At twenty-seven."
"Why did you become a nun?"
"Because I became a widow."
"If you became a widow, why didn't you remarry?"
"If I remarried, I would be estranged from both my family and my husband's family. Since becoming a nun, I can come and go freely."
S and R nodded. Her view on life seemed to have its own logic. S asked again,
"Do you freely visit your family and your late husband's family?"
"Of course. What is there to be ashamed of? Once you enter another family's household, how can you hold your head high and visit?"
"That sounds plausible."
"You have no children, of course?"
"I had one son, but he died."
"In any case, you must have suffered much heartache."
"Not really. It's all due to fate (팔자소간)."
"How do you make a living?" <101>
"With the bit of land I have, and by occasionally preparing meals for visitors, it's just enough to get by."
"Did you bring the land with you when you renounced the world?"
"Who would give land to someone becoming a nun? It's all inheritance from our deceased head nun."
"Then who inherits from you, nun? Shall I come and do it?"
R, who had been listening quietly, asks.
"Absolutely not. When there's such a wonderful husband."
S and R looked at each other and laughed.
"Who is that nun?"
"She's my cousin."
"Unlike others, you must rely on each other."
"That's right. We pity each other."
"Is she the nun's heir (상제)?"
"Yes, but she's still young, so I worry about what will happen when I die."
A nun appearing to be fifteen or sixteen years old was going in and out of the kitchen.
"I offered to come and inherit after you pass away, nun, but that seems unlikely now." R says, laughing.
"Avalokiteshvara Bodhisattva, Namu Amitabha Buddha," the elderly nun mutters as she goes into the kitchen.
"The meal is ready, shall I bring it in?"
"Yes, please bring it in."
They seasoned dried gourds, added crushed bonnet bellflower roots (투각), bracken (고비나물), and bellflower root (도라지나물) side dishes, and mixed it deliciously with tofu stew (두부 소전골) broth. The two ate affectionately, and R nudged S, gesturing with her eyes and laughing as they saw the face of the heir novice girl peeking through the gap in the sliding door of the upper room.

The dinner table was cleared. They paid for the meal and stood up.
"Please come again," said the two elderly nuns.
"Goodbye," said the young nun, her voice trembling and her face flushed. S and R looked back towards them as they crossed the mountain ridge. The setting sun was slowly sinking, and the electric lights were just beginning to become visible. The two had many fragmented conversations. They boarded the streetcar at Ch'ŏngnyangni Station and parted ways inside the streetcar with a promise to meet again soon. Thereafter, one day, the office boy from Lawyer S's office delivered a letter. It was in S's handwriting.

"The other day was very pleasant. I am now sick in bed with a cold. If you have nothing special to do, please come visit."
Sincerely, S. (卽日 S拜上)

R changed her clothes and went with the office boy leading the way. S was indeed lying down on a pallet in a side room (leaving the large room empty).
"I apologize for asking a painter and writer of Chosŏn's first rank, no less, to come."
"Why are you always so sarcastic (ヒニク)?"
"How I wanted to see you." S tightly grasped R's hand. R touched S's forehead. <102>
"Oh my goodness, you have a high fever."
"R, I'm going to die."
He suddenly plays the victim.
"Don't die."
"If I die, I have no regrets, but R will become even lonelier, so that's unfortunate."
"Thank you. Who would think of me like that?"
"If dying were so easy, everyone would do it, and I would have died long ago. They say dying is harder than living."
R placed S's head on her lap. S put his arms around R with both hands, his breathing grew heavy, and he panted. S also panted. The two were silent for a moment.
"Hey, let's lie down over here."
R adjusted the pillow and placed S's head on it.
"Hey, let's have a kiss."
"No. When you're sick, such words and thoughts are forbidden."
"R, you're too rigid (カタイ)."
"It's not that I'm rigid, it's that I must be rigid."
"Why is that?"
"So that our friendship can continue and our relationship can be lasting (永續的). By the way, if there's a suitable person, why don't you marry quickly? What will you do lying sick alone like this?"
"What about R?"
"Don't worry about me."
"If I get married, won't R become even lonelier?"
"Thank you, but if two people cannot marry, it's best if one resolves the matter sooner."
"What prevents us two from marrying?"
"Because I am not someone who can absolutely obey the man who becomes my husband."
"That's the right word. A woman with a strong personality like R, I wouldn't be able to handle her. Let me be honest, R."
"Yes. That's why I dislike it all."
"But as a female friend, there are few who can understand me as well as R."
"Let's be best friends (빼스트 푸랜드) forever, okay?"
The two shook hands firmly.
"By the way, R, Y introduced M, do you know M well too?"
"Of course, I know her."
"What kind of person is she?"
"She's suitable." (관게치 안치 - literally 'not related', context implies suitability)
"How is she suitable?"
"She is suitable as the kind of woman you desire."
"Is that really so?"
"Absolutely. But look, although I urged you to get married, <103>
your financial foundation (경제 긔초) isn't established yet, what will you do if you just increase the number of mouths to feed?"
"Yes, you understand my situation well. But it's so bothersome, I can hardly stand it."
"That's also true. It might be good to settle (カタツケル) that matter quickly."
"If M is decent enough, I might just do it."
"Try dating her."
"I think I'll try that."
After that, for several months, neither visited the other. R was being considerate (エンりヨ) so as not to interfere with his marriage issue. S was too busy enjoying himself with M to have time to see R.

One day, the postman delivered a Western-style envelope to R. It was an invitation announcing that S and M would hold their wedding ceremony at a certain place on a certain date. R held it and stood for a while, gazing at the distant mountains.

They are living a life sweet as honey. A year after marrying, they had a son like a chubby dumpling (떡둑게비). R bought traditional baby socks (타래버선) and went to congratulate them. Whenever R visited, S and his wife greeted her gladly. S also, if passing by, would sometimes intentionally visit R, and R too, when occasionally short of funds, would go to S to ask to borrow some money. They continued to associate closely like this.

R was living together with Y's sister-in-law when Y's father-in-law passed away. S came to offer condolences. After paying respects, he came up to the second floor to Y's place. S said,
"It's chaotic here, let's go for a walk."
"Let's. Where shall we go?"
"Let's go eat at a temple."
"Let's go."
The two boarded the Tongdaemun-bound streetcar and got off at the terminus. S walked ahead, and R followed behind, passing a quarry (採石場), crossing a small mountain ridge, heading towards the Tapgol monks' quarters.
"This is my first time taking this path."
"Is that so?"
"How do you know the way so well then?"
"I stayed here for two months before."
The two emerged onto an open mountain ridge.
"Ah, it's truly refreshing. The air is so good."
"Indeed it is."
"By the way, will you take on a case for me?"
"What case?"
"To vent my anger on C."
"You've only just realized now. It's late, too late."
"He's being too cruel, it makes me want to resist."
"Of course. It's something worth trying."
"Will you do your best (ツクスして) for me?"

Thus, although infinite pain and infinite conflict were repeated between the two, still, when apart they missed each other, felt affection, were happy to meet, and had much to talk about. How many times did boiling blood and beating hearts threaten to ruin that friendship, yet they still get along as beautiful siblings. Not only in the past and present, but it will likely be so in the future as well.
R says these words.
Losing trust with one man is losing trust with all men. I intend to forge a new life while faithfully preserving old friendships.
The new sheds the old skin as it sprouts. The new seed existed within the old husk. Therefore, it rises using the old husk as a stepping stone. My beloved older brother (옵바야), who knows if you might become the stepping stone for my new life? <74>

**Summary**

The article, penned by Na Hye-sŏk under the guise of a fictional narrative about 'R' (likely herself) and 'S', explores the complexities of platonic friendship between a man and a woman in 1930s colonial Korea. R, an independent-minded female intellectual and artist, navigates a close but non-romantic relationship with S, a handsome, recently divorced man with traditional views on marriage. Despite mutual affection, intellectual connection, and moments of emotional and physical tension, they consciously maintain their bond as "best friends" or "beautiful siblings," acknowledging their incompatibility for marriage due to differing ideals (R's strong individuality vs. S's desire for an obedient wife). The story follows their interactions, S's eventual remarriage, and the enduring nature of their friendship, concluding with R's reflection on using past relationships, perhaps including the one with S, as a foundation for her "new life."

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its portrayal of a modern woman's perspective on relationships, independence, and societal constraints in colonial Korea. Na Hye-sŏk's thinly veiled autobiographical elements offer insights into the challenges faced by educated women questioning traditional gender roles. The explicit discussion of divorce, female individuality versus male expectations of submission, the comparison of Korean society to America and Europe, and the frank depiction of emotional intimacy and boundaries in a cross-gender friendship provide valuable glimpses into the social and intellectual currents of the era, particularly concerning the evolving status and self-perception of 'New Women'.
