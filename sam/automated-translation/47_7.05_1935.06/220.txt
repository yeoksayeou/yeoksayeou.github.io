**Sŏn Sunhyo's Three Cups of Wine**
Kim Tongin

It was during the reign of King Sŏngjong—.
King Sejo's beloved grandson, who, at the age of nine, while sitting with his father and several other royal grandsons, remained calm amidst a sudden, tremendous clap of thunder while all the other royal grandsons were startled and didn't know what to do—such was the wise King Sŏngjong, successor to the throne.

After the Yi Chosŏn dynasty experienced the civil administration of King Sejong and the military might of King Sejo, it was now firmly established. By the reign of King Sŏngjong, <154> it had become as solid as bedrock. Enthroned as the king of Chosŏn in this era of peace, King Sŏngjong (though he faced some domestic unpleasantness due to issues with the queen) spent a life full of harmony as a monarch of a prosperous age.
Among the officials King Sŏngjong greatly cherished was Sŏn Sunhyo, who held the position of Ch'ansŏng Kyŏm T'aehaksa (贊成兼太學士, Second State Councilor and Head Tutor of the Royal Academy). Like a scholar of a peaceful era, he excelled in poetry and music, and on top of that, he enjoyed drinking alcohol.
The king, deeply fond of Sŏn Sunhyo, worried about his excessive drinking (過酒) and often advised him, "When you drink, do not exceed three cups." Sunhyo would reply, "How could I disobey Your Majesty's instruction?" and would vow not to exceed three cups.

One day, the king entered the council hall and was reviewing diplomatic documents submitted by the Sŭngmunwŏn (承文院, Office of Diplomatic Correspondence). Finding a certain official message (표문) unsatisfactory, he wished to have it rewritten and urgently summoned Sŏn Sunhyo.
However, the messenger who rushed to Sunhyo's house returned without him. He reported that he had searched all likely places but could not find him. The king sent another messenger. And another. Messengers went again and again—ten men were sent, but none could bring Sunhyo back.
Each time a messenger returned, the king would even rise from his throne in anticipation, but the awaited Sunhyo did not appear, only the messenger returning empty-handed.
Thus, messengers went back and forth about ten times—only around dusk did Sunhyo finally enter the palace. By this time, however, the king was extremely displeased because of Sunhyo.
The king's mood was already foul, but seeing Sunhyo's appearance—his official attire disheveled, his face flushed with alcohol, his gait unsteady as he stumbled in and performed a sloppy bow—the king could no longer contain his displeasure.
"Your Majesty, upon your command, your servant has arrived."
As Sunhyo spoke with slurred words, the king looked down at him for a moment with displeasure and asked where he had been, having sent messengers multiple times.
"Your servant's daughter is married and I hadn't seen her for a long time. Today, passing by, I happened to stop by briefly, and time passed while I was there."
This was Sunhyo's excuse.
"I have strictly told you before not to exceed three cups of wine. What is the meaning of this state today?"
"How could your servant disobey Your Majesty's instruction? I did not exceed three cups (三盃酒)." <155>
"Three cups? You get this drunk on just three cups?"
"Yes, Your Majesty. Just three cups. Just three... bowls (주발)."
It couldn't be helped. Relying on the king's affection (애총 愛寵), he was acting spoiled before the throne.
No matter how great a scholar and skilled in poetry he was, being this intoxicated, he couldn't be entrusted with drafting the official message (하표문). So the king summoned the deputy head tutor (부제학) to have them draft it together, but Sŏn Sunhyo refused.
"I shall do it alone. No matter how drunk your servant is, surely I can manage my duties?"
He insisted.
The king, fond of Sunhyo, decided to test his ability and granted permission. Sunhyo shook his drunken head this way and that a few times, then declared he was finished and asked for paper and brush.
No matter how skilled his calligraphy, how could drunken writing (취필 醉筆) possibly suffice? The king intended to summon a scribe (사서 寫書), but this drunkard stubbornly refused, requesting to write it himself. So, the king, as a test, ordered an inkstone and brushes (필연 筆硯) to be brought. Sunhyo picked out several brushes, squinted his drunken eyes repeatedly, tried writing on his palm, then threw them all down, complaining they were all unusable—a drunkard's fuss over brushes.
By royal command, the king's personal writing set (어필연 御筆硯) was brought out again. He selected one brush from it and said,
"This brush will barely do. However, as your servant is old and it is difficult to bend my back, please provide a desk."
Another complaint.
A desk was provided. Kneeling before it, Sunhyo squinted repeatedly as if focusing his eyes, drafted the text, reread it once, and presented it to the king.
The king took it and read. As he read down the lines, one, two, an expression of delight gradually appeared on his royal face (용안 龍顔).
Whether in composition or calligraphy, it was a splendid official message with nothing to fault. Although he had ordered it as a test, this was completely unexpected.
The king sent Sunhyo's drunkenly composed message directly to the Sŭngmunwŏn (承文院) to be dispatched. He then ordered the Sayongwŏn (司甕院, Office for Royal Liquors) to hold a grand banquet and gave Sunhyo enough wine to become thoroughly drunk (대취하도록).

Soon, when Sunhyo was so drunk he seemed senseless, the king asked him again if he could still compose adequately. To this inquiry (하문 下問), Sunhyo immediately replied that he would do as commanded. The king then gave the poetic theme of Chang Liang (張良) and the rhyme character 'chung' (中). As soon as the rhyme character left the king's lips, Sunhyo instantly recited in a drunken yet clear voice:
"奇謀不售 浪沙中 (His brilliant scheme failed amidst the treacherous sands)"
Next, the king called out the character 'kong' (公) <156>
"杖劒的來 相沛公 (Sword in hand, he came to serve the Duke of P'ei)"
He responded immediately. Thus, following this pattern, as each rhyme character was given, a poem was instantly formed:

借筯己能 成漢業 (Borrowing chopsticks, he could already shape the Han enterprise)
令弟却自讓齊封 (His younger brother, however, yielded the fief of Qi)

平生智略 傳黃石 (His lifelong strategies were passed down from Huangshi)
末政功名付赤松 (His final political achievements entrusted to Chisong)

堪恨韓彭竟菹醞 (Regrettable that Han and Peng ended up pickled)
功成身退是英雄 (Achieving success and retiring—that is a true hero)

"Truly, the older the ginger, the spicier it gets (老將益壯)."
The king, who had intended to test him, was finally filled with admiration.
Greatly pleased, the king summoned court ladies to play the pipa and sing, ordering Sunhyo to rise and dance. However, Sunhyo was too drunk to even stand up and instead collapsed on the spot. The king personally took off the robe (의대) he was wearing, covered his beloved official (시하) with it, and retired to the inner chambers (내전).

After the founding by King T'aejo, wise rulers (명군) followed one after another, the country flourished beautifully and prosperously, and the factional strife that would later ruin this nation had not yet become severe. In those times, the ruler and his officials spent such joyful and harmonious days together.
(From Osan Sŏllim Ch'ogo 五山說林草藁)

**Aryang Pavilion and the White Butterfly**
Yun Paengnam

What was utterly astonishing was that every new magistrate (府使) appointed would turn up dead the very next morning. Indeed, the entire city of Miryang (密陽) was gripped by great terror due to this unseen, dark hand of the devil.
People's minds were in turmoil. Rumors spread quickly. <157>

After the magistrate Yun Ch'unam (尹春菴) (Ch'unam was likely his pen name (雅號); this happened during King Sejong's (世宗) reign) moved out of the inner office (내아 內衙), every new magistrate appointed to this county would invariably perish on the night of their arrival. Among them was even the eldest son of Chief State Councilor Ch'oe (崔政承), newly appointed from Seoul, who also died silently on his first night.
Come and die, come and die—
This had happened six times already. Was it an assassin with a hidden dagger taking the new official's life without a trace? Or was it a ghost, riding the wind by day and the stars by night, secretly wandering and causing these tragedies? In any case, it was a bizarre incident (괴기한 사번). On the shaman's advice that goblins were attached to the three old apricot trees lined up before the main hall of the county office (동헌대청 東軒大廳), the magistrate ordered servants (사령) to chop them down with axes (독긔). Yet, the magistrates still perished upon arrival. This terrifying rumor spread like waves across the sea, reaching all of Chosŏn. As officials kept dying so frequently, the royal court eventually found it impossible to find anyone willing to go as the Miryang magistrate.
Anyone asked to go to "Miryang Magistrate" would shake their head.
At this time, there lived a poor scholar named An Chŏngsun (安正淳), sipping cold water and picking his teeth, dwelling shabbily day and night in a corner of Seoul's Southern Village (남촌), seeking only the Way of Confucius and Mencius (孔孟之道). Although living amongst poor scholars in the Southern Village corner, having aimed for the military examination (武科) in his youth, he possessed considerable courage (담력 膽力).
"My dear wife, I shall volunteer to be the Miryang magistrate."
"Goodness gracious, what are you saying? You want to go where every appointee turns up dead?"
"Will everyone who goes necessarily die? Even if I were to die, wouldn't it be better than living so wretchedly like this? At least posterity will remember my name as a magistrate."
Thus, he volunteered and was appointed to Miryang.

On the day the new official (新官), Magistrate Chŏngsun, arrived, he received congratulatory greetings (하례 賀禮) from various local officials in the main hall (동헌대청 東軒大廳). That night, he had lanterns lit in every room of his bedchamber, and sitting in the centermost room, leaning on a writing desk (서안 書案), he was reading Chŏng Chip'yŏn (正氣篇, Song of Righteousness) by Mun Ch'ŏnsang (文天祥). Midnight had passed. He ordered the guards and servants stationed at the corners of the main hall to go sleep, and continued reading alone. About two hours later, the first cock's crow echoed from outside the fortress walls.

Just then, though there was no wind, he sensed a human presence. The magistrate looked up.
From the far end of the wooden porch (대청마루), the sound of shuffling straw shoes (갓신) approached. A moment later, the double doors of the porch quietly opened, and a peerless beauty dressed in white mourning clothes, with a knife stuck <158> in her neck, entered and bowed respectfully before the magistrate.
Magistrate An felt goosebumps rise on his skin. He felt chills run down his limbs. But thinking, "If I handle this wrong, I'll die," he gathered his wits and roared loudly:
"What audacious creature appears in this guise in the dead of night?"
"..."
"Wench, quickly withdraw! Why have you come here as a ghost?"
He slammed his fist on the writing desk. The sound echoed with a sharp crack—'tchŏng'. The woman rose again, bowed, and then,
"Magistrate, I have come with a plea. Please forgive my rude and strange appearance."
Neither smiling nor crying, she knelt calmly with a serious expression and related the following story.

When the former magistrate Yun Ch'unam was in this county, he had a precious only daughter, like gold and jade (금지옥엽). Her name was Aryang (阿娘)!
This year she turned eighteen, and her beautiful appearance made all the young men in Miryang burn with desire.
On the bright moonlit night of the fourteenth day of the fourth lunar month, Puyong (부용 - another name for Aryang) was reading the Book of Odes (詩傳) in the study of the rear garden (후원서재 后園書齋), when her elderly maidservant invited her to go
moon-gazing.
Reciting poems written about the moon by ancient poets, she followed the old woman, step by step, until they reached the vicinity of Miryangnu Pavilion (密陽樓).
The spring moon hung low. It seemed one could reach up and grasp it. Puyong felt happy to have come out for moon-gazing. Soon, they entered a narrow path through a bamboo grove (대수풀).
The surroundings were quiet. Only the sound of the wind rustling the bamboo leaves could be heard. At that moment, completely unexpectedly, a young man suddenly appeared before them. As she cried out in surprise, a strong arm clamped over her mouth. She looked pleadingly at the old woman standing beside her, but the woman just smiled faintly and disappeared alone into the bamboo thicket (대밧속).
Puyong was dragged by the man into the bamboo grove. The outcome was clear. Puyong resisted the beastly demands of the man, starved for lust (淫慾). The man ultimately stabbed her to death with the knife he carried, discarded her body in a secluded spot, and left.
The magistrate, unaware of what happened to his daughter, searched everywhere, shocked and weeping over her mysterious disappearance (怪奇한 失踪), eventually falling ill (성병 成病) and moving out of the inner office.
Thus, a single flower had scattered from the world, unknown to her parents or anyone else.
"That Puyong is myself,"
said the young woman who had been pleading until now, finally collapsing <159> in tears.
The magistrate listened to the entire story from beginning to end and sighed deeply. Pity welled up in his heart.
"Then do you know who the killer is?"
"Yes, it was the servant (퇴인놈 退引) who ran errands in the main hall."
"What was his name?"
"I do not know his name, but I know his face. I beseech you, Magistrate, tomorrow gather all the servants, retired servants (퇴인), and runners (사령). At that time, I will become a white butterfly and land on that man's head. Please identify him as the culprit and interrogate him."
"I shall do so. How pitiful. Go to the other world and live well in the White Jade Pavilion (백옥루 白玉樓). By the way, where is your body (시채)?"
"It is in the bamboo grove (대수풀) behind Miryangnu Pavilion."
As soon as she finished speaking, Puyong smiled a satisfied smile, bowed, and then vanished like smoke without a trace of leaving.
The magistrate wondered if it had all been a dream.

The next morning at dawn, as the magistrate sat properly dressed in his official robes, the sound of hammering—'tchŏng, tchŏng'—came from outside as a coffin (관 棺) was being made. Then, three or four runners shouted:
"This time, Kim Pŏnsu, you carry out the corpse!"
"No, this one looked big as a wild boar! Pak Pŏnsu, you go tie him up and bring him out!"
Rambunctiously, they burst open the door to the magistrate's chamber.
"You insolent rascals!"
The magistrate's roar startled them so much that several collapsed outside. They had come assuming he would be dead, ready to tie up the corpse.
In the morning, the magistrate gathered all the guards, servants, retired servants (퇴인), and clerks (아전) in the open courtyard (녀른 마당) before the main hall and made them kneel.
Just then, as expected (아스나 다르랴), from the shade of the apricot tree, a single white butterfly appeared out of nowhere, fluttering about, and landed on the head of a certain retired servant (퇴인놈). The magistrate immediately ordered the man seized, tied to the interrogation rack, and questioned. At first, the servant vehemently denied it, ready to die, but eventually, he confessed.
That night, the magistrate executed (처형 處形) the servant. Then, following the directions the young woman had given him the previous night, he went to the bamboo grove behind Yŏngnamnu Pavilion (녕남루뒤). Sure enough, just as she had described, the beautiful young woman who had visited him last night lay there, identical in face and clothing, with the knife embedded in her neck. When the magistrate reached out and touched the knife, it came out easily. Then, instantly, as if she might come back to life, color seemed to rise to her face. The magistrate performed a grand funeral ceremony (성례 盛禮) for this young woman who had died a violent death while trying to protect her chastity. He then built a shrine on the spot where she died. <160>
If you go to Miryang, the scenic spot of Yŏngnam, you will see the elegant Aryang Pavilion (妸娘閣), adorned with red railings and painted walls (주란화벽 朱欄畵壁), standing beside the soaring Yŏngnamnu Pavilion. That is the pavilion this magistrate built for Aryang.
It is said that even now, the kisaeng of Yŏngnam hold memorial rites there in spring and autumn. (End)

**The Heaven-Made Stone Buddha of Kyŏngju - Product of Silla Art's Golden Age**
Yu Kwangnyŏl

The stone Buddha (石佛) of Kyŏngju Sŏkkuram Grotto (慶州石窟菴) shines brilliantly in the history of world art (世界美術史) and is said to represent the golden age (黃金時代) of Silla art (新羅美術). Along with Sŏkkuram, there is a famous legend about Kim Tae-sŏng (金大成), the person who created many artistic treasures that give us much aesthetic pleasure (藝術의 단젓), such as the stone pagodas (石塔) of Pulguksa Temple (佛國寺).
Of course, Pulguksa and Sŏkkuram represent the ancient art of Chosŏn, products of their era. Attributing the achievement solely to Kim Tae-sŏng is not something those who view history scientifically (科學的) would do, but legends also have their own value.
In Moryangni (牟梁里) of Silla (新羅), there lived a poor woman named Kyŏngjo (慶祖) who had a son. His head was large and the nape of his neck was flat like a fortress wall, so he was named Tae-sŏng (大城, meaning 'Big Fortress'). However, the family was poor and couldn't make ends meet, so he went to work as a servant at the house of the wealthy Pokan (福安), farming the fields of that household for food and clothing. At that time, a monk named Chŏmgae (漸開) from Hŭngnyunsa Temple (興輪寺) was organizing a Yukryunhoe (六輪會, Six Wheels Assembly) and came to Pokan's house asking for donations (시여 施與). Pokan readily donated 54 p'il of hemp cloth (布). Chŏmgae accepted the offering and chanted:
檀越好而施, 天神常發持, 施一得萬信安樂壽命長
(The patron makes a good offering, Heavenly spirits always protect, Offer one and receive ten thousandfold, Faith brings peace, comfort, and long life)
Tae-sŏng heard this invocation (추원 祝願) chanted in a clear voice and ran to his mother. <161>
"Mother, I just heard the monk chanting that if you offer one, you receive ten thousand. We must have done no good deeds in our past lives (전생 前生), which is why we are so poor in this life. If we don't make any offerings in this life, won't we be even poorer in the next life (래생)? How about we offer the field (밧 田) we tenant farm at the Buddhist assembly (법회 法會) and await future blessings?"
His mother replied,
"Your words are right."
So they offered their remaining tenant field.
A short time later, Kim Tae-sŏng passed away. That night, at the home of Kim Munyang (金文亮), a renowned high official (재상 宰相) in Silla at the time, a voice suddenly called from the heavens:
"The boy named Tae-sŏng who farmed in Moryangni has now been entrusted to your house. Know this."
The household members were startled by the voice and sent someone to Moryangni to investigate. Indeed, Tae-sŏng had died.
On the very day and hour that the voice from heaven announced Tae-sŏng's rebirth into their house, Kim Munyang's wife conceived and gave birth to a son. For seven days after birth, his left hand remained tightly clenched and would not open. On the seventh day, when his hand opened, the two characters Tae-sŏng (大城) were clearly inscribed on his palm in golden letters (금글시). So he was named Kim Tae-sŏng. His previous life's mother, Kyŏngjo, was brought from Moryangni to live with them, and he grew up caring for (보양하게) both his mothers from his past and present lives.
Kim Tae-sŏng, raised in the lap of luxury in a high official's family, loved hunting and went daily to Mount T'oham (吐含山). One day, he shot and killed a bear, left it there, and went to sleep in a village at the foot of the mountain. In his dream, the bear appeared as a ghost and confronted him:
"I did you no wrong, why did you shoot and kill me? I will bite you to death too!"
It lunged at him.
Kim Tae-sŏng was terrified in his dream and begged for forgiveness, pleading for his life. The bear replied:
"Then will you build a Buddhist temple (불사 佛寺) for me?"
Kim Tae-sŏng promised,
"I will do so."
He woke up drenched in sweat, his bedding soaked.
After that, he stopped hunting in the fields and plains (원야 原野) and built a temple called Changsusa (長壽寺, Long Life Temple) on the spot where he had killed the bear. As days passed, his pious wish (비원 悲願) grew stronger. He built Pulguksa Temple (佛國寺) for his present-life parents and Sŏkkuram Grotto for his past-life parents. When Tae-sŏng was trying to create the stone Buddha, after much painstaking effort, he had carved a large stone, <162> but it suddenly broke into three pieces. Frustrated, Tae-sŏng fell asleep right there. During the night, heavenly gods (천신 天神) descended, completed the statue, and left. Indeed, even today when one visits, it seems impossible that human hands could have created something so beautiful; it must have been the work of heavenly beings (천선 天仙). **The Tragic Love of the Lady and the High Monk - They Became Fossils by Changyŏn Lake, Weeping for Eternity**
Kim P'allyŏn

Young begging monk,
If you look upon this just once,
You shall go to the Lotus Pedestal.
Namu Amit'a-bul (나무아비 타-불)
Kwanseŭm Posal Nam-nam-nam (관세음보살 남-남-남)

Towards sunset (夕陽), a monk carrying an iron staff descended from Mount Changbaek (長白山) and reached the front yard of Chang Chabu's (張子富) house. He struck his wooden fish clapper (목탁) 'tak-tak' and chanted Buddhist prayers (념불). His appearance, wearing a conical hat (꼿갈) and monk's robe (장삼), was neat and clean. His resonant voice, like a blue swallow crying in the sky, and the way he rattled his hundred-bead rosary (百人念珠), made him seem descended from the immortal realm (仙間).
The sun had already begun to dip below the mountain ridge. Light and shadow mingled across the distant mountains and rivers. The sound of evening gulls splashing playfully from the sea could be faintly heard.
The monk gazed absently at his long shadow cast on the yard, then, seemingly pressed for time, struck his wooden fish again and chanted Namu Amit'a-bul. However, his voice now sounded deep and dignified, like the booming of a temple bell accompanying the chanting of the Eighty Thousand Sutras (八萬大歲經) in the main hall (法堂).
Look! In the Western Pure Land (서역정토),
Is there anything that lasts a hundred years?
Namu Amit'a-bul
Kwanseŭm Posal Nam-nam-nam <163>
Although the monk (道僧) earnestly sought a handful of alms (布施) at the gate, the house remained silent, as if he were a crane crying in the ownerless green mountains. The monk looked up blankly at the sky. Purplish clouds near the Milky Way (銀河水) clustered like lotus blossoms (蓮꼿) in a Lotus Hall (蓮花堂), looking down upon the world below. He lifted the edge of his kasaya (袈裟), seemed to murmur a silent prayer for a moment, then struck the ground with his staff again, chanting Namu Amit'a-bul inwardly, thousands, tens of thousands of times. Indeed, his lips must have already uttered countless prayers, striving to save the eighty-four trillion sentient beings (八兆八億의 衆生). And judging by the worn tip of his iron staff, one could tell how diligently he had wandered the world (轍還天下) seeking the Way (道).
However, his second chant was also useless; Chang Chabu's house remained deathly still. Chang's household was famously known throughout Chosŏn as one of the wealthiest, perhaps rivaled only by Pyŏn Chinsa (邊進士) of Suchinbang Valley. He had cleared land spanning over 10 ri (里), like preparing the site for Kyŏngbok Palace (景福宮), and built a multi-roomed mansion (高臺廣室) with blue tiles (靑기와) and red danch'eong paint. On either side stood twelve warehouses (倉庫), built imposingly. These twelve storages held, respectively: first, gold; second, silver; then silk and brocade; warehouses for fruits like dates, pears, persimmons, tangerines, cherries; a warehouse for restorative items like ginseng, deer antler, bellflower root; granaries for the five grains (五穀); livestock sheds (文畜倉庫?), and so on, arrayed more grandly than the king's palace, supporting a luxurious lifestyle. However, Chang Chabu was more famous for his miserliness than for the splendor of his dragon palace-like (龍宮) house, despite acquiring his wealth through all sorts of wicked deeds, akin to theft. Though coins rotted in his storehouses daily and grain became infested with weevils, becoming unusable, he wouldn't lend even a single measure (되) of rice to starving neighbors. Though his thousand fertile fields (千里沃土) lay fallow, he wouldn't give away even one furrow of land. Even on major holidays like the first full moon (정월대보름), though he slaughtered cows and pigs and made heaps of rice cakes, he never showed generosity to the village houses. It went so far that one year, on the night of the first full moon, when young women from Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道) passed by his house while moon-treading, they were so offended by his stinginess that they threw a dried pollack tail (明太꼬리) over his wall. Chang Chabu, seeing this, supposedly threw it back, shouting, "You eat yours, I eat mine!" The women were so disgusted they collectively agreed never to marry into his family via palanquin—a kind of marriage boycott (一種拒婚同盟).
Whether the monk knew what a stingy house he had approached or simply came drawn by the tall mansion, the master paid no heed, while the monk relentlessly sought a handful of pure offering (淨財). The situation became quite awkward. Night had already begun to deepen, and the peach blossoms on the wall bowed their heads. The monk finally chanted his third and fourth prayers.
Suddenly, from within the quiet house, like a serene temple hall (宣化堂), the double doors burst open, and the master, Chang Chabu, radiating a fierce aura like a bear, strode out. <164>

Cow Dung for Buddha
"Hey, monk (道士)! Where do you think you're rushing into like a newborn calf? Living in the eight provinces of Chosŏn, didn't you know about my house? Take your Lotus Pedestal and Buddha and get lost!"
"All sentient beings (象生) are weeping. I beg for a single coin of pure offering (淨財)." The monk's voice was gentle yet dignified (凜凜). He continued, "Last time it rained, the main hall (法堂) collapsed. We are trying to repair it (量修), seeking out the compassionate hearts of the eighty-eight billion sentient beings (八億八千의 象生) like this. If you offer a handful of rice or a single coin, your esteemed household's prosperity (貴宅榮華) will last ten thousand years. Indeed, I hear your warehouses overflow with gold, silver, and treasures. Would a handful of offertory rice (齊米) be more than a drop in the ocean? Namu Amit'a-bul, Namu Amit'a-bul."
"Ha, ha, ha! If the rice rots, I'd rather feed it to the rats. What strange requests!" he spat near the monk's feet. The monk looked resolutely at Chang Chabu. His eyes shone like firelight. His tightly pressed lips, well-defined nose—a noble face (氣品잇는 얼골) rarely seen in the secular world (俗世). And though thought to be an old monk (老僧), he was a young seeker of the Way (青年求道僧) of about twenty. However, the monk, as if having found the words 'forgiveness' (容恕) in the Tripitaka (大藏經), bowed his head again and spoke gently, "Our temple is T'aebaeksa (太白寺), located on the slopes of Mount Changbaek. Initially, a kind-hearted water carrier saved money earned from selling water, putting it into a well. After twelve years, the copper coins (葉錢) filled the well. Believing this was meant for Buddha to save sentient beings (*), he built the main hall on that spot. It has stood for over 1,000 years, but now the incense fire (香火) before Kwanseŭm (관세음) is about to die out. I only appeal to your broad intention of saving lives (生濟度)."
"Hmph, you stubborn beggar monk!" Angrily, he scooped up cow dung from the yard with a dung fork (가래) and shoved it into the monk's begging pouch (동냥 전대). The monk then rose fiercely.
"You shall receive Heaven's punishment (天罰)! Because of you alone, the population of Hamgyŏng Province has halved! Return what you stole, give back what you cheated, or blood will fill your twelve warehouses!"
Chang Chabu tried to stuff the monk's mouth with the dung fork and chased him out the main gate. The monk raised both arms, chanted some incantation (呪文), and turned away. Before him was a pond (련못). As the monk was about to board a small boat (쪽배) to return to his temple and row away, a young woman rushed out after him.
"Reverend Monk (大師님), please forgive him! My father's sin is perhaps unparalleled in the world, but please forgive him with compassion (慈悲). This is offering rice (誠米) I saved each time I pounded grain. Please accept it and use it for one morning's offertory rice (齊米)."
Saying this, she presented about a measure (되) of polished white rice. Moved by her sincere words, the monk looked up at the young lady. Truly, had she been a bit younger, wouldn't one call her a flower blooming by the riverside? The way she shyly plucked at her skirt hem made her seem as beautiful as a fairy (仙女) emerging from the Heavenly Lake (天地) of Mount Paektu (白頭山). The monk, entranced (황홀), didn't know what to do for a moment, then placed his palms together (無數合掌) before the lady, <165 is skipped> <166> accepted the offering rice, and departed.
The lady also watched his departing figure intently until the boat was out of sight. The moon reflected on the river (江) shone even brighter.

A Love Unknown Even to Buddha
The next day, the young monk visited Chang Chabu's house again. And the day after, and the day after that. Each time, chased away with the dung fork, he would come to the lakeside (湖水) and receive offering rice from the daughter, the Lady (娘子), before returning to his temple. But no matter how deeply hidden in the mountains a monk dedicated solely to the Buddhist path (佛道) might be, he was still human. Sometime, somehow, feelings of affection for the butterfly-like maiden (處子) had entered his heart. Previously, he had come down to the human settlements (人家) seeking donations (布施) to rebuild the temple, but now, meeting Chang Chabu's daughter became more important than filling his begging pouch. Deceiving Buddha to meet a young lady? What a fearful thing! Just like that, ten days passed. On that day too, as he received the offering rice by the shore (맷전), his burning heart led him to grasp not just the rice but also the lady's hands holding it. "Lady!" he called out yearningly. It was the first time either had spoken such words. Their faces flushed crimson (朱紅), hearts pounded like drums. The monk squeezed the lady's hand, which felt like it might melt away, once more. "Lady! Lady!" he called again, deeply moved. No other words came out.
As the two youths were about to... suddenly, the young monk seemed to realize something. He abruptly released the hands he was holding, cried "Ah!", turned, fled, rowed his boat swiftly, and disappeared. This monk was a monastic trainee (修道僧). He had entered T'aebaeksa temple on Mount T'aebaek for a ten-year commitment. After entering the mountain, he sometimes descended to human villages (人村) to beg for alms (동냥), sometimes secluded himself in a hermitage (草菴) reading Buddhist scriptures like the Tripitaka (大藏經), sometimes went to the main hall to offer holy water (齋水). He had spent over nine years diligently like this, and now only a hundred days remained. After just one hundred days, he would attain Buddhahood (成佛), achieving enlightenment (入道). Realizing this precious goal, fearing he might ruin ten years of effort (十年工夫) for a mere maiden, he had abruptly pushed her away and fled like this.
From that day on, the young monk's shadow never appeared at Chang's gate again. The maiden was heartbroken (斷腸). Just as a cassia tree (桂樹) grows mysteriously in the moon, flowers blooming, so too in the heart of an eighteen-year-old (二八), the flower of love, nourished by the heart's blood, was already about to bloom. From then on, the lady wept often. Sometimes, overcome by longing (愛慾), she would go to the shore, tie two flowers together, and float them on the water. Sometimes she would cut bamboo, write a letter to the young man on a strip the width of her little finger, put it inside the bamboo, and float it on the lake (湖水). The flowers were sent with the poignant hope that they might meet as the waves washed over them; the bamboo was sent with the vague expectation that it might reach his hermitage (菴子) near the temple.

Autumn Night at the Temple
The young monastic trainee, having repelled the fearsome temptation of the secular world, from that day cut off all contact with the outside world (저자), shut himself in his hermitage (菴子), striking the wooden fish (木魚) <166> and chanting prayers (念佛) with utmost concentration. Thus spring passed, summer passed, and late autumn arrived. Tomorrow would be the eve of his ten-year completion (10年 成道). Thinking of achieving the Way and saving sentient beings (衆生), he chanted the Buddhist scriptures (佛經), filled with boundless joy. Outside, wild geese flew under the autumn moonlight. The sound of wind through the pines (松風) echoed softly, and the eight bells of T'aebaeksa Temple chimed 'ung-ung-' simultaneously, resounding through the vast expanse of Mount Changbaek, seemingly intending to sanctify this significant night.
Suddenly, there was an urgent knocking at the door.
"Hello? Hello?"
Perhaps because it was night, the voice sounded utterly sorrowful (凄愴). At first, thinking it was just a squirrel rustling dry leaves, the monk tried to return to his scriptures. But then, more urgently and pitifully, "Hello! Hello!" came again. It was clearly a woman's voice (女聲). In the dead of night, in such a remote mountain temple (九重山寺), what could enter if not a demon (妖鬼)? Startled, he threw open the door—Ah! Standing there, completely unexpectedly (千萬意外), was Chang Chabu's daughter, her hair disheveled. He felt his whole body stiffen like a stone Buddha (돌부처).
From that time on, the Lady had gone into the bamboo grove (참대밧), cut bamboo, built a raft (배), waited for nightfall, slipped out of her house, and sailed it, desperately searching for the young monk who had vanished like a dream. Wherever she heard chanting, wherever a temple bell (法堂梵鍾) rang, she searched hermitages and temples, wandering east and west (之東之西), enduring all hardships. This evening, she finally heard from a nun (女僧) at a lower temple that he was at T'aebaeksa, and arrived late at night like this. Her white socks (버선) were torn to shreds by tree roots, and her jade-colored skirt (玉色치마) was smeared with mud here and there. Seeing the young monk, she was overcome with ecstatic joy (狂喜), but then, as he slammed the door shut, she collapsed onto the ground, weeping pitifully through the long night. At that moment, the monk's heart trembled like a dry leaf. What use is attaining Buddhahood (成佛)? What use is becoming Sakyamuni's (釋迦) great disciple? What use is going to the Western Pure Land (西域淨土)? Is it right according to the Buddhist path (佛道) to turn away the one who loves me and came these dozens of ri searching for me? Ah, what should I do? Should I just curse the evil karma (惡綠) from a past life (前世)? His heart burned and burned, consumed by the flames of love and the flames of the Way (法道). Finally, moved by the pitiful sound of the Lady's fists knocking softly on the doorframe, forgetting all consequences (前後不覺), he opened the door again, intending to embrace her. But the sound of the dawn bell tolling 'ung-ung-' from afar stopped his steps. He reaffirmed his resolve and chanted the scriptures loudly (놈흐게).
In the vast, empty mountains, the night moon hung lonely. In this hermitage (章菴?), like two souls meeting at the crossroads of Paradise (極樂) and Hell (地獄), one wept sorrowfully outside, knocking on the door, while the other, inside, sought Sakyamuni Buddha (釋迦如來) and Maitreya Bodhisattva (彌륵보살), passing the long night. What karmic connection (因綠) was this?

Do Stone People Also Love?
The next morning, in front of Chang Chabu's gate, another monk appeared, striking a wooden fish and asking for alms. However, <167> before he could even utter the name of Namu Amit'a-bul, the master Chang Chabu came out with the dung fork and chased the monk away. This time, the monk left the gate without a word. Once outside, he sighed deeply.
"Alas, does your glory end today!"
He turned and walked towards the mulberry field. It was the young monk from the previous evening, who had ultimately completed his ten years of training (10年 修道) and attained enlightenment (成道).
As he looked back regretfully at Chang Chabu's house and took a few steps, someone suddenly grabbed the sleeve of his monk's robe (長衫). It was Chabu's only daughter, the Lady. She had been waiting for him for a long time. As if not fully awake from last night's nightmare, they embraced tightly for a while, silent. Truly, at this moment, the world seemed quiet, as if Heaven and Earth (天地) were covered by a lid. Then, the monk's firm lips fell upon the lady's hot cheek. Ah, burning passion (情熱)—if Noah's flood came, could it extinguish this fire? Their two bodies ignited into one point. It was a mysterious and sacred moment, as if Heaven became man and Earth became woman, recreating the universe (宇宙). The monk held the woman's face in his hands.
"Will you come with me?"
"Where to?"
"To a faraway place. A far, far place from which we can never return."
"What about my father?"
"Leave him to receive Heaven's punishment (텬벌). Go. Today, Heaven itself shall execute the judgment of the world's people—"
The maiden looked sadly back at her home, gazed out at the thousand fertile fields (千里沃土), thought for a moment, then buried her face in the monk's chest and resolutely said,
"Let's go!"
The young man led, the lady followed, climbing the steep path (장태) of Mount Changbaek. Love is great. Even parents felt lighter compared to a lover. As they reached the foot of the mountain, the clear sky suddenly turned black, and raindrops the size of fists began to fall torrentially (沛然히). The monk, with a solemn expression, said,
"Whatever happens, absolutely do not look back. Until we cross the ridge. If you do, a great disaster (禍變) will occur." The lady nodded as if she understood and followed him, step by step (타박타박).
She seemed to anticipate some mysterious and sacred event, her whole being filled with light (光明). In the autumn wind, the hems of the two youths' robes and skirt fluttered gracefully together on the slopes of Mount T'aebaek. After walking some distance, having almost reached the mountain peak, the maiden thought, "No matter how wonderful the place I go with my lover, my parents are still back there. Even if the whole world spits curses at them, aren't they my precious parents? Let me just take one last look before it's too late." As she turned her head—Ah! Her house, which had soared like a Dragon Palace (龍宮), instantly collapsed with a clap of thunder and lightning (雷聲霹靂), vanishing without a trace. Behind it, vast <168> waters surged and flowed in, instantly creating a great, boundless sea. Startled, she tried to call out to her lover, but what was this? Her own mouth froze shut, her limbs grew heavy, and gradually her body turned into rock (바윗돌).
The young man walking ahead saw this, threw down his staff, embraced the fossilized statue (化石된 愛人像) of his lover, and cried,
"Buddha, it ended like this after all! Bah! What use is pursuing the Buddhist path? I shall live eternally with my beloved!" Wiping his tears with his sleeve, he chanted something for a few moments, then he too instantly transformed into a life-sized stone statue (等身大의 石像一基), standing neatly beside her.
The story ends here. The waters that filled Chang Chabu's former fertile lands and grand mansion became Changyŏn Lake (長淵湖), where even now, morning and evening, the waves crash forcefully. At the western edge of the lake, below the Changbaek mountain range, amidst overgrown vines (藤넝쿨), stand two stone figures: one, a stone Buddha wearing a robe and conical hat, and the other, a lady's stone statue clutching her skirt. These are the two young people who sought the land of love.
If the lake lasts ten thousand years, the fossilized statues of the young man and woman will also last ten thousand years. No, even if the lake dries up, as long as this story remains among humans and the sky and earth exist, their love will not fade. Years ago, passing by that place, azaleas bloomed profusely around the two fossil statues, and cuckoos were crying. Changyŏn Lake is located in Chibang-dong, Ŏrang-myŏn, Kyŏngsŏng-gun, North Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡北道 鏡城郡 漁郞面 池坊洞), famous for its 99 bends (99曲). It is said that even today, local people sometimes find tableware, gold and silver chopsticks, etc., used by Chang Chabu when the lake water overflows. <169>

**Summary**

This article compiles four distinct pieces: three folktales/historical anecdotes and one retelling of a local legend, followed by a list of popular song titles by singer Wang Subok. The first story recounts how the Chosŏn official Sŏn Sunhyo cleverly excused his drunkenness before King Sŏngjong using wordplay and then impressed the king by composing excellent prose and poetry while heavily intoxicated. The second tells the Miryang legend of Magistrate An Chŏngsun, who solves the mystery of his predecessors' deaths by communicating with the ghost of a murdered girl, Aryang (Puyong), who identifies her killer via a white butterfly, leading to the establishment of Aryang Pavilion. The third relates the Silla legend of Kim Tae-sŏng, born poor but reborn into nobility, who builds Pulguksa and Sŏkkuram temples, the latter's Buddha statue supposedly completed by heavenly gods after it broke. The final, longest piece is a tragic romance about a young monk and the daughter of the cruel miser Chang Chabu; their forbidden love leads them to flee, but the daughter looks back, causing her father's estate to become Changyŏn Lake and both lovers to turn into stone statues on its shore.

From a historical perspective, these tales offer insights into Korean folklore, Buddhist influence on legends and morality tales, and perceptions of historical figures like King Sŏngjong and the Silla artist Kim Tae-sŏng during the colonial period. The stories reflect social structures (kings, officials, commoners, monks, kisaeng), regional identities (Miryang, Kyŏngju, Hamgyŏngdo), and moral values (filial piety, chastity, consequences of greed vs. generosity, Buddhist piety vs. human desire). The inclusion of contemporary popular song titles by Wang Subok at the end provides a snapshot of popular culture in 1935. Historians might find the blend of embellished history (史話) and folklore (野談), the specific articulation of these legends by prominent writers like Kim Tongin and Yun Paengnam in the mid-1930s, and the juxtaposition of ancient tales with modern entertainment indicative of cultural production and consumption during that era.

잡지명	삼천리 제7권 제5호
발행일	1935년 06월 01일
기사제목	史話와 野談
기사형태	문예기타

史話와 野談
孫舜考의 三盃酒
김동인

성종대왕때의 일-.
세조대왕의 사랑하시는 손주님으로서, 아홉살때에 여러 왕손과 함깨 한 아머님을 모시고 앉어 있는데 갑자기 뇌성ㅅ소리가 굉장히 *며 모든 왕손들은 놀라서 어쩔 줄 모르고 덤비는 가운데, 혼자 태연히 있었더라는 영특한 왕의 후신인 성종대왕

리씨조선도 세종대왕의 문치와 세조대왕의 무비를 다 격고나서, 인제는 튼튼히 자리 잡혀서, 성종대왕의 대에는＜154＞ 반석과 같이 굳게 되였다. 이 태평시대에 조선의 국왕으로 올라앉으신 성종대왕은 (왕비문제 때문에 가정적으로는 약간 불쾌한 일은 보섰으나) 성대의 군왕으로서 화기로 찬 일생을 보내섰다.
이 성종대왕이 매우 사랑하시든 신하 가운데, 찬성겸태학사(贊成兼太學士)로서 손순효가 있었다. 태평시대의 선비다히 시에 능하고 가무에 능하고 그 우에 또한 술을 질겨 하는 사람이었다.
왕은, 이 손순효를 매우 사랑하시나 사랑하시기 때문에 그의 과주(過酒)를 근심하서서 늘 「술을 먹으되 석잔은 넘기지 말라」고 충고하고 하섰다. 그러면 순효는 「하교를 어찌 거역하겠읍니까」하고 석잔을 넘기지 않기로 맹서하고 하였다.

어떤날 왕은 편전에 납서서, 승문원(承文院)에서 올린 사대문서를 보시다가, 그 표문이 뜻에 맞지 않으서서, 표문을 다시 짓게 하시려고, 급히 손순효를 부르섰다.
그러나 순효의 집까지 달려갔든 사자는 순효를 동반하지 못하고 돌아왔다. 어디 있는지 갈만한 곳을 다 찾어 보앗지만 알 수 없다는 것이었다. 왕은 다시 사자를 보내섰다. 또 다시 보내섰다. 사자는 가고 또 가고-열 사람이나 갔었지만 아모도 순효를 못데리고 왔다.
사자가 되돌아 올 때마다 왕은 용상에서 몸까지 일으키시며 기두리섰지만, 기다리는 순효는 안오고, 사자만 뷘 손으로 돌아오고 하였다.
이리하여 사자가 가고 또 가기 십여차-황혼이 거이 되여서야 순효가 입궐을 하였다. 그러나 이때는 왕은 순효 때문에 마음이 매우 불쾌하시게 된 때었다.
왕의 심사가 가따가나 불쾌한데, 순효의 꼴악사니를 보니 의관이 정제되지 않고 술ㅅ기운이, 얼굴에 차고 넘고, 걸음걸이조차 똑똑지 않게 들어와서 되는 대로 끓어 곡 배를 드리는 것이었다.
왕은 인제 더 그 불쾌를 참으실 수가 없으섰다.
「전하 어명에 의하와 신이 등대하왔읍니다」
혀꼽으라진 소리로서 순효가 이러케 아뢸 때, 왕은 불쾌한 안정으로 잠시 굽어 보시다가, 누차 전인을 했는데 어디를 갔었느냐고 물으섰다.
「신의 딸이 출가하와 오래 보지 못하엿삽다가, 오늘 지나는 길에 우연히 잠깐 들러서 거기서 시간이 지체되왔읍니다」
이것이 순효의 번명이었다.
「내 일즉이 술은 석잔을 넘기지 말라고 단단히 말해 두었는데, 오늘 이꼴은 웬일이오?」
「신이 전하의 하교를 어찌 어기오리까. 三배주를 넘기지 않었사읍니다」＜155＞
「석잔? 석잔에 그러듯 취한단 말이오?」
「네이. 단 석잔이읍니다. 주발로 단 석잔...」
헐 수 없다. 애총을 믿고 어전에 떼거리를 쓰는 것이다.
아모리 대제학이요 시에 능하다 할지라도 이러틋 취해서는 하표문 선정을 말길 수가 없었다. 그래서 왕은 부제학을 부르서서, 둘이서 함께 선정케 하려 하니, 이것은 손순효가 승낙하지 않는다.
「신이 혼자 하오리다. 신 아모리 취햇다 하오나 신의 직책이야 못감당하오리까」
하여 듣지 않는다.
왕도 사랑하시든 순효라, 어디 그 능력을 시험해 보고저 허럭하시매, 순효는 취한 머리를 몇번 이리저리 젓는가 하더니, 다 됏으니 지필을 줍시샤고 한다.
아모리 글씨에 능하지만 취필이 어찌 능히 당하랴고, 사서(寫書)할 사람을 부르려 하시나 이 주정꾼은 그냥 벋히고, 스스로 쓰기를 청한다. 그래서, 왕도 시험삼아 필연을 내주라 명하시니, 순효는 그 가운데서 붓 몇 자루를 골라 취한 눈을 연하여 찡글며 손바닭 우에 글을 써보다가 모도다 내던지고 모도, 못쓸 붓뿐이라고 붓투정을 한다.
어명에 의지해서 다시 어필연이 나왔다. 그러매 그 가운데서 한자루 골라 잡고,
「이 붓이 겨우 소용되읍니다. 그러나 신이 연노하와 허리를 굽히기가 힘드오니, 책상을 하나 주시옵소서」
고 또다시 투정이다.
책상도 등대되엿다. 그 책상앞에 꿀어 앉어, 순효는 눈의 초점을 맟후는 듯이 연하여 찡글며 글을 다 초하여, 한번 다시 읽어 보고는 어전에 바친다.
왕은 받아 보섰다. 한줄, 두줄, 읽어나려가시는 동안 용안에 차차 어리는 환희의 표정.
글로던 글씨로던, 어디 한곤대 나므럴 데가 없는 훌륭한 표문이었다. 시험삼아 명하시기는 하였지만, 이건 온 뜻밖이었다.
왕은 이 순효의 취중표문을 그대로 숭문원에 나리서서 보내게 하시고, 사용원(司甕院)에 명하시어, 크게 잔치를 하시고 순효에게 대취하도록 술을 주섰다.

색시도 배가 안 보일 때까지 그 뒤 양자를 물끄럼히 바라보고 잇섯다. 江우에 떠러진 달은 더욱 밝엇다.

부처님조차 모르는 사랑
그 이듬날도 젊은 중은 張子富의 집안을 차젓다. 또 그 이듬날도 또 이듬날도 그럴 때마다 똥가래로 쪼이고 쪼지면 湖水가에 와 그 딸되는 娘子에게서 誠米를 바더가지고 절로 도라갓다. 그러나 아모리 집흔 산중에 드러안저 佛道만 닥는 중이라 할지라도 그도 사람이라 어느 사이에 나비가튼 그 處子에게 연모하는 마음에 들엇섯다. 이전에는 절을 중수할 布施를 바드려 人家로 나려왓건만은 돈냥 전대를 채우는 것보다 張子님 富의 딸을 맛나보는 것이 더 긴요한 일이 되엇다. 부처님을 속이고 젊은 색시와 맛나는 일? 엇지도 두렵지 안은 일이랴. 이럭저럭 열흘날만이다. 그 날도 맷전에서 誠米를 바들 때 그만 타는 가슴이 쌀을 쥔 색시의 두손까지 그만 껴쥐고 「娘子여!」하고 애연하게 불럿다. 불니우는 이나 북르는 이나 처음 거는 말이라. 얼골은 朱紅빗이 되도 가슴은 콩틔듯 팟틔듯 하얏다. 중은 사르르 낙아버리듯한 색시의 손을 다시 한번 꼭 쥐고 「娘子여! 娘子여!」하고 곰둘려 불럿다. 그밧게 아모 말도 나오지 안엇다.
두 靑春은 할 때로 하랴 할 때 갑작이 靑年僧은 무엇을 깨달은 드시 안으랴든 두 손을 홱 집어 뿌리고 앗-하면서 다라나와 급히 배를 저어 종적을 감추어 버럿다. 그중은 修道僧이엇다. 십년을 작정하고 太白山절에 入山한 뒤 혹은 人村에 내려 동량을 빌고 혹은 草菴에 파뭇처 大藏經등 佛書를 닑고 혹은 法堂에 이르러 齋水도 올니고 하야 9년 남저지를 純實하게 지낸 뒤 이제 백날만 남은 터이라. 백날만 지나면 그는 成佛되드시 入道하는 것이다. 貴重한 목적을 깨닷고서 한낫 處子때문에 그르칠가 하야 十年工夫南무아미타-불을 면하고저 이갓치 가작히 뿌리치고 내다른 것이엇다.
그제부터는 다시 젊은 중에 그림자는 張의 집문깐에 나타나지만엇다. 처녀는 斷腸할 드시 슬펏다. 마치 달속에 누가 심은 양업시 桂樹나무가 나서 자라서 꼿이 피어지드시 二八의 젊은 인생의 가슴에도 사랑의 꼿이 心臟의 피 우에서 자라서 벌서 꼿피려 하는 것이다. 색시에는 그제부터 우름이 만헛다. 글로부터 혹은 愛慾에 못이겨 뱃전에 나와 꼿두송이를 억매어 물에 띄어 보기도 하고 참대를 꺽거 그 속에 가줄에 갓재 정도로 그 靑年에게 便紙를 써서 湖水에 띄우기도 하엿다. 꼿은 치는 물결에 갈아안지며 맛나리라 하는 애처러운 히망에서 나온 것이요. 참대는 행여 절간 압 그이 菴子에 전해지라는 막연한 긔대에서 나온 것이엇다.

절깐의 가을밤
靑年修道僧은 그때 무서운 現世의 誘惑을 물니치고 그날부터는 저자에 一切 발을 끈코저 菴子에 들어안저 木魚치고＜166＞ 念佛외우기에 專心하엿다. 이럭저럭 봄가고 여름가고 느진 가을을 당도하야 이제는 래일이면 10年 成道하는 저녁을 당하엿다. 그는 이제는 道를 이루어 衆生을 건질 생각을 하면서 佛經을 외우고 잇슬나니 깁부기 限업섯다. 밧갓헤는 가을달밤에 기럭이 난다. 멀니선 松風이 은은히 울고 太白寺 여덜종이 한시에 웅-웅-울어 長白山의 너르나 너른 산속을 뒤집으면서 뜻잇는 이 하로 밤을 거룩하게 새어주려 하는 듯 하엿다.
그 때 불시에 밧갓해서 황망히 문을 두드리는 소리잇다.
「여보세요, 여보세요」
밤소리되어 그런지 그 목소리는 한끗 凄愴하엿다. 처음은 가랑닙흘 지나는 다람쥐 소리니 하고 중은 다시 經書를 대하려 할 때에 이번은 급하게 더 애처럽게 「이보세요! 이보세요!」한다. 그는 분명 女聲이엇다. 깁흔 밤중에 이런 九重山寺에 妖鬼가 아니면 드러올 것이 잇스랴. 그는 놀라 문을 여러 제치니 앗-계는 머리를 산산히 풀어허친 張子富의 따님이 千萬意外에 서 잇쟌느냐. 그는 돌부처갓치 全力이 빳빳히 구더짐을 깨달앗다.
娘子는 그 뒤부터 참대밧헤 드러가 참대를 따다가 배를 무어 밤되기를 기대려 집을 빠저나와 타고서 꿈갓치 단녀간 그 靑年僧의 뒤를 찻기에 애썻다. 념불소리 들니는 곳마다 法堂梵鍾이 울니는 곳마다 菴子를 찻고 절간을 차저 之東之西로 가진 고생을 다하다가 오늘 저녁애야 이 아랫절 女僧에게서 太白寺에 잇다는 말을 듯고 밤늣게 이러케 차저온 것이엇다. 그의 하얀 버선은 나무뿌리에 긁치워 산산히 째지고 玉色치마도 군데군데 진흙이 무덧다. 그는 靑年僧을 보더니 미칠듯 狂喜하다가 다시 門을 닷는 바람에 땅바닥에 잣바저 긴긴밤을 애처럽게 울며 지낸다. 그때 중도 마음이 가랑닙갓치 흔들니엇다. 成佛하면 무얼하나 釋迦의 高弟가 되면 무얼하나 西域淨土로 가면 무얼하나 이몸을 차저 이러케 數十里를 온 사랑하는 이를 물니처야 佛道에 맛는가. 아 엇저면 조흐랴 前世움˜ 惡綠을 咀呪나 하고 말랴. 그의 가슴은 사랑의 불길과 法道의 불길에 타고 타고 또 타고 하엿다. 그는 마츰내 娘子의 문지방을 똑똑 두다리는 애처러운 주먹소리에 前後不覺으로 門을 다시 열고 안아드리려 하다가 멀니서 웅웅 울려고는 새벽종소리에 다시 거름을 멈추고 다시 決心하고서 經을 놈흐게 웨엿다.
너르나 너른 空山에는 夜月이 외로히 걸렷는데 이 章菴에서는 마치 極樂과 地獄으로 서로 相克되는 길을 것는 두 生靈이 마조친 모앙으로 하나는 문밧게서 문을 뜨드며 슬피 울고 하나는 안에서 釋迦如來를 찻고 彌륵보살을 차즈며 긴 밤을 보냇다. 이 어인 因綠인고.

돌사람끼리도 사랑하나
이톨날 아츰이나 오래간만에 張子富의 참 압마당에는 또다시 중 하나가 나타나 木魚을 치며 布施를 구하엿다. 그러나＜167＞ 역시 나무아미타-불의 낫잣를 부르기도 전에 主人 張子富는 똥가레를 가지고 나와 중을 쪼찻다. 중은 이번은 아모 말도 업시 문깐으로 나왓다. 그는 다나오자 길게 한숨을 쉬며
「슬푸다 네 영화가 오늘로 끗을 맷단말가!」
하고 도라서 뽕나무 밧녁을 나간다. 그는 10年 修道에 끗끗내 成道하고만 어제저녁의 그 젊은 중이엇다.
그가 앗가운드시 張子富의 집안을 돌아다보며 한거름 두거름 나갈 때에 불시에 長衫소매를 잡는 이가 잇다. 그는 子富의 외딸 娘子엇다. 娘子는 그를 기대린지 오래다. 둘은 어제밤 악몽을 다 깨지 못한드시 한참은 서로 얼사 안고 아모 말도 업섯다. 실로 이때만은 天地가 덥개를 덥고 자는 듯 고요하엿다. 그리다가 중의 단입살은 색시의 뜨거운 빰 우에 떠러젓다. 아 불붓는 情熱이어-노아의 홍수엇더면 이불을 끄엇슬가. 두몸은 함點에 불타섯다. 하늘은 사내되고 땅은 女子되야 다시 宇宙를 創造하는 듯 神秘하고 거룩한 순간이 지나갓다. 중은 녀자의 얼골을 두손으로 밧들어 보며
「날 따러 오려오?」
「어대로」
「먼곳으로 다시는 못올 먼-먼-곳으로」
「아버지는 엇저고?」
「텬벌을 밧게 내버려두고, 가시요. 세상사람의 재판을 오늘 하늘이 대신하야 하게 되엿스니깐-」
處女는 슬푼드시 자긔 집을 돌나보고 千里沃土를 내다보고 한참 무엇을 생각하더니 중의 가슴에 낫을 파뭇으며 決然히
「자지요!」
靑年은 압시고 색시는 뒤서서 長白山 장태를 올나간다. 사랑은 크다. 부모도 애인에 비하면 가벼운 것이엇다. 둘이 산아래에 이르자 不時에 그 晴晴하던 날새가 까막케 흐리어지며 주먹가튼 비방울이 沛然히 떠러진다. 중은 엄숙한 낫빗으로
「엇던 일이 잇슬지라도 결단코 뒤를 도라 보지 마시오. 장래를 넘기까지는 만일 그러면 큰 禍變이 날터이니」 색시는 알엇다는 드시 골을 끄덕이며 그 뒤를 따라 타박타박 거러 올라간다.
그는 무슨 神秘하고 거륵한 일을 豫期하는 드시 全力이 光明에 차섯다. 가을바람에 두 靑春의 長衫자락과 치마자락이 가지련히 太白山 장태 우에서 팔락팔락 운치잇게 날렷다. 얼마를 거른 뒤 거지반 산꼭대기에 이르럿슬 때 찹시는 아모리 愛人을 따라 조흔 곳을 간대도 내 父母가 저곳에 잇쟌으냐? 世上사람은 다 춤을 배터도 나에겐 소중한 父母가 아니냐. 너머  기전에 잠깐 마즈막 돌아보자하고 골을 돌니자 앗! 龍宮갓치 소사 잇든 自己의 집은 금시에 雷聲霹靂이 치면서 자최조차 업시 쓰러지고 그 뒤는 洋洋한＜168＞ 물결이 구품처 흘러 드러와 삽시간에 망망한 큰 바다를 만들고 만다. 놀라서 愛人을 부르자 할 때에 이게 또 웬일인가. 自己 입이 구더지고 四肢까지 무거워지며 점점 제 몸은 바윗돌로 化하고 만다.
압헤가든 靑年은 그 꼴을 보고 그만 막대를 집어 뿌리며 化石된 愛人像을 안고
「불사, 끗내 이리 되엇구나. 에라 佛道 닥거 내 무엇하나 사랑하는 이와 永生하리라고 하면서 팔사매로 눈물을 씻고 무어라고 두어마듸 외오더니 자기도 곳 等身大의 石像一基로 化하야 그겻헤 가지련히 선다.
이약이는 이에 끗낫다. 張子富의 녓날 沃土와 大厦高樓에 드리차든 물결은 長淵湖가 되야 지금도 아츰저녁 파도가 세고 湖水의 西便長白山脈아래 藤넝쿨이 버더진 곳에는 장삼입고 꼬갈 쓴 돌부처 하나와 치마를 염어 쥔 색시의 石像이 가지련히 노힌 것이 잇스니 그는 사랑나라를 찻든 두 젊은이들이다.
湖水가 萬年을 간다면 두 靑春男女의 化石像도 萬年을 가리라. 아니 湖水는 마를지라도 人世에 이약이가 남어잇고 하늘따가 잇는 동안까지는 둘의 사랑을 꺼지지 안으리라. 年前에 그곳을 지날나니 두 化石像에는 진달내꼿이 어즈러히 피엇고 두견새 울고 잇더라. 長淵湖는 咸鏡北道 鏡城郡 漁郞面 池坊洞에 잇는 것으로 湖水의 구비가 99曲이라고 하야 有名하며 지금도 지방사람들은 각금 못물이 넘어 날 때면 張子富가 쓰든 식기와 金銀수절 등을 줏는 일이 잇다 한다.＜169＞
