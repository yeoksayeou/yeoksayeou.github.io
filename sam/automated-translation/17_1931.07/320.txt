Magazine Title: 삼천리 제17호
Publication Date: 1931-07-01
Article Title: 新女性들은 男便의 밥과 옷을 지어본 적 잇는가? 업는가? (Have 'New Women' Cooked Meals and Made Clothes for Their Husbands, or Not?)
Author: Kim Kyŏngjae
Article Type: 설문 (Survey)

Have 'New Women' Cooked Meals and Made Clothes for Their Husbands, or Not?
Kim Kyŏngjae

Mr. Kim is a social activist released from prison two years ago. His wife is the accomplished poetess Ms. Cha Myosŏk, who, after graduating from Sukmyŏng Girls' High School years ago, gained literary fame as a female reporter for the "Chosŏn Chubo." Their sweet home is next to the spring vegetable patch in Ch'ungsadong. Just the two of them and their son live there.

Since marriage, I have lived wearing the clothes my wife made and eating the food she cooked. I don't know how much talent for cooking and clothes-making she learned during her girls' school days, but she knows well how to cook rice and make trousers and jackets from cut cloth.
Living like this, lately, the weather has become extremely hot, and it seems the mother's body is quite exhausted from raising a young child, so for the first time a few days ago, we hired an "eomeom" (female servant) to cook rice and do laundry. However, in our house, it can still be seen that my wife cooks the meals and makes the clothes.

Pak Kyŏnghŭi

Madam Pak is one of Chosŏn's leading vocalists, having graduated from Tōkyō Music School and then specialized in music for three years upon reaching Shanghai. Her husband is Mr. Yi Yŏsŏng, an employee of the Chosŏn Ilbo and an authority on the movement for weak and small nations. Their sweet home is in Iksŏndong within the city.

When we were in Shanghai, if he bought groceries, I would cook the food and serve him. In return, when I was in labor and groaning in that foreigner's apartment on Paoshan Road in Shanghai, he, along with the nurse, would boil hot water and make porridge for me.
After returning to Korea, the number of mouths to feed increased, and having a young child, I have no time to cook everything myself, so generally, I employ a female cook (식모) to prepare the meals. However, it goes without saying that I always go to the kitchen myself to supervise the side dishes and the cooking of the rice.
Occasionally, when my father-in-law and mother-in-law from the countryside come to visit, I must pay extra attention to the cooking, so I roll up my sleeves and work in the kitchen. But since this is an infrequent occurrence, it can be said that opportunities for me to cook meals for my husband with my own hands have become rare lately.
Since he always wears Western clothes (洋服), making traditional Korean clothes is rare. And naturally, things like underwear (內服) end up being washed by the "eomeom."<78>

Kim Hyŏngwŏn

Mr. Kim is the Editor-in-Chief of the Chungoe Ilbo and a famous life-philosophy poet. His wife is Ms. Kim, a teacher at Chinmyŏng Girls' High School. Their sweet home is a new house in Sinkyo-dong, which the two jointly designed and built, and they have several children (flesh and blood) under their care (膝下).

My wife cooks the meals. Even while attending school, she wakes up twice as early as I do in the morning to cook for me. And speaking of clothes, she makes all of them. Though "all" just means underwear and the like, since I always wear Western clothes, the old rags attached to my body are mostly underwear (內服 等屬), but she washes and irons everything – even my socks – for me to wear.
Perhaps it's fortunate; as she happens to be in charge of the home economics department (家事科) at her school, although it's a bit embarrassing for me to say it, she seems to be quite skilled at cooking and making clothes.

Ch'oe Ŭisun

Ms. Ch'oe is a graduate of Tōkyō Women's Higher Normal School and currently a reporter for the Tonga Ilbo. Her husband is Mr. Chin Changsŏp, a graduate of Waseda University and a former teacher at Hwimun High School.

Yes, in the past. Probably right after getting married, since there wasn't anyone particularly idle at home, I mostly cooked the meals. But after having a young child and needing to strictly keep the working hours of my job, I unavoidably had to entrust it to the "eomeom." And lately, as my health is also weak, we moved to a house inside Tongdaemun and live with my mother, so naturally, there are many times when my mother cooks.

I also used to make clothes in the past, but now I hardly make any. Not that there's much to make since he always wears Western clothes (양복) anyway–

Ch'oe Yonghwan

Mr. Ch'oe is a reporter for the Tonga Ilbo. His wife is currently a teacher at the Girls' Commercial School and a graduate of Tōkyō Women's Higher Normal School.

Since both my wife and I have jobs that take us outside from morning till evening, we naturally have no choice but to entrust household matters (가사범절) to the hands of the "eomeom." Therefore, the food we eat morning and evening is all prepared by the "eomeom." However, in the three years since we married, I think I've probably eaten meals cooked by my wife maybe twice. While it's undoubtedly a happy thing for a husband to eat meals cooked by his wife, various circumstances prevent it.

Clothes are also the same situation. Fortunately, since I always wear Western suits (양복), there's no need to fussily make traditional Korean clothes according to the seasons. However, even my underwear waits to be washed by the "eomeom"... This too is unavoidable due to time constraints–

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae

Mr. Chŏng holds a German medical doctorate and is the director of Chisŏng Hospital in Tagol (茶屋町) within the city. His wife is a graduate of a certain school in P'yŏngyang. Their sweet home is connected to the hospital in Tabang-gol (다방골).

In Chosŏn, especially in Seoul, the family system (가정제도) customarily involves keeping servants (하인), so even if a wife wants to cook for her husband, it seems difficult to do so. The same goes for clothing and related items. Therefore, perhaps only a husband with a very devoted wife might receive side dishes she prepared herself.
However, our household is not like that. Probably, my wife cooks most of the time.
Regarding clothes, since I wear Western suits day and night (밤낫), there isn't much particular work, but my wife washes my underwear (내복 등손).

Kim Ilyŏp

Ms. Kim is a female writer (女流文士). Her husband is the principal of an elementary school in Sŏngbuk-tong, outside the city. Their sweet home is in Sŏngbuk-tong, outside the city.

Since it's just the two of us, I generally cook the meals.

I also make the clothes.<79>

An Chŏngok

Madam An is a graduate of Kŭnhwa School. Her husband is the novelist and former Chosŏn Ilbo reporter, Mr. Sim Hun. Their sweet home was established in Sŏdaemun-oe. They were newly married last autumn.

As we are not in a position (처지) to keep a servant (下人), and only he and I live alone in the house, who else would cook the morning and evening meals (끼니) if I didn't? Therefore, starting from the day after our wedding, I took charge (도마터서) of the kitchen work. I don't find it the least bit difficult (괴롭지 안코); rather, I don't know how pleasant (유쾌한지) it is.

Clothes – since he always wears Western suits, there isn't much to tailor – but I wash everything, whether it's socks or underwear. Although there are times when he washes them himself (저손소) sometimes (각금) –

Hŏ Yŏngsuk

Madam Hŏ graduated from Tōkyō Women's Medical College, and her husband is Mr. Yi Kwangsu, the Editor-in-Chief of the Tonga Ilbo. They have been married for 7 or 8 years. They have two young children, and their sweet home is in Sungsamdong within the city.

Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu's pen name) is always in a physical condition where his health (健康) can easily decline, so special care must be taken with his daily living environment (日常 居處) and food and drink (飮食). And since I know his illness (病) well, I must pay particular attention to what he eats morning and evening, especially preparing side dishes with fresh vegetables (신선한 채소) for him. Things like rice are cooked by the "eomeom" at home, but I always make the side dishes myself.
And lately, since he commutes to the newspaper company, he always wears Western clothes (洋服), so there isn't much mending (손질할 것) to do. But the traditional Korean clothes he wears when at home (집에 계실때) are mostly made by me.

Sim Ŭnsuk

Madam Sim is an activist (鬪士) of the Kŭn'uhoe. Her husband is Mr. Hong Kimun of the Sin'ganhoe. Their sweet home is at Chongno 4-ch'ŏme within the city. They have one young son in the family.

Looking at the general family system (일반가족제도) in Seoul, even if a new woman gets married (시집을 왓다), unlike in the countryside (시골), it seems she doesn't immediately start cooking food and making clothes. This is because households of the middle class or above (중인 이상) customarily keep servants living in the outer quarters (행낭 사람), and those servants handle things like the household cooking... However, I do cook sometimes.

Regarding clothes, since he mostly wears Western suits, there's no need to make traditional Korean clothes according to the season. As for things like socks, sometimes I wash them, sometimes he washes them himself, and sometimes the servants wash them...

Hwang Sindŏk

Madam Hwang graduated from Japan Women's University and currently teaches at Myŏngsŏng Women's Academy. Her husband is Mr. Im Pongsun, a reporter for the Tonga Ilbo. Their sweet home is in Sinsŏl-li, outside the city.

Since getting married, there have been many times I've cooked, and many times I haven't. If I was busy with work, I had the person who works at home cook. Especially since starting to care for a young child, the opportunities to make meals or side dishes became quite scarce. Goodness, you're conducting quite the detailed investigation. Hahaha.

As for clothes, he always goes out wearing Western suits, so there aren't many worries about clothes. If there were any, it might be underwear, white shirts, or socks and the like. Those too are entrusted to the person who works at home for washing (세탁).
In the position of a busy professional woman, how could one possibly have time for laundry as well?<80>

**Summary**

This 1931 survey article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interviews several prominent educated Korean women ("New Women"), many with careers, about whether they personally cook meals and make clothes for their husbands. The responses vary: some women, particularly those newly married or without servants, handle most domestic tasks themselves, often finding joy in it. Others, especially those with demanding careers, young children, or from households affluent enough to employ domestic help ("eomeom" or "sikmo"), delegate cooking and laundry, although some maintain supervision or cook on special occasions. The commonality of husbands wearing Western clothing (양복) also reduced the need for wives to engage in traditional clothes making. This article offers historians a valuable glimpse into the lives of elite, educated Korean women during the colonial period, showcasing the tension between traditional gender expectations and the realities of modern life, including professional careers and changing household structures influenced by class and modernization. The frequent mention of domestic servants highlights social stratification, while the discussion around Western versus Korean clothing reflects cultural shifts.

新女性들은 男便의 밥과 옷을 지어본 적 잇는가? 업는가?
金璟載

氏는 2년 전 出獄한 社會運動家. 氏의 夫人은 年前 淑明女子高普을 마친 뒤 「朝鮮週報」女記者로 文名을 날니고 잇든 閨秀詩人 車妙錫氏로 쓰윗트.홈은 崇四洞 春菜밧 엽헤 잇다. 단 두 분과 아드님이 사신다.

나는 結婚 以後 家妻가 지은 밥과 家妻가 지은 옷을 입고 지금까지 살어왓소. 그가 얼나나 한 作食, 作衣의 才能을 女學校時代에 배워두엇는지는 모르나 쌀을 익히고 끈어온 옷감을 바지, 저고리 말들 줄은 잘 아더이다.
이러케 지내어 오다가 요지음에는 날도 몹시 덥고 어린 아해 양육에 母體가 몹시 지치우는 모양이기에 처음으로 數日前에 「어멈」 한 사람를 어더다가 밥짓고 빨내를 하게 하엿슴니다. 그러나 우리 집은 밥과 옷은 역시 안해가 지어준다고 볼 것이외다.

朴慶姬

女史는 東京音樂學校를 마치고 上海에 이르러 3년 간을 音樂 專攻한 朝鮮 有數의 聲樂家이요 그의 夫君은 朝鮮日報社員으로 弱少民族運動의 權威인 李如星氏인바 쓰윗트.홈은 市內 益善洞집에 잇다.

上海에 잇슬 때에는 그분이 작만할 거리를 사오면 제가 음식을 지어 대접하엿지요. 그 대신 제가 해산하느라고 上海 寶山路의 그 外國人 아파-.트멘트에서 呻吟할때는 看護婦와 함께 그이가 더운물도 끄리어주고 죽도 써주드구만요.
歸國한 뒤는 食率도 만케 되고 어린 아해까지 가진 몸이 되매 一一히 作食할 겨을이라고 업서 大槪는 食母를 두고 밥을 짓게 함니다. 그러나 늘 나도 부억에 나가서 반찬이고 밥짓는 것을 감독하는 것은 물론임니다.
혹 시골게신 시아버님, 시어머님께서 다니려 오신 때에는 작식에 일층 주이하여야 하겟기에 제가 팔을 거두고 부억에 나서서 일을 함니다. 그러나 이것은 흔히 업는 일이니까 近來는 제 손으로 밥을 지어 男便에게 대접할 機會라고 드물다 할 것이외다.
