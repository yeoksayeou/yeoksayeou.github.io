雜感
						파인 (P'ain)
無名義人의 墓 (Tomb of the Nameless Righteous Person)

Yi Sunsin at least has a tomb. Kim Okkyun, Yi Sangjae, Yi Sŭnghun, and others known by name all possess their own tombs, even if only fist-sized mounds. However, for the many pioneers within and outside the country whose names we are not even free to utter, forget tombs, even the dates and places of their deaths are not remembered. Looking through the Imjin War, one cannot deny that Yi Sunsin is indeed a meritorious figure who extended our history by 300 years, and one can also see that Kim, Yi, and Yi were all individuals who sacrificed their lives, property, and families for the prosperity of Chosŏn and the Chosŏn people. But how many countless pioneers also shed hidden blood and effort, no less than them? When considering tombs, we see too much unfairness here.

Of course, pioneers merely殉 (died for a cause) for their principles; they never desired large tombs, splendid shrines, biographies passed down on 竹帛 (bamboo and silk; historical records), or adoration from descendants. However, we should not carelessly abandon their bodies on the sandy shores of rivers and trample their names like dust. Shouldn't we understand the situation by looking at the examples of other nations, which have tombs for Sun Wen, Lenin, Washington, and Garibaldi, and also possess tombs for unknown soldiers everywhere in places like London, Paris, Berlin, and Moscow? Honoring the meritorious blood of those who could not even have their own tombs, thereby upholding courtesy towards our predecessors, and on the other hand, creating opportunities to receive encouragement, inspiration, and suggestion for ourselves—this is the wise attitude of advanced nations and advanced citizens towards nameless sacrifices.

At this time, we have much material for thought. Recently, the sad fate (悲運) of Admiral Yi Sunsin's tomb site (忠武公 墓所) acted as a spur, stirring the sincere feelings of society's members towards their ancestors, and sincere donations amounting to over ten thousand won are being collected. This should not be poured solely into a memorial project for Yi Sunsin alone, but should be expanded into a project commemorating the pioneers of modern times in all fields—hidden thought movements, politics, society, humanities, sciences, etc.—who could not even have tombs. This would be a wise effort. Tomb of the Nameless Righteous Person! Ah, wouldn't it have been good to have had it already? And wouldn't it be proper for perhaps appreciative descendants of this land to visit in autumn and spring, scattering flowers and shedding tears?

Messrs. Paek Kwansu, An Chaehong, Yi Kwangsu, Yun Ch'iho, Song Chinu, and other concerned parties—do not forget that Admiral Yi Sunsin's true intention might lie in this direction<70>, and I hope that on the occasion of this epoch-making commemorative project, efforts will be made in this regard.

거지 (The Beggar)

I went out to Namdaemun Station to catch the Kyŏngŭi Line train, but there were still about two hours left until departure time, so finding it difficult to wait in the waiting room, I entered a nearby café and ordered tea.

At that moment, a Japanese woman (日女), her cheeks rouged red like camellia flowers, came forward to serve, smiling brightly. Then, in the midst of her laughter, a piece of hard candy she had in her mouth suddenly clattered down and rolled towards the threshold. Just then, a young beggar boy in torn clothes, whistling휙휙 (whistle sound) as he passed by on the asphalt outside the door, quickly picked up the candy, popped it into his mouth, and walked away without even smiling.

The waitress remarked, 「まあ, いやらしい, 塵もとらないで-」 (Oh, how disgusting, without even brushing off the dust—), clutching her sides and laughing as if she would die. Seeing here some fateful shadow of a certain group, I felt unpleasantness and quickly rushed out.

Who made that boy so starved that he could not even sense ridicule?

Beneath my shoe soles, the asphalt echoed with a dull, thudding sound (뚜걱뚜걱), further oppressing my own heart.

虛無僧的 放歌 (A Nihilistic Monk's Unrestrained Song)

Even inside a coffin would be fine, if only I could live for just a day or two where that person is not; all I've gained in these precious 7 or 8 years is the burning fire of belated realization and regret.

Try piercing this flesh, joint by joint (마듸마듸), with a huge bamboo needle (왕대바눌). Is there even a single drop of impure blood in my whole body? Oh, wood and stone, oh, ill-fated connection.

When did I ever try to become a paragon of virtue? I only wished to live like a human being. Who blocks this wish? To block it is to ask for my life.

I soothe my body, weary from wandering (轉轉) for four years, in the dead of night. How many more days can this flesh endure upon the flames of this agony? Ah, I am I! Suddenly kicking the wall and rising in the middle of this night.

Rising up, like that crane that beats its wings (두날개 툭툭 치고) after a sudden rain shower and cries out loudly, shall I stride freely (두 활개 훨이 훨이 치고) through the wide world?<71>

**Summary**

This article by P'ain consists of three distinct parts: a call to honor nameless Korean patriots alongside famous figures like Yi Sunsin, suggesting funds raised for Yi's tomb be used more broadly; a poignant anecdote about a beggar boy snatching a dropped candy, highlighting poverty and perhaps indifference or colonial dynamics; and a short, anguished poem expressing personal suffering and a desire for freedom. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its overt nationalist sentiment in advocating for recognition of unnamed independence activists and pioneers ("先駈者") during the colonial period. It subtly critiques the societal inequalities (contrasting the honored dead with the forgotten, the waitress with the beggar) and expresses a palpable sense of frustration and yearning for liberation, reflecting the atmosphere of Japanese-occupied Korea in 1931.

필자	파인
기사형태	문예기타

雜感
						파인
無名義人의 墓

李舜臣은 그래도 무덤을 가지고 잇다. 金玉均, 李商在, 李昇薰 等 누구누구라는 이들도 모다 주먹만한 墳墓라도 제각금 제것을 가지고 잇다. 그러나 海內 海外의 일홈조차 부를 自由업는 여러 先駈者들에게는 무덤은 고사하고 죽은 날자 죽은 場所조차 記憶이 되지 못하고 잇다. 壬辰亂을 通하여 볼 때에 李舜臣은 확실히 우리 歷史를 300년 간 延長식혀노은 功人임을 否定하지 못할것이나 또 金, 李, 李도 모다 제 生命, 제 財産, 제 妻子를 犧牲하여 가면서까지 朝鮮 及 朝鮮民族의 繁榮을 위하든 분임을 알 수 잇겟스나 그러나 그에 못지지 안케 숨은 功血을 부은 만흔 先駈者들이 또한 엇더케 만흐냐 무덤을 사이에 두고볼 때 우리는 여기에서 너무 만흔 不公平을 본다.
그야 勿論 先駈者라면 主義에 殉하엿슬 따름이지 그네는 決코 큰 무덤, 燦爛한 祠堂, 竹帛에 흐른 傳記, 後裔의 讚仰等等을 바라는 것이 아니다. 그러나 무리되어서 그분들의 肉身을 江邊모래벌에 遺棄하고 그분들의 일홈을 몬지와 가치 즛밟고 지나가서는 안 된다. 孫文墓, 레-닌墓, 華盛頓墓, 칼발틔墓를 가지고도 倫敦, 巴里 伯林, 莫斯科等 處處에 無名戰士의 무덤을 가지고 잇는 列國의 例를 보아도 這間의 消息을 알 것이 아니냐. 그 獨自의 무덤조차 못가진 그분들의 功血을 紀念하는데서 先人에 대한 禮儀를 직히고 한편 제 自身의 鼓舞, 激勵, 暗示를 밧는 機緣을 지음이 오로지 先進 國民, 先進 市民의 無名犧牲者에 대한 聰明한 態度엇다.
이때에 잇서 우리는 만히 생각할 材料를 가진다. 最近 忠武公 墓所의 悲運이 針이 되어 社會人士의 先人에 대한 至情이 끄러 萬餘圓의 誠金이 모여지고 잇다. 이것을 一忠武公 追慕事業에만 너허버리지 말고 近代의 무덤조차 못가진 숨은 思想運動, 政治, 社會, 人文科學 等 온갓 方面의 先駈者을 紀念하는 事業에까지 展開식혀야 聰明한 努力이 될 것이다. 無名義人의 墓! 아하 벌서부터 가지고 잇서 조흘 것이 아니엇든가. 그래서 행혀 뜻잇는 이 땅의 後孫들이 가을 봄철 차저 꼿도 뿌리고 눈물도 뿌리게 함이 떳떳하지 안엇든가.
白寬洙, 安在鴻, 李光洙, 尹致昊, 宋鎭禹 等 當事者 諸氏여 오히려 忠武公의 本意가<70> 이 這間에 잇슬 것을 잇지 말고저 劃時期的의 紀念事業에 際하야 이 方面에 대한 努力이 잇기를 바라노라.

거지

京義線車 타려 南大門停車場으로 나갓더니 아직도 車시간이 두어시간이나 남어 잇길래 待合室에서 기다리기 어러워 附近 카페-집에 드러가 茶를 請하얏다.
그때 압설에 冬栢꼿가치 빨가케 연지 찍은 日女하나가 방글방글 우스며 써-비스로 나온다. 그러다가 웃는 설내에 그만 입에 물엇든 구슬사탕 한 개가 딱다그르 떠러저 문지방 잇는데로 구을너 나간다. 그때에 마츰 문박갓 애쓰팔트우로 희파람 휙휙 불며 지나가든 찌저진 옷 입은 어린 거지 하나가 얼는 그 사탕을 집어 제 입에 너코 웃지도 안코 지나가 버린다.
女給은 「まあ, いやらしい, 塵もとらないで-」
한마듸를 남기고 허리를 안코 죽을드시 웃는다. 나는 여기에서 어느 集團의 宿命的인 엇든 暗影을 바라보고 不快하여 곳 뛰어나왓다.
누가 그 少年을 嘲笑조차 感覺하지 못하게 주우리게 만들엇나.
나의 구두발미테 채우는 애쓰팔트에서는 뚜걱뚜걱하는 沈鬱한 音響이 내 自身의 가슴을 더욱 壓迫할 뿐.

虛無僧的 放歌

棺속에라도 조와 그 사람 업는 곳에 하로 이틀만 살다 나왓스면, 앗가운 7,8年에 엇은 것이 晩覺과 後悔의 心火뿐일네.

왕대바눌노 마듸마듸 이 살을 찔너 보아라. 내 온몸에 不純한 피 한 방울이나 잇든가, 木石이어 惡緣이어.

내가 언제 道德君子되려 햇든가. 다만 사람답게 살기를 願햇슬뿐, 이 뜻을 누가 막나. 막는다는 것 내 목숨을 달나는 일일네.

轉轉 4년의 지친 몸을 夜半三更에 스사로우노라. 이 肉身이 이 苦惱의 불길우에 몃칠이나 더 지탱할가. 아아, 나는 나의 나이라 猛然히 壁을 차고 이러나는 이 한밤중.

차고 이러나서 소낙비뛰 두날개 툭툭 치고 큰 소리치는 저 학두룸가치 너른 세상에 두 활개 훨이 훨이 치고 갈거나.<71>
