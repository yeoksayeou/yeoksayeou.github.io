The Beggar
						金東仁
It is a frightening world.
A world like today, where purpose and result, cause and effect, are so twisted, is too frightening to simply dismiss with the explanation that it is the "End Times" (末世).
I committed a murder. Of a vagrant (漂浪客)... .
- Only darkness lies in your future. For you, life is no different from "suffering." Joy? Happiness? Peace? Can you even dream of such things in your future? I declare, in your future, there is only darkness, suffering, and hardship. From this gate to the next, and then the next gate, seeking a lump of rice—or perhaps a penny coin—it is your fate to drag your worn-out shoes for the rest of your life. And that is an act that insults even your death.—Did I kill that vagrant out of such sympathy?
- Your existence is a cancer upon the world. When you stand at someone's gate, the housewife of that house cannot help but record another penny spent in the household ledger (家計簿). After you leave a house, they cannot help but spend some amount on disinfectant to break the bowl you ate from. Countless lice wander near where you sleep. Many daughters-in-law do a double washing because of you. People avoid your vicinity, so your single existence makes this already small Earth even smaller. Existing without providing any benefit to the world, your existence causes much inconvenience. Therefore, your "existence" is not as good as "extinction."—Did I kill that vagrant with such a world-saving (活世的) meaning?

The house was completely empty. The servant boy (행낭 아범) had gone out to earn money. The maid (어멈) had gone to church with the Mistress (主婦, my wife). The children had gone out to play. Inside the house, there was no one but me. Originally, my wife and I had planned to use this Sunday to go for a walk together, but it was cancelled due to my cold symptoms.
Keeping watch over the house alone was eerie. Moreover, experiencing this for the first time, my nerves were extremely on edge. Even the slightest sound at the gate made my ears perk up. Even when a cat ran across the yard, I opened the door and looked out. There were even times when, though there was no sound, I felt like I heard something and went out to inspect every nook and cranny. Amidst this, I discovered a husbandly aspect of my wife and couldn't help but smile wryly to myself. And I waited eagerly for the church service to end and for her to return.
Creak! Suddenly, the gate made a small sound. Lying down, I reflexively lifted my head from the pillow. And I focused all my nerves on listening. Creak again! The gate opened a little more.
I knew it wasn't my wife returning. My wife, the owner of lively footsteps, would not open the gate so listlessly.<81> After that, I could hear faint footsteps entering the gate area. Then, the sound of someone humming something indistinctly came from inside the gate.
I abruptly stood up and went out to look. And I saw a person—a vagrant—standing inside the gate. No, to put it accurately, I discovered some object in the shape of a person leaning against the wall, lying down.
As I cast eyes of wonder and unease upon this strange creature, some unintelligible humming sound came from its mouth again.
I went back into the room, took out a one-jeon coin from my wallet, and came out. I was about to throw the gift towards him, but seeing his appearance, extremely weakened and seemingly unable even to move his body freely, I went a little closer to him, stretched out my arm, and held out the charitable offering before him.
He glanced at the money. But he did not try to take it. He hummed something again.
"What is it?"
I asked in return. He hummed something again.
"What is it?"
I asked again. And straining my ears, I barely understood that he was asking for a lump of cold rice rather than money.
I looked at him again. He was not yet a seasoned vagrant. Perhaps this path today was his maiden begging (處女求乞) attempt. A man likely just over 40, still healthy, but his complexion was very poor and his back was bent due to hunger and malnutrition. He did not let out the pleading cries peculiar to beggars. He did not show vile blinking eyes.
- I haven't eaten in four days.
- I lost my job.
Although his pronunciation was unclear due to hunger, his appeal, spoken word by word, phrase by phrase, was a common plea of people facing the modern era, and it moved my heart.
I put the one-jeon coin I had held out back into my pocket and went into the kitchen. However, being unfamiliar with the kitchen, I couldn't tell where anything was. I searched all the shelves (시렁) and cupboards (찬짱). Then I found some stew (찌개) on a shelf and discovered my leftover rice in a cupboard. I considered taking it out to him as it was, but changing my mind, I poured about half the stew into the rice bowl to make it easy to mix (뷔비기), stuck a spoon (숫갈) in it, and brought it out to the vagrant.
He took it and abruptly sat down. The next moment, he was transferring the rice to his mouth with terrifying speed. Watching this, I quickly went back into the room, took out a 50-jeon silver coin, and tossed it beside him.
I said I quickly went into the room. In fact, I had gone into the room at full speed. Possessing the disposition of a modern man, even while showing kindness (효의) to the vagrant, I could not let go of my wariness. To guard the spoon and bowl, worth only a few pennies, I came back out and ceaselessly kept a wary eye on him, pretending not to. And if he had secretly tucked the spoon (슷갈, worth no more than 10 jeon) <82> into his waistband, I would certainly not have hesitated to run up and slap his cheek (따귀).
The vagrant was an honest man. For a while, he was absorbed only in eating, but after finishing, he first cast his eyes on the money I had tossed earlier. And there, he unexpectedly discovered the 50-jeon silver coin. He hesitated, picked it up, looked back and forth between me and the coin a couple of times, then
"Sir (나리),"
he called, looking for me.
I understood his heart. I blushed. Just moments before, because of my strange wariness... And I told him not to worry (념녀말고) and to take the 50-jeon coin.
He thanked me several times. Then, with steps vastly different and more energetic than when he arrived, he left.
After sending him off, I returned to the room, covered myself with the blanket, and lay down. Savoring the pleasure felt from things like charity, relief, and sympathy...
My wife returned. As soon as she came back, she changed her clothes, and then, as if busy with something, she immediately rushed out to the kitchen.
"I wonder if it ate..."
She mumbled a strange phrase whose meaning I didn't grasp. Then, having gone out, she immediately came back in.
"It ate half of it."
Again, words I couldn't comprehend, spoken to herself...
"Ate what?"
I asked, looking up at her from where I lay.
"Hmm? Oh, the rats were getting too bold, so I mixed arsenic (亞砒酸) into the stew and left it out, and it ate half of it. Who knows when it ate it, but they must be all dead by now..."
What? I shut my eyes forcefully. My muscles trembled (부들부들).
"Which stew?"
"The beef stew on the shelf..."
Ah. I had fed the vagrant arsenic. I lifted my legs, then dropped them. I lifted my head, then dropped it. Strange groans escaped my mouth.
"What's wrong?"
"Ugh-mm."
"Do you have a fever?"
"N-no."
"Then?"
"Please give me some cold water."
She said she had put two grams of arsenic in the stew. Then the vagrant must have eaten at least one gram. In his completely empty stomach, the arsenic would exert its full power. By now, he might already be a corpse.
The human body is strange. Although I had cold symptoms, my body, which hadn't shown a fever, suddenly began to develop one. Moment by moment, the fever worsened. A little later, I had become a seriously ill patient. Amidst this, I ceaselessly saw the image of that vagrant from earlier, gaunt and pale. And I kept thinking, if he had to die, let him die somewhere I don't know, during a time I don't know. And even after death, let the news not reach my ears.
Suddenly, I heard some chattering sound. Feverish,<83> I seemed to have drifted off to sleep at some point. Startled awake, I listened and realized it was the children, who had gone out to play, returning.
"Mother! Mother!"
My thirteen-year-old eldest child came in chattering.
"Shh. Be quiet. Your father isn't feeling well."
My wife stopped the chattering child.
But the children seemed unable to keep the precious report they brought back to themselves. This time, the seven-year-old girl, whispering (though their breathless voices were hardly whispers), called again.
"Mother! Mother!"
"What is it?"
"Someone died over there."
"What kind of person?"
"A beggar."
What? I shot up.
"Where? Where?"
"Father. A person - a beggar -"
"Where is it? Where?"
"Over- there. Right nearby."
I stood up, flung the door open, and ran out. Without even looking back at my wife, who followed asking in surprise where I was going.
I discovered—a crowd of people gathered at a distance. And among them, a policeman's cap bobbed up and down. Several sanitation workers were spraying disinfectant with pumps as they moved around.
Running up, I saw, right in the middle of the human enclosure, the vagrant from earlier lying quietly almost naked (거즉아레). Around him lay vomit soaked with disinfectant.
"Address and name unknown."
"Possessions: one 50-jeon silver coin. Four 1-jeon copper coins."
"Died from cholera (醫士호렬자)." (*Translator's note: This likely means 'Suspected Cholera Victim' or 'Death diagnosed as cholera by doctor', though 호렬자 usually means cholera.*)
"Estimated age 42."
Fragments of such talk reached my ears. A little later, the body was taken away for temporary burial (가매장). After that, the onlookers also dispersed. But I remained standing there, stunned.
Someone tapped my shoulder (억기). I turned around; it was a policeman.
"Why are you standing here like this?"
"No reason. Just... such a tragic fate."
"Do you know that person?" (Continued on page 69) <84>
"Yes."
"Who is he? Where does he live?"
"I don't know that. He came begging at my house earlier, so I gave him a 50-jeon silver coin and sent him away. The 50-jeon silver coin among his possessions is that one."
Even here, I couldn't bring myself to say I had given him food.
The policeman looked at me. Then he shook his head a couple of times.
"It seems there's no choice but for the 50 jeon you charitably gave to go into the National Treasury (國庫). What relative would he have? Even if he had one, who would step forward prepared to bear the funeral expenses? It's a common occurrence."
And then he went on his way.
The rice I gave out of sympathy for his hunger took his life. The money I gave out of sympathy for his poverty ended up going to the national treasury. That day, I wrote this in my diary:
"Modern people, who cannot even show compassion without strict scrutiny (吟味), are truly miserable." (End) <69>

**Summary**

The narrator, feeling unwell and alone at home, encounters a starving beggar. Moved by pity, he gives the man leftover rice and stew, along with a 50-jeon coin. Shortly after, his wife returns and reveals she had laced the stew with arsenic to kill rats. Horrified, the narrator realizes he has inadvertently poisoned the beggar. Later, his children report finding a dead beggar nearby. The narrator rushes out to find the scene, overhears officials recording the death (mistakenly attributing it to cholera), and confirms the beggar's only possession was the 50-jeon coin he gave. He reflects bitterly on the tragic irony: his act of compassion led to death, his monetary gift went to the state, concluding that modern life makes even simple charity a potentially dangerous act requiring careful consideration.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the poverty and desperation prevalent in colonial Korea (implied by the date and context), where begging and starvation were common sights. The casual mention of arsenic used for rat poison reflects sanitation issues and perhaps a certain normalization of harsh realities. The narrator's internal conflict—pity mixed with suspicion and class-based wariness towards the poor—and his ultimate, devastatingly ironic failure of charity highlight the social anxieties and moral complexities of the era. The detail about the 50 jeon going to the "National Treasury" (國庫) subtly points to the colonial state apparatus.

거지
						金東仁
무서운 세상이다.
목젹과 걸과, 의사와 事後가 이러틋 억으러지는 지금 세상은 末世라는 간단한 설명으로 넘겨버리기에는 너무도 무서운 세상이다.
余는 살인을 하엿다. 한 漂浪客을... .
- 그대의 장내에는 암담이 노혀 잇슬 뿐이외다. 삶이라 하는 것은 그대에게 잇서서는 「苦」라는 것과 조금도 다름이 업사외다. 樂? 喜? 安? 그대는 그대의 장내에서 이런 것을 夢想이라도 할 수 잇슬가? 余는 단언하노니 그대의 장내에는 暗과 苦와 辛이 잇슬뿐이외다. 이 문깐에서 저깐으로 또 그다음 문깐으로, 한덩이의 밥을 구하기 위하여- 혹은 한푼의 동전을 엇기 위하여, 그대의 그 해여진 신을 종신토록 끄으는 것이 그대의 운명이겟사외다. 그러고 그것은 그대의 죽음조차 侮辱하는 행동이외다.- 余는 이러한 동정심으로 그 漂浪客을 죽엿든가.
