Views on Chastity and Love of Proletarian and Bourgeois Female Students

The thinking of female students is currently undergoing great upheaval. As society itself is currently experiencing significant mental and material turmoil, it is only natural that the thoughts and emotions of female students, who breathe the air of the modern era, cannot help but be shaken. Recently, however, this trend has become noticeably more pronounced.

Now, good material has been provided to us to observe this aspect concerning marriage and love. This comes from a certain girls' school in Seoul, where an anonymous vote was conducted among all students to glimpse the thoughts within their hearts.

What draws the most attention is that this distinguishes between bourgeois female students and proletarian female students. That is:
A = Daughters from families of executives, landowners, wealthy merchants, aristocrats
B = Daughters from middle-class or lower families who must support their parents and siblings after graduation

"First, after getting to know [the person]."
A = 99 out of 100

Looking at this, among A, there are absolutely no students hoping for an arranged marriage (媒介結婚). However, among the propertyless class, many female students do not consider marriage separate from economic life, and state they would marry after getting to know the partner even if love does not develop. This clearly shows an attitude of yielding to social forces.
The fact that 96% of A students and 79% of B students hope for a partner with assets shows that A's view of love is calculating (打算的), while B holds practical hopes. It is also quite a spectacle that the children of the upper class, who completely deny arranged marriages (媒介결혼), are in reality having their marriages dictated by strategic family traditions (政略因襲).

Indeed, it is an accurate statistic that among university and vocational school students, the average number of virgins (童貞者) is only 51 out of 100. Innocent virgins are realizing this reality. Of course, this is because they are modern female students who recognize <70> and understand the social deficiencies that lead to this situation.

**Summary**

This 1932 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents findings from an anonymous survey conducted at a girls' school in Seoul, contrasting the views of bourgeois ('A') and proletarian ('B') female students on chastity, love, and marriage. Bourgeois students overwhelmingly favoured love matches and rejected arranged marriages, yet showed more calculation regarding a partner's wealth and were less insistent on premarital virginity or lifelong fidelity compared to proletarian students. Proletarian students, while also valuing virginity and fidelity, were more accepting of arranged marriage and marrying without love, likely due to economic pressures, and were far more likely to expect to work after marriage and support their families. Both groups showed awareness of contraception, though bourgeois students claimed higher knowledge. For historians, the article offers a valuable glimpse into the evolving social consciousness, class divisions, and changing gender attitudes among educated young women in colonial Korea, revealing tensions between modern ideals (love marriage, individual choice) and traditional/economic realities, particularly stark between different social strata. The differing views on work, family support, and even location preference (Seoul vs. countryside) underscore the significant impact of socio-economic background on life choices and aspirations during this period.
