女裁判官 (Female Judge)
						許秀萬 (Hŏ Su-man)

Female judge! One cannot help but feel that the name itself sounds novel. However, there is no need to be so surprised. Now, I wish to briefly introduce the Soviet female judge.

In Moscow, there are 38 People's Courts. Half of the judges there are women. The majority of them are young women.
Here, one cannot help but take Mrs. Natalia Mikhailovna (나다리아 미카엘나) as an example. She is 27 years old this year and a graduate of Moscow University. It was not a law college, because the Soviet Union (쏘베트 同盟) has no law schools—those aspiring to be judges achieve this by completely studying Marx (맑스), Engels (엥겔쓰), Lenin (레-닌), and the Soviet "Code of Laws" (法典).
The courts of that country exist for the workers, and their laws are written with the purpose of protecting them.
According to Mikhailovna, the People's Courts of the Soviet Union handle all crimes except for political matters. Dishonest workers in factories, murderers, those who do not support their children, those who fought over housing issues... all are judged by the People's Court. If one is dissatisfied with the verdict, they can appeal to the regional court, and further still, go up to the Supreme Court (大審院). When Mrs. Mikhailovna was asked, "Can you manage well as a female judge?" she smiled faintly and said,
"When working in the Soviet Union, we do not think about the distinction between men and women.
Regardless of sex, anyone does the work if they are suited for it. Needless to say, women are succeeding as judges. If they were not, wouldn't there be fewer female judges?"
Soviet judges work four and a half hours each day, together with two workers. These two workers, usually one man and one woman, come from factories or rural villages for a week to maintain contact with the court, and after a week, they are replaced by new people. The salary of a People's Court judge is said to be 240 units (弗) per month.<43>
The people and the courts are in close contact. If a case concerns a factory, a special feature is that the court hearing might take place at the factory. Workers come running to assist with the judge's work.
In 1931, the cases Mikhailovna handled amounted to 4,790 civil cases and 1,409 criminal cases; one-fourth of the civil cases were housing issues. Let us take a glimpse at the courtroom scene where the judge, accompanied by workers, resolves a case together.
Curtains hang on the three large glass windows of the courtroom. There are no decorations whatsoever in the courtroom, only wooden benches untidily arranged, crudely marked with the initials for Plaintiff and Defendant. A picture of Lenin is placed on the table. That is the judge's bench of the court. A Red Army soldier leans against the back corner, and two men are discussing a factory issue. Mikhailovna sits at the judge's bench. To her right is Fedel "Tozharovich" (페델 「토쟈로잇치」), a railway worker with a beard. To her left is "Tatyana Kozlova" (타데이안나―코스로아)! This woman is responsible for clothing distribution at a factory. She appears tired, but is actually lively and somehow gives a sense of humor.
In front of the judge's bench, three men stand quietly. In the row behind them, their friends are seated.
"Mikhailovna" called the locomotive engineer. Presently.
"Name? Be aware that if you do not state the facts in this court, you will be subject to appropriate punishment." The engineer [summary of statement]—I allowed this man (the person next to him) to live with me in my room. I work at the factory. I told this person he could stay for only six months and then had to move out.
I asked this person to sign a document stating he would move out by June, but even though he was going to enter a new dormitory in June, he not only refused to sign but instead told me to leave.
Student: The engineer does not let me live in his room. Therefore, he has no such right. I have never even had a glass of cold water in that room. Ask the people nearby. Six months have passed, but this person has never entered the room.
Judge: "Six months?"
Engineer: "I had travel for special duties. But during that time, I always paid the rent."
Student: "That's right. It was I who paid." Engineer: "Hmm, that's right. You paid<44>—with the money I sent you..."
Judge (to the student): "How long have you been residing in that room?"
Student: "Two and a half years."
Judge: "Was the purpose temporary cohabitation, or was it a permanent agreement?"
Engineer: "No, it was temporary. I wanted him to leave after I returned from my travels. At first, he was going to leave, but later he said he wouldn't budge an inch. So, he circulated a petition among the apartments claiming I didn't live there, collecting signatures from people who couldn't read. This is the petition."
Student: "Judge! I know absolutely nothing about such a petition."
At this time, a female witness wearing a black coat and a woolen scarf on her head was called before the judge.
Judge: "Did you sign this paper for this student?"
Witness: "Yes."
Judge: "Did you read it?"
Witness: "No. I cannot read."
At this moment, a person from the audience shouted loudly.
"Presiding Judge! Please pass judgment on that fellow somehow. This case is so troublesome, we cannot continue living like this."
"Mikhailovna" announced that the court would deliberate. Then, she entered the deliberation room with the two accompanying worker judges (陪席判事). Soon, it seemed a decision was reached without much disagreement. The three returned.
"The student must move out by June 1st." And the female judge added, "If you are dissatisfied with this verdict, you can appeal to the district court."

This is the new way of thinking and system operation of the female judge.<45>

**Summary**

The article introduces the role of female judges in the Soviet Union to a Korean audience in 1932. It highlights that half the judges in Moscow's People's Courts are women, often young, trained not in traditional law schools but through studying Marxist-Leninist ideology and Soviet law. The piece emphasizes gender equality, stating men and women perform roles based on suitability, not sex. It describes the court system as worker-focused, with judges collaborating with lay assessors from factories or farms, and even holding hearings in factories. An example court case involving a housing dispute is detailed to illustrate the process, presided over by a female judge named Mikhailovna alongside two worker assessors. For historians, this article is interesting as it presents a positive view of the Soviet judicial system, particularly its egalitarian aspects concerning gender and class, within a magazine published under Japanese colonial rule in Korea. This could reflect a genuine interest in alternative social systems, a subtle critique of colonial inequalities, or perhaps just the specific editorial line of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine at the time, navigating the complexities of information dissemination under censorship.

女재판관! 일홈부터 처음 듯는듯한 感이 不無하다. 그러나 그리 놀낼 것은 업다. 이제 간단하나마 쏘베트 女재판관을 소개코저 하는 바이다.

모스코-에서는 38個所의 인민재판소가 잇다. 거기에 半은 婦人재판관이 잇다. 그들의 대부분은 절문 여성들인 것이다.
이곳서 나다리아 미카엘나여인을 例題로 삼지 안을 수 업다. 그는 今年 27세이다. 모스코대학 출신이다. 그것이 法科대학은 안이다. 쏘베트 同盟에는 법률학교가 업스니까―判事희망자는 맑스엥겔쓰 及 레-닌 그리고 쏘베트의 「法典」을 완전히 연구하면 성취하는 것이다.
그 나라의 法廷은 노동자때문에 존재하며 其法律은 彼等의 보호를 목적 삼아 쓴 것이다.
미카엘나의 말에 의한다면 쏘베트同盟의 人民재판소는 정치관계를 제외하고서는 凡犯罪를 취급한다고 한다. 공장에 잇서서 不正노동자 살인범 幼兒를 扶養치 안는 者 居室문제로서 싸혼 者... 등등은 모다 인민재판소에서 判決하여 주는 것이다. 만일 判決이 不服이면 지방재판소에 上告할 수 잇수며 更히 大審院까지 갈 수 잇는 것이다. 미카엘나婦人께 「婦人재판관으로서 잘 해 나갈 수 잇슴니까」 問한즉 그 婦人은 한번 빙그시 웃고 말하엿다.
「쏘베트同盟에서 일할 때에 우리들은 남자와 여자와의 구별에 말을 생각지 안슴니다.
性에 관계치한코 적합한 일이면 누구든지 하는 것님니다. 더 말할 것업시 재판관으로서 婦人은 성공하고 잇슴니다. 만일 그러치 못한다면 만흔 女재판관이 업슬 것이 아님니까?」
쏘베트의 判事는 2人의 노동자와 갓치 매일 4시간반 근무하는 것이다. 此2명의 노동자는 보통 一便에는 남자고 對一便에는 여자로서 재판소와 연락을 取키 위하야 공장이나 농촌으로부터 來參하기를 一週間이나 一週間 후에는 새사람을 교환하는 것이다. 인민재판소 判事의 給料는 每月 240弗이라 한다.＜43＞
국민과 재판소가 밀접히 접촉하는 것이다. 만일 사건이 공장에 관한 사건이면 法廷이 공장화하는 特點이 잇다. 노동자가 달려와서 判事의 일을 돌보아 주는 것이다.
1931년도에 미카엘나가 취급한 사건은 民事사건이 4,790件과 刑事사건이 1,409件으로서 民事사건의 4分之1은 居室문제이엿다. 재판관이 노동자외 同伴와 공동으로 사건해결의 法廷光景을 엿보아 보기로 하자.
法廷의 3個의 큰 가라스窓에는 카텐이 걸녀 잇다. 法廷에는 아모러한 裝飾도 업시 다만 原告 被告의 頭文字를 난폭히 쓴 木펜취가 不整頓히 羅列하엿슬 뿐이다. 레-닌의 사진이 데-불에 노여 잇다. 그것이 재판소의 判事席이다. 赤軍兵士가 後便 취우에 허리를 걸엇고 2명의 남자가 공장문제를 論하고 잇다. 재판관席에는 미카엘나가 안저 잇다. 그 여자의 右手便에는 수염자랜 鐵道노동자인 페델 「토쟈로잇치」 그 여자의 左手便에는 「타데이안나―코스로아」 이! 여자는 공장의 의복給與 책임자이다. 피곤한 모양으로 뵈어나 實은 즉 활발한 그 어대인가 유모아적 感을 갓게 하는 婦人이다.
재판관席의 압페는 3명의 남자가 靜立하고 잇다. 뒤엣 列에는 彼等의 友人인 안저 잇다.
「미카엘나」는 機關手를 불넛다. 이윽고.
「姓名은? 此法廷에 잇서서 사실을 申告하지 안는 때는 상당한 罰에 처할 것으로 생각하시오」 機關手 前略―나는 이 남자(겻헤사람)에게
나의 방에 同居를 許하엿든 것임니다. 나는 공장에서 일하고 잇슴니다. 나는 이 사람께 6개월까지만 잇고는 退房을 말햇슴니다.
나는 이 사람에게 6월까지 退房하리라는 證書에 署名을 요구하엇지만 이 사람은 6월에는 새로히 기숙사에 드러감에도 不抱하고 署名을 거절할 뿐더러 반대로 나에게 退去하라고 하는 것임니다.
