Don José Rizal, the Ardent Poet of the Philippines
						Saebalk

Mr. Rizal was born on June 4, 1862 [sic], in Calamba, Laguna Province, Luzon Island, Philippines. At age 13, after finishing the municipal school there, he left his hometown for the University of Manila on the same island. As he left, he composed a song titled "Meeting by the Pasig Riverbank," surprising people. He wrote a poem called "To the Filipino Youth," winning first prize at the Liceo Artístico-Literario. He gained fame by submitting a piece titled "The Council of the Gods" to the Astronomical Society, which hosted the centenary celebration for the Spanish literary giant, Mr. Cervantes. In 1882, he entered the University of Madrid in Spain's capital, obtaining a Doctor of Medicine degree, and received a Licentiate degree in Philosophy and Literature. Afterward, he toured Europe. Besides his native Tagalog and Spanish, he mastered Latin, Greek, and Hebrew. Wishing to study Japanese literature and art, he learned Japanese, and also knew French, English, German, and Italian. His medical achievements were numerous; medical dictionaries list a type of scabies named after him. During his stay in Europe in 1886, he published a novel-like book titled "Noli Me Tángere" (Touch Me Not), exposing the inevitability that the Spanish government could not permanently rule the Philippine Islands and the [oppression] of the Spanish Catholic priests towards those islands. This masterpiece was translated and published in France. Returning to Manila in 1887, he faced the animosity of the Spanish Catholic priests while researching and publishing works on his mother country's literature and customs. He then crossed the Pacific, toured the United States, and set foot in Europe again, staying in London where he perused ancient classics with the courtesy of the museum. In 1890, passing through Paris, he reached Madrid from Barcelona, Spain, where he headed the magazine "La Solidaridad," spewing forth [patriotic] fervor. Then, moving to Ghent in Belgium, in 1891 he published the political novel "El Filibusterismo" (The Reign of Greed), a sequel to "Noli Me Tángere," themed around the [suffering] of the Filipino people groaning under Spain's [rule]. Unable to ignore the yearning of his compatriots, he decided to return to his homeland in 1891. Just then, disturbances broke out in his hometown of Calamba. Landing in San Francisco, United States, on December 31 of that year, he sent a letter to the Spanish Governor-General of the Philippines, Despujol, stating the necessity of resolving the Calamba incident and requesting protection for himself. The Governor-General thanked him for his goodwill and guaranteed his safety. As soon as Rizal arrived in Manila in July 1892, Despujol arrested him<46> and sentenced him to indefinite exile in Dapitan on Mindanao Island in that country. During his full four years there, he established a charity school for the islanders, educating the children, opened an eye clinic to treat the poor, taught modern farming methods to improve agriculture, and engaged in scholarly and literary writing. Indeed, it was during this time that he drafted a thesis on Tagalog verbs in English. In August 1896, Spain moved him to Manila. On September 3, he was sent to Barcelona on the steamship "Isla de Panay" to be dealt with by the Minister of War and the Minister of Colonies. The then Governor-General of the Philippines, Blanco, tried his utmost to intervene on his behalf, but the Catholic Church, determined to have him executed, conspired diligently. Arriving safely in Barcelona on October 6 of that year, he was imprisoned in Montjuïc Castle. At 2 PM that day, he was interrogated by Despujol (the former Governor-General of the Philippines who had deceived and arrested him, now Governor-General of Barcelona). At 5 PM that day, escorted by a police officer, he returned to Manila on October 13 and was imprisoned in Fort Santiago. On December 30, 1896, he was executed by firing squad at the Bagumbayan execution ground. He was in the prime of his life, a full 25 years old! [sic]

His Last Poem – "My Final Thoughts"
My most beloved country,
Compared to nature's given pearl,
Known as the Garden of Eden,
I now leave you behind.

That my bitter life
Is forsaken for your sake brings joy,
And if I lived, gloriously
I would guard your path ahead,
Urging my compatriots, without doubt or regret,
To charge into the battlefield of the struggle for existence.
Even if crucified and vanishing as dew on the battlefield,
Or falling beneath the shade of trees,
If it is for the country, how could I refuse?

The dim twilight vanishes,
As the morning sun reddens,
I shall soon depart.<47>
When the new dawn light reddens,
Wring out my blood and pour it thereon,
To make that light even brighter.
Though I die, shall I utterly perish?
Another day, when that smiling face
Like a pearl in the East Sea
Shines its light upon the four corners,
My spirit—my devotion—
Will seek to see it once more.

Ah, though you may grieve that I am going,
I, for your [freedom],
Depart your skies.
It brings joy to leave my soul forever in this land.
Another day, amidst the wild grass on my grave,
If a single lovely flower blooms,
Know that my soul resides there.
May your warm breath
Blow into the ice-cold coffin,
And I shall feel its touch upon my forehead.

If the deep moonlight shines upon me,
I will perceive it as the nation's glory;
If birds come and sing upon my grave,
Shall I but hear it as a song of peace?
The hot ocean steam
Carried my sorrow to the heavens.
Ah, my country!
When others pray for me,
With a pure heart, please
Pray for my afterlife.

When the deep night covers the grave,
And the lush pine trees strum a zither,
My most beloved country,
That will be the song I sing for you.
When my grave lies desolate,
With neither cross nor stone marker,
Why forbid even the farmer's plow?
Before my body crumbles,
May I become fertilizer with the grass in my country's garden,
Bringing joy by aiding my country's people.

My soul will always fly within your heavens and earth,
Becoming a clear musical score to your ears,
Letting my principles be heard by my compatriots.
My most beloved country,
My most beloved compatriots,
Please heed my bitter last words.

I leave my overflowing affection
Upon this land.
From here,
—One line omitted (original text)—
I go to the land of bliss.
Oh, Mother,
Brothers,
Beloved children,
Childhood friends,
Do not weep because I am leaving.
I escape the sea of suffering and go to the land of bliss.
Who knows, my compatriots,
Perhaps death is rest.<48>

**Summary**

This 1932 article from the Korean magazine *Samch'ŏlli* details the life and death of José Rizal, the Philippine national hero. It covers his birth, education in the Philippines and Europe (listing his linguistic and medical achievements), his famous novels *Noli Me Tángere* and *El Filibusterismo* which criticized Spanish colonial rule and the clergy, his exile to Dapitan, his final arrest, and execution by firing squad in 1896. The article concludes with a Korean translation of Rizal's final poem, "Mi último adiós" (presented here as "My Final Thoughts"). The article inaccurately states Rizal's birth year as 1862 (actually 1861) and his age at death as 25 (actually 35).

For historians studying Korean colonial history, this article is significant. Published during the height of Japanese colonial rule in Korea, portraying a national hero from another colonized nation (the Philippines under Spain) who fought against foreign oppression through literature and ultimately sacrificed his life serves as an implicit commentary on Korea's own situation. The emphasis on Rizal's intellectual achievements, patriotism, critique of colonial power, and martyrdom would have resonated deeply with Korean readers facing similar circumstances under Japan. The inclusion of his final poem, focusing on love for country and willingness to die for its future, acts as a veiled encouragement of Korean nationalism and resistance, albeit carefully framed to avoid direct confrontation with Japanese censors (note the use of 'XX' for potentially sensitive words).

「리사-루」氏는 1862년 6월 4일에 「필립핀」 「呂宋島」 「라꾸나」州 「카람빠」에 출생하야 13歲에 그곳 市立학교를 마추고 고향을 떠나 同島의 「마네라」대학으로 떠나면서 「하-시꾸」 河畔의 會合이라는 노래를 지어 사람을 놀내고 「필립핀 소년」이라는 詩歌를 지어 미술문예학교의 一等賞을 밧고 「스페인」文豪 「갈번데스」氏의 百年祭를 主催한 天文學會에 「天神회의」라는 一篇을 들여 이름을 날니엇스며 1882년에 스페인」의 서울 「마드리드」대학에 들어 醫學의 「딱터-」 學位를 엇고 철학과 문학은 「리센샤드」 學位를 엇은 뒤에 「유로파」를 遊覽하니 그는 제 種族 「다꺄로」語와 「스파인」語 이외에 「라틴」語 「끄리시아」語 히부리語를 통하고 「니혼」의 문예미술을 연구코저 하여 「니혼」語를 배우고 또 「프란쓰」語 「잉글리쓰」語 「떠이」語 「이탈리」까지 알앗고 醫學的 業蹟이 만하야 醫語字彙에는 그의 일음씨운 疥癢 一種의 病名이 잇스며 「유로파」 滯在中 1886년에 「스파인」政府가 「필립핀」群島를 永久히 XX할 수 업는 必然性과 「스파인」 천주교도의 그 群島에 대한―을 폭로하야 「노-리-메, 단헬레」 (그대는 나에게 간섭치 말라」는 소설體의 一書를 발표하니 이 걸작은 「프란쓰」에서 譯本이  나왓스며 1887년에 「마네라」로 돌아와 「스파인」 천주교도의 미움을 밧으면서 母國의 문학과 風習을 연구 발표하다가 태평양을 건너 合衆國을 遊覽하고 「유로파」 다시 밟아 「론돈」에 滯在하면서 박물관의 優待로 古典을 閱覽하고 1890년에 「파리」를 거처 「스페인」의 「빠-셀로나」로부터 「마드리드」에 이르러 「라소리따리다트」잡지를 主幹하야 ―的 氣焰을 吐하닊�가 「뻴끼」의 「깐』에 가서 1891년에 「스페인」의 XX에 呻吟하는 「필립핀」人의 XX을 題材로 한 정치소설 「엘, 휘리뿌스데리모스」 (流浪주의 「노-리-, 메, 단헬례」의 續篇을 발표하엿스며 同胞의 渴望을 저바릴 수 업서 1891년에 故國으로 도라오게 되엿는데 때마츰 고향 「카람빠」에 擾亂이 일어낫슴으로 合衆國의 「산프란씨스코」에 下陸하야 그해 12월 31일에 「스페인」의 「필립핀」總督 「떠스푸호루」 장군에게 글을 붓처 「카람빠」의 變을 해결함이 당연함을 말하고 自己의 보호를 講求하엿더니 저는 그의 好意를 謝禮하고 안전을 保證하엿슴으로 1892년 7월에 「마네라」로 오자마자 저는 그를＜46＞ 체포하야 無期流刑으로 그 나라의 「민따나오」島 「따피단」에 謫居케 하니 그는 이에 잇슨지 滿4년에 島民을 위하야 慈善학교를 세워 子弟를 교육하고 眼科병원을 열어 貧民을 치료하고 新式耕作을 가르처 농업을 改良하면서 學術 문예의 著術에 從事하니 「다까로」語의 動詞에 관한 논문을 「영어로 起草함은 實로 이때의 사실이엿스며 1896년 8월에 「스페인」은 그를 「마네리」로 음겨 9월 3일에 汽船 「이스라 파나이」號로 「빠-셀로나」에 보내여 陸軍大臣과 拓殖大臣으로 하여금 처분케 하니 그때의 「필립핀」總督 「뿌랑코」는 그를 위하야 極力周旋하엿스나 천주교에서는 死刑에 處케 하고저 하야 盡心陰謀하더니 그해 10월 6일에 「빠-셀로나」에 安着한 그는 「몬쥬익」城內에 抱禁되엿다가 그날 오후2시에 「떠스푸호루」 (그를 속여 체포한 元 「필립핀」總督, 現「빠-셀로나」總督)의 審問을 밧고 그날 오후5시에 一警官에게 押送되여 10월 13일에 「마네라」에 돌아와 「샨챠고」 堡壘에 監禁된엿다가 1896 년 12월 30일에 「빠꿈빠얀」 刑場에서 砲刑을 밧으니 芳年이 滿25!
