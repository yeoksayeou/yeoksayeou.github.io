North Wind Comes!
						Hwang Sŏk-u

Wild dance of the North Wind! Loud shouts of the North Wind! Charging cries of the North Wind!
The North Wind advances, the North Wind blows its clear trumpet and advances.
Oh- North Wind blows! Oh North Wind comes! Oh the general assault of the North Wind! Oh- the North Wind has mobilized.
The ranks of the North Wind, the array of the North Wind stretches for ten thousand leagues.
The North Wind surges forth like great waves swallowing the sea.
Oh- the North Wind rushes in at once like a fierce tiger squad escaped from the dense forest, like stark naked partisans' assault troops, like knightly orders, like volunteer corps, or like field soldiers sprung from ambush.
The North Wind cries out like the great wrath of heaven and comes pouring down.<90>

**Summary**

This poem by Hwang Sŏk-u personifies the North Wind as an overwhelming, militaristic force arriving with great power and fanfare. Using imagery of advancing armies, trumpets, mobilized troops, battle formations, raging waves, fierce tigers, partisans, knights, volunteers, and ambushing soldiers, the poem depicts the wind's arrival as an unstoppable, all-encompassing invasion, akin to the wrath of heaven. From a historical perspective, written in 1932 during the Japanese colonial period, the poem's intense militaristic metaphors and depiction of an overwhelming natural force could be interpreted by historians as reflecting the harsh atmosphere of the era, or potentially as a veiled expression of suppressed national energy or a desire for radical change, using nature as a powerful, uncontrollable metaphor.
