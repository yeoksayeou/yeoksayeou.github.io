**Body Text Translation:**

What Do Korean Officials Hope for Chosŏn?

Sin Ch'ang-sŏp, County Magistrate of Hongsŏng, Ch'ungch'ŏngnam-do

To expand educational institutions and swiftly implement compulsory education.

Chang Hŏn-gŭn, Council Member, Hamgyŏngbuk-to

1.  A mainland (Japanese) Buddhist master proficient in the Korean language.
2.  Middle-ranking youth with sound ideology.
3.  Various facilities established through charitable works.
4.  Improvement of medical institutions.

O Se-nam, Head of the Sanitation Department, Ch'ungch'ŏngbuk-to

Government-established orphanages.
The sight of young orphans, beggars, and vagrants wandering the streets in the severe cold of winter with nowhere to turn truly evokes heartbreaking feelings (斷腸의 念). I hope for the establishment of government-run orphanages in several locations within Chosŏn.

Yi Sŏn-ho, County Magistrate of Tŏgwŏn, Hamgyŏngnam-do

Mountain management (治山).
Flood control (治水).

Abe Tsune, County Magistrate of Tanch'ŏn, Hamgyŏngnam-do

1.  The provision of lodging expenses (宿料) to Korean officials as well, because Korean officials do not have their homes in the location of their workplaces.
2.  The implementation of the election law for the House of Representatives (衆議院).

Han Hong-sŏk, County Magistrate of Changjin, Hamgyŏngnam-do

1.  Implementation of military service obligation.
2.  Granting of political rights (suffrage).

Kim Hyŏng-sun, County Magistrate of Yangdŏk, P'yŏngannam-do

I earnestly desire the emergence of persons of high virtue who have truly transcended fame and fortune.

Kim Si-myŏng, County Magistrate of Hongch'ŏn, Kangwŏn-do

What I desire for Chosŏn is the spirit of self-reliant independence among the youth.<18> For Chosŏn to prosper, the youth must awaken from the conventional tendency of valuing salaried life (officialdom valued over the populace, 官尊民卑) and pioneer a new life in their chosen path with a spirit of self-reliance and independence. According to the old harmful custom (弊風), graduates from schools—be it university, professional school, middle school, or primary school—generally aim for employment. However, if they were to strive for independence using the knowledge acquired in school, they could secure a considerable income and stable livelihood, significantly invigorating various sectors of society (社社 각 방면에) and greatly contributing to the promotion of general welfare. Unless the spirit of Caesar—preferring to be the chicken's head (鷄頭) rather than the ox's rear (牛後)—becomes more pervasive, Chosŏn cannot prosper. <19>

**Summary**

This 1937 survey captures the hopes of various Korean officials serving under the Japanese colonial administration. Their desires range from practical improvements like expanding education (including compulsory education), establishing orphanages, enhancing healthcare, and undertaking public works (mountain/flood control), to addressing officials' welfare (lodging expenses). Some responses reflect accommodation or integration with the colonial system, such as wishing for Japanese Buddhist teachers proficient in Korean or advocating for military service. Others express aspirations for greater political autonomy, calling for suffrage (House of Representatives elections) and political rights. A strong theme emerging, particularly in Kim Si-myŏng's response, is the need for a fundamental shift in mindset among Korean youth away from seeking government positions towards self-reliant entrepreneurship and independence, seen as crucial for national prosperity. For historians, this article provides a valuable glimpse into the diverse perspectives and priorities of the colonized elite, revealing a mix of pragmatic concerns, assimilationist tendencies, and burgeoning nationalist sentiments focused on self-strengthening and political rights within the constraints of colonial rule. The call for a change in the traditional 'official over populace' mindset is particularly revealing.
