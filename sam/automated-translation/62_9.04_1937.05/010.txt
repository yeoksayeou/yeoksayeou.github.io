Article Type	대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

Publisher Kim Tonghwan
						Financial Magnates Speak on "Money and Business," Messrs. Han Sangnyong, Min Kyusik, Kim Kidŏk, Pak Hŭngsik, Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - 5 gentlemen

**How Much Capital Are You Working With?**

Kim Tonghwan (Our Company) - The public seems to think you are doing business with perhaps several million won or tens of millions of won. About how much money are you actually operating your businesses with?
Pak Hŭngsik - The public probably knows better than we do.
Kim Tonghwan - When people talk about the "Pak Hŭngsik Konzern," they count various directly and indirectly related companies. What is the capital structure like?
Pak Hŭngsik - Well, let's see. The businesses I'm currently involved in are Hwasin Department Store (和信백화점) with 2 million won, Sŏnil Chimul (鮮一紙物) with 500,000 won, Hwasin Chain Stores (和信연쇄점) with 2 million won, and Taedong Hŭngŏp (大同興業) with 2 million won. That's roughly the nominal capital stock. Among these, some have had only the first installment paid, while others have only had up to the second installment paid.
Kim Tonghwan - And indirectly related ones?
Pak Hŭngsik - Are you asking about those where I hold a director position? There are quite a few. Affiliated companies include Pukch'ŏn Paper Chemical Industry (北鮮製紙화학공업), Chosŏn Oil (朝鮮石油), Kyŏngsŏng Spinning (京城紡績), P'yŏngan Railway (平安 철도), and several others.
Kim Tonghwan - Then, how much money circulates annually through the four directly related companies you mentioned earlier – Hwasin, Hwasin Chain Stores, Taedong Hŭngŏp, and Sŏnil?
Pak Hŭngsik - Well, it's business. If the economic situation looks favorable, we might even borrow beyond our capital stock to operate, but generally, the amount of capital stock should explain the scope of our business.
Kim Tonghwan - The current view is that three major chaebol-like Konzerns are forming in Korea. First, the Kim Sŏngsu group; second, the Min Kyusik group; and third, the Pak Hŭngsik group. Mr. Min, how about your business?
Min Kyusik - Are you asking about the total investment amount?
Kim Tonghwan - That and the scope of your business.
Min Kyusik - Well, Yŏngbo Hapmyŏng (永保合名) is the main entity. Yŏngbo Hapmyŏng was formed by consolidating various farms, forests, and other miscellaneous businesses previously held individually into a corporation with a capital stock of 2.5 million won. Then there's Chosŏn Saengsa (朝鮮生絲), a 300,000 won company that took over Chosŏn Chesa (朝鮮製絲) located outside Tongdaemun Gate. And there's Tongbang Choksan (東邦殖産), which has a capital of 500,000 won with 200,000 won paid in. Besides these, there is one ice manufacturing company (製氷회사).
Kim Tonghwan - All together, does the total investment amount reach 4 or 5 million won?
Min Kyusik - It should be around that level.
Kim Tonghwan - Lately, many of Mr. Min's buildings are appearing on the streets of Seoul. How many buildings are there?
Min Kyusik - The Yŏngbo Building (永保삘딩) in Chongno 2-chŏme cost about 300,000 won for the building alone, excluding the land price. Mr. Pak Hŭngsik's Hwasin Department Store building cost about 200,000 won just for construction, excluding land. The one across from Tongil Bank (東一銀行) cost 60,000 or 70,000 won.
Kim Tonghwan - Including the land, the building investments alone would be over 1 million won, wouldn't they? What about the investment in Tongil Bank?
Min Kyusik - Well, I think our family holds more than half the shares. The bank's nominal capital stock is 2 million won.
Kim Tonghwan - I see. I roughly understand the outline. Mr. Kim, it was once said that most of the land in Najin belonged to you, and that Taech'odo and Soch'odo islands were entirely yours.
Kim Kidŏk - I sold some of the Najin land to the Navy and the South Manchuria Railway (滿鐵).
Kim Tonghwan - During Najin's peak period, some sources estimated your wealth at 3 million won? <3>
Kim Kidŏk - Who knows? Hahaha.
Kim Tonghwan - Currently, how many businesses are you invested in, and how much in each?
Kim Kidŏk - I have some land investments in the Pukch'ŏn region and Seoul, a timber business in the Musan area, and some involvement in North Manchuria (北滿) and other places.
Kim Tonghwan - Recently, in mining as well?
Kim Kidŏk - I've secured several dozen mining claims, but I don't have any mines under full-scale investment and management yet.
Kim Tonghwan - Land, timber, fisheries (sardines), mining – as your business scope is quite broad, the investment amount must also be substantial. Please give us specific numbers. Since your business network is organized into corporations with clear nominal capital, the public doesn't know much about it.
Kim Kidŏk - They will find out gradually. And what does it matter if they don't know?
Kim Tonghwan - Mr. Pak, only the bank?
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - My business is just continuing the family business, protecting the bank. As you all know, Sangŏp Bank (상업은행) has a capital stock of about 8.9 million won, but my shareholding is only about 6,700 shares. Besides that, I am only indirectly involved in several companies like Chosŏn Trust (朝鮮信托) and the broadcasting station.
Han Sangnyong - It's the same for me. I'm just protecting Chosŏn Life Insurance (朝鮮生命). Chosŏn Life is a 500,000 won company, and my shareholding is only 800 shares. Apart from that, I have some minor involvement in Chosŏn Trust and other companies... but is it even worth counting?

**Buildings, Paper Goods, Manchurian Investment**

Kim Tonghwan - (Our Company) In a place like Korea, which is currently trying to industrialize, isn't it outdated for capitalists to just hold onto land? If you compare the income from land cultivation with the income from stocks or securities, isn't the latter much better?
Min Kyusik - Since Korea's stage is inherently small, only a few emerging industries across the board are that profitable. However, the harvest from fields and paddies is constant, barring natural disasters. It's also reliable.
Kim Tonghwan - These days, with the Ministry of Finance (大藏省) even discussing transferring land taxes to local authorities, land tax actually isn't a major part of total taxation. Also, isn't clinging solely to land just carrying on the old notion from turbulent times that thieves might steal gold and jewels but can't carry away the land itself? As a practical example, how is the building rental business that you manage? Isn't it actually better?
Min Kyusik - In the city, especially in a city like Seoul which anticipates a population of one million in the future, managing buildings is an interesting business. As I mentioned earlier, we currently have three, but they are not yet generating huge profits.
Kim Tonghwan - Even if there aren't huge profits, it's surely much better than the 4 or 5 percent interest from banks, right?
Min Kyusik - Well, that's true.
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - I think so too. Wealthy people from the provinces who own land sometimes consult me, asking if they should sell their land, come up to Seoul, and manage a rental office building. But I always advise them not to, telling them to just hold onto their land and stay in the countryside. If people without modern management knowledge come up to Seoul to do business, they end up spending all their money and failing. I haven't calculated the profitability of the building business, but for now...
Kim Tonghwan - Currently in Seoul, Mr. Im Hŭngsun of the Imga Building has started construction, and there's a rumor that Mr. Sin O-u is also planning a 5 or 6-story building in Chongno. It seems several more will appear. Mr. Min, aren't you planning further expansion?
Min Kyusik - Well, we built because we already owned land in several places around Chongno. Now, there aren't many suitable places to build – and we don't have expansion plans yet.
Kim Tonghwan - When Noguchi's 8-story building in Hwanghŭm-chŏng is completed this fall, it will stimulate the building business. The question of land versus buildings will become a major issue again for asset holders in the city. By the way, how is Hwasin doing? It's not losing money, is it?
Pak Hŭngsik - No. It seems there are some rumors spread by those engaged in negative propaganda, but everything is proceeding smoothly.
Kim Tonghwan - There's talk that Sŏnil Chimul paid dividends as high as 40 percent. Does the paper goods (紙物) business yield such huge profits?
Pak Hŭngsik - Hahaha, how could that be possible? With clear market prices in Tokyo and Osaka, and even prices for foreign paper from places like Canada (加奈多) all transparent, how could there be a business that yields 30 or 40 percent profit?
Kim Tonghwan - If buildings and paper goods don't yield such huge profits, what about land? I heard recently that due to Nippon Steel (日鐵) and Mitsubishi developing the Musan iron mine, various industrial companies involved in rayon spinning, hydroelectric power, oil refining, etc., are flocking to North Korea (北鮮), turning it into an era of industrialization, and consequently, many land nouveaux riches have emerged. How about land investment in places like Najin and Ch'ŏngjin?
Kim Kidŏk - Land speculation yields huge profits only if you anticipate, say 10 or 20 years in advance, how an area might be used when industry develops and engage in large-scale land purchases (土地買占メ). If you just move according to the current market trends, you won't amass a great fortune, even if you catch a lucky break.
Kim Tonghwan - In that case, what are you gentlemen currently choosing and actively pursuing as the most advantageous investment target? Is it land in Manchuria? How much is invested in land within Manchukuo?
Min Kyusik - Our company called Tongbang Choksan (東邦殖産) was primarily established to invest in Manchuria. We put the 200,000 won paid-in capital into land there. However, this was also done with the long-term future, many years ahead, in mind.
Kim Tonghwan - The public rumor was that Mr. Min's investment in Manchuria was much larger.
Min Kyusik - It must be just a rumor. Hahaha, it's a 500,000 won company with only 200,000 won paid in, so how could we have invested more than the money that came in?
Kim Tonghwan - Mr. Kim?
Kim Kidŏk - I also invested some amount in places like Mutanchiang and North Manchuria (北滿) right after the Manchurian Incident, but the outcome is still uncertain.
Kim Tonghwan - Will land investment in Manchuria be profitable in the future?
Han Sangnyong - It's probably correct to view the speculative era as having passed. With public order established, settlement areas designated, and public sentiment stabilized, land prices have become somewhat fixed. Therefore, the era of broker-style (뿌로카-식) investment is over. However, investing steadily with the aim of large-scale farming for profitable harvests should be good at any time.
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - I think so too. In the past, Koreans investing in Manchukuo land likely didn't suffer losses, and it should remain profitable in the future as well.

**Film and Theater Business?**

Kim Tonghwan - In Tokyo, Mr. Kobayashi Ichizō has entered the film and theater sectors with significant capital and achieved great success. Don't you gentlemen feel any interest in venturing into these fields?
Pak Hŭngsik - If the concentration of population in cities becomes more advanced, and culture becomes more widespread and sophisticated, wouldn't there come a day when the calculations (珠算) add up, leaving no reason for capitalists not to enter? When Seoul reaches 5 million, P'yŏngyang 3 million, Taegu 2 million – like Tokyo's 7 million,<4> Osaka's 5 million, Nagoya's 3 million – if the urban population density increases like this, there will surely be many good businesses that serve social culture and are profitable according to calculations.
Min Kyusik - That's right. If you ask whether one can invest, say, one million won into the film business and establish it as an independent enterprise with a distribution network within Korea, it seems difficult still.
Kim Tonghwan - It's said that the annual consumption of records in the Korean market is 4 to 5 million discs, amounting to 4 to 5 million won. With such a consumer base, wouldn't it be a profitable business?
Han Sangnyong - I don't know if the current management status of various companies balances income and expenditure, but in the future, there will surely come a day when anyone can see that it's a worthwhile business.
Kim Tonghwan - Currently, businesses like film, theater, and records are incredibly interesting, and many young entrepreneurs are struggling immensely to establish them. They are just suffering from a lack of funds. Besides these, wasn't the management of amusement parks a business where Kobayashi Ichizō profited? For example, the Takarazuka (寶塚) hot springs resort outside Osaka, centered around hotels, theaters, etc. What are your views on such ventures?
Pak Hŭngsik - When Seoul's population exceeds one million, large-scale civic recreation facilities will become absolutely necessary, so such businesses will also emerge. As a side note, there might already be people considering and planning ventures in that direction.
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - The Ch'ŏnhyang* Hotel is emerging, and the hotel business might also be worth watching. (*Note: 天香 might be a specific hotel name or a typo/unclear reference)

**What About Social Contribution?**

Kim Tonghwan - From the perspective of coexistence and shared prosperity for humanity, what are your thoughts on wealthy individuals contributing to cultural and social projects for the poor or general society?
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - Contributing to social culture and serving the public with as much sincerity as possible has become common sense now.
Kim Tonghwan - Do you have any specific plans, such as in education?
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - I have often thought about creating something like a museum centered on old documents and paintings (古文書畵) to preserve our traditional culture and prevent its dispersal (散佚), but it's not a concrete idea yet.
Kim Tonghwan - Mr. Pak, don't people suggest you start a school or a newspaper company?
Pak Hŭngsik - Occasionally, yes. Of course, investing money and effort into cultural enterprises like schools and newspaper companies would be good, but I have this view on social contribution. Firstly, I believe it's good to allocate (割하여) a portion of one's wealth to build hospitals or schools only after one's own business is firmly established and unshakable. If one spreads attention thin across various things before the core business is solid, both financial resources and mental energy will be depleted. Also, even if it's not necessarily schools or newspapers, if one's own stores or factories expand and become substantial, employing many unemployed people, and widely introducing domestic products to the global market, fostering trade so that much money flows into Korea, I believe this too is an excellent way to contribute to society.
Min Kyusik - That's one way to look at it. Often, the public assumes that when a capitalist starts a business, it's for exploitation, but in reality, sometimes we undertake unprofitable businesses due to personal connections or compassion. For example, when Chosŏn Chesa (朝鮮製絲) outside Tongdaemun Gate incurred losses and eventually hundreds of factory workers and office staff faced unemployment, we were compelled to take it over.
Kim Tonghwan - Do you have any intention to expand Hwimun High Common School (徽文高普) into a college or university?
Min Kyusik - It seems unlikely to be realized soon.<5> Even building the Hwimun Common School (徽文普通學校) on the site of the former Andong detached palace cost over 400,000 won, and making it truly complete and perfect would still require a substantial additional amount.
Kim Tonghwan - Do you have any plans regarding Hansŏng Commercial School (漢城商業)?
Kim Kidŏk - Well, after moving the school building to the Posŏng College (普成專門) site in Songhyŏn-kol, we are still grappling with the school building issue.
Kim Tonghwan - Generally, with inheritance taxes at 40 or 50 percent now, and corporate income tax also around 30 percent, no matter how much money one earns, the portion one actually keeps is likely not that large. And with budget expansion, it seems all these tax rates will likely increase gradually. Given this situation, don't you feel the desire to spend money a bit more brilliantly and well?
Looking at trends in Tokyo and Osaka, big tycoons not only contribute large sums as National Defense Donations (國防義捐金) but also spend lavishly on building science museums, libraries, giving raises to their factory workers, or establishing schools. They spend truly splendidly.
Kim Tonghwan - In reality, Korean chaebol are worth only a few million won. Compared to the hundreds of millions or tens of millions in Tokyo and Osaka, it's hardly comparable. They spend well because the number of wealthy people there is inherently large, and the amount of assets they possess is enormous.

**Family Mottos, Speculation, Etc.**

Kim Tonghwan - Do you have any family rules (家規), perhaps, or beliefs (信條)? Any principles you strictly adhere to? For instance, in business, do you avoid activities close to speculation, like rice/beans, stocks, or mining?
Min Kyusik - I inherently don't know much about things like mining or rice/beans, so I don't get involved. I've decided not to venture (投足치) into anything unless I understand it well and am confident it will succeed.
Kim Kidŏk - Me too. I hold the conviction that one shouldn't conduct business dreaming of a windfall (일확천금), so I've decided not to step into speculation. Firstly, that mindset itself is uninteresting (자미없어요). If one gets absorbed in such things, there's a concern that genuine work might slip away.
Pak Hŭngsik - Absolutely. It makes not only oneself but also the people under one lose their "sincerity" (진실성), so I avoid it entirely. There are plenty of ways to make money if one works sincerely and intelligently without resorting to that.
Pak Yŏngch'ŏl - I also avoid speculation.
Han Sangnyong - Speculation is taboo for a businessman.
Kim Tonghwan - Regarding conducting business, whereas in the past it was done individually, now it's gradually shifting towards establishing corporations. A common failing (痛弊) in Korean society is said to be that joint-stock companies formed by many people don't do well. Isn't that the case? Based on your experience.
Min Kyusik - When I established Yŏngbo Hapmyŏng (永保合名) in Showa 8 (1933), many friends advised against transferring family assets (家財) into a company structure, and there was opposition within the family too. However, I didn't change my conviction that a company must be created for the long term – 50, 100 years ahead – because an individual's energy is finite. Another point is the need to separate the descendants from the family's business system. Just because a child comes from a wealthy family doesn't mean they must necessarily take over the father's business (父業) and become a farmer if the father was a farmer, or a merchant if he was a merchant. They should be allowed to pursue their own inclinations (天稟), whether in literature or medicine, according to their own desires. Meanwhile, the family business should be nurtured independently.
Kim Tonghwan - And prevent them from carelessly touching the assets?
Min Kyusik - That's right. Company money is company money, family money is family money; a clear distinction (섬연히) and an inviolable barrier must be established between them. This is good for both the business and the descendants.
Pak Hŭngsik - Also, in a capitalist society organized such that small and large sums of money are pooled together to conduct business, the only way to pool capital is through a company structure. It seems some Koreans still have a weak sense of coexistence and co-prosperity (共存 共榮) regarding joint-stock companies, leading to conflicts among executives, but that depends on management. Furthermore, to gather talent widely, a large organization is necessary. And since individual capital and energy are both finite, it's right to conduct business through a company with permanence.
Kim Tonghwan - Thank you for speaking with us for several hours. Initially, I intended to discuss various topics like the issue of foreign capital, pioneering international markets, and the future trends of the Korean economy, but we will postpone those for another opportunity.<6>

**Summary**

This 1937 roundtable interview features five prominent Korean businessmen (Min Kyusik, Pak Hŭngsik, Kim Kidŏk, Pak Yŏngch'ŏl, Han Sangnyong) discussing their capital, business scope, investment strategies, and philosophies with Kim Tonghwan, the publisher of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine. They detail their holdings in various sectors like department stores, paper, trading, real estate (buildings), banking, insurance, agriculture, forestry, mining, and investments in Manchuria. They also share views on the profitability of different ventures (land vs. buildings, paper goods), the potential for entertainment industries (film, theater, amusement parks), the nature of social contribution (business success as contribution vs. philanthropy), and the importance of corporate structures and avoiding speculation. The discussion reveals the scale of Korean capital accumulation, the diversification strategies of the elite, their cautious approach compared to Japanese counterparts, and evolving ideas about modern business management and social responsibility under colonial rule. For historians, the article offers valuable insights into the mindset and operations of the Korean capitalist class during the late 1930s, their adaptation to the colonial economic system, their views on Manchuria as an investment frontier, and their perspective on balancing traditional asset holding (land) with modern industrial and commercial ventures. The candid discussion on wealth, corporate structure, and social obligations provides a rare glimpse into the economic elite's self-perception and strategic thinking.
