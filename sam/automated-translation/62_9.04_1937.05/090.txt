The Group of New Women Knocking on Hospital Doors Late at Night for "Birth Control"

How interested are intellectual women in "birth control"? Furthermore, to what extent are they practicing it? Though it's an embarrassing matter of the inner chambers (閨門) and something behind the red curtain (緋帳), aren't they surely knocking secretly on hospital doors late at night (暮夜) to ask doctors about medical methods?
Here, we decided to hear the actual stories from those directly involved.

Roundtable Participants
Medical Doctor Pak Ch'anghun
German-trained Medical Doctor Chŏng Sŏkt'ae
Medical Practitioner Chang Mun'gyŏng

What Kind of Women Come Seeking Help?

Reporter: Don't intellectual women who graduated from girls' schools come to the hospital thinking that if they have children frequently, their beauty will be ruined? Like Yang Guifei taking medicine because she didn't want to bear children.

Pak Ch'anghun: Many people come to our hospital with such issues, but rather than the problem of beauty, many come for consultations to stop having children due to more pressing life problems. You'd probably know the names if I mentioned them, but the husband is a well-known social figure, and the wife is not only highly educated, having graduated from a girls' school, but also counted among the beauties of the capital. This wife is only twenty-nine this year, but she already has six children, almost one every year. The family lives merely a "salaryman" life, so what are they to do? Therefore, they were seriously considering "birth control."

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: That's right. There are many. Many women are crying in such situations. Often, people think "birth control" is a means used by leisure class or propertied class families who dislike having children in order to enjoy their marital life, but perhaps it's the Creator's mischief. In such fortunate families, they struggle to conceive even when they try hard; it doesn't happen easily at will. Rather than birth control, they cry because they have no children. They yearn for children. Look here. Even kisaeng think they'll hit the jackpot if they meet a rich man and have a child, but for some reason, those who desire children don't have any. Conversely, if they live with a penniless, skinny good-for-nothing, they easily conceive. Therefore, it's not an exaggeration to say that the trend of seeking "birth control" for pleasure has disappeared now. (Photo is of Mr. Pak Ch'anghun)

Chang Mun'gyŏng: Many women come to my place too, thinking we're fellow women, to consult about all sorts of things, and there are truly many pitiful situations.

Reporter: Aren't there many cases of women before marriage trying to cover up the consequences of misconduct, or kisaeng and barmaids who also cannot continue in their profession if they have children... aren't there many who come for such reasons? (Photo is of Mr. Chŏng Sŏkt'ae)

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: People guilty of such moral offenses are too afraid to actually come to the hospital.

Chang Mun'gyŏng: They know they'll be turned away even if they come.

Pak Ch'anghun: But there are cases like this. It's a custom among Korean women to consider having children when old as shameful. Medically, the age of menopause (斷産期) is considered to be from 43 to 45, but old-fashioned women think they can't have children once they pass 40. If they happen to conceive, they consider it incredibly embarrassing. In other words, it has been viewed that once a couple is over 40, they should naturally live in separate rooms and have no physical relations, so if they conceive (抱胎) after 40, they feel ashamed before the world and inquire with the meaning, "Please make it so I don't have any more." Just recently, there was a case concerning the wife of a certain noble house's great family.

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: I also experience this occasionally. Wives from wealthy families. They say it's embarrassing for their daughters-in-law to see.

Pak Ch'anghun: One day, a respectable-looking woman of about forty came into the examination room with a child. I asked if it was her grandchild, and her face turned red as she said, "No. It's my son." Hahaha. She was incredibly embarrassed.

What Happens When They Come?

Reporter: So, what do you do? When women come seeking help, how do doctors handle it?

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: The medical law states this: For instance, if the mother's life is in danger, the pregnancy can be aborted (墮胎), but only with the agreement of two doctors.

Reporter: Do you have to get police permission each time?

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: No. It's done at the doctor's discretion.

Pak Ch'anghun: That's right. If it's recognized that the mother is weak and cannot give birth without risking her life, for example, if pregnancy exacerbates pulmonary tuberculosis, or if there is eclampsia (子癎), severe morning sickness (惡咀), or nephritis (臟腎炎), it can be done.

Reporter: By the way, what is the distinction between "birth control" and "contraception"?

Pak Ch'anghun: "Contraception" is one part, one method of "birth control."

Reporter: Isn't it that one employs contraceptive methods because one wants to practice birth control?

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: If you interpret it that way, yes. However, contraception is currently seen as sinful. Modern law does not permit the elimination of a life that is naturally produced. (Photo is of Ms. Chang Mun'gyŏng)<14>

Pak Ch'anghun: That's why advertisements for contraceptive drugs are prohibited in newspapers and magazines.

Reporter: But does it matter if a married couple regulates things between themselves using some method?

Chang Mun'gyŏng: That would be fine. How could one prevent regulation to avoid pregnancy within the inner chamber (內房)?

Reporter: Returning to the earlier question, what do you do when groups of intellectual women come visiting late at night (暮夜)? Do you just send them away?

Chang Mun'gyŏng: Many female students or young wives come to my place for such consultations, but I explain carefully that complete birth control is impossible and send them away. In reality, it's a matter of relative effectiveness; it's difficult to guarantee complete success.

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: Often, condoms (곤도-무) or pessaries (닷지벳사리) are used, but they are not completely effective. On the contrary, if pregnancy occurs while using such methods, it's easy to give birth to a child with disabilities. Since they do not provide perfect contraceptive effects but carry the risk of producing disabled children, users must be extremely careful.

Pak Ch'anghun: That's right. Rather than such imperfect methods, there are methods of pregnancy termination like Roentgen therapy (렌도겔 요법). However, this is only used in special cases permitted by medical law, such as when the mother's life is endangered.

Various Methods

Reporter: Even if there is no perfect artificial method, what methods can be used for relatively effective birth control?

Chang Mun'gyŏng: Firstly,
1. Devices
Devices are sometimes used as a method of birth control. For example, there are things like pessaries (벳사리-) and sacs (삿그), but these are all imperfect. Medically, there is Roentgen therapy (렌도겐 요법), but its effect is lost and becomes invalid when a certain cycle arrives.
2. Abstinence
Practicing abstinence is absolutely impossible. Sexual desire is a basic instinct of living beings. Instinct cannot be absolutely suppressed. Because that is unnatural.
3. Surgery
Surgery is the most rational method of birth control. However, it cannot be performed unless the person has a maternal constitution that necessitates surgery from a medical standpoint. That is, surgery is absolutely not permitted unless there is an absolute indication that pregnancy would pose a critical danger to the woman's life if surgery is not performed.

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: That's right. Generally, one can list three types: devices, abstinence methods, and surgery, but since they all go against divine providence, they are not perfect.

Reporter: Why do you say it goes against divine providence?

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: Heaven's arrangements (配劑) are truly wondrous, one must say. Excluding special cases, children are not given to wealthy and happy families, while many children are given to poor and unhappy families. Also, it seems there are places where nations and peoples are destined to sow their seed and places where they are not meant to, and there are aspects that cannot be changed by human effort no matter what. Look. Russia or America have vast lands and abundant resources, capable of supporting another 100 or 200 million people, yet their populations do not increase well. How hard does a country like France strive for population growth through government efforts? But it doesn't go as intended. Conversely, isn't it that places with small lands and few natural resources like Germany, Japan proper, or Korea are continuously spreading their seed?

Pak Ch'anghun: That statement is correct. Looking at the situation in Korea, people tend to criticize poor families for having many children, but that's not the case. In poor families, children become capital (한미천). Just as we engage in money-making (돈버리), for them, having children is a great asset. Since those who don't wear Western clothes have no money, they don't think of educating their children, so it doesn't cost much money, and by the time the children are just thirteen or fourteen (열삼사세), they can easily earn their own keep, and if they grow a little more, they help manage the household. There's no explanation other than to say it's God's will. This...

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: There is that aspect. And just because a family is good at producing children doesn't mean they will always continue to do so well. Just as a person accumulating money might think 100,000 won will become a million, and a million will become ten million, but it naturally stops abruptly at a certain limit, the population growth rate of a family, nation, or people also naturally halts when it reaches a certain threshold.

Reporter: So, you mean we should gratefully accept what heaven gives without manipulating it with unnatural means?

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: In principle, that would be correct. Hahaha.

Reporter: However, let's move beyond the universally known principle and consider, based on this real life, whether birth control is necessary or not.

Chang Mun'gyŏng: Of course, it's necessary. Firstly, looking at it economically

I believe birth control must definitely be practiced. Rather than having ten children who are starved, poorly clothed, and uneducated, the mother's ideal should be to feed, clothe, and educate well even just one child.
2. Birth Control Viewed Morally
Morally speaking, birth control is a sin. Limiting the growth and reproduction of living beings is a great contradiction and crime against "nature." Furthermore, the development of a people is influenced by its population growth rate. Limiting births is a great sin from the perspective of national development.
3. Birth Control Viewed Eugenically
Eugenically speaking, birth control must definitely exist. To produce even one bright child rather than five or ten foolish ones, birth control absolutely must be practiced.

Chŏng Sŏkt'ae: That's right. For example, just as having a hundred or a thousand fruits hanging densely on a pear or peach tree doesn't mean they will all turn out well, and the fruit harvest is better if only a few are left (멧 놈만 끼치고) and the rest are picked off, it's the same for people. If among ten potential offspring, only four or five are born well and raised, it becomes easier and better for both raising and educating them. Also, if those living in the servants' quarters (행낭방) continuously have children without limit, there's eventually the concern of causing death due to malnutrition or creating mentally deficient children by being unable to provide proper education.

Pak Ch'anghun: Correct. Economically, eugenically, humanely – from whichever perspective one looks, the need for birth control is something everyone keenly feels now without needing further explanation. The only issue for us is how to research and find a perfect method that anyone can easily implement. For the sake of having a superior race and excellent citizens, I think this is a problem everyone should think about seriously.

Reporter: Thank you. We will listen to more specific details later on. <23>

**Summary**

This 1937 roundtable discussion features three doctors (Pak Ch'anghun, Chŏng Sŏkt'ae, Chang Mun'gyŏng) discussing the increasing interest in and practice of "birth control" (産兒制限) among Korean women, particularly educated "New Women." They explore various motivations, including preserving beauty, economic hardship (especially for salarymen), and the shame associated with childbirth at an older age (over 40). The doctors note that requests often come from poorer families struggling with many children, contrasting with wealthier families who sometimes struggle to conceive. They discuss available methods like condoms, pessaries, abstinence, X-ray therapy (Roentgen therapy), and surgery, acknowledging their imperfections and risks, including potential birth defects or the need for strict medical justification (danger to the mother's life) for procedures like abortion or sterilization surgery. The conversation touches upon the legal prohibition of promoting contraception, moral objections (viewing it as unnatural or sinful), nationalistic concerns about population decline versus eugenic arguments for producing fewer, healthier, and better-educated children.

For historians, this article offers a fascinating glimpse into colonial Korean society grappling with modernity, changing gender roles, and economic pressures. It reveals the private concerns of educated urban women, the perspectives of Western-trained medical professionals navigating traditional customs and modern medicine, and the complex interplay of economic, eugenic, moral, and nationalistic discourses surrounding family planning during a period of significant social transformation under Japanese rule. The frank discussion of methods, motivations (including avoiding shame or consequences of premarital sex), and the doctors' gatekeeping role provides valuable insight into reproductive health and social attitudes of the time.

朴昌薰=그런 문제로 우리 병원에 차저 오는 분이 퍽으나 만치만 미모문제라기 보다 더 절실한 생활문제 때문에 아이 그만 나을 상담을 오는 이가 만치요. 아마 일홈을 말해도 아실터이지만 남편도 일홈 잇는 社會客이요 부인도 어느 여학교 마처 교양이 놉흘뿐더러 「미모」로 장안서 꼽히는 이지요. 이 부인이 올해 스물 아홉인가 밧게 안되엿는데 年年生격으로 벌서 아이 여섯이나 잇서요. 집안은 「살라리맨」생활에 불과하니 이를 어떠케 함니까. 그러니까 「산아제한」을 심각하게 생각하고 잇더군요.

張文卿=저이 집으로도 가튼 여성끼리란 생각으로 온갓 상의하러 오는 여성이 많은데 참으로 딱한 사정이 만터군요.

張文卿=와도 거절당할 줄 아니까요.
朴昌薰=그런데 이러한 일이 잇지요. 조선 여성의 습관으로 늙어서 자식 가지는 일을 수치로 알지오. 의학상으로는 43으로부터 45세까지를 斷産期로 보는데 구식 여성들은 벌서 40만 넘으면 아해 못 가지는 것으로 알고 잇지요. 혹 가지게 되면 그를 여간 붓그럽게 알지 안어요. 말하자면 부부 40이상만 되면 그는 의레히 딴 방에 별거하여 육체상으로는 서로 교섭이 없는 것으로 보아 오는데 그런데 40 이상이 되어 抱胎나 하게되면 세간에 붓그럽다고 「인제 더 나케 말어달라고」 그런 의미의 문의가 있더군요. 바로 요 전만 하여도 모 귀족집 大家 마나님에 대한 일이엇는데요.
