Article Title: 美貌의 書店 매담, 文士 盧春城 부인 李俊淑씨 (The Bookstore Beauty, Mrs. Yi Chun-suk, wife of writer No Ch'un-sŏng)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

The Bookstore Beauty, Mrs. Yi Chun-suk, wife of writer No Ch'un-sŏng
An intelligent woman shop owner who quit her job as a girls' school teacher to enter the professional world, and occupations suitable for intelligent women.

One spring afternoon, with the haze filling the streets of Seoul, I thought of seeking out Mrs. Yi Chun-suk, the proprietress of the Mimosa Bookstore, which has recently become a hot topic among Seoul's intelligentsia. I caught a bus at Chongno O-chŏngmok heading towards Tongsomun. The bus passed Kogong (Higher Technical School) and Chedae Pyŏngwŏn (Imperial University Hospital), stopping in front of Tongsong Sang'ŏp Hakkyo (Tongsong Commercial School) at the Tongsomun terminus. My feet naturally carried me to the Mimosa Bookstore, nestled low beside the road leading to Kosang (Higher Commercial School).
"I came today to hear about your experience running the bookstore," the reporter began. "What is there for me to say?" she replied, half-modestly, welcoming me warmly.
(Photo: Right is Mrs. Yi Chun-suk, Left is her younger sister, Miss Kyŏng-suk)

From Quitting as a Girls' School Teacher to Starting a Bookstore Business

Reporter: When did you start this bookstore?
Yi: I think it was last August.
Reporter: When did you first conceive the plan to run a bookstore?
Yi: Let me see. The thought of trying to manage something on my own came when I quit teaching at the Westerners' school, so I think it was about five years ago now.
Reporter: What motivated you to have such thoughts?
Yi: Firstly, because life was difficult, I thought of helping with our livelihood through my own efforts. If you work tied to someone, you are bound by time constraints, so you can't even find time to focus on the children's education. Also, I always wanted to plan things myself and immerse myself in that work, rather than working for someone else. Of course, I couldn't look down on any business, but among them, something like a bookstore seemed to best fit my personality and interests. This was because my husband not only enjoys literature but also made it his main profession, so naturally, I became interested in books. Although I graduated from the music department, I developed an attachment to literature that I couldn't let go of, so I always wanted to be close to books, and that's how it was. So, I decided to run a bookstore, but where could I get the funds? A long time passed since I first had that thought, but the idea never left my mind. Thus, I always maintained an interest in bookstore management and often visited various bookstores. During this time, I heard the story of a certain university professor's wife who, after her husband was imprisoned in some incident, faced financial difficulties. She started a bookstore in Ponjŏng based on the books her husband owned, and it was doing better than expected. Hearing her experience gave me even more passion and confidence. So, I decided that since I couldn't secure large capital, I would start a bookstore right away using only the books we had at home.
Reporter: So, you opened right away?
Yi: No. After deciding to do so, I searched all over Seoul every day trying to find a location, but where could I find a suitable place? Of course, if I had a lot of capital, there were places in Ponjŏng and Kwanhun-jŏng, but trying to find the most suitable place with minimal funds wasn't easy. After doing this for five months, I finally settled on this location.
Reporter: You must have gone through a lot of trouble.
Yi: Yes. I put in a lot of effort.
Reporter: So, what made you decide this place was suitable?
Yi: This place is a focal point for entering Sŏngbuk-chŏng and Myŏngnyun-chŏng, where many intellectuals live. Also, schools like Chedae (Imperial University), Kosang (Higher Commercial School), Chungjŏn (Middle/Specialized School), Posŏng Kobo (Posŏng Higher Common School), and Tongsong Sang'ŏp (Tongsong Commercial School) surround the area, so it seemed possible to attract both intellectuals and students as customers.

Starting with 400-500 Won Capital, Now Making 20 Won Daily Sales

Reporter: So, how much was your initial investment?
Yi: Can you even call it an investment? It was truly like starting with empty fists. Rent and house repairs cost a little over 100 won, and the books and bookshelves were just what we had at home. Of course, there might have been other expenses for that... If you include the books and shelves, perhaps it was around 400 won?
Reporter: Starting like that, how were the initial sales?
Yi: Considering the insufficient facilities, they were quite good. Daily sales were about 5 or 6 won, and since these were all books we already owned, you could say it was all pure profit. However, there were more books customers looked for that we didn't have than ones we did, which was truly frustrating. After that, I put more effort into buying books than selling them.
Reporter: Is it going well now?
Yi: Compared to the beginning, it has expanded more than tenfold, but there are still many books we lack.
Reporter: That's understandable. The variety of books is almost limitless, so how could you possibly stock everything? You said initial sales were 5-6 won, but since it has expanded tenfold and the bookstore's name is widely known, sales must have increased tenfold or more, perhaps reaching 50-60 won now?
Yi: No, it hasn't reached that level. We are still focusing on ordering (仕入 - stocking). We sell about 20 won worth per day.
Reporter: How do you handle procurement?
Yi: We mainly purchase from the Japanese mainland, places like Tonggyŏng (Tokyo) and Taep'an (Osaka). Large publishing houses like Nauka-sa (ナウカ社) often have clearance sales to dispose of surplus stock, and at such times, you can buy good books very cheaply.
Reporter: So, do you only wait for such chances to buy?
Yi: No. It just means that at those times, you can buy much cheaper than usual. Since we handle old books, we also buy books brought in by customers, but they are usually not very good ones.
Reporter: Then, what is your profit margin?
Yi: For Korean editions, it's 20% <44> to 30%, and for Japanese mainland editions, it's 40% to 50%. I try to buy as cheaply as possible and sell cheaply, but while selling cheaply is easy, buying cheaply is difficult. I aim for small profits and high volume.
Reporter: What about your approach to customers?
Yi: I try to be as kind as possible and accommodate their needs. Unlike other businesses, the customers are all refined people. However, sometimes customers ask for books I've never even heard of. Each time that happens, I feel embarrassed and inwardly resolve that I must study more, even for the customers' convenience.
Reporter: What type of books sell the most?
Yi: Literary books are at the top. Among magazines from Tokyo, *Eiga no Tomo* (映畵之友), *King* (キング), *Hinode* (日の出), and *Shufu no Tomo* (主婦之友) sell well. For Korean magazines, *Samch'ŏlli*, *Chogwang*, and *Yŏsŏng* sell the most.
Reporter: What do students buy most?
Yi: Chedae (Imperial University) students mainly look for medical books, but we often lack what they need. Kosang (Higher Commercial School) students often look for economics and history books, and middle school students mostly buy poetry collections and detective novels.

Dreams from School Days and Current Feelings

Reporter: If you divide customers by gender, which side is more numerous?
Yi: You can't even compare. It's no exaggeration to say there are almost no women.
Reporter: Then who buys the women's magazines?
Yi: It seems young men always buy the women's magazines. When I ask why they buy them... their reply... well, eventually they must end up in women's hands, they say.
Reporter: So, men are acting as errand boys for women?
Yi: You could interpret it that way, but it seems women just don't read. Even women who studied diligently during their student days seem to become slaves to the household once they marry, cutting ties with the outside world. Firstly, women don't have economic power, so they probably can't buy books freely and have to ask men. My words are straying a bit, but—it seems Korean women, once they enter a household, completely kill their individuality and just try to become faithful housewives. But Western people aren't like that. Even after having a family, they remain faithful housewives while continuing to nurture and develop their individuality as much as possible. Among my classmates from Ihwa Yŏjŏn (Ewha Women's College), there were several who, at the time, both they and others believed would surely become great figures after graduation. But once they entered a household, their very existence became untraceable. That, more than anything, is sad. I too, upon graduation, had the lofty ambition of becoming a great musician, but once I had a family, things didn't go as planned. However, I still can't give up that ambition. Besides music, I enjoy literature, and since starting the bookstore, I get to read more, so I consider that fortunate.
Reporter: Since you read a lot, you must write a lot too?
Yi: For now, I intend to read a lot.
Reporter: Recently, among writers and journalists... there's a rumor that Mr. No Cha-yŏng is like a millionaire, known for spending money freely. That must be thanks to you, Madam?
Yi: Ho ho... ho... Not at all. It's probably just because, among the generally poor writers and journalists, he always seems to have money for tea.
Reporter: Don't you ever feel weary from work?
Yi: No. I always think of work as an enjoyable hobby, so rather, the busier I am, the more pleasure I feel.
Reporter: Are sales centered in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul)?
Yi: Of course, Kyŏngsŏng is the center, but we also focus on mail-order sales to the provinces. We advertise in newspapers and magazines, and this spring, we spent about 300 won creating a catalog.
Reporter: Will you also put effort into publishing?
Yi: Yes. In the future, I intend to do a lot of publishing as well. We published a collection of letters (When the *Hongmihwa* (紅薇花 - Hollyhock) Blooms) as our first venture, and the results were quite good. So, as our strength allows, we plan to put effort into the publishing business too.
Just then, her husband, Mr. No Cha-yŏng, burst in, and the topic shifted elsewhere. Furthermore, Mrs. Yi Chun-suk, after graduating from the music department of Ewha Women's College, held a teaching post at a certain girls' school for many years before, as an intellectual woman, undertaking the management of a bookstore on her own—something others had not yet considered. <45>

**Summary**

This 1937 interview features Yi Chun-suk, wife of writer No Ch'un-sŏng (pen name No Cha-yŏng), who quit her job as a girls' school teacher to open the Mimosa Bookstore in Seoul. Motivated by financial need and a desire for independence and intellectual engagement (fueled by her and her husband's love for literature), she overcame challenges like finding capital and a suitable location near universities and intellectual neighborhoods. She describes her business strategy: focusing on literary works, catering to students and intellectuals, importing books (especially discounted ones) from Japan, operating on low margins, and providing good customer service. Yi laments that Korean women, unlike Western women, tend to abandon personal development and reading upon marriage, noting the scarcity of female customers and how men often buy women's magazines for them. She expresses her continuing ambition in music and literature and plans to expand into publishing. Historically, the article offers a valuable glimpse into the life of an educated Korean woman venturing into entrepreneurship during the colonial period. It highlights the socio-economic constraints on women, contrasts Korean and Western women's perceived roles post-marriage, details aspects of the colonial book trade (reliance on Japanese imports, popular genres and magazines), and reflects the cultural milieu of Seoul's intelligentsia.

美貌의 書店 매담, 文士 盧春城 부인 李俊淑씨
여학교 교사를 그만두고 직업선상에 나선 인테리 여점주와 인테리 여류의 할만한 직업

봄 아즈랑이 서울 장안에 가득찬 어느날 오후 나는 요사히 서울 인테리층에 큰 화제를 이르키고 있는 미모사 서점의 녀주인 李俊淑 씨를 차즐 생각으로 종로 오정목서 다시 동소문가는 뻐-스를 잡어탓다. 뻐-스는 고공(高工) 제대병원(帝大病院) 앞을 지나 동소문 종점 동성상업학교 앞에 멈추니 나의 발길은 자연 고상 가는 길 엽에 나즈막하게 자리잡고 있는 미모사 서점으로 옴기여젓다.
오날은 선생의 서점 경영담을 드르러 왓소이다 라고 첫 말을 던지는 긔자에게 제가 무슨 말삼 드릴 것이 있어야지요 라고 반 사양하시면서 반겨 마져주시엿다.
(사진은 右便이 李俊淑여사 左便 여사의 동생 璟淑양)

여교사 그만두고 서점 경영에 나서기까지

기-서점은 언제부터 시작하섯슴니까.
李-작년 팔월부터인가 봅니다.
기-서점을 경영할려는 게획은 언제부터 가지시엿든가요.
李-글셰요. 저의 힘으로 무엇이나 한 가지 자영하야 볼려는 생각을 가지기는 제가 서양사람의 학교를 그만두든 때부터이니 지금으로부터 아마 오년 전인가 봅니다.
기-그런 생각을 가지시게 된 동기는?
