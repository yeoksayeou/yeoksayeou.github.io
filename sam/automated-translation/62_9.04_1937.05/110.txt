Article Title: 「나그내」와 文藝峰 (“Nagnae” and Mun Yebong)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

“Nagnae” and Mun Yebong
Words currently circulating on the streets of Seoul–
That “Nagnae” made a profit of 450,000 won.
That the Meijiza theater, which screened “Nagnae,” saw sell-out crowds for the first time since its opening, day after day.
“That up to 80% of that packed audience were Koreans.”
“That Mun Yebong’s popularity is like a star in the sky.”
With these various rumors in mind, I sought out Miss Mun Yebong, the star of the masterpiece “Nagnae,” and peeked into the Meijiza ‘green room’ (樂屋). On the left and right of the entrance path, wreaths sent from the Koryŏ distribution company and various other places were lined up brilliantly. Pushing through these wreaths, after quite a while, I found Miss Mun, shining like the morning star among many people.

What kind of place is Miryang Kangbyŏn (Miryang Riverside)?

“Where did you film the scenes set by the Miryang River?”
“Yes, it was about 30 *ri* out from Taegu, perhaps near the Naktonggang River? It was a desolate and lonely riverside.”
“In the film, there’s a scene of a white sandy plain without a single flower or blade of grass. Was it really such a desert-like place without vegetation?”
“Since we were depicting a rural village, we sought out such a place. Moreover, the lack of flowers and the sparse appearance of three or four poplar trees reflected the season, as filming began in early November last year, during late autumn and early winter.”
“So, you must have stayed at an inn in Taegu and gone out to film every day?”
“Yes.”
“How many months did it take?”
“We were there for about a month altogether, but during that time, we stopped filming on days when it snowed or the wind blew fiercely. So, the actual filming days amounted to about 20 days, I think.”
“It must have been difficult, with uncomfortable food and lodging…”
“Actually, I was happy. The director, the cameraman, Mr. Wang P'yŏng, Mr. Pak Chehaeng, and everyone else worked with true 'earnestness' (眞劍), so I too was very sincere, diligent, and cheerful.”

How many times did you cry while filming?

“Did you cry many times while filming?”
“Yes, three or four times.”
“During which scenes?”
“When the child gets burned in the fire at the hut.”
“And?”
“When writing the letter to my husband and going to find the barbershop owner.”
“How come?”
“I naturally felt as if I were really in that situation, and the tears came out. Would it work even if I tried to cry intentionally? It would seem awkward anyway.”
“Do you feel confident now? That you could take on any major production, any significant role?”
“Not at all. What do I know? I just did as Director Yi Kyuhwan instructed, and I had good chemistry with Mr. Wang P'yŏng.”
“How many times did you have to do retakes (‘yarinaoshi’)?”
“This time, there wasn't a single scene that needed a retake. It went that well.”
“This time, there were some scenes reminiscent of ‘Imja Ŏmnŭn Narutpae’ (Ownerless Ferryboat).”
“The bonfires (모새불이)?”
“Besides that too.”
“There might have been some similarities, but the story of ‘Nagnae’ this time was completely different.”
“Including this appearance in ‘Nagnae,’ how many films have you starred in?”
“It must be around twenty times. I’ve appeared in about 20 works. Ch'unhyangjŏn, Changhwa Hongnyŏn Chŏn, etc.”
“You must not be afraid of the camera anymore.”
“Yes, somewhat…”

The hardship of recording in Tōkyō

“After finishing filming, how long were you in Tōkyō?”
“About two months.”
“Did you record every day?”
“No. P.C.L. (Photo Chemical Laboratory) was incredibly busy with their own work. We had to be able to borrow their equipment and technicians. We recorded whenever we could find gaps, so the actual working days were probably around two weeks or less.”
“The music?”
“We borrowed the Polydor (포리돌) orchestra.”
“What do you think of the songs like Yangsando or Arirang that play with each scene, the background music?”
“To bring out the characteristic of a Korean film, I think doing that is very good.”
“Do you like folk songs?”
“Yes, aren't those the songs that truly touch our heartstrings?”
“You met Iriguchi Takako?”
“Yes, briefly.”
“Your impression?”
“Well, she seemed very busy. She was rushing around for some filming, so we only met for a moment.”
“How was she?”
“Since she is a senior in the film world, I wanted to hear various things from her, but there wasn’t time. We just greeted each other happily, took a commemorative photo, and that was it.”
“Mizutani Yaeko too?”
“Yes. I met her briefly as well. She was extremely busy preparing to appear on some large stage for the 50th anniversary celebration of the *shinpa* theater troupe. So, we barely got to see each other's faces.<16>”
“Do they all have thorough knowledge?”
“Well. I heard that most have secretaries who speak for them.”

If Stahnberg asks you to come, will you go overseas?

“Just as Dr. Fanck (팡크 박사) called Hara Setsuko of ‘Atarashiki Tsuchi’ (新らしき土 - The New Earth) to Germany and is filming a second work, if Stahnberg (스탄-박), who praised you highly when he came to Seoul last year, sees this ‘Nagnae’ and asks you to come film for something like ‘London Film’ (倫敦필림) in England, would you go?”
“Oh, it sounds too much like a dream…”
“Why?”
“Could such a thing really happen?”
“It might not be impossible. Film critics all say that compared to Hara Setsuko, you are far superior in both looks and acting.”
“Oh my, you’re saying such things…”
“Since you succeeded with ‘Nagnae’ this time, now that the film is touring the entire peninsula, mainland Japan, and will tour the world including China, America, England, etc., earning hundreds of thousands of won, is there any reason they couldn’t send just you, Miss Mun, to study abroad?”
“Another dream… However, when filming ‘Nagnae’ this time, there was talk from the beginning about sending it abroad, so I felt very tense and thought that I absolutely could not let this work fail. I really gritted my teeth and tried my best. Please appreciate that point at least.”
“Never lose that attitude. By the way, what about the second work?”
“Yes, it’s currently under discussion. I’ve rested for a while, and since everyone has praised it so highly, I must repay them again.” <17>

**Summary**

This 1937 interview with actress Mun Yebong focuses on the success of her recent film "Nagnae" (Wanderer). She discusses the filming process near Taegu, emphasizing the collaborative and earnest atmosphere despite challenging conditions. Mun describes the emotional depth required for certain scenes and her growing, yet humble, confidence as an actress. She recounts her trip to Tōkyō for sound recording at P.C.L., noting the difficulties in scheduling studio time and her brief, busy encounters with Japanese stars Iriguchi Takako and Mizutani Yaeko. The interviewer highlights the film's immense popularity among Korean audiences, its financial success, and the positive reception of incorporating Korean folk songs. The conversation concludes with speculation about potential international opportunities for Mun, comparing her favorably to Japanese actress Hara Setsuko and mentioning interest from figures like Josef von Sternberg ("Stahn-Bark") and London Film, prompting Mun to express both disbelief and a determined commitment to her craft fueled by the film's success and potential global reach.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its portrayal of the burgeoning Korean film industry under Japanese rule. It highlights the massive success a Korean film could achieve among Korean audiences, suggesting a strong sense of cultural identity and enthusiasm for local productions. The mention of filming logistics, reliance on Japanese facilities (P.C.L. in Tōkyō) for advanced post-production (sound recording), comparisons with Japanese stars, and the discussion of international potential (specifically mentioning European interest via Stahnberg/Fanck) reveal the complex interplay between local cultural production, colonial infrastructure, and global aspirations within the Japanese empire.
