Two Letters
Regarding the National Problem - In Response to Mr. To Yu-ho in Berlin -

Kim Myŏng-sik
I received your letter dated September 28, 1931, forwarded by Samch'ŏlli-sa (삼천리사), through the company's kind transmission, and read it with respect on November 7th of the same year. Receiving earnest enlightenment on our grave problems from an unknown fellow self-studying comrade, feelings of respect and affection for you and inspiration regarding our problems arise together. I am not unaware that it is my duty to send a reply reaching your lodging, but as the question you asked is our grave problem, and since communicating views on this problem socially seems more meaningful, I borrow the pages of Samch'ŏlli-sa to offer my humble opinion. Please forgive me greatly.

Recently, the intellectuals of Chosŏn research the national problem extensively. However, because it is almost entirely specialized research concerning policy, research with truthful content and practical coherence cannot emerge outside the researcher's study; it would likely not be an exaggeration to say that such valuable research is almost unknown to the public. Meanwhile, writings on the national problem by Lenin, Stalin, Kuno Bauer, etc., have undeniably caused a major sensation in our reading circles.

Mr. Yi Kwang-su's (李光洙) theory of national reconstruction and Mr. Sin Il-yong's (辛日鎔) refutation of it have both already become documents of the past.

I did not have the opportunity to read your thesis on the national problem published in the July, August, and September issues of *Tonggwang* (東光), but I carefully read the short piece published in the December issue of the same magazine and received much enlightenment.

As you say, [censored] through the natural growth of small nations is not possible, and I know that the self-withdrawal of the opposing entity is unthinkable. And specifically, the internal turmoil of the opposing entity can be a problem, but generally, apart from the mutual relationship between the whole and the part within the international scope, even the opposing entity and the fact of opposition itself will be forgotten; I think the economic relations of all colonies becoming decolonized into capitalist state units argue this more eloquently than anything. Currently, in all colonies, the interests of the native landlords and the commercial/industrial bourgeoisie conflict with those of the workers and peasants, but almost align with foreign capital. Isn't it unthinkable that the internationality and universality of capital between two nations, where all barriers seem to have been abolished, can be easily blocked by traditional concepts?

Here, I believe the problem of small nations is economically a special [censored] problem of foreign capital and the native capital allied with it against the native workers and peasants, and politically, it is a special [censored] problem. Therefore, the problem of small capital versus large capital and the special [censored] and [censored] problem present conditions for cooperation between workers, peasants, and the petit bourgeoisie. However, the interests of the petit bourgeoisie not only conflict with a certain power but also with the workers and peasants, so its orientation is not fixed. When its conflict with the former is more intense than with the latter, it cooperates with the latter, but if the opposite is true, it is drawn to the former.

Then, the question arises whether the interests of the petit bourgeoisie conflict more with the former or the latter, which cannot be said generally, but as the workers and peasants advance en masse, the interests of the petit bourgeoisie conflict more with the workers and peasants – doesn't the Swaraj movement in India (印度) prove this well? <82>
Therefore, the issue of cooperation versus non-cooperation is not an abstract, general problem but a specific, concrete one. That is, I think cooperation with workers and peasants is possible only to the extent that the interests of the petit bourgeoisie are more strained against large capital and the special [entity] at a certain time. And while the national problem can be studied from various aspects, I think that in our situation, research centered on policy is needed above all. It is regrettable that valuable research cannot be published for the time being, but it seems possible in German in Berlin (伯林), and I believe that if there is anything published in German, it could be widely introduced. This is all for now.
I hope you stay healthy and frequently show us your profound research.

To Mr. Kim Yak-su (金若水)
Pak Sŏk-hong (朴錫洪)

Yaksu hyŏng!
The moment I pick up my pen to write a letter to you, hyŏngnim, I suddenly feel anew those times when you and I escaped to China (中國), experienced all sorts of bitter hardships, and even discussed ultimate suicide. However, since this has already become a historical fact, there is no need to chatter much about it here, but anyway, I cannot now deny that I dreamed of being heroes with you back then. While greatly reflecting on all the sins committed ideologically in the past, I confess that even with my constantly changing thoughts, my longing for you, hyŏngnim, remains largely unchanged from then to now. By the way, hyŏngnim, your worship of the 18-year-old Zhou Yu (周愈) of the Three Kingdoms period as a hero, and my admiration for the 24-year-old Xiang Yu (項羽) of the Chu-Han period as a hero, can only be called the thoughts of vigorous young braves (靑春好漢). That's right. While strolling in a certain park, our repeated lament, "There are heroes, but no place to wield their power (英雄이 有之나 無用武之地)," clearly shows that the blood of discontent and indignation, born from national sentiment, was still pulsing uncooled even in our young hearts (가삼). In fact, it is an undeniable confession that even now, whenever I encounter certain irrational objective conditions, this feeling sometimes threatens to explode.

That such feelings arise from time to time is, of course, related to my lack of liberation in many aspects, but anyway, whether Westerner or Easterner, when they demonstrate that unpleasant sense of national superiority, truly unbearable indignation surges like white waves crashing against the sky (白浪拍天). If one interprets this favorably from the perspective of non-resistance, one might even feel pity for them (彼等), but thinking again from the standpoint of resistance, an instinctive human impulse, one indeed cannot help but hate them. Therefore, while recalling Mr. Tolstoy's non-resistance ideology, which taught that it was good to outwardly comply sweetly even with the harsh exploitation (苛歛誅求) of the Russian (로시아) Tsarist era, there is also room to consider Mr. Sorel's ideology of resistance, which suggested that one who does not know hatred does not know love, and one who denies violence also [censored] justice. The public debate surrounding the two ideologies mentioned above, namely non-resistance and resistance, certainly draws our attention, but seeing that social movements today generally run towards extremism, it truly seems as though human life might be set right by extremism. However, a question newly arises here. If the life of great harmony (大和大化) for humanity is produced by extremist revolutionary movements, why couldn't the extremist revolution of France (佛蘭西) since the 18th century, not to mention distant antiquity, and also the recent Bolshevik <83> revolution in Russia, purify the social life of human society? I intend to reflect on this absurdity internally. I think there will be an opportunity to comment on this matter another day, so I will not elaborate in this text.

Observing the field of your activities, hyŏngnim, I cannot help but think of you as a materialist grounded in a materialist conception of history (유물사관적 인생관). So, hyŏngnim, you are a possessor of Marx's materialist communist thought and also Lenin's [omitted - original]. Yes. Speaking of hyŏngnim, you are clearly a disciple of Marx and an apprentice of Lenin. Indeed, I think of you, hyŏngnim, as someone who puts much effort into propagating Marxist thought and implementing Leninist policy.

In fact, as a pioneer of communism and a vanguard element of the proletarian class movement, one cannot help but acknowledge you, hyŏngnim, perhaps more than anyone else in our Chosŏn. Wasn't it true that your activities in Tōkyō (東京) with the [Organization Name Censored]會 as a background, and your activities in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), Chosŏn, with the [Organization Name Censored]會 as a background, were aimed more at planning the economic liberation (解X) of the propertyless masses (無産群) than for the [Meaning Censored]X of the ideology? Surely, it must be so. While the majority of humanity [omitted - original] under the monistic law of capitalism, it is indeed true that we must strive and struggle to defend the immediate interests of the propertyless, in other words, for their food, clothing, and shelter.

This, so to speak, is seen in the sense Marx hinted at: "Where there is contradiction, there is struggle; where there is struggle, there is development." Therefore, Marx's emphasis that human history is the history of class struggle also has a point.

However, hyŏngnim, is it justifiable to regard human history solely through the lens of class struggle, like Marx? Why did Marx observe human history only as a history of class struggle? If humanity had consistently engaged only in struggle since ancient times, then by now, it would be difficult to even find a shadow of humanity, and this world would either be devoid of humans or only a few intelligent, wealthy, powerful individuals would remain.

But reality is the opposite; humanity has continuously reproduced (繼繼生生), and even in current society, statistics showing the global human population as 1.9 or 2 billion are published in magazines and newspapers. This suggests that the human spirit of life is not competitive, conflictual, or murderous. It is regrettable. How could a sharp mind and keen observation like Marx's conclude that human history is solely conflictual? And why couldn't he observe human history from the perspective of mutual aid (상호부조)? Hyŏngnim, isn't that so? Rather than pointlessly calling human history a history of class struggle, wouldn't it be more rational to call human history a history of mutual aid? Regardless of the era or society, those who claim to be historians, through their narrow observations, record only the misfortunes occurring locally in human life, strongly emphasize the struggle for existence, and finally perceive humanity as class-struggling beings – what a contradictory act this is! Look. If we speak of an era and society of evil stratagems (惡術) and contradictions, perhaps there is no society more deteriorated than the present one. Even in this current society, where a downpour (沛雨) of murder falls ceaselessly, seeing humanity live on without forgetting the great path of self-existence and saving others, one realizes that humanity's fundamental spirit is indeed not rooted in struggle.

If so, humanity is by no means competing for survival; on the contrary, it is clearly aiding survival (生存을 부조함), and this is precisely what is called mutual aid (相互扶助), which constantly pulses beneath the surface of human social life. This mutual aid allows humans to infinitely expand the self. In other words, can we deny the principle that through mutual aid, the inherent mission of life, A lives, B lives, and C also lives? In fact, if one observes the inner life of humanity, the spirit of aid (부조적 정신), consciously or unconsciously, transcends all classes and governs human life; this is the sympathy (동정), human nature (인성), <84> morality (도덕), and ethics (윤리) we talk about. Apart from this, hope, future, endeavors, success – everything for humanity returns to nothing (수포에 歸할 뿐). Despite this, Marx thinking of humans as incarnations of the struggle for existence shows that his view of biology and economics also could not escape from the observations of Darwin and Smith. However, Marx, angered by irrational objective social conditions, first advocating the liberation of the proletarian class and subsequently struggling towards social, political, and economic balance, can be considered a necessary struggle in human life to some extent. But what is greatly regrettable is that he, even if considered transitional, approved of so-called centralism or something similar, which is fundamentally contrary to the free, volitional life of human beings. This is surely a kind of pathological thought that failed to understand the fundamental principles of human life. How can this not be called Marx's feudal, monarchical, heroic sense of superiority? Moreover, even his proclaimed objective labor theory of value cannot be said to be without contradictions. I will discuss my perceived rights and wrongs on this matter another day, but for now, I think the value of things is ultimately unquantifiable (계량치 못할 것). Trying to quantify unquantifiable value (不可 계량의 가치를 계량하랴ꊳ�) is the contradiction.

To reveal the exact value of any object, wouldn't it be impossible to determine its proper value without first quantifying the value of the entire universe? If so, no matter how great the power of science is, how can one temporally quantify the ever-flowing and changing cosmic drift (우주회유) that occurs in split seconds? Let's assume it can be quantified. What is the need to quantify the value of things in human life? Discussing use-value or exchange-value, debating the size, length, thickness, weight (大小, 長短, 厚薄, 輕重) of things, and speaking of the resulting interests – these are words spoken in the life of a pathological society, definitely not things seen in healthy social life.

Therefore, in a truly social human life, one simply produces according to ability and consumes according to need. Pointlessly quantifying the value of things to discuss interests is a contradiction inconsistent with the principles of human life.

Therefore, I think Marx's ideology and assertions lack the elements to guarantee security in human life; instead, unfortunately, they carry the great danger of trampling upon the subjective life of the human self, ultimately leading to the mechanization and animalization of humans.

Hyŏngnim! And thinking superficially, Marxism seems like the only weapon against imperialism, but since its ideological essence was also conceived and birthed (胞胎分血) from imperialism, even if the current imperialism is [defeated], when a sharp new imperialism eventually emerges with double the arrogance (倍前跋扈), human life will surely be utterly destroyed. What should be done about this? Indeed, those who study Marxism and advocate Leninist policies should not merely be drawn to the curiosity of the immediate moment but should consider the long-term, profound future of human life and only then stand on reality and proceed.

Hyŏngnim!! I reject the economic theories touted by various figures of the past. Thus, I think these economic theories have already become mere classical doctrines. Look, Malthus's "An Essay on the Principle of Population" (人口論), which rationalized the survival-of-the-fittest, competitive economic life; Smith's "The Wealth of Nations" (富國論), which taught industrial freedom and the revolution of its control; and Marx's "Capital" (資本論), which clarified the conflicting interests between the labor and capital classes, taught class struggle, and sought to establish a dictatorial society of the proletariat – ultimately cannot avoid being regarded as upholding injustice and contradiction deviating from economic principles, that is to say, departing from human dignity, freedom, justice, and mutual aid.

These thinkers placed the fundamental economic thought solely on material production relations, thus witnessing human thought becoming attached to material things and human life enslaved by material things, yet they thought of it nonchalantly (凡然히). **Summary**

This article presents two letters exchanged between Korean intellectuals in 1932. The first, from Kim Myŏng-sik to To Yu-ho, analyzes the "national problem" of colonial Korea through an economic lens, influenced by Marxist thought. Kim discusses the alignment of native bourgeoisie interests with foreign capital against workers and peasants, suggesting alliances between the proletariat, peasantry, and petit bourgeoisie are possible only under specific conditions where the latter's conflict with large/foreign capital is paramount. The second letter, from Pak Sŏk-hong to Kim Yak-su, is a more personal and critical reflection on revolutionary ideals and Marxism. Pak reminisces about youthful nationalist fervor, then critiques Marx's emphasis on class struggle, arguing that human history is better understood through "mutual aid." He questions Marx's labor theory of value and expresses concern that Marxism, despite its anti-imperialist potential, could lead to authoritarianism and dehumanization, ultimately stemming from similar roots as imperialism itself.

For historians, these letters offer a valuable glimpse into the complex intellectual landscape of colonial Korea. They showcase the engagement with international ideologies like Marxism, the intense debate surrounding nationalism and class, the search for strategies of resistance and liberation under Japanese rule, and the evolving, sometimes critical, perspectives on revolutionary theories even among those initially drawn to them. Pak Sŏk-hong's emphasis on "mutual aid" as an alternative framework to class struggle is particularly noteworthy as a distinct intellectual current. The censorship marks (XX) also hint at the constraints on expression during this period.

便紙 2장
民族問題에 對하야 - 伯林에 게신 都宥浩 씨에게 答함 -

金明植
귀하의 1931년 9월 28일발 삼천리사 轉交로 주신 書簡은 同社의 친절한 전수를 입어 同年 11월 7일에 배독하얏슴니다. 미지의 독학동지로부터 우리의 중대한 문제에 대한 叮嚀한 계시를 밧고 귀하에 대한 경애와 우리 문제에 대한 감흥이 아울너 이러남니다. 귀하의 여관에 이르도록 답서를 드리는 것이 도리인 줄 알지 못함은 아니오나 귀하의 무르신 문제가 우리의 중대한 문제이오, 또 이 문제에 대한 견해는 사회적으로 교통하온 것이 보다 더 의미가 잇슬 듯 함으로 삼천리사의 지면을 빌어서 愚見을 드림니다. 만히 용서하야 주시오.
최근 조선의 식자는 민족문제에 대하야 만히 연구함니다. 그러나 거의 정책에 관한 전문연구인 까닭에 진실한 내용과 실천적 조리를 가진 연구는 연구자의 서재 박그로 나올 수 업스니 그러한 귀중한 연구는 일반에 알려진 것이 거의 업다고 하야도 과언이 안일 듯 함니다. 그리고 그간 민족문제에 대한 레닌, 쓰타린, 쿠노바우에르 등등의 저서는 우리 독서계에 일대 공황을 이르킨 감이 업지 아니하얏슴니다.
李光洙씨의 민족 개조론과 그에 대한 辛日鎔씨의 박론은 둘 다 발서 과거의 문헌이 되엿슴니다.
귀하의 東光 7, 8, 9월호에 발표한 민족문제에 관한 논문은 배독할 기회를 엇지 못하얏고 同誌 12월호에 발표한 단편은 정독하얏는데 만흔 계시를 밧앗슴니다.
귀하의 말삼과 가치 약소민족의 자연성장에 의한 XX은 가능한 것이 아니오, 또 대립자의 自退하는 것은 생각하야 볼 수 업는 일인 줄로 암니다. 그리고 특수적으로는 대립자의 내부동요가 문제 될 수 잇지만은 일반적으로는 국제적 범위에서 전체와 부분과의 상호관계로부터 떠나서는 대립자와 대립 사실까지도 몰각하게 되고 말 것은 자본적 국가단위로 비식민지화하는 일체 식민지의 경제관계가 무엇보다도 雄論하다고 생각함니다. 현재 모든 식민지에서 元住지주 밋 상공뿌르조아지의 이해는 노동자농민과는 대립하되, 외래 자본과는 거의 일치함니다. 원래 모든 장벽이 철폐된 양 민족간의 자본의 국제성과 보편성은 전통적 관념으로 능히 저지할 수 잇는 것이라고 생각할 수 업는 일이 안임닛가.
여긔서 나는 약소민족문제는 경제적으로는 元住노동자농민에 대한 외래 자본 밋 그와 합동한 元住 자본의 특수 XX문제이오, 정치적으로는 특수 XX문제라고 생각함니다. 그럼으로 대자본에 대한 소자본 문제와 특수 XX 밋 XX문제는 노동자농민과 소뿌르조아지가 협동할 조건을 제시함니다. 그러나 소뿌르조아지의 이해는 어느 세력과 대립할 뿐만 아니라 노동자농민과도 대립함으로 그의 향배는 일정치 아니함니다. 그는 전자와 대립이 후자와 보담 긴장한 때에는 후자와 협동하지마는 그와 반하면 전자에로 끌림니다.

형님이여! 그리고 얼풋 생각한다면 맑스주의가 제국주의를 하기에는 유일한 무기가 될 듯 하나 그러나 그 주의적 본질이 亦 제국주의에서 胞胎分血된 이상, 비록 현 제국주의가 되엿다 할지라도 드듸어 예리한 신제국주의가 倍前跋扈하는 그때는 과연 인류생활이 여지업시 파멸되고야 말 것이니 여긔 대해서는 맛당히 여하히 처리할 것이오, 실노 맑스주의를 연구하고 또 레-닌정책을 고조하는 인사로는 다만 眼前 찰나의 好奇에만 끌니지 말고 悠久深遠한 인류생활의 장래를 고려하야 비로소 현실에 입각, 출발하여야 될 줄노 생각합니다.
형님!! 나는 과거의 누구누구들이 떠들어 노은 경제설을 부정합니다. 그래서 此等 경제설은 발서 한 개의 고전적 학설이 되고 마럿다고 생각합니다. 보시오, 약육강식의 생존경쟁적 경제생활을 합리화에 끄을어다 부처노은 말사스의 「人口論」이며, 산업의 자유와 그 통제의 혁명을 가르친 스미스의 「富國論」이며 그리고 勞, 資 양 계급간의 이해상반을 闡明하야 계급투쟁을 가르치고 푸로레타리아의 독재적 사회를 수립코자한 맑스의 「資本論」은 필경 인간의 인격과 자유, 정의와 부조를 떠난, 말하자면 경제적 원리에 위반된 불의와 모순을 把持한 것으로 간주치 안을 수 업슴니다.
