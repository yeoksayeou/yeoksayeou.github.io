잡지명	삼천리 제4권 제2호
발행일	1932년 02월 01일
기사제목	民族的 公敵인 分裂主義者 撲滅, 地方熱과 派爭打破論 (An Argument for Eradicating the Divisionists, Public Enemies of the Nation, and Overcoming Regional Fervor and Factional Strife)
필자	李鍾麟
기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Argument)

民族的 公敵인 分裂主義者 撲滅, 地方熱과 派爭打破論
						李鍾麟
1. The time when I took up this topic was January 1st, a moment when I intended to cast off all the multifaceted scenes where I had spent half my life, and soon become a solitary crane with a single zither (孤鶴隻琴), drifting freely (悠悠自適), ready to flutter my sleeves and depart aloofly (飄然 拂袖冷然長往) to some mountain of cultivation or waters of recitation. At that time, I was lying immersed in remorse for the past and anticipation for the future, a silent self that could not be broken by anything, be it gold like water or beauties like flowers (黃金如水美人如花). However, as this topic concerns the issue that has scattered our nation and reduced our country to ruins (邱墟), my silence could not remain unbroken.

2. Regional fervor and factional strife might seem an unavoidable principle for us humans who live gathered in groups (類聚群居). Does not regional passion grow into national unity, and does not factional struggle (黨派爭) expand to establish national control? If so, we should indeed cultivate this as a virtue nationally and protect it as a constant way (常道) for the state. Yet, why is it that because of this, our nation has become scattered, and because of this, our country has been reduced to ruins? There are two reasons for this: one is that regional unity was originally not unity for the benefit of the nation, but unity for the benefit of individuals, and factional struggle was never a struggle for the survival of the state, but a struggle for the survival of one's own faction (自黨). The other reason is that even if it were unity aimed at individual benefit and struggle centered on the survival of one's own faction, there should have been the virtue within it to draw the entire nation, consciously or unconsciously, or failing that, the strength to overcome any opposing faction. However, among them, until today, there has not been a single person possessing that virtue and strength.

3. The leaders of regional fervor and factional strife, unable even to influence the one wife embraced in their own bosom, how could they embrace the entire nation, no matter how much they flex their stunted arms? Who would be embraced in this bosom? Even if there were such people, they are like promiscuous women (淫婦) who would sell their chastity even for a single glance. Being promiscuous women, they invariably make jealousy their sole chastity, thus engaging in alienation and slander. What unity can there be in this? Unable even to control the children clinging beneath their knees, how could they control the entire nation, no matter how much they clench their dead fists? Who would be subdued under this fist? Even if there were such people, they are like cripples (병신들) who lose their minds just seeing their deceased father <8> in a dream. Being cripples, they invariably make superstition their sole destiny, thus engaging in fortune-telling and exorcism (占相 祈祓). What control can be established here? Look through the 500-year history of factional strife in the Yi Dynasty (李朝). Which page is not a record of the jealousy of promiscuous women, and which phrase is not the fatalism of cripples?
Released figures from the People's Convention

4. In a household where only cripples live, the healthy person becomes the madman instead. In a village where only promiscuous women live, the virtuous woman becomes the ugly woman instead. Similarly, in our society filled with the promiscuous women of regional fervor and the cripples of factional strife, isn't the person who sees north, south, east, and west as one region considered an unchaste ugly woman instead, and the person who sees the 23 million people as one party considered a senseless madman instead? Who can break down this regional fervor and factional strife that have completely transformed customs and character (移豐易性)? Suppose there is such a person. Those who promote regional fervor will inevitably attack them, and those who brew factional strife will inevitably reject them. Look! Our society is full of those who would break this down and full of those who encourage it. In a field where the bright moon and reed flowers blend into one color (明月蘆花 渾一色), how can we find the truly supra-regional, supra-factional person and the truly anti-regional, anti-factional theory, like searching for a white crane (皓鶴)? If we cannot trust the person first, how can we trust only their theory? If we do not accept the theory, how can we adopt only the method of that theory? Therefore, to break this down, more than any theory or method, we must first choose the person. And to choose the person, rather than eloquence, strategy, or financial power, we must choose a person of principle (主義) who cannot be cornered by any eloquence, dissolved by any strategy, or moved by any financial power. Once we have found such a person, we must absolutely believe in their theory and method. Believing means not trusting the person's individual self, but trusting the principle (主義) that the person believes in. A person with a thorough principle (主義) has no intimacy or enmity (親疎가 업고 恩仇가 업는) outside of that principle. Since there is no intimacy or enmity, what region or faction can there be? If there were intimacy or enmity, <9> it would be intimacy due to the principle; if there were enmity, it would be enmity due to the principle, never intimacy and enmity on a human level. In other words, people with the same principle are brothers even if they are strangers (超越), and people without the same principle are strangers even if they are brothers.
Worldly affairs (天下事), whatever theories or strategies are employed, are ultimately decided by power (力), and the powers of the world, whatever financial or military might is involved, the absolute strongest is solely principle (主義). Do not be surprised. Someone like Jesus (耶蘇), though a monotheist, embraces almost the entire world, does he not? Someone like Marx (맑쓰), though a materialist, now threatens the world, does he not? Therefore, before a strong principle (主義), there are no theories or strategies. There is no military power or financial power.

5. Generally, the origin (起因) of regional fervor began in regions where people, originally the same citizens performing the same duties (應役), could not enjoy the same rights. The starting point (起點) of factional strife began in the struggle for political power. As times changed and the national situation shifted, its downstream effects (末流) eventually led to causes tied even to the kindness of a single meal (一飯之恩) or the resentment from a single cup (一盃之怨), and relationships formed even from the displeasure of a single frown (一嚬之憾) or the affection of a single glance (一顧之情). Therefore, the bonds of these people can be sufficiently destroyed by a bowl of rice or a cup of wine, and they can also be united by a single frown or smile. However, ants gathered by a stench disperse when the stench is gone (聚羶之蟻 羶盡則散), and flies gathered by foulness leave when the foulness dries up (集腥之蠅 腥枯則去). Unless the fundamental nature of these sorts is eradicated, rather than breaking up their groups and allowing their contagion to spread in other directions, isn't it the soundest and most appropriate method to simply leave them alone and let even just two people, if they share the same principle (主義), form a separate bond, aiming for something, and taking care that there is no filth (汚穢物) or rottenness (臭腐性) around that principle that could attract those others? However, at this time when discussions about building a central organization and reconstructing a pan-national representative body are rising, proposing, even theoretically, that just two people with the same principle should form a separate <10> bond might be misunderstood as provoking factional strife. But unless it is a genuine union of principle (主義), no matter what gold signboard (金간판) is hung or what iron organization (鐵조직) is formed, that signboard will eventually fall, and that organization will surely break apart. Without needing to cite distant past examples, the recent Sin'ganhoe (新幹會) representatively demonstrates this. Not only that, but factions uniting with factions (종파종파끼리 결합) and classes confronting classes (계급계급끼리 대립) are principles and truths that cannot be moved by any theory or policy. Therefore, the main point of today's argument is not to beat down various factions (각 종파) to create a single lineage (宗統) or to break various classes (각 계급) to build a single party (파당), but solely to overcome the regional fervor and factional strife arising within a single faction or a single class.

6. However, as the saying goes, "Each person sweeps the snow before their own gate; never mind the frost on another's roof" (各人自掃門前雪 不管他人屋上霜), for groups with fundamentally different ideologies and objectives to interfere in the internal affairs of other groups might be considered excessively presumptuous (猥越). Yet, even between nations (國與國), there is mediation and restraint. If, setting aside who is right or wrong (孰是孰非), we ignore the fights among people of the same bloodline simply because we claim no connection, isn't this a society without authority as a society, a nation whose affection as a nation has dried up? If so, from now on, regardless of class or faction, if the strife is an unavoidable fight truly based on principle (主義), that may be understandable. But if it is a fight over power or emotion, it must be socially exposed and publicly restrained. The method (뮀©식 - likely 방식 'method' or 형식 'form') of restraint should be to establish a public agreement (공약) that assigns a corresponding period to the central figures of the strife, during which they are denied all rights as members of society. The only question is whether our society today indeed has such authority and courage. If only this could be implemented, the promiscuous women who sell jealousy as chastity would be driven away with other promiscuous women, and the cripples who see superstition as destiny would be cast out with other cripples.
Only after that will our society become a truly unified society, and our nation become a truly controlled nation. Only after that will there be some outcome, regardless of success or failure, gain or loss (成敗得失). <11>

**Summary**

Yi Chong-nin argues vehemently against regionalism ("regional fervor") and factionalism ("factional strife") in 1932 Korea, identifying them as the primary reasons for national division and weakness ("public enemies of the nation"). He contends that these divisions stem from personal gain and power struggles rather than genuine national interest or ideological commitment. He uses harsh metaphors ("promiscuous women," "cripples") to condemn leaders lacking virtue and strength. Yi proposes that true unity can only be achieved through adherence to strong, shared principles ("主義"), which transcend personal ties, regional loyalties, and even material power. He suggests that while different groups (classes, sects) will naturally exist, internal strife based on power or emotion within these groups must be publicly exposed and sanctioned by society, perhaps by temporarily stripping factional leaders of their social rights. He concludes that only by overcoming these divisions through principle-based unity can the Korean nation achieve genuine cohesion and strength.

Historically, this article is significant as it reflects the deep frustration among Korean intellectuals with internal divisions hindering nationalist efforts during the Japanese colonial period. The call for unity based on "主義" (principles/ideology) over personal or regional ties echoes broader debates within the nationalist and socialist movements of the time about the best path forward. The mention of the recently dissolved Sin'ganhoe (an attempt at a united front organization) underscores the practical difficulties and failures in achieving such unity. Yi's proposed solution of social sanction against divisive figures reveals a desire for internal discipline within the Korean community itself, even under colonial constraints.

1. 내가 이 論題를 마튼 그때는 내가 나의 반생을 消受하야 바린 다각적 장면을 다 처 바리고 잠차 孤鶴隻琴, 悠悠自適의 몸이 되여 수양의 산이나 朗吟의 水, 어듸로든지 飄然 拂袖冷然長往하랴든 1월 1일이엇다. 이때의 나는 과거의 悔悟와 장래의 想望에 글처 누어서 黃金如水美人如花, 그 무엇으로든지 깨트릴 수 업는 침묵의 나이엿지마는 이 논제가 민족을 이산하고 국가를 邱墟한 문제임으로 나의 침묵은 깨여지지 아니할 수 업다.

2. 지방열과 파쟁은 類聚群居 하야사는 吾人의 피할 수 업는 원칙일 것이다. 지방적 정열, 이것이 자라서 민족적 단결이 생기고 黨派爭, 이것이 커저서 국가적 통제가 서지는 것이 아닌가. 그러타면 吾人은 맛당히 이것을 민족적으로 배양하여야 할 미덕이고 이것을 국가적으로 옹호하여야 할 常道이겟거늘, 我 등은 이것 때문에 민족이 이산하야지고, 我 등은 이것 때문에 국가가 邱墟하여진 것은 何故인가. 이에 두 가지 원인이 잇스니 하나는 지방적 단결은 본래 민족의 이해를 위하여서의 단결이 아니라 개인의 利害를 위하여서의 단결이엇스며 당파적 투쟁은 一事도 국가의 존망을 위하야서의 투쟁이 아니라.
自黨의 존망을 위하야서의 투쟁인 까닭이엇스며 또 하나는 旣是 개인의 이해를 목표로한 단결이고 自黨의 존망을 중심으로한 투쟁일지라도 이에 부지불식 중 전민족이 끌닐만한 그 덕이 잇거나 그러치 못하거든 엇더한 반대당이 잇더라도 이것을 능히 누르고 나갈 만한 힘이 잇서야 할 것이어늘 저들 중에는 오날까지 그 덕과 그 힘을 가진 사람이 한아도 업슨 까닭이다.
