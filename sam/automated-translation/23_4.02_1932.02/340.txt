Article Title: 女俳優와 貞操와 사랑 (Actresses, Chastity, and Love)
Author: 李瑞求
Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

女俳優와 貞操와 사랑
						李瑞求 (Yi Sŏgu)
The fate of a wanderer drifting without a fixed home

A: Where are you going?
B: Well, I suppose I just go where the troupe leader tells me to go.

A: What are you seeking to gain by wandering around enduring this hardship?
B: What choice do I have if I don't wander?

A: Until when will you keep wandering?
B: I guess I have to keep going until something good happens.

What a stifling answer! What a tearful answer!
There was an instance when a troupe of Japanese beauties called the K Opera Troupe performed at Theater A in the city. It was a troupe that set out intending to tour Manchuria and Chosŏn (Korea), but disaster struck with the conflict between Japan and China (일지동란), blocking their path to tour Manchuria, so it seemed they had no choice but to tour only Chosŏn.

There was a vivacious twenty-year-old beauty, the star of the troupe, named "Mariya." Her beautiful face, her balanced physique, her lovely voice, her shining eyes—the audience seated in the hall—the audience members who had been thinking, "What kind of insignificant things would come to a corner like Theater A?"—wiped their eyes and straightened their seats as soon as Mariya appeared like a comet.

The desire to take in more and more of her beauty began to burn.

I met her in a private setting and asked her the questions posed to A. With reddish lips intoxicated by whiskey and coffee (휘스키란 컵피) fluttering lightly, she let me hear the desolate answers of B.

It is a fact that the thoughts of the dozen or so girls belonging to this troupe are no different from Mariya's answers. If you look at each one individually, they are all flower-like young ladies, and if you strike up a conversation, they surprisingly offer educated words and sensible answers without hesitation. How did they end up wandering to this country, <92> this land where even love freezes in the northern winter?

On the day I went to see their dance performance, the admission fee was a flat thirty-five *chŏn*, and the number of attendees, including the police officer present, was fifty-two. Among them, about ten seemed to be free spectators. The troupe appeared to have about twenty members, so how do they make a living? With what money do they manage to acquire even a pair of shoes (구쓰) or a single overcoat?

As someone involved in the theater world, I found it utterly pathetic. After the play ended, the pitiful girls, shivering in Chosŏn's early cold in a *tatami* room without even a fire, unable to get even a warm late-night snack, would likely pull thin, ice-like blankets over two or three bodies huddled together, drifting off to a dreamland longing for their mothers' embrace on lonely pillows.

Thinking this, I couldn't bring myself to turn away. They are ignored by the world.
"Those things wandering around low-class theaters like that"—only sharp glances of ridicule and contempt are directed at them. But I couldn't possibly see it as someone else's affair. Aren't my beloved adopted daughter and my comrades, with whom I held hands and shared aspirations, currently suffering hardships not far removed from the K Opera Troupe, attached to touring troupes either in the west or the north? In the Yŏn'gŭk Sijang troupe (연극시장) is my most beloved adopted daughter. When she passed through Kyŏngsŏng last time, she was shivering violently because she had no overcoat. In the Yŏn'gŭksa troupe (연극사) is a younger sister whom I think of like my own blood sibling.
"Brother—I don't hope for luxury, but I should at least have enough money each month to buy the newly published magazines (삽지나) promptly."
I heard her lament. She said she doesn't have the means to afford even one book, hating to fall behind the times even if she goes hungry or wears rags. Because I have seen and heard, to the point where it pierces my bones, the tearful laments of the stars of touring troupes surrounded by such circumstances, I could not face these pitiful troupes flowing into Kyŏngsŏng without tears.

A: Why do you want to become an actress?
B: Yes—I want to devote my life to stage art.
A: And what do you hope to gain?
B: Yes, I dream of applause from enthusiasts wherever I go <93> and a life of glory.
A: How long do you intend to do this job?
B: It would be honorable for a soldier to die on the battlefield and an actor to die on the stage, wouldn't it?
When you first ask aspiring actresses these questions, nine out of ten give such answers. But before many years pass, their answers become the tragic ones of Miss Sawa Mariya.

Considering Miss Sawa Mariya's status as an actress, in Japan, she would likely rank below twentieth-rate. However, in Chosŏn, even after scouring the entire country, it is difficult to find an actress better than Sawa Mariya, the star of this meager troupe that draws only fifty attendees at a thirty-five *chŏn* admission fee. What is to be done about this situation?
Yi Kyŏngsŏl (리경설)
Yi Aerisŭ (리애리스)
Sin Ŭnbong (신은봉)
Chŏn Ok (전옥)
Ra P'umsil (라품심)
Sin K'anariya (신카나리야)
It's not that there are no actresses; they are all people who greet me warmly.
There is not even a fingernail's worth of reason for me to deliberately make such comparisons to pull down the "level" of Chosŏn's top stars.

However, facts are facts up to a certain point, and I just don't want to deceive the judgment that fairly descends upon my mind, which is why I have reached such a conclusion. In terms of social treatment and fame, it is certainly almost insulting to compare a star of an obscure troupe, whose name is unknown in the central theater world, with Chosŏn actresses who tread the boards in Kyŏngsŏng, the capital of Chosŏn, and are praised with photos larger than this size published by newspaper companies representing the nation. However, in terms of income, knowledge, beauty, and art, I would strictly not hesitate to give Sawa Mariya one more point.

But there is a major reason why Chosŏn actresses are poor and their circumstances (주제) are shabby (람루한데).
Hmph, who dares to criticize Chosŏn actresses for showing signs of poverty (궁끼)?
That very fact is their great pride and peerless treasure. I have consistently held this view (고르 거처왓다).
"By the way, with what money do actresses adorn themselves (떨드리고 다닌담) with silk clothes and silk stockings? Money couldn't possibly come out so plentifully from a poor Chosŏn theater troupe..."
I occasionally meet friends who make remarks implying that our actresses, as soon as the play is over, are whisked away in cars or rickshaws, frequenting the market of human flesh (인육의 시장) to rake in money.
"Hmph, don't say such things. What luxury? Even just going to Tokyo or Osaka, top actresses spend hundreds, even thousands of *won* each month just on buying clothes."
"What extravagance is that? Do you know how many days they agonize just to get one pair of silk stockings?"
I give such replies. Truly, there is no one as pitiable as a Chosŏn actress, and there are no ladies quite like Chosŏn actresses. This alone is one of the prides held by the Chosŏn theater world.
"Those actress bitches (녀베우년들) probably think having affairs (서방질) is trivial (우습게 알레이지)." There are people who make such violent remarks.
"Where is there an actress who doesn't have affairs (서방질 안하는 녀배우가 어듸 잇담)?"
There are even *kisaeng* who say such audacious things.
However, that is baseless speculation, merely words stemming from a lack of understanding (인식부), assuming it must be so.
It has been nearly thirteen years (십유삼년) since I started frequenting the dressing rooms of theater troupes; before and after my direct involvement (즉접 관게) with troupes for three years, I am second to none in knowing the inner workings of the troupes, but to this day, I have never seen an actress who, blinded by money, sells her body for, as the world calls it, an overcoat or a pair of shoes. This alone is an admirable (감축한) and tearful fact. That Ri Wŏrhwa (리월화), Pok Hyesuk (복헤숙), and Yang Kun (량군), who should have served as seniors in the troupe, worked as *kisaeng* might be considered a dishonor, but since they did the work of *kisaeng* openly and fairly, there is no room to make an issue of it <94>.

In their flower-like youth, possessing the glamorous profession of an actress, yet treading the path of innocent maidens—their lives as human beings are unspeakably (말단아) tearful and noble.
When the play is over, a daily wage of one *won* per person (매인 압식) is paid (if there's no income, it might not be paid or might be reduced). Then, with that one *won*, after paying their own lodging fees (려관비), if there is money left over, they consult with several colleagues and buy a late-night snack. And if there is still money left over (겨오 남은 돈이 잇스면), then they pool it using a cooperative system (조합제도). "Let's add today's leftover money to buy a *chŏgori* (저고리) for our Chŏngsuk (정숙)." If the discussion reaches consensus, they gather the remaining twenty or thirty *chŏn* per person and buy (끗는다) a length of fabric for Chŏngsuk's *chŏgori*. The next night, it becomes a contribution towards the price (갑으로) of another girl's silk stockings, and the following day, it becomes money for another girl to get (찻는) shoes. Entwined (얼크러저서) like this, harmoniously (의좃케) holding hands and dreaming of a glorious (광영) future (압날), to harbor even a fingernail's worth of impure thoughts towards these pitiful young ladies of the theater troupe—the sin would be close to (갓가울) deserving death.

This is not to say they do not know love. "I cannot live even a moment without your love."
Such lines are invariably included at least once per program. Unless they are made of wood or stone, how could they not know love (어지 사랑을 모르랴)? Don't Misses Sin Ŭnbong, Chŏn Ok, Yi Aerisŭ, and Ra P'umsil all have husbands? However, I have not found the slightest impure reason in their marriages. There is no difference from a young lady from an ordinary family getting married (싀집을 갓다는 것). Therefore,
"Actresses are promiscuous women (잡년)"
—it is better (좃타) to refrain from such violent remarks. When I was involved with the Yŏn'gŭk Sijang troupe, a sixteen-year-old girl sent a love letter (염서) to a young musician (청넌악사) in the same troupe. It was truly a love letter overflowing with pure affection. However, that musician already had a wife and children. Unaware of this, the girl worried (태웟든) by herself. The executives who learned of this fact immediately ordered the girl to leave the troupe on the grounds that she had disturbed the troupe's discipline (규률을 어즈럽게 하고) and dishonored (욕되게 하얏다는) the sanctity of a maiden. That girl is now serving drinks at a cafe (카페) somewhere in the city.
Where else could there be such a strict society? For that reason, people who know the inside story, seeing the hardships actresses endure, have even jokingly said:
"There are no fools like you girls (너이들 가튼 바보는 업느니라). Rich men's sons are gulping with desire (침을 꿀덕꿀떡 삼키는데); if you just pretended to hang out (놀너도 다니고) with them or have a romance (련애라도 좀 하는 체 하면), you wouldn't be living like this (저런 꼴은 하고 다니지 안치)."
I have heard such things (드러보왓다). But the young ladies of Chosŏn just blush bright red and say,
"That's unpleasant to hear (듯기 스려요)."
And turn away abruptly (팩 도라서 바린다). I have thought that this scene alone could be recorded (박여서) as an educational film <96> and shown (빗처도) during ethics class at girls' schools (녀학교 수신시간).

At Ihwa Haktang (리화학당), considered Chosŏn's highest educational institution, flower-like Miss Koreas hold a drama festival (연극제사) every year. That's how much aptitude and interest they have in theater. However, not a single one says they will become an actress after graduating from school.
1. If one becomes an actress, society mocks them (세상이 웃는다).
2. If one becomes an actress, it leads to hardship.
They possess reasons against it concerning both mental and material aspects. However, I have this response ready (준비하고 잇다).
1. Society laughs because there are only people worthy of being laughed at. Please, you step forward with great realization and roll up your sleeves to significantly elevate the social status of actresses. Otherwise, the theater you appreciate will never shine.
2. No matter how hard it gets, will you starve or go naked? If even you shrink back (움츠러들면) seeing the hardships of current actresses, the day when actresses can live comfortably will never come (영원히 오지 안켓구려).
After hearing such an answer (이가튼 대답을 듯고 나서), they will inevitably turn their heads away, saying, "Well, let me think about it some more." But I will persistently (축은축은스럽게) add one more word (마듸).
"You clearly look down on the actresses currently in Chosŏn's theater troupes (현하 조선의 극단에 잇는 녀배우들을 멸시하고 잇슴니다). It is true that in every respect, they are inferior to you who attend professional schools.
However, the current actresses do not exist because they want to monopolize Chosŏn's theater world (결코 조선의 극단을 독점하고 잇고자 해서 존재해 잇는 것은 안임니다).
Isn't there ample room for you to step forward and achieve status improvement, content innovation, and build a paradise of ideals on the stage?
However, even if you have the intention to step forward, firstly (첫재), your families will oppose it (가정에서 반대를 하시리다). Secondly (둘재), your schools will oppose it (학교에서 반대를 하시리다). When dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi (최승희 녀사) graduated from Sungmyŏng (숙명) and went to study dance under Mr. Ishii Baku (석정막 씨의 문하로 무용을 배러갈 때), Sungmyŏng Girls' School (숙명녀학교) absolutely opposed it. When Japanese actress Mori Ritsuko (삼뉼자 - 森律子) appeared at the Imperial Theater (제국 극장), her alma mater, Atomi Higher Girls' School (아도미 고등녀학교), deemed it disgraceful (루추하다고) and deleted her name (일홈을 삭제햇섯다) from the alumni register. However, within a few years (몃해가 안가), when they succeeded and raised the social status of actresses (녀배우의 사회적 지위를 놉혀 놋코) and the artistic status of dancers, eventually the Atomi Alumni Association organized a support group for Mori Ritsuko, and Sungmyŏng Girls' School donated wreaths to the Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi Dance Company and even had current students (재학섕) attend performances as a group for support."
Actress (녀배우)
—it seems characteristic of Chosŏn people to immediately associate this word with a woman of loose morals who sells her chastity (정조를 판매하는 절조 업는 계집). Therefore, both families and schools seem to oppose it at least once. How can excellent actresses ever emerge in the Chosŏn theater world, enveloped in such a stifling lack of understanding (이가튼 기막힌 인식부족)?
When Yun Simdŏk (윤심덕 양) once (일즉이) appeared on the T'owŏrhoe (토월회) stage, the world was momentarily startled. —"What? Yun Simdŏk is an actress (녀베우)?"
And the intellectual class (식자계급) paid renewed attention (새삼스러히 주목도 햇섯다) to the existence of T'owŏrhoe.
Tokyo Music School graduate—vocalist—Girls' Higher Common School (녀자고등보통학교) teacher—actress
Nobody knew (아모도 몰낫다는 것이다) that the endpoint of such an association (련상 끄테) would be "actress." Fortunately, Yun Simdŏk might have raised the standing (긔세도 좀 놉하젓슬는지 모른다) of actresses somewhat. However, she soon left T'owŏrhoe and threw herself into a life of wandering. And so, the conclusion returned to:
"Well, what did you expect? Yun Simdŏk became an actress, and that's what happened to her—"
Meaning, she became a woman who didn't value her chastity (뎡조를 귀하게 역이지 안는 게집이 되고 마랏다).
That's what they say. However, Yun Simdŏk did not become corrupted after entering the theater world; she was already corrupted, had even been to Harbin (합이빈), and then sought the theater world as her final path (마조막 길을 극단으로 차저 드러왓든 것이다).

A new spring has come. What storms and revolutions will arise in our theater world during this coming year? In next year's New Year issue, I want (십다) to convey news that is a bit more dignified and valuable (갑 잇는) than this.
(Reckless words deserve death - 妄言死罪) <96>
<92-96>

**Summary**

The author, Yi Sŏgu, laments the difficult lives and poor social standing of Korean actresses in 1932. He contrasts their dire economic situation and societal prejudice with a visiting minor Japanese actress, Sawa Mariya, arguing that despite outward appearances, Korean actresses possess superior virtue and resilience. He defends their chastity (chŏngjo), countering common derogatory assumptions, and details their frugal, mutually supportive lives within touring troupes. Yi criticizes the societal and institutional barriers (family, schools like Ewha and Sungmyŏng) that prevent educated women from entering the profession and raising its status, citing the initial opposition faced by dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi and Japanese actress Mori Ritsuko. He refutes the idea that actress Yun Simdŏk's tragic path resulted from her stage career, stating she was already on that path before joining. The author expresses hope for a better future for Korean theater and its actresses.

From a historical perspective, this article is revealing about the socioeconomic conditions of performers, particularly women, in colonial Korea. It highlights the stark poverty within Korean theatre troupes compared to even minor Japanese counterparts, reflecting broader economic disparities under colonial rule. The intense focus on actresses' chastity speaks volumes about the patriarchal social mores of the time and the specific prejudices faced by women entering public professions perceived as morally ambiguous. The author's plea for educated women to uplift the profession underscores the class dynamics and aspirations for cultural development within the Korean intellectual sphere. Historians might find the specific examples (names, troupes, schools, incidents like Yun Simdŏk's) and the author's passionate defense valuable for understanding cultural life, gender roles, and nationalist sentiment in 1930s Korea.

얼마나 기막힌 대답이랴! 얼마나 눈물겨운 대답이랴.
K 오페라단이라는 일본 미인의 일단이 시내 A관에서 개연을 한 일이 잇섯다. 만주, 조선을 순회코자 출발된 단뎨 이엇스나 일지동란의 재앙을 입어 만주 순회의 길이 막혀 하는 수 업시 조선만을 순회하는 모양이엇다.

일단의 스타―「마리야」라는 밧련 이십세의 미희가 잇섯다―그 미모, 그 균형된 육선, 그 고흔 음성, 그 빗나는 동자―장내에 안젓든 관객―이까짓 A관 구석에 오즉한 것들이 왓슬랴 하고 잇든 관객들은 마리야가 헤성갓치 낫하나자 눈을 씻고 자리를 곳처 안게 되엿다.

좀 더 좀 더 그의 아릿답음을 마저드리랴는 욕심이 불타오르기 시작한 것이다.

나는 그를 사석에서 맛나가지고 A에게 질문을 하얏다. 그는 휘스키란 컵피에 도취된 불근 입살을 가뷔야히 나붓기며 쓸쓸한 B의 대답을 나에게 들녀준 일이 잇섯다.

이 단체에 몸을 붓치고 잇는 십여명 소녀들의 생각이 누고나 다- 마리야의 대답에 지내지 못할 것은 사실이다. 한사람식 내세우면 모도가 꼿 가튼 자근 아씨요, 수작을 붓처보면 뜻밧게 유식한 소리, 지각잇는 대답도 선선히 들녀주는 그들노 엇지하야 북국의 겨을 사랑조차 얼어바리는 이 나라,＜92＞ 이 땅에 유랑하야 다니게 되고 마랏는가?

그들의 춤구경을 갓든 날 입장료는 삼십오전 균일이요, 입장자 수효는 임석경관을 가산하야 오십이명, 그중에 공구경ㅅ군이 십여명은 되는 모양이엇다. 일단의 인구가 이십인은 되는 모양이니 무엇을 먹고 산단 말이냐, 무슨 돈으로 구쓰 한켜레, 외투 한 개 이나마 어더 입는단 말이냐.

극단에 뜻을 두고 지내는 나인만큼 한심하기 짝이 업섯다. 연극이 파한 뒤 가여운 소녀들은 불도 못땐 다다미 방에서 조선의 첫 추위에 어러 떨며 밤참 한그릇을 따뜻하게 어더먹지 못하고 어름가튼 쪼각 이불을 두사람, 세사람식 얼너덥고 외로운 벼개에 어머니의 품을 그리는 꿈나라로 사못처바릴 것이다.

이 생각을 하니 차마 발길이 도라스지를 안앗다. 세상에서는 도라보지도 안는 그들이다.
그까짓 하류극장으로 떠도라 다니는 것들- 비우슴과 멸시의 눈찌만 날카라올 뿐이다. 그러나 나로서는 도저히 남의 일 갓치는 볼 수가 업섯다. 내가 귀애하는 수양딸과 내가 손목을 마조잡고 뜻을 갓치 하든 동무들은 지금 혹은 서선에, 혹은 북선에 순회극단에 몸들을 붓처 「킹오페라단」에서 머지 안은 고초를 격고 잇지 안켓는가. 연극시장에는 내가 가장 사랑하는 나의 수양딸이 잇다. 지난번 경성을 통과할 때에 외투가 업서 발발 떨어섯다. 연극사에는 내가 피를 난호은 동긔나 갓치 생각하는 누의가 잇다.
옵바―호강은 바라지도 안으나 매삭 신간삽 지나. 또박또박 사볼 만한 돈은 좀 잇서야겟서요.
하는 하소를 드럿다. 배는 주려도 옷은 헐벗드라도 시대에 뒤떠러지기 실타는 그에게 책 한권 구할 여유가 업다지 아는가. 이가튼 환경에 싸힌 순희극단의 스타- 제군의 눈물겨운 하소를 뼈에 사못치도록 보고 드르니만콤 나는 경성에 흘너 들어오는 이가튼 가여운 극단의 친고들을 눈물 업시는 대할 수 업섯든 것이다.

사와마리야 양의 녀배우로써의 지체를 따지고 보면 일본서야 이십등 이하의 배우에 지나지 못할 것이다. 그러나 조선에서는 전국을 달달 뒤저야 삼십오전 균일에 오십명 밧게 입장자가 업는 이 빈약한 극단의 스타 사와마리야보다 나흔 녀배우조차 구하기가 힘이 드니 이 일을 장차 엇지한단 말이냐.

내가 구타혀 이가튼 비교를 해서 조선의 일류 스타-의 「레벨」을 꺼러나려 노흐랴는 고의가 생길 리유라고는 손톱끗 만치도 업슬 것이다.

그러나 사실은 어대까지든지 사실이며 나의 머리에 공평히 나리는 판단만은 속히고 십지 안아서 이가튼 결론에 다다랏슬 뿐이다. 그 사회적 대우에 잇서서 그 명성에 잇서서는 물론 중앙극게에서는 일홈도 모르는 일개가 극단의 스타와 적어도 조선의 수부인 대경성에서 무대를 밥고 민족을 대표한다는 신문사에서 이단 이상의 사진을 내걸고 찬양하는 조선의 녀배우에 참아 비하기도 죄송스럽다. 그러나 그 수입에 잇서서 그 식견에 잇서서 그 미모와 예술에 잇서 나는 엄연히 사와마리야에게 한점을 더 놋키를 주저치 안켓다.

그러나 조선의 녀배우가 가난한 것, 주제가 람루한데는 일대 원인이 잇다.
흥, 누가 조선 녀배우에게 궁끼가 끼웟다고 흉을 보느냐.
그것만이 그네의 큰 자랑이요, 둘도 업는 보배란다. 나는 이갓치 고르 거처왓다.
대체 녀배우들은 무슨 돈으로 비단옷, 비단양말에 떨드리고 다닌담. 조선극단 갓치 빈한한 데서 돈이 그럿케 넉넉이 나아올니는 업는대...  .
맛치 우리 녀배우들이 연극만 파하면 자동차 인력거에 실녀서 인육의 시장으로 출몰하야 돈이나 모화드리는 줄노 아는 의미의 수작을 내놋는 친고들을 가끔 맛난다.
흥, 말 말게. 호강이 무슨 호강인가. 동경, 대판만 가도 일류녀배우들은 매삭 옷해 입는데만 수백원, 수천원식 드린다네.
그게 무슨 사치인가. 비단양말 한켜레를 엇어 신으라면 그네들이 몃칠이나 고심을 하는지 아는가.

멀 녀베우년들이 서방질이야 우습게 알레이지. 이가튼 폭언을 하는 사람이 잇다.
서방질 안하는 녀배우가 어듸 잇담.
이가튼 방자스러운 소리를 하는 기생도 잇다.
그러나 그것은 근거업는 추측이요, 그러려니 하는 인식부에서 나아오는 말일 뿐이다.
내가 극단의 화장실을 기웃거린지 거의 십유삼년 내 자신이 극단에 즉접 관게를 하기 전후 삼개 성상 극단의 내막을 알기에는 남의 뒤에 떠러질 니가 업스나 오늘에 이르기까지 돈에 눈이 어두어 그야말노 세상에서 이른바 외투 한 개, 구쓰 한켜레에 몸을 파는 녀배우는 본 일이 업섯다. 이것만은 감축한 일이요, 눈물겨운 일이다. 극단의 선배 노릇을 해야 할 리월화, 복헤숙, 량군이 기생노릇을 한 것만 이 불명예라 하겟스나 엇잿든 기생노릇을 공명정당이 한만콤 문제 삼을 여지가 업슬＜94＞ 것이다.

꼿가튼 절문 나에 녀배우라는 호화로운 직업을 가지고도 순진한 처녀의 길을 밟는 그들의 사람으로서의 생애야 말단아 눈물겨옵고 거륵한 것이다.
