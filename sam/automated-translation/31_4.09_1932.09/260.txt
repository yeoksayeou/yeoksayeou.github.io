Magazine Title: 삼천리 (三千里)
Publication Date: 1932-09-01
Article Title: 朝鮮婦人運動의 將來 婦人의 참다운 동무는 누구냐? (The Future of the Korean Women's Movement: Who is a Woman's True Comrade?)
Author: 金銀姬 (Kim Ŭnhŭi)
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

朝鮮婦人運動의 將來 婦人의 참다운 동무는 누구냐?
						金銀姬

My husband earns 9 won a month
Therefore I am poorly clothed...

This is the first verse of a song sung by women in a certain region of Japan. Everyone would surely think that if the husband, a laborer, had an income sufficient to comfortably support his family, and if he could provide enough clothing so that his beloved wife, whom he thinks of, would not be poorly clothed, would not shiver and suffer through the three winter months of snow and cold, then women would not have sung such a sad song.

Who doesn't know that the amount of wages (sakchŏn) that husbands or their children receive from factories or other workplaces can extend or shorten the lives of the entire family? However, what is commonly seen in ordinary households is that women can only sigh deeply (長太息), believing it's something beyond their power. Parents work themselves to death, yet their food and clothing are always insufficient. In such times, they inevitably have no choice but to send their sons or daughters to find work in factories, mines, the sea, fields, or mountains. And in the old days (though it is still true now), what was called a woman's whole world, the home, she has escaped from that narrow world and come out into the wider world seeking labor just to make ends meet.

"Women are weak, their work is inferior compared to men's, and they have less endurance than men" – this is commonly said by capitalists who employ female laborers. Thus, it is common for the wages of female laborers to be indescribably lower than those of men. Let's compare how much difference there is based on the investigation by the officials of the Government-General here.

From the table above, readers, you can clearly see the significant difference (差異) in wages (sakchŏn) between female laborers and male laborers. You have likely seen in other tables the large difference in wages between Japanese laborers and Korean laborers. That is<80> not the subject here, so we will exclude it and look only at Korean laborers. Even when a male laborer receives 1 won 15 chŏn (in rice milling), a female laborer receives merely 60 chŏn, meaning a female laborer receives only about half of what a man does. It is not that female laborers work shorter hours than men or receive any special different treatment in the factory. Perhaps some might harbor doubts here. That is, since rice milling involves very strenuous work, female laborers, even if they work the same hours as men, ultimately do less work. However, that is merely a concern. Why? Because it is machines that do the labor, not strength. But to further dispel that doubt, it is good to take the examples of silk reeling or tobacco factories, where women can actually work more and faster than men, work that doesn't require strength and can be done just with fingertips. Even there, we can see that when a man receives 92 chŏn, a woman receives no more than 62 chŏn, and in silk reeling factories, when a man receives 91 chŏn, a woman receives only 75 chŏn. This clearly shows that capitalists exploit the difference in status between female and male laborers—due to the fact that women are subordinate to men—to create such discrimination even in wages, thus exploiting (搾取) women even more cruelly (慘酷), two-fold, three-fold. Such cheap wages for female laborers consequently cause the lowering (低下) of wages for the entire working class, including male laborers, and drive male laborers out of all workplaces, thus increasing the number of unemployed laborers here. (Although the fundamental cause of unemployment is that the capitalist system has XX [flaws/contradictions]). In this way, the emergence of the unemployed and the lowering of male laborers' wages subsequently make the lives of the entire working class even more miserable.

Female laborers, dear sirs! Are you truly weaker than men, and do you perform only as much labor as your lower wages compared to men reflect? Or rather, have you ever had your working hours shortened compared to men? Or despite having many inconveniences related to your sex—women have menstruation, pregnancy and childbirth (孕産), etc.—have you not failed to receive any special treatment? That's right. Female laborers perform the same labor as men, in the same place, for the same hours, certainly no less than men, and yet, under the pretext of being women, they are even made to do various miscellaneous chores (雜役, i.e., women are sometimes made to clean the factory or equipment). Despite this, they suffer XX [exploitation] that is 30% or 40%, sometimes even 50% or 60% more severe than men. This ultimately is not just discriminatory treatment against female laborers, but is being senselessly used by capitalists to further intensify the XX [deterioration/impoverishment] of men and the entire working class. Therefore, laborers who understand such cunning (巧妙) tricks of the capitalists and try to fight without being deceived by such tactics demand, "Pay the same wage for the same labor!" and absolutely support that demand, not only for the sake of female laborers but simultaneously for the sake of the men themselves. Therefore, this becomes a demand of the entire proletarian (無産) class, and here, women will have realized who their truest comrade is, the one who most faithfully (充實) supports their interests and fights for their demands.

Also, housewives in laborers' families who are not factory women might doubt what has been said above because they are not in a factory, but there is no need to speak further. Just by thinking of the song from the Japanese region I quoted at the very beginning of this chapter:
My husband earns 9 won a month
Therefore I am poorly clothed...
is enough, I believe, to more than understand that a woman's comrade is the man who loves her and, consequently, the working class that fights most powerfully for women. Now I turn my pen to speak for rural women. Without the leadership of the working class and their<81> victory, the liberation of the peasantry is absolutely unthinkable, and the liberation of rural women absolutely cannot happen without the liberation of the peasantry. To understand this, one only needs to think about why the lives of peasants are so impoverished (窮乏) and due to whom they have come to live such difficult lives. Let us first talk about why peasants live such tearful lives, incomparable even to animals.

Some peasants sing this song:
... After paying the tenant fee (소작료) and giving the taxes
Even selling sons and daughters isn't enough...

Starting from the still cold early spring, clearing away the snow to begin plowing, sowing seeds, applying fertilizer, and during the midsummer heat (炎天) of May and June reaching 100 degrees, when oily sweat flows from the flesh, the skin (皮膚) darkens, peels, and burns, and eyes droop heavily from the heat, peasants must labor for 15 or 16 hours in the fields or paddies, from before the morning star sets in the West Mountain until the evening moon rises in the Eastern Sky and is nearly overhead. And it is the peasants who sigh deeply (長太息) from the time the golden grain stalks hang low in autumn, worrying about rice to eat and wood to burn for the coming winter. It is also the peasants who weep and worry about the water-use association fees, fertilizer costs, and money to pay taxes. The rice, barley, and beans that peasants have painstakingly cultivated throughout the year with blood and sweat—such hardship of the peasants is truly so painful that no one other than the peasants themselves could endure it—for whom were they truly grown? Peasants sing like this:
... The blood and sweat shed in the May-June heat (炎天)
... Surely wasn't for the landlord (地主)...

Peasants know full well from their daily lives why they must live such impoverished (窮乏) lives. It is inevitable that after farming without rest for even a minute or second, they cannot even eat the delicious rice grown with their own hands, cannot even eat Manchu millet (滿洲粟) that is half-rotten and smells bad (악취), clutch their hungry stomachs, lament and resent the world. They abandon their beloved families and relatives, leave their fertile fields (沃土良田), and drift away to the desolate Manchurian plains called Hanko (寒固), or to the cold lands of Siberia (西伯利亞) where the language and customs are different, or follow the labor market to Japan (日本) or other distant places. The cause of this difficult and bitter life is that capitalists and landlords have made tenant farmers this way, which is well known without needing to be stated. Here, to make this clear, rather than relying on our own investigation, I want to find and cite a table investigated by officials.

Survey Table of Tenant Farmer Income and Expenditures

The above table was surveyed by the Kyŏnggi Province Agricultural Association (京畿道 農會) in Taishō 13 [1924], a time when the world was said not yet to be in such a severe monetary crisis (錢荒). Thus, there will be a large difference compared to today when the agricultural depression is starving farmers worldwide (Continued on page 86)<82> (Continued from page 82). However, even looking at this table, it is clear that farmers' incomes are insufficient compared to their expenditures. From this, one can understand how difficult (苦-로운) the lives of peasants are. The world is gradually experiencing an even more severe monetary crisis (錢荒), the price of grain (ꜘ價), which is their entire income, plummets (폭락), and general commodities are very expensive (빗사니) compared to grain prices, so the poverty of peasants inevitably worsens.

The poverty of rural women is because the lives of peasants are impoverished (窮乏) as described above. Peasant life is due to high tenant fees (소작료), high water-use association fees, many taxes, and expensive prices for everything, while on the other hand, peasants have no income worthy of the name. Therefore, women! You should be able to clearly see who your comrades are and who your XX [enemies/exploiters] are. And urban laborers, always willing to make all sacrifices for the peasants, are the bravest and most important allies. Consequently, women too can find their truest comrades among them.<86>

**Summary**

This 1932 article by Kim Ŭnhŭi argues that Korean women, both urban factory workers and rural peasant wives, suffer severe economic exploitation under the existing system dominated by capitalists and landlords. It highlights the drastic wage disparity between male and female laborers, demonstrating how capitalists use gender division to suppress wages for the entire working class. The author connects the plight of factory women earning meager wages despite equal or harder work (including extra chores) to the suffering of peasant women whose families face crushing debt from high rents and taxes, forcing migration. The article concludes that women's true allies are not the exploiting classes but the male workers and the broader proletarian/peasant class, whose collective struggle, led by the working class, is necessary for the liberation of all, including women.

For historians, this article is significant as it provides a primary source perspective on labor conditions, gender inequality, and rising class consciousness in colonial Korea during the 1930s. It clearly illustrates the low wages (especially for women and Koreans compared to Japanese), the harsh realities of factory and farm life, and the framing of women's issues through a Marxist/socialist lens, identifying class struggle as the path to liberation. The specific wage data (despite potential typos) and the descriptions of rural hardship offer valuable insights into the socio-economic realities faced by Koreans under Japanese rule. The mention of migration to Manchuria and Siberia also reflects known historical trends.

그럼으로 나는 헐벗고 잇서...
이것은 엇던 日本지방에서 여자들이 부르는 노래의 1절이다. 만일에 남편인 노동자가 한집 식구를 넉넉히 살릴 수 잇스리만큼 한 수입이 잇다하면 그리고 자기의 사상하는 愛妻의게 헐벗기지 안으리만큼 三冬雪寒에 떨고 지내지 안으리만큼 입게 하여 주엇드랴면 여자들은 이와 갓흔 구슯흔 노래를 부르지 안엇슬 것이라고 누구나 다갓치 생각할 것이다.
가정에 잇는 부인들이 자기의 남편이나 혹은 그 자식들이 공장이나 기타 자기네의 일자리에서 밧어오는 賃金(삭전)의 다소는 그 一家 식구의 생명을 느리기도 하며 주리기도 한다는 것을 누가 모르리요만은 그것은 부인의 힘으로는 엇지할 수 업는 일이라는 다만 長太息하고 잇기만 하는 것이 보통가정에서 보는 바이다. 부모들이 죽도록 애쓰고 노동해도 그 의식이 항상 부족하게 되는 것이니 그 때에는 하는 수 업시 아들이나 딸을 공장에로 광산에로 또는 바다에로 들이나 산에로 노동할 자리를 구하야 가게 하는 것이다. 그리고 녯날에는 (지금 역시 그러하기는 하나) 여자는 가정이 자기의 천지라고 하던 것이 그 좁은 천지에서 버서 나와서 자기의 입에 풀칠하기 위하야 노동을 구하야 넓은 천지에 나오게 되는 것이다.
「여자는 약하고 일하는 것이 남자에 비하야 못하고 남자보담 견딜 性이 적다」는 것은 부인노동자를 부리고 잇는 자본가들의 공통적으로 말하는 것이다. 그리하야 부인노동자들의 賃金은 남자에 비하야 말못하리만큼 적은 것이 보통이다. 얼마나 만흔 차이가 잇는가 하는 것을 여기에 총독부 나리들이 조사한 것에 의하야 비교하여보자.

우에 잇는 표에서 독자 여러분은 부인노동자가 남자들의게 비하야 그 賃金(삭전)에 큰 差異(차의)가 잇는 것을 자세히 알 수 잇슬 것이다. 日本人 노동자와 朝鮮人 노동자의 賃金에도 큰 차이잇는 것을 여러분은 다른 표에서 자세히 보앗슬 것이다. 그것은<80> 여기에서 말할 바가 아니니 제외하고 朝鮮노동자에 한하여서만 보자. 남자노동자가 1원15전을 밧는 때도(정미소읫 것) 부인노동자는 겨우 60전을 밧을 뿐이니 부인노동자는 남자의게 약 반박게 못밧게 되는 것이다. 부인노동자라고 해서 결코 남자보담 노동시간이 짜르다거나 공장에서 특별한 무슨 다른 대우를 밧고 잇슴도 아니다. 혹시 엇던 이들은 여기에서 이런 의심도 가질 이가 잇슬런지도 모른다. 즉 精米業은 힘이 매우 드는 일이 잇가 부인노동자는 남자의게 비하야 갓흔 시간은 노동한다 하더라도 남자가 결국은 더 만흔 일을 하게 된다고. 그러나 그것은 한 걱정에 지내지 못한다. 왜 그러냐하면 노동은 힘이 아니라 기계가 하는 것인 까닭에 그러나 나는 다시 그 의심을 풀기 위하야 남자보담도 여자가 오히려 더 만흔 일을 速하게 할 수 잇는 그리고 힘이 들지안코 손끗으로만 활 수 잇는 製絲공장이나 煙草공장의 예를 들어보면 조흔 것이다. 역시 그 곳에서도 남자가 92전 밧는 때에 여자는 62전에 지나지 못하고 製糸공장에 잇서서도 남자가 91전 밧을 때에 여자는 75전 박게 밧지 못하고 잇는 것을 볼 수 잇스니 이것은 확실히 자본자들이 부인노동자와 남자노동자들의 지위가 차이(차의)잇다는 것을 이용하야―부인은 남자의게 예속되여 잇다는 사실로 인하여 그 賃金에까지 이런 차별을 두어서 부인을 더욱 慘酷(참혹)하게 2중 3중으로 搾取(착취)하는 것이다. 이와 가튼 부인노동자의 갑싼 賃金은 남자노동자에게 딸아서 全노동자 계급의 賃金을 低下(갑을 내리게 하는 것) 시키게 되는 것이며 남자노동자들은 모든 일터에서 쪼차버리는 것이니 실업노동자는 여기에서 그 數를 느려가게 되는 것이다. (실업자 생기는 그 근본원인은 자본주의 제도가 XX을 가지고 잇는 까닭이지만은)이러케 실업자가 생기고 남자노동자들의 賃金이 저렴하게 되는 것은 딸아서 全노동계급의 생활이 더 일층 비참하게 하는 것이다.

이상의 표는 京畿道 農會에서 大正 13년 아직 세상은 과히 錢荒하지 안타고 하던 때에 조사하엿던 것이니 今日과 갓흔 농업  공황이 세계의 농민을 굶어죽(第86頁에)<82>(제82頁에서 續)게 만드는 때와는 큰 차이가 잇슬 것이다. 그러나 이 표를 본다하더라도 농민은 확실히 자기의 수입이 지출하는 돈에 비하야 부족되는 것이니 여기에서 농민의 생활이 얼마나 苦-로운가를 알 것이다. 세상은 점점 錢荒이 더욱 심하여가고 그 수입의 全部인 ꜘ價는 폭락(몹시 떠러지는 것)되고 일반 상품은 ꜘ價에 비하야 매우 빗사니 농민의 빈곤은 더욱 심하여질 수 박게 업는 일이다.
