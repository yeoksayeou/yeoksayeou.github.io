A Travelogue of Kuksŏm Island
						Kim Sang'ik (金相翊)
Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

You might have read in your esteemed newspaper that an exploration of Kuksŏm (國島), a famous scenic spot in Chosŏn, was planned, hosted by the Taewŏn Club (大元 구락부) and sponsored by the Wŏnsan Branch of the Samch'ŏlli Company (三千里社 元山支). Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng! This Kuksŏm Island is also called Chukto (竹島) because it is said to have much bamboo (참대 [竹]), and also Kukto (麴島) because the rocks (바우 [岩]) resemble clumps of yeast (누룩). These points piqued our curiosity, and added to that were various legends, such as the Kuksŏm stone cave (國島石窟) containing the elixir of life (불로초), or stories about Mr. Yang Pongnae (楊蓬萊). These were the major reasons for organizing this exploration trip.

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

On the early morning of July 24th, as the quiet waves by the mosquito net gently teased my cheeks, called by two or three friends, I rubbed my sleepy eyes, still heavy from slumber, and boarded the Tŏkhŭng-maru (德興丸) at the reclaimed land in front of the Wŏnsan First Fish Market (元山 제일 魚市場 압 海面埋 입지), eager to explore the scenic beauty of Kuksŏm.

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Our Tŏkhŭng-maru, carrying over a hundred male and female sightseers, cut across the surface of the vast Wŏnsan sea (元山海). As we rounded the end of the Kalma Peninsula (葛麻半島), the point called Tangdu-namu k'ŭt'ŭl (당두나무 끄틀), a refreshing breeze, hidden by the island's shadow, suddenly blew, brushing against the sleeves of us hundred-plus explorers. Everyone unknowingly exclaimed, "How refreshing, how refreshing!"

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

From here on, it was the open sea (날바다 [生海]). The Tŏkhŭng-maru, like a wild duck (鳧), glided leisurely towards Kuksŏm through the beautiful scenery where Tae-do (大島) and So-do (小島) dotted the misty waves (煙波) like floating blue seashells (靑螺), sometimes seeming to stitch (눕이 [縫]질하다) through them, sometimes appearing lost, sometimes as if drunk and frolicking.

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Inside and outside our Tŏkhŭng-maru's cabins, cheers and laughter overflowed. One group, including Messrs. Cho Chong-gu (趙鍾九), Kim Kyŏng-jun (金景俊), Han Kun-p'il (韓君弼), Kim Yong-bin (金用斌), Nam Myŏng-sŏk (南命祏), and Yi Ch'ang-wŏn (李昌元), started playing *hwatu* (화투) gambling for cigarettes (卷煙). Whoever ran out of cigarettes or lost at *hwatu* would have to pawn their watch or hand over their necktie, leading to rounds of playful mischief. Another group, like bees seeking flowers (貪花蜂蝶式), tried to sit next to the *kisaeng* (기생) only to be rebuffed, sparking bursts of laughter. And yet another group<67> became pleasantly drunk (陶然히 취해서) with "a cup, a cup, and yet another cup" (一盃一盃 復一盃), each loudly enjoying themselves. Ah, Hyŏng! One might say there's excitement in the sea, excitement in the drink, and yet another excitement in oneself. Is there any greater pleasure than dispelling the heat of summer, drunk on wine and the sea? Leaving the dusty alleys of the world (紅塵의 巷) to sing freely and dance drunkenly (放歌醉舞), feeling like Chao Hsia Yao (趙夏遙一) surveying the world between Heaven and Earth (皞睨 天地間), seems like one of the daily consolations and pleasures for a city dweller.

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

While I, along with my two younger sisters on the deck, looked left towards the Yŏnghŭng Bay Inner Island Lighthouse (永興灣內의 麗島灯坮), which stands majestically 21 *ch'ŏk* (呎) high on the dark blue sea (紺壁의 海水上), a beacon that guides ships by casting light like the eyes of a great demon (巨魔의 眼光) through dark nights of roaring winds (風咆哮) and crashing waves (怒濤逆捲), marking the lifeline (命의 網) for seafarers, the Tŏkhŭng-maru swiftly passed by, without even a moment to glance right at Myŏngsa Simni (明沙十里) with its white sands and green pines. You know well, Hyŏng, that Hwangt'o Island (黃土섬) is famous for its many snakes. We passed its cape tip (岬端). Ah, far away, appearing like a painting amidst the hazy clouds and mist (雲煙*糊中), was Kuksŏm, our destination for the day.

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

After about two hours of sailing, we arrived at Kuksŏm. All the sightseers went up on deck and could only exclaim in unison, "Kuksŏm is absolutely beautiful!" The quick-witted captain, aiming to amplify our shouts of admiration, directed the crew to circumnavigate the island. The water seemed to churn white foam as if rejoicing, the bamboo groves (竹林) seemed to bow their heads in welcome, and the white clouds floating in the blue sky seemed to bloom like cotton and smile. Amidst this beautiful scenery, unfamiliar seabirds, startled by human voices, skimmed the water's surface, flying back and forth (飛去飛來). All of this, Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng, was a scene from a painting (畵中의 景).

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Shall I call them strange, or shall I call them marvelous? Rock formations stood toweringly (巨然히 屹立하여), resembling folding screens (병風) or large buildings (큰 삘-딩). Along the bow-shaped (弓形) coast, large and small rocks, looking as if kneaded like yeast (누룩가티 주멀너 노흔), lay strewn about (즐비히). Behind this belt of rocks (岩石帶), a bamboo grove covered the area, peacefully facing the sea. Surrounded by such grand nature, our group became so mesmerized (황홀하여젓음니다) that we lost ourselves. Ah, Hyŏng! If only this Kuksŏm were a bit closer to Wŏnsan, I imagine it would draw waves of tourists (人波) every summer. Because it is so far from the city (都 會와 머-」 관계), we today savored the distinct pleasure of having it all to ourselves. Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng, what was especially surprising about Kuksŏm was the growth of bamboo (참대竹). Bamboo is typically a product of temperate regions, yet its flourishing on such a lone island (孤島), especially one subject to roaring winds and rough seas (風咆哮海荒波), seems to add to Kuksŏm's unique charm (이채). Ah, Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng! It seems as though the Creator (造化翁), in his spare time after creating heaven and earth, crafted such a wondrous island (妙島) out of sheer amusement.<68>

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Our Tŏkhŭng-maru docked its starboard side at the island's boat landing (島中 船창). The sightseers rushed ashore (爭頭로 下陸하야), carrying the food and drinks (酒食) they had brought. They scattered in groups – some tilting jade cups (玉盃) filled with wine and appetizers (酒肴), some eating meals, some chatting loudly – engaging in all sorts of diversions, laughing and enjoying themselves at this moment (찰나). Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng! Suddenly, the sky grew dark (은밀하여지며), making it difficult to distinguish between mountain and water. A downpour (폭우) began to lash down from the heavens, and from the vast sea (망망한 大海), waves surged forward (押寄하야 오는 파도) as if throwing bombs (폭탄을 擲하는 듯이), violently striking (突摯함에) the steadfast rocks. Trees and plants shook, and the mountains echoed the valleys' cries (山鳴谷應하옴니다). Some among the group became gravely frightened (숙연히 恐하야), muttering "It is too perilous to remain!" (凜乎其 不可留), and boarded the boat to escape the wind and rain. The remaining thirty or so of us stood firm (巨然히 立한) with the unwavering Kuksŏm (一波不動의 國島), some standing, some sitting steadfastly (坐而不動 스리 안진), some lying peacefully. Finishing our commemorative photograph in this state was thrilling enough, but it was also fascinating to see the fishing boats (어선들), which moments before (俄者) had been darting left and right (左馳右往) with their white and blue sails full of the fair wind (淸風을 가득 실고 滿孕), now flocking towards Kuksŏm, calling out to each other in hard-to-understand dialects (解得키 難한 方言), likely fearing disaster from the storm and rough waves (폭풍과 荒波에 遭難을 恐함인지).

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Although the storm and rough waves seemed to have subsided somewhat, a light rain continued to fall (小雨는 一向 내리나니). Mindful of the saying, "If a person has no long-term worries, they will surely face immediate troubles" (人無遠慮면 必有近憂라는 생각), we urged the group to board the ship and then urged departure. Leaving Kuksŏm behind, we reached the waters off Haptchin (蛤津冲). The gloomy sky (憂天), which until moments before (俄者까지도) had shown some signs of waves and rain, cleared up refreshingly, and the sea surface was now as smooth as if ironed (아이롱을 괘한 듯이 平滑하여 젓나니다). At this, the sightseers all clamored together, saying, "Let's make up for the fun we missed on Kuksŏm by playing on Haptchin Island (蛤津島)!" After repeated consultations between the organizers (주최자 측 간부들) and the captain, the Tŏkhŭng-maru dropped anchor (投錨를 하엿나니다) in the waters off Haptchin. Groups of sightseers headed towards Haptchin Island, some by lighter boat (艀舟), others swimming (히염처) in their bathing suits (해수욕복 着한 그대로). Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng! I also joined in for a swim (한추염 기엿나니다).

Ch'ŏngmong Hyŏng!

Haptchin is a small fishing village (어촌) with no more than about 30 households (총 호수 30여호). The friendliness of the islanders (島人의 친목), the cleanliness of the roads (道路의 정결) – everything somehow gave the impression of having escaped the worldly dust (浮世의 俗塵) that we cannot see in our cities. Leaving behind this lingering attachment to nature, the Tŏkhŭng-maru, carrying our group, docked at the Wŏnsan coast (元山海岸) around 9:30 in the evening.
—(The End)―<69>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Kim Sang'ik describes a day trip to Kuksŏm Island near Wŏnsan in July 1932, organized by the Taewŏn Club and sponsored by the Samch'ŏlli Company's Wŏnsan branch. The author details the boat journey aboard the Tŏkhŭng-maru with over 100 passengers, the lively atmosphere onboard, the scenic beauty of the sea and islands, and the unique characteristics of Kuksŏm (also called Chukto or Kukto), including its rock formations and surprising bamboo groves. A sudden storm interrupts their enjoyment on the island, forcing many back to the ship, but adding a dramatic element to the experience. On the return journey, the weather clears, and the group makes an impromptu stop at Haptchin Island before arriving back in Wŏnsan late in the evening. For historians, the article offers a glimpse into leisure activities and tourism among Koreans during the colonial period, highlighting interactions between urban dwellers and nature, social dynamics onboard (including gambling and interactions with *kisaeng*), and perceptions of relatively remote locations like Kuksŏm and Haptchin. The mention of specific organizations like the Taewŏn Club and Samch'ŏlli-sa branch provides context for social and cultural activities of the time.

國島遊記

淸夢兄!

大元 구락부의 주최와 三千里社 元山支의 후원으로 朝鮮의 명승지인 國島探勝을 圖한다함은 혹자 貴報紙上으로 읽어 알앗을 줄 밋읍니다. 淸夢兄! 이 國島는 참대 「竹」가 만흐다하야 일명 竹島라고도 칭하고 또 바우 「岩」가 누룩 뭉처노흔 것과 갓다해서 일명 麴島라고도 하는 이러한 점이 우리의 호기심을 끄으는데다가 國島石窟에는 불로초가 잇다는 등 또는 楊蓬萊씨에게 대한 형형색색의 전설 등등이 금반의 探勝을 주최한 일대의 원인이엿나이다.

淸夢兄!

蚊帳의 靜波가 余의 兩頰을 살낭살낭 희롱하는 7월 24일 일흔 아츰 2,3友人의 호출로 困睡의 接眼을 부비면서도 國島의 景勝을 探하고저 元山 제일 魚市場 압 海面埋 입지에서 德興丸上에 몸을 실렷나니다.

淸夢兄!

百有餘名의 남녀 탐승객을 실은 우리 德興丸은 縹妙한 元山海의 수면을 다름질하야 葛麻半島의 종단인 당두나무 끄틀 굽이처 돌 때 島影에 숨어잇든 청풍이 颯然이 불어와서 우리 백여명 탐승객의 袂를 拂함에 모두가 나오는 줄 몰으게 상쾌상쾌라고 떠들어덜 대엿나이다.

淸夢兄!

이제부터는 날바다 「生海」이외다. 鳧와 如한 德興丸은 煙波사이에 大島, 小島가 점점하야 마치 靑螺가 떠잇는 듯한 美景속을 눕이 「縫」질하는 듯 路를 迷한 듯, 혹은 취하야 농하는 듯 유유히 國島를 바라고 닷나이다.

淸夢兄!

우리 德興丸 선실 내외는 환성과 笑聲으로 넘치엿나니다. 趙鍾九, 金景俊, 韓君弼
金用斌, 南命祏, 李昌元씨 등의 일파는 卷煙내기 화투로 누구던지 卷煙이 떠러지고 화투에 지면 시계를 전당하는 등 넥타이를 글너가는 등 해서는 한바탕식의 작란이 시작되고 또 엇던 派는 貪花蜂蝶式으로 기생여페 자리에 안치려다가는 쪽이여가고는 해서는 함바탕식 웃옴이 터지고 또 어떤<67> 派들은 一盃一盃 復一盃로 陶然히 취해서는 각자히 떠들고 노나이다. 아-兄이야말로 海에 一興酒에 一興 자기자신의 又 一興이라 하겟나이다. 酒에 醉, 海에 醉로 고열의 夏를 消하랴는 快味도 또다시 업는 쾌락이며 紅塵의 巷을 떠나 放歌醉舞도 趙夏遙一 세상하고 皞睨 天地間 함은 도회인으로 일일의 위로될 쾌락의 한아일 듯 함니다.

淸夢兄!

余는 여동생 두 사람과 함게 갑판상에서 항해업자의 命의 網, 風咆哮 怒濤逆捲하는 闇夜, 巨魔의 眼光갓흔 비츨 放하야써 針路를 제시하는 灯坮紺壁의 海水上 그는 21呎의 장소에 巍然히 起한 永興灣內의 麗島灯坮를 左顧하는 사이 白沙靑松의 明沙十里를 右眄할 절을도 업시 르게 德興丸은 黃土섬 뱀 만키로 유명한 줄 兄은 잘 알지요. 岬端을 버서저 낫음니다. 아-머-ㄹ이 雲煙*糊중으로 一幅의 그림과 가티 나타나 뵈이는 것이 당일 목적한 國島이외다.

淸夢兄!
