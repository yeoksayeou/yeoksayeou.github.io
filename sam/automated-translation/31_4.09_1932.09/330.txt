**Body Text Translation:**
That Woman
						Kang Kyŏngae

Startled, she lifted her head and opened her eyes wide. After looking around for a while and finding no sign of anyone, her tense mind calmed down somewhat.
Hearing a bird chirping, "Cheep! Cheep!" somewhere, she barely opened her eyes, thinking it was a dream, and then suddenly remembered her own state as she had frantically risen earlier. She sat up straight, looking towards the doorpost.
The chattering sound of the sparrows seemed to sing of her youth, and her whole body felt a wave of new strength. And in that moment, it felt as though all glory existed just for her.
She stroked her breast, and the thought, "What if this hand were a man's?" suddenly made her ears flush hotly. Though she pulled her hand away from her face, she felt a certain pleasure. As she pulled her clothes on, she saw in the mirror hanging on the wall her own shapely shoulders revealed, roundly. And below them, her hair, like lacquer, cascaded down in waves. In that moment, forgetting even to put on her clothes, she sat there vacantly for a long time, as if possessed by something.
This dream-like room gradually brightened. As the electric light suddenly flickered and went out, she abruptly stood up, put on her clothes, and opened the back door.
The fresh breeze coming in from outside made her whole body feel like flying. And into the poplar grove glistening with dew, the thick, fresh scent of late spring seeped deeply.
Leaning her body against the doorpost, she lifted her head. The dense tree leaves like stars on a moonless night, the blue sky peeking through them, seemingly smiling or laughing, and the rays of light tracing red lines around the edges of the leaves – she drifted far away from herself, wandering into the void.
Her round shoulders, reflected in the mirror moments ago, appeared, and the faces of countless men bowing down around her flashed by, one after another. Accordingly, her short piece published in a magazine and her own photograph came clearly to mind.
She smiled faintly, and unconsciously, the words "Those fools, what good does it do if they act like that for a hundred days?" slipped out, a sneer playing on her lips.
As the letters she received from men increased, and as her writings were published monthly in some magazine, her self-esteem soared sky-high. And she felt as if she were standing atop some high tower.<96>
Like her appearance, her senses were keen. Just as there is no one who hasn't tried composing a verse of poetry at some time, nor anyone who hasn't read a novel, as the times were what they were, a tender sentiment flowed within her too.
Perhaps because of that, whenever she saw a newspaper or picked up a magazine, she always looked through the literature section first. After trying her hand playfully a few times based on what she saw, she happened to write something at the end of replying to a letter from a male acquaintance, and with that as a catalyst, she suddenly became a female literary figure.
She didn't even try to reflect on how she had become a female writer so easily. There was nothing beyond the thought that talents like hers were rare. Therefore, whenever she met anyone, she assumed that the other person already knew her name, Maria, exceedingly well, even if she didn't mention it. When she went out on the street, it felt as if everyone's eyes were focused solely on her, and she thought of herself as that much of a popular figure. More than anything, it was because she recognized that since there were few women writers, it was possible only for her.
Even now, she felt her chest swell with this thought. And looking meaningfully at all the scenery unfolding before her, she tried to gather her thoughts this way and that, wondering what she could write. But somehow, though there seemed to be plausible ideas, the moment she tried to pick up a brush and write, they all vanished somewhere.
"Teacher, please have your meal."
Startled, she turned towards the student and glanced down at her wrist. The watch she expected wasn't there, only the distinct mark where the watch had been remained on her white arm. For a moment, her heart sank, and turning her head towards the head of the bed, she saw the watch beside the mirror ticking away affectionately, "Tick, tock." Relieved, she picked up the watch.
"Why is breakfast so early?"
Her tone completely different from before, she asked with a slight smile.
"Teacher, you're going to the outer village today, aren't you?"
"Oh, right... I forgot. Yes, I'll go."
Only then did she vaguely remember staying up late last night looking for Bible verses for her sermon. Accordingly, the name of the place she was going today, Ŏltugu (二頭溝), came freshly to mind.
The student turned and left. The dangling ribbon at the end of her hair, like braided silk, clearly caught her eye. Following this, she felt a longing for her own student days of the past.
As the sound of the student's footsteps faded, she took a towel and soap and went outside. Who knew when it had been filled, but the washbasin was full of water, faint ripples forming on the surface with the passing breeze. Amidst the chirping of sparrows, she faintly heard the chattering sounds of students and gently put her hands into the water.
After washing her face, she entered the room and sat down before the mirror. Facing the mirror, her plump shoulders, which had been reflected earlier, seemed visible again. She put cream on her hands, rubbed them together lightly, and began massaging it onto her reddish face. The room was filled with the scent of cream.
Following the sound of lightly rustling tree leaves, a single ray of sunlight shimmered in the middle of the room. The room suddenly seemed to brighten. And her face gradually whitening.
Having finished her makeup, she pulled the Bible lying open beside the mirror towards her and, looking into the mirror, nodded her head, thinking, "Indeed, even if I were a man, I would fall for you upon seeing you." Just having thought this, she felt anxious, wondering if someone might be there, and only felt relieved after looking back.
She flipped through the Bible, found the verses she had looked up yesterday, and thought about them again. Following that, various images of the peasants seemed to appear. And the familiar figures of peasants she had seen in her hometown appeared clearly. "Will they understand?" she wondered, frowning slightly.
The peasants she had seen in her hometown seemed to know nothing <97> but eating, bearing children, and working. To pinpoint what more those among them knew, perhaps they might know about Yu Ch'ungnyŏl or Cho Ung from old tales, but otherwise, they were utterly complacent, unaware of what was happening to the country or the nation.
Carrying a few gourds on a mountain carrier (멧산자보), shouldering heavy loads on their backs and A-frames, leading their young children, even as they set out, they couldn't reflect on why they had to turn their backs on their beloved hometown, why their property had been ruined. They were peasants who simply blamed fate and cried wretchedly.
Thinking about this, Maria lost the desire to go to Ŏltugu. It seemed that peasants everywhere were the same. She thought the most foolish were the peasants, and simultaneously, the most pitiful people were the peasants. They seemed like such pitiful humans who could not be saved, no matter how one tried.

After breakfast, Maria, seen off by the students, settled herself onto the carriage. Following her, a pockmarked evangelist woman, carrying a black cloth bundle, climbed up.
The smell of horse dung hit her strongly, making her feel nauseous. She took a handkerchief from her sleeve and held it to her mouth. The light scent of cream rising from the end of the handkerchief faintly brushed past the evangelist woman sitting beside her.
The coarse-looking coachman yelled something loudly and swung his whip, and the horse leaped forward on all fours.
Having agreed once, and especially having received the principal's order, Maria couldn't refuse and thus set off, feeling somehow unpleasant and reluctant. However, on the other hand, the thought that "literary figures sometimes need to travel" and the curiosity to see the natural beauty of the countryside rather than the peasants, along with the hope that perhaps she could compose some masterpiece from it, made her hold onto a great expectation.
This was probably the first time she had gone out to an outer village like this since coming to Yongjŏng (龍井). This time too, it was because the Women's Youth Association established within the Ŏltugu (二頭溝) Christian Church had requested a speaker from the principal of Chŏnghwa Girls' School, and it happened that Maria was the one to go.
She looked back. The students were still standing there, waving. She waved back, forcing a slight smile, as the carriage turned a corner around some house.
The smell of bean oil struck their noses sharply, and the sound of frying oil sizzled noisily. She glanced over and saw the firewood fire burning redly in the hearth through the kitchen door.
Maria somehow felt a sense of sadness and looked back again. Above the rooftops, the branches of the poplar trees fencing the dormitory grounds were green against the sky. At that moment, she thought, "What if I quit this school job and went back home for good like this?" and looked back once more.
The city of Yongjŏng, hazy in the morning smoke, was enveloped in the smells of bean oil and pork fat. And the sounds of water carriers coming and going, and the thick, strong voices of Chinese vendors shouting in clumsy Korean, "Buy fish—buy cabbage—" rolled out from every corner.
Next to the shops, long wooden poles were invariably stuck up, with planks nailed across them, upon which signs like "天興號 (Ch'ŏnhŭngho)" or "元興泰 (Wŏnhŭngt'ae)" were clearly written. And in front of the doors of houses, where five-colored paper was bundled round like a sieve and hung on either side of the entrance, dark wooden boards were placed, upon which freshly steamed buns emitted thin wisps of steam.
Entering the main road, they looked at the roadside trees shining with new green leaves. And between the trees, Chinese and Korean children could be seen intermittently, running around holding hands.
At every intersection, hunched-over policemen, looking cold for some reason, still wore padded trousers and jackets bulkily, holding their rifles horizontally, glancing sideways at the people passing by. Their foreheads shone with grease, their fingernails were sharp like hawk talons, and they occasionally picked their noses with them.
Startled by the sudden ringing of a bell, Maria looked around <98> wondering where such a sound came from, and realized that such a splendid sound came from the carriage she was riding in. Therefore, looking at the back of the coachman's neck, she chuckled, thinking, "He rings the bell quite well."
A vegetable seller coming towards them moved aside at the sound of the bell, wiping his sweat. The cabbage loaded in his basket, slightly dewy, lay piled up, dusted with soil.
"Look at that cabbage! It's grown so much."
The evangelist woman, perhaps coveting the cabbage, said this. The coachman also glanced back, smiled faintly as if he understood somewhat, revealing his yellow teeth. Maria was so startled by his teeth that she turned her head away. And feeling as if what she had just eaten might come up, she clamped her mouth shut and lowered her eyes.
The evangelist woman, perhaps noticing this, smiled slightly and said,
"Those people probably never brush their teeth in their lives."
"Oh my, how can they..."
Maria muttered this, finding it distressing to have to look at that sight all day. And the question suddenly arose in her mind, "Can those people even be called human?"
Having left the city streets behind, Maria looked out at the green fields and then casually glanced back. In the gradually receding city of Yongjŏng, the sunlight glittered dazzlingly on the reddish and blue brick houses.
On the distant mountain slopes, milky mist drifted lazily, and above the endless green stretching to the horizon, sunlight tore down in thousands, tens of thousands of rays, making it even greener.
As they went on, the earthen forts along the road were white with wild bird droppings. They wore the sad expression of a loving old person waiting for a grandchild who never returns.
Along the roadside, nameless tiny flowers bloomed red and yellow. In that green grassy field, even in the world where ants dug tunnels and dung beetles rolled dung, this nature embraced them.

That afternoon at two o'clock, Maria stood high on the platform in the Ŏltugu Christian Church hall and began her lecture on the theme of "Faith," using John 3:16.
The church building was barely ten *kan* (a traditional unit of space) wide, but the congregation filled it to bursting. Maria knew well, of course, that it wasn't just church members.
Everyone entering the door looked like they belonged to a dark-skinned race. Their shabby clothes and faces, tanned dark by the sun, were dreadful even to look at.
Though Maria's mouth chattered on about something, inwardly, other thoughts kept intruding. That is to say, she felt as if she were a phoenix mixed in a flock of chickens, or a white person mixed among dark-skinned people. Consequently, thinking about how beautifully they must see her, how impressed they would be by her words, an unexpected eloquence she hadn't even thought of poured out like a waterfall, without her realizing it.
Although she had presented the Bible and brought up the topic of faith, her speech diverged completely from the subject, crying out for laborers and peasants, and exposing the realities of Chosŏn society.
The crowd, silent as if rain had just stopped, stared at Maria's moving lips and her darting eyes. Somehow, she seemed like a different race from them, lacking passion and blood; in other words, like a pretty doll speaking mechanically. When "laborers" and "peasants" rolled off her tongue, they felt apologetically awkward and embarrassed. And they couldn't help but wonder, "How did she come to know about laborers and peasants?"
Maria's complexion, like the early stages of tuberculosis, and only her blinking eyes beneath heavily drawn eyebrows seemed alive, while her fluttering lips appeared utterly unnatural in relation to her whole being. Consequently, the thought "a studied new woman," that all women who know something look like that, became deeply ingrained in their minds.
"Everyone, even if you die, die in my land; even if you live, live in my land, my land! Why did you come all the way here! Yes,<99> isn't that right? Yes, laying my bones *in the*** is the sole inheritance, right? Do you know, everyone? Don't you know? My land of clear mountains and beautiful waters!"
Maria pounded the desk with her white-jade-like hand and shouted.
The crowd unconsciously let out a sigh, "Heung!" mixed with derision, a sigh that had been suppressed out of boredom until then. More than anything, the memory of leaving their hometown, crying and weeping, leading their young wives and children, surfaced.
"Still, if we were within our land, we wouldn't suffer this pain, this humiliation. Yes, what have we done coming here, dealing with others' wives and daughters? Huh? No matter what, we only have our compatriots. When we are lonely, when we are happy, when we are worn down by poverty, who will cry with us, worry with us? Isn't it our compatriots? Therefore, even if this neck is cut off, even if this body is crushed to pieces, we must live in our land! Yes?"
Tears even flowed from Maria's eyes. The crowd could bear it no longer; anger that had settled deep in their stomachs surged up. Before their eyes, the image of the landlords appeared as if real.
That field, touched and retouched until their hands and feet wore out, until it felt soft to the fingertips! That field, where they could count, if asked, the countless roots of grass spread in the furrows, even the small stones hidden in the corners of the paddy field; that field they were so attached to! When it was unjustly, unreasonably taken away, ah, how their hearts must have felt!
If they had any joy and happiness, it was only this; if they had any courage and attachment to life, it lay here.
But the cruel words falling from those lightly moving, fearsome lips in just one morning had driven them out.
Were they, as Maria said, people who shared sorrow and suffering together? Had those people ever considered their situation even a hair's breadth? Before the crowd's eyes, the eyes, the face of that landlord appeared starkly large again. And the moment they were driven out appeared once more.
"What is the nation! What is my land!"
From outside the window on that side, a voice like thunder erupted. Instantly, Maria's heart leaped. And "Kando peasants" flashed through her mind.
This was because she had previously heard that the Kando peasants were quite fearsome.
Maria lightly swept away the rising fear with a sigh, thinking, "At the very least, I graduated from the top academy in Chosŏn, moreover, I am a rare female writer in Chosŏn, and besides, I am the protagonist of lovely beauty." Thinking this, she glared at them with sharp eyes (Continued on Item 51)<100> A definite sneer played on her lips... "Aren't they peasants?" Maria cried out inwardly.
When the crowd saw Maria's attitude, they realized their mistake in having perceived her until now only as a pretty, cute mummy. And they felt the characteristics of the rich women they intensely hated surging through Maria's entire being. The gentle sway of Maria's fluttering white skirt seemed to mock, to sneer at the crowd's ignorance. At that moment, the crowd saw their wives, their sisters, their beloved daughters, who had died a few days ago without even a warm bowl of millet, appearing to the left and right of Maria.
Weren't their sisters and wives deprived of food, clothing, and education, didn't they shed blood and flesh until their thumbs bled, until they contracted hideous diseases, all to feed and clothe this woman beautifully, to educate her, to make this woman's skin white? After thinking this, even the pastor and elders seated behind Maria seemed like vampires sucking their flesh and blood. No, they *were* vampires.
They suddenly surged up violently. And before they knew it, the church was trampled, and the bell tower collapsed.
Beside the bell tolling its last scream, Maria, her clothes torn to shreds, lay collapsed, yet still afraid only of marring her beauty, she covered her face tightly with both hands and trembled violently.<51>

**Summary**

The story follows Maria, a young, educated Korean woman from the city, a budding writer who travels to a rural village in Kando (Jiandao) called Ŏltugu to give a speech at a church. Vain and conscious of her status as an educated "new woman" and writer, she looks down on the poor, sun-beaten peasants, viewing them as ignorant and pitiful. During her speech, intended to be about faith, she delivers an impassioned but detached lecture on national identity and returning to "their land," oblivious to the harsh realities that drove the peasants from Korea—namely, exploitation by landlords. Her condescending attitude and hypocritical words about shared suffering ignite the crowd's suppressed anger, leading them to violently destroy the church, culminating in Maria cowering on the ground, concerned only for her appearance. The story highlights the stark disconnect and class antagonism between the urban, educated elite and the impoverished rural peasantry in colonial Korea. For historians, it offers a vivid depiction of the peasants' resentment not just towards colonial powers but also towards the seemingly complicit or detached Korean elite, the harsh conditions faced by Korean migrants in Manchuria (Kando), and the complex interplay of nationalism, class, and gender identity during the colonial period. The violent climax underscores the deep-seated frustrations of the marginalized peasantry.

필자	姜敬愛
기사형태	소설

그 녀자
						姜敬愛
그는 엇결에 머리를 들며 눈을 번쩍 떳다. 그리하야 한참이나 사면을 둘너보다가 아모 인긔척도 발견치 못함에 그의 긴징되엿든 머리는 다소 진정되엿다.
어듸선가 짹! 짹! 하는 새 소리에 그는 꿈인가 하야 겨우 눈을 뜨고보니 앗가 밋친 듯이 일떠나든 자신의 꼴이 얼핏 생각키워 문켠을 바라보며 선 듯 이러 안젓다.
재즐대는 참새소리는 그의 젊음을 노래해주는 듯 그의 전신은 엇든 새 힘이 물결침을 늣겻다. 그러고 이 순간에 모든 영화은 자긔만을 워하야 존재한 듯 십헛다.
그는 젓통을 어루만지며 이 손이 만일 남자의 손이라면 하는 생각이 돌자 갑작이 귀밋이 확확 달아 얼굇 손을 떼이면서도 엇든 쾌감을 늣기엿다. 그러고 옷을 끄러단이며 보니 벽에 걸인 면경속으로 아담스러운 그의 억개 우가 둥그럿케 드러낫다. 그러고 그 밋흐로 칠갓흔 머리카락이 구실구실 나리워 잇섯다. 이 순간에 그는 옷 입을 생각도 잇고 무엇에 홀인 사람처럼 한참이나 우독허니 안저 잇섯다.
꿈 갓흔 이 방안도 차츰 새여온다. 어느듯 전긔불이 껌풋하고 꺼저부림에 그는 벌컥 이러나 옷을 입고 뒷문을 열엇다.
밧그로부터 드러오는 산듯한 바람은 그의 전신을 날 듯이 해 주엇다. 그러고 이슬에 빗나는 백양나무 숩속으로 느진 봄 짓흔 풋냄새가 그윽히 새여들엇다.
그 문켠에 몸을 기대고 서서 머리를 들엇다. 금음밤에 별갓치 종종한 나무입과 나무입 그 속으로 우슬 듯 웃는 듯이 낫하나는 파란 하눌 그러고 나무입가으로 붉은 선을 치고 도라가는 광선에 그는 자신을 떠나 멀니 허공으로 헤매엿다.
앗가 면경속으로 빗치우는 그의 둥근 억개 우가 낫하나며 그를 중심으로 업벅드는 수 만흔 사내들의 얼골이 꼬리에 꼬리를 물고 휙휙 지나첫다. 따라서 잡지에 실인 그의 소곡과 자긔의 사진이 선히 떠올낫다.
그는 생긋 우스며 『놈들 저들이 백날 그러면 소용이 무언가』 무의식간에 이런 말이 굴너나오며 입 모슴에는 비우슴이 떠돌고 잇섯다.
그에게 남자들에게서 오는 편지가 만흘사록 그러고 그의 지은 글이 엇든 잡지에 달마다 실니게 되엿슬때 그의 자존심을 아케 놉하저갓다. 그러고 그는 엇든 놉흔 탑 우에 선(立) 듯 하엿다.<96>
그는 생김생김과 갓치 감각이 예민하엿다. 누구에게나 어느 시긔에 잇서서는 시 한귀 지여보지 안는 사람이 업고 소설권이나 읽지 안는 사람이 업는 것처럼 시긔가 시긔인 것만콤 그에게 잇서서도 애틋한 정서가 흘넛다.
그래서 그런지 그는 신문을 보거나 잡지을 대하게 되면 반드시 문예난부터 뒤저보군 하엿다. 그래서 본대로 몃 번 작란 비슷이 지여 보다가 엇든 아는 남자 편지 화답 끗헤 써 보낸 것이 동긔로 그는 일약 여류문사가 되여버리고 말엇다.
그에게 잇서서는 엇재서 자긔가 이럿케 쉽사리 녀류작가가 되엿는지 반성해 보려고도 하지안엇다. 그저 자긔와 갓흔 재사는 두물다는 것 그것 밧게는 업섯다. 그럼으로 누구를 대하든지 먼저 상대자가 마리아라는 자긔의 일홈은 말치 안어도 다 알고도 지나친 것으로 생각되엿다. 길가에 나스면 모든 사람들의 눈이 자긔 한 사람에게로 집중된 듯 하며 그만큼 자긔는 인긔 인물갓치 생각되엿다. 무엇보다도 녀자로서는 글 쓰는 사람이 적은 것만콤 자긔 한사람에게만이 가능하다고 인정됨으로서엿다.
그는 지금도 이러한 생각으로 가슴이 뻐듯함을 늣겻다. 그러고 압헤 전개되는 모든 경치를 의미잇게 바라보며 무엇이라드 써볼가하고 요리조리 뜻어모아 보앗다. 그러나 엇전지 모르게 그럴듯 그럴듯한 곳은 잇건만은 막상 붓을 들고 쓰려고 하니 홀랑 어듸로 다라나버리고 만다.
『선생님 진지 잡수시여요』
그는 놀나 학생 선켠을 도라보며 자긔 손목을 나려다 보앗다. 이스려니한 시계는 업고 힌팔우에 시계자리만이 음숙한이 남아 잇섯다. 순간에 그는 가슴이 선 듯 타하야 머리맛켠으로 머리를 숙이니 면경 엽헤서 잿각 잿각하는 다정스러운 시계소리에 그는 안심하고 시계를 집엇다.
『무슨 아츰이 그리 일흐냐』
앗가와는 딴판으로 살작 우스며 이럿케 물잇다.
『선생님 오늘 외촌에 가신다면요』
『오 참... 내 이젓구나 그래 나가마』
그제야 엇저녁 늣도록 연제될 성경절 찻든 생각이 얼핏 들엇다. 따라서 오늘 자긔가 갈 얼두거우(二頭溝)라는 지명이 새삼스럽게 생각키웟다.
학생은 도라서 나갓다. 그의 삼단갓흔 머리ㅅ채 끗헤 나풀거리는 단기꼬리가 뚜렷이 그의 눈에 빗첫다. 여기에 따라 녯날 그의 학생시절이 다시금 그리워젓다.
학생의 신발소리가 멀어지자 그는 수건과 비누를 가지고 밧그로 나왓다. 언제 떠나왓는지 세수 소래에는 물이 가득하야가는 바람결에 잔주름이 약간 잡혓다. 그는 참새소리 틈에 어렴풋이 들이는 학생들의 시시대는 소리를 드르며 가만히 물속에 손을 너엇다.
세수를 다한 그는 방안으로 드러서자 면경 압흐로 닥어 안젓다. 면경을 대하니 앗가 빗치우든 자긔의 토실토실한 그 억개가 다시금 보이는 듯 햇다. 그는 크림을 손에 뭇처 가지고 가볍게 부벼친 후 붉으레한 얼골 우에 마찰을 시작하엿다. 방안은 크림냄새로 자욱하엿다.
가볍게 흑들이는 나무입 소리를 따라 외줄기 광선이 방안 가온데 얼신얼신 떠러젓다. 방안은 갑작이 환해지는 듯 하엿다. 그러고 차츰 히여가는 그의 얼골.
화장을 다 맛친 그는 면경 엽헤 펼처 잇는 성경책을 끄러단이며 『과연 내가 사내놈이라도 너를 보면 반하겟다』하고 면경속을 드려다보며 머리를 끄득이엿다. 이럿케 생각만을 하고서도 누가 잇지나 안엇나하는 불안으로 뒤를 도라보고야 안심하엿다.
그는 성경책을 뒤적그려 어제 차자본 성경절을 차자놋코 다시금 생각해 보앗다. 뒷이어 농민들의 이모양 저모양이 보이는 듯 햇다. 그러고 그의 고향에서 본 선히 낫 익은 농부들의 모양이 보엿다. 『그들이 알아들을가?』하고 그는 얼골을 잠간 찌푸렷다.
그가 고향에서 본 농부들이란 오직 먹는 것과 애 낫는 것 일하는 것 밧게는 아모것도<97> 몰으는 듯 햇다. 좀 더 그들 중에서 무엇을 안다는 것들 기여코 지정하자면 요담에 나오는 류충열이나 조웅을 알 법이지 그 외에는 나라가 엿지되는지 민족이 엇지되는지 그저 태평이엿다.
멧산자보에다 박아지 멧 짝을 다라매고 구륵짐 멱짐 질머지고 어린 것들을 압 세우고 나스면서까지도 엇재서 자긔네는 그리운 고향을 등지게 되나? 엇재서 가산을 탕패케되엿나?를 생각해 보지 못하고 다만 운명에 돌이고 못나게 우는 농부들이엿다.
그런 생각하니 마리아는 얼두거우에 가고 십흔 생각이 업섯다. 농부들은 어듸 농부들이나 마찬가지로 생각되엿든 것이다. 제일 못난 것이 농부들인 동시에 제일 불상한 사람이 농부들이라고 생각되엿다. 구할내야 구할 수 업는 그런 불상한 인간들로 생각되엿든 것이다.
아츰을 먹은 마리아는 학생들의 전송을 밧으며 마차 우에 몸을 실엇다. 뒤따라서 심얼심얼 얽은 전도부인이 깜안 책보를 들고 올낫다.
말똥냄새가 훅 끼치며 가슴이 멧씩헤지는 것 가타. 그는 소매로부터 수건을 내여 입에 대엿다. 수건 끗에서 가볍게 이는 크림냄새는 겻헤 안진 전도부인에게까지 몸쿤 스치엿다.
보기에도 험상구진 마부는 무엇이라고 소리를 꽥-지르며 채찍을 둘너메니 말은 네굽을 안고 뛰엿다.
한 번 대답해논 것이라 더구나 교장의 명령을 밧아 할 수 업시 마리아는 이럿케 떠나 엇전지 불쾌하고 끄름직하엿다. 그러나 한편으로 문예가는 때때로 여행도 해야한다드라하는 생각을 하자 농부들보다도 농촌의 자연미를 구경하는 호기심 그게 서 엇든 명작이나 하나 엿을가하는 바람이 그로 하여곰 크단 기대를 갓게 하엿다.
그가 룡정에 드러온 후에 이럿케 외촌으로 나가보기는 아마 이번이 처음일 것이다. 이번도 얼두거우(二頭溝) 예수교안으로 설치된 부인청년회에서 정화녀학교교장에게 연사 부탁한 것이 하필 마리아가 떠나게 된 것이다.
그는 도라보앗다. 아직도 학생들은 서서 손질을 하엿다. 그도 마조 손질을 하머 약간 미소를 뛰윗슬때 마차는 엇든 집모통이를 도라섯다.
콩기름 냄새가 그들의 코를 훅-찔으며 기름 튀는 소리가 복질복질 부지지 하엿다. 그는 얼핏 바라보니 부엌문 새이로 아궁에서 일어나는 장작불이 발가케 보엿다.
마리아 이는 엇전지 섭섭한 생각이 들어 다시금 뒤를 도라보니 지붕과 지붕 우으로 긔숙사 울타리인 백양나무 가지가 반공중에 푸르러 잇섯다. 그때에 『내가 이 학교일을 그만 보고 아조 고향으로 이럿케 간다면』하는 생각을 하며 다시금 뒤을 도라보앗다.
아츰 연긔속에 어리인 룡정시가는 콩기름과 도야지기름 냄새로 둘너싸웟다. 그러고 가고오는 물지게소리며 고기-사오-백채사오-하는 섯투른 조선말로 웻치는 중국인의 굵고도 줄기찬 소리가 모통이모통이 이에서 굴너 나왓다.
