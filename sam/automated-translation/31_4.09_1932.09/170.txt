An Interview with Miss Chŏng Yong-hŭi, an 18-Year-Old Female Painter in the Bloom of Youth

Though it was a hot day when feet moving over the asphalt felt heavier than walking through a mud pit, this reporter wandered the narrow alleys of Tangju-dong to meet the young girl painter, Miss Chŏng Yong-hŭi.

The young lady looked very innocent and also kind-hearted. She was not very tall, and her build was a bit stout for her height, but her chubby cheeks, her very dark hair worn long, framing clear, dark eyes—everything about her left an impression of charm.
Her skirt was worn long, and her feet in beautifully sewn padded socks (*posŏn*) were also beautiful.

She treated this reporter, whom she was meeting for the first time, so courteously that I even felt apologetic.

"It must be exhausting to be engrossed in your painting studies in this heat, isn't it?"
"Not at all. I'm fine, but even on such a hot day..." she trailed off, just offering a slight eye-smile.

"I've already seen your paintings. How do you manage to paint so well?"
"Is there anything yet that could be called well-painted?"

"By the way, where were you born?"
"I'm from Yongsŏng-ri, Samsŏng-myŏn, Umsŏng-gun, North Ch'ungch'ŏng Province (忠淸北道 陰城郡 三成面 龍城里)."

"Is that so? Then when did you come here?"
"I came up to Seoul last spring, on the 12th day of the 2nd lunar month."

"Are you staying here alone?"
"No, I came with my father."

"Excuse me, but how old are you now?"
"Me? I turned eighteen this year."

"You're quite young. So, when did you start studying painting?"
"It hasn't been particularly long; it’s barely been 2 or 3 years."

"Please tell me what motivated you to start painting."
"The motivation, you ask? I think it was when I was in the 3rd grade of common school. My father showed me a picture on a folding screen and asked if I thought I could draw it. So, quite boldly, I boasted, 'Why wouldn't I be able to draw something like that?' and after trying it, it wasn't particularly difficult. Also, many people saw what I drew and praised me lavishly, calling me a genius. From then on, I often spent time studying painting."

"Your father must have said that because you originally had talent in painting, wouldn't you say?"
"Well, I wonder."

"Under which teacher did you study painting?"
"When I was at home, I couldn't learn from a teacher<62>, but after graduating from common school, I studied using a painting album called 'Nanjukpo' (蘭竹譜 - Album of Orchids and Bamboo) by Haegang (海岡), Teacher Kim Kyu-jin (金圭鎭). However, trying to study just by looking at a painting album was so frustrating. Now that I learn directly from a teacher, I feel quite refreshed."

"Which teacher are you learning from now?"
"I am still learning from Teacher Haegang. I came here trusting in him."

"I suppose you are learning every day."
"Well, lately, the teacher has gone to a summer retreat, so I haven't been able to learn. In a little while, I intend to learn Japanese Painting (日本畵) from Teacher Yi Sang-bŏm (李象範)."

"Are there many students where you are learning now?"
"Including men and women, there are about 20, but there are only eight women."

"Are they all school graduates?"
"I don't know much about the men, but among the women, it seems there's only one who hasn't attended school; besides her, they are all people who have studied at school."

"As a maiden confined to inner quarters (閨中處女), do you think learning painting is a good thing?"
"Well, perhaps because I regret not being able to graduate from higher common school, I think it would be good if painting could advance alongside other academic knowledge."

"Did you not have any interest in other subjects?"
"As I just mentioned, since I couldn't attend higher common school, I haven't yet developed interests beyond art."

"Then, do you plan to enter higher common school in the future?"
"I very much want to enter, but I'm also old, and circumstances don't permit it, so I don't think it will happen," she says, mixing a faint expression of sorrow into her smiling face as she laughs.

"So, you plan to only study art in the future?"
"Yes—if possible, I'd like to go as far as an art school in Japan (日本), but will that happen?"

"I hope it turns out as you wish. After studying art, what do you intend to do in the future?"
"It feels like no matter how much I learn and research, there will be no end. I want to study East Asian painting (東洋畫), which is losing its strength, as much as I possibly can."

"Do you have any thoughts of holding something like a solo exhibition in the future?"
"It's not that I don't, but if I have the support of various teachers, I would like to try," she said, her face seemingly full of hope.

"Where do you practice your painting?"
"I do it here, and also in Naeng-dong."

"Where is your lodging?"
"My actual home is in Naeng-dong, but that house is too busy, making it inconvenient for painting, so in the afternoons, I am here. Sometimes I go to a friend's house in An'guk-dong to paint, though."

"What time are your lessons?"
"They run from half-past nine until one in the afternoon."

"Do you have many siblings?"
"Yes, many. There are 11 siblings in total, and I am the very youngest daughter."

"Then your parents must be quite old?"
"Yes, they are. My father is 75, and my mother is 57."

"Seeing that your father accompanied you all the way here, he must strongly approve of your painting, Miss Yong-hŭi?"
"Yes—my father wants to help me succeed by any means necessary."

"You're already a success as it is. Among the 11 siblings, are there others with special talents?"
"No, there aren't. They all didn't attend school but learned Classical Chinese in a village study hall (漢房)."

"What does your family do back home (宅)?"
"We farm."

"Ah, I forgot. I know nothing about East Asian painting—what is the most difficult subject within East Asian painting?"
"Among the Four Gentlemen (四君子 - Plum, Orchid, Chrysanthemum, Bamboo), the Orchid and Bamboo (蘭竹) are the most difficult."

"It seems there are many more questions I'd like to ask, but you seem very hot, so I'll stop here."
"I'm just answering, so I'm not particularly hot," the young lady said, still fanning me. Feeling too apologetic, I couldn't talk further and departed. (Woman reporter)<63>

**Summary**

This 1932 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine is an interview with Chŏng Yong-hŭi, an 18-year-old female painter from rural Ch'ungch'ŏng Province who came to Seoul with her father to study painting under the renowned artist Kim Kyu-jin (Haegang). Despite her youth and limited formal education beyond common school, she shows talent and dedication, expressing regret at not attending higher common school but aspiring to study Japanese painting under Yi Sang-bŏm and potentially attend art school in Japan. She aims to dedicate herself to East Asian painting, which she feels is weakening. The interview highlights her modest demeanor, her family's background (11 siblings, elderly parents, farming), her father's strong support for her artistic pursuits, and her learning environment among other students in Seoul.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the lives of aspiring female artists during the Japanese colonial period in Korea. It touches upon the limitations placed on women's education ("maiden confined to inner quarters"), the value placed on traditional arts like East Asian painting alongside the growing influence of Japanese painting styles (Nihonga), the path for artistic training (studying classics like the *Nanjukpo*, seeking established masters like Kim Kyu-jin and Yi Sang-bŏm), and the ambition to study further in Japan, reflecting Japan's central role in higher education and cultural trends at the time. Her father's strong support, despite her being the youngest of 11 from a farming family, is also noteworthy against traditional expectations.

芳年 十八歲의 女流畵家 鄭用姬孃의 訪問記
아스팔투 우에 옴겨지는 발이 진흙구뎅이를 것는 것 보다 더 무거워지든 더운 날이엿스나 기자가 唐珠洞 좁은 골목을 해매엿슴은 어린 소녀화가 鄭用姬孃을 맛나러 함이엿슴니다.
孃은 몹시 순진해보이고 또한 착해보헛슴니다, 키가 그리 크지안코 몸집이 키에 비해서는 좀 굽은 편이엿으나 오동통하게 살진 볼 깜악케 꼭 백인 눈 검은 머리를 길게 드리운 것이라든지 어느 것이나 귀염성스럽다는 인상을 남겨주엇슴니다.
치마는 길게 입고 보선을 곱게 기워 시는 발 이것도 또 아름다웟슴니다.
初대면인 기자에게 어떠게도 상양스럽게 대해주는지 미안한 늑김까지 가지게 되엿슴니다.
「더운데 그림공부 하시게 얼마나 골몰하심닛가?」
「안요. 뎌는 괜찬치만은 이럿케 더운 날에도」하고는 말을 맛치지 안코 눈우슴만 살잭이 우서버리겟지요.
「그림은 벌서 보앗서요. 엇저면 그럿케 잘 그리심닛가?」
「아직 잘 그란다고 할것이 잇슴닛가?」
「그런데 어데 태생이심닛가?」
「忠淸北道 陰城郡 三成面 龍城里이람니다」
「그럿슴닛가? 그러면 언제 이 곳에 왓슴닛가?」
「지난 봄 음역 2월 12일에 상경하엿지요」
「혼자 와 게심닛가?」
「안이요 뎌의 아버지와 함께 왓슴니다」
「실예지만 지금 몃 치심닛가?」
「뎌요 금년니 열여덜이올시다」
「퍽 어리심니다. 그런데 그림공부는 언제 하엿슴닛가?」
「별노 오라지 안치요 겨우 2,3년 박게 되지 안슴니다」
「처음 그림 그리게 된 동긔를 말슴해주십시요」 동긔말슴임닛가 뎨가 보통학교 3학년 적인가바요 아버지가 병풍의 그림을 보혀주시면서 네가 이것을 그릴 수 잇겟느냐고 하시겟지요. 그래서 대담하게도 그까짓 것을 못그리겟느냐고 장담한 후 그려보앗드니 별노 어럽지 안코 또 그린 것을 여러 사람들이 보고 천재라고 칭찬이 자자햇답니다. 그 후부터 종종 그림공부하느라고 햇지요.
「본래 그림에 소질이 잇섯스니까 아버님께서 그럿케 말슴하엿겟군요」
「글세올시다」
「그림을 공부하는데 엇던 선생안태서 뵈우섯슴닛가?」
「집에 잇슬 적엔 선생안태서 뵈우지 못하고<62> 보통학교졸업 후부터 海岡 金圭鎭선생의 「蘭竹譜」라는 화보를 보고서 공부햇슴니다,만은 화보만 보고서 공부하려니 엇더케도 답답하든지요 지금은 선생님안태서 직접 뵈우니 퍽 맘니 상쾌함니다」
「지금은 어느 선생안태서 뵈우심닛가?」
「역시 海岡선생안태서 뵈움니다. 그 선생님을 밋고 차저 왓으닛가요」
「네-날마당 뵈우는 중이겟슴니다」
「그런데 요새는 선생님이 피서지에 가서서 뵈우지 못함니다 인제 좀 더 잇다가는 李象範선생안태서 日本畵을 뵈우려고 함니다」
「지금 뵈우는데 학생들 만슴닛가?」
「남녀를 합하면 20명쯤 되는데 여자만은 여덜 박게 업슴니다」
「모다- 학교 졸업한 사람들일까요?」
「남자들은 잘 모르지만 여자들 중에 학교에 단여보지 못한 사람은 한아밧게 업은 모양이고 그 외엔 죄다 학교 공부한 분들이얘요」
「규중처녀로써 그림 뵈우는 것이 좃타고 생각함닛가?」
「글세요 제가 고등보통학교를 졸업하지 못한 것이 한이 되는 까닭인지는 모르나 그림도 다른 학식과 병진하엿으면 조흘 것 갓슴니다」
「다른 학과에 대해서는 취미를 가지지 못햇슴니까?」
