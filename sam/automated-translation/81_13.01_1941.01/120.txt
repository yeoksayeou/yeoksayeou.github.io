A Volunteer Soldier's Diary, A Record of Their Daily Life
						1st District Unit, 3rd Training Platoon, Shigemitsu Kunio

I was born in a village in Chŏlla-pukto. In Shōwa 13 [1938], I finished normal school and was working as a teacher at Ch’ŏngp’ung Elementary School in Hwasun, Chŏlla-namdo, when last August, my glorious enlistment was permitted. However, my becoming a volunteer soldier after working as a teacher is not because I seek some kind of worldly success. Just as water flows to lower ground and a child shows filial piety to their parents, it is only natural for us, as Japanese men, to become soldiers of His Majesty. Seeing people praise or think strangely of something so natural, I feel regret that there is no one rebuking me for becoming a volunteer soldier so late. I think this is a shameful matter for our Peninsular compatriots. I want to say this: the very first thing to do to achieve Naisen Ittai (Japan and Korea as One Body) is to become a volunteer soldier.

My beloved Peninsular compatriots! If you truly intend to become subjects of the Empire (皇國臣民) and if you intend to practice Naisen Ittai, come to this training center. Anyone is welcome. May the sons of wealthy families come first. Won't you share together the hardships, the doubts, and also this deep emotion of mine?

I hope you do not lose such a fine opportunity.<145>

**Summary**

The author, Shigemitsu Kunio (likely a Korean using an adopted Japanese name), describes his journey from being a teacher in Chŏlla-namdo to joining the Japanese volunteer soldier system in 1940. He dismisses any notion of personal ambition, framing military service as a natural duty for "Japanese men" (including Koreans) towards the Emperor. He strongly advocates for other Koreans, especially the elite, to enlist as the primary way to achieve "Naisen Ittai" (Japan and Korea as One Body) and prove their loyalty as Imperial subjects, expressing disappointment that his own enlistment wasn't seen as belated. From a historical perspective, this article is a stark example of pro-Japanese propaganda authored by a Korean collaborator during the late colonial period. It vividly illustrates the intense pressure for assimilation and mobilization for the Japanese war effort, showcasing the rhetoric used to promote the volunteer soldier system as a path to becoming "true" subjects of the Japanese Empire. Historians would find it valuable for understanding the mindset of collaborators, the promotion of imperial ideology, and the social pressures related to military service in colonial Korea.

志願兵일기, 그네의 生活 一日 記錄
						第1區隊 第3訓練班 重光國雄
나는 전라북도 촌에서 낳습니다. 소화 십삼년에 사범학교를 마치고 전라남도 화순 청풍 소학교에서 선생노릇을 하다가, 지난 팔월에 영광스러운 입소가 허가되었읍니다. 그러나 나는 선생노릇을 하다가 지원병이 된 것을 무슨 출세를 하려는 것은 아닙니다. 물은 얕은 데로 흘으며 자식은 부모에게 효도하는 것과 같이 일본남자인 우리들이 폐하의 군인이 되는 것은 의레이 할 일입니다. 그렇게 당연한 것을 칭찬한다든지 이상하게 생각들을 하는 것을 보면 내가 지원병이 늦게 된 것을 꾸지람하는 이가 없는 것을 섭섭하게 생각합니다. 이것은 반도동포의 부끄러운 일이라고 생각합니다. 나는 말하고 싶습니다. 내선일체가 되는데 가장 먼저 할 것은 지원병이 되는 것입니다.
사랑하는 반도 동포들이여! 참으로 황국신민이 될 생각이 있거든 그리고 내선일체를 실행하려고 생각하거든 이 훈련소로 오시요. 누구든지 좋습니다. 부자집 자제가 맨 먼첨 들어 오시요. 괴로움이나 의심이나 또한 나의 이 감격을 한가지로 난호지 않으시렵니까.
이렇게 좋은 기회를 잃어버리지 않기를 바랍니다.＜145＞
