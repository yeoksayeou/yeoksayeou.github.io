**Roundtable Discussion Recalling the Fierce Fighting of Returned Volunteer Soldiers Who Participated in Actual Combat in Shanxi and Wuhan**
Date and Time: November 18, Shōwa 15 (1940)
Attendees:
Keijō Division Press Corps Side: Colonel Ishigaki, First Lieutenant Yasui, First Lieutenant Usshino (?) (失野 - reading uncertain, could be Shitsuno/Ushino)
Returning Volunteer Soldier Side: Lance Corporal Matsumoto, Lance Corporal Rikuyama (132), Superior Private Kunimoto, Superior Private Kinzan, Superior Private Matsubara, Private First Class Sugiyama, Private First Class I Amu (李岩雨)

Moderator: Today, through the kind arrangement of the Keijō Division Press Corps members, we will now begin the roundtable discussion with the volunteer soldiers who went to the battlefront. As the recruitment of volunteer soldiers for the 16th year (1941) is set to begin on a large scale throughout all of Korea starting in January, we wished for you to come despite your busy schedules, hoping that hearing your various firsthand experiences—from being volunteer soldiers, going through the training center, joining the army, and serving on the front lines for the first time—will serve as a reference for the young men of all Korea. We hope to make this roundtable meaningful by having you speak your thoughts frankly. As for proceeding with the meeting, I will entrust it to First Lieutenant Yasui, who has joined us here. Please begin.

**The Emotion of Going to War**
First Lieutenant Yasui: Even if asked to speak about experiences, they cover various aspects, so it might be difficult to talk about everything at once. Let’s take the format of me suggesting suitable topics and you responding to them. However, if you have any other interesting stories, please feel free to share them.
First, I imagine you all must have been deeply moved and experienced many things when you were first dispatched to the battlefield you had longed for. Please tell us about that, starting with Lance Corporal Matsumoto.
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: When I first heard the rumor that we would be sent to the front, I was overjoyed and jumped for joy. However, since we were the first graduating class, I was quite worried, thinking, "What if they don't send us...?" But fortunately, we were able to go, and I was truly happy. And we all pledged to die for the country (一死報國) and departed valiantly. (133) At that time, I was truly overflowing with gratitude, emotion, and joy, a feeling beyond description.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Does anyone have experiences regarding the roadside send-offs during departure or the emotion upon seeing the Japanese flag (日章旗) on the battlefield? Please speak up.
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: Upon arriving at the battlefront, more than anything, I wanted to see the enemy's face as soon as possible. Anyway, after being on a train for about a week, when I first got off at OO Station and saw the faces of the senior soldiers, they were all tanned dark with long beards, giving me a real sense of the battlefield. Then, entering OO Castle, meeting the unit commander, and saluting the military flag (軍旗) for the first time—that honor was one of the things I could never forget in my lifetime.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Was there anyone who felt particularly deep emotion upon encountering the military flag?
Superior Private Kunimoto: We advanced together with the military flag and fought with the determination to die together with the military flag, under the military flag.
Colonel Ishigaki: You men went off after receiving fervent send-offs along the way. What were your thoughts at that time? Superior Private Kunimoto.
Superior Private Kunimoto: Yes. Thinking of the honor of being selected as the first group from the Peninsula, and thinking that we were representatives of 23 million compatriots, I was determined only to die for the country once I reached the battlefront, so as to meet the expectations of the 100 million citizens.
Colonel Ishigaki: That must have been your resolve when leaving the barracks gate. What about your feelings when leaving the barracks, getting on the train, and receiving send-offs with banners and drums at every station?
Private First Class Sugiyama: At the time of departure, I thought I would fight bravely as a representative of Korea and return, but after receiving the heartfelt send-offs from the numerous crowds at every station, I felt it would be shameful to return alive. I thought I absolutely must die and return that way.
Colonel Ishigaki: That must be right.
Superior Private Kunimoto: I keenly felt what a great force the home front (銃後國民) population's send-off has in boosting the morale of officers and soldiers.
First Lieutenant Usshino (?): At that time, as the transport adjutant, I took them to the battlefield and returned immediately, so I couldn't witness the volunteers' fierce fighting myself. But it was extremely hot, and from Fengtai onwards, bandits still occasionally appeared here and there in front, so the transport required extreme caution. The units containing volunteer soldiers boarded their respective required trains and departed full of vigor. The roadside send-offs were far more enthusiastic than for units from the mainland (內地). At departure, Governor-General Ōno (大野 政務總監) especially wrote words of encouragement on the national flags owned by each volunteer soldier. And the director of the Volunteer Training Center, Colonel Kaida (海田大佐), saw them off as far as Sinŭiju, and when they finally parted, he gave detailed instructions with the warm affection of a loving father. The volunteer soldiers were thereby deeply impressed (134) and showed signs of pledging to fight to the death. Thus, I believe the fervent support from the people of the Peninsula and others at the time of departure became a great force enabling the volunteer soldiers to achieve today's results. As they advanced from Manchurian territory into North China, the volunteer soldiers' morale grew steadily higher, and they appeared eager to reach the battlefield as quickly as possible.

**Finally, to the Battlefield**
Colonel Ishigaki: How far did you go?
Superior Private Matsubara: It was up to a place called OO. While traveling from OO to OO, we were told we would face frequent train attacks, so we stood guard on top of the train, but strangely, the enemy didn't appear at all. We were tense, thinking, "Tonight, we'll surely encounter the enemy," but there was nothing except for an overturned locomotive from a train attack that had occurred two or three days prior.
First Lieutenant Yasui: I heard that the campaign in North China involved extremely difficult marches due to rain this time. Anyone with specific experiences of this difficult march, please share.
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: The most difficult place to march during my time at the front was between Yicheng (翼城) and Qinshui (沁水) in Shanxi. It lasted for two weeks, and that area is entirely clay soil; your legs sink halfway in. There were stragglers, people being hospitalized, and several military horses died trapped in the mud—it was truly incredibly difficult. We carried four days' worth of food, but the equipment got soaked in the rain, making it even heavier. Especially near Qinshui, it's all mountains, and walking on the rocks, my blistered feet hurt so much I even thought dying might be better. In any case, completing the two-week difficult march without falling behind was quite gratifying.
First Lieutenant Yasui: There are no bridges over rivers in China, so there were many times you had to cross fully clothed. Any such experiences?
Superior Private Kunimoto: When crossing the Qinchuan River (沁川), the river width was about 200 meters, and the water came up to my chest.
First Lieutenant Yasui: That was during the OO operation, wasn't it?
Superior Private Kunimoto: Yes. At that time, a steel cable was stretched across, and we held onto it while carrying our gear on our heads to cross.
First Lieutenant Yasui: On the battlefield, I imagine you encountered abandoned enemy corpses wherever you went. What were your feelings at that time?
Private First Class Sugiyama: When we attacked and captured a mountain ridge line, there were enemy soldiers still breathing and dead ones scattered around. Seeing that, and thinking how Japanese soldiers never abandon their comrades (戰友) under any circumstances, I felt quite glad and thought those enemies were pitiful. (135)
Colonel Ishigaki: What was the feeling like when, after strenuous effort, you finally charged and captured an impregnable enemy position? Kinzan?
Superior Private Kinzan: The last battle we experienced was during the Pinglu (平陸) operation, and that was our first field battle. On a mountain peak said to be 1,600 meters high, the enemy had built pillboxes (トーチカ) and were resisting stubbornly. At first, I was quite doubtful whether we could capture such a mountain, but we started the action around 2 AM and captured it by about 10:30 AM. The joy at that moment is impossible to express.
Colonel Ishigaki: When you captured it and shouted "Banzai!", did a loud voice come out? In my experience, tears blurred my vision, and no words came out...
Superior Private Kunimoto: Yes. Looking at the faces of my comrades, my eyes filled with tears.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Any experiences of suffering due to severed food supply lines from the rear, or your feelings when rice finally arrived after a long time...?
Superior Private Kunimoto: We arrived in Zezhou (澤州) on July 20th last year (Shōwa 14 - 1939). As soon as we arrived in Zezhou, the rice supply was cut off, and for two weeks, we survived on porridge made from 2 *gō* (approx. 0.36 liters) of millet per person per day. Then, finally, rice started arriving at a rate of 2-3 *gō* per day. You can't imagine how happy I was then. I had never felt the preciousness of rice like that before. There are no rice paddies there, so it was either corn or millet.
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: I also had an experience near OO, when I went out to support the OO unit. We climbed a mountaintop, stayed vigilant all night without sleeping, returned the next day, and then immediately went out again. This continued for 15-16 days. Due to rain and muddy ground (泥濘), neither trucks nor horse carts could come, so the rice supply was cut off. We survived on potatoes and peanuts to avoid starvation. When the rice finally arrived for the first time, everyone shed tears.

**Memories of Fierce Battles**
First Lieutenant Yasui: Was there ever a time when, under a hail of enemy bullets (敵彈雨飛), you resigned yourself to this being the end?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: It was during the OO attack in the first operation. The enemy was attacking fiercely, and casualties were occurring on our side; I felt incredibly angry. Finally, the charge order was given, and we all charged at once and captured it. From about 200 meters away, we fired at the fleeing enemy, and I could clearly see them falling as they were hit. It was truly refreshing. When a real battle is unfolding, it's probably accurate to say you just fight in a state of oblivion (無我夢中) with the attack spirit you learned.
Colonel Ishigaki: When you were told, "Tomorrow, we attack such-and-such place," (136) what was your feeling the night before?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: When receiving such an order, waiting for dawn truly feels tedious. Wondering how many enemies I'll kill, my arms tremble. I fix my boots so my feet won't swell, wrap my leggings, make thorough preparations, wait for dawn, and feel the urge to see the enemy's face quickly.
Moderator: Did you ever write something like a last will?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: I didn't write such things. We all had the confidence that we wouldn't die even if hit by three Chinese bullets...(Laughter)
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: It wasn't exactly a last will, but when we left here, we generally put our affairs in order.
Superior Private Kunimoto: Some people cut their hair and nails before departing. My mother was critically ill, so I gave them [hair/nail clippings?] to my younger sibling, saying, "If I die in battle, I won't be able to return, so keep this safe," and told my mother I was just going on guard duty for a short while. My mother passed away two weeks before I returned.
First Lieutenant Yasui: It would have been nice if it had been just two weeks earlier.
Superior Private Kunimoto: Yes. While I was at the front, the letters said my mother's illness was gradually recovering, so rest assured. I only knew that she had fully recovered.
Moderator: You must have wanted to show your mother how splendidly you worked and returned.
Superior Private Kunimoto: ... .
First Lieutenant Yasui: Generally, people take protective amulets (お守り) or thousand-stitch belts (千人針) when going to war. Do you have any thoughts on those?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: When departing, I took the thousand-stitch belt and amulet sent by the citizens of Keijō Prefecture. When I wore them, I felt like I wouldn't be hit by bullets. If the amulet and thousand-stitch belt got wet in the rain, I would dry them carefully, put them back on, and treated them very preciously.
First Lieutenant Yasui: What were your feelings when receiving comfort bags (慰問袋) at the front line, especially when receiving comfort bags from Korean compatriots?
Private First Class Sugiyama: In the midst of field battles with no particular comforts, after finishing duty and returning to the barracks, the feeling of receiving letters from home or friends and other comfort bags is unforgettable. We rejoiced just like two- or three-year-old children. The pleasure of opening the comfort packages one by one was a delight that could never be experienced on the home front. Especially, we were fighting so hard, wondering how things were on the home front, so we wanted to see newspapers and magazines. If, perhaps, in an old newspaper scrap, we saw an article saying the home front was always thinking of the front line and weaving straw mats and ropes at night, our courage surged even more. (137)

**Not Inferior in Physical Strength Either**
First Lieutenant Yasui: As volunteer soldiers, what is your pride? Is there anything that makes you feel proud, thinking, "Volunteer soldiers are this outstanding"?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: During marches, not a single one of us volunteer soldiers fell behind. When we reached the barracks and were praised, "Well done," it felt quite good. I believe physical strength is the pride of the volunteer soldiers.
First Lieutenant Yasui: This time, please tell us about a fierce battle you experienced.
Superior Private Kunimoto: The fiercest battle was on August 10th last year. There was a high ground between the outposts of our company and the neighboring company, and about 200 enemy soldiers were there. Our outpost had only 15 or 16 men, but when we called the company headquarters, the order was to capture it. So, 15 of us began the assault. On that high ground, there were three mounds (瘤), and about 100 enemy soldiers were on each mound. The distance between the mounds was about 70 meters. We attacked under the cover of grenade launchers (擲彈筒) and light machine guns (輕機), but they fought well without retreating. So, in the end, we launched a human-wave attack (肉彈戰). The first mound was cleared easily, but since the enemy from the first mound had moved to the second and third, they resisted very stubbornly at the third mound. We fought fiercely for about two hours, but they still didn't run away and responded with hand grenades (手榴彈). So, we all shouted a war cry for a bayonet charge (空突擊). Then the enemy shouted, "It's a charge!" threw all their hand grenades, and ran away. (Laughter) We carried out the charge by deceiving the enemy like this. Finally, the enemy fled. Because it was such a fierce fight, some enemies remained in the trenches, unable even to lift their heads. We caught them from above like digging potatoes, but some guys came at us with knives.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Hoh. They resisted quite a bit.
Superior Private Kunimoto: So, we killed them as they came out. I killed two or three myself. In just about 20 minutes, the enemy fled, leaving behind about 30 abandoned corpses. Our side, with 15-16 men, endured without a single casualty. At this time, I came to have the religious conviction that our Imperial Army (皇軍) is protected by the gods (神).
First Lieutenant Yasui: Have you ever felt deeply moved by the preciousness of comrades (戰友)?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: It was at Qinchuan (沁川). When setting up a position on high ground, we always sent out an outpost. I went up the high ground with several others for guard duty. When morning came, it was time for the meal to arrive, but no matter how long we waited, it didn't come. Finally, around lunchtime, looking far away, I saw the small figures of two comrades struggling towards us carrying rice bowls. Seeing them approach truly moved me. (138)

**Enemy Night Raid**
First Lieutenant Yasui: Were you ever flustered when experiencing an enemy night raid for the first time?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: I have experience being extremely flustered during my first night raid. I was on guard duty on a mountain about the size of Namsan (南山) in OO. There were 7 of us, and we had built something like a pillbox (トーチカ) and took turns standing watch on top. However, perhaps because the sentry was too fatigued and let his guard down, we didn't realize the enemy had approached to within about 5 *ken* (approx. 9 meters) for a night raid. There were about 100 enemies with automatic rifles, attacking the entrance of the pillbox; it was impossible to go out as they swarmed in darkness. The platoon leader at the time, Sergeant Shio (潮軍曹 - reading uncertain), loaded about 30 rounds into the light machine gun and fired rapidly, but there was no time to reload. He wanted to use hand grenades, but they were inside the pillbox and couldn't be retrieved. After firing everything he had, just when it seemed we were done for, we received supporting fire from a battalion gun (大隊砲) from a neighboring unit. At that moment, I truly thought it was divine help. The enemy retreated around dawn.
First Lieutenant Yasui: What happened to the red padded jackets sent by the Korean Military Support League (朝鮮軍事後援聯盟)?
Private First Class Sugiyama: They didn't reach us, but last October in OO, when an entertainment troupe (慰問演藝團) came from here [Korea], we were very happy.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Any experience parting with a beloved horse (愛馬)?
Private First Class I Amu: Since I'm infantry with no connection to horses, I have no such experience.

**Memories of Garrison Duty**
First Lieutenant Yasui: Did you hold recreation parties or have any interesting events?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: We greeted November 3rd in OO. That day, starting from 8 PM, we held a recreation party, did wrestling (씨름), and had other amusements. At the end, I sang the Korean Arirang (아리랑) and was very popular. (Laughter)
First Lieutenant Yasui: What were your feelings when seeing a comrade fall dead during a fierce battle?
Superior Private Kinzan: When I saw a comrade fall, I couldn't stand the anger. The thought of somehow capturing that place and taking revenge filled my heart.
Colonel Ishigaki: When you received orders to go on reconnaissance (斥候), what was on your mind?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: If only enlisted men went, I'd be unbearably anxious, but going with a superior officer, like an NCO scout (下士官斥候) or officer scout (將校斥候), I become completely absorbed in the fight (無我夢中).
Colonel Ishigaki: How about sentry duty (步哨)?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: It's a bit lonely. (Laughter) After arriving there, I stood sentry duty for two nights on a railway line about 3 kilometers from the main unit. Around two or three in the morning, my heart (139) pounded like crazy. The sound of a dog barking in the distance seemed like the enemy's noise, and the rustling of leaves looked like the enemy's shadow; it was unbearable. (Laughter)
Private First Class I Amu: After doing it two or three times, your mind calms down, and you become bolder (膽이 커집디다).

**Baptism of Bullets**
Superior Private Kunimoto: The very first time receiving the baptism of bullets (彈丸의 洗禮), even one passing high overhead feels like it's coming for you, making you jump. But after going into actual combat two or three times and getting tempered, you become daring (大胆).
Colonel Ishigaki: By the time you bow to an enemy bullet, the bullet is already 10 *ri* away.... (Laughter)
First Lieutenant Yasui: What were your impressions upon meeting ordinary Chinese civilians (良民)?
Superior Private Kunimoto: Because we lived too comfortably in Japan proper (日本國內), we didn't appreciate much being born Japanese. But seeing the Chinese populace wandering here and there, fleeing the ravages of war, I truly felt the gratitude of being born a subject of the Empire (皇國臣民).
First Lieutenant Yasui: What were your thoughts when capturing prisoners of war (捕虜)?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: I couldn't bear to see the pitiful state of the prisoners. When the translator interrogated the captured prisoners, watching from afar, they bowed their heads to the ground countless times, pleading for something—it was truly pathetic (惻隱).
Superior Private Kunimoto: Chinese soldiers think they will be killed if captured. When marching together with them for 2-3 days, although they might be hit initially during interrogation, later, when given food and cigarettes, they don't run away. After a week or ten days, if told they are no longer needed and can go, they still don't leave. After about a month, they work quite well, and some even carried ammunition to the front line. When parting with prisoners I had spent a long time with and worked alongside to return here, I felt as if I were parting with a comrade. (Laughter)

**Chinese Civilians**
Colonel Ishigaki: During your stay there, when conducting pacification work (宣撫工作), what were the Chinese people's feelings towards you? Generally, the bad guys and the wealthy ones flee, leaving only the poor. Do they come solely out of a desire for food, or because they admire the so-called morality, believing the Japanese army won't harm them?
Superior Private Kunimoto: At first, I believe they mostly come seeking food. If you distribute food and build schools for the children to attend, then gradually, even the wealthy start to gather.
Colonel Ishigaki: Do the people who come that way appear genuinely grateful? (140)
Superior Private Kunimoto: We can't know their inner thoughts, but outwardly, they appear so. On the morning we go out for subjugation (討伐), they come out early holding Japanese flags to send us off.
Superior Private Matsubara: Women also come and wash our dirty shirts, and bring things like udon or steamed buns (饅頭).
Private First Class I Amu: When the pacification team (宣撫班) teaches Japanese songs to Chinese children, seeing them sing and play well felt similar to Korea.
First Lieutenant Yasui: What were your feelings when you were about to return from the battlefield...?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: Thinking that three colleagues I worked with had died in battle, I truly felt like my hair was being pulled out. Returning from the desolate fields of Shanxi, we should naturally have been happy, but the entire company was silent, not a single person making noise.
Colonel Ishigaki: Here's one point to address: please tell us your motivation for volunteering, from the implementation of the volunteer soldier system (志願兵制度) until you actually volunteered.

**Motivation for Volunteering**
Superior Private Kunimoto: Since the time of the Manchurian Incident (滿洲事變), I had been trying hard to enter the military, eventually deciding to join the Youth School (靑年學校). During that time, the volunteer soldier system was announced, and I wanted to volunteer before anyone else. Of course, my father also supported my wish.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Your thoughts on being promoted to Lance Corporal (兵長)?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: Thinking that someone like me, who is unremarkable, was promoted to Lance Corporal, I am simply overwhelmed with emotion. While thinking how pleased my training center instructors and my parents back home would be, I also keenly felt the increased weight of responsibility.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Do you have any hopes for your juniors (後輩)?

**Hopes for Juniors**
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: More than anything, I hope they come having fully learned the national language (國語 - i.e., Japanese). We are still not proficient in the national language and sometimes use incorrect words.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Do you mean learning the national language well from elementary school onwards?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: Yes, that's right. Also, in terms of athletics, there are areas where we fall short compared to soldiers from the mainland (內地兵丁).
First Lieutenant Yasui: For example, things like bayonet practice (銃劍術) done in the army?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: No. We are strong in things like that, but in activities like the horizontal bar (鐵棒) or apparatus gymnastics (機械體操)...
Superior Private Kunimoto: However, in terms of physical strength (體力), I think the volunteer soldiers are actually stronger.
First Lieutenant Yasui: Do you have confidence that you are superior in physical strength? (141)
Superior Private Kunimoto: I am confident. During marches, it's the volunteer soldiers who compete for first and second place.
Moderator: How did your families greet you upon your return from the battlefield?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: I had never seen my mother so happy before. She shed tears of joy.
Moderator: Did you receive any words of encouragement as well?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: My father originally encouraged me to become a volunteer soldier, so he encouraged me, saying, "It's okay to die for the country, so do as you wish."
First Lieutenant Yasui: Do you hope that many volunteer soldiers will emerge in the future?
Lance Corporal Matsumoto: Yes, I greatly hope so.
Colonel Ishigaki: There must be many things to say, but currently, amidst the great world war, Japan is fighting earnestly to build a New Order in East Asia (東亞의 新秩序). You fortunately had a strong sense of national consciousness (國家的 觀念) burning fiercely, leading you to volunteer and strive directly on the front lines. The 23 million compatriots of Korea are also diligently working as a logistics base (兵站基地). Do you have any thoughts on how things could be improved further? This is a big question, but having gone to war, fought, and returned, do you have any hopes regarding how other young people or the general populace should cooperate in this matter?
Lance Corporal Rikuyama: More than anything, I wish for the conscription order (徵兵令) to be issued in Korea swiftly. I hope all Korean youth can receive the education of the military, this training ground for humanity (人間道場).
Private First Class Sugiyama: Also, I hope compulsory education (義務敎育) is implemented quickly, so that there isn't a single person who hasn't graduated from elementary school, no matter which village you go to.
Superior Private Matsubara: Everyone possesses patriotism (愛國心), but true patriotism is learned only upon entering the military. I hope the conscription order comes soon so that all youth of the Peninsula can gain the experience of military life.
Colonel Ishigaki: Indeed, that's right. It's a mentality unknown to those who haven't entered the military. Only after entering does one realize the military is truly good. Outwardly, marching and other activities, sweating profusely, seem very arduous, but once you enter the military and undergo daily training (鍛鍊), your thoughts, mindset, and attitude progress and improve. You are the ones who must thoroughly disseminate these ideas. Ultimately, you are like specimens (標本). Please strive greatly. It won't do if the specimens are bad.
Moderator: Thank you very much for sharing your very meaningful words for such a long time. Whether you are promoted to NCOs or work as core young leaders, I hope you will make the most of this precious experience and strive diligently. (142)

**Summary**

This 1941 article from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* presents a roundtable discussion with Korean volunteer soldiers who served in the Japanese Imperial Army during the Second Sino-Japanese War, specifically in Shanxi and Wuhan. Moderated by Japanese officers from the Keijō (Seoul) Division Press Corps, the soldiers recount their motivations for volunteering, combat experiences, hardships (difficult marches, food shortages, fierce battles, night raids), interactions with Chinese civilians and prisoners, and feelings upon returning. They express pride in their physical strength, loyalty to the Emperor and the military flag, and gratitude for home front support. The discussion concludes with the soldiers expressing a strong desire for the implementation of conscription in Korea so all young Korean men can experience military training, which they view as essential for cultivating true patriotism and national consciousness.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant as propaganda promoting the Korean volunteer soldier system, portraying the volunteers as brave, loyal Imperial subjects fully integrated into the Japanese military effort. The use of Japanese-style names for the Korean soldiers (Matsumoto, Rikuyama, etc.) and the emphasis on learning the "national language" (Japanese) highlight the assimilation policies of the late colonial period. The soldiers' expressed desire for conscription reflects the militarized atmosphere and the official narrative that Koreans were eager to share the burden of empire, paving the way for the actual implementation of conscription for Koreans in 1944. The discussion provides insight into the experiences and expressed attitudes (whether genuine, coerced, or shaped by propaganda) of some Koreans who participated directly in Japan's war effort.
