I Have Returned Safely, from the Tokyo Imperial Hotel
						Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi

Upon receiving your company's (貴社) letter sent to the Imperial Hotel (帝國호텔), I intended to write something immediately as you requested, but currently, my luggage (族裝) is only roughly unpacked at the Imperial Hotel, and I am truly busy receiving newspaper and magazine reporters (新聞雜誌記者) and friends and acquaintances (親知) who come looking for me.

Thanks to your thoughtful concern (念慮해 주시는 德澤), my baby has grown up well and peacefully during this time. I was surprised at how much she has grown and changed in various ways over the three years. She speaks quite well now, and all her actions (行動) have become like those of an adult. She doesn't feel distant at all from her mother, who was away for a long time. She is only overjoyed and jumping around.

How can I best tell you the story of sailing across the Pacific (太平洋)? If we were sitting face-to-face, there would be many truly interesting and funny stories, but I cannot convey them all in a letter. In any case, I think sea travel (바다旅行) is always more interesting and romantic than land travel (陸地旅行). Although I grew tired of riding trains (汽車) and steamships (汽船) over the past three years, the journey back this time was quite pleasant (愉快). I suppose it was because the joy of returning to my homeland (故鄕) was great. The weather wasn't particularly cold, so I came out onto the deck (甲板) each day, rejoicing as the land of my home country (故國) drew one mile closer, and I forgot all the fatigue (疲勞) from the long journey (旅路).

You asked for my diary (日記) from the time I was sailing the Pacific, and I apologize (罪悚합니다) for only talking about these trivial things. Why must you ask for the diary? If I had known beforehand that you would ask for such a thing, I would have jotted down the interesting (212) events of each day. Perhaps it was due to the long journey, but my body was always tense, so I was completely exhausted (疲困하여서) and didn't think at all about writing such things.
Anticipating being pressed by newspaper and magazine reporters upon landing in Yokohama (橫濱), I knew speaking individually would be difficult, and I'm not a good speaker either, so I prepared written material beforehand to answer their questions (質問).

Leaving Japan (日本) in December of Shōwa 12 (1937), I first went to North America (北米) to perform, and from there continued performing centered around Paris (巴里) in Europe (歐洲), traveling through Poland (波蘭), Belgium (白耳義), Germany (獨逸), and Britain (英國). While performing in these countries, I encountered the current Great War (大戰) and had a truly hard time. I wrote about those hardships in several letters sent home from Europe, so I won't repeat the story, but I truly had a very hard time.

So, last September, didn't I go back to North America? After touring various places there, my very last performance was in Mexico (멕시코). Since many of our compatriots (同胞) live there, I keenly felt their warm affection (情).

It is a fact (事實) that my performances received favorable reviews (好評) during this world tour (世界巡回公演). Not only were the theaters (劇場) first-rate (一流), but in most countries, the managers were also first-rate individuals with broad perspectives (見識) and refinement (敎養), so there were no unpleasant incidents (氣分 傷하는 일).
The dances that received good critical acclaim (評別) were the Eastern ones (東洋的인 것들).(213) For some reason, some critics preferred that I not perform purely Western dances (純西洋춤) and advised (注意시켜 주는) that it would be better if I performed Eastern dances embodying Eastern culture (東洋의 文化), Eastern colors (東洋의 色彩), and atmosphere.

Therefore, among the 30 pieces in my repertoire (레파-트리-), most were Eastern. They were particularly pieces where I tried hard to introduce the classics (古典) of Korea (朝鮮). For that reason, I feel I discovered many Eastern sentiments (東洋情緖) that I hadn't known or realized when I was in my home country (本國). Usually, when dancers (舞踊家) return from performing in Europe or America (歐米公演), they import (輸入) Western dance, but I seem to have done the opposite (叉對). Because I imported Eastern dance. In any case, this tour taught me a great deal in many ways.

I will tell you the other stories when we meet; for now, I will just mention this. I haven't even finished organizing (整理) my luggage yet, so my mind is not settled (安定되지 못 합니다). Until I return to Korea (朝鮮), please convey my greetings (인사) on my behalf (代身) to everyone in Korea.
December 10th
From the Tokyo Imperial Hotel (東京帝國호텔)
Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi
To Samch'ŏlli Company (三千里社 貴中)(214)

**Summary**

Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi writes from the Tōkyō Imperial Hotel in December 1940 (published Jan 1941) upon returning from a three-year world tour. She expresses joy at being back and reuniting with her daughter but apologizes for not keeping a detailed diary due to exhaustion. She recounts her tour through North America, Europe (where she faced difficulties due to the war), and finally Mexico, noting the warm reception from Korean compatriots there. She highlights that her Eastern and Korean classical dances received the most critical acclaim, with some critics advising her to focus on these rather than purely Western styles. Consequently, she feels she has "imported" Eastern dance back, having gained a deeper appreciation for Eastern sentiments during her travels.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it portrays a world-famous Korean artist navigating global performance circuits just before the height of WWII and the Pacific War. Her mention of hardship during the "Great War" in Europe provides a glimpse into the challenges faced by international travelers. Significantly, her emphasis on the positive reception of *Eastern* and specifically *Korean* dance, even being encouraged by Western critics to focus on it, speaks volumes about cultural identity and artistic expression during the Japanese colonial period. It reflects a complex positioning – achieving international fame while highlighting Korean cultural elements, potentially as both a personal artistic choice and a subtle assertion of identity within the constraints of the time.

無事히 도라왔습니다, 東京帝國호텔에서
						崔承喜
帝國호텔로 보내신 貴社의 편지를 받고 곧 무엇이나 말슴하신대로 적어보내려 하였사오나, 위선 族裝을 帝國호텔에 대강 풀어 놓고 있는 形便이고, 또 찾어주시는 新聞雜誌記者며 親知들 接待에 참으로 바쁘게 지냅니다.
애기는 念慮해 주시는 德澤으로 그 동안 평안히 잘 자랐습니다. 3年 사이에 어떻게 크고 여러 가지로 달러젔는지 놀랐습니다. 말도, 썩 잘 하고 모든 行動이 어른같어 젔습니다. 오래 떠러저 있던 엄마를 조금도 서먹해 하지 않습니다. 오직 기뻐서 날뛸 뿐입니다.

太平洋을 航海하던 이 얘길 어떻게 하면 잘 해디릴까요. 마주 앉어서라면 참 재미있는 이얘기, 웃우운 이얘기가 많지만 편지로는, 다 못하겠습니다, 어쨋던 陸地旅行보다 바다旅行이란 늘 재미있고 로맨틱한 거라고 생각합니다. 3年동안 汽車, 汽船을, 실증나게 타 보았건만 이번 돌아오는 길은 퍽 愉快하였습니다. 故鄕에 돌아오는 기쁨이 컷기 때문이었던가 봐요. 날세가 별루 춥지 않어서 甲板에 나와 하루하루 故國의 땅이 한마일씩 가까워 지는 것을 기뻐하며 오-랜 旅路의 疲勞도 다 잊었습니다.
太平洋을 航海하던 때의 日記를 달라 말슴 하셨는데 이런 쓸데없는 이얘기만 해서 罪悚합니다. 하필 日記를 달라십니까. 그런 것을 달라실 줄 미리 알었더면 그날 그날 재미있던(212) 일들을 적어둘 것을 그랬습니다. 원악 오랜 旅行을 한 탓도 있겠지만 늘 緊張하고 있던 몸이라 아주 疲困하여서 그런 것 쓸 생각은 조금도 하지 못했습니다.
橫濱에 내리면 新聞雜誌記者들께 쪼들릴 줄 알고 일일이 말하는 건 힘이 들고, 또 말을 잘 할 줄도 몰으므로 그 이들 質問에 答할 材料만은 미리 써 가지고 왔습니다.

昭和 12년 12月에 日本을 떠나 第一 먼저 北米에 건너가서 公演을 하고 거기서 歐洲 巴里를 中心하여 公演을 계속하여 波蘭, 白耳義, 獨逸, 英國, 各國을 도라 다니며 公演하는 中에 이번 大戰을 만나서 혼이 단단이 났습니다. 그 혼나던 이얘기는 歐洲에서 여러번 고국에 보낸 書信中에, 씨여 있었기 때문에 다시 이 얘기하지 않겠습니다. 마는 참 혼이 단단이 났습니다.
그래서 昨年 9月에 다시 北米로 가지 않었습니까. 거기서 各 곳을 다 巡回한 뒤에, 第一 最後로 멕시코에서 公演을 하게 되었는데 우리 同胞들이 많이 가 살고 있는 곳이라 同胞들의 따뜻한 情을 무척 느꼈습니다.

이번 世界巡回公演에 好評을 받은 것만은 事實입니다. 劇場도 제일流였을 뿐 아니라 대개 어느 나라에서나 마네-저두 見識이 넓고 敎養있는 一流들이어서 氣分 傷하는 일이 없었습니다.
춤으로 評別이 좋은 것은 東洋的인 것들이었습니다.(213) 웬일인지 批評家들 中엔 純西洋춤 추는 것을 좋와하지 않고 東洋의 文化, 東洋의 色彩, 냄새를 딴 東洋춤을 췄으면 좋겠다고 注意시켜 주는 분도 있었습니다.

그러므로 레파-트리- 30個中 대개는 東洋的인 것이었습니다. 特히 朝鮮의 古典을 紹介하려고 애를 쓴 것들이었습니다. 그런 까닭에 本國 있을 때에 알지 못했던, 깨닫지 못했던 東洋情緖를 많이 發見한 것 같습니다. 대개 舞踊家들이 歐米公演을 하고 돌라오면 西洋춤을 輸入해 오는 것이 普通이인데 저는 아마 그 叉對가 되었나 봅니다. 東洋的 춤을 輸入해 왔으니까요. 어쨋던 이번 公演이 여러 가지로 제게 배워준 것이 많습니다.

다른 이얘긴 만나 뵈올 때 하기로 하고 위선 이것만 말슴디립니다. 아직 짐짝도 채 整理 못 하고 해서 마음이 安定되지 못 합니다. 朝鮮 돌아가기까지 제 代身 朝鮮 게신 여러분께 인사 傳해 주십시요.
12月 10日
東京帝國호텔에서
崔承喜
三千里社 貴中(214)
