Romance of Prison Life

The Woman Who Came with a Bouquet of Flowers
Kim Kyŏngjae

While confined in Sŏdaemun Prison for four years, watching the sun rise and the moon pass beneath the iron bars, there were many times I was sad and many times I was happy. Among them, there was one event that left the deepest impression. I will now write down that romance.

Though it was April, the inside of the prison was still chilly. I was sitting in a south-facing solitary cell, reminiscing about the past and imagining the future, trying to make it as romantic as possible. For some reason, I couldn't bear the loneliness and was staring blankly at the ceiling. Already, the evening sun was beginning to set over the peak of Muagwan mountain, and the myriad houses of the capital (長安萬戶) were about to be covered by the thin evening clouds. Just then, from outside, the beautiful singing voice of a young woman could be faintly heard.
Just like in "Les Misérables," when Jean Valjean, holding Cosette, fled from Javert and climbed the old castle wall, and the nun's (修道女) song at midnight shook the dawn dream, brushing past the ears as quietly as a cat's steps—so softly, so beautifully, so affectionately, the slender and sorrowful voice of a young lady came drifting in.
Feeling as if my senses had suddenly awakened, I quickly jumped up onto the lid of the chamber pot and looked out over the wall towards the road. There, beyond the brick wall on the narrow path, just as I had imagined, stood a young woman with a strikingly dark, glossy hair and a certain face, holding a large bouquet of flowers (perhaps azaleas, their color was red), singing. As my face poked through the cell window, she turned towards me, lifted the bouquet she held high, waved it a couple of times, and sang even louder.
Behind her was the road leading to the Appak-kol water source, but as it was sunset, no one else was visible, and the sight of her standing there alone seemed even more profound.
Checking if the guard was watching, I carefully monitored the corridor, stood fully on my tiptoes, and continued to watch her.
However, though I could hear the song, the distance was too great, and no matter how much I rubbed my eyes, I couldn't make out who she was.
A moment later, as the shadow of the dark-clothed guard patrolling outside the prison appeared, the woman, like someone on a pier waving goodbye to a departing ship, raised her hand and waved it gently a couple of times, then quickly disappeared towards the Independence Gate.
As I climbed down from the chamber pot lid, I thought to myself,
"Who could that be?"
She seemed to know me, but I had no way of knowing who she was.
Nevertheless, the joy in my heart that night was beyond compare.

The next day, at about the same time, the same singing voice was heard from outside again.
I quickly jumped up and looked outside as I had the day before. Still holding red azaleas, this time two women had come and were singing.
Feeling as if my world had finally arrived, I was overjoyed beyond measure and truly enjoyed those five minutes immensely.
After the women hastily disappeared due to the guard's rounds, I still didn't know who they were and spent another curious night.

On the evening of the third day, a beautiful voice was again heard faintly from beyond the wall. I looked out, and this time the woman was alone. Instead of flowers, she was waving a handkerchief back and forth, still singing in her lovely voice. Then, finally, she took off the black <39> mando (overcoat) she was wearing and hung it on an acacia tree by the roadside.
At that moment, the woman in the pink *chŏgori* and white *ch'ima*.
I could clearly see her face. It was
Chŏng Chongmyŏng (鄭鍾鳴)!
She is now in prison.
Later, I found out that the woman who came and sang with her at that time was Miss Cho Wŏnsuk (趙元淑).
Hearing that we, related to the XX Incident (XX事件), were all confined in cells facing the Appak-kol area favored by the Left Wing (X翼), the two of them had come to console us as the spring season arrived. I heard after my release that after the third day, the guards made arrangements (安排) to prevent them from going near that area, so they had to stop.
I will never forget the events of those three days.

The Happy Feeling of Sending Something
U Pongun

I have no experience of having been inside a prison. During the time when the social movement was burgeoning in Seoul and many people were swept off to prison, I was wandering around Siberia (西伯利亞). Since then, I have been in and out of police station detention centers (류치장) several times, but I have never dragged this body into a prison.
Therefore, if asked to speak of "prison romance," I have nothing to offer, but if I were to speak of my time in detention centers, there are a few things I recall.

While I was in the Chongno Police Station detention center, one day, someone went to great lengths trying to send in clothes, toothpaste (치분?), and a toothbrush (치솔). Somehow, inside the detention center, I found out about this, and I cannot express how grateful I was.
When one enters such a place, one feels an inexplicable loneliness and emptiness (空虛), but whenever I heard such happy news, I truly couldn't contain my joyful heart.
In the end, that delivery (差入) was not permitted, and I couldn't even receive a single sheet of paper, but I remain endlessly grateful for the sentiment.
This is one impression that remains unforgettable even now.

A Poem on a Moonlit Night
Han Yongun

It's already been 10 years, hasn't it?
Since we thirty-three people (三十三人) were imprisoned in Sŏdaemun Prison for the March 1st Incident (己未年事件).
During my several years of prison life back then, I experienced emotional impulses (衝動) more than once or twice.
However, because it was a life where nothing could be done freely, even these emotions (情緖) were often shattered into pieces, more times than I can count.

It was an autumn night. The weather had been clear for days, truly a fine autumn night where one could say the sky was purely blue (好天一碧), fresh and bright. If I were a free man, it was the kind of time I would have wanted to stroll through the woods, reciting Emperor Wu of Han's (漢武帝) "Song of the Autumn Wind" (秋風辭) or perhaps Su Dongpo's (蘇子膽) "Red Cliff Rhapsody" (赤壁賦)—
Drawn by the moonlight quietly flowing in through the iron bars, I lifted my head over the chamber pot and looked up at the sky. My mind and spirit (心神) felt refreshed beyond words.
The *sijo* verse about wanting to carve out that moon to make my heart came to mind. I also recalled the ancient poem about Su Zi (蘇子) with guests and wine on the 16th day of the 7th autumn month (秋7월 旣望).

Unable to contain myself, I composed a poem (詩 한 首) dedicated to the moon.
And then, I spent the rest of the night until late, gazing at the moonlight. The bright moon (明月) surely shines everywhere—on mountains, fields, the capital (長安), and farmhouses (田家)—but the moon I gazed at then from beneath those iron bars, I will never forget it. <40>

**Summary**

This article compiles three short memoirs about experiences related to imprisonment during the Japanese colonial period in Korea. Kim Kyŏngjae recounts a "romance" where unknown women, later identified as Chŏng Chongmyŏng and Cho Wŏnsuk, visited outside Sŏdaemun Prison for three consecutive days to sing and wave encouragement to political prisoners associated with a censored "XX Incident." U Pongun describes the profound gratitude felt in a police detention center for an attempted delivery of basic necessities, even though it failed. Han Yongun reflects on a moment of poetic inspiration while gazing at the autumn moon from his Sŏdaemun Prison cell, where he was held as one of the 33 signers of the March 1st Declaration of Independence.

From a historical perspective, these accounts offer valuable personal insights into the emotional lives of political detainees and activists under Japanese rule. They highlight the small but significant acts of solidarity and resistance from outsiders, like the risky visits described by Kim Kyŏngjae, which provided crucial moral support. The mention of specific events like the March 1st Movement and the censored "XX Incident" (likely related to socialist or communist activities, given the "X翼" or "Left Wing" reference) underscores the political context of their imprisonment. Han Yongun's piece reveals the enduring intellectual and artistic spirit even in confinement, connecting his experience to classical literature. Historians can appreciate these firsthand narratives for their humanization of historical figures and events, illustrating the blend of hardship, camaraderie, and unexpected moments of beauty or connection found even within the prison system.

獄中生活 로―맨스
꼿다발 가지고 온 女子
金璟載

서대문 형무소에 가치어 사년동안을 철창미테서 솟는 해와 지난 달을 보고 지내는 사이에 나는 여러 번 슬퍼한 때도 잇섯고 깁버한 때도 잇섯다, 그 중에도 가장 감회가 깁든 일이 한가지 잇섯다, 나는 이제 그 로-만스를 적을란다,

사월이라 하여도 아직 감옥안은 선들선들 한 때엇다, 나는 南向한 독방에 안저 지나간 일을 回想도 하고 압흐로 닥처올 일을 되도록 로만틱하게 꾸미며 생각도 하고 잇섯다. 그리며 엇전지 寂寞을 참을 길이 업서 먼-히 天井을 처다보고 잇섯다, 이미 夕陽은 무아관 산머리로 누엇누엇
너머가기 시작하야 長安萬戶는 열분 저녁 구름에 덥히려 한 때다, 그리할 때에 밧갓헤서 돌연히 젊은 녀자의 아릿따운 창가소리가 은은히 들니엇다.
마치 「례,미제라뿔」에 코셋트를 안은 장팔찬이 자벨트에게 쫏기어 古城에 올나 왓슬 때 夜半에 修道女의 노래소리가 새벽 꿈을 흔들면서 고양이 거름으로 고요히 귀까를 스치드시―가장 부드럽고 가장 아름답고 기장 다정스러웁게 젊은 시악시의 가늘고도 哀然한 소리가 들니어 오는구나,
나는 정신이 번쩍 뜨이는 것 갓해서 얼는 뛰어 일어나 똥통 뚝겅에 올나 서서 담장 너머 한길을 바라보앗다. 거기 벽돌 담장너머 오솔길에 내가 상상하든 모양으로 엇든 얼골히고 머리깔이 까마 반즐한 젊은 여성이 꼿을(아마 철축꼿인 듯 빗이 붉엇다) 한아름 안고서 노래 부르고 잇섯다, 내 얼골이 살창가운데로 쑥 내닷자 그는 나 잇는 쪽으로 얼골을 돌니며 안엇든 꼿을 놉히 치어 두어번 내어 흔들면서 더욱 놉흐게 노래를 불넛다.
그 녀자의 뒤편는 압박골 물터로 가는 길이지만 그때는 夕陽때가 되어서 아모개도 보이지안코 그 녀자만이 서고 잇슨 것이 더욱 이미 깁흔 듯이 생각되엇다.
나는 간수가 보지안나하고 복도를 주의하여 가면서 발뒤굼치를 잔뜩 치켜 들고 여전히 그 녀자를 내다보앗다.
그러나 노래는 들려도 원체 거리가 머러서 아모리 눈을 부비고 보아도 그 얼골을 누군지 알 수가 업섯다.
조곰 잇다가 감옥 밧갓흘 순시하는 거문 간수의 그림자가 비최자 그 녀자는 마치 떠나가는 배를 향하야 부두에 선 사람이 인사하드시 손을 드러 두어번 너울너울 젓고는 이내 독립문 부근으로 자최를 감추고 만다.
나는 똥뚜겅에서 내려서면서
「그게 누굴고?」
하고 혼자 곰곰 생각하엿다. 저켠은 나를 아는 모양인데 끗끗내 나는 알 길이 업섯다.
그러치만 그날 밤 내 마음이 유쾌한 품은 어대에다 비할 길이 업섯다.

그 이튼날 그만한 시각에 또 밧갓흐로부터 그러한 노래소리가 들니엇다.
나는 얼는 뛰여 일어나 어적게 모양으로 밧갓흘 내다 보앗다. 여전히 빨간 철축꼿을 안고 이번에는 두녀자가 와서 노래 부른다.
나는 내 세상이 인제 다닥처 온드시 엇더케 깁분지 몰나서 오분간을 정말 유쾌하게 엔조이 하엿다.
간수의 순희에 그 녀자들이 총총히 사라진 뒤 나는 역시 그가 누구인줄 모르고 궁금한 하로밤을 또 지냇다.

우리들 삼십삼인이 긔미년사건으로 서대문 감옥에 갓치어 잇든 것이,
나는 그때 수년 옥중생활을 하는 사이에 정서적으로 衝動을 밧어 본적이 한 두번이 아니엇다.
그러나 모든 것이 맘대로 못되는 생활임으로 말하자면 이 情緖조차 쪼각쪼각 바서저 버리는 때가 엇더케 만엇는지 모른다.
