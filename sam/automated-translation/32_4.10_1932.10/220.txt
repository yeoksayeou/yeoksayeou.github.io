Publication Date: 1932년 10월 01일
Article Title: 露西亞께베우 (Russian GPU)
Author: Not specified
Article Type: 소식 (News/Information)

Let's talk a bit about the content of the G.P.U. (께, 페, 우).
The G.P.U. is called the State Security Department (國家保安部), but it shouldn't be interpreted so simply. If you will, it combines the work of the Metropolitan Police Agency, the Police Security Bureau, the Gendarmerie, and the Ministry of Justice. How powerful must it be? It commands 150,000 elite troops beneath it, holding the right of life, death, rise, and fall over the populace. Within it, there is a Foreign Department (외국部) which acts as the henchman for Soviet Russian embassies and legations abroad, dealing with things like spies, communist propaganda (赤化宣傳), and revolutionary agitation (革命煽動).

Its organization is truly extensive, consisting of:
1. Military – Red Army (赤軍) domestic troops, border troops
2. Foreign Department – Spies (密偵), revolutionary propaganda, disciplinary unit for Soviet Russian officials abroad
3. Intelligence Department, Economic Department – General information spies (情報密偵)
4. Administrative Department
5. Publishing and Printing House
6. People's Police (民警)

Indeed, it is extensively organized.
Especially, the ultimate point of the G.P.U. is its meticulously prepared Intelligence Department (牒報部); its spy network (스파이綱) stretches across the entire country, with hundreds of thousands engaged in this, and furthermore, the network of voluntary informers (自由密告) must reach several million people.
Communist party members have the duty to immediately report to the G.P.U. if they hear any anti-communist talk.
Therefore, even non-party members inevitably become spies and serve the Party loyally.
Prostitutes (賣笑婦) are also spies (密偵), professors and doctors are spies (密偵), and even elementary school students are among them. Therefore, if three Russians gather, there is always a spy (密偵). Sometimes, all three gathered are spies (密偵).

"What kind of work does the Economic Intelligence Department do?"
"First, it investigates the people's spending. For example, if someone wears slightly higher-quality clothing, the G.P.U. tails them, and restaurant bills are copied<62> and notifications are sent to the G.P.U.
If someone spends about 10 rubles at once, the source of that money is investigated. Therefore, foreign spies who don't know the situation in the meantime are exposed through their spending.
Correspondence (書信), whether domestic or foreign, is naturally censored."

"How are conspiracies (陰謀) and the like suppressed?"
"Unlike in other countries, they don't nip it in the bud. They let it grow sufficiently until it's deemed unable to progress any further. Then they round them up all at once. This is because the G.P.U. has strong self-confidence.
Not only that, but where there are some disgruntled elements, spies infiltrate, incite and expand that dissatisfaction, and then annihilate those elements."

"What is the work and content of the Foreign Department?"
"In a previous year, when the Beijing government concluded a secret treaty with a certain major power, the then Soviet Russian Ambassador Karakhan (카라한) requested to obtain a copy of the treaty text within 24 hours. The spy handed it over to Karakhan within 12 hours, which astonished the Chinese authorities at the time.
When the three monarchists entered Russia in August 1927, the G.P.U. knew about it two weeks in advance and shot the three dead at the Finnish border (芬蘭土國境).
In January 1930, General Kutepov (구테-포흐) was lured away in Paris (巴里) by the G.P.U.
These are merely glimpses of the activities of the Foreign Department's spies abroad."

"Does the Foreign Department also engage in world revolution?"
"Of course. It does everything: military and economic espionage in various foreign countries, communist propaganda (赤化宣傳), preparation for revolutionary riots, inciting the unemployed, strikes (怠業), boycotts (盟파), instigating and supporting resistance against authorities – collaborating with the Red Army (赤軍) General Staff. It planned a communist revolution in Germany (獨逸) and caused internal strife in the Persia (페르샤) and Afghanistan (아프간) regions.
This is because it cleverly utilizes the communist party members of those countries. The method of utilization starts with monetary temptation.
When the G.P.U. enters a foreign country, it does so openly under the guise of embassy staff, commercial mission staff, or consulate staff. Of course, forging visas (*造의 査證) is easier than eating.
The second and third secretaries of embassies are mostly G.P.U. spies (密偵), it is said. It is said that not a few enter Japan (日本) or China (支那) disguised as Americans (米國人). The expenses are also said to be sent from the United States (米國). Welcomed as guests from the kingdom of the dollar, their true identity is said to be spies for communist propaganda (赤化선전). Inside the G.P.U. (깨, 베, 우), there is a document forgery department (*造部), so they can freely create any country's documents, thus many enter disguised as actors or musicians."

"Then, do they also carry out acts of terror domestically?"
"Executions are not infrequent, but they are divided into those made public and those kept secret. Public announcements are limited to times when they want to instill fear in the people and create a favorable influence for the communist party authorities; otherwise, they are secretly shot.
When carrying out an execution, they say, 'We're changing your room, put on your clothes,' and lead them outside. Then, they lead them round and round the corridor and guide them to a certain room. Just as they are about to enter the room, they shoot them once in the back of the head with a pistol. Then, everything is over (萬事는 休矣), and no one besides the person involved knows."

"Is it true that they use torture (拷問)?"
"The most common method is sleep deprivation. Day and night, a high-intensity electric lamp is shone directly into the defendant's eyes. On top of that, whenever they try to fall asleep, the light is turned off and on again dozens of times overnight. Between the sleep deprivation and the glaring light, no matter how stubborn one is, they inevitably fall into a state of semi-madness and nervous breakdown (神經衰弱症) within 2-3 days.
Unable to endure this suffering, the defendant writes a statement (調書) as desired by the G.P.U. official. They sign it."

"Then finally, will the power of the Communist Party be perpetual (永續的)?"
"Regarding that point, speaking of the current situation as it is, one can only say that for now, it seems perpetual.
Unless Russia (러시아) is defeated in a major war with other powers or suffers an unavoidable great disaster due to extraordinary natural or terrestrial changes, Russia will become more and more the Russia of the Communist Party (Remainder omitted - original text)<63>

**Summary**

This 1932 article from the Korean magazine *Samch'ŏlli* describes the Soviet secret police, the GPU (referred to as "Kke, P'e, U"), portraying it as an immensely powerful and terrifying organization. It details the GPU's structure, extensive domestic surveillance network utilizing millions of spies and informants (including ordinary citizens, professionals, and even children), methods of suppressing dissent (allowing plots to grow before crushing them, inciting discontent), censorship, control over spending, use of torture (specifically sleep deprivation), and secret executions. The article also highlights the GPU's Foreign Department's international espionage, propaganda, and revolutionary activities, mentioning specific incidents in China, Finland, and France, and its infiltration tactics using diplomatic cover or forged identities. The piece concludes by suggesting the Communist Party's power seems perpetual barring a major war or disaster. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it reflects the strong anti-communist propaganda prevalent under Japanese rule. It served to warn Koreans about the perceived dangers of the Soviet system and communism, potentially shaping public opinion and justifying the colonial government's own suppressive measures against leftist movements in Korea. Historians might find it valuable for understanding the dissemination of anti-Soviet narratives in colonial Korea and the specific details emphasized (pervasive surveillance, brutality, international subversion) to create fear and distrust.
