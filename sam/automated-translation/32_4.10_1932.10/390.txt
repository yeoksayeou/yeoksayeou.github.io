A Lonely Evening
						Kim Tongin

However, a moment later, the old Viscount's eyes, blazing with anger, swung around and fixed squarely on his son's face.
"To you— to you—"
The old Viscount gasped for breath due to his anger—
"Do I seem that senile to you, my son!"
The countenance of the old Viscount, who had once made his name known for both good governance and tyranny as a tiger-like prime minister, emerged here. Though his face was sallow and thin, the voice that resonated from it made the room rumble.
As Tusik stood bewildered and flustered before his father, who was becoming agitated again, the father shouted once more, calling for the loyal servant Wangbo in the room yonder.
"Hey, Wangbo-ya! Wangbo-ya!"
He was the faithful Wangbo. Wangbo, whose attention towards his master never ceased even while sleeping, immediately rushed to this room upon hearing his master's call. Towards Wangbo, the old Viscount pleaded his plight like a child.
"Wangbo-ya. I want to sleep a little. But this— this— this person is bothersome, so I cannot sleep. He says I'm senile. Please, just let me sleep."
Wangbo bowed his waist. Then, approaching the old Viscount's bed and adjusting the Viscount's blanket once, he slowly turned his eyes to Tusik.

Tusik withdrew from his father's room.
Returning to his own bedroom, Tusik threw his large body onto the bed. Then, with his legs on the floor and his upper body lying on the bed, he lit a cigarette.
Awakened from a heavy night's sleep, and having witnessed consecutive bizarre events after waking, the present moment felt like a dream to Tusik.
He woke to the sound of a pistol shot. Rushing to the detached villa (별당), he found his father unconscious and the electric light shattered. Bullets were embedded here and there. He brought his father to the Western-style house (양관). From his father, he only heard an incomprehensible explanation. When he doubted it, his father became angry.
—Who on earth was Kim Tŏksam? Who is Kim Tŏksam's son?
Earlier, when the name Kim Tŏksam came from his father's lips, his father's face looked extremely anxious. Clearly, his father seemed to know the name Kim Tŏksam. And judging by his father's anxious expression then, it seemed beyond doubt that there was some strange connection between his father and Kim Tŏksam. His father, who had never known fear in his nearly 70 years of life, clearly lowered his voice fearfully when mentioning the name Kim Tŏksam. <87>
Then who on earth is Kim Tŏksam? What connection exists between Kim Tŏksam and his father?
"Right. Let's ask Wangbo."
Wangbo, who had served the old Viscount for 50 years, knew everything concerning the old Viscount. If his father knew the name Kim Tŏksam, Wangbo probably knew it too. And if he knew the background of Kim Tŏksam, it seemed some glimmer of light might appear regarding the strange events of tonight.
At first, Tusik considered pressing the bell to call a servant to summon Wangbo to this room. But, thinking again, he got up himself. Although Wangbo was a servant of this household, he was directly attached to his father, and it seemed somewhat inappropriate for Tusik to have a servant fetch him. So, he decided to go out and meet Wangbo himself.
Tossing the nearly finished cigarette into the ashtray, Tusik stepped out into the corridor and saw the old Wangbo standing guard, bent at the waist, in front of his father's room yonder. Tusik slowly walked towards him. And addressing Wangbo, who bowed even lower upon seeing him, he asked why he wasn't sleeping.
Wangbo did not answer. After bowing once more, he merely shook his head slowly.
"I'll call another young servant to stand guard, so how about you go in and sleep for a bit?"
Wangbo looked up at his young master's face. Then he shook his head again.
"You're working hard in your old age. In any case, couldn't you come to my room just for a moment? There's something I'd like to ask about tonight's events..."
Tusik spoke again in a low voice. In response to these words as well, Wangbo slowly turned his head, glanced once at the door of the old Viscount's bedroom, and then just shook his head again.
Towards this stubborn and utterly loyal Wangbo, Tusik had no choice but to use other means.
Tusik went downstairs, woke another young servant, and came back up.
"Entrust this spot to this person for just 15 minutes and come to my room. Come now, look here. Wangbo, while you're in my room, you must not leave this spot for even a moment. You mustn't even glance away briefly. Now, Wangbo. Rest assured and come to my room for just a moment. I have something to ask you."

Tusik brought Wangbo to his bedroom. Then, forcefully offering a chair to the vehemently refusing Wangbo, he sat down facing him.
"Wangbo. What do you make of tonight's incident?"
At these words, Wangbo raised his old eyes and stared intently at his young master, then said,
"I cannot understand it, sir. It feels like a dream," and dropped his gaze again.
"It is shameful for me to ask you about my father's affairs which I myself do not know, but do you have any inkling about tonight's events?"
"I do not know, sir."
"Might you know someone named Kim Tŏksam?"
Wangbo raised his eyes and looked at Tusik again for a moment. Then, turning his eyes downward again, after a long pause, he asked,
"Which Kim Tŏksam might that be?"
"I don't know which Kim Tŏksam either, but might there be a Kim Tŏksam who holds some grudge against my father?"
"—There is, sir. However,"
"However?"
"Yes. That Kim Tŏksam is dead. And I do not know of any other Kim Tŏksam." <88>
"When did that Kim Tŏksam die?"
"Thirty— twenty-nine— exactly 30 years ago he died. Does that Kim Tŏksam have any connection to this incident?"
"How old would Kim Tŏksam's son be this year?"
"Kim Tŏksam had no heir."
Tusik looked at Wangbo. Wangbo sat quietly, his eyes cast deeply downward.
"Tŏksam had no heir?"
"No, sir. He did not."
"Are you absolutely sure?"
"I know well, sir. He did not have one."
"Even so—"
Tusik opened his eyes wide and spoke distinctly, word by word.—
"The fellow who came last night claims to be Tŏksam's son."
"Sir? Who says so?"
"His Excellency (대감)."
Wangbo's eyes, still lowered, shot open. His startled eyes met Tusik's and seemed to fall again in embarrassment. But in that instant, Tusik saw unspeakable terror flare up in Wangbo's eyes as well. Even his hands resting on his knees trembled slightly.
"He had none, sir. Tŏksam had no heir."
"Even so—"
"He had none, sir. Definitely none."
Tusik sensed that some bizarre and terrifying fact was hidden here. Connecting his father's terror, something he had never known before, with the terror now shown by Wangbo, it was clear that some strange fact lay hidden behind this incident.
"Look here. Listen to me. The strange man who came earlier clearly told my father himself that he is Kim Tŏksam's son, so why do you declare so definitively that Tŏksam had no heir?"
"Tŏksam never married before he died."
"Hmm. Still, even if he didn't marry, there's no rule saying he couldn't have had a child, is there? Isn't that right?"
"There was no place he was affectionate towards either."
"No place he was affectionate towards?"
"Yes. He was fond of one *kisaeng*—"
"And so?"
"That *kisaeng*—"
"Not 'that *kisaeng*-nim', just 'that *kisaeng*', right?"
"Yes. That *kisaeng*-nim—"
"*Kisaeng*."
"Yes."
"And so?"
Wangbo did not answer. After sitting silently for a while, when he opened his mouth, words unexpected by Tusik came out.—
"Master. Please kill me."
"Hey, Wangbo. Pull yourself together. For some reason, you're also extremely agitated. Collect yourself. —What on earth happened to that *kisaeng*?"
"..."
"Hmm?"
"That person could not have descendants."
"Ah. So, Tŏksam died a bachelor, the only place he was fond of was a *kisaeng*, but he couldn't have children from that *kisaeng* either, is that right?"
"Yes." <89>
"Then what about the fellow who came earlier?"
"I cannot know, sir."
"How exactly did Tŏksam die?"
"..."
"Hmm?"
"..."
"Hmm?"
Tusik asked three times in succession. But Wangbo kept his mouth firmly shut on that matter and did not answer.
"Wangbo."
"Yes, sir."
"Why don't you answer?"
"..."
"What do you think of this house?"
"I could never forget this family's kindness even in death."
"You know that an unusual, strange incident occurred in this house earlier, right?"
"Yes..."
"I am asking you now to investigate it, and if it is a dangerous matter, to save this house from that danger— yet why do you remain silent despite knowing?"
"I will tell you, sir."
"Why did Tŏksam die?"
"He died from a beating."
"When and where?"
"When His Excellency was serving as the Governor of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, at the provincial office (영문) in Kongju..."
Tusik finally began to understand.
"Ah. So, when Father was serving as the Governor of Ch'ungch'ŏng, he had Tŏksam punished, you mean?"
"Yes, sir."
"For what crime?"
"..."
"Hmm?"
"For the crime of smoking tobacco in the provincial office grounds..."
"For the crime of smoking tobacco in the provincial office grounds?"
"Yes. He died by beating for that crime."
"Look here. Don't hold back your words. If he died *for that crime*, what else is there to say?"
Wangbo quietly closed his eyes. He thought for a long while with his eyes closed, then opened them.—
"I will confess my transgression."
"Go on."
"Tŏksam was a camp official (영리). Tŏksam was close with a certain *kisaeng*. Therefore, His Excellency always looked upon him unfavorably, and then he was caught smoking tobacco standing in the provincial office grounds, which is why he died."
"Ah. I see. So, you mean that *kisaeng* was one favored by my father?"
Wangbo lowered his head, indicating affirmation.
"What happened to that *kisaeng*?"
"She died."
"When?"
"..."
"Hmm?"
"I cannot know, sir."
"You cannot know? If you cannot know, do you know how she died?"
"..."
"Speak clearly. Is she dead or alive?"
"—I cannot know, sir."
"You said she died."
"I cannot know, sir."
Towards Wangbo who stubbornly insisted he didn't know, Tusik lost <90> the energy to question further and lowered his own head.
After thinking for a while, he spoke to Wangbo.—
"Is there anything else you know regarding Tŏksam?"
"No, sir."
"Nothing more to say?"
"No, sir."
"But isn't it strange? You say Tŏksam definitely had no son, yet the person who came today claimed himself to be Tŏksam's son. How would you interpret this fact?"
Wangbo remained submerged in thought for a long while with his eyes closed. After some time, he barely opened his eyes. And he moved his lips slightly as if to say something. But he sealed his lips again.
"Well?"
"Still, I do not know, sir."
"You don't know?"
"No, sir..."
"Is there no one who would know?"
"..."
"..."
"No one?"
"I do not know, sir."
Wangbo seemed to know a bit more about the incident.
But for some reason, he wouldn't open his mouth further. Tusik had no choice but to let Wangbo go.
"Thank you for your trouble. You may leave."
Released by the young master, Wangbo let out a quiet sigh and slowly shuffled towards the door. But upon reaching the door, he turned back.
"Master."
"Yes?"
"For some reason, I am frightened, sir."
"What?"
"Tŏksam definitely died without an heir back then. But who on earth was that who came earlier? When I think about it, I am frightened, sir."
"Nonsense—"
Although he dismissed Wangbo's words with a single utterance, a chill ran down Tusik's own spine.
"Besides, I don't remember clearly, but the day Tŏksam died was also a dark autumn night like today. Perhaps it was even this very day, 30 years ago. Thinking about that, I'm too scared to bear it."
"Still afraid at 70? Don't be like that. Go back, tell Okkil to watch His Excellency's room, and get some sleep."
Thus, Tusik sent Wangbo away.
However, from the moment he sent Wangbo away, a feeling that could only be described as fear began to arise and grow within Tusik's own heart. Reflecting on Wangbo's story again and comparing it with his father's words, the strangeness of the situation gradually weighed heavily on Tusik's mind.
Wangbo insists Tŏksam definitely died without an heir. Then who indeed was the figure who came earlier? A ghost?
Tusik, raised with a modern education, was too scientific a person to accept the intruder as a ghost. Then Tŏksam's son?
But when Wangbo emphasized so strongly that he died without issue, there must have been grounds for it. Then an ordinary person?
To interpret it that way, what his father experienced earlier was too bizarre. —The paper sliding door opened without a sound. Clearly, no one entered through the gap. Moreover, although his father fired all twelve rounds of his revolver, not a single shot hit. This was too strange to be <91> explained as an ordinary person. Furthermore, the intruder's voice was said to be a strange sound, as if resonating from some machine.
Thinking this way, although he considered it mere delusion and superstition, Tusik simply could not suppress the terrifying thought gradually growing in a corner of his mind.
Tusik looked at his watch. It was half-past four.
"Still two hours."
There were still two hours left until dawn. In his current agitated state, sleep seemed utterly impossible. And with his current terrifying state of mind, two hours felt terribly long. Tusik wanted to spend those two hours reading some interesting book, completely forgetting the earlier incident. Tusik got up, intending to fetch a book. And he reached the door to go to the study.
It was just as he reached the door and was about to grasp the doorknob. Before Tusik's hand could touch the doorknob, the doorknob itself moved slightly.
A chill ran through Tusik's entire body. His vision even dimmed. His moving body froze rigidly to the spot as if nailed down. While this happened, the doorknob slowly, slowly turned, and the door began to open silently, bit by bit. Tusik's arms and legs trembled uncontrollably. All his blood rushed to his face. Amidst this, while Tusik mentally measured the distance between where he stood now and the head of the bed (where the pistol was), the slightly opening door swung wide open. Simultaneously, right opposite Tusik, stood a young man, face to face.
"Excuse me. I am this person."
Closing the door again with his left hand, holding a name card in his right, he bowed slightly to Tusik, who was almost losing consciousness due to excessive shock.
Before him, Tusik could only stand blankly. —(To be continued)— <92>

**Summary**

This installment follows Tusik as he tries to understand the mysterious events of the night: gunshots, his father (the old Viscount) collapsing, and the mention of an intruder claiming to be the son of Kim Tŏksam. Tusik questions the loyal family servant, Wangbo, who reveals that Kim Tŏksam was an official executed 30 years prior by the Viscount (then a provincial governor) allegedly for smoking in the governor's compound, but hints the real reason involved a *kisaeng* both men were involved with. Wangbo insists Kim Tŏksam died childless, deepening the mystery and fear. The episode ends cliffhanger-style as Tusik, terrified by the inexplicable events and Wangbo's fear, is confronted in his room by a young man presenting a name card. From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the lingering power and potential ruthlessness of the former aristocracy/officialdom (Viscount/Governor) in the colonial period, the role of personal vendettas, the precarious position of figures like *kisaeng*, and the clash between traditional beliefs/fears and modern elements (pistols, Western house, rational thought vs. superstition). The execution for a seemingly minor offense hints at the arbitrary nature of power wielded by officials, potentially reflecting late Joseon or early colonial realities.

적막한 저녁
						金東仁
그러나 한순간 뒤에 노자작의 노염에 불붓는 눈은 휙 돌아와서 아들의 얼골에 정면으로 부어젓다.
「네게는- 네게는-」
노염으로 말미암어 노자작의 숨은 허덕엿다―
「네게는 아비가 그러틋 노쇠해 뵈드냐!」
일즉이 호랑이가튼 재상으로서 선정(善政)에 학정에 가치 그 이름을 울리든 노자작의 면목은 여기서 나타낫다. 얼골은 누러케 여웻지만 거기서 울려 나오는 음성은 방을 드렁드렁 울리웟다.
다시 흥분해 가는 아버지의 아페 두식이가 어쩔줄을 모르고 창황하여 할때에 아버지는 다시 고함처서 저편ㅅ방에 잇는 충복 왕보를 불넛다.
「야. 왕보야- 왕보야-」
츙실한 왕보엿다. 비록 잘 때라도 주인에게 대한 주이는 끈치치안코 잇든 왕보는 주인의 부름에 곳 이방으로 달려왓다. 그 왕보에게 향하여 노자작은 마치 어린이가치 자긔의 처지를 호소하엿다.
「왕보야. 나는 좀 자고십구나. 그런데 이- 이- 이 사람이 구찬케 굴어서 잘 수가 업다. 날더서 노쇠햇다는구나. 날 제발 좀 자게 해다구」
왕보는 허리를 굽혓다. 그러고 노자작의 침대에 각가히 가서 한번 자작의 이불을 고처 드린 뒤에 천천히 눈을 두식이에게 돌렷다.

두식이는 아버지의 방에서 물러나왓다.
자긔의 침실로 돌아온 두식이는 몸을 커다라케 침대에 내여 던젓다. 그러고 다리는 마루위에 상반신은 침대우에 눕힌 뒤에 권연을 부처물엇다.
밤의 곤한 잠에서 깨여나고 깨여난 뒤에 련하여 긔괴한 일을 본 두식이에게는 지금이 마치 꿈과 가텃다.
단총소리에 깨엿다. 별당으로 달려가보니 아버지는 긔절하고 전등은 부서젓다. 탄환이 여기저기 백엿다. 아버지를 양관으로 모서왓다. 아버지에게서는 역시 리해할 수 업는 설명을 들을 뿐이엇다. 그것을 의심하매 아버지는 성을 내엿다.
―대체 김덕삼이가 누구든가. 김덕삼의 아들이 누구든가.
아까 아버지의 입에서 김덕삼이라는 이름이 나올 때에 아버지의 얼골은 몹시 불안한 듯 하엿다. 분명히 아버지는 그 김덕삼이란 이름을 아는 모양이엇다. 그러고 그때의 아버지의 불안한 표정으로 아버지와 김덕삼의 새에 무슨 긔괴한 인연이 잇든 것도 의심할 여지가 업시 보엿다. 70년에 가까운 생애를 아직 두려움을 모르든 아버지도 김덕삼의 이름을 말할 때는＜87＞ 분명히 두려운 듯이 목소리를 나추엇다.

「아직 두시간」
밝기까지는 아직도 두시간이나 더 잇서야 할 것이다. 지금의 흥분상태로서는 도저히 잠이 들 뜻도 안하엿다. 그러고 또한 지금의 무시무시한 마음쌍으로는 두시간이 몹시 길ㅅ것 갓치 생각되엿다. 그 두시간ㅅ동안을 두식이는 무슨 재미잇는 책이라도 닑으면서 아까의 그 사건을 온전히 닛고 지나고 시펏다. 두식이는 무슨 책이라도 한 권 가저오량으로 몸을 이르켯다. 그려고 서재로 가려고 문에까지 이르럿다.
그가 문아페서 바야흐로 문을 열으러고 손잡이를 잡으러 할 때엿다. 두식이의 손이 채 손잡이에 가 닷기전에 손잡이가 스스로 조금 음지겻다.
두식이의 왼몸에는 소름이 쪽 끼첫다. 눈까지 아득하여젓다. 음지기러든 몸을 못박힌 듯이 그곳에 딱 부텃다. 그럴 동안에 손잡이는 차차 차차 돌고 문이 소리업시 조금씩 열니기 시작하엿다. 두식이의 팔다리가 우들우들 떨렷다. 피는 모도 얼골로 모혀들엇다. 이러한 가운데서 두식이가 자긔의 지금 서잇는 곳과(단총이 잇는) 침대ㅅ머리와 거리를 머리로 측량을 할동안 조금식 열리든 문이 홱 열렷다. 동시에 두식이의 마즌편에는 웬 청년하나이 딱마조 버티고 섯다.
「실례함니다. 이런 사람이외다」
외인손으로는 문을 도로 다드며 오른손으로는 명함을 쥐고 과도한 경악때문에 거이 정신을 일흘 듯이 된 두식이에게 그는 약간 머리를 수겻다.
그 아페 두식이는 망연히 서 잇슬 뿐이엇다. -(게속)- ＜92＞
