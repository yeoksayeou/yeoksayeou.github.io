Dr. Ch'oe's Conscience
						Yi Chong-myŏng
I
It was already half past eleven at night when Dr. Ch'oe returned after finishing a house call. Having unwittingly fallen asleep to the sensation of the rickshaw swaying while exposed to the cool night air, he was startled awake just as the rickshaw arrived before the hospital gate.

"There's another patient waiting now."

The night-duty nurse who came out to meet him took his bag and spoke thus.

"Another patient?"

Dr. Ch'oe frowned for a moment. As a stern scholar and someone who took pride in his profession as a kind of benevolent art (仁術), Dr. Ch'oe truly believed his life was one of diligence and thrift, a sacrifice that disregarded his own well-being.

However, being only human, hearing that another patient was waiting just after returning from a house call that had roused him from the cusp of a weary first sleep naturally made him frown.

But he immediately smoothed his brow and, feigning nonchalance, followed the nurse back to his room.

First, he took off his Western-style jacket and changed into his examination gown, then took out a cigarette from his pocket and slowly lit it.

"Is the patient someone who comes regularly?"

"No, it seems like their first time."

"A man?"

"It's a young woman."

"Hmm–" he said, blowing the cigarette smoke he held in his mouth towards the ceiling, then asked,
"What illness is it?"

"Well, I can't tell for sure. When I asked, she said she wanted to speak directly to you, Doctor. She seems to be acting very strangely."

The nurse said this, looking up at Dr. Ch'oe with eyes full of sleepiness, yet still managing a faint smile.

He stubbed out the cigarette butt in the ashtray, adjusted his clothes, and then, followed by the nurse, opened the door to the examination room where the patient was waiting.

There, a young woman who looked to be around twenty-three or twenty-four, who seemed to have been deep in thought with her head bowed in the chair, suddenly stood up startled at the sound of the door opening. She was a woman with an extremely pale face. Moreover, the clothes draped around her neck seemed to explain that her life was by no means comfortable.

Waiting for Dr. Ch'oe to sit in the examination chair (交椅), the woman opened her mouth with extremely hesitant<96> words.
"Um– are you the doctor!"

"Yes–" he replied, thinking it would be strange to say, "Yes, I am the doctor."
"I am the physician," he said, a slight smile playing on his lips as even he found his own words amusing. Hearing this, the woman looked back and forth between Dr. Ch'oe's face and the nurse standing beside him, her expression a complex mix of seeming relief and extreme bewilderment. Then she silently lowered her head again.

Based on his many years of experience as a doctor, he already had a general idea of the demeanor of young women who specifically chose a quiet time to come to the hospital for an examination of an illness they found difficult to speak about.

However, this particular woman tonight somehow seemed different from an ordinary patient. Recalling the nurse's smiling comment earlier, "She seems to be acting very strangely," he thought, indeed, this was not the kind of patient he had anticipated.

Thus, concealing his curiosity and maintaining the composed dignity befitting a cool-headed doctor, he opened his mouth.
"Where are you feeling unwell!"

Hearing this, the woman finally raised her head and looked up at the doctor's face again with pleading eyes. Then, shifting her gaze back to the nurse, she seemed extremely hesitant, as if unsure herself what she should do.

He already surmised why she was hesitating. He gave a subtle glance to the nurse standing respectfully behind him, and the quick-witted nurse, already understanding, silently exited the examination room.

After the nurse left, only he and the woman remained sitting opposite each other in the spacious examination room. Looking at the woman's appearance once more under the brilliant fifty-candlepower electric light, her unkempt hair and unironed, wrinkled clothes looked exceedingly shabby. Moreover, her socks revealing her big toe and her rough fingertips seemed to explain that her profession was not a very refined one.

"Now– please speak. Where are you feeling unwell–"

Waiting for the nurse to leave, Dr. Ch'oe turned his face towards the woman again and asked this. However, the woman, still with a fearful and anxious expression, stammered,
"Well– just a little–"

"There's no need to feel awkward about speaking. Everyone has ailments... Now, please sit a little closer here..."

"Well... my insides are just terribly..."

"Yes. How are they terribly painful!"

He inwardly thought there was probably no need to be concerned about the nurse's presence for such a matter, but still, his hand mechanically reached for the stethoscope lying on the desk.

"Please loosen the front of your chest. And please describe your symptoms in detail, how you are feeling pain."

He pulled his chair closer to the woman and placed the stethoscope in his ears. However, the woman, seeming startled that the doctor was turning to examine her, moved her chair back a step.

"Um, it doesn't hurt that much... I was thinking maybe I should try taking some me- medicine–"

The woman's words were still incomprehensible. Dr. Ch'oe removed the stethoscope he was about to use, placed it back on the desk, and looked again at this baffling patient. He thought, surely she couldn't be a madwoman, yet he still couldn't fathom what her behavior signified. <97>
"Even if you take medicine, I need to know the illness to give it to you– The hospital doesn't just sell medicine like that."

"Yes. I know that too, but–"

"Then what exactly is bothering you and how?"

"Well–" the woman began, then hesitated again, looking at the doctor's face.
"I am currently carrying a child."

"Ah, so you are pregnant. How many months along are you?"

"It's the fourth month now–"

"Fourth month!"

"Yes."

"And you're saying your insides feel uncomfortable?"

"Well, not really like that..."

"Then have you experienced some shocking event that might have caused a disturbance of the fetus (動胎)?"

Dr. Ch'oe, finding it frustrating to wait for this hesitant woman's answer, pressed her with questions. But the woman lowered her head again, silently looking down at her feet without replying. Her two hands, placed neatly on her lap, trembled convulsively, as if expressing the intense emotion (激情) stirring within her heart. Then, as if steeling herself, the woman abruptly lifted her head.

"Actually, the reason I came to see you tonight, Doctor, is because I intend to get rid of the child."

"Get rid of the child?"

Even Dr. Ch'oe couldn't help but be surprised by this.
"Meaning you want an abortion (락태), is that right?"

"Yes."

"Ho–"

Ch'oe leaned back in his chair and stared at the woman's face, dumbfounded. He found the ignorance of this woman, coming to him, a private practitioner (開業醫) she had never met before, in the dead of night asking out of the blue for an abortion, more baffling than amusing.

He finally connected this with her extreme reluctance about the nurse's presence and her difficulty in speaking.

This wasn't the first time Dr. Ch'oe had encountered such an experience as a doctor. Each time, he had sternly scolded those immature women (Dr. Ch'oe interpreted it as immature behavior) and sent them away. This was because he considered it the most righteous act dictated by his conscience (량심) as both a doctor and a human being. Therefore, the words he used to reason with this type of woman were truly stern, like a parent scolding a child or a teacher reprimanding a student, elaborately arguing right and wrong. However, this woman tonight seemed somewhat different from the previous ones. Compared to the daughters of wealthy families who got pregnant through secret affairs unknown to their parents, or those women who, despite lacking nothing in their household, sought abortions simply because they found raising a child bothersome, there seemed to be something more profound hidden behind this woman's reason for wanting an abortion.

Moreover, looking at her huddled small at the edge of the chair, seemingly frightened by her own words after having spoken them, he somehow felt it would be remiss to simply refuse her outright. Perhaps a pitiable tragedy lay hidden behind this woman's desire for an abortion. If so, while he couldn't necessarily fulfill her request, perhaps trying to help her towards happiness as much as his power allowed would also be fulfilling the mission of benevolent art inherent in his profession, he thought.

Therefore, Dr. Ch'oe opened his mouth with a compassionate expression.
"Where is your home?" <98>

The woman, seemingly surprised that the doctor who had been so shocked by her words moments ago was now asking gently, stared back at Dr. Ch'oe's face for a long moment, then replied,
"My home is right here in front, in XX-dong."

"And where are you currently employed?"

"Yes, I work at the XX Printing House."

"At a printing house!"

To a scholar like Dr. Ch'oe, a woman working at a printing house was new knowledge. So he asked,
"What do you do there?"

"I'm in the bookbinding (製本) department."

"Bookbinding, you mean binding books? Do women do that too?" he asked, showing brief surprise.

"Do you have a husband?"

He wasn't unaware that asking such a question to a pregnant woman was rude, but the woman didn't seem particularly offended.

"Yes."

"Are you living together now?"

"Yes."

"Then why are you thinking such thoughts?"

"But he's disabled, unable to use one arm."

"However, the fact that your husband is disabled surely isn't a reason for an abortion, is it?"

The woman paused briefly, averted her gaze from the doctor, and lowered her head. After a while, she lifted her head again, her large eyes blinking.

"But if I give birth to the child, I'll be kicked out of the printing house. If that happens, the five members of my household will all at once..."

The woman trailed off without finishing her sentence, lowering her head again. Five members all at once... what would happen to them? Dr. Ch'oe found himself unwittingly drawn into the woman's story.

"In any case, please tell me in detail the reason why you intend to get rid of the child."

"Well, that's not difficult, but if you hear the story, will you give me the medicine then?"

"Well, it's difficult to promise I'll give it to you right now–" he said, a slight, wry smile appearing on his lips as he found the woman's extreme naivety perplexing.
"Anyway, please tell your story," he said.

The woman, perhaps thinking the doctor's response offered somewhat more hope than she had expected, suddenly gained strength and began the following story.

In the woman's house, there lived a total of five family members: her elderly mother-in-law, nearly seventy; her husband; two young children; and herself. Since her husband became disabled, they had been barely sustaining themselves solely on her income.

Before her husband became disabled, although their life was poor, they had never skipped meals. But three years ago, one day while her husband was working at the XX Printing House, he made a mistake and got his right arm caught in a belt (皮帶), tearing it off from the shoulder blade area, rendering him disabled (廢人). Since then, their household situation had become truly destitute. At that time, the printing house, under the guise of medical expenses (치료비), gave them a sum of fifty Won – acting as if they were doing them a huge favor – and afterwards, never showed any sign of looking back or caring. Fifty Won as the price (갑) for the arm of a grown man who supported a family of five was far too cheap (헐하엿다). However, they coldly refused further discussion, saying the arm breaking was his own mistake, not their fault, and telling them to be grateful for the fifty Won.

"Honestly," the woman paused here in her story and let out a sigh, "what difference would it make if we received fifty Won or five hundred Won? Would that equal even one of my husband's arms?" – To be continued in the next issue – <99>

**Summary**

Dr. Ch'oe, a doctor committed to the "benevolent art" of medicine, encounters a poor, desperate young woman late one night who requests an abortion. Initially shocked and inclined to refuse based on his principles, he listens to her story. She reveals her husband lost his arm in an industrial accident at a printing press, receiving only minimal compensation (50 Won). She now supports their family of five (including his elderly mother and two children) by working in the same printing press's bookbinding department. She fears losing her job if her pregnancy becomes known, which would lead to starvation for the entire family. The story ends as Dr. Ch'oe listens, contemplating her plight. From a historical perspective, this story vividly portrays the harsh realities of urban poverty and lack of worker protection in 1930s colonial Korea. It highlights the devastating impact of industrial accidents, the inadequacy of compensation, the precariousness of female labor, and the extreme choices forced upon individuals by economic hardship.

최박사의 良心
						李鍾鳴
一
최박사가 왕진을 마치고 도라온 것은 벌서 밤 열한시가 치고 다시 삼십분이나 지난 후엿다. 서늘한 야긔를 쏘이며 차체가 흔들니는 촉감에 부지중 잠이 드럿다가 깜작놀나 깨인 때는 벌서 인력거가 병원문압헤 다다럿슬 때엿다.
「지금 또 환자가 와서 기다리고 잇슴니다」
마중을 나온 숙직 간호부가 그에게서 가방을 밧어들며 이러케 입을 여럿다,
「또 환자가?」
하며 최박사는 잠깐 눈쌀을 집흐렷다. 근엄한 학구(學究)로서 또는 자긔자신의 직업을 일종 인술(仁術)로서 자부하는 점에 잇서서 실로히 최박사의 생활은 근검 그것이요, 자긔몸을 도라보지안는 희생 그것이라고 밋고잇는 그이엿다.
그러나 그도 사람인 이상 고단한 첫잠이 어림풋이 든 때에 깨워가지고 왕진을 갓다오자 또 환자가 와서 기다리고 잇다고 하는 소리를 드르니 자연히 눈쌀이 집흐려지지 안니할 수 업섯다.
그러나 그는 즉시 눈쌀을 펴고 아모럿치 안흔 듯이 간호부를 따라 자긔의 방으로 도라왓다.
우선 양복저고리를 벗고 진찰복으로 가러입은 후 주머니에서 담배를 하나 끄내서 천천히 피여물엇다.
「환자는 게속해 다니는 사람인가?」
「아니요, 처음 온 모양에요」
「남자?」
「녀자얘요」
「음-」하며 그는 입에 물엇든 담배연긔를 천장에다 뿜엇다 하드니
「무슨 병이래?」
「글세, 자세히 알 수 업서요. 제가 물어보앗드니 선생님을 직접 맛나서 말슴하겟다고 그래는대 퍽 행동이 이상해보여요」
간호부는 이러케 말하며 졸님이 가득한 두눈으로 최박사를 치어다보드니 그래도 우슴을 처보핫다.
그는 담배꽁지를 잿터리 속에 집어너코 의복을 바로 잡은 후 간호부를 다리고 환자가 기다리고 잇는 진찰실의 문을 여럿다.
그 곳에는 이십삼사세 쯤 되여보이는 절문 녀자 한 명이 무엇을 깁히 생각하고 잇섯든 듯이 의자에 고개를 숙이고 안젓다가 문여는 소리에 비로소 놀난 듯이 벌떡 이러섯다, 몹시 얼골빗이 창백한 녀자이엿다. 그 우에 그녀자의 목에 걸치고 잇는 의복이, 그 녀자의 생활이 결코 넉넉지 못하다는 것을 설명하고 잇는 것 갓해보엿다.
최박사가 교의에 안기를 기다려 그 녀자는 몹시 주저하는＜96＞ 말씨로 입을 여럿다.
「저- 선생님이 심니까!」
「네-」 하며 설마 내가 선생님이요 할 수는 업는 터이다.
「내가 의사임니다」하고 자긔가 생각해도 그말이 우수운 듯이 입설에 잠낀 미소를 띄웟다. 녀자는 이 말을 듯더니 비로소 마음을 노흔 듯한, 또는 몹시 당혹하는 듯한 복잡한 표정으로 최박사의 얼골과 그 엽헤 서잇는 간호부의 얼골을 번가라 바라보앗다. 하드니 묵묵히 고개를 숙여 버리고 말엇다.
의사로서 다년간 지내온 그의 경험에 의할 것 가트면 절문녀자가 특히 조용한 시간을 가리여 병원에 와서 말하기 거북해하는 병을 진찰밧을적의 태도는 벌서 대강 짐작하고 잇는 터이엿다.
그러나 오날밤 지금 이녀자만은 엇전지 심상한 환자가 안인 것 가치 생각되엇다, 앗가 간호부가 눈우슴을 치며 「행동이 퍽 이상해보여요」하든 생각을 하고 아닌게 아니라 이것은 자긔가 예상하엿든 것과 가튼 환자는 아니로구나햇다.
그래서 그는 호긔심을 감추고 따는 랭정한 의사다웁게 위엄을 지어 입을 여럿다.

그는 벌서 그녀자가 주저하는 까닭이 무엇인지 짐작햇다. 등뒤에 대령하고 서잇는 간호부에게 감간 눈찟을 하엿드니 눈치빠른 간호부는 벌서 짐작하고 묵묵히 진찰실 박그로 나간다.
간호부가 나간 뒤 넓은 진찰실안에는 단지 그와 그녀자만이 마조안저 잇게되엿다, 오십촉 전등이 휘횡하게 빗나고 잇는 불밋헤서 다시 한번 그녀자의 모양을 바라보니 헛푸수수한 머리라든지 구김살이 펴지지안흔 의복이 넘어도 초라하게 보엿다, 더구나 엄지발구락이 나온 양말이라든지 거치른 손끗흘 바라볼 것 갓흐면 그 녀자의 직업이 그리 고상한 것 아니라는 것을 설명하는 듯 햇다.

「저- 좀-」

「압가슴을 좀 푸르십시요. 그리고 읏떠케 압흐신지 그 증상을 자세히 말슴하십시요」
그는 교의를 그 녀자 압흐로 닥어 끌며 청진긔를 두 귀에 꼬잣다, 그러나 그 녀자는 의사가 자긔를 진찰하랴고 도는 것을 보고 놀나운 듯이 교의를 한거름 뒤로 물녓다.

그 녀자의 말은 역시 무엇을 의미하는 것인지 대종할 수 업섯다. 최박사는 대이랴든 청진긔를 뽑아서 도로 책상 우에다 노흐며 이 알 수 업는 환자를 다시금 한번 바라보앗다. 설마 미친녀자는 아니겟지하고 생각햇지만 그래도 그 녀자의 행동은 무엇을 의미하는 것인지 짐작해 알수가 업섯다.＜97＞
「약을 잡순는다 하드래도 병을 알어야 약을 듸릴 터인데- 병원에서는 그대로 약을 팔지는 안흠니다」
「네. 저도 그런줄을 알지만-」
「그럼 대체 어듸가 엇떠케 불편하시다는 말슴임니가」

「저 실상 오늘밤에 선생님을 뵈러온 것은 어린애를 지워볼랴고 온 것임니다」
「어린애를 지랴고요?」

그는 비로소 그녀자가 간호부잇는 것을 몹시 끄리는 일을, 그리고 말하기 어려워하든 일을 련상해보앗다.
최박사는 의사로서 이러한 경험을 처음 당한 것은 아니엿다. 그럴적마닥 그는 그 철업는(최박사는 그것울 철업는 짓이라고 해석햇다) 녀자들을 준절하게 나물해서 돌려보냇다. 그것은 의사로서 또는 사람으로써 자긔의 량심이 식히는 가장 떳떳한 일이라고 생각햇든 까닭이다. 그래서 그가 이런 종류의 녀자들에게 타일느는 말은 부모가 자식을 나물하듯이 선생이 제자를 꾸짓듯이 실로히 준절한 것이고 시비곡진한 것이엿다. 그러나 오늘밤의 이 녀자만은 그전의 녀자들과는 조곰 달흔 곳이 잇는 듯 햇다. 부모 아지못하게 난봉을 피우다가 자식을 밴 돈잇는 집 처녀라든지 또는 살님에는 아모 부족함이 업건만 단지 어린 것을 기르기가 귀찬타는 생각에서 락태를 해보랴고 드는 그러한 녀자들과 비교해보면 읏전지 이 녀자가 략태하랴는 원인은 좀 더 생각한 무엇이 숨어잇슬 뜻 십헛다.
더구나 말을 해바린 후 그 녀자 자신도 자긔의 말에 스사로 겁낸 듯이 교의 끗헤 조고마케 쪼구리고 안저잇는 모양을 바라보랴니까 읏전지 그대로 거절해바리기만은 미안한 듯하게 생각되엿다. 이 녀자가 락태를 하랴고드는 그 반면에는 엇지면 가엽슨 비극이 숨어잇술는지도 알 수 업는 것이다. 만약 그러타고 하면 그 녀자가 요구하는 것까지는 알 수 업지만 하여간 자긔의 힘이 미치는 데까지는 그 녀자 행복을 위하야 도와주어보는 것도 자긔의 직업이 가진 바 인술의 사명을 다하는 것이 안인가 하고 생각햇다.
그래서 최박사는 자애스러운 낫빗으로 입을 여럿다.
