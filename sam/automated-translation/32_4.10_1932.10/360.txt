炭坑脫走記
						名古屋 韓聖俊
Mehoya
Mehoya! Mehoya!
It is the cry of the young coal miners (炭坑夫). I miss my mother. I long for my hometown. When will destiny arrive for me to escape this coal mine (炭坑) and see the bright world where those people live? When the sun sets, they look up at the sky and cry, "Mehoya, mehoya, save me." (Abbreviated - original text) Here, there isn't even a shadow of religion, education, morality, or art; now, they don't even know if such things exist or not. They just eat breakfast, grab a pickaxe (곡괭이), go to the copper mine (銅山) or coal mine (炭坑), fight with sweat all day long, eat when hungry, and return to sleep when tired. They have even become inhumane beings who simply turn their heads away when they see collapses or casualties (死傷者). —This is coal mine society.— The novice laborers (初人夫) and young laborers (어린人夫) call out "Mehoya" whenever the sun sets. When the seasoned laborers (묵은 人夫) hear this sound, they shout and curse. But sometimes, when they call out that sound in silence, the seasoned ones too, wordlessly, just dimly look up at the faces of those sitting stiffly like wooden statues (木神). —This is the coal miner (炭坑夫).
I drafted the first half of this manuscript (秒稿) in that coal mine pit, and the latter half and <74> revisions are being written 15-odd days after escaping and returning to Nagoya. The story begins 3 days before going to the coal mine, in the big city of Nagoya—

The night of July 9, 1932—I directed my steps toward Nagoya's sleepless city of pleasure (不夜城), "Hirokōji (廣小路)," searching for a job. Turning at the entrance of Honmachi Itchōme (本町一丁目), there is Sūninji Temple (崇仁寺) in the left corner. Next to it stood a communal tombstone (共同碑石), and inside that area, a single streetlamp (街樹燈) stood, and beneath it, gloomy two-story houses (家屋) stood side-by-side. Feeling tired, I first sat down on the stone steps (石 階段) in the temple's rear garden. The sounds of people enjoying themselves, the noise of streetcars, and faintly, the songs of cabaret girls (캬래껄) from a distant café drifted together. But this place was quiet enough that even a murder could happen unnoticed. While trying to pass by the two-story house, chased by prostitutes (淫賣女), I saw an advertising tower. "Travelers and Craftsmen (旅者職人) Urgently Wanted." I stopped. I returned to the stone steps and thought. Railroad construction, road repair, harbor laborer (築港人夫)—rather than suffering like this and wandering the city (都會), the convenience of a wandering life (放浪生活) seemed better, so I entered that house. It was the Nagoya Branch Office (名古屋 出張所) of the Kume Copper Mine (久米銅山), which the police knew about but overlooked. I first realized this in the train on the way to the copper mine.

Thus, after waiting about two hours on the second floor, I went with the house clerk to Ōzone (大曾根). It was a gloomy workers' camp (飯場, hanba). About 20 men wearing happi coats (합비) were lying, sitting, and standing around. The clerk told me to ask the supervisor and left. The supervisor gestured for me to come up. His actions and expression were utterly cold. And so, I became one of those 20 men. Among them were boys like me. Four supervisors walked around restlessly. Feeling uneasy (氣味가 나뻐저), I just stared at the supervisor's face. The supervisor also looked at me for a while and asked, "Where are you from (何處だ)?" "Korea (朝鮮)." "Alone (一人か)?" I nodded. A laborer lying nearby suddenly sat up. "Are you Korean (朝鮮)?" Instantly (卒時), I felt glad. I went over to that laborer. After exchanging stories of our circumstances (身勢이약이), I asked this: "Where are we going, Senior Foreman (大關切)?" That person didn't know either. "Anyway, I heard we're going to Sendai (仙臺), but it doesn't seem like road construction. The camp master (飯場主人) also mentioned something about going to some fishing ground (魚場), but anyway, we'll know when we get there. Don't worry. Rather than suffering like that, isn't it much better to follow these people, whatever the work may be?"—After 12 o'clock, everyone formed a line and went out to Ōzone (大曾根) Station. It was a desolate station. We sat in a small waiting room. Supervisors stood at every door. There were also 6 or 7 family members who came to see them off. They talked from inside and outside the windows. They were all expressionless. A little later, the 24 of us boarded the train and left Nagoya.

Whether they had some lingering attachment to Nagoya, they looked out the windows and shouted. Supervisors stood at the front and rear doors. After passing two or three stations, it became quiet. Most people fell asleep. Rain was already falling outside the window. Even the person who looked up at the supervisor's face like a soldier hearing gunfire in the barracks (바락크) fell asleep, and the Korean person next to me was also asleep. Perhaps tired, the supervisor also sat down heavily on the chair. I observed the supervisor's attitude closely. "Supervisor! Where exactly are we going (監督さん! 私たちは 一體 何處へ 行くんかえ)?" "Doesn't matter, you'll find out later (何んでもいい後で分る)." At the cold reply, I turned my head away. I felt disappointed. But the train kept going. It was the express bound for Aomori (靑森). It ran like the wind (疾風) along the endlessly sorrowful railway line. Nothing could be heard but the rolling sound and the rain. Only the mournful whistle (汽笛) could be heard when passing small stations. Across the aisle, a young laborer was searching through his bundle. It was the package (物作) he received from his wife earlier at Ōzone (大曾根) Station. Inside were a yukata (俗衣), a shirt (사쓰), tabi socks (足下), caramels (캬라맬), etc. He took out the caramels to chew, saw me, and tossed me a pack. At Shizuoka (靜岡) Station, two more laborers were added. Through the gloomy forests (森林) late at night, heading toward the silent horizon, turning right towards the coastal beach (海濱) for 300 ri, towards the vast continent's dark blue horizon (地平線), the train hurriedly carried these people, ignorant of their destination. The rain still hadn't stopped. Everyone fell asleep. Across from me, by the window, a young boy who had been sitting and watching me intently also fell asleep.<75> Maybe everyone else was sleeping, but I was the only one with my eyes open. First, I took out the caramels I received from the laborer, chewed them, and looked outside. In the pitch-dark wilderness (曠野), only the tiny lights from the windows of distant villages (村落) were visible. An old man (老䊺) lying across from me, whom I thought was asleep, opened his eyes wide and asked me, "Do you know where we're going (行く處を知つとるか)?" "I don't know (知りません)." "It's Kume Copper Mine (久米銅山だ)." "Eh!" "A coal mine (炭坑だよ)." I was startled. And so, I stood up in panic (隍隍이). "It's too late. Since you're young, you'll probably go to the copper mine side (遲いぞ君は年が若いから銅山の方だらうよ)." The old man's second remark was faint. I ***** [went to?] the supervisor. "I'm getting off at the next station (次の驛で降りる)." "Idiot, if you make trouble, you'll get this (馬鹿野郞さわぐと此れだ)." Saying this, he pulled out a pistol (短銃) and aimed (견우웟다) it at me. I was short of breath (숨이 갑헛다). I clenched my teeth and returned to my seat. Unconsciously, tears flowed down both cheeks. "I'll ask the copper mine side for you, it'll be much easier for you there (銅山の方に賴んでやらう、そちはとんと樂たぜ)." Then the old man lay back down. The supervisor came over to my side. "If you make trouble at the next station, I'll shoot (次の驛でさわぐと打つぞ)." And then he went back. The old man added, "Be careful. In a place like this, killing one person is nothing (注意しな、此處な處は人間一匹Xすなか分けはないぞ大同然だ)." I cried my heart out. When the train stopped at the next station, I sat perfectly still like a wooden statue (木神). Until we got off at the destination station, I remained sitting just like that, like a wooden statue. And so, we arrived at our destination the next morning. The 27 of us were loaded onto 4 horse carts (馬車) and taken 30 ri into the mountains. The horse carts turned back, and we walked along the mountain path. Like the Israelites (이스라엘 민족) who had their *** [possessions?] taken by the Judeans (유데아) and were dragged in chains to the Aribba desert [likely meant Arabia], we too crossed this mountain path. In two lines, with supervisors in front and behind, each held a pistol (短銃) in their hand.

We are criminals. Why are we being dragged to prison (監獄)? Life—ah, life—I learned even more deeply here that it is more fearsome than anything. Its power is terrifying and immense. It made me leave my mother's embrace, which I so hated to leave, and brought tears to her eyes. It completely stole the freedom I firmly believed was mine alone, and turned my former bloody and vigorous spirit (意氣) into a rotten wooden statue (木神). Now, obedience to life is more pressing than obedience to the supervisor's abuse (虐待) and pistol (短銃). This side was far more terrifying (恐怖). They already knew they were going to a coal mine (炭坑), and they already knew how cruel (慘酷) a coal mine was. The notion arose in everyone that they didn't eat to live, but lived to eat. As we crossed this mountain (세상, 웨누리 - world, outside?), the cold wind awakened them and fiercely stimulated the terrifying notion of life's dread. When a powerless old man stumbled, he was kicked without hesitation. But without any resistance, he just dimly looked up at the supervisor's face. Seeing the supervisor's unchanged actions and expression, our curses (咀呪) and hatred (憎惡) intensified. On the other hand, seeing the old man rise without resistance, our feelings of sympathy (同情) and the strength of solidarity (단결) grew stronger. And so, when the sun rose overhead, we descended to our destination. We saw security guards (守衛兵/番兵) too. Already, the sounds of people and dynamite (다이나마잇트) were loud. We entered a workers' camp (飯場, hanba) the size of an elementary school classroom, took off our footwear/leggings (간발) and tabi socks (足下, 다비), and went to the stream (내(川)). We drank freely as much as we wanted. Exhausted, we slept soundly until evening. And at night, absorbed (全然 夢中) in the experienced tales of the seasoned laborers (묵은 人夫), we all went to bed after one o'clock. Unable to sleep, I got up again around two. I sat in the middle of the bodies lying stretched out horizontally and vertically. Moonlight streamed through the window, illuminating one corner of the room (房). In that corner, the young boy from before was also awake, sitting up and dimly looking at me. "Shall we go outside (外に出やうか)?" "It's dangerous (危い)." "Just around here (此の邊よ)." And together with that boy, I went outside.

The moon was out. The stars were out too. Their light shone brightly over the mountains. We crossed the stream (내물) in front of the camp yard (飯場 뜰압), even into the copper mine (銅山) and coal mine (炭坑) areas, and sat on a large rock. That boy's circumstances (身勢) were exactly the same as mine. I sympathized (同情) with him. He also sympathized with me. Without a word, we looked up at the sky for a long time. Tears flowed down the boy's cheeks. Unknowingly, the moonlight seemed dim to me too. "We're still young. Someday we'll get out of here and meet our mothers (俺たちは又若いんだ いつか 此處をぬけて 母さんに 逢へるだらうよ)." We promised to watch for a chance to escape (脫走) and, mustering our courage, returned to the camp (飯場). The next morning,<76> like wooden statues (木神), like mechanical men, we each held a pickaxe (곡괭이) and followed the supervisor to the copper mine (銅山). We swung the pickaxes as instructed, put them down, and started digging earth. This is our duty (義務). Their movements are identical. Everyone except the supervisor is expressionless. Wooden statues—machines—from 6 in the morning until 7 in the evening, leaving only 20 minutes for the daytime meal break (晝夜시간?), it's the same pattern.—Wooden statues—machines—And so, after seeing six sunsets (六面의 夕陽) at this copper mine, one rainy afternoon. Suddenly, a loud noise erupted, and laborers shouted. "Rockslide! Get out of the way! (岩崩れた, のけろ!)" Looking up, they scattered left and right. A large boulder rolled down among them. At that moment, a piece of rock flew off and hit the supervisor's right leg. "Serves you right! Divine punishment! (じやま見ろ! 天罰だ)" Everyone shouted. At that moment, the only ones who cried out "Ah!" in surprise were a few of the novice laborers (初人夫) who had come with us. No matter how much they hated the supervisor, seeing them lying there seriously injured (重傷), yet showing no sign of shock, instead shouting triumphantly (意氣揚揚), gave me chills. Ah—could even instinct (本能) be extinguished? I too had been beaten mercilessly by the supervisor without reason. On the very second day after arriving, when the boy Toshio (俊雄) and I decided to rest for a day and were lying in the camp (한바), the supervisor came back, roared like thunder, and beat us countless times with a club/bat (매스). The more we resisted, the worse it got.

Because of this, we couldn't go out to the workplace (任事場시고도바) until the day after that. Despite this, looking at the supervisor's suffering face, I still felt pity (치근한 생각). Returning to the camp (한바), I found him lying there with bandages wrapped around his leg and head, groaning. The others came in, pretended not to see him, untied their footwear (간발), and went off to the stream (내(川)). As I followed them down, "Give me a cup of water (水を 一杯くれ)." The boy Toshio (俊雄) pulled me along, "Let's go, leave him! (やらう, ほつとけ!)" As we were about to leave, a loud groan came out. Taking a big step away from the main path, I approached the supervisor's side, "Here, drink! (さ, 飮め!)" The supervisor turned his head, looked at me for a moment, then said, "Han (강, 韓), thank you (ありがどう)." I looked at the supervisor's face again for a while. Then I went outside. "Han (강, 韓), thank you (ありがどう)." Today was the first time he had ever called me by my name and said thank you sincerely. Unconsciously (不知不覺), I smiled (微笑).—But tonight, Toshio (俊雄) and I firmly promised to escape (脫走). The reason we had delayed until today was that on the second day after arriving, we saw the dead body (死屍) of another laborer who tried to escape and was struck down by a guard's (番兵) pistol (短銃). Also, I had received much sympathy (同情) from the Korean woman who cooked meals at the camp (飯場). Whenever the sun set, I would always go to her room and chat, telling stories about my hometown. It was my only pleasure (亨樂). She would always collect the dried scorched rice (마른 누른밥) that I enjoyed and secretly put it in my pocket. Also, she would grill red bean pancakes (팟전) for me, and even while being nagged numerous times by the Japanese woman, she would avoid her notice and grill them for me whenever she had a chance. "Auntie, please stop. Because of me, you're openly getting disapproved of by those people (公然히 그 사람들의게 눈치를 보힘니다)." Then the woman would say this: "Disapproval? What disapproval? This is also a joy (樂) for me. It would feel wrong to just sit here and see you return in the evening." I felt truly sorry. I was sincerely grateful. Why did she endure those people's glares to be kind (善心) to me? I had done nothing to deserve her gratitude, nor had I done anything worthy of receiving such kindness.—Actually, I had received kindness from her before. When I was beaten by the supervisor and lying in the camp (한바), she boiled medicine (藥) and gave it to me. At that time, all I said was, "Auntie, thank you (아저머니 감사함니다)." Just that one phrase. Was that word worthy of receiving such kindness in this reality?—Was it the longing for the hometown word "Auntie, thank you" that made her cherish me? Was it pity (哀꾸저) for the young body lying there after being beaten by the supervisor that made her feel sorry (측은히) for me? Was it hearing the sincere word "Auntie," after only hearing shouts and curses every day from those brutal laborers, that made her value me? I know well that the woman lacked none of these conditions. And (love for one's own people/同族愛) welled up from her heart. I had long starved for a mother's love.<77>
She too had long starved for a child's love. We tried to fill each other's emptiness (限). I wanted to call her Mother. She too surely wanted to call me son. —The realm of hungry ghosts (俄鬼道) of human affection and peace—Night after night, ah—the desolate nights, the nights of coal mines where melancholy (憂鬱) and devil-like silence continued, nights of terror where rotting living corpses (피썩는 산송장) gathered and stared at each other, the pale yellowish corpse-like faces (黃白色의 송장갓튼 얼골) collapsed from the day's fatigue, the ice-cold groans (차뒤찬 呻吟), the thunderous snores, the cries of "Mehoya" from young laborers weeping for their mothers and hometowns on the camp (한바) grounds and rocks by the stream—like staying up all night at a house in mourning (초상난 집)—I found it agonizing to hear and covered my ears with my hands, pitiful (哀꾸저) to see and closed my eyes. But then, not hearing and not seeing, the continuation of deep melancholy (沈沈한 憂鬱) became agonizing again, so I lowered my hands and opened my eyes again, countless times. Fatigue during the day was preferable to the melancholy of the night. After receiving sympathy from that woman, I gained more courage for the daytime labor and received pleasure from the nighttime silence. She was my strength and help.

The night of July 19th—Having made a firm promise with the boy Toshio (俊雄) to escape (脫走), I even received a pistol (短銃) from Ichirō (一郞) (also a Korean) and hid it under a rock by the stream (내(川)). "Wherever I go, I will never forget your kindness (은혜), Ichirō (一郞)." "Kindness? What kindness? If we're fated (인연), we'll meet again someday. Don't worry. If you get caught by the guards (番兵), shoot them and run. I'll take care of the aftermath (뒤-ㅅ監當)." Then, as Toshio and I were passing between the sleeping laborers, the sick supervisor opened his eyes. "Hey, Han! Where are you going so late at night? (オイ, 韓, 夜深けに 何處に 行くの)" "Just for a little walk (少し 散步さ)." "Be careful. You might unknowingly get hurt by the guards (氣を 付けたよ, 知らず 番兵にやられるからな)." I looked at the supervisor's face again for a while. Even though he beat me, I still felt sorry for him. Ichirō (一郞) also followed us out to the door. As we were about to pass the kitchen (정지), the door was open and the auntie was sitting there. "Aigoo, where are you going in the middle of the night?" "Uh—" Saying this, I looked back at Ichirō (一郞). He signaled with his eyes. I hated lying, especially to Auntie. Having no choice, I said, "We're going to play by the stream (내ㅅ(川)) for a bit." "Be careful." Leaving only those words behind, we left. "Ichirō (一郞), stay well." Toshio (俊雄) and I crawled up the mountain. Until we were out of sight, Ichirō (一郞) stood beside the camp (한바). "Toshio, wait (俊雄待つてくれ)." "What's wrong (どうした)?" "I'm going to say a word to that Auntie (あの 姉さんに 一言云つてくる)." "Fool, it's dangerous (ばかな危いぞ)." "No, just for a moment (いや少の間だ)." My conscience pricked me for lying to the woman who had shown me sincere affection. If I left now, I might never see her again, and leaving without even saying goodbye felt too heartless (沒人情). Leaving Toshio (俊雄) alone, I went down to the camp (한바) and peeked into the room. "Auntie, what I said earlier was a lie." "Why?" "Toshio and I are running away tonight." Her startled face gradually turned into a happy one. "Alright, go. You've thought well." "No matter my circumstances, I will never forget your kindness (은혜)." "If by chance you go to Korea (朝鮮), come look for me in Anjŏng-ŭp (安城邑). I might go there, who knows? Wait a moment." Then she went to the kitchen (정지), packed rice in a cloth bundle (책보), gave it to me, and said, "If you lose your way, it's easy to just keep going deeper into the mountains, so take this." I accepted the rice Auntie gave me. "Stay well (平安히 게십시요)." "Go safely (잘 가게)." She cried, and I cried too. And so, until I was out of sight, she stood beside the camp (한바). Climbing up the mountain, I looked back for a while. And so, Toshio and I crawled over the mountain. Seeing a guard (番兵), we crouched (움퍽어) and hid, sweating profusely several times. Naturally, the hand holding the pistol (短銃) and my legs trembled. "Let's just push through! (思ひ 切つて 越え-)" And we crossed over desperately (決死的). Ah—perhaps with heaven's help, we safely passed the guards' (番兵) cordon (警戒網). And then, yelling "Save me!", we ran straight ahead. We crossed several mountains. We passed through several forests (숩) and sandy areas (砂場). Still, no road was visible. Blood flowed from our feet. Short of breath, we rested briefly under rocks several times. The sound of birds singing could be faintly heard. The sound of roosters crowing could also be heard. Far away, the sound of a temple bell (절鍾) could also be heard. We headed towards the sound of the bell. And so, we found a road. We saw a village (洞內) too. We saw a farmer walking with a basket (광이) on his back.<78>
The east brightens. The sun has risen—Dawn—Ah—Dawn! "Toshio, we're saved! We can see the bright world! Let's live strongly! Let's work hard! (俊雄助かつな, 光明な 世界が 見られる, 强く 生きやう, 勵んで 働かう)" Then, eating the rice received from Auntie, we marched forward bravely (快勇히), shouting and singing like triumphant generals (凱旋將軍).—They too have human feelings (人情). If expressed, others follow suit. But it's lamentable (願痛) that they remain submerged only in brutality (暴惡), apart from expressing it. Ah—their dim eyes, their living-corpse-like movements and expressions, the supervisor's face looking at me upon first feeling human affection (人情), Ichirō (一郞) who saved me out of love for his own people (同族愛), the warm embrace of the magnanimous (넑으니 慈愛하신) Auntie, the dried scorched rice and red bean pancakes, the sound of the young laborers' tenacious cries of "Mehoya"—
As I marched, these thoughts kept surfacing in my head. And so, I stopped and looked back.

"Still have lingering feelings? (未練でもあるのか)"
"Yes (あるさ)."
"Fool, what lingering feelings could you have for such a living hell? (ばかな, あんな生地獄に 何んの 未練があるんだ)"

I gazed dimly at that mountain. "Ah—even in that mountain, which is a living hell (生地獄), I still had lingering feelings."
—Returning to Nagoya in August 1932— <53>

**Summary**

This harrowing memoir recounts the experience of Han Sŏngjun, a young Korean man in Nagoya, Japan, who, while searching for work in 1932, is deceptively recruited into forced labor at the Kume Copper Mine (likely encompassing coal mining as well). He describes the brutal, dehumanizing conditions, the constant surveillance by armed supervisors, the despair driving young miners to cry out "Mehoya," and the loss of humanity even among the laborers. Despite the "living hell," Han finds moments of connection: camaraderie with a fellow young worker named Toshio, critical help from a Korean laborer named Ichirō who provides a pistol, and profound maternal affection and practical aid from a Korean kitchen worker ("Auntie"). These connections fuel his and Toshio's desperate, successful escape through the mountains. The memoir highlights the extreme exploitation and cruelty faced by Korean laborers in Japan during the colonial period, portraying the mine as a place where human life is cheap and basic instincts are nearly extinguished. Historians would find valuable the firsthand depiction of forced labor recruitment tactics, the daily life and psychological toll within the mine camp (飯場), the explicit mention of violence (beatings, threat of shooting), the complex dynamics between laborers and supervisors (including a flicker of humanity in a supervisor), and the vital role of ethnic solidarity (同族愛) and small acts of kindness in maintaining hope and enabling resistance like escape.

메호야! 메호야!
어린 炭坑夫들의 웨치는 소리다. 어머니가 보고십다. 고향이 그리웁다, 언제나 이 炭坑을 버서나 저 사람들이 사는 光明한 세계를 볼 운명이 닥처올가 해만 지면 하날 치어다 보고 매호야 메호야 나를 구하라고 웨친다.(略-원문) 여기는 종교, 교육, 도덕, 예술이라고는 그림자도 업거니와 인제는 이러한 것이 잇는지 업는지도 모르고 잇다. 다만 아침밥 먹으면 「곡괭이」 들고 銅山과 炭坑으로 가서는 웬종일 땀과 더부러 싸호다가 배곱흐면 밥먹고 피곤하면 도라와 잠잘 뿐이다. 붕괴, 死傷者를 보면 그데로 머리를 돌여버리는 沒人情한 인간까지 되여 잇다.―이것이 炭坑사회다.―初人夫와 어린人夫들은 해만 지면 매호야를 부런다. 묵은 人夫들은 이 소리를 드르면 고암을 치며 辱을 한다. 그러나 어느때 침묵중에서 그 소리를 부를 때 문은 그들도 아모 말업시 木神갓치 우덕허니 안즌 그들 얼골만 히미하게 치어다 본다.―이것이 炭坑夫다.
나는 이 秒稿를 그 炭坑窟에서 前半을 秒하고 後半과＜74＞ 修正은 脫走하여 名古屋으로 도라와 15餘日後에 稿한다. 이약이는 炭坑으로 가기 3日前 大都會 名古屋에서―

1932년 7월 9일밤―名古屋 亨樂의 不夜城 「廣小路, 히로고-지」로 직업을 차즈려 나는 발을 옴겻다. 本町一丁目 어구로 도라가면 웬便구속으로 崇仁寺가 잇다. 그 엽흐로 공동碑石이 잇엇고 그안으로 街樹燈이 한 개 서잇고 그 밋흐로 陰浸한 2層 家屋이 나라니 하여 잇다. 나는 피로에 늣겨 爲先 절 後園石 階段우헤 안젓다. 사람들의 즐기는 소리와 電車소리와 멀니 「카페」에 「캬래껄」의 노래가 아울려 듯겨 온다. 그러나 이곳은 殺人사건이 일어나도 모를만침 고요하엿다. 淫賣女들에게 쫏기여 2層屋으로 지나가려다가 廣告塔을 보앗다. 「旅者職人 大募集」 나는 발을 멈추엇다. 다시 石계급으로 도라와 생각하엿다. 鐵道공사, 도로改修, 築港人夫, 아모레도 고생하고 都會로 彷徨하는 것보다 放浪, 생활의 便이 오히러 나허리라고 생각하고 그 집을 드러갓다. 그곳이 警察이 알면서 덥허두는 久米銅山 名古屋 出張所이엿다. 내가 처음 알기는 銅山으로 가는 車中에서 알엇다.
그리하여 2層에 두時가량 기다리고 잇다가 그집 書記와 함게 「大曾根, 오호조내」로 갓다. 陰浸한 「飯場, 한바」이다. 「합비」를 입은 사람이 20여명 누워 잇고 안자 잇고 서 잇다. 書記가 감독의게 무러라 그리고 가버렷다. 감독이 나의게 올나오라고 손짓한다. 그 동작과 표정은 아조 냉정하엿다. 이리하야 나도 그 20명 가운데 한사람으로 끼엿다. 그 중엔 나와 갓흔 소년도 끼여 잇섯다. 4人의 감독이 삐지,안코 도라단인다. 나는 氣味가 나뻐저 감독의 얼골만 치어다 보왓다. 감독도 나를 한참 보드니 「何處だ」 「朝鮮」 「一人か」 나는 머리로 대답하엿다. 그 압해 누어 잇든 人夫 한시람이 벌덕 이러나며 「너 朝鮮이니」 卒時 나는 반가웟다. 그 人夫의 겻흐로 갓다. 서로 身勢이약이를 주고 밧다가 나는 이럿케 무럿다. 「大關切 어데로 가는가요」 그 사랄도 몰낫다. 「하엿튼 仙臺로 간다는 말은 들엇스나 도로공사는 안인 듯 하고 「飯場, 한바」 主人말은 무슨 魚場으로 간다는 말도 듯기나마 하엿튼 가보아야 알지 極情말게 그러케 고생할 바야 무슨 일이든지 이 사람들 따러가는 것이 훨신 낫지안는가」―12時지나 一同은 列를 지어 「大曾根, 오호조내」 驛으로 나갓다. 슬슬한 停車場이다. 우리들은 조고마한 待合室에 안젓다. 감독이 문마다 서잇다. 傳送하러 온 가족들도 6,7人 잇섯다. 窓안과  박게서 이악이한다. 그들은 모-다 무표정이엇다. 조곰후 24명은 車에 몸을 실여 名古屋을 떠낫다.

그럼으로 그 잇튼날까지 「任事場시고도바」에 못나갓다. 이러하여스나 苦悶하는 감독의 얼골을 처어다 볼 때는 역시 치근한 생각이 낫다. 도라와 「한바」에 드러가니 다리와 머리로 繃帶를 감고 누워 꿍꿍 알코 잇다. 이들은 올나와 본칙만척하고 간발을 풀어놋코 내(川)가로   가버린다. 나도 따라 내려가자 「水を 一杯くれ 俊雄, 도시오」 소년이 나를 끌며 「やらう, ほつとけ!」 그러며 나가랴니 심음하는 소리가 놉흐게 낫다. 나는 큰거리에 한거름 떠서 감독의 겻흐로 노흐며 「さ, 飮め!」 그러고 감독은 머리를 돌여 한참 치어다 보더니 「강, 韓」 ありがどう」 나는 감독의 얼골을 다시 한참 치어다 보앗다. 그리고 밧글 나갓다. 「강, 韓ありがどう」 오날까지 나의 일홈을 부르며 거짓업시 고맙다는 말은 오날에야 비로서 처음으로 들엇다. 不知不覺에 나는 微笑하엿다.―그러나 오날밤에는 脫走하기를 「도시오, 俊雄」와 단단히 약속하엿다. 오날까지 미리여 둔 것은 처음 온 그 잇튼날 또 하사람의 人夫가 脫走하다가 番兵의 短銃에 넘으진 死屍를 본 까닭이다. 또 「飯場, 한바」에 밥하는 朝鮮여자의게 나는 만히 同情함을 바덧다. 나는 은제든지 해만 지면 그 여자의 방에 가서 고향의 이약이를 하여 가면서 노랏다. 나의 유일의 亨樂이엿다. 은제든지 내가 즐기는 「마른 누른밥」을 모와 두웟다가 남모르게 나의 호주머니에 너허 주엇다. 또 나 줄야고 「팟전」을 굽다가 日本여자의게 몃번이나 잔소리를 드러가면서도 그 눈치를 피하야 틈틈히 꾸어서 나의게 주엇다. 「아지머니 그만 두서요. 나따문에 公然히 그 사람들의게 눈치를 보힘니다」 그러면 그 여자는 이럿케 말하엿다. 「눈치는 무슨 눈치 나도 이것이 한 樂이 다. 그대로 잇다가 저녁에 도라오는 자네를 보긴 미안하이」 나는 참으로 미안하엿다. 진심으로 감사하엿다. 무엇띠문에 그 사람들의 눈살을 마저 가면서 굿태히 나의게 善心을 쓰는가. 나는 그 여자의게 고맙게 한 일도 업거니와 그만한 善心을 밧들 만한 행위를 한 일도 업섯다―처음에 내가 도로 은혜를 입엇다. 내가 감독의게 마저 「한바」에 눕엇슬대 그 여자가 藥을 끌러서 나의게 주엇다. 긋때 나는 「아저머니 감사함니다」 이말 한마데박게 한일이 업다. 그 말이 무워 그리이 현실에 善心을 바들만한 행위인가―「아저머니 감사함니다」라는 鄕土의 말이 그리워 나를 사랑케 하엿는가 감독에게 맛고 누워잇는 어린몸이 哀꾸저 나를 측은히 넉녓는가 저 暴惡한 人夫들에게 매일매일 고암소래와 辱소리만 듯다가 「아저머니」라는 眞情의 말소래를 듯고 나를 귀하게 넉녓는가 그 여자는 이 조건에 하나도 빠지지안흔 줄 나는 잘 안다. 그리고 (同族愛)가 가슴으로부터 떠올낫다. 나는 오래동안 어머니의 사랑에 굶줄엿다.＜77＞
그 여자도 오래동안 자식의 사랑에 굶줄엿다. 서로서로 限을 체우랴 하엿다. 나는 어머니라고도 부르고 십헛다. 그 여자도 應當 나를 아달이라고 불느고 십헛다.―人情과 평화의 俄鬼道―밤밤 아―서운밤 그 憂鬱과 惡魔갓흔 침묵이 연속되는 炭坑의 밤 피썩는 산송장들이 모혀 안저 서로서로 처어다 보는 恐怖의 밤 웬종일 피로에 넉겨 그데로 뻣처 슬어진 黃白色의 송장갓튼 얼골들과 차뒤찬 呻吟과 우뢰갓튼 코소래 「한바」의 뜰과 냇가의 바위우헤서 어머니가 고향이 그리워 우는 어린 人夫들의 매호야의 웨치는 소래 초상난 집의 밤 샘하는 것 갓치도―나는 듯기가 괴로워 손으로 귀를 막고 보기가 哀꾸저 눈을 감엇다. 그러나 또 듯지안코 보지안코 沈沈한 憂鬱의 연속이 도로 괴로워 손을 놋코 도로 눈을 뜨기를 몃번이나 하엿다. 오히러 피로의 낫지 憂鬱의 밤보다 快하엿다. 나는 그 여자의게 同情을 바든 후로부터는 낫의 노동에 한層 용기를 어덧고 밤의 침묵의 쾌락을 바듯다. 나의 힘이요. 도음이엿다.
7월 19일의 夜―「俊雄, 도시오」 소년과 脫走할냐고 굿게 약속하고 「一郞, 이지로-」 (갓치 朝鮮사람)의게 短銃까지 바더 내(川)가의 바위밋헤 숨겨 두엇다. 어데를 가든지 「一郞이지로-」의 은혜만은 永永 잇지안켓슴니다」 「은혜는 무슨 은혜 「인연」이 잇스면 언제 맛나도 또 맛난다. 념려마라 番兵에게 들키거든 쏘아버리고 날아나거라 뒤-ㅅ監當은 내가 다하마」 그리고 「도시오」와 누워 자는 人夫사히로 지나오다가 病든 감독이 눈을 뜨며 「オイ, 韓, 夜深けに 何處に 行くの」 「少し 散步さ」 「氣を 付けたよ, 知らず 番兵にやられるからな」 나는 감독의 얼골을 또 한참 치어다 보왓다. 매는 마젓스나 역시 가엽다. 「一郞, 이지로-」도 문압까지 딸아 나아왓다. 「정지」를 지나랴다가 문을 열어노코 아주머니가 안저 잇다. 「아이그 밤중에 어데 가는가」 「저―」그리며 「이지로-, 一郞」를 도라 보왓다. 눈짓을 한다. 「아주머니에게마는 거짓말하기는 실렷다. 허는 수 업시 「내ㅅ(川)가에 좀 놀다오겟슴니다」 「조심하게」 그말만 남겨두고 떠낫다. 「이지로-, 一郞」 평안히 게십시요」 「도시오, 俊雄와 山으로 기어 올라갓다. 우리들이 보이지 안흘 때까지」 이지로- 一郞」가 「한바」의 여페서 잇섯다. 「俊雄待つてくれ」 「どうした」 あの 姉さんに 一言云つてくる」 「ばかな危いぞ」 「いや少の間だ」 나의게 진심으로 사랑하여 주는 여자의게 거짓말한 것이 나는 양심에 쩨이엿다. 지금 떠나가면 못맛날지도 모르는데 간다 말한마데 안니하고 떠남은 넘무나 沒人情하다. 「俊雄, 도시오」을 혼자 남겨두고 「한바」로 내러가 방안을 엿보왓다. 「아저머니 먼첨 한 말을 거짓말임니다」 「웨」 「도시오㊀�와 갓치 오날밤에 달아나겟슴니다」 깜작 놀난 얼골이 점점 깁뿐 낫흐로 변하엿다. 「오냐 가거라 잘 생각하엿다」 平時에 그 은혜는 아모러한 身分에 잇드라도 굿게 잇지안켓슴니다」 행여나 朝鮮으로 가거든 安城邑으로 차저 와보게 혹 갈지도 모르니 잠간 잇게」 그리며 정지로 가드니 밥을 책보에 갈 사서 나의게 주며 「만일 길을 잘못 들면 작고 山中으로만 가기 쉬우니 가저가게」 아주머니의 주는 밥을 나는 바덧다. 「平安히 게십시요」 「잘 가게」 그 여자도 울고 나도 울엇다. 그리하야 내가 보히지 안흘 때까지 「한바」의 엽헤서 잇섯다. 山으로 올나가 나는 한참 보왓다. 그리하야 「도시오」와 기여 山을 넘엇다. 番兵을 보고 움퍽어 숨어 몃번의 땀을 흘엿다. 自然 短銃쥔 손과 다리는 떨니엿다. 「思ひ 切つて 越え-」 그리고 決死的으로 넘엇다. 아-하늘에 도음인지 우리들은 番兵의 警戒網을 무사히 넘엇다. 그리하야 날 살여라 하고 일직선으로 쫏찻다. 몃개의 山을 넘엇다. 몃개의 숩과 砂場을 지낫다. 아직 길은 보히지 안엇다. 발에서는 피가 흘엇다. 숨이 갑허서 바위밋헤 조곰식 쉬기도 몃 번이나 하엿다. 새 우는 소리가 은은히 듯기엿다. 닭 우는 소리도 듯기엿다. 멀-니 절鍾소래도 듯기엿다. 鍾소래 듯기는 곳올 向하엿다. 그리하야 길을 차젓다. 洞內도 보왓다. 광이 매고 가는 농부도 보왓다.＜78＞
東쪽이 밝가 온다. 해가 떳다-黎明-아-黎明 「俊雄助かつな, 光明な 세계が 見られる, 强く 生きやう, 勵んで 働かう」 그리고 아주머니의게 바든 밥을 먹으며 凱旋將軍과 갓치 고암질너 노래브르며 快勇히 행진하엿다.―그들의게도 人情이 잇다. 발휘하면 서로 따라서 발휘한다. 그러나 발휘를 떠나 暴惡속에만 잠겨 잇는 것이 願痛하다. 아-그들의 히미-한 눈 산송장갓흔 동작과 표정 人情에 처음으로 觸感하고 나를 처어다 보는 감독의 얼골 同族愛에서 나를 구하여 준 「이지로, 一郞」 넑으니 慈愛하신 아주머니의 땃뜻한 품안 마른 누른밤과 팟전 어린人夫들의 꿋게 부르는 매호야의 소래,―
행진하며 머리에 작고 떠올낫다. 그리하야 나는 발을 멈추고 뒤를 도라 보왓다.
