Author: 李庸岳
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

In the Wind, (-You who were my mother's son along with me, my brother who collapsed on the roadside of Pogranichnaya and passed away forever-)
						Yi Yong'ak

Embracing the driving wind, where should I go,
Treading the snowy path, where should I head,
To be able to see you?

Around the castle bend or in a dim, gloomy tavern,
Wherever<154>
I might meet you, from your heart, I
Will share with you the saddest of sad nights.

Please wave your hand, even from afar.

When you, swollen from opium, pressed your cheek to the sheet of ice,
Growing cold just like the ice sheet,
When, crawling, crawling, trying to stand, you floundered on the ground,
When eternal darkness descended, surrounding you,

What was it that the night of Pogranichnaya
Silently settled into the dying eyes
Of the wayfarer?

When only the small wooden box, said to contain all of you—
The fields, hills, and valleys<155>
You groped along—returned,
When your funeral bier, an empty bier,
Hurriedly walked toward the seashore, the seashore,

Were you only Mother's beloved son?

Even burnt tree roots, even stones,
Roll toward me.
In the faded colors, you and I,
Shall we not meet again, weeping?

Please wave your hand, even from afar.<156>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Yong'ak is a lament for the poet's deceased older brother, who died alone and destitute ("collapsed on the roadside") in Pogranichnaya, a town in the Russian Far East. The speaker yearns to meet his brother again, recalling his suffering, possibly from opium addiction, and his lonely death far from home. The return of only a small box containing his essence, and the image of an empty funeral bier heading to the sea, underscore the sorrow of displacement and loss. From a colonial history perspective, the poem powerfully evokes the hardships faced by Koreans forced to migrate or wander during the Japanese occupation. The brother's death in a foreign land, possibly driven by poverty or political circumstances, and his apparent addiction, reflect the social disintegration and personal tragedies experienced by many Koreans living precariously in border regions like Manchuria and the Russian Far East during this era.
