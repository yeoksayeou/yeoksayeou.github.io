**Correspondence of Literary Figures and Poets, Ten Letters Between Male and Female Writers**

To Ms. Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑)

Yu Chin'o (兪鎭午)
Although I saw you on the street just a moment ago, and we are in the same Changan (長安, i.e., Seoul), able to meet whenever there is something to discuss, and though there is no particular matter to address, it is quite amusing to be writing a letter. However, once again, I have made a sad compromise with journalism and have taken up this pen. The magazine company treats this writing as something quite grand, and readers might approach it searching for some great meaning. But what significance, what meaning could possibly lie within this text? Wasn't it George Gissing who said something to the effect that "There is nothing as blissful as writing a letter to an acquaintance when the mood strikes"? <110> Interpreting that statement conversely, it means I am currently experiencing great misfortune—
However, just because I take up a pen I don't wish to hold doesn't mean there's nothing to talk about. We are both Korean people, both people who write, so there are countless common topics; and I am a man, and you, madam, are a woman, so there could be many unique topics as well. It's just that I dislike taking up this pen merely to put on a show for others, as if stepping onto some stage. For someone involved in literature, there is only literature; what use is jazz-style promotion? Among shallow young literary aspirants, there seem to be those who view literature as the supreme and greatest means of seeking fame (賣名), and who regard literary figures as disciples of fame (賣名의 徒) driven by vanity, but that must surely be the greatest misconception. No matter what, there is likely nothing as arduous, practical, and demanding of steady effort as literature.
Truly, if we dreamed of splendor and longed for luxury, why would we, in the middle of the night when others are comfortably playing and resting, hunch over our desks like toads, enduring aching shoulders and dizziness, writing one character only to erase two, writing one page only to tear up two? How splendid must be the life of an official who moves tens of thousands with a single command, and how luxurious the feast of a businessman intoxicated by fragrant fine wine with a beautiful woman (美姬) at his side? Yet, worldly people point to us as a group living in vanity! What a mortifying false accusation.
Last winter too, I received this kind of rebuke from someone I had until then considered a very close friend, and I couldn't suppress the regretful feeling that a decade of friendship seemed to vanish in an instant. If literature appears so splendid and seems a tool for seeking fame (賣名의 具), let the person who criticizes the literary figure try, even just once, to weave together a readable work!
Madam Yunsuk (允淑 先生), <111>
Was it Windelband (뷘델반드) who said something meaning, "The modern philosopher is like someone who arrives at the banquet hall after the feast has ended"? It seems that Korean literary figures cannot even set foot in the banquet hall from the start. On the one hand, they are viewed with disdain as fame-seekers (賣名の徒), while on the other, even callow youths (黃口 乳臭) cast contemptuous remarks, saying Korean literature is insignificant. Perhaps it would be enough to dismiss them all as vulgar sorts and cultivate aloofness, but literary figures are also human beings with bodies; though they try to do so, they cannot, which is also distressing.
Ms. Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑)
Though addressed as a letter to you, madam, it has turned into me listing my own grievances. But I brought this up because I feel that this unjust situation is even more severe in the case of female writers (女流) like yourself. In general, this land of Korea (朝鮮) is overly sensitive to others' private matters (私私). Especially for female literary figures, who are rarer than stars in the dawn sky, couldn't they be treated with a bit more consideration and care?
I am not a poet, so I cannot say much about your poetry, madam, but I have seen in your poems the foundation for writing good prose, should you choose to write it, and have always anticipated that. That is why I was immensely glad upon encountering your "Elegy of Ren" (렌의 哀歌). I recall mentioning at the time that it was like a "female version of *Strait is the Gate*", but its lofty grace, refined sentences, and sharp dissection of inner life were certainly things rarely seen in our literature up to that point.
However, just as Mr. Ch'oe Chaesŏ (崔載瑞) asserted it was poetry, perhaps "Elegy of Ren" was more an extension of poetry than a work based on the spirit of prose. Whether poetry or novel, literature is general, but novels demand a more comprehensive structure and view of life. In this sense, I see the recent "Daybreak" (未明) as a great leap forward for you into prose.
"Daybreak" certainly falls into the category of prose literature. But why is it that when encountering "Daybreak," I do not feel the same emotion as when I encountered "Elegy of Ren"? Your sentences, honed through poetry, were of a rarely seen fluency. <112> Yet, why do they fail to impart a heart-rending impression on the reader? I believe it is because, again, the novel demands an even more comprehensive structure and deeper immersion into human nature than poetry.
What I intended to say begins now, but because the introduction was long, the letter has already become lengthy. It has ended up being cut short, but I pray that your literary fortune (文運) shines ever brighter and will close here.
May 7th, Yu Chin'o (兪鎭午) Respectfully

To Mr. Yu Chin'o (兪鎭午氏)

Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑)
I am reading the picture book [likely refers to a serialized novel or illustrated story Yu Chin'o was publishing] with great interest. Since it's not finished, it's difficult to state definitively, but the impression gained from reading so far leads me to anticipate that this work will achieve a very excellent result.
Firstly, I express my respect for how it is being constructed, possessing realistic power while also being imbued with artistic fragrance.
The workings of the minds of the contemporary Korean intelligentsia, especially the wandering nature of the new woman's character, are depicted without any camouflage (캄푸라지), brightly and clearly, yet possess the power to make the reader think further.
Next, there is something I dare to say to you, Mr. Yu, please forgive me and listen.
I am dissatisfied that you are currently expending your passion on the duties of an educator. It seems to me that Mr. Yu's primary duty ought to be focused on novels, but in the current state, I worry that you might be regarding novel writing as a side job.
For a novelist, shouldn't a wider world, a freer realm, be the homeland of their breath? Of course, school life may also be ideal, but I worry whether perhaps constraints might arise in exploring the boundless world of art due to this ideal discipline.
In any case, isn't the fundamental responsibility of Mr. Yu's character not in beautifying instinct through the duties of the law department head <113> but rather in harmonizing a free and magnanimous spirit to produce great works? Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑) Respectfully

To Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)

Yi T'aejun (李泰俊)
It has been many days since I last saw you. In the meantime, spring has brought many blossoms. I hope that for Ms. Ch'oe too, these have been days of many flowers blooming.
Receiving Ms. Mo Yunsuk's letter the other day, I felt that you two have a very interesting relationship. I believe such delightful scribbles were born because you two were together in one place. Meeting good friends is a virtue that extends wonderfully even to others.
I read "Human Connections" (人脈) yesterday. Perhaps the excitement of writing "Human Connections" has not yet cooled. "Human Connections" is a work considerably trampled not by others, but by the author herself. The excitement of trampling one's own hem—this, harshly enough, is a technical issue for which I believe you have already received both praise and censure from critics.
There is no race more suspicious than novel readers. The author and the reader can never be one family. They believe newspaper articles unconditionally, but in a novel, they demand that even the coincidence of a single freckle appearing on a character's face be "inevitable." No matter how splendid a character, once the author has conceived them in their mind, the author must act as the strictest judicial chief and conduct a relentless interrogation. Otherwise, it cannot proceed to indictment in the reader prosecutor's office. Non-indictment means the denial of the case, that is, the failure of the police.
Ms. Ch'oe, you sympathized with "Sŏnyŏng." Sympathize with the outcome, but sympathy during interrogation is strictly forbidden. Characters in first-person novels often use cunning words and pleasing expressions towards the author.
"Sŏnyŏng" is a fine character worthy of illuminating the literary court once. The non-indictment of Sŏnyŏng—Ms. Ch'oe, you must be angry at "Sŏnyŏng." You must re-arrest her (彼女). <114>
Ms. Ch'oe, while writing the novel "Human Connections," why did you act the poet? Was meeting the poet Mo, then, a demerit for the novel? Haha.
This is an anonymous tip from a fellow officer of the literary police, urging the re-arrest of "Sŏnyŏng."
April 12th, Yi T'aejun (李泰俊) Respectfully

To Mr. Yi T'aejun (李泰俊 先生님)

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)
Mr. Yi, I have just read the gracious letter (惠函) you sent to the Samch'ŏlli (三千里社) editorial department—in other words, the letter Mr. Yi sent to me. Actually, on the very day the editorial department here ordered me to write a piece addressed to you, Mr. Yi, I wrote it immediately, but I take up my pen again now after reading your gracious letter out of gratitude for the trouble you took to read my "Human Connections" (人脈), and also out of appreciation for pointing out things I hadn't realized. I had no idea that I had sympathized with the protagonist "Na"—Sŏnyŏng—in "Human Connections." Now that you've told me so, it seems I really did write it with the excitement of stepping on my own hem. I will be extremely careful henceforth. Such kind words from you, Mr. Yi, seem likely to make me write better.
Two years ago, when I wrote "Record of Silence" (靜寂記), you also pointed out every single flaw in that poorly written diary-style piece, offered critique and reprimand, and, I recall you even quoted someone's words to the effect that,
"When we read novels, we expect something to happen. A clash between life and fate, we hope for drama. We hope the characters are lively and not meaningless." Truly, back then—and it's the same now—I didn't know how to write novels. But because you explained so meticulously and critiqued in detail, afterwards, even when writing whatever came to mind, I would recall your words <115> and pause. However, due to my own severe shortcomings, I still cannot follow your teachings exactly. I will study hard in the future and write well. By doing so, I will grow my flawed self. Lastly, I would like to inquire about the contents of the piece I intended to send you last time. In it, I didn't talk about myself at all, but mentioned that I read the serial novel "Flourishing Youth" (靑春茂盛) you are currently writing for the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報) every morning without fail, as if waking up early just to see it; that until now, I hadn't read any newspaper novels because I assumed they were just written haphazardly for mere entertainment, but my perception changed while reading "Flourishing Youth"; that the title "Flourishing Youth" was less to my liking, but because the writing is so fine, good, and beautiful, my dissatisfaction with the title vanished before I knew it. After saying that, I briefly expressed my request for you to foster many good female writers through the magazine "Munjang" (文章) which you are in charge of, and my opinion that it would be good to strongly encourage someone like Ms. Im Ogin (林玉仁), the author of the selected work "Balsam" (鳳仙花), to write. Well, that's all for today. May you always be well (來來 安康하옵소서).
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙) Respectfully

To Mr. Yi Hyosŏk (李孝石氏)

Chang Tŏkcho (장덕조)
Mr. Yi Hyosŏk (李孝石)
As you may know, madam [assuming female form used generally, but addressing a male], magazine editors sometimes issue commands that are bafflingly awkward. Moreover, this time, being asked to write a letter to you, sir, whom I do not know well and have never met, is embarrassing for me, and I worried it might be rather rude to you, an unknown gentleman. So I tried to simply decline, but due to repeated requests, I have reluctantly taken up my pen. Spreading out the paper, I recall your family photograph that appeared in some women's magazine sometime ago.
And I also remember seeing <116> your wife's writing published in the pages of "Samch'ŏlli" (三千里) the other day. From this, one can imagine a very harmonious and pleasant home life with three cute children and a virtuous wife.
May your literary fortune (文運) flourish even more.
As someone who can hardly be called a figure of the literary world yet, either in the quality or quantity of my work, I wish to avoid offering impressions or criticisms of your works in this letter as well.

To Ms. Chang Tŏkcho (張德祚氏)

Yi Hyosŏk (리효석)
Being constantly pressed to write a letter to you, madam, whom I do not know, I was just hesitating, unsure of what words would be appropriate. What on earth should I write, having never met you, and due to laziness, having never read your works either? The only thing that comes to my mind right now is your photograph, which I often see in magazines. I reflect once again on the misfortune and impoliteness of having to write a letter using that respected image as a guide.
Judging by your gentle and neat appearance, your character must also be mild, and consequently, you must enjoy peaceful pleasures at home, and the novels you write must surely be harmonious and clear. Photographs deceive like nothing else, but I hope my intuition (취직 - 推測?) is correct, and in that respect, it is also true that I feel a certain sense of secure sentiment, shall I say, towards you.
While I have met and exchanged greetings with most other female writers, it is a regret, if it is one, that I haven't met you alone. However, there are times when it is more enjoyable to freely imagine a beautiful world through fantasy alone; is it forced to try and conceal my oversight with such thoughts? In any case, the anticipation of meeting you in the future is now a source of joy, and I believe that after meeting you, I will naturally be able to write a much better letter.
Today I have written extremely impolite and pointless things, <117> but I hope you will forgive me generously. I pray you are always well and, furthermore, wield a healthy pen (健筆). I shall end here hastily.
April 24th, Yi Hyosŏk (리효석) Respectfully
Ms. Chang Tŏkcho (장덕조 선생)

To Ms. Yi Sŏnhŭi (李善熙氏)

Pak T'aewŏn (朴泰遠)
Have you been well? I (生) am fine. I believe we briefly met at the Munjang Publishing (文章社) roundtable discussion during the year-end market last December, and that was it. Nearly half a year has already passed since then.
However, I have occasionally heard news of you through Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi. It seems you are always spending fortunate days, and this time, I hear you have given birth to a jade-like baby boy; congratulations indeed. I also heard from Ms. Ch'oe that both the baby and the mother are extremely healthy; what could be more gratifying than this?
I (生) have been spending my days without doing much of anything lately. Building a tiny house outside Dongsomun Gate (東小門) has turned out to be quite a big undertaking for me (生), and my mind is completely preoccupied with it, unable to grasp any other task. Nevertheless, the earthwork is already half finished, and in about another fortnight, it should become a house, albeit a humble one. Please come visit once I've moved.
I, too, intend to visit often with Ms. Ch'oe to see the baby (叅堂), and at that time, please ensure the master of the house (밖앝 어른) doesn't go out anywhere either, and receive us warmly with the whole family present.
May 4th, Pak T'aewŏn (朴泰遠) Again Respectfully
Ms. Yi Sŏnhŭi (李善熙씨) Seat <118>

Mr. Kubo (仇甫 선생)

Yi Sŏnhŭi (李善熙)
It is early morning now. I sat down, dipping my pristine new pen nib, laden with blue ink, into the holder, intending to write a letter to you, sir.
Rose-colored sunlight streams through the back door of the main room floor (마루). A fresh feeling pervades quite pleasantly after a long time.
However, no one comes to my window singing "O Sole Mio!" The sunlight gradually turns yellowish and faint.
I used to write letters well. But now, I write perhaps once or twice a year, and other than writing to my father, I don't write anywhere else. Therefore, trying to write a letter to you now, Mr. Kubo (仇甫), my skill feels terribly clumsy. By the way, have you been well (氣體 太平) lately? When I saw you last time, why were you so like a mute, your mouth expressionless, only glancing furtively over your glasses? I was quite disappointed.
Those times when you chatter away thoughtlessly are what make me think most fondly of you. Please converse much more in the future. In that "Riverside Scenery" (川邊 風景) style.
If Mr. Kubo (仇甫군) had been a bit more of a delinquent youth, how easily I might be able to write this letter— But to Mr. Kubo (仇甫씨), who is neither "Romeo" nor "Don José," but the big master of the teahouse (茶房) alley, what sort of letter content should I create?
Already, flies are buzzing around the drain hole (수채 구녁). What medicine should be applied to kill flies? Those creatures should certainly be killed off, but when I hear the buzzing sound of flies in early summer (初夏), I feel an endlessly lazy sentiment in my body.
I have written many useless things. Are the children growing well (善長), and is your wife also well (安寧)? When are you moving into the new house? For the housewarming (집드리), I intend to buy matches and taffy (엿).
I think I first came to know you, sir, when "A Day in the Life of Novelist Mr. Kubo" (小說家 仇甫氏의 一日) was published in the newspaper, and at that time, I thought very highly of that novel and its author.
Wasn't the time that novel came out hot just like now? And I think there was talk of "Soda Water" (소-다水) and "Calpis" (칼피쓰), perhaps because of that, when I think of you, sir, you are distinctly a person of early summer; winter doesn't suit you.
Today is a day I particularly crave "Soda Water" and "Calpis." Well then, please stay well always. Perhaps we might run into each other by chance on some teahouse (茶房) road soon. <119>

**Summary**

This article presents a series of ten letters exchanged between prominent male and female Korean writers in 1940, published in the magazine *Samch'ŏlli*. The letters reveal personal and professional relationships within the literary circle, containing mutual praise, critiques of recent works (like Mo Yunsuk's "Mimyeong" and Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi's "Inmaek"), discussions about the nature of literature versus fame, the challenges of the writing process, and reflections on the pressures faced by writers, particularly female writers, in colonial Korean society. Personal news, like Yi Sŏnhŭi's childbirth and Pak T'aewŏn's house construction, is also shared. From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable glimpse into the intellectual life and social dynamics of the Korean literary elite during the late colonial period. It highlights the ongoing debate about artistic integrity under societal and potentially political pressures (implied by the "compromise with journalism"), the specific difficulties and scrutiny faced by women intellectuals ("rarer than stars"), and the blend of modernist sensibilities (references to Western figures, psychological depth in literature) with traditional Korean social norms (polite forms of address, family updates).
