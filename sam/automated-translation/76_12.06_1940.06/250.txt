Humanities Department, Song Mong-gyu

1. Literature

*   *Death*
    I think it may have shown some light of resolution to the hundreds of thousands of young men hesitating and doubting in the face of war and death. To quote a few lines:
    (Japanese text follows - 9 lines)
    [Translation of Japanese passage]: *"Indeed, you must choose. Choose... Between what and what?... Between continuing to live this way, in this anguish, in this dread, or ending it... Ah, to end it... How simple it seems... A slight pressure on this trigger... And it's over... Over... What?... Life... Yes, life... The life I have cursed so much... The life that has made me suffer so much... The life that I have dragged along like a burden... But still, life... That is, this possibility of feeling, of thinking, of willing... This possibility of loving... Yes, loving... Even if it's only in memory... Even if it's only in regret... Loving..."*

*   *The Importance of Living*
    Within the humor, sharp observation and precise critique abound. Often, while laughing, I couldn't help but be stimulated and flinch.

*   *The Good Earth*
    In Part 1, Wang Lung's wife says, "Stop it, my life isn't worth that much. With that kind of money, you could buy fine land."
    "That won't do. I will die. I will die someday. Only the land will remain even after I die." From this, one can understand the Chinese peasant's view of land, and I wonder if it isn't a similar sentiment to the people here [in Korea]. It wouldn't be an exaggeration to call it an anatomy of Chinese society rather than a novel.
    In Part 2, the clash between the old generation and the new generation is revealed, and one cannot help but sympathize with the new generation.
    In Part 3, one could vividly see the newly rising China and the trends among the youth of that time. The typical Chinese Wang Yuan and Meiling, common figures like Wang Sheng and Ailan who live in vanity, the hot-blooded Wang Meng -- all were good contrasts, and this part drew in the youthful heart the most among all three parts.

*   *Madame Curie*
    It was more of a biography than a literary work, so it was just okay – however, I read the first half with tears. The middle part was a common story, and I couldn't shake the feeling of watching an American movie in the latter half.

In *Mumyŏng*, don't you think those who are hungry and want to eat, who hate because they hate, are more human than the hypocrite called 'I'? I cannot sympathize with the teacher's perspective, which only saw their ugly side.

Buddhism is needed by old people – passive people – but for vibrant youth, it is an opiate. Unless, of course, one doesn't know that even Christ was a human who made wine in his first miracle, then it's a different matter.

Humanities Department, Kim Sam-bul

1. Literature

*   *Demian* (Hesse)
    When I was under 20, I didn't know what solitude was. Even if I felt solitude, it was momentary, and I treated this <176> illusion of sentimentality, which quickly vanished, as if it were solitude. Nowadays, I have discovered a new kind of solitude within me, different from that.
    And to escape this strange solitude, though I flailed and swam in loneliness, my body was confined to a solitary island in the great ocean, and only when I realized I had fallen into a deep pit from which I could not escape, did I decide to face solitude as fate, after all the weeping and wailing to get out. That is, I came to harbor a great wound within myself, within all of humanity.
    Having already observed the world for two years within the white veil of solitude, the greatest gain was a broad interpretation of the "sentiment or affection" (情味 혹은 애정) people possess, and also that while there are many ways for people to live, a solitary life is appropriate for understanding humans.
    A solitary life is always introverted, therefore life is truthful. As the protagonist "Kuhn" [likely referring to Knulp] in *Demian* [Note: Kim Sam-bul refers to the book as "고독" (Solitude), likely meaning Demian or perhaps Knulp, another Hesse work focusing on an outsider] said:
    "Ultimately, making happiness or unhappiness an issue is foolish. Because even the unhappiest day of my life is more precious than many happy days."
    Thus, the entirety of the novel *Solitude* is a record of the pure life of the disabled and solitary "Kuhn". His disability, brought on by the tender first love of age 20, fated him to solitude along with it. People, when young, are always engrossed only in planning their future happiness, and extremely individualistic thoughts know no sacrifice. "Kuhn" sought love from several women but could not obtain it because he was disabled. He lamented his fate and attempted suicide, but ultimately, it was not the same as living. Thus, the solitary soul found two paths in life.
    "As Christians say, if the world is fallen and ugly, we must practice asceticism and renounce everything. Therein we will find satisfaction. Another is, if this world and life are happy and right, we should live together and then die peacefully. Thus life is completed—"
    Whichever path "Kuhn" chose, there is a deep understanding of life here.
    "When I succeeded as a musician, socializing with noble and high-class people could not become my true life; my true life was still solitude, and what I always thought about was what fate my closest friends had followed afterward."
    Thus, the unchanging loneliness even amidst the welcome of any fame is his solitude, and I wonder if this isn't true solitude.<177>

*   *The Importance of Living* (Lin Yutang)
    It is an easy yet difficult, charming yet truthful discovery of life. It was the first time in my life I read a book with such a generous heart. And I have rarely seen a book that revealed the pure happiness of humanity before my eyes as much as this one. Here lies rich content of life. As one of the rarest discoveries Lin Yutang found in life, I want to mention his theory of "humor."
    It goes without saying how much humor lubricates life, and it is also true that humor simplifies people.
    And it is natural that humor is connected to the leisurely life that is the main theme of this book, and more than anything else, the fact that the Chinese know humor best allows us to glimpse their high-minded magnanimity (海容性). Lastly, heed Lin Yutang's words that in these times when the Great War has broken out, only humor can save the world.
    When the Great War ends, there will surely be a Peace Treaty. It seems the best plan would be for President Roosevelt [likely referring to the US President] to follow Lin Yutang's wise counsel, convene a conference, gather humorist ambassadors from each country, hold a humor convention, and conclude the treaty there. Then, there would definitely be no unreasonable treaties, and I believe Lin Yutang would deservedly receive the Nobel Peace Prize the following year.

Humanities Department, Song Pyŏng-jin

*   *The Good Earth* (Pearl S. Buck)
    *The Good Earth* is one of the novels I read with deep impression. As is well known, this novel is a trilogy, translated along with the same author's *The Mother*, and has also already been screened [as a film].
    To confess frankly, I have not yet finished reading *The Good Earth*. Last spring vacation, I hurriedly read up to page 173 of Part 2, then came up to Seoul leaving it unfinished, so I still cannot suppress my regretful feeling. Because it was someone else's book. However, I read more than half, and indirectly read several other works by the same author, so it would be a lie to say I have no impressions about it at all.
    It wouldn't be wrong to view *The Good Earth* as Recent Chinese History. Since it's a work spanning three generations, it covers the recent hundred years of history. While unconsciously turning the pages, we taste endless interest, and at the same time, the face of China appears clearly before our eyes (眼前에 서언하게 나타난다). Thus, even after reading, the atmosphere doesn't leave the body for several days. Comparing the characters that appear in it, imagining the first generation as our parents, the second as our siblings, the third as our descendants, is also interesting (滋味있다). When we make such assumptions, they often fit well. Thus, my younger brother and I <178> have often burst out laughing, holding our stomachs.
    The writing style flows gracefully, like spring water trickling down from a mountain valley. The refined brushwork makes one think of realism throughout, yet there is no trace, i.e., flaw, of realism. Should this be called Pearl S. Buck's unique description? If I become a novelist in the future, I will definitely learn from that of Pearl S. Buck.
    While reading *The Good Earth*, we not only recognize the Chinese lifestyle, that is, customs and habits, but we can also rediscover those of our Chosŏn (朝鮮). For instance, the etiquette of the funeral rites performed after Wang Lung's death seems almost identical to Chosŏn's. Here, I recall seeing in a movie sometime ago that Indian customs were similar to Chosŏn's. When recalling the historical fact that Chinese Confucianism and Indian Buddhism were imported into our Chosŏn, this phenomenon is not the least bit surprising (疑訝할 것이 안된다).
    Lastly, one more word to add is the corruption of the classical Chinese education we are currently receiving. Confucius and Mencius, from who knows how many thousands of years ago, are the sole and unique teaching materials we are learning. In today's world, where yesterday's literature already feels old today, what is this? We must break away from this as soon as possible and, just like all other subjects, draw material from the modern era. From this perspective as well, I recognize the importance this *The Good Earth* gives us.

1. Philosophy and Religion

*   *Philosophical Anthropology*
    Contemporary thought has long been steeped in the philosophy of life (生의 철학). What is man? What makes man human? People will ask the meaning of these questions. Max Scheler was right to say that philosophical anthropology is the fundamental study of human essence. According to Scheler, man's specific characteristic compared to other living beings is not wisdom (叡智) or the ability to choose, nor life (生), but spirit (精神), the collective term for the entire domain of action. The center of that action is personality (人格). However, man is by no means merely a being of spirit; he is an independent, single, concrete whole composed of two opposing principles: life and spirit. Spirit idealizes life, and life vitalizes spirit.

Humanities Department, Ko Hŭng-sang

1. Literature

*   *Mr. Yi T'ae-jun and His Works*
    Before discussing the works, I want to briefly discuss the writer, Mr. Yi T'ae-jun.
    When thinking about Mr. Yi T'ae-jun's literary art, what first comes to our minds are his writing style and ideology (思想性).
    This may seem like repeating past public opinion, but without mentioning this, one cannot speak of the writer Mr. Yi T'ae-jun.
    In this writer, the writing style reaches the peak of its refinement (粹). The polished sentences have clear rhythm (리즘이 맑고), high fragrance, and the dialogue is full of wit (機智), making Mr. Yi a master in the way of writing (文章道).
    It is like a beautifully decorated flower garden. However, when the sensory moment this garden provides passes, and the reader unifies their post-reading impressions with reason, they feel dissatisfaction. So, why do works possessing such beautiful writing and witty dialogue fail to suppress this dissatisfaction?
    It is because ideology is lacking in the works.
    However, looking at principles, there are two types of ideology in literature.
    One is, when a literature takes the ideas flowing as the trend of its era as the spirit within the work, that is, needless to say, the ideology of literature. Another could be considered the ideology the individual author holds as the ideology of literature. However, this is vague and unclear. Also, we can <180> call this morality (모랄).
    Indeed, an author's morality shapes the unique ideological form of their literature.
    Demanding ideology from writers in this land also refers to this latter ideology. Of course, what we demand from writer Mr. Yi is also the latter kind of ideology.
    Without the latter kind of ideology, the literature lacks the power to unify and purify the whole. Similarly, writer Mr. Yi lacks this.
    With the above preliminary knowledge, let's examine the work *Moonlit Night*.
    This work has no connection between incidents; it merely strings together momentary scenes that popped into the author's head into an interesting "story."
    Here, what kind of person is the existence called "I"? Also, the protagonist "I" thought the place was countryside because fools were conspicuous. And originally, fools are generally seen as dull and naive.
    Such a person helps evoke a simple countryside sentiment for a newly arrived guest, and the story begins there.
    The fool Hwang Su-gŏn! Only fragments of the life of the foolish newspaper deliveryman are briefly depicted.
    Here, the reader might be able to find the customary melancholy flowing behind writer Mr. Yi T'ae-jun's literary art.
    However, since the connection of events here is not placed in the development of thought, readers who finish reading this feel dissatisfaction.
    With only the melancholy Mr. Yi possesses, we cannot eternally possess the writer Mr. Yi T'ae-jun. Melancholy is nothing more than the scent of the work.
    Writer Mr. Yi T'ae-jun must possess "morality." A system of thought – this is the most crucial issue for writer Mr. Yi T'ae-jun.

*   Reading *The Importance of Living* (Lin Yutang, translated by Sakamoto Masaru)
    As a Chinese person possessing sharp observation and cool critical power, no, as a broad-minded international figure, frankly criticizing the Chinese people who have established their view of life through an eccentric (though this word might be problematic) way of thinking, while at the same time boldly <181> scolding the thinking of Westerners who prioritize logic and analysis based on inductive methods, not in a self-serving manner (我田引水), but in fact praising the pure humanity of the Chinese people (especially Bai Letian (白樂天), Su Dongpo (蘇東坡), Tu Chishui (屠赤水), Yuan Zhonglang (袁中郞), Li Zhuowu (李卓吾), Zhang Chao (張潮), Li Liweng (李笠翁), Yuan Mei (袁枚), Jin Shengtan (金聖嘆)) drew great interest.
    As the author says, "The universe is a great book, and life is a great school," he studied life with an erudition (에루디슌) overwhelming Kagawa Toyohiko, and with an essay style that would make A. A. Milne or Kenkō Hōshi (Yoshida Kenkō) weep, kneaded with humor, frankly presenting concrete thoughts on various methods concerning life issues, politics (especially the critique of totalitarianism based on biological reasons sparked further interest), art, religion, society, etc., was very fascinating (滋味있였다).
    If even an amateur cameraman (of course, a famous director is unnecessary) were to film the endlessly changing faces of readers moment by moment as they read each phrase and paragraph, they would surely collect the full 70-chŏn admission fee of the currently running permanent theaters.

Humanities Department, O Yong-sun

1. Film

*   *Burgtheater* (The Passion of Joseph Rainer)
    "If Santa Claus appeared in a dream and asked, 'What present do you want to receive, and what present will you give to the people of the world?' I would instantly reply, 'Bread of passion, bread of passion, and also what I will give to the people of the world is bread of passion, bread of passion!'"
    This is a passage from my diary's New Year's resolution. Why? Because passion gives birth to great deeds, great love, great inventions. In other words, passion is precisely their mother. If there is anyone here without passion, he is a lame cripple unable to give birth, and if there is anyone who abandons passion midway, he will surely be a mother who aborts or miscarries.
    I believe that your, my, or our grandfathers' most frightening illness is passion-anemia (情熱貧血病) and passion-abandonment disease (情熱遺棄病). What great history can be built here, what splendid art can be created? Ah, I resolutely wanted to cry out repeatedly, "Bread of passion, bread of passion!"
    I found one typical expression of such passion in the young Rainer of *Burgtheater*. Rainer's passion to go to the Burgtheater and become a world-famous actor, passion that seemed like it could cause an earthquake, passion that seemed like it could melt the sun, almost fanatical passion (in fact, I confess that whenever I faced Rainer, I clenched my fists and unknowingly entered a state of resonance (共鳴)) – what did it produce? When rehearsing *Romeo and Juliet*, the woman who admired him, initially regarding Rainer with great displeasure = Mrs. Mitterer = was drawn straight into love, and when Mitterer, through her own experience, rejected his ambition with cold laughter, he cried out. "If I cannot live on the stage, I would rather die on the stage!" What fearsome passion this is! That he later became a world-famous actor must have been an inevitable result.
    The theme of this film, Mitterer's psychology – she who lived 50 years surrounded by fame and vanity, trying to restart as a true human being, a true person – this, of course, could not be watched without exclamations of admiration. However, as she had already abandoned human ambition and cursed and shunned fame, I only felt weariness and decline. Compared to this, Rainer's passion, Rainer's passion that rushes forth even when I close my eyes, even when I close my eyes, cannot be praised highly enough. The acting of Eichberger [likely Willy Eichberger/Carl Esmond] who played Rainer (continued on page 168)<185> was also extremely good.
    Leaving the cinema (사진관 - photo studio, but likely meaning cinema here), I firmly pledged to myself. When we (the protagonists of the era) remove the atmosphere called 'modernity,' the answer of gray melancholy, anxiety, and despair emerges – this is undoubtedly true. However, we must not be mirrors that receive it as is and reflect it as is. Rather, we must become drivers of passion, grasping you [the audience/contemporaries] and rushing towards great construction – drivers with passion like Rainer's. (Next time: Keijō Imperial University Humanities Department, Philosophy Department Student Edition)<168>

**Summary**

This article from the June 1940 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents a survey of the cultural tastes of humanities students at Yŏnhŭi Specialized School (now Yonsei University) in Seoul. Students share their impressions on selected works of philosophy, literature (Korean, Japanese, Western, Chinese), music (performances by both Western and Korean artists), and film (mostly Western and Japanese). The responses reveal a keen engagement with contemporary intellectual currents, including existential themes, social critique (poverty, societal norms), and reflections on art and life. Notable are the critiques of established Korean writer Yi Kwang-su, the appreciation for detailed social realism in works like *The Good Earth* and *The Tale of Im Kkŏk-chŏng*, and the strong emotional responses to both literature (Hesse, Lin Yutang) and film (*Prison Without Bars*, *Burgtheater*).

For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the intellectual life and cultural consumption of elite Korean students during the late colonial period. It showcases the complex cultural landscape they navigated, influenced by Western philosophy and literature, Japanese contemporary works, Chinese perspectives (Lin Yutang, Pearl Buck's portrayal), and a developing modern Korean literary scene. The students' critical engagement, their concerns with authenticity, passion, social issues, and national identity (evident in comments on Yi Kwang-su and the study of Korean language through *Im Kkŏk-chŏng*), reflect the anxieties and aspirations of a generation coming of age under colonial rule, seeking meaning and direction through diverse cultural resources. The specific choices of books, music, and films also map the available cultural imports and local productions shaping their worldview.

『大地』
제1부에 있어서 王龍의 안해가 한 말 『그만 두세요, 내 목숨은 그런 값이 없어요, 그만한 돈이 있으면 훌륭한 땅을 살 수 있는데요』
『그건 안 돼요, 나는 죽어요. 어느 때나 죽어요. 땅만은 내가 죽어도 남아 있어요』 여기서 支那농민의 토지에 대한 생각을 알 수 있고 이 곳 백성과 비슷한 심경이 아닐까 생각한다. 소설이라고 하기 보다 支那사회의 해부도래도 과언이 아닐게다.
제2부에 있어서 구세대와 신세대의 충돌이 드러났고 신세대에 동감을 不禁하다.
제3부 새로 일어나는 中國과 당시 청년들의 동향을 瀝瀝히 볼 수 있었다. 전형적인 支那人 王淵과 美齡 이곳에도 흔한 王生, 愛蘭 같은 허영에 사는 인물, 다혈질인 王猛-- 모두다 좋은 대조였고 전3부 중 가장 젊은 마음을 이끌었다.

李泰俊씨의 문학 예술을 생각할 때 먼저 우리들 머리에 떠오르는 것이 있으니 그것은 그의 문장과 사상성이다.

여기서 독자는 작가 李泰俊씨 문학예술의 배후에 흐르고 있는 예의 애수적인 것을 찾일 수 있을는지도 모른다.

『부르크劇場』 (조셉라이나-의 情熱)
「싼타크로스영감이 꿈에 나타나 『너는 무슨 푸레센트를 받고 싶으며 또 무슨 푸레센트를 세상 사람들게 주겼니?』하고 묻는다면 나는 즉석에서 『정열의 팡, 정열의 팡, 그리고 또 내가 세상 사람들게 줄 것도 정열의 팡 정열의 팡이외다』하고 대답하리라」
이것은 내 일기책 중 신년 각오의 一節이다. 웨냐면? 정열은 위대한 사업을 위대한 사랑을 위대한 발명을 낳아 놓기 때문이다. 다시 말하면 정열은 곧 그들의 산모이기 때문이다. 여기 만일 정열이 없는 자가 있다면 그는 입비투리 절룸바리도 날 수 없는 산모요 또 중간에 가서 정열을 버리는 자가 있다면 그는 반듯이 낙태하고 유산할 산모가 될 터이다.
그대들이나 나나 또는 우리 하라버지들이 나의 가장 무서운 병은 정열빈혈병이오 정열遺棄病이라고 나는 믿는다. 여기에 무슨 위대한 역사가 건축되며 화려한 예술이 꾸며지랴? 하아 나는 단연코 정열의 팡, 정열의 팡이라고 연겹퍼 부르짖고 싶었든 것이다.
그렇든 정열의 한 전형적 표현을 나는 부르크극장 라이나-청년에게서 찾었든 것이다. 부르크극장에 나아가 세계적 名優가 되려는 라이나-의 정열, 지진이라도 일쿨 듯한 정열, 태양이라도 녹일 듯한 정열, 거이 광적인 정열(사실 나는 라이나-를 대할 때마다 주먹을 부루 쥐고 어느새 共鳴상태가 되였든 것을 고백한다)는 무엇을 낳아 놓았는가? 「로미오와 주리엣」을 연습할 때 제- 밧하부인=밋테라를 연모한 여인, 처음에는 라이너-를 대단 불쾌히 역였다=은 일직선으로 사랑에 끌여 들어갔고 밋테라가 자기의 경험을 통하야 그의 야망을 냉소로서 거절할 때 그는 부르짖었다. 『차라리 무대에서 살지 못하면 차라리 무대에서 죽으리라』고 이 얼마나 무서운 정열인지! 그가 후일에 세계적 名優가 된 것은 필연적 결과일 것이다.
이 영화의 주제인 밋테라의 심리, 50평생을 名望과 허영에 둘려쌓여 살아온 그가 다시 참된 인간으로서 참된 사람으로서 재출발하려는 심리. 이는 물론 감탄사를 놓고는 볼 수 없었다. 허나 그가 벌서 인간적인 야망을 버리고 名望을 저주하고 꺼리는데 나는 오-직 권태와 쇠퇴를 느꼈을 뿐이다. 여기에 비해 라이나-의 정열, 눈을 감어도 눈을 감어도 달여드는 라이나-의 정열은 아모리 높이 평가하여도 부족하리라. 라이나-로 분장한 아이히벨가의(168頁에 계속)<185> 연기도 대단 좋았다.
사진관을 나와 나는 내 스스로 굳게 盟誓하였든 것이다. 현대라는 분위기를 우리(시대의 주인공)로서 除할 때 회색의 우울과 불안과 실망의 답이 나온다는 것은 틀림없는 일이다. 허나 우리는 그대로 받어디려 그대로 반사하는 거울이여서는 않된다. 보다도 우리는 그대들을 붓잡고 위대한 건축을 향해 다려가는 정열의 드라이버- 라이나-같은 정열을 가진 드라이버-가 되여야 한다고. (次回는 京城 帝國 大學 文科, 哲學科 學生篇)<168>
