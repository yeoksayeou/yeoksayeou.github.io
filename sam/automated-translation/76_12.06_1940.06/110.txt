Article Title	上海朝鮮婦人團 「故國山河」 訪問記 (Account of the Shanghai Korean Women's Group's Visit to the "Homeland's Mountains and Rivers")
Article Type	대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

Account of the Shanghai Korean Women's Group's Visit to the "Homeland's Mountains and Rivers"

Four Korean women residing in Shanghai, some born in Central China places like Hankow or Kiukiang and having never seen the scenery of their homeland, recently visited Seoul for the first time since the Incident [Second Sino-Japanese War began]. They stepped onto the longed-for, welcome land of their dreams, filling their eyes with the sights. Our company invited these four women one evening for a chat, and we convey their boundless words to our readers.

Nostalgia and International Marriage Romance

Reporter - I am endlessly pleased to host you ladies, who are visiting the homeland for the first time in decades or since birth, and to share our mutual feelings. You must be tired from the long journey, but we would be grateful if you could speak frankly about the nostalgia you felt for the homeland while in a foreign land, the various sentiments you experienced upon visiting your native mountains and rivers, other impressions, and the situation of fellow Koreans residing in Shanghai. But first, please tell us the purpose of this inspection tour.
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - This "Shanghai Korean Women's Inspection Team" was organized jointly by the Shanghai Korean Residents' Association and the Government-General of Chosŏn's Shanghai Office, with the support of the Shanghai Japanese Embassy's Central China Police Affairs Department. The purpose of the inspection is to select women belonging to the most influential leading class among Koreans residing in Shanghai, dispatch those who have not inspected the situation in Chosŏn and Naichi [Japan] for a long time, have them observe the conditions in Chosŏn and Naichi which are undergoing rapid development, and thereby contribute to the thorough guidance and enlightenment of the general compatriots residing in Shanghai.
Reporter - How many years has it been since you all left Chosŏn?
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - I graduated from Kyŏnggi High School for Girls 22 years ago and went to Shanghai, and I haven't set foot in the homeland once during that time. So, after longing for Chosŏn with constant nostalgia while abroad for so long, to encounter my homeland's mountains and rivers like this—the joy is indescribable. Truly, it has changed unrecognizably in the meantime, and there have been many transformations.
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - I went to Shanghai 14 years ago and have always lived in the French Concession, where I still live now. My hometown is P'yŏngyang, so I graduated from P'yŏngyang's Sungŭi Girls' School. I did visit Chosŏn once, 6 years ago. Coming now, I see that the Chosŏn of 6 years ago and the Chosŏn of today are different again.
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - I was born in Hankow, and Kiukiang is my hometown. I have never once been to Chosŏn until now;<77> this is my first time. Although I am Chinese, my husband is Korean, so I think of Chosŏn as my homeland and have always yearned for Chosŏn. Wondering when I would ever get to see Chosŏn, I longed for the unknown homeland, and now that I encounter it, I am truly pleased beyond measure. Moreover, meeting my husband's relatives whom I had never met before, receiving their overflowing love, and having my husband's relatives and friends from his hometown of Sŏnch'ŏn follow me all the way to Kyŏngsŏng, sparing no time to show me around for many days and giving me excessive hospitality—I cannot express how I feel. I thought they might not welcome a foreign woman so warmly, but to receive such overflowing love beyond my expectations is truly moving beyond words. Indeed, the affection with which they treat me, transcending national borders with love greater and deeper than even my own parents and siblings, brings tears to my eyes.
Reporter - How did you come to marry a Korean person? Please tell us the romance of your marriage days.
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - Oh my, is there really any romance to speak of?
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - Ms. Yu Kŭk-kyŏm and I have been close friends for a long time, and moreover, I am like a real sister to her husband, Mr. Ch'a Kyun-ch'an, so I will speak. Mr. Ch'a is a graduate of Shanghai's Hujiang University and after graduation, he got a job and went to Nanchang. At that time, Mrs. Yu had graduated from an American mission school, and they met in Nanchang and fell passionately in love. Afterwards, when Ms. Yu tried to get her parents' permission to marry, her parents and older brothers vehemently opposed it. They strongly objected, saying, how could she marry a foreigner, especially a Korean? But Ms. Yu did not yield in the slightest and held her wedding ceremony in scenic Lushan, where Chiang Kai-shek is said to have a villa. Of course, neither her beloved mother, nor her older brothers, nor a single relative came to the ceremony. Ms. Yu's father had passed away early, and she had her mother, two older brothers, and two younger brothers;<78> her family was quite well-off in Kiukiang. Even after the marriage, she remained cut off from her relatives, and they refused to meet her even when her first child was born. Then, on the baby's first birthday, her mother came to visit, regretted her prejudice, and said, "Since I couldn't even provide the wedding feast, let me at least do it for the baby's birthday." And she held a grand birthday feast.
Reporter - Next, Ms. Uetsuki, please tell us how you came to marry a Korean person.
Uetsuki - I was born in British Hong Kong and have never been to Naichi or Chosŏn until now; like Mrs. Yu, this is my first time. My husband, Mr. Chang Tuch'ŏl, was originally in Shanghai; he was a talented man working at a bank run by Westerners. Then he was transferred and came to Hong Kong, and we got married there.
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - Ms. Uetsuki speaks Korean extremely well. After they got married long ago, they made it a rule to only speak Korean at home, so the children all speak Korean well too.
Uetsuki - Why does everyone call me Uetsuki? As soon as I got married, I took my husband's surname Chang, and until now, I've been called Chang both at home and outside. Coming to Chosŏn and suddenly being called Uetsuki feels a bit awkward and unnatural. (Laughter)

Education Issues and Other Matters for Compatriots Residing in Shanghai

Reporter - Currently, about how many Korean compatriots live in Shanghai?
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - There are about 7,000 Koreans, and if you include people from Naichi [Japanese], I think it would be around 70,000. Probably before long, it will reach 100,000.
Reporter - How is the education for Koreans in Shanghai? How many schools are run by Koreans, and how many schools are run by people from Naichi?
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - There used to be one elementary school run by Koreans, but it closed before the Incident, so currently, there are no<79> schools run by Koreans at all. That's why Korean children's education is a problem. After the Incident, 3 or 4 Naichi elementary schools were established in the Hongkou area, which is very good as many send their children there, but Shanghai is such a vast place that only those near Hongkou can commute. Those in the French Concession or other concessions cannot commute. The distance is far, and although public order is maintained by the Imperial Army, there are still lawless elements (不逞 分子), and incidents of people going missing occur. Besides that, there are ruffians (無賴漢) who engage in kidnapping, making it very dangerous to send children to school from the British and French Concessions.
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - I also live in the French Concession, so because of the danger, I cannot send my children to the Naichi elementary schools in Hongkou where many Naichi people live. Instead, I send them to a French school. And even if I could send them, they are now all at the age to graduate elementary school, and having learned only English or French without knowing the national language (Naichi language), it would be difficult for both the Naichi elementary school side and the transferred student if they were to transfer. Therefore, the authorities also allow only those under 12 to transfer, while those over 12 continue attending foreign schools.
Reporter - How are the prices?
Uetsuki - Of course, they have risen considerably compared to before the Incident.
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - Even though prices have risen, if you have money, you can buy as much rice or other goods as you want. It's not that you can't buy them or anything like that.
Reporter - While you were in Shanghai, what kind of rumors were circulating about the situation in Chosŏn?
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - What we heard in Shanghai was that Chosŏn and Naichi were experiencing severe material shortages and were in quite a state of chaos. But now that we've actually come, it's completely different from the rumors. It doesn't feel like a country at war at all, and although it's not that there's absolutely no shortage of things like rice, it's different from what we heard. When we return, we will widely convey this actual situation inside Chosŏn to the resident compatriots so they can be reassured.<80>

Shanghai During and After the Incident

Reporter - Where were you all during the recent Incident?
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - I was in Nanchang at that time. After Shanghai and Nanjing fell, Japanese planes started bombing Hankow and Nanchang, causing chaos in the cities with fires and casualties. We also fled to Hong Kong by plane. My eldest brother has now returned to Hankow, and my second older brother is in Szechwan Province, Chiang Kai-shek's base behind Ch'ungch'ing. My mother and younger siblings evacuated to Hong Kong and are still living there. We had a house and land in our hometown Kiukiang, but we abandoned it all and left. My husband and I escaped Nanchang by plane, went to Hong Kong, and after some time, returned to Shanghai by a foreign steamship. Truly, thinking back to that time still gives me chills.
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - I was living in the French Concession then too, so it wasn't that dangerous, but during the battle for Shanghai, when dozens of Imperial Army planes and Chinese planes took to the sky for a major air battle, stray bullets constantly fell even in the British and French Concessions, causing many casualties. So, in the French Concession, the French police completely forbade citizens from going outside, and for a while, we lived as if confined to jail. And even if we went out into the garden in the evening, the sound of the rain of bullets popping like beans and the glow from the fierce fires burning in the Hongkou direction brightly illuminated even the front yard of our house in the French Concession. It was truly terrifying and tremendous.
Reporter - What is the attitude of the Chinese people after the Incident? Please tell us about their attitude towards Koreans compared to before, and their attitude towards Mr. Wang Ching-wei's new government.
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - Of course, initially, the Chinese people held misunderstandings about the Empire, but now they have become aware of the Empire's stance, and many cooperate in the construction of the New East Asia, supporting and welcoming Mr. Wang Ching-wei.<81>
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - Before the Incident, Chinese people generally mocked and insulted Koreans, calling them "*wangk'ŏllu* (왕껄루)." Whether in elementary school or university, students would mock Korean students calling them "*wangk'ŏllu*," and ordinary Chinese people also always insulted us by calling us "*wangk'ŏllu*," using "*wangk'ŏllu*" almost like a proper noun for Koreans. It was incredibly unpleasant, but now you can't hear that sound even if you try. And they've become quite kind; it's completely different from before.

The Beauty of Chosŏn's Scenery and Clothing

Reporter - Especially Ms. Uetsuki and Ms. Yu Kŭk-kyŏm, how do you find Chosŏn now that you've seen it for the first time? Please tell us about the scenery, the clothing of Korean women, Korean customs, and other things.
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - It's truly a place with beautiful mountains and rivers, quite different from what I imagined while in China. It is truly regrettable that I couldn't see Mt. Kŭmgang, which I had set my heart on seeing this time. I was supposed to go to Mt. Kŭmgang with the other three ladies, but I thought it more proper to go to my husband's hometown, Sŏnch'ŏn, to meet and greet my husband's relatives whom I was meeting for the first time. So, I put aside the famed Mt. Kŭmgang and went to Sŏnch'ŏn alone. Furthermore, since his elderly mother lives there, I felt it would be presumptuous for me to wait in Kyŏngsŏng for her to come, so even though I don't know Korean or the geography well, I went to Sŏnch'ŏn first to find her. I consider this more joyful than going to Mt. Kŭmgang.
And when I first arrived in P'yŏngyang, I saw the clothing of Korean women and was truly surprised by its simple beauty. I had seen it occasionally in Shanghai, but those were clothes worn by older women, so they looked very unbecoming. However, the clothes of the young women and female students I first saw in P'yŏngyang looked truly beautiful. They seemed like Western-style (洋裂 같으면서도) yet not extravagant (奓侈하지 않고), fitting the body snugly, their simplicity<82> and utmost economy and plainness truly seemed better than Western clothes or Chinese clothes. Seeing them this time in Chosŏn, I felt the urge to wear Korean clothes myself. My mother-in-law followed me to Kyŏngsŏng this time and said she would send me Korean clothes as a gift for Christmas, one set each for me and the baby. If she sends them, I will wear them.
Uetsuki - I too was amazed that Chosŏn's mountains and rivers are more beautiful than I had heard. Especially going to Mt. Kŭmgang, I cannot find words to describe its superb scenery (絶勝). I don't want to go back to Shanghai; I want to live here forever. If I save money, I will definitely come to Chosŏn to live. And I saw Korean women's clothing for the first time on this trip too, and it's very nice.
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - This time in P'yŏngyang, the P'yŏngyang Prefectural Governor held a banquet and invited us. Saying we probably hadn't seen Korean customs, he called first-rate kisaeng to dance and sing, but I didn't find that particularly interesting. And I greatly enjoy Korean food. I eat kimchi very well and am quite fond of red pepper powder and gochujang. Since my time in Shanghai, I yearned for the food of my husband's homeland and cultivated the habit of eating kimchi and gochujang often in Shanghai, so now I can eat them quite well, not losing to anyone.

Will You Not Seek Chosŏn in Old Age?

Reporter - Do you intend to end your lives in Shanghai? Or will you seek your homeland's mountains and rivers in your old age?
Uetsuki - Of course, after saving money, I intend to come to Chosŏn and spend the rest of my life here.
Yu Kŭk-kyŏm - Although my language skills are insufficient and customs may be unfamiliar, after returning to Shanghai, I want to learn the language and spend my old age in Chosŏn. Right now, I can roughly understand Korean, but I cannot speak it. If I study hard, I'll become proficient, right? (Continued on page 59)<83>
Kim Wŏn-gyŏng - Of course, I will return to Chosŏn and bury my white bones (백골을 파묻지요) in Chosŏn soil. This was my first time seeing Mt. Kŭmgang too, and I had never seen such beautiful scenery before.
Yi Ŭn-yŏng - Even though I live in Shanghai now, I too will save money, come find Chosŏn, and spend the rest of my life in Chosŏn. How can I abandon a land with such magnificent mountains and rivers like Mt. Kŭmgang and go live elsewhere?
Reporter - Thank you for taking time out of your busy schedules to share many wonderful stories with us. (End)

**Summary**

This 1940 roundtable discussion features four Korean women residing in Shanghai (two Korean-born, one Chinese-born married to a Korean, one Hong Kong-born married to a Korean) visiting Korea, some for the first time. They discuss their impressions of a rapidly changing Korea under Japanese rule, comparing it favorably to rumors of wartime hardship heard in Shanghai. They share experiences of nostalgia, international marriage (including facing family opposition), the challenges of Korean children's education in Shanghai due to lack of Korean schools and safety concerns outside Japanese-controlled areas, and encounters with anti-Korean sentiment (*wangk'ŏllu*) from Chinese people pre-war, which they note has subsided. They express admiration for Korea's scenery (especially Mt. Kŭmgang) and the simple beauty of Korean clothing, with most expressing a desire to return to Korea permanently in their old age.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing about the lives of diasporic Koreans in Shanghai during the Sino-Japanese War, highlighting complex identities and allegiances. It showcases the official sponsorship of the tour by Japanese and Korean colonial authorities, framing it as a way to promote a positive image of colonial Korea and Japan ("guidance and enlightenment") to overseas Koreans. The women's comments on improved Sino-Korean relations post-Japanese occupation of parts of Shanghai, the contrast between rumors and reality regarding wartime conditions in Korea/Japan, and the practical difficulties faced by Koreans in the international city provide valuable insights into the period. The casual mention of Japanese military presence and control (e.g., "Imperial Army," "Hongkou area") alongside descriptions of daily life underscores the wartime context.

기사제목	上海朝鮮婦人團 「故國山河」 訪問記
기사형태	대담·좌담

上海朝鮮婦人團 「故國山河」 訪問記
漢口, 혹은 九江 같은 中支에서 출생하여 한번도 故土 풍물을 찾아보지 못한 上海있는 朝鮮夫人 네 분이 이번에 사변 후 처음으로 서울을 찾아 와서 滿目에 그립고도 반가운 夢昧의 땅을 밟고 갔다. 본사에서는 一夕, 이 네 분을 청하여 담소하면서 그 不盡하는 말씀을 들어 독자에 전하노라.

鄕愁와 國際結婚로맨스

기  자 - 몇 십년만에 혹은 출생 후 처음으로 故土를 찾아 주시는 여러분을 모시고 피차의 感懷를 나누게 됨을 기뻐하여 마지않습니다. 遠路에 피로하실 터이오나 異域에서 故土를 그리워하시던 鄕愁와 고향 산천을<76> 찾아 오셔서 느끼신 여러 가지의 情懷, 기타의 감상과 및 上海在住동포의 상황 등을 기탄없이 말씀해 주시면 고맙겠습니다. 그런데 爲先 今次의 시찰 목적부터 말씀해 주셔요.
金元慶 - 금번 우리 「在上海朝鮮부인시찰단」을 上海 居留 朝鮮人會와 朝鮮총독부 上海사무소의 공동 주최로 上海 日本대사관 中支경무부의 후원을 받아서 조직되었습니다. 시찰 목적은 上海 在留 朝鮮人 중 가장 유력 지도 계급에 속하는 부인들을 선발하여 오랫동안 朝鮮과 內地실정을 시찰한 일이 없는 자들을 파견하여서 비약적 발전을 하고 있는 朝鮮 及 內地의 상황을 시찰케 하여써 上海 일반 재류 동포로 하여금 선도 교화의 철저에 資코저 함이외다.
기  자 - 여러분께서 朝鮮을 떠나신지는 몇 해나 되십니까.
金元慶 - 저는 22년 전에 京畿高女를 졸업하고 上海로 갔었으며 그 동안 한 번도 故土를 밟아 본 일이 없어요. 그래서 오랫동안 외국에서 늘 鄕愁를 갖고 朝鮮을 그리워 하다가 이렇게 고향 산천을 대하게 되니 그 기쁨이란 이로 형용할 수가 없습니다. 참 그 동안 몰라보게 달라젔으며 변천도 많았군요.
李恩泳 - 저는 14년 전에 上海에 갔었는데 그 동안 늘 佛蘭西 租界에서 살았으며 지금도 거기에서 삽니다. 제 고향이 平壤이기 때문에 平壤崇義女學校를 졸업했어요. 그리고 6년 전에 한 번 朝鮮에 왔다간 일이 있습니다. 지금 와 보니 6년 전의 朝鮮과 지금의 朝鮮이 또 달라요.
