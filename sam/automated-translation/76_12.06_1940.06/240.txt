**Body Text Translation:**
Chronicle of "Favorite Works"

Survey 1. Authors and Works avidly read in one's youth, around age 20.
2. Authors and Works avidly read in young adulthood, around age 30.
3. Authors and Works avidly read in middle age, around age 40.

Yi Ki-yŏng

Respectfully submitted, my answers to your questions are as follows:
1. Around age 20, I avidly read Ch'unwŏn's "Mujŏng" (Heartlessness) and "Kaech'ŏkcha" (The Pioneer), as well as Yuktang's "Ch'ŏngch'un" (Youth) magazine and the "T'aesŏ Munye Sinbo" (Western Literature News).
2. Around age 30, mainly works by Nordic authors.
3. Around age 40, works from world literature collections.

Kim Ŏk

1. It is difficult to pin down exact ages. However, the works or authors from that time were Wilde, Verlaine, and Baudelaire. Occasionally, I also read Turgenev's works. I most enjoyed reading Andersen's "The Improvisatore" (鄕異詩人).
2. Chekhov's short stories, Tagore's poetry collections, Molière's dramatic works. In any case, if it was mysticism, I loved it to the end; if not, I loved realistic beauty.
3. I read more Eastern works than Western ones, and the more I read, the more I found that Eastern works not only had more points to make one think but also seemed more familiar. This is why I abandoned Western poetry. I have truly come to realize now that I do not know the real taste of Western poetry.<169>

Pak Yŏng-hŭi

Around age 20, I read a lot of poetry. Goethe, Byron, Shelley, Baudelaire, Poe, Symons, etc. Various European and American poems.
Around age 30, philosophy, economics, historical materialism, etc.
Around age 40, I read even more on religion, history, etc.
How can I list all the works and authors one by one? I read a lot of poetry by Poe and Baudelaire in my youth, reread "The Sorrows of Young Werther" several times, and when I was a bit older, I avidly read "Les Misérables," and then various works by Turgenev, Chekhov, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Galsworthy, Balzac, etc.
Recently, being busy with many affairs (有事多忙), I haven't been able to read many novels and have only managed to read "Peking Picnic" (北京의 날).

Mo Yun-suk

Around 20: Ch'unwŏn ("Mujŏng," "Kaech'ŏkcha," "Chaesaeng" [Regeneration]), Tolstoy ("Resurrection," "Anna Karenina," "War and Peace"), Anatole France ("Thaïs"), Chekhov ("The Cherry Orchard"), Dostoevsky ("Crime and Punishment"), Tagore ("Gitanjali" [롱산직이 - likely a reference to Gitanjali or similar]).
Around 30: Balzac ("Lily of the Valley"), George Sand ("Indiana"), Pearl Buck ("The Good Earth").

Kim Ki-jin

I answer your questions. The reading process around age 20 was: 1st period - Goethe's "The Sorrows of Werther," Byron's "Poems," Dumas' "The Count of Monte Cristo," Roka's "The Cuckoo" (不如歸); 2nd period - Verlaine, Baudelaire, Symons, Verhaeren; 3rd period - various works by Dostoevsky, Turgenev, Henri Barbusse, Nakanishi Inosuke; around age 30, it was unified by works of domestic and foreign proletarian authors; now, just before 40, I am not avidly reading any particular work, just occasionally reading this and that unsystematically (散讀).

Ham Tae-hun

Around age 20:
Hardy's "Tess of the d'Urbervilles" (likely refers to Tess, "앨리챠의 日記" might be a misunderstanding or specific translation), H.G. Wells' "The Country of the Blind," Victor Hugo's "Les Misérables." I read whatever came to hand. I just read anything interesting (滋味).
Around age 30:
I mainly read works by Russian authors. Various works by the humanitarian Tolstoy, various works by the disillusioned and sorrowful Chekhov, various works by the nihilistic Artsybashev, etc. During this period, I read mainly with an attitude of literary research.
Age 40 is not far off (不遠), and for several years I have read Dostoevsky, Baudelaire, Schnitzler.<170> At such times, lately (요지음), I feel that life is ultimately futile and morbid, and I read such kinds of writings here and there.
Last year, I liked Nietzsche's philosophy of power, but starting this year, for some reason, I want to read writings close to nihilistic disillusionment. At the same time, I also want to write them.

Yi Sŏk-hun

1. Around age 20, I never dreamed of becoming a writer, and therefore did not read literary works even as a hobby. At that time, as was the style, I avidly read social sciences.
2. I mainly read Chekhov. There was also a time when I felt infinite pleasure in reciting Turgenev's prose poems in the original language, though very clumsily. (This is presumptuous talk, but understanding the meaning is different from generally knowing the taste, so I speak in the latter sense.)
3. ...

Ŏm Hŭng-sŏp

1. Heine's poetry collection, Ishikawa Takuboku's poetry collection, Kunikida Doppo's short stories, Ch'unwŏn's early works, Tolstoy's "Resurrection," Goethe's "The Sorrows of Young Werther," etc. etc.
2. Dostoevsky, Maxim Gorky, Turgenev's novels and Chekhov, Maupassant, Gogol's short stories.
3. As I am still under 40, I will postpone this for a later date.

An Hoe-nam

I cannot point to any single individual, but around age 20, I read many works by mainland (內地 - i.e., Japanese) authors. However, about 5 or 6 years ago, I cut off all ties with them. The reason is that I do not receive even a fingernail's worth of literary benefit. Now, I am solely engrossed in reading the major works of the West. In the future, I will try to delve into Tolstoy.

Kye Yong-muk

I cannot list here all the works I avidly read during those times, but I will mention only those I haven't forgotten to this day.
1. Tolstoy's "Resurrection," Artsybashev's "Sanin" (싸-닝), D'Annunzio's "Triumph of Death," Andreyev's "To the Stars" (별의 世界 - likely translation), "The Life of Man," "He Who Gets Slapped," Kurata Hyakuzō's "The Priest and His Disciples" (出家와 그 弟子), Mushanokōji Saneatsu's "Long Live Mankind" (人間萬歲).
2. Kaiser's "From Morn to Midnight," Sorge's "The Beggar" (乞食), Toller's "Mass Man" (群集人間), "The Machine Wreckers" (機械破壞者), "Hinkemann" (獨逸男 힝케만), Turgenev's "Fathers and Sons," Dostoevsky's "Crime and Punishment," Ibsen's "Pillars of Society" (社會의 支柱), "The Wild Duck" (野鴨), "An Enemy of the People" (民衆의 敵).<171> "Brand" (푸란드), Strindberg's "To Damascus," Maeterlinck's "The Death of Tintagiles" (단다지일의 死), "The Blue Bird" (파랑새).
3. Chekhov's "Kashtanka" (붉은 洋襪 - lit. Red Western Socks, likely a specific translation title for Kashtanka or another story), "The Cherry Orchard," various short stories by Anatole France. Ishikawa Tatsuzō's "Sea Fog" (海霧), Hakuden Gyūdō's (薄田泣董 - likely Hakuda Kyūkin/Susukida Kyūkin) "Praise of the Earth" (大地讚頌) (essay), Yi Hyo-sŏk's "The Field" (들), Tanizaki Jun'ichirō's "Tattoo" (靑刺).

Kim Ki-rim

1. Ch'oe Sŏ-hae, his "T'alch'ulgi" (Escape Log).
2. Lawrence, his "Sons and Lovers."

Kim Kwang-sŏp

1. Around age 20, I mainly read works by Byron and Hardy.
2. Around age 30, I avidly read Lawrence, Shelley, and besides them, poems by countless major and minor poets.

Yi Kwang-su

1. Around age 20, I read all the works of Tolstoy and Natsume Sōseki.
2. Around age 30, besides reading the Bible daily, I did not particularly avidly read any literary books.
3. Around age 42 or 43, I enjoyed reading the complete works of Shakespeare and Greek dramatic poems. Since then, I have consistently been reading Buddhist scriptures.

No Cha-yŏng

Around my 20s, I liked to read Heine's poetry collection, Baudelaire's "Les Fleurs du mal" (惡의 華), "The Sorrows of Werther." Around age 30, I liked Tolstoy's "Anna Karenina," Turgenev's "Spring Torrents" (春波), "Fathers and Sons" (畑氣 is likely a typo/misprint for 父子), and also avidly read Artsybashev's "Sanin" and "At the Brink" (最後의 一線). Recently, I enjoy essay collections like Pascal's "Pensées" (小品集 - collection of small pieces) and Montaigne's "Essais" (隨想錄).

Yi Hyo-sŏk

1. Shelley, Yeats, etc., and their works.
2. Balzac, etc., and his novels.

Kim Nae-sŏng

1. Around 20: Shimada Seijirō's "On the Ground" (地上), Shakespeare's "Romeo and Juliet," Dumas' "The Count of Monte Cristo," various works by Tanizaki Jun'ichirō.
Other domestic and foreign detective novels in general.
2. Around 30:
I haven't particularly avidly read any works.

Pak Chong-hwa

1. Byron, Heine, Goethe, Verlaine – I mainly read their poetry.
2. I learned calligraphy and read the collected works of past scholars (先人들의 文集).
3. I tried as much as possible to read about the path trodden by the Practical Learning (實學派) school within Korean studies (朝鮮學),<172> but I barely grasp the outline faintly.

Chang Hyŏk-chu

1. Mérimée, Turgenev. Kikuchi Kan, Akutagawa Ryūnosuke, Tanizaki Jun'ichirō, and several others.
2. Dostoevsky, Balzac.
Joyce, Gide, Izumi Kyōka, Nagai Kafū, and several others.
3. My age is still insufficient, but I plan to reread Eastern and Western classics (古曲).

Chŏng In-t'aek

1. Various works by Hugo, Tolstoy, Gorky, Akutagawa Ryūnosuke. I especially enjoyed reading "Les Misérables."
2. Nietzsche's "Thus Spoke Zarathustra." Guyau's "Art from a Sociological Point of View" (社會學上으로 본 藝術).
3. I plan to savor (味讀) Korean classics.

Yi Hŏn-gu

1. Until age 20: Yi Kwang-su's "Mujŏng," Tolstoy's "Resurrection," Goethe's "The Sorrows of Werther," etc.
2. From 20 to 30: Romain Rolland's "Jean-Christophe," Stendhal's "The Red and the Black," Gide's "Strait is the Gate" (좁은 門), etc.
3. From 30 to today: Huxley's "Point Counter Point," Ève Curie's "Madame Curie," Roger Martin du Gard's "The Thibaults" (티보오家의 사람들).

Ch'ae Man-sik

1. "Ch'unhyangjŏn" (Tale of Ch'unhyang), "Simch'ŏngjŏn" (Tale of Sim Ch'ŏng), "Kuunmong" (The Cloud Dream of the Nine), "Ongnumong" (The Jade Pavilion Dream), "Samgukchi" (Romance of the Three Kingdoms), "Suhogi" (Water Margin), etc., were from my youth. When my eyes truly opened to literature, it was modern Russian writers, especially Turgenev's short stories, and also Zola, Flaubert, etc.
2. Greek classic drama, Ch'unwŏn's various works, especially "Hŭk" (Soil) and "Chaesaeng" (Regeneration).
3. Balzac, Hyo-sŏk's various short stories, and these days, I'm enjoying reading du Gard's "The Thibaults."

Kim Tong-in

1. 20 years ago, the mainland (內地 - Japanese) literary scene was bustlingly adopting Russian literature, it was the golden age of Russian literature. At that time, caught up in the trend, I also read works by various writers like Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Turgenev, Chekhov, etc., randomly as I came across them.
2. Around age 30, I read many books related to Ancient Korean History.
3. Currently, around age 40, I mostly read the political and social pages of newspapers, and sometimes magazines, but otherwise, there are hardly any books I read.

Sin Sŏk-chŏng

I answer the survey.
1. I most avidly read Tagore and Turgenev.
2. I have dabbled a bit in Laotzu/Taoist philosophy (老花철학 - likely 老莊哲学, Taoist philosophy).
3. I will answer after it arrives.<173>

**Summary**

This 1940 survey published in *Samch'ŏlli* magazine chronicles the reading habits of prominent Korean intellectuals and writers across different stages of their lives (around ages 20, 30, and 40). The responses reveal a strong initial influence of Western literature, particularly Russian (Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Turgenev, Chekhov), French (Hugo, Balzac, Baudelaire), and British/German (Shakespeare, Byron, Goethe, Shelley) authors, alongside influential Korean (Yi Kwang-su/Ch'unwŏn) and some Japanese writers (Natsume Sōseki, Akutagawa Ryūnosuke). As they aged, some shifted towards philosophy, social sciences, history (including Korean history and classics like *Sirhak*), religious texts (Bible, Buddhist scriptures), or developed more focused literary interests (e.g., proletarian literature, specific authors like D.H. Lawrence). The survey reflects the cosmopolitan literary exposure of the Korean elite during the colonial period, their evolving intellectual interests, and a noticeable trend among some towards rediscovering Eastern or Korean traditions later in life. Historians might find the specific authors and works cited revealing about international literary trends influencing Korea, the formation of Korean modern literary tastes under Japanese rule, the intellectual trajectories of key figures, and the subtle turn towards national heritage (Korean classics, history) by some amidst colonial assimilation pressures.
