季節風
						아구네쓰·뉴-톤·케이쓰 作 (By Agnes Newton Keith)

Regarding 『季節風』 (Seasonal Wind)
This novel won last year's "Atlantic Monthly" magazine Reader's Award ($5,000). The author, Agnes Newton Keith, is an American who married an Englishman, an American forest conservator stationed in North Borneo. Simultaneously with her marriage, she moved to her husband's post, spending about four years in the tropical land of Borneo under the sweltering seasonal winds. She abruptly transitioned from modern America with its skyscrapers (摩天樓) to a primitive life adjacent to the jungle. This book captures her unique experiences as accurately as a camera and as sensitively as a microphone.
Elephants appear and disappear in the forest one mile (一哩) ahead, and orangutans roam freely. Despite being in such a remote village (蕃地), the British adhere to old customs (舊習) like afternoon tea at 4 o'clock, and the laundryman is from Singapore (씬가포-ᄅ). The author's heart, laughing at various contradictions, loving the scenery, and simply trying to understand Borneo, overflows with honesty and purity. Particularly, the record of her journey deep into the forest region following her husband depicts the tearful emotions of a wife.
However, despite dealing with unique material, there is none of the deliberate attempts at humor often found in books of this kind, nor any falsehoods. <223>

1
My husband, while working as an administrator for 14 years, also sent rare animals to various zoos around the world, raised some himself, contributed to scientific journals, and had thoroughly made the Borneo life his own, becoming quite proficient in the Malay language (馬來語).
Since we both attended American schools, my older friend (옵바 - likely referring to her husband as a childhood friend) and I were old acquaintances. He came to visit my home just before returning to Borneo—it was 1934.
And so, three months later, I boarded a ship bound for Borneo as his wife.
When the shadow of the continent finally disappeared beyond the horizon,<222> I turned to my husband and said,
"I can never forget this moment. I really think it was the right decision. It's not just that we're together, it's truly just the two of us. No matter how well we get along or how long we live together, I don't think I could ever experience this feeling again."
"Yes."
My husband nodded and then replied with a serious face,
"I hope you still feel the same way after four years in Borneo."
"It's not thinking, it's feeling."
"Yes, that's true, but..."
"Are you saying my feelings will change?"
"That's not it."
My husband said, gently patting my shoulder.
"You'll still be surprised when you get there."
A sweltering life in the dense jungle, where even the branches were hidden under the seasonal wind, awaited us newlyweds.
However, even now, five years later, the feeling of that moment remains unchanged in my heart.
"What did you say, Harry?"
I feel victorious recalling my smiling words to my husband's solemn face.<223>

2
Now, North Borneo, under British protection (保護), has a population of 270,000, is located southwest of the Philippines (휘이맆퓐), and requires a six-week sea voyage from America. There isn't much of a climate to speak of, but in a word, it's a climate that makes moving bothersome.
"Oof."
One cannot get up from a chair without uttering such a sound. That is my husband Harry's post (任地).
Our house is in Sandakan, the capital of this region along the east coast. It has a population of 14,000, of which only 75 are Europeans (歐洲人). The rest are native Borneo peoples (土着의 土民), Filipinos (휘이맆퓐人), Malays (馬來人), East Indians (東印度人), Japanese (日本人), etc.
World-touring (周遊) journalists ignored Sandakan, and few people stayed for long. Moreover, with only 17 miles (哩) of roads in total, the only things to see are the Golf Club (꼴프歐樂部), the wireless station, plantations, and the Sandakan Club (싼다칸 俱樂部). A 45-minute car ride covers all the sights, so visitors probably feel no need to linger. Therefore, the Borneo stories written by such travelers are often baseless nonsense; tales of adventure set against the jungle backdrop are frequently written carelessly. Because they write without seeing...
In truth, even in this remote land (蕃地), we live according to European customs, like a picture from an English tea magazine, maintaining a European lifestyle within our small club, drinking tea at 4 p.m. Occasionally, elephants appear (出沒) on roads several miles away, and crocodiles (鰐魚) are captured alive at the customs pier.
When I finally arrived in Sandakan, I was asked whether I would choose a fairly well-maintained bungalow (방-가로) or the old house on the hill where my husband had lived until then. Without hesitation, I chose the house on the hill.
From that house, the entire Sandakan Bay was visible.
It was morning.
The water was calm and flat as a mirror, blue like a full-color picture postcard. The red roofs (집웅) of the Chinese houses shone in the morning sunlight (해ᄉ발), the hillside was covered in green, and far beyond, the deep forest blocked the view.
It was just like a painting. I wanted to print "Greetings from Borneo to everyone" on this picture and send it to my friends.<224>

3
The northeast monsoon blew fiercely from the Sulu Sea (슬海), across five miles (五哩) of forest, as if to lift the houses of Sandakan, filled with the breath of lush trees, off the ground. And when the southwest monsoon brought black clouds from the bay, it was like being covered with a wet cloth (겁); everything became damp. When it rained, every window and door had to be closed, but water still seeped through the cracks, soaking everything. In the bedroom, we lit charcoal (숯) in a brazier to combat the dampness, but the shoes in the shoe closet (신장) got completely wet, bonbons (본본) melted inside their cork stoppers (막애), and all the envelopes in the desk drawer (설합) stuck together.
Only one place, a small room specially built in the middle of the bedroom, was our refuge. With constructed walls and screened doors, we could breathe a sigh of relief when the lamp was lit in that narrow room. This was just enough to avoid the nuisance of winged termites (羽蟻), beetles (甲虫), mosquitoes, and cicadas (매아미).
The bed in the bedroom was placed high up in the middle of the room, like a boat. Other furniture was hung on the walls. Even with small wardrobes (장농) nearby, it was suffocatingly hot as they blocked the wind.
Bathing involved standing directly under the water pipe (水道) in a corner of the bedroom. Next to it was a small washbasin area, called a W.C. in the local parlance. Having a W.C. here is quite a luxury (사치), comparable to having an air conditioning system (냉방장치) or a swimming pool (스이밍푸-ᄅ) in a private house in America. I couldn't help but be surprised when, even visiting the home of the government's administrative chief (行政長官), let alone ordinary homes, I was told, "We don't have such things."
When the rain stopped, the intense sun suddenly blazed (쨍쨍합니다). This time, every window and door was opened wide (개방하여) to drive out the dampness pervading the house.
The flowers immediately revived (소생하여); the yellow grape-like fruits of the Allamanda ripened, the rouge-like petals (花辨) of the African Tulip (亞佛利加 튜맆) bloomed, the bright red (새빩간) Flame of the Forest (赤森焰) blossomed, and the faint (은은한) fragrance of the Frangipani (赤素馨) wafted on the wind.
The outdoors must be the only decoration of this region. However, when trying to sun (쪼일려고) the bed, for some reason (웬일인지), it completely (왼통) fell apart.
As the sun set, the sound of anchor chains (碇鎖) being wound could be heard from the sea, <225> the striking of clocks from the town, the clatter of wooden shoes (木靴) on the streets, and inside the room, a candlestick (燭臺) resembling a Turkish (土耳古) jewel burned brightly.

4
According to single (獨身) men, bringing a wife to this land is quite a gamble (도박). They say not even one in ten succeeds.
The men each have their work. But the wives must create their own work, or they have none. When children are born, they are entrusted to the care of Chinese "amahs" (支那人의 「아마」) and then taken back to the home country at the age of five. There are no educational institutions here—.
It could be said that the 20 European (歐洲人) wives hardly live in this land. They are all wives of the men who came here; the only unmarried European woman is the head nurse at the hospital.
Men who come to work here are generally not allowed to marry formally until they have passed an eight-year term of service. Consequently, scandals often arise surrounding the few women, and much gossip (꼬십) circulates (떠도는).
Therefore, various unwritten rules (不文律) have emerged to avoid causing rumors.
Do not visit a wife in the morning.
Do not adopt a motherly attitude towards young trainees.
Do not go to the club on Saturdays or Sundays.
Do not play tennis wearing shorts (쇼-쯔).
And so on, mostly very amusing things, but these are the unwritten rules of Sandakan's European society. Even in this small, sweltering country, men dress up (채려입습니다) in formal wear for banquets. Of course, they suffer terribly while wearing dinner coats (데이나-코-트) or tailcoats. It's no easier (헐할 것은 없습니다) for us women. Dry cleaning is sent all the way to Singapore (씽가폴-). It takes three weeks for it to be done and returned.
Fashion, of course—by the time we see the styles in books, people back home may have already discarded them.

5
In 1935, the Johnsons (존손) happened to come to Borneo to film a movie. They set up camp in Abai (아베이), 50 miles <226> from Sandakan, built a small village for filming, and spent a full year feeding all kinds of wild animals that could be caught in North Borneo, living like a small zoo. Even at our house in Sandakan, various animals truly gathered. Apes (類人猿), orangutans (오란그-탄), otters (獺), parrots (鸚鵡), wild dogs, cats—various animals gathered.
The first ape I befriended was named "Jojo" (죠죠). He was truly attached and felt my affection to the point where I felt sorry for him if I didn't show love. He spent more time coming in and out of the house than staying in the mango tree branches. When Jojo wasn't visible, if I called out,
"Ooh-ooh-ooh-ooh—"
He would immediately jump down from a high branch, scurry (종종거름으로) down the green branches into the flower bed, part the flowers and plants, make whimpering sounds beside me, and finally leap into my outstretched arms.
The joy he showed at that moment made him seem unbelievably cute for a monkey.
Nobody wanted to hold Anjibila (안지비라), the gibbon (手長猿). Sensing its mood, I would spread my arms saying, "Come here." It never once showed its teeth to us.
Besides these, various other animals freely came and went from our house as if it were their own. While we changed clothes for dinner, a baby monkey named George (죠-지) would dart around the room like lightning, licking (할트며) our arms and fingers. Eventually (나종엔), it became bothersome (수선스러울 정도입니다) for us. When the baby grew surprisingly large, it would sometimes try to bite. This usually happened when the seasonal wind was blowing.
While uninvited guests from the forest sometimes became my friends, on the other hand, since my husband Harry's return to his post (歸任), a continuous series of invitation banquets awaited us. Harry's few sturdy shirts couldn't withstand the washing, and as mentioned earlier, we grew weary (권태) of the suffocating (숨맥힐 듯한) formality and fuss of the small social circle.
So, we went deeper into the forest. Or rather, I insisted on being taken along on my husband's forest inspection tour (시찰).
I was prepared, but ah! The hardship (괴로움) of that journey!
I don't know how many times I thought I could never come back to such a place again. I overcame that hardship countless times.<227>
"Do you still want to go further?"
My husband asked me several times.
"Yes."
I replied each time. To be honest, it wasn't out of confidence, but rather like a child, simply following unconditionally, believing my husband would surely take me along. Let's go as far as we can. That was my feeling.
Whatever I was anxious about, it's no exaggeration to say that nothing in my past or future could give me as much anxiety as this journey did.

6
We traveled upstream on a river in a native dugout canoe (土人의 丸木舟). The canoe was about 20 feet long and wide enough for only one person to sit comfortably.
Luggage was placed fore and aft for balance before we set off paddling, but any thought of comfort was impossible from the start. My feet got completely soaked by the splashing water with every stroke of the oars.
The white man built roads and organized the coastline, bringing it completely under law. But on the waterways, there was nothing but the native dugout canoe.
"All sturdy, sturdy."
The native boat captain repeated this forcefully several times to reassure us.
For about an hour, we traveled upstream along the K'aerabang (캐래방) and Chango (쟝고) rivers. The river gradually turned into rapids, and the natives steered the boat towards the bank while paddling.
When we reached the rapids churning with white water, I couldn't even blink. I lay down in the boat and looked up at the blue sky. The dense jungle (叢林) on both banks was a thick wall of green leaves (綠葉); lush foliage and branches were completely piled up, and vines hung down repeatedly like curtains (「바알」 - probably 'veil' or 'curtain').
The undergrowth was so dense it felt heavy; if one took cover for even a moment, they might not be found, and the person hiding might not be able to find their way out again.
Therefore, when we moored the boat at the bank at night and set up the tent, I, the only woman in the group, couldn't go to the latrine. If I carelessly stepped into the thicket, the group might lose me, and I myself wouldn't be able to find my way back. Fortunately, my husband <228> noticed and told them to clear a path for me when setting up the tent.
At Pengkalan (펭그가랑) - a sort of landing place in the jungle (叢林中) - we moored the boat, and the group decided to proceed on foot. Though called marching, there was no real path; we advanced by cutting through the undergrowth. Harry and the servant walked much faster than me, and I always just diligently followed behind them. If I fell 10 yards behind, they would already disappear into the dense thicket. I wouldn't even be able to tell the path they had just trodden and would stand there blankly. Then, the porters ("Coolies" - 苦力) carrying the luggage would come up from behind. When I met them, I could breathe easily again, but pretending I wasn't bewildered, I'd say my legs hurt and I rested for a moment, or that I was waiting for the rest of the party coming behind, and walk with them. They pretended to be fooled by my words, but they well knew I was sitting there with wide (휘둥그래서), startled eyes. Trying not to fall behind (떠러안지려고), my unshod feet were torn (짖기고) by branches and bitten by insects. Among them, the leeches (거마리) were the worst.
The leech would stealthily attach itself to the skin. When trying to brush it off, it would stick to the hand.
"I'm never coming back."
Spitting out the insects and dirt flying into my mouth, brushing off the leeches crawling onto my neck, I don't know how many times I cried this out.
Opening my diary from that time, I read:
"Set up tent at 3 o'clock. Rain all day. Exhausted to death."
"If our marriage isn't destroyed by this trip, nothing on earth can destroy it."
"What a day. Too tired to write. Soaked through."
"Jungle travel is the worst."
Thus, the journey became more grueling day by day. Truly unbearable hardship (辛苦) would become enjoyable again the next day. When sloshing (질벅질벅) through the mud (泥濘), I felt like I was going mad. Many times, I lost sight of my husband walking ahead, fought through the trees, and couldn't even find the place I had broken through. Once, I tumbled down a riverbank. Fortunately, it was muddy (泥土), so when I buried my face in it,
"Ha!"
The "Coolie" (苦力) laughed as if watching something amusing. Unexpectedly, tears of frustration streamed down my cheeks.
Thinking back, for the natives here, who wouldn't care (아무렇지도 않은) even if they lost an arm or a leg, <229> my suffering was probably less significant than dirt on their feet.
"Master, my heel (발굼치) is half torn off."
When a young, child-like Coolie came to our tent one night and showed us his heel, my hair felt like it was standing on end (꺼꾸로 솟은 듯했습니다).

7
Not long after, as the difficult journey was nearing its end one night, I sat in the damp tent applying alcohol to leech bites.
"Look, Harry, I don't know how to make the bleeding stop (머믈찌 몰라요)."
"It will never heal (안 나아요) if you do that."
"These awful sandflies (砂蠅)! Worse than leeches."
"Hey, darling,"
Harry called.
"Why did you want to come along on a trip like this? I have to do it because it's my job, but you didn't need to collect such unpleasant memories."
"I think this is the most enjoyable (질거운) time of my life."
"What?"
Harry looked bewildered.
"What do you mean by that?"
I sat blankly. In my mind, I saw the face of a woman with the same expression. Wearing a soft rubber corset (골세트), just like everyone else.
"I think I shouldn't fall into a complacent (안일한) life."
"Hmm."
Whether he understood or not, my husband replied like this.
I wanted to say this:
"I respect you. Therefore, I want to do as you do."
And,
"I didn't come to this country to be a parasite (기생). I want to become a part of this country. I want to know the natives (토인) like you do, speak the native language (土語), and joke with the natives. I wanted to go into the jungle, even if I got covered in mud and bled from leech bites."
And also, I thought,
"Even saying this, the body cannot keep up (채워갈 수 없는데), but, <230> I will stand up in spirit... In the end, I've only been a hindrance."
Harry stared intently at my face, then quickly got up and jumped into his own bed. His blanket (이볼) and mattress seemed much less (떨) warm than mine.
"Aren't you cold? Shall I give (디릴까요) you some of mine?"
"No. It's okay."
Harry said this and smiled faintly.
"Go to sleep quickly. Tomorrow is the hardest journey, the last day of the jungle trip."
"It's okay."
Replying thus, I also lay down in my bed.
I heard the sound of rain falling on the leaves. But soon, the rain and the flowing water drifted away from my mind. I reached out and touched my husband's shoulder. A strong shoulder, that was for me. Amid the pleasure of this thought, I felt my own weakness, but weak as I was, that too belonged to my husband.
I resolved not to show weakness tomorrow.
A long, rapidly flowing downstream current appeared before our eyes again. Twelve men paddled the dugout canoe towards the rapids, churning with entirely white water.
Compared to that river, how small humans seemed.
In this group of 12, it would be impossible to distinguish who was white and who was black. Every person was a small creature. Of the two white people, who was Harry and who was I?
The dugout canoe sped through the white water like a carriage. The sound of the water was so tumultuous that it was deafening (귀가 메일 지경이었습니다). <231>

**Summary**

This text is a Korean translation of an excerpt from Agnes Newton Keith's "Land Below the Wind," recounting her arrival in British North Borneo in the 1930s as the American wife of a British official, Harry. It details her adjustment from modern American life to the challenging, primitive conditions of Sandakan, including the climate, housing, wildlife encounters (apes, orangutans), the small, formal European social circle, and culminates in a grueling, dangerous journey with her husband deep into the jungle interior by canoe and foot, testing her physical and emotional limits but ultimately strengthening her resolve to understand and be part of her new environment, not just an observer or "parasite."

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article, published in 1940, offers a glimpse into how Western colonialism in Southeast Asia was presented to a Korean audience under Japanese rule. Historians might find it interesting as it portrays the hardships and adaptations of a Westerner in a colonized 'primitive' land, potentially offering subtle points of comparison or contrast with the Korean experience under Japan. The focus on the 'exotic' environment, the interactions between Europeans and natives (referred to as 土人/土民), and the wife's struggle and determination could have resonated in complex ways, reflecting on themes of cultural encounter, endurance, and the nature of colonial life from an external perspective.

잡지명	삼천리 제12권 제8호
발행일	1940년 09월 01일
기사제목	季節風
필자	아구네쓰·뉴-톤·케이쓰 作
기사형태	소설

季節風
						아구네쓰·뉴-톤·케이쓰 作
『季節風』에 대하여
이 소설은 작년도 「아트랜테이크·맨스리-」誌의 讀物賞(5,000弗)을 획득했다. 저자 아구네스·뉴-톤·케이스는 미국인으로 북부 볼네오駐在의 米國森보호관인 영국인과 결혼하고 결혼과 동시에 남편의 任地인 열대 볼네오 무더운 계절풍 아래의 나라에서 약 4년간을 지났다. 摩天樓의 현대 아메리카로부터 급작스레 쟝글에 인접한 원시생활에 옮았든 것이다. 특이한 경험을 카메라와 같이 정확히 마이크와 같이 예민하게 백힌 것이 이 一書다.
一哩앞 森林에는 象이 출몰하고 오인우-탄이 挑梁하고 있다. 그러한 蕃地의 부락에 와서까지 오후 4시의 「茶」라는 舊習을 고수하는 영국인들, 그리고 洗濯쟁이는 씬가포-ᄅ. 여러가지 모순을 웃고, 風光을 사랑하고, 다만, 볼네오를 이해하려는 저자의 마음에는 솔직과, 청순이 넘치고, 특히 남편을 쫓아 森林지대로 깊이 들어가는 여행의 기록에는 눈물겨운 안해의 감정이 그려저 있다.
그러나 특이한 재료를 취급하여 이 종류의 서적에 있슴즉한 故意로 재미있게 하려고 한 거즛도 없다.＜223＞

1
내 남편은 관리로서 14년간 근무하고 있는 한편, 진귀한 동물을, 여러 나라 동물원에 보내고. 또 손수 길르고, 과학잡지에, 기고하기도 하고 ³¼네오의 생활을 전혀 자기의 것으로 하고 馬來語도 꽤 익숙히 할 수 있었습니다.
미국학교를 나왔으므로 옵바와 나와도, 옛날부터의 동무였는데 보르네오에 도라 간다는 前-1934년이었습니다-집에 놀러왔습니다.
그래서 그로부터 3ケ月後. 나는 그의 안해로서 보르네오의 便船을 탔습니다.
대륙의 그림자가 드디어 수평선 저쪽에 사라저 버렀을 때＜222＞ 나는 남편을 도라다 보고 말했습니다.
「전 이 순간을 잊을 수가 없어요, 역시 좋왔다구 생각해요, 당신과 단 함께라기보다 정말, 우리 둘뿐이얘요. 아무리 새좋게 오래 산다구 하드래두 이런 마음은 경험할 수 없을 것 같애.」
「응.」
하고, 남편은 고개를 끄덕인 후 심각한 얼굴로 대답했다.
「보르네오에서 사년간 지난 뒤 지금처럼 생각해 준다면 좋지만.」
「생각하는 거 안애요, 느끼는 거얘요」
「응 그건 그렇지만.」
「제 마음이 변한단 말슴이얘요?」
「그런 건 아니지.」
남편은 그렇게 말하고, 내 억깨를 가볍게 두드려 주었읍니다.
「가서 보면 역시 놀날 걸.」
신혼의 우리들을 기다리고 있는 것은 季節風 아래에 가지도 보이지 않을만큼 무성한 쟝글의 무더운 생활이었습니다.
그러나, 그로부터 5년 지난 지금에도 그 순간의 감정은 조금도 변하지 않고, 내 마음에 남어있습니다.
「뭐래요 하리이.」
엄숙한 얼굴을 한 남편에게 웃으며 이렇게 말한 내 말에 나는 승리를 느끼는 것입니다.＜223＞

2
그런데 영국 보호 아래에 있는 보르네오 북부는, 인구 27만人, 휘이맆퓐 남서에 위치를 가지고, 미국에선 항로로, 6주간을 요합니다. 기후랄 것 없지만, 한마디로 말슴하면, 움직이기가, 귀찮을 정도의 기후입니다.
「어이쿠.」
이렇게 소리없이는 의자에서 이러날 수가 없습니다. 그것이 남편 하리이의 任地입니다.
우리 집은, 동해안에 沿한 이 지방 수도 싼다칸에 있는데 여기는 인구 1만 4천人으로, 그 중, 歐洲人은, 전부 75人이 있을 뿐, 남은 것은, 보르네오 土着의 土民, 휘이맆퓐人, 馬來人, 東印度人, 日本人 등입니다.
세계 周遊의 쪄-낼리스트는 싼다칸을 묵살하여 별로 滯在해 가는 사람도 없었습니다. 더구나, 도로가 전부 17哩, 볼 것이라는 것은, 꼴프歐樂部와 무전국과 농원과, 싼다칸 俱樂部뿐, 자동차로 달려 45분이면 구경이 끝나므로 손님이 머믈을 필요가 없을 것입니다. 그러므로 이런 여행자들이 쓰는 보르네오 이야기라는 것은 밑도 끝도 없는, 엉터리로, 쟝글을 배경한 모험담 등은, 정말 함부로 쓴 것이 적지 않었습니다. 보지도 않고 쓰는 까닭에...
정말은, 蕃地에 있어도, 우리들은, 歐洲 풍습대로, 말하자면, 紅茶좋지의 그림과 같이, 적은 끌럽만으로 歐洲의 생활을 하느라고 오후 4시에는 茶를 마심니다. 數哩 떠러진 도로에는 때때로, 象이 출현하고, 세관 선착장에는 鰐魚가 생포되고 했습니다.
마침내, 싼다칸에 도착했을 때, 나는, 꽤 손질 잘한 방-가로와, 또 하나는 그때까지 남편이 살고 있었다는 언덕 위 낡은 집과, 어느 쪽을 택하겠느냐고 말했을 때, 나는 주저없이 언덕 우에 집을 택했습니다.
그 집에서는 싼따칸灣이 죄다 보였습니다.
아침이었습니다.
물은 조용히 거울과 같이 평평하게, 마치 원색 색판의 그림엽서와도 같이 푸른 빛이고, 支那가옥의 붉은 집웅에 아침해ᄉ발에 빛나고, 언덕 一面은 녹음에 덮이고 멀리 저쪽은 깊은 삼림으로 시야를 막었습니다.
전혀 한 폭의 그림과 같이 나는 이 그림 우에, 「보르네오에서 여러분께」라 하고 인쇄해서 동무들에게 보내고 싶습니다.＜224＞

3
북동의 계절풍은, 슬海로부터 삼림지대를, 五哩를 건너서 싱싱한 나무들 숨결이 숨은 싼따칸의 집들을 大地로부터 떠없애려는 것같이 격렬하게 불었습니다. 그리고 남서의 계절풍이 灣으로부터 黑雲을 몰아가지고 오는 때는 꼭 젖은 겁으로 덮은 것처럼 어느 것 하나 젖지 않은 것 없이 되어 버립니다. 비가 오면 창이란 창, 문이란 문은 죄다, 닫치는데, 그래도, 틈틈에서, 새여 들어서 무엇이나 젖어버리고 맙니다. 침실에는, 화로에 숯을 피우고, 습기를 막기는 하나, 신장 속의 구두는 전혀 젖고, 본본은 콜크막애 속에서 아주 녹아버리고, 책상 설합 封筒는 죄다, 한테 붙어버리고 말었습니다.
꼭 한군데, 침실가운데, 특별히 지은 적은 방만이 우리들의 안식소였습니다. 벽을 만들고, 스크린을 친 문, 그러한 좁은 방에 등불이 켜지는 때, 우리들은, 숨을 활 내쉬였습니다. 이것만으로 羽蟻와, 甲虫과, 모기와, 매아미의 피해를 입지 않을만한 정도겠지만.
침실의 침대는, 배와 같이 방 한가운데, 높이 밫어 놓았습니다. 다른 가구들은 벽에 달아 매놓았습니다. 적은 장농들이 옆에 있어도 바람을 막으므로 숨막힐 정도로 무더웁습니다.
목욕은, 침실 한쪽에 있는 水道에서 그대로, 물을 맞습니다. 그 옆에 적은 세면소가 있고 여기 말로 하면 W·C람니다. W·W가 있는 것은 여기서는 아주 사치한 것으로, 미국에 비해서 말한다면, 개인주택에 냉방장치라든가 스이밍푸-ᄅ이 있는 거나 마찬가지랍니다. 보통가정은 물론 정부 행정장관의 가정을 찾을 때에도 「그런 거 없어요」 라고 하는 데는 놀라지 않을 수 없었습니다.
비가 끝이면 갑자기 강렬한 태양이 쨍쨍합니다. 창이란 창, 문이란 문을, 이번엔 죄다 개방하여, 집안에 풍기는 습기를 쫓아냅니다.
화초는 당장 소생하여 에비스草의 누른 포도와 같은 과실이 익고 亞佛利加 튜맆의 루-즈같은 花辨이 피고, 새빩간 赤森焰이 피고, 赤素馨의 은은한 향기가 바람에 부러오고 했습니다.
戶外야말로, 이 지방 유일의 장식일 것입니다. 그런데, 침대를 해볕에 쪼일려고 하면, 웬일인지 왼통 부서지고 맙니다.
해가 지면, 바다에서는, 碇鎖를 감는 소리가 들리고＜225＞거리에서는 시계치는 소리가, 들리고, 길가는 木靴자국소리가 뚜걱 뚜걱 들려오고, 방 속에는, 土耳古의 玉과 같은 燭臺가 불꽃을 태우고 있습니다.

4
독신남자들의 말에 의하면, 이 토지에 안해를 대리고 오는 것은 전혀, 도박과 같은 것이다. 열에 하나도 성공할 수 없다고 합니다.
남자들은 각각 일을 가지고 있습니다. 하나 안해들은 자기가 할 일을, 자기가 만들지 않어서는 할 일이 없습니다. 아이가 나면, 支那人의 「아마」들 손에 맡겼다가 5세가 되면 고국에 대려 갑니다. 여기에는 교육기관이 하나도 없으므로-.
20人의 歐洲人 부인이 이 토지에 사는 일이 없다고 해도 좋을 만합니다. 그것도, 다, 여기 온 사람들의 夫人들로, 미혼의 歐洲부인이라면 병원의 간호부장뿐입니다.
여기 와서 일하는 남자들은, 8년 임기를 지나지 않으면 정식 결혼은 할 수 없는 규칙이 되어 있으므로, 소수의 여성을 둘러싸고, 스캔달이 생기는 일도 있고, 또 꼬십도 떠도는 일이 많습니다.
그러므로, 소문내지 않기 위해서 여러 가지 不文律이 생깁니다.
오전 중에, 부인을 방문치 않을 일.
젊은 실습생에게 어머니와 같은 태도를 취하지 말 일.
토요, 일요에는 구락부에 가지 말 일.
쇼-쯔를 입고 테니쓰를 하지 말 일.

유행이란 물론, 우리들이 스타일, 책을 보는 때에는 故國 사람들은 벌서 그것들을 벗어 놓았을지 모릅니다.
