잡지명	삼천리 제12권 제8호
발행일	1940년 09월 01일
기사제목	赴戰高原 (Journey to Pujŏn Plateau)
필자	毛允淑
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

赴戰高原
						毛允淑

It is an early morning in early autumn. Having gathered some luggage, K and I went out to Sŏhamhŭng Station (西咸興驛). The wind felt quite chilly, seeping under my ramie jacket. I bought a few more melons and green apples, put them in a worn cloth bag, and boarded the train. A group of about 50 schoolgirls, perhaps from some school organization, were chattering noisily in the car just ahead.

"Hey, they say there's green grass there. That's why it's cold like winter even in summer."
"Wow, the water is really beautiful."

The Hamhŭng dialect was quite cheerful and loud inside the narrow-gauge train climbing along the banks of the Sŏngch'ŏn River (성천강).
K was reading a novel by Maupassant, and I was just looking outside, lost in my own thoughts.(112)

Scattered here and there, laborers busy with construction work lined the railway tracks, and the mountains, stretching upwards, had been dug into, dug into, with the crumbling red earth looking limp and powerless even from afar.

As noon arrived, the sunlight beat down quite hotly on my back. In the cramped train car, the smell of sweat and breath mingled, making it hard to breathe. My seat was uncomfortable, so I spent several hours standing up and sitting down restlessly. It was only when we arrived at Songhŭng Station (松興驛) that we could finally get off the train and cool down a bit. As I got off, I saw a valley to the left where white stones spread out. Its name is Songhŭng River (宋興江), but it was dry, so no water could be seen, only white quartz pebbles glistening unbearably under the sunlight.

Hearing that a noodle shop was right in front, we decided to eat noodles during the transfer time and entered a house with a low porch roof shaded by a long hemp cloth.
The noodles had a rather unpleasant taste, a combination of the fishy smell of the soy sauce and the undercooked smell of buckwheat flour.
Just as the station worker shouted that it was time, we left our noodle bowls and rushed out.

From here, we were to take a train up to the highland area via an incline railway.
K and I tried to get good seats, but because there was the group and also many travelers, we couldn't find suitable spots. K squatted down next to an old man wearing a *kat* (traditional hat), and I stood at the very back.

With a sharp sound, the train departed and rumbled upwards. Looking up through the train window, the steep mountains stretched up intimidatingly. It was hard to believe that this train, pulling so many people, could climb such a sheer place. Soon, something like a hazy smoke drifted in from both sides; they said it was fog. Gradually, the green mountains visible on both sides disappeared, and the swift, powerful swirling of the pale fog covered the peaks. Standing, I soon found myself unable to maintain my posture, swaying back and forth, dizzy, unsure where to place my feet. The train ascended in a straight line, but they said it was extremely safe due to electric power. Peeking through gaps in the whitish clouds, I saw black iron pipes connected up the mountain. I heard that the water from the lake we were heading to is drawn down through these iron pipes, and that water is then used to generate electricity. I couldn't help but feel anew how great the power of science and human effort is.(113) In this remote mountain range, separated from the human world since time immemorial, life is breaking the solemn silence of nature's mystery and establishing a cradle of happiness here.

Along with the humming sound of the machine motors, the mountain lives, nourished by a new beauty.

In the deep, layered clouds
The turning siren
The sound of life's hoes, hammers,
Beside the collapsing earth embankment
Though the body is weary, we gaze towards the world of light.

The fatigue gradually faded, and the majesty of the newly visible high mountains and the appearance of unfamiliar plants sparked new curiosity. These were pure alpine plants, untouched by human hands. Their shapes were all vibrant with natural life, giving an impression of abundance, which naturally made my heart feel abundant too.

The train ran through this scenery for a while and then stopped at the very top of the plateau. From a small wooden building, seemingly a rest stop, a woman wearing a padded jacket came out carrying a wooden basin. Peeking inside, I saw a small stove lit. They said ice had formed here even this morning. I wasn't sure about the exact temperature, but since K and I had come wearing ramie jackets, we couldn't help but be surprised by the climate here. Someone opened a train window slightly. A cold wind, pushing the fog, rushed in with a whoosh. From here, the route was said to be downhill, though not as steep as the ascent, perhaps about one-third the gradient. The whistle blew, and the train started moving downwards. Descending, I heard that this area was Changjin (長津) territory. It was the Changjin land famous for its potatoes and barley, which I had heard about since childhood.

The mountain terrain was relatively less rugged than before, but because a forest fire had occurred last year or sometime, all the trees were burnt, and the remaining charred pine and fir trees stood starkly, revealing desolate scenery in every valley.
The fog still swirled over the mountains, and far down in the quiet valley, patches of blue water began to appear intermittently. That water was said to be the beginning of Pujŏn Lake (부전湖水), called Sasu (泗水). When the train finally reached Sasu, our group disembarked and transferred to a boat. The water had been murky since last year's flood and had not cleared up yet. Sitting on the motorboat and looking ahead, the impression was less of a lake and more like a small sea, so vast was the expanse of water.(114)

The boat sped forward, cutting through the water quite fast. I felt that the impression of this lake was somewhat lacking compared to what I had heard. Firstly, I had never imagined such a large lake, nor had I imagined crossing a lake on a steamboat-like vessel. I had envisioned a lake like a hometown for the soul, with blue water, a small area where one could float a gondola and enjoy the sentiment of the sky, wind, stars, and sunlight. However, one must not forget that this lake is not a romantic playground endowed with dreams and beauty, but rather a womb of life meant to illuminate the progress of civilization. The circumference of the lake is said to be 300 *ri*. It is said that this is the only artificial lake of this size in the East. Although the motorboat traveled at considerable speed, it seemed unlikely to cover the distance in a short time. By the time the sun had set and patches of red afterglow drifted in the western sky, I finally felt like I could hear the voice of my own heart floating on the lake. The scenery also became more profound than before, and from every curve of the mountains, the sound of water splashing could be heard even from within the folds. Now, as night falls, will the lake lull the tree shadows to sleep, embrace the quiet stars, and dream tranquil dreams? Mulling over such thoughts, I sought to rest my weariness upon the bosom of the lake, steeped in evening. Gradually, the pine shadows descended onto the water, and the red water, tinged with the night light, covered up tumultuous emotions, allowing me to share my dreams with the lake.

I suddenly recalled the poem "The Lake" by the French poet Lamartine. My heart was drawn to the shore of that lake, the cradle of people, memories, and happiness.
K sat looking only at the sky, perhaps associating the lake with the heavens.

It was around 7 PM when the boat reached a shore. The water lapped gently against the bank, where a neatly built two-story stone house stood. Wondering how such a culturally sophisticated building came to be here, I learned it was the only hotel in the area.
K and I agreed to stay there, and upon requesting a room, we were fortunate to find one vacant and could easily unload our luggage. We wanted a room facing the lake, but they were all occupied, so we ended up with a room facing the mountain at the back.
Despite it being someone else's room, K frequently went over, sat on the windowsill, and gazed out at the lake. To relax, we first went to the bathhouse. They said the water was drawn from the lake and heated. There was a small(115) window on the north side of the bathroom, and the lake could be seen looking down to the southwest.

Sitting in the bathroom, gazing at the endlessly visible lake, I thought of ancient times when Egyptian women drew water from the Nile River, filled their baths, and enjoyed all sorts of mythology. Occasionally, through the north window, delicate leaves fluttered against the glass pane. Looking up, I saw the crescent moon, risen sometime unnoticed, shining gently through the high tree branches.

"Hey, the moon is out! Let's hurry out, eat dinner, and take a walk, okay?"
"Today is the fifth day of the lunar month, I think," K said, and perhaps tempted by the moonlight, splashed out of the bath. Dinner was truly delicious. Although the food was mainland (内地) cuisine I wasn't used to, the pickled radish (다꾸왕) was tasty, so I asked for two more pieces twice. We changed into the light one-piece dresses we had brought and went outside the hotel, each holding a walking stick (집행이).

We kept climbing up the winding hill. The young mainland couple (內地人) who had arrived on the same boat also went around the mountain that way.
Occasionally, cold dew flicked up from the tip of the walking stick, creating a sensation on my stockinged legs.
The surrounding mountains stood solemnly, submerged in eternal mystery.
K and I walked up the gentle path in silence. On the mountain pass stretching wide, the blue sky seemed to drag endless miles from my chest, feeling infinitely distant.

After a while, K sang softly:
"Nearer, my God, to Thee,
Though it be a cross that raiseth me;
Still all my song shall be,
Nearer, my God, to Thee."
After singing, she took a deep breath, stopped her walking stick, and looked at me, saying,
"Coming to a place like this naturally makes one feel reverent. Isn't this truly an indescribable landscape?"
I didn't reply but quietly hummed Ave Maria.

The crescent moon, already beginning to set, hung there with a strange allure. It felt like the moon existed solely here, isolated. Over the vast lake surface, the moon poured out its burning passion, lonely. K and I paused our walk for a moment, caressing this overflowing harmony of nature with our eyes and souls.(116) Along the lake's edge, dark tree shadows shimmered in the moonlight, creating an enchanting ink painting (믁화).

As a fragrant scent wafted by, K said,
"There must be wild strawberries. Doesn't it smell just like ripened strawberries?"
She then probed the bushes with her walking stick. Then,
"Oh, look! Aren't these strawberries? Gosh, how can they smell so good?"
She scooped up a handful and held them before my eyes.
Red wild strawberries. Soft, plump strawberries covered in white dew. We felt a sort of wonder, holding them in our palms for a long time without eating, lost in praise of the strawberries.

"Look, it's dark now, let's pick our fill tomorrow."
"Okay. Let's head back down now (이켄). It feels eerie in the mountains." I urged K.
"Exactly. It would be reassuring if a man (맨이) came with us to places like this," she said, then smiled at me sweetly as if she had said something awkward.

We turned around a nameless mountain corner and followed a path down along the edge of a hill where there was something like a small pond with blue water pooled in it.
It was deep in the mountains. Blue water filled a sunken valley. This water also seemed to be an extension of the lake, but for some reason, the water color wasn't murky but vividly blue, noticeable even at night.

"Wow, what's that? Look at that."
K startlingly hit my shoulder. I looked where she pointed and saw a truly unfamiliar object soaring towards the sky, its form massive and imposing. A water mill.
It was enormous, standing alone without any houses nearby, and occasionally, when the water filled, its head shot up towards the sky, making one's hair stand on end with fear. Perhaps because it was dusk, its rising motion was eerily frightening. K and I unknowingly clasped our sweaty hands together and ran down. It felt as if that very water mill was shouting and chasing us from behind.

When we reached the hotel entrance, half-laughing, I caught my breath and asked,
"Hey, were you really scared like me?"
K blinked her teary eyes once, then became serious and said,
"My dear! Were you really scared? Why was it so frightening? It looked just like(117) a corpse suddenly standing up (송장이 왁하고 이러서는 것같이). Maybe because the water mill was grayish, it looked really ominous (숭허기두 허이)."
She muttered half to herself, apparently quite shaken.

As I took off my shoes and put them in the shoe rack, I said to K,
"Shall we go see it again tomorrow? It should be fine during the day."
I suggested it again, but she replied,
"Oh, I don't want to! Did we come to Pujŏn Plateau just to lose our minds? What's the point of seeing that thing again?"

After going up and changing into pajamas, we chattered nonsensically about strawberries and unknown grasses until one of us fell asleep first.

The next day, we set out early in the morning with our walking sticks, carrying packed lunches (벤또) in hand pouches for mountain and lake sightseeing. Besides this Pujŏn Lake, there is said to be another large lake called Changjin Lake (長津호수), possessing magnificent scenery. However, the road was apparently washed out and currently impassable. With light steps, we crossed the mountain peak we had climbed the previous evening. An immense patch of wild strawberries appeared. K and I rushed into the strawberry patch without thinking. K dropped her whole lunch box (벤또) and laughed for a while searching for it. We picked and ate them instantly. The fresh sweetness was indescribable; one cannot speak of it without tasting it. Descending one steep mountain slope after another, we met an old woman living there and learned the names of some plants.

We learned names like bracken (고사리), *nŏnch'ul dotori* (넌출도토리 - likely a type of acorn vine), mountain hawthorn (산사나무), lilac (개회나무), winged spindle tree (화살나무), *kunggung'i* (궁궁이 - Angelica polymorpha), *myŏnŭribap'ŭl* (며느리바플 - dayflower), *kajisongip'ul* (가지송이풀 - Pedicularis), *moekkori'pul* (뫼꼬리풀 - Veronicastrum), Kousa dogwood (산딸나무), miniature Kousa dogwood (애기산딸나무), *norukkump'ul* (노루꿈풀), cinnamon tree (생달나무), *kamagwijjoknamu* (가마귀쪽나무 - Litsea japonica).
The names were so interesting that we passed several red strawberry patches without noticing.

"I'm going this way now (나는 이질누 간당이). Have a good trip (귀공으 잘하구 갑새)."
The old woman spoke affectionately as she turned towards a village visible to the left.
We once again attached ourselves to the strawberry patch, unwilling to leave, picking and eating. I thought of Ilsŏn at home and tried to gather some in a handkerchief, but K kept taking them all, so I had no harvest.

We walked towards the river visible below. A village was neatly nestled there, and a plain building, perhaps some kind of night school (야학교), was also visible.
There were quite a few white stones on the riverbank. We took off our shoes, dipped our feet in the water, and unpacked our lunches (벤또). This side felt much cozier (118) than the large lake, and the water was blue and clear, which I liked. Also, this lake was said to be a naturally formed lake from ancient times, unlike the one behind it, which had human intervention (人工). Across the flat field in the middle stood something like a cement factory, with smoke billowing from its chimney; it seemed to be some building related to this lake.

K and I decided to walk around the perimeter of this lake. We set off wearing only sneakers on our bare feet. The path was monotonous, but the valleys surrounded by cliff-like mountains, filled with unfamiliar flowers, grasses, and trees, created a sense of being in a different world (別有天地). Autumn foliage had already turned yellow on the mountaintops. Since the weather (日氣) is cold here, the autumn colors seem to arrive earlier. When our legs got tired (困), we rested frequently, and in about two hours, we managed to circle the lake. Occasionally, we saw women walking barefoot, carrying large baskets full of huge potatoes on their heads, their chests exposed (앞가슴은 다- 흩으러진 채). Their faces showed resilience, and their strides were very energetic, clearly exhibiting the characteristics of North Hamgyŏng (北咸興).

Here, women generally manage the farming (롬사지여) and household, while men go to big cities (大都會) to work as laborers (뇌동업) and send money back. The women of this village are said to not only grow potatoes and barley but also raise dogs, cows, chickens, and pigs themselves to sell. Yet, they reproduce prolifically (生殖은 할대로 해서), and there are few women without children on their backs. Their education level (교육정도) is barely beyond night school, perhaps knowing only a smattering (나브랑이나) of the Korean script (언문). I pondered whether the existence of only such a primitive village next to such a beautiful lake might be an issue to consider for the future.

We returned to the hotel only around evening. After dinner, we heard the radio playing from the lobby (로-까) downstairs. Even in this remote mountain village, radio facilities were reliably present. The song seemed to be a popular tune (유행가), and it was a great comfort to my ears, which had been bored for several days. This place is said to be at an altitude of 5,000 feet. Because the lake's expanse is so vast, like a plain, it doesn't strongly feel like a high plateau.
One cannot help but express high respect (敬意) for the effort and achievement of carving out the mountains and diverting water by human hands (人工) to create the lake.

K and I sat leisurely on the edge of an empty boat by the lakeside, unaware of the passing night, engrossed in songs and conversation. Clouds had gathered, obscuring the moonlight. The lake was completely shrouded in darkness, with only the occasional sound of wind crossing the water, making it lap, lap, lap.
I dreamed of a beautiful future for this lake, hoping it would soon become a place where lanterns adorn the shore (119) and people sing through the night. The lake surface was calm. Our conversation was also endlessly quiet, and we decided to enjoy the midnight hours aboard the boat bobbing gently on the water.

Back in the room, K seemed to be writing letters to friends. I also wrote a long letter, addressed to no one in particular. After finishing and reading it, I found it very interesting. But the name of the person it was written to did not exist in the world. I put the letter in an envelope but couldn't write the recipient's name, as it seemed no one I currently knew could receive this letter. No one seemed likely to understand the feelings contained within this letter. Having written a letter without a recipient, I carefully arranged the grasses I had picked once more and fell asleep.

At noon the next day, we boarded the boat for our return journey. The water was calmer than when we came, and the sky was clearer, making it better for observing the scenery.
I have never been to other countries, but I convinced myself (스사로) that few places possess such abundant nature as Korea (조선). With these thoughts, I took the nonsensical letter I had written last night from my handbag and threw it into the lake. I have a disposition for enjoying (질기는) such imaginative mischief (공상적 작란).

All the way to Songhŭng Station (松興驛), K teased me about the letter. K is an amusing companion.
Pujŏn Plateau (赴戰高原) is famous for its wild blueberries (들죽). While wondering if there was any way to obtain some, women happened to be selling them right in front of Songhŭng Station. I bought two bottles worth 1 Won and packed them away. K bought three bottles, saying she would take them all the way to Seoul. Sitting back on the narrow-gauge train, I suddenly felt nostalgic for the image of the lake and regretted leaving. The unforgettable strawberry patches, the astonishingly large water mill, the names of various unknown flowers and grasses – they all flashed through my mind again, evoking a fresh sensation.
K and I were busy checking our bags frequently to ensure the flower berries hadn't wilted and confirming we hadn't lost anything. Hamhŭng Station (함흥역) was extremely crowded. K insisted (욱였으나) on going to Seoul that night, but I firmly persuaded (구지 붙잡어) her to come to my house. Until midnight, we were busy potting and arranging the grasses and flowering plants we had collected (캐여온) in pots and vases, oblivious to our fatigue. K worried that one plant, called *myŏnŭrip'ul* (며느리풀 - likely another name for dayflower), had disappeared (없어젔다). (End) (120)

**Summary**

This travelogue by Mo Yunsuk describes a trip with her companion "K" to the Pujŏn Plateau in North Hamgyŏng Province in early autumn 1940. They travel via narrow-gauge railway and an electric incline railway, marveling at the engineering involved in the Pujŏn hydroelectric project and the vast artificial Pujŏn Lake. The author contrasts the raw, beautiful, high-altitude nature (alpine plants, wild strawberries, stunning views) with the signs of modern development (power lines, dams, hotel, radio). She reflects on the lake's beauty, local life (particularly the hardworking women of Changjin), and shares moments of awe, fear (a giant water mill), and quiet contemplation with K. The piece blends appreciation for natural beauty with an awareness of technological change and includes observations about the local culture and landscape.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into colonial-era infrastructure development (railways, hydroelectric power) driven by Japanese industrial needs, presented here with a sense of wonder at human ingenuity. It also provides a glimpse into rural Korean life in a remote area, highlighting the gendered division of labor and the interaction between locals and visitors (including "mainlanders"). The author's mix of romantic appreciation for nature and acknowledgment of modernization reflects common themes in literature of the period, navigating traditional landscapes altered by colonial development.

『아유 난 싫으우, 부전고원 왔다가 넋잃고 가게? 그까짓건 또 봐 뭘해?』
올라가서 파쟈마로 가라입고 나서 우리는 딸기얘기와 일름모를 풀얘기를 두서없이 짖거리다가 누가 먼저 잠이 들었는지 잠이 드렀다.
이튿날은 새벽에 일직이 집행이를 끄을고 손주머니에 벤또를 넣어 가지고 산구경 호수구경을 떠났다. 이 부전호수 말고도 長津호수라는 큰 호수가 있어서 장엄한 풍광을 가추고 있다한다. 자동차 길이 문어저서 지금은 통행할 수 없다 한다. 우리는 경쾌한 걸음으로 어제저녁 올랐던 山峯을 넘었다. 엄청나게 딸기 숲이 들어찼다. K와 나는 정신없이 딸기 숲으로 빨리 들어가서 K는 벤또를 다- 잃고 한참 웃으며 찾으러 다녔다. 금시 따서 금시 입에 넣었다. 형요할 수 없이 신선한 甘味여서 먹어보지 않고는 말할 바가 못된다. 비탈진 山峯을 하나하나 나려오며 우리는 그 곳서 사는 노파 한분을 만나게 되어 풀이름을 더러 알었다.
거기 고사리며, 넌출도토리, 산사나무, 개회나무, 화살나무, 궁궁이, 며느리바플, 가지송이풀, 뫼꼬리풀, 산딸나무, 애기산딸나무, 노루꿈풀, 생달나무, 가마귀쪽나무, 이런 이름들을 배와 알었다.
이름들이 하도 재미나서 붉은 딸기숲을 몇이나 그냥 지나첬다.
『나는 이질누 간당이, 귀공으 잘하구 갑새.』
노파는 왼편으로 보이는 동리를 향하고 갈라지며 정답게 말한다.
우리는 또다시 딸기숲에 드러붙어 떠러질 줄을 모르고 뜨더 먹었다. 집에 있는 일선이 생각이 나서 수건에다 좀 모으면 모으는 대로 K에게 다 빼끼고 아무 수획다 없었다.
우리는 내려다뵈는 강을 향하고 걸었다. 동리가 얌전하게 둘러있고 무슨 야학교인 듯한 공공한 집도 하나 보였다.
강기슭에 힌 돌이 꽤 많었다. 우리는 발을 벗어 물에 잠그고 벤또를 끌렀다. 큰 호수보다 이쪽편이(118) 훨신 아담한 맛이 있고 물빛도 파랗고 맑아서 맘에 드렀다. 그리고 이 호수는 옛적부터 천연적으로 되어있는 호수요 저- 뒤엣 것 모양으로 人工을 加하지 않었다 한다. 건너편 펀한 들 한가운데 세멘트공장같은 것이 서있고 굴둑에서 연기가 내솟는데 아마 이 호수에 관연한 무슨 건물인 듯 싶다.
K와 나는 이 호수의 주위를 한박휘 돌아본다. 운동화만 맨발에 신고 떠났다. 길은 단조로웠으나 절벽같이 둘러선 山峽으로 보지 못한 꽃과 풀, 나무들이 別有天地에 감을 자아 내였다. 산꼭대기엔 벌서 단풍이 누렇게 물드렸다. 日氣가 치운 곳이니까 단풍도 먼저 찾어오는 모양이다. 가다가 다리가 困하면 쉬고쉬고하여 두어시간만에 우리는 호수주위를 한번 돌아올 수 있었다. 이따금 커다란 감자를 한 함지식 이고 앞가슴은 다- 흩으러진 채 맨발로 걸어오는 여인네를 본다. 얼굴엔 억센 기운이 돌고 걸음거리도 대단히 활기가 있어 北咸興의 특색을 잘 나타내고 있다.
여기는 대개 여자들이 롬사지여 살림하고 남자는 大都會로 나가 뇌동업을 해서 돈을 벌어 디린다 한다. 이 부락의 여인들은 감자, 보리농사는 물론 개, 소, 닭, 도야지를 손수 길러서 판다 한다. 그러면서도 生殖은 할대로 해서 아이 업지 않은 여인네가 별로 없다. 교육정도래야 겨우 야학을 치렀을 뿐으로 언문 나브랑이나 알가말가한 정도다. 이렇게 아름다운 호수 옆에 이렇게 야생적인 부락만이 존재한다는 것은 미래를 위해선 좀 생각할 문제가 아닌가도 생각해 보았다.
우리는 저녁 때나 되어서야 호텔로 들아왔다. 저녁을 먹고 나니 아랫층 로-까에서 라디오 소리가 들린다. 이런 벽촌 深山에도 라디오시설은 어김없이 되어 있다. 노래는 유행가인 모양인 데 몇일 심심하던 귀에는 대단히 큰 위안이 되는 것을 알었다. 여기가 해발 5천尺 高地라 한다. 호수폭이 하도 평원같이 넓어서 고원지대같은 느낌을 덜 이르킨다.
人工으로 山岳을 파헷치고 물을 디리대여서 호수를 만드렀다는 그 노력과 공적엔 높은 敬意를 표하지 않을 수 없다.
K와 나는 한가하게 호수 언저리 빈배머리에 걸터앉아서 노래와 이야기로 밤가는 줄을 몰랐다. 구름이 끼여 달빛도 볼 수 없다. 호수는 어둠에 함뽁 가리었고 이따금 바람 건느는 소리가 물을 차고 찰락찰락할 뿐이다.
湖水 언저리로 동불을 장식하고 노래로 밤을 새는(119) 그런 호수가 어서 되었으면 하고 나는 이 호수의 아름다운 미래를 꿈꾸어 보았다. 조용한 호수면이다. 우리의 이야기도 한없이 조용하여 물우에서 기웃기웃하는 배에 몸을 실은 채 한밤이 즐거웁기로 했다.
방에 돌아와 K는 동무들께 편지를 쓰는 모양이었다. 나도 누구에겐지 모르게 기다란 편지를 썼다. 다- 쓰고 나서 읽어보니 대단히 자미있다. 그러나 누구에게 썼는지 그 이름이 세상에는 없는 것이었다. 나는 봉투에 편지를 넣고 편지 받을 사람 이름을 쓰랴했으나 내가 현재 아는 이중엔 이 편지 받을 이가 없는 것 같았다. 아모도 이 편지 안에 담긴 심경을 이해해줄 듯 싶지 않었다. 나는 상대없는 글을 써놓고 뜯어온 풀들을 다시 한번 가즈란이 세여놓고 잠들었다.
이튿날 정오에 우리는 배를 타고 들아오는 길에 올랐다. 올 때보다 물도 잔잔하고 하늘도 고요해서 풍경을 살피기에 좋왔다.
나는 아직 다른 나라에 가본 일이 없으나 조선만치 풍부한 자연을 가진 곳도 드물 것이라고 스사로 믿어버렸다. 나는 이런 생각을 하며 핸드빽에서 어제밤 써 두었던 문체의 편지를 그대로 호수에 던젔다. 나는 이렇게 공상적 작란을 질기는 성미를 가젔다.
松興驛까지 오며 내내 K가 편지를 가지고 나를 놀려댔다. K는 재미있는 동무다.
赴戰高原엔 들죽물이 유명하다. 어떻게 구할 길이 없을가 하고 망서리는 지음에 마침 松興驛前에서 여인네들이 들고나와 팔고 있다. 나는 1圓짜리 두병을 사서 꾸려 넣었다. K는 서울까지 가지고 간다고 세병이나 샀다. 경편철도에 다시 앉으니 그제서야 호수의 面影이 새로 그립고 떠나온 것이 서운하였다. 못잊어운 딸기숲이며, 놀라도록 크다란 물방아며, 이름 모를 가지가지 꽃일름, 풀이름이 다시 한번 머리에 펀득펀득 신선한 감을 이르킨다.
K와 나는 꽃열매가 시들지 않도록 자조자조 망태에서 꺼내보고 잃어버리지 않었다하여 조사에 분망하였다. 함흥역은 대단이 복잡하였다. K는 오늘밤으로 서울을 간다고 욱였으나 구지 붙잡어 우리 집으로 끄을었다. 열두시가 되도록 캐여온 풀과 꽃나무를 화분에, 화병에 모시노라고 고단한 줄도 몰랐다. 그 중에 며느리풀이라는 풀이 하나 없어젔다고 K가 근심하였다.(끝) (120)
