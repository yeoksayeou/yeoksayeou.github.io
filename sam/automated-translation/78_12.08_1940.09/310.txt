Magazine Title: 三千里 제12권 제8호
Publication Date: 1940년 09월 01일
Article Title: 初秋의 半島文壇 (The Peninsula Literary Scene in Early Autumn)
Author: 朴啓周 (本社側 - Representing this Magazine)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Discussion/Roundtable Talk)

The Peninsula Literary Scene in Early Autumn
A Roundtable Talk with Women Poets and Novelists on "Literature and Film"
Attendees:
Poet Mo Yunsuk, Novelist Yi Sŏnhŭi
Poet No Ch'ŏnmyŏng, Novelist Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi
Representing this Magazine: Pak Kyeju
Date: July 17th
Location: Kyŏngsŏng Kŭmch'ŏndae Hall (京城金千代會舘)

During the hottest days of summer (三伏), four women poets and novelists gathered in one place to discuss recent serialized newspaper novels, pure novels, poetry, and their authors. They then freely critiqued writers and works from the mainland Japan (内地) literary scene, as well as other foreign works and films, before the topic shifted to plans for summer vacation spots. <180>

Novels and Authors
Pak: It's a pity to interrupt your interesting conversation, but I hope you can continue discussing the topics we intend to cover with the same enthusiasm. First, please share your thoughts on the novels currently being serialized in the newspapers.
No: I haven't read them at all...
Mo: I'm reading Yi T'aejun's "Ch'ŏngch'un Musŏng" (靑春茂盛 - Flourishing Youth), and I quite liked it at first. But lately, the part where Ch'oe Tŭkchu seems to yield her body without affection to a rich man… is it possible to yield one's body without love? Even if it's just for that moment of yielding, whether it's a delusion or clear-headed, offering one's body just to get money, without liking the person, is something a cultured woman should contemplate. Of course, it might be with the intention of getting money for some venture.
Yi: It's the same now as it was initially; there's just no leeway. It feels like a novel written solely for subsistence. It's just so monotonous... As a long novel, it can't compare to Yi Kwangsu's work.
Ch'oe: Why, the first few installments were truly good. There were even passages that felt like reading poetry... Sometimes, while reading, I felt a real sense of urgency. Lately, when scenes depicting café waitresses (女給) lamenting their fates appeared, my interest waned, so I missed a few installments. Just the other day, I saw a rather dull man named Yun appear. I don't know the details because I missed the preceding parts, but it seemed somewhat awkward. It might be difficult for Mr. Yi T'aejun to portray such characters.
No: I read it occasionally, and his clean prose always seems worth appreciating.
Ch'oe: Perhaps it's because I have an excitable personality, but one morning, I found myself thinking of Wŏn Ch'iwŏn as Yi T'aejun himself, even feeling anxious that he too might be experiencing an unforgettable love.
Yi: Really?
Pak: Next, what about others? How is Yi Hyosŏk's serialized novel "Ch'anggong" (蒼空 - Blue Sky)?
Yi: I only read the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報), so I haven't read it.
Ch'oe: Since I read it when I come to the office, I haven't read all of it, just occasionally. I don't know if it's because I read with the preconceived notion that if it's by an author I like, it must be well-written, but even reading just one installment, I could tell he would carry it through without any mistakes.
Mo: I haven't read Yi Hyosŏk's work, but I did read Yu Chino's "Hwasangbo" (華想譜), and I thought it was a work filled abundantly with artistic fragrance while possessing realistic power.
Pak: What about Kim Namch'ŏn's "Sarangŭi Sujokkwon" (사랑의 水族舘 - Aquarium of Love)? <181>
Ch'oe: I've only read that sporadically too, so I don't know it well. It's rash to comment without reading the whole thing.
No: It was quite good that he dealt with scientific civilization, which others haven't typically handled. Like the industrial engineer (産業技師) appearing in "Aquarium of Love" and catching fish with dynamite.
Yi: Right, featuring an industrial technician (産業技手) was certainly interesting.
Mo: He does write well. Much better than before.
Ch'oe: How about Pak T'aewŏn's "Aegyŏng" (愛經) published in "Munjang" (文章)? Have you all read it? I only read one installment, but gosh, he wrote the Korean dialogue (國語對活) so noisily/chaotically (싯구리 오게).
Yi: Anyway, Pak T'aewŏn writes well with his unique style. The scene where the waitresses (女給) describe their husbands is really well drawn. Yi T'aejun is also depicting cafés and writing about waitresses in "Ch'ŏngch'un Musŏng" lately, but Pak T'aewŏn is much better.
Ch'oe: That's probably true; it's a bit difficult for Yi T'aejun's temperament (體質) to write such things. The conversation between that man named Yun and Tŭkchu when he comes to the café, which I mentioned earlier, just doesn't fit at all. He seems to be trying his best, but it feels like he's just fumbling around and then stopping. Perhaps this is because we know Mr. Yi T'aejun... that prim and proper man, even if he spent 10 years at a café counter (죠-바, likely 'bar'), it wouldn't work, it just wouldn't. He should just be gently tending to orchid leaves (蘭草)... writing before an incense burner.
Mo: Yes, that's right. I wish he were a bit more robust.
Pak: And regarding short stories, please critique those published in various magazines this month as you've read them.
Yi: I read Yu Chino's "Chup'ung" (酒朋 - Drinking Companions). According to the author's postscript (附記), it was translated from something published in a mainland Japan (内地) literary magazine. But still, it's not such a good novel that it deserved to be translated and republished, is it?
Ch'oe: I read it too, but I doubt it was republished because the novel itself was good. The magazine probably kept pestering him, so he just fulfilled his obligation (免責) quickly for the time being. I found the portrayal of the characters Cho-gun and Yi-gun quite interesting and well-drawn. At first, I thought it would just depict drinking buddies drinking and having fun, but I liked how it introduced a weighty incident towards the end.
Yi: Right, the ending makes it a heavy novel.
Ch'oe: But looking at this month's "Munjang" (文章), the short stories seemed utterly trivial, so reading them felt trivial too. I did read Ch'unwŏn's (pen name of Yi Kwangsu) "Kimssi Puinjŏn" (金氏夫人傳 - The Story of Mrs. Kim), An Hoenam's "Ŏdum Sok esŏ" (어둠 속에서 - In the Darkness), and Chŏng Int'aek's "Ŏpk'o" (業苦 - Karmic Suffering), and put off the slightly longer ones for later... <182>
No: I wish he would stop with the Buddhist talk now.
Mo: Exactly. His Buddhist narratives just aren't moving (感銘되지 않으니), what can you do? Even without exhorting readers to chant Namu Amitabul within the work, he could make it believable, couldn't he? Our desire to have faith (信仰) and his chanting of Namu Amitabul just don't align at all.
Ch'oe: For Yi Kwangsu, that has become his ideology, so it can't be helped. He probably wouldn't enjoy writing if he excluded that ideology... But I feel this way: although I can't join any specific religious group like Buddhism (佛教), Christianity (예수敎), or Catholicism (天主敎), I do think that faith (信仰) is something one must hold onto. I always end up thinking that the ultimate end (終極) of art is religion (宗教)...
No: Perhaps because I grew up in a Catholic (天主敎) family since childhood, I really want to believe in a religion (宗敎).
Ch'oe: Do you think it will happen? It's absolutely impossible. It would be nice if one could believe so blindly (盲目的), but one has to actually believe. Perhaps our religion is just an illusion (幻影).
Yi: I could never believe in religion, not even if I died.
Pak: How about "Yumang" (流氓 - Vagrants) in "Inmun P'yŏngnon" (人文評論)?
Mo: They don't give us the magazine, so I couldn't read it.
Yi: Right, they don't give us the magazine, so it feels like our connection with them is completely severed.
No: They don't give you the magazine because you don't write for them.
Ch'oe: This person must have been bribed (와이롤 먹은게군 - slang, lit. 'eaten a bribe') by Inmun P'yŏngnon-sa. (Everyone laughs) Since they don't give us the magazine, I can't feel any affection (情). So I don't feel like writing for them. If I write, I end up giving it elsewhere.
Yi: Yes, I just can't feel any affection (情).
Mo: Feeling too much affection (情) is also a problem. (Everyone laughs)
Pak: Cho Yongman published a novel after a long time, did you see it?
Mo: I haven't read it.
Yi: The first half (前半) is a memoir (手記), the latter half (後半) is a novel (小說), but it's still a work written without much effort.
Ch'oe: I started reading it but stopped because the writing lacked charm! How was it?
Yi: The writing seems impeccable.
Pak: How about An Hoenam's piece in this month's "Munjang" (文章)?
Ch'oe: Compared to his other works, it's a lesser piece, but it's about the Sindangjŏng street (新堂町 街) and the delinquent gangs (不良牌) of the Umigwan alley (優秀館 골목), something only a Seoul native (本土) could write. I thought it became an interesting novel due to his distinctive style (獨特한 筆致) and penetrating psychological description (心理描寫).
Yi: By the way, reading Yi T'aejun's "Pamkkil" (밤길 - Night Road) again, I felt that while reading his novels, one feels infinite emotion from the letters (文字) themselves, but once you close the book, there's nothing left. With some novels, you don't feel much emotion while reading, <183> but you feel greater emotion after closing the book, right...
No: But Mr. Yi's writing is clean, so you can feel its fragrance.
Mo: Exactly. Writing requires more than just technique (技巧). It needs to contain soul (魂). Only then can it stir another's soul (魂). Kim Namch'ŏn's writing is dense and such, but after reading it, you feel something weighty.
Yi: His "Sonnyŏnhaeng" (少年行 - A Boy's Journey) is truly good. That might be a work fitting the "spirit of indictment" (告發의 정신) he himself used to cry out for.
Ch'oe: People say "Sonnyŏnhaeng" is good, so I got the book, but his novels somehow just don't suit my taste.
Yi: By the way, he definitely has one flaw. When he writes a novel, he consciously aims (네라우하구 - from Japanese ねらう, nerau) for it to cause various issues (問題) when it goes out into the world...
Ch'oe: Aiming well, meaning aiming purely, would be great, wouldn't it?
Pak: Several Korean authors wrote for "Munyē" (文藝 - Japanese literary magazine). You've all read them, right?
Yi: But all of those novels, truly, were unpleasant to the extreme because they were written with the prior consideration of what subject matter (取材) would gain recognition. They seem to think that expressing Korean sentiment (朝鮮情調) requires including scenes of pounding grain or the Arirang song... Even without overtly showing Korean-ness (朝鮮的인 것) in the subject matter, they could write in a way that clearly shows their difference from those people [Japanese], but lacking talent, they seem to try to gloss over it with just Korean-ness (朝鮮的인 것).
Mo: And when dealing with intellectual women (인테리 여성), I wish they would think more carefully. If the intellectual women introduced in their works seem frivolous and rash, those people [Japanese] might end up recognizing all Korean intellectual women as being like that.
Ch'oe: That's true. I felt that way too after reading Kim Saryang's "Ch'ŏnma" (天馬 - Pegasus). There's a female poet in it, a very frivolous and insignificant woman. If you're going to feature a female poet, I wish they wouldn't portray her in such a manner. His "Hikari no Naka ni" (光の中に - In the Light) was something I read with truly no regrets (遺憾) – far from regrets, I couldn't suppress the joy of discovering another good writer. Really, I cried reading "Hikari no Naka ni." "Ch'ŏnma" is also decent as a work. The personality of the protagonist, a character failure (性格破産者), seems quite interestingly drawn.
Mo: They should just quit writing novels recklessly just to cause trouble (問題).
No: Trying to make a hit through exoticism (엑조티즘) is impure.
Ch'oe: And if they fail as artworks after doing that, it's truly embarrassing (붓그런). It looks servile to those people [Japanese] too. In that respect, women seem truly simple and pure. <184>
Yi: If we were to write like that, would we become like that too?
Ch'oe: Women can't do that. One has to be able to write [sincerely].
No: Men are fundamentally politically (政治的) inclined.
Ch'oe: Would someone like Yi T'aejun be like that too?
Yi: No, he wouldn't. He's the cleanest in that regard.

Poets and Poetry
Pak: Let's stop talking about novels now and discuss poetry.
No: Poets read novels, but novelists don't seem to read poetry.
Ch'oe: Why not? I really read every single poem. Firstly, because there are fewer words, it's easier too.
No: Lately, there have been many truly good poems by Pa In (巴人). It seems he has improved considerably (벗적) recently.
Mo: Why would he suddenly (갑잭이) start writing so well?
Yi: Looking at his poems, he just doesn't seem to age. He's still young. But somehow, he always looks lonely.
Ch'oe: Ch'ŏnmyŏng's "Manghyang" (望鄕 - Homesick) published in "Inmun P'yŏngnon" (人文評論) was also good. It's softer than the poems Ch'ŏnmyŏng has written so far.
No: Paek Sŏk's (白石) poems were good too. And Yi Yuksa (李陸史) also writes good things occasionally these days.
Mo: Yi Yongak (李庸岳), O Changhwan (吳章煥) also write good poems, don't they?
Ch'oe: Yi Yongak's poem "Param Sok esŏ" (바람 속에서 - In the Wind) in Samch'ŏlli (三千里) was good too. He's still young, but his writing seems quite mature. And Kim Kwangsŏp's (金珖燮) poems are good too. His poems that always appeal with sad and lonely feelings are truly good. Like anyone who truly writes, it's clearly evident in his writing that he enjoys (享樂) loneliness for the sake of writing. Isn't "Uae" (友愛 - Friendship) in Samch'ŏlli a masterpiece too? O Changhwan's bold, nihilistic, and sad sentiment (情緖) is also good...
No: Anyway, they are all formidable powerhouses: Kim Kwanggyun (金光均), O Changhwan (吳章煥), Chang Manyŏng (張萬榮), Yi Yongak (李庸岳).
Pak: How about Mo Yunsuk's poems?
No: "Haesu" (海愁 - Sea Sorrow), I think it was, in "Samch'ŏlli" (三千里) was good. Only there did she break away from her conventional Rossetti-esque style. But I don't know why new talents (新進) aren't emerging.
Mo: They can't emerge because the men are incompetent, what else?
No: When Ms. Mo debuted, the conditions must have been good. Now, you have to emerge with real skill, so it seems difficult. By the way, I wonder why Chi Yong (芝溶) isn't writing these days. Yŏngnang (永郞) – that person wrote something truly good.
Ch'oe: Yes, his poems have an open quality.
No: Yŏngnang is stylish, and his vocabulary (語彙) is also very rich.
Yi: Still, Chi Yong is someone who defined an era. Yŏngnang is still young.
No: That's not true.
Ch'oe: Yŏngnang seems like a person with a lot of talent. <185> He seems like a poet who will continue to grow abundantly in the future.
No: Kim Kirim's (金起林) poems are quite dense too. You feel a coldness from his poems that are preoccupied with technique (技巧).
Ch'oe: Why, what he writes these days isn't like that compared to before. After returning from mainland Japan (内地), there's a warmth. Not just in his poems, but even in his short writings (쩔막한 글), you can tell he wrote with feeling.
Yi: Right. The modernism from before is gone. It's certain that he has changed. There's blood flowing in his poems.
Ch'oe: He'll probably get better in about 10 years.
Mo: O Changhwan's (吳章煥) poems are Korean (朝鮮的) yet also have a Greek (希臘的) quality.
Ch'oe: How about Chang Manyŏng's (張萬榮) "Hyangsu" (鄕愁 - Nostalgia)? Isn't it truly good? It's the one I liked most among his poems. I felt a truly good human nature (人間性) and beautiful emotion (感情). But some other poems aren't like that at all. Some seem like they were written by a completely different person.

Film (映畵)
Pak: Shall we move on to films (映畵)?
Mo: "Ch'ang Ŏmnŭn Kamok" (창없는 監獄 - Prison Without Windows) was good. The personality of the protagonist (主人公), the young girl (少女), was good. I see her as an image (像) of the new era. Her way of loving a man is also frank and dynamic (動的)... Anyway, I liked that she was different from conventional women (在來의 女性).
Yi: Did you see the movie "Circus" (써거쓰)? The one starring Françoise Rosay (푸란쏘와 로제)...
Ch'oe: I saw it. She's my favorite actress (女俳優), how could I not? The movie itself is nonsense (넌센쓰), but because Françoise Rosay makes it good, it has value as a film. You know, the part where the people who go ahead to set up the temporary stage – Sakkanori, or whatever they're called – drive iron stakes (쇠말뚝) with iron hammers (쇠망치)?
That pounding sound, clang clang, stayed in my ears even after leaving the theater, I couldn't stand it. That couldn't possibly happen if the movie wasn't good.
Yi: Françoise Rosay's 'hmph' and shoulder shrug is great. It's truly charming (매력). And the part where she sends off her husband and cries with trembling lips is also a masterpiece.
Ch'oe: That might be why the sound of the iron hammer disturbed (散亂케 했을지) my ears for so long.
Mo: But would men like a woman like Rosay?
Yi: Who knows. Men tend not to like women whom other women like.
Ch'oe: That might be true, but there are specific men who would like Rosay.
Mo: If you were to look for a woman like Rosay in Korea, who would it be?
Yi: Hmm, perhaps someone like Pak Indŏk? She resembles her in many ways.
Pak: Are there any other movies you've seen? <186>
Mo: "Muyŏkp'ung" (貿易風 - Trade Winds) was good.
Ch'oe: "Trade Winds" isn't really a movie worth calling good... worth discussing here...
Yi: I just remembered, Pak Yongch'ŏl (朴龍喆), who passed away, used to say that the woman's crying when she parts from the man in "Samak'ŭi Hwawŏn" (沙漠의 花園 - Garden of Allah / Desert Garden) was a masterpiece for the ages... If even one part can be deeply impressed (백여질수) upon one's memory, it's decent.

Reading (讀書)
Pak: Among foreign works you've read recently - were there any good ones?
Mo: I read Balzac's (발작크) "Valley of the Lily" (谷間의 百合), and it was truly good. An older woman loves a younger boy, and while telling herself she shouldn't love him, her heart keeps getting drawn to him; it's really interesting.
Yi: I read a few short stories by Thomas Hardy (토마쓰 하디), but if a Korean (朝鮮) author wrote like that, they'd be criticized for writing trash (駄作). "The Woman Who Pursued a Phantom" (幻影을 쫓는 여자), "The Melancholy Hussar" (슲음의 포기兵), "An Imaginative Woman" (이룰 수 없는 所望 - actually title is 'An Imaginative Woman', Korean title means 'Unattainable Desire') - all these are works that weave a single story while depicting fantasy (幻影). In other words, there's hardly any description (묘사), and no characterization.
Ch'oe: In my opinion, though perhaps it's because I still don't know what a novel is, there are writings one really wants to write, right? In such cases, rather than forcing various things in to make it resemble a novel, I think writing down exactly what one thinks is more precious and valuable. Maybe that's why I read Hardy's works with deep emotion. I also envy a society that understands such works. This is off-topic, but have you read Chŏng Pisŏk's (鄭飛石) novel "Ch'orogŭi Pyŏn" (草綠의 辨 - Discourse of Green) in "Yŏsŏng" (女性)?
Mo: Haven't read it.
Yi: They don't give us the magazine, how would I know if it came out?
No: "Yŏsŏng" (女性) should really distribute the magazine more.
Ch'oe: Reading "Ch'orogŭi Pyŏn," I thought it was quite interesting. A few lines of dialogue grated on my nerves, but among the works he's written, it's a novel I read with interest. Though it's lighter than "Sŏnghwangdang" (城隍堂).
Mo: The newcomers (新進) write well these days.
Ch'oe: Yes, people like Kim Tongni (金東里) and Hyŏn Tŏk (玄德), I believe, possess better writerly qualities (作家的 素質) than anyone.
No: Just because someone debuted in the literary world (文壇) long ago doesn't mean they write well.
Pak: Are there any other things you've read?
Mo: I'm reading "Amiel's (아미엘) Journal" (日記).
No: I read George Sand's (죠르즈 쌍드) "Indiana" (안디아나) and Huxley's (학스레) "Point Counter Point" (포인트카운트포인트), they were good.
Pak: Have you read anything by mainland Japanese (內地作家) authors?
No: I read Hori Tatsuo's (掘辰雄) "Seikazoku" (聖家族 - The Holy Family). It's very clean writing.
Ch'oe: It's writing that makes one think of the world of fairy tales (童話). The narrowness of its world might be a flaw, but <187> the glass-clear (유리같이 맑은) prose (文章) is also truly good.
Yi: I haven't read any mainland Japanese (内地) works at all.
No: I read Ishikawa Tatsuzō's (石川達三) "Kekkon no Seitai" (結婚の生態 - The Ecology of Marriage), it was good.
Ch'oe: I'm planning to read Okamoto Kanoko (岡本かの子) these days. I've only read one piece, but she seems like a truly rich woman.
Pak: Please tell us about your impressions of the mainland Japanese (內地作家) authors you've met.
Mo: I've only met Kume Masao (久米政雄) and Hamamoto Hiroshi (濱本浩).
Yi: When did you meet them all? I haven't seen a single one.
Mo: When those people came, they held a roundtable discussion at the Chosŏn Hotel (朝鮮호텔), didn't they? So I went and saw them then.
Ch'oe: You went as the chief representative, I suppose. I met Shimaki Kensaku (島木健作). When he was sitting and talking, he was fine, but when he stood up, he was so short that my affection completely vanished (정이 똑 떠러지겠지). Even the affection I felt when I enjoyed reading "Goku" (獄 - Prison) completely disappeared.

Where to for Summer Vacation (避暑) This Year?
Pak: Where are you all going for summer vacation (避暑) this year?
Mo: I'm going to the Nile River (나일강).
Yi: Here starts Mo Yunsuk's fantasy (夢幻) again.
Yi: Songjŏn (松田) seems good. Let's all go to Songjŏn. I have to bring all my kids, maybe Ch'ŏnmyŏng and Chŏnghŭi can each take care of one...
No: Okay, I'll watch your child. But I'll take the little one; I don't like the big one. What if he keeps asking for money? (Everyone laughs)
Ch'oe: I like the big one; he's chubby (숭굴숭굴해서), so asking for money isn't an issue. I like that he looks like a Westerner. (Everyone laughs)
No: Even if I watch the kid, I won't change diapers (똥은 안 칠테야). (Everyone laughs)
Yi: If he poops, just call me. (Everyone laughs)
No: By the way, if we're cooking for ourselves (자췰할려면), we need to prepare and bring all the food, right?
Mo: Let's each take charge of one thing.
No: I don't have anything. No soy sauce (장), no red pepper paste (고추장).
Ch'oe: I'll take care of things like that. But I think Monggŭmp'o (夢金浦) sounds better than Songjŏn (松田). Perhaps because the name sounds nice, I really want to go there.
No: It's too desolate, you couldn't live there.
Ch'oe: Isn't desolate better?
No: That's easy to say, but it's utterly unbearable.
Ch'oe: How wonderful is a loneliness so unbearable you feel like dying?
Yi: I'd like to experience such loneliness just once. -(End)- <188>

**Summary**

Four prominent female Korean writers – Mo Yunsuk, Yi Sŏnhŭi, No Ch'ŏnmyŏng, and Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi – engage in a lively roundtable discussion in July 1940. They critique contemporary Korean novels (serialized and short stories) and poetry, discussing authors like Yi T'aejun, Yi Kwangsu, Yi Hyosŏk, Kim Namch'ŏn, Pak T'aewŏn, Yu Chino, Pa In, Paek Sŏk, and others. They also touch upon Japanese literature (mentioning authors like Hori Tatsuo, Ishikawa Tatsuzō, Okamoto Kanoko, Shimaki Kensaku), foreign works (Balzac, Hardy, Sand, Huxley), films (including French cinema), and their personal reading habits, before concluding with lighthearted plans for summer vacation. The conversation reveals their distinct personalities, critical standards, and engagement with the literary trends of the time.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for capturing the voices and opinions of leading female intellectuals during the late colonial period. Their candid discussion about Korean authors' attempts to appeal to Japanese audiences (critiquing superficial "Korean-ness" and exoticism), interactions with Japanese writers, and concerns about how Korean women are portrayed reveal the complex cultural dynamics under colonial rule. The mention of specific magazines like "Munjang," "Inmun P'yŏngnon," and the Japanese "Munyē" provides insight into the publishing landscape, while the casual discussion of contemporary Korean, Japanese, and Western literature and film illustrates the cultural consumption patterns of the educated elite in 1940s Seoul (Kyŏngsŏng).

初秋의 半島文壇
女流詩人과 小說家의『文學, 映畵』를 말하는 座談會
出席者
詩人 毛允淑 小說家 李善熙
詩人 盧天命 小說家 崔貞熙
本社側·朴啓周

場所·京城金千代會舘

三伏에 여류시인, 소설가 4氏가 一席에 모여 最近作 신문장편과 순수소설과 시와 그 작가를 논하고, 다시 內地 문단人과 작품, 기타 외국작품과 영화 등을 縱橫으로 評하여 말한 후, 稍夏의 避暑地 풀랜에까지 話題가 미치다.＜180＞

小說과 作家
朴. 여러분이 재미있게 이얘기하시는데, 안됐습니다. 마는 지금 말씀하시는 그 기분대로, 인제 앞으루 하고저 하는 얘기두 끌구 나갔으면 합니다. 그러면, 먼저 요새 신문에 연재되는 소설부터 말씀들 해주십시요.
盧. 난 통 보질 안어서...
毛. 난 李泰俊氏 「靑春茂盛」을 읽는데, 처음엔 퍽 좋드군요. 그런데, 요샌 최득주가 부자사람에게 마음에도 없이 육체를 허락하는 것 같은 것, 사랑하지 않으면서 육체를 허락할 수 있을까. 육체를 허락하는 그 순간만이래두 착각이든, 똑바루든, 그 사람이 좋와 안지는데 돈을, 얻기 위해서 몸을 받친단건 교양있는 여자로서 생각할 문제라구 봐. 물론 돈을 얻어서 무슨 사업이나 하자는 마음에서 겠지만.
李. 처음이나 지금이나, 마찬가지안야, 도모지, 여유가 없는게. 생활을 위해서 쓰는 소설루 박겐 생각되잖아. 도모지, 單調하구...장편으로선 李光洙氏를 따를 수 없어.
崔. 웨, 처음 몇 회는 참 좋왔지. 꼭 시를 읽는 듯한 구절두 있구... 난 어떤 땐, 읽으면서 정말 절박한 걸 느끼는 때가 있었어. 요새 카페-女給들이 제 신세 타령하는 장면이 나오면선 흥미가 별루 없어서 몇 회 못읽었지만. 그적껜가 어적껜가 보니까, 아주 똔퐌같은 윤이란 남자가 나타나드군 그래. 그 전의 것들을 못 읽어서 모르긴하지만, 어떻게 어색한 것 같드군. 李泰俊氏는 그런 인물 그리긴 힘들지두 몰라.
盧. 난 가다감 읽어 보는데, 그의 깨끗한 문장이 늘 살만 하드군.
崔. 난 흥분을 잘하는 성격이라 그런지, 어떤 날 아츰엔 원치원을 李泰俊氏 자신같이 생각하군, 그이두 못잊을 사랑을 하는거 아닐까 하는 안타까움까지 느끼게 된다우.
李. 그랬어?
朴. 그담, 또 다른 것, 李孝石氏 장편 「蒼空」은 어떻습니까?
李. 朝鮮日報밖에 안보니까, 못읽었어요.
崔. 社에 나와서 읽게 되니까, 다는 못읽구 각금 읽었는데 언제나, 난 내가 좋아하는 작가의 것이면 으레 잘 썼으려니 하는 선입관념을 가지고 읽어서 그런지 모르지만 단 한 회를 읽드래두 그이가 아모런 실수 없이 끌어 나가리라는 것을 알게 되드군.
毛. 난 李孝石氏건 못읽고, 兪鎭午氏 「華想譜」는 읽었는데, 현실적 박력이 있으면서, 예술적 향기를 담북 담은 작품이라 생각했어.
朴. 金南天氏의 「사랑의 水族舘」은 어떻습디까.＜181＞
崔. 그것두 각금 가다가 읽어서 잘 몰르겠는데요. 다 읽지 않구. 말하는 건 경솔하구.
盧. 종래에, 다른 사람들이 취급 않든 과학문명을 취급한게 퍽 좋왔어요. 「사랑의 水族舘」에 産業技師가 나와서, 다이나마이트로 고기를 잡는 것이라든지.
李. 그렇지, 産業技手를 등장식혔다는 건 분명히 재미있었어.
毛. 그이가 쓰긴 잘 씁디다. 전보다 퍽 났습디다.
崔. 朴泰遠氏가 「文章」에 실린 「愛經」은 어때? 다들 읽어? 난 다는 못 읽구 한 횔 읽엇는데 거참 國語對활 싯구리 오게 썼든데.
李. 어쨌든 朴泰遠씬 그 독특한 문장을 가지구 잘 써. 女給들의 남편을 그리는 장면, 참 잘 그려요. 李泰俊씨두 요새 「靑春茂盛」에서 카-페를 그리고 女給들 얘길 쓰는데 朴泰遠씨가 훨신 나아.
崔. 그럴거야, 李泰俊氏 체질룬 그런 걸 쓰기가 좀 힘들지. 내 앗가 말한 윤 뭐라는 사나이가 카-페와서 득주와 하는 수작같은 거 도모지 얼리지 않어. 자기로선 깃껏 해보느라구 하는데 어르만지다가 그만 두는 것 같단 말야, 이런건 아마 우리가 李泰俊씰 알기때문인진 모르지만... 그이 그 샌님같은 이가, 카페-가서 죠-바에 10년 있는대두 틀렸어 틀렸어. 그인 거저 곱게, 蘭草 잎이나 닥거주며... 향불 앞에서 글쓸 이야.
毛. 그래 마졌어. 좀 씩씩했으면 좋겠어.
朴. 그리구 단편은 이달에 나온 각 잡지 것을 읽으신대루 評해 주십시요.
李. 兪鎭午氏 「酒朋」을 읽었어요. 작가의 附記를 보면 內地문단 어느 잡지에 실렸든 것을, 번역했다구 했드군. 그렇지만, 번역까지 해서 다시 실릴만한 좋은 소설은 못되잖어?
崔. 나두 읽었지만, 그거야 소설이 좋와서 다시 실린게 아니겠지. 잡지사에서 작구 졸르니까, 위선 급한대루 免責한 거겠지. 조군이라는 者, 이군이란 者의 성격이 퍽 재미있게 잘 그려젔다구 보았어. 처음엔, 거저 술친구들이 술먹구 노는 걸 그렷겠거니 했는데 나종에 가서 생각을 무겁게 하는 사건을 끌어댄 것이 퍽 좋왔어.
李. 그렇지, 나종이 무거운 소설이지
崔. 그런데 이번 달 「文章」을 보니까, 소설 짧은 건 도모지 작난같애서 읽는 것두 작난같두만. 읽긴 春園선생의 「金氏夫人傳」, 安懷南氏의 「어둠 속에서」, 鄭人澤氏의 「業苦」 등을 읽구 좀 긴 건 나중으로 돌렸지만...＜182＞
盧. 그부의 부첫님 얘긴 이젠 좀 그만뒀으면.
毛. 그러게 말이야. 그이 佛敎 얘기하는 건 도모지 感銘되지 않으니 어떡해. 작품 속에서 나미아미타불을 부르라구 역설하지 않드래두 믿을 수 있도록 할수 있잔어. 우리가 신앙을 가지려는 마음과 그이가 나무아미타불을 부르는 것이 도모지 일치 안돼.

毛. 요새 新進들이 잘 써.
崔. 그래 金東里, 玄德같은 분은 누구보다두 우린 좋은 작가적 소질을 가젔다구 봐.
盧. 文壇에 나온지 오래다구 좋은 걸 쓰는 것두 아니지.
朴. 그 담에 읽으신 건 없습니까.
毛. 「아미엘의 日記」를 읽구 있죠.
盧. 죠르즈, 쌍드의 「안디아나」와 학스레-「포인트카운트포인트」를 읽었는데 좋습디다.
朴. 內地작가 것은 읽은 거 없습니까.
盧. 掘辰雄의 「聖家族」을 읽었어요. 퍽 깨끗한 글이얘요.
崔. 童話의 세계를 생각하게 하는 글이지 세계가 좁은 것이 탈이겠지만,＜187＞ 유리같이 맑은 文章두 참 좋와.
李. 난 內地건 통 못 읽었어.
盧. 石川達三의 「結婚의 生態」를 읽었는데 좋와.
崔. 난 요새 岡本かの子 걸 읽으려구 해. 한 개밖에 못 읽었지만, 참 풍부한 女잔 것 같어.
朴. 內地작가 만나보신 인상들이나 말슴해 주십시요.
毛. 久米政雄, 濱本浩, 두 분박게 못 만났어요.
李. 언제 다 그리 만나봤어, 우뤀° 하나두 못 봤구만.
毛. 그 사람들이 왔을 때, 朝鮮호텔에서 좌담회 하드군 그래, 그래 그때 가서봤지.
崔. 총대표루 가셨군. 난 島木健作을 만났는데 그이가 앉어서 이얘기만 할 땐 괜찮었는데, 이러서니까 키가 어떻게 적은지 정이 똑 떠러지겠지. 「獄」을 읽으며 좋와 하든 때의 정까지 아주 딱 떠러지구 말었어.

今年 避暑는 어디로?
朴. 금년 피서는 어디들 가십니까.
毛. 나일강으루 가겠어요.
李. 이거 또 毛允淑의 夢幻이 시작되는군.
李. 松田이 좋을 것 같이. 우리 다들 松田가자구. 난 아이들이랑 다 대리구 가야 할텐데, 天命 貞熙 하나씩 마터보구...
盧. 그래 내 아이 봐주지, 그런데 난 작은 애나 보지 큰 앤 싫어. 이게 글세 작구 돈만 달래문 어째.(一同笑)
崔. 난 큰애가 좋와, 숭굴숭굴해서 돈 달래는 거 쯤은 문제안야. 그거 서양 놈 같은 게 좋드라.(一同笑)
盧. 앤 봐두 난 똥은 안 칠테야.(一同笑)
李. 똥을 싸거든 나 불러대라구.(一同笑)
盧. 그런데 자췰할려면, 뭐 먹을 것들 죄다 마련해가지구 가야지.
毛. 죄 각각 한가지식 마터가지구 가지.
盧. 난 아무 것두 없어. 장두 없구 고추장두 없구.
崔. 그런건 내가 담당하지. 그런데 난 松田보다 夢金浦가 졸 것 같어. 이름이 좋와서 그런지 꼭 가보구 싶어.
盧. 너무 쓸쓸해서 못 살어.
崔. 쓸쓸한 게 더 조찬어.
盧. 말이 그렇지, 도모지 견딜 수 없어요.
崔. 견딜 수 없는 죽구 싶도록 되는 고독이 얼마나 좋와.
