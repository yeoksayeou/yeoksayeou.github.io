Striving for the Establishment of a New Order in East Asia, Statement by the New Konoe Cabinet

The world situation has recently taken a sudden turn, showing astonishing changes. The old world order began to collapse first in Europe and is now about to extend to other regions of the world. Faced with this momentous global change, our country must also strive for a renewal of its domestic system. Considering this, if various opinions within the country clash and contend with each other, we cannot concentrate our strength on external relations and will lose the opportunity for decisive action by looking left and right. Generally, the evils of past political parties are these two. First, in the purpose of their establishment, they adopted liberalism, democracy, or socialism, and their fundamental worldview and view of life were already incompatible with the national polity (國體); this point must be rapidly re-evaluated today and fundamentally revised. Second, their main objective in party formation was the struggle for political power; this is decidedly not the way of assisting the Imperial rule (大政翼贊) in the legislature. Discarding these two evils, we must return to the true form of Japan, look up to the great Imperial will (大御心), and strive for sincere service with one hundred million hearts as one (一億一心). Thus, the problem is by no means limited to political parties alone. In civil and military affairs, in the navy and army, in the government and the public, high and low, truly with one mind, we must assist the great Imperial rule as directed by His Majesty (陛下). That is, in this new system (新體制), the hundred million subjects (億兆), with one mind, must serve the great Imperial will, and thereby swiftly and appropriately resolve the numerous problems piling up at home and abroad amidst this historic, momentous global change. I first had to strive for the realization of this new system within the government departments, and fortunately, seeing complete unity and cooperation between the Army and Navy, and between the Army/Navy and the Foreign Ministry, I believe this cabinet, under an unshakable policy, can overcome any difficulties henceforth and forge ahead on a single path. Now, I wish to state two or three points of that policy. First, in foreign policy, we must stand firm to the end in the Empire's independent <43> position and walk the Empire's unique path. Though called a unique path, this by no means signifies a passive, independent foreign policy. Not merely responding to this global change, we must have the resolve to proactively guide global change and establish a new world order through our own strength. Therefore, foreign policy must not be caught up in immediate movements but must think 10, 20, 50 years ahead and proceed independently, actively, and constructively to the very end.

Next, in economics, to powerfully realize the aforementioned foreign policy goals, we must escape from the system of dependence on foreign countries as quickly as possible. Economic cooperation with our allies Manchu (滿洲) and China (支那), and development towards the South Seas (南洋) region are, in this sense, becoming increasingly necessary. Of course, for the time being, there will likely be shortages of goods, and supply and demand may not be smooth. In response, the government intends to exert its full power to secure essential daily necessities for the people. However, this problem truly relates to the daily lives of each of the 100 million citizens, and all citizens must discard selfishness, strive diligently for active production increases on the one hand, and endeavor towards conservation on the other.

Generally, no nation that indulges in luxury and pleasure (奢侈逸樂) has ever prospered; history shows no such example. The government also intends to make cuts in the budget as much as possible, eliminating non-urgent items and curtailing wasteful spending. However, various controls will have to be imposed on the private sector—but this too is not intended to needlessly suppress individual initiative or trample upon the aspirations of the people. Rather, in this extraordinary time at home and abroad, lastly, education. At this time when we attempt a major shift in foreign policy and economics to respond to the times and overcome great difficulties, it is impermissible for the field of education alone to cling stubbornly to old ways (舊態墨守).

When thinking about the future of the nation, the success or failure of national policy and the rise or fall of the people depend entirely on education. The so-called training of Imperial subjects (皇國民鍊成)—we must realize this not just in words, but from the very root of the soul. We must deeply reflect on the failing of past scholarship, which discussed abstract speculations and was ignorant of practical matters. However, to truly cultivate second-generation citizens who will bear the responsibility of the state based on the national polity, educators themselves must possess the sincerity to value the Way (道). The government intends to promote educational reform under this policy. Above, I have stated a part of my thoughts, and though it is just a part, it is the most crucial point. Therefore, on the occasion of the launch of this new cabinet, I declare this, hoping for the cooperation of all citizens, while I myself will also strive diligently for its realization, humbly endeavoring to fulfill the path of assisting the Imperial rule (大政輔翼). <44>

**Summary**

This article presents a statement from the new Konoe Cabinet in 1940 outlining its policy for establishing a "New Order in East Asia." It stresses the need for national unity under the Emperor, advocating the dissolution of political parties deemed incompatible with the "national polity" (Kokutai) and focused on power struggles. The statement emphasizes a shift towards an independent, expansionist foreign policy aimed at guiding global change and establishing a new world order. Economically, it calls for self-sufficiency, breaking dependence on foreign powers through cooperation with Manchukuo and China and expansion into the "South Seas," while urging public austerity and increased production. Finally, it highlights the critical role of educational reform to discard impractical scholarship and cultivate "Imperial subjects" (皇國民) loyal to the state and the Emperor.

For historians of colonial Korea, this statement is significant as it explicitly lays out the ideological and policy framework Japan intended to impose across its sphere of influence, including Korea. It reveals the justification for increased totalitarian control, the suppression of dissenting ideologies (liberalism, democracy, socialism), the economic mobilization underpinning the nascent Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere, and the intensification of assimilation policies through education aimed at creating "Imperial subjects"—all of which directly and profoundly impacted the Korean populace under Japanese rule.

대체 奢侈逸樂을 일삼고 興隆한 국가는 아직 그 예를 볼 수 없다. 정부도 예산에 있어 가급적 삭감을 가하여 不急을 除하고 冗費를 절약하려 한다. 그런데 민간에 대하여는 여러가지 통제를 하지않으면 안되겠는데-이것도 헛되히 개인의 總意를 억제하여 민간의 희망을 蹂躙함과 같은 생각은 없다. 차라리 내외의 非常한 時勢에 최후로 교육인데 외교에 있어 경제에 있어 時勢에 應하여 大轉回를 試하여 大困難을 극복하려는 이 때에 교육방면만 舊態를 墨守함은 不許하는 바이다.
