Article Title: 民族의 祭典 (Festival of Peoples)
Article Type: 회고·수기 (Reminiscence/Memoir)

Festival of Peoples
Hearing reminiscences from the World Champions Mr. Son Ki-jŏng and Mr. Nam Sŭng-nyong about the Olympia film "Festival of Peoples" from the Berlin Games 4 years ago.

Critique of the Film "Festival of Peoples"
Kim Tong-hwan: Please share your impressions after watching the film "Festival of Peoples," which had a preview screening at the Kyŏngil Cultural Film Theater on July 2nd and 3rd.
Son Ki-jŏng: To see the events of four years ago, especially the scenes where I ran, through film <48> now is truly filled with deep emotion. Through this movie, I felt a different kind of emotion than when I directly witnessed the scenes at the Berlin Games back then. "Festival of Peoples" is a sports film, and thus a documentary film, but I paid respects to the groundbreaking composition that pioneered a new domain through the artistic talent of Ms. Leni Riefenstahl. As you all saw at the preview, I will avoid lengthy descriptions, but through this film, watching the blood and flesh of the youth from the five continents boiling and leaping for the honor of their homelands, continuing the most sincere and pure national struggle amidst tension and excitement—in that "Leap of Physical Beauty," I believe viewers will feel a sublime humanism and magnificent physical beauty, experiencing the "Praise of Humanity" with the same tension and excitement as the athletes.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: In any case, the Olympic Games are a grand festival of peoples where nations from the five continents compete in a "Peaceful Exchange." Through the film "Festival of Peoples," the earnest and pure emotions boil up with every single move of each athlete in every event. Yet, it doesn't just end as a documentary film; the fact that the personalities are well depicted and it exudes an artistic fragrance makes me, too, not hesitate to express my gratitude to Ms. Riefenstahl.
Mo Yun-suk: I feel that the director of "Festival of Peoples," Leni Riefenstahl, <49> is not just a film director but also seems like a splendid poet. I was moved by the depiction of the formal beauty of the ancient Greek architectural sculptures at the Olympia ruins and the graceful forms of the male and female nude sculptures.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: In my opinion, a male director likely couldn't have represented the Olympic scenes so meticulously and clearly, even more vividly than seeing them with one's own eyes, in a documentary film. In other words, it's because she was a female director that such a wonderful film was made.
Son Ki-jŏng: Ha! Oh my, you're praising women too much. (All laugh)
Pak Kye-ju: One might view the Black American athlete Owens as a grotesque monster, excluding him from humanity, but how human he appears in the film "Festival of Peoples"! His sharp gaze on the starting line, his heavy breathing, the state of extreme tension where he moistens his dry lips with the tip of his tongue—it's truly human, a majestic figure brimming with humanity.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: I felt a certain charm in Owens' sincere attitude, struggling with anxiety and tension, and then when he suddenly breaks into a smile. Indeed, even President Hitler, whom I had known until then as just a stoic figure, shows a different kind of expression when his country's athletes compete, especially the female athletes even more so than the male athletes. It made me realize, "Ah, he too is a human being with blood flowing through him," and I found it somewhat interesting.
Son Ki-jŏng: In any case, I think the film demonstrated the highest level of the capabilities of cinema. I also wrote a review of "Festival of Peoples" in the June issue of "Samch'ŏlli" (三千里). The 11th Olympiad film is divided into Part 1 and Part 2. Part 1 is "Festival of Peoples," and Part 2 is "Festival of Beauty."
For this Olympia film, the German government formed the Olympia Film Association with Ms. Leni Riefenstahl as the head of production, deciding to devote all efforts to recording the Games on film. The entire German film industry was mobilized for this, and it's said that there were 44 front-line cameramen just for shooting. It particularly employed the essence of modern German camera technology. From recording to editing, Ms. Leni Riefenstahl spent a whole year, and the 44 cameramen were fully mobilized to shoot from every three-dimensional angle.
"Festival of Peoples" (Fest der Völker) consists of 12 reels in total, starting from the sacred flame on Olympia's Kronos Hill, covering all track and field events, and closing with the finish of the marathon. During the filming, there was even aerial photography using balloons, that is, bird's-eye view shots. <50>

Origin of the Olympic Games
Kim Tong-hwan: Please tell us a bit about the origin of the Olympic Games.
Son Ki-jŏng: The Olympic Games first began 2,700 years ago, in 776 BC, in Olympia, Greece. At that time, the Olympiad was held as a ritual offering to the god Zeus (제우스). Thereafter, the games continued to be held every four years along with the ritual offering to Zeus, and it's said that the splendid culture of Ancient Greece flourished centered around this ritual. However, entering the Roman (羅馬) era, due to the influence of Christianity, which overly emphasized spiritual life over physical life, the Olympic Games gradually declined. Furthermore, in 396 AD, coinciding with the 293rd Olympiad, Emperor Theodosius I proclaimed a ban on the Olympic festival two years later in 394 AD. Consequently, the historic games, which had continued for 1,168 years, inevitably faced the misfortune of cessation. On top of that, when the Temple of Zeus was moved to Constantinople in 476 AD, it burned down. The Goths invaded the sacred site of Olympia, and later, there was even an earthquake, causing the once luxurious Olympia to be buried 20 feet underground.
Thus, the Olympic Games, which had fallen into a state of eternal extinction, saw the ideas of evolution and collectivism, which were flourishing in Europe after the Renaissance, become the cultural womb for the Olympic revival movement. Entering the 19th century, many archaeologists emerged in Europe who took an interest in the study of Ancient Greece, leading to the popularity of excavation projects. Among these, the decisive success was achieved through the excavation work of the German scholar Ernst Curtius (에룬스트·클티우스). Over seven full years, from 1874 to 1881, the ruins of Olympia were almost completely revealed on the surface once again.
The excavation of these Olympia ruins led to a revival of the Hellenic spirit. Inspired by this, the modern revival of the Olympic <51> Games was attempted through the dedicated efforts of the French educator Baron Pierre de Coubertin (삐-엘·드·크멜단) in 1896, with the first Games opening in the historic city of Athens (雅典), Greece.
Pak Kye-ju: After that, where were the Games held up to the 11th?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: The 2nd Games were held in Paris (巴里), France in 1900; the 3rd in St. Louis (聖路易) in 1904; the 4th in London (론돈), England in 1908; the 5th in Stockholm (스톡홂), Sweden in 1912; the 6th was canceled due to World War I; the 7th in Antwerp (安府) in 1920; the 8th in Paris, France in 1924; the 9th in Amsterdam (암스터-담) in 1928; the 10th in Los Angeles (로스안젤스), USA in 1932; and the 11th was held in Berlin (伯林), Germany.
Mo Yun-suk: From what year did Japan start participating in the Olympic Games?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: It was the 5th Olympic Games held in Stockholm, Sweden in 1912. At that time, athlete Kanaguri Shisō (金栗四三) participated in the marathon. Since then, marathon runners were sent every time, but they couldn't seize the championship. It was only after 24 years, thanks to Son's fierce fight, that the long-cherished world supremacy was finally realized. I cannot help but think this was entirely due to the support of our fathers, brothers, mothers, and sisters back home, and at the same time, I feel immense gratitude. And we cannot forget our seniors, Kim Ŭn-bae (金恩培) hyŏng, who placed 6th, and Kwŏn T'ae-ha (權泰夏) hyŏng, who placed 9th in the marathon at the 10th Olympic Games held in Los Angeles.
Son Ki-jŏng: That's really true. If it weren't for those two seniors, we wouldn't have received much stimulus to participate in the world games. Consequently, the righteous indignation and courage we gained, wanting to avenge the misfortune of our two seniors who couldn't win the championship—though it's embarrassing to say—we believe it was because of them. Yet, today's Chosŏn society seems to have completely forgotten the existence of those two. Even if they haven't forgotten, the fact that they don't even look after them spiritually is utterly unpleasant. Making a huge fuss as if the heavens would fall if someone gets 1st place, but not supporting those who got 6th and 9th even psychologically—I can't help but think it's ignoring the merit of those two who represented all of us and fought hard for a long time to make 'Sporting Chosŏn' (運動朝鮮) known widely to the world. <52>

Opening Ceremony Scene and Stadium
Kim Tong-hwan: It's said that 51 countries participated in the 11th World Olympic Games held in Berlin in Shōwa 11 (1936). Approximately how many athletes were there from each country?
Son Ki-jŏng: There were over 3,000 athletes gathered from various countries, and the crowd numbered over 100,000.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: How many Japanese athletes attended?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: The total number of Japanese athletes who went to the Berlin Games was 176 across all events, but if you include coaches, trainers, and other staff, nearly 300 people attended.
Mo Yun-suk: The games started on August 1st. When did you leave Kyŏngsŏng?
Son Ki-jŏng: We left on June 4th of that year. So, it seems we departed nearly two months before the games began.
Kim Tong-hwan: Did you spend the entire two months there just practicing hard?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: Yes, even after arriving, we couldn't sightsee the much-talked-about facilities of European civilization; we spent all our time practicing for the competition.
Pak Kye-ju: Please tell us first about the scene at the opening ceremony back then.
Son Ki-jŏng: As you saw in the film "Festival of Peoples," German President Hitler entered, guided by the International Olympic Committee President Count Baillet-Latour (랏-루男爵) and the German Olympic Committee President Lewald (레-왈드氏). Next, the athletes from each country entered, following a person carrying a placard with their country's name and a person carrying the national flag. Greece (希臘), the birthplace of the Olympics, led the procession with Louis (루이스), the winner of the 1st Olympic marathon (1896), followed by other countries in alphabetical order (ABC順), with the host nation Germany (獨逸) entering last. When the entrance was complete, President Hitler stood up and declared the opening of the 11th Olympic Games. Congratulatory cannons roared, and dozens of doves flew into the sky above the stadium. At that moment, the resounding chorus of the Olympic Hymn composed by Richard Strauss (리히알트·스트라우스) echoed. Then, the final torchbearer, Fritz Schilgen (푸릿쯔·실겐) (a former holder of the 5,000m and 10,000m records), arrived at the stadium carrying the torch from the Alpheios valley in Greece and lit the flame atop the Marathon Tower. This Olympic flame continued to burn until the closing ceremony on August 16th. To briefly explain the origin of the sacred flame here, it seems to be a kind of myth where, in ancient times, the sacred flame was lit by the sun's rays by the hands of Greek virgins. As you also saw in the movie, the film screen shows the Greek athlete Konstantinos Kondylis (콘스탄스·고이리스君) <53> leading a relay-style procession, passing through the venues of the seven previous host countries, finally entering the Berlin stadium, 3,075 kilometers away. Thus, once the sacred flame was lit on the tower, the athlete representative Rudolf Ismayr (루돌푸·이스마엘選手) (he was the previous weightlifting champion and world record holder) followed and took the oath, which goes like this: "We swear that we will take part in the Olympic Games in loyal competition, respecting the regulations which govern them and desirous of participating in them in the true spirit of sportsmanship, for the honor of our country and for the glory of sport."
Pak Kye-ju: By the way, watching the opening ceremony scene through the film "Festival of Peoples," all 51 participating countries' athletes entered majestically as one team, but it seemed some countries from the East (東洋) were not visible entering. That can't be right, can it?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: Yes, among the participating countries, it seems some might not have been shown in the film.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: How big is the Berlin stadium this time? How many times larger is it than the Tokyo (東京) Jingu Stadium?
Son Ki-jŏng: It's not necessarily many times larger, but it was bigger than the Tokyo Jingu Stadium. Perhaps if you put the Tokyo Jingu Stadium inside the Berlin stadium, it would fit right in? (Turns head towards Mr. Nam and asks)
Nam Sŭng-nyong: That's right, it's about that size.
Mo Yun-suk: Then it's not extraordinarily large.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: Still, the stadium is much higher (놉아서) than Tokyo's, so it looks immense. From the very top, you probably couldn't see the events well; you'd need binoculars to watch.

Olympic Village and Cheering Squads

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: Did you come out to the stadium every day to watch?
Son Ki-jŏng: We couldn't. On the first day, during the opening ceremony, and the day after, Mr. Nam and I attended together to watch the events. After that, Mr. Nam and I spent all our time just practicing for the marathon.
Kim Tong-hwan: Did you always practice at the Olympic Village?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: No. We always ran on the actual marathon course, the 'Avus' (아브스) road. The marathon race started on August 9th, the final day of the Olympic track and field events, at 3 PM, with 52 athletes from various countries participating. After circling the stadium track once, we exited through the main gate, passed through the Grunewald (클비왈드) forest, ran back and forth on the 'Avus' road, and then returned to the stadium. But there were hills along the way, making it a truly difficult course (難코-스). <54>
Pak Kye-ju: Did athletes from other countries always practice on the 'Avus' road too?
Son Ki-jŏng: We didn't really see many athletes practicing there. It always felt like just the two of us, like crazy men, running up and down those hills. Most athletes from other countries practiced in the Olympic Village, but they didn't run every day like that. We only found out about the British athlete Harper (하-바君), who took 2nd place in this marathon, after the race; we didn't know him before.
Mo Yun-suk: Please tell us stories about socializing with athletes from other countries in the Olympic Village.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: The Olympic Village is the dormitory where athletes from all countries stay. It was built with the plan to use it as barracks for aviation soldiers after the Olympics ended. We didn't really socialize much with foreign athletes in the Olympic Village. Firstly, it's probably because we were busy practicing the marathon, running around on the 'Avus' road. But actually, perhaps the British athletes weren't particularly keen on socializing, but we couldn't figure out where they were hiding at all. Other foreign athletes also seemed mostly focused on their own tasks and didn't particularly mingle much.
Kim Tong-hwan: Did you not get to socialize at all with the Black American athlete, Mr. Owens?
Son Ki-jŏng: We didn't really socialize with Mr. Owens either. He set a record of 10.2 seconds in the 100-meter dash and won 1st place in the long jump with 8.06 meters, an Olympic record. That guy runs well, but he jumps well too. (All laugh)
Kim Tong-hwan: In the film, Owens' sharp gaze, looking ahead with wide eyes, panting with tension and excitement before the start, truly expressed his raw humanity; it was quite a sight.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: Indeed, look at President Hitler watching in the stadium. When his country's athlete fails to get 1st place and is competing for 2nd or 3rd, he unconsciously scratches his knees in frustration, lifts his bottom up and down, fidgeting restlessly. Seeing that spectacle, even the hero of the age seems like a child, doesn't he?
Mo Yun-suk: What can you do, he's human too. He'd grimace intensely out of sheer frustration, and if his country's athlete lost, he'd plop down in his seat, smacking his lips just like a lion. And when his country's athlete won, the sight of him jumping up and clapping was truly full of human flavor.
Pak Kye-ju: When did you meet President Hitler?
Son Ki-jŏng: It was after the marathon race ended. Before that, I had no face (面目) to meet him, but after the marathon ended, quite confidently <55> (Laughs), along with Team Leader Shibuya (澁谷團長), I visited President Hitler who was sitting in the stadium. President Hitler stood up to greet us and offered a handshake, so I shook his hand. Before shaking hands, I first saluted Hitler in the German style, shouting "Heil Hitler!" (하이라·히틀러-) while extending my arm straight out. Then Hitler patted my back a few times, praising me. The way he patted my back felt exactly like he was treating me as a child, which felt a bit awkward. (All laugh) And, that fellow's eyes and mustache are truly fearsome and strangely shaped marvels. (All laugh)
Nam Sŭng-nyong: And the expressions of the people from each nation cheering in the stadium, filled with tension, excitement, and fervent enthusiasm—as you all saw in the film—both the competing athletes and the cheering sections truly form a grand symphony of each nation's struggle.

Competition Records and Marathon Conquest Story
Kim Tong-hwan: It seems there weren't many events in which Chinese athletes participated. In the film "Festival of Peoples," besides seeing an athlete named Wang (王) in the High Jump who didn't place, there was nothing else. Was that actually the case in the competition?
Son Ki-jŏng: In fact, they didn't participate much in significant events, and there were no winners at all. It was truly pitiful (뽕rn라들이드군) that a country with the world's largest population of 400 million (4億萬) couldn't produce a single noteworthy athlete from among those 400 million people. (All laugh) 49 Chinese athletes did participate, though.
Pak Kye-ju: Please tell us just the first-place records for the track and field events this time.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: In the Discus Throw (圓盤投), American athlete Carpenter (카-펜타-君) won first place with 50.48m (Olympic record). In the Women's Discus Throw, German athlete Mauermayer (마우엘마이엘) won first with 47.63m (Olympic record). In the Women's Javelin Throw (女子槍投), German Fleischer (푸라이쉘) won first with 45.18m. In the Women's 80m Hurdles (女子80米障碍), Italian Valla (바-라) won first with 11.7 seconds. In the Hammer Throw (鐵槌投), German Hein (하인君) won first with 56.49m. In the 100m Race (100米競走), American Owens (오웬스君) took 1st with 10.3 seconds. In the Women's High Jump (女子走高跳), Hungarian Csák (쟉크) won first with 1.60m. In the Shot Put (砲丸投), German Woellke (웰케君) won first with 16.20m. In the 800m Final (800米競走決勝), American Woodruff (웃드라푸君) took 1st with 1:52.9. In the Triple Jump (三段跳), Japan's Tajima Naoto (田島直人) won first with 16m (World record). In the Long Jump (走巾跳), American Owens won first with 8.06m. In the 5,000m Race (5,000米競走), New Zealander Lovelock (라부록크君) took 1st with 3:47.8 (World record). <56> In the High Jump (走高跳), American Johnson (죤손군) won first with 2.03m. In the 110m High Hurdles (110米高障碍), American Towns (타운스君) won first with 14.2 seconds. In the Javelin Throw (槍投), German Stöck (스텍크君) won first with 71.84m. In the 10,000m Final (10,000米決勝), Finn Salminen (살미-넨君) took 1st with 30:15. In the Pole Vault (棒高跳), American Meadows (메도우스君) won first with 4.35m. In the Women's 400m Relay Final (女子400米繼走決勝), the American team took 1st with 46.9 seconds. In the 400m Relay (400米繼走), the American team took 1st with 39.8 seconds (World record). In the 1,600m Relay (1,600米繼走), the British team took 1st with 3:09.0. In the Marathon (마라손), our Son Ki-jŏng (孫基禎君) took 1st with 2 hours 29 minutes 19.2 seconds (Olympic record).
The records mentioned above are only for the track and field portion shown in "Festival of Peoples." Swimming, skating, skiing, etc., appear in the second part of the Olympia film, "Festival of Beauty" (美の祭典), which we will omit here.
Kim Tong-hwan: Next, please tell us about the Marathon Conquest.
Son Ki-jŏng: That day was August 9th, the final day of the Olympic track and field events, and the marathon was the final event. When I stepped onto the field, my heart was pounding, and my anxiety was beyond description. Since it was a struggle of nations (民族의 투쟁), a crushing defeat in this festival felt like not just my personal defeat, but the defeat of my entire nation. Carrying this heavy burden, no matter how much I held the world record or how much I had practiced, standing on the final field of battle, my heart couldn't help but pound.
In the Olympic Games, the marathon is the representative premier event, and its distance is 42.195 kilometers (42粁195 - 4,275米 likely typo for 42,195m, 26 miles 1/4), which translates to 107 ri (里) in Chosŏn measure. To run these 107 ri in two and a half hours, you have to run about as fast as a bicycle. This event was added to the Olympics based on an ancient Greek legend (古代希臘의 故事), making it the most significant event throughout the Olympic Games. Not only does winning first place bestow the glorious title of world hero, but only the first-place marathon winner has a bronze statue erected in the host country.
Japan (日本) had apparently used team tactics in the marathon whenever participating in the Olympics, which led to failures until now. Because of that, during the Berlin Games, team tactics were abandoned, and Mr. Nam and I used individual tactics. Also, although four marathon runners were selected from Japan, due to the rule allowing only three participants per country in the marathon, myself, Mr. Nam, and Mr. Shiaku (鹽飽君) participated.
As I mentioned earlier, at 3 PM that day, 52 athletes from various countries started, circled the stadium track (츄럭) once, went outside the stadium, passed through the Grunewald (클비왈드) forest, and ran back and forth on the 'Avus' road. <57> Right after the start, the previous Olympic marathon winner, the Argentinian Zabala (자바라君), sped up considerably, running at the front and showing good form. Until about 8 kilometers after the start, I was running side-by-side with the British athlete Harper (하-바-君) in second place. Even then, I didn't know Harper was British. Even when turning back at the halfway point (回歸點), Zabala was still in the lead, and Harper and I were still following side-by-side. However, at about the 30-kilometer mark, I realized Harper was British. This was because a British athlete running with him had fallen behind and, on the way back, was eagerly cheering for Harper, which is how I knew he was British.
However, both Mr. Nam and I feared the three Finnish athletes coached by the Finn named Nurmi (누루미) more than the previous winner Zabala running at the front or the British athlete Harper. They were our strongest opponents. Still, since I didn't know Harper's capabilities, I couldn't rest easy, but noticing his breathing showed fatigue, I pulled ahead and ran. After that, Harper couldn't keep up with me and kept falling behind. Zabala, who had been running at the front, had run too fast from the start and, upon reaching the hill at the 32-kilometer mark, was completely exhausted (氣盡脈盡). He was assisted by medical staff and withdrew, so from that point on, I was running in the very front.
By the way, the day before the race, the Japanese cheering squad had decided to cheer at the 6-mile mark, but when I reached the 6-mile point, there was no one cheering at all. It was truly a deserted spot (꿩 구어먹은 자리), which was quite discouraging. Thus, I ran through the crowds lining both sides of the road without a single person cheering for me, feeling lonely, but I couldn't look back. Therefore, I didn't know how far behind the trailing runners were, which made me very anxious. Then, after passing the hill, I met Coach Sasō (佐勝코-취) and finally guessed that the finish line ('꼴') was not far off. When I re-entered the stadium and ran one lap, I still didn't see anyone, so I thought, "Now, first place is definitely mine," and ran into the finish line ('꼴') with good rhythm. If the race had continued then, I felt confident I could have run another 40 ri (里). On this point, Mr. Nam also said that if the marathon race had continued longer, he too felt confident he could run another 40 or 50 ri. Anyway, I later heard from Mr. Kwŏn T'ae-ha that when I entered the stadium entrance and ran from the track (츄럭) to the finish line ('꼴'), it took 13 seconds. That timing was measured by an American coach <58>. And so, with a time of 2 hours 29 minutes 19.2 seconds, I obtained the glorious laurel wreath (月桂冠). Thinking about it now, I feel deeply moved, believing it was all thanks to the sincere support and encouragement of all my compatriots (全同胞).
Pak Kye-ju: What was your record back in Chosŏn?
Son Ki-jŏng: It was 2 hours 26 minutes 42 seconds. That was the world record.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: For me, when climbing the final hill on the return course (回路), this was the most difficult and grueling part of the course. Ahead of me were Zabala in the very lead, then Son and Harper. Right behind them, the three Finnish athletes had been running together side-by-side from the beginning. I, too, feared these three athletes coached by Nurmi more than anyone else. I needed to break away from these three, but they were running right in front of me, always together, not giving me an opportunity. So, I was partly worried. I thought I might lose to these three athletes and was just looking for a way to break through them, following right behind. Then, while climbing past the midpoint of the hill, a car passed by the side of the three athletes. At that moment, I thought, "Alright, this is it!" Using the car as cover, I subtly slipped out so they couldn't see me and ran out in front of them. When the car passed, they were startled to see me unexpectedly appear in front of them. From then on, they abandoned their side-by-side running and competed fiercely with each other, trying to shake me off. I also ran with all my might, determined not to fall behind them. I ran gritting my teeth (악을 악을 쓰며), but they couldn't shake me off in the end and finished 4th, 5th, and 7th. And I finished 3rd with a time of 2 hours 31 minutes 43 seconds. The British athlete Harper, who finished 2nd, had a time of 2 hours 31 minutes 23.2 seconds.

Travel Stories from Various Countries Around the World
Kim Tong-hwan: Please share some travel stories from your trips to various countries.
Son Ki-jŏng: I wrote in detail about my world travels—leaving Kyŏngsŏng, going through Siberia, visiting Paris, London, Rome, the Mediterranean, India, and returning—in the June issue of "Samch'ŏlli" (三千里), so I'll skip the travelogue here and just talk about India (印度).
We landed in Bombay (孟買), India, intending to stay for a day, but unexpectedly, the ship had mechanical trouble, so we ended up staying for a week. <59> Firstly, the impression that India is a very hot place still remains vivid. Indeed, when passing through the Suez Canal (스에스運河) and entering the Red Sea (紅海), it was so hot that even sitting naked on the deck at night, sweat poured down (좌악좍 솓아지는게), making sleep impossible. I had never experienced such heat anywhere in the world. And on the streets of India at night, people come out onto the sidewalks (左右人道) and sleep all stretched out. That was quite a sight. They don't cover themselves with blankets, just lie spread out sleeping on both sides of the street; it really felt strange. Also, during the day, everyone carries an umbrella (雨傘). Even the traffic police (交通巡査) carry umbrellas tucked at their sides; that too was quite a sight. I couldn't tell if they carried umbrellas because it was hot or because they didn't know when it might rain, but seeing everyone on the streets carrying umbrellas was a phenomenon I hadn't seen elsewhere. Even detectives (刑事) carried umbrellas tucked at their sides! (All laugh) And because of the heat, Indian people walk around the streets wearing only loincloths (훈도시). Movies also started late, around 10:30 PM, because of the heat.
Nam Sŭng-nyong: Also, Indian people, while walking on the street, buy red tree bark sold by the roadside for one sen (一錢), chew it, and then spit it out (배앝아버리는데), just like chewing and spitting out gum.
Son Ki-jŏng: When I first saw that, I thought they were coughing up blood (喀血) and was startled, thinking, "Wow, there must be a tremendous number of tuberculosis patients (肺病者) in India!" But later I found out it was just red saliva (唾液). (Laughs) Also, the resident Japanese people (在留內地人) living in India told us that religious conflicts (宗敎싸움) are very severe there. Disputes between different sects (宗派) frequently lead to major brawls (一大 亂鬪), resulting in a huge number of casualties each time. Even so, the British authorities (英國官憲) supposedly just surround the area and watch, neither stopping nor interfering, apparently waiting for many casualties. They said this is the notorious cunning British policy (老繪한 英國의 惡政策) hoping for the Indian nation to perish and its population to decrease. All the Japanese who witnessed this condemned it. Also, they don't sell medicine (藥). I needed medicine once and couldn't buy it after searching all day. It seems this might also be a policy to let Indian people just die if they get sick.
Pak Kye-ju: What about their level of culture (文化)? Compared to us Chosŏn people (朝鮮人)?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: It's difficult to assert definitively after just a brief observation, but they seemed much further behind us. Their attire (채림채림), housing system (家屋制度), customs (風俗), culture, etc., all appeared inferior. It felt like how we might view undeveloped Manchurian people (滿洲人).
Kim Tong-hwan: After the Berlin Games ended, where <60> did you receive the warmest welcome?
Son Ki-jŏng: It was from the resident Japanese civilians (內地人民留民) in India. In Paris, there was a banquet at the Japanese embassy, and similarly in London and Rome, but only in India did they welcome us so sincerely.
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi: What sport are Indian athletes best at?
Son Ki-jŏng: It's a sport called 'hockey' (혹케-). Indian athletes are the best at it in the Olympics, and they hold the championship.
Mo Yun-suk: What are the temperaments (기질) of athletes from different countries like?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: German youth are neat, valiant, and British youth seem completely uninterested in anything that doesn't concern them; they truly are gentlemen (젠틀맨). And the ones who are loudest, try to start arguments during competitions, and act like rascals (얄개질) even in movie theaters are the Italian youth. Every night in the Olympic Village, they showed films of that day's events, and only the Italian athletes were noisy and mischievous.
Son Ki-jŏng: British people are truly faithful to their duties. After the marathon ended, I thought the British athlete Harper should come find me first to offer congratulations, since I was the 1st place winner, I shouldn't need to go find him first. So I puffed out my chest (뱃장을 내여밀고) and waited, but he never came. (All laugh) So eventually, I decided to yield and went to find him first, but guess what? This fellow had already returned to Britain after the competition ended. He's a factory worker (工塲의 一職工), and he supposedly rushed back to his home country by plane to get back to his factory job. Truly loyal to his duties.

Aiming for the World Games
Kim Tong-hwan: When did you start practicing the marathon with the goal of the World Olympic Games?
Nam Sŭng-nyong: I spent exactly 11 long years practicing until I competed in the Games. To succeed in anything, it doesn't happen overnight; long effort and training are necessary. People might think lightly of the marathon, assuming you just need to run well, but it's absolutely not like that.
In my hometown, Sunch'ŏn (順天), South Chŏlla Province (全南), when I was in elementary school, my older cousin (從兄) always won first place in marathons hosted by the military, and I was very envious, so I started practicing running then. Then, at age 16, the South Chŏlla Athletics Association (全南體育協會) sent me to the Chosŏn Shrine Games (朝鮮神宮競技) held in Kyŏngsŏng because I had won first place in Sunch'ŏn. I suffered a crushing defeat in the 5,000 meters, but I wasn't discouraged at all. I went back to my hometown, practiced again, <61> and subsequently came to Kyŏngsŏng every year, winning first and second place in the 10,000 meters. Later, while attending university in Tokyo, I felt frustrated that seniors Kwŏn T'ae-ha and Kim Ŭn-bae couldn't win at the Los Angeles Games. So, I couldn't even go to the tea rooms (茶房) I wanted to visit, abstained from alcohol and tobacco, and even forbade myself from pursuing romance (연애) which I wanted to do (All laugh), living a life of abstinence like a monk (修道士). This isn't because marathon runners absolutely cannot smoke or drink, but the marathon race requires running 107 Chosŏn ri in 2 hours and 30 minutes. Most runners give up around 70 ri. If you can endure the indescribable pain at the 70-ri mark, you can succeed. To practice this endurance, that is, because mental training for perseverance is needed more than anything, one lives a life of complete abstinence like a monk. Mr. Son, just like me, doesn't drink or smoke, not because he doesn't know how, but for the sake of mental and physical training to endure and overcome anything. When I was attending university in Tokyo, I practiced the marathon every morning and evening without fail. After practice, I didn't even know about the famous tea room called "Uronja" (우론쟈) next to the public bathhouse I frequented daily, despite passing by it for 6 years. One day, students were talking about "Uronja," "Uronja," so when I was about to graduate, I asked about it. They teased me, saying, "Ah, you idiot, you don't even know that famous tea room near your house?" To succeed in anything, I believe one must focus solely on that goal and remain indifferent to other things.
Son Ki-jŏng: I practiced the marathon for 7 years before competing in the Games. In my hometown, Sinŭiju (新義州), I won first place in the 5,000 meters in elementary school (普通學校). Thanks to that, I came to the Kyŏngsŏng games, but in the first year, I only managed 2nd place. The next time, the 2nd time, I won 1st place, but even then, I was truly a country bumpkin (촌띠기) who didn't even know the word 'marathon'. (All laugh) Later, I tried what I thought was my first marathon in the Kyŏngsŏng-Yŏngdŭngp'o (京城 永登浦間) marathon race hosted by the Tonga Ilbosa (東亞日報社), but I didn't get first place. (All laugh) After that, I also competed in the Kyŏngsŏng-Inch'ŏn (京城 仁川間) marathon. Thanks to being good at the marathon, though I was poor (貧寒), I was able to enter Yangjŏng Middle School (養正中學). After that, I practiced consistently for 5 years without missing a single day, every morning and evening, regardless of whether it rained, the wind blew, the day was cloudy, or it snowed. Usually, the course started from Yangjŏng School, went around Hwan'gŭmjŏng (黃金町), Tongdaemun (東大門), Ch'angdŏkkung (昌德宮), past the Government-General Building (總督府 앞), and back to school. Sometimes, I would go past Yongsan (龍山), cross the Han River (漢江), and go as far as Noryangjin (鷺梁津) and back. Competitions can easily happen on rainy days, cloudy days, or even very hot days, <62> so you always need to practice regularly in any weather condition; thus, I ran and ran every day. As Mr. Nam said, success in anything requires "Effort First" (努力第一), "Patience First" (忍耐第一), and "Training First" (訓練第一).

Nurturing Successors and Stadium Construction
Kim Tong-hwan: Do you both intend to focus more on the marathon in the future? And do you have any intention of raising successors?
Son Ki-jŏng: Why wouldn't we? I briefly mentioned this in the June issue of "Samch'ŏlli" as well. If the 12th Olympic Games were to be held, I had dreamt of not getting married, not taking a job, and definitely aiming for consecutive victories. However, predicting that the 12th Games would be canceled due to the war in the East and West, I ended up getting married and taking a job. And once employed, forget practice, I can't even get to the Kyŏngsŏng stadium once a month even just to watch; it's truly pathetic (寒心). Still, when I returned victorious from Berlin, I never dreamt things would turn out like this. That's not to say I expected a high position or to live without working, but I thought society would give me the leeway to continue practicing the sport I enjoy and be active in that field. Far from society giving leeway, even figures active in the sports field pretend not to know us, which is astounding (氣가 막혔습니다). To give a common example, Yangjŏng Middle School is my alma mater and a school that puts the most effort into track and field, yet they never once try to contact us, nor do they call us to share our experiences with the juniors or even let us coach them. Of course, theoretically, the physical education teacher might be better than us, but in terms of experience, though it sounds arrogant, aren't we, who trained for nearly 10 years, likely better? (Laughs) It's not just my alma mater; any athletic association or sports organization also takes this attitude. Is it an attitude of, "You guys just ran well, what could you possibly know?" or is it an attitude of, "You're world champions, how could we dare contact you?" (All laugh) Mr. Nam's hometown, Sunch'ŏn, did invite us once, and Mr. Nam and I went together to coach, but after that, contact with the sports world has been completely cut off (連絡杜絶).
Nam Sŭng-nyong: It's really true. It's something Mr. Son and I always talk about when we meet: we want to raise successors so that the marathon title isn't lost to other countries, and we want our Japan (우리 日本) to win every time. So, Mr. Son always says, Kwŏn T'ae-ha, Kim Ŭn-bae, the four of us should do business <63> together, establish something like a Marathon Association or Athletics Association, make a strong showing in the world as 'Marathon Japan' (마라손日本), nurture successors to keep world supremacy in our hands, and continue it forever. But society doesn't support us, so what can we do? It's hard enough just to make ends meet (입에 풀칠하기), so what more is there to say?
Pak Kye-ju: Does that mean you are giving up on the project for successors?
Son Ki-jŏng: No. We want to realize it anytime if there are good supporters (應援者). And if middle schools or professional schools contact us, we will do our best from the most humble position, using our experience to strive for nurturing successors.
And one thing I hope for from society is, when I went to the West last time, cities like Berlin, Paris, and Rome had one sports ground per district (町), paying considerable attention to and encouraging health and physical education. Even in a place like our Kyŏngsŏng, while we might not be able to place one in every district, I hope they could install at least 3 or 4 more. This is a most beneficial project for public health (保健). When I went to Rome, there was the 'Mussolini Stadium' (뭇소리니-스타지암). In the same way, in Chosŏn, instead of focusing only on establishing schools or other social projects, I wish there were stadiums invested in by individuals in each city. For example, Kyŏngsŏng Sports Ground alone is insufficient, so wealthy gentlemen (富豪諸氏) like Mr. Ch'oe Ch'ang-hak (崔昌學氏), Mr. Yi Chong-man (李鍾萬氏), Mr. Yun Ch'i-ho (尹致昊氏), and also Mr. Pak Hŭng-sik (朴興植씨), Mr. Min Kyu-sik (閔奎植) could make donations, just like the 'Mussolini Stadium,' creating 'Ch'oe Ch'ang-hak Stadium,' 'Yun Ch'i-ho Stadium'—constructing many such sports grounds would be good for health and sports development, and that social project would be very good in every way. I earnestly hope for this to happen.
And looking ahead, our shining athletes who will make a great showing as 'Sporting Chosŏn' (運動朝鮮) include: in weightlifting (力技), Mr. Nam Su-il (南壽逸氏); in the triple jump, Kim Wŏn-kwŏn (金源權), who even competed in Helsinki (헬싱키市), Finland; in horse racing (競馬), Mr. Min Pyŏng-sŏn (閔병선君), son of Mr. Min Tae-sik (閔大植氏); in wrestling (레스링), Kim Myŏng-ha (金明夏君) and Kwak Chŏng-un (郭정운君) from Meiji University (明治大學); and there are quite promising athletes in skiing, swimming, boxing, etc. In soccer, there's the son of Pastor Kim Ik-tu (金益斗목사), who has deep theoretical and scientific research—they are all the pride of Sporting Chosŏn. Isn't there a duty and responsibility in our society to further encourage and promote them to become world champions? <64>

**Summary**

This 1940 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine features an interview with Son Ki-jŏng and Nam Sŭng-nyong, the Korean gold and bronze medalists in the 1936 Berlin Olympics marathon (competing for Japan). They reflect on their Olympic experience four years later, discussing Leni Riefenstahl's film "Olympia" ("Festival of Peoples"), the history of the Olympics, their rigorous training, the race itself, observations from traveling abroad (particularly India), and their interactions with figures like Hitler and Jesse Owens. They express profound gratitude for the support from Koreans back home but also lament the lack of continued support and recognition within Korean society and sports institutions after their return, hindering their ability to train successors or remain active in athletics. They conclude by appealing for better sports infrastructure in Korea, suggesting wealthy individuals fund stadiums, and highlighting promising younger Korean athletes in various disciplines. From a historical perspective, the article is revealing for its candid portrayal of the athletes' experiences under Japanese colonial rule: competing under the Japanese flag, the subtle expression of national pride ("our Chosŏn," "compatriots"), the use of colonial terminology ("Naichi-jin" for Japanese), and their critique of societal neglect which implicitly points to the underdeveloped state of Korean sports infrastructure and support systems during that era. Their desire to nurture "Sporting Chosŏn" and maintain marathon supremacy underscores a nascent national consciousness expressed through athletics.

잡지명	삼천리 제12권 제8호

기사제목	民族의 祭典
기사형태	회고·수기

民族의 祭典
世界的 覇者「孫基禎, 南昇龍」氏로부터 4年前 伯林大會의 올림피아映畵「民族의 祭典」에 대하여 回想을 듣다.

映畵『民族의 祭典』評
金東煥-지난 7월2일과 3일 양일간 京日文化映畵劇場에서 試寫會가 있은 영화『民族의 祭典』에 대해서 그 보신 감상을 말씀해주십시오.
孫基禎-4년전의 일을, 더욱이 내가 달른 장면을 필림을 통해서＜48＞ 이제 다시 보게 된다는 것은 실로 感慨無量하기 짝이 없으며 당시 伯林에서 대회광경을 직접 보던 때와는 또 다른 감격을 이 영화를 통해서 보았습니다.『民族의 祭典』은 스포-쯔영화요 따라서 한 개의 기록영화지만 레-니·리-펜슈타-ㄹ여사의 예술적 天分에 의해서 새로운 영역을 개척해준 획기적인 構圖에 경의를 표했습니다. 여러분도 시사회에서 보셨으니 구태여 여러 말을 피합니다마는, 이 영화에서 五大陸 전세계의 끓고 뛰는 젊은이의 피와 肉이 조국의 명예를 위해서, 긴장과 흥분 속에서 가장 眞摯하고 순수한 민족투쟁을 계속하는 그『肉體美의 跳躍』에 보는 이로 하여금 숭엄한 인간미와 장렬한 육체미에 경기자와 같은 긴장과 흥분 속에서「人間讚美」를 갖게 되리라고 생각합니다.
南昇龍-여하간, 올림픽대회는 五大陸의 각 민족이「平和의 交歡」속에서 투쟁하는 一大民族의 제전인데, 영화『民族의 祭典』을 통해서 각 경기의 각 선수의 一擧手一投足에 그 절실하고 순수한 감정이 끓어오르며, 그러면서도 그것이 기록영화에만 끄치지 않고 성격이 잘 묘사된 것과 예술적 芳香을 잘 풍겨주는 것에는 저 역시 리-팬슈타-ㄹ여사에게 사의를 표하기를 주저치 않는 바입니다.
毛允淑-전『民族의 祭典』의 감독 레리·리-펜슈타-ㄹ여사가＜49＞ 1個의 영화감독만이 아니라 훌륭한 시인인 것만 같이 생각돼요. 그 오림피아 유적인 古代希臘건물조각과, 남녀 裸像조각의 端正한 형식미의 묘사에 감격했어요.
崔貞熙-내 생각엔 남자감독이라면 기록영화로 하여금 그렇게까지 치밀하게 그처럼 또렷하도록, 오림픽 光景을 눈으로 본 이상으로 나타내지 못했으리라고 생각해요. 다시 말하면 여자감독이기 때문에 그렇게 훌륭한 영화를 맨들었다는 거얘요.
孫基禎-허어! 이거, 여자자랑을 너무 하십니다 그려.(一同笑)
朴啓周-米國선수로 나오는 흑인 오웬스를 一個의 人間除外의 구로테스크한 괴물로 보겠지만 영화『民族의 祭典』에서는 어떻게도 인간적인지요! 스타-트線上의 그의 날카로운 응시와, 격한 호흡과, 입살이 말라서 혀끝으로 적시는 그 긴장의 극치에 달한 자태는 실로 인간적이오, 인간미를 담뿍 품은 장엄한 자태드군요.
崔貞熙-전 오웬스가 그 초조와 긴장에 쌓여서 허덕이는 진지한 태도와, 그러다가도 그 친구가 씩 웃는데서 어떤 매력을 느꼈어요. 하긴 히틀러總統도 이때까지는 그저 木石과 같은 인간으로 알아왔지만, 자기나라 선수가 出場하면, 특히 여자선수일땐 더욱 남자선수가 나왔을 때보다, 또다른 한가지 표정을 하는데「그도 亦是 피가 흐르는 인간이로구나」하고 一種의 흥미를 갖게 됐어요.
孫基禎-어쨋던 영화를 통해서 영화가 가진 기능의 最高度를 발휘한 것으로 생각합니다.「三千里」6월호에도 제가『民族의 祭典』의 鑑賞記를 썼지만, 제11회 올림피아영화는 제1부 제2부로, 노니여 있는데 제1부는『民族의 祭典』이고, 제2부는『美의 祭典』입니다.

『民族의 祭典』(Fest der Volker)은 전12권으로 올림피아·쿠-로스丘의 聖火로부터 시작하여 육상경기 전부와 마라손의 決勝으로 막을 닫게 되는데, 그 촬영중에는 氣球에 依하여 공중촬영, 즉 俯瞰촬영도 있습니다.＜50＞

올림픽競技의 由來
金東煥-올림픽경기의 유래를 좀 이야기해 주십시요.
孫基禎-올림픽경기는 지금으로부터 2천7백년전인 西紀前776년에 希臘나라 올림피아에서 처음 시작되었었는데, 당시「제우스」神前에 올리는 祭事로서 올림아피경기가 개최되었던 것입니다. 그 뒤로도 4년마다 한번씩「제우스」神前에 올리는 祭事와 함께 경기도 계속 거행되었으며, 이 祭事를 중심하여 古代希臘의 그 絢爛한 문화가 생성하였다고 합니다. 그러나 로마(羅馬)시대에 들어서면서부터 정신생활을 육체생활보다 過重視하는 기독교의 영향으로 말미아마 올림피아경기는 점차 쇠퇴하게 되었다고 합니다. 그런데다가 서기396년, 즉 제293회의 올림피아祭를 맞이하여서부터 2년뒤인 서기394년에 데오드시아스大帝가 올림피아祭의 금지령을 선포하였기 때문에 1천1백68년간이나 계속해 오던 올림피아祭의 역사적 경기는 그만 폐막의 비운에 봉착되지 않을 수 없게 되었다 합니다. 그런데다가 서기476년에 제우스神殿이 콘스탄티노불에 옮기게 되자 불타버렸고, 올림피아聖地에는 코-트族이 濫入하고, 그후 지진까지 있게되어 豪華를 자랑하던 올림피아는 지하20尺 밑에 매장되고 말았습니다.
이리하여 永滅狀態에 含하였던 올림피아경기는, 文藝復興期이후, 歐羅巴에 있어서 한창 전성시대의 觀을 呈하고 있던 進化論사상과 團體的사상이 올림피아 부활운동의 문화사적 母胎가 되었었으며, 19세기에 들어서서는 歐羅巴에 배출된 많은 考古學 학자들은 古代希臘의 연구에 흥미를 가지고 遺蹟발굴사업이 성행되었습니다. 그 중에서 결정적 성공을 보게된 것은 獨逸학자 에룬스트·클티우스氏의 발굴사업에 의하여 1874년부터 1881년까지의 만7년간에 올림피아유적은 거의 완전하게 그 姿態를 地上에 再現케 되었지요.
이 올림피아유적의 발굴로 인하여 希臘的정신의 부활을 보게 되었으며, 이에 자극된 올림피아＜51＞경기의 近代的 부흥을 企圖한 것은 서기1896년 佛蘭西의 교육자 삐-엘·드·크멜단男爵의 헌신적 노력에 의해서 제1회 대회가 由緖 깊은 希臘나라 아데네(雅典)에서 개막되었다 합니다.
朴啓周-그 뒤로 제11회 대회까지의 대회장소 순번은 어디어디었습니까.

毛允淑-日本에서 올림픽대회에 참가하기는 어느해부텁니까.

孫基禎-사실 그래요. 만일 그 두분 형이 아니었드면 우리들은 세계대회에 출정할 자극도 그리 받지 못했을 것이오, 따라서 두분 선배가 불행히 제패못한 그 雪怨을 부끄러운 말씀이나, 우리가 갚아야겠다는 義憤과 용기를 얻은 것도 두분 선배 때문이라고 생각합니다. 그런데 오늘의 朝鮮사회는 그 두분의 존재를 씻은 듯이 잊어버리고, 만일 잊어버리지 않었다고해도 그들을 정신적으로까지 돌보지않는다는 것은 불쾌하기가 짝이 없습니다. 1着을 해야 天地가 문어갈 듯이 떠들고, 6着 9着을 했다고 그들을 心的으로까지 후원 지지하지 않는다는 것은, 그 두분이 우리 전체를 대표해서 『運動朝鮮』을 널리 세계에 알리려고 장구한 시일을 亙하여 奮鬪努力한 그 功을 몰라보는 일이라 생각지 않을수 없습니다.＜52＞

金東煥-昭和11년, 伯林에서 개최되었던 제11회 세계 올림픽 경기대회에 51개국이 참가하였다는데 각국 선수는 약 몇 명가량이었나요.

孫基禎-영화『民族의 祭典』에서 보시다싶이 獨逸 히틀러總統은 국제올림픽위원장 랏-루男爵과 獨逸올림픽위원장 레-왈드氏에게 인도되어 입장하고, 다음 각국 선수가 각기 자기나라 국명을 쓴 牌木든 사람과 國旗를 든 사람의 뒤를 따라 입장하는데, 올림픽 발상지의 希臘이 제1회(1896년)올림픽·마라손 우승자 루이스를 선두로, 이하 각국은 ABC順에 따라 서고, 주최국 獨逸이 맨 나종에 입장했습니다. 이리하여 입장이 끝나자 뒤를 이어 히틀러總統이 서서 제11회 올림픽대회 개회를 선언하고, 祝砲聲이 요란히 울리며 數十마리의 비들기가 대회장의 창공을 날지요. 그때 리히알트·스트라우스작곡의 올림픽頌歌의 합창이 우렁차게 울리는데 이때 希臘 알페오의 궁전으로부터 炬火를 가저오는 최후의 走者 푸릿쯔·실겐(일찌기 5천米와 1만米 기록 保持者)이 會場에 도착하여 마라손塔上에 점화합니다. 이 오림픽聖火는 8월16일인 폐회식 날까지 계속해서 불붙고 있지요. 여기에서 漸間 그 聖火의 유래를 말씀하면, 一種 神話인 모양인데 옛날 希臘처녀의 손에 의해서 태양열에 聖火가 점화되었다하며, 여러분도 영화에서 보섰겠지만, 영화 화면에는 希臘선수 콘스탄스·고이리스君이＜53＞ 先頭로 리레式으로 이때까지 열린 7개국대회장을 거쳐서 마지막으로 3,075키로를 隔한 伯林경기장에 들어옵니다. 이리하여 聖火가 塔上에 점화되자 뒤를 이어 선수대표로서 루돌푸·이스마엘선수(그는 前回의 重量揚優勝者世界記錄保持者)가 宣誓하는데 그 선서는 이러합니다. 『우리들은 올림피아경기에 있어서 光榮 있는 戰士로서 경기규칙을 遵奉하며 武士道정신에 쫓아 조국의 영예와 스포-쯔의 光榮을 위해서 싸우기를 서약하노라』

孫基禎-못했습니다. 첫날 입장식 때와 그 이튿날 한번 南君과 가치 참석해서 경기구경을 하고는 그 뒤로는 줄곧 南君과 둘이 마라손 연습에만 시간을 보냈어요.

그리고 한가지 사회에 바라는 바는, 전번 西洋에 가보니 伯林이나 巴里나 羅馬에는 한 町에 한 운동장이 있어서 보건과 체육에 여간 留意獎勵하지 않는데, 우리 京城같은 데도 每町에 하나씩 둘 수는 없어도 3, 4箇는 더 설치해주었으면 하고 기대합니다. 이것은 保健을 위해서도 가장 유익한 사업인데, 羅馬에 가보니「뭇소리니-스타지암」이 있어요. 그와 만찬가지로 朝鮮에서도 학교창설이나 그밖에 사회사업에만 유의할 것이 아니라, 각 도시에 각개인 투자의 스타지암이 있었으면 합니다. 예컨대, 京城에도 京城運動場 하나만으로는 부족하니 崔昌學氏나, 李鍾萬氏나, 尹致昊氏나, 또 朴興植씨, 閔奎植같은 富豪諸氏가 義捐하여서「뭇소리니-스타지암」이라 하듯이「崔昌學스타지암」,「尹致昊스타지암」, 이 모양으로 많은 운동장을 건설하는 것이 보건과 체육발전을 위해서도 좋고, 또 그 사회사업이 어느 모으로든지 매우 좋다고 봅니다. 이렇게 해주시기를 간절히 기대합니다.
그리고 앞으로「運動朝鮮」으로 大氣焰을 吐할 우리의 빛나는 선수들로는, 力技에 南壽逸氏, 三段跳에 芬蘭헬싱키市까지 출정나갔던 金源權, 競馬에 閔大植氏의 子弟분인 閔병선君, 레스링에는 明治大學 金明夏君, 郭정운君, 기타 스키, 수영, 권투 등에 상당히 유망한 선수들이 있습니다. 축구에는 이론적, 과학적 연구가 깊은 金益斗목사 아드님되는 분도 다 運動朝鮮의 자랑이오, 이들을 더 激勵助長해서 세계대회의 우승자가 되게해야 할 의무와 책임이 우리사회에 있지않을까요?＜64＞
