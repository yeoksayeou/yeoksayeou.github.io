Artists and Film

The Male Actor I Like
No Ch'ŏnmyŏng

When I see a good movie, the actor whose performance was excellent in that picture, along with impressive scenes, might linger in my head for a few days, but I have a habit of forgetting the actors even before the overall impression received from the entire picture fades.
It would be quite something if I had a male actor I could single out as that good.
Once, I wrote about Robert Taylor for a newspaper's entertainment section as requested, but the exaggerated title attached by the editor made it seem as if I was excessively fond of a Western entertainer. I was quite embarrassed and troubled for several days afterward, and since then, I've come to strongly dislike the male actor named Robert Taylor, and I don't have a male actor I particularly like.<151>

Night Flight and Others
Yi Hyosŏk

The development of film art has recently been astonishing day by day. Of course, film is film, and a novel is a novel, but one also considers how much more economical it is to watch a neatly organized story in just an hour and a half compared to the effort of finishing a novel over a day or two. Film lacks psychological description, but this difficulty seems to be gradually overcome, and I anticipate that techniques even a step ahead of the novel might be invented in the future. The charm conveyed by writing is sufficiently compensated for by film's more precise visual elements and the unique atmosphere it creates.
It feels newly marvelous to be able to appreciate a slice of life so easily just with one's eyes.
Among the films I've seen throughout this year, many remain in my memory. Among those that somewhat embodied modern thought and possessed considerable dramatic or theatrical elements are *The Petrified Forest* (化石의 수풀) and *The Witness* (目擊者). Their shared interest lies in how they clearly exhibit the temperament and characteristics of modern American playwrights and suggest the affinity and interchangeability between film and theater. Such expression of thought is necessary in modern films, and perhaps it's a kind of irony that America, flooded with sequels to shabby musicals, produced such films instead.
Films suitable for setting aside serious contemplation about life's observations or whatnot, sitting like an idiot, and just staring blankly were *Stage Door* (女人 俱樂部), *Lost Horizon* (일허진 地平線), *Les Beaux Jours* (아름다운 친구들), and the like. It is enough to be captivated by the multitude of women's bodies, dream of the nature in the basin called the remote region of Tibet (西藏), and fully savor the scenery of the French (불란서) countryside. Showing similarly beautiful things, yet regrettable to just watch and let pass, were *Maria Chapdelaine* (마리아 삽드레에느), *The Garden of Allah* (아라아의 花園), and *The Trail of the Lonesome Pine* (외로운 소나무 그림자). Over the white virgin land scarce of human shadows flowed an irretrievable sorrow of life; over the colored desert overflowed sultry passion; and in the pine forest, the struggle of life, begun since time immemorial, never ceased. On Silva Sidney's <152> sky-blue skirt, dyed with the color of the heavens, the history of the village's struggles seemed inscribed.

*Pension Mimosas* (펜슌미모사), *Un grand amour de Beethoven* (베토벤의 위대한 사랑), *Anna Karenina* (안나 카레니나), *Night Flight* (南方 飛行) - under which category would it be appropriate to classify this series of films? Generally, there are no works that do not deal with love and desire to some extent, but in this series of works, the very theme is love and desire.
Things as strange and eerie as the desires lingering at Pension Mimosas (미모사 舘) are rare; the adoptive mother's heart-wrenching feelings for her adopted son evoke a pitiful and earnest quality that makes one straighten their collar. Rosay, both physically and emotionally, is perfectly cast as the adoptive mother, and her performance is nearly flawless. Through her tormented physique and puzzling eyes, a gentle, hidden affection comes vividly into view. When thinking again about this type of theme of love and desire and this director's skill, a fantasy arises: couldn't Cocteau's *Les Enfants Terribles* (레 상팡 텔리 부울) be recreated as a film? It might truly be a fantasy, as filming this difficult work of subtle psychological conflict would still be an extremely arduous task (至難의 業) for now. The one person who could undertake this difficult task (至難事) would likely be the director of Pension Mimosas, and if he collaborated with the author Cocteau, one can imagine to what extent it could be carved out and expressed. Of course, this would be in the near future. If we speak of strange themes of love and desire, other suitable materials include, for instance, the ancient *Oedipus Rex* (에디프스 王) or the modern *Desire Under the Elms* (느름나무 아래의 愛慾). Future filmmakers should possess the high artistic discerning eye (炯眼) to notice at least such things.
In *Un grand amour de Beethoven* (베토펜의 偉大한 사랑), one is more overwhelmed by the musical effect than the work's theme, but similar works with clear themes are *Anna Karenina* (안나 카レ니나) and *Night Flight* (南行飛行), and the success of these two works is, of course, entirely thanks to the original works. The strengths of both works are entirely the strengths of the originals, and works where the charm of the original is so clearly revealed are rare. Setting aside the overly famous *Anna Karenina*, the piece *Night Flight* was the biggest recent attraction. Rather than the director's skill, the intention and design (意匠) of the original author, Saint-Exupéry, emerge so clearly that it creates the work's great charm.
The author's intention (意想), of course, lies in expressing the necessity and inevitability of action in human life, but that action is not necessarily manifested only through the dramatic behavior of the protagonist, Jacques Bernis (짝베르니스), piloting an airplane through a dangerous route <153> on a stormy night. Rather, it is more primarily manifested, of course, in the crucial turning point and resolution to finally complete his love with the heroine, Geneviève (쥬느베에브). No matter how loud the roar of the airplane and how numerous the scenes of harsh deserts and battles with soldiers may be, the truly beautiful scene of action is the moment when Bernis and Geneviève finally leave the ambassador's residence and undertake their escape. It is a momentous action of an instant that allows no room for injustice (不義) or immorality (背德). Future? The future is what we create—this is what Bernis said to encourage Geneviève at the moment of departure. What is important in human life is not necessarily the calculation and measurement of happiness; transcending such things, the will to act pushes forward the spine of love like a heavy cart. Success or failure is not the issue; there is only the unbearable execution. Bernis cannot be seen as ultimately successful in love, and he ultimately failed in his flight path. But what are such failures before his passionate will? He acted bravely and departed contentedly. He is certainly one person who lived beautifully.
While this concept of action is beautiful, the author's talent (才華) is shown in choosing the urgent love situation of the two protagonists as its concrete expression. The actions of the two characters are the most beautiful part throughout the entire screen, and for some reason, no amount of praise feels sufficient. The form of love depicted in *Anna Karenina* is similar, but why does this one feel more direct and impressive? The performances of Clark Gable (월름라) and Myrna Loy (올토) are each suitable roles and performances—anyway, *Night Flight* was a recent masterpiece (快篇). If I were to express one desire, it would be to have placed more beautiful nature as the stage and background for the love of the two protagonists. Of course, it's true that the story itself had geographical constraints, but the depiction of nature was certainly too sparse. What comes to mind suddenly is the countryside scenery appearing in *Les Beaux Jours* (아름다운 친구들); how much more vitality (生色) the story would have had and how much more beautiful the impression would have been if those beautiful pictures of forests, streams, and trees had been drawn behind the two characters. But perhaps this is an excessive desire.<154>

Encounters at the Movie Theater
Yi Hŏn'gu

On the last day of last December, I went to the public bath with Kwang-hyŏng (光형) and met H-hyŏng, a middle school classmate, for the first time in 14 years. It might be called a coincidence, but we often meet entirely unexpected people in ordinary places, and it's commonplace to coincidentally meet people we know well in unexpected places. Here, I will write about a few people I met by chance, sometimes unexpectedly, at the movie theater.
Mr. K - He is known as our only essayist, but he lives in a world almost separate from movies. However, three years ago, on a warm late autumn Sunday afternoon, I went to Theater D (D社) to see Simone Simon's *Lac aux Dames* (處女湖). The concurrently released film was *Ch'unhyangjŏn* (春香傳). After finally getting a seat and some time passing, I looked around and saw this Mr. K sitting right in front of me. I was surprised on one hand, and finding it so unexpected, I inwardly smiled wryly (微苦笑) and thought of some teasing remark. I went up to him, tapped his shoulder, and said,
"Ah! What brings you here? To a place like this?"
"Huh! Well, *Ch'unhyangjŏn* is so famous!"
His voice was awkward yet tinged with bashfulness (含羞). I thought, as if making an unexpected discovery, about how effective movie advertisements and temptations could be. Since then, I haven't talked about movies when meeting Mr. K, and he continues to live completely detached from them.
Miss K - It was around April last year. It happened when Simon's *Green Fields*? (綠園) was showing at Theater Y (Y 극장). On my way from work, tired as I was, I went alone to see it and sat in a corner on the second floor. Since the matinee had just ended, the seat next to me was empty. Not even five minutes after the evening program started, I sensed two women sitting down next to me with bustling footsteps and breathless whispers. It wouldn't have been impolite to glance over, but seeing Simon's face on the screen was enough, and my immediate interest didn't stray sideways. It was dark. <155>
They probably wouldn't have quickly recognized the person next to them either. Then, there was a rustling sound, like unwrapping paper (包紙). It seemed the four hands of the two women were each searching for something in the dark. I thought to myself, "Why are they rustling about so noisily?" and turned my head to the left, just as their giggling laughter erupted. That laugh! The heroine was Miss K, whom I knew well. "What brings you here?" I couldn't help but ask in surprise.
"Mr. R (R 선생) is quite something too. We just ran over from X Garden (X원). We didn't have time for dinner. Here, have one."
On the women's laps lay shoe cream cakes (슈- 크림 케- 키) and such on shiny smooth paper. Miss Chŏng (丁양) was her companion. Miss K said she had come yesterday too, and today was her second time. Overwhelmed by her excessive Simone fandom, I even felt a sense of disappointment, as if my beautiful private world had been stolen.
Mr. J - While it's usual to go see movies with close friends, there are many times when I just go alone on impulse. Especially in the last 2 or 3 years, I've done so. But 5 years ago, even then, there was maybe one or two good movies a month, if that. It was February then, and that year too, the late cold snap was severe. On that particular day, it was snowing and raining, making the streets gloomy and bitterly harsh (辛酸). Dietrich's *Dishonored* (간첩 X27), which I had eagerly awaited for three years since *Morocco* (모로코), was showing at the now burnt-down Chogŭk Theater (朝劇). Since it was only shown at night, I ate dinner and went to sit downstairs. At that time, when it was really cold, you had to buy a brazier to endure it; the cold inside the theater, especially the chill rising from the ground floor, was unbearable. "I wish it would start soon," I thought, hugging my knees, burying my head in my overcoat, fighting the cold. Just as the bell rang for the start, I glanced at the people sitting on the right side, and right there, two rows ahead at the far right end, I saw Mr. J's long hair. "J!" I called softly, but J immediately recognized the voice and, looking very surprised, asked, "When did you get here?" smiling, he came and sat next to me. The cold grew increasingly severe. Both legs were freezing, and my feet were so cold I couldn't stand it. But more than that, overwhelmed by Dietrich's intense yet cool passion on the screen, moved and awed by her every gaze (視綠), every pose (포-즈), we ended up clinging to each other, hardly daring to breathe deeply, completely captivated (陶醉). Thinking back, if there's a history to my movie-watching, the fact that I was so moved and captivated until the end while fighting the cold like that night <156> seems unparalleled. After that, another time, at the Yu Theater (유舘), which I rarely went to, I went to see Franchot Tone's? (프레쟝) *Cry Out, Volga!?* (絶叫하라 볼가여!) and ran into T, who happened to be visiting Seoul. We laughed, saying, "What kind of mischievous fate is this?" recalling the Chogŭk incident. I admired the acting and beauty of "Datyu" (다튜-) in that movie, but I never dreamed that actress would be today's "Datyu" (Danielle Darrieux?).
Miss C - This was when *The Life of Beethoven* (樂聖 베- 토- 펜) was showing at Theater M (M座). Some time after I entered, one film ended, and as seats became available, I was milling about looking for a spot when I saw this Miss M (likely a typo for C). She said the seat next to her was empty. I barely sat down, the lights went out, and *Beethoven* began. While the acting wasn't particularly remarkable, I was moved by the pure heart of Thérèse (the sister role) played by Annie Ducaux (아니, 듀코오). With the end of the musical genius's story, the theater lit up again. Just then, Mr. R (R군), Miss C's sole close acquaintance, came over looking pleased, greeted us, and said he was leaving. I was quite flustered and asked Miss C after Mr. R left,
"Didn't you arrange to meet Mr. R?" She replied very coolly with just one word, "Ah, no." Since I had some idea about the relationship between the two, I worried needlessly, wondering if Mr. R might have misunderstood me, feeling both uneasy and unpleasant. When I told Mr. R the next day about the unpleasantness I felt during that awkward moment, he immediately scolded me, saying, "Why be so timid? If you start misunderstanding things like that, how can we trust each other and live!" However, I later heard and saw incidents where this kind of misunderstanding arising in such places was not unreasonable.
Pastor R - (His position is more than just a pastor). After 10 years, *Seventh Heaven* (第七天國), starring Simone Simon (시뭉) and James Stewart? (스튜아-트), was recently shown at Theater M (M座). That night, I went to see it with two friends, K and H (K H 2友). It was quite crowded, so we took scattered seats here and there. During the newsreel, Pastor R guided his accompanying family down the aisle next to me towards the very front. It felt awkward to go find him and greet him, so I just stayed seated. After the newsreel, *Seventh Heaven* began. Not long after, a scene appeared where a cathedral priest refutes the atheism of a mere janitor, Chico (시- 코). While it wasn't entirely unexpected for a religious figure like Pastor R to come to such a place, what struck me more was that the priest's appearance (風貌) on screen closely resembled Pastor R, and even the tone of his sermon in English was similar <157> to him. I kept wondering how interestingly Pastor R must be watching such a scene, and it remained in my head until the end, giving me a somewhat strange and curious feeling. Moreover, since Pastor R is renowned as one of the most fluent English speakers in Korea (조선), I watched all the priest's scenes in the movie, substituting Pastor R for him. It might be disrespectful, but I found it quite amusing (자미잇게) to imagine this triangle myself.
Madame T - Was it 6 years ago? It was when *Remord?* (旅愁) was shown, jointly recommended by the arts sections of three newspapers. I went to see it with T-hyŏng from D School (D 校). Just as we were about to enter the entrance, I saw the lady's figure from behind among the crowd entering in a line. I briefly lost my composure. She was someone I had been close enough to leave a scar (傷痕) on my inner world, and by then, she already had a new family. From this movie, the sound of the cold, smooth, sad lake water lapping against the old boat (槽) at night still remains in my eardrums (耳膜). Sitting in a corner on the second floor, I searched for the phantom of her, who must have been sitting somewhere in the same place, occasionally feeling the sharp taste of chewing betel nuts? (게자) throughout my body. On the way back that night, J-hyŏng mentioned that "T" had come, and I deliberately feigned ignorance, just replying with an awkward "Yes!" (네!), feeling even more flustered by J-hyŏng's glance, which seemed to scrutinize my feelings. Should I call it a strange encounter (奇遇)?

My Film School Days
Kim Munjip

Perhaps no one would be particularly surprised if I said I was a classmate of stars of the silver screen from over 10 years ago like Kosugi Isamu (小杉勇) or Shimada Kōji (島田耕二), but if I said we were classmates at a Japanese film actor school, there might be no one who wouldn't be surprised.
It was during middle school. While dreaming a grand delusion—unaware it was a dream—of building a massive 10 million won film studio in Chosŏn (조선), introducing Chosŏn culture to the world while importing world culture into Chosŏn, and personally adapting my literary works into films to guide the world <158>, I secretly, without my older brother (who was a painter) or my guarantor knowing, prepaid a 100 won monthly tuition fee and enrolled in that film school located in Nishikata-machi, Hongō-ku (本鄕區 西片町), right after the Great Kanto Earthquake (震災).
Were there 5 or 6 female students and about 20 male students? The school building (校舍) was a rented house, quite splendid, worthy of being the private residence (私邸) of a wealthy university professor. Here, we underwent rigorous practice in the basics of dance, getting hit with sticks, and received instruction on various theories and practical aspects of theater and film from authorities in the field (斯界). There were 1st, 2nd, and 3rd-year classes horizontally, but vertically, classes were divided into beginner, intermediate, and advanced levels. There were four students in the advanced class: a young man with a broad face wearing a Shimada hat (島打帽) who claimed to have graduated from Nihon University's Art Department (日本大學藝術科), a rather experienced-looking woman (who later joined Shochiku (松竹), resembling Hayashi Chisato (林千歲)), myself, who was quite cheeky (なまいき), and besides these three, Shimada Kōji. We received lectures on the King Lear section of *Shakespeare Monogatari* (沙翁物語) in the original text from the renowned Mori Iwao (森岩雄). Even English literature graduates struggle to read *Shakespeare Monogatari*, so it goes without saying that we four students, all roughly at the same level (오십보 백보), were hardly qualified to study that book.
At that time, Kosugi Isamu was in the intermediate class. The Kosugi of that time was an extremely simple country fellow (朴調한 시굴 녀석).
Twice a month, there were stage play performances, and sometimes filming (*영). And as a group, we went on free study trips to Mizutani Yaeko's (水谷八重子) Geijutsuza Theater (藝術座) in Shibuya (澁谷).
Once, Kikuchi Kan's? (菊池寶) play *Junban* (順番 - Order/Turn) was performed at the school. As usual, many people related to this field gathered. The night deepened in a very familial atmosphere. The protagonist, a hereditary mentally ill person, is confined in a place like an animal cage (獸檻) at a zoo.
The principal, Mr. Mizuguchi Biroku (水口微鹿), one of the contributors to the Waseda-affiliated (早大系) theater culture, seemed to favor me particularly, subtly hinting at connecting me with one of his two daughters—this was because he mistook me for the son of a wealthy Korean family (조선의 부호).
On the night of *Junban*, the principal stayed by my side, diligently offering various points of advice about the acting. This was because I was tasked with critique after every performance.
The animal in the cage (檻內)—that was none other than Kosugi Isamu.
After the curtain fell, I pointed out the exaggeration in the main actor's mad cries (狂聲). Kosugi retorted, asking me to demonstrate practically how the sound should be made <159>. I replied that since I wasn't a madman, I couldn't imitate it, but it was true that his sound felt unnatural. Shimada Kōji also agreed.
Before a few months passed, my enrollment in the actor school was discovered (發露). Telegrams and whatnot flew back and forth between my brother, guarantor, both schools, Taegu (大邱)...
After the storm subsided—as days turned into months and months into years—I safely enrolled in a certain high school in a region blessed with scenic beauty (風光明眉). By then, Kosugi and Shimada were not just with Nikkatsu (日活) but had become nationally recognized top stars, dazzling the silver screen.
However, the name Shimada Kōji is somewhat uncertain (未詳). He was a handsome man type (이로오도꼬) who became famous even before Kosugi, and since we went around together filming a movie scene on the rooftop of the premier Ginza department store (銀座 제일 상호빨 옥상), his face is more familiar than Kosugi's, but perhaps because I haven't seen his name in the credits for the past few years, or because there are many similar names, I can't distinguish clearly.
In any case, for me, it's an unforgettable era. Attending the Musashino-kan (武藏野舘), Tokyo's (東都) sole top-tier movie theater, diligently every week, having my short film reviews accepted and published in its program, collecting photograph albums (푸로마이드 앨뺌) that reached 20 volumes, sending unintelligible English love letters (戀書) across the Pacific to Lilian Gish (리리안 낐슈) and lamenting the utter lack of replies (絶無), and so on... the stories are endless.
During those film school days, earnest and tearful letters arrived almost daily from a certain boy in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), pleading, "Please, somehow get me into the school where Teacher was."
In the spring two years ago, when I mentioned this briefly in a film reminiscence published in *Yŏsŏng* (女性) magazine, Sin Kyŏnggyun (申敬均) stepped forward, claiming to be the protagonist of those letters, took me all the way to the Han River (漢江), and confessed. I was quite pleased.
Although I haven't watched many movies since coming out of Korea (조선), there was a time when I wandered around skipping three meals a day, so I suppose I'm not exactly an amateur (바이 시로도는 아닌 셈이다).<160>

**Summary**

This multi-author article from the October 1938 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine gathers personal reflections on cinema from four Korean intellectuals: No Ch'ŏnmyŏng, Yi Hyosŏk, Yi Hŏn'gu, and Kim Munjip. No expresses disillusionment with actors after an incident involving Robert Taylor. Yi Hyosŏk compares film favorably to novels in efficiency, praises several contemporary Western films (especially *Night Flight*), and anticipates film's artistic evolution. Yi Hŏn'gu shares anecdotes of chance encounters with acquaintances (an essayist, fans, a pastor, a past love interest) in various Seoul movie theaters, highlighting cinema's role as a social space. Kim Munjip reminisces about briefly attending a Japanese film acting school in Tokyo alongside future stars Kosugi Isamu and Shimada Kōji, his youthful cinematic ambitions, and a later connection with actor Sin Kyŏnggyun. For historians, the article offers valuable insights into the film culture and reception among Korean intellectuals during the late colonial period. It reveals their engagement with Western cinema, awareness of international film stars and directors, the social function of movie theaters in Seoul (Kyŏngsŏng), and personal aspirations related to film, including dreams of establishing a Korean film industry and cross-cultural connections through institutions like Japanese acting schools.

盧天命
좋은 영화를 본 때면 그 사진에서 연기가 훌륭햇든 배우쯤 몇일 동안 인상적인 장면과 함께 머리에 떠오르는 정도로 그 사진 전체에서 받은 인상보다는 더 먼저 배우들은 잊어버려 버리고 마는 버릇이 있읍니다.
그렇게 좋다고 내세울 만한 남배우를 가젔으면 제법이지요.
언젠가 신문 연예면에 한 번 로벗테일러-를 주문대로 써주었다가 편즙자가 부처준 과장적 제목일래 마치 내가 서양 광대 한 사람을 그처럼 좋아나 하는 것 같이 되여버려 자못 봉변을 당하고 몇인 동안을 두고 마음을 알았든 일이 있은 뒤로는 로벋테일러- 라는 남배우가 무척 싫여젔을 뿐 좋아하는 남배우를 갓지 못합니다.＜151＞

南方飛行其他
李孝石
영화 예술의 발달은 최근 날로 놀나운 것이었다. 물론 영화는 영화, 소설은 소설이나 하로 나 이틀을 걸녀 소설 한 권을 떼는 노력보다 불과 시간 반에 단정하게 정리된 한 편의 이야기를 보는 편이 얼마나 경제적인가도 생각된다. 영화에는 심리 묘사가 欠乏되나 이 난관도 차차 극복되어가는 듯이 보이며 앞으로는 소설보다도 도리혀 한 수 앞선 수법까지 발명될 듯이 짐작된다. 문장이 주는 매력은 영화의 한층 적확한 시각적 요소와 그것이 비저내는 특수한 분위기가 충분히 건저준다.
생활의 한 폭의 단면을 그렇게도 수월하게 눈만으로 감상할 수 있음이 새삼스럽게 경이로 느껴진다.
작금 연중으로 본 영화 가온대에 기억에 남는 것이 허다하다. 현대적 사상을 얼마간 구체화 시키고 극적 혹은 무대적 요소를 다분히 띄인 것에 「化石의 수풀」과 「目擊者」가 있으니 미국 현대 희극작가의 기질과 특징을 여실히 가추운 점과 영화와 연극과의 類緣 교통성을 암시한 점에 양 편의 공통된 흥미가 있다. 현대 영화에는 우선 이 정도의 사상의 표현도 필요한 것이며 허름한 가무의 속편이 범람하는 미국에서 도리혀 이런 영화를 나은 것이 일종의 비꼬움이라고도 할까.
인생의 관조니 무어니 하는 심각한 주름살을 펴고 천치같이 안저서 우두커니 바라만 보기에 족한 것이 「女人 俱樂部」 「일허진 地平線」 「아름다운 친구들」 등이였다. 여인의 육체의 군상에 혼을 뽑히우고 西藏의 오지라고 일카르는 분지의 자연을 꿈구고 불란서 전원의 풍광 滿喫하면 족하다. 비슷한 아름다운 것을 보이면서도 그대로 보아만 넘기여 아까운 것은 「마리아 삽드레에느」 「아라아의 花園」 「외로운 소나무 그림자」 등이다. 사람의 그림자 귀한 흰 처녀지 우에는 건질 수 없는 인생의 哀感이 흘렀고 채색한 사막 우에는 무더운 열정이 넘첫고 소나무 선 수풀 속에는 태고 적부터 시작된 인생의 싸홈이 끈일힐 새 없었다. 실바, 시드니의＜152＞ 하눌빛으로 물드린 새파란 치마폭에는 그대로 마을의 싸홈의 역사가 적혀 있는 듯도 하였다.
「펜슌미모사」「베토벤의 위대한 사랑」「안나 카레니나」「南方 飛行」- 이 일련의 영화는 무슨 항목 아래 분류함이 적당할까. 무릇 다소간에 애욕을 취급하지 안는 작품이라는 것은 없으나 이 일련의 작품에 있어서는 그 주제가 바로 애욕이다.
미모사 舘에 서리운 욕망같이 야릇하고 기괴한 것은 드므니 양자에게 대한 양모의 애끗는 회포에는 옷섭은 바로잡게 하는 측은하고 절절함이 있다. 육체로나 정서로나 로제에는 양모로서 완전한 적역이며 그의 연기에는 거의 흠잡을 곳이 없다. 번민하는 육체 수수꺽기의 눈추리를 통해서 은근한 숨은 애정이 눈 앞에 생생하게 보여온다. 이런 류의 애욕의 주제와 이 감독의 수완에 다시 생각이 니를 때 한 가지 몽상이 솟는 것은 콕토오의 「레 상팡 텔리 부울」- 이것을 영화로서 재현시시킬 수 없을가 함이다. 참으로 일종의 몽상일지도 모르니 이 미묘한 심리적 갈등의 어려운 작품을 영화화함은 지금으로서는 아직 至難의 業일 것임으로다. 이 至難事를 담당함 즉 한 사람은 역시 미모사 舘의 감독자쯤일 것이며 그가 작자 콕토오와 협력한다면 어느 정도까지 색여서 표현시킬 수 있을 것이 짐작된다. 물론 가까운 장래의 일일 것이다. 기괴한 애욕의 주제라면 이 외에도 가량 고대의 「에디프스 王」이나 근대의 「느름나무 아래의 愛慾」 같은 것은 適好의 재료이며 적어도 이런 것에 착안할 만한 높은 예술적 炯眼을 앞으로의 영화인은 가지게 되여야 할 것이다.
「베토펜의 偉大한 사랑」에 있어서는 작품의 주제보다도 더 많이 음악적 효과에 압도를 당하게 되나 주제가 선명하면서 비슷한 것이 「안나 카레니나」와 「南行飛行」이며 이 두 작품이 성공한 것은 물론 완전히 원작의 덕이다. 두 작품의 장점은 모다 바로 원작의 장점이며 원작의 묘미가 이렇게 歷然히 들이는 작품도 드믈다. 너무도 유명한 「안나 카레니나」는 고사하야 두고 「南行飛行」의 일편은 최근의 가장 큰 매력이였다. 감독의 수완이라는 것 보다도 원작자 상텍쥬베리의 의도와 意匠이 너무도 명료하게 들어나서 그것이 작품으로서의 큰 매력을 비저 낸다.
원작자의 意想은 물론 인간 생활에 있어서의 행동의 필요성 불가피성을 말하랴는 곳에 있으나 그 행동이라는 것이 반다시 주인공 짝베르니스가 폭풍우의 밤 위험한 항로를＜153＞ 건너 비행기를 조종하는 그 야단스런 거동을 통하야서만 나타난 것이 아니라 참으로 여주인공 쥬느베에브와의 사랑을 완성시키고야마는 그 중대한 轉機와 결의에 더 一義的으로 나타난 것은 물론이다. 비행기의 폭음이 아무리 요란하고 험한 사막과 사병과의 싸홈의 장면이 아무리 허다하게 나온다고 하야도 참으로 아름다운 행동의 장면은 베르니스와 쥬느베에브가 드되어 대사의 관저를 떠나 도피행을 감행하는 그 시간의 一場인 것이다. 불의니 背德이니의 容■을 허락하지 안는 순간의 중대한 행동이다. 미래? 미래는 우리가 창조하는 것이다- 떠나는 순간 베르니스가 쥬느베에브를 격려한 말이 바로 이것이다. 인간 생활에 있어서 중요한 것은 반다시 행복의 타산과 계량만이 아니요 그런 것을 건너서 행동의 의욕은 마치 육중한 수레와도 같이 사랑의 등줄기를 앞으로 밀어버리고야 만다. 성공여부가 문제가 아니요 다만 참을 수 없는 수행이 있을 뿐이다. 베르니스는 사랑에도 최후적으로 성공하였다고는 볼 수 없으며 비행기의 항로에서도 실패하고야 말엇다. 그러나 그의 뜨저운 의욕 앞에 그런 실패쯤이 무었이랴. 그는 용감히 행동하고 흔연히 가버린 것이다. 그는 확실히 아름답게 산 한 사람이다.
이러한 행동의 상념도 아름다운 것이지만 그 구체적 표현으로 주인공 두 사람의 핍박한 애정의 경우를 선택한 곳에 이 작가의 才華가 보이며 두 사람의 거동은 전 화면을 통하야 가장 아름다운 부분이여서 웬일인지 아무리 찬양하여도 부족을 느끼 된다. 「안나, 카레니나」에 나타난 사랑의 형태도 이와 같은 것이지만 그보다도 이 편이 더 단적이고 인상적임은 웬일일까. 월름라, 올토의 연기도 각각 적역 적연이며 - 엇더튼 「南方飛行」의 일편은 근래의 快篇이였다. 한 가지의 욕심을 말하면 주인공 두 사람의 사랑의 무대와 배경으로 좀 더 아름다운 자연을 배치하였드면 하는 것이다. 물론 이야기 자체의 지리적 제약이 있었든 것은 사실이나 자연 묘사가 확실히 너무도 빈약하였다. 문득 생각나는 것은 「아름다운 친구들」에 나오는 전원의 풍경이니 수풀과 시내와 수목과의 그 아름다운 그림을 두 사람 뒤에다 그려놓았든들 얼마나 한층 이야기에 生色이 있고 인상이 더 아름다웠을가 생각된다. 그러나 이것은 지나처 과한 욕심일는지 모른다.＜154＞

필자가 小杉勇이니 島田耕二이니 하는 10여 년 이래의 은막의 名星들과 동창이였다고 해도 별로 놀라는 사람은 없겠지마는 일본 영화배우학교에서의 동창이였다고 하면 놀라지 않을 사람은 없을 것이다.
중학교 때다. 문호를 꿈꾸든 하便 조선에 천만원의 대 촬영소를 건설해서 조선 문화를 세계에 소개하는 일방 세계 문화를 조선메 주입하고 내 자신 문학의 작품을 내 손으로 영화화해서 이 또한 세계를＜158＞ 지도하리라는 과대망상의 깊은 꿈을 꿈인 줄 모르고 꿈꾸고 있었든 나는 동거의 형도 화가인 보증인도 아모도 모르게 일금 100원의 월사금을 선납하고 本鄕區 西片町에 있었든 震災 직후의 그 영화 학교에 입학했엇든 것이다.
생도는 여자가 5, 6명 남자가 20명이나 되였을까. 校舍는 유복한 대학 교수의 私邸라고 할 만한 상당히 훌융한 貰ㅅ집이였다. 여기서 우리는 매를 맞어가면서 무용의 기초를 맹연습했고 斯界의 권위들에게 연극과 영화에 관한 各般의 이론과 실지를 교수받었다. 橫으로 1 2 3기가 있었으나 語字만은 縱으로 초, 중, 고등의 3과로 난우어 있었다. 고등과 반의 생도는 넷이였다. 일본 대학 예술과를 졸업했다는 얼굴 넙적한 島打帽의 청년과 後松竹에 (입사한 林千歲 비슷한) 상당히 달가먹은 여자 하나 그리고 퍽 나마이끼 했든 나와 이 세 사람 외에 島田耕二가 있었다. 우리는 例의 森岩雄씨에 램게의 원서 沙翁物語 중 리어왕 편의 강의를 받었다. 영문과 출신자도 잘못 읽는 沙翁物語라 서로들 오십보 백보인 우리 네 생도가 한갓 그 책을 배홀 자격이 없었음은 물론이었다.
