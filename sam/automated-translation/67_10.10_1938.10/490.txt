Summer Night
						Chang Tŏk-cho

It was a night unusually filled with buzzing mosquitoes. The husband, evening paper in one hand, lay flat on his back, swatting sharply at mosquitoes landing on his pulled-up calves, estimating their position before striking.

"Oh dear, why do you keep hitting the exact same spot? What crime did your legs commit?"
The wife, who had been turned away writing a letter, burst out laughing.
"Is slapping your legs a hobby too? With that constant slapping sound from earlier, my mind is so confused I can hardly write anything."

"What can I do if those darn mosquitoes insist on landing right there?"
Only then did the husband lift his leg high, looking at the reddened spot.
"By the way, what is that you're writing?"

"A letter!"

"What kind of letter? For it to be causing trouble even over the sound of me swatting mosquitoes, it must be something unusual, <299> right?"

"Oh, you’re just trying to shift the blame because you feel bad for disturbing me. What do you mean, what kind of letter? Do I have many places to write to? It's usually just my mother back home."

"No, no, so you mean you send all those letters you're always writing just to your mother?"
The wife, looking resentful, deliberately bit her lip and glared.
"Honestly, am I always writing letters? Maybe once every five days, or once every four days, that's all."

"That's not acceptable either."
The husband abruptly sat up.
"Are you having an affair with your family home? Even once every four days is unacceptable. At most, once a month or once every two months should be enough. Anything more than that is strictly forbidden."
He lit a cigarette, trying to hide the smile playing on his lips.

"Why?"

"The reason is, well... the reason is, firstly, you're not in good health, and if you keep lying prone writing letters like that, it's bad for your health, and the other side has to write replies, so you're making others do something difficult. And right, there's more, more."

"What is it?"

"Economically, too, it's a loss. Let's see. Once every four days means five times a month, so four times five is twenty... wait, let's calculate properly... five times a month? No, a month has about 30 days, so 30/4 is about 7.5 times. Let's say 7 times. That's 7 stamps. If a stamp costs 3 chon... ah, let's re-read. Ah, he says five times a month. Okay. So, five times a month... wait, 4 * 5 = 20... no, that doesn't make sense. Ah, maybe he meant five letters total in a month cycle? '나흘에 한 번이면 한 달에 다섯 번이니' - if it's once every four days, it's five times a month?? That math is wrong (30/4 ≈ 7.5). Let's follow his faulty logic: five times a month means 5 stamps, let's assume 4 chon per stamp then 20 chon a month? Then '사 오 이십하고 일년이면 이원 사십전' - 4*5=20 (chon?) and a year is 2 won 40 chon. This implies 20 chon/month * 12 months = 240 chon = 2 won 40 chon. So, his faulty math leads to 2 won 40 chon per year. And '량편에서 오원돈' - from both sides, about 5 won. Okay, proceeding with his numbers. ...Let's see. If it's once every four days, that's five times a month, so four times five is twenty, and in a year, that's two won and forty chon. Economically, it's a loss of about five won for both sides."

"Oh, honestly..."
The wife, looking dumbfounded, was about to twist her lips into a smile but stopped.
"Really, what are you talking about? Two won and some tens of chon a year? Is that what you're making a fuss about?"
she pressed, taking issue anew.
"I know everything. Even if you don't say it, I've known it all for a while now."

"Known what?" <300>

"Well, how you've been getting irritable for no reason when you come home lately, and trying to oppress people. There's clearly another reason, isn't there?"
The husband, trying to cover up the fact that the spark had unexpectedly landed elsewhere, laughed out loud, "Ha ha ha," but she continued.
"Think I'll be fooled just because you laugh? I haven't said anything until now because I thought you'd get even angrier if I told you the truth. But look here. They say even a hunchback knows the court's affairs (곱사등이 조종일 안다구); even cooped up inside the house like this, I hear all the rumors the world has to offer."
The wife, having brought it up, pushed aside what she was writing and turned around, seemingly determined to argue it out thoroughly.

The husband couldn't just remain silent and accept defeat.
"Well, what kind of rumor is it? Tell me. Let me hear what strange rumor it is, and if it concerns me greatly, I'll admit it as it is, okay?"

"You won't get angry?"

"Let me hear it first."

"No. Promise me you won't get angry. Otherwise, I won't tell you."

"No. You have to tell me the truth. No. What nonsense did some scoundrel come and blabber to you? Hurry up and tell me."

"Well then..."

"What?"

"Uh... that you've been stuck in drinking houses (요릿집) almost every day lately, having quite a bit of fun. And that you're very skillful in handling kisaeng."

"And what did you say?"

"I said I was furious enough to die. That someday I'd sit you down calmly and properly give you a hard time."

"Tsk, you fool!"
The husband suddenly shouted, and the wife closed her mouth.
"Because you're always so weak and unreliable (ヘナヘナ), you hear such things from others. Huh? Just try being firm and dependable (식카리). Who would dare speak ill of a husband in front of his wife?"

"..." <301>

"If you're going to talk such nonsense, just go back to your parents' home. I'll help you pack tomorrow if you want. Please, just go down to that home you can't forget, writing love letters back and forth every four days."
As he spoke, the husband found it strange how his attitude changed significantly when speaking to his wife compared to when he thought about her alone.
His cherishing and loving affection for his wife was famous enough to be talked about among his friends.
"Mr. Kim and eleven o'clock."
That was a kind of "nickname" known among close friends and kisaeng.

"Right, if you have things to pack, roughly gather them even tonight, and plan to leave on the first train tomorrow, understand?"
The wife, seemingly unable to bear it any longer, abruptly stood up and went into the inner room. Then, loud enough for him to hear, came the sound of choked sobbing for a good while.
The husband, deliberately pretending to be indifferent, lay down flat again.
"Sŏkt'an paekt'an
Where they burn
My heart..."
he sang a verse of a poorly sung common song (사발가).

Only the swarm of mosquitoes renewed their clamorous attack. Somewhere, a clock struck eleven. The husband finally began to feel sorry for his wife, huddled up in the dark and stuffy corner of the inner room.
An impulsive urge to hug his wife surged within him, but he forcibly suppressed such feelings.
After a while, when his mood had calmed,
"Dear,"
he called out most gently, but the wife, perhaps exhausted, made no sound.
Again, the mosquitoes buzzed and charged.

Right before his eyes, the raw silk mosquito net (생초 모기장), half rolled up, spread its wings, and plump bedding, freshly changed (갖 요잇을 가라싳은), lay piled up, <302> but there was no one to lower the net.
Having no choice,
"Come on, what's there to sulk about?"
he tried raising his voice slightly, but still no answer came. Yet, getting up himself to lower the mosquito net and spread the bedding was too bothersome. Now, more than emotion, the practical inconvenience came first.
"Honestly, wife, there's no need for this. So what if I made a little joke? Huh? Come on out here. Don't get needlessly bitten by mosquitoes!"
However, instead of his wife's gentle reply, the husband heard a strange sound.
*Snore, snore, snore.* The wife had already fallen fast asleep long ago.

"Damn it! A fool is a fool, can't help it. Huh? When a person is being irritable, she hangs on every word, afraid of missing something, but when you try to offer some warm comfort, she's dead to the world, sprawled out. Go ahead, see if I care. If she's upset, it's her own loss. Her own loss." Still, feeling awkward and angry, he unknowingly smacked his stinging calf with all his might.
A mosquito flew away with a buzz!
"Damn it! Fly away quickly and go bite that person in the room. Damn! Hasn't a machine been invented yet that lowers the mosquito net and lays out the bedding by itself?! Honestly!"
The husband lay there just like that for a long, long time, staring at the ceiling with wide eyes. - (End) - <303>

**Summary**

This short story depicts a marital spat on a stuffy summer night plagued by mosquitoes. The husband idly swats mosquitoes while the wife writes a letter to her mother. He teases her about the frequency of her letters, leading to an argument where she confronts him with rumors of his spending time with kisaeng at drinking houses. He angrily denies this, criticizes her for being weak-willed (using Japanese loanwords), and tells her to leave. Later, feeling remorseful, he tries to reconcile, only to find she has cried herself to sleep. He is left alone, frustrated by the mosquitoes and annoyed that he must set up the mosquito net himself, lamenting the lack of a self-deploying bed-making machine.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a glimpse into the domestic life of a middle-class Korean couple during the Japanese colonial period. The casual integration of Japanese words like "ヘナヘナ" (henǎhenǎ - weak/unreliable) and "식카리" (shikkari - firmly/reliably) into Korean conversation highlights the linguistic influence of the colonizer. The mention of "요릿집" (yori-chip - restaurants often associated with kisaeng entertainment), the economic calculation involving won and chon, and the social nickname "Mr. Kim and eleven o'clock" (suggesting a reputation for returning home at a set time, perhaps dutifully) provide small but revealing details about the social norms, economic realities, and cultural blending of the era. The power dynamic and communication style between the husband and wife also reflect contemporary gender roles and marital relations.

「여보」

그러나 남편은 안해의 상냥한 대답 대신 이상한 음향을 들었다.
