Publication Date: October 1, 1938
Article Title: 戰時下의 新聞社長의 態度, 思想統制·物資統制의 長期戰 下인 오늘날에 잇서 三大 新聞社의 今後 進路는 如何한가 (The Stance of Newspaper Presidents Under Wartime Conditions: What is the Future Path of the Three Major Newspaper Companies Today, Under the Long War of Thought Control and Material Control?)
Author: 本社 金東煥 (Kim Tong-hwan, Samch'ŏlli)
Article Type: 설문 (Survey/Interview)

The Stance of Newspaper Presidents Under Wartime Conditions: What is the Future Path of the Three Major Newspaper Companies Today, Under the Long War of Thought Control and Material Control?
Mr. Paek Kwan-su, President, Tonga Ilbo
Mr. Im Chŏng-yŏp, Business Manager, Tonga Ilbo
Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo, President & Business Manager, Chosŏn Ilbo
Mr. Yi Sang-hyŏp, Vice President, Maeil Sinbo
(As President Ch'oe Rin was absent attending a meeting, we sought out the Vice President)
Kim Tong-hwan, Our Company <30>

Countermeasures for Paper Conservation
As Tokyo's Yomiuri Shimbun, boasting a "circulation of 1 million," suddenly took the lead and implemented a 2-page reduction (from 16 pages to 14 pages), the newspaper societies across Tokyo, Osaka, and the mainland (内地 - *naichi*, i.e., Japan proper) have received a huge shock. How are the three major newspapers in Chosŏn (Korea) planning to respond to the Government-General's policy to conserve newsprint by 12%? Is it page reduction? Or is it a reduction in the number of readers? What is your company's stance on this?

Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo (President, Chosŏn Ilbo)
= Of course, it's a serious issue, but until the Ch'unch'uhoe (春秋會), the joint association of all daily newspapers in Chosŏn, fundamentally decides after embodying the authorities' policy, I cannot definitively state the stance of our Chosŏn Ilbo alone. However, speaking of the situation on the mainland, since the Yomiuri Shimbun, which holds great influence in the Kantō region, has swiftly reduced pages by 2, it's possible that various newspapers in Osaka and Tokyo might also follow suit and reduce pages to demonstrate the 12% paper conservation. But Chosŏn has different social circumstances, so our company will absolutely not reduce the current 12 pages (though there are papers like the Maeil Sinbo currently at 10 pages). We plan to achieve paper conservation by increasing the frequency of the 8-page reduction, which currently happens twice a month, to perhaps several more times a month, but in principle, we will not reduce pages. You ask what about the price? It's currently 1 won 20 chŏn, and it would be logical to lower it somewhat due to the page reductions, but the advertising revenue side also takes a hit because of these reductions, leading to decreased income. If we were to lower subscription fees on top of that, the newspaper company's finances might not hold up, which is a big headache. We intend to decide on whether to reduce subscription fees after studying this point further.
In short, the principle is to maintain the current 12 pages daily, keep the subscription fee as is if possible, and achieve the 12% paper conservation through the method of occasional page reductions.

Mr. Paek Kwan-su (President, Tonga Ilbo)
= The Kyŏngsŏng Ilbo has 16 pages, the Chosŏn Sinmun 12 pages, the Chosŏn Ilbo 12 pages, the Maeil Sinbo 10 pages, and the Tonga Ilbo 12 pages – this is the current state of the Chosŏn newspaper world. Now, if we were to uniformly reduce pages by 12% as the authorities say, the Kyŏngsŏng Ilbo would have to cut about 2 pages, and the Chosŏn Ilbo and our company about 1.5 pages. However, since there are various special circumstances in Chosŏn, rather than reducing the number of pages <31> published daily, it would be better to achieve the same ratio of paper conservation in total quantity by increasing the frequency of suspending the 4-page evening edition, which used to happen twice a month, to perhaps double, triple, or quadruple that, say 6 or 8 times. Therefore, although the Ch'unch'uhoe's decision is yet to be made, the Tonga Ilbo plans to maintain the current 12 pages while implementing occasional reductions, and we intend to keep the current price of 1 won 20 chŏn.
Also, the issue of paper conservation has not yet materialized concretely; it will probably be decided around October. The contractual relationship with the paper manufacturing company that supplies paper (조히) has also not been decided yet and remains unresolved. I expect the paper company will probably inform us about restrictions on paper sales.

Mr. Yi Sang-hyŏp (Vice President, Maeil Sinbo)
= When our Maeil Sinbo previously reduced its page count to the current 10 pages, we stated to both Ōji Paper (王子제지) and the Government-General authorities that we were doing so for paper conservation. That is, our company had already reduced 2 pages before the issue of reduction/abolition (減廢-止) arose. Therefore, for the sake of national policy (국책상), we intend to maintain the current 10 pages as much as possible. However, if there is a need and we are told to do something more, we must comply.
Generally, the requirement to conserve 12% is a fundamental condition decided in principle, but the specific issue of page reduction has not yet been decided. It will probably be decided this fall.
In any case, issues like newspaper subscription fees and whether to reduce pages will be decided after consultation among the company executives.

Occupied Territories and Newspaper Fluctuation
Comparing before the Incident (事變 - i.e., the start of the Second Sino-Japanese War) and today under the long war, what is the increase or decrease in your paper's readership and advertising volume? It is observed that advertising volume might have decreased significantly due to the gradual retreat of peace industries, and conversely, readership might have increased due to supplying war news. What is the reality? Are the newspaper company's finances straitened (궁핍乎) compared to before the Incident? Or optimistic (낙관乎)?
During the Sino-Japanese (日淸) and Russo-Japanese (日露) wars, mainland newspapers grew dramatically, seizing the opportunity of war. However, faced with the era of material control in this war, how do you foresee the expansion of newspaper company influence (社勢伸長) going forward?
Also, what is the status of your paper's development in the Imperial Army's occupied territories, namely North China (北支那) and the Central China (中支那) region? Furthermore, what has been the development of your reporting (귀보) in South and North Manchuria (南北滿洲) during the six years since the Manchurian Incident? Also, do you have any plans (복안이 有乎) to raise advertising rates as a countermeasure to the decrease in advertising volume? <32>

Mr. Im Chŏng-yŏp (Business Manager, Tonga Ilbo)
= Rather than 'before the Incident,' it would be more convenient to compare the situation before our newspaper's ban was lifted (해금되엿으니까) last June and the present. Regarding subscriber numbers, according to statistics compiled the other day, there's been exactly a 30% increase. As for advertising, while the general observation and our own concern was that it would decrease since peace industries like patent medicines (賣藥), cosmetics, and books are shrinking while heavy industries like munitions manufacturing are developing, in reality, that hasn't been the case. While we can't say it has increased, we are maintaining the volume from before the publication ban (정간). The reason is that major advertisers on the mainland have mostly suspended the advertising they used to place in the North and Central China regions, seeing that product consumption is difficult there due to the ongoing war, and are redirecting that volume back to Chosŏn and the Manchukuo region. Therefore, the impact on advertising revenue received in Chosŏn hasn't been that significant yet.
However, if this war becomes even more protracted and the peace industry sector shrinks completely, I expect advertising volume will drop sharply. As for countermeasures, this is too large an issue affecting all sectors of society, so we haven't thought about it yet.
In the Imperial Army's occupied territories, the fires of war have not yet died down, so the number of settled Koreans is small, meaning there's no increase in readership. We have established branch offices in places like Peking (北京) and Shanghai (上海) in preparation for the future, but in Manchukuo, although Koreans are gradually gathering in key locations like Sin'gyŏng (新京), Pongch'ŏn (奉天), Harbin (하얼빈), and Yŏn'gil (延吉), the vast majority are not overseas compatriots leading cultured lives with stable incomes. Therefore, the subscription network isn't growing as much as expected, but it's gradually improving compared to before the Incident. I don't know the numerical ratio yet.
Regarding advertising rates, we have unavoidably decided to implement an increase of about 30% starting from this coming October 1st. This is the minimum request to society necessary for managing the newspaper company.

Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo (President & Business Manager, Chosŏn Ilbo)
= Looking at the trends in sales figures over the approximately one year since the Incident began, since it's a war staking the nation's rise or fall, the public's attention naturally focuses on it. I've heard that radios (「라지오」) have greatly increased in Chosŏn, and movie theaters screening war news have also increased. It’s unthinkable that newspapers, which are even more indispensable (不可缺), wouldn't have increased. From the start of the Incident and through major turning points like the fall of Shanghai and Nanking, newspaper circulation has followed a path of increase, showing a considerable rise currently.
Also, looking at trends in advertising, at a time when leather factories are converting to munitions factories, and cosmetics or pharmaceutical factories are also shifting towards heavy industry, making business difficult and reducing sales channels for products, and furthermore, under the Incident where people don't buy goods as readily as before even if they have money, due to policies like material conservation <33> and waste control, we thought advertising volume would decrease. However, up to now, it's been much the same as before, and in some existing areas, it has even increased.
Going forward, I don't think we can be as optimistic as now with the protraction of the Incident, so we are considering countermeasures. In Tokyo and Osaka, they are all drastically raising advertising rates to compensate for the quantitative shortfall with qualitative income to manage their newspapers, and I believe Chosŏn will likely follow suit.
That is, while the extent of the price increase (値上) is still unknown, we will eventually raise the current advertising rates. We will also reduce advertising space (스페-스) as a countermeasure for page reduction. And for the sake of the readers, I believe the news articles should not be reduced below the current level.

Mr. Yi Sang-hyŏp (Vice President, Maeil Sinbo)
= The Maeil Sinbo was newly established this spring as a joint-stock company with 1 million [yen? implied] capital, and it has shown surprising development in all aspects compared to the old Maeil Sinbo era, so much so that a comparison between the start of the Incident and the present is hardly possible due to the great changes.
Regarding newspaper circulation, its reputation (세평) is already established, so I won't elaborate, but the increase is not just one or two times. Advertising volume has also increased significantly since the business department became independent, but I cannot recall the exact ratio offhand right now.
Therefore, discussing countermeasures for decreased advertising volume is not an issue from the outset. Since we already raised advertising rates last July, regardless of what other companies do, our company will maintain them going forward.

The Hankow Decisive Battle and the Dispatch Issue
Through the mediation (斡旋) of the military and the Cabinet Information Bureau, about 20 literary figures have been mobilized to the approaching Hankow (漢口) decisive battle zone. Major newspaper companies like Dai Mainichi (大每), Osaka Asahi (大朝), and Yomiuri (讀賣) have recently dispatched large groups of 50 or 60 correspondents to the Hankow front. Also, to Changkufeng (張鼓峰), the site of the Japanese-Soviet clash, numerous foreign news agencies including Reuters (로이텔) have deliberately sent special correspondents to conduct on-site investigation (實地調査), research, and comfort the Imperial troops (황군위문). What is your company's stance? Also, what measures has your company taken regarding the recent flood damage in Hamnam and Hambuk provinces?

Mr. Paek Kwan-su (President, Tonga Ilbo)
= Yomiuri sent 50-60 people, Dentsu (電通), Dai Mainichi, and Osaka Asahi each dispatched over 100 reporters to the battlefield, <34> engaging in communications, news, and film activities. While it's natural for a newspaper company to do this, based on our company's experience sending rear-area reporters to the North China front early in the Incident, if you're going to send people, you need to send several dozen, equip them with wireless communication devices, and have airplanes to transport the manuscripts – you need to do it on a large scale like this to obtain war news completely and swiftly. However, sending just a few people to such a vast battlefield yields meager material (材料取한대야), and it's impossible to publish the gathered material without falling behind Dentsu or Dai Mainichi.
Therefore, we have decided it's faster and more accurate to receive war news directly at the head office via "Telegraphic Communications" (「전보통신」).
The flood damage in Hamnam and Hambuk resulted in over 2,000 casualties, making it a disaster of no small scale. We have also sent comfort funds (위문금) and are collecting donations through the Chosŏn Social Work Association (조선사회사업협회).

Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo (President, Chosŏn Ilbo)
= Early in the Incident, we dispatched reporters to the North China front to accompany the troops (종군케), and immediately after the recent Changkufeng Incident, we dispatched the chief editor to the site (현지파견).
Although I myself have not yet visited North or Central China, we have organized industrial inspection teams (산업시찰단) several times, and prominent staff members, including the chief editor (주필), have frequently traveled to the region, focusing efforts on introducing the local culture and industry. We intend to continue doing so whenever opportunities arise.
Regarding the Hankow decisive battle, we currently have no plans to dispatch reporters to accompany the troops. If the need arises in the future, it goes without saying that we will do so.
At the time of the recent floods in Hamnam and Hambuk, we immediately dispatched our staff to the affected areas, reported on the situation (기사보도), consoled the disaster victims (災民慰弔), and upon seeing the reports in the paper, received donations of about 5,000 won and over 10,000 items from individuals across various regions. These were sent to the disaster areas through the Chosŏn Social Work Association.

Mr. Yi Sang-hyŏp (Vice President, Maeil Sinbo)
= We heard that major newspapers in Tokyo and Osaka are sending numerous special correspondents as the Hankow decisive battle approaches. However, since our newspaper has already dispatched correspondents to Shanghai, Nanking, and other locations, we don't feel the need to send more from the head office for now. If it becomes necessary in the future, we will decide after consulting with company executives.
Regarding flood relief, as a member newspaper of the Social Work Association, we plan to continue our efforts in the future.

Future Direction of Emphasis <35>

At one time, you called for "Literacy Promotion" (문자보급), and at another time, aimed for "Industrial Development" (산업개발) – meaning there has always been a main current of the newspaper company's spirit, a direction towards which the newspaper's activities were relatively focused. What points are you emphasizing now?
Also, papers like the Osaka Asahi have a "Science Prize" (「科學賞」) system providing pensions to scientists, and France has an art prize, serving as driving forces to encourage societal culture. Does your company have any plans (복안이 없읍니까) to establish a prize system for any sector in Chosŏn?

Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo (President, Chosŏn Ilbo)
= We are striving for the promotion (선양) of Local Culture (鄕士文化), which you probably already know from our paper.
In the realm of modern culture, literature, film, theater, and music are all important, so we are putting effort into those areas. If we could establish facilities to award prizes like a film prize or literature prize with pensions, either through our company's funding or by obtaining contributions from some benefactor (有志), I believe it would be excellent. However, we don't have any specific plans (아모 복안은 없음니다) yet.

Mr. Paek Kwan-su (President, Tonga Ilbo)
= To elevate the spirit (의기) of youth, the field of sports (스포-츠) is good. On the other hand, to cultivate sentiment (정서) in a broad sense, we hope for the emergence of good literature, theater, music, and film in this land, so we naturally intend to put effort into those areas as well. <36>

**Summary**

This 1938 survey article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interviews the presidents/executives of the three major Korean newspapers (Tonga Ilbo, Chosŏn Ilbo, Maeil Sinbo) about their operations under Japanese wartime conditions. Key topics include strategies for dealing with mandatory newsprint rationing (preferring reduced publication frequency over daily page cuts), the mixed impact of the war on circulation (increased due to war news demand) and advertising (some decline in 'peace industries' but compensated by redirection of ad budgets from China and potential rate hikes), reporting challenges and approaches regarding the Sino-Japanese war fronts (Hankou, Changkufeng) and occupied territories (China, Manchukuo), and future cultural contributions like promoting sports or arts, possibly through prize systems.

Historians would find this article valuable for its direct insights into the pressures and compromises faced by the Korean press during the wartime mobilization period under Japanese rule. It reveals the practical business challenges (paper shortages, advertising revenue fluctuations) intertwined with navigating political controls (Government-General directives, "national policy," reporting restrictions). The executives' careful balancing act—expressing compliance while seeking ways to maintain their publications and readership—and their discussion of expanding into Japanese-occupied territories offer a glimpse into the complex realities of colonial media operation on the eve of intensified wartime controls that would eventually lead to the forced closure of Korean-language newspapers.

戰時下의 新聞社長의 態度, 思想統制·物資統制의 長期戰 下인 오늘날에 잇서 三大 新聞社의 今後 進路는 如何한가

同 營業局長 林正燁씨
朝鮮日報社長 兼 營業局長 方應謨씨
每日新報副社長 李相協씨
(社長 崔麟氏가 會議 出席 不在하기에 副社長을 찻다)
本社 金東煥 ＜30＞

用紙節約의 對策

한때는 「문자보급」을 부르지젓고 또 엇든 때는 「산업개발」을 목표로 했고 - 즉 신문사의 정신적 주류가 흐르는 방향이 늘 있어서 그 코-스에 향하야 비교적 신문사의 작용이 만햇는데 현재는 엇든 점에 주장 힘쓰심니까.
또 大阪朝日 같은데는 「科學賞」이 있어 과학자에게 연금주는 제도가 있고 불란서에는 예술상이 있어 그 사회 문화를 격려하는 추진력을 삼고 있는데 귀사에서는 조선의 었든 부문에 대하야 상금제도를 실시할 복안이 없읍니까.

方應謨씨(朝鮮日報社長)
= 鄕士文化의 선양을 위하야 노력하고 있는데 그는 이미 지상으로 보섯슬 줄 알며
현대문화부문에 있어 문예, 영화, 연극, 음악이 모다 중요하기 때문에 그 방면에 노력하고 있음니다. 이 여러 문화부문에 가령 우리 社의 출자도 조코 또는 다른 어느 有志의 출자를 어더서라도 영화상, 문학상하고 연금제의 상금을 주는 시설을 할 수 있다면 대단히 좋을 줄 압니다. 그러나 아직 아모 복안은 없음니다.
