**Magazine Title:** 三千里 제10권 제10호
**Publication Date:** 1938년 10월 01일
**Article Title:** 女流作家 議會 (Female Writers' Symposium)
**Author:** 毛允淑 (Mo Yunsuk), 盧天命 (No Ch'ŏnmyŏng), 李善熙 (Yi Sŏnhŭi), 崔貞熙 (Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi), 金東煥 (Kim Tonghwan - Moderator)
**Article Type:** 대담·좌담 (Discussion/Roundtable)

**1. Have you ever gathered material from slums, courtrooms, or the lives of kisaeng?**

Kim Tonghwan - If I hadn't carried a gun and gone directly into battle myself, no matter how much of a genius I might be, a work depicting the battlefield as clearly as Remarque's "All Quiet on the Western Front" would likely not have emerged. Similarly, without experiencing the life of a penal colony prisoner in Siberia, Dostoevsky-esque works filled with suffocating shadows like "Demons," "Crime and Punishment," "The Idiot," and "The House of the Dead" would not have come about.
In this sense, I believe that a great writer cannot emerge without the grand record of their own life experiences. Even if one cannot have direct personal experience, I think one must at least understand, see, and hear about such deep and broad lives – that is, at least possess knowledge about them. What draws interest now is from which aspects of Chosŏn's reality the female writers gathered here intend to take material for their works, and from what angle they view these matters. I will ask a few specific questions. Have you ever visited a slum? That village outside Seoul's Sigumun gate where the "living dead" gather? Its entrance? Inside its kitchens?... Have you asked about and investigated the food they eat, their clothes, their ideals in life? Since the slum is an extension of our lives, and also one aspect of it, it's not out of curiosity, but truly in the sense of dissecting and depicting our own lives, that it becomes a most important facet.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - Well, back when I attended Ewha Women's Specialized School's liberal arts department, the English practicum building was right on the cliff outside Sŏdaemun gate. So, on my way back and forth carrying my books morning and evening, I saw, even in broad daylight, the gloomy atmosphere – the dust, the smell of rotting meat (the Kyŏngsŏng Municipal dump was there at the time), and the piercing smell of children's urine that seemed to stifle breathing. Amidst this, I saw impoverished people, their faces blue from hunger and cold, stirring like worms. Seeing this, I thought, "Reality is truly cruel," and resolved that if I were to write in the future, I would write about the appeals of people like those, their grievances, and compose their songs. At that time, their words and breaths spun vividly in my head...

Kim Tonghwan - Spun vividly, you say. So, after graduating, how much of that have you actually treated in your works since then? <199> Are there any pieces in your recent poetry collection, "Coral Forest (柵瑚林)"? Just as there's the scene outside P'yŏngyang's Ch'ilsŏngmun gate in Mr. Kim Tongin's "Potato," is there something similar in your collection?

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - No. I thought about writing it, but I haven't written a single piece yet. It's not just the slums; I'm also interested in the lives of female workers in places like rice mills or textile factories. Let's not just look at the beautiful side; let us women deeply portray the lives of women, their hardships in reality...

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Of course, since we are women, our attention is particularly drawn to the lives of fellow women in various professions. Not only the lives of poor mothers and daughters in slums, but also the street peddlers, the waitresses in teahouses (喫茶店), the aging female missionaries, the dispirited sewing machine saleswomen, female teachers, even the notions peddler grandmothers, the street prostitutes – how could our eyes not constantly fall upon their fates and the state of their lives? However, when a writer gathers material for a work, no matter how ugly it may be, one only takes it up when one discovers aesthetic sensibility within it. If "aesthetic sensibility" is inappropriate, "inspiration" might be better.
But somehow, within the lowest strata of Chosŏn life, I cannot find the aesthetic sensibility that could become a novel, a play, a poem, or a song. In foreign countries, no matter how ugly, evil, or poor the life is, even if fighting, jealousy, murder, and robbery are repeated, somehow I feel there is "something" that strongly appeals to the writer's taste. This is my honest feeling.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Well, I don't think that's necessarily true. Western writers also initially selected insignificant material from these squalid lowest strata of life, organized it, depicted it, infused it with dreams and romance, and let it ferment, didn't they? Perhaps that's how the works of art we romantically admire today came into being?
If we haven't been able to grasp Chosŏn-specific, attractive subject matter from these streets where we live, isn't that due to our lack of skill? When Steinbeck (스탄박) came to Seoul some years ago, after first viewing the magnificent beams of Kyŏngbok Palace, he apparently turned his steps again, went to some tavern outside Tongdaemun gate, had a glass of makkeolli, and deeply appreciated its atmosphere, calling it "wonderful," "beautiful." An artist's focus is usually different like this. Can't we see how he picked a prostitute from "Morocco" (모록코) (Marlene Dietrich's (데- 트릿지) role) <200> and created a masterpiece by pouring purity and dreams into it? Gorky's (골키-) "The Lower Depths (ドン底)" too, wasn't it ultimately conceived by selecting villains who commit adultery, robbery, and murder from a place like the slum outside Sigumun gate, and simply infusing it with humanity and love? Therefore, the key lies in our attitude and skill. Didn't To Hyang (稻香) produce works like "Diary of a Tram Conductor" from the life of a tram conductor who holds the handle from morning till night...?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Even so, I somehow feel differently. Since Chosŏn's cultural level is different from the West anyway, even if Western writers pick material from the same slums, brothels, or tavern streets, the aspects of their lives are much broader, deeper, and more beautiful, allowing them to handle it freely. But Chosŏn is too simple, lacks depth... It's always the same pattern, just so-so.

Mo Yunsuk - I haven't been to a slum myself, but when I was teaching in Kando (間島), I often saw the lives of migrants moving north to find a livelihood in the barbarian lands (胡地), tying gourds to their backs and seating grandmothers on mules. Sitting by the Haeran River (海蘭江), the river in front of Yongjŏng (龍井), in the evening twilight, whenever I saw those groups of movers – the prematurely aged young men with earthy complexions and faces full of wrinkles, leading the old and young – moving north, various thoughts occurred to me. And a sudden desire arose to truly see and investigate their actual lives, not just conceptually. While worn down and distorted by misery, within that group, I could see something like the life force of Indian tribes seeking Texas, undulating and surging – a will to live. I wanted to connect with that life force, to share their agony. If only I had the talent, a masterpiece that could truly move the generation might have come from this...

Kim Tonghwan - In the end, all four of you are confronting reality – especially the life conditions of the lower classes where all evil and ignorance surge – and showing an attitude of wanting to gaze upon it. I understand well. That attitude is trustworthy. Next, have you ever investigated the courthouse, the courtroom, and the brothels, which could be called abysses where all the misery and sin of this world gather? Just as Mr. Yŏm Sangsŏp's (廉相涉) work "Prosecutor's Office Waiting Room" was made from material he obtained while frequenting the court as a newspaper reporter...

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - When I was a reporter for the Chungang Ilbo, I deliberately went to see the trial of Yi Sut'ak (李洙倬). At that time, listening to the criminal facts and the defendant's testimony, I became extremely agitated. Things like the concubine system (蓄妾制度), and the psychological state of the defendants leading up to that point <201> – rather than wanting to write this in a poem or novel, I still feel the urge to write about it, perhaps borrowing the form of some treatise.

Mo Yunsuk - I have never deliberately rummaged through court records or gone out to see criminals wearing handcuffs entering and exiting just to gather material. However, like the experience of the university student Raskolnikov in "Crime and Punishment," there are times when I feel an impulse – what if my body committed a grave crime, received a life sentence or a death sentence, and I agonized intensely over the attachment to life? To experience being chased day and night by police officers (捕吏) or hiding in streets unseen by daylight – hours of fear and trembling, a state of mind filled with agony and despair. It feels like a great work could be written from such an experience. I originally dislike lukewarm lives. I dislike such personalities too. Either become a complete aristocrat, or else wear rags like Hugo's hunchback of Notre Dame and live the life of a beggar. As a woman too, either become a prima donna or a Beatrice, or else just become a prostitute – anyway, I truly dislike the intermediate, petit-bourgeois life that is neither hot nor cold. I dislike that state of mind too.
Perhaps for that reason, I want to experience, just once, the state of mind of becoming a major felon, agonizing and agonizing until fainting (悶絶). Earnestly.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - But unless you yourself commit a crime, cry out, curse and condemn the world like Katiuscia, and take on a sense of nihilism, just sitting as a third party trying to experience this or that psychology, like a photographer holding a camera trying to take pictures here and there, true agony won't arise – it's merely what writers call "contemplation," a form of detached enjoyment...

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - That's right. Is there time for such leisure? Suppose a novelist goes to court and sees a female prisoner crying and returning after being sentenced, "You are sentenced to 10 years in prison." One couldn't have experienced the prisoner's state of mind exactly as it was, nor would it happen. One might feel a thrill, or derive pleasure from another's crime, perhaps...
Speaking of other things, I myself went through a hard time in Chŏnju (全州) without having done anything, and you wouldn't know that psychology of darkness (無明), that despair, unless you become a prisoner yourself.
I heard many miserable stories from the female inmates I shared a room with back then. There were various types of prisoners: <202> one who married young to a child-husband and committed the crime of poisoning her original husband due to sexual dissatisfaction, a female student who killed her infant, thieves (窈盜), arsonists. And adultery cases were the most numerous, it seems. They ended up in this miserable plight ultimately due to the struggle for sex and the struggle for food. While I was there, I tried very hard to solve the proposition "What made her do that? (何が彼女をそうさせたか?)" but ended up leaving without being able to solve it...
Anyway, it seems female writers too should frequently visit places like this, the very bottom of life, think about "Crime and Punishment," and constantly bring it up in their works. Let's not always stay in the greenhouse, living only in dreams and romance. It seems we need to broaden our perspective somewhat, and greatly widen the scope of our works.

Kim Tonghwan - The slums and courtrooms are one thing, but have you ever peeked into the society of kisaeng (기녀)? Have you ever visited a kisaeng, seeking to include in your work the kind of kisaeng house atmosphere and life aspects received from Mr. Yi T'aejun's (李泰俊) "Hwang Chini (黃眞伊)" or Pak Yŏngch'ae in Ch'unwŏn's (春園) "The Heartless (無情)"?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - In the old days, Sŏl T'o (薛濤), Hwang Chini (黃眞伊), and others composed sijo, painted the Four Gracious Plants (사군자), strummed the kayagŭm, and plucked the kŏmun'go, so a subtle fragrance flowed from their faces and hearts. But these days, it seems there are kisaeng, but no truly renowned ones (名妓). There might be alluring bodies, but there seems to be truly no spirit that understands refinement (風趣). Therefore, they are not highly valued, and I don't feel like visiting them...

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - And they seem somehow like products of the fin-de-siècle; the desire to sit face-to-face with those who have chivalry (俠氣), know art, and discuss literature with them doesn't arise in the first place. Somehow, those people seem temperamental, decadent (デカダン), and the new culture they claim to know is just superficial (生カチリ), seeming to serve merely as playthings (弄玩物) for men. Do they really have a spiritual life? Living devoted to religion or art – since their very lives don't inspire fondness, I don't feel like trying to understand them either.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - But the life of a prostitute seems like it would be profound. Didn't they become what they are not because they wanted to, but due to various circumstances of reality, knowing it's a painful and sorrowful abyss, yet becoming sellers of themselves (賣女) in that market of ruin, throwing their bodies into the arms of profligates? Therefore, it seems there must be some deep and broad aspect within their hearts. Kisaeng too, although there might be differences in degree, surely there must be pure-hearted individuals among them? Can there not be those who possess tears and sighs unlike others?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Even if we assume there are, they would be microscopic, extremely few existences. And firstly, we can't even visit a kisaeng house, so we have no choice but to say we don't know them well... If we were men, <203> we could seek out the thresholds of Wŏrhyang or Ch'unsim.

Mo Yunsuk - I want to dissect the brain of a demonic prostitute, and with humanitarian sentiment, think it through until the conclusion reaches a critique of the social system. Is it because their faces and attitudes are beautiful that they tempt men, or is it because they were born with such demonic natures that they fit into that society of profligates?... I also have much I'd like to say to a kisaeng like the Hwang Chini written about by Mr. Yi T'aejun (李泰俊).

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Anyway, in this frivolous society today, the equation seems to be beautiful woman equals famous kisaeng, so both the women and the men who elevate them seem equally superficial. A certain kisaeng near my house was said to be a famous kisaeng of the capital in the past, but now that she's old, no one seeks her out. Isn't it surprising not only the materialism (即物主義) of men but also the lack of self-awareness among kisaeng?

Kim Tonghwan - Fundamentally, do you even want to have contact with kisaeng society?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - I do want to know about it. And as a writer's desire, I am full of the thought of perhaps finding one or two women like the Hwang Chini of this age within it... But still...

**2. What kind of men possess the conditions to be the heroes (히로인) of the century?**

Kim Tonghwan - Just as when Ch'unwŏn (春園) picks up his pen, he invariably seeks the protagonist of his work from the ideal type of figure for that century, resulting in idealists and humanitarians appearing as protagonists; and just as heroic types like Romain Rolland's "Jean-Christophe (짠, 그리스톱)," Hugo's "Jean Valjean (잔팔찬)," Dostoevsky's "Karamazov (가라마좁)," Artsybashev's "Sanin (싸- 닝)," Turgenev's Rudin (루진) emerge; in this manner, if you were to choose the heroes of the era as protagonists for your works, what kind of men would you grasp? Please try to paint a picture of such men. To rephrase this, what kind of men attract your interest? Of course, as characters to be included in your works.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - I see it this way. At least as perceived through our female eyes, the various men who frequent the teahouses (喫茶店) on the streets cannot be the heroes of the era, I think. They seem lacking in will, empty inside, frivolous. To seek socially leading youth among them seems like utter fantasy. Progressive young men don't seem to be among them. Therefore, <204> we cannot find the type of man we idealize among the men who gather in teahouses.

Kim Tonghwan - Could that be true? But among the men found in street teahouses, aren't there the most conscientious and knowledgeable groups? In this society, if there are intellectual groups discussing the art and literature of this land, aren't they the crowds gathering in teahouses? Aren't those who feel the agony of this era most acutely also gathering there? Although they may have frail physiques and faces worn down by poverty, can't we see the most noble and pure individuals frequenting those places?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Even so, although those people might seem to represent the entire generation, their agony isn't profound. And even if they are the group discussing what spiritual life is, with religion and art as topics, isn't what they know not very deep? Anyway, it should be called a place where young men weak in willpower gather. At least, they are not action-oriented – wouldn't the male crowds frequenting not only teahouses but also bars, cafes, and restaurants all be like that?
We are not unaware of spirited young men who, frustrated by lack of opportunity (略軻不遇), deliberately immerse themselves in city taverns (市井 酒肆). But judging by the current situation, such meaningful men don't seem to be among them.

Kim Tonghwan - Then where are they?

Mo Yunsuk - (Omitted section marker) I agree. Not just as heroes for works, but realistically, it seems quite difficult to find in this era such strong men who can fully take charge of the life of an age. However, the kind of man I'm talking about doesn't refer to young men with money or knowledge, but rather a person who, even if their real life is extremely miserable, harbors great hope for the future, a man of fortitude (雄健), a man with spirit (氣槪), a man who intends to live for tomorrow rather than today. Such a person is fervently admired.

Kim Tonghwan - Could you give a more concrete example?

Mo Yunsuk - Like the Hwarang (花郎) appearing in the history of Silla (新羅). For the world to become better, they discard their lives like blades of grass; if it's a righteous cause, they forget to eat and sleep, wandering about; they understand refinement (風流); they are imposing figures (軒軒 丈夫).

Kim Tonghwan - They shoot bows, ride horses-.

Mo Yunsuk - Of course. They recite poems, sing songs-, and they are warriors (武士)-.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Here come the dreams again. (All laugh)

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - In short, not the frivolous talented types (輕薄 才子層), but those with depth in character, chivalry (俠氣), who sacrifice their lives for a great cause-. <205> I don't know on which street, on which corner such people might be, but the ones we women admire would be like that. It's fine even if that person is a "nihilist." Though disillusioned with today, they will surely tackle the construction of tomorrow with fervor-.

**3. Attitudes towards Folk Songs, Sijo, and Historical Novels**

Kim Tonghwan - Looking at our literary scene, many good works have emerged from the field that could be called historical novels, such as Hong Myŏnghŭi's (洪命憙) "The Tale of Im Kkŏkjŏng (林巨正傳)," Kim Tongin's (金東仁) "Those Young People (젊은 그들)," Pak Wŏlt'an's (朴月灘) "Blood on the Golden Robe (金衫의 피)," Ch'unwŏn's (春園) "The Death of Ich'adon (異次頓의 死)," "A Sad History of King Tanjong (端宗哀史)," "The Hemp-Clad Prince (麻衣太子)," etc. As today's reality gradually becomes more difficult, wouldn't it be right for writers to rather take their pens and delve into the veins of history?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - That is unreasonable. I see it as unreasonable for three reasons. Namely:
First, with historical literature, it would be difficult to construct events as complex and multifaceted as those of the modern era.
Second, modern writers generally have poor knowledge of history.
Third, if one keeps going back along the lines of history, there's a danger of easily getting caught in old moral codes and ending up merely depicting loyal subjects, filial sons, and virtuous women.
Therefore, although there might be room to avoid certain constraints and use imagination, historical novels, which risk falling into the category of anecdotal tales (野談類), should be left to only a few writers. One cannot generally tell current writers to follow that path.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - That's right. Those qualified to write historical novels would need to know history first, so it's probably a task for only a few like Kim Tongin (金東仁), Hong Myŏnghŭi (洪命憙), Yi Kwangsu (李光洙). However, we too feel such ambition. Seeing "The Hemp-Clad Prince (麻衣太子)" or "The Death of Ich'adon (異次頓의 死)," one becomes enchanted by the fact that such beautiful and good material exists in the society where our ancestors lived.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - One truly becomes enchanted by what others have written. One gets to know the old atmosphere too-.

Mo Yunsuk - The beauty of vibrant life might rather be found in digging into history, precisely because our real lives are too impoverished.

Kim Tonghwan - What about folk songs (민요) or sijo (시조)? Especially the issue of imitating the sijo form?

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - At first, I too had quite a bit of interest, tried to appreciate old sijo, and also tried to imitate them by composing in the 7-5-7-5 style <206> of the three-chapter form (초중종 3장형). However, the ideals and emotions contained in old sijo are mostly about appreciating nature (음풍영월), the tempo is slow, in the style of "leisurely gazing at the southern mountain" (悠然見南山). So I realized they couldn't compare to modern poetry, which is scientific, realistic, and capable of containing long breaths. The same goes for folk songs like "Over there on Kalbi Peak (갈비봉)..." Somehow, they seem to contain emotions and tones detached from real life.
Whether it's "White Gull (白鷗), don't leap so high..." or "The skylark is singing (노고지리 우지진다)..." they somehow don't feel like songs that came from the soil. Moreover, the vessel of the sijo is small, making it impossible to contain poetic thoughts freely. Though small, it's neat and flawless, making it pretty, but it simply cannot compare to modern poetry (新詩), where one can control the rhythm – slowing down, speeding up – at will. It's like how a Seoul nobleman's dining table (飯床), spread with 7 or 8 side dishes (첩드리), looks superficially gorgeous with many items neatly arranged, but when you actually pick up chopsticks, there isn't much to eat. Whereas a rural farmhouse table, though it might have coarse grain rice, soybean paste soup (된장꾹), and randomly placed side dishes like radish, is somehow more savory and tastier. Comparing sijo and modern poetry, there seems to be such a difference.

Mo Yunsuk - However, just by constantly reading, listening to, and memorizing old sijo, one can find many good vocabulary words, and also absorb the unrestrained medieval-style emotions – that old-fashioned, classical spirit – that ancient people possessed. But the poetic form (詩型) is inadequate, so one cannot freely contain the heart's rhythm; that's all there is to say.

**4. Who are the established active writers considered "stalwarts"?**

Kim Tonghwan - Comparing the works of the established (중견) writers active in the literary scene now with the works from about 10 years ago, when the literature of this land was being passionately pioneered, built, and developed, which side is better? In other words, has Chosŏn literature progressed compared to 10 years ago?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - It's regrettable, and somewhat infuriating, but it seems worse than before. Much worse. Even reading the works from that time now, they are truly good. I believe the works of today's established writers lack the depth of those from that time.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Perhaps because I read them when I was young, but Ch'unwŏn's early works like "The Pioneer (開拓者)" and "The Heartless (無情)," Yŏm Sangsŏp's "The Green Frog in the Specimen Room (標本室의 靑개고리)," "Sunflower (해바라기)," "Before the Hurrahs (만세 前)," P'unghŏ's (馮虛) "The Face of Chosŏn (朝鮮의 얼굴)," "The Dawning Mist (지새는 안개)" <207> To Hyang (稻香), Sŏhae (曙海) – they were all good.

Mo Yunsuk - Better, of course. Besides those, Kim Tongin's (金東仁) "Flogging (笞刑)," "Potato (감자)," "Life (목숨)" were all good too. Especially something like To Hyang's "Phantasmagoria (幻戱)" was truly good. Poetry was the same. Passion flowed from the writers of that time. Passionate, suffocatingly captured Chosŏn sentiment – it flowed abundantly in every poem.
Also Sowŏl (素月) and others, especially Sowŏl's "Golden Grass (金잔듸)" or "Waiting (기다림)," or Nojak's (露雀) folk songs – it seems unlikely such naive and fresh works will appear again. Even reading them now, they are so good they are unforgettable. The artistic fragrance is truly high and deep, making one reluctant to put them down.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - That's right. Of course, there are good works in modern literature too, but looking overall, the writers and works of the Ch'angjo (創造), Paekcho (白潮), P'yeho (廢墟) eras were better. Especially being in the poetry scene, this is even more true. Looking at the poems written by contemporary poets, only the technique has developed, crafted meticulously without leaks, but they lack a lively taste. One cannot find poems with big breaths and pulsating life like the poems of the past. In those poems, there was poetry behind the poetry. Though seemingly crude, there was a breathing life force (氣脈) flowing within them. Works like Mr. Chu Yohan's (요한) "Playing with Fire (불노리)" or Mr. Yi Sanghwa's (李相和) "Madonna (마돈나)," Mr. Han Yongun's (韓龍雲) various pieces, Mr. Im Hwa's (林和) "Older Brother and the Brazier (옵바와 화로)." Compared to those, today's poets seem self-satisfied if they themselves like it, becoming intoxicated with their own technique and getting excited. They seem self-righteous and small-scale (小形). I dislike the artificiality (作爲) that seems carved and twisted. Therefore, overall, the spirit (氣魄) of 10 years ago cannot be found in today's poets. Though one cannot ignore the spirit of the times either-

Kim Tonghwan - That's an interesting observation. Now, please mention the contemporary established writers whom you acknowledge.

Mo Yunsuk - In the poetry scene, there are Chŏng Chiyong (鄭芝溶), Paek Sŏk (白石), Kim Tongmyŏng (金東鳴), etc. In novels, although he passed away, Mr. Kim Yujŏng (金裕貞), and then Yi T'aejun (李泰俊), Pak T'aewŏn (朴泰遠), Yu Chino (兪鎭午) 씨, etc.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Most probably see it the same way. Me too. Yi T'aejun (李泰俊), Pak T'aewŏn (朴泰遠), Yi Hyosŏk (李孝石), Yi Muyŏng (李無影), Yu Chino (兪鎭午), Ch'ae Mansik (蔡萬植), Ŏm Hŭngsŏp (嚴興燮) - these gentlemen.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Me too, same. Besides them, Mr. Han Sŏrya (韓雪野) is also an established writer.

Kim Tonghwan - Ms. Ch'ŏnmyŏng?

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - Well... I'll just stick to saying it's not as good as the era of Yŏm Sangsŏp (廉相涉), Kim Tongin (金東仁), Hyŏn Chin'gŏn (玄馮虛).

Kim Tonghwan - Then, among Ch'unwŏn's various works, which ones would you choose as representative works? <208> If possible, one contemporary piece and one historical piece-.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - Others say "The Pioneer (開拓者)" is good, but even reading it again after growing up, I can't find particularly good passages in that work.
Of course, being the work of a master, it doesn't tire the reader at all, the descriptions are fresh, and the prose is brilliant without effort, needless to say more-.

Mo Yunsuk - The events are also very interesting. One bows one's head to the consistent sublime spirit-.

Kim Tonghwan - Please point out individual works. The representative ones-.

Mo Yunsuk - For historical works, "A Sad History of King Tanjong (端宗哀史)" is likely the peak. For contemporary works, it's "Earth (흙)."

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - For historical works, I'd like to pick "A Sad History of King Tanjong (端宗哀史)," and for contemporary works, it's definitely "The Heartless (무정)."

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - I haven't read them all so I don't know, but "Affection (有情)" was good, and for historical works, it would naturally be "A Sad History of King Tanjong (端宗哀史)."

Yi Sŏnhŭi - I don't see it that way. For historical works, it's "The Hemp-Clad Prince (麻衣太子)"; for contemporary works, "His Autobiography (그의 自叙傳)" is good.

Kim Tonghwan - Then, please tell us which works by which authors, among those recently read from our literary scene, can be considered masterpieces (*작 - likely 秀作, sujak, meaning excellent work).

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Yi Sang's (李箱) "Wings (날개)" (Chogwang)
Kim Yujŏng's (金裕禎) "Spring, Spring (봄, 봄)"
Yi T'aejun's (李泰俊) Hwang Chini

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Kim Yujŏng's (金裕禎) various works and Yi Sang's (李箱) "Wings (날내 - likely typo for 날개)," Kim Tongni's (金東里) "Rock (바위)" and "Skull (촉루)," the Chosŏn Ilbo prize-winning work "Reeves' Turtle (남생이)"

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - I found a rustic beauty like the smell of mountain greens in Paek Sŏk's (白石) poetry collection, which was nice, and I choose Pak T'aewŏn's (朴泰遠) "Scenes by the Stream (川邊風景)" for its good subject matter gathering (취재).

Mo Yunsuk - Yi Muyŏng's (李無影) "The Book of Burnt Passion (불살나 버린 情熱의 書)"
Kim Yujŏng's (金裕禎) "Spring, Spring (봄, 봄)"
That's about it.

**5. Please critique the critics from a writer's perspective?**

Kim Tonghwan - As you sit in the position of novelists or poets, please look at the figures in the current criticism scene (評壇) and critique them boldly and frankly.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - The authority in the criticism scene is still Mr. Pak Yŏnghŭi (朴英熙).

Kim Tonghwan - Have there been instances where you were impressed by criticism given about your own work? <209>

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Many times. Listening to his words and recalling the work I wrote, there are many passages where I feel heartfelt reflection.
Even apart from such personal incidents, he indeed possesses deep scholarship and fair appreciation ability. I want to respect him as a senior and a master.

Kim Tonghwan - Is that true both then and now?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - His authority might have fallen a bit compared to before. In the past, during the heyday of KAPF (카프), his pen was sharp and much more progressive, but after things changed, his pen's edge dulled somewhat. And his perspective on works changed, it seems. Previously, he prioritized social justice, but now, perhaps due to a change of heart, he seems to lean towards art for art's sake. Anyway, one way or another, his arguments are accurate and have aspects that command respect from writers. Also, his contributions to our criticism scene are arguably number one.

Kim Tonghwan - What about others?

Yi Sŏnhŭi - Let me talk about Mr. Paek Ch'ŏl (白鐵). Shall we call him a "Dense Forest Critic (成林 비평가)"? Reading his writing, it's unclear what he intends to say, with too many diverging paths, as if leading the reader into a dense forest (森林) and then being unable to find the way out. His principles and arguments are also inconsistent, lacking authority. Although his arguments are complex and multifaceted, and his claims change constantly, I only respect his passion for scholarship. And Mr. Kim Munjip (金文輯) – strictly speaking, this person mixes much personal feeling (私感) into his criticism, and his arguments contain much extraneous talk (客說), so he cannot be considered an orthodox critic.

Mo Yunsuk - As for me personally receiving criticism, when I published "Len's Elegy (렌의 哀歌)," Mr. Yu Chino (兪鎭午) took up the pen for a newspaper review. He said he couldn't understand the spirit that denied the body regarding love, but his other points were all worth listening to. Also, when I published the poetry collection "Shining Region (빛나는 地域)," Mr. Kim Kirim (金起林) wrote about it. He understood it well, but told me I elevated individuality too much, that it was poetry belonging to the Muse era, and advised me to change that attitude as much as possible. However, since that's a fundamental issue of artistic view, I didn't intend to change it. In that respect, even if my work receives poor or neglectful treatment, it can't be helped.

Kim Tonghwan - What about generally?

Mo Yunsuk - Ultimately, if a critic's writing provides enlightenment, they come to be regarded as an authoritative critic. And the second condition is that they fully understand the writer's feelings...

Kim Tonghwan - So, please praise <210> or critique specific critics, saying "so-and-so critic is like this..."

Yi Sŏnhŭi - In terms of critical writing (評文), Mr. Yi Wŏnjo (李源朝) is probably the clearest.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - When I published the collection of reviews for "Coral Forest (珊瑚林)," Mr. Yi Wŏnjo (李源朝) and Mr. Ch'oe Chaesŏ (崔載瑞) lent their pens, and they both understood my state of mind well. I feel trust in both their writings. Mr. Ch'oe Chaesŏ is conscientious, and Mr. Yi Wŏnjo is sharp.

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Among critics, the guidance of Messrs. Pak Yŏnghŭi (朴英熙), Im Hwa (林和), Yi Wŏnjo (李源朝) seems good, but I think advice given by a writer in a private setting is more effective than criticism written unkindly for publication.
And regarding Mr. Kim Munjip (金文輯), when it comes to pointing out flaws in a work, he is truly genius-like and intuitive, like a master physician using forceps to pick out flaws. He's almost demonic (귀신) at seeing works. However, his criticism is something to be heard in a private setting; his published critiques lean too much towards personal feelings, risking damage to the whole piece.

No Ch'ŏnmyŏng - That's absolutely true. He shows signs of having a talented disposition, but inserting personal feelings is just terrible. He is extremely sharp ("スルトイ" - surudoi), pinpointing weaknesses very well. But as Ms. Chŏnghŭi said, he includes personal feelings. His attitude towards Mr. Chang Hyŏkchu (張赫宙), or towards Mr. Yu Chino's (兪鎭午) works! That's what I dislike.

Yi Sŏnhŭi - That's right. He is sharp, but his critical writings have many flaws, so he probably can't be considered a serious critic anyway.

Mo Yunsuk - What you all see is almost the same as what I see. Anyway, Mr. Kim Munjip (金文輯) is an enigmatic figure; he seems like someone who will eventually cause some kind of incident. His unique ability to keenly pick out good and bad passages in a work, that intuitive aspect, is all good. But if you look closely at his critical essays, it seems like he lays everything out but then hesitates, unable to quite finish tidying up.

Kim Tonghwan - Thank you for speaking for such a long time. -(End)- <211>

**Summary**

Four prominent female writers of colonial Korea – Mo Yunsuk, No Ch'ŏnmyŏng, Yi Sŏnhŭi, and Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi – discuss literary topics moderated by Kim Tonghwan. They explore potential sources for literary material like slums, courts, and kisaeng life, debating the possibility of finding authentic inspiration and aesthetic value there compared to Western literature. They share their ideal "hero" for the era, generally desiring strong, principled men of action, often contrasting them with the perceived superficiality of contemporary intellectuals frequenting teahouses. The writers also assess the value of historical novels and traditional forms like sijo, finding limitations compared to modern genres. They evaluate contemporary literature, generally feeling it lacks the passion and depth of the works from the 1920s. Finally, they offer candid critiques of major literary critics of the day, discussing their strengths, weaknesses, biases, and influence.

For historians, this discussion is valuable as it provides direct insight into the thoughts and concerns of elite female intellectuals in late 1930s Korea. It reveals their engagement with social realities like poverty and the justice system, their complex views on tradition versus modernity in literature, their perspectives on gender roles and ideal masculinity, and their critical assessment of the colonial literary scene, including prominent male writers and critics. Their frankness about the limitations they felt in finding "Chosŏn-like" material and their comparisons to Western literature highlight the cultural and artistic anxieties of the period under Japanese rule.

女流作家 議會
毛允淑, 盧天命, 李善熙, 崔貞熙, 金東煥

1. 貧民窟, 法廷, 妓女 生活에서 取材하여 본 적이 잇는가
金東煥 - 제가 총메고 직접 전쟁에 나가보지 안었으면 제 아모리 천재라도 「루마루케」의 「西部戰線 異狀없다」같은 刻明한 전장 묘사를 한 작품이 생기지 않었겠고, 마찬가지로, 西伯利亞의 徒刑囚 생활을 격지 안코서는 「惡靈」「罪와 罰」「白痴」「死人의 家」와 같은 숨막힐 듯한 음영을 담은 토스토엡스키적 작품이 나오지 않었을 것이겟지요.
이러한 점에서 위대한 작가란, 거창한 제 자신의 생활의 기록이 없이는 되앀¬지지 안는 것이라고 생각되여저요. 비록 자신의 직접 체험까지는 못 가더래도 적어도 깁고 폭이 넓분 그런 생활을 이해하고, 보고 듯고 - 즉 그에 대한 지식만이라도 갖어야 얀³을 줄 생각하여요. 이제 흥미를 끄으는 것은, 여기 모힌 여류 작가 여러분들이 조선 현실의 었던 면에서 그 작품의 재료를 취하려 들며, 또 었던 각도에서 그 사물을 바라보고 있는지 그런 점인데, 구체적으로 내가 몇 가지를 무러보리다. 여러분은 빈민굴을 찾어가 본 적이 있습니까. 서울 屍口門 밖에 있는 「산송장」들이 모여 사는 그 촌락을? 그 문어구를? 그 부엌 속을?... 그래서 그네가 먹는 음식물을, 의복을, 그 생활의 이상을 무러보고 캐여보고 한 적이 있었든가요? 빈민굴은 우리 생활의 연장이요, 또 일면이니까, 호기심으로서가 아니요, 진실로 우리들 자신의 생활을 해부, 묘사한다는 의미에서 가장 중요한 일면이 되는 것이니까요.
盧天命 - 저는요, 예전 梨專 문과 단닐적에, 영어 실습소가 바루 서대문박 城낭 낭떠러지 우에 잇었기 까닭에 책보 끼고 조석으로 그 앞을 지나가는 길에 백주에도 그 어둑침침한- 그 먼지와 고기 썩는 냄새와 (부근에 京城府의 塵埃 버리는 데가 그 때는 거기 있었으니까) 코를 찔으는 어린애 오줌 냄새가 호흡을 막는 듯한 속에서 주우림과 추위에 얼굴이 파래진 빈민들이 굼베지 모양으로 움실거리고 사는 양을 보고, 현실이란 참으로 참혹하고나 하는 생각이 들어서 나도 장래에 작품을 쓰는 날이 있다면 바로 저런 사람들의 하소연도 써보고, 저네들의 넉두리도, 저네들의 노래도 지어 보려고 생각했더니요, 그 때에는 머리 속에 그네들의 말과 호흡이 팽팽 돌더니만...
金東煥 - 팽팽 돌더니만, 그래 졸업하고 나서 그 뒤에＜199＞ 작품 속에 취급하여 본 것이 얼마나 되어요? 近作 시집 「柵瑚林」에도 몇 편 드러갓나뇨? 金東仁 씨의 감자 속에 「平壤 七星門 박」광경이 있드시 그 시집 속에도요?
盧天命 - 없어요. 쓰려고는 생각했지만 아직것 한 편도 없었서요. 비단 이 빈민굴 뿐 아니라, 精米 공장이나 방직 공장 같은 데 다니는 여공들의 생활도 다 흥미를 늣겨저요. 아름다운 면만 바라보지 말고, 우리들 여인은 여인의 그 생활 등을, 그 현실고를 깊이 깊히 그려보자고요...
李善熙 - 그야 우리가 여성이니만치 같은 여성 직업군의 생활 같은 데는 유별나게 주의가 가게 되지요. 빈민굴 속의 가난한 모녀의 생활 뿐 아니라 거리의 떼파-드의 셀스맨, 喫茶店의 여아, 늙수구레한 傳道 부인, 김빠진 재봉틀 판매녀, 여 교사, 하다 못해 박물장사 할머니, 거리의 갈보까지 그 운명과 그 사는 꼬락신이에 늘 눈이 안 갈 리가 있겠어요. 그러치만, 작가가 한 작품을 취재하는 것은 아모리 추하더래도 그 속에서 미감을 발견하여저야 비로소 치켜들게 되지요. 미감이란 말이 부적당하다면 감흥이라 해도 좋아요.
그런데 었쩐지 조선의 최하층 생활 속에서는 그가 소설이 되고, 희곡이 되고, 시가 되고, 노래가 되여질 미감을 찾어낼 수가 없어요. 외국 같으면 그 생활이 아모리 추하고, 악하고, 가난하더래도, 또 싸홈과 질투와 살인과 강도가 되푸리하더래도, 그 속에선 었쩐지 작가의 食味를 강렬하게 당시는 「썸싱」이 있는드시 늣겨지지만요. 이것은 거짓없는 나의 실감이여요.
崔貞熙 - 그야 그러치도 안켓지요. 서양 작가들도 이 지저분한 최하층 생활 속에서 처음엔 보잘 것 없는 소재를 골나서 그를 조직하고 묘사하고 꿈과 로-맨스를 집어너허 발효식혀내니까, 오늘날 우리네가 로맨틱하게 동경하는 그런 예술품이 되여진 것이 안이였을가요?
우리가 우리들 사는 이 거리에서 조선적인 매력있는 제재를 붓잡지 못했다면 그는 우리들 재조의 부족이 아닐가요. 스탄박은 연전에 서울와서 처음에 景福宮의 장려한 대들보를 구경하고 나서는 다시 거름을 돌처 동대문 밖 었떤 선술집에 이르러 막걸네 한 잔을 들고 그 정조를 원더풀, 빠유틔풀 불너가며 심각하게 艦賞하더래요. 예술가의 착안력이란 대개 이러케 다 달느지요. 그가 「모록코」의 창부 (데- 트릿지 배역)를 골나가지고 어떻게나＜200＞ 순정과 꿈을 부어넣어 걸작을 만들었는가를 볼 수 있지 않어요. 골키- 가 그린 「ドン底」도 필경은 저 屍口門 박 빈민굴 같은 그런 곳에서 간음, 강도, 살인하는 惡德漢들을 골나 가지고, 다만 거기에 인정과 사랑을 부어 넣어서 구상화한 것이 그 작품이 아니였을가요. 그러기에 要는 우리들의 태도와 재조에 달렷지요. 예전 「稻香」은 아침부터 밤까지 핸들을 잡고 운전하는 전차 차장의 생활 속에서 「電車車掌의 日記」같은 작품을 내지 않었든가요...
李善熙 - 그러터래도 나는 었쩐지 달는 듯해요. 조선은 문화 수준이 아모래도 서양과는 달르니까 서양 작가들은 같은 빈민굴이나 창부가나 선술집 거리에서 재료를 줏더래도 그네의 생활면은 훨신 넓고, 깁고 아름다우니까 마음대로 요리할 수 있지만은 조선이야, 너무 단순하고 깁히가 없고... 밤낮 천편일률, 그저 그러치요.
毛允淑 - 나는 빈민굴에는 일즉 가보지 못했지만 間島서 교원 노릇을 할 때에 박아지 달아매고 노새 등에 할머니 앉치고 胡地로 생활의 근거를 찾어가는 이주민들의 생활을 본 일이 여러 번 있었지요. 저녁 황혼 때 龍井 앞내 강변인 海蘭江가에 나앉으면, 황토색 얼굴들을 한, 주름살이 가득 잡힌 早老한 장정들이 老幼를 끄을고 북으로 작고 드러가는 그 이사꾼의 자태를 볼 적마다, 여러 가지를 생각하였서요. 그리고 그네의 실생활을, 관념적이 아니게, 진실로 보고 캐고 하여보고 싶은 생각이 불붓뜻 났었서요. 그 비참에 깍겨 찌그러지면서도 그 일군 속에는 택사스를 찾어가는 인디앤 족속같은, 살려는 생명력이 구불구불 물결치는 것 같은 것을 볼 수 있었어요. 그 생명력에 접해보고 그 고민을 같이 고민하여 보고 싶었어요. 이 속에서 다만 내게 才分이 있었다면 참으로 일세를 울닐 걸작이 나왔을 수 있었으련만은요...
金東煥 - 결국 네 분이 모다 현실- 그 중에도 모든 악과 무지가 용소슴치는 하층 계급의 생활상에 직면하여서 그를 응시하려드는 태도구뭔요. 잘 알었어요. 그 태도가 미듬즉하여요. 그 다음엔 또한 현세의 모든 비참과 죄악이 모혀있는 심연이라 할 재판소 법정과 창부가를 캐여본 적이 있었나요? 廉相涉 씨에 「檢事局 待合室」같은 작품이 그가 신문 기자로 법정에 다닐 때에 어든 재료로 되여젔드시...
盧天命 - 저도 中央日報 기자로 있을 적에 李洙倬의 공판 광경을 일부러 찾어가서 본 적이 있어요. 그 때 저는 범죄 사실과 피고의 답변을 듣고서 몹시 흥분되여지더구만요. 蓄妾 제도라든지, 그렇게 되기까지의＜201＞ 그 피고들의 심리상태 등, 이것은 시나 소설에 쓰고 십다기보다는 무슨 논문의 형식을 비러 꼭 쓰고 싶은 충동을 지금도 갓고 있어요.
