**Body Text Translation:**
Scene at the Training Center for Korean Soldiers

At the main gate of the large auditorium of the Imperial University in Tongsung-chŏng, Seoul, hangs a wooden sign with bright ink marks reading:
"Volunteer Training Center"
This is the volunteer training center where 200 men, selected from 3,000 applicants, are being trained to become soldiers for the army.
They have already been training for three months; since the program is originally for six months, in another three months, they will each become fine soldiers and be assigned to the 19th and 20th Barracks Gates (營門).
Therefore, although they are not yet soldiers, their daily life is said to be no different from that of a military barracks. I headed towards the Imperial University one day when the summer's scorching sun (曝陽) beat down on my back like a red furnace (紅爐), intending to visit this place. Entry to the Imperial University is strictly controlled.
When the reporter first stated the reason for entry to the Gatekeeper (門衛),
"Are you here to see a trainee?"
"No. I came from Samch'ŏlli magazine to write a visitor's report."
At this, he became more courteous and said,
"Then please enter this way and go to the Reception Desk (受付) at the front entrance."
I passed through the first gate without incident. Even so, it was military-style right from the first encounter. Originally, in a military camp (군영), even a rookie soldier (초년병) who has just entered is not allowed any outings until the first inspection (檢閲) is finished. Also, if there are visitors, it is all strict, and one must go through the guardhouse (위병소) to finish their necessary talk (用談) in the visitors' room. Therefore, it was not strange that here at the university entrance, or rather, until the training center gate opened, entry was questioned military-style, asking who each person was. So, just as he said, I went to the front entrance reception desk, and the handling there was quite different from outside, being very kind.
I was guided into the office. The director, Governor-General's Bureau of Educational Affairs Director Mr. Nohara (壚原), <184> was absent, and Army Infantry Colonel (大佐) Kaida Kaname (海田要) was present as the head instructor. This man would likely be a regimental commander when returning to his unit. Below him were Assistant Instructors (조교수) Mr. Morimoto (森本) and Mr. Tanaka (田中). According to the Imperial Decree (칙령), the training center has such staff.
"We are very busy because there are few instructors," the Colonel said.
"The cadets must be like rookie soldiers."
"Perhaps more like boy soldiers than rookie soldiers. It's quite strenuous."
"So, how have the results been so far?"
"Yes, very good. In any case, excellent youths have been selected from all over Korea (朝鮮), so they are young men with great futures."
"What kind of educational methods are you using now?"
"The issue I struggled with most initially was how to immerse these 200 peninsular youths, who clearly grew up in a Korean mold, into the true life (眞生活) of the Japanese."
"What about the daily routine?"
"At 6 AM, everyone wakes up and first performs worship from afar (遙拜) towards the Imperial Palace. With the highest form of bow (崔敬禮)."
When this command fell from the Colonel's lips, the 200 cadets simultaneously bowed their heads towards the Imperial Palace; this is said to be the prime time for spiritual training. The morning assembly is scheduled to end by 7:05 AM as part of the daily routine, so the rule is to wash up before then.

Daily Routine (日常의 行事)
Every day
1. 6:40 AM = Roll call (점호) begins. <185>
1. 8:05 AM = Breakfast begins. It's entirely Japanese barracks food (병영식); although there's no kitchen duty, the meals prepared by city caterers are known to contain nutrition comparable to that of a middle-class Korean household.
1. Until 8:30 AM = Preparation begins
1. Until 11:35 AM = Training is conducted, mainly consisting of saluting, unarmed marching (徒手 행진), and various military movements; rifle drill (집총 교련) has not yet begun. However, for the training center cadets, this must be the thing they anticipate the most.
"The goal is not just to create soldiers in form only. They must become Japanese soldiers even in their hearts," the Colonel said, under the conviction that rifle drill will not be conducted until the spiritual training is sufficient.
"The purpose of this training center is not to make soldiers, but to make humans who will become soldiers, so it is fine even without rifle drill."
In a few months, practice for "Present Arms" (받으러 총 - *likely uketore총*) will begin, but such things are not done yet.
The Colonel continued to explain the purpose of the training further.
"The trainees' desire is fervent, so if they fully understand the spirit of the national anthem they sing every morning, no, not just that, if their mindset becomes the spirit of true Japanese subjects, with their excellent physique, they will be able to shoulder the future of Japan."
he says. In daily usage, not a single word of Korean is allowed other than the national language [Japanese]. Even if parents or siblings come to visit, the regulation forbids them from speaking in Korean.
Having heard about the character of this training center up to this point, I went outside with the Colonel to observe the actual training.

Strict Training (嚴格한 調練)
"Attention! (氣着!) Align right! (우로 나라잇!)"
The Colonel calls this a drill ground, but it's the yard behind the research building attached between the auditorium and the school building, designated for use until the training center is completed by August.
As the Army Infantry Sergeant (伍長), acting as an assistant instructor, shouted the command, the 50 cadets of one platoon, who had been practicing putting on leggings (脚絆), instantly assumed the position of attention (부동자세) and fixed their gaze on the Colonel. It was perfectly military-style.
However, the Colonel did not accept this salute. This was because he saw one cadet fumbling, not having finished putting on his leggings <186>.
"No good, no good. Do it again. When the command 'Attention!' is given, you must drop everything else and come to attention."
Thus, rolling again from the Sergeant's mouth,
"Attention! (氣着!)"
"Align right! (우로 나라잇!)"
The commands followed one after another.
The Colonel boasted to the reporter about the good physique of the cadets. All average 5 shaku 4 sun [approx. 164cm or 5'4"].
And,
"Their education is also good; they are all elementary school 4th year graduates or higher."
Looking at the educational background of the cadets who entered here:
Middle School Graduates: 4
Vocational School (실업학교) Graduates: 5
Middle School 5th Year Completion: 5
Same, 4th Year: 1
Same, 3rd Year: 5
Same, 2nd Year: 31
Same, 1st Year: 20
Besides these, most are elementary school graduates or have completed 4 years or more.
The total number is 202, and breaking this down by occupation:
Agriculture: 107
Clerk (점원): 18
Government Office Boy (관청 급사): 13
Office Worker (사무원): 11
Commerce (상업): 11
Artisan/Worker (직공): 3
Company Employee (회사원): 2
Hired (雇) Clerk (점원): 3
Technician (技手): 3
Student (학생): 1
Unemployed (무직): 10
Among these, there are 55 married men (妻帶者). In any case, such strict and intense training continues until 4 PM. This involves dividing the 200 cadets into 4 platoons, each educated by a platoon leader. Then, bathing (입욕) is at 5 PM, laundry at 6 PM, dinner (석식) at 7 PM, lecture (講話) at 8:30 PM, self-study at 9 PM, and then lights out at 9:30 PM. This 9 PM self-study time is when they write letters to their parents, wives, and children back home, or gather together to chat (談語). Once entered into this training center, no personal belongings other than underwear are permitted. This is to prevent distinctions of wealth among the cadets.
In this way, the 200 stalwart youths (健兒) are forging iron-like minds and bodies (심신) day and night, aiming to become soldiers of tomorrow. <187>

**Summary**

This 1938 article reports on a visit to the Volunteer Training Center established at Keijō Imperial University in Seoul, where 200 selected Korean men undergo a six-month program to become soldiers in the Japanese Imperial Army. The piece details the rigorous daily schedule, the emphasis on strict military discipline, physical fitness, and spiritual training focused on loyalty to the Japanese Emperor and assimilation into Japanese identity. Notably, the use of the Korean language is strictly forbidden, even with visiting family, and rifle training is postponed until the cadets are deemed sufficiently imbued with the "Japanese spirit." For historians, the article offers a contemporary glimpse into Japan's colonial policy of assimilating Koreans into the military structure during the Second Sino-Japanese War, highlighting the methods used to foster imperial loyalty, the prohibition of Korean language and culture within the training, and the profile (education, occupation) of the early Korean volunteers.

이러하고 이 가운데 妻帶者가 55명이 있다. 엇쨋든 이렇게 엄격하고 맹렬한 훈련이 오후 4시까지 게속된다. 이것은 200명 생도를 4개 반에 나누어 각 반장이 교육하는 것이다. 그러고는 입욕이 午接 5시 세탁이 6시, 석식이 7시, 講話가 8시 30분, 자습이 9시, 그러고는 9시 반이 취침이다. 이 9시의 자습 시간에 고향의 부모 처자들에게 편지를 쓰기도 하고 서로 모여 안저 談語도 하는 시간이다. 그러고 이 훈련소에 일단 드러오면 내복 이외에는 일절로 私物을 허락하지 않는다. 그것은 생도이 사이에 빈부의 차를 두지 안키 위하여서이다.
