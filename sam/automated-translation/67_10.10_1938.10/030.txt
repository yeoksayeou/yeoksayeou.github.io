The Order of the Golden Kite (金鵄勳章) and Annuities
Whether in the Manchurian Incident or the Sino-Japanese Incident, if one goes to the front during wartime, achieves military distinction, and dies an honorable death in battle, the state confers the highest decoration, the Order of the Golden Kite. The Golden Kite ranges from Merit 1st Class (功一級) to Merit 7th Class (功七級), and all come with an annuity. Specifically:
O Merit 1st Class: 1,500 yen
O Merit 2nd Class: 1,000 yen
O Merit 3rd Class: 700 yen
O Merit 4th Class: 500 yen
O Merit 5th Class: 350 yen
O Merit 6th Class: 250 yen
O Merit 7th Class: 150 yen
This annuity is paid not only during the recipient's lifetime but also to the bereaved family for five years after death.
Listing those soldiers and officers with military merit who received this decoration in the Manchurian and Shanghai Incidents:
Merit 1st Class - The late Field Marshal Mutō (武藤), General Honjō (本庄)
Merit 2nd Class - The late Lieutenant General Tamon (多門)
Merit 3rd Class - Itagaki (板垣), current Minister of War; Colonel Ishiwara (石原)
Merit 4th Class - Engineering Captain Fujita (藤田) (long-distance aviator)
Merit 6th Class - The three human bombs: Sergeants Eda (江下), Kitagawa (北川), and Sakue (作江)
In the current Sino-Japanese Incident as well, among the already 6,605 fallen officers and soldiers, a large number, 5,834 heroes, have been awarded the Order of the Golden Kite.

Manila, August 18th, Dōmei News Agency (同盟通信)

"Regarding the plan to neutralize the Philippines after its independence is realized, Japanese politicians, without exception, seem to agree. I harbor no concerns whatsoever about Japan's attitude after Philippine independence.

Insiders' Report on the Financial and Mining Worlds
Tens of Millions of Russian Rubles in Korean Hands – Considerable Amounts in Hamgyŏng North Province Alone
Recently, currency used during Russia's old Imperial era, namely the Romanov dynasty's ruble (留), has begun to gain value, causing a stir. The truth is this: The first sounds of seeking Russian currency began secretly in the Baltic states bordering the Maritime Province, and their reach gradually extended to the Far East, namely Khabarovsk, Vladivostok (海參威), etc. The recent price is said to be "15 francs per 1,000 rubles." While these 15 francs convert to only a few sen in Japanese currency, value has nevertheless begun to appear in what was waste paper.
This Russian currency became purely waste paper, playthings for children, simultaneously with the fall of the old Romanov dynasty when Lenin's radical faction seized Bolshevik power. Thus, this paper money, which no one had paid attention to for about 20 years, is now suddenly being sought by speculators, causing international ripples.

Soviet Crisis Causing This Phenomenon? Or Perhaps a Plot to Overthrow the Current Regime?

Why has old Russian currency suddenly gained value, causing such an international stir? The details are not yet known, but such talk is circulating in the streets. According to information obtained by Marshal Blücher (쀼류헬) via an emissary from England (英斯科?), Soviet munitions factories are reportedly incurring huge losses of 15 billion rubles annually, putting the Soviet state finances in dire straits. Furthermore, Soviet-made armored cars and tanks are said to be made with poor-quality raw materials prone to mechanical failure, <18> making them likely to suffer a major defeat if they were to fight Germany or a certain Eastern power (東邦 某强國). Because of this, the Soviet Union is facing an extreme crisis, leading to speculation that the old Imperial-era currency might be used again in the future – that's one reason. Another theory is that anti-Stalinist factions are secretly maneuvering, buying up old Russian currency to build a large power base and overthrow the current regime, but the truth of this is unknown.

Tens of Thousands of Yen Worth Just in Hamgyŏng Province
Kim Ki-dŏk, Hong Chong-hwa, and Many Others Hold Large Amounts
According to sources familiar with the matter, the total amount of Russian currency dormant within Chosŏn and Manchukuo likely reaches a truly enormous sum. The region where the most is likely held is Hamgyŏng Province.
Due to Hamgyŏng Province's border with Siberia, many Hamgyŏng merchants traded throughout the Maritime Province, and through their hands, vast amounts of Russian currency flowed in. Trade at that time involved exporting many thousands, tens of thousands of live cattle from Hamgyŏng Province to places like Yŏnch'u (煙秋 - Yanji?) and Vladivostok, importing herring or rubber shoes from the Maritime Province, and much other commerce in grains, marine products, cotton cloth, etc.
Even when the Kerensky government was established, the Russian ruble was worth 70-80 chŏn per Japanese yen, but after the Bolshevik takeover, it gradually fell to 50 chŏn, 40 chŏn, and eventually became useless paper worth less than 1 chŏn.
Among Hamgyŏng personages holding the most of this Russian currency are Messrs. Kim Ki-dŏk and Hong Chong-hwa, each reportedly holding several million yen worth. Besides them, it is rare to find anyone engaged in commerce to some extent who doesn't hold at least one or two million yen worth.
In any case, the fact that Russian currency is gaining value is an interesting phenomenon.

Ten Million Working Women in Mainland Japan <19>

Since the outbreak of the China Incident (支那事變), the advance of women into the professional workforce in our country (我國 - refers to Japan) is truly remarkable. This is partly due to the sharp increase in demand for workers in the munitions industry, but also likely related to the awakening of women on the home front and the economic impact on households. While the exact number of working women is not yet clear, according to the occupational survey statistics from Shōwa 5 (1930), there were just under 10,590,000 female workers. The breakdown was: agriculture approx. 6.4 million, mining approx. 40,000, fisheries approx. 16,000, manufacturing approx. 1.43 million, commerce approx. 1.46 million, transport approx. 79,000, public service/liberal professions approx. 350,000, domestic servants approx. 700,000, and others approx. 83,000. Let's estimate the starting salary for female occupations at 20-30 yen. First, for jobs primarily requiring intelligence:
Elementary school teacher approx. 40 yen, female doctor (hospital) 70-80 yen, newspaper reporter approx. 60 yen, company employee 25-35 yen. Next, for jobs primarily requiring technical skills: Typist (European script 40 yen), telephone operator 30-45 yen, elevator girl 25 yen. Finally, for jobs primarily involving manual labor: Government enterprise female worker daily wage 60-80 sen, spinning mill female worker daily wage approx. 55 sen, bus conductor (including allowance) 35 yen, department store waitress daily wage approx. 70 sen.

**Summary**

This 1938 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine presents a collection of short news items ("Confidential Room") covering diverse aspects of Korean society under Japanese rule. It details the editorial staff of major newspapers (Chosŏn Ilbo, Tonga Ilbo, Maeil Sinbo), notes the reinstatement of professors linked to nationalist groups, lists prominent Seoul figures, reports on a new Christian newspaper, catalogues patriotic wartime slogans displayed by institutions, lists military inscriptions at Sŏgwangsa Temple, quantifies Korean donations to the Japanese war effort (including aircraft purchases), discusses the Order of the Golden Kite medal, quotes the Philippine president affirming Japan's intentions, mentions Japanese population in Hawaii, covers financial news (speculation in old Russian rubles, Oriental Development Company loans, working women in Japan), reports on entertainment (Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi's tours, a "Ch'unhyangjŏn" play, Okada Yoshiko rumors, German filming in Kŭmgangsan, Meiji-za theater finances), lists top-earning kisaeng and austerity measures imposed on them in P'yŏngyang, and notes the sale of the old Central Daily News building and a major inheritance lawsuit involving the Min family.

For historians, this article offers valuable glimpses into colonial Korean society during the intensified wartime mobilization period. The lists of names (journalists, intellectuals, social figures, kisaeng), the pervasiveness of Japanese wartime slogans and mobilization efforts (donations, labor), the reporting on sensitive topics like the Hŭngsadan incident (albeit obliquely) and Christian responses to shrine worship, the economic details (ruble speculation, Tōtaku loans, kisaeng earnings), and the cultural news (theater, film) provide rich source material on media control, social stratification, economic conditions, cultural activities, and the complex interplay between collaboration, resistance, and adaptation under colonial rule. The mention of censored names (e.g., An--, Sin--) and the juxtaposition of patriotic fervor with kisaeng income also reveal the contradictions of the era.
