文人書翰集 (Collection of Writers' Letters)

Ch'unwŏn's Letter
To the One Who Holds a Doll
Chagŭm, feeling the lack of charm in a lover's heart that seems to smile as it turns away, seems to embrace as it collapses, holds a doll instead. If well cared for, the doll will live longer than you, and even after you reach 100, it may remarry two or three times.
The Romans sought to embrace friendship. Whether they embraced friendship after holding a doll is unknown, but the writings of their sages valued friendship higher than romantic love or even the affection of blood relatives. It seems the intention was not merely to choose the lesser pain when friendship changes.
It is said that a certain Han Chinese bowed to a rock. A rock is not one that rejoices in love and weeps in heartbreak. Though a rock may break and become sand, each broken grain is still like the rock. When friendship breaks, it becomes enmity, but when love breaks, does it not become poison (鴆毒), as Kŭmja might say? When a doll breaks, it merely becomes dust, and if the room (屋間) is opened for a moment, it becomes clean again<72>. The affection for holding a doll seems the most harmless.
Therefore, the Sage said, do not love anything, have no desires. Does this perhaps mean not to trust friendship, not to bow to rocks, and not to hold dolls?
Returning the doll you lent me, without any feeling of regret or gratitude.
February 9th
Ch'unwŏn

To Hwadon, Elder Brother in Poetry
Postscript. My lover is a French doll. (Refer to Yŏsŏng, May issue) This beloved doll – this doll I cannot live without even for a day – with great determination, I sent her to Ch'unwŏn in his sickroom for a promised three days, allowing her (厥女) to comfort the teacher. The three-day promise stretched to a month. Feeling sorry for him, I couldn't bear to call my lover back. But finally, my own agony from being separated from my lover grew too great. So I declared to the teacher that I would take my lover back. He said, "Bear it just one more day, then I will send her tomorrow." The next day, indeed, wrapped not in a wedding veil but in newspaper – yet with quite careful handling – my beloved returned to her lover's arms. Her name is "Ave Maria." This letter, of course, was not delivered by a subordinate of the postmaster general, but brought by a certain polite young man of literature who escorted her. I believe this writing is a masterpiece for the ages.
I chose this letter not for that reason, but because among the countless letters I currently possess, it is the one that made me think the most.
Kim Munjip

Letter from Mr. Pak Yŏnghŭi (Hoewŏl)
Second Letter to My Respected Wŏlt'an
Have you seen the first letter? At that time, the severe stimulus of pain shattered the crystallization of my thoughts.
Brother! What could my own suffering have meant? Could it signify the glorious, venerably shining cross of victory of a Saint? Or does it mean the richness of well-oiled, lustrous (潤채나는) skin? Does it mean a beautiful woman dancing in a city <73> afflicted with vanity? Ah—what is my suffering?
It signifies the tender tears of a weakened spirit for the fragrant ruin of the flesh; it signifies the spirit's fragrant (燻香), new, mystical construction for the ruin of the decaying flesh shattered by fate. It signifies the spirit's romanticism arising from the flesh's doubt about how to find the spirit's eternal freedom for the sake of the burning flame of the spirit's yearning pain. It is my suffering, wandering迷路) alone, clutching the spirit's breast, exhaling flames burning with agony, seeking a new, untrodden path in a desolate wild garden (荒蕪한 野園). I can no longer see the pleasures of a wild garden full of blooming roses; I intend to taste the black terror of a lake howling (吼叫) in the deep night. I must realize anew that when the flesh's pleasure is chaotic, the spirit's silence deepens. When the flesh is in pleasure, the spirit inevitably experiences a pain transcending the human. Therefore—look at the "Decadent" school, the Aesthetes, the Hedonists of the fin de siècle in Europe (歐洲). Their pleasure of the flesh signified their infinite spiritual pain. Thus, unable to endure the spirit's pain, they sometimes drank alcohol, sometimes took opium, eventually returning to Death. However, I neither condemn nor affirm their pleasure and their Death. As one who intends to fight for art, I certainly do not deny them. But my pleasure is not the pleasure they received through their individualistic art; it is the pleasure received through my art, and the consciousness of wanting to artistically cool the dark spirits of the non-artistic masses generally torments my spirit. However, I am not an advocate of humanism or popular art; rather, while denying art for the people and art for the people's lives, I intend to call the general populace to share true pleasure through that art, establishing a country of a new, unnamed spirit. That is, I intend to let the general populace concretely taste art for art's sake. Life is brief, life is imperfect, and life is non-artistic. In other words, life refers to the "flesh," and it means the general populace made of that flesh. Therefore, rather than the humanism of art for life, the artistic transformation of life for art, a neo-idealism, painfully comes to mind. This might not be a New Ism. Perhaps this resembles the Neo-Romanticism that arose again from modern = skepticism to destruction = romanticism, from romanticism to individualism <74>, from individualism to naturalism, from naturalism. I have digressed too much, like some treatise on thought, getting overly logical (論法스럽게).
= Regarding my poetry recently =
The time we shared, brother, was very short. Therefore, my Poetic Sensibility (詩感) should not be different. However, my use of the term Poetic Sensibility is by no means a non-artistic term; I mean the thought process when we write poetry. I am now in a dark room facing West (西便). Therefore, I have never once seen the sun rise, never once seen the moon rise, only when I wake in the deep night, the gray Moonlight, about to set in the west, shines fearfully. Ah—, I see only terrifying destruction. Therefore, my poetic sensibility seeks to attain new creation, intending to celebrate the feast of construction with soft melodies. The strong musical cadence (調子) of the creation of Love, the mystical construction of the Spirit, trembles. But it is still only a longing, not yet written.
= Enough for now due to headache =
Even if I cannot write often, please write frequently, brother. Shatter those false Men of Letters (文士) and build something new. We must be in the new literary world, and a devotion like that of the Renaissance (루네산스) period is necessary.
I bought about 4 books, but haven't had the leisure yet.
To brother Wŏlt'an
- Taishō 9 (1920), October -

Letter from Ms. Pak Hwasŏng
Impressions of a Shining Poetry Collection
My passionate poet, possessing the mission of a star burning and burning even within layers upon layers of surrounding clouds!
If you were to ask me to give you a name, I believe you would acknowledge me calling you thus. Please call me Elder Sister Pak Hwasŏng. I have no qualms of conscience in believing that I am a Pak Hwasŏng who possesses these three: a conscience, love, and magnanimity sufficient to embrace all your various complaints and passions. In return, do you too have the confidence to become a younger brother who can embrace all these passions and possess such Trust (信)? If you do, come running bravely, cast off the mask or the yellow veil you wore when facing the artists <75>, and turn those bright, clear pupils of the sparkling star towards me. If you have the confidence to pledge this, call me Elder Sister Pak Hwasŏng, and I trust you will let this shadow, along with your artistic soul, dwell forever in your breast.
Thank you anew for the poetry collection. Did you give it to Mr. Yun Sŏkchung (尹石重)? Ah, if you're going to, please give it quickly. One thing I want to say is that the author's preface to "Shining Region" (빗나는 지역) is truly a well-written preface flowing with the virtue of humility (謙讓之風). I rejoiced reading it. Reading the content, I admired your energetic Talent (才操). However, I will list what I think stands out. This is my evaluation: The last line of "Storm" (폭풍) is a Masterpiece (絶唱). I think its literary value is also top-rank.
"Parting" (리별), "Wife's Wish" (안해의 소원): In terms of poetic conception, I think they are passionate, deep, and the best.
"Life" (목슴): Terribly dear, pitiful, and lovely.
Mr. X's unique wit sparkles (才氣發發).
"Departing Katyusha" (떠나는 카쥬샤): Powerful and lacks roughness.
I gave the above evaluations for these five pieces. What is your esteemed opinion (高見如何)? Please tell me if I am mistaken. I think the other poems are simply beautiful, lovely, and pretty poems. As you say, our path is visible, and I expect robust, red poems, transcending personal complaints, large, wide, and deep, for humanity and representing the classes, to emerge from my precious younger brother, and I only pray that "Shining Region" becomes its process, truly a "shining process."
Please let me know immediately when Teacher Ch'unwŏn arrived, and write soon.
Our child is currently sick (알코), my palm hurts, and I am in an extremely painful, lonely, and restless state; please comfort me with gentle, but sincere writing. The publication commemoration party is on the 9th, I want to go too.
Maybe I'll be able to go around then.
Shōwa 11 (1936), November 5th, 4 PM
Pak Hwasŏng

My Dearly Thought-of Younger Brother! (毛弟)
Received your letter Yesterday (昨日) and imagined how the mercilessly blowing cold wind from Pugak (北岳) must be making my Dear Brother (吾弟) unbearably cold <76>, feeling even more sorry and sad for you. However, since a passion that doesn't cool or extinguish must always be burning even in that small breast, how much should one fear the external Cold Wind (凉風)? I think of this to console myself.
Today is November 9th! Tonight at 6:30! The day the first light of my precious Dear Brother's debut shines! Please rebuke the lack of spirit of this Foolish Elder Sister (愚兄) who cannot attend. Truly, I feel regretful sometimes. How shining this night, this occasion must be... From afar, I commemorate this hour and pray that my Dear Brother's path ahead will always shine thus; please understand this Sad Affection (哀情), and may you fulfill without fail the expectations placed upon you by the many people gathered tonight. Lonely, harsh, and also darkness and light alternately startle us. Let us Sisters (妹姉), intending to enter literature, lead each other, rely on each other, help each other, and fulfill for each other a role like Moses' (모세) staff of old.
The child is sick (앞으고), my palm still hurts so I can't even wash my face, think of me suffering like this every day, and overcome even considerable pain.
I plan to leave on the night of the 14th and arrive in Kyŏngsŏng (京城) on the morning of the 15th. Work piles up, and my mind is anxious beyond endurance. I am going with plans to stay in Kyŏngsŏng for a few days before heading North (北行).
November 9th
Respectfully, Pak Hwasŏng

Letter from Na Pin (Tohyang)
Wŏlt'an, Kind Brother, eternal communicator of the Spirit!
Lately, I see the Clear Sky Day and Night (晴天 霄夜) of the heavens, but I cannot see the clear sky of my spirit. It is always Cloudy Heavens (雲天) and Rainy Nights (雨夜). Yet, it's not as if a storm blows and Heavy Rain (大雨) falls, causing a terrifying mental change as if Heaven and Earth are transforming (天變地移), washing away the messy, unpleasant, and murky things in my mind. Instead, like the Plum Rains (梅雨) of spring, a damp feeling constantly envelops my mind. Looking around in all directions, everything is gray. Not even a little bit of yang energy can be seen, nor any pink hue. All doubts about life push into this tiny Chest Cavity (胸腔) of mine.
Even the hot blood in the bright red heart cools and melts. All concepts, all mental thoughts, just seem scorched into blue ash. <77>
I am ordering and reading the "Lectures on Modern Thought" (近代思想講話). Now I am reading Turgenev's (튜르께네프) "Fathers and Sons" – featuring a nihilist as the protagonist – I gained much from the "Lectures on Modern Thought." However, "Fathers and Sons" truly brings forth only small, small confirmations.
By reading that new thing, my thoughts have instead come to wander more in doubt. The assertions, doctrines, and theories of those various thinkers, philosophers, and artists – they themselves believed in them, relied on them, worked out their life's sole faith based on them, affirmed it, and tried to create a Conclusion (締結) there, but I cannot do so. All those people merely gave me homework. They gave me ?. While there are points where I resonate with one or another of them, it cannot become my faith, nor the conclusion of my life. Everything is Doubt (疑) and Confusion (惑). And all the joys of life just seem like falsehoods. But even so, it's not that I intend to sacrifice This Life (此生) for the sake of a spiritual life after death, like a Christian. Rousseau's (루소) saying "return to nature" seems plausible, Nietzsche's (니체) saying "act according to instinct" doesn't seem false, and the theory of eternal recurrence seems plausible, but Tolstoy (톨스토이) doesn't appeal to me. Dostoevsky (떠스터에프스키)- is pitiful. Tagore (타골) has a Profound (幽玄) taste. But James (쩸쓰?) and Bergson (빽손?) do not appeal to me.
All people are researchers of partial anatomy of all life. Just like those who have studied parts of the human body comprehensively, they only assert what they have seen, heard, and known. One cannot see anyone who has completely dissected and studied the entirety of life. Our life is such that everyone's own doctrine, own theory is advocated according to their own environment, their own personality. That is, the confectioner, a physiological natural person, kneads, rolls, puts us humans into molds as they please, makes us sweet, bitter, tart, and sour, and then each declares their own taste and things similar to their taste as Good (善).
No matter how great a person, great thinker, great artist, or great philosopher one may be, they probably cannot boldly say they were not raised (길니지) in the hands of that confectioner.
In that case, isn't it that even I, now looking at writings, writing things, possessing some kind of powerful confidence, having inherited my entire life's circumstances or my lineage (一生涯者), that is, the Heredity (遺傳) from our ancestors' time, being born in this region, Receiving (受) all the joyful, sad, spicy, and bitter stimuli of the Four Gardens (四園?), and that influence forms my character <78>, dominates my thoughts, and from there I affirm and approve the emerging Doctrines (主義) and Assertions (主唱), thus forming my own single taste? If so, isn't the lord of creation pitiful? Truly pitiful. That bustling and leaping about is just one turn of the universe (略-original text)
And Wŏlt'an must know, but I am a person who cannot but use my hands and feet for labor for my Livelihood (衣食) throughout my Life (一生涯). That is why I left Seoul and came all the way to Andong (安東). All my Skills (技能) and so-called ideals cannot be fully trained and cultivated due to the labor and time required to secure that Livelihood. This tasteless, lonely, tearful life, I must accept it sweetly and carry on. I am pitiful among pitiful humans. Just the power that came equally at birth has made me into this pitiful person.
And you too, all those forces – Convention (因襲) – make you take a nap in the neatly arranged drawing room (사랑방). The centripetal force of reality pulls our centrifugal force. But when the centripetal force wins, one is a dead person. Cold Ash (灰).
Next summer, I'll go up [to Seoul] and if possible, I hope you can arrange for me to stay in Seoul.
Taishō 9 (1920), December
Respectfully, Tohyang

Mr. Pak Chonghwa
Letter from Mr. Yi Sanghwa
Even without saying, you probably guess, but how much must be the longing heart, burning inwardly for each other in the silence. <79> Isn't that truly the silence that speaks more than words? Mr. Chonghwa! The pale face (얼골) of yours when I went to your house to see you comes floating up before my longing eyes. When coming down, I thought I would see you again soon - that thought turned into the sleep-talk of a sleepless child. But can't I express such wishes again through writing? When human Reason (理智) fails to achieve self-awareness, one lives a life of pure idiocy, and when Reason achieves self-awareness, one lives the life of a wise idiot who submits while knowing everything - if one thinks this way, it must be said to be a proper thing not to realize this.
The "Secret Song of the Black Room" (黑房秘曲) you sent! A flower from the flowerpot nurtured and raised by your heart, which resides in a neighborhood not far from my heart! Your affection (親愛) in throwing me one blossom of that flower - I offer thanks until tears flow. When I received your "Collection of Writings" (글 모듬), the impression of its "First Sigh" it gave me was the meaning of the title - a summer's dark night (금옴 밤) - that night's black fog enveloping my head. Needless to say trivial details, as the Scope (範圍) won't exceed it, I dare not offer trivial words. Just that, like your heart, the pitiful state of the Binding (裝愼 - likely typo for 裝幀, binding) cannot be entirely avoided, for some righteous reason perhaps, I cannot suppress a bitter smile. More than anything, I believe - and pray again even more - that the precious reward of sacrificing most of your young heart will loom over the hearts of us White-Clad People (白衣人).
Mr. Chonghwa! Please do not, with your exceedingly Humble (謙讓性) heart, take these inadequate words of mine as improper. How could the words I wish to say be only these, but as writing them down somehow won't relieve my feelings, I will report only this much, and I hope you Protect (護寧) your body and that your heart may unfold doubly.
- Taishō 9 (1920), December -

"Letter from P'ing'hŏ · Hyŏn Chin'gŏn"

Brother! Please forgive me. Thoughtless me, even while coming here, completely forgot to let you know with a few words. The Affectionate Letter (情札) that fell upon me, who needlessly trims the Empty Lamp (空燈) at the Guest Window (客窓) on an Autumn Night (秋夜), was both welcome and embarrassing.
But I shall brazenly trust that my generous brother <80> will not mind such things.
Brother! As you may have already heard, I came here seeking Tranquility (閑靜) and solitude. But I am already weary of Tranquility and saddened by solitude. The sound of the stream rustling the pillow, the bright moon caught in the tree branches, the clear-voiced cry of the cuckoo (杜鵑) – why is it so desolate? Why is it so mournful? Moreover, it is autumn. Autumn, full of thoughts and longing for people. It is this time when the desolate Golden Wind (金風), the Cuckoo (不如歸) in the Empty Mountain (空山), join together to sing a Tragic (悲愴) melody. Standing, treading on my lonely shadow, thinking of home, thinking of friends, how many times (몃번) have I felt tears welling in my eyes (눈 구비)! I intend to go (가랴하나이다). I intend to go to Seoul where my dear friends are. Starved for interesting talk and affectionate chatter, I've become unable to endure even a single day more. It seems it won't be long before I grasp your warm wrist again.
By the way, brother, you seem quite well (좃습듸다그려). The Joy (樂) of your hometown must be endless. Your wife's writing was truly a Masterpiece (絶唱). Starved for laughter, when I saw this writing, for the first time, a single flower of laughter bloomed on my lonely lips. Her feelings when writing it must have been miserable (처량하섰지요). And her heart must have surged with joyous waves when she saw her Husband (家郞) secretly reading it? The scene described as "Leaving the mountain at sunset, clouds obscure the path home; alone on the Crimson Terrace (紅愁瑤臺), the bright moon shines" (出山田暮雲無歸跡. 紅愁瑤臺明月孤) must have been a dream long past. "The sunset clouds over the mountain now know to return; cloud and rain on the Jade Terrace (雲雨瑤臺), the moon shines bright and lonely" (出山田暮雲今知還하니 雲雨瑤臺月明孤라), and so on.
Reading Wŏlt'an's letter, it says Munhwasa is being organized and Paekcho (白潮) is being revived? That is truly joyful news. How could your efforts, shedding Blood and Sweat (血汗), be in vain? I'll save the remaining words for when we meet, and close for now!
Seventh Month of Im-Mu Year (壬戊之秋 7月 旣望 - likely 1922)
P'ing'hŏ

Letter from Mr. Chang Hyŏkchu
Kind Brother Nosŏk (露雀 - likely Kang Kyŏngae's pen name?)
Last Night (昨夜), until Late at Night (深更), I took out magazines containing your writings and reread them. What I read Last Night were the three short stories "Salt" (소곰), "Manuscript Fee 200 Won" (원고료 200원), "Dismissal" (해고), and the two Essays (隨想) "Hometown Sky" (고향의 창공) and "Today, Suddenly" (오늘 문득). I glanced at "Human Problem" (인간 문제) occasionally in the newspaper, but since I don't like newspaper serializations, I haven't read it completely. I will buy and <81> read this later if it is published as a standalone book. Although I read parts here and there, what I did read still remains in my memory with a fresh feeling, and I believe it is undoubtedly a masterpiece (力作).
The first time I learned your name was with "Salt"; I do not know when you emerged in the literary world. I emerged in the Korean literary world with "Rainbow" (무지개), but I am not yet recognized as an artistic writer, merely having the honor of having my name included at the very end of the Korean literary scene as a mere popular writer. But you cannot imagine how much I anticipate the emergence of masterpieces from writers like you. The Chinese literary world, which began its new literature 5 or 6 years behind the Korean literary world, is today rather surpassing the Korean literary world; thus, my anticipation and impatience for a masterpiece to emerge in Korean literature are greater than others'.
Last night, while reading "Salt" thoroughly, how joyful and encouraged I was when I felt that the Hope (望待) I gained from "Human Problem" was not shattered but grew even larger? Teacher Kang Kyŏngae (姜敬愛)! "Salt" is a masterpiece. Of course, I too can point out flaws here and there and a lack of power (魄力) throughout the piece. However, it is undeniable that one masterpiece has been added to Korean literature.
In the New Year issue of the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報), I once wrote that still no one could follow Tongin (東仁), Sangsŏp (想沙 - Yŏm Sangsŏp), and P'ing'hŏ (憑虛 - Hyŏn Chin'gŏn). What I wrote then was based on my impressions from several years prior, but how could I have ignored the Various Works (諸作) of Minch'on (民村 - Yi Kiyŏng?) and the works of gentlemen like Sŏlya (雪野), Hyosŏk (孝石 - Yi Hyosŏk), Hyŏnmin (玄民), etc.? What I said then was referring only to technique.
However, in today's Korean literature, besides the works of the aforementioned male writers, there are even more works worth seeing among the works of female writers (女流作家); compared to China and mainland Japan, this is a peculiar phenomenon.
In literature, "Realism" (리알리즘) alone cannot be said to be high art, but for Korean literature now, "Realism" is definitely necessary. As its representative writer, I can mention Minch'on, and Minch'on's works are by no means inferior to the level of foreign works. While it cannot yet be said that they have surpassed the standard, they are definitely not below standard. Those with discerning eyes (具眼) will all acknowledge that this is by no means an overestimation just because it is our Korea.
Compared to Minch'on, I have discovered <82> two writers among the female writers whose shoulders can rub against his. Pak Hwasŏng (朴花城) is one, and you, teacher, are the other.
Therefore, your "Salt" greatly pleased me, and realizing it holds its own compared to foreign female writers, I felt quite delighted.
"Salt" realistically depicted people wandering Manchuria (滿洲) after leaving Korea.
Teacher! Do you yourself realize that you are - in the whole world - the only writer depicting Manchuria? In that sense too, you are a precious existence, so please take care of yourself (自重).
The Title (題) "Salt" has no special connection to salt, except that the female protagonist gets caught smuggling salt and a few descriptions in the first chapter feeling the lack of "salt." The life story - suffering - of Pongshik's mother forms its theme. We in the mainland (內地 - likely Japan or Korea proper) are very curious about the subsequent stories of those who left for Manchuria.
"Salt" greatly satisfied that desire of mine.
Furthermore, your detailed description of peasant life amazed me. (In the first chapter)
... Thinking this, she swept every corner of the room. Then, picking up potatoes that rolled out (대구르르 대구르르) from the end of the broom, she put them in a basket (바다기 - likely 바구니) and tidied the shelf (시렁)... (Emphasis by Chang - original text)
Teacher! Unless one has lived in the countryside and personally done such sweeping in such a room, one could never produce such a description.
... Having tidied the shelf, she began sorting the red beans (팟) kept in the basket (바구미 - likely 바구니). In the quiet room, only the sound of red bean grains rustled (재르럭 자르르). (Emphasis Chang - original text)
You showed such excellent description in "Human Problem" as well, which someone without direct experience could never write, and also in "Salt" and "Dismissal," so I wondered whether you were a rural woman or someone raised in the city.
Then, reading the May issue of "Sin Kajŏng" (新家庭), I realized, "Ah, indeed," and understood. From "Hometown Sky":
... Among them, the smallest and oldest house is ours. I don't know how many decades it's been since that house was built, but anyway, not a single wall is properly plastered, not a single pillar is sound. <83> On rainy days, the smell of rotting pillars rises thickly (물쿤하게)... (Excuse me for frequently adding emphasis)
Teacher! Did your mother weave thatch (이엉) herself?
... I stood silently for a while, then went beside Mother and picked up bundles of thatch straw (이엉초), placing them in Mother's hand...
Therefore, please also acknowledge yourself as the most suitable person to describe rural villages.
Teacher! You are a writer with excellent skill, seeming to have performed miracles up to this point. However, with that alone, the hope for greater growth and development may be small. For instance, "Salt" allows us to know the hardships of one person, Pongshik's mother, and through Pongshik's mother, we can also learn a little about the lives of other peasants, but that is depicted rather weakly. Even when depicting the lives of people like Pongshik's mother, we must present Pongshik's mother as one representative of the group behind her, and by doing so, Pongshik's mother will become clearer and more alive.
Simply put, "Salt" is handled too subjectively. There are such parts in "Manuscript Fee 200 Won" as well, and in other works too. (Remainder omitted - original text)

Letter from Mr. Kim Kijin (P'alp'ong)
Wŏlt'an brother!
As I write this letter now, I hope the words I am about to write do not evoke sad memories for you.
Wŏlt'an brother
- At times like this, I sadly realize that our writing has no authority whatsoever. What can I chatter (지꺼려서) to console your sorrow even a little?! I realize once again that death generally transcends the promises of our writing or noisy (呶呶) words. Isn't it naturally right that our writing and our language have no authority over something called Death, which leaps beyond all norms promised with real life? Therefore, I will not write various words here but merely lament my own incompetence. <84>
Wŏlt'an brother
Our literature must depart from here - from defiance against Death, that is, protest against fate, reconsideration of reality. Now, facing this opportunity where you receive a great mental stimulus and blow, I can only pray that your conviction regarding literature becomes even firmer and stronger. Letter from Ms. Kang Kyŏngae
I gladly received your handwritten letter, written on the night of May 11th, today. It was a heavy envelope, so at first, I opened it with some curiosity, but unexpectedly, it was a long letter sent by you, teacher, and I cannot express how happy and glad I was. So I read it again and again, two, three times. (omitted)
The first time I encountered your esteemed name was when your debut work, "Path of Hungry Ghosts" (餓鬼道), won the prize in "Kaejo" (改造). Of course, among those with even a little interest in literature, how many did not admire your honorable prize at that time, and how many did not read "Path of Hungry Ghosts"? At that time, seeing the advertisement for Kaejo <87> in the newspaper, I rushed to buy "Kaejo" and started searching for and reading "Path of Hungry Ghosts" first. However, before I had read even half of it, my chest felt like it was tearing apart, and my head felt as if hit several times with a club (동맹이), leaving me stunned (멍하니) for a while, then I continued reading and reading. Although I haven't read many works, this was the first time I encountered a Korean work with such power (박력) and weight. So for several days, whenever I was bored, I would take it out and read it again and again. My memory of "Path of Hungry Ghosts" is faint now, but scenes like dying after falling from that terrifying cliff while going to the mountains to pick herbs because there was nothing to eat still remain in my head; however, the writing style and structure are completely dark (캄캄하옵니다). I realize it's because I was that young.
After that, I briefly saw "The Pursued Person" (追はるる人) at a Bookstore (冊店) and intended to buy it, but the next day or so when I went to the bookstore to buy it, someone had gotten there first and it was "Cut Out" (切取 - pages likely removed). I returned disappointed, retracing my memory of what I briefly saw at the bookstore. The scene where a village maiden and bachelor played, circling around the well curb (우물귀), greatly stimulated my interest, and I was curious about their future. From then on, I knew that your masterpieces were coming out month after month, but since I came out to this Jiandao (間島 - Kando), I couldn't often access "Kaejo." So while I was wondering, news frequently arrived that your standalone book titled "The Man Called Kwon" (權と云う男) was published, and that it was being translated into Esperanto (에쓰페란트) or Chinese. And I read about 20 or 30 installments of "Rainbow" (무지개) appearing in the Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報), but inevitably couldn't read even that, and when my memory of you was fading, I came to read "Incident on the Night of the Funeral" (葬式の夜の出來事) in "Munye" (文藝). Thus, the only piece I possess until now is this one.
"Incident on the Night of the Funeral"
Upon encountering this work, what immediately came to mind was, "Isn't this a 'model' novel (모텔 소설)?" However, this is merely my speculation... I saw the main purpose of this work as exposing the rotten inner side of feudal figures like Mr. Pak Ch'anggyu (朴昌圭) and old man Yi Changgil (李長吉), while simultaneously implicitly hinting at the ugly backdoor dealings of the bureaucratic class. Taking material from feudal remnants, which are not such a big issue in the late stage of capitalism <88>, likely had relatively little mass appeal. However, I cannot but express respect for the fact that the entire Work (作) is tightly woven without any leaks. And regarding the concise dialogue that reveals the characters' personalities, their every action seems as if seen from the side, and their words seem as if heard directly. For example, Mr. Pak Ch'anggyu holding a beer bottle (삐루병) and laughing boisterously, saying "Kanpai da! Kanpai da!" (乾杯た 乾杯た!), or the personality of Mr. Pak Ch'anggyu being well revealed through the bold and audacious Greeting (人事) when called upon due to old man Yi Changgil's Mother's Death (母喪). Especially regarding the character of Ch'oe Uryŏl (崔愚烈), who consistently acts despicably, traces of your effort, teacher, are visible. And the profound descriptions here and there are truly surprising.
"...I realized it was the room where the corpse was laid, and I was startled..." (『 ... 私はその屍體を安置した室だと知りきよつとした ... 』)
Ordinary writers might not pay attention even in such places. Because the characters in the story have stayed up several nights due to the Funeral (喪事), there's a risk that even the author's emotions might become dull, so generally, it's easy to deaden the emotions of the characters. However, that you, teacher, did not look away even in these minute details and brought the characters to life, I don't know what praise I should offer. In short, it shows how seriously you approached the work when writing it. Also, in another place:
"...Baka (バカ)! I stared at Ch'oe Uryŏl's drunken, monkey-like (猿のヤウな) face and yelled. 'Where are you planning to go sleep instead?'" (「それよやかどこへ行つて寢るつもりだい」)
"Sleep? Haha..." (「寢る? はは ... 」)
Here too, one can see two living human beings.
When an author writes a work, what is needed more than anything is a serious attitude. Therefore, I think one must observe with all one's might and savor with all one's might all the objects one intends to describe and express.
"...Ch'oe Uryŏl threw up the half-dried persimmon (柿奧つ魚 - likely typo for 干し柿 dried persimmon) at me..." (「 ... 崔愚烈は柿奧つ魚を私に吐きつけた ... 」)
Teacher, reading this scene, I smelled Ch'oe Uryŏl's breath. And I instantly felt that his despicableness permeated his very core (五臟 속속들이). If I were to give such examples, there would be no end, so I will stop here.
Finally, causing the fight between old man Yi Changgil's siblings over the corpse <89> to break out, and simultaneously having its cause revealed through the mouth of Mr. Pak Ch'anggyu, tells how skilled you are as a writer. And regarding the writing style, perhaps it's my prejudice, but it seems much smoother and more brilliant than the writing I encountered in "Three Curves" (三曲線) or "Rainbow." I have taken too much of your time.
Please forgive me.
Teacher, didn't you once write something under the Title (題) "My Ambition"? Perhaps... I still remember you saying there that with a little more effort, there's no reason you couldn't follow Balzac (빨자크). That's right - indeed, I believe you will soon follow in the footsteps of the masters (未久の先輩達). Please strive hard, teacher. And please become the Gorky (꼬리키-) of Korea, and thus become a great torch in our lonely literary world.

Teacher, you must be bored. Shall I tell you stories of this Manchuria (滿洲)? Better yet, please overcome all difficulties and come out to Manchuria once. Here, mountain-like piles of raw material are waiting for an elder like you. Please do come. And thus, give birth to an immortal masterpiece.
Finally, wishing for your health, I will close.

Ansŏ brother,

Spring flowers depart. The pig follows, travels afar south of the river, enjoys and forgets to return. Peonies (芍藥) lie on the ground, tree peonies (牧丹) turn to dust. With a thousand gold pieces at the waist, will he pursue the gallants (遊俠) of Xianyang (咸陽 - ancient Chinese capital, here metaphorically Seoul?) in the red dust? Fine ramie blouse (細苧袗), jade-colored skirt (玉色裙), fluttering lightly, fragrant sweat smell steams (汗香氣氳), the person like jade. Straw hat askew (麥帽斜), drunken eyes hazy (醉眼朦朧), steps unsteady (步蹣跚), spirit like a rainbow (氣如虹), passion like a melting furnace (情似鎔鑛爐), the person imposing (軒軒). Is this not perhaps a scene on Chongno street (鍾路街上)? As for the role (脚色), brother, you decide for yourself. Kwang (光 - Ch'unwŏn's given name, referring to himself) is a patient near death. Gasping (喘喘), mumbling (唵唵). Neither able to live, nor able to die. What is on the desk (案頭)? Lotus Sutra (法華), Zhuangzi (南華), Tao Yuanming's poems (淵明詩). What is thought in the heart (心中)? Eating and drinking and losing and drowning (食而飮而失而溺 - basic bodily functions/despair?). Truly a pitiful existence (可憐的存在).
Gratefully received the gift of lilies (百合花), extremely fierce ones comparable to you (君 - referring to Kim Tonghwan?). The sweetness of the loquats (枇杷) anticipates Hwadon's (花豚 - Kim Tonghwan's pen name) sequel (別傳). Unforgettable affection, deeply grateful, deeply grateful (感荷感荷). Please forgive these playful writings (戱筆海恕焉).
June 18th, Sick Younger Brother Ch'unwŏn signing off (拜上) <95>

**Summary**

This article compiles personal letters exchanged between prominent Korean writers, primarily active in the 1920s and 1930s. The letters, addressed to fellow writers like Pak Chonghwa (Wŏlt'an), Kim Tonghwan (Ansŏ/Hwadon), and Kang Kyŏngae, reveal intimate thoughts on personal struggles (illness, loneliness, financial hardship), literary creation (poetic sensibility, realism, influences), critiques of contemporaries and works (including self-critique), aspirations for Korean literature, and reflections on the socio-political environment (references to Manchuria, societal change in Seoul). Writers like Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn), Pak Yŏnghŭi (Hoewŏl), Kim Sowŏl, Hyŏn Chin'gŏn, Yi Sanghwa, Kim Kijin, Pak Hwasŏng, and Chang Hyŏkchu discuss everything from French dolls and philosophy (Nietzsche, Dostoevsky) to specific works, literary movements (Paekcho, T'owŏrhoe), and the role of the writer in society.

From a historical perspective, this collection is valuable for its candid glimpse into the intellectual and emotional lives of key figures shaping modern Korean literature during a period of intense change and colonial pressure. The discussions about literary direction (realism vs. aestheticism, art for art's sake vs. social engagement), the awareness of international literary trends, the explicit mention of Korean writers needing to depict the experiences of Koreans in Manchuria (Kang Kyŏngae by Chang Hyŏkchu), and the palpable sense of anxiety and aspiration provide rich material for understanding the cultural landscape of colonial Korea. Kim Tonghwan's ambivalent portrayal of Seoul's modernization juxtaposed with widespread disillusionment is particularly striking.

인형을 안는 이
웃는 듯 돌아스고 안는 듯 슬어지는 애인의 마음의 멋없음을 느낀 子金이 인형을 안도다. 인형은 잘만 간수하면 그대보다 오래 살아 그대 100세 후에도 再嫁 三嫁 하리로다.
로마인은 우정을 안으려 하엿도다. 인형을 안고나서 우정을 안앗는지는 모르되 그 현인의 글에는 우정을 애정보다도 골육의 정보다도 높이 평가하엿도다. 우정이 변할 때에 덜 아픔을 취함만이 아닐 뜻하도다.
어떤 漢人은 바위에 절하엿다 하도다. 바위는 사랑에 깃버하고 실연에 우는 자- 아님이로다. 바위 부서져 모래 되거니와 그 부서진 알알이 다 바위와 같음이로다. 우정이 부서질 때에 원수 되거니와 사랑이 부서질 때에는 鴆毒이 되더라고 金子- 말하지 아니하시랴는가. 인형이 부서질 때에는 다만 몬지되어 잠간 屋間을 열어 놓으면 다시＜72＞ 깨끗하리로다. 인형을 안는 정이 가장 무독한 듯 하도다.
그러매 聖人은 무릇 사랑하지 말고 구함이 없으라 하섯도다. 우정도 믿지 말고 바위에 절도 하지 말고 인형도 안지 말라 하는 뜻인가 싶도다.
빌려주셧던 인형을 조곰도 섭섭할 것도 감사할 것도 없이 돌려보내여 들이면서.
