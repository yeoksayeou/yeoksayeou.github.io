Before Marriage and After Marriage, The Growth of a Seven-Year Romance
						Kim Yŏngp'al

"Hello there, Tŏksun-ssi!" Having received a request from the editor at Samch'ŏlli Publishing to write engagingly about the time of our courtship and our life after our love marriage, that is, our life today, I readily agreed. This was because I thought it wasn't a particularly difficult request. But oh my—sitting down now to trace back that warm nest of love from bygone days, my life in the meantime has been far too busy. That's why, just for today, I decided to return to the past and use polite language (敬語) with my wife. Accordingly, today I also wanted to try becoming the person I was in that utterly sweet (?) state of love before marriage. When I suddenly started using polite language towards her, acting very formal, she looked up at me sideways with a smile, not knowing what was going on, and didn't answer. Without hesitation, putting on an innocent face, I called out again, "Tŏksun-ssi—". She smiled again and replied dismissively, "Has this person gone mad?"

"Tŏksun-ssi— Heavens, what are you saying? Mad? Ah—me, mad? Please, feel my chest. How can you look at someone whose flames of love are burning like this and say they're mad? Ah-ha—" I tried acting dramatically. But the other party, seeming cool, retorted:
"This isn't quite the No Sŏbang style, nor the Orator (辯士) style, is it?"
Now I had completely lost anything to say. Having no choice, I burst out laughing.
"Oh, forget it— Can't you just reply properly, like in a fun play?"
"Ouch, ouch, ouch, you're pinching my cheek."
"Shh— If you just kept quiet, maybe others wouldn't know.—"
But this too was a failure. If she had responded, I would have tried writing it down, however awkwardly, but since that didn't happen, I had no choice but to write down what I myself thought.—

It might be an overgeneralization to define what kind of person I am broadly, but most people close to me nickname me a talkative person, that is, a chatterbox. And indeed, I don't have the kind of personality that would earn praise for being demure. Simply put, I am dynamic, not static. Nevertheless, when I liked someone of the opposite sex, I sought someone quite the opposite of me. Take the most important requirement, the face, for example: in contrast to my round face, an oval face; contrary to my somewhat plump build, a slender figure; contrary to my rather cheerful personality, a gentle disposition. If I were to list such comparisons, there would be no end, so I'll stop here. In any case, the woman I fell in love with—and who is now my wife—possesses these qualities to some extent, a fact I know better than anyone else. Therefore, during our courtship, our past love involved one side being active while the other was passive; one side being passionate while the other didn't show such expressions. Yet, her simple personality was often made to cry by surrounding circumstances, and she was often disappointed by my sarcastic remarks—things I heard afterwards, of course. Anyway, we nurtured and lived through such a life—crying, rejoicing, whispering, singing, burning with anxiety, being disappointed, cursing, resenting, hating, yet still—for the long, long period of seven years within our romantic life.

Such a person eventually became my wife. Of course, it must have been fated, but for us, it was undoubtedly a matter of great satisfaction.
Nevertheless, to ask if anything has perhaps (?) changed is impolite of the asker. Yet, being human, to say that nothing has changed might, conversely, seem impolite of me. <46> Especially in our life of meager means (업는 살님)—in today's circumstances where not everything goes as planned, where we grow amidst constant disillusionment, there are times when we too feel the disillusionment of love. However, perhaps because it wasn't a "modern style" (모던식) romance, it doesn't seem to be cheapening easily. Moreover, the other party keeps trying to return to the past, striving to become a model of the "wise mother and good wife" (현모양처), so there's no reason for dissatisfaction about love cooling, and of course, the thought of divorce wouldn't presumptuously cross her mind. I am a separate issue, though—.
˜ As one example, just a few years ago, while she might not have approved of drinking, she wasn't fond of it, but recently, even if I drink for half the year, she just looks at me and smiles.
In this way, after marriage, the two of us have become ordinary. Perhaps it's because we've come to love our young child more—. Gone are those sweet, heart-fluttering scenes where, hiding from the eyes of the family in the kitchen (부역 - 廚房), unable to even breathe loudly, we would meet, have nothing to say, just hold hands blankly, stay up all night, and yet long to meet again the very next day... (End) <47>

**Summary**

The author, Kim Yŏngp'al, reflects on his seven-year courtship and subsequent married life. He contrasts the passionate, dramatic, and sometimes tumultuous pre-marital romance with the more mundane, yet stable, reality of marriage. While acknowledging the potential for disillusionment, especially given their modest financial situation, he notes that their love hasn't diminished in value. His wife has adopted a more traditional role, becoming tolerant of his habits (like drinking), and their focus has shifted, perhaps towards their child, replacing the clandestine excitement of courtship with ordinary domesticity. From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into changing social norms around love marriage (연애결혼) versus arranged marriage in 1930s Korea, gender expectations (the ideal of 현모양처), the realities of daily life and economic hardship ("업는 살님"), and personal reflections on the evolution of romantic relationships within the colonial context. The comparison to "modern style" romance and the reference to specific conversational "styles" (No Sŏbang style, Orator style) also provide glimpses into contemporary cultural references.
