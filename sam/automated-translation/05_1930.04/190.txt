Publication Date: 1930년 04월 01일
Article Title: 아리랑 (Arirang - Korean Folk Song)
Author: 파인(謠), 석영(畵) (P'ain (Song), Sŏgyŏng (Illustration))
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Arirang
						P'ain (Song), Sŏgyŏng (Illustration)
Arirang Arirang Arariyo
Crossing over Arirang Pass (Refrain repeated below)

Does the Haet'ae extinguish the burning fire in the capital Seoul?
Who can extinguish the burning flames within this heart?

Within this speechless heart are three *dongi* of blood
Though blood cannot speak, fists surely can.

Don't sneer because I wear torn clothes
Do you think the world you live in is sound?<53>

**Summary**

This poem uses the structure of the famous folk song "Arirang" to convey deep sorrow, suppressed anger, and defiance. The poet contrasts the uncontrollable fire within their heart with the fires of Seoul, questioning who can quell such inner turmoil. It speaks of unspoken suffering accumulating like "three *dongi* of blood," hinting that while words fail, action ("fists") remains possible. The final stanza challenges societal judgment based on appearances, questioning the integrity of the critic's own world. For historians, this piece is significant as it reflects the intense suffering and simmering resentment felt by Koreans under colonial rule, using a traditional and beloved folk song format to express potentially subversive feelings of injustice and a longing for agency. The reference to the Haet'ae, a traditional guardian symbol, failing to quell the fires might subtly critique the powerlessness or indifference of authority figures towards the people's plight.

아리랑

아리랑 아리랑 아라리요
아리랑 고개를 넘어간다(以下 仝)
서울 장안 붓는 불은 해태가 끄나
이 가슴 속 붓는 불길 누가 꺼

말 못하는 가슴 속에 피가 세 동이
피-는 말 못해도 주먹은 하겟지

찌저진 옷 입엇다 비웃지마라
네 사는 세상은 성한 줄 아나＜53＞
