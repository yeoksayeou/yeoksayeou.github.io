Magazine Title: 삼천리 제5호
Publication Date: 1930년 04월 01일
Article Title: 産兒制限의 絶呌!!, 醫學上 四大方法 (The Desperate Cry for Birth Control!!, Four Major Medical Methods)
Author: 醫學搏士 鄭錫泰 (Doctor of Medicine, Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

Reporter: Considering our current reality, is birth control necessary? I hear that in Japan and other countries, it is already being openly implemented.

Chŏng: Of course, it is necessary. Firstly, due to the economic problems of the Korean people, and secondly, for the prosperity of superior lineage, that is, from the perspective of eugenics (優生學), it is necessary.

Reporter: Although regulating childbirth artificially in Chosŏn might seem laughable, I hear that many foreign countries have already entered the implementation phase. As you have traveled abroad for many years, what is the attitude of foreign women towards this?

Chŏng: Among the foreign countries I have visited, France was where birth control was most prevalent. When entering hotels in Paris (巴里) or other cities in France, next to the women's bedrooms, there is usually a square frame made of white ceramic <30> set up. At first, I wondered what it was, perhaps a place to urinate, but I found out that after sexual intercourse [性X], women sit on it, turn on a switch, and wash their private parts with a spray of water. It goes without saying that this is an act to avoid pregnancy (懹姙). It was a form of birth prevention. If such facilities are openly available and encouraged in places like hotels visible to all, can't we easily imagine how universally it must be practiced in homes?

Reporter: Is washing with cold water truly effective? I thought the method required complicated machinery and expensive chemicals that were difficult to use. If washing with cold water is effective, couldn't not only wealthy women but anyone practice it discreetly?

Chŏng: Of course. From a medical perspective, there are generally four methods for regulating childbirth.

Reporter: Yes, the first one?

Chŏng: The first is using medication. However, as current regulations do not permit this one, I will refrain from explaining it in detail here. But the principle is like this. (Explaining with a diagram) When this one goes like this, that one is made like this (Omitted)... alkaline property is acidic... (Abbreviated) <31>.

Reporter: That method seems difficult before the regulations are revised. And explaining it is rather explicit... Let's leave that for now. What about the second method?

Chŏng: The second method involves using devices. First, there's using the well-known 'ru-de sakku' (루-데삭구), and second is the washing method (洗滌法). The 'ru-de sakku' is widely used for preventing venereal diseases, so there's no need for a detailed explanation here, but people tend to dislike using it due to the risk of rupture during use and its impact on sensation. Second, washing – this is probably the simplest and most universally applicable method. The method used by French women, as mentioned earlier, falls into this category.

Reporter: I still find it hard to believe that cold water is effective as you say. Could you please explain it once more from a scientific perspective?

Chŏng: It's like this. Let me explain how women conceive – using chemistry. In chemistry, when an alkaline substance (아루가리性) and an acidic substance (酸性) come into contact, doesn't a neutralization reaction occur? If a neutralization reaction (Omitted) occurs for the man's, the sperm [벌네 - lit. worm/bug] dies. For reference, 'alkaline property' refers to things like lye (caustic alkali - 苛性아루까리), and 'acidic property' refers to sour things, like vinegar, fruit, or Japanese umeboshi. Spermatozoa (精虫) can only live perfectly <32> in either an alkaline or acidic independent state. Once the two combine, they neutralize, and the sperm's motility is broken. Therefore, even if the sperm [벌네] enters the ovary (卵巢) [Translator's Note: Likely referring to the female reproductive tract or proximity to the egg, not the ovary itself], pregnancy does not occur. What's important to note is that water is neutral. When water enters, the sperm inevitably loses its power to cause conception.

Reporter: Yes. Although a layman cannot fully understand, it sounds plausible. But the cold water washing method seems quite bothersome to do every single time... What about the next, the third method?

Chŏng: It's the physical method (理學的 方法). This includes, first, the method of applying X-rays, and second, the surgical method. However, exposing oneself to X-rays even once doesn't just limit childbirth; it makes pregnancy (懹姙) completely impossible, so it's permanent birth prevention. Surgery is also simple in its method, but it too makes having children permanently impossible, so its meaning is slightly different from limitation. For both of these, today's medical law stipulates that the procedure cannot be performed without the presence of two or more physicians.

Reporter: Then please tell us about the final, fourth method.

Chŏng: That can be called the theoretical method (學理的 方法). This is a truth discovered through the many years of experience of the world's famous medical scholars, so it is undoubtedly correct. It is a method of avoiding pregnancy (懹姙) <33> based on the timing of menstruation and sexual activity. It is scientifically determined which days each month sexual activity should be avoided. For example, let's say it's a long month. And let's assume a woman has her period on January 16th of that month (let's assume it's January). The period usually lasts until around the 19th. At that time, from the next day, the 20th, until the 24th, having sexual intercourse makes it easy to conceive. Then, from the day after that, the 25th, until the 1st of the next month, that is, February 1st, if sexual intercourse occurs, pregnancy will definitely result. Then, between February 2nd and 15th, it is absolutely impossible to conceive.

Reporter: What about a short month?

Chŏng: Let's assume February for ease of explaining a short month. If menstruation occurs on February 20th, it usually lasts until the 23rd. Between the 24th and 28th, as before, there is sometimes a possibility of pregnancy (懹姙), and from the 29th to March 6th, it will definitely result in pregnancy. Then, from March 7th to the 19th, it is absolutely impossible. Other specific details can be answered by consulting a physician at the time.

Reporter: Have many people succeeded using these four methods?

Chŏng: Of course, one can say there are many. However, I will refrain from pointing out specific individuals. Based on what I saw abroad, there were significantly more. <34>

Reporter: But what happens in the case where one used one of the above four methods, believing contraception was successful, but against all odds, pregnancy occurs?

Chŏng: In that case, one would have to have an abortion (墮胎). However, the law strictly prohibits it, so it absolutely cannot be done. It is especially important to state that abortion and birth control are entirely different issues, so please, please do not confuse them. Do not misinterpret these words.

Reporter: Nevertheless, looking at the judgment of the Tokyo (東京) Great Court of Cassation, it was said that even if one is pregnant, if it is only within 3 or 4 months and has not formed the shape of an infant but remains just a blood clot, discarding it is not a crime.

Chŏng: I believe that is absolutely not the case. Once fertilization occurs and the process begins, it is considered conception.

Reporter: By the way, if one uses the four methods mentioned earlier, what is the effect on the mother's health? Could there be body weakness or any physical discomfort?

Chŏng: Absolutely not. (Continued below) <35>

**Summary**

This 1930 interview with Dr. Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae discusses the perceived necessity of birth control (産兒制限) in colonial Korea, primarily for economic reasons and eugenics ("superior lineage"). Dr. Chŏng details four methods: medication (legally restricted), mechanical means (condoms - 'ru-de sakku', and douching, citing French practices), physical methods (X-rays and surgery for permanent sterilization, requiring multiple doctors), and the rhythm method (calculating fertile/infertile days based on the menstrual cycle). He emphasizes that these contraceptive methods are safe for women's health and distinct from abortion (墮胎), which is illegal, despite citing a supposed (and likely misunderstood or misrepresented) court ruling regarding early-term termination. Historically, the article is significant as it reveals open discussion about contraception, influenced by Western practices (like France), within the context of colonial modernity in Korea. It starkly highlights the intertwining of economic concerns, public health discourse, and the problematic ideology of eugenics (우생학) prevalent during that era, reflecting anxieties about population quality and national strength under colonial rule.
