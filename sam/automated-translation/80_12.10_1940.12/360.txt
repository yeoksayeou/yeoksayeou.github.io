Publication Date: 1940년 12월 01일
Article Title: 「아름다운 季節」 (Beautiful Season)
Author: 金東煥
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Beautiful Season
						Kim Tonghwan (金東煥)

At the head of the vineyard path
Not just one cluster, but several clusters of black grapes, wet with dew,
Wrapped in two or three layers of green grape leaves still attached to the end of the same vine,
Held carefully in both hands so that not even one grape bursts,
Standing quietly there at the head of the long-furrowed vineyard—
It feels as if the forgotten person of old, pulling along those days, might appear just as they were,
So I watch intently this evening twilight path.

Like the grape clusters that bud, stretch branches, and bear fruit whenever forgotten,
This heart, wishing to forget, finds that shadow stretching towards it again,
Making me pace restlessly when the season for ripening grapes arrives.
But all those past seasons were beautiful,
So I gaze upon them, wishing they would remain forever with grape clusters hanging <224>
Even if the snowy season comes, if only the grape clusters remain,
I would pass the time singing by this vineyard path, longing for the person of old.

Spring (泉)

According to the traveler coming over the mountain, over the pass,
Over the mountain, over the pass, there in the distant forest,
A clear spring, which no one drinks, flows brimmingly, they say.
Even when the *Sambok* (三伏) season comes, when rivers and streams all dry up,
From the rocky ridge in the forest, only that water wells up and flows endlessly, they say.
Gathering all the dozen or so maidens of this village,
Carrying water jars on their heads, we run over the mountain, over the pass.
Even if, while going, the water dries up and the spring site becomes a sandy plain,
Won't the first one or two jars to arrive be filled to the brim with spring water?

Because they say the spring water flows shouting over the mountain, over the pass,
Today too, like yesterday, we go carrying water jars on our heads, over the mountain, over the pass.

Longing

Like a willow leaf in full bloom whipping the paper window in a gust of wind <225>
If only I could have glanced past indifferently, just once.
Like a pear blossom bursting forth beautifully on a summer day,
If only I could have laughed tenderly, just once.

Even where no one sees is fine,
Before you, like this...

Silkworm (누베)

Just as the tough, coarse mulberry leaf, touched by a cute mouth, enters,
And comes back out unraveled as soft, lustrous silk thread,
This frustrating, sorrowful heart, too, if it brushes your chest just once,
Blooms into fond memories and fragrant impressions.
Like the silkworm that swallows green leaves and spins out white threads,
May your always gentle heart gather up my sorrow.

Cocoon (분두지)

Melting its own flesh to make thread and frame its dwelling,
Did a hateful one lead it there to make a tomb and bury it?
No, it carefully confined and sealed itself, unable to bear harming another. <226>
But not long after, heaven gave it wings
And let it fly in the third month of spring; look favorably upon that heart.
Because it is a body like the cocoon, burying sorrow back into its own breast,
Looking to tomorrow, I too immerse this body in the "tomb of sorrow."

Carrying a Birdcage on my Back (새장 둘너메고)

Sparrows chirp on dew-wet branches,
A calf wanders in a field of lush grass,
The morning sun rises into a sky without a wrinkle,
And a cool August wind slides down into the mountain valley.
When all things find their place and enjoy their allotted blessings,
Shall I too sling a birdcage (chicken coop - 鷄櫃) over my back and go to the market?
Thinking of the words of that child who asked for a hair ribbon for the Ch'usŏk holiday.

Spring (샘)

Because it's spring water barely welling up over a rocky ridge,
Not even a handful,
It subsides before it can even reach the sea.
Yet, even where it flowed, <227>
Grass seeds sprout, flowers bloom,
The hard ground splits open, and spring mist unravels from within.
My devotion is also like that spring water,
Wishing to moisten the dry garden at least as far as it goes.

Gentle Touch (부드러운 손길)

Even a drop of worthless water pooled in an ox footprint by the roadside,
Rises to the sky in the sunlight, gathers into clouds, flies away,
And becomes a drop of rain that makes daffodils bloom in a deep lake behind the back fence.
Though I am a body wandering lonely along streets with low eaves,
If the right wind blows, someday it will reach out as a welcoming, stroking hand.

Spirit (넋시)

The flowers blooming in Nyŏngbyŏn (寧邊) are red azaleas.
Fearing they'll be trodden underfoot, they don't bloom on the mountainside path,
Will they fly far away? Even on windy days, they bow their heads.
Blooming high, then gently falling in their own place,
The azaleas of Nyŏngbyŏn—
They must be the spirit of that Sŏdo maiden who draws my heart. <228>

After Parting (別后)

The moment sorrow ends, the clinging fire goes out,
On the path reaching the sandy field,
The stream's course subsides and ends.
However, though sorrow is done and the stream has dried, no matter how much it burns, it doesn't know how to burn completely,
And no matter how much it subsides, it doesn't know how to subside completely—
This unforgettable heart after you left.

At the End of This Road (이길 끝간데에)

Though unseen, somewhere a gust of wind passes,
Making the poplar trees along the road shudder like that.
And because clouds carrying rain pass even in a corner of the distant sky,
The banana leaves are dyed blue-green like that.
Perhaps even at the very end of this street, some woman resides,
As inexplicable joy overflows like this in my body setting out on the twilight path.

To Miss D (D孃에게)

Knowing we must part where time comes and the road ends, <229>
These steps turn back, unable to bear it.
Even if you go forever and that beautiful body turns to ashes (骨灰),
Don't yesterday's voice and image remain for me?
Knowing this, yet even this evening when time comes and the road ends,
This heart, this footstep, turns back again, unable to forget.

Narrow Path (오솔길)

The lonely narrow path, half-bound in darkness,
Pulls the evening traveler towards the village.
The narrow path gently herds the weary travelers,
Who wandered from mountain to mountain, towards the village, towards the village.
Joining them, let's spend the night at the village tavern, you and I,
Remembering how well we walked on nights like this in boyhood, even without fireflies.

Letter Sent on the Autumn Wind (秋風에 부치는 便紙)

Dancing gently even without wind,
Spreading beautiful green leaves even without flowers blooming,
Like the poplar tree that gracefully adorns July and August.
Though standing by the river, even if transplanted to a waterless sandy plain, <230>
Living then by drinking the wind not through roots but through leaves,
Like the poplar tree that gracefully adorns the *Sambok* summer days.
Even if its bark is peeled off by a passing shepherd boy's sickle,
Its own sap flows, becomes bark again, embraces its own tree, and lives,
Like that poplar tree enduring the heat of July and August.

Since you are the one who loses this and receives that, "Live life like this,"
This is a passage from the letter I sincerely send on the autumn wind.

To Ms. B (B女士에게)

As grass grows on a rocky mountain,
As fire catches in the green plants of May and June,
With such burning passion;

Like a single wild rose blooming on the Nakdong River's sandy shore,
Blooming amidst snowdrifts, bearing fruit even within them,
With such tenacious spirit, I wish to love you. <231>

Fallen Leaves (落葉)

Spread out, buried, rotting,
Lying as fertilizer deep beneath the distant earth,
Gently receiving in my breast the grains of grass seed that might fall, blown by the wind,
Nurturing them to ripeness with all my essence,
And sending them back out to the surface as new sprouts—I am the fallen leaf in the rotting earth.

When the sprout emerges, moistened by the earth's energy, sprouting cotyledons,
When those cotyledons grow again to a height of three feet,
And a single, beautiful, bright red flower blooms splendidly atop them,
Intoxicated by that joy, I wish to be buried deeper and rot more,
The fallen leaf of late autumn turning into fertilizer.

The more I rot, the richer the fertilizer I become,
Producing large, brilliant flowers—I am the fallen leaf, spread out, buried, rotting. <232>

**Summary**

This collection of poems by Kim Tonghwan, titled "Beautiful Season," explores themes of nature's cycles, memory, longing, resilience, and quiet devotion through evocative imagery. The poems frequently use natural elements—grapes, springs, silkworms, trees, fallen leaves—as metaphors for human emotions and experiences like enduring sorrow, the persistence of memory, unwavering love, and the generative power found even in decay and seeming insignificance. The overall tone is lyrical, melancholic yet often imbued with a sense of quiet strength and hope found in observation and connection to the natural world or enduring human bonds. From a historical perspective, the poems, published in 1940 during a harsh period of Japanese colonial rule, are interesting for their apparent focus on personal emotion and natural imagery, largely avoiding direct political commentary. This could be interpreted as a form of inner resistance or escapism, finding solace and expressing Korean sentiment through timeless themes rather than overt nationalism, which would have been censored. The emphasis on endurance (poplar tree, spring, cocoon) and the cycle of decay and rebirth (fallen leaves) might subtly reflect the desired persistence of the Korean spirit amidst difficult times.
