Actor's Notes, A Compilation from Chosŏn's Representative Theatre Troupe
Cheongchun-jwa: Han Ilsong, Pak Okch'o, Pok Wŏn'gyu, Kang Nosŏk

*Im Kkŏkchŏng*, etc.

By Han Ilsong of Cheongchun-jwa

For the past eight years, the Cheongchun-jwa theatre troupe has literally struggled like an orphan growing up, and now we mark the first anniversary of our renewal. Looking back now, it truly was a year spent traversing a thorny path, filled with sorrow and anxiety. In other words, as a target of mistreatment, we were mocked and frowned upon while dragging a heavy burden over a treacherous pass. However, reaching our destination was all too short-lived. Faced with this reality, I know all too well that if our actions now lose the resolve to make a fresh start, all our efforts thus far will have been in vain.
Allow me then, through these pages, to write a few words about the anxious feelings of the past year. <193> As you, dear readers, well know, Cheongchun-jwa received much love over the previous four years and was the reigning champion in the commercial theatre world.
Even for the mighty Cheongchun-jwa, however, a great storm arrived in early September last year. The single entity that was Cheongchun-jwa faced the unfortunate fate of being shattered into pieces, scattered to the four winds. Core actors each went their separate ways, while I devoted all my energy to gathering the remaining few colleagues to restore this fragmented entity. Eventually, we shook hands with unfamiliar comrades gathering from here and there, shouldered the banner of "renewal," and made a fresh start. However, our theatre, unable to stand firmly, inevitably stagnated. Truly, the past year held unspoken sorrows, tears, and sighs emerging from aching hearts.
But we considered this a temporary difficult passage, and with tears streaming, we stood up, clenched our fists, gritted our teeth, driven by the sole desire to contribute our meager strength to pioneering the great path of Chosŏn theatre.
We focused intensely on solidifying the willful unity of our colleagues and strengthening our internal structure.
We have now become one entity again. And each of us possesses the resolute strength to push through any turbulence or obstacles for the improvement of theatre culture and the establishment of a true national theatre.
Therefore, to express the powerful force of our union, we are currently examining genuine research into the dramatic arts and practical strategies.
When we consider that theatre is our life and the stage is our secure refuge, our mission feels immensely significant.
How can we best bring the characters in a play to life and breathe together with the audience?
This is the immediate task facing us actors, and also a subject that we performers must sincerely research for the advancement of theatre culture. <194>
In the past, our theatrical activities lacked any clear direction. We were merely laborers who received monthly wages from Mr. Kim T'ae-yun, the manager of Tonggŭk (東劇), and moved our muscles mechanically. However, when the young entrepreneur Mr. Kim T'ae-yun realized Cheongchun-jwa was becoming a focal point of popularity during provincial tours, he encouraged us to strive harder. We then conscientiously worked to explore new frontiers.
Thus, we staged teacher Yi Kwang-su's (李光洙) *Yujŏng* (有情), the biography of a patriot *Kim Okkyun* (金玉均), and teacher Hong Pyŏkch'o's (洪碧初) *Im Kkŏkchŏn-jŏn* (林巨傳), adapted by Yi Unbang (李雲芳).
Then, in the latter half of last year, we secretly planned the major undertaking of staging teacher Yi Kwang-su's masterpiece, *That Woman's Life* (그 여자의 일생), and commissioned Pak Song (朴松), active in the new theatre movement, to adapt it. However, when the script was completed a month later and we held a reading, the Kŭktan Kohyŏp (劇團高協) troupe was already performing *That Woman's Life* in the southern provinces. We, who had been preparing with a central performance in mind, had no choice but to yield for Kohyŏp. Of course, if we had made a commercial calculation at that time, Cheongchun-jwa would have performed *That Woman's Life* before Kohyŏp's central performance. However, Cheongchun-jwa's stance was to applaud the genuine efforts of our fellow theatre colleagues rather than prioritize significant profits. Recognizing that Cheongchun-jwa's plan had been sound, we sent a congratulatory telegram to our colleagues at the Kohyŏp troupe.
In the future, we will continue to devote ourselves wholeheartedly to staging plays demanded by the public. We will discard the commercialistic old ways of the past and exert our utmost efforts for the development of theatre culture.
Thus, we intend to breathe together with the populace, gladly receive their guidance and encouragement, and dedicate all our strength, heart, and sincerity to the future of Chosŏn theatre.
Therefore, whether our theatrical activities proceed smoothly or encounter turmoil, I myself am resolved to firmly join hands with the veteran actor Pyŏn Kijong (卞基鐘) and lead Cheongchun-jwa to the very end.
Under a new slogan, and also considering the renewed plans of manager Kim T'ae-yun, Cheongchun-jwa will devote all its energy to preserving the prominence, however slight, it has already established in the Chosŏn theatre scene.
Therefore, our future theatrical activities will be the practical implementation emerging from the ideals of the entire Cheongchun-jwa troupe.
However, it is truly questionable to what extent we can realize our ideals this year.
Since we ourselves are actors, it is impossible to write scripts ourselves, so we must rely on the help of playwrights.
But if playwrights fail to write works adapted to the times, our future plans and ambitions cannot be fulfilled. No, rather than being unachievable, it will require immense effort.
Therefore, if Cheongchun-jwa shows no qualitative improvement in the future, it will serve as proof exposing the incompetence of the literary department, and simultaneously become an issue of manager Kim T'ae-yun's capability <195> as head of planning.
However, if the audience finds no interest in the theatre, it will become a matter of responsibility for us performers. Thus, we will dedicate our entire heart and energy to portraying the characters in the plays. In any case, as one theatre person, I wish to hold onto the hope that starting from the Kōki year 2601 (1941), our envisioned Cheongchun-jwa will operate smoothly. <196>

Stage Recollections

By Pak Okch'o of Cheongchun-jwa

Greeting the year 2601 (1941) reminds me of eight years ago when I first started in a theatre troupe, debuting on the new stage at Tansŏngsa (團成社) in the role of a maid in Yi Unbang's (李雲芳作) *Madame X*. I recall how much I liked and yet feared appearing on stage back then, how my heart pounded, and how I couldn't even sleep, engrossed in the role. When the performance day arrived and I stepped onto the stage for the first time, the audience seemed like twinkling stars in a dark cloud, and I couldn't remember what I was saying at all. As time passed, during intermissions, sketches (스켓취) or music were inserted between acts, which added interest for the audience. I also appeared during these intermissions, singing solos and performing in sketches, becoming somewhat indispensable to the troupe. When recruiters came from the Mainland (內地 - Japan) looking for members for a Chosŏn opera troupe, I inwardly resolved to go to the Mainland, diligently learn operatic arts, and hopefully start a girls' opera troupe back in my hometown. For two years, I endured all kinds of hardships in Tokyo (東京), studying male roles, while another friend learned dance and song. After two years of such hardship and study, seven of us friends returned home five years ago this spring, in March, with the ambition of starting our own girls' opera troupe. Upon arriving in Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul), several teachers helped us, finding us a house with an apple orchard at the foot of Pukhan Mountain (北漢山). There, we organized an all-girls musical theatre troupe called Nangnang-jwa (娘娘座). At that time, it was truly a gathering of about twenty innocent young girls. We ate simple meals with plain side dishes sweetly and practiced with all our might for over 40 days. We then eagerly held our first performance at the Tongyang Theater (東洋劇場), pouring out our passion to showcase something new. It was March turning into April then; forsythia and azaleas were in full bloom in every valley of Pukhan Mountain. The back garden, front garden of our practice house, and even the distant villages were flower fields, all in full bloom. It was a world solely for us girls. Buried at the foot of the quiet mountain, accompanied by the natural music of trickling streams and the morning songs of various birds coming and going, we sang, danced, and played, practicing daily on nature's flowery stage. Then, the splendid performance day arrived. <196> The day we had counted down to and waited for had come. Preparations were complete, and the day to show off our pride was upon us. The day before the performance, leaving Pukhan Mountain behind, we moved into group lodging in Kyŏngsŏng city. At that moment, recalling my stage debut, my eyes grew hot with tears unnoticed. Those dreamlike days passed, and after the performance, my friends scattered one by one. The untrustworthy hearts of these friends were incredibly resentful. However, touring the provinces, I experienced many more hardships than expected, and naturally, I grew weary. Thinking about it, I felt it was perhaps too early for a girls' opera troupe in Chosŏn. Feeling quite desolate yet disappointed in our group, I firmly decided to make a fresh start and joined the Hwahwasŏn (豪華船) troupe, directly affiliated with the Tongyang Theater. In Hwahwasŏn, I played almost every type of role imaginable: old women, young women, maids, vamps. Currently, I perform with Cheongchun-jwa, but every spring, these old memories resurface. Yet, I know well that I shouldn't just dwell on past memories. Starting this spring, I intend to strive even harder. Personally, I have never complained about any role assigned to me thus far. Perhaps because of that, whatever role I am given, I feel I haven't messed it up too badly. But I am absolutely not satisfied with this. Thinking about it now, theatre is so difficult; I become increasingly dissatisfied with my own acting, realizing the boundless difficulty of theatre. When playing an old woman, I've intently watched the gestures and movements of elderly people walking down the street or inside streetcars. When playing a young woman, being young myself, I find it hard to pinpoint flaws and strengths. From now on, I plan to focus on studying and learning young women's roles. Having been an actress for eight years now, I am resolved that if being an actress is my destiny, I will collapse on the stage on the day I fall. They say the stage is the mirror of life, so I resolve to live, reflecting and refining myself in life's mirror. Lastly, as I am alone, I ask for your abundant support. <197>

Travel Journal

By Pok Wŏn'gyu of Cheongchun-jwa

Being asked to write a travel diary, I hesitate, unsure which region's experiences to depict, as we undertake such incredibly extensive travels. Let me try to draw the hardship side! When it comes to provincial tours, my heart sinks. Why? Firstly, you have to take the train. I'm so utterly sick of trains that I can hardly stand them; I've been worn out by trains. For 12 years now, I've considered the train a corner of my home and have grown old in train compartments. So, wouldn't I be fed up with trains? Secondly, the inns. At inns, can you expect decent food or truly comfortable bedding? <197> It's bedding that countless men cover themselves with day after day, and surely teeming with invisible bugs crawling around. I consider myself lucky that my flesh hasn't wasted away after being bitten by inn bugs for 12 years. Spending spring, summer, autumn, winter all the same as a traveler, I've journeyed through Chosŏn (朝鮮), Manchukuo (滿洲), and the Mainland (內地 - Japan), treating everywhere, east, west, south, north, as my hometown for the day, and the compatriots in each place as my parents and siblings, losing track of time during these long 12 years as a companion of the road. Having wandered like this, there were daily lives, local characters, all sorts of bizarre and strange occurrences; tears, joys, laughter, happy encounters, frightening things, dirty things – I experienced a myriad of things. But there is one place I pass through in a sort of haze: Manchukuo. Whenever I enter Manchukuo, I somehow feel energized, yet my spirit becomes somewhat rougher. For some reason, whether it's the rough water, the rough soil, or the rough temperament of the people, an indefinable atmosphere stirs my energy and roughens my spirit. Because of this, pretending to know things I don't, the sight of me dealing with Chinese people (支那人) like a half-mute must be quite unbecoming. Traveling by train, if luckily the compartment isn't full and there are many empty seats, one can sleep comfortably regardless of distance. But unfortunately, when the seats are full, with no place to sit, let alone stand, it's extremely difficult. It might be bearable during the day (晝), but when traveling at night, being human and not wood or stone, one's eyes do get heavy. When they do, one must cast aside decorum and find a place to sleep. Going to the conductor's room to plead, spreading paper at the entrance of the restroom or compartment to sleep – among these, the most comfortable sleep was climbing onto the luggage rack. This is for me, a man; for women, travel must be even more difficult. At night in the inn, the even lonelier guest room... clothes taken off become a wardrobe, a desk, substitutes for pots (솟), pans, dishes, household items. Gazing at them, forming a household, sometimes I fall asleep. Sometimes, wrestling with the silently moving air, the ceiling, and the smooth walls before falling asleep... staring at the electric light, enveloped in past fantasies, I draw pictures of beautiful celestial maidens, converse with them, fall in love with them, engaging in all sorts of fantasies before collapsing into a dream world as if it were reality, passing the day. Occasionally, there's a teahouse (茶房) where I get lost for a while, enveloped in that 'sentimental' (『쎈치』한) record music, cigarette smoke, and tea steam. Sometimes I climb a mountain for no reason and just shout at the top of my lungs. Sometimes, mingling with local friends, lost in drunken revelry, I kill the day dreaming of beautiful women. Travel for a truly spiritual theatre troupe should be like this, and I believe there wouldn't be much else beyond this. Thinking again, under the new system (新體制), there are many things that should be recorded in a travel diary. Waking up early to visit a Shrine (神社) and pray! Ceremonies (祝禮) thinking of the Imperial soldiers fighting in the Holy War (聖戰), silent prayers (黙禱), the Pledge of Imperial Subjects (皇國臣民の誓詞) – besides these, one must perform duties related to the new system. Wandering east, west, south, north like this, I've aged half my life in trains and inns. My travel diary is rambling... it seems my responsibility in travel has been simply to make people laugh as a digestive aid; I've spent half my life this way. <198>

Record of Moving North and South

By Kang Nosŏk of Cheongchun-jwa

Month X, Day X
Exhausted from the long train ride, we arrived at the inn and took a common nap.
This is Mokp'o (木浦). It's a place I never tire of visiting, and it always leaves a deep impression whenever I come. Before I fully awoke, an unexpected call came from friends.
They were Yi Hwasam (李化三君) and Pak Tong (朴東君). We arranged to meet at the Paekkungh tabang (White Palace Teahouse - 白宮다방) around 2 PM.
Meeting friends unexpectedly in an unfamiliar place after a long time, we spent a short while reminiscing. Although Mokp'o is the hometown of both Yi and Pak, meeting them, who mostly live elsewhere, in Mokp'o was truly coincidental.
We finished with brief words at the teahouse, promising to meet again after the performance, and parted ways. After the play finished, I met them again with Han Ilsong (韓一松君) and we had drinks. Yi suggested going somewhere a bit more exotic (엑소틱한 곳), so we went to Kanggyŏngok (江景屋), famous in Mokp'o.
The conversation here was very beneficial, containing discussions indispensable for our future. Yi pledged strong spiritual support (後援) for our future theatrical activities and promised practical assistance (助力) as well. After Ilsong passionately spoke about the future direction of our Cheongchun-jwa, the gathering ended on a pleasant note.
The next day was Kwangju (光州)! Waking up in the morning, I climbed Yudalsan (儒達山) mountain to look down upon the impressive Mokp'o, breathing the fresh air, and laughed out loud. It was a laugh born from immense pleasure mixed with the sorrow of parting.

Month X, Day XX
This is Sŏnch'ŏn (宣川), near the border.
The weather is extremely cold. Our stay here coincides with the last day of the year. Perhaps because the comrades had pledged to make this last day memorable by performing with more effort, a different 'plan' (『풀랜』), and fervor than the previous day, everyone seemed to possess a passion unseen before (though it's not that they lacked passion on other days). During an intermission, I went out to give the interval greeting. I analyzed Cheongchun-jwa's past, present, and future, emphasizing not just my personal view but Cheongchun-jwa's 'slogan' (『슬로간』) regarding our theatre world, which is in a transitional period. At the end of my impassioned speech, the Sŏnch'ŏn audience became very excited and responded very favorably. After the play concluded, we comrades gathered, regardless of rank, to meaningfully commemorate the passing year and celebrate the coming new year. We drank, shared individual opinions about the past and future, held hands firmly, sang songs, and spent a truly joyful night together, bidding farewell to the significant Kōki year 2600 (1940).
Even thinking back now, it was a very joyful day.

Month X, Day XX
The day after Sŏnch'ŏn.
This is Sinŭiju (新義州).
I was filled with the joy of being able to meet Mr. An (安君), who had persevered with strong will from the central stage all the way to the Hyŏptong Yesuljwa (協同藝術座) troupe, <199> and was now working in the Arts and Culture Department (學藝部) of the Amnokkang Daily News (鴨江日報).
My guess was correct; I met Mr. An. We crossed into Antung (安東) and, immersed in the unique atmosphere of China (中國), began our conversation.
Previously, I had seen a year-end review written by a certain director/critic in the arts section of some newspaper, and I became extremely agitated.
Why? Because they had written about the two troupes affiliated with Tonggŭk (東劇) without understanding our internal situation or our firm resolve for the future, offering an evaluation that was externally or objectively far too dismissive (等閑).
Mr. An had also read this review and questioned me quite doubtfully.
I countered passionately.
Of course, Tonggŭk was originally organized around 'stars' (『스타-』), and it has already been a year since its renewal last year. During this year, under a capitalist, amidst constant anxiety (戰戰兢兢), we nevertheless remained solely faithful to theatre. And we have been fighting tooth and nail (惡戰苦鬪) to resolve the problems that have been our destiny for many years, and just at this moment when we are about to put our efforts into practice in the near future (不遠間), it felt very unpleasant. Furthermore, we did not want to be satisfied merely with theory completely detached from practice, or with propaganda (宣傳) far removed from our actual abilities. Of course, the influence of the times cannot be denied – but relying solely on exaggeration after exaggeration like some XX will inevitably lead to unfavorable outcomes, we predict (豫感). And I stated that we utterly reject (不認) Mr. So-and-so's words and will strive for sincere practice.
Of course, we too would like to present ourselves splendidly with direction, stage design, and publicity from people with different colors, but we are not satisfied with just that.
We intend to reform ourselves fundamentally, starting from the content (內容).
So I told Mr. An.
As Mr. An resonated (共鳴) with my words, I was filled with the joy of having found a friend (벗).
Sinŭiju! The 40-day tour (巡業) has ended, making me even happier.
-The End- <200>

**Summary**

This collection of four short memoirs by actors from the Cheongchun-jwa troupe in 1941 offers a glimpse into the struggles and aspirations of Korean theatre performers during the late colonial period. Han Ilsong recounts the troupe's near collapse, renewal efforts, artistic goals (like staging works by Yi Kwang-su), and the tension between artistic integrity and commercial pressures under manager Kim T'ae-yun. Pak Okch'o nostalgically recalls her debut, the brief existence of the all-female Nangnang-jwa troupe, and her dedication to mastering diverse roles despite hardships. Pok Wŏn'gyu vividly describes the grueling life of touring actors, particularly the discomforts of train travel and lodging, experiences in Manchukuo, and the imposition of "New System" rituals like shrine visits and pledges. Kang Nosŏk details meaningful encounters with colleagues and supporters in Mokp'o and Sinŭiju/Antung, defending the troupe against dismissive criticism and reaffirming their commitment to substantive theatrical reform.

Historians might find these accounts particularly interesting for their firsthand perspectives on the precarious state of Korean theatre under Japanese rule, navigating both commercial demands and increasing political control (the "New System"). The texts reveal the actors' awareness of their role in shaping a "national theatre," their interactions with prominent literary figures like Yi Kwang-su, the challenges of troupe management and internal conflicts, and the harsh realities of provincial touring, including mandated participation in emperor-worship rituals. The mention of specific plays, troupes (Kohyŏp, Nangnang-jwa, Hwahwasŏn), and locations provides concrete details about the cultural landscape of the time.

林巨正 기타

靑春座 韓一松

靑春座의 연극은 과거 8년동안 문자 그대로 孤兒的 성장으로 발버등을 첫고 이제 또한 혁신 1년이란 數字의 날을 마지했다. 지금 내 자신이 다시 회고를 해도 과연 荊棘의 길인 동시에 서글푸고 초조한 지내 간 1년이다. 다시 말하면 우리는 학대 대상의 한 현상으로 嘲笑를 받고 눈총을 받으면서 背負된 重荷를 끌고 험악한 고개를 넘어섰다. 그러나 그도 또한 목적지에까지 도달함에는 너무도 단기적이였다. 이런 현실에 目到한 現下 우리 행동이 만일 여기에서 재출발의 용단성을 喪失한다면 지금까지의 노력은 수포에 돌아갈 것은 넘우도 잘 아는 나다.
그러면 지나간 1년 동안에 초조하든 감회를 지면을 통해서 몇 자 써 보기로 하자. <193>  독자 여려분께서도 잘 아시는 바와 같이 靑春座는 과거 4년 동안에 많은 사랑을 받는 동시에 흥행극계에서는 覇者였다.
그렇게 기세가 당당하든 靑春座에도 작년 9월 초순 일대 폭풍이 불어왔다. 靑春座란 한 덩어리가 산산쪼각이 나서 동서남북으로 分離될 비운에 있었다. 중견배우들은 저마다 행동을 달리하는 동시에, 남어지 몇 사람의 동지를 붙들고 이 쪼각난 한 덩어리를 恢復시키기에 전력을 다했다. 그래서 마츰내는 이곳저곳에서 뫃여드는 낯서른 동무들과 握手를 해 가지고 혁신이란 문자를 등에 걸머지고 재생의 출발을 했었으나 앙삼불이 서 있지 못한 우리의 연극은 침체하지 않을 수가 없었다. 정말 과연 1년은 눈물겨운 남모른 서름이 있었고, 쓰라린 가슴을 통해서 나오는 한숨이 있었다.
그러나 우리들은 이것을 한때의 험난한 고비로 생각하고 눈물을 뿌리면서 朝鮮 연극의 大道를 開拓하는데 微弱하나마 힘을 도으려는 일념을 가지고 주먹을 쥐고 이를 악물고 일어섰다.
그리고 동지들의 의지적 결합을 견고하게 하고 대내의 진용을 강화하기에 고심했다.
우리들은 이제야 한 덩어리가 되었다. 그리고 연극문화의 향상과 진정한 국민극의 수립을 위해서는 여하한 波瀾과 重櫐이 있드라도 이것을 박차고 나갈 勇斷力을 각자가 갖이고 있다.
그래서 합류의 힘찬 세력을 표현하기 위해서 我 등은 진정한 劇藝의 연구와 실천방침을 검토하고 있다.
연극은 우리들의 생명이요 무대는 우리들의 안정한 피난처란 것을 생각할 때 우리들의 사명은 너무나 큰 것 같다.
어떻게 하면 作中 인물의 성격을 잘 살릴 수 있으며 관중과 호흡을 같이할 수 있을가?
이것은 우리 배우들의 당면과제인 동시에 또한 연극문화의 향상을 위해서 <194> 우리들 연기자가 진심으로 연구할 대상이 된다.
우리는 과거에 있어서 연극행동을 아모런 主見도 없이 했다. 그저 東劇의 지배인 되는 金泰潤씨에게 그 달 그 달의 월급을 받고 기계적으로 筋肉을 움즉이는 노동자가 되었었다. 그러나 젊은 기업가 金泰潤씨는 靑春座가 지방순연에 있어서 인기의 초점이 되는 것을 알고 좀 더 노력하기를 격려하자. 우리는 새로운 경지를 타개할 方路를 탐구하기에 양심적인 활동을 했었다.
그래서 李光洙,/pers>선생의 「有情」을 상연하고 志士의 傳記 「金玉均」을 상연하고 <pers>洪碧初선생의 「林巨傳」을 李雲芳 脚色으로 상연했다.
그리고 작년 하반기에 들어가자 우리는 李光洙선생의 심혈의 걸작 「그 여자의 일생」을 상연하려고 남몰래 큰 기획을 하고 신극운동에 활약하고 있는 朴松씨에게 각색을 담당시켰든 바 1개월 후에 각본이 완성되어 朗讀을 할 때는 발서 劇團高協이 南鮮에서 「그 여자의 일생」을 상연하는 날이여서 중앙공연을 목표하고 준비했든 우리는 高協을 위해서 양보않을 수 없었다. 물론 그때 우리가 상업적인 타산을 했드라면 高協이 중앙공연을 갖기 전에 靑春座가 먼저 「그 여자의 일생」을 공연했을 것이나 靑春座로써의 입장은 많은 이윤보다도 연극동지들의 참다운 사업에 讚意를 표하고 싶었었다. 그래서 우리들은 靑春座의 기획이 정당했다는 것을 알고 高協극단의 동지들에게 축전을 첬다.
우리는 앞으로도 대중이 요구하는 연극을 상연하기에 정성을 다하겠다. 그래서 과거에 취하고 있든 상업적인 舊態를 버리고 연극문화 발전을 위해서 최선의 노력을 하겠다.
그래서 민중과 더부러 呼吸하고 민중의 지시와 편달을 기쁘게 받으면서 明日의 朝鮮 연극계를 위해서 힘과 마음과 정성을 다 받칠 작정이다.
그러므로 우리들의 연극행동이 순조롭거나 波瀾이 生기거나 나 자신은 중진배우 卞基鐘씨와 굳게 손을 잡고 靑春座를 끝까지 끌고 나갈 결심이다.
靑春座는 새로운 슬로간 하에 또한 刷新한 金泰潤씨의 기획를 참고해서 미약하나마 朝鮮극계에 이미 내여 논 두각을 보존하기에 전력을 다할 것이다.
그러므로 앞으로의 우리들의 연극행동은 靑春座全座員의 이상에서 나온 實踐일 것이다.
그러나 우리들의 이상을, 금년간 어느 정도까지 실천할 수 있을는지 그것은 정말 의문이다.
우리들 자신이 배우인 이상 손수 각본을 쓰기는 불가능함으로 극작가의 힘을 빌지 안으면 않된다.
그러나 극작가들이 시대에 적응한 작품을 쓰지 못한다면, 우리들의 今後의 기획과 포부는 달할 수 없다. 아니 달성할 수 없는 것보다도 무수한 힘이 들것이다.
그러므로 今後 靑春座가 질적 향상이 없다면은 그것은 문예부의 무능을 폭로하는 입증이 될 것이고 동시에 기획부장 金泰潤 지배인의 실력문제가 <195> 될 것이다.
그러나 관중으로부터 연극의 흥미가 없다면은 그것은 우리들 연기인의 책임문제가 될 것임으로 우리들은 작중인물의 성격묘사에 전심전력을 할 것이다. 아무튼 皇紀 2,601년부터는 우리들의 포부되는 靑春座가 運轉되어 갔으면 하고 한사람의 연극인인 나로서는 희망을 갖고 싶다. <196>

舞臺回想

靑春座 朴玉草

2,601년을 마지하니 벌서 8년 전에 극단에 처음 출발했든 신무대에서 李雲芳作 마담X에 하녀역으로 初舞臺를 團成社에서 밥든 그 시절이 회상됨니다. 그때에는 어찌나 무대에 등장하는게 조키도 하고 무섭기도 한지 그리고 가슴이 두근거리며 잠좇아 이루지 못하고 말었든 役에 열중했으며 상연 당일이 닥처서 첫무대에 올나서니 객석에는 거문 구름에 빤짝거리는 벌들만이 보이는 것만 같고 무슨 말을 하고 있엇는지 도무지 생각이 나지 안했섯읍니다. 이러하자 어언간 세월이 흘러서 그 시절에는 막간이라고 해서 스켓취나 음악이 드러가 막사이에 지버 넌 것이 손님에게 보기 한층 흥미을 주었읍니다. 이 막간에도 출연하여 독창도 부르고 스켓치도 하여 단체에서도 없서는 않될만치 되자 內地에서 朝鮮가극단을 모집하러 왔을 때 그때 마음속에 內地로 가 열심이 가극을 배워 우리 고향에도 소녀가극을 맨드러 볼 욕심에 2년간은 있는 힘을 다하여 가진 고생을 다하며 東京에서 저는 남자역을 배웠섰고, 어느 동무는 춤과 노래 이같이 고생하고 배우는 동무 7인이 그 후 2년 후에 귀향하여 소녀가극단을 우리 7인이 맨드러 보겟다는 욕망을 갖고서 지금으로부터 5년 전 봄 3월에 귀향했었음니다. 京城에 와 몃몃 선생님들이 힘써 주시여 北漢山 밑 사과밭 있는 집 한 채를 어더주시여 娘娘座라는 이름으로 純少女樂劇團을 조직했섰음니다. 이때에는 정말 어린 소녀들만의 모힌 20여명의 순진스러운 기분으로써 반찬없는 밥도 달게 먹으며 있는 힘 다하여 연습만 40여일 했섰음니다. 그래서 東洋극장에서 기대하든 첫공연을 갖이게 되자 새로운 기염을 토하기에 정열을 다했음니다. 그때는 春 3월로부터 4월이라 개나리, 진달래가 北漢山 골골마다 만발하였으며 우리 연습하던 집 後園, 前園, 멀니 떠러진 동리까지도 꼿밭이였으며 만개했든 시기였음니다. 우리 소녀들만의 세계요. 고요한 산밑에 파무치여 자연의 음악소리 쫄쫄 흘으는 시내물소래 새벽이면 온갖 오고가는 새소래에 맟우어 우리들은 노래불으며 춤추고 자연의 꽃무대에서 매일같이 뛰돌고 연습을 하며 놀앗습니다. 그래서 찰란하게도 공연일이 닥처 왔섰음니다. 손꼽고 기달이던 공연일 준비는 진행이 다 되였고 우리들의 자랑을 보여줄 날이 <196> 닥치여 왔섰음니다. 우리들은 공연 전일 北漢山을 등에 지고 京城시내로 합숙소를 어더 옴기여 왔섰음니다. 이때에는 제가 初舞臺를 밟은 그때가 회상되며 남몰으는 가운대에 눈이 뜨거워졌섰음니다. 꿈꾸든 이때도 녯날 한 동무 두 동무 공연 그 후 뿔뿔이 헤여지고만 이 믿지 못할 동무들의 마음이 원망스럽기 짝이 없었습니다. 그러나 지방공연 나가보니 고생을 이외에도 많이 맛보았섰고 자연히 나도 권태을 느끼여 생각하니 아서라 아즉 朝鮮에는 소녀가극단이라니 일느다 싶어, 퍽 쓸쓸하나 우리들의 집단에 실망을 느끼고 재출발하자는 마음을 굳게 먹고, 東洋극장 직속의 豪華船에 기차에 오랬동안 시달렸기 때문에 여관에 드러선 우리는 흔이 있을 수 있는 낮잠을 잤다.
여기는 木浦다. 언제나 와 봐도 실치 않고, 올 적마닥 깊은 인상을 주는 데는 이 木浦다. 잠이 채 깨기 전에 뜻허지 않는 동무에게서 전화가 왔다.
그는 바루 李化三君과 朴東君이다. 우리들은 장소를 白宮다방으로 정하고 오후 2시경에 만났다.
오래간만에 뜻허지 않은 장소에서 동무를 만나게 되여 지나간 이약이를 하면서 단시간을 그대로 보냈다. 본래 李君과 朴君의 고향이 木浦이면서도 객지생활만 하는 그들을 木浦에서 만나게 된 것을 참 우연에 가까운 일이다.
다방에서 간단한 말로 끗치고 연극을 끗친 담에 만나기로 약속하고 헤여젓다. 연극을 맟인 담에 韓一松군과 같이 만나 酒席을 베푸럿다.  李君에 말이 좀 더 엑소틱한 곳으로 가자고 해서 木浦에서 유명한 江景屋으로 갔다.
여기서 오고 가는 말이 매우 유익하고 우리에 압날에 업지 못할 論說이였다. 李君이 금후로는 연극행동에 있어서 우리를 매우 정신적으로 後援하며 실천에 있어서도 助力하겠다고 했으며 금후 우리 靑春座에 연극행동에 있어서 一松君이 力說한 후 이 자리를 유쾌한 그대로 끗치고 말었다.
