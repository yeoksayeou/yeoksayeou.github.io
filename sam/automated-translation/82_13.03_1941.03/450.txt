**Ten Years on the Road**

By Hwahŏsŏn Yi Ch'ŏngsan

The train I transferred to at Songjŏng-ni last night is still running through the Kimje Mangyŏng fields. The end-of-year market season... Occasionally, pellet-like white snowflakes strike the window mercilessly, but the train car is warm enough for steam to form.

Yesterday I rode the train, today I ride the train, and it seems I'll have to ride the train tomorrow too... Riding trains frequently and performing reruns are the most exhausting things about life in a theater troupe.

I had fallen asleep some time ago and was snoring deliciously, but in the next compartment, a dispute broke out between a country bumpkin, who must have boarded somewhere unknown, and a young woman over a seat. With shouts of "You're right," "No, I'm right," "It's this way," "It's that way," I woke up startled, though no one shook me. Swallowing down the surge of irritation, I lit a cigarette to stifle a sleepy yawn. Exhaling a mouthful of smoke, I glanced over absentmindedly. The low-class struggle over the seat seemed to have just ended, and it appeared the country bumpkin had lost. He came towards my side, grumbling to himself as if with a bitter taste in his mouth.

Thinking of how he disturbed my sweet sleep, he was infuriating, but seeing the white frost beneath his ears, I couldn't bring myself to blame him. In the spirit of 'giving an extra rice cake to the one you dislike,' I gruffly offered him a seat, saying, "Sit next to me..." The country bumpkin plopped his rear down without showing much gratitude. A few moments passed in light silence. The train slowed slightly as it rounded a mountain curve somewhere, letting out a couple of toots as if making a grand announcement. After a while, I broke the silence. "Where are you headed?"... It's the customary question strangers ask when they happen to sit together in a train car. The country bumpkin... "Going to Pongch'ŏn (奉天)..." he replied, without bothering to turn around. Me... "What business do you have in Pongch'ŏn?" I asked again. This time, in a slightly lower voice, the country bumpkin... "Going to find my daughter," he sighed, looking down. His somber voice and gloomy expression hinted at some deep story... My curiosity was suddenly piqued.

I first offered him a cigarette, let him light it, and then struck up conversation again. Me... "What is your daughter doing in Pongch'ŏn?" Country bumpkin... "How should I know what the hell she's been up to? She ran away three years ago <206> and I haven't heard a thing since, thought she was dead. But recently, someone who came from China told me the wretch is in Pongch'ŏn. Says she's working for a salary at a place called Myŏngwŏlgwan (明月館), changed her name to Yurikko-sang (유리꼬상), so maybe I'll find her if I look for that name, who knows. The wretch is probably making money hand over fist and spending it all on herself. If I find her this time, I won't let her be." Me... "If you don't let her be, what do you plan to do?" Country bumpkin... "She and I will die together. What's the point of letting someone live who doesn't recognize her father or mother..." He turned his face away as if further talk was tiresome, apparently getting angry just talking about his daughter. I didn't ask any more questions. But the more I thought about it, the more pitiful it seemed. A country old man who doesn't even know where Pongch'ŏn is, traveling thousands of *ri* with singular determination (*우 단심*) to find his wayward daughter. What if he can't find her? Even if he luckily finds his daughter by going to Myŏngwŏlgwan and asking for Yurikko-sang, what if she gives him the cold shoulder? Though it's someone else's problem, I couldn't help but feel anxious and worried.

Lost in these random thoughts, I suddenly looked back at the old man. As if completely unaware of the frightening reality that might soon confront him... he was already dozing off, head nodding. The white hair fluttering above his wrinkled face, which spoke of the hardships of bygone years, looked even more desolate. This old man has a child like me, and I have parents like this old man... Comparing his situation to my own, my heart suddenly clenched. My thoughts traced the thread of memory back to the low-roofed house under the lamplight in my distant hometown. Ah! Father! Mother! Are you sleeping peacefully tonight too, dreaming of seeing this son? I gently closed my eyes. My eyelids grew progressively hotter. On one side, an old man yearning for his child; on the other, me, longing for my parents... Ah! The train moves on. The indifferent train, carrying Yurikko-sang's father and me, rushes onward as if busy on its own. The night deepens... (Aboard the Honam Line Train) <207>

**Touring Diary**

By Hwahŏsŏn Sin Chahyŏn

The 365 days of stage life have cunningly wrinkled the Year of the Dragon (戊辰) and passed. This year too has given me nothing but a few more wrinkles on my face and the skill of smoking cigarettes. Let's flip through the worn-out 'Record' booklet, shiny from handling.

X Month X Day
Out of a year, I spend six months on trains. I'm sick and tired of riding trains. Today again, we leave for the next venue on the 5 AM train.
"Get up!" An ice-cold voice. A tremendous shout, loud enough to shake the inn's courtyard, <207> rings out twice.
"Get up—" He shouts again. My head burrows deeper into the covers. Outside, it sounds like preparations for departure are underway; the rumbling sounds of chatter, coughing, doors opening, and cries of "Aaii, cold—" draw nearer, then suddenly someone pounds on the sliding door. "Damn it, this will kill a person," the words escape my lips involuntarily. "Hey, get up," the voice grates on my ears. My appetite completely vanishes. It seems I was fated only to catch early morning trains on this tour; this is already the third time. Reluctantly, I get up, gather my clothes, and wake K-kun sleeping beside me. He sits bolt upright and snaps, "Damn nuisance!" A very grumpy tone. I look up; his eyes are closed as he sits on the bedding, just sulking. It's a good 30-40 minute walk from the inn to the station. Due to "gasoline" (까소린) conservation, hiring a shared taxi (乘合自動車) is impossible. Grumpy grumbling could be heard from various corners outside the inn gate. But we have to go, so we walk in clumps. The town streets are deeply asleep under the dim electric lights. The barking of shaggy dogs echoes from afar. Occasionally, roosters crow. When we arrive at the station, the vast hall is empty. People find spots here and there, collapse, and some shameless fellows are already snoring. We are a group that sees each other morning and night, talking together for hours. But only when catching these early morning trains, everyone clams up like ill-tempered people, mouths shut tight, just waiting resignedly for the train to arrive. As soon as the announcement to board the train ends, everyone grabs their "trunks" (도랑크) and crawls inside. With fortunes like ours, how could there be seats even inside the train? People are seated row after row, eyes closed as if chanting Buddhist prayers; there's no room to squeeze in shamelessly. There's no choice but to go to the washroom area and use the "trunk" as a chair. Just as I managed to secure a corner and was about to sit, "Sir, may I sit here too? Please?" A delicate female voice. I glance up; it's one of our actresses. A bitter taste fills my mouth. But there's nothing to be done, so I stand up without a word. The train runs on. Standing for a while makes my back and legs ache, and my back feels stiff enough to kill me. Ah, <208> damn it, let's climb onto the luggage rack. Barely pushing past passengers' backs, I crawl onto the rack. It seems better than standing. Let's just lie down boldly, forget decorum and embarrassment. Lying down, I realize that being unusually tall, with luggage placed horizontally, I can't even stretch my legs. Since the space is so uncomfortable, I naturally shift this way and that. The person sitting below seems very uneasy; unable to speak, they just glare up at me with resentful eyes. Finally, as if finding it unbearable, they complain, "Hey, dust is falling." I flinch. Pretending not to hear, I let out a loud "Drrrreong" snore. It looks like we still have 4 or 5 hours to reach the next venue. Some time passes like this. The cursed sleep comes even in such situations. After a bit, "Moshi moshi, orite kudasai." (Excuse me, please get down.) Someone grabs my clothes and shakes me. I slowly open my eyes and look down; the conductor is waking me. Ah well, since it's come to this, let's try bluffing.
"Ano... hanahada sumimasen ga, chotto byōki de, tatte ikaremasen node." (Um... I'm terribly sorry, but I'm a bit sick, so I can't stand up.) I say this. "Sore wa komarimasu na." (That's problematic.) The conductor grumbles. K-kun, standing near the door, quickly chimes in, "Ano, netsu ga hijō ni arimashite, hanahada meiwaku desu ga, hitotsu onegai itashimasu." (Um, he has a very high fever, it's quite troublesome, but please do us this favor.) He speaks smoothly and plaintively, so the conductor, seemingly resigned, says, "Komarimasu ne," (This is troubling,) and walks away. I groan "Ung, ung," a few times, pretending to be ill. Seeing this whole charade, our troupe members smile faintly. I also look up at the ceiling and smile. I wish we could get there faster, wish I could whip the train. Morning sunlight begins to creep through the window. The smoke from chain-smoked cigarettes drifts towards the ceiling. Lying on the rack, the smoke stings my nose, making me sneeze. Damn it, I have no choice but to climb down again. Still three hours to go. <209>

**Summary**

These two memoirs from 1941 depict the grueling life of traveling actors in colonial Korea. Yi Ch'ŏngsan recounts a weary train journey where he encounters an old man desperately seeking his runaway daughter in faraway Pongch'ŏn (Mukden), reflecting on his own longing for home and family. Sin Chahyŏn details the sheer exhaustion and discomfort of constant travel, particularly the misery of early morning train departures, crowded conditions forcing him onto a luggage rack, and the necessity of feigning illness in broken Japanese to avoid being moved by the conductor. The articles highlight the physical hardships, lack of comfort, and emotional toll experienced by performers, painting a picture of itinerant life filled with fatigue, shared struggles, and moments of poignant reflection or dark humor. For historians, these pieces offer valuable insights into the daily realities of travel (train conditions, perceived gasoline shortages impacting taxi availability), social issues (family breakdown, migration to Manchuria), interactions within the colonial context (using Japanese with officials like train conductors), and the general atmosphere of weariness and precariousness for Korean performers during the late colonial period.

旅路十年

豪華船 李靑山

X월 X일 밤 갈아탄
松汀里에서 가라 탄 汽車가 아즉도 金堤만경뜰을 달니고 있다. 섯달 대목...있다금 차창에는 쌀애기같은 흰 눈빨이 사정없이 부딋는대 車깐은 김이 서리드록 훈훈하다.
어제도 기차를 타고 오늘도 기차를 타고 또 래일도 기차를 타야 할 모양...기차를 자조 타는 것과 재상연 하는 것과는 극단생활에서 제일 질력나는 일이다.
한참 전에 잠이 들어서 맛있게 코를 굴고 누어 있는 판인대 저편 칸에서 어대서 올라 탓는지도 몰으는 촌영감이 젊은 여자와 자리를 다투는 시비가 일어나서 네가 옳으니 내가 옳으니 이외다 저외다 작자공이 치는 바람에 나는 흔들어 깨우는 사람도 없는데 놀나 깨었다. 짜증이 불끈 소사 올으는 것을 꿀컥 삼키고 졸닌 하품을 죽이려고 담배를 피워 물었다. 한입 가뜩 담뱃연기를 내어 풍기며 무심코 그 쪽을 바라보니 자리를 다투는 저급한 투쟁이 인제 겨오 끝장이 난 모양인데 겸국은 村영감이 젓다. 어슬렁어슬렁 입맛이 쓴 것처럼 혼자 투덜거리며 이쪽으로 온다.
남의 단잠을 깨운 것을 생각하면 미웁기도 한량이 없으나 귀밑에 흰 서리를 보니 참아 남으렐 수도 없고. 그야말로 미운 놈 떡 하나 더 준다는 격으로 『내 옆에 앉으시요...』하고 툭명스럽게 자리를 권하였드니 村영감... 별로 고마웁다는 표시도 하지 않고 쑥 엉둥이를 드리민다. 잠깐 가벼운 침묵으로 몇 순간이 지났다. 기차는 어느 산모통이 카-부를 돌아가는지 약간 속력을 덜 내면서, 큰 생색이나 내는 것처럼 삑삑하고 두어 번 고동을 울닌다. 한참만에 나는 입을 열었다. 『어대까지 가시요』...車깐에서 우연히 한자리에 앉게 되면 낯 몰으는 사람들끼리 의례껀 물어 보는 말이다. 村영감...『奉天 가라오...』 하고 구타여 돌아다 보려고도 하지 안는다. 나...『奉天 뭘하러 가시오?』 하고 또 물어 보았드니 이번은 약간 나즉한 목소리로 村영감...『딸년 차지러 가라오』 하고 알에를 보며 한숨을 쉰다. 그의 沈鬱한 음성이나 음산한 표정이 무슨 깊은 사연이 있는 듯...나는 호기심이 문뜩 생겼다.
나는 위선 담배 한 개를 권하야 피여 물게 하야 놓고 또 다시 수작을 걸었다. 나...『따님은 奉天서 무얼하고 계심니까?』 村영감...『무슨 지랄을 헤쌋는지 내가 알어라오, 3년 전에 도망해 <206> 나갔는데 입때 아무 소식도 몰나 싸코 죽은 줄만 알고 있으니께, 얼마 전에 淸國서 나온 사람 편에 들응께로 잡것이 奉天 있더레유, 明月館인가 하는 듸서 월급 먹고 있는듸 유리꼬상이라고 일홈을 고첬으니께 그렇게 찾으면 맞나볼넝가 원 몰느지라오, 잡것이 돈 막벌고 혼자 처먹나분데 이번 가서 맞나기만 하면 그냥 안 둬라오』 나...『그냥 안두면 어떡하실 작정이서요?』 촌영감...『저하고 나하고 죽지라오, 아배도 몰으고 어매도 몰으는 걸 살려두면 뭐하듸요...』하고 더 말하는 것이 긴 않은 듯이 얼굴을 돌려바리는 것을 보니 아마 딸년이야기만 해도 화가 나는 모양이다. 나도 더 물어 보려고 하지 않었다. 그러나 생각하면 생각할수록 딱한 일이다. 奉天이 어디 붙었는지도 몰으는 촌노인이 바람둥이 딸자식을 맞나 보려고 수 천리 길을 *우 단심하고 찾어 갔다가 만일 못 맞나면 어찌 할가.  명월관 유리꼬상을 찾어가서 요행히 자기 딸을 맞난다 하드라도 혹시 푸대접이나 하지 않을는지? 남의 일인대도 공연히 궁금중이 나고 걱정스러워 견딀 수가 없다.
