Special Japanese Language Edition, Roundtable Discussion on the "Cultural Problem" of Korea

Date: Mid-February
Place: Peninsula Hotel
—◇—
Attendees:
Head of Culture Department, National Service League: Yanabe Eizaburō
Professor, Keijō Imperial University: Karashima Takeshi
Member, Culture Department Committee: Kim Eok
Arts and Culture Editor, Keijō Nippō: Terada Akira
Culture Department Staff: Tanaka Kazuo
Member, Culture Department Committee: Matsuda Reikō
Professor, Posŏng College: Yu Chin-o
Chairman, Korean Film Association: Yasuda Tatsuo
President, Korean Theater Association: Makiyama Mizukyū
President, Samch'ŏlli: Kim Tong-hwan
~~~~~~~
Managing Director, Maeil Sinbo: Kim Tong-jin
Arts and Culture Editor, Maeil Sinbo: Paek Ch'ŏl

Managing Director Kim, Maeil Sinbo: The news that the National Service League (Kokumin Sōryoku Renmei) has newly established a Culture Department and that Mr. Yanabe has been appointed as its head was reported in the newspapers earlier, for which we are truly grateful. The general populace also holds great expectations for this Culture Department. We in the newspaper business, along with all cultural organizations, feel even more strongly our responsibility to share in the burden of this mission carried by the League's Culture Department. We sincerely hope that today you will share your frank opinions and provide much guidance regarding the future direction of our Korean cultural policy.

Arts and Culture Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: The first thing I would like to ask about is the Culture Department itself. From the perspective of the general populace, the existence of the newly created Culture Department is somewhat vague, and there is a feeling that the scope of its work or the nature of its tasks is unclear. This seems to be the impression not only among the general public but also among the cultural figures participating in the Culture Department, who feel its work is quite vague.
Indeed, this is not just the case in Korea; general cultural figures' criticisms regarding the Culture Department of the Imperial Rule Assistance Association (Yokusan-kai) in the mainland (Naichi) were also vague.
Therefore, what main focus will the Culture Department pursue? What is the significance of the Culture Department, its mission, its overall scope of activities, and, on the other hand, facing the increasingly serious situation of the times, what <40> kind of resolve will the Culture Department adopt? I believe it is an urgent task for the Culture Department to present such matters clearly before the eyes of the general populace. Mr. Yanabe, Head of the Culture Department, please...

Head Yanabe, National Service League Culture Department: The general populace wishes for clarity on what kind of work the National Service League Culture Department will generally undertake, or how it will proceed, as its current state is somewhat vague. This is because the preparations and policies of the Culture Department are not yet fully established, and I ask for everyone's understanding regarding the points that are not yet clear.
As for how the Culture Department will develop in the future, we are currently deliberating on the general framework through frequent dinner meetings, called Culture Department Conferences, with everyone involved in the department. To ensure the thoroughness of these deliberations, we believe it is necessary to fully listen to opinions from various fields. That is why we recently decided to establish a Culture Committee and organized it by bringing together cultural figures from various fields. These Culture Department Committee members will, in carrying out specific tasks in the future—for example, musicians for music, film professionals for movies, writers for literature, etc.—be entrusted with their respective specialized areas. We have selected sixty-eight committee members with the intention of holding liaison meetings with those who have a deep understanding and consulting through each specialized division.
Therefore, I think some time must pass before the activities of the Culture Department can be shown more clearly to the public.
Furthermore, determining the policy direction for the Culture Department is an extremely difficult issue, but the National Service League headquarters has precisely defined the practical guidelines (jissen yōkō), and we intend to follow them. I sincerely hope that everyone will follow this line and cooperate.
Now, how these will be implemented in various fields, or the organization, preparation, and methods for doing so, will be decided gradually. All of you present here are in important positions to guide or influence the general populace, while aligning your own work with these practical guidelines. <41> Therefore, if everyone from fields such as film, theater, art, literature, music, etc., approaches the general populace with the same goal and the same spirit, calling out to them, I believe we can perfectly fulfill this mission as one hundred million hearts united, without dispersion.
Also, if any of you in your respective departments encounter difficulties in guiding the populace along the lines of national policy (kokusaku), that is, in the direction required by the state today, this Culture Department is prepared to participate in consultations at any time.
In short, rather than the Culture Department guiding or leading cultural figures, I believe that you yourselves should proactively create culture that aligns with today's national policy.

II

Editor Paek: Professor Karashima, do you have anything to say?

Professor Karashima, Keijō Imperial University: Well, let me see... I'm somewhat unsure about which point to emphasize in my remarks. Regarding the work of the Culture Department, we are merely in a position to assist with the work under the consideration of the Head of the Culture Department, so I don't have anything specific to say.

Kim Tong-hwan: I would like to take this opportunity to express to Mr. Yanabe something I have been thinking about. That is, on which segment of the population should the Culture Department focus its efforts going forward? I believe this is an extremely pressing and important matter.
Looking at the current situation in Korea, there are about 800,000 mainland Japanese (Naichijin) on the peninsula. These mainlanders have generally received a high level of education from the start and are currently fulfilling their duties as loyal subjects of the Emperor (Kōkoku Shinmin) in wartime at their respective workplaces in government offices, banks, and companies.
However, looking at the Korean side, those who could be called the intellectual class—counting students from elementary school to university today—number about 1.5 million. If we consider adults who have graduated from school to be twice that number, approximately 3 million can be roughly considered the intellectual class. But the remaining 20 million are almost entirely an illiterate class lacking knowledge. This is truly a troubling phenomenon.
—Therefore, the segment of the population that the Culture Department should focus on most vigorously in the future is not the 800,000 mainlanders, nor the 3 million peninsula intellectuals, but indeed, first and foremost, these 20 million people in the illiterate class.
I believe we should ensure that these masses, lagging in culture, are not left neglected.
As one example, the number of volunteer soldiers (shiganhei) this year reportedly reached over 130,000. If it's 130,000, that's roughly one person per household, meaning 130,000 households were affected, and 130,000 mothers were involved.
Furthermore, most volunteer soldiers currently come from agricultural, mountain, and fishing villages rather than cities. Thus, the mothers of these volunteer soldiers are mostly people with little connection to literacy.
This is because to have a young man around twenty years old today, the mother's <42> age must be over forty, reaching fifty. Since it has only been thirty years since the annexation of Korea, these mothers are women from before the annexation who were not blessed with opportunities for education. I suspect this phenomenon will likely continue for the next ten years. After ten years, the mothers of volunteer soldiers will probably be graduates of elementary or girls' high schools.
However, we cannot just suddenly bring these mothers to school now and educate them.
If these mothers had been educated women, there would undoubtedly have been two or three times as many applicants this year.
Of course, the cultural movement should not solely emphasize the training of volunteer soldiers, but under the current circumstances, we must first consider the mothers of volunteer soldiers.
In short, we must educate this culturally deprived, illiterate population of 20 million. To do so, enlightenment movements like the National Language Promotion Movement (Kokugo Fukyū Undō) are certainly necessary, but what is more necessary, I believe, is to initiate cultural movements that teach through sight and sound, such as theater, film, and popular storytelling (yadam). Therefore, I sincerely request that the focus of the Culture Department's work be placed on this point.

Head Yanabe: Mr. Kim Tong-hwan's points are truly excellent. The Culture Department is also racking its brains about how best to guide these people in agricultural, mountain, and fishing villages—perhaps we could call it a "Rural Life Cultural Movement." For this, we are currently considering methods suitable for rural areas, such as plays that enhance understanding of the current situation, films, popular storytelling (yadam), or other methods like picture-story shows (kamishibai). We would like to proceed with substantial cooperation from people in each respective field on this matter.

President Makiyama, Theater Association: We have formed the Korean Theater Association, but since it was formed not long ago, we haven't done much yet. However, come spring, just as President Kim of Samch'ŏlli mentioned, we intend to try working in that direction. That is, through rural entertainment theater, we aim to educate and entertain the people in rural villages, while also fostering, shall we say, the resolve to send out volunteer soldiers, and making them aware of what they must know as citizens. We are currently preparing for this. We plan to borrow theaters in various regions, and where there are no theaters, perhaps the large auditoriums of elementary schools, and have a troupe of about ten members conduct regional tours. I heard from Professor Karashima that this kind of thing is already being implemented with great effect in the mainland, Germany, and Italy, and apparently, in places like China (Shina) and Spain (Supein), they show plays like this to brave soldiers heading to the battlefield. In any case, I believe it is meaningful. That being said, the immediate problem we face is the need for scripts. While we in the Theater Association are making efforts in selecting them, we would appreciate it if the Culture Department could also lend its strength, perhaps by sponsoring script contests.

Mr. Tanaka, Culture Department: But wouldn't the implementation process for something like that be difficult?

President Makiyama: That's true. <43>

Mr. Tanaka: Isn't something like folk drama necessary as wholesome entertainment? They are doing a lot of this in the mainland—

President Makiyama: Of course, it's necessary. We probably can't implement it right away, but we intend to try eventually. First, if we coordinate with the leaders of villages and hamlets, it probably won't be too difficult.

Mr. Tanaka: Another point is, isn't the training of directors and actors necessary?

President Makiyama: Absolutely necessary. We definitely cannot continue as things are now.

Mr. Tanaka: We hope the Theater Association will pay particular attention to that point as well. I imagine you are very busy with various things, but—

President Makiyama: Yes, we are busy. Because we have to handle those things as well. Another method is to frequently perform plays, thereby making the actors themselves respond to national policy.

Mr. Tanaka: In short, merely providing entertainment serves no purpose. It must have what could be called constructive significance. It would be good if its effects could immediately penetrate deeply into the public, and the citizens could assimilate with it.

President Makiyama: That's absolutely right. Regarding that, just as Mr. Tanaka said, we want to select scripts under that kind of philosophy, scripts that will produce such effects.

Mr. Tanaka, Culture Department: We have high expectations. Please strive so that a part of the national life naturally infiltrates culture.

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Isn't that true for everything? Novels, music, films—they all must become like that, right?

Editor Terada, Keijō Nippō: Allow me to say a word. This is separate from the current discussion, but I took over the Arts and Culture department of the Keijō Nippō (Kyōjō Nichihō) in January of Shōwa 13 (1938). Until then, the Keijō Nippō, not just the arts and culture section but everything from the household section onwards, had simply transplanted newspapers from the mainland as they were, with no effort towards making it Korean. It merely took dispatches sent from the mainland, gave them titles, and published them. Frankly speaking, there was no need to make a newspaper published in Korea identical to those in the mainland. Therefore, recognizing that it couldn't become intimate with the populace unless it developed as a Korean "local" newspaper, and also because its sales performance was poor, we made efforts that were completely different from the past Keijō Nippō, things one couldn't even imagine: hiring Korean women reporters, visiting homes, publishing articles on how to make pickles, describing how to tailor Korean clothes, and so on. And it's a Japanese-language newspaper. This newspaper, in parallel with the general spread of the national language (Japanese), gained considerable circulation among the Korean people. Especially in the arts and culture section, we avoided publishing works by mainland writers as much as possible and made it a principle to publish manuscripts from cultural figures residing in Korea. Initially, we received contributions from professors at Keijō Imperial University, then medical colleges, law colleges, commercial colleges, etc. As my social circle expanded, we started receiving support from Korean writers as well, which is the current state. I believe this way of fostering intimacy with both mainlanders and Koreans <44> serves as an aid to Japan-Korea unity (Naisen Ittai). Especially since the beginning of the China Incident, this feeling has deepened. Comparing it to the current Imperial Rule Assistance Association in the mainland and the National Service League movement in Korea, the future path can be predicted. In any case, I believe the right thing to do is to take the initiative and respond to the national policy line before being told "Do this! Do that!" by the National Service League. As one example, those of us in the newspaper world, no matter how good a manuscript we receive, must first check if it is constructive before handling it. From now on, it must be this way. And as an educational institution for the general populace, we intend to ensure thoroughness and fully demonstrate our responsibility as bearers of that role.

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Mr. Terada's remarks just now concerned newspapers, but I think they provide a very good reference regarding the issue of deepening Japan-Korea unity through culture today. Next, changing the topic slightly, I believe there must be some basic direction for the Culture Department's work going forward. Regarding this—and this is just my personal opinion—I think there are two paths the Culture Department should take. One, during times like these when the situation with countries like the United States and other foreign nations is tense, given that Korea has had a tendency to relatively worship Anglo-American culture, is to criticize that Anglo-American culture. The other aspect is to build up the national culture from within. These are two aspects. What are your thoughts on this point, Mr. Head of the Culture Department?

III

Head Yanabe: Yes, well, this might seem like my personal bias, but looking at the process through which Japanese culture has evolved up to now, our country has a relatively excellent ability to absorb foreign cultures. This point has sometimes led to misunderstandings, such as the tendency to think that Japanese people are merely good at imitation or lack creativity. However, I believe that in the process of cultural progress, while there might initially be imitation of various things, as these accumulate, something else, something superior and creative, emerges. Also, this thing called imitation—if there is something excellent, it's foolish not to adopt it just because it belongs to someone else. I believe imitation itself is not something anyone can achieve and should not be dismissed. To speak concretely, we humans, from elementary school until graduating university, merely receive instruction and imitate, and only then gradually become able to sustain ourselves. I think it's roughly the same kind of thing. In other words, Japanese culture is now at the boundary between the era of imitation and the era of creation, so I believe it will demonstrate its true value before long. And in the future, once the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere (Tōa Kyōeiken) is established, I anticipate that we will be able to incorporate the cultures of various countries like Thailand (Taikoku), the Dutch East Indies (Ran'in), etc., expand upon them, and create a novel culture. Therefore, we must stop unconditionally tilting towards foreign cultures, <45> have the peoples of the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere recognize the uniqueness of Japanese culture, and establish a new culture. This is what I earnestly desire, and I believe it is the mission entrusted to our country henceforth.

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Mr. Yu Chin-o, anything...?

Professor Yu, Posŏng College: I believe there are currently quite a few individuals in Korea who have a certain degree of understanding of mainland culture. However, it is truly regrettable that their ability to understand the unique culture of the mainland is lacking. Of course, a reason for this might be that scholars, thinkers, or leading philosophers in the mainland have also been heavily influenced by foreign cultures. Consequently, even when people made the effort to study abroad in Tokyo, they might have been somewhat negligent in respecting or recognizing points about mainland culture. However, Japanese culture is progressing by leaps and bounds, so I believe these notions can be corrected before long. For example, the fact that Nishida philosophy (西田哲學) has garnered significant interest lately, and that we students are also eagerly studying it, suggests this trend.
One more thing I'd like to mention is that among Koreans, those proficient in the national language (Japanese) familiarize themselves with mainland literature and the cultures of various Western countries through Japanese. However, for those who cannot speak Japanese—this might be the job of the Writers' Association (Bunjin Kyōkai)—I think it would be very beneficial to translate outstanding mainland works into Korean to allow them to become as familiar as possible with mainland works. Although there have been some translations accordingly in the past, it seems that due to inadequate manuscript fees, even requests to prominent writers did not proceed as desired. I hope that this point too can be made much more vigorous through cooperation between the Writers' Association and the Culture Department. An example of this kind of effort was the translation and publication of Mr. Hino Ashihei's (火野葦平) "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥と兵隊) some time ago, which was very well received. Translating mainland works even to this extent is also a very effective endeavor, I believe.

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Mr. Matsuda, from the perspective of a painter, what are your thoughts?

Mr. Matsuda, Culture Department Committee: I don't have anything particular to say.

Mr. Tanaka, Culture Department: Of course, in our country, it would be good if we could also foster recognition of ancient literature, which incorporated culture received from China and Korea and added Japanese thought and aesthetic sensibility (fūryū).

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Professor Karashima, how was Chinese literature digested after it entered Japan?

Professor Karashima, Keijō Imperial University: Well, let me see... One thing I want to emphasize is the continuity of Japanese culture, perhaps. For example, looking at something like Confucianism, its true value is actually being demonstrated more in the mainland and Korea than in its birthplace, China. In China, it remains largely in form only, which is the current situation. I'll omit the specialized details. Regarding the cultural issue in agricultural, fishing, and mining areas discussed earlier by Mr. Kim Tong-hwan, <46> I strongly agree with the idea of developing culture in these areas through theater, film, etc. What should not be forgotten here is the intellectual class. Currently, those showing great interest in the Culture Department and cultural figures are, conversely, from the intellectual class. How to integrate this intellectual class is perhaps the biggest issue. Regarding this, I think it is necessary for us within the Culture Department to frequently create such opportunities and cooperate constructively on how to guide those spheres.

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Mr. Kim Eok, from your standpoint of cooperation and guidance for the Korean poet community, could you say a word?

Mr. Kim, Culture Department Committee: Professor Yu mentioned translation earlier, and indeed, I think translation is something all cultural figures are concerned about. I too would like to say something regarding translation. No matter the culture, if one wants to truly become familiar with it, translations might allow one to grasp the outline, but it's impossible to ever connect with the true meaning, the unique flavor. Therefore, for the illiterate, just as they say "Start studying from forty," it's not too late even now. I think it's appropriate to make utmost efforts in spreading the national language (Japanese) and gradually have them read materials starting with simple content. I also believe this would be considerably effective for Japan-Korea unity (Naisen Ittai).

Editor Paek, Maeil Sinbo: Earlier, Mr. Tanaka spoke of striving so that a part of the national life naturally enters into culture. I infer this ultimately means fully utilizing the professional skills we possess to guide the masses towards culture. In this sense, Mr. Yasuda, could you share your opinion regarding the field of film...?

Chairman Yasuda, Film Association: I'll speak briefly. Currently, most films handled in Korea are from the mainland or foreign countries. Korean films have produced some works up to now, but they are extremely weak in terms of both structure and technique. It's troubling that they often go against the role of guiding the general populace. In response to this, how does the National Service League Culture Department intend to guide the film industry? I would like to ask for Mr. Yanabe's opinion.

Head Yanabe: Well, suddenly being presented with such a difficult question... My opinion is that in Korea right now, setting aside actors for later, there aren't many figures capable of guiding the general populace. Perhaps there is no other way than to first train personnel in that field?

President Makiyama, Theater Association: I would like to speak about the weakness of Korean cinema. One reason is the inability to fully recoup the costs of established films. That is, just screening them in the few movie theaters across all of Korea might be manageable for costs around 10,000 or 20,000 yen, but when it comes to productions costing as much as 100,000 yen, the sums don't add up. If the work were good enough to be exported to the mainland or foreign countries, it would be a different matter, but that's not feasible either. That's why things have been stagnant <47> in that state until now. The second reason is the lack of patrons willing to readily invest large sums like 100,000 or 200,000 yen. The third reason, I think, is that due to the lack of funds, they end up using worn-out cameras and all sorts of other equipment used in the mainland, making it impossible to produce good films—

Professor Karashima, Keijō Imperial University: Furthermore, isn't the lack of outstanding scriptwriters and directors also a cause? However, I believe the organization will be improved from now on, allowing for great progress.

Professor Yu, Posŏng College: I saw a preview screening of "Angels Without a Home" (家なき天使) the other day, and I hadn't realized Korean cinema had developed that much. I hear "Tuition" (授業料) was also good. I think "Angels Without a Home" is a rare work in recent times; the film technique was also remarkably advanced.

Mr. Tanaka, Culture Department: Yes, "Angels Without a Home" was good, wasn't it? I watched it twice. I think its cinematography and direction were unprecedented. It will soon catch up to the level of mainland films.

President Makiyama, Theater Association: Despite the poor equipment, I hope people appreciate the internal struggles involved in producing a work of that caliber.

Mr. Tanaka, Culture Department [Note: Likely Tanaka Kazuo, previously identified as Culture Department staff, not部長]: Capital and all equipment may be lacking, but looking to the future, we hope you will be very active. However, one difficult issue is the public psychology towards Korean films. It's understandable that those accustomed to watching foreign films might not feel close to conventional Korean films, but I wish they would stop criticizing them from one to ten without even watching them. Seeing how France continues to produce excellent films even after being defeated in the war allows one to infer the state of its national psychology.

Paek: What about cultural films (bunka eiga)?

Chairman Yasuda, Film Association: Recently, the enthusiasm for cultural films in the mainland—though likely with official support—has been developing at a considerable pace, which I think is noteworthy in film history. Korea is still weak, and at the same time, we don't have a single telescopic lens for our cameras, so we are in a state of borrowing from the mainland. There are many other inadequacies as well, so activity in that area must be left to the future; there's no other way.

President Makiyama, Theater Association: Nevertheless, the appeal of film is quite significant. In cities, of course, but even in rural villages without movie theaters, projecting films on trucks or in open squares is apparently very popular.

Professor Karashima, Keijō Imperial University: The authorities should also cooperate enthusiastically regarding rural films. We want to strive harder in that direction.

Professor Yu, Posŏng College: Indeed, I hope it will be so. Also, in rural areas, when showing films, explanations must be provided, right? <48>

**Summary**

This 1941 roundtable transcript details a discussion among Japanese officials, Japanese cultural figures, and collaborating Korean intellectuals about the newly formed Cultural Department of the National Service League in colonial Korea. The dialogue centers on the department's mission to align Korean culture with Japanese wartime national policy (kokusaku). Key topics include strategies for cultural dissemination, particularly debating whether to target the largely illiterate rural masses (proposed by Kim Tong-hwan using film and theater for propaganda, including promoting the volunteer soldier system) or the educated urban class. The participants discuss using various media like theater, film, storytelling (yadam), and picture-story shows (kamishibai) for "enlightenment" and promoting Japanese language (kokugo) and Japan-Korea unity (naisen ittai). The discussion also touches upon the perceived need to critique Anglo-American cultural influence, the state and challenges of the Korean film industry (lack of funding, equipment, talent, need for better scripts and constructive criticism), and the importance of translating Japanese works into Korean versus promoting direct Japanese language acquisition.

For historians, this article is revealing about the mechanisms and mindset behind Japanese cultural assimilation policies in late colonial Korea. It showcases the direct involvement of the National Service League (a government-mobilization body) in cultural affairs, the explicit goal of using culture for state propaganda and shaping public consciousness towards imperial aims (like military volunteering), and the collaborative role played by certain Korean intellectuals (Kim Eok, Yu Chin-o, Kim Tong-hwan, Kim Tong-jin, Paek Ch'ŏl) alongside Japanese figures. The debate on targeting cultural efforts (illiterate masses vs. elites) and the frank discussion of the Korean film industry's weaknesses and potential for propaganda provide valuable insights into colonial cultural management and the complex dynamics of collaboration and control.

每日新報社金本常務―今度 國民總力聯盟に 文化部が 新らしく 設けられ それに 矢鍋先生が 文化部長に 就任せられましたことは 夙に新聞紙上に 報導された通りで誠に感謝に堪えません 一般國民も この 文化部に對しては、實に大なる 期待を持つて居ります、我等 新聞紙を始め凡有 文化國體も 總力聯盟の文化部が 負荷つて居る この 使命の一翼を 分擔しなければならぬ 責任感をより 一層强く 感ずる 次第で御座います、何卒 今日は腹藏なき 御意見を 御話し下さつて 今後 我が朝鮮文化政策の行き方に就いて 御示すこと多からんことを御願致します。
白每新學藝部長―そこて 眞先きに御話し願ひ度いことは 文化部 その自體に 對してであります。一般國民の 立場より しますれば 新しく生れた 文化部の存在が 多少 漠然として居りまして その仕事の 限界又は 仕事の 性質が はつきりしてゐない感じが致します。これは 一般大衆のみでなく 文化部に叅加して居る 文化人等も文化部の 仕事は 甚だ漠然なものだとの感じを受けで居る樣です。
否、これは 朝鮮に於てのみ そうでなく、內地の翼賛會の文化部に 對しても 一般文化人等の批評は 漠然たるものでありました。
それで 文化部が 如何なる 主眼を持つて進むものか、又は文化部の意義、文化部の使命これ等 文化部の 全面的活動範圍と、又は一方 漸次 重大性 帶びて居る 時局に處して 文＜40＞化部として 如何なる 覺悟を持つ樣になるのか、さう云ふ樣な事柄を 一般國民の眼前に示すのが 文化部の 急務たと 存じます。矢鍋文化部長、何卒……
矢鍋國民總力文化部長—國民總力聯盟文化部が 大體 どう云ふ 仕事を して行くのか又は どんなにして やつて往くのか、が多少漠然として居るが それを 先づ 確つきりして慾しいと云ふのが 一般國民の 希望だとの 御話しでありましたが それは未だ 文化部の準備とか 方針とかが完全に 整備されてゐない爲で明確な點に就いでは 皆樣の 御諒解を得たいと思ひます。
それから 文化部を どんなに、今後進展するかは 文化部に 從事する 皆樣と 屢々 文化部會議と云ふ會食を 開いて 大體の 綱目を審議中であります。この審議の萬全を 期せむとせば 各方面の意見を 十分に聞かねば ならぬと 思ひまして この前、文化委員會を 設置する樣になり 各方面 文化人を 網羅して 文化部委員會を組織した次第であります。この文化部委員は 今後 具體的な仕事をして往く上に、一例を擧ぐれば、音樂に 關しては 音樂家、映畵に 關することは 映畵人、文藝に 關することは文人等 それぞれ その專門的な方面に任かして、深き理解を 持つて居る方々へ 連格會を開く等 各々 專門部門を經て協議して行く意味に於て 六十八名と云ふ 委員を選出する樣に なつたのであります。
從つて 文化部の動きが も少し 明確に 世間の人へ 示さるるには 日時が 經なければ ならぬと思ひます。
それから 文化部に於ては どう云ふ方針で進むかは 非常に 困難な 問題でありまナが 總力聯盟本部に於てこの實踐要綱を 正確に定めて居るので これに 從つて行く積りであります、皆樣も 何卒 この筋に 從ひ 協力して 戴き度いのです。
それでは 各方面に於て 如何なる形ちで それ等を 實行してゆくか、又は その組織なり 準備、方法 これ等は 漸次 決めてゆくことにして 此處に 居られる 皆樣は 御自身の 仕事を この實踐要綱に 從はしめる一方、一般國民を 指導、若くは 感化して行く重要なる任務に 居られる方＜41＞々で ありますから 映畵、演劇、美術、文學、音樂等各方面 擧つて 同じ目標、同じ氣持ちて一般國民ヘ 臨んで呼びかけるならば 一億一心分散せずこの使命を 完全に 果し得るものと思ひます。
又 各部門に 居られる 皆樣が 國策に沿らて、即ち今日 國家が 要求する方面へ 國民を 導びいて行く上に 若し困難な點があるならば この文化部が 何時でも その協議に 預る積りであります。
要するに 文化部が 文化人を 導ひいで ゆくとか 引張つて行くと 云ふよりも 皆樣御自身が 自ら進んで今日の國策に副ふ 文化を創造して ゆくべきものだと 思ひます。

―それで 文化部が 今後最も力瘤を入れて 働き掛けるべき 國民層は 八十萬の內地人側でも なければ 三百萬の 半島智識層でもなく 實に眞先に此等 二千萬の 文盲階級に 向けらるるべきだと存じます。
文化に 遲れた 此等の民衆を その儘 ほつたらかにして 置かない樣にすベきたと思ひます。
一つの例でありますが、今年の志願兵の數を 見ますれぱ 十三萬名以上に逹したそうであります。十三萬と云へばほぼ一戶から一人つゞの割あてで、實に 十三萬の家庭が 動き、十三萬人の 母が 動いたことになります。
それから 現に志願兵は 都會よりも 大部分 農山漁村から 出て居ますが、尙更 この 志願兵の母たちは文字と餘り緣のない人ぱかりであります。
それと 申すのは 今日 二十歲前後の 靑年を持つには その 母親の＜42＞歲が 四十以上 五十に達せねば なりませんので 日韓併合して 今漸く三十年であります故 これ等の母は 合併前の女達ちで 學問を受ける 機會に 惠まれで居なかつたのです。今後、十年間は 恐らく 斯ふ云ふ 現象が續くだらうと思ひます。十年以後からは志願兵の 母たちも 小學校や高等女學校の出身者になるものでせうが。
それだからとて 今急に 之等の 母親連を 學校へ 伴れて來て 敎育させる譯にも 行きませんし。
もし 之等の 母が 敎養ある 婦人であつたならば今年も二倍、三倍の應募者を出しだに 違ひありません。
無論 文化運動が 志願兵養成にのみ重きを 置くべきでは ありませぬが我等はやはり この時局下、志願兵の母のことを 眞先きに考へたいのです。
要するに この 文化に 惠まれなかつた 二千萬の 文盲層に對し 敎化しなければ なりませぬが、そうするには國語普及運動の如き啓蒙運動も無論必要でありましようが もつと必要なことは 目と耳とで敎ヘる處の演劇、映畵、小說の如きの文化運動を 起すのが 大事な事だと 思ひます。ですから何卒文化部の仕事の重點は是非この點に置いて進ませて戴きたいのです。

矢鍋文化部長―そうですね、これは私の偏見の樣でありますが 日本の 今迄の文化が進化して來た過程を見ますと我が國は外來の文化を吸收するのに比較的 秀れた力を以て居る點が、ややもすれば 日本人は 眞似を うまく するとか 或は創造力が ないとか 斯ふ云ふ樣に 誤解される傾向がありました。然し 時として 文化が進步する過程に於て 最初には 凡ゆる物事を 或は眞似することもありまようが それが蓄積されると同時に 他の秀れた 創造的の 或物が 出で來るものと 私は考へで居ます。又 此の模倣と云ふものは或る 優秀な事柄が あるとすれば 之又 他人のものたとで 採用せんのも 思かなことで模倣も その實 誰もが 成し得るものでなく非離すベき 事柄でないと 信じます。具體的に御話し 申上げば、我等人間と云ふものが、小學から 大學を卒へる迄 只 敎へを亨け眞似をして 而して 後 漸く 自れを 保存する樣に なるものであつで 言はば これと 畧 同じ 樣なものだと 事ひます。即ち 日本の文化は 今 模倣時代と 創作時代の 境界線にありますので 遠からず その眞價を 發揮することと信じて 居ります。そして 將來 東亞共榮圈が成立てば 泰國、蘭印等 色々の國の 文化を取入れて それを 廣大して 斬新なる文化を作り得るものと 豫想して 居りますだからとして 無條件的に 外來文化に對して 傾けた 心揖けは止めて、日＜45＞本文化の 獨自的なものを 東亞共榮圈の民衆をして 認識せしめ 新らしき文化を樹立することになるを切に要望し 又 今后我國に荷負された 使命だと 思ひます。
白每日學藝部長―兪鎭午氏 何か…
兪普專敎授―現在朝鮮には 內地の文化に對して 或る程度の 理解を持たれる人士が 相當多いと思ひます。然し 彼等が 內地の獨特なる文化を理解する力の 尠ないことは 誠に 遺憾に 思つて居ります。勿論 之れに 對する原因は 內地の學者や 思想家 又は一流哲學者等が之亦 外國文化の 影響を多く 受けで居る關係から 折角 東京迄 遊學に 往つても內地文化に對し 尊敬するとか 認識する點があるとか することに 多少 粗忽たつた原因が あるのではないかと 思ひます だが 然し 日本文化は 飛躍的に 進展して居りますので 之等の 觀念は遠からず 是正出來ると思ひます。例へば 是迄 西田哲學が 非常に關心を 持たれ 我等學徙も 熱心に 之を 硏讚して居る點からも その傾向を窺はれます。
も一つ 申上げたいことは 朝鮮の人の中には 國語に堪能なる 人々は國語を通じ 內地文學とか歐米各國の文化に 親んで居りますが 國語を 語れない人々の爲に 之れは 文人協會の仕事になるかも 知りませぬが 內地の優秀な 作品を 朝鮮語にで 飜譯し 成る丈け 內地の作品に親ましめる樣に するのが 大變良いことたろうと存じます。從來 それ相應の飜譯物もあり 秀れた文士に 依賴しても 原稿料が 合はぬので 意の如くならなかつた感がありましたが この點も 文人協會と文化部とが 協力してもつと もつと 旺んにしたいものです。そう云ふ樣な 例としては この前火野葦平氏作「麥と兵隊」を 飜譯して出したことが あるが 甚た よかつたのです。この程度でも 內地の作品を 飜譯して 見るのも 甚た効果的なことと存じます。
白每日學藝部長―松田さん 畵家の立場から どう御考ヘに なりますか。
松田文化部員―別に 之と云つて申上げる お話がありません。
田中文化部員―素より 我國では 支那と朝鮮よりも文化を受入れて日本的なる 思想とか 風流を 加味した 古代文學をも 認識せしめたら よからうと思ひます。
白每日學藝部長―辛島敎授 支那文學が 日本に入つて來て どう云ふ風に消化されたのでしよう。
辛島城大敎授―さうですね、たつた一つ 强調したいことは 日本文化の持續性といゝませうか 即ち儒敎の樣なものを見でも わかりますが 儒敎は本場である支那よりも 却つて その眞價が內地や朝鮮で 發揮してゐるし支那では形式的に殘つてゐるのが 今の現狀でありまして、その專門的部分に 對しては略します。先程、農村漁村 又は 鑛山方面の文化問題に於いて、金東煥氏より お話しがあります＜46＞た通り、此の方面に演劇、映畵等で文化を發展せしめよううとするのは大贊成です。それにこゝで忘れではならないことは知識階級です。現在文化部や文化人に對して 多大なる關心を持つてゐるのは却つて知識層より 此の知識層の統合如何が もつとも 大きな問題だと思ひます。これに於いては我等文化部內部の人からと 度々 さう云ふ機會を造つて さう云ふ方面を 如何に指導すベきかに、建設的に協力する必要があると思ひます。
白每日學藝部長―金億さん 朝鮮の詩人層に對して協力指導の立場からひとごとお話ございませんでせうか。
金文化部委員―先程俞敎授からの飜譯に關してのお話がありましたが 實際飜譯だけは文化人誰でも關心を 持つことと思ひます。私も 飜譯に關してひとごと申し上げのいのです。何處の文化にしでも その文化に ほんとの 親しみを持つとすれば、飜譯物位では その輪廓だけは 把握するかも知りませんが その眞意、その獨特な味覺等には 到底接することが 出來ません。だから 文盲人には「四十から 勉强を始める」との如く、今からでも 晚くないから 國語普及に極力努力して 平易な內容のものから 漸次讀ませるのが 適當だと思ふし、又それが 內鮮一體上にも 相當効果があるんぢやないかと思ひます。
白每日學藝部長―先程、田中氏から 國民生活の一部分が 自ら文化の中へ侵入する樣努力なさるとのお話でしたが それは結局 我等が持つてゐる職能を 充分發揮して 國民大衆を文化へ捷せらるる意妹でないかと 私は推測するんですが かう云ふ意味に於いて、安田さん、映畵方面に關して一つ御意見を……。
安田映協理事長―簡單に 申し上げます。現在朝鮮で取扱つてゐる映畵は大部分內地や外國物です。朝鮮映畵も今まで 幾何かの作品を出してはゐましたが その構成からも、技術からも非常に貧弱です。一般國民を指導する入場から逆行することが 往々ありまして困ります。これに對して國民總力聯盟文化部は 映畵方面を いかに指導するんでせうか、矢鍋先生の御意見をうかゞひたいと存じます。
矢鍋文化部長―どうも、突然、さう云ふむつかしい問題に 提起れて…私の意見としては、今の朝鮮には俳優は後㢠しにして、一般國民を 指導する程の人物が あまり ゐないんではないかと見てゐますが、先づ その方面の人物を養成する他、方法はないんぢやないでせうか。
牧山演劇協會長―朝鮮映畵の貧弱さについて お話し申し上げたいのです。その一つの原因は 旣成映畵を充分、消化せしめることが出來ない故です。即ち全朝鮮に いくらもない映畵劇場の 上映だけでは 一萬圓や二萬圓位の費用なら さうでもないんでせうが 十萬圓もかゝる程の製作物となると、ソロパンが合ないんです。それが 內地なり外國へ輪出させる程の作品であれば 問題はむりませんが、さうも いかないし、それで、今まで＜47＞その狀態から 足ふみをしてゐるわけであります。第二の原因は、十萬なり二十萬圓の巨金を心やすく出して吳れ篤志家が ゐないからです。第三は 資金の不足で撮影機なり その他の凡ての物を內地の方で つかひ古びたものを つかふ樣になるのでいゝ映畵が製れないのだと 思ふんですが―
辛島城大敎授―それに、また、優秀なる脚本家演出家がゐないのも 原因に なるんぢやないのでせうかね？ですが これからは その組織も 改善して、大いに 飛躍出來ると思ひます。
兪普專敎授―この間「家なき天使」の試寫を見ましたが 朝鮮映畵がそれ程まで發展しだとは 思ひませんでしたね、聞くどころに依れば「授業料」もよかつたざうですね、「家なき天使」も近來希れなる作品だと 思ひますが、映畵技術も 非常に 發達してゐました。
田中文化部員―さうですね「家なき天使」は よかつたですね 私は二度も見たんですよ 撮影技術も 演出も 前例になかつた作品だと 思ひます。もうすぐに 內地映畵水準を追ひつくでせう。
牧山演劇協會長―貧弱な設備にも拘らず それ程までの作品を出して吳れたその內面の苦心を察してもらひたいですね。
田中文化部長―資本も 凡ての設備も 不足でありませうが 將來を見込んで 大いに活躍して頂きたいのです。併しそれがね、一つの難問題は朝鮮映畵に對しての國民心理です。外國映畵を見てゐた彼等が 在來の朝鮮映畵と親しめないのは 無理もないでせうが、だからと云つて 見もしない中から 一から十まで 惡評する そのことだけは やめてもらひたいのです佛蘭西が戰爭に敗れてからも、あいかわらず 優秀なる映畵を 出してゐるのを見でも その國民心理如何が察せられます。
