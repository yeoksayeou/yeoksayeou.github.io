Unexplained Fatigue Common in Winter, Tuberculosis, Beriberi, Chronic Gastrointestinal Diseases Warrant Caution
						By Okabe Taku, M.D.

Everyone experiences fatigue after working, but there are people who often feel fatigue or lethargy despite having no particular reason (mentally or physically).
Generally, when fatigued from work, one can easily recover by resting appropriately or sleeping. However, fatigue or lethargy with unclear causes, in other words, fatigue and lethargy arising from the prodrome or latency of an illness, cannot possibly be recovered from by merely resting or sleeping until the cause is eliminated.

Diseases That Follow Fatigue

First, considering the symptoms when a disease is latent, tuberculosis (結核) can be mentioned. In already developed tuberculosis, fatigue is always accompanied by symptoms like coughing (咳嗽), fever (發熱), and gastrointestinal disorders (胃腸障害), but often, unexplained fatigue and lethargy appear as initial symptoms (初發).

Next, regarding beriberi (脚氣), noticeable symptoms include slight numbness or edema (浮腫) in the lips or legs (脚部). Besides these, even when one just feels fatigued or lethargic, lacks appetite, and cannot focus on work, it is necessary to check for beriberi or vitamin B deficiency (缺乏症).

Also, when there are gastrointestinal disorders (胃腸障碍), fatigue and lethargy are accompanied, especially common in chronic diseases like gastric atony (胃아토니) and gastroptosis (胃下垂). It is particularly severe during gastric catarrh (胃腸가다루) when digestive function is impaired and one feels like having diarrhea (泄瀉氣分), but this is often fatigue and lethargy stemming from nutritional decline and general weakness.

Countermeasures for Fatigue

So, what is the cause that brings about this fatigue or lethargy? It is true that the causes differ according to the disease, but ultimately, fatigue, simply explained, is fundamentally caused by the decline of the body's metabolic function, leading to altered distribution or deficiency of nutrients.
Metabolism (신진대사) is the general term for the processes where nutrients in the body are broken down, assimilated, absorbed, or excreted. The metabolism of carbohydrates (含水炭素), which constitute the majority of our nutrient intake, involves their breakdown into glycogen (그리꼬-겐). If one contracts diseases like tuberculosis, <76> gastrointestinal disease, or beriberi, the toxic effects of pathogens (病菌) and, on the other hand, nutritional decline cause the production of this glycogen to decrease, severely hindering metabolic processes and leading to feelings of fatigue and lethargy.
According to recent research, it has become clear that carbohydrates require the power of the vitamin B complex to be utilized as glycogen in metabolism.
Therefore, to invigorate the body's metabolism, prevent fatigue, and further cultivate resistance against diseases, it is necessary to supplement this glycogen along with the vitamin B complex to promote the overall activation of physiological functions. In this sense, taking "Wakamoto" (『와까모도』), famous as a complex medicinal yeast preparation (複合藥用酵母劑), is recommended (推獎됩니다).

**Summary**

This 1941 article by Dr. Okabe Taku discusses unexplained fatigue common in winter, linking it not just to overwork but potentially to underlying conditions like latent tuberculosis, beriberi (vitamin B deficiency), and chronic gastrointestinal problems. It explains fatigue physiologically as a result of impaired metabolism, specifically the poor processing of carbohydrates into glycogen, often hindered by disease toxins or nutritional deficiencies. The article strongly recommends taking "Wakamoto," a complex yeast supplement rich in vitamin B complex, digestive enzymes, glycogen, amino acids, and other nutrients, claiming it revitalizes cells, boosts metabolism, strengthens digestion, detoxifies tuberculosis toxins, and builds overall resistance to combat fatigue and illness. From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it reflects medical advice during Japan's colonial rule and wartime mobilization, blending modern concepts like vitamins and metabolism with the promotion of a specific Japanese commercial product (Wakamoto) as a panacea for fatigue and various ailments prevalent at the time. It also highlights concerns about tuberculosis and beriberi, significant public health issues in colonial Korea.
