鑑賞
						李泰俊

My fondest memories of reading books inevitably fly back to my Tōkyō days. For three or four days at a time, without even washing my face, with the spring rain-drizzled *amado* (雨戶 - rain shutters) left dimly shut, the Turgenev novels I read tucked inside a *tanzen-dotera* (丹前褞袍 - padded lounging robe); the bare-headed paths of Musashino, autumnal until the end of November; the narrow paths (*hosomichi*, 細道) through the oak forests; the Chekhov short stories I read while strolling there; the "Nihilism" (虛無) that excited me alongside Bazarov; Otënka, Lidochka; and the tears of sorrow (哀愁) shed with Katya—from what music, what art, what poetry could I have been so moved to tears by life? Thinking back now, it was my literary springtime, my "Age of Poetry in Reading."

I haven't yet reread any of Turgenev's works. As for Chekhov, I recently reread several of his short stories. I was astonished by every work I reread. It was because the knowledge I had previously gained from reading them was not reliable or complete at all. In "Chastity" (貞操), where I only remembered the heroine's actions, I now noticed the author's technique, more mature than in any other work; in "A Dreary Story" (우울한 이야기), I could only now taste that young Katya's state of mind was, rather than old Professor Nikolai's<126>, a despair towards life that was perhaps even more profound.

The power to appreciate a work of art, being a matter of mental strength rather than physical strength, must grow throughout one's life. Just as it's said that one should read works like "Faust" after the age of 50, it seems that the true face of all prose, its prose-like quality, waits for the reader themself to first reach a state of "prose" in life before revealing itself. If merely tasting it is like this, how overwhelming a life task must it be to write prose splendidly!

These days, I feel I've gained some understanding of others' works. Is this what "freedom from doubts" (不惑 - age 40) requires, such seniority (年祖)? Oh, let my holy "Age of Prose in Reading" begin, allowing me henceforth to savor the true taste (眞味) of literature!

The difficulty of appreciation (鑑賞難) is not confined to literature alone. In an essay titled "Flower Bed" (花壇), I once vented strong liberal indignation (自由主義的 義憤) against a garden owner who loved his trees by cutting them with shears and binding them with wire. Nowadays, I don't think I could write such words so boldly. It was the winter before last. I went to a nursery to buy plum blossoms (梅花) and happened to watch them being potted. A graying, bent-backed gardener (*uekiya-san*, 植木屋樣) sat inside the greenhouse, playing the role of a miniature Creator (造物主). First, he trimmed the tree (*mok*, 木) as if giving it a haircut, selected a suitable flat pot, matched the tree's aspect to the pot's aspect, added supplementary soil, built a slight slope near the base, placed a rock there, planted winter aconite (福壽草) beneath the rock, and below the hill where the aconite bloomed, he spread pure white sand as if a stream would soon flow there, and then smiled faintly... Of course, it was undoubtedly vulgar taste (俗趣). But considering the old *uekiya-san*'s absorbed affection (愛撫) and the fact that this skill was all he possessed, it felt that evaluating him a bit more highly, or casting a loving gaze upon even such a small, contained piece of nature shaped by his hands, would hardly be an abuse of excessive tolerance. Just a few days ago, I watched a friend who had bought daffodils (*suisen*, 水仙) shape them.<127> The way he sliced away only the white bulb sections with the tip of a sharp knife, his hands trembling lest he damage the flower buds, like a young girl's veins, showed he was not cruel to the daffodils but rather someone who cherished them more dearly than anyone. It was less a processing (加工) of nature than care (愛護), and the time spent doing so was itself a splendid appreciation (翫賞) of nature. The Creator would surely not be angry with them. Rather, He would smile.

Calligraphy and painting (書畵), and old pottery (古器), are also among the difficult things to appreciate. I used to like Western painting, but gradually I've come to like Eastern painting. I feel more attachment to ink wash painting (水墨) than to color painting (彩色). I'm more interested in works where the artist's seal and signature (落款) are unassuming. Even with Wandang's calligraphy (阮書), the taste I get looking at it with this year's eyes is different from last year's. Vessels acquired with difficulty at one time now become disliked, even to the point of hiding them away in a cupboard.

Nature, why did you not give us perfect eyes from birth? But perhaps Nature would answer us thus:
"It is not that I did not give you perfect eyes from birth. It is likely because you yourselves have dimmed those eyes with your distracting thoughts and desires (雜念雜慾)."<128>

**Summary**

Yi T'aejun reflects on how his appreciation for literature, particularly Russian authors like Turgenev and Chekhov experienced during his youth in Tokyo, has deepened with age and life experience. He contrasts his earlier, more superficial readings with the nuanced understanding he gains upon rereading, likening this maturation to reaching an "Age of Prose" in both life and reading. He extends this idea to appreciating other arts, recounting how his youthful indignation towards manipulated nature (like bonsai or flower arranging) has softened into an understanding of the affectionate care involved. He concludes that appreciating art, like calligraphy or pottery, is a difficult, evolving skill, perhaps hindered not by nature's design, but by human "distracting thoughts and desires." From a historical perspective, the article reveals a Korean intellectual's nostalgia for formative experiences in colonial Japan (Tokyo, Musashino), his deep engagement with Western (specifically Russian) literature, and his contemplation of traditional East Asian aesthetics (gardening, ink painting, calligraphy) alongside Japanese cultural practices (*uekiya-san*). It reflects the complex cultural landscape of colonial Korea, where influences from Japan, the West, and Korea's own traditions coexisted and were reflected upon by its intellectuals.

鑑賞
						李泰俊
나의 冊 읽던 즐거운 追憶은 아모래도 東京時節로 날러가군 한다. 사흘 나흘씩 세수도 않고, 봄비 뿌리는 「아마도」는 굼게 담은 채, 「단넨도꼬」속에서 보던 트르게네프의 長篇들, 11月 末까지 가을인 武藏野의 무발머리 길들, 참나무 숲의 「호소미찌」들, 거기를 거닐며 읽던 체호프의 短篇들, 빠사로프와 함게 흥분하던 「虛無」, 오텡카, 리-도치카, 그리고 카-챠와 더브러 먹읒던 哀愁의 눈물들, 어느 音樂, 어느 美術, 어느 詩에서 이처럼 인생을 눈물에 사모쳐 감동하였으랴! 지금 생각하면 나의 文學的 靑春時節이였고 나의 「讀書의 韻文時代」였었다.

트르게네프의 것은 아직 再讀한 것이 없다. 체호프의 것으로 短篇인 것은 최근에 맺 가지 再讀해 보았다. 나는 再讀하는 작품마다에서 놀랐다. 전에 읽고 가졌던 그 작품에의 知識이란 하나도 믿을만한, 온전한 것이 못됨에서였다. 女主人公의 行動만 기억이 되던 「貞操」에서 作者의 어느 作品에서보다 무르녹은 技術이 이번에야 눈에 띄였고, 「우울한 이야기」에서 젊은 카-챠의 心境이 니코라이＜126＞ 老敎授보다 차라리 深刻한 人生에의 絶望이였던 것은 이번에야 비로서 맛볼 수 있었다.
한 藝術品을 鑑賞해 내는 힘도 體力이 아니라 心力인 이상, 일생을 두고 자랄 것이다. 「파우스트」같은 것은 50以後에 읽으란 말도 있거니와, 모든 散文의 散文다운 面目이란, 독자 그 자신이 인생으로 먼저 散文에 이르기를 기다려서 보히는 듯 하다. 맛보기만도 그러허니 散文을 훌륭히 쓴다는 노릇이야 얼마나 벅찬 人生政治리오!
나는 요즘에 이르러서 남의 작품에 다소 터득이 생긴다. 「不惑」이란 이처럼 年祖를 必要하는 것인가! 오! 나로 하여금 이로부터 文學의 眞味를 享樂시킬, 나의 거룩한 「讀書散文時代」여!

鑑賞難이란 文學에서 만도 아니다. 나는 「花壇」이란 글에서, 나무를 가새로 짜르며 철사로 동이며 사랑하는 庭園主에게 强烈한 自由主義的 義憤을 吐한 적이 있다. 요즘만 같어도 나는 그런 말을 果敢히 써내지 못할 것 같다. 再昨年 겨울이다. 梅花를 사러 어느 花園에 갔다가, 마침 梅花 登盆시키는 것을 구경하였다. 半白이나 된, 허리 굽은 「우에끼야상」은 溫室속에 드러앉어 조그만 造物主노릇을 하고 있었다. 먼저 *木을 理髮을 시키듯 다듬고, 그 나무에 맞을 발딱한 盆을 고르고, 나무面과 盆面을 마추고, 補土를 하고, 그루 가까이를 비스듬이 언덕을 짖고 거기 바위를 하나 놓고, 그 바위 밑에 福壽草를 심고, 福壽草 피는 언덕 밑에는 얼마 안 가면 냇물이라는 듯이 새하얀 모새를 깔고 그리고 빙그레 웃는... 물론 俗趣임엔 틀림없었다. 그러나 늙은 「우에끼야상」의 그 골독한 愛撫와 그 재주하나 밖엔 없는 자신에는, 좀 더 높이 평가해 주는 것이나, 또 그의 손으로 담겨지는 그런 한 그릇 앙당한 自然우에도 사랑하는 시선을 보내는 것이 過히 寬大의 濫用일리는 없을 것 같이 느껴지는 것 이였다. 메칠 전에도 어느 친구가 水仙을 사다 *形을 만드는 것을 구경하였다.＜127＞ 날카러운 칼끝으로 少女의 靜脈같은 꽃순은 다칠세 손을 떨어가며 흰 살쪽만 제며내는 모양은, 그는 水仙에게 殘酷한 사람이 아니라 누구보다도 水仙을 살뜰히 사랑하는 사람이였다. 自然에의 加工이라기보다는 차라리 愛護요 그렇게 하는 그 동안도 훌륭한 자연에의 翫賞이였다. 造物主는 결코 이들에게 憤怒하지 않을 것이다. 차라리 微笑하리라.

書畵, 古器도 鑑賞中에 어러운 것의 하나다. 西洋畫가 좋더니 차츰 東洋畫가 좋와진다. 彩色보다 水墨에 더 愛着이 간다. 落款도 무관심한 것에 오히려 興味가 있다. 같은 阮書도 昨年 눈으로 보던 맛과 今年 눈으로 보는 맛이 다시 다르다. 한 때는 무리를 해 入手한 그릇이 벽장 속에 감취두기 조차 싫여진다.
自然이여 어찌하여 우리에게 낳면서부터 完全한 눈을 주지 않었는가? 그러나 자연은 우리에게 이렇게 대답할 지도 모른다.
「너이에게 낳며서부터 완전한 눈을 주지 않은 것은 아니다. 너이들의 雜念雜慾으로 말미암아 그 눈을 흐렷뜨린 때문이리라」＜128＞
