**The Flag at the Front** (獨) von Schirach

Forward! Forward! The advance bugle sounds thunderously
Forward! Forward! The young have no fear
Though we die and disappear
Germany, you will shine in glory

Forward! Forward! The advance bugle sounds thunderously
Forward! Forward! The young have no fear
The road ahead is long and harsh
But the young have no fear
The flag flutters at the front line
Risking the future all at once, we all
Walk through the night and hardship for Hitler
The youth fly the flag of freedom and bread (팡)
Our flag flies at the front line
Our flag is a new era
The flag will guide us forever
Yes! The flag will overcome death!

Youth! Youth! Warriors of the coming day
Youth! Youth! Those who will bear the great task ahead
Yes, those who try to block us <140>
Will fall before our fists
Youth! Youth! Warriors of the coming day
Youth! Youth! Those who will bear the great task ahead
Führer, we are all yours
We are sin, we are all yours
The flag flutters at the front line
Risking the future all at once, we all
Walk through the night and hardship for Hitler
The youth fly the flag of freedom and bread
Our flag flies at the front line
Our flag is a new era
The flag will guide us forever
Yes, the flag will overcome death!

**We Believe** (獨) Wolfram Brockmeier

Germany, though we die, you
Will never die
You will blossom, we will wither
All we make is a gift for you
All we offer is a sacrifice (祭物) for you
In the future, when sons are born and grandsons grow
They will live, work, and fight for you
Oh, Germany!

Love offered to you cannot be excessive
You are the eternal, abundant womb
Your unceasing spring quenches our thirst
Brewing wine, bearing grain
If there is one who betrays (背反) you, one blinded, possessed by demons
One who defiles the mother's name
You, oh Germany
Germany, sacred motherhood, receiving your breath
Even while sleeping, the soul leaps, rises, grows, and ripens!
You, our old, old friend, faithful companion
Land of eternity (悠遠), of abundance, that embraces and raises us <141>
Oh Germany, the spring waters sing of you under the dreamy, hazy moonlight
When the blue-silver light awakens above the eaves (채양위), above the alleys
Temples (伽藍) praise you, castle walls (城廓) hold you fast
You bloom in the broom (金雀) on your hills, bloom in the linden trees (普提樹) of the marketplace.

**Boy Volunteer Soldier** (獨) Georg Britting

Under the hazy Flanders sun
It's truly hard to pierce the smoke screen
When the very first shrapnel flies and bursts
The faces of beardless boy soldiers
Startled, they completely flatten themselves

Receiving orders, the garrison soldier who came with the boys
Crawls through the turnip field together with them
Seeing the boy soldier, the senior soldier (큰 兵丁) says
"How long have you boys been soldiers?
Has it been ten weeks?" The answer is "Yes, sir."

The boy soldier answers, "It has been ten weeks for us
Ten weeks ago, the war broke out
Since then, through fields of grass and open plains
We have run, jumped, and crawled
Through the land of our fatherland."
"The place where we stand now is Flanders
Counting you mister and all the other misters,

Your duty is like this; for us, it's ten weeks
Other than that, there's no difference at all
Ah, the smoke screen is clearing
Do you hear the call 'Charge!'?"

Here, the boys sang a great song <142>
Stumbling, feet caught in the turnip field
Falling down and getting up as they went
Lying flat on the earth, they sang the song.
The garrison soldier sang along too

Under the hazy Flanders sun
When the shrapnel flew and burst
There was nothing that could be discerned

The song echoed far away
Shaking the hearts of countless people
Arousing great emotion
Thus the boys sang the song

When night came and moonlight shone through the fog
Still, that song could be heard by the ear
That song resounding beyond all things
That song holding eternal life. <143>

**Summary**

This article from the December 1941 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents Korean translations by Chu Yohan of three German poems: "The Flag at the Front" (von Schirach), "We Believe" (Wolfram Brockmeier), and "Boy Volunteer Soldier" (Georg Britting). The poems glorify German nationalism, militarism, Hitler, and the sacrifice of soldiers, particularly youth, in war. Published under Japanese colonial rule just as the Pacific War was escalating, the inclusion of pro-Nazi German poems translated by a prominent Korean intellectual is historically significant. It reflects the pervasive influence of Axis ideology and propaganda within the Japanese empire, including colonial Korea, and highlights the complex position of Korean intellectuals navigating the political pressures of the era.
