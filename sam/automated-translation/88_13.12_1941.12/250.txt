The Spires of Oxford - Winifred M. Letts

This poem is by an Irish woman poet. The original poem has a very<144> good rhythm, making it especially well-suited for reading aloud.

I pass by
And see the spires of Oxford
Piercing the pearl-colored sky
Seeing the gray spires, now I
Think of the Oxford classmates
Who went out to the battlefield to die

Oxford's joyful golden days
Fled like an arrow
When the old colleges
Looked down on them playing joyfully
Suddenly the noisy bugle sound was heard
It was the bugle declaring war
War!
They threw down what they were playing with

Leaving the peaceful homeland and rivers and mountains
Leaving the finely trimmed grassy grounds of Oxford
They ran to the bloody battlefield
There they cast away their joyful youth
For the fatherland, for God

Blessed gentlemen
God will grant you rest in peace
For goodness' sake
Tossing off cap and gown
You who went out holding military uniforms and guns
God will lead you to a beautiful blessed land
A blessed land better than Oxford—<145>

With an Old Horse
- In Time of 'The Breaking of Nations' -
- Thomas Hardy

This poem departs from the miserable scenes (처참한 일) or the sorrow that comes from them, which we usually see in war poetry. While being a war poem, this poet sings of simple primitive life and nature—that is, plowing fields, sowing seeds, or harvesting—or our youth, or love, and so on. The point that these things transcend war in human society and continued yesterday, today, and will continue eternally in the future is thought to be extremely interesting.
- Translator -

With a nodding old horse
There is only a man plowing the field alone
Slowly and quietly
As if dozing, leisurely

Faint smoke without flame
Rises thinly from a pile of grass.

Though an era passes away
Only this scene will continue unchanged

Over there a maiden and her lover
Come whispering affectionately
Though crude history disappears
Such whispers of humans will last forever— <146>
<144-146>

**Summary**

This article presents Korean translations by No Ch'ŏnmyŏng of two British war poems: "The Spires of Oxford" by Winifred M. Letts and Thomas Hardy's "In Time of 'The Breaking of Nations'". Letts' poem evokes the patriotic sacrifice of Oxford students going off to war, while Hardy's contrasts the transient nature of conflict with the enduring cycles of rural life and love. The translator's notes emphasize the poems' artistic merit and thematic depth, particularly Hardy's focus on timeless human experience over wartime destruction. From a historical standpoint, the publication date (December 1, 1941) is significant, occurring just before the attack on Pearl Harbor and the major escalation of the Pacific War. Featuring Allied (British) war poetry, especially Hardy's poem which foregrounds peace and continuity, in a Korean magazine under Japanese rule might reflect a complex cultural space, possibly hinting at underlying desires for peace or contemplation of universal human values amidst intensifying global conflict and Japanese wartime controls.
