Article Title: 朝鮮運動의 現陣容展望 (A View of the Current Lineup of the Korean Movement)

Article Type: 소식 (News/Report)

**Chosŏn Farmers' Alliance (朝鮮農民總同盟)**
By Yi Chi-t’ak (李智鐸)

It was already a bygone era. It was a time when the old site of Kwanghwamun Gate (光化門) still remained, and the current white building (白堊舘) had not yet taken its place. On a late autumn evening, accompanied by two or three friends, I hastened my steps towards Yŏngch'umun Gate (迎秋門), hugging the wall of the old Kyŏngbok Palace (慶福宮). The wide street in front of the not-so-prosperous "Haet'ŏap" was enveloped in the fading evening sunlight, creating quite a desolate scene. However, more than anything else, the rattling sound of the small gate-window hanging on the upper floor of Kwanghwamun Gate, shaken by the destructive wind (破風), made my heart feel intensely desolate. That small thing, rattling in the wind, opening and closing by itself, seemed like a lament embodying the historical background and sorrow of the large building, its main fortress.

But why now do I find myself repeating this old story from the past like this?

Recently, on a certain occasion, after a long time, I visited the Tŏgwŏn Building (德元삘딍) in central Chongno (鍾路) and took another glimpse at the camp of the Chosŏn Farmers' Alliance (朝鮮農總). At the entrance of the building, long signboards for "Sin'ganhoe (新幹會)," "Nodong Ch'ongdongmaeng (勞總 - General Federation of Labor)," "Nongch'ong (農總 - Farmers' Alliance)," etc., stood neatly side by side, equal in height. My quick steps knocked on the door of the Nongch'ong office on the 3rd floor within just a few seconds.

Inside the office, the tables and chairs I had seen before were arranged here and there. On the tables, only various newspapers, like evening and morning editions, were scattered about. Where had the countless activists (X士), who used to bustle about, vanished to? Where were their voices, from which blood drops seemed to spurt, now whispering? The incessantly ringing telephone bell, the thumping of busy footsteps, the numerous small meetings (小會議) in this corner and that corner – all these past scenes of its prosperity made one feel an even greater desert-like desolation when faced with its current decline (衰沈?). However, the author intends not to judge whether this is decline or progress in terms of its indicative nature.

A certain central committee member, guarding the old remnants of Nongch'ong, spoke in a tone seemingly filled with deep emotion:
"Well, what can I say? Is there anything specific happening? We are just maintaining the framework of the central body, that's all. Of course, we exchange documents with the branches (支會) in the provinces, but even when disputes arise, the headquarters is in no position to dispatch anyone. Even things like communication expenses (遞信費)..."
He trailed off, seemingly reluctant to continue, uttering only a few opinions as if forced by the author's probing.

However, one cannot dismiss the entire Nongch'ong lineup based solely on the appearance of its central body. Its [revolutionary] strength (XX力) is rather concentrated and latent in the distributed networks across various regions. Branches encompassing over 3,000 members exist in four locations throughout Hamgyŏng North and South provinces (咸南北): Chŏngp'yŏng (定平), Hongwŏn (洪原), Tanch'ŏn (端川), and Yŏnghŭng (永興). Furthermore, in Kyŏngsang Province (慶尙道), it holds significant influence in Yangsan (梁山), Kimhae (金海), P'ungsan (豊山), and Ŭiryŏng (宜寧). Additionally, Yonggang (龍江) and Kangso (江西) in the P'yŏngan South (平南) region are quite substantial. Synthesizing this, the total number of affiliated associations nationwide is 43, and the total number of members counts over 30,000.

Such is the approximate reality of Nongch'ong, but its objective condition, with the ban on assembly (集會禁止) not yet lifted, makes it utterly impossible for it to exercise its functions. <12>

**The Full Picture of Kŭnuhwoe (槿友會)**
By Kim Ŭn-hŭi (金銀姬)

1
Kŭnuhwoe (槿友會 - Rose of Sharon Society) was born in July 1927, aiming for the "improvement of the status of Korean women" and their "firm solidarity," and grew with irresistible force (破竹の勢). By 1928, the total number of branches reached over 40, encompassing 3,000 members.

However, their organization was not a [revolutionary] organization (XX的 組織) based on specific life relations but merely a contingent gathered under vague liberal ideas, thus failing to show any mass practice. Of course, among them were a small number of conscious elements with class consciousness (階級意識), but even they could not carry out activities to strengthen their independence. Moreover, looking at the constituent elements organized at that time, most were housewives, followed by intellectual women without regular jobs, and the remaining very small number were women workers and peasants. Therefore, a definite guiding spirit could not, in fact, be established from a class perspective. If they had been masses organized through struggle (X爭), they would have marched towards a specific struggle (X爭) goal, at least for a time. However, most of them were reluctantly drawn under the Kŭnuhwoe banner through persuasion (勸誘), and furthermore, even factional consciousness (分派的 意識) spread among them. Leaders split into four or five factions (4分 5裂) and did everything possible to protect and expand their own factions, causing some of the masses to feel disillusioned and even withdraw voluntarily (自退).

In the meantime, due to the rapid changes in objective circumstances (客觀的 情勢), the laboring and peasant masses (勞農大衆) generally developed class consciousness, while on the other hand, they became leftist (左翼化) and [radicalized] (XX化). Conversely, the dispersed middle strata became powerless and isolated. Thus, although there was some call for dissolution (解消) from a few conscious elements within Kŭnuhwoe, it failed to produce any results and naturally disappeared (自然消滅).

2
Originally, an independent organization solely for women might feel necessary in a pre-modern feudal society (封建社會). However, feudalism, which is disappearing under the infiltration and growth of capitalism (資本主義), and its resistant elements can never be the main force (主體的 部隊) of the [revolution] (XX) but only an auxiliary force (副隨的 部隊); this is a principle.

However, ignoring this principle in Korea, claiming that because it is a resistant element against feudal remnants (封建的 殘滓), it holds its own [revolutionary] strength (XX力) and organizational body responsible for a historical mission at a certain stage, was an action arising from a misconception (錯覺) that misunderstood organizational principles. Therefore, placing any great expectation (企待) on it was nothing more than a fleeting cloud (浮雲).

Of course, because capitalism did not develop normally and possessed atypical economic organization (츠이型的 經濟組織), women themselves were subjected not only to capitalist [censored oppression/exploitation] (XX) but also to the double [censored oppression/exploitation] (XX) of feudal [censored oppression/exploitation] (XX). Moreover, it was an unavoidable fact that women were further bound by [censored oppression/exploitation] (XX) due to their unique treatment.

However, their fundamental demand coincides with that of the proletariat in capitalist society. Also, since their [revolutionary] (XX的) direction is the same, women cannot achieve [liberation] (XX) without the proletariat's [revolution] (XX). Therefore, their independent organization or activity holds no significance whatsoever.

Only when they achieve firm class formation (鞏固한 계급적 結成) as part of the entire laboring masses (全勤勞大衆), belonging to their class composition based on their class position and economic relations, will the historical duty of women become clearer step by step, and simultaneously, they will be able to fulfill their entire mission.

3
Nevertheless, stimulated by the birth of the Sin'ganhoe (新幹會), which lacked class consciousness, in 1927, they imitated it exactly, self-identifying as a sister organization (姊妹團體) of Sin'ganhoe and positioning themselves simply as a nationalist movement organization (民族運動團體). Their sole platform decided on a reformist (改良主義的) direction: "to improve the status of Korean women." However, later they went a step further and even adopted a party-like platform (政黨的 綱領) aiming to acquire full political and economic benefits, but this was a reflection of a kind of conceptual consciousness (觀念意識) and not a purification of action gained from any practical struggle (實踐的 XX).

Their only method of movement, besides provincial tours and occasionally inviting famous people (名士) to hold ideological lectures (思想講演), was the so-called annual convention (大會召集). Thus, the growth of their class consciousness could not be expected from this. However, anti-[censored] struggle (反X的XX) by some local organizations, though extremely weak, was not entirely absent. But this was partial struggle, and it cannot be presented as their sole source of pride.

Thus, Kŭnuhwoe, due to its organizational contradiction, failed to carry out any powerful [struggle] (*XX) and has now transformed into a useless organizational body that cannot stand in the ranks of class alignment. This is thought to be not due to a lack of activity or leaders, but because this organization was inherently unable to grow in the current stage, and its constituent elements were generally not troops capable of [struggling] (XX) on the front lines. Therefore, its natural disappearance now is considered a necessary fact in the course of history.

Kŭnuhwoe died like this, but the class advancement of women has grown and strengthened due to objective circumstances.
This is directly proportional to the growth of the class struggle (階級的 XX) of the working class in general, so there is no need to discuss it anew here. (Omitted - original text)
Now, as we are introducing the full picture of Kŭnuhwoe, I intend to conclude this article by presenting their organizational composition by membership and occupation.

**Overview of the Hyŏngp'yŏng Movement (衡平運動)**
By Ŏ Ku-sŏn (魚龜善)

1
1. Humans possess the right to freedom and equality. What meaning can life have for those without the right to freedom and equality?
1. We, who have been in the adverse circumstances of slavery (奴隷) for half a millennium <13>, must find our lost human rights.
1. Rise up! Paekchŏng class (白丁階級)! Gather under this Hyŏngp'yŏng (衡平 - Equality) banner!
This was the declaration raised high when the Paekchŏng class of Korea first cried out for the Hyŏngp'yŏng Movement and was born in Chinju (晉州) on April 25, 1923, initiated by Chang Chi-p’il (張志弼) and 7 others.

Thus, the Hyŏngp'yŏng Movement of Korea, under this declaration, has steadily carried out its organization, training, and struggle (X爭) day after day for the past unwavering 10-odd years. Currently, the number of its branches (支社) is said to be 332, and the number of members (社員) is 387,000.
It is said that merely 19 central committee members represent and control all the affairs of this huge collective body embracing such a large number of members. The current key executives (重要幹部) include Central Chairman Mr. Chang Chi-p’il, Secretary-General Mr. Kim Chong-t’aek (金鍾澤), and others.
Compared to other general social organizations, this group shows financial stability. This is likely because most members have a regular income, so the payment rate of dues (義務金 納入) from each branch is better than other organizations. Consequently, the matter of publishing an official organ (機關紙), resolved at last year's convention, has already been realized, and a monthly magazine titled "Nagaja" (나가자 - Let's Go Forward) has reached its 3rd issue.

2
The original intention behind the founding of this organization, as clearly expressed in the declaration at that time, was rooted in the resistance consciousness (反抗意識) of the Paekchŏng class against Korea's traditional feudal customs (封建的 因襲). In other words, it began to ferment and brew from the confrontational relationship between the Yangban class (兩班階級) and the Paekchŏng class.
Therefore, although thousands of major and minor dispute cases (爭議事件) have occurred since the organization's founding, only 2 or 3 were labor-capital disputes (勞資關係의 爭議), while the rest were entirely dispute cases arising from feudal discrimination in human rights (封建的 人權差別), it is said.
Considering the facts above, it is not unreasonable that the dissolution issue (解消問題), which was temporarily pending at last year's convention, was deemed premature considering the actual situation (實情) of Korean society at the time and remained pending.
A convention of this organization is scheduled for April 24th and 25th this year. What new resolutions regarding its future lineup will emerge from this convention remains to be seen. However, no matter how distortedly developed Korean capitalism may be due to rapid political changes, considering all the current circumstances (情勢) in Korea, for this movement to continue adhering to the declaration from 10 years ago at its founding, still primarily focusing vaguely on feudal human rights issues (封建的 人權問題) just like in the past, gives a sense that this movement is somewhat lagging behind.
When we look back at the battle record (戰跡) of this movement over the past 10 years, we can glimpse how steadfast its history of struggle (X爭) has been. Major dispute cases alone include Yech'ŏn (醴泉), Kimhae (金海), Chinju (晉州), Hadong (河東), Hamyŏl (咸悅), Hyŏnp'ung (玄風), Ipchang (笠場), Suwŏn (水原), Puyŏ (扶餘), Yesan (禮山), Taep'yŏngni (大平里), Poryŏng (保寧), Kaesŏng (開城) (labor-capital), Kyŏngsŏng (京城) (labor-capital), etc., totaling over 360 major dispute incidents. Among the thousands of victims (犧牲者), the number of deaths alone is said to be over 10.
However, despite this movement producing over 360 major dispute cases and thousands of victims, only two cases, in Kaesŏng and Kyŏngsŏng, were labor-capital disputes. This fact makes it difficult to see that even the leadership (指導者層) of this movement has completely liquidated its feudal residual consciousness (封建的 殘滓意識), considering that most of these many dispute cases are seen as disputes over feudal human rights issues.
In other words, even within the same Paekchŏng class, aren't there strata, albeit a minority, who possess capital worth tens or hundreds of thousands of won and act distinctly as capitalists (資本家), as well as exploited laboring strata (被搾取의 勤勞層)? Given this reality even within the single Paekchŏng class, in this movement which cannot detach itself from the whole, wouldn't incidents with the same meaning as those that occurred in Kaesŏng or Kyŏngsŏng be found elsewhere if one looked for them? This indicates a lack of sharp focus (敏活한 着眼點) from the leadership.
All things in human society cannot defy the laws of social evolution (社會進化의 法則). Therefore, it is believed that the transition period (轉換期) of this movement, by inevitable law, is not far off. <14>

**Summary**

This multi-part article from 1932 assesses the state of three major Korean social movements. The Chosŏn Farmers' Alliance (Nongch'ong), while appearing weakened at its central headquarters, maintains considerable strength through its regional branches, boasting over 30,000 members despite restrictions. The women's group Kŭnuhwoe is critiqued as having failed and dissolved naturally because it was founded on vague liberal, nationalist principles rather than a class basis, rendering it irrelevant as class consciousness grew among laboring women who should align with the broader proletarian movement. The Hyŏngp'yŏng Movement, fighting for the rights of the traditionally discriminated-against Paekchŏng class, is noted for its large membership (387,000) and persistent struggle against feudal discrimination, but criticized for not adapting to emerging class divisions within the Paekchŏng community itself and focusing insufficiently on capitalist exploitation, suggesting a need for ideological transition. For historians, the article offers valuable contemporary insight into the ideological shifts within the Korean nationalist and social movements in the early 1930s, highlighting the growing influence of Marxist class analysis, the internal debates and factionalism within organizations like Kŭnuhwoe, the perceived stagnation or need for reorientation in movements like Hyŏngp'yŏngsa, and the resilience of grassroots organization (Nongch'ong) despite central decline and colonial suppression. The use of censored terms ('XX') also points to the political constraints of the time.

朝鮮運動의 現陣容展望
朝鮮農民總同盟
李智鐸
때는 임이 지나친 녯날이엇다. 아즉도 光化門의 녯터가 그대로 남아 잇고 지금의 白堊舘이 그의 위치를 차지하지 안앗슬 때이엇다. 나는 느진 가을 그 어느 黃昏길에 2,3友와 作伴하야 녯날 慶福宮의 담장을 엽헤 끼고 迎秋門을 향하야 거름을 재촉하엿다. 그다지 繁華롭지 안은 『해터압』의 넓은 街道는 사라지는 저녁 해빗에 휩싸이여가며 어지간이 쓸쓸한 장면의 하나이엇다. 그러나 그 어느 것보다도 먼저 破風에 흔들니는 光化門 樓上에 매달닌 조그마한 門窓의 덜커덩 대는 그 소리가 몹시도 필자의 마암을 적막하게 만들엇던 것이다. 바람에 불니여 덜커덩거리며 스스로 열니엇다 닷첫다하는 그 조그마한 씨인은 그의 本城인 커다란 건물의 역사적 배경을 가진 悲哀담은 아-필의 하나가치 생각되엿다.
그러나 이제 이르러 내가 왜? 지나친 이 녯 이약이를 이러케 重言復語하게 되는가.
나는 지난 엇던 기회에 오래간만에 中央 鍾路의 德元삘딍을 차자 朝鮮農總의 陣營을 한번 다시 엿보앗다. 삘딍의 입구에는 『新幹會』 『勞總』 『農總』 등의 기다란 간판이 키를 나란이 하야 규율잇게 붓허 잇섯다. 나의 速한 스텝은 불과 몃 초 동안에 3층 우의 農總事務所 또아를 노크하엿다.
