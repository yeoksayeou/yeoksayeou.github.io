**Body Text Translation:**

To the Lover Who Left Me
While Clinging to Maternal Love

Paek Sunŭm

CK! I feel like I'm going mad. Today, I received a letter from the wife of Mr. X, who lives near your house, saying that my most beloved, pitiful Yŏngja is ill. You can imagine how desperately, like a burning fire, I wanted to run to Yŏngja's side the moment I received that letter, but cursing my fate that prevents me from going, I send this letter instead.

CK! They say there is nothing greater in the world than maternal love. I am not that sad about separating from you. If only Yŏngja didn't exist, I feel I could cleanly forget the entire past. You might misunderstand again because I am writing this letter, but I hold no lingering attachment to you whatsoever. Day and night, only Yŏngja's face is drawn before my eyes.

I always see the pitiful, gaunt face of Yŏngja, growing up under the rough hands of your second wife – Yŏngja's step(?) mother.

CK! Yŏngja is sick, you say; what illness is it? Since when has she been sick? Which room is she lying in? For some reason, I cannot bear the sight of Yŏngja's pale face, lying alone in a dark back room, calling out "Mommy, Mommy."

CK! I have heard. I heard that your daughter, born to your second wife, plays charmingly every day, calling "Mommy, Daddy." And I heard that you wish for her to grow quickly so you can send her to kindergarten, counting each day like a month.

While you wish to send a child who hasn't even grown yet to kindergarten, why do you keep Yŏngja, who has reached the age to go to kindergarten, confined in a corner of the house, receiving abuse from a fearsome stepmother? Isn't that too heartless? Isn't that too malicious?

CK! When I left your house, Yŏngja was four years old. Yŏngja was very bright then; as I stepped out of your gate shedding tears, the unknowing Yŏngja followed me, saying, "Mommy, where are you going? Let's go, the three of us, with Daddy," clutching my skirt hem and blocking my path. It doesn't feel like three years ago; it feels like yesterday.

CK! Is Yŏngja still sick today? They say her fever is over 40 degrees; how much pain must the little child be in? What precious existence is she that she clings to life even with a fever of 40 degrees? It would be better if she just died; she herself would be at peace, and my mind would be relieved too!

How much more comfortable would death be than living, being abused, unable to eat properly, unable to dress properly?

CK! This is my final request: please, quickly eliminate Yŏngja's existence from this world.

If you won't kill her, I will sharpen a knife until it gleams blue, go there, and stab the frail Yŏngja, whose chest is heaving, right in the neck and kill her.

I hate to see it. I hate to see the image of Yŏngja lying alone in a dark back room, never mind being sick, but smacking her lips from hunger.

When she is thirsty, who will fetch her water? When she is terribly sick, who will sit beside her and stroke her head?

CK! You are human too, right? If you are human, you must have compassion. Moreover, you are an educator, aren't you? You are someone known in society as a so-called prominent figure, right?

CK! However, I see you as neither human nor compassionate. You are an underdeveloped man who tries to sell the name of a gentleman in the world, fabricating only lies. If you were human, your actions would not be like this. You are an inhuman being who knows neither human relations nor morality. And yet, you carry the signboard of an educator? I am by no means thinking badly of you just because we separated. Are we the only ones who live together and then divorce? When we cannot understand each other and feel a sort of weariness, divorce is natural. Therefore, I still think our divorce was natural, but your behavior towards Yŏngja is truly unjust, an impersonal – no, inhuman – act. Even if you cherished and pitied Yŏngja, my heart as a mother would ache terribly, but since even you treat Yŏngja so cruelly, how much must my heart ache, and how pitiful must Yŏngja be? How desperate must I be to say I would kill my own Yŏngja, more precious than my own life?

CK! Yŏngja must be crying now. She probably cannot cry out loud. She is afraid of her father and her step(?) mother.

You once said something. When Yŏngja was two, you held her on your lap and said, "Let's grow up quickly and go to kindergarten," kissing Yŏngja's chubby cheeks countless times. That was right when my mother was visiting our house. My mother still talks about it often. Even in her sleep at night, as if talking in her sleep, she says, "That wretched fellow, making such a fuss back then."

Hey, CK! If you are a person with a conscience, you should remember the past.

CK! I ask nothing more from you. Just give Yŏngja to me until she reaches the age to finish higher common school. I will sacrifice myself and raise Yŏngja at my mother's house. If you are human, you will permit this. CK! If I receive word from you, I will disregard even the dark night road, run to where Yŏngja is, carry the pitiful Yŏngja on my back, and return. If there is no word from you, I cannot go. What a truly contradictory situation (事際).

While cursing and resenting everything, I only wish for Yŏngja to be before me. What's the use of writing? Even as I write, I still feel like I'm going mad. <46>

From Moscow

Yu Yŏngsŏn

I received the letter you sent after such a long time. Just as you said, it has indeed been exactly three years. You wouldn't know how much your letter, received after three years, has disturbed my mind.

Why are you bringing up such foolish talk now, dredging it up anew?

You, who once yelled, "Get out, you wench! I can't stand the sight of you for even a day," with what nerve have you picked up a pen to write to me?

You went to see P, who knows my current situation well, and asked about me? You must be someone with nothing better to do. And you say you were overjoyed to hear that I haven't remarried? What reason do you have to be so happy that I haven't remarried?

Although your letter strongly stated that the reason needs no explanation, I have no way of knowing it.

Your letter said there is no one as pitiful and wretched as you, but I cannot understand even that. You cut out an article I wrote and my photo published in some newspaper, put them in your pocket, and pull them out to look at whenever you have a moment? Why have I, whom you hated three years ago, suddenly become so appealing? I do not understand the psychology of men like you.

You say that even while pushing a pen on the third floor of the X government office (X廳), boiling hot, you gaze blankly at the sweat dripping onto the paper, blaming yourself and cursing the world?

So that means you are a pitiful and wretched person? That might be pitiful, if you say so, but what I think is more pitiful is that you yourself still haven't settled your accounts (清算).

My past departed far from me the moment I left you. So, what could your letter possibly throw at me now? Although I am writing a letter to you now, I don't even have your face left in my memory; I merely feel that you were someone I saw somewhere long, long ago.

You asked me to return to Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) as soon as possible. However, at this point, I have no desire to show myself to you, nor do I intend to see you with my own eyes.

I intend to advance onto the life front I believe in. I do not intend to dredge up the past anew and write at length. Nor do I wish to ramble on about how I was in the past.

However, the one thing I will say is that my divorce, for me, was a great leap forward (飛躍) for myself, and that is what I rejoice in.

With the material gained from those three years of submissive (屈服的) life, I thought not only about myself but also about the many women in the world. From that, I realized that women's liberation cannot be resolved in the current society. Thus, I understood well that women in today's society are divided into two classes. Therefore, I finally realized what path I must take. You might laugh, thinking I am the same old Yŏngsŏn, saying, "What does someone like you know, talking so big?" but isn't there a saying that even a worm squirms when stepped on? Furthermore, being born human in this universe, even if a woman is laughed at by men as the weaker sex, how could she remain without any reaction to severe stimulation? I have already found my path in life. I have also prepared myself to walk that path without hesitation. I see the path of life opening brightly before my eyes.

I ask one last thing of you.

If you have time to write letters to me, if you have time to think of me, use that time to think about settling your own accounts.

Then, discover your proper self as yourself. At that time, you will be able to understand me. And you will also be able to understand that women do not exist in the world for men. You say Kyŏngsŏng is boiling so fiercely that sweat drips from the back of your hand, but Moscow is not yet that hot. Even though I am writing such a long letter, sweat doesn't flow, not even from my forehead, let alone my hands. Perhaps it's because my heart is gradually growing colder! I will not write longer. I do not wish to receive your writing again, so please do not waste your effort sweating over it again. -End-
(Mid-June) <47>

To Sukcha, Who Followed Money

Yi Ch'ŏrho

Sukcha! You are truly a beautiful woman. You are the modern beauty with a lovely face, as people say. Therefore, when you step out onto the street, everyone praises you.

You once remarked, didn't you, "I don't know why they stare so much when I go out on the street." When I didn't reply, you continued, "I guess I must be pretty. Today too, on the street (?), some modern boy said 'Hello Miss X,' you know." To that, I asked this: "How would they know you are Miss Ra (羅)?" Then, you surely remember what you answered. "Why wouldn't they know? Since I attended XX Higher Common School, I was counted as the top beauty. How many people are still looking for me, not even knowing I'm married now? And those who know I'm married walk around like they've suffered a huge failure. That's why yesterday, I met a friend on the street, and she said to me, 'Why, out of so many suitors, did you have to pick such a beggar-like person?' It was a bit embarrassing, but what could I say?" you recounted at length.

My reply to that story was simple. "How can a penniless fellow like me – a beggar, as you people say – live with a splendid woman like you? You should go marry a splendid, rich man." As soon as my words fell, what did you answer me? Coquettishly, you replied, "What good is lots of money? As long as our ideals (理想) match, we can live together."

You, having attended up to higher common school, at least heard the word somewhere and remembered the term "ideal." Alas, you were someone who didn't know what it means for ideals to match.

Hey, Sukcha! When there's no mutual understanding, where can there be matching or unmatching ideals?

I do not resent you. I only laugh at my own foolishness.

Not recognizing that a penniless beggar could not marry someone like you. What a foolish fellow I was.

Sukcha! I wasn't the only foolish one. You were foolish too. If I hadn't been a student at the highest institution of learning, I believe I wouldn't have gained the title of your husband.

You did not love me; you loved my academic degree. You were a woman carried away by vanity.

That's right. As you said, if ideals match, you can live together. However, rather than understanding and loving me, you sold your precious self to my academic degree – the title of a university student – so how could that be a complete union?

You did well to go. To a rich man. Your words, "As long as our ideals (理想) match, we can live together," still remain in my memory.

So, does it have to be a rich man to match your ideal? I understand well.

If you need money so badly, why don't you grab hold of a few more rich magnates besides your current husband?

A few days ago, I saw you at the X Department Store, in front of the valuables display case, picking up and putting down a one-thousand-won diamond ring.

So I knew you didn't have the money to buy it. Are there only one or two rich people in the world? There are plenty more, so try taking some other path to buy the ring you desire.

And in my opinion, you seem to prefer being the property – the plaything – of many people rather than the property of one person. Sukcha! Although I take such a cold attitude, you have departed far from me, but I took up this pen because I truly understood and loved you. Because you are too pitiful. You might scoff at this word "pitiful," but to me, you are a very wretched human being.

You are a blind person with open eyes who cannot see the chains that bind you.

You have ears but are a deaf person who cannot hear. And you are a moving doll-human. A lump of meat sold for money.

Hey, Sukcha! Open your eyes and ears and break the chains that bind you. You probably don't even understand the meaning of these words.

My final, emphatic request is that you stop being someone's property and recognize that you yourself are a human being. Then, you will be able to discover your own faults yourself.
-End- <48>

**Summary**

This article presents three letters written to former lovers in 1932 Korea. The first, by Paek Sunŭm, is a desperate, heart-wrenching plea to her ex-husband regarding their sick daughter, Yŏngja, who is suffering under a stepmother's care; the mother oscillates between begging for the child's return and wishing for her death to end her suffering. The second, by Yu Yŏngsŏn writing from Moscow, is a cold rejection of her ex-husband's attempt at reconciliation, dismissing his pity and declaring her commitment to a new life path focused on women's liberation, which she views as impossible within the current societal structure. The third, by Yi Ch'ŏrho, is a bitter address to his ex-wife, Sukcha, accusing her of vanity and marrying him for his status (university student) before leaving him for a wealthy man, whom he cynically suggests better fits her "ideal"; he pities her as a "doll" enslaved by money.

From a historical perspective, these letters offer poignant insights into the personal dimensions of life in colonial Korea. They illuminate complexities surrounding divorce, child custody, and remarriage, particularly the vulnerability of women and children (Paek Sunŭm's letter). Yu Yŏngsŏn's letter from Moscow hints at transnational connections and the burgeoning discourse on women's liberation and class consciousness among some Korean women, even seeing divorce as a "leap forward." Yi Ch'ŏrho's letter reflects anxieties about materialism, the symbolic value of modern education versus wealth, and traditional versus modern views on marriage and female agency, portraying his ex-wife as tragically bound by societal expectations and vanity. Historians might find the raw emotional expression, the specific social critiques (inhumanity of an "educator," class division among women, vanity vs. ideals), and the geographical reference (Moscow) particularly interesting.

날 버리고 간 애인에게
모성애를 孤守하면서

白順音

CK씨! 나는 밋칠 듯 십소이다. 오늘 나는 당신 집 이웃에 사는 某씨의 부인으로부터 내가 가장 사랑하는 불상한 영자가 알는다는 편지를 밧엇습니다. 그 편지를 밧든 길노 영자의 겻흐로 뛰여가고 십흔 생각이 얼마나 불붓듯 햇겟습닛가마는 못 가는 내 운명을 저주하면서 이 글을 보내나이다.
CK씨! 세상에 무엇보다도 더 위대한 것은 모성애람니다. 나는 당신과 떠나게 된 것은 그닥지 슯허하지 안습니다. 영자만 업다면 모-든 과거를 깻끗하게도 잇즐 것 갓습니다. 내가 이 글을 쓴다고 당신은 또 오해할는지 몰으겟소만은 나는 당신에게 대해서는 아모 미련도 남지를 안엇습니다. 밤이나 낫이나 영자의 얼골만 눈앞에 그리고 잇을 뿐임니다.
제2세의 당신의 부인-영자의 살틀(?)한 어머니의 것츠른 손에서 자라나는 영자의 가엽슨 여윈 얼골이 언제나 내 눈에 보임니다.
CK씨! 영자가 알코 잇다니 무슨 병임닛가? 언제부터 앞엇는지요. 어느 방에서 알슴닛가? 나는 웬일인지 어둠컴컴한 뒷골방에 혼자 누어서 「엄마 엄마」하고 부르는 영자의 햘슥한 얼골이 뵈여서 못살겟서요.

몃츨 전에 나는 당신이 X백화점 귀중품 진열장 앞에서 일천원짜리 다이야 반지를 만젓다 노앗다 하는 것을 보앗습니다.
그래서 당신안태 그것을 살만한 돈이 업섯다는 것도 알엇습니다. 세상에 돈 잇는 사람이 한 사람이나 두 사람뿐이겟습닛가. 또 얼마든 잇으닛가 당신이 요구하는 반지 사기 위해서 엇더한 길을 또 취해보시지요.
그리고 내 생각엔 당신이 한 사람의 소유물이 되는 것보다 여러 사람의 소유물-노르개가 되기를 조와하는 것 갓습니다. 숙자씨! 내가 이러케 냉정한 태도를 취함니다만은 내게서 멀니 떠난 당신이오나 나는 당신을 진정으로 리해하고 사랑햇기 때문에 이 붓을 들엇나이다. 당신이 너무도 불상해서요. 「불상하다」는 이 말에 당신은 오히려 조소를 보낼는지는 몰으겟습니다만은 당신은 나보기에 너무도 가엽슨 인간임니다.
당신은 당신을 구속하는 쇠사술을 볼 줄 몰으는 눈뜬 장님이외다.
귀가 잇서도 드를 줄 몰으는 귀먹어림니다. 그리고 당신은 동하는 인형 인간이올시다. 돈에 팔여 단이는 고기 덩어리외다.
여보시요 숙자씨! 눈을 뜨고 귀를 열어서 당신을 구속하는 쇠사슬을 끈허 버리시요. 당신은 이 말이 무삼 말인지도 의미를 몰을 것임니다.
끗흐로 힘잇게 부탁할 것은 당신은 사람의 소유물이 되지 말고 당신 자체가 인간이라는 것을 인식하여 달나는 것임니다. 그러면 그 때엔 당신의 잘못을 당신 자신이 발견할 수 잇을 것임니다.
