기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

白衣女工의 死
						名古屋 韓聖俊

It was because I went to the Yodogawa River that I heard the factory girl’s confession. But my going to the Yodogawa River was by chance. It was noon on May 16th. Six days before that factory girl died. My first meeting with her was during the 15 minutes before the factory whistle blew for the start of work that day. Taking advantage of a 20-minute break for a boiler (鼎) inspection at the factory, about 30 young factory girls rushed busily to the riverbank. Still wearing their aprons, they jumped into the water and played. They picked up stones and threw them into the river, plucked every flower on the riverbank, and someone sang songs. I also went down and walked past the riverbank, passing in front of a thicket, when I saw a small factory girl leaning against a tree, coughing severely. I was about to pass by when I heard the sound of crying mixed with the coughing. I stopped and turned around to see her spitting up red blood. Then, from amidst the crying, I faintly heard the word “Mother.” Only then did I realize that the factory girl was Korean. And so, I asked her in detail. "My hometown is Suwŏn. After my father passed away, we went bankrupt and moved to the countryside, but life became even harder, so having no choice, I relied on my aunt (고모) in Nagoya and came alone to Japan last autumn. I entered the factory, but after paying for meals each month, only 1 won and 50 chŏn remain, so to this day, I haven't been able to send even a penny to my mother. I've been sick since I was back home, but after crossing to Japan and entering the factory, it suddenly worsened. The old Japanese man across the street told me not to go to the factory and rest a bit, but hounded by my aunt's sharp eyes, I went even while spitting blood. (She was let go from the factory two days before she died, when they realized she was sick, so she worked at the factory until two days before her death.) And at the factory, too, they don't trust me because I'm a Chosŏnin (朝鮮人), nor do they talk to me. The only people I know are the two Korean girls who commute with me, one Japanese girl, the old Japanese man across the street, my aunt, and her husband – only six people in total. The only people who showed affection were the Korean girls I commuted with, but once they knew I was sick, they stopped even talking to me." As she spoke up to this point, the start-of-work whistle blew, and we parted. "I sympathize with you. I'll wait for you later at the Samgaegyo tram stop after work finishes, so please come." Hearing this, she looked overjoyed, as if about to cry, and said, "Really! I'll definitely go, so please wait. I'm truly happy. Really, please wait." She pleaded several times and then went off to the factory.

I felt very sorry for that factory girl.
Because I too am Korean and share the same history. So, after returning home, I heard the whistle and hurried to the tram stop, and there she stood on the safety island, holding a lunchbox (벤도) at her side, smoothing her dishevelled hair, and looking around. Seeing me, she rushed over, saying, "Aigo, you really came!" I took her wrist. "Let's not take the tram, let's talk while walking." And so, we walked for about 30 chō (町). There wasn't much special conversation that day. We shared our histories and ideals with each other. When we reached the front of the Noritake (노리다게) Post Office, she told me to go back. I offered to walk her home, but she refused, pleading with me, "Tomorrow too, after work finishes, I'll definitely wait at the Samgaegyo tram stop, will you come?" And we parted there.

It was the next day. May 17th.
Five days before the factory girl died. We met again at the tram stop that day. She looked even more drained than the previous day. And we talked while walking along the same path as the day before. I asked her this:
"Don't you want to go back home?" Without a word, she lowered her head and walked for a while, then shook her head, saying, "I've resolved not to go home until I earn money." "If you keep living this factory life, when will you earn money to go home?"
"If I can't earn it, I can't go," she said with a bitter smile (苦笑) as she walked.
"It truly pains me to see a young body, especially a sick one, doing labor." Hearing this, her expression changed, she glanced up at me once, and spoke forcefully, matching her heart:
"Hmph. You haven't seen our factory, have you? Women over 40 work until they're out of breath, their faces and hands smeared with sweat and grease. They are all kind mothers. They work with pleasant faces at the factory, but at home, their young children are crying incessantly (백빽). Many are women without husbands. Even if they have one, he leaves early in the morning and returns at midnight. Or he's unemployed. They don't have nannies either. When they come to the factory in the morning, they give a milk bottle to the baby and entrust it to an unknowing older sibling before coming to the factory. Then, the unknowing child looks after the baby all day long. The baby cries all day long. How can the mother work with peace of mind? On top of that, the work is so hard (뒨지요). Milk barely comes out of their breasts (젓). They barely have enough to eat, so how could they afford enough milk? They stuff all their extreme worries (極情) inside and work, drenched in sweat. We, working side-by-side, have cried many times. We truly feel sorry. Every time this happens, we work with even more courage. I'm having it easy. Do you know how many people in that factory have fates more pitiful than mine? I am just one single person."
I was deeply moved (感懷無量). I just lowered my head and walked. And the factory girl continued,
"Before I came to the factory, when I attended school, I looked down on factory girls (workers). But now that I myself have become a factory girl, seeing other factory girls, I have come to see them as precious for the first time. I truly see them as human beings. I want to shout out to the world that I am a factory girl. I never, ever consider my position lowly. I know clearly that positions other than ours are truly lowly."
We reached the Post Office. We parted again. <50>
"Tomorrow too, I'll wait at the tram stop, so please come."

The next day, May 18th.
Four days before the factory girl died.
Ah – this day was the last day with the factory girl in this world.
Today too, she arrived before me and waited. Her hair was still dishevelled, and her face looked yellower than the previous day. Today too, we walked the streets together. There was much silence this day. As we almost reached the Post Office, she spoke to me like this:
"It wouldn't matter if I called you Oppa (옵빠 - older brother), right?"
"Me? Uh-huh! As you please." Hearing this, she seemed very pleased. Her cough was quite severe that day. She fell behind and cried softly. "Don't cry. I'll arrange for you to rest from the factory." She just refused.
"Please don't come to my aunt's house. The coming 3rd is a holiday. Let's go play at Nagoya Castle (名古屋城) that day. And on normal days, let's always meet at the Samgaegyo tram stop. And starting tomorrow, I'll definitely call you Oppa." We reached the Post Office again. She gave me a letter. We parted. For the last time in this world, but neither the factory girl nor I knew it. We only parted harboring the happy expectation of meeting at the tram stop tomorrow.

It was the next day. May 19th.
Three days before the factory girl died.
I rushed to the tram stop. But she wasn't there. The faint image of that factory girl—with her yellow face, blue clothes, brown sash, wearing geta, holding a small lunchbox at her side, smoothing her dishevelled hair, standing on the safety island waiting for me—lingered. Only the lamppost stood in that spot. I stood where the factory girl had stood the day before and looked around. Everyone had left the factory, and as the sound of the iron gate closing rang out, I returned home.

The next day too, I rushed there, but she wasn't seen.
I became melancholic.

The next day, May 21st.
The day the factory girl left this world. It rained that day. I rushed there again. But she wasn't there. There was no way she could be. If she were, perhaps she was watching me from that other world, waiting for her. Getting soaked by the drizzling rain, I went right up to the factory gate. I asked a Korean woman, who told me she had been sent away from the factory two days prior because of lung disease (페ㅅ병 - tuberculosis) and they hadn't heard anything since. I asked her to show me the house, and we took the tram together. When we arrived in front of the house gate, the woman turned back and left. It was a shabby (누추한) house. I peered inside. It was quiet. The rain continued to fall. The smell of disinfectant stung my nose. My hair stood on end. After a short while, a woman came out from inside carrying a bundle of clothes and set it on fire. I asked about the factory girl. The woman said, "She died this afternoon (낫에)." Ah—
The sound of the drizzling rain, the flames consuming the clothes, the rising smoke. Ah— I felt dizzy and everything went dark. I lowered my head deeply and hurried away for a while. The Post Office. Ah— the Post Office where the factory girl told me to turn back every day. I avoided that place and hurried on again. I sat down on a bench in a park. For the first time, I let out a great sigh. Then, composing myself, I recalled the past events.

It was because I went to the Yodogawa River that I heard the factory girl’s confession. I loved her. But my going to the Yodogawa River was by chance.
The young factory girls swarming to the riverbank like a swarm of bees (蜂群). After battling with grease and sweat all day, the 15 minutes of leisure they obtained was like 15 years to them. They run freely. A group of maidens (處女群) in white aprons. All scattered on the riverbank. But it's only 15 minutes. Weak women laboured all day long. Only this 15-minute period was their liberation. During that time, they boast of beauty. They boast of flowers. At that moment, I saw something.
