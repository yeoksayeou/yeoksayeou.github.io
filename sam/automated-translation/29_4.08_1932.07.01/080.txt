Ordeal in the GPU Prison
						O Sŏkchu

The motive for taking up this pen, setting aside the value of the writing or the beauty of the sentences, is that I would be extremely fortunate if the ordeal I suffered due to my rash actions four years ago could be disclosed (披露) to the readers and become something to consider (一考).

It was a day in September, four years ago, when three comrades were arrested (被捉) in Kyŏngsŏng due to a secret society (秘社) incident. At that time, I was working as a head clerk (番頭) in a shop in a place 50 *ri* distant (隔한) from Andong. It was a time when the situation was chaotic, and I was living anxiously. When I came to Andong for shop business, I heard that detectives had arrived from Kyŏngsŏng and that a storm of arrests would blow that night. I met with Comrade K, who was involved in the same matters as me, and we reached an agreement. Should we boldly sit and face the situation (坐而對事)? Or should we seek exile? As it was a critical time (危險之秋), we agreed to go into exile. Thus, Comrade K and I bid farewell to Andong as the evening glow (晩霞) fluttered over the Naktonggang riverside (洛東江畔) and the setting sun passed over Sŏaksa temple (西岳寺). We knocked on the door of a friend's house in a village not far from the town late at night, stayed one night, and amidst pouring rain, we entered Ŭisŏng via narrow mountain paths (狹路). Though it was a place where my wife, who wets her night pillow (밤벼게) with tears, resided, and where my elderly parents, whom I hadn't seen for several years and missed dearly, lived, I couldn't visit them for fear of revealing my appearance (행색). What grave sin had I committed to become such an unfortunate child unable even to see his parents? If it is a sin (2 lines omitted - original text)

Crossing mountains and rivers, four days after leaving Andong, amidst a night where all things were utterly silent (萬像이 俱寂한), I boarded a train at Kumi station on the Kyŏngbu line and became a body traveling north, north. It was just when the Taejŏn Ceremony (大典式) was said to be held, a time when the eyes of the mobile police (異動警察) on the train were sharp. Hadn't they spread a search net everywhere in Andong to arrest us? Wouldn't I be bound with ropes (오라) on the train and end up in front of the post office again? I worried about these myriad possibilities. Like being both mute and sick, could Comrade K be any different? Somehow, the train was already only one stop away from Kyŏngsŏng. Just as the train was about to pass Noryangjin, didn't we feel like we were carrying explosives and entering a fiery pit? The mobile police officer looked suspiciously at our appearance (행색), would arrest us at Kyŏngsŏng station, then Chongno, then the Prosecutor's Office (檢事局), the red brick house in Hyŏnjŏ-dong (峴底洞)? Thinking this, the suffering endured through four nights of tossing and turning (轉輾四夜) became useless suffering (虛ㅅ고생). Then let's jump. Let's jump, avoiding the officer's eyes. I signaled Comrade K with my eyes, and we jumped from the speeding train. Fortunately, we were unharmed. We spent the day on the Map'o opposite bank (麻浦對岸) like a daughter-in-law fleeing a burning house, crossed the Han River at night, and stayed for about ten days at a certain place in Sungsam-dong. Should we go to Manchuria (滿洲)? Or to Russia (러시아)? We decided that if we were going anyway, we should go to Russia. We took a train from Ch'ŏngnyangni to Hoeryŏng and safely crossed the Tuman River from Hoeryŏng. Having experienced wandering in China (中國) for a year during the March 1st Movement, I wasn't too clumsy at begging for food, but the geography was unfamiliar. In the high mountains and deep valleys (高山深谷), literally at dusk with the road ended (日暮道窮), there were countless times when my heart felt like collapsing. Holding onto the hope that sweetness comes after bitterness, we pushed forward, forward. Passing Hunchun like viewing flowers on horseback, yearning for Russia as if a small child longs for its loving mother's (자모) embrace, thinking some great opportunity would arise there, stumbling along (顚之倒之), three days after leaving Hoeryŏng, as the sun crossed the distant horizon, passing through a place overgrown with wild grass, neither mountain nor field, a Chinese woodcutter (樵夫) told us this was the Sino-Russian border (中露 국경) and that if caught by the "Kkebeu" (GPU), our lives would be in danger. We thought inwardly, 'Don't worry about us, just gather your firewood. We are people who have come thousands of *ri* to a foreign land, enduring all difficulties, and must go further.' Going a little further, we saw a three-story red brick building and houses. Suddenly, someone shouted from 4 or 5 *kan* (approx. 7-9 meters) away from us and aimed a gun. As we stood at attention like a squad, the soldier who was about to shoot gestured for us to come, more gently than threateningly. Since we were two people who had sought out their country, we followed with welcoming hearts. We entered the three-story building we had seen earlier. After a thorough and solemn search of our entire bodies, they confiscated everything except our clothes, hats, and shoes, and threw us straight into a jail cell. This was truly beyond expectation (誠是念外), and the inability to communicate (語不통) was the biggest headache.

However, thinking, "Okay, once day breaks, we'll go through Vladivostok (海參威) to Moscow (莫斯科), and then to university," we spent the night awake. The next morning, they gave us black bread and tea, but there was no news all day, and lunch and dinner were zero. The following day too, only a piece of bread in the morning, and we starved all day. This place's "one meal a day-ism" wasn't wrong. They butchered pigs, made soup, and ate well three times a day. The smell of food pierced my nose. Yet, they just kept us starving, which was the greatest disappointment. Only after three days, through a Korean interpreter, did we explain the fact that we had come seeking asylum and asked to be sent to Moscow (莫斯科). That evening, in the pouring rain, soldiers on horseback, wearing swords and carrying guns, surrounded us front and back and told us to go. We followed them until midnight to a place we didn't know, which was also a "Kkebeo" (GPU) office. Exhausted from walking dozens of *ri* in the rain all day, the cold and hunger pangs (腹空症) were unbearable. However, judging from past experience, bread was not to be hoped for (非可望), and first, it felt like we would die from the cold. Fortunately, we survived the night without dying, but what would happen today? That day too, it was only a piece of bread in the morning. When the setting sun crossed the western mountains, we were escorted (압송) just like yesterday. We crossed deep streams fully clothed, and there was no time to pick our way carefully over knife-like stones. Mounted soldiers front and back repeatedly shouted "Haji! Haji!" (Go! Go!), so how could there be leisure to take off our shoes and cross the stream? Enduring untold hardships, we finally arrived at the Yŏnch'oe prison, the main "Kkeoppio" (GPU) office in the vicinity, late at night. How could I have imagined it? The joy of meeting an old acquaintance in a foreign prison—
I met K, a friend and comrade. With tear-filled eyes seeing tear-filled eyes due to shared suffering, we could only look at each other. Comrade K had also arrived here via the same route as us, already three weeks ago. There were over ten Korean men and women compatriots in white clothes here. Men and women were in the same cell, singing and smoking were freely allowed, and they even let us walk in the yard three times a day, but the starvation was unbearable. While there was some freedom, we were on the verge of starving to death; compared to prisons in Korea, it had its pros and cons.

After about a week passed, our interrogation (訊問) began. The interrogator was a Korean, said to be a Mr. Pak (朴모), influential during the revolution and now a prominent figure. The interrogation wasn't much, just asking about our past political activities back home and the fact that we had recently sought exile.

After that, another week passed without a single word. K, who had been arrested earlier, and the K who had accompanied me, were expelled, leaving me alone. Parting with two comrades in a foreign prison felt like a separation across the vast ends of the earth, never to meet again. What had happened? We had crossed the border under exactly the same circumstances, so why was I kept here alone? I heard someone say that if you are suspected of being a spy, they keep you like this and then execute you by firing squad. Then, was I not suspected and destined to be shot? Since my two expelled comrades and I were in the same situation, there was no way they would send only me to Moscow (莫斯科). Had I incurred some unfathomable suspicion, destined to become dew in Russian territory (俄領), a startled soul at the point of a gun? Would I die without achieving even one of my grand ambitions? Would the red blood of my heart, surging with righteousness, be unjustly spilled onto a handful of earth with the bang of a gunshot? Thinking this, I saw the black muzzle of a loaded gun, and my body falling, bleeding. No matter how much I thought about it, I hadn't committed a crime deserving death upon arriving in this land, but there was no place to explain (辨明無地) and no place to appeal (呼訴無處). If they killed me, I had no choice but to die. Waiting pitifully for the day of death, even the single piece of bread given once a day, which used to taste like honey, now seemed like dog dung from Kangnam. What use was the rising moon, and what hope did the rising sun bring? As I spent my days thinking about dying without a trace and about my father, mother, and friends back home, another week passed. On the evening of the day marking three weeks since I became imprisoned (囹圄) here, they called my name. This must be my end. What lingering attachment to life is this? Even this shuddering cell, being a place I had lived in, I looked back at it again and followed the guard soldier (派守兵) out. For some reason, they were preparing for an escort (압송). Was I being expelled, I who had only been waiting to be killed? Anyway, I had no choice but to do as ordered. A soldier with a gun pointed towards me and said "Haji!" (Go!). Let's go, wherever it may be. If I die, I die; if I live, it's luck. I followed for I don't know how many tens of *ri*. We reached a place by the coast with rows of houses and three or four-story buildings here and there, whose name I didn't know. I slept in the detention center there. The next day, I put my hand seal (手章) on a document—perhaps my death sentence, I didn't know—and they showed me the money and belongings confiscated when I crossed the border, saying something. It seemed they were asking if it was mine, so I nodded my head and, with the command to leave, followed the guard out of the cell where I had rested my weary body the night before. For some reason, they gave me millet rice (粟飯) cooked with milk and some fish. Though my bowels had been starved for a month, it felt like a final meal given before death, so I couldn't bring myself to eat it. In the afternoon (夕後), they told me to go again. With the power of life and death (生殺之權) in their hands, I followed as they led. I ended up right back in the disgusting Yŏnch'oe prison, the cell where I had starved so much that I picked up and ate raw beans and potatoes fallen on the ground. What were they planning now? Were they going to kill me? Let me live? It was utterly impossible to fathom (料量不得). I slept there that night and was escorted again, spending a day<36> and night in a small "Kkeoppio" (GPU) office by the seashore, before being expelled to Chinese territory. Why I had to suffer more than my comrades who were expelled earlier, I have no way of knowing, nor anywhere to ask. But it was fortunate enough that my life, which I thought was lost, was saved. However, my pockets were completely empty, my hometown was far beyond the clouds (雲外), and I was in a state where I couldn't go even if I wanted to. But since my path to survival (生途) here was uncertain, rather than starve to death (아사), I resolved to return home, see my parents, and then turn myself in (自進) to prison, resolutely setting out on the road back to my country.
On the way back (歸路), the instances of trading clothes for food in North Jiandao (北間島), trading Western shoes (洋靴) to cross the Tuman River, walking barefoot through Unggi (雄基) to Ranam (羅南)—the hardships during the journey were numerous beyond description. But let's stop, as it seems like rambling. A month after leaving Jiandao, I finally arrived home, and surprisingly, things were calm and uneventful. If only I had been a bit bolder and watched how the situation unfolded, I wouldn't have had this recent suffering. I greatly regretted my own timidity (憺小), cowardice, and rash behavior (경거망동).
The incident for which I went into exile two years prior came to light a year later, in July 1930, as the Kyŏngbuk XX Party incident, and in August, I received a sentence of four years, suspended (집유). Thinking back, where is Comrade K, from whom I parted in that Russian prison, and what is he doing now?
-(End)-<35>

**Summary**

O Sŏkchu recounts his harrowing experience fleeing potential arrest in Korea in 1928 due to involvement in a secret society. He and a comrade decided against facing the Japanese authorities and instead attempted to seek refuge and possibly education in the Soviet Union via Manchuria. Upon crossing the border, they were immediately arrested by the Soviet GPU ("Kkebeu"), subjected to harsh prison conditions including near-starvation, interrogated, and moved between detention centers. While his companions were eventually expelled, O Sŏkchu was held longer, fearing execution as a suspected spy, before finally being expelled himself to China, penniless. He endured further hardships returning to Korea, only to be arrested later for the original political activities and receive a suspended sentence.

The article is historically significant for its firsthand portrayal of the disillusionment faced by some Korean activists who looked to the Soviet Union as a potential ally or refuge during the Japanese colonial period. It highlights the brutal reality of the Soviet border regime and GPU practices, even towards those claiming political asylum, contrasting sharply with the hopeful expectations O Sŏkchu initially held. Historians might find the account valuable for understanding the diverse and often perilous paths taken by Korean exiles, the complex geopolitical situation involving Korea, Japan, China, and the USSR, and the personal toll of anti-colonial resistance and subsequent persecution from multiple authorities.

께베우 감옥 수난기
						吳石舟
이 붓을 잡게 된 동기는 글의 가치 유무와 문장의 미추 여하는 고사하고 4년 전 필자의 경거망동으로 인하야 수난한 체험을 독자 앞에 披露하야 一考가 되얏스면 심히 다행한 일이다.
