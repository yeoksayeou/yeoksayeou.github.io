名俳優, 名監督이 모여 「朝鮮映畵」를 말함
(Famous Actors and Directors Gather to Discuss "Korean Cinema")

On a clear autumn evening in September, with a cool breeze gently blowing and even the crescent moon shining brightly, we spent a pleasant night with renowned actors and directors from our film industry discussing "Korean Cinema." As there has never been a record of gathering such authorities in this field in one place for a talk, extremely important and fascinating topics followed one after another, unfolding endlessly throughout the night. We hereby convey these beautiful sounds directly to the heartstrings of our tens of thousands of readers. <82>

Attendees

Mr. Ra Un-gyu (羅雲奎氏) (Actor, Film Director, and Original Writer, creator of works such as *Arirang*, *The Ownerless Ferryboat*, *Goldfish*, *Deaf Sam-ryong*, *Across That River*, *Seeking Love*)
Miss Mun Ye-bong (文藝峰孃) (Lead actress in numerous works including Ch'unhyang in *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, Changhwa in *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*)
Miss Pok Hye-suk (卜惠淑孃) (Lead actress who appeared in many films such as *Falling Flowers and Flowing Water*, *Changhanmong*)
Mr. Kim Yu-yŏng (金幽影氏) (Director of *Hwaryun*, scenario writer, chief editor of *Yŏnghwa Sidae* magazine)
Mr. Pak Sang-yŏp (朴祥燁氏) (Reporter for the Seoul Press English newspaper, film critic)
Mr. Yi Myŏng-u (李明雨氏) (Director at Kyŏngsŏng Studio, producer of films such as *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*, *Arirang Pass*)
Miss Kim Yŏn-sil (金蓮實孃) (Lead actress in numerous films such as *Hwaryun*, *Hong Kil-tong jŏn*)
Mr. Pak Ki-ch'ae (朴基采氏) (Director of many works including *Spring Breeze*, director at Chosŏn Film Co., Ltd.)
From this company (本社): Kim Tong-hwan, Pak Sang-hŭi, Im Pu-wŏn
Evening of September 7th
Garden, Kyŏngsŏng Hotel, Namsan-chō, Seoul.

Our Film World's Three Great Masterpieces

Kim Tong-hwan (本社主幹 - Company Chief Editor) - If we were to choose the three best masterpieces among Korean films—spanning the past 10-plus years, including both silent and talkie films—which ones would you pick?
Ra Un-gyu. Calling them masterpieces (명작) sounds a bit pretentious. <83>

Kim Tong-hwan. If calling them masterpieces is awkward, calling them fine works (佳作) is also fine. Anyway, looking at examples from foreign countries, film critics gather annually to select the world's "Best Ten," and don't they do the same in places like Tokyo (東京)? Through this, they create precedents for socially recognizing authoritative films. That's all it is. Since Korea hasn't produced many films, let's try picking about three as the "Best Three."

Ra Un-gyu. If so, I'll speak. In my opinion, I'd like to choose:
*Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* (薔花紅蓮傳) (Kyŏngsŏng Studio production, starring Mun Ye-bong, directed by Yi Myŏng-u)
*When the Day Breaks* (먼동이 틀 때) (Kyerim Film Company production, written and directed by Sim Hun)
*Camellia* (椿姬) (Chŏng Ki-dok production, starring Kim Il-song, directed by Chŏng Ki-dok)
Among these, *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* is a recent masterpiece. Looking at the actors' performances, cinematography techniques, and all aspects, I actually had ambitions for *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* myself and tried to adapt it several times but failed. I want to express my admiration for the scriptwriter who condensed it into about 10 reels like that. For *Camellia* (춘히), the cinematography techniques, meaning the lighting (라잇트) or the cameraman's work, and the director's methods were outstanding. Sim Hun's *When the Day Breaks* also had good intentions (作意), an interesting story, and good actors.

Yi Myŏng-u. I would like to recommend these:
*Arirang* (이라랑) (Ra Un-gyu production, starring Ra Un-gyu, Sin Il-sŏn, written and directed by Ra Un-gyu)
*Spring Breeze* (春風) (Koryŏ Film Company production, starring Mun Ye-bong, directed by Pak Ki-ch'ae, written by An Sŏk-yŏng)
*When the Day Breaks*
As anyone would agree, there hasn't been a work that impressed me as much as *Arirang*. It had a large scale, overflowed with local color (鄕土味), and the skills (技倆) of the cast were excellent. *Spring Breeze* was a clean, beautiful, and well-organized work. Its most distinctive feature was the excellent use of lighting (라잇트). If *Arirang* is called primitive (野性的), *Spring Breeze* could be called the most refined modern work. Regarding *When the Day Breaks*, Ra Un-gyu's critique is spot on.

Pok Hye-suk. Among Korean films, I've appeared in quite a few myself and have seen most others without missing many, but I haven't seen many talkies since their introduction. Therefore, if I were to pick the "Number One" that remains in my head, it would be:
*Arirang* <84>
Having an actor like Mr. Ra Un-gyu, who is bold-lined, passionate (情熱的), and character-driven (性格的), paired with an actress like Sin Il-sŏn (申一仙), who is delicate and gentle, like a lily (百合花), beautiful, soft, and fine-lined, was the first condition for success. And Ch'unsa's (春史 - Ra Un-gyu's pen name) original work was also excellent as a story.

Kim Yu-yŏng. When I guided Mr. Sternberg (스탄벅 - likely Josef von Sternberg) to see *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* screened at Tansŏngsa, I was astonished. Having seen few talkies from Kyŏngsŏng Studio besides *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, I didn't know much, but with *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*, I marveled at the cinematography, the clarity of accent, and the passionate performance (熱演) of the lead actress. Also, Ra Un-gyu's works *Arirang* and *The Ownerless Ferryboat* were good. The impression of *Arirang* remains vivid even today, 5 or 6 years later, and it was a masterpiece that caused a "storm" (폭풍우) of throbbing (鼓動) and emotion in the audience's chests. Generally, with works like *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, it's easier for the director to draw out figures and social aspects (社會相) of that era and arrange them suitably. But with a work like *Arirang*, the story itself had an original beauty, which was good. *The Ownerless Ferryboat* most vividly (克明) captured the tearful reality we face. In this respect, I would recommend these as the "Best Three":
*The Ownerless Ferryboat* (임자업는 나룻배) (Ra Un-gyu production, starring, directed, and written by Ra Un-gyu)
*Arirang*
*Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*

Mun Ye-bong. I haven't seen many films other than the ones I starred in, so I don't know well. However, the one that touched my heart was:
*Sun-ae and Su-il* (『順愛와 守一』)
That is, *Changhanmong*. The characters of the actors all came alive, and the classical scenic beauty of Pubyŏktu and Ulmiltae near the Taedong River (大同江) in P'yŏngyang (平壤), captured through location shooting (로케), was blended in without any unnaturalness. I can still picture the scenes in my head.

Pak Ki-ch'ae. I see it this way:
*Sim Ch'ŏng jŏn* (심청전) (Kyŏngsŏng Studio production, starring ?, directed by Yi Myŏng-u)
*Arirang*
*Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*
Among films that faithfully adapted old legends into period dramas (時代劇的), there were *Hong Kil-tong jŏn* (洪吉童傳) and various others, but few works stimulated the audience's minds like *Sim Ch'ŏng jŏn* (沈淸傳). It was superior <85> in terms of pure artistic value and invaluable for its moralizing (敎化的) purpose. *Arirang* and *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* were both excellent, as many have already explained.

Kim Yŏn-sil. The ones I also thought were good are:
*Arirang Pass* (「아리랑고개」) (Kyŏngsŏng Studio production, starring Mun Ye-bong, written by Ra Un-gyu)
*Arirang*
These two films. I believe *Arirang* is a masterpiece that will be passed down for decades to come.

Pak Sang-yŏp. Just as Mr. Sternberg praised it, I also found *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* excellent. It contains the sentiment (情緖) that strikes the mood of Koreans. Ideology (이데오로기) is a separate issue. So, if I ultimately had to pick three, I would list these three:
*Arirang*
*Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*
*The Ownerless Ferryboat*

Filming Literary Works

Kim Tong-hwan. Do you have any ambition to try filming a work by any author active in the Korean literary scene?
Pak Ki-ch'ae. There are a few short stories by Mr. Yi T'ae-jun (李泰俊) that I'd like to try my hand at. Short stories are better than long novels. Those short stories have climaxes ("고개" - passes/peaks), are interesting as stories—so it seems possible to produce a good work without incurring exorbitant production costs. On another note, I've always wanted to make a work by a female writer (女流作家). I've already decided that if Miss Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi (崔貞熙) writes a novel, I will definitely try it—.
Mun Ye-bong. Yes. I also avidly read Mr. Yi T'ae-jun's works. I really want to play the heroine of *Madonna* (聖母), which was serialized in the Chungang Ilbo before. <86> The character, the environment, the impression I received from the heroine was immense.
Yi Myŏng-u. A maiden playing the role of a *Madonna* (聖母)? (Everyone laughs)
Ra Un-gyu. I also prefer trying short stories over novels. I once made Ra To-hyang's (羅稻香) *Deaf Sam-ryong* (벙어리 三龍이), but this work was a failure. Literary works should be made primarily for artistic beauty, but at that time, the trend was that audiences liked Douglas Fairbanks' (다크라스) movies, so the protagonist had to jump around here and there, requiring action flavor (活劇味). Also, due to the influence of Western films, they liked using many extras (엑스츄라), having dozens or hundreds of people bustling about on screen (畵面). Bound by promises to the investors (出資主), I struggled to make this work into a popular piece focused on box office success, resulting in a flawed film. If To-hyang were alive in the underworld, I might have received protests. However, I want to remake this as a talkie someday.
And *Arirang Part 3*, released recently, failed for the same reason. The investor said they would only provide funds if I wrote and made a sequel to *Arirang*. What could I do? I couldn't defy the person who put up over ten thousand won, so I forced the story to continue, focusing on box office appeal. Could it avoid failure?
Kim Tong-hwan. You also did Ch'unwŏn's (春園 - Yi Kwang-su) *The Pioneer* (開拓者) long ago, didn't you?
Ra Un-gyu. It was an early work, so it can't be called a success. Generally speaking, because Ch'unwŏn's works have many readers and are well-promoted, they do well at the box office.
Kim Yu-yŏng. That's right. Ch'unwŏn's works are good, but long novels might be difficult to film. Short stories are better.
Kim Tong-hwan. What about works by authors like Yŏm Sang-sŏp (廉想涉), Pinghŏ (憑虛 - Hyŏn Chin-gŏn), or Tokch'ŏn (獨鵑 - O Sang-sun)?
Pok Hye-suk. Well. I read all the serialized novels in the newspapers these days, but there isn't one where I particularly wish I could play the protagonist and make it into a film. Perhaps it's because I'm too old now <87> to play the role of a young bride or the heroine of a sweet romance novel.
Kim Tong-hwan. If you were to appear in a film again, what kind of role would you like to play?
Pok Hye-suk. Just the role of a "mom" (어멈) or a "kisaeng" (妓生), I could do a splendid job. Hoho.
Kim Tong-hwan. I heard there was talk at Kyŏngsŏng Studio about wanting to film Ch'unwŏn's *The Heartless* (無情) with Mr. Yi Myŏng-u directing?
Yi Myŏng-u. I considered it for a while but gave up. I heard news that it's being produced as the first work of the newly established Chosŏn Film Company (朝鮮映畵會社).
Mun Ye-bong. I would like to try playing the role of the heroine "Kŭm-bong" in Teacher Ch'unwŏn's *That Woman's Life* (그 女子의 일생). Although I can't match her beauty (美貌) and my talent also falls far short, I want to try playing such a romantic and tragic heroine role once.
Kim Tong-hwan. Miss Kim Yŏn-sil, isn't there a work whose heroine you'd like to play?
Kim Yŏn-sil. I want to be the protagonist in Ms. Kim Mal-bong's (金末峰女史) *The Jungle* (密林), which was being serialized in the Tong-A Ilbo (東亞日報). Once, I heard from Ms. Mal-bong that although she had already written 170 or 180 installments, it was only about half finished. I heard the entire plot of the unpublished story, and there were quite a few parts where the protagonist's state of mind (心境) resonated with my current feelings. I was captivated (心醉) and wanted to perform it with all my talent. It's quite regrettable that the Tong-A Ilbo was suspended, causing this novel to be interrupted. Moreover, I can almost see Ms. Mal-bong's distress.
Kim Tong-hwan. Have you ever thought of doing something like Pak Hwa-sŏng's (朴花城氏) *White Flower* (白花) or Chang Tŏk-cho's (張德祚氏) *Lullaby* (자장가)?
Kim Yu-yŏng. I hadn't thought of those, but Mr. Yi Hyo-sŏk's (李孝石氏) works... I wanted to do his short stories too.
Ra Un-gyu. Ch'oe Sŏ-hae's (崔曙海) *Red Flame* (紅焰) is also good material (題材), but it's doubtful whether such an ideological (이데올로기) work would pass censorship now.
Pak Sang-yŏp. On another note, future Korean films will likely choose works well-regarded in the literary world. *Taki no Shiraito* (瀧ノ白系), which was a hit in Tokyo, is by Izumi Kyōka (泉鏡花); *Yukinojō Henge* (雪之氶變化) is by Mikami Otokichi (三上於蒐吉); besides them, Tanizaki Jun'ichirō (谷崎潤一郞), Kojima Masajirō (小島政二郞), Kikuchi Kan (菊池寬)—haven't the film adaptations of their works been most successful commercially and popular as well? Moreover, if luck holds, the names of the writer, actors, and director will all be recorded together in history (竹帛)...
Kim Yu-yŏng. It will eventually become like that. Looking at Ozaki Shirō's (尾崎士郞) *Jinsei Gekijō* (人生劇場), if a director handles well something <88> that has already gained popularity through newspapers or magazines, it achieves even greater success.
Yi Myŏng-u. And my final hope is this: just as Korean playwrights write without considering the stage, leading to plays that cannot be performed, I wish novelists would also write works keeping the issue of film adaptation in mind, avoiding elements unacceptable ideologically or scenes difficult to capture through location shooting (로케이슌) as much as possible. I want to express this hope on this occasion.
Kim Tong-hwan. If a literary work is adapted into a film, how much are the manuscript fees (原稿料)?
Yi Myŏng-u. I don't know about other places, but the standard at Kyŏngsŏng Studio is generally from 50 won to 100 won per work.
Pak Ki-ch'ae. For *Spring Breeze*, we paid the original author An Sŏk-yŏng 70 won.
Kim Yu-yŏng. For *Passion Flowing in the Milky Way* (銀河에 흐르는 情熱), it's said to be 100 won.
Ra Un-gyu. It was meager in the past, but I think everyone will be paid considerably more in the future.

How About Love Scenes?

Kim Tong-hwan. How can love scenes ("러브신") be done effectively? In Korea? In the recent *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, the scene where Ch'unhyang, played by Mun Ye-bong, returns from Kwanghallu Pavilion, and Yi Toryŏng, who visited Ch'unhyang's house at the third watch on the 15th night with Pangja carrying the inkstone set, has a "love scene"—when he tries to feed Ch'unhyang rice from a spoon while facing her across the dining table, and finally Mun Ye-bong opens her mouth saying "Ah-" and eats—isn't that a uniquely Korean love scene that foreign directors wouldn't think of?
Yi Myŏng-u. Mr. Yi Ki-se suggested that doing it that way would bring out the Korean flavor, so we used that technique, and everyone said that scene was a success.
Mun Ye-bong. In Korean customs, there's no "kiss" (키쓰) and no embracing (抱擁), so doing "love scenes" seems the most difficult. I struggled the most with that in the role of Ch'unhyang.
Pok Hye-suk. I also have experience, but love scenes are the most sweat-inducing. Goodness, do these men know how to kneel before the woman they love? Do they know how to say "I love you" with a pounding heart, as if they're about to die...? So the female counterpart, blushing and fiddling with her jacket ties (저고리 고름), at best, turns away and pretends to hide her face with her skirt tie (치마고리). It's incredibly bland. That's why the feeding scene in *Ch'unhyangjŏn* this time was a unique masterpiece.
Kim Tong-hwan. Placing Yi Toryŏng's traditional shoes (갓신) and Ch'unhyang's traditional shoes (갓신) side by side on the wooden veranda (퇴마루) <89> was also a masterpiece; such things are effective in arousing amorous feelings (春情).
Kim Yu-yŏng. The passionate "love scene" in *Morocco* or the impressive "love scene" in *Quatorze Juillet* (巴里祭 - Paris Festival) are truly captivating poses, but how can such styles fit the Korean context? Korean love scenes are just the internal kind, where one dreams and interprets the dream alone in one's heart. If it were young men and women sitting alone in a secluded room, it might be fine, but deriving that flavor from the "actors' expressions and movements" on screen—how does that fit? The "climax" (야마 - yama, Japanese loanword) and most important part of a film is the "love scene," Korean directors need some ingenuity here.
Ra Un-gyu. Instead of kissing or embracing, twisting the body is good. Expressions of contorting up and down are also good... On a moonlit night, among the bushes, the woman stands far off, unable to face him directly, perhaps snapping a willow branch, fiddling with her jacket ties, her body tense. Then the man also can't approach closely, digging the ground 탁탁 with the heel of his shoe... This is indeed Oriental (東洋的). It might not be modern (近代的), but it has a Korean flavor. I feel that if I put a little more thought into this, some splendid scene could be created.
A funny thing happened once with a work by Mr. Chŏng Ki-dok called *Phoenix Crown* (鳳凰에 면류冠). A young man returns from the West and falls in love with a Korean lady. Trying to do it Western-style, the two run towards each other like in a marathon race, embrace, and "kiss." This seemed to be the representative love scene of that time. Then it went for censorship (검열), and the Kyŏngmu-guk (警務局 - Police Affairs Bureau) censor cut it out, sternly reprimanding, "Where did such a wicked custom come from?" Truly, such Western-style love scenes don't suit us. It's forced.
Kim Tong-hwan. If you pair people who actually have feelings for each other, meaning they are currently in love, do they perform "love scene" acting well?
Pok Hye-suk. Probably. Since they like each other, those feelings naturally emerge. Like Okada Yoshiko (岡田嘉子) with Takeuchi Ryōichi (竹內良一), Clark Gable with Carole Lombard (크라, 케-물에 구로벨 - Likely Gable and Lombard, "구로벨" is a phonetic approximation of Lombard).
Ra Un-gyu. There are cases where that's not true. In a work I directed—I won't name it—I cast lovers together. But they were shy with each other, couldn't speak freely, and the man couldn't look the woman straight in the eye (正視). No matter how much I told him to look directly, <90> he feared eye contact, feared skin contact, kept avoiding and being timid. How could it possibly turn out well? It ended up being a complete failure.
Kim Tong-hwan. Miss Kim Yŏn-sil, if you were to film a love scene, who would you like to act with?
Kim Yŏn-sil. A love scene would be difficult. Just in a light sense, as an older brother and younger sister... perhaps in a Tokyo Nikkatsu (日活) film? Like in a movie called *Sisters* (姉妹). If so, I'd like to be paired with Mr. Chu In-gyu (朱仁奎).
Pok Hye-suk. Sly one! Can't speak plainly about a love scene, talking about older brother and younger sister... what a trickster... (Everyone laughs heartily)
Kim Tong-hwan. Mr. Ra Un-gyu, it must be Miss Sin Il-sŏn for you?
Ra Un-gyu. Sin Il-sŏn is a talented and gentle woman. We played "love partners" many times in films. So even after that, I tried hard to bring Sin Il-sŏn back, but her personality was weak, so it didn't work out. As my counterpart for "love scenes," Sin Il-sŏn is already past her time. It's unfortunate and sad.
Kim Tong-hwan. How about Miss Mun Ye-bong?
Mun Ye-bong. As long as they are better actors than me, anyone is fine. I think I could do it. I haven't specifically thought, "I want to do it with this person."
Kim Tong-hwan. Then, as a "director," which actress would you absolutely want to cast in your film? Like Sternberg always thinking of Dietrich (데이도릿지), or Inagaki Hiroshi (稻垣浩) thinking of Natsukawa Shizue (夏川靜江) or Irie Takako (入江다가 子)?
Pak Ki-ch'ae. I'd like to cast Miss Namgung Sŏn (南宮仙孃) once. I think it would surely be a success.
Kim Tong-hwan. Mr. Kim Yu-yŏng?
Kim Yu-yŏng. Like Chaplin (촤푸린) discovering an unknown woman hidden in the city streets, like Sternberg discovering the insignificant third-rate cabaret singer Dietrich (도-도릿지), I want to discover a new talent—and use them to my heart's content as I please.

Comedies and Tragedies During Location Shoots

Kim Tong-hwan. There must be many comedies and tragedies from failures during location shoots (로-케슌)?
Pok Hye-suk. More than just many! Once, while filming *Falling Flowers and Flowing Water* (落花流水), I was dragged by the director to the Han River (漢江) for location shooting. It was during the rainy season, so the river was swollen, and the dark, muddy water was terribly unpleasant. And I had to jump into this water to commit suicide. Even if it's just moving pictures, it's a chilling task. So, the camera started rolling, and the director blew the whistle, <91> urging me to jump in. On the Han River bridge, passing pedestrians got excited, thinking "a woman is committing suicide," and gathered to watch... So, finally, I plunged in. The current was strong, and I ended up completely submerged underwater. Mr. Yi Wŏn-yong (李源鎔) was supposed to rescue me as my "lover," but instead of grabbing my hair firmly and swimming while pulling me, which would have been good, he grabbed my waist. So my body floated, but my head kept getting submerged in the water, and I drank plenty of that muddy water. Later, when I got out, I don't know how many bowls of water I vomited. I was sick for several days... It was chaos.

Ra Un-gyu. This is a story from when I was filming *Goldfish* (금붕어) with Sin Il-sŏn. There's a scene like this: a young office worker on payday jumps down from the second-floor office to avoid a debt collector and runs away... So, we chose the backyard of the Chosŏn Ilbo newspaper company (朝鮮日報社) in Kyŏnji-dong (堅志洞) as the location shoot site, and I directed it while also operating the camera. The lead actor, Yi Ch'ang-yong (李創用), who was good at sports, was supposed to jump from the third floor of the newspaper building using a rope. He said it would be easier to jump if several knots ("옹도리") were tied in the rope, so we did that. But he got caught on a knot and fell from that high third floor, bang! onto the bare ground. Seeing this from outside the fence, I was so shocked <92> I jumped over the tin fence, and there he was on the ground, seemingly dead. How startled I was! We immediately hospitalized him, and he received treatment for many days. After that, I avoided such dangerous location shoots. I learned my lesson. I heard that Kim To-san (金陶山) also hurt himself falling from the Han River while filming a serial drama (連鎖劇) and died because of it, becoming disabled first.
Kim Yu-yŏng. There was such talk. Action scenes (活劇) are dangerous.
Ra Un-gyu. Even now, young people in some theater troupes perform action scenes, lifting people high up on stage and slamming them down, causing many injuries. In my view, it seems few young men doing that will live past 30.
Yi Myŏng-u. It was autumn last year. During the biting cold early third lunar month, filming *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* (장화홍련傳), we were shooting the scene where Changhwa jumps into the Lotus Pond (蓮塘). We dug a large pond inside the studio grounds and filled it with water using sacks. Miss Mun Ye-bong, as Changhwa, was supposed to jump in. Since it was a talkie shoot, twenty-four technicians, including cameramen, were lined up waiting for the director's whistle. At that moment, I pushed Miss Mun (as Changhwa), who had let down her hair to commit suicide, from behind. Instantly, she plunged into that cold water. But because the water was too cold and shallow, Changhwa, who should have disappeared completely under the pond, remained with her head sticking out. (Everyone laughs) It was unbelievable... We cut that take. Then, this time, I pushed "Hongnyŏn" too, but perhaps because the water was so cold and frightening, "Hongnyŏn," after entering the water, just yelled "Mom!" and jumped right back out. Hahaha. Later, when we took off their clothes, Miss Mun Ye-bong's body had scratches all over, as if cut by a knife, from the ice. It was hard to look at.
Mun Ye-bong. I had a terrible time then. I jumped in with desperate determination into that cold water, but it was too shallow for my whole body to submerge. Moreover, they had me wear a skirt made of artificial silk (人造絹), and all the flimsy fabric floated on the water. No matter how hard I tried, things didn't go well, so I just kept crying by myself.
Pok Hye-suk. I had another experience like this. Also during *Falling Flowers and Flowing Water*, we went to Ch'ŏngnyangni for location shooting. The scene was: "While I'm riding in a car, a villain comes chasing on a motorcycle (오도빠이), jumps onto my car, and tries to assault (暴行) me." The villain role was played by Mr. Yi Wŏn-yong, who was said to be outstanding in sports. But that day, even though the car was moving very slowly, this man, usually so agile and nimble, couldn't jump onto it. The June sun was beating down relentlessly, the child I was holding kept crying, my makeup <93> was washing off with sweat... Yet, even after 5 or 6 takes, the villain, Mr. Yi Wŏn-yong, couldn't jump onto my car. I was so angry that finally, I thought, "Forget the movie," yelled, "Hey! If you want to be an actor, be reborn!" cursed him out, fought, and came back crying. A little later, someone rushed over from Tansŏngsa theater, saying Yi Wŏn-yong had taken poison and committed suicide out of anger after being cursed by Pok Hye-suk. My father, who was sitting in the living room, grabbed his overcoat (두루맥이) and ran to Tansŏngsa. I also ran frantically to Tansŏngsa, unable to even put on my skirt properly. Indeed, Mr. Wŏn-yong was lying there as if lifeless, seemingly trying to commit suicide. We barely managed to hospitalize him, and he was okay, but... all sorts of things happen.
Pak Ki-ch'ae. Sternberg apparently said the scene in *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* where "Changhwa" jumps into the water was very well done. Despite not knowing about such failed attempts during location shooting...
Kim Dong-hwan. If an actor can't perform well due to their mood that day, do you curse and hit them?
Yi Myŏng-u. When things don't go well like that, we just take a break for the day.
Mun Ye-bong. When things don't go well, the director gets anxious, but I also just end up crying. I always cry 4 or 5 times per film.
Yi Myŏng-u. That's right. Mun tries her best, but when it doesn't work out and we have to "reshoot" several times, she always cries.

Is Actor Life Happy? Etc.

Kim Tong-hwan. What are the recent hit films?
Kim Yu-yŏng. Probably *Ch'unhyangjŏn*. Being the first talkie helped.
Kim Tong-hwan. How much profit did it make? Tens of thousands of won?
Yi Myŏng-u. We don't know yet. It has played in most cities within Korea. Recently it went to Tokyo and Osaka. In Osaka (大阪), it had a continuous run (續映) for 3 weeks, so it seems to be doing very well.
Ra Un-gyu. It ran for nine days in Seoul, right?
Yi Myŏng-u. It was nine days. And distributors now have the courage to buy works at high prices.
Pak Ki-ch'ae. *Spring Breeze* also did well.
Kim Yu-yŏng. In the provinces, *Hong Kil-tong jŏn* is said to have the best results.
Ra Un-gyu. It did well in Seoul too.
Kim Yu-yŏng. *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn* must have done well too. Anyway, the time has come when Korean films can make a profit just by being produced and released.
Pok Hye-suk. The one I did, *Dark Shadow* (暗影), ended up losing money. <94>
Kim Tong-hwan. How much does it cost to produce a 10-plus reel talkie like *Ch'unhyangjŏn*, *Hong Kil-tong jŏn*, or *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*?
Yi Myŏng-u. Around 7,000-8,000 won (圓).
Kim Tong-hwan. And a silent film?
Kim Yu-yŏng. 3,000-4,000 won should be enough.
Kim Tong-hwan. From now on, it seems only talkies will be viable, right?
Ra Un-gyu. That's likely. They will be dominated by talkies.
Pak Ki-ch'ae. Silent films will probably still come out for a while. The works from Chosŏn Film Company are said to be half silent, half sound.
Kim Tong-hwan. In foreign countries, there are systems like the Best Actor Award or Best Director Award, where the government or some organization gives prizes to the most outstanding directors and actors. If such a system existed here, wouldn't your lives improve?
Ra Un-gyu. It would boost artistic passion too, which would be good.
Kim Yu-yŏng. On another note, Mr. Ra seems to have lost his old passion (情熱). I wish he would return to the Ra of the *Chal Itkŏra* (잘 있거라 - Farewell) and *Arirang* era, boiling with fervor.
Ra Un-gyu. I've aged since then, so that's probably why. Haha.
Pok Hye-suk. I don't despair about getting older; everything has to age.
Pak Sang-yŏp. In terms of passion (熱), there's still no one like Mr. Ra Un-gyu. I hope he returns to the *Chal Itkŏra* era and produces a masterpiece greater than *Arirang*. Sternberg is over 40 now but was thinking of living to 100.
Kim Tong-hwan. How do Korean film production costs compare to Tokyo's?
Kim Yu-yŏng. 1 won in Korea is like 10 won in Tokyo, and 10 won in Tokyo is like 100 won in the West.
Kim Tong-hwan. Is the compensation for actors and directors proportional to that?
Yi Myŏng-u. It wouldn't be wrong to assume so. Nowadays, intellectuals find it difficult without a monthly income of 100 won. Gradually, famous actors and directors in Korea should also reach that level.
Ra Un-gyu. People often make a fuss about actors when trying to film Korean movies, but that's not the issue. If, for instance, actors had opportunities to appear in works every month, it would be different. But if they only get to appear in maybe two works a year, they spend 6 months pawning things, borrowing money from friends, owing money for lodging and meals. Then, when they finally get an opportunity, they have to pay back the most urgent debts first. So they ask for some money to use. This tendency isn't unreasonable. The problem is, if they could be given a "fixed monthly salary" (一定한 月給), none of this would be an issue. Some friends criticize me, saying I've degenerated by following second-rate and third-rate theater troupes (劇團) around the countryside. <95> But if I hadn't done theater, I would have starved to death or had nothing else to do but steal. Because life is so harsh, I inevitably follow rural troupes. Then, after earning just enough to live on for a few months, I come up to Seoul and make films. My life is miserable.
Kim Tong-hwan. Are Mr. Yi Myŏng-u and Miss Mun Ye-bong at Kyŏngsŏng Studio on a monthly salary (月給制)?
Yi Myŏng-u. Yes. Though it's not much...
Kim Yu-yŏng. Kim Yŏn-sil appeared in *The Ownerless Ferryboat* for fifteen days on a daily wage (日給), getting 10 won per day, totaling 150 won, I heard.
Ra Un-gyu. A daily wage of 10 won. It might sound like a lot to others, but what can you do when you only earn that daily wage perhaps once a year?
Yi Myŏng-u. Generally, earlier someone mentioned it would be good to establish some Film Award, but what's more necessary is a support organization for theater and film. Also, now that two major film companies (2大映畵會社) have emerged in Korea and the film business is becoming serious, there's a shortage of technicians (技術者). For talkies, even shooting the smallest cut requires the simultaneous work of 24 technicians, including cameramen and lighting men. Training technicians is also a very urgent task.
Kim Tong-hwan. On another note, how are actresses generally discovered, through what motivations?
Ra Un-gyu. When I was first filming *Arirang*, I was struggling to find a counterpart. I went to Pusan (釜山) and saw a talented woman singing at a theater there. I scouted her and brought her to Seoul. That was Sin Il-sŏn.
Kim Yu-yŏng. Miss Mun Ye-bong, of course, is the daughter of Mr. Mun Su-il (文秀一氏), from a family involved in theater for generations, so discovery isn't an issue...
Kim Yŏn-sil. As for me, I came following Mr. Ra Un-gyu's words.
Ra Un-gyu. She came with a letter of introduction from a friend. If I may say something impolite, when she first started, I sat opposite Yŏn-sil holding a brush and powder to do her makeup (메이크, 압), but her face had no definition, making it impossible to apply the brush. How disappointed I was! But as she gradually aged, her bone structure (骨格) and the lines of her nose and mouth became distinct, turning out nicely. Miss Pok Hye-suk, too, when she first debuted, her face didn't suit the stage or screen well, but now she's lost weight, and her facial bones have become more prominent, making her look good.
Pok Hye-suk. I myself feel that I've lost some weight now and look better than before. But now that movies have become <96> talkies, one needs a pretty face and clear speech to appear.
Kim Tong-hwan. What do you think? Are newcomers better, or are experienced actors easier to work with?
Kim Yu-yŏng. I've worked with both Kim Chŏng-suk (金貞淑) and Yŏn-sil, but I feel more at ease when using "skilled veterans" (숙년한 녯사람). My hope is to work with actors who possess truly outstanding acting skills and popularity! That's what I think.
Ra Un-gyu. That's right. The longer someone has been doing it, the better. If I have a choice between someone with 3 years of experience and someone with 4 years, I want to use the one with 4 years.
Kim Tong-hwan. Bringing an actor's face to life is purely the director's skill, isn't it? For instance, Miss Mun Ye-bong looks extremely good in profile (푸로필), that is, from the side (側面)...
Pak Sang-yŏp. Sternberg also said not to use lighting from below on Miss Mun's face, because of her eyes and mouth...
Ra Un-gyu. I also thought Ye-bong was only good in profile, but seeing her in the recent *Rainbow* (무지개), her front view (正面) was good too. They say Mary Pickford (메리, 삑포드) always presented her left cheek because it was prettier.
Kim Tong-hwan. Do actors have various "habits" (버릇) during filming?
Yi Myŏng-u. Yes, they do. Miss Mun Ye-bong cries easily.
Ra Un-gyu. Yŏn-sil goes to the restroom frequently... Anyway, she goes 4 or 5 times per scene, maybe to memorize dialogue (세리푸 - serifu, Japanese loanword for 'lines/dialogue') for talkies... haha.
Kim Tong-hwan. Are there many aspiring actors (俳優志望者)?
Yi Myŏng-u. At our studio (撮影所), 5 or 6 people visit every day. They come and go before even two weeks pass. Some get disillusioned seeing the difference between dream and reality and retreat; some run away because it's too hard...
Kim Yu-yŏng. I also tried recruiting actors in the past, but it was difficult to find astonishing talent among the newcomers who volunteered.

Possibility of Korean Cinema's International Advancement

Kim Tong-hwan. Finally, is there a possibility for Korean cinema to advance internationally? That is, can it be assimilated into foreign markets?
Yi Myŏng-u. I firmly believe there is. Currently, the number of permanent theaters (常設舘) and cinemas (劇場) in Korea is about sixty-five or sixty-six. Relying only on these, the Korean film business cannot sustain itself. In mainland Japan (日本內地), there are over 20,000 theaters. Therefore, to operate profitably, Korean cinema must first secure important markets wherever overseas Koreans live. Mainland Japan means Tokyo (東京), <97> Osaka (大阪), Nagoya (名古屋); Manchukuo (滿洲) means Xinjing (新京), Fengtian (奉天), Harbin (哈爾賓), Dalian (大連); Beiping (北平), Shanghai (上海), and further away, Hawaii (布哇), North America (北米), etc. The future of Korean cinema will be bright only when Korean films can reach these places. And the current trend is increasingly moving in that direction. Decent films can now run continuously for about 2 weeks if taken to Tokyo or Osaka.
Ra Un-gyu. Targeting Tokyo, Osaka, Xinjing, or Shanghai is good, but beyond that, I believe we can aim for purely foreign markets like Britain, America, Germany, France (英米獨佛). To achieve this, our film industry (映畵界) needs to make significant technological advancements. If we can capture the common sensibility (共通된 感性) felt by people worldwide, based on the mountains and rivers (山河) and sentiment (情調) of Korea, I believe advancing into the world market will not necessarily be difficult.
Kim Tong-hwan. If so, in what direction should Korean cinema be guided so that people around the world would pay to watch it? There seem to be two paths. First, realistically filming Korea's unique customs and habits, unseen anywhere else in the world, much like a tourism association introduces its attractions to visitors, thereby appealing to foreigners' sense of wonder and curiosity... Put simply, should we develop in the style of African lion hunt films or South American custom photographs (南米의 風俗 寫眞)?
Or should we create truly grand artistic films (藝術映畵) like *Ekstase* (흐름 - Nŭrŭm/Flow), *Man of Aran* (아란), *Quatorze Juillet* (巴里祭 - Paris Festival), *Morocco*, and conquer overseas markets that way?
Pak Ki-ch'ae. In my opinion, we should take the path of beautifully depicting sentiments, landscapes, and ways of life that people in other countries haven't seen, heard, or known, and showing them widely. I believe that will lead to success.
Kim Yu-yŏng. I agree with that, but we shouldn't do it in the manner of introducing the customs and habits of savage lands in Africa (亞弗利加 蠻地). Like *Man of Aran* (아랑), which depicted the scenery of northern Ireland using realistic techniques (寫實的 手法) yet contained universal emotions and stories, or *Ekstase* (흐름 - Nŭrŭm/Flow), which depicted Czechoslovakian (첵크슬로바키야) scenery, we need to produce artistically excellent works.
Ra Un-gyu. My opinion differs from you two. It's not about sending out films showing Koreans wearing traditional hats (갓) and coats (도포). Rather, we must exert all our efforts to create artistically outstanding films comparable to *Quatorze Juillet* (巴里祭), *Bolero* (西班牙狂想曲 - Spanish Rhapsody), *Foreign Legion* (外人部隊). The path of introducing landscapes and customs (山河 風物) might incidentally yield such effects, but it is short-lived and merely satisfies their curiosity <98>, which is not what we should desire. We must aspire to produce great actors like Emil Jannings (에밀, 야닝쓰), Clark Gable (크락, 케불), Greta Garbo (그레타갈보), Dietrich (데-도릿지), renowned directors like Sternberg (스탄-벅), and great writers like Tolstoy (톨스토이), Dostoevsky (엡스키), Wells (웰스) from among ourselves, and think about fundamentally captivating them.
Kim Tong-hwan. Understood. There will be another day to discuss this issue further. By the way, to aid the development of the Korean film industry, if you were to invite famous foreign actors or directors, whom would you hope for?
Pok Hye-suk. America's Mae West (메이, 웨스트). Because she writes her own original stories, adapts them, stars in them, and is a famous actress known for modern, sensual directing that leads cutting-edge society.
Pak Ki-ch'ae. I'd choose Clark Gable (크락, 케불) and Dietrich (데-도릿지).
Ra Un-gyu. Rather than people, I wish we could bring animals from the Hollywood (하리웃드) zoo. Hahaha.
Kim Tong-hwan. Thank you for your time. We will create another opportunity next time, so please come again and share your valuable insights. Now, let's go out into the garden, where the moon is bright and the woods are dense, for a walk. (Ended at 11:30 PM) <99>

**Summary**

This 1936 roundtable discussion features prominent Korean film actors and directors debating the state and future of Korean cinema. They select the "best three" Korean films to date, mentioning *Arirang*, *Changhwa Hongnyŏnjŏn*, *When the Day Breaks*, and *The Ownerless Ferryboat* most frequently. Participants discuss adapting Korean literary works, highlighting the challenges of turning novels into screenplays and mentioning authors like Yi T'ae-jun and Yi Kwang-su (Ch'unwŏn). The difficulty of portraying love scenes authentically within Korean cultural norms (without kissing or embracing) is debated, alongside amusing and dangerous anecdotes from location shooting. The conversation touches on the financial struggles of actors and the industry, the transition to talkies, the need for trained technicians, and how actors/actresses were discovered. Finally, they contemplate the potential for Korean films to succeed internationally, debating whether to emphasize unique Korean cultural elements or strive for universally appealing artistic masterpieces competitive with Western cinema.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its candid look into the nascent Korean film industry under Japanese colonial rule. The discussion reveals the blend of artistic ambition and commercial constraints, the awareness of and comparison with Japanese and Western cinema, and the specific challenges faced, such as censorship (implied concern over "ideology") and limited resources (low budgets, lack of trained personnel). The participants' aspirations for international recognition, despite the colonial context, and the debate on how to achieve it (cultural exoticism vs. universal artistry) reflect broader cultural anxieties and ambitions of the period. The mention of specific films, actors, directors, and writers provides valuable data for film history.

秋9月 凉風이 徐來하고 초생달조차 明朗한 淸宵一夕을 우리 영화계의  名俳優, 名監督 諸氏로부터 「朝鮮映畵」를 말하는 유쾌한 하로밤을 가지다, 일즉 斯界權威를 이러케 한자리에 모여 說話를 어든 기록이 업섯느니만치 심히 중요하고 滋味잇는 화제가 꼬리에 꼬리를 물고 이 밤에 곳업시 펴지다, 써 數萬讀者의 心琴에 그대로 아릿따운 이 소리를 전하기로 하는 바이노라. <82>

金東煥(本社主幹)- 조선 영화 가운데서 가장 잘된 명작 세 편을 골누자면 무엇 무엇을 골느겟서요-과거 10여년 전부터 오늘까지 무성(無聲)이든 토키이든 모두 통떠러서요.
羅雲奎. 명작이라면 어페 잇군요.<83>

金東煥. 명작이라고 하기 거북하면 가작(佳作)이라 일홈부처도 조코요. 어째든 外國가튼 예를 보면 영화비평가들이 매년 모여 세계의 「뻬스트 텐」(10大 名作)을 골느거나 동경(東京) 가튼 데서도 그리하지 안습니까. 그래서 권위잇는 영화를 골나 사회적으로 공인(社會的 公認)하는 예를 만들지 안습니까. 그저 그것이지요. 다만 우리 조선은 제작된 영화수효가 만치 못하니까 세 편쯤 「뻬스트 트리」라 하여 골너봅시다.

羅雲奎. 그러타면 말하여보지요. 내 생각에는
薔花紅蓮傳(京城撮影所作品, 文藝峯主演, 李明雨監督)
먼동이 틀 때(鷄林映畵社作品, 沈薰原作, 監督)
椿姬(鄭基鐸作品, 金一松主演, 鄭基鐸監督)
이러케 골느고 십허요. 그 중에서도 「장화홍련전」은 근래의 걸작이여요. 배우의 연기 촬영기술 모든 점으로 보아서 실상 나도 장화홍련전에 야심(野心)이 잇서 여러 번 각색(脚色)하려다가 실패햇는데 그러케 10여 권으로 압축하여 노흔 각색자에게도 경이를 표하고 십허요. 「춘히」는 촬영기술 말하자면 라잇트(照明)라든지 카메라맨의 위크라든지 감독의 수법이라든지 뛰여 낫섯지요. 심훈씨의 「먼동이 틀 때」도 작의(作意)도 조왓고 스토리도 자미잇섯고 배우도 조왓섯지요.
李明雨. 나는 이러케 취천하고 십허요.
이라랑(羅雲奎作品, 羅雲奎, 申一仙主演, 羅雲奎原作監督)
春風(高麗映畵社作品, 文藝峯主演, 朴基采監督, 安夕影原作)
먼동이 틀 때
누구나 그랫겟지만 「아리랑」가치 감명을 준 작품이 업섯서요. 스켈이 크고 향토미(鄕土味)가 넘처 흐르고 출연배우의 기량(技倆)이 우수한 것으로요. 「춘풍」은 깨끗하고 아름답고 정돈된 작품이엇지요. 무엇보다도 특장은 「라잇트」를 잘 쓴 점이지요. 아리랑을 야성적(野性的)이라하면 「춘풍」은 근대적 가장 세련된 작품이라 할 걸요. 「먼동이 틀 때」에 대하여는 라운규군의 비평이 적평이지요.
卜惠淑. 나는 조선 영화치고 내 자신이 출연햇든 작품도 꽤 만엇고 남의 작품을 별로 빼어노치 안코 거개 보아 왓지만 다만 토-키 이후의 작품은 그리 만히 보지 못햇서요. 그러기에 내 머리에 남은 「남버-원」을 골는다면
「아리랑」<84>
이여오. 라운규씨 가튼 선이 굵고 정열적(情熱的) 성격적(性格的) 배우에다가 신일선(申一仙)이 가치 연연작작 마치 백합화(百合花) 가튼 아름답고도 부드럽고 선이 가는 녀우를 타이업시킨 것이 성공의 첫 조건이며 그리고 춘사(春史)의 원작이 스토리로서 또한 퍽으나 우수햇서요.
金幽影. 「스탄벅」씨를 안내하여 단성사에서 상영한 「장화홍련전」을 보고 나는 놀낫서요. 「춘향전」이나 그박게 경성촬영소로부터 나왓다는 토키를 본 것이 별로업서 몰낫다가 이 「장화홍연전」에 와서는 그 촬영기술 악쎈트의 명낭 주역배우의 열연(熱演)에 감탄햇서요. 그리고는 역시 라운규군의 작품인 「아리랑」과 「임자업는 나룻배」가 조왓서요. 「아리랑」의 인상은 5, 6년 후인 오늘까지 뚜렷하고 관중의 가슴에 「폭풍우」와 가튼 고동(鼓動)과 감명을 준 명작이엇지요. 대체로 「춘향전」 가튼 것은 그 시대의 인물 그 시대의 사회상(社會相)을 끄집어내어 가지고 감독이 적이 안배하는 편이가 잇지만 「아리랑」의 작품에 이르러는 전혀 「스토리」부터 독창미 잇는 것이 조왓서요. 「임자업는 나룻배」에서는 눈물겨운 우리의 현실을 가장 극명(克明)하게 바덧섯지요. 이런 점에서 나는 이러케 「뻬스트-트리」를 천하려 합니다.
임자업는 나룻배(羅雲奎作品 羅雲奎主演, 監督原作)
아리랑
장화홍련전
文藝峯. 저는 제가 출연한 작품이외에는 남의 것은 만히 보지 못하엿기 잘 몰으겟서요. 다만 제 가슴을 울니든 것은
『順愛와 守一』
이라하든 저 「장한몽」이엇습니다. 출연하는 배우의 성격이 모다 저마다 살앗고 「로케」를 하여 캣취하여 너흔 평양 대동강(大同江) 변부벽두 울밀대의 그 고전적 승경이 조곰도 부자연스럽지 안케 삭여져 너켯서요. 지금도 장면 장면 머리속에 그릴 수 잇구만요.
朴基采. 나는 이러케 봅니다.
심청전(京城撮影所作品,    主演, 李明雨監督)
아리랑
장화홍련전
녯 전설을 시대극적(時代劇的)으로 그 스토리를 충실하게 쪼차가며 작품화한 영화로서 홍길동전(洪吉童傳)도 잇고 그박게 여러 가지가 잇섯지만 「심청전」(沈淸傳) 가치 관중의 머리를 충동준 작품이 드물엇서요. 순수한 예술가치로도<85> 상승(上乘)이거니와 교화적(敎化的) 뜻으로도  갑놉핫서요. 「아리랑」, 「장화홍련전」은 이미 여러분이 설명한대로 모다 우수햇지요.
金蓮實. 저도 역시 조케 생각한 것이
「아리랑고개」(京城撮影所作品, 文藝峯主演, 羅雲奎作品)
「아리랑」
두 편이여요. 「아리랑」은 앞흐로 몃 십년을 끼처 갈 명작인줄 밋으니깐요.
朴祥燁. 스탄박씨가 격찬 하드시 나도, 「장화홍련전」을 우수하게 보앗서요. 거기에는 조선 사람의 무-드를 치는 정서(情緖)가 흘러잇서요. 「이데오로기」는 딴 문제로 하고요. 그래서 나도 결국 세 가지를 골느라면 이러케 세 개를 들겟서요.

文藝作品의 映畵化

金東煥. 조선 문단에서 활약하는 어느 작가의 작품을 한번 영화화하여 보고 십다는 야심이 업서요?
朴基采. 李泰俊씨의 단편 중에 몃 개는 손대보고 십흔 것이 잇서요. 장편보다는 단편이 나어요. 그 단편들은 「고개」도 잇고 스토리로도 자미잇고-그래서 제작비도 턱업시 먹이지 안코서도 조흔 작품을 내일 수 잇슬 것 갓해요. 딴 말이나 나는 늘 女流作家의 작품을 하나 만들어보고 십흔데 崔貞熙씨가 장편을 하나 쓰면 꼭 해보겟다고 벌서 마음먹고 잇지만요-.
文藝峯. 그래요. 저도 李泰俊씨 작품을 애독하는데 전번에 中央日報에 연재되든 「聖母」의 여주인공으로 꼭 나오고 십허요. 그 성격이라든지<86> 환경이라든지 녀주인공에서 밧는 감격이 컷서요.
李明雨. 아직 처녀각씨가 「聖母」노릇이라니요.(一同笑)
羅雲奎. 나도 장편보다 단편에 손대보고 십허요. 내가 예전에 羅稻香의 「벙어리 三龍이」를 만드러본 일이 잇는데 이 작품은 실패엿지요. 문예작품은 예술미를 주로 하고 만들어야 할 터인데 그때 시세가 관중들이 「다크라스」의 영화를 조화하든 때로 주인공이 이리고 저리고 해서 활극미(活劇味) 잇서야 조와햇고 또 서양영화의 영향으로 엑스츄라를 만히 써서 수십 명, 수백 명이 화면(畵面)에서 욱적북적하야 조와들 햇지요. 출자주(出資主)와의 약속에 속박되어 이 작품을 흥행중심의 통속물 만들기에 애썼기 때문에 결함이 만흔 작품을 만들고 말엇지요. 지하의 도향이 살앗더면 항의라도 밧엇슬 듯. 그러나 다시 한번 토키로 이것을 개작하고 십허요.
그리고 요지간에 내논 「아리랑 第3篇」도 마천가지 의미에서 실패햇지요. 아리랑 그 뒤 이야기를 서서 만들어야 출자주가 돈을 내 주겟다고 하니 할 수 잇나요. 만여 원이나 내노흔 사람의 말을 거역할 수도 업서 억지로 이야기를 연속식혀 흥행중심으로 만들어 보왓스니 실패 안할 수 잇나요.
金東煥. 옛날 春園의 「開拓者」도 하섯지요.
羅雲奎. 초긔 작품인지라 다 성공이라 할 수 업섯지요. 대체로 보아 세상에 독자만코 선전이 잘된 점으로 春園의 작품이 흥행성적은 조치요.
金幽影. 그러치요. 춘원 것이 조키는 조흔데 장편은 영화하기 어럽겟서요. 단편이 낫지요.
