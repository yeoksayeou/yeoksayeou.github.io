長篇作家會議 (Conference of Novelists)
Attending Novelists
Ch’unwŏn Yi Kwang-su (Author of "Beyond Love and Desire" serialized in Chosŏn Ilbo)
Sangsŏp Yŏm Sang-sŏp (Author of "Discontinuous Line" serialized in Maeil Sinbo)
Hoewŏl Pak Yŏng-hŭi (Author of "Companion" serialized in Samch'ŏlli)
Manhae Han Yong-un (Author of "Regret" serialized in Chungang Ilbo)
Sanghŏ Yi T'ae-jun (Author of "Hwang Chin-i" serialized in Chungang Ilbo)
Wŏlt'an Pak Chong-hwa (Author of "Blood on the Brocade Robe" serialized in Maeil Sinbo)
Chang Hyŏk-chu (Author of "Dawn" serialized in Tonga Ilbo)
Kim Mal-bong (Author of "The Jungle" serialized in Tonga Ilbo)
Han Sŏl-ya (Author of "Twilight" serialized in Chosŏn Ilbo)
From this Magazine: Kim Tong-hwan, Pak Sang-hŭi<54>

1. Teacher, when writing your novel, do you primarily center the "characters and events" in the city or the countryside? Or do you gather material and express things from the modern world, or from the world of past history? What era and world do you believe today's Chosŏn writers must focus on in their work?

Yi Kwang-su (Author of "Beyond Love and Desire"): The novel "Beyond Love and Desire" I am currently writing (for the Chosŏn Ilbo) centers its "characters and events" in the city. And of course, I try to gather material and express things from the present world.
However, regarding the question of which "era and world" today's Chosŏn writers should focus on, I think it's utterly inappropriate to demand or dictate to all writers that they must focus on a specific era or world. Even if some critic demands it, it's impossible for it to happen that way.
There will be writers who depict this era and this world, and there will be writers who depict that era and that world. Or, depending on the individual writer, they can depict anything else.
If it's a true work of art aimed at the genuine happiness of humanity, then all writers, according to their individual personalities and characters, can depict this or that. We believe all writers should be allowed to create "art" that comes from a true heart, according to their own personalities and characters, without receiving any restrictions or interference; it doesn't matter what they depict.

Yi T'ae-jun (Author of "Hwang Chin-i"): I lost the paper with the agenda items for your question, so I cannot answer point by point. As the author of "Hwang Chin-i," I will just briefly say a word about something I wanted to tell the readers.

I heard from a reader that my "Hwang Chin-i" differs from historical facts (史實), and in case other readers might also make an issue of this, I want to write my opinion regarding historical facts and novels.

A novelist is a novelist and nothing else. They should write novels, not history. Just as one seeks out novelistic<55> characters and novelistic events in reality, one should seek out only such characters and events in history. Seeking means writing what should be written as it is, and creating what needs fabrication, even if it's a non-existent fact or historical event. Why create non-existent facts or historical events? Simply put, to construct literary truth.
Historical materials (史料), when you look into them, are laughable. Even for Hwang Chin-i's materials, the most detailed record, Yi Tŏk-tong's (李德洞) "Songdo Kii" (松都記異), was written by someone who hadn't seen Chin-i, based on what he heard from an 80-year-old woman named Chin (陳) at least several decades after Chin-i's death. So, how much of Hwang Chin-i's truth could appear there is a doubt that even history must naturally entertain. Therefore, even from a historian's perspective, to find the real Hwang Chin-i, one must scrutinize the historical materials and strive to pull out the Hwang Chin-i hidden behind them. How much more so for a novelist, who has not the slightest obligation to be faithful to historical materials?
A novel is not a historical lecture (歷史 講話). It derails if it becomes an enlightening popular adaptation (演義). First, even the term "historical novel" (歷史小說) itself is laughable if you think about it. If we call it a historical novel because the model was taken from history, should we call novels with models from reality "reality novels," and those from the future "future novels"?
This is by no means saying that novelists should ignore historical materials; it just means they should be thoroughly examined. A novelist, critic, or reader who insists on blind obedience to historical records knows little about novels and, more broadly, does not know what art is. Hattori Shisō (服部之總), though a historian, actually said this:
"When a historian criticizes historical literature by saying 'it's not according to the facts,' how disrespectful is that statement to literature? Rather, being able to write facts beyond the facts is the privilege of literature, and precisely because we are historians, we sometimes cannot help but envy that literary privilege. Historical materials are the sole guide to reaching the full picture (全貌) of historical reality, and yet they are also obstacles that conceal that full picture to some extent." We can only hope that our society will soon have more historians, scholars, and people with common sense who understand literature, so that writers themselves don't have to make such defenses.
September 4th, at Songjŏn<56>

Pak Yŏng-hŭi (Author of "Companion"): 1. Regarding characters and events, I mainly focus on the city and choose men and women living here.
2. I try to gather material from the modern world.
3. The importance regarding era and world differs depending on the writer, but for me, I want to express characters suffering in modern life.

Han Yong-un (Author of "Regret"): "Regret" is certainly not "countryside-centered" in its "characters and events," nor is it easy to pinpoint it as "city-centered." However, if I must say, it deals with city figures, so it could be said to be closer to the "city-centered" side. And "Regret" attempts to gather material and express the most recent Chosŏn reality within the modern world.
Now, regarding the question of which era and world today's Chosŏn writers should focus on, I think this way. "Art" does not necessarily have to depict only a certain era and world; I believe it can depict the "Heavens" or the "Underworld," transcending time and place. The question then lies in whether it is truly excellent "art" or not. If "art," unlike what some literary figures claim today, is not necessarily for one specific class or a few individuals, but like a flower (花), something anyone can appreciate and enjoy, then "artistry" cannot be said to require depicting only one society or class, let alone only one era or reality.
However, all things considered, as people of this era living in this reality, it seems appropriate in terms of order to naturally depict this era and world first, rather than because someone ordered it or out of some necessity.

Yŏm Sang-sŏp (Author of "Discontinuous Line"): City-centered, modern characters. I cannot quite grasp the meaning of "which era and world should be emphasized" in producing work.

Kim Mal-bong (Author of "The Jungle"): I write with a focus on the city. Rather than the past, I try to depict the modern world, and my ambition is to write in a way that offers some suggestion for the society to come, but I find it very difficult. I believe it is right for Chosŏn writers, regardless of whether they draw material from the past or the present, to write in order to guide us towards a better society than the present.

Pak Wŏlt'an (Pak Chong-hwa) (Author of "Blood on the Brocade Robe"): 1. "Blood on the Brocade Robe" intends to depict King Yŏnsan<57> and the scholars (선비) of that time, so rather than city or countryside, it aims to write with the palace and the period's ideology of the intellectual class of that era as its core.
The question asking which era and world to focus on for material is ambiguous. A writer grabs hold of something they think would be good to tackle and creates a piece of work from it; that lies in the state of mind reflected in the writer's artistic eye (藝術眼) at that moment, and one cannot say they must focus their pen strictly on a certain era.

Chang Hyŏk-chu (Author of "Dawn"): Countryside part is countryside-focused - from 10 years ago until now.
Capital part is capital-focused.
Focusing on the modern era.

Han Sŏl-ya (Author of "Twilight"): My humble work "Twilight," currently serialized in the Chosŏn Ilbo, mainly places its characters and events among the urban petit bourgeoisie and in factories. Of course, as has been my creative stance traditionally, "Twilight" also draws material from the modern world. I believe that the primary task of a writer, not just today but in any era, should always be sought in the reality of the respective generation. In a place like Chosŏn, where historical literature that hits the mark (正鵠) is scarce, I think this should be even more so. When even in recent history that we have directly heard and seen, the interpretations of so-called historians (史家) vary widely, what more needs to be said about the past? Interpretations by historians who lack a rigorous scientific methodology regarding history are truly like the blind feeling an elephant (群盲撫象).
Therefore, today's so-called historical novels are even more nonsensical fiction. It would be more honest to call them fireside tales (野談) or storyteller's narratives (講談).
However, just as we have come to see admirable scholars like Paek Nam-un, Yi Ch'ŏng-wŏn, Kim T'ae-jun, Ch'oe Yong-dal, and several others in the field of history, I believe good writers will emerge, albeit belatedly, in the field of historical novels as well. I touched upon this slightly in an article titled "Regarding Popular Novels" published previously in the Tonga Ilbo, but the editor's scissors made it somewhat ambiguous.

2. Considering the readers' level of knowledge (知識程度) and censorship (檢閱關係), how about expressing things through satirical literature (諷剌文學) or the form of historical tales/anecdotes (史話史譚)? Moreover, when writing a newspaper novel, what kind of struggles (苦心) do you undergo regarding the harmony between "artistry" (藝術性) and "popularity" (通俗性), or "pure literature" (純粹文學) and "mass literature" (大衆文學)?<58> And if possible, to which of the various schools like Realism, Idealism, Romanticism, Naturalism, etc., would you classify the work you are currently writing under?

Han Yong-un: I don't consider the forms of "satirical literature" or historical tales/anecdotes as excellent artistic expressions.
However, in newspaper novels (more so than other novels), rationally, one should combine "artistry" and "popularity," "purity" and "mass appeal." But if that's not possible, I think it might actually be better to lean towards "popularity" over "artistry," and towards the mass-oriented side rather than the purely literary one. Originally, it's a fundamental principle that "art" should be for the masses. If, no matter how much "artistry" is maintained and how "purely literary" it is, there is no mass readership (though it wouldn't be entirely absent, but extremely small), then one cannot help but see the more popular and mass-oriented side as better.
And while people classify works, saying this is realism, that is romanticism, this is naturalism, I believe it's impossible to make such strict distinctions. For instance, even in a highly psychological work, I doubt there's a single piece composed solely of psychologism from beginning to end, without elements of romanticism or realism. If observed closely, it can be said that any work contains bits of romanticism, realism, naturalism, and all sorts of schools. However, one can generally say that a certain writer tends significantly towards a particular school. In this sense, I could be said to belong to realism.

Pak Wŏlt'an: Satirical literature exists, but there probably isn't such a thing as historical tale (史話) or historical anecdote (史譚) literature. Historical tales and anecdotes are just that. If a work created under artistic contemplation (藝術的 觀照) takes a step further into being a historical novel, this requires a literary technique, so the term historical literature could be used. Using satirical literature and historical novels might offer some convenience (便宜) when considering censorship levels or the reader's knowledge level. However, that level of struggle is the same for any novel or poem.
Regarding the question about the struggle concerning the harmony between artistry and popularity, or pure literature and mass literature,<59> I cannot answer simply in one word, but to state the main point: one cannot draw a line and definitively say this is an artistic novel and this is a popular novel.
A novel with a lot of the self-absorption (自기도취) that all artists possess might be called an artistic novel, while something that is willing to sacrifice the self somewhat to talk with others, i.e., the masses, gets labeled a popular novel. So, someone humming to themselves in an ivory tower, happy alone whether others understand or not, might be considered true art in this sense. However, since we don't live in a world alone, when considering the social utility (社會的 功利) of literature or artists, we realize that an artist cannot simply be absorbed in themselves. Even if it means sacrificing some personal absorption, if it can be beneficial to this society, I want to share, realize, and learn together with the masses. This, of course, doesn't mean forgetting artistry. It means slightly lowering one's own level to talk together so that readers can understand, in order to raise their level.
And I dislike doing foolish things like conforming blindly (附和雷同) just because someone else, especially a foreigner, has established a new school, or clinging to outdated things like 'what school is this?' I just intend to try writing my text according to my own style when I feel like it.

Han Sŏl-ya: When writing newspaper novels, one generally develops many unnecessary concerns. If one becomes a so-called newspaper novelist and is content with that, conscience and concerns hardly matter; one just aims for the vulgar taste (俗趣味) of the majority and writes endlessly. However, if one tries even slightly to uphold the conscience of the era and consider artistry, one cannot do that. Yet, if one consistently adheres only to one's own convictions or persistence, it won't please the newspaper company, which prioritizes acquiring a large readership, so getting published again becomes an issue.
Thus, one inevitably has various concerns and considerations spanning both refined and popular (雅俗) aspects.
We often hear people say, "Write easily. Write works that can be read effortlessly," and there's a tendency, common to both the knowledgeable and the less knowledgeable, to think of this as truly excellent novel writing. But this is, so to speak, a desire to cater to vulgar tastes and remain within the bounds of worldly common sense. If so, the depth of artistic allure that truly makes one think will be castrated <60>and disappear. Here, artistry is hardly an issue.
However, pick up any work considered a world masterpiece. See if there are any works that can be read without using one's head, like the works of our so-called master novelists who aim for Kikuchi Kan or his mere imitators.
This, of course, does not mean one should deliberately write difficultly, nor does it mean stringing together stiff theories or abstract concepts wildly.
In newspaper novels, the aspect of simplicity (平易性) can never be neglected. Therefore, a person with artistic conscience faces many hidden hardships when writing novels for Chosŏn newspapers, which have such a severe lack of understanding of art, and finds it extremely difficult to strike a balance between the refined and the popular. Furthermore, when it comes to scenes expressing the "spirit of tomorrow" (明日の意氣), the general readership lacks interest in this, and it's a generation that has generally lost grand ideals. Consequently, the reactionary mood of the snobbish intelligentsia (소뿌르·인테리 - *soppŭrŭ-int'eri*, likely 'snob-intelligentsia'), who find such scenes unpleasant, deepens. Also, the author themself often becomes clumsy and constrained due to a lack of deep accumulation in the tradition of this emerging art.
Amidst such internal and external struggles, it might be plausible, as your question suggests, to borrow forms like satire or historical tales, anecdotes, and other secondary means. In any case, when taking a direct course (直線 코-쓰) is difficult, one might resort to roundabout methods and expediency (權道), so that might be acceptable too.
The harmony between artistry and popularity, or pure literature and mass literature, is, as mentioned above, one of the major concerns and struggles when writing newspaper novels. Popularity or mass literature is likely the demand and taste of the majority. For someone trying to write a truly proper work, it's impossible to simply drift into vulgarity (卑俗), base passions (劣情), and dilettantism (듸렛탄틔즘). Therefore, I primarily prioritize consideration for artistry and take the path of granting it universality so that the general public can easily understand it.
So-called mass literature, historical novels, etc. – what are called universal popular novels – are undoubtedly novels from a psychological perspective. What, then, generally forms the core distinction between popular novels and artistic novels?
Here, a clear definition (鮮明) of "artistry" should be brought up. This is a matter that should be written as a sequel (續稿) to "Regarding Popular Novels" published previously in the Tonga Ilbo,<61> so I will postpone the specific discussion (論) to the next opportunity and offer only a very general overview here.
Form (형상) in art refers to the artistic response to perceptible reality (感觀的 현실). That is, form is thought or idea manifested in a sensory format (△覺的 형식 - *likely 知覺的 형식, perceptual format*). Therefore, form must be a unity of thought and sensation. The difference between art and other sciences lies in this formal quality (형상성). Other sciences grasp things conceptually, but art must express them within a sensory format, i.e., within form. Therefore, whether the artist has expressed thought within a sensory format is the central issue of artistic evaluation and simultaneously the standard for judging artistry. To put it more simply, how well the content embedded in the work corresponds to reality—to the essence of reality—is the indicator for judging art. That is, the more the content matches reality, the higher its artistry, and the less it does, the weaker the artistry becomes.
To correspond well with reality, one must perceive reality well. Knowing reality well means not just knowing sensory phenomena but also discovering the underlying cause (物自體의 현상으로부터 다른 무엇인 원인) through reasoning. In other words, one must know the inner nature, the essence, not just the simple external phenomena. This is extremely difficult, and embedding this into artistic form is even more difficult. The objective world exists in complexity, diversity, and developmental motion. Its phenomena and essence, while actually two sides of the same coin, take different shapes and have different life durations. Expressing this reality within form is extremely difficult. This is why the works of numerous artists called geniuses differ in color, depth, and life.
Furthermore, form can only be truly artistic when it possesses the ability to generalize (개괄). That is, individual phenomena should not be depicted as isolated individual things, but as related to the whole—as part of the whole—so that the individual phenomenon can depict the whole and generalize the universal. Only then can one go beyond merely showing fixed phenomena and special cases, and make the individual phenomenon suggest the universal.
When unifying the general and the particular, science expresses the particular in the form of the general. Art, conversely,<62> when unifying the general and the particular, expresses the general in the form of the particular.
However, popular novels, while undoubtedly art from a psychological standpoint as mentioned above, lack this generalizing power. They only show individual phenomena confined within their scope. That is, they stop at the fixed. A simple analogy would be comparing a portrait photograph and a portrait painting. A photograph stops at the external appearance of an individual with specific features, but an artistic portrait painting imbues an inner life—breath, pulse, color, circulation (血行)—that generalizes all people. Yet, the figure does not lose its unique individuality and appearance, while also suggesting humanity as a whole. Therefore, although a photograph surpasses a painting in superficial accuracy, in terms of artistic value as discussed above, the photograph cannot possibly compare to the painting.
If we compare popular novels, which meticulously depict every nook and cranny of daily life without organization or selection, to photographs, then artistic form, which grasps the nature of reality and humanity in its growth and movement, can be compared to painting.
In short, artistic novels always adopt realistic form, while popular novels adopt photographic form, fixed form, or forms of fantasy (空想的), anecdote (逸話的), storyteller's narrative (講談的), or simple stories fabricated according to the author's subjectivity and taste. I will discuss this separately later and move on.
As can be guessed from the above, I adhere to realism. I believe this conviction will never waver in the future.
Bourgeois realism (뿌르·리알리즘) seemed to intend, for a start, to place its viewpoint on social reality, but in fact, its starting point was the individual, so the social reality appearing within it is merely social reality filtered through that individual. The objective world exists independently of human consciousness. Therefore, it is not moved in the slightest by individual subjectivity, nor is it properly perceived through individual subjectivity.
Therefore, social reality demands strictly objective perception. It goes without saying that the pioneer in this field in art is proletarian realism (푸로·리알리즘). I have discussed this several times, and my colleagues and friends continue to develop the discussion. Due to space limitations, I cannot elaborate further here, so I will abbreviate,<63> but I want to mention one thing: the distinction between romanticism (낭만주의) and romantic quality (낭만성). Recently, some editors boldly confuse these. "Roman" (로-만), as its etymology suggests, means creation or invention. Similarly, the romantic quality in literature is destined to be inseparable from art from its very genesis. It is by no means something that has become an issue only today. It's just that its intensity (稀薄) and weight (輕重) have varied with the times.
However, romanticism—some try to see it merely as a reaction against classicism or a new banner for human revival—was, in fact, always the art of a declining class. Although there are quite a few works called romanticist among the progressive writers of generations where class differentiation and its conflicts arise and develop, they merely possessed a strong romantic quality. There was always something else within them, and it was that something else that primarily moved, developed, and pioneered new territory, as we can clearly discover.
I will discuss this on another occasion and conclude here for now.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: 1. It is difficult to write according to the readers' level of knowledge. However, I think it's good if everyone can read and understand it. And I intend to elevate the artistic quality of the work. I believe the highest value of a work lies in its artistry. Some people seem to think that artistic works are often difficult to understand, but I believe that is quite a mistaken notion. The more artistic a work is, the clearer the author's view of life, and the more universally understood and enjoyable it is for everyone.
2. As for what school my current work will fall under, it seems best to wait for others' classification after the work is completed. I cannot say yet.

Yi Kwang-su: No matter how much one considers the censorship level and the readers' knowledge level, I don't think things like satirical literature are very good as art. Of course, even if you tell a writer who doesn't know how to write satire to create satirical literature because of a temporal need, it won't happen. And even if you tell a writer talented in satire not to write it, it's utterly impossible. I just don't think satirical literature is a very good art form as art itself.
Depending on the era and environment, satirical literature might flourish, or it might fade away.<64>
Next, not just for newspaper novels, but whenever I write any novel, I have never once thought about so-called "artistry," "popularity," or "pure literature." Even if world critics say Yi Kwang-su lacks artistry, is too popular, or whatever, I don't care about such debates. I just think that I've done my best to write this out of a sincere heart for true humanity, and I have no further thoughts beyond that. Also, whether writing a newspaper novel or any other novel, my thought is always just to write the most excellent work stemming from a true heart. I don't have the slightest thought like, "This is a newspaper novel, so I'll write it to be more 'popular'," or "This is a certain kind of work, so I'll emphasize 'artistry' more."
However, these days, the world seems to interpret so-called "artistry" as something inherently difficult to understand, a sort of pathological thing that only a few intellectuals can appreciate. But I think that originally, "art" should be something anyone can read and understand, meaning it should be popular (대중적). Therefore, I find it extremely difficult to strictly distinguish between so-called "artistry" and "popularity."
However, if "art" is something that the broad masses can enjoy and possess, perhaps rather than something excessively "artistic" or excessively "purely literary," it might be better to be "popular" and "mass-oriented" to be read by and engage with the masses. For any writer, it must be exceedingly difficult to create a work that preserves both "artistry" and "popularity," being both "purely literary" and "mass-oriented."
Also, when writing any work, I don't have the slightest thought like, "I'll write this romantically," or "I'll try writing that idealistically." In my view, I just create something that comes from a true heart through the form called art. It doesn't matter if critics later say this work belongs to this school or that.

Kim Mal-bong: I think adopting the form of well-refined satirical literature or transcendent historical tales and anecdotes would be a very good thing.
The harmony of artistry and popularity! It's hard to explain this in one word.<65> But even when I want to write psychological descriptions or the progression of events more profoundly and concretely, thinking about it being a newspaper novel naturally leads me to prioritize interest and 'not being boring,' and consequently, the work seems to deviate from the path of pure literature. I don't know which school my "The Jungle" belongs to. But it doesn't seem to be naturalism.

Chang Hyŏk-chu: Satire, historical tales – I think they are good.
I struggle greatly with the harmony of artistry and popularity. I cannot write about it briefly here.
Realism.

Yŏm Sang-sŏp: The harmony of "pure" and "mass" is certainly difficult; if done well, it would seem to be the pinnacle of newspaper novels. I struggle with it, but I cannot judge the effect myself. I write realistically.

3. Is there a "model" for the novel you are writing now? If so, what aspects interested you and led you to use them as a model? What was the motivation, and do you write the novel installment by installment daily? How many pages do you write per day, at what time do you write, and please share any struggles related to writing.

Kim Mal-bong: There are no models at all. On days I write a lot, it's about 100 pages (15 characters x 8 lines); on days I write little, it's one or two pages, but 30-40 pages (매 - *mae*, manuscript sheets) is probably average. Time is from 10 AM to 4 PM.
My writing struggle is the four children, aged eight down to three, sequentially pestering me.

Han Yong-un: There is a "model." It was just last summer. At that time, I was living in Sajik-tong within Seoul city. Since it was the hot summer season, I used to go up to the nearby Sajik Park (社稷公園) daily and pass the time under the shade of the zelkova trees. Then, as now, this Sajik Park was always a resting place for fortune tellers (사주쟁이), opium addicts (아편쟁이), and beggars who gathered from all directions. One day, I discovered a man who looked about 40, an opium addict and blind, and his wife. After that, I encountered the blind opium addict couple daily. Throughout the summer, the wife of the blind opium addict (around 30 years old) constantly begged ("동냥") to earn money for her husband's opium habit, providing for him and always sparing no effort for her husband's sake. I observed this scene intently. At that time, I was deeply moved<66> in my heart. I thought, "So this is what human love, especially marital love, is!" From then on, I had the idea that I must write a novel based on this "fact," which led me to start "Regret" this time. This was the "model" for this novel, and the main interest in bringing this "model" to life stemmed from the thought of depicting true marital love arising from the lowest stratum of humanity like that.
The amount I write daily is inconsistent. There are times when I write 3-4 installments consecutively, and times when I don't write anything for 4-5 days. Usually, I write early in the morning before breakfast and again in the evening. When inspiration doesn't flow well and I can't get hold of the pen, I don't even think about writing and don't touch the pen for 4-5 days. Once I've written a piece, I always read it over at least three times before sending it off.

Yi Kwang-su: There isn't really a specific "model" for the novel I'm writing now. I can't write every day. On days I write a lot, I write 3-4 installments, but on days I can't write at all, I don't manage even a single page. Usually, I write it in the morning. I often read it two or three times after writing, but when I'm extremely busy, there are times I send it off just once without reading it over.

Chang Hyŏk-chu: There are no models. (Or maybe there are, if you insist).
1 to 2 installments per day. In the morning.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: 1. There is no specific model, but perhaps it could be called a composite type (?) of similar individuals. In any case, it ends at that level and is almost non-modeled.
2. Nighttime is good for writing. Because it's quiet. I cannot speak of struggles yet because I am still in the process of writing, so I don't know how it will turn out. However, since it's not an easy task, how could I avoid struggles?

Pak Wŏlt'an: I am trying to write about a single naked individual named Yŏnsan-ju (燕山主). Of course, not the monarch Yŏnsan, but Yŏnsan as a human being. For this reason, I am currently writing horizontally about the scholar purges (士禍) and thought of that era.

Yŏm Sang-sŏp: No "model." I write one installment daily before breakfast.

Han Sŏl-ya: My humble work "Twilight," currently being serialized, has no particular basis in real people or events as models. If there is a model, perhaps it could be said that "reality" itself is the model, whether depicted well or poorly. In one or two places, I used my own experiences as a foundation, but they were treated quite differently<67> from the actual facts, and I don't recognize the need to disclose them publicly.
I have written up to 140 pages (200 characters per line, 10 lines) in a day, and writing around 100 pages daily seems manageable even if continued for a year. Recently, however, my health is poor, and due to being busy with worldly affairs (俗事多忙), I write at most 5-6 newspaper installments per day even when writing well.
There is no set time for writing. My house is bustling like a market day because of the many children. Emerging from that chaos, perhaps I could even write on the street, I don't know. Currently, pressed by miscellaneous tasks, I mainly write early in the morning and after dinner. If there's an urgent manuscript, I write even lying sick in bed, setting aside my bread-and-butter work and stealing glances from colleagues. This manuscript too was written "illegally" (非合法) in a convenient corner, ignoring the constitution of breadwinning.
My writing struggles are mostly covered above, so I will omit them. My psychological struggles as a mediocre talent (樗才) are the same now as before, but the most painful thing recently is that the rheumatism (류-마지쓰) that troubled my walking has spread to my right shoulder, the headquarters (本營) of my breadwinning.

4. Have you received criticism or advice about your novel from fellow writers? Also, do you have any hopes for the illustrators (揷畵家), hopes for the critics (評論家)? And do you reply individually to letters of protest or encouragement from readers? What are your feelings about those?

Chang Hyŏk-chu: I have received advice.
I reply as much as possible, but I often forget due to being busy.

Pak Wŏlt'an: 1. Not yet, but I look forward to receiving advice given out of friendship and sincerity in the future.
Remainder omitted (以下略).

Yi Kwang-su: There isn't much private criticism or advice. Also, I have no particular hopes for illustrators or critics. I often receive various heartfelt letters from readers, and I usually reply to each one promptly.

Kim Mal-bong: I have heard criticism and advice sometimes (from Im Hwa-ssi and Paek Ch'ŏl-ssi). I offer great thanks to the illustrator (Chŏng Hyŏn-ung-ssi).
Regarding letters from readers, I used to reply initially, but recently, being busy and also lacking sincerity, I don't reply often. However, the debt I feel in my heart for not replying grows larger.<68>

Han Sŏl-ya: I haven't heard any criticism or advice from fellow writers about my humble work "Twilight." Perhaps it's the same for myself, but it seems rare even among so-called novelists to continuously read long novels. It seems even among my closest writer friends, there isn't a single one who reads it continuously.
I don't have particular hopes for critics, but recently I do have a considerable desire for them to steadfastly attack (論擊) the degenerate critics who abandon clear water and try to bloom again in the mire like flowers of evil (惡の華 - *Aku no Hana*), and the protective-colored critics (保護色 評家) who look back and forth between sides but actually serve as lackeys (走狗) for the bourgeoisie.
I have not received letters of protest from readers. However, I imagine such people exist. Perhaps they don't send them because they don't know the address or lack the passion (熱). I sometimes imagine pale, powerless readers (拾讀者 - *lit. picking-up readers, casual readers?*) who start writing protest letters to me but give up, and addle-brained (뇌-란) individuals steeped in romance novels and fireside tales. As for letters expressing goodwill, perhaps encouragement? In any case, I occasionally receive such letters. Of course, compared to the letters received by the so-called "greats" of Chosŏn from waitresses (女給), kisaeng (妓生), modern girls (모-던 껄), etc., mine are nothing in terms of quantity or their beautiful, fragrant quality, but I consider not receiving that kind of praise as a point of pride.
My satisfaction comes not from these letters of encouragement understanding or praising my work, but from realizing, upon receiving them, that the distance between that person and me is extremely close. That is where I feel satisfaction and joy.

Yŏm Sang-sŏp: Nothing particular.

Pak Wŏlt'an: The illustrations by Mr. Yi Sŭng-man, the illustrator currently drawing for my novel, reach an exquisite realm (絶妙入境), which I always admire (敬服). I am still satisfied and only hope for the great success (大成) of my esteemed colleague (杏仁 - *lit. apricot kernel, likely a term of respect/endearment*); I have no other hopes.
Sometimes there is encouragement from close friends (親友) and inquiries from unknown persons (未知人). If asked, I simply answer kindly.

Han Yong-un: No criticism or advice from writers. No hopes for illustrators or critics. No particular correspondence from readers yet. <69>

**Summary**

This 1936 roundtable discussion features prominent Korean novelists discussing their craft, particularly concerning serial novels for newspapers. Key themes include the choice between contemporary urban/rural settings versus historical subjects, with Yi T'ae-jun defending artistic license over strict historical accuracy in historical fiction, while Han Sŏl-ya criticizes inaccurate historical novels. There's a significant debate on balancing artistic integrity ("artistry," "pure literature") with mass appeal ("popularity," "mass literature"), especially given newspaper demands and reader levels. Writers like Han Yong-un suggest popularity might be preferable if artistry alienates readers, while Pak Yŏng-hŭi and Han Sŏl-ya prioritize artistry, though Han acknowledges the struggle for clarity and universality. Several writers discuss their adherence to realism, with Han Sŏl-ya distinguishing it from bourgeois realism and affirming proletarian realism, while also clarifying the difference between romanticism (as a school) and romantic quality (as an artistic element). They also share practical aspects like writing habits, dealing with distractions (Kim Mal-bong's children, Han Sŏl-ya's rheumatism), the use (or lack) of models, and interactions with readers and critics.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is revealing. The explicit mention of "censorship" (檢閱關係) highlights the constraints writers operated under. The debate around historical fiction could partly reflect a search for safer ground for social commentary, though Han Sŏl-ya's sharp critique suggests this wasn't straightforward. The intense discussion on "artistry" vs. "popularity" reflects the broader tension between developing a modern national literature and the commercial/political pressures of the colonial media landscape (newspapers). Han Sŏl-ya's references to bourgeois vs. proletarian realism, class consciousness, and criticism of "degenerate critics" point directly to the ideological struggles (KAPF influence vs. others) within the literary scene of the era. The writers' awareness of their audience's "knowledge level" and the need to potentially "lower" their standard (Pak Wŏlt'an) or find a "middle ground" (Han Sŏl-ya) speaks to the challenges of fostering a national readership under colonial conditions.

史料란 소설가라 하여 구태여 무시하란 것이 결코 아니요 충분히 음미할 것 뿐이라는 말이다. 史的 기록에 맹종해야 한다는 소설가나 평론가나 독자는 적게 소설을 모르며 크게 예술이 무엇인지 모르는 사람이다. 服部之總이란 이는 사학자이되 도리혀 이런 말을 했다.

그런데 오늘날 朝鮮 작가는 어느 시대와 세상에 치중해서 작품을 써야하겠느냐 하는 무름은, 나는 이렇게 생각한다. 「예술」이라 하는 것은 반듯이 어떤 시대와 세상만을 그려야만 하는 것이 아니라, 시대와 세상을 떠나서 「天上」을 그릴 수도 있는 것이요, 「地下」를 그릴 수도 있다고 생각한다. 문제는 그러면 그것이 과연 훌늉한 「예술」이냐 아니냐에 있을 것이다. 「예술」* 오늘날 일부의 문학자들이 말하는 거와 같이 반듯이 어느 한 계급이나 멫멫 개인을 위하는 것이 아니고 예를 꽃(花)에 비해서, 누구나 감상할 수 있는 것, 즐길 수 있는 것이라면 「예술성」 그것은 어느 한 사회나 계급은 물론이요, 어느 한 시대나 현실만을 그려야 한다고 할 수는 없을 것이다.
그러나 이왕이면, 이 시대의 사람이요 이 현실에서 살어가는 사람이라면 누가 시킨다든지 어떠한 필요에서보담도 자연히 이 시대와 세상을 먼저 그려내는 것이 순서를 보아서도 온당한 일이라고 보겠다.
廉相涉(「不連續線」 작자로서) 도시 중심, 현대 인물이외다. 작품제작에 「어느 시대와 세상에 치중하여야 올켔다」는 의미는 잘 알 수가 없읍니다.
金末峰(「密林」 작자로서)  도시 중심으로 써갑니다. 과거보다도 현대의 세상을 그려보고 따라서 장차 올 사회에 어떤 암시가 되도록 썼으면 하는 것이 나의 야심이올시다만 대단히 어렵다고 생각함니다. 朝鮮 작가는 그 취재가 과거와 현대를 불문하고 그것이 지금보다 나흔 사회를 유도하기 위하여 쓰는 것이 올켔다고 신념하옵니다.
朴月灘(「錦衫의 피」 작자로서)  1. 「錦衫의 피」는 燕山君과<57> 그 때 선비들을 그리려고 한 것이니 도시, 또는 농촌보다 궁중과 그 시대의 지식계급의 시대 사상을 핵심으로 하야 써내려 가려는 것입니다.
어느 시대와 세상에 취재를 치중하여야 올켔느냐고 무르신 말슴은 모호한 소리입니다. 작가는 자기가 한번 손을 대여 보았으면 좋게다 생각하는 그것을 부뜰어 가지고 한 개 창작을 맨드러 놓는 것이니 그것은 그 때 작가의 藝術眼에 비최인 심경에 있는 것이고 어느 시대에만 한하야 꼭 붓을 들어 치중한다고는 말할 수 없는 일입니다.
張赫宙(「黎明期」 작자로서)  농촌편은 농촌 중심-10년전부터 지금까지.

韓雪野(「昏」 작자로서)  目下 朝鮮日報에 연재중인 졸작 『黃昏』은 인물과 사건을 주로 都市小市民層과 공장에 두고 있읍니다. 물론 종래의 나의 창작적 태도도 그랬지만 『황혼』도 현대의 세상에서 취재하였읍니다. 오늘날 작가뿐 아니라 어느 시대에든지 작가의 第一義的 과제는 항상 當該 세대의 현실에서 구하여야 할 줄 압니다. 朝鮮과 같이 正鵠을 얻은 사학문헌이 적은 데에서는 더욱 그래야 할 것이라고 나는 생각합니다. 우리가 직접 聞見하고 있는 最近世 역사에 있어서도 소위 史家의 해석이 區區不一하거던 하물며 기왕 것이야 말하야 무엇하겠읍니까. 역사에 대한 엄정한 과학적 방법론이 서지 못한 史家의 해석이란 실로 이렇게 群盲撫象격입니다.
그런데 今日의 이른바 역사소설이란 더욱더 말할 수 없는 엉터리 소설들입니다. 野談이나 講談이라고 하면 오히려 정직한 맛이나 있겠지요.
그러나 이미 사학방면에 있어서 白南雲, 李淸源, 金台俊, 崔容達 기타 數氏와 같은 존경할 만한 학자를 보게된 것 같이 역사소설 방면에서도 때늦게나마 좋은 작가가 나오리라고 생각합니다. 여기 대해서도 전자 東亞日報에 실린 「通俗小說에 대하야」라는 一文 중에서 약간 건드려본 일이 있으나 편집자가 다소 가위질을 해서 모호하게 되여버렸드군요.

2. 독자의 知識程度와 檢閱關係 등을 고려할 때에 諷剌文學 又는 史話史譚의 형식을 취하여 표현함이 如何할는지요? 더구나 신문소설을 써나려가실 때 「藝術性」과 「通俗性」 又는 「純粹文學」과 「大衆文學」의<58> 조화에 대하야 어떠한 苦心을 하여가십니까. 그리고 될 수 있으면 현재 집필 중의 그 작품을 類別하자면 寫實主義, 理想主義, 浪漫主義, 自然主義 등등 여러 주의 중 어디에 속하도록 써 가십니까.

韓龍雲  「풍자문학」이나 史話史譚의 형식을 훌늉한 예술적 표현으로는 생각되지 않는다.
그런데 新聞小說(다른 소설보다도)에 있어서는 합리적으로는 「예술성」과 「통속성」, 「순수성」과 「대중성」을 겸하여야 하겠지마는 그렇지 못할 경우에는, 「예술성」보다는 「통속성」에 순수적인 것보다는 대중적인 편이, 도리혀 좋지 않을가 나는 생각된다. 본래 「예술」이란 대중적이여야 하는 것은 근본 원리인데, 아무리 「예술성」을 지키고 「순수문학」적이라 하드라도 독자대중이 없다면(전연 없지는 않겠지만은 극소수인 경우), 좀 더 통속성과 대중적인 편이 낫다고 보지 않을 수 없다.
그러고 어떠한 작품을 놓고, 이것은 사실주의니 낭만주의이니 자연주의이니 하지만은 그렇게 엄밀히 구별할 수는 도저히 없을 줄로 안다. 가령 아무리 心理主義的인 작품이라 하드라도 다른 낭만이니 사실이니 하는 것이 없이 처음부터 끗까지 심리주의로만 구성된 작품은 하나도 없을 줄 안다. 엄밀히 관찰한다면 어떠한 작품을 물론하고 그 한 개의 작품속에는 낭만, 사실, 자연 등 별별 주의가 조금식은 거이 포함되여 있다고 말할 수 있다. 그러나 대체로 어느 작가는 어떤 주의의 경향을 다분히 가지고 있다고는 말할 수 있을 것이다. 이런 의미로라면 나는 사실주의에 속한다고 할 수 있는 것이다.
朴月灘,  풍자문학은 있지만은 史話나 史譚문학이란 말은 없을 겝니다. 史話와 史譚은 제대로 史話요 史譚이겠지요. 한거름 나아가 예술적 觀照아래서 창작해 놓은 게 역사소설이라면 이것은 한 개 문학적 기교을 요구하는 것이니 곧 역사문학이란 말을 사용할 수 있겠지요. 풍자문학과 역사소설을 가지고 검열수준, 또는 독자의 지식정도를 고려할 때에 다소 便宜한 점도 있겠지요. 그러나 그것은 어느 소설이나 시문을 물론해 놓고 다 그만한 고심이 있는 것은 맛찬가지겠지요.
예술성과 통속성 또는 순수문학과 대중문학과의 조화에 대하야 어떠한 고심을 가젓느냐고 물으시는 말슴에 대하야는 얼는 한 말로 간단하게 대답할 수 없으나 그 綱領만을 말슴한다면, 예술소설과 통속소설이란<59> 어떤 금 하나를 그어놓고 이것은 예술소설이오 이것은 통속소설이다 劃然하게 말할 수 없는 것입니다.
예술가마다 다 가지고 있는 자기도취를 다분히 가지고 있는 소설을 예술소설이라 하겠고 좀 자기를 희생해서라도 남 곧 대중과 이야기하고 싶어하는 것이 통속소설이란 이름을 듯고 있는 것이겠지요. 그리게 남들이 알건 몰으건 저 혼자 좋아서 상아탑 숙에서 콧노래를 부르는 사람이 이 점으로 보면 진정한 의미의 예술이겠지요. 그러나 우리는 혼자 사는 세상이 아니니가 문학 또는 예술가에게 대한 사회적 功利를 생각할 때, 이렇게 예술가 한 사람만 도취해서는 않될 것을 깨닷고 있읍니다. 좀 自家의 도취를 희생해서라도 그것이 이 사회에 유익한 것이 된다면 대중과 더부러 난호어 깨닷고 배호고 싶습니다. 그것은 물론 예술성을 망각한다는 말은 않이겠지요. 독자의 수준을 끌어올리기 위하야 자기의 수준을 약간 떠러트려서라도 같이 알어듯도록 이야기한다는 말입니다.
그리고 무슨 주의니 무엇이니 하는 케케목은 데다가 그렇지 않으면 외국사람 누가 하나 새 주의를 세워놓았다고 附和雷同하는 철없는 짓을 하기는 싫읍니다. 그저 나는 내 주격대로 내 글을 쓰고 싶은 때 써보랴고 할 뿐입니다.
韓雪野  신문소설을 쓰게 되면 대체로 불필요한 관심을 많이 가지게 됩니다. 이른바 신문소설쟁이가 되어버리고, 그것으로 자족해한다면 양심도 관심도 별로 문제될 거 없이 그저 다수자의 俗趣味를 목표로 하고 쓰는데 끊지겠지만, 다소라도 시대의 양심을 살리랴하고 예술성을 고려하게 되면 그렇게 할 수 없게 됩니다. 그렇다고 전연 자기의 소신이나 고집으로만 일관해나가면 독자 다수 획득을 主眼으로 하는 신문사의 비위에 맞지 않을 것이니 다시 써먹기가 문제지요.
해서 그야말로 雅俗 양면에 亘하야 여러 가지의 관심과 고려를 가지지 않을 수 없게 됩니다.
우리는 흖이 『쉽게 써라. 수월히 읽을 수 있는 작품을 써라』하는 말을 듯고 있으며, 또 그것을 참말 훌늉한 소설인 것같이 생각되는 경향이 識者나 또는 그렇지 못한 사람에게 공통되는 상식이 되어 있는 것을 보고 있으나 이것은 말하자면 俗趣味에 맞고, 속세간의 상식범위에 머물기를 바라는 말이니 그러면 참말 무엇을 생각케하는 예술적 매력의 심도는 去勢되고 <60>말 것입니다. 여기서는 예술성같은 것은 거이 문제되지 않습니다.
그러나 세계적 명작이라는 그 어느 작품이든지 집어보십시요. 菊池寬이나 겨우 그 亞流를 목표로 나가는 우리들의 이른바 소설쟁이 大家들의 작품같이 머리를 씀이 없이 읽을 수 있는 작품이 있는가 시험해 보십시요.
이것은 물론 위정 어렵게 쓰라는 말도 아니요, 또는 生硬한 이론이나 추상적인 개념을 야단스레 나열하라는 말은 않임니다.
신문소설에 있어서는 평이성이라는 일면을 결코 등한시 할 수 없음니다. 그러기 때문에 예술적 양심을 가진 사람이 朝鮮의 신문같이 예술에 대한 몰이해가 심한 신문에 소설을 쓰는 데에는 남모르는 고충이 많은 것이며, 또는 雅俗의 중용을 취하기가 非常히 곤란합니다. 더욱이 『明日의 意氣』를 표현하는 장면에 이르러서는 일반 독자가 아직 여기 대한 관심이 없는 데다가 일반적으로 커다란 이상을 잃어버린 세대이니만치 차라리 이런 장면을 불유쾌로 대하는 소뿌르·인테리의 반동기분이 깊어가고 또는 작자 자신이 이러한 신흥 예술의 깊이 있는 전설의 축적이 부족한 관계로 서투르고 군색하게 되는 수가 많습니다.
이렇게 내로 외로 고민하는 남아에 貴問과 같이 諷剌 혹은 史話, 史譚 기타의 형식과 부차적 수단을 빌게 되는 수도 있을 법합니다. 아모려나 직선 코-쓰를 취하기 곤란한 때에는 외도의 수단방법과 權道도 취하는 수가 있으니까 그도 무방하겠지요.
예술성과 통속성 又는 순수문학과 대중문학의 조화라는 것은 우에서도 말한 바와 같이 신문소설을 쓰는 데에 있어서의 중요한 관심의 하나요, 또 고심의 하나입니다. 통속성이나 대중문학은 필시 다수자의 요구일 것이오 취미일 것이다. 참말 옳은 작품을 쓰랴는 사람으로서는 도저히 卑俗과 劣情과 듸렛탄틔즘에 흘러버릴 수는 없는 것이니, 주로 나는 예술성에 대한 고려를 第一義로 하고, 그것을 일반이 이해하기 쉽도록 보편성을 부여하는 길을 취하고 있읍니다.
소위 대중문학, 역사소설 등- 보편성 통속소설이라고 불러지는 것도 심리학적으로 보면 소설임에는 틀림없읍니다. 그러면 대체로 무엇이 통속소설 또는 예술소설을 구별짓는 핵심이 되는가?
여기서 마땅히 『예술성』에 관한 鮮明이 上程되어야 할 것이다. 이것은 전자 東日亞報報에 발표한 『통속소설에 대하야』의 續稿로 써야 할 문제이기로 그 구체적인<61> 論은 다음 기회로 미루고 여기서는 극히 개괄적으로 약간 논급하기로 하겠읍니다.
예술에 있어서의 형상이라는 것은 感觀的 현실의 예술적 반응을 이르는 말입니다. 즉 형상은 △覺的 형식 중에 나타난 사상, 관념입니다. 그러기 때문에 형상은 사상과 감각의 통일이여야 합니다. 예술이 다른 과학과 다른 점도 이 형상성에 있는 것입니다. 다른 과학은 사물을 개념에 있어서 포착하지만 예술은 감각적 형식 중에서 즉 형상 속에서 그것을 표현해야 합니다. 때문에 예술가가 사상을 감각적 형식중에 표현하였는가 않하였는가가 예술평가의 중심문제요 동시에 예술성 판단의 기준도 여기에 있는 것입니다. 다시 쉽게 말하자면 예술적 형상의 根扺에 가로누어 있는 사물과 사상이 얼마나 현실에- 현실의 본질에 적응한가가 예술을 판단하는 지표가 되는 것입니다. 즉 작품에 담기인 내용이 현실에 부합되면 될수록 그 예술성은 고도인 것이며, 그렇지 않으면 못할수록 예술성이 희박해지는 것입니다.
현실에 잘 부합되자면 현실을 잘 인식해야 하는데, 현실을 잘 안다는 것은 다만 감각적 현상을 알 뿐 아니라 사유에 의하야 物自體의 현상으로부터 다른 무엇인 원인을 알어내야 하는 것입니다. 즉 단순한 외적인 현상에 끊지지 않고 내적인 본연, 본질을 알어야 한다는 말입니다. 이것은 非常히 어려운 일인데, 이것을 예술적 형상에 담는 일은 또 더욱 어려운 일입니다. 복잡성, 다양성, 발전 운동 중에 있는 객관세계 현상과 본질이 기실은 같은 것의 양면이면서도 형태를 달리하고 생명의 지속을 달리하는 현실 세계를 형상 가운데에 나타내는 것은 非常히 어려운 일이니 천재로써 불러지는 幾多의 예술가의 작품이 각각 색채와 심도와 생명을 달리하는 것도 이 때문입니다.
그런데 더욱이 형상은 개괄하는 능력을 가진 때에만 참말 예술적일 수 있읍니다. 즉 개개의 현상을 동떠러진 개개의 것으로 그리지 않고, 전체와 관련있는 것으로- 전체의 일환으로 그려서 그 개개의 현상이 능히 전체를 그리고 일반적인 것을 개괄하여야 합니다. 그래야 고정적인 현상과 특별한 경우만을 보이는 데에 끊지지 않고 나아가서 그 개개의 현상으로서 일반적인 것을 방불케 할 수 있는 것입니다.
과학은 일반적인 것과 개별적인 것을 통일하는 경우에 있어서 일반적인 것의 형식 중에서 개별적인 것을 표현하나 예술은 그와 반대로 일반적인 것과 개별적인 것을<62> 통일하는 때에 있어서 개별적인 것의 형식 중에서 일반적인 것을 표현하는 것입니다.
한데 통속소설이란 것은 우에서도 말한 바와 같이 심리학상으로 보면 역시 예술임에는 틀림없으면서도, 이 개괄력이 없음니다. 즉 개개의 현상을 다만 그 범위 내에 국한한 것으로밖에 더 보이지 못합니다. 즉 고정적인 것에 끊일 뿐입니다. 한 개의 손쉬운 예로 인물사진과 인물화를 끄러다 본다면, 사진인 경우에는 특수한 相貌를 가진 한 개의 외면적 인물에 끊지나 예술적인 인물화인 경우에는 만인을 개괄하는 내적 생명- 호흡과 맥박과 색채와 血行이 부여되는 것입니다. 그리면서도 그 인물은 그 개성과 개체의 독특한 相貌를 잃지 않는 것이며 또는 인간전체를 방불케 하는 것입니다. 그러기 때문에 표면적 정확성에 있어보면 사진은 회화보다 우월하면서도 이상에서 말한 바 예술적 가치에 있어서 보면 사진은 도저히 회화의 比가 되지 못합니다.
일상 생활의 구석구석을 무정리, 무선택하게 샅샅이 그리는 통속소설을 사진에 비긴다면, 현실과 인간의 본연을 그 生長과 운동 중에서 파악하는 예술적 형상을 회화에 비길 것입니다.
한 말로 끊지자면 예술소설은 늘 현실적 형상을 취하는 것이오 통속소설은 사진적 형상, 고정적 형상, 또는 작가의 주관과 취미에 의하야 비저진 空想的, 逸話的, 講談的, 이얘기의 형상을 취하는 것입니다. 여기 대해서는 論을 달리하기로 하고 다음으로 옮기겠읍니다.
우에서 말한 데에서도 추측할 수 있을 것이나 나는 사실주의를 취하고 있읍니다. 앞으로도 이 신조는 결코 움지기지 않으리라고 믿습니다.
뿌르·리알리즘은 그 관점을 一應, 사회현실에 두랴고 한 듯하나 기실은 그 출발점을 개인에 두었드니만치, 그 속에 나오는 사회현실은 그 개인을 통과한 사회현실임에 지나지 않습니다. 객관세계란 인간의 의사와는 독립해서 존재해 있는 것입니다. 그럼으로 그것은 개인의 주관에 의하야 조금도 움지겨짐이 없으며, 개인의 주관을 통하야 정당히 인식되는 것이 아닙니다.
그리기 때문에 현실사회는 엄정히 객관적 인식을 요구하는 것임니다. 예술방면에 있어서의 이 개척자는 푸로·리알리즘임은 두말할 것도 없는 일이나, 여기 대해서 필자도 여러 번 논해온 것이오 또 同僚 諸友도 지금까지 게속하야 論을 전개하고 있으며 지면의 관계로써 더 논할 수 없음으로 이에서는 略하거니와 다만 한<63> 가지 말하고 싶은 것은 낭만주의와 낭만성의 구별임니다. 근래의 일부 編者는 이것을 대담히 혼동하고 있옵니다. 『로-만』이라는 것은 그 어원이 말하는 바와 같이 창작, 또는 창조를 의미하는 말인 것 같이 문예의 낭만성이란 것은 예술의 발생과 동시에 예술과 끝까지 떠러지지 못할 운명을 가지고 있는 것임니다. 그것은 결코 오늘날에 와서 비로소 문제되는 것도 아닙니다. 다만 시대에 따라서 그 稀薄과 輕重이 있었을 뿐임니다.
그런데 낭만주의란 것은 혹자는 고전주의에 대한 반발로 또는 인간부흥의 새로운 기치로만 보려고 드나 사실은 언제든지 이것은 몰락하는 계급의 예술이였든 것입니다. 게급 분화와 그 대립이 생성발전하는 세대의 진보적인 작가의 작품에도 낭만주의로써 불러지는 작품이 적지 않으나, 그것은 낭만성을 다분히 가젔달 뿐으로 그 속에는 항상 딴 무엇이 있었고 그딴 무엇이 주로 움지기고 발전하야 新境地를 개척한 것을 우리는 歷然히 발견할 수 있는 것입니다.
