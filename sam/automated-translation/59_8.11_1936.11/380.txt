詩經(4)
						岸曙 (Ansŏ)
召南第二 (Shaonan Part 2)

Magpie's Nest                  巢  鵲 (Cháo Què)

In the magpie's nest,            維鵲有巢 (Wéi què yǒu cháo)
Does the turtledove stay?        維鳩居之 (Wéi jiū jū zhī)
When the princess gets married,  之子于歸 (Zhī zǐ yú guī)
A hundred chariots meet her.     百輛御之 (Bǎi liàng yù zhī)

In the magpie's nest,            維鵲有巢 (Wéi què yǒu cháo)
Does the turtledove live?        維鳩方之 (Wéi jiū fāng zhī)
When the princess gets married,  之子于歸 (Zhī zǐ yú guī)
A hundred chariots see her off.  百輛將之 (Bǎi liàng jiāng zhī)

In the magpie's nest,            維鵲有巢(226) (Wéi què yǒu cháo)
Does the turtledove fill it?     維鳩盈之 (Wéi jiū yíng zhī)
When the princess gets married,  之子于歸 (Zhī zǐ yú guī)
A hundred chariots attend her before and behind. 百輛成之 (Bǎi liàng chéng zhī)

Gathering Artemisia              采  蘩 (Cǎi Fán)

By the pond and the sea edge,    于以采蘩 (Yú yǐ cǎi fán)
Why gather artemisia?            蘩于沼于 (Fán yú zhǎo yú)
For the rituals of the Duke,     于以用之 (Yú yǐ yòng zhī)
We will perform the ancestral rites. 公候之事 (Gōng hóu zhī shì)

In the valley stream,            于以采蘩 (Yú yǐ cǎi fán)
Why gather artemisia?            于澗之中 (Yú jiàn zhī zhōng)
In the Duke's ancestral shrine,  于以用之 (Yú yǐ yòng zhī)
We will perform the public ancestral rites. 公候之宮 (Gōng hóu zhī gōng)

With hair neatly bound,          被之憧憧 (Pī zhī chōng chōng)
Attending the shrine rites day and night, 夙夜在公 (Sù yè zài gōng)
With hair neatly bound,          被之郊郊 (Pī zhī jiāo jiāo)
We shall soon return home.       薄言還歸 (Bó yán huán guī)

Grass Insects                  草  蟲 (Cǎo Chóng)

When the grasshopper sings "ssyŏl ssyŏl," 喓喓草蟲(227) (Yāo yāo cǎo chóng)
It hops here and turns there again. 趯趯阜蟲 (Tì tì fù chóng)
How many years since my beloved left? Alone and lonely, 未見君子 (Wèi jiàn jūnzǐ)
My heart, waiting in the room, is anxious. 憂心忡忡 (Yōu xīn chōng chōng)
If only I could see him once,    亦旣見止 (Yì jì jiàn zhǐ)
If only I could meet him once,   亦旣覯止 (Yì jì gòu zhǐ)
How much my heart would be eased. 我心則降 (Wǒ xīn zé jiàng)

Going up that South Mountain,    陟彼南山 (Zhì bǐ Nánshān)
Shall I try picking bracken ferns and playing? 言采其蕨 (Yán cǎi qí jué)
How many years since my beloved left? Alone and lonely, 未見君子 (Wèi jiàn jūnzǐ)
My heart, waiting in the room, is filled with sorrow. 憂心綴綴 (Yōu xīn zhuì zhuì)
If only I could see him once,    亦旣見止 (Yì jì jiàn zhǐ)
If only I could meet him once,   亦旣觀止 (Yì jì guān zhǐ)
How joyful my heart would be.    我心則說 (Wǒ xīn zé yuè)

Going up that South Mountain,    陟彼南山 (Zhì bǐ Nánshān)
Shall I try gathering edible wild plants and pass the time? 言秉其薇 (Yán bǐng qí wēi)
How many years since my beloved left? Alone and lonely, 未見君子 (Wèi jiàn jūnzǐ)
My heart, waiting in the room, is grieving. 我心傷悲 (Wǒ xīn shāng bēi)
If only I could see him once,    亦旣見止 (Yì jì jiàn zhǐ)
If only I could meet him once,   亦旣觀止 (228) (Yì jì guān zhǐ)
How much my heart would be calmed. 我側則夷 (Wǒ cè zé yí)

Gathering Water-Chestnuts       采 蘋 (Cǎi Pín)

In the southern waters,          于以采蘋 (Yú yǐ cǎi pín)
Gathering water-chestnuts,       南澗之濱 (Nán jiàn zhī bīn)
In the pooled water, the reservoir, 于以采蘋 (Yú yǐ cǎi pín)
Gathering water-weeds.           于彼行潦 (Yú bǐ xíng lǎo)

Square and round,                于以盛之 (Yú yǐ shèng zhī)
Put them in baskets,             維筐及筥 (Wéi kuāng jí jǔ)
In pots and cauldrons,           于以湘之 (Yú yǐ xiāng zhī)
Boil them thoroughly,            維尊及及 (Wéi zūn jí jí - *Translator's note: Original Korean seems to misinterpret or adapt the Chinese significantly here. 湘 is boiling/cooking, but 尊 usually refers to ritual vessels.*)

In the ancestral shrine of the main family, 于以尊之 (Yú yǐ zūn zhī - *Translator's note: Again, 尊 is interpreted differently*)
Offer the rites,                 宗室蹁下 (Zōngshì pián xià - *Translator's note: Chinese text differs*)
Performing the shrine offering is 維其戶之 (Wéi qí hù zhī - *Translator's note: Chinese text differs*)
The esteemed young lady.         有齋季女 (Yǒu zhāi jì nǚ)

Sweet Pear Tree                 棠  甘 (Táng Gān - *Translator's note: Likely intended as 甘棠 Gāntáng*)

The densely growing sweet pear tree, 蔽芾甘棠(229) (Bì fèi gāntáng)
Do not cut it, do not fell it.   勿剪勿伐 (Wù jiǎn wù fá)
It's where our lord of the past rested. 召伯所我 (Shào bó suǒ wǒ - *Translator's note: Chinese text usually has 召伯所茇 Shào bó suǒ bá*)

The densely growing sweet pear tree, 蔽芾甘棠 (Bì fèi gāntáng)
Do not cut it, do not fell it.   勿剪勿伐 (Wù jiǎn wù fá)
It's where our lord of the past halted. 召伯所說 (Shào bó suǒ shuō - *Translator's note: Chinese text usually has 召伯所憩 Shào bó suǒ qì*)

The densely growing sweet pear trees, 蔽芾甘棠 (Bì fèi gāntáng)
Do not cut them, do not fell them. 勿剪勿伐 (Wù jiǎn wù fá)
It's where our lord of the past took pleasure. 召伯所說 (230) (Shào bó suǒ shuō)

**Summary**

This article presents Korean translations of five poems from the "Shaonan" section of the ancient Chinese "Book of Odes" (Shijing). The poems cover themes of a noblewoman's wedding procession ("Magpie's Nest"), women performing ritual duties ("Gathering Artemisia", "Gathering Water-Chestnuts"), longing for an absent beloved ("Grass Insects"), and reverence for a respected past ruler, Duke Shao ("Sweet Pear Tree"). The translations aim to capture the essence of the original poems in colloquial Korean of the 1930s, using older spellings and phrasing. For historians, this piece reflects the continued engagement with Chinese classics in colonial Korea, potentially as a way to assert cultural continuity or explore universal human emotions (marriage, duty, longing, respect for good governance) within the constraints of the colonial period. The choice of poems, particularly those emphasizing ritual propriety and respect for just rulers of the past, might hold subtle contemporary resonances.
