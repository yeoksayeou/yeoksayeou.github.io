**Collection of Southern Homeland Poems**

**Poems from an Old Garden**
Yun Kŏn'gang

*Zelkova Tree*
Long, long, very lo-ong ago
They say it was here since the land was first parceled out
The zelkova tree, its insides ho-llow like a cave
The zelkova tree, where even an old, deaf serpent is said to live

The virtuous people living in the village
Felt anxious even pulling a single blade of grass
Feared even breaking a single tree branch <248>
Everything that lived kindly in the fields and mountains
They cherished and tended with one heart and mind, they say

If they met a viper, they ripped the viper apart
If they met a wildcat, they struck and caught the wildcat
If they met a raccoon dog, they beat it down
They blocked the troubles of the fields and mountains, they say

On misty mornings, they did not go far
On rainy or snowy days, they huddled at home
On nights with thunder and lightning, they knelt and prayed
Huddling together, chatting, chatting, they lived, they say

*Hometown*
Coming to the old house overgrown with giant bamboo <249>
Turning off the light and lying down in the musty, stale small room
The sound of distant village dogs drifting over the ridge
Like ill rumors of the heart left behind
The heart twists the wick of impatience
And beloved faces light up like lamps in the eyes

**Diary of Withering Mountain (Pyŏnsan)**
Sin Sŏkchŏng

1. Chunggye
The waters of Pyŏnsan's twelve valleys gather here
And here again flow out to the Yellow Sea (黃海), this Chunggye (中溪)

Crossing a stepping-stone bridge at the mouth of Chunggye <250>
There is a tavern selling moonshine (密酒).

Drinking liquor that makes bubbles pop
And lips stick together tightly,

Eating pheasant meat caught today and savory sorghum rice,
We slept using wooden pillows black and grimy with age.

As we left, we whispered like an old tale
That we should come back to this region famous for its persimmons this autumn too.

2. Saji mokchae (Lion Pass - 獅子峙)
The snow that had piled up deep enough to sink your shanks melts and flows down,
Water covers all the stepping-stone bridges and rushes away.
At Saji mokchae, where they said a wild boar was caught and carried over yesterday, <251>
Here and there, clots of blood staining the white snow looked frightening.

This is the pass just beyond which lies the auspicious site called 'Lion Gazing at Heaven' (獅子仰天).
Atop the pass, an old water seller was dozing off intermittently.

3. Nŭnggabong (楞伽峯)
Nŭnggabong, visible from Silsangsa Temple (實相寺),
They say that long, long ago, a pottery boat (사기ㅅ배) passed by and was wrecked,
So even now there are pottery shards, and it has the name 'Pottery Peak' (사기峯).

White clouds drift across like flocks of sheep
And at night, small blue stars pass over
Even atop Ch'ŏnwangbong (天王峯), around that time, the tide must have flowed in
And among those rocks, strands of seaweed must have hung lushly,
When I think of this, it is so ve-ery distant that I suddenly felt lonely. <252>

4. Kamaso (Cauldron Swamp - 釜沼)
On the pass leading over to 'Kamaso', there is a small cave with a small stone door,
Where people supposedly took refuge long ago.

Crossing over, it's the mountain valley called 'Puktan'gol' (北堂골).
There are only three households (家戶).

An old man who came here seeking refuge from Naju (羅州) some twenty years ago,
'Scholar Yi' (李生員), whose face is reddish as if blood is welling up, also lives in Puktan'gol.

The story that bamboo leaves as large as a grown person float down
Must have originated from 'Kamaso', where mountain bamboo (山대) is abundant.

5. Ch'ŏngnim (靑林)
The mountain wind is truly fragrant like *saeyangdae* (? fresh shoots/herbs), <253>
We crossed the high 'Saejae' (Bird Pass) in one breath.

Stepping carefully like a squirrel, choosing fields where water flows,
The place we turn into following the waterway is Ch'ŏngnim (靑林).

In Ch'ŏngnim, a teacher from the mountain school,
At the request of a newspaper reporter living in Seoul,

Asked me earnestly to copy and send
The poems of 'Maech'ang' (梅窓).

**Rock**
Yu Ch'ihwan

When I die, I shall become a single rock <254>
Not tinged with pity or affection (愛憐) at all
Not moved by joy or anger (喜怒)
Just as the wind erodes
Just as the rain washes
In the billion-year heartless silence (緘黙)
Whipping myself inwardly, inwardly

Finally forgetting even life (生命)
Distant clouds
Faint distant thunder (遠雷)
Dreaming but not singing
Shedding unseen, dripping (淋漓한) blood
Even if shattered into two pieces
I shall become a rock that makes no sound <255>

**Lotus Poem**
O Changhwan

The grain ripens. Insects cry in the grassy patches. At such times, the lotus leaf falls. Without attaching a single cool, lonely petal to the stem, the lotus leaf falls.
Throughout the year, in the black ditch whose murky water is never clear, the turtle was watching.
The ice sheets breaking in spring, the tadpoles splashing about seeking summer. Everything settled (沈澱). Everything just sank down.
When the lotus leaf withers, when the lotus leaf withers, upon the quiet water surface, another year's fragrance permeates,

Duck feathers slowly settling in the water,
Into this, a guest comes. Without any expression (表情), without any sign (氣脈), the night dew descends and becomes frost.
On the evening path returning with the cow, on the ridges of the evening path's rice paddies, farmers scatter, flappingly playing funeral music (풍장치며?).
The five grains (五穀) were ripe. The grain was ripe. <256>

In the pool (웅뎅이), a layer of fallen leaves piled up on the water, each night autumn insects cried in the grassy patches, and on the day the fallen leaves all sank again, the tiny fish (魚族) made a fence of lotus roots in the soft mud and fell into a long, long winter sleep.
Though the lotus leaf, which once held glistening dew on its broad surface, now covers the sleeping loach and its head, on the back of the turtle, submerged in deep thought with eyes closed, above the turtle's sky, thin ice forms and gradually thickens.
Even in the dark night sky that presses down on the black head, not a single twinkling star reflects, not one star, and on the dimly white ice sheet (어름ㅅ장), only red-tinted persimmon leaves are stuck.
Though a whole winter piles snowdrifts upon the frozen pool, the turtle sunk in darkness, stretching its neck, drinking dirty water, like a prison window (獄窓) in Russia where night continues for half a year, oh, in tasteless weeping, oh-tasteless weeping, pushes aside the soft mud pit of regret (悔恨) and eats the loach and its head.
Even if utter darkness flows on and on outside the thick ice sheet, even if the crescent moon (上弦ㅅ달) rises in the clouds, above the sky the turtle bears, only the dimly white ice sheet lies, only a layer of sleet is piled. <257>

**Dandelion Flower**
Sŏ Chŏngju

Hey fool, the white dandelion has bloomed.
Beneath the sky of Yongch'ŏn (龍泉) spring that wets your eyebrows
Heehee, fool, heehee, it's funny (우숩다).
Everyone, like the Namsadang troupe (男寺黨派),
Inside their bosom (고이안에서?) where blood stains the belt (허리띄),
Breathes breaths that would be big trouble if discovered.
After falling down somewhere in a barley field
And eyes, nose, and lovesick dreams (相思夢) all disappear,
Like soju (燒酒), like soju,
I too will fly up and scatter (푸를리라) into the air. <258>

**Summary**

This collection features five prominent Korean poets writing in 1941, focusing primarily on themes of nature, rural landscapes, memory, and introspection. Yun Kŏn'gang evokes traditional village life and nostalgia for a lost hometown. Sin Sŏkchŏng provides a travelogue through the Pyŏnsan region, capturing specific locations and encounters with local life. Yu Ch'ihwan's poem uses the metaphor of a rock to express a desire for stoic endurance and transcendence over emotion. O Changhwan employs the life cycle of the lotus and a stagnant pool to explore themes of time, decay, endurance, and submerged life. Sŏ Chŏngju's poem presents a stark, almost nihilistic vision juxtaposing the simple beauty of a dandelion with hidden anxieties and a desire for oblivion. From a historical perspective, the poems are interesting for their near-total avoidance of overt political commentary during a period of intense Japanese colonial control and wartime mobilization. The focus on nature, tradition, and inner states could be interpreted as a form of passive resistance, an escape, or simply a reflection of the constrained artistic environment, offering historians insight into the subtle ways Korean identity and sentiment were expressed under duress.
