Victory of Unity
						Kim Tong-hwan

1
The Singanhoe, too, divided into two factions, then three, and finally dissolved completely. Even now, within Ch'ŏndogyo, Buddhism, and Christianity, there are new and old factions fighting, and there isn't a school, newspaper company, or any organization that isn't divided into two, three, five, or ten factions fighting amongst themselves. Because of this, we crush our own strength amongst ourselves, striving eagerly to devour one another. It is a situation worthy of tears. Where else could internal strife (內爭) be this severe?

When the former German Emperor, Kaiser, declared the Great War against the entire world on August 1, 1914, he personally stood at the front lines and cried out in blood to the generals and soldiers defending the German fatherland:
"For me, there are no Prussian Royalists nor Socialists. There is only the fatherland, Germany."
The Kaiser is gone, but these words remain a famous saying for the ages (2 lines omitted - original text)

Truly, it is time for us to awaken. What corner do we have to rely on that we do not cease this fight amongst kin (骨肉싸홈)? All sympathizers of our people (吾族) are leaving us day by day. What remains are only those around us who seek to exploit us. From this day forward, let us not believe that the strength of outside help (他助) will be available to us. From this day forward, let us not believe that a favorable opportunity will come to us on its own. There is nothing for us to believe in. Know that all that remains are these beautiful mountains and rivers, and our brethren in sheep-like white clothes. Truly, from this day forward, even if we count our assets, we can only count these mountains and rivers and these brethren; even if we count our honor, we can only count these mountains and rivers and brethren; even if we count our strength, we can only count these mountains and rivers and brethren. (One line omitted - original text)

Thinking about it, our society's financial resources (財源) are now dried up, the culture of our people (吾族) is not yet fully awakened, and the opportunity we awaited has not yet arrived. Nevertheless, we absolutely, absolutely do not lament. We do not weep in despair. Why? Because we believe that one day, surely, the dried-up financial resources will be replenished, the darkened culture will brighten, and the distant opportunity will arrive right before our eyes. To achieve this, we believe it will come at the very moment when we awaken in our spirit, rise up, abandon all infighting among kin (骨肉相爭), discard the evil custom of calling each other "P'yŏngan-do bastards" or "Hamgyŏng-do bastards," stop discriminating between Kim and Yi, cover up ambition (野心) and vain desire (虛慾), and all together dedicate this body and this sincerity to the great task.

Oh! Where has all that ardent love for our people (愛族心) gone? End the internal strife (內爭)! Abandon the factional strife (派爭)! On the day we do this, the day we unite and consolidate (一致結束), all dishonor will be recovered, and all violence can be repelled. Compatriots! Mountains and Rivers! Do you hear? Do you hear my words? <1>

**Summary**

The author, Kim Tong-hwan, passionately laments the pervasive factionalism and internal strife within Korean society in 1933, citing the dissolution of the Singanhoe and divisions within religious groups, schools, and other organizations. He urges Koreans to abandon regionalism, personal ambition, and infighting ("internal strife," "factional strife," "fight amongst kin") and unite with fervent patriotism ("love for our people"). Referencing the German Kaiser's call for national unity during WWI, Kim argues that Koreans can only rely on themselves – their people and their land – and that only through complete unity (結束) can they overcome their current hardships, restore honor, and eventually achieve their goals. For historians, this article is significant as it vividly portrays the deep-seated problem of factionalism that plagued the Korean nationalist movement during the colonial period, hindering efforts towards independence. It reflects the frustration with internal divisions and provides a strong, emotional appeal for unity as the sole path forward, highlighting the reliance on self-strengthening (자강 - 自强) ideals common at the time.

新幹會도 두 패 세 패로 나누이더니 끗끗내 마서지고 말엇다. 지금도 天道敎나 佛敎 基督敎에 新舊派가 잇서 싸우고 학교나 신문사나 엇던 단체에나 두패 세패 다섯패 열패로 나누어 싸우지 안는 곳이 업다. 이래서 우리들은 저이끼리 제 힘을 뭇지르고 서로 못먹어 애쓴다. 눈물 날 노릇이다. 이러케도 內爭이 심할 변이 어대잇느냐.
前獨逸皇帝 카이자-는 1914년 8월 1일 전세계를 敵으로 大戰을 선포할 때에 스사로 陳頭에 나서서 케루만 護國의 將卒에게
「나에게는 普魯西勤王黨도 社會黨도 업다. 오직 祖國 獨逸이 잇슬 뿐.」
이라고 피로써 절규하엿다. 카이자-는 갓스나 이 말은 千古의 명언으로(2行略-원문)

실로 우리들은 깰 때이다. 우리에게 무슨 밋을 구석이 잇다고 骨肉싸홈을 끗치지 안코 잇느냐. 吾族의 모든 同情者는 날로 날로 떠나가고 잇다. 남엇다면 우리를 이용하려드는 周圍가 잇슬 뿐이리라. 금일 이후 우리에겐 他助의 힘이 잇스리라고 밋지 말 것이다. 금일 이후 우리에겐 스사로 조흔 기회가 와질 것이라고 밋지 말 것이다. 우리에겐 밋을 것이라고 아모 것도 업다. 오직 남어 잇다면 그는 수려한 이 산하와 羊가튼 힌 옷의 동포가 잇는 줄 알나. 실로 금일 이후 우리는 재산을 헤재도 이 산하와 이 형제를 들 뿐이요. 명예를 헤재도 이 산하와 형제를 들 뿐이요. 힘을 헤재도 이 산하와 형제를 들 뿐임을.(一行略-원문)

생각컨대 우리 사회의 재원은 지금 말넛고 吾族의 문화는 지금 채 깨지 못하엿고 기다리든 기회도 닥처오지 안엇다. 그러컨만 우리는 결코 결코 탄식을 하지 안노라. 절망에 울지 안노라. 엇재서? 그는 우리는 어느 날은 반드시 말넛든 財源도 채어지고 어둡든 문화도 밝어지고 멀엇든 기회도 눈 압헤 닥처올 것을 밋는 까닭이니. 그리자면 오직 우리들이 우리 정신에 깨어 이러나 일체의 骨肉相爭을 버리고 平安道 놈 咸鏡道 놈 하든 악습을 버리고 金哥 李哥를 가리지 말고 야심과 허욕을 덥퍼버리고서 모다 모다 큰 일에 이 몸과 이 정성을 바치자 할 그 순간에 와 질 것을 밋는 까닭이다.
오! 열열한 愛族心을 다 어대에 두엇든가. 內爭을 끗처라. 派爭을 버리라. 이리하는 날 一致結束하는 날 모든 불명예는 회복되고 모든 폭력은 물리칠 수 잇스리라. 동포여 山河여 듯는가. 듯는가. 나의 말을.＜1＞
