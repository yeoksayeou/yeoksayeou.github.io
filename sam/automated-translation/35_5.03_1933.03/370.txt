Article Title: 三月의 詩壇 (Poetry Scene of March)
Author: Sin Sŏkchŏng (辛夕汀), Sin Pulch'ul (申不出), Pak Kŭm (朴錦), Mo Yunsuk (毛允淑), Elisabeth Lincoln Otis (translated by Kim Chahye (金慈惠))
Article Type: 시 (Poetry)

Spring— Can You Guard My Bed?

Sin Sŏkchŏng

How did you know
That I was calling you near my bed?
And can you, without fail,
Move your careful footsteps beside my bed?
Oh, Spring—.

I know better than anyone that you brought this cheerful green bed,
And also know that, to make me sleep soundly, even before crossing that mountain,
Under the deep shade of that distant, far-off forest, preparing a peaceful night,
You will gently send it to me over the misty lake.
Oh, Spring—.

Even the small mountain birds enjoying the sunlight and the young wild doves,
After sending them all back to their simple beds,
And after putting to sleep the waves washing the banks of the distant river's undulations—
After dropping the blue stars, one by one, two by two, into that quiet river,
And after veiling even the moon that brightens your night,
Will you, sitting on some familiar hill, just quietly watch
The green dreams of these things blooming silently from the earth...?

Oh, Spring! Just as you give me a green bed, soft and cheerful like a nursing infant's cheek,
And majestically offer me a peaceful night,
Before my greatest task of daily life, "Sleep," falls peacefully without dreams, <93>
Can you promise to bring me a magnificent, wonderful morning,
And until my "Sleep" is awakened,
Can you guard my bed?
Forever...
Oh, Spring—.

Nonsense (lit. Dog's Work)

Sin Pulch'ul

Devoting their bodies to lofty principles, their sacred deeds
Clearly laid bare in the middle of the blue sky,
I wish to show them to those thoughtless ones of today.

Ignoring the start and thinking only of the result,
Though the words may be right, how can one stop the setting sun?
Think about it, person: things differ according to the times.

We have ruined ourselves by mouth until today;
When the matter is obvious, what's with all the chatter?
Not lifting a hand for the work to be done, only engaging in nonsense.

The world is rough, and my body is worn out.
When numerous circumstances seize and shake me, why couldn't I smash them all with my fist?

Idle Thoughts on a Train

Tying up the things to be forgotten if we go, one strand each,
Weaving a dream of a meaningless world, it is endless.
Tying my laces anew, I gaze at the distant sky.

Entering nature (江湖) again, the atmosphere is different from before;
The world grows younger, and people grow older.
Using contempt as a side dish for drink, I will return intoxicated.
—On the train from Tokyo to Osaka—

Recollection of Thoughts

Pak Kŭm

Alas, world! Do not point at me (勿指我), do not glare at me (勿目我).
Fish leap in the abyss (魚躍干淵), the abyss itself permits it (淵自許).
Alas, world! Do not push me forward (勿進我), do not push me back (勿退我).
Kites fly reaching the heavens (鳶飛戾天), heaven does not oppress them (天不壓).

Don't point fingers. Don't glare either.
Does the water find bothersome the fish leaping as they please?
Don't tell me to come. Don't tell me to go either.
Does the vast sky dislike the bird flying on its own?

Reminiscence

Mo Yunsuk

On the day the golden twilight descended silently upon the earth,
White chrysanthemums rustled, releasing their fragrance,
When cotton-like clouds swam fluttering lightly <94>
You sang the song of autumn beside me.

On the day the yellow autumn leaves engulfed my clothes,
The wind-chilled sky held a cold sorrow,
When the ash-colored crescent moon shed tears,
You warmly touched my cold breast.

At dawn, listening to the sound of the late autumn rain,
The sound of electric wires on the street flowed like cicadas,
When I saw the figure who had awakened me slightly from sleep and left,
You became a phantasm of joy before the door.

If a Woman

By Elisabeth Lincoln Otis, Translated by Kim Chahye

If you adorn yourself enough to be popular,
But are not captivated by vulgar makeup,
If you swim and play healthily and vigorously,
But do not lose your gentle demeanor,
If you trust and love friends,
But do not drift into romance,
And are kind even to the weak, the lonely, the old—

If you excel in learning and skills,
But are not arrogant,
If you wrap yourself in fine silks (綾羅) and adorn yourself with jewels (寶玉),
But do not regard cotton (무명) and hemp clothes (벼옷) meanly,
If you know how to use a saw or a hammer (마치),
And can do even a man's work—

If you can cook deliciously,
Sew neatly,
And arrange the house tidily (아달스럽게)—
If you are adept at socializing,
And give pleasure to many people,
Everyone will cherish you.
Even if unavoidably—

If, struck by love's arrow on that distant day,
You build a home with conviction and peace,
And if your spirit as a clever wife and wise mother
Can achieve the plan developed through the centuries,

Oh! My daughter.
You will become a saintly model (聖人다운 모범),
And the whole world will bow before your feet. <95>

**Summary**

This collection features five poems from the March 1933 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine. Sin Sŏkchŏng's lyrical poem addresses Spring personified, asking if it can provide solace and guard his sleep. Sin Pulch'ul offers three sijo stanzas reflecting critically on contemporary society's inaction and empty talk compared to past sacrifices, concluding with personal frustration. Another set of sijo, "Idle Thoughts on a Train," captures fleeting reflections on memory, the passage of time, and alienation during a journey from Tokyo to Osaka. Pak Kŭm's "Recollection of Thoughts" uses classical Chinese couplets (translated into Korean) and vernacular lines to assert individual freedom against societal judgment. Mo Yunsuk's "Reminiscence" evokes poignant memories of a past love through autumnal imagery. Finally, a translation by Kim Chahye of Elisabeth Lincoln Otis's "If a Woman" outlines ideals for modern womanhood, blending traditional domestic skills with education, physical activity, social grace, and inner strength, culminating in the role of wife and mother as a "saintly model." The mix of styles—lyrical modernism, traditional sijo reflecting on social issues and personal feelings, classical allusions, and a translated didactic poem on female ideals—reveals the diverse literary landscape and cultural concerns (modernity, tradition, gender roles, societal critique) present in colonial Korea during the 1930s. Historians might find Sin Pulch'ul's critical sijo and the translated poem on womanhood particularly reflective of contemporary social commentary and evolving gender expectations under Japanese rule.

봄이여― 당신은 나의 寢臺를 지킬 수가 잇슴니까?

辛夕汀

당신은 어찌하야 내가 나의 침대 가까히
당신을 불으는 줄을 알으십니까?
그리고 당신은 어김업시 나의 침대엽헤
그 조심스런 발자욱을 옴길 수가 잇겟슴니까?
봄이여―.

나는 당신이 이 명랑한 녹색침대를 가저 온 줄을 누구보다도 잘 알고
또 나로 하여곰 고요한 잠을 재우기 위하야해도 채 저 산을 도라가기 전에
저-아득한 머언 숩의 지튼 그늘 밋헤서 평화한 밤을 준비하야
안개 자욱한 호수 우르로 감안히 나의게 보낼 것을 알고 잇슴니다.
봄이여―.

해ㅅ볏을 즐기는 저 작은 山ㅅ새들과 어린 들비들기들 까지도
그들의 소박한 침대로 다 돌려보내고
머-ㄴ 강의 출넝―기슭을 씻는 물ㅅ결도 잠을 재운 뒤
푸른 별들을 하나 식 두울 식 그 고요한 江ㅅ속으로 흘려 보내고
당신은 당신의 밤을 밝키는 저 달의게까지 面紗를 씨운 뒤
대지에서 소리업시 피여나는 이것들의 푸른 꿈을 글세 감안히 바래보시렴니까?
그 어느 나지익한 언덕에 안저서... .

봄이여! 당신은 젓멕이의 볼처럼 부드럽고 명랑한 녹색침대를 나의게 주고
장엄하게도 평화한 밤을 나의게 제공하드시
내 생활의 일과 중에서 가장 큰「잠」이 꿈도 업시 평온하게 들기전에 ＜93＞
굉장하게도 훌륭한 아츰을 나의게 가저올 것을 약속하고
나의 「잠」이 깨워질 때까지
당신은 나의 침대를 지킬 수가 잇슴니까?
언제 까지나...
봄이여―.

만일 녀자가

엘리사벳·린컨·옷스 作, 金慈惠 譯

만일 네가 인기 끌 만치 몸을 꿈이되
천한 화장에 사로잡히지 안고
건강하고 활긔잇게 헤염치고 노릿되
점잔은 몸매를 일치 안는다면
만일 친구들을 밋고 사랑하되
로만쓰로 흘느지 안으며
弱한이 외론이 늙은 이에게도
친절하다면―

만일 네가 학식과 기능에 뛰여나되
교만스럽지 안으며
綾羅로 몸을 감고 寶玉으로 장식하되
무명과 벼옷을 천히 역이지 안으며
톱이나 마치도 쓸 줄 알어
남자의 일이라도 할 수 잇다면―

만일 네가 맛잇게 요리하며
얌전하게 바누질 하고
아달스럽게 집을 꿈일 수 잇다면―
만일 네가 사교에 능난하고
뭇사람에게 유쾌를 준다면
모든 사람은 너를 귀여워하리라.
엇절 수 업시라도

만일 네가 아득한 그 날 사랑의 활살을 맛고
신념과 평화로 가정을 일우어
령리한 안해 어진 어머니로의 네 정신이
世紀를 통하여 발전된 게획을
일울 수 잇다면

오! 내 딸아.
너는 성인다운 모범이 되며
온 세상이 네 발압헤 절하리라. ＜95＞
＜93-95＞
