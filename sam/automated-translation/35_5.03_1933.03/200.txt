**Body Text Translation:**
A Visit to America's Liberty Bell Pavilion
						Pak In-dŏk

Liberty Bell! Liberty Bell! How pleasant a name to hear, how precious a name to call? "Proclaim LIBERTY Throughout All the Land unto All the Inhabitants Thereof." This was the phrase engraved on the precious entity, the Liberty Bell.

This Liberty Bell is said to have been ordered from Great Britain in 1753 by a government office within the United States of America. Its weight is 2,080 pounds, height 2 chi 3 ch'on, circumference 12 chi, thickness 3 ch'on, the bell clapper was 3 chi 2 ch'on, and its price was said to be over 600 wŏn.

When I went to see this bell, it was late autumn, when the crimson maples of the season had lost their color; it was three years ago from now.

About an hour by express train from New York brings you to the city of Philadelphia. Having already known of the existence of the Liberty Bell in Philadelphia, I had tried very hard to visit the city but couldn't get the chance, until I finally had the opportunity to speak with the state university students there, leading me to go to the city I had long yearned for.

After finishing my talk with the students, I asked them to show me the famous sights of the city, and the very first place they guided me to was an extremely old building called Independence Hall (獨立舘).

Although the building was indeed extremely old, the precious and beautiful Liberty Bell sitting inside must have imparted an indescribable, heavy impression upon any sightseer.

Of course, over 100 years ago, the Liberty Bell must have been hung in a tall bell tower, but the Liberty Bell I saw seemed to have lost its freedom, its large body forlornly placed inside the old building. <46>

The phrase engraved on its surface—"Proclaim LIBERTY Throughout All the Land unto All the Inhabitants Thereof."—was still clearly written even then, but it was a half-mute thing that could no longer make a sound!

A long crack ran vertically down it.

However, this bell had fulfilled its duty, completed its mission. It seemed as though it did not grieve that it had become half-mute, that its large body had broken and cracked.

It was said that until some time ago, it had been preserved in a glass case, but because everyone wanted to touch its history-laden large body, it was taken out of the glass case.

I too had thought I would definitely touch it before seeing it, but somehow, when I got close, I hesitated to touch it. Perhaps it was because its body seemed too large and great! My heart felt simply empty, and also tightly constricted.

It is said that when it was first ordered from Great Britain, it cracked when struck with the clapper for the first time, so it was recast in America. After being made, for 82 years, whether in times of sorrow or joy, or when enemies invaded, regardless of the occasion, it rang clearly only in special circumstances. And on the day the United States of America declared independence and announced the Declaration of Liberty, this bell also rang intensely.

Standing before the Liberty Bell, I could picture the scene from over 100 years ago: when the great bell rang resoundingly, spreading high into the clear sky! Crowds formed processions, marching and celebrating wildly in the main streets.

Even after the United States gained independence, the Liberty Bell continued to ring for twenty-three years, until it cracked from the strike of the clapper when tolling for the death of America's Chief Justice, Mr. Marshall (마스氏).

Thus, because the Liberty Bell, along with Marshall, became forever unable to make a sound, it was said to have been moved from the bell tower where it hung to Independence Hall.

At first glance, the Liberty Bell looked like a very pitiful Liberty Bell that had lost its liberty. However, it had not lost its liberty; rather, like the phrase written on its body, it had proclaimed liberty to the whole world and all humankind.

As the late autumn sunbeams slanted to the west, I left the presence of this bell.

On the first morning of the New Year, welcoming 1930 after the end of 1929, hearing the bell sounds ringing out for 15 minutes from New York City, I once again recalled this Liberty Bell and closed my eyes. <47>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Pak In-dŏk describes her visit to see the Liberty Bell in Philadelphia, likely in late 1929. She reflects on its history, the inscription promoting liberty, its significance during the American Revolution, and its eventual cracking. Pak expresses profound emotion standing before the bell, seeing it not just as a broken object but as a symbol that fulfilled its mission of proclaiming liberty, a sentiment that resonates deeply as she recalls it upon hearing New Year's bells in New York. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects a Korean intellectual's engagement with symbols of freedom and independence from the West during a period when Korea itself lacked sovereignty under Japanese rule. Pak's focus on "liberty" (自由 - Chayu) and the bell's message to "all humankind" implicitly contrasts with Korea's situation, making her reverence for the bell and its history particularly poignant and potentially suggestive of Korean aspirations for their own independence.

美國自由鐘閣 訪問記
						朴仁德
自由鐘! 自由鐘! 얼마나 듯기에 반가운 일홈이며 부르기에 귀한 일홈인가? 『왼 世上 全人類에게 자유를 廣表하여라.』이것은 귀여운 존재인 自由鐘에 색여잇는 文句섯엿다.
이 自由鐘은 아메리카合衆國內에 잇는 府廳에서 1753년에 英國에다가 주문해 왓든 것이라는데 중량이 2,080파운드요 놉히가 2尺3寸 周圍가 12尺이며 둡게 3寸 종 두다리는 방맹이 3尺2寸이엿고 그 가격은 600餘圓이엿다고 한다.
내가 이 종을 보러 갓슬 때는 느진 가을이여서 한 때의 붉은 단퐁도 제빗츨 일엇슬제 지금으로부터 3년 전이엿섯다.
뉴욕에서 特急車로 한시간즘 걸니면 빌나델비아城에 이른다. 빌나델비아城에 잇는 自由鐘의 존재를 임이 알고 그 성을 한번 차즈려고 퍽으나 애를 썻스나 긔회를 엇지 못하다가 마츰 그 성에 잇는 주립대학생들과 이약이할 기회를 엇게되자 오랫동안 동경햇든 그 성으로 가게 되엿든 것이다.
학생들과 이약이를 맛친 뒤에 그 성의 名勝을 보혀달나고 원햇드니 제일 처음으로 인도하는 데가 獨立舘이라는 몹시 낡은 건물에로엿다.
집은 비록 몹시 낡앗섯다고 하나 그 안에 들어안즌 귀하고 아름다운 自由鐘만은 엇던 探勝客에게나 말 못할 무거운 감상을 주엇을 것이다.
지금으로부터 100여 년 전에는 물론 自由鐘은 놉다란 종각에 매여달엿을 것이엿으나 내가 갓섯을 때의 自由鐘은 자유를 일허버린 것 갓치 낡은 집안에 쓸쓸히 그 큰 몸 덩어리를 듸려 놋코 잇섯다. ＜46＞
표면에 색인 文句-「왼 세상 전 인류에게 자유를 광포하여라.」는 것만은 그때까지도 똑똑히 씨여잇엇지만 소리만은 낼 줄 모르는 반벙어리!
길이로 가르로 길게 짜르게 금이 낫섯다.
그러나 이 종은 자기의 임무를 다-햇으며 사명을 다-햇든 것이다. 그제야 반벙어리가 되든 자기의 큰 몸 덩어리가 부서저서 가르가 되든 그는 애닯이 하지 안는 듯 십헛다.
얼마 전까지는 유리장에 保藏되여잇섯다고 하나 누구나 歷史가 만흔 그 큰 몸 덩어리를 만저 보고저하는 까닭에 유리장에서 끄집어냇다고 한다.
나도 그를 보기 전에 꼭 만저 보리라는 생각을 가젓으나 엇전지 겻헤가서는 만저보기에 주저되엿다. 그 몸 덩어리가 너무나 크고 위대해 보혓든 까닭인지는 몰나도! 내 가슴은 거저 탕 비인 것 갓기도 하고 또 꼭 맥힌 것 갓기도 햇섯다.
英國에서 주문해 왓슬 때 처음 방맹이로 때렷드니 깻토려젓기에 米國에서 다시 맨드럿다고 한다. 만드러서 82년 동안 슯흔 일이나 깃븐 일이나 또 원수가 드러올 때나 엇던 때를 물론하고 특별한 경우에만 잘잘 울엇다. 그리고 아메리카合衆國이 독립해서 자유의 선언을 발표하든 날도 이 종이 몹시 울엿다.
自由鐘 압헤 섯슬때에 100여년 전 큰 종이 울엉차게 울여서 맑은 하늘에 놉히 퍼질 때! 군중은 행렬을 지어 대로에 행진하며 날뛰든 광경이 눈 압헤 보혓다.
自由鐘은 合衆國이 독립한 뒤에도 스믈 세해나 게속해 울드니 아메리카의 재판장 마스氏가 죽어서 나갈제 치는 방맹이에 깨여지고 말엇다.
그래서 自由鐘은 마스과 함끠 永永 소리를 내지못하게 되엿든 까닭에 달엿든 종각에서 獨立舘으로 옴겨진 것이라고 한다.
自由鐘은 얼핏보기에 몹시도 가런한 자유를 일흔 自由鐘갓치 뵈엿다. 그러나 그는 자유를 일흔 것이 안이고 그 몸 덩이에 씨인 文句와 갓치 온 세상 全인류에게 자유를 광포햇든 것이다.
느진 가을 햇발이 서쪽에 기우러질 때 나는 이 종 압흘 떠낫섯다.
1929년 마즈막 가고 1930년을 마저드리는 새해의 첫 아츰에 나는 뉴-욕市에서 15분간 울려나오는 종소리에 또 한번 이 自由鐘을 연상하면서 눈을 감엇다. ＜47＞
