That Woman and 13
						Sŏ Kwang-je

The snow that had fallen silently during the night had thickly dressed the entire earth in white clothes.
The morning sun reflecting off the snow, like ultraviolet rays (紫外線), was bitterly cold. The snow, still untouched by human feet and piled softly, was tempting like a maiden's breast.
Only at the sound of the rooster crowing in praise of the morning and the creaking sound of the water carrier's jigae entering did K finally blink his eyes at the thread-like sunlight sneaking through the window crack.

It was already 10 o'clock when K finished his breakfast, tucked his briefcase under his arm, and left for work at the X Magazine Company. K had been in a very bad mood since morning. This was because the calendar hanging behind the wall showed it was the 13th when he stood before the mirror tying his necktie. Moreover, while shaving, he had nicked himself in two places with the sharp blade, and when putting on his shoes, a lace broke, making his mood even worse.
On his way into the magazine office, K began organizing manuscripts.
K was, in every way, a modern boy. He had a slender build with long legs, a modern, healthily elongated face, and his hair was stylishly parted six-to-four and slicked down. His attire consisted of a tuxedo-style suit, a double-breasted overcoat with a broad collar, a white silk scarf, a Knox fedora hat, and Broadway-style shoes – he was a modern boy fit not only for the pavement of Sŏul but also for strolling the pavements of nearby Tokyo or Shanghai.
His aversion to the number 13 had started, unknowingly, through infection from American movies.
However, he was not one to believe in superstitions, nor was he an idealist (唯心論).
Yet, without realizing it, influenced by Americans' dislike of the number thirteen, which he often saw in American movies and from which he learned ultra-modernism, K gradually developed a kind of aversion towards the number 13.
This was because when he went with friends to visit someone on the 13th or someone at address number 13, they seldom met the friend they intended to see.
And having playfully disliked 13, he now genuinely felt unpleasant when encountering the number 13.
However, K always tried hard to laugh off this useless thought of his. <96>

A little after 12 o'clock, a bundle of mail arrived. Among them were letters from unknown women.
The content of the letters wasn't anything special. They merely expressed a liking for literature and asked for guidance on that path, and separately, there was one manuscript.
K read all the letters and then looked at the manuscript sent by the woman.
The manuscript was a short story of about 20 pages, and for a woman, the conception and technique were quite cleverly written.
Having been at newspaper or magazine companies for many years, he had received countless such letters and manuscripts, but this was the first time he had received such writing, especially from a woman. K felt a sort of curiosity and read the manuscript once more. Then, he sent it off for censorship along with the manuscripts collected yesterday.

As K left the magazine office, he thought to himself.
Receiving a letter and manuscript from an unknown woman on the 13th, and moreover sending the manuscript off for censorship, might it perhaps get censored...
But K quickly dismissed his foolish thought and boarded a streetcar. Finding the date '13' stamped on the transfer ticket he received from the conductor unpleasant, he decided to walk from Chongno to Anguk-tong and simply crumpled up the transfer ticket and threw it away.
That night, when K went to see a show at the X Theater, the movie "Lucky 13" (幸運의 13) was playing.
The movie's theme was that whether gambling, betting on horse races, playing billiards, participating in sports matches, pursuing romance, getting a job, getting married, whatever it might be, encountering the number 13 brought good fortune.
Thus, people deliberately tried to ride car number 13 among hundreds passing by, sought out room number 13 when booking hotels, leading hotels to intentionally create many room 13s, and caused chaos trying to get telephone number 13 – it was a movie aimed at dispelling the traditional American aversion to the number 13.
K felt refreshed, as if his chest would burst. And he secretly vowed to himself not to have such a peculiar disposition and not to get upset over trivial things.
After the theater let out, he went to play billiards.
On any other 13th, K, who usually wouldn't even go out, let alone play billiards, would have stayed home looking at newspapers or books and then simply collapsed into bed.
Having gone to play billiards in a good mood, no matter how loudly the game boy shouted "Jūsanten game!" (13-point game), K merely smiled faintly and hit the balls well. And indeed, the balls went as he wished.
Thus, even after returning home, he resumed writing the manuscript he had put down the previous evening after reaching page 12, reluctant to write the number 13.
K stayed up late that night until he finished the manuscript, and only then did he sleep.

Between K and the woman, letters were exchanged many times. This came about as K requested manuscripts from her, and also as she frequently sent manuscripts to K and made various inquiries about novel writing techniques or the literary scene, making K and the woman quite close through correspondence.
However, their relationship could not remain satisfied with just that.
Moreover, K began writing letters with a different meaning after realizing that the woman was someone he had met three years prior when he went to Kyŏngsŏng N Girls' School (N녀학교) to teach acting practice for the students during their 5th-anniversary celebration.
When the woman first wrote to K, she had sent the manuscript <97> under a pseudonym, so he didn't know. But as their letters became more frequent, she revealed her real name to K.
The woman had known the man named K well from the start, and furthermore, having been directly taught acting practice by K when she attended N Girls' School, she had since then always read K's writings first whenever they appeared in newspapers or magazines.
K also had felt favorably towards the intelligent, intellectual-looking woman ever since he first met her at N Girls' School.
However, their acquaintance, lasting only about 20 days, meant they knew little more about each other than their names and faces.
Even afterwards, when they occasionally met on the street, due to her maidenly shyness, they would only slightly blush and exchange faint greetings.
After she finished H School, spent a year in Japan, and returned to her hometown, Ch'ŏngjin (淸津), they could no longer meet on the streets at all.
However, when K occasionally met her former classmates on the streets of Sŏul, thoughts of her would suddenly arise. This was because among the dozen or so girls he taught acting to at that time, only she had instilled a certain fantasy in K's mind.

Between K and the woman, more than letters about literature, K's sentimental letters, common among artists, went to her, and romantic letters came from her. Amidst this exchange, K felt a deep longing for the northern province where she resided.

Lucky Seven... This referred to K's age this year, twenty-seven... this lucky seven...
Baseball's lucky seven signified a fortunate marriage for K. <98>
This year, even if he couldn't have a formal wedding, he wanted at least to get engaged, not wanting to let this lucky seven just pass by. Turning one year older would make him twenty-eight... When K thought about this, he knew his youth was gradually approaching its climax.
When they felt melancholic, or when facing the bright night sky, or when their hearts inexplicably yearned to embrace something... previously, they would have wanted to write and send poems or songs arising from their hearts to some unknown destination... how much more so in their current situation, which must be the poetry of passionate youth.
Seeing young couples walking by, talking intimately, K felt as if he had forgotten something and immediately poured out his heart to the woman.
And right then and there, he wanted to run to the station and go see the woman in the northern province.
In fact, K's recent circumstances had made him extremely gloomy. His family life, once cold as ice, had completely dissolved, and rolling around alone in a lodging house room... especially when trying to collapse into sleep late at night in an empty room after drinking with friends, he couldn't help but feel the emptiness intellectuals often feel.
He even felt nostalgic for his former wife, who had stubbornly refused to grant a divorce, thinking of her pitifully, and wondered if life had to be such a struggle just for daily bread. At such times, K couldn't help but laugh hollowly like a madman. What's the point of reading books, what's the point of writing...?
Even amidst this, K struggled greatly to turn around his depressive life situation.
K only told S-kun, who worked at the same magazine company, and finally boarded the northbound train bound for Hoeryŏng. Since he hadn't told anyone else, there was not a single person to see him off.
On the train, lost in various thoughts, just as he was about to fall asleep, the conductor came to check tickets. As he handed over the ticket, he glanced indifferently at the ticket number. K frowned and quickly took back the ticket the conductor returned.
The reason was that the ticket number was 1,013.
Having corresponded eagerly for three months and finally setting off today to meet the longed-for woman, encountering the number 13 he disliked again made him feel a slight disappointment, wondering if misfortune might befall him on the long journey.
As the train sped north, he lay down and got up so many times that eventually his back ached, and he paced up and down inside the train car.
This long journey of 23 hours was the first such lengthy trip within Korea for him, apart from train rides when he studied in Tokyo several years ago.
After passing Wŏnsan, Hamhŭng, and Sinbukch'ŏng, the conductor came in again to check tickets. K handed the ticket to the conductor while looking out the window. And without looking at the ticket again, he put it back in his pocket.
When the train passed Sŏngjin and reached Kyŏngsŏng (鏡城), K went into the washroom, washed his face, and adjusted his suit.
The train arrived at Ch'ŏngjin station without being a minute late. K, calming his strangely trembling heart, picked up his bag and got off the train.
He looked around in all directions, but the woman hadn't come out. He had definitely sent a telegram when departing from Sŏul, so her absence meant either the telegram got lost somewhere along the way, or perhaps she was waiting at the station exit. Thinking this, he ambled slowly towards the exit.
Remembering that the ticket number was 1,013, his mood inexplicably soured slightly.
However, K was not disappointed at all. Because just a few days ago, passionate letters like burning fire had been exchanged between him and the woman, so even if she hadn't received the telegram, <99> he believed that if he called the X Company where she worked early tomorrow morning, she would surely be delighted and rush out to meet him.
Just as he was about to step out of the platform, a young man approached K's side and asked if he wasn't Mr. K.
K, inwardly thinking, "Instead of meeting the woman I wanted to see, I'm meeting a detective first," replied curtly, "That's right." At this, the man took off his hat, laughed very cheerfully, extended his hand to K, introduced himself as the woman's older cousin (사촌 오빠), and said that actually, his younger cousin (누이동생) had received the telegram herself and intended to come out, but unfortunately, it was the accounting period, so she was working late and couldn't make it. He told K not to feel disappointed at all, adding that he had known all about the relationship between K and his cousin for a long time, and moreover, he had known K through his writings for a while.
Thus, K went with him to a lodging house not far from the station.
The train had arrived at Ch'ŏngjin station at 8:30 PM. However, the cousin said they still had to wait an hour and a half for the woman to get off work from the company. And so, K, weary from the train ride, asked him where a bathhouse might be and stepped out into the streets of the northern port city at night.
The smell of fish strongly assailed the nose of K, who had grown up only in the city.
The cousin told K to hurry back from the bath, saying he would stay at the lodging house as his cousin might arrive soon. Hurriedly taking a bath at the bathhouse right in front of the lodging house, K reached the entrance of the lodging house. A pair of woman's shoes were placed there, and the faint sound of the cousin talking with someone could be heard. (To be continued in the next issue - original text) <100>

**Summary**

The story follows K, a Sŏul-based writer and self-proclaimed "modern boy," who harbours a superstition against the number 13, likely picked up from American films. His aversion is tested when various events involving the number occur on the 13th, including receiving an impressive manuscript from an unknown woman. He discovers this woman is someone he vaguely knew years ago and develops a romantic correspondence with her. Feeling lonely and seeking connection, K decides to travel north to Ch'ŏngjin to meet her, a journey marked by his unlucky number appearing on his train ticket. He arrives to find she couldn't meet him due to work, but her cousin greets him, hinting at the impending meeting. The story ends cliffhanger-style as K returns to his lodging to hear the cousin talking with a woman.

From a historical perspective, the story offers glimpses into Korean urban life in the 1930s under Japanese rule. It highlights the influence of Western (specifically American) culture ("modern boy," "Broadway shoes," "Knox fedora," "American movies," "Lucky Seven," billiards, ultra-modernism) on young intellectuals. The narrative also portrays modern forms of courtship through correspondence, the challenges of travel within Korea (a 23-hour train journey from Sŏul to Ch'ŏngjin), and the social interactions of the educated class, including work in publishing and companies, mentioning censorship and interactions with potential detectives (형사).

그 女子와 13
						徐光霽
소리 업시 나리든 눈은 밤 동안에 온 대지의 흰옷을 둡겁게 입혀주엇다.
자외선(紫線外)과 갓치 눈 우으로 반사되는 아참의 태양은 몹씨도 쌀쌀하엿다. 아즉도 사람의 발에 발피지안코 폭은히 싸힌 눈은 처녀의 유방도 갓치 유혹뎍이엿다.
아참을 찬미하는 닭의 우름 소래와 삐걱거리며 드러오는 물장수의 물지게 소래에 그제야 K는 창틈으로 숨여드러오는 실마리 갓흔 태양에 눈을 부시엿다.

K가 아참을 먹고 가방을 끼고 X잡지사에 출근하기도 시게가 10시를 가라친 때이엿다. K는 오늘 아참부터 기분이 매우 좃치 못하엿다. 그가 거울 압헤서서 넥타이 맬 때에 벽 뒤 걸늰 캐펜더-가 십삼일(13日)을 가라친 까닭이엿다. 더구나 면도를 하다가 날카로운 칼에 두 군대나 포를 떳스며, 구두를 신을 때 끈이 끈어저서 그의 기분을 더욱 불쾌하하여 주엇다.
K는 잡지사에 드러가는 길노 원고를 정리하기 시작하엿다.
K는 어듸로 보든지 모던뽀이 이엿다. 키가 늘신한데다가 다리가 길죽하고 얼골은 현대뎍으로 건강미잇게 길죽하며, 머리는 륙분(六分) 사분(四分)으로 멋잇게 갈넛 붓첫다. 양복은 택키시트에 외투로 따불 비로도에리에 하얀 씰크 목도리에 모자는 넉쓰 증절모, 구두는 뿌로드웨이 식으로 서울의 페이보멘트 뿐 안이라 각가운 동경이나 상해의 페아보멘트를 거릴드라도 현대의 모던뽀이이엿다.
그가 13이라는 숫자를 시려하기 시작한 것도 미국영화에서 아지 못하는 사히에 감염되엿든 것이다.
그러나 그는 미신을 밋거나 또는 유심론(唯心論)자는 안이엿다.
그러면서도 아지 못할 사히에 미국인이 십삼이라는 수ㅅ자를 시려하는 것이 그가 미국영화를 자조 보며 또는 울트라·모던을 그 속에서 배우는 K는 무의식뎍으로 13이라는 수ㅅ자를 대하다가 점점 그에서 일종의 악감을 밧게 되엿다.
그것은 동모들과 갓치 혹은 13일날 누구를 차저보러 가든지 또는 13번지의 누구를 차저 보러 갈 때에 차저가 보려는 동모를 맛날적이 퍽 적엇든 것이다.
그리고 작난삼어 13이라는 것을 실케 역이다가 지금은 뎡말 13이란 글, 를 불유쾌하게 대하게 되엿다.
그러나 K는 언제이나 자기의 이 쓸데업는 생각을 코우숨 처버리게 애를썻다. ＜96＞
12시가 조곰 지나서 편지가 한뭉치 왓다. 그 중에 아지 못하는 녀자로붓터 온 편지도 석기엿다.
편지의 내용은 별 것이 안이엿다. 그저 문예를 조와하니 그 길노 잘 인도하여 달나고 하엿고, 따로히 원고 한아가 잇섯다.
K는 편지를 다 읽고 그 녀자가 보낸 원고를 일거 보앗다.
그 원고는 20매 가량되는 단편소설노 녀자로써는 구상이라든지 기교가 퍽 재치잇게 써 잇섯다.
심문사나 잡지사에 여러 해 잇섯든 만큼 이러한 편지와 원고는 수업시 바덧스나, 더구나 녀자로부터 그만한 글을 밧기는 처음이엿다. K는 일종의 호기심을 갓고 다시 한번 그 원고를 읽엇다. 그리고 어제 모아두엇든 원고와 함게 검열을 드러보냇다.

K가 잡지사의 문을 나스면서 그는 속으로 이와갓흔 생각을 하엿다.
13일날 아지 못하는 녀자로부터 편지와 원고를 밧고 더구나 원고 검열을 드러보냇스니 혹시나 삭제나 되지 안 올가...
그러나 K는 곳 자기의 어리석은 생각을 차버리고 뎐차를 집어 탓다. 차장에게서 밧은 승환표의 13이란 날자 박은 것이 보기 실혀 종로에서 안국동까지 거러갈냥으로 하고 그냥 그 승환표를 비비질저 버렷다.
그날 밤 K가 X극장에 구경을 갓슬 때에는 행운(幸運)의 13이란 영화가 봉절되엿섯다.
그 영화의 내용은 도박을 하든지, 경마투표를 하든지, 삘리야드를 하든지, 운동시합을 하든지, 연애, 취직, 결혼 무엇이고 할 것 업시 13이란 수ㅅ자를 대하면 행운이 온다는 것이다.
