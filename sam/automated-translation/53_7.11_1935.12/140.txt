Publication Date: 1935년 12월 01일
Article Title: 玉통소 (Jade T'ongso)
Author: 파인
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

玉통소
						파인

Jade T'ongso
						P'ain

Who plays the jade t'ongso?
On this foreign road (他關客路), the day grows late.
Though the foreign road grows late,
Listening, resting, listening, I go.

The thousand-ri road to Hanyang,
Though the fine rain delays, the blue mule knows not how to go?
The mountain moon rises; shall I rest at the tavern?

The stone monument at Sŏjangdae (西將台) stands right there.
The tears I didn't shed when leaving my hometown,
To this tavern, this t'ongso, I cry the long night away.
(This piece was published in a certain magazine (某誌), but due to 3 or 4 typos (誤字) making the meaning unclear (文意不通), it is republished here.) <104>
<104-104>

**Summary**

This poem by P'ain (Kim Tong-hwan) expresses the melancholy and homesickness of a traveler journeying towards Hanyang (Seoul). The sound of a jade t'ongso (transverse flute) heard at a tavern late at night evokes memories of home and brings forth tears the traveler had suppressed upon departure. The poem captures a sense of loneliness and weariness on a long journey, possibly reflecting the broader atmosphere of displacement or longing during the colonial period. For historians, the poem offers a glimpse into personal sentiments expressed through traditional poetic themes during a politically charged era, using imagery like the "foreign road" (他關客路) and the specific landmark Sŏjangdae, potentially adding layers of meaning related to national identity or loss. The author's note about republication due to typos also hints at the practicalities of publishing during that time.

옥통소 누가 부노
他關客路 느저간다.
타관객로 느저가도
드르며 쉬며 드르며 가지

한양길 천리길
곱비는 느처도 靑노샌 갈 줄 모르데
山달이 오르니 주막서 쉬어갈가

西將台 돌비석이 바로 보섯지
내고향 떠날 적에 울지 안튼 우름을
이 주막 저 퉁소에 긴긴밤 울며 새이네
