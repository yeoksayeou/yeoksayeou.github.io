**Body Text Translation:**

Dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi, Going to London and Paris
A Letter to My Sister Sŭnghŭi

Ch'oe Sŭngil

Sŭnghŭi! It is night now. A moonlit night. A moonlit night in early winter. The paper on the door frame weeps at the rustling sound of the winter night's wind brushing against the thatched roof eaves.
When winter comes, I think of that time back then. You might wonder what <77> time and what event I mean by "that time," but it was when you were fifteen, in the fourth year of Sukmyŏng Girls' High School. A cold night on a winter day – that day, I received a small manuscript fee from Mr. Cha Ch'ŏngo of Kaebyŏk Publishing Company (開闢社), had a few drinks at a tavern with Hoewŏl-gun, and on my way up, I bought two *mal* of Taiwanese rice and two *doe* of red beans. At a general store in Chŏksŏn-dong, I bought a pair of black socks, and then at a confectionery across from Chŏksŏn Bathhouse, I bought twenty *jŏn* worth of *mochi* rice cakes. By the time I got home around ten o'clock, the house was all quiet. As I entered, I called out,
“Sŭnghŭi-ya,” “Sŭnghŭi-ya.” Then you replied, “Yes,” and got up, seemingly turning up the oil lamp. Ah – back then, we couldn't even have electricity and used an oil lamp. Mother came out onto the dark wooden floor, took the rice, poured it into the rice jar, and we four siblings gathered around the oil lamp.
“Yŏnghŭi-ya (my older sister), you wear these socks. And Sŭnghŭi! You eat these *mochi* cakes.”
I think I fulfilled the wishes of my two younger sisters that night. Why? Because a few days before, I had seen Yŏnghŭi sighing, unable to even patch the hole in her socks, and I remembered you smiling brightly, saying, “Ah, I wish I could have some *mochi* cakes.”
“Oppa, you've been drinking again. The days Oppa drinks are good days for me.”
“Why?”
“Because you buy me things.”
Everyone laughed.
But after a little while, as you were eating the *mochi* cakes, tears seemed to well up in your eyes, and you said,
“Oppa, starting next month, the school is exempting me from tuition. So I cried for a long time in the school's backyard.”
“Why cry? Isn't it great not paying tuition? You must be an honor student. Who told you?”
“Oh, Oppa. Teacher Sŏng (Teacher Sŏng Ŭigyŏng) said so – but the tears just kept falling.”
“You cried well. Those are the tears of gradually learning about the world.”
“But Oppa, what should I do after I graduate next year?”
“What do you think you’d like to do?”
“Music school.”
“Right. You sing well and are good at rhythmic gymnastics. Just wait. I have a plan.”
“What plan?”
“Sŭnghŭi-ya! Listen for a moment. I wonder if you're old enough to understand my words yet. But today, for the first time, <78> I'll tell you. I currently earn about forty or fifty *wŏn* some months writing novels and translating stories. But I realize I absolutely cannot support our parents and my own wife and children with that, so I think before long, I'll have to do some serious, heavy labor. If that happens, my efforts towards art will diminish, and I'll have to squeeze out different energy for the sake of livelihood. And thus, I'll completely become a salaried household drudge. And then, your older brother is suited to be a housewife in someone else's home, so I won't even touch that. And your younger oppa is already attending middle school and has a wife and child. On top of that, our family has no money. So, the day you graduate, you'll be in a situation where you have to do even meager piecework. Then, the only one left is you. Just wait. People have chances. Let's wait for next spring.”
And so, the following spring, you ended up going with Mr. Ishii Baku (石井漠).

Three years passed – one day of some month, I remember receiving a letter like this from you.
“– Excerpt – Oppa! Lately, I am coming to understand what dance art (舞踊藝術) is and what an artist's conscience (良心) is. It is discovered in things like this: Why is there such a difference in spirit (精神) and inspiration (感興) between the works choreographed (按舞) by Teacher Ishii when he first returned from Germany with accompaniment by Mr. Yamada Kōsaku (山田耕作), and the works he creates nowadays? I am gradually becoming disillusioned (幻滅) with Teacher Ishii. Lately, there is no poetry (詩) in his art. It’s not unreasonable. He has to feed dozens of mouths by dancing. He has no house, so he must build one. But I can no longer bear it. My heart lately burns like a pine knot fire. I want to see the art where the traditional dance (傳統的 舞踊) originating from Russia's Imperial Ballet School (帝室舞踊學校) is shattered, and the power of the masses (民衆) and the poetry (詩) of labor (勞働) are transformed into dance (舞踊化). And I want to see Mary Wigman's dance in Germany – dance that kicks away the subordination (從屬化) to music like Isadora Duncan's or Nijinsky's, dance that possesses its own life, independent of music (舞踊獨自). Oppa! Is this a bad thought? Is it too early for me? Please write back – End excerpt –”
And so, not even a few months later, you returned home, and at the age of eighteen, you tried to arrange passage to Russia through Mr. Kim Un (金薀) who was at the Russian Consulate <79>, intending to go there. But things didn't go as planned, so you boldly put up a sign for a Research Institute (硏究所) on the hill in Koji-machi (古市町) and tried choreographing (按舞) – do you remember? In the deep night, in the quiet room, you choreographed Kreisler's 'Indian Lament' in front of me, shedding tears – the passion (情熱) you had for going to Russia, we placed it upon the melody of 'Indian Lament' that night.

A few days ago, I saw the news of your trip abroad (洋行說) published in the newspaper. And that night, for some reason, I couldn't fall asleep until late. I think it was because I couldn't contain my gladness and joy, reaching a state of tension that was almost excitement (心境). And the other day, I received a letter like this from you.
“Oppa – Lately, I am traveling around in good health, but for some reason, I sometimes feel fear (恐怖). It concerns my health (健康). You might have seen it in the newspapers or magazines, but people like Mr. Sugiyama (杉山平助) or Mr. Yamamoto (山本) from Kaizōsha (改造社) tell me I work three times (三倍) as much as an ordinary person. In fact, I am that busy. I worry, what if I collapse on stage like this? But wherever I go throughout Japan, there's welcome and encouragement, and whenever that happens, the thought ‘I am Korean’ comes to mind, and on one hand, I feel a tearful joy, and accordingly, no matter what happens, I want to show them until the very end, until I collapse, that I possess a great passion (情熱) like a bomb (爆彈).”
Two days later, you sent me another letter like this –
“Oppa – Oppa, my respected Oppa – Please rejoice. My letter <80> arrives later than the newspaper, but next spring, I will be making the long-hoped-for circumnavigation (一週) from the East side of the Earth (地球) to the East side. My only sponsor is the Railway Ministry's Tourist Bureau (鐵道省觀光局). Of course, there are no material sponsors. Holding just one bag in my hand, carrying the rhythm (リ듬) of the East (東洋) in my body, I plan to go around the Earth once. This is Kure (吳) city. Seeing the large warships (軍艦) floating on the sea, one can understand the greatness of human power (力). And the muzzles (砲口) of the cannons (大砲) mounted on the decks (艦上) are thrusting their arms vigorously towards the blue sky (蒼空). Warships fight for the country. But I am crossing over to the West to fight with the rhythm of Korea (조선의 리듬) – broadly speaking, the rhythm of the East. Ah – I am joyful. My courage is multiplied a hundredfold (勇氣百倍). But one thing I doubt is whether I myself truly possess the breath of Korea – the rhythm of Korea. That is the question. Since I am Korean, I think I must possess the soul (魂) of Korea – the rhythm of Korea, but –
Oppa – I think. In any situation, the nation (民族) does not perish, and the art (藝術) of that nation absolutely does not perish (亡) either. Egypt (埃及) perished (亡), but its people and its people's art did not perish; Judea (猶太) perished, but its people did not perish. – Excerpt ends.”
Right, that's it. I agree (首肯) with your words. I found what you said the other day in the Osaka Asahi Shimbun, in a conversation (會談中) between the Tokyo poet (詩人) Mr. Noguchi Yonejirō (野口未次郞) and the Chinese novelist Mr. Lu Xun (魯迅).
“– Excerpt – Even if China perishes, the state may perish, but the people will never perish eternally.”
Thus he spoke. That's right. You must deeply recognize (再認識) that Joseon has now put forth one person of the Joseon nation, Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi, onto the world stage (世界舞臺).
And you seem to wonder to what extent you possess the rhythm of Joseon, but I think this way. Sometime back, you and I watched Mr. Ishii Baku's dance titled 'Caricature,' performed in Korean clothes, and found it extremely unpleasant. So we immediately consulted with Mr. Yi Kise (李基世) and choreographed to the Kaya sanjo chinyang chungmori, titling it 'Our Caricature.' Wasn't that the first time you danced to a 'Joseon rhythm'? Public opinion at the time was favorable, but I rejoiced inwardly then, thinking, 'You are a daughter of Joseon.' Why? Because until that time, you had never even seen <81> a Joseon dance. Despite that, the dance movements that emerged, though the tempo (탐포) was somewhat fast and some body movements stemmed from Western basic practice (基本練習), I actually thought that was good. Why? Because Kabuki (歌舞技) today is different in form from the Kabuki of three hundred years ago. That's because it has followed historical evolution (進化). Therefore, no matter how much we talk about the Joseon rhythm, I believe there must be an unknowable change between the Joseon rhythm of five hundred years ago and the Joseon rhythm of today, and I think there should be.
However, there is one thing you must be careful about. As your fame spreads throughout the world these days, the self-importance and pride that artists easily acquire can sometimes lead to very dangerous derailments (脫線) for an artist, so you must be wary of that; moreover, it's easy to fall into. How many such examples have we seen in the past? I believe you are a bright child and surely won't be like that – And another worrisome thing is the rumor (데마) circulating lately that 'Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi sells out Joseon.' This is a most critical issue if it is one. Why does such a rumor arise? I think it started somehow after word got out that performing Joseon dance in Tokyo received favorable reviews. However, I believe it is the duty and a great task of an artist to properly inherit and understand their national heritage (民族的 遺産) and artisticize (藝術化) it. And by doing so, it becomes national art (民族藝術) and simultaneously 'international art.'
Writing up to here, the night has already passed, and it's dawn. It's now two in the morning. I'm scribbling this letter tucked between sleeping children, but where are you now, in which inn, having cold dreams? I suppose today you might be around Tokushima (德島) city in Shikoku (四國). Ah – when I think of you, I feel truly desolate (寂寞). You might say, 'Why desolate, all of a sudden, Oppa –' but this desolation is indescribable. You probably don't know my state of mind (心境) right now. Why do I feel this way towards you, who are now vigorously advancing (進出) onto the world stage? Perhaps because it is this quiet, lonely winter dawn, my heart feels sorrowful beyond my own understanding. Is that all? Sometime ago, after reading my letter, you sent a reply containing the line, 'Oppa is becoming too secularized (世俗化).' That one phrase truly <82> saddened me. But I endure. And I rejoice. This 'secularization' of your brother is something I resolved myself to when you and I put up the sign for the Dance Research Institute in Koji-machi – during those two or three years I spent with you then. So, in return, where will this brother find replenishment for bright (明朗), refreshing (爽快), and joyful feelings?
It's in a passage from the letter you sent from Tokyo when you were eighteen:
'Lately, there is no poetry (詩) in the dances Teacher Ishii creates. He thinks about money.'
Such frank words, and another phrase from the letter you sent me recently:
'Carrying the rhythm of Joseon – broadly speaking, the rhythm of the East – slinging a knapsack, I intend to walk from this end of the Earth to the other.'
This one phrase – I feel endless happiness in having such an admirable younger sister.
This has become too long. I'll write again. My constant worry is also your health. And wherever you go, please be prudent (自重) and humble (謙遜). And you must not forget that you are a 'daughter of Joseon.'
Finally, when you return to Joseon after circling the globe, 'What will you bring back, and how?' – That is the great expectation (期待) of this older brother. – (End) – <83>

A Message to My Brethren in the Homeland

Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi, Embarking on a World Tour

Having to speak about one's own art is, for an unfinished (未完成) dancer like myself, a form <83> of suffering, certainly not a happy occasion.
However, the reason I venture to speak about my dance is not to impose upon various viewers (鑑賞者) an understanding of some originality in my works, but rather, I would be fortunate if it could merely serve as material for just criticism for these viewers.

Although I include Joseon dance in my repertoire alongside Western-style (洋風) dance, I intend to refrain somewhat from speaking about my Western-style dance.
I merely intend to strive for the acquisition of all those points often criticized about my dance's shortcomings – the lack of delicate sentiment (情緖) or artistic chiaroscuro (暗影), or deficiencies in fine technique (纖細한 데크닉크).
To think that possessing a constitution naturally larger than others is the inevitable result that made delicate sentiment lacking is one of the greatest sorrows for a dancer.
In reality, my dance may be nothing original (獨創的) at all, perhaps merely a formal imitation of Western (西歐) dance.
If it is said that I uncritically absorbed Kreutzberg's dance and that Sakharoff-esque elements are simply arranged (配列) and expressed, then for a young dancer like myself, this would signify a great distance from the self-reliance (自立性) of such dance.
Doesn't enriching one's dance mean acquiring an expansion of expressiveness, depth, breadth, and greatness in one's own dance, rather than arranging various other dances?
Being creative or self-reliant doesn't just mean pursuing珍奇 (rare/curious) things that have existed since long ago; I believe it lies in adapting one's dance to the life one belongs to and establishing 'self'-formation based on the possibilities of one's own body.

Next, the reason I include Joseon-style dance in my repertoire is, ultimately, to attach Joseon dance, even if dragged along limply, to the world stage, letting it follow along even clinging to the corners of its cloak, thereby introducing it widely abroad.
However, this task will likely not be as easy as I think. <84>
It seems Joseon possessed its own splendid dance long ago, but in later generations, the descendants of this land knew how to grope in the dark (暗中摸索) for Su Dongpo (蘇東坡), Du Fu (杜甫), or Li Taibai (李太白) amidst old memories, yet turned only eyes of contempt towards the superior artistic heritage of their own kin that was close at hand.
Because of this, today, Joseon dance barely maintains its near-death (瀕死) state, circulated only by *kisaeng* (긔생) at drinking parties (酒席) and the like; besides this, there is virtually no existence that can be called dance, nor any literature about it left to be found.
In the dance scene of this land, which hasn't produced a single La Argentina or a single Kackgoro [likely refers to Sakharoff - Sacharoff/Sakharov was known in Japan then] – nor any dancer worth mentioning – the revival of our ancient art will surely be far more difficult and complicated than imagined.

My Joseon-style dance amounts to no more than taking the extremely few remaining elements in Joseon as material (素材) or perhaps creating something new, and trying to digest it on stage.
And regarding my stylization of this kind of national (民族) dance, some people might not acknowledge any originality or creativity in it. My method of stylization might be a formal imitation of the La Argentina-esque method in Spanish dance or the Uday Shankar-esque method in Indian dance. However, the methods of these two seniors (先輩) are, for me, the most valuable international experience.
Although my Joseon-style dance has undoubtedly not yet escaped the realm of incompletion, my aim lies in digging out and searching for the Eastern color (色) and fragrance (香氣) within my dance.
Therein also lies the true meaning of my art, and I know it is the path I must dedicate my life to and walk along.
The many varied criticisms regarding my Eastern dance probably stem from the difference (相違) in interpretation as to whether my dance is truly creative or merely a simple arrangement of things that have existed since long ago.

Finally, I believe it is possible to shorten the distance, however slightly, between modern dance and stylized national dance. <85>
As I am now holding recitals in various places like Tokyo and Kansai (關西) with this purpose, I offer these few words beforehand.

Furthermore, receiving overwhelmingly enthusiastic support and expectations not only from Tokyo society but broadly from the populace of all Japan, I have gained the opportunity to step onto the 'world stage' that I have long idealized and hoped for. My heart leaps boundlessly, and I await that day amidst great excitement and anticipation.
This opportunity is also the most precious and crucial period in my dance training (修道).
On this journey, I will step onto the world stage, see correctly, and learn correctly all the dances of various countries around the world. Also, within that, I will discover the creativity of my dance and clarify its self-reliance.
Accordingly, on this occasion, I will show the world the stylization of Joseon dance as folk (民俗) dance to the people of the world.
And I believe I will show the people of the world our Eastern color and fragrance.
Already, the muscles of my entire body are tensing.

A Message to the World-Class Dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi

Yamamoto Sanéhiko, President of Kaizōsha

It's not that I've known you well for a very long time. Since first meeting you through Mr. Yi Pyŏng's [likely 李丙, needs confirmation if possible, but rendering as Yi Pyŏng] introduction sometime ago, strangely, through my "recommendation," you entered Shinkō Kinema, and after that, I had frequent opportunities to meet you. Everyone says you are a beautiful woman (麗人) born of the Peninsula (半島). <86> Of course, I believe there's no doubt about that. With a height (身長) of 5 *ch'ŏk* 4 *ch'on* 5 *pun* [approx. 165 cm] and a splendid physique, you possess, in other words, the build of a heroine ("女丈夫"). Indeed, you have an ideal physique. Even if not the wife of Genghis Khan, no one would raise an objection (異議) to the praise that spiritual energy flickers in your eyes and radiance flows from your face. You are truly the epitome of the modern. However, please rest assured beforehand that I have not even a tiny bit (寸毫) of intention to flatter your beauty (美) or bow my head before your physique (肉體). But when I think about the unspeakable agony (苦腦) and endurance (忍耐) piled up that brought you to where you are today, I just feel my mind growing dark. Caught in the rough gap between peoples, you must have more than once heard backbiting like, "That thing raised on red pepper powder!" To think how heart-wrenching it must be to pierce through such circumstances and walk steadfastly, I truly feel a warmth beyond human understanding in my heart. I possess no connoisseurship (鑑識) regarding dance art. Therefore, I know I am not qualified to comment on whether your dance has sentiment or technique. However, the art born from your character gives me great peace of mind, as I trust your character, and thus your future holds great promise. It is a fact that you have overcome numerous temptations (誘惑) until today. There hasn't been even a fingernail's worth of the unsavory rumors common in such circles about you. This alone shows how strong your willpower must have been. And after marrying your husband fair and square, you stood up, brandishing a decisive blade against all circulating rumors. Just for this, I think many actresses (女優) should prostrate themselves at your feet. Isn't the current state of modern actresses to receive support from many people behind the scenes, getting help with costumes (衣裳) and personal affairs (身邊)? On top of that, another heavy pressure (重壓) was placed upon your head – as mentioned before, it was the rough and cold gaze wearing the colored glasses of ethnicity. From now on, you must powerfully push aside such obstacles and walk forward confidently. The road ahead will indeed hold many <87> heart-wrenching things. Also, you must constantly bear in mind the fickle sentiments of outsiders who initially welcome you with enthusiasm, and steadfastly fight against the world. Truly, the path forward from now on must be a blood-soaked battle. It will be a physical battle. I trust your physical strength (肉體力). However, you must pay even more attention to the mental strength needed to endure it competently. A person lacking sufficient mental strength cannot become a truly vital artist. And you must know that one cannot overcome a hundred hardships and trials. Whether you succeed or end in failure will likely be different from the case of an ordinary dancer. I only hope you always remember that many Koreans stand behind you. Also, I sincerely hope that you will strive to find good partners and advisors (相談役) in art from now on. And I hope you will live in a fresh atmosphere, or ignite firmly in your heart the resolve to overcome new struggles. It has already been quite a long time since I saw your 'Monk's Dance' (僧의 舞). I believe, conjecturing, that there will be many leaps in your art in various aspects. Also, the thought of seeing your dance before long is a very joyful thing. Out of an old man's excessive concern, I have rambled on with these few words, but I sincerely hope that you will further cultivate the beauty of the body you possess and your strong mental power of endurance, becoming a master (名人) whom others cannot follow. Even now, you have the reputation of being number one in Japan (日本一), but I sincerely hope that your brilliant future, soon advancing onto the 'world stage,' will shine even brighter.<88>

**Summary**

The article compiles three perspectives on the renowned Korean dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi as she prepares for a world tour in 1935. Her brother, Ch'oe Sŭngil, writes a nostalgic and encouraging letter, recounting her early passion and struggles, and urging her to remember her identity as a "daughter of Joseon" while representing Korean rhythm internationally. Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi herself writes about her artistic philosophy, acknowledging Western influences but emphasizing her commitment to reviving and stylizing traditional Korean dance for a global audience, despite the difficulties and the near-extinction of its forms. Finally, Yamamoto Sanéhiko, president of Kaizōsha publishing, praises her beauty, talent, and resilience in overcoming ethnic prejudice and societal pressures in Japan, urging her to maintain her mental fortitude for the challenges ahead on the world stage. Historically, the article is fascinating for its direct engagement with issues of cultural identity, artistic creation, and national representation under colonial rule. Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi's determination to showcase "Joseon's rhythm" and Yamamoto's somewhat patronizing yet supportive acknowledgment of the "ethnic prejudice" she faced highlight the complex dynamics of cultural production and identity politics for Koreans within the Japanese empire.

『승희야! 너- 좀 드러볼내. 네가 아즉 내 말을 알아드를 수가 잇을 나고. 그러나 오늘 내 처음으로 너한테<78> 말하여보지 나는 지금 소설을 쓰고 이야기를 번역하야 어느 달에는 한 사오십원의 수입도 잇다. 그러나 그것을 가지고는 도저히 부모를 뫼시고 처자를 거느리지를 못하리라고 생각이 되니 나는 머지안어 엉청 뛴 노동을 하지 아니하면 아니 되리라고 생각이 된다. 만일 그러케 된다면 내가 예술에 대한 노력은 적어지고 생활을 위하야 다른 「에네르기」을 짜내여야 될 것이다. 그리하야 완전히 월급쟁이 살님군이 되고야 말 것이다. 그러고 그다음 네의 형은 사상이나 됨됨이가 남의 집 주부감이니 나는 손도 대이지 안켓다. 그러고 네의 자근 옵바는 발서 중학교에 단기는 몸으로 처자가 잇다. 그런데다가 우리집안에 돈이 업다. 그러니 졸업을 하면 그날부터 다만 멧푼짜리 품파리나마 하여야만 될 형편이다. 그러면 다만 남어지는 너 한 사람이다. 감안히 잇거라. 사람이란 『찬스가 잇는 것이다. 래년 봄을 기대리자』

삼년이 지낸 - 어느 달 어느 날 나는 네의게서 이런 편지를 바든 것을 기억한다.
『- 전약 - 옵바! 저는 요사히 무용예술(舞踊藝術)이란 엇더한 것이라는 것과 예술가의 양심(良心)이라는 것을 깨다라감니다. 그것은 이런데서 발견이 됨니다. 석정선생이 처음 독일에서 도라와 산전경작(山田耕作)씨의 반주로 안무(按舞)된 작품과 요사히 맨드는 작품의 차이가 웨 그다지도 그 정신(精神) 그 감흥(感興)이 달음니까? 저는 차차 석정 선생의게 환멸(幻滅)을 늣기여 감니다. 요사히 그의 예술에는 시(詩)가 업서요. 그것도 무리는 아님니다. 그는 춤을 추어서 수십명 구가 먹고 살어야 함니다. 집이 업스니 집을 지어야 함니다. 그러나 저는 인제는 더- 참을 수가 업슴니다. 제의 마음은 요사히 마티 관솔불과 가티 탐니다. 로시아의 제실무용학교(帝室舞踊學校)에서 울어난 전통적 무용(傳統的 舞踊)을 부수어 바리고 민중(民衆)의 힘과 노동(勞働)의 시(詩)가 무용화(舞踊化)된 예술을 보고 십습니다. 그러고 독일의 욱크맨의 무용 - 이사도라 당캉이나 이진스키의 음악에 종속화(從屬化)된 무용을 박차버리고 무용 독자(舞踊獨自)의 생명을 가진 음악이 업는 그의 무용을 보고 십허요. 옵바! 이것이 납뿐 생각일까요. 제게는 너무도 일늘가요? 편지하여 주서요 - 후약-』
이리하야 멧달이 못되여 너는 집으로 도라와 가지고 열여덜살된 몸으로 로서아를 가랴고 로서아령사관에<79> 잇든 김온(金薀)군을 통하야 로서아행을 운동을 하엿섯지. 그러나 그것이 뜻과 갓지 아니하야 고시정(古市町) 언덕에다 연구소(硏究所) 문패를 붓치고 대담하게 안무(按舞)를 하여 보앗것다 - 너 생각나니? 깁흔 밤 고요한 방에 너는 내 압헤서 크라이슬러의 『인듸안·라멘트』을 눈물을 흘리여 가면서 안무하든 생각을 - 로서아로 가랴든 정열(情熱)을 우리는 그 날 그 밤에 『인듸안·라멘트』 메로듸 우에다 언젓것다.

멧칠 전에 나는 신문에 발표된 네의 양행설(洋行說)을 보앗다. 그러고 나는 그날 밤 왼일인지 밤이 깁도록 잠을 이루지 못하엿다. 그것은 아마 너무도 반갑고 깁뿜을 닉이지 못하여 거의 흥분까지 된 긴장된 심경(心境)에 이르기 때문이 아닌가하고 생각한다. 그러고 일전에 나는 너의게서 이러한 편지를 바닷다.
『옵바 - 저는 요사히 몸 성히 잘 다니기는 함니다마는 왼일인지 때로는 공포(恐怖)를 늣김니다. 그것은 제의 건강(健康)임니다. 옵바는 신문이나 잡지에 난 것을 보섯겟지만 스기야마(杉山平助)씨나 개조(改造)사에 야마모도(川本)씨 갓흔 분은 저더러 보통사람의 일하는 것 삼배(三倍)는 한다고 함니다. 사실 저는 그만큼 밧붐니다. 이러다가 저는 혹시 무대우에서 걱구러지면 엇지하나하고 걱정이 됨니다. 그러나 일본전국에 가는 곳마다 환영이고 격려이니 그럴때마다 저는 『내가 조선사람이라』는 것이 생각될 때 한편으로 눈물겨웁게 깁뿜을 늣기기도 하고 따라서 엇더한 일이 잇든지 나는 폭탄(爆彈)과 가튼 위대한 정렬을 가젓다는 것을 그들에게 끗까지 내가 걱구러질때 까지 보여주고 십슴니다』
이틀이 지난 후 너는 또다시 나의게 이런 편지를 주엇지 -
『옵바 - 옵바 나의 존경하는 옵바 - 깁버하여 주십시요. 신문보다는 제 편지가<80> 늣게 감니다마는 저는 명년 봄에 바라고 바라든 지구(地球)의 동편에서 동편으로 일주(一週)를 하게 되엿음니다. 후원이라고는 철도성 관광국(鐵道省觀光局)뿐임니다. 물론 물질적 후원자는 업슴니다. 저는 가방 한아를 손에다 들고 동양(東洋)의 「리듬」을 몸에다 지니고서 지구를 한박퀴 돌 작정이올시다. 여기는 구레(吳)시임니다. 바다에  큰 군함(軍艦)을 볼 때에 사람의 「힘」의 큼을 알 수가 잇읍니다. 그러고 함상(艦上)에 걸리여 잇는 대포(大砲)의 포구(砲口)는 창공(蒼空)을 향하야 기운차 팔둑질하고 잇읍니다. 군함은 나라를 위하야 싸홈니다. 그러나 나는 조선의 리듬』 - 크게 말하면 동양의 리듬』을 가지고 서양으로 싸흠을 건너감니다. 아 - 나는 깁뿜니다. 용기백배(勇氣百倍)임니다. 그러나 한 가지 의심되는 것은 저는 제 자신이 확실히 조선의 호흡 - 조선의 『리듬』을 가지고 잇는지 그것이 의문임니다. 저도 제가 조선 사람인 바에야 조선의 혼(魂) - 조선의 『리듬』은 가젓으리라고 생각함니다마는 -
옵바 - 저는 생각해요. 엇던 경우라도 민족(民族)은 망하지 아니하고 그 민족의 예술(藝術)도 결단코 망(亡)하지를 아는다고요. 애급(埃及)이 망(亡)하엿으나 그 민족과 그 민족의 예술은 망하지 아니하엿으며 유대(猶太)는 망하엿으나 그 민족은 망하지 아니하엿읍니다. - 후략』
올타 그것이다. 나는 네 말에 수긍(首肯)된다. 네가 한 말을 나는 일전에 대판조일신문에서 동경의 시인(詩人) 야구미차랑(野口未次郞)씨와 중국의 소설가 노신(魯迅)씨의 회담중(會談中)에서 발견하엿다.
『- 전약 - 중국이 망할지라도 국가는 망하여도 민족은 영원히 아니 망한다』
이러케 그는 말하엿다. 그러타. 이제 우리 조선은 최승희라는 한 사람의 조선민족을 세게무대(世界舞臺)에 내여놋케 되엿다는 것을 너는 깁히 재인식(再認識)하여야 할 줄로 안다.
