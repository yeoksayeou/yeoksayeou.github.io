Article Title: 端宗哀曲, 重調歌談 「寧越斷膓曲」 (King Tanjong's Lament, A Ballad in Chungjo Meter: "The Heartbreak Song of Yŏngwŏl")
Author: 曉鍾 玄僖運 原作 (Original Work by Hyojong Hyŏn Hŭi-un), 鮮鬱軒 玄哲 唱演 (Performed by Sŏnuk'hŏn Hyŏn Ch'ŏl)
Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary/Etc.)

King Tanjong's Lament, A Ballad in Chungjo Meter: "The Heartbreak Song of Yŏngwŏl"
						Original Work by Hyojong Hyŏn Hŭi-un, Performed by Sŏnuk'hŏn Hyŏn Ch'ŏl

"Where is Yŏngwŏl? A deep place in Kangwŏn Province.
The moonlight shining on Yŏngwŏl reaches even Hyŏnlŭng.
Surely, it must shine the same way.
Oh, heart-rending cuckoo, why do you cry?
The tears shed by our young lord,
Each drop has gathered to become resentment.
Every sigh he heaves is a turn of heartbreak.
*Sangam* (His Majesty, the Deposed King): 'Ok Hŭi! If you hadn't come to this mountain valley, what would I have done? Human fortune's vicissitudes are truly unknowable, but even so, could there be anything more futile than this?
My father, King Munjong, passed away before reigning even two years. I, young as I was, inherited the great throne at just twelve years old. With the many wise ministers whom the late king had passed down, I intended to inherit the great work of King Sejong, the heaven-sent great sage <143>, for all our people and create an era of great peace. But against all expectations, due to the ambition of Grand Prince Suyang and the collusion of treacherous ministers, they killed hundreds of wise ministers bestowed by the late king outside in one night. Inside, starting with his younger brother Grand Prince Anp'yŏng, among all the close relatives, if anyone displeased him even slightly, they killed human lives like blades of grass. Ultimately, they even seized the throne, which the late king had worried so much about. Still unsatisfied, they deceived me and exiled me to this Yŏngwŏl. My own body, being that of a person with many sins from a past life, doesn't matter whatever hardship I endure. But because of me, this ill-fated person, everyone around me suffers endless hardship. How could my heart not ache? Above, I betray the dying instructions of the late king, and below, I fail to protect the loyal retainers of two reigns. What should I do? Ah! My heart is so oppressed, I cannot bear it.'
Ok Hŭi: 'Your Majesty, please do not worry so much. There are still countless loyal ministers in the court and country. Surely, they wouldn't let Your Majesty suffer such hardship for long? It seems likely there will be some good news before long, so please do not worry widely. The night is late, and the mosquitoes are many, so please retire to the bedchamber. The people who escorted you from Seoul are still here; we don't know what wicked schemes they might plot upon returning. Please forget all this and that, and preserve your precious health until good times return.'
*Sangwang* (King Emeritus): 'Yes, everything is Heaven's will. I try to forget, try to forget, but naturally, I cannot. How have things become in Seoul? Has the Queen Dowager suffered any great harm? It's already been ten days since we arrived here, hasn't it? Tomorrow, some of the people who brought me here are said to return to Seoul? They too suffer much needless hardship because of an unlucky king like me! Ah! What will become of me tomorrow....'
Ok Hŭi: 'Your Majesty, what use is there in thinking about this and that? It only troubles your royal mind needlessly. The night is late, so it would be best to retire to the bedchamber.'
*Sangwang*: 'Alright! Let's sleep! The night has indeed grown quite late! You must be tired. Go and sleep too.'
Saying this, he sighed deeply and was about to lie down, when—

Amidst the silence of all nature (萬籟는 寂寞에),
The song of Wang Pang-yŏn, overflowing with sorrow <144>
Reached the ears of our King,
Unable to sleep in a foreign place.
"A thousand *li* long road,"
"Parted from my beautiful one,"
"My heart has no place to rest,"
"Sitting by the stream,"
"That water, like my heart,"
"Weeps and travels the night path."
Piercing the listening King Emeritus's heart,
Tears unknowingly welled in his eyes upon the mat (席眼).

*Sangwang*: 'Oh! Ok Hŭi! Where is that sound coming from now? Who is singing? Did they not sing, "A thousand *li* long road, Parted from my beautiful one, My heart has no place to rest, Sitting by the stream, That water, like my heart, Weeps and travels the night path"? Ah! Even amongst these people, there is still someone who cannot forget my old affection? Ok Hŭi, when will I too, without betraying the hearts of the many people striving for me, once again under the clear blue sky, fulfill the wishes of all the wise ministers as the late king intended, and give them release...?'

"As the years pass and months go by,
The accumulated sorrow deepens.
Receiving the virtuous influence of successive sage reigns above,
Wise kings and enlightened rulers succeeded the throne.
Among them, especially,
Grandfather King Sejong,
A sage king sent by heaven,
An enlightened ruler of wisdom, sagacity, culture, and military might.
As the nation's founding was not long past,
And various cultural institutions were newly established,
He had Kim Chong-sŏ
Develop the Six Garrisons (六鎭),
Pacifying the Jurchens.
He had Pak Yŏn
Create court ritual music (雅樂),
Establishing the national music.
He invented astronomical and geographical instruments,
Clarifying the principles of the universe.
He established the Hall of Worthies (集賢殿),
Gathering wise scholars from across the land,
Nurturing loyal pillars of the state.
And among these, especially <145>
He created Han'gŭl,
Passing it down to later generations.
Though myriad nations under heaven have national scripts,
Where else is there one like Han'gŭl,
With its orderly grammar and clarity of structure?
The protagonist of this ballad, King Tanjong,
Is the grandson of such a sage king,
And the son of King Munjong."

This sage king, Sejong, had seventeen sons. The eldest son was King Munjong, who was King Tanjong's father. Second was Grand Prince Suyang, that is, Sejo, who drove out his nephew King Tanjong and became king. Next were Grand Prince Anp'yŏng, Grand Prince Imyŏng, Grand Prince Kwangp'yŏng, Grand Prince Kŭmsŏng, Grand Prince P'yŏngwŏn, and Grand Prince Yŏngŭng—these eight were born to Queen Consort Sim. Following them were Prince Hwaui, born to Royal Noble Consort Yŏng of the Kang clan; and Prince Kyeyang, Prince Ŭich'ang, Prince Milsŏng, Prince Ik'hyŏn, and Prince Yŏnghae—these five were born to Royal Noble Consort Sin of the Kim clan. Next were Prince Hannam, Prince Such'un, and Prince Yŏngp'ung—these three were born to Royal Noble Consort Hye of the Yang clan. As for daughters, there were two princesses (公主) and two lesser princesses (翁主), making it 21 royal siblings in total. King Munjong had his son King Tanjong and daughters Princess Kyohye and Princess Kyŏngsuk. Besides these, King T'aejong's son and King Sejong's older brother, Grand Prince Yangnyŏng, was the only one alive at that time. In terms of a private family, this could be seen as quite a flourishing household. However, whether in a private family or the royal house, if the parents' affection among several siblings is unequal, or if there is no fraternal love among the siblings, naturally discontent arises, resentment grows, and while they may harbor daggers against each other inwardly, suppressed by the parents or the older brother, they say nothing. But when the parents pass away or the older brother is gone, that resentment can be directed at the nephew. In extreme cases of immorality and perversion (廢倫忘常)—indeed, even in today's world, there are many wicked people who engage in court battles between father and son, or among brothers—but someone like King Sejong, possessing extraordinary natural talent and abundant virtuous influence, ensured perfection in general affairs and extended his sagely virtue to all royal relatives without exception. Thus, there was no reason for any descendants to feel discontent. Furthermore, his eldest son, King Munjong, was not only supremely filial but also bestowed grace upon all state affairs and royal relatives, exceeding even King Sejong. He showed exceptional fraternal love, especially towards his many brothers. Since Grand Prince Suyang, the first son [after Munjong], enjoyed hunting all his life, <146> whenever Munjong heard he was going hunting, he would often grant him wine and meat. And since the second younger brother, Grand Prince Anp'yŏng, enjoyed calligraphy and painting, if there was any poetry gathering, he would bestow food (賜饌). Not only that, but it is common in both private families and the royal household to particularly cherish the sons within the close kin group. King Sejong especially cherished his youngest son, Grand Prince Yŏngŭng, and wished to bestow upon him treasures he had used during his lifetime, but passed away before accomplishing this. Therefore, King Munjong, understanding this intent, bestowed all the treasures his father had used upon Grand Prince Yŏngŭng. He inherited the late king's will even in such small matters. Also, concerned that fraternal relations might be harmed by wealth or power, he conducted every matter with utmost virtue and constantly warned the Grand Princes, worrying countless times that they might inflict harm upon the people under the authority and power of being a Grand Prince. Despite outwardly achieving an era of great peace and inwardly having a harmonious royal family, there was one persistent worry from King Sejong's time: namely, the Crown Prince at the time, Munjong, was frail and short of breath (體弱氣短), his health was not robust, and yet he had not produced a royal grandson even as he approached the age of thirty. King Sejong's earnest wish was extreme, and then, on the 23rd day of the 7th month of the Sinyu year [1441], the royal grandson prince was born. King Sejong's joy was indescribable; not only the palace but also the common people rejoiced. Amidst this nationwide rejoicing, King Sejong harbored a hidden worry. It was nothing other than the fact that while the various Grand Princes were strong and robust, the Crown Prince was weak, and the royal grandson was young. He thought that perhaps some misfortune might occur after his own passing. One day, addressing the Queen Consort, he said:
Sejong: 'Human lifespan is unknowable, the length of life uncertain, but with the Crown Prince being so weak and the royal grandson still young, while the Grand Princes are so strong—especially Suyang, whose forceful spirit (氣强) is extreme—I fear the future may be worrisome.'
Queen Consort: 'Your Majesty's concerns seem valid, but surely Suyang also has thoughts. Considering the lofty affection of his father king and the deep fraternal love of his elder brother, is it likely he would harbor ulterior motives? But even if, perchance, he were to harbor improper thoughts, such a thing could not be accomplished by him alone. Since the senior ministers of the court are all moved to tears by Your Majesty's sagely virtue, <147> who would conspire with Suyang? Moreover, the scholars currently in the Hall of Worthies possess talent, virtue, and learning that are paramount in the nation, and all are individuals who will become pillars of the state in the future. Therefore, whenever the opportunity arises, if you entrust the royal grandson to them, the future should be peaceful.'
Sejong: 'The Queen Consort's words are indeed profound.' After this, King Sejong increasingly, whenever opportunity arose, would see the scholars of the Hall of Worthies and entrust the matter of his descendants to them. Among them, he particularly entrusted much to Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu, and others who were leaders in the Hall of Worthies. Sometimes he bestowed royal wine, personally gave them the royal meal (水剌) he had been eating, or if he saw them sleeping cold during duty in winter, he would personally take off the fine hemp coat he was wearing and cover them. He pre-appointed Chŏng In-ji as tutor from the day the royal grandson was born, even saying, "After my passing, you, sir, must help this royal grandson and become a cornerstone (盤石) for the nation." Not only that, but after King Sejong passed away and King Munjong ascended the throne, upholding the will of his father King Sejong above and thinking of the future affairs of the Crown Prince below, his earnest entreaty towards these scholars—especially Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu, and others—was said to be extremely sincere. However, before this entrustment faded from their ears, they betrayed that grace, abandoned their duty, cast aside the sagely favor received from two reigns, as vast as rivers and seas, like worn-out shoes. Aided by Grand Prince Suyang, they obstructed and thwarted the young King Tanjong in every matter, ultimately causing him to be exiled to Yŏngwŏl, forcing him to harbor resentment for eternity with utmost grief and pain.

"Born as a precious royal prince,
Due to what karma from a past life,
Less than three days after entering the world,
He was eternally separated from his virtuous mother queen.
The young royal grandson, wrapped in swaddling clothes,
Entered the loyal embrace
Of Royal Noble Consort Hye of the Yang clan, who was like a grandmother,
And received the sage king's early education.
But are vicissitudes the way of human affairs?
In the Pyŏngin year [1446], when he turned six,
His grandmother,
Queen Consort Myŏnghŏn (明憲王后), passed away.
In the second month of the Kyŏngo year [1450], when he turned ten,
King Sejong, the heaven-sent sage ruler,
Suddenly fell ill and passed away.
Heaven and earth became dim at once. <148>
The traces of tears flowing on the royal face
Had no time to dry on the dragon robe (袞龍袍).
King Munjong, enveloped in sorrow,
Ascended the throne, but less than three years later—that is, after observing the third-year memorial for King Sejong in the second month—he suddenly passed away in the fifth month. This year was the third year of Munjong, Imsin [1452].

**Summary**

This article presents a dramatic retelling, likely intended for performance (*ch'angyŏn* suggests sung performance, perhaps akin to *p'ansori* or *ch'anggŭk*), of King Tanjong's tragic exile in Yŏngwŏl. It blends poetic verses lamenting his fate with dramatic dialogue between Tanjong and a court lady, Ok Hŭi, expressing his despair and sense of betrayal by his uncle, Grand Prince Suyang (later King Sejo). The narrative then provides historical background, detailing the lineage of King Sejong, Munjong's concerns about his son Tanjong's succession due to his own frailty and Suyang's ambition, Sejong's efforts to secure Tanjong's future by entrusting him to loyal scholars like Chŏng In-ji and Sin Suk-chu, and the ultimate betrayal by these figures. The piece concludes by recounting the rapid succession of deaths leading up to Tanjong's vulnerable position.

From a colonial history perspective, this article is interesting as it represents the popularization and adaptation of traditional Korean historical narratives and performance styles during the Japanese occupation. Publishing a story focusing on usurpation, betrayal by trusted officials, and the suffering of a legitimate Korean king (Tanjong) could subtly resonate with contemporary feelings of loss of sovereignty and injustice under colonial rule, even if presented as historical drama. Historians might find value in its use of older Korean language and spelling conventions from the 1930s, the specific format blending verse and prose dialogue (重調歌談, *chungjo kadam*), and its role in keeping Korean historical consciousness alive through popular cultural forms like magazine articles based on performed arts.
