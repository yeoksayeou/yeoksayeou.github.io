諸家推薦 新人作品集-兪鎭午 薦, 流浪民(소설)
Thoughts After Selecting "Wanderers" (流浪民選後感)

Yu Chin-o (兪鎭午)

In total, five creative works were forwarded (迴送된 創作) to me this time: "An Anonymous Writer's Notebook" (無名作家의 雜記帳), "Running for Office" (出馬), "Wanderers" (流浪民), "A New Start" (再出發), and "Wife" (안해). Reading through them, what was very disappointing was the absence of the powerful work (力作) I had hoped for. Although the magazine company insists I recommend one or two pieces, I found it difficult to judge the superiority or inferiority (優劣を判斷) among the three: "Wanderers," "A New Start," and "Wife." "Wanderers" has a concise style (文體는 簡潔하나), but the author's excitement (作者의 興奮) precedes objective description (客觀的描寫). "A New Start" raises interest (興味を 도두나) in its theme (테-마), but lacks artistic refinement (藝術的琢磨가 不足하며). "Wife" depicts one aspect (一面) of the melancholy (憂鬱) of an intellectual (知識人), but the author's eye for reality (현실을 보는 作者의 눈) is not firm (確乎치 못하며) and thus it is rambling (散漫하다). Though it may seem very unkind, I wish to avoid this responsibility (責任) by leaving it entirely to the magazine company's discretion whether to publish all three or select any one of them.<242>

Wanderers (流浪民)

Kim Puk-wŏn (金北原)

Prologue (푸로로그)

The Nakdong River (락동강), swollen by a great flood (대범람) unprecedented since the beginning of history (유사이래 미증유)—the damage inflicted upon (항한) lives and property (생명 재산) along its banks is hard to describe even with one brush stroke (일필 난기). As a result (말미아마), the residents along the entire riverbank area lost their means of livelihood completely (송두리채)... Due to relief efforts (구제로 인하야) by the authorities (당국) dealing with this, northbound trains (북행열차) began carrying them away, some to Manchuria (만주), some to collective villages (집단부락), some to farms, some to the labor market (노동시장) of North Korea (북조선). It was two years ago from now that a portion of them—Changsu's (창수) family and many other families like Changsu's—threw themselves into the labor market of North Korea.

I.

A thicket of lush green assorted trees (청청한 잡목 숲) on a mountain corner (산모퉁이)—yet the branches and leaves droop listlessly like frostbitten (서리맞은) cabbage leaves. A road construction site, cutting down these trees and opening a new path. The midsummer sun (복중의 뙉볕) pours down its fiery heat (염열) mercilessly onto their bodies—onto the exposed skin between the sweat-soaked nape (땀 배인 목덜미) covered by a short jacket (적삼) and the sagging trousers (처진 주의). No, it is scorching them. The Black people of America (아메리카의 흑인),<243> the naked people (발가숭이) of the tropics, and these laborers digging the earth here—yes. They have undoubtedly been of the same race (종족) since the distant past. This is no novel discovery or anything. It is merely an existence that has spanned (질러진) the long, eternal (유구한) time of the past, and as long as there is no transcendent (초탈적) change in history, it will also be an immortal (불멸) existence that will always shine upon this earth.
"Ah—it's hot..."
Changsu (창수) plants the pickaxe (곡광이) he was digging with and awkwardly (엉거주춤이) straightens his back.
Sweat! The scorching sun bears down on his sweat-drenched hair and face. The moment the sunlight reflects off his glistening forehead—he frowns at the transmitted heat. It is painful. The horizontal wrinkles (횡곡선) on his forehead, unbefitting his thirty years of age—they clearly speak (역역히 대변하는 것이다) of his current suffering, his past, the past of his life.
"Let's rest a bit."
His wife, letting out a pained breath and straightening her back, also looks aged (로태) beyond her years.
Husband and wife find a spot under a shade tree (그늘나무).
A cool gust of wind (시언한 한떨기 바람)! How they must long for it! But the craved (갈구) wind seems to be spreading its wings (나래) on some high attic floor (다락 우에서); not even a blade of grass stirs.
A heavy, breathless (묵중한 숨갑뿐) silence flows around them.
Changsu's hand, sitting idly, plucks a blade of grass (새초잎). The plucked grass blade moves to his mouth. He purses his lips and blows a long breath. The breath catches on the grass blade—a small vibration—wind—arises.
He blows again long (길-게) across the blade of grass. The grass blade flutters faintly, splitting the air current (기류). The current splits left and right. From this, a sound (음향) arises. It is undoubtedly a refreshing sound. A whistle!
He continues to blow. As he continues, the sound forms a melody (음율). The melody then weaves a song (곡), long and short, high and low. A folk song (민요) overflowing with the sentiment (정조) of the southern country, "Menari" (메나리), which seems unending.
The song gradually reaches its peak (진경에로 달닌다). As the song enters its peak, he sinks into infinite sentiment (무한한 정취에 잠기는 것이다).
Then, when his eyes fall back upon his reality, he suddenly becomes melancholic (우울하여진다). His face crumples... Flowing sorrow (애수), rising memories of days past—earth and life, farmer and poverty, and upon that, the descending natural disaster (천재), the rampaging flood (몸설치는 홍수)—
The time was two years ago, still fresh in memory, on a day after the seventh day of the seventh lunar month (칠석), when the midsummer heat of July had passed and a cool autumn wind (서늘한 갈바람) had begun to blow morning and evening (조석). On this day too, Changsu had cut the grass on the paddy ridge (논뚝의 풀을 깍고) and, watching the waves of rice ears bowing their heads beautifully (고읍게) under the evening sun, smiled faintly with an indescribable joy. And then, various thoughts (각가지 심산) followed in his head.<244>
(—Somehow, it's a good harvest this year. It must be. God isn't entirely heartless. If it were always bad, how could we survive?—)
At the end of such vague (무*기한) feelings,
(What good is it even if it turns out well? Haven't there been good harvests before? This damned life—) Just as he was blaming (타매도 할지음) his bitter reality, his wife, her hands still wet (물무든 손) from preparing dinner, came running down the village path, calling "Honey! Honey!"
"What is it?"
He met his wife flusteredly (황겁히).
"Hurry! Father—"
His wife, out of breath (숨이 턱에 올라), gasped and gestured. Though he didn't know the reason (영문), it was clear that something urgent had happened to his father. He stopped asking in further bewilderment (당황히) and ran quickly. When he arrived, his seventy-year-old father lay collapsed, having lost consciousness.
"Father! Father? What's wrong?"
"Father! Father? What happened? Huh?"
He shook and called to him several times like this, but his father just lay there silently.
Thus, neighbors gathered, a doctor came, acupuncture was applied, medicine was administered, and by the next morning, his father finally regained consciousness. However, after the long period of unconsciousness following his efforts (노력에 장시간 혼도한 남어지에), although he barely regained his senses, he could not get up.
Medical treatment (의료)? Administering medicine (시약)? But the anguish (번민) of helplessness! The day ended amidst this anguish.
That night.
The grey darkness was as gloomy (음울하였다) as his heart.
"Looks like it might rain?"
Mr. Chang (장서방), a neighbor who had come to check on the patient, said as he stepped down from the porch floor (마루아래).
"Probably will!"
Changsu also replied, looking up at the heavily settled (탁 깔앉은) sky (한울) as he saw him off.
Early evening, the third watch (초경 삼경), as the night deepened towards dawn, the sound of rain, patting (두다리며) the weary hearing (청각) of the son and daughter-in-law (며누리) who couldn't sleep at the father's bedside, continued ceaselessly, now loud, now soft, sometimes deep.
And the next day, and the day after that too, the ceaseless downpour (빗발) continued. Following the increasing force (우세), the muddy water—the flood!—already overflowed the streams and broke the banks. A frightening memory, a bitter wound still fresh for the residents along the riverbank. Changsu, leaning his body on the handle of his shovel (수금푼(삽)자루), spent the long day without leaving the paddy ridge (논뚝). He blocked the crumbling bank with an aching (죄이는) heart. But it was a sandcastle (모랫성) built by the sea—only futile (애꾸진) effort was contributed (이바지하였을 뿐); his effort and the overwhelming force of the water (수세) were in too great an inverse proportion.
When pitch-black darkness trampled upon Changsu's fruitless efforts and deepened, the dark death throes (새캄한 단말마) finally began to writhe upon the earth, but in the darkness echoed screams (규호)! Screams (비명)!<245>
When Changsu reached his house in urgent steps—it was already too late.
The muddy torrent (탁유) flowing in the darkness! His hovel (오막사리) was hidden within it—no, it had been swept away.
The whereabouts of his sick old father, his wife, and young children? Rending grief (왈작 치미는 비통)!
It was right then. Faintly heard,
"Honey—" the sound. Wasn't it the familiar voice of his wife? And following it, the cries of the rescue team (구호단).
Changsu ran headlong (천방지축) towards the back bank where the sound came from. A crowd gathered in the darkness. The crowd's cries and laments (규호비탄)! Enveloped within it, his wife's lament! The facts it recounted. With each word, Changsu's heart felt as if it were being gouged out (어여내는 듯 하였으니)—his sick old father and the newborn baby swept away by the muddy torrent—
Two days later!
As if having no relation to the tragic events (변사) of the meantime, the red sun poked its head out from its unbroken disc (어그러지지 않은 방우에서) when the miserable aftermath of the tragedy that had swept by was revealed. The gift left by the flood—countless losses of lives and property (생명재산에 수없는 잃음). The remaining survivors losing their means of livelihood (잃어버린 생활수단). Impending famine (닥치는 기근)—Under the thankful arrangement (알선) of the authorities, having lost everything (old father, child, means of livelihood), Changsu threw himself into the northbound train—his heart breaking (가슴이 뻐겨지던)! Changsu's heart, feeling it now—
"Why aren't you working like this? Why aren't you working, huh!"
It's the pouting (볼먹은) voice of the foreman (구미쥔). He drinks enough alcohol that his stomach supposedly hurts if he misses even a day. His usual red face is full of anger (노기가 만만한 양), an unusual look. But Changsu, with a composed attitude,
"Boss (오야상), you've arrived," bows his waist.
The foreman barely acknowledges the greeting.
"Why are you playing around instead of working like this!" he shouts angrily. Changsu says,
"Yes. We were just resting because it's hot. Why wouldn't we work?" and picks up his tool. The foreman mutters,
"Not working like this, just asking for rice (싸리), taking our attendance record (우리 데즈라(出面)나다아라). Idiot (바가야로)—ga," and scurries off to the other side.
Although Changsu doesn't know the meaning of "dejura" (데즈라), he vaguely guesses it refers to the count of workers. In fact, although it has taken exactly a month to complete this section of road worth twenty won, whether because the unit price for the work was low or his effort was insufficient, he has only managed to complete just over half a day's worth (겨우 반일 남짓하게). The amount he has taken from the boss (구미) during that time is about fifteen won. If he tries to finish this with the remaining five won, it's utterly impossible. Changsu felt wretched. After suffering through the exhausting hardships at every work site (일깐마다) in Unggi and Rajin (웅기라진), he had come searching here (Musan, 茂山) wondering if it would be any different, but as the old saying goes, 'the further you go, the worse it gets' (갈수록 스문산 이라더니), it was just the same<246> style (그식이 장식이다). And the first means of livelihood (밥턱) he found after coming here turned out like this again, so what was he to do?
The family of three had made porridge with the remaining measure of rice for dinner last night to barely fill themselves, and breakfast was the same. Today he needed to get at least a measure of rice again, but judging by the foreman's attitude, it seemed unlikely.
"Mom—"
—His son Changsoni (장손이) comes out into the shade of the tree.
"Why did you come? Aren't you watching the house—"
The mother's sharp eyes, yet full of affection, a gentle scolding.
"Mom—"
The son presses further seeing his mother's softening attitude.
"What is it—"
"Lunch—"
Lunch? Indeed, they had been hungry for a long time. They had been working while barely supporting their aching backs. But if they, as adults, felt this way, how hungry must he be, who, starting this year (금년 잡아들면서부터), ate as much as an adult (어른 밥 대답하는)? Thinking this, their parental hearts ached.
"Mo-om! Give me food (밥주우)."
Finally, the child burst into tears (울움집을 터놓았다). The cries of a young child unable to bear hunger—thinking of this and that, Changsu suddenly flared up in anger. "Go away! You brat!" he clenched his fist and glared at his son.
"Mommy—"
The son, terrified, runs away.

II.

When the fiery June sun hid its tail beyond the western mountains and grey twilight melted into the earth, Changsu and his wife returned from the work site (일깐).
Night lamps and smudge fires for mosquitoes burning brightly in every house in the village, revealing scenes of family harmony (일가단락 정화)— But the chimney of Changsu's barracks (빠락크) gapes open like a demon's maw (마귀같이 악아리만), yawning.
Changsu, sitting with his head in his hands lost in endless thoughts, clutched his pouch and left the barracks.
The pale June moon hung over the western mountain. Its dim, diffused light (으스름한 그 사광) shone on the path between the fields. The chirping of nameless insects (버레소리) echoed from all around. A tall sorghum field (수수밭), reminiscent of the mood of Manchuria (만주의 정조), the broom-like heads of sorghum ears stretching their necks long above. Fireflies flickered before him. Far away, across the river, the sound of a dog (호개) barking. The summer night at the border (국경) was deepening in mystery.
But he walked on like an unfeeling stone (육감없는 돌맹이), like a robot (로뽀트) moving only as directed, just placing one heavy foot (무거운 발길) after another, ploddingly (지척지척).
Judging by the foreman's behavior during the day, it seemed unlikely he would get anything. Yet,<247> there was no other way. Whether they ate or starved was up to his (the foreman's) decision.
This situation was sorrowful. Crying was useless. Changsu, like a prisoner awaiting final judgment (미결수같이), moved his heavy steps with a pounding heart towards the office door and pulled it open. Inside, under the dazzling light of a large lamp (휘황한 대람포), a clerk (서기) sat alone facing a table (톄불). Changsu
"Good evening (안녕하서요)," bowed his waist.
The clerk merely nodded, then rolled his eyes behind his Crookes glasses (크록쓰안경 속으로) as if to ask, "What do you want?"
Changsu hesitated, then asked, glancing at his expression, "Is the boss (오야상) in?"
The clerk replied,
"No—" leaving just that one word, then glanced sideways (흘끔) at the pouch clutched in Changsu's hand and, as if saying
"He's not here. Scram (미끄러저라)," quickly resumed his work.
He was indifferent, and Changsu too remained silent for a while. The room was filled only with the sound of their breathing and the scratching of the steel pen (철필소리) on the ledger.
The lamplight flickered (껌벅) as if about to go out, then flared up again (다시 춰 선다). Two moths (나븨) that had rushed in, mistaking it for the aurora (오로라) of the night, were flung back (되풍겨 날은 것이다) by the heat radiating from the bright light.
"Damn moth—(고라독조-)" the clerk leaped up, grabbed a fan from the table, and chased the moth. In the fierce pursuit, the moth was chased here and there, bumping against the wall, the bookshelf, the windowpane, the lamp, then sharply changing direction, flitting away. The clerk struck repeatedly, but the moth flew up when struck, dodged when it flew. Enraged, the clerk chased it haphazardly, then, with a rather uncertain strike, sent it flying with a "smack—" Indeed, the moth was hit, but "Crash!" the windowpane shattered. The clerk's face suddenly changed color, and as he awkwardly gathered the broken glass pieces, the maid (메시다끼), startled by the sound of the breaking window, ran in.
"Mr. Kim (긴상), what is it (난데쓰까)? A thief (도로보)?"
"Uh, it's nothing (응 난데모나이)? A poisonous moth (도구조가)?—"
"A poisonous moth came in and you broke the window (도꾸조-가하잇데 마도-고와시다노)?"
The clueless maid burst out laughing, while the clerk, embarrassed, nudged her side hard and signaled with his eyes, "Hey—be quiet (어이사와구나)."
"What? You broke the glass window (난다 가라스마도 고와시다노)?" Finally, the foreman appeared, his voice rough, his face angry. The clerk, shrinking like a turtle pulling in its neck, didn't know where to put himself. Changsu quickly stood up and bowed. The maid, far from covering for another's mistake, instead teased,
"He says a poisonous moth (도꾸조가)<248> came in, so he hit the glass window (유리창을 따렸구면요)," and giggled.
"If the door had been shut— (도 시멧데오게바-)"
Before the foreman could finish, the clerk, as if finding an escape route, quickly said,
"It was shut tight (요꾸시멧데이다노오)—that guy opened it (아이쓰가 아겟데이데)—" putting the blame squarely on the innocent Changsu (애꾸진 창수를 새에 넣고 부빈다).
However, Changsu, not understanding what was happening, simply met the foreman's sideways glances with a smile. The foreman, who knew no other way to treat this fellow who seemed to cause him trouble in various ways, asked,
"Why did you come (단신나와와소)?" his usual red face showing snake-like eyes rolling with anger. Changsu, thinking his chance had finally come, replied happily,
"Yes—we've run out of rice!" and scratched (씨서 올린다) his head.
"No rice (쌀이 어브소)?"
"Yes. We came because we have no rice."
"Don't know—(와까랑-)" The foreman cut him off flatly with one phrase, then turned to the clerk.
"Hey, how does it stand (어이도-났단다)? This guy (고이쓰)—has he run up a debt (가시꼬이와 났도랑까)?"
At the command, the clerk searched the ledger. Uncertain of Changsu's name, he looked at the boss.
"This guy—is Ch'oe-something, right (고레-사이난데쇼)!?"
"Hmm, Ch'oe Ch'ang-su (사이쇼-슈(최창수))," said the foreman, looking quite displeased, as if the clerk should have known.
Changsu, unable to understand their conversation, simply waited silently (묵연히) for the result.
Harboring a somewhat vague hope. Feeling somewhat (저윽히) uneasy at the foreman's unusual attitude—the bitter taste was too great. Like the dog that chased the chicken looking up at the roof (닭 쫓은 개 집웅 쳐다보듯), his crumbling dream met the harsh reality. He felt as if struck by a club, his head ringing (띙-하고), the world turning dark before him.
Suddenly, a sound struck his ears.
"Seize him! You damn brat!" A rough man's voice.
"Uwah—Mommy—" A child's crying.
"Seize him! A brat like this needs to be taught a lesson that'll make the world spin—Seize him—" Following the man's voice, a sharp 'smack!' (쩔컥!) sound, and the child's cry.
"Aigo, Changson—Sir (나리), please forgive him. He's just a child, he doesn't know better—"
A woman's pleading (애걸하는) voice. In that instant, Changsu stopped abruptly and pricked up his ears.
(Changsoni? Wife?)
(No, no—Why our Changsoni—)
He shook his head, denying it. But the scene approaching him only inflamed his feelings further.
"Annoying! (우루사이!)"<249>
At this truly heartless phrase, Changsu had no choice but to leave the foreman's office.

III.

The road back—is pitch dark. The sliver of moon that hung over the western mountain has also set. The earth lies silent as death under a heavy curtain of漆black darkness.
Only the sound of Changsu's footsteps, echoing whenever he takes a heavy, listless step—stepping high, then low, stepping low, then high—leaves a small sound on the sleeping earth.
"How cruel," the resentment welling in his chest.
"What do I do?" Worry raised its head—no matter how he thought, there was no solution. The labor market of North Korea, which he had yearned for like a sole sanctuary that would bring a ray of light, future prosperity, upon his ruined life—what it gave him was...
"He hasn't run up a debt (가시꼬시와 낫도랑데쓰네). There's still five won worth of construction funds remaining (마다고엔노 고-징가 노곳데아리마쓰)."
The clerk's report.
"Hmm. Five won left (움. 고엔노곳데오룻데)? Troublesome fellow (시오-나이 야쓰다). The work isn't even half done yet!" The foreman's muttering. The result was obvious. A simple final judgment: refusal of rice. Although five won of the construction fund remained, the calculation was that the remaining work couldn't be done with that amount, so it was better to cut ties completely at this point today.
The last tower of hope (한칭탑)—even that had crumbled for Changsu. What was left for him now? Only cheap pleading. But no matter how much he appealed to his tearful circumstances, in a world where everything is weighed by money and ruled by it, where could sympathy possibly exist, and how could his plight be accepted? Each word of his explanation only provoked
"You brat, seize him! Sit down wherever you please!"
"Smack! (쩔컥!)"
"Mo-om—"
"Aigo, Changson—Sir (나-리), please forgive him. He's just a child, he did it because he doesn't know better—" Wasn't that clearly the voice of his wife and child?
The next moment, the scene that finally appeared before his eyes was an undeniable fact that he couldn't shrink back from (움숙할려야 움죽할수 없는 엄연한 사실), and his face turned deathly pale (깜앟게 질리어간다).
The young child, unable to bear the hunger, had slipped away from his mother's sight and entered a neighbor's cucumber patch. Cucumbers hanging invitingly! Just as he reached out to pick one, driven by strong craving (밧짝 당기는 구미에), he was discovered by the sharp eyes of the field owner who had been watching for thieves. As he was being dragged away, his mother, who had come out looking for the child, followed behind, pleading (애원하는 것이다).<250>
Changsu was dumbfounded (어간이 벙벙하야) and speechless.
Seeing his father's face, the child
cried, trembling with fear. The wife's reproachful eyes shot (쏜다) at her husband. The field owner, now seemingly having found the person he needed to talk to, began to speak (말머리를 끄내기 시작한다). Changsu could only bow his head deeply in apology.
"He's an ignorant child who must have done it thinking only of his hunger. Please kill me instead and forgive him this once!" Changsu's voice was low, trembling.
Perhaps moved by pity that it was an ignorant child who couldn't bear hunger and picked a cucumber, or perhaps regretting that his earlier treatment had been too harsh, the field owner nodded his head.
"Everything is—my fault.—"
Changsu's head remained bowed.
"Fault? But it cannot be forgiven just by saying it's a fault. Unless it's a statement coming truly from the heart, that is, a manifestation of your sincerity, it cannot be forgiven. Why? Because it's common for people in the world to gloss over (풀칠하는 것이 일수이니까) their own faults with false apologies and insincere reflection! If it were acceptable to casually take others' property just because one is poor and circumstances are dire, who in this world would shed sweat? They'd just sit idly with folded hands and seize (탈취하지) the fruits of others' labor, wouldn't they? That would be a world of bandits (불한당)!」
The field owner's sermon, though seemingly low, was high, seemingly gentle, yet sharply pierced Changsu's heart. With every word, Changsu trembled. The field owner continued,"
"I was going to drag him to the police substation (주재소까지라도) and teach him a harsh lesson, but considering the child's circumstances, it is pitiful, so I forgive him. Take him away. And in the future (후차엔), teach your child well so he doesn't do such things again. The habit of living by one's own strength without relying on others—" At this lenient treatment, Changsu and his wife bowed deeply in gratitude.

IV.

The night deepens, passing silently into dawn. Dimly, the rafters (석가래) on the ceiling of the barracks (빠락크) emerge, one by one, their outlines becoming gradually clearer (명요하여 진다). It comes clearly into the vision of Changsu too, lying awake, unable to sleep, his eyes red with feverish heat (눈에 열이 올라 붉어진 동자), staring at the ceiling.
Lying at the warmer end of the room (아래목), two hungry souls still fast asleep!
The dried tear tracks (말라붙은 눈물 자죽) around their eyes! The dawn light (예명) reveals those pitiful traces (애처로움 형적) to Changsu's sight.

Late in the morning, a group of travelers with bundles (봇짐꾼) leaves the village.
The man carries a load on his back (지고), the woman carries one on her head (이고), the child walks ahead—one shabby bundle of bedding (람루한 이불꾸럼이), one small earthen pot (옹솟하나), a few bowls (사발 몇개)—their household goods (세간) are simple!<251> The June sunlight beats down relentlessly onto the heads of those unable to walk properly due to hunger.
Their steps turn eastward, eastward, climbing up a narrow, winding mountain path.
Where are they going?
There is no fixed destination (정처는 없다).
Only dimly (어슴푸레히) remaining in Changsu's memory are stories passed from mouth to mouth about a work site (버리터) at an airplane factory (비행기공장) and a coal mine (탄광) somewhere in Hoeryŏng (회령).(End)<252>

**Summary**

The article begins with Yu Chin-o's commentary selecting Kim Puk-wŏn's short story "Wanderers" (Yurangmin) for publication in Samch'ŏlli magazine, noting its concise style but overly emotional narration. The story itself depicts the plight of Ch'angsu, a Korean farmer displaced two years prior by a devastating flood on the Naktong River that killed his father and baby. Seeking work, he and his wife moved to the northern Korean border region (near Musan), finding grueling labor at a road construction site under an exploitative Japanese or Japanized foreman (kumijwin). Denied further rice advances despite remaining work funds, and shamed when his starving son is caught stealing a cucumber, Ch'angsu decides to leave with his wife and son, heading east towards Hoeryŏng, vaguely hoping for work in rumored airplane factories or coal mines.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the hardships faced by Korean peasants under Japanese colonial rule, including displacement due to natural disasters exacerbated by inadequate infrastructure or relief, forced migration for survival into precarious labor markets (including Manchuria and northern Korea), exploitation by Japanese foremen (using Japanese commands and insults like 'bakayarō'), and the desperation leading to minor crimes like theft out of hunger. The mention of specific locations like the Naktong River, Musan, Unggi, Rajin, and Hoeryŏng, along with the context of northbound trains carrying displaced people and the reference to Manchuria and northern labor markets, grounds the narrative firmly in the socio-economic realities of 1930s colonial Korea, making it a valuable piece for historians studying internal migration, labor conditions, and the impact of Japanese colonialism on rural Korean society.

二.

三.

주림을 못이기는 어린 자식은 어머니의 눈을 피하야 이웃의 오이밭에 발길을 디려 놓았던 것이다. 주렁주렁 매어 달린 오이! 밧짝 당기는 구미에 따먹으려 할지음 도적을 노리던 밭주인의 독한 눈초리에 발견된 바 되어끌리여 갈때 어린 것을 찾어 나왔던 그 어머니 뒤를 따르며 애원하는 것이다.<250>
창수는 어간이 벙벙하야 아무 말이 없다.
아버지의 얼굴을 볼 때 어린것은
공포에 떨며 느끼어 운다. 안해의 원망의 눈초리는 남편을 쏜다. 밭주인은 인제 만날 사람을 만났다는 듯 말머리를  끄내기 시작한다. 창수는 다 못 머리 숙여 사과할 뿐이다.
『철모르는 어린것이 배 곱흔 것만 생각하고 그런 짓을 한 모양이오니 죽일 때라 한번 용서하여주십시요! 하는 창수의 음성은 낮다 떨린다.
철없는 어린것이 주림을 못이기어 오이를 딴 것이람에 측은한 정이 움직이었던지 전자의 자긔의 취급은 너무나 혹독하였다고 뉘우침이었던지 밭주인 머리를 끄덕인다.
『모든 것이 다- 저의 잘못입니다.-』
창수의 머리는 그대로 숙여진다.
『잘못? 그러나 다못 잘못이라고만 하여서는 용서할 수가 없는걸 가슴속 진심으로부터 우러나오는 말 즉 그것이 그대의 충성으로의 표백이 아니면 용서할 수 없는 것이오. 웨 그러냐 하면 자기의 잘못에 대하야 세상사람들은 거짓의 사과를 반성아닌 반성으로 풀칠하는 것이 일수이니까! 가난타고 자기의 사정이 궁하다고 남의 물건을 함부로 집작하여도 관계없다면 이 세상에 누가 땀을 흘리겠오. 손을 매씨고 가만이 있다가 남의 노력의 결정을 탈취하지, 안그렀오 그건 불한당의 세상이요!」
밭주인에 설교는 낮은 듯 하면서도 높고 부드러운 듯하면서도 날카롭게 창수에 가슴을 찔은다. 그말 마디마다 창수는 화들화들 떤다. 밭주인은 다시 말을 이어」
「어디 주재소까지라도 끌고 가서 톡톡히 버릇을 가르처 보려하였지만 어린것의 정상이 일편 생각하면 가긍하기로 용서하는 것이니 다리고 가시요. 그리고 후차엔 그런 짓을 안하도록 자식을 잘 교훈하시요. 남에게 의지하지 않고 내 힘으로 살아나가는 버릇말이요-」하고 관대한 처분에 창수의 부처는 허리를 굽혀 감사를 올린다.

四.

밤은 깊어 소리없이 새여간다. 어룽어룽 빠락크의 천정의 석가래가 한개 두개 그 윤곽이 점점 명요하여 진다. 눈에 열이 올라 붉어진 동자를 천정에 보내고 누어 잠못이루는 창수의 시각에도 그것은 확연히 들어온다.
아래목에 누워 그대로 잠들어 있는 두 개의 주린 혼!
그 눈 가장자리에 말라붙은 눈물 자죽! 예명은 그 애처로움 형적을 창수의 시각에 비치여준다.

늦인 아침때 마을을 떠나는 한 떼의 봇짐꾼이있다.
사나히는 지고 여인은 이고 어린것은 앞서고-람루한 이불꾸럼이 하나, 옹솟하나 사발 몇개 그들의 세간은 간단하다!<251> 불 그대로 나려쪼이는 륙월의 햇볕은 허기저 거름을 못가는 그들의 머리 우에 사정없이 나려 쪼인다.
그들의 발거름은 동쪽으로 동쪽으로 구비자아 좁은 산길에로 톱아 오른다.
어디로 가는 것이랴!
정처는 없다.
