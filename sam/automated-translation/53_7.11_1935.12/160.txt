Couple Roundtable - Mr. and Mrs. Yŏ Unhyŏng, Feeling Newlywed After Twenty Years
						Samch'ŏllisa Woman Reporter Kang Sunok

We decided to visit renowned figures (名士) and artists (藝術家), have both husband and wife present in one place, and listen to their various thoughts, including those about their family regarding:
1. Child education policy
2. The couple's view on love and marriage
3. Marriage policy for their children
4. Plans for household economy
5. Reminiscences of their newlywed days
We call this a Couple Roundtable (夫婦座談會) and plan to continue visiting various "sweet homes" every month from now on.

The first place the reporter directed her steps was Kye-dong (桂洞), Seoul. Facing the chilly frost-laden wind of a winter morning blowing over the ridge of Pukaksan (北岳山), the reporter, on the twentieth day of last month as this year too draws to a close, set out early in the morning, determined to fulfill today's mission by visiting the home of Mr. Yŏ Unhyŏng (呂運亨), which she had planned beforehand.
Mr. Yŏ, who once wandered far overseas and at another time weathered many storms, is now the president of a newspaper company. I wondered curiously how our Mr. Yŏ—eloquent, magnanimous, vigorous, and fond of sports—spends his days at home, how his home is arranged, and what kind of life he leads. Picturing this in my mind, I quickened my steps towards the narrow alleyways of Kye-dong.
As Mr. Yŏ is always busy, I decided to seize the short time after he finishes breakfast and before he leaves for the newspaper office. Dodging the crowds of students rushing to school during the morning commute time in the narrow alley, I went up a little further, passing the red brick wall of Hwimun School, turned into the first alley, and found Mr. Yŏ's house—a newly roofed 'culture house' (문화주택) at the dead end on the left.

Guided by a young student, I entered the concise and neat reception room on the right, which combined Korean style with Western elements.
Scanning the room, not a single picture could be seen on the walls; only a round table stood in the middle, surrounded by four chairs. What immediately caught my eye was that the tablecloth on the table and the velvet on the chairs were all made of a deep crimson<107> color. It suddenly occurred to me that the master of this house must be quite fond of crimson.
Soon, the sound of footsteps echoed from the inner wooden-floored hall (maru), followed by a resonant voice.
Sharpening my senses, I pricked up my ears thinking,
"That must be Mr. Yŏ!"
Perhaps it was a brief word addressed to his wife, or maybe to his son or daughter; I couldn't catch the rest of the sentence.
Then, entering the reception room where I sat was none other than Mr. Yŏ himself.
He was a magnanimous elder with a broad forehead showing sharp angles and spirited eyes that seemed to sparkle with fire.
I offered simple greetings and explained the reason for my visit. He responded with a slightly smiling, stern face and a "commoner-like" (평민적) attitude, which pleased me.
A little later, his wife also came out and sat beside Mr. Yŏ. She was a plainly dressed, modest, old-style wife, looking well over forty.

Me - Perhaps the Shanghai (上海時代) period was the most glamorous and romantic time during your life together. Please tell me about that time first.
Yŏ - Was it just Shanghai? Where I traveled... first I was in Nanking (南京), then came to Shanghai, stayed there for several years, traveled through North and South Manchuria (南北滿洲), also went to Mongolia (蒙古), Russia (아라사), and sometimes even visited India (印度) or the Philippines (比律賓). You could say my footsteps have touched almost everywhere in Asia (亞細亞一帶). However, while I can't say the Shanghai period was necessarily the best, it's true that I always lived with tension and vitality.
Me - How many years in total were you away before coming back to Korea?
Yŏ - I returned after eighteen years of living abroad.
Me - So, while the "man of the house" (밧갓 어룬) was running here and there, living freely, how much hardship must the wife have endured with the young children?

[Photo of Yŏ Unhyŏng's Family]

Wife - What's the use of telling everything? My heart burned, and there were many times I worried so much<108> it felt like my black hair turned white overnight. But there was one incident in Shanghai that I always remember and cannot forget. It was during the time when the "man of the house" was actively evading capture, involved in movements like opposing Chiang Kai-shek (장개석). It was a time when he was running around day and night for such movements. Because of that, he would only drop by home briefly, like lightning, once every few days. One day, after being out somewhere for several days with no news at all, he came in around dawn. With a very anxious and tense expression, he asked for some food. I quickly prepared a meal tray for him. Just as he was hastily eating, suddenly, armed Chinese soldiers with guns and swords appeared out of nowhere and surrounded the place. The master quickly sensed the situation, threw down his spoon, and agilely jumped over the back wall, disappearing somewhere. For four or five days after that, there was absolutely no news. How my heart must have burned! At times like that, I would stand outside the back door every night, braving the dew, wondering if he might come, staying up through the long night. <109>
Yŏ - Did such a thing happen? Hahaha.
Wife - Was that all?
Me - The worries of serving a great man must be immense. I want to express my gratitude to you, Madam.
Wife - Oh my, why do you say such things again? Hohoho. Since I am utterly uneducated, how could I say whether what the master does is right or wrong? I just felt that he must be doing the right thing, and I thought I just needed to raise these young ones well inside the house. Therefore, I have never disobeyed any words of the "man of the house." That's why our family always lives peacefully.
Me - How many years were you in Shanghai?
Yŏ - Since she (pointing to his wife) came looking for me, we set up a household and lived there for about ten years, I suppose.
Wife - Exactly ten years.
Yŏ - Right, that sounds about right. Since you came eight years after I crossed over to China, it adds up to about ten years.
Me - Was yours a love marriage (戀愛結婚)?
Yŏ - What is a love marriage? Was the term 'love' even around back then? Among them, we had a very old-fashioned marriage. At that time, my mother was seriously ill, and she hastily arranged the marriage out of a desire to see a daughter-in-law quickly, without saying anything to me. I got married without knowing anything, in a sense.
Wife - (Silently lowers her head. Remembering that time, she still seems shy.)
Yŏ - So, what affection could have possibly developed back then? Confessing now, truly, trying to live a married life without affection, how could that work? So, at that time, while working as an elementary school teacher (小學校敎員), I essentially wandered around the countryside alone, living a single life.
Me - Your age back then?
Yŏ - It was when I was twenty-one.
Me - And the wife?
Wife - I was living with and serving my parents-in-law. I served them devotedly.
Me - And then...<110>
Yŏ - As the times changed, I quit my job as an elementary school teacher and just rushed off to China. While I was living there for about eight years, my wife came over. So naturally, we started our family life from then on. When she came over to Shanghai, she brought our eldest son, who later died of typhoid fever (장질부사) – It was only from then that we truly came to know the real love between husband and wife. And there, we had the four children we have now. Hahaha… But excuse me, I have a prior appointment, so I must leave for the office (社). Please - go into the inner room (anbang) and talk more with my wife, rest slowly, and then go.
Saying this, he stood up from his seat.
Guided by the wife, I saw Mr. Yŏ off and went across to the inner room. Passing through the spotlessly polished wooden-floored hall (maru), I entered the inner room.
Inside the room, there was an "ŭigŏri" wardrobe (의거리 장농 - type of clothing chest) and a grandfather clock (괘종 시게), and nothing else was visible. Only on the wall hung a single embroidered picture of a white crane sitting under a pine tree.
As for the house, including the bathroom (목욕탕) and the children's study room, it is said to have six rooms (kan) in total.
Directly in front, the wide playground of Hwimun School spreads out below, and far in the distance, the hazy peak of mist-covered Namsan (南山) is faintly visible.
"How many sons and daughters do you have in total?"
I continued asking the wife questions.
"My eldest son is eighteen this year and in the 4th grade at Yangjŏng High School (양정고보). Next, there are three daughters attending school, and the youngest son is six years old this year. If the eldest son who died were alive now, he would be twenty-two this year—The six-year-old, no matter how much his father tells him to go to kindergarten, refuses to go. He complains why only he has to go to 'kindergarten' when all his older siblings go to 'school,' and just plays mischief at home. He enjoys making 'warships' (군함) and 'guns' (총) out of paper (조희), and rides his 'bicycle' (자전거), running around outside, oblivious to the cold."
Me - How much money does your household require per month? <111>
Wife - We manage economically, so it costs about one hundred won each month.
Me - What methods do you employ for children's education at home?
Wife - What do I know? It's what the master does... But from what I see, he doesn't seem to interfere at all. He doesn't communicate with the school, nor does he seem to tell them to study. Every day at 4 PM, they come back from school, and without anyone saying anything, they play when it's time to play and study diligently when it's time to study. Also, like their father, they love sports and hiking. Even now, on Sundays, my eldest daughter follows her father to the outskirts or mountains and spends the whole day there.
Me - Have you lived with and served your parents-in-law?
Wife - Yes—just before following the master to Shanghai, I lived under my parents-in-law for two years. I revered serving my parents-in-law like heaven, even more than my husband.
Me - How many years did you manage a household in Shanghai?
Wife - After I went over, I managed what could be called a household for exactly 10 years. After that, when the master somehow ended up coming to Korea, I naturally came out to Korea too, bringing the young ones (leaving only the eldest son in Shanghai). So, for the three years the master was absent, I lived like that with the young children. Living alone like that, I faced all sorts of hardships.
Me - Now that Mr. Yŏ commutes to the newspaper office every day, you must not have such worries? He probably comes home late at night?
Wife - Yes. He always seems busy. He always comes home late in the evening. Even in the morning, guests start visiting from dawn, so there's hardly any time for the family members to gather together and talk leisurely. We only chat a bit among the family during meal times. And even now, if something unexpected happens and he comes home after midnight, or if he doesn't return at the usual time, somehow, a corner of my heart feels subtly anxious and worried. The thought, "Could he have gotten into trouble somewhere for saying something too harsh!" often wells up in my chest. <112>
Me - What are your thoughts on the children's marriage issues?
Wife - Since none of the children are of marriageable age yet, the master doesn't talk about it like this or that... But sometimes when such talk comes up within the family, the master always says this: If they like each other and get married, that's enough; parents have no business interfering... And rather than having a grand wedding ceremony at some church or with a pastor, he always says that just having a simple ceremony among themselves is sufficient. He always asks what need there is for things like a pastor's sermon!
Me - Don't you teach the children habits like saving money (貯金)?
Wife - The master absolutely forbids saving. Since we are barely getting by, we don't really have money to save anyway, but he says, "Living for today is enough," and "How can one know what will happen several years or decades later to save money?" He always says that just getting through each day without incident is good enough, so he is completely opposed to such things.
Me - Does Mr. Yŏ eat well?
Wife - Yes. He eats very well. Since he has a large build, he eats a lot, but he also eats anything readily available without being picky. And he washes his body exactly three times a week. So, the family naturally follows suit.

Already, the warm sunlight of early winter streams through the glass window of the front yard.
On the wide playground of Hwimun School, students chatter noisily, perhaps having finished a class.
The grandfather clock has already struck eleven.
The reporter hastily stepped out of the main gate of this house. Over the ridge of Kyesan (桂山) yonder, a crow flies by, letting out a caw-caw cry. The early winter midday in Hanyang (漢陽) is also desolate. <113>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from Samch'ŏlli magazine features an interview with Korean nationalist figure Yŏ Unhyŏng and his wife at their home in Kye-dong, Seoul. Conducted by reporter Kang Sunok, the interview explores their family life, covering topics like child-rearing (laissez-faire approach, emphasis on sports), their arranged marriage (which Yŏ admits lacked affection initially but grew later, particularly during their time in Shanghai), household finances (living day-to-day, opposing savings), and future marriage plans for their children (preferring love matches and simple ceremonies). Yŏ's wife recounts the hardships and dangers faced while supporting her husband's activities abroad, including a dramatic escape from Chinese soldiers in Shanghai. The article contrasts Yŏ's public persona with his private life and highlights his wife's traditional, supportive role.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the domestic life of a prominent Korean nationalist leader during the Japanese colonial period. Yŏ Unhyŏng's frank comments about his arranged marriage and later development of affection, his modern views on children's marriages, and his opposition to saving money reflect both personal idiosyncrasies and perhaps the precariousness felt by activists. His wife's narrative vividly illustrates the personal costs and dangers faced by families involved in independence or anti-establishment movements, even in exile. The description of their modest 'culture house' and lifestyle provides a glimpse into the social conditions of the educated middle class in 1930s Seoul.

夫婦座談會-二十年만에 新婚 氣分나신다는 呂運亨氏 夫妻
						三千里社 婦人記者 康順玉
명사(名士)와 예술가(藝術家)를 차저 그 부부 두 분을 한자리에 계시게 하고 두 분과 밋 그 가정의
1. 자녀 교육 방침
2. 부부의 연애관
3. 자녀에 대한 결혼 방침
4. 가정 경제에 대한 안
5. 신혼 당시의 회상
등 여러 가지 말슴을 듯기로 하다. 이를 가르처 부부좌담회(夫婦座談會)라 하고 압흐로 매달 계속하여 여러 「쓰윗트, 홈」을 찻기로 하엿노라.

제일착으로 긔자가 발거름을 옴긴 곳은 서울 계동(桂洞)이라. 북악산(北岳山) 넘에로 모라넘는 겨을 아츰의 쌀쌀한 서릿바람을 마즈며 기자는 이해도 마지 저무러가는 지난 수무하루ㅅ날 일은 아츰 오늘의 임무를 다하기 위하야 미리부터 마음먹고 잇든 呂運亨선생의 가정을 찻기로 작정하고 집을 나섯다.
한때에는 해외로 멀니 돌아단이시기도 하엿고 또 한때에는 여러 가지 풍랑을 만히 격그섯치만 지금에는 한 신문사의 사장으로 게신 呂선생님, 말 잘하고 호긔스럽고 씩씩하고 운동조와하시는 우리 呂선생님이 가정에서는 어떠케 그날그날 지내시는지 또한 그 가정은 어떠케 꾸며놓고 어떠한 생활을 하시는지 굼굼하게 머리ㅅ속으로 그려보며 총총한 발자국을 옴겨노와 계(桂)동 좁을 골목길을 행해 올너갓다.
