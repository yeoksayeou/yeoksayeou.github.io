Article Title: 拜金思想 痛擊論 (A Fierce Critique of Materialism/Mammonism)
Author: 柳光烈

A Fierce Critique of Materialism
						Yu Kwang-yŏl
P’ain hyŏng (Brother P’ain),
You truly are a person with a peculiar disposition. Otherwise, I know you are suffering considerably for the sake of editing the magazine. Last year, you gave me the topic of Western cemeteries and made me sweat trying to write about it, and this time you ask me to write a refutation of gold, which the world is so eagerly pursuing (營營而逐). Aren't you perhaps deliberately choosing these topics just to torment me?

P’ain hyŏng,
Taking up this topic, I sit holding my brush, and my originally dull writing becomes even more sluggish (澁滯). In today's world where everything is sold to gold, how can one possibly refute gold? First of all, since my own life is also in a position led by the neck and swayed by gold, how could the courage to refute gold possibly emerge?

P’ain hyŏng,
Who said it? That the 20th century is the age of coal, iron, and oil... Or that the modern era is the age of electrical civilization... However, I would say that the modern era is the age of gold. An Englishman once said that political power can do anything except turn a man into a woman and a woman into a man, but in the modern era, gold seems to show the vigor to do anything except perhaps such things.<69>

P’ain hyŏng,
I suddenly recall a memory from childhood. There were two boys playing merrily (嘻嘻). One boy boasted,
『If you just have money, you can buy anything.』
Then the other boy laughed and said,
『Kid, you say if you just have money you can buy anything, then go buy me a monk's topknot, resin from a jujube tree, and sleep from a dragonfly's eye』 - I recall hearing this said. Indeed, if asked to buy something nonexistent like this, it might be impossible, but otherwise, it seems everything can be bought.

P’ain hyŏng,
Having written this much, it turns out that I, too, have ended up writing a treatise in praise of gold. However, since the title says it's a refutation of gold, shall I try to force myself to write a bit? The great philosopher Chuang Tzu (莊子), who said, 『Observe the subtle essence by constantly being without desire (常無欲以觀其妙)』, also stated, 『Do not value goods that are hard to obtain, so the people do not become thieves; do not display desirable things, so the people's hearts are not disturbed (不貴難得之貨, 使民不爲盜, 不見可欲, 使民心不亂)』. If there are goods hard to obtain in the world, it is gold; because of gold, many thieves arise, and the people's hearts are thrown into confusion, so gold should be refuted.

P’ain hyŏng,
The great philosopher of the same school, Chuang Tzu, said, 『Eliminate sageliness, discard wisdom, and great robbers will cease to be; pick apart jade, destroy pearls, and petty thieves will not arise (絶聖棄智, 大盜乃止, 摘玉毁珠, 小盜不起)』. If jade and pearls are treasures placed in the same rank as gold, then gold too must be destroyed and discarded for theft not to arise; therefore, gold should be refuted.
In the old days, when someone brought a gold bribe, a certain person refused it, saying it was unjust. The giver then said, 'Who would know what was done on a certain night when no one was aware?' to which the reply was, 『Even if no one saw, Heaven knows, the spirits know, I know, and you know (天知神知我知子知)』, so how could I accept it? And when So Kwang (䟽廣) became the Grand Tutor to the Crown Prince, the Son of Heaven gave him 20 catties of gold, and the Crown Prince gave him 50 catties of gold. He did not hoard it <70> but gathered his clansmen and guests and spent it all. Someone asked him,
『Why don't you buy fields and houses for your descendants – why spend it all?』
Kwang replied,
『Am I so foolish (悖) as not to think of my descendants? Consider, I already have old fields and dwellings. If my descendants work diligently there, it is sufficient to provide for their clothing and food, making them equal to ordinary people. If I now increase their wealth, making it excessive (嬴飾), it will only teach my descendants laziness (怠惰).』
And when P'o Chŭng (包拯) became the Metropolitan Prefect (京尹), there was someone who entrusted ten thousand *nyang* of white silver (백금; *paekkŭm*) to him during a meal. The person who entrusted it died, and P'o told the son about it and tried to give it to him, but the son refused, so P'o asked him to accept it. P'o Chŭng called the son and offered it, but the son said, 『I have never heard that my late father entrusted white silver to anyone』 and did not accept it. Chu Cha (朱子) compiled these kinds of admirable words and good deeds (喜言善行) and presented them to later scholars. Although these people might not have outright refuted gold, it is true that they were extremely indifferent to it.

P’ain hyŏng,
However, throughout history, where has there been a monarch or a ruler who disliked gold? Looking at our country's history, one can often see prohibitions on gold and silver in clothing, but this stemmed rather from valuing gold and silver so highly that the highest class sought to monopolize them.
Also, in the past, if traces of gold or silver were found, mining was prohibited by state law (國禁). This wasn't because gold and silver were disliked, but because there was fear that foreign powers would invade if they learned that such gold existed. Although gold was kept secret like this, foreign enemies like the Ming (明) and Qing (淸) first listed '100 nyang of gold, 100 nyang of white silver' at the head of Chosŏn's tribute items, clearly showing how much gold was valued even then.

P’ain hyŏng <71>
However, the reason gold surpasses all other commodities today lies solely in modern capitalism. Although gold, as a precious treasure, has been valued by the general populace since ancient times, it gained absolute power only after being used as currency, exchangeable for everything. One economist said,
『As the circulation of commodities expands, the power of wealth that takes an absolutely social form, which can be utilized at any time, in other words, the power of money, increases.』
Citing Columbus as an example:
『Gold is a strange thing. He who possesses it is lord of all he wants. With gold, one commands whatever is desired. Moreover, with the power of gold, it is not impossible even to make souls go to heaven.』
This saying is the same as the Korean proverb, 『Even a baby in the womb will come out if you give money.』 He continues:
『All things are transformed into money. All things become sellable. Circulation is the great ‘retort’ that receives all things and discharges them as crystallized money. Not even the bones of saints (聖徒의 白骨) can resist being rendered down in the alchemist's furnace (鍊金所), how much less so the more subtle holy relics (聖物) that the hand of commerce cannot reach.』
And further, as commentary:
『In the eyes of the Phoenicians (페니시아), who were characterized as a commercial people, money was merely the transformed shape of all things. Therefore, it was rather a natural consequence that maidens who surrendered their bodies to unknown men during the festival of the goddess of love offered the money received as payment to the goddess.』 And again:
『Just as all qualitative differences among various commodities vanish in money, money, for its part, also becomes an extreme egalitarian, erasing all distinctions.』 <72>

P’ain hyŏng,
Therefore, even someone who was a dog butcher or carried night soil buckets yesterday can command the prime minister today if they only possess gold. And even a prostitute (賣春買笑의 천기) from yesterday can make ladies from good families kneel at her feet if she only possesses gold.

P’ain hyŏng,
In your letter too, you mentioned that materialism (배금주의; *paegŭmjuŭi*) is rampant (彌滿) among the youth today. The reason for this is actually that gold is an extreme egalitarian, erasing all distinctions. Needless to say, if you, hyŏng, had gold, wouldn't it be clear that you wouldn't content yourself with being the chief editor of a magazine like *Samch'ŏlli* but would be sitting in the president's chair of the *Tong-a Ilbo* or *Chosŏn Ilbo*? Haha.

P’ain hyŏng,
This is really a joke, but when I was residing in Iksŏn-dong (益善洞), I had a vacant room and took in several middle school students aged 15 or 16. One day, I overheard their conversation:
『After I graduate, I'm going to become a loan shark (고리대금). That way, I must become the richest man in Chosŏn (朝鮮).』
And another boy said,
『In the future, I'm going to do gold mining. That way, I must become a big rich man.』
So I asked them,
『What will you do when you become rich?』 One said he would establish a university and become its president, and the other said he would create a cultural enterprise institution and become its president. At that, I told them,
『If you just have money, you could even become the prime minister, why necessarily cultural enterprises or schools? You could even set a whole country straight.』<73>
I encouraged them to have bigger ambitions.

P’ain hyŏng,
Gold, as currency, reigns over all things (萬者) and commands them. Its birth came from social power, but ultimately it becomes the private power of individuals. Therefore, modern society welcomes the holy grail of gold, treating it as the radiant manifestation (權化) of its own life principle.

P’ain hyŏng,
Let me say it again: what is there that cannot be bought with gold? The integrity of a scholar (선비), the chastity of a maiden, the scholarship of a PhD – is there any one of these that does not bow its head before this gold?

P’ain hyŏng,
How can we refute gold, which possesses such absolute power and can perform both all good and all evil? Once, when the Chinese revolutionary Sun Yat-sen (孫逸仙) was asked what was most necessary for revolution, he sternly composed his expression and said,
First, money!
What next?
Second, money!
What next?
Third, money!
And again, when asked,
‘Premier Sun (先主), what do you like most in this world?’<74>
he reportedly answered,
『What I like most in this world are revolution and beautiful women (어엽뿐 색시).』
Therefore, if even someone above the comma like Sun Yat-sen reached this state, it is not unreasonable for us ordinary people below the comma to be materialistic.

However, P’ain hyŏng,
Will gold eternally dominate the human race on this earth? I believe it will not. Just because you publish a refutation of gold in the magazine and I write a refutation of gold doesn't mean the power of gold will diminish or materialism will disappear.
Capitalism, possessing the soul of liberalism and the body of individualism, appeared on the stage of history from the 17th century. And gold became its weapon for the freedom of commerce and industry centered on this individual.
Therefore, this gold gains absolute power only as a tool for free circulation in an individual-oriented society. If society becomes society-oriented or adopts controlled circulation, gold will lose its power the very next day.
When one is hungry, can gold be cooked into rice? Can it be boiled into soup? Robinson Crusoe, having salvaged grain seeds, tools, and even a pen for keeping records from the shipwreck, lifted a heavy money pouch as he landed on the island. Finding it full of gold, he angrily exclaimed,
『This—utterly useless thing, I pointlessly brought it all the way here!』
and furiously threw it into the sea.
Therefore, gold has no use whatsoever on a deserted island without the societal background that provides its power. However, even with a societal background, if gold becomes state-owned (國有), and the state controls everything, making all circulation based on tokens representing labor,<75> then gold again becomes useless. Therefore, gold becomes tyrannically powerful (橫暴自在) only as private power based on the absolute freedom of the individual against the backdrop of society.
Thus, this refutation of gold inevitably clashes head-on with the modern system. If, instead, one were to refute gold with medieval integrity (廉潔), the weeping would rather be laughable and pitiable (可笑可憐), even worse than materialism itself.
However, I am not pessimistic. This is because I believe in the greatness of human history and trust that the vast masses (浩浩茫茫한 광범층 민중), broader than the ocean, have the inevitable destiny to reject the tyranny of this gold. In the current society, the past (accumulation of gold) dominates the present, but in the coming society, the present (effort/labor; 努力) will dominate the future.<76>

**Summary**

The author, Yu Kwang-yŏl, reluctantly agrees to write a critique of materialism ("paegŭm sasang") for the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* at the request of the editor, "P'ain hyŏng". He argues that gold (money) holds absolute power in the modern capitalist era, capable of buying almost anything and erasing social distinctions, making even former outcasts powerful. While acknowledging historical figures who showed indifference to wealth, he notes that rulers always valued gold, and its power surged with capitalism, where it became the universal equivalent. Despite its current dominance, exemplified even by Sun Yat-sen's focus on money for revolution, the author expresses hope that gold's tyranny is not eternal. He posits that gold's power relies on individualistic, free-market capitalism and will wane in a society-oriented system based on collective control or labor value, suggesting a future where present effort, not past accumulation, holds sway.

From a historical perspective, this article offers a fascinating glimpse into Korean intellectual discourse during the colonial period (1935). It reflects a deep engagement with the disruptive forces of modern capitalism and its associated ideology of materialism, contrasting it with traditional East Asian values (Confucian/Daoist). The critique of gold's power as an "extreme egalitarian" that erases old social hierarchies, alongside the observation of youth aspiring to wealth through potentially exploitative means (loan-sharking, resource extraction), highlights the complex social transformations underway. The tentative suggestion of a future society where gold loses power hints at the influence of alternative socio-economic ideas (possibly socialist) circulating among intellectuals resisting the dominant colonial capitalist order. Historians might find the blend of traditional philosophical references (Chuang Tzu, Chu Hsi) with modern economic critique (referencing Columbus, implicitly Marx via the discussion of money and circulation), the specific examples of changing youth aspirations, and the underlying anti-capitalist/anti-individualist sentiment particularly interesting for understanding intellectual responses to modernity and colonialism in 1930s Korea.

拜金思想 痛擊論
						柳光烈
巴人
형은 참으로 기이한 성벽을 가진 분이외다. 그러치 아니하면 잡지 편집을 위하야 엔간히 고심하는 줄을 알겟슴니다. 작년에는 서양인 묘지를 주며 글을 쓰라하야 땀을 빼게 하드니 이번에는 또 거세가 營營而逐하는 황금 배격론을 쓰라하니 형은 일부러 이러한 제목을 골라서 나를 괴롭게 하는 것이나 안닌가 하나이다.
巴人형
나는 이 문제를 들고 붓을 잡고 안젓으니 원래의 鈍筆이 한층 더 澁滯하여지나이다. 오늘날 모든 것이 황금에게 팔리는대 어떠케 황금을 배격할 수 잇으리까? 위선 나의 이 생명도 역시 황금에게 목을 끌니어 좌우되는 처지이니 어떠케 황금 배격의 용기가 나오릿가.
巴人형
누가 말하엿습니까. 20세기는 석탄과 철과 석유의 시대이라고... 또는 현대는 전기 문명의 시대이라고... 그러나 나는 현대는 황금의 시대라고 하겟나이다. 영국 사람은 정권은 남자를 여자로 만들고 여자를 남자로 만드는 이외에는 못하는 일이 업다 하엿거니와 현대에 황금이야말로 이러한 일 외에는 무엇이든지 할 것 가튼 기세를 보이고 잇슴니다.<69>
巴人형
나는 문득 어릴 때의 생각이 나나이다. 嘻嘻히 노는 두 소년이 잇어 한 소년이
『돈만 가지면 무엇이든지 사올 수가 잇다』
고 장담을 하니까 한 소년이 우스며
『이애 네가 돈만 가지면 무엇이든지 사온다 하니 중의 상투와 대추나무 송진과 잠자리 눈꼽을 사오나라 - 하는 말을 들은 일이 잇엇읍니다. 과연 이러케 세상에 없는 것을 사오라하면 몰으거니와 그러치 안흔 것은 모다 사올 수가 잇을 것입니다.
巴人형
이만콤 쓰고 보니 결국은 나도 황금 숭배론이 되고 말앗소이다. 그러나 제목이 황금 배격론이라 하엿으니 억지로라도 좀 써 볼가요? 『常無欲以觀其妙』한다는 거대한 哲人莊子는 『不貴難得之貨, 使民不爲盜, 不見可欲, 使民心不亂』이라 하엿거니와 世에 難得의 貨가 잇다면 그것은 황금이니 황금때문에 도적도 만히 생기고 민심도 현란되는 것이니 황금은 배격하여야 할 것임니다.
巴人형
가튼 유의 大哲人 莊子는 『絶聖棄智, 大盜乃止, 摘玉毁珠, 小盜不起』라 하엿으니 玉珠도 황금과 동열에 노이는 보화라면 역시 황금을 毁破하여 버리어야 그 不起할 터이니 황금은 배격하여야 할 것임니다.
넷날에 어떤 사람은 황금 뇌물을 갓다 주니까 그 사람이 불의한 것이다 하야 밧지안엇더니 주는 이 말이 某夜無知中에 한 일을 누가 알겟느야고 하엿더니 그 대답이 『아모도 안보앗다 할지라도 天知神知我知子知』인데 엇찌 바드랴하엿고 太子太傳되엿든 䟽廣은 天子가 황금 20십근을 주고 太子가 황금 50근을 주니까 그것을 축적치 아니하고<70> 族人賓客을 모아서 모다 써 버리엇다. 어떤 사람이 그의게
『자손을 위하야 田宅을 사지 왜 - 다 써 버리느야』
고 믈으니 廣은
『吾豈壹悖 不念子孫哉, 顧自有舊田廬, 令子孫勤力其中 足以共衣食 與凡人齊 今後增益之, 以爲嬴飾 但敎子孫怠惰耳』
라 하엿고 包拯이 京尹이 되엇을 때에 食中에 백금 만냥을 맛긴 사람이 잇엇는데 그 맷긴 사람이 죽고 그 아들에게 그 말을 하고 주엇드니 밧지안으니 주어달나하는지라 包拯이 그 아들을 불너 내어주니 그 아들은 『선친이 백금을 남에게 맛기엇다는 말을 듯지 못하엿다』고 밧지 아니 하엿다는 것을 喜言善行으로 朱子가 편술하야 후학에게 제시하엿으니 이런 이들은 황금을 배격까지는 아니하엿을지라도 극히 냉담하엿든 것 만는 사실이다.
