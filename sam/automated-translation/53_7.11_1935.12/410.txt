Thoughts After Selecting "Immigrant"

Im Hwa

I am finally sending the poetry manuscripts you sent me, Brother. Generally, there was nothing outstanding. Perhaps those sent to me were particularly so, but truly, from the bottom of my heart, there were few that made me muster the courage to recommend them for your esteemed magazine thinking, "This is it." Among them, the one I managed to choose is a piece called "Immigrant" (移民) by one Yi Koryŏ (李高麗). Next, considering so-called language, poetic technique, etc., comes Mr. Hong Sun-ch'ŏl (洪淳哲) (I have seen this person's work before, but it seems inferior to the work "Homesickness" (鄕愁) published in the June issue of *Sin Tonga* (新東亞)). Furthermore, considering the emptiness of content, abstraction of language, lack of imagery, and especially the deficiency in poetic sentiment, I have chosen the former for the first place and the latter for the second. The reasons for recommending "Immigrant" for the first place are generally as follows, I believe.

This poem, in a word, is a poem that sings of good content with clumsy words. However, the reason for selecting this poem does not lie entirely in its "theme." Although there are considerable flaws, such as the lack of order in language choice, the unnatural combination of speech sounds, and especially the damage to "rhythm" caused by the disorderly mixing of prose, the formal characteristic of this poem is the point that it is being narrated directly in response to the complex nature of the content's reality. In this respect, "Immigrant" is unconditionally the best work among the nine poems sent to me. Indeed, the form and nature of our lives today probably cannot be poetically visualized without a highly conceptual "rhythm" employing such vibrant, contemporary language. The most likely and fatal flaw in such cases is precisely the loss of conciseness in language and "rhythm." This flaw is also fatal in "Immigrant." However, fearing flaws and seeking ease is not the attitude today's poets should adopt, I think. Many newcomers in our Chosŏn (朝鮮) are treading the line of least resistance regarding this language. Instead of language that conveys the vibrant, vivid breath of life, language suited for poeticizing contemplative, resigned, shallow sentimentality is prevalent. Expressing such power, regardless of its flaws, earns my respect.<238> Not only that, but the "theme" of this poem is first-rate. The reality shown by the theme of this poem is the most common fact in our recent lives, and not having one's heart stirred before such facts is a disgrace for a modern poet.

These characteristics of language for such content are logical; only good content, good consciousness, can possess good language.

However, I hope this author bears in mind that the weakness in language stems from the weakness in the poet's attitude towards reality.

This poem is precisely an honest reflection of this fact.
That is, the author's centripetal approach and grasp of the reality of "Immigration" are not very accurate.
Specifically, there is much artificial complication in the factual content of this poem, and it seems the author failed to feel the tragedy or injustice from the depths of the heart (마음의 臭處로부터 - from the stinking place of the heart?). Therefore, at times, the author gallops over reality with facile, fabricated concepts. Consequently, although its content should surely convey something profoundly lingering to the reader, the impression is faint, and after reading, it possesses a looseness that makes it difficult to firmly embrace a unified something in the heart. This prosaic naivety towards reality is precisely what prevented this author from choosing capacious poetic language holding deep and broad content, and strong, associative language that conveys immense emotion, like a heart being torn, from an innocuous phrase. Poetic language should be the most associative among all literary languages, yet the failure to achieve this is inseparable from this author's inability to explain a hundred things with one, and to select phenomena possessing core, typical qualities from within reality.

I offer this simple post-reading impression in place of words of recommendation and pray for the author's dedicated effort. <239>

Immigrant

Yi Koryŏ

It was a rainy day,
The afternoon of that day when the wind was fiercely cold. When you came seeking my dilapidated house without rain gear.
That day, your lips already sadly spoke of today's parting,
I still cannot forget your two eyes that stared intently into space. Your tangled, scattered, rough hair.
Are you going?

Around the narrow mountain ridge, seventy *ri* inside and out,
Huffing and puffing, carrying a load of wood, going in and out of the town center (邑內),
That too will stop.
Ah, your shoulders soaked with effort, the A-frame carrier, the load of wood, my eyes will likely never see them again.
Placing the sack filled with dark blue millet onto the A-frame carrier, groping along the dark mountain path, whenever you returned to the village,
Sharing words together about the brightness [or hardships?] of the future, that too will now cease. <240>

Guardians of the Great Heart

Hong Sun-ch'ŏl

Men of letters, even at this dawn, already
taking up your brushes, what do you intend to draw on the pages of history?
To cultivate a single furrow (畝) of a slash-and-burn field on a rough mountainside,
just as the hoe tip bends against stones and tree stumps,
how many times have your sturdy brush stems broken?

With reason as bright as the May sun
and spirit as firm as fire-forged iron,
you who wield the unbending brush stem—
The blue and red ink are the blood of veins and arteries!
Our blood pressure that connects the pulse of the masses,
have you shed it again today until the solemn moment?
Have you flipped the switch! Is the rotary press turning?
With a mighty roar...

Look—at that solemn moment!
Like the active crater of Vesuvius,
Like a troika descending a snowy peak,
Our strong "matelots" (마도로스 - sailors/marines)
building high the Babel of light without suffering...

Men of letters! Shout loudly! Awaken!
That spring comes even to this rough land, that the spring of life surely comes!
Sing the heart of the skylark crying out to the very edge of the sky in the clouds!
Until the earth's core resounds in the first furrow of the old field,
Until they light the lamps in the unlit lighthouse on the night coast...

Oh—our Guardians of the Great Heart!
Cast away your steel pen nib like a spear point!
And thus, on the pages of history, write today's arduous journey leading to a brilliant tomorrow,
a diary painful yet glorious!
Ah—that the sound of the advance bugle is heard
From the field ridges! From the factories! From the streets!
From the docks! And from everywhere we are alive...

**Summary**

Im Hwa reviews poetry submissions for *Samch'ŏlli* magazine, selecting Yi Koryŏ's "Immigrant" despite its "clumsy" language because its theme realistically engages with the harsh contemporary reality of Koreans forced into migration and hardship. He criticizes its lack of linguistic refinement and the author's somewhat naive grasp of the subject but praises its attempt to use contemporary language for relevant content, contrasting it favorably with the technically better but emptier work of Hong Sun-ch'ŏl. Yi Koryŏ's poem depicts the sorrowful departure of a friend, likely forced to emigrate, recalling shared moments of hard labor. Hong Sun-ch'ŏl's poem, "Guardians of the Great Heart," is a rousing call for writers ("Men of letters") to act as vanguards, using their "pens" like weapons to chronicle the struggles of the present and herald a brighter future for the masses.

Historically, this piece is interesting for several reasons. Im Hwa's review reflects the leftist literary debates of the 1930s, prioritizing social realism ("content") and contemporary relevance ("theme") over purely aesthetic concerns ("poetic technique," "language"). His critique of Yi Koryŏ's poem suggests a desire for art that not only reflects but also deeply analyzes and conveys the emotional weight of social issues like forced migration, a significant reality for colonial Koreans. Hong Sun-ch'ŏl's poem, with its militant metaphors ("spear point," "bugle") and address to "Men of letters" as "Guardians," exemplifies the era's politicized view of literature's role in social change and national consciousness, even if presented somewhat abstractly.

「移民」選后感

林和

弟게 보내주신 詩稿 이제야 겨우 보냄니다. 대체로 훌늉한 것이 업섯슴니다. 弟에게 온 것이 특히 그런지도 모르겟슴니다만 정말 衷心으로 이것이면 하고 貴誌에 推薦할 勇氣를 갓게 하는게 적엇슴니다. 그중에 겨우 한아 정한 것이 李高麗라는 이에 「移民」이란 一篇임니다. 다음에 소위, 言語, 作詩術 기타로 보아 洪淳哲氏(이분에 작품은 전에도 본 일이 잇는대 「新東亞」 6월호에 실엇든 「鄕愁」란 작품보다 떠러지는 것 갓슴니다. 또 內容의 空虛, 言語의 抽象性, 形象性과, 특히 詩的情操의 缺如등으로 보아 前者들 一席으로 택하고 後者를 次席으로 하엿슴니다. 「移民」을 一席으로 한 推薦理由는 대개 아래와 갓흔데 잇다고 생각함니다.
이 詩는 一言으로 말하면 조흔 내용을 서투른 말로 노래한 詩임니다. 그러나 이 詩를 取한 이유가 전혀 그 「테-마」에만 잇는 것은 아님니다. 비록, 言語撰擇의 秩序라든가, 語音의 그 自然한 結合, 특히 散文의 無秩序한 混用에 의한 「리듬」의 破損이 不少한데 불구하고 이 詩의 形式的特點은 그것이 內容現實의 복잡한 性質에 즉하야 이 얘기되고 잇다는 一點임니다. 이점에서 「移民」은, 내게 온 9篇의 詩가운대 무조건적으로 웃뜸가는 작품임니다. 정히 오늘날 우리 生活의 形態와 性質이란, 이러한 生命잇는 現代的言語에 의한 고도의 觀念的 「리듬」아니고는 詩的으로 形象化할 수 업슬 것임니다. 오즉 이러한 곳에서 가장 잇기 쉬웁고, 또 致命的인 缺陷은, 言語와 「리듬」의 簡潔의 상실 그것임니다. 「移民」가운데도 역시 이 결점은 치명적임니다. 그러나, 缺陷을 무서워, 安易을 찻는 것은 오늘날의 詩人의 취할 배 아닌가 함니다. 우리 朝鮮의 만흔 新人들은 이 言語上에 잇서 最低抵抗線을 것고 잇슴니다. 오즉 躍動하고 生生한 생활의 呼吸을 전하는 言語가 아니라, 觀照的, 諦觀的, 엿흔 感傷性이 詩化에 적응한 言語가 流行하고 잇는데서 이러한 勢力을 발생하는 것은 그 缺陷의 如何를 불구하고 저는 敬意를 표함니다.<238> 뿐만 아니라, 이 詩의 「테-마」는 第一流的임니다. 이 詩의 「테-마」가 보혀주는 것 갓흔 現實은 최근의 우리 생활중의 가장 만흔 事實이며, 이런 事實의 압헤 가슴의 물결을 혀치지 못함은 현대 詩人에 恥辱임니다.
이러한 內容에 이러한 言語의 特點은 順理的인 것으로, 조흔, 內容, 조흔 意識만이, 조흔 言語를 가질 수 잇는 것임니다.
그러나, 言語上의 弱點은, 그 詩人의 「現實에 대한 態度에 弱點으로부터 由來함을 이 作者는 銘記해 주기를 바람니다.
이 詩는 곳 이 사실에 한개 正直한 反映임니다.
즉 「移民」의 現實에 대한 作家의 向心及, 把握의 態度는, 그리 的確치 안슴니다.
즉 이 詩에 內容的 事實에는 만흔 人爲的 複雜化가 잇고, 그 悲慘이라든가 억울함에, 마음의 臭處로부터 情感하지 못한 것 갓슴니다. 그럼으로, 때때로, 安逸한 조작된 觀念으로 現實우를 다름질치고 잇습니다. 따라서 이것이 그 내용으로 보아 讀者에게 반듯이 綿綿 絶絶한 무엇을 전할 것임에 불구하고 印象이 희미하고 읽고나서 統一한 무엇을 콱 가슴에 안키기 어려운 散慢을 가젓슴니다. 이 現實에 대한 散文的素朴性 그것이야말노 이 作者로 하야금 深廣한 內容을 가진 容量的인 詩語와 아무러치도 안흔 한 구절의 말에서 가슴을 찟는 듯한 거대한 感情을 전하는, 강한 聯想性잇는 言語를 擇하지 못하게 한 것임니다. 詩語는 모든 文學語가운데서 가장 만흔 聯想性을 가진 言語이여야 할 것임에 불구하고 그럿치 못햇슴은-이 作家가, 한가지를 가지고 百을 說明하고, 現實가운데 한 個核心的이고 典型의 性質을 가진 現象을 가리어 내지 못한 것과 不可分의 것임니다.
이 素朴한 讀後感으로 推薦의 말에 대신하며 氏의 精進을 빔니다. <239>
