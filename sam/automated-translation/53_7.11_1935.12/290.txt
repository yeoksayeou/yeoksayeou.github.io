Shoes, Yŏngae, and the Waitress
						By Yi Sŏgu

"It looks like surgery is necessary."
The doctor, after mercilessly pressing her thigh with his fingertips, spoke heavily.
"What! Surgery?"
"Yes."
"Ah! Cut here?"
She pointed to her plump thigh with a tearful expression.
"If we don't cut, it won't heal. The bone is decaying."
Yŏngae closed her eyes tightly. Her pretty lips pursed completely. Her curly, tousled hair shook from side to side.
"No! No!"
It was almost a childish tantrum. Yŏngae's mother wished <178> to proceed with the cutting quickly. But she was swayed by her daughter's stubbornness.
"Child, stop this foolishness and have the surgery quickly so you can get better, shouldn't you?"
"Mommy!"
Yŏngae shouted. Yŏngae was the only daughter of a widow. Her childish way of speaking remained, calling her mother "Mommy." She slowly opened her closed eyes. Bead-like pupils shone between long eyelashes!
"So even you, Mother, are telling me to scar my body?"
It was an unreasonable complaint. But for her now, pain—death—those were secondary issues. How regrettable and mortifying it must be to have a knife scar left on the body she had cherished and cared for so preciously.
"But what can we do..."
Her mother sighed deeply.
"I'll just die."
Yŏngae jumped down from the examination table.
She insisted she would rather let the bone decay and die than scar her body. The doctor, calmly observing Yŏngae's attitude, was a seasoned veteran.
"You shouldn't be so stubborn. Nowadays, surgeons are very careful about leaving scars, especially when operating on the bodies of pretty young ladies like yourself! You can rest completely assured on that point, so please undergo the surgery."
At these words, Yŏngae, who was about to run out, stopped in her tracks. Head bowed, she said nothing. The doctor, as if soothing an angry person, gently took Yŏngae's shoulders and pulled her,
"Now—come here and lie down. Let's take a closer look."
A sense of prudence flowed from the smooth words coming through the middle-aged doctor's beard.
"Well—this doctor is quite something—saying he cares about feminine physical beauty. Maybe I should listen to what he has to say."
She knew well that the illness had to be treated anyway, and that surgery was necessary to cure it. But not knowing how the doctor would cut was her biggest worry.
"Now—lie still. I'll use the finest scalpel. And I'll make the incision as short as possible."
The doctor coaxed her gently, as if persuading a child.
"Then just cut a tiny little bit, okay?"
"Yes—don't worry."
Yŏngae's cherished, plump nineteen-year-old legs were bared up past her waist. Lying face down again as if dead, Yŏngae's breathing even became quiet. Just as she was usually stubborn, she was also decisive. It seemed she was clenching her teeth, resigned to death.
"Now! Just bear it for a moment." <179>
The doctor prepared to operate, and the nurse began to gently hold down Yŏngae's legs.
"Doctor!"
A single cry from Yŏngae, who had been lying as if dead.
"Yes?"
"Please wait just a moment."
She sat up abruptly again. Oh dear, it's off again!
"Mommy!"
"What!"
"Lend me your ear!"
She whispered into her mother's ear. Her mother, looking utterly bewildered, wondered what on earth she was planning to do so suddenly.
"Mommy, your cute daughter is pleading, aren't you talking too much?"
Yŏngae always swayed her mother as she pleased using phrases like 'cute daughter' and 'pleading'.
"Uh—Doctor, I'm terribly sorry, but could you possibly postpone the surgery for a moment while I go home and come back?"
The mother, bound by love for her daughter, pleaded with the doctor, just as her daughter had requested. The doctor had seen Yŏngae's flustered state, as if some emergency had been resolved but time was running short.
"Yes, please do."
He gave his consent without knowing the reason. As the mother stepped outside the hospital door, he called her back.
"Please take a rickshaw."
After her mother left, Yŏngae glanced sideways at the infected boil, as if looking at something frightening.
"Uh—Doctor."
She called.
"Yes."
"Please tell me the absolute truth..."
"It is the truth. Do doctors lie?"
"Exactly where and how far will you cut?"
"Well, let's see!"
"Please don't be like that, tell me clearly."
"It will probably start from here and need to be cut up to here!"
He drew a line about two chi (寸 - approx. 6 cm) long, starting from the bone supporting her waist. She, who he expected to be startled, was surprisingly calm.
"Yes—okay."
Looking at the nurse, she said,
"Uh—excuse me, could you please bring me a brush dipped in ink?"
The nurse, finding it exceedingly strange, ran off as if witnessing some spectacle and returned with a brush dipped in ink.
"Doctor, please draw a line exactly where you will cut."
"Ah—draw an ink line on the sterilized area?"
"Oh! We can just wipe it off and sterilize it again. Please, I'm pleading."
When this young lady wanted to push her stubbornness through quickly, the singular phrase 'pleading' <180> would emerge.
"My, what a prankster."
The doctor, amused, wanting to see what would happen, drew the ink line for her.
"Cut exactly this long, right on this spot."
"Yes!"
"Thank you."
"Shall we cut then?"
"No. Please wait just a moment."
Her mother returned. She came in carrying a bundle, glancing sideways at her daughter. Yŏngae, with an embarrassed expression, glanced furtively at the doctor several times and then said,
"Please step outside for a moment."
The doctor was chased out. Only then did she unwrap the bundle. It was the swimsuit she had dried and put away last summer. She shook out the swimsuit and put it on. She wanted to know if the ink line drawn by the doctor showed below the edge of the swimsuit or not.
"If the scar shows outside the swimsuit, I'd rather die than have surgery!"
"Ah—look! Can you see it? It's fine now."
Her mother, eyes wide with relief, rejoiced first.
"What—it doesn't show?"
"No."
"It's perfectly placed. Look—see how the hem covers the ink line exactly?"
The nurse chimed in supportively.
"Let me see for myself."
She lay down flat on the operating table as if she were on the white sands of Wŏnsan beach, stretched out her legs, and looked down at them. Heaven's help! Indeed, the ink line the doctor had drawn did not appear outside the swimsuit.
"Uh, Doctor!"
"Yes."
"Please come back in and do the surgery!"
Nowadays, surgeons treating young ladies aren't doctors. They are beauticians.

"Yŏngae got her leg cut"!
The hundred or so waitresses at Café 'Eden' began whispering in every corner.
"Maybe it's because she couldn't take care of herself properly."
"Seriously, what happened? Why would a boil appear on her thigh, of all places?"
"Exactly. They say it festered from the inside, isn't that strange?"
"It is strange. How suspicious!"
As they were girls in their blossoming late teens, pursuing the 'tips' handed over by frivolous men under the neon lights, it was inevitable that they would first think of relationships with men in connection with anything that happened. However, according to the doctor's diagnosis, the cause of the illness was definitely not men. It was said to be 'cow' (소 - so). A strange diagnosis indeed, that the cause of a waitress's thigh festering from within was a 'cow'. Hearing this, the mother sighed.
"See? That's why I told you not to wear them." <181>
"But everyone else is wearing new shoes; should I be the only one wearing old ones?"
For Yŏngae, being less than others was worse than death. The result was this situation.

There was a promise to go to the baseball game at Kyŏngsŏng Stadium (京城運動場) next Sunday, paired up together. Ahead of this, the eight 'fairies' of 'Eden' had been vying with each other, planning how to dress up. Of course, Yŏngae, tormented by this, ended up ordering yet another pair of shoes, even though she already had several. The shoes, promised by ten o'clock on Sunday, were thirteen minutes late. Yŏngae sent the maid out twice, and finally even dispatched her mother, barely managing to retrieve the new-style shoes she had found in a Western magazine. She was already wearing silk stockings.
"Now—give me the shoes. Let's put them on quickly and go!"
Thus began the relationship between the 'cow' leather and Yŏngae's feet. But the result—
"Ouch!"
A single cry! With her right foot barely inside the shoe, she lifted it sharply, struggling and shaking it.
"What's wrong?"
The young shop assistant who had brought the shoes stared wide-eyed.
"My heel is pinched, it hurts."
"Oh—that's terrible."
The assistant habitually scratched his head.
"What should we do? Time is running out."
Yŏngae genuinely looked like she was about to cry. Her mother, unable to watch any longer, coaxed her.
"What can we do? Even if they're old shoes, they're barely worn. Just wear the ones you have today and ask them to remake those shoes."
"Mother, you don't understand. I'd rather die than not wear new shoes."
The 'I'd rather die' phrase was a sure way to shut her mother up.
"Die or live, what can you do if they don't fit?"
"Agh, so frustrating!"
Despite the pain, she forced her left foot into the other shoe.
"Ouch—ouch."
Unable to straighten her legs and wincing, she still took a few steps across the wooden floor. The assistant, feeling terribly sorry, said,
"If it really hurts, perhaps you shouldn't wear them."
"Don't say things you don't understand."
With that sharp retort, the assistant just stepped aside. In front of thousands of spectators, she had the burning ambition to stride gracefully on her slender legs, more confident than anyone in her high-heeled new shoes. Enduring the foot pain seemed necessary, a grim determination took hold.
"It's okay. Just leave them and go."
The assistant, standing awkwardly, fled as if escaping after being caught and released. <182>
"Mother, I'll be back."
Yŏngae limped out the main gate.
"Child, will you be alright?"
Her mother followed her to the gate, worried.
"Don't worry."
Yŏngae headed towards 'Eden', shifting her painful legs. Indeed, that day, Yŏngae's leg-flaunting, shoe-wearing appearance stood out from the rest. But that night, she couldn't go out to the 'hall' because her feet hurt too much. The heel that had been pinched by the back of the shoe was completely chafed and raw. Still, for a few days, feeling stifled, she limped out onto the streets. However, less than ten days later, her left thigh began to ache from within—eventually, it festered.
The infection that entered through the wound on her heel had crept up the lymphatic vessel (림파선) to her thigh and finally began to fester externally – that was the verdict she received.
"Damn him. Giving me shoes like that!"
Yŏngae, lying in bed, cursed the shoemaker every time she tended to the wound, receiving gauze ('까제' - kaje, from 'Gaze').
"Stop it. If you hadn't gone out wearing them that day, this wouldn't have happened."
Her mother scolded her every time. At night, her mother placed her bed in the next room to watch over her sick daughter. Waking her tired, sleeping mother each night required considerable effort from Yŏngae. So, inevitably, they tied a thread to her mother's toe, draped the end over to Yŏngae's bed, and agreed that Yŏngae would pull the thread if anything urgent happened.
"But unless it's really urgent, don't shake me awake."
Her mother strictly instructed. Indeed, on the first night, when the night was deep, an emergency signal came through to the mother's toe. The mother, startled, sat up.
"Child. What is it?"
Her eyes were wide.
"Ha!"
"What do you mean, 'Ha!'"
"It's funny. You get up just like a machine."
"Oh, honestly, this girl!"
But her daughter was always cute in her eyes. She went again to her daughter's bedside, wiped the sweat from her forehead, looked at her wan cheeks, and her eyes welled up with tears.
"That shoemaker is a wicked man too."
Even the mother ended up hating the shoemaker.

After 60 days, she was finally discharged from the hospital. Not discharged, really, but ran away. Firstly, there was no money; secondly, she felt stifled; thirdly, it seemed like it would heal gradually anyway—money had been lacking from the start. The family had been managing rent,<183> electricity bills, rice store payments, and firewood shop payments with the three or five won Yŏngae earned each evening at 'Eden'. Now that the earner couldn't earn and was instead spending money, there was no way to endure. The wardrobe bought on a four-month installment plan was sold, the cherished gramophone (축음긔) was sold, silk clothes and even cotton clothes were pawned, and money was borrowed from every possible source. Now, the widow mother and her spoiled daughter were on the verge of being left destitute in the wide world.
"Mommy, let's leave the hospital. It's mostly healed now, so I can go home and commute for treatment."
They couldn't stay longer anyway, as the hospital fees were overdue. With downcast, embarrassed faces, mother and daughter held onto each other, left the hospital, and returned desolately to their rented house in Kwanhun-dong (관훈동).
"Mommy. Why has our house become like this?"
Yŏngae collapsed onto the bedding bundle she brought from the hospital and wept at the edge of the wooden floor. No furniture, no proper bedding, even the electricity was cut off—an empty house! Starting tonight, Yŏngae had to survive again in this empty place.
"Child, your leg will hurt. Come—let's go into the room and lie down."
Yŏngae, held by her mother, collapsed just inside the warmest part of the main room, still dressed. A bundle of firewood, a measure of rice, a few side dishes—the last things her mother managed to procure. While her mother cooked dinner in the kitchen, she wept in the room. Discharged after two months, there was no one to welcome her back, only the dusty corners of the empty rented house, perhaps only the creditors (빚장이들) were 'happy' to see them?
"Oh well—I have to earn money again. There's nothing else."
As soon as the day broke, on her way to the hospital, she first stopped by a bar called 'K'uin' (쿠인). The owner of this place had approached Yŏngae several times while she was at 'Eden', asking her to come work for him, offering high pay.
"Ah—when were you discharged?"
"Uh, yesterday."
"Are you all better now?"
"Just about, not completely. But I can walk around and get treatment easily enough."
"That's wonderful."
"Um, would you hire me?"
"Ah—but you're sick—"
"It's alright."
"If it's alright, come today."
"Then, could you advance me some money?"
"How much?"
"One hundred Won!"
"One hundred Won?"
"Is it too much?"
"It's not too much, but I don't have it right now."
Yŏngae finally felt relieved. She had been anxious, afraid he would say she wasn't even worth a hundred Won advance.
"Then please give me just a little for now, and the rest later is fine too."<184>
"Ah—if so, that would be really great. Then how much for today?"
"Just twenty-five Won!"
She was about to ask for fifty but worried he might say he was short of cash again, causing mutual embarrassment, so she asked only for enough money to buy clothes first. It was a tearful investment for a body setting out to earn money.
"Yes. I'll give it to you right away."
And so, Yŏngae, unable to use one leg properly, immediately went and bought an outfit, silk stockings, and a carton of cigarettes for her mother, then returned home.
"Mommy. I'll earn for you again, please make these clothes for me, even if it takes all night."
Yŏngae watched her mother sewing clothes by candlelight, feeling as if she were watching some tragic scene from a movie, pulled the blanket over her head, and fell asleep. The next morning, she finally went to the dressing table to put on makeup, only to find empty cosmetic cases. Having no choice, wearing the new clothes, new stockings, and the new shoes her regular shoemaker had apologetically remade and left for her, she ran to the beauty parlor (미장원). After getting her hair done, facial hair removed, and new makeup applied, she emerged feeling no envy towards the beautiful and wealthy daughter of a millionaire.
"This is enough, Yŏngae is alive again."
Her sad thoughts blew away like the wind, and she felt she could once again reign confidently over the streets of Chongno. That night, the owner of 'K'uin' came to value Yŏngae's shining popularity even more highly. He felt it would be a waste to send her elsewhere. His intention was to tie her down with money.
"Here, take the rest of the money and use it."
He respectfully presented a one hundred Won bill. It wasn't the remainder. It was calculated as giving another hundred Won anew.
"Thank you."
Yŏngae nodded her thanks and accepted the money. That night, more than anything else, she first went to Ponjŏng (본정) and bought a French doll (불난서 인형). She paid the electricity bill. The brilliance of the 16-candlepower light bulb, seen after two months! She was so happy to see the light that she exclaimed,
"Mother, now look—just flick the switch (스잇치) like this, and the light turns on!"
She played with the light switch several times.
"Now—I'll cover the bedding with the new bedsheet cover (시기후), put on the new pajamas (파자마), and sleep hugging the French doll."
The mother watched her only daughter's peaceful sleep after two months. On her sleeping face, a cheerful glow, unseen for months, finally shone. In this youth, this beauty, it seemed there was a house, land, and even a fine son-in-law. <185>

**Summary**

This 1935 short story by Yi Sŏgu depicts the life of Yŏngae, a young, vain café waitress (yŏgŭp) in colonial Seoul. Driven by a desire to wear new, fashionable shoes to a baseball game despite the pain, she develops a severe leg infection from a blister. Her initial refusal of surgery due to fear of scarring, the ensuing financial ruin for her and her widowed mother during her hospitalization, and her eventual return to the nightlife, securing an advance from a bar owner to buy clothes and cosmetics, paint a picture of her precarious existence, balancing vanity, poverty, and the demands of her profession.

The story is interesting for colonial history as it vividly portrays the intersection of emerging consumer culture (Western shoes, swimsuits, makeup, cafés, baseball), modern aspirations (desire for fashion and social visibility), and the harsh economic realities faced by young women like Yŏngae in urban colonial Korea. The role of the 'yŏgŭp', the casual use of loanwords, the mention of specific Seoul locations like Kwanhun-dong and Ponjŏng (Honmachi), and the underlying themes of female agency (however constrained), vanity versus survival, and generational dependence offer valuable insights into the social fabric and cultural landscape of 1930s Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul).

「안만해도 수술을 해야겟슴니다.」
넙적다리를 손 끗으로 인정없시 눌너 보고난 의사는 무겁게 입을 여럿다.
「머! 수술요.」
「네.」
「아! 여긔를 째요.」
토실토실한 자긔의 넙적다리를 울 듯한 표정을 하며 가릇컷다.
「째지 안으면 낫지 안슴니다. 뼈각 썩은 것임니다.」
英愛는 고만 눈을 감앗다. 어엿분 입살이 모조리 음츠러 드럿다. 곰슬곰슬 지저흣튼 머리가 좌우로 흔들닌다.
「시려! 시려!」
거의 어린광이다. 英愛의 어머니는 어서<178> 째고 십흔 편이다. 그러나 딸의 고집에 끌닌다.
「이애 그 철업는 소리 말고 어서 수술을 하고 하로밧비 나아야 하지 안늬.」
「엄니!」
英愛는 소리를 질넛다. 英愛는 과부의 외딸이다. 어리광 피든 말시가 그저 나마 어머니를 「엄니」하고 부른다. 그는 감앗든 눈을 가만히 떳다. 긴 눈섭 사히로 되록되록하는 구슬가튼 눈동자!
「그래 어머니까지 날더러 몸에다가 험집을 내시우.」
생트집이다. 그러나 지금의 그에게는 아픈 것-죽는 것-그런 것은 뎨2의 문제이다. 앗겨라 고와라고 위해 오든 몸에 칼자죽을 남기는 것이 얼마나 애석하고 원통하겟는가.
「그럿치만 엇더케 하니...」
어머니는 한숨을 길게 쉬엇다.
「그냥 죽지요.」

英愛는 병원에서 들고 온 이불보통이 우에 업듸려 마로끗에서 우럿다. 세간도 업고 이불자리 조차 업고 전등도 끈힌 채 텅부인 집! 오늘밤부터 英愛는 이 빈터에서 다시 사라나지 안으면 안이된다.
「아가 다리켱긴다. 자-어서 방으로 드러가 눕자.」

「오-냐 돈을 또 버러야한다. 그 외에는 아모 것도 업다.」
그는 날이 새자마자 병원에 가는 길에 몬저 『쿠인』이라는 빠-에를 들넛다. 이 집 주인은 英愛가 『에덴』에 잇을 때부터 고급을 줄게 와 달나고 여러번 교섭이 잇든 사람이다.

英愛는 겨오 안심하얏다. 자긔가 백원 선금짜리도 되지 못한다고 할가바 가슴이 저렷섯다.
「그럼 우선 조금만 주시고 남어지는 나종 주시어도 좃슴니다.」<184>

오십원이라고 브르랴다가 또 돈이 부족하다면 피차에 창피할가바 위선 옷해 입을 돈만을 달낸 것이다. 돈을 벌너 나스는 몸에 드리는 눈믈겨운 투자이다.
