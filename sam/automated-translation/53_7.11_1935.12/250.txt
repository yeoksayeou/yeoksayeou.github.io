Searching for the Girls' Schools of the Street (Part 2), The Tobacco Girls' School Singing the Tobacco Ditty
Around 700 Maidens in Their Twenties Gathered

Guya guya tobacco ya
Tobacco of Dongnae or Ulsan ya

Did you come bearing silver?
Did you come bearing gold?

No silver, no gold
I came bringing tobacco seeds
Yonder, yonder, at the foot of that mountain, scatter the tobacco seeds, whoosh whoosh
At night, taste the cold dew; by day, receive the yang energy<158>
Carefully, carefully raise them
To become the tobacco of this land
Green leaves, blue smoke
A hundred years of life are here
With a southern jacket and crimson skirt
You hold the basket
Raising the three-ch'ŏk long robe high
I pick the leaves
Under the fence, in the sunny spot
Diligently dry them out
Lighting one pipeful
All worldly affairs become a spring dream

Tobacco! This song, born from the tobacco that is handled countless times an hour by hand and smoked by mouth, is so famously and long imprinted deep within the cameras of our minds, and perhaps even more so. Perhaps there are few, young or old, who haven't heard at least a snatch of this song, even if just carried on the passing wind!
Why should only this song leave a deep impression on us? As if it has some inseparable relationship with us, tobacco never leaves our pockets, our fingertips, our lips. Not smoking it won't harm our lives or cause nutritional deficiency, yet for some reason
Sitting up from sleep, tobacco
Going to the outhouse, tobacco
After eating, tobacco
Sitting down face-to-face with someone, tobacco
In sadness, tobacco
In joy, tobacco
Thus, it is consumed by us, dozens of times a day, as if necessary. Many feel they cannot live even an hour without smoking.
Someone said, "People who don't smoke are too lonely." Someone else said, "Unless I light up a cigarette when facing someone, I can't start talking," and remarked that the taste of watching the smoke gently drift away after lighting one is quite exquisite. Yet another said, "There's no pleasure in sitting and associating with a miser who won't even offer a cigarette." Despite loving and praising tobacco like this, many probably don't know by whose fingertips and how it is made. The writer's intention here is to reveal the process by which the female workers of the tobacco manufacturing factory (Tobacco Girls' School) work to provide tobacco to us.

On the 8th of last month, when a chilly wind brushed past one's cheeks, I set out to find the Tobacco Girls' School, looking up at the sullen winter sky, like the eyes of a spiteful, wicked woman. First, to get some preliminary knowledge and statistical data about this Tobacco Girls' School, I knocked on the door of the General Affairs Section, Documents Desk (庶務課文書係) of the Government-General Monopoly Bureau. An attendant came out and asked whom I had come to meet. Handing over my business card, I requested an interview with Mr. Takemori (竹森). The attendant quickly ran off and returned, saying, <159> "Please come in," and guided me. The head of this Documents Desk, Mr. Takemori, greeted me, "Please come in. It's gotten very cold. So, what is your business?" After I explained my purpose, he readily took out statistical charts without hesitation, gave a brief explanation, and then wrote the following introduction on his business card: "Introducing Mr. XX. Please provide all necessary assistance. To Mr. Monma, Ŭiju-t'ong Factory Manager (門馬義州通工場長殿)." Clutching this card, I hurried to the Ŭiju-t'ong factory. As I arrived at the gate, the high wall surrounding the large brick building was as solid as a prison wall, built so that even a swift bird couldn't easily enter or exit. I asked the guard (守圍) where the office was. The guard asked, "Who are you, and whom have you come to meet?" Only then did I present the introduction card. He said, "Wait just a moment," went to the office, and returned after about five minutes, saying, "Please come in," and guided me. In the neatly arranged reception room, photos of the female workers making cigarettes were posted here and there, and fragrant white chrysanthemums (白色菊花) were placed on the reception table (應接테불). After 2 or 3 minutes, the factory manager entered. After exchanging greetings, we talked about the female workers, the students of this Tobacco Girls' School. As we were about to tour the factory work site, having drunk the tea brought by the attendant, the factory manager personally brought a box (函) of various cigarettes made that morning and offered me one to smoke. Gratefully lighting up a Kaida cigarette, we discussed the philosophy of tobacco a bit. Then, the factory manager introduced Mr. Takimoto Riichirō (瀧本利一郞) as a guide, saying, "Please learn the details from this gentleman while he guides you." Following this guide, I moved towards the factory floor.

Seven Hundred Maidens in Uniforms and Caps

Stepping over the threshold of the workshop, the factory floor, I felt as if all the young women of Seoul city had gathered here. There were full of young ladies, about 17 or 18 years old, like beautiful flower buds blooming in a spring garden. It felt as if I had arrived in a country inhabited only by women. When I asked the guide the total number of female workers, he said there were seven hundred. Most are from this Kyŏnggi Province. Seated on chairs arranged neatly in rows in a place better and cleaner equipped than any school classroom, they worked vigorously, not showing the tired look common among other factory workers. This sight evoked a unique feeling in me that day. Dressed neatly in snow-white *bogoji* caps and grey work uniforms, like nurses in a hospital, they sat and worked in an orderly fashion, seemingly fulfilling some human duty and responsibility. I wanted to express more respect for these students of the Tobacco Girls' School than for the women who spend several tens of won a month attending high-collar schools (하이깔나학교). Glancing at me furtively, <160> they worked with vitality, as if saying, "We live by our own labor, without any shame in this world and without relying on others." This scene unexpectedly stirred my heart with joy. I felt immense respect for them. So, what time do they clock in and until what time do they work? And how much pay do they receive? I will write this down below.

Ten Hours for Forty-Two Won Nine Chŏn per Month

They start work at eight in the morning and return home only at eight in the evening. During the workday, they don't go outside but stay inside. In this season, this means leaving before dawn and returning after dark. Out of these ten working hours, one hour is for rest, divided into three breaks: Morning, Noon, and Afternoon. They also get days off for national holidays, the anniversary of the city's founding, year-end and New Year (6 days), and also the first and second days of the lunar New Year. If there's a death anniversary of a parent or a death in the family (喪事), they are given three days of leave. Also, if they are unavoidably unable to come to work due to epidemics or natural disasters, an organization called the Incentive Association (獎勵會) pays half a day's wages. For all the above absences, wages are paid even if they don't attend work fully. However, one thing worth noting is the difference in monthly salaries. Based on the salary amounts at the end of March this year, the highest is 42 won 9 chŏn, and the lowest is 8 won 7 chŏn. There is no other reason for this than that, like a machine running automatically, skilled workers who work quickly receive higher wages based on their work efficiency performance, while those who are newly hired or are lazy and cannot produce efficiently see their wages drop accordingly. Listening to the guide's detailed explanation, I proceeded to the first room, the "Leaf Sorting" (葉紹) room.

Skilled Worker Handles 2,800 Packs a Day by Hand; Machine Produces 1,000 Cigarettes per Minute

In this "Leaf Sorting" room are tobacco leaves gathered from various places. Manchurian, Chinese, US Mainland (米國內地), and Korean (朝鮮) products are said to be the most numerous. These tobacco leaves are untied from large bales, put into a large machine container, and moistened with steam (水蒸氣). Then they are passed to the second room, "Stem Removal" (除骨), where female workers remove <161> the stems from these tobacco leaves with their delicate hands. If there is a saying about farmers, "Every single grain comes from hard labor" (粒粒皆辛苦), then for these female workers, there must be a saying, "Every single leaf comes from hard labor" (葉葉皆辛苦). Thousands of leaves are handled by their fingertips every day. I listened with interest to the guide's story about the large tobacco leaves that grew absorbing the earth's energy (地氣) from faraway places. Turning around again, I reached the third room, the "Cutting" (切刻) room. Tobacco bundles are fed into one side, and thinly shredded tobacco, like thread, comes out the other side. This is put into another large machine container, sprayed with perfume (香水) to enhance the tobacco's aroma, and then moved to the fourth room, the "Rolling" (卷上) room. Here too, large machines are set up. Shredded tobacco is fed into one side, and excellent cigarettes, neatly wrapped in white paper, come out the other side. This machine produces 1,000 cigarettes per minute. Hearing that even though hundreds of thousands of cigarettes are produced like this daily and shipped out by truck (도락구), it's still not enough, the staggering thought occurs that the number of cigarettes we smoke daily is quite substantial. Again, I reached the "Packing" (獎置) room on the 3rd floor. This place is indeed cleaner and quieter than any school. Looking from the north end to the south end, it seems dizzyingly far. As it was just when the sun was high in the middle of the sky, the sunlight streaming through the glass windows reflected off the white caps of the female workers sitting in rows, making them look just like clusters of white chrysanthemums (白菊花) blooming in some quiet garden field, neatly arranged and pleasant to see. At the head of the line, there is a machine; just feed in the rolled cigarettes, and they automatically come out as cigarette packs. The name of this machine is "Morins" (모린스), said to be the most ingenious in this factory. It is mainly used to make products like Mako or Maple, apparently. The reason is none other than that for high-grade products (高級品) like Kaida or Pigeon, machine production is clumsy, so only cheaper items are made by machine, while high-grade products wrapped in cellophane (세루판紙) are all made by the hands of the female workers. Each sits at a small table placed before her, perched on a chair, holding a machine about the size of a Kaida pack (匣) called a "Katari" (가다리) in her hand, wrapping the packaging so quickly one can hardly follow with the eyes. - It is said one person wraps 2,800 packs a day - There is no sound in this room except for the slight rustling sound of cellophane paper.
Turning my eyes to look at the types of cigarettes produced and laid out on one side, they are as follows:
For double-cut paper-wrapped cigarettes (兩切紙卷煙草), there are 8 types: G.G.C., Kŭmgang, Kaida, Pigeon, Ŭnha, Ran, Mako, Moktan. For rough-cut tobacco (荒刻煙草), there are 5 types: Purŏyŏn, Changsuyŏn, Hŭiyŏn, Pokch'o. For fine-cut (細刻), there are 2 types: Sakki, Ayame. A total of 15 types are made in this factory. After looking at the various kinds of cigarettes, as I turned around to go see the dining hall, the guide introduced one of the workers who was the prettiest <162> and most graceful, saying she is a model student, and if I had any questions, I should ask her. I began to probe (打珍) her state of mind.

A Q&A with Miss Yi Chŏng-sun, the Single Red Leaf Among Ten Thousand Leaves (萬葉叢中一葉紅)

This model student is Miss Yi Chŏng-sun (李貞順), whose family register is in Kwandong within the city and who has worked at this factory for a long time. If there is anyone who regrets not having seen Yang Kwei-fei (楊貴妃), famous for her beauty in China, I would not hesitate to recommend seeing Miss Yi Chŏng-sun at this factory as a lovely woman. She is a well-formed beauty, lacking nothing in face or figure. The guide initiated the conversation first, telling her to answer this guest's questions. Following this,
"Me": I'm very sorry to trouble you like this when you're busy.
"Yi": Not at all.
"Me": Isn't the work difficult?
"Yi": Well... I don't particularly feel it's difficult; time just passes.
"Me": When are you happiest?
"Yi": When I feel a bit tired from working, going up to the rooftop (屋上) and playing golf at the Pea-bee golf course (뼈-비 골푸장) is pleasant; and carrying my evening lunchbox (변도) home is joyful. Also, the two days a year the factory gives us off as a group are the happiest times. In spring, when various plants and trees turn green in the mountains and fields, and skylarks soar high in the sky singing, we go for a picnic (비구닉구) to Inch'ŏn, and that one day is truly joyful. And in autumn, when the cool breeze blows gently, the entire factory holds a large athletic meet at the Kyŏngsŏng Ground (京城구라운드), and that day is also joyful.
"Me": Then when are the sad times?
"Yi": Well... Sometimes sadness arises due to family matters or circumstances around me. But right now, I don't know what would be a good answer.
"Me": What about marriage in the future?
"Yi": Haha, how can you ask even that? I still don't know about such things.
And she lowers her slightly blushing face.
"Me": Well, I've taken up much of your time.
"Yi": "Not at all. Goodbye." As I looked at Miss Yi saying goodbye, the female workers sitting nearby asked, "Hey, Chŏng-sun, what did he say?" Their words struck my attentive (客耳三尺) nerve. I headed towards the dining hall with the guide.

Culturally Equipped Dining Hall and Lecture Hall

Opening the door, I entered a room as clean as a sanctuary (聖堂). It seemed capable of seating about a thousand people. The south corner was the Dining Hall, and the north corner was a Reading Room filled with newspapers and magazines. They supposedly sing folk songs like Hŭngt'aryŏng and the Tobacco Ditty here during breaks. Chairs were arranged in rows like in an assembly hall (公會堂). Food is sold cheaper <163> here than elsewhere, so while those who bring their own lunchboxes (변도) are a separate matter, those who don't mostly buy food here. And after finishing their meal, they browse through magazines and newspapers. The reason this room was made so large is none other than that there are occasional lectures (講化) on self-cultivation (修養) and worker consolation meetings (慰安會), all held in this lecture hall. They comfort the tired workers with entertaining movies (映畵) and invite famous speakers (名士) to improve (行狀) the workers' self-cultivation. One more thing to mention here is that although most have received education beyond elementary school, among these seven hundred, there are a few who have not graduated from elementary school (普通學校). Thus, this factory has set up separate classrooms (敎室) and hired teachers (先生) to provide education equivalent to elementary school for those who haven't graduated. On this day too, when we reached this place, teachers and students (生徒) were coming out of the classroom. In any case, one cannot help but say that this factory is completely equipped as a factory. The guide then showed me the bathing facilities (沐浴湯), laundry room (洗濯所), etc., and guided me to the rooftop Pea-bee golf course.

Daily Radio Calisthenics on the Rooftop

On this rooftop, a Pea-bee golf course was built in the south corner. Workers come here to play golf during holidays or rest periods during work. On one side of the golf course, benches (뼌치) are arranged in a square, like a rest area set up in a park. I sat down on a bench with the guide. The guide pointed to the spacious rooftop (屋上) exercise ground and said that early in the morning before starting work, over a thousand male and female workers and staff members do morning exercises here, following the radio (라듸오). Also, when there is instruction (訓話) for all workers or special notices, they assemble (朝會) here, and the factory manager gives the instruction. After walking around this rooftop exercise ground once, observing the surrounding scenery, I came back down to the staff office.

Romance Between Male and Female Workers Watched, But Relationships with Outsiders Ignored

Returning to the office, the factory manager asked me if I had a good tour. After replying that I had seen everything well with great thanks, I continued,
"Me": If something like a romantic relationship arises between male and female workers, what do you do?
"Manager": Since that is a matter of public morals (風紀上), we pay close attention as much as possible.
"Me": If a definite deep relationship is discovered, are there cases of dismissal or punishment?
"Manager": We don't dismiss or punish them, but we change their workplaces (職業場所) so they cannot meet frequently.
"Me": Then what about romantic relationships with outsiders (外人) outside the gate?
"Manager": Unless there were specific dormitories (寄宿舎) here, <164> we don't know, but currently there are no dormitories, so we don't make an issue of it.
"Me": If you just leave it alone (放任), and it goes beyond a certain limit (限界), causing her to gain a bad reputation (評判) or damage the company's reputation (體面), what happens?
"Manager": Anyway, we do not interfere at all with matters arising outside this factory.
"Me": What happens if female workers get married while working at this factory?
"Manager": If she quits voluntarily, we can't help it, but the Factory does not tell her to quit. However, most of them quit after getting married.
"Me": (Pointing to the women resting outside in the courtyard) It looks like they came outside because it's break time now, right?
"Manager": That's right. It's break time now. However, the budget is still insufficient, so we haven't been able to equip everything as desired. In the future, if the budget allows, we plan to create a recreation ground (遊園地) in the field and expand the Factory further.
"Me": And are there no other facilities you plan to install?
"Manager": Besides that, everything else is already installed. Did you see? Over there (pointing with his hand), if you go around, there is the medical office (醫務室). We have four contracted doctors (囑託醫師) and two nurses (看護婦) to provide treatment at company expense (官費) if someone gets injured while working or falls ill.
"Me": Are the work uniforms (作業服) worn by the female workers provided free of charge?
"Manager": That's right. Even the footwear (신발) is given free.
"Me": Then, the medical office, education room, recreation facilities (娯楽場), bathing room, laundry facilities, the holding of lectures (講話會) and movie screenings (映畵會) are ultimately all for promoting the workers' welfare (便宜), I suppose.
"Manager": That's right. Is that all? We also look after the workers' welfare in various ways under names like line-of-duty death payment (殉職金), medical treatment payment (治療金), childbirth payment (産婦金), sickness payment (疾病金), death payment (死亡金), disaster payment (災害金), withdrawal payment (脱退金), and long-service payment (勤続金). Following these words, I said, "I have caused much trouble in various ways. Well then, I shall take my leave for today." Stepping out of the Tobacco Girls' School office, the sun had already passed noon (午正) and begun to tilt towards the western sky.

Riding the streetcar back, I thought once more about the sight of them working energetically. More than anything, recalling the factory manager's earlier mention of occasionally holding lectures on self-cultivation, the principles of the Suyangdan (修養團), a "Sweat and Love" (汗愛) doctrine group, came to mind. "Train through sweat" (流汗鍛練), "Love fellow countrymen" (同胞相愛), "Ensure not a single person fights in our village. Ensure not a single person is lazy in our village." Perhaps they were moved by lectures emphasizing hard work and abandoning reliance on others, inspired by those who are promoting the "White Ethics" (白色倫理) movement in this land with these principles? <165> In any case, the sight of them working vigorously, seemingly fulfilling all human duties and even forgetting their hardships, made me reflect once more. If everyone in the world lived like the principles of this Suyangdan, without anyone living idly, but working diligently with sweat on their brow, or if people lived helping and loving each other instead of deceiving, fighting, and stealing from one another, wouldn't that indeed be an ideal world? O, all women of this world! All you idlers (遊食輩)! Live diligently and work hard like the women of this Tobacco Girls' School, I say!<166>

**Summary**

The article reports on a visit to the Government-General Monopoly Bureau's tobacco factory in Ŭiju-t'ong, Seoul, metaphorically called the "Tobacco Girls' School." It details the working lives of the approximately 700 young female employees, describing their 10-hour workday (8 AM to 8 PM), wages ranging from 8 to 42 won based on skill and productivity, and various company benefits like holidays, sick leave compensation, medical care, free uniforms, recreational facilities (including a rooftop golf course), educational opportunities for less-educated workers, and various financial aids. The reporter interviews a "model worker," Yi Chŏng-sun, and observes the highly organized, clean, and disciplined environment, contrasting the diligence of these working women with idle elites. The reporter concludes by exhorting readers, especially women and "idlers," to emulate the workers' industriousness, linking it to the principles of hard work and mutual help promoted by groups like the Suyangdan. From a historical perspective, the article offers insight into the industrial workforce in colonial Korea, specifically female labor under Japanese management. It highlights the paternalistic control ("cultural rule") aspects, blending modern factory systems, welfare provisions, and disciplinary measures with moral exhortations towards productivity and self-improvement, reflecting colonial industrial policies and social discourses of the time. The detailed description of factory processes, wages, benefits, and the mention of specific cigarette brands provide valuable data for social and economic historians.
