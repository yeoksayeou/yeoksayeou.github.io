「奈落」選後感 (Thoughts After Selecting "Narak")

Ch'unwŏn Yi Kwangsu (春園 李光洙)

Although I was entrusted with recommending a work, unfortunately, among those I reviewed, neither the novels nor the poems were fully formed. Among them, Mr. Yi Yongu's (李龍雨氏) "Narak" (奈落) is the best, but it still lacks refinement in literary style, description, etc. However, his way of focusing on life is very sincere (眞摯), offering hope for the future. As an observer of life, he has grasped the knack of depicting a slice of living existence. His treatment of the love affair, the illness incident, etc., is quite immature (稚拙), but still, the protagonist's morbid personality and his parents' psychology, typical of old Chosŏn (朝鮮) parents, are expressed simply (素樸).

One should not try to forcefully fabricate novels or scripts. Literary creation is not about concocting entertaining stories or tales satirizing some ideology. Only that which gives the feeling of having cut out a side or a piece of life and presented it before the reader's eyes is truly literary creation. Literature is something composed yet uncomposed. Composing as if uncomposed is literary creation.

So, what is a side or a piece of life? It is the inevitable comedic or tragic result arising from the reactions of several personalities centered around a certain event, and the realistic, yet selective—maximally compressed and focused—description of the path leading to that result. From this comes verisimilitude (逼眞性). A sense of reality emerges. Therefore, clearly depicting the personalities which are the cause of the event's causal development is the fundamental requirement.

However, looking at the several novels I reviewed, there was nothing clear regarding individuality (個性), the causal and inevitable development of events (因果的 必然的 展開), or the author's perspective (觀角) on life. To put it in a word (一言以敝之), it seemed the applicants whose works I saw were merely the first attempts (試作) of those without literary training (文藝修養). Among them,<188> I believe the author of "Narak" has at least grasped the essence of what literature (文藝) is.

Literature is also a kind of technique (技術). Technique arises only through sincere (誠意), long-term refinement (練磨), and refinement means the scholarly, careful reading (硏究的 精讀) of masters' works and actually writing a great deal. Furthermore, literature is an explanation of life, a warning (警告) and sermon (說敎) to life. It is a warning and sermon that does not take the form of a warning or sermon. Because of this, the literary artist must first acquire a view of life (人生觀). They must be the possessor of philosophy (哲學) or the possessor of faith (信仰). Even if the story is simple (平淡), if it is a work that the general public (一般大衆) could read or not read, it is a waste of life (人生浪費). Isn't the ultimate goal of all cultural activities a benefit (裨益) to life?

The current standard of literature in Chosŏn (朝鮮) is by no means particularly low compared to various foreign countries. If there is something low, it is the philosophy and faith of the literary artists, not the technique (技巧) of expression. Therefore, anyone wishing to stand in the literary world (文壇) of Chosŏn today must first learn the Korean language and script (朝鮮語文) and the techniques of Chosŏn literature before stepping forward. Trying to create literary works without knowing the conventions (結束) and rules (規則) regarding grammar (文法), spelling (綴字), and style (文體) is like trying to paint a picture for an exhibition (展覽會) without knowing the methods of light and shadow, color, and composition (明暗色彩配合法). We Chosŏn people have superior talent (재조), but there is a sense that we do not know the power of study (工夫). Literature, like music or painting, is a skill that improves only through sincere and long study. Moreover, because literature is conceptual art (觀念藝術), it requires understanding (通曉) of life's history, psychology, philosophy, faith, and customs (習慣), as well as the writer's own clear view of life. Therefore, those who aspire to literature must first study greatly.<189>

奈落 (Narak - Abyss/Hell)

Yi Yongu (李龍雨)

Hwang T'aewŏn's (黃泰元) father loved T'aewŏn boundlessly. There was not the slightest room for doubt about this. And it wasn't that T'aewŏn didn't know it, but for T'aewŏn to accept (甘受) that love felt exceedingly unpleasant, bitter, and like an insult to his self-esteem.

T'aewŏn could only think of his father's love for him as a love for self-satisfaction stemming from his selfish standpoint (利己的立場). Of course, this might be unconscious (無意識的), but perhaps it could sometimes stem from consciousness.

There could be no understanding (理解) in such love.—It is a love that fails to understand all of his son's tastes (趣味), thoughts (思想), principles (主義), and the like, and tries to assimilate (同化) all such things into the father's own.

T'aewŏn attended Y Professional School (Y專門學校). So he expected somewhat more treatment befitting a professional school student from his father and hoped for understanding. His stubborn and uncomprehending (沒理解)<190> father had no way of satisfying him. No matter how much his son grew, his father always thought of him as a two- or three-year-old child and treated him with a dogmatic (獨斷的) attitude and love.

But T'aewŏn could not accept this. He always suffered amid complaints and dissatisfaction.

T'aewŏn's father was even concerned about T'aewŏn playing with his friends (同毛). He intensely disliked T'aewŏn going out and friends coming to visit T'aewŏn.

When a friend who hadn't visited in a long time called from the gate, "T'aewŏn, T'aewŏn-gun," his father would look out from the window lattice and send him away, saying, "He went out."

"Why do you say I'm out when a friend calls?" T'aewŏn, his anger flaring up, would thrash about, his face looking like he was about to cry as he spoke irritably.

"You shouldn't associate with such bad friends. Just study at home. How can a student go around meeting friends just to drink? That's all delinquency (不良)."

When his father said this with an indifferent, calm attitude, T'aewŏn felt like fire was roaring up from his chest.

He felt a flame-like hatred (憎惡) and sank deep into an unbearable feeling of displeasure.

"Why is Father like that?"

Discontent and discomfort were clearly visible in his clear pupils. He slammed the front door hard. Along with the "bang" sound, the words "Why the door like that?" were heard from behind him.

T'aewŏn snorted "Hmph" and went into his room, slumping powerlessly at his desk.

(If you love your child, why can't you show a more understanding love—one that satisfies the child's heart and lets him stay home cheerfully?) He thought of this impossible hope, then scratched his head vigorously as if he had nothing better to do.

He felt infinite dissatisfaction and boredom (卷怠) being cooped up in his room every day after returning from school. He wanted to live a life with strong stimulation (刺戟). He hated spending day after day squirming like a maggot amidst boredom and dissatisfaction more than death itself.

He wanted to become the leader of bandits (馬賊), commit all sorts of evil deeds (惡漢), run freely across the vast, snow-covered plains, and wash away the pent-up gloom (鬱積) in his heart with the fiercely (凜冽) blowing north wind. Or else, take hundreds of thousands of won (十萬圓) and, where the sound of jazz (짜즈) rings out loudly, get drunk on strong liquor like brandy (푸란디-) or something that bites the tongue (舌) sharply—get drunk in his heart—fully indulge (滿喫) in the seductive laughter (妖笑) of a femme fatale (毒婦), and wander from a world of pleasure (歡樂) to seek another world of pleasure.<191>

He thought that without such a stimulating life, living had no meaning (意味).

T'aewŏn yawned wearily (屈託) and stretched his limbs, letting out a groan, "Eo-eo-dae-," then abruptly stood up and sat at his desk.

Not even two hours after dinner, it was already pitch black outside, as if ink had been poured.

As the unpleasant feelings towards his father gradually dissipated, he felt them changing into lonely solitude and sorrow (哀愁). T'aewŏn's pale face, illuminated by the electric light, took on an even deeper shade of melancholy (憂愁). He opened a book on the desk, flipped through it idly, and then his eyes fixed on one passage.

One
Beyond that mountain over there,
If you could guess what place lies,
Here, there is nothing but snow everywhere.
I want to go far away, without hesitation.
If I go, I'll go, surely I can, where did I come from?

Two
Beyond that mountain over there, black against the sky,
The eagle bravely soars!
Using all its blood-boiling young strength,
Chasing after them, charging forward,
Gazing at the distant riverbank.
(Remainder omitted - original text)
From Byŏryuson's "Arunae" (아루내)

After everyone fell asleep, the house was silent without any sound. Only the sound of crickets could be heard, plaintively and mournfully, as if pleading (哀訴), lamenting, or perhaps seeking something.

T'aewŏn had read the novel (小說) "Arunae" several times before.

The young poet (詩人) "Arunae," yearning for and dreaming of a southern land. "Arunae," who pines for the south but cannot abandon his mother, suffering in agony. But "Arunae" had a flower-like love for beautiful nature (自然) and God. But—T'aewŏn suddenly thought of himself—what do I have? In an environment of desolate solitude, I must spend the long days, one after another, amidst gloom, complaints, and anguish (煩悶)! I too want to go far away. Far, far away, to a place where the shadow of my father, and all other shadows, cannot be seen. T'aewŏn lifted his face, contorted with sentimentality (感傷).

A teardrop fell from his eye.

T'aewŏn woke from a deep sleep as the floorboards outside the door creaked. He half-opened his eyes, looked towards the door, thought, "Sounds like they're eating breakfast," closed his eyes again, and turned over, facing away from the door.<192>

The door facing southeast shone brightly, receiving the morning light. And the sunlight entering through the glass pane embedded in the door shone intensely on the head-side where T'aewŏn was sleeping. He closed his eyes again and tried to fall back asleep, but useless fantasies arose in his head, preventing sleep.

"Is T'aewŏn still sleeping? It's almost seven o'clock already."

His father's angry voice could be heard.

"Hmph, Father's nagging is starting again."

As soon as he opened his eyes, his mood soured, and he spent the entire day feeling unpleasant.

T'aewŏn forced his tired body up, took his toothbrush, and went outside with a face like someone who had drunk bitter medicine.

"It looks like it will be quite hot outside today too."

He thought as he drew water for washing from the faucet (水道).

Though he didn't even feel like eating, he forced down two or three spoonfuls just because he hated hearing the nagging, then picked up his bag and went out the door.

"Are you taking your lunchbox (밴또)?"

This was something he heard without fail every single morning. T'aewŏn awaited these words every morning, fearfully and with a sense of self-abandonment (自棄), like a prisoner awaiting the judge's verdict in court (法廷).

Although T'aewŏn didn't believe in superstitions (迷信)—and was absolutely opposed to them... he had the contradictory (矛盾) emotion of anticipating some pointless thing and firmly believing it would happen. So, although he felt unpleasant every time he heard the usual morning words, "Are you taking your lunchbox?", on the other hand, he felt relief (安心), thinking, "Today is safe."

He stepped out the main gate, head bowed low, his frowning face looking as if it had never once been smooth since he came into the world, and walked slowly towards the tram stop with listless steps.

At the tram stop, every morning without fail, he encountered a woman smartly dressed in Western clothes (洋裝), like a female office worker (女事務員). Upon seeing her, T'aewŏn finally smoothed his furrowed brow (상판) and his face took on a cheerful glow.

That woman—her eyebrows drawn long and slender, her lustrous, tender (艶艶) eyes, her smoothly high nose, her reddish lips, and her rosy cheeks (兩頰)—that woman was beautiful.

Occasionally, when T'aewŏn couldn't spot the woman's beautiful figure at the tram stop, he felt somehow disappointed, and that whole day felt meaningless (無意味).

Listening to drowsy lectures (講義), resonating (共鳴) with the insipid (無味) talk of his peers, or engaging in disputes (論爭) due to clashes of opinion (意見) over pointless matters, he returned home after school let out, to the house that felt overwhelmingly suffocating.<193>

Upon returning home, T'aewŏn went straight into his dark room.

"T'aewŏn, you're back? Come greet this elder."

His father's thick voice came from the main room. But he pretended not to hear and remained silent.

"T'aewŏn!" The voice called again.

"Yes..."

T'aewŏn, with great annoyance, flung open the door, stomped across the wooden floor, and went to the main room.

In the main room, an old man sat in the place of honor (웃목). The old man's face, neck, and white clothes (白衣) were pitch black, as if he had never seen a washbasin (목강통) in his life. A foul smell, like something rotting, emanating from the old man filled the room, piercing the nose.

T'aewŏn, his face filled with utter hatred (憎誤) and insult, internally snorted "Hmph," bowed his head briefly, and left.

"So—that's T'aewŏn? I really wouldn't have recognized him."

"Indeed, it's already been over ten years (十年) since he came up to Seoul."

These words followed T'aewŏn from behind.

T'aewŏn returned to his room and opened the lengthy novel (長編) by Turgenev (트루게넵흐) he had been reading for the past two or three days, but his attention was drawn to the voices coming from the main room.

"Are you saying you'll survive if I take away the five *majigi* plot in the upper village? We've been living trusting only that land (土地). Taking it away means telling us to die. It won't do. Somehow, please return that land."

The old man's tone (語調), sounding pitiful and resentful, made T'aewŏn's chest ache.

"Haha, you really are persistent. I may be the landowner (地主), but everyone entrusts everything to Chongsŏngil (鍾吉). You should discuss it with Chongsŏngil; what use is there in coming to talk to me?"

"No, sir. Isn't it because Chongsŏngil wants to take the land I farm and give it to his relative (親戚), P'albongi (八峰이)? So please speak to him kindly, somehow... Besides, P'albongi can manage even without that land, but as you know, if I don't have that land, I'll be on the verge of death. And when have I ever failed to pay the rent (小作料)? No matter how bad the harvest (凶年), I've always paid the rent punctually as agreed..."

T'aewŏn couldn't listen to the conversation any further (以上). To listen anymore felt like his heart would burst with pain. He closed the book he was reading, went outside, and headed to the deserted forest behind the Government-General building (總督府), a place he often frequented. In the dark forest where a cold wind blew, he thought alone.

Tenant farmers...

The farmers weeding the fields under the scorching summer sun (炎天)! Their backs peeling over and over from the sun's rays, becoming raw and stinging, so tired that even when sleeping at night, their backs<194> hurt too much to lie flat, forcing them to sleep propped up. Eating meals worse than beggars in the city (都會), yet still working hard and diligently. If there's a drought or a flood, they feel more grief than if their child died, melting their insides with worry, pouring all their strength and devotion into raising the crops—what becomes of them? After deducting the rent paid to the landlord, the fertilizer costs (肥料代), and taxes (稅金), what remains? What returns is only fatigue and despair.

What hope do they have, what enjoyment (亨樂)?

Is the three o'clock mealtime their happiest moment? Is watching the five grains they nurtured grow day by day the peak of satisfaction (滋味)? Is gathering together after finishing a hard day's work, sharing discussions and old stories, the pinnacle of comfort (慰安)?

But during the spring hardship (春窮) when food runs out, eating itself becomes distressing. Watching the growing crops, thinking about where they will eventually go, brings sighs. Gathering to talk, thinking about household matters brings irritation and sorrow (悲哀).

Even amidst this, the mother-in-law torments the daughter-in-law; the father, after being scolded by the landlord, takes his anger out on the entire household; the front house and the back house curse each other because too little food was brought during ancestral rites; uncle and nephew fight over money from selling a cow and cease visiting (往來); brothers envy and hate each other over inheritance; the farmhand curses the master for giving him insufficient wages (給量); Father P'andol and Old Man Ch'ildol fight while drinking *makgeolli* and break each other's heads; Sŏkhi from the lower village, infatuated with the liquor seller's daughter, divorces his first wife (本妻); P'algani, trusting in the power of his wealthy relative (親戚), complains when he receives no help at all, spreading gossip about the rich relative throughout the village...

Ignorant people trying to tear each other down—is this the reality of the farming village?

The sun setting in the west cast its last weak (微弱) light through the trees.

The dark blue pine forest gradually sank into darkness, and the sound of a distant tram grinding, as if heard from afar, carried a kind of sorrow (哀愁), sounding lonely.

The old man left the next morning. It was unknown how things were resolved, but the old man, with a look of joy (喜氣) on his wrinkled face as he went out the gate, repeated several times, as if pleading, "I'm leaving trusting you completely. I'm going down trusting you completely."

"Poor man." T'aewŏn muttered inwardly, suppressing the welling tears. He then went straight to school.

At the tram stop, he spotted the woman as usual. But she was standing with a man.<195>

T'aewŏn momentarily felt an emotion close to jealousy (嫉妬) towards the man, but blushing as if ashamed of his feelings, he mocked himself. He boarded the tram and sat opposite the woman.

The man who would marry that woman? Whenever this thought arose, he covered his eyes with both hands as if frightened. And he felt unbearable jealousy and hatred (憎惡) towards the man who would be her husband, his stomach churning.

"That woman too, in the end, all her beauty will be monopolized (獨占) by a man, and she'll become a man's slave (奴隷), right?" Thinking this, T'aewŏn saw everything as demonic (惡鬼), and felt as if these demons would strangle him.

"I need to buy books and also a record (레코-드), so please give me five won (五圓)."

T'aewŏn, sometime after dinner, hesitated and forced the words out.

"What books are you always buying? Are you trying to buy novels again? Just study, what's the point of novels?"

T'aewŏn glanced at his father, looking enviously at his younger sibling sleeping in the warmest part of the room (아랫목).

"In the literature department (文科), studying things like novels is what we do," he said, watching his father's reaction.

"In the literature department, studying novels is the study? Reading useless novels is studying? Stop talking nonsense."

His father glared at him with bloodshot eyes.

T'aewŏn looked at his father with a face that seemed both scornful and resentful, then stormed out.

Extremely agitated, he went into his room and ripped up the books and notebooks lying on his desk. Still unable to calm his agitated mind, he drank three or four glasses of the 'port wine' (포도주) he had bought as medicine (藥) in quick succession. Then he went to his mother, who was ironing laundry with the maid in the lower room.

"Mother, just three won (三圓)—" he said, sounding angry.

"What do you need it for? Why do you always ask only me for money?"

His mother spoke as if gently reasoning with him, her face gentle.

"Just give it to me, whatever I need it for."

"Please don't drink alcohol, though," his mother said, taking money from her pocket.

"Use it sparingly. Father always says you spend too much money."

"Hmph. Father spends money on useless things, so why is he fixing the eaves again?"

"Well, fixing the house is Father's work."

His mother said this, smiling slightly.

Once outside the gate, he became agitated (激昻) again, his chest feeling constricted, as if he would go mad.<196>

He wanted to tear apart anyone he encountered.

"Destruction (破壞)!" he cried out inwardly, biting his lip hard. He wanted to destroy himself. And he wanted to destroy everything around him. His eyes burning with murderous intent (贐意), he strode rapidly.

As he was about to pass the elementary school, he saw a crowd of people packed near the school gate. "What is it?" he wondered, peeking over, then with a disappointed look, muttered, "A medicine seller (*약장수*), I see," and walked on.

He wandered aimlessly for a while, then, as if deciding arbitrarily, walked towards Ponjŏng (本町). Walking amidst Japanese people wearing yukata (浴衣) and carrying fans, T'aewŏn felt the clattering sound of geta (게다) intolerably grating on his nerves (神經)!

He peeked into bookstores (冊店) here and there, then entered a beer hall (삐야홀).

The white walls and neat furnishings brought a fresh (新鮮) and cool pleasant sensation to T'aewŏn, who had walked through the sultry outside. The whirring sound of the electric fan also felt pleasant.

Drinking a glass of cold beer (삐-루) just taken from the refrigerator (冷裝庫), his insides felt refreshed, and he experienced a cheerful feeling, as if all the pent-up gloom (鬱積) that had filled him had been washed away. He lit a cigarette, took a deep drag, and was about to exhale forcefully when he startled, clamped his mouth shut, widened his eyes, and trembled slightly.

—At a table (톄-불) further inside, sat the woman he always encountered at the tram stop. And opposite her was the man who had been talking with her at the tram stop before...

T'aewŏn stared at them, his face showing he didn't know what to do—a face blending shock, jealousy, and hatred!

He gripped the cup in his left hand tightly, lowered his head as if heartbroken and lonely, then gulped down the beer in the cup and looked towards the inner area again.

When he saw them whispering, completely disregarding (度外視) his pitiful heart, he felt exceedingly sorry for himself.

The quiet sound of a serenade (세레나-데-) flowing from the speakers (스탠드) seemed to both console him and mock him.

T'aewŏn drank beer until he was quite drunk. Then, with staggering steps, he left the street and walked home.

Entering the gate, his father's angry scolding voice sounded like a lullaby to his drunken ears.

When he woke up in the morning, both (兩) his shoulders (억개) felt drained of strength and limp. His mouth felt dry and sticky, he felt nauseous, and his head was spinning. His whole body ached and hurt, and even when he tried to get up, his legs wouldn't obey.<197>

But having no choice, he mustered all his strength and went to school. After getting off the tram, he forced his unsteady steps forward, barely managing to lift his feet. Cold sweat streamed down his entire body, his stomach, empty from missing breakfast, felt both hungry and not hungry, and the morning sunlight beating down on his back felt scorching hot just for T'aewŏn.

His listless footsteps, trudging along one step at a time, made him look like someone who had walked a thousand *ri* (千里).

Entering the classroom, all his strength drained away, and he rested his head on the desk, lying still.

The teacher's lecture began, and no matter how hard T'aewŏn tried to keep his eyes open, his eyelids naturally drooped, causing him to nod off repeatedly. He would startle awake, but after just two or three minutes, his eyes would close unknowingly (不知中), and his mind would wander far away.

His temples (간진노리) throbbed and pulsed sharply, and the back of his head felt like it was being stirred with a blunt knife.

Barely finishing school, he returned home and immediately spread out his bedding and lay down.

"Are you sick somewhere? Why are you lying down?"

His father, who had opened the door slightly to see what he was doing, spoke upon seeing T'aewŏn lying down.

"I'm not sick. I'm just lying down because there's nothing else to do."

He spoke as if annoyed and closed his eyes.

"A young fellow, acting tired? What tiring work did you do?" he said, flinging the door wide open.

"Why keep the door shut tight in May or June?"

(If only he would leave me alone to do as I please, just for today,) he pleaded inwardly.

His father, having crossed to the main room, said irritably,

"Looks like T'aewŏn is sick somewhere. Go ask him where it hurts."

T'aewŏn frowned, his face showing utter disgust, and pulled the blanket over his head.

A little later, his mother came in.

"T'aewŏn, where does it hurt?" she asked, pulling back the blanket to feel his forehead.

"Oh my, you have quite a fever. You must have caught a cold walking around in the chilly wind last night. Let's go to the pharmacy and get some cold medicine," she said, looking at T'aewŏn with a startled expression.

"Mmm," T'aewŏn grunted and turned over.

After his mother left, the sound of his father angrily scolding the household members grated intolerably on T'aewŏn's nerves.

"Hurry up and build a charcoal fire! Don't just dawdle there! Why do you always make the baby cry? Hurry up and soothe him!"

As evening approached, the headache grew worse, and hot air rushed out from inside him with every breath. Exhausted by the fever, he even began to talk deliriously.<198>

His mother, alarmed, ran off to call a doctor, while his father stayed in the lower room, grumbling and frowning.

The doctor who usually came inserted a thermometer (體溫計) under his armpit.

"Does your head hurt?" he asked, showing a kind (親切) attitude as he began the examination. His mother watched T'aewŏn over the doctor's shoulder with a worried expression.

After finishing the examination, the doctor removed the thermometer from under the armpit, looked at it, tilted his head slightly, and said, "The fever is quite high. It's close to forty degrees." He continued,

"Anyway, since the fever is high, let's give an injection (注射) to bring it down," he said, rattling things in his bag.

Hearing the word injection, T'aewŏn's heart leaped, and he felt scared, murmuring "Uh-uh-uh" as if refusing, but then closed his eyes tightly as if resigned.

"What illness could it be?"

His mother looked at the doctor with concern.

"Well. It's probably a cold. I can't say for sure, but he should be better by tomorrow."

The doctor spoke these words as if to reassure her, but his face showed anxiety.

The next day too, T'aewŏn could not get up. His condition gradually worsened, and the fever seemed to intensify in the evening.

"Maybe he caught a cold from being exposed to the chilly wind the evening before last," his mother fretted, her face clouded with worry (愁心), staying by T'aewŏn's side.

T'aewŏn's nerves became increasingly hypersensitive. Just hearing his father's voice made him jump. His father, who occasionally came into the room, felt T'aewŏn's forehead, and left frowning, seemed unreasonably unpleasant, hateful, and frightening.

Unable to bear the pain, he tossed and turned, moaning "Nnngh... nnngh...". Yet, like lightning, for a fleeting moment (刹那的), the phantom image (幻影) of the woman from the tram stop arose in his mind.

Did that woman come to the tram stop today too?

And what is the relationship between that man and her? Her round, blessed face appeared dimly before his eyes.

He thought how happy he would be to die under her care (看護), sinking into the pitiful sentimentality (쎈치맨탈) common among the sick, holding back tears. Without realizing it, he found himself unable to control his gradually developing attachment to the woman.

(It's an unrequited love that won't work out anyway.) He thought this mockingly, trying his best not to think about her, but he couldn't help his feelings (제252頁에)<199> (Continued from page 199) of longing for her. T'aewŏn, becoming deeply, deeply attached to the woman, thought he seemed destined to end up in solitude and loneliness due to his heart-wrenching unrequited love.

Around eight o'clock in the evening (八時), the doctor came but only gave an injection and asked them to come pick up medicine, without saying what the illness was. He too seemed unsure of what T'aewŏn's illness truly was. T'aewŏn grew progressively weaker, his eyes sinking in and his flesh wasting away rapidly. He couldn't eat at all, and hadn't had a bowel movement for several days.

He wished he could just die from this illness, but when exhausted by the fever and his consciousness fading, he would think, "Am I dying now?" and, not knowing how to handle the fear of death, would grip his mother's hand tightly, close his eyes, and pray to the unseen hand of death.

After several more days passed, the doctor finally diagnosed it as typhoid fever (腸窒扶斯).

At this news, everyone in the family lost color (色), and the house became as desolate as one in mourning.

The day T'aewŏn was admitted to the University Hospital (大學病院), a major commotion occurred at home.

A disinfection vehicle came from the Municipal Health Department (府廳衛生課) and relentlessly sprayed disinfectant all over the house with a pump (뽑뿌). <252>

**Summary**

Yi Kwangsu reviews Yi Yongu's short story "Narak," praising its sincere portrayal of life despite technical flaws in style and description. He emphasizes that true literature captures a slice of life realistically, requiring the author to possess technique refined through study and practice, as well as a clear philosophy or faith. He critiques other submissions for lacking individuality and coherence, and while noting Korean literary technique isn't necessarily low compared to other nations, he feels Korean writers often lack philosophical depth. Yi Yongu's "Narak" follows Hwang T'aewŏn, a student suffocated by his father's controlling, albeit loving, traditional ways. T'aewŏn feels alienated, fantasizes about escape or rebellion, observes social injustices like the plight of tenant farmers, develops an unrequited infatuation with a woman he sees daily, and eventually falls seriously ill with typhoid fever, reflecting his deep despair.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing. Yi Kwangsu's commentary reflects the nationalist cultural aspirations and anxieties of the colonial period, emphasizing the need for technical mastery of the Korean language and the development of a distinct Korean literary "philosophy" or "faith" to elevate national literature. The story "Narak" vividly portrays the generational conflict between a traditionalist father and a modern-educated son, a common theme reflecting the rapid social changes and tensions under colonial rule. T'aewŏn's alienation, escapist fantasies, sensitivity to social issues like tenant farming, and eventual physical collapse into illness serve as a powerful metaphor for the broader sense of powerlessness, frustration, and societal malaise experienced by young intellectuals in 1930s colonial Korea.

諸家推薦 新人作品集-李光洙 薦, 奈落(소설)
「奈落」選後感

春園 李光洙

推薦作品이라는 付托이나 不幸히 내가 본 것 中에는 小說이나 詩나 成樣된 것이 업섯다. 그중에서 李龍雨氏의 「奈落」이 고작이지마는 아직 文體, 描寫 等의 練磨가 부족하다. 그러나 그의 人生에 着眼하는 法이 매우 眞摯하야 將來의 囑望을 주는 바가 잇다. 그는 人生의 觀察者로서 一片 산 人生을 그리랴는 要領을 알앗다. 그의 戀愛事件, 病事件 等은 퍽 稚拙하지마는 그래도 主人公의 病的인 性格과 그 父母의 옛 朝鮮父母的 心理는 素樸하게 表現이 되엇다.
小說이나 脚本을 억지로 지으랴서는 안된다. 재미잇는 이야기로, 또는 어떤 이데올로기 하나를 諷한 이야기로 만들어 내는 것이 文藝創作은 아니다. 一生의, 一面, 또는 一片을 베어서 讀者의 眼前에 내어노앗다 하는 感을 주는 것만이 비로소 文藝創作이다. 짓고도 안 지은 것이 文藝다. 안 지은 드시 짓는 것이 文藝창작이다.
그런데 人生의 일면, 또는 一片이란 무엇이냐 하면 어떤 事件을 중심으로 하는 몃個 人格의 反應으로 생기는 必然한 喜劇的 또는 悲劇的 結果와 그 결과에 達하는 經路의 如實的, 그리고도 撰擇的인-最大限度로 壓縮되고 集中된 描寫다. 여기서 逼眞性이 오는 것이다. 實感이 도는 것이다. 그럼으로 事件의 因果的 展開의 原因인 性格이 뚜렷하게 그려지는 것이 根本要件이다.
그런데 내가 본 몃 篇의 小說로 보면 個性이라든가 事件의 因果的 必然的 展開라든가, 또는 作者가 人生을 바라본 觀角이라든가가 分明한 것이 업섯다. 一言以敝之하면 내가 본 作品의 應募者들은 文藝修養이 업는 이들의 最初의 試作들만인 것 가탓다. 그중에서<188> 「奈落」의 作者는 文藝란 무엇인가의 要領만은 捕捉하엿다고 본다.
文藝도 一種의 技術이다. 技術은 誠意잇는 長時間의 練磨로만 생기는 것이니 練磨라면 大家의 작품의 硏究的 精讀과 실제로 만히 지어보는 것을 이른다. 그리고 또 文藝란 人生의 說明이오. 人生에게 대한 警告요 說敎다. 警告와 說敎의 體裁를 가초지 아니한 警告요, 說敎다. 이러하기 때문에 文藝家는 먼저 人生觀을 엇어야 할 것이다. 그는 哲學의 所有者 또는 信抑의 所有者라야 할 것이다. 이야기가 平淡한 것은 조터라도 一般大衆이 읽으나 마나한 作品일진댄 그것은 人生浪費다. 모든 文化活動의 結局의 目的은 人生에의 裨益이 아니냐.
朝鮮의 今日의 文藝의 水準은 諸外國에 비겨서 결코 그다지 나즌 것은 아니다. 나즌 것이 잇다면 그것은 文藝家의 哲學이오, 信仰이지 표현의 技術은 아니다. 그럼으로 오늘날 朝鮮의 文壇에 서랴는 사람은 우선 朝鮮語文과 朝鮮의 文藝의 技巧만은 배호고 나서지 아니하면 아니 될 것이다. 文法, 綴字, 文體에 대한 結束과 規則도 모르고 文藝作品을 지으랴는 것은 마치 明暗色彩配合法도 모르고 展覽會作品의 그림을 그리랴는 것과 갓다. 우리 朝鮮사람은 재조가 勝하지마는 工夫의 힘을 모르는 感이 잇다. 文藝란 音樂이나 그림과 가치 誠意잇고 오랜 工夫를 통하여서만 上達하는 재조다. 게다가 文藝는 觀念藝術이기 때문에 人生의 歷史, 心理, 哲學, 信仰, 習慣에의 通曉, 그리고 作家自身의 분명한 人生觀을 요하는 것이니 文藝에 뜻을 두는 자 반다시 먼저 크게 공부할 것이다.<189>

奈落

李龍雨

황태원(黃泰元)의 아버지는 태원이를 한없이 사랑하였다. 여기에는 조곰는 의심할 여지도 없다. 그리고 태원이도 그것을 몰으는 것은 아니였으나 그렇나 태원이가 그 사랑을 감수(甘受)하기에는 너머도 아니 꼬웁고 쓰라리고 자긔 자존심을 모욕하는 것 같었다.
태원이는 그의 아버지가 자긔를 사랑한다는 것을 그의 이기적 입장(利己的立場)에서 나온 자긔만족을 위한 사랑이라고밖에 생각하지 못하였다. 무론 이것은 무의식적(無意識的)이겟지만 혹은 의식적에서 나올 때도 있을지 몰은다.
이러한 사랑에 이해(理解)라는 것이 있을 도리가 없다.-자긔 아들의 모-든 취미(趣味)라든가 사상(思想)이라든가 주의(主義)같은 것을 이해 못하여 주고 그런 것을 모다 아버지의 그것에 동화(同化)식힐려는 사랑이다.
태순이는 Y전문학교(專門學校)에 단니고 있었다. 그래 그는 아버지에게 좀더 전문학교학생의 대우(待遇)를 기대하였고 이해를 바랫섯다. 완고하고 몰리해(沒理解)한<190> 그의 아버지가 그를 만족식힐 도리가 없었다. 그의 아버지는 아모리 자긔 자식이 성장하더래도 어느 때까지나 그를 두서너살 먹은 어린 아해로만 생각하야 독단적(獨斷的) 태도와 사랑을 하였다.
그러나 태원이는 이를 감수는 못하였다. 그는 늘 불평과 불만 가운데서 고민하엿다.

태원이에 아버지는 태원이가 동모와 노는 것까지에도 관심하였다. 태원이가 외출하는 것과 동모가 태원이를 찾어오는 것을 퍽 실어하였다.
오래간만에 찾어온 동모가 문에서 「태원이 태원군」하고 불으며는 그의 아버지는 문창에서 내다보며 「밖에 나갔오」하고 돌려보내 버린다.
「동모가 불으면 왜 없다고 그리시누. 태순이는 화가 치켜올나 몸부림을 치며 우는 듯한 얼골로 짜징을 내며 말한다.
「그런 못된 친구와 사귀서는 안되. 집에서 공부나 하지 학생이 동무나 사귀서 술이나 마시려 단이다니 그런 것이 다 불양(不良)이야.
아버지의 무관심한 태연한 태도로 이러케 말할 적에는 태원이도 가삼에서 불이 콸콸 타올르는 것 같다.
그는 불꽃같은 증오(憎惡)를 늣기며 견딀수 없는 불쾌한 기분에 깊이 잠기였다.
「왜 아버지는 저러실까?
그의 맑은 눈동자에는 분명히 불만과 불편이 보이엿다. 그는 앞문을 힘껏 다첫다. 「탕」하는 소리와 함께 「왜 문을 거래」하는 소리가 그의 뒤에서 들였다.
태원이는 「흥」하고 코우슴을 하고는 자긔방으로 드러가서는 힘없이 책상에 앉었다.
(자식을 사랑한다며는 왜 좀더 이해(理解)있는-자식의 마음을 만족식키며 유쾌히 집에 잇도록 할-사랑을 못하여 주실가?) 그는 도저히 기대할 수 없는 희망을 생각하다가는 머리를 할일 없는 듯이 썩썩 극는다.
그는 학교에서 도라와서도 매일(每日)같이 방안에 꼭 붙어 있는데에 무한한 불만과 권태(卷怠)를 늑끼였다. 그에게는 굿센 자극(刺戟)이 있는 생활을 하고 싶다. 권태와 불만가운데에서 구데기 모양으로 꿈 꿈질 그날그날을 보내는 데에는 죽는 것 보담도 실었다.
마적(馬賊)의 수령이 되여 모-든 악한(惡漢)을 다하며 눈 쌓인 넓고 넓은 들판을 마음대로 뛰여단니며 마음에 가득차인 울적(鬱積)을 름럴(凜冽)히 불어오는 북풍에다 씻고 싶다. 그렇지 않으면 몇십만원(十萬圓)의 돈을 가지고 『짜즈』소래 요란하게 들려오는 데서 「푸란디-」나 「아무 산같은 혀(舌)를 학 쏘을 독한 술에 취하여- 마음속 지 취하여-독부(毒婦)의 요소(妖笑)를 마음껏 만긱(滿喫)하며 환락(歡樂)의 세게(世界)에서 환락의 세게를<191> 찾어 다니고 싶다.
이러한 자극의 생활이 아니면 사라가는 의미(意味)가 업다고 생각하였다.
태원이는 굴탁(屈託)스러히 하품을 하고는 사지를 쭉 펴며 「어어대-」하고는 벌떡 일어나서 책상에 앉었다.
저녁밥을 먹고 두시간도 못되여 벌서 밖같은 먹을 가라 부은 듯이 캄캄하엿다.
아버지에 대한 불쾌한 기분이 차차 소멸함을 따라 이제는 웨로운 고적과 애수(哀愁)로 변하여 감을 늑끼였다. 태원이의 창백한 얼골은 전등불을 받어서 더한층 우수(憂愁)의 빛을 띄우며 책상의 책(冊)을 펴서 훌훌 넘기다가 한군데 눈을 쏘왓다.

그는 아무나 닥치는 대로 찌저버리고 싶었다.
「파괴(破壞)!」 그는 속으로 부르짖으며 입술을 꽉 물었다. 그는 그 자신을 파괴하고 십다. 그리고 주위의 모든 것을 파괴하고 싶다. 그는 진의(贐意)에 타올으는 눈을 부루뜨고 확확 거러갓다.
보통학교 앞을 지날려할 때 그는 학교문 있는 대 사람들이 꽉 서서 있는 것을 보고 「무엇인가.」하고 끼웃 넘어다 보고는 실망한 듯한 얼골로 「약장*로군.」하며 다시 걸었다.
그는 어대로 갈 목적(目的)도 없이 한참 주저하다간 아무렇게나 하는 듯이 본정(本町)쪽으로 거렀다. 『유가다』 (浴衣)입고 부채든 일본사람들의 틈 끼여서 거르며 태원이는 「게다」 끄는 소리의 딸거락딸거락 하는 대에 신경(神經)이 못견듸게 집어 흔들리는 것 같다!
그는 책점(冊店)을 끼웃끼웃하다가 어서 『삐야홀』로 들어갔다.
힌벽과 삿뜻한 장치는 무더운 박같을 거러온 태원이에게 신선(新鮮)하고 시원한 쾌감을 이러키였다. 선풍기의 웡웡하는 소리도 쾌감을 늣기게 하였다.
냉장고(冷裝庫)에서 갓내여온 싸늘한 『삐-루』를 한컵 마시고는 속이 시원하여 지금까지 콱차였든 울적(鬱積)이 다-시처버려지는 듯한 쾌활한 기분을 늣기였다. 그는 담배에 불을 부처 죽 빨아들여서는 마음끝 확 뿜다가 깜짝 놀라 입을 다물고 눈을 크게 뜨며 몸을 약간 떠렀다.
-안쪽 『톄-불』잇는 정류장에서 늘 맛나는 그 여자가 앉어 있었다. 그리고 그 마즘편에는 전에 정류장에서 그 여자와 이야기하든 사나히다...
태원이는 어이 할 바를 몰르는 얼골로 그들을 처다보왔다-놀냄과 질투와 증오와를 한데 뭉친 얼골로!
그는 왼*에 쥐여있는 컵을 꼭 쥐며 애달푼 듯 외로운 듯이 고게를 숙이였다가 컵의 『삐-루』를 훌쩍 마시고는 다시 안쪽을 바라보앗다.
그들이 태원이의 애처러운 마음을 전엔 도외시(度外視)한 속삭임을 볼 때 그는 자긔 자신이 넘어도 불상하였다.
『스탠드』에서 흘녀오는 고요한 『세레나-데-』의 소리는 그를 위로하는 듯도 또는 그를 비웃는 듯하다.
태원이는 잔뜩 취하두록 『삐-루』를 마시였다. 그리고 비틀거리는 거름으로 거리를 나와선 집으로 거렀다.
문을 드러가 아버지의 골내며 야단하는 소리가 취한 그에게는 자장가처럼 들였다.

아침에 일어낫을 때에는 양(兩) 억개가 힘이 쭉 빠저서 녹신하엿다. 입안이 텁텁하며 구역이 나며 머리가 핑핑 돌았다. 온 전신이 아리고 아푸고 하여 일어나라해도 다리가 말을 듯지 않었다.<197>
그러나 하는 수 없서 온 힘을 다하여 학교에 나갔다. 전차에서 내려 걸키지 안는 거름을 억지로 억지로 근근히 발을 띄여놓는다. 진땀이 전신에서 줄줄 흐르며 아참 못먹은 배는 곱흔 듯도 하고 안곱흔 듯도 하며 등어리를 쪼이는 아침 해빛은 태원이에만은 불같이 뜨거웠다.
터벅터벅 한거름 거러가는 힘없는 발자죽은 마치 철리(千里) 길을 걸어온 사람처럼 보인다.
교실에 들어가서는 왼 힘이 탁 풀려서 책상에 머리를 두고 가만히 누어있었다.
선생의 강의가 시작하여 태원이는 아무리 눈을 바로 뜰녀해도 눈까풀이 자연히 내려와서는 꾸벅꾸벅 졸다가 깜작 놀래서 눈을 뜨나 이삼분만 있으면 또 불지(不知)중에 눈이 깜어지며 정신이 먼곧으로 떠돈다.
간진노리가 팔딱팔딱 뛰며 뜩금뜩금 하며 머리 뒷통은 뭉텅한 칼노 골을 휘젓은 것 같다.
겨우 학교를 마치곤 그는 집에 돌아와 바로 이불을 깔고 드러누었다.
「어대 알우야. 왜 누었니‘」
무엇하나하고 문을 좀 열어본 아버지는 태원이가 누어있음을 보고 말한다.
「안 앞아요. 아무대도 되니깐 누었지요.」
그는 지겨운 듯이 말하고 눈을 깜엇다.
「절문 놈이 되기는 무슨 된 일을 했다구.」하고는 문을 확 열어재치며
「오육월에 문은 왜 꼭꼭 다처.」
(오늘만이라도 좀 나는 내 마음대로 내버려 두시였스면)그는 애원하는 듯이 속으로 빌었다.
큰방으로 건너간 아버지는
「태원이가 어대 앞은 모양이야. 어대가 앞은지 가 물어봐.」하며 짜징을 낸다.
태원이는 상을 찌푸리며 지긋지긋한 얼골을 하며 이불을 폭 덥헛섯다.
조곰 있다 어머니가 들어와서
「태원아 어대가 앞으니.」하며 이불을 볏기여 머리를 만저보고
「아이구 열이 퍽 있내. 어제 저녁에 찬바람를 쏘이며 다니드니만 약국에 가서 감기약이나 가저오자.」하며 놀랜듯한 얼골로 태원이를 바라본다.
「음.」하고 태원이는 돌아누었다.
어머니가 나간 뒤에 아버지의 화를 내여 집안사람에 야단하는 소리가 태원이의 마음을 못견듸게 하였다.
「얼는 숫불피워. 거기서 얼는 거리지말구. 어린애는 왜 늘 울여 얼는 안 달내니.」
저녁때가 되니 두통은 점점 더 심하여가서 숨쉴 때마다 속에서 뜨거운 열기가 확확 나왔다. 그리고 열에 지처서 그는 헛소리까지 시작하였다.<198>
어머니는 놀래서 의사 불르려 뛰여가고 아버지는 아랫방에서 꿍꿍하며 상을 찌프리고 있다.
늘 오는 의사는 체온계(體溫計)를 겨들양에 끼우며
「머리가 앞으새요.」하며 친절(親切)한 태도를 보이고 진찰을 시작한다. 어머니는 근심스러운 낯으로 의사의 억깨넘어로 태원이를 바라보고 있다.
진찰을 마친 의사는 겨들앙에 끼였든 체온계를 빼여보며 약간 고개를 기우리며 「열이 매우 심함니다. 사십도 각가원대요.」하고 다시 말을 이어서
「하여간 열이 만으니 열이나 읍새게 주사(注射) 노업시다.」하며 가방속을 딸거락거렸다.
태원이는 주사란 말을 듯고 가삼이 들컥하며 겁을 먹고 거절하는 듯이 「어어어」하였더나 단념한 듯 눈을 꼭 깜었다.
「무슨 병일가요.」
어머니는 근심하는 빛으로 의사를 바라본다.
「글세요. 아마 감기겠지요. 머라고 확실히 몰으나 내일이면 나흘검니다.」
의사는 위안하는 듯이 이러케 말하였으나 얼골에는 불안이 되여 있었다.

그 잇튼날도 태원이는 일어나지 못하였다. 병세는 차차 더 심하여가며 저녁이 되면 열이 더 심한 것 같었다.
「그적게 저녁에 찬바람을 쐬서 감기가 걸리잔었을가」 어머니는 수심이 돗는 얼골로 태원이의 옆을 떠나지 않는다.
태원이의 신경은 점점 과민하여가며 아버지의 소리만 들어도 솔음이 콱콱 직켜올라 가끔가끔 그 방에 들어와서 태원이의 머리를 만저보고는 얼골을 찌프리고 나가는 아버지가 이유(理由)없시 불쾌하고 미웁고 두려웠다.
그는 고통을 못이기여 이리저리 굴며 「응응...」하고 말었다. 그러면서도 번개같이 찰나적(刹那的)으로 정류장에서 맛나는 그 여자의 한영(幻影)이 마음에 이러난다.
그 여자는 오늘도 정류장에 나왔을가?
그리고 그 남자는 그 여자와 어떠한 관게가 잇나? 그의 보고잇는 눈에는 그 여자의 둥글고 복스러운 얼골이 희미하게 어리친다.
그는 그 여자의 간호(看護)압헤서 죽었으면 얼마나 행복스러울가하는 생각을 이르키며 병자들이 가지는 애처러운 『쎈치맨탈에 잠기여 눈물을 멈춘다. 그는 자기도 몰으는 동안에 차츰차츰 그 여자에 익걸려가는 마음을 엇지 할 수 없었다.
(아무래도 되잔흘 짝사랑을) 그는 비웃는 듯이 이렇케 생각하며 될 수 있는대로 그 여자를 생각지 않을여하나 그(제252頁에)<199> (제199頁 계속) 여자를 동경하는 자기 감정을 엇지 할 도리가 없었다. 그 여자에 깊이깊이 익걸려가는 태원이는 결국 애달푼 짝사랑에 고적과 왜로움에 맟일 운명을 버서나지 못할 것 같이 생각하였다.
저녁 여들시(八時)쯤 되여 의사가 왔으나 주사만 놓고 약가지려 오라고만 하며 무슨 병인지는 말하지 않었다. 그도 태원이의 병이 과연 어떠한 병인지 몰으는 듯 하였다. 태원이는 점점 쇠약하여가서 눈이 쑥 들어가며 살이 쭉쭉 빠저갔다. 조곰도 밥을 먹지 못하며 대변도 몃칠식 나오지 않었다.
그는 차라리 이 병으로 죽어버렸으면 하였으나 역시 열긔가 지처서 정신이 까물까물할 적에는 「어저는 죽는가」하고 죽엄에 대한 공포를 엇젓줄 몰라 어머니의 손을 꼭 쥐고 눈을 깜으며 보이지 않는 죽엄의 손에 비렀다.
그리고 몇일이 지나 의사는 결국 장질부사(腸窒扶斯)라고 진단하였다.
이 소리에 집안식구는 모다 색을 일어버리고 집안은 초상친 집 같이 쓸쓸하였다.

태원이가 대학병원(大學病院)에 입원한 그 날 집안은 일대 소동이 일어낫다.
부청위생과에서 소독자동차가 와서는 온 집안을 『뽑뿌』로 사정없이 소독물을 피여 뿌렸다. <252>
