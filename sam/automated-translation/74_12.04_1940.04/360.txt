Magazine Title: 삼천리 제12권 제4호
Publication Date: 1940년 04월 01일
Article Title: 女人藝術, (3. 女流作家의 小說集) (Women's Art, (3. A Collection of Short Stories by Female Writers))
Author: 張德祚
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Novel)

Women's Art,
Female Artist
Chang Tŏk-cho

That Yŏng-hwa temporarily paused the family life she loved so much and left again for Tokyo to study vocal music was not so much due to her husband's understanding, but rather his strong urging.
"How about studying a bit more?"
When these words first came from her husband's lips, Yŏng-hwa was more surprised than pleased.
"Study what?"
"Well, wasn't that our promise? When we got married—"
Only then did the young wife seem to remember, snorting and shrugging her shoulders. It was the year Yŏng-hwa finished the music department at E Women's College. She had prepared everything to go to Tokyo for music studies, but it had been halted due to the matter of marriage.
At that time, after long deliberation between the two issues of art and marriage, she had chosen the latter, but her still vibrant heart couldn't give up its longing for the glamorous stage. She had sought her husband's understanding by saying she would keep house for about two years and then, when the opportunity arose, leave again for music studies. <316>
But of course, it was an idle promise, and as she settled into family life and had a child, she naturally became tied down by this and that, having long forgotten about such possibilities.
"Any woman can play the role of an ordinary wife. But the role of a great vocalist can only be played by one in a thousand, one in ten thousand, who is born with the talent. When I see you gradually getting buried in household chores and forgetting even the talent you were chosen for, I feel guilty, as if I've committed some sin."
Her husband was an exceedingly kind man.
He tried to give hope to his hesitant wife by persuading her with many words.
"Just go study for about two more years. Come back and become an even better housewife, that's all. Why, aren't people making a fuss these days? Arguing whether a female artist and domestic life can go hand in hand. How about you prove that the greater the female artist, the better the homemaker she can be, hmm?"
Finally, Yŏng-hwa dusted off her trunk.
"Don't worry about the house, and don't worry about the child. I won't send the baby to a daycare, nor will I give her to someone else. I'll raise her right beside me. I'll take great care, raise her even better than when you were here—"
Still, the young mother, even after boarding the train, kept burying her face in the child's tiny forehead, cheeks, hands, and feet, crying continuously.
"Be careful and have a good trip."
As the train started moving, the one-year-old child, perhaps realizing her mother was truly leaving her behind, let out a gasping cry and wailed.
Even after passing Suwŏn and Taejŏn, the image of the child's fern-like fingers, held and swayed in her husband's large hand, lingered before Yŏng-hwa's eyes, and she couldn't stop her tears no matter what.

For two years in Tokyo, Yŏng-hwa truly dedicated herself to her vocal studies. <317>
The sincerity of her husband, who sent a considerable amount for research expenses each month from his fixed income and savings, was appreciated, but whenever the vision of her child playing alone with a wooden horse on a warm sandy beach after sending her mother away came to mind, she truly clenched her teeth.
Her efforts bore fruit.
When Yŏng-hwa returned after completing her studies, the musicians gathered at the Chosŏn Hotel for a preview concert praised her singing with one voice.
Her recital was also a great success.
Of course, her husband had spared no meticulous attention, from negotiating with the organizers and sponsors to the lighting and the placement of the piano.
On the night after finishing her solo recital, Yŏng-hwa stretched out her limbs to her heart's content for the first time in a long while, feeling as if she had compensated for even a fraction of the guilt she felt towards her child and husband.
Truly, from now on, as her husband had said, she would become an excellent homemaker and fully atone for the guilt of her past disloyalty to the child, she thought—.

However, Yŏng-hwa's noble resolution—
As the days went by, she couldn't help but notice another phenomenon: she had somehow become quite famous.
First, the number of letters she received multiplied several times compared to before.
Initially, when she read letters sent from unknown people—writing things like how they were moved after hearing the teacher's solo performance and couldn't sleep for nights, or how Chosŏn finally had a true soprano singer—Yŏng-hwa felt both shy, like a child receiving undue praise, and happy, yet somehow uneasy.
Especially on the day she received a letter from a certain female student currently studying music, expressing earnest longing and asking for the teacher's signature to carry as a talisman, hoping to become a great vocalist like the teacher—
"Do they think I'm an actress? What's this about a signature?" <318>
She pouted, but in truth, she was quite pleased.
Perhaps "Byron," who woke up one morning to find himself famous, had experienced similar fortune, she thought.

It wasn't just letters.
Cars sent from newspaper and magazine companies for roundtable discussions and from the broadcasting station busily panted up and down the sloping hill in front of her house. Women's magazines featured her life story in large print. And the situation with close-up portrait photos.
When she went to church, she was suddenly entrusted with conducting the choir, and at gatherings, she naturally sat side-by-side with those she had looked up to as teachers and seniors just a few years ago.
When meeting friends on the street:
"Yŏng-hwa has become so successful, it's hard for people like us to even stand next to you."
They appreciated her acknowledging them.

There's likely no one who dislikes being praised.
When she went into department stores to buy something, the saleswomen would glance at her and whisper among themselves.
Surely, they must be saying (That's the famous soprano So-and-so) and envying her, she thought.
There were also times on the streetcar when a group of male students, who had been loudly chatting, suddenly fell silent and wore smiles expressing respect around their mouths.
They must be people who had heard her sing, she thought.
In any case, less than half a year after returning to the capital, Yŏng-hwa seemed to have certainly become one of the city's famous figures.
At first, Yŏng-hwa felt embarrassed and undeserving of such honor (?), but after experiencing such situations so often, the praises and admiring attitudes now felt like rights she deserved. On days when no letters came and no one praised her, her mood naturally soured.
At such times, even if it was already evening, she had to change her clothes and go out for a stroll to calm her mind. <319>
Whether in Chongno or Ponjŏngt'ong, just going out to a crowded place allowed her to find one or two pairs of admiring eyes.
In this way, the seeds of arrogance and self-satisfaction gradually began to sprout in Yŏng-hwa's once peaceful heart.

The husband did not regard his wife's state as a pleasant matter at all.
He also didn't look favorably upon Yŏng-hwa's frequent outings and her unusual new taste in clothes and makeup.
While listening to Yŏng-hwa's forced excuses—that since she didn't know where someone who knew her might be, a wife appearing shabby would bring dishonor even to the husband—the husband sighed, looking at his wife's dressing table covered with ochre powder like mud dust and red enamel nail polish.
The husband of a famous person---that term somehow felt sad, piercingly sad to the bone.
Even after several admonitions and arguments, the wife's outings did not decrease.
Later, perhaps influenced by something she encountered outside, she began to wish for separation from her husband.
She argued that to further develop herself as an artist, she needed unrestricted time, and to always refresh her mood, she needed to travel freely.
The husband explained point by point that her thinking was wrong.
The wife, having responsibilities too, did not insist on the final word against her husband's arguments.
However, she seemed to constantly spread the wings of her imagination about a free artistic life detached from the family.
Occasionally, when she was in a bad mood, she would lament her fate of being tied to the family and unable to move, despite having high aspirations and excellent talent, sometimes even voicing it aloud.
She resented her husband and cursed her family.
Later, partly out of rebellion against her husband, whom she deemed uncomprehending and lacking compassion, leaving the house every morning became almost a habit. <320>
Just as a person, especially a woman, who develops a habit of staying indoors finds even necessary outings bothersome, once the habit of going out takes hold, one must find some excuse to go out onto the streets.
Yŏng-hwa's case was exactly that.
Sometimes, she would genuinely resolve not to go out and try to occupy herself with chores, but then she would feel inexplicably irritated and suffocated, ultimately ending up going out again.
Moreover, the streets of Seoul fortunately offered numerous tea houses and theaters for such people.
Less than a year after her first solo recital, the question of whether a great vocalist could also be a great housewife was no longer an issue between the couple.
The kind and meek husband tried to bind Yŏng-hwa's heart within the family, within the bounds of not infringing upon his wife's human rights, while the famous wife had long denied the idea of art as a mere pastime (餘技) of housekeeping, or music as a plaything (玩弄物).
Carrying two irreconcilable assertions, the home that was supposed to become happier had somehow become completely unhappy. Some time later, one day, they finally had a major argument.
---◇---
It was the day the wife had accepted a position as a full-time lecturer at a certain music academy, which had been discussed for some time, solely on her own decision.
When Yŏng-hwa returned by car late at night, around twelve o'clock, there seemed to be a faint smell of alcohol on her breath.
Her husband had not gone to bed yet and was sitting silently hunched beside the extinguished brazier.
As she took off her scarf in front of him and sat down, her reddish skirt flaring, she said,
"Really, women just can't help it. No matter how great their passion or enthusiasm for art is, they just can't. Men can live without a wife or child, but for a woman, family is simply fate."
Then, pausing, she continued,
"Well, today I dropped by that music academy for a moment, and Mr. H and Mr. K were there, and they just attacked me. Saying I'm indulging in ease, buried in house<321>hold chores, or something. It seems everyone knows that my refusal of the professor position offered by the academy was more your decision than mine."
The wife chattered cheerfully, but the husband remained silent.
"And they said that we women lack the kind of passion for art that men have, you know, the kind of passion one could even fall in love with. If you consider art a profession, what do household chores matter? I was so wronged, I almost died."
"………………"
"Ah, then everyone jumped in from here and there, adding their opinions. Saying there are definitely points for female artists to reflect on. That one must have a strong passion, enough to throw oneself into it, and that the notion of singing or playing the piano in the틈틈이 (spare moments) between this and that chore, like a mere pastime (餘技) of housekeeping, is completely wrong."
Still, the husband said nothing.
Yŏng-hwa secretly stuck out her lower lip at her husband.
"So, I was so indignant that I boasted I'd show them whether we women have the passion to consider art as life itself."
However, before the wife could even finish her sentence, she had to close her mouth.
Her husband had abruptly lifted his face.
"I never intended for you to become such a great artist that you had to sacrifice family life."
It was a harsh voice she had never heard before.
His face pale, his bloodshot eyes wide open and glaring, the wife, who had only ever thought of her husband as a kind feminist and a good person, was momentarily bewildered, unsure how to interpret his appearance.
"What I wished for you was to remain a peaceful family person until the end. Even encouraging you to study music further was primarily out of a desire to improve our family life... Only now do I realize my thinking was wrong. You shouldn't throw pearls before swine (되지헌테 진줄 던저주지); you shouldn't bestow favors upon an unenlightened <322> person."
Then, suddenly lowering his voice,
"I was thinking about this earlier too. When we were in middle school, the man who ran the shop in front of our boarding house deeply regretted not having studied enough himself, so he sent his wife (예펜네) to school. I remember it vaguely even now, but he truly put in all his effort. I don't remember which school, but anyway, he sent her up to about middle school level. And what do you think happened after that wife finished school? She fell in love with another young man and ran away. Really, the state of the man left behind was pitiful (가아엽드라니)……"
He muttered these words as if talking to himself.
Yŏng-hwa, who had initially sat blankly, finally began to understand the meaning behind her husband's words.
"Ugh, disgusting. So, you're that shopkeeper, and I'm that wife?"
The wife's voice was also harsh.
"And I thought you were a person of character with understanding. Ai, how cowardly, who are you comparing me to? So, just because I strive for art, I'm going to betray you and run away like that wife?"
An expression of clear displeasure crossed the husband's face.
"Right (올소)! You jump at the comparison to that shopkeeper's wife, but frankly speaking, you're exactly the same kind of person. Of course, you have education and all, so you won't run off (다라나진) with another man like that woman, but instead, you're striving (앨쓰구) to follow some philanderer (바람둥일) called art or something, which is even more absurd (기가맥히지). Indeed, looking at it rightly, your situation might be several times more ridiculous than that shopkeeper's wife's case."
"Ai, how infuriating! Is that what you say, you who always pride yourself on having understanding? If that was really your true feeling, you should have tied your wife up with iron chains (쇠사실루 매여놓지) like a dog. Just talking empty words (힌소릴 허구)."
"If things could be controlled as I wish by tying you with iron chains, I would. I'm just a person who wants the family to be peaceful and our life to be happy." <323>
"Family happiness? Hmph, anyone can have family life, but who was it that said only one in several thousand, several tens of thousands, can play the role of a chosen vocalist? One moment you make a fuss about nurturing someone's talent, and now you're agitated because you can't nip that bud."
"Ha, who told you to kill your talent? It's just that there's no need to make the family unhappy."
"Don't say anything. No matter what, I will complete my art. Even for the heaven-sent talent I possess, I cannot remain like this. No matter who says what, starting the day after tomorrow, I will go to the school and teach. And I will prepare for another solo recital."
"You wretched thing! That's not loving art. It's vanity, vanity! Self-intoxication more dangerous than playing with fire. So, shut up and stay put in the house starting tomorrow. I'll explain it reasonably when you've calmed down a bit."
"What?"
Fierce rebelliousness flared up like fire.
Her husband intends to bury this awakened female vocalist, this artist with genius talent, using this small family and the contribution of his two years of support as bait. Indeed, if she considers art her life and vocal music her profession, it would inevitably cause inconvenience for her husband and child. She felt sorry about that. However, compared to darkening the future of a rarely seen genius vocalist, even if certain sacrifices had to be endured, such sacrifices would be nothing. No, perhaps it was their duty to accept it gladly---along with the slight intoxication from the alcohol, Yŏng-hwa's nerves became increasingly agitated.
She composed herself and looked directly into her husband's eyes.
"Honey, so you mean you oppose my striving as an artist?"
After asking this final question, she felt a delusion, as if she were a hero about to commence some heroic act.
Indeed, if her husband failed to understand her noble passion to the end, she was thinking of boldly storming out of this house of confinement, just like Ibsen's Nora.
The husband also knew his wife's feelings. <324>
He thought it pitiful.
He wanted, somehow, to correct her mistaken thoughts.
In front of his wife, who sat with an exaggerated pose as if performing in a play, he tried his best to remain calm and said,
"As I've said many times, that attitude isn't true inspiration from art, just romantic excitement or something like that. Think about it calmly. Firstly, you're not such a genius as to devote your entire life to music, and just because Mr. K or Mr. H praised you, you're all flushed (벍엇케) and excited, but you shouldn't just accept what other men say out of flattery (아첨) at face value (그대루). Why, does vocal music performed while doing housework (살님사리) always smell of diapers (기적이냄새)? The more you hear such things, the less you think of properly refuting (반발헐) them---"
However, the wife, already excited by her own thoughts, was in no state to meekly listen to her husband's opinion.
"No need for long words. Now, the final question. So, you're determined to make (맨들) me just a mother?"
"Ha, really, am I asking you to become just a mother? I'm saying to nurture you as an artist, nurture you very well, and try to make housekeeping and art compatible."
"What if they can't be compatible?"
"Why can't they? They can. Just don't mind what other people chatter about."
"What if they really can't be?"
"You'd give up music, what else? It's awkward for me to say it, but please consider the peaceful happiness of an ordinary person until the end."
"No, no! You're a scientist, so you're rational (이지적) like that, but I'm an artist, and because I'm an artist, I'm emotional (감정적). Hmph, the happiness of an ordinary person? Then what about me as a true human being, and what about my mission (사명) as an artist?"
The husband looked incredulously at his wife's dramatic words and gestures (제스추어), like lines from a play (대사), and then,
"Has she gone mad?"
He muttered, running a hand through his hair.
"Only when she hits her head against a cliff will she come to her senses. I can't watch her getting carried away, intoxicated, and running wild pointlessly. <325> Think about responsibility, responsibility! If you ask your conscience, would such words come out of your own mouth? Just playing along with the tongues of insincere men."
And then,
"Did you read the biography of Madame Curie I bought you the other day? Compare yourself to her. Who exactly is Madame Curie? Did you see the chronology attached in the appendix? While leaving such achievements, did she abandon her family? Did she search for 'herself as a human being' smartly like someone? Well, there are many similar people in Chosŏn too, like Madame Chŏng Sŏng-mo, she's a vocalist just like you, so you must have heard at least as much about her as I have. For a woman, beyond being female, everything else is secondary, tertiary."
However, the wife did not back down either.
"When the wife is unknown, you want to make her famous; when the wife becomes famous, pointless jealousy (질투) arises. It's a common occurrence."
"Ugh, you wretched thing!"
"In a short life, only art is long. I believe that anew."
"………………"
"Tomorrow from six in the afternoon is the celebration party for my appointment as a professor (취임축하연). Do you know how excited the principal is that someone like me is taking up the position at that music academy?"
"………………"
"Even just for the wishes (요망) of such people, I have to make an effort. You should understand that much."
"Ai, wicked (납분)……. Shameless thing."
"Wicked what? Hmph, let's see."
They didn't speak to each other until the next morning.
---◇---
The next day, partly out of defiance towards her husband, Yŏng-hwa left the house around noon (오정때쯤). <326>
She felt she might have been a bit harsh towards her husband last night in her excitement, but she excused herself thinking that such decisiveness (과단) was necessary to enlighten (게몽하기에는) her insensitive husband. After visiting a friend, she strolled alone down Ponjŏngt'ong and entered her usual tea house.
There weren't many customers, but a woman sitting in a chair in the far corner
"Ah!"
greeted her warmly.
It was a doctor's wife whom acquaintances called a "socialite."
Though from a different department, she was a fellow graduate from E Women's College with Yŏng-hwa.
"I hated sitting blankly at home, so I came out for tea, but I was bored. So, I was just waiting to see if anyone I knew would come in."
She wore a flowing velvet durumagi (두루맥이) and placed a dark fox-fur scarf on the side of the table (테불).
Then, even before Yŏng-hwa had fully settled into her seat, she began,
"You know that pianist, Mr. S?"
"Yes, that Mr. S. Apparently, he grabbed the wrist of a female student who comes to his house for piano lessons, or something like that. You really can't tell about people. Last night, several acquaintances went to see a movie at the Meijiza Theater (명치좌), and on the way out, we stopped right (바루) here. Listening to the talk going around, the world is truly frightening."
The friend, as was her habit, narrowed one eye suggestively and continued,
"Among them were novelists, critics, and also people in music, but how well they know other people's affairs, and how ugly the hidden sides (이면) of the people who become topics of conversation are. Famous figures (명사) are one thing, artists are another, and famous people even more so. But they say that regarding Yŏng-hwa, since your husband (밖았선생님) is so proper (시카리), there haven't been any scandals (말성) yet." <327>
Whether the friend was being sarcastic or flattering, she smiled brightly, but Yŏng-hwa felt quite unpleasant listening.
"Besides, maybe artists are inherently like that, their pride is ridiculously high. How loudly they boast 'I'm the best!' The person with them feels embarrassed. Even if they are novelists or whatever, the only people who truly acknowledge them are probably just literary youths; do they amount to even one-hundredth of what they think of themselves?"
"So, what did they say about me?"
"It's human nature to smear honey on their lips to your face and stab you in the back once you turn around. Even if they murmur things about you, like your volume (성량) is big but your tone color (성색) is wrong, I already heard it as mere inventory-taking (다나오로시) born out of jealousy. It's all nonsense."
Her husband's words from last night seemed to have followed her here, and Yŏng-hwa lost her appetite for tea.
"Seriously, those people, what intentions (행방) do they have? To your face, they say 'Teacher, Teacher,' but as soon as they turn around, they call your name (일음) casually, 'So-and-so did blah blah,' as if you were their house servant (하인). People like Yŏng-hwa probably live knowing all this already, but still, it's easy to just judge by appearances (겉만보기). It might sound presumptuous (입찬소린진), but if you only see the flattering (추켜올니는) side and think you're a queen (왕), you'll get badly hurt. You know they encourage you to climb the tree (나무에 오르라구 흔드는것알어)."
More than the love affair of Mr. S, whom Yŏng-hwa had also known as a man of character, or the embezzlement incident involving Mr. Y and a friend's wife, what bothered Yŏng-hwa most was the fact that everyone had gathered to tear apart and mock (찟고 까불고) her art, and she wondered what else those people, who had indeed praised her like a queen to her face, had said behind her back.
"If you look closely, they are all two-faced (속딴관 겉딴관). Really, even as we sit here like this, who knows where and who is talking about us? But Yŏng-hwa, well, since your husband (밖았선생님) is so proper (식카리)……"
Yŏng-hwa declined her friend's offer of new tea and left for the street.
Her mood was completely ruined.
What made it even more unpleasant was that she couldn't deny the truth of what she had just heard. The friend's attitude, scratching around the issue instead of offering frank advice, was also unpleasant. <328>
Knowing full well that these were people who would dig up every fault once her back was turned, she felt angry at herself for having unknowingly climbed onto the tips of their tongues, and at the same time, a rebellious spirit arose, determined not to be broken by mere backbiting.
Trying to shift her unpleasant mood, Yŏng-hwa found a flower shop and bought carnations (카네이숀) and roses.
The two types of flowers, blooming in different seasons, pressed their heads together inside thin paraffin (파라핀) paper. To her eyes, they looked unnatural, like herself and her husband living together in one house with different thoughts.
Leaving the flower shop, she checked her watch; it was half-past four.
There was still an hour and a half until the six o'clock movie time.
She turned towards Meiji-chō and walked slowly towards Chongno, wondering how to spend this not-so-short time.
Of course, she didn't want to go home in her still gloomy mood.
Yet, going to a tea house alone was also tiresome---just then, the sign for the XXX Beauty Salon (미용원) caught her eye.
Yŏng-hwa felt pleased as if she had discovered an oasis (오아시쓰) to relieve her melancholy.
Although it hadn't been long since she last had her hair done, the young hairdresser (미용사) at that salon was, after all, Yŏng-hwa's ardent admirer (찬미자) and hopeful fan (美望家). Or at least, that's what Yŏng-hwa herself believed.
Expecting not so much a hairdo but the greatest welcome and words of admiration, she opened the door to the beauty salon.
However, perhaps it truly was an unlucky day, the beauty salon was bustling, and the young hairdresser, Yŏng-hwa's admirer, was also head over heels (곤두박질) busy. Instead of the expected welcome, she merely nodded (끗덕함하며) and smiled from the mirror.
Yŏng-hwa was quite dissatisfied and plopped down onto a chair placed to one side.
It was then.
Three or four kisaeng (기생) huddled together on the sofa (쏘까) near the door glanced her way and giggled.
Yŏng-hwa, starved for any sign of human warmth all day, felt her eyes light up.
She assumed they recognized her. <329>
Thinking that even among this class (계급) of women, kisaeng, with whom she had absolutely no contact, there were admirers, seemed to dispel at least a part of the unpleasantness she had endured since morning.
Yŏng-hwa straightened her posture and sent them her most dignified and benevolent smile.
In her mind, she was already prepared not to disdainfully dismiss even if some among them came forward to offer praise, following the example (례) of Mary Magdalene (막달나마리아) who washed Jesus' feet.
However, things didn't turn out that way. Upon meeting Yŏng-hwa's benevolent gaze, the kisaeng somehow all turned their eyes away, looked elsewhere, and snickered again.
It was utterly incomprehensible.
Yŏng-hwa felt a displeasure several times greater than before—or rather, a feeling closer to humiliation—and picked up a discarded magazine.
It was a strange day.
It felt as if a demon was following her, constantly trying to interfere.
Of course, the letters in the magazine didn't register in her eyes.
No matter how long she waited, the hairdresser's hands didn't become free. Glancing over, the kisaeng who had laughed earlier were now nonchalantly chatting among themselves.
Five-ten, five-twenty, five-thirty---
Yŏng-hwa couldn't bear it any longer.
Suppressing the anger that made her want to tear the ceiling down, she stormed out into the corridor.
"Ugh, those wretched women! How dare they, towards me……"
As she descended the stairs, she suddenly remembered she had left the flowers behind on the chair where she had been sitting.
Although she felt awkward re-entering the salon she had just stormed out of irritably, Yŏng-hwa turned back without a second thought.
Trying to be quieter than before, she reached the door, and heard bursts of unrestrained laughter. <330>
She felt awkward and stopped, shuffling her feet.
Immediately following the laughter came a sharp voice,
"What was that just now, the one who left?"
Yŏng-hwa lacked the courage to twist the handle (헨들).
"Oh my, I almost died laughing. Gosh, is this beauty salon a public toilet (소통 제거인가)? So young and acting so stuck-up (뻐기기는)! Damn it (제엔장)!"
"And even then, when we laughed, she actually grinned back? The silly woman!"
"Her poor husband (서방놈)."
"No, what on earth was that?"
Although the lower half of the wall was solid with a door, the upper half was open, so the voices from inside were clearly audible, as if grasped in her hand.
"Really, in this world, there are all sorts of men and women."
"Of course there are! There are people like you, and people like Inspector Hwang."
"Crazy woman!"
"Why, there are people like me, and people like the woman who just left……"
Yŏng-hwa couldn't understand why she had to endure such humiliation.
She stood trembling, one hand on the handle.
Wouldn't the hairdresser clarify soon?
Wouldn't she introduce her as Kim Yŏng-hwa to that ignorant swarm of bluebottles (청파리떼)?
Wouldn't she teach them that she was a person of honor deserving seventy-seven times the pride she had shown earlier, making those women shiver with awe and tremble with apology?
Yŏng-hwa waited for the hairdresser to utter her name, intending to push the door open the moment it was spoken. <331>
Thus, upon the heads of the kisaeng bowing in apology (배례), she would reclaim her honor as an artist.
She waited intently for the hairdresser's voice.
After a while, the hairdresser's voice was heard, answering a kisaeng's third question.
However, the tone was unexpected.
"The person who left earlier? Probably a musician or something, but apparently she's always that arrogant. As the old saying goes, 'Empty vessels make the most sound (속없는게 뻐긴다드니),' probably someone like that, huh?"
When Yŏng-hwa heard that voice, she felt a shock, as if struck hard on the bridge of her nose!
Her ears were ringing.
Into those darkened ears came the sound of,
"What kind of musician is she?"
"Haven't you seen that kind of chanteuse (샹롱?) on record shop (유성기상점) signs?"
"Just nonsense, probably. Pfft, pfft (풍아 풍?)."
"Gosh, and that makeup!"
"Hey, is that powder or mud plaster (진흙칠)?"
Another burst of laughter erupted.
Yŏng-hwa didn't know what to do.
Thinking she was being insulted by kisaeng, she trembled with rage.
But however angry she was, she couldn't barge in and vent her anger. If those ignorant, shameless creatures pointed out facts one by one to refute her, what could she say?
"Bitches to kill, bitches to beat to death!" <332>
She bit her lip, twisting her body to calm this raging turmoil (격동).
Last night, her husband had said she would eventually hit her head against a cliff (이마를 땅 부디치리라고), and indeed, she had hit her head.
However, she truly hadn't known that less than a day and night (일주야) after her husband made such a prophecy, cliffs to hit her head against would be prepared continuously like this.
Even her husband, who said such things, probably wouldn't have known.
Yŏng-hwa thought, like religious people (신앙가) say, that God truly exists and punishes sinful humans.
"Frightening. Frightening."
She wanted to writhe and weep aloud.
The sound of shoes approached, climbing the stairs.
Yŏng-hwa, lacking the strength to stand any longer, wrapped her scarf (목도리) around her face as much as possible to hide it and walked away quietly (초연히).

Evening darkness (어둠) was already settling calmly on the street.
Anew, the various insults she had just received pierced her bones.
Her legs trembled.
Hurrying along the pavement (포도), Yŏng-hwa tried hard to dismiss those kisaeng as worthless, as beings below consideration (안긴이하의 레벨), to laugh them off.
---Hmph, ignorant, dirty, shameless creatures who do nothing but suck up to men like mosquitoes, bedbugs, fleas--- she tried not to even consider them an issue.
"As the common saying goes, one avoids dung not because it's scary, but because it's dirty---"
So, because the dung was dirty, she had retreated without arguing with them, she tried to think. <333>
However, no matter how many reasons she attached to console herself, the indignation she felt did not subside one bit.
The more she looked down on those who insulted her, the more her own value, having been insulted by such worthless beings, seemed to diminish, and she couldn't help it.
"Frightening world. Frightening world."
Her eyes saw countless packs of wolves (이리떼).
The various artists she had respected as apostles of art, that young hairdresser, the many people who claimed to admire her from long ago, those kisaeng just now, and finally, the numerous people coming and going on the street—all seemed like wolves wearing human skin.
And those wolf packs seemed to be secretly licking their lips and sneering,
"What kind of musician is she?"
Yŏng-hwa wanted to cover her ears and close her eyes.
Of course, she had not the slightest intention of attending the reception.
Ah, must all her pride (긍지), honor, and talent be trampled mercilessly by the habitual slander of mere kisaeng? Was the existence of the female vocalist Kim Yŏng-hwa, who until yesterday, no, until just moments ago, prided herself as a chosen genius, truly so worthless? --- As Yŏng-hwa walked, choosing dark shadows, without even thinking of taking the streetcar, an indescribable sorrow gently (유연히) welled up in her chest as the indignation gradually subsided.
As a reaction to the trembling rage, she now unconditionally felt herself to be an extremely worthless person deserving such insults.
She seemed to regret having fiercely resisted her husband's gentle (온자한) advice, yet being unable to utter a single word in response to the insults of those kisaeng and turning away (돌처선). She also felt sorry to face her husband again.
Suddenly, a fiery affection for her husband surged through her entire body.
She missed her child too.
When she reached the dark slope in front of her house, quickening her steps, thinking about how she had been mingling among the wolves of the world, receiving all sorts of backbiting and insults, leaving behind such a husband and such a child, regret for her foolish (미욱했든) self and pity (린민) surged up, and she felt hot tears moistening her cheeks. <334>
She felt she now deeply understood the "peaceful happiness of an ordinary person" that her husband had so insistently (찾궂이) spoken of.
Why hadn't she realized it until now? Why hadn't she?
On the middle of the sloping hill, Yŏng-hwa looked up (울으러보았다) at the sky.
Over there, the streetlamp (가둥) of her house shone.
She mustered her courage and took a step forward (내드디었다).
But the next moment, she felt a sharp impact on the left side (좌반신) of her body, and the heel of her left shoe snapped off.
It was a pair of high heels (하이힐) she had bought just a few days ago, even though the ones she wore before were still perfectly fine (말장하건만).
"From now on, I should wear rubber shoes (고무신)."
This was the very first (먼첨) thought Yŏng-hwa had.
At any other time, she would have first looked around to see if anyone was watching, picked up the heel, tried to fix (마처보고) it, but today, maintaining her posture, she waited for the pain in her ankle to subside, then walked on without looking around.
A bright electric light was on in the main room (마루) of Yŏng-hwa's house, and the smell of boiling cabbage stew (욱어지찌개) drifted through the crack in the door.
Like a prodigal son (탕자) finding his father's house after a long time, she hesitated for a moment, then shook the main gate.
"Halmŏm, please open the gate for me."
But before her words even finished, the sound of a sliding door opening was heard, and thump-thump-thump, the child ran out onto the main floor.
"Oh my, Mommy! Mommy, Mommy, Mommy's home!"
Yŏng-hwa almost pushed aside the old woman (할멈) opening the gate and rushed to the edge of the main floor.
"Mommy, how come you're home early? Daddy said you'd be late again today and told me not to wait up and go to sleep. But I said I didn't want to.
Daddy was just saying that, right? Daddy was lying (거짓뿌렁)."
"No, dear. Daddy's words were right. Everything... everything... everything..."
Unable to hold back any longer, the young mother hugged her young son's neck tightly (얼싸안고) and sobbed, "Heu-heu-heu-heu." (End) <335>

**Summary**

Kim Yŏng-hwa, a talented soprano, returns to her music studies in Tokyo with her husband's encouragement, leaving her young child behind. Upon her return after two years, she achieves great success and fame. However, this fame leads to vanity and neglect of her family duties, causing friction with her supportive but increasingly concerned husband. He argues for a balance between art and family, while she insists on pursuing her artistic "destiny," viewing family as a hindrance. After a harsh argument and a series of humiliating encounters—being gossiped about by peers, realizing supposed admirers are insincere, and being openly mocked by kisaeng—Yŏng-hwa experiences a profound crisis of confidence. Humbled and disillusioned with the superficiality of fame, she returns home early, breaks her high heel (symbolizing her vanity), and tearfully embraces her child, finally understanding her husband's plea for ordinary family happiness.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the lives of educated, "New Women" in late colonial Korea, grappling with the tension between traditional gender roles (wife and mother) and modern aspirations (a career in the arts). It reflects contemporary debates about whether women could successfully balance public careers and domestic life. The story also portrays aspects of urban life in Seoul (Keijō), including social gatherings, the importance of reputation, the presence of modern institutions like department stores, tea houses, and beauty salons, and the mixing of Korean and Japanese cultural elements (e.g., Japanese loanwords like *shikkari* and *tanaoroshi*, mentioning Tokyo, Meijiza Theater). The protagonist's internal conflict and eventual return to domesticity could be interpreted in various ways, perhaps reflecting societal pressures or the author's own views on the "proper" sphere for women, even talented ones, during that era.

그러나 영화의 이 같이 갸륵한 결심은
그는 날이 갈수록 또 다른 어떤 현상을 깨닫지 않을 수 없었으니 그것은 어느새 자기가 무척 유명해저 있다는 사실이었다.

하고 입을 삐죽이었으나 실상은 여간 유쾌하지 않었다.
하로 아침 눈을 뜨니 어느새 유명해저 있드라는 「빠이론」도 아마 자기같은 행운이었든가 부다고 생각한다.
비단 편지뿐이 아니었다.
신문 잡지사의 좌담회 같은 것이며 방송국에서 마중 온 자동차가 바뿌게 집 앞 비스듬한 언덕길을 헐덕이며 오르나렸다. 여성 잡지에는 커단 활자로 그의 반생기가 실니였다. 그리고 크로쓰업된 초상화보의 사태.
교회에 가면 어느새 찬양대의 지휘를 맡기고 무슨 회합같은 데서는 몇 해전까지 선생으로 선배로 우러러 보든 이들과 당연히 자리를 나란히 한다.
길에서 동무를 맞나면
「영화는 너무 성공을 해서 우리들 겉은 게 마주 서기도 어려워」
하고 이 편에서 아른 척 해준 것을 고마워한다.
남이 칭찬을 해 주어 싫은 사람은 없을 것이다.
백화점 같은데 무엇을 사러 들어가고 여점원들이 힐끔힐끔 자기를 바라보며 서로 속삭인다.
필시(저이가 유명한 쏘푸래노 아무개야)하며 부러워 하는 것이리라 생각된다.
전차를 탔을 때 어태 커다랗게 떠들고있든 남학생패들이 갑자기 잠잠해지며 입가에 경의(敬意)를 표하는 우슴을 띠우는 일도 이었다.
역시 자기노래를 들은일이 있는 사람이리라 생각한다.
아무튼 귀경한지 반년도 못되여 영화는 확실히 장안 유명인의 한사람이 된 모양이었다.
영화도 처음에는 이같은 명예(?)가 낯간지러운것같기도 하고 과만한 것 같기도 했지만 하도 여러번 그런 경우를 당하는동안에 이제와서는 그같은 찬사며 동경하는 태도들이 당연히 받아야할 권리인냥 편지도 오지않고누가 칭찬하는 말도 해주지안는날은 자연 기분까지 조치않었다.
이러한 때면 비록 다 저녁때라도 옷을 가라입고 한박휘 휘돌아와야 마음이 가라앉는다.＜319＞
종노나 본정통이나 아무튼 사람많은 곳에 나가기만하면 한두사람 선망의 눈동자를 발견할 수가 있었다.
이러히게 하여 평온하든 영화의 가슴속에 차차 교만과 득의의 싹이 터오르기 시작하였다.
남편은 안해의 이같은 상태를 결코 유쾌한 일로 생각하지 않었다.
외출이 자즌 영화가 의복이며 화장이며 그런것에 대해서 전에 없이 이상한 취미를 가지기시작한데 대해서도 좋게 녁이지 않는모양이었다.
언제 오느곳에 자기를 아는 사람이 있는지도 알수없는데 안해의 꼴이 추한 것은 남편까지 불명예가 아니겠느냐고 욱여대는 영화의 변명을 들으면서도 남편은 진흙가루같이 싯누런 오클의 분이며 매니규어요 빨간 에나멜이 얹회있는 안해의 경대우를 바라보고 한함을 쉬었다.
유명인의 남편---그런 술어가 웬일인지 슬프다 슬픈 말인 듯 뼈에 사모처 느껴지는 것이다.
몇 번의 충고 몇 번의 언쟁이 있은뒤에도 안해의 외출은 줄어들지 않었다.
나중에는 어디가서 무슨 충동이를 받고왔는지 남편과의 별거를 희망하기 시작하였다.
예술가로써의 자기자신을 좀 더 성장시키기 위하여 속박없는 시간도 있어야하겠고 항상 기분을 새롭게하기 위하여 자유로운 여행같은것도 해야한다는 것이다.
남편은 그 생각이 잘못된 것을 루루히 설명해주었다.
안해도 책임이 있는만큼 구지 남편의 말에 대하여 최후의 고집은 하지 않었다.
그러나 가정을 떠난 자유로운 예술가 생활이란것에 대하여 공상의 날개는 끊짐없이 펴보는 모양이었다.
어쩌다 기분이 좋지못할때에는 높은 포부와 훌륭한 소질을 가졌으면서도 가정에 매여 꼼짝할 수 없는 운명을 탄식하고 입에내여 말하는 일도 있었다.
남편을 분개하고 가정을 저주하였다.
나중에는 몰리해하고다 인정되는 남펀에게 대한 반발도 겸하여 아침만 필하면 의례 집을 나서는 것이 한습관이 되다싶이 되었다.＜320＞
사람이란 더구나 여자란 나가지않는 버릇이 생기면 부득이한 외출도 귀찮아지는 반면 한번 위출하는 습관이 붙기만하면 무슨 핑계를 작만하고라도 거리에 나서야 한다.
영화의 경우가 그것이었다.
어떤때는 정말 마음을 도사려먹고 외출하지 않으리라하여 일감같은걸 붙잡아도 보나 그럴때면 괜히 짜증이 나고 갑갑하고 그래 결국은 또 외출을 하고만다.
더군다나 서울거리에는 이러한 사람들을 위하여 다행이도 무수한 찻집이란것과 극장이란 것이 있었다.
첫 번 독창회를 연지 일년이 체못되어 그들 부부사이에는 훌륭한 성악가일수록 훌륭한 주부도 될수있는지 없는지 다시 그런 것은 문제가 되지않었다.
선량하고 심약한 남편은 안해의 인권을 무시하지않는 범위내에서 영화의 마음을 가정안에 구속하려 들었고 유명한 안해는 살님의 여기(餘技)로써의 예술, 완농물로써의 음악 이런 것을 부인한지 오래다.
타협할수 없는 두 주장을 담고 일층 행복되자든 가정은 어느새 완연히 불행한채 다시 얼마가 지난 어느날 그들은 드디어 큰 입다툼을 하고 말었다.
---◇---
그것은 안해가 단독으로 벌서부터 말이 있어오든 어떤 음악학원의 전임강사를 승낙하고 온날이었다.
밤이 늦어 열두시나 되었을 때 자동차로 돌아온 영화의 입에서는 약간 술냄새까지 있는것같았다.
남편은 그때까지 눕지도 않고 불죽은 화로옆에 묵언히 쪼구리고 앉아있었다.
그눈앞에서 목도리를 들으고 붉은빚많은 치맛자락을 펄치고 앉으며
「참 여잔 할 수 없어, 암만 예술에대한 정열이나 열심히 대단해두 할수없다니까. 남자들은 안해나자식이 아니드라두 살아갈수 있지만 여자에게 가정이란 아주 숙명이니까」
그리고는 말을 끊었다가
「글세 오늘 그 음악학원에 잠간 들넜드니 H씨랑 K씨랑 와서있다가 그냥 날 공격하는군요. 살님사리에 매＜321＞여 안일을 탐한다나 어쩐다나. 아마 그학원에서 말해도든 교수자리에 여태 거절해온건 내의사보담 당신 의사인걸 모두들 알고있는 모양이두군요」
안해는 기분이 좋아 재절거리나 남편은 마무말도 없었다.
「그리군 우리여자들이 자기네들 남자들처럼 예술에 대해서 그만한 정열 뭐 연애라두 헐만헌 정열이 없다나요. 예술을 직업으로 알문 그까짓 살님사리가 뭐겠느냐구. 아주 억울해 죽을뻔 했다우」
「………………」
「아 그랬드니 여기저기서 모두를 튀여나와 한마디씩 참견을 허는군요. 여류가 반성할 점은 확실히 있다구. 아주 몸을 던질만한 강한 정열을 가저야지 그저 이일 저일하는 틈틈이 일테면 살님의 여기(餘技)두 노랠하구 피아노를 치구하는건 아주틀닌 관념이라구」
그래도 남편은 아무말도 없다.
영화는 남편몰래 아랬입술을 한번 쑤욱 내밀었다.
「그래 내 하두 분허길내 어디 우리 여자들헌테두 예술을 생명으루 알 수 있는 정열이 있는지 없는지 보여주겠다구 장담을 허구 왔자」
그러나 안해는 제말이 채 끝나기도 전에 입을 담을지않을수 없었다.
남편이 번쩍 얼굴을 든 것이다.
「난 원래 당신이 가정생활까지 희생해야하는 그런 훌륭한 예술가가되라는건 아니었더」
그것은 일즉 들어보지못했든 거칠은 목소리었다.
창백한 얼굴에 충혈된 눈을 부릅뜨고 노려보는 그의 모양을 남편이란 그저 선량한 페미니스트요 한호인이라고만 생각하고 있었든 안해는 어떻게 해석을 해야할찌 잠시 어리둥절하였다.
「내가 당신헌테 바란 것은 끝까지 평화한가정인이었소. 음악공부를 더 권한것도 첫재는 우리가정생활을 좀더 향상시키자는 마음에는 였드니……. 이제와서야 내 생각이 잘못됐든걸 알았어. 되지헌테 진줄 던저주지 자각없는＜322＞인간헌테 은헬 배풀건 아니드군」
그리고는 갑자기 목소리를 낯우어
「내 아까도 그런생각을 허구 있었지만 우리 중학 다닐 때 하숙앞에서 가갤보든 사람이 제가 충분히 배우지 못헌걸 아주 한으루 생각해서 예펜네헌테 학교교육을 시켰드라우. 지금두 막연히 기억허지만 참 가진정성을다썼지. 무슨학교든가 아무튼 중학정도까지 보냈든가봐. 그랬드니 그 여편네 학교마치자 어떻게 됐겠수. 다른 모던청년허구 연앨허구 도망을 가구. 참, 뒤에 남았든 사내꼴 가아엽드라니……」
혼자ㅅ말처럼 이런소리를 중얼거리는 것이다.
처음 멀건이 앉아있든 영화도 비로소 남편의 말뜻을 알아듣기 시작하였다.
「아이 더러. 그래 당신은 그 가개장수구 난 그 예펜네란말이죠」
안해의 목소리도 거칠었다.
「난 그래두 당신을 이해있는 인격자라구 생각햇수. 아이 비겁해, 어따 사람을 비긴단말유. 그래 나두 예술에 정진헌다구 그예펜네처럼 당신을 배반허구 도망헐게란 말이지」
남편의 얼굴에는 완연히 불쾌한 표정이 날아났다.
「올소 당신은 그 가개장수 예편네헌테 비긴다구 펄쩍 뛰지만 바른대루 말허면 당신두 꼭같은 사람야 그야 당신은 교양두있구 그러니 그여자처럼 사낼 얻어서 다라나진 않겠지만 사내대신 예술인가 뭔가 허는 바람둥일따라갈려구 앨쓰구 있으니 더 기가맥히지. 허긴 바른눈으루보면 당신경우가 가개장수예편네 경우보담 몇배나 더 어이없을게요」
「아이분해. 그게 밤낮 이해있다구 내세우는 당신 말이우. 그게 정말 당신 진심이었거든 개처럼 예펜넬 쇠사실루 매여놓지. 입으루만 힌소릴 허구」
「쇠사실루 매놓아서 일이 내맘대루 된다면 매여놓기라도 하겠오. 난 가정이 평화하고 우리생활이 행복되기만 바라는 사람이니까」＜323＞
「가정의 행복? 흥, 가정생활은 누구든지 할수있지만 택함받은 성악가노릇은 몇천 몇만명중에 한사람밖에할수없다든건 누군데. 언젠 남의 소질을 살려주겠다고 야단을허고 또 지금와서 그 싹을 꼬집지못해 성화야」
「허, 누가 당신소질을 죽이랬나. 가정을 불행하게할것까진 없다는것이지」
「말슴말우 난 천하없어두 내 예술을 완성허구 말테니. 하늘이 주신 내 천분에 대해서라두 이러구만 있을수 없어요, 암만 누가 뭐래두 모레부터는 학교에 나가서 가르키구. 독창회 준비두 또허구헐걸」
「망헌 것, 그것만이 예술을 사랑허는게 아냐. 허영이야 허영, 언내 불작난보담두 더 위험헌 자기도취여, 그러니 잔말말구 낼부터는 집구석에 들어앉았어. 좀 광ㅅ중이 가라앉거든 따저 설명해 줄테니」
「뭐요?」
맹열한 반발심이 불같이 타올랐다.
남편은 이 조그만 가정과 자기의 이년동안 이바지한 공노를 미끼로 이 자각한 여류성악가 천재적소질을 가진한 사람의 예술가를 매장하려한다. 아닌게아니라 자기가 예술을 생명으로 알고 성악을 직업으로 안다면 남편이나 아이에게는 불편한 점이 없지않으리라, 그것은 미안하게도 생각하는바다. 그러나 비록 여하한 희생을 당한다하드라도 한사람의 드물게보는 천재성악가의 전도를 어듭게 하는데 비하면 그만희생쯤 아무것도 아닐것이다. 아니 오히려 달갑게 알아야할 그들의 의무인지도 모른다---약간오른 주기와 함께 영화의 신경을 점점흥분되여갔다.
그는 정색을 하고 똑 바로 남편의 눈을 마주보았다.
「여보, 그럼 당신은 내가 예술가로써의 정진에 반대한단 말이죠」
한마디 최후의 질문을 하고나니 자기자신이 무슨 영웅적행동을 개시하려는 용사와같은 착각이났다.
허긴 남편이 끝까지 자기의 고상한 정열을 이해하지 못하면 지금 그는 입센의노라와같이 당당하게 이속박의집을 뛰처나가리라고까지 생각하고 있는 것이다.
남편도 이같은 안해의 마음을 안다.＜324＞

「웨 나겉은것두 있고 지금 나갑신년 것은 겉두 있는데……」
영화는 웨 자기가 이런 오욕을 받아야하는지 알수없었다.
그는 헨들을 한손에 잡은채 부들부들 몸을 떨렀다.
어서 미용사가 석명을 해주지안나.
자기가 김영화인 것을 저 무지한 청파리떼에게 소개해주 안나.
아까 뻐긴것의 일흔일곱배를 뻐기어도 오히려 부족한 명예인인 것을 가르켜주어 어서 저년들의 간담을 서늘케하고 황송한 생각에 떨게해주지않을까.
영화는 미용사의 입에서 자기일음이 떠려지는대로 문을 밀고 드려가리라하였다.＜331＞
그리하여 속죄의 배례를 들이는 기생년들의 머리우에 예술가로써의 명예를 도로 찾으리라하였다.
그는 열심히 미용사의 목소리를 기다렸다.
얼마후 어떤 기생의 세 번째 물음에 대답하는 미용사의 소리가 들렸다.
그려나 그것은 의외의 어조이었다.
「아까 나간이 말예요? 아마 음악가인가본데 원래 그랗게 교만은 하두군요 옛말에도 속없는게 뻐긴다드니 아마 그런 사람이죠. 뭐」
영화는 그 소리를 들었을 때 코ㅅ마를 쾅! 얻어마즌것같은 충동을 받었다.
귀가 확신하였다.
그 캄캄해진 귀ㅅ속에
「그까짓게 무슨 음악가야」
하는 소리가 들리어왔다.
「유성기상점 간판에서두 그런 샹롱 본일 없었어?」
「괘애헌 소리지 뭐. 풍아 풍」
「이이구 그 화장헌거 하구」
「애 그게 분칠이드냐 진흙칠이지」
또 한바탕 웃는소리가 났다.
영화는 어쩔줄을 몰랐다.
기생헌테 모욕을 당한다 생각하니 분해 치가 떨린다.
그려나 아무리 분하드라도 뛰여 들어가 분푸리를 할 수가 없었다. 무지하고 염체없는것들이 일일이 사실을 지적하여 반박해 오면 무어라구 대항하나
「죽일년들, 때려죽일년들」＜332＞
그는 허리를 비틀어가며 이놀 격동을 진정하려 입술을 깨물었다.
어저께밤 남편이 언제든지 절벽에다 이마를 땅 부디치리라고 하드니 참말 이마를 부디친 셈이다.
그려나 남편이 그같은 예언을 한지 일주야도 못된 시각에 이렇게 게속하여 이미를 부디처야할 절벽들이준비되여있을줄은 참말 몰았다.
그런소리를 한 남편도 알지못했을 것이다.
영화는 신앙가들의 말과같이 정말 신(神)이 게시고 그 신이 죄많은 인간에게 징벌을 내리시는것이라 생각한다.
「무서운 일이다. 무서운 일이다」
그는 몸부림을 치고 통곡이 하고 싶었다.
칭칭대를 올라오는 신발소리가 난다.
영화는 다시 더 서있을 기력도 없이 되도록 그얼굴의 많은 부분이 가려지도록 목도리를 둘려싸며 초연히 그곳을 물러났다.
거리에는 벌서 저녁 어둠이 차분히 나리고 있었다.
새삼스럽게 아까 받은 모욕의 갖가지가 뼈에 사모치게 느껴진다.
다리가 부들부들 떨리었다.
허둥허둥 포도를 걸으며 영화는 되도록 아까 그 기생들을 가치없는것들 안긴이하의레벨에 처해있는것들이라 일소에 부처버리려하였다.
---흥, 무식하고 더럽고 파렴치한 행동으로 사냇놈들의 뒤나 빨아먹는 모기 베룩 빈대만두 못한년들---하고 문제도 삼지않으려하였다.
「상말에 똥이 무서워 피하는게 아니라 똥이 더러워 피한다드니---」
그래 똥이 더러워 자기는 그 똥들과 시비를 가리지 않고 물너나온것이라 생각하려하였다.＜333＞
그러나 암만 여러 가지 이유를 부처 자위(自慰)하려하여도 한번 느낀 분함은 조금도 가라앉지 않었다.
모욕하든 것들을 업수이 역이면 업수이 역일수록 그런 가치없는것들에게 모욕을 당한 자기의 가치는 일층 저하되는 것을 어찌할수없었다.
「무서운 세상. 무서운 세상」
그의 눈에는 무수한 이리떼가 보였다.
예술의 사도라고 서로 존경했든 여러 예술가며 그 젊은 미용사와 일즉부터 자기를 숭배한다는 여러사람들 아까 그 기생 나종에는 길가에 오고가는 수많은 사람들이 모다 인간의 껍질을 덮어쓴 이리들로 뵈이는 것이다.
그리하여 그 이리떼들이 몰내 혀끝을 날름거리며
「저게 무슨 음악가야」
하고 비웃는듯하다.
영화는 귀를 막고 눈을 감고 싶었다.
물론 환영회에 출석할 의사는 털끝만큼도 없다.
아, 조그만 기생들의 상습적인 험구에 자기의 모든 긍지와 명예와 실력이 여지없이 짓밟혀야 한단말인가. 어저께까지도 아니 아까까지도 택함받은 천재라고 자부하고있든 여류성악가 김영화의 존재란 그렇게 무가치한것이었든가.---전차를 탈 생각도 않고 어두운 그늘을 골라 것는 영화의 가슴속에는 차차 분함이 가라앉는대로 형용할 수 없는 슬픔이 유연히 끊어올랐다.
몸이 떨리게 격분했든 반동을 이제는 무조건하고 자기가 그런모욕을 받아야할 심히 가지없는 인간으로 생각되는 것이다.
남편의 온자한 충고에는 기를 쓰고 반항하면서 그런 기생들의 모욕에 말한마디 못해주고 돌처선 것이 후회되는것도 같고 다시 남편에게 대하기도 미안한것같기도 했다.
갑자기 남편에게 향하는 불같은 애정이 온몸을 구비처 돌았다.
어린것도 보고싶었다.
거름을 빨리하여 집앞 어둑한 비탈길까지 왔을 때 그같은 남편 그같은 어린 것을 두고 세상의 이리떼속에 섞＜334＞이어 가진 뒷손구락과 가진 모욕을 받고다니든 것을 생각하면 미욱했든 자신에게 대하여 후회와 린민의 정이 치밀어 뜨거운 눈물이 뺨을 적시는 것을 느낀다.
남편이 그렇게도 찾궂이 말하든「평범인의 평화한 행복」이란 것을 이제야 절절히 느끼는 것같다.
웨 자기는 여태 그것을 깨닫지 못했든고. 웨 못했든고.
비스듬한 언덕 중턱에서 영화는 하늘을 울으러보았다.
저만큼 내집의 가둥이 비최고 있다.
그는 용기를 내여 한발을 내드디었다.
그러나 다음순간 몸의 좌반신에 비끗하는 충격을 느끼며 왼편 구두굽이 딱 붙어졌다.
먼저 신든 것이 아즉 말장하건만 몇일전에 새로 지어신은 하이힐이였다.
「이제부터는 고무신을 신어야겠다」
영화가 제일 먼첨 느낀 것은 이런생각이었다.
다른때같으면 먼첨 누가 보지않었나 사방을 돌아보고 굽을 집어 마처보고 할것이나 이날 그는 똑바로 자세를 가진체 한참발목의 아픔을 진정하자 주위도 살펴보지않고 그대 걸어갔다.
영화의 집 마루에는 밝은 전등불이 키어있고 욱어지찌개 끌는 냄새가 문틈을 흘러나왔다.
그는 정말 오란만에 아비의 집을 찾아온 탕자같이 한참 주저를하다가 대문을 흔들었다.
「할멈 나 문좀 열어주」
그러나 그말이 채 끝나기도 전에 미닫이 여는 소리가 드윽 나며 콩쿵콩 아이가 마루로 뛰여나왔다.
「어규 엄마유, 엄마, 엄마, 엄마왔네」
영화는 대문을 여는 할멈의 몸을 미러 버리듯하고 마루 끝에 달려갔다.
「엄마 어떻게 일즉왔우. 저 아빠가 오늘두 엄마 늦게 온다구 나더러 기다리지말구 자라구. 그래두 제싫다.
구 그랬지. 아빠 괜히 그러지, 아빠가거짓뿌렁」
「아니다. 아빠말슴이 옳으시단다. 모두 모두……모두……」
더 참을수없이 젊은 엄마는 어린아들의 목을 얼싸안고 흐흐흐흐 소리를 내여 울고말었다. (끝)＜335＞
