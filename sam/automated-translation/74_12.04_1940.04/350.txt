Women's Art,

Sea Grief
Mo Yunsuk

Along the edge of the sandy shore,
Digging footprints,
I walked alone.

The salty scent (香) blown by the white water
Leans on the wind and comes near;
Below my eyebrows, it feels refreshing,
And my drowsy face awakens.

My body's shadow, as if about to fly,
Coils with the haze<305>
Stretching its shade upon the sand.
On the vast, sleeping sea,
A sail rests quietly.

Like a young lady's villa,
The cement building's windows are broken,
And inside, white laughter lies frozen.
Even fragments of a turquoise vase,
Which might have held asparagus,
Are swept away by the long trail of water.

Here, where no one is,
Only the abandoned blue dreams
Swell like soft foam near the eyes, then vanish.

Those times when life was joyful with me<306>
Where have they all been erased to?
On the empty water's edge,
A crumpled cement house, and I—
Today, I lie drinking in the lonely sun.

My fingertips, so scarlet red
They seem they would burn if touched, grow numb.
Where can I go to warm my body?
Let a mountain bird pass by,
Let the waves rise,
Let something collide with me.
O Sea, like a tranquil painted scene, what are you doing?
Truly, I want to make you angry.

February 16th, At the Inch'ŏn seaside<307>

Hospital Room
No Ch'ŏnmyŏng

A white Western-style room with neat glass doors.
Over there, they are busy repairing a motor's breakdown.
A beautiful morning.

In Box No. 6,
Memories of that woman's beautiful days
Are ruminated upon day after day on the bed.

The lady who visited like a swallow, leaving red carnations,
Does not visit again.

My temperature chart drew a parabola again today.

On the opposite side, where they say water is drawn from the ribs,<308>
A patient like a ginseng root
Has another visitor—
Mother brought something,
And siblings come and go until dusk.

I rather turn away
And stare like an eagle at the main building's clock tower.

The afternoon melancholy grows thick as mugwort.

The doctor truly doesn't know where I ache.
Chlorocalcium—he calls for the same injection today.
Meekly offering my arm to the syringe,
Turning my head to look out the window,
I actually think this—

Just to spit out blood like camellia flowers,
Copiously,
And die before turning thirty, leaving the twenties behind—

How splendid that would be—<309>

Deceased Mother
Chu Suwŏn

Believing without grounds
That I could always serve you,
You vanished like embers
In an illness of just a few days.
The heavy resentment in your heart,
How did you leave it behind?

In sixty years of life, was there ever
A single day of comfort?
The regrets you said couldn't be resolved<310>
Even if written in books piled higher than the path,
Without a single word,
You left silently.

As the world is harsh,
Your kind heart suffered more,
Lacking blessings in life,
You endured greater hardship.
Though I weep blood after you've gone,
What comfort can it be?

Throughout your life, like a single day,
You offered your body, ground your bones,
Forgetting sleep and meals, you sacrificed—
May God repay you.
This sin of failing in my filial duty,
Where can I go to appeal?

January 15th<311>

Kwanak Mountain
Chang Chŏngsim

Above are myriad peaks, below are thousand valleys,
In every valley are green pines, everywhere spring flowers.
The whole mountain, red and blue, resembles waves of clouds.

Pink flowers among the pines are like shy maidens,
Flowers holding buds are like a young girl's smile.
Life's journey, too, may it be like a spring path.

Approaching Yŏmburam temple, the monk's voice
Enters deep into my ears; the worldly person seems transformed,
Forgetting all myriad worries, feeling like a monk.<312>

At quiet Sammaksa temple, while the group slept,
The sound of the dog barking by the gate woke even the worldly person;
Would not the divine Buddha have already awakened?

Shōwa 14 (1939), April 20th<313>

Sigh
Son Ch'o'ak

If there were no love in this world,
If there were no tears of men,
I too might have become stronger.
Like this, my purpose unchanged,
Like this, not becoming a fool,
I wouldn't have lived in deception.
Ah, I too might have known happiness.

Untitled
How many times have I buried my face in the damp earth,
Exhausted, I tried to reflect.
Fate demands sacrifice in a straight line,
Bringing no small harvest.

Will I become a living corpse, without blood or breath?
No! Strength! Strength alone breaks through all difficulties.
And so I set out again,
To gather fragments of strength.

**Summary**

This collection features poems by five Korean women poets published in 1940. Mo Yunsuk's "Sea Grief" expresses desolate loneliness and lost dreams by the sea. No Ch'ŏnmyŏng's "Hospital Room" delves into the isolation of illness and hidden despair, culminating in a startling wish for a dramatic death. Chu Suwŏn's "Deceased Mother" is a poignant lament filled with grief and filial regret. Chang Chŏngsim's "Kwanak Mountain" paints a serene picture of nature's beauty and finds spiritual solace in a temple setting. Son Ch'o'ak's poems convey disillusionment with love ("Sigh") and a weary but determined resolve to find strength amidst hardship ("Untitled"). The poems offer a glimpse into the emotional and artistic lives of Korean women during the late colonial period, reflecting themes of loss, suffering, nature, and resilience. Historians might find the raw expression of personal sorrow, existential angst (particularly No Ch'ŏnmyŏng's poem), and the articulation of female interiority within the constraints of the era particularly valuable. The blend of traditional lyrical forms and modern sensibilities is also noteworthy.

女人藝術,
海愁
毛允淑

모랫가 언저리로
발자욱을 파며
나는 혼자 갔다.

힌 물이 뿜어 오는 간간한 내(香)가
바람에 기우려 가까이 오면
눈섭 밑이 상그러워
조으롭든 얼굴이 깬다.

날릴 듯한 몸 그림자
연기를 서리며＜305＞
모래 우에 그늘을 이여 가고
한 폭 잠든 바다엔
돗폭이 조용히 안기였다.

아씨의 별장인 듯한
세멘트건물은 창이 부서지고
그 속엔 힌 웃음들이 얼어 있다
아스파라카쓰라도 담겼을 듯한
유록빛 화병조박도
기ㄴ물자락에 쓸여 가고.

아무도 없는 여기
버리고 간 푸른 꿈만이
연한 거품처럼
눈ㅅ가에 부푸렀다 꺼진다.

나와 함께 인생이 즐겁든 그 시간들도＜306＞
다- 어데로 지워저 가고
빈 바다 물기슭에
찌그러진 세멘트집 하나 그리고 나
오늘 저 외로운 태양을 마시며 누어 있다.

그으면 탈듯도 한
새빨간 손끝이 저려 온다
어데 가서 몸을 녹이나?
산새라도 지나렴
파도라도 일어나렴
무에라도 내게 부디처 오렴
靜한 그림 폭인 양 바다야 그게 뭐냐?
진정 나는 너를 노엽게 하고 싶다.
