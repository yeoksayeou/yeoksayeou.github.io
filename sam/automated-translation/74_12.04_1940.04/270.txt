**Body Text Translation:**
Thoughts of My Departed Mother
						Yang Chŏnghyang (梁貞香)

Miss Yang Chŏnghyang is the daughter of Mr. Yang Kŭnhwan (梁槿煥) and graduated last spring from the literature department of Ewha Women's Specialized School (梨花女子 專門學校). Having lost her mother at the age of four, she spent a sensitive girlhood. Now, she pens this piece yearning for her birth mother, a Japanese woman (내지인), who has gone far away.

The magazine company asked me to write about "Memories of Mother," but truthfully, I left my mother's warm embrace when I was just four years old. So how could I possibly have memories (追憶) of my mother? I wish even the faint scent of her milk, tasted in my infancy, remained in my memory (記憶). Therefore, I most enjoy listening to the recollections (追憶談) told by Mrs. Pak Sunch'ŏn (朴順天), Mrs. Hwang Sindŏk (黃信德), and several others who knew my unknown mother well when they were in Tokyo (東京). The stories flowing from these ladies' mouths, starting with "Your mother was..."—hearing about my mother's daily life (日常 生活), her words and actions (言語 動作), her sorrow (悲哀) at the time of parting (離別), and yet how she remained a thoroughly <195> steadfast woman (女性)—these various tales are, to me, my "Memories of Mother." Listening to these stories, I smile outwardly, but sometimes I feel my heart burn as if tearing apart. Tears well up in my eyes. However, I have never told anyone that I miss my mother. Whenever someone asks, "You miss your mother, don't you?" I always reply, "What? How could I miss a mother whose face I can't even remember?" But this is merely bravado (虛勢), isn't it? Who wouldn't long for their "parent," and who would consciously push their child into a dark pit of misfortune (不幸)? I sometimes hear from my father how much my mother cried, forgetting to eat or sleep, when she had to send me away. I sometimes hear about the various things my mother endured back then and picture the scene (場面) in my mind. And I weep inwardly. At that time, my mother was only my current age, a young woman (女人). How heartbroken she must have been to lose her beloved husband and two lovely young daughters all at once. As I grow older, I cannot help but sympathize with the hardships (苦難) she endured, not just as a mother, but as an unknowable woman (女性). But my mother walked on bravely. She walked on firmly, relying (依賴) on no one. Some people (或人) say she was too cold-hearted (冷情) a woman, but I alone can understand her feelings at that time. I am glad that I can hear from anyone's lips that my mother's appearance (容貌), as well as her heart, was as soft and beautiful as silk.

A "mother" is, of course, the protector (保護者) of a young life (生命), and is she not a bright light like the sun (太陽)? That is why, having lost that bright light, the shadow (陰) of dark fate (運命) first fell upon our two lives (生命). Everyone must have thought, likely since the time humans (人間) were created (創造), that among the myriad, countless words (言語) humans can use, these three letters, "mother," form the most beautiful and deeply yearned-for word (言語). This is because, excluding those in special circumstances and fates (運命), everyone was raised under the touch of a mother's warm hand, realizing and experiencing (體驗) how precious a mother's love is and how indispensable it is for a "young life (生命)". <196> However, I have grown up (成長) without ever knowing a mother's love. Of course, when I was young, my grandmother's (祖母) devoted love and the relatives surrounding (周圍) me became my mothers, and as I grew older, my school teachers and society (社會) took the place of my mother. But these too were merely an abstract (抽象的) "mother" and nothing more than abstract (抽象的) "motherly love." In the end, it boils down to having grown up (成長) safely thanks to social love (社會愛) and human love (人類愛).

But society (社會) is always cold (冷情). And dutiful (義務的). It has no warm feelings (感情). It holds no sense of loyalty (義理). It only stares piercingly with cold eyes of criticism (批判). Sometimes, it was crueler than any substitute (代身) mother. The young soul (魂) that felt that cruelty (冷酷) shivering through its entire body (全身) instinctively (本能的) called out for the unseen "mother." At first, I just called out vaguely, dutifully (義務的). But as days went by, as my soul (魂) grew along with my body (肉體), whenever I felt unbearable constraint (不自由), irrepressible loneliness (寂廖), and unfathomable sorrow (憂愁), "mother" became absolutely (絶對) necessary (必要) for me. Yet, my "real mother" was not given to me. Sometimes my heart would weep, standing before the tightly shut iron gate of fate (運命), pleading (呼訴), and sometimes I would pound on it with my small fists in frustration, but that gate (門) did not open.

For over ten years (十餘年), I persistently, sometimes dutifully (義務的), sometimes with desperate sincerity (眞心), tried pounding on and even attempting to break down the iron gate of fate that hid "my mother." But these were all just futile wishes (祈願) of my past (過去). Futile efforts (努力). However, even if the past (過去) was futile and the present (現在) is impossible (不可能), who can absolutely (絶對) deny (否認) the possibility (可能性) of future (未來) success (成功)? Therefore, I do not despair (失望). Until the day I can joyfully burn away the roots (根) of the longing (心情) for "mother," the dissatisfaction (不滿), and the sorrow (悲哀) I have tasted, all deeply embedded in my heart, <197> I will inwardly and steadily prepare myself just to meet "mother." Even if I cannot meet her before my life ends, isn't living while dreaming (空想) of "mother" also one beautiful dream within life (人生)? I know well that "a mother's love" transcends bloodline (血統), different customs (風俗), and even the Great Wall (萬里城) of rigid boundaries. Born between humans (人間) of different bloodlines (血統), I am undeniably a mixed-heritage woman (混血女). Can you even imagine (想像) the secret agony (苦憫), anguish (懊惱), and suffering (受難) of a mixed-heritage child (混血兒)? Society (社會), unconsciously, persecutes (迫害) us due to the seemingly inviolable conservative thought (保守的 思想) unique to Chosŏn (조선). Although this applies only to a very small number (少數) of people! Just as Chosŏn's old feudalistic thought (舊封建的 思想) or moral concepts (道德的 觀念) looked down upon the children of concubines (妾), they look down upon mixed-heritage children. What sin (罪) lies with the illegitimate child (庶子) or the mixed-heritage child (混血兒) themselves? Illegitimate children simply bear the full impact (影響) of their parents' (父母) immoral acts (非道德的 行爲). They too are nothing more than great living sacrifices (祭物). Aren't mixed-heritage children (混血兒) also just a byproduct (副産物) of a new era (新世紀) and a transitional period for the world? The sorrow (悲哀) and anguish (懊惱) of mixed-heritage children should indeed belong to another century (世紀).

Thinking quietly, the young men and women (青年 男女) of today (現代) carry various kinds of anguish (懊惱). Suppressed (禁壓) from moving right or left, they are merely gasping in the suffocating (窒息) air (空氣) of stagnation (沈滯). To the anguish (懊惱) of growing up is added (加重) the anguish (懊惱) of livelihood (生活). To internal (內的) anguish (懊惱) is added (加重) external (外的) anguish. To physiological (生理的) anguish is added (加重) social (社會的) anguish (懊惱). I myself, living in this modern era (現代), am also one person suffering just like them. But just as they live on bravely, I too will walk forward looking only ahead. Although my fate (運命) sprouted (芽) under a heavy stone (石), I will push that stone (石) aside and live on. Thinking back now, I feel that my circumstances, which they <198> called adversity (逆境), were infinitely fortunate (多幸). This is because I gained experiences (體驗) others did not have and could experience psychology (心理) others did not, having spent a different kind of childhood without a mother. Even now, firmly suppressing the persecution (迫害) from my surroundings (周圍) and the deep, unknowable sorrow (憂愁) welling up from within (內部) myself, as I walk steadfastly, my mother, from far away, in some corner (一隅) of this world, will surely be watching (凝視) the resolute life journey (人生航路) of the beloved daughter she bore, wishing her happiness (幸福), with a pure heart and endlessly gentle eyes that transcend bloodline (血統) and the boundaries of mountains and rivers (山川). Just as I long for my mother, surely (勿論) my mother too silently worries and longs for "me." Why were we destined to long for each other yet remain separated? But how strong are we, mother and daughter (母女), who can love beyond the barriers (壁) like nationality (民族) and borders (國境) that humans have so prided themselves on preserving (保守) since ancient times? And don't you think it beautiful? The anguish (懊惱) and sorrow (悲哀) of sacrificial victims (犧牲者) like us will still occupy the hearts and circumstances (環境) of countless people (人間) in the future (未來). But based on their anguish (懊惱) and sorrow (悲哀), surely someday a beautiful flower garden will be created upon this earth. Isn't this also the original intention of the God (神) who created our humankind (人類)? (End) <199>

**Summary**

The author, Yang Chŏnghyang, reflects on growing up without her Japanese birth mother, whom she lost at age four. Lacking personal memories, she pieces together an image of her mother through others' stories, feeling both longing and admiration for her mother's strength amidst sorrow. She discusses the pain of maternal absence, the coldness of society, and the specific anguish of being a mixed-heritage woman (혼혈녀) in conservative 1940s Korea, facing discrimination similar to that against illegitimate children (서자). Despite personal struggles compounded by the general anxieties of youth in her era, she resolves to live bravely, believing her mother's love transcends borders and that their shared sacrifice might contribute to a better future.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its rare, personal testimony of the experiences of a Korean-Japanese mixed-heritage individual during the colonial period. It highlights the social stigma and discrimination ("persecution") faced by such individuals within Korean society, linking it to existing patriarchal and conservative social structures (e.g., attitudes towards 서자). The author's explicit discussion of being a "byproduct (副産物)" of a "transitional period," her feeling of being caught between cultures, and her assertion of love transcending nationality offer a poignant insight into the complexities of identity and human connection under Japanese rule. Historians might find the candid discussion of societal prejudice (even if attributed to a "small number") and the comparison between discrimination against mixed-heritage children and *sŏja* particularly revealing about social hierarchies and anxieties in late colonial Korea.

가신 어머니 생각
						梁貞香
梁貞香규수는 梁槿煥씨의 따님으로 作春 梨花女子 專門學校 문과를 졸업하였는데 네 살때에 그 어머니를 여이여 多感한 소녀기를 지낸 분인데, 이제 멀리 가 버린 생모인 내지인 여성을 그리는 이 一文을 草하다.

저에게 「어머님의 추억」을 쓰라고 社에서 말슴하시였으나 사실 저는 네 살쩍에 어머니의 따스한 품 안을 떠났읍니다. 그러니 어머니의 추억(追憶)이 어찌 있겠읍니까. 어리였을 때 맛 보았을 그 향긋한 젓내라도 제 기억(記憶) 속에 남어 있었으면 한답니다. 그러함으로 제가 모르는 우리 어머님을 동경(東京) 있을 때 잘 아시든 박순천(朴順天) 아주머님과 황신덕(黃信德) 아주머님 기외 여러분들의 말슴하시는 추억담(追憶談)을 듣기를 가장 즐긴답니다. 아주머님들의 입에서 흘러 나오는 「너의 어머니는...」 하면서 지난 날 어머니의 일상 생활(日常 生活)이라든가 언어 동작(言語 動作)이며 리별(離別) 당시의 어머니의 비애(悲哀)와 그러되 어디까지나＜195＞ 굳굳한 여성(女性)이였다」는 가지 가지 들을 수 있는 이야기가 곧, 제게는 「어머님의 추억(追憶)입니다. 그 이야기를 들으며 겉으로는 웃읍니다마는 때로는 가슴이 바지지 타오름을 느낍니다. 눈물이 핑 돕니다. 허나 저는 결ㅎ고 제 어머님이 보구 푸다고 남더러 말해 본 적은 없읍니다. 누가 「어머니가 보구푸지?」하는 무름에 저는 번번이 「무얼요 보구 싶기는 얼굴도 기억(記憶)할 수 없는 어머니를」 합니다. 허나 이는 단순한 허세(虛勢)에 지나지 아니 하겠지요. 그 누가 자기의 「어버이」를 그리지 아니 하며 누가 자기의 자식을 의식적(意識的)으로 검은 불행(不幸)의 구렁텡이로 쓰러 넣겠읍니까. 저는 각금 아버님에게 우리 어머님이 저이를 떠내 보낼 때 얼마나 침식을 잊어버리고 울으셨다는 말을 듣읍니다. 저는 각금 그때의 가지 가지 어머님이 당하신 일을 드르며 그 장면(場面)을 눈에 그려 봅니다. 그리고 가슴 속으로 흐느껴 운답니다. 그때 저 의 어머님의 나이가 겨우 현재의 제 나이밖에 아니 되신 젊은 여인(女人)이 사랑하는 남편과 그리고 귀여운 어린, 두 딸을 한꺼번에 잃게 되니 얼마나 마음이 아퍼겠읍니까, 제 나이가 차차 드니까 어머님의, 어머니로써가 아니고 한 아지 못하는 여성(女性)으로써라도 그가 당한 고난(苦難)을 동정 아니 할 수 없읍니다. 허나 저의 어머니는 씩씩하게 걸어 갔읍니다. 아모에게도 의뢰(依賴)하지 않고 굳세게, 걸어 갔읍니다. 혹인(或人)은 너머 냉정(冷情)한 여인이라 합니다 마는 저만은 어머니의 그때의 심정을 알 수 있읍니다. 저는 저의 어머님이 그의 용모(容貌)는 물론이요 그 마음세가 비단결 같이 보드라웁고 그리고 아름다웠다는 것을 그 누구의 입에서나 드를 수 있음을 기뻐합니다.
「어머니」는 어린 생명(生命)의 보호자(保護者)인 것은 물론이요, 태양(太陽) 같이 밝은 빛이 아님니까. 그러기에 밝은 빛을 잃어버린 우리 두 생명(生命) 우에는 비로서 검은 운명(運命)의 그늘(陰)이 깃드리였든 것입니다. 「어머니」라는 이 세 글자는 모름직이 인간(人間)이 창조(創造) 되었을 그때부터 인간(人間)들이 사용할 수 있는 만가지 억가지 언어(言語) 중에 가장 아름다웁고 그리고 그리웁디 그리운 언어(言語)라고 모도들 생각하여 왔을 것입니다. 그들은 특수한 환경과 운명(運命)을 제외(除外)하고는 모도 다 어머님의 따스한 손길로 어루만짐을 받으며 길리웠기 때문에 어머님의 사랑이 얼마나 고마웁고 그리고 「어린 生命」에게 없어서는 아니 될 귀(貴)한＜196＞ 것이라는 것을 깨닫고 체험(體驗)하였기 때문입니다. 허나 저는 여지것 어머님의 사랑을 통여 아지 못하며 성장(成長) 하였읍니다. 물론, 어리셔는 조모(祖母)님의 지극하신 사랑과 제 주위(周圍)를 둘러싼 친척들이 어머니가 되어 주었고 좀 더 커서는 학교의 선생님들 그리고 사회(社會)가 어머니 대신을 해 주었읍니다. 그러나 이것들도 역시 추상적(抽象的) 「어머니」였고 추상적 「어머니의 사랑」에 지나지 않는 것입니다. 결국 한 마디로 말한다면 사회애(社會愛)와 인류애(人類愛)로 무사히 성장(成長)하였다는데 지나지 않습니다.
